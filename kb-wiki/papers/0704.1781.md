# $^{10}$Li spectrum from $^{11}$Li fragmentation

> A recently developed time dependent model for the excitation of a nucleon from a bound state to a continuum resonant state in the system n+core is applied to the study of the population of the low energy continuum of the unbound $^{10}$Li system obtained from $^{11}$Li fragmentation. Comparison of the model results to new data from the GSI laboratory suggests that the reaction mechanism is dominated by final state effects rather than by the sudden process, but for the population of the l=0 virtual state, in which case the two mechanisms give almost identical results. There is also, for the first time, a clear evidence for the population of a d$_{5/2}$ resonance in $^{10}$Li.

# $^{10}$Li spectrum from $^{11}$Li fragmentation

## Key claim

The reaction mechanism for populating the low-energy continuum of unbound $^{10}$Li from $^{11}$Li fragmentation is dominated by final-state effects (neutron-core interaction) rather than by a sudden process, except for the population of the $l=0$ virtual state where the two mechanisms give almost identical results. There is clear evidence for the population of a $d_{5/2}$ resonance in $^{10}$Li for the first time.

## Method

A time-dependent perturbation theory model for inelastic-like excitation of a neutron from a bound state to a continuum resonant state in the n+core system was applied to the elastic breakup (diffraction reaction) of the two-neutron halo nucleus $^{11}$Li on a $^{12}$C target at 264 A.MeV. The model calculates the one-neutron-core relative energy spectrum using a semiclassical approximation with a delta-function neutron-target interaction, where the initial bound state wave functions are taken from a Woods-Saxon potential fitted to the $^{11}$Li separation energy and the final continuum states are calculated in a Woods-Saxon potential plus a particle-vibration coupling correction term $\delta V(r)$. The cross section is obtained by integrating the excitation probability over impact parameter, weighted by a core survival probability.

## Key numbers

- Two-neutron separation energy of $^{11}$Li: $0.3 \pm 0.27$ MeV
- Spectroscopic factors for $^{11}$Li initial state: $C^2S = 0.31$ for the $2s$ component, $C^2S = 0.45$ for the $p_{1/2}$ component
- $1p_{1/2}$ resonance in $^{10}$Li: energy $\varepsilon_{res} = 0.63$ MeV, width $\Gamma_j = 0.35$ MeV
- $1d_{5/2}$ resonance in $^{10}$Li: energy $\varepsilon_{res} = 1.55$ MeV, width $\Gamma_j = 0.18$ MeV
- Scattering length of the $2s$ continuum state in $^{10}$Li: $a_s = -17.2$ fm$^{-1}$ (from $\lim_{k \to 0} \delta_0/k$); effective range fit gives $a_s = -14.8$ fm, $r_e = 7.5$ fm
- Delta-interaction strength $v_2 = -8625$ MeV fm$^3$, obtained by matching volume integral to a n-$^{12}$C Woods-Saxon potential of strength $-50.5$ MeV, radius 2.9 fm, diffuseness 0.75 fm
- Strong absorption radius $R_s \approx 1.4 (A_p^{1/3} + A_t^{1/3})$ fm with diffuseness $a = 0.6$ fm

## Context

This work extends the time-dependent fragmentation model previously applied to $^{11}$Be, $^{14}$Be, and $^{14}$B (Blanchon et al., 2007) to the case of $^{11}$Li, and compares with new experimental data from GSI (Chulkov et al., 2007). The results contradict the "common wisdom" that continuum resonance strengths in the daughter nucleus directly reflect bound-state occupations of the same angular momentum in the mother nucleus, as discussed in recent work by Nunberg et al. (2007). The model enables extraction of neutron-core potential parameters from coincidence measurements and provides a framework for interpreting future fragmentation experiments on other two-neutron halo nuclei such as $^{6}$He and $^{14}$Be.
