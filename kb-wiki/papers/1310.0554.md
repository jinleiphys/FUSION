# Discriminating hadronic and quark stars through gravitational waves of fluid pulsation modes

> We investigate non-radial oscillations of hadronic, hybrid and pure self-bound strange quark stars with maximum masses above the mass of the recently observed massive pulsars PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0348-0432 with $M \approx 2 M_{\odot}$. For the hadronic equation of state we employ different parametrizations of a relativistic mean-field model and for quark matter we use the MIT bag model including the effect of strong interactions and color superconductivity. We find that the first pressure mode for strange quark stars has a very different shape than for hadronic and hybrid stars. For strange quarks stars the frequency of the p1 mode is larger than 6 kHz and diverge at small stellar masses, but for hadronic and hybrid stars it is in the range 4-6 kHz. This allows an observational identification of strange stars even if extra information such as the mass, the radius or the gravitational redshift of the object is unavailable or uncertain. Also, we find as in previous works that the frequency of the g-mode associated with the quark-hadron discontinuity in a hybrid star is in the range 0.4-1 kHz for all masses. Thus, compact objects emitting gravitational waves above 6 kHz should be interpreted as strange quark stars and those emitting a signal within 0.4-1 kHz should be interpreted as hybrid stars.

# Discriminating hadronic and quark stars through gravitational waves of fluid pulsation modes

## Key claim

The first pressure mode (p₁) of strange quark stars has frequencies above 6 kHz and diverges at small stellar masses, while for hadronic and hybrid stars it remains in the range 4–6 kHz; this difference allows observational identification of strange stars even without knowledge of the mass, radius, or gravitational redshift, provided the observed gravitational wave signal exceeds approximately 6 kHz.

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for stellar structure and then integrated the non-radial fluid oscillation equations within the relativistic Cowling approximation (setting metric perturbations to zero) using a shooting method to find eigenfrequencies. For hadronic matter they employed the GM1 and NL3 parametrizations of a relativistic mean-field model; for quark matter they used the MIT bag model including strong interaction effects via the a₄ parameter and color superconductivity via a CFL condensation term proportional to Δ²μ². Hybrid stars were constructed with a sharp hadron-quark discontinuity at which pressure and Gibbs free energy per baryon are continuous, with additional junction conditions imposed at the discontinuity for the oscillation equations.

## Key numbers

- The p₁ mode frequency for hadronic and hybrid stars lies in the range 4–6 kHz for stellar masses between 1 and 2 M⊙
- The p₁ mode frequency for strange quark stars is larger than 6 kHz and diverges at small stellar masses
- The g-mode associated with the quark-hadron discontinuity in hybrid stars falls in the range 0.4–1 kHz for all masses
- The f-mode frequency for all three star types clusters around approximately 2 kHz, with overlapping curves for masses above about 1 M⊙
- Strange stars cannot emit gravitational waves with f-mode frequency below approximately 1.7 kHz for any mass value
- Sources with mass in the range 1–1.5 M⊙ emitting an f-mode signal in the range 2–3 kHz would be strange stars
- The maximum masses of the hadronic models are 2.32 M⊙ (GM1) and 2.73 M⊙ (NL3)
- The observed pulsars PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0348-0432 have masses M ≈ 2 M⊙ (specifically 1.97 ± 0.04 M⊙ and 2.01 ± 0.04 M⊙ respectively)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of non-radial oscillations by Benhar (2004, 2007) and Sotani (2011), which used equations of state yielding maximum masses well below 2 M⊙ and thus incompatible with the massive pulsars PSR J1614-2230 (Demorest 2010) and PSR J0348-0432 (Antoniadis 2013). The authors also incorporate color superconductivity effects not addressed in those earlier works. The results enable a proposed observational discriminant: compact objects emitting gravitational waves above 6 kHz should be interpreted as strange quark stars, while those emitting a signal within 0.4–1 kHz should be interpreted as hybrid stars, providing a method to probe compact star interiors using upcoming detectors such as Advanced LIGO and Advanced VIRGO.
