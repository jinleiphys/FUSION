# Rapidity correlations test stochastic hydrodynamics

> We show that measurements of the rapidity dependence of transverse momentum correlations can be used to determine the characteristic time $τ_π$ that dictates the rate of isotropization of the stress energy tensor, as well as the shear viscosity $ν= η/sT$. We formulate methods for computing these correlations using second order dissipative hydrodynamics with noise. Current data are consistent with $τ_π/ν\sim 10$ but targeted measurements can improve this precision.

# Rapidity correlations test stochastic hydrodynamics

## Key claim

Measurements of the rapidity dependence of transverse momentum correlations can determine both the shear viscosity $\nu = \eta/sT$ and the relaxation time $\tau_\pi$ for pressure isotropization, but current data only constrain the ratio $\tau_\pi/\nu \sim 10$, a value twice that predicted by kinetic theory, and targeted measurements are needed to improve precision.

## Method

The authors formulate a stochastic hydrodynamic model starting from a Langevin equation for shear momentum modes, then apply the fluctuation-dissipation theorem to obtain deterministic evolution equations for the deviation of the momentum correlation function from its equilibrium value. They compare first-order (Navier-Stokes) and second-order (Müller-Israel-Stewart) diffusion equations for the correlation function $\Delta r$ against experimental data on the transverse momentum covariance $\mathcal{C}$ as a function of relative rapidity.

## Key numbers

- The ratio $\tau_\pi/\nu \sim 10$ is obtained from best fits to experimental data, which is double the value predicted by kinetic theory.
- The relaxation time for $\Delta r$ is half the relaxation time of the average shear mode, analogous to the Brownian motion result where $\Delta r$ relaxes with timescale $1/2\gamma$ compared to $1/\gamma$ for $\langle v \rangle$.
- The equilibrium correlation function is $r_{eq} = wT \delta_{ij} \delta(x_1 - x_2)$, where $w = e + p$ is the enthalpy density and $T$ is the temperature.

## Context

This work extends the stochastic hydrodynamic approach to two-particle correlations in heavy-ion collisions, building on the Brownian motion analogy and the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. The authors compare their calculations to experimental data from Agakishiev (2011) on transverse momentum covariance, finding that first-order hydrodynamics fails to reproduce the double-bump structure seen in central collision data, while second-order hydrodynamics with the relaxation time $\tau_\pi$ captures this feature. The results enable future extraction of $\tau_\pi$ and $\nu$ from targeted correlation measurements, with the authors noting that more realistic parameters in their evolution equation could improve the current estimate.
