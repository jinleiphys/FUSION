# Large space shell model calculations with small space results

## Key claim

Large space shell model calculations sometimes yield results whose form can be reproduced by smaller space calculations, but certain observables, particularly magnetic moments of excited 2+ states in Ca isotopes, require the large space approach because they show gross violations of the single-j shell model that cannot be captured in a truncated space.

## Method

The authors compare experimental data and large space shell model calculations for magnetic moments and quadrupole moments in N=28 isotones and Ca isotopes against predictions from the single-j shell model (valence protons in the f7/2 shell) and first-order perturbation theory. They examine the g-factor quenching behavior for ground states and the sign reversal of g factors for excited 2+ states, and they analyze quadrupole moments using the single-j formula Q = -(2j+1-2n)/(2j+2) * <r^2> e_eff from Lawson’s book.

## Key numbers

- Experimental magnetic moment of 49Sc: 5.615(35) μN (T. Ohtsubo et al.)
- NN+3N calculated magnetic moments for even-odd Ca ground states: A=43: -1.56 μN, A=45: -1.45 μN, A=47: -1.38 μN
- NN+3N calculated quadrupole moments for Ca isotopes: A=43: -0.0246, A=45: +0.0252, A=47: +0.0856 (units implied as eb)
- Schmidt g factor for f7/2 protons: (3+2.793)/3.5 = +1.655
- Schmidt magnetic moment for 41Ca: -1.913 μN; experimental value: -1.58 μN
- g factor of an f7/2 neutron: -1.193/3.5 = -0.547

## Context

This work extends the discussion of Arima, Horie, and Noya (1960s) on first-order perturbation quenching of g factors, and the experimental program of Speidel et al. on N=28 isotones. It also relates to the NN+3N calculations of Garcia et al. for Ca isotopes and the measurements of Taylor et al. for 46Ca. The paper highlights that while single-j shell models work for ground-state moments in some cases, the positive g factors of 2+ states in 42Ca and 44Ca require large space treatments with ~50% deformed admixtures, and suggests that similar non-perturbative admixtures (~15%) may explain the flat experimental trend of even-odd Ca magnetic moments versus the predicted downslope.
