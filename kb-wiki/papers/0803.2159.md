# Elie: an event generator for nuclear reactions

> An event generator for the description of nuclear reactions in the Fermi energy range is briefly introduced and first comparisons with experimental data are shown.

## Key claim

The ELIE event generator reproduces experimental INDRA data for nuclear reactions in the Fermi energy range using a schematic model that abandons equilibrium statistical hypotheses, instead relying on a frozen approximation of the initial nucleon momentum distribution and a random partition process constrained by geometry, conservation laws, and a maximum internal temperature of about 5.5 MeV.

## Method

The model uses a two-step scenario: first, an entrance channel phase where the geometrical overlap of projectile and target determines projectile-like, target-like, and participant masses, with participant partition generated by randomly picking nucleons from the initial Fermi momentum distribution subject to a maximum temperature constraint; second, a phase using the SIMON event generator for space-time propagation and secondary decay. The mean free path for nucleon-nucleon collisions is the single adjustable parameter, evolving from 30 fm at 25 MeV/u down to 5 fm at 82 MeV/u.

## Key numbers

- Maximum temperature T_max = 5.5 MeV used to reject fragments with excitation energy exceeding this value, with a level density parameter of A/10 and excitation energy E* = 3 MeV/u
- Mean free path evolves from 30 fm at 25 MeV/u to 10 fm at 50 MeV/u and down to 5 fm at 82 MeV/u
- Central Xe+Sn collisions at 25, 32, and 50 MeV/u compared with INDRA data, requiring at least 80% of total charge and total linear momentum detected
- For central collisions, reduced impact parameter lower than 0.5 and centered around 0.3, corresponding to theta_flow larger than 25 degrees
- Minimum bias Ni+Ni collisions at 82 MeV/u compared with INDRA data

## Context

This work extends the participant-spectator picture from high-energy nuclear physics to the Fermi energy range, contrasting with equilibrium statistical models such as SMM (Statistical Multifragmentation Model) which assume low-density equilibrium. The model provides an alternative to microscopic transport models, which are computationally lengthy, and to statistical models that require severe event selections. The approach enables full comparison with experimental data without kinematical cuts or selection criteria, and suggests that multifragmentation can be explained by a random process with Fermi motion and entrance channel memory rather than a compression-expansion scenario.
