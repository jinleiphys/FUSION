# Compton Form Factor Extraction using Quantum Deep Neural Networks

> We extract Compton form factors (CFFs) from deeply virtual Compton scattering measurements at the Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility (JLab) using quantum-inspired deep neural networks (QDNNs). The analysis implements the twist-2 Belitsky-Kirchner-Müller formalism and employs a fitting strategy that emulates standard local fits. Using pseudodata, we benchmark QDNNs against classical deep neural networks (CDNNs) and find that QDNNs often deliver higher predictive accuracy and tighter uncertainties at comparable model complexity. Guided by these results, we introduce a quantitative selection metric that indicates when QDNNs or CDNNs are optimal for a given experimental fit. After obtaining local extractions from the JLab data, we perform a standard neural-network global CFF fit and compare with previous global analyses. The results support QDNNs as an efficient and complementary tool to CDNNs for CFF determination and for future multidimensional studies of parton distributions and hadronic structure.

## Key claim
Quantum-inspired deep neural networks (QDNNs) can extract Compton form factors (CFFs) from deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS) data with higher predictive accuracy and tighter uncertainties than classical deep neural networks (CDNNs) at comparable model complexity, but only in kinematic bins where a data-driven "DVCS quantum qualifier" indicates they are optimal; the advantage is demonstrated on a quantum simulator, not on physical quantum hardware.

## Method
The authors implemented both classical deep neural networks (CDNNs) and quantum-inspired deep neural networks (QDNNs) using the twist-2 Belitsky-Kirchner-Müller (BKM10) helicity-amplitude formalism to perform local CFF extractions from unpolarized DVCS cross sections. The QDNNs used parameterized quantum circuits with 6 qubits and StronglyEntanglingLayers as trainable feature maps, implemented via PennyLane on a state-vector simulator, with gradients computed by the parameter-shift rule. Both network types were trained with the same physics-informed loss function (weighted least-χ²) on pseudodata replicas generated from JLab experimental kinematics, and the extracted CFFs were used to construct a global DNN parameterization for comparison with previous global analyses.

## Key numbers
- The dataset comprises 3,885 data points from JLab Hall A (E12-06-114, E07-007, E00-110) and Hall B (e1-DVCS1), spanning Q² from 1.11 to 8.51 GeV², -t from 0.11 to 1.373 GeV², and x_B from 0.126 to 0.617.
- The QDNN uses n=6 qubits, providing a 2⁶-dimensional Hilbert-space embedding.
- The pseudodata generation model uses a parameterization G(x_B,t) = (a x_B² + b x_B) e^{c t² + d t + e} + f, with specific parameter values listed for each CFF (e.g., for Re e H: a=-4.41, b=1.68, c=-9.14, d=-3.57, e=1.54, f=-1.37).
- The kinematic cuts for twist-2 validity are Q² > 1.5 GeV² and |t|/Q² ≤ 0.25.

## Context
This work extends the neural-network approach to CFF extraction pioneered by Kumerički et al. (2011) and the DNN-based methods of Grigsby and Kriesten, applying quantum-inspired architectures to the DVCS analysis framework previously used by Diaz (2025). The results enable future multidimensional studies of parton distributions and hadronic structure, and the quantitative selection metric (DVCS quantum qualifier) provides a data-driven criterion for choosing between classical and quantum-inspired models in experimental fits.
