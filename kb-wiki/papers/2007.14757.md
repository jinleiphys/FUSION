# Thermoelectric transport coefficients of quark matter

> A thermal gradient and/or a chemical potential gradient in a conducting medium can lead to an electric field, an effect known as thermoelectric effect or Seebeck effect. In the context of heavy-ion collisions, we estimate the thermoelectric transport coefficients for quark matter within the ambit of the Nambu-Jona Lasinio (NJL) model. We estimate the thermal conductivity, electrical conductivity, and the Seebeck coefficient of hot and dense quark matter. These coefficients are calculated using the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation within relaxation time approximation. The relaxation times for the quarks are estimated from the quark-quark and quark-antiquark scattering through in-medium meson exchange within the NJL model.

## Key claim

The authors estimate thermoelectric transport coefficients (Seebeck coefficient, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity) for hot and dense quark matter within the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, finding that the Seebeck effect requires a finite baryon chemical potential to be non-zero, and that the relaxation times derived from in-medium meson exchange produce nontrivial temperature dependence near the chiral crossover.

## Method

The authors solve the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation in the relaxation time approximation for two-flavor quark matter, using the NJL model to compute medium-dependent constituent quark masses and meson (sigma and pion) masses. Relaxation times for quarks and antiquarks are obtained from quark-quark and quark-antiquark scattering cross sections calculated to order 1/Nc through sigma and pion exchange in s- and t-channels, with meson propagators evaluated in the random phase approximation including both masses and widths. The Seebeck coefficient, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity are then computed from momentum integrals over the equilibrium distribution functions weighted by the energy-dependent relaxation times.

## Key numbers

* Chiral crossover temperatures: approximately 188 MeV for mu=0, 180 MeV for mu=100 MeV, and 153 MeV for mu=200 MeV, defined by the peak in dM/dT.
* Mott temperatures (where pion mass equals twice the quark mass): approximately 198 MeV for mu=0, 192 MeV for mu=100 MeV, and 166 MeV for mu=200 MeV.
* NJL model parameters: current quark mass m0=5.6 MeV, three-momentum cutoff Lambda=587.9 MeV, coupling G*Lambda^2=2.44, yielding constituent quark mass M=397 MeV in vacuum.
* Pion mass used for parameter fitting: m_pi=135 MeV; pion decay constant: f_pi=94 MeV; vacuum quark condensate: <u u> = <d d> = (-241 MeV)^3.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of thermoelectric transport in hadronic matter using the Hadron Resonance Gas model (Bhatt 2018, Das 2020) to the deconfined partonic phase described by the NJL model, which incorporates nontrivial vacuum structure and chiral symmetry breaking near the critical temperature. The authors note that prior estimates of the Seebeck coefficient in partonic matter (Dey 2020, Zhang 2020) used relaxation times from perturbative QCD valid only at asymptotically high temperatures, whereas the present calculation uses medium-dependent meson exchange scattering rates that capture the behavior near the phase transition. The results enable more realistic modeling of electromagnetic field evolution and charge transport in heavy-ion collisions at finite baryon density, particularly relevant for future experiments at FAIR and NICA.
