# Structure effects in the $^{15}$N($n,γ$)$^{16}$N radiative capture reaction from the Coulomb dissociation of $^{16}$N

> Purpose : The aim of this paper is to calculate the $^{15}$N($n, γ$)$^{16}$N radiative capture cross section and its subsequent reaction rate by an indirect method and in that process investigate the effects of spectroscopic factors of different levels of $^{16}$N to the cross section. Method : A fully quantum mechanical Coulomb breakup theory under the aegis of post-form distorted wave Born approximation is used to calculate the Coulomb breakup of $^{16}$N on Pb at 100 MeV/u. This is then related to the photodisintegration cross section of $^{16}$N($γ, n$)$^{15}$N and subsequently invoking the principle of detailed balance, the $^{15}$N($n, γ$)$^{16}$N capture cross section is calculated. Results : The non-resonant capture cross section is calculated with spectroscopic factors from the shell model and those extracted (including uncertainties) from two recent experiments. The data seems to favor a more single particle nature for the low-lying states of $^{16}$N. The total neutron capture rate is also calculated by summing up non-resonant and resonant (significant only at temperatures greater than 1 GK) contributions and comparison is made with other charged particle capture rates. In the typical temperature range of $0.1-1.2$ GK, almost all the contribution to the reaction rate comes from capture cross sections below 0.25 MeV. Conclusion : We have attempted to resolve the discrepancy in the spectroscopic factors of low-lying $^{16}$N levels and conclude that it would certainly be useful to perform a Coulomb dissociation experiment to find the low energy capture cross section for the reaction, especially below 0.25 MeV.

## Key claim

The paper calculates the $^{15}$N($n, \gamma$)$^{16}$N radiative capture cross section and reaction rate using an indirect Coulomb dissociation method, finding that the data favor spectroscopic factors close to shell model predictions (near unity) for the low-lying states of $^{16}$N, which would support a single-particle nature for these states; however, the authors note that a direct Coulomb dissociation experiment would be needed to resolve remaining discrepancies in spectroscopic factors, especially for capture energies below 0.25 MeV.

## Method

The authors use a fully quantum mechanical post-form finite range distorted wave Born approximation (FRDWBA) to calculate the Coulomb breakup of $^{16}$N on a Pb target at 100 MeV/u beam energy. The relative energy spectra from this breakup are related to the photodisintegration cross section of $^{16}$N($\gamma, n$)$^{15}$N, and then the principle of detailed balance is invoked to obtain the $^{15}$N($n, \gamma$)$^{16}$N radiative capture cross section. The non-resonant capture cross section is calculated using spectroscopic factors from the shell model and from two recent experiments (Bardayan 2008 and Guo 2014), and the total reaction rate is obtained by summing non-resonant and resonant contributions.

## Key numbers

- One-neutron separation energy of $^{16}$N ground state (2$^-$): 2.491 MeV
- Excited state energies of $^{16}$N: 0.120 MeV (0$^-$), 0.298 MeV (3$^-$), and 0.397 MeV (1$^-$)
- Shell model spectroscopic factors used: 0.93 (2$^-$), 0.95 (0$^-$), 0.87 (3$^-$), 0.96 (1$^-$)
- Woods-Saxon potential parameters: radius 1.25 fm, diffuseness 0.65 fm
- Direct measurement data available at neutron lab energies of 25, 152, and 370 keV (Meissner 1996)
- In the temperature range 0.1-1.2 GK, almost all contribution to the reaction rate comes from capture cross sections below 0.25 MeV
- Resonant contribution becomes significant only at temperatures greater than 1 GK
- The 862 keV resonance is the only relevant resonance contributing at high temperature

## Context

This work extends the Coulomb dissociation method previously applied to $^8$Li($n,\gamma$)$^9$Li and $^{14}$C($n,\gamma$)$^{15}$C reactions to the $^{15}$N($n,\gamma$)$^{16}$N system, which plays an important role in red giant star nucleosynthesis and inhomogeneous big bang nucleosynthesis. The paper addresses discrepancies in spectroscopic factors of low-lying $^{16}$N levels between shell model predictions, the experiment of Bohne (with 30% uncertainty), Bardayan (2008), and Guo (2014), and finds that shell model values (supported by Bardayan 2008) best reproduce the direct capture data of Meissner (1996). The calculated reaction rates are compared with those of Rauscher (1994), Bardayan (2008), Guo (2014), and Meissner (1996), and the neutron capture rate is shown to dominate over charged particle capture reactions ($^{15}$N($p,\alpha$)$^{12}$C, $^{15}$N($p,\gamma$)$^{16}$O, $^{15}$N($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{19}$F) at temperatures below 0.25 GK.
