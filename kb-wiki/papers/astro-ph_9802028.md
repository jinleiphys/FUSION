# Phase Transitions in Neutron Stars

> Phase transitions in neutron stars due to formation of quark matter, kaon condensates, etc. are discussed with particular attention to the order of these transitions. Observational consequences of phase transitions in pulsar angular velocities are examined.

# Phase Transitions in Neutron Stars

## Key claim
Phase transitions in neutron stars (to quark matter, kaon condensates, etc.) are expected to be weakly first order rather than second order when finite Coulomb and surface energies of mixed-phase structures are included, and these transitions produce characteristic signatures in pulsar spin-down such as a diverging braking index as the critical angular velocity is approached from below.

## Method
The authors solve a generalized rotating version of the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation for neutron stars with a first-order phase transition at the center, incorporating centrifugal forces. They derive analytic expressions for the moment of inertia as a function of angular velocity near the critical point, and compute the resulting braking index from the rotational energy loss equation assuming dipole radiation (n=3). Cooling-induced phase transitions are treated analogously by replacing angular velocity with temperature.

## Key numbers
- Typical sizes of nuclear and quark matter structures in a ~1.4 solar mass neutron star are ~10^{-14} m.
- Normal nuclear matter exists only at moderate densities, rho ~ 1-2 rho_0 (where rho_0 is normal nuclear matter density).
- The coefficient c_2 in the moment of inertia expansion is proportional to Delta_rho * Omega_0^3, where Delta_rho is the density difference between phases and Omega_0 is the critical angular velocity.
- Observed glitches in Crab and Vela pulsars have Delta_Omega/Omega ~ 10^{-8}.
- The braking index diverges as n(Omega) ~ c_2 / sqrt(1 - Omega^2/Omega_0^2) near the critical angular velocity.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of mixed phases in neutron stars by Glendenning (1992) and Heiselberg, Pethick, and Staubo (1993), who examined quark-nuclear mixed phase structures (droplets, rods, plates). It also builds on Hartle's (1967) general relativistic treatment of slowly rotating stars, which the authors note cannot be applied directly due to density discontinuities at first-order transitions. The paper enables observational tests for phase transitions by searching for characteristic divergences in pulsar braking indices, and connects to glitch mechanisms proposed by Glendenning, Pei, and Weber (1997) where a sudden softening of the equation of state causes a backbending moment of inertia.
