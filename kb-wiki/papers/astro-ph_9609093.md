# Spin-Down of Neutron Stars and Compositional Transitions in the Cold Crustal Matter

> Transitions of nuclear compositions in the crust of a neutron star induced by stellar spin-down are evaluated at zero temperature. We construct a compressible liquid-drop model for the energy of nuclei immersed in a neutron gas, including pairing and shell correction terms, in reference to the known properties of the ground state of matter above neutron drip density, $4.3 \times 10^{11} g cm^{-3}$. Recent experimental values and extrapolations of nuclear masses are used for a description of matter at densities below neutron drip. Changes in the pressure of matter in the crust due to the stellar spin-down are calculated by taking into account the structure of the crust of a slowly and uniformly rotating relativistic neutron star. If the initial rotation period is $\sim$ ms, these changes cause nuclei, initially being in the ground-state matter above a mass density of about $3 \times 10^{13} g cm^{-3}$, to absorb neutrons in the equatorial region where the matter undergoes compression, and to emit them in the vicinity of the rotation axis where the matter undergoes decompression. Heat generation by these processes is found to have significant effects on the thermal evolution of old neutron stars with low magnetic fields; the surface emission predicted from this heating is compared with the $ROSAT$ observations of X-ray emission from millisecond pulsars and is shown to be insufficient to explain the observed X-ray luminosities.

## Key claim

If the initial rotation period of a neutron star is ~ms, spin-down causes nuclei in the ground-state matter above a mass density of about 3 × 10^13 g cm^−3 to absorb neutrons in the equatorial region (where matter undergoes compression) and emit neutrons near the rotation axis (where matter undergoes decompression), but the resulting heat generation is insufficient to explain the observed X-ray luminosities of millisecond pulsars from ROSAT observations.

## Method

The authors constructed a compressible liquid-drop model for nuclei immersed in a neutron gas in the inner crust (above neutron drip density 4.3 × 10^11 g cm^−3), adding pairing and shell correction terms to the Baym-Bethe-Pethick (1971) formalism to reproduce the Hartree-Fock results of Negele and Vautherin (1973). For the outer crust (below neutron drip), they used experimental nuclear masses from Audi and Wapstra (1993) and extrapolations from Moller et al. (1995) within the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland (1971) framework. They calculated pressure changes in the crust during spin-down using the general relativistic rotating star structure formalism of Hartle (1967), assuming a 1.4 solar mass, 10 km neutron star with magnetic dipole spin evolution.

## Key numbers

- Neutron drip density: 4.34 × 10^11 g cm^−3, at pressure 4.93 × 10^−4 MeV fm^−3, with electron chemical potential 26.21 MeV
- The initial rotation period considered is ~1 ms (comparable to the shortest observed pulsar period of 1.6 ms)
- The ratio of rotational energy to gravitational potential at 1 ms rotation: ~0.1
- The redshift factor at the surface of the non-rotating star: 2GM/(R_s c^2) ~ 0.4
- The spin-down timescale: τ_s ~ 6 × 10^5 [10^10 G / B(G)]^2 [Ω_0/Ω(t=0)]^2 yr
- Magnetic field values for pulsars range from ~10^8 G to ~10^12 G
- The surface displacement formula: ξ(R_s,θ)/R_s = (−0.039 + 0.149 sin^2 θ)(Ω/Ω_0)^2

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of compositional changes in neutron star crusts under compression/decompression by Lattimer et al. (1977) for tidal disruption scenarios and by Sato (1979) and Haensel and Zdunik (1990a) for accreting neutron stars. It applies these ideas to the new context of isolated neutron stars undergoing spin-down, where centrifugal force changes cause compression in equatorial regions and decompression near the poles. The predicted surface emission is compared with ROSAT observations of millisecond pulsars (Ogelman 1995), finding the heating insufficient to explain the observed X-ray luminosities, which opens the question of what other heating mechanisms might be at work in old, low-magnetic-field neutron stars.
