# Solutions of the Bohr hamiltonian, a compendium

> The Bohr hamiltonian, also called collective hamiltonian, is one of the cornerstone of nuclear physics and a wealth of solutions (analytic or approximated) of the associated eigenvalue equation have been proposed over more than half a century (confining ourselves to the quadrupole degree of freedom). Each particular solution is associated with a peculiar form for the $V(β,γ)$ potential. The large number and the different details of the mathematical derivation of these solutions, as well as their increased and renewed importance for nuclear structure and spectroscopy, demand a thorough discussion. It is the aim of the present monograph to present in detail all the known solutions in $γ-$unstable and $γ-$stable cases, in a taxonomic and didactical way. In pursuing this task we especially stressed the mathematical side leaving the discussion of the physics to already published comprehensive material. The paper contains also a new approximate solution for the linear potential, and a new solution for prolate and oblate soft axial rotors, as well as some new formulae and comments, and an appendix on the analysis of a few interesting numerical sequences appearing in this context. The quasi-dynamical SO(2) symmetry is proposed in connection with the labeling of bands in triaxial nuclei.

## Key claim

This paper is a taxonomic compendium of known analytic and approximate solutions of the Bohr hamiltonian for quadrupole degrees of freedom, presented in a didactical manner with emphasis on the mathematical derivations rather than on the physical interpretation, which is left to other published material. The author claims the paper contains a new approximate solution for the linear potential, a new solution for prolate and oblate soft axial rotors, and proposes the quasi-dynamical SO(2) symmetry for labeling bands in triaxial nuclei.

## Method

The author systematically reviews and derives solutions of the Bohr hamiltonian eigenvalue equation for various potential forms, separating the treatment into gamma-unstable cases (where the potential depends only on beta) and gamma-stable cases (axial and triaxial). For each case, the author presents the separation of variables into beta and gamma-angular equations, gives the relevant quantum numbers (tau, nu, K, L), and provides the analytic forms of the eigenfunctions and energy spectra, often in terms of special functions such as Bessel functions, Laguerre polynomials, and Hermite polynomials.

## Key numbers

* For the harmonic oscillator solution (Bohr, 1952): the energy spectrum is given by E = (N + 5/2) hbar omega, with N = 2n_beta + tau, and the ratio R_{4/2} = E(4+)/E(2+) = 2.
* For the infinite square well (Wilets and Jean, 1956; Iachello, 2000): the ratio R_{4/2} is 2.20, and the eigenenergies are given by epsilon_{xi,tau} = (x_{xi,tau} / beta_w)^2, where x_{xi,tau} are zeros of the Bessel function J_{tau+3/2}(z).
* For the Davidson potential (Elliott et al.): the energy spectrum is E = hbar omega (2n + 1 + sqrt((tau+3/2)^2 + G)) - 2A, where G is a parameter; for G=0 the harmonic oscillator spectrum is recovered, and for G approaching infinity the ground state band obeys the tau(tau+3) rule.

## Context

This compendium extends and systematizes the work of Bohr (1952), Wilets and Jean (1956), Elliott and collaborators, and Iachello (2000), among others, by collecting all known solutions of the Bohr hamiltonian in a single taxonomic framework. The paper enables future researchers to easily compare different potential forms and their predictions for nuclear spectra, and provides new solutions for the linear potential and for soft axial rotors that can be applied to describe shape phase transitions and collective excitations in nuclei.
