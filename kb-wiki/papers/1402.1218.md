# Fusion using time-dependent density-constrained DFT

> We present results for calculating fusion cross-sections using a new microscopic approach based on a time-dependent density-constrained DFT calculations. The theory is implemented by using densities and other information obtained from TDDFT time-evolution of the nuclear system as constraint on the density for DFT calculations.

# Fusion using time-dependent density-constrained DFT

## Key claim
The DC-TDHF method can reproduce experimental fusion cross-sections for several light and medium-mass systems without introducing constraining operators, but the calculated cross-sections at higher energies (2–3 times the barrier height) may be systematically larger than experimental data because breakup channels and other inelastic processes are not naturally included in the TDHF dynamics.

## Method
The authors perform time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) time-evolution of colliding nuclei with no restrictions, then at selected times during the evolution they freeze the instantaneous neutron and proton densities and perform a static Hartree-Fock minimization constrained to those densities. This yields the ion-ion interaction potential along the dynamical path, and fusion cross-sections are subsequently calculated by integrating the Schrödinger equation using the incoming-wave boundary condition (IWBC) method.

## Key numbers
- For the $^{12}$C + $^{16}$O system, the DC-TDHF potential barrier has a peak energy of 7.77 MeV.
- For the $^{12}$C + $^{24}$O system, the barrier peak is 6.64 MeV, occurring at a slightly larger internuclear distance.
- The DC-TDHF calculations for $^{16}$O + $^{16}$O show excellent agreement with low-energy experimental data from Refs. [o16o16expdata_1, o16o16expdata_2].
- For the $^{40}$Ca + $^{48}$Ca system at higher energies, the calculated cross-sections are larger than experimental ones by about a factor of two.

## Context
This work extends the DC-TDHF method previously developed by the group (Umar and Oberacker, 2006) to calculate fusion barriers and cross-sections for a range of systems including neutron-rich light nuclei relevant to accreting neutron star crust composition (O+O and C+O systems) and medium-mass Ca+Ca systems studied experimentally by Montagnoli et al. (2011). The method provides a fully microscopic alternative to coupled-channel approaches such as those of Esbensen (2010), and the authors note that for lighter systems at high energies, adding the microscopically calculated excitation energy to the ion-ion potential (analogous to repulsive-core coupled-channel potentials) may improve agreement with data, though this requires further study.
