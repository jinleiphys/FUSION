# Properties of High-Density Matter in Neutron Stars

> This short review aims at giving a brief overview of the various states of matter that have been suggested to exist in the ultra-dense centers of neutron stars. Particular emphasis is put on the role of quark deconfinement in neutron stars and on the possible existence of compact stars made of absolutely stable strange quark matter (strange stars). Astrophysical phenomena, which distinguish neutron stars from quark stars, are discussed and the question of whether or not quark deconfinement may occur in neutron stars is investigated. Combined with observed astrophysical data, such studies are invaluable to delineate the complex structure of compressed baryonic matter and to put firm constraints on the largely unknown equation of state of such matter.

## Key claim

This short review surveys the various states of matter proposed to exist in neutron-star cores, with the central hedge being that the strange quark matter hypothesis, that quark matter made of up, down, and strange quarks may be more stable than ordinary nuclear matter, remains unconfirmed or rejected after three decades, so it remains a serious but unproven possibility.

## Method

The authors review theoretical models of superdense matter in neutron stars, comparing neutron stars with hypothetical strange quark stars. They discuss the role of quark deconfinement, color superconductivity, and the strange quark matter hypothesis, and examine astrophysical phenomena that could distinguish neutron stars from quark stars, such as surface electric fields, mass-radius relations, rotational properties, and thermal emission. The review also covers recent calculations using local and non-local 3-flavor Nambu-Jona Lasinio (NJL) models with repulsive vector interactions to study quark deconfinement in high-mass neutron stars, and the possible formation of quark-hadron mixed phases with geometrical structures.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of strange quark stars: approximately 2 solar masses (same as neutron stars).
- Radii of strange quark stars: less than or equal to 10–12 km; neutron star radii greater than or equal to 10–12 km.
- Electric surface fields on bare strange quark stars: approximately 10^18 V/cm for non-superconducting quark matter, exceeding 10^19 V/cm for color-superconducting quark matter.
- Kepler period for a strange star of 1.45 solar masses: 0.55 to 0.8 milliseconds, compared to about 1 millisecond for neutron stars of the same mass.
- Luminosity of electron-positron pairs from a hot strange star surface at 10^11 K: up to approximately 3 × 10^51 erg/s.
- Maximum masses of neutron stars with a mixed phase of hadronic matter and deconfined quarks: 2.1 to 2.4 solar masses, depending on the strength of quark vector repulsion.
- Radii of these high-mass neutron stars: between 12 and 13 km.
- Deleptonization densities in the mixed phase: 4.55 times nuclear saturation density (rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3) for zero vector repulsion, and 5.33 rho_0 for vector repulsion G_V/G_S = 0.09.
- Bag constant used for MIT bag model calculations: B = 57 MeV/fm^3 (B^(1/4) = 145 MeV).
- Duration of possible stellar spin-up ("backbending") due to quark deconfinement: up to approximately 10^8 years.
- Temperature below which neutrino emissivity from electron-blob Bremsstrahlung in the mixed phase is important: less than or equal to 10^8 K.

## Context

This review extends the long-standing discussion of the strange quark matter hypothesis, originally proposed by Witten (1984) and Farhi and Jaffe (1984), and the possibility of strange stars. It builds on prior work by Weber (2005), Glendenning (1997), and others on neutron star structure and quark deconfinement. The review also incorporates recent calculations using NJL models (Orsaria et al., 2013, 2014) to study quark deconfinement in high-mass neutron stars, and discusses observational signatures such as anomalous braking indices (Glendenning, 1997) and thermal emission from central compact objects and X-ray dim isolated neutron stars. The work enables future observational tests with missions like NICER, FAST, and SKA to constrain the equation of state of dense matter and potentially confirm or rule out the existence of quark stars.
