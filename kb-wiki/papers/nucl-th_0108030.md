# Nuclear equation of state and spectral functions

> An overview is given of the theoretical work on nucleon spectral functions in finite nuclei. The consequences of the observed spectral strength distribution are then considered in the context of the nuclear-matter saturation problem. Arguments are presented suggesting that short-range correlations are mainly responsible for the actual value of the observed charge density in 208Pb and by extension for the empirical value of the saturation density of nuclear matter. This observation combined with the general understanding of the spectroscopic strength suggests that a renewed study of nuclear matter, emphasizing the self-consistent determination of the spectral strength due to short-range and tensor correlations, may shed light on the perennial nuclear saturation problem. First results using such a scheme are presented. Arguments are discussed that clarify the role of long-range correlations and their relevance for nuclear saturation.

# Nuclear Equation of State and Spectral Functions

## Key claim
Short-range correlations (SRC) are mainly responsible for the empirical value of the saturation density of nuclear matter, and a self-consistent treatment of nucleon spectral functions including only SRC, while discarding long-range pion-exchange correlations, may yield correct saturation properties without requiring three-body forces or relativistic effects.

## Method
The authors review experimental (e,e'p) data on spectroscopic factors and occupation numbers in finite nuclei, then present self-consistent Green's function calculations for nuclear matter. The ladder-diagram effective interaction is solved using dressed particle and hole spectral functions, iterating the Dyson equation until self-consistency is achieved for the full propagator. Calculations are performed at two densities using the Reid potential, including contributions from \(^1S_0\), \(^3S_1\)-\(^3D_1\), and higher partial waves.

## Key numbers
- Spectroscopic factor for the \(3s_{1/2}\) proton removal in \(^{208}\)Pb: 0.65
- Depletion of deeply-bound proton orbitals in \(^{208}\)Pb: about 15%
- Central density of heavy nuclei: 0.16 nucleons/fm\(^3\) corresponding to \(k_F = 1.33\) fm\(^{-1}\)
- In \(^{16}\)O, quasihole states contribute only 37% of the total binding energy, with 63% from continuum terms
- Self-consistent calculations performed at \(k_F = 1.36\) fm\(^{-1}\) and \(k_F = 1.45\) fm\(^{-1}\)
- Quasiparticle pole strength at the Fermi surface: \(z_{k_F} = 0.7\) (reduction from 1.0 in Fermi gas)
- For comparison, three hole-line calculations yield saturation at \(k_F = 1.565\) fm\(^{-1}\) with binding energy -16.18 MeV

## Context
This paper extends the experimental and theoretical work on nucleon spectral functions from (e,e'p) reactions (Mougey 1976, Lapikas 1993) and earlier nuclear-matter calculations (Ramos 1989, Muther 1995). It challenges the conventional hole-line expansion approach (Day 1981, Baldo 1999) by arguing that long-range pion-exchange ring diagrams, which contribute strongly to binding in nuclear matter, are not relevant in finite nuclei (Pandharipande 1975). The self-consistent scheme builds on earlier approximate implementations (Dickhoff 1981, Dewulf 2000) and provides a new framework for calculating the nuclear equation of state that can be directly tested against (e,e'p) data on high-momentum components (JLab experiment).
