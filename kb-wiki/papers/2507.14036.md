# Deep Image Reconstruction for Background Subtraction in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Jet reconstruction in an ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collision suffers from a notoriously large, fluctuating thermal background. Traditional background subtraction methods struggle to remove this soft background while preserving the jet's hard substructure. In this Letter, we present DeepSub, the first machine learning-based approach for full-event background subtraction. DeepSub utilizes a model based on Swin Transformer layers to denoise jet images and disentangle hard jets from the heavy-ion background. DeepSub significantly outperforms existing subtraction techniques by reproducing key jet observables such as jet $p_\mathrm{T}$ and mass, and substructure observables such as girth and the energy correlation function, at the sub-percent to percent level. As such, DeepSub paves the way for precision heavy-ion measurements in hitherto inaccessible kinematic regimes.

## Key claim

DeepSub is the first machine-learning-based approach for full-event background subtraction in heavy-ion collisions, using a Swin Transformer architecture to denoise jet images. The authors claim it reproduces key jet observables at the sub-percent to percent level, but this performance is demonstrated primarily on JEWEL-generated events on which the model was trained, and the paper acknowledges that out-of-distribution validation on CoLBT-Hydro events still shows deviations of 1-5% in the core of distributions and larger in the tails.

## Method

The authors generate dijet events using JEWEL with PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=5.02 TeV and 0-10% centrality, then embed them into simulated thermal background events with multiplicity sampled from a Gaussian (mean 15000, std 122) and pT from a Boltzmann distribution (mean 1.2 GeV). The eta-phi plane is discretized into a 128x128 grid covering |eta|<3 and 0<=phi<pi, creating input images of total transverse momentum per bin. DeepSub uses 6 Residual Swin Transformer Blocks with 6 Swin Transformer Layers each and 6 attention heads, trained with MSE loss between the reconstructed and true signal images on 360k training events.

## Key numbers

- Jet pT spectrum: DeepSub remains within 1% of truth for jets between 200-800 GeV, while ICS overshoots by up to 10%; above 1 TeV, DeepSub fluctuates by about 5% versus ICS at about 20%
- Jet mass: DeepSub overpredicts by about 40% in the lowest mass bins (3-20 GeV), while ICS overshoots by an order of magnitude; in the intermediate region (20-60 GeV), DeepSub stays within 1-5% while ICS underestimates by a factor of 2-3
- Jet girth: DeepSub overpredicts by 1-5% at girth values less than 0.075, while ICS overshoots by 80-100%; beyond girth 0.12, DeepSub holds within 5-10% while ICS falls to 10% of truth
- Energy correlation function: DeepSub remains within 1-5% of truth across most of the range, while ICS shows deviations up to an order of magnitude
- Model has 11.5M parameters and uses about 5 GFLOPs on forward pass
- DeepSub processes 80k events in 3 minutes on an Nvidia RTX A6000 GPU, versus 20 minutes for ICS on a 32-core Intel Xeon

## Context

This work extends machine learning applications in heavy-ion physics beyond existing approaches that reconstruct only one-dimensional jet transverse momentum (PhysRevC.99.064904, bossi2022rdependenceinclusivejetsuppression, Mengel:2024fcl, li2024jetmomentumreconstructionqgp). It contrasts with traditional methods like the area-based method (Cacciari 2012) and iterative constituent subtraction (Berta 2014, Berta 2019), which the authors show systematically oversubtract or undersubtract jet substructure. The approach enables precision measurements of jet substructure observables in kinematic regimes previously inaccessible due to large background subtraction uncertainties, which are often an O(1) fraction of total systematic uncertainty in heavy-ion jet analyses.
