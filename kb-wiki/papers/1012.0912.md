# Compact binary mergers: an astrophysical perspective

> This paper reviews the current understanding of double neutron star and neutron star black hole binaries. It addresses mainly (nuclear) astrophysics aspects of compact binary mergers and thus complements recent reviews that have emphasized the numerical relativity viewpoint. In particular, the paper discusses different channels to release neutron-rich matter into the host galaxy, connections between compact binary mergers and short Gamma-ray bursts and accompanying electromagnetic signals.

## Key claim

This review argues that compact binary mergers (double neutron star and neutron star-black hole systems) are the most likely central engine for short gamma-ray bursts and a promising site for r-process nucleosynthesis, but acknowledges that the case is "far from being settled" and that current models cannot yet go "the full way from the central engine to the production of the detectable emission."

## Method

The paper reviews the current understanding of compact binary mergers from a nuclear astrophysics perspective, complementing recent reviews focused on numerical relativity. It synthesizes results from hydrodynamic simulations (Newtonian, post-Newtonian, conformal flatness approximation, and full general relativity), neutrino transport calculations, and population synthesis models to discuss three channels for neutron-rich matter ejection: dynamical tidal ejection, neutrino-driven winds from merger remnants, and viscous/nuclear disintegration of accretion disks. The review also examines observational evidence connecting mergers to short gamma-ray bursts, including host galaxy properties, redshift distributions, and afterglow detections from the SWIFT mission.

## Key numbers

- Merger rates for double neutron star systems: approximately 40 to 700 Myr$^{-1}$ in a Milky Way equivalent galaxy, with an uncertainty of about an order of magnitude in each direction
- Dynamically ejected masses from double neutron star mergers: between $4 \times 10^{-3}$ and $4 \times 10^{-2}$ M$_\odot$, with the most common case (1.38 and 1.42 M$_\odot$ with negligible spins) ejecting about $3 \times 10^{-3}$ M$_\odot$
- Neutrino-driven wind mass loss rate: $\langle \dot{M} \rangle \approx 10^{-3}$ M$_\odot$ s$^{-1}$, releasing approximately $10^{-4}$ M$_\odot$ per merger with electron fraction $Y_e \approx 0.1 - 0.2$
- Viscously/nuclearly disintegrated accretion disks: approximately $3 \times 10^{-2}$ M$_\odot$ released with $Y_e$ from 0.1 to 0.5 (peak around 0.3) and entropies from 3 to 20 $k_{\rm B}$ per baryon
- Neutron star compactness: $\zeta \equiv G M_{\rm ns}/R_{\rm ns} c^2 \approx 0.2$ (compared to $\sim 10^{-6}$ for the Sun)
- Measured masses of PSR 1913+16: $m_1 = 1.4414$ M$_\odot$ and $m_2 = 1.3867$ M$_\odot \pm 0.0002$ M$_\odot$
- Neutron star mass in J1614-2230: $M_{\rm ns} = 1.97 \pm 0.04$ M$_\odot$, placing the threshold for metastable central objects at least 2.66 M$_\odot$
- Short gamma-ray burst isotropic energy: approximately $10^{51}$ erg, about two orders of magnitude lower than for long bursts
- Short gamma-ray burst jet opening half-angles: $\theta_j \sim 15^\circ$ (compared to $\sim 5^\circ$ for long bursts)
- Short gamma-ray burst redshifts: between 0.2 and 2, with a mean of 0.4
- Median projected offset of short gamma-ray bursts from host galaxy centers: approximately 5 kpc

## Context

This review extends the work of Faber (2009) and Duez (2010), which emphasized numerical relativity, by focusing on nuclear astrophysics aspects. It builds on the foundational proposal by Lattimer and Schramm (1974) that neutron star-black hole mergers could produce r-process material, and the subsequent work by Eichler and collaborators (1989) connecting mergers to gamma-ray bursts. The paper addresses open questions including whether merger rates and ejecta masses overproduce r-process material relative to Galactic observations, and whether current models can self-consistently produce the ultra-relativistic outflows required for gamma-ray bursts. The review enables future work on self-consistent 3D neutrino-radiation-hydrodynamics simulations that include both cooling and heating processes, and on connecting central engine physics to observable electromagnetic signals across the full range of spatial scales.
