# Experimental evidence of the effect of nuclear shells on fission dissipation and time

> Nuclear fission is still one of the most complex physical processes we can observe in nature due to the interplay of macroscopic and microscopic nuclear properties that decide the result. An example of this coupling is the presence of nuclear dissipation as an important ingredient that contributes to drive the dynamics and has a clear impact on the time of the process. However, different theoretical interpretations, and scarce experimental data make it poorly understood. In this letter, we present the first experimental determination of the dissipation energy in fission as a function of the fragment split, for three different fissioning systems. The amount of dissipation was obtained through the measurement of the relative production of fragments with even and odd atomic numbers with respect to different initial fission energies. The results reveal a clear effect of particular nuclear shells on the dissipation and fission dynamics. In addition, the relative production of fragments with even and odd atomic numbers appears as a potential contributor to the long-standing problem of the time scale in fission.

# Experimental evidence of the effect of nuclear shells on fission dissipation and time

## Key claim

The paper presents the first experimental determination of dissipation energy in fission as a function of fragment split for three fissioning systems, revealing that nuclear shell structure (particularly spherical Z=50 and octupole-deformed Z=52) directly influences dissipation and fission dynamics. The results are conditional on a phenomenological model that extracts dissipation energy from the local even-odd effect in fragment yields, with the interpretation that the correlation between dissipation and the sensitivity parameter G breaks down beyond a threshold asymmetry where proton transfer times become comparable to scission times.

## Method

The local even-odd effect δ_Z (the relative difference between yields of even-Z and odd-Z fragments as a function of fragment atomic number) was measured for three fissioning systems (²³⁸U, ²³⁹Np, ²⁴⁰Pu) at different excitation energies using transfer-induced fission reactions in inverse kinematics with the VAMOS++ spectrometer at GANIL. The dissipation energy E^dis(Z) was extracted by fitting the measured evolution of δ_Z with excitation energy above the fission barrier E^Bf to a phenomenological generalization of the relation E^int ≈ -4 ln(δ), where the intrinsic energy E^int = E^Bf + E^dis and a pairing gap Δ is subtracted for energies above the pair-breaking threshold.

## Key numbers

- Dissipation energy E^dis reaches a peak beyond 10 MeV near the octupole-deformed shell Z=52 in ²³⁸U and ²⁴⁰Pu
- E^dis drops to a minimum of approximately 2 MeV around the spherical closed shell Z=50
- Average E^dis values are 5.4 MeV for ²³⁸U and 6.4 MeV for ²⁴⁰Pu, compared to previous constant-fraction estimates of approximately 7 MeV and 9 MeV respectively
- The sensitivity parameter G averages approximately -3 MeV for ²³⁸U and -3.4 MeV for ²⁴⁰Pu, compared to the reference value G = -4 MeV from earlier work (Gon91)
- For very asymmetric splits beyond Z=58 in ²⁴⁰Pu, the magnitude of G increases by nearly a factor of 10
- The threshold asymmetry for loss of sensitivity in δ_Z occurs around Z=60 for ²⁴⁰Pu

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the global even-odd effect in fission (PomNPA, PerNPA) and the local even-odd effect (Tracy, GonNIM, OlmEPJA15) by providing the first systematic measurement of δ_Z as a function of excitation energy. The results contradict the assumption of a constant dissipation fraction independent of fragment shell structure used in models by RejNPA00, AsgJPC, JurJPG15, and SchGEF, and provide a larger value than the 11.3 MeV calculation for ²⁴⁰Pu by MolPRC. The identification of threshold asymmetries where δ_Z loses sensitivity to intrinsic energy offers a potential observable for comparing fission timescales across different systems, addressing the long-standing problem of fission time scales discussed by HilAPF92 and PauARNPS94.
