# The 2025 Evaluation of Experimental Thermonuclear Reaction Rates (ETR25)

> This work describes the formalism for estimating thermonuclear reaction rates for astrophysical applications, emphasizing modern statistical approaches such as Monte-Carlo sampling and Bayesian models. We discuss related topics including the calculation of resonance energies from nuclear Q values, indirect estimates of particle partial widths, and matching of reaction rates at elevated temperatures to statistical-model results. We have evaluated available experimental data on cross sections, resonance energies and strengths, partial widths, life-times, spin-parities, and spectroscopic factors. Based on these results, we have estimated numerical values of 78 experimental charged-particle thermonuclear reaction rates for target nuclei in the A = 2 to 40 mass region, for temperatures ranging from 1 MK to 10 GK. For each reaction, three rate values are provided: low, median, and high, corresponding to the 16th, 50th, and 84th percentiles, respectively, of the cumulative reaction rate probability density distribution. Additionally, we present the factor uncertainty of each rate at each temperature grid point. These results enable users to sample the reaction rate probability density in nucleosynthesis calculations, facilitating uncertainty estimates of nuclidic abundances. The rates presented here refer to their laboratory values. For use in stellar model simulations, these values need to be corrected for the effects of thermal excitations of the interacting nuclei. For each reaction, we include graphs that illustrate the fractional contributions to the overall reaction rate along with the associated uncertainty. These visuals are designed to assist both stellar modelers and nuclear experimentalists by identifying the primary sources of rate uncertainty at specific stellar temperatures. A graphical comparison with earlier Monte-Carlo rates is also provided.

## Key claim

This work provides statistically meaningful estimates of 78 experimental charged-particle thermonuclear reaction rates for target nuclei in the A = 2 to 40 mass region, using Monte-Carlo sampling and Bayesian methods, but the rates are presented for bare nuclei in the laboratory and must be corrected for thermal excitations of the interacting nuclei before use in stellar model simulations.

## Method

The authors evaluated available experimental data on cross sections, resonance energies and strengths, partial widths, lifetimes, spin-parities, and spectroscopic factors for proton- and alpha-induced reactions. They computed thermonuclear reaction rates using Monte-Carlo sampling methods and Bayesian techniques, employing the Breit-Wigner formula for isolated resonances (with the Thomas approximation for broad resonances) and potential model calculations for nonresonant direct capture contributions. For each reaction, three rate values were derived from the cumulative reaction rate probability density distribution at each temperature grid point.

## Key numbers

- 78 experimental charged-particle thermonuclear reaction rates were evaluated for target nuclei in the A = 2 to 40 mass region
- Temperature range covered: 1 MK to 10 GK
- For each reaction, three rate values are provided: low (16th percentile), median (50th percentile), and high (84th percentile) of the cumulative reaction rate probability density distribution
- The Thomas approximation for broad resonances introduces less than 10% error on the reaction rate for a single isolated resonance
- Nuclear Q-values are reported for each reaction, e.g., D(p,γ)³He: 5493.428 keV; ³He(α,γ)⁷Be: 1586.892 ± 0.070 keV; ¹⁴N(α,γ)¹⁸F: 4414.05 ± 0.46 keV

## Context

This work extends the 2010 evaluation of Monte-Carlo-based experimental reaction rates (ETR10) by Iliadis et al. (2010) and updates earlier evaluations by Fowler (1967), Caughlan and Fowler (1988), the NACRE collaboration, and others. The use of Monte-Carlo sampling (Longland 2010) and Bayesian techniques (Iliadis 2016, Odell 2022) provides a major advantage over earlier results by quantifying recommended values and associated uncertainties in terms of probability densities. The results enable users to sample reaction rate probability densities in nucleosynthesis calculations, facilitating uncertainty estimates of nuclidic abundances, and the fractional contribution graphs identify primary sources of rate uncertainty at specific stellar temperatures to guide both modelers and experimentalists.
