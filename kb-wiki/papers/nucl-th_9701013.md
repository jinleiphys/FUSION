# Pion Suppression in Nuclear Collisions

> The pion multiplicity per participating nucleon in central nucleus-nucleus collisions at the energies 2-15 A GeV is significantly smaller than in nucleon-nucleon interactions at the same collision energy. This effect of pion suppression is argued to appear due to the evolution of the system produced at the early stage of heavy-ion collisions towards a local thermodynamic equilibrium and further isentropic expansion.

## Key claim

The observed suppression of pion production per participating nucleon in central nucleus-nucleus collisions at 2–15 A GeV, relative to nucleon-nucleon collisions, is argued to arise from the evolution of the initially nonequilibrium hadronic matter toward local thermodynamic equilibrium and subsequent isentropic expansion, rather than from secondary pion production as one might intuitively expect.

## Method

The authors model the early stage of a central A+A collision as a superposition of N+N interactions, producing a hadronic system with an excess of delta resonances over their chemical equilibrium value. They then treat the system's evolution toward chemical equilibrium at constant volume, using ideal-gas thermodynamics with van der Waals corrections, and separately analyze the effect of isentropic hydrodynamic expansion on pion multiplicity. The pion suppression factor is computed as the difference between the final pion-to-participant ratio in A+A collisions and that in N+N collisions, parameterized by baryon density, temperature, and a delta-surplus parameter.

## Key numbers

- At 2.1 A·GeV/c, the pion yield per nucleon in central A+A collisions is smaller than in N+N interactions by a factor of about 3.
- The experimentally measured mean pion suppression factor (difference in pion multiplicity per participating nucleon between A+A and N+N) is approximately −0.35, independent of collision energy up to BNL AGS energies.
- The calculated suppression factor from chemical equilibration alone ranges from −0.2 to −0.4 across the physically reasonable domain of temperature (50–150 MeV) and baryon density (2–5 times normal nuclear density ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³).
- The suppression from isentropic expansion reaches about −0.4 at initial temperature T=150 MeV and freeze-out density ratio ρ_B^f/ρ_B = 0.1.
- The combined suppression from both mechanisms varies between −0.2 and −0.7; for gold nuclei at 11.6 A·GeV/c, the observed suppression equals about −0.6.

## Context

This work extends the experimental findings of Rohrich (1995) and Gazdzicki (1995), who first reported pion suppression at BNL AGS energies and below, by providing a thermodynamic explanation based on chemical equilibration of delta resonances and isentropic expansion. The model reproduces the approximate independence of the suppression factor on participant number and collision energy observed in those data. The authors note that at CERN SPS energies (160–200 A·GeV), a pion enhancement is observed instead, which cannot be explained by this model and may require new mechanisms such as quark-gluon plasma formation (Gazdzicki, 1995) or other processes (Miklos, 1996).
