# Unearthing radial independence for prediction of alpha-decay half-lives

> Nuclear radial distance is a prerequisite for generating any alpha-decay half-life formula by taking a suitable effective potential. We study the emission process of alpha particles from an isolated quasi-bound state generated by an effective potential to a scattering state. The effective potential is expressed in terms of Frahn form of potential which is exactly solvable and an analytical expression for half-life is obtained in terms of Coulomb function, wave function and the potential. We then derive a closed form expression for the decay half-life in terms of the parameters of the potential, Q-value of the system, mass and proton numbers of the nuclei valid for alpha-decay as well as proton-decay. From the nature of variations of half-life as a function of radial distance, we trace the radial independence region where decay time is almost constant. Finally by overviewing our results and picking that particular radial distance we predict the half-lives of a series of nuclei by using the closed form expression.

# Unearthing radial independence for prediction of alpha-decay half-lives

## Key claim
The authors derive a closed-form expression for alpha-decay and proton-decay half-lives using a Frahn form of potential, and identify a radial independence region where the calculated half-life is nearly constant with respect to the chosen radial distance, allowing them to fix a specific radius for predictions without requiring detailed knowledge of the nuclear interior.

## Method
The authors model alpha emission as a transition from a quasi-bound state to a scattering state using an effective potential expressed in the Frahn form, which is exactly solvable for S-wave. They solve the Schrodinger equation analytically using hypergeometric functions, compute the decay width via the Gell-Mann-Goldberger transformation, and derive a closed-form logarithmic expression for the half-life in terms of the Q-value, mass numbers, proton numbers, and potential parameters. They then vary the radial distance parameter to locate the region where the calculated half-life remains approximately constant, and use that fixed radius to predict half-lives for a series of nuclei.

## Key numbers
- For alpha-decay, the closed-form expression uses parameters: a = 0.9889, d = -45.2631, and a constant factor c_f = 0.19 with r_0 = 0.98 fm for l=0.
- For proton-decay, the constant factor is c_f = 2.8 with r_0 = 0.97 fm for l=0.
- The barrier radius R_0 is given by R_0 = r_0 (A_alpha^{1/3} + A_D^{1/3}) + 2.72 fm, with r_0 = 0.97 fm.
- The Coulomb radius parameter is R_C = r_c (A_alpha^{1/3} + A_D^{1/3}) with r_c = 1.2 fm.
- For the alpha+Pb-214 system (Q=6.115 MeV), the barrier radius R_0 = 10.0616 fm and the radial independence region R_w extends from 9.6 to 15.1 fm.
- The power series Coulomb wave function requires a multiplication factor x_m = 70 for alpha-decay and x_m = 5.9 for proton decay.
- The paper presents predicted half-lives for 144 even-even nuclei and 112 even-odd nuclei, with experimental comparisons; for example, Po-212 (Q=8.954 MeV) has log10(T_1/2^expt) = -6.524 s and log10(T_1/2^pred) = -6.173 s.

## Context
This work extends the authors' earlier Sahu16 formula (Sahu, 2016) by introducing a Frahn form of potential markedly different from the potential used previously, and demonstrates its applicability to both alpha-decay and proton-decay. The closed-form expression is similar in structure to the Viola-Seaborg-Sobiczewski formula (Viola, 1966; Patyk, 1989; Pomorski, 2001) but with well-defined parameters rather than fitted coefficients. The radial independence finding enables predictions without requiring detailed knowledge of the nuclear interior wave function, and the results are compared against experimental data from the compilation of Shan (2015).
