# Importance of strange sea to the charge radii and quadrupole moment of $ J^P=\frac{1}{2}^+,\frac{3}{2}^+$ baryons

> A statistical framework in conjugation with the principle of detailed balance is employed to examine the low-energy properties i.e. charge radii and quadrupole moment of J$^P$=$\frac{1}{2}^+$ octet and J$^P$=$\frac{3}{2}^+$ decuplet baryon. The statistical approach assumes the expansion of baryonic system in terms of quark-gluon Fock states. We systematically apply operator formalism along with the statistical approach to study the charge radii and quadrupole moment of baryons. Based on the probabilities of all possible Fock states in spin, flavor and color space, the importance of sea with quarks and gluons is studied. The individual contribution of the constituent quarks and sea which contains terms of scalar, vector and tensor is explored. Due to large mass difference between strange and non-strange content, the SU(3) breaking effect are also investigated. The extent to which strange quark-antiquark pair is considered in sea is constrained by the mass of hadrons, the free energy of gluons in conformity with experimental indications. We focus on the individual contribution of strange and non-strange sea (g, $\langle u\bar u\rangle$, $\langle d\bar d\rangle$ and $\langle s\bar s\rangle$) accomodability in the respective hadrons for their charge radii and quadrupole moment. The present work has been compared with various theoretical approaches and some known experimental observations. Our computed results may provide important information for upcoming experimental findings.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the charge radii and quadrupole moments of octet and decuplet baryons can be successfully described by a statistical model that includes strange and non-strange sea components (quark-antiquark pairs and gluons), but only when the extent of strange quark-antiquark pairs in the sea is constrained by hadron masses and gluon free energy in conformity with experimental indications, and when SU(3) symmetry-breaking effects due to the strange quark mass are incorporated via a mass-dependent parameter.

## Method

The authors employ a statistical framework combined with the principle of detailed balance to expand baryonic states as ensembles of quark-gluon Fock states in spin, flavor, and color space. They apply operator formalism to compute matrix elements of charge radii and quadrupole moment operators for both J^P = 1/2^+ octet and J^P = 3/2^+ decuplet baryons, using statistical coefficients that encode the probabilities of scalar, vector, and tensor sea components. SU(3) symmetry-breaking effects are introduced through a mass-dependent parameter r = m_u/m_s that accounts for the strange quark mass difference.

## Key numbers

- Proton charge radius: r_p = 0.8409 ± 0.0004 fm (experimental value from PDG)
- Neutron charge radius squared: r^2_n = -0.115 ± 0.0017 fm^2 (experimental value from PDG)
- Sigma-minus charge radius: Σ^- = 0.78 ± 0.10 fm (experimental value from PDG)
- Ratio of strange to non-strange quark momentum fraction: 2(s+ s̄)/(u+ ū+ d+ d̄) = 0.477 ± 0.063 ± 0.053 (from NuTeV collaboration)
- Electric quadrupole to magnetic dipole amplitude ratio for N→Δ transition: E2/M1 = -0.025 ± 0.005 (experimental value)

## Context

This work extends the statistical model approach previously applied by the authors and others to baryon masses, spin distributions, and magnetic moments (Bhall and Upadhyay, 2023) by now computing charge radii and quadrupole moments. It builds upon theoretical frameworks including the chiral constituent quark model of Sharma and Dahiya (2013, 2014), the general QCD parametrization method of Buchmann and Henley (2001), and lattice QCD calculations of decuplet charge radii. The results are compared with experimental data from PDG and various theoretical approaches (non-relativistic quark model, relativistic quark model, light-front holographic method, QCD sum rules, 1/N_c expansion), and may provide predictions for upcoming experiments at FAIR (PANDA-GSI), BESIII, and J-PARC.
