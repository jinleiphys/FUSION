# A study of the almost sequential mechanism of true ternary fission

> We consider the collinear ternary fission which is a sequential ternary decay with a very short time between the ruptures of two necks connecting the middle cluster of the ternary nuclear system and outer fragments. In particular, we consider the case where the Coulomb field of the first massive fragment separated during the first step of the fission produces a lower pre-scission barrier in the second step of the residual part of the ternary system. In this case, we obtain a probability of about $10^{-3}$ for the yield of massive clusters such as \nuclide[70]{Ni}, \nuclide[80-82]{Ge}, \nuclide[86]{Se}, and \nuclide[94]{Kr} in the ternary fission of \nuclide[252]{Cf}. These products appear together with the clusters having mass numbers of $A = 132$--$140$. The results show that the yield of a heavy cluster such as \nuclide[68-70]{Ni} would be followed by a product of $A = 138$--$148$ with a large probability as observed in the experimental data obtained with the FOBOS spectrometer at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research. The third product is not observed. The landscape of the potential energy surface shows that the configuration of the Ni + Ca + Sn decay channel is lower about 12 MeV than that of the Ca + Ni + Sn channel. This leads to the fact, that the yield of Ni and Sn is large. The analysis on the dependence of the velocity of the middle fragment on mass numbers of the outer products leads to the conclusion that, in the collinear tripartition channel of \nuclide[252]{Cf}, the middle cluster has a very small velocity, which does not allow it to be found in experiments.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that collinear ternary fission proceeds via an "almost sequential" mechanism where the two necks rupture with a very short time delay, and the Coulomb field of the first separated massive fragment lowers the pre-scission barrier for the second rupture. The authors obtain a probability of about 10^{-3} for the yield of massive clusters such as Ni, Ge, Se, and Kr in the ternary fission of Cf-252, but note that the predicted mass range of the accompanying product (A = 132-140) differs from the experimentally observed range (A = 138-148), which they attribute to using ground-state binding energies rather than deformation-dependent values.

## Method

The authors model the collinear tri-nuclear system (TNS) using the dinuclear system (DNS) framework, calculating the total potential energy as the sum of the reaction balance energy Q_ggg and the nucleus-nucleus interaction potentials between neighboring fragments. The potential energy surface (PES) is computed by varying the charge and mass numbers of two fragments and the distances between fragment centers, with the nuclear interaction obtained from a double folding procedure and the Coulomb part from the Wong expression. The yield of ternary fission products is then calculated from the driving potential using a statistical equilibrium probability multiplied by decay probabilities that depend on the pre-scission barriers between neighboring fragments.

## Key numbers

- The probability for the yield of massive clusters (Ni-70, Ge-80-82, Se-86, Kr-94) in the ternary fission of Cf-252 is about 10^{-3}
- The PES value of the Ni + Ca + Sn configuration is about 12 MeV lower than that of the Ca + Ni + Sn configuration
- The presence of the third massive fragment Sn makes the pre-scission barrier shallower by 4 MeV, increasing the decay probability of the Ni + Ca system
- The minimum of the nucleus-nucleus interaction occurs at R_{13} = 11 fm and R_{32} = 12 fm for the Ni + Ca + Sn configuration
- The middle fragment has a small velocity (|v_3| < 0.25 cm/ns) for the case of A_1 = 60-80 and A_3 = 24-64, corresponding to A_2 = 108-168
- For symmetric masses (A_1 ~ A_2 ~ A_3), the middle fragment velocity is v_3 = 0.3-0.4 cm/ns

## Context

This work extends the sequential ternary fission model of Tashkhodjaev et al. (2011) by introducing the "almost sequential" mechanism where the Coulomb field of the first separated fragment lowers the second pre-scission barrier, explaining the observed yields of heavy clusters like Ni, Ge, and Se isotopes in coincidence with massive products of A = 138-148 from FOBOS spectrometer experiments at JINR (Dubna). The results contradict earlier assumptions that the third product should be easily observable, showing instead that its very small velocity (typically below 0.25 cm/ns) explains why it is not detected in experiments. The analysis enables future studies to incorporate deformation-dependent binding energies to resolve the remaining discrepancy between the predicted mass range (A = 132-140) and the experimentally observed range (A = 138-148) for the accompanying massive product.
