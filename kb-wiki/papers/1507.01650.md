# Thermal Resonance Fusion

> We first show a possible mechanism to create a new type of nuclear fusion, thermal resonance fusion, i.e. low energy nuclear fusion with thermal resonance of light nuclei or atoms, such as deuterium or tritium. The fusion of two light nuclei has to overcome the Coulomb barrier between these two nuclei to reach up to the interacting region of nuclear force. We found nuclear fusion could be realized with thermal vibrations of crystal lattice atoms coupling with light atoms at low energy by resonance to overcome this Coulomb barrier. Thermal resonances combining with tunnel effects can greatly enhance the probability of the deuterium fusion to the detectable level. Our low energy nuclear fusion mechanism research - thermal resonance fusion mechanism results demonstrate how these light nuclei or atoms, such as deuterium, can be fused in the crystal of metal, such as Ni or alloy, with synthetic thermal vibrations and resonances at different modes and energies experimentally. The probability of tunnel effect at different resonance energy given by the WKB method is shown that indicates the thermal resonance fusion mode, especially combined with the tunnel effect, is possible and feasible. But the penetrating probability decreases very sharply when the input resonance energy decreases less than 3 keV, so for thermal resonance fusion, the key point is to increase the resonance peak or make the resonance sharp enough to the acceptable energy level by the suitable compound catalysts, and it is better to reach up more than 3 keV to make the penetrating probability larger than 10^{-10}.

# Thermal Resonance Fusion

## Key claim
The paper proposes that low-energy nuclear fusion of light nuclei (such as deuterium) could be realized through thermal vibrations of crystal lattice atoms coupling with absorbed light atoms via resonance, potentially overcoming the Coulomb barrier. However, the penetrating probability decreases very sharply when the input resonance energy falls below 3 keV, so the mechanism requires resonance peaks reaching above 3 keV to achieve a penetrating probability larger than 10^{-10}.

## Method
The author models thermal vibrations of crystal lattice atoms and absorbed deuterons as coupled simple harmonic oscillators, using classical vibration theory with damping parameters and quality factors (Q). The WKB method is applied to calculate the tunnel-effect penetrating probability for two deuterons at various resonance energies and internuclear distances (2, 3, 5, and 10 fm). The analysis considers how different metals or alloys (such as Ni, Pd, or Pt) could serve as catalysts to produce sharp resonance peaks with high Q values.

## Key numbers
- For a coupling system with quality factor Q = 500 and parameter α = 0.1, the maximum resonance energy E_m is 25 keV, and the maximum amplitude ratio A/A_0 can reach 10^3 with ζ = 0.0005.
- At 25 keV resonance energy, the tunnel-effect penetrating probabilities are 0.0011 (2 fm interval), 0.0017 (3 fm), 0.0036 (5 fm), and 0.0132 (10 fm).
- For 1 g of D-gas absorbed by Ni to release 1 J of heat via D + D → T + p (4.04 MeV per fusion), approximately 3 × 10^12 deuteron fusions are needed, corresponding to a required fusion probability of 2 × 10^{-11} per deuteron pair.
- The penetrating probability drops below 10^{-10} when the input resonance energy decreases below 3 keV.
- The binding energy of deuterium is 2.2246 MeV, giving a nearest nucleon-nucleon distance of 1.526 fm under the uncertainty principle; at 10 fm distance the binding energy needed for two neutrons decreases to 52 keV.

## Context
This work proposes a new mechanism to explain anomalous heat production observed in nickel-catalyzed deuterium gas experiments (reported in CF14), which could not be adequately explained by conventional thermonuclear fusion or quantum tunnel-effect fusion alone. The paper extends concepts from solid-state physics (crystal lattice vibrations, phonon modes) and vibration mechanics (Fahy 1998) into nuclear fusion research. If confirmed experimentally, thermal resonance fusion could open a new research field between nuclear physics and atomic physics, potentially providing a clean and safe energy source. The author notes that experimental detection of characteristic neutrons, protons, or photons from deuteron fusion would be needed to confirm the mechanism.
