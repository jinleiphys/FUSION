# Semiclassical description of a sixth order quadrupole boson Hamiltonian

> A sixth order quadrupole boson Hamiltonian is treated through a time dependent variational principle approach choosing as trial function a coherent state with respect to zeroth $b^{\dagger}_0$ and second $b^{\dagger}_2+b^{\dagger}_{-2}$ components of the quadrupole bosons. The coefficients involved in the model Hamiltonian are chosen so that the classical effective potential energy term has two distinct minima. The equation of motion for the radial coordinate is analytically solved and the resulting trajectories are extensively studied. One distinguishes three energy regions exhibiting different types of trajectories. When one passes from the region characterized by two wells to the region of energies higher than the maximum value of the effective potential the trajectories period exhibits a singularity which reflects a phase transition. The classical trajectories are quantized by a constraint similar to the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition. The semiclassical spectra corresponding to the two potential wells have specific properties. The tunneling process through the potential barrier is also studied. The transmission coefficients exhibit jumps in magnitude when the angular momentum acquires certain values.

# Semiclassical description of a sixth order quadrupole boson Hamiltonian

## Key claim

The authors show that a sixth-order quadrupole boson Hamiltonian, treated semiclassically via a time-dependent variational principle with coherent states, yields an effective potential with two distinct minima (a normal and a superdeformed well) for angular momentum L < 59, and that the period of radial trajectories diverges at the energy equal to the maximum of the effective potential, signaling a phase transition. The transmission coefficients for tunneling through the potential barrier exhibit discontinuous jumps in magnitude when the angular momentum takes certain specific values (e.g., from L=10 to 11, 23 to 24, 29 to 30), which the authors suggest could enhance alpha or heavy cluster emission rates.

## Method

The authors treat a sixth-order quadrupole boson Hamiltonian using a time-dependent variational principle, choosing as trial function a coherent state with respect to the zeroth and second components of the quadrupole bosons. The classical energy function obtained has no momentum powers higher than two, and the resulting classical system has two degrees of freedom (radial coordinate r and angular coordinate theta) with two constants of motion (energy and the third component of angular momentum). The equation of motion for the radial coordinate is solved analytically, and the classical trajectories are quantized using a Bohr-Sommerfeld-like quantization condition on the action integral, with the angular momentum quantized as L3 = 2L hbar for L = 0, 1, 2, ...

## Key numbers

- The model parameters used for numerical applications are: A = 3 MeV, D = -0.4 MeV, F = 0.01 MeV, A' = 0.0025 MeV
- For L < 59, the effective potential V_eff(L;r) exhibits a two-minima shape; at the critical value L = 59, the minimum of the left well merges with the maximum, creating an inflection point
- For L ≤ 10, the number of states with energy less than V_eff_max in the right well is 48; for the next 12 angular momenta (L = 11 through 22), the number is 49
- The transmission coefficient D(E,L) shows large jumps when L changes from 10 to 11, 23 to 24, 29 to 30, 35 to 36, 40 to 41, 44 to 45, 47 to 48, 50 to 51, and 53 to 54
- The period of radial motion diverges as the energy approaches V_eff_max from either above or below, with the hypergeometric function having a simple pole at k^2 = 1

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of fourth-order boson Hamiltonians (Raduta, 2002) by adding a sixth-order term, which introduces a second (superdeformed) minimum in the effective potential that was absent in the earlier work. The formalism builds on the coherent state model (CSM) developed by Raduta and collaborators, and the classical treatment parallels approaches used in the liquid drop model and the variable moment of inertia formula of Mariscotti (1969) and Holmberg (1970). The results enable a semiclassical description of both normal and superdeformed rotational bands, and suggest that the observed jumps in transmission coefficients could have implications for alpha decay and heavy cluster emission rates, though experimental verification is not provided.
