# Bound state formation within the Lindblad approach

> The Lindblad master equation is a frequently used Markovian approach to describe open quantum systems in terms of the temporal evolution of a reduced density matrix. Here, the thermal environment is traced out to obtain an expression to describe the evolution of what is called a system: one particle or a chain of interacting particles, which is/are surrounded by a thermal heat bath. In this work, we investigate the formation of non-relativistic bound states, involving the Pöschl-Teller potential, in order to discuss the formation time and the thermal equilibrium, applying scales from nuclear physics. This problem is borrowed from the field of heavy-ion collisions, where the deuteron is a probe which is measured at temperature regimes around the chemical freeze out temperature, while the deuteron itself has a binding energy which is much lower. This is known and often described as a ``snowball in hell". We use a reformulated Lindblad equation, in terms of a diffusion-advection equation with sources and therefore provide a hydrodynamical formulation of a dissipative quantum master equation.

## Key claim

The authors reformulate the Lindblad master equation as a conservative advection-diffusion equation with sources, providing a hydrodynamical formulation of a dissipative quantum master equation, and demonstrate that bound states (modeled on the deuteron) do thermalize to a Boltzmann distribution, but the formation time of individual states differs from the full thermalization time of the system.

## Method

The Lindblad equation is rewritten in coordinate-space representation as a system of advection-diffusion equations for the real and imaginary parts of the reduced density matrix, which is then solved numerically using a finite-volume Kurganov-Tadmor scheme. The system is a single non-relativistic particle of mass 470 MeV (the reduced deuteron mass) in a Pöschl-Teller potential with depth and range set to deuteron parameters, coupled to an Ohmic heat bath. The density matrix is evolved on a 40 fm x 40 fm grid with 300 x 300 cells, and the eigenstates of the potential (up to N=50, covering energies from -2.3 MeV to 640 MeV) are used to project the time-dependent density matrix onto state populations.

## Key numbers

* Deuteron binding energy is approximately 2.3 MeV, while chemical freeze-out temperatures in heavy-ion collisions are 60-150 MeV.
* The reduced deuteron mass is set to m = 470 MeV.
* The computational domain is 40 fm x 40 fm, discretized into 300 x 300 cells.
* The Hilbert space is truncated at N=50, giving an energy spectrum from -2.3 MeV to 640 MeV.
* For a bath temperature T=250 MeV, damping gamma=0.1 MeV, and cutoff Omega=4T, the real part of the density matrix is shown evolving from t=0 fm/c to t=8 fm/c.
* Equilibrium distributions are shown at t_eq = 100 fm/c for bath temperatures T = 100, 150, 200, 250, 300 MeV.
* Typical deuteron yields at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV Pb-Pb collisions with 0-10% centrality are dN/dy approximately 10^{-1}.

## Context

This work extends the application of Lindblad dynamics, previously successful for heavy quarkonia (Brambilla 2016, Blaizot 2017, Andronic 2024), to the deuteron bound state problem in heavy-ion collisions, where the deuteron's binding energy (2.3 MeV) is orders of magnitude below the hadronic freeze-out temperature (60-150 MeV), a puzzle known as the "snowball in hell" paradox. The authors compare their results to the pure Caldeira-Leggett master equation and find that both formulations lead to thermal states with similar results. This enables a quantum-mechanical explanation of deuteron formation in heavy-ion collisions via open quantum systems, complementing existing coalescence and interference approaches (Liu 2019, Hillmann 2021, Kittiratpattana 2021).
