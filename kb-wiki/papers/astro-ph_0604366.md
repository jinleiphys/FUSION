# Analysis of White Dwarfs with Strange-Matter Cores

> We summarize masses and radii for a number of white dwarfs as deduced from a combination of proper motion studies, Hipparcos parallax distances, effective temperatures, and binary or spectroscopic masses. A puzzling feature of these data is that some stars appear to have radii which are significantly smaller than that expected for a standard electron-degenerate white-dwarf equations of state. We construct a projection of white-dwarf radii for fixed effective mass and conclude that there is at least marginal evidence for bimodality in the radius distribution forwhite dwarfs. We argue that if such compact white dwarfs exist it is unlikely that they contain an iron core. We propose an alternative of strange-quark matter within the white-dwarf core. We also discuss the impact of the so-called color-flavor locked (CFL) state in strange-matter core associated with color superconductivity. We show that the data exhibit several features consistent with the expected mass-radius relation of strange dwarfs. We identify eight nearby white dwarfs which are possible candidates for strange matter cores and suggest observational tests of this hypothesis.

## Key claim

The paper presents marginal evidence for a bimodal distribution in white dwarf radii, with a subset of stars being significantly more compact than standard electron-degenerate models predict, and proposes that these compact objects could contain strange-quark-matter cores rather than iron cores, though the authors acknowledge the data are too sparse and uncertain to conclusively determine this.

## Method

The authors compiled masses and radii for 22 nearby white dwarfs from Hipparcos parallax distances, proper motion studies, effective temperatures, and binary or spectroscopic masses, using data from Provencal et al. (1998, 2002). They constructed a projection of white-dwarf radii for a fixed effective mass of 0.5 solar masses to remove the mass dependence from the radius distribution, then fit Gaussian distributions to identify potential bimodality. They numerically integrated the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation for general relativistic hydrostatic equilibrium using the MIT bag model equation of state for strange-quark matter and the Hamada and Salpeter (1961) equation of state for normal matter, comparing the resulting mass-radius relations to the observational data via chi-squared analysis.

## Key numbers

- The normal white dwarf peak in the radius distribution is centered at R ≈ 0.014 ± 0.005 solar radii for a fixed mass of 0.5 solar masses
- The more compact peak is centered at R = 0.012 ± 0.010 solar radii for a fixed mass of 0.5 solar masses
- The total chi-squared for all stars associated with a normal carbon white-dwarf equation of state is 119, corresponding to a reduced chi-squared of 5.2 for 23 degrees of freedom
- When eight stars are identified with a strange-matter equation of state instead, the chi-squared is 78 (reduced chi-squared of 3.2), giving a Δχ² = 41 (approximately 6 sigma) preference for the strange-dwarf population
- For comparison, an iron-core population gives chi-squared of 105 (reduced chi-squared of 4.4), a Δχ² = 14 (approximately 4 sigma) improvement
- For strange-dwarf models with bag constant B^(1/4) = 145 MeV, the central density varies from 4.1 to 4.2 × 10^14 g cm^-3
- For models with B^(1/4) = 160 MeV, the central density varies from 6.1 to 6.2 × 10^14 g cm^-3
- Strange dwarfs are in a mass range of 0.3 to 1.35 solar masses
- The strange-matter core radius is approximately 80% that of normal white dwarfs
- The neutron drip density is 4.3 × 10^11 g cm^-3

## Context

This work extends the concept of strange dwarfs proposed by Glendenning et al. (1995a, 1995b), who suggested that white dwarfs could form with strange-quark-matter cores via accretion of strange-matter nuggets during the progenitor main-sequence phase. The study expands on an earlier candidate search by Vartanyan (2004) and addresses the puzzle noted by Provencal et al. (1998) that some white dwarfs appear too compact for standard carbon/oxygen compositions, where an iron core was previously suggested as a possible explanation. The paper identifies eight nearby white dwarf candidates for strange-matter cores and proposes observational tests based on asteroseismology and cooling rates, including mode trapping from the crust-core discontinuity and anomalously rapid cooling in hot DOV stars, which could enable future confirmation or rejection of the strange-dwarf hypothesis.
