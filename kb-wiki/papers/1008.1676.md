# Proton scattering observables from Skyrme-Hatree-Fock densities

> Proton and neutron densities from Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations are used to generate non-local (g-folding) proton-nucleus optical potentials. They are formed by folding the densities with realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions. The potentials are then used to calculate differential cross sections and spin observables for proton scattering. Good agreement with data has been found, supporting those found previously when using SHF charge densities in analyses of electron scattering data. That agreement was improved by use of (shell model) occupation numbers to constrain the HF iterations. That, in part, is also the case with analyses of proton scattering data. The g-folding method is extended to exotic nuclei by including data for neutron-rich sd-shell nuclei from the inverse kinematics of scattering from hydrogen.

## Key claim

Proton and neutron densities from Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations, when folded with realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions in a parameter-free g-folding model, produce proton-nucleus optical potentials that yield good agreement with measured differential cross sections and spin observables for stable and neutron-rich sd-shell nuclei, but the agreement is not uniformly improved by constraining the SHF iterations with shell-model occupation numbers (SHF-SM), for heavier sulfur and argon isotopes the SHF-SM constraint did not improve the level of agreement.

## Method

The authors used Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations with the SKXcsb and SKM* interactions to generate proton and neutron ground-state densities for isotopes of S, Ar, and Ca. These densities were folded with a complex, energy- and density-dependent effective g-matrix (the Melbourne g-folding approach) to produce non-local proton-nucleus optical potentials containing central, two-body spin-orbit, and tensor terms, with no parameter adjustments to fit the scattering data. The resulting potentials were used to calculate differential cross sections and analyzing powers for elastic proton scattering at energies between 28 and 200 MeV, comparing results from three structure models: basic SHF, SHF constrained by shell-model occupation numbers (SHF-SM), and conventional shell-model wave functions.

## Key numbers

- For 65 MeV proton scattering from 28Si, all three models (shell, SHF, SHF-SM) give very little difference in differential cross-section predictions and describe the data well; differences emerge in the analyzing power, with the shell model result doing best.
- For 200 MeV proton scattering from 28Si, both SHF models do better than the shell model in predicting the cross section above 20 degrees.
- For 29.6 MeV proton scattering from 32S, all models reproduce the shape and magnitude of the data without ad hoc renormalizations of potentials or use of phenomenological Mn/Mp ratios.
- For 28 MeV proton scattering from 36S, all models (shell with b=1.9 fm, SHF, SHF-SM) do equally well in describing the data, though agreement above 35 degrees is somewhat poorer.
- For 39 MeV proton scattering from 38S, all models underestimate the data in the region of the minimum between 30 and 40 degrees.
- For 33 MeV proton scattering from 36Ar, all model results agree with data up to 32 degrees but fail to reproduce shape or magnitude beyond that angle.
- For 65 MeV proton scattering from 40Ca, all models describe the differential cross-section data to 80 degrees equally well; the packed shell model provides a better description of the analyzing power.
- For 65 MeV proton scattering from 48Ca, the SHF and SHF-SM models are almost in complete agreement, as the ground state is predominantly a filled 0f7/2 neutron shell.

## Context

This work extends the Melbourne g-folding approach (Amos 2000) to neutron-rich sd-shell nuclei using SHF densities, building on earlier studies that used SHF charge densities to analyze electron scattering data (Richter 2003) and that identified signatures of exotic structures in reaction cross sections (Amos 2006). The results are compared with previous JLM model analyses (Mares 1999, Schiffer 2000) that required renormalizations of the real and imaginary parts of the optical potential, renormalizations that the present parameter-free approach does not allow. The calculations enable predictions for elastic scattering from exotic calcium isotopes (52Ca, 54Ca) where the magic number assignment is debated (Holt 2002), and the authors hope future radioactive beam facilities will provide data to test these predictions.
