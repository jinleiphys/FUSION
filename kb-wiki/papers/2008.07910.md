# Analytical and numerical expressions for the number of atomic configurations contained in a supershell

> We present three explicit formulas for the number of electronic configurations in an atom, i.e. the number of ways to distribute $Q$ electrons in $N$ subshells of respective degeneracies $g_1$, $g_2$, ..., $g_N$. The new expressions are obtained using the generating-function formalism. The first one contains sums involving multinomial coefficients. The second one relies on the idea of gathering subshells having the same degeneracy. A third one also collects subshells with the same degeneracy and leads to the definition of a two-variable generating function, allowing the derivation of recursion relations. Concerning the distribution of population on $N$ distinct subshells of a given degeneracy $g$, analytical expressions for the first moments of this distribution are given. The general case of subshells with any degeneracy is analyzed through the computation of cumulants. A fairly simple expression for the cumulants at any order is provided, as well as the cumulant generating function. Using Gram-Charlier expansion, simple approximations of the analyzed distribution in terms of a normal distribution multiplied by a sum of Hermite polynomials are given. The Edgeworth expansion has also been tested. Its accuracy is equivalent to the Gram-Charlier accuracy when few terms are kept, but it is much more rapidly divergent when the truncation order increases. While this analysis is illustrated by examples in atomic supershells it also applies to more general combinatorial problems such as fermion distributions.

## Key claim

The paper presents three explicit analytical formulas for the number of ways to distribute Q electrons among N subshells with given degeneracies, but the authors note that the formulas containing alternating signs can lead to numerical instability, and the practical utility depends on the specific case and truncation order chosen for the Gram-Charlier or Edgeworth expansions.

## Method

The authors used the generating-function formalism to derive three expressions for the number of electronic configurations: the first involves sums of multinomial coefficients; the second groups subshells by equal degeneracy and yields sums of products of binomial coefficients; the third also groups by degeneracy and defines a two-variable generating function from which recursion relations are obtained. For the case of N subshells with identical degeneracy g, they computed moments and cumulants analytically, then used Gram-Charlier and Edgeworth expansions to approximate the distribution of configurations as a function of electron number Q.

## Key numbers

- The total number of configurations for N subshells of degeneracy g is (g+1)^N (Equation M0).
- The variance of the distribution of configurations for N subshells of equal degeneracy g is sigma^2 = (1/12) g (g+2) N (Equation varS).
- The excess kurtosis for the same distribution is kappa_4 / sigma^4 = -6 (g^2 + 2g + 2) / [5 g (g+2) N].
- For g=1, the distribution reduces to the binomial form: S(1;N;Q) = binomial(N, Q) (Equation Pascal).
- The symmetry relation for N subshells of equal degeneracy g is S(g;N;Q) = S(g;N;gN - Q) (Equation symmetryS).

## Context

This work extends earlier combinatorial approaches to counting atomic configurations, such as the double recursion relations of Gilleron (2004), Wilson (2007), and Pain (2009), and the generating-function methods of Katriel (1983, 1989). The new explicit formulas and recursion relations (including recurrences S2, S3, S4, and recurr_g, which the authors state have not been published previously) enable efficient computation of configuration counts and their statistical moments. The analysis applies beyond atomic physics to general fermion distribution problems and can be used to characterize unresolved transition arrays in plasma spectroscopy, as studied by Bauche and collaborators (2015).
