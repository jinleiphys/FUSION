# Composite-particle decay widths by the generator coordinate method

> We study the feasibility of applying the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) of self-consistent mean-field theory to calculate decay widths of composite particles to composite-particle final states. The main question is how well the GCM can approximate continuum wave functions in the decay channels. The analysis is straightforward under the assumption that the GCM wave functions are separable into internal and Gaussian center-of-mass wave functions. Two methods are examined for calculating decays widths. In one method, the density of final states is computed entirely in the GCM framework. In the other method, it is determined by matching the GCM wave function to an asymptotic scattering wave function. Both methods are applied to a numerical example and are found to agree within their determined uncertainties.

# Composite-particle decay widths by the generator coordinate method

## Key claim

The Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) can provide a viable computational framework for calculating decay widths of composite particles into composite-particle final states, but only under the strong assumption that the GCM wave functions are separable into internal and Gaussian center-of-mass wave functions. The two methods examined for calculating decay widths agree within their determined uncertainties, but the factorizability assumption remains the most critical approximation and may not hold in practice if excited internal states contribute significantly.

## Method

The authors apply Fermi's Golden Rule to compute decay widths, where the initial state is a mean-field configuration of the parent cluster and the final state is represented on a finite basis of GCM configurations with constrained relative coordinates. Two methods are examined for determining the density of final states: Method I computes the density entirely within the GCM framework by taking the energy difference between eigenstates bracketing the initial state energy; Method II matches the GCM wave function to an asymptotic scattering wave function (RGM) at a matching point outside the cluster interaction region. The methods are tested on a numerical example using a linear ramp potential.

## Key numbers

- The recommended mesh spacing for the GCM basis is Δz = 5^(1/2) s, where s is the Gaussian width parameter of the center-of-mass wave function.
- For the ramp potential example with F = 0.4 and 13 configurations on a mesh from -6Δz to 6Δz, the third excited state has energy E = 1.029 (in units of ℏ²/2MΔz²).
- Method I yields Γ_I = (3.8 ± 0.07) v₀² for the decay width, where v₀ is the interaction matrix element.
- Method II with halved mesh spacing yields Γ_II = (3.77 ± 0.06) v₀², showing agreement within uncertainties.
- The zero-point kinetic energy scale is E₀ = ℏ²/(4 M s²), which sets the energy scale for validity of the GCM basis.
- For the free-particle case, the GCM representation has less than 15% error in kinetic energy up to k ≈ π/(2Δz).

## Context

This work extends the long history of applying the Generator Coordinate Method to cluster reactions in nuclear physics, following earlier approaches such as the Resonating Group Method (RGM) and prior GCM studies by Horiuchi (1973), Friedrich (1975), and others. The paper addresses a specific gap: decay rates were hardly discussed in early GCM theory apart from semiclassical alpha-decay treatments. The results enable future calculations of decay widths for heavy nuclei, particularly in the context of nuclear fission where the transmission coefficient between fused and separated clusters becomes the relevant physical quantity. The authors note that at higher energies where decay widths exceed level spacings, only statistically weighted averages are needed and the transmission coefficient approaches its unitary limit of T=1.
