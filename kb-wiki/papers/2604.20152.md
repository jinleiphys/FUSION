# Impact of nuclear deformation on particle production in $Ne+Ne$ collisions at \texorpdfstring{\five}{sqrt(sNN)=5.36 TeV} from AMPT-SM

> We present a systematic study of particle production in $Ne+Ne$ collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}} = 5.36$ TeV using the A Multi-Phase Transport (AMPT) model with string melting (SM) configuration. The analysis compares spherical and deformed configurations of ${}^{20}\mathrm{Ne}$ to investigate the influence of initial-state nuclear deformation on bulk observables. Charged-particle pseudorapidity ($\langle dN_{\mathrm{ch}}/dη\rangle$) densities, identified particle yields ($dN/dy$), transverse momentum ($p_T$) spectra, mean transverse momentum ($\langle p_{\mathrm{T}} \rangle$), and $p_{\mathrm{T}}$-differential particle ratios ($K/π$ and $p/π$) are studied as functions of multiplicity and centrality. The results show that all observables exhibit the expected dependence on event activity, including smooth multiplicity scaling, mass ordering in $\langle p_{\mathrm{T}} \rangle$, and characteristic features associated with radial flow and quark coalescence. Differences between the two configurations on bulk observables remain small across all observables, typically at the level of a 2\%--6\% percent, with slightly enhanced sensitivity observed in peripheral collisions. These findings suggest that, within the AMPT-SM framework, the collective dynamics and hadrochemical composition are primarily governed by the overall system density and interaction dynamics, while the influence of initial-state deformation is subleading. This study provides a baseline for understanding deformation effects in light-ion collision systems and highlights the limited sensitivity of bulk observables to initial nuclear geometry in transport-based approaches.

## Key claim

Within the AMPT-SM transport framework, the influence of initial-state nuclear deformation on bulk particle production in Ne+Ne collisions at 5.36 TeV is subleading, with differences between spherical and deformed configurations typically at the 2%–6% level; the abstract's claim of "limited sensitivity" is qualified by the observation that slightly enhanced sensitivity is observed in peripheral collisions, where lower particle density allows a modest imprint of initial geometry to persist.

## Method

The study used the A Multi-Phase Transport model with string melting (AMPT-SM) to simulate Ne+Ne collisions at √sNN = 5.36 TeV, comparing spherical and deformed configurations of ²⁰Ne nuclei. The deformed configuration included non-zero quadrupole (β₂ = 0.548) and octupole (β₃ = 0.281) deformation parameters in the Woods-Saxon density profile, while the spherical case used β₂ = β₃ = 0. A total of 10 million minimum bias events were generated for each configuration, and observables were analyzed as functions of multiplicity and centrality using the standard AMPT-SM parameters (αs = 0.33, parton screening mass μ = 3.2 fm⁻¹, parton scattering cross section 1.5 mb, Lund parameters a = 0.5 and b = 0.9 GeV⁻²).

## Key numbers

* Charged-particle pseudorapidity density differences between spherical and deformed configurations reach approximately 4% in peripheral (70–80%) collisions, while central (0–10%) collisions show negligible differences.
* The ratio of spherical to deformed charged-particle yields across centrality shows deviations at the level of approximately 2%, with slightly larger deviations toward peripheral collisions.
* Transverse momentum spectra ratios between spherical and deformed configurations show deviations at the level of 2%–6% in peripheral collisions, depending on pT and particle species.
* Mean transverse momentum (⟨pT⟩) ratios between spherical and deformed configurations show deviations within 1%–2% across the full multiplicity range.
* Particle ratios K/π and p/π show deviations between spherical and deformed configurations of at most 1%–3% across all pT and centralities.

## Context

This work extends previous AMPT-SM studies of particle production in larger systems such as Au+Au and Pb+Pb collisions (Lin 2005, Xu 2011) and in smaller systems including O+O and p+Pb collisions (Khan 2024, Ashraf 2024). The study provides a baseline for understanding deformation effects in light-ion collision systems, which are intermediate in size between proton-proton and heavy-ion collisions. The results highlight that bulk observables show limited sensitivity to initial nuclear geometry in transport-based approaches, suggesting that future studies should explore more sensitive observables such as anisotropic flow to probe initial geometry in intermediate-size systems.
