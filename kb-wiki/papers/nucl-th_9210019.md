# Finite volume of baryons and the mass limit for neutron stars

> We confirmed the following idea by numerical calculation: The extended structure of baryons may make the nuclear equation of state stiffer at higher density while keeping the compression modulus for normal nuclear matter around its empirical value $K=240MeV$.The model built in this wayy fits all empirical data for normal nuclear matter and gives the mass limit of neutron stars compatible with the observation.

## Key claim

The authors confirm by numerical calculation that introducing a finite volume (hardcore) for baryons makes the nuclear equation of state stiffer at high density while keeping the compression modulus for normal nuclear matter at the empirical value of 240 MeV, but they note that the Van der Waals approximation used is essentially nonrelativistic and may violate causality at very high density, so the quantitative result below a density of 8 times normal nuclear density gives a mass limit of 2 solar masses rather than the unconstrained value over 2.5 solar masses.

## Method

The authors start from a local relativistic mean field theory with nucleons interacting with scalar, omega, and rho meson fields. They incorporate the finite volume of baryons via the Van der Waals approximation, replacing the relation between number density and Fermi momentum with one that subtracts the excluded volume (n = 2p^3 / (3π^2 + 2v p^3) for nucleons). They fit five model parameters (including the baryon radius a = 0.62 fm) to four empirical normal nuclear matter data points (equilibrium density, binding energy, asymmetry energy, and compression modulus K = 240 MeV). They then generalize to neutron star matter including the lowest baryon octet, electrons, and muons, solve for the equation of state under charge neutrality and beta equilibrium, and integrate the Oppenheimer-Volkov equation to find the neutron star mass limit.

## Key numbers

- Compression modulus of normal nuclear matter: K = 240 MeV, used as a fitting constraint.
- Equilibrium nuclear matter density: n0 = (4π/3 r^3)^{-1} with r = 1.175 fm.
- Binding energy per nucleon: B/A = 15.986 MeV, used as a fitting constraint.
- Asymmetry energy: E_as = 36.5 MeV, used as a fitting constraint.
- Baryon radius in the simplest parameter set: a = 0.62 fm.
- Effective nucleon mass in normal nuclear matter: χ = 0.84 (in units of free nucleon mass m).
- Maximum neutron star mass from the unconstrained calculation: over 2.5 solar masses (M⊙).
- Mass of the observed heaviest neutron star 4U0900-40: 1.85 M⊙.
- Under the causal limit (P = E), the reliable density limit is n_limit = 8 n0, giving a mass limit of 2 M⊙.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Glendenning (1985), who found that local relativistic mean field theory with a compression modulus around 200 MeV gave too low a neutron star mass limit (below 1.85 M⊙). The introduction of derivative coupling between baryons and the scalar meson field (as in earlier work by the same group) improved the result but destroyed renormalizability. The authors instead use the Van der Waals approximation for baryon hardcores, which has been successfully used in nuclear physics for decades and in recent works by other authors. This enables a consistent description of both normal nuclear matter data and the high-density equation of state needed for neutron stars, and suggests further investigation of finite volume effects in heavy ion collisions and the need for a relativistic treatment of the baryon finite volume effect.
