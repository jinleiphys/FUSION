# $α$-cluster structures above double shell closures via double-folding potentials from chiral effective field theory

> $α$-cluster structures above double shell closures are among the cornerstones for nuclear $α$-cluster physics. Semi-microscopic cluster models (SMCMs) are important theoretical models to study their properties. A crucial ingredient of SMCM is the effective potential between the alpha cluster and the doubly magic nucleus. We derive new double-folding potentials between $α$ clusters and doubly magic nuclei from soft local chiral nucleon-nucleon potentials given by chiral effective field theory ($χ$EFT) at the next-to-next-to-leading order. The $α$-cluster structures in ${}^{8}\text{Be}$, ${}^{20}\text{Ne}$, ${}^{44,52}\text{Ti}$, and ${}^{212}\text{Po}$ are explored to validate these new double-folding potentials. The $α$ decay of ${}^{104}\text{Te}$ is also studied in the light of recent experimental results. Our study shows that double-folding potentials from $χ$EFT are the new reliable effective potentials for the SMCM approach to $α$-cluster structures above double shell closures, with both conceptual and phenomenological merits.

## Key claim

Double-folding potentials derived from soft local chiral nucleon-nucleon potentials at next-to-next-to-leading order (N2LO) provide reliable effective interactions for semi-microscopic cluster model (SMCM) studies of alpha-cluster structures above double shell closures, with the caveat that the approach assumes pure alpha-cluster configurations from the outset and cannot by itself describe the emergence of clusters from nucleon degrees of freedom.

## Method

The authors construct double-folding potentials between an alpha cluster and doubly magic core nuclei by folding local chiral nucleon-nucleon potentials at N2LO with the density distributions of the two constituents, using only the central components since both the alpha particle and doubly magic nuclei have saturated spin-isospin configurations that suppress spin-orbit and tensor contributions. The resulting potentials are then used in semi-microscopic cluster model calculations for the alpha+alpha (8Be), alpha+16O (20Ne), alpha+40,48Ca (44,52Ti), and alpha+208Pb (212Po) systems, with spurious states identified via Wildermuth conditions. Bound states are treated with modified Thomas-Fermi approximation (MTPA) and resonant states with complex scaling method (CSM).

## Key numbers

- For 8Be: the 0+1 state has a theoretical alpha-decay width of 6.09 x 10^-6 MeV compared to the experimental (5.57 +/- 0.25) x 10^-6 MeV; the 2+1 state width is 1.72 MeV versus 1.513 +/- 0.015 MeV; the 4+1 state width is 3.18 MeV versus approximately 3.5 MeV
- For 20Ne: the 6+1 state alpha-decay width is 3.64 x 10^-4 MeV (theory) versus (1.1 +/- 0.2) x 10^-4 MeV (experiment); the 8+1 state width is 1.98 x 10^-4 MeV versus (3.5 +/- 1.0) x 10^-5 MeV
- For 20Ne: the B(E2 down) value for the 2+1 state is 13.0 W.u. (theory) versus 20.3 +/- 1.0 W.u. (experiment); for the 4+1 state it is 17.1 W.u. versus 22 +/- 2 W.u.
- For 44Ti: the B(E2 down) value for the 2+1 state is 9.9 W.u. (theory) versus 13 +/- 4 W.u. (experiment); for the 4+1 state it is 13.4 W.u. versus 30 +/- 5 W.u.
- For 52Ti: the B(E2 down) value for the 2+1 state is 7.1 W.u. (theory) versus 7.5^{+0.4}_{-0.3} W.u. (experiment); for the 4+1 state it is 9.6 W.u. versus 9.5^{+1.4}_{-1.1} W.u.

## Context

This work extends the application of chiral effective field theory to semi-microscopic cluster models, bridging a gap between modern ab initio nuclear interactions and traditional cluster-model effective potentials. It builds on earlier double-folding potentials using Schmid-Wildermuth, Hasegawa-Nagata-Yamamoto, and M3Y interactions (Satchler 1979, Khoa 1997), and follows recent successful benchmarks of chiral-EFT-based double-folding potentials in heavy-ion scattering (Durant 2017, Durant 2020). The results enable systematic improvement of SMCM calculations across the nuclide chart with potentials that have a clearer connection to QCD, and allow extraction of alpha-formation probabilities by comparing theoretical and experimental alpha-decay widths.
