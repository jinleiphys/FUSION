# Sensitivity study of explosive nucleosynthesis in Type Ia supernovae: I. Modification of individual thermonuclear reaction rates

> We explore the sensitivity of the nucleosynthesis due to type Ia supernovae with respect to uncertainties in nuclear reaction rates. We have adopted a standard one-dimensional delayed detonation model of the explosion of a Chandrasekhar-mass white dwarf, and have post-processed the thermodynamic trajectories of every mass-shell with a nucleosynthetic code, with increases (decreases) by a factor of ten on the rates of 1196 nuclear reactions. We have computed as well hydrodynamic models for different rates of the fusion reactions of 12C and of 16O. For selected reactions, we have recomputed the nucleosynthesis with alternative prescriptions for their rates taken from the JINA REACLIB database, and have analyzed the temperature ranges where modifications of their rates have the strongest effect on nucleosynthesis. The nucleosynthesis resulting from the Type Ia supernova models is quite robust with respect to variations of nuclear reaction rates, with the exception of the reaction of fusion of 12C nuclei. The energy of the explosion changes by less than \sim4%. The changes in the nucleosynthesis due to the modification of the rates of fusion reactions are as well quite modest, for instance no species with a mass fraction larger than 0.02 experiences a variation of its yield larger than a factor of two. We provide the sensitivity of the yields of the most abundant species with respect to the rates of the most intense reactions with protons, neutrons, and alphas. In general, the yields of Fe-group nuclei are more robust than the yields of intermediate-mass elements. Among the charged particle reactions, the most influential on supernova nucleosynthesis are 30Si + p \rightleftarrows 31P + γ, 20Ne + α \rightleftarrows 24Mg + γ, and 24Mg + α \rightleftarrows 27Al + p. The temperatures at which a modification of their rate has a larger impact are in the range 2 < T < 4 GK. (abridged)

## Key claim

The nucleosynthesis resulting from Type Ia supernova models is quite robust with respect to variations of nuclear reaction rates, with the exception of the fusion reaction of two 12C nuclei; the authors qualify that no species with a mass fraction larger than 0.02 experiences a yield variation larger than a factor of two when individual rates are varied by factors of ten.

## Method

The authors adopted a standard one-dimensional delayed detonation model (DDTc) of a Chandrasekhar-mass white dwarf explosion, then post-processed the thermodynamic trajectories of every mass-shell using the nucleosynthetic code CRANK. They increased or decreased the rates of 1196 nuclear reactions (simultaneously with their inverse reactions) by a factor of ten, one reaction pair at a time, and recomputed the nucleosynthesis. For the fusion reactions of 12C and 16O, they also computed full hydrodynamic models with modified rates, and for selected reactions they used alternative rate prescriptions from the JINA REACLIB database.

## Key numbers

- Explosion energy changes by less than ~4% when the rates of 12C+12C or 16O+16O are multiplied by a factor of 10 or 0.1.
- No species with a mass fraction larger than 0.02 experiences a variation of its yield larger than a factor of two due to modification of fusion reaction rates.
- The ejected mass of 56Ni in the reference model is 0.675 M_sun, and the kinetic energy of the ejecta is 1.16 x 10^51 erg.
- The central 0.4 M_sun of the white dwarf reach nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE), with roughly 0.24 M_sun experiencing a moderately alpha-rich freeze-out.
- The three most influential charged-particle reactions on supernova nucleosynthesis are 30Si + p ⇄ 31P + γ, 20Ne + α ⇄ 24Mg + γ, and 24Mg + α ⇄ 27Al + p, with the temperature range of strongest impact being 2 < T < 4 GK.
- Among species with yields larger than 10^-8 M_sun, 35S has the largest sensitivity to nuclear reaction rates.

## Context

This work extends the methodology of The et al. (1998), who varied individual reaction rates for 44Ti nucleosynthesis, to the full nucleosynthesis of Type Ia supernovae for the first time. It contrasts with the Monte Carlo approach of Hix et al. (2003) for nova nucleosynthesis and the nuclear mass model testing of Arcones et al. (2010) for the r-process. The results show that uncertainty in individual thermonuclear reaction rates cannot account for discrepancies of a factor of two between isotopic ratios in Type Ia supernovae and those in the solar system, especially within the Fe-group, and that the explosion model itself chiefly determines element production.
