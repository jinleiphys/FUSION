# Efficacious qubit mappings for quantum simulations of the $^{12}$C rotational band

> Solving atomic nuclei from first principles places enormous demands on computational resources, which grow exponentially with increasing number of particles and the size of the space they occupy. We present first quantum simulations based on the variational quantum eigensolver for the low-lying structure of the $^{12}$C nucleus that provide acceptable bound-state energies even in the presence of noise. We achieve this by taking advantage of two critical developments. First, we utilize an almost perfect symmetry of atomic nuclei that, in a complete symmetry-adapted basis, drastically reduces the size of the model space. Second, we use the efficacious Gray encoding, for which it has been recently shown that it is resource efficient, especially when coupled with a near band-diagonal structure of the nuclear Hamiltonian.

# Efficacious qubit mappings for quantum simulations of the \(^{12}\)C rotational band

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that noisy quantum simulations using Gray encoding, combined with a noise-resilient technique and a symmetry-adapted basis, can reproduce the lowest \(2^+\) excitation energy of \(^{12}\)C within acceptable accuracy, provided the Hamiltonian bandwidth is sufficiently large (e.g., \(K \geq 3\)) and the model space is reduced to only eight basis states via symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry.

## Method
The authors use the variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) with a parameterized circuit ansatz to compute the ground state (\(0^+_{\text{g.s.}}\)) and first excited \(2^+\) state of \(^{12}\)C. They employ a symmetry-adapted basis that exploits the symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry of the nucleus, reducing the model space to eight non-negligible basis states from a harmonic oscillator space of 14 shells. The nuclear Hamiltonian is taken from a symmetry effective field theory (SymEFT) with a quadrupole-quadrupole interaction, and the matrix is truncated to bandwidth \(2K+1\). Two qubit encoding schemes are compared: one-hot encoding (8 qubits) and Gray encoding (3 qubits), with the Gray encoding mapping basis states to bitstrings differing by a single bit. Simulations are performed with noiseless, shot-noise (1000 shots), and noisy (using a fake IBMQ backend ibm_manila) conditions, and a noise-resilient technique extracts final energies from noisy parameters.

## Key numbers
- The model space is reduced to **8** basis states from \(\sim 10^{11}\) states in 14 harmonic oscillator shells, using the symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry.
- The Gray encoding uses **3** qubits for \(N=8\) basis states, while the one-hot encoding uses **8** qubits.
- The Hamiltonian parameters include \(\hbar\Omega = 18\) MeV and \(\gamma = 1.7 \times 10^{-4}\).
- The final theoretical \(2^+\) excitation energy (true value) for \(K=7\) is **6.414 MeV**.
- For \(K=3\), the Gray encoding already provides a reasonable excitation energy that agrees with the experimental first \(2^+\) state (at 6.414 MeV) rather than the second \(2^+\) state (at 9.87 MeV).
- For \(N=32\) basis states, the one-hot encoding would require **32 qubits** and **61 two-qubit gates**, while the Gray encoding would require only **5 qubits** and **12 two-qubit gates** with 3 layers.

## Context
This work extends earlier quantum simulations of nuclear systems, such as the deuteron by Eugen (2018), to the twelve-particle nucleus \(^{12}\)C, which is a significantly more complex system. It builds on the symmetry-adapted basis approach developed by Dytrych et al. (2007) and Launey et al. (2021) that exploits the symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry, and on the Gray encoding method shown by Rethinasamy et al. (2024) to be resource-efficient for band-diagonal Hamiltonians. The Hamiltonian used was previously shown by Dreyfuss et al. (2013) to describe the Hoyle state in \(^{12}\)C. This work enables future quantum simulations of heavier nuclei and larger model spaces on near-term NISQ devices, particularly by demonstrating that the Gray encoding with noise-resilient training can produce acceptable results even with noisy hardware.
