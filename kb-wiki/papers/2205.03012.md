# J-PARC hadron physics and future possibilities on color transparency

> The J-PARC is a hadron-accelerator facility to provide secondary beams of kaons, pions, neutrinos, muons, and the others together with the primary proton beam for investigating a wide range of science projects. High-energy hadron physics can be studied by using high-momentum beams of unseparated hadrons, which are essentially pions, and also primary protons. In this report, possible experiments are explained on color transparency and generalized parton distributions (GPDs). These projects are complementary to lepton-scattering experiments at JLab, COMPASS/AMBER, and future electron-ion colliders. Because of hadron-beam energies up to 30 GeV, the J-PARC is a unique facility to investigate the transition region from the hadron degrees of freedom to the quark-gluon one. It is suitable for finding mechanisms of the color transparency. Such color-transparency studies are also valuable for clarifying factorization of hadron-production processes in extracting the GPDs from actual measurements. These studies will lead to the understanding of basic high-energy hadron interactions in nuclear medium and to clarifications on the origins of hadron spins, masses, and internal pressure mechanisms.

## Key claim

The J-PARC hadron facility, with beam energies up to 30 GeV, is uniquely suited to investigate the transition region from hadron degrees of freedom to quark-gluon degrees of freedom, and can resolve the unexplained drop in nuclear transparency observed by the BNL-EVA collaboration at momenta above 10 GeV/c, a result that contradicts the standard color transparency prediction.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework of hard exclusive hadron reactions, including the constituent counting rule and the factorization of cross sections into hard perturbative QCD parts and soft distribution amplitudes. It then describes how nuclear transparency is measured in reactions such as \(p A \rightarrow 2p (A-1)\) and \(e A \rightarrow e' p (A-1)\), and proposes new \(2 \to 3\) reaction processes (\(\pi^- A \to \pi^- \pi^+ A^*\) and \(pp \to p \pi B\)) as well as exclusive Drell-Yan processes (\(\pi^- p \to \mu^+ \mu^- B\)) to study color transparency and generalized parton distributions (GPDs) at J-PARC.

## Key numbers

- The BNL-EVA experiment showed nuclear transparency increasing with beam momentum up to about 10 GeV/c, then dropping at momenta above 10 GeV/c, in contradiction with color transparency predictions.
- The J-PARC primary proton beam energy is 30 GeV, and the high-momentum beamline provides unseparated hadrons (mainly pions) with momentum up to about 20 GeV/c.
- A \(\Xi^-\)–\(^{14}\)N bound system was observed with a \(\Xi^-\) binding energy of \(1.27 \pm 0.21\) MeV.
- The JLab color-transparency measurement in 2021 found no transparency even up to \(Q^2 = 14\) GeV\(^2\).
- Calculated nuclear transparencies for the \(2 \to 3\) process \(\pi^- A \to \pi^- \pi^+ A^*\) are shown for beam energies in the 20–200 GeV range, covering J-PARC to COMPASS/AMBER energies.
- The exclusive Drell-Yan process at J-PARC probes GPDs in the region \(0.1 < x < 0.3\) using a pion beam momentum of 15 GeV/c.

## Context

This work extends the line of color-transparency investigations initiated by the BNL-EVA collaboration (2004) and the recent JLab measurement (2021), which both produced puzzling results. The proposed J-PARC experiments are complementary to lepton-scattering experiments at JLab, COMPASS/AMBER, and future electron-ion colliders. The GPD studies via exclusive Drell-Yan and \(2 \to 3\) reactions at J-PARC would complement spacelike GPD measurements at JLab and timelike GPD measurements at KEK-B, and could help clarify the origins of hadron spins, masses, and internal pressure mechanisms.
