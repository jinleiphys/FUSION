# Discrepancy in low transverse momentum dileptons from relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> The dilepton transverse momentum spectra and invariant mass spectra for low $p_T <0.15$~GeV/c in Au+Au collisions of different centralities at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 200 GeV are studied within the parton-hadron-string dynamics (PHSD) transport approach. The PHSD describes the whole evolution of the system on a microscopic basis, incorporates hadronic and partonic degrees-of-freedom, the dynamical hadronization of partons and hadronic rescattering. For dilepton production in p+p, p+A and A+A reactions the PHSD incorporates the leading hadronic and partonic channels (also for heavy flavors) and includes in-medium effects such as a broadening of the vector meson spectral functions in hadronic matter and a modification of initial heavy-flavor correlations by interactions with the partonic and hadronic medium. The transport calculations reproduce well the momentum integrated invariant mass spectra from the STAR Collaboration for minimum bias Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 200 GeV, while the description of the STAR data - when gating on low $p_T < 0.15$ GeV/c - is getting worse when going from central to peripheral collisions. An analysis of the transverse momentum spectra shows that the data for peripheral (60-80\%) collisions are well reproduced for $p_T>0.2$ GeV/c while the strong peak at low $p_T < 0.15$ GeV/c, that shows up in the experimental data for the mass bins ($0.4 < M < 0.7$ GeV and $1.2 < M < 2.6$ GeV), is fully missed by the PHSD and cannot be explained by the standard in-medium effects. This provides a new puzzle for microscopic descriptions of low $p_T$ dilepton data from the STAR Collaboration.

## Key claim

The parton-hadron-string dynamics (PHSD) transport approach, which includes three standard in-medium effects (broadening of vector meson spectral functions, modification of heavy-flavor correlations, and partonic interactions), reproduces STAR Collaboration dilepton data down to pT ≈ 0.15 GeV/c for all centralities but fully misses a strong enhancement at pT < 0.15 GeV/c in peripheral (60-80%) collisions, creating a new puzzle that cannot be explained by standard microscopic descriptions.

## Method

The authors employed the PHSD transport approach, which describes the full evolution of heavy-ion collisions on a microscopic basis with hadronic and partonic degrees of freedom, to calculate dilepton transverse momentum spectra and invariant mass spectra for Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV. They compared PHSD results with STAR Collaboration data for different centrality classes (10-40%, 40-60%, and 60-80%) using acceptance cuts of pT > 0.2 GeV/c for each lepton and |ye| ≤ 1, and separately analyzed the low pT < 0.15 GeV/c region.

## Key numbers

- The yield of low-mass dileptons (0.4 < M < 0.79 GeV) within acceptance cuts is 4.85 × 10⁻³ for 10-40% central, 1.05 × 10⁻³ for 40-60% central, and 2.1 × 10⁻⁴ for 60-80% central Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV.
- The yield of intermediate-mass dileptons (1.2 < M < 2.6 GeV) within acceptance cuts is 1.0 × 10⁻³ for 10-40% central, 2.2 × 10⁻⁴ for 40-60% central, and 4.5 × 10⁻⁵ for 60-80% central Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV.
- The experimental data for 60-80% central collisions show a yield of low-mass and intermediate-mass dielectrons at pT < 0.15 GeV/c that is about ten times larger than the PHSD prediction.
- The PHSD reproduces experimental data well for pT > 0.2 GeV/c in peripheral (60-80%) collisions, but the anomalous enhancement appears below pT ≈ 0.15 GeV/c.

## Context

This work extends previous PHSD studies (Song 2018) by incorporating dilepton production from hadronic and partonic bremsstrahlung processes using the soft-photon approximation, finding these radiative corrections are subleading. The results contradict the expectation that standard in-medium effects (vector meson broadening, partonic interactions, heavy-flavor modification) can explain the low-pT dilepton enhancement observed by the STAR Collaboration (Yang 2018), and the puzzle remains open for future theoretical work beyond current microscopic models that have been validated from SIS to LHC energies.
