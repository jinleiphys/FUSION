# Analytic calculator for determination of $γ$-ray angular distribution coefficients and tensors in aligned and partially-aligned nuclei

> A program has been developed to calculate a complete set of precise $γ$-ray angular distribution coefficients and statistical tensors in maximally- and partially-aligned nuclei. For practical nuclear structure and reaction purposes, there is no imposed constraint on any arguments that are likely to arise in the determination of these quantities. The program can also be used as a stand-alone vector-coupling calculator for the exact evaluation of Clebsch-Gordan and Racah coefficients, the closely-related Wigner 3-$j$, 6-$j$, and 9-$j$ symbols, as well as Gaunt coefficients. These quantities, which frequently arise in quantum mechanical applications involving angular momentum coupling and recoupling schemes, provide the underlying machinery in angular distribution calculations.

## Key claim

The paper presents an open-source Python toolkit, PyGammaRAD, that calculates gamma-ray angular distribution coefficients and statistical tensors for both maximally- and partially-aligned nuclei without the spin, multipole-order, or tensor-rank restrictions that limit the existing printed tables of Yamazaki (1967), Rose and Brink (1967), and Der Mateosian and Sunyar (1974). The program reproduces all tabulated data from these references with full numerical precision and identifies a small number of typographical errors in Rose and Brink (1967).

## Method

The program evaluates gamma-ray angular distribution coefficients and statistical tensors using standard double-precision and extended-precision floating-point arithmetic on the well-known sum formulae for angular momentum coupling. The underlying machinery computes Clebsch-Gordan coefficients, Racah coefficients, and Wigner 3-j, 6-j, and 9-j symbols, which are then combined to calculate the F_k coefficients, B_k population tensors, and A_k anisotropy coefficients that appear in the angular distribution formula W(theta) = sum_k A_k P_k(cos theta). For partially-aligned nuclei, the magnetic-substate populations are modeled as a Gaussian distribution characterized by a width parameter sigma, and the program evaluates the resulting attenuated coefficients across a continuous range of sigma/J values rather than the discrete set provided in the printed tables.

## Key numbers

- The printed tables of Yamazaki (1967) cover initial spins up to J = 15 hbar (integral) and J = 31/2 hbar (half-integral), with tensor ranks k = 2 and 4 only, and multipole orders L = 1 to 3
- The tables of Rose and Brink (1967) extend to multipole order L = 4 and tensor rank k = 8, but restrict initial spins to J = 10 hbar (integral) and J = 19/2 hbar (half-integral) for angular distribution coefficients
- The partial-alignment tables of Der Mateosian and Sunyar (1974) cover tensor ranks k = 2 and 4 only, evaluated at discrete sigma/J values from 0.1 to 2.0, for spins up to J = 26 hbar (integral) and J = 51/2 hbar (half-integral), restricted to dipole and quadrupole transitions
- The test suite contains more than 200 unit tests validated across Python versions 3.6 to 3.13 on Linux and Mac OS platforms

## Context

This work extends the foundational tabular resources of Yamazaki (1967), Rose and Brink (1967), and Der Mateosian and Sunyar (1974) by providing a programmable, open-source alternative that removes the spin and multipole-order restrictions inherent in printed tables. The program consolidates functionality previously scattered across multiple Fortran codes (Macias 1976, Rouse 1978), Java tools (Stevenson 2002), and symbolic Python packages (SymPy) into a single unified library, enabling integration with modern automated analysis pipelines for experiments using detector arrays such as Gammasphere and GRETINA. The toolkit enables future applications in heavy-ion fusion-evaporation reactions, inelastic neutron scattering measurements, and nuclear fission studies where spin alignment of fission fragments is quantified through Monte Carlo calculations (Chalil 2024).
