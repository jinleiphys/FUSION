# Scattering off unstable states

> Unstable states that live long enough may appear as in(out)going particles in scattering experiments. Yet, the standard QFT approach strictly applies only to fully stable asymptotic states. This is evident when scattering involving unstable particles develops a $t$-channel singularity at specific angles. We employ a finite-time formalism leading to analytic results without the singularity (even in the infinite-time limit), thus solving the problem at a phenomenological level. In turn, the approach also justifies treating long-lived particles, like weakly decaying pions, as stable during strong interactions.

# Wiki Page: Scattering off Unstable States

## Key claim

The standard QFT approach to scattering with unstable initial or final particles produces a divergent t-channel singularity at specific angles; a finite-time QFT formalism that explicitly incorporates the finite lifetime of the unstable particle yields analytic cross-sections that are free of this singularity for any finite time interval, and the cross-section vanishes smoothly in the infinite-time limit rather than recovering the divergent standard result.

## Method

The authors consider a scalar field theory with three fields A, B, and C, where C is unstable (m_C > m_A + m_B) and decays via C → AB. They compute the tree-level scattering amplitude for AC → AC via B exchange using a finite-time S-matrix formalism, where the time interval T between creation and detection is kept finite. The unstable nature of C is incorporated by modifying the plane-wave phases with exponential decay factors proportional to Γ_C/(2γ_C), where γ_C is the Lorentz factor; the resulting amplitude is integrated over the final-state momentum with a finite-T kernel, yielding a differential cross-section that depends explicitly on T.

## Key numbers

- The decay width for C → AB is Γ_{C→AB} = g² |k_A| / (8π m_C²), where k_A is the three-momentum of outgoing particle A.
- For the scalar model with m_C = 1 and m_A = 1/10 [a.u.], the singular momentum range for the t-channel singularity extends from |p_C|_min = 0.276 [a.u.] to |p_C|_max = 0.733 [a.u.] when m_B = 0.5 [a.u.].
- The singular scattering angle for the choice m_B = 0.5 [a.u.] and |p_C| = 0.4 [a.u.] is θ_sing = 1.15 rad.
- The finite-time differential cross-section is shown for T = τ/5 (where τ = 1/Γ_C), and the total cross-section is plotted as a function of T, showing a maximum followed by smooth decay to zero.

## Context

This work addresses the t-channel singularity problem originally identified by Peierls (1961) in the context of hadronic N*π scattering, and more recently discussed by Grzadkowski (2021) for processes such as W⁺e⁺, Z⁰e⁻, and μ⁺μ⁻ scattering. The authors argue that previously proposed solutions, finite beam size (Melnikov 1996, Dams 2002), finite width of the exchanged particle, ad hoc complex mass for the unstable particle (Ginzburg 1995), and reformulation as three-body scattering (Goebel 1964, Mai 2017, Jackura 2018, Mikhasenko 2019), are either incomplete or reintroduce divergences under limiting conditions. The finite-time formalism provides a phenomenological resolution that also justifies treating long-lived particles (e.g., weakly decaying pions) as stable during strong interactions, and opens the way to applications in femtoscopy, such as φK scattering where φ decays to K̅K.
