# Sound modes in hot nuclear matter

> The propagation of the isoscalar and isovector sound modes in a hot nuclear matter is considered. The approach is based on the collisional kinetic theory and takes into account the temperature and memory effects. It is shown that the sound velocity and the attenuation coefficient are significantly influenced by the Fermi surface distortion (FSD). The corresponding influence is much stronger for the isoscalar mode than for the isovector one. The memory effects cause a non-monotonous behavior of the attenuation coefficient as a function of the relaxation time leading to a zero-to-first sound transition with increasing temperature. The mixing of both the isoscalar and the isovector sound modes in an asymmetric nuclear matter is evaluated. The condition for the bulk instability and the instability growth rate in the presence of the memory effects is studied. It is shown that both the FSD and the relaxation processes lead to a shift of the maximum of the instability growth rate to the longer wave length region.

# Sound modes in hot nuclear matter

## Key claim

The propagation of isoscalar and isovector sound modes in hot nuclear matter is significantly influenced by Fermi surface distortion (FSD) and memory effects in the collision integral, with the FSD effect being much stronger for the isoscalar mode than for the isovector one; the zero-to-first sound transition occurs with increasing temperature, and the attenuation coefficient exhibits a non-monotonous (bell-shaped) behavior as a function of temperature due to memory effects.

## Method

The authors employed collisional kinetic theory based on the linearized Boltzmann equation with a collision integral that includes memory effects via a frequency-dependent relaxation time. They derived local equations of motion for the particle density, displacement field, and pressure tensor, assuming Fermi surface distortions up to multipolarity ℓ ≤ 2, and obtained dispersion relations for both stable sound modes and unstable density fluctuations in hot nuclear matter.

## Key numbers

- The additional incompressibility due to FSD is ΔK ≈ 200 MeV, derived from the kinetic Fermi energy eF ≈ 40 MeV, which is comparable to the static incompressibility K ≈ 220 MeV.
- For the isoscalar mode, the zero sound velocity c0 ≈ √2 c1, where c1 = √(K/9m) is the first sound velocity.
- For the isovector mode, the symmetry energy Esymm ≈ 30 MeV, with an additional contribution ΔEsymm ≈ 10 MeV from FSD, giving c0 = √(2(Esymm + ΔEsymm)/m).
- The relaxation time parameter for the isoscalar mode is α = 9.2 MeV, and for the isovector mode α = 4.6 MeV.
- The critical temperature for liquid-gas phase transition is Tcrit ≈ 17 MeV.
- The bulk density at saturation is ρeq = 0.1453 fm⁻³.
- The instability regime with F0 < -1 is reached at ρ0 ≲ 0.5 ρeq.
- The calculation of instability growth rate used Skyrme force SIII at density ρ0 = 0.3 ρeq and temperature T = 6 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the kinetic theory of sound propagation in Fermi liquids, building on the foundational treatment by Lifshits and Pitaevsky (1993) and earlier work by Abrikosov and Khalatnikov (1959) on collision regimes. It addresses the limitations of hydrodynamic models such as the Goldhaber-Teller and Steinwedel-Jensen models, which fail for isoscalar giant resonances where FSD effects are important as noted by Bertsch (1975), Holzwarth and Eckart (1979), and Nix and Sierk (1980). The results enable improved understanding of the transition between zero and first sound in hot nuclear matter and provide a framework for analyzing spinodal instability in nuclear matter, including the shift of the most unstable mode toward longer wavelengths, which affects cluster formation in nuclear disintegration.
