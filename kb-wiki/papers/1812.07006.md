# Chiral QCD phase in equilibrium with Hadron Gas and the location of the critical point

> We develop a description of the equation of state of QCD matter with restored chiral symmetry, which is in thermal and chemical equilibrium with the hadronic phase. The hadron gas is described with thermodynamically consistent volume corrections. The chiral phase is composed of a set of few quark condensates, each of which corresponds to a family of hadrons with specific quark content. On the boundary between the two phases we apply the requirement of conservation of particle numbers per family. We use lattice calculations for temperatures below the transition curve to determine hadronic volumes. We find that the pion system plays decisive role in the shift of the transition from higher order (crossover) to first order. For four volume models we calculate the location of the critical point as function of critical temperature $T_c$ at vanishing baryon density. Particularly, if we additionally impose the equality between the densities of quarks contained in mesons and baryons, we find a critical point residing in the interval of baryon chemical potential $μ_B \simeq$ 233-267 MeV and of temperature $T \simeq$ 153-158 MeV.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the critical endpoint of QCD matter can be located by requiring particle-number conservation per hadron family across the chiral transition, and they find that the pion family is the decisive system driving the transition from crossover to first order. The claimed critical point interval (μ_B ≈ 233–267 MeV, T ≈ 153–158 MeV) depends on imposing an additional condition of equal densities of quarks contained in mesons and baryons, and on the specific hadron volume model used.

## Method

The hadron gas phase is described with thermodynamically consistent volume corrections using Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac statistics, with hadron volumes fitted to lattice QCD pressure at zero baryon density. The chiral phase is modeled as a set of quark condensates (quasi-particles), one per hadron family, whose masses are temperature-dependent and bounded above by the lightest hadron mass in that family. On the transition curve (taken to coincide with the chemical freeze-out curve), the authors impose equality of particle numbers per family between the two phases, together with pressure equality and strangeness neutrality, and solve for the chiral masses; the critical point is identified as the point where a family's chiral mass reaches its upper bound.

## Key numbers

* Critical point interval (with the additional meson–baryon quark-density equality): baryon chemical potential μ_B ≈ 233–267 MeV and temperature T ≈ 153–158 MeV.
* The critical temperature at zero baryon density, T_c, is taken as a free parameter; a value of T_c = 154 MeV is suggested by lattice QCD (citing lat2+1).
* The chemical freeze-out curve at zero baryon density is parametrized with T_{ch,0} ≈ 166 MeV (citing freeze), but the authors adopt T_{ch,0} ≈ T_c.
* The lightest hadron masses used as upper bounds for chiral masses include: pion (138.04 MeV), η (547.86 MeV), η' (957.78 MeV), nucleon (938.92 MeV), Δ (1232 MeV), kaon (495.64 MeV), Λ (1115.68 MeV), Σ (1193.15 MeV), Ξ (1318.29 MeV), Ω (1672.45 MeV).
* The hadron volume is related to radius via v_{ij} = 4(4π/3) r_{ij}^3.

## Context

This work extends the approach of modeling the QCD phase transition by treating the high-temperature phase as composed of hadron-like quark condensates, building on earlier observations that the chiral condensate changes sharply with temperature while deconfinement is broader (citing ch-d and Polyakov). It also follows the idea of parity doubling of baryons in the chiral phase (citing paritydoubling1 and paritydoubling2) and the concept of quarkyonic matter (citing quarkyonic). The method enables a systematic determination of the critical point location as a function of the critical temperature T_c, and the results can be compared with lattice QCD and heavy-ion collision freeze-out data.
