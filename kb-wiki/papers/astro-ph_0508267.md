# Time-integrated supernova neutrino flux from a nearby cluster

> The rate of gravitational collapse (type II) supernovae in our galaxy is uncertain by a factor of three or more. One way to determine the galactic supernova rate is through a radiogeochemical experiment (for example, the molybdenum-technetium experiment) that would integrate the neutrino flux over several million years. While such a measurement is designed to integrate the flux over the entire galaxy, nearby star-forming regions could skew the results. We model the flux from a recently identified such region, the Scorpius-Centaurus OB association, and compare with the flux from the rest of the galaxy.

# Time-integrated supernova neutrino flux from a nearby cluster

## Key claim

The time-integrated neutrino flux from the Scorpius-Centaurus OB association (Sco-Cen) is roughly equal to the flux from the entire rest of the galaxy *if* the galactic supernova rate is 1 per century, meaning this nearby cluster could significantly skew radiogeochemical measurements of the overall galactic supernova rate; at higher galactic rates the Sco-Cen contribution becomes relatively small.

## Method

The authors modeled the time-dependent neutrino flux from the three subgroups of Sco-Cen (Lower Centaurus Crux, Upper Centaurus Lupus, and Upper Scorpius) using published data on their distances, proper motions, and supernova rates (approximately 1 SN per My per subgroup). They solved the differential equation for daughter nucleus accumulation (97Tc) with an exponential decay term, and derived an effective distance that accounts for the time-integrated flux. They also applied a finite-size correction for the spatial width of each subgroup (20–30 pc rms) and performed Monte Carlo simulations to investigate the probability of extra-near supernova events.

## Key numbers

- The total average neutrino flux from Sco-Cen is 450 cm⁻² s⁻¹.
- The galactic supernova neutrino flux is given by R × N₅₈ × 1.3 × 10³ cm⁻² s⁻¹, where R is the galactic SN rate per century (1 to 3) and N₅₈ is the number of neutrinos emitted per collapse in units of 10⁵⁸ (about 0.45 for electron neutrinos).
- The standard solar model flux of ⁸B solar neutrinos is ~6 × 10⁶ cm⁻² s⁻¹.
- The ⁹⁸Tc half-life is 4.2 My; the ⁹⁷Tc half-life is 2.6 My.
- The threshold for the ⁹⁸Mo + ν → ⁹⁷Tc + n + e⁻ reaction is 7.28 MeV.
- Sco-Cen subgroups had distances between 100 and 240 pc from Earth over the past 10 My, with supernova windows of 3 My (Upper Scorpius), 7 My (Lower Centaurus Crux), and 10 My (Upper Centaurus Lupus).
- In Monte Carlo simulations with 20 SNe, only a fraction of a percent of runs placed a SN closer than 50 pc; increasing the rms width to 40 pc yielded a near-Earth (< 50 pc) event only 12% of the time.

## Context

This work extends earlier radiogeochemical proposals for measuring the galactic supernova rate, particularly the molybdenum-technetium experiment originally suggested by Cowan and Haxton (1982) and further developed by Haxton and Johnson (1988). The authors specifically model the Sco-Cen OB association identified by Benitez, Maiz-Apellaniz, and Canelles (2002), who argued it could explain the ⁶⁰Fe anomaly in deep-sea crust found by Knie et al. (1999, 2004). The paper enables future radiogeochemical experiments to account for nearby star-forming regions as a systematic background, and suggests that the statistical linkage between Sco-Cen and the ⁶⁰Fe anomaly is weaker than previously claimed.
