# Berry phase for coherent states in spin systems

> In this paper we obtain Berry phase from Schrödinger equation. For vector states, basic kets are coherent states in real parameterization. We calculate Berry phase for spin S=1/2 and spin S=1 in SU(2) group and Berry phase for spin S=1 in SU(3) group.

## Key claim

The paper derives the Berry phase for spin systems using coherent states in a real parameterization, showing that the geometric phase for spin S=1/2 and S=1 in SU(2) equals minus one-half and minus one times the solid angle, respectively, and that the SU(3) result reduces to the SU(2) case when the parameter g is set to zero.

## Method

The authors start from the Schrödinger equation and the adiabatic approximation, expressing the wavefunction as a coherent state multiplied by a phase factor. They compute the Berry phase as the closed-path integral of the Berry connection \(A_k = i\langle \phi | \partial_k \phi \rangle\) for coherent states in real parameterization, using Stokes' theorem to convert the line integral into a surface integral of the curvature form. The calculation is performed explicitly for spin S=1/2 and S=1 in the SU(2) group, and for spin S=1 in the SU(3) group.

## Key numbers

- For spin S=1/2 in SU(2): Berry phase \(\Theta_B = -\frac{1}{2} \Omega(\lambda)\), where \(\Omega(\lambda)\) is the solid angle of the surface S in parameter space.
- For spin S=1 in SU(2): Berry phase \(\Theta_B = -\Omega(\lambda)\), where \(\Omega(\lambda)\) is the solid angle of the surface S in parameter space.
- For spin S=1 in SU(3): Berry phase \(\Theta_B = \int (\cos 2g - \cos\theta) d\phi - \int (1 - \cos 2g) d\gamma\); setting \(g=0\) recovers the SU(2) result.

## Context

This work extends the Berry phase formalism of Berry (1984) to coherent states in real parameterization for SU(2) and SU(3) groups, building on the coherent state representations of Radcliffe (1971) and others. The results enable further calculation of Berry phases for higher SU(N) groups with N greater than or equal to 4, and have potential applications in quantum computation, optics, magnetic resonance, and molecular and atomic physics.
