# Dynamical constraints on phase transitions

> The numerical solutions of nonlocal and local Boltzmann kinetic equations for the simulation of central heavy ion reactions are parameterized in terms of time dependent thermodynamical variables in the Fermi liquid sense. This allows one to discuss dynamical trajectories in phase space. The nonequilibrium state is characterized by non-isobaric, non-isochoric etc. conditions, shortly called iso-nothing conditions. Therefore a combination of thermodynamical observables is constructed which allows one to locate instabilities and points of possible phase transition in a dynamical sense. We find two different mechanisms of instability, a short time surface - dominated instability and later a spinodal - dominated volume instability. The latter one occurs only if the incident energies do not exceed significantly the Fermi energy and might be attributed to spinodal decomposition. In contrast the fast surface explosion occurs far outside the spinodal region and pertains also in the cases where the system develops too fast to suffer a spinodal decomposition and where the system approaches equilibrium outside the spinodal region.

# Dynamical constraints on phase transitions

## Key claim

The paper identifies two distinct mechanisms of instability in heavy ion collisions near the Fermi energy, a short-time surface-dominated instability and a later spinodal-dominated volume instability, but the spinodal decomposition occurs only if incident energies do not exceed significantly the Fermi energy, while the surface explosion persists even when the system develops too fast to undergo spinodal decomposition.

## Method

The authors solved nonlocal and local Boltzmann kinetic equations (including Enskog-type nonlocal corrections with time delays and spatial shifts derived from scattering phase shifts) for central heavy ion collisions, parameterizing the numerical solutions in terms of time-dependent thermodynamic variables (temperature, density, energy, pressure) in the Fermi liquid sense. They constructed a temperature-independent combination of thermodynamic observables (scaled product of pressure and energy density versus energy density) to locate instabilities and possible phase transitions under the nonequilibrium "iso-nothing" conditions (non-isobaric, non-isochoric). The mean field was parameterized with a soft Skyrme potential, and the nonlocal kinetic equation included two-particle correlations beyond the Born approximation.

## Key numbers

- For Xe+Sn at 25 MeV per nucleon, the point of highest compression is reached around 60 fm/c with a temperature of 9 MeV.
- The nonlocal kinetic equation yields a temperature approximately 2 MeV higher than the local BUU result, attributed to conversion of two-particle correlation energy into kinetic energy.
- For Xe+Sn at 50 MeV per nucleon, trajectories come to rest outside the spinodal region in all thermodynamic plots, and no second minimum (spinodal) is observed.
- For Ni+Au at 25 MeV per nucleon, the surface emission instability is less pronounced while the spinodal instability is much more important, with even a third minimum appearing.
- The nonlocal corrections produce an observable effect in dynamical particle spectra of around 10%, with enhanced high-energy tails due to more energetic two-particle collisions in the early phase.

## Context

This work extends kinetic theory treatments of multifragmentation beyond standard BUU approaches (e.g., Landau equation treatments by PR87, DDM90, DRS91; BUU studies by KH94, ACC95) and Boltzmann-Langevin pictures (SASB90, AG90, RR90, ASBB92, ColBur93, CoCh94). The nonlocal kinetic equation used was derived by SLM96 and implemented numerically by MLSCN98. The paper connects to experimental observations: emulsion data by Schussler et al. (NINNP93) showing two fragment velocity groups (0.16c and 0.25c), and TAPS collaboration hard photon measurements (Sch97) indicating a transition between two mechanisms when bombarding energy exceeds 50-60 MeV. The approach enables prediction of which instability mechanism (surface compression denoted C, spinodal decomposition denoted S) dominates for specific reactions and energies, as summarized in the paper's table for systems including Ni+Au, Xe+Sn, Gd+U, and Ta+Au at 15-60 MeV.
