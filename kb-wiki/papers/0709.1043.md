# Signals of the QCD Phase Transition in the Heavens

> The modern phase diagram of strongly interacting matter reveals a rich structure at high-densities due to phase transitions related to the chiral symmetry of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) and the phenomenon of color superconductivity. These exotic phases have significant impacts on high-density astrophysics as the properties of neutron stars and the evolution of astrophysical systems as proto-neutron stars, core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers. Most recent pulsar mass measurements and constraints on neutron star radii are critically discussed. Astrophysical signals for exotic matter and phase transitions in high-density matter proposed recently in the literature are outlined. A strong first order phase transition leads to the emergence of a third family of compact stars besides white dwarfs and neutron stars. The different microphysics of quark matter results in an enhanced r-mode stability window for rotating compact stars compared to normal neutron stars. Future telescope and satellite data will allow to extract signals from phase transitions in dense matter in the heavens and will reveal properties of the phases of dense QCD. Spectral line profiles out of x-ray bursts will determine the mass-radius ratio of compact stars. Gravitational wave patterns from collapsing neutron stars or neutron star mergers will even be able to constrain the stiffness of the quark matter equation of state. Future astrophysical data can therefore provide a crucial cross-check to the exploration of the QCD phase diagram with the heavy-ion program of the CBM detector at the FAIR facility.

# Signals of the QCD Phase Transition in the Heavens

## Key claim
A strong first-order chiral phase transition in dense QCD matter can produce a third family of compact stars (hybrid stars with a pure quark matter core) alongside ordinary neutron stars and white dwarfs, but the exact location of the phase transition lines in the QCD phase diagram cannot be fixed in a model-independent way, so it remains unclear which color-superconducting phase is realized in nature, e.g., in the core of a neutron star.

## Method
The paper reviews the modern QCD phase diagram at high baryon densities, incorporating color-superconducting phases (2SC, gapless 2SC, CFL, gapless CFL, metallic CFL) derived from symmetry arguments. It examines the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff solutions for pure quark stars (MIT bag model and perturbative QCD to order α_s^2) and for hybrid stars with a hadronic outer layer and quark matter core. Astrophysical observables are surveyed: mass-radius relations from pulsar timing and x-ray burst spectroscopy, r-mode stability windows for rotating compact stars, gravitational wave signatures from neutron star mergers and collapses, and gamma-ray burst patterns from consecutive phase transitions.

## Key numbers
- Hulse-Taylor pulsar mass: M = (1.4414 ± 0.0002) M_⊙, uncertainty comparable to that of the gravitational constant G.
- Pulsar J0621+1002 mass: 1.69 M_⊙ with 68% confidence range 1.53 to 1.80 M_⊙ (Kasian 2007).
- Combined data from pulsars Terzan 5I, J, and B1516+02B give 99% probability that at least one neutron star has mass greater than 1.77 M_⊙ (Freire 2007).
- Pulsar J1748–2021B median mass: 2.73 M_⊙, with lower limit M > 2 M_⊙ at 99% confidence level (Freire 2007).
- X-ray burster EXO 0748–676: M ≥ 2.10 ± 0.28 M_⊙ and R ≥ 13.8 ± 1.8 km (Ozel 2006).
- Maximum mass of pure quark stars in MIT bag model: 2.1 M_⊙ with radius 12 km.
- Minimum neutron star mass: M ∼ 0.1 M_⊙ at R ∼ 200 km.
- 1122 Hz signal from low-mass x-ray binary XTE J1739 (Kaaret 2006).
- Pulsar J0437–4715 radius limited within 99.9% confidence to R > 6.7 km for assumed mass 1.4 M_⊙ (Bogdanov 2006).
- Double pulsar PSR J0737-303 mass M_A = 1.3381(7) M_⊙ (Kramer 2006).
- Adiabatic index for quark matter EoS in collapse simulations: Γ = 1.75 to 1.95 (Lin 2005, Yasutake 2007).

## Context
This review updates Schaffner-Bielich (2004) and extends the discussion of QCD phase transitions in astrophysics to include modern color-superconducting phases mapped by Ruster (2005) for β-equilibrium and neutrino-trapped matter. It critically assesses recent pulsar mass measurements (Stairs 2006 compilation) and the mass-radius constraints from Ozel (2006) for EXO 0748–676, showing that quark stars and hybrid stars are not ruled out by current data (Alford 2006). The work enables future cross-checks between astrophysical observations and the heavy-ion program of the CBM detector at FAIR, with specific predictions for gravitational wave patterns (Lin 2005, Yasutake 2007), r-mode stability windows (Drago 2007), and spectral line profiles from x-ray bursts (Strohmayer 2004, Bhattacharyya 2005) that could be tested by Constellation-X, LISA, and the Square Kilometre Array.
