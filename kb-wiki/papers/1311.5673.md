# Cosmic Separation of Phases: the microsecond universe and the neutron star

> It is entirely plausible under reasonable condition, that a first order QCD phase transition occurred from quarks to hadrons when the universe was about a microsecond old. Relics, if there be any, after the quark hadron phase transition are the most deciding signatures of the phase transition. It is shown in this paper that the quark nuggets, possible relics of first order QCD phase transitions with baryon number larger than $10^{43}$ will survive the entire history of the universe uptil now and can be considered as candidates for the cold dark matter. The spin down core of the neutron star on the high density low temperature end of the phase diagramme initiates transition from hadrons to quarks. As the star spins down, the size of the core goes on increasing. Recently discovered massive Pulsar PSRJ 1614-2230 with a mass of 1.97$\pm$0.04 $M_{\odot}$ most likely has a strongly interacting quark core. What possible observables can there be from these neutron stars?

# Cosmic Separation of Phases: the microsecond universe and the neutron star

## Key claim
Quark nuggets with baryon number larger than 10^43 can survive the entire history of the universe and serve as cold dark matter candidates, but only if the QCD phase transition in the early universe was first order rather than a crossover as suggested by lattice calculations. The massive pulsar PSRJ 1614-2230 most likely contains a strongly interacting quark core, and the spin-down of neutron stars may reveal the hadron-to-quark phase transition through deviations in the braking index.

## Method
The author reviews the theoretical framework for a first-order QCD phase transition in the early universe, invoking the "little inflation" scenario of Boeckel and Schaffner-Bielich (2012) to reconcile a large initial baryon asymmetry from the Affleck-Dine mechanism with the observed baryon asymmetry at CMB temperatures. For neutron stars, the author examines the spin-down behavior of rotating neutron stars where increasing central density drives a phase transition from hadronic matter to quark matter, using the braking index formalism of Glendenning (1997) to identify signatures of this transition.

## Key numbers
- Quark nuggets with baryon number N_B,in ≥ 10^43.25 survive forever; those with N_B,in ≤ 10^43.25 evaporate completely (assuming Hubble constant H_0 = 75 km s^−1 Mpc^−1 and a flat universe)
- The percolation time for quark nugget formation is t_P ≡ 27 μs
- The massive pulsar PSRJ 1614-2230 has a mass of 1.97 ± 0.04 M_⊙
- For strange quark nuggets with baryon number b_N = 10^42, the critical temperature for clumping is T_cl = 1.6 MeV, with N_N = 2.44 × 10^14 nuggets coalescing to a total mass of 0.24 M_⊙
- For b_N = 10^44, T_cl = 4.45 MeV, N_N = 1.23 × 10^11, total mass = 0.01 M_⊙
- For b_N = 10^46, T_cl = 20.6 MeV, N_N = 1.1 × 10^7, total mass = 0.0001 M_⊙
- The upper limit on baryon number of individual quark nuggets that would close the universe is N_B^QN ≤ 10^49
- Milky Way halo MACHOs are expected in the mass range 0.15–0.95 M_⊙, likely near 0.5 M_⊙

## Context
This work extends the seminal proposal by Witten (1984) that first-order QCD phase transitions produce stable strange quark nuggets, and builds on calculations by Bhattacharjee (1993) for nugget evaporation timescales and by Raha (2005), Alam (1999), and Bhattacharya (2000) for nugget survival and dark matter implications. The neutron star discussion connects to the work of Glendenning (1997) on braking indices, Alford et al. (2005, 2008) on quark matter equations of state, and Özel et al. (2010) on massive neutron stars. The paper enables future searches for quark nuggets as MACHOs and motivates studies of neutron star spin-down as a probe of the hadron-quark phase transition, potentially observable with CBM-type detectors.
