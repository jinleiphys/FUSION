# Quark degrees of freedom in hadronic systems

> Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is the theory of the strong interactions. We review descriptions of hadronic systems motivated by QCD, analyzing the recent controversy between gluonic and bosonic degrees of freedom under the prism of the Cheshire Cat Principle. Our analysis leads to an optimal scheme to study hadronic properties. We proceed to extend this low energy descriptions to the deep inelastic regime.

## Key claim

The paper argues that neither a purely gluonic nor a purely bosonic description of hadrons is natural; instead, hybrid models incorporating both quarks and pions (Goldstone bosons) are the most economical way to describe medium-energy hadronic physics, with the Cheshire Cat Principle serving as a quality-control check on the approximations used.

## Method

The author reviews QCD-motivated models of hadron structure, comparing the quark-gluon approach (e.g., the MIT Bag Model, non-relativistic quark models with one-gluon exchange) with chiral approaches that incorporate Goldstone bosons (e.g., the Chiral Bag Model, Chiral Quark Model). The Cheshire Cat Principle is applied to test whether physical observables are approximately independent of the bag radius, and the models are extended from static properties to deep inelastic scattering via operator product expansion and evolution equations.

## Key numbers

- The running coupling constant is given by α_S(Q²) = 12π / [(33 − 2n_f) log(Q²/Λ²)], where Λ introduces the scale for perturbative phenomena.
- The chiral symmetry breaking scale is Λ_χSB = 4π f_π, with f_π ≈ 93 MeV (pion decay constant).
- From hyperfine splitting, the strong coupling constant is estimated as α_s ≈ 0.3.
- The hadronic scale used for parton distribution evolution is μ_0 = 0.34 GeV².
- The confinement scale is approximately R ∼ 1/Λ_QCD, where Λ_QCD ≈ 100–300 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the author's prior work on the Cheshire Cat Principle (Rho, 1990s) and chiral quark models (Manohar and Georgi, 1984), and addresses the Isgur–Glozman debate on whether gluonic or bosonic degrees of freedom are more fundamental for hadron structure. The analysis enables future work using hybrid models to describe both low-energy static properties and high-energy deep inelastic scattering data, as demonstrated by the author's collaborations with Traini, Scopetta, and Gonzalez.
