# Analyses of multiplicity distributions and Bose-Einstein correlations at the LHC using negative binomial distribution and generalized Glauber-Lachs formula

> This study aims to analyze the data on multiplicity distributions and Bose-Einstein correlations collected at the LHC by the ATLAS and CMS Collaborations using a double-generalized Glauber-Lachs formula (D-GGL) and double-negative binomial distribution (D-NBD). From this investigation, it can be inferred that the D-GGL formula performs as effectively as the D-NBD. Moreover, our results show that the parameters estimated in multiplicity distributions (MD) (P(n)) are related to those contained in the BEC formula.

## Key claim

The double-generalized Glauber-Lachs (D-GGL) formula performs as effectively as the double-negative binomial distribution (D-NBD) for describing multiplicity distributions and Bose-Einstein correlations at LHC energies, with the caveat that the choice of better fit depends on the specific energy and dataset (e.g., D-GGL gives lower chi-squared at 7 TeV for ATLAS BEC data, while the conventional formula gives lower chi-squared at 0.9 TeV).

## Method

The authors fitted ATLAS and CMS multiplicity distribution data (with pseudorapidity intervals |eta| < 2.5 for ATLAS and |eta| < 2.4 for CMS, and transverse momentum cuts of p_t > 100 MeV for ATLAS and p_t > 0 GeV for CMS) using both the D-NBD formula (a weighted sum of two negative binomial distributions) and the D-GGL formula (a weighted sum of two generalized Glauber-Lachs distributions with fixed k_G = 2). They then used the parameters extracted from the multiplicity distribution fits to analyze Bose-Einstein correlation data from the same collaborations, employing the exponential form exp(-RQ) for the exchange function and including a long-range correlation term (1 + epsilon Q).

## Key numbers

- ATLAS multiplicity fits at sqrt(s) = 13 TeV: D-NBD gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 263/81 with alpha = 0.66, k1 = 2.00, <n1> = 44.2, k2 = 2.46, <n2> = 10.9; D-GGL gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 280/81 with alpha = 0.66, p1 = 0.90, <n1> = 44.1, p2 = 0.60, <n2> = 10.7
- ATLAS BEC fits at sqrt(s) = 7 TeV (n >= 2): D-NBD gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 743/75 with R1 = 1.8 fm, R2 = 3.1 fm; D-GGL gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 629/75 with R1 = 3.6 fm, R2 = 3.2 fm; conventional formula gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 919/75 with lambda_eff = 0.72, R = 2.1 fm
- CMS BEC fits at sqrt(s) = 7 TeV: D-NBD gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 691/194 with R1 = 1.6 fm, R2 = 3.2 fm; D-GGL gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 683/194 with R1 = 3.9 fm, R2 = 2.7 fm; conventional formula gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 738/194 with lambda_eff = 0.62, R = 1.9 fm
- ATLAS BEC at sqrt(s) = 0.9 TeV (n >= 2): D-GGL gives R1 = 2.8 fm, R2 = 2.7 fm; D-NBD gives R1 = 1.7 fm, R2 = 1.7 fm
- CMS multiplicity fits at sqrt(s) = 0.9 TeV: D-NBD gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 4.55/61 with alpha = 0.40, k1 = 5.72, <n1> = 29.6, k2 = 3.52, <n2> = 12.1; D-GGL gives chi-squared/n.d.f. = 3.95/61 with alpha = 0.57, p1 = 0.31, <n1> = 25.3, p2 = 0.29, <n2> = 10.6

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analyses using the NBD and GGL formula (Mizoguchi 2010) to LHC energies, and builds on the D-NBD approach originally proposed by Fuglesang (1989) to explain the violation of KNO scaling observed by the UA5 collaboration at sqrt(s) = 900 GeV. The results enable concurrent analysis of multiplicity distributions and Bose-Einstein correlations using a unified two-component framework, and suggest that the interaction range R increases with multiplicity (contrary to the ATLAS collaboration's claim of saturation), while also providing a comparison with the triple-NBD approach of Zborovsky (2013).
