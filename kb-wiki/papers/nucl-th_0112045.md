# The canonical effect in statistical models for relativistic heavy ion collisions

> Enforcing exact conservation laws instead of average ones in statistical thermal models for relativistic heavy ion reactions gives raise to so called canonical effect, which can be used to explain some enhancement effects when going from elementary (e.g. pp) or small (pA) systems towards large AA systems. We review the recently developed method for computation of canonical statistical thermodynamics, and give an insight when this is needed in analysis of experimental data.

# The canonical effect in statistical models for relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim
Enforcing exact conservation laws (canonical ensemble) instead of average ones (grand canonical) in statistical thermal models can explain enhancement effects when moving from small systems like pp or pA to large AA systems, but the need for full canonical treatment depends on collision energy and system size; at SPS energies the strangeness-canonical approximation overestimates the canonical effect compared to the full baryon-strangeness-charge canonical calculation.

## Method
The authors review a group-theoretical projection method that transforms the grand canonical partition function into a canonical one by integrating over U(1) group parameters for each conserved charge (baryon number, strangeness, electric charge). They eliminate the baryon integration analytically, reducing the triple integral to a double integral that can be computed numerically for baryon numbers up to O(100), whereas previous methods were limited to B ~ 20. Mean particle numbers are obtained by taking derivatives of the canonical partition function with respect to fictitious fugacities, yielding chemical factors that replace the grand canonical fugacities.

## Key numbers
- Full canonical calculations are now possible up to baryon number O(100), whereas old methods could only reach B ~ 20 with very long computation times (Cleymans 1997).
- At GSI energies (1.7 GeV per nucleon in SIS AuAu collisions), the relative difference between full canonical and strangeness-canonical results for K+/N_part is 13% for B=2, decreasing to 4% for B=10 and to 1% for B=40.
- At AGS energies (11.6 A GeV momentum), chemical freeze-out temperature is around T=120 MeV with baryon density close to normal nuclear density; from B=2 to B=60 the full canonical and strangeness-canonical results are essentially the same.
- At SPS energies (158 A GeV Pb-Pb reactions), the best fit temperature is T ~ 160 MeV; calculations use baryon density 0.3 fm^-3 to probe the largest reasonable canonical effect.

## Context
This work extends the canonical statistical model approach developed by Cleymans (1997), Redlich (1998), and Kebe (2000) for relativistic heavy ion collisions, where exact charge conservation replaces grand canonical fugacities. The authors compare three levels of approximation: full canonical (B,S,Q), strangeness-canonical (S canonical with B,Q grand canonical), and grand canonical, using thermal parameters from their previous work (Becattini 2001). The method enables systematic assessment of when full canonical treatment is needed versus simpler approximations, with implications for analyzing data from GSI SIS, AGS, and SPS experiments.
