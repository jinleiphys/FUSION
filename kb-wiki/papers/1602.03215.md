# Effect of topological defects on "nuclear pasta" observables

> [Background] The "pasta" phase of nuclear matter may play an important role in the structure and evolution of neutron stars. Recent works suggest nuclear pasta has a high resistivity which could be explained by the presence of long lived topological defects. The defects act as impurities that decrease thermal and electrical conductivity of the pasta. [Purpose] To quantify how topological defects affect transport properties of nuclear pasta and estimate this effect using an impurity parameter $Q_{\text{imp}}$. [Methods] Contrast molecular dynamics simulations of up to 409\,600 nucleons arranged in parallel nuclear pasta slabs (perfect pasta) with simulations of pasta slabs connected by topological defects (impure pasta). From these simulations compare the viscosity and heat conductivity of perfect and impure pasta to obtain an effective impurity parameter $Q_{\text{imp}}$ due to the presence of defects. [Results] Both the viscosity and thermal conductivity calculated for both perfect and impure pasta are anisotropic, peaking along directions perpendicular to the slabs and reaching a minimum close to zero parallel to them. In our 409\,600 nucleon simulation topological defects connecting slabs of pasta reduce both the thermal conductivity and viscosity on average by about 37\%. We estimate an effective impurity parameter due to the defects of order $Q_{\text{imp}}\sim30$. [Conclusions] Topological defects in the pasta phase of nuclear matter have an effect similar to impurities in a crystal lattice. The irregularities introduced by the defects reduce the thermal and electrical conductivities and the viscosity of the system. This effect can be parameterized by a large impurity parameter $Q_{\text{imp}}\sim30$.

## Key claim

Topological defects in nuclear pasta reduce thermal conductivity and viscosity by about 37% on average in the 409,600 nucleon simulation, but this reduction is highly anisotropic: it is much stronger for directions perpendicular to the pasta slabs (factors of 5 to 20) than for directions parallel to them (about 30%). The effective impurity parameter due to defects is estimated as \(Q_{\text{imp}}\sim30\), but the authors caution that finite-size effects may cause this to be an underestimate.

## Method

The authors performed classical molecular dynamics simulations of up to 409,600 nucleons interacting via a central two-body potential fitted to nuclear properties, with a background electron gas providing screening. They compared "perfect pasta" (parallel slabs obtained by applying and then removing an external sinusoidal potential) with "impure pasta" (slabs connected by topological defects, obtained from random initial configurations) at density \(n=0.050\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\), proton fraction \(Y_p=0.40\), and temperature \(T=1\ \mathrm{MeV}\). From the proton structure factor \(S_p(\boldsymbol{q})\) they computed Coulomb logarithms and then derived the shear viscosity \(\eta\) and thermal conductivity \(\kappa\) as functions of scattering angle relative to the slab normal.

## Key numbers

- Thermal conductivity and viscosity are reduced on average by **37%** in the 409,600 nucleon simulation with topological defects compared to perfect pasta.
- The effective impurity parameter due to defects is estimated as \(Q_{\text{imp}}\sim30\).
- Average thermal conductivity \(\bar{\kappa}\) for perfect pasta: \(6.66\times10^3\ k_B\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}\); for impure pasta: \(4.15\times10^3\ k_B\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}\).
- Average viscosity \(\bar{\eta}\) for perfect pasta: \(87.7\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\); for impure pasta: \(55.5\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\).
- Average effective charge \(\bar{Z}^*\) for perfect pasta: 5.5; for impure pasta: 50.2.
- Fermi temperature \(T_F=166\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and Fermi momentum \(k_F=0.84\ \mathrm{fm}^{-1}\) for the electron densities considered.
- The reduction in thermal conductivity along directions nearly normal to the slabs (\(0\degree\leq\theta\leq10\degree\)) is a factor of **5 to 20**; along directions nearly parallel (\(60\degree\lesssim\theta\leq90\degree\)) it is about **30%**.
- The screening length was set to \(\lambda=10\ \mathrm{fm}\), slightly smaller than the value for non-interacting relativistic electrons (\(12.35\ \mathrm{fm}\)).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous qualitative estimate of topological defects as impurities in nuclear pasta (Schneider et al. 2015, Phys. Rev. Lett. 114, 031102) by providing quantitative calculations of transport properties and an effective impurity parameter. It builds on the formalism of Horowitz and Berry (2008, Phys. Rev. C 78, 035806) for computing pasta observables from structure factors, and on the impurity parameter framework of Itoh and Kohyama (1993) and the molecular dynamics studies of correlated impurities by Horowitz et al. (2009, Phys. Rev. E 79, 026103) and Daligault and Gupta (2009, Astrophys. J. 703, 994). The results enable more realistic modeling of thermal and electrical transport in neutron star crusts, particularly for interpreting cooling curves of accreting neutron stars such as LMXB 1659-29, though the authors note that larger simulations are needed to confirm that finite-size effects do not underestimate defect density.
