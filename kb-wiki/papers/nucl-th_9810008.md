# Multifragmentation in Xe(50A MeV)+Sn Confrontation of theory and data

> We compare in detail central collisions Xe(50A MeV) + Sn, recently measured by the INDRA collaboration, with the Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) model in order to identify the reaction mechanism which leads to multifragmentation. We find that QMD describes the data quite well, in the projectile/target region as well as in the midrapidity zone where also statistical models can be and have been employed. The agreement between QMD and data allows to use this dynamical model to investigate the reaction in detail. We arrive at the following observations: a) the in medium nucleon nucleon cross section is not significantly different from the free cross section, b) even the most central collisions have a binary character, c) most of the fragments are produced in the central collisions and d) the simulations as well as the data show a strong attractive in-plane flow resembling deep inelastic collisions e) at midrapidity the results from QMD and those from statistical model calculations agree for almost all observables with the exception of ${d^2 σ\over dZdE}$. This renders it difficult to extract the reaction mechanism from midrapidity fragments only. According to the simulations the reaction shows a very early formation of fragments, even in central collisions, which pass through the reaction zone without being destroyed. The final transverse momentum of the fragments is very close to the initial one and due to the Fermi motion. A heating up of the systems is not observed and hence a thermal origin of the spectra cannot be confirmed.

## Key claim

The Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) model reproduces the INDRA data for central Xe(50A MeV)+Sn collisions quite well, but the agreement between QMD and statistical model calculations for midrapidity observables (except the double-differential cross section d²σ/dZdE) makes it difficult to extract the reaction mechanism from midrapidity fragments alone; the simulations suggest fragments form very early and retain their initial Fermi momentum, with no thermal heating of the system observed.

## Method

The authors compared central collisions of Xe(50A MeV) + Sn measured by the INDRA 4π detector with QMD simulations using a soft equation of state and a database of about 60,000 events over a large impact parameter range. They applied an experimental filter to the QMD events to account for detector acceptance, energy thresholds, and particle identification, then selected central events using a cut on total transverse energy of light charged particles (E_trans ≥ 450 MeV). The analysis separated midrapidity (60° ≤ θ_CM ≤ 120°) from forward/backward emission and compared charge distributions, multiplicity distributions, angular distributions, and energy spectra between filtered QMD, INDRA data, and statistical model (SMM) calculations.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 50A MeV for Xe projectile on Sn target
- Central event selection cut: E_trans ≥ 450 MeV, corresponding to average impact parameter 3.6 fm in QMD simulations
- Average IMF multiplicity: 6.6 for INDRA data, 5.3 for filtered QMD, 7.5 for unfiltered QMD
- Mean kinetic energy/slope of "protonlike" particles: 20 MeV / 10.5 MeV for filtered QMD, 16 MeV / 12.2 MeV for INDRA data
- Fragment multiplicity in QMD simulations is about 30% too low compared to experiment
- Biggest fragments lose about 35% of their initial velocity in both data and simulations
- QMD reproduces charge distribution well up to Z ≈ 15 but overestimates production of larger fragments

## Context

This work extends the comparison of dynamical and statistical approaches to multifragmentation at intermediate energies (30–150A MeV), where the Fermi spheres of projectile and target become separated and stopping power reaches a minimum. The authors note that previous work by Bonasera et al. (1995) and others applied statistical models like SMM to similar reactions, while the QMD approach of Aichelin and Hartnack provides a dynamical alternative. The results confirm the theoretical prediction of minimum stopping power at these energies and show that midrapidity charge distributions and azimuthal distributions cannot distinguish between thermal (SMM) and dynamical (QMD) mechanisms, though the double-differential cross section d²σ/dZdE remains a discriminating observable. The findings enable future studies to focus on more exclusive observables and many-body correlations rather than inclusive spectra.
