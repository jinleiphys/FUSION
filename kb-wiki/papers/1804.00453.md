# Pulsars as Weber gravitational wave detectors

> A gravitational wave passing through a pulsar will lead to a variation in the moment of inertia of the pulsar affecting its rotation. This will affect the extremely accurately measured spin rate of the pulsar as well as its pulse profile (due to induced wobbling depending on the source direction). The effect will be most pronounced at resonance and should be detectable by accurate observations of the pulsar signal. The pulsar, in this sense, acts as a remotely stationed Weber detector of gravitational waves whose signal can be monitored on earth. With possible gravitational wave sources spread around in the universe, pulsars in their neighborhoods can provide us a family of \textit{remote} detectors all of which can be monitored on earth. Even if GW are detected directly by earth based conventional detectors, such pulsar detectors can provide additional information for accurate determination of the source location. This can be of crucial importance for sources which do not emit any other form of radiation such as black hole mergers. For the gravitational wave events already detected by LIGO (and Virgo), our proposal suggests that one should look for specific pulsars which would have been disturbed by these events, and will transmit this disturbance via their pulse signals in any foreseeable future. If these future pulsar events can be predicted with accuracy then a focused effort can be made to detect any possible changes in the signals of those specific pulsars.

## Key claim

A gravitational wave passing through a pulsar will induce a change in the pulsar's moment of inertia, altering its spin rate and pulse profile; the effect is most pronounced at resonance when the GW frequency matches a natural frequency of the neutron star, and the resulting signal should be detectable by precision pulsar timing observations.

## Method

The authors model the neutron star as an initially spherical object that undergoes ellipsoidal deformation due to the tidal field of a passing gravitational wave. They relate the change in the star's quadrupole moment to the GW strain amplitude using the tidal deformability parameter, which depends on the second Love number and the neutron star radius. From the quadrupole moment change, they derive the fractional change in the moment of inertia tensor and equate this to the fractional change in the pulsar's spin rate. They then estimate the detectability of this spin-rate change for a binary neutron star merger source located one light-year from a pulsar detector.

## Key numbers

- For a binary neutron star merger with peak GW frequency about 1 kHz, using a neutron star mass of 1.0 solar masses and radius 10 km, with Love number k2 = 0.1, the fractional change in moment of inertia is Delta I / I = 10^(-2) h, where h is the GW strain amplitude.
- For the BNS merger detected by LIGO-Virgo with peak strain h = 10^(-19) at a distance of about 130 million light years, if a pulsar detector is one light year from the source, the strain at the pulsar is enhanced to h = 10^(-11), giving a fractional spin-rate change Delta nu / nu = 10^(-13).
- Certain millisecond pulsars have pulse timings measured to an accuracy of about 10^(-15) seconds.
- For a source in the Virgo cluster at 50-60 million light years, the expected strain is h = 10^(-18) to 10^(-19), leading to a fractional spin-rate change of 10^(-20) to 10^(-21), which is too small for current pulsar timing accuracy (best fractional change measurement about 10^(-13)) without resonance enhancement.

## Context

This proposal extends the concept of Weber bar gravitational wave detectors to pulsars as remotely stationed detectors, fundamentally differing from pulsar timing arrays (PTA) which monitor gradual changes in pulse arrival times from very low frequency GW sources below 10^(-6) Hz. The authors note that Dyson (long ago) proposed using Earth itself as a GW detector via seismometers, and more recent proposals have suggested detecting GW effects on solar acoustic modes (helioseismology and astroseismology). The present work enables a new method for GW source localization, particularly for black hole mergers that emit no electromagnetic radiation, and suggests revisiting already-detected LIGO-Virgo events by searching for disturbed pulsars whose signals may show future changes.
