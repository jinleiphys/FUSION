# Simple model for decay of superdeformed nuclei

> Recent theoretical investigations of the decay mechanism out of a superdeformed nuclear band have yielded qualitatively different results, depending on the relative values of the relevant decay widths. We present a simple two-level model for the dynamics of the tunneling between the superdeformed and normal-deformed bands, which treats decay and tunneling processes on an equal footing. The previous theoretical results are shown to correspond to coherent and incoherent limits of the full tunneling dynamics. Our model accounts for experimental data in both the A~150 mass region, where the tunneling dynamics is coherent, and in the A~190 mass region, where the tunneling dynamics is incoherent.

## Key claim

The paper presents a simple two-level model that unifies two previously contradictory theoretical approaches to superdeformed band decay, showing that the earlier results correspond to coherent and incoherent limits of the full tunneling dynamics, and that the appropriate expression for the spreading width depends on the decay widths of both wells rather than the level spacing of normal-deformed states.

## Method

The authors construct an effective two-level system consisting of a superdeformed state S and the normal-deformed state N to which it couples most strongly, described by a Hamiltonian that includes tunneling between the two states and electromagnetic decay widths within each band. They calculate the retarded Green's function for this system via Dyson's equation, using a self-energy matrix with imaginary components representing the decay widths, and derive the time-dependent probabilities for the nucleus to be in each state as well as the integrated branching ratio for decay out of the superdeformed band.

## Context

This work resolves a contradiction between two previous theoretical approaches to superdeformed decay: the statistical model of Vigezzi, Broglia, and Dossing (1990) and the spreading-width approach of Weidenmuller, von Brentano, and Barrett (1998). The model accounts for experimental data in both the A~150 mass region (where tunneling is coherent, with $\Gamma^{\downarrow} \gg \Gamma_S, \Gamma_N$) and the A~190 mass region (where tunneling is incoherent, with $\Gamma^{\downarrow}, \Gamma_S \ll \Gamma_N$). The results enable direct comparison of the branching ratio formula with individual nuclei rather than ensemble averages, and the authors note that recent work by Aberg (1999) on chaotic enhancement of tunneling is not inconsistent with their two-level model.
