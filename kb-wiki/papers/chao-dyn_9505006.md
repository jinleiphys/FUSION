# Universal Behavior of Lyapunov Exponents in Unstable Systems

> We calculate the Lyapunov exponents in a classical molecular dynamics framework. The system is composed of few hundreds particles interacting either through Yukawa (Nuclear) or Slater-Kirkwood (Atomic) forces. The forces are chosen to give an Equation of State that resembles the nuclear and the atomic $^4He$ Equation Of State respectively near the critical point for liquid-gas phase transition. We find the largest fluctuations for an initial "critical temperature". The largest Lyapunov exponents $λ$ are always positive and can be very well fitted near this "critical temperature" with a functional form $λ\propto |T-T_c|^{-ω}$, where the exponent $ω=0.15$ is independent of the system and mass number. At smaller temperatures we find that $λ\propto T~ ^{0.4498}$, a universal behavior characteristic of an order to chaos transition.

# Universal Behavior of Lyapunov Exponents in Unstable Systems

## Key claim
The largest Lyapunov exponents in classical molecular dynamics simulations of fragmenting systems (nuclei and atomic helium) follow universal scaling laws near the critical temperature for the liquid-gas phase transition, with the exponent ω = 0.15 ± 0.04 in the relation λ ∝ |T − T_c|^(−ω), independent of the system and mass number. At lower temperatures, the exponents scale as λ ∝ T^(0.4498), consistent with a transition from order to chaos characterized by the Feigenbaum constant δ = 4.669.

## Method
The authors performed classical molecular dynamics simulations of 100 particles (with tests up to 400 particles) interacting through Yukawa potentials for the nuclear system (neutrons and protons) and Slater-Kirkwood potentials for atomic ⁴He. The systems were prepared in their ground state, then given initial momenta according to a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution at temperature T, and the largest Lyapunov exponents were extracted from the exponential growth of the phase-space distance between initially close trajectories, averaging over 100 events per temperature with 10 perturbed trajectories per event.

## Key numbers
- Largest Lyapunov exponent maximum at T ∼ 4.5 MeV for the nuclear system and at T ∼ 21.8 K for the atomic helium system
- Critical exponent ω = 0.15 ± 0.04 in the fit λ ∝ |T − T_c|^(−ω) near the critical temperature
- Scaled critical temperature T_c/|E_b| ∼ 0.43 for both systems (binding energy E_b = 10.5 MeV for nuclei, 50.5 K for ⁴He)
- Low-temperature scaling exponent 0.4498, derived from ln 2 / ln δ with Feigenbaum constant δ = 4.669
- Ground state density 0.16 fm^(−3) and binding energy −16 MeV/nucleon for infinite nuclear system; −10.5 MeV/nucleon for finite systems without Coulomb
- Compressibility K = 200 MeV for soft EOS (K = 380 MeV for stiff EOS gives same results)
- Typical scaling frequency λ_0 = 0.155 c/fm for nuclear case; λ_0 = 8.82 × 10^(−5) α c / a_0 for atomic case
- Lyapunov exponents independent of system mass from 50 to 400 particles
- Uncertainties in Lyapunov exponents approximately 10%

## Context
This work extends the classical molecular dynamics studies of multifragmentation by Latora et al. (1994), Bonasera et al. (1995), and Belkacem et al. (1995), who found power-law mass distributions with exponent τ = 2.23 and critical exponents γ = 0.86, β = 0.33 near the liquid-gas phase transition. It provides the first estimate of Lyapunov exponents for systems exhibiting critical behavior, going beyond the mean-field approximation used by Bai (1995) for two-dimensional systems in the spinodal region. The results enable connecting thermodynamic phase transitions to dynamical chaos indicators, suggesting that Lyapunov exponents can serve as a signature of criticality in finite systems such as those produced in heavy-ion collisions and atomic clusters.
