# Partial Rate Matrix for Dark Matter Scattering

> I present a highly efficient integration method for scattering calculations, and a ``partial rate matrix'' that encodes the scattering rate as a function of the $SO(3)$ orientation of the detector. This replaces the original multidimensional rate integral with a simple exercise in vector multiplication, speeding up the rate calculation by a factor of around $10^8$. I include a scheme to fully factorize the dark matter particle model, its astrophysical velocity distribution, and the properties of the target material from each other, enabling efficient calculation of the partial rate matrix even in studies comparing large sets of these input functions. This is now the only sensible way to evaluate the dark matter scattering rate in anisotropic detector materials. It is straightforward to generalize this method to other difficult but linear problems.

## Key claim

The paper presents a "vector space integration" method that factorizes the dark matter scattering rate calculation into separate projections of the astrophysical velocity distribution, the dark matter particle model, and the target material properties onto orthogonal basis functions, replacing the original multidimensional rate integral with simple vector multiplication and achieving a speedup factor of around 10^8 for anisotropic detector materials with many orientations.

## Method

The author projects the dark matter velocity distribution g_χ(v) and the material-specific form factor f_S^2(q, E) onto bases of spherical harmonics combined with radial orthogonal functions (spherical wavelets), representing each as a vector of inner product coefficients. The scattering operator, which depends on the dark matter mass m_χ and form factor F_DM, is precomputed as a matrix that is diagonal in the angular momentum indices ℓ and m due to rotational invariance, and the scattering rate for any detector orientation is obtained by applying Wigner D-matrices to the partial rate matrix K^(ℓ)_mm' via simple matrix multiplication.

## Key numbers

- Speedup factor of approximately 10^8 for anisotropic detectors in the limit of large number of detector orientations N_R, cited from Lillard (2023)
- Typical evaluation time for a single numerical integral T_num.int. ~ 600 seconds for analytic models, compared to T_Tr ~ ℓ_max^3 · 10^(-9) seconds for the vector trace evaluation
- Projection time T_proj. ~ a few seconds per function, matrix element evaluation time T_I ~ 10^(-3) seconds, and matrix assembly time T_K ~ ℓ_max^3 · 10^(-5) seconds
- With N=10^4 basis coefficients, the basis expansion reproduces the original function f_S^2(q) within ~0.1% error for a particle-in-a-box model with dimensions (L_x, L_y, L_z) = (4, 7, 10) × (α m_e)^(-1)

## Context

This work extends the standard direct detection rate calculation formalism of Lewin (1996) and Essig (2012, 2016) by solving the computational scaling problem where the rate must be evaluated for N_R detector orientations, N_gχ velocity distributions, N_DM particle models, and N_fS material models simultaneously. The method enables practical statistical analyses that account for uncertainties in the dark matter velocity distribution (e.g., from unvirialized streams as studied by Diemand (2008) and Necib (2019)) and in material form factors, and it allows rapid determination of optimal detector orientations for anisotropic materials such as those studied by Hochberg (2016), Blanco (2021), and Catena (2023).
