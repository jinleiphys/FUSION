# Leveraging neural control variates for enhanced precision in lattice field theory

> Results obtained with stochastic methods have an inherent uncertainty due to the finite number of samples that can be achieved in practice. In lattice QCD this problem is particularly salient in some observables like, for instance, observables involving one or more baryons and it is the main problem preventing the calculation of nuclear forces from first principles. The method of control variables has been used extensively in statistics and it amounts to computing the expectation value of the difference between the observable of interest and another observable whose average is known to be zero but is correlated with the observable of interest. Recently, control variates methods emerged as a promising solution in the context of lattice field theories. In our current study, instead of relying on an educated guess to determine the control variate, we utilize a neural network to parametrize this function. Using 1+1 dimensional scalar field theory as a testbed, we demonstrate that this neural network approach yields substantial improvements. Notably, our findings indicate that the neural network ansatz is particularly effective in the strong coupling regime.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that neural-network-parametrized control variates can substantially reduce variance in lattice field theory calculations, but the improvement is contingent on the coupling strength and lattice size, with the method being particularly effective in the strong coupling regime where linear control variates fail.

## Method

The authors construct control variates using Stein's identity, which guarantees zero expectation value, and parametrize the underlying function \(g[\phi]\) with a feed-forward neural network. The network is trained by minimizing a loss function that approximates the variance of the observable minus the control variate, using stochastic gradient descent with the ADAM optimizer on a single sample per step. Translational symmetry is imposed by defining the network output from a single function \(g_0\) applied to translated field configurations, and parity symmetry is enforced by using an odd activation function (arcsinh) and removing bias terms.

## Key numbers

* On a \(20 \times 20\) lattice with \(\lambda = 0.5\) (weak coupling), the ratio of standard deviations \(\sigma_{\text{Raw}}/\sigma_{\text{CV}}\) reached approximately 100 after training with \(10^4\) samples for the mid-lattice correlator at \(t = L_0/2 = 10\).
* On a \(20 \times 20\) lattice with \(\lambda = 24.0\) (strong coupling), the ratio \(\sigma_{\text{Raw}}/\sigma_{\text{CV}}\) reached approximately 10 after training with \(10^3\) samples, while a linear control variate (no hidden layers) showed essentially no improvement.
* On a \(40 \times 10\) lattice with \(m^2 = 0.01\) and \(\lambda = 0.1\), using a control variate optimized at \(t = L_0/2 = 20\) reduced the uncertainty of the mass estimate from 0.1935(40) to 0.1920(13), a factor of approximately 3 improvement, corresponding to a configuration set roughly 9 times larger.
* Using transfer training to optimize control variates at every time slice on the \(40 \times 10\) lattice yielded approximately 20 times improvement in standard deviation at every point, with the mass estimate improving to 0.1938(2) from \(2 \times 10^3\) samples, consistent with the raw result from \(2 \times 10^5\) samples (0.1940(4)).

## Context

This work extends the control variates approach of Bhattacharya (2023), who used a linear combination of optimal control variates for a free field theory, by replacing the linear ansatz with a neural network parametrization. The method is tested on the same 1+1 dimensional scalar field theory used as a testbed by Detmold (2018, 2020) and Lawrence (2022) for signal-to-noise and sign problem approaches. The results enable more precise extraction of masses from lattice correlators with limited statistics, which is directly relevant to the signal-to-noise problem in lattice QCD that prevents first-principles calculations of nuclear forces as described by Parisi (1983) and Lepage (1989).
