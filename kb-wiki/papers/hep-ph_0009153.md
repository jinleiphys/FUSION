# Tools for RHIC: Review of Models

> We discuss the present status of microscopic models for RHIC, with an emphasis on models being realized via the Monte Carlo technique. This review is to a large extent based on the OSCAR3 workshop, where general concepts and new trends in this field have been discussed.

# Tools for RHIC: Review of Models

## Key claim
Microscopic Monte Carlo event generators for RHIC heavy ion collisions should be defined by equations (an effective theory) rather than by algorithms, so that the computer code becomes a rigorous solution of those equations; however, the paper is a review and does not itself present a new model or result.

## Method
The paper reviews the status of microscopic models for RHIC, based largely on discussions at the OSCAR3 workshop. It surveys the different stages of heavy ion collisions, primary interaction, pre-equilibrium, equilibrium, and post-equilibrium, and evaluates the theoretical approaches applicable to each stage, including Gribov-Regge theory, parton cascades, hydrodynamics, and hadronic transport. The review emphasizes the need for modular, equation-based event generators and critiques common practices such as longitudinal excitation models and inconsistent energy sharing in Gribov-Regge theory.

## Key numbers
- At RHIC, the longitudinal size of nuclei due to the gamma factor is of the order 0.1 fm.
- The relaxation time for parton equilibration peaks around 1.5 fm, with the LHC value being somewhat bigger than the RHIC one.
- In Gribov-Regge theory, ignoring energy conservation roughly doubles the width of the distribution of the number of multiple scatterings.

## Context
This review extends discussions from the OSCAR workshop series (first two workshops at Brookhaven in 1997 and 1999, third in Nantes in 2000) and surveys models such as HIJING (Wang 1996), VENUS (Werner 1993), QGS (Kaidalov 1982), DPM (Capella 1994), UrQMD (Bass 1998), ART (Li 1995), and JAM (Nara 1997). It critiques the common practice of extending models developed for one aspect of the collision to cover all stages, and advocates for modular combinations such as hydrodynamics plus UrQMD (Bass 2000) or neXus plus SPheRIO (Aguiar 2000). The review enables future work by identifying open questions, particularly the need for consistent energy sharing in Gribov-Regge theory and for rigorous testing of cascade algorithms as solutions of transport equations.
