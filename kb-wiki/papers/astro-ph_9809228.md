# How Well Do We Know the Beta-Decay of N16 and Oxygen Formation in Helium Burning?

## Key claim

Contrary to claims that the problem has been solved, the astrophysical E1 S-factor of the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction is not yet well known, and a small S-factor solution [S_E1(300) less than 20 keV-b] cannot be ruled out. The authors dispute both the alteration of the Mainz (1971) beta-delayed alpha-particle emission data performed by the TRIUMF collaboration and the justification for that recalibration.

## Method

The authors compare the unaltered Mainz (1971) data on beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of 16N with data from TRIUMF (1994), Seattle (1995), and Yale-UConn (1996), using R-Matrix fitted curves. They examine the TRIUMF collaboration's recalibration of the Mainz spectrum, which claimed a different energy dispersion (10.45 keV/ch instead of the original 10.60 keV/ch), and test whether the centroid of the 2+ state can be accurately extracted from the Mainz data by fitting a fourth-order polynomial background and a gaussian with centroid fixed at the expected energy (E_alpha = 2.0115 MeV).

## Key numbers

- The original Mainz (1971) calibration used a 10B(n,alpha)7Li procedure resulting in an energy uncertainty of ±10 keV.
- The TRIUMF recalibration changed the energy dispersion from 10.60 keV/ch to 10.45 keV/ch, with differences ranging from 6.5 keV at the low end to 18 keV at higher energies.
- The TRIUMF collaboration claims to extract a centroid with ±2 keV precision, a factor of 5 better than the original ±10 keV calibration, using data with a signal-to-background ratio of approximately 1% above background compared to the original calibration's signal-to-noise ratio of 12:1.
- The background fit to the Mainz data gave chi-squared per degree of freedom of 0.7 for channels 92-100 and 5.2 for channels 110-114.
- The extracted centroid of the 2+ state from the Mainz data ranges from channel 105.2 to 107.5, depending on background choice.

## Context

This work extends the debate over the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction rate, a key nuclear physics input for stellar helium burning and nucleosynthesis. The authors argue that the TRIUMF collaboration (Azuma 1994, 1997; Buchmann 1993) incorrectly recalibrated the Mainz data (Hattig 1969, 1970; Neubeck 1974), and that the unaltered Mainz data agree with both the Seattle (Zhao 1995) and Yale-UConn (France 1997) measurements. The paper enables future work to re-evaluate the p-wave astrophysical S-factor, noting that the 16N spectrum alone cannot determine whether interference between bound and quasi-bound 1- states is constructive or destructive, and cannot rule out a small S-factor solution below 20 keV-b (Hale 1997).
