# A simple explanation of the absence of the spherical nuclei with the hidden-color states

> Inspired by the EMC effect, the Cd puzzle and the SU3-IBM, a hypothesis can be given for a nucleus, that only the nucleus itself is a trivial (0,0) representation of the SU(3) group, which leads to the simple conclusion that spherical nucleus does not exist and the spherical mean field is not allowed. The key conclusion is that the whole nucleus should have many hidden-color states, and the quark-gluon degrees of freedom are required even at the low-energy excitation of the nucleus.

## Key claim

The paper hypothesizes that only the whole nucleus itself is a trivial (0,0) representation of the SU(3) group, which implies that no part of the nucleus (including the mean field and long-range correlations) can be the (0,0) representation. This leads to the conclusion that spherical nuclei do not exist and that hidden-color states involving quark-gluon degrees of freedom are required even for low-energy nuclear excitations.

## Method

The author combines the EMC effect, the Cd puzzle, and the SU3-IBM (extended interacting boson model with SU(3) higher-order interactions) to argue that the color SU(3) symmetry of quantum chromodynamics must be linked to the SU(3) symmetry observed in nuclear collective behavior. The argument uses group-theoretic reasoning: if the nucleus as a whole is a color-singlet (0,0) representation, then the product of the SU(3) representations of all nucleons must equal (0,0), but individual nucleons or their intermediate couplings cannot themselves be (0,0) representations, implying hidden-color states exist throughout the nucleus.

## Context

This work extends the Cd puzzle findings of Heyde and Wood (2011, 2016) and Garrett, Wood, and Yates (2018), which refuted the traditional spherical phonon vibrational interpretation of Cd nuclei. It builds on the author's own SU3-IBM framework (Wang 2022, 2024) that explained the Cd puzzle using SU(3) symmetry. The paper connects these low-energy nuclear structure results to the EMC effect (Arneodo 1994, Hen 2017) and prior discussions of hidden-color states in few-nucleon systems by Harvey (1981), Ji (1986), and Miller (2014, 2019, 2020). It enables a new theoretical framework where quark-gluon degrees of freedom must be considered even in low-energy nuclear structure, challenging the traditional separation of scales between high-energy QCD and low-energy nuclear physics.
