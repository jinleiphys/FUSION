# Equation of State and Progenitor Dependence of Stellar-Mass Black-Hole Formation

> The core collapse of a massive star results in the formation of a proto-neutron star (PNS). If enough material is accreted onto a PNS it will become gravitationally unstable and further collapse into a black-hole (BH). We perform a systematic study of failing core-collapse supernovae in spherical symmetry for a wide range of presupernova progenitor stars and equations of state (EOSs) of nuclear matter. We analyze how variations in progenitor structure and the EOS of dense matter above nuclear saturation density affect the PNS evolution and subsequent BH formation. Comparisons of core-collapse for a given progenitor star and different EOSs show that the path traced by the PNS in mass-entropy phase space $M_{\mathrm{grav}}^{\mathrm{PNS}}-\tilde{s}$ is well correlated with the progenitor compactness and almost EOS independent, apart from the final endpoint. Furthermore, BH formation occurs, to a very good approximation, soon after the PNS overcomes the maximum \textit{gravitational} mass supported by a hot NS with constant entropy equal to $\tilde{s}$. These results show a path to constraining the temperature dependence of the EOS through the detection of neutrinos from a failed galactic supernova.

## Key claim
The path traced by the proto-neutron star in mass-entropy phase space is well correlated with progenitor compactness and almost independent of the equation of state, apart from the final endpoint; black-hole formation occurs soon after the PNS exceeds the maximum gravitational mass supported by a hot neutron star with constant entropy equal to the most common entropy value within the PNS.

## Method
The authors performed spherically symmetric core-collapse supernova simulations using the FLASH code with a general relativistic effective potential and energy-dependent two-moment neutrino transport. They simulated 51 non-rotating presupernova progenitors from Woosley (2002), Woosley (2007), and Sukhbold (2018) using 49 Skyrme-type equations of state from Schneider (2019), plus additional EOS variations. For validation, they compared results against fully general relativistic simulations using the GR1D code for selected progenitors.

## Key numbers
- BH formation times for the least compact progenitor (s50WHW07, compactness 0.22) ranged between 3.4 s and 5.2 s after core bounce across both EOS sets
- BH formation times for the most compact progenitor (u75WH02, compactness 0.88) ranged from 0.40 s to 0.46 s for EOS set s_P and from 0.24 s to 0.59 s for EOS set s_M
- Full GR simulations took 3% to 8% longer to form BHs than GREP simulations for progenitors with compactness above 0.4, and 8% to 16% longer for progenitors with compactness below 0.4
- The GREP1 and GREP2 effective potentials predicted PNS masses within 1% of GR values at late times, while GREP3 overestimated PNS mass by about 2%
- PNS and shock radii were overestimated by up to 8% for GREP1 and GREP2, and up to 16% for GREP3

## Context
This work systematically extends the study of O'Connor (2011), who simulated 106 progenitors with 4 EOSs, by using 51 progenitors and 49 EOSs to isolate how zero-temperature and thermal components separately affect BH formation. The results explain patterns seen in Sumiyoshi (2006), Nakazato (2012), Char (2015), and Pan (2018), and confirm the finding of Hempel (2012) and Steiner (2013) that BH formation time correlates with the maximum hot PNS mass at constant entropy. The work enables using neutrino signals from a failed galactic supernova to constrain both progenitor compactness and the temperature dependence of the nuclear equation of state.
