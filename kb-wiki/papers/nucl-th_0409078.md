# Using Electron Scattering Superscaling to predict Charge-changing Neutrino Cross Sections in Nuclei

> Superscaling analyses of few-GeV inclusive electron scattering from nuclei are extended to include not only quasielastic processes, but now also into the region where $Δ$-excitation dominates. It is shown that, with reasonable assumptions about the basic nuclear scaling function extracted from data and information from other studies of the relative roles played by correlation and MEC effects, the residual strength in the resonance region can be accounted for through an extended scaling analysis. One observes scaling upon assuming that the elementary cross section by which one divides the residual to obtain a new scaling function is dominated by the $N\toΔ$ transition and employing a new scaling variable which is suited to the resonance region. This yields a good representation of the electromagnetic response in both the quasielastic and $Δ$ regions. The scaling approach is then inverted and predictions are made for charge-changing neutrino reactions at energies of a few GeV, with focus placed on nuclei which are relevant for neutrino oscillation measurements. For this a relativistic treatment of the required weak interaction vector and axial-vector currents for both quasielastic and $Δ$-excitation processes is presented.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that superscaling analyses of inclusive electron scattering can be extended from the quasielastic region into the Delta-resonance region, and that the resulting empirical scaling functions can be inverted to predict charge-changing neutrino-nucleus cross sections at few-GeV energies with uncertainties potentially reduced to the 10% level, provided one focuses on the quasielastic region and the region up to inelasticities where the Delta contribution reaches its maximum.

## Method

The authors extracted the experimental quasielastic scaling function from longitudinal electron scattering data for carbon-12 and oxygen-16, then subtracted the reconstructed quasielastic contribution from the total inclusive cross section to isolate the residual strength in the Delta region. They divided this residual by the elementary N-to-Delta transition cross section using a new scaling variable suited to resonance kinematics, obtaining a second scaling function that describes the Delta region. The two empirical scaling functions were then multiplied by the corresponding weak-interaction single-nucleon cross sections (including both vector and axial-vector currents) to predict charge-changing neutrino reaction cross sections.

## Key numbers

* The empirical quasielastic scaling function reaches a maximum value of approximately 0.6, compared to the Relativistic Fermi Gas prediction of 0.75.
* At the peaks of the quasielastic or Delta responses, mean-field modeling and the scaling approach differ by about 25%; with mean-field effects this discrepancy is reduced to perhaps 20%.
* Meson-exchange current contributions enter at roughly the 10% level in electron scattering.
* Deviations between the assembled scaling-based total cross section and experimental data are at most 10-20% for specific kinematics.
* The proton electric form factor used the dipole parametrization with the form 1/[1+4.97*tau]^2.

## Context

This work extends the superscaling analyses of Donnelly (1999) and Maieron (2001) from the quasielastic region into the Delta-resonance region, building on the observation that the longitudinal response superscales while transverse scaling violations arise from meson-exchange currents and inelastic processes. The approach enables predictions for neutrino oscillation experiments such as MiniBooNE, K2K/T2K, MINOS, NOvA and MINERvA, where targets of carbon-12 and oxygen-16 are used, and provides an alternative to relativistic mean-field modeling which typically fails to reproduce the long tails seen in the empirical scaling function.
