# Bounds on transport from univalence and pole-skipping

> Bounds on transport represent a way of understanding allowable regimes of quantum and classical dynamics. Numerous such bounds have been proposed, either for classes of theories or (by using general arguments) universally for all theories. Few are exact and inviolable. I present a new set of methods and sufficient conditions for deriving exact, rigorous, and sharp bounds on all coefficients of hydrodynamic dispersion relations, including diffusivity and the speed of sound. These general techniques combine analytic properties of hydrodynamics and the theory of univalent (complex holomorphic and injective) functions. Particular attention is devoted to bounds relating transport to quantum chaos, which can be established through pole-skipping in theories with holographic duals. Examples of such bounds are shown along with holographic theories that can demonstrate the validity of the conditions involved. I also discuss potential applications of univalence methods to bounds without relation to chaos, such as for example the conformal bound on the speed of sound.

# Bounds on transport from univalence and pole-skipping

## Key claim

The paper introduces a new set of mathematical methods based on univalent (complex holomorphic and injective) functions that provide sufficient conditions for deriving exact, rigorous, and sharp bounds on all coefficients of hydrodynamic dispersion relations, including diffusivity and the speed of sound, but these bounds depend on the existence of a known point ω₀(z₀) within a univalent region U and on the specific choice of conformal map used to transform U to the unit disk.

## Method

The author combines the analytic properties of hydrodynamic dispersion relations with the theory of univalent functions. For a given hydrodynamic mode (diffusion or sound), the dispersion relation ω(z) is shown to be locally univalent at z=0 by Puiseux's theorem, and a region U of univalence is identified, for example through the condition Re f'(z) > 0 or through group velocity conditions in complexified momentum space. A conformal map φ: U → 𝔻 (the unit disk) is constructed, and the normalized dispersion relation f(ζ) is expressed as a power series of the form f(ζ) = ζ + Σ bₙ ζⁿ. Standard theorems on univalent functions, the growth theorem and the de Branges theorem (Bieberbach conjecture), then yield inequalities on the hydrodynamic coefficients D, vₛ, and all higher-order coefficients cₙ and aₙ. The pole-skipping phenomenon in holographic theories provides specific known points ω₀(z₀) that relate transport to quantum chaos.

- For energy diffusion in a maximally univalent holographic model with broken translational invariance: D = 1/(2πT), with bounds 1/(4πT) ≤ D ≤ 9/(4πT); c₂ = 1/(8π³T³), with bounds 0 ≤ c₂ ≤ D/(π²T²) ≤ 9/(4π³T³); c₃ = 1/(16π⁵T⁵), with bounds -27/(32π⁵T⁵) ≤ c₃ ≤ 9/(4π⁵T⁵)
- For momentum diffusion in 4d 𝒩 = 4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory at finite temperature: D = 1/(4πT) ≈ 0.080/T, with bounds 0.046/T ≤ D ≤ 0.201/T; radius of convergence R ≈ 87.800 T²; Lyapunov exponent λ_L = 2πT; butterfly velocity v_B = √(2/3)

## Context

This work extends the program of deriving exact bounds on transport coefficients, building on earlier heuristic bounds such as Sachdev's relaxation time bound (Sachdev, 2011), the Mott-Ioffe-Regel limit (Ioffe, 1960; Mott, 1972), lower bounds on diffusion and viscosity (Kovtun, 2004; Hartnoll, 2014; Blake, 2016), and the conformal bound on the speed of sound (Cherman, 2009; Hohler, 2009). The methods are inspired by the exact Maldacena-Shenker-Stanford bound on quantum Lyapunov exponents (Maldacena, 2015) and the relation between transport and chaos through pole-skipping (Grozdanov, 2017; Blake, 2017; Blake, 2018). The techniques enable rigorous derivation of bounds that previously were only heuristic, and they open the possibility of finding the tightest possible bounds within the scope of univalence methods, as well as applications to weakly coupled theories, kinetic theory, and quasihydrodynamic theories with long-lived gapped modes (Grozdanov, 2018).
