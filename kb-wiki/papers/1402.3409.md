# Proposal for Resonant Detection of Relic Massive Neutrinos

> We present a novel method for detecting the relic neutrino background that takes advantage of structured quantum degeneracy to amplify the drag force from neutrinos scattering off a detector. Developing this idea, we present a characterization of the present day relic neutrino distribution in an arbitrary frame, including the influence of neutrino mass and neutrino reheating by $e^+e^-$ annihilation. We present explicitly the neutrino velocity and de Broglie wavelength distributions for the case of an Earthbound observer. Considering that relic neutrinos could exhibit quantum liquid features at the present day temperature and density, we discuss the impact of neutrino fluid correlations on the possibility of resonant detection.

## Key claim

The relic neutrino background, which is cold (mass much greater than temperature) and highly degenerate, should form a quantum Fermi liquid whose density correlations produce a structured impact noise spectrum; this structure can be exploited for resonant detection of the cosmic neutrino background via mechanical force amplification, in contrast to earlier studies that treated the background as an unstructured dilute gas and found the drag force undetectable.

## Method

The authors characterize the free-streaming relic neutrino distribution using a Fermi-Dirac-Einstein-Vlasov (FDEV) distribution that accounts for neutrino mass, the effective number of neutrinos \(N_\nu\) (which parameterizes entropy transfer from \(e^+e^-\) annihilation), and the relative velocity of an Earthbound observer with respect to the cosmic neutrino background (CNB). They compute the velocity, energy, and de Broglie wavelength distributions in the Earth frame, and then argue that the high phase-space density (degeneracy) of the cold neutrino background implies quantum fluid behavior with density correlations. Using a simple cubic-lattice model for the correlated impacts, they estimate the characteristic shot-noise frequency of neutrino scattering on a detector.

## Key numbers

* The relative velocity of the solar system with respect to the CMB is \(v_\oplus = 369.0 \pm 0.9\) km/s, with an annual modulation in the range \((-29.2, 29.3)\) km/s.
* For each neutrino flavor, the present-day number density is \(n_\nu = [-0.36\,\delta N_\nu^2 + 6.7\,\delta N_\nu + 56]\,\text{cm}^{-3}\), where \(\delta N_\nu \equiv N_\nu - 3\).
* The estimated characteristic shot-noise frequency for correlated neutrino impacts in the Earth frame is \(f_{\text{shot}} = (-0.43\,\delta N_\nu^2 + 5.6\,\delta N_\nu + 140)\,\frac{v_{\text{rel}}}{369\,\text{km/s}}\,\text{MHz}\).
* Including the annual velocity modulation and the range \(0 \leq \delta N_\nu \leq 0.8\) gives \(129\ \text{MHz} \leq f_{\text{shot}} \leq 156\ \text{MHz}\); if only half the neutrinos participate, the central frequency reduces to \(111\) MHz for \(\delta N_\nu = 0\) and \(v_{\text{rel}} = 369\) km/s.
* In the homogeneous Universe (no gravitational accretion), approximately \(50\%\) of neutrinos have a de Broglie wavelength longer than the mean interparticle separation \(L = n_\nu^{-1/3}\); this fraction rises to \(85\%\) for a factor-of-10 density enhancement due to gravitational accretion.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of relic neutrino detection via mechanical drag force (Opher, Lewis, Stodolsky (1975), Cabibbo (1982), Langacker (1983), Smith, Gelmini, Ringwald (2009), and others) which concluded that the effect from an unstructured gas is undetectable. It also relates to the PTOLEMY experiment (Weinberg) that aims to detect relic neutrinos via electron capture on tritium. The authors introduce the new idea that the cold, degenerate neutrino background forms a quantum Fermi liquid, with correlations that produce a structured noise spectrum; this opens the possibility of resonant detection in the 110–160 MHz range, a frequency band where the variation of detector noise with the Earth’s velocity relative to the CMB has not yet been explored.
