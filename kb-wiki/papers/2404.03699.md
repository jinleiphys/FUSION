# Nuclear uncertainties associated with the ejecta of a neutron-star black-hole accretion disk

> The simulation of heavy element nucleosynthesis requires input from yet-to-be-measured nuclear properties. The uncertainty in the values of these off-stability nuclear properties propagates to uncertainties in the predictions of elemental and isotopic abundances. However, for any given astrophysical explosion, there are many different trajectories, i.e. temperature and density histories, experienced by outflowing material and thus different nuclear properties can come into play. We consider combined nucleosynthesis results from 460,000 trajectories from a neutron star-black hole accretion disk and the find spread in elemental predictions due solely to unknown nuclear properties to be a factor of a few. We analyze this relative spread in model predictions due to nuclear variations and conclude that the uncertainties can be attributed to a combination of properties in a given region of the abundance pattern. We calculate a cross-correlation between mass changes and abundance changes to show how variations among the properties of participating nuclei may be explored. Our results provide further impetus for measurements of multiple quantities on individual short-lived neutron-rich isotopes at modern experimental facilities.

## Key claim

The spread in elemental abundance predictions due solely to unknown nuclear properties of short-lived neutron-rich isotopes is a factor of a few, even after summing over 460,000 trajectories from a neutron-star black-hole accretion disk simulation. The authors find that mass variations dominate uncertainties in the heavy mass region (A greater than about 130), but beta-decay rates and neutron capture rates can have comparable influence in specific regions of the abundance pattern.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis simulations using the PRISM reaction network code, incorporating over 460,000 trajectories from a 1.2-second hydrodynamic simulation of a neutron-star black-hole accretion disk. They varied nuclear masses using four models (FRDM2012, HFB32, KTUY, UNEDF1), beta-decay rates using three datasets (Moller 2019, Marketin 2016, Ney 2020), and neutron capture rates using two statistical Hauser-Feshbach codes (CoH and TALYS) with four distinct model configurations. The spread in final weighted mass fractions at 1 Gyr was quantified by computing the geometric mean and relative uncertainties across the different nuclear model predictions.

## Key numbers

- Spread in elemental predictions due to unknown nuclear properties: a factor of a few
- Maximum deviation in second peak height among four mass models: factor of 1.53
- Position of second peak uncertain by: 4 units in mass number
- Uncertainty in production of A=129 mass chain: over a factor of 3
- Third peak location spread across: 7 mass numbers
- Third peak height varies by: factor of 2 among different mass models
- Second peak spread in A with beta-decay variation: 3 units
- Second peak height variation with beta-decay models: factor of 2.75
- Neutron capture rate variations found to be subdominant for heavy mass region A greater than about 130

## Context

This work extends previous sensitivity studies of r-process nucleosynthesis by considering a realistic astrophysical environment with a large number of trajectories, rather than single trajectory approximations. The authors build on prior work by Moller (2019), Marketin (2016), and Ney (2020) for beta-decay rates, and use the FRDM2012, HFB32, KTUY, and UNEDF1 mass models. The results provide impetus for measurements of multiple nuclear properties on individual short-lived neutron-rich isotopes at modern experimental facilities, and the cross-correlation method between mass changes and abundance changes offers a new approach for prioritizing future measurements.
