# The Nuclear Physics of Solar and Supernova Neutrino Detection

> This talk provides a basic introduction for students interested in the responses of detectors to solar, supernova, and other low-energy neutrino sources. Some of the nuclear physics is then applied in a discussion of nucleosynthesis within a Type II supernova, including the r-process and the neutrino process.

## Key claim

This talk provides a basic introduction for students to the nuclear physics underlying solar and supernova neutrino detection, with the caveat that the discussion is pedagogical and selective rather than comprehensive, focusing specifically on how detector responses connect to nuclear physics and then applying those concepts to nucleosynthesis in Type II supernovae.

## Method

The paper reviews the nuclear physics of neutrino interactions relevant to detectors, including charged-current and neutral-current reactions on nuclei, and then applies this framework to discuss the r-process and neutrino process nucleosynthesis in Type II supernova environments. The treatment is qualitative and explanatory, aimed at building student understanding rather than presenting new calculations.

## Key numbers

Review-type paper: no original numerics.

## Context

This work provides a pedagogical bridge between nuclear physics and neutrino astrophysics for students, extending the conceptual framework of earlier reviews on solar neutrino detection and supernova nucleosynthesis. It enables students to understand how nuclear reaction rates and neutrino cross sections determine detector responses and influence nucleosynthesis yields, setting the stage for more detailed treatments such as those by Woosley and collaborators on the r-process and by Fuller and Meyer on the neutrino process.
