# Connection between the "Strutinsky level density" and the semiclassical level density (published version)

> We establish an analytical link between the level density obtained by means of the Strutinsky averaging method, and the semiclassical level density. This link occurs only in the so-called "asymptotic limit". It turns out that the Strutinsky method amounts to an approximation to the semiclassical method. This approximation contains an unavoidable remainder which constitutes an intrinsic noise in comparison to the semiclassical method. Thus, the "old" problem of the dependency of the Strutinsky procedure on the two free smoothing parameters of the averaging is intimately connected to this noise. On the other hand, we demonstrate that the noise of the method is small in the average density of states and in the average energy, whereas it might be non-negligible in the shell correction itself. In order to improve this method, we give a "rule" which consists simply of minimizing the relative error made on the average energy.

## Key claim

The Strutinsky averaged level density is an approximation to the semiclassical level density, not an exact equivalent, and this approximation holds only in the "asymptotic limit" where the Fermi level lies many mean shell-spacings above the bottom of the well (lambda - epsilon_0 >> gamma, with gamma at least of order the mean shell spacing). The approximation contains an unavoidable remainder that constitutes an intrinsic noise, and the well-known dependence of the Strutinsky procedure on the smoothing parameters (width gamma and order M) is directly connected to this remainder.

## Method

The authors derive an analytical link between the Strutinsky level density and the semiclassical level density by substituting the semiclassical density for the exact quantum density in the Strutinsky folding integral, then expanding the semiclassical density in a Taylor series around the Fermi level lambda and exploiting the property that the Strutinsky curvature-correction polynomial acts as a delta function for polynomials of order up to M. The remainder term is expressed as a function of the (M+2)-th derivative of the semiclassical density, the smoothing parameters gamma and M, and a known coefficient C_{M+2}. The relative error on the average energy is derived similarly by integrating the remainder.

## Key numbers

* For the harmonic oscillator with M=0, the relative error in the Strutinsky level density is (1/2)(gamma^2 / lambda^2), valid when lambda >> gamma and gamma >= hbar omega.
* For the harmonic oscillator with M=2, the remainder vanishes exactly (relative error = 0) because the semiclassical density is a parabola.
* For the infinite cubic box with M=0, the relative error in the Strutinsky level density is -(1/16)(gamma^2 / lambda^2), valid when lambda >> gamma and gamma >= hbar omega.
* For the infinite cubic box with M=2, the relative error in the Strutinsky level density is +(15/512)(gamma^4 / lambda^4), valid when lambda >> gamma and gamma >= hbar omega.
* In practical calculations with M between 16 and 30, the absolute relative error |R_{M,gamma}(lambda)| is less than 0.01.
* The typical ranges for the smoothing parameters are 6 <= M <= 20 and hbar_omega <= gamma <= 2 hbar_omega, where hbar_omega is the mean shell spacing near the Fermi level.

## Context

This work extends the formal understanding of the Strutinsky shell-correction method (Strutinsky 1967, 1968) by providing an analytical connection to the semiclassical Wigner-Kirkwood expansion (Wigner 1932, Kirkwood 1933) and the partition-function method (Balian and Bloch 1970). The authors show that the Strutinsky level density is not strictly equivalent to the semiclassical density, contrary to common claims, and that the residual dependence on the smoothing parameters is a direct consequence of the remainder term. This analysis enables a quantitative criterion (the "rule of the relative remainder") for choosing the smoothing parameters by minimizing the relative error on the average energy, and it explains why the Strutinsky method fails near the top of the well for finite potentials. The work also relates to the Extended Thomas Fermi plus Strutinsky integral (ETFSI) method of Pearson and collaborators (1991), which uses a self-consistent semiclassical energy as the macroscopic part.
