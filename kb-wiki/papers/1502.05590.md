# Enhancement of annihilation cross sections by electric interactions between the antineutron and the field of a large nucleus

> Data relative to antineutron and antiproton annihilation on large nuclei in the range 75-200 MeV/c present two unexpected features: (a) antineutron and antiproton cross sections have a similar size, (ii) the rise of the antineutron cross section at decreasing energy is much steeper than predictable for an inelastic process of purely strong nature at that energy. The observed behavior of antineutron-nucleus annihilations is similar to what would be expected for antiproton-nucleus annihilations, where Coulomb attraction focusses antiproton trajectories towards the nucleus, enhancing the inelastic cross section by a factor O(1/p) with respect to antineutrons on the same target. This results in a 1/p^2 behavior at small energies. The presence of a similar enhancement in the antineutron case may only be justified by an interaction with a longer range than strong interactions. Excluding a Coulomb force because of the antineutron neutrality, and taking into account that an intrinsic electric dipole is forbidden for the antineutron, the next choice is an electric dipole that is induced by the nuclear electric field. Recent theoretical works have shown that a non-negligible electric polarization may be induced in a neutron by QED vacuum polarization. Assuming this as a possibility, we have used a simple model to calculate the polarization strengths that are needed to fit the available data in terms of this effect. These are within the magnitude predicted by the vacuum polarization model. We have also discussed alternative scenarios that could induce an electric polarization of the antineutron as a consequence of the interplay between strong and e.m. interactions.

## Key claim

The steep rise of antineutron-nucleus annihilation cross sections at low momenta (below 100 MeV/c) cannot be explained by purely strong interactions and requires a medium-range attractive force; the authors propose that an electric dipole induced in the antineutron by the nuclear electric field, possibly via QED vacuum polarization effects, could produce this enhancement, and they fit a simple classical model to the data to extract the required polarizability strengths.

## Method

The authors use a semiclassical trajectory-bending model where an antineutron with an induced electric dipole moment \(D(r) = \beta E(r)\) experiences a force \(F(r) = D(r) \partial E(r)/\partial r\) from the nuclear electric field \(E(r) = Ze/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2)\). They start from a baseline fit of the form \(a + b/p\) to the annihilation cross section data of Astrua (2002) for momenta above 100 MeV/c, then multiply this fit by a geometric correction factor \(R_{\text{dip}}^2 / R_{\text{int}}^2\), where \(R_{\text{dip}}\) is the maximum impact parameter for which trajectories reach the nuclear surface under the dipole force and \(R_{\text{int}} = 1.2 A^{1/3}\) fm is the strong-interaction radius. The polarizability \(\beta\) (reparameterized as a length \(L\) via \(D = e Z L (1\ \text{fm}^2)/r^2\)) is varied to reproduce the data point at 76 MeV/c for Cu and Sn targets.

## Key numbers

- The baseline fit parameters from Astrua (2002) for the \(\bar{n}\)-nucleus cross section are \(a = 66.5 \pm 3.0\) mb and \(b = (1.987 \pm 0.086) \times 10^4\) mb MeV/c.
- The fitted \(L\) parameter for Cu is \(3.8 \pm 0.7\) fm; for Sn it is \(1.7 \pm 0.4\) fm.
- At the nuclear surface, the induced dipole moment is \(e \cdot 4.8\) fm for Cu and \(e \cdot 2.4\) fm for Sn.
- At the Sn surface, the antineutron dipole force is 9/10 of the Coulomb force on an antiproton; at 2 fm off the surface it is about 1/3; at 6 fm off the surface it is 1/10.
- The nuclear electric field at the surface of a heavy nucleus has magnitude \(10^{21}\) V/m; the Sauter critical field is \(10^{18}\) V/m.
- The antineutron data cover the momentum range 76–380 MeV/c on targets from carbon to lead.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of antineutron-nucleus annihilation data from Astrua (2002) and compares with antiproton-nucleus data from Bianconi (2011) and others. It addresses the puzzle that \(\bar{n}\) and \(\bar{p}\) cross sections on medium-to-large nuclei are similar in size below 200 MeV/c, contrary to expectations from Coulomb focussing for antiprotons. The authors invoke the theoretical prediction by Dominguez (2009) that a neutron can acquire an induced electric dipole via QED vacuum polarization in strong electric fields, and the application to neutron-nucleus scattering by Zimmer (2012). The work suggests that new antineutron data points in the 50–100 MeV/c region and improved antiproton data on medium-to-heavy nuclei below 100 MeV/c are needed to constrain the proposed mechanism.
