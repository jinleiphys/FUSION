# Semi-analytical calculation of the trajectory of relativistic nuclear collisions in the QCD phase diagram

> We extend a semi-analytical model that includes the finite nuclear thickness to calculate the energy density $ε(t)$ and conserved-charge densities including the net-baryon density $n_{\rm _B}(t)$ produced at mid-spacetime-rapidity in central Au+Au collisions. Assuming the formation of a quark-gluon plasma with an ideal gas equation of state of either quantum or Boltzmann statistics or with a lattice QCD-based equation of state, we extract the temperature $T$ and chemical potentials $μ_{\rm _B}$, $μ_{\rm _Q}$ and $μ_{\rm _S}$ as functions of time. This then allows us to semi-analytically calculate the $T-μ_{\rm _B}$ trajectory of relativistic nuclear collisions in the QCD phase diagram, which should benefit the studies of high density physics including the search for the critical end point. This model is also useful for exploring the trajectories in the more general $T-μ_{\rm _B}-μ_{\rm _Q}-μ_{\rm _S}$ QCD phase space.

## Key claim

The authors extend a semi-analytical model that includes finite nuclear thickness to calculate the temperature and chemical potential trajectories in the QCD phase diagram for central Au+Au collisions, but the extracted trajectories depend on the chosen formation time and equation of state, and at low collision energies (below approximately 4 GeV) the lattice QCD-based equation of state cannot extract a full trajectory.

## Method

The authors use a semi-analytical model that accounts for finite nuclear thickness to calculate the time evolution of energy density ε(t) and net-baryon density n_B(t) at mid-spacetime-rapidity in central Au+Au collisions. They then extract temperature T and chemical potentials μ_B, μ_Q, and μ_S by assuming either an ideal gas equation of state with quantum (Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac) statistics, Boltzmann statistics, or a lattice QCD-based equation of state with Taylor-expanded susceptibilities up to fourth order. The model assumes partons are produced uniformly within a diamond-shaped production region in the t-z plane and free-stream until their formation time τ_F, with the net-baryon density calculation following the same piecewise functional form as the energy density.

## Key numbers

- Peak energy density ε^max increases with collision energy √s_NN, while peak net-baryon density n_B^max mostly decreases with √s_NN (except for an increase at low to moderate √s_NN and small τ_F)
- For the quantum ideal gas equation of state, the magnitude of μ_Q in the full solution is very small (≤ 30 MeV)
- The partial-1 solution (assuming μ_Q=0, μ_S=μ_B/3) reproduces the T(t) and μ_B(t) results from the full solution almost exactly over the entire time evolution
- For the lattice equation of state, at √s_NN = 2.0 GeV no T and μ solutions are found for the densities at any time during the evolution; for √s_NN ≥ 4.0 GeV the trajectory can be extracted, though at low energies only around the time of ε^max
- Two constant energy density lines from the partial-1 solution for the quantum equation of state that go through the endpoints of the FRG crossover curve correspond to ε = 0.51 and 1.23 GeV/fm^3
- At √s_NN ≥ 4.4 GeV both the outgoing and returning parts of the trajectory intersect the FRG crossover curve, while at √s_NN ≤ 3.6 GeV neither part intersects it

## Context

This work extends earlier semi-analytical models by Bjorken (1983), Lin (2018), and Mendenhall (2021) that progressively improved the treatment of finite nuclear thickness effects on initial energy density production in nuclear collisions. The results enable studies of high-density physics including the search for the critical end point (CEP) in the QCD phase diagram, and the model is useful for exploring trajectories in the more general T-μ_B-μ_Q-μ_S QCD phase space. The trajectories can be compared with results from dynamical models such as hydrodynamic models and the AMPT transport model (Lin 2005, Wang 2021), and the lattice QCD-based equation of state uses susceptibility coefficients calculated by Borsanyi (2018) on a 48^3×12 lattice in the temperature range 135-220 MeV.
