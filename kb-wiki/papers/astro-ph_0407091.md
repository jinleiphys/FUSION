# Distinguishing Bare Quark Stars from Neutron Stars

> Observations to date cannot distinguish neutron stars from self-bound bare quark stars on the basis of their gross physical properties such as their masses and radii alone. However, their surface luminosity and spectral characteristics can be significantly different. Unlike a normal neutron star, a bare quark star can emit photons from its surface at super-Eddington luminosities for an extended period of time. We present a calculation of the photon bremsstrahlung rate from the bare quark star's surface, and indicate improvements that are required for a complete characterization of the spectrum. The observation of this distinctive photon spectrum would constitute an unmistakable signature of a strange quark star and shed light on color superconductivity at stellar densities.

# Distinguishing Bare Quark Stars from Neutron Stars

## Key claim
Bare quark stars can be distinguished from neutron stars not by their masses and radii, which are similar in the observed range of 1–2 solar masses, but by their surface photon emission: a bare quark star can emit photons at super-Eddington luminosities for extended periods (days to decades), and the bremsstrahlung spectrum from its electron electrosphere provides a distinctive signature, though the calculation is limited to the soft-photon regime and requires further improvements for a complete spectral characterization.

## Method
The authors calculated the photon bremsstrahlung emissivity from the degenerate relativistic electron layer (electrosphere) at the surface of a bare strange quark star, using Low's approximation in the soft-photon limit. They derived analytic expressions for the emissivity in two temperature regimes (T ≪ μe and T near μe), including screening effects and the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal (LPM) suppression factor, and compared these to numerical integrations and to the pair-annihilation photon flux. The spectrum was characterized by its mean energy and deviations from a blackbody spectrum via the ratio ⟨ω²⟩/⟨ω⟩².

## Key numbers
- Baryon density at vanishing pressure for strange quark matter: 2 to 3 times n₀, where n₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ (saturation density of nuclear matter).
- Radial electric field at the quark star surface: ~5 × 10¹⁷ V cm⁻¹, exceeding the critical value for electron-positron pair production.
- Eddington luminosity limit: ~10³⁸ erg s⁻¹; super-Eddington emission can persist from about a day to decades.
- Electrosphere thickness scale H = 501.3 × (10 MeV / μe(0)) fm; for μe(0) = 10–20 MeV, the electrosphere is about 10³ fm thick or more.
- Mean photon energy from bremsstrahlung: ⟨ω⟩ ≈ 0.5 MeV.
- LPM suppression factor: for μe = 10 MeV (μ₁₀ = 1), emissivity reduced by factor ~60; for μe = 20 MeV (μ₁₀ = 2), reduced by factor ~350.
- Bremsstrahlung dominates pair-annihilation photon emission for electron plasma temperatures below ~0.1 MeV.
- For T ≤ 10⁹ K, bremsstrahlung is the dominant photon emission process; for T < 10⁷ K, the emissivity turns over and decreases rapidly due to an exponential factor.

## Context
This work extends the earlier proposal by Page (2002) that e⁺e⁻ pair production and annihilation at the bare quark star surface can produce super-Eddington luminosities, by adding the bremsstrahlung process from the electron electrosphere. It also builds on the electrosphere model of Nötzold, Kettner, and Gnedin (1995) and the pair-production rates of Usov (2001). The results enable calculations of the thermal evolution and surface luminosity versus age of bare quark stars, which can be compared with observations from the INTEGRAL satellite (launched 2002) to distinguish them from neutron stars. The authors note that a more rigorous quantum calculation is needed for photon energies above the plasma frequency, and that the bremsstrahlung process also applies to superconducting phases of quark matter as discussed by Alford and Rajagopal (2002).
