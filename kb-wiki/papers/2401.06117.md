# Landscape of nuclear deformation softness with spherical quasi-particle random phase approximation

> We investigate the stability and softness of nuclei against quadrupole, octupole, and hexadecapole deformation. By applying the spherical Skyrme-force Hartree-Fock Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer quasi-particle random phase approximation, we diagnose ground-state deformation when imaginary solutions are obtained, i.e., the spherical ground state {\em collapses}. We also calculate the multipole polarizability in spherical nuclei with no collapse, as a measure of softness. This numerically light and theoretically sound method is found able to capture deformation patterns across the nuclide chart. The connection between the intrinsic shape of nuclei and the dynamics of their low-lying collective states is established and the role of shell structure is discussed.

# Landscape of nuclear deformation softness with spherical quasi-particle random phase approximation

## Key claim
The spherical Skyrme-HFBCS-QRPA method can diagnose ground-state deformation (when imaginary solutions appear, indicating "collapse" of the spherical state) and quantify softness via multipole polarizability in nuclei that remain spherical, capturing deformation patterns across the nuclide chart for quadrupole, octupole, and hexadecapole channels simultaneously, though the approach cannot determine whether multiple deformation types coexist in a single nucleus.

## Method
The authors solved the self-consistent spherical Skyrme-force Hartree-Fock Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer quasi-particle random phase approximation (HFBCS-QRPA) for even-even nuclei using the SLy5 and SkM* Skyrme functionals. When the QRPA stability matrix yields imaginary eigenvalues, the spherical ground state is unstable against deformation in that multipole channel (quadrupole, octupole, or hexadecapole); when all solutions are real, the multipole polarizability (inverse energy-weighted sum rule) is calculated as a measure of softness. The method was applied to all even-even nuclei with experimental data available in the NuDat database.

## Key numbers
- Octupole polarizability C3 is around 1 W.u./MeV for 96Ru (stiff) and 4 W.u./MeV for 96Zr (soft octupole nucleus)
- Nuclei with enhanced octupole transition strength B(E3) > 0.5 × A W.u. are classified as soft (e.g., light nuclei with no collapse but high polarizability)
- Hexadecapole collapse regions appear around neodymium (Z = 60) and polonium (Z = 84)
- Enhanced hexadecapole transition strength B(E4) > 0.5 × A W.u. found in certain Ce, Nd, Sm, and Gd isotopes with N ≥ 90 in the lanthanide region (Z = 57-71)
- Octupole-driving magic numbers identified as 16, 34, 56, 88, and 134 (soft-octupole numbers)
- Original magic numbers 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, and 126 correspond to stiff-multipole numbers
- 122Te, 152Ce, 152-158Nd, 210Pb, and 206-218Po identified as candidates for static quadrupole-hexadecapole deformation

## Context
This work extends the spherical QRPA approach previously applied to octupole deformation in LAPA23 to quadrupole and hexadecapole channels systematically across the nuclear chart. It contrasts with traditional mean-field approaches such as the finite-range-droplet model (FRDM) of Moller (2016) and deformed Skyrme HFBCS calculations of Goriely (2001), offering a computationally lighter method that avoids full deformed calculations. The results connect to the NpNn scheme of Casten (1985) and the P-factor of Casten (1987) for quadrupole deformation, and to the dual-shell mechanism proposed by Martinou (2021) for shape coexistence. The method enables rapid identification of candidate nuclei for detailed microscopic studies of quadrupole-octupole and quadrupole-hexadecapole deformation, and can guide future experimental investigations at exotic beam facilities.
