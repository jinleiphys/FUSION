# A pedagagical introduction to the Lifshitz regime

> We give an elementary and pedagogical review of the phase diagrams which are possible in Quantum ChromoDynamics (QCD). Currently, the emphasis is upon the appearance of a critical endpoint, where disordered and ordered phases meet. In many models, though, a Lifshitz point also arises. At a Lifshitz point, three phases meet: disordered, ordered, and one where spatially inhomogeneous phases arise. At the level of mean field theory, the appearance of a Lifshitz point does not dramatically affect the phase diagram. We argue, however, that fluctuations about the Lifshitz point are very strong in the infrared, and significantly alter the phase diagram. We discuss at length the analogy to inhomogenous polymers, where the Lifshitz regime produces a bicontinuous microemulsion. We briefly mention the possible relevance to the phase diagram of QCD.

## Key claim

The authors argue that while mean field theory predicts a Lifshitz point where three phases (disordered, ordered, and spatially inhomogeneous) meet, fluctuations in three spatial dimensions are so strong in the infrared that they completely wipe out the Lifshitz point, leaving only a "Lifshitz regime" where the point itself is not accessible and the critical endpoint may be completely wiped out, leaving only a line of first order transitions.

## Method

The authors perform a mean field analysis of a scalar field theory with a negative kinetic term (Z < 0) and a higher-derivative term ~ (∂²φ)², then compute one-loop corrections to the mass term from anisotropic fluctuations in the symmetric phase. They analyze the renormalization group flow at the Lifshitz point, finding the upper critical dimension is eight and the lower critical dimension is four, and draw an analogy to the bicontinuous microemulsion phase in inhomogeneous polymer mixtures.

## Key numbers

- In three spatial dimensions, the upper critical dimension for the Lifshitz point is eight, and the lower critical dimension is four.
- The effective mass in the symmetric phase is given by m_eff² = m² - Z²M²/4, where M is the mass scale from the higher-derivative term.
- The condensate in the spatially inhomogeneous phase is φ₀² = -m_eff²/λ.
- In the simplest Nambu Jona-Lasinio model, the sigma meson mass is twice the constituent quark mass, m_σ/m_qk = 2, causing the critical endpoint and Lifshitz point to coincide.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of phase diagrams in QCD beyond the standard critical endpoint scenario discussed by Asakawa (1989), Stephanov (1998), and Stephanov (1999). The authors draw on the condensed matter analysis of Brazovski (1975) and Hohenberg (1995) regarding fluctuation-induced first order transitions, and draw a detailed analogy to inhomogeneous polymer systems studied by Fredrickson (1997, 2006), Duchs (2003), Jones and Lodge, and Cates (2012), where the Lifshitz regime produces a bicontinuous microemulsion. The paper enables future work to distinguish between critical endpoint fluctuations and Lifshitz regime fluctuations in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC.
