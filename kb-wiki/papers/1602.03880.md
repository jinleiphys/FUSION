# On neutron stars in f(R) theories: small radii, large masses and large energy emitted in a merger

> In the context of f(R) gravity theories, we show that the apparent mass of a neutron star as seen from an observer at infinity is numerically calculable but requires careful matching, first at the star's edge, between interior and exterior solutions, none of them being totally Schwarzschild-like but presenting instead small oscillations of the curvature scalar R; and second at large radii, where the Newtonian potential is used to identify the mass of the neutron star. We find that for the same equation of state, this mass definition is always larger than its general relativistic counterpart. We exemplify this with quadratic $R^2$ and Hu-Sawicki-like modifications of the standard General Relativity action. Therefore, the finding of two-solar mass neutron stars basically imposes no constraint on stable f(R) theories. However, star radii are in general smaller than in General Relativity, which can give an observational handle on such classes of models at the astrophysical level. Both larger masses and smaller matter radii are due to much of the apparent effective energy residing in the outer metric for scalar-tensor theories. Finally, because the f(R) neutron star masses can be much larger than General Relativity counterparts, the total energy available for radiating gravitational waves could be of order several solar masses, and thus a merger of these stars constitutes an interesting wave source.

## Key claim
For the same equation of state, the apparent gravitational mass of a neutron star in f(R) theories, as defined by matching to the Newtonian potential at infinity, is always larger than its general relativistic counterpart; consequently, the observation of two-solar-mass neutron stars imposes essentially no constraint on stable f(R) theories, though star radii are generally smaller than in General Relativity.

## Method
The authors solved the modified Einstein field equations for static, spherically symmetric spacetimes in metric f(R) gravity, using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta algorithm to integrate six coupled first-order ordinary differential equations for the metric functions A(r), B(r), the curvature scalar R(r), and the pressure p(r). They employed realistic neutron-matter equations of state from Hebeler et al. (2013), labelled stiff, middle, and soft, and matched interior solutions to exterior solutions at the star's edge, then continued integration to large radii where the Newtonian potential identifies the gravitational mass. The analysis was performed for quadratic R² and Hu-Sawicki f(R) models.

## Key numbers
- For the quadratic R² model with a = -1 km², the maximum mass reaches approximately 2.5 M⊙ for the stiff equation of state, compared to about 2.2 M⊙ in General Relativity.
- For the Hu-Sawicki model with n = 1 and c₂ = 1, the maximum mass reaches approximately 3.0 M⊙ for the stiff equation of state.
- The total energy available for radiating gravitational waves in a merger of f(R) neutron stars could be of order several solar masses.
- The stability condition for oscillatory exterior solutions requires γ < 0, which for f(R) = a R² implies a < 0.

## Context
This work extends prior perturbative treatments of neutron stars in f(R) gravity by Arapoglu (2010) and Astashenok et al. (2013), which inherited the General Relativity mass definition without proper matching at infinity. The authors introduce a physically observable gravitational mass defined via the Newtonian potential at large radii, correcting the approach of Capozziello et al. (2015) and Yazadjiev et al. (2014). The results enable predictions for gravitational-wave signals from neutron star mergers in f(R) theories, potentially distinguishing them from General Relativity without requiring black-hole mergers to produce multi-solar-mass emissions.
