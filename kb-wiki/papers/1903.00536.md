# Applications of the worldline Monte Carlo formalism in quantum mechanics

> In recent years efficient algorithms have been developed for the numerical computation of relativistic single-particle path integrals in quantum field theory. Here, we adapt this "worldline Monte Carlo" approach to the standard problem of the numerical approximation of the non-relativistic path integral, resulting in a formalism whose characteristic feature is the fast, non-recursive generation of an ensemble of trajectories that is independent of the potential, and thus universally applicable. The numerical implementation discretises the trajectories with respect to their time parametrisation but maintains a continuous spatial domain. In the case of singular potentials, the discretised action gets adapted to the singularity through a "smoothing" procedure. We show for a variety of examples (the harmonic oscillator in various dimensions, the modified Pöschl-Teller potential, delta-function potentials, the Coulomb and Yukawa potentials) that the method allows one to obtain fast and reliable estimates for the Euclidean propagator and use them in a certain time window suitable for extracting the ground state energy. As an aside, we apply it for studying the classical limit where nearly classical trajectories are expected to dominate in the path integral. We expect the advances made here to be useful also in the relativistic case.

## Key claim

The authors adapt the "worldline Monte Carlo" formalism, originally developed for relativistic path integrals in quantum field theory, to non-relativistic quantum mechanics, claiming that the method yields fast and reliable estimates for the Euclidean propagator and ground state energy across a variety of potentials. However, the method suffers from a systematic "undersampling" effect at large propagation times that causes deviations from linearity in the logarithmic propagator, limiting the usable time window for energy extraction.

## Method

The Euclidean propagator is expressed as an expectation value over paths with fixed endpoints and Gaussian velocity distribution, where the paths are decomposed into a straight-line classical trajectory plus fluctuations. The fluctuations are rescaled to unit loops with a standardized Gaussian distribution independent of mass and propagation time, then discretized into a finite number of points per loop. An ensemble of such discretized trajectories is generated non-recursively using the "linearly shifted open loops" (LSOL) algorithm, and the line integral of the potential along each trajectory is computed to estimate the expectation value via Monte Carlo averaging.

## Key numbers

- For the harmonic oscillator in one dimension, the ground state energy is \(E_0 = \frac{1}{2}\omega\) (from the analytic expression \(E_n = (n + d/2)\omega\)).
- The free particle propagator normalization factor is \(\left(\frac{m}{2\pi t}\right)^{d/2}\).
- The discretized velocity distribution uses \(N_p\) points per loop with weight \(\exp\left(-\frac{N_p}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{N_p}(q_i - q_{i-1})^2\right)\).
- The standard error of the mean (SEM) is computed as \(\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{N_l} [(\cdots)_i - \langle (\cdots) \rangle]^2 / [N_l(N_l - 1)]}\) over \(N_l\) loops.

## Context

This work extends the worldline numerics approach of Gies and Langfeld (2001), who introduced efficient loop-generation algorithms for one-loop calculations in quantum field theory, and the earlier Feynman-Schwinger representation Monte Carlo method of Nieuwenhuis and Tjon (1990s) for relativistic bound states. The authors adapt these techniques to non-relativistic quantum mechanics, aiming for a universal method applicable to any potential without iterative adaptation. The paper enables future application of the worldline Monte Carlo formalism to relativistic bound-state problems, which was the original motivation, by first resolving technical difficulties in the simpler non-relativistic setting.
