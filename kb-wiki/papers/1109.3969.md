# Dynamics of nuclear single-particle structure in covariant theory of particle-vibration coupling: from light to superheavy nuclei

> The impact of particle-vibration coupling and polarization effects due to deformation and time-odd mean fields on single-particle spectra is studied systematically in doubly magic nuclei from low mass $^{56}$Ni up to superheavy ones. Particle-vibration coupling is treated fully self-consistently within the framework of relativistic particle-vibration coupling model. Polarization effects due to deformation and time-odd mean field induced by odd particle are computed within covariant density functional theory. It has been found that among these contributions the coupling to vibrations makes a major impact on the single-particle structure. The impact of particle-vibration coupling and polarization effects on calculated single-particle spectra, the size of the shell gaps, the spin-orbit splittings and the energy splittings in pseudospin doublets is discussed in detail; these physical observables are compared with experiment. Particle-vibration coupling has to be taken into account when model calculations are compared with experiment since this coupling is responsible for observed fragmentation of experimental levels; experimental spectroscopic factors are reasonably well described in model calculations.

## Key claim

Particle-vibration coupling (PVC) makes the dominant contribution to single-particle structure in doubly magic nuclei, but the paper's claim is conditional: the hybrid model that adds PVC corrections to mean-field polarization effects (deformation and time-odd mean fields) is only a reasonable approximation because the PVC model is not variational and cannot calculate polarization effects self-consistently, so the two contributions are treated as additive.

## Method

The authors performed fully self-consistent relativistic particle-vibration coupling (PVC) calculations using the NL3* parametrization of covariant density functional theory (CDFT), with phonon modes computed within relativistic random phase approximation (RRPA). Polarization effects due to deformation and time-odd mean fields in odd-mass nuclei were treated separately using cranked relativistic mean field (CRMF) theory in a three-dimensional Cartesian basis. The two sets of corrections were combined additively in a hybrid model and compared to experimental single-particle energies extracted from binding energy differences of doubly magic cores and their odd neighbors.

## Key numbers

- The effective Lorentz mass in CDFT is m*_L(k_F)/m ≈ 0.66, which is lower than typical non-relativistic DFT effective masses and leads to stretched single-particle spectra on the mean-field level.
- The Dirac effective mass is constrained empirically to 0.57 ≤ m*_D(k_F)/m ≤ 0.61 based on spin-orbit splittings in finite nuclei.
- In deformed actinide nuclei, the majority of one-quasiparticle states are described with accuracy better than 0.5 MeV, but some states deviate from experiment by as much as 1 MeV.
- The phonon basis included multipolarities 2+, 3-, 4+, 5-, 6+ with energies below 15 MeV; phonon modes with transition probabilities less than 5% of the maximal ones for each Jπ were neglected.
- The oscillator basis truncation used fermionic NF=20 and bosonic NB=20 shells with oscillator frequency ℏω0 = 41 A^{-1/3} MeV.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic particle-vibration coupling model of Litvinova and Ring (2006), which was applied only to 208Pb, to a systematic study of doubly magic nuclei from 56Ni through 100Sn, 132Sn, 208Pb, and the superheavy 292120 nucleus. It uses the improved NL3* parametrization rather than the earlier NL3 set. The paper addresses the long-standing issue that mean-field CDFT calculations produce too-stretched single-particle spectra compared to experiment, a problem previously noted by Mahaux, Bortignon, Broglia, and collaborators (1985) and by Rutz, Bender, Reinhard, and Maruhn (1998, 1999). The results enable future work on whether PVC loosens the constraints on the Dirac effective mass and whether it improves the relative placement of states with different orbital angular momentum l.
