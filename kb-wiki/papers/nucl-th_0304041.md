# Electromagnetic Dissociation as a Tool for Nuclear Structure and Astrophysics

> Coulomb dissociation is an especially simple and important reaction mechanism. Since the perturbation due to the electric field of the (target) nucleus is exactly known, firm conclusions can be drawn from such measurements. Electromagnetic matrixelements and astrophysical S-factors for radiative capture processes can be extracted from experiments. We describe the basic elements of the theory of nonrelativistic and relativistic electromagnetic excitation with heavy ions. This is contrasted to electromagnetic excitation with leptons (electrons), with their small electric charge and the absence of strong interactions. We discuss various approaches to the study of higher order electromagnetic effects and how these effects depend on the basic parameters of the experiment. The dissociation of neutron halo nuclei is studied in a zero range model using analytical methods. We also review ways how to treat nuclear interactions, show their characteristics and how to avoid them (as far as possible). We review the experimental results from a theoretical point of view. Of special interest for nuclear structure physics is the appearence of low lying electric dipole strength in neutron rich nuclei. Applications of Coulomb dissociation to some selected radiative capture reactions relevant for nuclear astrophysics are discussed. The Coulomb dissociation of 8B is relevant for the solar neutrino problem. The potential of the method especially for future investigations of (medium) heavy exotic nuclei for nuclear structure and astrophysics is explored. We conclude that the Coulomb dissociation mechanism is theoretically well understood, the potential difficulties are identified and can be taken care of. Many interesting experiments have been done in this field and many more are expected in the future.

# Electromagnetic Dissociation as a Tool for Nuclear Structure and Astrophysics

## Key claim

Coulomb dissociation is a well-understood reaction mechanism for extracting electromagnetic matrix elements and astrophysical S-factors, provided that nuclear interactions can be avoided or properly accounted for and that higher-order electromagnetic effects are small for high-energy, forward-angle scattering. The authors emphasize that the method works best when the adiabaticity parameter ξ is small and the fragments are emitted into a narrow forward cone, even when the Coulomb parameters η_a and η_c are large.

## Method

The authors develop and compare several theoretical frameworks for Coulomb breakup: a zero-range model for neutron-halo nuclei solved analytically in both prior-form and post-form decompositions of the Hamiltonian, using plane-wave Born approximation (PWBA), Coulomb Wave Born Approximation (CWBA), and semiclassical straight-line approximations. They evaluate the T-matrix analytically using the Nordsieck formula and hypergeometric functions, and compare the post-form DWBA (which treats core-target Coulomb interaction to all orders) with the prior-form semiclassical approach (which treats the center-of-mass motion classically). The model serves as a "theoretical laboratory" to study postacceleration effects, higher-order electromagnetic effects, and the conditions under which the Born approximation remains valid even for large Coulomb parameters.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy E_0 of the (c+n) system enters through κ = √(2μE_0)/ħ, with bound-state wave function φ_0 = √(κ/2π) exp(-κr)/r
- For deuteron breakup on ¹⁹⁷Au at E_d = 12 MeV, the postacceleration effect shifts the proton energy maximum to ~7.5 MeV while the neutron maximum remains at ~2.5 MeV
- The adiabaticity parameter ξ = ωb/v_a = 2η_a q_∥/q_⊥; for large ξ (q_⊥ < 2η_a q_∥) the breakup probability is suppressed exponentially with ξ
- The full CWBA amplitude acquires a prefactor exp(-πξ/2) relative to the Born result, which vanishes in the limit ξ → 0
- The Coulomb push momentum transfer is q = q_a - q_c - q_n, with perpendicular component q_⊥ ≈ q_aθ and longitudinal component q_∥ = ω/v_a

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of Coulomb excitation developed by Alder and Winther (1975) and earlier work on deuteron breakup by Baur and Trautmann (1972, 1976) to neutron-halo nuclei studied at exotic beam facilities. The zero-range analytical model builds on prior work by Typel and Baur (1994) and provides a rigorous testbed for comparing post-form and prior-form DWBA approaches, which had shown disagreement for low-energy deuteron breakup (Rybicki and Austern, 1971). The results enable reliable extraction of astrophysical S-factors for radiative capture reactions such as ⁸B breakup relevant to the solar neutrino problem, and establish that postacceleration and higher-order electromagnetic effects are negligible at the high beam energies used in modern experiments with halo nuclei like ¹¹Be and ¹⁹C (Nakamura et al., 1994, 1999).
