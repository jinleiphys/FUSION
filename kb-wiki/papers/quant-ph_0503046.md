# Effect of phase relaxation on quantum superpositions in complex collisions

> We study the effect of phase relaxation on coherent superpositions of rotating clockwise and anticlockwise wave packets in the regime of strongly overlapping resonances of the intermediate complex. Such highly excited deformed complexes may be created in binary collisions of heavy ions, molecules and atomic clusters. It is shown that phase relaxation leads to a reduction of the interference fringes, thus mimicking the effect of decoherence. This reduction is crucial for the determination of the phase--relaxation width from the data on the excitation function oscillations in heavy--ion collisions and bimolecular chemical reactions. The difference between the effects of phase relaxation and decoherence is discussed.

# Effect of phase relaxation on quantum superpositions in complex collisions

## Key claim

Phase relaxation (characterized by the spin off-diagonal phase-relaxation width beta) reduces the interference fringe contrast between clockwise and anticlockwise rotating wave packets in strongly overlapping resonances, mimicking decoherence; however, the two effects can be distinguished because fast decoherence would wash out cross-section energy fluctuations (reducing normalized variance to D/Gamma << 1), whereas fast phase relaxation preserves them.

## Method

The authors model the time power spectrum P(t,theta) of the decay intensity for a highly excited intermediate complex formed in binary collisions, using a sum over strongly overlapping resonance levels (Gamma/D >> 1) with a J-window form for the partial reaction probability W(J) proportional to exp[-(J-Jbar)^2/d^2]. They calculate P(t,theta) for the 12C+24Mg system using parameters extracted from earlier analysis of excitation function oscillations at theta=180 degrees, and compare the sensitivity of interference fringes and cross-section energy autocorrelation functions C(epsilon,theta) to different combinations of the J-window width d and the phase-relaxation width beta.

## Key numbers

* Phase-relaxation width beta = 0.01 MeV, J-window width d = 3, average spin Jbar = 14, angular velocity hbar*omega = 1.45 MeV, total decay width Gamma = 0.3 MeV (base parameter set from KVCG02 for 12C+24Mg)
* Two additional parameter sets tested: (d=4, beta=0.04 MeV) and (d=8, beta=0.075 MeV)
* Even the largest possible beta value (0.1 MeV) is more than one order of magnitude smaller than the spreading width Gamma_spr approximately 5 MeV estimated from giant resonance widths
* For 12C+24Mg elastic scattering at theta=180 degrees, the excitation function shows about 15 local maxima on the energy interval Delta E_cm = 13-22 MeV, with quasiperiodicity period about 2.9 MeV yielding hbar*omega = 1.35-1.45 MeV
* Energy step in the experimental data (Mermaz81) is Delta E_cm = 133 keV, while the minimal characteristic energy scale Gamma = 0.3 MeV
* For the H+D2 reaction, the rotational wave packets involve total spin values J=15-20 (d approximately 5)
* For theta=170.6 degrees, the difference between C(epsilon,theta) for beta=0.01 MeV, d=3 and beta=0.075 MeV, d=8 reaches up to 0.4 in absolute value for epsilon >= 4-5 MeV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of spin off-diagonal phase correlations in strongly overlapping resonances previously developed for heavy-ion scattering (Kun 1993, 1994, 1997; KVV98; KRV99; KVG01; KVCG02) and for atomic cluster collisions (KBCGH03). It contrasts with the conventional random matrix theory assumption of random phase relations in the overlapping resonance regime (GMGW98; Zewail83; Smil). The approach enables determination of both the J-window width d and the phase-relaxation width beta from interference fringe contrast, which was not possible from backward-angle data alone. The method is applicable to bimolecular chemical reactions such as H+D2 (Nat02; Alt1; Alt2) and F+HD (Alt3; Alt4), where similar rotational wave packets and interference have been observed in calculations. The authors also describe how to extract the time power spectrum from excitation function data, analogous to femtochemistry pump-probe techniques (Zewail 2003).
