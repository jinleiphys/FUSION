# Reply on the "Comment on "Investigating the phase diagram of finite extensive and nonextensive systems" by Al. H. Raduta and Ad. R. Raduta" by A. S. Botvina and I. N. Mishustin

> This is a reply to the the nucl-th/0203059 manuscript by Botvina and Mishustin. We show that their pressure versus volume curves (converging to finite values when the system's density approaches the normal nuclear density) are not realistic. Some other comments are also included.

# Reply on the "Comment on 'Investigating the phase diagram of finite extensive and nonextensive systems'" by A. S. Botvina and I. N. Mishustin

## Key claim
The pressure versus volume curves presented by Botvina and Mishustin, which converge to finite values when the system density approaches normal nuclear density, are not realistic because they rely on an assumption of non-overlapping spherical fragments at freeze-out. The authors argue that a deformed fragment scenario, which yields diverging pressure near normal density, is the physically correct description.

## Method
The authors analyze the free volume parametrization used in their original paper (Raduta and Raduta, 2003) and contrast it with the spherical fragment scenario employed by Botvina and Mishustin. They argue that dynamical simulations show fragments at break-up have various shapes varying from event to event, meaning the system's position coordinates are shared between fragment center-of-mass positions and additional deformation coordinates. This raises the power of the free volume term in the partition function above N-1, causing pressure to diverge as volume approaches the normal nuclear volume V0, consistent with van der Waals behavior.

## Key numbers
- The pressure P converges to a finite value when the volume V approaches the volume of the source nucleus at normal density (V0) in the spherical fragment scenario, as shown by Botvina and Mishustin.
- In the deformed fragment scenario, the power of the free volume Vf is larger than N-1, causing P to diverge as V approaches V0, leading to the well-known van der Waals behavior.

## Context
This reply extends the debate on the liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclear systems, specifically addressing Coulomb effects. The authors distinguish their free volume approach from the spherical fragment scenario used in prior statistical models such as the MMMC model, Koonin and Randrup model (1990), Das Gupta and Mekjian calculations (1990), and recent SMM model calculations (Elliott, 2002). The work enables future refined treatments that properly account for fragment deformation degrees of freedom, sharp conservation of center of mass, and accurate binding energies in statistical multifragmentation models.
