# Dark Matter Interpretation of the Neutron Decay Anomaly

> There is a long-standing discrepancy between the neutron lifetime measured in beam and bottle experiments. We propose to explain this anomaly by a dark decay channel for the neutron, involving one or more dark sector particles in the final state. If any of these particles are stable, they can be the dark matter. We construct representative particle physics models consistent with all experimental constraints.

# Dark Matter Interpretation of the Neutron Decay Anomaly

## Key claim
The 4.0 sigma discrepancy between neutron lifetime measurements from beam experiments (888.0 ± 2.0 s) and bottle experiments (879.6 ± 0.6 s) can be explained if approximately 1% of neutrons decay via a dark channel involving one or more dark sector particles, rather than the standard beta decay channel. This explanation requires that the branching fraction for neutron decay into a proton plus anything is only about 99%, not 100% as assumed in the Standard Model.

## Method
The authors construct effective field theory operators for neutron dark decay into final states containing dark sector particles, including n → χγ (dark matter plus photon), n → χφ (two dark particles), and n → χe⁺e⁻ (dark matter plus electron-positron pair). They impose constraints from proton decay (requiring final state masses between m_p − m_e and m_n), nuclear stability (using the most stringent bound from ⁹Be stability requiring M_f > 937.900 MeV), and dark matter stability. They then build two explicit renormalizable particle physics models: Model 1 with a scalar Φ = (3,1)_{-1/3} and a Dirac fermion χ realizing n → χγ, and Model 2 adding two more singlet fermions and a scalar to realize n → χφ.

## Key numbers
- Neutron lifetime discrepancy: Δτ_n ≈ 8.4 s, corresponding to ΔΓ_n^exp ≈ 7.1 × 10⁻³⁰ GeV
- Bottle experiment average: τ_n^bottle = 879.6 ± 0.6 s
- Beam experiment average: τ_n^beam = 888.0 ± 2.0 s
- Discrepancy significance: 4.0 sigma
- Required branching fraction for dark decay: approximately 1% (Br(n → p + anything) ≈ 99%)
- Dark matter mass range for n → χγ: 937.900 MeV < m_χ < 938.783 MeV
- Monochromatic photon energy range for n → χγ: 0.782 MeV < E_γ < 1.664 MeV (if χ is dark matter); 0 < E_γ < 1.664 MeV (if χ is not dark matter)
- Most stringent nuclear constraint: ΔM = 937.900 MeV from ⁹Be stability
- Model 1 parameter: |λ_q λ_χ|/M_Φ² ≈ 6.7 × 10⁻⁶ TeV⁻² for m_χ = 937.9 MeV
- Model 2 parameter: (|λ_q λ_χ|/M_Φ²)(|λ_φ|/0.04) ≈ 4.9 × 10⁻⁷ TeV⁻² for m_χ̃ = m_χ = 937.9 MeV
- Scalar mass bound from LHC: M_Φ ≳ 1 TeV
- Suppressed e⁺e⁻ branching fraction in n → χγ: at most 1.1 × 10⁻⁶
- Radiative process branching fraction in n → χφ: 3.5 × 10⁻¹⁰ or smaller
- Dark matter relic density coupling for Model 2: λ_φ ≈ 0.037 for m_χ = 937.9 MeV, m_φ ≈ 0

## Context
This work addresses the long-standing neutron lifetime discrepancy between beam experiments (Byrne 1996, Yue 2013) and bottle experiments (Mampe, Serebrov 2004, Pichlmaier 2010, Steyerl 2012, Arzumanov 2015) that has persisted for over twenty years. The proposal extends earlier speculation by Greene about neutron-mirror neutron oscillations (Berezhiani 2005) and differs from the suppressed neutron decays considered by Davoudiasl (2014) which were too small to explain the anomaly. The models avoid constraints from neutron-antineutron oscillation and dinucleon decay searches (Abe 2011, Gustafson 2015) that excluded Majorana particle scenarios such as that of McKeen (2015). Subsequent experimental work by Tang (2018) challenged the n → χγ scenario for photon energies above 0.782 MeV, while Sun (2018) excluded the n → χe⁺e⁻ case for pair energies above 2m_e + 100 keV. The implications for neutron stars have been explored by McKeen (2018), Baym (2018), and Motta (2018), and the proposal has been invoked to explain unexpectedly high ¹⁰Be production in ¹¹Be decay (Pfutzner 2018, Riisager 2014).
