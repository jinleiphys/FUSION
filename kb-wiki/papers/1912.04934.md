# Microscopic predictions for production of neutron rich nuclei in the reaction $^\mathbf{176}\mathbf{Yb}+{}^\mathbf{176}\mathbf{Yb}$

> Background: Production of neutron-rich nuclei is of vital importance to both understanding nuclear structure far from stability and to informing astrophysical models of the rapid neutron capture process (r-process). Multinucleon transfer (MNT) in heavy-ion collisions offers a possibility to produce neutron-rich nuclei far from stability. Purpose: The $^{176}\mathrm{Yb}+{}^{176}\mathrm{Yb}$ reaction has been suggested as a potential candidate to explore the neutron-rich region surrounding the principal fragments. The current study has been conducted with the goal of providing guidance for future experiments wishing to study this (or similar) system. Methods: Time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) and its time-dependent random-phase approximation (TDRPA) extension are used to examine both scattering and MNT characteristics in $^{176}\mathrm{Yb}+{}^{176}\mathrm{Yb}$. TDRPA calculations are performed to compute fluctuations and correlations of the neutron and proton numbers, allowing for estimates of primary fragment production probabilities. Results: Both scattering results from TDHF and transfer results from the TDRPA are presented for different energies, orientations, and impact parameters. In addition to fragment composition, scattering angles and total kinetic energies, as well as correlations between these observables are presented. Conclusions: $^{176}\mathrm{Yb}+{}^{176}\mathrm{Yb}$ appears to be an interesting probe for the mid-mass neutron-rich region of the chart of nuclides. The predictions of both TDHF and TDRPA are speculative, and will benefit from future experimental results to test the validity of this approach to studying MNT in heavy, symmetric collisions.

## Key claim

The $^{176}\mathrm{Yb}+{}^{176}\mathrm{Yb}$ reaction is a viable candidate for producing neutron-rich nuclei in the mass region around $A\sim176$ via multinucleon transfer, but the predictions from time-dependent Hartree-Fock and its time-dependent random-phase approximation extension are speculative and will require future experimental results to test the validity of this approach to studying MNT in heavy, symmetric collisions.

## Method

Time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations were performed on a 3D Cartesian grid with the SLy4$d$ Skyrme functional to obtain scattering characteristics (scattering angles, total kinetic energies) for $^{176}\mathrm{Yb}+{}^{176}\mathrm{Yb}$ collisions at two center-of-mass energies and two initial orientations (tip-tip and side-side) over a range of impact parameters. The time-dependent random-phase approximation (TDRPA) extension was then applied to compute fluctuations and correlations of neutron and proton numbers, from which primary fragment production probabilities were estimated using Gaussian bivariate normal distributions.

## Key numbers

- Center-of-mass energies studied: $E_\mathrm{c.m.}=660$~MeV and $E_\mathrm{c.m.}=880$~MeV
- Ground state prolate deformation of $^{176}\mathrm{Yb}$: $\beta_2\simeq 0.33$ from HF calculation
- Oblate solution binding energy difference: approximately $5$~MeV higher than prolate ground state
- TKE saturation for side-side collisions: approximately $350-400$~MeV for the most central collisions at both energies
- TKE saturation for tip-tip collisions: approximately $250-300$~MeV
- Numerical box dimensions: $66 \times 66$ points in the reaction plane, 36 points perpendicular to the reaction plane, with $1.0$~fm grid spacing
- TDRPA calculations used $\epsilon=2\times10^{-3}$ for the time-reversed evolution
- Total computational cost: $200$ full TDHF evolutions, each taking $10-55$ hours of wall time, corresponding to roughly $250$ days of computation time
- Cross section contours in the primary fragment production map: innermost contour at $1$~millibarn, subsequent contours every $0.2$~mb, with a boundary contour at the microbarn level

## Context

This work extends the application of TDHF and TDRPA methods, previously used for deep-inelastic collisions in lighter systems (Simenel 2011, Williams 2018) and for quasifission studies (Wakhle 2014, Hammerton 2015, Umar 2016), to a symmetric heavy system suggested by Oganessian et al. for experimental investigation at Dubna. The predictions provide guidance for future experiments wishing to study this or similar systems, and the results suggest that large-angle detectors would be beneficial for fragment detection. The authors note that subsequent statistical decay of primary fragments would bring final products closer to the valley of stability, and that including such decay following the approach of Sekizawa (2017), Umar (2017), or Wu (2019) would be needed to predict evaporation residue cross-sections.
