# Resonant Destruction as a Possible Solution to the Cosmological Lithium Problem

> We explore a nuclear physics resolution to the discrepancy between the predicted standard big-bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) abundance of 7Li and its observational determination in metal-poor stars. The theoretical 7Li abundance is 3-4 times greater than the observational values, assuming the baryon-to-photon ratio, eta_wmap, determined by WMAP. The 7Li problem could be resolved within the standard BBN picture if additional destruction of A=7 isotopes occurs due to new nuclear reaction channels or upward corrections to existing channels. This could be achieved via missed resonant nuclear reactions, which is the possibility we consider here. We find some potential candidate resonances which can solve the lithium problem and specify their required resonant energies and widths. For example, a 1^- or 2^- excited state of 10C sitting at approximately 15.0 MeV above its ground state with an effective width of order 10 keV could resolve the 7Li problem; the existence of this excited state needs experimental verification. Other examples using known states include 7Be+t \rightarrow 10B(18.80 MeV), and 7Be+d \rightarrow 9B(16.71 MeV). For all of these states, a large channel radius (a > 10 fm) is needed to give sufficiently large widths. Experimental determination of these reaction strengths is needed to rule out or confirm these nuclear physics solutions to the lithium problem.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that the 3-4 times discrepancy between predicted and observed primordial lithium-7 abundance could be resolved by previously overlooked resonant nuclear reactions that destroy beryllium-7 or lithium-7 during Big Bang Nucleosynthesis, but notes that these solutions require fortuitously favorable nuclear parameters such as large channel radii (a > 10 fm) and experimental verification of the required excited states.

## Method
The authors systematically searched nuclear databases (TUNL and NNDC) for all possible compound nuclei with mass numbers 8-11 that could be formed from two-body destruction channels of beryllium-7 or lithium-7 with projectiles n, p, d, t, helium-3, alpha, and gamma. They applied a narrow resonance approximation to evaluate candidate resonances, then ran BBN numerical codes with the resonant rates included to map the parameter space of resonance energy and effective width that would reduce the mass-7 abundance to observed levels.

## Key numbers
- The predicted BBN lithium-7 abundance is (5.12^{+0.71}_{-0.62}) x 10^{-10} relative to hydrogen, while the observed halo star plateau abundance is (1.23^{+0.34}_{-0.16}) x 10^{-10}, giving a discrepancy factor of 2.2-4.2
- A 1^- or 2^- excited state of carbon-10 at approximately 15.0 MeV above its ground state with an effective width of order 10 keV could resolve the lithium problem
- The baryon-to-photon ratio from WMAP is eta = 6.19 +/- 0.15 x 10^{-10}
- Any new destruction rate must be 2-3 times greater than the current dominant destruction channels (lithium-7(p,alpha)alpha for lithium-7 and beryllium-7(n,p)lithium-7 for beryllium-7)
- Non-resonant rates would require unphysically large astrophysical S-factors of order 10^5 to 10^9 keV-barn
- The resonance energy limit considered is E_res <= 650 keV, with total widths less than about 40 keV for narrow resonances

## Context
This work extends the approach of Cyburt and Pospelov (2009) who identified a resonant level at 16.71 MeV in boron-9 that could enhance beryllium-7 destruction via beryllium-7(d,p)alpha alpha and beryllium-7(d,gamma)boron-9 reactions. The paper systematically searches all possible compound nuclei (mass 8-11) for resonant destruction channels, finding that candidate resonances require large channel radii (a > 10 fm) similar to those found by Cyburt and Pospelov (2009). The results provide specific targets for experimental nuclear physics to confirm or rule out these conventional nuclear physics solutions before invoking beyond-Standard-Model explanations such as cosmological variation of fundamental constants or late-decaying massive particles.
