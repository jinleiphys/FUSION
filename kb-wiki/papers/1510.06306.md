# Do hyperons exist in the interior of neutron stars ?

> In this work we review the role of hyperons on the properties of neutron and proto-neutron stars. In particular, we revise the so-called "hyperon puzzle", go over some of the solutions proposed to tackle it, and discuss the implications that the recent measurements of unusually high neutron star masses have on our present knowledge of hypernuclear physics. We reexamine also the role of hyperons on the cooling properties of newly born neutron stars and on the so-called r-mode instability.

## Key claim

This review argues that while hyperons are energetically favored to appear in neutron star interiors at densities of 2–3 times nuclear saturation density, their presence softens the equation of state and reduces the maximum mass; the "hyperon puzzle" is whether hyperons can still exist given the observational requirement that maximum masses reach at least 2 solar masses.

## Method

The authors survey both phenomenological approaches (Skyrme-type interactions and relativistic mean-field models) and microscopic many-body methods (Brueckner–Hartree–Fock, Dirac–Brueckner–Hartree–Fock, and Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo) that include hyperonic degrees of freedom. They compare predictions for the equation of state, maximum mass, and radius against the measured masses of PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0348+0432, and they review proposed solutions to the hyperon puzzle: repulsive hyperon–hyperon interactions, hyperonic three-body forces, and phase transitions to deconfined quark matter.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear saturation density: \(\rho_0 \sim 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Canonical neutron star mass: \(1.4\)–\(1.5 M_\odot\)
- PSR J1903+0327 mass: \(1.667 \pm 0.021 M_\odot\)
- PSR J1614-2230 mass: \(1.97 \pm 0.04 M_\odot\)
- PSR J0348+0432 mass: \(2.01 \pm 0.04 M_\odot\)
- Hyperons appear in the inner core at densities of \(\sim 2\)–\(3 \rho_0\)
- Most relativistic models including hyperons obtain maximum masses in the range \(1.4\)–\(1.8 M_\odot\)
- Some models (e.g., QMC, chiral SU(3), RMF with varied couplings) achieve maximum masses of \(1.9\)–\(2.1 M_\odot\) with hyperons
- Binding energy of a single \(\Lambda\) hyperon in infinite nuclear matter at \(\rho_0\): \(\sim 28\) MeV
- \(\Xi\)-nucleus interaction attractive by about \(-14\) MeV
- \(\Xi^-\)-\(^{14}\)N binding energy: \(4.38 \pm 0.25\) MeV (Nakazawa et al., 2015)
- \(\Lambda\Lambda\) bond energy in \(^6_{\Lambda\Lambda}\)He: around 1 MeV (KEK, 2001)
- Radii of hyperonic stars with masses \(1\)–\(1.6 M_\odot\) must be larger than 13 km (Fortin et al.)
- Radii from quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries: Steiner et al. give \(10.4\)–\(12.9\) km; Guillot et al. give \(\sim 10\) km or less

## Context

This review extends the long line of work on hyperons in neutron stars that began with Ambartsumyan and Saakyan (1960) and was formalized in the 1980s by Glendenning using relativistic mean-field models. It directly addresses the tension created by the precise mass measurements of PSR J1614-2230 (Demorest, 2010) and PSR J0348+0432 (Antoniadis, 2013), which require a maximum mass of at least 2 solar masses, while most hyperonic equations of state predict lower maxima. The paper surveys proposed resolutions including repulsive hyperon–hyperon interactions (e.g., Weissenborn et al., Dexheimer and Schramm), hyperonic three-body forces (e.g., Yamamoto et al., Lonardoni et al.), and phase transitions to quark matter (e.g., Alford et al., Zdunik and Haensel). It also discusses how future observations of neutron star radii, cooling (e.g., the Cas A compact star studied by Sedrakian, 2013), and r-mode instability can further constrain the strangeness content of dense matter.
