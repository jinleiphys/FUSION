# Radial oscillations of pulsating neutron stars: The UCIa equation-of-state case

> Radial oscillations provide a clean dynamical test of the high-density stiffness of neutron-star equations of state. We study spherically symmetric pulsations of nonrotating relativistic stars built from cold, charge-neutral, $β$-equilibrated pure nucleonic matter described within relativistic mean-field theory. As a baseline we adopt the UCIa parameter set [Astron. Astro-phys. 689, A242 (2024)], and we implement high-density stiffening via the $σ$-cut scheme by adding a regulator potential $U_{\rm cut}(σ)$ [Phys. Rev. C 92, no.5, 052801 (2015), Phys. Rev. C 106, no.5, 055806 (2022)]. For representative choices $f_s=0$ (no cutoff) and $f_s=0.58$ (stiffened), we solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff and tidal perturbation equations to obtain equilibrium sequences, mass-radius relations, and tidal deformabilities. We then derive and solve the linear general-relativistic radial pulsation equations to compute the eigenfrequencies and eigenfunctions of the fundamental and overtone modes. The $σ$-cutoff suppresses the growth of the scalar field at supranuclear density, increases the pressure, and shifts the maximum mass, radii, and $Λ_{1.4}$ accordingly, while systematically raising the radial-mode frequencies at fixed mass. Using the sign change of $ω_0^2$ as a stability criterion, we identify stiffened models that remain radially stable up to the observed $\sim 2M_\odot$ mass scale and are consistent with current multimessenger constraints, demonstrating how radial spectra complement static EoS tests.

## Key claim

The sigma-cutoff potential stiffens the equation of state at high density while preserving the low-density calibration, and the resulting neutron-star models remain radially stable up to the observed approximately 2 solar mass scale, but only for specific choices of the cutoff parameter (here demonstrated for f_s = 0.58) that simultaneously satisfy current multimessenger constraints.

## Method

The authors constructed cold, charge-neutral, beta-equilibrated pure nucleonic matter equations of state using relativistic mean-field theory with the UCIa parameter set as baseline, implementing high-density stiffening via a sigma-cutoff regulator potential U_cut(sigma). They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations and tidal perturbation equations to obtain equilibrium sequences, mass-radius relations, and tidal deformabilities, then derived and solved the linear general-relativistic radial pulsation equations to compute eigenfrequencies and eigenfunctions of the fundamental and overtone modes for two representative cutoff choices: f_s = 0 (no cutoff) and f_s = 0.58 (stiffened).

## Key numbers

- The sigma-cutoff potential starts to affect the equation of state at 2.65 times nuclear saturation density n_0 (where n_0 is approximately 0.16 fm^{-3}); below this density the stiffened and original equations of state overlap
- The stiffened models (f_s = 0.58) remain radially stable up to the observed approximately 2 solar mass scale, using the sign change of omega_0^2 as the stability criterion
- The dimensionless parameter f_s takes values 0.00 (original UCIa) and 0.58 (stiffened) for the present study
- The regulator potential parameters are alpha = m_pi^4 and beta = 120

## Context

This work extends the UCIa parameter set (Malik 2024) by adding the sigma-cut scheme originally developed by Maslov (2015) and Patra (2022) to stiffen the high-density equation of state. The study demonstrates that radial oscillation spectra provide a complementary dynamical consistency test beyond static observables (mass, radius, tidal deformability) for dense-matter models, and the results are consistent with multimessenger constraints from NICER (Riley 2019, Miller 2019), GW170817 (Abbott 2018, Abbott 2019), and pulsar mass measurements (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013, Cromartie 2019). Future third-generation gravitational-wave detectors such as the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer may enable direct detection of these radial oscillation modes.
