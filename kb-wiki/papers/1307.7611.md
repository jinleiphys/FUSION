# Characterization of initial fluctuations for the hydrodynamical description of heavy ion collisions

> Event-by-event fluctuations in the initial conditions for a hydrodynamical description of heavy-ion collisions are characterized. We propose a Bessel-Fourier decomposition with respect to the azimuthal angle, the radius in the transverse plane and rapidity. This allows for a complete characterization of fluctuations in all hydrodynamical fields including energy density, pressure, fluid velocity, shear stress and bulk viscous pressure. It has the advantage that fluctuations can be ordered with respect to their wave length and that they can be propagated mode-by-mode within the hydrodynamical formalism. Event ensembles can then be characterized in terms of a functional probability distribution. For the event ensemble of a Monte Carlo Glauber model, we provide evidence that the latter is close to Gaussian form, thus allowing for a particularly simple characterization of the event distribution.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a Bessel-Fourier decomposition of fluctuating initial conditions for hydrodynamical descriptions of heavy-ion collisions that allows for a complete, orthonormal characterization of fluctuations in all hydrodynamical fields, with the advantage that fluctuations can be ordered by wavelength and propagated mode-by-mode; the authors provide evidence that for a Monte Carlo Glauber model event ensemble, the functional probability distribution is close to Gaussian form, but this is a specific finding for that model rather than a general claim.

## Method

The authors decompose event-by-event fluctuations in the initial conditions for relativistic heavy-ion collisions using a Bessel-Fourier expansion in azimuthal angle, transverse radius, and rapidity, applied to scalar fields (energy density, pressure), vector fields (fluid velocity), and tensor fields (shear stress, bulk viscous pressure). They characterize event ensembles by a functional probability distribution in this basis, and demonstrate for a Monte Carlo Glauber model that the distribution is approximately Gaussian, allowing characterization by expectation values and two-mode correlators. The paper also develops the mathematical formalism for discrete Bessel transformations and orthogonality relations needed for numerical implementation.

## Key numbers

- The Bessel-Fourier expansion uses zeros \(z_l^{(m)}\) of the Bessel function \(J_m(z)\), with orthogonality relation \(\int_0^R dr\; r\; J_m(z_l^{(m)} r/R) J_m(z_{l'}^{(m)} r/R) = \frac{R^2}{2} [J_{m+1}(z_l^{(m)})]^2 \delta_{ll'}\)
- The discrete Bessel transformation uses \(N\) discretization points at radii \(r_\alpha^{(m)} = z_\alpha^{(m)} / z_N^{(m)} \cdot R\), with the boundary condition \(h(R)=0\)
- The inverse Bessel transformation is approximated by a finite sum: \(h_l \approx \sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} \frac{4}{[z_N^{(m)}]^2 [J_{m+1}(z_l^{(m)})]^2 [J_{m+1}(z_\alpha^{(m)})]^2} h(r_\alpha^{(m)}) J_m(k_l^{(m)} r_\alpha^{(m)})\)

## Context

This work extends the characterization of fluctuating initial conditions beyond the common cumulant expansion in terms of eccentricities used by Teaney and Yan (2011) and others, which cannot isolate single positive-definite modes. It builds on the established paradigm that heavy-ion collisions produce an almost perfect fluid, as demonstrated by ALICE (2011), CMS (2012), ATLAS (2013), and STAR (2013) collaborations, and by viscous hydrodynamic simulations by Schenke, Jeon, and Gale (2012) and Qiu and Heinz (2011). The Bessel-Fourier approach enables mode-by-mode fluid dynamic propagation of fluctuations, which the authors develop further in a companion paper (Floerchinger and Wiedemann, 2013), and can help relate experimental event shape engineering (Schukraft, Timmins, and Voloshin, 2013) to sub-classes of initial conditions.
