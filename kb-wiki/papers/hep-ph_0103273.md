# How to observe fluctuating temperature?

> We provide arguments that event-by-event (EBE) analysis of multiparticle production data are ideal place to search for the possible fluctuation of temperature characterizing hadronizing source in thermodynamical approach.

# How to observe fluctuating temperature?

## Key claim

Event-by-event analysis of multiparticle production data can in principle detect fluctuations of temperature occurring *within a single event*, not just from event to event. The authors argue that such intra-event fluctuations would manifest as a power-law (Lévy) distribution of transverse momentum already at the single-event level, rather than only after averaging over many events.

## Method

The authors derive that averaging an exponential Boltzmann distribution over fluctuations of the inverse temperature parameter (1/T), assumed to follow a gamma distribution, yields a power-law distribution of the Tsallis form with entropic index q > 1. They then contrast two scenarios: (1) temperature constant within each event but fluctuating event-to-event, yielding exponential single-event pT spectra; (2) temperature fluctuating within each event, yielding power-law single-event pT spectra. Simulations for central Pb+Pb collisions at beam energy 3 A·TeV with rapidity window -1.5 < y < 1.5 and particle density dN/dy = 6000 are used to illustrate the observable differences.

## Key numbers

- For scenario (1): single-event pT distributions shown for T = 200 MeV (black symbols) and T = 250 MeV (open symbols), corresponding to typical temperature uncertainties expected at LHC due to different initial conditions.
- For scenario (2): single-event pT distribution shown with T = 200 MeV and q = 1.05, producing a power-law curve that bends slightly upward compared to the exponential.
- In the simulated typical event for scenario (2): approximately 18000 secondaries (practically the maximal possible number), with highest pT points corresponding to single particles.
- The relation ω = q - 1 = 1/C, where C is the total heat capacity of the system.

## Context

This work extends earlier proposals for indirect measurement of temperature fluctuations by Karsch and Mustafa (1999) and more direct event-to-event fluctuation studies by general references. It builds on the authors' own prior work (Wilk and Włodarczyk, 2000) showing that fluctuation of a parameter in an exponential distribution leads naturally to power-law (Lévy) distributions. The paper argues that while inclusive processes have already observed Lévy-type distributions, only event-by-event analysis of heavy ion collision data can distinguish whether the power-law shape arises from event-to-event temperature variation or from intra-event temperature fluctuations. Measuring the nonextensivity parameter q would provide the total heat capacity C, offering information about the thermodynamics of heavy ion collisions and possible phase transitions.
