# Effective field theory description of halo nuclei

> Nuclear halos emerge as new degrees of freedom near the neutron and proton driplines. They consist of a core and one or a few nucleons which spend most of their time in the classically-forbidden region outside the range of the interaction. Individual nucleons inside the core are thus unresolved in the halo configuration, and the low-energy effective interactions are short-range forces between the core and the valence nucleons. Similar phenomena occur in clusters of $^4$He atoms, cold atomic gases near a Feshbach resonance, and some exotic hadrons. In these weakly-bound quantum systems universal scaling laws for s-wave binding emerge that are independent of the details of the interaction. Effective field theory (EFT) exposes these correlations and permits the calculation of non-universal corrections to them due to short-distance effects, as well as the extension of these ideas to systems involving the Coulomb interaction and/or binding in higher angular-momentum channels. Halo nuclei exhibit all these features. Halo EFT, the EFT for halo nuclei, has been used to compute the properties of single-neutron, two-neutron, and single-proton halos of s-wave and p-wave type. This review summarizes these results for halo binding energies, radii, Coulomb dissociation, and radiative capture, as well as the connection of these properties to scattering parameters, thereby elucidating the universal correlations between all these observables. We also discuss how Halo EFT's encoding of the long-distance physics of halo nuclei can be used to check and extend ab initio calculations that include detailed modeling of their short-distance dynamics.

## Key claim

Halo EFT provides a universal leading-order description of electromagnetic observables for one-neutron s-wave and p-wave halo nuclei, with corrections from short-distance physics and p-wave final-state interactions entering at higher orders that are parametrically suppressed by powers of the ratio of the halo momentum scale to the core mass scale.

## Method

The authors construct an effective field theory for halo nuclei by treating the core and valence nucleons as point particles with short-range interactions, using dimer fields to represent shallow bound states. They include photons via minimal substitution and gauge-invariant non-minimal operators, then compute electromagnetic observables such as the B(E1) strength distribution for Coulomb dissociation, electric form factors, and electric radii by evaluating Feynman diagrams at leading and next-to-leading order in the expansion parameter M_halo/M_core.

## Key numbers

- For the universal E1 strength distribution of one-neutron s-wave halos, the dimensionless quantity μ_σ S_1n^2 d(B(E1))/dE equals (C_σ^2/C_σ,LO^2) × (3 α_em Q_eff^2/π^2) × x^(3/2)/(1+x)^4, where x = E/S_1n
- For ¹⁹C, the Coulomb dissociation data yield S_1n = 575 keV (mean) with 1σ confidence interval (520,630) keV, and effective range r_0,σ = 2.6 fm with interval (1.7,3.2) fm
- For ¹¹Be, the NLO Halo EFT prediction for the point electric radius squared is ⟨r_E²⟩_pt = 0.39 fm², compared to the measured value 0.54(17) fm²
- For ¹⁹C, the predicted point electric radius squared is ⟨r_E²⟩_pt = 0.09^(+0.02)_(-0.03) fm², with an upper bound of 0.38 fm² from charge-changing reaction cross sections
- For deuterium, the Halo EFT prediction for the point electric radius is ⟨r_E²⟩_pt^(1/2) = 1.977 fm, consistent with the measured values 1.97556(57) fm (from 1S-2S isotope shift) and 1.97335(102) fm (from muonic atoms)

## Context

This review extends the Halo EFT framework developed by Hammer, Ji, and Phillips (2011) and applied to specific nuclei by Acharya et al. (2013) and Rupak et al. (2012). The universal E1 strength distribution reproduces the zero-range potential model result of Bertulani and collaborators (1988) and is shown to agree with Coulomb dissociation data for ¹⁹C from Nakamura et al. (2003) and ¹¹Be from Fukuda et al. (2004). The formalism enables connections between scattering parameters, binding energies, and electromagnetic observables, and can be used to check and extend ab initio calculations such as the No-Core Shell Model with Continuum approach of Calci et al. (2016).
