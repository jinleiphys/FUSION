# Structure Calculations Without Effective Interactions

> Good approximate eigenstates of a Hamiltionian operator which poesses a point as well as a continuous spectrum have beeen obtained using the Lanczos algorithm. Iterating with the bare Hamiltonian operator yields spurious solutions which can easily be identified. The rms radius of the ground state eigenvector, for example, is calculated using the bare operator.

## Key claim

Good approximate eigenstates of a Hamiltonian operator that possesses both a point and a continuous spectrum can be obtained using the Lanczos algorithm with the bare Hamiltonian operator, provided that spurious solutions (which arise from projecting the operator onto a finite basis) are identified by checking whether the eigenstates of the projected operator are also eigenstates of \(\hat{H}^2\).

## Method

The authors constructed an \(n\)-dimensional matrix representation of a single-particle Hamiltonian (harmonic oscillator \(\hat{H}_0\) with \(\hbar\omega=1\) plus a Gaussian potential \(V = V_0 \exp(-a r^2)\) with \(V_0=-11\) and \(a=1\)) in an s-wave oscillator basis. They applied the Lanczos algorithm iteratively and, after each iteration, computed \(\Delta_{l \lambda} = |e_{l \lambda}^2 - \langle e_{l \lambda} | \hat{H}^2 | e_{l \lambda} \rangle|\) for each converging eigenpair; exact bound states of \(\hat{H}\) yield \(\Delta\) identically zero, while spurious states converge to a non-zero positive value. The ground state eigenfunction was then used to calculate the rms radius directly from the bare operator.

## Key numbers

- With 30 basis states and after 11 Lanczos iterations, the approximate energy eigenvalues converged to \(e_{0,8} = 12.397\) and \(e_{2,11} = -3.579\) with a convergence factor of \(\leq 10^{-3}\).
- After 30 iterations, the rms radius of the ground state eigenvector was \(0.322\) (units not specified in the text, consistent with the harmonic oscillator length scale \(\hbar\omega=1\)).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Secrest, Conroy, and Miller (2003) for identifying spurious states in non-relativistic eigenvalue problems, and of Alhaidari, Miller, and Yüksel (2009) for relativistic problems, by demonstrating that the same Lanczos-based identification method works for a bare Hamiltonian with both bound and continuum spectra. The method avoids the difficulties of constructing effective Hamiltonians (as in Bloch and Horowitz (1958), Suzuki and Lee (1980), and others), which are non-linear, non-Hermitian, and require mapping each observable operator onto the subspace. This enables direct calculation of observables like the rms radius without effective operators.
