# Sensitivity of the r-process to nuclear masses

> The rapid neutron capture process (r-process) is thought to be responsible for the creation of more than half of all elements beyond iron. The scientific challenges to understanding the origin of the heavy elements beyond iron lie in both the uncertainties associated with astrophysical conditions that are needed to allow an r-process to occur and a vast lack of knowledge about the properties of nuclei far from stability. There is great global competition to access and measure the most exotic nuclei that existing facilities can reach, while simultaneously building new, more powerful accelerators to make even more exotic nuclei. This work is an attempt to determine the most crucial nuclear masses to measure using an r-process simulation code and several mass models (FRDM, Duflo-Zuker, and HFB-21). The most important nuclear masses to measure are determined by the changes in the resulting r-process abundances. Nuclei around the closed shells near N=50, 82, and 126 have the largest impact on r-process abundances irrespective of the mass models used.

## Key claim

The paper identifies which specific neutron-rich nuclei have the greatest impact on r-process abundance yields by varying individual neutron separation energies by ±25% within a single mass model, rather than comparing different global mass models. The authors hedge that the most important nuclei cluster near closed shells (N=50, 82, 126) irrespective of the mass model used, but the exact ranking of nuclei shifts depending on the chosen mass model and astrophysical conditions.

## Method

The authors used a fully dynamical r-process nuclear network code with fixed astrophysical conditions (H-scenario from Qian et al. (1998), initial temperature T₉ = 1.5, density 3.4×10² g/cm³, dynamical timescale 0.86 s, seed nucleus ⁷⁰Fe, neutron-to-seed ratio 67). They varied the neutron separation energy of each of 3010 nuclei (from ⁵⁸Fe to ²⁹⁴Fm) one at a time by ±25%, while keeping all other nuclear inputs (β-decay rates from Moller et al. (2003), neutron capture rates from Rauscher and Thielemann (2000)) fixed to FRDM-based values. The sensitivity was quantified by the quantity F = (F₊ + F₋)/2, where F± = 100 Σ_A |X_baseline(A) – X_±ΔSₙ(A)|, summing mass fraction differences over all mass numbers A. The procedure was repeated with four additional mass models (Duflo-Zuker, ETFSIQ, HFB-21, F-spin) to test model dependence.

## Key numbers

* The study varied neutron separation energies for 3010 nuclei from ⁵⁸Fe to ²⁹⁴Fm.
* The variation applied to each separation energy was ±25%.
* The initial astrophysical conditions were: T₉ = 1.5, density = 3.4×10² g/cm³, dynamical timescale = 0.86 s, neutron-to-seed ratio = 67.
* The top-ranked nucleus in the FRDM model was ¹³⁸Sn with F = 24.59; in Duflo-Zuker, ¹³²Cd with F = 36.54; in HFB-21, ¹⁴⁰Sn with F = 17.59.

## Context

This work extends prior sensitivity studies that compared r-process results using different global mass models (e.g., Wanajo (2004), Farouqi (2010), Arcones (2011)) by instead varying individual neutron separation energies within a single model to pinpoint the most influential nuclei. It identifies two mechanisms by which a single separation energy affects abundances: an equilibrium effect via the Saha equation (first recognized by Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957)) and a freezeout photodissociation effect described by Surman (2009). The results provide a prioritized list of neutron-rich nuclei for measurement at facilities such as CARIBU at ANL, FRIB at MSU, and others, and the authors note that a future paper will explore the dependence on different astrophysical scenarios.
