# Inferring identified hadron production in $pp$ collisions with physics-informed machine learning at the LHC

> Machine learning has become a powerful tool in high-energy collider experiments, which enables the studies based on data-driven approaches to complex reconstruction and regression tasks. The study of identified hadron spectra in pseudorapidity regions beyond detector acceptance, which is limited to mid-rapidity regions, carries important information about particle production, yet remains unmeasured. In this work, we develop a physics-informed neural network, trained on PYTHIA8 $pp$ collisions at $\sqrt{s}=13.6$ TeV, to infer $p_{\rm T}$ spectra of $Ď€^{\pm}$, $K^{\pm}$, $p/\bar{p}$, $Î›/\barÎ›$, and $K^{0}_{\mathrm{s}}$ in different rapidity regions. Physics-motivated constraints, including particle yield ratios, spectral shape, and smoothness, are incorporated into the loss function. A staged hyperparameter optimization strategy is used to ensure stability. The model achieves yield uncertainties of ${\sim}1.5\%$, $1.8\%$, and $5.83\%$ in the training, interpolation, and extrapolation regimes, respectively, outperforming XGBoost and LightGBM. It further reproduces key observables such as particle yield ratios, the multiplicity dependence of $\langle p_{\rm T} \rangle$, and kinetic freeze-out parameters, indicating that the model captures the underlying physics and provides reliable predictions beyond the measured phase space.

# Inferring identified hadron production in $pp$ collisions with physics-informed machine learning at the LHC

## Key claim
The physics-informed neural network (PINN) can infer transverse momentum spectra of identified hadrons in pseudorapidity regions beyond detector acceptance, achieving yield uncertainties of approximately 5.83% in the extrapolation regime, but this performance is contingent on a staged hyperparameter optimization strategy and the inclusion of physics-motivated constraints (particle yield ratios, spectral shape, and smoothness) in the loss function; without these constraints, tree-based models overfit and produce extrapolation errors above 12%.

## Method
The authors generated approximately 10^10 minimum-bias proton-proton collision events at sqrt(s)=13.6 TeV using PYTHIA8 with the Monash tune via the MAGE pipeline, extracting transverse momentum spectra for pions, kaons, protons/antiprotons, Lambda/anti-Lambda, and K0_s in the pseudorapidity range 0 to 3 with bin width 0.4. They trained a physics-informed neural network with a staged three-step hyperparameter optimization (exploration, refinement, final search) using Optuna and Tree-structured Parzen Estimator, incorporating curvature regularization, shape consistency, and particle yield ratio constraints (K/pi, p/pi, Lambda/K0_s) into the loss function with periodic physics updates every 5 epochs. The model was compared against XGBoost and LightGBM gradient-boosted decision trees, with performance evaluated in training (|eta| < 1.2 plus 1.4-1.6), interpolation (1.2-1.4), and extrapolation (1.6-3.0) regions.

## Key numbers
- Total events generated: 9.7 x 10^9 minimum-bias pp collisions at sqrt(s)=13.6 TeV
- Pseudorapidity range: 0 to 3 with bin width Delta eta = 0.4 (15 bins total)
- Transverse momentum range: 0.0 to 10.0 GeV/c with non-uniform binning (0.1 GeV/c below 3.0, 0.5 GeV/c from 3.0-6.0, 1.0 GeV/c from 6.0-10.0)
- PINN best-seed yield uncertainties: 1.08% (training), 1.78% (interpolation), 5.83% (extrapolation)
- XGBoost best-seed yield uncertainties: 0.97% (training), 4.07% (interpolation), 12.57% (extrapolation)
- LightGBM best-seed yield uncertainties: 0.94% (training), 4.01% (interpolation), 12.50% (extrapolation)
- PINN mean yield uncertainty across 30 seeds: 1.25% (training), 1.70% (interpolation), 10.22% (extrapolation)
- Three multiplicity classes: class 1 (0 < Nch < 20), class 2 (20 <= Nch < 50), class 3 (Nch >= 50)
- Total spectra: 225; total datapoints: 9000; input features per datapoint: 8

## Context
This work extends machine learning applications in high-energy physics, where methods have been applied to reconstruction, classification, and regression tasks (Sahoo 2025, Goswami 2024, Prasad 2023, Mallick 2021-2023). It addresses the limitation of detector acceptance at mid-rapidity (ALICE 2008, 2023) by using physics-informed neural networks to predict particle spectra in unmeasured pseudorapidity regions, incorporating constraints that preserve spectral smoothness, shape consistency, and particle yield ratios. The approach enables reconstruction of particle yields in eta gaps where direct experimental measurements are unavailable, and allows extraction of bulk properties such as kinetic freeze-out temperature and radial flow velocity (Schnedermann 1993, Prasad 2021, ALICE 2013) across a wider kinematic range than currently accessible.
