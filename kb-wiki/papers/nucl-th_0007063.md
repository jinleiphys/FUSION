# New method for measuring azimuthal distributions in nucleus-nucleus collisions

> The methods currently used to measure azimuthal distributions of particles in heavy ion collisions assume that all azimuthal correlations between particles result from their correlation with the reaction plane. However, other correlations exist, and it is safe to neglect them only if azimuthal anisotropies are much larger than 1/sqrt(N), with N the total number of particles emitted in the collision. This condition is not satisfied at ultrarelativistic energies. We propose a new method, based on a cumulant expansion of multiparticle azimuthal correlations, which allows to measure much smaller values of azimuthal anisotropies, down to 1/N. It is simple to implement and can be used to measure both integrated and differential flow. Furthermore, this method automatically eliminates the major systematic errors, which are due to azimuthal asymmetries in the detector acceptance.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new method for measuring azimuthal anisotropies (flow) in nucleus-nucleus collisions that can detect values down to \(1/N\) rather than the \(1/\sqrt{N}\) limit of standard methods, but this improved sensitivity requires that the flow satisfies \(v_n \gg 1/N\) and that higher-order harmonic contributions (e.g., \(v_{2n}\)) are not too large relative to \(v_n\).

## Method

The method uses a cumulant expansion of multiparticle azimuthal correlations to isolate the collective flow contribution from "nonflow" correlations (such as those from resonance decays, momentum conservation, and quantum statistics). By measuring \(2k\)-particle azimuthal correlations and computing the corresponding cumulant, one eliminates nonflow correlations of order less than \(2k\), leaving a signal proportional to \(v_n^{2k}\) plus a residual direct \(2k\)-particle correlation of order \(N^{1-2k}\). The integrated flow is extracted from moments of the distribution of the \(Q_n\)-vector (event flow vector), while differential flow is obtained from correlations between particle azimuths and the \(Q_n\)-vector.

## Key numbers

* The standard two-particle correlation method is reliable only if azimuthal anisotropies are much larger than \(1/\sqrt{N}\), where \(N\) is the total number of particles emitted in the collision.
* The new method allows measurements of azimuthal anisotropies down to \(1/N\).
* Nonflow correlations from sources such as resonance decays and momentum conservation scale as \(1/N\) relative to the uncorrelated part of the two-particle distribution.
* For a \(2k\)-particle correlation, the flow contribution scales as \(v_n^{2k}\) while the direct correlation scales as \(N^{1-2k}\), so \(v_n\) can be measured if \(v_n \gg N^{-1+1/2k}\).
* At SPS energies, \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) are of order a few percent, close to the limit of detectability with standard methods.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier studies (Dinh, Borghini, 2000) showing that nonflow correlations are comparable in magnitude to flow signals at ultrarelativistic energies, and it contradicts the assumption used in standard analyses (Danielewicz, 1985; Poskanzer, 1998) that all azimuthal correlations arise from the reaction plane. The method enables reliable flow measurements at RHIC and LHC energies where flow is weak and nonflow correlations from minijets and other sources become significant, and it provides a systematic way to eliminate detector acceptance asymmetries.
