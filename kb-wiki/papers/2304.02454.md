# Temperature and Strong Magnetic Field Effects in Dense Matter

> We study consistently the effects of magnetic field on hot and dense matter. In particular, we look for differences that arise due to assumptions that reproduce the conditions produced in particle collisions or astrophysical scenarios, such as in the core of fully evolved neutron stars (beyond the protoneutron star stage). We assume the magnetic field to be either constant or follow a profile extracted from general relativity calculations of magnetars and make use of two realistic models that can consistently describe chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement to quark matter, the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) and the Polyakov-loop extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) models. We find that net isospin, net strangeness, and weak chemical equilibrium with leptons can considerably change the effects of temperature and magnetic fields on particle content and deconfinement in dense matter. We finish by discussing the possibility of experimentally detecting quark deconfinement in dense and/or hot matter and the possible role played by magnetic fields.

## Key claim

The authors find that net isospin, net strangeness, and weak chemical equilibrium with leptons can considerably change the effects of temperature and magnetic fields on particle content and deconfinement in dense matter, but these effects depend on whether the system is modeled as neutron-star matter or heavy-ion collision matter, with the magnetic field assumed either constant or following a profile from general relativity calculations of magnetars.

## Method

The authors used two relativistic models that consistently describe chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement to quark matter: the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model and the Polyakov-loop extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) model. They applied these models to hot and dense matter under strong magnetic fields, considering two scenarios: neutron-star matter (charge neutral, in weak chemical equilibrium with leptons and strangeness) and heavy-ion collision matter (isospin symmetric, zero net strangeness). The magnetic field was treated either as constant or following a profile extracted from general relativity calculations of magnetars, with anomalous magnetic moment (AMM) effects included for baryons and leptons.

## Key numbers

* For the CMF model at T=0, just before the phase transition (energy density 3.38 fm^-4), there is an 11% pressure increase from B=0 to B=1.44x10^19 G, and an additional 11% pressure increase when accounting for AMM effects in the latter case.
* For neutron-star matter at T=0 with B=1.44x10^18 G, the baryon chemical potential at the deconfinement phase transition is 1344 MeV, with energy density jump of 8.09 fm^-4 across the transition.
* For neutron-star matter at T=0 with B=1.44x10^19 G, the baryon chemical potential at the deconfinement phase transition is 1368 MeV, with energy density jump of 9.14 fm^-4 across the transition.
* For neutron-star matter at T=45 MeV with B=1.44x10^18 G, the baryon chemical potential at the deconfinement phase transition is 1306 MeV, with energy density jump of 7.98 fm^-4 across the transition.
* For neutron-star matter at T=45 MeV with B=1.44x10^19 G, the baryon chemical potential at the deconfinement phase transition is 1323 MeV, with energy density jump of 8.82 fm^-4 across the transition.
* The magnetic field profile coefficients are: a = -7.69x10^-1 G^2/(A m^2), b = 1.20x10^-3 G^2/(A m^2 MeV), c = -3.46x10^-7 G^2/(A m^2 MeV^2), with the critical field for the electron B_c = 4.414x10^13 G.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of dense matter under magnetic fields that typically considered either zero temperature or zero baryon chemical potential, motivated by the 2017 detection of gravitational waves from a neutron star merger by LIGO/Virgo. It builds on prior approaches using Walecka-type models (Strickland 2012, Ferrer 2019, Rabhi 2011, Isayev 2011) and NJL/PNJL or MIT bag models (Dexheimer 2012, Fune 2019, Gyory 2022, Mishra 2022) that studied quark systems in neutron-star or heavy-ion collision conditions, but now simultaneously accounts for magnetic field and temperature effects with different conservation laws and equilibria. The results enable more realistic modeling of neutron-star mergers and heavy-ion collisions, where both temperature and magnetic fields are significant, and allow discussion of experimental detection of quark deconfinement in dense and/or hot matter.
