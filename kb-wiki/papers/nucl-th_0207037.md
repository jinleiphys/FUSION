# Correlation Functions and Cumulants in Elliptic Flow Analysis

> We consider various methods of flow analysis in heavy ion collisions and compare experimental data on corresponding observables to the predictions of our saturation model proposed earlier. We demonstrate that, due to the nature of the standard flow analysis, azimuthal distribution of particles with respect to reaction plane determined from the second order harmonics should always be proportional to $\cos 2(ϕ-Ψ_R)$ independent of the physical origin of particle correlations (flow or non-flow). The amplitude of this distribution is always physical and proportional to $v_2$. Two-particle correlations analysis is therefore a more reliable way of extracting the shape of physical azimuthal anisotropy. We demonstrate that two-particle correlation functions generated in our minijet model of particle production are in good agreement with the data reported by PHENIX. We discuss the role of non-flow correlations in the cumulant flow analysis and demonstrate using a simple example that if the flow is weak, higher order cumulants analysis does not significantly reduce the contribution of non-flow correlations to elliptic flow observable $v_2$ in RHIC data.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard method of determining the reaction plane from the second harmonic of particle multiplicity forces the azimuthal distribution of particles with respect to that plane to be proportional to cos 2(φ − Ψ_R) regardless of whether the underlying correlations are physical flow or non-flow effects; consequently, two-particle correlation functions are a more reliable way to extract the true shape of azimuthal anisotropy.

## Method

The authors analyze the standard reaction-plane method for elliptic flow in heavy ion collisions, deriving the expected azimuthal distribution of particles with respect to the experimentally determined reaction plane under both autocorrelation (no physical correlations) and weak pairwise correlation scenarios. They then calculate two-particle azimuthal correlation functions using their previously proposed minijet model based on saturation physics, relaxing the large-rapidity-interval approximation to match PHENIX detector acceptance, and compare the model predictions to experimental data. Finally, they examine higher-order cumulant analysis by computing fourth and higher cumulants in a model where all of the standard v₂ arises from non-flow two-particle correlations.

## Key numbers

* Saturation scale estimate: Q_s² ≈ 2 GeV², corresponding to α_s(Q_s) ≈ 0.3, from fits to RHIC data (citing Kharzeev, Levin, Nardi).
* Parametric estimate of non-flow v₂ from two-particle correlations: v₂^{non-flow} ∼ α_s / √(S_⊥^A) ∼ α³, while geometric contributions from classical fields give v₂^{geom} ∼ α⁴, showing non-flow correlations are parametrically dominant when coupling is small.
* Parametric estimate of HBT correlations: dN^{HBT}/(d²k₁ dy₁ d²k₂ dy₂) ∼ S_⊥^{A²}/α_s² ∼ 1/α_s¹⁰, comparable to the uncorrelated piece but confined to |k₁ − k₂| ≲ 1/R.
* In the autocorrelation example with N independent particles, the distribution with respect to reaction plane is (1/(2π)²)[1 + √(π/N) cos 2(φ − Ψ_R)] up to o(1/N) corrections.
* For weak physical correlations, the distribution becomes (1/(2π)²)[1 + √(π N) v₂(p_T,B) v₂(B) cos 2(φ_{p_T} − Ψ_R)], with reaction plane resolution Δ = (√(π N)/2) v₂(B).
* PHENIX detector pseudorapidity acceptance is limited to 0.7 units in the central rapidity region.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier saturation-based minijet model (Kovchegov and Tuchin, 2002) that successfully described the saturation of v₂(p_T) at high p_T and its centrality dependence. It directly addresses the reliability of flow analysis methods, showing that the reaction-plane method cannot distinguish flow from non-flow correlations in the azimuthal shape, contrary to assumptions in standard flow analyses (Poskanzer and Voloshin, 1998). The paper also responds to the cumulant method proposed by Borghini, Dinh, and Ollitrault (2001) as a way to reduce non-flow contributions, demonstrating with a concrete example that for weak flow at RHIC energies, higher-order cumulants do not significantly reduce the non-flow contribution to v₂, consistent with STAR analysis results (STAR Collaboration, 2002). The comparison to PHENIX correlation function data (PHENIX Collaboration, 2002) tests the model's predictions beyond the large-rapidity-interval approximation used in the original work.
