# Before and After: How has the SNO NC measurement changed things?

> We present "Before and After" global oscillation solutions, as well as predicted "Before and After" values and ranges for ten future solar neutrino observables (for BOREXINO, KamLAND, SNO, and a generic p-p neutrino detector). We have performed global analyses using the full SNO day-night energy spectrum and, alternatively, just the SNO CC and NC rates and the day-night asymmetry.The ``Before'' case includes all solar neutrino data (and some theoretical improvements) available prior to April 20, 2002 and the ``After'' case includes, in addition, the new SNO data on the CC, NC, and day-night asymmetry. The LMA solution is the only currently allowed MSW oscillation solution at ~99% CL. The LOW solution is allowed only at more than 2.5 sigma, SMA at 3.7 sigma or 4.7 sigma (depending upon analysis strategy), and pure sterile oscillations at 4.7sigma. Small mixing angles are ``out''(pure sterile is ``way out''); MSW with large mixing angles is definitely ``in''. Vacuum oscillations are allowed at 3 sigma, but not a 2 sigma. Precise maximal mixing is excluded at 3.2 sigma for MSW solutions and at 2.8 sigma for vacuum solutions. Most of the predicted values for future observables for the BOREXINO, KamLAND, and future SNO measurements are changed only by minor amounts by the inclusion of the recent SNO data. In order to test the robustness of the allowed neutrino oscillation regions and the predictions for future observables, we have carried out calculations using a variety of strategies to analyze the SNO and other solar neutrino data.

# Before and After: How has the SNO NC measurement changed things?

## Key claim

The LMA (Large Mixing Angle) MSW solution is the only currently allowed oscillation solution at approximately 99% CL, but this conclusion depends on the assumption that errors are well understood for all reported measurements; the authors test robustness by using three different analysis strategies and find that the allowed regions and predictions are rather robust with respect to the choices among different analysis procedures.

## Method

The authors performed global analyses of solar neutrino data using three different analysis strategies: (a) treating the 8B neutrino flux as a free parameter, (b) constraining the 8B flux by the BP00 standard solar model prediction, and (c) including the total Super-Kamiokande rate explicitly with a free normalization factor. They used a two-step procedure to account for energy dependence of the survival probability for each value of Delta m^2 and tan^2 theta, and alternatively used the full SNO day-night energy spectrum. The "Before" case included all solar neutrino data available prior to April 20, 2002, and the "After" case added the new SNO data on CC, NC, and day-night asymmetry.

## Key numbers

- LMA best-fit: Delta m^2 = 5.0 x 10^-5 eV^2, tan^2 theta = 4.2 x 10^-1, chi^2_min = 45.5 with 46 degrees of freedom (49% goodness-of-fit)
- LOW solution allowed only at 98.8% CL (2.5 sigma), with Delta chi^2 = 8.8 relative to LMA
- SMA excluded at 3.7 sigma or 4.7 sigma depending on analysis strategy
- Pure sterile oscillations excluded at more than 4.7 sigma
- Precise maximal mixing (tan^2 theta = 1) excluded at 3.2 sigma for MSW solutions and at 2.8 sigma for vacuum solutions
- LMA 3-sigma allowed range: 2.3 x 10^-5 < Delta m^2 < 3.7 x 10^-4 eV^2
- LMA 3-sigma allowed mixing: 0.24 < tan^2 theta < 0.89
- LOW 3-sigma allowed range: 3.5 x 10^-8 < Delta m^2 < 1.2 x 10^-7 eV^2
- LOW 3-sigma allowed mixing: 0.43 < tan^2 theta < 0.86
- Best-fit 8B flux from LMA: f_B = 1.07 +/- 0.08 (1-sigma) in units of BP00 prediction (5.05 x 10^6 cm^-2 s^-1)
- SNO NC measurement: f_B = 1.01 [1 +/- 0.12] (1-sigma)

## Context

This paper extends the global oscillation analyses previously performed by the same authors (Bahcall, Gonzalez-Garcia, and Pena-Garay, 2001) and others including Fogli et al. (2002), Barger et al. (2002), and the SNO collaboration itself (2002). The work was contemporaneous with papers by Barger et al., Strumia et al., and the Indian group, though those did not calculate predictions for the ten future solar neutrino observables studied here. The results enable refined predictions for BOREXINO, KamLAND, and future SNO measurements, and demonstrate that the LMA solution is robustly favored while small mixing angles and pure sterile oscillations are definitively excluded.
