# Nonlinear waves in strongly interacting relativistic fluids

> During the past decades the study of strongly interacting fluids experienced a tremendous progress. In the relativistic heavy ion accelerators, specially the RHIC and LHC colliders, it became possible to study not only fluids made of hadronic matter but also fluids of quarks and gluons. Part of the physics program of these machines is the observation of waves in this strongly interacting medium. From the theoretical point of view, these waves are often treated with li-nearized hydrodynamics. In this text we review the attempts to go beyond linearization. We show how to use the Reductive Perturbation Method to expand the equations of (ideal and viscous) relativistic hydrodynamics to obtain nonlinear wave equations. These nonlinear wave equations govern the evolution of energy density perturbations (in hot quark gluon plasma) or baryon density perturbations (in cold quark gluon plasma and nuclear matter). Different nonlinear wave equations, such as the breaking wave, Korteweg-de Vries and Burgers equations, are obtained from different equations of state (EOS). In nuclear matter, the Walecka EOS may lead to a KdV equation. We explore equations of state such as those extracted from the MIT Bag Model and from QCD in the mean field theory approach. Some of these equations are integrable and have analytical solitonic solutions. We derive these equations also in spherical and cylindrical coordinates. We extend the analysis to two and three dimensions to obtain the Kadomtsev-Petviashvili (KP) equation, which is the generalization of the KdV. The KP is also integrable and presents analytical solitonic solutions. In viscous relativistic hydrodynamics we have second order patial derivatives which physically represent dissipation terms. We present numerical solutions and their corresponding algorithms for the cases where the equations are not integrable.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how the Reductive Perturbation Method (RPM) can systematically derive nonlinear wave equations (breaking wave, Korteweg-de Vries, Burgers, Kadomtsev-Petviashvili) from relativistic hydrodynamics for strongly interacting fluids, going beyond the standard linearized treatment; the specific nonlinear equation obtained depends on the chosen equation of state (EOS) and on whether ideal or viscous hydrodynamics is used.

## Method

The authors apply the Reductive Perturbation Method to the equations of ideal and viscous relativistic hydrodynamics (the relativistic Euler and Navier-Stokes equations) using a stretched-coordinate expansion. They then insert different equations of state, including the Walecka model for nuclear matter, the MIT Bag Model for quark-gluon plasma, and mean-field QCD approaches, to derive specific nonlinear wave equations for energy-density or baryon-density perturbations. The analysis is extended to spherical and cylindrical coordinates and to two and three spatial dimensions, yielding the Kadomtsev-Petviashvili equation. For non-integrable cases, numerical solutions and algorithms are presented.

## Key numbers

* The speed of diffusion for a mode with wavenumber \(k\) in the acausal relativistic Navier-Stokes equation is \(V_{diff}(k) = 2 \eta / (\varepsilon_0 + p_0) \, k\), which diverges as \(k \to \infty\).
* After introducing the Maxwell-Cattaneo relaxation time \(\tau_\pi\), the causal diffusion speed becomes \(V_{diff_{causal}}(k) = \sqrt{\eta / [(\varepsilon_0 + p_0) \tau_\pi]}\), which remains finite as \(k \to \infty\).
* The dispersion relation for the causal diffusion equation gives \(|w|^2 = k^2 \, \eta / [(\varepsilon_0 + p_0) \tau_\pi]\).

## Context

This review extends earlier work on nonlinear waves in nuclear matter, such as the derivation of a KdV equation for baryon density in cold nuclear matter using nonrelativistic ideal hydrodynamics by Fogaça, Navarra, and collaborators (2010), and previous studies on nonlinear waves in cold QGP in the mean-field approach (2011) and their three-dimensional extension (2013). The paper systematizes the RPM approach across multiple EOS and dimensions, enabling future searches for solitonic solutions (e.g., KdV solitons) that could explain phenomena like nuclear transparency in proton-nucleus collisions, and provides a framework for studying Mach cones and wave propagation in the quark-gluon plasma produced at RHIC and LHC.
