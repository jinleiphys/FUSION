# On supercorrelated systems and phase space entrainment

> It is demonstrated that power-laws which are modified by logarithmic corrections arise in supercorrelated systems. Their characteristic feature is the energy attributed to a state (or value of a general cost function) which depends nonlinearly on the phase space distribution of the constituents. A onedimensional dissipative deterministic model is introduced which is attracted to a supercorrelated state (phase space entrainment). Extensions of this particular model may have applications in the study of transport and equilibration phenomena, particularly for supply and information networks, or for chemical and biological nonequilibrium systems, while the qualitative arguments presented here are believed to be of more general interest.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that power-laws modified by logarithmic corrections arise in "supercorrelated systems," where the energy (or cost function) attributed to a state depends nonlinearly on the phase space distribution of the constituents; the authors introduce a one-dimensional dissipative deterministic model that is attracted to such a supercorrelated state, but note that the model in its present form cannot thermalize and that extensions are needed for realistic applications.

## Method

The authors define supercorrelated systems via a correlation energy \(E_j C_j[p]\), where \(C_j[p]\) is a product of particle distributions \(p\) with correlation degree \(c\). They apply a variational principle to the Shannon entropy with nonlinear constraints to derive the equilibrium-like distribution \(p_x(E_j)\), obtaining a transcendental equation solved in terms of the Lambert \(W\)-function. They then introduce a two-body Hamiltonian model for interacting cells, add phenomenological damping terms that conserve total energy, and analyze the asymptotic solutions of the equations of motion to show that phase space entrainment (convergence of trajectories) leads to a supercorrelated state with \(c=2\).

## Key numbers

* The transient dynamical behavior in relativistic heavy-ion collisions commences at times of order \(0.3-0.9 \times 10^{-24}\) s (from the Introduction, citing experimental observations).
* The asymptotic distribution for \(\eta \ll 1\) yields a power-law: \(p_x(E_j) = Z_x^{-1} \left(1 + (c-1) Z_x^{1-c} \beta N_c E_j\right)^{-1/(c-1)}\) (Eq. 13).
* The asymptotic distribution for \(\eta \gg 1\) yields a log-modified power-law: \(p_x(E_j) = Z_x^{-1} \left( F\left((c-1) Z_x^{1-c} \beta N_c E_j\right) / \left((c-1) Z_x^{1-c} \beta N_c E_j\right) \right)^{1/(c-1)}\), with \(F(x) \equiv \ln(x) \left(1 + \ln(\ln(x))/\ln(x)\right)^{-1}\) (Eqs. 14-15).
* The damped oscillator equation for the energy difference \(E_-\) is \(\ddot E_- + \gamma \dot E_- + \omega^2 E_- = 0\), with \(\omega^2 \equiv (4\mathcal{E}/(\xi\zeta))^2\) (from Section 3).
* The amplitude of the oscillating energy difference \(E_-\) is damped as \(\propto \exp[-\gamma t/2]\) (from Section 3).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier study (Elze and Kodama, 2004, cited as "Us") on power-laws emerging from dynamically generated correlations, and contrasts with stochastic approaches to power-laws (e.g., Denisov, Beck, Biro, all cited as 2003-2004). The paper introduces a deterministic dissipative model as an alternative to stochastic explanations, and the resulting log-modified power-laws resemble Tsallis distributions (Tsallis, 1988; Tsallis, 2004) and Zipf-Mandelbrot laws (Zipf, 1949; Mandelbrot, 1953). The model is intended as a testing ground for prethermalization phenomena observed in heavy-ion collisions (Berges, 2004) and may enable future studies of transport and equilibration in condensed matter, polymer networks, and biological nonequilibrium systems.
