# Quantum Simulating Nature's Fundamental Fields

> Simulating key static and dynamic properties of matter -- from creation in the Big Bang to evolution into sub-atomic and astrophysical environments -- arising from the underlying fundamental quantum fields of the Standard Model and their effective descriptions, lies beyond the capabilities of classical computation alone. Advances in quantum technologies have improved control over quantum entanglement and coherence to the point where robust simulations are anticipated to be possible in the foreseeable future. We discuss the emerging area of quantum simulations of Standard-Model physics, challenges that lie ahead, and opportunities for progress in the context of nuclear and high-energy physics.

## Key claim

Quantum simulation of Standard-Model fields is anticipated to become possible in the foreseeable future due to advances in quantum technologies, but the authors emphasize that the full range of Standard-Model Hamiltonians may not be available in analog simulators because subatomic components such as quarks and gluons cannot be externally controlled due to confinement, making the answer to whether colorless objects can mimic colorful quark and gluon dynamics presently unknown.

## Method

The paper reviews the workflow of quantum simulation for field theories, which consists of preparing a non-trivial initial state, performing unitary time evolution (often via Trotterization on digital platforms or continuous evolution on analog platforms), and measuring observables. The authors discuss the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian formulation of lattice gauge theories, where fermions are distributed over a staggered lattice and gauge bosons are placed on links, with local gauge invariance enforced by Gauss's law operators that commute with the Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

* SU(3) Yang-Mills gauge theory electric energy of a single plaquette was calculated on ibmq_Manila for up to 100 Trotter-steps, including the 1, 3, 3-bar, and 8 irreducible representations of SU(3)
* Vacuum-vacuum transition rates for a 1+1D scalar field theory were obtained using ibmq_Manhattan for three lattice sites with two qubits per site
* Local matter density after a quantum quench in a 1+1D quantum link model was evaluated on a 71-site optical-lattice quantum simulator, asymptoting to a constant value with matching temperatures
* Inversion probability of two out of four coherently evolved neutrinos was evaluated as a function of Trotterized time using the Quantinuum H1-1 trapped-ion computer
* For two-flavor QCD on one spatial site (two staggered sites), the Hilbert space content maps to 12 qubits

## Context

This review extends the vision of Feynman (1982) for quantum simulation and builds upon the foundational Kogut-Susskind (1974) Hamiltonian formulation of lattice gauge theories, as well as the Byrnes-Yamamoto (2005) digitization scheme for gauge links. The paper surveys recent progress since the review by Banuls (2019) and the report by Bauer (2022), covering developments in analog, digital, and hybrid quantum simulation platforms for gauge theories, effective field theories, and neutrino physics. The work enables future first-principles simulations of finite-density systems and real-time dynamics that are intractable on classical computers due to sign problems, and identifies open questions regarding thermalization dynamics, fragmentation in colliders, and collective neutrino oscillations in astrophysical environments.
