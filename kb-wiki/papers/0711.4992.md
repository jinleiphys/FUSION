# Isoscaling, Symmetry Energy and Thermodynamic Models

> The isoscaling parameter usually denoted by $α$ depends upon both the symmetry energy coefficient and the isotopic contents of the dissociating systems. We compute $α$ in theoretical models: first in a simple mean field model and then in thermodynamic models using both grand canonical and canonical ensembles. For finite systems the canonical ensemble is much more appropriate. The model values of $α$ are compared with a much used standard formula. Next we turn to cases where in experiments, there are significant deviations from isoscaling. We show that in such cases, although the grand canonical model fails, the canonical model is capable of explaining the data.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the commonly used formula relating the isoscaling parameter α to the symmetry energy coefficient (α ≈ 4(C_s/T)[(Z_0(1)/A_0(1))^2 - (Z_0(2)/A_0(2))^2]) is only approximately correct; the relationship depends on temperature and system size, and a different functional form (δμ_n ≈ 4C_s(Z_1/A_1 - Z_2/A_2)) emerges naturally from mean-field theory, while at high temperatures δμ_n becomes independent of symmetry energy entirely.

## Method

The authors computed the isoscaling parameter α and the neutron chemical potential difference δμ_n in three theoretical frameworks: a finite-temperature Hartree-Fock mean-field model with Skyrme interactions, a canonical thermodynamic multifragmentation model using recursion relations for partition functions, and a grand canonical ensemble model. They compared these calculations to the standard approximate formula and to experimental data from Sn+Sn and Ni+Be collisions, examining cases where isoscaling breaks down for small fragmenting systems.

## Key numbers

- For symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density ρ_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}, the symmetry energy coefficient C_s = 23.5 MeV, composed of C_s(k.e) ≈ 12.3 MeV and C_s(p.e) = 11.2 MeV
- The canonical model calculations used dissociating systems with A_0 = 168, Z_0 = 75 (reaction 1) and A_0 = 186, Z_0 = 75 (reaction 2), representing ^{112}Sn+^{112}Sn and ^{124}Sn+^{124}Sn central collisions
- For Ni+Be systems, the dissociating systems were N_0 = 35, Z_0 = 32 (^{58}Ni+^9Be) and N_0 = 41, Z_0 = 32 (^{64}Ni+^9Be)
- The freeze-out volume used in thermodynamic calculations was V = 6V_0
- At high temperature, δμ_n approaches T ln(N_2/N_1), independent of symmetry energy
- The canonical model calculations used temperature T = 5 MeV for Ni+Be comparisons after secondary decay corrections

## Context

This work extends previous studies of isoscaling in heavy-ion collisions by Xu, Tsang, and collaborators (early 2000s) and addresses deviations from isoscaling observed by Mocko (2006) for smaller fragmenting systems. The authors compare their thermodynamic model results to the standard formula derived from the expanding excited source (EES) model by Tsang (2001) and to antisymmetrised molecular dynamics calculations by Ono (2004). The paper enables more accurate extraction of the symmetry energy coefficient from experimental isoscaling data by accounting for temperature dependence and system-size effects, and provides a canonical framework that can explain deviations from isoscaling where the grand canonical model fails.
