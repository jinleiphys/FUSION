# Particle production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions: A consistent hydrodynamic approach

> We derive relativistic viscous hydrodynamic equations invoking the generalized second law of thermodynamics for two different forms of the non-equilibrium single-particle distribution function. We find that the relaxation times in these two derivations are identical for shear viscosity but different for bulk viscosity. These equations are used to study thermal dilepton and hadron spectra within longitudinal scaling expansion of the matter formed in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. For consistency, the same non-equilibrium distribution function is used in the particle production prescription as in the derivation of the viscous evolution equations. Appreciable differences are found in the particle production rates corresponding to the two non-equilibrium distribution functions. We emphasize that an inconsistent treatment of the non-equilibrium effects influences the particle production significantly, which may affect the extraction of transport properties of quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim

The paper derives second-order viscous hydrodynamic equations from two different forms of the non-equilibrium single-particle distribution function and shows that the relaxation times for shear viscosity are identical for both forms, but the relaxation times for bulk viscosity differ; using the same non-equilibrium distribution function consistently in both the hydrodynamic evolution and the particle production prescription leads to appreciable differences in dilepton and hadron yields between the two cases.

## Method

The authors start from the entropy four-current expressed in terms of the single-particle distribution function and impose the generalized second law of thermodynamics to derive second-order evolution equations for bulk and shear viscous pressures. They consider two forms of the non-equilibrium correction to the distribution function: Case 1 (Eq. phi1) which includes terms up to second order in momenta consistent with Grad's 14-moment approximation, and Case 2 (Eq. phi2) which includes only quadratic momentum corrections. These equations are then applied to the Bjorken longitudinal scaling expansion scenario, and thermal dilepton and hadron spectra are computed using the same non-equilibrium distribution function in the particle production prescription as in the derivation of the evolution equations.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature T0 = 310 MeV and initial time tau0 = 0.5 fm/c, typical for the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider
- Freezeout temperature = 160 MeV
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio eta/s = 1/(4pi), corresponding to the conjectured lower bound
- Bulk relaxation time for Case 1: tau_Pi^(1) = (epsilon+P)/(P T) * (zeta/s)
- Bulk relaxation time for Case 2: tau_Pi^(2) = (18)/(5T) * (zeta/s)
- Shear relaxation time (same for both cases): tau_pi = (6/T) * (eta/s)
- Critical temperature Tc approximately 184 MeV
- At times tau greater than approximately 3 fm/c (temperatures less than approximately 1.2 Tc), bulk viscous pressure dominates shear pressure
- Dilepton invariant mass values studied: M = 1, 1.5, 2, 3 GeV/c^2
- Rho(770) meson mass m_rho = 775 MeV and decay width Gamma_rho = 149 MeV
- Peak in dilepton invariant mass spectrum at M = 0.77 GeV/c^2 from rho(770) decay

## Context

This work extends the derivation of second-order viscous hydrodynamics from the entropy four-current approach, building on the framework of Israel (1979) and Romatschke (2009). It contrasts with prior studies such as Monnai (2009) and Dusling (2008) which used ideal hydrodynamic evolution followed by non-ideal particle production, and Bhatt (2011), Denicol (2009), and Dusling (2011) which used second-order viscous evolution but ideal or Navier-Stokes corrections at freezeout. The paper demonstrates that inconsistent treatment of non-equilibrium effects in evolution and particle production significantly affects yields, which has implications for extracting transport properties of quark-gluon plasma in hydrodynamic simulations.
