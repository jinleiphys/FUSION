# Surface Boiling - a New Type of Instability of Highly Excited Atomic Nuclei

> The evolution of the nuclear matter density distribution with excitation energy is studied within the framework of a finite-range interacting Fermi gas model and microcanonical thermodynamics in Thomas-Fermi approximation. It is found that with increasing excitation energy, both infinite and finite systems become unstable against infinitesimal matter density fluctuations, albeit in different ways. In modeling, this instability reveals itself via an apparent negative heat capacity of the system and is seen to result in the volume boiling in the case of infinite matter and surface boiling in the case of finite systems. The latter phenomenon of surface boiling is unique to small systems and it appears to provide a natural explanation for the observed saturation-like patterns in what is commonly termed caloric curves and what represents functional dependence of nuclear temperature on the excitation energy.

# Surface Boiling - a New Type of Instability of Highly Excited Atomic Nuclei

## Key claim

The authors claim that finite nuclear systems exhibit a "surface boiling" instability at excitation energies well below the volume boiling point of infinite nuclear matter, and that this surface boiling provides a natural explanation for the observed saturation of caloric curves (temperature versus excitation energy) at temperatures around 5 MeV. The claim is conditional on the specific finite-range interacting Fermi gas model in Thomas-Fermi approximation, and the authors note that the surface boiling is "unique to small systems" where the surface domain plays a relatively important role.

## Method

The authors model nuclear matter using a finite-range interacting Fermi gas model with a Skyrme-type equation of state (compressibility modulus K=220 MeV) and microcanonical thermodynamics in Thomas-Fermi approximation. For bulk matter, they compute isotherms and follow the zero-pressure adiabatic trajectory to identify the boiling point where entropy no longer has a maximum as a function of density. For finite systems (A=100 nucleons), they parameterize the density profile using an error function with half-density radius and Sussmann surface width, then evaluate configuration entropy via the level density parameter and zero-temperature energy, searching for the onset of negative heat capacity signaling instability.

## Key numbers

- Volume boiling temperature for bulk matter: T_boil = 10.8 MeV at zero pressure, with density approximately 0.6 rho_0 (where rho_0 = 0.168 fm^-3)
- Excitation energy per nucleon at the onset of negative heat capacity in bulk matter: E*/A = 17 MeV (zero pressure)
- Latent heat for volume boiling: E/A_F - E/A_B approximately 5.5 MeV per nucleon
- Excitation energy per nucleon at the bulk boiling point: E/A_B approximately 12.5 MeV
- Surface boiling in finite A=100 system sets in around excitation energy E*/A approximately 4.5-5 MeV, with temperatures around 5 MeV
- Model parameters: a = -62.43 MeV, b = 70.75 MeV, sigma = 2.0, Fermi energy E_Fermi_o = 38.11 MeV, folding width lambda = 1.4 fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' ongoing program (Toke and Swiatecki, various years; Toke and collaborators, various years) to construct a microcanonical thermodynamic framework for highly excited nuclear systems produced in heavy-ion collisions. It specifically identifies the negative heat capacity previously observed in thermally expanding bulk matter (Toke, 2010) as the boiling point, and introduces the new concept of surface boiling for finite systems. The surface boiling temperature of approximately 5 MeV matches the saturation temperatures observed experimentally in caloric curves by Natowitz and collaborators (various years), which volume boiling at over 11 MeV could not explain. The work enables future studies of isospin effects in surface boiling and quantum effects on boiling temperatures for systems of various sizes.
