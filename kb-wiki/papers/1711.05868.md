# A complex path around the sign problem

> We review recent attempts at dealing with the sign problem in Monte Carlo calculations by deforming the region of integration in the path integral from real to complex fields. We discuss the theoretical foundations, the algorithmic issues and present some results for low dimensional field theories in both imaginary and real time.

## Key claim

The paper reviews a method for mitigating the sign problem in Monte Carlo calculations by deforming the integration contour from real to complex fields, but notes that this approach does not provide a general solution and that a general solution is extremely unlikely to exist, as the sign problem is NP-complete in at least one specific model.

## Method

The method deforms the domain of integration from real fields ℝ^N to an N-dimensional manifold ℳ_T embedded in complexified field space ℂ^N, obtained by evolving every point ζ of ℝ^N along the anti-holomorphic flow dφ_i/dt = ∂S/∂φ_i (complex conjugate) for a fixed time T. The flow increases the real part of the action while keeping the imaginary part constant, so at large T the manifold ℳ_T approaches the combination of "thimbles" (multidimensional generalizations of steepest descent contours) equivalent to the original integration domain. The integral over ℳ_T is then computed by sampling real fields ζ with an effective action S_eff = S[φ_T(ζ)] - log det J(ζ), where J is the Jacobian of the flow map, using a Metropolis algorithm with reweighting of the residual phase.

## Key numbers

- The average sign ⟨e^{-iS_I}⟩_{S_R} decays exponentially as the spacetime volume βV grows, making the sign problem severe in the thermodynamic limit.
- The computational cost of computing the Jacobian determinant is of order N^3, where N is the number of field degrees of freedom.
- For free theories (quadratic action), the estimator W_1 equals the exact Jacobian J, making it a good estimator in weakly coupled theories.

## Context

This work extends the stationary phase/steepest descent method for one-dimensional integrals to many-dimensional path integrals, building on the thimble approach introduced by Cristoforetti et al. (2012). It addresses the sign problem that affects finite-density systems in nuclear physics (cold dense hadronic/quark matter) and condensed matter models like the Hubbard model away from half-filling, as well as real-time calculations. The method enables Monte Carlo calculations in systems where the Euclidean action is complex, but open questions remain about whether the approach will work in the continuum and infinite volume limits for four-dimensional theories, and whether single-thimble dominance holds in those limits.
