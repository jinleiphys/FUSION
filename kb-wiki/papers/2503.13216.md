# A multi-messenger mass determination method for LISA Neutron-Star White-Dwarf Binaries

> Determining the mass of the neutron stars (NSs) accurately improves our understanding of the NS interior and complicated binary evolution. However, the masses of the systems are degenerate with orbital inclination angle when using solely gravitational waves (GWs) or electromagnetic measurements, especially for face-on binaries. Taking advantages of both GWs and optical observations for LISA neutron-star white-dwarf (NS-WD) binaries, we propose a mass determination method utilising multi-messenger observational information. By combining the binary mass function obtained from optical observations and a GW mass function, that we introduce, derived from GW observations, we demonstrate how we can set improved constraints on the NS mass and break the degeneracy in the mass and viewing inclination determination. We further comment on the universal relation of the error bar of the GW mass function versus GW signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), and propose a simple method for the estimate of capability of GW observations on mass determination with {LISA}. We show that for ultra-compact NS-WD binaries within our Galaxy, the mass of the NS can be constrained to within an accuracy of +- 0.2 \solarmass with the proposed method.

## Key claim
By combining a conventional binary mass function from optical observations with a newly introduced "GW mass function" derived from gravitational wave data, the degeneracy between neutron star mass and orbital inclination angle can be broken for LISA-detected neutron-star-white-dwarf binaries, enabling neutron star mass constraints to within ±0.2 solar masses for ultra-compact Galactic systems.

## Method
The authors used the gbmcmc analysis pipeline to generate mock LISA data and perform Markov Chain Monte Carlo parameter estimation for hypothetical neutron-star-white-dwarf binaries with circular orbits, orbital periods of 10-60 minutes, distances of 1-30 kpc, neutron star mass of 2 solar masses, and companion masses of 0.1-0.6 solar masses. They constructed a GW mass function from the gravitational wave amplitude and chirp mass, then combined it with the optical binary mass function to constrain the orbital inclination and thereby determine the neutron star mass. The signal-to-noise ratio threshold was relaxed to SNR=2 to account for definitional differences across the literature.

## Key numbers
- Neutron star mass can be constrained to within an accuracy of ±0.2 solar masses for ultra-compact NS-WD binaries within our Galaxy
- The GW frequency drift over 4 years (expected LISA mission lifetime) is approximately Δf_GW ≈ 1 × 10^(-11) Hz, much smaller than the GW frequency itself
- The effective GW strain for a typical system is approximately 3 × 10^(-20) × I(ι) × (30 min/P_orb)^(7/6) × (M/(2/5)^(1/5) M_sun)^(5/3) × (T/4 yr)^(1/2) × (10 kpc/d)
- The GW frequency derivative is approximately 1.8 × 10^(-11) Hz yr^(-1) × (η/0.3) × (M/2.6 M_sun)^(5/3) × (1 hr/P_orb)^(11/3)
- The LISA arm length is L = 2.5 Gm, with characteristic frequency f_* = 19.09 mHz

## Context
This work extends the multi-messenger approach demonstrated by GW170817 (Abbott 2017) to continuous gravitational wave sources, specifically the approximately 100 detectable NS-WD binaries predicted by Tauris (2018) for LISA. The method addresses the mass-inclination degeneracy that limits both standalone gravitational wave measurements and electromagnetic observations, particularly for face-on binaries. The approach enables improved constraints on the neutron star equation of state by providing cleaner mass measurements, building on prior work by Demorest (2010), Antoniadis (2013), Cromartie (2020), and Fonseca (2021) who identified massive neutron stars in NS-WD binaries. The Galactic population of NS-WD binaries detectable by LISA is estimated at a few to a few hundred based on population synthesis models by Nelemans (2001), Benacquista (2001), Cooray (2004), Chen (2020), and Korol (2023).
