# Quark-Novae in Low-mass X-ray Binaries II: Application to G87-7 and to GRB 110328A

> We propose a simple model explaining two outstanding astrophysical problems related to compact objects: (1) that of stars such as G87-7 (alias EG 50) that constitute a class of relatively low-mass white dwarfs which nevertheless fall away from the C/O composition and (2) that of GRB 110328A/Swift J164449.3+57345 which showed spectacularly long-lived strong X-ray flaring, posing a challenge to standard GRB models. We argue that both these observations may have an explanation within the unified framework of a Quark-Nova occurring in a low-mass X-ray binary (neutron star- white dwarf). For LMXBs where the binary separation is sufficiently tight, ejecta from the exploding Neutron Star triggers nuclear burning in the white dwarf on impact, possibly leading to Fe-rich composition compact white dwarfs with mass 0.43M_sun < M_WD < 0.72M_sun, reminiscent of G87-7. Our results rely on the assumption, which ultimately needs to be tested by hydrodynamic and nucleosynthesis simulations, that under certain circumstances the WD can avoid the thermonuclear runaway. For heavier white dwarfs (i.e. M_WD > 0.72M_sun) experiencing the QN shock, degeneracy will not be lifted when Carbon burning begins, and a sub-Chandrasekhar Type Ia Supernovae may result in our model. Under slightly different conditions, and for pure He white dwarfs (i.e. M_WD < 0.43M_sun), the white dwarf is ablated and its ashes raining down on the Quark star leads to accretion-driven X-ray luminosity with energetics and duration reminiscent of GRB 110328A. We predict additional flaring activity towards the end of the accretion phase if the Quark star turns into a Black Hole.

# Quark-Novae in Low-mass X-ray Binaries II: Application to G87-7 and to GRB 110328A

## Key claim

A Quark-Nova (explosive transition of a neutron star to a quark star) occurring in a tight low-mass X-ray binary with a white dwarf companion can explain both the existence of low-mass iron-rich white dwarfs like G87-7 and the long-lived X-ray flaring of GRB 110328A, provided that the white dwarf can avoid thermonuclear runaway, an assumption that ultimately requires testing via hydrodynamic and nucleosynthesis simulations.

## Method

The authors derive analytic conditions for the fate of a white dwarf (WD) companion when Quark-Nova ejecta impact it, based on binary separation, WD mass, and the temperature induced by the shock. For CO WDs with mass between 0.43 and 0.72 solar masses, the shock temperature (approximately 70 keV or higher) can exceed the Fermi energy, lifting degeneracy and allowing controlled nuclear burning to iron-group elements without thermonuclear runaway. For pure He WDs below 0.43 solar masses, the WD is ablated and its ashes accrete onto the resulting quark star, powering X-ray emission via Bondi accretion.

## Key numbers

- WD mass range for controlled burning without ablation: 0.43 solar masses < M_WD < 0.72 solar masses
- Threshold temperature for carbon-carbon burning in a CO WD: approximately 70 keV
- Threshold temperature for helium burning via triple-alpha process: 8.6 keV
- Binary separation condition for carbon burning in a 0.43 solar mass CO WD: a_10 < 0.5 (where a_10 is separation in units of 10^10 cm)
- Roche lobe separation for a 0.43 solar mass WD with a 1.8 solar mass NS: 3.7 x 10^9 cm
- Total energy release from nuclear burning in a 0.43 solar mass WD: approximately 4 x 10^50 erg
- Ejection speed of processed nuclei: approximately 3.1 x 10^3 km/s (for fiducial parameters)
- WD escape speed for 0.43 solar mass: approximately 3.2 x 10^3 km/s
- Fermi energy of a 0.43 solar mass WD: approximately 35 keV
- GRB 110328A average X-ray luminosity: L_X ~ 2.5 x 10^47 erg/s over ~10^5 s, total energy E_X,tot ~ 2.5 x 10^52 erg
- Minimum accretion energy release from ablated WD material: approximately 2.3 x 10^52 erg for M_WD = 0.25 solar masses
- Fiducial accretion rate onto the quark star: approximately 5 x 10^-5 solar masses per second
- Estimated duration of early active accretion phase: approximately 2.9 days

## Context

This work extends the Quark-Nova model previously applied to short gamma-ray bursts (Ouyed, Staff, Jaikumar 2011) to two specific astrophysical puzzles. The existence of low-mass white dwarfs with iron-rich composition, exemplified by G87-7 (Provencal et al. 1998), has been difficult to explain via standard stellar evolution; alternative explanations include strange dwarf models (Mathews et al. 2006; Gilles et al. 2007). GRB 110328A (Bloom et al. 2011; Burrows et al. 2011) showed X-ray flaring lasting days rather than hours, previously attributed to tidal disruption of a star by a massive black hole. The model predicts that such events should be associated with globular clusters where LMXBs are common, and that additional flaring may occur if the quark star transitions to a black hole.
