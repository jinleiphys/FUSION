# Electron Ion Collider: The Next QCD Frontier - Understanding the glue that binds us all

> This White Paper presents the science case of an Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), focused on the structure and interactions of gluon-dominated matter, with the intent to articulate it to the broader nuclear science community. It was commissioned by the managements of Brookhaven National Laboratory (BNL) and Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility (JLab) with the objective of presenting a summary of scientific opportunities and goals of the EIC as a follow-up to the 2007 NSAC Long Range plan. This document is a culmination of a community-wide effort in nuclear science following a series of workshops on EIC physics and, in particular, the focused ten-week program on "Gluons and quark sea at high energies" at the Institute for Nuclear Theory in Fall 2010. It contains a brief description of a few golden physics measurements along with accelerator and detector concepts required to achieve them, and it benefited from inputs from the users' communities of BNL and JLab. This White Paper offers the promise to propel the QCD science program in the U.S., established with the CEBAF accelerator at JLab and the RHIC collider at BNL, to the next QCD frontier.

# Electron Ion Collider: The Next QCD Frontier - Understanding the glue that binds us all

## Key claim
This White Paper presents the science case for an Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) as the next QCD frontier, articulating that such a facility is needed to understand the structure and interactions of gluon-dominated matter. The document is a community-wide summary of scientific opportunities and goals, commissioned as a follow-up to the 2007 NSAC Long Range plan, rather than a report of completed experimental results.

## Method
The paper describes two proposed EIC accelerator designs: eRHIC at Brookhaven National Laboratory, based on a multi-pass Energy Recovery Linac (ERL) colliding with the existing RHIC hadron facility, and MEIC at Jefferson Lab, a ring-ring collider using the 12 GeV CEBAF recirculating linac. Both designs include detailed specifications for interaction regions, beam cooling systems (Coherent electron Cooling for eRHIC, multi-phase electron cooling for MEIC), polarization schemes, and projected luminosity performance across multiple ion species.

## Key numbers
- eRHIC: polarized electron beam up to 21 GeV colliding with polarized protons up to 250 GeV, covering center-of-mass energy range 30 to 145 GeV for polarized e+p
- eRHIC: center-of-mass energy range 20 to 90 GeV for e+A (for large A)
- eRHIC: luminosity in the range 10^33 to 10^34 cm^-2 s^-1
- eRHIC: maximum electron beam power loss due to synchrotron radiation limited to about 12 MW, corresponding to 50 mA electron beam current at 16 GeV and about 18 mA at 21 GeV
- eRHIC: hadron bunch intensity of 0.3 x 10^11 protons/bunch, with future upgrade (HL-eRHIC) to 3 x 10^11 protons/bunch
- MEIC: design point of 5 GeV electrons colliding with 60 GeV protons yields luminosity 5.6 x 10^33 cm^-2 s^-1 for full-acceptance detector
- MEIC: luminosity above 10^34 cm^-2 s^-1 per interaction point for high-luminosity detector configuration
- MEIC: electron ring stores 3 to 12 GeV polarized electrons; ion collider ring stores 25 to 100 GeV protons or up to 40 GeV/u light to heavy ions
- MEIC: 80% polarization for both electron and light ion beams
- MEIC: stored beam currents up to 0.5 A for protons or ions and 3 A for electrons
- MEIC: electron synchrotron radiation power should not exceed 20 kW/m
- MEIC: ion collider ring circumference approximately 1470 m
- MEIC: expected polarization up to 85% for ion beams

## Context
This White Paper extends the QCD science program established with the CEBAF accelerator at JLab and the RHIC collider at BNL, building on community workshops and a focused ten-week program at the Institute for Nuclear Theory in Fall 2010. It was commissioned by the managements of BNL and JLab as a follow-up to the 2007 NSAC Long Range plan. The document describes two complementary accelerator concepts (eRHIC and MEIC) that would enable next-generation measurements of gluon distributions, parton saturation, and hadron structure, with the second edition (2014) sharpening the science case in light of recent data from BNL, CERN, and JLab experiments.
