# BBN And The CMB Constrain Light, Electromagnetically Coupled WIMPs

> (Abridged) In the presence of a light WIMP (< 30 MeV), there are degeneracies among the nature of the WIMP, its couplings to standard model particles, its mass m_chi, and the number of equivalent neutrinos beyond the standard model, Delta N_nu. These degeneracies cannot be broken by the CMB constraint on the effective number of neutrinos, N_eff. However, big bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) is affected by a light WIMP and equivalent neutrinos, so the combination of BBN and CMB can break some of the degeneracies. Here, BBN predictions for abundances in the presence of a light WIMP and equivalent neutrinos are explored, and estimates of their observationally determined relic abundances are used to limit m_chi, Delta N_nu, and the present Universe baryon density (Omega_B h^2). These constraints are explored for Majorana and Dirac fermion WIMPs, as well as for real and complex scalar WIMPs that couple to electrons, positrons, and photons. In a separate paper this analysis is repeated for WIMPs that couple only to the standard model neutrinos, and constraints for the two cases are contrasted. Without a light WIMP but allowing for equivalent neutrinos, the combined BBN and CMB constraints favor N_eff = 3.46 +/- 0.17, Omega_B h^2 = 0.0224 +/- 0.0003, and Delta N_nu = 0.40 +/- 0.17. In this case, standard BBN (Delta N_nu = 0) is disfavored at ~98% confidence, and the presence of one sterile neutrino (Delta N_nu = 1) is disfavored at > 99% confidence. Allowing a light WIMP and Delta N_nu equivalent neutrinos together, the combined BBN and CMB data provide lower limits to the WIMP masses (> 0.5 - 5 MeV) that depend on the nature of the WIMP, favor m_chi ~8 MeV slightly over no WIMP, and loosen constraints on equivalent neutrinos, Delta N_nu = 0.65+0.46-0.35. While Delta N_nu = 0 is still disfavored at ~95% confidence with a light WIMP, Delta N_nu = 1 is now allowed.

## Key claim

The combination of BBN and CMB constraints can break degeneracies among light WIMP mass, its nature, and the number of equivalent neutrinos that the CMB alone cannot resolve; the analysis finds that a light WIMP with mass around 8 MeV is slightly favored over no WIMP, and that the presence of one sterile neutrino (Delta N_nu = 1) becomes allowed when a light WIMP is present, whereas it is disfavored at >99% confidence without one.

## Method

The authors modified the Kawano BBN code to include the energy density and entropy of light WIMPs (Majorana and Dirac fermions, real and complex scalars) coupled to electrons, positrons, and photons, assuming instantaneous neutrino decoupling at 2 MeV. They computed primordial abundances of deuterium, helium-4, helium-3, and lithium-7 across a grid of WIMP masses and equivalent neutrino numbers, then performed a joint likelihood analysis combining these BBN predictions with Planck CMB constraints on N_eff and Omega_B h^2.

## Key numbers

- Without a light WIMP but allowing equivalent neutrinos: N_eff = 3.46 +/- 0.17, Omega_B h^2 = 0.0224 +/- 0.0003, Delta N_nu = 0.40 +/- 0.17 (all at 68% C.L.)
- Standard BBN (Delta N_nu = 0) is disfavored at ~98% confidence when no light WIMP is present
- One sterile neutrino (Delta N_nu = 1) is disfavored at >99% confidence when no light WIMP is present
- Allowing a light WIMP and equivalent neutrinos together gives lower limits on WIMP mass of m_chi > 0.5 - 5 MeV depending on WIMP nature
- With a light WIMP present, the best-fit equivalent neutrino number is Delta N_nu = 0.65 (+0.46, -0.35)
- With a light WIMP present, Delta N_nu = 0 is disfavored at ~95% confidence, while Delta N_nu = 1 is now allowed
- The analysis favors m_chi ~ 8 MeV slightly over no WIMP

## Context

This work extends earlier BBN calculations by Kolb et al. (1986) and Serpico and Raffelt (2002) and more recent analyses by Boehm et al. (2013), which did not allow for equivalent or sterile neutrinos alongside light WIMPs. It also builds on the CMB-focused analysis by Steigman (2013, "Chimera" paper) that demonstrated degeneracies between WIMP mass and equivalent neutrinos. The results enable future joint constraints from BBN and CMB to distinguish between electromagnetically coupled and neutrino-coupled WIMPs, with the companion paper addressing the neutrino-coupled case.
