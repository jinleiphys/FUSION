# The Role of Nuclear Physics in Understanding the Cosmos and the Origin of Elements

> This popular lecture, given in the conference celebrating contributions of Akito Arima to physics on the occasion of his 80th anniversary, outlines the role of nuclear physics in understanding the origin of elements.

## Key claim

This popular lecture argues that nuclear physics is essential for understanding the origin of elements, but it explicitly acknowledges that "one should not be left with the impression that we understand everything about the origin of the elements," citing the unresolved discrepancy between observed and predicted primordial lithium-7 abundances as an open question.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of nuclear astrophysics, starting with Hans Bethe's identification of the pp-chain and CNO cycle as the energy sources for stars, then traces the evolution of solar neutrino experiments (Homestake, SAGE, Gallex/GNO, Kamiokande/SuperKamiokande, SNO) that demonstrated neutrino flavor transformation. It then discusses the r-process for producing elements heavier than iron, focusing on core-collapse supernovae as a candidate site and the role of neutrino-neutrino interactions in setting the neutron-to-proton ratio.

## Key numbers

- Solar neutrinos: "Trillions of solar neutrinos go through your body every second"
- Davis's Homestake experiment: located "4800 feet underground" in South Dakota
- Supernova energy: the proto-neutron star carries "99% of the gravitational binding energy of the pre-supernova star (about 10^53 ergs)"
- Supernova neutrino emission: "Altogether 10^58 neutrinos are emitted"
- Stellar mass thresholds: core collapse occurs for stars with masses "8 to 12 M⊙" (oxygen-neon-magnesium core) and "more than 12 M⊙" (iron core)
- Neutrino flavor sensitivity: electron neutrino scattering cross-section is "about six times larger" than for other flavors
- Solar neutrino energy: neutrinos at the high-energy tail carry "about 20 times as much energy as the rest mass of electron"

## Context

This lecture extends the foundational work of Bethe (1938, 1939) on stellar nuclear reactions and Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957) on nucleosynthesis in supernovae. It summarizes the solar neutrino program initiated by Bahcall and Davis (1964) and the subsequent experiments (SAGE, Gallex/GNO, Kamiokande, SNO) that confirmed neutrino flavor transformation. The paper enables future work on refining nuclear physics inputs into solar models (Adelberger 1998) and understanding the role of neutrino-neutrino interactions in r-process nucleosynthesis (Balantekin 2004, Duan 2009, Raffelt 2010).
