# Relativistic quantum theories and neutrino oscillations

> Neutrino oscillations are examined under the broad requirements of Poincaré-invariant scattering theory in an S-matrix formulation. This approach can be consistently applied to theories with either field or particle degrees of freedom. The goal of this paper is to use this general framework to identify all of the unique physical properties of this problem that lead to a simple oscillation formula. We discuss what is in principle observable, and how many factors that are important in principle end up being negligible in practice.

## Key claim

Neutrino oscillations can be derived from a Poincaré-invariant S-matrix formulation without assuming a specific field-theoretic or particle-based dynamics, but the standard oscillation formula only emerges when external particles have momentum distributions (wave packets) and the neutrino kinematics are ultrarelativistic; the paper identifies that many representation-dependent features of different theoretical approaches become unobservable under these conditions.

## Method

The authors formulate neutrino oscillations using second-order time-ordered perturbation theory within Poincaré-invariant quantum mechanics, treating the neutrino as an intermediate virtual state between two weak interactions at macroscopically separated space-time points. They construct asymptotic states with localized wave packets for the external particles (protons, electrons, neutrons) and analyze the S-matrix element for the reaction sequence p + e → n' + ν followed by n + ν → p' + e', generalizing to all lepton flavors. The derivation examines how the energy denominator, momentum conservation, and space-time translation operators combine to produce the oscillation phase, and identifies which theoretical choices (kinematic subgroup, scattering-equivalent Hamiltonians) do not affect the observable S-matrix.

## Key numbers

- Neutrino mass-squared differences are constrained by experiments to a scale "much less than that of the virtual neutrino momenta," making neutrinos "very relativistic"
- The energy approximation for ultrarelativistic neutrinos is given as E_j ≈ p_j + m_j²/(2p_j), with phase differences proportional to (m_i² - m_j²)
- The space-time displacement x_ba = x_b - x_a transforms like a four-vector under Lorentz transformations, with the spatial component L known to within a scale characteristic of the reactor size (e.g., "a few kilometers" for a reactor experiment)
- The principal-value term in the energy denominator is "exponentially suppressed" relative to the delta-function term for large time differences T, where T is "very large on microscopic scales"

## Context

This work extends the S-matrix approach to neutrino oscillations previously examined by Cardall (2000) using quantum field theory, but shows the conclusions are not limited to field-theoretic treatments. It addresses the long-standing debate among approaches by Kayser (1981), Giunti (1991, 2003), Grimus (1996), Beuthe (2002, 2003), Lipkin (2004), Cohen (2008), and Akhmedov (2009), demonstrating that all lead to the same oscillation formula despite differing perspectives. The formalism enables a clear separation of what is in principle observable from representation-dependent features, and shows that external wave packets are essential for oscillations to occur, a point emphasized by Cohen (2008) and Akhmedov (2009), while also explaining why many theoretical distinctions become negligible in practice.
