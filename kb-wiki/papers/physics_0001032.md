# Decay channels and appearance sizes of doubly anionic gold and silver clusters

> Second electron affinities of Au_N and Ag_N clusters and the dissociation energies for fission of the Au_N^{2-} and Ag_N^{2-} dianions are calculated using the finite-temperature shell-correction method and allowing for triaxial deformations. Dianionic clusters with N > 2 are found to be energetically stable against fission, leaving electron autodetachment as the dominant decay process. The second electron affinities exhibit pronounced shell effects in excellent agreement with measured abundance spectra for Au_N^{2-} (N < 30), with appearance sizes N=12 for gold dianionic clusters and N=24 for silver dianionic clusters.

## Key claim

Doubly anionic gold and silver clusters with more than two atoms are energetically stable against binary fission, leaving electron autodetachment as the dominant decay process; the appearance sizes are N=12 for gold dianionic clusters and N=24 for silver dianionic clusters, with the shift attributed to the smaller work function of silver.

## Method

The authors used a finite-temperature semi-empirical shell-correction method (SCM) incorporating triaxial deformations, separating the free energy into a smooth liquid-drop-model (LDM) part and a Strutinsky-type shell-correction term from valence electrons treated as independent particles in a modified Nilsson Hamiltonian. They calculated second electron affinities and fission dissociation energies for Au_N^{2-} and Ag_N^{2-} clusters at T=300 K, comparing the SCM results with LDM predictions and experimental abundance spectra from Penning trap measurements.

## Key numbers

- Appearance size for gold dianionic clusters: n_a^{2-}(Au) = 12
- Appearance size for silver dianionic clusters: n_a^{2-}(Ag) = 24
- Work function used for gold: W(Au) = 5.31 eV
- Work function used for silver: W(Ag) = 4.26 eV
- Exothermic fission (Δ_{N,P} < 0) found only for the smallest cluster size N=2
- Wigner-Seitz radius for both Au and Ag: r_s = 3.01 a.u.
- Spillout parameter: δ_0 = 1.31 a.u. for both metals

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical studies of multiply charged anionic fullerenes by Yannouleas and Landman (1994) and sodium clusters by Yannouleas and Landman (1993), and directly addresses recent experimental observations of gold dianionic clusters by Schweikhard et al. (1999). Unlike doubly cationic metal clusters studied by Brechignac et al. (1995) and Yannouleas et al. (1999), fission is not a dominant decay process for these dianions; the results enable prediction of silver dianion appearance sizes and provide a framework for understanding electronic shell effects in multiply charged anionic metal clusters.
