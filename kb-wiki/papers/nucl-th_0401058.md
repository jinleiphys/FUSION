# Five-Dimensional Fission-Barrier Calulations from Se-70 to Cf-252

> We present fission-barrier-height calculations for nuclei throught the Periodic Table based on a realistic macroscopic-microscopic model. Compared to other calculations: (1) we use a deformation space of sufficiently high dimension, sampled densely enough to describe the relevant topography of the fission potential, (2) we unambiguously find the physically relevant saddle points in this space, and (3) we formulate our model so that we obtain continuity of the potential energy at the division point between a single system and separated fission fragments or colliding nuclei, allowing us to (4) describe both fission-barrier heights and ground-state masses throughout the Periodic Table.

## Key claim

The authors present fission-barrier-height calculations for nuclei from Se-70 to Cf-252 using a macroscopic-microscopic model with a five-dimensional deformation space sampled on millions of points, which they argue is necessary to correctly identify saddle points; the abstract glosses over that the model parameters were readjusted in a single iteration and that a self-consistent redetermination of ground-state and saddle-point deformations would change calculated energies by less than 0.1 MeV.

## Method

The authors used the macroscopic-microscopic finite-range liquid-drop model (FRLDM) generalized to include shape-dependence of the Wigner and A^0 macroscopic terms, with the three-quadratic-surface (3QS) shape parameterization providing five deformation degrees of freedom: elongation, neck diameter, mass asymmetry, and left and right fragment deformations. They defined a grid of 2,610,885 points in this five-dimensional space and applied an immersion method (analogous to imaginary water flow) to unambiguously identify saddle points by progressively filling the ground-state minimum and determining when a prespecified point in the fission valley becomes wet. Six macroscopic model parameters were then readjusted to optimize reproduction of both ground-state binding energies and fission-barrier heights.

## Key numbers

- RMS error of 0.999 MeV for calculated fission-barrier heights compared to experimental data for 31 nuclei
- RMS error of 0.752 MeV for calculated ground-state masses compared to the 1989 mass table
- Grid of 2,610,885 points in the five-dimensional deformation space, with 15 points each in neck diameter and left and right fragment deformations, 20 points in mass asymmetry, and 41 points in nuclear elongation
- Calculated barrier for Se-70: 37.58 MeV (experimental: 39.40 MeV, difference: 1.81 MeV)
- Calculated barrier for U-238: 5.48 MeV (experimental: 5.50 MeV, difference: 0.01 MeV)
- Calculated barrier for Cf-252: 5.63 MeV (experimental: 4.80 MeV, difference: -0.83 MeV)
- Calculated barrier for Mo-90: 40.92 MeV (experimental: 40.92 MeV, difference: -0.00 MeV)
- Calculated barrier for Hg-198: 21.41 MeV (experimental: 20.40 MeV, difference: -1.01 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic approach of Moller (1995) by using a five-dimensional deformation space with over 10,000 times as many grid points as previous calculations, which the authors argue is necessary to correctly identify saddle points that were previously missed. The calculations address discrepancies noted by Sierk (1985, 1986) where earlier model predictions for light nuclei near A=100 were 1 to 5 MeV too low compared to new experimental data from McMahan (1985), Delis (1991), and Jing (1999). The improved model enables consistent calculation of both fission-barrier heights and ground-state masses throughout the Periodic Table, and the authors note that the immersion method for saddle-point identification could be applied to self-consistent mean-field models if those models can overcome their current difficulties with effective forces and saddle-point location.
