# Detailed microscopic calculation of stellar electron and positron capture rates on $^{24}$Mg for O+Ne+Mg core simulations

> Few white dwarfs, located in binary systems, may acquire sufficiently high mass accretion rates resulting in the burning of carbon and oxygen under nondegenerate conditions forming a O+Ne+Mg core. These O+Ne+Mg cores are gravitationally less bound than more massive progenitor stars and can release more energy due to the nuclear burning. They are also amongst the probable candidates for low entropy r-process sites. Recent observations of subluminous Type II-P supernovae (e.g., 2005cs, 2003gd, 1999br, 1997D) were able to rekindle the interest in 8 -- 10 M$_{\odot}$ which develop O+Ne+Mg cores. Microscopic calculations of capture rates on $^{24}$Mg, which may contribute significantly to the collapse of O+Ne+Mg cores, using shell model and proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory, were performed earlier and comparisons made. Simulators, however, may require these capture rates on a fine scale. For the first time a detailed microscopic calculation of the electron and positron capture rates on $^{24}$Mg on an extensive temperature-density scale is presented here. This type of scale is more appropriate for interpolation purposes and of greater utility for simulation codes. The calculations are done using the pn-QRPA theory using a separable interaction. The deformation parameter, believed to be a key parameter in QRPA calculations, is adopted from experimental data to further increase the reliability of the QRPA results. The resulting calculated rates are up to a factor of 14 or more enhanced as compared to shell model rates and may lead to some interesting scenario for core collapse simulators.

# Detailed microscopic calculation of stellar electron and positron capture rates on \(^{24}\)Mg for O+Ne+Mg core simulations

## Key claim
The pn-QRPA electron capture rates on \(^{24}\)Mg are enhanced by up to a factor of 14 or more compared to earlier shell-model rates, which may significantly alter core-collapse simulations of O+Ne+Mg stars; however, the enhancement factor depends strongly on density, decreasing to roughly a factor of 4 at the highest densities (\(\rho \approx 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\)).

## Method
Electron and positron capture rates on \(^{24}\)Mg were calculated using the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) with a separable interaction, employing Nilsson-model single-particle states and BCS pairing. The deformation parameter was taken from experimental data (0.606) rather than from theoretical mass models, and experimental excitation energies and log \(ft\) values were substituted for theoretical ones where available within 0.5 MeV. Rates were computed on a fine temperature-density grid (132 excited states up to ~40 MeV excitation) suitable for direct interpolation in simulation codes.

## Key numbers
- Electron capture rates on \(^{24}\)Mg are enhanced by up to a factor of **14 or more** compared to shell-model rates at densities around \(\rho = 10^{3}\) g cm\(^{-3}\).
- At \(\rho \approx 10^{7}\) g cm\(^{-3}\), the enhancement factor is roughly **13**; at \(\rho \approx 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\), it decreases to about **4**.
- The total calculated B(GT+) strength (quenched) is **3.34**; the experimentally extracted strength up to 7 MeV excitation is **1.36** (Rakers et al., 2003), while the pn-QRPA value for that region is **2.53**.
- The deformation parameter used is **0.606** (Raman et al., 2001), compared to 0.435 used in earlier pn-QRPA calculations (Nabi and Klapdor, 1999).
- The quenching factor applied to Gamow-Teller strength is **0.77**.

## Context
This work extends earlier pn-QRPA calculations of weak rates for sd-shell nuclei (Nabi and Klapdor, 1999) by using an experimentally determined deformation and a finer temperature-density grid. It contrasts with the shell-model rates of Oda et al. (1994) and the earlier compilation of Fuller, Fowler and Newman (1982), which gave lower electron capture rates. The enhanced rates may help resolve discrepancies in O+Ne+Mg core-collapse simulations: Kitaura et al. (2006) and Gutierrez et al. (2005) used shell-model rates and obtained delayed or no explosions, whereas the present work suggests that faster electron capture could lower electron fraction and entropy, potentially transforming a collapse into an explosion in future multi-dimensional models with consistent neutrino transport.
