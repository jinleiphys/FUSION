# Mass fractions in stellar interior during presupernova evolution

> We assume nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) conditions and use Saha Equation to calculate mass fractions in stellar interior during presupernova evolution of massive stars. Our ensemble contains 728 nuclei. The distinguishing feature of our calculation is a state by state summation of nuclear level densities up to 10 MeV for all nuclei considered in our ensemble. This leads to a more realistic description of nuclear partition function which poses one of the largest uncertainties in the mass fraction calculations under conditions of NSE. The Coulomb correction term was taken into account for the calculation of ground state binding energies of the nucleons. Our calculated mass fractions are up to four orders of magnitude smaller than previous calculation. The current calculation could prove useful for study of stellar matter during presupernova phases of massive stars.

# Mass fractions in stellar interior during presupernova evolution

## Key claim
The authors calculate mass fractions in stellar interior under nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) using a state-by-state summation of nuclear level densities up to 10 MeV for all 728 nuclei in their ensemble, which leads to mass fractions up to four orders of magnitude smaller than previous calculations by Liu (2007). The key qualifier is that this large discrepancy arises from the combination of three factors: the extended energy range for discrete level summation, inclusion of Coulomb correction in binding energies, and the much larger nuclear ensemble.

## Method
The authors assume NSE conditions and use the Saha equation to compute mass fractions for an ensemble of 728 nuclei up to mass number 100. They calculate temperature-dependent nuclear partition functions by summing over discrete energy levels (experimental and pn-QRPA theoretical) up to 10 MeV excitation energy, then use a uniform Fermi gas level density formula for the continuum from 10 to 20 MeV. Coulomb correction to ground-state binding energies is included using Hartree-Fock approximation in Wigner-Seitz cells, and mass fractions are computed as functions of electron fraction Ye at fixed temperature and density.

## Key numbers
- Ensemble contains 728 nuclei with mass number up to 100
- Discrete energy levels summed up to 10 MeV excitation energy for all nuclei
- Calculated mass fractions are up to four orders of magnitude smaller than Liu (2007) at T = 5×10^9 K, ρ_st = 1×10^7 g/cm^3, and Ye = 0.5
- At T = 5×10^9 K and ρ_st = 1×10^9 g/cm^3, 92Mo appears among the most abundant nuclei and alpha particles are very abundant
- For even-even nuclei, nuclear partition functions from this work and Rauscher (2000) compare well; for odd-A and odd-odd nuclei, discrepancies of one order of magnitude or more are seen
- At high temperatures, nuclear partition functions from Rauscher (2003) can be larger by an order of magnitude or more compared to this work

## Context
This work extends the NSE mass fraction calculations of Liu (2007) and Hartmann (1985) by using a much larger nuclear ensemble (728 vs. 65 nuclei in Dim02) and by extending discrete level summation from 3 MeV (as in Dim02) to 10 MeV. The authors compare their nuclear partition functions with those of Rauscher (2000) and Rauscher (2003), finding significant differences for odd-A and odd-odd nuclei. The calculated mass fractions are intended to serve as initial conditions for explosive nucleosynthesis and r-process network calculations, and the authors state they are in the process of calculating the rate of change of lepton-to-baryon ratio for typical presupernova conditions.
