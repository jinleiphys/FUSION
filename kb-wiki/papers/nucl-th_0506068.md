# Evolving model-free scattering matrix via evolutionary algorithm: $^{16}$O-$^{16}$O elastic scattering at 350 MeV

> We present a new procedure which enables to extract a scattering matrix $S(l)$ as a complex function of angular momentum directly from the scattering data, without any a priori model assumptions implied. The key ingredient of the procedure is the evolutionary algorithm with diffused mutation which evolves the population of the scattering matrices, via their smooth deformations, from the primary arbitrary analytical $S(l)$ shapes to the final ones giving high quality fits to the data. Due to the automatic monitoring of the scattering matrix derivatives, the final $S(l)$ shapes are monotonic and do not have any distortions. For the $^{16}$O-$^{16}$O elastic scattering data at 350 MeV, we show the independence of the final results of the primary $S(l)$ shapes. Contrary to the other approaches, our procedure provides an excellent fit by the $S(l)$ shapes which support the ``rainbow'' interpretation of the data under analysis.

## Key claim

The authors present a model-free evolutionary algorithm that extracts the scattering matrix \(S(l)\) directly from elastic scattering data without imposing any a priori analytical form, and they demonstrate that for \(^{16}\text{O}\)-\(^{16}\text{O}\) elastic scattering at 350 MeV the resulting monotonic \(S(l)\) shapes support the "rainbow" interpretation, contrary to other flexible approaches that yield nonmonotonic structures.

## Method

The procedure treats each value of the nuclear absorption phase \(\delta_a(l)\) and nuclear refraction phase \(\delta_r(l)\) as independent fitting parameters, forming a population of \(N\) individuals (each a pair of \(l_{\max}\)-dimensional vectors). An evolutionary algorithm with diffused mutation evolves the population from arbitrary primary analytical shapes (Fermi, Gauss, McIntyre, phenomenological, or power-type functions) by applying a replication transformation that adds a Gaussian diffusing function centered at a random mutation point, with mutation amplitude and diffuseness automatically tuned and decreased during the run. The fitness function combines penalties enforcing monotonicity and smoothness conditions on the phases and their derivatives (up to third order) with a standard \(\chi^2\) measure of fit to the experimental differential cross section, computed via Legendre polynomial expansion.

## Key numbers

- The \(\chi^2\) values for fits to the \(^{16}\text{O}\)-\(^{16}\text{O}\) elastic scattering data at 350 MeV are \(2.4-2.5\) per data point.
- The nuclear rainbow angle, corresponding to the minimum of the deflection function \(\Theta(l)\), is \(\theta_r = 61-64^\circ\).
- The maximum in the differential cross section interpreted as the primary nuclear rainbow is observed near \(50^\circ\).
- The number of angular momenta used is \(l_{\max} = 120\); the number of experimental data points is 105.
- When all smoothness requirements on \(\delta_{a,r}(l)\) are abandoned, the procedure yields fits with \(\chi^2 \approx 0.5-0.6\).

## Context

This work extends the line of \(S\)-matrix approaches for heavy-ion elastic scattering at intermediate energies (e.g., McIntyre (1960), Kauffmann (1977), Berezhnoy and Pilipenko (1995), Berezhnoy and Molev (2003)) and the optical potential models of Kobos, Brandan, and Satchler (1988) and Brandan and McVoy (1991), which all argued for a "rainbow" interpretation but could not describe all data features. It contrasts with more flexible methods that introduce nonmonotonic structures via pole-like terms (Allen et al., 1993) or spline basis functions (Cooper, McEwan, and Mackintosh, 1992; McEwan, Cooper, and Mackintosh, 1993), which achieve excellent fits but lose the rainbow interpretation. The procedure enables future extraction of the optical potential directly from data and can be extended to test whether nonmonotonic \(S(l)\) structures are truly necessary in resonance-dominated or parity-dependent scattering cases.
