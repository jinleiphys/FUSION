# Valid QCD Sum Rules for Vector Mesons in Nuclear Matter

> QCD sum rules for vector mesons (rho, omega, phi) in nuclear matter are reexamined with an emphasis on the reliability of various sum rules. Monitoring the continuum contribution and the convergence of the operator product expansion plays a crucial role in determining the validity of a sum rule. The uncertainties arising from less than precise knowledge of the condensate values and other input parameters are analyzed via a Monte-Carlo error analysis. Our analysis leaves no doubt that vector-meson masses decrease with increasing density. This resolves the current debate over the behavior of the vector-meson masses and the sum rules to be used in extracting vector meson properties in nuclear matter. We find a ratio of rho-meson masses of $m_{ρ^*}/m_ρ= 0.78\pm0.08$ at nuclear matter saturation density.

# Valid QCD Sum Rules for Vector Mesons in Nuclear Matter

## Key claim

Vector-meson masses (rho, omega, phi) decrease with increasing nuclear matter density, with the direct sum rule being the only reliable method; the first derivative sum rule is marginal and higher derivative sum rules are invalid when the operator product expansion is truncated at dimension six. This resolves the debate between Hatsuda and Lee (1992), who found decreasing masses using the direct and first derivative sum rules, and Koike (1995), who claimed increasing masses using the first and second derivative sum rules.

## Method

The authors reexamine QCD sum rules for the rho, omega, and phi vector mesons in nuclear matter by analyzing the correlator of conserved vector currents at zero three-momentum in the nuclear medium rest frame. They adopt a pole-plus-continuum ansatz for the spectral density, truncate the operator product expansion at dimension six, and use a Monte-Carlo error analysis with Gaussian distributions for condensate values and input parameters to estimate uncertainties. The validity of each sum rule is assessed by requiring that the highest-dimensional condensate contributes no more than 10% to the QCD side and the continuum contribution is less than 50% of the total phenomenological side, defining a fiducial Borel region where spectral parameters are extracted via chi-squared fits.

## Key numbers

- Ratio of rho-meson masses at nuclear matter saturation density: m_rho*/m_rho = 0.78 ± 0.08
- Ratio of phi-meson masses at nuclear matter saturation density: m_phi*/m_phi = 0.99 ± 0.01
- Nuclear matter saturation density: rho_N^0 = (110 MeV)^3
- Vacuum condensate values used: a = -4 pi^2 <q-bar q>_0 = 0.62 ± 0.05 GeV^3; b = 4 pi^2 <(alpha_s/pi) G^2>_0 = 0.4 ± 0.15 GeV^4
- Sigma term: sigma_N = 45 ± 7 MeV
- Gluon condensate nucleon matrix element: <(alpha_s/pi) G^2>_N = -650 ± 150 MeV
- Strong coupling: alpha_s/pi = 0.117 ± 0.014 at 1 GeV scale

## Context

This work extends the finite-density QCD sum-rule analyses of Hatsuda and Lee (1992) and Asakawa and Ko (1993, 1994) by systematically assessing the reliability of each sum rule individually rather than using the ratio method. It directly contradicts the conclusions of Koike (1995), who claimed vector-meson masses increase in medium based on the second derivative sum rule, which the authors demonstrate is invalid when the OPE is truncated at dimension six. The results support the scaling ansatz of Brown and Rho (1991) and provide the first systematic uncertainty estimates for in-medium hadronic properties via Monte-Carlo error analysis, enabling more reliable predictions for dilepton spectra in heavy-ion collisions.
