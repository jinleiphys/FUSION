# All the Fun of the FAIR: Fundamental physics at the Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research

> The Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research (FAIR) will be the accelerator-based flagship research facility in many basic sciences and their applications in Europe for the coming decades. FAIR will open up unprecedented research opportunities in hadron and nuclear physics, in atomic physics and nuclear astrophysics as well as in applied sciences like materials research, plasma physics and radiation biophysics with applications towards novel medical treatments and space science. FAIR is currently under construction as an international facility at the campus of the GSI Helmholtzzentrum for Heavy-Ion Research in Darmstadt, Germany. While the full science potential of FAIR can only be harvested once the new suite of accelerators and storage rings is completed and operational, some of the experimental detectors and instrumentation are already available and will be used starting in summer 2018 in a dedicated research program at GSI, exploiting also the significantly upgraded GSI accelerator chain. The current manuscript summarizes how FAIR will advance our knowledge in various research fields ranging from a deeper understanding of the fundamental interactions and symmetries in Nature to a better understanding of the evolution of the Universe and the objects within.

## Key claim
The Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research (FAIR) will advance knowledge across hadron physics, nuclear physics, atomic physics, nuclear astrophysics, and applied sciences, but its full science potential can only be harvested once the new suite of accelerators and storage rings is completed and operational (expected full operation in 2025), while some experimental detectors and instrumentation are already available and were used starting in summer 2018 at the upgraded GSI accelerator chain.

## Method
The paper surveys the scientific program of FAIR as organized into four international collaborations: PANDA (hadron spectroscopy via proton-antiproton annihilation), CBM (compressed baryonic matter in heavy-ion collisions), NUSTAR (exotic nuclei far off stability and nuclear astrophysics), and APPA (atomic physics, plasma physics, biophysics, and materials research). It reviews the accelerator complex centered on the superconducting fast-ramping heavy-ion synchrotron SIS100, supplemented by a proton linear accelerator for antiproton production, storage rings for cooled ions and antiprotons, and the Superconducting Fragment Separator (SFRS) for secondary beams of short-lived ions.

## Key numbers
- The lightest scalar glueball is predicted below 2 GeV; glueballs with higher spin and exotic quantum numbers in the mass range from 2.5 to 5 GeV are only accessible with PANDA at FAIR.
- The $D_{s0}^*(2317)$ sits only 45 MeV below the $DK$ threshold; its width is predicted to be 10 keV or less in the quark model but larger than 100 keV if it is a molecule; the current upper limit on its width is 3.8 MeV.
- PANDA simulations indicate that a width resolution of order 100 keV is feasible for the $D_{s0}^*(2317)$.
- The maximum net-baryon density in heavy-ion collisions occurs around 30 AGeV beam energy, where a density of up to ten times that of nuclear ground state is reached.
- The STAR collaboration's preliminary data show a strong increase of the fourth-order cumulant of net-proton distribution at the lowest RHIC energy of $\sqrt{s}=7.7$ GeV, which cannot be explained by presently available models.

## Context
This paper summarizes the scientific scope of FAIR as described in the 2017 NuPECC long range plan, which lists FAIR as a top priority in all six chapters. It extends the experimental programs at RHIC and LHC (which established the Quark-Gluon-Plasma at high temperature and low net-baryon density) into the high-density region of the QCD phase diagram, and builds on the spectroscopy revolution triggered by discoveries such as the $D_{s0}^*(2317)$ at Babar (2003), the $X(3872)$ at Belle (2003), and pentaquark indications at LHCb (2015). The paper enables future searches for the QCD critical point, measurements of hyperon-nucleon and hyperon-hyperon interactions relevant to neutron star structure, and precision tests of strong-field quantum electrodynamics and matter-antimatter symmetries.
