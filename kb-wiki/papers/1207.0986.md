# Reexamining the "finite-size" effects in isobaric yield ratios using a statistical abrasion-ablation model

> The "finite-size" effects in the isobaric yield ratio (IYR), which are shown in the standard grand-canonical and canonical statistical ensembles (SGC/CSE) method, is claimed to prevent obtaining the actual values of physical parameters. The conclusion of SGC/CSE maybe questionable for neutron-rich nucleus induced reaction. To investigate whether the IYR has "finite-size" effects, the IYR for the mirror nuclei [IYR(m)] are reexamined using a modified statistical abrasion-ablation (SAA) model. It is found when the projectile is not so neutron-rich, the IYR(m) depends on the isospin of projectile, but the size dependence can not be excluded. In reactions induced by the very neutron-rich projectiles, contrary results to those of the SGC/CSE models are obtained, i.e., the dependence of the IYR(m) on the size and the isospin of the projectile is weakened and disappears both in the SAA and the experimental results.

## Key claim

The "finite-size" effects in the isobaric yield ratio for mirror nuclei (IYR(m)) that were claimed to prevent obtaining precise physical parameters in the standard grand-canonical and canonical statistical ensembles (SGC/CSE) method are not present in reactions induced by very neutron-rich projectiles, where the dependence of IYR(m) on system size disappears in both the statistical abrasion-ablation (SAA) model and experimental results; however, for projectiles that are not very neutron-rich, the size dependence cannot be excluded.

## Method

The authors used a modified statistical abrasion-ablation (SAA) model, a two-stage model in which the first stage describes nucleon-nucleon collisions in the overlap zone of projectile and target to determine primary fragment distributions, and the second stage models the deexcitation of primary fragments via conventional statistical evaporation. The SAA model incorporates Fermi-type neutron and proton density distributions for the projectile and target, and calculates fragment yields from transmission probabilities based on free nucleon-nucleon cross sections. The IYR(m) values were calculated for 140A MeV reactions of various calcium isotopes (38-52Ca) with 12C, and for 72Zn, 96Zr, and 120Sn projectiles with the same N/Z ratio as 48Ca, then compared to experimental data from 140A MeV 40,48Ca + 9Be, 1A GeV 124,136Xe + Pb, 1A GeV 56Fe + p, and 40A MeV 64,70Zn + Sn reactions.

## Key numbers

- For the 140A MeV 48Ca + 9Be reaction, linear fitting of SAA results gave y = (0.62 ± 0.02)x - (1.24 ± 0.16), while experimental data gave y = (0.75 ± 0.06)x - (0.92 ± 0.50)
- For the 140A MeV 40Ca reaction, the IYR(m) showed a decreasing behavior when x > 8.5 (where x = 2(Z-1)/A^(1/3))
- For the 140A MeV 38Ca projectile, the IYR(m) decreased when x > 7
- For neutron-rich 46-52Ca projectiles, the IYR(m) increased quickly when x of fragments was larger than 0.85 times the x of the projectile
- The IYR(m) for 72Zn, 96Zr, and 120Sn projectiles (same N/Z as 48Ca) showed very little difference when x < 12
- An increase of 0.15 in a_c/T (Coulomb-energy coefficient divided by temperature) introduces a difference of 0.6 to 1.5 in the extracted symmetry energy when x increases from 4 to 10

## Context

This work extends the debate on "finite-size" effects in isobaric yield ratios originally raised by Souza (2012), who claimed that SGC/CSE methods show finite-size effects that prevent obtaining precise nuclear properties. The authors argue that SGC/CSE models lack information on neutron and proton density distributions and use fixed temperatures, making them questionable for neutron-rich nucleus induced reactions. The SAA model, which has been shown to reproduce fragment yields in reactions induced by neutron-rich projectiles (Brohm 1994, Gaimard 1991, Ma 2009), demonstrates that the finite-size effects claimed in SGC/CSE disappear for very neutron-rich projectiles, enabling the continued use of the isobaric yield ratio method to study symmetry energy in such reactions.
