# Born-Oppenheimer study of two-component few-particle systems under one-dimensional confinement

> The energy spectrum, atom-dimer scattering length, and atom-trimer scattering length for systems of three and four ultracold atoms with $δ$-function interactions in one dimension are presented as a function of the relative mass ratio of the interacting atoms. The Born-Oppenheimer approach is used to treat three-body ("HHL") systems of one light and two heavy atoms, as well as four-body ("HHHL") systems of one light and three heavy atoms. Zero-range interactions of arbitrary strength are assumed between different atoms, but the heavy atoms are assumed to be noninteracting among themselves. Both fermionic and bosonic heavy atoms are considered.

## Key claim

The Born-Oppenheimer approximation, when applied to one-dimensional three-body (HHL) and four-body (HHHL) systems with zero-range interactions, consistently overestimates critical mass ratios for the appearance of new bound states by approximately 0.3-0.4 compared to high-accuracy hyperspherical calculations, with the percentage error decreasing monotonically as the mass ratio increases.

## Method

The author solved the one-dimensional Schrödinger equation for three-body (one light, two heavy) and four-body (one light, three heavy) systems using the Born-Oppenheimer factorization, separating the light-particle dynamics from the heavy-particle coordinates. For the HHL system, the adiabatic potential curves were obtained by solving a transcendental equation for the eigenvalue, and the diagonal correction (nonadiabatic term) was calculated explicitly; bound-state energies were computed variationally using b-spline expansions, while atom-dimer scattering lengths were extracted from Numerov-propagated wavefunctions matched to asymptotic forms. For the HHHL system, the two-dimensional potential energy surface for heavy particles was first computed in the extreme adiabatic approximation, then used to generate hyperradial adiabatic potential curves and couplings, with the resulting coupled hyperradial equations solved for bound states and atom-trimer scattering lengths.

## Key numbers

- For the equal-mass case (β = 1) with λ = 0 bosons, the extreme adiabatic approximation (EAA) gives a three-body ground state energy E₃,EAA = -2.4227 (in units of B₂), approximately 16% deeper than the correct value of E₃ = -2.087719 from Kartavtsev (2009); the uncoupled adiabatic approximation (UAA) gives E₃,UAA = -1.8561, underbinding by about 11%
- The critical mass ratio β⁻¹ for the appearance of the second trimer state (a_AD → ∞) for λ = 0 bosons is 3.255 in this work versus 2.86954 from Kartavtsev (2009); for λ = ∞ fermions, the first trimer appears at β⁻¹ = 1.170 in this work versus the exact result of 1 from Kartavtsev (2009)
- For the tenth trimer state (n=10) with λ = 0 bosons, the critical mass ratio for a_AD → ∞ is 221.133 in this work versus 220.765 from Kartavtsev (2009); for λ = ∞ fermions, the tenth trimer appears at β⁻¹ = 246.331 versus 245.973
- The asymptotic threshold energy in the UAA including the diagonal correction is -1 + [β/(2+β)]², while without the diagonal correction the asymptotic value is -1 - β/(2+β)

## Context

This work extends the mass-dependent calculations of three-body binding energies and atom-dimer scattering lengths by Kartavtsev (2009), who used the exact adiabatic hyperspherical representation, by instead applying the Born-Oppenheimer approximation and testing its accuracy through direct comparison. The four-body HHHL calculations are motivated by studies of heavy fermions in three dimensions by Castin (2010), who found an infinite set of four-body states for mass ratios 13.384 < m_H/m_L < 13.607, and by Guevara (2012), who argued these are distinct from Efimov states using a Born-Oppenheimer calculation with attractive 1/r² interactions. The results enable future investigation of whether the fermionic trimer state appearing in one dimension at β⁻¹ ≈ 1.170 is continuously connected to universal trimer states in higher dimensions, as suggested by the work of Levinsen and Parish (2013) on dimensional crossover.
