# Black Hole Formation in Core-Collapse Supernovae and Time-of-Flight Measurements of the Neutrino Masses

> Early black hole formation in core-collapse supernovae may be triggered by mass accretion or a change in the high-density equation of state. We consider the possibility that black hole formation happens when the flux of neutrinos is still measurably high. If this occurs, then the neutrino signal from the supernova will be terminated abruptly (the transition takes $\lesssim 0.5$ ms). The properties and duration of the signal before the cutoff are important measures of both the physics and astrophysics of the cooling proto-neutron star. For the event rates expected in present and proposed detectors, the cutoff will generally appear sharp, thus allowing model-independent time-of-flight mass tests for the neutrinos after the cutoff. If black hole formation occurs relatively early, within a few ($\sim 1$) seconds after core collapse, then the expected luminosities are of order $L_{BH} = 10^{52}$ erg/s per flavor. In this case, the neutrino mass sensitivity can be extraordinary. For a supernova at a distance $D = 10$ kpc, SuperKamiokande can detect a $\barν_e$ mass down to 1.8 eV by comparing the arrival times of the high-energy and low-energy neutrinos in $\barν_e + p \to e^+ + n$. This test will also measure the cutoff time, and will thus allow a mass test of $ν_μ$ and $ν_τ$ relative to $\barν_e$. Assuming that $ν_μ$ and $ν_τ$ are nearly degenerate, as suggested by the atmospheric neutrino results, masses down to about 6 eV can be probed with a proposed lead detector of mass $M_D = 4$ kton (OMNIS). Remarkably, the neutrino mass sensitivity scales as $(D/L_{BH} M_D)^{1/2}$. Therefore, {\it direct} sensitivity to all three neutrino masses in the interesting few-eV range is realistically possible; {\it there are no other known techniques that have this capability}.

## Key claim

If black hole formation in a core-collapse supernova occurs early enough (within a few seconds after core collapse) while the neutrino flux is still measurably high, the abrupt termination of the neutrino signal enables model-independent time-of-flight measurements of all three neutrino masses down to the few-eV range, a capability the authors state is not available through any other known technique.

## Method

The authors model the neutrino signal from a proto-neutron star that forms a black hole, assuming constant luminosities and temperatures before an abrupt cutoff (transition time less than 0.5 ms). They derive analytic expressions for the number of delayed neutrino events after the cutoff, relating the neutrino mass to the event rate at the cutoff time, the distance to the supernova, and a central energy determined by the thermal spectrum and cross section. They then evaluate the mass sensitivity for SuperKamiokande (using charged-current detection of electron antineutrinos) and for a proposed lead detector (OMNIS, using neutral-current detection of muon and tau neutrinos), considering two scenarios: early black hole formation (luminosity of order 10^52 erg/s per flavor) and late black hole formation (luminosity of order 10^51 erg/s per flavor).

## Key numbers

- For a supernova at distance D = 10 kpc, SuperKamiokande can detect an electron antineutrino mass down to 1.8 eV by comparing arrival times of high-energy and low-energy neutrinos in the reaction antineutrino_e + proton -> positron + neutron.
- For muon and tau neutrinos assumed nearly degenerate (as suggested by atmospheric neutrino results), masses down to about 6 eV can be probed with a proposed lead detector of mass M_D = 4 kton (OMNIS).
- The neutrino mass sensitivity scales as (D / L_BH M_D)^(1/2), where L_BH is the luminosity at black hole formation and D is the distance.
- In the early black hole formation scenario, the expected luminosities are of order L_BH = 10^52 erg/s per flavor; in the late scenario, luminosities are of order 10^51 erg/s per flavor.
- The cutoff transition takes less than or about 0.5 ms.
- The time delay for a neutrino of mass m (in eV) and energy E (in MeV) over distance D (in 10 kpc) is Delta t = 0.515 (m/E)^2 D seconds.

## Context

This work extends earlier time-of-flight mass tests using supernova neutrinos (SKpaper, SNOpaper, 1990s) that could only reach sensitivities of about 30-45 eV for muon and tau neutrinos, by exploiting the sharp cutoff from black hole formation to achieve eV-scale sensitivity. It builds on models of black hole formation by accretion (Burrows 1988; Mezzacappa and Bruenn) and by equation-of-state softening (Baumgarte et al. 1996), and on the neutrino mass sensitivity scaling derived in SNOpaper. The proposed technique enables direct measurement of all three neutrino masses, complementing indirect constraints from cosmology (about 1-10 eV sensitivity) and tritium beta decay (about 3 eV limit on electron neutrino mass).
