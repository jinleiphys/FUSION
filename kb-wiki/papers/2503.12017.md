# Quantification of the evaporation process during fragmentation of space-relevant nuclei on elemental targets

> This study examines charge-changing cross sections for 12C, 14N, 16O, and 20Ne projectiles on elemental targets (C, Al, Cu) at a beam energy of around 290 MeV/nucleon. The two-stage abrasion-ablation model is used, with the abrasion stage described via the Glauber model, incorporating validated single-nucleon density distributions from proton elastic scattering data. In the ablation stage, where particle evaporation occurs, the contribution to charge-changing cross sections is estimated using two approaches. First, a statistical decay model is employed to analyze the evaporation of protons following neutron removal in the abrasion stage. The second approach estimates evaporation contributions by subtracting the direct process component (abrasion) from the experimental charge-changing cross section data. A comparison between these estimated contributions from the experimental data and the predictions of the statistical model enables a systematic evaluation of the evaporation process. The key factors influencing evaporation, such as excitation energy distribution parameters, decay width of the emitted particles, and nucleon separation energies, are analyzed. A strong correlation is observed between the maximum excitation energy available for evaporation and the neutron separation energy of the projectiles across different targets, highlighting the role of evaporation dynamics in the charge-changing cross sections.

## Key claim

The study quantifies the evaporation contribution to charge-changing cross sections within the two-stage abrasion-ablation model, finding that the Glauber model alone systematically underestimates experimental data and that proton evaporation following neutron removal in the abrasion stage accounts for the missing component, with a strong correlation observed between the maximum excitation energy available for evaporation and the neutron separation energy of the projectiles across different targets.

## Method

The abrasion stage was modeled using the Glauber formalism with the finite-range optical limit approximation (FROLA), employing single-nucleon density distributions validated against proton elastic scattering data (mixed-type for 12C, harmonic oscillator shell model for 14N, 4α cluster for 16O, and 16O+α cluster for 20Ne). The ablation stage was treated using the Gaimard-Schmidt approach for excitation energy distributions and the LISE++ (GEMINI++) code based on Hauser-Feshbach theory to calculate proton evaporation probabilities after neutron removal. Evaporation contributions were estimated both by direct statistical model calculation and by subtracting the calculated abrasion component from experimental charge-changing cross section data.

## Key numbers

- Charge-changing cross sections in the abrasion stage at 290 MeV/nucleon: 12C on C = 650 mb, on Al = 1001 mb, on Cu = 1568 mb; 14N on C = 720 mb, on Al = 1070 mb, on Cu = 1648 mb; 16O on C = 772 mb, on Al = 1130 mb, on Cu = 1722 mb; 20Ne on C = 911 mb, on Al = 1308 mb, on Cu = 1951 mb (all rounded to nearest integer)
- Cross section variations between different density configurations were generally within 2%
- The mean excitation energy per removed nucleon is given by ⟨E⟩ = (x/(x+1)) × Emax, yielding 10 MeV for first neutron removal with Emax = 20 MeV
- Excitation energy distributions were calculated with Emax values of 20 MeV, 40 MeV, and 60 MeV for removal of one, two, and three nucleons respectively

## Context

This work extends the abrasion-ablation framework previously applied by Scheidenberger, Tanaka (2022), and others to quantify the evaporation process in nuclear fragmentation, addressing the systematic underestimation of charge-changing cross sections by the Glauber model alone as noted by Yamaguchi (2011) and Tanaka (2022). The study enables improved modeling of fragmentation cross sections for space radiation transport codes used by NASA (such as NUCFRAG, NUCFRG2, NUCFRG3) and for ion beam therapy dose calculations, where accurate charge-changing cross sections are essential for predicting Bragg peak profiles and secondary radiation fields.
