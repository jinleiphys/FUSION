# New Procedure for the Evaluation of Fission Product Yields: Application to the Spontaneous Fission of $^{252}$Cf

> Over the last decade, there has been significant improvement in the understanding and modeling of the decay of fission fragments by both prompt and delayed emission. These model improvements open the door for performing consistent evaluations across multiple fission observables, providing not only mean values but also covariances between observables. One such model is the Hauser-Feshbach Fission Fragment Decay model implemented in $\texttt{BeoH}$, which uses distributions of initial conditions of fission fragments to perform a Hauser-Feshbach decay for prompt neutron and $γ$-ray emission and evaluated decay data to calculate cumulative fission product yields. This manuscript describes a new evaluation procedure for independent and cumulative fission product yields, including full correlations among the fission products. We use a Bayesian Kalman filter to fit both experimental cumulative fission product yields and those from the ENDF/B-VIII.0 evaluated library, producing mean values and covariances. In addition to comparing the fission products from these optimizations, we calculate prompt and delayed neutron and $γ$-ray multiplicities using the fitted parameters and compare to some available experimental data. We see reasonable agreement, even when these quantities are not included in the optimization.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new Bayesian evaluation procedure for independent and cumulative fission product yields that uses a Kalman filter to simultaneously fit experimental cumulative yields and those from the ENDF/B-VIII.0 library, producing mean values and covariances; the authors note that the resulting prompt and delayed neutron and gamma-ray multiplicities show reasonable agreement with experiment even when these quantities are not included in the optimization, but caution that the procedure is demonstrated for spontaneous fission of 252Cf and that experimental covariances were not included in this demonstration.

## Method

The authors use the Hauser-Feshbach Fission Fragment Decay model implemented in the BeoH code to calculate independent and cumulative fission product yields from initial fission fragment distributions parameterized by multi-Gaussian mass yields, Wahl systematics for charge distributions, and temperature-ratio parameters for excitation energy sharing. A Bayesian Kalman filter is applied iteratively to optimize the BeoH model parameters against experimental cumulative fission product yields from EXFOR and the ENDF/B-VIII.0 evaluated library, using sensitivity matrices computed from 1% parameter variations and a multi-step procedure that progressively includes nuclei with smaller yields.

## Key numbers

- Total chi-squared per data point for the fit to experimental data: 6.158
- Average percent difference between Kalman filter results and full BeoH calculation (delta_KB): 0.27%
- Initial average total kinetic energy (TKE) for 252Cf spontaneous fission: 185.78 MeV; fitted value when using experimental data: 185.97 MeV (0.10% change)
- Spin cutoff scaling factor f: initial value 2.286; fitted to experimental data: 2.060 (9.886% change)
- Largest parameter changes in the fit to experimental data: R_T(A=149) changed by 10.673%, R_T(A=154) changed by 10.510%, and R_T(A=139) changed by 8.520%
- Cumulative yield cutoff for inclusion in optimization: 0.2%
- Five optimization steps used: yields greater than 5%, then 2%, 1%, 0.5%, then 0.2%

## Context

This work extends the consistent modeling approach of the BeoH code (Okumura 2018, Lovell 2021) by adding a Bayesian Kalman filter optimization procedure that simultaneously constrains fission product yields against experimental data and evaluated libraries, building on earlier evaluation methods such as the England-Rider systematics (England 1994) used in ENDF/B-VII.1 and the corrections applied in ENDF/B-VIII.1 (Mattera 2021, Mattera 2023). The new procedure enables the production of evaluated yields on a fine energy grid with full covariance matrices, including cross-energy correlations, and can be applied to neutron-induced fission reactions up to 20 MeV as demonstrated in Lovell (2023) and Lovell (2025).
