# Conformal duality of the nonlinear Schrödinger equation: Theory and applications to parameter estimation

> The nonlinear Schrödinger equation (NLSE) is a rich and versatile model, which in one spatial dimension has stationary solutions similar to those of the linear Schrödinger equation as well as more exotic solutions such as solitary waves and quantum droplets. Here we present the unified theory of the NLSE, showing that all stationary solutions of the local one-dimensional cubic-quintic NLSE can be classified according to a single number called the cross-ratio. Any two solutions with the same cross-ratio can be converted into one another using a conformal transformation, and the same also holds true for traveling wave solutions. Further, we introduce an optimization afterburner that relies on this conformal symmetry to substantially improve NLSE parameter estimation from noisy empirical data. The new method therefore should have far reaching practical applications for nonlinear physical systems.

# Conformal duality of the nonlinear Schrödinger equation: Theory and applications to parameter estimation

## Key claim
All stationary solutions of the local one-dimensional cubic-quintic NLSE can be classified by a single number called the cross-ratio, and any two solutions with the same cross-ratio can be converted into one another using a conformal transformation; this also holds for traveling wave solutions. The authors introduce an optimization afterburner that uses this conformal symmetry to improve NLSE parameter estimation from noisy empirical data, but the improvement is demonstrated only on synthetically generated data, not real experimental data.

## Method
The authors analyze the time-independent cubic-quintic NLSE in one spatial dimension by rewriting it in amplitude-phase representation, yielding a differential equation for the density σ(x) governed by a quartic polynomial P(σ). They classify stationary solutions according to the discriminant Δ of this polynomial and the nature of its roots (simple real, complex conjugate, or multiple roots). They then apply a Möbius (conformal) transformation to map between different density solutions and their corresponding phase profiles, with the spatial coordinate undergoing an affine transformation. For parameter estimation, they apply random conformal transformations to noisy synthetic density data, fit the transformed data, and transform the fitted parameters back to the original space.

## Key numbers
- The quartic polynomial P(σ) has coefficients: (4/3)a₄ for σ⁴, 4a₃ for σ³, -8a₂ for σ², -16a₁ for σ, and -4a₀ for the constant term.
- The cross-ratio k² is a real-valued invariant in the interval (0,1) that classifies solutions.
- The discriminant Δ = a₄⁶ ∏ⱼₖ(σⱼ - σₖ) determines three solution classes: Δ < 0 (complex conjugate roots), Δ = 0 (multiple roots), Δ > 0 (simple real roots).
- For the oscillatory solution fit, there are five free parameters; for the solitonic solution fit, there are four free parameters.
- The conformal fitting afterburner starts after 100 epochs of standard fitting and shows improvement within "few additional epochs."

## Context
This work provides a unified classification of solutions for the cubic-quintic NLSE, extending prior studies of individual solution families such as those by Akhmediev (1987), Gagnon (1989), and Carr (2000). The conformal duality connects the cubic-quintic NLSE to the cubic NLSE and the linear Schrödinger equation as limiting cases, similar in spirit to the Kramers-Wannier duality in statistical mechanics (Kramers, 1941) and the Montonen-Olive duality in quantum field theory (Montonen, 1977). The parameter estimation method addresses the problem of local minima and barren plateaus in fitting routines, as discussed by McClean (2018) and Larocca (2024), and is immediately applicable to one-dimensional Bose-Einstein condensates described by higher-order Gross-Pitaevskii equations (Petrov, 2016; Salasnich, 2002) and to nonlinear fiber optics.
