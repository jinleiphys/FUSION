# Statistical Theory of Finite Fermi-Systems Based on the Structure of Chaotic Eigenstates

> The approach is developed for the description of isolated Fermi-systems with finite number of particles, such as complex atoms, nuclei, atomic clusters etc. It is based on statistical properties of chaotic excited states which are formed by the interaction between particles. New type of ``microcanonical'' partition function is introduced and expressed in terms of the average shape of eigenstates $F(E_k,E)$ where $E$ is the total energy of the system. This partition function plays the same role as the canonical expression $exp(-E^{(i)}/T)$ for open systems in thermal bath. The approach allows to calculate mean values and non-diagonal matrix elements of different operators. In particular, the following problems have been considered: distribution of occupation numbers and its relevance to the canonical and Fermi-Dirac distributions; criteria of equilibrium and thermalization; thermodynamical equation of state and the meaning of temperature, entropy and heat capacity, increase of effective temperature due to the interaction. The problems of spreading widths and shape of the eigenstates are also studied.

## Key claim

The paper develops a statistical theory for isolated finite Fermi-systems (such as complex atoms, nuclei, and atomic clusters) based on the structure of chaotic eigenstates, introducing a "microcanonical" partition function that replaces the standard canonical distribution. The authors claim that for systems with few particles, the standard Fermi-Dirac distribution may give incorrect results, and their F-distribution provides a correct description even when the number of particles is small.

## Method

The authors separate the total Hamiltonian into a mean-field part H0 and a residual two-body interaction V with random matrix elements, then express exact eigenstates as superpositions of shell-model basis states. They introduce a microcanonical partition function Z(E) = Σ_k F(E_k - E) based on the average shape of chaotic eigenstates (the F-function), and use it to compute occupation numbers n_s(E) = [Σ_k n_s^(k) F(E_k - E)] / [Σ_k F(E_k - E)] for a fixed total energy E. The F-distribution is then compared analytically and numerically to the canonical Gibbs distribution and the Fermi-Dirac distribution, with calculations performed for a model of n randomly interacting Fermi-particles distributed over m single-particle levels.

## Key numbers

- The number of principal components N_pc in excited compound nuclei is ~10^4 to 10^6, and in excited rare-earth or actinide atoms is ~100.
- For the Gaussian density of states, the thermodynamic temperature is given by T_th = σ²/(E_c - E), where σ² = σ₀² + (ΔE)² is the variance of the perturbed spectrum.
- The canonical temperature is T_can = σ²/(E_c - E + Δ), where Δ is a shift proportional to the width σ of the canonical averaging function.
- For the random two-body interaction model with n ≥ 4 particles, the density of states follows a Gaussian form.
- The shift in temperature due to finite spreading width γ of single-particle orbitals is estimated as ΔT ≈ γ²/(16T) for γ ≪ μ.

## Context

This work extends the statistical description of finite Fermi-systems beyond the standard canonical and Fermi-Dirac distributions, which are derived for infinite systems or systems in a heat bath. The approach builds on earlier studies of chaotic eigenstates in the Ce atom (Flambaum et al., 1994), the s-d nuclear shell model (Zele et al.), and random two-body interaction models (Flambaum et al., 1996; Flambaum and Izrailev, 1996). The paper enables analytical calculation of occupation numbers and thermodynamic quantities for isolated systems with few interacting particles, and provides criteria for when the standard Fermi-Dirac distribution becomes valid as the number of particles increases.
