# Thermodynamics of Bose gases from functional renormalization with a hydrodynamic low-energy effective action

> The functional renormalization group for the effective action is used to construct an effective hydrodynamic description of weakly interacting Bose gases. We employ a scale-dependent parametrization of the boson fields developed previously to start the renormalization evolution in a Cartesian representation at high momenta and interpolate to an amplitude-phase one in the low-momentum regime. This technique is applied to Bose gases in one, two and three dimensions, where we study thermodynamic quantities such as the pressure and energy per particle. The interpolation leads to a very natural description of the Goldstone modes in the physical limit, and compares well to analytic and Monte-Carlo simulations at zero temperature. The results show that our method improves aspects of the description of low-dimensional systems, with stable results for the superfluid phase in two dimensions and even in one dimension.

# Thermodynamics of Bose gases from functional renormalization with a hydrodynamic low-energy effective action

## Key claim

The functional renormalization group (FRG) with a scale-dependent interpolation between Cartesian and amplitude-phase (hydrodynamic) field representations yields stable thermodynamic results for weakly interacting Bose gases in one, two, and three dimensions, including a stable superfluid phase in low-dimensional systems where standard Cartesian FRG treatments fail.

## Method

The authors employ a functional renormalization group approach where the boson fields are parametrized with a k-dependent interpolation function that transitions from a Cartesian representation (real and imaginary parts) at high momenta to an amplitude-phase (radial and Goldstone) representation at low momenta, with the switch-over controlled by the healing scale. They use a gradient expansion of the effective action up to fourth order in fields and second order in derivatives, with an exponential regulator, and solve the resulting flow equations for thermodynamic quantities including pressure, density, and entropy. The pressure is obtained by integrating the density and entropy density using Maxwell relations rather than directly from the flow of the effective potential.

## Key numbers

- In three dimensions, the initial condition for the interaction coupling is given by $u^{\text{exp}}_{2,\Lambda} = [m/(4\pi a_{3D}) - m/(4\pi^{3/2})\Lambda]^{-1}$, where $a_{3D}$ is the s-wave scattering length
- In two dimensions, the initial condition is $u^{\text{exp}}_{2,\Lambda} = (4\pi/m)[-\gamma_E - \log(a_{2D}^2 \Lambda^2/4)]^{-1}$, where $\gamma_E \approx 0.5772$ is Euler's constant
- In one dimension, the bare coupling is $g_{1D} = -2/(m a_{1D})$ where $a_{1D}$ is the one-dimensional scattering length (negative for repulsive interactions)
- The interpolation parameters are chosen as $\alpha = 1$ and $\nu = 3$ for the function $b_k = \sqrt{\rho_0}[1 + (\alpha w_k)^\nu]$, where $w_k = (Z_\sigma k^2/2m)/(2u_2\rho_0)$
- The microscopic sound velocity is given by $c_s = [(Z_m/2m)/(Z_\phi^2/2u_2\rho_0)]^{1/2}|_{k=0}$

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous development of an interpolating field representation for classical O(2) models (Isaule, Birse, Walet, 2018) to quantum Bose gases at zero and finite temperatures. It addresses known failures of standard Cartesian FRG treatments in low dimensions, where the superfluid stiffness shows unrealistic decay (Grater and Wetterich, 1995; Gersdorff and Wetterich, 2001) and where Jakubczyk et al. (2017) required fine-tuning of the regulator. The approach builds on Popov's hydrodynamic effective theory (1987) and follows the suggestion of Defenu et al. (2017) that using the amplitude-phase representation in the IR overcomes these issues, while also treating the UV regime properly where the Cartesian representation is more appropriate. The method enables consistent FRG calculations of thermodynamic properties in low-dimensional systems and can be extended to related systems such as paired fermions and relativistic bosonic field theories.
