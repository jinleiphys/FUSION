# Dynamical tidal response of neutron stars as a probe of dense-matter properties

> Dynamical tidal deformations play a crucial role in the gravitational waves emitted by binary neutron star systems during their late inspiral. In this work, we systematically explore how relativistic (dynamical and dissipative) tidal deformations depend on the internal structure of a neutron star using two analytic classes of equations of state. The first class is a nucleonic model that is parameterized by nuclear physics observables, such as the symmetry energy coefficients and saturation properties. The second class is a toy model of quark matter, the MIT bag model. To model tidal dissipation, we self-consistently include contributions from weak-interaction-driven bulk-viscous effects while considering both the nucleonic and the quark-matter equations of state. The dissipative tide is sensitive to frequency and temperature, but its magnitude, as predicted by weak-interaction-driven bulk-viscous effects, is too small (within the equation-of-state models studied here) to be detectable by current or future observations. However, we find that the (conservative) dynamical tidal response function depends strongly on the slope of the symmetry energy and on higher-order coefficients of the symmetry energy; this implies that gravitational-wave observations could be used to probe higher-order coefficients of the symmetry energy through their effect on the (conservative) dynamical tide.

## Key claim

The (conservative) dynamical tidal response function depends strongly on the slope of the symmetry energy and on higher-order coefficients of the symmetry energy, implying that gravitational-wave observations could probe these higher-order coefficients; however, the dissipative tidal response from weak-interaction-driven bulk-viscous effects is too small to be detectable by current or future observations within the equation-of-state models studied.

## Method

The authors compute the full quadrupolar tidal response in general relativity for two analytic classes of equations of state: a nucleonic (npe) meta-model parameterized by symmetry-energy coefficients (S_sym, L_sym, K_sym) and a three-flavor MIT bag model for quark matter. They self-consistently include weak-interaction-driven bulk-viscous effects by incorporating Urca rates (for nucleonic matter) and strangeness-changing reaction rates (for quark matter) through an evolution equation for the species fraction, rather than using effective bulk-viscosity approximations.

## Key numbers

- The symmetry-energy parameters are scanned over S_sym in [29, 34] MeV and L_sym in [40, 120] MeV, guided by experimental and theoretical constraints.
- The saturation density is taken as n_sat = 0.17 fm^{-3}, with the averaged vacuum nucleon mass m_B = 938.9 MeV.
- The direct Urca rate expression includes terms proportional to T^4, delta_mu_Q^2 T^2, and delta_mu_Q^4, with the prefactor G^2(1+3g_A^2)/(240 pi^5) where G^2 = 1.1 x 10^{-22} MeV^{-4} and g_A = 1.26.
- The f-mode resonance occurs at gravitational-wave frequencies of order 2 kHz, while g-mode resonances occur at order 100 Hz to 400 Hz.
- The dissipative tidal deformability is found to be a few orders of magnitude smaller than what is potentially observable by third-generation gravitational-wave detectors.

## Context

This work extends previous studies that explored the impact of symmetry-energy coefficients (e.g., L_sym) on neutron-star radii, static tidal deformabilities, and f-mode frequencies (Lattimer 2000, Yagi 2013, Yagi 2015, De 2018, Raithel 2018) by computing the full frequency-dependent tidal response in general relativity. It also goes beyond Newtonian models of tidal interaction (Yu 2016, Passamonti 2022, Andersson 2019) and effective bulk-viscosity approximations (Most 2021, Hegade K. R. 2024, Saketh 2024) by including reaction rates directly through an evolution equation for the species fraction. The results enable future gravitational-wave observations to potentially provide independent constraints on both K_sym and L_sym through their complementary effects on the dynamical tidal response.
