# Resonances Do Not Equilibrate

> We discuss, in qualitative and quantitative fashion, the yields of hadron resonances. We show that these yields, in general, are not in chemical equilibrium. We evaluate the non-equilibrium abundances in a dynamic model implementing the $1+2\leftrightarrow 3$ resonance formation reactions. Due to the strength of these reactions, we show the $Σ(1385)$ enhancement, and the $Λ(1520)$ suppression explicitly.

# Resonances Do Not Equilibrate

## Key claim

The observed yields of hadron resonances are generally not in chemical equilibrium, because resonance abundances can evolve through post-hadronization reactions (such as 1+2↔3 formation and decay) without altering the final stable particle yields. The direction and magnitude of the deviation from equilibrium depend on the competition between inelastic resonance formation rates and elastic scattering rates in the expanding fireball, leading to enhancement of some resonances (e.g., Σ(1385)) and suppression of others (e.g., Λ(1520)).

## Method

The authors develop a kinetic model implementing the 1+2↔3 resonance formation reactions, using detailed balance relations to connect production and decay rates. They integrate the time evolution of resonance fugacities during the expansion phase of the fireball, assuming a model of longitudinal and transverse expansion with temperature evolution given by dT/T = -(1/3)(2vτ/R⊥ + 1)/τ dτ. Initial conditions are set for RHIC head-on Au-Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV, with hadronization temperatures T₀ = 140, 160, and 180 MeV, and the constituent quark fugacities γq and γs fixed by entropy conservation and strangeness pair-yield maintenance.

## Key numbers

- Hadronization temperature T₀ = 180 MeV yields γq = 1; at T₀ = 140 MeV, γq = 1.6 (close to the maximum possible value defined by Bose-Einstein condensation condition).
- For T₀ = 140 MeV, the ratio Σ(1385)/Λtot ≈ 0.45 at T ≈ 100 MeV (and for the entire range 95–135 MeV), in good agreement with experimental data.
- For T₀ = 140 MeV, kinetic freeze-out temperatures ≈ 95–105 MeV are required for the ratio Λob(1520)/Λtot to reach the experimental domain < 0.042 ± 0.01.
- The threshold energy cutoff for resonance production channels is ΔE > 300 MeV; channels above this threshold are excluded.
- The maximum temperature reached in heavy ion collisions is TMax ≈ 2–5 Tc, where Tc ≃ 160 MeV is the deconfinement temperature.

## Context

This work extends the statistical hadronization model SHARE (Torrieri 2004, 2006) by addressing the post-hadronization evolution of resonance yields, which the SHARE model does not capture. The authors resolve the "resonance puzzle" observed in experiments (Broniowski 2003, Markert 2002, Adams 2006, Salur 2006, Witt 2007, Abelev 2008) where some resonance yields deviate from statistical model predictions. The study quantifies the earlier expectation of resonance quenching in a dense hadron medium (Rafelski 2001, Bleicher 2002) and provides a framework for using resonance yields as a probe of the final breakup conditions of the quark-gluon plasma fireball.
