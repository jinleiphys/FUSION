# Dense Quark Matter in Nature

> According to quantum chromodynamics (QCD), matter at ultra-high densities will take the form of a color-superconducting quark liquid, in which there is a condensate of Cooper pairs of quarks near the Fermi surface. I present a review of the physics of color superconductivity. I give particular attention to the recently proposed gapless CFL (gCFL) phase, which has unusual properties such as quasiquarks with a near-quadratic dispersion relation, and which may well be the favored phase of quark matter in the density range relevant to compact stars. I also discuss the effects of color superconductivity on the mass-radius relationship of compact stars, showing that one would have to fix the bag constant by other measurements in order to see the effects of color superconductivity. An additional parameter in the quark matter equation of state connected with perturbative corrections allows quark matter to imitate nuclear matter over the relevant density range, so that hybrid stars can show a mass-radius relationship very similar to that of nuclear matter, and their masses can reach 1.9 M_solar.

## Key claim

Color-superconducting quark matter may occur in compact star cores, but the effects of color superconductivity on the mass-radius relationship are difficult to distinguish from changes in the bag constant or perturbative corrections; with an additional parameter in the quark matter equation of state, hybrid stars can mimic nuclear matter and reach masses up to 1.9 solar masses, so observing a mass above about 2 solar masses would rule out the quark matter equations of state studied here.

## Method

The author reviews the physics of color superconductivity in dense QCD, focusing on the gapless CFL (gCFL) phase and its dispersion relations. For the mass-radius analysis, the author uses a bag-model equation of state for quark matter with a color-superconducting gap parameter Delta and a perturbative correction parameter c, competing with the APR98 nuclear matter equation of state, and tunes the bag constant so that the nuclear-to-quark matter transition occurs at a fixed density of 1.5 times nuclear saturation density.

## Key numbers

- The strange quark mass is approximately 150 MeV, while up and down quark masses are less than 10 MeV.
- In the NJL model used, the pairing gap at mu=500 MeV and M_s=0 is Delta_0=25 MeV.
- The gapless CFL phase appears at M_s^2/mu = 2 Delta_CFL.
- For a compact star made entirely of CFL quark matter, a gap as small as Delta=1 MeV is ruled out by observations of millisecond pulsars.
- A mixed phase of nuclear and quark matter only occurs if the surface tension is less than about 40 MeV/fm^2.
- With fixed bag constant B^{1/4}=180 MeV and m_s=200 MeV, mass-radius curves are shown for Delta=0 and Delta=100 MeV.
- With the bag constant tuned for transition at 1.5 nuclear saturation density, hybrid stars with c=0.3 and Delta=0 can reach masses up to about 1.9 M_sun.
- An observed mass greater than about 2 M_sun would be inconsistent with the quark matter equations of state studied.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on color superconductivity in QCD, building on the BCS mechanism applied to quark pairing (Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer, 1957) and the color-flavor-locked (CFL) phase proposed by Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1999). The gapless CFL phase was recently proposed by the author and collaborators (2003). The mass-radius analysis extends the work of Alford and Reddy (2003) and Fraga et al. (2001) on the equation of state of quark matter, and shows that distinguishing color superconductivity from other effects in compact star observations will require independent measurements of the bag constant.
