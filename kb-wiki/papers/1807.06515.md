# Effect of the magnetized medium on the decay of neutral scalar bosons

> The decay of a heavy neutral scalar particle into fermions and into charged scalars are analyzed when in the presence of an external magnetic field and finite temperature. Working in the one-loop approximation for the study of these decay channels, it is shown that the magnetic field leads in general to a suppression of the decay width whenever the kinematic constrain depends explicitly on the magnetic field. Our results are also compared with common approximations found in the literature, e.g., when the magnitude of the external magnetic field is smaller than the decaying product particle masses, i.e., in the weak field approximation, and in the opposite case, i.e., in the strong field approximation. Possible applications of our results are discussed.

## Key claim

The decay width of a neutral scalar boson into fermions or charged scalars in a magnetized medium is generally suppressed by the magnetic field, except for the lowest Landau level contribution which grows linearly with the field; the full one-loop result shows a decreasing trend with increasing magnetic field when the kinematic constraint depends explicitly on the field, contrary to some earlier studies that found enhancement.

## Method

The authors computed the one-loop self-energy of a neutral scalar field coupled to fermions via a Yukawa interaction and to charged scalars via a trilinear coupling, using the Schwinger proper-time formalism for charged particle propagators in a constant external magnetic field. They evaluated the imaginary part of the self-energy via the optical theorem to obtain the decay width, performing the calculation for arbitrary magnetic field strength and at finite temperature, then compared with the weak field approximation (expanding to order \((q_f B)^2\)) and the strong field approximation (keeping only the lowest Landau level).

## Key numbers

- The weak field approximation for fermionic decay at zero temperature gives \(\Gamma^{\mathrm{w}}_M(T=0,B) = \frac{g^2 M}{8\pi}\left(1-\frac{4m_f^2}{M^2}\right)^{3/2} \Theta(M^2-4m_f^2) \left[ 1 - \frac{2(q_fB)^2}{M^4} \left(\frac{8}{3} - \frac{2m_f^2}{M^2}\right) \left(1-\frac{4m_f^2}{M^2}\right)^{-2} \right]\), showing suppression at order \((q_f B)^2\).
- The strong field (LLL) approximation for fermionic decay gives \(\Gamma_{M,LLL} = g^2\frac{q_fB}{2\pi M} \left(1-\frac{4m_f^2}{M^2}\right)^{1/2} \left[1-2n_F\left(\frac{M}{2}\right)\right] \Theta(M^2-4m_f^2)\), linear in \(B\).
- The maximum Landau level index for fermionic decay is \(N_{\mathrm{max}}(B) = \mathrm{Integer}\left[\frac{M^2-4m_f^2}{8 q_f B}\right]\), and the maximum magnetic field for \(n \geq 1\) contributions is \(q_f B_{\mathrm{max}} = (M^2 - 4 m_f^2)/8\).
- For bosonic decay, the strong field (LLL) approximation gives \(\Gamma_{M,LLL}^b = \frac{g^2q_b B}{4\pi M^3} \left[1-\frac{4(m_b^2+q_b B)}{M^2}\right]^{-1/2} \left[1+2n_B\left(\frac{M}{2}\right)\right] \Theta(M^2 - 4 m_b^2)\).
- The weak field approximation for bosonic decay is \(\Gamma_{M,\mathrm{weak}}^b \simeq \frac{g^2}{16\pi M^2} \sqrt{1-4\frac{m_b^2}{M^2}} \left[1+2n_B\left(\frac{M}{2}\right)\right] \Theta(M^2 - 4 m_b^2) \left[1 - \frac{2 (q_b B)^2}{3M^4} \left(1-40 \frac{m_b^2}{M^2}\right)\right]\).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of particle decays in magnetized media, which had shown conflicting results: some found enhancement (Bali 2018, Bandyopadhyay 2016, Tsai 1974, Kuznetsov 1997, Satunin 2013) while others found suppression (Piccinelli 2017, Sogut 2017, Kawaguchi 2016) or mixed behavior (Ghosh 2017). The authors resolve these discrepancies by showing that the full sum over Landau levels yields a decreasing decay width with magnetic field, while the LLL-only approximation (often used in strong field studies) gives an increasing linear behavior that is only valid in a limited range. The results enable more accurate modeling of scalar particle decays in magnetized astrophysical environments such as magnetars and in heavy-ion collision experiments where strong transient magnetic fields are present.
