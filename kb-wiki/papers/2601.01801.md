# Searching for the Tetraneutron Resonance on the Lattice

> The nature of the tetraneutron ($4n$) system remains a pivotal question in nuclear physics. We investigate the $4n$ system using nuclear lattice effective field theory in finite volumes with a lattice size up to $L=30$~fm, employing both a high-precision N$^3$LO interaction and a simplified SU(4) symmetric one. The ground-state energy is found to decrease smoothly with increasing box size, showing no plateau characteristic of a resonance. We further compute the dineutron-dineutron scattering phase shift using Lüscher's finite-volume method. At the smallest relative momenta, the extracted $2n$--$2n$ $S$-wave phase shift is small, consistent with a weak interaction in the dilute limit. At intermediate momenta, it exhibits a weak attraction with a peak of approximately $10^\circ$ at relative momentum of 60--84~MeV. While this structure does not constitute a resonance, the corresponding confined $4n$ energy of 1.7--3.3~MeV lies close to the experimentally observed low-energy peak.

## Key claim

The tetraneutron system shows no resonance behavior in finite-volume lattice calculations up to 30 fm, but the confined 4n energy of 1.7–3.3 MeV lies close to the experimentally observed low-energy peak, and the extracted dineutron-dineutron S-wave phase shift exhibits a weak attraction with a maximum of approximately 10° at relative momentum 60–84 MeV without the rapid rise through 90° that would signal a narrow elastic resonance.

## Method

The authors used nuclear lattice effective field theory (NLEFT) with a lattice spacing of 1.32 fm and box sizes from approximately 10 fm to 30 fm, employing both a high-precision N3LO chiral EFT interaction and a simplified SU(4)-symmetric interaction. They computed the ground-state energy of the 4n system in finite volumes using Euclidean time projection with plane-wave Slater determinant initial states and angular momentum projection onto the A1+ irreducible representation. They extracted dineutron-dineutron S-wave scattering phase shifts from finite-volume 4n and 2n energies using Lüscher's finite-volume method together with a composite-dineutron finite-volume dispersion relation, treating the infinite-volume dineutron binding energy as an auxiliary near-threshold parameter scanned over 0.01–0.09 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacing of a = 1.32 fm for N3LO calculations and a = 1.64 fm for SU(4) calculations
- Box sizes L from approximately 10 fm to 30 fm (L = 8 to 23 lattice sites)
- The extracted 2n–2n S-wave phase shift shows a peak of approximately 10° at relative momentum of 60–84 MeV
- The confined 4n energy in the range 1.7–3.3 MeV for box sizes L ≃ 20–15 fm
- The experimentally reported correlated 4n peak at 2.37 ± 0.38(stat.) ± 0.44(sys.) MeV from Duer (2022)
- The experimentally reported candidate tetraneutron resonance at 0.83 ± 0.65(stat.) ± 1.25(sys.) MeV from Kisamori (2016)
- Ratio of neutron-dineutron to neutron-neutron scattering lengths a_{n-2n}/a_{n-n} = 1.08 ± 0.06 for B_{2n} = 0.09 MeV, 1.09 ± 0.11 for B_{2n} = 0.07 MeV, 1.13 ± 0.27 for B_{2n} = 0.05 MeV, 1.29 ± 0.99 for B_{2n} = 0.03 MeV, and 3.35×10^{-7} ± 2.70 for B_{2n} = 0.01 MeV at lattice spacing a = 1.64 fm

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical investigations of the tetraneutron system using methods including Faddeev-Yakubovsky equations (Lazauskas 2005, Hiyama 2016, Carbonell 2017), the Gaussian expansion method (Hiyama 2016, Carbonell 2017), the no-core Gamow shell model (Fossez 2016), AGS equations (Deltuva 2018, Deltuva 2019), and adiabatic hyperspherical methods (Higgins 2020), which converge on the absence of a 4n resonance. The results are qualitatively consistent with previous investigations of 2n–2n scattering phase shifts using rigorous solutions of FY and AGS equations (Deltuva 2019), which found that a bound tetraneutron state evolves into a virtual state rather than a resonance. The authors plan to extend the NLEFT framework to investigate the 8He(p,p4He)4n reaction and understand the correlated structures observed in experiments such as Duer (2022).
