# Magnetically supramassive neutron stars

> It is commonly believed that neutron stars exceeding the maximum mass limit for stability could be formed in the aftermath of binary neutron star mergers, enjoying a short life of metastability before losing centrifugal support and collapsing to a black hole. It is suggested here that a similar scenario could take place when the remnant's excess mass is supported by an ultra-strong $(\gtrsim 10^{17}\,\mbox{G})$ magnetic field that could be generated during, and shortly after, coalescence. We show that such 'magnetically supramassive' neutron stars could stave off collapse and survive for a few years before their magnetic energy is sufficiently dissipated due to ambipolar diffusion. In addition, we speculate on multi-messenger signatures of such objects and discuss the robustness of our results against limitations placed by neutron superfluidity and magneto-thermal evolution.

## Key claim
The paper suggests that a neutron star remnant from a binary merger could be stabilized against collapse not by rapid rotation (as in standard supramassive stars) but by an ultra-strong internal magnetic field (greater than about 10^17 G), surviving for a few years before magnetic energy dissipates via ambipolar diffusion. The authors hedge that it is unclear whether such extreme field strengths are ever reached in Nature, and that the results depend on assumptions about neutron superfluidity and magneto-thermal evolution.

## Method
The authors use analytic energy arguments to estimate the maximum mass sustainable by a magnetic field, comparing gravitational binding energy to magnetic energy, and derive a formula for the magnetically supramassive mass limit. They then model magnetic field decay via ambipolar diffusion using a phenomenological evolution law, coupled with a modified Urca cooling law for the core temperature, to estimate the collapse timescale as a function of initial field strength and equation-of-state parameter.

## Key numbers
- The ambipolar diffusion timescale is approximately tau_amb = 25 L_5^2 B_17^{-2} T_9^2 (rho/rho_nuc)^{2/3} yr, where L_5 is the field variation scale in units of 10^5 cm, B_17 is the field in units of 10^17 G, and T_9 is the temperature in units of 10^9 K.
- For a n=1 polytropic equation of state, the baryonic mass increases from 2.19 M_sun to 2.28 M_sun (a 4% increase) for a stellar model with radius 13.8 km and magnetic dipole moment 2 x 10^35 G cm^3.
- The maximum magnetic field from merger simulations saturates at about 6 x 10^16 G for a magnetic energy of 2 x 10^51 erg and radius 15 km.
- The collapse timescale t_col for a birth mass of 1.03 M_TOV shows marginal variation with initial field B_0, with magnetically supramassive stars unlikely to last more than about a decade after birth.
- The electromagnetic spin-down timescale is tau_em ~ 50 (B_p/10^16 G)^{-2} (nu/kHz)^{-2} s for polar field B_p and spin frequency nu.
- The characteristic gravitational wave amplitude is h ~ 4 x 10^{-24} (10 Mpc/D) (epsilon/10^{-2}) (nu/kHz)^2 for distance D and ellipticity epsilon.

## Context
This work extends the standard classification of merger remnants (stable, supramassive, hypermassive) by proposing a fourth option where magnetic pressure, rather than rotation, provides the extra support against collapse. It builds on prior work by Cardall et al. (2001) and Dexheimer et al. (2017) on magnetized neutron star structure, and on ambipolar diffusion studies by Goldreich and Reisenegger (1992) and Ho (2012). The paper enables future searches for multi-messenger signatures: a short-lived bright X-ray plateau after a short gamma-ray burst (without abrupt cutoff after ~100 s) followed years later by a non-repeating fast radio burst, or continuous gravitational waves from a magnetically deformed star.
