# On variational trial functions in the extended Thomas-Fermi method

> Parametrized nucleon density distributions are widely employed for the calculation of the properties of atomic nuclei and dense inhomogeneous matter in compact stars within the Thomas-Fermi method and its extensions. We show that the use of insufficiently smooth parametrizations may deteriorate the accuracy of this method. We discuss and clarify the smoothness condition using the example of the so-called "nuclear pasta" in the neutron star mantle.

## Key claim

The paper shows that parametrized nucleon density distributions used in the extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) method must satisfy specific smoothness conditions, specifically, zero gradient at the center of the Wigner-Seitz cell, to avoid divergences in the fourth-order gradient corrections, particularly for cylindrical and plane-parallel (pasta) phases in neutron star mantles.

## Method

The authors analyze the fourth-order gradient corrections to the kinetic energy density in the ETF model, focusing on the term proportional to \((\nabla^2 n_q)^2 / n_q\). They evaluate the integral of this term near the center of spherical, cylindrical, and plane-parallel Wigner-Seitz cells for trial density distributions that have a non-zero first derivative (a kink) at the origin. They also examine several common parametrizations (Oyamatsu, Fermi, modified Fermi, damped Fermi) and propose new parametrizations (two-sided damped Fermi and weakly damped Fermi) that satisfy the required smoothness conditions.

## Key numbers

* The mean nucleon number densities for pasta phases are in the range \(\bar{n} \sim 0.05\) fm\(^{-3}\) to \(0.08\) fm\(^{-3}\).
* The nuclear saturation density \(n_\mathrm{sat}\) is quoted as \(0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\) (most often cited) with recent experimental estimates of \(0.15 \pm 0.01\) fm\(^{-3}\).
* The neutron-drip density is \(n_\mathrm{drip} \sim (2-3) \times 10^{-4}\) fm\(^{-3}\).
* Modern ETF theory with shell and pairing corrections reproduces measured masses of heavy nuclei with typical errors of \(\sim 0.7\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the ETF method developed by Brack et al. (1985) and Hodges (1973), who derived the fourth-order gradient corrections and noted that surface terms vanish when density gradients vanish at boundaries. The paper addresses a practical problem in restricted variational calculations of nuclear pasta phases, which were first described by Ravenhall et al. (1983) and Lorenz et al. (1993). The analysis enables more reliable ETF calculations of neutron star mantle properties, including elasticity studies by Pethick and Potekhin (1998) and more recent work by Zemlyakov and Chugunov (2023), by clarifying which parametrizations of nucleon density profiles are mathematically admissible.
