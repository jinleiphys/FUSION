# Constraints in modeling the quasielastic response in inclusive lepton-nucleus scattering

> We show that the quasielastic (QE) response calculated with the SuSAv2 (superscaling approach) model, that relies on the scaling phenomenon observed in the analysis of (e,e') data and on the relativistic mean-field theory, is very similar to that from a relativistic distorted wave impulse approximation model when only the real part of the optical potentials is employed. The coincidence between the results from these two completely independent approaches, which satisfactorily agree with the inclusive data, reinforces the reliability of the quasielastic predictions stemming from both models and sets constraints for the QE response. We also study the low energy and momentum transfer region of the inclusive response by confronting the results of the relativistic mean-field model with those of the Hartree-Fock continuum random-phase approximation model, which accounts for nuclear long-range correlations. Finally, we present a comparison of our results with the recent JLab (e,e') data for argon, titanium and carbon, finding good agreement with the three data sets.

## Key claim

The quasielastic (QE) response from the SuSAv2 model, which uses a blending function to merge relativistic mean-field and plane-wave impulse approximation regimes, is shown to be very similar to that from relativistic mean-field models that solve the wave equation with energy-dependent real optical potentials, provided only the real part of those potentials is employed. This coincidence between two independent approaches reinforces the reliability of both models for the QE response, but the agreement is conditional on using only the real part of the optical potentials and on avoiding the very low momentum transfer region (q below approximately 300 MeV) where SuSAv2 overestimates the RMF result.

## Method

The authors compared the quasielastic scaling functions and inclusive cross sections from the SuSAv2 model with those from several relativistic mean-field based models: RPWIA, RPWIA with Pauli blocking (PB-RPWIA), RMF, ED-RMF (energy-dependent RMF with a blending function), and the phenomenological energy-dependent optical potentials EDAD1 and EDAI. For the low-energy region, they also compared RMF results with those from a non-relativistic Hartree-Fock continuum random-phase approximation (HF-CRPA) model that accounts for long-range nuclear correlations. The models were tested against recent JLab inclusive (e,e') data for carbon, argon, and titanium, with additional contributions from single-pion production and meson-exchange currents added to the QE response for the data comparison.

## Key numbers

- For carbon, the Fermi momentum used is 228 MeV; for argon, 240 MeV.
- The momentum transfer range studied is 50 < q < 1500 MeV.
- The ED-RMF blending function is parametrized as f_b(T_N) = L(T_N) + F(T_N), where L(T_N) = 0.85/((T_N/200)^2 + 3.5) + 0.29 and F(T_N) = 0.48/(exp[(T_N-90)/23] + 1), with T_N in MeV in the lab frame.
- The EDAD1 and EDAI optical potentials were fitted to elastic proton-nucleus scattering data in the range 22 < T_p < 1100 MeV.
- For q values above approximately 300 MeV, the scaling functions from SuSAv2 and the energy-dependent relativistic mean-field models (ED-RMF, EDAD1, EDAI-C) notably agree.
- The CRPA provides additional enhancement of the cross section for q up to around 200 MeV; for q greater than or approximately 300 MeV, the CRPA reduces the HF cross section.

## Context

This work extends the SuSAv2 model (Gonzalez-Jimenez 2014, Megias 2016) and compares it with relativistic mean-field models using energy-dependent optical potentials (Cooper 1993, 2009) and the HF-CRPA model (Pandey 2015, 2016). The results set constraints on the QE response for inclusive lepton-nucleus scattering, which is directly relevant for neutrino-oscillation experiments such as DUNE (DUNE 2016) and HyperKamiokande (HyperK 2015) that require percent-level understanding of neutrino-nucleus interactions and the capability to extrapolate between different nuclear targets.
