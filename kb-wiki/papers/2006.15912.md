# Hawking radiation from acoustic black holes in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> We propose a new analogue model of gravity - the evolving quark gluon plasma (QGP) produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions. This quark gluon plasma is the "most inviscid" fluid known. Such low kinematic viscosity is believed to reflect strongly correlated nature for QGP in these experiments. Hence, it may provide a good example of a quantum fluid naturally suited to studies of acoustic Hawking radiation. Due to rapid longitudinal expansion, presence of a sonic horizon is also naturally guaranteed here, though, in general, this horizon is not static. Using Ultra relativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) simulations, we show that, under certain conditions, the longitudinal velocity of the plasma, near the sonic horizon, can become time independent for a short span during the evolution of the system. During this period, we can have a conformally static acoustic metric with a (conformal) Killing horizon coinciding with the apparent horizon. An asymptotic observer will then see a thermal flux of phonons, constituting the Hawking radiation, coming from the horizon. For the relatively low energy collision considered here, where the resulting QCD system is governed by non-relativistic hydrodynamics, we estimate the Hawking temperature to be about 4-5 MeV (with the temperature of the QCD fluid being about 135 MeV). We discuss the experimental signatures of this Hawking radiation in terms of a thermal component in the rapidity dependence of the transverse momentum distribution of detected particles. We also discuss extension to ultra-relativistic case which should lead to a higher Hawking temperature, along with the effects of dynamical horizon leading to blue/red shift of the temperature.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the evolving quark-gluon plasma (QGP) produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions can serve as a new analogue model of gravity, and they show that under specific conditions the longitudinal velocity of the plasma near the sonic horizon becomes approximately time-independent for a short duration (about 2 fm), allowing a conformally static acoustic metric with a Killing horizon. The Hawking temperature is estimated to be about 4-5 MeV, but this result is derived for a relatively low-energy collision where the system is governed by non-relativistic hydrodynamics, and the authors note that extension to ultra-relativistic collisions should lead to higher Hawking temperatures.

## Method

Using Ultra relativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) simulations for Au-Au central collisions at a laboratory energy of 10.7 GeV (BNL-AGS Experiment), the authors extracted the longitudinal velocity profile of the nucleonic fluid as a function of position and time. They identified a time window (9-11 fm after collision) where the longitudinal velocity near the sonic horizon became almost time-independent, allowing them to approximate the acoustic metric as conformally static. From this static metric, they computed the surface gravity of the (conformal) Killing horizon and derived the Hawking temperature for an asymptotic observer at the centre of the collision.

## Key numbers

- Hawking temperature estimated to be about 4-5 MeV for the low-energy collision studied.
- Temperature of the QCD fluid (nucleonic plasma) determined from Fermi-Dirac fitting to be about 135 MeV (with a central cell temperature of approximately 154 MeV at 6 fm time).
- Speed of sound in the hadronic system taken as constant at \(c_s = 0.35\) (in units of speed of light), based on an equation of state \(p \simeq 0.12 \epsilon\) from prior work.
- Acoustic horizon located at approximately \(\lvert z \rvert \approx 2.5\) fm during the 2 fm time window (9-11 fm after collision).
- The time window of nearly static horizon lasts for about 2 fm.
- The plasma velocity becomes time-independent for positions \(-4 \text{ fm} < z < 0\) during the 9-11 fm interval.

## Context

This work extends the analogue gravity programme of Unruh (1981) and Visser (1998) to a new physical system: the quark-gluon plasma produced in heavy-ion collisions. It contrasts with earlier analogue models using Bose-Einstein condensates (Steinhauer 2015, 2016; de Nova 2018) and superfluid helium, and it specifically addresses the challenge of a dynamical horizon that arises in the Bjorken scaling expansion model (Bjorken 1982). The results enable future studies of acoustic Hawking radiation in ultra-relativistic collisions at RHIC and LHC, where higher Hawking temperatures are expected, and motivate investigation of dynamical horizon effects leading to blue/red shifts of the Hawking temperature.
