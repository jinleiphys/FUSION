# Early time behavior of spatial and momentum anisotropies in kinetic theory across different Knudsen numbers

> We investigate the early time development of the anisotropic transverse flow and spatial eccentricities of a fireball with various particle-based transport approaches using a fixed initial condition. In numerical simulations ranging from the quasi-collisionless case to the hydrodynamic regime, we find that the onset of $v_n$ and of related measures of anisotropic flow can be described with a simple power-law ansatz, with an exponent that depends on the amount of rescatterings in the system. In the few-rescatterings regime we perform semi-analytical calculations, based on a systematic expansion in powers of time and the cross section, which can reproduce the numerical findings.

# Early time behavior of spatial and momentum anisotropies in kinetic theory across different Knudsen numbers

## Key claim

The early-time growth of anisotropic flow coefficients $v_n$ follows a simple power-law $v_n(t) \propto t^{\gamma_n}$, where the exponent $\gamma_n$ transitions from $\gamma_n = n+1$ in the few-collisions regime to $\gamma_n = n$ in the hydrodynamic regime, with the crossover occurring at smaller Knudsen numbers for higher harmonics (e.g., $v_3$ transitions at smaller Kn than $v_2$).

## Method

The authors performed two-dimensional transport simulations of massless hard-sphere particles using a covariant transport algorithm, covering Knudsen numbers from approximately 0.02 (hydrodynamic regime, ~25 rescatterings per particle) to approximately 10 (quasi-collisionless, <0.1 rescatterings per particle). For the few-rescatterings regime, they developed a semi-analytical approach based on a Taylor expansion of the phase-space distribution in powers of time, using the Boltzmann equation to replace time derivatives with spatial derivatives and powers of the cross section, truncated at orders up to $t^{15}$ and $\sigma^5$. Both approaches used a fixed initial geometry with a Gaussian spatial profile modulated by eccentricity parameters $\tilde{\varepsilon}_2$ and $\tilde{\varepsilon}_3$, and a thermal momentum distribution with position-dependent temperature.

## Key numbers

- The scaling exponent $\gamma_2$ transitions from $\gamma_2 \approx 3$ (few-collisions regime, $v_2 \propto t^3$) to $\gamma_2 \approx 2$ (hydrodynamic regime, $v_2 \propto t^2$) as Knudsen number decreases from Kn $\approx 10$ to Kn $\approx 0.02$.
- The scaling exponent $\gamma_3$ transitions from $\gamma_3 \approx 4$ (few-collisions regime, $v_3 \propto t^4$) to $\gamma_3 \approx 3$ (hydrodynamic regime, $v_3 \propto t^3$) over a smaller Kn-range than for $v_2$.
- The initial spatial eccentricities were set to $\varepsilon_2^{\bf x} \simeq 0.15$ and $\varepsilon_3^{\bf x} \simeq 0.15$ (derived from $\tilde{\varepsilon}_2/4$ and $\tilde{\varepsilon}_3/\sqrt{2\pi}$ respectively).
- The characteristic system size $R$ was 6.68 fm in the calculations.
- Simulations used $N_{\rm p} = 2 \times 10^5$ test particles with $N_{\rm iter.} = \mathcal{O}(10^3)$ iterations, giving effective particle counts $> 10^8$.
- At Kn $\approx 5$, about 16% of initially present particles disappear during the whole system evolution in the $2\to 0$ collision model.
- Analytical calculations at $\mathcal{O}(\sigma^3)$ reproduced numerical $v_2$ results within 10% at $t/R = 1$ for Kn $\approx 25$, and up to $t/R \approx 0.6$ for Kn $\approx 5$.

## Context

This work bridges the gap between known results in fluid dynamics, where $v_n(t) \propto t^n$ at early times (Heinz, 2002; Kolb, 2002; Teaney, 2010), and transport/analytical studies in the few-collisions regime finding $v_n(t) \propto t^{n+1}$ (Heiselberg, 1998; Borghini, 2010; Gombeaud, 2007; Alver, 2010). The authors extend their previous analytical Taylor-expansion approach (Borrell, 2021) to systematically include higher orders in both time and cross section, enabling comparison with numerical simulations across the full Knudsen range. The work enables future studies of how higher-order flow harmonics ($v_4$, etc.) behave at early times, and provides a framework for testing whether the observed Kn-dependent scaling exponents can be reproduced by summing multiple terms in the Taylor expansion rather than requiring non-integer exponents.
