# The MexNICA Collaboration in the MPD-NICA Experiment at JINR: Experimental and Theoretical Achievements

> The MexNICA Collaboration coordinates the activities of Mexican scientists, engineers, postdoctoral fellows and students in the Multi-Purpose Detector experiment at the Nuclotron-based Ion Collider fAcility of the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia. Established in 2016, the collaboration brings together five Mexican institutions whose contributions span detector development as well phenomenological and theoretical studies, including modeling by means of Monte Carlo simulations. This work summarizes the main achievements of MexNICA, consisting of the development of the miniBeBe trigger detector as well of results of phenomenological investigations of the baryon-rich region in the QCD phase diagram accessible at NICA energies, and theoretical advances based on lattice QCD and effective models.

# The MexNICA Collaboration in the MPD-NICA Experiment at JINR: Experimental and Theoretical Achievements

## Key claim

The MexNICA Collaboration has developed the miniBeBe trigger detector and produced phenomenological and theoretical predictions for the baryon-rich region of the QCD phase diagram accessible at NICA energies, but the lattice QCD simulations using the O(4) non-linear sigma model found no clear signature of a Critical End Point within the explored baryon chemical potential range up to approximately 309 MeV, with hints suggesting the CEP may lie near the boundary of the accessible domain.

## Method

The collaboration pursued three interconnected pillars: experimental development of the miniBeBe trigger detector using plastic scintillators coupled to Silicon Photomultipliers; phenomenological studies employing the UrQMD transport model with GEANT detector simulations, the CRAB correlation afterburner code, and the Linear Sigma Model with quarks; and theoretical lattice QCD simulations using the O(4) non-linear sigma model as an effective theory for two-flavor QCD in the chiral limit, where topological charge serves as a proxy for baryon number to circumvent the sign problem.

## Key numbers

- The miniBeBe detector targets a time resolution of less than 100 ps using plastic scintillators EJ232 of dimensions 20 mm x 20 mm x 5 mm, with 320 SiPMs across the entire system
- The nominal Fast Forward Detector of MPD demonstrates efficiency exceeding 50% only for central and semi-central collisions with multiplicities above 25 particles
- The miniBeBe detector has a total length of 1611 mm, detector radius of 156 mm, maximum deformation below 1 mm under operational loads, and total mass of 26.18 kg
- The cooling system maintains SiPM operating temperatures below 22.6 degrees Celsius with water inlet at 18 degrees Celsius and outlet at 21.4 degrees Celsius
- Prototype electronics achieved resolution time of less than 200 ps for lowest energy radiation and around 50 ps for high-energy muons in laboratory tests
- At NICA energies, magnetic fields can reach magnitudes where |eB| approaches 20,000 MeV^2
- Thermal statistical models predict baryon-to-meson transition at temperature around 140 MeV and baryon chemical potential around 420 MeV, corresponding to center-of-mass energy per nucleon pair approximately 8.2 GeV
- Lattice QCD simulations show critical temperature decreases monotonically with increasing baryon chemical potential up to approximately 309 MeV, where critical temperature remains above approximately 106 MeV
- Lattice QCD establishes that at vanishing baryon chemical potential, the transition is a crossover occurring at pseudocritical temperature approximately 155 MeV

## Context

This work extends the scientific program of the MPD experiment at the NICA facility at JINR, complementing the RHIC Beam Energy Scan at Brookhaven National Laboratory (7.7-200 GeV) and LHC operations at CERN (TeV energies with low baryon chemical potential). The phenomenological predictions for hyperon polarization build on the observation of global Lambda polarization at RHIC, with recent HADES data showing hints of increasing polarization toward lower energies consistent with the predicted rising edge of the peak. The lattice QCD approach using the O(4) model circumvents the sign problem that prevents standard lattice QCD calculations at finite baryon density, and the results provide non-perturbative constraints that complement effective model studies such as the Linear Sigma Model with quarks. The miniBeBe detector addresses the limitation that the nominal Fast Forward Detector of MPD has efficiency exceeding 50% only for central and semi-central collisions, enabling trigger capability for peripheral and fixed-target collisions.
