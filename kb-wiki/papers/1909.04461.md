# Traces of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition in the analytic properties of hot QCD

> The nuclear liquid-gas transition at normal nuclear densities, $n \sim n_0 = 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$, and small temperatures, $T \sim 20$ MeV, has a large influence on analytic properties of the QCD grand-canonical thermodynamic potential. A classical van der Waals equation is used to determine these unexpected features due to dense cold matter qualitatively. The existence of the nuclear matter critical point results in thermodynamic branch points, which are located at complex chemical potential values, for $T > T_c \simeq 20$ MeV, and exhibit a moderate model dependence up to rather large temperatures $T \lesssim 100$ MeV. The behavior at higher temperatures is studied using the van der Waals hadron resonance gas (vdW-HRG) model. The baryon-baryon interactions have a decisive influence on the QCD thermodynamics close to $μ_B = 0$. In particular, nuclear matter singularities limit the radius of convergence $r_{μ_B/T}$ of the Taylor expansion in $μ_B/T$, with $r_{μ_B/T} \sim 2-3$ values at $T \sim 140-170$ MeV obtained in the vdW-HRG model.

## Key claim

The nuclear liquid-gas phase transition, which ends at a critical point near \(T_c \simeq 20\) MeV and \(\mu_B^c \simeq 900\) MeV, leaves thermodynamic branch point singularities in the complex chemical potential plane that persist to much higher temperatures (up to \(T \lesssim 100\) MeV with moderate model dependence) and can limit the radius of convergence of the Taylor expansion in \(\mu_B/T\) at crossover temperatures \(T \sim 140\)–\(170\) MeV, even though the phase transition itself is not present there.

## Method

The authors use the classical van der Waals (vdW) equation for nucleons to derive analytic expressions for the thermodynamic branch points associated with the nuclear liquid-gas transition. They then compare these analytic results to numerical solutions from three more realistic nuclear matter models: the quantum van der Waals (QvdW) model, the Skyrme mean-field model, and the Walecka relativistic mean-field model. Finally, they extend the analysis to higher temperatures using the van der Waals hadron resonance gas (vdW-HRG) model, which includes all known hadronic resonances and applies vdW interactions to all baryon-baryon (and antibaryon-antibaryon) pairs, and they compute the radius of convergence of the Taylor expansion in \(\mu_B/T\) around \(\mu_B = 0\) using up to 120 Taylor coefficients.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear matter critical point in the QvdW model: \(T_c \simeq 19.7\) MeV, \(n_c \simeq 0.07\) fm\(^{-3}\), \(\mu_c \simeq 908\) MeV.
- Nuclear matter critical point in the classical vdW model: \(T_c = 8a/(27b) \simeq 28.5\) MeV, \(n_c = 1/(3b) \simeq 0.10\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- Nuclear matter critical point in the Skyrme model: \(T_c = 21.9\) MeV, \(n_c = 0.06\) fm\(^{-3}\), \(\mu_c \simeq 895\) MeV.
- Nuclear matter critical point in the Walecka model: \(T_c = 18.9\) MeV, \(n_c = 0.07\) fm\(^{-3}\), \(\mu_c \simeq 909\) MeV.
- In the vdW-HRG model, the radius of convergence in \(\mu_B/T\) is \(r_{\mu/T} \sim 2\)–\(3\) at temperatures \(T \sim 140\)–\(170\) MeV.
- The real part of the limiting branch point chemical potential crosses zero at about \(T \simeq 180\) MeV in the vdW-HRG model.
- At \(T = 100\)–\(200\) MeV, the radius of convergence \(r_{\mu/T}\) can be estimated with 10% accuracy using 5–10 nonzero Taylor expansion coefficients (Mercer-Roberts estimator) or 8–22 coefficients (modified Mercer-Roberts estimator), depending on temperature.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of thermodynamic branch points from the classical van der Waals equation (Hemmer, 1964) to realistic nuclear matter models and to the hot hadronic phase described by the hadron resonance gas model. It builds on prior studies showing that nuclear matter criticality influences conserved charge susceptibilities in hot QCD matter (Vovchenko, 2016) and along the freeze-out curve in heavy-ion collisions (Fukushima, 2014; Vovchenko, 2017; Poberezhnyuk, 2019). The results suggest that the nuclear liquid-gas transition, rather than a hypothetical QCD chiral critical point, may be the dominant singularity limiting the convergence of lattice QCD Taylor expansions at moderate baryon densities, and they provide a framework for interpreting future lattice QCD results and heavy-ion collision data from the CBM experiment at FAIR.
