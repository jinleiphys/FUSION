# Opportunities for Nuclear Physics & Quantum Information Science

> This whitepaper is an outcome of the workshop Intersections between Nuclear Physics and Quantum Information held at Argonne National Laboratory on 28-30 March 2018 [www.phy.anl.gov/npqi2018/]. The workshop brought together 116 national and international experts in nuclear physics and quantum information science to explore opportunities for the two fields to collaborate on topics of interest to the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) Office of Science, Office of Nuclear Physics, and more broadly to U.S. society and industry. The workshop consisted of 22 invited and 10 contributed talks, as well as three panel discussion sessions. Topics discussed included quantum computation, quantum simulation, quantum sensing, nuclear physics detectors, nuclear many-body problem, entanglement at collider energies, and lattice gauge theories.

## Key claim

This whitepaper argues that quantum information science (QIS) can address several challenges faced by nuclear physics, and that QIS in turn will benefit from development through real-world problems, but the authors emphasize that significant remaining technical and theoretical challenges mean the realization of this potential will require cooperative efforts of academia, national laboratories, and industry.

## Key numbers

- 116 national and international experts participated in the workshop at Argonne National Laboratory on 28-30 March 2018
- The workshop featured 22 invited and 10 contributed talks, plus three panel discussion sessions
- Industry quantum computers with order 50 qubits (without error correction) have been unveiled by companies including Google, IBM, Intel, and Microsoft
- IBM provides access to two 5-qubit, one 16-qubit, and two 20-qubit processors through the IBM Q Experience
- Rigetti announced a 128-qubit processor with plans to make it available by August 2019
- D-wave has built a quantum annealer consisting of 2000 qubits
- Analog quantum simulators with up to 50 trapped ions or atoms (qubits) have already been built
- A quantum computer with around 300 qubits would contain more possible states than there are atoms in the universe
- A realistic implementation of Shor's algorithm that includes error correction would currently require millions of physical qubits
- The deuteron ground-state binding energy was determined to within a few percent of the empirical value using the IBM QX5 and Rigetti 19Q cloud quantum computing resources

## Context

This whitepaper complements the earlier white paper "Quantum Computing for Theoretical Nuclear Physics" from the workshop held at the Institute for Nuclear Theory, University of Washington during 14-15 November 2017. The paper extends discussions from prior work on quantum link models for non-abelian gauge theories by Wiese (2013) and Zohar (2015), and builds on demonstrations such as the real-time dynamics of spontaneous particle-antiparticle creation in 1+1 quantum electrodynamics studied by Martinez (2016) using a few-qubit analog quantum simulator. The document identifies five broad research opportunities to facilitate QIS research in the context of nuclear physics, including developing a theory program for digital quantum computers and quantum simulators, supporting QIS technology development for nuclear physics applications, and fostering a new workforce skilled in both fields.
