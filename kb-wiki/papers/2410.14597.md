# The interplay of astrophysics and nuclear physics in determining the properties of neutron stars

> Neutron star properties depend on both nuclear physics and astrophysical processes, and thus observations of neutron stars offer constraints on both large-scale astrophysics and the behavior of cold, dense matter. In this study, we use astronomical data to jointly infer the universal equation of state of dense matter along with two distinct astrophysical populations: Galactic neutron stars observed electromagnetically and merging neutron stars in binaries observed with gravitational waves. We place constraints on neutron star properties and quantify the extent to which they are attributable to macrophysics or microphysics. We confirm previous results indicating that the Galactic and merging neutron stars have distinct mass distributions. The inferred maximum mass of both Galactic neutron stars, $M_{\rm pop, EM}=2.05^{+0.11}_{-0.06}\,M_{\odot}$ (median and 90\% symmetric credible interval), and merging neutron star binaries, $M_{\rm pop, GW}=1.85^{+0.39}_{-0.16}\,M_{\odot}$, are consistent with the maximum mass of nonrotating neutron stars set by nuclear physics, $M_{\rm TOV} =2.28^{+0.41}_{-0.21}\,M_\odot$. The radius of a $1.4\,M_{\odot}$ neutron star is $12.2^{+0.8}_{-0.9}\,$km, consistent with, though $\sim 20\%$ tighter than, previous results using an identical equation of state model. Even though observed Galactic and merging neutron stars originate from populations with distinct properties, there is currently no evidence that astrophysical processes cannot produce neutron stars up to the maximum value imposed by nuclear physics.

## Key claim

The paper jointly infers the dense-matter equation of state and two distinct neutron star populations (Galactic neutron stars observed electromagnetically and merging binary neutron stars observed with gravitational waves) and finds that, while the mass distributions of these populations are different, there is currently no evidence that astrophysical processes cannot produce neutron stars up to the maximum mass imposed by nuclear physics (the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit).

## Method

The authors model the dense-matter equation of state using a mixture of Gaussian processes, which allows for flexible EoS morphologies including phase transitions. They separately model the Galactic neutron star population with a bimodal Gaussian distribution and the merging binary neutron star population with a truncated power-law, each with its own maximum mass cutoff. A multi-stage reweighting scheme is used to jointly infer the EoS and mass distribution parameters from radio pulsar mass measurements (74 pulsars), NICER X-ray pulse-profile observations (three pulsars), and gravitational-wave data (GW170817 and GW190425), with gravitational-wave selection effects accounted for.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of Galactic neutron stars: \(M_{\rm pop, EM} = 2.05^{+0.11}_{-0.06}\,M_{\odot}\) (median and 90% symmetric credible interval)
- Maximum mass of merging binary neutron stars: \(M_{\rm pop, GW} = 1.85^{+0.39}_{-0.16}\,M_{\odot}\)
- Maximum mass of nonrotating neutron stars (TOV limit): \(M_{\rm TOV} = 2.28^{+0.41}_{-0.21}\,M_{\odot}\)
- Radius of a \(1.4\,M_{\odot}\) neutron star: \(R_{1.4} = 12.2^{+0.8}_{-0.9}\,\)km
- 90% lower bound on the maximum speed of sound squared anywhere inside the neutron star: \(0.59\)
- Difference between maximum Galactic neutron star mass and TOV mass at 90% credibility: \(0.53\,M_{\odot}\)
- Difference between maximum merging binary neutron star mass and TOV mass at 90% credibility: \(0.73\,M_{\odot}\)

## Context

This work extends the approach of Legred (2021), which used the same Gaussian process EoS model, by jointly inferring the EoS with two separate astrophysical populations rather than assuming a single mass distribution. It confirms the finding of the LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA collaboration (2021) that Galactic and merging neutron stars have distinct mass distributions. The joint inference framework enables future studies to determine whether the maximum mass of neutron stars in different subpopulations is limited by nuclear physics or by astrophysical formation processes, and mitigates biases that can arise when inferring either the EoS or mass distribution alone with improper assumptions about the other.
