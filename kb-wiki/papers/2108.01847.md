# Anisotropic turbulence in relativistic plasmas

> Signs of turbulence have been observed at the relativistic heavy ion collision at high collision energies. We study the signatures of turbulence in this system and find that there are significant departures from isotropic turbulence in the initial stages of the collision. Since the anisotropic fluctuations are sub leading to the isotropic fluctuations, the Kolmogorov spectrum can usually be obtained even for the initial stages. However, the energy spectrum and the temperature fluctuations indicate deviations from isotropic turbulence. Since a strong momentum anisotropy exists between the transverse and the longitudinal plane, we study the energy density spectrum in these two planes. The geometrical anisotropy is reflected in the anisotropic turbulence generated in the rotating plasma and we find that the scaling exponent is different in the two planes. We find that the scaling exponents in the longitudinal plane are independent of centrality while the scaling exponent in the transverse plane depends on the centrality range. We also obtain the temperature spectrum in the initial stages. The spectrum deviates from the Gaussian spectra expected for an isotropic turbulence. All these seem to indicate that the large scale momentum anisotropy persists in the smaller length scales for the relativistic heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper claims that anisotropic turbulence exists in the initial and pre-equilibrium stages of relativistic heavy-ion collisions, with different scaling exponents in the longitudinal and transverse planes, but the authors hedge that anisotropic fluctuations are subleading to isotropic fluctuations, so the Kolmogorov spectrum can usually still be obtained even for the initial stages.

## Method

The authors used the AMPT (A Multi-Phase Transport) model in its string melting version to simulate Au-Au collisions at RHIC energies. They extracted particle positions and velocities from the simulation, separated the laminar and turbulent velocity components using a grid-based spatial averaging, and computed the velocity correlation tensor and its Fourier transform to obtain the kinetic energy spectrum. For the longitudinal plane (x-z), they applied a Lorentz boost correction to the velocity correlators to account for relativistic motion along the beam axis, while for the transverse plane (x-y), no boost was needed. Temperature fluctuations were studied in the pre-equilibrium stage by computing the temperature from energy density under the assumption of local thermal equilibrium at the grid scale.

## Key numbers

* Kolmogorov length scale for the QGP system: approximately 1.24 fm, corresponding to a maximum wave number k_max approximately 5 fm^-1
* Minimum wave number for the system: k_min = 0.524 fm^-1 for a system size of 14.4 fm in each direction
* Reynolds number Re >= 8.52 for the system created at RHIC energies, giving a Kolmogorov length scale of approximately 2.4 fm and a minimum k_max of 3 fm^-1
* Longitudinal plane scaling exponent at 200 GeV collision energy: nu = -1.596 for 0-10% centrality, nu = -1.568 for 20-40% centrality, nu = -1.663 for 40-80% centrality (all approximately -5/3, the Kolmogorov value)
* Transverse plane scaling exponent at 200 GeV collision energy: nu = -1.264 for 0-10% centrality, nu = -1.183 for 20-40% centrality, nu = -1.389 for 40-80% centrality (closer to -4/3 than to -5/3)

## Context

This work extends studies of turbulence in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, which have been observed experimentally at RHIC and LHC, by explicitly examining anisotropy in the turbulence spectra. The authors build on prior work showing that chromo-Weibel instabilities can form in QGP with momentum anisotropy (Weibel, 1959; Mrowczynski, 1993) and on studies relating relativistic fluctuations to non-relativistic Navier-Stokes equations (Floerchinger, 2016). The finding that longitudinal plane exponents are independent of centrality while transverse plane exponents depend on centrality enables future work connecting the initial geometric anisotropy to the turbulent cascade and to observables such as the Schmidt number, which differs between the two planes.
