# Production cross section of neutron-rich isotopes with radioactive and stable beams

> The production cross section of neutron-rich isotopes of Ca, Zn, Te, Xe, and Pt are predicted in the diffusive multi-nucleon transfer reactions with stable and radioactive beams. With these isotopes one can treat the neutron shell evolution beyond N = 28, 50, 82, and 126. Because of the small cross sections, the production of nuclei near the neutron drip line requires the optimal choice of reaction partners and bombarding energies.

## Key claim

The production cross sections of neutron-rich isotopes of Ca, Zn, Te, Xe, and Pt are predicted using diffusive multi-nucleon transfer reactions with both stable and radioactive beams, but the abstract's promise of "predicting" cross sections is hedged by the fact that the calculations assume thermal equilibrium and use theoretical mass excesses and neutron separation energies from the finite-range liquid-drop model for unknown nuclei, making the results model-dependent upper limits rather than definitive predictions.

## Method

The authors employ the dinuclear system (DNS) model, treating the reaction as a three-step process: formation of an initial DNS at the capture stage, diffusion in charge and mass asymmetry coordinates via multi-nucleon transfer, and decay of the DNS into two fragments. The production cross section is calculated as the product of the capture cross section (determined by bombarding energy and angular momentum) and a formation-decay probability derived from the potential energy surface of the DNS, using a statistical method that accounts for barriers against decay in the relative distance coordinate and toward more symmetric configurations. The calculations focus on reactions where the projectile and target are chosen so that the potential energy of the DNS containing the desired neutron-rich nucleus is close to that of the initial DNS, and the excitation energy of the product is kept below its neutron separation energy to avoid losses from neutron evaporation.

## Key numbers

- For neutron-rich Ca isotopes (N>28) in the reaction 136Te + 48Ca, cross sections larger than 1 pb are predicted for isotopes up to 60Ca; replacing 134Te with 136Te leads to about 5 times larger yields.
- For neutron-rich Zn isotopes (N>50) in reactions of 142,144Xe + 68,70Zn, the yield decreases by almost two orders of magnitude when the neutron number increases by 2 units; the largest cross section for 86Zn occurs in the 144Xe + 70Zn reaction.
- For neutron-rich Te isotopes (N>82) in reactions of 134,136Te + 48Ca, cross sections larger than 0.1 pb are predicted for isotopes up to 142Te; a 0.5 MeV uncertainty in the neutron separation energy causes about 20% uncertainty in the cross section.
- For neutron-rich Xe isotopes (N>93) in reactions with a 144Xe beam, cross sections larger than 0.01 pb are predicted for 148,150,152Xe; the 48Ca target is most favorable for producing 152Xe.
- For neutron-rich Pt isotopes (N around 126) in the reaction 198Pt + 48Ca, cross sections for 202,204,206Pt are predicted; the cross section for 204Pt in the 2n evaporation channel is larger than 0.1 nb.
- In the reaction 72Ni + 198Pt, the isotope 210Pt (N=132) can be produced with a cross section of about 1 pb.
- The production rate for a cross section of 1 nb is roughly estimated as 0.01-1 particle per day with a radioactive beam intensity of 10^7-10^9 particles per second; a cross section of 1 pb would require a beam intensity of at least 10^10 particles per second for reasonable irradiation time.

## Context

This work extends the DNS model approach previously applied by Adamian et al. (2010) to predict production cross sections for neutron-rich isotopes via multi-nucleon transfer reactions, now considering a wider range of projectile-target combinations including radioactive beams. The calculations are relevant for planning experiments at facilities like the Rare Isotope Science Project in Korea (KoRIA, 2013) and complement fragmentation and fission methods for producing nuclei near the neutron drip line, which the authors note are less effective for extremely neutron-rich species. The results enable future experimental searches for shell evolution beyond the magic numbers N=28, 50, 82, and 126, particularly for isotopes of Ca, Zn, Te, Xe, and Pt.
