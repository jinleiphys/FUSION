# Information content of the low-energy electric dipole strength: correlation analysis

> We study the information content carried by the electric dipole strength with respect to isovector and isoscalar indicators characterizing bulk nuclear matter and finite nuclei. To separate isoscalar and isovector modes, and low-energy strength and giant resonances, we analyze the E1 strength as a function of excitation energy $E$ and momentum transfer $q$. We use the self-consistent nuclear density functional theory with Skyrme energy density functionals, augmented by the random phase, to compute the E1 strength, and covariance analysis to assess correlations between observables. Calculations are performed for spherical, doubly-magic nuclei $^{208}$Pb and $^{132}$Sn. We demonstrate that E1 transition densities in the low-energy region below the giant dipole resonance exhibit appreciable state dependence and multi-nodal structures, which are fingerprints of weak collectivity. The correlation between the accumulated low-energy strength and symmetry energy is weak, and dramatically depends on the energy cutoff assumed. On the other hand, a strong correlation is predicted between isovector indicators and the accumulated isovector strength at $E$ around 20 MeV and momentum transfer $q\sim 0.65$ fm$^{-1}$. Momentum- and coordinate-space pattern of the low-energy dipole modes indicate a strong fragmentation into individual particle-hole excitations. The global measure of low-energy dipole strength poorly correlates with the nuclear symmetry energy and other isovector characteristics. Consequently, our results do not support the suggestion that there exists a collective "pygmy dipole resonance," which is a strong indicator of nuclear isovector properties.

## Key claim

The low-energy electric dipole strength in heavy nuclei does not constitute a collective "pygmy dipole resonance" that strongly correlates with nuclear isovector properties; instead, the correlation between accumulated low-energy strength and symmetry energy is weak and dramatically depends on the assumed energy cutoff, while strong correlation with isovector indicators is found only at higher excitation energies around 20 MeV and nonzero momentum transfer.

## Method

The authors used self-consistent nuclear density functional theory with Skyrme energy density functionals (SV-min, SV-bas, and UNEDF0), augmented by the random phase approximation (RPA), to compute the electric dipole (E1) strength in the spherical doubly-magic nuclei 208Pb and 132Sn. They performed covariance analysis to assess correlations between observables, and studied the E1 strength as a function of excitation energy E and momentum transfer q to separate isoscalar and isovector modes and to distinguish low-energy strength from giant resonances.

## Key numbers

- The accumulated isovector strength at E around 20 MeV and momentum transfer q ~ 0.65 fm^{-1} shows strong correlation with isovector indicators.
- The base parameterization SV-bas had fixed nuclear matter properties: incompressibility K = 234 MeV, isoscalar effective mass m*/m = 0.9, symmetry energy a_sym = 30 MeV, and TRK sum rule enhancement κ = 0.4.
- The TRK sum rule enhancement κ varied across functionals: 0.4 for SV-bas, 0.25 for UNEDF0, and 0.08 for SV-min.
- The energy-dependent folding width used for RPA strength was Γ(E_ν) = max[0.2 MeV, (E_ν - 8 MeV)/(6 MeV)].
- The cutoff energy E_max = 10 MeV was used for the low-energy E1 strength analysis in 208Pb.
- The isovector accumulated strength at q = 0.65 fm^{-1} shows strong correlation with isovector indicators in a wide energy range above the GDR region and below 25 MeV.
- The isoscalar accumulated strength at q = 0.65 fm^{-1} shows sensitivity to incompressibility K at low energies below the GDR region and in the high energy region above 20 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the covariance analysis of Reinhard (2010) that suggested lack of correlation between pygmy dipole resonance strength and nuclear isovector properties, and contradicts studies by Piechaczek (2006), Klimkiewicz (2007), and Tsoneva (2008) that advocated a collective PDR mode correlated with symmetry energy. The results enable future experimental studies at nonzero momentum transfer (q > 0) to better characterize E1 strength and relate it to fundamental nuclear properties such as incompressibility and symmetry energy, and the authors note that extension to other models including relativistic EDF will be carried out in a forthcoming study by Paar (2012).
