# Emulation of Proton-Deuteron Scattering via the Reduced Basis Method and Active Learning: Detailed Description

> Nucleon-deuteron ($Nd$) scattering can be used to constrain three-nucleon forces in chiral effective field theory ($χ$EFT). However, high-fidelity calculations, such as the Hyperspherical Harmonic (HH) method, are computationally expensive, making it difficult or even prohibitive to explore the vast parameter space of $χ$EFT\xspace. To address this challenge, specifically for proton-deuteron ($pd$) scattering below the deuteron breakup threshold, we developed model-driven emulators based on the Reduced Basis Method (RBM) and active learning techniques, as presented in \href{https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.01844}{arXiv:2511.01844}. The method exploits the similarities between solutions at different parameter points to significantly reduce computational costs. In this companion paper, we provide a comprehensive description of our HH-based high-fidelity calculations and implementation of both variational-method-based and Galerkin-projection-based scattering emulators. We demonstrate the effectiveness of active learning in the form of greedy algorithms for selecting optimal training points in the parameter space, and the high accuracy and speed of the emulators, for two different nucleon forces and two scattering channels (${1/2}^+$ and ${1/2}^-$). For example, in a two-dimensional parameter space, the relative emulation errors can be reduced to $10^{-7}$ with fewer than 10 training points. Our work paves the way for the efficient calibration of $χ$EFT\xspace nucleon interactions using Bayesian statistics, and the methodology can be applied to other nuclear scattering processes (including neutron-deuteron scattering), as well as other finite quantum systems.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that model-driven emulators based on the Reduced Basis Method (RBM) and active learning can achieve relative emulation errors as low as 10^{-7} for proton-deuteron scattering below breakup threshold using fewer than 10 training points in a two-dimensional parameter space, but only for the specific case where the deuteron internal wave function is fixed and only the three-nucleon force parameters c_E and c_D are varied.

## Method

The authors constructed high-fidelity scattering solutions using the Hyperspherical Harmonic (HH) method with the Kohn variational principle, then built reduced-order models by projecting the full linear system onto a low-dimensional subspace spanned by snapshots at selected parameter points. They implemented both variational-method-based and Galerkin-projection-based emulators, using greedy algorithms with error estimators to iteratively select optimal training points in the two-dimensional parameter space of the chiral three-nucleon force parameters c_E and c_D. The emulators were tested for two versions of the Norfolk chiral interaction (NVIIa and NVIIb) and two scattering channels (1/2^+ and 1/2^-).

## Key numbers

- Relative emulation errors reduced to 10^{-7} with fewer than 10 training points in a two-dimensional parameter space
- High-fidelity HH calculations use matrices with dimensionality reaching 10^3 to 10^4 for converged Nd scattering amplitudes below breakup
- Two versions of the Norfolk force tested: NVIIa with regulators (R_L, R_S) = (1.2, 0.8) fm and NVIIb with (R_L, R_S) = (1.0, 0.7) fm
- Both forces fitted to NN scattering data up to 200 MeV in the laboratory frame

## Context

This work extends the Reduced Basis Method (also known as eigenvector continuation) previously applied to nuclear ground states and resonance states (Frame 2017, Sarkar 2020, Konig 2019) and two-body scattering (Furnstahl 2020, Drischler 2021, Melendez 2021, Zhang 2021) to the more challenging case of three-body proton-deuteron scattering. The emulators enable efficient exploration of the chiral effective field theory parameter space for calibrating three-nucleon forces using Bayesian statistics, which would be computationally prohibitive with direct high-fidelity HH calculations. The methodology can be extended to neutron-deuteron scattering and other finite quantum systems.
