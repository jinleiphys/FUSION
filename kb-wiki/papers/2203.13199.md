# Snowmass 2021 White Paper: Electron Ion Collider for High Energy Physics

> Electron Ion Collider (EIC) is a particle accelerator facility planned for construction at Brookhaven National Laboratory on Long Island, New York by the United States Department of Energy. EIC will provide capabilities of colliding beams of polarized electrons with polarized beams of proton and light ions. EIC will be one of the largest and most sophisticated new accelerator facilities worldwide, and the only new large-scale accelerator facility planned for construction in the United States in the next few decades. The versatility, resolving power and intensity of EIC will present many new opportunities to address some of the crucial and fundamental open scientific questions in particle physics. This document provides an overview of the science case of EIC from the perspective of the high energy physics community.

## Key claim

The Electron Ion Collider (EIC) will provide orders of magnitude higher luminosity than HERA, the only previous electron-proton collider, and will be the first lepton-ion collider with both beams polarized, enabling direct extraction of parity-violating electroweak neutral-current cross sections and the first measurement of polarized neutral-current structure functions, though the precision of the weak mixing angle extraction is limited by electron polarimetry uncertainty rather than parton distribution function uncertainties at high luminosity settings.

## Method

The paper presents projections for parity-violating deep inelastic scattering asymmetries using the ECCE detector configuration and the luminosity assumptions from the EIC Yellow Report, with 20 million events generated per beam energy combination using the Djangoh generator (version 4.6.16) that includes full electromagnetic and electroweak radiative effects. Events were selected based on deep inelastic scattering conditions to limit photoproduction background and ensure high electron sample purity, then unfolded to correct for bin migration, with systematic uncertainties including a 1% relative particle background uncorrelated across bins and a 1% relative electron polarization uncertainty correlated across bins of the same center-of-mass energy. The weak mixing angle was extracted using parton-model structure functions, with parton distribution function uncertainties evaluated using CT18NLO, MMHT2014nlo_68cl, and NNPDF31_nlo_as_0118 sets.

## Key numbers

- Annual luminosity assumptions: 15.4 fb^-1 for 18x275 GeV ep, 100 fb^-1 for 10x275 GeV ep, 44.8 fb^-1 for 10x100 GeV ep, and 36.8 fb^-1 for 5x100 GeV ep; for eD collisions: 15.4 fb^-1 for 18x137 GeV, 100 fb^-1 for 10x137 GeV, 44.8 fb^-1 for 10x100 GeV, and 36.8 fb^-1 for 5x100 GeV (deuterium energy per nucleon)
- The gamma-Z interference suppression factor eta_gammaZ can reach tens of percent at EIC energies, defined as (G_F M_Z^2 / (2 sqrt(2) pi alpha)) * (Q^2 / (Q^2 + M_Z^2)) with G_F = 1.1663787(6) x 10^-5 GeV^-2 and M_Z = 91.1876 +/- 0.0021 GeV
- Electron beam polarization assumed at 80% with a relative 1% uncertainty
- Projected results for sin^2(theta_W) shown for five energy and luminosity combinations for both ep and eD collisions, with a combined "YR reference point" using 100 fb^-1 ep at 18x275 GeV and 10 fb^-1 eD at 18x137 GeV

## Context

This white paper extends the science case for the EIC beyond nuclear physics into high energy physics, particularly electroweak and beyond-the-Standard-Model physics, building on the EIC Yellow Report (AbdulKhalek et al., 2021) and the ECCE detector studies. The paper demonstrates complementarity between EIC and LHC measurements within the Standard Model Effective Field Theory framework, showing that EIC parity-violating asymmetry data can resolve flat directions in Wilson coefficient constraints that arise when using only LHC Drell-Yan data from ATLAS (2016). The projected weak mixing angle measurements bridge the energy scale between high-energy colliders like LEP, SLC, Tevatron, and LHC and low-energy experiments such as P2 (Becker et al., 2018), MOLLER, and SoLID PVDIS (Chen et al., 2014; Erler et al., 2014).
