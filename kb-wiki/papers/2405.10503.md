# Renormalizability of the leading order operator for neutrinoless double beta decay with the effects of finite nucleon size

> The fundamental process of neutrinoless double beta decay, $nn\to ppe^-e^-$, dominated by the exchange of light Majorana neutrinos, is studied in the framework of chiral effective field theory. Considering neutrinos as virtual states, we evaluate the contributions of finite nucleon size to the transition amplitude in a non-perturbative manner, as opposed to expanding these effects in powers of momentum. Based on the nucleon form factors expressed in terms of the dipole and Kelly parametrizations, we find that, at the leading order, the present scheme could renormalize the amplitude in the context of the standard mechanism and provide predictions consistent with the previous investigations. Consequently, we argue that the impact of the effects of finite nucleon size on the amplitude is comparable to that of the leading-order contact term introduced in [Phys. Rev. Lett.120, 202001(2018)] in a perturbative scheme. Our results provide not only a benchmark calculation for the transition amplitude between two schemes but also evidence for the reasonableness of non-perturbative treatment for the effects of finite nucleon size in conventional nuclear many-body methods.

# Renormalizability of the leading order operator for neutrinoless double beta decay with the effects of finite nucleon size

## Key claim
The authors show that including finite nucleon size effects via momentum-dependent form factors in a non-perturbative manner renders the leading-order neutrinoless double beta decay amplitude finite and regulator-independent, without requiring any short-range contact operator. This non-perturbative treatment yields results consistent with previous renormalization schemes that introduced a contact term, and the authors argue the finite nucleon size effects are comparable in magnitude to that contact term.

## Method
The authors study the $nn\to ppe^-e^-$ transition in the $^1S_0$ channel using heavy-baryon chiral effective field theory, with the light Majorana neutrino-exchange mechanism. They incorporate finite nucleon size effects by introducing momentum-dependent vector and axial-vector nucleon form factors (using both dipole and Kelly parametrizations) directly into the neutrino potential, rather than expanding them in powers of momentum. The leading-order amplitude is computed by solving the Lippmann-Schwinger equation with a Gaussian regulator, and cutoff independence is demonstrated by varying the momentum cutoff while fitting the strong-interaction low-energy constant to the neutron-proton scattering length $a_{np} = -23.74$ fm.

## Key numbers
- Neutron-proton scattering length used to fit strong interaction: $a_{np} = -23.74$ fm
- Dipole parametrization cutoff masses: $\Lambda_V = 0.84$ GeV (vector), $\Lambda_A = 1.05$ GeV (axial)
- Pion decay constant: $f_\pi = 92.2$ MeV; pion mass: $m_\pi = 138$ MeV; nucleon axial coupling: $g_A = 1.27$
- At kinematic point $|\pmb{p}| = 25$ MeV and $|\pmb{p}'| = 30$ MeV, the absolute amplitude $|A_\nu| = 0.01956(8)$ MeV$^{-2}$, where the error stems from the dependence on parametrization
- Amplitudes from dipole and Kelly parametrizations differ by only about 1% at any cutoff value
- Results consistent with previous estimations (Cirigliano 2020, Yang 2023) within 10% uncertainty

## Context
This work extends the chiral EFT treatment of neutrinoless double beta decay by Cirigliano (2018, 2019, 2020, 2021), who showed that naive dimensional analysis leads to a logarithmic divergence requiring a leading-order contact term with an undetermined low-energy constant. The present non-perturbative approach using nucleon form factors provides an alternative renormalization scheme that avoids unknown constants, similar in spirit to the relativistic method of Yang (2023). The results validate conventional nuclear many-body calculations (Simkovic 1999, 2009; Hyvarinen 2015; Yao 2021) that treat finite nucleon size non-perturbatively, showing these calculations do not miss leading-order lepton-number-violating physics. This enables more precise nuclear matrix element calculations and can be generalized to non-standard decay mechanisms.
