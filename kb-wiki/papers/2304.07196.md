# Jet substructure observables for jet quenching in Quark Gluon Plasma: a Machine Learning driven analysis

> We present a survey of a comprehensive set of jet substructure observables commonly used to study the modifications of jets resulting from interactions with the Quark Gluon Plasma in Heavy Ion Collisions. The \jewel{} event generator is used to produce simulated samples of quenched and unquenched jets. Three distinct analyses using Machine Learning techniques on the jet substructure observables have been performed to identify both linear and non-linear relations between the observables, and to distinguish the Quenched and Unquenched jet samples. We find that most of the observables are highly correlated, and that their information content can be captured by a small set of observables. We also find that the correlations between observables are resilient to quenching effects and that specific pairs of observables exhaust the full sensitivity to quenching effects. The code, the datasets, and instructions on how to reproduce this work are also provided.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that most jet substructure observables are highly correlated and that their information content can be captured by a small set of observables, with correlations resilient to quenching effects; however, these findings are based specifically on simulations using the JEWEL event generator with a particular set of medium parameters (initial temperature 440 MeV, critical temperature 170 MeV, centrality 0-10%) and may not generalize to other models or medium conditions.

## Method

The authors generated 320,000 simulated events each for quenched and unquenched jets using JEWEL+Pythia (version 2.2.0) at sqrt(s) = 5020 GeV with jet transverse momentum between 40 and 250 GeV/c and rapidity |y| < 2.5. They computed 28 jet substructure observables per jet, including angularities, N-subjettiness, jet charges, SoftDrop observables, and dynamical grooming observables, then applied three machine learning analyses: linear correlation analysis with Principal Component Analysis (PCA), a Deep Auto-Encoder for non-linear relations, and a classification analysis to distinguish quenched from unquenched samples.

## Key numbers

- 320,000 events generated for each of the quenched and unquenched samples
- Jet transverse momentum range: p_T in [40, 250] GeV/c
- Rapidity cut: |y| < 2.5
- Center-of-mass energy: sqrt(s) = 5020 GeV
- Medium parameters for quenched sample: tau_i = 0.4 fm/c, T_i = 440 MeV, T_c = 170 MeV, centrality 0-10%
- Analysis selection: jets with p_T > 80 GeV/c
- 28 observables studied in total across all categories
- PCA analysis shows that approximately 5-6 principal components capture most of the variance (R^2 near 1.0) for both quenched and unquenched samples

## Context

This work extends previous studies of jet quenching in heavy-ion collisions that used machine learning on low-level data such as jet images (Du 2020, Lai 2021, Apolinario 2021), by instead analyzing high-level jet substructure observables commonly reported by experiments. The study enables future work to focus on a reduced set of observables that capture the full sensitivity to quenching effects, and provides publicly available code and datasets for reproducibility. The authors note that most prior work privileged low-level data, while a machine learning driven analysis of high-level jet substructure variables had been missing in the literature.
