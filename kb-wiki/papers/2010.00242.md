# Influence of fast emissions and statistical de-excitation on the isospin transport ratio

> Isospin transport ratio is a powerful method to estimate the neutron-proton (n-p) equilibration in heavy-ion collisions, and extensively used to obtain information on the asy-stiffness of the nuclear Equation of State. In fact such a ratio is expected to bypass any perturbations introducing a linear transformation of the chosen observable. In particular, it is supposed to overcome contributions due to emission, either of dynamical or statistical nature, from the primary fragments formed during the collisions. In this paper we explore the validity of this assumption, looking at the quasi-projectile n-p ratio ($N/Z$) in peripheral and semi-peripheral events for Ca+Ca reactions at 35\amev{}, simulated via the Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics transport model, coupled to different statistical decay codes. The statistical de-excitation of the primary fragments introduces a linear transformation at relatively high excitation energies (above 2\amev{}) when the residue approaches the Evaporation Attractor Line, while some effect is produced at lower excitation energies due to the occurrence of some non-linearities. As for fast emissions after the end of the projectile-target interaction it is shown that they introduce a non-linear transformation too.

## Key claim

The isospin transport ratio, widely used to extract the degree of neutron-proton equilibration in heavy-ion collisions, is not fully immune to particle emissions as previously assumed: statistical de-excitation introduces non-linear transformations at low excitation energies (below 2 MeV/nucleon), and fast emissions after projectile-target separation also produce non-linear distortions, both of which affect the ratio's ability to cancel such perturbations.

## Method

The authors simulated peripheral and semi-peripheral Ca+Ca reactions at 35 MeV/nucleon using the Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD) transport model with a stiff symmetry energy parametrization (L=108 MeV), stopping the dynamical calculation at 500 fm/c. The hot quasi-projectile nuclei were then fed into three different statistical decay codes (GEMINI++, GEMINIf90, and SIMON) to model secondary de-excitation. To isolate fast emission effects, a time back-tracing procedure was applied to identify the quasi-projectile at the moment of projectile-target separation (t_DIC), allowing comparison of the isospin transport ratio at three stages: at separation, at 500 fm/c, and after statistical decay.

## Key numbers

- Reactions studied: 40Ca+40Ca, 48Ca+48Ca, and 48Ca+40Ca at 35 MeV/nucleon
- Statistical de-excitation introduces non-linear effects on the isospin transport ratio for excitation energies below 2 MeV/nucleon, causing discrepancies of about 0.1 in the ratio value
- Above 2 MeV/nucleon, the transformation from statistical decay becomes approximately linear as residues approach the Evaporation Attractor Line
- Fast emissions between t_DIC and 500 fm/c become significant for semi-central events with average reduced impact parameter below 0.8
- The AMD code predicts QP-QT separation times of approximately 50-150 fm/c depending on impact parameter
- For the most central considered events, excitation energy decreases by about 1 MeV/nucleon between t_DIC and 500 fm/c due to fast emissions
- The selected binary event sample (Z>=12) represents 62% of total statistics and 98% of events with reduced impact parameter b_red >= 0.4

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Coupland et al. (2011) who investigated mean-field and in-medium cross-section effects on the isospin transport ratio using the BUU model, but did not clarify the role of statistical de-excitation and fast emissions. The results directly inform the FAZIA collaboration's experimental program studying isospin equilibration in Ca+Ca systems, and demonstrate that the isospin transport ratio method requires careful treatment of emission effects when extracting the symmetry energy from experimental data. The findings also connect to the Evaporation Attractor Line concept introduced by Charity (1998), showing that linearity of the evaporation transformation depends on how close residues approach this line.
