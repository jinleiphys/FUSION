# A general numerical solution of dispersion relations for the nuclear optical model

> A general numerical solution of the dispersion integral relation between the real and the imaginary parts of the nuclear optical potential is presented. Fast convergence is achieved by means of the Gauss-Legendre integration method, which offers accuracy, easiness of implementation and generality for dispersive optical model calculations. The use of this numerical integration method in the optical-model parameter search codes allows for a fast and accurate dispersive analysis. PACS number(s): 11.55.Fv, 24.10.Ht, 02.60.Jh

## Key claim

The paper presents a Gauss-Legendre integration method (GLIM) as a fast, accurate, and general numerical alternative to both the slow Simpson integration method and the approximate analytical solutions previously derived for the dispersion integral in the nuclear optical model. The key condition is that the method works for any energy functional form of the imaginary part of the optical model potential, unlike the analytical approach which depends on the assumed functional form.

## Method

The authors replaced the Simpson integration method (SIM) with Gauss-Legendre integration (using N=10 points per interval) to solve the dispersion integral linking the real and imaginary parts of the nuclear optical potential. They divided the integration interval into two parts (from the Fermi energy up to an intermediate energy, then to a cut-off energy of 1000 MeV) and used a residual integral formula for the tail beyond the cut-off. The method was tested using the "partially constrained set" of dispersive optical model parameters for neutron scattering on 209Bi up to 80 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Maximum deviation between GLIM, SIM (100 keV step, more than 10000 integration points), and the analytical solution was less than 1 keV in the whole energy range from the Fermi energy up to 150 MeV for the surface dispersive correction.
- For the surface correction, the integration interval was divided at E_int = 10 MeV, with a cut-off energy E_cut = 1000 MeV.
- For the volume correction, the integration interval was divided at E_int = 40 MeV, with a cut-off energy E_cut = 1000 MeV.
- The GLIM used 20 integration points total, compared to 6000 steps (50 keV each) needed by the Simpson method to calculate the integral up to 300 MeV in prior work.
- The Fermi energy for 209Bi was taken as -5.98 MeV, with an offset energy of 3.26 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the dispersive optical model analysis developed by Mahaux and co-workers (Mahaux and Ngo, 1984; Mahaux and Sartor, 1991; Mahaux and Sartor, 1987) and directly addresses the slow convergence problem of the Simpson integration method reported by VanDerKam, Weisel and Tornow (2000), who derived approximate analytical solutions. The method enables fast and accurate dispersive analysis in optical-model parameter search codes for large-scale nuclear data calculations, and can treat any energy functional representation of the imaginary part of the optical model potential.
