# Can we observe effects of early nonequilibrium dynamics using collective flow

> The rapid expansion of the fireball created in a heavy-ion collision causes strong departures from local equilibrium. Such effects are especially important in the very early phase of the collision, bringing a substantial pressure asymmetry. We investigate effects of this early stage pressure asymmetry in a kinetic model without boost-invariance. In the kinetic evolution the pressure asymmetry results from the competition of the longitudinal expansion and the kinetic relaxation. Unlike the transverse flow, the directed flow is found to be very sensitive to the equilibration rate and the pressure anisotropy.

# Can we observe effects of early nonequilibrium dynamics using collective flow

## Key claim
Directed flow is very sensitive to the early-stage pressure asymmetry and equilibration rate in a kinetic model without boost-invariance, unlike transverse flow which is almost indistinguishable across different relaxation times. The abstract's claim that directed flow is "very sensitive" is conditional on the system lacking boost-invariance and on the kinetic evolution being limited to the very early stage (1 fm/c).

## Method
The author solves a kinetic equation in the relaxation-time approximation for massless particles, using a reduced phase-space distribution that depends on two spatial variables (one transverse dimension x and longitudinal space-time rapidity eta) and the particle momentum angles. The initial density is tilted in the x-eta plane to generate directed flow, and three calculations are compared: free-streaming evolution and two kinetic solutions with different relaxation times. The evolution is followed for 1 fm/c without switching to viscous hydrodynamics.

## Key numbers
- The ratio of longitudinal to transverse pressure at the fireball center never reaches the isotropic equilibrium limit during the evolution, even where density is largest.
- After 1 fm/c of kinetic evolution, the directed flow slope (rapidity dependence of average flow in the transverse x direction) shows a "very different rate" depending on the relaxation time, with free-streaming giving the smallest directed flow and faster relaxation (shorter relaxation time) giving larger directed flow.
- The transverse flow is "almost indistinguishable" among the three calculations (free-streaming and two relaxation times) despite very different pressure asymmetries.

## Context
This work extends the finding of Vredevoogd (2008) that transverse flow is insensitive to early pressure asymmetry, by showing that directed flow is not similarly insensitive. It builds on the kinetic description of early nonequilibrium dynamics from Florkowski (2013) and the tilted fireball model for directed flow generation from Bozek (2010). The result suggests that directed flow measurements could serve as an observable to probe nonequilibrium effects in the very early stage of relativistic heavy-ion collisions, complementing viscous hydrodynamic approaches (Niemi, 2014) that cannot describe far-from-equilibrium dynamics.
