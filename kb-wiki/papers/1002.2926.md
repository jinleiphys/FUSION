# How to increase the applicability of integral transform approaches in physics?

> Integral transform approaches are numerous in many fields of physics, but in most cases limited to the use of the Laplace kernel. However, it is well known that the inversion of the Laplace transform is very problematic, so that the function related to the physical observable is in most cases unaccessible. The great advantage of kernels of bell-shaped form has been demonstrated in few-body nuclear systems. In fact the use of the Lorentz kernel has allowed to overcome the stumbling block of the ab initio description of reactions to the full continuum of systems of more than three particles. The problem of finding kernels of similar form, applicable to many-body problems deserves particular attention. If this search were successful the integral transform approach might represent the only viable ab initio access to many observables that are not calculable directly.

## Key claim

The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method has successfully enabled ab initio calculations of reactions to the full continuum for few-body nuclear systems (up to seven particles), but extending it to many-body systems faces a fundamental computational bottleneck: the need to handle extremely large basis sets to obtain enough discrete eigenvalues within the width parameter σ_I for reliable inversion.

## Method

The authors analyze the LIT method, where a Lorentzian kernel K(σ_R, σ_I, E) = 1/[(E-σ_R)² + σ_I²] replaces the problematic Laplace kernel. The transform L(σ_R, σ_I) = ⟨Q|1/(Ĥ-E₀-σ_R+iσ_I) · 1/(Ĥ-E₀-σ_R-iσ_I)|Q'⟩ is computed by solving inhomogeneous equations (Ĥ - E₀ - σ_R - iσ_I)|Ψ̃⟩ = |Q⟩, which have localized solutions because σ_I ≠ 0. The transform is then expanded over N discrete basis states, yielding a sum of Lorentzians weighted by matrix elements, and the continuum response r(E) is recovered by numerical inversion of the transform.

## Key numbers

- The LIT method has been applied to reactions in systems of up to **seven** particles (Bacca 2002, Bacca 2004a).
- The Lorentz kernel width parameter σ_I is set to a finite value (e.g., **σ_I = 10** in the illustrative example) to control resolution; as σ_I → 0 the Lorentzian approaches a δ-function.
- For reliable inversion, one needs a sufficient number of discrete eigenvalues ϵ_ν^N within the interval [σ_R - σ_I, σ_R + σ_I] around each scan point σ_R.

## Context

This work extends the LIT method pioneered by Efros (1994) and reviewed by Efros (2007), which solved the longstanding problem of ab initio continuum reactions for few-body nuclei (up to A=7). The paper identifies the key obstacle to many-body applications: the exponential growth of basis size needed to achieve adequate eigenvalue density within the Lorentzian width. It suggests exploring Monte Carlo techniques for bell-shaped kernels other than the Laplace kernel, such as combinations of exponentials with a Heaviside function (Gatti 2009), which could enable Coupled Cluster or Monte Carlo approaches to compute the transform for larger systems.
