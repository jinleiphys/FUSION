# Resonant enhancement of oscillating electric field in atom

> When an atom is placed into an oscillating electric field with frequency far from atomic resonances, the atomic electrons partly shield this field at the nucleus. It is conjectured that when the frequency of electric field reaches an atomic resonance, the electric field at the nucleus may be significantly enhanced. In this paper, we systematically study the mechanisms of this enhancement and show that it may reach five orders in magnitude in particular cases. As an application, we consider laser-assisted neutron capture in 139-Lanthanum nucleus and screening and resonance enhancement of nuclear electromagnetic transitions by electrons.

## Key claim

When the frequency of an oscillating electric field is tuned to an atomic resonance, the electric field at the nucleus can be enhanced by up to five orders of magnitude relative to the applied field, but this enhancement is conditional on the field being weak enough that the Rabi frequency satisfies \(2\Omega^2 \ll \Gamma^2\) and on the absence of significant collisional or Doppler broadening.

## Method

The authors model the atom as a two-level system with a ground state \(|0\rangle\) and an excited state \(|1\rangle\) that decays spontaneously with rate \(\Gamma\). They solve the optical Bloch equations in the rotating-wave approximation to obtain the steady-state density matrix, then compute the expectation value of the electric field operator at the nucleus from the atomic electrons. The total field at the nucleus is the sum of the applied oscillating field and this induced electron field.

## Key numbers

- In xenon (\(Z=54\)), for a transition at \(\omega = 8.44\) eV with \(\Gamma \approx 2\times 10^{-7}\) eV and dipole matrix element \(\langle 1|z|0\rangle = -0.66\,a_{\rm B}\), the applied field amplitude needed to saturate the transition is \(E_0 \approx 40\ \text{V/cm}\), and the maximum induced field at the nucleus is \(E_{2,\text{max}} \approx 4.3\times 10^6\ \text{V/cm}\), giving an enhancement ratio \(E_{2,\text{max}}/E_0 \approx 10^5\).
- For the \(^{139}\)La nucleus (\(Z=57\)), using the excited state \(|4f6s^2\rangle\) at energy \(1.88\) eV with \(\Gamma \approx 6\times 10^{-9}\) eV and \(\langle 0|z|1\rangle = -1.3\,a_{\rm B}\), the saturation field is \(E_0 \approx 0.6\ \text{V/cm}\) and the maximum induced field is \(E_{2,\text{max}} \approx 4\times 10^5\ \text{V/cm}\), an enhancement of six orders of magnitude.
- Off resonance in lanthanum, the shielding factor for the applied field is \(\alpha_{zz}\,\omega^2 m_e/(e^2 Z) \approx 0.003\), reducing a field of \(8700\ \text{V/cm}\) to only about \(44\ \text{V/cm}\) at the nucleus.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier study of partial shielding of oscillating electric fields in atoms (Flambaum 2018) by treating the resonant case where the standard perturbative screening formula breaks down. It contradicts the expectation from the Schiff theorem that static electric fields are completely screened at the nucleus, showing that resonant oscillating fields can instead be strongly enhanced. The results enable a reinterpretation of laser-assisted neutron capture experiments in \(^{139}\)La (Lomonosov 2007, Dzublik 2012, and the experimental works La-exp1, La-exp2, La-exp3), where the observed lack of enhancement is attributed to off-resonant shielding, and suggest that tuning the laser to an atomic resonance could produce the predicted capture enhancement.
