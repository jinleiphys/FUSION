# Ideal Boson Particle-Antiparticle System at Finite Temperatures

> The thermodynamic properties of an ideal bosonic system composed of particles and antiparticles at finite temperatures are examined within the framework of a scalar field model. It is assumed that particle-antiparticle pair creation occurs; however, the system is simultaneously subject to exact charge (isospin) conservation. To implement this constraint, we first consider the system within the Grand Canonical Ensemble and then transform to the Canonical Ensemble using a Legendre transformation. This procedure provides a formally consistent scheme for incorporating the chemical potential at the microscopic level into the Canonical Ensemble framework. To enforce exact conservation of charge (isospin, N_I), we further analyze the thermodynamic properties of the system within the extended Canonical Ensemble, in which the chemical potential becomes a thermodynamic function of the temperature and conserved charge. It is shown that as the temperature decreases, the system undergoes a second-order phase transition to a Bose-Einstein condensate at the critical temperature T_c, but only when the conserved charge is finite, N_I=const \ne 0. In a particle-antiparticle system, the condensate forms exclusively in the component with the dominant particle number density, which determines the excess charge. We demonstrate that the symmetry breaking of the ground state at T=0 results from a first-order phase transition associated with the formation of a Bose-Einstein condensate. Although the transition involves symmetry breaking, it is not spontaneous in the strict field-theoretic sense, but is instead induced by the external injection of particles. Potential experimental signals of Bose-Einstein condensation of pions produced in high-energy nuclear collisions are briefly discussed.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in an ideal bosonic particle-antiparticle system with exact charge (isospin) conservation, a second-order phase transition to Bose-Einstein condensation occurs at a critical temperature \(T_c\) only when the conserved charge is finite (\(N_I = \text{const} \neq 0\)), and that the ground-state symmetry breaking at \(T=0\) results from a first-order phase transition which is not spontaneous in the strict field-theoretic sense but is instead induced by external injection of particles.

## Method

The authors start from a free complex scalar field Lagrangian and apply Bogolyubov's decomposition to separate the condensate field from thermal fluctuations. They first formulate the system within the Grand Canonical Ensemble, then perform a Legendre transformation to move to the Extended Canonical Ensemble, where the chemical potential becomes a thermodynamic function \(\mu_I(T,n_I)\) of temperature and conserved charge density. The thermodynamic properties are then analyzed using the statistical operator \(\rho(T,n_I) = \exp\left[-\frac{1}{T}\left(H - \mu_I(T,n_I) \hat N_I\right)\right]\), which allows exact charge conservation to be enforced at the microscopic level across different temperatures.

## Key numbers

- The Stefan-Boltzmann limit for the energy density at high temperatures is \(\lim_{T \to \infty} \varepsilon/T^4 = \pi^2/15\), which is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant for a two-component boson system.
- The critical temperature \(T_c\) for Bose-Einstein condensation is defined, with the condition that condensation occurs only when the conserved charge is finite, \(N_I = \text{const} \neq 0\).
- The condensate forms exclusively in the component with the dominant particle number density, which determines the excess charge.

## Context

This work extends the study of relativistic boson systems with charge conservation originally investigated by Haber and Weldon (1981, 1982), who derived expansions for thermodynamic quantities above the critical temperature and noted that the chemical potential is a dependent parameter. The paper also builds on the approach of Salasnich (2002), who demonstrated the method of moving from Grand Canonical to Canonical Ensemble via Legendre transformation. The results enable future investigation of Bose-Einstein condensation signals in multi-pion states produced in high-energy nuclear collisions, as previously explored by Begun et al. (2007, 2008, 2015) and Deb et al. (2021).
