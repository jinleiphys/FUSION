# Interdependence of different symmetry energy elements

> Relations between the nuclear symmetry energy coefficient and its density derivatives are derived. The relations hold for a class of interactions with quadratic momentum dependence and a power-law density dependence. The structural connection between the different symmetry energy elements as obtained seems to be followed by almost all reasonable nuclear energy density functionals, both relativistic and non-relativistic, suggesting a universality in the correlation structure. This, coupled with known values of some well-accepted constants related to nuclear matter, helps in constraining values of different density derivatives of the nuclear symmetry energy shedding light on the isovector part of the nuclear interaction.

# Interdependence of different symmetry energy elements

## Key claim

The authors derive analytic relations linking the nuclear symmetry energy coefficient and its density derivatives (slope L₀, curvature K_sym⁰, skewness Q_sym⁰) that hold for any energy density functional with quadratic momentum dependence and power-law density dependence, and demonstrate that these relations are followed by nearly all existing relativistic and non-relativistic functionals, suggesting a universal correlation structure. The hedge is that the relations are exact only within the chosen premises (mean-field level, zero temperature, specific interaction forms), and the claimed universality is an empirical observation from 500 functionals rather than a proven theorem.

## Method

Starting from the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem and the Gibbs-Duhem relation for asymmetric nuclear matter at zero temperature, the authors derive analytic expressions for the symmetry energy coefficient C₂(ρ) and its density derivatives K_sym⁰ and Q_sym⁰ at saturation density ρ₀, assuming a single-particle potential that separates into momentum-dependent and rearrangement terms with a power-law density dependence. They then test the predicted linear correlation between K_sym⁰ and the combination (3C₂⁰ − L₀) against 500 Skyrme and relativistic mean-field energy density functionals from the Dutra et al. compilations (2012, 2014), and use the derived relations together with empirical nuclear constants (ρ₀, e₀, K₀, C₂⁰, C₂¹, m₀*/m) to compute L₀, K_sym⁰, Q_sym⁰, and the neutron-proton effective mass splitting Δm₀*.

## Key numbers

- Correlation coefficient between K_sym⁰ and (3C₂⁰ − L₀) for 500 EDFs: r = −0.95
- Linear regression: K_sym⁰ = d₁[3C₂⁰ − L₀] + d₂, with d₁ = −4.97 ± 0.07 and d₂ = 66.80 ± 2.14 MeV
- Correlation coefficient between K_sym⁰ and L₀ alone: r = 0.87
- Correlation coefficient between Q_sym⁰ and (3C₂⁰ − L₀): r = 0.66
- Input nuclear constants used: ρ₀ = 0.155 ± 0.008 fm⁻³, e₀ = −16.0 ± 0.2 MeV, K₀ = 230 ± 40 MeV, C₂⁰ = 32.1 ± 0.3 MeV, C₂¹ = 24.1 ± 0.8 MeV, m₀*/m = 0.70 ± 0.05
- Calculated symmetry energy slope: L₀ = 60.3 ± 14.5 MeV
- Calculated curvature: K_sym⁰ = −111.8 ± 71.3 MeV
- Calculated skewness: Q_sym⁰ = 296.8 ± 73.6 MeV
- Calculated effective mass splitting: Δm₀* = (0.17 ± 0.24) δ
- Calculated L at crossing density ρ₁ = 0.1 fm⁻³: L(ρ₁) = 49.3 ± 4.2 MeV
- Calculated symmetry incompressibility: K_δ = −378.6 ± 17.0 MeV
- Q₀ (third derivative of SNM energy) calculated as −364.7 ± 27.7 MeV
- For 16 selected Skyrme models, root-mean-square deviations between predicted and model values: 1.3 MeV in L₀, 21.4 MeV in K_sym⁰, 32.5 MeV in Q_sym⁰

## Context

This work extends earlier efforts to find correlations among symmetry energy elements, particularly the linear relationship between K_sym⁰ and L₀ noted by Yoshida (2006), Danielewicz (2009), Chen (2009), Vidana (2009), Ducoin (2011), Dong (2012), and Santos (2014). The authors improve on these by showing that the fundamental correlation is between K_sym⁰ and the combination (3C₂⁰ − L₀) rather than L₀ alone, achieving a much stronger correlation (r = −0.95 versus r = 0.87). The work also builds on the use of the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem by Chen (2012) and the analytic framework for SNM by De (2015). The derived relations enable constraining the poorly known higher-order symmetry energy elements (K_sym⁰, Q_sym⁰, Δm₀*) using only well-established empirical constants, and the resulting L₀ value (60.3 ± 14.5 MeV) agrees with the global average of 58.9 ± 16 MeV from Li (2013). The calculated K_δ matches the experimental extraction from Sn-isotope breathing modes by Li (2007) as corrected by Pearson (2010). Future work requires more precise determinations of C₂⁰, C₂¹, and ρ₀ to narrow the remaining uncertainties in the density dependence of the symmetry energy.
