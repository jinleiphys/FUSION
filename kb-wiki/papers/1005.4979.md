# Azimuthal anisotropy: transition from hydrodynamic flow to jet suppression

> Measured 2nd and 4th azimuthal anisotropy coefficients v_{2,4}(N_{part}), p_T) are scaled with the initial eccentricity \varepsilon_{2,4}(N_{part}) of the collision zone and studied as a function of the number of participants N_{part} and the transverse momenta p_T. Scaling violations are observed for $p_T \alt 3$ GeV/c, consistent with a $p_T^2$ dependence of viscous corrections and a linear increase of the relaxation time with $p_T$. These empirical viscous corrections to flow and the thermal distribution function at freeze-out constrain estimates of the specific viscosity and the freeze-out temperature for two different models for the initial collision geometry. The apparent viscous corrections exhibit a sharp maximum for $p_T \agt 3$ GeV/c, suggesting a breakdown of the hydrodynamic ansatz and the onset of a change from flow-driven to suppression-driven anisotropy.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that eccentricity-scaled azimuthal anisotropy coefficients show scaling violations consistent with a quadratic increase of viscous corrections with transverse momentum for pT below 3 GeV/c, but exhibit a sharp maximum above 3 GeV/c that signals a breakdown of the hydrodynamic description and the onset of a transition from flow-driven to suppression-driven anisotropy. The authors hedge that the extracted specific viscosity and freeze-out temperature depend on which model for the initial collision geometry (MC-KLN or MC-Glauber) is used.

## Method
The authors analyzed high-precision PHENIX measurements of v2 and v4 for charged hadrons in Au+Au collisions at sqrt(sNN) = 200 GeV, scaling these coefficients by the initial eccentricities epsilon2 and epsilon4 calculated from Monte Carlo simulations of the collision geometry. They parametrized the observed scaling violations (deviations from flat Npart dependence) using a Knudsen number ansatz, fitting v2/epsilon2 and v4/epsilon4 versus Npart for each pT selection to extract the viscous correction parameter K*(pT). The pT dependence of K* was then used to extract the mean free path, specific shear viscosity, and freeze-out temperature for two different initial geometry models.

## Key numbers
- For MC-KLN geometry: K = 0.09 ± 0.01 (intercept), Tf = 162 ± 11 MeV (from slope of K* vs pT^2 fit)
- For MC-Glauber geometry: K = 0.17 ± 0.007, Tf = 173 ± 11 MeV
- For MC-KLN geometry: lambda ~ 0.17 ± 0.018 fm (essentially independent of Npart)
- For MC-Glauber geometry: lambda ~ 0.33 ± 0.02 fm (central collisions), with mild increase for peripheral collisions
- 4pi*eta/s = 1.1 ± 0.1 (MC-KLN geometry) and 4pi*eta/s = 2.1 ± 0.2 (MC-Glauber geometry)
- The linear dependence of K* on pT^2 holds for <pT>^2 < 10 (GeV/c)^2; trend inversion occurs for 10 < <pT>^2 < 40 (GeV/c)^2
- K0 constant estimated as 0.7 ± 0.03 from transport model
- Sound speed cs = 0.47 ± 0.03 c from lattice calculations; mean temperature T = 220 ± 20 MeV

## Context
This work extends prior efforts to extract the specific shear viscosity of the quark-gluon plasma from flow measurements, including comparisons to viscous relativistic hydrodynamic calculations by Romatschke (2007), Luzum (2008), Song (2008), and others. The method builds on the Knudsen number parametrization approach of Bhalerao (2005) and Drescher (2007). The results provide experimental constraints on the freeze-out temperature and the pT dependence of viscous corrections (delta f) that are commonly assumed in hydrodynamic simulations, and identify the pT range where the hydrodynamic description breaks down, enabling more detailed viscous hydrodynamic calculations with constrained input parameters.
