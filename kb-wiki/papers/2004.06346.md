# Scission configuration of $^{239}$U from yields and kinetic information of fission fragments

> The simultaneous measurement of the isotopic fission-fragment yields and fission-fragment velocities of $^{239}$U has been performed for the first time. The $^{239}$U fissioning system was produced in one-neutron transfer reactions between a $^{238}$U beam at 5.88 MeV/nucleon and a $^{9}$Be target. The combination of inverse kinematics at low energy and the use of the VAMOS++ spectrometer at the GANIL facility allows the isotopic identification of the full fission-fragment distribution and their velocity in the reference frame of the fissioning system. The proton and neutron content of the fragments at scission, their total kinetic and total excitation energy, as well as the neutron multiplicity were determined. Information from the scission point configuration is obtained from these observables and the correlation between them. The role of the octupole-deformed proton and neutron shells in the fission-fragment production is discussed.

# Scission configuration of $^{239}$U from yields and kinetic information of fission fragments

## Key claim

The simultaneous measurement of isotopic fission-fragment yields and velocities for $^{239}$U at a mean excitation energy of 8.3 MeV (with a standard deviation of 2.7 MeV) provides evidence that octupole-deformed proton and neutron shells, rather than spherical closed shells alone, drive the fragment production; however, the data are limited to asymmetric fission splits because the symmetric region lacks statistical significance.

## Method

A $^{238}$U beam at 5.88 MeV/nucleon impinged on a $^{9}$Be target, producing $^{239}$U via one-neutron transfer reactions. Fission fragments were identified in inverse kinematics using the VAMOS++ spectrometer at GANIL, with the fissioning system tagged by detecting $\alpha$-$\alpha$ coincidences from $^{8}$Be decay in the SPIDER silicon telescope. The isotopic identification of the full fission-fragment distribution and fragment velocities in the center-of-mass frame were obtained, from which pre-neutron-evaporation masses, neutron excess at scission, neutron multiplicity, total kinetic energy, and total excitation energy were derived via momentum conservation and Coulomb repulsion formulas.

## Key numbers

- Mean excitation energy of $^{239}$U: 8.3 MeV with a standard deviation of 2.7 MeV.
- Fission barrier of $^{239}$U: $B_f = 6.4$ MeV.
- Mass resolution of fragments: 0.8% for lighter fragments down to 0.5% for heavier ones; atomic number resolution: average below 1.3%.
- Total uncertainty in reconstructed center-of-mass velocity ($v_{c.m.}$): less than 1.3%.
- Mass-number error from pre-neutron-evaporation reconstruction: smaller than 0.4%.
- Mass number of the fissioning system: $A^{FIS} = 238.92$, from an average evaporation of 0.08 neutrons.
- Remaining contamination from other fissioning systems: estimated lower than 0.9%.
- Systematic uncertainties in mass yields: 2% in heavier fragments up to 10% in lighter ones.
- Scission configuration of $^{239}$U at $Z=50$ is approximately 1 fm more compact than that of $^{240}$Pu.

## Context

This work extends the experimental campaign in inverse kinematics at GANIL, following previous measurements on $^{240}$Pu by Caamano (2015) and Ramos (2018). The results provide simultaneous isotopic yields and kinetic data for $^{239}$U, enabling direct comparison with the GEF code (version 2019/1.1) and with evaluated data from ENDF/B-VIII.0. The findings confirm the role of octupole-deformed shells ($Z=52,56$ and $N=56,84,88$) as proposed by Scamps (2018), and show that the total excitation energy in asymmetric splits is nearly flat, with scission elongation determined primarily by the heavy fragment. This enables future constraints on microscopic fission models and supports the use of GEF for nuclear applications.
