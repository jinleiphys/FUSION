# Neutrino Spectrum from SN 1987A and from Cosmic Supernovae

> The detection of neutrinos from SN 1987A by the Kamiokande-II and Irvine-Michigan-Brookhaven detectors provided the first glimpse of core collapse in a supernova, complementing the optical observations and confirming our basic understanding of the mechanism behind the explosion. One long-standing puzzle is that, when fitted with thermal spectra, the two independent detections do not seem to agree with either each other or typical theoretical expectations. We assess the compatibility of the two data sets in a model-independent way and show that they can be reconciled if one avoids any bias on the neutrino spectrum stemming from theoretical conjecture. We reconstruct the neutrino spectrum from SN 1987A directly from the data through non-parametric inferential statistical methods and present predictions for the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background based on SN 1987A data. We show that this prediction cannot be too small (especially in the 10-18 MeV range), since the majority of the detected events from SN 1987 were above 18 MeV (including 6 events above 35 MeV), suggesting an imminent detection in operational and planned detectors.

## Key claim
The paper shows that the Kamiokande-II and Irvine-Michigan-Brookhaven data sets from SN 1987A can be reconciled without assuming a common thermal spectrum, provided one avoids theoretical bias on the neutrino spectrum shape; the combined data imply a Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background flux that is nearly as large as typical model predictions, especially in the 10–18 MeV range, suggesting imminent detection.

## Method
The authors used nonparametric (distribution-free) inferential statistical methods to reconstruct the effective \(\bar{\nu}_e\) spectrum directly from the detected positron energies, replacing each discrete event with a Gaussian of width \(1.5 \Delta E_i\) to smooth the spectrum. They then computed the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background flux by integrating this reconstructed spectrum over redshift, using a piecewise-linear star formation rate parametrization and a Baldry–Glazebrook initial mass function, and also employed a delta-function prescription to avoid smoothing artifacts.

## Key numbers
- The Kamiokande-II detector had \(N_t \simeq 1.43 \times 10^{32}\) free-proton targets; the Irvine-Michigan-Brookhaven detector had \(N_t \simeq 4.55 \times 10^{32}\) targets.
- Kamiokande-II had a threshold of \(E_{thr} \sim 7.5\) MeV; Irvine-Michigan-Brookhaven had a threshold of \(E_{thr} \sim 19\) MeV.
- 6 events from SN 1987A were detected above 35 MeV.
- The combined-data neutrino spectrum yields a time-integrated luminosity \(L_\nu \sim 6 \times 10^{52}\) erg and an average energy \(E_0 \sim 12\) MeV.
- The Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background event rate from the combined data in the 10–18 MeV range is 1.0–1.2 events per 22.5 kton per year (delta-function and smooth methods, respectively); in the 18–26 MeV range it is 0.4–0.5 events per 22.5 kton per year.
- The overall Poisson uncertainty on the 19 detected positrons is \(1/\sqrt{19} \sim 25\%\).

## Context
This work extends earlier attempts to reconcile the Kamiokande-II and Irvine-Michigan-Brookhaven data, such as those by Jegerlehner (1996) and Costantini (2006), by avoiding parametric spectral fits. It builds on the empirical Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background normalization approach of Fukugita and Kawasaki (2002) and Lunardini (2005), but uses a nonparametric reconstruction to avoid over-interpretation of limited data. The results enable more robust predictions for current detectors like Super-Kamiokande and for future gadolinium-enhanced detectors, suggesting that the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background is close to detection.
