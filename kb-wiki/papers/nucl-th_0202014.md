# Benchmark Calculation of the Three-Nucleon Photodisintegration

> A benchmark is set on the three-nucleon photodisintegration calculating the total cross section with modern realistic two- and three-nucleon forces (AV18, UrbIX) using both the Faddeev equations and the Lorentz Integral Transform method. This test shows that the precision of three-body calculations involving continuum states is considerably higher than experimental uncertainties. Effects due to retardations, higher multipoles, meson exchange currents and Coulomb force are studied.

## Key claim

The benchmark demonstrates that modern three-nucleon photodisintegration calculations using realistic two- and three-nucleon forces (AV18, UrbIX) achieve a precision "considerably higher than experimental uncertainties," but the agreement between the two methods (Faddeev and Lorentz Integral Transform) is slightly poorer in the peak region when the three-nucleon force is included, with a relative shift of 0.2 MeV.

## Method

The total photodisintegration cross section for three-nucleon systems (³H and ³He) was calculated using two independent theoretical approaches: the Faddeev equations (solved in momentum space) and the Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) method (solved in configuration space with correlated hyperspherical harmonics). Both methods employed the AV18 two-nucleon potential and the Urbana IX three-nucleon force, with the Faddeev approach additionally including retardation effects, higher multipoles, meson exchange currents (MEC), and the Coulomb force, while the LIT method was restricted to the unretarded dipole approximation.

## Key numbers

- The relative shift between Faddeev and LIT results in the peak region when the 3N force is included is 0.2 MeV.
- Retardation effects reduce the cross section by 1% or less below 50 MeV, by 5% at 100 MeV, and by 17% at pion threshold.
- Higher multipole contributions increase the cross section by up to 30% at pion threshold.
- Neglect of the Coulomb force increases the cross section by about 10% at 7 MeV, but becomes negligible beyond 15 MeV.
- The three-body breakup cross section becomes larger than the two-body breakup cross section at 14 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier approximate calculations with simple NN-potential models (BaP70, GiL76, Fang78, Vost81) and the first LIT calculations with semirealistic and realistic forces (ELO97, ELOT00, ELOT01). It provides the first quantitative discussion of retardation, higher multipoles, and meson exchange currents for three-body nuclei, and enables future high-precision experiments by establishing a benchmark that shows theoretical accuracy exceeds current experimental uncertainties.
