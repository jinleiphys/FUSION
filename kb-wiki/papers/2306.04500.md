# Structure Factors for Hot Neutron Matter from Ab Initio Lattice Simulations with High-Fidelity Chiral Interactions

> We present the first ab initio lattice calculations of spin and density correlations in hot neutron matter using high-fidelity interactions at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N3LO) in chiral effective field theory. These correlations have a large impact on neutrino heating and shock revival in core-collapse supernovae and are encapsulated in functions called structure factors. Unfortunately, calculations of structure factors using high-fidelity chiral interactions were well out of reach using existing computational methods. In this work, we solve the problem using a computational approach called the rank-one operator (RO) method. The RO method is a general technique with broad applications to simulations of fermionic many-body systems. It solves the problem of exponential scaling of computational effort when using perturbation theory for higher-body operators and higher-order corrections. Using the RO method, we compute the vector and axial static structure factors for hot neutron matter as a function of temperature and density. The ab initio lattice results are in good agreement with virial expansion calculations at low densities but are more reliable at higher densities. Random phase approximation codes used to estimate neutrino opacity in core-collapse supernovae simulations can now be calibrated with ab initio lattice calculations.

## Key claim

The authors present the first *ab initio* lattice calculations of vector and axial static structure factors for hot neutron matter using high-fidelity chiral interactions at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N3LO), enabled by a new rank-one operator (RO) method that avoids exponential scaling of computational effort in perturbation theory. The results are in good agreement with virial expansion calculations at low densities but are presented as more reliable at higher densities, where the virial expansion converges slowly.

## Method

The authors use nuclear lattice effective field theory (NLEFT) with auxiliary-field Monte Carlo simulations, employing the pinhole trace algorithm for finite-temperature calculations in the canonical ensemble. They bridge the gap between a simple interaction and the high-fidelity N3LO chiral interaction using first-order perturbation theory, accelerated by wave function matching, and compute the static structure factors via the rank-one operator method which inserts one-body operators without derivative-based exponential scaling. Grand canonical ensemble results are constructed from a series of canonical ensemble calculations using the Widom insertion method to determine chemical potentials.

## Key numbers

- Spatial lattice spacing: \(a = 1/(150 \text{ MeV}) \approx 1.32\) fm; temporal lattice spacing: \(a_t = 1/(1000 \text{ MeV})\)
- Simulations performed on \(L^3 = 6^3, 7^3, 8^3\) cubic lattices
- At \(T = 20\) MeV, deviations between lattice results and Virial4 [Unitary] results can be as large as \(\sim 25\%\) at density \(n \approx 0.053 \text{ fm}^{-3}\) and \(\sim 5\%\) at \(n \approx 0.015 \text{ fm}^{-3}\) for the axial structure factor \(S_{\rm{a}}\)
- At \(T = 10\) MeV, momentum-dependent structure factors are computed at density \(\rho_G = 0.018 \text{ fm}^{-3}\) (specifically \(\rho_G = 0.01758(4) \text{ fm}^{-3}\))
- Lattice cutoff momentum: \(\pi/a = 470\) MeV
- Overall systematic uncertainty estimated at the 5% level

## Context

This work extends prior *ab initio* lattice calculations of neutron matter structure factors that were limited to pionless effective field theory at leading order (Alexandru 2020, Alexandru 2021), which are suitable only for momenta below 100 MeV. The new results provide benchmarks for calibrating random phase approximation (RPA) codes used in core-collapse supernovae simulations, which previously relied on model-dependent Skyrme interactions such as NRAPR, SGII, SVmin, and UNEDF. The rank-one operator method is presented as a general technique applicable to Monte Carlo simulations of fermionic many-body systems in nuclear physics, condensed matter, ultracold atomic gases, and quantum chemistry.
