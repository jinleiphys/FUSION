# Fast Equilibration of Hadrons in an Expanding Fireball

> Due to long chemical equilibration times within standard hadronic reactions during the hadron gas phase in relativistic heavy ion collisions it has been suggested that the hadrons are "born" into equilibrium after the quark gluon plasma phase. Here we develop a dynamical scheme in which possible Hagedorn states contribute to fast chemical equilibration times of baryon anti-baryon pairs (as well as kaon anti-kaon pairs) inside a hadron gas and just below the critical temperature. Within this scheme, we use master equations and derive various analytical estimates for the chemical equilibration times. Applying a Bjorken picture to the expanding fireball, the kaons and baryons as well as the bath of pions and Hagedorn resonances can indeed quickly chemically equilibrate for both an initial overpopulation or underpopulation of Hagedorn resonances. Moreover, a comparison of our results to $(B+\bar{B})/π^{+}$ and $K/π^{+}$ ratios at RHIC, indeed, shows a close match.

# Fast Equilibration of Hadrons in an Expanding Fireball

## Key claim

Hagedorn states (heavy resonances with an exponential mass spectrum near the critical temperature) can drive baryon-antibaryon and kaon-antikaon pairs into chemical equilibrium within the hadron gas phase of relativistic heavy ion collisions, provided the system temperature remains near 170–180 MeV; the hadrons need not be "born" into equilibrium at the onset of the hadron gas phase, contrary to earlier suggestions.

## Method

The authors construct master rate equations for the abundances of pions, Hagedorn states, and baryon-antibaryon (or kaon-antikaon) pairs, using a truncated Hagedorn mass spectrum from 2 to 7 GeV with a Hagedorn temperature of 180 MeV. They apply a Bjorken expansion model to a cooling fireball, holding total entropy constant, and solve the coupled rate equations numerically for various initial fugacities of pions, Hagedorn states, and baryon/kaon pairs. Analytical estimates for equilibration times are derived by dividing the evolution into three stages: far-from-equilibrium pions, near-equilibrium pions, and quasi-equilibrium.

## Key numbers

- Chemical equilibration time for baryon-antibaryon pairs in a static environment at T = 170–180 MeV: τ_BB̅ = 0.2–0.7 fm/c
- Chemical equilibration time for kaon-antikaon pairs in a static environment at T = 170–180 MeV: τ_KK̅ = 0.1–0.3 fm/c
- Pion equilibration time when pions are far from equilibrium (λ_π ≈ 0): τ_π⁰ = 0.1–0.4 fm/c at T = 170–180 MeV
- Pion equilibration time when pions are near equilibrium (λ_π ≈ 1): τ_π = 0.02–0.06 fm/c at T = 170–180 MeV
- Quasi-equilibrium time scale for pions: τ^QE_π = 2.7–3.7 fm/c at T = 170–180 MeV
- Total equilibration time (including lightest resonance at 2 GeV): τ^tot = 3.5–4.5 fm/c at T = 170–180 MeV
- Baryons reach chemical equilibrium by T = 165 MeV, corresponding to t − t₀ ≈ 2–3 fm/c in the expanding fireball
- Kaons reach chemical equilibrium at T = 160–140 MeV
- Final (B+B̅)/π⁺ ratio ≈ 0.3, matching PHENIX and STAR data at √s = 200 GeV
- Final K/π⁺ ratio levels off between 0.16 and 0.17, matching PHENIX and STAR data
- Hagedorn state decay widths range from Γ_i = 250 to 1100 MeV, with baryon-antibaryon decay widths Γ_i,BB̅ = 15 to 400 MeV
- Average baryon number per Hagedorn decay: ⟨B⟩ = 0.06 to 0.4
- Average kaon number per Hagedorn decay: ⟨K⟩ = 0.4 to 0.5
- Initial fireball radius r₀(t₀) = 7.1 fm; average transverse velocities v₀ = 0.3c, 0.5c, 0.7c with corresponding accelerations a₀ = 0.035, 0.025, 0.015

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Greiner (2004) and Noronha-Hostler (2007) that proposed Hagedorn resonances as a mechanism for fast baryon production, and addresses the problem identified by Kapusta and by Huovinen (2003) that standard multi-mesonic reactions at RHIC temperatures give equilibration times (≈10 fm/c) longer than the hadronic fireball lifetime (<5 fm/c). The dynamical Bjorken expansion model with Hagedorn states resolves the discrepancy that led Braun-Munzinger and others to suggest hadrons are "born" into equilibrium at the phase transition. The results enable future work incorporating strange baryonic degrees of freedom and systematic studies of initial condition dependence.
