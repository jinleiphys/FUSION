# Identified Particle Spectra and Anisotropic Flow in an Event-by-Event Hybrid Approach in Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV

> The first results from heavy ion collisions at the Large Hadron Collider for charged particle spectra and elliptic flow are compared to an event-by-event hybrid approach with an ideal hydrodynamic expansion. This approach has been shown to successfully describe bulk observables at RHIC. Without changing any parameters of the calculation the same approach is applied to Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV. This is an important test if the established understanding of the dynamics of relativistic heavy ion collisions is also applicable at even higher energies. Specifically, we employ the hybrid approach with two different equations of state and the pure hadronic transport approach to indicate sensitivities to finite viscosity. The centrality dependence of the charged hadron multiplicity, $p_T$ spectra and differential elliptic flow are shown to be in reasonable agreement with the ALICE data. Furthermore, we make predictions for the transverse mass spectra of identified particles and triangular flow. The eccentricities and their fluctuations are found to be surprisingly similar to the ones at lower energies and therefore also the triangular flow results are very similar. Any deviations from these predictions will indicate the need for new physics mechanisms responsible for the dynamics of heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim
The event-by-event hybrid approach with ideal hydrodynamics, using the same parameters as for RHIC Au+Au collisions, yields a reasonably good description of ALICE data for Pb+Pb collisions at √sNN = 2.76 TeV, but the author notes that any deviations from the predictions for identified particle spectra and triangular flow "will indicate the need for new physics mechanisms responsible for the dynamics of heavy ion collisions."

## Method
The author employed the UrQMD transport approach for the initial non-equilibrium stage, with string excitation and fragmentation for particle production and PYTHIA for hard collisions (momentum transfer Q > 1.5 GeV). At a starting time of t_start = 0.5 fm, particle distributions were converted to energy, momentum, and net baryon density using three-dimensional Gaussian distributions (width σ = 1 fm), followed by an ideal (3+1)-dimensional relativistic one-fluid hydrodynamic evolution using two different equations of state (hadron gas and deconfinement cross-over). The transition back to hadronic transport occurred on a constant proper time hypersurface via the Cooper-Frye equation on transverse slices of thickness Δz = 0.1–0.2 fm below an energy density of 5 ε0 ≈ 730 MeV/fm³.

## Key numbers
- Charged particle multiplicity at midrapidity (|η| < 0.5) as a function of number of participants: the UrQMD transport predictions for central collisions are "right on top of the ALICE data," while with decreasing centrality the charged particle number is "a little lower than in the data."
- Transverse momentum spectra: the hybrid approach describes data well for pT < 3 GeV; in the range 4–6 GeV the UrQMD calculation provides a better description.
- Elliptic flow v2: good agreement between hybrid calculations and ALICE data, especially between pT = 0.8–2.5 GeV; at very low pT the hybrid approach underpredicts the data.
- Initial eccentricity ε2 and triangularity ε3 probability distributions for Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV "match almost exactly" the distributions for Au+Au at √sNN = 200 GeV for similar centrality classes.
- Triangular flow v3: for impact parameters up to b = 5 fm, the pure UrQMD transport result is "consistent with zero."

## Context
This work extends the event-by-event hybrid approach previously developed for SPS energies (Petersen 2008) and successfully applied to Au+Au collisions at RHIC (Petersen 2010) to the higher energy LHC collisions without parameter tuning. The approach builds on earlier hybrid models by Bass (2000), Teaney (2001), Hirano (2005), and Nonaka (2006) that coupled hydrodynamics to hadronic transport. The predictions for triangular flow and identified particle spectra enable experimental tests of whether the quark-gluon plasma remains an almost perfect liquid at LHC energies, with the pure UrQMD comparison providing a qualitative estimate of viscous effects.
