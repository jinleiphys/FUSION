# Q-alpha values in superheavy nuclei from the deformed Woods-Saxon model

> Masses of superheavy (SH) nuclei with $Z=98-128$, including odd and odd-odd nuclei, are systematically calculated within the microscopic-macroscopic model based on the deformed Woods-Saxon potential. Ground states are found by minimizing energy over deformations and configurations. Pairing in odd particle-number systems is treated either by blocking or by adding the BCS energy of the odd quasiparticle. Three new parameters are introduced which may be interpreted as the constant mean pairing energies for even-odd, odd-even and odd-odd nuclei. They are adjusted by a fit to masses of heavy nuclei. Other parameters of the model, fixed previously by fitting masses of even-even heavy nuclei, are kept unchanged. With this adjustment, the masses of SH nuclei are predicted and then used to calculate $α$-decay energies to be compared to known measured values. It turns out that the agreement between calculated $Q_α$ values with data in SH nuclei is better than in the region of the mass fit. The model overestimates $Q_α$ for $Z=111-113$. Ground state (g.s.) configurations in some SH nuclei hint to a possible $α$-decay hindrance. The calculated configuration-preserving transition energies show that in some cases this might explain discrepancies, but more data is needed to explain the situation.

## Key claim

The deformed Woods-Saxon model, extended to odd and odd-odd nuclei by introducing three constant mean pairing energy parameters fitted to heavy nuclei masses, predicts Q-alpha values for superheavy nuclei that agree with experimental data better than in the region of the original mass fit, though the model systematically overestimates Q-alpha for elements Z=111-113 and configuration hindrance may explain some remaining discrepancies.

## Method

The authors performed systematic calculations of ground-state masses for 1364 superheavy nuclei with Z=98-128 using a microscopic-macroscopic model based on the deformed Woods-Saxon potential, minimizing energy over axially-symmetric deformations (beta2, beta4, beta6, beta8) and configurations. Pairing in odd-particle-number systems was treated either by blocking or by adding the BCS energy of the odd quasiparticle, with three new constant parameters (interpreted as mean pairing energies for even-odd, odd-even, and odd-odd nuclei) adjusted by a fit to masses of heavy nuclei with Z>=82 and N>126 from the 2003 atomic mass evaluation. Q-alpha values were then calculated from the predicted masses and compared to experimental data, including configuration-preserving transition energies to assess possible alpha-decay hindrance.

## Key numbers

- RMS deviation for even-even nuclei in the blocking model: 0.284 MeV (74 nuclei)
- RMS deviation for odd-odd nuclei in the blocking model: 0.666 MeV (53 nuclei)
- RMS deviation for even-odd nuclei in the blocking model: 0.435 MeV (69 nuclei)
- RMS deviation for odd-even nuclei in the blocking model: 0.425 MeV (56 nuclei)
- RMS deviation for even-even nuclei in the quasiparticle method: 0.251 MeV (74 nuclei)
- RMS deviation for odd-odd nuclei in the quasiparticle method: 0.366 MeV (53 nuclei)
- Average deviation of Q-alpha values for 204 nuclei in the fit region: 326 keV for blocking method, 225 keV for quasiparticle method
- RMS deviation of Q-alpha values for 204 nuclei in the fit region: 426 keV for blocking method, 305 keV for quasiparticle method
- For 225U, including octupole deformations increased the calculated Q-alpha by about 0.8 MeV, giving 8.2 MeV compared to the measured 8.00 MeV
- Energy shift h for odd-odd nuclei in blocking method: 1.703 MeV, corresponding to a neutron-proton energy of 134 keV

## Context

This work extends the microscopic-macroscopic Woods-Saxon model, previously applied mainly to even-even nuclei (Jachimowicz, Kowal, Skalski, earlier works on fission barriers and ground states), to odd and odd-odd superheavy nuclei. It improves upon earlier Woods-Saxon calculations for odd superheavy nuclei (Lojewski, Baran; Cwiok and collaborators; Parkhomenko and Sobiczewski) by using a different macroscopic energy, more extensive deformation searches, and systematic blocking. The results enable more reliable predictions of alpha-decay energies for the heaviest elements, which are essential for identifying new elements and understanding nuclear structure near the predicted shell closures at N=184 and deformed subshells at N=152 and N=162.
