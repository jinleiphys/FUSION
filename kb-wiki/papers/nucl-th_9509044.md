# Mesonic Contribution to the Compton Scattering Amplitude for Heavy Nuclei

> The contribution of mesonic exchange currents to nuclear Compton scattering is investigated within the framework of a Fermi gas model of nuclear matter in the non-relativistic limit. The additional interaction between the nucleons is accounted for by including two- and three-body diagrams. As a test of this model, the enhancement constant $κ$ is calculated. The full correlators for the central and tensor part of the nucleon-nucleon interaction due to pion exchange are obtained and the energy dependence of the amplitude is investigated. The contribution of the $Δ$-excitation to the mesonic part of the Compton amplitude is calculated explicitely using an effective Hamiltonian in the static limit.

# Mesonic Contribution to the Compton Scattering Amplitude for Heavy Nuclei

## Key claim

The mesonic contribution to the nuclear Compton scattering amplitude for heavy nuclei is calculated using a Fermi gas model with perturbative corrections from pion and rho-meson exchange, finding that three-body correlations are as important as two-body correlations and that the tensor part of the nuclear correlation function has a dominant effect on the enhancement constant kappa. The results are restricted to photon energies below approximately 150 MeV due to the use of the static (nonrelativistic) approximation for the meson-nucleon interaction.

## Method

The authors start from a Fermi gas model of nuclear matter and calculate corrections to the nuclear wave function using second-order perturbation theory with respect to pion and rho-meson exchange. They evaluate two-body and three-body diagrams to obtain the full central and tensor parts of the nuclear correlation function, which enters the mesonic seagull amplitude. The contribution from virtual Delta-isobar excitation is also calculated using an effective Hamiltonian in the static limit, and the enhancement constant kappa is computed as a test of the model.

## Key numbers

- Enhancement constant kappa = 1.01, obtained from the sum of central contribution kappa_C = 0.15 and tensor contribution kappa_T = 0.86, with an estimated accuracy of 20 per cent; using only the lowest-order central correlator gives kappa_0 = 0.2
- Modified nucleon electromagnetic polarizabilities (in units of zeta = e^2/(4Mm^2) = 7.5 x 10^-4 fm^3): bar_alpha = -3.23 x 10^-4 fm^3, bar_beta = 1.73 x 10^-4 fm^3; using only the lowest-order central correlator gives bar_alpha_0 = -4.5 x 10^-4 fm^3 and bar_beta_0 = 1.95 x 10^-4 fm^3
- Sum bar_alpha + bar_beta = -1.5 x 10^-4 fm^3, giving a correction of order 10 per cent to the free nucleon value; difference bar_beta - bar_alpha = 5 x 10^-4 fm^3
- Delta-isobar contribution to magnetic polarizability: bar_beta_Delta = 0.7 x 10^-4 fm^3
- Fermi momenta: p_F^(p) = 2.27 m (Z/A)^(1/3), p_F^(n) = 2.27 m (N/A)^(1/3), with p_F = 1.8 m for N=Z; nuclear radius R = 1.2 A^(1/3) fm
- The low-energy form of the amplitude reproduces the exact energy dependence up to 100 MeV

## Context

This work extends the approach of Ericson and collaborators (Ericson, 1980s) who used a Fermi gas model for forward-direction Compton scattering and showed that nuclear degrees of freedom can be described by a correlation function. The authors provide analytical expressions for both central and tensor correlators, going beyond earlier work that used numerical nuclear matter calculations (Ericson, 1980s) or variational methods (Fantoni, 1980s). The results enable microscopic calculation of the non-resonant part of the Compton amplitude, which is needed to resolve discrepancies between phenomenological approaches (Martin, 1970s; Ziegler, 1980s) and recent experimental data on helium and carbon (Lund, 1990s). The calculation of the enhancement constant kappa can be compared with experimental photoabsorption data, and the modified polarizabilities provide input for analyzing Compton scattering experiments at intermediate energies.
