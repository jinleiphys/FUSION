# Neutron-Star-Merger Equation of State

> In this work, we discuss the dense matter equation of state (EOS) for the extreme range of conditions encountered in neutron stars and their mergers. The calculation of the properties of such an EOS involves modeling different degrees of freedom (such as nuclei, nucleons, hyperons, and quarks), taking into account different symmetries, and including finite density and temperature effects in a thermodynamically consistent manner. We begin by addressing subnuclear matter consisting of nucleons and a small admixture of light nuclei in the context of the excluded volume approach. We then turn our attention to supranuclear homogeneous matter as described by the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) formalism. Finally, we present results from realistic neutron-star-merger simulations performed using the CMF model that predict signatures for deconfinement to quark matter in gravitational wave signals.

## Key claim
The paper presents a unified, thermodynamically consistent equation of state (EOS) for neutron star mergers that models matter from subnuclear densities (including light nuclei) to supranuclear densities (including hyperons and quarks) using the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) formalism, and it predicts that the deconfinement to quark matter during a merger produces observable signatures in gravitational wave signals.

## Method
The authors construct the EOS in two stages. First, subnuclear matter (nucleons and a small admixture of light nuclei) is treated using an excluded volume approach. Second, supranuclear homogeneous matter is described by the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model, which self-consistently includes nucleons, hyperons, and quarks. The resulting EOS is then used as input for realistic neutron-star-merger simulations to search for gravitational wave signatures of quark deconfinement.

## Context
This work extends the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) approach, previously applied to cold neutron stars, to the finite-temperature, out-of-equilibrium conditions of neutron star mergers. It builds on prior studies of dense matter EOS that include hyperons (e.g., work by Schramm and others) and quark deconfinement. The results enable future gravitational wave observatories (e.g., LIGO and Virgo) to potentially identify the onset of quark matter in post-merger remnants, linking nuclear physics to multi-messenger astronomy.
