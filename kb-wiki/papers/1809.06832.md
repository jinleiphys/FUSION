# Final-state interactions of the Higgs boson in quark-gluon matter

> In the first version of this paper \cite{dEnterria:2018bqi}, we presented a study of the final-state interactions of the Higgs boson in the hot and dense quark-gluon systems produced in pp, pPb, and PbPb collisions at CERN LHC and FCC energies. By computing the leading-order diagrams of the Higgs-parton scattering cross sections in perturbative QCD, and by embedding the produced Higgs bosons in an expanding quark-gluon medium modeled with 2D+1 viscous hydrodynamics with various QCD equations of state, we presented estimates of the expected scalar boson yields as functions of transverse momentum $p_{\rm T}^{H}$, and produced medium space-time size. A moderate suppression of the scalar boson yields was predicted due to medium-enhanced $H\to gg,q\bar{q}$ decays, in detriment of the $H\toγγ, 4\ell$ channels that are typically used to observe the Higgs particle. After our work appeared, J. Ghiglieri and U. Wiedemann \cite{Ghiglieri:2019lzz} have presented thermal-field-theory calculations that indicate that the $H\to gg,q\bar{q}$ partial decays widths remain basically unaffected by interactions with surrounding partons in the kinematic range of relevance of our study. Such a theoretical result, in contradiction with our estimates, has brought us to revisit our calculations and to realize of the quantitative importance of thermal virtual corrections, neglected in our first work, that are as large as the real ones and of opposite sign. Such virtual corrections significantly reduce the Higgs-parton "absorption" cross sections originally computed in Ref. \cite{dEnterria:2018bqi}, and make the Higgs boson suppression negligible in the kinematic regime considered.

## Key claim

The authors' original claim of moderate Higgs boson suppression in quark-gluon plasma due to medium-enhanced decays is retracted: thermal virtual corrections, neglected in their first work, are as large as the real absorption and emission terms and of opposite sign, making the net medium impact on Higgs partonic partial widths negligible for LHC and FCC temperatures.

## Method

The authors computed leading-order Higgs-parton scattering cross sections using CalcHep and MadGraph5 perturbative QCD calculators with an extra K=3 factor for missing higher-order corrections, and embedded the produced Higgs bosons in an expanding quark-gluon medium modeled with 2D+1 viscous hydrodynamics with various QCD equations of state. After Ghiglieri and Wiedemann (2019) presented thermal-field-theory calculations using an operator product expansion approach that included real emission, real absorption, and thermal virtual contributions, the authors revisited their work and recognized that the virtual corrections cancel the real contributions. The final result follows a (T/m_H)^4 dependence from the OPE approach, valid for T less than m_H/10.

## Key numbers

- Higgs mass m_H = 125 GeV, compared to typical QGP temperatures T less than or about 1 GeV at LHC and FCC
- Relative thermal correction to digluon and diquark partial widths: delta-Gamma_{gg,qqbar}/Gamma_{gg,qqbar} approximately kappa * alpha_s * (T/m_H)^4, with prefactor |kappa| approximately 30 to 400 depending on parton flavor
- Resulting medium impact on Higgs partonic partial widths: delta-Gamma_{gg,qqbar}/Gamma_{gg,qqbar} approximately 10^{-6} to 10^{-8}
- Original Higgs-parton absorption cross sections estimated at about 1 to 50 microbarns in the kinematic range of interest

## Context

This paper corrects the authors' earlier work (d'Enterria and Loizides, 2018) which had predicted moderate Higgs suppression in quark-gluon plasma, contradicting the thermal-field-theory calculations of Ghiglieri and Wiedemann (2019) that showed negligible medium effects. The corrected result aligns with the OPE-based thermal width calculations and resolves the discrepancy by including previously neglected virtual corrections. This work enables future studies to focus on Higgs production in heavy-ion collisions without expecting significant medium modifications at currently accessible temperatures, though the authors note that modifications could become significant near the electroweak phase transition temperature.
