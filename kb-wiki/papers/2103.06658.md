# A cornucopia of antineutrons and hyperons from super J/psi factory for next-generation nuclear and particle physics high-precision experiments

> In order to study the interactions and structure of various types of matter one typically needs to carry out scattering experiments utilizing many different particles as projectiles. Whereas beams of $e^\pm$, $μ^\pm$, $π^\pm$, $K^\pm$, proton, antiproton, and of various heavy ions have been produced and have enabled many scientific breakthroughs, beams of antineutrons, hyperons ($Λ$, $Σ$ and $Ξ$) and their antiparticles are typically not easy to obtain. Here we point out and investigate a new high-quality source of these particles: a super $J/ψ$ factory with capability of accumulating trillions of $J/ψ$ decays each year. In the relevant $J/ψ$ decays the desired particle is produced together with other final state particles that can be tagged. This allows accurate determination of the flux and momentum of the projectile, enabling unprecedented precision-study of the corresponding interactions with a broad range of targets. These novel high-statistics sources of baryons and antibaryons with precisely known kinematics open fresh opportunities for applications in particle and nuclear physics, including antinucleon-nucleon interaction, nonvalence $s\bar{s}$ component of the nucleon, (anti)hyperon-nucleon interaction, OZI violation, (multi-strange) hypernuclei, exotic light hadron spectroscopy and many others, as well as calibration of Monte Carlo simulation for hadronic and medical physics.

## Key claim

The authors propose that a super J/ψ factory, capable of accumulating trillions of J/ψ decays per year, can serve as a high-quality source of antineutrons and hyperons (Λ, Σ, Ξ) and their antiparticles, with the crucial qualifier that the momentum and direction of these particles can be determined precisely by tagging the recoiling particles in the J/ψ decay, enabling precision studies that were previously impractical.

## Method

The authors analyze the production of antineutrons via the decay J/ψ → p π⁻ n̅ and hyperons via decays such as J/ψ → Λ Λ̅, using the existing BESIII experiment with 10 billion J/ψ events as a case study. They calculate the number of tagged particles that can reach the beam pipe material (beryllium and carbon) by considering branching fractions, tagging efficiencies, and the decay lengths (cτ) of the hyperons. They then extrapolate to a future super J/ψ factory with 100 times higher luminosity, discussing how detector modifications and custom targets could expand the physics reach.

## Key numbers

- The branching fraction for J/ψ → p π⁻ n̅ is (2.12 ± 0.09) × 10⁻³.
- With 10 billion J/ψ events and ~40% tagging efficiency, 8 million antineutrons can be tagged.
- The maximum momentum of the antineutron from J/ψ → p π⁻ n̅ is 1174 MeV/c; the minimum momentum is zero.
- The antineutron momentum uncertainty at BESIII is about 6–7 MeV/c over the full momentum range.
- The branching fraction for J/ψ → Λ Λ̅ is 1.89 × 10⁻³, yielding 2.6 × 10⁶ Λ̅ particles reaching the beam pipe.
- The branching fraction for J/ψ → Σ⁺ Σ̅⁻ is 1.50 × 10⁻³, yielding 4 × 10⁵ Σ̅⁻ particles reaching the beam pipe.
- The branching fraction for J/ψ → Ξ⁰ Ξ̅⁰ is 1.17 × 10⁻³, yielding 7 × 10⁵ Ξ̅⁰ particles reaching the beam pipe.
- The branching fraction for J/ψ → Ξ⁻ Ξ̅⁺ is 0.97 × 10⁻³, yielding 3 × 10⁵ Ξ̅⁺ particles reaching the beam pipe.
- The branching fraction for ψ(2S) → Ω⁻ Ω̅⁺ is 5.2 × 10⁻⁵, yielding 5 × 10³ Ω̅⁺ particles reaching the beam pipe.
- The production cross section of J/ψ in e⁺e⁻ annihilation at BEPCII is 3500 nb with an energy spread of 0.9 MeV.
- A future super J/ψ factory could accumulate 10¹² J/ψ events in one year, 100 times more than the BESIII dataset.

## Context

This work extends the limited experimental studies of antineutron interactions, previously performed at BNL E-767 (momentum 100–500 MeV/c) and CERN OBELIX (momentum 50–400 MeV/c), by proposing a new source with higher statistics and a wider momentum range, including antineutrons above 500 MeV/c which are unique. It also addresses the long-standing difficulty of producing hyperon and antihyperon beams, which have been studied since the 1960s using π± or K± beams with only tens to hundreds of events per experiment (Engelmann 1966, Sechi-Zorn 1969, Alexander 1969, Eisele 1971, Kadyk 1971, Hauptman 1977, Ahn 1997, Aoki 1998, Kondo 2000, Ahn 2005). The proposed method enables simultaneous studies of antinucleon-nucleon interaction, hyperon-nucleon interaction, OZI violation, (multi-strange) hypernuclei, and exotic hadron spectroscopy, as well as calibration of Monte Carlo simulation codes like FLUKA and GEANT4 for hadronic and medical physics.
