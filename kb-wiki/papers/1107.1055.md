# The Quest for the Heaviest Uranium Isotope

> We study Uranium isotopes and surrounding elements at very large neutron number excess. Relativistic mean field and Skyrme-type approaches with different parametrizations are used in the study. Most models show clear indications for isotopes that are stable with respect to neutron emission far beyond N=184 up to the range of around N=258.

# The Quest for the Heaviest Uranium Isotope

## Key claim

Most self-consistent nuclear models (relativistic mean-field and Skyrme-type) show indications for uranium isotopes that are stable with respect to neutron emission far beyond the previously expected neutron number N=184, extending up to around N=258, but the exact location of the neutron drip line depends strongly on the chosen model parametrization and whether deformation is included.

## Method

The authors performed relativistic mean-field (RMF) calculations with the NL-Z2 parametrization and non-relativistic Hartree-Fock Skyrme-type calculations with SkM*, SkI4, and SLy6 forces, as well as a chiral effective Lagrangian model. Calculations were done in spherical symmetry (one-dimensional spatial grid) and with axially symmetric deformation (two-dimensional spatial grid), with BCS-type pairing using delta force pairing for grid calculations and constant force for harmonic oscillator basis calculations.

## Key numbers

- The heaviest neutron-stable uranium isotope predicted by the relativistic NL-Z2 model is **N=258** (²⁵⁸U) in both spherical and deformed calculations, with a binding energy of **2021.34 MeV** in the 2d calculation.
- Skyrme forces give varying results for the heaviest uranium isotope: SkM* gives **N=220** (2d), SkI4 gives **N=218** (2d), and SLy6 gives **N=206** (2d).
- The neutron skin thickness for ³⁵⁰U (NL-Z2) is **1.08 fm**, computed as r_n = 7.72 fm minus r_p = 6.64 fm.
- The isospin asymmetry (N-Z)/(N+Z) for ³⁵⁰U is approximately **0.48**.
- For elements from Z=92 (uranium) to Z=104 (rutherfordium), the NL-Z2 model predicts the most bound isotope at **N=258** for all Z values in this range.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the neutron drip line by Dobaczewski (1984, 1994, 1995), Toki (1991), Hirata (1991), and others, and specifically goes beyond the calculation by Hilaire (2007) which found particle-stable uranium isotopes only up to N=204. The results support the concept of "peninsulae" of stable isotopes extending beyond the smooth drip line, as previously suggested by Gridnev (2005, 2006) and Tarasov (2008, 2010). The findings enable future correlation of these extreme neutron-rich states with neutron star observables and crust properties, though the authors note that experimental production of such nuclei remains a distant prospect.
