# Nuclear Forces from Lattice Quantum Chromodynamics

> A century of coherent experimental and theoretical investigations have uncovered the laws of nature that underly nuclear physics. The standard model of strong and electroweak interactions, with its modest number of input parameters, dictates the dynamics of the quarks and gluons - the underlying building blocks of protons, neutrons, and nuclei. While the analytic techniques of quantum field theory have played a key role in understanding the dynamics of matter in high energy processes, they encounter difficulties when applied to low-energy nuclear structure and reactions, and dense systems. Expected increases in computational resources into the exa-scale during the next decade will provide the ability to numerically compute a range of important strong interaction processes directly from QCD with quantifiable uncertainties using the technique of Lattice QCD. These calculations will refine the chiral nuclear forces that are used as input into nuclear many-body calculations, including the three- and four-nucleon interactions. I discuss the state-of-the-art Lattice QCD calculations of quantities of interest in nuclear physics, progress that is expected in the near future, and the impact upon nuclear physics.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD calculations, currently performed at unphysically heavy light-quark masses (m_pi ~ 400-800 MeV), will within the next five years postdict or predict the spectrum and interactions of the lightest nuclei and hypernuclei with fully-quantified uncertainties, contingent on expected increases in computational resources to exascale.

## Method

The author reviews the state of Lattice QCD calculations relevant to nuclear physics, including the production of gauge-field ensembles using Hybrid Monte Carlo on leadership-class supercomputers, the computation of quark propagators via iterative inversion algorithms, and the construction of correlation functions for multi-baryon systems using recently developed contraction algorithms that reduce the factorial scaling of Wick contractions. The review covers calculations of hadron spectra, meson-meson scattering, nucleon-nucleon scattering lengths and effective ranges, and ground-state binding energies of light nuclei and hypernuclei, all performed at multiple lattice spacings and volumes with extrapolations using chiral effective field theory.

## Key numbers

- m_pi+ a_pipi^{I=2} = -0.04330 ± 0.00042 from mixed-action n_f=2+1 LQCD calculations at the physical pion mass (Beane 2007)
- m_pi+ a_pipi^{I=2} = -0.04385 ± 0.00028 ± 0.00038 from n_f=2 LQCD calculations extrapolated to the physical pion mass (ETM collaboration, Feng 2009)
- m_pi a^{(3S1)} = 7.45^{+0.57}_{-0.53}{}^{+0.71}_{-0.49} and m_pi r^{(3S1)} = 3.71^{+0.28}_{-0.31}{}^{+0.28}_{-0.35} for the nucleon-nucleon scattering length and effective range in the triplet S-wave channel at the SU(3) symmetric point (m_pi ~ 800 MeV)
- a^{(3S1)} = 1.82^{+0.14}_{-0.13}{}^{+0.17}_{-0.12} fm and r^{(3S1)} = 0.906^{+0.068}_{-0.075}{}^{+0.068}_{-0.084} fm for the same quantities converted to physical units
- The ratio of scattering length to effective range in the triplet S-wave channel is ~2 at the SU(3) symmetric point, compared to ~3 at physical quark masses
- LQCD calculations of the I=2 pion-pion scattering length at the physical pion mass agree with the Roy equation determination of m_pi+ a_pipi^{I=2} = -0.0444 ± 0.0010 (Leutwyler 2008)
- LQCD calculations of the rho-resonance were performed at m_pi ~ 390 MeV (Hadron Spectrum Collaboration, Dudek 2012)
- LQCD calculations of light nuclei binding energies were performed at m_pi ~ 800 MeV and m_pi ~ 400 MeV (NPLQCD, Yamazaki 2012)
- The dineutron is bound at m_pi ~ 800 MeV but becomes unbound at some intermediate pion mass

## Context

This review extends the program of using Lattice QCD to compute nuclear forces and light nuclei properties, building on the chiral effective field theory framework pioneered by Weinberg (1990, 1991, 1992) and the KSW expansion of Kaplan, Savage, and Wise (1998). The work connects to nuclear structure calculations using the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) and Green's Function Monte Carlo (GFMC) methods, as demonstrated by Maris (2012) for 7Li and 8B. The review enables a pathway for LQCD calculations to constrain chiral nuclear forces beyond what is possible with experiment, particularly for three- and four-nucleon interactions and for the light-quark mass dependence of nuclear observables.
