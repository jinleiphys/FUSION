# Lattice extraction of the Collins-Soper kernel using the auxiliary field representation of the Wilson line

> The Collins-Soper (CS) kernel may be obtained through the TMD soft function by formulating the Wilson line in terms of 1-dimensional auxiliary fermion fields on the lattice. Our computation takes place in the region of the lattice that corresponds to the "spacelike" region in Minkowski space, i.e., Collins' scheme. We explore two methods for obtaining the CS kernel. The "ratio method"; which would allow us to obtain the soft function as well as the CS kernel. And the "double ratio"; which allows us to achieve a high degree of statistical precision, but only produces the CS kernel. The matching of our result to Minkowski space is achieved through the mapping of the complex auxiliary field directional vector to the Wilson line rapidity. We present a preliminary extraction of the CS kernel using the "double ratio", and discuss the methodology employed.

# Lattice extraction of the Collins-Soper kernel using the auxiliary field representation of the Wilson line

## Key claim

The Collins-Soper (CS) kernel can be extracted from lattice QCD by computing the TMD soft function using Wilson lines formulated with one-dimensional auxiliary fermion fields, where the Euclidean directional vectors have purely imaginary time components that map to space-like Wilson lines in Minkowski space (Collins' scheme). The authors present a preliminary extraction using the "double ratio" method, which achieves high statistical precision but only yields the difference between CS kernel values at two different transverse separations, requiring perturbative matching to obtain the absolute CS kernel.

## Method

The Wilson line is represented via auxiliary fermion fields whose propagator satisfies a Green function equation with a complex directional vector $\tilde n = (in^0, \vec 0_\perp, n^3)$ in Euclidean space. The soft function (butterfly loop) is computed on quenched lattice configurations at four lattice spacings, using the auxiliary field propagator solved iteratively in Euclidean time following the method of Horgan et al. (2009). A single ratio of soft functions at two different transverse separations $b_\perp$ removes cutoff effects from the auxiliary propagator, and a double ratio at two different values of the directional vector parameter $r$ eliminates linear divergences in $1/a$. The resulting double ratio is matched to perturbative N$^3$LO results for the CS kernel at small $b_\perp$ to extract the CS kernel at larger $b_\perp$.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacings used: $a = 0.081$ fm ($24^3 \times 48$), $0.060$ fm ($32^3 \times 64$), $0.048$ fm ($40^3 \times 80$), and $0.041$ fm ($48^3 \times 96$), with 400, 400, 250, and 341 quenched configurations respectively
- Perturbative matching window chosen as $3a \leq b_\perp \lesssim 0.2$ fm, with the lower bound to avoid discretization errors and the upper bound to reduce uncertainty in the perturbative CS kernel
- The $24^3 \times 48$ and $32^3 \times 64$ lattices were omitted from the CS kernel extraction due to insufficient perturbative window
- Renormalization scale for $\overline{\rm MS}$-scheme CS kernel varied by 10% around a central value of $2$ GeV to estimate systematic uncertainty
- Data from $64^3 \times 128$ configurations at $a = 0.03$ fm are being processed for improved control over the perturbative window

## Context

This work extends the proposal of Ji, Liu, and Liu (2020) to compute the soft function using heavy quark effective theory, but differs by using Euclidean directional vectors that map to space-like (rather than time-like) directions in Minkowski space and by employing a different method for handling UV cutoff effects. The authors verify to one-loop in perturbation theory that their Euclidean computation with complex directional vectors recovers the Minkowski space result of Ebert et al. (2019). The approach enables lattice extraction of the CS kernel, which governs the rapidity evolution of transverse momentum dependent (TMD) observables and is essential for mapping the three-dimensional structure of hadrons.
