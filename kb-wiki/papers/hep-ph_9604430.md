# Solving the Gluon Dyson-Schwinger Equation in the Mandelstam Approximation

> Truncated Dyson-Schwinger equations represent finite subsets of the equations of motion for Green's functions. Solutions to these non-linear integral equations can account for non-perturbative correlations. We describe the solution to the Dyson-Schwinger equation for the gluon propagator of Landau gauge QCD in the Mandelstam approximation. This involves a combination of numerical and analytic methods: An asymptotic infrared expansion of the solution is calculated recursively. In the ultraviolet, the problem reduces to an analytically solvable differential equation. The iterative solution is then obtained numerically by matching it to the analytic results at appropriate points. Matching point independence is obtained for sufficiently wide ranges. The solution is used to extract a non-perturbative beta-function. The scaling behavior is in good agreement with perturbative QCD. No further fixed point for positive values of the coupling is found which thus increases without bound in the infrared. The non-perurbative result implies an infrared singular quark interaction relating the scale Lambda of the subtraction scheme to the string tension sigma.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in the Mandelstam approximation (neglecting ghosts and fermions), the gluon Dyson-Schwinger equation yields a running coupling that increases without bound in the infrared with no further fixed point for positive coupling values, but the authors note that this conclusion changes qualitatively when ghosts are included, as shown in their later work (von Smekal, Hauck, and Alkofer, 1997).

## Method

The authors solve the Dyson-Schwinger equation for the gluon propagator in Landau gauge QCD under the Mandelstam approximation, which uses a simplified form for the three-gluon vertex derived from the Slavnov-Taylor identity while neglecting ghost and quark contributions. They combine an asymptotic infrared expansion of the solution (calculated recursively as a double power series) with an analytically solvable differential equation in the ultraviolet, then obtain the full solution numerically by matching the two regimes at appropriate momentum points. The renormalized equation is cast in a renormalization group invariant form, and the running coupling is extracted from the solution via the renormalization condition.

## Key numbers

* Infrared exponent for the gluon renormalization function: gamma_0 approximately 1.2705, obtained from solving a cubic equation derived from the integral equation for the Brown-Pennington version of the Mandelstam equation.
* Leading ultraviolet coefficient kappa_0 = 14 for the asymptotic expansion of the gluon self-energy function, compared to the perturbative QCD value kappa_0 = 11 for N_f = 0 (pure Yang-Mills).
* Leading ultraviolet coefficient for Mandelstam's original equation: beta_0 = (25/2)/(4pi)^2 and gamma_A^0 = (25/4)/(4pi)^2.
* The anomalous dimension of the gluon field in the Mandelstam approximation: gamma_A^0 = 7/(4pi)^2, consistent with the identity Z_g Z_3 = 1 (delta = 1).
* Memory required for the numerical program: 200 kB; typical running time: approximately 10 seconds on a DEC Alpha 500 workstation.

## Context

This work extends the earlier studies of Mandelstam (1979), who first proposed the approximation, and Atkinson et al. (1981), who provided an existence proof and asymptotic infrared expansion for Mandelstam's original equation. It also addresses the modified equation of Brown and Pennington (1988, 1989), who identified the correct transverse projection to avoid spurious quadratic ultraviolet divergences. The authors note that their results differ from those of Brown and Pennington (1989) in the deep infrared, where the latter's numerical solution becomes singular at finite momentum. The paper enables a detailed comparison with the coupled gluon-ghost system presented in von Smekal, Hauck, and Alkofer (1997), where the inclusion of ghosts leads to an infrared stable fixed point for the running coupling, qualitatively changing the infrared behavior.
