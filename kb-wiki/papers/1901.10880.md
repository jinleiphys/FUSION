# Dynamical Symmetries and Beyond: Lessons and Advances

> A central theme in Iachello's quest for understanding simple ordered patterns in complex quantum systems, is the concept of dynamical symmetry. Relying on his seminal contributions, we present further generalization of this notion to that of partial dynamical symmetry (PDS), for which solvability and good quantum numbers are maintained by only a subset of states. Hamiltonians with a single PDS and multiple PDSs are constructed explicitly and their relevance to nuclear structure is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper generalizes the concept of dynamical symmetry (DS) to partial dynamical symmetry (PDS), where solvability and good quantum numbers are maintained by only a subset of states, and extends this further to Hamiltonians with multiple PDSs that can describe coexisting nuclear shapes at critical points of quantum phase transitions.

## Method

The author constructs PDS Hamiltonians in the interacting boson model (IBM) by identifying n-boson annihilation operators that annihilate specific basis states of a chosen DS chain, then forming a normal-ordered Hamiltonian from products of these operators and adding Casimir operators of sub-algebras. For multiple PDSs, the same annihilation operators are required to annihilate basis states from two or more incompatible DS chains simultaneously, yielding a Hamiltonian with solvable eigenstates in each symmetry while other states are mixed.

## Context

This work extends the dynamical symmetry concept pioneered by Iachello in the IBM (Arima and Iachello, 1975-1979) and builds on the PDS notion introduced by Leviatan (2011). It provides explicit Hamiltonians with single PDS for SU(3), SO(6), and U(5) chains, and constructs multiple-PDS Hamiltonians for spherical-prolate, spherical-gamma-unstable, prolate-oblate, and triple shape coexistence at critical points of first-order quantum phase transitions. The results enable analytic descriptions of selected bands in transitional nuclei and provide parameter-free predictions for electromagnetic transitions that can be tested against data, as demonstrated for \(^{168}\)Er, \(^{156}\)Gd, \(^{196}\)Pt, and \(^{110}\)Cd.
