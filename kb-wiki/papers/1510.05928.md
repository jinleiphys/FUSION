# Theoretical Optical Potential Derived From Nucleon-Nucleon Chiral Potentials

> Background: Elastic scattering is probably the main event in the interactions of nucleons with nuclei. Even if this process has been extensively studied in the last years, a consistent description, i.e. starting from microscopic two- and many-body forces connected by the same symmetries and principles, is still under development. Purpose: In this work we study the domain of applicability of microscopic two-body chiral potentials in the construction of an optical potential. Methods: We basically follow the KMT approach to build a microscopic complex optical potential and then we perform some test calculations on 16O at different energies. Results: Our conclusion is that a particular set of potentials with a Lippmann-Schwinger cutoff at relatively high energies (above 500 MeV) has the best performances reproducing the scattering observables. Conclusions: Our work shows that building an optical potential within Chiral Perturbation Theory is a promising approach to the description of elastic proton scattering, in particular, in view of the future inclusion of many-body forces that naturally arise in such framework.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that microscopic two-body chiral potentials at fourth order (N3LO) can be used to construct a first-order optical potential for elastic proton scattering, with the best performance achieved when the Lippmann-Schwinger cutoff is set above 500 MeV, though the approach is presented as promising rather than definitive, pending future inclusion of three-body forces.

## Method

The authors follow the Kerman-McManus-Thaler (KMT) multiple scattering theory to build a first-order microscopic optical potential in momentum space, using the optimum factorization approximation to express the potential as a product of the free nucleon-nucleon (NN) t matrix and the nuclear matter density. The NN t matrix is computed from two different versions of chiral potentials at fourth order (N3LO) developed by Entem and Machleidt (EM) and by Epelbaum, Glockle, and Meissner (EGM), as well as the CD-Bonn potential for comparison, with partial waves included up to J=8. Test calculations are performed on 16O at various energies, solving the Lippmann-Schwinger equation in partial wave representation to obtain scattering observables.

## Key numbers

- The chiral potentials used are at fourth order (N3LO) in the chiral expansion, with partial waves included up to J=8
- The best-performing potentials have a Lippmann-Schwinger cutoff above 500 MeV
- For a 16O target at 200 MeV, up to 30 partial wave L values are needed to represent the nuclear optical potential
- The Moller factor imposes Lorentz invariance when passing from the NA to the NN frame, with the energy fixed as ω = T_lab/2 = (1/2)(k_lab^2/(2m))

## Context

This work extends the long-standing program of microscopic optical potential construction from realistic NN potentials, previously pursued with potentials from Nijmegen (PhysRevC.49.2950, 1994), Paris (PhysRevC.21.861, 1980), Bonn (machleidt1989), and Argonne (PhysRevC.51.38, 1995), by instead using chiral potentials from Chiral Perturbation Theory. The approach enables future inclusion of chiral three-body forces at N2LO, as recently explored by Epelbaum et al. (PhysRevLett.115.122301, 2015) and others (PhysRevC.88.024614, 2013; PhysRevC.90.051601, 2014), and can be extended to unstable nuclei where experimental data are not yet available.
