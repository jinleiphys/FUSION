# The r-Process in Metal Poor Stars and Black Hole Formation

> Nucleosynthesis of heavy nuclei in metal-poor stars is generally thought to occur via the r-process because the r-process is a primary process that would have operated early in the Galaxy's history. This idea is strongly supported by the fact that the abundance pattern in many metal-poor stars matches well the inferred Solar r-process abundance pattern in the mass range between the second and third r-process abundance peaks. Nevertheless, a significant number of metal-poor stars do not share this standard r-process template. In this Letter we suggest that the nuclides observed in many of these stars are produced by the r-process, but that it is prevented from running to completion in more massive stars by collapse to black holes before the r-process is completed, creating a "truncated r-process," or "tr-process." We find that the observed fraction of tr-process stars is qualitatively what one would expect from the initial mass function, and that an apparent sharp truncation observed at around mass 160 could result from a combination of collapses to black holes and the difficulty of observing the higher mass rare earths. We test the tr-process hypothesis with r-process calculations that are terminated before all r-process trajectories have been ejected. We find qualitative agreement between observation and theory when black hole collapse and observational realities are taken into account.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that many metal-poor stars whose abundance patterns do not match the standard r-process template were nevertheless enriched by the r-process, but that the process was truncated by the collapse of the progenitor star to a black hole before the r-process could run to completion, creating a "truncated r-process" or "tr-process." The authors find qualitative agreement between observation and theory when black hole collapse and observational difficulties in detecting higher-mass rare earths are taken into account, but note that the observed fraction of candidate tr-process stars (approximately 55%) is significantly higher than the 13% predicted from a Salpeter initial mass function.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis calculations using a network code based on libnucnet with nuclear data from the JINA reaclib database, applied to trajectories 24–40 from the Woosley et al. (1994) hydrodynamics model of neutrino-driven winds in core-collapse supernovae. They assumed that wind elements were emitted successively from the neutron star and that collapse to a black hole terminated further emission, so that summing the nucleosynthesis yields from trajectory 24 up to some higher trajectory number (and excluding later trajectories) simulated the truncated r-process. The calculations assumed adiabatic expansion with constant entropy within each trajectory, and the resulting abundance patterns were compared to observations of the metal-poor halo star HD 122563.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of stars expected to collapse to black holes (25–40 solar masses) to those expected to collapse to neutron stars (8–25 solar masses) is 0.13, based on a Salpeter initial mass function with dN/dm ∝ m^(-2.35).
- Stars with [Eu/Fe] > +1.0 comprise less than 10% of all stars with [Fe/H] < -2.0, based on the surveys of McWilliam (1995) and Barklem et al. (2005).
- From Ba abundances in the Barklem et al. (2005) survey, the authors estimate a lower limit of approximately 55% of metal-poor stars as candidates for enrichment by a truncated r-process.
- The apparent sharp truncation in observed abundance patterns occurs around mass 160 (near dysprosium).
- The star HD 122563 has [Fe/H] = -2.7 and is deficient in heavy neutron-capture elements (Ba and heavier) relative to light neutron-capture elements (Sr through Cd).
- The calculation including trajectories 24 through 31 reaches the mass 195 u peak but with abundance nearly two orders of magnitude below that of the full r-process, making higher-mass nuclides difficult to observe observationally.

## Context

This work extends the standard r-process interpretation of metal-poor star abundances (Sneden et al., 2003) by addressing the significant fraction of stars that do not match the standard r-process template, as documented by Roederer et al. (2010). The tr-process hypothesis builds on the neutrino-driven wind model of Woosley et al. (1994) and the concept of fallback supernovae in stars of roughly 25–40 solar masses (Heger et al., 1992), where the neutron star subsequently collapses to a black hole. The paper suggests that the tr-process, combined with other proposed nucleosynthesis channels (Qian et al., 2008; Farouqi et al., 2009; Wanajo et al., 2011; Nakamura et al., 2011), may help explain the underproduction of nuclides in the mass 110–120 u region that has been problematic for standard r-process calculations (Kratz et al., 2007; Farouqi et al., 2009).
