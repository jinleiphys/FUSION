# Analytic Solution for the Revised Helicity Evolution at Small $x$ and Large $N_c$: New Resummed Gluon-Gluon Polarized Anomalous Dimension and Intercept

> We construct an exact analytic solution of the revised small-$x$ helicity evolution equations derived recently. The equations we solve are obtained in the large-$N_c$ limit (with $N_c$ the number of quark colors) and are double-logarithmic (summing powers of $α_s \ln^2(1/x)$ with $α_s$ the strong coupling constant and $x$ the Bjorken $x$ variable). Our solution provides small-$x$, large-$N_c$ expressions for the flavor-singlet quark and gluon helicity parton distribution functions (PDFs) and for the $g_1$ structure function, with their leading small-$x$ asymptotics given by \begin{align} ΔΣ(x, Q^2) \sim ΔG (x, Q^2) \sim g_1 (x, Q^2) \sim \left( \frac{1}{x} \right)^{α_h} , \notag \end{align} where the exact analytic expression we obtain for the intercept $α_h$ can be approximated by $α_h = 3.66074 \, \sqrt{\frac{α_s \, N_c}{2 π}}$. Our solution also yields an all-order (in $α_s$) resummed small-$x$ anomalous dimension $Δγ_{GG} (ω)$ which agrees with all the existing fixed-order calculations (to three loops). Notably, our anomalous dimension is different from that obtained in the infrared evolution equation framework developed earlier by Bartels, Ermolaev, and Ryskin (BER), with the disagreement starting at four loops. Despite the previously reported agreement at two decimal points based on the numerical solution of the same equations, the intercept of our large-$N_c$ helicity evolution and that of BER disagree beyond that precision, with the BER intercept at large $N_c$ given by a different analytic expression from ours with the numerical value of $α_h^{BER} = 3.66394 \, \sqrt{\frac{α_s \, N_c}{2 π}}$. We speculate on the origin of this disagreement.

## Key claim

The paper provides an exact analytic solution to the revised small-x helicity evolution equations (KPS-CTT) in the large-Nc limit, yielding a small-x intercept that rounds to 3.66 but disagrees with the BER intercept at the third decimal point (3.66074 versus 3.66394 times sqrt(alpha_s N_c / (2 pi))), with the disagreement in the anomalous dimension starting at four loops.

## Method

The author solves the closed system of four coupled integral equations for the polarized dipole amplitudes G, G2, Gamma, and Gamma2 by writing the amplitudes as double inverse Laplace transforms in the variables ln(zs x_10^2) and ln(1/(x_10^2 Lambda^2)). The resulting double-Laplace images are manipulated to obtain analytic expressions for the amplitudes, which are then used to derive analytic forms for the helicity parton distribution functions, transverse momentum distributions, and the g1 structure function via previously derived relations. The small-x asymptotics are extracted from the rightmost singularity in the complex-omega plane, which comes from a branch point in the function gamma^-_omega.

## Key numbers

* Small-x intercept for helicity evolution: alpha_h = 3.66074 sqrt(alpha_s N_c / (2 pi))
* BER intercept for comparison: alpha_h^BER = 3.66394 sqrt(alpha_s N_c / (2 pi))
* Resummed anomalous dimension expansion: Delta gamma_GG(omega) = 4 bar_alpha_s / omega + 8 bar_alpha_s^2 / omega^3 + 56 bar_alpha_s^3 / omega^5 + 496 bar_alpha_s^4 / omega^7 + O(alpha_s^5), where bar_alpha_s = alpha_s N_c / (2 pi)
* BER anomalous dimension expansion: Delta gamma_GG^BER(omega) = 4 bar_alpha_s / omega + 8 bar_alpha_s^2 / omega^3 + 56 bar_alpha_s^3 / omega^5 + 504 bar_alpha_s^4 / omega^7 + O(alpha_s^5)
* All helicity PDFs and g1 have leading small-x asymptotics proportional to (1/x)^(alpha_h)

## Context

This work extends the KPS-CTT evolution equations derived by Cougoulic, Tarasov, and Tawabutr (2022), which corrected the earlier KPS (Kovchegov, Pitonyak, Sievert) evolution, and provides the first analytic solution to these equations. The results are compared to the infrared evolution equation framework of Bartels, Ermolaev, and Ryskin (BER) from 1996, revealing a small disagreement in the intercept and anomalous dimension that was not visible in the earlier numerical solution. The analytic expressions enable precise comparison between the two formalisms and provide resummed results that can be used in phenomenological applications of small-x helicity evolution.
