# Nuclear Superfluidity and Specific Heat in the Inner Crust of Neutron Stars

> We analyse the temperature dependence of pairing correlations in the inner crust matter of neutron stars. The study is done in a finite-temperature HFB approach and by using a zero range pairing force adjusted to the pairing properties of infinite neutron matter. Within the same approach we investigate how the specific heat of the inner crust depends on temperature, matter inhomogeneity, and the assumption used for the pairing force. It is shown that in a physical relevant range of densities the pairing properties of inner crust matter depend significantly on temperature. The finite-temperature HFB calculations show also that the specific heat is rather sensitive to the presence of nuclear clusters inside the inner crust. However, the most dramatic change of the specific heat is determined by the scenario used for the neutron matter superfluidity.

# Nuclear Superfluidity and Specific Heat in the Inner Crust of Neutron Stars

## Key claim

The temperature dependence of pairing correlations and specific heat in neutron star inner crust matter is significant in physically relevant density ranges, but the most dramatic changes in specific heat arise from the choice of pairing force scenario (Gogny-type versus induced-interaction-type) rather than from temperature or matter inhomogeneity alone.

## Method

Finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (FT-HFB) calculations were performed for representative Wigner-Seitz cells of inner crust matter, using the Skyrme SLy4 interaction for the particle-hole channel and a zero-range density-dependent pairing force with two parameter sets adjusted to reproduce either the Gogny-force pairing gap (maximum ~3.2 MeV) or the induced-interaction pairing gap (maximum ~1 MeV) in infinite neutron matter. The FT-HFB equations were solved with Dirichlet-Neumann boundary conditions for two representative cells containing Z=50 protons and N=1750 or N=900 neutrons, corresponding to baryonic densities of 0.0204 fm^-3 and 0.00373 fm^-3, respectively.

## Key numbers

- Two representative Wigner-Seitz cells studied: one with baryonic density 0.0204 fm^-3 (cell radius 27.6 fm, N=1750 neutrons) and one with density 0.00373 fm^-3 (cell radius 39.3 fm, N=900 neutrons)
- Maximum pairing gap in neutron matter for the Gogny-force scenario: approximately 3.2 MeV at Fermi momentum k_F ~ 0.9 fm^-1
- Maximum pairing gap in neutron matter for the induced-interaction scenario: approximately 1 MeV
- Pairing force parameters for the first scenario: V_0 = -430 MeV fm^3, eta = 0.7, alpha = 0.45; for the second scenario: V_0 = -330 MeV fm^3
- FT-HFB calculations performed up to a maximum temperature of T = 0.5 MeV
- Physical temperature range in the inner crust: from about T = 0.1 MeV in the low-density region to about T = 0.3 MeV at the inner edge
- At T = 0.1 MeV for the high-density cell (1800Sn), the presence of the nuclear cluster increases the specific heat by about 6 times compared to uniform neutron gas
- For the high-density cell at T = 0.1 MeV, the specific heat predicted by the two pairing forces differs by about 7 orders of magnitude

## Context

This work extends zero-temperature HFB calculations of inner crust superfluidity by Broglia, Barranco, Pizzochero, and Sandulescu (various years) to include finite-temperature effects, which are necessary because the condition that the pairing gap be much larger than the temperature is not fulfilled for all density regions. The results show that temperature dependence must be included in microscopic calculations of inner crust properties, and that the uncertainty in the pairing force (Gogny-type versus induced-interaction-type) leads to specific heat differences of several orders of magnitude in high-density cells, making neutron and electron contributions to total specific heat competitive in the second scenario. This enables future work on heat diffusion along the whole inner crust and its implications for neutron star cooling times, as well as the inclusion of ground state correlations that modify neutron single-particle properties such as effective mass and mean free path.
