# Isotope thermometery in nuclear multifragmentation

> A systematic study of the effect of fragment$-$fragment interaction, quantum statistics, $γ$-feeding and collective flow is made in the extraction of the nuclear temperature from the double ratio of the isotopic yields in the statistical model of one-step (Prompt) multifragmentation. Temperature is also extracted from the isotope yield ratios generated in the sequential binary-decay model. Comparison of the thermodynamic temperature with the extracted temperatures for different isotope ratios show some anomaly in both models which is discussed in the context of experimentally measured caloric curves.

# Isotope thermometry in nuclear multifragmentation

## Key claim

The double-ratio isotope thermometer, commonly used to extract nuclear temperatures from fragment yields, is systematically affected by fragment-fragment interaction, quantum statistics, γ-feeding, and collective flow in the prompt multifragmentation model; in the sequential binary-decay model, the extracted temperatures are very low (~3 MeV) and weakly dependent on initial excitation energy, raising questions about the interpretation of experimentally measured caloric curves as evidence for a liquid-gas phase transition.

## Method

The authors performed calculations in the prompt multifragmentation (PM) model using a grand canonical approach with a freeze-out volume of 6V₀ for the representative system ¹⁵⁰Sm, generating fragment multiplicities under successive approximations: classical Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics with no interactions, then adding fragment-fragment interaction (nuclear plus Coulomb), quantum statistics, γ-feeding from particle-stable excited states, and collective flow energy (25% or 50% of total excitation). They also generated fragment yields from the sequential binary-decay (SBD) model using the transition-state method of Swiatecki, with Monte-Carlo sampling of relative kinetic energies and trajectory integration under Coulomb forces, and extracted double-ratio temperatures using the Albergo prescription from both models.

## Key numbers

- For the prompt multifragmentation model, freeze-out volumes of 6V₀ and 10V₀ were used, where V₀ is the normal volume of the fragmenting system.
- At lower excitations, an uncertainty in the extracted temperature Tᵣ of ~2.0 MeV was found between the (Li-d) and (Li-He) thermometers, increasing progressively with excitation energy.
- In the sequential binary-decay model, double-ratio temperatures were very low (~3 MeV) even at the highest excitation energy studied (13.5 MeV per nucleon).
- The initial excitation energies per particle used for SBD calculations were 13.5, 10.0, and 6.0 MeV.
- The level density parameter was taken as A/10 MeV⁻¹.
- The flow energy was taken as 25% or 50% of the total excitation energy in the PM calculations.

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of isotope thermometry beyond the original Albergo prescription (Albergo et al., 1985) by quantifying the effects of approximations that were previously treated separately or qualitatively. It builds on the caloric curve measurements from the ALADIN Group (Pochodzalla et al., 1995) in Au+Au collisions at 600 AMeV and the EOS collaboration (Hauger et al., 1996; Elliott et al., 1996), and on earlier studies of quantum statistics effects by Gulminelli and Durand (1996) and Majumder and Das Gupta (1994), side-feeding effects by Kolomiets et al. (1996) and Bondorf et al. (1995), and flow effects by Shlomo et al. (1996). The finding that both PM and SBD models produce caloric curves with apparent phase-transition signatures implies that additional observables beyond the caloric curve are needed to establish the nature of nuclear disassembly.
