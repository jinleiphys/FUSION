# Nuclear matter, nuclear and subnuclear degrees of freedom

> We report here theoretical investigations on the complexity of nuclear structure, which have been carried out in the framework of different many-body approaches, typically applied to nuclear matter and quark matter studies. The variational, functional and perturbative scheme are illustrated in their latest developments. The effect of various nucleon-nucleon interactions are tested, particularly in the context of the nuclear response functions, against a large body of experimental data. The properties and decay widths of hypernuclei are shortly revisited, while the equation of state of isospin asymmetric nuclear matter leads us toward nuclear systems of astrophysical interest, like the neutron stars. Finally the transition from hadronic matter to the deconfined phase of quark-gluon plasma endorses the application of many-body and field theoretical techniques to a system with subnucleonic degrees of freedom.

## Key claim

This paper reviews theoretical many-body approaches to nuclear structure, covering variational, functional, and perturbative methods applied to nuclear matter and quark matter, but it is a comprehensive review rather than a report of a single new result; the abstract's claim of testing nucleon-nucleon interactions against experimental data is hedged by the fact that the tests are performed within specific model frameworks (e.g., Correlated Basis Function theory, Random Phase Approximation) and the comparisons are illustrative of method performance rather than definitive validations.

## Method

The paper surveys three categories of many-body techniques for calculating nuclear response functions in nuclear matter: (1) the variational method based on the Fermi Hypernetted Chain (FHNC) scheme with correlated wave functions; (2) the functional (path-integral) method employing a loop expansion from a Lagrangian describing nucleons, pions, and rho mesons coupled to an external electromagnetic field; and (3) perturbation theory including Hartree-Fock, Brueckner-Hartree-Fock, and Random Phase Approximation (RPA) with continued-fraction expansions. These methods are applied to evaluate electromagnetic response functions for inclusive and exclusive electron scattering, as well as to K+-nucleus scattering and neutrino-nucleus scattering, with comparisons to experimental data on 40Ca.

## Key numbers

- Transverse response enhancement from two-body meson exchange currents ranges from ~20% at momentum transfer q=300 MeV/c to ~10% at q~500 MeV/c (nuclear matter at saturation density, compared to 40Ca data)
- The parity-violating asymmetry normalization factor is A_o ≈ 3.1×10^{-4} τ, where τ = Q^2/(4M^2)
- Neutrino-nucleus cross sections are calculated in an energy range from 200 MeV to 1 GeV using relativistic independent particle models
- Nuclear matter responses are computed at Fermi momentum k_F = 195 MeV/c and q = 500 MeV/c for comparison of free, ring, and RPA-CF1 approximations
- K+-40Ca cross sections are shown at q = 290 MeV/c and q = 480 MeV/c

## Context

This review extends earlier work on nuclear response functions by comparing variational (FHNC), functional (path-integral), and perturbative (RPA, continued-fraction) methods within a unified framework, building on prior calculations by Fabrocini (1990s), Cenni et al. (1990s), and De Pace et al. (1990s). It connects nuclear matter studies to finite nuclei through the Hybrid model and semiclassical approaches (Chanfray, 1990s), and links hadronic matter to the quark-gluon plasma phase transition relevant to neutron stars and early Universe conditions. The work enables future comparisons between parity-violating electron scattering and K+ scattering for isospin separation in nuclear response functions.
