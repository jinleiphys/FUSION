# Equation of state dependence of Mach cone like structures in Au+Au collisions

> In jet quenching, a hard QCD parton, before fragmenting into a jet of hadrons, deposits a fraction of its energy in the medium. As the parton moves nearly with speed of light, much greater that the speed of sound of the medium, quenching jet can generate Mach shock wave. We have examined the possibility of Mach shock wave formation due to jet quenching. Assuming that the deposited energy quickly thermalize, we simulate the hydrodynamic evolution of the QGP fluid with a quenching jet and subsequent particle production. Angular distribution of pions, averaged over all the jet trajectories, resembles 'conical flow' due to Mach shock wave formation. However, speed of sound dependence of the simulated Mach angles are at variance with that due to shock wave formation in a static medium or in a medium with finite velocity.

# Equation of state dependence of Mach cone like structures in Au+Au collisions

## Key claim
The simulated Mach angles extracted from jet-trajectory-averaged azimuthal distributions of pions in the "Hydro+Jet" model show a speed-of-sound dependence that is inconsistent with theoretical Mach angles expected from shock wave formation in either a static medium or a medium with finite fluid velocity, suggesting that the observed conical flow-like structures may not originate from Mach shock waves.

## Method
The authors model a quenching jet as a time-dependent source term in the energy-momentum conservation equations of a QGP fluid, assuming the deposited energy quickly thermalizes. They solve the hydrodynamic equations using a modified version of the AZHYDRO code in (τ,x,y,η) coordinates with boost invariance, using a simple equation of state p = c_s² ε for square speeds of sound c_s² = 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, and 0.4. For central Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV, they simulate single jet events and then average over all possible jet production angles on the fireball surface to obtain trajectory-averaged azimuthal distributions of π⁻ mesons in three p_T ranges.

- Parton energy loss reference value: dE/dx|_0 = 28 GeV/fm at reference entropy density s_0 = 140 fm⁻³ (used to enhance jet quenching effects; average energy loss per jet trajectory is approximately 10-15 GeV)
- Initial peak energy density: ε_i = 30 GeV/fm³ at initial proper time τ_i = 0.6 fm/c
- Freeze-out temperature: T_F = 100 MeV, with instantaneous hadronization assumed
- Speed of sound values studied: c_s² = 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, and 0.4
- Jet production point on fireball boundary: fireball radius R = 6.4 fm for Au+Au collisions at b=0

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous "Hydro+Jet" model studies (Chaudhuri 2005, 2006, 2007, 2008) that showed trajectory-averaged azimuthal distributions can produce double-peaked structures resembling conical flow. It directly tests whether these structures follow Mach's law by examining their speed-of-sound dependence. The results contradict the expectation from shock wave formation in a moving medium (Satarov 2005) and agree with findings by Noronha (2008) that supersonic strings in AdS/CFT correspondence produce Mach cone-like peaks that do not follow Mach's law. The study enables future work to explore alternative mechanisms for the observed away-side peak splitting in STAR (Wang 2004) and PHENIX (Jacak 2005) data, such as gluon Cerenkov radiation (Dremin 2005, Koch 2005) or parton cascade models (Ma 2006).
