# ESO Expanding Horizon White Paper: Revealing the properties of matter at supranuclear densities with gravitational waves

> Understanding dense matter under extreme conditions is one of the most fundamental puzzles in modern physics. Complex interactions give rise to emergent, collective phenomena. While nuclear experiments and Earth - based colliders provide valuable insights, much of the quantum chromodynamics phase diagram at high density and low temperature remains accessible only through astrophysical observations of neutron stars, neutron star mergers, and stellar collapse. Astronomical observations thus offer a direct window to the physics on subatomic scales with gravitational waves presenting an especially clean channel. Next-generation gravitational - wave observatories, such as the Einstein Telescope, would serve as unparalleled instruments to transform our understanding of neutron star matter. They will enable the detection of up to tens of thousands of binary neutron star and neutron star - black hole mergers per year, a dramatic increase over the few events accessible with current detectors. They will provide an unprecedented precision in probing cold, dense matter during the binary inspiral, exceeding by at least an order of magnitude what current facilities can achieve. Moreover, these observatories will allow us to explore uncharted regimes of dense matter at finite temperatures produced in a subset of neutron star mergers, areas that remain entirely inaccessible to current instruments. Together with multimessenger observations, these measurements will significantly deepen our knowledge of dense nuclear matter.

## Key claim

Next-generation gravitational-wave observatories such as the Einstein Telescope will enable detection of up to tens of thousands of binary neutron-star and neutron-star–black-hole mergers per year and provide precision in probing cold, dense matter during binary inspiral that exceeds current facilities by at least an order of magnitude, but the paper hedges that only a small fraction of events will have signal-to-noise ratios high enough to measure subdominant effects like mode excitations or post-merger signals.

## Method

The paper is a white paper reviewing the scientific case for the Einstein Telescope as a tool for nuclear astrophysics, synthesizing arguments from gravitational-wave astrophysics, nuclear theory, and multimessenger observations. It discusses how gravitational waves from binary neutron-star inspirals, post-merger phases, core-collapse supernovae, and isolated neutron stars can probe the equation of state of dense matter at supranuclear densities, and contrasts the projected capabilities of third-generation detectors with current LIGO, Virgo, and KAGRA networks.

## Key numbers

- Up to tens of thousands of binary neutron-star and neutron-star–black-hole mergers per year will be detectable with the Einstein Telescope, a dramatic increase over the few events accessible with current detectors.
- Precision in probing cold, dense matter during binary inspiral will exceed current facilities by at least an order of magnitude.
- A small fraction of detected mergers will have signal-to-noise ratios of more than several hundred, enabling precise measurements of neutron-star masses and tidal deformability.
- The post-merger phase of binary neutron-star collisions remains entirely inaccessible to current instruments but will become observable with third-generation observatories for a subset of events.

## Context

This white paper extends the program initiated by the first gravitational-wave multi-messenger detection of a binary neutron-star merger, GW170817 (LIGO Scientific Collaboration, 2017), which provided constraints on neutron-star tidal deformabilities and the equation of state. It builds on radio pulsar mass measurements (Fonseca, 2021) and X-ray pulse profile observations (Miller, 2019; Riley, 2019) that have placed lower bounds on the maximum neutron-star mass and provided mass-radius constraints. The paper enables a roadmap for using the Einstein Telescope to probe the quantum chromodynamics phase diagram at high density and low temperature, including finite-temperature regimes in merger remnants that are inaccessible to current detectors and terrestrial collider experiments.
