# Nuclear energy release from fragmentation

> Nuclear energy released by splitting Uranium and Thorium isotopes into two, three, four, up to eight fragments with nearly equal size are studied. We found that the energy released come from equally splitting the $^{235,238}$U and $^{230,232}$Th nuclei into to three fragments is largest. The statistical multifragmentation model is employed to calculate the probability of different breakup channels for the excited nuclei. Weighing the the probability distributions of fragments multiplicity at different excitation energies for the $^{238}$U nucleus, we found that an excitation energy between 1.2 and 2 MeV/u is optimal for the $^{235}$U, $^{238}$U, $^{230}$Th and $^{232}$Th nuclei to release nuclear energy of about 0.7-0.75 MeV/u.

# Nuclear energy release from fragmentation

## Key claim
The authors find that splitting heavy nuclei (Uranium and Thorium isotopes) into three nearly equal fragments releases the most nuclear energy per nucleon (0.95 MeV/u), but this ternary breakup is only probable at excitation energies between 1.2 and 2 MeV/u; at lower excitation energies binary fission dominates, and at higher excitation energies the average energy released per nucleon decreases as higher-multiplicity channels open.

## Method
The authors used the experimental binding energy table AME2012 to calculate the energy release from splitting 235U, 238U, 230Th, and 232Th into 2 through 8 fragments of nearly equal size via the mass defect formula. They then employed the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) to compute the probability distributions of different breakup channels for these excited nuclei, and weighted the energy release by these probabilities to obtain the average energy released per nucleon as a function of excitation energy.

## Key numbers
- For 238U splitting into three equal fragments, the energy released is 0.95 MeV/u, compared to 0.7-0.8 MeV/u for splitting into two equal fragments.
- For 238U splitting into four equal fragments, the energy released is 0.80 MeV/u.
- For the reaction n+235U -> 141Ba+92Kr+3n, the total energy released is about 0.85 MeV/u (0.70 MeV/u from fission and 0.15 MeV/u from decay of prefragments).
- At excitation energy E* = 1 MeV/u for 238U, the probability is approximately 0.6 for binary events and 0.4 for ternary events.
- An excitation energy between 1.2 and 2 MeV/u is optimal for 235U, 238U, 230Th, and 232Th to release nuclear energy of about 0.7-0.75 MeV/u.

## Context
This work extends studies of nuclear energy release beyond the standard binary fission process, which typically releases about 0.85 MeV/u for uranium. The authors compare their results to the specific binding energy curve, noting that 56Fe has the largest specific binding energy at approximately 8.79 MeV/u. The statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) used here has been previously applied to intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions (SMM1) and spallation reactions (SMM2). The results suggest that if multifragmentation into three fragments could be harnessed, it would release more energy per nucleon than conventional binary fission, but the required excitation energy window (1.2-2 MeV/u) and the probabilistic nature of the breakup channels present practical challenges for energy applications.
