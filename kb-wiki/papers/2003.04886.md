# Hadronic vacuum polarization: $(g-2)_μ$ versus global electroweak fits

> Hadronic vacuum polarization (HVP) is not only a critical part of the Standard Model (SM) prediction for the anomalous magnetic moment of the muon $(g-2)_μ$, but also a crucial ingredient for global fits to electroweak (EW) precision observables due to its contribution to the running of the fine-structure constant encoded in $Δα^{(5)}_\text{had}$. We find that with modern EW precision data, including the measurement of the Higgs mass, the global fit alone provides a competitive, independent determination of $Δα^{(5)}_\text{had}\big|_\text{EW}=270.2(3.0)\times 10^{-4}$. This value actually lies below the range derived from $e^+e^-\to\text{hadrons}$ cross-section data, and thus goes into the opposite direction as would be required if a change in HVP were to bring the SM prediction for $(g-2)_μ$ into agreement with the Brookhaven measurement. Depending on the energy where the bulk of the changes in the cross section occurs, reconciling experiment and SM prediction for $(g-2)_μ$ by adjusting HVP would thus not necessarily weaken the case for physics beyond the SM (BSM), but to some extent shift it from $(g-2)_μ$ to the EW fit. We briefly explore some options of BSM scenarios that could conceivably explain the ensuing tension.

## Key claim
The paper finds that a global electroweak fit alone provides a competitive, independent determination of the hadronic contribution to the running of the fine-structure constant, yielding a value that lies below the range derived from electron-positron annihilation data. This means that modifying hadronic vacuum polarization to resolve the muon anomalous magnetic moment discrepancy would not necessarily eliminate the case for physics beyond the Standard Model, but would instead shift the tension from the magnetic moment to the electroweak fit.

## Method
The authors performed a Bayesian global fit to electroweak precision observables using the HEPfit package, including the Z-boson mass, Fermi constant, fine-structure constant, Higgs mass, top mass, and strong coupling constant. They considered three scenarios: using the hadronic vacuum polarization from electron-positron data as a prior, using no prior on hadronic vacuum polarization, and using a projection where hadronic vacuum polarization is adjusted to bring the muon anomalous magnetic moment into agreement with experiment. They then compared the resulting fits and examined the pulls on individual observables.

## Key numbers
- Hadronic vacuum polarization from electroweak fit alone: Δα^(5)_had|_EW = 270.2(3.0) × 10^(-4)
- Hadronic vacuum polarization from electron-positron data: Δα^(5)_had|_e+e- = 276.1(1.1) × 10^(-4)
- Projection for hadronic vacuum polarization if changes extend to all energies: Δα^(5)_had|_proj,∞ = 283.8(1.3) × 10^(-4)
- Projection for changes only below 11.2 GeV: Δα^(5)_had|_proj,≤11.2GeV = 280.3(1.3) × 10^(-4)
- Projection for changes only below 1.94 GeV: Δα^(5)_had|_proj,≤1.94GeV = 277.9(1.1) × 10^(-4)
- Tension between electroweak fit and the all-energies projection: 4.2 sigma
- Tension between electroweak fit and the below-11.2-GeV projection: 3.1 sigma
- Tension between electroweak fit and the below-1.94-GeV projection: 2.4 sigma
- Information criterion values: scenario (1) = 20.5, scenario (2) = 17, scenario (3) = 36

## Context
This work extends the earlier analysis by Passera (2008) on the correlation between hadronic vacuum polarization contributions to the muon anomalous magnetic moment and the running of the fine-structure constant, now using modern electroweak precision data including the Higgs mass measurement. The authors compare their electroweak fit result to the Gfitter group result from 2018, finding compatibility. The paper enables future work on whether lattice QCD calculations of hadronic vacuum polarization (such as those by Borsanyi and collaborators in 2020) can be reconciled with both the muon anomalous magnetic moment and electroweak precision data, and explores possible beyond-Standard-Model scenarios involving vector-like leptons, Z-prime bosons, composite Higgs models, or the minimal supersymmetric Standard Model that could explain the tensions.
