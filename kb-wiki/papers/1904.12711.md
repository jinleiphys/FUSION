# Extracting the strangeness freeze-out temperature from net-Kaon data at RHIC

> Using the moments of the net-kaon distribution calculated within a state of-the-art hadron resonance gas model compared to experimental data from STAR's Beam Energy Scan, we find that the extracted strange freeze-out temperature is incompatible with the light one extracted from net-proton and net-charge fluctuations. Additionally predictions for net-$Lambda$ fluctuations are made that also appear to be consistent with a higher freeze-out temperature for strange particles. This strangeness freeze-out temperature is roughly $10-15$ MeV higher than the corresponding light freeze-out temperature. We also discuss cross-susceptibilities using different identified particles, which may be a further test of this two freeze-out temperature picture. Finally, we lay out the necessary updates needed in relativistic hydrodynamic models to take into account for this two freeze-out temperature scenario and present preliminary results of $Λ$ spectra at RHIC for AuAu $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV collisions that indicate a higher freeze-out temperature is preferred.

# Extracting the strangeness freeze-out temperature from net-Kaon data at RHIC

## Key claim
Using moments of the net-kaon distribution from a hadron resonance gas model compared to STAR Beam Energy Scan data, the extracted strange freeze-out temperature is incompatible with the light freeze-out temperature extracted from net-proton and net-charge fluctuations, with a difference of roughly 10-15 MeV at top RHIC energies. However, at lower beam energies there are signs of convergence between the two freeze-out temperatures, and the results depend on the completeness of the hadron resonance gas particle list, as missing strange mesonic resonances may affect the extracted temperature.

## Method
The authors calculated susceptibilities of the net-kaon distribution within a hadron resonance gas model by summing over charged kaons and resonances that decay into them, applying experimental acceptance cuts in rapidity (-0.5 to 0.5) and transverse momentum (0.2 to 1.6 GeV/c). They compared the ratio chi_1^K/chi_2^K (equivalent to M/sigma^2 from experiment) to STAR data across beam energies, using isentrope trajectories from lattice QCD to constrain the relationship between temperature and baryon chemical potential, thereby extracting the strange freeze-out parameters. For the light sector, they used previously published results from combined fits of net-proton and net-charge fluctuations.

## Key numbers
- The strangeness freeze-out temperature is roughly 10-15 MeV higher than the corresponding light freeze-out temperature at top RHIC energies.
- At sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, the allowable temperature range from net-kaon measurements reaches quite high, but the highest temperatures are considered unlikely due to incompatibility with lattice QCD strange particle hadronization temperature estimates from Bellwied (2013).
- Hydrodynamic calculations at T_FO = 150 MeV under-predict Lambda spectra at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, while T_FO = 165 MeV produces reasonable fits compared to experimental data from STAR.
- At sqrt(s) = 39 GeV, thermal fit results from the STAR collaboration with and without strange baryons coincide almost perfectly with the corresponding light and strange freeze-out temperatures extracted in this work.

## Context
This work extends the approach of Alba (2015), who used net-proton and net-charge fluctuations to determine light freeze-out temperatures, and addresses the long-standing "p/pi puzzle" tension between lower freeze-out temperatures for protons and pions versus higher temperatures for strange particles noted by Floris (2014). The results are consistent with lattice QCD calculations by Bellwied (2013) showing approximately 20 MeV difference in hadronization temperatures between light and strange particles. The paper also makes predictions for net-Lambda fluctuations and cross-cumulants that can be tested against future STAR data, and outlines necessary upgrades to relativistic hydrodynamic models to implement a two-freeze-out-temperature scenario, including new equations of state with three conserved charges being developed by Noronha-Hostler (2019) and Monnai (2019).
