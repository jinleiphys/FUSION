# Angular Momentum Role in the Hypercritical Accretion of Binary-Driven Hypernovae

> The induced gravitational collapse (IGC) paradigm explains a class of energetic, $E_{\rm iso}\gtrsim 10^{52}$~erg, long-duration gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) associated with Ic supernovae, recently named binary-driven hypernovae (BdHNe). The progenitor is a tight binary system formed of a carbon-oxygen (CO) core and a neutron star companion. The supernova ejecta of the exploding CO core triggers a hypercritical accretion process onto the neutron star, which reaches in a few seconds the critical mass, and gravitationally collapses to a black hole emitting a GRB. In our previous simulations of this process we adopted a spherically symmetric approximation to compute the features of the hypercritical accretion process. We here present the first estimates of the angular momentum transported by the supernova ejecta, $L_{\rm acc}$, and perform numerical simulations of the angular momentum transfer to the neutron star during the hyperaccretion process in full general relativity. We show that the neutron star: i) reaches in a few seconds either mass-shedding limit or the secular axisymmetric instability depending on its initial mass; ii) reaches a maximum dimensionless angular momentum value, $[c J/(G M^2)]_{\rm max}\approx 0.7$; iii) can support less angular momentum than the one transported by supernova ejecta, $L_{\rm acc} > J_{\rm NS,max}$, hence there is an angular momentum excess which necessarily leads to jetted emission.

# Angular Momentum Role in the Hypercritical Accretion of Binary-Driven Hypernovae

## Key claim

The angular momentum transported by supernova ejecta onto a neutron star companion during the hypercritical accretion process in binary-driven hypernovae exceeds the maximum angular momentum that a maximally rotating neutron star can support, necessarily leading to jetted emission; however, this conclusion depends on the assumption that the supernova ejecta entering the Bondi-Hoyle capture region circularizes into a disk and transfers angular momentum to the neutron star with an efficiency parameter of ξ = 0.5.

## Method

The authors first estimated the angular momentum transported by supernova ejecta into the gravitational capture region of the neutron star using the Bondi-Hoyle accretion formalism, expanding the density and velocity fields of the ejecta to first order around the binary separation distance. They then performed numerical simulations of the angular momentum transfer to the neutron star during hypercritical accretion in full general relativity, integrating the neutron star spin-up equation simultaneously with the Bondi-Hoyle mass accretion rate, using a homologous expansion model for the supernova ejecta and pre-supernova density profiles from Kepler stellar evolution code simulations for low-metallicity progenitors with ZAMS masses of 15, 20, and 30 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- Maximum dimensionless angular momentum of the neutron star: [c J/(G M^2)]_max ≈ 0.7, reached only for the case with initial neutron star mass 2.0 solar masses ending at the mass-shedding limit
- For the example system with M_NS(0) = 2.0 solar masses, M_CO = 9.4 solar masses (M_ZAMS = 30 solar masses), and ejecta velocity v_0_star = 2 × 10^9 cm/s, the maximum binary period for black hole formation is P_max ≈ 73 minutes for the NL3 equation of state
- The neutron star reaches a maximum angular momentum J = 6.14 × 10^49 g cm^2 s^-1 (or j_NS ≈ 7) with a corresponding critical mass M_crit^(J≠0) = 3.15 solar masses for the 30 solar mass progenitor with initial period P_0 = 4.85 minutes
- The circularization radii of supernova ejecta around the neutron star range from r_st/r_mb ∼ 10 to 10^3, where r_mb is the most bound circular orbit
- The infall time in the disk relative to the characteristic accretion time is t_fall/Δ_acc ∼ 10^-3

## Context

This work extends the induced gravitational collapse (IGC) paradigm previously developed by Ruffini et al. (2008, 2012) and the numerical simulations of hypercritical accretion by Fryer et al. (2014), which adopted a spherically symmetric approximation. The paper provides the first estimates of angular momentum transport in these systems, building on earlier treatments of accretion from stellar winds in binary systems by Shapiro and Lightman (1976) and Wang (1981). The results enable a quantitative distinction between Family-1 long GRBs (where the neutron star does not collapse, producing a new neutron star binary) and Family-2 long GRBs (where the neutron star collapses to a black hole, producing a neutron star-black hole binary), as discussed by Rueda et al. (2015).
