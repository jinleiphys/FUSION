# Designing Optimal Experiments: An Application to Proton Compton Scattering

> Interpreting measurements requires a physical theory, but the theory's accuracy may vary across the experimental domain. To optimize experimental design, and so to ensure that the substantial resources necessary for modern experiments are focused on acquiring the most valuable data, both the theory uncertainty and the expected pattern of experimental errors must be considered. We develop a Bayesian approach to this problem, and apply it to the example of proton Compton scattering. Chiral Effective Field Theory ($χ$EFT) predicts the functional form of the scattering amplitude for this reaction, so that the electromagnetic polarizabilities of the nucleon can be inferred from data. With increasing photon energy, both experimental rates and sensitivities to polarizabilities increase, but the accuracy of $χ$EFT decreases. Our physics-based model of $χ$EFT truncation errors is combined with present knowledge of the polarizabilities and reasonable assumptions about experimental capabilities at HI$γ$S and MAMI to assess the information gain from measuring specific observables at specific kinematics, \emph{i.e.}, to determine the relative amount by which new data are apt to shrink uncertainties. The strongest gains would likely come from new data on the spin observables $Σ_{2x}$ and $Σ_{2x^\prime}$ at $ω\simeq140$ to $200$ MeV and $40^\circ$ to $120^\circ$. These would tightly constrain $γ_{E1E1}-γ_{E1M2}$. New data on the differential cross section between $100$ and $200$\,MeV and over a wide angle range will substantially improve constraints on $α_{E1}-β_{M1}$, $γ_π$ and $γ_{M1M1}-γ_{M1E2}$. Good signals also exist around $160$ MeV for $Σ_3$ and $Σ_{2z^\prime}$. Such data will be pivotal in the continuing quest to pin down the scalar polarizabilities and refine understanding of the spin polarizabilities.

## Key claim

The paper develops a Bayesian experimental design framework that accounts for both theory uncertainty (from chiral Effective Field Theory truncation errors) and experimental precision to identify which proton Compton scattering measurements would most efficiently reduce uncertainties on nucleon polarizabilities, with the caveat that the conclusions are conditional on using chiEFT for the extraction and on the specific prior assumptions about polarizabilities used in the analysis.

## Method

The authors combine a physics-based Gaussian process model of chiEFT truncation errors (Melendez 2019) with Bayesian experimental design, using the expected Kullback-Leibler divergence as a utility function to quantify information gain from hypothetical new measurements. They simulate three levels of experimental precision corresponding to different beam times at the HIgammaS and MAMI facilities, and compute the expected utility for each combination of observable, photon energy, and scattering angle, using existing polarizability determinations from McGovern (2012) and Griesshammer (2015) as the prior.

## Key numbers

- Optimal kinematics for spin observables Sigma_{2x} and Sigma_{2x'} are at photon energies omega = 140 to 200 MeV and angles 40 degrees to 120 degrees
- Differential cross section measurements between 100 and 200 MeV over a wide angle range will improve constraints on alpha_{E1} - beta_{M1}, gamma_pi, and gamma_{M1M1} - gamma_{M1E2}
- Good signals exist around 160 MeV for Sigma_3 and Sigma_{2z'}
- The chiEFT truncation error is approximately proportional to (m_pi / Lambda_b)^{nu/2}, where m_pi is the pion mass and Lambda_b is the breakdown scale

## Context

This work extends the sensitivity analysis of Griesshammer (2017) by providing a quantitative, Bayesian framework for comparing competing experimental designs, rather than just assessing sensitivities qualitatively. It builds on the Bayesian truncation error model of Melendez (2019) and applies it to experimental design for the first time in nuclear Compton scattering. The approach enables future work to perform a full Bayesian extraction of polarizabilities from existing Compton data using the same framework, and can be extended to other reactions where EFT predictions are available.
