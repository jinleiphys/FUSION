# Mapping neutron star data to the equation of state using the deep neural network

> The densest state of matter in the universe is uniquely realized inside central cores of the neutron star. While first-principles evaluation of the equation of state of such matter remains as one of the longstanding problems in nuclear theory, evaluation in light of neutron star phenomenology is feasible. Here we show results from a novel theoretical technique to utilize deep neural network with supervised learning. We input up-to-date observational data from neutron star X-ray radiations into the trained neural network and estimate a relation between the pressure and the mass density. Our results are consistent with extrapolation from the conventional nuclear models and the experimental bound on the tidal deformability inferred from gravitational wave observation.

## Key claim

The authors use a deep neural network with supervised learning to infer the neutron-star equation of state from observational mass-radius data, finding results consistent with conventional nuclear models and the tidal deformability bound from GW170817. The method is presented as complementary to Bayesian analysis, and the deduced EoS should be regarded as the most likely extrapolation from the SLy4 nuclear EoS at densities below the normal nuclear density.

## Method

The authors compiled observational data from 14 neutron stars, projecting each two-dimensional mass-radius probability distribution onto one-dimensional marginal distributions along the mass and radius axes, then fitting each with a Gaussian to obtain 56 parameters (mean and variance for mass and radius per star). They generated 5,000,000 training datasets by randomly constructing EoSs using piecewise polytropes with five sound-velocity segments in the density range [ρ₀, 8ρ₀] (where ρ₀ is normal nuclear density), solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain genuine mass-radius curves, and then sampling 14 data points with random Gaussian fluctuations. A feedforward neural network with 56 input neurons, three hidden layers (60, 40, 40 neurons with ReLU activation), and 5 output neurons (with tanh activation for sound velocities) was trained to map the 56 observational parameters to 5 EoS parameters (squared sound velocities in each density segment).

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density: ρ₀ ≃ 2.7 × 10¹⁷ kg/m³
- Density range for EoS generation: [ρ₀, 8ρ₀], divided into 5 segments equally spaced in logarithmic scale
- Training dataset size: 5,000,000 sets (100 × 100 × 500 ensembles)
- Validation dataset size: 100 sets (1 × 1 × 100)
- Neural network architecture: input layer (56 neurons), hidden layers (60, 40, 40 neurons), output layer (5 neurons)
- Uncertainty width for radius at M = 1.4 M⊙: 1.3 km (68% CL) from the 10-NN method; 1.7 km from the validation method
- Tidal deformability from deduced EoS: Λ(1.4 M⊙) = 320 ± 120
- Experimental bound from GW170817: Λ(1.4 M⊙) = 190⁺³⁹⁰₋₁₂₀

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior neural-network approach (Fujimoto 2017) to real observational data, providing an alternative to standard Bayesian inference methods used by Steiner et al. (2012) and Özel & Freire (2016). The deduced EoS is compared with conventional nuclear models (APR4, BSk20, ENG, SLy4), chiral effective field theory predictions (Hebeler 2013), and hybrid constructions (QHC18 from Baym 2017). The method enables future incorporation of gravitational-wave measurements and nuclear-physics experimental data into a unified machine-learning framework for multi-messenger neutron-star astrophysics.
