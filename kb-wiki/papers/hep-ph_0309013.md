# Antineutrinos from Earth: A reference model and its uncertainties

> We predict geoneutrino fluxes in a reference model based on a detailed description of Earth's crust and mantle and using the best available information on the abundances of uranium, thorium, and potassium inside Earth's layers. We estimate the uncertainties of fluxes corresponding to the uncertainties of the element abundances. In addition to distance integrated fluxes, we also provide the differential fluxes as a function of distance from several sites of experimental interest. Event yields at several locations are estimated and their dependence on the neutrino oscillation parameters is discussed. At Kamioka we predict N(U+Th)=35 +- 6 events for 10^{32} proton yr and 100% efficiency assuming sin^2(2theta)=0.863 and delta m^2 = 7.3 X 10^{-5} eV^2. The maximal prediction is 55 events, obtained in a model with fully radiogenic production of the terrestrial heat flow.

## Key claim

The paper presents a reference model for geoneutrino fluxes that incorporates the best available geological and geochemical information on uranium, thorium, and potassium distributions in Earth's crust and mantle, but the authors explicitly note that uncertainties for the lower mantle completely rely on theoretical arguments rather than observational data, and that a detailed geological study of the surroundings near a detector is needed for more precise flux estimates.

## Method

The authors constructed a reference model by combining a detailed global map of Earth's crust (with 2-degree grid resolution, distinguishing continental crust sublayers, oceanic crust, and sediments) with the Preliminary Reference Earth Model (PREM) for mantle density profiles. They adopted reference abundances for uranium as averages of published estimates for each crustal layer and the upper mantle, derived thorium and potassium abundances from the average Th/U and K/U ratios, and obtained lower mantle abundances by subtracting crust and upper mantle masses from the Bulk Silicate Earth (BSE) model constraint. Fluxes were then computed by integrating over Earth's volume, and event yields were calculated including neutrino oscillation effects using the survival probability with best-fit oscillation parameters.

## Key numbers

- At Kamioka, the predicted event yield for uranium plus thorium is N(U+Th) = 35 ± 6 events per 10^32 proton years at 100% efficiency, assuming sin^2(2θ) = 0.863 and δm^2 = 7.3 × 10^(-5) eV^2.
- The maximal prediction at Kamioka is 55 events, obtained in a model with fully radiogenic production of the terrestrial heat flow (40 TW).
- The reference uranium flux at Kamioka is Φ_U = 3.7 × 10^6 cm^(-2) s^(-1); at Gran Sasso it is Φ_U = 4.2 × 10^6 cm^(-2) s^(-1); at the top of Himalaya it is Φ_U = 6.7 × 10^6 cm^(-2) s^(-1); at Hawaii it is Φ_U = 1.3 × 10^6 cm^(-2) s^(-1).
- The reference model yields an average uranium abundance in the continental crust of a_CC(U) = 1.54 × 10^(-6), with a low estimate of 0.9 × 10^(-6) and a high estimate of 1.8 × 10^(-6).
- The BSE model total masses are M(U) = 0.81 × 10^17 kg, M(Th) = 3.16 × 10^17 kg, and M(K) = 0.49 × 10^21 kg, producing a total radiogenic heat of about 18 TW out of the observed terrestrial heat flow of approximately 40 TW.
- The no-oscillation yield at Kamioka is N_no = 61 TNU (terrestrial neutrino units, events per 10^32 proton years), compared to 43 TNU from Rothschild (1997) and 75 TNU from Raghavan (1997).
- The extreme model predictions at KamLAND are N_low(U+Th) = 29 and N_high(U+Th) = 74 events per 10^32 proton years.

## Context

This work extends earlier geoneutrino flux calculations by Rothschild (1997) and Raghavan (1997) by using a more detailed crustal map, the PREM density profile, and updated abundance data that yield uranium fluxes 30-50% larger than those of Rothschild (1997). The paper provides a systematic uncertainty framework that enables future experiments like KamLAND and Borexino to interpret geoneutrino signals in terms of Earth's radiogenic heat production and to distinguish between models where radiogenic heating accounts for all or only part of the terrestrial heat flow, as previously discussed by Fiorentini (2002, 2003).
