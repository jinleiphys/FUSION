# From Full Dynamic to Pure Static: A Family of $GW$-Based Approximations

> We introduce a systematic hierarchy of one-body Green's function methods derived from the $GW$ approximation, constructed by progressively reducing the dynamical content of the self-energy. Starting from the fully dynamical Dyson formulation, we generate a family of approximations that interpolates between the standard $GW$ approximation to purely static effective single-particle Hamiltonians. This framework enables a controlled investigation of the role of dynamical effects and particle-hole coupling in the description of ionization potentials. Within this unified formalism, the hole and particle branches can be selectively decoupled through downfolding strategies into reduced one-particle spaces. By benchmarking the different members of this hierarchy on molecular ionization energies, we assess their accuracy, numerical robustness, and algorithmic complexity. We demonstrate that consistently derived partially static schemes can yield reliable quasiparticle energies while significantly simplifying the underlying eigenvalue problem. We further introduce a novel static Hermitian self-energy obtained as the static limit of this hierarchy. Despite its conceptually distinct origin, it produces results remarkably close to those of qs$GW$, thereby providing an alternative static route toward partial self-consistency.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a systematic hierarchy of approximations derived from the $GW$ framework by progressively reducing the dynamical content of the self-energy, and demonstrates that partially static schemes can yield reliable quasiparticle energies while significantly simplifying the eigenvalue problem, provided they are derived consistently rather than through ad hoc truncations.

## Method

Starting from the supermatrix representation of the $GW$ approximation, the authors construct a family of approximations by selectively downfolding the two-hole-one-particle (2h1p) and two-particle-one-hole (2p1h) configuration spaces onto either the full 1h+1p space, a reduced 1h or 1p space, or a single diagonal element. The dynamical content is reduced by evaluating specific branches of the self-energy at a symmetric energy point $\omega = (\epsilon_p + \epsilon_q)/2$, yielding static Hermitian contributions, while retaining full frequency dependence for the other branch. The hierarchy is benchmarked on 58 valence ionization potentials from small molecular systems in the aug-cc-pVTZ basis, comparing against fully dynamical $GW$ results and theoretical best estimates from full configuration interaction.

## Key numbers

- The difference between the full dynamical 1h+1p treatment and the diagonal approximation has a mean absolute error (MAE) of 0.015 eV, a mean signed error (MSE) of -0.007 eV, and a maximum absolute error of 0.118 eV.
- Restricting the reference space to 1h only yields MAE and MSE values of -0.012 eV relative to the 1h+1p treatment.
- The non-Dyson 1h half-and-half scheme yields a MAE of 0.021 eV, a MSE of -0.021 eV, and a maximum absolute error of 0.075 eV when compared to the fully dynamical 1h+1p results.
- With regularization (flow parameter $s = 500$), the 1h+1p half-and-half scheme shows MAE/MSE/Max of 0.014/0.001/0.076 eV relative to the fully dynamical 1h+1p results.
- The fully dynamical 1h+1p treatment yields a MAE of 0.453 eV and MSE of 0.406 eV when compared to theoretical best estimates.

## Context

This work extends the supermatrix formulation of the $GW$ approximation developed by Bintrim (2021), Monino (2022), and others, and provides a unified perspective on the relationship between Dyson and non-Dyson schemes, the latter being related to the algebraic-diagrammatic construction (ADC) framework of Schirmer (1982, 2018). The hierarchy enables a controlled investigation of the role of dynamical effects and particle-hole coupling, and the authors introduce a novel static Hermitian self-energy whose results are remarkably close to those of qs$GW$, providing an alternative static route toward partial self-consistency.
