# Measurement of neutron capture on $^{48}$Ca at thermal and thermonuclear energies

> At the Karlsruhe pulsed 3.75\,MV Van de Graaff accelerator the thermonuclear $^{48}$Ca(n,$γ$)$^{49}$Ca(8.72\,min) cross section was measured by the fast cyclic activation technique via the 3084.5\,keV $γ$-ray line of the $^{49}$Ca-decay. Samples of CaCO$_3$ enriched in $^{48}$Ca by 77.87\,\% were irradiated between two gold foils which served as capture standards. The capture cross-section was measured at the neutron energies 25, 151, 176, and 218\,keV, respectively. Additionally, the thermal capture cross-section was measured at the reactor BR1 in Mol, Belgium, via the prompt and decay $γ$-ray lines using the same target material. The $^{48}$Ca(n,$γ$)$^{49}$Ca cross-section in the thermonuclear and thermal energy range has been calculated using the direct-capture model combined with folding potentials. The potential strengths are adjusted to the scattering length and the binding energies of the final states in $^{49}$Ca. The small coherent elastic cross section of $^{48}$Ca+n is explained through the nuclear Ramsauer effect. Spectroscopic factors of $^{49}$Ca have been extracted from the thermal capture cross-section with better accuracy than from a recent (d,p) experiment. Within the uncertainties both results are in agreement. The non-resonant thermal and thermonuclear experimental data for this reaction can be reproduced using the direct-capture model. A possible interference with a resonant contribution is discussed. The neutron spectroscopic factors of $^{49}$Ca determined from shell-model calculations are compared with the values extracted from the experimental cross sections for $^{48}$Ca(d,p)$^{49}$Ca and $^{48}$Ca(n,$γ$)$^{49}$Ca.

## Key claim

The non-resonant direct-capture model can reproduce the thermal and thermonuclear experimental data for the $^{48}$Ca(n,$\gamma$)$^{49}$Ca reaction, but the measured cross-section at 25 keV is about 25% lower than the calculated direct-capture cross-section and the 1/v extrapolation from thermal, possibly due to destructive interference with a resonance that was not observed in prior time-of-flight measurements.

## Method

The thermal capture cross-section was measured at the BR1 reactor in Mol, Belgium, using a Ge crystal to detect prompt and decay gamma-rays from $^{49}$Ca, with calibration via the $^{35}$Cl(n,$\gamma$) standard reaction. The thermonuclear cross-sections at 25, 151, 176, and 218 keV were measured at the Karlsruhe pulsed 3.75 MV Van de Graaff accelerator using the fast cyclic activation technique, with $^{48}$CaCO$_3$ samples enriched to 77.87% sandwiched between gold foils serving as capture standards. The data were analyzed using the direct-capture model combined with folding potentials, with potential strengths adjusted to the scattering length and binding energies of final states in $^{49}$Ca.

## Key numbers

- Thermal capture cross-section: $\sigma_\gamma = (0.982 \pm 0.046)$ barn, measured via prompt and decay gamma-ray lines from $^{49}$Ca
- Thermonuclear capture cross-sections measured at neutron energies: 25 keV, 151 keV, 176 keV, and 218 keV
- Emission probability of the 3084.54 keV gamma-ray from $^{49}$Ca decay: $f = 0.921 \pm 0.010$
- Emission probability of the 4071.9 keV gamma-ray from $^{49}$Ca decay: $f = 0.070 \pm 0.007$
- Half-life of $^{49}$Ca: $(8.716 \pm 0.011)$ minutes
- Neutron scattering length on $^{48}$Ca: $b = (0.36 \pm 0.09)$ fm
- Coherent elastic scattering cross-section: $\sigma^{\rm coh} = (16.3 \pm 8.1)$ mb
- Thermonuclear reaction-rate factor: $N_{A}\left<\sigma v \right> = 1.28 \times 10^5 \,{\rm cm}^3\,{\rm mole}^{-1}\,{\rm s}^{-1}$
- Spectroscopic factors extracted from thermal capture for the $3/2^-$ ground state and $1/2^-$ first excited state at 2023 keV, with uncertainties smaller than those from a recent (d,p) experiment

## Context

This work extends prior measurements by Cranston and White (1971) at thermal energy and by Kaeppeler et al. (1985) at thermonuclear energies, finding a thermal cross-section 10% lower than the earlier value. The results confirm the direct-capture model calculations of Krausmann et al. (1996) and demonstrate that resonant compound-nucleus contributions are negligible up to at least 220 keV. The extracted spectroscopic factors and the calculated reaction-rate factor enable extrapolation of neutron capture rates to neutron-rich unstable target nuclei in the mass range A=36 to A=66, which is essential for explaining isotopic anomalies in Ca-Al-rich inclusions of primitive meteorites.
