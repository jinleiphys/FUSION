# Viscosity at RHIC

> A variety of physical phenomena can lead to viscous effects. In this talk we review several sources of shear and bulk viscosity with an emphasis on the bulk viscosity associated with chiral restoration. We show that in the limit of a second order phase transition, the viscosity peaks in a singularity at the critical point.

# Viscosity at RHIC

## Key claim
The paper reviews multiple sources of shear and bulk viscosity in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, with a specific focus on bulk viscosity arising from chiral restoration. The authors show that in the limit of a second-order phase transition, the bulk viscosity peaks in a singularity at the critical point, but note that the Navier-Stokes expression is only applicable for small expansion rates and that Israel-Stewart equations would result in moderated effects.

## Method
The authors derive Kubo relations for shear and bulk viscosity from linear response theory, then review five physical sources of viscosity: non-zero mean free paths, non-zero interaction range, classical electric fields, non-equilibrium chemistry, and dynamic mean fields. They calculate the bulk viscosity in a linear sigma model with quarks, assuming a width Gamma = 400 MeV and a chemical equilibration time tau_chem = 1 fm/c, for a Bjorken expansion with an isentropic expansion starting at epsilon = 8 GeV/fm^3 and tau = 1 fm/c.

## Key numbers
- For the linear sigma model with coupling g = g_c = 3.554, the transition is second order with T_c = 172 MeV
- The bulk viscosity was calculated assuming width Gamma = 400 MeV and chemical equilibration time tau_chem = 1 fm/c for a density of one quark per fm^3
- The isentropic expansion starts with epsilon = 8 GeV/fm^3 at tau = 1 fm/c
- For a Bjorken expansion, the expansion rate is given by nabla dot u = 1/tau

## Context
This work extends prior studies of viscosity in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, where experimental observations of large elliptic flow pointed to small shear viscosity and inspired the term "perfect liquid" (RHIC Whitepapers). The paper builds on earlier Boltzmann calculations by Cheng (2001) showing that finite-range scattering kernels affect elliptic flow by a factor of 2, and on the linear sigma model work by Paech (2003, 2005). The authors note that Israel-Stewart equations (Israel 1979, Heinz 2005) would give identical results for small velocity gradients but moderated effects compared to Navier-Stokes. The findings could help explain HBT measurements showing rapid expansion with sudden disintegration (Retiere 2003).
