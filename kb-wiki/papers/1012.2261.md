# Comparing parton energy loss models

> The similarities and differences between three commonly used formalisms for radiative parton energy loss in hot strongly interacting matter are discussed. The single gluon emission spectra are evaluated for a model system consisting of a homogeneous medium with a fixed length, the `TECHQM brick'. Sizable quantitative differences are found and the origins of these differences are discussed.

## Key claim
The three commonly used formalisms for radiative parton energy loss (BDMPS/ASW, GLV/opacity expansion, and AMY) show sizable quantitative differences in single gluon emission spectra when evaluated on the common "TECHQM brick" model system, and these differences can be attributed to specific approximations regarding large-angle radiation and interference effects between vacuum- and medium-induced radiation.

## Method
The single gluon emission spectra from three radiative energy loss formalisms (multiple-soft scattering BDMPS/ASW, opacity expansion GLV, and Hard-Thermal-Loop AMY) were evaluated for a homogeneous medium of fixed length (the "TECHQM brick") with temperature T = 300 MeV and length L = 2 or 5 fm. The transport coefficient q̂ for the BDMPS and ASW calculations was set using the q ≪ T limit of the HTL scattering rate to enable comparison with AMY. The spectra were compared as functions of gluon momentum k and transverse momentum k⊥, with attention to kinematic limits and finite-length effects.

## Key numbers
- Medium temperature T = 300 MeV, with Debye screening mass μ² = g²T² = 0.34 GeV² and mean free path λ = 1.0 fm
- Medium lengths compared: L = 2 fm and L = 5 fm
- The qmax used in the opacity expansion calculation: qmax = √(3 μ T)
- Hadron production at RHIC is suppressed by a factor 4–5 compared to expectations from independent superposition of nucleon-nucleon collisions

## Context
This work extends the comparison of parton energy loss formalisms initiated by the TECHQM collaboration, following earlier work by Baier, Dokshitzer, Mueller, Peigné and Schiff (1990s), Salgado and Wiedemann (2000), Gyulassy, Levai, and Vitev (1999), and Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2002). The paper identifies that the N=1 opacity expansion is most accurate for short path lengths while AMY and BDMPS/ASW-MS agree better with the full path integral for longer path lengths, as shown by Caron-Huot and Gale (2008). The results enable future calculations to include all known interference terms and to move beyond the collinear approximation using Monte-Carlo approaches such as those by Zapp (2008) and Armesto (2007, 2009).
