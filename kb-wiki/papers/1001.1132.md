# The parton bubble model compared to central Au Au collisions (0% to 5%) at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$=200 GeV

> In an earlier paper we developed a Parton Bubble Model (PBM) for RHIC, high-energy heavy-ion collisions. PBM was based on a substructure of a ring of localized bubbles (gluonic hot spots) which initially contain 3-4 partons composed of almost entirely gluons. The bubbles emitted correlated particles at kinetic freezeout, leading to a lumpy fireball surface. For a selection of charged particles (0.8 GeV/c $<$ $p_t$ $<$ 4.0 GeV/c), the PBM reasonably quantitatively (within a few percent) explained high precision RHIC experimental correlation analyses in a manner which was consistent with the small observed HBT source size in this transverse momentum range. We demonstrated that surface emission from a distributed set of surface sources (as in the PBM) was necessary to obtain this consistency. In this paper we give a review of the above comparison to central Au Au collisions. The bubble formation can be associated with gluonic objects predicted by a Glasma Flux Tube Model (GFTM) that formed longitudinal flux tubes in the transverse plane of two colliding sheets of Color Glass Condensate (CGC), which pass through one another. These sheets create boost invariant flux tubes of longitudinal color electric and magnetic fields. A blast wave gives the tubes near the surface transverse flow in the same way it gave transverse flow to the bubbles in the PBM. In the GFTM the longitudinal color electric and magnetic fields have a non-zero topological charge density $F \widetilde F$. These fields cause a local strong CP violation which effects charged particle production coming from quarks and anti-quarks created in the tube or bubble.

# The parton bubble model compared to central Au Au collisions (0% to 5%) at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$=200 GeV

## Key claim

The Parton Bubble Model (PBM) can explain high-precision RHIC correlation data and HBT source size measurements for central Au Au collisions at 200 GeV within a few percent, provided that the emitting sources are a distributed ring of surface bubbles rather than a single small source. The bubble substructure can be directly connected to the Glasma Flux Tube Model (GFTM), where flux tubes near the fireball surface expand from an initial transverse size of ~1/4 fm to ~2 fm at kinetic freezeout.

## Method

The PBM places a ring of twelve adjacent 2-fm-radius spherical bubbles (gluonic hot spots) on an 8 fm radius ring perpendicular to the beam axis at mid-rapidity, each containing 3-4 partons composed almost entirely of gluons. The bubbles are located on the expanding fireball surface treated in a blast wave model moving at maximum velocity 3c/4 at freezeout. PYTHIA fragmentation functions generate final state particles from the bubble partons, with HIJING providing background particles (with jets removed due to strong jet quenching). Two-particle correlation functions C(Δφ, Δη) are constructed from same-event and mixed-event pairs for charged particles with transverse momentum 0.8 GeV/c < p_t < 4.0 GeV/c, and compared to STAR experimental data for 0-5% centrality Au Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Bubble radius: ~2 fm at kinetic freezeout
- Ring radius: ~8 fm, composed of twelve adjacent bubbles
- Each bubble contains 3-4 partons, determined from STAR correlation data
- Blast wave surface velocity at freezeout: 3c/4
- Transverse momentum selection range: 0.8 GeV/c < p_t < 4.0 GeV/c
- HBT source radii reduce from ~6 fm at p_t ~0.2 GeV/c to ~2 fm at p_t ~1 GeV/c
- Average observed correlation angles: approximately 30° in Δφ, 70° in Δη
- Initial flux tube transverse size in GFTM: ~1/4 fm (since Q_s ~1 GeV/c)
- Initial flux tube φ angle wedge: ~1/20 radians or ~3°
- Initial flux tube radius at surface: ~5 fm
- For triggered ridge study: trigger particle p_t between 3.0-4.0 GeV/c correlated with particles having p_t between 1.1-3.0 GeV/c
- 4% of central Au Au events (0-5%) at √s_NN = 200 GeV have a charged particle with p_t between 3.0-4.0 GeV/c with at least one other charged particle above 1.1 GeV/c from the same jet (after 80% jet reduction)
- Jet reduction of 80% corresponds to single π^0 suppression

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier Parton Bubble Model (Longacre, 2008) and its extension PBME (Longacre, 2009) which explained correlation data across centralities. The model connects to the Glasma Flux Tube Model developed by Dumitru and collaborators, which describes flux tubes formed from Color Glass Condensate sheets creating longitudinal color electric and magnetic fields with non-zero topological charge density. The paper builds on Van Hove's early 1980s work on QGP bubbles and the work of Kharzeev and Pisarski on CP-odd bubbles. The PBM explains both the HBT measurements from the STAR experiment and the angular correlation data from central production analyses, and enables predictions for the ridge phenomenon observed in triggered events and for signatures of strong CP violation from the glasma fields.
