# Excitation of $^{229m}$Th at Inelastic Scattering of Low Energy Electrons

> Excitation of the anomalously low lying nuclear isomer $^{229m}$Th$(3/2^+, 8.28 \pm 0.17$ eV) in the process of inelastic electron scattering is studied theoretically in the framework of the perturbation theory for the quantum electrodynamics. The calculated cross sections of $^{229m}$Th by the extremely low energy electrons in the range 9 eV--12 eV for the Th atom and Th$^{1+,4+}$ ions lie in the range $10^{-25}$--$10^{-26}$ cm$^2$. Being so large, the cross section opens up new possibilities for the effective non-resonant excitation of $^{229m}$Th in experiments with an electron beam or electron (electric) current. This can be crucial, since the energy of the isomeric state is currently known with an accuracy insufficient for the resonant excitation by photons. In addition, the cross section of the time reversed process is also large, and as a consequence, the probability of the non-radiative $^{229m}$Th decay via the conduction electrons in metal is $\approx10^{6}$ s$^{-1}$, that is, close to the internal conversion probability in the Th atom.

# Excitation of $^{229m}$Th at Inelastic Scattering of Low Energy Electrons

## Key claim

The calculated cross sections for inelastic electron scattering excitation of the $^{229m}$Th isomer are large enough ($10^{-25}$--$10^{-26}$ cm$^2$) to enable effective non-resonant excitation, which is crucial because the isomer energy of $8.28 \pm 0.17$ eV is currently known with insufficient accuracy for resonant photon excitation. The cross section for the time-reversed process implies a non-radiative decay probability via conduction electrons in metal of approximately $10^6$ s$^{-1}$, comparable to the internal conversion probability in the Th atom.

## Method

The cross section for inelastic electron scattering from the $^{229}$Th nucleus was calculated using second-order perturbation theory in quantum electrodynamics, with the Feynman diagram describing the exchange of a virtual photon between the electron and nuclear currents. The Dirac equations for electron wave functions were solved numerically using self-consistent electron shell potentials obtained from density functional theory (DFT) for the Th atom and Th$^{1+,4+}$ ions, with the nuclear current matrix elements expressed through reduced transition probabilities $B(M1)$ and $B(E2)$ from two data sets based on experimental data and modern nuclear model calculations.

## Key numbers

- Isomeric state energy: $E_{\text{is}} = 8.28 \pm 0.17$ eV
- Calculated cross sections for inelastic electron scattering in the energy range 9 eV--12 eV for Th atom and Th$^{1+,4+}$ ions: $10^{-25}$--$10^{-26}$ cm$^2$
- Probability of non-radiative $^{229m}$Th decay via conduction electrons in metal: $\approx 10^6$ s$^{-1}$
- Reaction rate $\langle \sigma(E_e) v(E_e) \rangle$ for Maxwellian electron distribution: $10^{-18}$--$10^{-17}$ cm$^3$ s$^{-1}$
- Estimated fraction of excited nuclei in laser plasma: $N_{\text{is}}/N_{\text{gr}} \approx 10^{-6}$ for electron density $n_e \approx 10^{19}$--$10^{20}$ cm$^{-3}$ and pulse duration $\tau \approx 10^{-8}$--$10^{-9}$ s
- Estimated excitation rate in solids with implanted nuclei density $\rho_{\text{Th}} = 10^{18}$--$10^{19}$ cm$^{-3}$, target thickness $h = 10$ nm, and current $j_e = 1$ A: $dN_{\text{is}}/dt \approx 10^5$--$10^6$ s$^{-1}$
- Reduced probabilities used: Set 1: $B_{\text{W.u.}}(M1, 5/2^+ \rightarrow 3/2^+) = 0.031$, $B_{\text{W.u.}}(E2, 5/2^+ \rightarrow 3/2^+) = 11.7$; Set 2: $B_{\text{W.u.}}(M1, 5/2^+ \rightarrow 3/2^+) = 0.0076$, $B_{\text{W.u.}}(E2, 5/2^+ \rightarrow 3/2^+) = 27$
- Internal conversion coefficient for $^{229m}$Th: $\alpha = 1.6 \times 10^9$
- Radiation width of isomeric transition: $\Gamma_{\text{is}}^{\text{rad}} \approx 3.6 \times 10^{-19}$ eV

## Context

This work extends theoretical studies of $^{229m}$Th excitation, which previously focused on resonant laser excitation via the electron bridge process (Tkalya 1992, Porsev 2010, Muller 2019, Borisyuk 2019) and nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) (Goldanskii 1976, Doolen 1978). The non-resonant inelastic electron scattering approach avoids the need for precise knowledge of the isomer energy, which has been measured over decades from $\leq 100$ eV (Kroger 1976) to the current $8.28 \pm 0.17$ eV (Seiferle 2019). The results enable new experimental schemes for $^{229m}$Th excitation in laser plasma, solid-state targets with electric current, and high-current electron beams, as well as studies of the isomer decay in metallic environments where the decay probability via conduction electrons approaches the internal conversion rate in the neutral atom (Strizhov 1991, Seiferle 2017).
