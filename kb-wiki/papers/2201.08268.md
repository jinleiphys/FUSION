# On the Stability of Superheavy Nuclei

> Potential energy surfaces of even-even superheavy nuclei are evaluated within the macroscopic-microscopic approximation. A very rapidly converging analytical Fourier-type shape parametrization is used to describe nuclear shapes throughout the periodic table, including those of fissioning nuclei. The Lublin Strasbourg Drop and another effective liquid-drop type mass formula are used to determine the macroscopic part of nuclear energy. The Yukawa-folded single-particle potential, the Strutinsky shell-correction method, and the BCS approximation for including pairing correlations are used to obtain microscopic energy corrections. The evaluated nuclear binding energies, fission-barrier heights, and Q-alpha energies show a relatively good agreement with the experimental data. A simple one-dimensional WKB model a la Swiatecki is used to estimate spontaneous fission lifetimes, while alpha-decay probabilities are obtained within a Gamow-type model.

## Key claim

The macroscopic-microscopic model, using either the Lublin-Strasbourg Drop (LSD) or the Moretto-type liquid-drop (MLD) mass formula, predicts fission-barrier heights and Q_alpha energies for superheavy nuclei that agree reasonably well with experimental data, but the predicted magic numbers (e.g., Z=114, N=178) and shell corrections are contingent on the single-particle potential used, and the barrier heights vanish for nuclei with A above approximately 310.

## Method

Potential energy surfaces (PES) of even-even superheavy nuclei with Z=104–128 and A=250–324 were evaluated in a 4D deformation space (elongation q2, non-axiality η, left-right asymmetry q3, neck formation q4) using a Fourier-type shape parametrization. The macroscopic energy was computed with both the LSD and MLD mass formulas, while microscopic shell and pairing corrections were obtained from the Yukawa-folded single-particle potential, the Strutinsky shell-correction method, and the BCS approximation with particle-number projection. Spontaneous fission lifetimes were estimated via a one-dimensional WKB model with a constant-mass-parameter transformation, and alpha-decay probabilities were computed using a Gamow-type model.

## Key numbers

- The r.m.s. deviation between experimental fission-barrier heights and the difference of the LSD saddle mass and measured ground-state mass (topographical theorem) is only about 310 keV for even-even actinide nuclei.
- The highest fission barriers exceeding 8 MeV are found in the region with Z=112–118 and A=280–294; above A≈310 the barriers practically vanish.
- The proton shell-correction energy at the magic number Z=114 reaches approximately -6 MeV for the Yukawa-folded potential and -3.5 MeV for the Woods-Saxon potential.
- The neutron shell-correction energy at the magic number N=178 is around -4 MeV for the Yukawa-folded potential and -5.5 MeV for the Woods-Saxon potential.
- The difference between MLD and LSD mass estimates is smaller than 0.2 MeV in actinide nuclei and does not exceed 0.6 MeV around the heaviest known superheavy nuclei.
- The difference between MLD and LSD liquid-drop fission-barrier heights does not exceed 0.2 MeV in the superheavy region.

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic approach of Möller et al. (1995) and the LSD model of Pomorski and Dudek (2003) to the superheavy region, using a Fourier shape parametrization previously shown to converge rapidly for fissioning shapes (Pomorski et al., 2015; Schunck et al., 2017). The results are compared with the Swiatecki (1955) systematics of spontaneous fission lifetimes and with experimental data from the NUDAT database. The study enables more reliable predictions of ground-state masses, fission-barrier heights, and decay half-lives for yet-unsynthesized superheavy nuclei, and highlights the model dependence of predicted magic numbers when comparing with self-consistent Skyrme (SIII, SkM*) and Gogny (D1S) calculations.
