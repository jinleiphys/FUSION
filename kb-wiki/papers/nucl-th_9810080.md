# Phenomenology of Large N_c QCD

> These lectures are designed to introduce the methods and results of large N_c QCD in a presentation intended for nuclear and particle physicists alike. Beginning with definitions and motivations of the approach, we demonstrate that all quark and gluon Feynman diagrams are organized into classes based on powers of 1/N_c. We then show that this result can be translated into definite statements about mesons and baryons containing arbitrary numbers of constituents. In the mesons, numerous well-known phenomenological properties follow as immediate consequences of simply counting powers of N_c, while for the baryons, quantitative large N_c analyses of masses and other properties are seen to agree with experiment, even when ``large'' N_c is set equal to its observed value of 3. Large N_c reasoning is also used to explain some simple features of nuclear interactions.

## Key claim

Large $N_c$ QCD provides a systematic expansion parameter $1/N_c$ that organizes all Feynman diagrams into a topological hierarchy, and this expansion appears to work phenomenologically even when the expansion parameter is set to $1/3$ (the physical value $N_c=3$), though the ultimate justification is whether calculations with $N_c$ as a free parameter set to 3 at the end agree with experimental measurements.

## Method

The paper develops large $N_c$ power counting by analyzing Feynman diagrams in 't Hooft's double-line notation, where quarks are represented by single lines and gluons by double lines. The scaling $g_s \propto 1/\sqrt{N_c}$ is derived from the requirement that QCD Green functions have smooth, finite limits as $N_c \to \infty$, using both topological arguments (Euler character of the diagram as a polyhedron) and the renormalization group equation. The resulting counting shows that planar diagrams with no internal quark loops dominate at leading order $N_c^{2-2G-B}$, where $G$ is genus and $B$ is number of quark-loop boundaries, with each nonplanarity costing $1/N_c^2$ and each internal quark loop costing $1/N_c$.

## Key numbers

* The gauge coupling scales as $g_s \propto 1/\sqrt{N_c}$ in the large $N_c$ limit, derived from requiring nontrivial and sensible QCD Green functions.
* Meson masses scale as $m_n^2 = O(N_c^0)$ and meson decay constants scale as $f_n = O(\sqrt{N_c})$, obtained from equating the sum of planar two-point diagrams ($O(N_c^1)$) to a sum of meson propagators.
* The 3-meson vertex scales as $1/\sqrt{N_c}$, while the amplitude to create 2 mesons from the vacuum scales as $N_c^0$.
* The general $g$-glueball, $m$-meson, $h$-hybrid vertex scales as $N_c^{1-g-m/2-h/2}$ for $m>0$ or $h>0$, and as $N_c^{2-g}$ when $m=h=0$.
* Glueball decay constants scale as $N_c^1$ and glueball masses scale as $N_c^0$.
* The glueball-meson coupling scales as $1/\sqrt{N_c}$, so glueballs and mesons do not mix at leading order.
* The meson-hybrid coupling is $O(N_c^0)$, meaning conventional and hybrid mesons mix freely in large $N_c$.
* The mass difference $m^2(\eta_1) - m^2(\eta_8) = O(1/N_c, m_s)$, explaining why the flavor singlet and octet mesons have comparable masses.

## Context

This lecture paper reviews and synthesizes the large $N_c$ approach to QCD originally developed by 't Hooft (1974) and extended to baryons by Witten, with later quantitative baryon analyses by Manohar (1997 Les Houches lectures) and Jenkins. The work extends the topological classification of Feynman diagrams to derive scaling rules for all hadronic vertices, showing that mesons become free, stable, and non-interacting in the $N_c \to \infty$ limit, and that the OZI rule, the dominance of resonance-mediated decays, and the near-degeneracy of the $\eta$ and $\eta^\prime$ masses all follow from $1/N_c$ counting. The 't Hooft model in 1+1 dimensions is cited as an exactly soluble example where confinement emerges automatically.
