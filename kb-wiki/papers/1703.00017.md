# Broken boost invariance in the Glasma via finite nuclei thickness

> We simulate the creation and evolution of non-boost-invariant Glasma in the early stages of heavy ion collisions within the color glass condensate framework. This is accomplished by extending the McLerran-Venugopalan model to include a parameter for the Lorentz-contracted but finite width of the nucleus in the beam direction. We determine the rapidity profile of the Glasma energy density, which shows deviations from the boost-invariant result. Varying the parameters both broad and narrow profiles can be produced. We compare our results to experimental data from RHIC and find surprising agreement.

## Key claim
Gaussian rapidity profiles of energy density in the Glasma can arise already from purely classical 3+1 dimensional CGC simulations if the incoming nuclei have a finite longitudinal thickness, without requiring next-to-leading order JIMWLK evolution. The width of these profiles depends crucially on both the collision energy and the infrared regulator, and the authors note their results should be regarded as more qualitative than quantitative.

## Method
The authors extended the McLerran-Venugopalan model by introducing a Gaussian longitudinal profile function f(x⁻) with thickness parameter L for the nuclear color charge density, then simulated the full 3+1 dimensional Yang-Mills evolution in the laboratory frame using a colored particle-in-cell (CPIC) method on a tetragonal lattice. They solved the Yang-Mills equations in covariant gauge, transformed to temporal gauge via Wilson lines, and evolved the system forward in time, recording the energy-momentum tensor components and computing the local rest frame energy density as a function of space-time rapidity η_s at proper time τ₀ = 1 fm/c.

## Key numbers
- For √s_NN = 200 GeV with m = 0.2 GeV, the Gaussian fit to the local rest frame energy density profile gives σ_η = 2.34, compared to the experimental pion multiplicity width σ_exp = 2.25 and the Landau model prediction σ_Landau = √(ln γ) ≈ 2.15.
- For √s_NN = 200 GeV with m = 0.4 GeV, σ_η = 1.66; with m = 0.8 GeV, σ_η = 1.28.
- For √s_NN = 130 GeV with m = 0.2 GeV, σ_η = 1.87; with m = 0.4 GeV, σ_η = 1.33; with m = 0.8 GeV, σ_η = 1.10; the Landau model gives σ_Landau ≈ 2.04.
- The simulation used 2048 cells in the longitudinal direction over 6 fm, 192² cells in the transverse plane over (6 fm)², and averaged over 15 events.
- The infrared regulator m was tested at values 0.2 GeV, 0.4 GeV, and 0.8 GeV; the UV cutoff was set to Λ = 10 GeV.

## Context
This work extends the classical CGC framework beyond the boost-invariant approximation of Kovner (1995), Krasnitz (1998), and Lappi (2011), which assumed infinitely thin nuclei. It builds on the CPIC method developed by Gelfand (2016) and the suggestion by Ozonder (2013) that finite nuclear thickness could break boost invariance at the classical level. The results show qualitative agreement with the Gaussian rapidity profiles from holographic calculations by Casalderrey-Solana (2013) and van der Schee (2014, 2015), suggesting the difference between weak and strong coupling simulations may stem from initial conditions rather than coupling strength. The authors note their results could serve as initial conditions for hydrodynamic simulations to make direct contact with experimental observables, and that the infrared dependence might be resolved by more realistic JIMWLK-evolved initial conditions.
