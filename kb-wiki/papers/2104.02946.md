# Role of triaxiality in deformed halo nuclei

> It is known that nuclear deformation plays an important role in inducing the halo structure in neutron-rich nuclei by mixing several angular momentum components. While previous theoretical studies on this problem in the literature assume axially symmetric deformation, we here consider non-axially symmetric deformations. With triaxial deformation, the $Ω$ quantum number is admixed in a single-particle wave function, where $Ω$ is the projection of the single-particle angular momentum on the symmetric axis, and the halo structure may arise even when it is absent with the axially symmetric deformation. In this way, the area of halo nuclei may be extended when triaxial deformation is considered. We demonstrate this idea using a deformed Woods-Saxon potential for nuclei with neutron number N=13 and 43.

## Key claim

Triaxial (non-axially symmetric) deformation can induce a halo structure in neutron-rich nuclei even when the halo does not appear with axially symmetric deformation, because the mixing of different Ω quantum numbers in the single-particle wave function allows s-wave components to enter orbitals that would otherwise lack them.

## Method

The authors solved the Schrödinger equation on a 3D mesh for single-particle states in a triaxially deformed Woods-Saxon potential with quadrupole deformation parameters β and γ. They computed the s-wave fraction, the expectation value of j_z, and the root-mean-square radius for the valence neutron orbital as functions of the single-particle energy, for nuclei with neutron numbers N=13 and N=43.

## Key numbers

- For N=43 with β=0.3 and γ=0° or γ=60°, the s-wave fraction P_s is zero; as γ increases from 0° or decreases from 60°, P_s gradually increases.
- At a separation energy S_n=0.2 MeV for N=43, the s-wave component increases significantly with triaxial deformation compared to the axial cases.
- For ¹⁹C (N=13) with β=0.343, the experimental rms radius of 5.5±0.3 fm (Nakamura 1999) and 6.4±0.7 fm (Kanungo 2016) with S_n=160±110 keV (Audi 1993) are simultaneously reproduced for γ=20°–50°, while γ=60° underestimates and γ=0° overestimates the rms radius.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of deformed halo nuclei that assumed axially symmetric deformation, such as Misu (1996), Hamamoto (2004), and Zhou (2010). The finding that triaxiality can produce halo structure where axial deformation cannot suggests that the region of halo nuclei may be enlarged, particularly in medium-heavy and heavy neutron-rich nuclei where recent FRDM calculations (Moller 2008) and symmetry-unrestricted HF+BCS calculations (Ebata 2017) indicate triaxial deformations occur.
