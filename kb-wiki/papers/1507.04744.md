# Dark Matter Velocity Spectroscopy

> Dark matter decays or annihilations that produce line-like spectra may be smoking-gun signals. However, even such distinctive signatures can be mimicked by astrophysical or instrumental causes. We show that velocity spectroscopy-the measurement of energy shifts induced by relative motion of source and observer-can separate these three causes with minimal theoretical uncertainties. The principal obstacle has been energy resolution, but upcoming experiments will reach the required 0.1% level. As an example, we show that the imminent Astro-H mission can use Milky Way observations to separate possible causes of the 3.5-keV line. We discuss other applications.

## Key claim

Velocity spectroscopy, measuring energy shifts due to relative motion between source and observer, can distinguish whether a sharp spectral feature originates from dark matter decay, astrophysical emission, or detector backgrounds, provided the detector achieves energy resolution at the 0.1% level. The method has minimal theoretical uncertainties, but its practical application depends on achieving this resolution threshold.

## Method

The authors model the expected energy shifts and line broadening for three scenarios: dark matter decay in a non-rotating Milky Way halo, astrophysical emission from co-rotating gas in the Galactic disk, and detector backgrounds with zero intrinsic shift. For each scenario, they compute the line-of-sight velocity distribution and resulting Doppler shift as a function of Galactic longitude, then convolve with the detector energy resolution to predict observed line centroids and widths. They apply this framework to the 3.5-keV line using the expected performance of the Astro-H Soft X-Ray Spectrometer (SXS).

## Key numbers

- Required energy resolution for velocity spectroscopy: 0.1% level
- Astro-H SXS goal energy resolution: sigma = 1.7 eV (4 eV FWHM)
- Typical Galactic virial velocities: ~10^-3 c, giving Doppler shifts ~0.1%
- Expected signal events for dark matter at l=20°, |b|=5°: N_s ~ 43 events per 2 Ms exposure
- Background events at same pointing: N_b ~ 16 events per 2 Ms (5.2 CXB + 5.4 MW gas + 5.4 detector)
- Detection significance for line flux at this pointing: > 7 sigma (Poisson probability P(n>=57 | mu=16))
- Uncertainty on LOS velocity at l=20°, |b|=5°: delta_v_LOS ~ 50 km/s
- Separation between dark matter and astrophysical (G2 model) signals at opposite longitudes: ~3.6 sigma with two observations at l = +/-20°
- Dark matter velocity dispersion at r ~ few kpc: sigma_v,r ~ 125 km/s
- Solar orbital speed: v_sun ~ 220 km/s
- Astro-H field of view: 9 arcmin^2; effective area: 200 cm^2
- Effective line width for dark matter: sigma_eff^DM ~ 200 km/s; for gas: sigma_eff^gas ~ 160 km/s; for detector: sigma_eff^det ~ 150 km/s
- If Astro-H resolution is 2.1, 2.5, or 3 eV instead of 1.7 eV, line shift significance becomes ~3.0, 2.4, or 1.9 sigma respectively

## Context

This work extends the concept of velocity spectroscopy, previously applied to the 1.809-MeV line from 26Al by Kretschmer (2013) using INTEGRAL-SPI, to the problem of distinguishing dark matter signals from astrophysical and detector backgrounds. The method directly addresses the disputed origin of the 3.5-keV line reported by Bulbul (2014) and Boyarsky (2014), which has been argued to have astrophysical explanations by Jeltema (2014) and Urban (2014). The paper enables future missions like ATHENA and HERD to incorporate velocity spectroscopy into their design requirements, and encourages the Astro-H Collaboration to perform dedicated studies of dark matter sensitivity across their full 0.3-12 keV energy range.
