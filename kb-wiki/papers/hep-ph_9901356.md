# Irreversibility, steady state, and non-equilibrium physics in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> Heavy ion collisions at ultrarelativistic energies offer the opportunity to study the irreversibility of multiparticle processes. Together with the many-body decays of resonances, the multiparticle processes cause the system to evolve according to Prigogine's steady states rather than towards statistical equilibrium. These results are general and can be easily checked by any microscopic string-, transport-, or cascade model for heavy ion collisions. The absence of pure equilibrium states sheds light on the difficulties of thermal models in describing the yields and spectra of hadrons, especially mesons, in heavy ion collisions at bombarding energies above 10 GeV/nucleon.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions, multiparticle production processes (such as string decays and many-body resonance decays) are irreversible and drive the system toward a Prigogine-type steady state rather than toward full statistical equilibrium, even when necessary conditions for local equilibrium (isotropy of pressure and velocity distributions) are satisfied.

## Method

The authors analyze microscopic Monte Carlo simulations of heavy ion collisions (using string-, transport-, and cascade models) to examine whether the criteria for local equilibrium are met in a central cell of volume 125 fm³. They distinguish between necessary conditions (isotropy of velocity and pressure) and sufficient conditions (time independence of hadronic spectra and yields), and compare the system's properties to those of infinite hadron matter simulated with the same microscopic models. They also discuss the difference between quantum mechanical detailed balance (time-reversal invariance of transition amplitudes) and statistical physics detailed balance (equality of forward and reverse reaction rates), showing that multiparticle processes satisfy the former but not the latter.

## Key numbers

- Central cell volume used for analysis: 125 fm³ (5 × 5 × 5 fm³)
- Time at which kinetic equilibrium (isotropy of pressure and velocity distributions) is attained in the central cell: approximately 10 fm/c, for bombarding energies from 10.7 AGeV (AGS) to 160 AGeV (SPS)
- Bombarding energy threshold above which thermal models have difficulty describing yields and spectra: above 10 GeV/nucleon

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of equilibration in microscopic models, particularly the finding by Geiger (1995) that partonic matter does not fully thermalize and the observation by Bravina et al. (1998) that hadronic matter in the central cell of heavy ion collisions does not reach chemical equilibrium. The paper contradicts the standard assumption of local equilibrium used in thermal and hydrodynamic models dating back to Fermi (1950) and Landau (1953). It explains why statistical models of ideal hadron gas, such as those by Stoecker et al. (1981) and Braun-Munzinger et al. (1996), systematically underestimate pion yields at high energies. The work enables future studies of non-equilibrium thermodynamics in heavy ion collisions and suggests that microscopic models can probe fundamental features of irreversibility at small space and time scales.
