# Physically Interpretable Machine Learning for nuclear masses

> We present a novel approach to modeling the ground state mass of atomic nuclei based directly on a probabilistic neural network constrained by relevant physics. Our Physically Interpretable Machine Learning (PIML) approach incorporates knowledge of physics by using a physically motivated feature space in addition to a soft physics constraint that is implemented as a penalty to the loss function. We train our PIML model on a random set of $\sim$20\% of the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME) and predict the remaining $\sim$80\%. The success of our methodology is exhibited by the unprecedented $σ_\textrm{RMS}\sim186$ keV match to data for the training set and $σ_\textrm{RMS}\sim316$ keV for the entire AME with $Z \geq 20$. We show that our general methodology can be interpreted using feature importance.

## Key claim

The authors present a Physically Interpretable Machine Learning (PIML) approach that achieves unprecedented accuracy in predicting nuclear masses by combining a physically motivated feature space with a soft physics constraint (the Garvey-Kelson relations) implemented as a penalty to the loss function, but the claimed performance depends on training on only about 20% of the Atomic Mass Evaluation data and the soft constraint can temporarily increase during training.

## Method

The authors used a Mixture Density Network (probabilistic neural network) built on the PyTorch framework with six hidden layers, eight hidden nodes, one Gaussian ad-mixture, and eight input features: proton number (Z), neutron number (N), mass number (A), odd-even nature of protons and neutrons, valence numbers of protons and neutrons from nearest closed shells, and isospin asymmetry. They trained on a random set of 450 nuclei from AME2016 with Z >= 20 (approximately 20% of the data) and implemented the Garvey-Kelson relations as a soft physics constraint with weight lambda_phys = 1 in the total loss function, using the Adam optimizer with initial learning rate 0.0002 and weight decay 0.01 for regularization over roughly 10^7 epochs.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square error sigma_RMS ~ 186 keV for the training set (20% coverage of AME2016 data)
- Root-mean-square error sigma_RMS ~ 316 keV for the entirety of AME2016 with Z >= 20
- Predictive capability sigma_RMS ~ 336 keV when comparing to the AME2020 evaluation
- Model uses six hidden layers and eight hidden nodes with one Gaussian ad-mixture
- Physics constraint weight lambda_phys = 1 and initial learning rate 0.0002
- Weight decay set to 0.01 for regularization
- Training performed for roughly 10^7 epochs
- Uncertainty bands diverge to about 3 sigma ~ 1 MeV for extrapolated isotopes

## Context

This work extends the previous machine learning approach of Lovell (2022) by incorporating a probabilistic neural network with physics-based constraints, achieving significantly improved accuracy over that prior work. The PIML approach is competitive with global mass models such as the macroscopic-microscopic FRDM2012 and microscopic models by Goriely (2009), and it enables more robust uncertainty quantification for extrapolations to exotic, short-lived nuclei that cannot be studied in laboratory settings, building on recent efforts using Gaussian processes and Bayesian methods by Neufcourt (2018), Melendez (2019), and Schunck (2020).
