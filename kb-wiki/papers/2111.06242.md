# A modified Brink-Axel hypothesis for astrophysical Gamow-Teller transitions

> Weak interaction charged current transition strengths from highly excited nuclear states are fundamental ingredients for accurate modeling of compact object composition and dynamics, but are difficult to obtain either from experiment or theory. For lack of alternatives, calculations have often fallen back upon a generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis, that is, assuming the strength function (transition probability) is independent of the initial nuclear state but depends only upon the transition energy and the weak interaction properties of the parent nucleus ground state. Here we present numerical evidence for a modified `local' Brink-Axel hypothesis for Gamow-Teller transitions for $pf$-shell nuclei relevant to astrophysical applications. Specifically, while the original Brink-Axel hypothesis does not hold globally, strength functions from initial states nearby in energy are similar within statistical fluctuations. This agrees with previous work on strength function moments. Using this modified hypothesis, we can tackle strength functions at previously intractable initial energies, using semi-converged initial states at arbitrary excitation energy. Our work provides a well-founded method for computing accurate thermal weak transition rates for medium-mass nuclei at temperatures occurring in stellar cores near collapse. We finish by comparing to previous calculations of astrophysical rates.

## Key claim

While the original Brink-Axel hypothesis (that the strength function is independent of the initial nuclear state) does not hold globally for Gamow-Teller transitions in pf-shell nuclei, strength functions from initial states nearby in energy are statistically similar, enabling the use of semi-converged initial states as proxies for fully converged states at high excitation energies.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale configuration-interaction shell model calculations using the GXPF1A interaction in the pf-shell valence space, employing the BIGSTICK code with Lanczos and thick-restart Lanczos algorithms to obtain initial states up to 80 MeV excitation energy for nuclei including 53Fe, 55Fe, 55Cr, 56Fe, and 57Co. They computed Gamow-Teller strength functions from fully converged states up to roughly 5-8 MeV and from semi-converged states at higher energies, then tested the similarity of strength functions within energy bins by examining binned averages and fluctuations. Thermal weak rates for 57Co → 57Fe were computed using the energy-localized Brink-Axel hypothesis and compared to prior calculations by Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo (1999).

## Key numbers

- For 57Co, the total number of levels in the shell model space is 980 million, with a Gaussian centroid at 63.4 MeV above ground state and width 12.55 MeV
- At a temperature of kT = 1 MeV (approximately 12 × 10^9 K), a typical excitation energy for A=57 nuclei is E_x ≈ 7 MeV, using the Fermi gas level density parameter a ≈ A/8 MeV
- The backshifted Fermi gas model parameters used for 57Co are a = 6.5 MeV and δ = 0.7 MeV, with spin-cutoff σ = 3.96
- For 57Co → 57Fe, the authors computed thermal rates up to a temperature of 10^10 K, finding very good agreement with Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo (1999) rates through most of the density and temperature range, with a sudden uptick beyond 10^10 K

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of Brink-Axel hypothesis validity by Misch, Fuller and Brown (sd-shell nuclei) and prior work on strength function moments by Johnson and collaborators, providing numerical evidence for an energy-localized modification in the pf-shell. The method enables computation of thermal weak transition rates for medium-mass nuclei at temperatures occurring in stellar cores near collapse, improving upon the approach of Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo (1999) who could only compute 4-12 parent eigenstates (up to 1-2 MeV excitation) and relied on the unmodified Brink-Axel hypothesis. The results open a path to more microscopic foundations for computing weak rates in extreme astrophysical environments, particularly for temperatures above 10^10 K where previous calculations may need revision.
