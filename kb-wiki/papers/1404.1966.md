# Neutron stars as probes of extreme energy density matter

> Neutron stars have long been regarded as extra-terrestrial laboratories from which we can learn about extreme energy density matter at low temperatures. In this article, I highlight some of the recent advances made in astrophysical observations and related theory. Although the focus is on the much needed information on masses and radii of several individual neutron stars, the need for additional knowledge about the many facets of neutron stars is stressed. The extent to which quark matter can be present in neutron stars is summarized with emphasis on the requirement of non-perturbative treatments. Some longstanding and new questions, answers to which will advance our current status of knowledge, are posed.

## Key claim

Neutron stars serve as extraterrestrial laboratories for studying extreme energy density matter at low temperatures, but the paper emphasizes that reliable extraction of masses and radii, and thus the equation of state, remains contingent on resolving significant uncertainties in atmospheric modeling, distance measurements, and the treatment of non-perturbative quark matter.

## Method

The paper reviews how neutron star masses are measured via pulse-timing techniques in binary systems, including Keplerian parameters and general relativistic effects such as periastron advance, gravitational redshift, orbital decay from gravitational radiation, and Shapiro delay. Radii are inferred from thermal emission of isolated neutron stars, quiescent neutron stars, and type I X-ray bursts undergoing photospheric radius expansion, using spectral fits that depend on atmospheric models and other corrections. The structure of neutron stars is determined by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations with an input equation of state (EOS), and a model-independent deconstruction method is described that uses simultaneous mass and radius measurements to reconstruct the EOS.

## Key numbers

- Observed neutron star masses lie in the range 1–2 solar masses, with radii about 10–15 km, giving GM/c²R ~ 0.1–0.3.
- The pulsar PSR J1614-2230 has a mass of 1.97 ± 0.04 solar masses; PSR J0348+0432 has a mass of 2.01 ± 0.04 solar masses.
- For the 1.97 solar mass neutron star, upper limits on central quantities are: energy density < 1.97 GeV/fm³, pressure < 1.32 GeV/fm³, baryon number density < 1.56 fm⁻³, chemical potential < 2.1 GeV.
- For the maximally compact EOS (causal limit), the maximum mass scales as M_max = 4.09 (ε_s/ε_0)^(1/2) solar masses, where ε_s ≈ 150 MeV/fm³ is the energy density at nuclear saturation density n_0 = 0.16 fm⁻³.
- For the MIT bag model quark matter EOS (s=1/3), M_max = 2.48 (ε_s/ε_0)^(1/2) solar masses and the baryon chemical potential at maximum mass is about 1.46 GeV.
- The isolated neutron star RX J1856.5-3754 has a distance of 120 ± 8 pc; inferred radii range from about 11.7 to 12.2 km depending on atmospheric model, with masses from about 1.33 to 1.86 solar masses.
- The minimum spin period for a maximum-mass configuration is P_min(M_max) = 0.83 (M_max/solar mass)^(-1/2) (R_max/10 km)^(3/2) ms.

## Context

This paper reviews and synthesizes work on neutron star masses and radii, extending earlier compilations by Lattimer (2012) and Prakash (1996, 2005, 2011). It discusses the impact of the high-mass measurements by Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013) that severely restrict the EOS, and examines hybrid star models by Alford (2013) that allow 2 solar mass stars with quark matter cores under specific conditions. The work enables future model-independent EOS determination via the deconstruction method of Lindblom (1992) and Bayesian analyses by Steiner (2010), provided multiple accurate mass-radius measurements become available.
