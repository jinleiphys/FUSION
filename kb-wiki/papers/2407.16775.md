# Investigating the rate of $^{10}$Be(n,$γ$)$^{11}$Be radiative capture reaction within the FRDWBA framework

> This study examines the radiative capture of a neutron by $^{10}$Be using the Coulomb dissociation approach within the FRDWBA theory. We analyze the elastic Coulomb breakup of $^{11}$Be on a $^{208}$Pb target at 72 MeV/A to determine the photodisintegration cross-section and radiative capture cross-section. Utilizing the Maxwell-averaged velocity distribution, we calculate the resulting radiative neutron capture reaction rate for the $^{10}$Be(n,$γ$)$^{11}$Be reaction. Comparative analyses are conducted with experimental data, theoretical results from direct radiative capture methods, and transfer reaction calculations. Additionally, we contrast our findings with the existing $^{10}$Be($α$,$γ$)$^{14}$C reaction rate and conclude the dominance of neutron capture over $α$ capture by $^{10}$Be.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the radiative neutron capture rate for \(^{10}\text{Be}(n,\gamma)^{11}\text{Be}\) can be reliably extracted from the Coulomb dissociation of \(^{11}\text{Be}\) using the FRDWBA framework, and that this neutron capture rate dominates over the \(^{10}\text{Be}(\alpha,\gamma)^{14}\text{C}\) rate under the conditions considered. However, the analysis is limited to pure Coulomb breakup (neglecting nuclear and interference contributions) and relies on a Woods-Saxon bound-state potential with a spectroscopic factor fixed to 1, which introduces model dependence.

## Method

The authors used the finite-range distorted-wave Born approximation (FRDWBA) to model the elastic Coulomb breakup of \(^{11}\text{Be}\) on a \(^{208}\text{Pb}\) target at 72 MeV/A. From the calculated relative energy spectrum, they extracted the dipole strength distribution and the photodisintegration cross-section via the virtual photon number method, then applied the principle of detailed balance to obtain the radiative capture cross-section \(\sigma_{(n,\gamma)}\). Finally, the Maxwell-averaged reaction rate was computed by folding \(\sigma_{(n,\gamma)}\) with a Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution over stellar temperatures.

## Key numbers

* One-neutron separation energy of \(^{11}\text{Be}\): \(S_n = 0.504\) MeV.
* Beam energy for the Coulomb breakup: 72 MeV/A on a \(^{208}\text{Pb}\) target.
* Total one-neutron removal cross-section from pure Coulomb breakup: \(\sigma_{-1n} = 1.76\) b; the experimental value including nuclear effects is \(1.8 \pm 0.4\) b (Nakamura et al., 1994).
* The spectroscopic factor used to reproduce the peak of the relative energy spectrum was taken as 1; a change of 8–20% in the Woods-Saxon radius and diffuseness parameters changed the spectroscopic factor by only 5%.
* The reaction rate was compared with the \(^{10}\text{Be}(\alpha,\gamma)^{14}\text{C}\) rate over the temperature range \(T_9 = 0\)–10, and the neutron capture rate was found to dominate.

## Context

This work extends the Coulomb dissociation method, originally proposed by Baur (1986), to the archetypal one-neutron halo nucleus \(^{11}\text{Be}\), following earlier experimental studies at GSI (Palit et al., 2003) and RIKEN (Nakamura et al., 1994; Fukuda et al., 2004). It builds on previous FRDWBA calculations for neutron-rich nuclei such as \(^{19}\text{C}\), \(^{30}\text{Mg}\), and \(^{34}\text{Na}\) (Singh et al., 2017; Dan et al., 2019; Shubhchintak et al., 2017). The results enable more accurate input for r-process network calculations and inhomogeneous Big Bang nucleosynthesis models, where the \(^{10}\text{Be}(n,\gamma)^{11}\text{Be}\) reaction competes with \(\alpha\)-capture pathways (Terasawa et al., 2001; Kajino et al., 1995).
