# Overlapping resonances in nuclei coupling to the atomic shells

> The resonant process of nuclear excitation by electron transition (NEET) in highly charged ions is investigated. In NEET, a bound electronic decay transition occurs with the simultaneous excitation of the nucleus, provided that the energies of the atomic and nuclear transition match. By varying the atomic charge state, the atomic transition energy can be tuned to a better match of the nuclear transition energy. We propose a new way to create the atomic hole in highly charged ions by dielectronic capture of a free electron. In order to derive the cross section for the three-step process composed by dielectronic capture, NEET, and the subsequent nuclear decay, a Feshbach projection operator formalism is developed. With the help of this formalism, the resonances in nuclei coupling to the atomic shell and the possible interference between several direct and resonant electronic and nuclear processes such as radiative recombination, dielectronic recombination and nuclear excitation by electron capture are described.

## Key claim

The authors propose that using dielectronic capture (DC) in highly charged ions to create the atomic hole for nuclear excitation by electron transition (NEET) can improve the energy match between atomic and nuclear transitions by at least one order of magnitude, potentially increasing the NEET probability by three orders of magnitude, but only for specific ionic configurations where the electronic hole can only be filled by the atomic transition that couples to the nuclear excitation.

## Method

A Feshbach projection operator formalism was developed to derive the cross section for a three-step process consisting of dielectronic capture (DC) of a free electron into a highly charged ion, followed by nuclear excitation by electron transition (NEET), and subsequent radiative decay of the excited nucleus. The formalism separates the Hilbert space into four subspaces (continuum electron states, bound excited electron states with ground state nucleus, excited nucleus states with ground state electrons, and photon states) and treats the electric and magnetic electron-nucleus interactions explicitly using the Dirac equation for high-Z elements. The resonance strength is expressed as a product of the Auger decay rate, the NEET probability, and the nuclear radiative decay rate.

## Key numbers

- The NEET probability for the 77.351 keV nuclear transition in $^{197}\mathrm{Au}$ corresponding to an atomic $K\rightarrow M$ transition in neutral atoms was determined experimentally to be $(5.0\pm 0.6)\times 10^{-8}$ (Kishimoto 2000)
- For the 69.5 keV nuclear transition in $^{189}\mathrm{Os}$, only an upper limit of $4.1\times 10^{-10}$ for the NEET probability could be deduced from experiments (Aoki 2001)
- The energy mismatch between nuclear and atomic transition energies for neutral atoms can be on the order of 1 keV, and varying the charge state can reduce this difference by at least one order of magnitude
- The interference between radiative recombination and DR with the NEET sequence is two to three orders of magnitude larger in cross section than the NEET process itself

## Context

This work extends the theoretical treatment of NEET from neutral atoms (Morita 1973, Tkalya 1992, Ho 1993) and plasmas (Morel 2004) to highly charged ions, where the atomic transition energy can be tuned by varying the charge state. The authors propose using dielectronic capture (known from dielectronic recombination experiments by Brandau 2008) as a new mechanism to create the electronic hole, rather than photoionization used in previous NEET experiments on $^{197}\mathrm{Au}$ (Kishimoto 2000), $^{189}\mathrm{Os}$ (Ahmad 2000, Aoki 2001), and $^{235}\mathrm{U}$ (Claverie 2004). The formalism enables future studies of interference between NEET and other resonant processes such as radiative recombination, dielectronic recombination, and nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC), and may be relevant for dense plasmas where dielectronic recombination is the dominant electron recombination mechanism.
