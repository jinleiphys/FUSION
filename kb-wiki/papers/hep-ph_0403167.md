# What can we learn from neutrinoless double beta decay experiments?

> We assess how well next generation neutrinoless double beta decay and normal neutrino beta decay experiments can answer four fundamental questions. 1) If neutrinoless double beta decay searches do not detect a signal, and if the spectrum is known to be inverted hierarchy, can we conclude that neutrinos are Dirac particles? 2) If neutrinoless double beta decay searches are negative and a next generation ordinary beta decay experiment detects the neutrino mass scale, can we conclude that neutrinos are Dirac particles? 3) If neutrinoless double beta decay is observed with a large neutrino mass element, what is the total mass in neutrinos? 4) If neutrinoless double beta decay is observed but next generation beta decay searches for a neutrino mass only set a mass upper limit, can we establish whether the mass hierarchy is normal or inverted? We base our answers on the expected performance of next generation neutrinoless double beta decay experiments and on simulations of the accuracy of calculations of nuclear matrix elements.

## Key claim

If neutrinoless double beta decay is not observed in next-generation experiments and the neutrino mass hierarchy is known to be inverted, an impractically large number (230 at 99.73% CL) of experiments with Majorana-level sensitivity would be required to conclude that neutrinos are Dirac particles, assuming the actual distribution of calculated nuclear matrix elements. The authors emphasize that this conclusion depends critically on the assumed probability distribution for the nuclear factor F_N, and that readers who are more optimistic about the accuracy of nuclear matrix element calculations may prefer different conclusions.

## Method

The authors performed a systematic quantitative analysis by combining probability distribution functions for the neutrino mass element |⟨m_ee^ν⟩| derived from two sources: (1) the expected sensitivity of next-generation neutrinoless double beta decay experiments (using the Majorana experiment as a reference), and (2) the expression for |⟨m_ee^ν⟩| in terms of neutrino oscillation parameters (mass splittings, mixing angles, and Majorana phases). They treated the nuclear factor F_N as a random variable with different assumed probability distributions (Gaussian, constant, and the actual distribution from 20 published calculations for ⁷⁶Ge), and computed the number of experiments required to reject the Majorana hypothesis at various confidence levels by evaluating the joint probability that the experimental non-observation and the neutrino parameter constraints are simultaneously satisfied.

## Key numbers

* For the case of no detected neutrinoless double beta decay with an inverted hierarchy, 230 experiments (at 99.73% CL) are required to conclude neutrinos are Dirac particles, using the actual distribution of F_N in linear scale.
* If the lightest neutrino mass is measured in ordinary beta decay as m = 1.0 ± 0.05 eV, only 1 experiment (at 99.73% CL) is needed to conclude neutrinos are Dirac particles.
* If the lightest neutrino mass is measured as m = 0.35 ± 0.07 eV, 5 experiments (6 for normal hierarchy) are required at 99.73% CL.
* If the lightest neutrino mass is measured as m = 0.30 ± 0.10 eV, 15 experiments (∞ for normal hierarchy) are required at 99.73% CL.
* For a detected neutrinoless double beta decay half-life of T_{1/2}(⁷⁶Ge) = (3.2 ± 0.2) × 10²⁵ yr, the total neutrino mass is constrained to the range [0.46, 9.56] eV ([0.48, 9.58] eV for normal hierarchy).
* For a detected half-life of T_{1/2}(⁷⁶Ge) = (1.0 ± 0.1) × 10²⁶ yr, the total neutrino mass range is [0.24, 8.34] eV ([0.28, 8.40] eV for normal hierarchy).
* For a detected half-life of T_{1/2}(⁷⁶Ge) = (3.2 ± 0.5) × 10²⁶ yr, the total neutrino mass range is [0.08, 5.68] eV ([0.16, 6.06] eV for normal hierarchy).
* The compiled 20 calculations of the nuclear factor F_N for ⁷⁶Ge span the range 2.7 × 10⁻¹⁵ to 2.9 × 10⁻¹³ yr⁻¹.

## Context

This paper extends the discussion of what can be learned from neutrinoless double beta decay experiments by providing a systematic, quantitative evaluation of the impact of nuclear matrix element uncertainties, which the authors argue had not been done previously. It contrasts with the more optimistic analysis of Bilenky (2004), which assumed the correctness of the renormalized quasi-particle random phase approximation (RQRPA) leading to the lowest nuclear factor values. The results enable experimentalists to understand how many experiments with different isotopes would be required to answer fundamental questions about neutrino nature and mass hierarchy, and highlight that even with detection of neutrinoless double beta decay, determining whether the mass hierarchy is normal or inverted may not be possible from beta decay experiments alone.
