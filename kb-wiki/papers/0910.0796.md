# Saturation and Critical Phenomena in DIS

> It is argued that the expected turn-down in $x- Q^2$ of the cross sections (structure functions $F_2(x,Q^2)$), assumed to result from the saturation of parton densities in the nucleon, is related to a phase transition from the (almost) ideal partonic gas, obeying Bjorken scaling, to a partonic "liquid". This can be quantified in the framework of statistical models, percolation and other approaches to collective phenomena of the strongly interacting matter. Similarities and differences between the case of lepton-hadron, hadron-hadron and nuclear collisions are discussed.

# Saturation and Critical Phenomena in DIS

## Key claim

The expected turn-down in the cross sections (structure functions $F_2(x,Q^2)$) at low $x$ and relevant $Q^2$, assumed to result from saturation of parton densities, is interpreted as a phase transition from an (almost) ideal partonic gas obeying Bjorken scaling to a partonic "liquid". The authors present this as a novel qualitative interpretation but acknowledge that the nature of this transition (first order, second order, or smooth cross-over) is not known and that further studies are needed to quantify the phenomenon.

## Method

The authors propose a thermodynamic and statistical approach to deep inelastic scattering, treating the excited nucleon as a system of partons (quarks, antiquarks, and gluons) in equilibrium at temperature $T$ within a spherical volume $V$ whose radius $R(s)$ increases with energy as $\ln s$ or $\ln^2 s$. They define the saturation line as the turning point of the logarithmic derivatives $B_Q(x,Q^2) = \partial F_2(x,Q^2)/\partial(\ln Q^2)$ and $B_x(x,Q^2) = \partial F_2(x,Q^2)/\partial(\ln(1/x))$, computed from a model structure function that interpolates between Regge behavior at low $Q^2$ and DGLAP evolution at high $Q^2$. They also apply percolation theory, considering extended (dressed) quarks and gluons as spheres that cluster within the nucleon, with two percolation thresholds for cluster formation and vacuum disappearance.

## Key numbers

- Saturation radius from dipole model fit to DIS data at $x < 0.01$: $R_0^2 = (x/x_0)^\lambda / Q_0^2$, with $Q_0^2 = 1$ GeV$^2$, $x_0 = 3 \times 10^{-4}$, and $\lambda = 0.29$.
- First percolation point (partial percolation) for arbitrary overlap of spheres: density $n_m \approx 0.35/V_0$, with largest cluster density about $1.2/V_0$.
- Second percolation point (vacuum disappearance) for arbitrary overlap: $n_v = 1.22/V_0$, when 70% of space is covered by spheres.
- For impenetrable spherical core with $R_c = R_0/2$: first percolation threshold $\bar n_m \approx 0.34/V_0$, vacuum percolation threshold $\bar n_v = 2.0/V_0$.
- Limiting temperature through meson fusion: $T_M(T,Q) \simeq 171 \ln(Q + m_\rho)$ MeV, where $m_\rho$ is the rho meson mass.
- For real photons ($Q=0$), vacuum disappearance density $\bar n_v \approx 0.93 \ln(Q + m_\rho)^3$ fm$^{-3}$ corresponds to about 5.5 times standard nuclear density.
- Limiting baryochemical potential at $T=0$: $\mu_v \simeq 1.12 \ln(Q + m_\rho)$ GeV.

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamic and percolation approaches previously applied to hadronic matter and heavy-ion collisions, particularly the percolation study of the phase diagram by Satz (2009) and the statistical model of parton distributions by Bhalerao (1997), Cleymans, and Soffer. The authors propose that saturation in DIS is analogous to the gas-to-liquid phase transition seen in other contexts, and they note similarities with the color glass condensate picture from heavy-ion collisions, while acknowledging differences such as the absence of hydrodynamic flow in DIS. The paper is a first step toward quantifying this phase transition interpretation, with the authors intending to return to the calculation of finite-volume correction parameters in a subsequent publication.
