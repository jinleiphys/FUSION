# Astromers: Nuclear Isomers in Astrophysics

> We develop a method to compute thermally-mediated transition rates between the ground state and long-lived isomers in nuclei. We also establish criteria delimiting a thermalization temperature above which a nucleus may be considered a single species and below which it must be treated as two separate species: a ground state species, and an astrophysical isomer ("astromer") species. Below the thermalization temperature, the destruction rates dominate the internal transition rates between the ground state and the isomer. If the destruction rates also differ greatly from one another, the nuclear levels fall out of or fail to reach thermal equilibrium. Without thermal equilibrium, there may not be a safe assumption about the distribution of occupation probability among the nuclear levels when computing nuclear reaction rates. In these conditions, the isomer has astrophysical consequences and should be treated a separate astromer species which evolves separately from the ground state in a nucleosynthesis network. We apply our transition rate methods and perform sensitivity studies on a few well-known astromers. We also study transitions in several other isomers of likely astrophysical interest.

## Key claim

The paper develops a method to compute thermally-mediated transition rates between ground states and long-lived isomers in nuclei, and establishes a thermalization temperature threshold: below this temperature, a nucleus must be treated as two separate species (ground state and "astromer") in nucleosynthesis networks because destruction rates dominate internal transition rates and the nuclear levels fall out of thermal equilibrium.

## Method

The authors compute effective transition rates between endpoint states (ground state and isomer) by solving a system of linear equations for the probabilities that intermediate states eventually reach each endpoint without passing through the other endpoint, using spontaneous gamma-decay rates modified by a thermal photon bath via Bose-Einstein statistics. They then construct weighted ensembles for each endpoint state using the "reverse ratio" formalism of Gupta and Meyer (2001), allowing computation of effective beta-decay rates for each ensemble. The method is applied to several nuclei using experimental data supplemented by shell model calculations with NuShellX and OXBASH codes using the usdb interaction, with Weisskopf approximations for unmeasured transition rates.

## Key numbers

- 26Al ground state beta-decay half-life: 717 kyr; isomer beta-decay half-life: 6.346 s
- 26Al isomer energy: 228.305 keV
- 26Al thermalization temperature: between 30 and 40 keV (uncertain range from sensitivity study)
- 26Al: at T ≈ 7 keV, state 3 becomes accessible and transition rates begin to rise; at T ≈ 13 keV, Λ₁₂ surpasses Λ₁β; at T ≈ 17 keV, state 3 contributes significantly to ensemble beta-decay rate; at T ≈ 35 keV, Λ₂₁ surpasses Λβ₂
- 26Al: 67 total states included (2 endpoint states, 65 intermediate states)
- Electron density assumed for all calculations: ρYₑ = 10⁵ g/cm³
- 81Se isomer at 103.00 keV transitions to ground 99.949% of the time

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Ward and Fowler (1980) and Coc et al. (1999) for thermally-mediated transitions in nuclear isomers, and builds on the steady-state method of Gupta and Meyer (2001). The paper provides a rigorous framework for identifying when isomers become "astromers" that must be treated as separate species in nucleosynthesis networks, addressing a known problem with 26Al where the ground state beta-decay half-life (717 kyr) differs dramatically from the isomer half-life (6.346 s). The authors find that the thermalization temperature of 26Al is uncertain within 30-40 keV, contradicting Coc et al. (1999) who concluded this temperature was insensitive to nuclear uncertainties. This work enables more accurate nucleosynthesis calculations by providing data tables for use in reaction networks and a method to assess when isomers have astrophysical consequences.
