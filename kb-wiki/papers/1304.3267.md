## Key claim

The paper claims that approximately 3000 nuclides are currently known, with an additional 3000-4000 predicted to exist, but this estimate is qualified by noting that the known nuclides probably constitute less than 50% of all nuclides that could potentially be observed, and that the discovery of new nuclides depends on continued technical advances in accelerators and detectors.

## Method

The author compiled a comprehensive historical review of all nuclide discoveries by surveying published literature in refereed journals, categorizing discoveries by experimental technique (mass spectroscopy, nuclear reactions with light ions, heavy-ion fusion-evaporation, target and projectile fragmentation) and by year. The review documents 3105 nuclides observed by the end of 2011, listing for each element the total number of isotopes, the lightest and heaviest isotope, and the year of first and most recent discovery.

## Key numbers

- Approximately 3000 nuclides are known at present, with about 3000-4000 predicted to exist
- 3105 nuclides had been observed by the end of 2011
- About 7000 nuclides are calculated to be bound with respect to neutron or proton emission (Erler 2012)
- The element with the most isotopes (46) is mercury, followed by thallium, lead and polonium with 42 each
- The heaviest nuclides are 294117 and 294118, though the observation of elements 117 and 118 has not been accepted by IUPAC
- 26 nuclides had only been reported in conference proceedings or internal reports until the end of 2011
- The number of known identified nuclides jumped from 40 to about 200 between 1919 and 1930, mostly due to Aston's work

## Context

This review extends the comprehensive nuclide discovery documentation project by Thoennessen and collaborators (2009-2012) that described the discovery of 3067 nuclides in a series of articles. It provides a historical framework connecting early work by Becquerel (1896), Rutherford (1908, 1919), Thomson (1913), and Aston (1919-1930) to modern discoveries at facilities like GSI, RIKEN's RIBF with BigRIPS, and NSCL. The work enables future planning for next-generation radioactive beam facilities and identifies specific nuclides that remain to be confirmed or discovered, including those only reported in conference proceedings and gaps in the chart of nuclides such as 21C, 30F, 33Ne, 36Na, and 39Mg.
