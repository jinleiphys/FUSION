# Ab initio nuclear thermodynamics

> We propose a new Monte Carlo method called the pinhole trace algorithm for {\it ab initio} calculations of the thermodynamics of nuclear systems. For typical simulations of interest, the computational speedup relative to conventional grand-canonical ensemble calculations can be as large as a factor of one thousand. Using a leading-order effective interaction that reproduces the properties of many atomic nuclei and neutron matter to a few percent accuracy, we determine the location of the critical point and the liquid-vapor coexistence line for symmetric nuclear matter with equal numbers of protons and neutrons. We also present the first {\it ab initio} study of the density and temperature dependence of nuclear clustering.

## Key claim

The authors propose a new Monte Carlo method called the pinhole trace algorithm (PTA) for *ab initio* calculations of nuclear thermodynamics, claiming a computational speedup of up to a factor of one thousand relative to conventional grand-canonical ensemble calculations, but only for "typical simulations of interest" and using a leading-order effective interaction that reproduces nuclear properties to "a few percent accuracy."

## Method

The authors perform nuclear lattice effective field theory (NLEFT) simulations at finite temperature using the pinhole trace algorithm, which evaluates the canonical partition function by importance sampling over both auxiliary fields and nucleon position/spin-isospin configurations (pinhole states). The chemical potential is extracted via a quantum adaptation of the Widom insertion method, and the pressure is obtained by integrating the Gibbs-Duhem equation. Simulations are performed on cubic lattices with volumes \(L^3 = 4^3, 5^3, 6^3\) using a pionless effective field theory Hamiltonian with two- and three-body contact interactions, with twisted boundary conditions to accelerate convergence to the thermodynamic limit.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature: \(T_c = 15.80(0.32)(1.60)\) MeV, where the first error bar combines statistical and thermodynamic-limit extrapolation errors, and the second is the estimated systematic error from omitted higher-order nuclear interactions.
- Critical density: \(\rho_c = 0.089(04)(18)\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- Critical chemical potential: \(\mu_c = -22.20(0.44)(2.20)\) MeV.
- Critical pressure: \(P_c = 0.260(05)(30)\) MeV/fm\(^3\).
- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter at zero temperature: \(\rho_0 = 0.205(08)(40)\) fm\(^{-3}\) with energy per particle \(E/A = -16.9(0.3)(1.7)\) MeV.
- Computational scaling of PTA is \(A^2 V L_t\) versus \(A V^2 L_t\) for the grand-canonical algorithm of Blankenbecler (1981), giving a speedup factor of \(V/A\).
- Lattice spacing: \(a = 1/150\) MeV\(^{-1} \approx 1.32\) fm; temporal lattice spacing: \(a_t = 1/2000\) MeV\(^{-1}\); momentum cutoff: \(\Lambda = \pi/a \approx 471\) MeV.
- Temperature range studied: 10 MeV to 20 MeV; density range: 0.0080 fm\(^{-3}\) to 0.20 fm\(^{-3}\).
- Statistical errors: less than 0.02 MeV for \(\mu\) and less than 0.002 MeV/fm\(^3\) for \(p\).

## Context

This work extends lattice effective field theory approaches to finite-temperature nuclear thermodynamics, overcoming the computational limitations of earlier grand-canonical lattice simulations by Lee (2004) and Muller (1999). The method enables the first *ab initio* study of nuclear clustering as a function of density and temperature, and provides a benchmark for comparison with many-body perturbation theory calculations by Baldo (1999) and Holt (2013), as well as self-consistent Green's function methods by Soma (2009) and Carbone (2018). The results open the door to higher-order chiral effective field theory calculations of the nuclear equation of state relevant for core-collapse supernovae, neutron star mergers, and heavy-ion collisions, and the pinhole trace algorithm may be applicable to condensed matter systems and ultracold atoms.
