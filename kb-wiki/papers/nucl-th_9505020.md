# Nuclear Shell Model Calculations with Fundamental Nucleon-Nucleon Interactions

> Some fundamental Nucleon-Nucleon interactions and their applications to finite nuclei are reviewed. Results for the few-body systems and from Shell-Model calculations are discussed and compared to point out the advantages and disadvantages of the different Nucleon-Nucleon interactions. The recently developed Drexel University Shell Model (DUSM) code is mentioned.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how different fundamental nucleon-nucleon (NN) interactions, when used to derive microscopic effective interactions for shell-model calculations, can produce substantially different binding energies and spectra for light nuclei, even when the underlying two-body matrix elements appear consistent. The authors note that the early Kuo-Brown (KB) effective interaction, derived from the hard-core Hamada-Johnston potential and ignoring folded diagrams, reproduces experimental binding energies and spectra for $^{18}$O, $^{18}$F, and $^{18}$Ne better than the more modern Bonn-A and Paris potentials, which overbind.

## Method

The authors performed conventional shell-model calculations for the $s$-$d$ shell nuclei $^{18}$O, $^{18}$F, and $^{18}$Ne using microscopic effective two-body interactions derived from three different NN potentials: the Hamada-Johnston (via the Kuo-Brown perturbative method), the Bonn-A, and the Paris potentials. The effective interactions were obtained via the folded-diagram method for the Bonn and Paris potentials, while the KB interaction used only the bare G-matrix and second-order core polarization diagrams. The calculations were carried out using the newly developed Drexel University Shell Model (DUSM) code, and the resulting binding energies and spectra were compared to experimental values after accounting for Coulomb energy corrections.

## Key numbers

- For $^{18}$O, the experimental shell-model binding energy is -12.188 MeV (WB method); KB gives -12.20 MeV, Bonn gives -13.96 MeV, and Paris gives -13.02 MeV (with $E_C=0$).
- For $^{18}$F, the experimental shell-model binding energy is -13.23 MeV (WB method); KB gives -13.36 MeV, Bonn gives -16.86 MeV, and Paris gives -15.44 MeV (with $E_C=3.543$ MeV).
- For $^{18}$Ne, the experimental shell-model binding energy is -12.188 MeV (WB method); KB gives -12.20 MeV, Bonn gives -13.96 MeV, and Paris gives -13.02 MeV (with $E_C=3.543$ MeV).
- The Bonn potential overbinds by about 1.8 MeV for $T=1$ states and 3.5 MeV for $T=0$ states relative to the KB results.
- For the $^{3}$H trinucleon system, benchmark binding energies (without three-body forces) are: RSC 7.02 MeV, Paris 7.31 MeV, Nijmegen 7.49 MeV, and Bonn-r 8.23 MeV, compared to the experimental value of 8.536 MeV.
- Adding the Tucson-Melbourne three-nucleon force (TM3N) to the RSC potential gives a $^{3}$H binding energy of 8.86 MeV for a cutoff mass $\Lambda=5.8 m_\pi$, but varies from 7.46 MeV ($\Lambda=4.1 m_\pi$) to 11.16 MeV ($\Lambda=7.1 m_\pi$).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to describe nuclear properties from fundamental NN interactions, comparing the early Kuo-Brown (1966) perturbative approach with more modern folded-diagram methods applied to the Bonn and Paris potentials. The results contradict the expectation that more sophisticated treatments of the effective interaction (including folded diagrams to all orders) would yield better agreement with experiment; instead, the simpler KB interaction performs best for $s$-$d$ shell nuclei. The paper also introduces the Drexel University Shell Model (DUSM) code as a new tool for shell-model calculations, and notes that systematic calculations for $f$-$p$ shell nuclei using the KB interaction (Nakada, 1994) show a similar trend. The findings suggest that further investigation of folded diagrams and possibly missing physical processes is needed to resolve the overbinding problem in modern potentials.
