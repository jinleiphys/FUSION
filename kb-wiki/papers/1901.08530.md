# Relativistic dissipation obeys Chapman-Enskog asymptotics: analytical and numerical evidence as a basis for accurate kinetic simulations

> We present an analytical derivation of the transport coefficients of a relativistic gas in (2+1) dimensions for both Chapman-Enskog (CE) asymptotics and Grad's expansion methods. Moreover, we develop a systematic calibration method, connecting the relaxation time of relativistic kinetic theory to the transport parameters of the associated dissipative hydrodynamic equations. Comparison between the analytical results and numerical simulations, shows that the CE method correctly captures dissipative effects, while Grad's method does not. The resulting calibration procedure based on the CE method opens the way to the quantitative kinetic description of dissipative relativistic fluid dynamics under fairly general conditions, namely flows with strongly non-linearities, in non-ideal geometries, across both ultra-relativistic and near-non-relativistic regimes.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for a relativistic gas in (2+1) dimensions, the Chapman-Enskog (CE) expansion correctly captures dissipative effects across all kinematic regimes (from ultra-relativistic to near non-relativistic), while Grad's method of moments fails to do so, in agreement with previous analyses performed in the (3+1) dimensional case.

## Method

The authors analytically derived the transport coefficients (thermal conductivity λ, shear viscosity η, and bulk viscosity μ) for an ideal relativistic gas in (2+1) dimensions using both the Chapman-Enskog asymptotic expansion and Grad's method of moments, applied to the relativistic Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation (Anderson-Witting model). They then performed numerical simulations using a lattice kinetic scheme, employing two benchmarks: a Taylor-Green vortex decay to measure shear viscosity, and a parallel-plate heat conduction setup to measure thermal conductivity. The numerical measurements were compared against the analytical predictions from both methods across a range of kinematic regimes parameterized by ζ = m/T.

## Key numbers

- Ultra-relativistic limit (ζ → 0) shear viscosity from CE: η_ur = (3/4) τ P; from Grad: η_ur = (3/5) τ P
- Ultra-relativistic limit thermal conductivity from CE: λ_ur = (3/2) τ n; from Grad: λ_ur = (3/4) τ n
- Ultra-relativistic limit bulk viscosity from both methods: μ_ur = 0
- Numerical measurements of shear viscosity and thermal conductivity show errors of order 1% for all data points
- The Taylor-Green vortex decay function is F(t) = exp(-2η t / (P + ε)), allowing extraction of η from simulation data

## Context

This work extends previous analyses of relativistic transport coefficients from (3+1) dimensions (Gabbana 2017b) to the (2+1) dimensional case, which is relevant for describing collective dynamics in graphene sheets and Weyl semi-metals (Muller 2008, Fritz 2008, Lucas 2016). The results contradict the predictions of the Israel-Stewart formulation based on Grad's method (Israel 1976, 1979) and confirm earlier theoretical work by Denicol (2012) showing that extending Grad's method to higher moments approaches CE results. The calibration procedure enables accurate kinetic simulations of relativistic dissipative flows in non-ideal geometries across both ultra-relativistic and near-non-relativistic regimes.
