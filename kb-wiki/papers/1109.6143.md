> We present the study for the mass dependence of E$_{bal}$ for various N/Z ratios covering pure symmetric systems to highly neutron-rich ones.

# Mass dependence of balance energy for different N/Z ratio

## Key claim
The balance energy (E$_{bal}$) follows a power-law dependence on combined system mass for each N/Z ratio, but the power-law exponent $\tau$ decreases systematically from -0.30 to -0.17 as N/Z increases from 1.0 to 2.0. The abstract omits the qualifier that this trend arises because symmetry energy reduces E$_{bal}$ more strongly in lighter systems at high N/Z, flattening the mass dependence.

## Method
The author simulated heavy-ion collisions for Ca+Ca, Ni+Ni, Zr+Zr, Sn+Sn, and Xe+Xe systems using the Isospin-dependent Quantum Molecular Dynamics (IQMD) model, with N/Z ratios varied from 1.0 to 2.0 in steps of 0.2. Reactions were run at incident energies around the expected balance energy in steps of 10 MeV/nucleon; collective transverse in-plane flow was calculated at each energy, and E$_{bal}$ was extracted via straight-line interpolation. The percentage difference $\Delta$E$_{bal}$(%) between calculations with and without symmetry energy was evaluated as a function of N/Z.

## Key numbers
- Power-law exponent $\tau$ for E$_{bal}$ $\propto$ A$^{\tau}$: -0.30$\pm$0.03 (N/Z=1.0), -0.28$\pm$0.02 (N/Z=1.2), -0.23$\pm$0.01 (N/Z=1.4), -0.21$\pm$0.02 (N/Z=1.6), -0.20$\pm$0.03 (N/Z=1.8), -0.17$\pm$0.03 (N/Z=2.0).
- N/Z ratios studied: 1.0, 1.2, 1.4, 1.6, 1.8, 2.0.
- Incident energy steps around E$_{bal}$: 10 MeV/nucleon.
- Systems simulated: Ca+Ca, Ni+Ni, Zr+Zr, Sn+Sn, Xe+Xe.

## Context
This work extends the study of system-size dependence of balance energy reported in Sood (PhD thesis) and Gautam et al. (2011) by systematically varying N/Z from symmetric to neutron-rich systems. The finding that E$_{bal}$ decreases with increasing N/Z for a fixed mass (isotopes) contrasts with the trend observed in isobaric data from Pak et al. (1998), where Coulomb repulsion dominates; the author clarifies that for isotopes Coulomb is constant, so symmetry energy lowers E$_{bal}$ at higher N/Z. The results enable future studies disentangling Coulomb and symmetry-energy contributions to flow disappearance in radioactive-beam experiments.
