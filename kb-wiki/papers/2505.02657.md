# Local equilibrium Wigner function for spin-1/2 particles

> Formal connections between the spin density matrix and the Wigner function for spin-1/2 particles forming a relativistic gas are explored to determine their general structures. They suggest that the commonly used form of the local equilibrium Wigner function should be replaced by a new expression. The latter fulfills the necessary condition for the normalization of the mean spin polarization, which the former fails to reproduce. The new definition of the Wigner function leads to generalized thermodynamic relations for perfect spin hydrodynamics, identical to those obtained earlier using the classical concept of spin. Moreover, one can prove that the perfect spin hydrodynamics based on the new equilibrium Wigner function is nonlinearly causal and stable. Finally, the selection rule for the Lagrange multipliers, which is satisfied by real systems, is discussed.

## Key claim

The commonly used form of the local equilibrium Wigner function for spin-1/2 particles (introduced by Becattini (2013)) fails to enforce the correct normalization of the mean spin polarization vector, which should be confined between 0 and 1; the authors propose a new expression that satisfies this normalization condition and is consistent with the spinor density matrix given in the Landau-Lifshitz course.

## Method

The authors start from the general Hermitian two-by-two spin density matrix for spin-1/2 particles and antiparticles, decompose it into a spin-averaged phase-space density and a polarization three-vector, and then derive the corresponding four-by-four spinor density matrix. They show that this general spinor density matrix takes the form \(X_s^\pm = f_0^\pm [1 + \gamma_5 \slashed{\zeta}^\pm]\), which can be rewritten as an exponential \(X_s^\pm = f_0^\pm \exp(\gamma_5 \slashed{a}_\pm)\); for local equilibrium they set the vector Lagrange multiplier \(a_\mu = -(1/(2m)) \tilde{\omega}_{\mu\nu} p^\nu\) and use the Boltzmann statistics form \(X_s^\pm = \exp[\pm \xi - \beta_\mu p^\mu + \gamma_5 \slashed{a}]\). From this Wigner function they compute the baryon current, energy-momentum tensor, spin tensor, and entropy current, and derive generalized thermodynamic relations and a generating function \(\chi\) to test nonlinear causality.

## Key numbers

- The mean spin polarization vector from the old formalism is \(\mathbf{P} = -\frac{1}{2} \tanh\left( \frac{\sqrt{\mathbf{b}_* \cdot \mathbf{b}_* - \mathbf{e}_* \cdot \mathbf{e}_*}}{2} \right) \frac{\mathbf{b}_*}{\sqrt{\mathbf{b}_* \cdot \mathbf{b}_* - \mathbf{e}_* \cdot \mathbf{e}_*}}\), which fails to guarantee \(\mathbf{b}_* \cdot \mathbf{b}_* - \mathbf{e}_* \cdot \mathbf{e}_* > 0\).
- For small polarization, the old formalism gives \(\mathbf{P} = -\mathbf{b}_*(x,\mathbf{p})/4\).
- The classical spin magnitude is \(\sqrt{3/4}\) (the Casimir eigenvalue for SU(2)), while the quantum spin magnitude for spin-1/2 is \(1/2\).
- The convergence condition for the integrals over the new equilibrium distributions is \(\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{ \mathbf{b}'^2 + \mathbf{e}'^2 + 2 |\mathbf{e}' \times \mathbf{b}'|} < m/T\), where primes denote vectors in the fluid rest frame.
- The quadratic corrections to \(N^\mu\) and \(T^{\mu\nu}\) from the quantum framework are identical to those from the classical-spin approach of Florkowski (2024).

## Context

This work solves a long-standing problem in relativistic spin hydrodynamics: the local equilibrium Wigner function introduced by Becattini (2013) was shown by Florkowski (2017) to violate the normalization of the mean polarization vector, limiting its validity to small spin polarization tensor \(\omega_{\alpha\beta}\). The new expression is consistent with the spinor density matrix from the Landau-Lifshitz course (Berestetskii, 1982) and with the classical-spin approach of Florkowski (2018) up to second order in \(\omega\). It enables well-posed numerical simulations of spin-polarized fluids by proving that the resulting perfect spin hydrodynamics is nonlinearly causal and stable, generalizing the result of Abboud (2025) from the classical-spin formalism.
