# Cross section predictions for hydrostatic and explosive burning

> We review different models used for reactions involved in nuclear astrophysics. The reaction rate is defined for resonant as well as for non-resonant processes. For low-density nuclei, we describe the DWBA method, the potential model, the R-matrix method, and microscopic cluster models. The statistical model is developed for high-level densities. Details of calculations in the low- and high-density regimes are illustrated with new results concerning transfer reactions and level densities.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that theoretical predictions for nuclear reaction cross sections in astrophysics must be divided into two distinct regimes (low and high level densities), each requiring fundamentally different models, and that bridging these two regimes remains a significant challenge.

## Method

The authors review and compare several theoretical approaches for predicting nuclear reaction cross sections at astrophysical energies. For systems with low level densities (typically light nuclei with A ≤ 20), they describe the DWBA method for transfer reactions, the potential model for capture reactions, the R-matrix method for parameterizing and extrapolating cross sections, and microscopic cluster models using the Generator Coordinate Method. For systems with high level densities, they present the optical model and the statistical Hauser-Feshbach approach, which uses averaged transmission coefficients and level densities.

- For the ¹⁷F(p,γ)¹⁸Ne reaction, the microscopic model gives a proton width of 21 keV for the 3⁺ resonance (compared to experimental 18 ± 3 keV) and a gamma width of 33 meV (compared to evaluated 29 ± 19 meV)
- The direct reaction mechanism is about 5 orders of magnitude faster than the compound nucleus mechanism

## Context

This review extends the work of Rolfs and Rodney (1988) and Clayton (1983) on nuclear astrophysics reaction rates, and provides an updated overview of theoretical methods including recent developments in microscopic cluster models and R-matrix formulations. The paper specifically addresses the challenge of predicting cross sections for nuclei far from stability that are relevant for explosive burning scenarios such as the r-process and rp-process, where experimental data are often unavailable. The authors identify that future experiments with unstable beams will be crucial for testing these theoretical predictions, particularly for transfer reactions used to extract spectroscopic factors.
