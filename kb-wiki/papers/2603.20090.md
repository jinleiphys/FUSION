# Interrogating the composition and distribution of nuclear magnetization via the hyperfine anomaly: experiment meets nuclear and atomic theory for short-lived $^{47}$K

> To date, the magnetic structure of nuclei has been poorly constrained, with limited information on its spatial distribution. In this work, we address the composition and distribution of nuclear magnetization in a precision study of short-lived $^{47}$K. We measure the Larmor frequency with part-per-million precision using liquid-state $β$-detected nuclear magnetic resonance at CERN-ISOLDE, improving determination of the experimental differential hyperfine anomaly relative to $^{39}$K by more than an order of magnitude. By combining these experimental results with relativistic all-orders atomic calculations and nuclear density functional theory, we obtain the relative spin and orbital contributions to the nuclear magnetic moments. Our analysis reveals an overestimation of the spin contribution predicted by nuclear theory, that persists even after considering two-body currents. Conversely, we show that the measured hyperfine anomaly is reproduced when adopting the spatial distribution of nuclear magnetization provided by density functional theory. The methodology introduced in this work establishes a means to probe the detailed magnetic structure of the nucleus. This is critical for benchmarking nuclear structure theory and calculations of symmetry-violating nuclear moments relevant to searches for physics beyond the Standard Model in atoms and molecules.

# Interrogating the composition and distribution of nuclear magnetization via the hyperfine anomaly: experiment meets nuclear and atomic theory for short-lived $^{47}$K

## Key claim
The differential hyperfine anomaly between $^{47}$K and $^{39}$K is measured with more than an order of magnitude improved precision, revealing that nuclear density functional theory overestimates the spin contribution to the magnetic moment even when two-body currents are included, yet the spatial distribution of magnetization from the same theory reproduces the measured hyperfine anomaly.

## Method
The Larmor frequency of $^{47}$K was measured with part-per-million precision using liquid-state $\beta$-detected nuclear magnetic resonance at CERN-ISOLDE. NMR shielding constants for K$^+$ in EMIM-DCA ionic liquid were calculated using classical force-field molecular dynamics followed by relativistic Dirac-Kohn-Sham calculations with a tailored B3LYP functional, yielding $\sigma_{iso}(\mathrm{IL}) = 1247(10)$ ppm. Nuclear magnetization distributions were computed using seven density functional theory functionals (UNEDF1, SAMi, SLy4, SIII, SkM*, SkO’, SkXc) with Landau parameter variation, and atomic hyperfine constants were calculated using the all-orders correlation potential method including Breit and QED corrections.

## Key numbers
- Differential hyperfine anomaly $^{39}\Delta^{47}$: $\epsilon = +0.59(62)\%$, extracted by comparing point-like theory hyperfine constant with experimental value for the $4s_{1/2}$ state of $^{39}$K.
- NMR shielding constant for K$^+$ in EMIM-DCA ionic liquid: $\sigma_{iso}(\mathrm{IL}) = 1247(10)$ ppm.
- NMR shielding constant for K$^+$ in water: $\sigma_{iso}(\mathrm{H_2O}) = 1284(12)$ ppm.
- Chemical shift between EMIM-DCA and water: $\delta_{iso} = 37$ ppm.
- Bulk volume susceptibility of EMIM-DCA: $\kappa(\mathrm{EMIM-DCA}) = -6.33$ ppm, measured by SQUID magnetometry.
- Bulk volume susceptibility of D$_2$O: $\kappa(\mathrm{D_2O}) = -8.866$ ppm from literature.
- Geometrical shape factor for D$_2$O reference NMR tube: $\alpha = 0.48$; for EMIM-DCA thin disk at $45^\circ$ to $B_0$: $\alpha = 0.507$.
- First solvation shell K$^+$-DCA$^-$ distance: 2.75 Å from radial distribution function.
- Calculated hyperfine constant for $^{39}$K $4s_{1/2}$ with point-like magnetization: 229.5(14) MHz; experimental value: 230.8598601(7) MHz.

## Context
This work extends the approach of Harding (2020) and Roberts (2022) by combining precision NMR measurements with relativistic atomic calculations and nuclear density functional theory to probe nuclear magnetization distributions. The methodology enables benchmarking of nuclear structure theories such as UNEDF1 and Skyrme functionals (SAMi, SLy4, SIII, SkM*, SkO’, SkXc) and provides a pathway for constraining symmetry-violating nuclear moments relevant to searches for physics beyond the Standard Model in atoms and molecules. The atomic calculations follow the correlation potential method of Dzuba (1989) and include Breit and QED corrections following Ginges (2017) and Flambaum (2005).
