# Scaling of Hadronic Form Factors in Point Form Kinematics

> The general features of baryon form factors calculated with point form kinematics are derived. With point form kinematics and spectator currents hadronic form factors are functions of $η:={1\over 4}(v_{out}-v_{in})^2$ and, over a range of $η$ values are insensitive to unitary scale transformations of the model wave functions when the extent of the wave function is small compared to the scale defined by the constituent mass, $<r^2 > \ll 1/m^2$. The form factors are sensitive to the shape of such compact wave functions. Simple 3-quark proton wave functions are employed to illustrate these features. Rational and algebraic model wave functions lead to a reasonable representation of the empirical form factors, while Gaussian wave functions fail. For large values of $η$ point form kinematics with spectator currents leads to power law behavior of the wave functions.

# Scaling of Hadronic Form Factors in Point Form Kinematics

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that with point form kinematics and spectator currents, hadronic form factors are functions of the kinematic variable η and become insensitive to unitary scale transformations of the model wave functions when the spatial extent of the wave function is small compared to the constituent mass scale (⟨r²⟩ ≪ 1/m²), but remain sensitive to the shape of such compact wave functions, a qualitative difference from instant and front form kinematics where scaling the wave function to zero yields form factors independent of momentum transfer.

## Method

The authors derive the general features of baryon form factors calculated with point form kinematics, using the full Lorentz group as the kinematic subgroup. They employ simple 3-quark proton wave functions with Gaussian, rational, and algebraic shapes, and compute electric and magnetic form factors using spectator currents with both point-like (structureless) and Dirac spinor single-quark current kernels. The form factors are evaluated as overlap integrals that include Jacobian factors from the transformation between constituent and spectator momenta, Wigner rotation coefficients, and current factors, with unitary scale transformations applied to vary the wave function extent.

## Key numbers

- The rms radius for the Gaussian model is r₀ = 1/b, and for the rational wave function r₀ = √(2/5)/b
- With b = 650 MeV the Gaussian model gives a mean square radius of approximately 0.1 fm², matching the wave function from Hamil (2002) that describes the nucleon spectrum
- With the rational wave function, the same mean square radius obtains with b = 410 MeV
- The constituent quark mass used is m = 340 MeV, giving the dimensionless parameter mr₀ = 0.52
- The rational wave function with mr₀ = 0.52 gives the magnetic moment as 2.86 nuclear magnetons, close to the empirical value of 2.79 nm
- For large η, point form kinematics with the single-particle current kernel implies electric form factors behave as η^{-(n+1/2)} and with n=3 quarks this gives η^{-7/2} for electric and η^{-2} for magnetic form factors
- With the algebraic wave function φ_I with exponent a = 11/4 in the point limit, the electric form factor closely matches the parameterization of Lomon (2002)
- With the wave function φ_I with a = 9/4 and m/b = 0.52, the calculated magnetic moment is 2.80 nm, coinciding with the empirical value

## Context

This work extends the point form kinematics approach of Dirac (1949) and Bakamjian and Thomas (1953), and directly addresses the recent constituent quark model calculations by Graz group (2002) that achieved remarkable agreement with nucleon form factor data using compact wave functions and point-like quark currents. The paper contrasts with instant and front form kinematics, where Helminen (2002) showed that comparably compact wave functions require either spatially extended quarks or vector meson dominance to match empirical form factors. The results enable a zero-order description of baryon form factors using the point limit of Lorentz kinematics, to be refined by finite quark masses as scale parameters, and suggest that adjustment of confinement shape and quark masses as in Dannbom (1997) may provide a realistic description of both the spectrum and form factors.
