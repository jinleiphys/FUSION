# Random close packing of binary hard spheres favors the stability of neutron-rich atomic nuclei

> In spite of the success of the Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula in its modern numerical and predictive implementations, the common-knowledge principle that it is electrostatics which, ultimately, favors neutron-rich nuclei still presents unclear aspects. For example, while it is true that the Coulomb interaction promotes the tendency towards neutron-rich nuclei, the opposite effects of Majorana exchange forces and Pauli exclusion are known to counteract this tendency. We show that a recent analytical progress in the mathematical description of random close packing of spheres with different sizes provides a missing contribution to the theoretical description of the $Z$ versus $N$ slope in the nuclides chart. In particular, the theory suggests, on geometric grounds and with a physically-reasoned assumption that the excluded-volume size of neutrons is 20\% larger than that of protons, that the most stable nuclei are those with ratio $Z/N\approx 0.75$. This new ``geometric'' random-packing contribution to the semi-empirical mass formula may be the missing aspect of nuclear structure that tilts the balance towards neutron-rich nuclei in the Segrè stability chart.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the observed neutron excess in stable nuclei (Z/N ≈ 0.75) can be explained not only by the balance between Coulomb repulsion and the asymmetry term from Pauli exclusion, but also by a previously neglected geometric contribution: the tendency of nuclei to maximize their packing density through random close packing of binary hard spheres, assuming the excluded-volume size of neutrons is 20% larger than that of protons.

## Method

The authors apply an analytical theory of random close packing (RCP) for binary mixtures of hard spheres, originally developed for jammed granular matter, to model the packing of neutrons and protons in atomic nuclei. They treat neutrons as larger spheres (radius ≈ 1.0 fm) and protons as smaller spheres (radius ≈ 0.84 fm), then solve the isostatic stability condition (coordination number z = 6) using the Lebowitz equilibrium pair correlation function and the Santos-Yuste-de Haro compressibility factor for binary hard-sphere mixtures. The RCP volume fraction is computed as a function of the proton fraction Z/A, and the composition yielding the maximum packing density is identified.

## Key numbers

* Proton radius: 0.84 fm (internationally accepted value from NIST).
* Neutron radius (excluded-volume size): approximately 1.0 fm, based on the point where the neutron charge density profile goes to zero (Atac 2021).
* Size ratio of protons to neutrons: σ₂/σ₁ = 0.84.
* Maximum random close packing density occurs at Z/A = 0.43, which directly implies Z/N = 0.75.
* The predicted Z/N = 0.75 coincides with the most quoted empirical trend value for the slope in the Segrè stability chart.

## Context

This work extends the classical analogy between nucleons in nuclei and random close packing of hard spheres, originally noted by Meitner and Frisch (1939) and central to the liquid drop model of Weizsäcker (1935), Bethe (1936), and Frenkel. The authors apply a recent analytical solution for RCP of polydisperse spheres (Zaccone 2022) and its binary-mixture extension (Anzivino 2023) to nuclear structure. The result provides a new geometric contribution to the semi-empirical mass formula that acts in synergy with the known Coulomb and asymmetry terms, and may offer predictive guidance for the experimental search of new heavy nuclei and for studies of nuclear matter equation of state, fission, and nucleosynthesis.
