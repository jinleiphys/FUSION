# Precision half-life measurement of the 4-fold forbidden electron capture of V-50

> A sensitive search of the 4-fold forbidden non-unique beta decay of V-50 has been performed. A total exposure of 185.8 kg x d has been accumulated. A reliable half-life value with the highest precision so far of $(2.29 \pm 0.25) \cdot 10^{17}$ years of the electron capture decay of V-50 into the first excited state of Ti-50 could be obtained. A photon emission line following the 4-fold forbidden beta decay into the first excited state of Cr-50 could not be observed, resulting in a lower limit on the half-life of the beta decay branch of $1.7 \cdot 10^{18}$ years. This is barely in agreement with a claimed observation of this decay branch.

# Precision half-life measurement of the 4-fold forbidden electron capture of V-50

## Key claim
The electron capture decay of V-50 into the first excited state of Ti-50 has a half-life of (2.29 ± 0.25) × 10^17 years, which is the most precise value to date; however, the beta decay branch into the first excited state of Cr-50 could not be observed, yielding only a lower limit of 1.7 × 10^18 years that barely agrees with a prior claimed observation.

## Method
The measurement used an ultra-low background p-type extended range Ge-detector (89% efficiency, 2.1 keV resolution at 1.33 MeV) located in the UDO underground laboratory at 1200 m water equivalent depth in the Asse salt mine. A 255.82 g sample of vanadium powder (natural isotopic composition, 0.25% V-50) was placed on the detector for 97.8 days, accumulating 185.8 kg·d of exposure. The oxygen content (6.13% by mass) and water content (1.445% by mass) of the sample were determined and subtracted from the total mass before calculating activity concentrations and half-lives. Detection efficiency was calibrated using Monte Carlo simulations (GESPECOR) with radionuclide solutions of known activity.

## Key numbers
- Electron capture half-life of V-50 → Ti-50 (1553.8 keV gamma): (2.29 ± 0.25) × 10^17 years, based on 123 net counts at 2.17% detection efficiency
- Lower limit on beta decay half-life of V-50 → Cr-50 (783.3 keV gamma): 1.7 × 10^18 years (significance level α = 5%), corresponding to an upper detection limit of 27 events at 3.35% efficiency
- Total exposure: 185.8 kg·d (255.8 g sample × 97.8 days)
- Combined total half-life lower limit for V-50: 2.0 × 10^17 years, implying at most 7.1% of decays occur via beta decay

## Context
This work improves upon the previous best measurement by Sim (1989), which reported an electron capture half-life of (2.05 ± 0.49) × 10^17 years and a claimed beta decay half-life of (8.2 × 10^17) years with large uncertainties. The new lower limit for the beta decay branch (1.7 × 10^18 years) is nearly twice the value currently listed in nuclear data tables (NuDat), indicating those tables are incorrect. The measurement was limited by intrinsic contamination of the vanadium sample from uranium and thorium decay chains; the authors note that purifying the vanadium would enable a more sensitive search for the beta decay mode in a follow-up measurement.
