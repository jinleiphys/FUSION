# Model independent properties of two-photon exchange in elastic electron proton scattering

> We derive from first principles, as the C-invariance of the electromagnetic interaction and the crossing symmetry, the general properties of two-photon exchange in electron-proton elastic scattering. We show that the presence of this mechanism destroys the linearity of the Rosenbluth separation.

# Model independent properties of two-photon exchange in elastic electron proton scattering

## Key claim
The two-photon exchange contribution to elastic electron-proton scattering destroys the linearity of the Rosenbluth separation, and any approximation of this contribution by a linear function in the variable ε is in contradiction with C-invariance and crossing symmetry of the electromagnetic interaction.

## Method
The authors derive the general spin structure of the matrix element for elastic eN-scattering using six invariant complex amplitudes, then reduce these to three amplitudes by applying lepton helicity conservation. They analyze the C-invariance and crossing symmetry properties of the one-photon and two-photon exchange mechanisms, connecting the s-channel (eN scattering) to the t-channel (e+e- → N anti-N annihilation) to derive the allowed angular dependencies of the interference contributions.

## Key numbers
- The two-photon exchange amplitudes ΔA₁,₂ and A₃ are of order α = e²/(4π) = 1/137
- The one-photon exchange cross section for e+e- → p anti-p has the form a(t) + b(t)cos²θ, where a(t) and b(t) are quadratic contributions of G_Ep(t) and G_Mp(t)
- The interference contribution to the differential cross section for e+e- → p anti-p has the form cosθ[c₀(t) + c₁(t)cos²θ + c₂(t)cos⁴θ + ...]
- The specific interference term from the A₃ amplitude in eN-scattering has ε-dependence proportional to (2ε/(1-ε))√((1+ε)/(1-ε))

## Context
This work addresses the discrepancy between proton form factor ratios extracted from Rosenbluth separation (Arrington, 1975) and from polarization transfer measurements at Jefferson Lab (Jones, 2000; Gayou, 2002). The authors extend earlier work by Guichon and Vanderhaeghen (2003) by showing that attempts to resolve the discrepancy while preserving a linear ε-dependence of the cross section violate C-invariance. The analysis uses the formalism of Goldberger (1957) for spin structure and applies crossing symmetry as developed by Gell-Mann and Goldberger (1966). The results enable experimental tests of two-photon exchange by measuring the ε-dependence of the differential cross section in terms of the variable √((1+ε)/(1-ε)), and provide a framework for analyzing polarization phenomena in future work.
