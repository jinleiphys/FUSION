# Hierarchical Qubit Maps and Hierarchical Quantum Error Correction

> We consider hierarchically implemented quantum error correction (HI-QEC), in which the fidelities of logical qubits are differentially optimized to enhance the capabilities of quantum devices in scientific applications. By employing qubit representations that propagate hierarchies in simulated systems to those in logical qubit noise sensitivities, heterogeneity in the distribution of physical-to-logical qubits can be systematically structured. For concreteness, we estimate HI-QEC's impact on surface code resources in computing low-energy observables to fixed precision, finding up to $\sim 60\%$ reductions in qubit requirements plausible in early error corrected simulations. Hierarchical qubit maps are also possible without error correction in qubit and qudit systems where fidelities are non-uniform, either unintentionally or by design. Hierarchical optimizations are another element in the co-design process of quantum simulations for nuclear and particle physics.

## Key claim

Hierarchically implemented quantum error correction (HI-QEC) can reduce physical qubit requirements by up to ~60% in early error-corrected simulations, but only when the Hilbert space is organized so that low-energy observables are exponentially less sensitive to noise on UV logical qubits, and the savings diminish as target precision increases.

## Method

The authors model a single-qubit isotropic depolarizing noise channel acting on logical qubits that encode a digitized Gaussian wavefunction in the binary (Jordan-Lee-Preskill) basis, which maps sequency (frequency) bands to individual qubits. They compute the linear-response coefficients γ_q that quantify the sensitivity of low-energy observables (e.g., ⟨φ²⟩) to noise on each logical qubit, then use the empirically determined surface-code error scaling P_L ∼ c₀(p/p_th)^{⌊(d+1)/2⌋} to translate heterogeneous logical fidelity requirements into heterogeneous physical qubit array sizes d_q.

## Key numbers

* For an eight-qubit Gaussian wavefunction (σ = 50/3) with physical qubit error rate p = 10⁻³ and target per-cycle fractional error ε/N_cycles = 10⁻⁵, the homogeneous surface-code distribution requires d = 13 per logical qubit, totaling 1352 physical qubits.
* Under the same conditions, the optimal heterogeneous distribution of surface-code sizes is d = {15, 13, 11, 11, 9, 7, 7, 5}, totaling 840 physical qubits, a ~37% reduction.
* For a per-cycle fractional error equal to the physical qubit error rate (p = 10⁻³), up to ~60% reductions in physical qubits are found; reductions remain ~20% near per-cycle fractional error rates around classical machine precision.
* For a four-qubit Gaussian (σ = 8/3, μ = 15/2), the linear noise sensitivities for ⟨φ²⟩ are γ = {0.094, 0.378, 2.151, 2.890} for qubits 0 (UV) through 3 (IR), showing the IR qubit is ~30 times more sensitive than the UV qubit.
* For an eight-qubit Gaussian (σ = 50/3), the sensitivity vector is γ = {31.15, 13.91, 3.86, 0.64, 0.15, 0.038, 0.0096, 0.0024}, showing exponential suppression for UV qubits.

## Context

This work extends the concept of hierarchical qubit mappings from the Jordan-Lee-Preskill (JLP) basis for scalar field theory (Jordan et al., 2012; Klco, 2018) and the binary mapping of harmonic oscillator modes (Klco, 2018) to the domain of quantum error correction, proposing that the same hierarchy that makes effective field theories useful can reduce physical qubit overhead. It contrasts with the standard approach of uniform physical-to-logical qubit ratios in general-purpose surface-code architectures (Kitaev, 2003; Dennis et al., 2002; Fowler et al., 2012) and enables co-design of quantum devices for specific scientific applications, particularly lattice gauge theories and QCD simulations where early precision goals are at the few to tens of percent level.
