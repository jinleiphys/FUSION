# Microscopic Calculation of Pre-Compound Excitation Energies for Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We introduce a microscopic approach for calculating the excitation energies of systems formed during heavy-ion collisions. The method is based on time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory and allows the study of the excitation energy as a function of time or ion-ion separation distance. We discuss how this excitation energy is related to the estimate of the excitation energy using the reaction $Q$-value, as well as its implications for dinuclear pre-compound systems formed during heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a microscopic method based on time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory to calculate excitation energies of dinuclear pre-compound systems formed during heavy-ion collisions, but the approach is only reliable for the early stages of the collision and the long-time evolution may be questionable.

## Method

The method divides TDHF motion into collective and intrinsic parts by assuming the collective part is determined by the density and current. The excitation energy is computed as the difference between the total TDHF energy (a conserved quantity) and the collective energy, where the collective energy is obtained from the kinetic part (using the current) and the density-constrained energy (found by minimizing energy while holding the instantaneous TDHF density constant). Calculations were performed in 3-D geometry using the full Skyrme force SLy4, with density constraint calculations every 20 fm/c and the ion-ion separation distance extracted from unrestricted TDHF runs using a hybrid method relating the coordinate to the quadrupole moment for small distances.

## Key numbers

- For the 16O+16O system, the Qgg value is +16.6 MeV; for the 40Ca+40Ca system, the Qgg value is -14.2 MeV.
- The numerical accuracy of static binding energies and deviation from point Coulomb energy in the initial state is on the order of 50-200 keV.
- For the 16O+16O system at Ec.m.=11 MeV, the statistical estimate for the energy width is approximately 6 MeV, calculated as ΔE ≈ sqrt(16 εF E* / (N π²)).
- Using the Fermi gas model (E* = a T² with a ≈ A/8 MeV⁻¹), the temperature range is 2.0-3.5 MeV for the 16O+16O system and 1.4-2.5 MeV for the 40Ca+40Ca system.

## Context

This work extends the density-constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) method previously developed by the same authors (Umar and Oberacker, 2006) for calculating ion-ion potential barriers, and builds on the general foundation of TDHF theory for low-energy heavy-ion reactions established by Negele (1982) and others. The approach provides a dynamical way to calculate excitation energies of intermediate states formed during collisions, which is important for understanding superheavy element formation in hot or cold fusion reactions (Hofmann, 2002; Oganessian, 2007) and for the stochastic mean-field approach to nuclear dynamics (Ayik, 2008; Ayik, 2009). The method enables future studies of heavier systems and superheavy nuclei, where the excitation energy at the point of capture critically influences the probabilities for various exit channels.
