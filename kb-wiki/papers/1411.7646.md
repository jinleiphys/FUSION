# Fragments' internal and kinetic temperatures in the framework of a Nuclear Statistical Multifragmentation Model

> The agreement between the fragments' internal and kinetic temperatures with the breakup temperature is investigated using a Statistical Multifragmentation Model which makes no a priori as- sumption on the relationship between them. We thus examine the conditions for obtaining such agreement and find that, in the framework of our model, this holds only in a relatively narrow range of excitation energy. The role played by the qualitative shape of the fragments' state densities is also examined. Our results suggest that the internal temperature of the light fragments may be affected by this quantity, whose behavior may lead to constant internal temperatures over a wide excitation energy range. It thus suggests that the nuclear thermometry may provide valuable information on the nuclear state density.

## Key claim

Using a Statistical Multifragmentation Model that imposes no a priori relationship between internal and kinetic temperatures, the agreement between these temperatures and the breakup temperature holds only in a relatively narrow range of excitation energy, and the internal temperature of light fragments may remain constant over a wide excitation energy range depending on the qualitative shape of the fragments' state densities.

## Method

The authors employed a modified version of the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) that decouples kinetic and internal temperatures by treating the total energy as a conserved, discretized quantity and using recursion relations to compute average primary fragment multiplicities. The density of states for fragments was parameterized using a Fermi-gas expression matched to a constant-temperature form at low excitation energies, with two variants of the matching energy (U_x and a capped version tilde U_x) to test sensitivity. Breakup temperatures were derived from the statistical weight Omega, kinetic temperatures from the average fragment kinetic energy, and internal temperatures from the logarithmic derivative of the state density at the average fragment excitation energy.

## Key numbers

- For the $^{120}$Nd source, the kinetic temperature agrees well with the breakup temperature for source sizes $A_0 \gtrsim 120$ over the range $2.0\ \text{MeV} \lesssim E^*/A \lesssim 10.0\ \text{MeV}$.
- The constant-temperature parameterization yields tau = 16.9 MeV for $^4$He and tau = 9.4 MeV for $^5$Li, corresponding to the saturation values of internal temperature observed in the model.
- For the $^{120}$Nd source, finite-size effects on the $^{16}$O fragment excitation energy distribution are small only in the range $4.0\ \text{MeV} \lesssim E^*/A \lesssim 7.0\ \text{MeV}$.
- The caloric curve shows a slight size dependence, with lower breakup temperatures for larger systems, and the asymptotic limit is apparently attained around $A_0 \lesssim 80$.

## Context

This work extends prior SMM studies by Souza et al. (2013) and temperatures by Bondorf et al. (1989), which treated kinetic temperature as an input parameter, by instead allowing both temperatures to emerge from the model without constraint. The results contrast with the standard SMM assumption that internal and kinetic temperatures equal the breakup temperature, showing this holds only for heavier sources and not at low excitation energies. The constant internal temperatures found for light fragments qualitatively resemble the saturation of emission temperatures observed experimentally by the MSU group (1994), Trautmann (2007), and others, but the model's saturation values are much higher than the experimental 5-6 MeV, suggesting that nuclear thermometry may provide information on the nuclear state density.
