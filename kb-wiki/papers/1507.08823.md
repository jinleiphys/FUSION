# Longitudinal vector form factors in weak decays of nuclei

> The longitudinal form factors of the weak vector current of particles with spin $ J = 1/2 $ and isospin $ I = 1/2 $ are determined by the mass difference and the charge radii of members of the isotopic doublets. The most promising reactions to measure these form factors are the reactions with large momentum transfers involving the spin-1/2 isotopic doublets with a maximum mass splitting. Numerical estimates of longitudinal form factors are given for nucleons and eight nuclear spin-1/2 isotopic doublets.

## Key claim

The longitudinal form factors of the weak vector current for spin-1/2, isospin-1/2 particles are determined by the mass difference and the difference between Lorentz-vector and Lorentz-scalar charge radii of the isotopic doublet members; the result is proportional to the mass difference and, for small momentum transfers, the difference between these charge radii, with the leading contribution coming from the charge radii of nucleons since nucleons are composed of relativistic quarks.

## Method

The authors used the Ward-Takahashi identity (WTI) for the weak vector current, generalized to account for isotopic symmetry breaking, to derive expressions for the longitudinal vector form factors at zero momentum transfer. They applied the MIT bag model to compute the vector and scalar charge radii of nucleons, and then extended the formalism to nuclear spin-1/2 isotopic doublets by decomposing nuclear charge radii into nucleon contributions and nuclear structure terms, assuming non-relativistic nucleon motion.

## Key numbers

- For the neutron-proton doublet: predicted longitudinal form factor \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{th}} = 1.1 \times 10^{-4} \) (Q-value 782.354 keV, mass difference 1,293.353 keV).
- For the \( ^{3}\mathrm{H} \)-\( ^{3}\mathrm{He} \) doublet: predicted \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{th}} = 4.4 \times 10^{-5} \); experimental value \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{expt}} = -0.005 \pm 0.040 \) from muon capture in \( ^{3}\mathrm{He} \) (Gazit 2008).
- For the \( ^{13}\mathrm{C} \)-\( ^{13}\mathrm{N} \) doublet: predicted \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{th}} = -1.8 \times 10^{-4} \) (Q-value 2,220.4 keV, mass difference -2,220.4 keV).
- For the \( ^{15}\mathrm{N} \)-\( ^{15}\mathrm{O} \) doublet: predicted \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{th}} = -2.3 \times 10^{-4} \) (Q-value 2,753.9 keV, mass difference -2,753.9 keV).
- For the \( ^{19}\mathrm{F} \)-\( ^{19}\mathrm{Ne} \) doublet: predicted \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{th}} = -2.7 \times 10^{-4} \) (Q-value 3,238.4 keV, mass difference -3,238.4 keV).
- For the \( ^{29}\mathrm{Si} \)-\( ^{29}\mathrm{P} \) doublet: predicted \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{th}} = -4.1 \times 10^{-4} \) (Q-value 4,943.1 keV, mass difference -4,943.1 keV).
- For the \( ^{31}\mathrm{P} \)-\( ^{31}\mathrm{S} \) doublet: predicted \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{th}} = -4.5 \times 10^{-4} \) (Q-value 5,396.1 keV, mass difference -5,396.1 keV).
- For the \( ^{33}\mathrm{P} \)-\( ^{33}\mathrm{S}^{*} \) doublet: predicted \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{th}} = -6.7 \times 10^{-6} \) (Q-value 284.5 keV, mass difference -81.5 keV).
- For the \( ^{69}\mathrm{Zn} \)-\( ^{69}\mathrm{Ga}^{*} \) doublet: predicted \( g_{v+s}^{\mathrm{th}} = 9.0 \times 10^{-5} \) (Q-value 906.0 keV, mass difference 1,099.0 keV).
- In the MIT bag model, the nucleon scalar charge \( \langle 1_s \rangle = 0.479 \); the vector charge radius coefficient is 0.531 \( R^2 \) and the scalar charge radius coefficient is 0.182 \( R^2 \), where \( R \) is the bag radius.

## Context

This work extends the earlier approach of Gerstein and Zeldovich (1955) and Feynman and Gell-Mann (1958), who used isotopic rotation to obtain the tensor component of the weak vector current in beta decay amplitudes. The authors generalize the Ward-Takahashi identity to account for isotopic symmetry breaking, building on their own prior work (PEPAN 2015). The results enable experimental tests of the longitudinal vector form factor in muon capture reactions on nuclei such as \( ^{31}\mathrm{S} \), \( ^{29}\mathrm{P} \), \( ^{19}\mathrm{Ne} \), \( ^{15}\mathrm{O} \), and \( ^{13}\mathrm{N} \), where the effect is largest, though current measurement accuracy must be improved by two to three orders of magnitude.
