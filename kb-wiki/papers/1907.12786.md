# Production of multi-charmed hadrons by recombination in heavy ion collisions

> We study the production of multi-charmed hadrons by recombination in heavy ion collisions by focusing on the production of $Ξ_{cc}$, $Ξ_{cc}^*$, $Ω_{scc}$, $Ω_{scc}^*$, $Ω_{ccc}$ baryons and X(3872) mesons. Starting from the estimation of yields for those hadrons at chemical freeze-out in both the statistical and coalescence model, we evaluate their transverse momentum distributions at mid-rapidity in the coalescence model. We show that yields of multi-charmed hadrons in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC are large enough, and thereby not only multi-charmed hadrons observed so far, e.g., the $Ξ_{cc}$ but also those which have not been observed yet, can be discovered sufficiently in heavy ion collisions. We also find that the transverse momentum distribution ratio between various multi-charmed hadrons sensitively reflects the interplay between quark contents of corresponding hadrons as well as the transverse momentum distribution of charm quarks at the hadronization point, and therefore we insist that studying both the transverse momentum distributions of multi-charmed hadrons themselves and transverse momentum distribution ratios between various multi-charmed hadrons provide us with useful information on hadron production mechanism involving charm quarks in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim
The authors argue that yields of multi-charmed hadrons in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC are large enough for discovery, but this conclusion depends on the specific hadronization model used; the coalescence model systematically gives smaller yields than the statistical hadronization model, with suppression factors ranging from about 0.01 to 0.18 depending on the hadron species.

## Method
The authors calculated production yields of $\Xi_{cc}$, $\Xi_{cc}^*$, $\Omega_{scc}$, $\Omega_{scc}^*$, $\Omega_{ccc}$ baryons and X(3872) mesons using both the statistical hadronization model (with charm quark fugacity factors) and the coalescence model (with harmonic oscillator Wigner functions). They then evaluated transverse momentum distributions at mid-rapidity in the coalescence model using quark transverse momentum distributions parameterized from heavy ion data, assuming boost-invariant longitudinal momentum distributions and the Bjorken correlation between spatial and momentum rapidities.

## Key numbers
- Charm quark fugacity factors: $\gamma_c = 22$ at RHIC and $\gamma_c = 39$ at LHC
- Hadronization temperature and volume: $T_H = 162$ MeV, $V_H = 2100$ fm$^3$ at RHIC; $T_H = 156$ MeV, $V_H = 5380$ fm$^3$ at LHC
- Total charm quarks from initial hard collisions: 4.1 at RHIC and 11 at LHC
- Coalescence model yield of $\Xi_{cc}$ at RHIC: $1.3 \times 10^{-3}$; at LHC: $4.9 \times 10^{-3}$
- Coalescence model yield of $\Omega_{ccc}$ at RHIC: $1.1 \times 10^{-6}$; at LHC: $5.3 \times 10^{-6}$
- Coalescence model yield of X(3872) as a four-quark state ($X_4$) at RHIC: $5.3 \times 10^{-5}$; at LHC: $1.3 \times 10^{-4}$
- Yield ratio $\Omega_{ccc}/\Xi_{cc}$ in coalescence model: 0.00079 at RHIC, 0.0011 at LHC
- Yield ratio $X_4/\Xi_{cc}$ in coalescence model: 0.061 at RHIC, 0.027 at LHC
- Charm quark oscillator frequencies for Wigner function: $\omega_c = 244$ MeV for RHIC and 278 MeV for LHC

## Context
This work extends previous coalescence model studies of exotic hadron production in heavy ion collisions by Cho and collaborators (2010, 2011, 2017) to include normal multi-charmed baryons ($\Xi_{cc}$, $\Omega_{scc}$, $\Omega_{ccc}$) that have been predicted by the quark model but not yet observed in elementary collisions, except for the $\Xi_{cc}$ recently reported by LHCb (Aaij, 2017). The authors compare yields from the statistical hadronization model (Andronic, 2005; Stachel, 2013) with the coalescence model (Greco, 2003; Fries, 2003) and find that transverse momentum distribution ratios between different multi-charmed hadrons can reveal information about the charm quark transverse momentum distribution at hadronization and the quark content of the hadrons. This enables future experimental measurements at RHIC and LHC to discriminate between different hadron structures, particularly for the X(3872) meson where two-quark and four-quark interpretations give different transverse momentum distributions.
