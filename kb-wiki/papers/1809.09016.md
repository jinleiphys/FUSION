# Efimov states of three unequal bosons in non-integer dimensions

> The Efimov effect for three bosons in three dimensions requires two infinitely large $s$-wave scattering lengths. We assume two identical particles with very large scattering lengths interacting with a third particle. We use a novel mathematical technique where the centrifugal barrier contains an effective dimension parameter, which allows efficient calculations precisely as in ordinary three spatial dimensions. We investigate properties and occurrence conditions of Efimov states for such systems as functions of the third scattering length, the non-integer dimension parameter, mass ratio between unequal particles, and total angular momentum. We focus on the practical interest of the existence, number of Efimov states and their scaling properties. Decreasing the dimension parameter from $3$ towards $2$ the Efimov effect and states disappear for critical values of mass ratio, angular momentum and scattering length parameter. We investigate the relations between the four variables and extract details of where and how the states disappear. Finally, we supply a qualitative relation between the dimension parameter and an external field used to squeeze a genuine three dimensional system.

## Key claim

The Efimov effect (infinitely many bound three-body states) for two infinitely large scattering lengths is always present in three dimensions regardless of mass ratio and the third scattering length, but decreasing the dimension parameter from 3 towards 2 causes the effect to disappear at critical values that depend on mass ratio, angular momentum, and the third scattering length; the paper provides a qualitative relation between the dimension parameter and an external field used to squeeze a genuine three-dimensional system.

## Method

The authors use a hyperspherical adiabatic expansion of the Faddeev equations with a centrifugal barrier containing an effective non-integer dimension parameter \(d\). They assume two identical particles (2 and 3) with infinitely large \(s\)-wave scattering lengths \(a_2 = a_3 = \infty\) and a third particle with finite scattering length \(a_1\), solving the transcendental eigenvalue equation (Eq. 8) for the angular eigenvalue \(\lambda\) as a function of \(\rho/(\mu_{23} a_1)\). The hyperradial Hamiltonian (Eq. 2) then determines bound-state properties through the parameter \(\xi^2 = -\lambda - (d-1)^2\), where the Efimov effect occurs when \(\lambda < \lambda_c \equiv -(d-1)^2\) over an infinitely large region of the hyperradius \(\rho\).

## Key numbers

* For \(d=3\) and \(L=0\), the Efimov effect exists for all mass ratios; for non-zero \(L\), critical mass ratios are \((m_2/m_1)_{\text{crit}} = 0, 14, 41, 72, 118\) for \(L = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4\) respectively.
* The number of Efimov states is proportional to \(\xi\) and scales as \(N \simeq (\xi/\pi) \ln(|a_2|/\rho_0)\), where realistic estimates with \(|a_2|/\rho_0 = 10^4\) to \(10^6\) yield only a few states.
* The scaling factor between neighboring Efimov states is \(\exp(2\pi/\xi)\), which becomes very large for moderate mass ratios unless \(a_1\) is negative and very large.
* Critical mass ratios as functions of \(d\) are shown in Fig. 5 for \(L=0,1,2\), with the minimum critical mass occurring near \(d=3\) for \(L=0\) and shifting to higher \(d\) for larger \(L\).

## Context

This work extends the known Efimov effect for three identical bosons in three dimensions (Nielsen et al., 2001) to systems with two identical heavy particles and one light particle, using a non-integer dimension technique previously applied to squeezed systems (Sanders et al., 2018; Rosenthal et al., 2018; Levinsen et al., 2014; Yamashita et al., 2015). The results enable predictions for cold-atom experiments where external fields can continuously deform the trap from three-dimensional to quasi-two-dimensional, with the dimension parameter \(d\) providing a qualitative mapping to the squeezing strength. The dependence on the third scattering length \(a_1\) extends earlier work by Wacker et al. (2016) on finite scattering length effects.
