# Nonergodicity and Central Limit Behavior for Long-range Hamiltonians

> We present a molecular dynamics test of the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) in a paradigmatic long-range-interacting many-body classical Hamiltonian system, the HMF model. We calculate sums of velocities at equidistant times along deterministic trajectories for different sizes and energy densities. We show that, when the system is in a chaotic regime (specifically, at thermal equilibrium), ergodicity is essentially verified, and the Pdfs of the sums appear to be Gaussians, consistently with the standard CLT. When the system is, instead, only weakly chaotic (specifically, along longstanding metastable Quasi-Stationary States), nonergodicity (i.e., discrepant ensemble and time averages) is observed, and robust $q$-Gaussian attractors emerge, consistently with recently proved generalizations of the CLT.

# Nonergodicity and Central Limit Behavior for Long-range Hamiltonians

## Key claim

The standard Central Limit Theorem (CLT) fails for sums of velocities taken along deterministic trajectories in the Hamiltonian Mean Field (HMF) model when the system is in a weakly chaotic, nonergodic metastable Quasi-Stationary State (QSS), where robust q-Gaussian attractors emerge instead of Gaussian ones; however, when the system is at thermal equilibrium and fully chaotic, ergodicity is restored and the standard CLT holds.

## Method

The authors performed molecular dynamics simulations of the HMF model, a system of N fully-coupled classical inertial XY spins (rotors), using water-bag initial conditions (initial magnetization M0=1). For each rotor, they constructed probability density functions (Pdfs) of the quantity y_j = (1/sqrt(n)) * sum_{i=1}^n (p_j(i) - <p_j>), where p_j(i) are velocities taken at fixed time intervals delta along deterministic trajectories. They compared ensemble averages (averaging velocities of all N rotors at a fixed time over many realizations) with time averages (averaging along single rotor trajectories) for system sizes N=100, 5000, and 50000 at energy densities U=0.4 (no QSS) and U=0.69 (QSS present).

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature T_c = 0.5, corresponding to critical energy per particle U_c = E_c/N = 0.75 for the HMF model's second-order phase transition.
- For N=100, U=0.69 in the QSS regime: time average Pdf fitted by a q-Gaussian with q = 1.65 ± 0.05 (n=50, delta=40, transient time 100 units).
- For N=100, U=0.69 after the QSS regime: time average Pdf with delta=100 and n=50000 (total simulation time 5×10^6) approaches Gaussian, but even delta=2000 insufficient for full Gaussian shape down to 10^-4.
- For N=5000, U=0.69 in the QSS regime: time average Pdf fitted by a q-Gaussian with q = 1.45 ± 0.05 (n=1000, delta=100, average over 200 realizations).
- For N=50000, U=0.69 in the QSS regime: time average Pdf from a single realization fitted by a q-Gaussian with q = 1.4 ± 0.05 (n=5000, delta=100, simulation time 500000 units).
- For N=100, U=0.4 (no QSS, equilibrium): time average Pdf with n=50 and delta=1000 yields Gaussian shape (q approaches 1); with delta=100, q = 1.45 ± 0.05.

## Context

This work extends the study of generalized CLT behavior from deterministic dynamical systems (e.g., logistic maps at the edge of chaos, as studied by Tirnakli (2006)) to a many-body Hamiltonian system with long-range interactions. It builds on extensive prior studies of the HMF model's out-of-equilibrium dynamics, including the existence of metastable QSS with nearly vanishing Lyapunov exponents and glassy dynamics (Pluchino (2004), Rapisarda (2005)). The results are consistent with the q-generalized CLT proposed by Umarov (2006) and others, and demonstrate that nonergodicity in coupled many-particle systems due to long-range interactions poses challenges to the standard practice of using ensemble averages instead of time averages, reminiscent of the Fermi-Pasta-Ulam problem (Fermi (1955)). The findings enable future application of q-generalized statistics to long-range Hamiltonian systems and may explain the ubiquity of q-Gaussians in complex systems such as financial markets (Mantegna (1995)) and turbulence (Beck (2001)).
