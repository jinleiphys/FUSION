# Isotropic Radio Background from Quark Nugget Dark Matter

> Recent measurements by the ARCADE2 experiment unambiguously show an excess in the isotropic radio background at frequencies below the GHz scale. We argue that this excess may be a natural consequence of the interaction of visible and dark matter in the early universe if the dark matter consists of heavy nuggets of quark matter. Explanation of the observed radio band excess requires the introduction of no new parameters, rather we exploit the same dark matter model and identical normalization parameters to those previously used to explain other excesses of diffuse emission from the centre of our galaxy. These previously observed excesses include the WMAP Haze of GHz radiation, keV X -ray emission and MeV gamma-ray radiation.

# Isotropic Radio Background from Quark Nugget Dark Matter

## Key claim
The isotropic radio excess observed by ARCADE2 at frequencies below 1 GHz may be naturally explained by thermal emission from quark nugget dark matter, without introducing any new parameters beyond those already used to explain the WMAP haze, keV X-ray emission, and MeV gamma-ray excesses from the Galactic center. The claim is conditional on the nugget temperature at last scattering being approximately 0.2 eV (fitted to the ARCADE2 data), which is consistent with the order-of-magnitude estimate of T_LS ~ 1 eV from the model.

## Method
The authors computed the thermal emission spectrum of quark nuggets using a previously derived expression based on positron scattering in the nugget's electrosphere, accounting for a low-energy cutoff at ~10^-4 eV due to finite nugget size. They then integrated the source density over all redshifts from the time of last scattering to the present day, using the temperature evolution T(z) = T_LS * ((1+z)/(1+z_LS))^(17/4) derived from balancing annihilation heating against thermal cooling, and correcting for the redshifting of emitted photons. The integration assumed no late-time reheating from structure formation, which was shown to introduce variations of only a few percent.

## Key numbers
- The nugget baryon charge is |B| ~ 10^24, giving a number density so low that the effective interaction cross section per mass is sigma/M ~ 10^-10 cm^2/g, well below the astrophysical limit of sigma/M < 1 cm^2/g.
- The predicted antenna temperature was computed assuming a mean nugget baryon number B ~ 10^25 and a nugget temperature of 0.2 eV at the time of CMB formation.
- The low-energy cutoff on the thermal spectrum is at E_cut ~ 10^-4 eV, corresponding to a typical nugget radius R ~ 10^-5 cm.
- The matter flux onto nuggets at the Galactic center is approximately 6 x 10^9 GeV/(cm^2 s), while at last scattering it is approximately 3 x 10^8 GeV/(cm^2 s).
- The observed antenna temperature grows with a power index of approximately three below the CMB peak, consistent with the ARCADE2 data.

## Context
This work extends the quark nugget dark matter model, previously applied to explain Galactic center excesses including the WMAP haze (Finkbeiner 2003), 511 keV emission from positronium decay observed by SPI Integral (Jean 2005), MeV gamma-ray excess from COMPTEL (Strong 2004), and X-ray excess from Chandra (Muno 2004), from galactic to cosmological scales. The model was originally proposed by Zhitnitsky (2002) and Oaknin and Zhitnitsky (2003) as a charge-separation mechanism during the QCD phase transition. The present analysis enables a unified explanation of multiple diffuse emission puzzles across 13 orders of magnitude in frequency, and suggests that the charge separation mechanism can be experimentally tested in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, where similar CP-odd environments with theta ~ 1 are achieved.
