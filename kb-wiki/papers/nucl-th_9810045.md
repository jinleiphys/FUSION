# Having a BLAST with Excited Baryons

> We discuss the role BLAST can play in the valuable kinematic window of low W and low Q^2.

## Key claim

BLAST can make a significant impact in the narrow kinematic window of low W and low Q^2 (up to roughly 0.5 GeV^2), but only if very high precision experiments are performed; anything less would not be useful to the physics community.

## Method

The paper reviews the potential of the BLAST detector at the upgraded Bates facility to study excited baryons via pion electroproduction at low W (first resonance region, Roper, N*(1535), N*(1520)) and low Q^2. It uses the effective Lagrangian approach of Davidson, Mukhopadhyay, and Wittman (DMW) to generate model predictions for differential cross sections and the E2/M1 ratio for the N-Delta(1232) transition, and discusses how these compare with existing data from Mainz, NIKHEF, GRAAL, and LEGS.

## Key numbers

- The chiral perturbation theory (CHPT) region of validity for pion threshold calculations is up to Q^2 of 0.2 GeV^2.
- Low Q^2 data up to roughly 0.5 GeV^2 are extremely important for testing nonrelativistic hadron models.
- The E2/M1 ratio for the N-Delta(1232) transition appears to change from negative to positive at about 0.15 GeV^2, then becomes negative again at about 0.4 GeV^2.
- Model predictions for differential cross sections are shown at Q^2 = 0.5 GeV^2, W = 1.23 GeV, and epsilon = 0.5, with E2/M1 values of 0%, -10%, and +10%.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of baryon resonance physics at low momentum transfer, where hadron models (bag model, quark model, Skyrme model) and lattice calculations are most reliable. It specifically references the DMW effective Lagrangian approach (Davidson, Mukhopadhyay, Wittman) for extracting the E2/M1 ratio, and notes that existing data from NIKHEF (Ben) and Mainz (Distler) lack polarization measurements. The paper enables future BLAST experiments to test CHPT, QCD-inspired models, and the hypothesis that the Roper resonance is a hybrid state, as well as to study the N*(1535) transition form factor which appears to fall more slowly than a dipole.
