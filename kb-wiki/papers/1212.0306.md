# Intermediate state interaction in the particle oscillations in absorbing matter

## Key claim
The self-energy of intermediate particles plays a crucial role in particle oscillations in absorbing matter, and the result is extremely sensitive to this self-energy; the uncertainty in the value of the lower limit on the free-space neutron-antineutron oscillation time is conditioned by the uncertainty in the value of the antineutron self-energy.

## Method
The author compares two field-theoretical models for neutron-antineutron transitions in a medium followed by antineutron annihilation: model **a** uses a bare antineutron propagator with zero self-energy, while model **b** uses a dressed propagator with a nonzero self-energy introduced via a residual scalar field. The process amplitude is calculated in lowest order in the neutron-antineutron conversion Hamiltonian, and the resulting transition probability and lower limits on the free-space oscillation time are derived for each model.

## Key numbers
- For model **b** with estimated antineutron self-energy Σ = 10 MeV and annihilation width Γ = 100 MeV, using the Super-Kamiokande experimental bound T_{n\bar{n}} > 1.77 × 10^32 yr, the lower limit on the free-space neutron-antineutron oscillation time is τ^b = 1.2 × 10^9 s.
- For model **a** (zero self-energy), the lower limit on the free-space neutron-antineutron oscillation time is τ^a = 10^16 yr.
- If Σ changes in the range 10 MeV > Σ > 0 MeV, the lower limit on the free-space neutron-antineutron oscillation time lies in the range 10^16 yr > τ > 1.2 × 10^9 s.
- For model **a** with Γt ≫ 1, the process probability follows W_a(t) ≈ ε_{n\bar{n}}^2 t^2, equal to the free-space neutron-antineutron transition probability.

## Context
This work extends the author's previous field-theoretical approach (Nazaruk, 2012) for neutron-antineutron transitions in matter, which had only considered the case of zero antineutron self-energy. The paper contradicts the standard one-particle potential model used in earlier calculations of particle oscillations in absorbing media (references [1,2] in the text), arguing that such models fail because they use non-Hermitian operators and violate unitarity. The results show that the self-energy of intermediate particles must be studied further before reliable predictions can be made for processes such as neutron-antineutron oscillations in nuclei or lambda-antilambda transitions in matter followed by antilambda decay.
