# Effects of Initial Nucleon-Nucleon Correlations on Light Nuclei Production in Au+Au Collisions at $\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}} = 3\ $ GeV

> Light nuclei production in heavy-ion collisions serves as a sensitive probe of the QCD phase structure. In coalescence models, triton ($N_t$) and deuteron ($N_d$) yields depend on the spatial separation of nucleon pairs ($Δr$) in Wigner functions, yet the impact of initial two-nucleon correlations $ρ(Δr)$ remains underexplored. We develop a method to sample nucleons in $^{197}$Au nuclei that simultaneously satisfies both the single-particle distribution $f(r)$ and the two-nucleon correlation $ρ(Δr)$. Using these nuclei, we simulate Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}}=3$ GeV via the SMASH transport model (mean-field mode) to calculate proton, deuteron, and triton yields. Simulations reveal a 36% enhancement in mid-rapidity deuteron yields across all centrality ranges and a 33% rise in mid-rapidity triton production for 0-10% central collisions. Calculated transverse momentum of light nuclei aligns with STAR data. We further analyze impacts of baryon conservation, spectator exclusion, and centrality determination via charged multiplicity. Notably, observed discrepancies in the double yield ratio suggest unaccounted physical mechanisms, such as critical fluctuations or inaccuracies in coalescence parameters or light nuclei cross-sections. This underscores the critical role of initial nucleon-nucleon correlations, linking microscopic nuclear structure to intermediate-energy collision dynamics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that incorporating initial two-nucleon correlations (the relative distance distribution between nucleon pairs) into the initial nuclear structure of gold nuclei produces a 36% enhancement in mid-rapidity deuteron yields across all centrality ranges and a 33% rise in mid-rapidity triton production for 0-10% central collisions in Au+Au collisions at 3 GeV, but the observed discrepancies in the double yield ratio suggest that additional physical mechanisms such as critical fluctuations or inaccuracies in coalescence parameters remain unaccounted for.

## Method

The authors developed a Monte Carlo sampling method for nucleons in gold nuclei that simultaneously satisfies both the single-particle Wood-Saxon distribution and the two-nucleon correlation function from ab-initio calculations. They then simulated Au+Au collisions at 3 GeV using the SMASH transport model in mean-field mode with event ensembles and Pauli-blocking, calculating proton, deuteron, and triton yields via both the SMASH resonance mechanism (for deuterons) and a coalescence model using Wigner functions (for tritons), with baryon conservation corrections applied to remove double counting of deuterons used in triton production.

## Key numbers

- 36% enhancement in mid-rapidity deuteron yields across all centrality ranges when initial nucleon-nucleon correlations are included
- 33% rise in mid-rapidity triton production for 0-10% central collisions with nucleon-nucleon correlations
- Skyrme potential parameters: a = -209.2 MeV, b = 156.4 MeV, tau = 1.35, with saturation density rho_0 = 0.168 fm^{-3}
- Wood-Saxon parameters for gold: rho_0 = 0.17 fm^{-3}, R_A = 6.38 fm, d_A = 0.535 fm
- 200,000 events simulated both with and without nucleon-nucleon correlation, distributed across four centrality classes (0-10%, 10-20%, 20-40%, 40-80%)
- Coalescence parameter sigma_rho for two-body triton set to 2.155 fm
- Symmetry potential S_pot = 18 MeV

## Context

This work extends prior studies using transport models such as BUU and QMD (Bass 1998, Bertsch 1984) and the SMASH framework (SMASH collaboration 2016) by adding realistic two-nucleon correlations from ab-initio calculations (Alvioli 2005, 2009) that were previously neglected in heavy-ion collision simulations. The results connect microscopic nuclear structure to intermediate-energy collision dynamics and provide a foundation for interpreting RHIC-STAR Beam Energy Scan data at 3 GeV, with the remaining discrepancies in the double yield ratio pointing toward the need for future investigations of critical fluctuations, coalescence parameter uncertainties, and light nuclei cross-sections.
