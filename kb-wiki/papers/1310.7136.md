# Are the Angular Correlations in pA Collisions due to a Glasmion or Bose Condensation ?

> Experiments at the LHC have recently reported results on the angular asymmetry coefficients $v_n[m]$, for various angular moments $n$ and orders of cumulants $m$, in high multiplicity p+Pb collisions. These coefficients are large, and have both even and odd moments. We discuss here some of the implications of these results for our understanding of the initial state of the collision (Color Glass Condensate) and for the evolution in the final state (Glasma and thermalized Quark Gluon Plasma). We show the Color Glass Condensate predicts large even moments, $v_n$ with $n$ an even integer. Odd moments are generated by final state interactions or fragmentation. For a multi-particle determination of $v_2[m]$, where m is the number of particles used to determine the correlation, we argue that if these coefficients approach equality for large $m$ in high multiplicity events, this may imply the existence of either solitonic solutions or Bose condensation either for the JIMWLK action that describes the CGC, or for the Glasma that might be produced in such a collision.

## Key claim

The paper argues that if the multi-particle cumulant coefficients \(v_2[m]\) approach equality for large \(m\) in high-multiplicity p+Pb collisions, this may imply the existence of either solitonic solutions or Bose condensation in the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) or the Glasma, rather than a conventional hydrodynamic flow field.

## Method

The authors analyze the theoretical predictions of the Color Glass Condensate for angular correlation coefficients \(v_n[m]\) in p+Pb collisions, comparing even and odd moments. They examine the behavior of \(v_2[2]\) and \(v_2[4]\) from experimental data and argue that the near-equality of these coefficients in high-multiplicity events suggests small event-by-event fluctuations, which could be explained by a dominant classical field configuration (soliton or condensate) rather than a fluctuating mean field.

## Key numbers

- The ratio \(r = v_2[4]^2/v_2[2]^2\) is approximately 0.8 for peripheral Pb+Pb collisions, indicating small fluctuations.
- For central Pb+Pb collisions, \(r \sim 0.2\), showing large fluctuation effects.
- In high-multiplicity p+Pb collisions, CMS data show that \(v_2[4]\) is quite close to \(v_2[2]\), with small fluctuations as quantified by \(\Delta = \sqrt{(v_2[2]^2 - v_2[4]^2)/(v_2[2]^2 + v_2[4])}\).
- The CGC computation yields a contribution proportional to \(\delta^{(2)}(\vec{q}_1 + \vec{q}_2) + \delta^{(2)}(\vec{q}_1 - \vec{q}_2) = (1/(\pi q_1))\delta(q_1-q_2)\sum_n e^{2ni(\theta_1 - \theta_2)}\), where all even moments have equal weight.

## Context

This work extends the CGC-based description of two-particle correlations in p+Pb collisions (Dumitru 2008, Dusling 2012) and addresses the puzzle that high-multiplicity p+Pb data show small flow fluctuations similar to peripheral Pb+Pb collisions, unlike central heavy-ion collisions where fluctuations dominate (ALICE 2012, PHOBOS 2006). The paper proposes that a solitonic or Bose-condensed configuration could explain the data, and suggests that measuring \(v_2[6]\) would test this hypothesis. It also references recent numerical evidence for area-law scaling of spatial Wilson loops and Z(N) vortex structure in classical fields from color charge collisions (Dumitru 2013).
