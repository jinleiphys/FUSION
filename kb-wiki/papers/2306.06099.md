# NuCLR: Nuclear Co-Learned Representations

> We introduce Nuclear Co-Learned Representations (NuCLR), a deep learning model that predicts various nuclear observables, including binding and decay energies, and nuclear charge radii. The model is trained using a multi-task approach with shared representations and obtains state-of-the-art performance, achieving levels of precision that are crucial for understanding fundamental phenomena in nuclear (astro)physics. We also report an intriguing finding that the learned representation of NuCLR exhibits the prominent emergence of crucial aspects of the nuclear shell model, namely the shell structure, including the well-known magic numbers, and the Pauli Exclusion Principle. This suggests that the model is capable of capturing the underlying physical principles and that our approach has the potential to offer valuable insights into nuclear theory.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a multi-task deep learning model trained only on proton number, neutron number, and task identity can achieve state-of-the-art precision for predicting nuclear observables, and that its learned latent representations spontaneously exhibit the shell structure, magic numbers, and the Pauli Exclusion Principle. The hedge is that the model's interpretable shell-structure emergence is most clearly visible when the imprecise Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula predictions are included as an auxiliary input, and that the final spiral structure of the embeddings requires further study to understand.

## Method

The authors train a neural network with learned embeddings for proton number Z, neutron number N, and task identity, concatenated and processed through residual blocks with a final multi-task output layer. They perform 100-fold cross-validation on the AME2020 dataset for nuclei with Z,N > 8, predicting binding energy per nucleon, charge radius, neutron and proton separation energies, and energies for alpha, beta, electron-capture, and beta-neutron decays, while withholding neighboring nuclei to prevent information leakage.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy prediction RMS: 130 keV (for nuclei with Z,N > 8, using AME2020 dataset)
- Charge radius prediction RMS: 0.011 fm (for nuclei with Z,N > 8, using compilation of Angeli (2013))
- Neutron separation energy RMS: 130 keV; proton separation energy RMS: 140 keV
- Decay energy RMS values: 139 keV (alpha), 180 keV (beta), 179 keV (electron-capture), 196 keV (beta-neutron)
- For nuclei with N,Z > 28, binding energy RMS is 79 keV, compared to binding energies of order 7000 times (Z+N) keV
- Model has 11,869,194 parameters, trained for 50,000 epochs with learning rate 0.01 and batch size 4096

## Context

This work extends the multi-task kernel ridge regression approach of Wu (2022) that achieved 140 keV precision for binding and separation energies, but unlike that model does not require high-precision analytical model predictions as inputs. The model achieves precision below 100 keV for binding energies and 0.01 fm for charge radii, which the authors note is the threshold needed for understanding r-process nucleosynthesis as discussed by Martin (2015) and Mumpower (2015). The interpretable embeddings offer a path toward learning physical principles from machine learning models, potentially enabling predictions for neutron star structure, nuclear landscape boundaries, and the CKM unitarity puzzle.
