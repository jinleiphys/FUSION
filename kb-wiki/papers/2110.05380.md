# Vortical Quantum Memory

> Quantum memory is a crucial component of a quantum information processor, just like a classical memory is a necessary ingredient of a conventional computer. Moreover, quantum memory of light would serve as a quantum repeater needed for quantum communication networks. Here we propose to realize the quantum memory coupled to magnetic modes of electromagnetic radiation (e.g. those found in cavities and optical fibers) using vortices in Quantum Hall systems. We describe the response to an external magnetic mode as a quench, and find that it sets an oscillation between vortices and antivortices, with the period controlled by the amplitude of the magnetic mode, and the phase locked to the phase of the magnetic mode. We support our proposal by real-time Hamiltonian field theory simulations.

## Key claim

The authors propose that a quantum memory coupled to magnetic modes of electromagnetic radiation can be realized using vortices in Quantum Hall systems, where the period of oscillation between vortices and antivortices is controlled by the amplitude of the magnetic mode and the phase is locked to the phase of the magnetic mode. However, the quantum state formed by the topological quench is an excited state, not a ground state of the Quantum Hall system at a given value of external magnetic flux.

## Method

The authors used a two-band model of a Quantum Anomalous Hall system described by a Hamiltonian with parameters \(R_x = \sin k_x\), \(R_y = \sin k_y\), and \(R_z = m - \cos k_x - \cos k_y\). They performed a "topological quench" by abruptly changing the mass parameter \(m\) from an initial value to a new value \(m'\) at time \(t=0\), then computed the time evolution of the system using real-time Hamiltonian field theory simulations, measuring the Loschmidt amplitude and the vorticity of the Berry connection in the Brillouin zone.

## Context

This work extends the concept of using topological systems for quantum memory, building on prior studies of vortex-antivortex oscillations in quantum Hall systems by Wang et al. (2007) which reported oscillations with period \(2\pi/\Delta E\). The proposal connects to experimental realizations of two-band models in photonic crystals (Ningyuan et al., 2016), microwave resonators (Peterson et al., 2018), superconducting qubits (Niu et al., 2021), and phononic metamaterials (Serra et al., 2018). The work enables future development of optical readout schemes for the quantum memory based on the oscillating Quantum Hall conductivity affecting reflection and transmission of electromagnetic waves.
