# Possible signatures for strange stars in stellar X-ray binaries

> Kilohertz quasi-periodic brightness oscillations (kHz QPOs) observed in certain X-ray burst sources may represent Keplerian frequencies in the inner regions of the accretion disk in such systems. If this assumption is strictly adhered to, we show here that if the central accretor in stellar X-ray burst sources is a strange star (made up of u, d and s quarks in beta equilibrium, referred to as strange matter) then the calculated QPO frequencies are reconcilable with the observed QPO frequencies (corresponding to the highest frequency of 1.22 kHz, observed so far from the source 4U 1636-53) only for particular values of the QCD-related parameters which describe the equation of state of strange matter. We demonstrate that QPO frequencies in the very high range (1.9-3.1) kHz can be understood in terms of a (non- magnetized) strange star X-ray binary (SSXB) rather than a neutron star X-ray binary (NSXB). Future discovery of such high frequency QPOs from X-ray burst sources will constitute a new astrophysical di- agnostic for identifying solar mass range stable strange stars in our galaxy.

## Key claim

If the highest observed kHz QPO frequency of 1.22 kHz (from source 4U 1636-53) is strictly assumed to originate at the marginally stable orbit of the central accretor, then a strange star explanation is admissible only for low values of the bag constant and very slowly rotating configurations; however, QPO frequencies in the range 1.9-3.1 kHz can be understood in terms of a non-magnetized strange star X-ray binary rather than a neutron star X-ray binary.

## Method

The authors constructed equilibrium sequences of rapidly rotating strange stars using a general relativistic formalism for axisymmetric stationary space-times, solving the Einstein field equations via a Green's function approach. They computed the Keplerian frequencies at the innermost stable circular orbit and at the stellar surface for three different equations of state: two based on the MIT bag model with different bag pressures, strange quark masses, and QCD coupling constants, and one phenomenological model by Dey et al. (1998). The resulting Keplerian frequencies were compared with observed kHz QPO frequencies under the assumption that these QPOs represent orbital motion in the accretion disk.

## Key numbers

- Highest observed kHz QPO frequency to date: 1.22 kHz, from the source 4U 1636-53
- For a non-rotating strange star with MIT bag model EOS (massless non-interacting quarks): $\nu_K(r_{ms}, M_{max}) = 1.081 (B/56)^{1/2}$ kHz, where B is in MeV fm$^{-3}$
- For a 1 solar mass strange star, the Kepler frequency ranges are: (2.2-2.3) kHz for EOS model (i), (1.8-1.9) kHz for EOS model (ii), and (2-2.6) kHz for EOS model (iii)
- For a 1.4 solar mass strange star, the Kepler frequency ranges are: (1.57-1.84) kHz for EOS model (i), (1.57-1.87) kHz for EOS model (ii), and (1.57-1.79) kHz for EOS model (iii)
- The maximum Kepler frequency from EOS model (iii) is 3 kHz
- For a 1 solar mass strange star, the radial location where $\nu_K = 1.22$ kHz occurs is about $4.5 r_g$ (where $r_g = 2GM/c^2$)
- For a 1.4 solar mass strange star, the radius at which $\nu_{QPO} = 1.22$ kHz is produced lies in the range (3.53-3.55) $r_g$

## Context

This work extends the study of kHz QPOs in X-ray binaries, previously used to estimate neutron star masses by Kaaret, Ford and Chen (1997), Zhang, Strohmayer and Swank (1997), and Kluzniak (1998), to the possibility of strange stars as the central accretor. It builds on earlier suggestions by Cheng et al. (1998) and Li et al. (1999a, 1999b) that sources like GRO J1744-28, SAX J1808.4-3658, and 4U 1728-34 may be accreting strange star binaries, and on the work of Bulik, Gondek-Rosinska and Kluzniak (1999) on consequences of the SSXB hypothesis. The results enable future discovery of QPOs in the 1.9-3.1 kHz range to serve as a diagnostic for identifying solar-mass stable strange stars in the galaxy.
