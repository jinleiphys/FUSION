# Gauge-covariant canonical formalism revisited with application to the proton spin decomposition

> We revisit the gauge-covariant canonical formalism by separating explicitly physical and gauge degrees of freedom. We show in particular that the gauge-invariant linear and angular momentum operators proposed by Chen et al. can consistently be derived from the standard procedure based on the Noether's theorem. Finally, we demonstrate that this approach is essentially equivalent to the gauge-invariant canonical formalism based on the concept of Dirac variables. Because of many similarities with the background field method, the formalism developed here should also be relevant to general relativity and any metric theories.

# Gauge-covariant canonical formalism revisited with application to the proton spin decomposition

## Key claim

The gauge-invariant linear and angular momentum operators proposed by Chen et al. can be consistently derived from the standard Noether's theorem procedure, provided one first separates the gauge field into physical and pure-gauge parts and then uses the pure-gauge covariant derivative in place of the ordinary partial derivative. This approach is essentially equivalent to the gauge-invariant canonical formalism based on Dirac variables, but the equivalence holds only up to a Stueckelberg symmetry that introduces a non-uniqueness in the decomposition.

## Method

The author decomposes the gauge field as \(A_\mu = A^\text{pure}_\mu + A^\text{phys}_\mu\), where the pure-gauge part satisfies \(F^\text{pure}_{\mu\nu}=0\) and transforms as a gauge field, while the physical part transforms as an internal tensor. The Lagrangian is then re-expressed in terms of fields and their pure-gauge covariant derivatives \(D^\text{pure}_\mu = \partial_\mu - igA^\text{pure}_\mu\). The standard canonical formalism (Euler-Lagrange equations and Noether's theorem) is applied using this covariant derivative, yielding gauge-covariant equations and conserved currents. The approach is applied to QCD to derive the gauge-invariant energy-momentum and angular-momentum tensors, and is shown to be formally equivalent to constructing gauge-invariant Dirac variables \(\hat\phi = U^{-1}_\text{pure}\phi\).

## Key numbers

- The gauge-invariant canonical energy-momentum tensor for QCD is \(T^{\mu\nu} = T^{\mu\nu}_q + T^{\mu\nu}_g - g^{\mu\nu}\mathcal{L}\), with quark contribution \(T^{\mu\nu}_q = i\,\bar\psi \gamma^\mu \stackrel{\leftrightarrow}{D}^\nu_\text{pure}\psi\) and gluon contribution \(T^{\mu\nu}_g = -2\,\text{Tr}[F^{\mu\alpha}\mathcal{D}^\nu_\text{pure} A^\text{phys}_\alpha]\).
- The gauge-invariant canonical generalized angular-momentum tensor for QCD decomposes into four terms: quark spin \(M^{\mu\nu\rho}_{q,\text{spin}} = \frac{1}{2}\,\bar\psi\{\gamma^\mu,\Sigma^{\nu\rho}\}\psi\), quark OAM \(M^{\mu\nu\rho}_{q,\text{OAM}} = i\,\bar\psi \gamma^\mu x^{[\nu}\stackrel{\leftrightarrow}{D}^{\rho]}_\text{pure}\psi\), gluon spin \(M^{\mu\nu\rho}_{g,\text{spin}} = -2\,\text{Tr}[F^{\mu[\nu} A^{\rho]}_\text{phys}]\), and gluon OAM \(M^{\mu\nu\rho}_{g,\text{OAM}} = -2\,\text{Tr}[F^{\mu\alpha}x^{[\nu} \mathcal{D}^{\rho]}_\text{pure}A^\text{phys}_\alpha]\).
- The pure-gauge field satisfies \(F^\text{pure}_{\mu\nu}=0\) by definition, and can be written as \(A_\mu^\text{pure}(x) = \frac{i}{g}\,U_\text{pure}(x)\partial_\mu U^{-1}_\text{pure}(x)\).
- The Stueckelberg transformation acts on the right of \(U_\text{pure}(x)\) as \(U_\text{pure}(x) \mapsto U^g_\text{pure}(x) = U_\text{pure}(x)U^{-1}_0(x)\), distinct from the ordinary gauge transformation which acts on the left.

## Context

This work extends the Schwinger approach of separating physical and gauge degrees of freedom (originally developed by Schwinger (1962) and Arnowitt and Fickler (1962)) into a full canonical formalism consistent with gauge symmetry, filling a gap noted by the author. It provides a derivation of the gauge-invariant decomposition of proton spin proposed by Chen et al. (2008, 2011) and Wakamatsu (2010), showing that their operators are the generators of translations and Lorentz transformations for physical fields. The formalism shares many similarities with the background field method introduced by DeWitt (1967), suggesting applicability to general relativity and metric theories. The Stueckelberg symmetry introduced by the decomposition is related to the path dependence of Wilson lines in parton distribution functions and to the Aharonov-Bohm effect (1959), and the approach should be considered a perturbative construction due to Gribov ambiguities in non-abelian theories.
