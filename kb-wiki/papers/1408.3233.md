# Boundary terms in quantum field theory and the spin structure of QCD

> Determining how boundary terms behave in a quantum field theory (QFT) is crucial for understanding the dynamics of the theory. Nevertheless, boundary terms are often neglected using classical-type arguments which are no longer justified in the full quantum theory. In this paper we address this problem by establishing a necessary and sufficient condition for arbitrary spatial boundary terms to vanish in a general QFT. As an application of this condition we examine the issue of whether the angular momentum operator in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) has a physically meaningful quark-gluon decomposition. Using this condition it appears as though this is not the case, and that it is in fact the non-perturbative QCD structure which prevents the possibility of such a decomposition.

## Key claim

The paper establishes a necessary and sufficient condition for spatial boundary term operators to vanish in the physical Hilbert space of a general quantum field theory, and applies this condition to argue that the angular momentum operator in QCD cannot have a physically meaningful quark-gluon decomposition because the non-perturbative QCD structure prevents the required boundary terms from vanishing.

## Method

The author first proves a theorem stating that a spatial boundary term operator of the form ∫ d³x ∂ᵢBⁱ vanishes in the physical Hilbert space ℋ if and only if it annihilates the vacuum state. This proof relies on the local (anti-)commutativity axiom, the Reeh-Schlieder Theorem, and the non-degeneracy of the inner product on ℋ. The author then applies this theorem to the QCD angular momentum decomposition problem by examining the Jaffe-Manohar decomposition, which requires dropping superpotential terms (total divergence terms) from the Lorentz current M^{μνλ}_{QCD} to obtain a partitioned form where individual terms can be interpreted as distinct angular momentum sources.

## Context

This work extends prior discussions by Ojima (1979) and Kastler (1980) on defining consistent local charge operators in quantum field theory, and directly addresses the proton spin decomposition problem initiated by the European Muon Collaboration (1988) experiment which suggested quarks account for only a small fraction of proton spin. The paper contradicts the assumption made in the Jaffe-Manohar decomposition (1990) and subsequent works by Ji (1997) that superpotential terms can be dropped using classical-type arguments, and instead demonstrates that the vanishing of such boundary terms depends on whether they annihilate the vacuum state, which is a non-perturbative condition that cannot be assumed in QCD.
