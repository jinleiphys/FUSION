# Mathematical aspects of the nuclear glory phenomenon; backward focusing and Chebyshev polynomials

> The angular dependence of the cumulative particles production off nuclei near the kinematical boundary for multistep process is defined by characteristic polynomials in angular variables, describing spatial momenta of the particles in intermediate and final states. Physical argumentation, exploring the small phase space method, leads to the appearance of equations for these polynomials in $cos (θ/N)$, where $θ$ is the polar angle of the momentum of final (cumulative) particle, the integer $N$ being the multiplicity of the process (the number of interactions). It is shown explicitly how these equations appear, and the recurrent relations between polynomials with different $N$ are obtained. Factorization properties of characteristic polynomials found previously, are extended, and their connection with known in mathematics Chebyshev polynomials of 2-d kind is established. As a result, differential cross section of the cumulative particle production has characteristic behaviour $dσ\sim 1/ \sqrt {π- θ}$ near the strictly backward direction ($θ= π$, the backward focusing effect). Such behaviour takes place for any multiplicity of the interaction, beginning with $n=3$, elastic or inelastic (with resonance excitations in intermediate states), and can be called the nuclear glory phenomenon, or 'Buddha's light' of cumulative particles.

# Mathematical aspects of the nuclear glory phenomenon; backward focusing and Chebyshev polynomials

## Key claim

The differential cross section of cumulative particle production exhibits a characteristic $d\sigma \sim 1/\sqrt{\pi - \theta}$ divergence near the strictly backward direction ($\theta = \pi$) for any multiplicity of interaction beginning with $n=3$, including both elastic and inelastic processes with resonance excitations in intermediate states, provided the small phase space method is applied to multistep processes near the kinematical boundary.

## Method

The author applies the small phase space method, developed previously in Kopeliovich (2002, 2003) and Kopeliovich (2005), to calculate the probability of multiple interaction processes near the corresponding kinematical boundaries for cumulative particle production. The method introduces quadratic forms in angular variables (azimuthal deviations $\varphi_k$ and polar deviations $\vartheta_k$ from the optimal kinematics where all scattering angles equal $\theta/N$) and integrates over these variables using delta-function constraints. Recurrent relations for characteristic polynomials $J_N^2(z)$ in $z = \cos(\theta/N)$ are derived, and their connection to Chebyshev polynomials of the second kind (Chebyshev-Korkin-Zolotarev polynomials) is established through the relation $(2z)^{N-1} J_N^2(z) = U_{N-1}(z) = \sin\theta / \sin(\theta/N)$.

## Key numbers

- For light particle rescattering, the maximal final energy is $\omega_N^{max} \simeq N (2m/\theta^2)$ at large $N$ and large incident energy.
- For nucleon rescattering, the maximal final momentum is $p_N^{max} \simeq N (2m/\theta^2)$, with preasymptotic corrections larger than in the light particle case.
- Including normal Fermi motion of nucleons inside the nucleus, the maximal momentum becomes $p_N^{max} \simeq N (2m/\theta^2) [1 + (p_F^{max}/2m)(\theta + 1/\theta)]$, with $p_F^{max}/m \simeq 0.27$.
- The determinant of the quadratic form matrix for $N=3$ is $J_3^2 = 1 - 1/(4\cos^2(\theta/3))$, and $J_3(\pi/3) = 0$ at $z=1/2$.
- For $N=4$, $J_4^2 = 1 - 1/(2\cos^2(\theta/4))$, and $J_4(\pi/4)=0$ at $z=1/\sqrt{2}$.
- For $N=5$, $J_5^2 = 1 - 3/(4z^2) + 1/(16z^4)$.
- For $N=6$, $J_6^2 = 1 - 1/z^2 + 3/(16z^4) = J_3^2 (1 - 3/(4z^2))$.
- The relation $(2z)^{N-1} J_N^2(z) \sin(\theta/N) = \sin\theta$ holds, giving $J_N^2(z) = \sin\theta / [(2z)^{N-1} \sin(\theta/N)]$.
- The limit $J_N^2|_{\theta \to 0} = N/2^{N-1}$.

## Context

This work extends the small phase space method developed by Kopeliovich (2002, 2003) and Kopeliovich (2005) for calculating multiple interaction process contributions to cumulative particle production cross sections near kinematical boundaries. It provides the mathematically complete explanation of the method's details that were previously incomplete, and establishes the connection between the characteristic polynomials $J_N^2$ appearing in the quadratic forms of angular deviations and Chebyshev polynomials of the second kind (Chebyshev-Korkin-Zolotarev polynomials) known since the mid-19th century. The factorization properties of these polynomials are extended, enabling simplified calculation of their roots and providing a rigorous foundation for the backward focusing effect (nuclear glory phenomenon or "Buddha's light" of cumulative particles), which had been noted in earlier work by Kopeliovich (2002) and Kopeliovich (2005).
