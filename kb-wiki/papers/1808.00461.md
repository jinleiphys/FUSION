# Long-term GRMHD Simulations of Neutron Star Merger Accretion Disks: Implications for Electromagnetic Counterparts

> We investigate the long-term evolution of black hole accretion disks formed in neutron star mergers. These disks expel matter that contributes to an $r$-process kilonova, and can produce relativistic jets powering short gamma-ray bursts. Here we report the results of a three-dimensional, general-relativistic magnetohydrodynamic (GRMHD) simulation of such a disk which is evolved for long enough ($\sim 9$s, or $\sim 6\times 10^5 r_{\rm g}/c$) to achieve completion of mass ejection far from the disk. Our model starts with a poloidal field, and fully resolves the most unstable mode of the magnetorotational instability. We parameterize the dominant microphysics and neutrino cooling effects, and compare with axisymmetric hydrodynamic models with shear viscosity. The GRMHD model ejects mass in two ways: a prompt MHD-mediated outflow and a late-time, thermally-driven wind once the disk becomes advective. The total amount of unbound mass ejected ($0.013M_\odot$, or $\simeq 40\%$ of the initial torus mass) is twice as much as in hydrodynamic models, with higher average velocity ($0.1c$) and a broad electron fraction distribution with a lower average value ($0.16$). Scaling the ejected fractions to a disk mass of $\sim 0.1M_\odot$ can account for the red kilonova from GW170817 but underpredicts the blue component. About $\sim 10^{-3}M_\odot$ of material should undergo neutron freezout and could produce a bright kilonova precursor in the first few hours after the merger. With our idealized initial magnetic field configuration, we obtain a robust jet and sufficient ejecta with Lorentz factor $\sim 1-10$ to (over)produce the non-thermal emission from GW1708107.

## Key claim

The GRMHD simulation of a neutron star merger accretion disk, evolved for ~9 seconds to achieve completion of mass ejection, ejects 40% of the initial torus mass as unbound material, twice the fraction seen in hydrodynamic models with shear viscosity, but the authors caution that their results are exploratory because higher spatial resolution, more physics, and more realistic initial conditions are needed for quantitative predictions of kilonova and r-process nucleosynthesis contributions.

## Method

The authors performed a three-dimensional general-relativistic magnetohydrodynamic (GRMHD) simulation of a black hole accretion disk formed in a neutron star merger, using the HARMPI code with a Kerr metric (black hole mass 3 solar masses, spin 0.8). The simulation started from an equilibrium torus with an initial poloidal magnetic field, included parameterized neutrino cooling via charged-current weak interactions on nucleons, and accounted for nuclear recombination into alpha particles, evolving the disk for ~9 seconds (~6 × 10^5 r_g/c) until most mass was either accreted or ejected. For comparison, axisymmetric hydrodynamic simulations with shear viscosity (alpha viscosity parameters 0.01, 0.03, and 0.10) were also performed using a modified version of FLASH3.

## Key numbers

- Total unbound mass ejected: 0.013 solar masses, or 39% of the initial torus mass (0.033 solar masses), compared to 16-22% in hydrodynamic models
- Average outflow velocity: 0.1c (10% of light speed)
- Average electron fraction of outflow: 0.16, with a broad distribution
- Lanthanide-poor ejecta (Y_e > 0.25): 0.04 of initial torus mass (0.0012 solar masses), with average velocity 0.22c
- Maximum magnetic field strength in initial disk: approximately 4 × 10^14 Gauss
- Approximately 10^-3 solar masses of material should undergo neutron freezout, potentially producing a bright kilonova precursor in the first few hours after merger
- Jet and ejecta with Lorentz factor ~1-10 sufficient to (over)produce non-thermal emission from GW170817

## Context

This work extends the pioneering 3D GRMHD simulations of Siegel and collaborators (2017, 2018), which evolved a similar disk for only ~400 ms and ejected 20% of the initial mass, by evolving the disk an order of magnitude longer to achieve completion of mass ejection. The results are compared with axisymmetric hydrodynamic models using alpha viscosity (Fernandez and Metzger 2013, Just et al. 2015) that previously provided the primary theoretical framework for long-term disk evolution. The simulation enables more realistic predictions for kilonova light curves and r-process nucleosynthesis yields from neutron star mergers, though the authors note that scaling the ejected fractions to a disk mass of ~0.1 solar masses can account for the red kilonova from GW170817 but underpredicts the blue component.
