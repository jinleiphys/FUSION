# Three-body model calculations for 16C nucleus

> We apply a three-body model consisting of two valence neutrons and the core nucleus $^{14}$C in order to investigate the ground state properties and the electronic quadrupole transition of the $^{16}$C nucleus. The discretized continuum spectrum within a large box is taken into account by using a single-particle basis obtained from a Woods-Saxon potential. The calculated B(E2) value from the first 2$^+$ state to the ground state shows good agreement with the observed data with the core polarization charge which reproduces the experimental B(E2) value for $^{15}$C. We also show that the present calculation well accounts for the longitudinal momentum distribution of $^{15}$C fragment from the breakup of $^{16}$C nucleus. We point out that the dominant ($d_{5/2})^2$ configuration in the ground state of $^{16}$C plays a crucial role for these agreement.

# Three-body model calculations for 16C nucleus

## Key claim

The three-body model with a finite-range Minnesota potential and a Woods-Saxon single-particle basis can simultaneously reproduce the experimental B(E2) value for both 15C and 16C using a core polarization charge consistent with particle-vibration coupling models, provided the ground state of 16C is dominated by the (1d5/2)^2 configuration (about 70%) rather than the (2s1/2)^2 configuration (about 18%) as previously assumed.

## Method

The authors diagonalized a three-body Hamiltonian for two valence neutrons plus an inert 14C core, using single-particle states from a Woods-Saxon potential with continuum states discretized in a 30 fm box. They employed the finite-range Minnesota potential for the neutron-neutron interaction, adjusting its strength to reproduce the experimental ground state energy of 16C at -5.47 MeV. The model space included single-particle angular momenta up to l=5 and two-particle states up to 30 MeV total single-particle energy.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy of 16C: -5.47 MeV (used to adjust the neutron-neutron interaction strength)
- Single-particle energies: 2s1/2 at approximately -1.21 MeV and 1d5/2 at approximately -0.47 MeV for all parameter sets
- Ground state probability of (1d5/2)^2 configuration: 0.699 (set A), 0.711 (set B), 0.696 (set C), 0.633 (set D)
- Ground state probability of (2s1/2)^2 configuration: 0.184 (set A), 0.177 (set B), 0.183 (set C), 0.206 (set D)
- Root-mean-square radius of 16C: 2.56 fm (sets A, B), 2.57 fm (set C), 2.64 fm (set D); experimental value 2.64 +/- 0.05 fm
- Excitation energy of second 0+ state: 2.32 MeV (set A), 2.35 MeV (set B), 2.39 MeV (set C), 2.48 MeV (set D); experimental value 3.00 MeV

## Context

This work extends the three-body model calculations of Suzuki et al. (2004, 2006) for 16C, who used a correlated Gaussian basis and found the (2s1/2)^2 configuration dominant in the ground state, leading to overestimation of the B(E2) value by a factor of about 2. The present calculation instead finds (1d5/2)^2 dominance, consistent with analyses of longitudinal momentum distributions from knockout reactions by Yamaguchi (2003) and Maddalena et al. (2001). The results also contradict earlier antisymmetrized molecular dynamics calculations by Kanada-En'yo (2005) and deformed Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations by Suzuki et al. (2004), which also overestimated the B(E2). The work enables further studies of neutron collective modes in neutron-rich carbon isotopes and demonstrates the importance of proper treatment of continuum couplings in three-body models for weakly-bound nuclei.
