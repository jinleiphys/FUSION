# Probing mass-radius relation of protoneutron stars from gravitational-wave asteroseismology

> The gravitational-wave (GW) asteroseismology is a powerful technique for extracting interior information of compact objects. In this work, we focus on spacetime modes, the so-called $w$-modes, of GWs emitted from a proto-neutron star (PNS) in the postbounce phase of core-collapse supernovae. Using results from recent three-dimensional supernova models, we study how to infer the properties of the PNS based on a quasi-normal mode analysis in the context of the GW asteroseismology. We find that the $w_1$-mode frequency multiplied by the PNS radius is expressed as a linear function with respect to the ratio of the PNS mass to the PNS radius. This relation is insensitive to the nuclear equation of state (EOS) employed in this work. Combining with another universal relation of the $f$-mode oscillations, we point out that the time dependent mass-radius relation of the PNS can be obtained by observing both the $f$- and $w_1$-mode GWs simultaneously. Our results suggest that the simultaneous detection of the two modes could provide a new probe into finite-temperature nuclear EOS that predominantly determines the PNS evolution.

## Key claim

The paper claims that simultaneous detection of the $f$-mode and $w_1$-mode gravitational waves from a proto-neutron star (PNS) in the postbounce phase of a core-collapse supernova can yield the time-dependent mass and radius of the PNS, thereby probing the finite-temperature nuclear equation of state. This claim is hedged by the assumption that the $w_1$-mode frequency multiplied by the PNS radius is a linear function of the compactness ratio $M_{\rm PNS}/R_{\rm PNS}$, a relation found to be insensitive to the two equations of state (SFHx and TM1) employed in the study, but which may not hold for other EOSs not tested.

## Method

The authors used background PNS models from three-dimensional general-relativistic supernova simulations of a $15M_\odot$ progenitor (Kuroda et al., 2016) with two nuclear equations of state (SFHx and TM1). They performed a linear perturbation analysis in the Regge-Wheeler gauge to solve for the axial-type $w$-mode quasi-normal mode frequencies and damping rates, and separately computed the $f$-mode frequencies using the Cowling approximation. The resulting frequencies were fit to universal relations linking $f_{w_1}R_{\rm PNS}$ to $M_{\rm PNS}/R_{\rm PNS}$ and $f_f$ to the square root of the average density $(M_{\rm PNS}/R_{\rm PNS}^3)^{1/2}$.

## Key numbers

- The $w_1$-mode frequency for PNSs is fit by $f_{w_1}^{\rm (PNS)}\ {\rm (kHz)} \approx [27.99 - 12.02 (M_{\rm PNS}/1.4M_\odot)(R_{\rm PNS}/10\ {\rm km})^{-1}] (R_{\rm PNS}/10\ {\rm km})^{-1}$ (Eq. 4).
- The $f$-mode frequency for PNSs is fit by $f_{f}^{\rm (PNS)}\ {\rm (Hz)} \approx 14.48 + 4859 (M_{\rm PNS}/1.4M_\odot)^{1/2} (R_{\rm PNS}/10\ {\rm km})^{-3/2}$ (Eq. 5).
- The $p_1$-mode frequency for PNSs is fit by $f_{p_1}^{\rm (PNS)}\ {\rm (Hz)} \approx 43.29 + 8602 (M_{\rm PNS}/1.4M_\odot)^{1/2} (R_{\rm PNS}/10\ {\rm km})^{-3/2}$ (Eq. 6).
- The damping time of the $w_1$-mode is typically $\tau_{w_1} \sim 0.1$ ms.
- The effective amplitude of the $w_1$-mode is estimated as $h_{\rm eff}^{(w_1)} \sim 7.7\times 10^{-23} (E_{w_1}/10^{-10} M_\odot)^{1/2} (4\ {\rm kHz}/f_{w_1})^{1/2} (10\ {\rm kpc}/D)$, with total radiated energy $E_{\rm T}^{(w_1)}$ assumed between $10^{-8}M_\odot$ and $10^{-4}M_\odot$.
- The maximum gravitational masses for cold NSs are $M_{\rm max}=2.13M_\odot$ (SFHx) and $2.21M_\odot$ (TM1), with radii $\bar{R}=12$ km (SFHx) and $14.5$ km (TM1).

## Context

This work extends the gravitational-wave asteroseismology technique previously applied to cold neutron stars by Andersson and Kokkotas (1998) to the case of proto-neutron stars, using realistic three-dimensional supernova models from Kuroda et al. (2016). It builds on the authors' own prior study of $f$-modes in PNSs (Sotani and Takiwaki, 2016) by adding the $w_1$-mode analysis, enabling a simultaneous determination of mass and radius rather than just average density. The results suggest that a single supernova event could provide a time-evolving mass-radius sequence, potentially constraining the finite-temperature nuclear equation of state, provided future detectors such as the Einstein Telescope or Cosmic Explorer can achieve the required sensitivity.
