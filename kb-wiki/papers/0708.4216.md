# Equation of state at high densities and modern compact star observations

> Recently, observations of compact stars have provided new data of high accuracy which put strong constraints on the high-density behaviour of the equation of state of strongly interacting matter otherwise not accessible in terrestrial laboratories. The evidence for neutron stars with high mass (M =2.1 +/- 0.2 M_sun for PSR J0751+1807) and large radii (R > 12 km for RX J1856-3754) rules out soft equations of state and has provoked a debate whether the occurence of quark matter in compact stars can be excluded as well. In this contribution it is shown that modern quantum field theoretical approaches to quark matter including color superconductivity and a vector meanfield allow a microscopic description of hybrid stars which fulfill the new, strong constraints. The deconfinement transition in the resulting stiff hybrid equation of state is weakly first order so that signals of it have to be expected due to specific changes in transport properties governing the rotational and cooling evolution caused by the color superconductivity of quark matter. A similar conclusion holds for the investigation of quark deconfinement in future generations of nucleus-nucleus collision experiments at low temperatures and high baryon densities such as CBM @ FAIR.

# Equation of state at high densities and modern compact star observations

## Key claim
Modern quantum field theoretical approaches to quark matter that include color superconductivity and a vector meanfield can produce hybrid star configurations satisfying the new observational constraints from high-mass and large-radius neutron stars, provided the diquark coupling and vector meson coupling are tuned within specific ranges. The resulting deconfinement transition is weakly first order, meaning that signals of quark matter may still be detectable through transport properties rather than excluded entirely.

## Method
The authors construct a hybrid equation of state using a two-phase description: the hadronic phase is described by the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) approach, and the quark matter phase is described by a three-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with color superconductivity and a vector meanfield. The phase transition between the two phases is obtained via a Maxwell construction, with the diquark coupling parameter (eta_D) and the vector meson coupling parameter (eta_V) treated as free parameters tuned to satisfy observational constraints. The resulting hybrid equation of state is then used to solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to generate mass-radius sequences for compact star configurations.

## Key numbers
- Neutron star mass M = 2.1 +/- 0.2 solar masses for PSR J0751+1807
- Radius R > 12 km for the isolated neutron star RX J1856-3754
- Mass M = 2.0 +/- 0.1 solar masses for the compact object in 4U 1636-536
- Mass M >= 2.10 +/- 0.28 solar masses and radius R >= 13.8 +/- 0.18 km for EXO 0748-676
- Nuclear saturation density n_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}
- Vector coupling parameter eta_V = 0.5 produces the necessary softening of the high-density equation of state in accordance with the flow constraint
- Diquark coupling parameter in the range eta_D = 1.02 - 1.03 gives an appropriate deconfinement density
- Flow constraint from heavy-ion collisions excludes equations of state that become too stiff above approximately 0.55 fm^{-3}

## Context
This work extends the debate initiated by Ozel (2006), who argued that new compact star constraints would exclude quark matter in compact star interiors, and responds to counterexamples provided by Alford et al. (2006) showing that quark matter cannot be excluded. The authors build on prior hadronic equation of state approaches including relativistic mean-field models (such as NLrho and NLrhodelta), density-dependent coupling models (D3C, DD-F4, KVR, KVOR), and microscopic approaches (APR, WFF, FPS, DBHF, BBG). The hybrid star sequences presented here "masquerade" as neutron stars by having very similar mechanical properties to purely hadronic DBHF stars, suggesting that distinguishing quark matter interiors will require observables based on transport properties such as cooling behavior (Blaschke 2004, 2006; Popov 2004, 2005) or r-mode stability in rapidly rotating stars (Madsen 1999; Drago 2007).
