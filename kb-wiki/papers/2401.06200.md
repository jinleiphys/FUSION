# Instability windows of relativistic r-modes

> The detectability of the gravitational-wave signal from $r$-modes depends on the interplay between the amplification of the mode by the CFS instability and its damping due to dissipative mechanisms present in the stellar matter. The instability window of $r$-modes describes the region of stellar parameters (angular velocity, $Ω$, and redshifted stellar temperature, $T^\infty$), for which the mode is unstable. In this study, we reexamine this problem in nonbarotropic neutron stars, taking into account the previously overlooked nonanalytic behavior (in $Ω$) of relativistic $r$-modes and enhanced energy dissipation resulting from diffusion in superconducting stellar matter. We demonstrate that at slow rotation rates, relativistic $r$-modes exhibit weaker amplification by the CFS instability compared to Newtonian ones. However, their dissipation through viscosity and diffusion is significantly more efficient. In rapidly rotating neutron stars within the framework of general relativity, the amplification of $r$-modes by the CFS mechanism and their damping due to shear viscosity become comparable to those predicted by Newtonian theory. In contrast, the relativistic damping of the mode by diffusion and bulk viscosity remains significantly stronger than in the nonrelativistic case. Consequently, account for diffusion and general relativity leads to a substantial modification of the $r$-mode instability window compared to the Newtonian prediction. This finding is important for the interpretation of observations of rotating neutron stars, as well as for overall understanding of $r$-mode physics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that accounting for general relativity and particle diffusion in superconducting neutron star matter substantially modifies the r-mode instability window compared to Newtonian predictions, but the relativistic effects are rotation-dependent: at slow rotation rates relativistic r-modes show weaker CFS amplification and stronger dissipation, while at rapid rotation the amplification and shear damping become comparable to Newtonian theory, though relativistic damping by diffusion and bulk viscosity remains significantly stronger.

## Method

The authors solve the linearized relativistic hydrodynamic equations in the Cowling approximation for nonbarotropic neutron stars, using a toy model consisting only of a liquid nonbarotropic core with one completely superconducting proton component. They compute the r-mode energy change rates from shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, particle diffusion, and gravitational radiation using the nondissipative oscillation eigenfunctions, then determine the instability window by finding where the total oscillation energy change rate in the corotating frame becomes positive.

## Key numbers

- The r-mode instability window is defined in the plane of angular velocity \(\Omega\) and redshifted temperature \(T^\infty\), with \(\Omega_{\rm K}\equiv\sqrt{GM/R^3}\) used as the unit of angular velocity
- The redshifted temperature is defined as \(T^\infty \equiv T/\Lambda\) where \(\Lambda = e^{-\nu(r)} + O(\Omega^2)\)
- The energy loss rate due to gravitational radiation is given by \(\dot{E}_{\rm GW} = -\frac{G}{c^{3}}\sum_{L=2}^\infty \sum_{M=-L}^L \left(\frac{\sigma}{c}\right)^{2L+2}N_L(|\mathcal{I}_{LM}|^2+|\mathcal{S}_{LM}|^2)\) with \(N_L = \frac{4\pi}{[(2L+1)!!]^2}\frac{(L+1)(L+2)}{L(L-1)}\)
- The evolutionary timescale for each mechanism is defined as \(1/\tau = |\langle \dot{\tilde{E}} \rangle_P|/(2\tilde{E})\) where \(\langle\dots\rangle_P\) denotes the average over the oscillation period \(P\)

## Context

This work extends the "minimal model" of Low (1998) for r-mode instability windows, which considered only shear and bulk viscosity, by incorporating three modifications: nonbarotropic stellar matter, general relativity, and particle diffusion in superconducting matter. The authors previously showed in Kraav, Gusakov, and Kantor (2021) that diffusive dissipation is significantly enhanced in superconducting matter and becomes dominant for sound waves, p-modes, and g-modes, but found it weak for r-modes in Newtonian theory. The present work demonstrates that in general relativity, diffusion becomes the leading dissipative mechanism at not too high stellar temperatures, contradicting earlier Newtonian results. This finding is important for interpreting observations of rotating neutron stars and for understanding why no reliable r-mode detection has occurred, as it modifies the expected parameter space where unstable r-modes could exist.
