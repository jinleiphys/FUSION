# Prolate dominance and prolate-oblate shape transition in the proxy-SU(3) model

> Using a new approximate analytic parameter-free proxy-SU(3) scheme, we make simple predictions for the global feature of prolate dominance in deformed nuclei and the locus of the prolate-oblate shape transition and compare these with empirical data.

## Key claim

Using the parameter-free proxy-SU(3) scheme, the authors predict that prolate dominance in deformed even-even nuclei arises from the breaking of particle-hole symmetry within each nuclear shell, and that the prolate-to-oblate shape transition occurs at neutron number N=116 in the rare earth region, in agreement with existing experimental data and microscopic calculations.

## Method

The authors apply the proxy-SU(3) model to determine the highest-weight SU(3) irreducible representations (irreps) for valence protons and neutrons in the 50-82 and 82-126 shells using the code UNTOU3. They then relate the SU(3) quantum numbers (λ,μ) to the collective model variables β and γ, where irreps with λ > μ correspond to prolate shapes (0° < γ < 30°) and those with λ < μ correspond to oblate shapes (30° < γ < 60°). The predicted shapes are compared to empirical R₄/₂ = E(4⁺₁)/E(2⁺₁) ratios from the literature.

## Key numbers

- The prolate-to-oblate shape transition is predicted at neutron number N=116 for nuclei with protons in the 50-82 shell and neutrons in the 82-126 shell.
- For the W isotope chain, ¹⁹⁰W (N=116) is suggested as the lightest oblate isotope.
- For the Os chain, ¹⁹²Os (N=116) lies at the shape/phase transition point, with ¹⁹⁴Os and ¹⁹⁸Os found to possess oblate character.
- In the chain ¹⁸⁰Hf, ¹⁸²⁻¹⁸⁶W, ¹⁸⁸⁻¹⁹²Os, ¹⁹⁴,¹⁹⁶Pt, ¹⁹⁸,²⁰⁰Hg, the transition from prolate to oblate shapes occurs between ¹⁹²Os and ¹⁹⁴Pt, both with N=116.
- For nuclei with both protons and neutrons in the 50-82 shell, a similar transition is predicted at N=72, but this lies far from the experimentally accessible region.
- Particle-hole symmetry is valid only up to 4 particles and 4 holes, and is broken in the middle of each shell.

## Context

This work extends the proxy-SU(3) model, previously described in Bonatsos et al. (PRC1, PRC2), to explain the long-standing puzzle of prolate dominance in deformed nuclei, first noted by Hamamoto and Mottelson. The predictions for the N=116 shape transition agree with experimental evidence from Alkhomashi, Namenson, Wheldon, Podolyak, and Linnemann, as well as with self-consistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus BCS calculations by Sarriguren and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations by Robledo and Nomura. The results enable parameter-free predictions for ground state shapes of even-even deformed nuclei across the nuclear chart.
