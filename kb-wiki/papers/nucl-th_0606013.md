# Progress in lattice QCD at finite temperature

## Key claim

Lattice QCD calculations of charmonium spectral functions indicate that 1S charmonia states (J/ψ and ηc) survive up to at least 1.6Tc with little modification, while 1P states (χc) dissolve near 1.1Tc; this contradicts potential model predictions of strong modification or melting at lower temperatures, but the results depend on the Maximum Entropy Method for spectral reconstruction and on the specific lattice discretization used.

## Method

The author reviews lattice QCD calculations of the finite-temperature deconfining transition and of charmonium spectral functions. Euclidean-time meson correlators are computed on anisotropic and isotropic lattices, and spectral functions are reconstructed using the Maximum Entropy Method (MEM) by inverting the integral relation G(τ,T) = ∫ dω σ(ω,T) K(ω,τ,T). To separate trivial temperature effects from genuine spectral changes, the reconstructed correlator Grecon(τ,T) using the zero-temperature spectral function is compared to the actual finite-temperature correlator via the ratio G(τ,T)/Grecon(τ,T).

## Key numbers

- Transition temperature estimates from lattice QCD with 2+1 flavors range between 167 MeV and 188 MeV, with large statistical and systematic errors.
- For pure SU(3) gauge theory (no dynamical quarks), the transition temperature is Tc/√σ = 0.632(2), where σ is the string tension.
- The pseudo-scalar (ηc) correlator shows only very small changes up to 1.5Tc, indicating the state survives with little modification.
- The scalar (χc0) correlator shows large changes already at 1.16Tc, suggesting strong modification or dissolution of the χc0 state at that temperature.
- Lattice calculations suggest the QCD transition for physical quark masses is a crossover, with first-order behavior only for three degenerate flavors at a pion mass of about 67 MeV.

## Context

This invited talk (Petreczky, 2006) reviews lattice QCD results that extend earlier work by the MILC collaboration, Karsch (2001), and Fodor (2004) on the transition temperature, and by Umeda (2002), Asakawa (2004), and Datta (2004) on charmonium spectral functions. The findings contradict potential model predictions (Karsch, 1988; Digal, 2001; Shuryak, 2004; Wong, 2004; Alberico) that had suggested strong modification of charmonia above Tc. The results enable more rigorous study of quarkonium melting as a signal for Quark-Gluon Plasma formation in heavy ion collisions, and motivate future calculations of bottomonium spectral functions and of the heavy quark diffusion constant from the transport peak in the vector channel.
