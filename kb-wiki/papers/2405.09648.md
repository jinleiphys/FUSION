# BSQ Conserved Charges in Relativistic Viscous Hydrodynamics solved with Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics

> Conservation laws play a crucial role in the modeling of heavy-ion collisions, including the those for charges such as baryon number (B), strangeness (S), and electric charge (Q). In this study, we present a new 2+1 relativistic viscous hydrodynamic code called CCAKE which uses the Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) formalism to locally conserve BSQ charges, together with an extended description of the multi-dimensional equation of state (EoS) obtained from lattice Quantum Chromodynamics. Initial conditions for CCAKE are supplied by the ICCING model, which samples gluon splittings into quark anti-quark pairs to generate the initial BSQ charge distributions. We study correlations between the BSQ charges and find that local BSQ fluctuations remain finite during the evolution, with corresponding chemical potentials of ($\sim100$--$200 \,\rm MeV$) at freeze-out. We find that our framework produces reasonable multiplicities of identified particles and that ICCING has no significant effect on the collective flow of all charged particles nor of identified particles when only one particle of interest is considered. However, we show specifically for Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.02$ TeV that ICCING does have an effect on collective flow of identified particles if two particles of interest are considered.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new 2+1 relativistic viscous hydrodynamic code (CCAKE) that locally conserves baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge using Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics, but only ideal BSQ currents are implemented in the charge sector while diffusive corrections are deferred to future work; the ICCING initial state model has no significant effect on collective flow of all charged particles or identified particles when only one particle of interest is considered, but does affect collective flow of identified particles when two particles of interest are considered for Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC.

## Method

The authors developed a 2+1D relativistic viscous hydrodynamic code called CCAKE based on the v-USPhydro code, using the Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) Lagrangian numerical method with shear and bulk viscosity implemented according to the Israel-Stewart-DNMR O(Re^{-1})^2 formalism. Initial conditions were supplied by the ICCING model, which samples gluon splittings into quark-antiquark pairs to generate initial BSQ charge distributions, using a cubic spline kernel instead of a Gaussian distribution for smoother charge density profiles. The code uses a 4D equation of state from lattice QCD and enforces a perturbative cut-off (E_q/E_Bg < 0.9) to avoid producing quark-antiquark pairs at energy densities too low to be found in the EoS table.

## Key numbers

- Chemical potentials at freeze-out: approximately 100-200 MeV for BSQ charges
- Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV (LHC) used for the study
- Normalization constant N = 125 fm for both TRENTo+CCAKE and TRENTo+ICCING+CCAKE (previously N = 119 in earlier v-USPhydro versions)
- Initial hydrodynamic time tau_0 = 0.60 fm/c
- Perturbative cut-off set to P = 0.9 (E_q/E_Bg < 0.9)
- Initial state mu_B varies from approximately +/- 400 MeV at temperatures ranging up to T ≈ 400-500 MeV
- Gaussian profile cut-off radius at r_g = 0.5 fm; kernel function profile radius at r_g = 1 fm

## Context

This work extends previous relativistic hydrodynamic simulations that incorporated one conserved charge (baryon number, Denicol 2018), two conserved charges (BS, Fotakis 2019), or three conserved charges (BSQ, Schafer 2021) by implementing the first Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics code with multiple conserved charges. The code enables studies of BSQ charge fluctuations with a realistic 4D lattice QCD equation of state (Noronha-Hostler 2019) in an expanding QGP medium, and opens the door to investigating how initial-state gluon splittings into quark-antiquark pairs affect identified particle observables, particularly for particles carrying multiple conserved charges such as Omega baryons.
