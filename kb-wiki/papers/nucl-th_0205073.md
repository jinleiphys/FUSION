# How random are random nuclei? Shapes, triangles and kites

> We study the origin of the regular features obtained in numerical studies of the IBM with random interactions, in particular the dominance of L=0 ground states and the occurrence of vibrational and rotational band structures. It is shown that the geometric shapes associated with IBM Hamiltonians play a crucial role in understanding these regular properties. Different regions of the parameter space are associated with definite geometric shapes, such as spherical and deformed shapes and a condensate of quadrupole bosons.

# How random are random nuclei? Shapes, triangles and kites

## Key claim
The regular spectral features observed in the interacting boson model (IBM) with random interactions, specifically the dominance of L=0 ground states and the appearance of vibrational and rotational bands, can be explained by the underlying geometric shapes associated with different regions of the IBM parameter space, rather than requiring specific, tuned interactions.

## Method
The authors studied the consistent-Q formulation (CQF) Hamiltonian of the IBM, taking the scaled parameters η and χ̄ randomly on the intervals 0 ≤ η ≤ 1 and −1 ≤ χ̄ ≤ 1. They classified the resulting Hamiltonians in a shape phase diagram using the control parameters r₁ and r₂, and compared the probability distribution of the energy ratio R = [E(4₁)−E(0₁)]/[E(2₁)−E(0₁)] for the random CQF ensemble against a general IBM Hamiltonian with random one- and two-body interactions drawn from a Gaussian distribution. A Hartree-Bose mean-field analysis was performed to identify the intrinsic states (spherical, deformed, and quadrupole-boson condensate) corresponding to each region of parameter space.

## Key numbers
- For the random CQF ensemble, 50% of Hamiltonians correspond to spherical shape, 25% to prolate deformed shape, and 25% to oblate deformed shape.
- The probability distribution P(R) for the random CQF shows two characteristic peaks: one at the vibrator value R = 2 and one at the rotor value R = 10/3.
- For the general IBM with random one- and two-body interactions, P(R) exhibits peaks at R ∼ 1.9 and R ∼ 3.3.
- In the general random IBM, the ground state has L = 0 in approximately 63% of cases, with the percentage ranging from ∼60–75% depending on boson number N.
- For N = 3k (a multiple of 3), there is an enhancement of L = 0 ground states and a decrease of L = 2 ground states.

## Context
This work extends earlier numerical studies by Bijker and Frank (2001) and others that found unexpected regular features in the IBM with random interactions, contradicting the traditional view that such regularity (e.g., the tripartite classification into seniority, anharmonic vibrator, and rotor regions) requires specific interactions like pairing or quadrupole-quadrupole forces (Casten and Zamfir, 1990s). The mean-field analysis explains both the ground-state angular momentum distribution and the occurrence of vibrational and rotational bands, and the same conclusions hold for the vibron model. For the nuclear shell model, the authors note that no definitive explanation for the dominance of L=0 ground states is yet available, and the full implications for nuclear structure remain to be clarified.
