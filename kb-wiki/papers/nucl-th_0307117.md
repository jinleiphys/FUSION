# Nuclear mass systematics using neural networks

> New global statistical models of nuclidic (atomic) masses based on multilayered feedforward networks are developed. One goal of such studies is to determine how well the existing data, and only the data, determines the mapping from the proton and neutron numbers to the mass of the nuclear ground state. Another is to provide reliable predictive models that can be used to forecast mass values away from the valley of stability. Our study focuses mainly on the former goal and achieves substantial improvement over previous neural-network models of the mass table by using improved schemes for coding and training. The results suggest that with further development this approach may provide a valuable complement to conventional global models.

## Key claim

Neural networks trained solely on experimental mass data, using a modified back-propagation algorithm and improved input coding, can achieve predictive accuracy approaching that of state-of-the-art theory-based models like the finite-range droplet model (FRDM), but the best neural-network model still shows a root-mean-square error on a test set of previously unseen nuclei that is about 0.25 MeV larger than the FRDM value.

## Method

Multilayer feedforward neural networks with logistic activation functions were trained to predict nuclear mass excess from proton number Z and neutron number N. The input coding used two analog neurons for Z and N plus two binary neurons encoding the parity (even/odd) of Z and N. A modified back-propagation algorithm (MB) was developed that replaces the standard momentum term with an epoch-averaged momentum, and the learning rate was adjusted adaptively based on validation set performance. Networks were trained on the 1323-nucleus "old" (O) set from Möller and Nix (1981) and validated on either the 351-nucleus "new" (N) set or the 158-nucleus NUBASE (NB) set, with a separate test set for final evaluation.

## Key numbers

- Best neural-network model (architecture 4-10-10-10-1 with 361 weights) achieved a root-mean-square error of 0.95 MeV on the NB test set of 158 nuclei, compared to 0.70 MeV for the FRDM model of Möller et al.
- On the 529 new nuclei from the AME03 evaluation, the same neural-network model gave a root-mean-square error of 1.03 MeV, versus 0.58 MeV for FRDM and 0.67 MeV for the HFB2 model of Pearson et al.
- The modified back-propagation (MB) algorithm consistently outperformed standard back-propagation (SB): for one model pair (set 3), MB gave learning error 0.71 MeV, validation error 1.25 MeV, and test error 1.88 MeV, versus SB values of 0.92 MeV, 1.58 MeV, and 2.44 MeV respectively.
- A neural-network model with architecture 4-10-10-10-1 (281 weights) trained on the M1 set (1303 nuclei) achieved a learning error of 0.41 MeV and a validation error of 0.47 MeV on the M2 set (351 nuclei).

## Context

This work extends the neural-network approach to nuclear mass systematics pioneered by Gazula et al. (1992), Gernoth et al. (1993), and Kalman (1994), achieving substantial improvements through the modified back-propagation algorithm and parity-aided analog input coding. The results are compared directly with the finite-range droplet model (FRDM) of Möller et al. (1995) and the HFB2 model of Pearson et al. (2002), which represent the state of the art in theory-based global mass models. The authors suggest that neural-network models may serve as a valuable complement to conventional models, and they identify the need for further refinements including pruning schemes, alternative training procedures, and the use of the newer AME03 mass evaluation.
