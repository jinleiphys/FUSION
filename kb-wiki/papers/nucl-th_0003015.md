# Level Densities by Particle-Number Reprojection Monte Carlo Methods

> A particle-number reprojection method is applied in the framework of the shell model Monte Carlo approach to calculate level densities for a family of nuclei using Monte Carlo sampling for a single nucleus. In particular we can also calculate level densities of odd-even and odd-odd nuclei despite a new sign problem introduced by the projection on an odd number of particles. The method is applied to level densities in the iron region using the complete $pf+g_{9/2}$-shell. The single-particle level density parameter $a$ and the backshift parameter $Δ$ are extracted by fitting the microscopically calculated level densities to the backshifted Bethe formula. We find good agreement with experimental level densities with no adjustable parameters in the microscopic calculations. The parameter $a$ is found to vary smoothly with mass and does not show odd-even effects. The calculated backshift parameter $Δ$ displays an odd-even staggering effect versus mass and is in better agreement with the experimental data than are the empirical values.

# Level Densities by Particle-Number Reprojection Monte Carlo Methods

## Key claim

The particle-number reprojection method enables calculation of level densities for a family of nuclei (including odd-even and odd-odd nuclei) using Monte Carlo sampling for a single even-even or N=Z nucleus, despite a new sign problem introduced by projection onto an odd number of particles. The method achieves good agreement with experimental level densities with no adjustable parameters in the microscopic calculations, though the reprojection approximation is valid primarily when reprojecting onto nuclei with N'-Z' values close to the original N-Z.

## Method

The authors apply the shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method using a Hamiltonian with monopole pairing and multipole-multipole interactions (quadrupole, octupole, hexadecupole) in the complete pf+g9/2 shell for nuclei in the iron region. Monte Carlo sampling is performed for a single nucleus (56Fe or 54Co), and thermal observables for neighboring nuclei are computed by reprojecting the one-body propagators onto different particle numbers using a ratio of canonical traces. The thermal energies are integrated to obtain partition functions, and level densities are extracted via the saddle-point approximation to the inverse Laplace transform, then fitted to the backshifted Bethe formula to obtain the single-particle level density parameter a and backshift parameter Δ.

## Key numbers

- Monte Carlo sampling performed for 56Fe and 54Co, with reprojection yielding level densities for 53-56Mn, 54-58Fe, and 54-60Co (from 56Fe) and 50-52Mn and 52,53Fe (from 54Co)
- Calculations performed for inverse temperatures β up to 2.5 MeV^-1, with step size Δβ = 1/16 for β < 1 and Δβ = 1/8 for larger β
- Approximately 4000 independent samples taken per β point
- Ground state energies extracted to within accuracy of ~0.3 MeV for odd-even nuclei
- Level densities fitted to the backshifted Bethe formula in the energy range E_x < 20 MeV, with fits good down to ~1 MeV for even-even nuclei and below 1 MeV for odd-A nuclei
- The parameter a varies smoothly with mass A and does not show odd-even effects
- The backshift parameter Δ displays odd-even staggering versus mass and shows better agreement with experimental data than empirical values from Dilg (1973) and Grimes (1974)

## Context

This work extends the SMMC method for level densities developed by Nakada and Alhassid (1997) by introducing a particle-number reprojection technique that avoids performing separate Monte Carlo calculations for each nucleus. The method enables economical calculation of level densities for many nuclei in the iron region, which is relevant for astrophysical nucleosynthesis calculations (neutron and proton capture rates in the statistical regime). The results contradict the empirical formulae of Holmes, Woosley, Fowler, and Zimmerman (1976) which predict similar level densities for nuclei with the same mass A, while the SMMC calculations show significant T_z-dependence. The method enables future systematic calculations of level densities across broader regions of the nuclear chart.
