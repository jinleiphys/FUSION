# Modification of the Brink-Axel Hypothesis for High Temperature Nuclear Weak Interactions

> We present shell model calculations of electron capture strength distributions in A=28 nuclei and computations of the corresponding capture rates in supernova core conditions. We find that in these nuclei the Brink-Axel hypothesis for the distribution of Gamow-Teller strength fails at low and moderate initial excitation energy, but may be a valid tool at high excitation. The redistribution of GT strength at high initial excitation may affect capture rates during collapse. If these trends which we have found in lighter nuclei also apply for the heavier nuclei which provide the principal channels for neutronization during stellar collapse, then there could be two implications for supernova core electron capture physics. First, a modified Brink-Axel hypothesis could be a valid approximation for use in collapse codes. Second, the electron capture strength may be moved down significantly in transition energy, which would likely have the effect of increasing the overall electron capture rate during stellar collapse.

## Key claim

The Brink-Axel hypothesis for Gamow-Teller strength distributions fails at low and moderate initial excitation energies in A=28 nuclei, but may be a valid tool at high excitation; however, the authors caution that results for states above approximately 20-25 MeV may be significantly impacted by model space restrictions, and that these findings in lighter nuclei may not directly apply to the heavier nuclei that dominate supernova core neutronization.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale shell model calculations using the code Oxbash with the USDB Hamiltonian for A=28 nuclei (primarily 28Si, also 24Mg and 28Mg), using a closed 16O core with 12 valence nucleons in the sd shell (0d5/2, 1s1/2, and 0d3/2 single-particle states). They computed Gamow-Teller strength distributions for individual nuclear eigenstates up to high excitation energies, averaging the strength in bins of initial excitation energy and fitting the distributions to double Gaussian functions. The density of states was examined to identify inflection points where model space restrictions become problematic.

## Key numbers

- Average excitation energy for a typical nuclear mass of ~120 in supernova core conditions is between 15 and 60 MeV, using the Bethe approximation E ≈ a(k_BT)^2 with a ≈ A/8 MeV^-1 and temperature ~1-2 MeV
- For 28Si, the inflection points in the density of states for T=0 and T=1 occur a little above 30 MeV, indicating results for states above the mid-20s MeV should be treated with circumspection
- For 28Si, the 1d state occupations have a linear dependence on nuclear excitation with slopes of roughly 1 particle per 12 MeV, while the 2s1/2 occupation is independent of excitation
- For 28Si, the GT strength distribution becomes almost independent of initial state energy for transitions proceeding from initial excitations greater than 12-16 MeV
- For 24Mg, the density of states inflection point is near 28 MeV, and strength distributions for states above the low 20s MeV are suspect
- For 24Mg, above 12 MeV excitation (and below the model space restriction range), the strength distributions are not strong functions of initial excitation
- For 28Mg, the inflection point in the density of states occurs at E ≈ 22 MeV

## Context

This work extends the foundational approach of Fuller, Fowler, and Newman (FFN, 1980-1985), who first adopted the Brink-Axel hypothesis for Gamow-Teller charged current transitions in stellar weak rate calculations. The authors find that the original FFN assumption that single particle distributions are independent of nuclear excitation energy is violated, as the d orbital occupations show linear dependence on excitation energy. The results corroborate earlier work by Frazier et al (1997) on 24Mg showing gradual increase of total GT strength with initial excitation, and are consistent with theoretical calculations by Nabi and Sajjad (2007, 2011, 2012) showing failure of Brink-Axel for Gamow-Teller interactions at modest excitation energies. The work enables a modified Brink-Axel approximation for use in supernova collapse codes, where the strength distribution at high excitation may be treated as approximately independent of initial state energy, potentially increasing overall electron capture rates during stellar collapse.
