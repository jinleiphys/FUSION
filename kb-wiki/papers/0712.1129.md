# Relativistic kinematics for reactions involving massless particles

> Some useful kinematical relations for the absorption of a photon by a nucleus and its recoil are derived for the relativistic incident energies. These expressions provided for the relativistic kinematics of photoabsorption reactions, though simple, will be immensely useful for experimentalists as well as theoreticians.

## Key claim

The paper derives simple relativistic kinematic formulas for photoabsorption reactions (γ + C → C*) and for γ + γ collisions, but the derivations assume the target nucleus is initially at rest in the laboratory frame and that the incident photon energy is in the relativistic domain.

## Method

The authors apply conservation of energy and momentum in the laboratory frame to derive expressions for the excitation energy, recoil velocity, and recoil kinetic energy of a nucleus after absorbing a relativistic photon. They then transform these quantities to the centre-of-mass frame using the relativistic Doppler effect and the velocity of the centre of mass, and extend the formalism to the case of two photons colliding head-on.

## Key numbers

- Excitation energy of the nucleus: \( E^* = m_0 c^2 [(1 + 2E_\gamma / m_0 c^2)^{1/2} - 1] \), where \( m_0 \) is the rest mass of the target nucleus and \( E_\gamma \) is the incident photon kinetic energy in the laboratory frame.
- Recoil velocity of the nucleus in the laboratory frame: \( v_r = E_\gamma c / (E_\gamma + m_0 c^2) \).
- Recoil kinetic energy of the nucleus in the laboratory frame: \( E_r = E_\gamma + m_0 c^2 - [m_0 c^2 (2E_\gamma + m_0 c^2)]^{1/2} \).
- Centre-of-mass velocity for the γ-nucleus system: \( v_{cm} = E_\gamma c / (E_\gamma + m_0 c^2) \), which equals the recoil velocity \( v_r \).
- Centre-of-mass kinetic energy for γ + γ collisions (head-on): \( E_{cm} = 2 \sqrt{E_1 E_2} \), where \( E_1 \) and \( E_2 \) are the laboratory energies of the two photons.
- Centre-of-mass momentum for γ + γ collisions: \( p_{cm} = \sqrt{E_1 E_2} / c \).

## Context

This work extends the standard non-relativistic kinematics for photoabsorption reactions to the relativistic regime, motivated by experiments at the Saskatchewan Accelerator Laboratory (SAL) and Jefferson Laboratory (JLab) that use highly energetic photons (Sa99, Sa00; Ce00, Ce01). The derived simple expressions are intended to be immediately useful for both experimentalists and theoreticians working with relativistic photon-induced nuclear reactions, and the formalism also covers the case of γ + γ collisions, which is relevant for high-energy photon-photon interactions.
