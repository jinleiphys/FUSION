# Unravelling strange quarks in nucleon structure

> We present a discourse on the stages of discovery that have led to a deeper understanding of the role played by strange quarks in the structure of the nucleon.

## Key claim

The strange quark contributions to nucleon structure are smaller than early estimates suggested, with the strangeness magnetic moment and sigma term both found to be small (at the 2-sigma level, less than about 6% of total values for the magnetic moment and charge radius), contingent on the use of finite-range regularisation (FRR) chiral extrapolation methods applied to lattice QCD results.

## Method

The analysis combines charge-symmetry relations among octet-baryon magnetic moments with a finite-range regularised chiral extrapolation framework to isolate the strangeness component of nucleon electromagnetic form factors from lattice QCD data. For the sigma term, SU(3) chiral extrapolation with FRR is applied to 2+1-flavour dynamical lattice QCD results for octet baryon masses from the PACS-CS and LHPC collaborations, holding light quarks fixed at the physical point while extrapolating in the strange quark mass.

## Key numbers

- Strangeness magnetic moment: \(G_M^s = -0.046 \pm 0.022\,\mu_N\) (nucleon, from combined charge-symmetry and chiral analysis)
- Strangeness sigma term: \(\sigma_{Ns} = 31 \pm 15\) MeV (nucleon, from SU(3) chiral extrapolation of 2+1-flavour lattice QCD)
- Strangeness magnetic form factor extracted at \(Q^2 \sim 0.23\) GeV\(^2\) (nucleon, from the same framework)
- Strangeness form factors evaluated at \(Q^2 = 0.1\) GeV\(^2\) (experimental summary point with combined 68% confidence interval)
- Proton weak charge measurement constrains new electroweak physics up to a characteristic energy scale of \(\sim 1\) TeV

## Context

This work extends the finite-range regularisation chiral extrapolation methods developed by Leinweber (1999), Young (2002), and Cloet (2003) for baryon masses and magnetic moments to the flavour separation of nucleon electromagnetic form factors. The theoretical predictions for strangeness form factors initially appeared to conflict with early parity-violation measurements from G0 (Armstrong, 2005) and HAPPEX (Aniol, 2005), but a comprehensive global analysis by Young (2006) incorporating consistent radiative corrections and anapole form factors brought experiment and theory into agreement. The small strangeness sigma term determination enables dramatically reduced uncertainties in dark matter cross sections for supersymmetric models, as shown by Giedt (2009), and is compatible with independent lattice extractions by Toussaint and Freeman (2009) and Ohki (2008).
