# Quantum field theory: Finiteness and Effectiveness

> A new attempt is demonstrated that QFTs can be UV finite if they are viewed as the low energy effective theories of a fundamental underlying theory (that is complete and well-defined in all respects) according to the modern standard point of view. This approach works for any interaction model and space-time dimension. It is much simpler in principle and in technology comparing to any known renormalization program.Unlike the known renormalization methods, the importance of the procedure for defining the ambiguities (corresponding to the choice of the renormalization conditions in the conventional program) is fully appreciated in the new approach. It is shown that the high energy theory(s) or the underlying theory(s) in fact 'stipulates (stipulate)' the low energy and effective ones through these definitions within our approach while all the conventional methods miss this important point. Some simple but important nonperturbative examples are discussed to show the power and plausibility of the new approach. Other related issues (especially the IR problem and the implication of our new approach for the canonical quantization procedure) are briefly touched.

# Quantum field theory: Finiteness and Effectiveness

## Key claim

Quantum field theories can be rendered UV finite without invoking ad hoc regularization or renormalization if they are treated as low-energy effective theories derived from a fundamental underlying theory, provided one respects that the low-energy limit operation and loop momentum integration do not commute for divergent amplitudes. The approach claims to work for any interaction model and space-time dimension, but the actual finite results contain undetermined polynomial ambiguities that must be fixed by experimental data or by constraints from the (unknown) underlying theory.

## Method

The author proposes that ill-defined Feynman amplitudes arise because the low-energy limit operator is incorrectly moved inside the loop integration before the integration is performed. For a one-loop amplitude with superficial divergence degree ω−1, the method differentiates the integrand ω times with respect to external momenta to produce a convergent integral, performs the integration, then integrates ω times with respect to external momenta to recover the original amplitude plus an arbitrary polynomial of order ω−1 in external momenta. For multi-loop graphs, the procedure is applied recursively: divergent subgraphs are treated by the same differentiation-integration technique, working from the smallest convergent subgraphs upward, with the low-energy limit operator moved across integrations only where convergence is assured. The arbitrary polynomial coefficients (the ambiguities) are to be fixed by physical requirements and experimental data, and the author argues that the underlying theory ultimately stipulates these constants.

## Key numbers

- The superficial divergence degree of a graph is denoted ω−1, and the method differentiates ω times with respect to external parameters to render the loop integral convergent.
- For a one-loop amplitude, the result is expressed as Γ⁰ = Γ_npl + N^ω, where N^ω is a polynomial of order ω−1 in external momenta with arbitrary coefficients {c̄_ω}.
- The author states that the chiral anomaly arises from "one kind of definite rational terms (independent of masses)" that are nonlocal and unambiguous, not from UV ambiguities; no numerical value for any anomaly coefficient is given.
- No explicit numerical results (cross sections, decay rates, coupling constants, or precision values) are presented in the text.

## Context

This paper extends the modern effective field theory viewpoint articulated by Weinberg (circa 1979) and the Wilsonian renormalization group approach, but argues that the Wilsonian approach as applied to ordinary QFTs is "questionable" and "ad hoc" for non-renormalizable interactions. The author contrasts the proposed method with the BPHZ renormalization scheme and with conventional regularization procedures, claiming that those approaches miss the physical role of the underlying theory in fixing ambiguities. The work aims to provide a universal formulation that encompasses all regularization and renormalization schemes as special choices of the ambiguous polynomial coefficients. The author suggests that this framework leads to a new classification of QFT models into those with consistent definitions (FT_I) and those without (FT_II), and speculates that extending the energy range would shrink FT_I toward a unique underlying theory analogous to superstring theories.
