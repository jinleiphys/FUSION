# Unraveling the global behavior of equation of state by explicit finite nuclei constraints

> We obtain posterior distribution of equations of state (EOSs) across a broad range of density by imposing explicitly the constraints from precisely measured fundamental properties of finite nuclei, in combination with the experimental data from heavy-ion collisions and the astrophysical observations of radius, tidal deformability and minimum-maximum mass of neutron stars. The acquired EOSs exhibit a distinct global behavior compared to those usually obtained by imposing the finite nuclei constraints implicitly through empirical values of selected key parameters describing symmetric nuclear matter and symmetry energy in the vicinity of the saturation density. The explicit treatment of finite nuclei constraints yields softer EOSs at low densities which eventually become stiffer to meet the maximum mass criteria. The Kullback-Leibler divergence has been used to perform a quantitative comparison of the distributions of neutron star properties resulting from the EOSs obtained from implicit and explicit finite nuclei constraints.

## Key claim

The paper shows that explicitly imposing constraints from measured binding energies and charge radii of finite nuclei (rather than using implicit empirical nuclear matter parameters) yields posterior distributions of the equation of state that are softer at low densities and stiffer at intermediate densities (1.25–2.5 ρ₀), with the explicit constraints producing narrower spreads and stronger correlations among nuclear matter parameters and neutron star properties.

## Method

The authors performed a Bayesian analysis using a relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with σ, ω, and ρ mesons, including nonlinear self-couplings and ω–ρ cross coupling. They generated posterior distributions of seven nuclear matter parameters (saturation density ρ₀, binding energy E₀, incompressibility K₀, skewness Q₀, effective mass m*/m, symmetry energy J₀, and slope L₀) under two scenarios: (1) implicit finite nuclei constraints using empirical values of nuclear matter parameters, and (2) explicit finite nuclei constraints using directly the measured binding energies and charge radii of ⁴⁰Ca and ²⁰⁸Pb. Both scenarios also included constraints from heavy-ion collisions, dipole polarizability, and astrophysical observations (pulsar masses, NICER radii, and GW170817 tidal deformability). The Kullback-Leibler divergence was used to quantify differences between the resulting distributions.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy of ¹⁶O: explicit FNC gives −129.4⁺¹·¹⁴₋₁·₁₆ MeV (experimental: −127.62 MeV); implicit FNC gives −135.85⁺⁸·⁹¹₋₉·₂₉ MeV
- Binding energy of ²⁰⁸Pb: explicit FNC gives −1632.94⁺⁷·⁷³₋₇·₆₆ MeV (experimental: −1636.43 MeV); implicit FNC gives −1620.19⁺⁸⁸·³⁷₋₈₇·₈₂ MeV
- Charge radius of ⁴⁰Ca: explicit FNC gives 3.44⁺⁰·⁰¹₋₀·₀₁ fm (experimental: 3.48 fm); implicit FNC gives 3.34⁺⁰·⁰⁵₋₀·₀₃ fm
- Charge radius of ²⁰⁸Pb: explicit FNC gives 5.53⁺⁰·⁰²₋₀·⁰² fm (experimental: 5.50 fm); implicit FNC gives 5.38⁺⁰·¹²₋₀·⁰⁶ fm
- Neutron star radius at 1.4 M⊙: explicit FNC median larger by ∼0.4 km compared to implicit FNC
- Neutron star radius at 2.08 M⊙: explicit FNC median larger by ∼0.4 km compared to implicit FNC
- Canonical tidal deformability Λ₁.₄: explicit FNC predicts higher values than implicit FNC, with smaller overlap with GW170817 constraints
- Kullback-Leibler divergence reaches maximum ∼3 bits at ∼1.5 M⊙ for tidal deformability distributions
- Pearson correlation coefficient r ∼ −0.7 between K_sym,0 and J_0 for explicit FNC (negligible for implicit FNC)
- Pearson correlation coefficient r ∼ 0.8 between J_0 and Λ₁.₄ for explicit FNC (negligible for implicit FNC)

## Context

This work extends the approach of Tsang et al. (2023) and Malik et al. (2019, 2020) by directly incorporating finite nuclei binding energies and charge radii into Bayesian EOS inference, rather than using empirical nuclear matter parameters derived from those data. The explicit treatment yields softer symmetry energy above 0.5 ρ₀ and stronger correlations between nuclear matter parameters and neutron star properties, enabling tighter constraints on parameters like J₀ and L₀ from future astrophysical observations. The analysis could be extended to include additional finite nuclei observables such as giant resonances for a diverse set of nuclei.
