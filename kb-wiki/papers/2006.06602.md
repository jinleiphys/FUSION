# Effects of long-range forces on the D-term and the energy-momentum structure

> The hadronic form factors of the energy-momentum tensor (EMT) have attracted considerable interest in recent literature. This concerns especially the D-term form factor D(t) with its appealing interpretation in terms of internal forces. With their focus on hadron structure, theoretical studies so far have concentrated on strongly interacting systems with short-range forces. Effects on the EMT due to long-range forces like the electromagnetic interaction have not yet been studied. Electromagnetic forces play a small role in the balance of forces inside the proton, but their long-range nature introduces new features which are not present in systems with short-range forces. We use a simple but consistent classical field theoretical model of the proton to show how the presence of long-range forces alters some notions taken for granted in short-range systems. Our results imply that a more careful definition of the D-term is required when long-range forces are present.

## Key claim

Long-range electromagnetic forces, though numerically small in the proton's interior, fundamentally alter the behavior of the energy-momentum tensor densities at distances beyond about 2 fm, introducing features such as additional nodes in the pressure and shear forces that are absent in systems governed solely by short-range forces. This implies that the standard definition of the D-term requires revision when long-range forces are present.

## Method

The authors employ a classical field-theoretical model of the proton (Bialynicki-Birula 1993) in which dust particles interact via a massive scalar field (attractive), a massive vector field (repulsive), and the electromagnetic field (repulsive). They solve the static field equations for a spherically symmetric ground state, compute the energy-momentum tensor densities (energy density, shear forces, and pressure), and analyze their behavior at both short distances (r less than about 2 fm) and long distances (r greater than about 2 fm) where the Coulomb contribution dominates over the exponentially decaying strong-field contributions.

## Key numbers

- The model yields a charge radius of 0.714 fm, which underestimates the experimental value by 20 percent.
- The binding energy per nucleon is -15.71 MeV, compared to -15.75 MeV in nuclear matter.
- The shear force s(r) changes sign at r = 2.144 fm, becoming negative beyond this point.
- The pressure p(r) exhibits a second node at r = 2.394 fm, after which it remains positive.
- The normal force (2/3)s(r)+p(r) changes sign at r = 1.881 fm.
- The asymptotic long-distance behavior of the densities is: T00(r) = (1/2)(alpha/4pi)(hbar c/r^4), s(r) = -(alpha/4pi)(hbar c/r^4), p(r) = (1/6)(alpha/4pi)(hbar c/r^4).
- The scalar field contributes -180.45 MeV to the total energy, the vector field contributes 163.79 MeV, and the Coulomb field contributes 0.95 MeV.
- The von Laue integral contributions are: scalar -10.916 MeV, vector 10.891 MeV, Coulomb 0.025 MeV.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of the D-term and energy-momentum tensor form factors in hadronic systems (Polyakov 1999, Polyakov 2002, Goeke 2007, Polyakov 2018) which all focused on strongly interacting systems with short-range forces. The classical proton model of Bialynicki-Birula (1993) is used because it consistently includes both strong short-range forces and the long-range electromagnetic field. The results show that the Coulomb contribution, though negligible for the internal force balance, qualitatively changes the long-distance behavior of the EMT densities, implying that future experimental measurements and lattice QCD calculations of the D-term must account for long-range electromagnetic effects.
