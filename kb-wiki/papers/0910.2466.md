# Magnetic Field-Decay-Induced Electron Captures: a Strong Heat Source in Magnetar Crusts

> We propose a new heating mechanism in magnetar crusts. Magnetars' crustal magnetic fields are much stronger than their surface fields; therefore, magnetic pressure partially supports the crust against gravity. The crust loses magnetic pressure support as the field decays and must compensate by increasing the electron degeneracy pressure; the accompanying increase in the electron Fermi energy induces nonequilibrium, exothermic electron captures. The total heat released via field-decay electron captures is comparable to the total magnetic energy in the crust. Thus, field-decay electron captures are an important, if not the primary, mechanism powering magnetars' soft X-ray emission.

# Magnetic Field-Decay-Induced Electron Captures: a Strong Heat Source in Magnetar Crusts

## Key claim

Magnetic field decay in magnetar crusts reduces magnetic pressure support, forcing the crust to increase electron degeneracy pressure and thereby raise the electron Fermi energy, which triggers exothermic electron captures that release heat comparable to the total magnetic energy stored in the crust. The mechanism is presented as an important, if not the primary, source of magnetars' persistent soft X-ray emission, but the authors stress that their estimates are upper limits and depend on the magnetic field being predominantly nonradial rather than radial or force-free.

## Method

The authors construct a plane-parallel hydrostatic model of a neutron star's outer crust, assuming a nonradial (toroidal) magnetic field that is stronger in the crust than at the surface and decays with time. They use the equation of vertical hydrostatic equilibrium in Lagrangian coordinates (column depth) to relate the sum of electron degeneracy pressure and magnetic pressure to gravitational support, then calculate the change in column depth at the electron capture threshold pressure as the field decays from an initial value B_i to a final value B_f. The total heat released is estimated by multiplying the column of matter that undergoes captures by the energy per nucleon released per capture, normalized to conditions near neutron drip.

## Key numbers

- The total energy released via field-decay electron captures is approximately 3 x 10^47 erg, normalized to B_i = 10^16 G, Q = 1 MeV per nucleon, R = 10 km, and M = 1.4 M_sun.
- The ratio of nuclear energy released to total magnetic energy in the crust is approximately 0.5, for mean molecular weight per electron mu_e = 3.2, Q = 1 MeV, and electron capture threshold energy E_F,ec = 27 MeV.
- The persistent soft X-ray luminosity of magnetars is L ~ 10^35 erg/s, with characteristic ages tau < 10^5 yr.
- The interior energy reservoir required to sustain persistent thermal emission is E_int > 3 x 10^47 erg, scaled to tau = 10^5 yr and L = 10^35 erg/s.
- The quantum critical field strength B_crit = 4.414 x 10^13 G.
- Landau quantization affects the electron pressure only when P_e / (B^2/8 pi) is less than about 6 x 10^-3.

## Context

This work proposes a new heating mechanism distinct from the usual explanation of Joule heating in magnetar crusts, as discussed by previous authors (e.g., Miralles et al. 1998, Aguilera et al. 2008, Urpin and Konenkov 2008, Pons et al. 2009). The mechanism is analogous to deep crustal heating in accreting neutron stars (e.g., Brown et al. 1998, Gupta et al. 2007, Haensel and Zdunik 2008), but here magnetic field decay rather than accretion drives the increase in electron Fermi energy. The results imply that field-decay electron captures should be included in future magneto-thermal evolution calculations and may reduce the interior magnetic field strength required to power magnetar phenomena. The authors also suggest that asymmetries in the internal magnetic field combined with field-decay-induced electron captures could generate density variations that intensify gravitational wave emission, similar to a mechanism proposed for weakly magnetic accreting neutron stars by Bildsten (1998) and Ushomirsky et al. (2000).
