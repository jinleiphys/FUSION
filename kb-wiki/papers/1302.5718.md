# Microscopic Description of Induced Fission

> Selected aspects of the description of neutron-induced fission in 240Pu in the framework of the nuclear energy density functional theory at finite temperature are presented. In particular, we discuss aspects pertaining to the choice of thermodynamic state variables, the evolution of fission barriers as function of the incident neutron energy, and the temperatures of the fission fragments.

# Microscopic Description of Induced Fission

## Key claim
The finite-temperature nuclear energy density functional theory can describe neutron-induced fission of 240Pu, but the equivalence between isothermal and isentropic thermodynamic representations is essential for predicting fragment temperatures, and the method's numerical accuracy for temperature extraction is limited to about 50 keV due to interpolation errors.

## Method
The authors performed finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (FT-HFB) calculations using the SkM* Skyrme energy functional with a four-dimensional collective space defined by axial quadrupole, triaxial quadrupole, octupole, and hexadecapole moments. They computed potential energy surfaces at constant temperature (isothermal) and then used spline interpolation to reconstruct the internal energy as a function of entropy (isentropic representation), verifying the Maxwell relation equivalence between the two representations.

## Key numbers
- The HFB wavefunctions were expanded onto 1100 harmonic oscillator basis states with contributions from up to 30 oscillator shells.
- A cutoff of 60 MeV limited the number of quasi-particles included in the density calculation.
- The numerical accuracy of extracting temperature from the entropy-versus-internal-energy function was of the order of 50 keV at worst.
- The two DFT solvers (HFBTHO and HFODD) agreed within a few eV for axial configurations.

## Context
This work extends the microscopic theory of fission originally developed by the CEA Bruyeres-le-Chatel group in the 1980s (Berger and collaborators, 1984 and 1989) and the time-dependent generator coordinate method approach (Goutte and collaborators, 2005). It builds on recent progress in energy functionals for fission (Kortelainen and collaborators, 2012) and understanding of scission quantum mechanics (Younes and Gogny, 2011). The method enables microscopic predictions of fission fragment temperatures, which are needed as input for reaction codes modeling neutron and gamma spectra from fission, and challenges the macroscopic-microscopic assumptions of either equal fragment temperatures (Wilkins and collaborators, 1976) or temperatures proportional to fragment mass ratios (Schmidt and collaborators, 2010).
