# Neutron Star Observations: Prognosis for Equation of State Constraints

> We investigate how current and proposed observations of neutron stars can lead to an understanding of the state of their interiors and the key unknowns: the typical neutron star radius and the neutron star maximum mass. A theoretical analysis of neutron star structure, including general relativistic limits to mass, compactness, and spin rates is made. We consider observations made not only with photons, ranging from radio waves to X-rays, but also those involving neutrinos and gravity waves. We detail how precision determinations of structural properties would lead to significant restrictions on the poorly understood equation of state near and beyond the equilibrium density of nuclear matter.

# Neutron Star Observations: Prognosis for Equation of State Constraints

## Key claim

Current and proposed observations of neutron stars can constrain the equation of state of dense matter near and beyond nuclear equilibrium density, but the two most important properties, maximum mass and typical radius, remain poorly known; the paper emphasizes that while some measurements suggest maximum masses near 2 M☉, the 2σ errors of all but two systems extend below 1.45 M☉, so caution is warranted before concluding firm evidence of large neutron star masses exists.

## Method

The authors perform a theoretical analysis of neutron star structure using general relativistic hydrostatic equilibrium (Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations) combined with causal limits on the equation of state, and review observational constraints from pulsar timing, thermal X-ray and optical observations of cooling neutron stars, X-ray bursts and accretion sources, quasi-periodic oscillations, and potential future neutrino and gravitational wave detections. They compare these constraints against a wide range of theoretical equations of state including non-relativistic potential models, relativistic field-theoretical models, and exotic matter scenarios involving hyperons, Bose condensates, and deconfined quark matter.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear equilibrium density: ρ_s ≈ 2.7 × 10^14 g cm^-3 or n_s ≈ 0.16 baryons fm^-3
- Maximum mass limit from causality (Rhoades & Ruffini): 3.2 M☉, later refined to M_max = 4.2 √(ε_f/ε_s) M☉
- Maximum redshift from causality (Lindblom): z ≤ 0.863, equivalent to R ≥ 2.83 GM/c^2
- Compactness limit from "minimum period" EOS: R_sph^max c^2/G M_sph^max = 2.825 (first line) or 2.87 (second line)
- Central density limit from Tolman VII plus causality: ρ_cent M^2 ≤ 15.3 × 10^15 M☉^2 g cm^-3
- For a 2.1 M☉ neutron star, limiting central mass density is 3.4 × 10^15 g cm^-3; if maximum mass is 10% larger, limit becomes 2.8 × 10^15 g cm^-3 (baryon density ~7 n_s)
- Minimum spin period for maximum mass configuration: P_min ≈ 0.83 (M☉/M_sph^max)^(1/2) (R_sph^max/10 km)^(3/2) ms
- Minimum spin period for arbitrary mass (not near maximum): P_min ≈ (0.96 ± 0.03) (M☉/M_sph)^(1/2) (R_sph/10 km)^(3/2) ms
- For 716 Hz pulsar J1748-2446ad: R < 14.3 km for 1.4 M☉, R < 15.3 km for 1.7 M☉
- For 1122 Hz source XTE J1739-285: maximum radii 10.8 km (1.4 M☉) and 12.2 km (2.0 M☉) from empirical relation; rigorous causal limit gives 12.6 km and 14.2 km
- Binary pulsar PSR 1913+16 masses: 1.3867 ± 0.0002 and 1.4414 ± 0.0002 M☉
- PSR J0751+1807 estimated mass: 2.1 ± 0.2 M☉ (1σ)
- Neutrino binding energy of neutron star: ~300 bethes (1 bethe ≡ 10^51 erg)
- Peak neutrino luminosity during core bounce: nearly 1000 bethes s^-1
- Estimated neutron star binary merger rate: ~1 per 10,000 years in the Galaxy; advanced LIGO might observe 40–400 events per year

## Context

This review extends the authors' prior work on neutron star structure and equation of state constraints (Lattimer & Prakash 2001, 2004, 2005) and builds on foundational results including the causal mass limit of Rhoades & Ruffini (1974), the compactness limit of Lindblom (1984), and the "minimum period" EOS analysis of Koranda et al. (1997). The paper synthesizes observational developments such as the 716 Hz pulsar discovery by Hessels et al. (2006), the 1122 Hz oscillation reported by Kaaret et al. (2006), and mass measurements of PSR J0751+1807 by Nice et al. (2005) and Ter 5 I and J by Ransom et al. (2005). It enables future work by identifying that precision mass and radius measurements could rule out entire families of equations of state, particularly those with exotic matter (hyperons, Bose condensates, quarks) that soften the EOS and reduce maximum masses below ~1.6 M☉, and by highlighting gravitational wave observations of binary mergers as a potentially unambiguous discriminator between normal neutron stars and self-bound strange quark matter stars.
