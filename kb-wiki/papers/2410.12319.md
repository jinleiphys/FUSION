# How Not to Measure a False QCD Critical Point

> Fluctuations of conserved charges are a golden channel for measuring a QCD critical point in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. These fluctuations are quantified by measuring high-order cumulants of baryon-number distributions at a given centrality. Using simulated proton-number cumulants as an example, we discuss how the correlation between particle identification and centrality measurements can distort particle-number distributions. These distortions can easily create large fluctuations in high-order cumulants that might be mistaken for a critical-point signature. We show that certain measurement choices can make the analysis more or less vulnerable to these false signals. We motivate this by considering how the two-dimensional probability space of proton-number versus multiplicity is shaped by analysis choices. We then demonstrate this vulnerability with simulated Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=3.9$ GeV in UrQMD, and two toy models of detector responses to certain classes of events. We explain how an analyzer might observe a false critical signature, and how to avoid doing so, even in a challenging experimental environment.

## Key claim

The dominant background for high-order cumulant measurements of proton-number distributions in heavy-ion collisions arises from the different ways that particle identification (PID) and centrality measurements respond to anomalous events, such as pileup or detector acceptance fluctuations, when different detectors are used for each measurement; this can create large fluctuations in high-order cumulants that might be mistaken for a critical-point signature, but strategic analysis choices can suppress these false signals.

## Method

The authors used simulated Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 3.9 GeV from the UrQMD model in cascade mode, with 6.4×10⁷ events, applying the same analysis window as STAR's fixed-target program (0.4 < pT < 2 GeV/c and -0.5 < y - yCM < 0). They constructed two toy models of detector responses: one simulating out-of-time pileup with a fast detector (0% efficiency for out-of-time tracks) and a slow detector (100% efficiency for out-of-time tracks), and another simulating a detector with unstable acceptance where for 1% of events half the detector was inactive. Proton-number cumulants and cumulant ratios were then evaluated for different combinations of which detector measured multiplicity versus proton number.

## Key numbers

- Pileup rate in STAR's 3 GeV fixed-target data was 0.46%
- Toy model 1 used a 0.2% out-of-time pileup rate
- Toy model 2 used 1% of events with the unstable detector half-dead
- With a 0.1% fraction of events sampled from a broader Gaussian (σ₂=15 vs σ₁=5), C₄/C₂ rose from consistent with 0 to 3.6±0.6
- When the slow detector identified ≈80% of protons with up to 1% pileup fraction, no significant enhancement of C₄/C₂ was observed
- When the slow detector was not used for PID, the enhancement in measured C₄/C₂ reached ≈10²
- Centrality bins used: 0-5%, 5-10%, 10-20%, 20-30%, 30-40%, 40-50%, and 50-60%

## Context

This work extends the understanding of experimental backgrounds in high-order cumulant measurements of conserved charges, which are a key observable in the search for the QCD critical point at high baryon chemical potential (μB ≈ 500-650 MeV). The paper builds on prior work by the STAR Collaboration (2021, 2022) on proton cumulant measurements at √sNN = 3 GeV, and on established treatments of volume fluctuations (Braun-Munzinger, 2014) and detector inefficiencies (Nonaka, 2017). The findings enable experimentalists to design analysis strategies that avoid false critical-point signals, particularly important for interpreting upcoming fixed-target data from the STAR Beam Energy Scan program.
