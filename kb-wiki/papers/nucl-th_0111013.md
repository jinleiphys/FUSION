# Anharmonic vibrations in nuclei

> In this letter, we show that the non-linearitites of large amplitude motions in atomic nuclei induce giant quadrupole and monopole vibrations. As a consequence, the main source of anharmonicity is the coupling with configurations including one of these two giant resonances on top of any state. Two-phonon energies are often lowered by one or two MeV because of the large matrix elements with such three phonon configurations. These effects are studied in two nuclei, 40Ca and 208Pb.

# Anharmonic vibrations in nuclei

## Key claim

The main source of anharmonicity in two-phonon nuclear states is the coupling to three-phonon configurations that include a giant monopole resonance (GMR) or giant quadrupole resonance (GQR) on top of any state, producing systematic downward energy shifts of one to two MeV; this effect is strongest in light nuclei like 40Ca where volume modes dominate, while in heavy nuclei like 208Pb the GQR plays a larger role than the GMR.

## Method

The authors constructed a boson image of the nuclear Hamiltonian truncated at fourth order in boson operators, starting from a mapping of fermion particle-hole operators into bosons with Pauli principle corrections. They solved the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) equations using the SGII Skyrme interaction to obtain collective one-phonon states, then diagonalized the Hamiltonian in spaces containing up to two-phonon states and separately up to three-phonon states, including all natural parity RPA phonons with J ≤ 3 that exhaust at least 5% of the energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR), without any energy cut-off on the three-phonon configurations.

## Key numbers

- Two-phonon energies in 40Ca are lowered by more than 1 MeV when three-phonon configurations are included, with shifts systematically downward.
- The matrix element between the double and triple GMR (M1 at 18.25 MeV) in 40Ca is -5.22 MeV, giving a -1.49 MeV second-order energy correction to the double M1 state.
- The matrix element between the double and triple GMR (M2 at 22.47 MeV) in 40Ca is -9.69 MeV, giving a -4.18 MeV second-order energy correction to the double M2 state.
- The 0+ member of the double low-lying octupole state in 40Ca shifts from 10.96 MeV (two-phonon space) to 9.27 MeV (three-phonon space), a downward shift of 1.69 MeV.
- In 208Pb, the 0+ member of the double low-lying octupole state shifts from 7.88 MeV (two-phonon space) to 6.96 MeV (three-phonon space), a downward shift of 0.92 MeV.
- The double M1 state in 208Pb shifts from 27.52 MeV (two-phonon space) to 26.23 MeV (three-phonon space), a downward shift of 1.29 MeV.
- Mixing coefficients for two-phonon states in 40Ca are about 0.2 to 0.4 or more; in 208Pb they are 0.15 to 0.3.

## Context

This work extends earlier microscopic calculations that considered only mixing of one- and two-phonon states, such as Lanza et al. (1997) which found anharmonicity limited to a few hundred keV. It goes beyond Ponomarev and Von Neumann-Cosel (1999), who studied fragmentation of double octupole states in 208Pb but introduced a low-energy cut-off that excluded GMR and GQR contributions. The results show that the breathing mode (GMR) plays an essential role in nuclear anharmonicity, a mechanism previously neglected in damping or coupling studies, and that the GQR is also important due to its extreme collectivity. The findings imply that future work on three-phonon spectra must include four-phonon configurations, and that the mixing of the monopole resonance may affect conclusions about nuclear compressibility.
