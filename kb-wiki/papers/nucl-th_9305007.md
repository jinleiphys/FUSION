# The column vector calculus for Thermo Field Dynamics of relativistic quantum fields

## Key claim

The paper presents a column vector notation that simplifies the calculation of Feynman diagrams in Thermo Field Dynamics (TFD) for relativistic quantum fields, claiming the method is even simpler to apply than the Matsubara technique. The formalism works specifically for propagators that can be decomposed into sums of terms with zero determinant, and the simplification relies on rewriting thermal Bogoliubov matrices as outer products of two column vectors.

## Method

The author extends a non-relativistic column vector calculus (developed by Cugnon and collaborators, 1993) to relativistic quantum fields at finite temperature, using a model with minimal coupling of fermions and scalar uncharged bosons in thermal equilibrium. The thermal Bogoliubov matrices are decomposed into outer products of column vectors, and two combination rules are defined for contracting these vectors at vertices: open vertices combine column vectors by multiplying corresponding components, while closed vertices contract to a scalar function. The method is demonstrated by deriving retarded self-energy functions for one-loop polarization and Fock diagrams, showing that the column vector structure of the full propagator is conserved under insertion of self-energy diagrams.

## Key numbers

- Bosonic occupation number: $n_B(E) = 1/(e^{\beta E} - 1)$, with $f_B(E) = e^{-\beta E}$
- Fermionic occupation numbers: $n^\pm_F(E) = 1/(e^{\beta(E \mp \mu_F)} + 1)$, with $f^\pm_F(E) = e^{-\beta(E \mp \mu_F)}$
- Free boson spectral function limit: ${\cal A}_B(E,\vec{k}) \rightarrow \text{sign}(E)\, \delta(E^2 - \vec{k}^2 - m^2)$
- Free fermion spectral function limit: ${\cal A}_F(E,\vec{p}) \rightarrow (p_\mu\gamma^\mu + M)\, \text{sign}(E)\, \delta(E^2 - \vec{p}^2 - M^2)$

## Context

This work extends the non-relativistic column vector calculus of Cugnon (1993) to relativistic quantum fields, building on the Thermo Field Dynamics framework developed by Umezawa and collaborators. The formalism enables simpler higher-order calculations compared to the Matsubara technique, and was applied in a subsequent paper by the same author (Henning, 1993) to calculate the pionic spectral function in a model with nucleons and Delta-33 resonances coupled to a pion field. The method is designed to handle propagators with continuous mass spectra rather than simple delta-function quasi-particles, addressing the need for consistent treatment of finite-temperature effects in nuclear matter.
