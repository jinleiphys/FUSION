# Fitting and Analysis Technique for Inconsistent Nuclear Data

> Consistent experiment data are crucial to adjust parameters of physics models and to determine best estimates of observables. However, often experiment data are not consistent due to unrecognized systematic errors. Standard methods of statistics such as $χ^2$-fitting cannot deal with this case. Their predictions become doubtful and associated uncertainties too small. A human has then to figure out the problem, apply corrections to the data, and repeat the fitting procedure. This takes time and potentially costs money. Therefore, a Bayesian method is introduced to fit and analyze inconsistent experiment data. It automatically detects and resolves inconsistencies. Furthermore, it allows to extract consistent subsets from the data. Finally, it provides an overall prediction with associated uncertainties and correlations less prone to the common problem of too small uncertainties. The method is foreseen to function with a large corpus of data and hence may be used in nuclear databases to deal with inconsistencies in an automated fashion.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a Bayesian method that automatically detects and resolves inconsistencies in experimental nuclear data by treating the experiment covariance matrix itself as uncertain, thereby producing predictions with uncertainties that are less prone to being too small compared to standard chi-squared fitting or Generalized Least Squares methods.

## Method

The method extends the standard Bayesian update formula by introducing additional parameters that adjust the experiment covariance matrix, specifically by adding a normalization uncertainty term to each experimental dataset's covariance block. The posterior distribution over these adjustment parameters is maximized to identify consistent subsets of data, and overall predictions with associated covariance matrices are obtained by marginalizing over the adjustment parameters using Monte Carlo integration combined with importance sampling. The prior for the adjustment parameters is chosen as a product of identical Laplace distributions, which favors sparse solutions where only a few datasets require correction.

## Key numbers

- The generalized chi-squared value chi^2(kappa')/N, where N is the number of measurement points, should be close to one to indicate that the chosen prior standard deviation delta for the Laplace distribution is adequate
- For the Laplace prior, the standard deviation delta should be set to a plausible value for normalization uncertainty, e.g., between 5% and 10% if such errors are expected
- When using an improper uniform prior for the adjustment parameters, relative standard deviations of the kappa_i parameters exceeded 1000%, which is deemed unreasonably large
- The marginal likelihood rho(sigma_exp | delta) is estimated via importance sampling using K samples drawn from a mixture distribution, with the estimate given by (1/(J*K)) * sum_i omega_m(kappa_i)

## Context

This work extends the procedures presented in Varet (2015) and Varet (2014) and Varet (2013), which dealt with estimating experiment covariance matrices separately from model fitting. The proposed method treats the estimation or correction of experiment covariance matrices as an integral part of the model fitting procedure, enabling automated handling of inconsistencies in nuclear databases. The improved uncertainty quantification is intended to lead to safer and more efficient nuclear facilities through more realistic propagation of uncertainties in evaluated nuclear data.
