# Instabilities of a hot expanded nuclear droplet

> LaTeX (uses epsfig.sty), 6 pages with 6 eps figures inside text. Talk given at XXVII International Workshop on Gross Properties of Nuclei and Nuclear Excitations, "MULTIFRAGMENTATION", Hirschegg, January 17--23, 1999

## Key claim

The stability of hot expanded nuclear droplets against small bulk and surface oscillations is examined, with the hedge that finite-size effects substantially reduce the area of spinodal instabilities relative to infinite nuclear matter, yet the expansion trajectories of hot nuclei still remain in the instability regime for soft equations of state.

## Method

The authors model a spherical nuclear droplet using a complete orthonormal set of functions based on spherical Bessel functions and spherical harmonics, with irrotational displacement fields derived from a displacement potential. Small oscillations around equilibrium are treated in harmonic approximation, yielding coupled equations for eigenmodes that are diagonalized to obtain eigenvalues; negative squared frequencies indicate exponential instability. Calculations are performed for two Skyrme forces (SkM* and SIII, representing soft and stiff equations of state) with contributions from volume, intrinsic kinetic energy, Weizsäcker, surface, and Coulomb terms in the energy-density functional.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density is taken as \(\rho_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- For a gold nucleus (A=197) with the soft equation of state, the expansion trajectories reach turning points around \(T \approx 4\) MeV and \(\rho/\rho_0 \approx 0.25\)–\(0.45\).
- Characteristic times for density instabilities around these turning points are on the order of 25–40 fm/c.
- For initial temperatures higher than 8 MeV, surface vibrations become unstable, but their characteristic times are about an order of magnitude larger than those for bulk oscillations.
- Contour lines for bulk instability characteristic times are shown at 20, 25, 30, 40, 60, and 100 fm/c; for surface instabilities at 150, 200, 250, 300, 400, and 600 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends earlier suggestions by Bertsch, Siemens, and Cugnon (1984) that spinodal instabilities could drive multifragmentation in hot expanding nuclear matter. The study quantifies how finite-size effects reduce the spinodal region compared to infinite nuclear matter, and it contrasts bulk instabilities (dominant at low densities and temperatures) with surface instabilities (dominant at higher temperatures and densities). The results enable comparison with expansion trajectories from prior work by Papp et al. (late 1990s) and experimental data from Herrmann, Jeong, Hsi, Pochodzalla, and the EOS collaboration, showing that characteristic growth times near turning points are short enough to initiate multifragmentation.
