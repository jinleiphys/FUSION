# Beta delayed emission of a proton by a one-neutron halo nucleus

> Some one-neutron halo nuclei can emit a proton in a beta decay of the halo neutron. The branching ratio towards this rare decay mode is calculated within a two-body potential model of the initial core+neutron bound state and final core+proton scattering states. The decay probability per second is evaluated for the $^{11}$Be, $^{19}$C and $^{31}$Ne one-neutron halo nuclei. It is very sensitive to the neutron separation energy.

## Key claim

The branching ratio for beta-delayed proton emission from one-neutron halo nuclei is calculated within a two-body potential model, and the authors find that the decay probability is very sensitive to the neutron separation energy, with the best candidate for observation being \(^{11}\)Be despite its separation energy not being very small.

## Method

The initial halo nucleus is treated as a core+neutron bound state described by a Woods-Saxon potential adjusted to reproduce the experimental neutron separation energy. The final state is a core+proton scattering state in the continuum, described by the same form factor with an additional point-sphere Coulomb potential. The decay probability per second is obtained by integrating the product of the Fermi integral and the reduced Fermi and Gamow-Teller decay probabilities over the relative energy of the core and proton.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{11}\)Be (separation energy 501 keV, half-life 13.8 s): total decay probability \(1.5 \times 10^{-9}\) s\(^{-1}\), branching ratio \(3.0 \times 10^{-8}\).
- For \(^{19}\)C (separation energy 580 \(\pm\) 90 keV, half-life 46.2 ms): total decay probability \(2.7 \times 10^{-12}\) s\(^{-1}\), branching ratio \(1.8 \times 10^{-13}\).
- For \(^{31}\)Ne (separation energy 0.33 \(\pm\) 1.07 MeV, half-life 3.4 ms, assuming \(p\)-wave ground state at \(-0.33\) MeV): total decay probability \(3.3 \times 10^{-10}\) s\(^{-1}\), branching ratio \(1.6 \times 10^{-12}\); for an \(s\)-wave ground state, the decay probability is about \(1.6 \times 10^{-9}\) s\(^{-1}\), five times larger.
- The \(Q\) value for \(^{11}\)Be is 0.281 MeV; for \(^{19}\)C it is 202 keV; for \(^{31}\)Ne it is 0.45 MeV.
- The asymptotic normalization constants \(C\) are 0.83, 0.96, and 0.69 fm\(^{-1/2}\) for \(^{11}\)Be, \(^{19}\)C, and \(^{31}\)Ne, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous three-body model study of the beta-delayed neutron-and-proton decay of \(^{11}\)Li (Baye and Tursunov, 2010) to the simpler case of one-neutron halo nuclei. The decay mode for \(^{11}\)Be was previously considered by Horoi and Zelevinsky (2003) but their results were not published. The calculations enable experimental searches for this rare decay mode, requiring high radioactive beam intensities and long measurement times, and highlight that a not-too-heavy one-neutron halo nucleus with a very small separation energy would be the best candidate for observation.
