# Vorticity and Polarization in Heavy Ion Collisions: Hydrodynamic Models

> Fluid dynamic approach is a workhorse for modelling collective dynamics in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. The approach has been successful in describing various features of the momentum distributions of hadrons produced in the heavy-ion collisions, such as $p_T$ spectra, flow coefficients $v_n$ etc. As such, the description of the phenomenon of polarization of $Λ$ hyperons in heavy-ion collisions has to be incorporated into the hydrodynamic approach. We start this chapter by introducing different definitions of vorticity in relativistic fluid dynamics. Then we present a derivation of the polarization of spin 1/2 fermions in the relativistic fluid. The latter is directly applied to compute the spin polarization of the $Λ$ hyperons, which are produced from the hot and dense medium, described with fluid dynamics. It is followed by a review of the existing calculations of global or local polarization of $Λ$ hyperons in different hydrodynamic models of relativistic heavy-ion collisions. We particularly focus on the explanations of the collision energy dependence of the global $Λ$ polarization from the different hydrodynamic models, the polarization component in the beam direction as well as on the origins of the global and local $Λ$ polarization.

## Key claim

This chapter reviews how hydrodynamic models can incorporate spin polarization of Lambda hyperons by assuming that spin degrees of freedom are at local thermodynamic equilibrium at hadronization, with the thermal vorticity as the key quantity linking fluid rotation to particle polarization; the agreement with data is described as "good" but the review notes that different models produce different explanations for the collision energy dependence of global polarization.

## Method

The author defines three types of relativistic vorticity (kinematical, T-vorticity, and thermal vorticity) and derives the mean spin vector of spin-1/2 fermions from a local thermodynamic equilibrium density operator expanded around the particlization hypersurface. The resulting formula for the mean spin vector involves an integral over the particlization hypersurface of the thermal vorticity weighted by the Fermi-Dirac distribution and its quantum correction factor (1-f). The review then compares three hydrodynamic models (UrQMD+vHLLE, PICR, and 3FD) applied to Au-Au collisions at center-of-mass energies from 7.7 to 62.4 GeV per nucleon pair, describing their different initial state treatments (UrQMD cascade, Yang-Mills fields, or three-fluid dynamics) and hydrodynamic implementations (viscous versus ideal fluid, different equations of state).

## Key numbers

- Estimated non-relativistic vorticity in heavy-ion collisions: 0.02 fm^{-1} ≈ 10^{22} s^{-1}, based on a velocity difference of 0.1c across a transverse size of about 5 fm.
- Hydrodynamic calculations cover collision energies sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7, 19.6, 27, 39, 62.4 GeV (the BES energy range) and also 200 GeV at RHIC and 2.76, 5.02 TeV at the LHC.
- In UrQMD+vHLLE, typical grid spacing: Δx = Δy = 0.2 fm, Δη = 0.05-0.15, timestep Δτ = 0.05-0.1 fm/c; a finer grid with Δx = Δy = 0.125 fm was used for peripheral collisions.
- The starting time of hydrodynamic evolution is given by τ_0 = 2R / sqrt((sqrt(s_NN)/(2m_N))^2 - 1), where R is the nuclear radius and m_N the nucleon mass.
- In the non-relativistic global equilibrium limit, the spin 3-vector reduces to S ≈ (1/4)(ω/T), where ω is the constant angular velocity and T the constant temperature.

## Context

This chapter extends the hydrodynamic description of collective flow in heavy-ion collisions (successful for pT spectra and flow coefficients v_n) to the phenomenon of Lambda hyperon polarization, building on the theoretical framework of Becattini (2013, 2015) for thermal vorticity and spin polarization. It reviews calculations from three specific models: UrQMD+vHLLE (Karpenko, 2015), PICR (Xie, 2017), and 3FD (Ivanov, 2019), which were applied to the Beam Energy Scan energy range. The work enables systematic comparison of different hydrodynamic approaches to explain the collision energy dependence of global polarization and the polarization component in the beam direction, and it identifies open questions about the origins of global versus local polarization patterns.
