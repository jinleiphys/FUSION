# Dark Matter Constraints from a Cosmic Index of Refraction

> The dark-matter candidates of particle physics invariably possess electromagnetic interactions, if only via quantum fluctuations. Taken en masse, dark matter can thus engender an index of refraction which deviates from its vacuum value. Its presence is signaled through frequency-dependent effects in the propagation and attenuation of light. We discuss theoretical constraints on the expansion of the index of refraction with frequency, the physical interpretation of the terms, and the particular observations needed to isolate its coefficients. This, with the advent of new opportunities to view gamma-ray bursts at cosmological distance scales, gives us a new probe of dark matter and a new possibility for its direct detection. As a first application we use the time delay determined from radio afterglow observations of distant gamma-ray bursts to realize a direct limit on the electric-charge-to-mass ratio of dark matter of |varepsilon|/M < 1 x 10^{-5} eV^{-1} at 95% CL.

## Key claim

Dark matter, even if electrically neutral, can induce a frequency-dependent index of refraction for light via quantum fluctuations, and this effect can be used to directly constrain the electric-charge-to-mass ratio of dark matter particles. The limit of |ε|/M < 1 × 10⁻⁵ eV⁻¹ at 95% CL is derived under the assumption that the photon energy is below the threshold for producing the charged particles to which dark matter couples, and that the dark matter mass is much larger than its temperature at the redshift of the observed gamma-ray bursts.

## Method

The authors analyze the forward Compton scattering amplitude for light passing through dark matter, expanding it in powers of photon frequency ω under assumptions of Lorentz invariance and low-energy theorems. They use the time delay between the initial gamma-ray burst detection at keV energies and the subsequent radio afterglow detection for 53 gamma-ray bursts with known redshifts, fitting the observed time lags as a function of K(z)/ν² to separate propagation effects from intrinsic source emission effects. The cosmological parameters from WMAP five-year data (H₀ = 70.5 ± 1.3 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹, Ω_M = 0.274 ± 0.015, Ω_Λ = 0.726 ± 0.015) are used to compute the cosmological distance integral K(z).

## Context

This work extends the concept of using light propagation effects to probe dark matter, building on earlier studies of vacuum Lorentz violation using gamma-ray bursts by Amelino-Camelia (1998) and Ellis (2000). It provides a direct, model-independent limit on dark matter millicharge that complements indirect constraints from laboratory experiments by Ahlers (2008), cosmic microwave background distortions by Melchiorri (2007), stellar evolution limits by Davidson (2000), and galactic structure constraints. The method enables a new observational probe of dark matter properties using existing and future gamma-ray burst observations, particularly at longer radio wavelengths where sensitivity could improve significantly.
