# Phase space characteristics of fragmenting nuclei described as excited disordered systems

> We investigate the thermodynamical content of a cellular model which describes nuclear fragmentation as a process taking place in an excited disordered system. The model which reproduces very well the size distribution of fragments does not show the existence of a first order phase transition.

## Key claim

The cellular model of nuclear fragmentation, which successfully reproduces fragment size distributions, does not produce a plateau in the caloric curve that would signal a first-order phase transition, even when thermodynamic equilibrium is enforced analytically. The authors hedge that the absence of a phase transition may stem from the rigid cellular structure making the system behave more like a disordered crystal than a liquid that can vaporize.

## Method

The authors constructed a microcanonical partition function for a classical cellular model where A particles occupy fixed cubic cells of side d, with positions and momenta drawn randomly. They generated approximately 10^6 configurations, computed total energy as the sum of kinetic energy (from momenta drawn uniformly in a Fermi sphere) and potential energy (from nuclear and Coulomb two-body interactions), and binned configurations by total energy to obtain the partition function Z(E, A, d). They also developed an analytical version using a truncated harmonic oscillator potential between nearest neighbors, computed the canonical partition function, performed an inverse Laplace transform to obtain the microcanonical partition function, and derived the caloric curve from T⁻¹ = (∂S/∂E)_V.

## Key numbers

- System size: A = 216 particles for numerical simulations; A = 125 for 1D analytical model; A = 5³ for 3D analytical model.
- Cell side d varied in the interval [1.8, 4] fm, corresponding to average densities ρ in [0.016, 0.17] fm⁻³.
- Numerical simulations used approximately 10⁶ configurations with χ² ≤ 1 for the entropy fit using Chebyshev polynomials.
- The analytical microcanonical partition function shows an algebraic dependence on energy E starting from threshold E = A(V₀ + Cd²) in 1D, with no exponential term that would indicate a first-order phase transition.
- In the 3D analytical model, the caloric curve resembles that of an ideal classical gas (T = 3/2 E) but with a different slope due to interactions.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior cellular model (Elattari, Richert, Wagner, Zheng, 1995) that reproduced bond percolation results and ALADIN experimental data on fragment charge distributions. It directly contradicts the interpretation of experimental caloric curves by Pochodzalla et al. (1995) and the thermodynamic equilibrium models of Gross (1985, 1990) and Barz, Bondorf, Donangelo, Schulz (1986), which both show first-order phase transition signatures. The results suggest that the observed fragment size distributions may reflect critical phenomena near a second-order phase transition point, and the authors indicate future work should relax the cellular constraint and implement Metropolis Monte Carlo selection while preserving agreement with fragment size data.
