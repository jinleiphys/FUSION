# Assessment of universal relations among second-order moments of relativistic stars via reformulated perturbation equations

> We assess the universal relations among second-order moments of relativistic stars, namely the moment of inertia, tidal deformability, and spin-induced quadrupole moment, via reformulated perturbation equations. After constructing the spherical background configuration by solving two ordinary differential equations as usual, these three moments are obtained by solving four additional ordinary differential equations. They are solved numerically from the stellar center to the surface, and we do not need to derive homogeneous solutions for obtaining the quadrupole moment. This small number of ordinary differential equations to be solved enables us to identify the primary variable for each second-order moment. Investigating the profile of these variables in the star, we speculate that their nonmonotonic behavior, enhanced typically for soft equations of state and/or high compactnesses, introduces the variety to the relations among these second-order moments unless the black-hole limit is approached. Because realistic relativistic stars are widely believed to be characterized by stiff equations of state, they enjoy the universal relation to a great extent.

## Key claim

The universal relations among the moment of inertia, tidal deformability, and spin-induced quadrupole moment of relativistic stars hold to great extent for stiff equations of state typical of neutron stars, but the author speculates that nonmonotonic behavior of the primary variables in the outer region, enhanced for soft equations of state and/or high compactnesses, introduces variety to these relations unless the black-hole limit is approached.

## Method

The author reformulates the perturbation equations for slowly rotating relativistic stars so that the spin-induced quadrupole moment is obtained by solving two ordinary differential equations from the stellar center to the surface without explicitly deriving homogeneous solutions. The moment of inertia, tidal deformability, and quadrupole moment are computed by solving six ordinary differential equations in total: two for the spherical background (TOV equations), one for the moment of inertia via the logarithmic derivative w, two for the quadrupole moment via new variables f and q-tilde, and one for the tidal deformability via the logarithmic derivative y. The method is validated by computing these moments for polytropic equations of state with indices n = 1/2, 1, 3/2, and 2.

## Key numbers

- For typical neutron stars with dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda approximately 100 to 1000, the universal relations hold within about percent accuracy.
- The compactness of the maximum-mass configuration ranges from C = 0.074 for n = 2 to C = 0.32 for n = 1/2.
- For configurations with C = 0.05, the tidal deformability spans from Lambda approximately 9e4 for n = 2 to approximately 7e5 for n = 1/2.
- For the maximum-mass configuration of n = 1/2 with C = 0.32, the first term in the expression for the normalized quadrupole moment involving q-tilde_s is larger than the second term involving f_s only by a factor of approximately 4.

## Context

This work extends the study of universal relations among second-order moments originally identified by Yagi (2013) for neutron stars, which showed that relations among I, Lambda, and Q depend only weakly on the equation of state under appropriate normalization. The reformulated perturbation equations simplify the computation of the quadrupole moment compared to the standard procedure of Hartle (1967, 1968), which required integrating homogeneous solutions. The author's identification of primary variables w, y, and q-tilde enables investigation of why universality tends to be violated for soft equations of state relevant to white dwarfs, as noted by Yagi (2013), while stiff equations of state typical of neutron stars enjoy the universality. The approach may be useful for gravitational-wave data analysis that directly samples the equation of state, as done in the LIGO Scientific Collaboration (2018) analysis of GW170817.
