# Probing Sub-MeV Dark Matter with Neutron-Capture $γ$ Spectroscopy

> We present a general, discovery-grade framework for searching for weakly coupled new particles emitted in nuclear de-excitation following neutron capture. Rather than relying on isolated spectral features, the method exploits correlated ``satellite-line combs'': multiple weak $γ$-ray lines appearing at a common energy offset $Δ$ below known capture transitions. By combining likelihood information across many parent lines and multiple target nuclei, the approach strongly suppresses nuclear-structure ambiguities and instrumental artifacts. We also discuss optimal target selection and practical experimental implementation with high-resolution HPGe detectors.

# Probing Sub-MeV Dark Matter with Neutron-Capture γ Spectroscopy

## Key claim
The paper presents a framework for searching for weakly coupled dark-sector particles (masses from a few keV to several MeV) emitted in nuclear de-excitation following neutron capture, using correlated "satellite-line combs" across multiple target nuclei. The method claims discovery-level sensitivity at branching ratios as low as 10⁻⁸, but this sensitivity requires a high-intensity cold-neutron flux of approximately 10¹² n/cm²/s (achievable by the planned HIBEAM experiment at ESS) and is conditional on the use of high-resolution HPGe detectors with FWHM ~1 keV.

## Method
The method searches for weak γ-ray satellite lines appearing at a common energy offset Δ below known capture transitions (parent lines) across multiple target nuclei. A two-stage likelihood analysis is performed: Stage I fits individual parent lines to determine centroid positions and intensities, while Stage II performs a fixed-offset scan over Δ, testing the hypothesis that satellite intensity is proportional to parent intensity via a target-specific satellite fraction f_t. The profile likelihood ratio test statistic q(Δ) is summed across targets, with global significances evaluated using toy Monte Carlo simulations to account for the look-elsewhere effect and the non-negativity constraint on satellite intensity.

## Key numbers
- Detector energy resolution: FWHM ~1 keV, corresponding to Gaussian width σ_det ≈ 0.42 keV, for HPGe detectors used in PGAA experiments
- Analysis window: ±20 keV centered on each parent line, with bin width ~0.2 keV
- Parent line centroid statistical accuracy: δμ₀ ~ 10–50 eV for lines with ≥10⁴ detected counts
- Neutron flux for sensitivity projection: φ_n ~ 10¹² n/cm²/s (high-intensity cold-neutron flux achievable by HIBEAM at ESS)
- Discovery potential: with BR = 10⁻⁸ and the high-flux beamline, combined significance exceeds 5σ (global significance, LEE-corrected)
- For a lower flux of φ_n = 6.81×10⁹ n/cm²/s with 4-hour exposure, BR = 10⁻⁵ yields maximum global significance below 3σ for all tested isotopes
- Target groups cover offset scan from 1–100 keV, corresponding to particle masses m_X ≈ Δ
- Minimum resolvable offset: Δ ≳ (3–4)σ_det due to parent peak tailing degeneracy
- Pile-up probability: P_pu ≲ 0.5–2.5% for per-crystal rates R ≲ 1–5 kHz with shaping times τ ~ 1–5 μs

## Context
This work extends searches for light dark-sector particles (axion-like particles, scalar mediators) in the keV–MeV mass range, where experimental constraints are weaker and more model-dependent than for weak-scale or ultralight candidates. Prior searches have focused on electromagnetic couplings (Moriyama 1995, TEXONO 2006) or individual isolated peak structures in capture spectra (Massarczyk 2021), with sensitivity limited to branching ratios of 10⁻³ to 10⁻⁴. The method enables reanalysis of existing datasets from facilities such as the ANNRI spectrometer at J-PARC, the EXILL array at ILL, and planned experiments like HIBEAM at ESS. The approach is complementary to direct detection and reactor-based axion searches, and the authors note that the same satellite signature could in principle appear in radioactive decay or fusion evaporation, but neutron capture maximizes sensitivity to very small branching fractions.
