# Self-consistent theory of large amplitude collective motion: Applications to approximate quantization of non-separable systems and to nuclear physics

> The goal of the present account is to review our efforts to obtain and apply a ``collective'' Hamiltonian for a few, approximately decoupled, adiabatic degrees of freedom, starting from a Hamiltonian system with more or many more degrees of freedom. The approach is based on an analysis of the classical limit of quantum-mechanical problems. Initially, we study the classical problem within the framework of Hamiltonian dynamics and derive a fully self-consistent theory of large amplitude collective motion with small velocities. We derive a measure for the quality of decoupling of the collective degree of freedom. We show for several simple examples, where the classical limit is obvious, that when decoupling is good, a quantization of the collective Hamiltonian leads to accurate descriptions of the low energy properties of the systems studied. In nuclear physics problems we construct the classical Hamiltonian by means of time-dependent mean-field theory, and we transcribe our formalism to this case. We report studies of a model for monopole vibrations, of $^{28}$Si with a realistic interaction, several qualitative models of heavier nuclei, and preliminary results for a more realistic approach to heavy nuclei. Other topics included are a nuclear Born-Oppenheimer approximation for an {\em ab initio} quantum theory and a theory of the transfer of energy between collective and non-collective degrees of freedom when the decoupling is not exact. The explicit account is based on the work of the authors, but a thorough survey of other work is included.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a fully self-consistent theory of large amplitude collective motion can be derived from a microscopic many-body Hamiltonian without any *ad hoc* constraining operators, and that when the decoupling of collective degrees of freedom is good, quantizing the resulting collective Hamiltonian yields accurate low-energy properties; however, this success is demonstrated only for simple model systems where the classical limit is obvious, while applications to realistic nuclear problems remain preliminary or incomplete.

## Method

The authors first develop a classical Hamiltonian framework using canonical transformations to identify decoupled collective submanifolds, deriving exact decoupling conditions (block-diagonal mass tensor, vanishing potential gradients, and vanishing mass-tensor derivatives orthogonal to the manifold). They then extend this to an adiabatic approximation allowing momentum-dependent transformations beyond point transformations, and transcribe the formalism to nuclear physics using time-dependent mean-field theory to construct the classical Hamiltonian from the many-body problem.

## Key numbers

- For the two-oscillator model with coupling term \(\beta q_1^2 q_2\), the collective Hamiltonian on the valley takes the form \(2H = M(q_1)p_1^2 + \omega_1^2 q_1^2 + 2\beta q_1^2 f(q_1)\) with mass coefficient \(M(q_1) = (1 + (f')^2)/(f')^2\), where \(f' = df/dq_1\)
- The decoupling conditions require \(\bar{B}^{ai} = 0\), \(\bar{V}_{,a} = 0\), and \(\bar{B}^{ij}_{,a} = 0\) for the non-collective coordinates \(q^a\) and collective coordinates \(q^i\)
- For the separable Hamiltonian example, the low-energy spectrum consists of states \((n_1, 0)\) with the high-frequency oscillator (\(\omega_2^2 \gg \omega_1^2\)) in its ground state \(n_2 = 0\)

## Context

This review extends the earlier constrained Hartree-Bogoliubov approach of Kumar and Baranger (1960s-1970s), which used an *ad hoc* constraining operator (the quadrupole moment) to define the collective potential, by developing a self-consistent method where the nucleus determines its own deformation internally. The work enables future calculations of rotational-vibrational motion of deformed nuclei and fission without external constraints, though the authors note they have not yet returned to these original motivating applications. The formalism also applies to molecular structure and reactions and to the general problem of approximate quantization of non-separable systems.
