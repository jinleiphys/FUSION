# Sequential Bayesian inference with correlated heavy-ion datasets

> Bayesian inference provides a natural framework for updating knowledge as new information becomes available, often in a sequential manner by incorporating datasets in stages or reusing previous posteriors as priors. In practice, this is commonly implemented using a factorized update in which datasets are treated as conditionally independent. When datasets are statistically correlated, however, this approximation becomes inconsistent with the joint likelihood and can lead to biased posterior estimates. In this work, we investigate this issue in a controlled setting using pseudo-data with a tunable covariance structure. We compare joint inference, factorized sequential updating, and a formulation based on the exact conditional likelihood. We show that factorized updates reproduce the joint posterior only in the limit of conditional independence, and otherwise lead to systematic deviations that grow with the correlation strength, while conditional updates remain consistent with the joint result. To interpret these deviations, we introduce an information decomposition that separates contributions into components that are new and components that are redundant across datasets. We show that correlations induce a structured, parameter-dependent redistribution of information, governed by the overlap of dataset sensitivities. The resulting mismatch between marginal and conditional information quantitatively explains the observed deviations. These results provide a practical diagnostic for assessing the consistency of sequential Bayesian inference with correlated datasets and highlight the need for a consistent treatment of correlations within a common probabilistic framework.

# Sequential Bayesian inference with correlated heavy-ion datasets

## Key claim

Factorized sequential Bayesian updates, which treat datasets as conditionally independent, reproduce the joint posterior only in the limit of conditional independence; otherwise they introduce systematic deviations that grow with cross-dataset correlation strength, while conditional sequential updates remain fully consistent with the joint result for Gaussian likelihoods regardless of update order.

## Method

The authors construct a reduced four-parameter model ($S$, $\varepsilon_2$, $\kappa_N$, $\kappa_v$) mapping to six observables (three yield-like $N_i$ and three flow-like $v_{2,i}$ in three centrality bins), with pseudo-data generated from a reference point plus Gaussian noise using a tunable block covariance matrix. They compare three inference strategies, joint likelihood, factorized sequential updating (marginal likelihood), and conditional sequential updating (exact conditional likelihood), varying the cross-dataset correlation strength $\rho$ continuously from 0 to 0.7, and analyze posterior deviations using Fisher information and an information decomposition into new versus redundant components.

## Key numbers

- Model parameters: $S \in [0.5, 1.5]$, $\varepsilon_2 \in [0.05, 0.5]$, $\kappa_N \in [0.5, 1.5]$, $\kappa_v \in [0.5, 1.5]$ (uniform priors)
- Reference observable values: $N^{\mathrm{ref}} = (1447.5, 649.0, 261.0)$ and $v_2^{\mathrm{ref}} = (0.0359, 0.0831, 0.0994)$ for central (0–10%), mid-central (20–30%), and peripheral (40–50%) centrality bins
- Cross-coupling coefficients: $\alpha_N = 0.12$ (yield sensitivity to $\varepsilon_2$), $\alpha_v = 0.18$ (flow sensitivity to $S$)
- Independent noise fraction: $f_{\mathrm{ind}} = 0.4$; correlation length $\ell = 1.0$ for bin-to-bin correlations
- Observable uncertainties scaled by factor 1.7 relative to ALICE-inspired reference uncertainties for Pb–Pb at 2.76 TeV
- Cross-dataset correlation strength $\rho$ varied across values including 0.0, 0.3, and 0.7

## Context

This work extends Bayesian inference methodology for heavy-ion collision analyses (Bernhard 2016, Bernhard 2019, Nijs 2020, JETSCAPE 2021, Ehlers 2024) by systematically studying how cross-dataset correlations affect sequential updating, a topic that has received less attention than prior propagation or emulator construction (Yamauchi 2023, Roch 2025). The controlled model isolates correlation effects from surrogate-model uncertainties, enabling future development of diagnostics for assessing consistency in multi-stage Bayesian analyses of heavy-ion data and other complex physical systems.
