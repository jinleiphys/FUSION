# Can cosmic rays provide sign of strangelets?

> We discuss the possible imprints of Strange Quark Matter (SQM) in cosmic ray data. In particular, we investigate the propagation of SQM through the atmosphere and discuss: i) direct candidates for strangelets, ii) exotic events interpreted as signals of SQM and iii) muon bundles and delayed neutrons in Extensive Air Showers. The physics and astrophysics of SQM is shortly reviewed. We point out the possibility that extreme energy cosmic rays are the results of the decay of unstable primordial objects. Finally, the abundance of possible candidates for strangelets and their mass spectrum are estimated and compared with the astrophysical limits and prospects of the possible observation of SQM in accelerator experiments are outlined.

## Key claim

Cosmic ray data may contain signatures of Strange Quark Matter (SQM) in the form of strangelets, but the evidence is indirect and relies on interpreting anomalous events, such as deeply penetrating particles, high-multiplicity muon bundles, and delayed neutrons, through a specific propagation model in which heavy strangelets lose mass by stripping in atmospheric collisions until they reach a critical size and disintegrate.

## Method

The authors model the propagation of strangelets through the Earth's atmosphere using two collision scenarios: a "standard model" (SM) in which each quark from the target nucleus interacts with one quark from the strangelet, reducing the strangelet mass number by the target mass number per collision, and a "tube-like model" (TM) in which all quarks in the geometrical intersection region participate. They compare the predicted atmospheric penetration depths, muon multiplicity distributions, and neutron time distributions against data from cosmic ray experiments including CosmoLEP (ALEPH detector), Chacaltaya emulsion chambers, and neutron monitor observations. The flux of strangelets at the top of the atmosphere is estimated by fitting the propagation model to reported candidate events (Saito, Price, Exotic Track, Centauro) and upper limits (JACEE, Concorde).

## Key numbers

* The critical mass number for strangelet stability against neutron emission is \(A_{crit} = 300\) to \(400\), with a specific value of \(A_{crit} = 320\) used in simulations.
* The rescaled radius \(r_0\) of strangelets is comparable to ordinary nuclei, with differences not exceeding 10%–20%.
* The estimated abundance of strangelets in the primary cosmic ray flux is \(F_S(A_0 = A_{crit}) / F_{tot} = 2.4 \times 10^{-5}\) at the same energy per particle.
* The flux of strangelets as a function of initial mass number follows \(\propto A_0^{-7.5}\) for the standard model and \(\propto A_0^{-2.8}\) for the tube-like model.
* In the CosmoLEP experiment, 5 muon events with multiplicities up to 150 were observed in \(3.7 \times 10^5\) muon events recorded over \(10^6\) seconds, with a momentum cut-off of 70 GeV.
* The high-multiplicity event with \(N_\mu \simeq 110\) on 16 m\(^2\) corresponds to \(\sim 5600\) muons with \(E_\mu \geq 70\) GeV or 1000 muons with energy above 220 GeV.
* The mean lifetime of a strangelet assumed to describe delayed neutron data is \(\tau \simeq 2000\) \(\mu\)s.
* The NA52 experiment at CERN collected statistics of several \(10^{12}\) interactions and reported a short-lived strangelet candidate with mass 7.4 GeV and charge \(Z = -1\).
* The E864 experiment at BNL-AGS set an upper limit of \(10^{-8}\) strangelets per central collision in 11.5 A GeV Au+Pb collisions.

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical proposals for SQM stability by Alcock and Farhi (1985) and Witten (1984), and builds on the authors' own propagation model developed in Rybczynski, Wlodarczyk, and Wilk (2000). It interprets specific cosmic ray anomalies, such as the Saito event (charge \(Z \cong 14\), mass \(A \cong 350\) and \(A \cong 450\)), the Price event (\(Z \cong 46\), \(A > 1000\)), the Exotic Track event (\(Z \cong 20\), \(A \cong 460\)), and Centauro events, as possible strangelet signatures. The analysis enables future searches for strangelets in accelerator experiments, particularly the CASTOR detector proposed for the ALICE experiment at LHC, and suggests that extreme-energy cosmic rays exceeding the GZK cutoff could originate from decaying primordial strangelets rather than accelerated protons.
