# Sensitivity of isotope shift to distribution of nuclear charge density

> It is usually assumed that the field isotope shift (FIS) is completely determined by the change of the averaged squared values of the nuclear charge radius $\langle r^2\rangle$. Relativistic corrections modify the expression for FIS, which is actually described by the change of $\langle r^{2 γ}\rangle$, where $γ=\sqrt{1 - Z^2 α^2}$. In the present paper we consider corrections to FIS which are due to the nuclear deformation and due to the predicted reduced charge density in the middle of the superheavy nuclei produced by a very strong proton repulsion (hole in the nuclear centre). Specifically, we investigate effects which can not be completely reduced to the change of $\langle r^2 \rangle$ or $\langle r^{2 γ}\rangle$.

# Sensitivity of isotope shift to distribution of nuclear charge density

## Key claim

The field isotope shift (FIS) in heavy atoms is not completely determined by the change of the averaged squared nuclear charge radius ⟨r²⟩, as usually assumed; relativistic corrections mean the FIS is actually described by the change of ⟨r^{2γ}⟩ where γ = √(1 − Z²α²), and additional corrections arise from nuclear deformation and from a predicted reduced charge density (hole) in the centre of superheavy nuclei that cannot be fully reduced to either ⟨r²⟩ or ⟨r^{2γ}⟩.

## Method

The authors performed relativistic Hartree-Fock (RHF) calculations with a correlation potential (Σ) for the E120⁺ ion, using nuclear parameters from nuclear structure calculations (Afanasiev). They computed isotope shifts via the random phase approximation (RPA) for four types of nuclear charge distribution variation: a hole at the origin, quadrupole deformation, change of skin thickness, and change of nuclear RMS radius. They compared the shifts for s, p₁/₂, and p₃/₂ states and for transitions between them across even isotopes from ²⁹²E120 to ³⁰⁶E120.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of the energy shift due to a hole (k=0.5) to the reference isotope shift between ²⁹²E120 and ²⁹⁴E120 is about 8% for s states.
- A hole parameter k=0.5 corresponds to a 14.5% decrease in the skin thickness parameter t in the Fermi distribution.
- Quadrupole deformation with β=−0.4 produces an energy shift of approximately 2 cm⁻¹ for s states, or about 20% of the reference isotope shift.
- For β=−0.4, the equivalent increase in skin thickness t is 30.5%.
- The difference in isotope shift for the 8p₁/₂ state between a deformed nucleus and a nucleus with changed RMS radius (chosen to match the 8s shift) is 4% or 0.014 cm⁻¹.
- For the 8s−8p₁/₂ transition, the difference between the realistic case (with deformation) and a model case (β=0, RMS radius fitted to s states) is −0.0028 cm⁻¹.
- For the 8s−8p₃/₂ transition, the corresponding difference is 0.0002 cm⁻¹.
- The modified formula F̃ δ⟨r^{2γ}⟩ shows only about 0.01% variation for spherical nuclei over the isotope range A=294 to A=306, but for deformed nuclei the value of F̃ jumps by several percent between isotopes.
- The value of F in the standard formula F δ⟨r²⟩ varies by less than 1% for neighbouring isotopes for both spherical and deformed nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic treatment of field isotope shifts developed by Flambaum, Geddes, and Viatkina, who showed that the FIS depends on ⟨r^{2γ}⟩ rather than ⟨r²⟩, and by Racah (1932) and Rosenthal (1932) on relativistic corrections. It builds on nuclear structure calculations for superheavy elements by Afanasiev and collaborators, and on earlier isotope shift calculations for elements Z=102-109 (No, Db, Sg-Mt). The results enable the use of isotope shift measurements to study nuclear deformation in even-even superheavy nuclei, which is otherwise inaccessible via atomic spectroscopy (in odd isotopes, electric quadrupole hyperfine structure can be used). The work also informs the interpretation of King plot nonlinearity in searches for new long-range forces (Berengut, 2017) and the prediction of spectra for neutron-rich superheavy elements near the hypothetical island of stability (Dzuba, 2017).
