# Collapse of the random phase approximation: examples and counter-examples from the shell model

> The Hartree-Fock approximation to the many-fermion problem can break exact symmetries, and in some cases by changing a parameter in the interaction one can drive the Hartree-Fock minimum from a symmetry-breaking state to a symmetry-conserving state (also referred to as a ``phase transition'' in the literature). The order of the transition is important when one applies the random phase approximation (RPA) to the of the Hartree-Fock wavefunction: if first order, RPA is stable through the transition, but if second-order, then the RPA amplitudes become large and lead to unphysical results. The latter is known as ``collapse'' of the RPA. While the difference between first- and second-order transitions in the RPA was first pointed out by Thouless, we present for the first time non-trivial examples of both first- and second-order transitions in a uniform model, the interacting shell-model, where we can compare to exact numerical results.

## Key claim

The random phase approximation (RPA) does not always collapse at a symmetry-restoring phase transition: first-order transitions, driven by even-parity modes such as quadrupole, allow stable RPA through the transition, while only second-order transitions, driven by odd-parity modes, cause the unphysical divergence known as RPA collapse.

## Method

The authors performed Hartree-Fock (HF) calculations followed by RPA in the interacting shell model, using the SHERPA code with realistic interactions (USD, Cohen-Kurath, Millener-Kurath) in the p-shell and sd-shell valence spaces. They drove the HF ground state between symmetry-conserving and symmetry-breaking states by varying single-particle energy differences, and compared HF+RPA results to exact shell-model diagonalizations using the REDSTICK code for nuclei including 28Si, 32S, and 12C.

## Key numbers

- For 28Si with the USD interaction, varying the 0d5/2 single-particle energy relative to the 1s1/2-0d3/2 energies by an amount Δ (Δ=0 corresponds to original Wildenthal values), the HF+RPA energy shows a discontinuity at the transition between oblate deformed and spherical states, with a significant region of coexistence of both locally stable HF solutions.
- For 28Si, the collapsing RPA eigenfrequency on the spherical side has degeneracy 5, indicating a quadrupole (J=2) mode; the corresponding |Y|² amplitude increases dramatically as the frequency approaches zero.
- For 32S, varying the 0d3/2 single-particle energy, a J=4 (degeneracy 9) hexadecupole mode falls below the J=2 mode, suggesting the hexadecupole mode becomes unstable first.
- For 12C in the 0p1/2-0p3/2-0d5/2-1s1/2 space, with Δ defined so that Δ=0 places the first 3- state at approximately 6.1 MeV above ground state, the HF+RPA energy dives (collapses) at the transition between exact-parity and parity-mixed HF states.

## Context

This work extends Thouless's original analysis (Thouless, 1961) of first- versus second-order transitions in RPA, which had been largely forgotten in favor of the well-known collapse example in the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model (Lipkin, Meshkov, and Glick, 1965). By demonstrating both types of transitions in realistic shell-model calculations with complicated forces, the paper shows that first-order transitions (driven by even-parity modes) do not cause RPA collapse, enabling future work to identify such transitions in practical RPA calculations without assuming collapse is inevitable.
