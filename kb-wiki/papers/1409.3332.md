# A possible global group structure for exotic states

> Based on the fact that the long expected pentaquark which possesses the exotic quantum numbers of $B=1$ and $S=1$ was not experimentally found, although exotic states of $XYZ$ have been observed recently, we conjecture that the heavy flavors may play an important role in stabilizing the hadronic structures beyond the traditional $q\bar q$ and $qqq$ composites.

# A possible global group structure for exotic states

## Key claim
The authors conjecture that heavy flavors (charm and bottom) play a crucial role in stabilizing multi-quark hadronic structures beyond the traditional $q\bar q$ and $qqq$ composites, based on the observation that the long-sought pentaquark with $B=1$ and $S=1$ was not found experimentally, while $XYZ$ states containing hidden heavy flavor have been observed.

## Method
The authors propose a global group structure $G = SU_c(3) \times SU_H(2) \times SU_L(3)$, where $SU_H(2)$ is a heavy-flavor symmetry doublet containing $c$ and $b$ quarks (analogous to the strong isospin doublet for light quarks), and $SU_L(3)$ is the standard light-flavor symmetry for $u,d,s$. They classify four-quark states according to their color-singlet representations and assign observed $XYZ$ mesons to specific $SU_H(2)$ and $SU_L(3)$ representations, drawing an analogy between the stability of hydrogen molecules versus positronium molecules to argue that heavy constituents stabilize multi-quark systems.

## Key numbers
- The hydrogen ion $H_-$ resolves by absorbing 0.75 eV, while a hydrogen molecule requires 4.45 eV to resolve into two hydrogen atoms, used to argue that systems with heavier constituents are more stable.
- The observed exotic mesons discussed include $Z_b(10610)$, $Z_b(10650)$, $Z_c(3900)$, $Z_c(4020)$, and $Z_c(4025)$, all containing hidden charm or bottom.
- The newly observed $d^*$ resonance contains six quarks with baryon number 2, posing a potential challenge to the authors' assumption.

## Context
This work extends the $SU(3)$ quark model of Gell-Mann (1964) and Zweig (1964) to accommodate recently observed $XYZ$ exotic states reported by CLEOc, BaBar, Belle, CDF, D0, BESIII, LHCb, and CMS collaborations. It is motivated by the failure to observe the pentaquark with $B=1$ and $S=1$ predicted by Gao (1999). The authors contrast their tetraquark interpretation with the molecular picture of $D\bar D^*$ and $D^*\bar D^*$ states, and note that Brodsky (2014) also argued that tetraquark states are obvious in the charm and bottom sectors but not in the light-quark sector. The work enables predictions of charged $b\bar c q\bar q$ mesons and $b\bar c qqq$ pentaquarks that could be searched for at LHCb.
