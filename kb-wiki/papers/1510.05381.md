# Evolution of $N = 28$ shell closure in relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov theory

> The $N = 28$ shell gap in sulfur, argon, calcium and titanium isotopes is investigated in the framework of relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory. The evolutions of neutron shell gap, separation energy, single particle energy and pairing energy are analyzed, and it is found that $N = 28$ shell gap is quenched in sulfur isotopes but persists in argon, calcium and titanium isotopes. The evolution of $N = 28$ shell gap in $N = 28$ isotonic chain is discussed, and the erosion of $N = 28$ shell gap is understood with the evolution of potential with proton number.

# Evolution of $N = 28$ shell closure in relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov theory

## Key claim
The $N = 28$ shell gap is quenched in sulfur isotopes but persists in argon, calcium, and titanium isotopes when calculated with the relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory using the PC-PK1 density functional, even under the assumption of spherical symmetry. The erosion of the shell closure in sulfur is attributed to the relatively slower rise of the 2p$_{3/2}$ single-particle energy compared to the 1f$_{7/2}$ level as proton number decreases, driven by changes in potential depth and surface diffuseness.

## Method
The authors solved the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov equations in coordinate space within a spherical box of radius 20 fm and step size 0.1 fm, using the PC-PK1 density functional for the particle-hole channel and a density-dependent delta pairing force with strength $V_0 = 685.0$ MeV fm$^3$ for the particle-particle channel. They calculated two-neutron separation energies $S_{2n}$, the neutron shell gap $D_n$ defined from one-neutron separation energies, single-particle energies, and pairing energies for sulfur, argon, calcium, and titanium isotopes. The evolution of the effective single-particle potential was analyzed to understand the mechanism of shell gap erosion.

## Key numbers
- The average shell gap between 2p$_{3/2}$ and 1f$_{7/2}$ for titanium isotopes is 5.10 MeV, for calcium isotopes 4.26 MeV, for argon isotopes 3.54 MeV, and for sulfur isotopes only 2.74 MeV.
- The shell gap between 2p$_{3/2}$ and 1f$_{7/2}$ decreases from 5.10 MeV in $^{50}$Ti to 2.71 MeV in $^{44}$S.
- The pairing strength $V_0 = 685.0$ MeV fm$^3$ was fixed by reproducing experimental odd-even mass differences of Ca isotopes, Sn isotopes, $N = 20$ isotones, and $N = 50$ isotones.
- The saturation density $\rho_0$ was set to the empirical value 0.152 fm$^{-3}$.
- The RCHB calculation was performed in a spherical box of radius $R = 20$ fm with step size 0.1 fm.

## Context
This work extends previous theoretical studies of the $N = 28$ shell closure using axial RMF theory (Werner 1996), triaxial RMF theory (Hirata 1996), and relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory based on harmonic oscillator basis (Lalazissis 1999), all of which found erosion of the $N = 28$ shell gap in neutron-rich sulfur isotopes. The present RCHB calculation shows that this erosion persists even with spherical symmetry, and provides a mechanism via the evolution of the effective single-particle potential. The results are consistent with experimental findings from mass measurements and spectroscopy (Glasmacher 1997, Sarazin 2000, Gaudefroy 2009, Force 2010, Santiago-Gonzalez 2011) showing the absence of the $N = 28$ shell closure in sulfur, and with recent mass measurements of $^{47-48}$Ar (Meisel 2015) demonstrating the persistence of the shell closure in argon. The authors note that further investigation with the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc) (Zhou 2010, Li 2012, Chen 2012) is necessary to assess the effects of deformation.
