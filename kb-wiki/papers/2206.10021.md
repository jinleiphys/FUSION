# Hidden-nucleons neural-network quantum states for the nuclear many-body problem

> We generalize the hidden-fermion family of neural network quantum states to encompass both continuous and discrete degrees of freedom and solve the nuclear many-body Schrödinger equation in a systematically improvable fashion. We demonstrate that adding hidden nucleons to the original Hilbert space considerably augments the expressivity of the neural-network architecture compared to the Slater-Jastrow ansatz. The benefits of explicitly encoding in the wave function point symmetries such as parity and time-reversal are also discussed. Leveraging on improved optimization methods and sampling techniques, the hidden-nucleon ansatz achieves an accuracy comparable to the numerically-exact hyperspherical harmonic method in light nuclei and to the auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo in $^{16}$O. Thanks to its polynomial scaling with the number of nucleons, this method opens the way to highly-accurate quantum Monte Carlo studies of medium-mass nuclei.

# Hidden-nucleons neural-network quantum states for the nuclear many-body problem

## Key claim
Adding hidden nucleons to the Hilbert space considerably augments the expressivity of the neural-network architecture compared to the Slater-Jastrow ansatz, achieving accuracy comparable to the numerically-exact hyperspherical harmonic method in light nuclei and to auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo in 16O, provided that point symmetries such as parity and time-reversal are explicitly encoded and improved optimization methods are used.

## Method
The authors generalize the hidden-fermion approach to continuous and discrete degrees of freedom by introducing a hidden-nucleon (HN) ansatz that augments the Slater determinant with additional fictitious nucleon coordinates. The wave function is parametrized using permutation-invariant neural networks with Deep-Sets architectures and logsumexp pooling, and the variational parameters are optimized using a stochastic-reconfiguration algorithm with an RMSProp-inspired diagonal regularization. Parity and time-reversal symmetries are directly encoded in the variational state, and the Metropolis-Hastings sampling is constrained to preserve total isospin projection and total spin projection.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state energy of 3He and 3H with Ah=3 hidden nucleons and positive-parity ansatz: the SR-RMSProp energies are noticeably closer to the numerically-exact hyperspherical harmonics (HH) result than those obtained with the original SR algorithm, and both are appreciably lower than the ANN-SJ value reported in Gnech (2021).
- 4He ground-state energy with Ah=4 hidden nucleons: the parity and time-reversal conserving wave function Psi_HN^{PT}(R,S) provides a variational energy consistent with the numerically-exact HH estimate of Gnech (2021), outperforming the parity-conserving wave function alone.
- 16O ground-state energy: with Ah=2 hidden nucleons, the hidden-nucleon wave function matches the VMC value; with increasing Ah, the variational energy becomes consistent with the AFDMC value from Schiavilla (2021) within error bars.
- The ground-state wave function of 16O with Ah hidden nucleons is determined by about 77,000 neural-network parameters.
- The calculation of the wave function requires A^3 operations, while evaluating the expectation value of the spin-isospin dependent component of the NN potential costs A^5 operations.

## Context
This work extends the hidden-fermion approach of Javier (2021), originally developed for lattice fermionic systems, to continuous nuclear coordinates and applies it to the nuclear many-body problem. It builds on earlier neural-network quantum state applications to nuclei by Adams (2020) and Gnech (2021), which used an ANN Slater-Jastrow ansatz for A ≤ 6 nuclei but highlighted shortcomings due to incorrect nodal surfaces. The method achieves accuracy comparable to the hyperspherical harmonics method of Kievsky (2008) for light nuclei and to auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo for 16O, while exhibiting polynomial scaling with nucleon number, opening the way to highly-accurate quantum Monte Carlo studies of medium-mass nuclei up to 40Ca.
