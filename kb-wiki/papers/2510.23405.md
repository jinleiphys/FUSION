# Observable Signatures of a Quarkyonic Phase in Neutron Stars

> Performing Bayesian inference on quarkyonic equation-of-state models for neutron star matter, we find they satisfy all current astrophysical observations, thus reinforcing the argument for the use of such neutron star matter equation-of-state models alongside traditional ones. To observationally differentiate between stars with and without a quarkyonic phase, we identify a novel observational signature: the slope of the mass-radius relation at some fixed mass in conjunction with the sound speed at the star's center. In this plane, we find quarkyonic stars in a region with high central sound speed and positive slope, that is distinct from purely nucleonic stars. High accuracy NS radii measurements facilitated by the next generation of detectors, coupled with ongoing studies of mapping astrophysical observables to microphysical properties like sound speed can be used for testing this signature. Our results indicate that a neutron star with these properties would be a strong evidence for existence of a quarkyonic phase or a similar crossover transition in its core.

## Key claim

The paper claims that neutron stars containing a quarkyonic phase can be distinguished from purely nucleonic stars by a novel observational signature: the combination of a high central sound speed (greater than about 0.4 in units of c squared) and a positive slope of the mass-radius relation at a fixed mass. The authors hedge that this signature requires high-accuracy radius measurements from next-generation detectors and indirect inference of central sound speed, meaning the test is not immediately feasible with current data.

## Method

The authors performed Bayesian inference on a seven-parameter quarkyonic equation-of-state model, using nested sampling with the UltraNest package to traverse the parameter space. They constrained the model against three progressively restrictive sets of multimessenger observations: Set α used NICER data for PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620, GW170817 tidal deformability, and chiral effective field theory bounds; Set β added NICER data for PSR J0437-4715 and PSR J0614-3329 with a maximum mass ceiling of 2.33 solar masses; Set γ reduced the maximum mass ceiling to 2.15 solar masses. For each parameter combination, they solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius and mass-tidal deformability relations, then computed the slope dR/dM at fixed masses and the central sound speed.

## Key numbers

- The quarkyonic transition density posterior median shifts from 0.345 fm^{-3} (Set α) to 0.405 fm^{-3} (Set β) to 0.464 fm^{-3} (Set γ), with 2σ upper bounds near 0.78 fm^{-3} for all sets.
- The symmetry energy slope L posterior median decreases from 63.8 MeV (Set α) to 53.3 MeV (Set β) to 53.5 MeV (Set γ), with 2σ ranges spanning approximately 34-95 MeV.
- The canonical neutron star radius R_{1.4} median decreases from 13.23 km (Set α) to 12.36 km (Set β) to 12.18 km (Set γ).
- The tidal deformability at 1.4 solar masses Λ_{1.4} median decreases from 661 (Set α) to 423 (Set β) to 373 (Set γ).
- The central sound speed squared at 1.4 solar masses has median values of 0.459 (Set α), 0.508 (Set β), and 0.427 (Set γ), with hadronic stars lying in a narrow band of approximately 0.2 to 0.4 while quarkyonic stars reach higher values.
- The slope dR/dM at 1.4 solar masses has median values of 0.390 km/M_sun (Set α), 0.135 km/M_sun (Set β), and -0.115 km/M_sun (Set γ), with quarkyonic stars preferring positive values and hadronic stars preferring negative values.

## Context

This work extends the quarkyonic matter model developed by McLerran (2007) and Zhao (2020) by performing Bayesian inference against the full suite of current multimessenger observations, going beyond earlier studies that only checked consistency with individual constraints. The identification of the mass-radius slope versus central sound speed as a diagnostic is new, building on prior work by Ferreira (2025) and Bauswein (2025) that used the mass-radius slope to infer hyperon onset, and by Tang (2025) that used ΔR = R_{1.4} - R_{2.0} for first-order phase transitions. The authors note that unlike hyperon or hybrid-transition signatures, their approach remains directly observable once two radius measurements at different masses exist, and that the strong positive correlation between ΔR and dR/dM at 1.4 solar masses means these quantities can be used interchangeably, reducing required measurement accuracy.
