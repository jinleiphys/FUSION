# Magnetic field at nucleus for ionized atoms

> The study of the magnetic properties of atoms and nuclei was performed. A linear dependence between the strength of the magnetic field at the nucleus and the effective charge divided by the major quantum number were analyzed and explained. The calculations of the magnetic field at the nucleus were done for a wide set of electron configurations within the framework of relativistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock method. Results are compared to experimental results of $g$-factor measurements.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates a linear dependence of the magnetic field at the nucleus on the effective charge divided by the principal quantum number for highly ionized atoms, but this linearity holds only under the condition that the electron configuration is constrained by the measured mean total electron spin (typically J = 9/2) and the effective charge derived from ion velocity.

## Method
The authors performed relativistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock calculations using the GRASP code to compute the magnetic field at the nucleus for a wide set of electron configurations across multiple nuclei. They compared these calculated fields to experimental g-factor measurements from Recoil in Vacuum (RIV) experiments for ions with atomic numbers Z = 44 to 64, using the Schwietz-Grande formula to determine effective charge from ion velocity and constraining configurations by the measured mean electron spin.

## Key numbers
- For 132Te, the calculated mean magnetic field for configurations with a paired 4s orbital was <B> = 8.12 kT, compared to the experimental value <B> = 8.8 kT (Stone et al., 2001)
- For 150Sm, the experimental magnetic field was 3.7 kT at v/c = 0.029, with GRASP calculation giving 3.28 kT (Ward et al.)
- For 136Nd, the experimental magnetic field was 1.23 kT at v/c = 0.01, with GRASP calculation giving 1.44 kT (Tucholski et al.)
- For 124Sn, the experimental magnetic field was 4.9 kT at v/c = 0.037, with GRASP calculation giving 5.01 kT (Allmond et al.)
- For 132Te, the experimental magnetic field was 8.8 kT at v/c = 0.062, with GRASP calculation giving 8.12 kT (Stone et al., 2001)
- The weighted mean electron spin calculated from the asymptotic attenuation factor G(infinity) for I=2 gave J = 4.48, close to the experimentally observed J = 9/2

## Context
This work extends the semi-empirical charge-state formulations of Betz (1972) and Grande (2004) by connecting them to relativistic magnetic field calculations, building on the pioneering hyperfine structure studies of Andrews (1970s) and Stuchbery (review). The results enable systematic prediction of magnetic fields at nuclei for g-factor measurements in heavy ions, reducing the range of possible electron configurations by combining velocity-dependent effective charge constraints with the observed preference for paired electron configurations and the characteristic mean spin J = 9/2.
