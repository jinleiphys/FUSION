# Predictions for Boson-Jet Observables and Fragmentation Function Ratios from a Hybrid Strong/Weak Coupling Model for Jet Quenching

> We have previously introduced a hybrid strong/weak coupling model for jet quenching in heavy ion collisions that describes the production and fragmentation of jets at weak coupling, using PYTHIA, and describes the rate at which each parton in the jet shower loses energy as it propagates through the strongly coupled plasma, dE/dx, using an expression computed holographically at strong coupling. The model has a single free parameter that we fit to a single experimental measurement. We then confront our model with experimental data on many other jet observables, focusing here on boson-jet observables, finding that it provides a good description of present jet data. Next, we provide the predictions of our hybrid model for many measurements to come, including those for inclusive jet, dijet, photon-jet and Z-jet observables in heavy ion collisions with energy $\sqrt{s}=5.02$ ATeV coming soon at the LHC. As the statistical uncertainties on near-future measurements of photon-jet observables are expected to be much smaller than those in present data, with about an order of magnitude more photon-jet events expected, predictions for these observables are particularly important. We find that most of our pre- and post-dictions do not depend sensitively on the form we choose for the rate of energy loss dE/dx of the partons in the shower. This gives our predictions considerable robustness. To better discriminate between possible forms for the rate of energy loss, though, we must turn to intrajet observables. Here, we focus on ratios of fragmentation functions. We close with a suggestion for a particular ratio, between the fragmentation functions of inclusive and associated jets with the same kinematics in the same collisions, which is particularly sensitive to the x- and E-dependence of dE/dx, and hence may be used to learn which mechanism of parton energy loss best describes the quenching of jets.

## Key claim

The hybrid strong/weak coupling model, which uses a holographically computed energy loss rate for partons in a strongly coupled plasma, successfully describes existing boson-jet observables from LHC heavy ion collisions, but most of these observables are not sensitive to the specific form of the energy loss rate. The authors identify a ratio of fragmentation functions between inclusive and associated jets in the same Pb-Pb collisions that is particularly discriminating between different energy loss mechanisms.

## Method

The authors combine PYTHIA Monte Carlo simulations for jet production and fragmentation at weak coupling with a holographically derived energy loss rate dE/dx for partons traversing the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma. They embed jets in viscous hydrodynamic simulations of the expanding medium, incorporate the effects of fluid flow on the energy loss rate via Lorentz transformations between the local fluid rest frame and the collision center-of-mass frame, and compare predictions for photon-jet and Z-jet observables against experimental data and against two control models (radiative and collisional energy loss forms).

## Key numbers

- The stopping distance for a massless quark in strongly coupled plasma is given by x_stop = (1/(2*kappa_sc)) * (E_in^(1/3) / T^(4/3)), where kappa_sc is the single free parameter of the model
- For gluons, the stopping distance is shorter than for quarks by a factor of (C_A/C_F)^(1/3) = (9/4)^(1/3), and kappa_sc is taken larger by this factor
- Predictions are provided for Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s) = 5.02 ATeV, with about an order of magnitude more photon-jet events expected in upcoming LHC Run 2 data
- The radiative energy loss rate is dE/dx = -kappa_rad * T^3 * x, and the collisional rate is dE/dx = -kappa_coll * T^2, with gluon rates larger by a factor of C_A/C_F = 9/4

## Context

This work extends the hybrid strong/weak coupling model introduced by Casalderrey-Solana et al. (2014) to boson-jet observables, following their earlier successful description of single-jet and dijet observables. The model improves upon that earlier implementation by incorporating fluid flow effects on energy loss and using viscous rather than inviscid hydrodynamic simulations from Shen et al. (2014). The authors find that photon-jet data can also be described by the perturbative treatment of Wang (2013), and note that Renk (2012) previously observed that dijet asymmetry is not particularly discriminating between energy loss models. The proposed ratio of fragmentation functions between inclusive and associated jets in the same Pb-Pb collisions is designed to overcome the hadronization uncertainties that limit the discriminating power of fragmentation function ratios normalized to p-p collisions.
