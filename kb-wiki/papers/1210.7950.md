# G2 gauge theories

> QCD can be formulated using any gauge group. One particular interesting choice is to replace SU(3) by the exceptional group G2. Conceptually, this group is the simplest group with a trivial center. It thus permits to study the conjectured relevance of center degrees of freedom for QCD. Practically, since all its representation are real, it is possible to perform lattice simulations for this theory also at finite baryon densities. It is thus an excellent environment to test methods and to investigate general properties of gauge theories at finite densities. We review the status of our understanding of gauge theories with the gauge group G2, including Yang-Mills theory, Yang-Mills-Higgs theory, and QCD both in the vacuum and in the phase diagram.

## Key claim

G2 gauge theories, despite having a trivial center, exhibit most of the salient features of SU(N) Yang-Mills theories, including a first-order finite-temperature phase transition, Casimir scaling of the Wilson potential, and chiral symmetry breaking; the paper reviews the status of these theories as a testbed for finite-density QCD methods, noting that most investigations remain at a qualitative and exploratory level.

## Method

The paper reviews lattice simulations of G2 Yang-Mills theory, G2 Yang-Mills-Higgs theory, and G2 QCD with one flavor of dynamical fermions. Simulations use the Macfarlane representation for G2 link variables in the 7-dimensional fundamental representation, with algorithms described in prior work (Maas 2007, Wellegehausen 2011, Maas 2012). Results include measurements of the Wilson potential, Polyakov loop, gluon propagator, running coupling, topological susceptibility, chiral condensate, and hadron masses on lattices of various sizes (e.g., 8^3 x 16 and larger), with scale set by the intermediate-distance fundamental string tension or the diquark mass.

## Key numbers

- The adjoint representation of G2 is 14-dimensional, yielding 14 gluons.
- The topological susceptibility of G2 Yang-Mills theory is roughly (150 MeV)^4 (with large systematic errors).
- The critical temperature for the first-order phase transition in G2 Yang-Mills theory is taken from Cossu (2007).
- For G2 QCD on an 8^3 x 16 lattice, the diquark mass changes from approximately 500 MeV at beta=0.90 to approximately 200 MeV at beta=1.10, assuming a nucleon mass of about 1 GeV.
- The saturation density for quark number density is n_q,sat = 2 * N_c * N_f = 14 quarks per lattice site.
- The onset transition in G2 QCD occurs at a chemical potential of approximately half the diquark mass, with a value of mu_0 approximately 0.5 in units of the diquark mass.
- The lattice constant a is approximately 0.2 fm for the equation-of-state results.

## Context

This review extends the proposal by Holland (2003) to use G2 as a test of center symmetry in confinement, and builds on lattice studies of G2 Yang-Mills theory by Pepe (2006), Greensite (2006), and Cossu (2007). It contrasts with QCD with gauge group SU(2) (QC2D) studied by Hands (2006, 2011), Strodthoff (2011), and Cotter (2012), and with QCD in the strong coupling limit by de Forcrand (2009) and Fromm (2011). The work enables benchmarking of finite-density lattice methods (e.g., analytical continuation, Taylor expansions, Lee-Yang zeros) and continuum approaches (e.g., functional methods, chiral perturbation theory) in a theory without a sign problem, and opens the question of whether G2 QCD can be Higgs-broken to ordinary SU(3) QCD.
