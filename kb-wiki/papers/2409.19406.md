# On the origin of the peak of the sound velocity for isospin imbalanced strongly interacting matter

> We study the properties of a system composed of strongly interacting matter with an isospin imbalance, using as an effective description of QCD the two-flavor Linear Sigma Model with quarks. From the one-loop effective potential, including the two light quarks, pions and sigma contributions, and enforcing the restrictions imposed by chiral symmetry, we show that the development of an isospin condensate comes together with the emergence of a Goldstone mode that provides a constraint for the chiral and isospin condensates as a result of a non-trivial mixing between the charged pions and the sigma. We compute the thermodynamical quantities of interest and in particular the sound velocity squared, showing that it presents a maximum for an isospin chemical potential similar to the one reported by lattice QCD results and also with a similar height. Therefore, we attribute the origin of the peak of the sound velocity to the proper treatment of the Goldstone mode and to the non-trivial mixing of the charged pions and sigma in the isospin condensed phase.

## Key claim

The peak in the sound velocity for isospin-imbalanced strongly interacting matter originates from the proper treatment of the Goldstone mode and the non-trivial mixing between charged pions and the sigma field in the isospin condensed phase, with the seed of the peak already present at tree-level when the Goldstone mode condition coincides with the tree-level gap equation for the isospin condensate.

## Method

The authors employed the two-flavor Linear Sigma Model with quarks (LSMq) as an effective description of QCD, computing the one-loop effective potential including contributions from two light quarks, pions, and the sigma field. They enforced chiral symmetry constraints via a Ward-Takahashi identity that relates the boson self-coupling to the quark-boson coupling, and used renormalization in the MS-bar scheme with stability conditions to fix counter-terms. The condensates (chiral and isospin) were determined by minimizing the renormalized one-loop effective potential while enforcing the Goldstone mode condition from the determinant of the inverse boson propagator, from which the pressure, isospin density, energy density, and sound velocity were computed numerically.

## Key numbers

- The vacuum pion mass is taken as \(m_\pi = 140\) MeV and the pion decay constant as \(f_\pi = 92.4\) MeV.
- The quark mass range considered is \(200\ \mathrm{MeV} < m_f < 300\ \mathrm{MeV}\), which requires \(424\ \mathrm{MeV} < m_\sigma < 616\) MeV via the relation \(m_\sigma^2 = 4 m_f^2 + m_\pi^2\).
- The peak of the sound velocity squared occurs in the range \(1.9\ m_\pi < \mu_I < 2.6\ m_\pi\) for the considered quark masses, with the peak position shifting to larger \(\mu_I\) and increasing in height as \(m_f\) increases.
- The sound velocity squared exceeds the conformal limit \(c_s^2 \leq 1/3\) for a wide range of \(\mu_I\) values, consistent with lattice QCD results.
- The model has a range of validity for \(m_\pi \leq \mu_I \lesssim 4\ m_\pi\), signaled by the development of a maximum in the energy density.
- The numerical error in the pressure is less than \(0.125\%\), while the cumulative error in \(c_s^2\) is limited to \(1.638\%\) in the peak region and \(4.880\%\) in the farthest plotted region.

## Context

This work extends previous effective model studies of isospin-imbalanced matter, such as those by Andersen (2023), Ayala (2023), and Lopes (2021), which found good agreement with lattice QCD results for thermodynamic quantities but failed to reproduce the peak in the sound velocity. The authors attribute the peak to the proper inclusion of the sigma field and its mixing with charged pions, which was neglected in earlier approaches that used medium-dependent couplings (Ayala, 2023) or non-local interactions (Carlomagno, 2024). The results enable a deeper understanding of the QCD phase diagram at finite isospin density and provide a benchmark for future studies incorporating additional degrees of freedom such as rho mesons, which become relevant for \(\mu_I \gtrsim m_\rho\).
