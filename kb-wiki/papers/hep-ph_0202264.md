# Double Beta Decay

> The motivation, present status, and future plans of the search for the neutrinoless double beta decay are reviewed. It is argued that, motivated by the recent observations of neutrino oscillations, there is a reasonable hope that neutrinoless double beta decay corresponding to the neutrino mass scale suggested by oscillations, <m_ν> of about 50 meV, actually exists. The challenges to achieve the sensitivity corresponding to this mass scale, and plans to overcome them, are described.

# Double Beta Decay

## Key claim

The authors argue that, motivated by recent neutrino oscillation observations indicating at least one neutrino has a mass greater than approximately 50 meV, there is a reasonable hope that neutrinoless double beta decay corresponding to that mass scale actually exists; however, they caution that if a non-zero neutrino mass of about 100 meV is indicated by an upcoming germanium experiment, the nuclear matrix element uncertainty of a factor of 2-3 would dominate the total uncertainty, compromising physics conclusions regarding the mass hierarchy at small minimum neutrino masses.

## Method

The paper reviews the motivation, present status, and future plans for neutrinoless double beta decay searches, surveying past experiments including the Heidelberg-Moscow and IGEX germanium experiments, the MIBETA bolometer experiment, the Gotthard xenon time projection chamber, the NEMO-2 tracking experiment, and scintillating crystal experiments. The authors describe the experimental criteria for sensitivity to the effective neutrino mass, including the scaling laws for background-limited and zero-background regimes, and discuss the various sources of background from natural radioactivity, cosmogenic and induced activities, artificial activity, and two-neutrino double beta decay itself.

## Key numbers

- The neutrino mass scale suggested by oscillation experiments is approximately 50 meV, representing the minimum mass of at least one neutrino.
- For a hypothetical upcoming germanium experiment indicating a neutrino mass of about 100 meV with an anticipated experimental uncertainty of approximately ±25 meV, the matrix element uncertainty of a factor of 2-3 would yield a possible range of effective neutrino mass from 33 meV to 300 meV.
- The best limit on neutrinoless double beta decay half-life for germanium-76 is >1.9×10^25 years (Heidelberg-Moscow) and >1.6×10^25 years (IGEX), corresponding to effective neutrino mass limits of <0.35 eV and <0.33-1.35 eV respectively.
- The MIBETA experiment used 20 TeO2 crystals totaling 6.8 kg, achieving an array-averaged energy resolution of approximately 8 keV FWHM (0.3%) at the 2.529 MeV endpoint energy.
- The Gotthard xenon experiment used 3.3 kg of 62.5% enriched xenon-136 with an energy resolution of approximately 165 keV FWHM (6.6%) at the 2.481 MeV endpoint.
- The Heidelberg-Moscow and IGEX experiments quote background levels in the neutrinoless double beta decay region of approximately 0.20 counts/(keV·kg·yr) before pulse shape discrimination and approximately 0.06 counts/(keV·kg·yr) after.
- The CUORE proposal plans 1000 TeO2 crystals of 750 g each, with expected energy resolution of about 5 keV FWHM (approximately 0.2%) and a projected background of approximately 0.01 counts/(keV·kg·yr).
- The two-neutrino double beta decay half-life for molybdenum-100 transition to the excited 0+ state at 1.13 MeV in ruthenium-100 is (6.8 ± 1.2) × 10^20 years.
- The best constraint on the Majoron-neutrino coupling constant comes from tellurium-128 with a limit of ≤ 3×10^-5.

## Context

This review extends the earlier survey of double beta decay by Moe and Vogel (1994) and summarizes the progress made in the subsequent eight years, particularly in light of the neutrino oscillation results that established non-zero neutrino masses. The paper enables the design of next-generation experiments such as CUORE, EXO, GENIUS, Majorana, and MOON, which aim to reach the 50 meV sensitivity region, and highlights the critical need for improved nuclear matrix element calculations to interpret any future positive signal. The authors note that the Heidelberg-Moscow collaboration claimed evidence for neutrinoless double beta decay in germanium-76 with a half-life of (0.8-18.3)×10^25 years, but state that this claim requires extensive substantiation and review.
