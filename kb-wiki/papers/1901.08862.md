# Scaling Properties of the Lipkin Model at the Critical Point

> The influence of Franco Iachello in Physics during the last 50 years and, in particular, in the use of algebraic methods applied to very different physical problems has been broad, extense and have permeated most branches of Physics, from Nuclear and Molecular to Particle and Condensed Matter physics. Apart of many other contributions, at the beginning of the 2000's he introduced the concept of critical point symmetry and triggered the study of the many faces of quantum phase transitions in nuclei and other mesoscopic systems. In this contribution, we present the analysis of the scaling properties of the Lipkin model spectrum at the phase transition point and we focus on the differences between first and second order quantum phase transitions. Moreover, we explain how the obtained results can be also of application for the interacting boson model.

## Key claim

The universal finite-size scaling power law (observables scaling as \(N^\theta\) at the critical point) is valid only for second-order quantum phase transitions, not for first-order ones; for first-order transitions in the Lipkin model the gap follows an exponential decay with system size, while in the Interacting Boson Model the gap approaches a finite value, contradicting claims of universal scaling for first-order QPTs.

## Method

The authors study the Lipkin model in its boson realization using a Consistent-Q Hamiltonian (CQF) with two control parameters \(x\) and \(y\). They construct the mean-field energy surface via a boson condensate to locate the critical points in the thermodynamic limit, then perform direct numerical diagonalization for finite particle numbers \(N\) to compute excitation spectra. The scaling behavior of the energy gap (excitation energy of the first excited state) at the critical point is analyzed for second-order (\(y=0\)) and first-order (\(y\neq0\)) quantum phase transitions, comparing results with the Interacting Boson Model (IBM).

## Key numbers

- For second-order QPT (\(y=0\)), the critical point is at \(x_c = 4/5\).
- For first-order QPT (\(y=1/\sqrt{2}\)), the critical point is at \(x_c = 9/11\).
- The coexistence region around the first-order critical point for \(y=1/\sqrt{2}\) has width \(\Delta x \approx 5 \times 10^{-5}\) for the Lipkin model.
- At the first-order critical point in the Lipkin model, the gap follows an exponential law \(\Delta \sim e^{-A N}\) (fitted in the inset for several \(y\) values).
- For the IBM at the first-order critical point \(x_c=9/11\), the gap approaches a finite asymptotic value \(\Delta = 3/11\) in the infinite-\(N\) limit.
- For second-order QPTs, the entire spectrum at the critical point scales as \(N^{-1/3}\), a result previously shown by Rowe (2004) and Dusu et al. (2005b) to be universal for two-level boson models.

## Context

This work extends the finite-size scaling analysis of quantum phase transitions from the Lipkin model to clarify differences between first- and second-order transitions, building on the foundational studies of Gilmore and Feng (1977) and the introduction of critical point symmetries by Iachello (early 2000s). The results contradict the claim by Will et al. (2010) of universal scaling in first-order QPTs, showing instead that first-order behavior is model-dependent (exponential in Lipkin, finite in IBM). The findings enable more precise understanding of finite-size effects in mesoscopic systems like nuclei, where the Interacting Boson Model is widely applied, and connect to the concept of Excited State Quantum Phase Transitions introduced by Caprio, Cejnar, and Iachello (2008).
