# An Open-Source Neutrino Radiation Hydrodynamics Code for Core-Collapse Supernovae

> We present an open-source update to the spherically-symmetric, general-relativistic hydrodynamics, core-collapse supernova (CCSN) code GR1D (O'Connor & Ott, 2010, CQG, 27, 114103). The source code is available at http://www.GR1Dcode.org. We extend its capabilities to include a general relativistic treatment of neutrino transport based on the moment formalisms of Shibata et al., 2011, PTP, 125, 1255 and Cardall et al., 2013, PRD, 87 103004. We pay special attention to implementing and testing numerical methods and approximations that lessen the computational demand of the transport scheme by removing the need to invert large matrices. This is especially important for the implementation and development of moment-like transport methods in two and three dimensions. A critical component of neutrino transport calculations are the neutrino-matter interaction coefficients that describe the production, absorption, scattering, and annihilation of neutrinos. In this article we also describe our open-source, neutrino interaction library NuLib (available at http://www.nulib.org). We believe that an open-source approach to describing these interactions is one of the major steps needed to progress towards robust models of CCSNe and robust predictions of the neutrino signal. We show, via comparisons to full Boltzmann neutrino transport simulations of CCSNe, that our neutrino transport code performs remarkably well. Furthermore, we show that the methods and approximations we employ to increase efficiency do not decrease the fidelity of our results. We also test the ability of our general relativistic transport code to model failed CCSN by evolving a 40 solar-mass progenitor to the onset of collapse to a black hole.

## Key claim

The paper presents an open-source update to the spherically-symmetric, general-relativistic hydrodynamics core-collapse supernova code GR1D that includes a general relativistic treatment of neutrino transport based on moment formalisms, with the claim that the numerical methods and approximations that remove the need to invert large matrices do not decrease the fidelity of results when compared to full Boltzmann neutrino transport simulations.

## Method

The authors extended the existing GR1D code to evolve the first two moments (energy density and momentum density) of the neutrino distribution function for each neutrino species and energy group, closing the system via an analytic Minerbo closure. They implemented an implicit-explicit time integration scheme where spatial flux terms and energy-group coupling terms are treated explicitly while geometric and matter interaction source terms are treated implicitly, avoiding large matrix inversions. The neutrino-matter interaction coefficients are provided by the accompanying open-source library NuLib.

## Key numbers

- The code was tested by evolving a 40 solar-mass progenitor to the onset of collapse to a black hole, demonstrating the ability to model failed core-collapse supernovae.
- The transition from thermal equilibrium and isotropic values occurs in the protoneutron star core at radii less than approximately 30 km, with neutrinos becoming essentially decoupled (optical depth approximately 0.1) in the gain region at radii around 100 km.
- The central density threshold for switching from TVD minmod reconstruction to piecewise parabolic method (PPM) during collapse is set at 10^12 g cm^-3.
- The Minerbo closure uses the flux factor f = (H^2/J^2)^(1/2) where the interpolation factor chi equals 1/3 in the optically thick limit and approaches 1 in the free streaming limit.

## Context

This work extends the spherically-symmetric, general-relativistic hydrodynamics code GR1D originally presented by O'Connor and Ott (2010) by adding neutrino transport based on the moment formalisms of Shibata et al. (2011) and Cardall et al. (2013). The open-source approach with the NuLib interaction library aims to enable robust models of core-collapse supernovae and predictions of the neutrino signal, with the numerical methods designed to scale efficiently to two and three dimensions for future implementations.
