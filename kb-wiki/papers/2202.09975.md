# Prospects for Distinguishing Supernova Models Using a Future Neutrino Signal

> The next Galactic core-collapse supernova (SN) should yield a large number of observed neutrinos. Using Bayesian techniques, we show that with an SN at a known distance up to 25 kpc, the neutrino events in a water Cherenkov detector similar to Super-Kamiokande (SK) could be used to distinguish between seven one-dimensional neutrino emission models assuming no flavor oscillations or the standard Mikheyev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein effect. Some of these models could still be differentiated with an SN at a known distance of 50 kpc. We also consider just the relative distributions of neutrino energy and arrival time predicted by the models and find that a detector like SK meets the requirement to distinguish between these distributions with an SN at an unknown distance up to $\sim 10$ kpc.

## Key claim

Using Bayesian techniques, the authors show that with a supernova at a known distance up to 25 kpc, neutrino events in a water Cherenkov detector similar to Super-Kamiokande (SK) could distinguish between seven one-dimensional neutrino emission models, assuming no flavor oscillations or the standard Mikheyev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein effect; some models could still be differentiated at a known distance of 50 kpc, and with an unknown distance up to approximately 10 kpc the relative distributions of neutrino energy and arrival time alone suffice for discrimination.

## Method

The authors employed seven one-dimensional core-collapse supernova neutrino emission models from the Garching group, differing in progenitor mass (9.6, 18.6, 20, and 27 solar masses) and nuclear equation of state (LS220 or SFHo). They simulated neutrino signals via the inverse beta decay channel in a Super-Kamiokande-like detector (32 kton fiducial mass, 0.75 detection efficiency, 7.5 MeV energy threshold) and an ideal detector, then used Bayesian evidence and Bayes factors to quantify distinguishability between model pairs for supernovae at known and unknown distances, considering no oscillations, normal hierarchy, and inverted hierarchy cases.

## Key numbers

- All seven models can be distinguished from each other by an SK-like detector with a supernova at a known distance up to at least 25 kpc (Tables show all model pairs satisfy the greater than 95% CL criterion at 25 kpc).
- Some models can still be distinguished at the greater than 95% CL with an SK-like detector at a known distance of 50 kpc (e.g., z9.6-LS220(NO) versus s18.6-SFHo(NO) gives mean ln Bayes factor 15.96 with standard deviation 4.58).
- With an unknown distance, an SK-like detector can distinguish between the relative energy and time distributions for a supernova up to approximately 10 kpc.
- Expected total IBD event numbers for the SK-like detector at 10 kpc range from 2502.54 (z9.6-LS220, no oscillations) to 7152.88 (s20-SFHo, no oscillations).
- For the ideal detector at 10 kpc, expected event numbers range from 3553.15 (z9.6-LS220, no oscillations) to 9938.71 (s20-SFHo, no oscillations).

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian comparison of supernova neutrino models previously applied to SN 1987A data by Olsen and Qian (2021), which was limited by sparse data. It complements studies by the Hyper-Kamiokande collaboration (2021) and Migenda (2019) that focused on five and four models respectively using Hyper-Kamiokande, by instead examining seven Garching group models not considered in those works, covering both the accretion and proto-neutron star cooling phases over 9 seconds of emission. The results enable future Galactic supernova observations to discriminate between different progenitor masses and nuclear equations of state, and potentially to identify the neutrino mass hierarchy if the underlying emission model is known.
