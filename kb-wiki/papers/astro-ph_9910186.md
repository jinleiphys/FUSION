# Lifetimes of 26Al and 34Cl in an astrophysical plasma

> We study here the onset of thermal equilibrium affecting the lifetimes of 26Al and 34Cl nuclei within a hot astrophysical photon gas. The 26Al isotope is of prime interest for gamma ray astronomy with the observation of its delayed (t_1\2=0.74 My) 1.809MeV gamma-ray line. Its nucleosynthesis is complicated by the presence of a short lived (t_1\2=6.34s) spin isomer. A similar configuration is found in 34Cl where the decay of its isomer (34mCl, t_1\2=32m) is followed by delayed gamma-ray emission with characteristic energies. The lifetimes of such nuclei are reduced at high temperature by the thermal population of shorter lived levels. However, thermal equilibrium within 26Al and 34Cl levels is delayed by the presence of the isomer. We study here the transition to thermal equilibrium where branching ratios for radiative transitions are needed in order to calculate lifetimes. Since some of these very small branching ratios are not known experimentally, we use results of shell model calculations.

## Key claim

The temperature threshold (0.4 GK) below which 26Al ground state and isomer must be treated as separate nuclides is unchanged from the earlier estimate of Ward and Fowler (1980), but the effective lifetime of 26Al below that temperature is shorter by up to four orders of magnitude than previously thought, because the dominant decay channel proceeds through internal transition to the isomer followed by its beta decay, not through beta decay from the 3+ level as Ward and Fowler had assumed.

## Method

The authors solved numerically the coupled differential equations governing the populations of the ground state, isomer, and intermediate excited levels of 26Al and 34Cl in a hot photon gas, treating each level as a separate "isotope" connected by gamma transitions and beta decays. For radiative transition probabilities not known experimentally (particularly the very small branching ratios linking the isomer and ground-state cascades), they used shell-model calculations with the USD interaction in the full sd shell, and validated the calculations by comparing theoretical and experimental gamma widths in 26Al, 34Cl, and neighboring odd-A nuclei.

## Key numbers

- 26Al ground state half-life: 0.74 My; 26Al isomer half-life: 6.34 s
- 34Cl ground state half-life: 1.53 s; 34Cl isomer half-life: 32 min; isomer lies 146 keV above ground state
- 34Cl isomer decays to 34S with delayed gamma lines at 2.128 MeV (43% branching ratio) and 3.304 MeV (12% branching ratio)
- The 3+ to ground state transition in 26Al (a to o) is 7.7 Weisskopf units; the M3 transition from the 3+ level to the isomer is calculated by shell model to be approximately 1 Moszkowski unit
- Most calculated radiative widths lie within a factor of three of experimental values
- 26Al equilibrium decay rate: lambda_eq = 9.9 x 10^-3 exp(-2.651/T_9)
- 26Al off-equilibrium transition probability: lambda_off = 2.97 x 10^-14 + 4.07 x 10^-2 exp(-4.839/T_9) + 2.10 x 10^8 exp(-12.28/T_9)
- 34Cl equilibrium decay rate: lambda_eq = (0.454 + 1.40 x 10^-3 exp(-1.699/T_9)) / (1 + 7 exp(-1.699/T_9))
- 34Cl off-equilibrium decay rate: lambda_off = 3.61 x 10^-4 + 8.77 x 10^6 exp(-3.651/T_9)
- Equilibrium between 26Al ground state and isomer is reached above approximately 0.4 GK; for 34Cl, above approximately 0.22 GK

## Context

This work revisits the treatment of thermal equilibrium in 26Al and 34Cl originally formulated by Ward and Fowler (1980), who had estimated unknown branching ratios using single Weisskopf or Moszkowski units. The authors replace those estimates with shell-model calculations and find that the dominant decay path for 26Al below equilibrium follows the channel identified by Vogelaar (1989) rather than the one assumed by Ward and Fowler. The results provide analytical formulas for effective lifetimes under astrophysical conditions (densities below 10^5 g/cm^3) that can be used in nucleosynthesis calculations for novae, AGB stars, and supernovae; for 34Cl, the short lifetime at nova temperatures (up to approximately 0.35 GK) suggests it is efficiently destroyed by induced transitions to the short-lived ground state, limiting its potential as a source of observable gamma-ray lines.
