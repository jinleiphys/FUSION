# Extracting three-body breakup observables from CDCC calculations with core excitations

> Core-excitation effects in the scattering of two-body halo nuclei have been investigated in previous works. In particular, these effects have been found to affect in a significant way the breakup cross sections of neutron-halo nuclei with a deformed core. To account for these effects, appropriate extensions of the continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) method have been recently proposed. We aim to extend these studies to the case of breakup reactions measured under complete kinematics or semi-inclusive reactions in which only the angular or energy distribution of one of the outgoing fragments is measured. We use the standard CDCC method as well as its extended version with core excitations (XCDCC), assuming a pseudo-state basis for describing the projectile states. Two- and three-body observables are computed by projecting the discrete two-body breakup amplitudes, obtained within these reaction frameworks, onto two-body scattering states with definite relative momentum of the outgoing fragments and a definite state of the core nucleus. The presented method provides a tool to compute double and triple differential cross sections for outgoing fragments following the breakup of a two-body projectile, and might be useful to analyze breakup reactions with other deformed weakly-bound nuclei, for which core excitations are expected to play a role. We have found that, while dynamical core excitations are important for the proton target at intermediate energies, they are very small for the Zn target at energies around the Coulomb barrier.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method to extract fully exclusive three-body breakup observables (double and triple differential cross sections) from CDCC and XCDCC calculations, enabling comparison with experimental data measured under complete kinematics or semi-inclusive conditions; the importance of dynamical core excitations depends strongly on the target and energy regime, being significant for proton targets at intermediate energies but very small for heavy targets near the Coulomb barrier.

## Method

The authors use the standard Continuum-Discretized Coupled-Channels (CDCC) method and its extended version with core excitations (XCDCC), employing a pseudo-state basis obtained by diagonalizing the projectile Hamiltonian in a Transformed Harmonic Oscillator (THO) basis. Two- and three-body breakup observables are computed by projecting the discrete two-body breakup amplitudes from the CDCC/XCDCC calculations onto two-body scattering states with definite relative momentum of the outgoing fragments and a definite state of the core nucleus, using the overlap integrals between the scattering states and pseudo-state wave functions.

## Key numbers

* For the $^{11}$Be + $p$ reaction at 63.7 MeV/nucleon, the main contributions to the total energy distribution arise from the $5/2^+_1$ resonance at $E_x$ = 1.78 MeV and the $3/2^+_1$ resonance at $E_x$ = 3.40 MeV.
* The $^{11}$Be structure model uses a permanent quadrupole deformation $\beta_2$ = 0.67 (deformation length $\delta_2$ = 1.664 fm) for the $^{10}$Be core, with only the ground state ($0^+$) and first excited state ($2^+$, $E_x$ = 3.368 MeV) included.
* The XCDCC coupled equations were integrated up to 100 fm and for total angular momenta $J_T \leq 65$ for the $^{11}$Be + $p$ system.
* The THO basis used $N = 12$ radial functions with valence-core orbital angular momenta $\ell \leq 5$ and continuum states up to $J = 5/2$ (both parities).

## Context

This work extends the CDCC framework for fivefold fully exclusive cross sections derived by Tostevin et al. (2001) to the XCDCC formalism incorporating core excitations, building on previous studies of core-excitation effects in breakup reactions of $^{11}$Be using DWBA (Crespo et al., 2011; Moro et al., 2012) and the XCDCC method (de Diego et al., 2014). The formalism enables analysis of breakup reactions with deformed weakly-bound nuclei where core excitations are expected to play a role, and can be used to compute observables for specific states of the core nucleus, which is useful for experiments using gamma-ray coincidences.
