# Mass radius and mechanical properties of the proton via strange $ϕ$ meson photoproduction

> In this work, the cross sections of $ϕ$ photoproduction are systematically investigated with the two gluon exchange model and the pomeron model. The results obtained show that the theoretical values of the two models agree well with the experimental data. Since the mass radius and mechanical properties of the proton are encoded in the scalar gravitational form factor of the momentum energy tensor, based on the differential cross sections of $ϕ$ photoproduction at near-threshold predicted by the two gluon exchange model, the average mass radius of the proton is derived as $\sqrt{\left\langle r^{2}\right\rangle_\mathrm{m} }=0.78 \pm 0.06$ fm. As a comparison, we directly extract the proton mass radius from the experimental data of $ϕ$ photoproduction to obtain an average value of $0.80\pm0.05$ fm, which is very close to the result given by the two gluon exchange model. Taking a similar approach, we extracted the average value of $m_{D}$ to obtain the distribution of the pressure and shear force contributed within the proton, and compared the results with other groups. The results of this paper may provide necessary theoretical information for the subsequent in-depth study of the internal structure and properties of proton.

## Key claim

The proton mass radius extracted from the two-gluon-exchange model prediction of near-threshold phi meson photoproduction differential cross sections is \(0.78 \pm 0.06\) fm, which is consistent with the value of \(0.80 \pm 0.05\) fm obtained by direct extraction from experimental data, but the authors note that the pomeron model yields a significantly larger value of \(1.02 \pm 0.01\) fm which is not considered reliable for this purpose.

## Method

The authors used the two-gluon-exchange model and the pomeron model to calculate total and differential cross sections for \(\gamma p \rightarrow \phi p\) near threshold, fitting the gluon distribution function parameters \(xg(x,m_\phi^2) = A_0 x^{A_1} (1-x)^{A_2}\) and the slope \(b_0\) to experimental data from CLAS, LEPS, and other collaborations. The scalar gravitational form factor was then extracted from the predicted differential cross sections using the relation \(d\sigma/dt \propto G^2(t)\) with a dipole form \(G(t)=M/(1-t/m_r^2)^2\), from which the mass radius \(\sqrt{\langle r^2 \rangle_m} = \sqrt{12}/m_r\) was derived, and the D-term was extracted using a tripole parametrization \(D(t)=D(0)/(1-t/m_D^2)^3\) to obtain pressure and shear force distributions.

## Key numbers

- Proton mass radius from two-gluon-exchange model: \(\sqrt{\langle r^2 \rangle_m} = 0.78 \pm 0.06\) fm
- Proton mass radius from direct extraction of experimental data (CLAS and LEPS, \(W \in [2.02, 2.29]\) GeV): \(0.80 \pm 0.05\) fm
- Proton mass radius from pomeron model: \(1.02 \pm 0.01\) fm (not considered reliable)
- Fitted gluon distribution function: \(xg(x, m_\phi^2) = 0.36 x^{-0.055} (1-x)^{0.12}\) with slope \(b_0 = 3.60 \pm 0.04\) GeV\(^{-2}\) and \(\chi^2/\)d.o.f. = 2.87
- Average \(\sqrt{\langle m_D^2 \rangle}\) from two-gluon-exchange model: \(1.15 \pm 0.05\) GeV
- Average \(\sqrt{\langle m_D^2 \rangle}\) from CLAS and LEPS data: \(1.09 \pm 0.07\) GeV

## Context

This work extends the approach of Kharzeev (2021), who first extracted the proton mass radius from \(J/\psi\) photoproduction obtaining \(0.55 \pm 0.03\) fm, and Wang (2021), who obtained \(0.67 \pm 0.03\) fm from combined vector meson data. The authors note that heavy charmonium extraction is strongly affected by momentum transfer \(|t|_{\text{min}}\) near threshold, making the lighter phi meson a more stable probe. The results are compared with Lattice QCD calculations by Pefkou (2021) giving \(0.747 \pm 0.033\) fm, Holographic QCD by Mamo (2022) giving \(0.682\) fm, and other extractions from charmonium and rho photoproduction, with the overall mass radius range across all methods being \(r_m \in [0.52, 0.94]\) fm. The mechanical property results are compared with DVCS extractions by Burkert (2018, 2021), Lattice QCD, Holographic QCD, and the light-front quark-diquark model.
