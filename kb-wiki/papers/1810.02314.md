# Holographic Bjorken Flow at Large-D

> We use gauge/gravity duality to study the dynamics of strongly coupled gauge theories undergoing boost invariant expansion in an arbitrary number of space-time dimensions (D). By keeping the scale of the late-time energy density fixed, we explore the infinite-D limit and study the first few corrections to this expansion. In agreement with other studies, we find that the large-D dynamics are controlled by hydrodynamics and we use our computation to constrain the leading large-D dependence of a certain combination of transport coefficients up to 6-th order in gradients. Going beyond late time physics, we discuss how non-hydrodynamic modes appear in the large-D expansion in the form of a trans-series in D, identical to the non-perturbative contributions to the gradient expansion. We discuss the consequence of this trans-series in the non-convergence of the large-D expansion.

## Key claim

The authors use gauge/gravity duality to study strongly coupled gauge theories undergoing boost-invariant (Bjorken) expansion in an arbitrary number of spacetime dimensions \(D\), finding that the large-\(D\) dynamics are controlled by hydrodynamics and that non-hydrodynamic modes appear as a trans-series in \(D\) identical to non-perturbative contributions to the gradient expansion; the abstract glosses over the fact that the analysis is performed by keeping the late-time energy density scale fixed and that the large-\(D\) expansion is shown to be asymptotic rather than convergent.

## Method

The authors solve Einstein’s equations in asymptotically anti-de Sitter (AdS) spacetime in \(D = n+1\) dimensions using an Eddington-Finkelstein-like metric ansatz tailored to boost-invariant flow, expanding metric functions in inverse powers of \(n\) (the number of boundary spacetime dimensions) in the near-horizon region. They match these near-horizon solutions to the near-boundary region to extract the dual field theory stress tensor, and compare the results to a late-time fluid-gravity gradient expansion in arbitrary \(D\) and to second-order hydrodynamic equations.

## Key numbers

* The leading-order near-horizon solution for the blackening function is \(A_0 = 1 - \frac{1}{R} \bar{a}_0\) with \(\bar{a}_0 = \frac{\alpha_0}{1 + \tau \Lambda}\) and \(\alpha_0 = 1\) fixed by the definition of the scale \(\Lambda\).
* The first-order correction to the blackening function is \(A_1 = -\frac{\bar{a}_1(\tau)}{R} - \frac{1}{(1+\tau\Lambda)^2} \frac{\log R}{R}\) with \(\bar{a}_1 = -\frac{\log(1+\tau\Lambda)}{1+\tau\Lambda}\).
* The second-order correction to the blackening function includes a term \(\bar{a}_2(\tau) = \frac{1}{2(1+\tau\Lambda)^3} \left( (1+\tau\Lambda)^2 (\log(1+\tau\Lambda))^2 - 2(2+4\tau\Lambda + (\tau\Lambda)^2) \log(1+\tau\Lambda) \right)\).
* The third-order correction to the blackening function includes \(\bar{a}_3(\tau)\) given by a lengthy expression involving \(\pi^2\), \(\log^3\), \(\log^2\), and \(\log\) terms, e.g., \(-\frac{18\Lambda\tau + \pi^2(3\Lambda\tau -1)+6}{9(\Lambda\tau+1)^4}\).
* The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio is the universal value \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\).
* The second-order transport coefficient \(\lambda_1 = \frac{n}{8\pi} \frac{\eta}{T}\).
* The first-order hydrodynamic correction to the energy density in the large-\(D\) expansion is \(\bar{c}^{\rm hyd_2}_1 = \frac{1}{2\Lambda^2\tau^2} - \frac{2}{\Lambda\tau} - \log(\Lambda\tau)\).

## Context

This work extends the large-\(D\) expansion technique, previously applied to holographic problems with parametrically small energy fluxes (e.g., Emparan (2013), Herzog (2016), Rozali (2017)), to the purely relativistic setup of Bjorken flow, which is phenomenologically important for heavy-ion collisions. It builds on the holographic description of Bjorken flow first given by Janik (2005) and on studies of the non-convergence of the hydrodynamic gradient expansion by Heller (2013). The results enable a better understanding of the asymptotic nature of the large-\(D\) expansion and its connection to non-hydrodynamic quasi-normal modes and hydrodynamic attractors.
