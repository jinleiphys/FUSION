# A relativistic calculation of the deuteron threshold electrodisintegration at backward angles

> The threshold electrodisintegration of the deuteron at backward angles is studied with a relativistic Hamiltonian, including a relativistic one-pion-exchange potential (OPEP) with off-shell terms as predicted by pseudovector coupling of pions to nucleons. The bound and scattering states are obtained in the center-of-mass frame, and then boosted from it to the Breit frame, where the evaluation of the relevant matrix elements of the electromagnetic current operator is carried out. The latter includes, in addition to one-body, also two-body terms due to pion exchange, as obtained, consistently with the OPEP, in pseudovector pion-nucleon coupling theory. The full Lorentz structure of these currents is retained. In order to estimate the magnitude of the relativistic effects we perform, for comparison, the calculation with a non-relativistic phase-equivalent Hamiltonian and the standard non-relativistic expressions for the one-body and two-body pion-exchange currents. Our results for the electrodisintegration cross section show that, in the calculations using one-body currents, relativistic corrections become significant ({\it i.e.}, larger than 10%) only at high momentum transfer $Q$ ($Q^2 \simeq 40$fm$^{-2}$ and beyond). However, the inclusion of two-body currents makes the relativistic predictions considerably smaller than the corresponding non-relativistic results in the $Q^2$ region (18--40) fm$^{-2}$. The calculations based on the relativistic model also confirm the inadequacy, already established in a non-relativistic context, of the present electromagnetic current model to reproduce accurately the experimental data at intermediate values of momentum transfers.

## Key claim

Relativistic corrections to the deuteron threshold electrodisintegration cross section become significant (larger than 10%) only at momentum transfers above Q² ≈ 40 fm⁻² when using one-body currents alone, but when two-body pion-exchange currents are included, the relativistic predictions become considerably smaller than non-relativistic results in the Q² region 18–40 fm⁻², and the relativistic model confirms the inadequacy of the present electromagnetic current model to reproduce experimental data at intermediate momentum transfers.

## Method

The authors solved a relativistic Hamiltonian (including a relativistic one-pion-exchange potential with off-shell terms from pseudovector coupling) for deuteron bound and np scattering states in the center-of-mass frame, then boosted these states to the Breit frame. They evaluated matrix elements of the electromagnetic current operator (including one-body and two-body pion-exchange currents with full Lorentz structure retained) between the boosted states, and compared results to a non-relativistic calculation using a phase-equivalent Hamiltonian based on the Argonne v18 potential and standard non-relativistic current expressions.

## Key numbers

- Relativistic corrections with one-body currents exceed 10% only at Q² ≥ 40 fm⁻²
- In the Q² region 18–40 fm⁻², relativistic predictions including two-body currents are considerably smaller than non-relativistic results
- Deuteron D-state probability: 6.26% (relativistic) vs 5.76% (non-relativistic)
- Deuteron D- to S-state ratio: 0.0260 (relativistic) vs 0.0250 (non-relativistic)
- Deuteron quadrupole moment: 0.272 fm² (relativistic) vs 0.270 fm² (non-relativistic)
- Pion-nucleon coupling constant: f²_πNN/4π = 0.075
- Monopole form factor cutoff: Λ_π = 1.2 GeV/c
- Calculations performed at electron scattering angle θ = 155° and np center-of-mass energy of 1.5 MeV

## Context

This work extends earlier non-relativistic calculations of deuteron threshold electrodisintegration (Leidemann 1990, Schiavilla 1991) and relativistic studies of deuteron form factors (Schiavilla 2002) to include both relativistic dynamics and two-body pion-exchange currents. The results confirm the conclusion of an earlier non-relativistic study (Schiavilla 1991) that the present electromagnetic current model is inadequate at intermediate momentum transfers, and suggest the need for additional short-range two-body currents. The work also corroborates the findings of Keister (1988) that one-body current calculations show little difference between relativistic and non-relativistic approaches at low Q².
