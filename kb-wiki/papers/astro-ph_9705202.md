# Signal for the Quark-Hadron Phase Transition in Rotating Hybrid Stars

> For the past 20 years it had been thought that the coexistence phase of the confined hadronic and quark matter phases, assumed to be a first order transition, was strictly excluded from neutron stars. This, however, was due to a seemingly innocuous idealization which has approximated away important physics. The reason is that neutron stars constitute multi-component bodies rather than single-component ones formerly (and incorrectly) used to describe the deconfinement phase transition in neutron stars. So, contrary to earlier claims, `neutron' stars may very well contain quark matter in their cores surrounded by a mixed-phase region of quark and hadronic matter. Such objects are called hybrid stars. The structure of such stars as well as an observable signature that could signal the existence of quark matter in their cores are discussed in this paper.

## Key claim

Contrary to the long-held belief that the mixed phase of hadronic and quark matter is excluded from neutron stars, the authors show that because neutron stars are multi-component bodies with two conserved charges (baryon and electric), the pressure in the mixed phase varies with density, allowing a mixed-phase region to exist in the star's interior; this means hybrid stars with quark cores surrounded by a mixed-phase region are possible.

## Method

The authors constructed three models for the equation of state of neutron star matter using relativistic mean field theory: a pure neutron star (neutrons and protons only), a hyperon star (including the full baryon octet), and a hybrid star (including a mixed phase of hadronic and quark matter). They solved Einstein's field equations self-consistently for rotating stars at various rotational frequencies, keeping baryon number constant during spin-down, and computed the moment of inertia and braking index as functions of rotational frequency.

## Key numbers

- The transition of confined baryonic matter to quark matter sets in at about twice nuclear matter density, where nuclear matter density is 140 MeV/fm³.
- Pure quark matter is obtained for densities greater than or equal to 7 times nuclear matter density (7 × 140 MeV/fm³).
- The nonrotating hybrid star mass used in the frequency-dependence calculations is approximately 1.42 solar masses.
- The nonrotating hyperon star mass used for comparison is approximately 1.40 solar masses.
- The quark matter core appears in the hybrid star for rotational frequencies below about 1250 s⁻¹ and radii below about 4.5 km.
- The Σ⁻ hyperon disappears from the hyperon star for rotational frequencies above about 4700 s⁻¹.
- The authors estimate that about 10 out of the approximately 700 known pulsars could be signaling the phase transition.
- The duration over which the braking index would be anomalous may be 1/100th of the active pulsar lifetime.

## Context

This work extends the earlier treatment of phase transitions in neutron stars by Glendenning (1991, 1997) and Hermann (1996), who first recognized that the mixed phase is not excluded when two conserved charges are considered, contradicting the single-component approximation used in all previous work over the preceding two decades. The paper enables the identification of pulsar braking index anomalies as a potential observable signature of quark matter in neutron star cores, and suggests that the Coulomb lattice structure of the mixed phase may have dramatic effects on pulsar observables including transport properties and glitch theory.
