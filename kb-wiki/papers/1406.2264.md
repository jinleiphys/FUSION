# Corrections to Eikonal Approximation for Nuclear Scattering at Medium Energies

> The upcoming Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory (NSCL) at Michigan State University has reemphasized the importance of accurate modeling of low energy nucleus-nucleus scattering. Such calculations have been simplified by using the eikonal approximation. As a high energy approximation, however, its accuracy suffers for the medium energy beams that are of current experimental interest. A prescription developed by Wallace \cite{Wallace:1971zz,Wallace:1973iu} that obtains the scattering propagator as an expansion around the eikonal propagator (Glauber approach) has the potential to extend the range of validity of the approximation to lower energies. Here we examine the properties of this expansion, and calculate the first-, second-, and third-order corrections for the scattering of a spinless particle off of a ${}^{40}$Ca nucleus, and for nuclear breakup reactions involving ${}^{11}$Be. We find that, including these corrections extends the lower bound of the range of validity of the down to energies of 40 MeV. At that energy the corrections provide as much as a 15\% correction to certain processes.

# Corrections to Eikonal Approximation for Nuclear Scattering at Medium Energies

## Key claim

The Wallace eikonal expansion, when carried to first, second, and third order, extends the lower bound of validity of the eikonal (Glauber) approximation down to beam energies of 40 MeV, where the corrections provide up to 15% corrections to certain processes; however, the expansion breaks down at lower energies due to derivative operators acting on the nuclear surface potential, and the authors find the second-order correction effective only to about 25 MeV and the third-order correction effective only to about 30 MeV for the specific potential studied.

## Method

The authors applied the Wallace eikonal expansion (Wallace 1971, 1973) to compute successive corrections to the scattering T-matrix for spinless particle scattering off a 40Ca nucleus using the Varner optical potential (Varner 1991), comparing the approximate results to exact partial-wave solutions of the radial Schrodinger equation. They then applied the same expansion to nuclear breakup reactions of the halo nucleus 11Be on targets with mass numbers from 9 to 208 at 40 MeV/nucleon, computing diffractive, neutron stripping, core stripping, and total absorption cross sections using the reaction formalism of Hencken, Bertsch, and Esbensen (1996).

## Key numbers

- The zeroth-order eikonal approximation has an error of at least 5% for proton-40Ca scattering up to 100 MeV; including correction terms reduces the error to less than 1% above about 45 MeV.
- At a beam energy of 40 MeV, the corrections to 11Be reactions can be as high as 15% for neutron stripping and diffractive scattering.
- The second-order correction is empirically effective to about 25 MeV for the Varner potential; the third-order correction is effective to about 30 MeV.
- At 100 MeV/nucleon, first-order corrections to 11Be reactions are less than 10%, and higher-order corrections are less than 1%.
- The 11Be halo nucleus binding energy used in the single-particle model is 0.503 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the eikonal approximation framework developed by Glauber (1959) and the systematic expansion method of Wallace (1971, 1973) to medium-energy nuclear scattering relevant for the upcoming Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB). The authors challenge the conclusion of Esbensen (2001) and Hansen (2003) that the eikonal approximation is accurate to within a few percent at energies as low as 20 MeV/nucleon, arguing that those studies used the eikonal approximation within a time-dependent Schrodinger equation and therefore did not actually test the eikonal approximation itself. The results enable more accurate modeling of nucleus-nucleus scattering at the medium energies (30-100 MeV) that will be used at FRIB, with the first-order corrections requiring minimal additional computational cost.
