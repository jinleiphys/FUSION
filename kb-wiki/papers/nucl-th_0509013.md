# Origin of the drastic decrease of fusion probability in superheavy mass region

> The fusion-fission process in the superheavy mass region is studied systematically by solving the time evolution of nuclear shape in three-dimensional deformation space using the Langevin equation. By analyzing the trajectory in the deformation space, we identify the critical area when the trajectory's destination is determined to be the fusion or the quasi-fission process. It is also clarified that the potential landscape around the critical area is crucial for estimating the fusion probability, and its dependence on the atomic number is presented.

# Origin of the drastic decrease of fusion probability in superheavy mass region

## Key claim
The drastic decrease in fusion probability for superheavy systems with atomic number Z > 102 originates from the instability of the composite nucleus against fragment deformation (parameter δ) in the critical area of the potential energy surface, rather than from the fission barrier along the elongation coordinate alone. This conclusion is drawn from macroscopic liquid-drop model calculations without shell effects, though the authors note that shell corrections can enhance fusion probability and modify the trend.

## Method
The authors solved the three-dimensional Langevin equation in a deformation space defined by two-center parameterization (coordinates: distance between potential centers z₀, fragment deformation δ, and mass asymmetry α) to simulate the time evolution of nuclear shape during fusion-fission reactions. They calculated mean trajectories (without the random force term) for eight compound systems from ²²⁴Th to ²⁹⁷118, all with initial mass asymmetry α=0.6 and excitation energy E*=50 MeV, using a liquid-drop model potential with hydrodynamical inertia and wall-and-window one-body dissipation. Fusion probabilities were then computed including fluctuations, both with and without shell correction energy, for the cold fusion reaction series on Pb targets.

## Key numbers
- Fusion probability decreases exponentially with increasing atomic number Z for systems with Z > 102, for both entrance channel asymmetries α=0.6 and α=0.0 at l=0.
- For Z=90, 94, and 98 systems with α=0.6, the fusion probability is almost unity (approximately 1.0).
- The critical transition in the gradient of the one-dimensional potential energy surface as a function of δ (with fixed z=0.3 and α=0.3) occurs at Z=102 (²⁵⁶No).
- Fusion hindrance in the cold fusion Pb-target reaction series becomes visible from Z₁×Z₂ > 1750, where Z₁ and Z₂ are the atomic numbers of target and projectile.
- The mean trajectory starting point is at (z, δ, α) = (1.56, 0.0, 0.6) for all systems, with the critical area located around δ ∼ 0.5.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies (Aritomo et al., 2004, 2005) and Ohta et al. (2003) on fusion in the superheavy region by explicitly identifying the critical area in deformation space where the trajectory's fate (fusion vs. quasi-fission) is determined. It contradicts the earlier critical distance model (Glas and Mosel, 1974) for Z < 70 and the extra-push model (Swiatecki, 1981; Bjørnholm and Swiatecki, 1982) for 70 < Z < 90, showing that for Z > 100 the geometrical inversion between contact point and saddle point (Blocki et al., 1986; Nix and Sierk, 1977) requires explicit treatment of fragment deformation. The results enable systematic prediction of fusion probabilities for superheavy element synthesis and are compared with experimental data from Oganessian (2000) on Pb-target reactions. The authors note that the temperature dependence of shell correction energy for very deformed nuclei remains an open question for future study.
