# Reconstruction of the Dipole Amplitude in the Dipole Picture as a mathematical Inverse Problem

> We show that the inference problem of constraining the dipole amplitude with inclusive deep inelastic scattering data can be written into a discrete linear inverse problem, in an analogous manner as can be done for computed tomography. To this formulation of the problem, we apply standard inverse problems methods and algorithms to reconstruct known dipole amplitudes from simulated reduced cross section data with realistic precision. The main difference of this approach to previous works is that this implementation does not require any fit parametrization of the dipole amplitude. The freedom from parametrization also enables us for the first time to quantify the uncertainties of the inferred dipole amplitude in a novel more general framework. This mathematical approach to small-$x$ phenomenology opens a path to parametrization bias free inference of the dipole amplitude from HERA and Electron--Ion Collider data.

## Key claim

The authors show that constraining the dipole amplitude from inclusive deep inelastic scattering data can be formulated as a discrete linear inverse problem analogous to computed tomography, enabling reconstruction without any fit parametrization of the dipole amplitude. This approach is demonstrated only at leading order accuracy and for simulated data, not yet applied to experimental HERA data.

## Method

The authors rewrite the leading-order dipole picture expression for the reduced cross section as a linear integral transform of the dipole amplitude N(r,x) at fixed Bjorken-x, with the kernel given by the z-integrated squared light-cone wavefunctions of the virtual photon. They then discretize this integral transform and apply standard inverse problems methods and algorithms to reconstruct known dipole amplitudes from simulated reduced cross section data with realistic precision, without imposing any functional parametrization on the dipole amplitude.

## Key numbers

* The reduced cross section is defined as σ_r = F_2 - [y²/(1+(1-y)²)] F_L, where y = Q²/(s x) is the inelasticity
* The dipole cross section is approximated as σ_{q\bar{q}}(r,x) ≈ σ_0 N(r,x), where σ_0/2 is the effective transverse area of the target proton
* The squared light-cone wavefunctions for transverse polarization contain Bessel functions K_0 and K_1 evaluated at ε_f r, where ε_f² = z(1-z)Q² + m_f²
* The longitudinal wavefunction squared contains the factor 8 N_c α_em e_f² Q² z²(1-z)² K_0²(ε_f r) / π

## Context

This work extends the line of dipole amplitude extraction from inclusive DIS data that has traditionally relied on fitting parametrized models such as the McLerran-Venugopalan model, the Golec-Biernat-Wusthoff model, or the IPsat model (Kowalski 2003). It contrasts with recent Bayesian inference approaches (Casuga 2023, Casuga 2025) that improve uncertainty quantification but still require a parametrized form of the dipole amplitude. The approach is similar in spirit to the NNPDF collaboration's use of neural networks for parton distribution function inference (NNPDF 2020, 2021, 2022, 2024) and to the work of Munier (2001) who solved for the impact parameter dependence of the dipole amplitude from vector meson electroproduction data using analytic invertibility of the Fourier transform. This method opens a path toward parametrization-bias-free inference of the dipole amplitude from HERA and Electron-Ion Collider data.
