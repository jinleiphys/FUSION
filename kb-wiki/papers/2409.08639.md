# Resonance suppression during the hadronic stage from the FAIR to the intermediate RHIC energy regime

> The energy and centrality dependence of the kaon resonance ratio $(K^{*0}+\Bar{K}^{*0})/(K^+ + K^-)$ is explored in the RHIC-BES and CBM-FAIR energy regime. To this aim, the Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model is employed to simulate reconstructable $K^{*}$ resonances in Au+Au and p+p collisions from $\sqrt{s_{\text{NN}}}=3-39$ GeV. We obtain a good description of the resonance yields and mean transverse momenta over the whole investigated energy range. The decrease of the $K^*/K$ ratio, with increasing centrality is in line with the available experimental data. We also observe the experimenatlly measured increase in $\langle p_{\text{T}}\rangle$ with increasing centrality which is interpreted as a lower reconstruction probability of low-$p_{\text{T}}$ $K^*$ due to the $p_{\text{T}}$ dependent absorption of the decay daughter hadrons. We conclude that the observed suppression of reconstructable $K^{*}$ resonances provides a strong sign of an extended hadronic rescattering stage at all investigated energies. Its duration increases from peripheral to central reactions as expected. Following a method, suggested by the STAR experiment, the "duration" of the hadronic stage is extracted using the $K^*/K$ ratios at chemical and kinetic freeze-out. The resulting lifetimes are in good agreement with the experimental data, but much shorter than the actual lifetime of the hadronic phase in the transport simulation. This indicates that the experimental method to estimate the life time of the hadronic stage is too simplified.

## Key claim

The observed suppression of reconstructable K* resonances in central heavy-ion collisions is a strong sign of an extended hadronic rescattering stage at all investigated energies, but the experimental method used to estimate the lifetime of this stage (based on the K*/K ratio) yields values much shorter than the actual lifetime of the hadronic phase in the transport simulation, indicating that the method is too simplified.

## Method

The Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model (version 3.5) in cascade mode was used to simulate Au+Au and p+p collisions at center-of-mass energies from 3 to 39 GeV per nucleon pair. K* resonances were reconstructed by tracking the decay daughters (kaon and pion) through the simulation; a K* was counted as reconstructable only if both daughter hadrons left the reaction zone without further interaction. The resulting yields, mean transverse momenta, and K*/K ratios were compared to experimental data from STAR and other experiments, and the STAR method for extracting the hadronic stage duration from the K*/K ratio was applied to the simulation results.

## Key numbers

- The K*/K ratio decreases by 30% from peripheral to central collisions at all investigated energies (3 to 39 GeV).
- The extracted hadronic stage lifetime estimates from the K*/K method range up to approximately 2 fm/c for central collisions, similar to the vacuum lifetime of the K* resonance (tau_K* = 1/Gamma_vac).
- The mean transverse momentum of K* resonances increases with centrality at all energies, with the effect being weakest at the lowest energy (3.0 GeV) and strongest at higher energies.
- At the lowest energies (3.0 and 4.5 GeV), the extracted lifetime becomes negative when using the p+p K*/K ratio as the chemical freeze-out baseline, because the elementary p+p reaction does not allow sufficient K* production near threshold.

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical predictions of resonance suppression due to hadronic rescattering (Bleicher 2002, Knospe 2015, Ilner 2016) and comparisons with experimental observations from STAR (2004, 2008, 2022) and ALICE (2014, 2017, 2019). The study uses the UrQMD model to test the STAR method for extracting the hadronic stage lifetime from K*/K ratios, finding that the method underestimates the true lifetime of the hadronic phase in the simulation. This enables future work to develop more sophisticated approaches that account for regeneration, collisional broadening, and the fact that the method only probes the last generation of resonances.
