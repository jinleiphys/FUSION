# Effect of quark anomalous magnetic moment on neutral dense quark matter under magnetic field

> We discuss the effect of the quark anomalous magnetic moment (AMM) on the neutral dense quark matter under magnetic fields based on the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model at finite baryon density. To address its correlation with the chiral symmetry, we consider a simplified situation: the model includes the two-quark flavors under constant magnetic fields, and incorporates the effective interaction of the quark AMM linked to the spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking. We then examine the equation of state (EoS) in cases with and without magnetization for anatomizing the thermodynamic quantities. Without the magnetization, a small magnetic field stiffens the EoS, but with increasing the magnetic field, the EoS tends to soften. The stiffness of the EoS is found to be influenced by the magnetic effect on the critical chemical potential of the chiral phase transition and the quark number density at this critical point. As a result, the mass and radius of the neutral dense quark matter increase with the small magnetic field but turn to decrease as the magnetic field further increases. By including the quark AMM, the critical chemical potential is decreased and the quark number density takes a smaller value. Thus, for the stronger magnetic fields, the quark AMM suppresses the softening effect of the magnetic field on the EoS, leading to increased mass and radius compared to when the quark AMM is absent. In contrast, for the small magnetic field, the contribution of the quark AMM to the EoS is marginal. When the magnetization is taken into account, the magnetic effect on the stiffness of the EoS is overshadowed by the contribution of the magnetization, and the significance of the quark AMM also becomes invisible in the mass-radius relation.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the quark anomalous magnetic moment (AMM), modeled as proportional to the square of the chiral condensate, suppresses the magnetic-field-induced softening of the equation of state for neutral dense quark matter at strong magnetic fields, leading to increased mass and radius of quark stars compared to the case without AMM; however, this effect is only significant for magnetic fields stronger than those expected in realistic magnetars (above approximately 1.69 × 10^19 G), and becomes invisible when magnetization is included in the thermodynamic treatment.

## Method

The authors employ a two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model at zero temperature with a mean-field approximation, incorporating an effective quark AMM interaction proportional to the square of the chiral condensate. They impose beta equilibrium and electric charge neutrality conditions to describe neutral dense quark matter under constant external magnetic fields, and solve the stationary condition for the scalar mean fields to obtain the chiral condensates and thermodynamic quantities. The equation of state is evaluated both with and without the magnetization contribution, and the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation is solved to obtain the mass-radius relation.

## Key numbers

*   The ultraviolet cutoff is set to Λ = 681.38 MeV, with current quark masses m_u = m_d = 4.552 MeV and coupling G_S Λ^2 = 1.86.
*   The AMM parameter values considered are v̄_{u,d} = 1 GeV^{-3} and v̄_{u,d} = 2 GeV^{-3}, with a maximum estimated from potential stability of approximately 2 GeV^{-3}.
*   Magnetic field strengths used are eB = 1–4 GeV^2, corresponding to B = 1.69–6.76 × 10^19 G.
*   The muon mass is taken as m_μ = 105.66 MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of quark matter under magnetic fields (Menezes 2009, Chu 2014) that did not include the quark AMM, and builds on thermomagnetic studies (Kawaguchi 2022) that proposed the effective AMM form proportional to the square of the chiral condensate to reproduce the chiral crossover observed in lattice QCD. It also goes beyond a prior finite-chemical-potential study (He 2022) that did not address neutral dense quark matter or thermodynamic properties such as pressure, energy, and magnetization. The results enable future investigations of whether the quark AMM could have observable effects on magnetar structure, though the magnetic fields required are stronger than those expected in realistic magnetars (surface fields ~10^10–10^15 G, interior possibly up to ~10^18 G).
