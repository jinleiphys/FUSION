# Quark and Gluon Helicity Evolution at Small $x$: Revised and Updated

> We revisit the problem of small Bjorken-$x$ evolution of the gluon and flavor-singlet quark helicity distributions in the shock wave ($s$-channel) formalism. Earlier works on the subject in the same framework resulted in an evolution equation for the gluon field-strength $F^{12}$ and quark "axial current" ${\barψ}γ^+γ^5ψ$ operators (sandwiched between the appropriate light-cone Wilson lines) in the double-logarithmic approximation (DLA: summing powers of $α_s\,\ln^2(1/x)$ with $α_s$ the strong coupling constant). In this work, we observe that an important mixing of the above operators with another gluon operator, $\overleftarrow{D}^i\,{D}^i$, also sandwiched between the light-cone Wilson lines (with the repeated index $i=1,2$ summed over), was missing in the previous works. This operator has the physical meaning of the sub-eikonal (covariant) phase: its contribution to helicity evolution is shown to be proportional to another sub-eikonal operator, $D^i-\overleftarrow{D}^i$, which is related to the Jaffe-Manohar polarized gluon distribution. In this work we include this operator into small-$x$ helicity evolution, and construct a novel evolution mixing all three operators ($D^i-\overleftarrow{D}^i$, $F^{12}$, and ${\barψ}γ^+γ^5ψ$), generalizing the previous results. We also construct closed DLA evolution equations in the large-$N_c$ and large-$N_c\& N_f$ limits, with $N_c$ and $N_f$ the numbers of quark colors and flavors, respectively. Solving the large-$N_c$ equations numerically we obtain the following small-$x$ asymptotics of the quark and gluon helicity distributions $ΔΣ$ and $ΔG$, along with the $g_1$ structure function, \[ΔΣ(x,Q^2)\simΔG(x,Q^2)\sim g_1(x,Q^2)\sim\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^{3.66\,\sqrt{\frac{α_s\,N_c}{2π}}},\] in complete agreement with the earlier work by Bartels, Ermolaev and Ryskin.

## Key claim

The small-$x$ evolution of quark and gluon helicity distributions in the shock wave formalism was missing an important mixing with the sub-eikonal operator $\overleftarrow{D}^i D^i$ (related to the Jaffe-Manohar polarized gluon distribution); including this operator yields a new set of evolution equations whose solution reproduces the earlier Bartels-Ermolaev-Ryskin (BER) asymptotics, resolving a previous discrepancy between the two approaches.

## Method

The authors derived small-$x$ helicity evolution equations in the double-logarithmic approximation (DLA, summing powers of $\alpha_s \ln^2(1/x)$) using the $s$-channel/shock wave formalism with light-cone Wilson lines, incorporating the previously missing $\overleftarrow{D}^i D^i$ operator alongside the gluon field-strength $F^{12}$ and quark axial current $\bar\psi\gamma^+\gamma^5\psi$ operators. They constructed closed DLA evolution equations in the large-$N_c$ and large-$N_c\&N_f$ limits, then solved the large-$N_c$ equations numerically to extract the small-$x$ asymptotics.

## Key numbers

* The small-$x$ asymptotics of the quark helicity distribution $\Delta\Sigma$, gluon helicity distribution $\Delta G$, and $g_1$ structure function are all given by $(1/x)^{3.66\sqrt{\alpha_s N_c/(2\pi)}}$ in the large-$N_c$ limit, in agreement with BER.
* The current experimental values of proton spin carried by quarks are $S_q(Q^2=10\text{ GeV}^2) \approx 0.15\div 0.20$ for $x\in[0.001,1]$, and by gluons $S_G(Q^2=10\text{ GeV}^2) \approx 0.13\div 0.26$ for $x\in[0.05,1]$.
* The evolution equations resum powers of $\alpha_s \ln^2(1/x)$ (double-logarithmic approximation), with $\alpha_s$ the strong coupling constant and $N_c$ the number of quark colors.

## Context

This work extends the earlier shock-wave formalism for helicity evolution by Kovchegov et al. (2015-2020) by identifying and including the $\overleftarrow{D}^i D^i$ operator that was missing in those previous calculations. The resulting asymptotics now agree completely with the earlier work by Bartels, Ermolaev and Ryskin (1996) who used infrared evolution equations. This enables future calculations of helicity distributions at small $x$ for the Electron-Ion Collider and resolves a long-standing discrepancy between the two theoretical approaches.
