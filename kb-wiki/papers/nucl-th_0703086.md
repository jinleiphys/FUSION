# Predictions of Alpha Decay Half lives of Heavy and Superheavy Elements

> Theoretical estimates for the lifetimes of several isotopes of heavy elements with Z=102-120 are presented by calculating the quantum mechanical tunneling probability in a WKB framework and using microscopic nucleus-nucleus potential obtained by folding the densities of interacting nuclei with the DDM3Y effective nuclear interaction. The $α$-decay half lives calculated in this formalism using the experimental $Q$-values are in good agreement over a wide range of experimental data. Half lives are also calculated using $Q$-values extracted from two mass formulae. The Viola-Seaborg-Sobiczewski (VSS) estimates of $α$-decay half lives with the same $Q$-values are presented for comparison. The half life calculations are found to be quite sensitive to the choice of $Q$-values. Comparison with the experimental data delineates the inadequacies of older mass predictions in the domain of heavy and superheavy elements as compared to the newer one by Muntian-Hofmann-Patyk-Sobiczewski, and highlights necessity of a more accurate mass formula which can predict $Q$-values with even higher precision.

## Key claim

The authors present theoretical alpha-decay half-life predictions for 314 isotopes of heavy and superheavy elements (Z=102-120) using a WKB tunneling framework with DDM3Y effective interactions, but emphasize that the calculations are extremely sensitive to the choice of Q-values and that existing mass formulae, including the newer one by Muntian, Hofmann, Patyk, and Sobiczewski, still lack the precision needed for fully reliable predictions.

## Method

The alpha-decay half-lives were calculated using the WKB approximation for quantum mechanical tunneling probability through a potential barrier. The microscopic nucleus-nucleus potential was obtained by double folding the density distributions of the alpha particle and the daughter nucleus with the density-dependent DDM3Y effective nuclear interaction. Theoretical Q-values were extracted from two mass formulae: the Myers-Swiatecki (MS) model and the macroscopic-microscopic model of Muntian et al. (M). For comparison, half-lives were also computed using the Viola-Seaborg-Sobiczewski (VSS) semi-empirical formula with the same Q-values.

## Key numbers

- For the N=183 isotope chain using Q-values from Muntian et al.: $^{297}114$ has T$_{1/2}$ = 1.47 $\times$ 10$^3$ s; $^{298}115$ has T$_{1/2}$ = 6.78 $\times$ 10$^2$ s; $^{299}116$ has T$_{1/2}$ = 1.84 $\times$ 10$^{-1}$ s; $^{300}117$ has T$_{1/2}$ = 2.46 $\times$ 10$^{-2}$ s; $^{301}118$ has T$_{1/2}$ = 6.45 $\times$ 10$^{-4}$ s; $^{302}119$ has T$_{1/2}$ = 1.63 $\times$ 10$^{-4}$ s; $^{303}120$ has T$_{1/2}$ = 1.27 $\times$ 10$^{-5}$ s.
- For $^{289}114$, the theoretical half-life using the Myers-Swiatecki Q-value is approximately 700 times the experimental value, while calculations using Q-values from Muntian et al. agree well with experiment.
- For Z=102, N=162, the half-life using Q$^M_{th}$ is 3.06 $\times$ 10$^{+09}$ s, the longest among the isotopes studied.
- For $^{302}118$ and $^{293}118$, the half-lives using Q$^M_{th}$ are 2.58 $\times$ 10$^{-04}$ s and 8.93 $\times$ 10$^{-04}$ s, respectively.
- Zero-point vibration energies used were: 0.1045Q for even-even, 0.0962Q for odd Z-even N, 0.0907Q for even Z-odd N, and 0.0767Q for odd-odd parent nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Samanta et al., 2006) which examined even-Z nuclei from Z=106-118, now including both even and odd Z nuclei from Z=106-118 with updated experimental Q-values from Oganessian et al. (2006), and presenting calculations for 314 isotopes of Z=102-120. The results are compared with the Viola-Seaborg-Sobiczewski (VSS) estimates (Sobiczewski, Patyk, and Cwiok, 1989) and with predictions from the generalized liquid drop model (GLDM) of Royer (2002, 2004). The paper demonstrates that the macroscopic-microscopic mass formula of Muntian et al. (2001, 2003) provides better Q-value predictions for superheavy elements than the older Myers-Swiatecki model, but highlights the need for even more accurate mass formulae. The calculations do not indicate a pronounced island of stability around Z=120, and for Z=114-120, N=183 isotopes are found to have longer half-lives than N=184 isotopes, contrary to expectations of neutron magic number at N=184.
