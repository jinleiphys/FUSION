# Simulating Stochastic Dynamics Using Large Time Steps

> We present a novel approach to investigate the long-time stochastic dynamics of multi-dimensional classical systems, in contact with a heat-bath. When the potential energy landscape is rugged, the kinetics displays a decoupling of short and long time scales and both Molecular Dynamics (MD) or Monte Carlo (MC) simulations are generally inefficient. Using a field theoretic approach, we perform analytically the average over the short-time stochastic fluctuations. This way, we obtain an effective theory, which generates the same long-time dynamics of the original theory, but has a lower time resolution power. Such an approach is used to develop an improved version of the MC algorithm, which is particularly suitable to investigate the dynamics of rare conformational transitions. In the specific case of molecular systems at room temperature, we show that elementary integration time steps used to simulate the effective theory can be chosen a factor ~100 larger than those used in the original theory. Our results are illustrated and tested on a simple system, characterized by a rugged energy landscape.

## Key claim

The authors present a field-theoretic renormalization group approach to analytically integrate out short-time stochastic fluctuations from the Langevin dynamics of classical systems, producing an effective theory that can be simulated with time steps approximately 100 times larger than those required by the original theory, but only when there is a clear separation between fast and slow time scales in the system's dynamics.

## Method

The authors start from the path integral representation of the overdamped Langevin equation and map it to an imaginary-time Schrödinger equation with an effective quantum Hamiltonian. They introduce a frequency cutoff Ω inversely proportional to the simulation time step Δt, split the Fourier modes into fast (above bΩ) and slow (below bΩ) components, and perform a perturbative integration over the fast modes using Feynman diagram techniques. The resulting effective action for the slow modes contains local corrections to the potential and diffusion coefficient organized by a "degree of slowness" L, which quantifies the power of 1/(bΩ) in each diagram. The effective theory is then simulated using a diffusion Monte Carlo algorithm with larger time steps.

## Key numbers

- Elementary integration time steps for the effective theory can be chosen a factor ~100 larger than those used in the original theory, for molecular systems at room temperature.
- The acceleration term m\ddot{x} in the full Langevin equation is damped at time scales of order 10^{-13} s, which is much smaller than the time scale of local conformational changes in molecular systems.
- The equal-time propagator sum over hard modes in the continuum limit yields a factor (1/(2p-1)π) × (1-b^{2p-1})/(bΩ)^{2p-1} for a generic even power 2p.
- The first correction to the effective action at degree of slowness L=1 is (D/π) × ((1-b)/(bΩ)) × ∫dt ΔV_eff(x_<(τ)), where D = 1/(βγ) is the diffusion coefficient.

## Context

This work extends renormalization group ideas from quantum field theory and statistical mechanics (Zinn-Justin, Kleinert) to the simulation of classical stochastic dynamics, providing an alternative to Markov State Models (MSM1, MSM2, MSM3) that does not require identifying metastable states or computing transition matrices from molecular dynamics. The approach is distinct from simply including higher-order Trotter expansion corrections (Trotter), as the decoupling of time scales leads to further simplifications. The method enables efficient simulation of rare conformational transitions in rugged energy landscapes, with potential applications to protein folding and molecular aggregation, though the authors note that the approach assumes a large separation of frequency scales and may require extension for systems where this condition is not met.
