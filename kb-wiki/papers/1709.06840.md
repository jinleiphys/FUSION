# Electromagnetic Strength Distributions from the Ab Initio No-Core Shell Model

> We present an ab initio approach for the description of collective excitations and transition strength distributions of arbitrary nuclei up into the sd-shell that based on the No-Core Shell Model in combination with the Lanczos strength-function method. Starting from two- and three-nucleon interactions from chiral effective field theory, we investigate the electric monopole, dipole, and quadrupole response of the even oxygen isotopes from 16-O to 24-O. The method describes the full energy range from low-lying excitations to the giant resonance region and beyond in a unified and consistent framework, including a complete description of fragmentation and fine-structure. This opens unique opportunities for understanding dynamic properties of nuclei from first principles and to further constrain nuclear interactions. We demonstrate the computational efficiency and the robust model-space convergence of our approach and compare to established approximate methods, such as the Random Phase Approximation, shedding new light on their deficiencies.

## Key claim

The authors present an ab initio approach combining the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) with the Lanczos strength-function method that can describe electromagnetic strength distributions for nuclei up into the sd-shell, but the method does not include explicit coupling to the continuum and therefore cannot capture escape width above particle thresholds.

## Method

The authors first solve the NCSM eigenvalue problem for the ground state using chiral two- and three-nucleon interactions softened via Similarity Renormalization Group transformation, employing importance truncation to manage basis dimensions. They then apply the Lanczos algorithm starting from a pivot vector constructed by applying the electromagnetic transition operator to the ground-state eigenvector, iteratively building a tridiagonal Hamiltonian matrix whose eigenvalues and eigenvectors yield the discrete strength distribution. The discrete strength is folded with a Lorentzian of 1 MeV width to produce continuous strength functions for comparison with experiment.

## Key numbers

- For oxygen-16 using the NN+3N(400) interaction, the isovector E1 strength distributions show the largest systematic dependence on Nmax truncation, with unnatural parity 1- states exhibiting slowly converging excitation energies
- The Tmax=2 truncation shifts giant resonance energies by about 20 MeV to higher energies compared to converged results, with the ground state shifting by about -25 MeV while excited states shift by about -5 MeV
- For the NNLO_sat interaction in oxygen-16, the dipole polarizability is 0.48 fm^3 compared to the experimental value of 0.58 fm^3
- For oxygen-24 using the NNLO_sat interaction, the dipole polarizability reaches 1.09 fm^3
- Strength distributions are fully converged after about 100 Lanczos iterations, with results shown using at least 400 iterations
- The SRG flow parameter used is alpha = 0.08 fm^4, with a Hartree-Fock basis using single-particle truncation emax=12

## Context

This work extends ab initio nuclear structure methods beyond ground-state and low-lying excited state calculations into the domain of collective excitations and giant resonances, which were previously dominated by phenomenological models like Random Phase Approximation (RPA) and its extensions. The approach is complementary to the Lorentz-integral transform (LIT) method combined with coupled-cluster calculations (Bacca 2013, Bacca 2014, Hagen 2015), which includes continuum physics but cannot address fragmentation and fine structure as well as the sub-threshold region. The comparison with RPA and Second RPA (SRPA) calculations reveals that the well-known energy shift problem in SRPA originates from an inconsistency between using the Hartree-Fock ground state while including 2p2h excitations, as demonstrated by the agreement between SRPA' (shifted by second-order perturbative ground-state energy correction) and the Tmax=2 truncated NCSM results.
