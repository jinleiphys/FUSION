# Quantified limits of the nuclear landscape

> The chart of the nuclides is limited by particle drip lines beyond which nuclear stability to proton or neutron emission is lost. Predicting the range of particle-bound isotopes poses an appreciable challenge for nuclear theory as it involves extreme extrapolations of nuclear masses beyond the regions where experimental information is available. Still, quantified extrapolations are crucial for a variety of applications, including the modeling of stellar nucleosynthesis. We use microscopic nuclear mass models and Bayesian methodology to provide quantified predictions of proton and neutron separation energies as well as Bayesian probabilities of existence throughout the nuclear landscape all the way to the particle drip lines. We apply nuclear density functional theory with several energy density functionals. To account for uncertainties, Bayesian Gaussian processes are trained on the separation-energy residuals for each individual model, and the resulting predictions are combined via Bayesian model averaging. This framework allows to account for systematic and statistical uncertainties and propagate them to extrapolative predictions. We characterize the drip-line regions where the probability that the nucleus is particle-bound decreases from $1$ to $0$. In these regions, we provide quantified predictions for one- and two-nucleon separation energies. According to our Bayesian model averaging analysis, 7759 nuclei with $Z\leq 119$ have a probability of existence $\geq 0.5$. The extrapolations obtained in this study will be put through stringent tests when new experimental information on exotic nuclei becomes available. In this respect, the quantified landscape of nuclear existence obtained in this study should be viewed as a dynamical prediction that will be fine-tuned when new experimental information and improved global mass models become available.

## Key claim

The paper provides quantified, probabilistic predictions for the limits of nuclear binding (particle drip lines) using Bayesian model averaging, but the authors explicitly state that these predictions are "dynamical" and will be "fine-tuned when new experimental information and improved global mass models become available," rather than definitive boundaries.

## Method

The authors combined eight nuclear density functional theory (DFT) models (Skyrme, Gogny, and BCPM functionals) with two global mass models (FRDM-2012 and HFB-24). For each model, Bayesian Gaussian processes were trained on the residuals between experimental and theoretical one- and two-nucleon separation energies, and the resulting predictions were combined via Bayesian model averaging (BMA) using weights based on predictive performance in neutron-rich and proton-rich domains. The posterior predictive distributions were used to compute probabilities of existence for nuclei throughout the nuclear chart up to Z=119.

## Key numbers

- 7759 nuclei with Z ≤ 119 have a probability of existence ≥ 0.5 according to the Bayesian model averaging analysis
- The BMA(n+p) analysis yields an average of 7708 ± 534 particle-bound nuclei with Z,N ≥ 8 and Z ≤ 119, with a 95% credibility interval of [6688, 8440]
- Individual model predictions for the number of particle-bound nuclei range from 6600 (HFB-24) to 8600 (SkM*)
- The BMA variants achieve root-mean-square deviations on the independent testing dataset (AME16-03) of approximately 302 keV for S1n, 453 keV for S2n, 410 keV for S1p, and 438 keV for S2p
- The reference neutron number N0(Z) for the heaviest isotope with experimental separation energy data ranges from N0=29 for Z=16 to N0=157 for Z=100

## Context

This work extends previous Bayesian analyses by Neufcourt et al. (2018, 2019, 2020) that applied Gaussian processes and Bayesian model averaging to specific nuclear regions (Ca region and two-proton emitters) to the entire nuclear landscape. It contradicts earlier uniform model mixing approaches such as Erler et al. (2012) by providing detailed posterior distributions for each model rather than simple averages, and it enables quantified uncertainty propagation for r-process nucleosynthesis simulations and experimental planning at next-generation facilities like FRIB.
