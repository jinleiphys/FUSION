# Light Meson Spectroscopy: Recent Developments and DAFNE

> In this contribution I discuss recent developments in light meson spectroscopy, and note specific areas in which DAFNE is an especially appropriate tool for future experiments. One topic of special relevance is the spectroscopy of excited vector mesons; quite narrow vector hybrids are predicted by the flux-tube model, which could be produced by DAFNE when operating in the $M_{e^+e^-}\approx 1.5-2$ GeV range. A second topic, which would be appropriate for a later date because it requires a rather higher beam energy, is the production of C=(+) mesons in $γγ$ collisions.

# Light Meson Spectroscopy: Recent Developments and DAFNE

## Key claim

The flux-tube model predicts narrow vector hybrid mesons that could be produced at DAFNE in the $M_{e^+e^-}\approx 1.5-2$ GeV range, but the experimental masses of reported exotic $1^{-+}$ hybrid candidates (at 1.4 and 1.6 GeV) are somewhat lighter than the theoretical expectation of $\approx 1.9-2.0$ GeV, so the existence of a corresponding $1^{--}$ hybrid at low mass remains an open question that DAFNE could test.

## Method

The paper reviews recent experimental results from hadron-beam facilities (LEAR, AGS/BNL, VES/Serpukhov) and $e^+e^-$ facilities (VEPP) on light meson spectroscopy, then compares the observed spectrum of glueball candidates, hybrid candidates, multiquark candidates, and conventional $q\bar q$ mesons against theoretical predictions from lattice gauge theory, the flux-tube model, the bag model, and the $^3$P$_0$ decay model. The author tabulates predicted and measured branching fractions for excited vector mesons ($\rho$, $\omega$, $\phi$) and two-photon widths of scalar mesons to identify where DAFNE can resolve ambiguities.

## Key numbers

- The scalar glueball mass predicted by Morningstar and Peardon lattice gauge theory: 1.73(5)(8) GeV
- The $f_0(1500)$ branching fraction to $\pi\pi$: 45.4% (10.4%); to $K\bar K$: 4.4% (2.4%), far from the flavor-singlet expectation of equality
- The $f_0(1710)$ branching fraction to $\pi\pi$: 3.9% (+0.2%/-2.4%); to $K\bar K$: 38% (+9%/-19%)
- Upper limit on $\Gamma_{\gamma\gamma}(f_0(1500))$ from ALEPH: $< 0.17$ keV (95% c.l.), far below the $\approx 5$ keV expected for an $n\bar n$ scalar
- The $\pi_2(1670)\to b_1\pi$ branching fraction upper limit from VES: $< 0.19\%$ (2$\sigma$ c.l.)
- The $\pi_2(1670)\to \omega\rho$ (S=2) branching fraction from VES: 1.9(0.4)(1.0)%
- The $\pi_2(1670)\to \omega\rho$ (S=1) branching fraction from VES: 0.9(0.2)(0.3)%
- Predicted total width of a hypothetical $\omega_H(1500)$ hybrid: $\approx 20$ MeV
- The $\rho(1465)$ total width: 310(60) MeV; the $\rho(1700)$ total width: 240(60) MeV
- The $\omega(1419)$ total width: 174(59) MeV; the $\omega(1649)$ total width: 220(35) MeV
- The $\phi(1680)$ total width: 150(50) MeV
- Two-photon widths of the $2^{++}$ multiplet: $f_2(1270)$ = 2.8(4) keV, $a_2(1310)$ = 1.00(6) keV, $f'(1525)$ $\approx$ 0.1 keV
- Predicted branching fractions for $\phi\to\gamma f_0(980)$ and $\phi\to\gamma a_0(980)$: $4\times10^{-5}$ for $K\bar K$ molecule assignment; $\simeq 1\times10^{-5}$ for $f_0(980)=s\bar s$; $\leq 10^{-6}$ for $n\bar n$ assignment

- Observed 2D multiplet mass: $\approx 2.0$ GeV, compared to Godfrey-Isgur prediction of $\approx 2.14$ GeV

## Context

This review extends the line of work on light meson spectroscopy from hadron-beam experiments at LEAR, AGS (BNL), and VES (Serpukhov), and from $e^+e^-$ results at VEPP. It builds on the lattice glueball calculations of Morningstar and Peardon (1999), the flux-tube hybrid model of Isgur, Kokoski, and Paton, and the $K\bar K$ molecule predictions of Close, Isgur, and Kumano (1993). The paper identifies DAFNE as uniquely suited to resolve the excited vector meson sector (where the PDG branching fractions are described as "woeful") and to measure two-photon widths of scalar mesons, which could distinguish between $q\bar q$, glueball, $K\bar K$ molecule, and multiquark assignments for states like $f_0(980)$, $a_0(980)$, $f_0(1500)$, and $f_0(1710)$.
