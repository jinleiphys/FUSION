# Interaction Cross Sections as a Structural Probe of the Hypertriton Halo

> The hypertriton (${}^{3}_Λ\mathrm H$) is the most weakly bound known hypernucleus and one of the most spatially extended quantum halo systems observed in nature. Despite decades of experimental and theoretical effort, its matter radius and $Λ$ separation energy remain incompletely constrained. We demonstrate theoretically that interaction cross-section measurements provide a direct and highly sensitive probe of both quantities. Realistic three-body hypertriton wavefunctions are combined with a coupled-channel Glauber theory incorporating proton, neutron, and hyperon densities together with $ΛN\leftrightarrowΣN$ channel coupling. The resulting interaction cross section changes by about 400 mb across the currently allowed range of $Λ$ separation energies while retaining theoretical uncertainties below approximately 5\%. A Bayesian inversion demonstrates that future interaction cross-section measurements can determine both the hypertriton matter radius and the $Λ$ separation energy with potentially unprecedented precision. These results establish interaction cross sections as a new structural observable for hypernuclear halo physics.

# Interaction Cross Sections as a Structural Probe of the Hypertriton Halo

## Key claim

The interaction cross section of the hypertriton (³_ΛH) on a carbon target varies by about 400 mb across the currently allowed range of Λ separation energies (0.10–0.50 MeV), with theoretical uncertainties below approximately 5%, establishing that experimentally achievable interaction cross-section measurements can determine both the hypertriton matter radius and the Λ separation energy with potentially unprecedented precision.

## Method

Realistic three-body hypertriton wavefunctions were generated using a stochastic variational method with correlated Gaussian basis functions, treating the system as p+n+Λ in Jacobi coordinates. These wavefunctions were combined with a coupled-channel Glauber theory incorporating proton, neutron, and hyperon densities together with ΛN↔ΣN channel coupling, and the interaction cross section for ³_ΛH on a ¹²C target at 1.5 GeV/nucleon was computed as a function of the Λ separation energy. A Bayesian inversion was performed to forecast the precision with which future measurements could extract the matter radius and separation energy.

## Key numbers

- Interaction cross section varies from approximately 1150 mb at B_Λ = 0.10 MeV to approximately 750 mb at B_Λ = 0.50 MeV, a variation of about 400 mb across the allowed range
- Theoretical uncertainty on the interaction cross section remains below approximately 5% throughout the interval studied
- Matter radii range from approximately 2.8 fm at B_Λ = 0.5 MeV to approximately 5.7 fm at B_Λ = 0.1 MeV
- The ratio r_m(0.1 MeV)/r_m(0.5 MeV) ≈ 2.1, close to the universal halo scaling r_m ∝ B_Λ^{-1/2}
- Linear fit yields dσ_I/dr_m ≈ 130 mb/fm, corresponding to a change of approximately 13% in the interaction cross section per femtometer change in matter radius
- For a representative 5% interaction cross-section measurement, the resulting uncertainty in the extracted separation energy is typically δB_Λ ≈ 0.05–0.10 MeV
- Reference wavefunction at B_Λ = 0.133 MeV gives one-body matter rms radius of 5.253 fm, hyperon rms radius of 6.973 fm, and Λ-NN separation of 10.34 fm
- Channel decomposition at B_Λ = 0.10 MeV: σ_breakup = 201 mb, σ_ΛΣ = 140 mb, σ_abs = 806 mb, σ_I = 1147^{+47}_{-52} mb
- Channel decomposition at B_Λ = 0.50 MeV: σ_breakup = 59.2 mb, σ_ΛΣ = 114 mb, σ_abs = 576 mb, σ_I = 750^{+22}_{-28} mb

## Context

This work extends the use of interaction cross-section measurements, which have been instrumental in determining matter radii and neutron skins of exotic nuclei (Tanihata 1985, Ozawa 2001, Teixeira 2021), to the hypernuclear halo regime. It addresses the "hypertriton puzzle", the challenge of reconciling hypernuclear spectroscopy, lifetime measurements, heavy-ion production observables, and microscopic few-body calculations, by providing a new observable that is complementary to spectroscopy, lifetime studies, and production observables. Prior theoretical studies using halo effective field theory (Hildenbrand and Hammer 2019) and pionless effective field theory (Ji, Phillips, and Platter 2012) predicted the rapid growth of the matter radius as binding energy decreases, and the present calculations are broadly consistent with those results while extending the analysis to reaction observables. The methodology opens a new avenue for precision investigations of hypernuclear halos and hyperon-nucleon interactions at future facilities such as FAIR and HIAF.
