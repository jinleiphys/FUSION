# The endpoint of the rp process on accreting neutron stars

> We calculate the rapid proton (rp) capture process of hydrogen burning on the surface of an accreting neutron star with an updated reaction network that extends up to Xe, far beyond previous work. In both steady-state nuclear burning appropriate for rapidly accreting neutron stars (such as the magnetic polar caps of accreting X-ray pulsars) and unstable burning of Type I X-ray bursts, we find that the rp process ends in a closed SnSbTe cycle. This prevents the synthesis of elements heavier than Te and has important consequences for X-ray burst profiles, the composition of accreting neutron stars, and potentially galactic nucleosynthesis of light p nuclei.

## Key claim

The rapid proton (rp) process on accreting neutron stars terminates in a closed SnSbTe cycle, preventing synthesis of elements heavier than tellurium (Z=54), under both steady-state and explosive burning conditions. The cycle is a consequence of the experimentally known low alpha separation energies of neutron-deficient tellurium isotopes and is therefore not subject to nuclear physics uncertainties, though the relative strength of sub-cycles depends on uncertain proton separation energies of antimony isotopes.

## Method

The authors performed one-zone X-ray burst and steady-state burning simulations using an updated nuclear reaction network extending from hydrogen to xenon, with theoretical Hauser-Feshbach reaction rates recalculated using the NON-SMOKER code. The X-ray burst model assumed an accretion rate of 0.1 times the Eddington rate, a crust flux of 0.15 MeV per accreted nucleon, and metallicity Z=10^-3, with ignition at density 1.1x10^6 g/cm^3 and peak temperature 1.9 GK. The steady-state model followed the approach of Schatz et al. (1998) at accretion rates up to 40 times the Eddington limit.

## Key numbers

- Ignition density: 1.1x10^6 g/cm^3; peak temperature: 1.9 GK; burst rise timescale approximately 4 s; cooling phase approximately 200 s
- The rp process reaction flow reaches Sn isotopes in the 99Sn-101Sn range approximately 80 s (time for half maximum) after the burst peak
- 104Sn has a half-life of 20.8 s and is the longest-lived nucleus in the SnSbTe cycle
- 106-108Te isotopes are alpha unbound by approximately 4 MeV
- Overproduction factors (produced abundance to solar abundance) of approximately 10^9 for p nuclei 98Ru, 102Pd, and 106Cd
- Surface gravity assumed: 1.9x10^14 cm/s^2
- Eddington accretion rate: 8.8x10^4 g/cm^2/s

## Context

This work extends previous rp process simulations that ended at 56Ni (early work), in the Kr-Y region (Wallace and Woosley 1981; Fushiki et al. 1987; Hencheck et al. 1983; Wiescher et al. 1994; Koike et al. 1999), or up to Sn (Schatz et al. 1997; Schatz et al. 1998 for steady-state burning). The discovery of the SnSbTe cycle resolves the long-standing question of the rp process endpoint and has direct implications for interpreting X-ray burst light curves (including millisecond oscillations observed by Strohmayer et al. 1998), neutron star crust composition and thermal/electrical conductivity (Brown and Bildsten 1998; Bildsten 1998), and the potential nucleosynthesis of light p nuclei such as 92,94Mo and 96,98Ru (Rayet et al. 1995). The finding that hydrogen is completely burned in the one-zone model challenges earlier proposals for unburned hydrogen as an explanation for short burst intervals (Taam et al. 1993) and extended X-ray flares (Taam et al. 1996).
