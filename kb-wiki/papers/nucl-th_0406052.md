# Analytical description of classical trajectories and deflection function in scattering

> Analytical expressions are derived for classical trajectories in repulsive Coulomb plus multi-step attractive potentials. Thereafter the closed form expressions are obtained for classical deflection functions. The expressions are expected to be of use in heavy ion interactions in nuclear physics.

## Key claim

The paper derives closed-form analytical expressions for classical trajectories and deflection functions in scattering by a repulsive Coulomb potential combined with an attractive multi-step (staircase) potential, but the practical utility depends on approximating smooth nuclear potentials (e.g., Woods-Saxon) by a finite number of discrete steps.

## Method

The authors start from the standard classical integral for the scattering angle as a function of impact parameter and distance of closest approach. They first treat the case of a single attractive square well added to the Coulomb potential, then generalize to a staircase potential composed of multiple square wells of different strengths and ranges. For each step, the distance of closest approach is determined by comparing ratios of step radii to computed distances, and the trajectory and deflection function are expressed in closed form using arctangent functions (denoted W) evaluated at the step boundaries.

## Key numbers

- The Coulomb distance of closest approach for head-on collisions is defined as \( d = Z_1 Z_2 e^2 / E \).
- For Coulomb alone, the distance of closest approach is \( a_C = d/2 + \sqrt{d^2/4 + b^2} \).
- For a single square well of strength \( V_0 \), the distance of closest approach when the barrier is overcome is \( a_0 = [d/2 + \sqrt{d^2/4 + b^2 (1+V_0/E)}] / (1+V_0/E) \).
- For the staircase potential with \( m+1 \) steps, the distance of closest approach in region \( s \) is \( a_s = [d/2 + \sqrt{d^2/4 + b^2 (1+V_s/E)}] / (1+V_s/E) \), where \( V_s = \sum_{i=0}^s v_i \).

## Context

This work extends the classic semiclassical scattering formalism of Ford and Wheeler (1959) by providing closed-form deflection functions for potentials more realistic than pure Coulomb. The analytical expressions are intended for heavy-ion scattering in nuclear physics, where the nuclear potential is often approximated by a Woods-Saxon form; the authors suggest discretizing that smooth potential into a few steps to gain insight. The results can also be applied to classical and semiclassical treatments of fusion and deep inelastic scattering.
