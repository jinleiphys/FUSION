# Flux predictions of high-energy neutrinos from pulsars

> Young, rapidly rotating neutron stars could accelerate ions from their surface to energies of $\sim 1$ PeV. If protons reach such energies, they will produce pions (with low probability) through resonant scattering with x-rays from the stellar surface. The pions subsequently decay to produce muon neutrinos. Here we calculate the energy spectrum of muon neutrinos, and estimate the event rates at Earth. The spectrum consists of a sharp rise at $\sim 50$ TeV, corresponding to the onset of the resonance, above which the flux drops with neutrino energy as $ε_ν^{-2}$ up to an upper-energy cut-off that is determined by either kinematics or by the maximum energy to which protons are accelerated. We estimate event rates as high as 10-100 km^${-2}$ yr$^{-1}$ from some candidates, a flux that would be easily detected by IceCube. Lack of detection would allow constraints on the energetics of the poorly-understood pulsar magnetosphere.

## Key claim

Young, rapidly rotating neutron stars could produce muon neutrinos with energies above ~50 TeV via resonant photomeson production, but the event rates are rough upper limits subject to many uncertainties, including the unknown acceleration length, depletion factor, and beaming geometry.

## Method

The authors calculate the neutrino energy spectrum from protons accelerated in the neutron star magnetosphere that undergo resonant pγ → Δ⁺ scattering with surface x-ray photons. They model the proton acceleration as a function of height above the stellar surface, account for the finite width of the Δ resonance, and integrate over the angular distribution of surface photons visible to the proton at each height. The resulting differential conversion probability is then scaled to obtain the muon neutrino flux at Earth, and event rates are estimated using the νμ → μ conversion probability in the Earth.

## Key numbers

- Neutrino spectrum turns on sharply at ε_ν ≈ 70 T_{0.1keV}⁻¹ TeV, corresponding to the onset of the Δ resonance
- Above the turn-on, the flux drops approximately as ε_ν⁻² up to an upper-energy cut-off
- Estimated event rates for the Crab pulsar: up to 45 km⁻² yr⁻¹ (assuming linear acceleration, L=0.1)
- Estimated event rates for the Vela pulsar: up to 25 km⁻² yr⁻¹ (assuming linear acceleration, L=0.1)
- Estimated event rates for Cassiopeia A: up to 50 km⁻² yr⁻¹ (with guessed parameters)
- The integrated conversion probability P_c for the Crab is 1.6×10⁻³ (linear acceleration, L=0.1)
- The integrated conversion probability P_c for Vela is 7.2×10⁻⁵ (linear acceleration, L=0.1)
- The potential drop across field lines is ΔΦ ≈ 7×10¹⁸ B₁₂ R₆³ p_ms⁻² Volts
- The proton threshold energy for the Δ resonance is ε_{p,th} ≈ T_{0.1keV}⁻¹ PeV
- The cross section for Δ⁺ production is σ₀ = 5×10⁻²⁸ cm⁻², with resonance width w ≈ 100 MeV
- The conversion probability in the Earth is P_{νμ→μ} ≈ 1.3×10⁻⁶ (ε_ν/1 TeV)

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier proposal (Link and Burgio 2005) that young, rapidly rotating neutron stars could be intense neutrino sources, providing improved estimates of the neutrino spectrum and event rates. The predicted event rates are a factor of ~10-30 lower than their earlier estimates due to phase space limitations from geometry. The paper enables searches for high-energy neutrinos from known pulsars by IceCube, AMANDA-II, and ANTARES, and notes that the first 807 days of AMANDA-II data showed 10 events from the Crab direction (over a background of 5.4). Non-detection would constrain the accelerating potential in the poorly-understood pulsar magnetosphere.
