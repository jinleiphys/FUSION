# Overview of the experimental quest for the giant pairing vibration

> The search for the giant pairing vibration (GPV) has a long standing history since the 1970's when it was predicted. First experimental measurements focused on (p,t) transfer reactions in the heavy nuclei and did not show convincing evidence. The discovery of a signal compatible with the GPV in the light carbon isotopes has renewed the interest for the GPV. It triggered new theoretical models showing that the GPV in the heavy nuclei might be too wide or too melted to be observed and triggered new experiments with radioactive probes based on ($^{6}$He,$^{4}$He) transfer.

## Key claim

The giant pairing vibration (GPV) has been predicted since the 1970s but remains unobserved in heavy nuclei; a signal compatible with the GPV was found in light carbon isotopes, but the mode in heavy nuclei may be too wide or too melted to be observed, or the (p,t) reaction mechanism may be ill-suited.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental searches for the GPV using two-neutron transfer reactions: (p,t) reactions on heavy targets (Pb, Sn) at proton energies of 35–60 MeV, and (⁶He,⁴He) reactions on ²⁰⁸Pb and ¹¹⁶Sn at 20 A MeV and 8 MeV/u respectively. In light nuclei, the (¹⁸O,¹⁶O) two-nucleon transfer reaction was used on carbon and beryllium targets at 84 MeV and 275 MeV. Tritons or alpha particles were detected using magnetic spectrometers (K=600 QDD, MAGNEX) or silicon detector arrays, with zero-degree measurements and time-of-flight particle identification to reduce background.

## Key numbers

- GPV predicted energy: approximately 70/A¹/³ MeV in two-neutron L=0 transfer channel, with width 7.8/A¹/³ MeV and cross-section 20%–100% of ground-state cross-section (theoretical, from Bro73, Oer01, For02).
- For ¹¹⁸Sn via (p,t) at Ep=50 MeV: upper limit on GPV cross-section between 0.13 and 0.19 mb over the spectrometer angular acceptance (±2 degrees) (Mou11).
- For ¹¹⁸Sn via (p,t) at Ep=35 MeV: fitted GPV width 1.5 ± 0.4 MeV (deN14).
- For light carbon isotopes via (¹⁸O,¹⁶O): GPV observed at about 17 MeV in ¹⁴C and 13.7 MeV in ¹⁵C, with widths of 1 to 1.5 MeV and cross-sections of 0.3 and 0.4 mbarn respectively at 84 MeV (Cap15).
- Proton background rate in (p,t) experiments at iThemba was 500 times higher than the triton rate of interest (Mou11).

## Context

This review extends the long-standing search for the GPV that began with predictions by Broglia (1973) and Broglia (1977), and follows the experimental work of Cappuzzello (2015) who identified a GPV signal in light carbon isotopes. The non-observation in heavy nuclei contrasts with theoretical expectations and has motivated new theoretical models by Laskin (2016) suggesting the GPV may be too wide to observe, and new experimental approaches using weakly-bound projectiles like (⁶He,⁴He) transfer as proposed by Oertzen (2001) and Dasso (2015). The review enables future searches using spectrometers rather than particle arrays, and extension to proton-proton GPV modes.
