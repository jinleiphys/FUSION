# Double Beta and Dark Matter Search - Window to New Physics beyond the Standard Model of Particle Physics

> Nuclear double beta decay provides an extraordinarily broad potential to search for beyond Standard Model physics, probing already now the TeV scale, on which new physics should manifest itself. These possibilities are reviewed here. First, the results of present generation experiments are presented. The most sensitive one of them - the Heidelberg-Moscow experiment in the Gran Sasso - probes the electron mass now in the sub eV region and will reach a limit of $\sim$ 0.1 eV in a few years. Basing to a large extent on the theoretical work of the Heidelberg Double Beta Group in the last two years, results are obtained also for SUSY models (R-parity breaking, sneutrino mass), leptoquarks (leptoquark-Higgs coupling), compositeness, right-handed W boson mass, test of special relativity and equivalence principle in the neutrino sector and others. One of the enriched $^{76}$Ge detectors also yields the most stringent limits for cold dark matter (WIMPs) to date by using raw data. A new Heidelberg experimental proposal (GENIUS) is described which would allow to increase the sensitivity for Majorana neutrino masses from the present level of at best 0.1 eV down to 0.01 or even 0.001 eV. It would further, already in a first step using only 100 kg of natural Ge detectors, cover almost the full MSSM parameter space for prediction of neutralinos as cold dark matter.

## Key claim

Nuclear double beta decay can probe the TeV scale for new physics beyond the Standard Model, with the Heidelberg-Moscow experiment already reaching sub-eV sensitivity for the electron neutrino mass and projected to reach approximately 0.1 eV within a few years, contingent on continued operation and background reduction.

## Method

The paper reviews results from the Heidelberg-Moscow experiment, which uses enriched germanium-76 detectors at the Gran Sasso laboratory to search for neutrinoless double beta decay (0νββ). One of the enriched germanium detectors also provides dark matter (WIMP) limits by analyzing raw data. The theoretical analysis derives constraints on beyond-Standard-Model parameters from the experimental half-life limits using nuclear matrix elements calculated within the QRPA framework, including contributions from neutrino exchange, supersymmetric particles, leptoquarks, compositeness, right-handed W bosons, and Majoron emission.

## Key numbers

- The Heidelberg-Moscow experiment probes the electron neutrino mass in the sub-eV region and will reach a limit of approximately 0.1 eV in a few years
- The proposed GENIUS experiment would improve sensitivity for Majorana neutrino masses from the present level of at best 0.1 eV down to 0.01 eV (one-ton version) or even 0.001 eV (ten-ton version)
- Accelerator limits on excited lepton masses are: m(e*) > 127 GeV, m(e*,ν*) > 91 GeV, m(ν*) > 180 GeV, m(q*) > 540 GeV
- Direct searches at HERA place lower limits on leptoquark mass M(LQ) ≥ 225-275 GeV depending on LQ type and couplings
- Compositeness scale limits from accelerators are all in the range Λ_C ≥ 1.6 TeV
- For R-parity violating SUSY, the derived constraints on λ' products with Λ_SUSY = 100 GeV give ε'_1 = 6.4×10^(-5), ε'_2 = 3.2×10^(-6), ε'_3 = 1.1×10^(-7), and ε = 6.5×10^(-8)

## Context

This review extends the work of the Heidelberg Double Beta Group and summarizes constraints on beyond-Standard-Model physics competitive with high-energy accelerators like TEVATRON and HERA. The paper describes the GENIUS proposal, which would enable testing the atmospheric neutrino problem and the large-angle solution of the solar neutrino problem, and for degenerate neutrino mass scenarios even the small-angle solution. The work connects to the Schechter-Valle theorem (1981) and its supersymmetric generalization by Hirsch, Klapdor-Kleingrothaus, and Kovalenko (1997), linking double beta decay to processes potentially observable at future colliders like the Next Linear Collider.
