# Stochastic quantum dynamics beyond mean-field

> Mean-field approaches where a complex fermionic many-body problem is replaced by an ensemble of independent particles in a self-consistent mean-field can describe many static and dynamical aspects. It generally provides a rather good approximation for the average properties of one-body degrees of freedom. However, the mean-field approximation generally fails to produce quantum fluctuations of collective motion. To overcome this difficulty, noise can be added to the mean-field theory leading to a stochastic description of the many-body problem. In the present work, we summarize recent progress in this field and discuss approaches where fluctuations have been added either to the initial time, like in the Stochastic Mean-Field theory or continuously in time as in the Stochastic Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock. In some cases, the initial problem can even be re-formulated exactly by introducing Quantum Monte-Carlo methods in real-time. The possibility to describe superfluid systems is also invoked. Successes and shortcomings of the different beyond mean-field theories are discussed and illustrated.

## Key claim

Stochastic approaches can incorporate quantum fluctuations and dissipative effects missing from standard mean-field theory by replacing a single mean-field trajectory with an ensemble of independent mean-field trajectories, but the practical applicability of methods that add noise continuously in time remains at the stage of formal development, while methods that add fluctuations only at the initial time (Stochastic Mean-Field theory) have already been applied to realistic physical systems.

## Method

The authors review stochastic extensions of Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) and its Density Functional Theory variant, where the many-body problem is replaced by an ensemble of independent-particle evolutions. Two main classes are discussed: Stochastic Mean-Field (SMF) theory, which adds fluctuations to the initial state only, and Stochastic Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock, which adds noise continuously in time. The review also covers the possibility of reformulating the problem exactly using real-time Quantum Monte-Carlo methods and extensions to superfluid systems via the Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) framework.

## Key numbers

* The one-body density matrix for an independent particle state satisfies Tr(ρ) = N and ρ² = ρ, indicating exactly N occupation numbers equal to one and the rest zero.
* The mean-field Hamiltonian is given by h[ρ] = T + Tr₂ ṽ₁₂ ρ₂, where the second term represents the average potential created by the N particles.
* The generalized density matrix for HFB theory is written as a 2×2 block matrix: ℛ = [[ρ, κ], [-κ*, 1-ρ*]], where κ is the anomalous density.
* The two-body correlation matrix in HFB theory takes the separable form C_{ijkl} = κ_{ij} κ*_{kl}.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on TDHF dating back to Bonche (1976) and the early recognition by Negele (1982) that mean-field alone cannot describe all aspects of nuclei, particularly the underestimation of quantal effects in collective space and the absence of dissipative effects from coupling to internal degrees of freedom. The stochastic approaches discussed provide a practical alternative to configuration mixing techniques (Ring and Schuck, 1980; Bender et al., 2003) and to more numerically demanding beyond-mean-field methods, since they can leverage existing mean-field codes and parallel computing. The review enables future applications of stochastic methods to realistic nuclear dynamics, particularly for describing giant resonance damping and collective fluctuations, while noting that continuous-time stochastic approaches still require further formal development before practical implementation.
