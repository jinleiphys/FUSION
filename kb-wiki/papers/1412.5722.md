# Hyperon Puzzle of Neutron Stars with Skyrme Force Models

> We consider the so called hyperon puzzle of neutron star (NS). We employ Skyrme force models for the description of in-medium nucleon-nucleon, nucleon-Lambda hyperon ($NΛ$), and Lambda-Lambda ($ΛΛ$) interactions. A phenomenological finite-range force for the $ΛΛ$ interaction is considered as well. Equation of state (EoS) of NS matter is obtained in the framework of density functional theory, and Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are solved to obtain the mass-radius relations of NSs. It has been generally known that the existence of hyperons in the NS matter is not well supported by the recent discovery of large-mass NSs ($M \simeq 2 M_\odot$) since hyperons make the EoS softer than the one without them. For the selected interaction models, $NΛ$ interactions reduce the maximum mass of NS by about 30~\%, while $ΛΛ$ interactions can give about 10~\% enhancement. Consequently, we find that some Skyrme force models predict the maximum mass of NS consistent with the observation of $2 M_\odot$ NSs, and at the same time satisfy observationally constrained mass-radius relations.

## Key claim

Some Skyrme force models that include both nucleon-Lambda and Lambda-Lambda interactions can predict a maximum neutron star mass consistent with the observed 2 solar mass neutron stars, while also satisfying observationally constrained mass-radius relations, suggesting a possible resolution to the hyperon puzzle. The authors find that Lambda-Lambda interactions can enhance the maximum mass by about 10%, partially compensating for the approximately 30% reduction caused by nucleon-Lambda interactions.

## Method

The authors employed Skyrme-type force models for nucleon-nucleon (SLy4, SkI4, SGI), nucleon-Lambda (HPΛ2, OΛ2, YBZ6), and Lambda-Lambda interactions (SΛΛ1, SΛΛ2, SΛΛ3, SΛΛ1', SΛΛ3', and a finite-range force model). They calculated the equation of state of neutron star matter within density functional theory under beta-equilibrium and charge neutrality conditions, then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius relations.

## Key numbers

- Without hyperons, the maximum neutron star masses for the three nucleon-nucleon models are: SLy4 gives 2.07 solar masses, SkI4 gives 2.19 solar masses, SGI gives 2.25 solar masses
- Including only nucleon-Lambda interactions reduces the maximum mass by 0.4 to 1.0 solar masses, a wider range than the 0.2 to 0.5 solar mass reduction predicted by relativistic mean field models
- The SΛΛ3 model combined with SGI nucleon-nucleon and YBZ6 nucleon-Lambda interactions gives a maximum mass of 2.04 solar masses
- The SΛΛ3 model combined with SkI4 nucleon-nucleon and YBZ6 nucleon-Lambda interactions gives a maximum mass of 2.03 solar masses
- The SΛΛ3' model combined with SGI nucleon-nucleon and YBZ6 nucleon-Lambda interactions gives a maximum mass of 1.97 solar masses
- The SΛΛ3' model combined with SkI4 nucleon-nucleon and YBZ6 nucleon-Lambda interactions gives a maximum mass of 1.96 solar masses
- Lambda-Lambda interactions change the maximum mass from the nucleon-Lambda-only values by -0.1 to +0.4 solar masses, larger than the ±0.1 solar mass change from relativistic mean field models
- The SΛΛ3 model predicts a double-Lambda binding energy of 7.60 MeV for 6ΛΛHe (experimental value: 6.93 ± 0.16 MeV) and 17.76 MeV for 13ΛΛB (experimental value: 23.30 ± 0.70 MeV)

## Context

This work addresses the hyperon puzzle, where the existence of hyperons in neutron star cores softens the equation of state and typically prevents reaching the observed 2 solar mass neutron stars (PSR J1614-2230 at 1.97 solar masses and PSR J0348+0432 at 2.01 solar masses). Previous relativistic mean field studies by Bednarek et al. (2005) and Miyatsu et al. (2010) found only minor effects from hyperon-hyperon interactions (±0.1 solar mass), while this work shows larger effects using Skyrme-type models fitted to double-Lambda hypernuclei data. The results enable further investigation of how repulsive Lambda-Lambda interactions, modeled through finite-range forces or specific Skyrme parameter sets, can stiffen the equation of state sufficiently to resolve the hyperon puzzle.
