# High-lying single-particle modes, chaos, correlational entropy, and doubling phase transition

> Highly-excited single-particle states in nuclei are coupled with the excitations of a more complex character, first of all with collective phonon-like modes of the core. In the framework of the quasiparticle-phonon model we consider the structure of resulting complex configurations using the $1k_{17/2}$ orbital in $^{209}$Pb as an example. Although, on the level of one- and two-phonon admixtures, the fully chaotic GOE regime is not reached, the eigenstates of the model carry significant degree of complexity that can be quantified with the aid of correlational invariant entropy. With artificially enhanced particle-core coupling, the system undergoes the doubling phase transition with the quasiparticle strength concentrated in two repelling peaks. This phase transition is clearly detected by correlational entropy.

## Key claim
The quasiparticle-phonon model for the $1k_{17/2}$ orbital in $^{209}$Pb shows that, even with one- and two-phonon admixtures, the fully chaotic Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) regime is not reached; however, the eigenstates carry significant complexity measurable by invariant correlational entropy (ICE), and an artificially enhanced particle-core coupling induces a doubling phase transition detected by ICE.

## Method
The authors applied the Quasiparticle-Phonon Model (QPM) to describe the structure of highly-excited states in odd spherical nuclei, using the $1k_{17/2}$ orbital in $^{209}$Pb as a test case. The Hamiltonian includes a Woods-Saxon mean field for the quasiparticle, an RPA-treated core Hamiltonian with separable residual interactions, and a particle-core coupling term. Wave functions were constructed as superpositions of quasiparticle, quasiparticle-plus-one-phonon, and quasiparticle-plus-two-phonon configurations, with core excitations up to 20 MeV for all spins and natural parity ($1^-$ through $8^+$). The invariant correlational entropy was computed by multiplying the coupling matrix elements by a random noise parameter drawn from either a Lorentzian or Gaussian distribution, then averaging the resulting density matrix over the noise ensemble.

## Key numbers
- The $1k_{17/2}$ orbital in $^{209}$Pb is located at 4.88 MeV above the Fermi level in the Woods-Saxon potential.
- 81% of the single-particle strength is concentrated between 5 and 13 MeV excitation energy.
- The spreading width of the $1k_{17/2}$ state is $\Gamma^{\downarrow}=1.5$ MeV.
- The number of quasiparticle-plus-one-phonon components included is 420; the number of quasiparticle-plus-two-phonon components is 1116.
- The Lorentzian smoothing parameter for the strength distribution was 0.2 MeV.
- For the ICE calculation, the Lorentzian distribution used $\Delta=0.8$ and the Gaussian distribution used $\sigma=0.5$.
- The summed correlational entropy $F(N)$ for the Gaussian noise was 37.50; for the Lorentzian noise it was 54.44.
- The maximum of the integral correlation entropy $F(N,k)$ occurs at $k_c=1.6$, where $k$ multiplies the particle-core coupling strength (with $k=1$ being the realistic value).
- For $k=0.2$, the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution is close to Poisson; for $k=1$ with only one-phonon components, the Brody parameter is 0.4; with two-phonon components included, the Brody parameter is 0.6.
- For $k=2.0$, the single-particle strength splits into two peaks: 31% at low energy and 21% at high energy.

## Context
This work extends the quasiparticle-phonon model studies of high-spin orbitals in $^{209}$Pb, previously applied to the $1j_{13/2}$, $2h_{11/2}$, and $1k_{17/2}$ states observed in one-nucleon transfer reactions (Gal 1994, Soloviev 1995). It introduces invariant correlational entropy (ICE) as a basis-independent measure of complexity, contrasting with the basis-dependent Shannon entropy used in earlier shell-model studies of quantum chaos (Brody 1981, Zelevinsky 1996). The doubling phase transition identified here is analogous to the phenomenon first described by Lewenkopf (1991) for giant resonances and known in quantum optics; the authors suggest it may be observable in real nuclear spectra, with similar calculations performed for $^{207}$Pb and $^{145}$Eu.
