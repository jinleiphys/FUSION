# Neutron stars in general relativity and scalar-tensor theory of gravity

> The masses and radii of neutron stars are discussed in general relativity and scalar-tensor theory of gravity and the differences are compared with the current uncertainties stemming from the nuclear equation of state in the relativistic mean-field framework. It is shown that astrophysical and gravitational waves observations of radii of neutron stars with masses $M \lesssim 1.4 M_{\odot}$ constrain only the nuclear equation of state, and in particular the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy. Future observations of massive neutron stars may constrain the coupling parameters of the scalar-tensor theory provided that a general consensus on the dense nuclear matter equation of state is reached.

## Key claim

The paper shows that current and near-future observations of neutron stars with masses below about 1.4 solar masses can only constrain the nuclear equation of state (specifically the density dependence of the symmetry energy), while constraining scalar-tensor gravity parameters requires both a consensus on the dense matter equation of state and future observations of massive neutron stars.

## Method

The authors compute neutron star structure by solving the modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations in both general relativity and scalar-tensor theory of gravity, using a family of relativistic mean-field models (FSUGold2 parametrizations) with different values of the symmetry energy slope L. They generate equations of state for neutron-star matter consisting of neutrons, protons, electrons, and muons in chemical equilibrium, and integrate the hydrostatic structure equations from the stellar center to infinity with appropriate boundary conditions.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass predicted by original FSUGold2 in general relativity: 2.07 solar masses
- General relativistic radius of a canonical 1.4 solar-mass neutron star from original FSUGold2: 14.11 km
- Range of symmetry energy slopes considered: L = 47, 60, 80, 100, and 112.8 MeV
- General relativistic radii for 1.4 solar-mass stars range from 12.77 km (L=47 MeV) to 14.11 km (L=112.8 MeV)
- Scalar-tensor radii for 1.4 solar-mass stars range from 12.75 km (L=47 MeV) to 14.11 km (L=112.8 MeV)
- General relativistic radii for 2.0 solar-mass stars range from 11.99 km (L=47 MeV) to 12.96 km (L=112.8 MeV)
- Scalar-tensor radii for 2.0 solar-mass stars range from 12.66 km (L=47 MeV) to 13.46 km (L=112.8 MeV)
- Maximum masses in scalar-tensor theory range from 2.109 to 2.159 solar masses across the L values
- Maximum masses in general relativity range from 2.024 to 2.073 solar masses across the L values
- Solar-system constraints on scalar-tensor coupling: alpha_0^2 less than about 2 x 10^{-5}, beta_0 greater than about -5

## Context

This work extends the analysis of neutron star structure in scalar-tensor gravity originally developed by Damour and Esposito-Farese (1996), who first identified the spontaneous scalarization effect for beta_0 greater than -4.35. The paper builds on the FSUGold2 parametrization from Chen and Piekarewicz (2014) and the method of tuning the symmetry energy slope via the Lambda_v coupling introduced by Horowitz and Piekarewicz (2000). The results enable future missions like NICER and gravitational wave observations to disentangle nuclear equation of state uncertainties from modifications to general relativity, provided that the dense matter equation of state becomes well-constrained first.
