# Observables of non-equilibrium phase transition

> A rapidly expanding fireball which undergoes first-order phase transition will supercool and proceed via spinodal decomposition. Hadrons are produced from the individual fragments as well as the left-over matter filling the space between them. Emission from fragments should be visible in rapidity correlations, particularly of protons. In addition to that, even within narrow centrality classes, rapidity distributions will be fluctuating from one event to another in case of fragmentation. This can be identified with the help of Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. Finally, we present a method which allows to sort events with varying rapidity distributions in such a way, that events with similar rapidity histograms are grouped together.

## Key claim

A rapidly expanding fireball undergoing a first-order phase transition will supercool and proceed via spinodal decomposition, producing observable signatures in rapidity correlations and event-by-event rapidity distribution fluctuations; however, the authors note that a sharp peak in bulk viscosity could also cause fireball fragmentation even without a first-order phase transition, questioning the uniqueness of fragmentation as a signature.

## Method

The authors used the DRAGON Monte Carlo generator, which parametrizes hadron emission from a blast-wave fireball with superimposed droplets from spinodal decomposition, to simulate events with varying droplet sizes and fractions. They computed proton correlation functions in relative rapidity using mixed-event techniques, applied the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test to compare rapidity distributions between events, and developed an iterative Bayesian sorting algorithm to group events with similar rapidity histograms.

## Key numbers

- Average droplet volume is \(2b\); simulations used \(b = 50\ \mathrm{fm}^3\) and \(b = 20\ \mathrm{fm}^3\)
- Droplet fractions tested: 25%, 50%, and 75% of hadrons from droplets
- Fireball settings: Gaussian rapidity distribution width of 1, acceptance window \(-1 < y < 1\), about 1200 hadrons per event, temperature 120 MeV, transverse velocity gradient \(\eta_f = 0.4\), chemical freeze-out temperature \(T_{ch} = 140\ \mathrm{MeV}\), baryon chemical potential \(\mu_B = 413\ \mathrm{MeV}\), energy density 0.7 GeV/fm\(^3\), transverse fireball size 10 fm, lifetime \(\tau = 9\ \mathrm{fm}/c\)
- The Kolmogorov-Smirnov test uses scaled distance \(d = \sqrt{n_1 n_2 / (n_1 + n_2)} D\) and a ratio \(R = (N_0 - N_{\mathrm{tot}}/B) / (N_{\mathrm{tot}}/B)\) to quantify non-statistical fluctuations
- Event shape sorting algorithm was tested on 5000 events with droplet fraction 25% and \(b = 50\ \mathrm{fm}^3\)

## Context

This work extends earlier proposals by Pratt (1994) and Randrup to use baryon clustering in rapidity correlations as a signature of spinodal decomposition, and elaborates on the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test approach from Schulc. The authors note that Torrieri (2007) and Rajagopal (2009) argued that bulk viscosity peaks could cause fragmentation even without a first-order phase transition, which would complicate interpretation. The results enable experimental searches at NICA for non-equilibrium phase transition signatures and provide tools for event classification that could be combined with models including hadronic rescattering.
