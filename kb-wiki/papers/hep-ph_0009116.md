# Roles of Chiral Symmetry and the Sigma Meson in Hadron and Nuclear Physics

> We first review the recent accumulating evidences of the existence of a scalar-isoscalar meson with the mass 500 to 800 MeV which may be identified with the sigma meson as the quantum fluctuation of the amplitude of the chiral order parameter. We indicate that phase shift analyses which respect chiral symmetry (ChS), analyticity and crossing symmetry of the scattering amplitude show the sigma meson pole in the $s$-channel as well as the $ρ$ meson pole in the $t$-channel in the pi-pi scattering in the $I=J=0$ channel. We emphasize that the existence of the $σ$ resonance does not contradict with the success of the chiral perturbation theory; phenomenological difficulties with the renormalizable linear sigma model do not necessarily deny the validity of the linear representation of ChS of QCD as given by the NJL-like models which not only admit the $σ$ resonance but also reproduce the coupling constants $L_i$ and $H_i$ appearing the nonlinear chiral lagrangian. We give some examples of the hadronic phenomena which are naturally accounted for with the $σ$ meson. We show that the the $σ$ meson as the amplitude fluctuation of the chiral order parameter may be more clearly identified than in free space in hot and/or dense matter, even in finite nuclei where partial restoration of ChS may be realized.

# Roles of Chiral Symmetry and the Sigma Meson in Hadron and Nuclear Physics

## Key claim

The sigma meson, as the quantum fluctuation of the amplitude of the chiral order parameter, may exist with a mass of 500–800 MeV and a large width, and its existence does not contradict chiral perturbation theory; however, the identification of the observed scalar-isoscalar pole with the chiral order-parameter fluctuation is not yet conclusive, and further work is needed to make the identification definitive.

## Method

The paper reviews phase shift analyses of pi-pi scattering in the I=J=0 channel that respect chiral symmetry, analyticity, crossing symmetry, and unitarity using the N/D method, comparing scenarios with and without a scalar resonance degenerate with the rho meson. The author also examines the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL)-like models that linearly realize chiral symmetry and reproduce the low-energy constants L_i and H_i of the nonlinear chiral Lagrangian. The scalar charges of baryons are calculated using the chiral quark model combined with the NJL model including a determinantal interaction, and the spectral function of the sigma meson in nuclear matter is analyzed using one-loop self-energy corrections.

## Key numbers

- The sigma meson mass is predicted to be 500–800 MeV, with an imaginary part Im m_sigma approximately 500 MeV from phase shift analyses.
- The scalar charges of the proton are calculated as: <uu>_P = 4.97, <dd>_P = 4.00, <ss>_P = 0.53, compared to naive quark model values of 2, 1, and 0 respectively.
- The pi-N sigma term is calculated to be 49 MeV, in good agreement with the experimental value.
- The KSRF coupling for the rho meson is given as m_rho^2/(2 f_pi^2), with an alternative value m_rho^2/(pi f_pi^2) from the Veneziano amplitude.
- The enhancement factor for the Delta I=1/2 rule from the sigma pole is expressed as (m_sigma^2 - m_pi^2)/(m_sigma^2 - m_K^2) * (F_K^2/F_pi^2).
- The scalar meson mass range used in one-boson-exchange potential models for nuclear force is 400–700 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the work of Nambu (1961) on the collective nature of the QCD vacuum and the work of Hatsuda and Kunihiro (1994) on the role of chiral symmetry in hadron dynamics. It addresses the controversy between the Juelich group's claim that pi-pi phase shifts can be reproduced without a sigma pole and the analysis by Igi and Hikasa showing that a degenerate rho-sigma scenario is required to match the data. The paper enables future experimental searches for the sigma meson in nuclear matter, particularly through the CHAOS collaboration's observations of spectral enhancement near the two-pion threshold, and motivates studies of partial chiral restoration in hot and/or dense matter.
