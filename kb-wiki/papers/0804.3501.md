# Lattice simulations for few- and many-body systems

> We review the recent literature on lattice simulations for few- and many-body systems. We focus on methods and results that combine the framework of effective field theory with computational lattice methods. Lattice effective field theory is discussed for cold atoms as well as low-energy nucleons with and without pions. A number of different lattice formulations and computational algorithms are considered, and an effort is made to show common themes in studies of cold atoms and low-energy nuclear physics as well as common themes in work by different collaborations.

## Key claim

This paper reviews the recent literature on lattice simulations for few- and many-body systems, focusing on methods that combine effective field theory with computational lattice techniques, but notes that for few- and many-body systems beyond two nucleons, lattice QCD simulations are presently out of reach due to the need for physical pion masses and large lattices, as well as exponentially small signal-to-noise ratios at large quark number.

## Method

The review examines lattice effective field theory formulations for cold atoms and low-energy nucleons, both with and without explicit pion degrees of freedom. It covers multiple lattice formulations including Grassmann path integrals without auxiliary fields, auxiliary-field Monte Carlo methods, and diagrammatic Monte Carlo techniques, applied to systems such as two-component fermions in the unitarity limit and nuclear matter. The paper also discusses power-counting schemes including Weinberg power counting, KSW power counting, and alternative schemes for handling singular potentials in chiral effective field theory.

## Key numbers

- Neutron scattering length: approximately -18.5 fm, with interaction range comparable to the pion Compton wavelength of about 1.4 fm
- Physical constants used in chiral effective field theory: nucleon mass m = 938.92 MeV, pion mass m_pi = 138.08 MeV, pion decay constant f_pi = 93 MeV, nucleon axial charge g_A = 1.26
- The unitarity limit is approximately realized when the interparticle spacing is about 5 fm for neutron matter
- The NNLO effective potential gave a prediction for the isospin-symmetric alpha binding energy accurate to within a fraction of 1 MeV

## Context

This review extends earlier reviews of quantum Monte Carlo methods for electronic orbitals (Hammond 1994), solid state materials (Foulkes 2001), superfluid helium (Ceperley 1995), and few-nucleon systems (Carlson), as well as reviews of Monte Carlo lattice methods for strongly-correlated lattice models (von der Linden 1992) and lattice QCD (Davies 2002). The paper discusses the first quantum lattice study of nuclear matter (Brockmann 1992) using a momentum lattice and the quantum hadrodynamics model, and the first study combining lattice methods with effective theory for low-energy nuclear physics (Muller 1999). It enables systematic improvement of low-energy nuclear physics calculations through lattice effective field theory, with direct links to analytic effective field theory calculations and the ability to study few- and many-body systems as well as thermodynamics at nonzero temperature and density using the same lattice action.
