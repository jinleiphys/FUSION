# Effect of Pauli principle on the deformed QRPA calculations and its consequence in the $β$-decay calculations of deformed even-even nuclei

> In this work, we take into consideration of Pauli Exclusion Principle(PEP) in the quasi-particle random phase approximation (QRPA) calculations for the deformed systems by replacing the traditional Quasi-Boson Approximation(QBA) with the renormalized one. With this new formalism, the parametrization of QRPA calculations has been changed and the collapse of QRPA solutions could be avoid for realistic $g_{pp}$ values. We further find that the necessity of renormalization parameter of particle-particle residual interaction $g_{pp}$ in QRPA calculations is due to the exclusion of PEP. So with the inclusion of PEP, we could easily extend the deformed QRPA calculations to the less explored region where lack of experimental data prevent effective parametrization of $g_{pp}$ for QRPA methods. With this theoretical improvement, we give predictions of weak decay rates for even-even isotopes in the rare earth region and the results are then compared with existing calculations.

# Effect of Pauli principle on the deformed QRPA calculations and its consequence in the β-decay calculations of deformed even-even nuclei

## Key claim

The paper claims that the need for a renormalization parameter \(g_{pp}\) (the strength of the particle-particle residual interaction) in previous deformed QRPA calculations arises primarily from the violation of the Pauli Exclusion Principle (PEP) through the Quasi-Boson Approximation (QBA), and that by using the renormalized Quasi-Boson Approximation (rQBA) with a sufficiently large model space, one can use the bare G-matrix elements (i.e., \(g_{pp}=1\)) and still obtain agreement with experimental half-lives within about a factor of two.

## Method

The author replaces the standard Quasi-Boson Approximation (QBA) in the proton-neutron QRPA (pn-QRPA) for deformed even-even nuclei with a renormalized Quasi-Boson Approximation (rQBA) that approximately accounts for the Pauli Exclusion Principle via a boson mapping technique. The QRPA equations are reformulated with a modified commutation relation that includes diagonal occupation factors \(\mathcal{D}\), and the equations are solved iteratively to obtain the forward and backward amplitudes. The method uses a Woods-Saxon mean field with deformation parameters from experimental data or the FRDM model, and the residual interaction is based on the CD-Bonn potential G-matrix, with the axial-vector coupling constant quenched to \(g_A = 0.75 g_{A0}\).

## Key numbers

- For the Kr-Mo region (Region I), the calculated half-lives using rQRPA with \(g_{pp}=1\) deviate from experimental values by at most 30%, with the theory slightly over-predicting decay rates.
- For the rare earth region (Region II), a factor of two deviation between the rQRPA results and the FRDM-based calculations of Möller et al. (2003) is observed for almost all nuclei, with the rQRPA rates being much faster.
- For the two long-lived isotopes \(^{154}\)Nd and \(^{158}\)Sm, the rQRPA results show a factor of two deviation from experimental half-lives.
- The model space used is N=0-6 for Region I and N=0-7 for Region II; enlargement from N=0-5 to N=0-6 in Region I causes only a small change of about several percent in decay rates.

## Context

This work extends the deformed QRPA calculations of Fang et al. (2013) by incorporating the Pauli Exclusion Principle via the renormalized Quasi-Boson Approximation, which was previously applied to spherical systems by Catara et al. (1994), Toivanen and Suhonen (1995), and Schwieger et al. (1996). The paper addresses the need for accurate weak decay rates in the neutron-rich rare earth region (Region II), which is crucial for understanding the formation of the rare earth peak (A~165) in r-process abundance simulations, as highlighted by Mumpower et al. (2011, 2012). The results provide an alternative to the global calculations of Möller et al. (2003) and the Skyrme-based calculations of Marketin et al. (2014), and the author notes that extension to odd-A and odd-odd nuclei is needed before the new rates can be fully applied to r-process simulations.
