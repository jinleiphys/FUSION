# Application of Stochastic Variational Method to Hydrodynamics

> We apply the stochastic variational method to the action of the ideal fluid and showed that the Navier-Stokes equation is derived. In this variational method, the effect of dissipation is realized as the direct consequence of the fluctuation dissipation theorem. Differently from the previous works \cite{kk1,kk2}, we parameterize the Lagrangian of SVM in more general form. The form of the obtained equation is not modified but the definition of the transport coefficients are changed. We further discuss the formulation of SVM using the Hamiltonian and show that the variation of the Hamiltonian gives the same result as the case of the Lagrangian.

# Application of Stochastic Variational Method to Hydrodynamics

## Key claim

The Navier-Stokes equation can be derived from the action of an ideal fluid using the stochastic variational method (SVM), where dissipation arises as a direct consequence of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem; however, the form of the obtained equation is not modified from previous derivations, but the definition of the transport coefficients is changed due to a more general parameterization of the stochastic Lagrangian.

## Method

The authors model the motion of a fluid element by a Langevin-type stochastic differential equation with noise strength parameter ν, introducing both forward and backward mean time derivatives (D and D̃) to handle the non-smooth trajectories. They construct a generalized stochastic Lagrangian density in the Lagrange coordinate with two free parameters α₁ and α₂, perform the stochastic variation using the Ito formula and a stochastic partial integration formula, and derive the equation of motion. They also develop a Hamiltonian formulation via Legendre transform, introducing two conjugate momenta, and show that variation of the Hamiltonian yields the same result as the Lagrangian approach.

## Key numbers

- The shear viscosity coefficient is given by η = α₁ (1 + 2α₂) ν ρ^m, where ν is the noise strength and ρ^m is the mass density.
- The kinetic viscosity η/ρ^m satisfies the Einstein relation: η/ρ^m = (1/3) α₁ (1 + 2α₂) ∫₀^∞ dt E[δv̂(t) · δv̂(0)].
- Setting α₁ = α₂ = 0 reduces the obtained equation to the Euler equation (with ζ = 0).
- Setting α₁ = 0, α₂ = 1/2 with appropriate ν and energy density ε yields the Gross-Pitaevskii equation.
- Setting α₂ = 0 with arbitrary finite α₁ gives an equation completely equivalent to the Navier-Stokes equation.
- The higher-order correction term to the Navier-Stokes equation is of second order in the noise magnitude ν and third order in spatial derivatives.

## Context

This work generalizes previous applications of SVM to hydrodynamics by Koide and collaborators (2010, 2011), which derived the compressible Navier-Stokes equation using a less general parameterization (α₁ = 1/2, α₂ = 1/2). The approach extends earlier SVM formulations used to re-derive the Schrödinger equation (Yasue, 1979; Morato, 1982) and the incompressible Navier-Stokes equation (Yasue, 1983). The Hamiltonian formulation with two conjugate momenta differs from the stochastic Hamiltonian approach of Cress (2009), which uses only one conjugate momentum. This work enables systematic derivation of dissipative equations from variational principles and suggests that the higher-order correction term, absent in the standard Navier-Stokes equation, may become important for very viscous fluids and shock wave behavior, and may be identified with the surface effect of the Thomas-Fermi approximation in nuclear physics.
