# Studying nuclear medium modification using the Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule

> The Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule is a generic relation that has been used to make significant contributions to research in hadronic physics. It connects the spin-dependent cross-section for photoproduction off a particle to the squared ratio of the particle's anomalous magnetic moment and its mass, $(κ/M)^2$. Thus, for a nucleon embedded in a nucleus, the sum rule relates the cross-section to $κ/M$ averaged quadratically over the nucleons comprising the nucleus. This quadratic averaging can be used to constrain the mechanism responsible for the medium modification of the nucleon. We also point out that the global properties of the embedded nucleon like its axial charge, mass or magnetic moment are observables measurable through sum rules.

## Key claim

The Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn (GDH) sum rule can distinguish between mean-field (MF) and short-range correlation (SRC) scenarios for nuclear medium modification because its static part involves a quadratic average of the anomalous magnetic moment over mass ratio, unlike most sum rules which depend linearly on static attributes; this quadratic averaging makes the GDH sum rule sensitive to whether the modification is spread uniformly over all nucleons or concentrated on a subset.

## Method

The authors derive the GDH sum rule for a nucleon embedded in a nucleus, expressing the medium-modified integral as a function of the modification factor chi (the ratio of the embedded nucleon's anomalous magnetic moment over mass to the free value). They compute the expected GDH integral under three scenarios: uniform mean-field modification (all nucleons modified equally), short-range correlation modification (20% of nucleons in SRC pairs modified, 80% unmodified), and SRC with D-wave depolarization (where paired nucleons have reversed spin polarization). The key insight is that the static part of the sum rule involves chi squared for uniform modification but requires quadratic averaging over the population fractions for non-uniform scenarios.

## Key numbers

- In the SRC scenario, approximately 20% of nucleons are involved in SRC pairs and are modified, while the remaining 80% are unmodified (based on Hen 2013)
- For the D-wave depolarization scenario, the effective polarization of SRC-paired nucleons is approximately -0.15 to -0.1, with the calculation using P_D = -0.1 and P_S = +1, yielding an average nuclear polarization P_N = 0.78
- Two predicted values of chi (the average medium modification of kappa/M) are cited: chi = 1.30 from Bass (2020) and chi = 1.16 from Dalton (2023), both based on the quark-meson coupling model
- For light nuclei with N approximately equal to Z, the difference between SRC and MF expectations for the GDH integral at chi = 1.3 is a 21% enhancement of SRC over MF; this drops to 16% for heavier nuclei with Z approximately 40
- The effective mass M* of embedded nucleons from various theoretical approaches is approximately 0.8 plus or minus 0.1 times the free mass

## Context

This work extends the discussion of the EMC effect, first observed by the European Muon Collaboration (1983), which showed that nucleon structure functions differ in nuclei compared to free nucleons. The two leading explanations are the mean-field mechanism (Ciofi degli Atti 2015), where all nucleons are similarly modified, and the short-range correlation picture (Frankfurt and Strikman 1988, Hen 2013), where only nucleons in SRC pairs are modified. The paper shows that the GDH sum rule can discriminate between these scenarios, and that this discriminatory power is directly relevant to understanding the EMC effect because the GDH integral is predominantly sensitive to the same medium modifications that cause the EMC effect, while being transparent to Fermi motion, anti-shadowing, and shadowing. The work also connects to the broader question of whether global properties of embedded nucleons (mass, magnetic moment, axial charge) are observable through sum rules.
