# Nuclear physics with a medium-energy Electron-Ion Collider

> A polarized ep/eA collider (Electron-Ion Collider, or EIC) with variable center-of-mass energy sqrt(s) ~ 20-70 GeV and a luminosity ~ 10^{34} cm^{-2} s^{-1} would be uniquely suited to address several outstanding questions of Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) and the microscopic structure of hadrons and nuclei: (i) the three-dimensional structure of the nucleon in QCD (sea quark and gluon spatial distributions, orbital motion, polarization, correlations); (ii) the fundamental color fields in nuclei (nuclear parton densities, shadowing, coherence effects, color transparency); (iii) the conversion of color charge to hadrons (fragmentation, parton propagation through matter, in-medium jets). We briefly review the conceptual aspects of these questions and the measurements that would address them, emphasizing the qualitatively new information that could be obtained with the collider. Such a medium-energy EIC could be realized at Jefferson Lab after the 12 GeV Upgrade (MEIC), or at Brookhaven National Lab as the low-energy stage of eRHIC.

## Key claim
A medium-energy Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) with center-of-mass energy sqrt(s) ~ 20-70 GeV and luminosity ~ 10^34 cm^{-2} s^{-1} would be uniquely suited to address outstanding questions in QCD regarding nucleon three-dimensional structure, nuclear color fields, and hadronization, but the paper is a mini-review compiled in preparation for the MEIC Conceptual Design Report and does not present new experimental results.

## Method
This is a conceptual mini-review that surveys the physics case for a medium-energy EIC, summarizing three major QCD topics: (i) three-dimensional nucleon structure via generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and transverse momentum distributions (TMDs); (ii) nuclear color fields studied through deep-inelastic scattering on nuclei, including shadowing, coherence effects, and color transparency; (iii) hadronization dynamics probed via nuclear medium effects on parton propagation and fragmentation. The authors describe the kinematic reach, luminosity requirements, and detection capabilities needed for each measurement program, referencing existing fixed-target experiments (JLab 12 GeV, SLAC, HERMES, COMPASS) and the HERA ep collider as context.

## Key numbers
- Center-of-mass energy range: sqrt(s) = 20-70 GeV for ep collisions
- Luminosity: ~ 10^34 cm^{-2} s^{-1} over most of the energy range
- Momentum transfer threshold: Q^2_min = 2 GeV^2 for kinematic coverage plots
- Bjorken x reach: down to x ~ few x 10^{-3} for polarized structure function g_1 measurements
- Coherence length: l_coh = (M_N x)^{-1}, with nuclear radius R_A ~ few fm
- Shadowing region: 0.001 < x < 0.1 for nuclear gluon density studies
- Energy range for hadronization studies: nu ~ 10-1000 GeV
- MEIC design: electron energy E_e = 3-11 GeV, proton energy E_p = 20-100 GeV, circumference ~ 1 km
- eRHIC design: proton/ion beam up to 325 GeV, electron beam 5 GeV (upgradable to 20-30 GeV)
- Coherent nuclear scattering recoil momentum: less than or equal to 100 MeV

## Context
This mini-review was compiled in preparation for the MEIC Conceptual Design Report at Jefferson Lab (2011) and builds on the 2007 NSAC Long-Range Plan. It extends the physics program of fixed-target experiments (EMC, SMC, SLAC, HERMES, COMPASS, JLab 12 GeV) and the HERA ep collider (unpolarized, protons only) by proposing a polarized ep/eA collider that would be the first high-energy electron-nucleus collider. The paper enables future detailed design studies for the MEIC at JLab or the low-energy stage of eRHIC at BNL, and connects to higher-energy facilities such as LHeC for studying gluon saturation.
