# New 26P(p,γ)27S thermonuclear reaction rate and its astrophysical implication in rp-process

> Accurate nuclear reaction rates for 26P(p,γ)27S are pivotal for a comprehensive understanding of rp-process nucleosynthesis path in the region of proton-rich sulfur and phosphorus isotopes. However, large uncertainties still exist in the current rate of 26P(p,γ)27S because of the lack of the nuclear mass and the energy level structure information of 27S. We reevaluate this reaction rate using the experimentally constrained 27S mass, together with the shell-model predicted level structure. It is found that the 26P(p,γ)27S reaction rate is dominated by a direct-capture (DC) reaction mechanism despite the presence of three resonances at E = 1.104, 1.597, 1.777 MeV above the proton threshold in 27S. The new rate is overall smaller than the other previous rates from Hauser-Feshbach statistical model by at least one order of magnitude in the temperature range of X-ray burst interest. In addition, we consistently update the photodisintegration rate using the new 27S mass. The influence of new rates of forward and reverse reaction in the abundances of isotopes produced in rp-process is explored by post-processing nucleosynthesis calculations. The final abundance ratio of 27S/26P obtained using the new rates is only 10% of that from the old rate. The abundance flow calculations show the reaction path 26P(p,γ)27S(\b{eta}+,ν)27P is not as important as thought previously for producing 27P. The adoption of the new reaction rates for 26P(p,γ)27S only reduces the final production of aluminum by 7.1%, and has no discernible impact on the yield of other elements.

## Key claim

The $^{26}$P(p,γ)$^{27}$S reaction rate is dominated by direct capture (DC) rather than resonant capture, despite the presence of three resonances at 1.104, 1.597, and 1.777 MeV above the proton threshold in $^{27}$S; the new rate is at least one order of magnitude smaller than previous Hauser-Feshbach statistical model rates across the X-ray burst temperature range, but this conclusion depends on the experimentally constrained $^{27}$S mass excess of 17678(77) keV and shell-model predicted level structure.

## Method

The authors calculated the $^{26}$P(p,γ)$^{27}$S reaction rate using the experimentally constrained $^{27}$S mass from Sun (2020) and shell-model predictions of $^{27}$S energy levels, spectroscopic factors, and electromagnetic transition probabilities from the KSHELL code with the USDC interaction. They computed resonant contributions for three states (1/2$^+$, 9/2$^+$, 7/2$^+$) and direct-capture contributions using a Woods-Saxon potential with the RADCAP code, including thermal population of the first excited state in $^{26}$P. The photodisintegration rate was consistently updated, and post-processing nucleosynthesis calculations were performed to assess astrophysical impact.

## Key numbers

- $^{27}$S mass excess: 17678(77) keV, from the β-delayed two-proton experiment of Sun (2020)
- Proton separation energy $S_p$ for $^{27}$S: 581(214) keV, derived from AME2020 and the new $^{27}$S mass
- Three resonance energies above proton threshold: 1.104 MeV (1/2$^+$), 1.597 MeV (9/2$^+$), 1.777 MeV (7/2$^+$)
- Direct-capture S-factor at zero energy $S(0)$: 33.67 keV·b for ground-state capture and 0.68 keV·b for capture on the first excited state of $^{26}$P
- The new $^{26}$P(p,γ)$^{27}$S rate is smaller than previous Hauser-Feshbach rates by at least one order of magnitude in the X-ray burst temperature range
- Final abundance ratio $^{27}$S/$^{26}$P using new rates is only 10% of that from the old rate
- Adoption of new rates reduces final aluminum production by 7.1%, with no discernible impact on other elements

## Context

This work extends the study of Parikh (2013) on the role of $^{27}$S mass in rp-process nucleosynthesis, replacing the earlier AME2003 mass uncertainty (17540 keV with 200 keV uncertainty) with the experimentally constrained mass from Sun (2020). The new rate contradicts previous Hauser-Feshbach statistical model calculations by Rauscher (2000) and Cyburt (2010), which overestimated the rate because the low level density of $^{27}$S makes the statistical model inappropriate. The results enable more accurate modeling of the rp-process path around $^{26}$P and $^{25}$Si, showing that the $^{26}$P(p,γ)$^{27}$S(β$^+$,ν)$^{27}$P path is less important for producing $^{27}$P than previously thought.
