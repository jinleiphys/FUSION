# Nuclear deexcitation simulator for neutrino interactions and nucleon decays of $^{12}\text{C}$ and $^{16}\text{O}$ based on TALYS

> Nuclear deexcitation associated with neutrino interactions and nucleon decays has a significant impact on a recent key observable for neutrino detectors: neutron multiplicity. However, most existing neutrino Monte Carlo event generators do not consider this process. For a comprehensive prediction of the neutron multiplicity, a nuclear deexcitation simulator based on TALYS named NucDeEx is developed. NucDeEx can be used for neutrino interactions and nucleon decayes for $^{12}\text{C}$ and $^{16}\text{O}$. The author provides the NucDeEx codes and sample codes for applying the NucDeex to widely-used neutrino Monte Carlo event generators, NEUT, NuWro, and GENIE.

## Key claim

The paper claims that NucDeEx, a new open-source nuclear deexcitation simulator based on TALYS, can predict neutron multiplicity from neutrino interactions and nucleon decays on $^{12}\text{C}$ and $^{16}\text{O}$ with sufficient accuracy for current and future neutrino detectors, but the validation shows agreement within about 15-20% for neutron branching ratios while significant discrepancies remain for triton emission and high-energy gamma rays.

## Method

The author developed NucDeEx, an event-by-event deexcitation simulator that uses pre-tabulated branching ratios calculated with TALYS version 1.96 (using the back-shifted Fermi gas level density model) for $s_{1/2}$-hole and multi-nucleon hole states, while using experimental data from Panin et al. (2016) and PhysRevC.48.1442 for $p_{3/2}$-hole states. The kinematics simulation uses ROOT libraries instead of Geant4 to minimize dependencies, computing particle emission isotropically in the center-of-mass frame with Lorentz boost to the laboratory frame, and the code is provided as an open-source package with sample interfaces for NEUT, NuWro, and GENIE.

## Key numbers

- For $^{11}\text{B}^*$ $p_{3/2}$-hole states: 82% go to ground state, 10% go to 2.13 MeV excited state, 8% go to 5.02 MeV excited state (from Panin et al. 2016)
- For $^{11}\text{C}^*$ $p_{3/2}$-hole states: 82% go to ground state, 10% go to 2.00 MeV excited state, 8% go to 4.80 MeV excited state (assumed by isospin symmetry)
- For $^{15}\text{N}^*$ $p_{3/2}$-hole states: 87% go to 6.32 MeV state, 6% go to 9.93 MeV state, 6% go to 10.70 MeV state (from PhysRevC.48.1442)
- At typical excitation energy of 23 MeV for $^{11}\text{B}^*$, neutron emission dominates at about 50%
- At typical excitation energy of 30 MeV for $^{15}\text{N}^*$, neutron emission dominates at about 50%
- NucDeEx predictions for $^{11}\text{B}^*$ neutron branching ratios agree with Panin et al. (2016) data within about 15%
- NucDeEx predictions for $^{15}\text{N}^*$ neutron branching ratios agree with Yosoi et al. (2004) data within about 20%
- For $^{15}\text{N}^*$ gamma rays: NucDeEx predicts 31.0% branching ratio for 3 MeV < $E_{\gamma,\text{tot}}$ < 6 MeV (experiment: $27.9\pm1.5^{+3.4}_{-2.6}$%) and 8.4% for $E_{\gamma,\text{tot}}$ > 6 MeV (experiment: $15.6\pm1.3^{+0.6}_{-1.0}$%)
- About 20$\pm$5% of $p_{3/2}$-hole states in $^{12}\text{C}$ are expected to have excitation energies of a few MeV due to nucleon-nucleon correlation
- Multi-nucleon holes are produced by FSI with a probability of about 30% in GeV-order neutrino reactions
- Residual nuclei tend to have about 200 MeV/c in momentum

## Context

This work extends previous deexcitation simulation studies using TALYS by Hu et al. (2022) and PhysRevD.103.053001, which were closed-source and limited to carbon targets for JUNO, by providing an open-source simulator applicable to both $^{12}\text{C}$ and $^{16}\text{O}$ for use with multiple neutrino generators. The predecessor simulator (Abe 2021, PhysRevD.107.072006) used Geant4 and was integrated only with NuWro, while NucDeEx uses ROOT and provides sample codes for NEUT, NuWro, and GENIE. The work enables comprehensive predictions of neutron multiplicity for ongoing detector upgrades such as Super-Kamiokande Gadolinium (Abe 2022) and ANNIE, which are critical for CP-phase measurements, diffuse supernova neutrino background searches, and nucleon decay searches.
