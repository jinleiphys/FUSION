# Thermal photon production in Au+Au collisions: viscous corrections in two different hydrodynamic formalisms

> We calculate the spectra of produced thermal photons in Au+Au collisions taking into account the nonequilibrium contribution to photon production due to finite shear viscosity. The evolution of the fireball is modeled by second-order as well as by divergence-type 2+1 dissipative hydrodynamics, both with an ideal equation of state and with one based on Lattice QCD that includes an analytical crossover. The spectrum calculated in the divergence-type theory is considerably enhanced with respect to the one calculated in the second-order theory, the difference being entirely due to differences in the viscous corrections to photon production. Our results show that the differences in hydrodynamic formalisms are an important source of uncertainty in the extraction of the value of $η/s$ from measured photon spectra. The uncertainty in the value of $η/s$ associated with different hydrodynamic models used to compute thermal photon spectra is larger than the one occurring in matching hadron elliptic flow to RHIC data.

# Thermal photon production in Au+Au collisions: viscous corrections in two different hydrodynamic formalisms

## Key claim

The uncertainty in extracting the shear viscosity-to-entropy ratio η/s from thermal photon spectra due to differences between second-order and divergence-type hydrodynamic formalisms is larger than the uncertainty arising from matching hadron elliptic flow to RHIC data. The divergence-type theory yields a considerably enhanced photon spectrum compared to the second-order theory, with the difference arising entirely from differences in the viscous corrections to photon production rather than from the hydrodynamic evolution itself.

## Method

The authors model the 2+1 dimensional boost-invariant evolution of the fireball created in Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV using both a second-order theory based on conformal invariance (generalizing Israel-Stewart) and a divergence-type theory that does not rely on velocity gradient expansions. They calculate thermal photon spectra from Compton scattering, qq̄-annihilation, and bremsstrahlung in the QGP phase, and from ππ → ργ, πρ → πγ, and ρ → ππγ in the hadron phase, incorporating viscous corrections to the distribution function via Grad's quadratic ansatz. The calculations are performed with both an ideal equation of state (p = ρ/3) and a realistic Lattice QCD-based equation of state featuring an analytic crossover between QGP and hadron gas.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature Ti = 333 MeV, freeze-out temperature Tf = 140 MeV, initialization time τ0 = 1 fm/c, critical temperature Tc = 170 MeV
- Impact parameter b = 3 fm for non-central Au+Au collisions
- Photon spectra calculated with η/s = 0.3 and η/s = 0.5
- Second-order transport coefficients from N=4 SYM: τπ = 2(2 - ln 2)η/(sT), λ1 = η/(2π T)
- Spectra using the realistic LQCD equation of state are two orders of magnitude larger than those using the ideal equation of state
- Differences between formalisms become appreciable at pT > 0.5 GeV and increase with increasing pT
- The spectrum from the divergence-type theory with η/s = 0.3 is very similar to that from the second-order theory with η/s = 0.5

## Context

This work extends earlier comparisons of second-order and divergence-type hydrodynamic formalisms for hadronic observables by Peralta-Ramos and Calzetta (2010) to thermal photon observables, which are sensitive to the full space-time evolution of the fireball. It builds on calculations of thermal photon spectra including viscous corrections by Dusling (2010) and Bhatt, Mishra, and Sreekanth (2010) using Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics, and on the finding by Baeuchle and Bleicher (2010) using a hydro-kinetic hybrid approach. The results enable future work to use combined photon and hadron observables from different hydrodynamic models to constrain the form of the nonequilibrium distribution function and improve freeze-out descriptions, though the authors note that bulk viscosity effects (known from Lattice QCD to become significant near Tc) and prompt photon contributions must be included for direct comparison with RHIC data from PHENIX.
