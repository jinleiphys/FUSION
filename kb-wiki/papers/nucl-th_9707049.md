# Theoretical Aspects of Science with Radioactive Nuclear Beams

> Physics of radioactive nuclear beams is one of the main frontiers of nuclear science today. Experimentally, thanks to technological developments, we are on the verge of invading the territory of extreme N/Z ratios in an unprecedented way. Theoretically, nuclear exotica represent a formidable challenge for the nuclear many-body theories and their power to predict nuclear properties in nuclear terra incognita. It is important to remember that the lesson learned by going to the limits of the nuclear binding is also important for normal nuclei from the neighborhood of the beta stability valley. And, of course, radioactive nuclei are crucial astrophysically; they pave the highway along which the nuclear material is transported up in the proton and neutron numbers during the complicated synthesis process in stars.

## Key claim

The paper argues that theoretical predictions for exotic nuclei far from stability are highly uncertain because the effective nucleon-nucleon interactions used in mean-field models are fitted to stable nuclei, and their isospin-dependent terms may be incorrect when extrapolated to extreme neutron-to-proton ratios; the authors emphasize that even reliable many-body methods like Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB) still carry large theoretical "error bars" due to the unknown isospin dependence of the force.

## Method

The authors review and compare several self-consistent mean-field approaches for describing exotic nuclei, focusing on the Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB) method with Skyrme-type effective interactions (SkP, SIII, SkM, SLy4) and the finite-range Gogny D1S interaction. They analyze neutron densities, pairing densities, two-neutron separation energies, and neutron radii for isotopic chains of tin, nickel, and semi-magic nuclei, contrasting HFB results with simpler HF+BCS calculations to demonstrate the necessity of proper continuum treatment. The paper also discusses the continuum shell model (CSM), Sturmian function expansions, Gamow states, and the continuum random phase approximation (CRPA) as tools for describing weakly bound systems.

## Key numbers

- There are only 263 stable nuclei; approximately 2,200 nuclei have been produced in laboratories, and several thousand more are unexplored exotic species.
- In HF+BCS calculations for nickel isotopes, the difference between HFB and HF+BCS neutron radii can be as large as 0.8 fm for neutron-rich systems.
- The predicted position of the neutron drip line for tin isotopes varies between N=118 (Gogny D1S) and N=126 (SIII interaction).
- The ground state of 11Be has a one-neutron separation energy of S_n=504 keV, with only one bound excited level (1/2- at 320 keV).
- The core 10Be has a quadrupole moment of |Q|=229 mb.

## Context

This review paper extends earlier work on nuclear structure far from stability, including the pioneering studies of shell quenching by Tondeur (1978) and later investigations by Dobaczewski (1994, 1995) and collaborators. It directly addresses the theoretical challenges raised by the experimental program of radioactive nuclear beams, which was then on the verge of exploring extreme N/Z ratios. The paper enables future systematic HFB-based calculations for r-process nucleosynthesis, particularly the need for microscopic quasiparticle RPA calculations using HFB densities to properly treat the particle continuum in weakly bound nuclei on the r-process path.
