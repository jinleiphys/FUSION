# Event-by-Event Jet Quenching

> High momentum jets and hadrons can be used as probes for the quark gluon plasma (QGP) formed in nuclear collisions at high energies. We investigate the influence of fluctuations in the fireball on jet quenching observables by comparing propagation of light quarks and gluons through averaged, smooth QGP fireballs with event-by-event jet quenching using realistic inhomogeneous fireballs. We find that the transverse momentum and impact parameter dependence of the nuclear modification factor R_AA can be fit well in an event-by-event quenching scenario within experimental errors. However the transport coefficient qhat extracted from fits to the measured nuclear modification factor R_AA in averaged fireballs underestimates the value from event-by-event calculations by up to 50%. On the other hand, after adjusting qhat to fit R_AA in the event-by-event analysis we find residual deviations in the azimuthal asymmetry v_2 and in two-particle correlations, that provide a possible faint signature for a spatial tomography of the fireball.

# Event-by-Event Jet Quenching

## Key claim

The use of averaged, smooth QGP fireballs in jet quenching calculations can underestimate the extracted transport coefficient qhat by up to 50% compared to event-by-event calculations using realistic inhomogeneous fireballs; however, after adjusting qhat to fit the nuclear modification factor R_AA in the event-by-event analysis, residual deviations in the azimuthal asymmetry v2 and in two-particle correlations provide a possible faint signature for spatial tomography of the fireball.

## Method

The authors generated an ensemble of realistic initial fireball distributions using the Glauber-based event generator GLISSANDO, taking the density of binary nucleon-nucleon collisions as both the distribution of hard processes and the fireball density. They propagated light quarks and gluons through these fireballs using the PPM code with two energy loss models: a deterministic LPM-inspired model (sLPM) and the non-deterministic Armesto-Salgado-Wiedemann (ASW) formalism, then compared the results to calculations using fireballs averaged over 500,000 events.

## Key numbers

- The transport coefficient qhat extracted from fits to R_AA in averaged fireballs underestimates the value from event-by-event calculations by up to 50% in the ASW model and up to 25% in the sLPM model.
- The fitted quenching strength parameter casw increased from 1.6 GeV (averaged fireballs) to 2.8 GeV (event-by-event) when fitting PHENIX neutral pion R_AA data at top RHIC energy.
- The fitted quenching strength parameter cslpm increased from 0.055 GeV (averaged fireballs) to 0.085 GeV (event-by-event).
- For impact parameters around b=11 fm, the residual suppression of v2 after adjusting quenching strength was about 25% at a transverse momentum of 4 GeV/c.
- The di-hadron correlation I_AA from event-by-event computations with refitted quenching strength was up to 25% smaller than for the averaged event.

## Context

This work extends prior studies by Bass et al. (2008) which reported a wide range of possible values for qhat (from 2.3 to 18.5 GeV^2/fm) depending on the energy loss model, and by Armesto et al. (2009) which showed that qhat extracted from single and di-hadron suppression factors are not necessarily compatible. The authors demonstrate that event-by-event fluctuations, previously recognized as crucial for low-PT observables like hydrodynamic elliptic flow by Miller et al. (2003), also significantly affect hard probe observables, and that a combination of R_AA, v2, and I_AA measurements could enable true spatial tomography of the fireball.
