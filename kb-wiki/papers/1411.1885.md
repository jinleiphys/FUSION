# Phase transitions in Core-Collapse Supernova Matter at sub-saturation densities

> We perform a three-dimensional, finite temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock study of inhomogeneous nuclear matter to determine the critical density and temperature for the phase transition between the pasta phase and homogeneous matter and its properties. We employ four different parametrizations of the Skyrme nuclear energy-density functional, SkM$^*$, SLy4, NRAPR and SQMC700, which span a range of saturation-density symmetry energy behaviours constrained by a variety of nuclear experimental probes. For each of these interactions we calculate free energy, pressure, entropy and chemical potentials in the range of particle number densities where the nuclear pasta phases are expected to exist, 0.02 - 0.12 fm$^{-3}$, temperatures 2 - 8 MeV and a proton fraction of 0.3. We find unambiguous evidence for a first-order phase transition to uniform matter, unsoftened by the presence of the pasta phases. No conclusive signs of first-order phase transition between the pasta phases is observed, and it is argued that the thermodynamic quantities vary continuously right up to the first-order phase transition to uniform matter. We compare our results with thermodynamic spinodals calculated using the same Skyrme parameterizations, finding that the effect of short-range Coulomb correlations and quantum shell effects included in our model leads to the pasta phases existing at densities up to 0.1 fm$^{-3}$ above the spinodal boundaries, thus increasing the transition density to uniform matter by the same amount. The transition density is otherwise shown to be insensitive to the symmetry energy at saturation density within the range constrained by the concordance of a variety of experimental constraints, and can be taken to be a well-determined quantity.

## Key claim
The paper finds unambiguous evidence for a first-order phase transition between the pasta phase and uniform matter, characterized by discontinuities in pressure, entropy, and chemical potentials. However, the authors note that no conclusive signs of first-order phase transitions between the different pasta phases themselves are observed, and any such transitions are likely weak at most.

## Method
The authors performed three-dimensional, finite-temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (3DSHF) calculations in cubic cells with periodic boundary conditions, allowing nuclear pasta shapes to emerge without bias. They used four Skyrme energy-density functional parametrizations (SkM*, SLy4, NRAPR, SQMC700) spanning the range of saturation-density symmetry energy slope L constrained by experimental probes (40 to 60 MeV). For each interaction, they calculated free energy, pressure, entropy, and chemical potentials at particle number densities 0.02 to 0.12 fm^-3, temperatures 2 to 8 MeV, and a fixed proton fraction of 0.3, and compared results with thermodynamic spinodal calculations using the same interactions.

## Key numbers
- Transition density for NRAPR at T=2 MeV: 0.110 fm^-3 (3DSHF) vs 0.099 fm^-3 (spinodal)
- Transition density for SQMC700 at T=2 MeV: 0.114 fm^-3 (3DSHF) vs 0.105 fm^-3 (spinodal)
- Transition density for SkM* at T=2 MeV: 0.109 fm^-3 (3DSHF) vs 0.100 fm^-3 (spinodal)
- Transition density for SLy4 at T=2 MeV: 0.111 fm^-3 (3DSHF) vs 0.099 fm^-3 (spinodal)
- At T=8 MeV, transition densities converge: NRAPR 0.091 fm^-3 (3DSHF) vs 0.088 fm^-3 (spinodal); SQMC700 0.099 fm^-3 (3DSHF) vs 0.094 fm^-3 (spinodal); SkM* 0.089 fm^-3 (3DSHF) vs 0.088 fm^-3 (spinodal); SLy4 0.094 fm^-3 (3DSHF) vs 0.089 fm^-3 (spinodal)
- The difference between transition densities for all Skyrme interactions at all temperatures is around 0.005 fm^-3
- The spinodal method underestimates transition densities by up to approximately 0.01 fm^-3 at the lowest temperatures
- The computational campaign used approximately 2.3 x 10^6 CPU hours on 45,000 processors

## Context
This work extends previous 3DSHF studies by Newton (2009) and Pais (2012) using the latest constraints on the Skyrme energy-density functional from Dutra (2012), who tested 240 Skyrme interaction parameter sets against nuclear experimental probes and neutron star mass constraints. The results contrast with statistical models such as Raduta (2010), which predict continuous phase transitions but do not include pasta phases, and with the Lattimer-Swesty equation of state (Lattimer 1991), which uses a Maxwell construction that removes the pressure discontinuity. The findings enable construction of four equations of state for supernova simulation models and provide neutron and proton density distributions that can be used to model neutrino transport through pasta formations, particularly relevant for understanding neutrino opacity during first-order phase transitions.
