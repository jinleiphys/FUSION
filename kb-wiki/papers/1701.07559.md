# Möbius domain-wall fermions on gradient-flowed dynamical HISQ ensembles

> We report on salient features of a mixed lattice QCD action using valence Möbius domain-wall fermions solved on the dynamical $N_f=2+1+1$ HISQ ensembles generated by the MILC Collaboration. The approximate chiral symmetry properties of the valence fermions are shown to be significantly improved by utilizing the gradient-flow scheme to first smear the HISQ configurations. The greater numerical cost of the Möbius domain-wall inversions is mitigated by the highly efficient QUDA library optimized for NVIDIA GPU accelerated compute nodes. We have created an interface to this optimized QUDA solver in Chroma. We provide tuned parameters of the action and performance of QUDA using ensembles with the lattice spacings $a \simeq \{0.15, 0.12, 0.09\}$ fm and pion masses $m_π\simeq \{310, 220,130\}$ MeV. We have additionally generated two new ensembles with $a\sim0.12$ fm and $m_π\sim\{400, 350\}$ MeV. With a fixed flow-time of $t_{gf}=1$ in lattice units, the residual chiral symmetry breaking of the valence fermions is kept below 10\% of the light quark mass on all ensembles, $m_{res} \lesssim 0.1\times m_l$, with moderate values of the fifth dimension $L_5$ and a domain-wall height $M_5 \leq 1.3$. As a benchmark calculation, we perform a continuum, infinite volume, physical pion and kaon mass extrapolation of $F_{K^\pm}/F_{π^\pm}$ and demonstrate our results are independent of flow-time, and consistent with the FLAG determination of this quantity at the level of less than one standard deviation.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that using gradient-flow smearing on dynamical HISQ ensembles significantly improves the chiral symmetry properties of valence Möbius domain-wall fermions, with the residual chiral symmetry breaking kept below 10% of the light quark mass on all ensembles, but this result is conditional on using a fixed flow-time of \(t_{gf}=1\) in lattice units and moderate values of the fifth dimension \(L_5\) and domain-wall height \(M_5 \leq 1.3\).

## Method

The authors implement a mixed lattice QCD action using valence Möbius domain-wall fermions solved on dynamical \(N_f=2+1+1\) HISQ ensembles generated by the MILC Collaboration, with the HISQ configurations first smeared using the gradient-flow scheme at a fixed flow-time of \(t_{gf}=1\) in lattice units. The Möbius domain-wall inversions are performed using the QUDA library optimized for NVIDIA GPU accelerated compute nodes, with an interface created in Chroma. The valence quark masses are tuned such that the pseudoscalar meson masses from the domain-wall action match those from the HISQ action within 2% or less.

## Key numbers

* Residual chiral symmetry breaking is kept below 10% of the light quark mass on all ensembles: \(m_{res} \lesssim 0.1 \times m_l\)
* The action is tuned on ensembles with lattice spacings \(a \simeq \{0.15, 0.12, 0.09\}\) fm and pion masses \(m_\pi \simeq \{310, 220, 130\}\) MeV
* Two new ensembles were generated with \(a \sim 0.12\) fm and \(m_\pi \sim \{400, 350\}\) MeV
* The domain-wall height is kept at \(M_5 \leq 1.3\) on all ensembles
* The benchmark calculation of \(F_{K^\pm}/F_{\pi^\pm}\) is consistent with the FLAG determination at the level of less than one standard deviation

## Context

This work extends the successful mixed-action approach previously used by the LHP Collaboration, which employed domain-wall valence fermions on asqtad staggered ensembles (Renner, 2004) and enabled numerous calculations including the first dynamical calculation of the nucleon axial charge (Edwards, 2005). The use of gradient-flow smearing instead of HYP smearing (Hasenfratz, 2001) provides improved suppression of residual chiral symmetry breaking, and the Möbius domain-wall action (Brower, 2012) offers reduced residual chiral symmetry breaking at fixed fifth-dimensional extent compared to the Shamir kernel. This action has already been used for calculations of neutrinoless double beta decay matrix elements (Nicholson, 2016) and the nucleon axial charge (Berkowitz, 2017).
