# Accessing high-momentum nucleons with dilute stochastic sources

> A novel stochastic technique combining a dilute source grid of $\mathbb{Z}_3$ noise with iterative momentum-smearing is used to study the proton correlation function at rest and in boosted frames on two lattice volumes. The technique makes use of the baryonic version of the so-called one-end trick, and the decomposition into signal and noise terms of the resulting stochastic proton correlation function is made explicit. The number and location of the source points in the dilute grid should be chosen so that the benefits of averaging over many locations overcomes the additional statistical error introduced by the noise terms in the desired fitting region. At all nontrivial momentum values considered we find that the choice of $N=4$--$8$ maximally separated source locations is shown to be optimal, providing a reduced statistical error when compared with a single point source. This enables us to successfully fit the proton energy at momentum values as high as $|\vec{p}| \simeq 3.75$ GeV and $|\vec{p}| \simeq 2.82$ GeV on the small and large volume respectively.

## Key claim

A dilute stochastic source grid of $\mathbb{Z}_3$ noise combined with iterative momentum-smearing can improve the statistical signal for high-momentum nucleon states, but only when the number of source points is limited to N=4-8 maximally separated locations; beyond this range, the stochastic noise from cross-terms overwhelms the averaging benefit.

## Method

The authors constructed stochastic propagators using a dilute grid of $\mathbb{Z}_3$ noise sources, where only N spatially separated sites on a fixed timeslice have non-zero support, and applied iterative momentum-smearing phases to these sources. They computed proton two-point correlation functions on two lattice volumes (24^3x48 and 32^3x64) using the baryonic one-end trick, explicitly decomposing the correlator into signal terms (sum over N source points of standard point-source correlators) and noise terms (products of noise vectors at distinct locations). The optimal number and spacing of source points was determined by comparing the statistical errors in the correlation function and fitted proton energies across different momentum values.

## Key numbers

- Maximum fitted proton momentum achieved: |p| ~ 3.75 GeV on the 24^3x48 lattice and |p| ~ 2.82 GeV on the 32^3x64 lattice
- Optimal number of source locations: N=4-8 maximally separated points, providing reduced statistical error compared to a single point source at all non-trivial momentum values
- Lattice parameters: inverse lattice spacing a^{-1} ~ 2.76 GeV, with pion masses m_pi = 902 MeV (24^3x48, 376 configurations) and m_pi = 295 MeV (32^3x64, 1000 configurations)
- For N=2 sources at rest frame P=(0,0,0), the ideal error reduction factor of 1/sqrt(2) was not achieved for any studied source separation; for boosted frame P=(1,1,2), the error dropped rapidly with separation and plateaued at or below the 1/sqrt(2) line
- At momentum P=(1,1,2) on the 24^3x48 lattice, N=4 and N=8 source locations provided smaller errors than N=1, 2, 27, or 64 source locations

## Context

This work extends the momentum-smearing technique of Bali et al. (2016) by combining it with dilute stochastic sources, building on the baryonic one-end trick formalism. The approach enables access to higher nucleon momenta than previously achievable, which is relevant for lattice QCD calculations of hadron form factors at large momentum transfer, quasi-PDFs as proposed by Ji (2013), and studies of parton distributions via the Compton amplitude as suggested by Chambers et al. (2017). The technique enables future calculations of nucleon structure observables requiring extrapolation to infinite momentum, such as the gluon spin contribution to the proton spin puzzle.
