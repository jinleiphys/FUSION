# Femto-cyclones and hyperon polarization in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We study the structure of vorticity and hydrodynamic helicity fields in peripheral heavy-ion collisions using the kinetic Quark-Gluon String Model. The angular momentum conservation within this model holds with a good accuracy. We observe the formation of specific toroidal structures of vorticity field (vortex sheets). Their existence is mirrored in the polarization of hyperons of the percent order.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in noncentral heavy-ion collisions, the vorticity field forms specific toroidal "tire-like" structures (vortex sheets) with cylindrical symmetry, and that these structures produce hyperon polarization at the percent level; however, the polarization magnitude depends on the calculation method, with the thermal vorticity approach giving values several times larger than the axial-current approach.

## Method

The authors performed numerical simulations using the kinetic Quark-Gluon String Model (QGSM) for Au+Au collisions at a center-of-mass energy of 5 GeV per nucleon and impact parameter b = 8 fm. They calculated velocity, vorticity, and hydrodynamic helicity fields by averaging over events and particles within three-dimensional cells to transition from kinetic to hydrodynamic description. Hyperon polarization was computed using two distinct methods: one based on the induced strange axial current and the other based on thermal vorticity.

## Key numbers

- Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s) = 5 GeV/u with impact parameter b = 8 fm
- Participants carry about 20% of the total angular momentum
- Hubble constant H = 0.024 to 0.028 (fm/c)^(-1), corresponding to a "little Universe" lifetime of about 40 fm/c
- Vorticity field localized in a relatively thin layer of 2 to 3 fm on the boundary between participant and spectator nucleons
- Hyperon polarization magnitude reaches a percent level

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier studies (Baznat 2013) on vorticity and helicity in heavy-ion collisions, confirming the phenomenon of helicity separation relative to the reaction plane. The paper connects to the broader research program on the Chiral Vortical Effect (CVE) and axial vortical effect, as discussed by Kharzeev (2007), Rogachevsky (2010), and Kalaydzhyan (2014). The results enable future experimental searches for hyperon polarization at facilities like NICA, and the comparison between the axial-current and thermal vorticity approaches (Becattini 2013) provides a framework for interpreting upcoming measurements.
