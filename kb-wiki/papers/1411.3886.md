# Large-scale shell-model calculations for unnatural-parity high-spin states in neutron-rich Cr and Fe isotopes

> We investigate unnatural-parity high-spin states in neutron-rich Cr and Fe isotopes using large-scale shell-model calculations. These shell-model calculations are carried out within the model space of $fp$-shell + $0g_{9/2}$ + $1d_{5/2}$ orbits with the truncation allowing $1\hbarω$ excitation of a neutron. The effective Hamiltonian consists of GXPF1Br for $fp$-shell orbits and $V_{\rm MU}$ with a modification for the other parts. The present shell-model calculations can describe and predict the energy levels of both natural- and unnatural-parity states up to the high-spin states in Cr and Fe isotopes with $N\le35$. The total energy surfaces present the prolate deformations on the whole and indicate that the excitation of one neutron into the $0g_{9/2}$ orbit plays the role of enhancing the prolate deformation. For the positive(unnatural)-parity states in odd-mass Cr and Fe isotopes, their energy levels and prolate deformations indicate the decoupling limit of the particle-plus-rotor model. The sharp drop of the $9/2_{1}^{+}$ levels in going from $N=29$ to $N=35$ in odd-mass Cr and Fe isotopes is explained by the Fermi surface approaching the $ν0g_{9/2}$ orbit.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that large-scale shell-model calculations within a model space including the $fp$-shell plus $0g_{9/2}$ and $1d_{5/2}$ orbits, with a truncation allowing only one neutron to be excited across the $N=40$ shell gap, can reproduce the observed energy levels of unnatural-parity high-spin states in neutron-rich Cr and Fe isotopes up to $N\le35$, with the caveat that the highest-spin $37/2_{1}^{+}$ state in $^{57}$Cr shows a backbending not well reproduced, indicating a limitation of the present model space and truncation.

## Method

Large-scale shell-model calculations were performed using the MSHELL64 and KSHELL codes to diagonalize Hamiltonian matrices with dimensions up to $1.8 \times 10^{10}$. The model space comprised $fp$-shell orbits ($0f_{7/2}$, $0f_{5/2}$, $1p_{3/2}$, $1p_{1/2}$) plus $0g_{9/2}$ and $1d_{5/2}$ orbits, with a truncation allowing only $1\hbar\omega$ excitation of a single neutron to the $gds$-shell for unnatural-parity states. The effective Hamiltonian combined GXPF1Br for the $fp$-shell part with a modified version of $V_{\rm MU}$ for cross-shell interactions, where two-body matrix elements $\langle 0g_{9/2}0f_{5/2}|V|0g_{9/2}0f_{5/2}\rangle_{J=2,3,T=1}$ were reduced by 1.0 MeV and those for $J=6,7,T=1$ were increased by 1.0 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The bare single-particle energy of $0g_{9/2}$ (and $1d_{5/2}$) was set to $0.793$ MeV, fitted to reproduce the $9/2_{1}^{+}$ excitation energies in $^{55,57,59}$Cr.
- The modified Hamiltonian reproduced experimental data within approximately $500$ keV for $^{57}$Cr.
- The intrinsic quadrupole moments at energy minima for negative-parity states were around $100$ fm$^{2}$, while for positive-parity states they were enlarged to around $120$ fm$^{2}$, indicating a $\sim 20\%$ increase in deformation due to one-neutron excitation into the $0g_{9/2}$ orbit.
- The calculated magnetic moment of the $9/2_{1}^{+}$ state in $^{61}$Fe was $-0.934\mu_{N}$, close to the experimental value of $-1.031(9)\mu_{N}$.
- The maximum $M$-scheme dimension reached $1.8 \times 10^{10}$ for $^{59}$Ni.

## Context

This work extends systematic shell-model studies of neutron-rich nuclei in the $fp$-shell region, building on the GXPF1A, GXPF1B, and GXPF1Br effective interactions developed by Honma et al. (2004, 2012) and the $V_{\rm MU}$ interaction of Otsuka et al. (2010). It addresses the breakdown of the $N=40$ shell gap in Cr and Fe isotopes, analogous to the island of inversion around $^{32}$Mg, and clarifies the shape evolution debate for unnatural-parity states where oblate deformation had been suggested for $^{59}$Cr by Zhu et al. (2006) but prolate deformation was supported by projected shell-model calculations. The results enable future studies of the $\nu 0g_{9/2}$ orbit evolution and provide a benchmark for extending calculations to higher neutron numbers and heavier isotopes.
