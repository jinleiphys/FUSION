# Neutron star crust properties: comparison between the compressible liquid-drop model and the extended Thomas-Fermi approach

> We present a detailed analysis of three models predicting the properties of non-uniform matter in the crust of neutron stars: the compressible liquid-drop model, the fourth order Extended Thomas Fermi (ETF) method, and ETF plus Strutinsky integral (ETFSI) correction. The former treats the nuclear clusters as uniform hard spheres, the second takes into account the density distribution which can be different for neutrons and protons, and the last one includes the proton shell effects within the Strutinsky approach. The purpose of this work is to understand the importance of the improvements in the nuclear modeling and to analyze the quantities which are the most sensitive to them. We find that thermodynamic quantities such as pressure, energy and chemical potential, as well as the electron fraction, are in very good agreement among the three models. This confirms previous results where we have shown that the improvement in the finite-size description of the nuclear clusters has a small impact on these quantities, since they are mainly constrained by the bulk properties. The refinements in the finite-size modeling are shown to impact mostly the composition of the nuclear clusters ($Z_{cl}$, $N_{cl}$) in an ordering which ranks according to the leptodermous expansion. This analysis is performed considering both the r-cluster and the e-cluster representations. The proton shell effects are shown to stabilize $Z_{cl}$, which consequently impacts the neutron number $N_{cl}$ as well.

## Key claim

The compressible liquid-drop model (CLDM), fourth-order Extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) method, and ETF plus Strutinsky integral (ETFSI) correction produce very good agreement for thermodynamic quantities (pressure, energy, chemical potential, electron fraction) when using the same Skyrme interaction BSk24, but the refinements in finite-size modeling impact mostly the composition of nuclear clusters (Z_cl, N_cl) in an ordering that ranks according to the leptodermous expansion.

## Method

The authors constructed a CLDM based on the nuclear meta-model (MM) adjusted to reproduce the uniform matter predictions of the BSk24 Skyrme interaction, with finite-size terms (Coulomb, surface, curvature) fitted to experimental nuclear masses from the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation. They compared CLDM predictions for neutron star crust properties against ETF and ETFSI calculations using the same BSk24 interaction, varying the surface parameter p_surf in the range [2.9, 3.1, 3.3, 3.5] and considering both the r-representation (cluster as hard sphere) and e-representation (neutron fluid overlaps cluster) of the Wigner-Seitz cell.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density n_sat = 0.1578 fm^-3 for BSk24
- Saturation energy E_sat = -16.048 MeV and symmetry energy E_sym = 30.0 MeV for BSk24
- Symmetry energy slope L_sym = 46.3967 MeV and incompressibility K_sat = 245.52 MeV for BSk24
- Effective mass m*_sat = 0.80 and effective mass splitting Delta m* = 0.21 for BSk24
- Surface tension parameter sigma_surf,sat ranges from 1.113 to 1.185 across FS models, with chi_E (standard deviation from experimental masses) ranging from 2.66 to 3.27
- Optimal p_surf value to reproduce ETF predictions for Z_cl is approximately 3.1, while for N_cl it is approximately 3.3
- Proton shell effects stabilize Z_cl to Z=40 for BSk24 across almost the entire inner crust

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Grams et al. (2022a, 2022b) and Carreau et al. (2019a, 2019b) that established the CLDM based on the nuclear meta-model as a tool for investigating uncertainties in neutron star crust predictions. The analysis confirms that finite-size terms play a minor role compared to bulk properties for thermodynamic quantities, as anticipated in earlier work, but shows that the surface parameter p_surf can be fine-tuned to achieve agreement between CLDM and ETF predictions for cluster composition. The results enable construction of unified equations of state for neutron stars and identify that the largest remaining uncertainties originate from the unknown nuclear interaction rather than the modeling approach.
