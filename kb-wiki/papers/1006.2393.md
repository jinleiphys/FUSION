# Efimov Physics in Atom-Dimer Scattering of Lithium-6 Atoms

> Lithium-6 atoms in the three lowest hyperfine states display universal properties when the S-wave scattering length between each pair of states is large. Recent experiments reported four pronounced features arising from Efimov physics in the atom-dimer relaxation rate, namely two resonances and two local minima. We use the universal effective field theory to calculate the atom-dimer relaxation rate at zero temperature. Our results describe the four features qualitatively and imply there is a hidden local minimum. In the vicinity of the resonance at 685 G, we perform a finite temperature calculation which improves the agreement of theory and experiment. We conclude that finite temperature effects cannot be neglected in the analysis of the experimental data.

# Efimov Physics in Atom-Dimer Scattering of Lithium-6 Atoms

## Key claim

The universal zero-range effective field theory describes the four observed features (two resonances and two local minima) in the atom-dimer relaxation rate of lithium-6 atoms only qualitatively at zero temperature; finite temperature effects near the 685 G resonance improve agreement with experiment but cannot resolve the discrepancy between the width parameter extracted from three-body recombination (η* = 0.016) and the much larger value required by atom-dimer relaxation data (η* = 0.34).

## Method

The authors solved the Skorniakov–Ter-Martirosian (STM) integral equation for three fermions in the three lowest hyperfine states of lithium-6, using zero-range interactions with an ultraviolet cutoff Λ = 456 a0⁻¹ and a complex width parameter η* = 0.016 determined from three-body recombination measurements. They calculated the atom-dimer relaxation rate constants βi from the T-matrix elements via the optical theorem, separating contributions from relaxation into shallow dimers and deep dimers, and performed an approximate finite temperature calculation near the 685 G resonance using a Boltzmann thermal average of the inelastic cross section derived from the atom-dimer scattering length approximation.

## Key numbers

- The resonance position predicted by the zero-temperature calculation is B* = 672 G, while the experimentally measured resonance is at B*exp = 685 G, corresponding to a 30% shift in scattering length: a23(B*exp)/a23(B*) = 1076 a0 / 835 a0 ≈ 1.3
- The width parameter extracted from three-body recombination is η* = 0.016, while atom-dimer relaxation data require η* = 0.34, more than an order of magnitude larger
- The cutoff parameter used is Λ = 456 a0⁻¹, with an alternative value Λ = 329 a0⁻¹ reproducing the experimental resonance position at 685 G
- The van der Waals length for lithium-6 is ℓvdW ≈ 65 a0, with the universal region defined for magnetic fields B > 608 G where all scattering lengths are at least two times larger than ℓvdW
- Finite temperature effects for η* = 0.016 suppress the peak height by an order of magnitude at T = 60 ± 15 nK; for η* = 0.34, the suppression is about 25%
- The discrete scaling factor for identical bosons is eπ/s0 ≈ 22.7 with s0 ≈ 1.00624
- The binding energy of a shallow dimer is ħ²/(m aij²) for positive scattering length aij

## Context

This work extends the universal effective field theory approach to atom-dimer scattering in the three-component fermionic system of lithium-6, following predictions by Braaten et al. (2009) of Efimov trimer crossings with the atom-dimer threshold. The calculations are compared with experimental data from Lompe et al. (2010) and Nakajima et al. (2010), who observed two resonances at 603 G and 685 G in the relaxation rate β1 and two local minima at 610 G and 695 G in β3. The discrepancy between the width parameter from three-body recombination and atom-dimer relaxation mirrors a similar discrepancy observed in bosonic cesium-133 systems by Knoop et al. (2008) and Helfrich et al. (2009). The results enable future experimental separation of shallow and deep dimer relaxation channels by tuning trap depths, and motivate full finite temperature calculations beyond the approximate treatment presented here.
