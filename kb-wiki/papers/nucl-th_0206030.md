# Signatures of Nucleon Disappearance in Large Underground Detectors

> For neutrons bound inside nuclei, baryon instability can manifest itself as a decay into undetectable particles (e.g., $\it n \to νν\barν $), i.e., as a disappearance of a neutron from its nuclear state. If electric charge is conserved, a similar disappearance is impossible for a proton. The existing experimental lifetime limit for neutron disappearance is 4-7 orders of magnitude lower than the lifetime limits with detectable nucleon decay products in the final state [PDG2000]. In this paper we calculated the spectrum of nuclear de-excitations that would result from the disappearance of a neutron or two neutrons from $^{12}$C. We found that some de-excitation modes have signatures that are advantageous for detection in the modern high-mass, low-background, and low-threshold underground detectors, where neutron disappearance would result in a characteristic sequence of time- and space-correlated events. Thus, in the KamLAND detector [Kamland], a time-correlated triple coincidence of a prompt signal, a captured neutron, and a $β^{+}$ decay of the residual nucleus, all originating from the same point in the detector, will be a unique signal of neutron disappearance allowing searches for baryon instability with sensitivity 3-4 orders of magnitude beyond the present experimental limits.

## Key claim

For neutrons bound inside nuclei, baryon instability can manifest as a disappearance into undetectable particles (e.g., n → ννν̅), and the authors calculate that specific de-excitation modes of the residual nucleus after such a disappearance from ¹²C produce a unique triple-coincidence signature in large liquid scintillator detectors, potentially enabling searches with sensitivity 3-4 orders of magnitude beyond current experimental limits, but only if the disappearing neutron originates from a particular nuclear shell and the de-excitation proceeds through specific channels.

## Method

The authors used the statistical model of compound nuclear reactions (Hauser-Feshbach formalism) implemented in the SMOKER code to calculate branching ratios for de-excitation of ¹¹C* following the disappearance of a neutron from the s₁/₂ or p₃/₂ shell in ¹²C. They assumed an excitation energy of 23 ± 1 MeV with a Lorentzian width of 7 MeV for the s₁/₂ hole state, and propagated the cascade through up to two emission steps of neutrons, protons, alphas, and gammas, tracking the residual radioactive daughter nuclei.

## Key numbers

- For neutron disappearance from the p₃/₂ shell in ¹²C, the probability of leaving ¹¹C in its first excited state (2.0 MeV gamma emission followed by β⁺ decay with half-life 20.4 min and Q_EC = 1.98 MeV) is 20 ± 5% per P-shell neutron.
- For neutron disappearance from the s₁/₂ shell in ¹²C, the branching ratio for the triple-coincidence channel ¹¹C* → n + γ + ¹⁰C_gs (with ¹⁰C β⁺ decay, half-life 19.3 s, Q = 3.65 MeV) is 2.8% per s₁/₂ neutron.
- The branching ratio for the channel ¹¹C* → n + ¹⁰C_gs is 3.0% per s₁/₂ neutron.
- The branching ratio for the four-hit channel ¹¹C* → n + n + ⁹C_gs (⁹C β⁺ decay, half-life 0.127 s, Q = 16.5 MeV) is 0.06% per s₁/₂ neutron.
- The integrated gamma de-excitation branching ratio from the s₁/₂ hole in ¹²C in the energy range 19-50 MeV is 1.1 × 10⁻⁴, similar to the (0.27-1.04) × 10⁻⁴ range calculated for ¹⁶O by Suzuki (1993).
- The existing experimental lifetime limit for neutron disappearance (n → 3ν) from Kamiokande is τ ≥ 4.9 × 10²⁶ years (Suzuki 1993), and the mode-independent nucleon lifetime limit is ≥ 1.6 × 10²⁵ years (Evans 1977).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Totsuka (1986) and Ejiri (1993) for detecting nucleon disappearance via nuclear de-excitation, and directly builds on the Kamiokande Collaboration method (Suzuki 1993) that searched for gamma rays from the s₁/₂ hole in ¹⁶O. The authors provide the first detailed calculation of de-excitation branching ratios for neutron disappearance from ¹²C, the dominant carbon isotope in liquid scintillator detectors like KamLAND, and identify specific multi-hit signatures that are far more background-rejecting than the single-gamma signature used previously. This enables next-generation searches that could improve neutron disappearance limits by 3-4 orders of magnitude beyond the current best limits from Kamiokande and the Fréjus Collaboration (Berger 1991).
