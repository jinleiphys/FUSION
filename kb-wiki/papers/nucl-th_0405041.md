# Continuum corrections to the level density and its dependence on excitation energy, n-p asymmetry, and deformation

> In the independent-particle model, the nuclear level density is determined from the neutron and proton single-particle level densities. The single-particle level density for the positive-energy continuum levels is important at high excitation energies for stable nuclei and at all excitation energies for nuclei near the drip lines. This single-particle level density is subdivided into compound-nucleus and gas components. Two methods were considered for this subdivision. First in the subtraction method, the single-particle level density is determined from the scattering phase shifts. In the Gamov method, only the narrow Gamov states or resonances are included. The level densities calculated with these two methods are similar, both can be approximated by the backshifted Fermi-gas expression with level-density parameters that are dependent on A, but with very little dependence on the neutron or proton richness of the nucleus. However, a small decrease in the level-density parameter was predicted for some nuclei very close to the drip lines. The largest difference between the calculations using the two methods was the deformation dependence on the level density. The Gamov method predicts a very strong peaking of the level density at sphericity for high excitation energies. This leads to a suppression of deformed configurations and, consequently, the fission rate predicted by the statistical model is reduced in the Gamov method.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the continuum of positive-energy single-particle states significantly influences the nuclear level density, and that two methods for subdividing the total single-particle level density into compound-nucleus and gas components (the subtraction method and the Gamov method) produce similar results for the level density's dependence on excitation energy and neutron-proton asymmetry, but differ substantially in their predictions for the deformation dependence, with the Gamov method predicting a strong peaking of the level density at sphericity at high excitation energies.

## Method

The authors calculated the nuclear level density within the independent-particle model using single-particle level densities obtained from a deformed axially-symmetric mean-field potential (nuclear, Coulomb, and spin-orbit components) with the "universal" parametrization of Dudek (1982). They solved the coupled-channels Schrodinger equation to obtain bound states, Gamov states, and scattering phase shifts, then computed the compound-nucleus single-particle level density via two approaches: the subtraction method (using scattering phase shifts via Eq. 6) and the Gamov method (including only narrow Gamov states with an exponential cutoff on their widths via Eq. 7). The level density was then obtained from the saddle-point approximation to the Laplace transform of the combinatorial problem.

## Key numbers

- The level-density parameter a = (pi^2/6)[g_n(mu_n) + g_p(mu_p)] is the standard two-component Fermi-gas expression used as a reference.
- The Fermi function changes from 90% to 10% occupancy over an interval Delta epsilon = 4.4T centered around the chemical potential.
- The function s (entropy contribution per level) has a full width at half maximum of 4.2T.
- The lowest-order correction to the level-density parameter gives a' = a[1 + (7g(mu_0)g''(mu_0) - 5g'(mu_0)^2)/(5g(mu_0)^3) * E*], showing the excitation-energy dependence.
- For the subtraction method, the single-particle level density g_CN^sub for neutrons in a spherical potential is negative between resonance peaks for epsilon < 6 MeV.
- The Gamov method uses an exponential cutoff exp(-Gamma_i^R / Gamma_0) on Gamov state widths to avoid discontinuities with deformation.
- The convolution function F for the subtraction method level density was a Gaussian with FWHM = 150 keV.
- The coupled-channels calculations included all channels with orbital angular momentum l <= 20.
- A quadrupole moment Q = 3.2 corresponds to a "superdeformed" prolate shape with major and minor axes differing by a factor of 2.

## Context

This work extends the independent-particle model treatment of the nuclear level density by focusing on the role of positive-energy continuum states, which had been suggested by Al-Quraishi (2001) to produce a neutron-proton asymmetry dependence. The subtraction method was originally proposed by Fowler, Engelbrecht, and Woosley (1978) and has been used by many subsequent investigators, while the Gamov method follows the suggestion of Weidenmuller (1964) and Mustafa (1992) that only resonances with lifetimes comparable to the compound-nucleus lifetime should be included. The results enable improved statistical-model calculations of nuclear decay, particularly for nuclei near the drip lines where continuum contributions are important at all excitation energies, and for fission calculations where the deformation dependence of the level density is critical.
