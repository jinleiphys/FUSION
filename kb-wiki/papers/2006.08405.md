# Analytic insights on the information content of new observables

> Uncertainty quantification has emerged as a rapidly growing field in nuclear science. Theoretical predictions of physical observables often involve extrapolations to regions that are poorly constrained by laboratory experiments and astrophysical observations. Without properly quantified theoretical errors, such model predictions are of limited value. Also, one often deals with theoretical constructs that involve fundamental quantities that are not accessible to experiment or observation. Particularly relevant in this context is the pressure of pure neutron matter. In this contribution we develop an analytic framework to answer the question of "How can new data reduce uncertainties of current theoretical models?" [P.-G. Reinhard and W. Nazarewicz, Phys. Rev. C81, 051303(R) (2010)]. Simple and insightful expressions are obtained to quantify the impact of one or two new observables on theoretical uncertainties in two critical quantities: the slope of the symmetry energy at saturation density and the pressure of pure neutron matter at twice nuclear matter saturation density.

# Analytic insights on the information content of new observables

## Key claim

The paper develops an analytic framework to quantify how new experimental data can reduce theoretical uncertainties in quantities that are not directly observable, such as the slope of the symmetry energy at saturation density and the pressure of pure neutron matter at twice saturation density. The central assumption is that the minimum of the chi-square function remains unchanged after incorporating new observables, which allows closed-form expressions for the posterior uncertainty reduction.

## Method

The authors start from a chi-square minimization framework for nuclear energy density functionals calibrated to experimental observables. They derive analytic expressions for the posterior variance of a target quantity after adding one or two new observables, using the Sherman-Morrison-Woodbury formula to invert the augmented curvature matrix. The formalism is applied to two case studies using the covariant energy density functional FSUGold2: (1) the impact of improved neutron radius measurements of 208Pb and 48Ca on the slope of the symmetry energy L, and (2) the impact of combined neutron star radius and neutron skin measurements on the pressure of pure neutron matter at twice saturation density.

## Key numbers

- The PREX-II campaign aims to reach a precision of 0.06 fm on the neutron radius of 208Pb (Rn208)
- The CREX campaign aims to determine the neutron radius of 48Ca (Rn48) with 0.03 fm precision
- The planned MREX experiment at MESA aims to determine Rn208 with 0.03 fm precision and Rn48 with 0.02 fm precision
- FSUGold2 predicts correlation coefficients: rho(Rn208,Rn48)=0.963, rho(L,Rn208)=0.966, rho(L,Rn48)=0.921
- The theoretical uncertainty in the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is set to 1.2 km
- FSUGold2 predicts: rho(R1.4,Rn208)=0.968, rho(P2,R1.4)=0.996, rho(P2,Rn208)=0.966
- A 0.5 km determination of the stellar radius of J0437-4715 would reduce the theoretical uncertainty in P2 by about 60%
- A "perfect" measurement of the stellar radius could reduce the theoretical uncertainty in P2 by nearly 90%

## Context

This work extends the framework of Reinhard and Nazarewicz (2010), who posed the question "How can new data reduce uncertainties of current theoretical models?" and addressed it numerically. The present paper provides fully analytic expressions that complement the numerical Bayesian inference approach. The formalism is applied to upcoming parity-violating electron scattering experiments (PREX-II, CREX, MREX at MESA) and neutron star observations from NICER and LIGO-Virgo, showing how combined measurements of neutron skins and stellar radii can constrain the equation of state. The authors note that while the strong correlation between the neutron skin of 208Pb and L is well established, the correlation between neutron star radius and the pressure of pure neutron matter at twice saturation density is model dependent, with FSUGarnet predicting a correlation coefficient of only 0.4.
