# Chiral perturbation theory

> We give a brief introduction to chiral perturbation theory in its various settings. We discuss some applications of recent interest including chiral extrapolations for lattice gauge theory.

## Key claim

Chiral perturbation theory (CHPT) provides a model-independent effective field theory framework for analyzing the low-energy consequences of spontaneous, explicit, and anomalous chiral symmetry breaking in QCD, but its predictive power relies on a dual expansion in small external momenta and small quark masses, with the low-energy constants (LECs) that appear at higher orders not determined by chiral symmetry itself and requiring phenomenological input or lattice QCD for their determination.

## Method

The authors construct the chiral effective Lagrangian by mapping QCD onto a theory of Goldstone boson fields (pions, kaons, eta) coupled to external sources, using the power counting scheme of Weinberg (1978) where the chiral dimension D = 2 + Σ N_d (d-2) + 2L orders contributions from vertices of dimension d and L loops. They employ dimensional regularization to handle loop divergences, absorbing them into renormalized LECs, and illustrate the formalism with a one-loop calculation of the pion scalar form factor in chiral SU(2).

## Key numbers

- Physical pion decay constant: F_π = 92.4 MeV, determined from pion decay π → νμ.
- Pion scalar radius: ⟨r_S²⟩ = (0.61 ± 0.02) fm², compared to the vector radius ⟨r_V²⟩ = (0.452 ± 0.013) fm².
- The LEC L_9, estimated from ρ-meson saturation as L_9 = F_π²/(2M_ρ²) ≈ 7.2 × 10⁻³, close to the empirical value L_9 = 6.9 × 10⁻³ at λ = M_ρ.
- The isovector Dirac radius of the proton at third order in the chiral expansion: ⟨r²⟩_1^V = (0.61 − (0.47 GeV⁻²) d̃(λ) + 0.47 log(λ/1 GeV)) fm², where d̃(λ) is a dimension-three pion-nucleon LEC.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on chiral perturbation theory by Gasser and Leutwyler (1983, 1984) and Weinberg (1978, 1994), covering both SU(2) and SU(3) formulations. It discusses the resonance saturation approach pioneered by Ecker, Gasser, Pich, and de Rafael (1988, 1989) and Donoghue, Holstein, and collaborators (1988) for estimating LECs, and addresses the combination of CHPT with dispersive methods as argued by Truong (1987) and Donoghue (1993, 1995). The paper enables chiral extrapolations for lattice QCD calculations, which are typically performed at unphysically large quark masses, and provides the theoretical foundation for two-loop calculations reviewed by Bijnens (2006).
