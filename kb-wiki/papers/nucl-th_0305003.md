# Supernova Neutrinos

> I describe how the signals corresponding to the supernova $ν_e$ and $\barν_e$ (charged current reactions) as well as all active neutrinos (neutral current reactions) can be separately observed in various existing detectors. These observations would make it possible to determine the flux and average energy (or temperature) for each of these three neutrino signal components. I argue that all these quantities are needed in order to understand the interplay between the so far poorly known supernova neutrino emission process and the neutrino oscillations.

# Supernova Neutrinos

## Key claim

By combining signals from multiple existing detectors (SuperKamiokande, SNO, KamLAND), it should be possible to separately determine the flux and average energy (or temperature) for each of the three neutrino signal components (ν_e, anti-ν_e, and ν_x) from a galactic supernova, provided the detectors can overcome parameter degeneracies in neutral current measurements and the supernova occurs within approximately 10 kpc.

## Method

The paper analyzes how charged current reactions (inverse neutron beta decay for anti-ν_e; deuteron disintegration and reactions on carbon and oxygen for ν_e) and neutral current reactions (neutrino-electron scattering, deuteron disintegration, inelastic scattering on oxygen and carbon, and elastic scattering on protons) can be used in SuperKamiokande (32 kton water Cherenkov), SNO (1 kt heavy water Cherenkov), and KamLAND (1 kt liquid scintillator) to extract the six parameters (flux and temperature for each flavor component). The author evaluates event rates, detection thresholds, and spectral information available from each reaction channel, noting that neutral current reactions generally lack spectral information except for neutrino-proton elastic scattering, which can resolve the degeneracy between ν_x luminosity and temperature.

## Key numbers

- Total gravitational binding energy emitted in neutrinos: E_B = 3 × 10^53 ergs, assuming a supernova at distance 10 kpc
- Characteristic neutrino emission time: approximately 10 seconds
- Typical luminosity per neutrino flavor: 5 × 10^51 erg/s (equal luminosity rule)
- Average energies assumed for the hierarchy: ⟨E_ν_e⟩ = 11 MeV, ⟨E_anti-ν_e⟩ = 16 MeV, ⟨E_ν_x⟩ = 25 MeV
- Expected events from inverse neutron beta decay in SuperKamiokande: approximately 8000 events from a standard supernova
- Expected events from anti-ν_e + d → e+ + n + n in SNO: about 80 events
- Expected events from ν_e + d → e- + p + p in SNO: about 80 events
- Expected events from ν_e + 16O → e- + 16F* in SuperKamiokande: about 20 events at standard ν_e average energy; increases to approximately 860 events if ν_e temperature rises to 8 MeV
- Neutrino-proton elastic scattering: approximately 300 events per kt above 200 keV effective (quenched) proton recoil energy
- Neutronization pulse energy: 2-5 × 10^51 ergs (5-10% of total ν_e energy), yielding approximately 10 ν_e-e scattering events in SuperKamiokande
- Diffuse relic supernova anti-ν_e flux estimate: approximately 10 anti-ν_e/(cm^2 s), resulting in about 0.5 events per kt per year

## Context

This work extends the analysis of supernova neutrino detection beyond the single observation of anti-ν_e from SN1987A (Kamiokande and IMB collaborations, 1987) by proposing how to measure all three flavor components using existing detectors. The paper builds on supernova simulations by Woosley and Totani that established the equal luminosity and temperature hierarchy rules, while noting that recent studies by Raffelt suggest these rules are highly model dependent. The proposed combination of charged and neutral current measurements would enable tests of neutrino oscillation effects on the way to Earth and improve understanding of the r-process nucleosynthesis site, while the diffuse relic neutrino flux measurement would constrain the average supernova rate over cosmological timescales.
