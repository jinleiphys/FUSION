# Glitch Behavior of Pulsars and Contribution from Neutron Star Crust

> Pulsars are highly magnetized rotating neutron stars with a very stable rotation speed. Irrespective of their stable rotation rate, many pulsars have been observed with the sudden jump in the rotation rate, which is known as pulsar glitch. The glitch phenomena are considered to be an exhibit of superfluidity of neutron matter inside the neutron star's crustal region. The magnitude of such rapid change in rotation rate relative to their stable rotation frequency can quantify the moment of inertia of the crustal region to the total moment of inertia of the star called as the fractional moment of inertia (FMI). In this paper, we have calculated FMI for different masses of the star using six different representative unified equations of state (EoS) constructed under Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) framework. We have performed an event-wise comparison of FMI obtained from data with that of theoretically calculated values with and without considering the entrainment effect. It is found that larger glitches can't be explained by crustal FMI alone, even without the entrainment.

# Glitch Behavior of Pulsars and Contribution from Neutron Star Crust

## Key claim

Even without considering the entrainment effect (which reduces the effective superfluid reservoir), a few percent of observed pulsar glitches cannot be explained by angular momentum transfer from the pinned superfluid in the neutron star crust alone, implying that the core superfluid must participate in at least the larger glitch events.

## Method

The authors calculated the fractional moment of inertia (FMI) of the crust relative to the total moment of inertia for neutron stars of different masses using six unified equations of state (EoS) constructed within the Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) framework: NL3, NL3ωρ, GM1, TM1, DDME2, and BHBΛφ. They performed an event-wise comparison of FMI values inferred from 335 individual glitch events (using the relation I_crust/I_total > 2τ_c (Δν/ν)/t_i) with the theoretically calculated FMI values, both with and without including the entrainment effect (using an average effective neutron mass ratio <m_n*>/m_n = 4.35).

## Key numbers

- Fractional spin-up values for observed glitches range from 10^-9 to 33 × 10^-6.
- Crust thickness ranges from approximately 1.4-1.8 km for a 1 M⊙ star to approximately 0.5 km for a 2 M⊙ star.
- Without entrainment, 1 to 20 percent of glitch events cannot be explained by crustal superfluid alone, depending on EoS and assumed NS mass (e.g., for NL3 at 1.4 M⊙: 8 out of 335 glitches; for BHBΛφ at 1.4 M⊙: 31 out of 335 glitches).
- With entrainment (<m_n*>/m_n = 4.35), the fraction rises to 4 to 36 percent (e.g., for NL3 at 1.4 M⊙: 31 out of 335 glitches; for BHBΛφ at 1.4 M⊙: 81 out of 335 glitches).
- The FMI for a polytropic inner crust (as used by Piekarewicz (2014)) can be overestimated by 0-8 percent for a 1.4 M⊙ star using the NL3ωρ EoS.
- The toroidal magnetic field mechanism proposed by Guge (2014, 2017) provides an FMI of superfluid associated with the toroidal field of order 0.3-1.2 × 10^-2.

## Context

This work extends previous glitch studies that used non-relativistic EoSs (Andersson 2012, Chamel 2013, Ho 2015, Delsate 2016, Li 2016, Pizzochero 2017) or RMF EoSs with a polytropic inner crust (Piekarewicz 2014) by employing unified EoSs where the inner crust and core are calculated within the same microscopic theory, avoiding arbitrary matching at the crust-core boundary. The use of individual glitch events rather than the average activity parameter (as done by Link 1999, Andersson 2012, Chamel 2013, Ho 2015, Eya 2017) allows identification of specific glitches requiring core participation. The results are strengthened by recent constraints from GW170817 (Abbott 2017), which favor softer EoSs like BHBΛφ that predict a larger fraction of unexplained glitches. The work enables future theoretical studies of pinning mechanisms in the core, such as mixed-phase crystalline structures (Glendenning 2000) or vortex-fluxoid interpinning (Sauls 1989, Dipankar), and the toroidal magnetic field pinning mechanism proposed by Guge (2014, 2017).
