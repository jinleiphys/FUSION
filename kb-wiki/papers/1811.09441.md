# Camouflage of the Phase Transition to Quark Matter in Neutron Stars

> It has been known for some time that compact stars containing quark matter can masquerade as neutron stars in the range of measured mass and radius, making it difficult to draw firm conclusions on the phases of matter present inside the star. Using the vector-enhanced Bag model (vBag), we examine mass-radius and mass-compactness relations with Maxwell and Gibbs construction for hybrid stars with transitions from nuclear matter to two or three-flavor quark matter, including sequential transitions. Not only can stable hybrid stars with either two or three-flavor quark matter mimic neutron stars (the traditional masquerade), it appears as well difficult to distinguish two-flavor from three-flavor quark matter even in cases where a phase transition can be said to have occurred, as in the presence of a distinct kink in the mass-radius relation. Furthermore, allowing for sequential flavor transitions, we find that the transition into an unstable branch can be caused by either a transition from a nuclear to unstable quark matter or the sequential transition from nuclear to stable but "masquerading" two-flavor to unstable three-flavor quark matter. Addressing chiral restoration as well as quark deconfinement in a model of the phase transition, as the vBag does, adds further flexibility to the high-density equation of state, motivating caution in using even high-precision $M$-$R$ data to draw firm conclusions on the nature of phases and phase transitions in neutron stars.

## Key claim

The authors argue that even with high-precision mass-radius data, it may be impossible to distinguish not only hybrid stars from ordinary neutron stars (the traditional masquerade), but also two-flavor from three-flavor quark matter in hybrid stars, and that sequential flavor transitions can produce unstable branches that mimic first-order transitions to either two or three-flavor matter.

## Method

The authors used the vector-enhanced Bag model (vBag) to construct equations of state for two-flavor and three-flavor quark matter, combined with the SL23 nuclear equation of state. They computed mass-radius and mass-compactness relations for hybrid stars using both Maxwell construction (local charge neutrality, sharp phase boundary) and Gibbs construction (global charge neutrality, mixed phase region), including sequential transitions from nuclear to two-flavor to three-flavor quark matter.

## Key numbers

- For the nuclear masquerade of 2-flavor quark matter with Maxwell construction: vector coupling $K_v = 7 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}$, effective bag constant $B_{\mathrm{eff}} = 70 \, \mathrm{MeV\,fm^{-3}}$
- For the nuclear masquerade of 2-flavor quark matter with Gibbs construction: $K_v = 9 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}$, $B_{\mathrm{eff}} = 70 \, \mathrm{MeV\,fm^{-3}}$
- For the nuclear masquerade of 3-flavor quark matter with Maxwell construction: $K_v = 23.5 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}$, $B_{\mathrm{eff}} = 78.5 \, \mathrm{MeV\,fm^{-3}}$
- For the nuclear masquerade of 3-flavor quark matter with Gibbs construction: $K_v = 32 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}$, $B_{\mathrm{eff}} = 80 \, \mathrm{MeV\,fm^{-3}}$
- For flavor camouflage with Maxwell construction (2f): $K_v = 5 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}$, $B_{\mathrm{eff}} = 70 \, \mathrm{MeV\,fm^{-3}}$; (3f): $K_v = 20 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}$, $B_{\mathrm{eff}} = 78 \, \mathrm{MeV\,fm^{-3}}$
- For flavor camouflage with Gibbs construction (2f): $K_v = 5 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}$, $B_{\mathrm{eff}} = 70 \, \mathrm{MeV\,fm^{-3}}$; (3f): $K_v = 22 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}$, $B_{\mathrm{eff}} = 80 \, \mathrm{MeV\,fm^{-3}}$
- For the unstable branch scenario with 2-flavor matter: $K_v = 1 \times 10^{-6} \, \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}$, $B_{\mathrm{eff}} = 140 \, \mathrm{MeV\,fm^{-3}}$
- The Maxwell construction leaves an imprint in the mass-compactness derivative slightly below compactness $C = 0.2 \, \mathrm{M_{\odot}/km}$
- For the 3-flavor Maxwell case, a kink appears at about $1.9 \, \mathrm{M_{\odot}}$

## Context

This work extends the masquerade problem first identified by Alford (2004), where hybrid stars with quark matter can mimic ordinary neutron stars in mass and radius. The authors introduce the concept of "flavor camouflage" as a new ambiguity, showing that even when a phase transition is detectable, one cannot determine whether the quark matter has two or three flavors. The study uses the vBag model developed by Klahn (2013) and builds on work by Alford (2017) on sequential transitions, as well as the generic CSS parameterization approach. The results motivate caution in interpreting even high-precision mass-radius data from observations such as GW170817, and suggest that other diagnostics such as mass-compactness derivatives may provide more information than mass-radius curves alone.
