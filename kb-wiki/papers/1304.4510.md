# Microwave and submillimeter molecular transitions and their dependence on fundamental constants

> Microwave and submillimeter molecular transition frequencies between nearly degenerated rotational levels, tunneling transitions, and mixed tunneling-rotational transitions show an extremely high sensitivity to the values of the fine-structure constant, alpha, and the electron-to-proton mass ratio, mu. This review summarizes the theoretical background on quantum-mechanical calculations of the sensitivity coefficients of such transitions to tiny changes in alpha and mu for a number of molecules which are usually observed in Galactic and extragalactic sources, and discusses the possibility of testing the space- and time-invariance of fundamental constants through comparison between precise laboratory measurements of the molecular rest frequencies and their astronomical counterparts. In particular, diatomic radicals CH, OH, NH+, and a linear polyatomic radical C3H in Pi electronic ground state, polyatomic molecules NH3, ND3, NH2D, NHD2, H2O2, H3O+, CH3OH, and CH3NH2 in their tunneling and tunneling-rotational modes are considered. It is shown that sensitivity coefficients strongly depend on the quantum numbers of the corresponding transitions. This can be used for astrophysical tests of Einstein's Equivalence Principle all over the Universe at an unprecedented level of sensitivity of ~10^-9, which is a limit three to two orders of magnitude lower as compared to the current constraints on cosmological variations of alpha and mu: Delta alpha/alpha < 10^-6, Delta mu/mu < 10^-7.

## Key claim
Microwave and submillimeter molecular transitions between nearly degenerate rotational levels, tunneling transitions, and mixed tunneling-rotational transitions can achieve astrophysical tests of Einstein's Equivalence Principle at a sensitivity of approximately 10^-9, which is two to three orders of magnitude more sensitive than current constraints on cosmological variations of the fine-structure constant (Δα/α < 10^-6) and the electron-to-proton mass ratio (Δμ/μ < 10^-7), provided that the molecular rest frequencies are known with an accuracy better than 100 Hz.

## Method
The authors performed quantum-mechanical calculations of sensitivity coefficients Qα and Qμ for molecular transitions by scaling the parameters of effective Hamiltonians (including spin-rotational and hyperfine parts) according to the dependence of each parameter on α and μ (e.g., spin-orbit constant A ∝ α², rotational constant B ∝ μ). They then numerically differentiated the resulting transition frequencies with respect to α and μ near their physical values to obtain the sensitivity coefficients for each transition, applying this method to diatomic radicals (CH, OH, NH⁺) and polyatomic molecules (NH₃, ND₃, NH₂D, NHD₂, H₂O₂, H₃O⁺, CH₃OH, CH₃NH₂) in their tunneling and tunneling-rotational modes.

## Context
This review extends the theoretical framework for using molecular spectra to test fundamental constant variation, building on earlier work by Uzan (2011) and others who established the sensitivity of atomic and molecular transitions to α and μ. The authors specifically calculate sensitivity coefficients for molecules commonly observed in Galactic and extragalactic sources (CH, OH, NH₃, H₂O₂, CH₃OH, and others), enabling next-generation radio-astronomical tests that can reach sensitivity levels two to three orders of magnitude better than current optical/UV methods (which are limited to ~10⁻⁵ for Δμ/μ from H₂ observations by Ubachs et al. 2007 and ~10⁻⁶ for Δα/α from Fe II lines). The work enables differential measurements comparing precise laboratory rest frequencies (known to better than 100 Hz for ammonia and OH) with astronomical observations to probe space-time invariance of fundamental constants.
