# Three-body continuum wave functions with a box boundary condition

> In this work we investigate the connection between discretized three-body continuum wave functions, in particular via a box boundary condition, and the wave functions computed with the correct asymptotics. The three-body wave functions are in both cases obtained by means of the adiabatic expansion method. The information concerning all the possible incoming and outgoing channels, which appears naturally when the continuum is not discretized, seems to be lost when the discretization is implemented. In this work we show that both methods are fully equivalent, and the full information contained in the three-body wave function is actually preserved in the discrete spectrum. Therefore, in those cases when the asymptotic behaviour is not known analytically, i.e., when the Coulomb interaction is involved, the discretization technique can be safely used.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that discretizing three-body continuum wave functions via a box boundary condition preserves the full information about all possible incoming and outgoing channels, contrary to the apparent loss of information when only a single wave function appears per discrete energy. The equivalence holds provided the discrete states are recognized as belonging to bins whose number of elements equals the number of open channels.

## Method

The author uses the adiabatic expansion method to compute three-body wave functions in hyperspherical coordinates, first solving the angular eigenvalue problem for fixed hyperradius to obtain adiabatic potentials and angular functions. The radial wave functions are then obtained by solving a coupled set of radial equations, either with correct asymptotic normalization using Bessel functions (for short-range interactions) or by imposing a box boundary condition that sets the wave functions to zero at a finite hyperradius L. The discrete continuum states from the box method are compared to the properly normalized continuum wave functions by applying a normalization matrix A that relates the two approaches.

## Key numbers

- For a three-body system of three identical spinless bosons with total spin and parity 0+ at E = 10 MeV, the radial wave functions from the box method (L = 400 fm) for states i = 257 (9.99 MeV), i = 258 (10.02 MeV), and i = 259 (10.03 MeV) show dominant terms matching the non-discretized calculation at 10 MeV.
- The energy separation between consecutive discrete states follows ΔE = (2π/L)(E_i/κ_i), where κ_i = √(2mE_i)/ħ, with the separation decreasing linearly with L.
- For L = 400 fm at E_i = 80 MeV, the expected energy separation is approximately 0.6 MeV, and the energy bins found are {79.17, 79.23, 79.27} MeV and {79.81, 79.87, 79.89} MeV.
- The number of discrete states with energies below 15 MeV scales linearly with L: 33 states for L = 50 fm, 72 for L = 100 fm, 153 for L = 200 fm, 315 for L = 400 fm, and 642 for L = 800 fm.

## Context

This work extends the adiabatic expansion method for three-body continuum states developed by Nielsen et al. (2001) and the integral relations for extracting the S-matrix from Romero-Redondo et al. (2011) and Barletta et al. (2009). The key advance is showing that the box discretization technique, previously used without rigorous justification for Coulomb systems, is formally equivalent to the full continuum treatment. This enables safe application of the discretization method to systems where the asymptotic behavior is not known analytically, such as the three-alpha system with Coulomb interactions, without requiring numerical determination of Coulomb function parameters.
