# Disentangling fission fragment evaporation models using TALYS

> Several computer codes based on phenomenological models are being developed with the aim of obtaining fission observables, such as neutron and gamma multiplicities and product yields. Key points in these calculations, which are handled differently by the various codes, are the sharing of the total excitation energy between the fragments and the generation of angular momenta. After the initial states of the fragments is set, the de-excitation through the emission of neutrons and photons can be model. However, many models also include tunable parameters that are used to make the calculations conform with literature data. As a result, it could be possible to obtain good agreement with experimental results even with questionable assumptions on the initial conditions. To test the assumptions made by different fission models the code DE$\ell$FIN has been developed. DE$\ell$FIN starts with the fission fragments as generated by the models and calculates the average neutron emission as a function of mass in a transparent and coherent way using the nuclear reaction code TALYS. Hence, the probabilities of neutron emission in competition with $Îł$ de-excitation comes from parameters of the TALYS code that have not been optimised. The results are then compared looking at general trends and at the difference to what is obtained with the stand-alone versions of the codes. In this study, the output of DE$\ell$FIN for five of the most commonly used fission codes is compared to the results of the stand-alone versions, and to experimental data, for the reactions $^{235}$U(n$_{\mathrm{th}}$,f), $^{239}$Pu(n$_{\mathrm{th}}$,f) and $^{252}$Cf(sf).

## Key claim

The paper tests whether different fission models make sound physical assumptions about initial fragment excitation energy and angular momentum by decoupling the de-excitation step from the models' own tunable parameters, using the TALYS code as a neutral, unoptimized de-excitation engine. The authors caution that good agreement with experimental data can be achieved even with questionable initial conditions, because many models include tunable parameters that compensate for incorrect assumptions.

## Method

The authors developed the code DE$\ell$FIN, which takes the fission fragment excitation energies (and angular momenta, where available) as generated by five fission codes (GEF, FIFRELIN, FREYA, PbP, CGMF) and feeds them into the nuclear reaction code TALYS to calculate the average prompt neutron multiplicity as a function of fragment mass, $\bar{\nu}(A)$. The TALYS parameters were left at their default values and not optimized for fission. The DE$\ell$FIN results were compared to the stand-alone versions of each fission code and to experimental data for three reactions: $^{235}$U(n$_{\mathrm{th}}$,f), $^{239}$Pu(n$_{\mathrm{th}}$,f), and $^{252}$Cf(sf).

## Key numbers

* For $^{235}$U(n$_{\mathrm{th}}$,f), the total average prompt neutron multiplicity from ENDF/B-VII.1 is 2.44; DE$\ell$FIN with GEF input gave 2.52 (light fragment average 1.39, heavy fragment average 1.14), while the stand-alone GEF gave 2.48 (1.29, 1.19).
* For $^{239}$Pu(n$_{\mathrm{th}}$,f), the ENDF/B-VII.1 total multiplicity is 2.87; DE$\ell$FIN with FREYA input gave 3.02 (1.55, 1.47), while stand-alone FREYA gave 2.94 (1.56, 1.38).
* For $^{252}$Cf(sf), the ENDF/B-VII.1 total multiplicity is 3.76; DE$\ell$FIN with CGMF input gave 4.08 (2.24, 1.83), while stand-alone CGMF gave 3.75 (2.12, 1.63).
* For $^{252}$Cf(sf), DE$\ell$FIN with FIFRELIN input gave a total multiplicity of 4.36 (2.35, 2.00), compared to the stand-alone FIFRELIN value of 3.69 (1.94, 1.75); after subtracting an estimated rotational energy contribution, the DE$\ell$FIN result dropped to 3.94 (2.15, 1.79).

## Context

This work extends the methodology first reported by Mattera (2017) to include two additional fission codes (FIFRELIN and CGMF) and three benchmark reactions. It directly compares the assumptions of five commonly used phenomenological fission models (GEF, FIFRELIN, FREYA, PbP, CGMF) by removing the variability in the de-excitation step, which is often tuned to data. The study enables future work to isolate which parts of the fission models (excitation energy sharing, angular momentum generation, rotational energy treatment) are responsible for discrepancies with experiment, and it advocates for new, more precise measurements in low-yield mass regions where model disagreements are largest.
