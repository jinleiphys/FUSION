# Few-body Physics in a Many-body World

> The study of quantum mechanical few-body systems is a century old pursuit relevant to countless subfields of physics. While the two-body problem is generally considered to be well-understood theoretically and numerically, venturing to three or more bodies brings about complications but also a host of interesting phenomena. In recent years, the cooling and trapping of atoms and molecules has shown great promise to provide a highly controllable environment to study few-body physics. However, as is true for many systems where few-body effects play an important role the few-body states are not isolated from their many-body environment. An interesting question then becomes if or (more precisely) when we should consider few-body states as effectively isolated and when we have to take the coupling to the environment into account. Using some simple, yet non-trivial, examples I will try to suggest possible approaches to this line of research.

## Key claim

The paper argues that few-body states, particularly Efimov trimers, are not isolated from their many-body environment, and that the key question is *when* (not *if*) the coupling to the background must be taken into account; the answer depends on whether the interparticle distance in the many-body system becomes comparable to the size of the few-body state.

## Method

The author uses momentum-space Skornyakov-Ter-Martirosian equations, modified to avoid the Thomas collapse, to compute the Efimov spectrum in the presence of an inert Fermi sea background that introduces Pauli blocking in the two-body propagator. For the Bose gas background, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation is employed with a light-particle Bose condensate containing two heavy impurity atoms, where the condensate modifies the low-energy dispersion relation. Both approaches are top-down, starting from formalisms that reproduce Efimov states when the background density goes to zero.

## Key numbers

- The Efimov scaling factor for the Fermi wave vector is \(e^{\pi/s_0} \sim 22.7\); a state is swallowed by the atom-dimer continuum each time the Fermi wave vector scales by this factor.
- For a three-component \(^6\)Li gas, the loss peak should be affected at densities of about \(10^{12}\) cm\(^{-3}\) and above, which is about one order of magnitude larger than reported experiments.
- In the Born-Oppenheimer limit for a Bose condensate background, the effective potential between two heavy impurities at resonance is \(V(R) = -\hbar^2 \Omega^2 / (m R^2)\) with \(\Omega \sim 0.567\).
- The number of three-body states in the Bose case is \(N_T \approx (1/\pi) \log(\xi / R_0)\), where \(\xi = 1/\sqrt{8\pi n a_B}\) is the condensate coherence length.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical study of Efimov physics, originally predicted by Efimov (1970) for three bosons in 3D and later observed in cold alkali gases by Kraemer et al. (2006), into the regime where the few-body system is embedded in a many-body environment. It builds on prior studies of Efimov states in a Fermi sea by Nishida (2009), MacNeill and Zhou (2011), and Endo and Ueda (2013), and on the author's own work with Nygaard (2011) for the Fermi case and Zinner (2013) for the Bose case. The results enable experimental searches for density-dependent shifts of Efimov loss peaks in systems such as three-component \(^6\)Li gases and mixtures of metastable \(^4\)He\(^*\) and \(^{87}\)Rb.
