# Self-consistent single-nucleon potential at positive energy produced by semi-realistic interaction and its examination via nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering

> Based on the variational principle, self-consistent single-particle (s.p.) potentials at positive energies are discussed, which correspond to the real part of the optical potential as the single folding potential (SFP). The nuclear-matter s.p. potential produced by the semi-realistic nucleonic interaction M3Y-P6, which has links to the bare nucleonic interaction, resembles those extracted from the empirical optical potential Applying M3Y-P6 both to the self-consistent mean-field calculations for the target nucleus and to the SFP for the scattered nucleon, we find that the differential cross-sections of the nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering are reproduced almost comparably to the empirical potentials up to $80\,\mathrm{MeV}$ incident energy. The results demonstrate that the s.p. potential compatible with available experimental data can be derived from a single energy-independent effective interaction in this wide energy range.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a single energy-independent effective interaction (M3Y-P6) can produce a self-consistent single-particle potential at positive energies that reproduces nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering differential cross-sections comparably to empirical optical potentials up to 80 MeV incident energy, though the authors note an upper limit of applicability around 80 MeV and acknowledge that the imaginary part of the optical potential is still taken from empirical sources rather than derived microscopically.

## Method

The authors apply the semi-realistic M3Y-P6 interaction, which has links to the bare nucleonic interaction, to both self-consistent mean-field (Hartree-Fock) calculations for the target nucleus and to the single folding potential (SFP) for the scattered nucleon. The real part of the optical potential is obtained from the SFP via the variational principle without energy-dependent parameters, while the imaginary part is taken from the empirical KD potential. The differential cross-sections and reaction cross-sections are computed using the SIDES code for proton and neutron elastic scattering on 16O, 40Ca, 90Zr, and 208Pb targets at incident energies from approximately 10 MeV to 80 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Differential cross-sections are reproduced "almost comparably to the empirical potentials up to 80 MeV incident energy" for proton-nucleus scattering on 16O, 40Ca, 90Zr, and 208Pb
- At 65 MeV incident proton energy, "notable agreement with the data" is found for all target nuclei
- At 80 MeV incident proton energy, the calculated differential cross-section is larger than data at center-of-mass angles greater than 60 degrees, though peak and dip positions are well reproduced
- The Gogny-D1S interaction gives differential cross-sections "seriously deviating from the data" at incident energies above approximately 65 MeV, while reproducing cross-sections below approximately 30 MeV
- The nuclear-matter single-particle potential is evaluated at saturation density rho = 0.16 fm^{-3} for comparison with empirical potentials
- The upper limit of applicability for the energy-independent M3Y-P6 interaction is estimated to lie around epsilon = 80 MeV

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent mean-field approach using the M3Y-P6 interaction, previously validated for nuclear structure at negative single-particle energies (Nakada, 2013; Nakada, 2020), to positive energies relevant to nucleon-nucleus scattering. It contrasts with earlier attempts using Skyrme and Gogny interactions (such as SLy4 and D1S) which showed limited applicability above approximately 30 MeV incident energy. The approach enables future extension to finite-temperature nuclear matter problems, where a single energy-independent effective interaction is needed to handle single-particle states at various energies simultaneously, and provides a foundation for microscopic derivation of the imaginary part of the optical potential in future work.
