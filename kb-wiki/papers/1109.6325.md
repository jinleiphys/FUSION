# Looking into the matter of light-quark hadrons

> In tackling QCD, a constructive feedback between theory and extant and forthcoming experiments is necessary in order to place constraints on the infrared behaviour of QCD's β-function, a key nonperturbative quantity in hadron physics. The Dyson-Schwinger equations provide a tool with which to work toward this goal. They connect confinement with dynamical chiral symmetry breaking, both with the observable properties of hadrons, and hence provide a means of elucidating the material content of real-world QCD. This contribution illustrates these points via comments on: in-hadron condensates; dressed-quark anomalous chromo- and electro-magnetic moments; the spectra of mesons and baryons, and the critical role played by hadron-hadron interactions in producing these spectra.

## Key claim

The Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) can connect confinement with dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) and both with observable hadron properties, but the infrared behavior of QCD's β-function, a key nonperturbative quantity, must be constrained through constructive feedback between theory and experiment, not assumed from static potential models.

## Method

The author employed Dyson-Schwinger equations for dressed-quark and -gluon propagators and vertices, together with Bethe-Salpeter equations for meson bound states and Faddeev equations for baryons, using symmetry-preserving truncation schemes. A vector×vector contact interaction with symmetry-preserving regularization was used to compute meson and baryon spectra, while a more sophisticated nonperturbative kernel incorporating dressed-quark anomalous chromomagnetic moments was applied to the ρ- and a₁-meson channels. The in-hadron condensate concept was derived from scalar form factors at zero momentum transfer using exact QCD relations.

## Key numbers

- Dressed-gluon mass at infrared momenta: M_g = m_g(0) ∼ 0.7 GeV
- Infrared value of the rainbow-ladder effective coupling: α_RL(M_g²)/π = 2.2
- Critical coupling for DCSB via gap equations in QED and QCD: α_c/π ≈ 1/3
- In a vector×vector contact-interaction in rainbow-ladder truncation: α_c/π ≈ 0.4; description of hadron phenomena requires α/π ≈ 1
- Chiral-limit value of the in-pseudoscalar-meson condensate: κ⁰ = (0.24 GeV)³
- In-vector-meson condensate is (1.13)³ times greater than in-pseudoscalar meson condensate near the chiral limit; in the m_Q → ∞ limit, this ratio rises to (1.43)³
- For B-mesons: κ_B^{m̄_b} = (0.60 ± 0.10 GeV)³ and κ_{B*}^{m̄_b} = (0.59^{+0.10}_{-0.05} GeV)³; κ_P^{0 m̄_b} = (0.27 GeV)³
- ρ- and a₁-meson mass splitting: more than 400 MeV (i.e., > m_ρ/2)
- Using the Ball-Chiu vertex alone: m_{a₁} − m_ρ = 0.21 GeV
- Unified spectrum of u,d-quark hadrons using contact interaction: rms = 13%
- DSE-computed Roper resonance (radial excitation of nucleon): 1.82 ± 0.07 GeV

## Context

This contribution extends the DSE-based approach to hadron physics reviewed in Roberts (2007), Holt (2010), and Chang (2011), and directly addresses the long-standing failure to explain the ρ-a₁ mass splitting in symmetry-preserving treatments. The work contradicts the notion that static quark potentials from quenched lattice QCD are relevant to confinement in real-world QCD, following the Schwinger function perspective of Krein (1990). It enables a unified treatment of meson and baryon spectra within a single symmetry-preserving framework, and the identification of the Roper resonance as the proton's first radial excitation, consistent with the bare masses used in coupled-channels models by Suzuki (2009) and Gasparyan (2003), opens the door to interpreting nucleon resonance electrocoupling data from CLAS as dressed quark cores surrounded by meson clouds.
