# Fundamental Physics with Electroweak Probes of Nuclei

> The past decade has witnessed tremendous progress in the theoretical and computational tools that produce our understanding of nuclei. A number of microscopic calculations of nuclear electroweak structure and reactions have successfully explained the available experimental data, yielding a complex picture of the way nuclei interact with electroweak probes. This achievement is of great interest from the pure nuclear-physics point of view. But it is of much broader interest too, because the level of accuracy and confidence reached by these calculations opens up the concrete possibility of using nuclei to address open questions in other sub-fields of physics, such as, understanding the fundamental properties of neutrinos, or the particle nature of dark matter. In this talk, I will review recent progress in microscopic calculations of electroweak properties of light nuclei, including electromagnetic moments, form factors and transitions in between low-lying nuclear states along with preliminary studies for single- and double-beta decay rates. I will illustrate the key dynamical features required to explain the available experimental data, and, if time permits, present a novel framework to calculate neutrino-nucleus cross sections for $A>12$ nuclei.

## Key claim

Microscopic *ab initio* calculations of nuclear electroweak properties have reached sufficient accuracy to enable the use of nuclei as probes for fundamental physics questions, but the abstract's claim of "concrete possibility" is hedged by the fact that these calculations are currently limited to light nuclei (A ≤ 12) and that the resolution of the "g_A problem" in single-beta decays appears to be primarily due to correlation effects in nuclear wave functions rather than many-body currents.

## Method

The paper reviews Green's Function Monte Carlo (GFMC) calculations of electroweak properties of light nuclei (A ≤ 12) using the AV18 two-body and IL7 three-body nuclear interactions, with electroweak couplings to one-, two-, and three-body currents derived from chiral effective field theory. For the specific study of the "g_A problem," numerically exact Quantum Monte Carlo calculations of Gamow-Teller matrix elements were performed in A=6-10 nuclei, systematically accounting for many-body effects in both nuclear interactions and axial currents. For neutrinoless double beta decay, the calculations were extended to A=6-12 nuclei, studying the effect of artificially turning off correlations in the nuclear wave functions.

## Key numbers

- Two-body electromagnetic currents contribute corrections as large as 40% in the calculated magnetic moment of ⁹C
- Two-body currents enhance the transverse response in quasi-elastic electron scattering by up to ~40%
- Many-body axial currents increase one-body Gamow-Teller contributions by only ~2-3% in A=6-10 nuclei
- Shell model calculations of reduced matrix elements are enhanced by ~8% (A=6-7) and ~17% (A=10) relative to experimental data, while GFMC calculations with correlations achieve excellent agreement in A=6-7 and at the 10% level in the ¹⁰C weak transition
- GFMC calculations using Norfolk chiral potentials bring the ¹⁰C prediction to only ~4% above the experimental datum
- Turning off correlations in neutrinoless double beta decay matrix elements for A=6-12 nuclei produces a ~10% increase in the calculated nuclear matrix elements for the light Majorana neutrino exchange mechanism
- The required "quenching" of g_A in the ¹⁰C weak transition evaluated in the shell model is ~0.83, while for neutrinoless double beta decay the required quenching is ~0.95

## Context

This work extends the program of microscopic *ab initio* calculations of nuclear structure pioneered by Carlson (1998) and Carlson (2015), and directly addresses the "g_A problem" identified by Chou (1993) and Engel (2016), where simplified nuclear calculations systematically overpredict Gamow-Teller matrix elements by ~20% in A ≤ 18 nuclei. The results enable future calculations of neutrino-nucleus cross sections for A > 12 nuclei and provide benchmark test cases for theoretical nuclear models used in interpreting neutrinoless double beta decay experiments, which are critical for understanding neutrino properties and the matter-antimatter asymmetry of the universe.
