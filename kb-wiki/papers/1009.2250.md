# A Linear Sigma Model with Three Flavors and Vector and Axial-Vector Mesons

> We outline the extension of the globally chirally invariant Nf = 2 linear sigma model with vector and axial-vector degrees of freedom to Nf=3. We present preliminary results concerning the scalar meson masses.

## Key claim

The extension of the globally chirally invariant Nf=2 linear sigma model with vector and axial-vector degrees of freedom to Nf=3 yields preliminary results suggesting that scalar states above 1 GeV may be interpreted as quarkonia, but the parameters still need to be calculated precisely and the results depend on the choice of parameters.

## Method

The authors constructed an Nf=3 linear sigma model Lagrangian with global chiral invariance, containing scalar, pseudoscalar, vector, and axial-vector degrees of freedom, including a covariant derivative and terms for explicit chiral symmetry breaking and the U(1)A anomaly. They implemented spontaneous symmetry breaking by shifting the sigma_N and sigma_S fields by their vacuum expectation values, which produced mixing between various meson fields that was subsequently removed. They then computed preliminary scalar meson masses in the limit where the large-NC suppressed parameter lambda_1 is set to zero.

## Key numbers

- Predominantly n-bar-n scalar state mass: between 1.0 and 1.3 GeV, depending on parameter choice
- Predominantly s-bar-s scalar state mass: between 1.3 and 1.6 GeV, depending on parameter choice

## Context

This work extends the Nf=2 linear sigma model with vector and axial-vector mesons from Parganlija et al. (2010) to three flavors, addressing open questions about strange meson structure that are ambiguous in the scalar sector. The approach differs from prior work using the linear sigma model for strange mesons (Gell-Mann and Levy, 1960) by including vector and axial-vector degrees of freedom. The results enable future studies of tetraquark and glueball mixing with scalar quarkonia, as well as more precise parameter determination using experimental data from the Particle Data Group (2010).
