# The story around the first 4n signal

> The GANIL campaign around the first 4n signal was very peculiar. The beginning and end were both dictated by unexpected events that, unfortunately, do not fit within the streamlined format of standard scientific publications. However, they illustrate many aspects of how basic research should work, or at least does work. Therefore, I take this opportunity to share them with those not involved in the campaign, hoping that they will offer a better perspective of that research in particular and of basic research in general. As a disclaimer, this is only a personal recollection of those events.

## Key claim

The first tetraneutron (4n) signal, observed in 2001 as an unexpected byproduct of a calibration test on data from a previous experiment, was consistent with a 4n state in the energy range of -1 to +2 MeV, but the author emphasizes that this was a personal recollection of events and that the official publication format obscures the real chain of unexpected results, failures, and null experiments that characterized the entire campaign.

## Method

The author and collaborators analyzed data from a 1997 GANIL campaign on 2n halo nuclei (⁶He, ¹¹Li, ¹⁴Be) using the DEMON neutron scintillator array to measure proton recoil energy (Eₚ) relative to neutron time-of-flight energy (Eₙ). They calibrated the Eₚ/Eₙ ratio, which should be below 1 for single neutrons, and identified six events with Eₚ/Eₙ > 1.4 that all belonged to the ¹⁴Be → ¹⁰Be reaction channel (loss of 4n). Subsequent dedicated experiments over five years using ¹⁴Be and ⁸He beams at GANIL were hampered by beam production problems, detector issues, and accelerator operator strikes, yielding no new signal.

## Key numbers

- Six events with Eₚ/Eₙ > 1.4 were observed, all from the ¹⁴Be → ¹⁰Be channel (loss of 4n), representing about 1% background level.
- The original 4n signal came from less than 2 days of data with a ¹⁴Be beam at 130 particles per second (pps).
- A later dedicated experiment (E378, September 2001) achieved only 20 pps of ¹⁴Be instead of the expected higher intensity.
- The first dedicated proposal (E415, December 2002) aimed for 8 days at 500 pps of ¹⁴Be but achieved only 100 pps with a background of 10,000 pps of lighter particles.
- The RIKEN double charge-exchange result (2016) was consistent with a 4n state at E(4n) = 0.8 ± 1.3 MeV (4.9σ).
- The strong confirmation from RIKEN (α removal from ⁸He) gave a 4n state at E(4n) = 2.4 ± 0.6 MeV (≫5σ).

## Context

This paper is a personal recollection of the events surrounding the first tetraneutron signal at GANIL (Marqués et al., 2002), which contradicted theoretical expectations from three-body calculations (3n system) and four-body calculations (4n system) that had concluded bound or resonant states were unlikely. The work extends the narrative of multineutron research reviewed by Marqués and Carbonell, and it sets the stage for the later strong confirmation at RIKEN (Duer et al., 2022). The author argues that the real research process, including null experiments, beam failures, and strikes, should be openly discussed to counter the misleading linear-success model of standard publications and to encourage risk-taking in basic research.
