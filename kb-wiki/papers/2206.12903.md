# Minkowski's lost legacy and hadron electromagnetism

> We revisit Minkowski's lost legacy on relativistic electromagnetism in order to resolve long-standing puzzles over the charge distribution of relativistic systems like hadrons. Hadrons are unique relativistic electromagnetic systems characterized by their comparable size and Compton wavelength $r_h \sim λ_C$. As such, it was recently realized that the traditional Sachs definition of the charge distribution based on a non-relativistic formula is invalid. We explain that this is the same problem pursued by Lorentz, Einstein and others, on the electromagnetism of a moving body. We show how various charge distributions proposed in hadronic physics naturally emerge as the multipole moment densities in the macroscopic theory of relativistic electromagnetism.

## Key claim

The traditional Sachs definition of the hadron charge distribution, based on a non-relativistic formula, is invalid for relativistic systems like hadrons because their size and Compton wavelength are comparable ($r_h \sim \lambda_C$); the paper shows that various charge distributions proposed in hadronic physics naturally emerge as multipole moment densities in Minkowski's macroscopic theory of relativistic electromagnetism, without requiring a special reference frame.

## Method

The authors apply the macroscopic field theory of relativistic electromagnetism developed by Minkowski, Einstein, and Laub to hadronic systems, treating the hadron as a relativistic matter wave that acts as an electromagnetic medium characterized by a polarization tensor $M^{\alpha\beta}$. They construct the full current from quantum field theory expectation values using wavepacket states, then derive the multipole moment densities by expanding the charge form factor in a power series around the hadron momentum, showing that the Sachs distribution corresponds to the electric monopole density and the light-front distribution emerges from an alternative expansion in inverse momentum.

## Key numbers

- Proton charge radius $r_p \simeq 0.84\,\text{fm}$, while its Compton wavelength is $M^{-1}_p \simeq 0.2\,\text{fm}$ (from PDG 2020)
- Proton mass radius $r_p^{\text{mass}} \simeq 0.55\,\text{fm}$ (Polyakov 2018, Kharzeev 2021)
- Pion charge radius $r_\pi \simeq 0.67\,\text{fm}$, while its Compton wavelength is $M^{-1}_\pi\simeq 1.4\,\text{fm}$ (from PDG 2020)
- For a Gaussian wavepacket, the r.m.s. charge radius $\langle r^2_\text{ch} \rangle_{\Psi} = -6G'_E(0) + 3R^2_\text{w}$ diverges as the wavepacket approaches the plane wave limit $R_\text{w} \to \infty$

## Context

This work extends the critique of the Sachs charge distribution by Miller (2007-2018), Jaffe (2020), and Freese (2021), who argued that the traditional definition is unphysical for relativistic systems. The paper connects this problem to the historical work of Lorentz, Einstein, and Minkowski (1908) on the electromagnetism of moving bodies, and shows that the multipole moment densities from macroscopic electromagnetism (de Groot 1972) provide a unified framework for understanding both the Sachs distribution and light-front distributions (Burkardt 2000). The formalism enables generalization to gravitational form factors and the energy-momentum tensor, leading to the conclusion that the equivalence principle applies to relativistic quantum systems, consistent with Teryaev's argument (1999) for the vanishing of the anomalous gravitomagnetic moment.
