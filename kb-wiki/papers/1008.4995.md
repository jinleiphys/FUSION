# Iterative solution of a Dirac equation with inverse Hamiltonian method

> We solve a singe-particle Dirac equation with Woods-Saxon potentials using an iterative method in the coordinate space representation. By maximizing the expectation value of the inverse of the Dirac Hamiltonian, this method avoids the variational collapse, in which an iterative solution dives into the Dirac sea. We demonstrate that this method works efficiently, reproducing the exact solutions of the Dirac equation.

## Key claim

The inverse Hamiltonian method avoids the variational collapse (where an iterative solution dives into the Dirac sea) by maximizing the expectation value of the inverse of a shifted Dirac Hamiltonian, provided the shift constant \(E_0\) is chosen between the lowest eigenvalue in the Fermi sea and the highest eigenvalue in the Dirac sea.

## Method

The method iteratively evolves a trial wave function using the operator \(1 + \Delta T/(H - E_0)\) in coordinate space, where \(H\) is the Dirac Hamiltonian with spherical Woods-Saxon potentials. For each iteration, the radial Dirac equation is discretized on a grid with spacing \(\Delta r\) and solved via a two-component recursion relation using Gauss elimination, imposing a box boundary condition at \(R_{\text{max}}\). The upper and lower components of the wave function are obtained simultaneously, and states in the Dirac sea are accessed by reversing the sign of \(\Delta T\).

## Key numbers

- For the neutron \(1p_{1/2}\) state in \(^{16}\text{O}\), the converged energy from the inverse Hamiltonian method was \(E = -18.974\) MeV, nearly identical to the exact value of \(E = -18.976\) MeV.
- The coordinate space grid used \(R_{\text{max}} = 15\) fm with \(\Delta r = 0.1\) fm.
- The energy shift was set to \(E_0 = -71.28\) MeV (the depth of the Woods-Saxon potential \(U(r)\)), and the step size was \(\Delta T = 10\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the imaginary time method from non-relativistic mean-field calculations (Davies et al., 1980; Bonche et al., 1985) to the relativistic Dirac equation, directly addressing the variational collapse problem noted by Zhang et al. (2010) in nuclear physics and earlier in quantum chemistry (Hill and Krauthauser, 1994). The method is complementary to the approach of Zhang et al. (2010) and Geng and Meng (2007), which applied the imaginary time method to the Schrodinger-equivalent form of the Dirac equation, and to the damped relaxation techniques of Burvenich et al. (1989). It enables future application to self-consistent relativistic mean-field calculations in three-dimensional space without requiring separate equations for the Fermi and Dirac seas.
