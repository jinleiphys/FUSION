# Exploring an experimental route of synthesizing superheavy elements beyond Z > 118

> Role of the Coulomb interaction, mean fissility, mass asymmetry, and charge asymmetry parameters on the synthesis of heavy and superheavy elements has been examined with respect to the deformation parameters of the projectile and target nuclei explicitly in light of the experimental results. The observed facts are classified into four categories and are then used to study several unsuccessful as well as planned reactions to synthesize the new superheavy elements $Z= 119, 120$. Concrete inference is too difficult to draw from these results because of excessive deviations in evaporation residue cross-section data. It is found that the arbitrary choice of excitation energy for the experiments studied was the root cause of such large deviations. Such a complex issue can be resolved well by theoretical excitation function studies using the advanced statistical model or the dinuclear system model and choosing the excitation energy corresponding to the energy where the excitation function curve shows the maximum. We believe this method may help us to predict whether the estimated evaporation residue cross-section can be measurable within the experimental limit of the existing facilities for the future reactions planned.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the large deviations in evaporation residue cross-section data for superheavy element synthesis reactions (Z = 119, 120) arise from the arbitrary choice of excitation energy in experiments, and that theoretical excitation function studies using the advanced statistical model or the dinuclear system model can resolve this issue by identifying the excitation energy corresponding to the maximum of the excitation function curve.

## Method

The authors examined the roles of Coulomb interaction parameter, mean fissility, mass asymmetry, and charge asymmetry on evaporation residue cross-sections for heavy-ion fusion reactions, classifying reactions into four categories based on the deformation of projectile and target nuclei (spherical-spherical, deformed-spherical, deformed-deformed, spherical-deformed). They fitted empirical equations to the experimental data for each deformation class and then applied these systematic trends to analyze both unsuccessful past reactions and planned future reactions for synthesizing elements Z = 119 and 120. Finally, they performed theoretical excitation function calculations using the advanced statistical model and the dinuclear system model to determine optimal beam energies.

- For the reaction $^{45}_{21}Sc+^{249}_{98}Cf \rightarrow ^{294}_{119}Uue$, the empirical average evaporation residue cross-section from entrance channel systematics is 1.6 +/- 3.1 pb.
- For the reaction $^{86}_{36}Kr+^{209}_{83}Bi\rightarrow^{295}_{119}Uue$, the empirical average evaporation residue cross-section is 34.5 +/- 392.5 pb.

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of entrance channel effects on superheavy element synthesis previously applied to hot fusion reactions by Manjunatha (2020) and to cold fusion reactions by Giardina (2000). The paper addresses the failure of recent experimental attempts to synthesize elements Z = 119 and 120, as reported by Khuyagbaatar (2013) and others, by identifying the arbitrary choice of excitation energy as the root cause of large deviations in cross-section data. The proposed method of using theoretical excitation function studies with the advanced statistical model or the dinuclear system model to select optimal beam energies could guide future experiments at facilities such as LBNL, JINR, and RIKEN toward measurable evaporation residue cross-sections.
