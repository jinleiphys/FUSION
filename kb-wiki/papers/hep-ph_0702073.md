# Modelling Bose Einstein Correlations via Elementary Emitting Cells

> We propose a method of numerical modeling Bose Einstein Correlations by using the notion of Elementary Emitting Cells (EEC). They are intermediary objects containing identical bosons and are supposed to be produced independently during the hadronization process. Only bosons in EEC, which represents a single quantum state here, are subjected to the effects of Bose-Einstein (BE) statistics, which forces them to follow a geometrical distribution. There are no such effects between particles from different EECs. We illustrate our proposition by calculating a representative number of typical distributions and discussing their sensitivity to EECs and their characteristics.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that Bose-Einstein correlations can be numerically modeled by assuming identical bosons are produced in independent "Elementary Emitting Cells" (EECs), each representing a single quantum state, with particles within an EEC following a geometrical distribution and no correlations between different EECs. The authors explicitly state that this work offers no comparison with experimental data and is limited to a thorough discussion of the algorithm, which is intended as a building block for future Monte Carlo event generators.

## Method

The authors implement a "Quantum Clan Model" (QCM) where particles are produced sequentially: a seed pion of randomly chosen charge is selected with energy from a distribution f(E), then additional pions of the same charge are added using a probability P = P0 * exp(-E/T) until the first failure marks the end of an EEC. The process repeats with new seeds until all available energy W is consumed, with each accepted pion in an EEC assigned an energy from a distribution G(E_i) centered on the seed energy, typically a Gaussian with width proportional to temperature T.

## Key numbers

- For the one-dimensional pionic lattice model: p_max = 10 GeV and N = 100 total lattice sites, with lattice spacing delta_p = 2*p_max/N
- The width of the correlation function sigma(C_2) is proportional to the product of average number of particles per cell <N_part> and average distance <delta_p> between particles on the lattice
- For the thermal-like model, the Gaussian width parameter sigma = sigma_0 * T, where T is the temperature
- Results are shown for total energy W = 91.2 GeV (corresponding to e+e- annihilations at LEP), with additional calculations at W(i) = 0.5W and W(iii) = 2W
- The probability P is defined as P = P_0 * exp(-E_i/T), leading to the Bose-Einstein distribution <N(E_i)> = 1/(P_0^{-1} * exp(E_i/T) - 1)

## Context

This work extends previous attempts to model Bose-Einstein correlations numerically, including the Metropolis importance sampling method of Zajc (1987), the acceptance-rejection method of Cramer (2005), and the Information Theory approach of the authors' group (2005). The paper builds on the concept of "speckles" or clusters observed in Zajc (1987) and the bunching property in rapidity space demonstrated in the Information Theory method, proposing a computationally practical algorithm that can be implemented in existing Monte Carlo event generators. The approach enables future inclusion of Bose-Einstein effects in event generators for e+e- annihilation and high-multiplicity events, though the authors note that distribution of energies, hadronization, and possible flows are outside the scope of this work.
