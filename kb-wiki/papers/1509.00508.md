# Current Status of Nuclear Physics Research

> In this review we discuss the current status of research in nuclear physics which is being carried out in different centers in the World. For this purpose we supply a short account of the development in the area which evolved over the last 9 decades, since the discovery of the neutron. The evolution of the physics of the atomic nucleus went through many stages as more data become available. We briefly discuss models introduced to discern the physics behind the experimental discoveries, such as the shell model, the collective model, the statistical model, the interacting boson model, etc., some of these models may be seemingly in conflict with each other, but this was shown to be only apparent. The richness of the ideas and abundance of theoretical models attests to the important fact that the nucleus is a really singular system in the sense that it evolves from two-body bound states such as the deuteron, to few-body bound states, such as $^4$He, $^7$Li, $^9$Be etc. and up the ladder to heavier bound nuclei containing up to more than 200 nucleons. Clearly statistical mechanics does not work for such finite system, neither does other theories applicable to condensed matter systems. The richness of nuclear physics stems from these restrictions. New theories and models are presently being developed. Theories of the structure and reactions of neutron-rich and proton-rich nuclei, called exotic nuclei, halo nuclei, or Borromean nuclei deal with the wealth of experimental data available in the last 35 years. Further, nuclear astrophysics and stellar and Big Bang nucleosynthesis have become a more mature subject. Due to limited space, this review only covers a few selected topics, mainly those with which the authors have worked with.

## Key claim
This review paper provides a broad overview of nuclear physics research, but the authors explicitly state that due to limited space, it only covers a few selected topics, mainly those with which the authors have worked, rather than offering a comprehensive survey of the entire field.

## Method
The authors present a narrative review of nuclear physics, tracing its evolution over nine decades since the discovery of the neutron. They discuss major theoretical models including the shell model, collective model, statistical model, and interacting boson model, and describe how these models have been applied to understand nuclear structure, nuclear reactions, and nuclear astrophysics. The review also covers recent developments in effective field theories, exotic nuclei, and the interface between nuclear physics and other scientific disciplines.

## Key numbers
- The triple-alpha process resonance in carbon is located at 7.65 MeV above the alpha emission threshold, as postulated by Hoyle (1954).
- The isotope $^{180m}$Ta exists in an isomeric state at $E^{\star} = 77.1$ keV with spin and parity $9^{-}$, and is the only stable nucleus to exist in such a state, with 0.012% natural abundance.
- In the hyper nucleus $^{7}_{\Lambda}$Li, the radius of $^{6}$Li is 20% smaller than that in free space.
- The number of odd-odd stable nuclei amounts to only 5: $^2$H, $^6$Li, $^{10}$B, $^{14}$N, and $^{180m}$Ta.
- Fusion of 4 protons into a $^4$He nucleus releases about 28 MeV ($\sim 4.5 \times 10^{-12}$ J) of energy per event.

## Context
This review extends the long tradition of nuclear physics overviews by connecting historical developments from Bethe (1939) and Hoyle (1954) to modern topics such as effective field theories, lattice QCD, and exotic nuclei research. The paper enables readers to understand how nuclear physics has evolved from early models like the shell model (Mayer 1949, Hazel 1949) and collective model (Bohr and Mottelson 1969) to contemporary approaches including chiral effective field theory and the study of neutron-rich nuclei at facilities like RIBRAS in Brazil. The authors note that the field has broadened to overlap with astrophysics, cosmology, and other areas, while also acknowledging the political dimensions of nuclear physics through national security and non-proliferation issues.
