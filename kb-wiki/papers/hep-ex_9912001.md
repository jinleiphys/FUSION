# Comment on ``Parton distributions, d/u, and higher twist effects at high x''

> In a recent paper Yang and Bodek extracted the free neutron structure function at large x by extrapolating the density dependence of the nuclear EMC ratios to the deuteron. We clarify why this approach is ill-defined for light nuclei, and introduces a large theoretical bias into the extraction of F_2n at large x.

## Key claim

The nuclear density model of the EMC effect, when extrapolated to the deuteron and other light nuclei (A ≤ 3), is ill-defined and introduces a large theoretical bias into the extraction of the free neutron structure function F₂ⁿ at large x, because a physically meaningful nuclear density cannot be defined for few-body systems.

## Method

The authors examine the density-extrapolation method used by Yang and Bodek (1999), which assumes that nuclear binding effects in the deuteron scale with average nuclear density following the approach of Frankfurt and Strikman (1988). They compare this method's predictions for A=3 nuclei (³He and ³H) against standard many-body calculations using Faddeev wave functions, and they analyze the conceptual problems of defining a nuclear density for the deuteron, noting that the probability of one nucleon overlapping with the other is given by the deuteron wave function at the origin, which has zero weight.

## Key numbers

- The EMC effect in the deuteron is claimed to be about 25% as large as in ⁵⁶Fe, with the same x dependence, according to the density extrapolation.
- For A=3 nuclei, the nuclear densities calculated from charge radii are ρ(³He) = 0.049 fm⁻³ and ρ(³H) = 0.068 fm⁻³, leading to a prediction that the EMC effect in ³H is 40% larger than in ³He.
- Standard many-body calculations using Faddeev wave functions predict less than 10% difference between the EMC effects in A=3 mirror nuclei.
- Omitting nuclear binding or off-shell corrections can introduce errors of up to 50% in F₂ⁿ/F₂ᵖ already at x ~ 0.75.

## Context

This comment directly challenges the extraction method of Yang and Bodek (1999), which relied on the density-scaling assumption of Frankfurt and Strikman (1988) that was empirically validated only for heavy nuclei using SLAC data (Gomez et al., 1994). The authors distinguish between the MST model (Melnitchouk, Schreiber and Thomas, 1994), which investigated relativistic and off-shell effects theoretically, and the MT analysis (Melnitchouk and Thomas, 1996), which performed an empirical extraction of F₂ⁿ using an iterative deconvolution procedure with deuteron wave functions. The comment enables future resolution of the issue through proposed experiments measuring structure functions of ³He and ³H simultaneously, parity-violating electron scattering, semi-inclusive pion production, or charged-current reactions.
