# Heavy flavor production at RHIC and LHC energy

> In a leading order pQCD model, we have studied the heavy flavor production in p+p collisions at RHIC and LHC energy. Leading order pQCD models require a K-factor. At RHIC energy, $\sqrt{s}$=200 GeV, we fix K such that the model reproduces the integrated charm yield, $dN^{c\bar{c}}/dy$, estimated by the STAR and the PHENIX collaboration in p+p collisions. The model then explains the STAR data on the transverse momentum distribution of open charm mesons $(D^0)$ and decay electrons in p+p collisions. The p+p predictions, scaled by the number of binary collisions, also explain the electron spectra in STAR p+d collisions and PHENIX Au+Au collisions in different centrality bins. Assuming that at LHC energy K-factor is of the order of unity, we have used the model to predict the transverse momentum distribution of $D$ and $B$ mesons and also of electrons from semileptonic decay of $D\to e$ and $B\to e$, in p+p collisions at LHC energy, $\sqrt{s}$=14 TeV.

# Heavy flavor production at RHIC and LHC energy

## Key claim
In a leading-order pQCD model with a K-factor fixed to reproduce the integrated charm yield from either STAR or PHENIX data, the model can describe the transverse momentum distributions of D mesons and decay electrons in p+p, d+Au, and Au+Au collisions at RHIC, and can predict heavy flavor production at LHC energy assuming the K-factor approaches unity. The agreement depends critically on using the Braaten et al. fragmentation function rather than the Peterson fragmentation function, which underpredicts yields by a factor of 2-3.

## Method
The authors computed heavy quark production in p+p collisions using leading-order pQCD parton-level cross sections for the subprocesses gg → c\bar{c}, q\bar{q} → c\bar{c}, gQ → gC, and qQ → qC, with CTEQ5L parton distribution functions. Heavy quarks were fragmented into mesons using the Braaten et al. fragmentation function (FF-I) or the Peterson et al. fragmentation function (FF-II), and semileptonic decays of D and B mesons into electrons were computed using rest-frame decay spectra. The K-factor was fixed by matching the integrated charm yield dσ^{c\bar{c}}/dy to the central values reported by PHENIX (K=1.8) or STAR (K=7.0) at √s=200 GeV.

## Key numbers
- At RHIC (√s=200 GeV), the K-factor was fixed to K=1.8 for PHENIX data and K=7.0 for STAR data, reflecting the large difference in measured integrated charm cross sections.
- PHENIX measured dσ^{c\bar{c}}/dy = 143 ± 13 ± 36 μb in the p_T range 0.4 GeV < p_T < 4 GeV.
- STAR measured dσ^{c\bar{c}}/dy = 0.29 ± 0.04 ± 0.08 mb in the p_T range 1 GeV < p_T < 4 GeV.
- For PHENIX Au+Au data, the p+p predictions scaled by the nuclear overlap function (binary collision scaling) described the electron spectra in different centrality bins.
- At LHC energy (√s=14 TeV), the model with K=1 predicts that high-p_T heavy meson yields increase by a factor of 1000 or more compared to RHIC.
- For p_T < 2 GeV at RHIC, bottom quarks contribute less than 10% to the total electron yield; in the p_T range 2-3 GeV, bottom contributes 10-20%; in the p_T range 3-4 GeV, bottom contributes 20-30%.
- For LHC predictions, below p_T=1 GeV, B mesons contribute less than 10% to the total electron yield; in the p_T range 2-4 GeV, B contribution rises to 30-50%.

## Context
This work extends leading-order pQCD modeling of heavy flavor production to both RHIC and LHC energies, building on earlier studies by Cacciari et al. (1998), Vogt (2002), and Cacciari, Nason, and Vogt (2005) who used FONLL (fixed-order plus next-to-leading-log) calculations. The model confirms the binary collision scaling of charm production observed by both STAR and PHENIX collaborations, in contrast to the strong violation of binary collision scaling seen in light hadron production at RHIC (STAR, PHENIX, PHOBOS, BRAHMS, all circa 2003-2004). The results enable baseline predictions for heavy flavor production in p+p collisions at LHC energy, which can be used to interpret future heavy-ion data and to study whether heavy quarks suffer energy loss in the medium produced in Au+Au collisions.
