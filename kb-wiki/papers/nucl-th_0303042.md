# Influence of microscopic transport coefficients on the formation probabilities for super-heavy elements

## Key claim
The formation probability for super-heavy elements increases by a few orders of magnitude when microscopic transport coefficients are used instead of macroscopic ones (such as liquid drop model and wall friction), but quantum effects in the collective motion itself (as opposed to the nucleonic dynamics) turn out to be of lesser importance in the temperature range studied (approximately 0.6 to 2.6 MeV).

## Method
The formation of a compound nucleus is modeled as a Gaussian Markov process over an inner barrier approximated by an inverted oscillator, using a Fokker-Planck equation to derive analytical expressions for the time-dependent formation probability. The authors compare results using microscopic transport coefficients (derived from linear response theory for quantal nucleon motion in a mean field) against macroscopic transport coefficients (from liquid drop model and wall-and-window friction), and within each case compare fully quantum diffusion coefficients against the high-temperature (classical Einstein relation) and zero-damping limits.

## Key numbers
- Formation probability increases by "a few orders of magnitude" when microscopic transport coefficients replace macroscopic ones; for example, at E = 10 MeV with R = 0.25, microscopic coefficients give formation probabilities orders of magnitude larger than macroscopic ones, where the effective barrier height is B_eff = 1.4 GeV down to 980 MeV for macroscopic versus B_eff = 15 to 180 MeV for microscopic.
- For microscopic transport coefficients at E = 10 MeV, the stationary formation probability increases by two orders of magnitude when going from R = 0 (no initial kinetic energy) to R = 0.25 (E0_kin = 5 MeV).
- The effective damping strength for microscopic coefficients changes from under-damped to over-damped at approximately T = 2 MeV; for macroscopic coefficients, motion is over-damped (eta_b = 5.9 to 4.9) even at very small temperatures.
- The barrier height is set to E_b = 10 MeV, and the incident energy range studied is E = 0 to 200 MeV, corresponding to temperatures T_0 = 0.6 to 2.6 MeV.
- For microscopic coefficients at E = 10 MeV, eta_b = 0.26 at T = 0.94 MeV (R = 0) and eta_b = 0.18 at T = 0.82 MeV (R = 0.25).
- The cut-off parameter for Drude regularization is omega_D = 10 MeV/hbar, with variation in momentum fluctuations for macroscopic coefficients by a factor of 0.8 to 1.3 at T = 1 MeV when omega_D varies from 5 to 20 MeV/hbar.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies on microscopic transport coefficients for nuclear fission (Hofmann and Ivanyuk, 1999; Hofmann, Ivanyuk, Ruck, and Yamaji, 2001) to the formation phase of super-heavy element synthesis. It contrasts with macroscopic approaches using liquid drop model stiffness and wall-and-window friction (such as those used by Shchepina, Kogut, and Abyzov, 2002; and Ivanyuk, Hofmann, Pashkevich, and Yamaji, 1997). The results enable more realistic calculations of fusion cross sections for super-heavy elements, though the authors note that a complete treatment requires a microscopic description of the entrance phase which is still lacking.
