# The Decay Q Value of Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay

> An earlier publication "The implication of the atomic effects in neutrinoless double beta (0$νββ$) decay" written by Mei and Wei has motivated us to compare the decay Q value ($Q_{ββ}$) derived from the decay of the parent nucleus to the daughter nucleus with the two ejected beta particles in the final state to the $Q_{ββ}$ directly derived from the decay of the initial neutral atom to the final state of double-ionized daughter ion with the two ejected beta particles in the final state. We show that the results are the same, which is the mass-energy difference ($ΔMc^2$) subtracted by the total difference of the atomic electron binding energy ($ΔE_{b}$) between the ground states of initial and final neutral atoms. We demonstrate that $ΔMc^2$ is the sum of $Q_{ββ}$ and the atomic relaxation energy ($ΔE_{b}$) of the atomic structure after the decay. Depending on the atomic relaxation time, the release of the atomic binding energy may not come together with the energy deposition of the two ejected beta particles.

## Key claim

The decay Q value for neutrinoless double beta decay is not simply the atomic mass difference between the initial and final neutral atoms (ΔMc²), as currently used by experiments, but rather that quantity minus the difference in total atomic electron binding energy (ΔE_b) between the mother and daughter neutral atoms; the atomic relaxation energy may not be deposited within the same detection time window as the two ejected beta particles.

## Method

The authors derived the decay Q value (Q_ββ) from two equivalent formulations: the decay of the parent nucleus to the daughter nucleus with two ejected beta particles, and the decay of the initial neutral atom to a double-ionized daughter ion with two ejected beta particles. They expressed nuclear masses in terms of neutral atomic masses and total atomic electron binding energies, showing both derivations yield the same expression: Q_ββ = ΔMc² − ΔE_b. They then computed ΔE_b for nine candidate 0νββ decay isotopes using an empirical formula for total electron binding energy as a function of atomic number Z.

## Key numbers

- The difference in total electron binding energy (ΔE_b) ranges from a minimum of 4.76 keV for ⁴⁸Ca → ⁴⁸Ti to a maximum of 21.89 keV for ¹⁵⁰Nd → ¹⁵⁰Sm.
- For ⁷⁶Ge → ⁷⁶Se, ΔE_b is 8.97 keV.
- For ¹³⁰Te → ¹³⁰Xe, ΔE_b is 17.73 keV.
- For ¹³⁶Xe → ¹³⁶Ba, ΔE_b is 18.74 keV.
- The currently used atomic mass differences (ΔMc²) measured by Penning-trap techniques are: 4267.98 keV for ⁴⁸Ca → ⁴⁸Ti, 2039.04 keV for ⁷⁶Ge → ⁷⁶Se, 2997.73 keV for ⁸²Se → ⁸²Kr, 3355.85 keV for ⁹⁶Zr → ⁹⁶Mo, 3034.40 keV for ¹⁰⁰Mo → ¹⁰⁰Ru, 2813.50 keV for ¹¹⁶Cd → ¹¹⁶Sn, 2527.01 keV for ¹³⁰Te → ¹³⁰Xe, 2457.83 keV for ¹³⁶Xe → ¹³⁶Ba, and 3367.72 keV for ¹⁵⁰Nd → ¹⁵⁰Sm.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier publication (Mei and Wei, 2022) on atomic effects in 0νββ decay, clarifying that the standard Q-value definition used by experiments such as GERDA, MAJORANA Demonstrator, EXO, and CUORE (which take Q_ββ as the neutral atomic mass difference) is incorrect. The paper argues that current and planned experiments (including LEGEND and CUPID) may need to re-evaluate their energy region of interest (ROI) to account for the atomic binding energy difference, which is larger than typical ROI widths. The atomic relaxation time, estimated using a formula from Qiao et al. (2022) for Rydberg atoms, may be very long for 0νββ decay, potentially causing the atomic relaxation energy to fall outside the detection time window.
