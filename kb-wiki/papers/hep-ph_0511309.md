# New proposal of numerical modelling of Bose-Einstein correlations: Bose-Einstein correlations from "within"

> We describe an attempt to numerically model Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) from "within", i.e., by using them as the most fundamental ingredient of a Monte Carlo event generator (MC) rather than considering them as a kind of (more or less important, depending on the actual situation) "afterburner", which inevitably changes the original physical content of the MC code used to model multiparticle production process.

## Key claim

The authors propose that Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) can be modeled from "within" a Monte Carlo event generator by constructing elementary emitting cells (EECs) as the fundamental ingredient, rather than adding BEC as an afterburner that inevitably changes the original physical content of the MC code; however, the method remains preliminary and far from complete, requiring further development for time-dependent emission, Coulomb interactions, and full multiparticle symmetrization.

## Method

The method constructs EECs by first selecting a seed particle of given energy and charge from an assumed energy spectrum f(E) ~ exp(-E/T). Additional particles of the same charge are added to the EEC with probability P(E) = P0 * exp(-E/T) until first failure, producing a geometrical (Bose-Einstein) distribution of particles per cell. Energies within each EEC are spread by a width sigma around the seed energy to generate finite widths of the correlation function C2(Q). For three-dimensional extension, pairwise symmetrization of wave functions between the seed particle and each subsequent particle in the EEC is performed, introducing a 1 + cos[(p1 - pi)*(r1 - ri)] term that connects spatial source extension to momentum space characteristics.

## Key numbers

- For the simple cascade model (CAS), examples of BEC patterns were obtained for source masses M = 10, 40, and 100 GeV, using constant weights P = 0.75 and P = 0.5, as well as Gaussian weights based on momenta and positions of particles.
- The correlation function C2(Q) is defined as C2(Q = |p1 - p2|) = dsigma(p1,p2) / [dsigma(p1) * dsigma(p2)], where Q is the relative momentum of particle pairs.
- For the quantum clan model, example calculations assumed a spherical source of radius R = 1 fm with spontaneous decay (no time dependence) and spherically symmetric momentum distributions, with energies selected from f(E) ~ exp(-E/T).
- The occupancy number per cell follows <n_p> = P(E) / [1 + P(E)], where P0 plays the role of chemical potential mu = T * ln(P0).

## Context

This work extends the information-theory approach of OMT (which first modeled BEC by dividing rapidity space into cells of size delta y) by allowing dynamically defined EECs that conserve energy-momentum, unlike the fixed-size cells in OMT. It also builds on the clan model of NBD (which produced negative binomial multiplicity distributions from logarithmically distributed clans) by introducing "quantum clans" with geometrically distributed particles to account for bosonic statistics. The method enables future investigation of whether BEC primarily reveal characteristics of emitting cells rather than the whole hadronizing source, and opens the possibility of modeling Bose-Einstein condensation effects in high-energy collisions.
