# Radiative corrections to elastic proton-electron scattering measured in coincidence

> The differential cross section for elastic scattering of protons on electrons at rest is calculated taking into account the QED radiative corrections to the leptonic part of interaction. These model-independent radiative corrections arise due to emission of the virtual and real soft and hard photons as well as to vacuum polarization. We analyze an experimental setup when both the final particles are recorded in coincidence and their energies are determined within some uncertainties. The kinematics, the cross section, and the radiative corrections are calculated and numerical results are presented.

## Key claim

The paper calculates the QED radiative corrections to the differential cross section for elastic proton-electron scattering in inverse kinematics, specifically for an experimental setup where both final particles are detected in coincidence and their energies are measured within some uncertainty. The authors note that while an experiment measuring the elastic cross section at very small momentum transfer cannot by itself produce a constraint on the slope of form factors, a combined series of low momentum transfer measurements with a physical parametrization of form factors will help for a meaningful extrapolation to the static point.

## Method

The authors compute the differential cross section for elastic scattering of protons on electrons at rest in the one-photon exchange (Born) approximation, then add model-independent QED radiative corrections from virtual photon emission at the electron vertex, vacuum polarization, and real soft and hard photon emission from the electron leg. The soft photon correction is integrated analytically under the condition that the maximum soft photon energy is much less than the electron mass, while the hard photon contribution is integrated numerically over the photon phase space with a weight function that accounts for the experimental energy resolution of the detected proton and electron. The kinematics are treated in the inverse kinematics regime where the electron mass cannot be neglected, and the formalism includes the full dependence on the proton electromagnetic form factors.

## Key numbers

- The maximum four-momentum transfer squared for proton scattering on electrons at rest is given by \((Q^2)_{max} = \frac{4m^2\vec p^2}{M^2+2mE+m^2}\), where \(m\) is the electron mass, \(M\) is the proton mass, and \(E\) and \(\vec p\) are the proton beam energy and momentum.
- The maximum recoil electron energy is \(\epsilon_{2,max} = m\frac{2E(E+m)+m^2-M^2}{M^2+2mE+m^2}\), which is proportional to the electron mass.
- The maximum scattering angle for the hadron is determined by \(\sin\theta_{h,max} = m/M_h\), where \(M_h\) is the hadron mass.
- For small momentum transfer (\(Q^2 \ll m^2\)), the vacuum polarization correction is \(\delta^{(\rm vac)} = \frac{2\alpha}{15\pi}\frac{Q^2}{m^2}\).
- For large momentum transfer (\(Q^2 \gg m^2\)), the vacuum polarization correction is \(\delta^{(\rm vac)} = \frac{2\alpha}{3\pi}\left[-\frac{5}{3}+\ln\frac{Q^2}{m^2}\right]\).
- The quantity \(\omega_s\), representing the maximal photon energy for emission in the whole angular phase space, is of the order of the electron mass \(m\) in a wide range of recoil electron energies and proton beam energies.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous proposal (Gakh 2013) for using proton elastic scattering on atomic electrons to measure the proton charge radius, where inverse kinematics allows access to momentum transfers up to four orders of magnitude smaller than presently achieved. The calculation is relevant to the proton radius puzzle, where muonic hydrogen measurements (Pohl 2010, Antognini 2013) give \(r_E = 0.84087(39)\) fm, smaller by seven standard deviations than the CODATA 2010 value of \(0.8775(51)\) fm from electron-proton scattering and hydrogen spectroscopy. The radiative corrections differ essentially from the electron-proton case because the electron mass must be taken explicitly into account, and the formalism follows the approach used for pion-electron scattering experiments at Serpukhov (Adylov 1974) and Fermilab (Dally 1977, 1982) as well as the CERN muon scattering experiment (Abbiendi 2016). The results enable precise analysis of proposed experiments at the PRAE platform and the Mainz Microtron that aim for per mille absolute precision in cross section measurements.
