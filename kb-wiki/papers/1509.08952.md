# Running Masses in the Nucleon and its Resonances

> An overarching scientific challenge for the coming decade is to discover the meaning of confinement, its relationship to dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) - the origin of visible mass - and the connection between them. In progressing toward meeting this challenge, significant progress has been made using continuum methods in QCD. For example, a novel understanding of gluon and quark confinement and its consequences has begun to emerge from quantum field theory; a clear picture is being drawn of how hadron masses emerge dynamically in a universe with light quarks; and ground-state hadron wave functions with a direct connection to QCD are becoming available, which reveal that quark-quark correlations are crucial in hadron structure. There is growing experimental support for this body of predictions in both elastic and nucleon-to-resonance-transition form factors.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the masses of the nucleon and its resonances, such as the Roper, emerge dynamically from quantum chromodynamics (QCD) through dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) and the formation of diquark correlations, but it qualifies that the computed "dressed-quark core" masses (e.g., 1.18 GeV for the nucleon and 1.73 GeV for the Roper) are not directly observable; they must be lowered by approximately 0.2 GeV via meson-baryon final-state interactions to match empirical values.

## Method
The study uses continuum methods in QCD, specifically Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) and Bethe-Salpeter equations, to solve the Poincaré-covariant Faddeev equation for baryons. This framework incorporates a renormalisation-group-invariant (RGI) running interaction derived from QCD's gauge sector, which unifies top-down (gauge-sector) and bottom-up (hadron-observable) approaches. The nucleon and its radial excitation (Roper) are treated as quark+diquark bound states, with diquark correlations (scalar and axial-vector) emerging from DCSB.

## Key numbers
- Dressed-quark mass function at infrared momenta: \(M(0) \approx 0.3\) GeV, with a constituent-like "spectrum mass" \(M_D \sim 350\) MeV.
- Gluon mass at zero momentum: \(m_g(0) \approx 0.5\) GeV, with a length-scale \(\varsigma \approx 0.5\) fm.
- Diquark correlation mass-scales: scalar diquark \(m_{[ud]_{0^+}} \approx 0.7-0.8\) GeV; axial-vector diquark \(m_{\{uu\}_{1^+}} \approx 0.9-1.1\) GeV.
- Computed quark-core masses: nucleon = 1.18 GeV; Roper = 1.73 GeV (compared to empirical pole locations of 0.939 GeV and 1.36 - i 0.091 GeV, respectively).
- Diquark content in both nucleon and Roper: scalar diquark probability \(P_{J=0} = 62\%\); axial-vector diquark probability \(P_{J=1} = 38\%\).
- Bare Roper mass from coupled-channels analysis: 1.76 GeV, in agreement with the quark-core result of 1.73 GeV.

## Context
This work extends continuum-QCD studies of hadron structure, building on prior DSE and lattice-QCD analyses of propagators and vertices (e.g., Bhagwat (2003), Bowman (2005), Binosi (2014)). It unifies top-down and bottom-up approaches to the QCD interaction, as shown in Fig. 1 of the paper, and provides the first continuum treatment of the Roper resonance using relativistic quantum field theory. The results enable future predictions for nucleon-to-resonance transition form factors, which can be tested at facilities like JLab 12, and they challenge static flux-tube models of confinement by emphasizing dynamical diquark correlations and meson-cloud effects.
