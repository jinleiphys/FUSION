# Domains and defects in nuclear "pasta"

> Nuclear pasta topology is an essential ingredient to determine transport properties in the inner crust of neutron stars. We perform semi-classical molecular dynamics simulations of nuclear pasta for proton fractions $Y_p=0.30$ and $Y_p=0.40$ near one third of nuclear saturation density, $n=0.05\,\mathrm{fm}^{-3}$, at a temperature $T=1.0\,\mathrm{MeV}$. Our simulations are, to our knowledge, the largest nuclear pasta simulations to date and contain up to $3\,276\,800$ nucleons in the $Y_p=0.30$ and $819\,200$ nucleons in the $Y_p=0.40$ case. An algorithm to determine which nucleons are part of a given sub-domain in the system is presented. By comparing runs of different sizes we study finite size effects, equilibration time, the formation of multiple domains and defects in the pasta structures, as well as the structure factor dependence on simulation size. Although we find qualitative agreement between the topological structure and the structure factors of runs with $51\,200$ nucleons and those with $819\,200$ nucleons or more, we show that simulations with hundreds of thousands of nucleons may be necessary to accurately predict pasta transport properties.

## Key claim

While simulations with 51,200 nucleons show qualitative agreement in topological structure and structure factors with runs containing 819,200 nucleons or more, simulations with hundreds of thousands of nucleons may be necessary to accurately predict pasta transport properties, as finite size effects in smaller simulations introduce quantitative differences in the structure factor peaks.

## Method

The authors performed semi-classical molecular dynamics simulations using the IUMD CPU/GPU hybrid code with a two-body nucleon-nucleon potential fit to properties of finite nuclei, pure neutron matter, and symmetric nuclear matter, plus screened Coulomb interactions between protons. They simulated systems at constant density n=0.05 fm^-3 and temperature T=1.0 MeV for proton fractions Y_p=0.30 (five runs with 51,200 to 3,276,800 nucleons) and Y_p=0.40 (seven runs with 51,200 to 819,200 nucleons). They developed an algorithm to identify sub-domains within the simulation volume by computing proton elastic structure factors and assigning each proton to the domain whose characteristic momentum transfer maximizes its individual structure factor.

## Key numbers

- Simulations contained up to 3,276,800 nucleons for Y_p=0.30 and 819,200 nucleons for Y_p=0.40, making them the largest nuclear pasta simulations to date
- All simulations performed at constant nucleon number density n=0.05 fm^-3 and temperature T=1.0 MeV
- For Y_p=0.30 runs: box side lengths ranged from 100.8 fm (51,200 nucleons) to 403.2 fm (3,276,800 nucleons)
- The 3,276,800 nucleon Y_p=0.30 run showed 5 domains plus a defects domain at the end of the run
- The 819,200 nucleon Y_p=0.30 run showed 1 domain plus a defects domain at the end
- The screening length lambda was set to 10 fm for all simulations
- The domain identification algorithm requires the angle between domain normals to be greater than approximately 5 degrees for reliable identification

## Context

This work extends previous molecular dynamics studies of nuclear pasta by Horowitz et al. (2004) and Schneider et al. (2014, 2016) to much larger system sizes, addressing finite size effects that have constrained earlier simulations. The results show that quantum molecular dynamics simulations by Nandi and Schramm (2018) containing 8,192 to 16,384 nucleons likely still suffer from considerable finite size effects, even though qualitative agreement in structure factors is encouraging. The domain identification algorithm and the demonstration that multiple domains can form in large simulation volumes enable more accurate predictions of transport properties in neutron star crusts and inform companion work by Caplan et al. (2018) on elastic properties of nuclear pasta.
