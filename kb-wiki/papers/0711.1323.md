# Super-horizon fluctuations and acoustic oscillations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We focus on the initial state spatial anisotropies, originating at the thermalization stage, for central collisions in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. We propose that a plot of the root mean square values of the flow coefficients $\sqrt{\bar {v_n^2}} \equiv v_n^{rms}$, calculated in a lab fixed coordinate system, for a large range of $n$, from 1 to about 30, can give non-trivial information about the initial stages of the system and its evolution. We also argue that for all wavelengths $λ$ of the anisotropy (at the surface of the plasma region) much larger than the acoustic horizon size $H_s^{fr}$ at the freezeout stage, the resulting values of $v_n^{rms}$ should be suppressed by a factor of order $2H_s^{fr}/λ$. With initial flow being zero, we discuss the possibility that the resulting flow could show imprints of coherent oscillations in the plot of $v_n^{rms}$ for sub-horizon modes. For gold-gold collision at 200 GeV, these features are expected to occur for n $\ge$ 5, with $n < 4$ modes showing supression. This has strong similarities with the physics of the anisotropies of the cosmic microwave background radiation (CMBR) resulting from inflationary density fluctuations in the universe. It seems possible that the statistical fluctuations due to finite multiplicity may not be able to mask such features in the flow data, or, at least a non-trivial overall shape of the plot of $v_n^{rms}$ may be inferred. In that case, the successes of analysis of CMBR anisotropy power spectrum to get cosmological parameters can be applied for relativistic heavy-ion collisions to learn about various relevant parameters at the early stages of the evolving system.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the root-mean-square values of flow coefficients \(v_n^{rms}\) for central heavy-ion collisions, plotted over a wide range of \(n\) from 1 to about 30, can reveal information about initial-state spatial anisotropies and their evolution, with super-horizon modes (wavelengths larger than the acoustic horizon at freezeout) being suppressed by a factor of order \(2H_s^{fr}/\lambda\). The claim is conditional on the assumption that initial flow velocities are negligible at thermalization and that statistical fluctuations from finite multiplicity may not mask these features.

## Method

The authors use HIJING simulations to generate initial transverse energy density distributions for central Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV/A, assuming thermalization at proper time \(\tau_{eq} = 1\) fm. They calculate Fourier coefficients \(F_n\) of spatial anisotropies from the energy-density-weighted boundary of the plasma region, and argue that the resulting momentum anisotropy coefficients \(v_n^{rms}\) will be suppressed for modes with wavelength \(\lambda\) larger than the acoustic horizon at freezeout \(H_s^{fr}\). The analysis draws an analogy to CMBR physics, treating the freezeout surface as analogous to the last scattering surface, with super-horizon fluctuations and possible acoustic oscillations for sub-horizon modes.

## Key numbers

- For Au-Au collision at 200 GeV/A, the average transverse radius at thermalization \(\bar{R}(\tau_{eq})\) is about 3 fm from HIJING.
- The freezeout time for flow anisotropies is estimated as \(\tau_{fr} = \bar{R}/c_s + \tau_{eq} \simeq 6\) fm, using sound speed \(c_s = 1/\sqrt{3}\).
- The acoustic horizon at freezeout is \(H_s^{fr} = c_s (\tau_{fr} - \tau_{eq}) = \bar{R} \simeq 3\) fm.
- For a fluctuation with wavelength \(\lambda = 2\) fm, the estimated time for sign reversal of spatial anisotropy is \(\tau_{flip} \simeq \lambda/(2c_s) \simeq 2\) fm.
- The minimum harmonic order below which suppression occurs is given by \(n_{min} = \pi (1 + 0.05 \bar{R}(\tau_{eq})/c_s^2)\), with features expected for \(n \ge 5\) and \(n < 4\) modes showing suppression.

## Context

This work extends the analogy between relativistic heavy-ion collisions and cosmology, specifically the analysis of CMBR anisotropy power spectra as developed in the cosmology literature (e.g., the standard inflationary paradigm). It builds on prior studies of elliptic flow and initial-state fluctuations in central collisions, such as those using HIJING simulations (Wang and Gyulassy, 1991) and investigations of eccentricity fluctuations (e.g., Manly et al., 2006). The paper proposes that the successes of CMBR analysis in extracting cosmological parameters could be applied to heavy-ion collisions to learn about parameters of the early-stage evolving system, such as thermalization time and freezeout conditions.
