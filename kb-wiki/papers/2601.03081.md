# Disturbing moving fluids

> In Newtonian physics, the excitation spectrum of a fluid is the same in all reference frames, up to a trivial shift. In special relativity, this is no longer the case. Relativity of simultaneity causes different inertial observers to measure markedly different excitation spectra, with stability being the only property known to be Lorentz invariant in all causal theories. Here, we show that, under a certain Onsager-like symmetry principle (which applies to kinetic theory and transient hydrodynamics), it is possible to place rigorous bounds on phase velocities, eigenmode convergence radii, spectral gaps, and equilibration rates in any inertial frame, using only information about the rest frame spectrum at zero wavenumber. The conventional intuition coming from time dilation is also shown to lead to generically wrong predictions, but becomes accurate if the fluid is non-relativistic in the rest frame.

## Key claim
Under an Onsager-like symmetry principle that applies to kinetic theory and transient hydrodynamics, rigorous bounds can be placed on phase velocities, eigenmode convergence radii, spectral gaps, and equilibration rates in any inertial frame using only information about the rest frame spectrum at zero wavenumber; the conventional intuition from time dilation leads to generically wrong predictions but becomes accurate if the fluid is non-relativistic in the rest frame.

## Method
The author considers linearized perturbations about a homogeneous equilibrium state governed by a first-order system of coupled partial differential equations with Hermitian constant background matrices, where the matrix multiplying the time derivative is positive definite in every inertial frame and the dissipation matrix is non-negative definite. The analysis derives bounds on the nonhydrodynamic spectrum (at zero wavenumber) in a boosted frame by applying spectral theory to the eigenvalue problem, using the properties of the orthogonal projector onto the orthogonal complement of the kernel of the dissipation matrix. The bounds are then extended to cases where signal propagation in the rest frame is bounded by a maximal speed less than the speed of light.

## Key numbers
- For a boost with velocity v=1/2, the diffusive mode ω=-ik² transforms into two branches ω'±=2k'+√3 i(1±√(1-√3 i k')), one of which is unstable and gapped, with both possessing only finite radius of convergence about k'=0.
- The nonhydrodynamic spectrum at k'=0 in a boosted frame is confined within the interval a(1-v)/γ ≤ iω' ≤ b/γ(1-v), where a and b are the lower and upper bounds of the rest-frame nonhydrodynamic spectrum at k=0 and γ=(1-v²)^{-1/2}.
- For Cattaneo's theory with a=b=1, the boosted nonhydrodynamic eigenfrequencies are iω'₁=1/γ(1-v²w²) and iω'₂,₃=1/γ, where w is the signal speed.
- For kinetic theory in the Relaxation Time Approximation with a=b=1, the boosted nonhydrodynamic excitations form a continuum iω'=γ(1+vw) with -1≤w≤1.
- The radius of convergence R' for the hydrodynamic mode in a boosted frame satisfies R' ≥ a'/2||E¹|| ≥ a(1-v)/2γ, where a' is the boosted spectral gap.
- When signal propagation is bounded by maximal speed w≤1, the boosted nonhydrodynamic frequencies are confined within a(1-vw)/γ ≤ iω' ≤ b/γ(1-vw).

## Context
This work extends the analysis of relativistic fluid spectra beyond the rest frame, where previous work by Hiscock and Lindblom (1983) and Kovtun (2019) established stability conditions, and Gavassino (2021, 2023) showed that stability is Lorentz invariant in causal theories. The paper builds on the symmetric-hyperbolic formulation of relativistic hydrodynamics developed by Geroch and Lindblom (1990) and applied to various frameworks including Israel-Stewart theory, divergence-type theories, and GENERIC. The results enable rigorous definition of density-frame hydrodynamics in any inertial frame, extending the work of Armas (2020) and Basar and Bhambure (2024), and raise questions about Lorentz invariance of quasi-hydrodynamic descriptions studied by Stephanov (2017), Grozdanov (2019), and others.
