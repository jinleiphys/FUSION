# Hindrance in heavy-ion fusion for lighter systems of astrophysical interest

> The hindrance in fusion of heavy-ion reactions crops up in the region of extreme sub-barrier energies. This phenomenon can be effectively analyzed using a simple diffused barrier formula derived assuming a Gaussian distribution of fusion barrier heights. Folding the Gaussian barrier distribution with the classical expression for the fusion cross section for a fixed barrier, the fusion cross section is obtained. The energy dependence of the fusion cross section provides good description to the existing data on sub-barrier heavy-ion fusion for lighter systems of astrophysical interest. Using this simple formula, an analysis has been presented from $^{16}$O + $^{18}$O to $^{12}$C + $^{198}$Pt, all of which were measured down to $<$ 10 $μ$b. The agreement of the present analysis with the measured values is better than those calculated even from the sophisticated coupled channels calculations. The relatively smooth variation of the three parameters of this formula implies that it may be exploited to estimate the excitation function or to extrapolate cross sections for pairs of interacting nuclei which are yet to be measured. Possible extensions of the present methodology and its limitations have also been discussed.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a simple diffused-barrier formula, derived by folding a Gaussian distribution of fusion barrier heights with the classical expression for fusion cross section, provides a good description of sub-barrier heavy-ion fusion data for lighter systems of astrophysical interest, with agreement better than sophisticated coupled-channels calculations. The hedge is that the formula is limited to monotonically rising excitation functions and cannot describe fusion at higher energies where incomplete fusion or deep-inelastic scattering occurs, nor systems with oscillations or resonance structures.

## Method

The authors assumed a Gaussian distribution of fusion barrier heights with mean barrier \(B_0\) and width \(\sigma_B\), and folded it with the classical expression for fusion cross section for a fixed barrier, which depends on the effective barrier radius \(R_B\). They then performed least-square fits of the three free parameters (\(B_0\), \(\sigma_B\), \(R_B\)) to experimental fusion excitation functions for seven reactions ranging from \(^{16}\)O + \(^{18}\)O to \(^{12}\)C + \(^{198}\)Pt, all measured down to cross sections below 10 \(\mu\)b.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{12}\)C + \(^{24}\)Mg: mean barrier height \(B_0 = 11.483\) MeV, width \(\sigma_B = 0.815\) MeV, effective radius \(R_B = 6.646\) fm.
- For \(^{12}\)C + \(^{30}\)Si: \(B_0 = 13.540\) MeV, \(\sigma_B = 1.090\) MeV, \(R_B = 8.300\) fm.
- For \(^{12}\)C + \(^{198}\)Pt: \(B_0 = 55.140\) MeV, \(\sigma_B = 1.749\) MeV, \(R_B = 11.179\) fm.
- For \(^{16}\)O + \(^{18}\)O: \(B_0 = 9.797\) MeV, \(\sigma_B = 0.859\) MeV, \(R_B = 7.743\) fm.
- For \(^{28}\)Si + \(^{64}\)Ni: \(B_0 = 50.403\) MeV, \(\sigma_B = 1.402\) MeV, \(R_B = 7.182\) fm.
- For \(^{58}\)Ni + \(^{58}\)Ni: \(B_0 = 98.278\) MeV, \(\sigma_B = 2.275\) MeV, \(R_B = 8.550\) fm.
- For \(^{64}\)Ni + \(^{64}\)Ni: \(B_0 = 92.646\) MeV, \(\sigma_B = 1.466\) MeV, \(R_B = 8.862\) fm.
- All experimental data were measured down to cross sections less than 10 \(\mu\)b.

## Context

This work extends the phenomenological analysis of sub-barrier fusion hindrance using a Gaussian barrier distribution, previously applied by Winther (2004) and others, to lighter systems of astrophysical interest relevant to carbon and oxygen burning in heavy stars. The authors compare their results to the same target-projectile combinations recently studied by Montagnoli et al. (2020), Montagnoli et al. (1997), Rath et al. (2020), and Weldon et al. (2021), and claim better agreement than sophisticated coupled-channels calculations. The smooth variation of the three extracted parameters suggests the formula can be used to estimate excitation functions or extrapolate cross sections for unmeasured pairs of interacting nuclei, with possible applications to superheavy element production via the sticking-diffusion-survival model of Antonenko et al. (1993).
