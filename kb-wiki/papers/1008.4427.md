# Lattice QCD and Nuclear Physics

> A short lecture given at the Summer School on "Modern perspectives in lattice QCD", Les Houches, August 3-28, 2009.

## Key claim

This lecture presents a method to extract nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials from lattice QCD using the Bethe-Salpeter wave function, with the caveat that the potential defined in this way is energy-independent and non-local, and its short-distance repulsive core can be theoretically investigated via the operator product expansion and renormalization group analysis.

## Method

The author first introduces Lüscher's formula, which relates the two-particle energy spectrum in a finite periodic box to the elastic scattering phase shift, using the Bethe-Salpeter (BS) wave function for the ππ system as an example. The method is then extended to define an energy-independent non-local NN potential from the BS wave function in the internal region where interactions exist, rather than only using the asymptotic region. Finally, the operator product expansion and renormalization group analysis in QCD are applied to investigate the origin of the repulsive core in the NN potential derived from the BS wave function.

## Key numbers

- The high-precision NN potentials fit more than 2000 data points with χ²/dof ≃ 1 at T_lab < 300 MeV.
- The long-range part of the nuclear force dominates at relative distance r > 2 fm, the medium-range part covers 1 fm < r < 2 fm with attraction of about 50–100 MeV, and the short-range repulsive core exists at r < 1 fm.
- The inelastic threshold for ππ scattering is given by k² < 3 m_π², where the total energy satisfies 2√(k² + m_π²) < 4 m_π.

## Context

This lecture extends the work of Lüscher (1990) on relating finite-volume energy spectra to scattering phase shifts, and presents an alternative approach developed by Ishii (2007), Aoki (2008), and Aoki (2010a) that defines the NN potential directly from the BS wave function. The approach enables first-principles lattice QCD studies of nuclear forces, potentially resolving long-standing questions about the origin of the repulsive core that phenomenological models such as those by Jastrow (1951) and various quark-based approaches have attempted to explain.
