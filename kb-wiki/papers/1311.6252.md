# An alternative explanation for the dibaryon suggested by experiments at the WASA facility at Julich

## Key claim

The dibaryon resonance at 2370 MeV claimed by the WASA collaboration (Pricking, Bashkanov and Clement) has logical flaws, and a natural alternative explanation arises from the production of the final state \(NN^*(1440)\), whose masses add to 2379 MeV, well within errors of the \(N^*(1440)\) mass. The narrow width of 70 MeV observed by WASA can be explained by the phase variation of the \(N^*(1440)\) amplitude over a 90 MeV range (using Nakamura's estimate) or 70 MeV (using the PDG mass), rather than requiring a genuine dibaryon resonance.

## Method

The paper re-examines the WASA collaboration's claim of a \(\Delta\Delta\) dibaryon at 2370 MeV by analyzing the reaction \(pd \to NN^*(1440)p_s\), where \(p_s\) is a spectator proton, producing five particles in the final state from three in the initial state. The author compares this interpretation with existing total cross section data from Rutherford Laboratory, the ABC effect (Abashian, Booth and Crowe), and the phase variation of the \(N^*(1440)\) amplitude from Nakamura's analysis, which shows the real part peaking at 1370 MeV and the imaginary part peaking at 1460 MeV. The paper also reviews historical dibaryon claims from the 1970s that were disproved by partial wave analyses including spin-dependent data from LAMPF and the Geneva group at PSI.

## Key numbers

- The WASA collaboration claims a dibaryon resonance at 2370 MeV with a width of 70 MeV.
- The alternative \(NN^*(1440)\) final state has masses adding to 2379 MeV.
- The WASA claim is for a 0.8 mb \(I=0\) signal in the final state \(np\pi^+\pi^-\), which is a factor of approximately 14 smaller than the peak cross section observed in \(I=0\) total cross sections at Rutherford Laboratory.
- The Rutherford Laboratory total cross sections had a statistical accuracy of \(\pm 0.1\%\) and point-to-point errors of \(\pm 0.2\%\), with a possible overall normalization error of 2 mb varying slowly over the entire range up to 8 GeV/c; the systematic uncertainty over the \(\Delta\Delta\) peak is less than 0.4 mb.
- The \(\Delta(1232)\pi\) branching ratio of \(N^*(1440)\) is 20-30% according to Particle Data Tables.
- The real part of the \(N^*(1440)\) amplitude peaks at 1370 MeV and the imaginary part peaks at 1460 MeV, with a \(45^\circ\) change in phase over this mass range.
- The phase shift of \(N^*(1440)\) reaches \(20^\circ\) at 1320 MeV and \(45^\circ\) at 1370 MeV.
- In the historical \(^1D_2\) amplitude from LAMPF experiments, a phase variation of \(33^\circ\) was observed, ruling out the \(360^\circ\) phase variation required for a dibaryon resonance.

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing debate on dibaryon resonances, following a series of WASA collaboration publications from 1997 to the present claiming a "hidden" dibaryon at 2370 MeV. The author contrasts this with earlier work by Faldt and Wilkin on the ABC effect, and with the historical dibaryon claims in the reaction \(pp \to d\pi^+\) over lab kinetic energies of 600-800 MeV (in \(^1D_2\), \(^3F_3\), and \(^3P_2\) partial waves) that were disproved by partial wave analyses including spin-dependent data from the Geneva group at PSI and LAMPF (Shypit, Hasan, Silbar). The paper also references recent data from Mielke et al. on \(dp \to ^3He\,\pi^+\pi^-\) and Nakamura's analysis of the \(N^*(1440)\) amplitude. The author concludes that what is needed is a polarized proton beam experiment measuring the same spin parameters as the LAMPF experiments to determine whether the \(NN^*(1440)\) channel accounts for the WASA data and how it interferes with \(\Delta\Delta\) final states.
