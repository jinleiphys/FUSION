# Nuclear liquid-gas phase transition studied with antisymmetrized molecular dynamics

> The nuclear liquid-gas phase transition of the system in ideal thermal equilibrium is studied with antisymmetrized molecular dynamics. The time evolution of a many-nucleon system confined in a container is solved for a long time to get a microcanonical ensemble of a given energy and volume. The temperature and the pressure are extracted from this ensemble and the caloric curves are constructed. The present work is the first time that a microscopic dynamical model which describes nuclear multifragmentation reactions well is directly applied to get the nuclear caloric curve. The obtained constant pressure caloric curves clearly show the characteristic feature of the liquid-gas phase transition, namely negative heat capacity (backbending), which is expected for the phase transition in finite systems.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD), a microscopic dynamical model already successful in describing nuclear multifragmentation reactions, can directly produce nuclear caloric curves showing the characteristic backbending (negative heat capacity) expected for a first-order liquid-gas phase transition in finite systems, provided the system is confined in a container and evolved long enough to sample a microcanonical ensemble.

## Method

The time evolution of a many-nucleon system confined in a spherical container of fixed volume was solved using AMD with the Gogny effective interaction, including stochastic two-nucleon collisions and a decoherence process. The state at each time step was treated as a sample of the microcanonical ensemble with fixed energy, volume, and particle number. Temperature was extracted from the average kinetic energy of gaseous nucleons, and pressure was obtained from the ensemble; constant-pressure caloric curves were then constructed by interpolating between constant-volume results.

## Key numbers

- The Gogny force yields nuclear matter incompressibility \(K = 228\) MeV in the uniform Hartree-Fock approximation.
- The normal nuclear matter density used is \(\rho_0 = 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The Gaussian wave packet width parameter is \(\nu = 0.16\) fm\(^{-2}\).
- The zero-point kinetic energy parameter is \(T_0 = 9.20\) MeV, close to the value \(3\hbar^2\nu/2M = 10.0\) MeV.
- The decoherence time is taken as \(\tau_0 = 500\) fm/\(c\) for the main calculations.
- Constant-pressure caloric curves are shown for pressures including \(P = 0.02, 0.03, 0.05, 0.07, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.40\) MeV/fm\(^3\).
- Constant-volume caloric curves are shown for average densities including \(\rho = 0.40\rho_0, 0.30\rho_0, 0.23\rho_0, 0.18\rho_0, 0.15\rho_0, 0.10\rho_0, 0.07\rho_0, 0.04\rho_0, 0.02\rho_0, 0.015\rho_0\).

## Context

This work extends earlier molecular dynamics studies of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition (e.g., Dorso, Peilert, Ohnishi (1997), Sugawa and Volkov) by using the same AMD framework (AMD/DS) that has already been successfully applied to nuclear collision simulations, and by employing the Gogny force, which satisfies nuclear matter saturation. It contrasts with conventional statistical models (e.g., Gross, Randrup, Bondorf) by deriving the caloric curve from nucleon-nucleon interactions rather than externally supplied binding energies and level densities, and by naturally including interactions among fragments. The results enable future comparisons between ideal thermal equilibrium calculations and dynamical multifragmentation reactions simulated with the same model.
