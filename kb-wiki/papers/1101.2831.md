# Physics around the QCD (tri)critical endpoint and new challenges for femtoscopy

> On the basis of exactly solvable models with the tricritical and critical endpoints I discuss the physical mechanism of endpoints formation which is similar to the usual liquids. It is demonstrated that the necessary condition for the transformation of the 1-st order deconfinement phase transition into the 2-nd order phase transition at the (tri)critical endpoint is the vanishing of surface tension coefficient of large/heavy QGP bags. Using the novel model of the confinement phenomenon I argue that the physical reason for the cross-over appearance at low baryonic densities is the negative value of QGP bag surface tension coefficient. This implies the existence of highly non-spherical or, probably, even fractal surfaces of large and heavy bags at and above the cross-over, which, perhaps, can be observed via some correlations. The model with the tricritical endpoint predicts that at the deconfinement transition line the volume (mass) distribution of large (heavy) QGP bags acquires the power law form at the endpoint only, while in the model with the critical endpoint such a power law exists inside the mixed phase. The role of finite width of QGP bags is also discussed.

## Key claim

The necessary condition for the deconfinement phase transition to become second-order at the (tri)critical endpoint is the vanishing of the surface tension coefficient of large/heavy QGP bags; the physical reason for the cross-over at low baryonic densities is the negative value of that surface tension coefficient, which implies highly non-spherical or fractal bag surfaces.

## Method

The author analyzes exactly solvable statistical models (Model 1 with a tricritical endpoint and Model 2 with a critical endpoint) that generalize the Fisher droplet model and the statistical multifragmentation model. These models use the isobaric partition function to study the volume (mass) distribution of QGP bags, parameterizing the surface free energy via a reduced surface tension coefficient Σ(T,μ) that can change sign. The surface tension of QGP bags is also related to the string tension of the confining color tube via an equation equating the free energies of a confining string and an elongated cylindrical bag.

## Key numbers

- The surface tension coefficient at T=0 is estimated as σ_surf(T=0) ≈ (0.183 GeV)^3 ≈ 157.4 MeV fm^{-2}, using the bag model pressure p_v(T=0) = -(0.25)^4 GeV^4, lattice QCD values R=0.5 fm and σ_str(T=0) = (0.42)^2 GeV^2.
- For the finite width model, the mean width of a resonance of mass M behaves as Γ(M) ≈ 400-600 [M/M_0]^{1/2} MeV with M_0 ≈ 2.5 GeV.
- The power κ < 1 in the bag effective surface term is a constant that may differ from the usual value 2/3.
- Model 1 corresponds to the Fisher parameter 1 < τ ≤ 2; Model 2 requires τ > 2.

## Context

This work extends the Fisher droplet model (Fisher 1967) and the statistical multifragmentation model to the QCD deconfinement transition, showing that the surface tension mechanism for critical endpoint formation is analogous to that in ordinary liquids. The analysis builds on earlier exactly solvable bag models (QGBSTM, QGBSTM2) and the Hills and Dales Model (Bugaev 2004, 2005). The results enable new femtoscopy challenges: distinguishing positive, zero, and negative surface tension via emission patterns from non-spherical bags; searching for power-law volume (mass) distributions of large bags; and investigating the influence of the finite width of QGP bags on their space-time evolution in heavy-ion collisions.
