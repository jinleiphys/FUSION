# Empirical formula for total inelastic cross-section of proton-nucleus scattering

> We propose a generic empirical formula for total inelastic cross-sections for various target nuclei scattered by a proton at different energies, which is applicable over a wide range of energy from $15 ~MeV$ to $1~ TeV$. The proposed model is parameterized based on the fitting of extensively studied experimental cross-section data for the Aluminium and Carbon nucleus targets, considering factorization over high-energy and low-energy regimes. The parameters in high-energy formula are determined by the fitting of the high-energy saturation value of the inelastic scattering cross-section data with mass numbers. The universality of the empirical formula is investigated by comparing the model prediction with the experimental data of inelastic proton-nucleus scattering over a wide range from light elements such as Deuterium to heavy elements such as Uranium. A detailed comparison with the existing models and GEANT4 simulation is also presented.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new empirical formula for proton-nucleus total inelastic cross-sections that achieves uniform precision across a wide energy range (15 MeV to 1 TeV) and target masses while remaining computationally simple, but the model parameters are determined by fitting only to Aluminium and Carbon data, and the universality claim is qualified by noting that experimental data are lacking for several nuclei and energy ranges.

## Method

The authors factorize the inelastic cross-section into high-energy and low-energy components. The high-energy formula (above 5 GeV) uses a power-law with oscillatory and heavy-element correction terms fitted to experimental data from Bobchenko et al. (1979) for multiple target masses. The low-energy correction includes an energy-dependent oscillatory function and a Coulomb barrier term, with parameters determined by least chi-square fitting of experimental data for Aluminium and Carbon targets using machine learning methods. The model is then tested against experimental data for 33 different nuclei from Deuterium to Uranium and compared with the Letaw et al. (1983), Shen (1991), Tripathi et al. (1997), and Nakano et al. (2021) models as well as GEANT4 simulations.

## Key numbers

- Energy range of applicability: 15 MeV to 1 TeV
- High-energy formula parameters: a = 47.4, b = 0.676, c = 0.045, d = 0.42, e = 0.000018, f = 1.63
- Reduced chi-square per degree of freedom for high-energy fit: 0.21
- Reduced chi-square per degree of freedom for Carbon fit: 3.29; for Aluminium fit: 3.72
- Low-energy function parameters: alpha = 0.3, beta = 3742, gamma = 68, lambda = -0.83, epsilon = 134
- High-energy saturation cross-section for p-2H: approximately 72.9 mb
- For p-9Be, the model predicts a peak around 25 MeV with maximum cross-section approximately 525 mb, while experimental data indicate a higher peak of about 600 mb at 15 MeV
- For p-28Si, the saturation value at high energy differs by about 19% from model results
- For p-4He at high energies, the model exhibits a deviation of approximately 17% from experimental measurements

## Context

This work extends the empirical parameterization approach initiated by Letaw et al. (1983), who proposed one of the earliest models for proton-nucleus inelastic cross-sections valid from 10 MeV to 1 GeV, and subsequent refinements by Shen (1991) who added Coulomb corrections, Tripathi et al. (1997) who developed a universal parameterization treating proton-nucleus collisions as a limiting case of nucleus-nucleus interactions, and Nakano et al. (2021) who incorporated Discrete Level Constraint and Coulomb effects. The authors claim their model achieves better performance across both light and heavy nuclei compared to these prior approaches, which each showed limitations for specific mass ranges or energy regimes. The formula enables prediction of unmeasured nuclear cross-sections for applications in medical radiation therapy, accelerator shielding, space radiation protection, and detector development.
