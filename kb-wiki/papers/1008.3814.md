# Determination of fragmentation functions and their application to exotic-hadron search

> We discuss studies on determination of fragmentation functions and an application to exotic-hadron search by using characteristic differences between favored and disfavored functions. The optimum fragmentation functions are determined for pion, kaon, and proton in the leading order (LO) and next-to-leading order (NLO) of the running coupling constant alpha_s by global analyses of hadron-production data in electron-positron annihilation. Various parametrization results are much different in disfavored-quark and gluon fragmentation functions; however, we show that they are within uncertainties of the determined functions by using the Hessian method for uncertainty estimation. We find that the uncertainties are especially large in the disfavored-quark and gluon fragmentation functions. NLO improvements are explicitly shown in the determination by comparing uncertainties of the LO and NLO functions. Next, we propose to use differences between favored and disfavored fragmentation functions for determining internal quark configurations of exotic hadrons. We make a global analysis for f_0 (980) for finding its internal configuration; however, uncertainties are too large to specify the structure at this stage.

## Key claim
The authors determine fragmentation functions (FFs) for pion, kaon, and proton at leading order (LO) and next-to-leading order (NLO) and show that large discrepancies among different parametrization groups for disfavored-quark and gluon FFs are actually within the estimated uncertainties of their own analysis, rather than indicating errors by any group. They then propose using differences between favored and disfavored FFs to identify internal quark configurations of exotic hadrons, but find that current data for the f0(980) meson yield uncertainties too large to specify its structure.

## Method
The FFs are parametrized at a fixed scale Q0^2 using a functional form N_i^h z^{alpha_i^h} (1-z)^{beta_i^h} for each parton type (favored light quarks, disfavored light quarks, gluon, charm, bottom). The parameters are determined by a chi-squared global analysis of hadron-production data from e+e- annihilation experiments (TASSO, TPC, HRS, TOPAZ, SLD, ALEPH, OPAL, DELPHI) at various center-of-mass energies. The Hessian method is used to estimate uncertainties by expanding chi^2 around its minimum and computing the covariance matrix of the parameters.

## Key numbers
- The total number of data points used for charged-pion production is 264, from experiments with center-of-mass energies ranging from 12 GeV to 91.28 GeV.
- For the pion at Q^2 = 1 GeV^2, the second moment of the favored fragmentation function D_u^{pi+} is 0.401 +/- 0.052; the disfavored D_{bar u}^{pi+} is 0.094 +/- 0.029.
- For the kaon at Q^2 = 1 GeV^2, the favored D_u^{K+} second moment is 0.0740 +/- 0.0268; the favored D_{bar s}^{K+} is 0.0878 +/- 0.0506; the disfavored D_{bar u}^{K+} is 0.0255 +/- 0.0173.
- For the proton at Q^2 = 1 GeV^2, the favored D_u^{p} second moment is 0.0732 +/- 0.0113; the disfavored D_{bar u}^{p} is 0.0084 +/- 0.0057.
- For the f0(980) meson, the determined second moments are M_u = 0.0012 +/- 0.0107, M_s = 0.0027 +/- 0.0183, M_g = 0.0090 +/- 0.0046; the ratio M_u/M_s = 0.43 +/- 6.73.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous global analysis of FFs (Hirai et al. 2007) by explicitly comparing LO and NLO results and quantifying uncertainties via the Hessian method, which had been applied earlier to parton distribution functions (PDFs) for nucleons and nuclei. The paper clarifies why earlier parametrizations by KKP, Kretzer, AKK, and DSS showed large differences in disfavored-quark and gluon FFs, showing they are consistent within the error bands. The proposed method for exotic-hadron search using favored/disfavored FF differences (Hirai et al. 2008) is applied to the f0(980) meson, but the large uncertainties prevent a definitive conclusion on whether it is a nonstrange q\bar q, strange q\bar q, tetraquark, or glueball state. Future data from Belle are expected to improve the determination.
