# The diffusion equation is compatible with special relativity

> Due to its parabolic character, the diffusion equation exhibits instantaneous spatial spreading, and becomes unstable when Lorentz-boosted. According to the conventional interpretation, these features reflect a fundamental incompatibility with special relativity. In this Letter, we show that this interpretation is incorrect by demonstrating that any smooth and sufficiently localized solution of the diffusion equation is the particle density of an exact solution of the relativistic Vlasov-Fokker-Planck equation. This establishes the existence of a causal, stable, and thermodynamically consistent relativistic kinetic theory whose hydrodynamic sector is governed exactly by diffusion at all wavelengths. We further demonstrate that the standard arguments for instability arise from considering solutions that admit no counterpart in kinetic theory, and that apparent violations of causality disappear once signals are defined in terms of the underlying microscopic data.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the diffusion equation is fully compatible with special relativity when interpreted as an exact hydrodynamic sector of a relativistic kinetic theory, contrary to the widespread belief that its parabolic character implies acausality and instability. The key hedge is that this compatibility holds only for smooth, spatially localized solutions that admit an embedding into the relativistic Vlasov-Fokker-Planck equation; the standard arguments for instability arise from considering solutions that have no counterpart in kinetic theory.

## Method
The author constructs explicit solutions of the relativistic Vlasov-Fokker-Planck (VFP) equation in one spatial dimension, showing that any smooth and sufficiently localized solution of the diffusion equation corresponds to the particle density of an exact VFP solution. The proof proceeds by defining a kinetic distribution function f(t,x,p) as an integral over Fourier modes, demonstrating by direct substitution that it satisfies the VFP equation, and showing that its momentum integral yields a function n(t,x) obeying the standard diffusion equation. The analysis is then extended to examine the stability of plane-wave perturbations and the causality of signal propagation in the kinetic theory.

## Key numbers
- The VFP equation contains a momentum-space diffusivity parameter (beta^2 * D)^(-1), where beta is the inverse temperature and D is the spatial diffusivity.
- For the ultrarelativistic limit m -> 0, the embedding kernel reduces to the polynomial pi*beta*(1 + D^2 * k^2), allowing closed-form evaluation of the distribution function.
- The unstable modes of the diffusion equation take the form delta n = exp((t - Vx)/(D * V^2)), which produce an infinite particle density when embedded into the kinetic theory, demonstrating they lie outside the admissible solution space.
- In the ultrarelativistic regime, the hydrodynamic mode has the diffusive form omega = -i*D*k^2, confined to the strip |Im k| < (2*D)^(-1) to ensure the covariant stability bound Im omega <= |Im k| is respected.

## Context
This work resolves a long-standing tension in relativistic hydrodynamics, directly contradicting the conventional view established by Morse and Feshbach (1953), Cattaneo (1958), and Israel and Stewart (1979) that parabolic dissipative theories are incompatible with relativity. It extends the alternative perspective advocated by Kost (2000), Geroch (1995), and Lindblom (1996) that diffusion appears problematic only when extended beyond its microscopic domain of validity. The results enable a reassessment of hyperbolic relativistic theories such as Cattaneo-type models, Israel-Stewart theory, and the BDNK framework, showing they remain indispensable as controlled approximations for boosted frames and rotating fluids, but are not intrinsically more "relativistic" than Fick's law.
