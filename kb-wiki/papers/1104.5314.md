# How Robust is the Froissart Bound?

> Proof of the Froissart theorem is reconsidered in a different way to extract its necessary conditions. Two physical inputs, unitarity and absence of massless intermediate hadrons, are indisputable. Also important are mathematical properties of the Legendre functions. Assumptions on dispersion relations, single or double, appear to be excessive. Instead, one should make assumptions on possible high-energy asymptotics of the amplitude in nonphysical configurations, which have today no firm basis. Asymptotics for the physical amplitude always appear essentially softer than for the nonphysical one. Froissart's paper explicitly assumed the hypothesis of power behavior and obtained asymptotic bound for total cross sections $\sim \log^2{(s/s_0)}$ with some constant $s_0$. Our bounds are slightly stronger than original Froissart ones. They show that the scale $s_0$ should itself slowly grow with $s$. Under different assumptions about asymptotic behavior of nonphysical amplitudes, the total cross section could grow even faster than $\log^2{s}$. The problem of correct asymptotics might be clarified by precise measurements at the LHC and higher energies.

## Key claim

The Froissart bound on total cross sections (growth no faster than \(\log^2 s\)) is not mathematically rigorous for hadron processes because it relies on assumptions about the high-energy asymptotics of amplitudes in nonphysical configurations that have no firm basis; under different assumptions about those nonphysical amplitudes, the total cross section could grow even faster than \(\log^2 s\).

## Method

The author rederives bounds for partial-wave amplitudes using only unitarity, the absence of massless intermediate hadrons, and analyticity properties of Legendre functions, without invoking single- or double-dispersion relations or axiomatic field theory. By deforming the integration contour for the partial-wave projection to the Lehmann ellipse and using the asymptotic form of the Legendre function of the second kind \(Q_l\), the author obtains a stricter exponential bound on \(|a_l|\) at large \(l\) than Froissart's original. This bound is then used to estimate the forward amplitude, fixed-angle amplitude, and fixed-\(t\) amplitude, with the number of essential partial waves \(L\) determined by the intersection of the unitarity bound and the exponential bound.

## Key numbers

* The total cross section bound is \(\sim \log^2(s/s_0)\) with a scale \(s_0\) that "should itself slowly grow with \(s\)" (no explicit numerical value for \(s_0\) is given).
* The fixed-\(t\) amplitude bound behaves as \(\sim Y^{3/2}/\alpha_0^2\), where \(Y = \alpha_0 L\) and \(\alpha_0 \approx 2\sqrt{t_0/s}\) at high energies, yielding a \(t\)-dependence \(\sim (-t)^{-1/4}\).
* The forward-peak slope from the bound is \(b^{\mathrm{(max)}} = Y^2/(4 t_0)\), with the same high-energy growth as the total cross section bound.
* The fixed-angle amplitude bound at high energies is \(\sim (Y/\alpha_0)^{3/2}\), which is softer than the forward bound \(\sim (Y/\alpha_0)^2\).

## Context

This work reexamines the derivation of the Froissart bound (Froissart 1961) and Martin's later proof (Martin 1963, 1965) that used axiomatic local field theory. The author argues that applying such axiomatic methods to hadron processes is dubious because QCD, the underlying theory, does not have isolated one-quark or one-gluon states due to confinement. The paper extends the discussion to fixed-\(t\) amplitudes, which Froissart did not treat, and suggests that precise LHC measurements could clarify whether the true asymptotic behavior is \(\log^2 s\) or a power law such as \(s^\delta\) with \(\delta \sim 0.08\) (as in fits by Landshoff).
