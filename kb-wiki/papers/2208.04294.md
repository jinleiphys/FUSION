# Degeneracy in the inference of phase transitions in the neutron star equation of state from gravitational wave data

> Gravitational wave (GW) detections of binary neutron star inspirals will be crucial for constraining the dense matter equation of state (EoS). We demonstrate a new degeneracy in the mapping from tidal deformability data to the EoS, which occurs for models with strong phase transitions. We find that there exists a new family of EoS with phase transitions that set in at different densities and that predict neutron star radii that differ by up to ~500m, but that produce nearly identical tidal deformabilities for all neutron star masses. Next generation GW detectors and advances in nuclear theory may be needed to resolve this degeneracy.

# Degeneracy in the inference of phase transitions in the neutron star equation of state from gravitational wave data

## Key claim
There exists a new degeneracy in the mapping from gravitational wave tidal deformability data to the neutron star equation of state, specifically for models with strong first-order phase transitions: families of EoS with phase transitions setting in at different densities can produce nearly identical tidal deformabilities across all astrophysically observed neutron star masses, even though their predicted radii differ by up to ~500 m. This degeneracy cannot be resolved from gravitational wave data alone with current detectors, and may require next-generation detectors combined with advances in nuclear theory.

## Method
The authors performed Bayesian inference of the neutron star equation of state using mock tidal deformability data generated from an EoS with a first-order phase transition at 1.7 times nuclear saturation density. They assumed Gaussian measurement uncertainties corresponding to the projected sensitivity of LIGO's fifth observing run (A+) and the proposed next-generation detector Cosmic Explorer, with 16 mock GW170817-like events spaced evenly across the mass range 1.2-2 solar masses. They also constructed explicit pairs of "tidal deformability doppelganger" EoS models with first-order phase transitions at different densities, and performed binary neutron star merger simulations to extract post-merger gravitational wave peak frequencies.

## Key numbers
- Neutron star radii predicted by doppelganger EoS pairs differ by up to ~500 m (from Bayesian inference) or ~300 m (from constructed model pairs)
- For A+ sensitivity, the 68% confidence interval constrains the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star to within 500 m and the pressure at 1.7 times saturation density to within a factor of 5.8
- For Cosmic Explorer sensitivity (sigma_Lambda = 8), the 68% confidence interval constrains R_1.4 to within ~500 m and pressure at 1.7 times saturation density to within a factor of 5.7
- Absolute differences in tidal deformability between doppelganger pairs are Delta Lambda less than or equal to 5, with fractional differences less than or equal to 1-2% for intermediate-mass stars
- For the A+ configuration, the anticipated 1-sigma measurement uncertainty in tidal deformability is sigma_Lambda = 46
- For Cosmic Explorer, the anticipated 1-sigma measurement uncertainty is sigma_Lambda = 8, with potential constraints of sigma_Lambda approximately 1-4 for a population of mergers
- Post-merger peak frequencies for one doppelganger pair (R_1.4 = 10.8 and 11.2 km) were both f_2 = 3.39 kHz; for another pair (R_1.4 = 12.8 and 13.2 km), f_2 = 2.71 and 2.65 kHz respectively
- Bayes factor between example doppelganger EoS models was 1.9 for A+ data (insufficient evidence) and 3.3 for XG data (substantial evidence)

## Context
This work extends the study of degeneracies in equation of state inference from gravitational wave data, building on prior work by Gamba (2019) which showed that changing the crust EoS at subnuclear densities can change radius without significantly affecting tidal deformabilities. The authors demonstrate that similar degeneracies exist at supranuclear densities for models with strong phase transitions, which has implications for efforts to identify deconfined quark matter or other exotic degrees of freedom in neutron star cores (as studied by Paschalidis 2017, Christian 2018, Dexheimer 2018, and others). The results motivate the construction of next-generation gravitational wave facilities such as Einstein Telescope, Cosmic Explorer, and NEMO, and highlight the importance of continued advances in nuclear theory constraints around nuclear saturation (Drischler 2021, Tews 2022) to break the degeneracy. The post-merger analysis confirms that existing quasi-universal relations (Bauswein 2012, Takami 2014, Vretinaris 2019, Raithel 2022) are not violated by the doppelganger models, but also that post-merger peak frequencies may not differentiate between some doppelganger pairs within current numerical uncertainties.
