# Nuclear Pasta Formation

> The formation of complex nonuniform phases of nuclear matter, known as nuclear pasta, is studied with molecular dynamics simulations containing 51200 nucleons. A phenomenological nuclear interaction is used that reproduces the saturation binding energy and density of nuclear matter. Systems are prepared at an initial density of 0.10fm$^{-3}$ and then the density is decreased by expanding the simulation volume at different rates to densities of 0.01 fm$^{-3}$ or less. An originally uniform system of nuclear matter is observed to form spherical bubbles ("swiss cheese"), hollow tubes, flat plates ("lasagna"), thin rods ("spaghetti") and, finally, nearly spherical nuclei with decreasing density. We explicitly observe nucleation mechanisms, with decreasing density, for these different pasta phase transitions. Topological quantities known as Minkowski functionals are obtained to characterize the pasta shapes. Different pasta shapes are observed depending on the expansion rate. This indicates non equilibrium effects. We use this to determine the best ways to obtain lower energy states of the pasta system from MD simulations and to place constrains on the equilibration time of the system.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the formation of nuclear pasta phases depends on the expansion rate of the simulation volume, indicating non-equilibrium effects; the authors use this dependence to place constraints on the equilibration time of the system rather than claiming to have reached equilibrium configurations.

## Method

The authors performed semiclassical molecular dynamics simulations with 51,200 nucleons using a phenomenological two-body nuclear interaction that reproduces saturation binding energy and density. Systems were initialized at a density of 0.10 fm$^{-3}$ and then expanded at different rates ($\dot\xi = 10^{-5}$, $10^{-6}$, and $10^{-7}$ c/fm) to densities of 0.01 fm$^{-3}$ or less, with additional constant-density runs at fixed densities of 0.010, 0.025, 0.050, 0.075, and 0.090 fm$^{-3}$ for comparison. The topological properties of the resulting pasta structures were characterized using Minkowski functionals (mean breadth B and Euler characteristic $\chi$) computed from charge-density isosurfaces.

## Key numbers

- Simulation size: 51,200 nucleons with proton fraction $Y_p = 0.40$ at temperature $T = 1$ MeV
- Initial density for expansion runs: 0.10 fm$^{-3}$; final densities: 0.01 fm$^{-3}$ or less
- Three expansion rates used: $\dot\xi = 1.0 \times 10^{-5}$, $1.0 \times 10^{-6}$, and $1.0 \times 10^{-7}$ c/fm
- Constant-density runs evolved for 500,000 fm/c with time step $\Delta t = 1$ fm/c
- Threshold charge density for Minkowski functional analysis: $n_{\text{th}} = 0.030$ fm$^{-3}$
- Gaussian width for nucleon density distributions: $\sigma = 1.5$ fm
- At $n = 0.090$ fm$^{-3}$, normalized mean breadth $B/A$ values range from $-1.5 \times 10^{-2}$ fm$^{-1}$ (no Coulomb) to $-1.0 \times 10^{-1}$ fm$^{-1}$ (fastest stretch)
- At $n = 0.010$ fm$^{-3}$, normalized Euler characteristic $\chi/A$ values range from $+1.5 \times 10^{-12}$ fm$^{-2}$ (no Coulomb) to $+3.5 \times 10^{-3}$ fm$^{-2}$ (constant density)

## Context

This work extends previous molecular dynamics studies of nuclear pasta by Horowitz et al. (2004-2013) that focused on transport properties, shifting emphasis to equilibration mechanisms and topological transitions. The results contrast with quantum molecular dynamics simulations by Watanabe et al. (2004-2009), who found significantly faster transition times between pasta phases. The study enables improved understanding of equilibration timescales for nuclear pasta, which is relevant for determining the structure and properties of neutron stars, the equation of state of nuclear matter, and neutrino transport in supernovae.
