# Phase diagram of QCD at finite temperature and chemical potential from lattice simulations with dynamical Wilson quarks

> We present the first results for lattice QCD at finite temperature $T$ and chemical potential $μ$ with four flavors of Wilson quarks. The calculations are performed using the imaginary chemical potential method at $κ=0$, 0.001, 0.15, 0.165, 0.17 and 0.25, where $κ$ is the hopping parameter, related to the bare quark mass $m$ and lattice spacing $a$ by $κ=1/(2ma+8)$. Such a method allows us to do large scale Monte Carlo simulations at imaginary chemical potential $μ=i μ_I$. By analytic continuation of the data with $μ_I < πT/3$ to real values of the chemical potential, we expect at each $κ\in [0,κ_{chiral}]$, a transition line on the $(μ, T)$ plane, in a region relevant to the search for quark gluon plasma in heavy-ion collision experiments. The transition is first order at small or large quark mass, and becomes a crossover at intermediate quark mass.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first lattice QCD simulations with dynamical Wilson quarks at finite temperature and chemical potential, using the imaginary chemical potential method to avoid the complex action problem. The authors claim that for four flavors of Wilson quarks, the phase transition is first order at small or large quark mass and becomes a crossover at intermediate quark mass, but they hedge that this conclusion is based on analytic continuation of data with imaginary chemical potential \(\mu_I < \pi T/3\) and that the exact locations of the boundaries between these regimes (\(\kappa_1\), \(\kappa_2\), and \(\kappa_{chiral}\)) are not precisely determined.

## Method

The authors performed Monte Carlo simulations of lattice QCD with four flavors of dynamical Wilson quarks on an \(8^3 \times 4\) lattice, using the imaginary chemical potential method where \(\mu = i\mu_I\). They measured the Polyakov loop, chiral condensate, and their susceptibilities as functions of the gauge coupling \(\beta\) at fixed hopping parameter \(\kappa\) (values: 0, 0.001, 0.15, 0.165, 0.17, 0.25) and imaginary chemical potential \(a\mu_I\). The transition points were identified from peaks in the susceptibilities, and the nature of the transition (first order vs. crossover) was determined by finite-size scaling analysis and by examining histograms and Monte Carlo histories for double-peak structure and flip-flop behavior.

## Key numbers

- Transition line at \(\kappa=0.15\) fitted as \(\beta_C = 5.169(9) + 0.954(33) (a\mu_I)^2\) for imaginary chemical potential, with error bars from the fit.
- After analytic continuation to real chemical potential: \(\beta_C = 5.169(9) - 0.954(33) (a\mu)^2\).
- Critical temperature at \(\mu=0\) set to \(T_C = 164\) MeV, taken from prior work with Kogut-Susskind fermions (Karsch, 2000).
- Transition points at \(\kappa=0.15\) for various \(a\mu_I\): \(\beta_C = 5.168(2)\) at \(a\mu_I=0.00\), 5.170(2) at 0.05, 5.180(2) at 0.10, 5.187(2) at 0.14, 5.200(2) at 0.18, 5.206(2) at 0.20, 5.217(2) at 0.22.
- First-order transition observed at \(\kappa=0.165\) with \(\beta=4.870\) and \(a\mu_I=0.1\), and at \(\kappa=0.17\) with \(\beta=4.736\) and \(a\mu_I=0.1\).
- No phase transition observed at \(\kappa=0.25\).
- Bounds on critical hopping parameters: \(\kappa_1 \in (0.001, 0.15)\), \(\kappa_2 \in (0.15, 0.165)\), \(\kappa_{chiral} \in (0.17, 0.25)\).

## Context

This work extends the study of the QCD phase diagram from Kogut-Susskind (staggered) fermions, as done by de Forcrand and Philipsen (2002) and D'Elia and Lombardo (2002), to Wilson fermions, which avoid the species doubling problem and preserve flavor symmetry at the cost of explicit chiral symmetry breaking. The results are consistent with those from Kogut-Susskind fermions within error bars, and the phase diagram in the \((\mu, T)\) plane for \(\kappa=0.15\) is compared directly to the D'Elia and Lombardo (2002) result. The work enables future studies of the continuum limit and the dependence of the phase structure on the number of quark flavors, and it suggests that the imaginary chemical potential method is viable for Wilson fermions.
