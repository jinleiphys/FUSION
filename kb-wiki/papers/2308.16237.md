# Secular Outflows from 3D-MHD Hypermassive Neutron Star Accretion Disk Systems

> Magnetized hypermassive neutron stars (HMNSs) have been proposed as a way for neutron star (NS) mergers to produce high electron fraction, high velocity ejecta, as required by kilonova models to explain the observed light curve of GW170817. The HMNS drives outflows through neutrino energy deposition and mechanical oscillations, and raises the electron fraction of outflows through neutrino interactions before collapsing to a black hole (BH). Here we perform 3D numerical simulations of HMNS-torus systems in ideal magnetohydrodynamics, using a leakage/absorption scheme for neutrino transport, the nuclear APR equation of state, and Newtonian self-gravity, with a pseudo-Newtonian potential added after BH formation. Due to the uncertainty in the HMNS collapse time, we choose two different parameterized times to induce collapse. We also explore two initial magnetic field geometries in the torus, and evolve the systems until the outflows diminish significantly ($\sim 1 - 2$ $\mathrm{s}$). We find bluer, faster outflows as compared to equivalent BH-torus systems, producing $M\sim 10^{-3} M_\odot$ of ejecta with $Y_e \geq 0.25$ and $v \geq 0.25c$ by the simulation end. Approximately half the outflows are launched in disk winds at times $t\lesssim 500$ $\mathrm{ms}$, with a broad distribution of electron fractions and velocities, depending on the initial condition. The remaining outflows are thermally-driven, characterized by lower velocities and electron fractions. Nucleosynthesis with tracer particles shows patterns resembling solar abundances in all models. Although outflows from our simulations do not match those inferred from two-component modelling of the GW170817 kilonova, self-consistent multidimensional detailed kilonova models are required to determine if our outflows can power the blue kilonova.

# Secular Outflows from 3D-MHD Hypermassive Neutron Star Accretion Disk Systems

## Key claim

The authors find that hypermassive neutron star (HMNS) systems produce bluer, faster outflows than equivalent black hole-torus systems, with approximately half the ejecta launched in disk winds and the remainder thermally driven; however, they explicitly state that their outflows do not match those inferred from two-component modeling of the GW170817 kilonova, and that self-consistent multidimensional kilonova models are required to determine if these outflows can power the blue kilonova.

## Method

The authors perform 3D numerical simulations of HMNS-torus systems using ideal magnetohydrodynamics in the FLASH4.5 code, with a leakage/absorption scheme for neutrino transport, the nuclear APR equation of state, and Newtonian self-gravity with a pseudo-Newtonian potential added after black hole formation. They initialize a torus with mass 0.1 solar masses orbiting a 2.65 solar mass HMNS, vary the HMNS collapse time between 30 ms and 100 ms, and explore two initial magnetic field geometries (poloidal and toroidal) in the torus, evolving the systems for approximately 1-2 seconds until outflows diminish significantly.

## Key numbers

- Ejecta mass of approximately 10^{-3} solar masses with electron fraction Y_e >= 0.25 and velocity v >= 0.25c by simulation end
- Approximately half of outflows launched in disk winds at times t <= 500 ms
- Torus initial density maximum at 50 km, with maximum density rho_max = 8 x 10^10 g cm^{-3}
- HMNS equatorial radius of 23.5 km and central rotation rate of 3780 rad s^{-1}, corresponding to a period of approximately 1.6 ms
- Initial torus magnetic field strengths: poloidal models with maximum |B_r| ~ |B_theta| ~ 4 x 10^14 G; toroidal models with maximum 8 x 10^14 G and average approximately 2 x 10^14 G
- HMNS initial magnetic energy of 2 x 10^47 erg
- Grid resolution of (N_r x N_theta x N_phi) = (580 x 120 x 64), with Delta r/r ~ 0.018, min(Delta theta) ~ 0.017, and Delta phi ~ 0.1
- Post-collapse inner boundary at r_min = 15.4 km and outer boundary at r_max = 5.23 x 10^5 km

## Context

This work extends previous 3D MHD simulations of black hole accretion disk systems (Fernandez 2019, Fahlman and Fernandez 2022) that found difficulty producing massive, high-velocity blue ejecta components needed to explain the GW170817 kilonova. The study addresses the proposal by Metzger (2018) and others that a short-lived HMNS phase could resolve this discrepancy, and joins a small set of 3D MHD HMNS simulations (Kiuchi 2022, Curtis 2023) that last approximately 1 second. The results enable future comparisons with self-consistent multidimensional kilonova radiative transfer models (Ristic 2023, Bulla 2023) to determine whether HMNS-driven outflows can power the observed blue kilonova component.
