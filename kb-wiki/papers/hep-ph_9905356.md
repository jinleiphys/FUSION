# Is SAX J1808.4-3658 a Strange Star ?

> One of the most important questions in the study of compact objects is the nature of pulsars, including whether they are composed of $β$-stable nuclear matter or strange quark matter. Observations of the newly discovered millisecond X-ray pulsar \sax with the Rossi X-Ray Timing Explorer place firm constraint on the radius of the compact star. Comparing the mass - radius relation of \sax with the theoretical mass - radius relation for neutron stars and for strange stars, we find that a strange star model is more consistent with SAX J1808.4-3658, and suggest that it is a likely strange star candidate.

# Is SAX J1808.4-3658 a Strange Star?

## Key claim
The paper suggests that SAX J1808.4-3658 is a likely strange star candidate, but acknowledges that available observational data are not sufficient or accurate enough to exclude the possibility that it could be a neutron star with anomalous magnetic fields.

## Method
The authors derived an upper limit on the stellar radius of SAX J1808.4-3658 from the condition that the inner accretion disk radius must lie between the stellar surface and the corotation radius for X-ray pulsations to be detected, using the observed range of X-ray flux during the 1998 April/May outburst. They then compared the allowed mass-radius region with theoretical mass-radius relations for six neutron star models (using equations of state UU, BBB1, BBB2, Hyp, BPAL12, and a kaon-condensation model) and for two strange star models (ss1 and ss2) based on an equation of state with asymptotic freedom and density-dependent scalar potential.

## Key numbers
- The maximum observed 2-30 keV flux at outburst peak was Fmax ≃ 3 × 10⁻⁹ erg cm⁻² s⁻¹, and the pulse signal became barely detectable when flux dropped below Fmin ≃ 2 × 10⁻¹¹ erg cm⁻² s⁻¹, giving a flux ratio Fmax/Fmin ≃ 100.
- The upper limit on the stellar radius is given by R < 27.6 (Fmax/Fmin)⁻²/⁷ (P/2.49 ms)²/³ (M/M⊙)¹/³ km, where P = 2.49 ms is the pulse period.
- The strange star models ss1 and ss2 have maximum masses of 1.44 M⊙ and 1.32 M⊙, and radii of 7.07 km and 6.53 km, respectively.
- The companion star mass is most likely less than 0.1 M⊙, and the pulsar mass more than 1 M⊙.
- The radius of a ~1 M⊙ strange star is generally 1.5-2 times smaller than that of a neutron star of similar mass, implying a surface field strength 3-8 times higher for the same magnetic moment.

## Context
This work extends the search for strange star candidates previously proposed for Her X-1 (Li et al. 1995; Dey et al. 1998) and 4U 1820-30 (Bombaci 1997), and directly tests the compactness of pulsars using the newly discovered millisecond X-ray pulsar SAX J1808.4-3658. The paper enables future observational tests of strange star models, including searches for the m=2 bar mode instability predicted for strange stars (Colpi and Miller 1992), and suggests that a strange star model may help explain the unusually hard X-ray spectrum and the fact that SAX J1808.4-3658 is the only known low-mass X-ray binary with a millisecond pulsar.
