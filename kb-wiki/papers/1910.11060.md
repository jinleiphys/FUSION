# Dirac sea and chiral anomaly in the quantum kinetic theory

> We revisit the chiral anomaly in the quantum kinetic theory in the Wigner function formalism under the background field approximation. Our results show that the chiral anomaly is actually from the Dirac sea or the vacuum contribution in the un-normal-ordered Wigner function. We also demonstrate that this contribution modifies the chiral kinetic equation for antiparticles.

## Key claim

The chiral anomaly in quantum kinetic theory arises specifically from the Dirac sea (vacuum) contribution in the un-normal-ordered Wigner function, not from Berry curvature or distribution function boundary terms; the Berry curvature contribution to the anomaly is always cancelled by another term and only appears to generate the anomaly for massless fermions with specific distribution functions satisfying \(f_{\bf p}+\bar f_{\bf p}=1\) at zero momentum.

## Method

The authors start from the Wigner function formalism for spin-1/2 fermions under the background field approximation, using the Wigner equation derived from the Dirac equation without normal ordering. They perform a semiclassical expansion to first order in \(\hbar\), express the Wigner components in terms of the scalar function \(\mathscr{F}\) and axial-vector \(\mathscr{A}_\mu\), and compute the divergence of the axial current \(\partial^\mu_x j_\mu^5\) by inserting the mass-shell solutions into the expression for the pseudo-scalar component \(\mathscr{P}\). The coefficient of the anomaly term \(F^{\mu\nu}\tilde F_{\mu\nu}\) is evaluated by separating the Dirac sea contribution \(\bar f_{\rm v}=-1\) from the normal distribution functions \(f^\pm\) and \(\bar f^\pm\) in the scalar function \(\mathcal{F}\).

## Key numbers

* The Dirac sea contribution to the scalar function is \(\bar f_{\rm v} = -1\), universal and independent of the system state.
* The vacuum contribution to the anomaly coefficient is \(C_{\rm v} = 1\), obtained by integrating \(\int d^3{\bf p}/(2\pi)\, {\mathbf \partial}_{\bf p}\cdot ({\bf p}/(2E_p^3))\).
* For massless fermions, the Berry curvature is \({\pmb \Omega} = {\bf p}/(2|{\bf p}|^3)\) with \({\pmb \nabla}\cdot{\pmb \Omega}=2\pi\).
* The chiral anomaly equation reads \(\partial^\mu_x j_\mu^{5} = -\frac{1}{8\pi^2} F^{\mu\nu}\tilde F_{\mu\nu}\) after including all contributions.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study Gao (2018) where singular boundary terms were found to contribute to the chiral anomaly, and clarifies the debate between approaches that attribute the anomaly to Berry curvature (Stephanov 2012, Manuel 2014, Chen 2012) and those that distinguish Berry phase from the anomaly (Fujikawa 2005, Mueller 2017). The result resolves the controversy by showing that the Dirac sea term is the universal origin of the anomaly for both massive and massless fermions, and it provides an updated chiral kinetic equation for antiparticles that includes the vacuum contribution \(-s\hbar E\cdot B (\bar\partial^\mu_p \Omega_\mu)\) in the last term. This enables a consistent first-principles derivation of the chiral anomaly from quantum field theory without relying on specific distribution functions or infrared boundary conditions.
