# Zubarev response approach to polarization phenomena in local equilibrium

> Using the expansion of Zubarev's density operator, we develop a linear response approach to study various spin physics in a locally equilibrated medium, particularly focusing on various polarization phenomena in heavy-ion collisions. Specifically, we connect familiar correlation functions and diagrammatic methods to the Zubarev formalism, enabling the use of established techniques like the Matsubara/imaginary time formalism to facilitate calculations. For a spin-1/2 particle, we re-derive its vector polarization using this Zubarev response approach, which exactly reproduces with our previous results based on Luttinger's method. For a spin-1 particle, we calculate the vector polarization and find the expected contributions from vorticity, temperature gradients, and shear, which are identical to those for spin-1/2 particles except for a factor of 4/3 as expected. For the tensor polarization and spin alignment of a spin-1 boson, we explicitly prove that the non-dissipative contribution is zero at leading order in gradients, and briefly reiterate our previous findings for the dissipative contribution with further discussions on several concerns. Additionally, we discuss several relevant subtleties and questions, including an alternative derivation for Zubarev response approach, the covariance issues of different spin density matrix definitions, a further explanation of slow and fast modes, the mode selection scheme, etc. We also discuss skeleton expansions, higher-order contributions, and non-perturbative methods, particularly their potential connection to lattice field theory. In summary, this work discusses the foundations and subtleties of Zubarev response approach, with specific examples from spin physics in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper develops a linear response approach based on Zubarev's density operator to study spin polarization in locally equilibrated media, but the key hedge is that the non-dissipative contribution to tensor polarization and spin alignment for spin-1 bosons is proven to be zero at leading order in gradients, while dissipative contributions (previously identified by the authors) are required to explain the observed large spin alignment magnitudes.

## Method

The authors expand Zubarev's non-equilibrium density operator to linear order in hydrodynamic gradients, separating the response into a non-dissipative (local equilibrium) part and a dissipative (dynamical) part. They connect the resulting expressions to retarded Green functions and spectral functions, then use analytical continuation to relate these to Euclidean-time correlation functions accessible via Matsubara/imaginary time formalism, diagrammatic perturbation theory, and non-perturbative methods such as lattice QCD. For spin-1/2 and spin-1 particles, they compute vector and tensor polarization by evaluating the linear response of spin operators to gradients of the thermal vector \(\beta^\mu(x) = \beta(x) u^\mu(x)\).

## Key numbers

* For spin-1 particles, the vector polarization from vorticity, temperature gradients, and shear is identical to that for spin-1/2 particles except for a factor of **4/3**.
* The non-dissipative contribution to tensor polarization and spin alignment of a spin-1 boson is **zero** at leading order in gradients.
* The dissipative contribution to spin alignment, first identified in the authors' prior work Li (2022), can potentially generate large spin alignment with dependencies on transverse momentum (\(p_T\)), centrality, and beam energy that resemble experimental trends.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous linear response treatments of spin polarization, which used Luttinger's method for spin-1/2 particles (Liu, 2021) and Zubarev's approach for spin-1 particles (Li, 2022), by unifying them into a single coherent framework based on Zubarev's formalism. It directly addresses the "spin sign puzzle" in heavy-ion collisions, where local Lambda polarization measurements (STAR, 2018; ALICE, 2021) showed a sign opposite to predictions from the thermal vorticity formula (Becattini, 2013), and the spin alignment puzzle for vector mesons (STAR, 2008; ALICE, 2019; STAR, 2022), where observed magnitudes are orders of magnitude larger than predictions from higher-order gradient contributions (Becattini, 2007; Xia, 2020). The approach enables future non-perturbative calculations using lattice QCD, Dyson-Schwinger equations, or functional renormalization group methods.
