# Neutron transfer reactions in accreting neutron stars

> I suggest a novel type of nuclear reactions in accreting neutron stars - neutron transfer, which is quantum tunneling of weakly bounded neutron from one nucleus to another. I estimate the rate of this process for fixed nuclei separation and then average the result over realistic distribution of nuclei to get the rate value for astrophysical conditions. The neutron transfer can modify reaction chains in accreting neutron stars, thus affecting their heating and cooling. In particular, it can suppress cooling by URCA pairs of nuclei, which is supposed to be crucial for the hottest neutron stars.

## Key claim

Neutron transfer reactions, quantum tunneling of a weakly bound neutron from one nucleus to another, can occur in accreting neutron star envelopes and may modify reaction chains, heating, and cooling, but the paper provides only order-of-magnitude estimates and notes that the nuclear part of the model requires improvement before quantitative astrophysical conclusions can be drawn.

## Method

The author calculates the neutron transfer probability per unit time between two static nuclei at a given separation using Fermi's golden rule, approximating the wave functions of the least bound neutron as decaying exponentials and the matrix element via an integral over a separating plane. The reaction rate per donor nucleus is then obtained by averaging this probability over the realistic internuclear separation distribution in the plasma, using a pair correlation function parametrized by a mean-force potential and applying a saddle-point (Gamow-like) integration. The resulting analytic expression is applied to specific URCA pairs and other candidate reactions using experimental nuclear masses from AME2016 or, where unavailable, extrapolated masses or the FRDM2012 and HFB21 mass models.

## Key numbers

- For fiducial nuclear radii \(r_\mathrm{d}=r_\mathrm{a}=4\) fm and separation \(l_{50}=l/50\) fm, the transfer probability per unit time is approximately \(W(l) \approx 3\times 10^{21}\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}\, l_{50}^{-2} \exp(-22.6\, l_{50} \sqrt{E/\mathrm{MeV}})\), where \(E\) is the neutron separation energy of the donor.
- The peak internuclear separation contributing to the rate is \(l_\mathrm{pk} = a_\mathrm{ad} / \sqrt{25/64 + 2\kappa a_\mathrm{ad}/\Gamma_\mathrm{ad}}\), which can be as large as 100 fm.
- For the URCA pair \(^{29}\mathrm{Mg} - ^{29}\mathrm{Na}\) at \(\mu_\mathrm{e}=13.7\) MeV, the neutron transfer reaction \(2\,^{29}\mathrm{Mg} \rightarrow ^{28}\mathrm{Mg} + ^{30}\mathrm{Mg}\) has \(Q_\mathrm{tr}=2.7\) MeV and donor separation energy \(S_\mathrm{n}^\mathrm{d}=3.7\) MeV.
- For the URCA pair \(^{31}\mathrm{Al} - ^{31}\mathrm{Mg}\) at \(\mu_\mathrm{e}=12.2\) MeV, the reaction \(2\,^{31}\mathrm{Mg} \rightarrow ^{30}\mathrm{Mg} + ^{32}\mathrm{Mg}\) has \(Q_\mathrm{tr}=3.5\) MeV and \(S_\mathrm{n}^\mathrm{d}=2.3\) MeV.
- For the URCA pair \(^{33}\mathrm{Al} - ^{33}\mathrm{Mg}\) at \(\mu_\mathrm{e}=13.9\) MeV, the reaction \(2\,^{33}\mathrm{Mg} \rightarrow ^{32}\mathrm{Mg} + ^{34}\mathrm{Mg}\) has \(Q_\mathrm{tr}=2.4\) MeV and \(S_\mathrm{n}^\mathrm{d}=2.3\) MeV.
- For the URCA pair \(^{55}\mathrm{Sc} - ^{55}\mathrm{Ca}\) at \(\mu_\mathrm{e}=12.3\) MeV, the reaction \(2\,^{55}\mathrm{Ca} \rightarrow ^{54}\mathrm{Ca} + ^{56}\mathrm{Ca}\) has \(Q_\mathrm{tr}=1.5\) MeV and \(S_\mathrm{n}^\mathrm{d}=1.3\) MeV (AME2016 extrapolated mass).
- For the HFB21 mass model, \(^{56}\mathrm{K}\) has \(S_\mathrm{n}\approx 0.3\) MeV, and the reaction \(^{56}\mathrm{K} + ^{56}\mathrm{Ca} \rightarrow ^{55}\mathrm{K} + ^{57}\mathrm{Ca}\) has a timescale \(\sim 10^{-14}\) s for high abundance of \(^{56}\mathrm{Ca}\).
- For the FRDM2012 mass model, \(^{56}\mathrm{K}\) has \(S_\mathrm{n}\approx 1\) MeV and the reaction \(^{56}\mathrm{K} + ^{56}\mathrm{K} \rightarrow ^{55}\mathrm{K} + ^{57}\mathrm{K}\) has a timescale \(\sim 2\times 10^{-8}\) s at \(T=5\times 10^8\) K for 100% abundance of \(^{56}\mathrm{K}\).

## Context

This work introduces a novel nuclear reaction channel, neutron transfer, into the modeling of accreting neutron star envelopes, which previously considered only electron captures and beta-decays as drivers of composition evolution before neutron drip (e.g., Sato 1979, Haensel and Zdunik 1990, 2003, 2008, Gupta et al. 2007, Chamel et al. 2015). The neutron transfer can suppress cooling by URCA pairs, which were shown by Schatz (2014) to be crucial for the hottest neutron stars and by Deibel et al. (2016) and Meisel and Deibel (2017) to affect superburst ignition. The paper also notes that neutron transfer interlaces reaction chains of different mass number \(A\), which were previously treated independently, and that it should be considered alongside the free-neutron emission mechanism suggested by Gupta et al. (2008). The author calls for future detailed reaction-network studies incorporating both neutron transfer and neutron emission from unbound states.
