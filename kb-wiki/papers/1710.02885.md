# Level density of the $sd$-nuclei $-$ statistical shell-model predictions

> Accurate knowledge of the nuclear level density is important both from a theoretical viewpoint as a powerful instrument for studying nuclear structure and for numerous applications. For example, astrophysical reactions responsible for the nucleosynthesis in the universe can be understood only if we know the nuclear level density. We use the configuration-interaction nuclear shell model to predict nuclear level density for all nuclei in the $sd$-shell, both total and for individual spins (only with positive parity). To avoid the diagonalization in large model spaces we use the moments method based on statistical properties of nuclear many-body systems. In the cases where the diagonalization is possible, the results of the moments method practically coincide with those from the shell-model calculations. Using the computed level densities, we fit the parameters of the Constant Temperature phenomenological model, which can be used by practitioners in their studies of nuclear reactions at excitation energies appropriate for the $sd$-shell nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors use the moments method, a statistical approach based on the spectral distribution of eigenvalues, to predict nuclear level densities for all sd-shell nuclei (excluding those with N,Z=8,20) without requiring full Hamiltonian diagonalization, and they show that the results "practically coincide" with exact shell-model calculations where diagonalization is feasible, but only for positive parity states.

## Method

The configuration-interaction nuclear shell model with the USDB effective Hamiltonian (63 two-body matrix elements) was applied to the sd-shell space (d5/2, s1/2, d3/2 orbitals above an 16O core). Instead of diagonalizing the Hamiltonian matrix, the moments method computes the first and second moments (centroid and width) of the Hamiltonian for each partition of valence particles, then sums Gaussian distributions weighted by the partition dimensions to obtain the level density. The level density for a specific spin J is obtained from the M-scheme density as the difference between ρ(M=J) and ρ(M=J+1).

## Key numbers

* Temperature parameter T for 24Mg: 2.64 MeV, with energy shift E0 = 1.49 MeV, from fitting the constant temperature model to the moments method level density over 0-10 MeV excitation energy.
* Temperature parameter T for 20Ne: 2.56 MeV, the lowest value among the listed sd-shell nuclei.
* Temperature parameter T for 28Si: 2.94 MeV, with E0 = -0.61 MeV.
* Temperature parameter T for 36Ar: 3.12 MeV, with E0 = -0.28 MeV.
* Typical temperature values for sd-shell nuclei range between approximately 2.56 MeV and 7.36 MeV across all isotopic chains.
* The moments method level density is provided up to the excitation energy where the peak of the bell-shaped curve is reached, with the fit performed in the interval 0-10 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the spectral distribution (moments) method previously developed by Sen'kov and Zelevinsky (2016) to the complete set of sd-shell nuclei, providing a systematic tabulation of level densities and constant temperature model parameters. It contrasts with the Bethe (1936) Fermi-gas model and the Ericson (1937) constant temperature approach by using a fully microscopic shell-model Hamiltonian (USDB interaction from Brown and Richter, 2006) that accounts for all two-body interactions, including pairing and quartic correlations. The results enable practitioners to use the fitted constant temperature parameters for nuclear reaction calculations in astrophysical nucleosynthesis and other applications, and the method is designed to be extendable to larger model spaces (pf-shell, pfg9/2) where exact diagonalization is impossible.
