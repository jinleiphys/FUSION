# Inclusive charm and bottom quark pair production cross sections at hadron colliders at next-to-next-to-leading-order accuracy

> The inclusive cross sections for charm ($\mathrm{c}\overline{\mathrm{c}}$) and bottom ($\mathrm{b}\overline{\mathrm{b}}$) quark-antiquark pair production in proton-proton, proton-antiproton, and proton-nucleus collisions are studied over a wide range of center-of-mass energies, $\sqrt{s}\approx 10$ GeV--400 TeV. All existing data over $\sqrt{s}\approx 10$ GeV--14 TeV are collected and compared to calculations at next-to-next-to-leading-order (NNLO) accuracy using the new fixed-order MaunaKea open-source code for varying sets of parton distribution functions (PDFs). Relative to next-to-leading-order (NLO) predictions, the NNLO cross sections are enhanced by up to a factor of two, with the associated theoretical scale uncertainties reduced by the same amount, leading to agreement with experimental data over the full range of collision energies. The NNLO results are also compared with NLO predictions obtained within the SACOT-$m_{_\mathrm{T}}$ general-mass variable-flavour-number scheme. Despite still sizable theoretical and experimental uncertainties, $\mathrm{c}\overline{\mathrm{c}}$ cross section at multi-TeV energies can provide extra constraints on the gluon density at very small-$x$ in global PDF analyses. In the bottom sector, more precise cross section measurements at low energies, $\sqrt{s}\approx 10$--100 GeV, can help constraint the bottom-quark pole mass.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that next-to-next-to-leading-order (NNLO) predictions for inclusive charm and bottom quark pair production, computed with the new MaunaKea code, agree with experimental data across a wide range of collision energies (sqrt(s) ≈ 10 GeV–13 TeV), but the abstract's claim of "agreement with experimental data over the full range of collision energies" glosses over the fact that the agreement still involves "sizable theoretical and experimental uncertainties," and the NNLO cross sections are enhanced by up to a factor of two relative to NLO predictions, with scale uncertainties reduced by the same amount.

## Method

The authors collected all existing experimental data for inclusive charm-anticharm and bottom-antibottom production cross sections in proton-proton, proton-antiproton, and proton-nucleus collisions over sqrt(s) ≈ 10 GeV–14 TeV, and compared them to fixed-order NNLO calculations using the new MaunaKea open-source code, which interfaces toppp matrix elements with pineappl fast interpolation grids. The NNLO predictions were evaluated with varying sets of parton distribution functions (PDFs) and compared to NLO predictions obtained within the SACOT-m_T general-mass variable-flavour-number scheme, with systematic assessment of theoretical uncertainties from missing higher-order corrections, PDFs, heavy-quark masses, and alpha_S.

## Key numbers

- Charm quark pole mass: m_c ≈ 1.67 GeV; bottom quark pole mass: m_b ≈ 4.8 GeV; top quark pole mass: m_t ≈ 172.5 GeV (from ParticleDataGroup:2024cfk)
- At top LHC energies, charm production represents about 20% and bottom production about 1% of the total inelastic pp cross section of sigma(pp → X) ≈ 75 mb
- Inclusive branching fraction B(b → J/psi X) = (1.16 ± 0.10)% and B(b → e± X) = (20.5 ± 0.7)% (from ParticleDataGroup:2024cfk)
- Inclusive fragmentation fraction f(c → ℓ+) = (9.71 ± 0.32)% (from ALEPH:2005ab)
- The NNLO cross sections are enhanced by up to a factor of two relative to NLO predictions, with theoretical scale uncertainties reduced by the same amount

## Context

This work extends previous NNLO calculations for top quark pair production (using hathor and toppp codes) to the lighter charm and bottom quarks, where the theoretical state-of-the-art had been NLO accuracy for differential cross sections (e.g., with the MNR code) complemented with NLL resummation in general-mass variable-flavour-number schemes (e.g., FONLL code). The study enables future constraints on the gluon density at very small-x from charm cross sections at multi-TeV energies (relevant for the Future Circular Collider at sqrt(s) ≈ 100 TeV and ultrahigh-energy cosmic ray collisions up to sqrt(s) ≈ 400 TeV), and on the bottom-quark pole mass from more precise bottom cross section measurements at low energies (sqrt(s) ≈ 10–100 GeV).
