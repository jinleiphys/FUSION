# Neural Network predictions of inclusive electron-nucleus cross sections

> We investigate whether a neural network approach can reproduce and predict the electron-nucleus cross sections in the kinematical domain of present and future accelerator-based neutrino oscillation experiments. For this purpose, we consider the large amount of data available to the community via the web-page ``Quasielastic Electron Nucleus scattering archive'', and use a residual, fully connected feedforward neural network. We illustrate the training performances of the neural network by comparing its results with experimental data for the electron double-differential cross section on carbon. The agreement between predictions and data is remarkable from quasielastic to deep-inelastic scattering. To test the predicting power of the neural network we consider the numerous kinematical conditions for which experimental cross sections on calcium are available. Furthermore, we show the predictions of the electron scattering cross sections on oxygen, argon, and titanium: nuclei of particular interest in the context of present and future accelerator-based neutrino oscillation program. The agreement between these predictions and the data is comparable to the one of other theoretical models commonly used to calculate electron and neutrino cross sections, such as SuSAv2 and GiBUU. Results obtained with GENIE, a Monte Carlo event generator, are also discussed for comparison. The good performances obtained with our neural network suggest that neural networks could be exploited for theoretical and experimental investigations of electron- and neutrino-nucleus scattering.

## Key claim

A residual fully connected feedforward neural network can reproduce and predict inclusive electron-nucleus double-differential cross sections with an accuracy comparable to established theoretical models (SuSAv2, GiBUU) and the GENIE Monte Carlo event generator, but the authors make no claim of optimality and note that the inclusion of four handcrafted input features (cos θ, ω_QE, ω_Δ, Q²) makes the optimization easier and allows a smaller network, though the exact improvement from these variables cannot be quantified.

## Method

The authors used a residual, fully connected feedforward neural network with 10 residual blocks of 5 layers each (50 connected layers total), implemented in TensorFlow, trained on a subset of the "Quasielastic Electron Nucleus scattering archive" containing about 20000 data points for nuclei from ⁴He to ⁵⁹Ni. The input features were nine variables: Z, A, E, θ, ω, cos θ, ω_QE, ω_Δ, and Q²; the target was the double-differential cross section divided by A. Training used the relative absolute error as the cost function with Adam optimizer (learning rate 0.001) and L₂ regularization, running for 4000 epochs on a 12GB NVIDIA Tesla K80 GPU (about 30 minutes). For testing, the neural network was trained on all data except the target nucleus or specific kinematical conditions, and predictions were averaged over 40–100 independent training runs with 95% confidence intervals.

## Key numbers

- The dataset contains about 20000 data points from approximately 600 different combinations of targets, energies, and angles.
- For ¹²C, the double-differential cross section values vary between a minimum of 7.7 × 10⁻⁵ nb/(GeV sr) and a maximum of 9.6 × 10⁵ nb/(GeV sr), a range of 10 orders of magnitude.
- The neural network has 50 connected layers (10 residual blocks of 5 fully connected layers each), with layer sizes ranging from 9 inputs up to 50 then down to 5 before the single output.
- Training takes about 30 minutes for 5000 epochs on a 12GB NVIDIA Tesla K80 GPU.
- For ⁴⁰Ca predictions, N=100 independent neural networks were averaged; for ¹⁶O, ⁴⁰Ar, and ⁴⁸Ti predictions, N=40 independent networks were averaged.
- The relative contribution of ¹²C to the dataset is 24.0%, ⁴⁰Ca is 10.5%, ⁴⁸Ca is 9.6%, ⁴⁰Ar is 1.4%, ⁴⁸Ti is 1.4%, and ¹⁶O is 1.0%.

## Context

This work extends the use of neural networks in lepton-nucleus scattering, following studies that applied generative adversarial networks to electron-proton scattering (Alanazi 2020) and physics-informed neural networks to reconstruct electromagnetic response functions (Raghavan 2020). The results are compared with the SuSAv2 approach (Amaro 2019), the GiBUU transport model (Leitner 2008, Mosel 2018), and the GENIE Monte Carlo event generator (Andreopoulos 2009, Ankowski 2020, electronsforneutrinos 2020). The work enables the use of neural networks as a fast surrogate for theoretical models in predicting electron-nucleus cross sections for nuclei of interest to neutrino oscillation experiments (T2K, NOvA, DUNE, Hyper-Kamiokande), and suggests potential applications to neutrino-nucleus scattering predictions.
