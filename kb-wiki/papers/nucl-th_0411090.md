# The shape of the nucleon

> We show that all four of the nucleon form factors can be very well explained using the manifestly covariant spectator theory. The nucleon is modeled as a spherical state of three constituent quarks with electromagnetic form factors, all determined by the fit to the data.

## Key claim

The authors show that all four nucleon electromagnetic form factors can be explained by a model in which the nucleon is a spherically symmetric state of three constituent quarks, contradicting suggestions that the proton must be deformed to fit the Jefferson Lab polarization transfer data.

## Method

The authors use the manifestly covariant spectator theory to model the nucleon as a bound state of three constituent quarks, with the diquark system treated as an on-shell spectator. The relativistic wave function is constructed from scalar functions with a double-pole (Hulthen-like) momentum dependence, and the constituent quarks are given their own electromagnetic form factors parameterized by vector-meson-dominance monopole forms plus a pion-cloud isovector dipole term. The nucleon form factors are then computed via the relativistic impulse approximation in the Breit frame, with 11 adjustable parameters fitted to the experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Model I gives a chi-squared per datum of 1.18; Model II gives 1.94
- Proton charge radius from Model I: 0.800 fm^2; from Model II: 0.771 fm^2 (experimental: 0.780(25) fm^2)
- Neutron charge radius from Model I: -0.065 fm^2; from Model II: -0.106 fm^2 (experimental: -0.113(7) fm^2)
- The models predict that G_Ep will go through zero at Q^2 approximately 8 GeV^2
- The asymptotic limit for Q^2 F_2p/(mu_p F_1p) is predicted to be 7.06 for Model I, with the flat behavior of Q F_2p/F_1p persisting only until Q^2 exceeds about 100 GeV^2

## Context

This work extends the covariant spectator formalism previously applied to three-body nuclear systems (Gross 1969, Stadler and Gross 1997) to the nucleon, and directly addresses the debate over nucleon deformation raised by Miller (2003) and Buchmann and Henley (2001). The authors show that a spherical three-quark model can fit the Jefferson Lab polarization transfer data as well as vector dominance models (Lomon 2002, Bijker and Iachello 2004) while explicitly modeling the quark structure, and they predict that the apparent flattening of Q F_2p/F_1p observed at Jefferson Lab is a temporary effect that will give way to the expected 1/Q behavior at higher Q^2.
