# Impurity Lattice Monte Carlo for Hypernuclei

> We consider the problem of including $Λ$ hyperons into the ab initio framework of nuclear lattice effective field theory. In order to avoid large sign oscillations in Monte Carlo simulations, we make use of the fact that the number of hyperons is typically small compared to the number of nucleons in the hypernuclei of interest. This allows us to use the impurity lattice Monte Carlo method, where the minority species of fermions in the full nuclear Hamiltonian is integrated out and treated as a worldline in Euclidean projection time. The majority fermions (nucleons) are treated as explicit degrees of freedom, with their mutual interactions described by auxiliary fields. This is the first application of the impurity lattice Monte Carlo method to systems where the majority particles are interacting. Here, we show how the impurity Monte Carlo method can be applied to compute the binding energy of the light hypernuclei. In this exploratory work we use spin-independent nucleon-nucleon and hyperon-nucleon interactions to test the computational power of the method. We find that the computational effort scales approximately linearly in the number of nucleons. The results are very promising for future studies of larger hypernuclear systems using chiral effective field theory and realistic hyperon-nucleon interactions, as well as applications to other quantum many-body systems.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the impurity lattice Monte Carlo (ILMC) method can be applied to compute binding energies of light hypernuclei using spin-independent nucleon-nucleon and hyperon-nucleon interactions, with the computational effort scaling approximately linearly in the number of nucleons; however, this is an exploratory study using simplified interactions, and the method introduces small higher-body interactions that are lattice artifacts vanishing in the limit of zero temporal lattice spacing.

## Method

The authors treat the Λ hyperon as a minority species that is integrated out and represented as a worldline in Euclidean projection time, while the nucleons (majority species) are treated as explicit degrees of freedom with their mutual interactions described by auxiliary Hubbard-Stratonovich fields. The hyperon worldline is updated stochastically using a Metropolis algorithm, and the nucleon-nucleon interaction uses a Wigner SU(4)-symmetric leading-order interaction with smeared densities. The ground-state energies are extracted from Euclidean projection amplitudes using the ratio method and adiabatic transfer matrices, with results benchmarked against exact Lanczos transfer matrix calculations.

## Key numbers

- Spatial lattice spacing: a = 1/(100 MeV) and temporal lattice spacing: a_t = 1/(300 MeV)
- Nucleon-nucleon coupling constant: C_NN = -7.5 × 10⁻⁶ MeV⁻², yielding scattering length a_NN = 6.86 fm and effective range r_NN = 1.77 fm
- Hyperon-nucleon coupling constant: C_YN = -1.6 × 10⁻⁵ MeV⁻², yielding scattering length a_YN = -0.45 fm and effective range r_YN = -0.45 fm
- For ³_ΛH at Euclidean time t = 1.0000 MeV⁻¹ (N_t = 300): ILMC energy = -1.8971(18) MeV, exact energy = -1.8954 MeV (periodic box length 15.8 fm)
- For ³_ΛH at Euclidean time t = 0.1667 MeV⁻¹ (N_t = 50): ILMC energy = -1.0878(6) MeV, exact energy = -1.0878 MeV

## Context

This work extends the nuclear lattice effective field theory (NLEFT) framework, previously applied to nuclear spectra and reactions, to include Λ hyperons in hypernuclei. The ILMC method was introduced by Elhatisari (2014) for spin-up/spin-down fermion systems and applied to Fermi polarons by Bour (2014); this is the first application where the majority particles are interacting. The approach avoids the sign oscillations that would arise from extending the four spin-isospin nucleon degrees of freedom to include hyperons, as there is no approximate SU(4) symmetry (Wigner, 1936) to protect against sign oscillations. The results enable future studies of larger hypernuclear systems using chiral effective field theory and realistic hyperon-nucleon interactions, as well as applications to other quantum many-body systems.
