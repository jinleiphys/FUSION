# On a possibility to determine the S-factor of the hep process in experiments with thermal (cold) neutrons

> The problem of the magnitude of the S--factor of the hep process $p + {^3\mathrm{He}} \to {^4\mathrm{He}} + e^+ + ν_e$ is one of the most important unsolved problems in nuclear astrophysics. The magnitude of $S(hep)$ has also an important impact on the interpretation of the Super-Kamiokande solar neutrino results in terms of neutrino oscillations. We point out the possiblity to determine the major hadronic contribution to $S(hep)$ from the measurement of the total cross section of the process $n + {^3\mathrm{H}} \to {^4\mathrm{He}} + e^- + \barν_e$ in experiments with high intensity beams of thermal or cold neutrons.

## Key claim

The hadronic matrix element of the process \(n + {}^{3}\mathrm{H} \to {}^{4}\mathrm{He} + e^{-} + \bar{\nu}_e\) is equal to that of the hep process \(p + {}^{3}\mathrm{He} \to {}^{4}\mathrm{He} + e^{+} + \nu_e\) due to isotopic SU(2) invariance, allowing the astrophysical S-factor \(S(hep)\) to be determined from a measurement of the total cross section of the neutron-tritium reaction using high-intensity thermal or cold neutron beams. The authors note that this relation holds for the dominant \(s\)-wave contribution; if \(p\)-wave contributions prove significant, the method would provide only a lower bound on \(S(hep)\).

## Method

The authors derive an analytic relation between the total cross section of the neutron-tritium reaction \(n + {}^{3}\mathrm{H} \to {}^{4}\mathrm{He} + e^{-} + \bar{\nu}_e\) and the astrophysical S-factor \(S(hep)\) of the hep process, using isotopic SU(2) symmetry to equate the hadronic matrix elements. They account for the absence of the Coulomb penetration factor in the neutron-induced reaction and correct for the different phase-space factors arising from the different Q-values (19.284 MeV for hep versus 21.107 MeV for the neutron-tritium process). The resulting expression is evaluated numerically for thermal, cold, and ultracold neutron energies to estimate the expected cross sections.

## Key numbers

- The Solar Standard Model value of the hep astrophysical S-factor is \(S_0(hep) = 2.3 \times 10^{-20}\ \mathrm{keV\,b}\).
- The Q-value for the hep process is \(\Delta = m_p + m_{{}^{3}\mathrm{He}} - m_{{}^{4}\mathrm{He}} \simeq 19.284\ \mathrm{MeV}\); for the neutron-tritium process it is \(\Delta' = m_n + m_{{}^{3}\mathrm{H}} - m_{{}^{4}\mathrm{He}} \simeq 21.107\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
- The ratio of phase-space factors gives \((\Delta'/\Delta)^5 \simeq 1.57\).
- The estimated cross section for the neutron-tritium process, assuming \(S(hep) = S_0(hep)\), is \(\sigma \simeq 1.3 \times 10^{-43}\ \mathrm{cm}^2\) for thermal neutrons (\(E \simeq 2.5 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{eV}\)), \(4.7 \times 10^{-43}\ \mathrm{cm}^2\) for cold neutrons (\(E \simeq 2 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{eV}\)), and \(15 \times 10^{-43}\ \mathrm{cm}^2\) for ultracold neutrons (\(E \simeq 2 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{eV}\)).
- The calculated hep cross section at \(E = 20\ \mathrm{keV}\) is \(\sigma(hep) \simeq 6 \times 10^{-51}\ \mathrm{cm}^2\) (from Carlson et al. and Schiavilla et al.).
- The Coulomb penetration factor for the hep process at \(E = 15\ \mathrm{keV}\) is \(P \simeq 8 \times 10^{-7}\) and at \(E = 20\ \mathrm{keV}\) is \(P \simeq 5 \times 10^{-6}\).

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing problem of the hep process S-factor, which is one of the most important unsolved problems in nuclear astrophysics and directly impacts the interpretation of Super-Kamiokande solar neutrino data in terms of neutrino oscillations. The authors propose an experimental alternative to the complex, model-dependent calculations of Carlson et al. and Schiavilla et al., which suffer from large uncertainties due to SU(4) symmetry suppression and cancellations between one-body and two-body exchange currents. The proposed method would enable a direct experimental determination of the dominant hadronic contribution to \(S(hep)\) and could test nuclear reaction theories relevant for astrophysics, though the authors acknowledge the experimental difficulty of working with a large tritium target and severe background issues, suggesting a possible underground reactor site such as Krasnoyarsk.
