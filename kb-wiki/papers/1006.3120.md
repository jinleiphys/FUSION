# Method to circumvent the neutron gas problem in the BCS treatment for nuclei far from stability

> Extending the Kruppa's prescription for the continuum level density, we have recently improved the BCS method with seniority-type pairing force in such a way that the effects of discretized unbound states are properly taken into account for finite depth single-particle potentials. In this paper, it is further shown, by employing the Woods-Saxon potential, that the calculation of spatial observables like nuclear radius converges as increasing the basis size in the harmonic oscillator expansion. Namely the disastrous problem of a "particle gas" surrounding nucleus in the BCS treatment can be circumvented. In spite of its simplicity, the new treatment gives similar results to those by more elaborate Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations; e.g., it even mimics the pairing anti-halo effect. The obtained results as well as the reason of convergence in the new method are investigated by a variant of the Thomas-Fermi approximation within the limited phase space which corresponds to the harmonic oscillator basis truncation.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that their Kruppa-BCS method, which subtracts free-Hamiltonian contributions from BCS expectation values, yields convergent calculations of spatial observables like nuclear radius for both stable and drip-line nuclei as the harmonic oscillator basis size increases, thereby circumventing the "neutron gas" problem that plagues ordinary BCS treatments, provided the chemical potential lies below the free particle threshold.

## Method

The authors extend Kruppa's prescription for continuum level density to expectation values of one-body operators, defining the Kruppa expectation value as the difference between the full-Hamiltonian and free-Hamiltonian BCS expectation values. They solve the modified gap and number constraint equations (Kruppa-BCS equations) using a seniority-type separable pairing interaction with a smooth cutoff function, diagonalizing both the Woods-Saxon and free Hamiltonians in a truncated harmonic oscillator basis. To analyze convergence, they develop an oscillator-basis Thomas-Fermi (OBTF) approximation that incorporates the limited phase space of the truncated basis and derive asymptotic expressions for the Kruppa level density and observable distributions in the infinite-basis limit.

## Key numbers

- Neutron rms radii for \(^{160}\)Sn (near drip-line) and \(^{120}\)Sn (stable) converge with Kruppa method as \(\noscmax\) increases, while ordinary BCS radii diverge; for \(^{160}\)Sn with \(\Delta_n = 1.0\) MeV, the Kruppa radius converges to a definite value by \(\noscmax \approx 20\), whereas the ordinary radius grows without bound.
- For the deformed nucleus \(^{208}\)Dy with \(\Delta_n = 1.5\) MeV and \(\noscmax = 30\), the ordinary BCS deformation parameter \(\beta\) deviates by about 10% from the converged Kruppa value.
- The Kruppa method yields radii converged within about 0.5% accuracy by \(\noscmax \approx 20\) for most drip-line nuclei with pairing gaps \(\Delta \ge 1\) MeV, as shown for \(^{88}\)Ni, \(^{126}\)Zr, \(^{208}\)Dy, and \(^{256}\)Hg.
- For Ni isotopes near the drip-line (\(A = 80\) to \(92\)), the Kruppa-BCS calculation with selfconsistent pairing gaps \(\Delta_n \approx 1.2\) to \(1.7\) MeV (using \(\Delta = 13/\sqrt{A}\) MeV to fix the strength) produces radii that increase more gradually than the no-pairing case, mimicking the pairing anti-halo effect seen in HFB calculations.

## Context

This work extends the Kruppa prescription (Kruppa 1998) from the Strutinsky shell correction method to BCS calculations of spatial observables, solving the "neutron gas" problem previously addressed only by more elaborate Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) theory (Dobaczewski et al. 1984, 1996). The method enables reliable calculation of radii and deformation parameters for nuclei far from stability using a simple BCS framework, and it reproduces the pairing anti-halo effect (Bennaceur et al. 2000) that suppresses halo formation in heavier nuclei, as demonstrated for Ni isotopes where the results closely match the coordinate-space HFB calculations of Grasso et al. (2001).
