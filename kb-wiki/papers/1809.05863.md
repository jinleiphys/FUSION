# Response functions of hot and dense matter in the Nambu-Jona-Lasino model

> We investigate the current-current correlation functions or the so-called response functions of a two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasino model at finite temperature and density. We study the linear response by using the functional path integral approach and introducing the conjugated gauge fields as external sources. The response functions can be obtained by expanding the generational functional in powers of the external sources. We derive the response functions parallel to two well-established approximations for the equilibrium thermodynamics: the mean-field theory and a beyond-mean-field theory taking into account the mesonic contributions. The response functions based on the mean-field theory recover the so-called quasiparticle random phase approximation. We calculate the dynamical structure factors for the density responses in various channels within the random phase approximation. We show that the dynamical structure factors in the baryon axial vector and isospin axial vector channels can be used reveal the quark mass gap and the Mott dissociation of mesons, respectively. Noting that the mesonic contributions are not taken into account in the random phase approximation, we also derive the response functions parallel to the beyond-mean-field theory. We show that the mesonic fluctuations naturally give rise to three kinds of famous diagrammatic contributions: the Aslamazov-Lakin contribution, the Self-Energy or Density-of-State contribution, and the Maki-Thompson contribution. Unlike the equilibrium case, in evaluating the fluctuation contributions, we need to treat carefully the linear terms in the external sources and the induced perturbations. These contributions from the mesonic fluctuations are expected to have significant effects on the transport properties of hot and dense matter around the chiral phase transition or crossover.

## Key claim

The paper derives response functions for hot and dense matter in the two-flavor NJL model both at mean-field level (recovering the quasiparticle random phase approximation) and beyond mean field including mesonic fluctuations, but the beyond-mean-field results are derived formally and their quantitative effects on transport properties are only expected, not computed numerically.

## Method

The authors use a functional path integral approach, introducing conjugate gauge fields as external sources to compute current-current correlation functions. They expand the generating functional in powers of the external sources to obtain response functions, working at two levels: mean-field theory (which yields the quasiparticle random phase approximation) and a beyond-mean-field Gaussian approximation that includes mesonic fluctuations. For the beyond-mean-field case, they derive the diagrammatic contributions (Aslamazov-Lakin, Self-Energy/Density-of-State, and Maki-Thompson) and treat linear terms in external sources and induced order parameter perturbations carefully.

## Key numbers

* Effective quark mass \(M\) as a function of temperature is shown for various chemical potentials in the chiral limit (\(m_0=0\)), with the chiral phase transition temperature around 200 MeV.
* Model parameters for the physical case: \(m_0 = 5\) MeV, \(G = 4.93\) GeV\(^{-2}\), \(\Lambda = 653\) MeV, yielding \(m_\pi = 134\) MeV, \(f_\pi = 93\) MeV, and \(\langle\bar{u}u\rangle_0 = -(250\ \text{MeV})^3\).
* In the chiral limit, model parameters are \(G = 5.01\) GeV\(^{-2}\) and \(\Lambda = 650\) MeV.
* The pion decay constant satisfies \(f_\pi^2 \approx -2i N_c N_f M^2 I(0)\), and the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner relation \(m_\pi^2 f_\pi^2 \approx -m_0 \langle\bar{\psi}\psi\rangle_0\) is recovered.
* In the chiral limit at low temperature, the pressure is given by a gas of noninteracting massless pions: \(p = \pi^2 T^4 / 30\).

## Context

This work extends the equilibrium thermodynamics of the NJL model developed by Hufner, Klevansky, Zhuang, and Voss (1994) and Zhuang (1994), which included mesonic degrees of freedom via Gaussian fluctuations, to the linear response regime. It parallels the treatment of the BCS-BEC crossover in strongly interacting Fermi gases, where pair fluctuations have been shown to be quantitatively important. The derived response functions including mesonic fluctuations are expected to enable future calculations of transport properties (such as conductivity and viscosity) of hot and dense matter near the chiral phase transition or crossover, where mesonic degrees of freedom remain important.
