# Strangeness Enhancement and System Size in the Hadronic Gas Model

## Key claim

Strange particle enhancement in relativistic ion collisions depends on the size of the system (volume and/or baryon number), with the canonical ensemble formalism required for small volumes (radius R ≤ 4 fm) and baryon numbers B < 30, while the grand canonical formalism becomes applicable at large B or volume; the hadronic gas model reproduces the observed quadratic-to-linear transition in kaon yield as a function of participant number without invoking a quark-gluon plasma.

## Method

The authors used a hadronic gas model with a thermal fireball description, calculating particle abundances in both canonical and grand canonical formalisms. They fixed the baryon density B/V and temperature T at freeze-out, then computed final-state particle yields by summing over all hadronic resonances from the Particle Data Booklet (excluding charm, bottom, and cascade particles) weighted by their decay branching ratios. Results were compared with experimental data from the E802/E859 and E866 collaborations for Si-induced and Au-Au collisions at the AGS (14.6 GeV/c).

## Key numbers

- Canonical formalism is required when the fireball radius R ≤ 4 fm and baryon number B < 30
- For K⁺/π⁺ ratio, the relevant freeze-out temperature is T ≈ 100 MeV
- Baryon density at freeze-out is in the range B/V ≈ 0.02–0.05 /fm³
- In the grand canonical ensemble, this corresponds to baryon chemical potential μB ≈ 540 MeV
- The K⁺/π⁺ and K⁻/π⁻ ratios were both 11% at mid-rapidity in p-pbar collisions at CERN ISR (vanishing baryon density)
- The K⁻/π⁻ ratio decreases for baryon densities beyond nB = 0.1/fm³

## Context

This work extends the canonical ensemble treatment of strangeness production developed by Hagedorn and others, showing that the hadronic gas model can reproduce the system-size dependence of kaon yields observed by the E802/E859 and E866 collaborations at the AGS. The paper builds on earlier work by Cleymans and collaborators showing that canonical and grand canonical formalisms converge at large baryon number or volume. The results provide a baseline for interpreting strangeness enhancement in heavy-ion collisions, noting that thermalization at freeze-out does not rule out quark-gluon plasma formation during earlier stages of the collision.
