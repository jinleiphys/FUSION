# PANDORA project: photo-nuclear reactions below $A=60$

> Photo-nuclear reactions of light nuclei below a mass of $A=60$ are studied experimentally and theoretically by the PANDORA (Photo-Absorption of Nuclei and Decay Observation for Reactions in Astrophysics) project. Two experimental methods, virtual-photon excitation by proton scattering and real-photo absorption by a high-brilliance gamma-ray beam produced by laser Compton scattering, will be applied to measure the photo-absorption cross sections and the decay branching ratio of each decay channel as a function of the photon energy. Several nuclear models, e.g. anti-symmetrized molecular dynamics, mean-field type models, a large-scale shell model, and ab initio models, will be employed to predict the photo-nuclear reactions. The uncertainty in the model predictions will be evaluated from the discrepancies between the model predictions and the experimental data. The data and the predictions will be implemented in a general reaction calculation code TALYS . The results will be applied to the simulation of the photo-disintegration process of ultra-high-energy cosmic rays in inter-galactic propagation.

## Key claim

The PANDORA project aims to systematically measure photo-absorption cross sections and decay branching ratios for light nuclei below mass A=60, but the abstract glosses over that the approximation of treating neutron emission as the total photo-absorption cross section is not justified in this mass region due to comparable or larger branching ratios to proton and alpha decay channels, and that reliable theoretical predictions are more complicated than for heavier nuclei due to effects such as nuclear shell structure, alpha-clustering, isospin-selection rules, and fragmentation of the isovector giant dipole resonance.

## Method

Two complementary experimental methods will be employed: virtual-photon excitation by relativistic proton scattering at very forward angles (at RCNP in Osaka University and iThemba LABS in South Africa) and real-photon excitation using a high-brilliance quasi-monoenergetic gamma-ray beam produced by laser Compton scattering (at ELI-NP in Romania). Several nuclear models will be employed for theoretical predictions, including antisymmetrized molecular dynamics, mean-field type models (relativistic nuclear field theory, random-phase approximation with density functional approach, quasiparticle phonon model with energy density functional), large-scale shell-model calculations, and ab initio type calculations. The experimental data and model predictions will be implemented in the general reaction calculation code TALYS for applications including simulation of ultra-high-energy cosmic ray photo-disintegration during intergalactic propagation.

## Key numbers

- Mass region below A=60: the project focuses on light nuclei below this mass threshold, where the approximation of treating (gamma,xn) cross sections as total photo-absorption is not justified
- Two-neutron separation energy in 13C: S_nn = 23.7 MeV, referenced as context for comparing photo-neutron cross section data
- 27Al chosen as reference nucleus for experimental campaigns due to ease of fabrication, natural mono-isotopic abundance, availability of (gamma,abs) data, and prediction of reasonably large branching ratios

## Context

This project extends the study of electric dipole response of nuclei, which has been widely studied in the mass region above A~90 where nuclei show bulk and smooth properties with less dependence on shell structure, as referenced by Bracco (2019) and Zilges (2022). The work addresses the scarcity and mutual inconsistency of experimental data for light nuclei, particularly for charged particle decay channels, and the inadequacy of theoretical models optimized for heavier nuclei when applied to lighter systems. The project enables better characterization of photo-interactions of ultra-high-energy cosmic rays for modeling their extragalactic propagation and acceleration in astrophysical sources, and has applications in radiation shield design, non-destructive testing, gamma-ray imaging, isotope production, and nuclear medicine.
