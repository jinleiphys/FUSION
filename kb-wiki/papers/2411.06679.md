# Finite nuclei in an extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model

> We propose a new theoretical framework to investigate the properties of finite nuclei based on an extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (eNJL) model, where the Dirac sea, the spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking, and the quark degrees of freedom are considered by extending the SU(3) NJL model and treating baryons as clusters of quarks. The eNJL model can then be readily adopted to examine the matter states ranging from baryonic matter to quark matter in a unified manner. In this work, by assuming spherically symmetric finite nuclei and neglecting the center-of-mass or rotational corrections, we systematically investigate the properties of finite nuclei based on the eNJL model with additional pairing correlations. It is found that our model generally reproduces the binding energies of the 2495 nuclei ($A>2$) from the 2016 Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME2016) with the root-mean-square deviations $5.38$ MeV. The deviations are mainly attributed to the too large shell gaps at magic numbers $N(Z) =28$, 50, and 82 as well as the spurious shell closures at $N(Z)=34$, 58, and 92. Meanwhile, the obtained charge radii of 906 nuclei are systematically smaller than the experimental values with root-mean-square deviations $0.127$ fm. In our future study, we expect to reduce the uncertainties of our predictions by carefully calibrating the density dependence of coupling constants and considering deformations with microscopic collective corrections from the nucleons in the Fermi sea and quarks in the Dirac sea.

# Finite nuclei in an extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model

## Key claim
The extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (eNJL) model, treating baryons as quark clusters and including the Dirac sea and spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking, reproduces the binding energies of 2495 nuclei from AME2016 with a root-mean-square deviation of 5.38 MeV, but only under the assumptions of spherical symmetry and with center-of-mass and rotational corrections neglected. The deviations are primarily due to too large shell gaps at magic numbers and spurious shell closures, while charge radii are systematically too small.

## Method
The authors extend the SU(3) NJL model by replacing quark condensates with scalar sigma fields and treating baryons as clusters of quarks, with density-dependent coupling constants for the scalar, vector, and isovector channels. They solve the Dirac equation for nucleons in a mean-field approximation assuming spherical symmetry, using Thomas-Fermi approximation for quarks in the Dirac sea, and add a phenomenological pairing energy (delta = 6 MeV) to account for odd-even staggering. The coupled equations for nucleon wave functions, quark masses, boson fields, and densities are solved iteratively in coordinate space with a box size of 12.8 fm and grid spacing of 0.1 fm.

## Key numbers
- Binding energies of 2495 nuclei (A > 2) from AME2016 reproduced with root-mean-square deviation of 5.38 MeV.
- Charge radii of 906 nuclei systematically smaller than experimental values (Angeli 2013) with root-mean-square deviation of 0.127 fm.
- Nucleon mass in vacuum obtained from the model is 938.9 MeV.
- Current quark masses adopted: m_u0 = m_d0 = 5.5 MeV, m_s0 = 140.7 MeV (Rehberg 1996).
- Omega meson mass set to m_omega = 2500 MeV to prevent unrealistic density fluctuations from the six-point interaction.
- Pairing formula uses delta = 6 MeV with A-dependence as delta/sqrt(A).

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous eNJL model (Xia 2024) from infinite baryonic and quark matter to finite nuclei, providing a unified framework that includes quark degrees of freedom, the Dirac sea, and chiral symmetry breaking. It contrasts with relativistic density functional theories (Meng 2016) and microscopic many-body theories (Hebeler 2021) that treat nucleons as point particles, and with the quark-meson-coupling model (Guichon 2018) that confines quarks in bags. The results enable future improvements by calibrating density-dependent couplings and including deformations and collective corrections from both nucleons in the Fermi sea and quarks in the Dirac sea.
