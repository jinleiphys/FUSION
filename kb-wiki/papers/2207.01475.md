# Semi-microscopic theory of two proton emission

> We propose a semi-microscopic model for the simultaneous emission of two protons. This model has the advantage of avoiding certain technical aspects of a fully microscopic 3-body framework, while also allowing the investigation of the influence of proton pairing on the total lifetime of the decaying nucleus. Thus, we use the standard singlet two-proton wave function on the nuclear surface, provided by the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) approach, as a boundary condition for the propagator operator. Our model allows for the estimation of all quantities related to the $2p$ emission process, since it provides the 3-body wave function over most of the domain. We show that reasonable agreement with experimental values can be reached by varying the $pp$ pairing strength outside the nucleus in an interval close to the "bare" singlet value.

## Key claim

The authors propose a semi-microscopic model for simultaneous two-proton emission that avoids the technical difficulties of a fully microscopic three-body framework (such as removing spurious two-body bound states) while still allowing investigation of proton pairing effects on the total lifetime. The model achieves "reasonable agreement" with experimental values only by varying the proton-proton pairing strength outside the nucleus within an interval close to the "bare" singlet value, rather than reproducing experimental values exactly from first principles.

## Method

The authors use the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) approach to compute the singlet two-proton wave function on the nuclear surface, which serves as a boundary condition for the propagator operator in the external region. The three-body wave function is expanded in hyperspherical harmonics, with the system solved using the renormalized Numerov algorithm for the external region (where nucleus-proton potentials are treated as point-charge Coulomb interactions and proton-proton interactions include a central Gaussian nuclear term plus Coulomb repulsion). The internal wave function is constructed from Woods-Saxon single-particle states with BCS pairing, and the decay width is extracted by matching the internal and external wave functions at a matching radius, using the Wronskian of the hyper-radial components.

## Key numbers

* For $^{19}$Mg: $Q_{2p} = 0.750$ MeV, angular momentum $l=2$, experimental $\log_{10}\Gamma_{\mathrm{exp}} = -10.121$ MeV
* For $^{45}$Fe: $Q_{2p} = 1.210$ MeV, angular momentum $l=3$, experimental $\log_{10}\Gamma_{\mathrm{exp}} = -18.941$ MeV
* For $^{54}$Zn: $Q_{2p} = 1.480$ MeV, angular momentum $l=1$, experimental $\log_{10}\Gamma_{\mathrm{exp}} = -18.911$ MeV
* The proton-proton interaction radius is fixed at $r_0 = 2$ fm throughout the calculations
* The decay width from the model agrees with experiment for $v_0 \simeq -43$ MeV, while the "bare" value is $v_0 \simeq -35$ MeV
* Convergence of the decay width is achieved at hyper-radii of approximately 30 fm for the nuclei studied
* The diagonal of the potential matrix accounts for 95% of the Frobenius norm at large distances
* For $^{54}$Zn at $v_0 = -45$ MeV, the stability plateau extends between 8 and 11.5 fm matching radius, where a variation of 15 MeV in $v_0$ changes the total decay width by a factor less than 5

## Context

This work extends the hyperspherical harmonics framework for three-body nuclear decays developed by Grigorenko (2001) and the two-body resonant state formalism of Delion (2013), while avoiding the technical complications of fully microscopic approaches such as coupled channels models (Grigorenko, 2001-2010) and R-matrix descriptions (Brown, 2003). The semi-microscopic approach enables systematic study of how proton pairing strength affects two-proton decay lifetimes across different mass regions, from light nuclei like $^{19}$Mg to medium-mass emitters like $^{45}$Fe and $^{54}$Zn. The model reveals that the three-body problem becomes effectively decoupled beyond a certain hyper-radius, with the wave function flowing predominantly into the lowest hyperspherical channels, suggesting that future calculations could be simplified by retaining only a few low-lying channels.
