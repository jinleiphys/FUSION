# Spontaneous mass generation and the small dimensions of the Standard Model gauge groups U(1), SU(2) and SU(3)

> The gauge symmetry of the Standard Model is SU(3)_c x SU(2)_L x U(1)_Y for unknown reasons. One aspect that can be addressed is the low dimensionality of all its subgroups. Why not much larger groups like SU(7), or for that matter, SP(38) or E7? We observe that fermions charged under large groups acquire much bigger dynamical masses, all things being equal at a high e.g. GUT scale, than ordinary quarks. Should such multicharged fermions exist, they are too heavy to be observed today and have either decayed early on (if they couple to the rest of the Standard Model) or become reliquial dark matter (if they don't). The result follows from strong antiscreening of the running coupling for those larger groups (with an appropriately small number of flavors) together with scaling properties of the Dyson-Schwinger equation for the fermion mass.

## Key claim

Fermions charged under gauge groups larger than the Standard Model's SU(3), SU(2), and U(1) acquire exponentially larger dynamical masses when all couplings are equal at a high Grand Unified Theory (GUT) scale, making them too heavy to detect today; this result holds only when the number of fermion flavors is below a critical value that preserves antiscreening.

## Method

The authors solved the rainbow-ladder Dyson-Schwinger equation for the fermion propagator in a simplified ansatz with unit wavefunction renormalization, using both a cutoff regularization at 10 GeV and a momentum-subtraction (MOM) renormalization scheme. They matched these non-perturbative solutions to one-loop perturbative running of the coupling and mass from the GUT scale down to the scale where the product of the color factor and coupling reaches 0.4, then used scaling properties of the DSE to extrapolate results for larger groups from the known SU(3) solution.

## Key numbers

* For SU(3) with a cutoff at 10 GeV and coupling fixed to yield a 300 MeV constituent quark mass, the coupling constant was g ≈ 15.1, corresponding to α_s ≈ 18.
* The GUT scale was taken as 10^15 GeV, with α_s(μ_GUT) = 0.017 and m(μ_GUT) = 1 MeV for all groups.
* The matching point σ where C_F α_s(σ) = 0.4 (with α_s = 0.3 for SU(3)) moves to higher scales for larger groups according to σ = μ_GUT × exp[(π/β_1)(1/α_s(σ) - 1/α_s(μ_GUT))].
* The critical number of flavors for SU(3) is estimated as N_f^critical = 8 ± 1 from DSE studies or 11 ± 2 from renormalization group equations, and scales approximately as N_f^critical ∝ N_c.

## Context

This work extends the study of dynamical chiral symmetry breaking in non-Abelian gauge theories to explain why the Standard Model gauge groups are small, contrasting with anthropic arguments. It builds on Dyson-Schwinger equation methods used by Roberts (2012) and Bashir (2012) for QCD, and on lattice studies by Maas (2010) showing similar Green's function structure for SU(2) and G2 groups. The result suggests that if larger gauge symmetries exist at the GUT scale, their fermions would be either too heavy to produce at colliders or, if decoupled from the Standard Model, could constitute relic dark matter.
