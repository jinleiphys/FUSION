# Regularizing thermo and magnetic contributions within nonrenormalizable theories

> The importance of implementing a proper regularization procedure in order to treat thermo and magnetic contributions within nonrenormalizable theories is investigated. Our study suggests that potential divergences should be isolated into the vacuum and purely magnetic contributions and then regularized while the convergent thermomagnetic contributions should be integrated over the full momentum range. This prescription is illustrated by applying the proper time formalism to the two flavor Polyakov--Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model, whose magnetic field dependent coupling has been recently determined. Observables such as the pressure, magnetization, speed of sound squared, and specific heat evaluated within our scheme are compared with results furnished by other three possible prescriptions. We show that these quantities display a thermomagnetic behavior which is physically more consistent when our scheme is adopted. In particular, we demonstrate that naively regulating the (entangled) vacuum, magnetic and thermomagnetic contributions leads to physically inconsistent results especially at the high temperature domain.

# Regularizing thermo and magnetic contributions within nonrenormalizable theories

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that in nonrenormalizable theories such as the Polyakov–Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) model, the physically most consistent regularization scheme isolates divergences into vacuum and purely magnetic contributions, then regularizes those while integrating the convergent thermomagnetic contributions over the full momentum range; naively regulating all entangled contributions together leads to physically inconsistent results, especially at high temperatures.

## Method

The authors apply the proper time formalism to the two-flavor PNJL model with a magnetic-field-dependent coupling determined from lattice QCD data by Endrodi (2019). They compare four regularization prescriptions: the standard proper time (SPT) scheme, the thermomagnetic regulated proper time (TRPT) scheme, the vacuum magnetic regularization (VMR) scheme proposed in this work, and the magnetic field independent regularization (MFIR) scheme. For each scheme, they compute the thermodynamical potential at the mean-field level and solve the gap equation simultaneously with the Polyakov loop equation to obtain the effective mass, then evaluate the pressure, magnetization, speed of sound squared, and specific heat as functions of temperature and magnetic field.

## Key numbers

- The cutoff is set to Λ = 675 MeV and the current quark mass to m_c = 3.5 MeV throughout all calculations.
- At eB = 0, the dimensionless coupling G(B)Λ² = 5.83200 for all four regularization schemes.
- At eB = 0.6 GeV², G(B)Λ² = 2.69719 for VMR and MFIR schemes, and 3.05269 for SPT and TRPT schemes.
- The pseudocritical temperature decreases with increasing magnetic field for all schemes, consistent with lattice QCD predictions.
- The TRPT scheme produces a maximum in the subtracted pressure at T ≈ 0.23 GeV for eB = 0.6 GeV², indicating loss of predictive power at high temperatures.
- The MFIR scheme predicts paramagnetic behavior (ℳ > 0) at low B and diamagnetic behavior (ℳ < 0) at high B, while the other schemes predict diamagnetic behavior up to T = 0.25 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of regularization procedures for magnetized quark matter in effective models, following the identification by Fukushima (2012) that pionic loops could resolve discrepancies between lattice QCD and mean-field predictions for the pseudocritical temperature. The authors build on the B-dependent coupling approach of Farias (2014) and Endrodi (2019), who determined G(B) from lattice QCD data. The study directly addresses inconsistencies identified in earlier works by Costa (2009, 2010) regarding the Stefan-Boltzmann limit at high temperatures, and by Avancini (2019), Allen (2015), and Duarte (2015) regarding spurious solutions from inappropriate regularization. The VMR scheme proposed here enables reliable calculation of magnetization, which was problematic in the MFIR scheme due to subtraction of mass-independent B-dependent terms, and avoids the high-temperature pathologies of the TRPT scheme.
