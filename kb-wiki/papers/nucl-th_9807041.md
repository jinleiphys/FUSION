# Nuclear Physics

> Nuclear Physics is the branch of physics that deals with the properties and structure of matter on the hadronic level. In this article we review briefly the history of this field, which has a major role in the development of our understanding of nature. We then proceed to give an outline of a current perspective of the field and of some of the issues that are now on its frontiers.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that nuclear physics, as of 1998, encompasses both the traditional many-body physics of nuclei and the emerging understanding of hadron structure from quantum chromodynamics (QCD), with the caveat that the transition from a description in terms of nucleons and mesons to one in terms of quarks and gluons is still not understood.

## Method

The authors provide a historical survey of nuclear physics from its origins in radioactivity and Rutherford's scattering experiments through the development of the shell model, collective model, and reaction theory, then review the current status of hadron physics (pions, nucleon structure, nuclear forces) and nuclear structure (high angular momentum states, limits of binding). The paper is a narrative review with references to specific experimental results and theoretical developments, including comparisons between chiral perturbation theory calculations and experimental data for pion photoproduction.

## Key numbers

- The amplitude for $\gamma p \rightarrow \pi^+ n$ at threshold is measured as $28.4 \pm 0.6$ in units of $10^{-3}/m_\pi^+$, compared to chiral perturbation theory predictions of 34.0 (lowest order) and 26.4 (next order)
- The amplitude for $\gamma p \rightarrow \pi^0 p$ at threshold is measured as $-1.31 \pm 0.08$ in the same units, compared to chiral perturbation theory predictions of 0 (lowest order), -3.58 (next order), and -1.16 (fourth order)
- The weak isovector axial coupling constant $g_A$ from neutron beta decay is 1.26, compared to the naive constituent quark model prediction of 5/3
- Deep inelastic scattering gives $\Delta \Sigma = 0.33 \pm 0.08$ for the fraction of proton spin carried by quark spins, compared to the naive constituent quark model prediction of 1
- The individual quark spin contributions are $\Delta u = 0.84 \pm 0.04$, $\Delta d = -0.42 \pm 0.04$, and $\Delta s = -0.09 \pm 0.04$
- Superdeformed bands in nuclei show 2:1 axis ratios, compared to normal deformed bands with approximately 1.3:1 axis ratios
- Chiral perturbation theory predicts three-body forces are smaller than two-body forces by the ratio $(p/\Lambda)^2$, where $\Lambda$ is a QCD scale of order 1 GeV; if the two-body potential averages 20 MeV, the three-body one would be about 1 MeV

## Context

This review summarizes the state of nuclear physics at the end of the 20th century, extending the historical narrative from the early work of Rutherford, Bethe, Goeppert-Mayer, Jensen, Bohr, and Mottelson through to contemporary developments in chiral perturbation theory (Weinberg, 1979) and high-precision nucleon-nucleon potentials such as Argonne V18 and CD Bonn. It enables readers to understand the connections between traditional nuclear structure physics and the emerging QCD-based approaches, while identifying open questions such as the proton spin puzzle, the nature of confinement, and the properties of nuclei near the drip lines.
