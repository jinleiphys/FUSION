# Impact of the radial profile of atomic nuclei on observables in high-energy collisions

> In heavy-ion phenomenology, the nucleon density distribution in colliding nuclei is commonly described by a two-parameter Woods-Saxon (WS) distribution. However, this approach overlooks the detailed radial structure in the density distribution that arises from the quantal filling patterns of neutrons and protons. These fine structures, as estimated by the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock density functional, cause slight deviations in heavy-ion observables from the WS baseline, which cannot be captured by simply readjusting the WS parameters. These deviations depend on centrality and observable but often exhibit similar shapes for different nuclei. To fully exploit the exceptional sensitivity of isobar collisions to nuclear structure, such fine structures should be considered.

## Key claim

The detailed radial "wiggle" structures in realistic nucleon density distributions from Skyrme-Hartree-Fock density functional theory cause deviations in heavy-ion observables from the standard Woods-Saxon baseline that are smaller than typical isobar-pair differences for most observables, but are comparable for triangular eccentricity \(\varepsilon_3\) and cannot be simultaneously absorbed by simply readjusting the Woods-Saxon parameters.

## Method

The authors generated nucleon density distributions for \(^{96}\)Zr, \(^{112}\)Sn, and \(^{197}\)Au using the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock model with the MSL0 force, then fitted Woods-Saxon parameters by matching the first and second radial moments. They performed Monte Carlo Glauber simulations (100 million minimum-bias events per species, nucleon-nucleon cross-section 42 mb) comparing five density profiles: the default Woods-Saxon, the full SHF distribution, a mixed distribution combining SHF at low radii with Woods-Saxon at high radii, and two Woods-Saxon profiles modified by typical isobar differences (\(\Delta a = -0.06\) fm and \(\Delta R_0 = +0.07\) fm). They computed the relative differences \(D_{\mathcal{O}}\) for \(\varepsilon_2\), \(\varepsilon_3\), and the probability distribution of participating nucleons \(p(N_{\text{part}})\) as functions of \(N_{\text{part}}\).

## Key numbers

- For \(^{96}\)A+\(^{96}\)A collisions, reducing the skin thickness \(a\) by 0.06 fm increases \(\varepsilon_2\) by 2% in mid-central collisions; increasing \(R_0\) by 0.07 fm decreases \(\varepsilon_2\) by about 0.4%.
- For \(^{197}\)A+\(^{197}\)A collisions, the same variations in \(a\) and \(R_0\) produce a 3.3% increase and a 0.9% decrease in \(\varepsilon_2\), respectively.
- The SHF-to-Woods-Saxon difference in \(\varepsilon_3\) is about 2% smaller for SHF in mid-central collisions across all three nuclei.
- The impact of SHF wiggles on \(p(N_{\text{part}})\) reaches about 0.4–0.6% in mid-central collisions for \(^{96}\)Zr.
- The mixed distribution (inner-region wiggles only) produces changes smaller than 0.5% in all observables.
- The extracted Woods-Saxon parameters from moment matching are: \(^{96}\)Zr (\(R_0 = 5.059\) fm, \(a = 0.5774\) fm), \(^{112}\)Sn (\(R_0 = 5.538\) fm, \(a = 0.5576\) fm), \(^{197}\)Au (\(R_0 = 6.610\) fm, \(a = 0.5552\) fm).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Shou (2014) and Xu (2017) that questioned the sufficiency of the Woods-Saxon parameterization in heavy-ion phenomenology, and directly addresses the caveat raised by Xu (2021) that Woods-Saxon fits miss detailed features of density functional theory distributions. The results show that while the Woods-Saxon form is adequate for single-system measurements with typical experimental uncertainties, the extreme precision of isobar ratios measured by STAR (2021), with uncertainties below 0.5%, requires accounting for realistic DFT structures. This enables future work incorporating nuclear deformation in DFT versus deformed Woods-Saxon parameterization (Ryssens, 2023) and including nuclear structure uncertainties in Bayesian analyses of heavy-ion collisions (Giacalone, 2023).
