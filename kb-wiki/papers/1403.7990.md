# Longitudinal hydrodynamics from event-by-event Landau initial conditions

> We investigate three-dimensional ideal hydrodynamic evolution, with Landau initial conditions, incorporating event-by-event variation with many events and transverse density inhomogeneities. We show that the transition to boost-invariant flow occurs too late for realistic setups, with corrections of $\order{20-30\%}$ expected at freezeout for most scenarios. Moreover, the deviation from boost-invariance is correlated with both transverse flow and elliptic flow, with the more highly transversely flowing regions also showing the most violation of boost invariance. Therefore, if longitudinal flow is not fully developed at the early stages of heavy ion collisions, hydrodynamics where boost-invariance holds at mid-rapidity is inadequate to extract transport coefficients of the quark-gluon plasma. We conclude by arguing that developing experimental probes of boost invariance is necessary, and suggest some promising directions in this regard.

## Key claim

The paper shows that if the initial state of a heavy-ion collision follows the Landau (full-stopping) picture rather than the Bjorken (boost-invariant) picture, the system does **not** evolve to approximate boost-invariance within realistic timescales; corrections of order 20–30% at freezeout persist, and the deviation from boost-invariance is correlated with transverse and elliptic flow.

## Method

The authors performed event-by-event (3+1)D ideal hydrodynamic simulations using the CL-SHASTA code, starting from Landau initial conditions with transverse density inhomogeneities generated by a Glauber Monte Carlo for Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV. The initial longitudinal flow was set to zero except for a local momentum imbalance given by the firestreak model (Eq. 3), with longitudinal thickness Δ = 0.1 fm and an ideal gas equation of state (p = ρ/3). They simulated 10,000 events, each evolving 8 million grid cells over 300 time steps up to 10 fm/c, and compared results to (3+1)D calculations with Bjorken boost-invariance assumed as an initial condition.

## Key numbers

- Corrections of order **20–30%** expected at freezeout for most scenarios when comparing Landau to Bjorken evolution.
- At freezeout (initial temperature ~300 MeV), the ratio y_f/y_s is above unity by about **40%** around midrapidity.
- At earlier stages relevant for transverse and elliptic flow formation (t ~ ε_r R/c_s), corrections are of order **50%**.
- The longitudinal thickness parameter Δ = **0.1 fm** was used for the Landau initial condition.
- The Glauber Monte Carlo used a Wood-Saxon distribution with radius **6.38 fm** and diffuseness **0.535 fm**, with nucleon-nucleon cross section σ_NN = **42 mb**.
- The grid covered **±10 fm** in each spatial dimension with **8 million grid cells**.
- The hydrodynamic evolution was run over **300 time steps** covering expansion until **10 fm/c**.
- The (3+1)D analytical solution from Wong (2008) was used for comparison with the 1D expansion.

## Context

This work extends the classic Landau hydrodynamics picture (Landau, 1953) and contrasts it with the widely used Bjorken boost-invariant framework (Bjorken, 1983) that underlies most modern hydrodynamic extractions of transport coefficients (Denicol, 2012; Bass, 2009; Schenke, 2012). The results contradict the common assumption that Landau evolution converges to Bjorken evolution after sufficient time (Landau, 1953; Mishustin, 2005; Bozek, 2011). The paper enables future work to extract transport coefficients (especially shear viscosity) that could be significantly different from those inferred from boost-invariant calculations, and suggests experimental tests using HBT radii (Miklos, 2005) and jet tomography (Jia, 2012) to distinguish between Landau and Bjorken initial conditions.
