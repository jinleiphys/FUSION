# Production and Distribution of $^{44}$Ti and $^{56}$Ni in a Three-dimensional Supernova Model Resembling Cassiopeia A

> The spatial and velocity distributions of nuclear species synthesized in the innermost regions of core-collapse supernovae can yield important clues about explosion asymmetries and the operation of the still disputed explosion mechanism. Recent observations of radioactive $^{44}$Ti with high-energy satellite telescopes (Nuclear Spectroscopic Telescope Array [NuSTAR], INTEGRAL) have measured gamma-ray line details, which provide direct evidence of large-scale explosion asymmetries in SN 1987A and in Cassiopeia A (Cas A) even by mapping of the spatial brightness distribution (NuSTAR). Here we discuss a 3D simulation of a neutrino-driven explosion, using a parameterized neutrino engine, whose $^{44}$Ti distribution is mostly concentrated in one hemisphere pointing opposite to the neutron star (NS) kick velocity. Both exhibit intriguing resemblance to the observed morphology of the Cas A remnant, although neither the progenitor nor the explosion was fine-tuned for a perfect match. Our results demonstrate that the asymmetries observed in this remnant can, in principle, be accounted for by a neutrino-driven explosion, and that the high $^{44}$Ti abundance in Cas A may be explained without invoking rapid rotation or a jet-driven explosion, because neutrino-driven explosions generically eject large amounts of high-entropy matter. The recoil acceleration of the NS is connected to mass ejection asymmetries and is opposite to the direction of the stronger explosion, fully compatible with the gravitational tugboat mechanism. Our results also imply that Cas A and SN 1987A could possess similarly "one-sided" Ti and Fe asymmetries, with the difference that Cas A is viewed from a direction with large inclination angle to the NS motion, whereas the NS in SN 1987A should have a dominant velocity component pointing toward us.

## Key claim

A three-dimensional neutrino-driven supernova simulation, without fine-tuning for progenitor or explosion properties, can reproduce the observed one-sided $^{44}$Ti asymmetry and high $^{44}$Ti yield of Cassiopeia A, provided the electron fraction in neutrino-processed ejecta is close to 0.5 rather than the slightly neutron-rich values obtained from the approximate neutrino transport.

## Method

The authors performed a 3D hydrodynamical simulation (model W15-2-cw-IIb) of a neutrino-driven explosion using the Prometheus code with a Yin-Yang grid, starting from a 15 solar mass red supergiant progenitor stripped of most of its hydrogen envelope to mimic a Type IIb supernova. They post-processed 131,072 tracer particles through a nuclear reaction network of 6300 species to compute $^{44}$Ti and $^{56}$Ni yields, testing two cases for the electron fraction in neutrino-processed ejecta: one using the progenitor's $Y_e$ values (near 0.5) and one using $Y_e$ from the simulation's approximate neutrino transport.

## Key numbers

- Total $^{44}$Ti yield in model W15-2-cw-IIb (progenitor $Y_e$): $1.57 \times 10^{-4} M_\odot$
- Total $^{56}$Ni yield in model W15-2-cw-IIb (progenitor $Y_e$): $9.57 \times 10^{-2} M_\odot$
- Total $^{44}$Ti yield in model W15-2-cw-IIb-Ye$_\mathrm{sim}$ (simulation $Y_e$): $1.58 \times 10^{-5} M_\odot$
- Total $^{56}$Ni yield in model W15-2-cw-IIb-Ye$_\mathrm{sim}$ (simulation $Y_e$): $4.29 \times 10^{-2} M_\odot$
- Explosion energy of the underlying model W15-2: $1.5 \times 10^{51}$ erg (1.5 bethe)
- NuSTAR/INTEGRAL inferred $^{44}$Ti yield for Cas A: $(1.0-1.7) \times 10^{-4} M_\odot$
- Neutrino-processed ejecta contribute 95% of $^{44}$Ti and 56% of $^{56}$Ni in the progenitor-$Y_e$ case
- Shock-heated ejecta contribute $8.66 \times 10^{-6} M_\odot$ of $^{44}$Ti and $4.20 \times 10^{-2} M_\odot$ of $^{56}$Ni in both cases

## Context

This work extends the series of 3D neutrino-driven supernova simulations by Wongwathanarat et al. (2010, 2013, 2015) and demonstrates that the observed asymmetries in Cassiopeia A can be explained without invoking rapid rotation or jet-driven explosions, contrary to earlier suggestions by Nagataki et al. (1997, 1998) that asymmetric explosions were needed for large $^{44}$Ti yields. The results enable direct comparison with NuSTAR observations by Grefenstette et al. (2014) and INTEGRAL data by Siegert et al. (2015), and imply that Cas A and SN 1987A may share similar one-sided Ti and Fe asymmetries viewed from different inclination angles.
