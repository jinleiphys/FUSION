# Hans Bethe: The Nuclear Many Body Problem

> We discuss the work of Hans Bethe and others in formulating a theoretical foundation for the nuclear shell model. Written for a general audience, this article describes the evolution from Brueckner's reaction matrix theory to the Moszkowski-Scott separation method and ultimately to the Reference Spectrum method of Bethe, Brandow, and Petschek. We also discuss connections with the recently developed low momentum nucleon-nucleon interactions.

# Hans Bethe: The Nuclear Many Body Problem

## Key claim

The paper argues that the nuclear shell model can be reconciled with Niels Bohr's "compound nucleus" picture because the mean free path of a nucleon added at the Fermi surface becomes infinite, allowing it to travel without exciting other particles. The authors present the evolution of Brueckner theory through the Moszkowski-Scott separation method and Bethe's Reference Spectrum method as the theoretical foundation that justifies the shell model, with the caveat that this works primarily for ground states and low-energy excitations.

## Method

The paper traces the historical and theoretical development of the nuclear many-body problem, beginning with the atomic shell model and its application to nuclei through the harmonic oscillator potential with spin-orbit coupling. It then describes how Brueckner's G-matrix theory, derived from the Lippmann-Schwinger T-matrix formalism, handles the strong short-range repulsion (hard core) between nucleons by scattering them to momenta above the Fermi sea, with the Pauli principle treated as a correction. The authors explain how the G-matrix equation G = V + V(Q/e)G is solved with a model space cutoff at k_max = 2.1 fm⁻¹, and how this evolved into the modern V_low-k effective interaction approach.

## Key numbers

- The hard core radius of the nucleon-nucleon interaction was initially characterized as 0.6 × 10⁻¹³ cm, later revised to 0.4 × 10⁻¹³ cm
- The nuclear radius is given by R = 1.2 A^{1/3} × 10⁻¹³ cm (replacing the earlier estimate of 1.5 A^{1/3} × 10⁻¹³ cm from Coulomb energy differences)
- The last nucleon in a nucleus is typically bound by about 8 MeV
- The fine structure constant α = e²/ħc = 1/137, characterizing the weakness of the electromagnetic interaction compared to the strong interaction
- The Rydberg energy for hydrogen is Ry ≃ 13.6 electron volts, the ionization energy of hydrogen
- The cutoff momentum for modern effective interactions is k_max = 2.1 fm⁻¹ = Λ
- The imaginary part of the optical potential used for averaging neutron strength functions is W = 3.1 MeV

## Context

This review extends the work of Brueckner (1950s) on the G-matrix approach to the nuclear many-body problem and connects it to the earlier shell model successes of Goeppert Mayer and Jensen (late 1940s), who explained magic numbers through strong spin-orbit splitting. The paper also discusses the Feshbach, Porter, and Weisskopf (1950s) demonstration that neutron strength functions follow single-particle potential envelopes, contradicting the earlier "black nucleus" model of Feshbach, Peaslee, and Weisskopf. The work enables modern low-momentum nucleon-nucleon interactions (V_low-k) as developed by Bogner and collaborators, and connects to Landau's Fermi liquid theory (1958) which explains why quasiparticles at the Fermi surface have infinite mean free paths.
