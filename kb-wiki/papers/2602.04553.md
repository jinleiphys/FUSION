# Exterior complex scaling enables physics-informed neural networks for quantum scattering

> Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) have emerged as a powerful tool for solving differential equations, yet their application to quantum scattering problems has been hindered by the oscillatory, non-decaying nature of scattering wave functions. In this work, I demonstrate that exterior complex scaling (ECS) transforms scattering boundary conditions into exponentially decaying waves suitable for neural network solutions, enabling PINNs to solve nuclear scattering problems for the first time. I develop a driven-equation formulation where the source term is confined to the real axis, avoiding the need to analytically continue nuclear potentials into the complex plane. The method is validated on nucleon-nucleus scattering (n+$^{40}$Ca at $E_{\text{lab}}=20$~MeV) with 21 partial waves, achieving phase shift accuracy of $Δδ< 0.1^\circ$ for most channels when compared to conventional solvers. I further demonstrate the approach on heavy-ion scattering ($^6$Li+$^{208}$Pb at 40~MeV) with 41 partial waves and strong Coulomb effects. This work establishes the foundation for extending PINNs to inverse problems where end-to-end differentiability enables direct fitting of optical potential parameters, coupled-channel reactions, and few-body scattering where traditional grid methods face exponential scaling.

# Exterior complex scaling enables physics-informed neural networks for nuclear reactions

## Key claim

Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) can solve nuclear scattering problems for the first time when combined with exterior complex scaling (ECS), which transforms oscillatory scattering boundary conditions into exponentially decaying waves; the method is validated on two benchmark systems but the phase shift accuracy degrades from Δδ < 0.1° for strongly absorbed channels to Δδ ≤ 0.60° for all channels up to ℓ = 10 in the n+⁴⁰Ca case.

## Method

The radial Schrödinger equation is transformed via exterior complex scaling, where the radial coordinate is analytically continued into the complex plane beyond a scaling radius R₀ using a smooth switching function, converting outgoing waves into exponentially damped functions. A driven-equation formulation separates the total wave function into an incident Coulomb wave and a scattered wave satisfying an inhomogeneous equation whose source term is confined to the real axis, avoiding analytic continuation of nuclear potentials. A fully-connected feedforward neural network with four hidden layers of 128 neurons and sinusoidal activations represents the scattered wave, with boundary conditions enforced through a sigmoid-capped multiplicative factor that prevents numerical overflow for high angular momentum channels. Training uses a two-stage procedure with Adam optimizer followed by L-BFGS, with an auto-adaptive anchor loss and linear warm-down for weak-source channels to prevent bias in the absorption-to-transparency transition region.

## Key numbers

- n+⁴⁰Ca at Elab = 20 MeV: phase shift accuracy Δδ < 0.1° for strongly absorbed channels (ℓ ≤ 4) and Δδ ≤ 0.60° for all channels up to ℓ = 10, computed with 21 partial waves using the Koning-Delaroche global optical potential
- ⁶Li+²⁰⁸Pb at 40 MeV: mean S-matrix accuracy |Δ|Sℓ|| ≈ 3 × 10⁻³ across the full angular momentum range of 41 partial waves, including the absorption-to-transparency transition region, with strong Coulomb effects
- ECS parameters chosen such that the damping factor exp[-k(Rmax - R₀) sinθ] at the outer boundary is below approximately 10⁻³
- For n+⁴⁰Ca: 2000 collocation points, 20,000 Adam epochs, and 800 L-BFGS epochs; for ⁶Li+²⁰⁸Pb: 4000 collocation points, 30,000 Adam epochs, and 1000 L-BFGS epochs
- Anchor warm-down threshold sₐ* = 0.1 with Nwd = 15,000 epochs for weak-source channels

## Context

This work extends the COLOSS code developed by Liu, Lei, and Ren (2025) which implemented complex scaling for optical model calculations using conventional discretization methods, and builds on exterior complex scaling techniques pioneered in atomic and molecular physics by Rescigno (2000) and others. The PINN-ECS approach enables end-to-end differentiability for gradient-based optimization of optical potential parameters in inverse problems, and offers a potential pathway to coupled-channel reactions and few-body scattering (e.g., Faddeev equations) where traditional grid methods face exponential computational cost, though the present work validates only single-channel benchmarks against established solvers.
