# Reconstructing the impact parameter of proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions

> In proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collision experiments, one determines the centrality of a collision according to the multiplicity or energy deposited in a detector. This serves as a proxy for the true collision centrality, as defined by the impact parameter. We show that the probability distribution of impact parameter in a given bin of experiment-defined centrality can be reconstructed without assuming any specific model for the collision dynamics, in both proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus systems. The reconstruction is reliable up to about 10\% centrality, and is more accurate for nucleus-nucleus collisions. We perform an application of our procedure to experimental data from all the CERN Large Hadron Collider (LHC) collaborations, from which we extract, in Pb+Pb and $p$+Pb collisions, the corresponding distributions of impact parameter.

## Key claim

The probability distribution of impact parameter at a given experimentally-defined centrality can be reconstructed without assuming any specific model for the collision dynamics, using only the measured distribution of the centrality-defining observable (multiplicity or energy). The reconstruction is reliable up to about 10% centrality and is more accurate for nucleus-nucleus than for proton-nucleus collisions, with the key improvement over prior work being the replacement of a Gaussian fluctuation kernel with a gamma distribution.

## Method

The authors model the probability distribution of the centrality-defining observable n at fixed impact parameter b, P(n|b), as a gamma distribution with two parameters (shape k and scale θ). They assume the scale parameter θ is independent of b (variance proportional to mean), and parametrize the shape parameter k as a function of the true centrality c_b (cumulative impact-parameter distribution) using an exponential polynomial. The measured distribution P(n) is then fit by integrating P(n|b) over all impact parameters, using Eq. (1) P(n) = ∫₀¹ P(n|c_b) dc_b. The fit returns the parameters of the gamma distribution at each c_b, from which the impact-parameter distribution at fixed experimental centrality is obtained via Bayes' theorem, Eq. (2) P(b|n) = P(n|b)P(b)/P(n).

## Key numbers

- In Pb+Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV, the knee position (mean n for b=0) is reconstructed with better than 1% accuracy in model tests.
- In p+Pb collisions at √s_NN = 5.02 TeV, the knee position is overestimated by 11% in model tests.
- The fraction of events above the knee, c_knee, is 0.28-0.35% for Pb+Pb collisions across LHC experiments, and 7.0-9.3% for p+Pb collisions.
- For Pb+Pb collisions, the standard deviation σ_n(0) for central collisions is overestimated by 4% in model tests; for p+Pb collisions it is underestimated by 5%.
- The inelastic cross section used for Pb+Pb is σ_inel = 777 fm²; for p+Pb it is σ_inel = 210 fm² (from the TRENTO model).

## Context

This work extends the reconstruction method of Das (2017) from nucleus-nucleus to proton-nucleus collisions by replacing the Gaussian fluctuation kernel with a gamma distribution, which naturally handles the large fluctuations and long tails observed in p+Pb systems. The gamma distribution is a continuous version of the negative binomial distribution (NBD) used in earlier multiplicity studies by Giovannini (1985) and in color-glass condensate models by Gelis (2009). The method enables extraction of impact-parameter distributions from published LHC data (ALICE, ATLAS, CMS, LHCb) without relying on Glauber Monte Carlo simulations or the concept of participant nucleons, and can be applied to any extensive centrality-defining observable.
