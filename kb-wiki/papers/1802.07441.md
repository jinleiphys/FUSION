# Nucleon radius effects on neutron stars in quark mean field model

> We study the effects of free space nucleon radius on nuclear matter and neutron stars within the framework of quark mean field model. The nucleon radius is treated self-consistently with this model, where quark confinement is adjusted to fit different values of nucleon radius. Corrections due to center-of-mass motion, quark-pion coupling, and one gluon exchange are included to obtain the nucleon mass in vacuum. The meson coupling constants that describe the behavior of many-body nucleonic system are newly-constructed by reproducing the empirical saturation properties of nuclear matter, including the recent determinations of symmetry energy parameters. Our results show that the nucleon radius in free space have negligible effects on nuclear matter equation of state and neutron star mass-radius relations, which is different from the conclusion drawn in previous studies. We further explore that the sensitivity of star radius on the nucleon radius found in earlier publications is actually from the symmetry energy and its slope.

# Nucleon radius effects on neutron stars in quark mean field model

## Key claim

The free-space nucleon radius has negligible effects on nuclear matter equation of state and neutron star mass-radius relations when the symmetry energy and its slope are held fixed at saturation density, contrary to earlier claims that the nucleon radius significantly affects neutron star radius.

## Method

The authors employ the quark mean field (QMF) model, where constituent quarks (mass 300 MeV) are confined by a harmonic oscillator potential whose parameters are fitted to reproduce the free nucleon mass (939 MeV) and three chosen values of the free nucleon radius (0.80, 0.87, and 1.00 fm). The density dependence of the nucleon radius is derived self-consistently from solving the Dirac equation for a nucleon in medium, including corrections from center-of-mass motion, quark-pion coupling, and one gluon exchange. Nucleon-meson coupling constants for sigma, omega, and rho mesons are newly fitted to reproduce empirical saturation properties of nuclear matter (saturation density, binding energy, incompressibility, symmetry energy, symmetry energy slope, and effective mass), and 81 new QMF parameter sets are generated for studying nuclear matter and neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- Free nucleon radius values used: 0.80 fm, 0.87 fm (CODATA 2014 value), and 1.00 fm, varied by approximately 10% around the intermediate value
- Quark mass fixed at 300 MeV; free nucleon mass fixed at 939 MeV
- Saturation density: 0.16 fm^-3; binding energy at saturation: -16 MeV; effective mass ratio M_N*/M_N: 0.74
- Incompressibility K values used: 220, 260 MeV (empirical range 240 ± 20 MeV) and 328 MeV (from earlier finite nuclei fitting)
- Symmetry energy J values: 28, 31, 34 MeV (empirical value 31.6 ± 2.66 MeV)
- Symmetry energy slope L values: 40, 60, 80 MeV (empirical value 58.9 ± 16 MeV)
- For extreme K = 328 MeV, decreasing r_N from 1.00 fm to 0.80 fm changes maximum mass from 2.01 solar masses to 1.87 solar masses and radii from 11.8 km to 11.4 km (approximately 3% variation)
- With fixed K = 260 MeV, changing J from 28 to 34 MeV gives approximately 1.8% variation in neutron star radius; changing L from 40 to 80 MeV gives approximately 2.9% variation

## Context

This work extends the quark mean field model approach of Toki et al. and Shen et al. to reexamine the claimed connection between free nucleon radius and neutron star radius found in earlier studies by the authors of Refs. [proton] and [nuclradius], who used a relativistic mean field model with a phenomenological density-dependent nucleon radius. The present study contradicts those earlier conclusions by showing that the sensitivity of neutron star radius to nucleon radius actually arises from the well-established dependence of radius on symmetry energy and its slope, not from the nucleon radius itself. The QMF model allows self-consistent treatment of nucleon radius in medium, unlike the earlier phenomenological approach, and produces results consistent with heavy-ion collision flow constraints above 2 times saturation density.
