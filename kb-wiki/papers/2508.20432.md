# Perturbative high-energy evolution in the IP-Glasma initial state

> We include the perturbative JIMWLK energy evolution into the IP-Glasma initial state description used to simulate the early-time dynamics in heavy ion collisions. By numerically solving the JIMWLK equation on an event-by-event basis, we obtain the energy (Bjorken-$x$) dependent structure of the colliding nuclei. Combining the initial state with hydrodynamic simulations, this enables us to predict how observables evolve when moving from RHIC to LHC energies.

## Key claim
By incorporating perturbative JIMWLK energy evolution into the IP-Glasma model on an event-by-event basis, the authors obtain the energy (Bjorken-x) dependent structure of colliding nuclei without relying on a parametrized saturation scale, though the evolution still requires non-perturbative input at the initial momentum fraction x0 = 0.01.

## Method
The authors start with the IP-Glasma model at initial x0 = 0.01, where Wilson lines describe the eikonal propagation of quarks through nuclear color fields. They numerically solve the JIMWLK equation event-by-event down to x = ⟨pT⟩/√sNN, then evolve the system to proper time τ0 = 0.4 fm/c using Yang-Mills equations. The resulting energy-momentum tensor initializes hydrodynamic simulations with MUSIC, followed by hadronic transport using UrQMD at switching energy density e_sw = 0.18 GeV/fm³.

## Key numbers
- Initial momentum fraction for IP-Glasma input: x0 = 0.01
- Proper time for switching from Yang-Mills to hydrodynamics: τ0 = 0.4 fm/c
- Switching energy density for hadronization: e_sw = 0.18 GeV/fm³
- Center-of-mass energy for light ion collision predictions: √sNN = 5.36 TeV (O+O and Ne+Ne at LHC)

## Context
This work extends the IP-Glasma model (Schenke 2012) beyond its previous parametrized energy dependence via the saturation scale Qs fitted to proton structure data (Kowalski 2003). By incorporating perturbative JIMWLK evolution (Mueller 2001), the authors enable first-principles predictions of how bulk observables evolve from RHIC to LHC energies. The setup enables systematic studies of geometry evolution effects in small systems like O+O collisions, which are sensitive to the JIMWLK evolution and will be measured at both RHIC and LHC.
