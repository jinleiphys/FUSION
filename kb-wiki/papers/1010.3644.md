# Warm pasta phase in the Thomas-Fermi approximation

> In the present paper, the pasta phase is studied at finite temperatures within a Thomas-Fermi (TF) approach. Relativistic mean field models, both with constant and density-dependent couplings, are used to describe this frustrated system. We compare the present results with previous ones obtained within a phase-coexistence description and conclude that the TF approximation gives rise to a richer inner pasta phase structure and the homogeneous matter appears at higher densities. Finally, the transition density calculated within TF is compared with the results for this quantity obtained with other methods.

## Key claim

The Thomas-Fermi (TF) approximation predicts a richer inner pasta phase structure and a higher density for the transition to homogeneous matter compared to the coexisting-phases (CP) method, but after correcting an error in the CP code (which had used zero-temperature surface tension for all cases), the agreement between the two methods improves, with CP results now much closer to TF ones.

## Method

The authors performed Thomas-Fermi (TF) calculations at finite temperature for nuclear pasta phases in neutron star crusts, using two relativistic mean field models: NL3 (constant couplings) and TW (density-dependent couplings). They solved the meson field equations and Poisson equation self-consistently for one-, two-, and three-dimensional periodic structures (droplets, rods, slabs, tubes, bubbles) at temperatures T = 0, 5, 7, and 8 MeV, for proton fractions Y_p = 0.5 and 0.3, and for beta-equilibrium matter with trapped neutrinos. They compared these TF results with those from the coexisting-phases (CP) method, which uses a Gibbs construction with surface and Coulomb energy corrections.

## Key numbers

- For NL3 at T = 0 and Y_p = 0.5, the TF calculation gives onset densities: droplet at 0.000 fm^-3, rod at 0.025 fm^-3, slab at 0.043 fm^-3, tube at 0.073 fm^-3, bubble at 0.089 fm^-3, homogeneous at 0.114 fm^-3; the CP method gives homogeneous at 0.093 fm^-3.
- For TW at T = 0 and Y_p = 0.5, TF gives homogeneous at 0.115 fm^-3; CP gives homogeneous at 0.093 fm^-3.
- For NL3 at T = 8 MeV and Y_p = 0.5, TF still shows droplet (0.0069 fm^-3), rod (0.025 fm^-3), tube (0.036 fm^-3), bubble (0.063 fm^-3), and homogeneous (0.092 fm^-3); CP shows no pasta phase at this temperature.
- The CP critical temperatures above which pasta disappears are: for NL3, T_c^CP = 7.73 MeV (Y_p = 0.5) and 8.09 MeV (Y_p = 0.3); for TW, T_c^CP = 7.95 MeV (Y_p = 0.5) and 8.42 MeV (Y_p = 0.3).
- For beta-equilibrium cold matter with NL3, TF gives homogeneous phase at 0.055 fm^-3; CP gives 0.068 fm^-3.
- For beta-equilibrium cold matter with TW, TF gives homogeneous at 0.073 fm^-3; CP gives 0.077 fm^-3.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of the pasta phase at zero and finite temperature using the coexisting-phases method (Avancini et al., 2010) and the Thomas-Fermi approximation at zero temperature (Avancini et al., 2009). It corrects an earlier CP calculation that had used zero-temperature surface tension for all cases, and now shows improved agreement between the two methods. The results enable more accurate calculations of neutrino opacities in protoneutron star matter, as the different pasta structures (droplets, rods, slabs, tubes, bubbles) significantly affect diffusion coefficients and neutrino mean free paths, which in turn influence the Kelvin-Helmholtz phase of protoneutron star evolution and supernova explosion simulations.
