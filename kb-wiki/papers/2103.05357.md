# Experimental study of intruder components in light neutron-rich nuclei via single-nucleon transfer reaction

> With the development of radioactive beam facilities, studies concerning the shell evolution of unstable nuclei have recently gained prominence. Intruder components, particularly s-wave intrusion, in the low-lying states of light neutron-rich nuclei near N=8 are of importance in the study of shell evolution. The use of single-nucleon transfer reactions in inverse kinematics has been a sensitive tool that can be used to quantitatively investigate the single-particle orbital component of selectively populated states. The spin-parity, spectroscopic factor (or single-particle strength), and effective single-particle energy can all be extracted from such reactions. These observables are often useful to explain the nature of shell evolution, and to constrain, check, and test the parameters used in nuclear structure models. In this article, the experimental studies of the intruder components in low-lying states of neutron-rich nuclei of He, Li, Be, B, and C isotopes using various single-nucleon transfer reactions are reviewed. The focus is laid on the precise determination of the intruder s-wave strength in low-lying states.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that single-nucleon transfer reactions in inverse kinematics are a sensitive quantitative tool for extracting intruder s-wave components in low-lying states of light neutron-rich nuclei near N=8, but emphasizes that only normalized or relative spectroscopic factors (not directly extracted ones) can be reliably compared across experiments due to sensitivity to optical model potentials and experimental conditions.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental studies of single-nucleon transfer reactions (primarily (d,p), (d,t), and (d,3He)) performed in inverse kinematics on neutron-rich He, Li, Be, B, and C isotopes. The missing mass method is used, where only the energies and angles of recoil light particles are measured. Spectroscopic factors are extracted by comparing experimental differential cross sections to Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) or adiabatic wave approximation (ADWA) calculations, and are then normalized using sum-rule methods to obtain the intensity (percentage) of intruder s-wave components.

## Key numbers

- The ground state of 11Be is dominated (~71%) by an intruder 2s1/2 neutron coupled to an inert 10Be(0+) core, giving it an unusual spin-parity of 1/2+
- For the 14B nucleus, the effective single-particle energy for the 2s1/2 orbital is about 0.5 ± 0.1 MeV and for the 1d5/2 orbital is about 2.0 ± 0.4 MeV, demonstrating that the s-orbital lies lower than the d-orbital
- In the 13B(d,p)14B reaction, the hole numbers determined from sum rules are 1.9 ± 0.2 for the 2s1/2 orbital and 5.9 ± 0.3 for the 1d5/2 orbital, close to the independent-particle model predictions of 2.0 and 6.0
- A consistent quenching factor of about 0.55 (with root-mean-square spread of 0.10) was reported for a large number of nuclei in transfer reactions (Kay, 2013)
- For the 13C(d,p)14C reaction in normal kinematics at 17.7 MeV, an average energy resolution of about 60 keV was achieved, clearly identifying three closely spaced excited states at Ex = 6.73, 6.90, and 7.34 MeV

## Context

This review extends the body of work on shell evolution in unstable nuclei, particularly the phenomenon of s-wave intrusion near N=8 that was established by studies of the halo nucleus 11Be (Schmitt; Aumann) and earlier work on the disappearance of traditional magic numbers (Otsuka, 2001). The paper synthesizes results from multiple experiments on isotopes of He, Li, Be, B, and C, and provides a detailed comparison of experimental setups worldwide. It enables future systematic studies of intruder components by clarifying the proper methodology for extracting and normalizing spectroscopic factors, and by documenting the challenges of inverse kinematics experiments such as low-energy light particle detection and target thickness optimization.
