# Neutron Star Properties and Femtoscopic Constraints

> We construct the equation of state of hypernuclear matter and study the structure of neutron stars employing a chiral hyperon-nucleon interaction of the Jülich--Bonn group tuned to femtoscopic $Λp$ data of the ALICE Collaboration, and $ΛΛ$ and $Ξ$N interactions determined from lattice QCD calculations by the HAL QCD Collaboration that reproduce the femtoscopic $ΛΛ$ and $Ξ^-p$ data. We employ the ab-initio microscopic Brueckner--Hartree--Fock theory extended to the strange baryon sector. A special focus is put on the uncertainties of the hyperon interactions and how they are effectively propagated to the composition, equation of state, mass-radius relation and tidal deformability of neutron stars. To such end, we consider the uncertainty due to the experimental error of the femtoscopic $Λp$ data used to fix the chiral hyperon-nucleon interaction and the theoretical uncertainty, estimated from the residual cut-off dependence of this interaction. We find that the final maximum mass of a neutron star with hyperons is in the range $1.3-1.4$ $M_\odot$, in agreement with previous works. The hyperon puzzle, therefore, remains still an open issue if only two-body hyperon-nucleon and hyperon-hyperon interactions are considered. Predictions for the tidal deformability of neutron stars with hyperons are found to be in agreement with the observational constraints from the gravitational wave event GW170817 in the mass range $1.1-1.3$ $M_\odot$.

## Key claim

The maximum mass of a neutron star containing hyperons is found to be in the range 1.3–1.4 solar masses, which is incompatible with the observed ~2 solar mass pulsars, meaning the hyperon puzzle remains unresolved when only two-body hyperon-nucleon and hyperon-hyperon interactions are considered. The authors explicitly state that three-body forces involving hyperons may be required to resolve this discrepancy.

## Method

The authors constructed the equation of state of hypernuclear matter using the ab-initio microscopic Brueckner–Hartree–Fock theory extended to the strange baryon sector, employing a chiral hyperon-nucleon interaction (NLO19 from the Jülich–Bonn group) tuned to femtoscopic Λp data from ALICE, and ΛΛ and ΞN interactions from HAL QCD lattice simulations that reproduce femtoscopic ΛΛ and Ξ⁻p data. They propagated two sources of uncertainty: the experimental error of the femtoscopic Λp data and the theoretical uncertainty estimated from the residual cutoff dependence of the chiral interaction. The Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff equations were solved to obtain neutron star mass-radius relations and tidal deformabilities.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of a neutron star with hyperons: 1.3–1.4 M⊙ (solar masses)
- Tidal deformability predictions agree with GW170817 constraints in the mass range 1.1–1.3 M⊙
- Nuclear saturation density ρ₀ = 0.17 fm⁻³
- Two values of nuclear incompressibility considered: K₀ = 160 MeV (soft) and K₀ = 270 MeV (stiff)
- Binding energy of symmetric matter at saturation: E₀ = -16 MeV
- Observed masses of millisecond pulsars: PSR J1614-2230 (1.928 ± 0.017 M⊙), PSR J0348+0432 (2.01 ± 0.04 M⊙), PSR J0740+6620 (2.14⁺⁰·¹⁰₋₀·₀₉ M⊙)
- Singlet ΛN scattering length f₀ = 2.50 fm (variants I) and f₀ = 2.91 fm (NLO19(600))
- Triplet ΛN scattering length f₁ ranges from 1.32 fm to 1.55 fm (variants I)
- ΛΛ scattering length: 0.99(0.30)(⁺⁰·¹⁷₋₀·₀₀) fm
- Ξ⁻p singlet scattering length: 1.25(0.03)(⁺⁰·⁰⁰₋₀·₁₂) + i2.00(0.40)(⁺⁰·³¹₋₀·₁₆) fm

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Schulze et al. (2006, 2011) that used Nijmegen meson-exchange YN and YY interactions, now employing interactions constrained by modern femtoscopic data from the ALICE Collaboration and lattice QCD from the HAL QCD Collaboration. The results confirm that the hyperon puzzle, the softening of the dense matter equation of state by hyperons leading to maximum masses below ~2 M⊙, persists when only two-body interactions are considered. This enables future work to focus on the role of three-body hyperon-nucleon-nucleon, hyperon-hyperon-nucleon, and hyperon-hyperon-hyperon forces as the likely resolution to the puzzle.
