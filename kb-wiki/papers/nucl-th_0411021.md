# Dark Matter in the Cosmos - Its Direct Detection and the Role Of Nuclear Physics

> Exotic dark matter together with dark energy or cosmological constant seem to dominate in the Universe. An even higher density of such matter seems to be gravitationally trapped in our Galaxy. The nature of dark matter can be unveiled only, if it is detected in the laboratory. Thus the accomplishment of this task is central to physics and cosmology. Current fashionable supersymmetric models provide a natural dark matter candidate, which is the lightest supersymmetric particle (LSP). Since the LSP is much heavier than the proton, while its average energy is in the keV region, the most likely possibility for its direct detection is via its elastic scattering with a nuclear target. In order to evaluate the event rate one needs the nuclear structure (form factor and/or spin response function) for the special nuclear targets of experimental interest. Since the expected rates for neutralino-nucleus scattering are expected to be small, one should exploit all the characteristic signatures of this reaction. Such are: (i) In the standard recoil measurements the modulation of the event rate due to the Earth's motion. (ii) In directional recoil experiments the correlation of the event rate with the sun's motion. One now has both modulation, which is much larger and depends not only on time, but on the direction of observation as well, and a large forward-backward asymmetry. (iii) In non recoil experiments gamma rays following the decay of excited states populated during the Nucleus-LSP collision. Branching ratios of about 6 percent are possible. (iv) novel experiments in which one observes the electrons prodused during the collisionof the LSP with the nucleus. Branching ratios of about 10 per cent are possible.

## Key claim

Direct detection of dark matter via neutralino-nucleus elastic scattering is possible, but the expected event rates are very small; therefore, all characteristic signatures must be exploited, including modulation due to Earth's motion, directional correlations with the Sun's motion, gamma rays from excited nuclear states (branching ratios of about 6 percent), and ionization electrons produced during the collision (branching ratios of about 10 percent).

## Method

The author computes differential event rates for neutralino-nucleus scattering by folding the LSP velocity distribution (Maxwellian with escape velocity 2.84 times the Sun's velocity of 229 km/s) with nuclear structure input: the nuclear form factor F(u) for coherent (scalar) scattering and the spin response function F₁₁(u) for spin-dependent scattering. The total non-directional and directional rates are obtained by integrating over the energy transfer from a detector-dependent minimum Q_min to a maximum set by the escape velocity, using static spin matrix elements from the literature for targets including ¹⁹F, ²⁹Si, ²³Na, ¹²⁷I, and ²⁰⁷Pb.

## Key numbers

- Cosmological densities: Ω_b = 0.05, Ω_CDM = 0.30, Ω_Λ = 0.65, with total Ω = 1.11 ± 0.07
- LSP density in the solar vicinity: ρ(0) = 0.3 GeV/cm³
- Average LSP kinetic energy: ⟨T⟩ ≈ 40 keV × (m_χ / 100 GeV)
- Hubble constant: H₀ = (65 ± 15) km/s/Mpc, or H₀ = 100 h km/s/Mpc with 0.5 < h < 0.8
- Critical density: ρ_c = 1.8 × 10⁻²³ h² g/cm³ = 10 h² nucleons/m³
- Escape velocity: v_esc = 2.84 v₀, with v₀ = 229 km/s (Sun's velocity around the galaxy)
- Coherent cross-section limit: σ_S ≤ 10⁻⁵ pb (present experimental limit)
- For ¹²⁷I, the ratio σ^{spin}_{p,χ⁰} / σ^{S}_{p,χ⁰} ≈ 10⁴; for ¹⁹F, ≈ 10²
- Modulation amplitude h in non-directional experiments is less than 2 percent
- Directional asymmetry in the Sun's motion direction: ≈ 0.97
- For ¹⁹F with Q_min = 0: c_coh(19) ranges from 2080 (m_χ = 20 GeV) to 6360 (m_χ = 200 GeV); c_spin(19) ranges from 5.7 to 16.7
- For ¹²⁷I with Q_min = 0: c_coh(127) ranges from 37294 (m_χ = 20 GeV) to 162945 (m_χ = 200 GeV); c_spin(127) ranges from 2.2 to 10.4

## Context

This paper reviews the role of nuclear physics in direct dark matter detection, extending the framework developed by DFS86, Verg, and COLLAR92 for event rate modulation due to Earth's motion, and by DRIFT for directional experiments. It builds on supersymmetric models providing the lightest supersymmetric particle (LSP) as a dark matter candidate, and on nuclear structure calculations from Ress and Dean, DIVARI, the Finnish group (Suhonen et al.), and the Ioannina team for spin matrix elements. The work enables experimental strategies that exploit multiple signatures (modulation, directionality, excited-state gamma rays, and ionization electrons) to discriminate dark matter signals from background, and highlights the tension between the DAMA modulation claim and null results from EDELWEISS and CDMS.
