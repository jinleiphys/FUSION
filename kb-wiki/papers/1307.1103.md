# Impact of Rotation on the Structure and Composition of Neutron Stars

> Depending on mass and rotational frequency, gravity compresses the matter in the core regions of neutron stars to densities that are several times higher than the density of ordinary atomic nuclei. At such huge densities atoms themselves collapse, and atomic nuclei are squeezed so tightly together that new particle states may appear and novel states of matter, foremost quark matter, may be created. This feature makes neutron stars superb astrophysical laboratories for a wide range of physical studies. And with observational data accumulating rapidly from both orbiting and ground based observatories spanning the spectrum from X-rays to radio wavelengths, there has never been a more exiting time than today to study neutron stars. The Hubble Space Telescope and X-ray satellites such as Chandra and XMM-Newton in particular have proven especially valuable. New astrophysical instruments such as the Five hundred meter Aperture Spherical Telescope (FAST), the square kilometer Array (skA), Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope (formerly GLAST), and possibly the International X-ray Observatory (now Advanced Telescope for High ENergy Astrophysics, ATHENA) promise the discovery of tens of thousands of new non-rotating and rotating neutron stars. The latter are referred to as pulsars. This paper provides a short overview of the impact of rotation on the structure and composition of neutron stars. Observational properties, which may help to shed light on the core composition of neutron stars--and, hence, the properties of ultra-dense matter--are discussed.

## Key claim

Rotation can drive significant changes in the core composition of neutron stars, potentially enabling or disabling particle processes such as hyperon production and the direct Urca process, but these effects depend on the star's mass, rotational frequency, and the still-uncertain equation of state of dense matter.

## Method

The authors model rotating neutron stars using general relativistic structure equations based on a stationary axisymmetric metric, solving Einstein's field equations for the metric functions and frame dragging frequency. The hadronic phase is described via relativistic mean-field theory with nonlinear sigma-meson self-interactions, while quark matter is modeled using a nonlocal SU(3) Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with a vector coupling treated as a free parameter. The mixed quark-hadron phase is determined using the Gibbs conditions for phase equilibrium with global conservation of baryon number and electric charge.

## Key numbers

- Kepler period for typical neutron star matter equations of state for 1.4 solar mass stars is around 1 ms
- Strange quark matter stars can withstand mass shedding down to periods of around 0.5 ms
- Mass increase of up to approximately 20% is typical for rotation at the Kepler frequency
- Density change in the core of a neutron star whose frequency varies from 0 to the Kepler frequency can be as large as 60%
- The most rapidly rotating known neutron star, PSR J1748-2446ad, rotates at a period of 1.39 ms (719 Hz)
- Mixed phases range from 3.2 to 8.2 times normal nuclear matter density (rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3) for G_V/G_s = 0, from 3.8 to 8.5 rho_0 for G_V/G_s = 0.05, and from 4.5 to 8.9 rho_0 for G_V/G_s = 0.1
- Maximum neutron star masses increase from 1.87 solar masses for G_V = 0, to 2.00 solar masses for G_V = 0.05 G_s, to 2.07 solar masses for G_V = 0.1 G_s
- The direct Urca process becomes operative if the proton fraction exceeds a critical limit of around 11% to 15%
- Rotation-driven direct Urca could explain the temperature evolution of the neutron star in Cas A if its mass lies between 1.5 and 1.9 solar masses and its birth frequency was between 40% (400 Hz) and 70% (800 Hz) of the Kepler frequency

## Context

This paper provides a short overview extending prior work on neutron star structure and composition by Weber (1999), Glendenning (1997), and others, with specific focus on how rotation modifies core composition and cooling behavior. The results enable future observational tests using rapidly accumulating data from Chandra, XMM-Newton, and upcoming facilities like FAST and SKA, particularly through measurements of pulsar braking indices and thermal evolution that could reveal the presence of quark matter or rotation-driven particle processes such as the direct Urca process.
