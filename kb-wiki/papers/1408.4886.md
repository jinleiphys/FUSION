# Study of Gamow-Teller transitions in isotopes of titanium within the quasi particle random phase approximation

> The Gamow-Teller (GT) transition is inarguably one of the most important nuclear weak transitions of the spin-isosopin $στ$ type. It has many applications in nuclear and astrophysics. These include, but are not limited to, r-process $β$-decays, stellar electron captures, neutrino cooling rates, neutrino absorption and inelastic scattering on nuclei. The quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) is an efficient way to generate GT strength distribution. In order to better understand both theoretical systematics and uncertainties, we compare the GT strength distributions, centroid and width calculations for $^{40-60}$Ti isotopes, using the pn-QRPA, Pyatov method (PM) and the Schematic model (SM). The pn-QRPA and SM are further sub-divided into three categories in order to highlight the role of particle-particle (pp) force and deformation of the nucleus in the GT strength calculations. In PM, we study only the influence of the pp force in the calculation. We also compare with experimental results and other calculations where available. We found that the inclusion of pp force and deformation significantly improves the performance of SM and pn-QRPA models. Incorporation of pp force leads to pinning down the centroid value in the PM. The calculated GT strength functions using the pn-QRPA (C) and SM (C) models are in reasonable agreement with measured data.

## Key claim

The paper compares Gamow-Teller (GT) strength distributions, centroids, and widths for titanium isotopes (mass 40-60) using three QRPA-based models (pn-QRPA, Pyatov Method, and Schematic Model), finding that inclusion of both particle-particle force and nuclear deformation significantly improves agreement with measured data, but the authors note that the Pyatov Method and Schematic Model generally produce larger total strengths and higher centroids than the pn-QRPA model, and that deformation effects were not yet incorporated into the Pyatov Method.

## Method

The authors calculated GT strength distributions for 21 titanium isotopes (40-60Ti) using three theoretical approaches: the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA), the Pyatov Method (PM), and the Schematic Model (SM). The pn-QRPA and SM were each subdivided into three categories: Model (A) with only particle-hole interaction in spherical nuclei, Model (B) adding particle-particle interaction, and Model (C) incorporating both particle-particle interaction and nuclear deformation. The PM was subdivided into only two categories (A and B), as deformation incorporation was left for future work. Single-particle energies and wave functions were calculated using the Nilsson model, pairing was treated in the BCS approximation, and GT strengths were quenched by a universal factor of (0.6)^2 for fp-shell nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Total GT strengths, centroids, and widths were calculated for 21 titanium isotopes (40-60Ti) using eight different model variants
- The re-normalized Ikeda sum rule in pn-QRPA is given by ISR_renorm = S^(-) - S^(+) ≈ 3 × (0.6)^2 × (N - Z), where the quenching factor f_q^2 = (0.6)^2
- For 40Ti, PM and SM models gave much bigger total GT strength values in the electron capture direction compared to pn-QRPA, with centroids placed at higher excitation energies
- For 42Ti, PM (B) brought down calculated centroid values roughly by a factor of three compared to PM (A)
- For 41Ti, SM (C) calculated the lowest centroid and biggest strengths among all models
- Total strength calculations were performed up to 20 MeV in pn-QRPA and up to 40 MeV in PM and SM models
- Experimentally adopted deformation parameters for 42,44,46,48,50Ti were taken from Raman et al. (1987); for other isotopes, deformation was calculated using δ = 125(Q_2) / [1.44 × Z × A^(2/3)] with quadrupole moments from Möller and Nix (1981)

## Context

This work extends previous pn-QRPA calculations by Nabi and Klapdor-Kleingrothaus (1999, 2004) for sd- and fp-shell nuclei, and compares three QRPA-based formalisms to assess theoretical systematics and uncertainties in GT strength distributions. The study is motivated by the importance of titanium isotopes in presupernova evolution, as identified by Aufderheide et al. (1994) and Heger et al. (2001), and by applications including 44Ti yield from supernova debris and 40,41Ti beta-decay for solar-neutrino detection. The results enable more reliable estimates of stellar weak interaction rates by identifying which model variants best reproduce measured GT strength distributions, though the authors note that PM and SM have not yet been used to calculate excited-state GT strength functions, which are needed for complete stellar rate calculations.
