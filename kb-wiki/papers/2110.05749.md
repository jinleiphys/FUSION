# Nuclear Shell Structure in a Finite-Temperature Relativistic Framework

> The shell evolution of neutron-rich nuclei with temperature is studied in a beyond-mean-field framework rooted in the meson-nucleon Lagrangian. The temperature-dependent Dyson equation with the dynamical kernel taking into account the particle-vibration coupling (PVC) is solved for the fermionic propagators in the basis of the thermal relativistic mean-field Dirac spinors. The calculations are performed for $^{68-78}$Ni in a broad range of temperatures $0 \leq T \leq 4$ MeV. The special focus is put on the fragmentation pattern of the single-particle states, which is further investigated within toy models in strongly truncated model spaces. Such models allow for quantifying the sensitivity of the fragmentation to the phonon frequencies, the PVC strength and to the mean-field level density. The model studies provide insights into the temperature evolution of the PVC mechanism in real nuclear systems under the conditions which may occur in astrophysical environments.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the fragmentation pattern of single-particle states in neutron-rich Ni isotopes evolves with temperature due to particle-vibration coupling (PVC), but the dominant fragment near the Fermi energy retains a spectroscopic factor of approximately 0.7-0.9 at all temperatures studied, with the degree of fragmentation varying for states far from the Fermi surface depending on temperature and isotopic composition.

## Method
The authors solved the finite-temperature Dyson equation for fermionic propagators using a dynamical self-energy kernel that incorporates particle-vibration coupling (PVC), built upon a basis of thermal relativistic mean-field (RMF) Dirac spinors. The calculations were performed in three steps: (i) self-consistent solution of the RMF equations with the NL3 parametrization and thermal fermionic occupation numbers, (ii) finite-temperature relativistic random phase approximation (FT-RRPA) to obtain phonon vertices and frequencies, and (iii) solution of the Dyson equation in a truncated configuration space with the PVC self-energy treated in the diagonal approximation. Pairing correlations were included via BCS at T=0 and neglected for T >= 1 MeV.

## Key numbers
- Temperature range studied: 0 <= T <= 4 MeV for nickel isotopes 68-78
- Zero-temperature neutron pairing gaps for 68-76Ni range from 1.30 MeV (76Ni) to 1.57 MeV (68Ni)
- Critical temperatures for superfluidity range from 0.78 MeV (76Ni) to 0.94 MeV (68Ni)
- Phonon model space included vibrations with spin-parities J^pi = 2+, 3-, 4+, 5-, 6+ below an energy cutoff of 20 MeV
- Particle-hole configurations with energies epsilon_ph <= 100 MeV and antiparticle-hole configurations with epsilon_alpha h >= -1800 MeV were included in the FT-RRPA basis
- Single-particle intermediate states were included under the condition |epsilon_k3 - epsilon_k1| <= 50 MeV
- Dominant fragments near the Fermi energy have spectroscopic factors of approximately 0.7-0.9
- Strongly fragmented states have two competing fragments with spectroscopic factors not smaller than 40% of the largest fragment's factor

## Context
This work extends the zero-temperature PVC model of Litvinova and Ring (2006) and Litvinova (2012) to finite temperature, building upon the relativistic quantum hadrodynamics framework of Serot and Walecka (1986) and the covariant density functional theory of Vretenar et al. (2005). The approach is applied to neutron-rich Ni isotopes on the r-process path, which are of interest for astrophysical environments such as neutron star mergers and supernovae as studied by Mumpower et al. (2016) and Langanke (2021). The results enable improved predictions of nuclear reaction rates at finite temperature for astrophysical modeling, particularly for the electron capture sensitivity region identified in prior work.
