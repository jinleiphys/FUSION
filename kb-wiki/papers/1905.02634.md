# Cross sections at sub-Coulomb energies: full optical model vs.\ barrier transmission for $^{40}$Ca + $α$

> Cross sections for $^{40}$Ca + $α$ at low energies have been calculated from two different models and three different $α$-nucleus potentials. The first model determines the cross sections from the barrier transmission in a real nuclear potential. Second, cross sections are derived within the optical model using a complex nuclear potential. The excitation functions from barrier transmission are smooth whereas the excitation functions from the optical model show a significant sensitivity to the chosen imaginary potential. Cross sections far below the Coulomb barrier are lower from barrier transmission than from the optical model. This difference is explained by additional absorption in the tail of the imaginary part of the potential in the optical model. At higher energies the calculations from the two models and all $α$-nucleus potentials converge. Finally, in contradiction to another recent study where a double-folding potential failed in a WKB calculation, the applicability of double-folding potentials for $^{40}$Ca + $α$ at low energies is clearly confirmed in the present analysis for the simple barrier transmission model and for the full optical model calculation.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for the $^{40}$Ca + $\alpha$ system at sub-Coulomb energies, the simpler barrier transmission model (pBTM) produces smooth excitation functions with relatively small uncertainties, whereas the more sophisticated optical model (OM) shows a strong sensitivity to the tail of the imaginary potential at large radii, leading to significantly different cross sections depending on the chosen imaginary potential parameters.

## Method

Total reaction cross sections for $^{40}$Ca + $\alpha$ were calculated using two models: pure barrier transmission (pBTM) with only the real nuclear potential, and the full optical model (OM) with a complex nuclear potential. Three different $\alpha$-nucleus potentials were employed: a squared Woods-Saxon (WS$^2$) potential from Koyuncu and Soylu (2017), the double-folding based ATOMKI-V1 potential, and the standard Woods-Saxon potential by McFadden and Satchler (1966). The pBTM calculations used the CCFULL code, while OM calculations used the code a0, with additional statistical model calculations performed using the TALYS code.

## Key numbers

- The effective Coulomb barrier for $^{40}$Ca + $\alpha$ is located at radii of approximately 8 fm with a height of 6.5 to 6.8 MeV, depending on the chosen nuclear potential.
- At 8 MeV (above the barrier), all calculations converge to a narrow range of 300 mb to 400 mb for the total reaction cross section, with variations of only about 30%.
- At 1 MeV, the OM calculation with the McF potential gives a total reaction cross section about a factor of 3 higher than the pBTM calculation using the same real potential.
- The imaginary part of the McF potential at 7 fm radius has a depth of only -0.35 MeV (about 1.4% of its central value of -25 MeV), yet truncating the imaginary potential at this radius reduces the OM cross section by a factor of three and brings it close to the pBTM result.
- The ATOMKI-V1 potential in the OM shows a dramatic odd-even staggering in partial cross sections at 3 MeV, with only the L=1 partial wave responsible for an enhancement.

## Context

This work extends the study by Koyuncu and Soylu (2017), who used a semi-classical WKB approximation for barrier transmission and claimed that double-folding potentials failed for $^{40}$Ca + $\alpha$ at low energies. The present analysis contradicts that claim by showing that double-folding potentials (ATOMKI-V1) produce similar cross sections to the WS$^2$ potential in the pBTM, and that the earlier failure was likely due to the WKB approximation's sensitivity to turning points. The work also builds on earlier elastic scattering studies by Atzrott (1993) and Ohkubo (1988) and bound state properties of $^{44}$Ti. The finding that OM cross sections far below the Coulomb barrier are dominated by the tail of the imaginary potential at radii beyond 7 fm raises questions about the reliability of OM predictions at very low energies, which is relevant for statistical model calculations of $\alpha$-induced reactions in nuclear astrophysics.
