# Nonlocal extension of the dispersive-optical-model to describe data below the Fermi energy

> Present applications of the dispersive-optical-model analysis are restricted by the use of a local but energy-dependent version of the generalized Hartree-Fock potential. This restriction is lifted by the introduction of a corresponding nonlocal potential without explicit energy dependence. Such a strategy allows for a complete determination of the nucleon propagator below the Fermi energy with access to the expectation value of one-body operators (like the charge density), the one-body density matrix with associated natural orbits, and complete spectral functions for removal strength. The present formulation of the dispersive optical model (DOM) therefore allows the use of elastic electron-scattering data in determining its parameters. Application to ${}^{40}$Ca demonstrates that a fit to the charge radius leads to too much charge near the origin using the conventional assumptions of the functional form of the DOM. A corresponding incomplete description of high-momentum components is identified, suggesting that the DOM formulation must be extended in the future to accommodate such correlations properly. Unlike the local version, the present nonlocal DOM limits the location of the deeply-bound hole states to energies that are consistent with (\textit{e,e}$^{\prime}$\textit{p}) and (\textit{p,2p}) data.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that replacing the local, energy-dependent Hartree-Fock-like potential in the dispersive optical model (DOM) with a nonlocal, energy-independent potential allows for a complete determination of the nucleon propagator below the Fermi energy, but the conventional functional form of the DOM still produces too much charge near the origin in \({}^{40}\)Ca when fitting the charge radius, indicating that high-momentum correlations are not properly described.

## Method

The authors replaced the local, energy-dependent Hartree-Fock-like potential in the DOM with a nonlocal, energy-independent potential using a Gaussian nonlocality form (Perey-type) with four parameters (depth, radius, diffuseness, and nonlocality parameter \(\beta\)). They solved the Dyson equation in coordinate space via complex matrix inversion, keeping all other DOM parameters from previous fits to \({}^{40}\)Ca data (Charity 2007) and applying an effective-mass correction to the imaginary part of the self-energy to maintain proper normalization.

## Key numbers

- The nonlocal potential parameters for \({}^{40}\)Ca: depth = -92.3 MeV, radius = 1.05 fm, diffuseness = 0.70 fm, nonlocality parameter \(\beta\) = 0.91 fm (Table I).
- The nonlocal DOM places the \(0s_{1/2}\) proton quasihole peak at -46.7 MeV, compared to -57.3 MeV for the local DOM and the experimental value of approximately -47 MeV (Table II).
- The spectroscopic factor for the \(1s_{1/2}\) proton orbit is 0.65 in the nonlocal DOM, compared to 0.67 in the local DOM (Table III).
- The summed proton strength for all orbits up to \(\ell=2\) (excluding \(f_{7/2}\)) is 19.48, but including \(\ell=3\) orbits (\(f_{7/2}\) and \(f_{5/2}\)) gives 21.43 total protons, overestimating the actual number for \({}^{40}\)Ca.

## Context

This work extends the dispersive optical model framework developed by Mahaux and Sartor (1991) by removing the restriction of a local, energy-dependent Hartree-Fock-like potential, which had been used in all previous DOM implementations including those for Ca isotopes by Charity (2006, 2007). The nonlocal formulation enables the use of elastic electron-scattering data (charge density) and spectral functions from \((e,e'p)\) and \((p,2p)\) reactions as direct constraints, and it corrects the overbinding of deeply-bound hole states that plagued the local version. The results identify the need for future DOM extensions to incorporate short-range correlations that generate high-momentum components, as observed experimentally by Rohe (2004) and predicted by microscopic calculations of Muther (1994, 1995).
