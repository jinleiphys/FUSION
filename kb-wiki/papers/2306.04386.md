# Framework for Multi-messenger Inference from Neutron Stars: Combining Nuclear Theory Priors

> We construct an efficient parameterization of the pure neutron-matter equation of state (EoS) that incorporates the uncertainties from both chiral effective field theory ($χ$EFT) and phenomenological potential calculations. This parameterization yields a family of EoSs including and extending the forms based purely on these two calculations. In combination with an agnostic inner core EoS, this parameterization is used in a Bayesian inference pipeline to obtain constraints on the e os parameters using multi-messenger observations of neutron stars. We specifically considered observations of the massive pulsar J0740+6620, the binary neutron star coalescence GW170817, and the NICER pulsar J0030+0451. Constraints on neutron star mass-radius relations are obtained and compared. The Bayes factors for the different EoS models are also computed. While current constraints do not reveal any significant preference among these models, the framework developed here may enable future observations with more sensitive detectors to discriminate them.

## Key claim

The paper constructs a parameterized pure neutron-matter equation of state that smoothly interpolates between chiral effective field theory and phenomenological potential calculations using a single translation parameter, but current multi-messenger observations do not have sufficient statistical power to discriminate between these nuclear models, as indicated by Bayes factors near unity.

## Method

The authors extended the FRZ equation of state model by fitting fourth-order polynomials to the energy per neutron from both chiral effective field theory data (Drischler 2020) and phenomenological quantum Monte Carlo calculations (Gandolfi 2012), capturing the dominant principal components of the covariance matrix. They combined this parameterization with a three-piece polytropic inner core model and performed Bayesian inference using observations of the pulsar J0740+6620, the binary neutron star merger GW170817, and NICER observations of pulsars J0030+0451 and J0740+6620, sampling a reduced parameter space identified through global sensitivity analysis.

## Key numbers

- The breakdown scale for chiral effective field theory was chosen as 600 MeV with a momentum cutoff of 500 MeV
- The parameter $\kappa$ in the energy per neutron expression was fixed to 25.5 MeV
- The two dominant eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for the chiral EFT residual energy account for more than 95% of the uncertainties
- The polynomial order for fitting $\Eres_0^{\chi}$, $\Eres_1^{\chi}$, and $\Eres_2^{\chi}$ was found to be 4, which is the minimum order satisfying the requirement that maximum variance in fitting error is less than $10^{-4}$
- The Bayes factor between $\FRZ{\chi}{1.5}$ and $\FRZ{\phi}{1.5}$ models is approximately 1, indicating both extreme points are equally favored
- The Bayesian Information Criterion for the $\FRZ{\zeta}{1.5}$ model is consistently higher by approximately 10 (the value of $\ln n_{\text{samp}}$) compared to the $\FRZ{\chi}{1.5}$ and $\FRZ{\phi}{1.5}$ models, demonstrating Occam's penalty for the extra parameter
- The inner core parameters, outer core parameters ($a_1$ and $a_2$), and $u_p$ have total effect indices with respect to maximum mass, radius, and tidal deformability that are at least 2 orders of magnitude higher than the remaining parameters

## Context

This work extends the FRZ equation of state model of Forbes (2019) by incorporating both chiral effective field theory results (Drischler 2020) and phenomenological quantum Monte Carlo calculations (Gandolfi 2012) into a unified parameterization, and replaces the previous quadratic speed-of-sound inner core model with a piecewise polytrope parameterization following Raaijmakers (2020) and Read (2009). The framework enables future multi-messenger observations with more sensitive detectors to potentially discriminate between nuclear models that current data cannot distinguish, and provides a general procedure for comparing data from different simulations in light of microscopic or macroscopic observations.
