# Magnetic Effect on Potential Barrier for Nucleosynthesis II

> We investigate the impact of magnetic fields on the potential barrier between two interacting nuclei. We addressed this by solving the Boltzmann equation and Maxwell's theory in the presence of a magnetic field, resulting in the determination of magnetized permittivity. Additionally, we derived the magnetized Debye potential, which combines the conventional Debye potential with an additional magnetic component. We then compared the Boltzmann approach with the Debye method. Both methods consistently demonstrate that magnetic fields increase permittivity. This enhanced permittivity leads to a reduction in the potential barrier, consequently increasing the reaction rate for nucleosynthesis. Furthermore, the dependence on temperature and electron density in each approach is consistent. Our findings suggest that magnetized plasmas, which have existed since the Big Bang, have played a crucial role in nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

The paper claims that magnetic fields increase the permittivity of the plasma, which reduces the Coulomb potential barrier between fusing nuclei and thereby increases nucleosynthesis reaction rates; however, this effect is most pronounced for weak magnetic fields (around $4\times 10^{-9}$ G) because stronger magnetic fields suppress the perturbation of electrons due to their strong constraining effect, causing the magnetized case to converge back toward the non-magnetized case.

## Method

The authors solved the Vlasov (Boltzmann) equation in the presence of a background magnetic field, combined with Maxwell's theory, to derive the magnetized longitudinal permittivity $\epsilon_l(k,\omega,B)$ using an anisotropic Maxwell distribution function and Bessel function expansions. They also derived a magnetized Debye potential by adding a magnetic-field-dependent component to the conventional Debye potential, and compared the two approaches (Boltzmann kinetic method versus Debye method) for consistency across varying temperature, electron density, and magnetic field strength.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic fields stronger than $4\times 10^{-9}$ G render certain terms in the permittivity expression negligible due to the cyclotron radius scaling as $r \sim 1/B$.
- For the Solar core (temperature $T \sim 10^7$ K), the reaction rate for deuterium synthesis in the pp chain is suppressed by a factor of $1.53\times 10^{-7}$; for the early Universe ($T \sim 10^{10}$ K), the suppression factor is $0.21$.
- For the subsequent $^3$He step, the suppression factors are $1.24\times 10^{-12}$ (Solar core) and $0.064$ (early Universe).
- The validity condition for the Debye potential is given as $\Phi \ll k_B T/e \sim 2\times 10^{-3} - 2\times 10^2$ (units implied as eV or equivalent).
- Example parameters used in numerical comparisons: $B = 4\times 10^{-9}$ G, $T = 2.38\times 10^6$ K, $n_{0e} = 8.18\times 10^{13}$ m$^{-3}$.

## Context

This work extends the classic electron screening model of Salpeter (1954) and the Debye-Huckel screening framework to include the effect of background magnetic fields, which were previously neglected in studies of nucleosynthesis reaction rates. It contrasts with prior unmagnetized plasma treatments by Bahcall et al. (1998), Gruzinov et al. (1998), and Dewitt et al. (1973), and addresses the dynamic screening criticisms raised by Shaviv et al. (1996) and Carraro et al. (1988). The results enable future studies of primordial nucleosynthesis and stellar fusion to incorporate the ubiquitous weak magnetic fields present since the Big Bang, potentially revising reaction rate calculations for the pp chain and other fusion processes.
