# Tensor correlations in the Unitary Correlation Operator Method

> We present a unitary correlation operator that explicitly induces into shell model type many-body states short ranged two-body correlations caused by the strong repulsive core and the pronounced tensor part of the nucleon-nucleon interaction. Alternatively an effective Hamiltonian can be defined by applying this unitary correlator to the realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction. The momentum space representation shows that realistic interactions which differ in their short range behaviour are mapped on the same correlated Hamiltonian, indicating a successful provision for the correlations at high momenta. Calculations for He4 using the one- and two-body part of the correlated Hamiltonian compare favorably with exact many-body methods. For heavier nuclei like O16 and Ca40 where exact many-body calculations are not possible we compare our results with other approximations. The correlated single-particle momentum distributions describe the occupation of states above the Fermi momentum. The Unitary Correlation Operator Method (UCOM) can be used in mean-field and shell model configuration spaces that are not able to describe these repulsive and tensor correlations explicitly.

## Key claim

The Unitary Correlation Operator Method (UCOM) provides a basis-free, analytical way to incorporate short-range radial and tensor correlations into simple shell-model or mean-field many-body states, allowing realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions to be used for ab initio calculations of nuclei up to about A=50 with comparatively little numerical effort, though the method's two-body approximation is best justified when the correlation length is shorter than the mean inter-nucleon distance.

## Method

The authors construct a unitary correlation operator C as a product of a radial correlator Cr and a tensor correlator CΩ, each defined as exponentials of hermitian two-body generators that depend on relative distance, relative momentum, spins, and isospins of nucleon pairs. The radial correlator shifts pairs radially outward to avoid the repulsive core using the radial momentum operator and a distance-dependent shift function s(r), while the tensor correlator aligns the relative coordinate vector with the total spin direction using perpendicular shifts controlled by a strength function ϑ(r). The correlated Hamiltonian is obtained by applying this unitary operator to the realistic interaction, and matrix elements are evaluated in a two-body approximation using a cluster expansion, with the correlated interaction then used in simple Slater-determinant trial states for He4, O16, and Ca40.

## Key numbers

- For He4, O16, and Ca40 with the Bonn-A interaction, the radial correlator reduces the potential energy by about 20-30 MeV per nucleon while increasing kinetic energy by about 5 MeV per nucleon; the tensor correlator adds 15-25 MeV per nucleon of binding with 5-10 MeV per nucleon kinetic energy increase, yielding net binding near -8 MeV per nucleon.
- For the Argonne V18 interaction, radial correlations lower potential energy by about 30-40 MeV per nucleon and tensor correlations give an additional 20-30 MeV per nucleon, with kinetic energy increases of 6-12 MeV (radial) and 8-15 MeV (tensor).
- Despite large differences in potential and kinetic energies between Bonn-A and Argonne V18, the net binding energies obtained after correlations are almost identical for the three nuclei studied.

## Context

This work extends the Unitary Correlation Operator Method previously introduced for central correlations (Neff and Feldmeier, 1998) to include tensor correlations, enabling ab initio calculations with realistic interactions for nuclei beyond the reach of Green's Function Monte Carlo (GFMC) calculations (Pieper et al., 2001) and the no-core shell model (Barrett et al., 2000), which are limited to A=8-10 and A=12 respectively. The results for He4 compare favorably with exact methods, while for O16 and Ca40 the authors compare with Correlated Basis Function (CBF) calculations (Fabrocini et al., 1998, 2000); the correlated interactions in momentum space are also compared with the Vlowk potential (Kuo et al., 2001), showing close agreement despite different derivation schemes. The method enables future inclusion of three-body forces and improved long-range correlations in the trial state.
