# New measurements of the proton's size and structure using polarized photons

> Improved measurements of the proton's structure are now possible thanks to significant technical advances that allow us to probe the proton with polarized photons. These measurements have shown that the proton is not as simple as previously believed: quark orbital angular momentum and relativistic effects play an important role and the spatial distribution of charge and magnetization do not simply mimic the spatial distribution of the quarks. Even more recently, the large scale structure and size of the proton have been examined more carefully, and a significant discrepancy has been observed between the charge radius of the proton as measured in the Lamb shift of muonic hydrogen and measurements using the electron-proton interaction.

## Key claim

Polarization measurements of the proton's electromagnetic form factors reveal that the simple non-relativistic quark model picture, where charge and magnetization distributions are identical, breaks down at all momentum scales; the charge radius extracted from electron-proton scattering (0.8772 ± 0.0046 fm) is 9σ above the muonic hydrogen Lamb shift result (0.8418 ± 0.0007 fm), suggesting either an error in the muonic measurement, missing QED corrections, or a fundamental difference between electron and muon interactions.

## Method

The author reviews elastic electron-proton scattering measurements using both Rosenbluth separation (extracting form factors from the ε-dependence of the reduced cross section at fixed Q²) and polarization transfer techniques (measuring the ratio G_E/G_M using polarized electron beams and proton recoil polarimeters). At low Q², new high-precision polarization data from Jefferson Lab achieve ≈1% total uncertainties on μ_p G_E/G_M for Q² from 0.3–0.7 GeV², combined with new Rosenbluth measurements from Mainz below 1 GeV². At high Q², polarization data show a nearly linear falloff of μ_p G_E/G_M with Q², now attributed to two-photon exchange corrections that affect Rosenbluth extractions but not polarization measurements.

## Key numbers

- Proton charge radius from combined electron scattering data: 0.8772 ± 0.0046 fm (9σ above muonic hydrogen result)
- Proton charge radius from muonic hydrogen Lamb shift (Pohl 2010): 0.8418 ± 0.0007 fm
- Proton charge radius from Mainz Rosenbluth extraction (Bernauer 2010): 0.879 ± 0.008 fm
- Proton charge radius from combined polarization + cross section data (Zhan 2011): 0.875 ± 0.010 fm
- CODATA 2010 value (mainly from hydrogen Lamb shift): 0.8775 ± 0.0051 fm
- Neutron charge form factor measured up to Q² = 3.4 GeV² (Riordan 2010)
- Up quark form factors appear to fall as Q⁻⁴ above Q² = 1.5 GeV², while down quarks decrease as Q⁻²
- New low-Q² polarization data achieve ≈1% total uncertainties on μ_p G_E/G_M for Q² from 0.3–0.7 GeV², compared to typical 3–5% from earlier cross section measurements

## Context

This paper reviews the revolution in proton structure studies enabled by polarized photon techniques at Jefferson Lab, following pioneering work at SLAC, BATES, and Mainz. The high-Q² polarization measurements (Punjabi 2005, Gayou 2002, Puckett 2010) contradicted decades of Rosenbluth cross section measurements (Arrington 2003) that showed μ_p G_E/G_M ≈ 1, now explained by two-photon exchange contributions (Guichon 2003, Arrington 2004). The low-Q² measurements extend the work of Fried (2011) on pion cloud effects and the BLAST collaboration, while the proton radius discrepancy with muonic hydrogen (Pohl 2010) opens questions about possible missing QED corrections or new physics in lepton universality. Future work includes the JLab 12 GeV upgrade (Dudek 2012) and definitive tests of two-photon exchange using positron-electron comparisons (Arrington 2009).
