# Reactor Neutrino Spectra

> We present a review of the antineutrino spectra emitted from reactors. Knowledge of these and their associated uncertainties are crucial for neutrino oscillation studies. The spectra used to-date have been determined by either conversion of measured electron spectra to antineutrino spectra or by summing over all of the thousands of transitions that makeup the spectra using modern databases as input. The uncertainties in the subdominant corrections to beta-decay plague both methods, and we provide estimates of these uncertainties. Improving on current knowledge of the antineutrino spectra from reactors will require new experiments. Such experiments would also address the so-called reactor neutrino anomaly and the possible origin of the shoulder observed in the antineutrino spectra measured in recent high-statistics reactor neutrino experiments.

## Key claim

This review argues that the uncertainty in reactor antineutrino spectra is dominated by subdominant corrections to beta-decay (finite nuclear size, weak magnetism, and radiative corrections), and that the authors' subjective estimate of about 5% uncertainty for the conversion method and up to 20% for the summation method does not represent statistical variances but rather educated guesses.

## Method

The authors review the two primary methods for determining reactor antineutrino spectra: conversion of measured aggregate electron fission spectra from the ILL experiments into antineutrino spectra, and summation over thousands of individual beta-decay branches using modern nuclear databases such as ENDF/B-VII.1. They examine the theoretical corrections applied in both methods, including the Fermi function, finite nuclear size corrections, radiative corrections, and weak magnetism corrections, comparing different formulations from Holstein, Behrens, Wilkinson, and Vogel.

## Key numbers

- The reactor neutrino anomaly represents a 6% discrepancy between expected and observed number of detected antineutrinos in all short baseline experiments
- For the Daya Bay experiment alone, the ratio of measured to expected yield is 0.946 plus or minus 0.022
- A global fit including all past measurements gives ratio R = 0.942 plus or minus 0.009 (experimental) plus or minus 0.025 (model)
- The uncertainty in antineutrino spectra derived by the conversion method is estimated at about 5%
- The uncertainties in the summation method are estimated at up to the 20% level in the upper part of the antineutrino spectrum
- The weak magnetism correction is approximately 0.5% per MeV of electron energy
- The finite size correction formula for uniform densities gives delta_FS = -(8/5)(Z alpha R E_e)/(hbar c) times (1 + (9/28)(m_e^2 c^4)/(E_e^2))
- The sterile neutrino mass squared difference is restricted to the interval 0.8 to 2.2 eV^2, with mixing angle sin^2(2theta) approximately 0.1 with a range of 0.06 to 0.22

## Context

This review extends the work of Mueller (2011) and Huber (2011) who provided the current standard predictions for reactor antineutrino spectra, and addresses the reactor neutrino anomaly first identified by Mention (2011). The paper enables improved interpretation of short-baseline reactor experiments (PROSPECT, SoLid, STEREO, DANSS, NEUTRINO-4) that aim to test the sterile neutrino hypothesis, and highlights the need for new measurements of the 238U spectrum to understand the 4-6 MeV shoulder observed by Daya Bay, RENO, and Double Chooz.
