# The Equation of State from Observed Masses and Radii of Neutron Stars

> We determine an empirical dense matter equation of state from a heterogeneous dataset of six neutron stars: three type I X-ray bursters with photospheric radius expansion, studied by Ozel et al., and three transient low-mass X-ray binaries. We critically assess the mass and radius determinations from the X-ray burst sources and show explicitly how systematic uncertainties, such as the photospheric radius at touchdown, affect the most probable masses and radii. We introduce a parameterized equation of state and use a Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithm within a Bayesian framework to determine nuclear parameters such as the incompressibility and the density dependence of the bulk symmetry energy. Using this framework we show, for the first time, that these parameters, predicted solely on the basis of astrophysical observations, all lie in ranges expected from nuclear systematics and laboratory experiments. We find significant constraints on the mass-radius relation for neutron stars, and hence on the pressure-density relation of dense matter. The predicted symmetry energy and the equation of state near the saturation density are soft, resulting in relatively small neutron star radii around 11-12 km for M=1.4 Msun. The predicted equation of state stiffens at higher densities, however, and our preferred model for X-ray bursts suggests that the neutron star maximum mass is relatively large, 1.9-2.2 Msun. Our results imply that several commonly used equations of state are inconsistent with observations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that astrophysical observations of neutron star masses and radii can, for the first time, constrain nuclear parameters such as incompressibility and symmetry energy to ranges consistent with laboratory experiments, but only if the assumption that the photospheric radius equals the stellar radius at touchdown during X-ray bursts is relaxed; under the preferred model where the photosphere is still extended at touchdown, the inferred neutron star radii are moderately larger and confidence intervals substantially wider than previously claimed.

## Method

The authors performed a Bayesian analysis using a Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithm with a parameterized equation of state, fitting mass-radius constraints from six neutron stars: three type I X-ray bursters with photospheric radius expansion (EXO 1745-248, 4U 1608-522, and 4U 1820-30) and three quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries. They critically reassessed the X-ray burst data by testing two interpretations of the photospheric radius at touchdown (r_ph = R versus r_ph >> R) and found that the assumption r_ph = R leads to unphysical solutions for the most probable observables, with fewer than 4% of Monte Carlo realizations accepted.

## Key numbers

- For the preferred model (r_ph >> R), the most probable neutron star radius for a 1.4 solar mass star is 11-12 km
- The predicted maximum neutron star mass is 1.9-2.2 solar masses under the preferred X-ray burst model
- For EXO 1745-248, the most probable value of alpha (a derived parameter combining observables) was 0.165^{+0.045}_{-0.028} when alpha was unrestricted, and 0.165^{+0.026}_{-0.018} under the r_ph >> R assumption with the alpha < 0.192 restriction
- For 4U 1608-522, the distance was 5.8 +/- 2.0 kpc, with the distance distribution cut off below 3.9 kpc
- For 4U 1820-30, the distance was 8.2 +/- 0.7 kpc, approximated by a boxcar with halfwidth 1.4 kpc
- The color correction factor f_c was selected from a boxcar distribution centered at 1.4 with uncertainty 0.07
- The hydrogen mass fraction X was assumed uniform in the range 0 < X < 0.7 for EXO 1745-248 and 4U 1608-522, while X = 0 for 4U 1820-30

## Context

This work extends the analyses of Ozel (2006, 2009) and Guver (2010, 2010b) by critically reassessing their assumption that the photospheric radius equals the stellar radius at touchdown, and by introducing a Bayesian framework to combine heterogeneous data from multiple neutron star sources. The results contradict several commonly used equations of state and enable future joint analyses of astrophysical observations with laboratory nuclear physics measurements, such as heavy-ion collisions and parity-violating electron scattering, to further constrain the dense matter equation of state.
