# Time-dependent HF approach to SHE dynamics

> We employ the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method to study various aspects of the reactions utilized in searches for superheavy elements. These include capture cross-sections, quasifission, prediction of $P_{\mathrm{CN}}$, and other interesting dynamical quantities. We show that the microscopic TDHF approach provides an important tool to shed some light on the nuclear dynamics leading to the formation of superheavy elements.

## Key claim

The authors show that the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method, while deterministic and limited to a single Slater determinant, can provide a fully microscopic description of capture cross-sections, quasifission dynamics, and ingredients for the compound-nucleus formation probability \(P_{\mathrm{CN}}\) in superheavy element reactions, with no adjustable parameters beyond the Skyrme effective interaction.

## Method

The authors performed TDHF calculations on a 3D Cartesian grid without symmetry restrictions, using the Skyrme SLy4d interaction including all relevant time-odd terms. For capture cross-sections, they employed the density-constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) method to compute ion-ion potentials and then obtained cross-sections via the Incoming Wave Boundary Condition method, including angle-averaging over orientations of the deformed \({}^{238}\)U nucleus using a semiclassical Coulomb excitation code. For quasifission studies, they evolved the TDHF equations for \({}^{40}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U and \({}^{48}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U systems at various center-of-mass energies and impact parameters, extracting contact times, fragment masses and charges, excitation energies, and total kinetic energies directly from the time evolution.

## Key numbers

- For \({}^{40}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U central collisions, contact times in the quasifission energy region \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=208-220\) MeV vary between 21.7 and 32.2 zs (1 zs = \(10^{-21}\) s), which is 16–24 times larger than the contact time at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=200\) MeV.
- For \({}^{40}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=208\) MeV, the light fragment mass and charge are \(A_{\mathrm{L}}=98.7\) and \(Z_{\mathrm{L}}=40.5\); at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=220\) MeV, \(A_{\mathrm{L}}=107.2\) and \(Z_{\mathrm{L}}=43.4\).
- For \({}^{48}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U central collisions, the quasifission region is confined to a narrow energy window \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=202-203.5\) MeV, with contact times between 12.1 and 19.7 zs and light fragment mass range \(A_{\mathrm{L}}=78.7-102.4\).
- Excitation energies in the quasifission region reach up to 60 MeV for the heavy fragment and 40 MeV for the light fragment in \({}^{40}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U, compared to up to 40 MeV for the heavy fragment in \({}^{48}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U.
- For \({}^{40}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=209\) MeV, the contact time decreases from about 21 zs for central collision to about 5 zs at impact parameter 2.2 fm, where the last quasifission events are observed.
- The TDHF-predicted total kinetic energies for \({}^{40}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U central collisions agree with the Viola systematics up to 10% above the barrier.

## Context

This work extends the DC-TDHF method previously applied to light and medium-mass systems (Umar et al., 2006–2014) and to the cold fusion reaction leading to element \(Z=112\) (Umar, 2010) to the hot fusion reactions \({}^{40,48}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U and the quasifission reaction \({}^{54}\)Cr+\({}^{186}\)W. The results directly compare with experimental capture cross-sections from Itkis (2002, 2004) and Oganessian (2007), and with experimental mass-angle distributions from Hammerton (2014). The finding that the neutron-rich \({}^{48}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U system shows considerably less quasifission and lower fragment excitation energies than the \({}^{40}\)Ca+\({}^{238}\)U system supports the experimental observation that neutron-rich projectiles have higher probability for superheavy element formation. The approach enables future microscopic calculations of the compound-nucleus formation probability \(P_{\mathrm{CN}}\) and provides a foundation for going beyond TDHF to include fluctuations.
