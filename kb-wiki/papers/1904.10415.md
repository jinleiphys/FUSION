# Disentangle contributions to small-system collectivity via scans of light nucleus-nucleus collisions

> The observation of multi-particle azimuthal correlations in high-energy small-system collisions has led to intense debate on its origin and the possible coexistence from two competing theoretical scenarios: one based on initial-state intrinsic momentum anisotropy (ISM), and the other based on final-state collective response to the collision geometry (FSM). To complement the previous scan of asymmetric collision systems ($p$+Au, $d$+Au and He+Au), we propose a scan of small symmetric collision systems at RHIC, such as C+C, O+O, Al+Al and Ar+Ar $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}=0.2$ TeV, to provide further insights in disentangling contributions from these two scenarios. These symmetric small systems have the advantage of providing a better controlled initial geometry dominated by the average shape of the overlap region, as opposed to fluctuation-driven geometries in asymmetric systems. A transport model is employed to investigate the expected geometry response in the FSM scenario. Different trends of elliptic flow with increasing charge particle multiplicity are observed between symmetric and asymmetric systems, while triangular flow appears to show a similar behavior. Furthermore, a comparison of O+O collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}=0.2$ TeV and at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}=2.76-7$ TeV, as proposed at the LHC, provides a unique opportunity to disentangle the collision geometry effects at nucleon level from those arising from subnucleon fluctuations.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that scanning small symmetric collision systems (C+C, O+O, Al+Al, Ar+Ar) at RHIC, in addition to previously studied asymmetric systems (p+Au, d+Au, He+Au), can disentangle contributions from initial-state intrinsic momentum anisotropy (ISM) and final-state collective response to collision geometry (FSM), but the authors hedge that the relative contribution of FSM vs. ISM for the v_n data in small systems is an area of intense ongoing debate.

## Method

The authors employ the AMPT model (v.2.25, string-melting mode, partonic cross section 1.5 mb) with Monte Carlo Glauber initial conditions to simulate four symmetric systems (C+C, O+O, Al+Al, Ar+Ar) and three asymmetric systems (p+Au, d+Au, He+Au) at sqrt(s_NN)=0.2 TeV, plus O+O at 2.76 TeV. They compute elliptic flow v_2 and triangular flow v_3 using both the participant plane (PP) method and the two-particle correlation (2PC) technique with a pseudorapidity gap of |Delta_eta|>1.5 and peripheral subtraction, for charged particles in 0.2 < p_T < 3 GeV/c and |eta| < 2.0.

## Key numbers

- Four symmetric collision systems proposed: C+C, O+O, Al+Al, Ar+Ar at sqrt(s_NN)=0.2 TeV
- Three asymmetric systems compared: p+Au, d+Au, He+Au at sqrt(s_NN)=0.2 TeV
- O+O collisions compared at sqrt(s_NN)=0.2 TeV and sqrt(s_NN)=2.76-7 TeV (LHC)
- AMPT model uses partonic cross section of 1.5 mb
- About 20 million AMPT events generated for each collision system at each energy
- v_n calculated for charged particles in 0.2 < p_T < 3 GeV/c and |eta| < 2.0
- Pseudorapidity gap of |Delta_eta| > 1.5 used for 2PC method
- v_2{PP} in symmetric systems shows a peak in the n_ch dependence, with peak positions increasing for larger systems
- v_3{PP} values for different symmetric systems follow a common increasing trend as a function of n_ch
- For O+O at 2.76 TeV, n_ch range spans about a factor of 2.5 larger than at 0.2 TeV
- v_3 in p+Au at RHIC is lower by more than a factor of two compared to p+Pb at LHC
- A one-week 200 GeV O+O run proposed by STAR is expected to provide 400 million minimum bias events and 200 million 0-5% central events

## Context

This work extends the previous geometry scan of asymmetric systems (p+Au, d+Au, He+Au) at RHIC by Nagle (2013) and the PHENIX Collaboration (2018), which showed a hierarchy in v_2 and v_3 consistent with FSM expectations. The proposed symmetric systems fill the gap between pp and Cu+Cu collisions, where Cu+Cu clearly exhibits final-state QGP effects such as collective flow (Abelev 2010, Adare 2014) and jet quenching (Adare 2008, Abelev 2009). The paper also connects to the LHC heavy-ion program discussed in the yellow report by Citron (2018), which proposes O+O runs at 2.76-7 TeV. The RHIC-LHC energy comparison of O+O collisions would enable disentangling nucleon-level geometry effects from subnucleon fluctuations, addressing the ongoing debate between ISM and FSM scenarios for small-system collectivity.
