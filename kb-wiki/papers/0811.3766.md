# Centrality dependence of strangeness production in heavy-ion collisions as a geometrical effect of core-corona superposition

> It is shown that data on strange particle production as a function of centrality in Au-Au collisions at \sqrt(s)_{NN}= 200 GeV can be explained with a superposition of emission from a hadron gas at full chemical equilibrium (core) and from nucleon-nucleon collisions at the boundary (corona) of the overlapping region of the two colliding nuclei. This model nicely accounts for the enhancement of phi meson and strange particle production as a function of centrality observed in relativistic heavy ion collisions at that energy. The enhancement is mainly a geometrical effect, that is the increasing weight of the core with respect to corona for higher centrality, while strangeness canonical suppression in the core seems to play a role only in very peripheral collisions. This model, if confirmed at lower energy, would settle the long-standing problem of strangeness under-saturation in relativistic heavy ion collisions, parametrized by $\gs$. Furthermore, it would give a unique tool to locate the onset of deconfinement in nuclear collisions both as a function of energy and centrality if this is to be associated to the onset of the formation of a fully equilibrated core.

# Centrality dependence of strangeness production in heavy-ion collisions as a geometrical effect of core-corona superposition

## Key claim

The observed enhancement of strange particle production from peripheral to central Au-Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV can be explained as a geometrical effect of superposing emission from a fully equilibrated hadron gas core (γs = 1) and single nucleon-nucleon collisions in the corona, with canonical suppression playing a role only in very peripheral collisions; the key probe is the φ meson, which is immune to canonical suppression and shows a centrality dependence that cannot be explained by a temperature increase or by a corona made of small equilibrated clusters.

## Method

The authors model the mid-rapidity density of each hadron species as a sum of two contributions: a corona term from singly-colliding nucleons (NPC/2 times the measured pp yield) and a core term from a fully equilibrated hadron gas with volume proportional to (NP - NPC). The number of corona participants NPC is defined as the total number of nucleons undergoing exactly one collision, calculated via Glauber Monte-Carlo simulation. The ratio RA = (2 dN/dy_AA) / (NP dN/dy_pp) is then expressed as RA = A + (NPC/NP)(1 - A), where A is a species-dependent constant fixed from one centrality bin, and the centrality dependence is predicted purely from geometry.

## Key numbers

- The φ meson RA ratio in Au-Au at √sNN = 200 GeV is well described by the model when fixing factor A from either the 2nd most central bin or the mid-peripheral bin, with excellent agreement across all centralities.
- For hyperons (Λ, Ξ, Ω), the model overshoots data in the most peripheral collisions, indicating that canonical suppression (not included in the simple A-constant model) becomes relevant there.
- The fitted number of corona participants NPC from the statistical hadronization model agrees well with the Glauber-calculated number of singly-colliding nucleons in peripheral and semi-central collisions; in central collisions the corona contribution is too small to fit reliably.
- The chemical freeze-out temperature is constant (~160 MeV) across all centralities, while the fitted γs parameter decreases from ~1 in central to significantly less than 1 in peripheral collisions.

## Context

This work extends the core-corona superposition idea previously applied to Pb-Pb collisions at SPS energies by Becattini and Manninen (2006) and by Hohne et al. (2006), and to charged hadron rapidity densities by Bozek (2005). It directly contradicts the canonical suppression explanation for strangeness under-saturation proposed by Redlich (2004), which predicted a flat φ meson yield as a function of centrality. The success of the model suggests that a fully equilibrated core forms at any centrality at √sNN = 200 GeV, and that measuring φ meson and hyperon production as a function of centrality at lower energies (from few GeV to 20 GeV) could locate the onset of deconfinement. The work also clarifies that the corona should be defined by the number of singly-colliding nucleons rather than by local density as used in the EPOS model by Werner (2007).
