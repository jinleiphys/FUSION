# Neutron single-particle strength in silicon isotopes: Constraining the driving forces of shell evolution

> Shell evolution is studied in the neutron-rich silicon isotopes 36,38,40 Si using neutron single-particle strengths deduced from one-neutron knockout reactions. Configurations involving neutron excita- tions across the N = 20 and N = 28 shell gaps are quantified experimentally in these rare isotopes. Comparisons with shell model calculations show that the tensor force, understood to drive the col- lective behavior in 42 Si with N = 28, is already important in determining the structure of 40 Si with N = 26. New data relating to cross-shell excitations provide the first quantitative support for repulsive contributions to the cross-shell T = 1 interaction arising from three-nucleon forces.

## Key claim

The tensor force, previously understood to drive collective behavior in 42Si at N=28, is already important in determining the structure of 40Si at N=26; new data on cross-shell excitations provide the first quantitative support for repulsive contributions to the cross-shell T=1 interaction arising from three-nucleon forces, though the observed excess of p3/2 strength relative to shell-model predictions indicates the N=28 shell gap may be reduced even more than present calculations suggest.

## Method

One-neutron knockout reactions were performed on 36,38,40Si secondary beams at 100, 95, and 85 MeV/u, respectively, impinging on a beryllium target at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory. Prompt gamma rays from de-excitation of knockout residues were detected with the GRETINA array, and partial cross sections to specific final states were extracted using gamma-ray tagging and parallel momentum distributions. The experimental cross sections were compared to shell-model calculations using the SDPF-MU and SDPF-U effective interactions, with eikonal reaction theory used to compute single-particle cross sections.

## Context

This work extends previous measurements of collective observables in neutron-rich silicon isotopes (Bastin 2007, Nowacki 2009, Utsuno 2012, Caurier 2014) by providing the first experimental investigation of single-particle observables in 36,38,40Si. The results confirm the importance of the tensor force for shell evolution, as proposed by Otsuka (2005), and provide the first quantitative support for repulsive contributions from three-nucleon forces to the cross-shell T=1 interaction, consistent with the Fujita-Miyazawa process shown to be crucial for the oxygen dripline (Otsuka 2010). The observed excess of p3/2 strength relative to shell-model predictions is consistent with previous one-neutron knockout measurements from 30,32Mg (Terry 2008) and 33Mg (Kanungo 2010), as well as a (t,p) transfer measurement in 32Mg (Wimmer 2010), suggesting a structure effect rather than a reaction theory defect.
