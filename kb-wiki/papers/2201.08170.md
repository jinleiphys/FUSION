# Unified Explanation of the Anomalies in Semi-Leptonic $B$ decays and the $W$ Mass

> The discrepancies between the measurements of rare (semi-)leptonic $B$ decays and the corresponding Standard Model predictions point convincingly towards the existence of new physics for which a heavy neutral gauge boson ($Z^\prime$) is a prime candidate. However, the effect of the mixing of the $Z^\prime$ with the SM $Z$, even though it cannot be avoided by any symmetry, is usually assumed to be small and thus neglected in phenomenological analyses. In this letter we point out that a mixing of the naturally expected size leads to lepton flavour universal contributions, providing a very good fit to $B$ data. Furthermore, the global electroweak fit is affected by $Z-Z^\prime$ mixing where the tension in the $W$ mass, recently confirmed and strengthened by the CDF measurement, prefers a non-zero value of it. We find that a $Z^\prime$ boson with a mass between $\approx 1-5\,\rm {TeV}$ can provide a unified explanations of the $B$ anomalies and the $W$ mass. This strongly suggests that the breaking of the new gauge symmetry giving raise to the $Z^\prime$ boson is linked to electroweak symmetry breaking with intriguing consequences for model building.

## Key claim

A heavy neutral gauge boson (Z') with a naturally expected mixing with the Standard Model Z boson can simultaneously explain the anomalies in semi-leptonic B decays and the tension in the W boson mass, provided the Z' mass lies between approximately 1 and 5 TeV. The authors stress that Z-Z' mixing, usually neglected in phenomenological analyses, is unavoidable and produces lepton flavour universal contributions that improve the fit to B data while also affecting the global electroweak fit in a way that prefers a non-zero mixing angle.

## Method

The authors performed a combined global fit of b to s l+ l- data (including 254 observables such as R_K, R_K*, angular observables like P5', and B_s to phi mu+ mu- branching ratios) together with electroweak precision observables (including the W mass, Z-pole observables, neutrino trident production, LEP bounds on 4-lepton contact interactions, and tau decay lepton flavour universality ratios). They considered a simplified Z' model with an SU(2)_L singlet gauge boson, assuming vectorial couplings to leptons motivated by an L_mu = -L_tau symmetry, left-handed b-s quark couplings, and a free Z-Z' mixing angle sin(xi). The fit was performed using the HEPfit code and the ACDMN flavour analysis code.

## Key numbers

* The best-fit point for the lepton flavour universal Wilson coefficient C10^U is +0.30, with a 1 sigma confidence interval of [+0.15, +0.45] and a 2 sigma interval of [+0.00, +0.61].
* The best-fit point for the lepton flavour violating Wilson coefficient C9mu^V is -0.96, with a 1 sigma interval of [-1.11, -0.80] and a 2 sigma interval of [-1.25, -0.64].
* The combined fit to b to s l+ l- data has a Pull_SM of 6.9 sigma and a p-value of 28.3 percent.
* The Z' boson mass is constrained to the range approximately 1 to 5 TeV.
* The preferred mixing angle is approximately |sin(xi)| = 3.5 x 10^{-3} x (1 TeV / m_Z').
* The B_s - anti-B_s mixing constraint gives (g^{dL}_{23} / 0.52)^2 x (10 TeV / m_Z')^2 = 0.110 +/- 0.090.
* The W mass tension is at least 3.7 sigma (conservative) and up to 6.5 sigma (standard) when comparing the experimental average (80.4133 +/- 0.0080 GeV) to the SM prediction (80.3499 +/- 0.0056 GeV).

## Context

This work extends previous analyses of B anomalies (Alguero 2021, Capdevila 2017, Altmannshofer 2021) by including the previously neglected effect of Z-Z' mixing, which generates lepton flavour universal contributions. It builds on the observation by Allanach (2021) that Z-Z' mixing affects the W mass, but provides the first combined fit of flavour and electroweak data showing that both anomalies can be explained simultaneously. The paper enables future model-building where the breaking of the new U(1)' gauge symmetry is linked to electroweak symmetry breaking, and predicts that improved measurements of angular observables in B decays and more precise W mass measurements at future colliders (FCC-ee, ILC, CEPC, CLIC) can test this scenario.
