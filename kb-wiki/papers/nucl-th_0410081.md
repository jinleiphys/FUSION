# Hydro-inspired parameterizations of freeze-out in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Popular parameterizations of the freeze-out conditions in relativistic heavy-ion collisions are discussed. Similarities and differences between the blast-wave model and the single-freeze-out model, both used recently to interpret the RHIC data, are outlined. A non-boost-invariant extension of the single-freeze-out model is proposed and applied to describe the recent BRAHMS data.

# Hydro-inspired parameterizations of freeze-out in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
The single-freeze-out model can describe both particle ratios and transverse-momentum spectra with a single temperature value (around 165–170 MeV) only if resonance decays are fully included, contrary to the simpler blast-wave model which yields lower temperatures (100–140 MeV) from spectra fits. The paper proposes a non-boost-invariant extension of the single-freeze-out model that reproduces BRAHMS data across a wide rapidity range, but explicitly cannot describe the rapidity dependence of the antiproton-to-proton ratio without further modification.

## Method
The authors derive the Cooper-Frye formula for spherically symmetric and boost-invariant freeze-out hypersurfaces, then extend the single-freeze-out model by introducing a Gaussian dependence of the transverse size on space-time rapidity, breaking boost invariance. They fit the resulting three-parameter model (proper time τ, maximum transverse size parameter α⊥^max, and Gaussian width Δ) to BRAHMS π^+, π^-, K^+, and K^- transverse-momentum spectra at multiple rapidities, using thermodynamic parameters fixed from midrapidity hadron ratios.

## Key numbers
- Temperature from midrapidity hadron ratios in the single-freeze-out model: T = 165.6 MeV, with baryon chemical potential μ_B = 28.5 MeV
- Optimal fit parameters for the non-boost-invariant model: τ = 8.33 fm, α⊥^max = 0.825, Δ = 3.33
- Temperature from the original blast-wave model fit to low-energy data (Ne + NaF at 800 MeV per nucleon): T = 44 MeV, with radial flow velocity v = 0.373
- The boost-invariant approximation is valid only in the limited rapidity range -1 < y < 1 at RHIC
- Discrepancies between model and data for pions at y = 0 are about 10%

## Context
This work extends the blast-wave model of Siemens and Rasmussen (1979) and the boost-invariant formulation by Schnedermann, Sollfrank, and Heinz (1993), and builds on the single-freeze-out model developed by Broniowski and Florkowski (2001–2002). The non-boost-invariant extension addresses the Gaussian rapidity distributions observed by the BRAHMS Collaboration (Bearden, 2004), which contradict the strict boost invariance assumed in earlier models. The approach enables simultaneous description of particle abundances and spectra across rapidity, but leaves open the problem of describing the rapidity dependence of baryon chemical potential needed for antiproton-to-proton ratios.
