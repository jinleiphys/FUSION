# A nucleonic NJL model for finite nuclei: dynamic mass generation and ground-state observables

> We test the compatibility of chiral symmetry, dynamic mass generation of the nucleon due to spontaneous breaking of chiral symmetry, and the description of finite nuclear systems by employing an NJL model understood as a chiral invariant effective theory for nucleons. We apply the model to nuclear matter as well as to finite nuclei. In the latter case, the model is adjusted to nuclear ground-state observables. We treat the case of a pure chiral theory and the physically more realistic case where a portion of the nucleon mass (160 MeV) explicitly breaks chiral symmetry. The best version of this current model is found to deliver reasonably good results simultaneously for both finite nuclei and the nucleon mass, which supports our motivation of probing a link between low-momentum QCD and the nuclear many-body problem. However, the observables calculated for finite nuclei are not as good as those coming from existing relativistic mean field models without explicit chiral symmetry.

## Key claim

The paper tests whether a nucleonic Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, which incorporates chiral symmetry and dynamic mass generation via spontaneous symmetry breaking, can simultaneously describe finite nuclear ground-state observables and the nucleon mass. The authors find that while the best version of the model delivers "reasonably good results" for both, the calculated finite-nuclei observables are not as accurate as those from existing relativistic mean-field (RMF) models that lack explicit chiral symmetry.

## Method

The authors construct a nucleonic NJL (NNJL) model with a chiral-invariant Lagrangian containing four-fermion and eight-fermion contact interactions, extended with derivative terms, isovector terms, and the Coulomb field for finite nuclei. They apply the model in the Hartree mean-field approximation, treating negative-energy Dirac sea states in a local density approximation with a sharp momentum cutoff, and determine the coupling constants by least-squares adjustments to nuclear matter pseudo-observables and to ground-state observables of seven finite nuclei (16O, 40Ca, 48Ca, 88Sr, 90Zr, 132Sn, 208Pb) plus the vacuum nucleon mass.

## Key numbers

- Vacuum nucleon mass reproduced to within 0.24%: predicted 941.2 MeV versus experimental 938.9 MeV for the finite-nuclei fit (set N-FN).
- Effective mass ratio at saturation for the finite-nuclei fit: m*/m_vac = 0.75, compared to 0.61 for the RMF-PC force PC-F1.
- Saturation density for N-FN: 0.155 fm^-3; binding energy per particle: -16.4 MeV; compressibility: 388 MeV.
- For 208Pb with N-FN: binding energy -1642.5 MeV (experiment -1636.4 MeV), diffraction radius 6.742 fm (experiment 6.806 fm), rms radius 5.438 fm (experiment 5.504 fm), surface thickness 0.852 fm (experiment 0.900 fm).
- Total chi-squared for N-FN: 174; for the RMF-PC force PC-F1: 35.
- Explicit chiral symmetry breaking mass term set to m0 = 160 MeV in the best fits.
- Momentum cutoff for N-FN: Lambda_c = 2.35 fm^-1.

## Context

This work extends earlier NJL model studies of nuclear matter by Koch et al. (2001) and applies the approach to finite nuclei for the first time with a realistic treatment including derivative terms, Coulomb force, and center-of-mass correction. The authors compare their results to the RMF-PC force PC-F1 from Buer02, which represents the accuracy achievable in modern mean-field approaches, and find that the NNJL model cannot match that accuracy due to constraints from chiral symmetry and the dynamically generated nucleon mass. The work enables further investigation of the link between low-momentum QCD and nuclear many-body physics, but the authors note that chiral models with nonlinear realization of chiral symmetry, such as those by Furnstahl et al. (1997) and Schuck et al. (2002), achieve accuracy comparable to modern RMF approaches.
