# A geometrical interpretation of the Thomas theorem and the Efimov States

> Using a generalized Bohr model and the hyper-spherical formalism for a three-body system, we derive the Thomas theorem assuming a simple interaction depending on the range of the potential. We discuss the conditions for which an unbound two-body system produces a bound three-body system and derive universal energy functions. We apply our model to $^{4}$He and Triton atoms as well as to the triton nucleus. Using their scattering lengths and effective ranges, we are able to reproduce the two-body or the three-body binding energies with only one parameter fitted. Prediction for excited (Efimov) levels are also given and in particular we demonstrate that for some hyper-angles two equal minima appear which indicate a phase (shape) transition similar to the Landau's theory of phase transition. We suggest that the observed excited levels in two different experiments for the triton nucleus are indeed Efimov levels and there may be more surprises.

# A geometrical interpretation of the Thomas theorem and the Efimov States

## Key claim
The authors derive the Thomas theorem and universal energy functions for three-body systems using a generalized Bohr model with hyper-spherical coordinates, showing that the transition between Thomas states (ground state) and Efimov states (excited levels) can be interpreted as a shape phase transition analogous to Landau's theory of phase transitions, with the critical condition occurring when the effective range is comparable to the scattering length.

## Method
The authors adopt a Bohr model with the Heisenberg relation p_ij * r_ij = n*hbar (restricted to n=1) and a short-range Gaussian two-body potential V(r_ij) = -c*(hbar^2/(m*a^2))*exp[-(r_ij - r_0)^2/a^2], where c is the single fitting parameter. They introduce hyper-radius R and hyper-angles alpha_k coordinates for the three-body system, with the three equal hyper-angles (pi/4) corresponding to an equilateral triangle configuration (Thomas state) and one hyper-angle approaching zero corresponding to a monomer-plus-dimer configuration (Efimov state). The parameter c is fitted to either the two-body binding energy or the three-body binding energy depending on the system.

## Key numbers
- For the deuterium nucleus: binding energy E_d = 2.225 MeV, triplet scattering length a_t = 5.42 fm, effective range r_t = 1.76 fm
- For the triton nucleus: binding energy E_t = 8.48 MeV; for helium-3 nucleus: E_{He-3} = 7.72 MeV
- For the triton nucleus, an excited level is predicted at approximately E_t* ≈ 7.0 MeV excitation energy, corresponding to a critical hyper-angle alpha_{ic} = pi/12.228
- For helium-4 atoms: experimental scattering length a = 197(+15/-34) a_0 (where a_0 = 5.29177e-11 m), theoretical effective range r_s = 13.85 a_0, dimer binding energy E_2 = 1.1(+0.3/-0.2) mK
- For helium-4 trimer: theoretical ground state E_3^0 = 125.8 mK and first excited state E_3^1 = 2.28 mK (Motovilov et al.); the authors' model gives E_3^1 = 1.98(+0.2/-0.06) mK
- For polarized triton (T) atoms: triplet scattering length a_t = -82.1 a_0, theoretical effective range r_t = 13.7 a_0, experimental trimer energy E_3 = 4.59 mK; the authors predict an Efimov state at E_3^1 = 1.53 mK
- Universal constant s_0 = 1.00624 for the Efimov relation N → (s_0/pi)*ln(|a|/r_0)

## Context
This work extends the Thomas theorem (Thomas, 1935) and Efimov's prediction of geometric series of excited levels (Efimov, 1970) by providing a geometrical interpretation using a Bohr-type model. The authors apply their model to atomic systems (helium-4 atoms, polarized triton atoms) where Efimov states have been experimentally demonstrated (Zaccanti, 2009; Greene, 2010; Kunitski, 2015) and to nuclear systems (triton nucleus), suggesting that observed excited levels in two independent experiments (Rogachev, 2003; Russian group) may be Efimov levels. The model enables predictions of excited state energies and identifies a critical hyper-angle where two equal energy minima appear, indicating a shape phase transition analogous to the isotropic-nematic transition in Landau's theory of phase transitions (Landau; Huang).
