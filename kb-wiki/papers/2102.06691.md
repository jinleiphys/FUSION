# Pocket resonances in low-energy antineutrons reactions with nuclei

> Upon investigating whether the variation of the antineutron-nucleus annihilation cross-sections at very low energies satisfy Bethe-Landau's power law of $σ_{\rm ann} (p) \propto 1/p^α$ behavior as a function of the antineutron momentum $p$, we uncover unexpected regular oscillatory structures in the low antineutron energy region from 0.001 to 10 MeV, with small amplitudes and narrow periodicity in the logarithm of the antineutron energies, for large-$A$ nuclei such as Pb and Ag. Subsequent semiclassical analyses of the $S$ matrices reveal that these oscillations are pocket resonances that arise from quasi-bound states inside the pocket and the interference between the waves reflecting inside the optical potential pockets with those from beyond the potential barriers, implicit in the nuclear Ramsauer effect. They are the continuation of bound states in the continuum. Experimental observations of these pocket resonances will provide vital information on the properties of the optical model potentials and the nature of the antineutron annihilation process.

# Pocket resonances in low-energy antineutrons reactions with nuclei

## Key claim

The authors claim that the unexpected regular oscillatory structures observed in antineutron-nucleus annihilation and elastic cross-sections in the 0.001 to 10 MeV energy range for large-A nuclei such as Pb and Ag are "pocket resonances" arising from quasi-bound states inside the effective potential pocket, contradicting the generally expected Bethe-Landau power-law behavior; however, these oscillations are only present for sufficiently weak absorption (imaginary potential W approximately less than 2.3 MeV) and are absent for small nuclei.

## Method

The authors performed optical-model calculations using a momentum-dependent optical potential with Woods-Saxon form, implemented via the ECIS code, to compute antineutron-nucleus annihilation and elastic cross-sections for C, Al, Fe, Cu, Ag, Sn, and Pb nuclei in the energy range 0.001 to 10 MeV. They then carried out a semiclassical S-matrix analysis following the Brink-Takigawa formulation for complex-potential scattering, decomposing the total S-matrix into barrier-wave and internal-wave contributions to isolate the interference term responsible for the oscillations. The condition for pocket resonances was identified through the quantization of the action phase angle delta_32 between the inner turning points of the effective potential pocket.

## Key numbers

- The oscillatory structures appear in the antineutron energy region from 0.001 to 10 MeV for large-A nuclei such as Pb and Ag.
- For the s-wave limit at very low energies (1.0 to 2.0 MeV/c momentum), the power-law exponent alpha_N for C, Ag, and Pb nuclei is approximately 1.0, 0.9, and 0.8 respectively, close to Bethe-Landau's prediction of alpha_n = 1.
- The estimated onset value of the imaginary potential W_o for pocket resonances to exist in the nbar-Pb reaction is approximately 2.3 MeV.
- The available nbar-Pb annihilation cross-section data are consistent with W = 2.8 MeV, which is comparable to the onset value W_o.
- The condition for annihilation pocket resonances is delta_32(E,L) approximately (n_L - n_L' + 1/2)pi, and for elastic pocket resonances is (delta_WKB + delta_32)(E,L) approximately (n_L - n_L' + 1/2)pi.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous optical-model study (Lee, 2018) of antineutron and antiproton annihilation cross-sections on various nuclei in the momentum range 50 to 500 MeV/c, which had already indicated deviations from Bethe-Landau power-law behavior. The discovered pocket resonances are distinct from Ramsauer resonances observed in neutron total cross-sections (Peterson, 1962; Marshak, 1970), which are broad potential resonances without a barrier that shift to higher energies with increasing nuclear mass, whereas these pocket resonances shift to lower energies with increasing nuclear mass. The semiclassical analysis follows the formalism of Brink and Takigawa (1977) and Ford and Wheeler (1959), and the results suggest that similar pocket resonances should be expected in other nuclear, atomic, molecular, and heavy-ion collision systems, such as the 12C+12C reactions important for nuclear astrophysics (Tang, 2020).
