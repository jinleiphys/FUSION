# Non-equilibrium photon production arising from the chiral mass shift

> We investigate the photon emission arising from the shift of the quark/antiquark masses during the chiral phase transition in the early stage of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. As this mass shift leads to spontaneous creation of quark-antiquark pairs and thus contributes to the formation of the quark-gluon plasma, our investigations are relevant in the context of finite-lifetime effects on the photon emission from the latter. Earlier investigations on this topic by Boyanovsky et al. were plagued by a UV divergent contribution from the vacuum polarization and the non-integrability of the remaining contributions in the ultraviolet domain. In contrast to these investigations, we do not consider the photon number-density at finite times but for free asymptotic states obtained by an adiabatic switching of the electromagnetic interaction according to the Gell-Mann and Low theorem. This approach eliminates possible unphysical contributions from the vacuum polarization and renders the resulting photon spectra integrable in the ultraviolet domain. It is emphasized that the consideration of free asymptotic states is indeed crucial to obtain such physically reasonable results.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the photon number-density from the chiral mass shift must be extracted for free asymptotic states in the limit \(t\rightarrow\infty\) with an adiabatic switching of the electromagnetic interaction, rather than at finite times as in earlier work by Boyanovsky et al. This procedure eliminates the unphysical divergent vacuum-polarization contribution and renders the photon spectra integrable in the ultraviolet domain, provided the time evolution of the quark/antiquark mass is modeled in a physically reasonable manner.

## Method

The authors model the chiral phase transition by introducing a Yukawa-like source term in the QED Lagrangian that couples quarks and antiquarks to a time-dependent classical scalar background field, giving them a time-dependent mass while conserving the Ward-Takahashi identities. They compute the photon number-density to first order in the electromagnetic coupling \(\alpha_e\) but to all orders in the coupling \(g\) to the background field, using a one-loop approximation for the photon self-energy. The photon number-density is evaluated in the asymptotic time limit with an adiabatic switching of the electromagnetic interaction according to the Gell-Mann and Low theorem, and the switching parameter is taken to zero at the end of the calculation.

## Key numbers

* For an instantaneous formation of the QGP, the photon number-density scales as \(1/\omega_{\vec{k}}^{3}\) in the ultraviolet domain, where \(\omega_{\vec{k}}=|\vec{k}|\) is the photon energy; this makes the total number density logarithmically divergent and the total energy density linearly divergent.
* For a formation over a finite time interval \(\tau = 1.0\;\mathrm{fm}/c\), the UV scaling is suppressed to \(1/\omega_{\vec{k}}^{3.8}\), rendering the total number density finite but leaving the total energy density UV divergent.
* The vacuum-polarization contribution to the photon number-density is eliminated by taking the successive limits \(t\rightarrow\infty\) and \(\varepsilon\rightarrow0\) in the correct order; if the limits are reversed at finite time, the vacuum contribution yields a linearly divergent integral over \(\dd^3p\) for given photon energy \(\omega_{\vec{k}}\).

## Context

This work extends earlier investigations by Boyanovsky et al. (2000-2003) on finite-lifetime effects in photon emission from the quark-gluon plasma, which were plagued by UV-divergent vacuum-polarization contributions and non-integrable photon spectra. It also addresses criticisms raised by Fraga et al. (2003-2004) regarding the use of finite-time photon number-densities and the inconsistent renormalization in those earlier approaches. The authors' previous model (Michler et al., 2009) using time-dependent occupation numbers achieved renormalization of the vacuum polarization but could not fully resolve the UV problem, which they attribute to a violation of Ward-Takahashi identities; the present chiral-photon-production scenario conserves these identities and provides a first-principle description that yields physically reasonable, UV-integrable spectra.
