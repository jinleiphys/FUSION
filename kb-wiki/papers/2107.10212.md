# Nuclear ab initio calculations of 6He $β$-decay for beyond the Standard Model studies

> Precision measurements of $β$-decay observables offer the possibility to search for deviations from the Standard Model. A possible discovery of such deviations requires accompanying first-principles calculations. Here we compute the nuclear structure corrections for the $β$-decay of $^6$He which is of central interest in several experimental efforts. We employ the impulse approximation together with wave functions calculated using the ab initio no-core shell model with potentials based on chiral effective field theory. We use these state-of-the-art calculations to give a novel and comprehensive analysis of theoretical uncertainties. We find that nuclear corrections, which we compute within the sensitivity of future experiments, create significant deviation from the naive Gamow-Teller predictions, making their accurate assessment essential in searches for physics beyond the Standard Model.

# Nuclear ab initio calculations of 6He β-decay for beyond the Standard Model studies

## Key claim

Nuclear structure corrections to the 6He β-decay observables, computed within the sensitivity of future experiments, create significant deviations from the naive Gamow-Teller predictions; however, the corrections depend on the electron kinetic energy and on the specific experimental setup, meaning that extracting the angular correlation coefficient requires an energy-weighted average rather than a simple constant shift.

## Method

The authors employed the impulse approximation together with wave functions calculated using the ab initio no-core shell model (NCSM) with chiral effective field theory (χEFT) interactions, specifically NNLO_opt (two-nucleon only) and NNLO_sat (including three-nucleon forces). They computed the needed multipole operators (axial charge, vector magnetic, and longitudinal axial operators) using translationally-invariant one-body densities to remove spurious center-of-mass contributions, and propagated nuclear-structure uncertainties from the Hamiltonian and model-space parameter variations to the final observables.

## Key numbers

- The energy-weighted average correction to the angular correlation coefficient: ⟨δ̃_a^(1+β-)⟩ = -2.54(68)·10^(-3), corresponding to a 7 per-mil correction to the Standard Model value a_βν^GT = -1/3.
- The nuclear-structure-induced Fierz-like term: b_F^(1+β-) = δ_b^(1+β-) = -1.52(18)·10^(-3), with an uncertainty of approximately 10^(-4).
- The corrected angular correlation coefficient extracted from the 1963 experiment: a_βν = -0.3331(32), after applying nuclear-structure and radiative corrections.
- The endpoint energy Q = 3.50521(5) MeV for the 6He(0+_gs) → 6Li(1+_gs) transition.
- The Coulomb energy difference between final and initial states: ΔE_c = 0.85(3) MeV from quantum Monte Carlo calculations, consistent with the experimental value 0.837(10) MeV.
- The empirical Gamow-Teller matrix element from the 6He half-life: |GT(6He)|_expt = 2.161(5), with a 1.5% deviation from the calculated L̂^A_1 operator.
- The estimated small parameter for the EFT expansion: ε_EFT ≲ 0.15, based on comparisons with magnetic moment and M1 transition data.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Glick-Magid et al. (2021) for β-decay corrections and applies it to 6He, which is of central interest in several experimental campaigns at Laboratoire de Physique Corpusculaire de CAEN, National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory, the University of Washington CENPA (He6-CRES Collaboration), and the SARAF accelerator. The calculations provide the first consistent nuclear-structure corrections to these observables using full nuclear dynamics from ab initio methods, building on earlier work by Calaprice (1976) that showed the importance of such effects. The results enable future experiments aiming for per-mil level precision to correctly distinguish Standard Model effects from possible beyond-the-Standard-Model signatures, particularly through the indirect linear dependence of the angular correlation on the Fierz-like term.
