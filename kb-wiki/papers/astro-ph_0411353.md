# The Moment of Inertia of the Binary Pulsar J0737-3039A: Constraining the Nuclear Equation of State

> We construct numerical models of the newly discovered binary pulsar J0737-3039A, both with a fully relativistic, uniformly rotating, equilibrium code that handles arbitrary spins and in the relativistic, slow-rotation approximation. We compare results for a representative sample of viable nuclear equations of state (EOS) that span three, qualitatively different, classes of models for the description of nuclear matter. A future dynamical measurement of the neutron star's moment of inertia from pulsar timing data will impose significant constraints on the nuclear EOS. Even a moderately accurate measurement (<~ 10 %) may be able to rule out some of these competing classes. Using the measured mass, spin and moment of inertia to identify the optimal model computed from different EOSs, one can determine the pulsar's radius.

## Key claim

A future dynamical measurement of the moment of inertia of pulsar J0737-3039A from pulsar timing data, even with only moderate accuracy (less than or about 10%), may be sufficient to distinguish between qualitatively different classes of nuclear equations of state (EOS), potentially ruling out whole classes of models rather than just refining parameters.

## Method

The authors constructed numerical models of the uniformly rotating neutron star J0737-3039A (gravitational mass M = 1.337 solar masses, angular velocity Omega = 276.8 inverse seconds) using two independent relativistic calculations: a fully relativistic equilibrium code handling arbitrary spins (Cook, Shapiro, and Teukolsky 1994) and the relativistic slow-rotation approximation. They computed the moment of inertia for six representative nuclear EOSs spanning three qualitatively different classes: nonrelativistic many-body calculations (APR and FPS), relativistic mean-field approximations (MS1 and GM3), and strange quark matter models (SQM1 and SQM3).

## Key numbers

- For Class I EOSs (APR, FPS): moment of inertia I = 1.24 and 1.14 (times 10^45 g cm^2), respectively, for a star of mass 1.337 solar masses and spin period 27.7 ms
- For Class II EOSs (MS1, GM3): moment of inertia I = 1.66 and 1.49 (times 10^45 g cm^2), respectively
- For Class III EOSs (SQM1, SQM3): moment of inertia I = 0.982 and 1.41 (times 10^45 g cm^2), respectively
- Class I predicts radius less than or about 12 km and moment of inertia less than or about 1.2 times 10^45 g cm^2; Class II predicts radius greater than or about 13 km and moment of inertia greater than or about 1.5 times 10^45 g cm^2
- The stellar frequency Omega = 276.8 Hz is much less than the Kepler frequency for all EOSs, with Omega/Omega_max less than 0.035
- The slow-rotation approximation error is about 0.1 percent, while interpolation error from sparse EOS datasets is closer to about 1 percent for some EOSs

## Context

This work extends the study of binary pulsar J0737-3039A, which is about ten times closer to Earth than the Hulse-Taylor binary B1913+16 and has significantly smaller binary separation, making relativistic spin-orbit coupling effects much larger. The authors note that Lens-Thirring precession, measurable from pulsar timing data as proposed by Damour and Schafer (1988) and Wex (1995), will yield the moment of inertia, analogous to the Gravity Probe B experiment measuring such effects for Earth. A measurement of I could distinguish between the "realistic" many-body nuclear models (Class I, based on nucleon-nucleon scattering data and light nuclei binding energies) and mean-field models (Class II, based on empirical symmetric nuclear matter properties), and could also constrain the free parameters in strange quark matter models (Class III).
