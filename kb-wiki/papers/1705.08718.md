# Emergence of clusters: Halos, Efimov states, and experimental signals

> We investigate emergence of halos and Efimov states in nuclei by use of a newly designed model which combines self-consistent mean-field and three-body descriptions. Recent interest in neutron heavy calcium isotopes makes $^{72}$Ca ($^{70}$Ca+n+n) an ideal realistic candidate on the neutron dripline, and we use it as a representative example that illustrates our broadly applicable conclusions. By smooth variation of the interactions we simulate the crossover from well-bound systems to structures beyond the threshold of binding, and find that halo-configurations emerge from the mean-field structure for three-body binding energy less than $\sim 100$keV. Strong evidence is provided that Efimov states cannot exist in nuclei. The structure that bears the most resemblance to an Efimov state is a giant halo extending beyond the neutron-core scattering length. We show that the observable large-distance decay properties of the wave function can differ substantially from the bulk part at short distances, and that this evolution can be traced with our combination of few- and many-body formalisms. This connection is vital for interpretation of measurements such as those where an initial state is populated in a reaction or by a beta-decay.

# Emergence of clusters: Halos, Efimov states, and experimental signals

## Key claim

Halo configurations emerge from the mean-field structure when the three-body binding energy is less than approximately 100 keV, but Efimov states cannot exist in nuclei because the mass ratio of one heavy core to two light neutrons produces an absurdly large Efimov scaling factor (on the order of 10^131 for 72Ca), making such states impossible for any realistic nuclear system.

## Method

The authors combine a self-consistent mean-field treatment of the core nucleons (using Skyrme forces with the SLy4 parametrization) with a three-body description of the two valence neutrons using the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method. They simulate approach to the neutron dripline by continuously scaling the global Skyrme parameters (t_i → S t_i) for the 70Ca+n+n system, which is borromean (bound as a three-body system while all two-body subsystems are unbound). The coupled Hartree-Fock and three-body equations are solved self-consistently, with Pauli forbidden states removed from the hyperspherical basis.

## Key numbers

- Halo configurations emerge for three-body binding energy less than approximately 100 keV, based on the abrupt increase in valence neutron distance seen in the rms radii curves.
- The s-wave n-core scattering length diverges (|a| = ∞) at a Skyrme parameter scaling factor of S = 1.201, marking the threshold for s-wave binding.
- The d5/2 neutron level becomes bound at S = 1.17, as seen from the two-body properties.
- For 72Ca, the Efimov scaling parameter ξ ≈ 0.01035, yielding an Efimov scaling factor s ≈ 10^131, which is absurdly large and precludes any Efimov state.
- For the proposed candidate 62Ca, ξ ≈ 0.01205 gives s ≈ 10^113; for 11Li, ξ ≈ 0.07382 gives s ≈ 10^16.
- The neutron-neutron scattering length is approximately 20 fm, which is much smaller than the required s-wave scattering length of at least 20000 fm needed for Efimov physics.
- The core radius of 70Ca is approximately 5 fm.

## Context

This work extends the line of investigation into halo nuclei and possible Efimov states near the neutron dripline, following earlier proposals for nuclear Efimov candidates in calcium isotopes by Hagino (2013) and in other systems by Jensen (2003) and Mazumdar (1997, 2000). The authors provide strong evidence contradicting these proposals by showing that the mass ratio of one heavy core to two light particles produces an enormous Efimov scaling factor, making Efimov states impossible in nuclei. The method enables future interpretation of decay measurements (such as those from reactions or beta-decay) by tracing how the wave function evolves from short-distance bulk properties to large-distance observable distributions, which can differ qualitatively depending on whether s-wave or d-wave contributions dominate at large distances.
