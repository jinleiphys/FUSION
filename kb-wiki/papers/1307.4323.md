# The proton spin decomposition : path dependence and gauge symmetry

> We discuss the different types of decomposition of the proton spin. We stress that, despite their lack of uniqueness, the Chen et al. and Wakamatsu decompositions are perfectly measurable. We argue that a large part of the recent controversies boils down to the fact that there actually exist two types of gauge transformations in the Chen et al. approach, where physical and gauge degrees of freedom of the gauge potential are explicitly separated. By carefully distinguishing these two types of gauge transformations, one can easily understand how the concepts of gauge invariance, Stueckelberg symmetry, path dependence and measurability are linked to each other.

## Key claim

The Chen et al. and Wakamatsu decompositions of the proton spin are perfectly measurable despite their lack of uniqueness, because measurability requires gauge invariance but not necessarily invariance under Stueckelberg (background/path) transformations; the Stueckelberg dependence is fixed by the factorization procedure used in experiments.

## Method

The paper reviews the four main types of proton spin decompositions (Jaffe-Manohar, Ji, Chen et al., and Wakamatsu) and analyzes the Chen et al. approach where the gauge potential is split into pure-gauge and physical terms. It shows that Stueckelberg transformations arise from the freedom to treat active and passive gauge transformations differently when a background field is present, and that this Stueckelberg dependence generalizes the path dependence of non-local gauge-invariant quantities. The author then classifies measurable quantities into "observables" (Stueckelberg-independent) and "quasi-observables" (Stueckelberg-dependent but still measurable).

## Key numbers

- The quark spin contribution is identical in all four decompositions: \(\uvec S^q = \int d^3x\,\psi^\dag \tfrac{1}{2}\uvec\Sigma \psi\).
- The gluon spin in the Jaffe-Manohar decomposition is \(\uvec S^g_\text{JM} = \int d^3x\,\uvec E^a\times\uvec A^a\), while in the Chen et al. and Wakamatsu decompositions it is \(\uvec S^g_\text{Chen} = \uvec S^g_\text{Wak} = \int d^3x\,\uvec E^a\times\uvec A^a_\phys\).
- The potential OAM term that distinguishes the Chen et al. and Wakamatsu decompositions is \(\uvec L_\text{pot} = -\int d^3x\,(\uvec{\mathcal D}\cdot\uvec E)^a\,\uvec x\times\uvec A^a_\phys\).
- The Ji relation connects the energy-momentum form factors to moments of generalized parton distributions: \(J^{q,g}_\text{Ji} = \frac{1}{2}[A^{q,g}(0)+B^{q,g}(0)] = \frac{1}{2}\int dx\,x[H^{q,g}(x,0,0)+E^{q,g}(x,0,0)]\).

## Context

This work extends the debate on the proton spin decomposition revived by Chen et al. (2008, 2009), who challenged textbook knowledge by providing a complete gauge-invariant decomposition. It reconciles contradictory claims in the literature by clarifying that the Chen et al. and Wakamatsu decompositions are gauge-invariant extensions of the Jaffe-Manohar (1989) decomposition, analogous to how the gluon helicity \(\Delta g\) is measurable via a non-local gauge-invariant expression that formally coincides with the Jaffe-Manohar operator in light-front gauge. The paper enables a clearer understanding of which spin decompositions are measurable and how the Stueckelberg dependence relates to path dependence in non-local operators.
