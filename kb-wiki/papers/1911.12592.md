# Derivation of the Strutinsky method from the least squares principle

> The main purpose of this paper is to rigorously establish the Strutinsky method from the least squares principle. Thus, it is the mathematical basis of this method (aspect often neglected) which is revisited in an extensive way. Some formulas previously given without demonstration or in a simplified way are set out here with all the details. In this respect, the most important mathematical properties of the averaging functions are also established in this paper. When some conditions are met, it turns out that Strutinsky's method is nothing more than a polynomial moving average of the semi-classical level density.

## Key claim

The Strutinsky method for extracting a smooth level density from the quantum level density can be rigorously derived from the least squares principle, specifically as a polynomial moving average, rather than being merely an ad hoc prescription. The derivation requires two steps: a local polynomial fit via least squares, followed by a moving average that shifts the Gaussian window with the evaluation point.

## Method

The author starts by minimizing the squared error between the quantum level density \(g(\epsilon)\) and a polynomial \(Q_M(\epsilon)\) of degree \(M\), weighted by a Gaussian of width \(\gamma\) centered at a point \(\lambda\). The polynomial is expressed as a sum of Hermite polynomials, and the minimization yields coefficients that depend on \(\gamma\) and \(\lambda\). To obtain a local average at any point \(\epsilon\), the Gaussian center is made to coincide with the evaluation point, effectively performing a moving average. This yields the standard Strutinsky expression: a Gaussian multiplied by a polynomial curvature correction, summed over the discrete energy levels. The integral form of the averaging function \(F_M(x)\) is then derived, and its properties are established, including the condition \(\gamma \gtrsim \hbar\omega\) for smoothing.

## Key numbers

* The smoothing condition requires \(\gamma \gtrsim \hbar\omega\), where \(\hbar\omega\) is one quantum of the system's energy.
* For the cubic box example with \(M=8\), the relative error in the Strutinsky approximation is of order \(10^{-6} (\gamma/\lambda)^{10}\).
* For the cubic box example with \(M=30\), the relative error is of order \(10^{-9} (\gamma'/\lambda)^{32}\).
* The integral of the averaging function \(F_M(x)\) from \(-a\) to \(+a\) for \(a=1\) and \(M=30\) is approximately 0.9914; for \(M=2\) and \(a=1\), it is 1.050.

## Context

This work provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for the Strutinsky shell correction method, originally introduced by Strutinsky (1967, 1968) as a prescription. It extends prior justifications based on the Hartree-Fock method and Fermi liquid theories (Bunatian 1972, Brack 1981) by addressing the often-neglected mathematical basis. The derivation clarifies the relationship between the Strutinsky method and semi-classical level densities, showing that under the asymptotic limit (\(\lambda \gg \hbar\omega\)), the Strutinsky density is a polynomial moving average of the semi-classical density, building on earlier work by Mohammed-Azizi (2006). This enables a more principled application of the macroscopic-microscopic model to fission barriers, nuclear masses, and equilibrium shapes.
