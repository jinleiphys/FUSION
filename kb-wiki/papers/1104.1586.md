# The stickiness of sound: An absolute lower limit on viscosity and the breakdown of second order relativistic hydrodynamics

> Hydrodynamics predicts long-lived sound and shear waves. Thermal fluctuations in these waves can lead to the diffusion of momentum density, contributing to the shear viscosity and other transport coefficients. Within viscous hydrodynamics in 3+1 dimensions, this leads to a positive contribution to the shear viscosity, which is finite but inversely proportional to the microscopic shear viscosity. Therefore the effective infrared viscosity is bounded from below. The contribution to the second-order transport coefficient $τ_π$ is divergent, which means that second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamics is inconsistent below some frequency scale. We estimate the importance of each effect for the Quark-Gluon Plasma, finding them to be minor if $η/s = 0.16$ but important if $η/s = 0.08$.

## Key claim

Thermal fluctuations in long-lived sound and shear waves generate a positive contribution to shear viscosity that is inversely proportional to the microscopic viscosity, placing an absolute lower bound on the total infrared viscosity; additionally, the contribution to the second-order transport coefficient τ_π is divergent, making second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamics inconsistent below a frequency scale that depends sensitively on the value of η/s.

## Method

The authors compute the contribution of thermally excited hydrodynamic shear and sound waves to the retarded stress-tensor correlation function G_R^{xy,xy}(ω) using a loop calculation within the hydrodynamic effective theory, with the velocity correlation functions Δ_S^{ij} and Δ_R^{ij} taken from Kovtun and Yaffe (2003). They extract the corrections to shear viscosity η and the relaxation time τ_π from the small-ω expansion of G_R^{xy,xy}, imposing a momentum cutoff p_max corresponding to the largest wavenumber where the gradient expansion converges.

## Key numbers

- The new contribution to shear viscosity from hydrodynamic fluctuations is η_new = (17 p_max T (ε+P)) / (120 π^2 η_cl), where η_cl is the "classical" microscopic viscosity.
- For QCD with η_cl/s = 0.08, s = 10 T^3, and τ_π = 3 η_cl/(sT), the new contribution gives η_new ≈ 0.036 s, raising the total η/s from 0.08 to 0.116.
- For η_cl/s = 0.16 with the same assumptions, η_new ≈ 0.01 s, a negligible correction.
- The frequency scale ω_min where the ω^(3/2) term equals the η τ_π ω^2 term ranges from ω_min ≈ 7 T (for η/s = 0.08, s = 10 T^3, τ_π/γ_η = 3) down to ω_min ≈ T/125 (for η/s = 0.16, s = 16 T^3, τ_π/γ_η = 5).
- The minimal possible η/s from the bound requiring a non-zero frequency window for second-order hydrodynamics varies from about 0.09 at T = 200 MeV to about 0.07 in the Stefan-Boltzmann limit.

## Context

This work extends the non-relativistic result of SBE (1970s) showing that the gradient expansion in hydrodynamics fails beyond first order, now demonstrated in the relativistic setting relevant for Quark-Gluon Plasma studies. It contradicts the assumption that second-order formalisms (Israel and Stewart, 1970s; BRSSS, 2008) are universally applicable, and shows that for η/s ≈ 0.08 (as suggested by some elliptic flow fits from Luzum and Romatschke, 2008) there is no range of scales where second-order hydrodynamics is valid. The calculation enables more precise bounds on the minimum possible shear viscosity of QCD matter and provides a criterion for when second-order hydrodynamic simulations of heavy-ion collisions are theoretically consistent.
