# Search for the Cosmic Neutrino Background and KATRIN

> The spectrum of the Cosmic Microwave Background follows Planck's black body radiation formula and shows a remarkable constant temperature of T = 2.7. About 380 000 years after the Big Bang at a temperature of T = 3000 Kelvin in the matter dominated era the electrons combine with the protons and 4He and the photons move freely in the neutral universe. So the temperature and distribution of the photons give us information of the universe 380 000 years after the Big Bang. Information about earlier times can, in principle, be derived from the Cosmic Neutrino Background (relic neutrinos). The neutrinos decouple already about 1 second after the Big Bang at a temperature of around 1 MeV or 10^{10} Kelvin. Today their temperature is about 1.95 Kelvin. Registration of these neutrinos is an extremely challenging experimental problem, which can hardly be solved with the present technologies. On the other hand it represents a tempting opportunity to check one of key elements of the Big Bang Cosmology and to probe the early stages of the universe evolution. The search for the Cosmic Neutrino Background with the induced beta decay: relic neutrino + 3H --> 3He + e-, is the topic of this contribution. The signal would show up as a peak in the electron spectrum by an energy with the neutrino mass above the Q value. We discuss the prospects of this approach and argue that it is able to set limits on the Cosmic Neutrino density in our vicinity. We also discuss critically ways to increase with modifications of the present KATRIN spectrometer the Tritium source intensity by a factor 100, which would yield about 170 counts of relic neutrino captures per year. Presently such an increase of the Tritium source intensity seems not to be possible. But one should be able to find an upper limit for the local density of the relic neutrinos in our galaxy.

# Search for the Cosmic Neutrino Background and KATRIN

## Key claim

The paper argues that detecting the Cosmic Neutrino Background via neutrino capture on tritium using the KATRIN spectrometer is not feasible with current technology, but that an upper limit on the local relic neutrino overdensity in our galaxy can still be derived. The authors qualify that even with an optimistic overdensity of 10^6, KATRIN would yield only about 1.7 counts per year, and increasing the tritium source strength by a factor of 100 to achieve 170 counts per year appears impossible with the present spectrometer design.

## Method

The authors calculate the relic neutrino capture rate on tritium (ν_e + ³H → ³He + e⁻) using Fermi's Golden Rule with the same reduced Fermi and Gamow-Teller matrix elements that reproduce the measured tritium half-life of 12.33 years. They then scale this per-atom capture rate by the effective tritium mass of KATRIN (20 μg, corresponding to 2×10¹⁸ T₂ molecules) and by the local overdensity n_{ν,e}/⟨n_{ν,e}⟩ relative to the cosmological average of 56 electron-neutrinos per cm³. The feasibility of increasing the source strength is evaluated against constraints from magnetic flux conservation, electron scattering in the source, and the energy resolution requirement of ΔE ≈ 0.93 eV.

## Key numbers

- Average relic electron-neutrino density today: 56 cm⁻³ (for T_0 = 1.95 K)
- Capture rate per tritium atom per year: 4.2 × 10⁻²⁵ × (n_{ν,e}/⟨n_{ν,e}⟩) [year⁻¹]
- KATRIN effective tritium mass: 20 μg, corresponding to 2 × 10¹⁸ T₂ molecules
- Capture rate at KATRIN for average density: 1.7 × 10⁻⁶ year⁻¹ (one count every 590,000 years)
- Capture rate at KATRIN for optimistic overdensity of 10⁶: 1.7 counts per year
- Capture rate with 2 mg tritium source and overdensity of 10⁶: 170 counts per year
- Tritium Q-value: 18.562 keV
- KATRIN energy resolution: ΔE = 0.93 eV
- Expected upper limit on electron neutrino mass from KATRIN: ≤ 0.2 eV (90% C.L.)
- Magnetic flux at source: 53 cm² × 3.6 Tesla ≈ 190 Tesla·cm²
- Magnetic flux at spectrometer: 63.6 m² × 3 Gauss ≈ 190 Tesla·cm²
- Accepted space angle at source for 1 eV resolution: ΔΩ/(2π) = 0.005 (0.5%), corresponding to a cone opening angle of 5.7°

## Context

This work extends earlier proposals for detecting relic neutrinos via induced beta decay on tritium, as discussed by Faessler et al. (previous work), Lazauskas, Vogel, and Volpe (2008), and Ringwald and Wong (2004). The paper critically examines the practical limitations of the KATRIN experiment, which is primarily designed to measure the electron neutrino mass with a sensitivity of 0.2 eV. The authors show that gravitational clustering estimates for the local neutrino overdensity vary widely from 10² to 10⁶, depending on whether clustering occurs on galaxy scales (Ringwald and Wong) or galaxy cluster scales (Lazauskas et al.). The analysis enables future work to set an upper limit on the local relic neutrino density even if direct detection remains impossible, and it identifies the specific spectrometer modifications (increased source area, reduced source magnetic field, high-voltage transport channel) that would be needed for a detection, while concluding these are not presently feasible.
