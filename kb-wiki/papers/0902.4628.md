# Possible stimulation of nuclear alpha-decay by superfluid helium

> It is suggested that superfluid helium (condensate of 4-He atoms) may stimulate nuclear alpha-decay in a situation when an alpha-emitter moves through superfluid helium with fine-tuned velocity, so that the backward-emitted alpha-particle is at rest in the laboratory frame. It is shown that the probability of stimulated alpha-decay in this case may be sizable enough to be detected.

# Possible stimulation of nuclear alpha-decay by superfluid helium

## Key claim
Superfluid helium may stimulate nuclear alpha-decay when an alpha-emitter moves through the superfluid with a velocity fine-tuned so that the backward-emitted alpha particle is at rest in the laboratory frame, but the effect is only sizable for nuclei with lifetimes in the range of 10^-7 to 10^-5 seconds, and the absolute probability of stimulated decay during transit through a ~10 cm layer is at most 10^-2.

## Method
The author calculates the ratio of stimulated to spontaneous alpha-decay probability by considering that the condensate of 4-He atoms (with density n_c ~ 0.1n) provides N_c occupied final states for the emitted alpha particle. Using Fermi's golden rule and a symmetrized N+1 particle wavefunction, the matrix element for emission into the condensate state is enhanced by a factor of sqrt(N_c+1). The dimensionless parameter b = n_c * lambda_alpha^2 * v_alpha * tau_sp determines whether stimulated decay dominates, where lambda_alpha is the de Broglie wavelength of the emitted alpha, v_alpha its velocity, and tau_sp the spontaneous lifetime. The probability during transit through a helium layer of thickness x is then P_st = w_st * x / v_alpha.

## Key numbers
- Condensate density of 4-He atoms: n_c = 0.22 * 10^22 cm^-3 (taken as 10% of total liquid helium density n = 2.2 * 10^22 cm^-3)
- For 212Po (E_alpha = 8.79 MeV, tau_sp = 4.3 * 10^-7 s): b = 0.46, P_sp = 2.3 * 10^-2, P_st = 4.3 * 10^-3
- For 213Po (E_alpha = 8.38 MeV, tau_sp = 0.6 * 10^-5 s): b = 6.6, P_sp = 1.6 * 10^-3, P_st = 2.3 * 10^-3
- For 214Po (E_alpha = 7.69 MeV, tau_sp = 2.4 * 10^-4 s): b = 2.7 * 10^2, P_sp = 4.4 * 10^-5, P_st = 4.9 * 10^-4
- For 213At (E_alpha = 9.08 MeV, tau_sp = 1.8 * 10^-7 s): b = 0.19, P_sp = 5.3 * 10^-2, P_st = 4.6 * 10^-3
- For 214At (E_alpha = 8.81 MeV, tau_sp = 0.8 * 10^-6 s): b = 0.85, P_sp = 1.2 * 10^-2, P_st = 3.9 * 10^-3
- All calculations use layer thickness x = 20 cm and condensate density n_c = 0.22 * 10^22 cm^-3
- The parameter b ~ 1 corresponds to tau_sp ~ 10^-6 s
- For tau_sp >> 10^-6 s (b >> 1), w_st/w_sp ~ sqrt(b/2); for tau_sp ~ 10^-5 s, P_st ~ 10^-2 for x ~ 10 cm

## Context
This work extends the concept of stimulated emission from quantum optics and Bose-Einstein condensation to nuclear alpha-decay, proposing that the macroscopic occupation of a single quantum state in superfluid helium (related to the Bose-Einstein condensation discovered in dilute gases by Cornell (2002) and Ketterle (2002)) can enhance nuclear decay rates. The author notes that neutron scattering experiments (Snow, 1995; Bogoyavlenskii, 1992; Giorgini, 1992; Balibar, 2003) show approximately 10% of 4-He atoms occupy the lowest state at T < 1 K. The proposed effect would enable experimental study of environmental influence on nuclear processes with MeV-scale energy release using only thermal-scale (10^-4 eV) perturbations, and could be tested by comparing alpha-decay probabilities above and below the lambda transition temperature T_lambda = 2.17 K.
