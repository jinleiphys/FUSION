# Gravitational wave emission from oscillating millisecond pulsars

> Neutron stars undergoing r-mode oscillation emit gravitational radiation that might be detected on earth. For known millisecond pulsars the observed spindown rate imposes an upper limit on the possible gravitational wave signal of these sources. Taking into account the physics of r-mode evolution, we show that only sources spinning at frequencies above a few hundred Hertz can be unstable to r-modes, and we derive a more stringent universal r-mode spindown limit on their gravitational wave signal, exploiting the fact that the r-mode saturation amplitude is insensitive to the structural properties of individual sources. We find that this refined bound limits the gravitational wave strain from millisecond pulsars to values below the detection sensitivity of next-generation detectors. Young sources are therefore a more promising option for the detection of gravitational waves emitted by r-modes and to probe the interior composition of compact stars in the near future.

## Key claim

The paper derives a more restrictive "universal r-mode spindown limit" on gravitational wave strain from millisecond pulsars, showing that for known sources this refined bound lies below the detection sensitivity of next-generation detectors like advanced LIGO, contrary to what the standard spindown limit might suggest.

## Method

The authors use an effective description of r-mode evolution that absorbs complicated interior physics of neutron stars into a few power-law parameters, then exploit the fact that the r-mode saturation amplitude is insensitive to individual source properties (mass, radius). They combine timing data from the entire ATNF pulsar database with this universal saturation behavior to derive a global upper bound on gravitational wave strain, and also compute a minimum spin frequency below which r-modes cannot be present due to cooling out of the instability region.

## Key numbers

* The strongest bound on r-mode saturation amplitude is $\alpha_{{\rm sat}}\lesssim1.2\times10^{-7}$ for the 533 Hz pulsar J0034-0534, which has spindown rate $\dot{f}\approx-1.4\times10^{-15}\,{\rm s}^{-2}$.
* In the constant saturation amplitude toy model, r-mode gravitational wave emission can only occur in sources spinning at frequencies $f\gtrsim225\,{\rm Hz}$, corresponding to gravitational wave frequencies $\nu\gtrsim300\,{\rm Hz}$.
* For the mode-coupling saturation mechanism, r-modes are only present in sources with spin frequencies $f\gtrsim160\,{\rm Hz}$, corresponding to gravitational wave frequencies $\nu\gtrsim215\,{\rm Hz}$.
* The universal spindown limit for the constant saturation model scales as $(f/f_{0})^{3}$ relative to the source with the strongest bound.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous effective description of r-mode spindown evolution (Alford 2012, Alford 2013) to old millisecond pulsars that have been spun up by accretion, complementing their earlier analysis of young pulsars. It contradicts the optimistic expectation from standard spindown limits that advanced LIGO could detect r-mode gravitational waves from known millisecond pulsars, showing instead that young sources (such as those associated with supernova remnants like SN 1987A) are a more promising option. The paper enables future searches to focus on young neutron stars rather than recycled millisecond pulsars for detecting r-mode gravitational radiation.
