# Statistical theory of deformation distributions in nuclear spectra

> The dependence of the nuclear level density on intrinsic deformation is an important input to dynamical nuclear processes such as fission. Auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) method is a powerful method for computing nuclear level densities. However, the statistical distribution of intrinsic shapes is not readily accessible due to the formulation of AFMC in a spherical configuration-interaction shell-model approach. Instead, theory of deformation up to now has largely relied on a mean-field approximation which breaks rotational symmetry. We show here how the distributions of the intrinsic quadrupole deformation parameters can be calculated within the AFMC method, and present results for a chain of even-mass samarium nuclei ($^{148}$Sm, $^{150}$Sm, $^{152}$Sm, $^{154}$Sm) which includes spherical, transitional, and strongly deformed isotopes. The method relies on a Landau-like expansion of the Helmholtz free energy in invariant polynomials of the quadrupole tensor. We find that an expansion to fourth order provides an excellent description of the AFMC results.

## Key claim

The authors show that the intrinsic-frame quadrupole deformation distribution can be extracted from auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) calculations performed in a spherical configuration-interaction shell-model basis, using a Landau-like expansion of the Helmholtz free energy in quadrupole invariants to fourth order, which they find provides an excellent description of the AFMC results for the samarium isotopes studied.

## Method

The authors compute the laboratory-frame distribution of the axial quadrupole operator \(\hat Q_{20}\) using the AFMC method, then extract the second, third, and fourth moments of this distribution. They relate these moments to the expectation values of rotationally invariant quadrupole tensors, which are expressed in terms of intrinsic deformation parameters \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\). The logarithm of the intrinsic-frame probability distribution \(P(T,\beta,\gamma)\) is expanded in quadrupole invariants to fourth order, yielding a Landau-like form with three temperature-dependent parameters \(a(T)\), \(b(T)\), and \(c(T)\); these parameters are determined by matching the invariants computed from the Landau distribution to those obtained from the AFMC moments. The state density as a function of excitation energy and intrinsic deformation is then obtained via a saddle-point approximation of the inverse Laplace transform of the shape-dependent partition function.

## Key numbers

* The Landau expansion parameters \(a, b, c\) are determined for four even-mass samarium isotopes: \(^{148}\)Sm, \(^{150}\)Sm, \(^{152}\)Sm, and \(^{154}\)Sm.
* The first-order shape transition temperatures (where \(\tau = ac/b^2 = 1/4\)) are found to be \(T = 0.81\) MeV for \(^{150}\)Sm, \(T = 1.03\) MeV for \(^{152}\)Sm, and \(T = 1.29\) MeV for \(^{154}\)Sm.
* The corresponding HFB transition temperatures from Ref. Gilbreth (2018) are \(T = 0.74\) MeV, \(T = 0.94\) MeV, and \(T = 1.10\) MeV, respectively.
* The AFMC calculations use an imaginary-time slice of \(\Delta\beta = 1/64\) MeV\(^{-1}\) and 5120 Monte Carlo samples (128 independent walkers of 40 samples each).
* The integrals over intrinsic deformation use a \(20 \times 20\) mesh extending up to \(\beta_{\mathrm{max}} = 0.3\).
* The spline fits for \(a, b, c\) use 7 (\(^{150}\)Sm), 10 (\(^{148,152}\)Sm), or 11 (\(^{154}\)Sm) spline segments.

## Context

This work extends the AFMC method for computing nuclear level densities (Nakada 1997, Alhassid 1999) to include dependence on intrinsic quadrupole deformation, which is essential for fission modeling. Previous calculations of deformation-dependent level densities relied on mean-field approximations that break rotational symmetry, while the present method preserves rotational invariance within the configuration-interaction shell-model framework. The approach builds on earlier work by Alhassid (2014) and Gilbreth (2018) that extracted deformation signatures from laboratory-frame quadrupole moments, and on the Landau theory of shape transitions developed by Levit (1984) and Alhassid (1986). The method enables microscopic calculation of state densities as a function of both excitation energy and intrinsic deformation parameters \(\beta, \gamma\) for nuclei across the spherical-to-deformed transition.
