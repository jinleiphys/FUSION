# Snowmass white paper: Need for amplitude analysis in the discovery of new hadrons

> We highlight the need for the development of comprehensive amplitude analysis methods to further our understanding of hadron spectroscopy. Reaction amplitudes constrained by first principles of $S$-matrix theory and by QCD phenomenology are needed to extract robust interpretations of the data from experiments and from lattice calculations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that comprehensive amplitude analysis methods, constrained by S-matrix principles and QCD phenomenology, are essential for extracting robust interpretations of hadron spectroscopy data from experiments and lattice calculations, but acknowledges that implementing all constraints simultaneously is extremely difficult and must be approached on a case-by-case basis.

## Method

The Joint Physics Analysis Center (JPAC) develops phenomenological amplitude parametrizations that respect S-matrix principles (unitarity, analyticity, crossing symmetry) as much as possible within a given kinematic domain, then fits these amplitudes to experimental data. For light hadrons, the approach uses the N/D formalism and coupled-channel analyses to separate resonance physics from background processes, while for heavy hadrons near thresholds, the method examines pole positions on different Riemann sheets to distinguish between genuine resonances, bound states, and virtual states.

## Key numbers

- The lightest glueball in pure Yang-Mills is expected around 1.5 to 2 GeV
- Four scalar and three tensor resonances were identified in J/psi radiative decays to pi0 pi0 and KS KS, with the f0(1710) showing affinity to the gluon-rich initial state and coupling to K Kbar larger by one order of magnitude than other scalars
- The Pc(4312) peaks approximately 5 MeV below the Sigma_c+ Dbar0 threshold
- The pi1(1600) hybrid candidate appears approximately 200 MeV heavier than the pi1(1400) in the rho pi and eta' pi channels
- Dispersive analyses of light mesons are usually applicable only up to approximately 1 GeV

## Context

This white paper extends the work of the Joint Physics Analysis Center (JPAC) and addresses the need for improved analysis methods following the discovery of unexpected exotic hadron resonances (tetraquarks, pentaquarks, molecules, and hadrons with gluonic degrees of freedom) that challenge the minimal quark model. The paper builds on prior dispersive approaches (Roy 1971, Hite 1973) and unitarized chiral perturbation theory (Oller 2020) while enabling future coupled-channel analyses of high-statistics data from COMPASS, BESIII, LHCb, and Belle II to resolve longstanding debates about the existence and nature of states like the pi1 hybrids and XYZ particles.
