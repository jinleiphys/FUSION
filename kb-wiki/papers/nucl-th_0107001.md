# The effect of finite-range interactions in classical transport theory

> The effect of scattering with non-zero impact parameters between consituents in relativistic heavy ion collisions is investigated. In solving the relativistic Boltzmann equation, the characteristic range of the collision kernel is varied from approximately one fm to zero while leaving the mean-free path unchanged. Modifying this range is shown to significantly affect spectra and flow observables. The finite range is shown to provide effective viscosities, shear, bulk viscosity and heat conductivity, with the viscous coefficients being proportional to the square of the interaction range.

## Key claim

Finite-range interactions in the collision kernel of the relativistic Boltzmann equation generate effective shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, and heat conductivity that scale with the square of the interaction range, and these viscous effects significantly alter final-state observables such as spectra and elliptic flow in relativistic heavy ion collisions, but only when the sampling factor is small (cascade limit), because large sampling factors effectively reduce the interaction range to zero.

## Method

The authors introduced a finite spatial range into the collision kernel of the relativistic Boltzmann equation while keeping the mean free path fixed by adjusting the cross section. They compared four different numerical implementations of the Boltzmann equation (ZPC, GROMIT-t, MPC, GROMIT-τ) using identical initial conditions based on a thermal Bjorken geometry with pions and protons, varying the oversampling factor λ from 1 to 32. They also derived analytic expressions for the viscous coefficients by considering energy transfer between particles separated by a finite distance r in a velocity gradient or temperature gradient.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity from finite-range interactions: η = (m n Γ r²)/30, where m is particle mass, n is density, Γ is collision rate, and r is interaction range.
- Bulk viscosity from finite-range interactions: ζ = (m n Γ r²)/18.
- Thermal conductivity from finite-range interactions: χ = (n Γ C_r r²)/12, where C_r is the change in radial kinetic energy per particle per change in temperature.
- Initial particle densities in simulations: 7.64 pions per fm³ and 0.764 protons per fm³ at τ = 1.0 fm/c.
- Initial temperatures: 165 MeV for pions, 180 MeV for protons.
- Cross sections tested: 0, 10, 20, and 40 mb.
- Sampling factors tested: λ = 1, 2, 8, 16, and 32.
- Transverse ellipse radii for elliptic flow calculations: 5 fm and 2.5 fm.
- Spectra slopes varied by approximately 20 MeV between algorithms in the cascade limit (λ=1).
- Interaction range approximately 1.1 fm when cross section is 40 mb, while initial interparticle separation is approximately 1/3 fm.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of viscous effects in relativistic heavy ion collisions beyond the standard treatment of finite mean free path, which was previously linked to viscosity by Weinberg (1972). The authors show that finite-range interactions provide viscous coefficients that scale with density squared and are proportional to cross section, in contrast to mean-free-path viscosities which are density-independent and inversely proportional to cross section. The study enables future work to incorporate finite-range effects into Boltzmann simulations in a controlled way, and highlights that comparisons between different cascade codes (ZPC by Zhang, 1998; MPC by Molnár; GROMIT by the RHIC Transport Theory Collaboration) only agree when large oversampling factors are used to eliminate finite-range artifacts.
