# Nuclear structure investigation of even-even Sn isotopes within the covariant density functional theory

> The current investigation aims to study the ground-state properties of one of the most interesting isotopic chains in the periodic table, 94-168Sn, from the proton drip line to the neutron drip line by using the covariant density functional theory, which is a modern theoretical tool for the description of nuclear structure phenomena. The physical observables of interest include the binding energy, separation energy, two-neutron shell gap, rms-radii for protons and neutrons, pairing energy and quadrupole deformation. The calculations are performed for a wide range of neutron numbers, starting from the proton-rich side up to the neutron-rich one, by using the density-dependent meson-exchange and the density dependent point-coupling effective interactions. The obtained results are discussed and compared with available experimental data and with the already existing results of relativistic Mean Field (RMF) model with NL3 functional. The shape phase transition for Sn isotopic chain is also investigated. A reasonable agreement is found between our calculated results and the available experimental data.

## Key claim

The covariant density functional theory (CDFT), using the density-dependent meson-exchange DD-ME2 and density-dependent point-coupling DD-PC1 functionals, reproduces the ground-state properties of even-even Sn isotopes from the proton drip line to the neutron drip line with reasonable agreement to available experimental data, but the DD-ME2 functional shows the lowest root-mean-square deviation for binding energies and two-neutron separation energies compared to DD-PC1 and the older RMF(NL3) model.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent CDFT calculations for even-even Sn isotopes from 94Sn to 168Sn (neutron numbers N=44 to N=118) using two density-dependent effective interactions: the meson-exchange DD-ME2 and the point-coupling DD-PC1. They solved the Dirac equation for nucleons and the Klein-Gordon-type equations for meson fields in a harmonic oscillator basis, including pairing correlations via a BCS approximation. The calculated binding energies, two-neutron separation energies, two-neutron shell gaps, root-mean-square radii for protons and neutrons, pairing energies, and quadrupole deformations were compared with available experimental data and with predictions from the relativistic mean field model using the NL3 functional.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per nucleon: the root-mean-square deviation from experimental total binding energies is 1.7136 MeV for DD-ME2, 2.3180 MeV for DD-PC1, and 1.7937 MeV for NL3 (Table rms).
- Two-neutron separation energy: the root-mean-square deviation from experimental S2n values is 0.8667 MeV for DD-ME2, 1.0121 MeV for DD-PC1, and 1.0166 MeV for NL3 (Table rms_S2n).
- Quadrupole deformation: the prolate deformation in the neutron-rich region 90 < N < 110 saturates at a value close to beta2 approximately 0.3 (Figure beta).
- Neutron skin thickness: the difference between neutron and proton rms radii reaches 0.6 fm for 168Sn (Figure RnRp, right panel).
- Shell closures: strong drops in S2n and sharp peaks in the two-neutron shell gap are observed at N=50 and N=82 (Figures S2n and ShellGap).
- Pairing energy: Epair vanishes at N=50 and N=82, confirming shell closure at these magic neutron numbers (Figure PairingEnergy).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of Sn isotopes using the relativistic mean field model with the NL3 functional (Lalazissis 1999) and nonrelativistic approaches such as the Hartree-Fock method (Bassem 2015, 2016, 2017). The CDFT framework with modern density-dependent functionals DD-ME2 (Niksic 2008) and DD-PC1 (Lalazissis 2005) provides improved accuracy over the older NL3 functional, particularly for binding energies and separation energies. The results enable predictions for Sn isotopes far from stability where experimental data are unavailable, and they identify a shape phase transition from spherical to prolate deformed shapes in the neutron-rich region above N=90, followed by a return to sphericity above N=110.
