# Completeness Rules for Spin Observables in Pseudoscalar Meson Photoproduction

> The number and type of measurements needed to ascertain the amplitudes for pseudoscalar meson photoproduction are analyzed in this paper. It is found that 8 carefully selected measurements can determine the four transversity amplitudes without discrete ambiguities. That number of measurements is one less than previously believed. We approach this problem in two distinct ways: (1) solving for the amplitude magnitudes and phases directly; and (2) using a bilinear helicity product formulation to map an algebra of measurements over to the well-known algebra of the $4\times 4$ Gamma matrices. It is shown that the latter method leads to an alternate proof that 8 carefully chosen experiments suffice for determining the transversity amplitudes completely. In addition, Fierz transformations of the Gamma matrices are used to develop useful linear and nonlinear relationships between the spin observables. These relationships not only help in finding complete sets of experiments, but also yield important constraints between the 16 observables for this reaction.

## Key claim

The paper claims that **8** carefully selected measurements (the differential cross section, three single-spin observables, and **four** double-spin observables) suffice to determine the four transversity amplitudes for pseudoscalar meson photoproduction without discrete ambiguities, which is **one less** than the 9 measurements previously believed necessary by Barker, Donnachie, and Storrow (BDS). The authors hedge that this result holds only when the four Type S observables are always measured and the double-spin observables are chosen appropriately; they do not claim that any set of eight measurements works.

## Method

The authors analyze pseudoscalar meson photoproduction using two distinct approaches. First, they solve directly for the magnitudes and phases of the transversity amplitudes, expressing the observables in terms of the amplitude magnitudes and relative phases. Second, they use a bilinear helicity product (BHP) formulation that maps the algebra of the 16 spin observables onto the algebra of Hermitian \(4\times4\) Gamma matrices, and then apply Fierz transformations of those Gamma matrices to derive linear and nonlinear relationships between observables. They test for discrete ambiguities by finding unitary linear and antilinear transformations that leave subsets of observables invariant.

## Key numbers

*   **8** measurements (4 Type S plus 4 double-spin observables) are sufficient to determine the four transversity amplitudes without discrete ambiguities, one fewer than the **9** measurements required by the BDS rule.
*   The 16 spin observables are classified into four sets of 4: Type S (single-spin), Type BT (beam-target), Type BR (beam-recoil), and Type TR (target-recoil).
*   For the explicit example given, the four double-spin observables used are \(G\), \(F\), \(L_x\), and \(T_x\), which together with the four Type S observables yield a unique solution.
*   The Fierz relations yield bounds such as \(1 + \Sigma^2 \geq P^2 + T^2\), \(1 + T^2 \geq \Sigma^2 + P^2\), and \(1 + P^2 \geq \Sigma^2 + T^2\), where \(\Sigma\), \(T\), and \(P\) are the three single-spin observables.

## Context

This work extends the classic analysis of Barker, Donnachie, and Storrow (BDS) (1975) on how many measurements are needed to determine the amplitudes for pseudoscalar meson photoproduction. It was stimulated by the observation of Keaton and Workman (1996) that the BDS rule does not always provide sufficient conditions for resolving all ambiguities. The paper provides explicit tables of complete sets of eight measurements and shows that the Fierz transformation method can be generalized to other reactions with more than four amplitudes.
