# The classification of diagrams in perturbation theory

> The derivation of scattering equations connecting the amplitudes obtained from diagrammatic expansions is of interest in many branches of physics. One method for deriving such equations is the classification-of-diagrams technique of Taylor. However, as we shall explain in this paper, there are certain points of Taylor's method which require clarification. Firstly, it is not clear whether Taylor's original method is equivalent to the simpler classification-of-diagrams scheme used by Thomas, Rinat, Afnan and Blankleider (TRAB). Secondly, when the Taylor method is applied to certain problems in a time-dependent perturbation theory it leads to the over-counting of some diagrams. This paper first restates Taylor's method, in the process uncovering reasons why certain diagrams might be double-counted in the Taylor method. It then explores how far Taylor's method is equivalent to the simpler TRAB method. Finally, it examines precisely why the double-counting occurs in Taylor's method, and derives corrections which compensate for this double-counting.

## Key claim

The paper clarifies two unresolved issues in Taylor's classification-of-diagrams technique: whether the simpler TRAB method (Thomas, Rinat, Afnan and Blankleider) is equivalent to Taylor's original method, and why double-counting of certain diagrams occurs when Taylor's method is applied in time-dependent perturbation theory. The authors show that double-counting arises from insufficient specification of the cut-structure of sub-amplitudes in channels other than the s-channel, and they derive correction terms to eliminate this over-counting.

## Method

The authors restate Taylor's topological classification method for Feynman diagrams, which sorts diagrams into five classes (C1 through C5) based on the types of r-cuts that can be made on them, and uses the Last Internal Cut Lemma to uniquely decompose diagrams into irreducible parts. They then compare Taylor's original approach with the simplified TRAB method to determine the conditions under which the two are equivalent, and analyze specific examples of double-counting in the NN-piNN system, including the example originally provided by Kowalski, Siciliano and Thaler (1979). Finally, they derive general correction terms that compensate for the double-counting by constraining the cut-structure of sub-amplitudes in channels other than the s-channel.

## Key numbers

- The condition for a unique latest r-cut in class C3 diagrams is given by 2(r-1) < m or r ≤ m, where m is the number of initial-state particles and r is the number of lines cut.
- For the case r ≤ m, a unique latest r-cut can always be found in each C3 diagram contributing to A^{(r)}_{n ← m}.
- The double-counting problem was first pointed out by Kowalski, Siciliano and Thaler (1979) in models of pion absorption on nuclei, specifically involving the crossed pole term in the piN t-matrix t^{(1)}.

## Context

This work extends the classification-of-diagrams technique originally developed by Taylor (1963, 1966) and addresses questions raised by Avishai and Mizutani (1983) regarding the equivalence of Taylor's method to the simpler TRAB approach used by Thomas and Rinat (1979) and Afnan and Blankleider (1980, 1985). The resolution of the double-counting problem enables confident application of the Taylor method to derive four-dimensional equations for systems such as NN-piNN, pion photoproduction, and pion absorption on the deuteron, without the need for time-ordering reductions that were previously used to circumvent the issue.
