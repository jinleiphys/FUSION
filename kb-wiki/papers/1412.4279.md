# Probing Dynamics of Phase Transitions occurring inside a Pulsar

> During the evolution of a pulsar, various phase transitions may occur in its dense interior, such as superfluid transition, as well as transition to various exotic phases of quantum chromodynamics (QCD). We propose a technique which allows to probe these phases and associated transitions by detecting changes in rotation of the star arising from density changes and fluctuations during the transition affecting star's moment of inertia. Our results suggest that these changes may be observable, and may possibly account for glitches and (recently observed) anti-glitches. Accurate measurements of pulsar timing and intensity modulations (arising from wobbling of star due to development of the off-diagonal components of moment of inertia) may be used to pin down the particular phase transition occurring inside the pulsar core. We also discuss the possibility of observing gravitational waves from the changes in the quadrupole moment arising from these rapidly evolving density fluctuations.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that rapid phase transitions (such as hadron-to-QGP or superfluid transitions) occurring inside a pulsar's core can produce observable changes in the star's rotation rate and wobbling, potentially explaining both glitches and anti-glitches, but only if the transition is a strong first-order one with very low bubble nucleation rates allowing a macroscopically large supercritical core to form before a single bubble nucleates and rapidly converts the entire core.

## Method

The authors model a strong first-order phase transition in a neutron star core using a Newtonian polytropic equation of state for the star's density profile, combined with quantum tunneling nucleation rates for bubble formation via O(4) symmetric instantons. They simulate the resulting density fluctuations from bubble nucleation and from topological defect formation (Z(3) domain walls and QGP strings for confinement-deconfinement transitions, and global strings for color-flavor locked phase transitions) using field theory simulations on lattices of size (7.5 fm)^3 and (15 fm)^3, tracking the time evolution of the moment of inertia components and quadrupole moment.

## Key numbers

- Fractional change in moment of inertia from a QCD phase transition in a core of radius R0 ≤ 0.3 km is less than 10^{-5}, using Eq. (1) with density change of about 30% and star radius R = 10 km
- For a superfluid transition with condensation energy density ≈ 0.1 MeV/fm^3, the core radius R0 may be as large as 5 km
- Critical density for hadron-QGP transition is ρ_c = 2.500 ρ_0, where ρ_0 ≈ 0.15 m_nucleon is nuclear saturation density
- At density ρ_nucl ≈ 2.502 ρ_0, exactly one bubble nucleates in a 300-meter radius core over one million years, with critical bubble radius R_c = 50 fm
- Neutron star mass at critical central density is M_1 = 1.564 M_0 (solar masses), increasing to M_2 = 1.567 M_0 when supercritical core reaches about 400 meters
- For bubble-generated density fluctuations in a 300-meter core with bubble radius r_0 = 20 meters, fractional change in moment of inertia ΔI/I ≈ 4 × 10^{-8}
- Off-diagonal components of moment of inertia and quadrupole moment from random bubble nucleation are of order 10^{-11} to 10^{-10} relative to initial moment of inertia

## Context

This work extends earlier discussions of phase transition effects on neutron star moment of inertia by Glendenning et al. (1997) by considering rapid, rather than continuous, phase conversion for strong first-order transitions, and by introducing the novel effect of density fluctuations during the transition. The scenario of a macroscopically large supercritical core nucleating a single bubble is analogous to Witten's (1984) discussion of the quark-hadron transition in the early universe. The paper enables a new observational probe: simultaneous measurement of pulsar timing changes and pulse intensity modulations (from wobbling) could distinguish between phase transition-driven glitches and the conventional vortex de-pinning model, and gravitational wave emission from rapidly evolving quadrupole moments may become detectable.
