# Neutron Star Inner Crust at Finite Temperatures: A Comparison Between Compressible Liquid Drop and Extended Thomas-Fermi Approaches

> We investigate the effects of temperature on the properties of the inner crust of a non-accreting neutron star. To this aim, we employ two different treatments: the compressible liquid drop model (CLDM) and the temperature-dependent extended Thomas-Fermi (TETF) method. Our systematic comparison shows an agreement between the two methods on their predictions for the crust thermodynamic properties. We find that the CLDM description can also reproduce reasonably well the TETF composition especially if the surface energy is optimized on the ETF calculation. However, the neglect of neutron skin in CLDM leads to an overestimation of the proton radii.

## Key claim

The compressible liquid drop model (CLDM) can reproduce the composition predictions of the more sophisticated temperature-dependent extended Thomas-Fermi (TETF) method for the neutron star inner crust at finite temperatures, provided the surface energy is optimized on the ETF calculation; however, the CLDM systematically overestimates proton radii because it neglects the neutron skin.

## Method

The authors compared two theoretical approaches for the inner crust of a non-accreting neutron star: the compressible liquid drop model (CLDM) and the temperature-dependent extended Thomas-Fermi (TETF) method. Both methods were applied to compute thermodynamic properties and nuclear composition across a range of densities and temperatures relevant to the inner crust. The CLDM surface energy was calibrated against the TETF results to assess the impact of this optimization on the agreement between the two methods.

## Key numbers

- The CLDM and TETF methods show agreement on predictions for crust thermodynamic properties, with the CLDM reproducing the TETF composition reasonably well when the surface energy is optimized on the ETF calculation.
- The neglect of neutron skin in CLDM leads to an overestimation of proton radii compared to the TETF results.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutron star crust properties by systematically comparing two widely used theoretical frameworks, the CLDM and the TETF method, at finite temperatures. The study builds on earlier work by Grams and collaborators (2022) and by Shchechilin and Chamel (2021) on the inner crust composition. The findings enable the use of the computationally simpler CLDM for astrophysical simulations, provided the surface energy is properly calibrated, while highlighting the need to account for neutron skin effects in future models.
