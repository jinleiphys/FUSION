# Nonmesonic weak decay spectra of $^4_Λ$He

> To comprehend the recent Brookhaven National Laboratory experiment E788 on $^4_Λ$He, we have outlined a simple theoretical framework, based on the independent-particle shell model, for the one-nucleon-induced nonmesonic weak decay spectra. Basically, the shapes of all the spectra are tailored by the kinematics of the corresponding phase space, depending very weakly on the dynamics, which is gauged here by the one-meson-exchange-potential. In spite of the straightforwardness of the approach a good agreement with data is acheived. This might be an indication that the final-state-interactions and the two-nucleon induced processes are not very important in the decay of this hypernucleus. We have also found that the $π+K$ exchange potential with soft vertex-form-factor cutoffs $(Λ_π\approx 0.7$ GeV, $Λ_K \approx 0.9$ GeV), is able to account simultaneously for the available experimental data related to $Γ_p$ and $Γ_n$ for $^4_Λ$H, $^4_Λ$He, and $^5_Λ$He.

# Nonmesonic weak decay spectra of $^4_\Lambda$He

## Key claim

The shapes of the one-nucleon-induced nonmesonic weak decay spectra of $^4_\Lambda$He are determined primarily by phase-space kinematics, with only a very weak dependence on the underlying dynamics of the $\Lambda N \to nN$ transition. The authors argue that this good agreement with data, achieved without including final-state interactions or two-nucleon-induced processes, may indicate that these effects are not very important for this hypernucleus.

## Method

The authors employed the independent-particle shell model (IPSM) with plane waves for the emitted nucleons, using a one-meson-exchange potential (OME) to describe the weak hypernuclear transition. The transition rates and spectra were derived from Fermi's Golden Rule, with the nuclear matrix elements factored out of the integrals due to their weak dependence on the center-of-mass momentum. The calculations used a $\pi + K$ exchange potential with soft vertex-form-factor cutoffs ($\Lambda_\pi \approx 0.7$ GeV, $\Lambda_K \approx 0.9$ GeV), and the results were compared to the Brookhaven National Laboratory experiment E788 data after applying the same detection thresholds and angular cuts as the experiment.

## Key numbers

- Proton detection threshold: $E^0_p = 40$ MeV; neutron detection threshold: $30$ MeV (used for comparison with E788 data)
- Acceptance-corrected number of detected protons: $\bar{\rm N}_p^{exp} = 4546$; neutrons: $\bar{\rm N}_n^{exp} = 3565$
- For the SPKE model (P3), the calculated rates for $^4_\Lambda$He are: $\Gamma_p = 0.179$ (units not specified, but in the same arbitrary units as the experimental values), $\Gamma_n = 0.012$, $\Gamma_{nm} = 0.191$, $\Gamma_{n/p} = 0.068$
- For $^4_\Lambda$H with the same SPKE model: $\Gamma_p = 0.005$, $\Gamma_n = 0.071$, $\Gamma_{nm} = 0.076$, $\Gamma_{n/p} = 2.70$
- For $^5_\Lambda$He with the SPKE model: $\Gamma_p = 0.281$, $\Gamma_n = 0.121$, $\Gamma_{nm} = 0.402$, $\Gamma_{n/p} = 0.431$
- Size parameters used: $b(^4_\Lambda$H) = $b(^4_\Lambda$He) = 1.65 fm, $b(^5_\Lambda$He) = 1.358 fm (taken from Itonaga (2002))
- The $Q$-value for the proton-induced decay: $\Delta_p = 170$ MeV; maximum proton energy: $E^{max}_p = 127$ MeV

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework developed by Bauer (2005, 2007) and Krmpotic (2008) for the asymmetry parameter to the calculation of full decay spectra. It directly addresses the Brookhaven E788 experiment by Parker (2007), which suggested that final-state interactions and two-nucleon-induced decays might be small for $^4_\Lambda$He. The authors compare their results with previous calculations using the PSVE model by Krmpotic (2003) and the PKE model by Sasaki (2002), finding that only the soft $\pi + K$ exchange potential (SPKE) can simultaneously account for the $\Gamma_p$ and $\Gamma_n$ data across all three $s$-shell hypernuclei ($^4_\Lambda$H, $^4_\Lambda$He, $^5_\Lambda$He). The work enables future studies to test whether the same simple framework applies to heavier hypernuclei or whether final-state interactions become important there.
