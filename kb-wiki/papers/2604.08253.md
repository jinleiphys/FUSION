# High-precision ab initio nuclear theory: Learning to overcome model-space limitations

> High-precision predictions of nuclear properties are a central objective of ab initio nuclear structure theory. However, state-of-the-art many-body methods rely on truncated model spaces to render the nuclear many-body problem tractable, which remains a major source of theoretical error in computations of nuclear observables. In recent years, machine learning, and artificial neural network approaches in particular, have emerged as a powerful data-driven framework for learning convergence patterns directly from ab initio calculations and enabling precision extrapolations beyond the reach of conventional schemes. This review focuses on model-space extrapolation methods developed for the no-core shell model and related many-body methods. We discuss machine learning extrapolation frameworks in comparison to conventional methods and assess their performance for energy spectra, radii, and electromagnetic observables, with particular emphasis on achievable precision and uncertainty estimates through statistical and correlation-based strategies. These developments establish machine learning as an increasingly important component of the precision toolbox in ab initio nuclear theory, enhancing the reliability and predictive power of ab initio nuclear structure calculations.

# High-precision ab initio nuclear theory: Learning to overcome model-space limitations

## Key claim

Machine learning extrapolation methods, particularly artificial neural networks, can overcome model-space truncation errors in no-core shell model calculations and enable high-precision predictions of nuclear observables with quantified uncertainties, but the reliability of these extrapolations depends critically on the quality and diversity of training data and the specific network architecture, with the TUDa approach converting the extrapolation problem into an interpolation task by training on few-body systems and then applying the same network to heavier nuclei.

## Method

The review compares three categories of model-space extrapolation methods applied to no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations: heuristic approaches using exponential or polynomial fits to energy convergence patterns at fixed harmonic oscillator frequencies; physics-motivated infrared extrapolations based on effective field theory that leverage the UV and IR cutoffs of the harmonic oscillator basis; and two machine learning approaches using artificial neural networks. The ISU approach trains neural networks with two input nodes (Nmax and hw) and one output node to learn the functional dependence of observables on model-space parameters, while the TUDa approach trains a larger network with twelve input nodes (four consecutive Nmax values at three different hw frequencies) to directly predict the full-space solution from truncated calculations, using training data from 2H, 3H, and 4He for 36 different SRG-evolved Hamiltonians.

## Key numbers

- The ISU approach originally proposed a single hidden layer with eight nodes, yielding 33 free parameters per network
- The TUDa FSPN uses three hidden layers with 48, 48, and 24 nodes respectively, amounting to over 4000 adjustable parameters
- Training data for the TUDa approach includes NCSM data for 2H, 3H, and 4He across 36 different SRG-evolved Hamiltonians and seven HO frequencies in a window from 12 to 32 MeV, resulting in more than 350,000 data samples
- The TUDa approach uses twelve input values (four consecutive Nmax steps for three different hw values) to predict the converged observable
- Depending on data quality and convergence pattern complexity, the ISU approach trains anywhere from a few hundred up to one million neural networks

## Context

This review extends the line of work on model-space extrapolation in ab initio nuclear theory, building on heuristic extrapolation methods (Roth 2009, Maris and Vary 2009, LENPIC collaboration 2021) and physics-motivated infrared extrapolations developed by Furnstahl, Hagen, and collaborators (2012-2015). The machine learning approaches discussed were pioneered by Negoita et al. (2018, 2019) and refined by Jiang et al. (2019) and Shirokov and collaborators (2024, 2025), with the TUDa approach developed by Knöll, Wolfgruber, and collaborators (2023-2025). The review enables future applications of these methods to electromagnetic observables and hypernuclei, while identifying open questions about uncertainty quantification and the need for more diverse training data for higher-order observables.
