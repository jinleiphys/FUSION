# Acceleration and Particle Field Interactions of Cosmic Rays I: Formalism

> The acceleration of ultra high energy cosmic rays is conjectured to occur through various interactions with the electromagnetic fields in different astrophysical objects, like magnetic matter clumps, besides the well-known shock and stochastic Fermi mechanisms. It is apparent that the latter are not depending on the particle's charge, quantitatively. Based on this model, a considerable portion of the dynamics, that derives a charged particle parallel to a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ and under the influence of a force $\mathbf{F}$, is assumed to be composed of an acceleration by a non-magnetic force $\mathbf{F}_{\parallel}$ and a gyromotion along $\mathbf{B}$ direction, plus drifts in the direction of $\mathbf{F}_{\perp}$. The model and its formalism are introduced. Various examples for drift motions and accelerating forces are suggested. The formalism is given in a non-relativistic version. Obviously, the translation into the relativistic version is standard. In a forthcoming work, a quantitative estimation of the energy gained by charged cosmic rays in various astrophysical objects will be evaluated.

## Key claim

The paper presents a formalism for cosmic ray acceleration that combines standard Fermi mechanisms (first- and second-order) with additional electromagnetic field interactions, claiming that the latter are necessary because Fermi mechanisms alone do not depend on particle charge and are too slow to reach ultra-high energies. The authors hedge that their model is given in a non-relativistic version, with the relativistic translation described as "standard" and deferred to a forthcoming quantitative work.

## Method

The authors decompose the motion of a charged particle in a magnetic field B under a general force F into three components: parallel acceleration by a non-magnetic force F_parallel, gyromotion along B, and drifts in the direction of F_perp. They derive equations of motion from the Lorentz force including gravitational terms, then expand around the Larmor radius and average over gyration periods to obtain guiding-center equations. Various drift velocities are calculated for spatially varying B, curved field lines, time-varying E, and time-varying B, and these are combined with the Fermi mechanism velocity increments (v ± 2u for second-order, βu for first-order) into expressions for total energy gain.

## Key numbers

- The GZK cutoff is given as 10^19.7 eV, above which protons lose energy via photopion production with CMB radiation.
- Black hole masses are cited as ranging from approximately (0.5–1.2) × 10^8 solar masses.
- The Alfven wave velocity is defined as v_A = B_0 / sqrt(4πρ), where B_0 and ρ are background magnetic field strength and matter density.
- The energy gained by a charged particle through electric field interaction alone (excluding Fermi mechanisms) is given as ε = 2 q r·E (Equation 12).
- For a particle entering a magnetic cloud with initial velocity v_i, the total energy including Fermi mechanisms is given by Equation 22, which contains terms proportional to m r_i · v_dot_i and q r_i · E_i.

## Context

This work extends the Hillas (1984) approach, which considered only magnetic field interactions with a special configuration where electric and magnetic forces are perpendicular, by allowing general electromagnetic field configurations and adding both electric field interactions and stochastic Fermi mechanisms. The formalism is intended to enable quantitative calculations of energy gained by cosmic rays in specific astrophysical objects (AGN, GRB, neutron stars, black holes) in a planned follow-up paper. The authors note that the second-order Fermi mechanism (Fermi 1949) and shock acceleration are included as essential components alongside the new field-interaction terms.
