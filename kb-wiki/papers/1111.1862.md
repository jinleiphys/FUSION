# Dispersion of longitudinal momentum distributions induced in fragmentation reactions

> On the basis of systematic measurements of fragmentation reactions, which provide a detailed overview on the velocity distributions of residual nuclei, an improved description of the kinematical properties of the fragmentation residues is established. This work is dedicated to the fluctuations of their momentum distributions. In contrast to previous investigations, limited to close-to-projectile fragments, we extended our study to the entire production range, down to the lightest observed fragments. In this context, beside the contribution of abrasion and evaporation processes, we considered the effect of the thermal break-up on the width of the momentum distributions. Using approximated theoretical descriptions of the different reaction stages, a new analytical formula for the variance of the momentum distribution is derived, which is well adapted to technical applications.

## Key claim

The paper derives a new analytical formula for the variance of longitudinal momentum distributions of fragmentation residues that is valid over the entire production range (from close-to-projectile fragments down to the lightest observed fragments), not just the close-to-projectile fragments that previous models addressed. The model incorporates contributions from abrasion, evaporation, and thermal break-up (multifragmentation), but explicitly excludes fission and fission-like processes.

## Method

The authors used systematic measurements of fragmentation reactions performed at the FRagment Separator (FRS) at GSI for three projectile-target systems: ⁵⁶Fe, ⁸⁶Kr, and ¹³⁶Xe. They developed an analytical model that calculates the momentum dispersion σ²_{p∥} by summing contributions from Fermi motion during abrasion, recoil from sequential evaporation, and (for fragments below a mass threshold) additional contributions from multifragmentation including Coulomb expansion. The model parameters were fixed using known nuclear properties (Fermi momentum, level-density parameter, break-up temperature) rather than fitted to data.

## Key numbers

- Fermi momentum formula: p_F = 281 · (1 − A_p^{−0.568}) MeV/c
- Average induced excitation energy per abraded nucleon: ΔE = 27 MeV
- Mean energy of one nucleon from Fermi motion: ⟨E_fermion⟩ = 20 MeV
- Inverse level-density parameter: K = A/a = 11 MeV
- Average consumed energy per evaporated particle: ε = 15 MeV
- Average thermal contribution to kinetic energy of evaporated neutrons or protons: E^{therm}_{kin} = 8 MeV
- Break-up temperature: T_bu = 5 MeV
- Volume expansion factor: V_exp = V_bu/V_0 = 1.5
- Momentum resolution (FWHM) of FRS: Δp/p ≃ 5·10⁻⁴
- Momentum acceptance of FRS: ±1.5%
- Angular acceptance of FRS: 15 mrad around beam axis
- Model validity range: from about 50 A MeV to at least 10 A GeV

## Context

This work extends the Goldhaber model (Goldhaber, 1974), which only described momentum distributions from Fermi motion for close-to-projectile fragments, and the Morrissey empirical formula (Morrissey, 1989), which was limited to small mass losses. The new model also incorporates ideas from de Jong et al. (1997) on evaporation effects and from Bauer (1988) on finite-temperature Fermi gas effects in multifragmentation. The formula enables realistic predictions of longitudinal momentum widths for fragmentation products across the full mass range, applicable to technical applications such as radioactive beam production and nuclear waste transmutation.
