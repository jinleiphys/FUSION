# Porter-Thomas fluctuations in complex quantum systems

> The Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) of random matrices has been widely employed to describe diverse phenomena in strongly coupled quantum systems. An important prediction is that the decay rates of the GOE eigenstates fluctuate according to the distribution for one degree of freedom, as derived by Brink and by Porter and Thomas. However, we find that the coupling to the decay channels can change the effective number of degrees of freedom from $ν= 1$ to $ν= 2$. Our conclusions are based on a configuration-interaction Hamiltonian originally constructed to test the validity of transition-state theory, also known as Rice-Ramsperger-Kassel-Marcus (RRKM) theory in chemistry. The internal Hamiltonian consists of two sets of GOE reservoirs connected by an internal channel. We find that the effective number of degrees of freedom $ν$ can vary from one to two depending on the control parameter $ρΓ$, where $ρ$ is the level density in the first reservoir and $Γ$ is the level decay width. The $ν= 2$ distribution is a well-known property of the Gaussian Unitary ensemble (GUE); our model demonstrates that the GUE fluctuations can be present under much milder conditions. Our treatment of the model permits an analytic derivation for $ρΓ\gtrsim 1$.

# Porter-Thomas fluctuations in complex quantum systems

## Key claim

The authors show that the effective number of degrees of freedom $\nu$ in the Porter-Thomas distribution of decay rates can vary from $\nu=1$ to $\nu=2$ depending on the control parameter $\rho\Gamma$ (level density times decay width), even when the underlying Hamiltonian is real and time-reversal invariant, contradicting the common assumption that $\nu=1$ always applies for such systems.

## Method

The authors construct a configuration-interaction Hamiltonian with two sets of Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) reservoirs connected by an internal bridge channel, where each GOE Hamiltonian is augmented by an imaginary energy $-i\Gamma/2$ on the diagonal to represent direct decays. They reduce the full Hamiltonian to a $4\times4$ effective Hamiltonian by treating the GOE states implicitly through self-energies, then compute the probability $P_b$ of decay through the second reservoir. The distribution of $P_b$ is sampled numerically for various values of $\Gamma_a$ and fitted to the Porter-Thomas distribution to extract the effective $\nu$ parameter.

## Key numbers

- The control parameter for the crossover is $\rho_{0a}\Gamma_a$, where $\rho_{0a}$ is the level density in the first reservoir and $\Gamma_a$ is the level decay width.
- For small $\rho_{0a}\Gamma_a \ll 1$, the distribution is consistent with $\nu=1$ Porter-Thomas.
- For $\rho_{0a}\Gamma_a \gtrsim 1$, the distribution approaches $\nu=2$ Porter-Thomas.
- The standard deviation of $|w_{kk'}|^2$ equals its expectation value, given by $2\pi v_k^2 v_{k'}^2 \rho_{0g}/\Gamma_g$, which matches the $\nu=2$ distribution where the standard deviation equals the expectation value (compared to $\nu=1$ where the standard deviation is twice the expectation value).
- The phenomenological fit to the extracted $\nu$ values is $\nu(y) = (1+8.28y^2)/(1+3.81y^2)$ with $y = \rho_{0a}\Gamma_a$.
- Numerical simulations used GOE spaces of dimension $N_g=100$ with Hamiltonian parameters $v_g=v_k=v_{k'}=\Gamma_g=0.1$ and hopping matrix elements $t_i=1$.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous model (Hagino and Bertsch, Ref. verA) originally constructed to test the validity of transition-state theory (Rice-Ramsperger-Kassel-Marcus theory in chemistry). It contradicts the common assumption that time-reversal-invariant Hamiltonians always yield $\nu=1$ Porter-Thomas fluctuations, and shows that coupling to decay channels can produce $\nu=2$ fluctuations normally associated with the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE). The crossover from $\nu=1$ to $\nu=2$ has been studied in random matrix models interpolating between GOE and GUE (Alhassid, 1998; Alhassid, 2000), but those studies did not relate the complex matrix elements to physical quantities when the underlying Hamiltonian is purely real. The model may help explain discrepancies in estimating the effective number of channels in unimolecular reactions (Pollak, 1990; Miller, 1990), where theoretical calculations were found to be off by a factor of two.
