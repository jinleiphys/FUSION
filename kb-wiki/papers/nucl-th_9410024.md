# Nuclear transparencies for nucleons, knocked-out under various semi-inclusive conditions

> Using hadron dynamics we calculate nuclear transparencies for protons, knocked-out in high-$Q^2$, semi-inclusive reactions. Predicted transparencies are, roughly half a standard deviation above the NE18 data. The latter contain the effects of binned proton missing momenta and mass, and of finite detector acceptances. In order to test sensitivity we compare computed transparencies without restrictions and the same with maximal cuts for missing momenta and the electron energy loss. We find hardly any variation, enabling a meaningful comparison with data and predictions based on hadron dynamics. Should discrepancies persist in high-statistics data, the above may with greater confidence be attributed to exotic components in the description of the outgoing proton.

# Nuclear transparencies for nucleons, knocked-out under various semi-inclusive conditions

## Key claim

Using conventional hadron dynamics (Glauber theory with binary and ternary final-state interactions), the calculated nuclear transparencies for protons knocked out in high-Q² semi-inclusive (e,e'p) reactions lie roughly half a standard deviation above the NE18 data. The authors find that the computed transparencies are only marginally sensitive to maximal cuts on missing momentum and electron energy loss, enabling meaningful comparison with data; should discrepancies persist in higher-statistics data, they may with greater confidence be attributed to exotic components such as color transparency rather than to shortcomings of conventional hadron dynamics.

## Method

The authors compute the semi-inclusive nuclear response per nucleon using a factorized Glauber approach, where the knocked-out proton's final-state interactions with the residual core are described by an eikonal profile function built from measured NN elastic scattering parameters (total and inelastic cross sections, ratio of real to imaginary parts, and interaction range). They evaluate the unrestricted transparency T (Eq. a15), the transparency after integration over missing momentum T^P (Eq. a16), and the transparency after additional integration over energy loss T^PE (Eq. a18), using nuclear densities and pair distribution functions for C, Fe, and Au targets. Binary and ternary collision contributions are treated in a cumulant expansion, and the authors compare results with and without cuts to test sensitivity to experimental binning and detector acceptances.

## Key numbers

- For C, Fe, and Au targets at Q² = 1.0, 3.0, 5.0, and 6.7 GeV² under NE18 kinematics, the unrestricted transparencies T are roughly half a standard deviation above the NE18 data.
- Changes from unrestricted T to T^P (integrated over missing momentum) are ≤ 2%, independent of nuclear species or Q².
- Changes from T to T^PE (additionally integrated over electron energy loss) amount to 4–6%.
- Ternary FSI contributions decrease the binary results by only a few percent.
- For Q² = 1.04 GeV² (q = 1.20 GeV), the pp total cross section is 36.6 mb, pp inelastic cross section is 12.6 mb, τ_pp = 0.30, and (Q₀)_pp = 0.84 GeV.
- For Q² = 3.0 GeV² (q = 2.40 GeV), the pp total cross section is 46 mb, pp inelastic cross section is 26 mb, τ_pp = -0.35, and (Q₀)_pp = 0.59 GeV.
- For Q² = 5.0 GeV² (q = 3.54 GeV), the pp total cross section is 42.4 mb, pp inelastic cross section is 27.1 mb, τ_pp = -0.37, and (Q₀)_pp = 0.56 GeV.
- For Q² = 6.7 GeV² (q = 4.48 GeV), the pp total cross section is 42.4 mb, pp inelastic cross section is 27.5 mb, τ_pp = -0.37, and (Q₀)_pp = 0.56 GeV.
- The binary FSI contribution computed from Eq. (a13b) with g (pair distribution function) is 15–20% of the PWIA result, whereas the approximation used by Nikolaev et al. (1993) gives only 5–6%.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for nuclear transparency developed by Kohama, Yazaki, and Seki (1992, 1993) and by Rinat and Jennings (1994), applying it to the NE18 experimental data from Makins et al. (1994) and O'Neill et al. (1994). The authors clarify that the transparency definition and calculation method must match the experimental conditions (cuts, binning, acceptances) to avoid confusion, and they critique the approximations used by Nikolaev et al. (1993) and by Benhar et al. (1992). The results enable a baseline for searching for color transparency effects at higher Q², though the authors note that the onset of color transparency is not expected for Q² as low as 1–1.5 GeV².
