# Multipole tomography of atomic nuclei with conserved symmetries

> We introduce two-body nuclear conditional probabilities that allow the definition of an intrinsic reference frame and the multipole moments of angular-momentum-$J$-conserving states. This enables the characterization of quadrupole deformations of states with $J\leq1/2$, which are not accessible via spectroscopic one-body quadrupole moments. We illustrate the method with nuclear density functional theory (DFT) calculations for $J=0$ states of $^{16}$O and $^{20}$Ne, the latter obtained by restoring rotational symmetry of prolate or oblate intrinsic configurations. We show that the two-body quadrupole shape characterizations are not equal to the one-body moments obtained from broken-symmetry states, mainly because of Pauli repulsion effects. Calculations of two-body multipole moments can be performed within various theoretical frameworks, but their experimental determination requires measuring two-body correlations.

## Key claim

The paper introduces two-body conditional probabilities and tomographic multipole moments as a way to characterize nuclear shapes for states with total angular momentum J ≤ 1/2, where conventional one-body spectroscopic quadrupole moments vanish due to selection rules. The authors show that these two-body shape characterizations are not equal to the one-body moments obtained from broken-symmetry states, primarily because of Pauli repulsion effects.

## Method

The authors define two-body conditional probabilities derived from the two-body local density matrix of an A-body nuclear wave function that conserves total angular momentum J. For J=0 states, they fix the position of one nucleon (the "observer") on the z-axis and compute the conditional probability density of finding another nucleon at a given position, which yields axially-symmetric tomographic densities. They illustrate the method using nuclear density functional theory (DFT) calculations with the Skyrme UNEDF1 functional, performing constrained Hartree-Fock calculations for ¹⁶O and ²⁰Ne and then projecting the intrinsic states to J=0.

## Key numbers

- For ²⁰Ne, the global (prolate) minimum occurs at intrinsic axial proton quadrupole moment Q^p_20 = +41.1 fm²
- For ²⁰Ne, an excited oblate configuration is found at Q^p_20 = -19.6 fm²
- The cumulative quadrupole tomographic moments for the spherical state of ¹⁶O show oblate proton moments of about -6 fm², illustrating the magnitude of the Pauli hole effect
- Tomographic moments are computed up to cumulative order n=10 for the quadrupole (λ=2) moments

## Context

This work extends methods previously applied to artificial atoms (Yannouleas 2000, Romanovsky 2006, Li 2006, Yannouleas 2023) to nuclear physics, providing a way to define an intrinsic reference frame for angular-momentum-conserving nuclear states. The approach contrasts with methods such as the Monte Carlo Shell Model (Otsuka 2022) and the pinhole algorithm in nuclear lattice effective field theory (Elhatisari 2017), where definitions of aligned states or intrinsic densities are not unique. The tomographic multipole moments can be evaluated within any many-body nuclear theory and constitute independent quantum observables that may serve as better characteristics of the initial state formed in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions.
