# Effective photon mass in nuclear matter and finite nuclei

> Electromagnetic field in nuclear matter and nuclei are studied. In the nuclear matter, because the expectation value of the electric charge density operator is not zero, different in vacuum, the U(1) local gauge symmetry of electric charge is spontaneously broken, and consequently, the photon gains an effective mass through the Higgs mechanism. An alternative way to study the effective mass of photon is to calculate the self-energy of photon perturbatively. It shows that the effective mass of photon is about $5.42MeV$ in the symmetric nuclear matter at the saturation density $ρ_0 = 0.16fm^{-3}$ and about $2.0MeV$ at the surface of ${}^{238}U$. It seems that the two-body decay of a massive photon causes the sharp lines of electron-positron pairs in the low energy heavy ion collision experiments of ${}^{238}U+{}^{232}Th$ .

## Key claim

The photon acquires an effective mass in nuclear matter and finite nuclei because the non-zero expectation value of the electric charge density spontaneously breaks the U(1) local gauge symmetry; the mass is about 5.42 MeV in symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density and about 2.0 MeV at the surface of 238U, which the authors suggest explains the sharp electron-positron pair lines observed in low-energy heavy-ion collision experiments.

## Method

The authors calculate the photon self-energy perturbatively in nuclear matter by evaluating the second-order S-matrix element for proton-proton interaction via photon exchange, using a proton field expanded in positive-energy solutions only (no antinucleons). They derive the effective photon mass from the Dyson equation under the on-shell condition for protons, obtaining an expression proportional to the scalar proton density. For finite nuclei, they apply a two-parameter Fermi model of the nuclear charge-density distribution to compute the effective mass at different radii. They also present an alternative derivation via the Higgs mechanism, showing that spontaneous symmetry breaking of the U(1) gauge symmetry in nuclear matter yields the same mass term.

## Key numbers

- Effective photon mass in symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}: about 5.42 MeV
- Effective photon mass at the surface of 238U: about 2.0 MeV
- For the finite nucleus 238U, the two-parameter Fermi model parameters: c = 6.874 fm, z = 0.556 fm, rho_c = 0.0635 fm^{-3}
- The fine structure constant used: alpha = 1/137

## Context

This work extends the study of electromagnetic fields in nuclear matter by proposing that the photon acquires mass through spontaneous symmetry breaking, analogous to the Higgs mechanism, due to the non-zero charge density of protons. The predicted massive photon mass of about 2.0 MeV at the surface of 238U is consistent with the neutral particle mass of 1.8 MeV suggested by Cowan et al. (1986, 1987) and Salabura et al. (1990) from electron-positron pair observations in 238U+232Th collisions, though the APEX experiment (1995) did not reproduce those sharp sum-energy lines. The result implies that the massive photon should be considered in relativistic mean-field calculations of nuclear structure and in the interpretation of heavy-ion collision data.
