# The link between rare earth peak formation and the astrophysical site of the $r$ process

> The primary astrophysical source of the rare earth elements is the rapid neutron capture process ($r$ process). The rare earth peak that is seen in the solar $r$-process residuals has been proposed to originate as a pile-up of nuclei during the end of the $r$ process. We introduce a new method utilizing Monte Carlo studies of nuclear masses in the rare earth region, that includes self-consistently adjusting $β$-decay rates and neutron capture rates, to find the mass surfaces necessary for the formation of the rare earth peak. We demonstrate our method with two types of astrophysical scenarios, one corresponding conditions typical of core-collapse supernova winds and one corresponding to conditions typical of the ejection of the material from the tidal tails of neutron star mergers. In each type of astrophysical conditions, this method successfully locates a region of enhanced stability in the mass surface that is responsible for the rare earth peak. For each scenario, we find that the change in the mass surface has qualitatively different features, thus future measurements can shed light on the type of environment in which the $r$ process occurred.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the rare earth peak in solar r-process abundances can be formed dynamically by a region of enhanced nuclear stability in the mass surface, but the specific shape and location of this stability feature depends on whether the astrophysical environment is a hot core-collapse supernova wind or a cold neutron star merger ejecta. The authors hedge that if no such mass surface feature is found experimentally, the peak could instead originate from fission fragments or from a mechanism not captured by their model.

## Method

The authors used the Duflo-Zuker mass model as a baseline, which produces no rare earth peak, and added a free mass term parameterized by coefficients a_N for each neutron number from N=95 to 115 and a Gaussian falloff in proton number centered at C. They applied the Metropolis Monte Carlo algorithm to vary these parameters, self-consistently recalculating beta-decay rates and neutron capture rates with the Hauser-Feshbach code CoH, and ran r-process simulations for both hot (supernova wind) and cold (neutron star merger) thermodynamic trajectories to match solar r-process residuals.

## Key numbers

* The Monte Carlo calculations converge in approximately 20,000 to 30,000 steps for each astrophysical trajectory considered.
* The falloff parameter f in the mass modification was fixed at f=40, and the initial center C was set to 60 (roughly the center of the rare earth peak in proton number).
* For hot r-process conditions, the relative minimum in the mass surface spans no more than 1 MeV from highest to lowest points along the Z=60 (Nd) isotopic chain.
* For very neutron-rich cold conditions, the mass differences span over 1 MeV between the highest and lowest points along the same chain.
* Under hot conditions, the local minimum in the mass surface relative to Duflo-Zuker occurs around N=100, 102, and 104; under cold conditions, it shifts to N=97, 99, and 101.

## Context

This work extends the dynamical rare earth peak formation mechanism proposed by Surman (1997) and Mumpower (2012a), which suggested a kink in neutron separation energies causes a pile-up of nuclei. The authors contrast their approach with the fission fragment hypothesis of Schramm (1971) and Goriely (2013), and note that some mass models like FRDM (1995) predict a feature while others like Goriely (2013b) do not. The method enables future mass measurements in the rare earth region to distinguish between core-collapse supernova and neutron star merger sites by comparing the depth and position of the predicted stability enhancement.
