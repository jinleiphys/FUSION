# Expansion rates at RHIC

> A detailed description of the temporal evolution of the thermodynamic fields in heavy ion collisions is presented within a hydrodynamic framework. Particular attention is devoted to the evolution of the collective flow fields and their space-time gradients.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the expansion rate of the fireball created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is generally larger than the local dilution rate, and that this difference increases with radial distance from the center, a nuance glossed over in the abstract which only mentions "evolution of the collective flow fields and their space-time gradients."

## Method

The study uses ideal hydrodynamics with boost-invariance and an optical Glauber initial condition, fitted to preliminary particle spectra from central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV. The evolution of thermodynamic fields (entropy density, temperature, radial velocity, expansion rate, and dilution rate) is computed for central collisions, and the spatial eccentricity and momentum anisotropy are tracked for non-central collisions, using an equation of state with a first-order phase transition at T_c=164 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Equilibration time tau_equ = 0.6 fm/c with initial entropy density s_equ = 110 fm^{-3} for central Au+Au at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV
- Critical temperature of the phase transition: T_c = 164 MeV
- Linear radial flow profile parameter xi = 0.07 fm^{-1} at freeze-out
- Expansion rate at tau=15 fm/c: 0.207 fm^{-1} at r=0, increasing to 0.247 fm^{-1} at r=10 fm
- Local expansion parameter alpha reaches alpha=3 at decoupling (freeze-out temperature T_d=100 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamic framework of Kolb et al. (2000) and Kolb et al. (2001) by providing a detailed space-time picture of the expansion dynamics at RHIC energies. The results enable quantitative studies of hard probe energy loss through the evolving medium, freeze-out dynamics, and resonance reconstruction, and they support the popular "blast wave" parametrization of Schnedermann et al. (1993) by showing that the radial flow profile becomes linear early in the evolution.
