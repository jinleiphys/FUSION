# Event-by-Event Simulation of the Three-Dimensional Hydrodynamic Evolution from Flux Tube Initial Conditions in Ultrarelativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> We present a realistic treatment of the hydrodynamic evolution of ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions, based on the following features: initial conditions obtained from a flux tube approach, compatible with the string model and the color glass condensate picture; event-by-event procedure, taking into the account the highly irregular space structure of single events, being experimentally visible via so-called ridge structures in two-particle correlations; use of an efficient code for solving the hydrodynamic equations in 3+1 dimensions, including the conservation of baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge; employment of a realistic equation-of-state, compatible with lattice gauge results; use of a complete hadron resonance table, making our calculations compatible with the results from statistical models; hadronic cascade procedure after an hadronization from the thermal matter at an early time.

## Key claim

The paper presents a unified, event-by-event hydrodynamic framework for ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions that combines flux-tube initial conditions (from the EPOS model) with a realistic cross-over equation-of-state, conservation of baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge, and a hadronic cascade afterburner; the authors claim that this approach can reproduce all essential soft observables from Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV, but they hedge that some features attributed to viscosity may instead be explained within ideal hydrodynamics when using a realistic formulation.

## Method

The authors generate event-by-event initial conditions from the EPOS flux-tube (string) model, which includes non-linear effects such as elastic and inelastic ladder splitting to mimic saturation. They perform a core-corona separation, where only high-density string segments are treated hydrodynamically, solving ideal 3+1D relativistic hydrodynamics with conservation of baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge, using a realistic cross-over equation-of-state (X3F) compatible with lattice QCD results. The hydrodynamic evolution is stopped at a hadronization temperature of 166 MeV, where the Cooper-Frye prescription converts the thermal matter into hadrons, which are then fed into the UrQMD hadronic cascade for the final non-equilibrium stage.

## Key numbers

- Hadronization temperature: 166 MeV, chosen as the end of the thermal phase, located in the cross-over transition region.
- Initial energy density maxima in Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV: well beyond 50 GeV/fm³, concentrated in longitudinal sub-flux-tubes.
- Transverse smoothing kernel width: σ⊥ = 0.25 fm, used to compute the energy-momentum tensor from string segments.
- Default initial collective transverse flow parameter: v₀ = 0.25, which gives a slight improvement of transverse momentum spectra compared to v₀ = 0.
- Equation-of-state parameter δ₀ = 0.15 for the X3F cross-over EoS, which reproduces lattice gauge results at zero chemical potentials.
- Particle yields (pions, kaons, protons) reproduced within 30% for pT values below 1.5 GeV/c, as reported for earlier calculations by other groups.

## Context

This work extends the event-by-event hydrodynamic approach pioneered by Spherio calculations using Nexus initial conditions, and replaces the older Nexus model with the more modern EPOS flux-tube framework, which has been optimized against pp, pA, and cosmic ray data. The paper enables a unified description of soft observables (spectra, elliptic flow, particle yields, and ridge correlations) within a single ideal-hydrodynamic framework, and it suggests that some features previously attributed to viscosity may be explainable with a realistic equation-of-state and event-by-event treatment. The approach contrasts with earlier calculations that used a first-order phase transition equation-of-state (e.g., those by the groups of Kolb and Heinz) and with statistical hadronization models that do not include hydrodynamic evolution.
