# Unified Cranking-Model Description of Bohr and VMI Approache

> A unified analysis based on the cranking model is presented, demonstrating that both the Bohr and Variable Moment of Inertia (VMI) models arise as limiting cases of this framework. This result resolves the apparent contradiction between the two models by showing that they correspond to different physical limits of the same formalism. In addition, this analysis explicitly reveals the contribution of Coriolis effects to the rotational energy and moment of inertia.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Bohr hydrodynamic model (moment of inertia proportional to deformation squared) and the Variable Moment of Inertia (VMI) model (linear dependence on deformation) are not contradictory but instead represent different limiting cases of the same cranking-model framework, valid under the condition of small centrifugal stretching where a second-order Taylor expansion justifies the linear approximation.

## Method

The authors apply the cranking model restricted to collective nuclear motion, decomposing the deformation parameter into static (centrifugal stretching) and dynamic (vibrational) components. They compute the static and dynamic contributions to the moment of inertia using the Inglis formula, then derive the total energy as a sum of a harmonic potential term and a rotational term, performing a second-order Taylor expansion to obtain an expression identical to the VMI form of Mariscotti et al. (1969).

## Key numbers

- Static moment of inertia: \(\Im^{static} = 3B\beta^{2}\), reproducing the Bohr model result
- Dynamic (Coriolis) contribution: \(\Im^{Dyn} = \eta = \frac{6\hbar}{E_{1}-E_{0}}\), where \(E_{1}-E_{0}\) is treated as a fitting parameter
- Total moment of inertia: \(\Im_{total} = 3B\beta^{2} + \eta\)
- VMI stiffness constant: \(C' = \frac{C}{36B^{2}\beta_{0}^{2}}\), independent of Coriolis effects
- VMI reference moment of inertia: \(\Im_{0} = 3B\beta_{0}^{2} + \eta\), which depends on Coriolis effects

## Context

This work resolves the long-standing contradiction between the Bohr hydrodynamic model (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975), which assumes moment of inertia proportional to \(\beta^{2}\), and the VMI model of Mariscotti et al. (1969), which found a linear dependence on deformation from fitting experimental data. The analysis extends the centrifugal stretching approach of Diamond (1964) by explicitly quantifying the Coriolis contribution, which Mariscotti et al. had noted as essential but did not detail. The formalism enables a unified treatment of ground-state bands and beta/gamma vibrational bands, explaining why their moments of inertia differ.
