# Schwinger mechanism revisited

> In this article, we review recent theoretical works on the Schwingermechanism of particle production in external electrical fields. Although the non-perturbative Schwinger mechanism is at the center of this discussion, many of the approaches that we discuss can cope with general time and space dependent fields, and therefore also capture the perturbative contributions to particle production.

## Key claim

This review argues that while the Schwinger mechanism is fundamentally a non-perturbative phenomenon requiring all-orders treatment of the external field, many modern approaches can also handle general time- and space-dependent fields and thus capture perturbative contributions to particle production, not just the non-perturbative tunneling component.

## Method

The authors review theoretical frameworks for particle production in external electrical fields, including the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism, Bogoliubov transformations, quantum kinetic equations, the Wigner formalism, lattice implementations, and the worldline formalism. They organize the discussion around a scalar field theory with quartic self-interaction coupled to an external source, using power counting to classify diagrams in the strong-source regime where the source strength scales as 1/g. They derive generating functionals for transition probabilities and show how inclusive observables simplify compared to exclusive ones due to unitarity.

## Key numbers

* The non-perturbative transition probability is proportional to exp(-pi m^2/eE), showing non-analytic dependence on the coupling e.
* The critical field for significant pair production is E_c = m^2/e, which for electrons surpasses experimentally achievable fields by several orders of magnitude.
* In the strong-source regime (j ~ 1/g), the vacuum survival probability scales as P_0 = exp(-a/g^2), where a is of order unity at tree level.
* The leading contribution to single-particle production probability scales as P_1 = exp(-a/g^2) * (b_1/g^2), with b_1 starting at order 1 in the strong-source regime.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on the Schwinger mechanism by Schwinger (1951) and earlier tunneling interpretations by Sauter (1931) and Heisenberg (1935). It connects to applications in heavy-ion collisions (Biro 1984, Kajantie 1985, Kharzeev 2006) and hadronic string decay (Andersson 1983), where chromo-electrical fields may be strong enough for observable pair production. The review enables future numerical simulations of particle production in realistic, space-time dependent gauge fields such as those described by the McLerran-Venugopalan model (1993).
