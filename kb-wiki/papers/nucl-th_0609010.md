# Space-time evolution of hadronization

> Beside its intrinsic interest for the insights it can give into color confinement, knowledge of the space-time evolution of hadronization is very important for correctly interpreting jet-quenching data in heavy ion collisions and extracting the properties of the produced medium. On the experimental side, the cleanest environment to study the space-time evolution of hadronization is semi-inclusive Deeply Inelastic Scattering on nuclear targets. On the theoretical side, 2 frameworks are presently competing to explain the observed attenuation of hadron production: quark energy loss (with hadron formation outside the nucleus) and nuclear absorption (with hadronization starting inside the nucleus). I discuss recent observables and ideas which will help to distinguish these 2 mechanisms and to measure the time scales of the hadronization process.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a scaling analysis of hadron attenuation data from semi-inclusive Deeply Inelastic Scattering (nDIS) on nuclear targets favors prehadron absorption (short formation time) over quark energy loss (long formation time) as the dominant mechanism for hadron suppression, but this conclusion is based on an indirect scaling analysis and requires confirmation from direct measurements like transverse momentum broadening.

## Method

The author performs a phenomenological scaling analysis of the multiplicity ratio R_M measured by the HERMES experiment for charged and identified hadrons on nitrogen, neon, and krypton targets at a beam energy of 27 GeV. The scaling variable tau = C * z_h^lambda * (1 - z_h) * nu is fitted to combined z_h and nu distributions, and the scaling exponent lambda is extracted by chi-squared minimization to distinguish between absorption models (lambda > 0) and energy loss models (lambda < 0). The analysis also compares predictions from the Lund string model for absorption and the BDMS formalism for energy loss against the experimental data.

## Key numbers

- R_AuAu ≈ 0.2 for hadron production in Au+Au collisions at RHIC, indicating strong suppression in hot nuclear matter
- At HERMES, the kinematic range is nu = 2-20 GeV and z_h = 0.2-1, giving hadron energies E_h = 2-20 GeV
- For a 10 GeV pion at HERMES, the long formation time estimate gives <t_h> ≈ 50 fm >> R_A (nuclear radius)
- In the Lund string model, <t_*> ≈ 4 fm < R_A and <t_h> ≈ 6-10 fm ≥ R_A at HERMES
- The prehadron-nucleon inelastic cross-section is sigma_*(nu) = 0.35 * sigma_h(nu), fitted to pi+ production on a Kr target
- The scaling exponent extracted from HERMES pion data is lambda_best ≈ 0.4 > 0, favoring absorption models
- The A-dependence fit gives alpha = 0.61 ± 0.14 for the power law 1-R_M = c A^alpha, compatible with 2/3 but excluding 1/3
- The Lund string model gives lambda ≈ 0.7 for the scaling variable

## Context

This work extends the debate between two competing frameworks for hadron suppression in nuclear media: quark energy loss models (Wang 2003, Arleo 2003) which assume hadron formation outside the nucleus, and nuclear absorption models (Accardi 2002, Kopeliovich 2004, Falter 2004) which assume hadronization starts inside the nucleus. The scaling analysis provides a new observable to distinguish these mechanisms, and the results favor short formation times consistent with prehadron absorption. This has direct implications for interpreting jet-quenching data in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, where assuming long-lived partons leads to a medium temperature estimate of T ≈ 400 MeV, while in-medium hadronization would lead to a different, presumably lower temperature. The paper also identifies future measurements at CLAS and the proposed Electron-Ion Collider as necessary to resolve remaining questions, particularly regarding heavy quark hadronization and the baryon anomaly.
