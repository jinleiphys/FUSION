# Electromagnetic Transition Form Factors of Baryon Resonances

> Recent experimental and theoretical advancements have led to significant progress in our understanding of the electromagnetic structure of nucleons ($N$), nucleon excitations ($N^\ast$), and other baryons. These breakthroughs have been made possible by the capabilities of modern facilities, enabling the induction of photo- and electro-excitation of nucleon resonances. Recent experimental advances have sparked notable developments in theoretical approaches. New theoretical methods have been tested and proven to be robust, marking the beginning of a new era in our understanding on baryons. We present a comprehensive review of progress in experimental data on $γ^\ast N \to N^\ast$ reactions. Additionally, we discuss various analyses and theoretical results. Some of these methods have matured in their predictive power, offering new perspectives on exotic hadrons with multiquark components. We place special emphasis on both the low-$Q^2$ and large-$Q^2$ regions to reinforce crucial physical constraints on observables that hold in these limits. Furthermore, we illustrate that the combination of lattice QCD with chiral effective field theory and quark models, respectively, proves beneficial in interpreting data and applying constraints within those different regimes. As a practical contribution and for future reference, we review the formulas for helicity amplitudes, multipole form factors and the relations between these two sets of functions for transitions to resonances with general spin $J \geq \frac{1}{2}$. These formulas are ubiquitous and play a pivotal role in experimental and theoretical studies on baryon structure. Notably, the multipole transition form factors for $J \ge \frac{3}{2}$ resonances serve as valuable tools to test perturbative QCD results in the large-$Q^2$ region, thanks to the correlations between electric and magnetic transition form factors.

## Key claim
This comprehensive review argues that the combination of lattice QCD with chiral effective field theory and quark models is beneficial for interpreting experimental data on electromagnetic transition form factors of baryon resonances, particularly in the low-$Q^2$ and large-$Q^2$ regions where crucial physical constraints hold. The authors emphasize that multipole transition form factors for resonances with spin $J \ge \frac{3}{2}$ serve as valuable tools to test perturbative QCD results in the large-$Q^2$ region, thanks to correlations between electric and magnetic transition form factors.

## Method
The authors present a comprehensive review of experimental data on $\gamma^\ast N \to N^\ast$ reactions, covering squared momentum transfers from $Q^2 = 0-0.01$ GeV$^2$ up to $Q^2 = 5$ or $8$ GeV$^2$. They compile and discuss formulas for helicity amplitudes and multipole form factors for transitions to resonances with general spin $J \geq \frac{1}{2}$, and compare results from various theoretical approaches including quark models (with or without meson cloud excitations), lattice QCD, Dyson-Schwinger equations, chiral effective field theory, the large $N_c$ limit, and AdS/CFT correspondence.

## Key numbers
- Squared momentum transfer range covered: from $Q^2 = 0-0.01$ GeV$^2$ up to $Q^2 = 5$ or $8$ GeV$^2$
- Light $\Delta$ baryons (isospin $\frac{3}{2}$, spin $\frac{3}{2}$) are about 30% heavier than the nucleon
- Constituent quark masses for light $u$ and $d$ quarks: approximately 200 to 350 MeV
- Strange quarks are approximately 150-200 MeV heavier than light quarks
- Nuclear medium densities where $\Delta$ baryons may mix with hyperons: approximately 2-3 times the nuclear saturation density

## Context
This review updates and complements previous valuable reviews on electromagnetic nucleon excitations, including those by Crede (2013), Aznauryan (2012), Burkert (2004), Capstick (2000), Eichmann (2016), Thiel (2022), and Pascalutsa (2007). The work is motivated by recent experimental advances at facilities including Jefferson Lab (CLAS), ELSA, MAMI, GRAAL, and SPring-8, which have enabled high-precision determination of electromagnetic couplings and the discovery of exotic tetra-quark and penta-quark hadrons. The review enables future work by providing a unified reference for formulas connecting helicity amplitudes and multipole form factors, and by establishing the current status of theoretical understanding of baryon electromagnetic structure across momentum scales.
