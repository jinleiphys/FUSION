# Tensor force and deformation in even-even nuclei

> The variational principle is used to build a model which describes open shell nuclei with ground state deformations. Hartree-Fock equations are solved by using single particle wave functions whose radial parts depend on the projection of the angular momentum on the quantization axis. Pairing effects are taken into account by solving Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer equations in each step of the minimisation procedure. The Gogny D1S finite-range interaction and an extension of it that includes tensor terms are consistently used in both parts of our calculations. The model is applied to study a set of isotopes with 34 protons and of isotones with 34 neutrons. Total energies, density distributions, their radii and single particle energies are analysed and the results of our calculations are compared with the available experimental data. We focused our attention on the effects of the deformation and of the tensor force on these observables. Our model describes open shell nuclei from a peculiar perspective and opens the possibility of future theoretical developments.

## Key claim
The paper claims that a Hartree-Fock-Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (HFBCS) model using single-particle wave functions whose radial parts depend on the angular momentum projection on the quantization axis can describe ground-state deformations in open-shell nuclei, but the authors acknowledge that the "optimal solution" (the deformation type giving the lowest energy) is often ambiguous, with energy differences between prolate and oblate solutions being at most 80 keV, close to numerical uncertainty, indicating shape coexistence rather than a uniquely defined ground-state deformation.

## Method
The authors solved Hartree-Fock equations using single-particle wave functions factorized into radial parts that depend on the projection of the total angular momentum on the z-axis and angular-spin parts given by spherical harmonics coupled with Clebsch-Gordan coefficients. Pairing was treated by solving Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer equations within each iteration of the minimization procedure, using the Gogny D1S finite-range interaction and its extension D1ST2a that includes tensor terms consistently in both the HF and BCS parts. The model was applied to 16 even-even selenium isotopes (Z=34) and 10 isotones with N=34, and results were compared with deformed Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations and spherical HF+BCS calculations from Anguiano (2014).

## Key numbers
- Energy differences between prolate and oblate solutions (δ_min) are at most 80 keV in absolute value for HFBCS with D1S; for HFB with D1S they are even smaller.
- Relative differences between HFBCS and HFB total energies per nucleon are smaller than 1% for both prolate and oblate deformations.
- Relative differences between D1ST2a (with tensor) and D1S (without tensor) total energies per nucleon are at most 2%.
- Average |δ_min| is about 17 keV for D1ST2a versus about 34 keV for D1S; for HFB with D1S the average is about 7 keV.
- Differences between HFBCS optimal solution total energies and experimental binding energies are at most 1% for D1S and about 2% for D1ST2a.
- The deformation parameter β₂ for ⁶²Ni is 0.002 (spherical) with D1S but -0.104 (oblate) with D1ST2a; for ⁷⁴Se it is -0.071 with D1S and 0.000 (spherical) with D1ST2a.

## Context
This work extends the deformed mean-field approach beyond the Nilsson model (Nilsson 1955) by making the deformed potential emerge self-consistently from an effective nucleon-nucleon interaction rather than being an external input. It builds on the spherical HF+BCS model of Anguiano (2014) by allowing the radial parts of single-particle wave functions to depend on the angular momentum projection, and it compares results with deformed HFB calculations from the Bruyères compilation (Capote 2009) and from Anguiano (2001a). The findings enable future theoretical developments incorporating beyond-mean-field correlations such as angular momentum and particle number projections, which the authors suggest may be necessary given the shape coexistence indicated by the small energy differences between prolate and oblate solutions.
