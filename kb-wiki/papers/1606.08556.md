# Thermal Relaxation, Electrical Conductivity and Charge Diffusion in a Hot QCD Medium

> The response of electromagnetic (EM) fields that are produced in non-central heavy-ion collisions to electromagnetically charged quark gluon plasma can be understood in terms of charge transport and charge diffusion in the hot QCD medium. This article presents a perspective on these processes by investigating the temperature behavior of the related transport coefficients, {\it viz.} electrical conductivity and the charge diffusion coefficients along with charge susceptibility. In the process of estimating them, thermal relaxation times for quarks and gluons have been determined first. These transport coefficients have been studied by solving the relativistic transport equation in the Chapman-Enskog method. For the analysis, $2\rightarrow 2$, quark-quark, quark-gluon and gluon-gluon scattering processes are taken into account along with an effective description of hot QCD Equations of state (EOSs) in terms of temperature dependent effective fugacities of quasi-quarks (anti-quarks) and quasi-gluons. Both improved perturbative hot QCD EOSs at high temperature and a lattice QCD EOS are included for the analysis. The hot QCD medium effects entering through the quasi-particle momentum distributions along with an effective coupling, are seen to have significant impact on the temperature behavior of these transport parameters along with the thermal relaxation times for the quasi-gluons and quasi-quarks

## Key claim

The authors compute electrical conductivity, charge diffusion coefficient, and charge susceptibility for a hot QCD medium by solving the relativistic transport equation using the Chapman-Enskog method, but the results depend significantly on which equation of state (EOS) is used to define the quasi-particle distributions and effective coupling, meaning the numerical predictions are not unique but rather EOS-dependent.

## Method

The authors employ the Effective Quasi-Particle Model (EQPM) to map hot QCD medium effects from three different equations of state (EOS1 at O(g⁵), EOS2 at O(g⁶ln(1/g)+δ), and a lattice QCD EOS) into temperature-dependent effective fugacities for quasi-gluons and quasi-quarks. They then solve the linearized relativistic Boltzmann transport equation in the Chapman-Enskog method, incorporating 2→2 elastic scattering processes (quark-quark, quark-gluon, gluon-gluon) with a transport weight factor (1−cosθ) to handle small-angle divergences, using an infrared cutoff k² = g²T². Thermal relaxation times for quarks and gluons are computed first from the interaction rates, and these relaxation times are then used to obtain the transport coefficients.

## Key numbers

- Thermal relaxation time for gluons: τ_g⁻¹ depends on terms with prefactors 9g⁴/(16π⟨s⟩_gg) for gluon-gluon scattering and g⁴/(4π⟨s⟩_gq) for gluon-quark scattering, with logarithmic factors ln(⟨s⟩_gg/k²)−1.267 and ln(⟨s⟩_gq/k²)−1.287 respectively
- Thermal relaxation time for quarks: τ_q⁻¹ depends on terms with prefactors g⁴/(4π⟨s⟩_qg) for quark-gluon scattering and g⁴/(9π⟨s⟩_qq) for quark-quark scattering, with logarithmic factors ln(⟨s⟩_qg/k²)−1.287 and ln(⟨s⟩_qq/k²)−1.417 respectively
- Thermal average gluon momentum: ⟨p_g⟩ = 3T × PolyLog[4,z_g]/PolyLog[3,z_g]
- Thermal average quark momentum: ⟨p_q⟩ = 3T × PolyLog[4,−z_q]/PolyLog[3,−z_q]
- Debye mass in the quasi-particle model: m_D² = 4πα_s(T)T² × (2N_c/π² PolyLog[2,z_g] − 2N_f/π² PolyLog[2,−z_q]), which reduces to m_D²(HTL) = α_s(T)T²(N_c/3 + N_f/6) in the ideal gas limit z_g,q → 1
- Number densities: n_g = (ν_g T³/π²) PolyLog[3, z_g] and n_q = (−ν_q T³/π²) PolyLog[3, −z_q]
- Pressure: P_g,q = ±(ν_g,q T⁴/π²) PolyLog[4, ±z_g,q]

## Context

This work extends previous quasi-particle model calculations of shear and bulk viscosities by Chandra et al. (2012) to the electromagnetic transport sector, specifically electrical conductivity and charge diffusion. It contrasts with lattice QCD approaches (Aarts 2007, Amato 2013, Brandt 2013, Francis 2015) that use Green-Kubo formulae and with holographic calculations (Finazzo 2014), while following the kinetic theory approach of Xu and Greiner (2008) and Greco et al. (2015). The results enable more realistic modeling of electromagnetic field evolution in non-central heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, and provide input for hydrodynamic simulations that include charge transport.
