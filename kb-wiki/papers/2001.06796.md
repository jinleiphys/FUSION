# Effective (kinetic freeze-out) temperature, transverse flow velocity and kinetic freeze-out volume in high energy collisions

> The transverse momentum spectra of different types of particles produced in central and peripheral gold-gold (Au-Au) and (inelastic) proton-proton ($pp$) collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC), as well as in central and peripheral lead-lead (Pb-Pb) and $pp$ collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) are analyzed by the standard distribution in terms of multi-component. The obtained results from the standard distribution give an approximate agreement with the measured experimental data by the STAR, PHENIX and ALICE Collaborations. The methodical behavior of the effective (kinetic freeze-out) temperature, transverse flow velocity and kinetic freeze-out volume with the mass dependence for different particles is obtained, which observes the early kinetic freeze-out of heavier particles as compared to the lighter particles. The parameters for emissions of different particles are observed to be different, which reveals a direct signature of the mass dependent differential kinetic freeze-out. It is also observed that the peripheral nucleus-nucleus ($AA$) and $pp$ collisions at the same center-of-mass energy per nucleon pair are close in terms of the extracted parameters.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the effective (kinetic freeze-out) temperature, transverse flow velocity, and kinetic freeze-out volume extracted from a two-component standard distribution fit to transverse momentum spectra reveal a mass-dependent differential kinetic freeze-out scenario, where heavier particles freeze out earlier than lighter ones. This conclusion is hedged by the observation that the extracted parameters are model-dependent, though the authors argue the mass dependence itself is not a model artifact.

## Method

The authors fitted transverse momentum spectra of pi-, K-, and antiprotons from central and peripheral Au-Au collisions at 62.4 GeV (STAR data) and Pb-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV (ALICE data), as well as pp collisions at both energies (PHENIX and ALICE data), using a two-component standard distribution (Boltzmann-Gibbs, Fermi-Dirac, or Bose-Einstein) with l=2 components. The effective temperature T was obtained as the weighted average T = k1*T1 + k2*T2, and the kinetic freeze-out volume V as V = V1 + V2. The kinetic freeze-out temperature T0 was extracted as the intercept of a linear fit of T versus rest mass m0, and the transverse flow velocity beta_T was extracted as the slope of a linear fit of average transverse momentum <pT> versus average moving mass <m>.

## Key numbers

- For central Au-Au at 62.4 GeV: T0 = 0.2769 +/- 0.006 GeV, beta_T = 0.3857 +/- 0.004 c
- For peripheral Au-Au at 62.4 GeV: T0 = 0.2022 +/- 0.004 GeV, beta_T = 0.3449 +/- 0.006 c
- For pp at 62.4 GeV: T0 = 0.1543 +/- 0.006 GeV, beta_T = 0.3567 +/- 0.006 c
- For central Pb-Pb at 5.02 TeV: T0 = 0.3593 +/- 0.006 GeV, beta_T = 0.4260 +/- 0.006 c
- For peripheral Pb-Pb at 5.02 TeV: T0 = 0.3293 +/- 0.005 GeV, beta_T = 0.4371 +/- 0.005 c
- For pp at 5.02 TeV: T0 = 0.3208 +/- 0.006 GeV, beta_T = 0.4048 +/- 0.006 c
- Average kinetic freeze-out volume in central Au-Au at 62.4 GeV: <V> = 2610 +/- 218 fm^3
- Average kinetic freeze-out volume in central Pb-Pb at 5.02 TeV: <V> = 10002 +/- 658 fm^3
- Average kinetic freeze-out volume in pp at 62.4 GeV: <V> = 77 +/- 10 fm^3
- Average kinetic freeze-out volume in pp at 5.02 TeV: <V> = 100 +/- 13 fm^3

## Context

This work extends previous studies of mass-dependent kinetic freeze-out scenarios reported by Chatterjee (2015) and others, and uses the linear relations T = a*m0 + T0 and <pT> = beta_T*<m> + b previously employed by Abelev (2008) and the authors' own prior work (Lao, 2017). The results confirm the differential freeze-out picture observed by the NA44 Collaboration and support the interpretation that peripheral AA collisions and pp collisions at the same center-of-mass energy per nucleon pair yield similar freeze-out parameters. The extracted kinetic freeze-out volumes and temperatures can inform models of the phase transition from hadronic matter to quark-gluon plasma and calculations of multiplicity, microcanonical heat capacity, and caloric curves.
