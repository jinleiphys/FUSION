# Conductance peaks in open quantum dots

> We present a simple measure of the conductance fluctuations in open ballistic chaotic quantum dots, extending the number of maxima method originally proposed for the statistical analysis of compound nuclear reactions. The average number of extreme points (maxima and minima) in the dimensionless conductance, $T$, as a function of an arbitrary external parameter $Z$, is directly related to the autocorrelation function of $T(Z)$. The parameter $Z$ can be associated to an applied gate voltage causing shape deformation in quantum dot, an external magnetic field, the Fermi energy, etc.. The average density of maxima is found to be $<ρ_{Z}> = α_{Z}/Z_c$, where $α_{Z}$ is a universal constant and $Z_c$ is the conductance autocorrelation length, which is system specific. The analysis of $<ρ_{Z}>$ does not require large statistic samples, providing a quite amenable way to access information about parametric correlations, such as $Z_c$.

## Key claim
The average density of maxima in the dimensionless conductance of open ballistic chaotic quantum dots is inversely proportional to the conductance autocorrelation length, with a universal proportionality constant that depends on the type of parametric variation (Lorentzian vs. squared-Lorentzian correlation function). This result is derived assuming Gaussian-distributed transmission derivatives, which is accurate in the semiclassical limit of a large number of open channels, but the paper shows numerically that even for moderate channel numbers the prediction gives results within about 10% precision.

## Method
The authors extend the "number of maxima method" originally developed for compound nuclear reaction statistics (Brink 1963) to open ballistic chaotic quantum dots. They derive an analytical expression for the average density of maxima ⟨ρ_Z⟩ in the dimensionless conductance T as a function of an external parameter Z, relating it to the second and fourth derivatives of the conductance autocorrelation function C_Z(δZ). They then test their analytical predictions using numerical simulations based on the Hamiltonian approach to the statistical S-matrix (Verbaarschot, Weidenmüller, Zirnbauer 1985), employing random matrix theory ensembles for the quantum dot Hamiltonian with perfect channel-resonance coupling.

## Key numbers
- For energy variations (Lorentzian autocorrelation): ⟨ρ_ε⟩ = √3/(πΓ) ≈ 0.55/Γ, where Γ is the conductance autocorrelation length in energy.
- For parametric Hamiltonian changes (squared-Lorentzian autocorrelation): ⟨ρ_X⟩ = 3/(π√2 X_c) ≈ 0.68/X_c, where X_c is the autocorrelation length for the external parameter.
- The numerical simulations show that even for moderate N (number of open channels), the semiclassical Gaussian prediction gives results within about 10% precision.
- The autocorrelation length for energy variations is given by the Weisskopf estimate: Γ = (N₁+N₂)Δ/2π = NΔ/π for the symmetric case N₁=N₂=N.
- For parametric Hamiltonian changes, the correlation length X_c scales as √N, shown numerically in the inset of Fig. 4.

## Context
This work extends the number of maxima method from compound nuclear reaction statistics (Brink 1963; Bizzeti and Bizzeti-Sona 1967) to open ballistic chaotic quantum dots, which are described by random matrix theory (Beenakker 1997; Alhassid 2000; Mello and Kumar 2004). The method provides a way to estimate the conductance autocorrelation length by counting maxima in a single conductance trace, avoiding the need for large statistical samples. This approach could be applied to other ballistic mesoscopic systems showing conductance fluctuations, such as graphene flakes (Ujiie et al. 2009; Tikhonenko et al. 2009; Ojeda et al. 2010), and potentially to diffusive systems as well.
