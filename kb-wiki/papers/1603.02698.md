# Masses, Radii, and Equation of State of Neutron Stars

> We summarize our current knowledge of neutron star masses and radii. Recent instrumentation and computational advances have resulted in a rapid increase in the discovery rate and precise timing of radio pulsars in binaries in the last few years, leading to a large number of mass measurements. These discoveries show that the neutron star mass distribution is much wider than previously thought, with 3 known pulsars now firmly in the 1.9-2.0 Msun mass range. For radii, large, high quality datasets from X-ray satellites as well as significant progress in theoretical modeling led to considerable progress in the measurements, placing them in the 9.9-11.2 km range and shrinking their uncertainties due to a better understanding of the sources of systematic errors. The combination of the massive neutron star discoveries, the tighter radius measurements, and improved laboratory constraints of the properties of dense matter has already made a substantial impact on our understanding of the composition and bulk properties of cold nuclear matter at densities higher than that of the atomic nucleus, a major unsolved problem in modern physics.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the current state of neutron star mass and radius measurements, claiming that the neutron star mass distribution is much wider than previously thought, with three known pulsars now firmly in the 1.9-2.0 solar mass range, and that radius measurements have been placed in the 9.9-11.2 km range with reduced uncertainties due to better understanding of systematic errors.

## Method

The authors compile and synthesize results from two primary observational techniques: radio timing of binary pulsars (including double neutron star systems and millisecond pulsar-white dwarf systems) to measure masses via Keplerian and post-Keplerian parameters, and X-ray observations of neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries to measure radii. Mass measurements rely on precise pulse timing to determine orbital parameters, with individual masses derived from post-Keplerian parameters such as periastron advance, Einstein delay, orbital period decay, and Shapiro delay, assuming general relativity as the correct theory of gravity.

## Key numbers

- Three pulsars are now firmly in the 1.9-2.0 solar mass range
- Neutron star radii are placed in the 9.9-11.2 km range
- Precise masses are known for approximately 35 neutron stars spanning the range from 1.17 to 2.0 solar masses
- PSR J0348+0432 has a mass of 2.01(4) solar masses
- PSR J1614-2230 has a mass of 1.928(17) solar masses
- PSR J0737-3039 system has component masses of 1.3381(7) and 1.2489(7) solar masses
- PSR B1913+16 has component masses of 1.4398(2) and 1.3886(2) solar masses
- Central densities of neutron stars are 5-10 times the nuclear saturation density of 2.8 x 10^14 g cm^-3
- The fastest known pulsar spin frequency is 716 Hz (PSR J1748-2446ad)

## Context

This review extends the work of Wheeler (1966), when no neutron stars were known, and synthesizes results from the rapid increase in pulsar discoveries and timing observations over the last decade. The combination of massive neutron star discoveries with tighter radius measurements and improved laboratory constraints on dense matter properties has already made substantial impact on understanding cold nuclear matter at densities higher than atomic nucleus density. The results enable future tests of general relativity in the strong-field regime and inform predictions for gravitational wave sources such as neutron star-neutron star and neutron star-black hole mergers that will be detected with Advanced LIGO.
