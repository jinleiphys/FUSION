# Investigating Possible Existence of Hyper-Heavy Nuclei in Neutron Star Environment

> The synthesis of hyper-heavy elements is investigated under conditions simulating neutron star environment. The Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) approach is used to simulate low energy collisions of extremely n-rich nuclei. A new type of the fusion barrier due to a "neutron wind" is observed when the effect of neutron star environment (screening of Coulomb interaction) is introduced implicitly. When introducing also a background of surrounding nuclei, the nuclear fusion becomes possible down to temperatures of 10$^{8}$ K and synthesis of extremely heavy and n-rich nuclei appears feasible. A possible existence of hyper-heavy nuclei in a neutron star environment could provide a mechanism of extra coherent neutrino scattering or an additional mechanism, resulting in X-ray burst or a gravitational wave signal and, thus, becoming another crucial process adding new information to the suggested models on neutron star evolution.

# Investigating Possible Existence of Hyper-Heavy Nuclei in Neutron Star Environment

## Key claim
Fusion of extremely neutron-rich nuclei (N/Z = 4) to form hyper-heavy nuclei with Z > 120 becomes feasible in neutron star environments at temperatures down to 10^8 K, but only when a nucleonic background is explicitly included in the simulations; without such a background, a repulsive "neutron wind" creates an artificial fusion barrier that prevents fusion even at higher energies.

## Method
The Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) approach was used to simulate low-energy collisions of the extremely neutron-rich system 140Ni + 460U, with Coulomb screening implemented via a distance cutoff corresponding to the Debye-Huckel length. Simulations were performed both without a nucleonic medium (using only Coulomb cutoff) and with a nucleonic background of 2000 nucleons in a periodic box at densities ranging from rho0/100 to rho0/5, using an equation of state with incompressibility K0 = 254 MeV and varying density dependence of symmetry energy (soft gamma=0.5, medium gamma=0.75, stiff gamma=1.0).

## Key numbers
- Fusion barrier observed between 1.25 MeV/nucleon and 1.5 MeV/nucleon for the 140Ni + 460U system when using Coulomb interaction cutoff of 2 fm without nucleonic background
- Long-term survival up to 30000 fm/c observed for 140Ni + 460U at kinetic energy of 6 keV with Coulomb cutoff of 1 fm
- Fusion becomes possible down to temperatures of 10^8 K (10-100 keV) when nucleonic background is introduced
- Nucleon bath densities explored: rho0/100, rho0/50, rho0/30, rho0/17, rho0/10, and rho0/5, corresponding to Coulomb cutoffs of 10, 7, 5, 4, 3, and 2 fm respectively
- Resulting compound nucleus dissolves in nucleon bath with density above rho0/30 on time scales up to 25000 fm/c
- For stiff symmetry energy, maximum lifetime observed between densities rho0/50 and rho0/30
- Simulations run up to 25000 fm/c (10^-20 s) for estimating effects on neutron star structure

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of superheavy and hyperheavy nuclei in neutron star crusts, including the seminal work of Baym (1971) predicting nuclei with Z=50-200 and A=186-2500 in the inner crust, and more recent calculations by Shen (2011) finding average mass numbers up to 3000 at high density. The authors previously used BUU simulations (without Coulomb interaction) to study 140Ni + 460U fusion, observing no fusion barrier except for the "neutron wind" effect. The present CoMD simulations with explicit nucleonic background resolve this issue and suggest that hyper-heavy nuclei could form in proto-neutron stars, potentially providing mechanisms for extra coherent neutrino scattering, X-ray bursts, or gravitational wave signals that could inform models of neutron star cooling and evolution.
