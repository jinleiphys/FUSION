# Burning of an hadronic star into a quark or a hybrid star

> We study the hydrodynamical transition from an hadronic star into a quark or a hybrid star. We discuss the possible mode of burning, using a fully relativistic formalism and realistic Equations of State in which hyperons can be present. We take into account the possibility that quarks form a diquark condensate. We also discuss the formation of a mixed phase of hadrons and quarks, and we indicate which region of the star can rapidly convert in various possible scenarios. An estimate of the final temperature of the system is provided. We find that the conversion process always corresponds to a deflagration and never to a detonation. Hydrodynamical instabilities can develop on the front. We estimate the increase in the conversion's velocity due to the formation of wrinkles and we find that, although the increase is significant, it is not sufficient to transform the deflagration into a detonation in essentially all realistic scenarios. Concerning convection, it does not always develop. In particular the system does not develop convection if hyperons are not present in the initial phase and if the newly formed quark phase is made of ungapped (or weakly gapped) quarks. At the contrary, the process of conversion from ungapped quark matter to gapped quarks always allows the formation of a convective layer. Finally, we discuss possible astrophysical implications of our results.

## Key claim

The conversion of a hadronic star into a quark or hybrid star always proceeds as a deflagration (slow combustion) and never as a detonation, even when hydrodynamical instabilities increase the conversion velocity; however, the increase is insufficient to transform the deflagration into a detonation in essentially all realistic scenarios.

## Method

The authors solved the fully relativistic fluidodynamics conservation equations (energy-momentum tensor and baryon flux) across the conversion front, using realistic Equations of State for hadronic matter (relativistic mean-field model with GM3 parameters, including or excluding hyperons) and for quark matter (MIT bag model for normal quarks and a simplified CFL phase with a diquark condensate). They classified the conversion type by comparing phase velocities with sound speeds, considered both beta-equilibrated and non-beta-equilibrated matter, and estimated the final temperature of the quark phase from the energy released. They also analyzed thermal nucleation of the mixed phase and the development of Rayleigh-Taylor instabilities and convection.

## Key numbers

- For a star with baryonic mass M_B = 1.7 M_sun, the gravitational mass of a purely nucleonic star is M_G = 1.53 M_sun, of an hyperonic star M_G = 1.52 M_sun, of a quark star made of unpaired quarks M_G = 1.43 M_sun, and of a CFL quark star M_G = 1.32 M_sun (for B^{1/4} = 155 MeV).
- For B^{1/4} = 165 MeV and M_B = 1.7 M_sun, a hybrid star made of nucleons and normal quarks has M_G = 1.48 M_sun, and a CFL quark star has M_G = 1.39 M_sun.
- For B^{1/4} = 185 MeV and M_B = 1.5 M_sun, the nucleonic star has M_G = 1.37 M_sun, the hyperonic star M_G = 1.36 M_sun, the hybrid star with ungapped quarks M_G = 1.36 M_sun, and the hybrid star with CFL quarks M_G = 1.33 M_sun.
- The critical surface tension for mixed phase formation is estimated at sigma approximately 30 MeV/fm^2, based on Voskresensky (2002).
- For the beta-equilibrium mixed phase with B^{1/4} = 165 MeV, the maximum allowed value of W_c/T for fluidodynamical description is (W_c/T)_max = 23.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic fluidodynamics approach of Cho (1993) and Lugones (1994, 2002) by using more realistic Equations of State that include hyperons, a mixed phase of hadrons and quarks, and the possibility of a diquark condensate (CFL phase). It contradicts the suggestion by Horvath (1988) that instabilities could transform slow combustion into detonation, finding instead that deflagration remains the only viable mode. The results enable future studies of the astrophysical signatures of quark star formation, particularly the time structure of gamma-ray bursts as discussed in Drago (2005), and provide constraints on the surface tension at the hadron-quark interface.
