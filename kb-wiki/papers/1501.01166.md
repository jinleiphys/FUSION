# Nuclear structure studies of $^{24}$F

> The structure of the $^{24}$F nucleus has been studied at GANIL using the $β$ decay of $^{24}$O and the in-beam $γ$-ray spectroscopy from the fragmentation of projectile nuclei. Combining these complementary experimental techniques, the level scheme of $^{24}$F has been constructed up to 3.6 Mev by means of particle-$γ$ and particle-$γγ$ coincidence relations. Experimental results are compared to shell-model calculations using the standard USDA and USDB interactions as well as ab-initio valence-space Hamiltonians calculated from the in-medium similarity renormalization group based on chiral two- and three-nucleon forces. Both methods reproduce the measured level spacings well, and this close agreement allows unidentified spins and parities to be consistently assigned.

## Key claim

The level scheme of $^{24}$F has been constructed up to 3.6 MeV using both beta decay of $^{24}$O and in-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy from projectile fragmentation, and the close agreement between experimental results and shell-model calculations (using standard USDA/USDB interactions and ab-initio IM-SRG Hamiltonians) allows consistent spin and parity assignments, though the ordering of the $2^+_2$ and $4^+_2$ states near the neutron separation energy remains uncertain and may require inclusion of continuum effects.

## Method

The $^{24}$F nucleus was studied at GANIL using two complementary techniques: (1) beta decay of $^{24}$O produced via fragmentation of a $^{36}$S beam on a Be target, with ions identified by energy loss and time-of-flight and implanted in a double-sided silicon-strip detector surrounded by four EXOGAM Ge clover detectors for beta-gamma coincidences; (2) in-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy from fragmentation of $^{27}$Na projectiles in a secondary carbon target, with prompt gamma rays measured by 74 BaF$_2$ detectors of the Chateau de Cristal array in coincidence with $^{24}$F nuclei identified in the SPEG spectrometer. Particle-gamma and particle-gamma-gamma coincidence relations were used to construct the level scheme, and experimental results were compared to shell-model calculations using USDA, USDB, and IM-SRG Hamiltonians derived from chiral two- and three-nucleon forces.

## Key numbers

- Half-life of $^{24}$O: T$_{1/2}$ = 80(5) ms, measured from the summed time-distribution of 521, 1309, and 1830 keV gamma rays
- Beta-delayed neutron emission probability: P$_n$ = 43(4)%, extracted from yields of the 1830 keV and 1309 keV transitions normalized to total $^{24}$O detected
- log$ft$ value of 4.25(6) for the 1830 keV state, consistent with an allowed Gamow-Teller transition
- Ground state of $^{24}$F unambiguously determined to be 3$^+$
- Excited states observed: 521(1) keV (2$^+_1$), 1830(1) keV (1$^+_1$), 2384(64) keV (tentative 4$^+_1$), 2739(14) keV (tentative 3$^+_2$), 3562(22) keV (tentative 4$^+_2$ or 2$^+_3$), and 3639(42) keV (tentative 2$^+_2$ or 1$^+_2$)
- Neutron separation energy: S$_n$ = 3.840(10) MeV
- In the NN+3N-full IM-SRG calculation, the ground state of $^{24}$F is overbound by 7.7 MeV, but all excited states below the one-neutron separation threshold are less than 200 keV away from corresponding experimental levels

## Context

This work extends previous beta-decay studies of $^{24}$O by Mueller et al. and Reed et al., which disagreed on the delayed-neutron emission probability (58(12)% versus 18(6)%) and had insufficient statistics to establish a level scheme. The improved statistics (approximately 10$^5$ $^{24}$O nuclei implanted, a factor of 10 larger than Reed et al.) allowed gamma-gamma coincidence analysis and identification of four new gamma rays. The results are compared to shell-model calculations using USDA and USDB interactions (Brown et al.) and to ab-initio valence-space Hamiltonians from the IM-SRG (Bogner et al., 2014), with the latter showing remarkably good agreement for excited states. The study enables future investigations of continuum effects near the neutron dripline, particularly for the $\pi d_{5/2} \otimes \nu d_{3/2}$ multiplet states, and complements recent coupled-cluster calculations for $^{24}$F.
