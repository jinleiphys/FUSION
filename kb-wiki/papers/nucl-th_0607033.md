# Dynamical aspects of isotopic scaling

> Investigation of the effect of the dynamical stage of heavy-ion collisions indicates that the increasing width of the initial isospin distributions is reflected by a significant modification of the isoscaling slope for the final isotopic distributions after de-excitation. For narrow initial distributions, the isoscaling slope assumes the limiting value of the two individual initial nuclei while for wide initial isotopic distributions the slope for hot fragments approaches the initial value. The isoscaling slopes for final cold fragments increase due to secondary emissions. The experimentally observed evolution of the isoscaling parameter in multifragmentation of hot quasiprojectiles at E$_{inc}$=50 AMeV, fragmentation of $^{86}$Kr projectiles at E$_{inc}$=25 AMeV and multifragmentation of target spectators at relativistic energies was reproduced by a simulation with the dynamical stage described using the appropriate model (deep inelastic transfer and incomplete fusion at the Fermi energy domain and spectator-participant model at relativistic energies) and the de-excitation stage described with the statistical multifragmentation model. In all cases the isoscaling behavior was reproduced by a proper description of the dynamical stage and no unambiguous signals of the decrease of the symmetry energy coefficient were observed.

## Key claim

The width of the initial isospin distribution in the dynamical stage of heavy-ion collisions significantly modifies the isoscaling slope of final isotopic distributions after de-excitation, and this effect can mimic a decrease of the symmetry energy coefficient; the paper argues that no unambiguous signals of a decrease of the symmetry energy coefficient are observed when the dynamical stage is properly described.

## Method

The author simulates the de-excitation stage using the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) code for initial isobaric distributions with masses A = 25, 50, and 100, approximated by two Gaussians with shifted centers (N/Z = 1.0, 1.3) and varying Gaussian widths (sigma_Z = 0.5, 1.5, 3.5 for A = 25; 0.5, 2.5, 4.5 for A = 50, 100) at excitation energies of 2.5 and 5.0 AMeV. For three selected experimental cases, the dynamical stage is described using the deep-inelastic transfer (DIT) model for Fermi-energy reactions, the spectator-participant abrasion model for relativistic energies, and the de-excitation stage is always treated with SMM.

## Key numbers

- For A = 50 at excitation energy 125 MeV, initial Gaussian widths sigma_Z = 0.5, 2.5, 4.5 are used to study the effect on isoscaling slopes.
- For A = 25 at excitation energy 63 MeV, initial Gaussian widths sigma_Z = 0.5, 1.5, 3.5 are used.
- For A = 100 at excitation energy 250 MeV, initial Gaussian widths sigma_Z = 0.5, 2.5, 4.5 are used.
- For the reactions $^{28}$Si+$^{124,112}$Sn at projectile energy 50 AMeV, the excitation energy dependence of the isoscaling slope exhibits a turning-point at E* = 4 AMeV.
- For the reactions $^{86}$Kr+$^{124,112}$Sn at 25 AMeV, the isoscaling slopes are constant for residue mass range A = 25–60, corresponding to primary events with maximum observed excitation energy of 2.2 AMeV.
- For target spectators at relativistic energies, the proportionality factor for excitation energy from abraded nucleons is 27 MeV.
- The calculated widths of target spectator isospin distributions at saturation lead to an apparent symmetry energy coefficient decrease from 25 MeV to about 20 MeV, comparable to the uncertainty in ground-state models (commonly 23 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the isoscaling studies of Tsang (2001) and Botvina (2002) by showing that the width of initial isospin distributions, rather than a decrease of the symmetry energy coefficient, can explain observed variations in isoscaling parameters. The paper reproduces experimental data from reactions studied by SiSnNExch (2004) for $^{28}$Si+$^{124,112}$Sn, by KrSn_1 (2003) for $^{86}$Kr+$^{124,112}$Sn, and by LeFevre (2005) for target spectators at relativistic energies, using DIT and abrasion models for the dynamical stage. The results enable future work to disentangle dynamical and thermodynamical effects in multifragmentation data, particularly by using full calorimetry or reliable simulations of the initial stage to reconstruct wide initial distributions.
