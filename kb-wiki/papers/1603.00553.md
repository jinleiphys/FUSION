# Chern-Simons diffusion rate across different phase transitions

> We investigate how the dimensionless ratio given by the Chern-Simons diffusion rate $Γ_{\textrm{CS}}$ divided by the product of the entropy density $s$ and temperature $T$ behaves across different kinds of phase transitions in the class of bottom-up non-conformal Einstein-dilaton holographic models originally proposed by Gubser and Nellore. By tuning the dilaton potential, one is able to holographically mimic a first order, a second order, or a crossover transition. In a first order phase transition, $Γ_{\textrm{CS}}/sT$ jumps at the critical temperature (as previously found in the holographic literature), while in a second order phase transition it develops an infinite slope. On the other hand, in a crossover, $Γ_{\textrm{CS}}/sT$ behaves smoothly, although displaying a fast variation around the pseudo-critical temperature. In all the cases, $Γ_{\textrm{CS}}/sT$ increases with decreasing $T$. The behavior of the Chern-Simons diffusion rate across different phase transitions is expected to play a relevant role for the chiral magnetic effect around the QCD critical end point, which is a second order phase transition point connecting a crossover band to a line of first order phase transition. Our findings in the present work add to the literature the first predictions for the Chern-Simons diffusion rate across second order and crossover transitions in strongly coupled non-conformal, non-Abelian gauge theories.

## Key claim

The paper provides the first holographic predictions for the Chern-Simons diffusion rate across second order phase transitions and crossovers in strongly coupled non-conformal, non-Abelian gauge theories, extending previous work that only examined first order transitions. The qualitative behavior of the dimensionless ratio Γ_CS/sT is determined by the nature of the phase transition encoded in the dilaton potential, not by the specific choice of axion-dilaton coupling function.

## Method

The authors employ bottom-up Einstein-dilaton holographic models originally proposed by Gubser and Nellore (2008), tuning parameters of the dilaton potential to mimic first order, second order, or crossover transitions in the dual gauge theory. They solve the master equation for the background geometry numerically, then compute the Chern-Simons diffusion rate using the membrane paradigm or near-horizon matching technique, which yields the simple formula Γ_CS/sT = Z(φ_H)/(2π), where Z(φ) is the axion-dilaton coupling and φ_H is the black hole horizon position. Two different functional forms for the axion-dilaton coupling are tested to verify robustness of the qualitative results.

## Key numbers

- For the first order phase transition model, the critical temperature occurs at φ_H^c ≈ 2.19, with T_min ≈ 0.9 T_c as the minimum temperature for black hole solutions.
- For the second order phase transition model, the critical temperature occurs at φ_H^c ≈ 4.26, identified as the point where the speed of sound squared vanishes.
- In the first order transition, Γ_CS/sT jumps discontinuously at T_c; in the second order transition, it develops an infinite slope at T_c; in the crossover, it varies smoothly but rapidly around the pseudo-critical temperature.
- In all cases, Γ_CS/sT increases with decreasing temperature, growing by orders of magnitude below T_c in the second order case.

## Context

This work extends the holographic study of the Chern-Simons diffusion rate by Gursoy et al. (2012), who first computed it in a first order phase transition using similar Einstein-dilaton models, and by Son and Starinets (2002) who pioneered the holographic calculation of this observable. The results are relevant for the chiral magnetic effect around the QCD critical end point, which is a second order phase transition point connecting a crossover band to a line of first order phase transitions, as probed by the Beam Energy Scan program at RHIC. The findings enable future phenomenological studies of topological charge fluctuations in heavy ion collisions near the QCD phase boundary.
