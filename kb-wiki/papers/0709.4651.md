# Comment on "Is There a "Most Perfect Fluid" Consistent with Quantum Field Theory?"

> This is a comment on hep-th/0702136

## Key claim

The metastable gas of heavy-light mesons proposed by Cohen (2007) does not constitute a counter-example to the conjectured viscosity bound, because the viscosity and entropy density exist in non-overlapping length-scale regimes; the system fails the bound only if one abandons the reasonable requirement that both quantities be defined in the same physical regime.

## Method

Son examines the length scales and lifetimes in the metastable system of heavy-light mesons in a large-N, large-flavor gauge theory. He compares the lifetime of the metastable state (which scales as a power of ξ in the limit ξ→∞, in units of the time a meson travels the mean interparticle separation) with the linear size required to contain all particle species (which must exceed exp(⅓ ξ⁴) in units of the interparticle separation). He then argues that any measurement of viscosity over such long distances would take much longer than the system's lifetime, so the hydrodynamic regime and the thermodynamic limit cannot be simultaneously achieved.

## Key numbers

- The number of flavors scales as exp(ξ⁴) in the limit ξ→∞.
- The linear size of a volume containing all particle species must be larger than exp(⅓ ξ⁴), in units of the interparticle separation.
- The lifetime of the metastable system scales as a power of ξ in the limit ξ→∞, in units of the time a meson travels the mean interparticle separation.

## Context

This comment directly addresses the construction of Cohen (2007), which claimed to invalidate the viscosity bound conjectured by Kovtun, Son, and Starinets (2004). Son also references Cherman (2007) regarding the lifetime scaling of the metastable system. The argument clarifies that the proposed counter-example fails because the thermodynamic limit and hydrodynamic regime cannot be simultaneously realized, preserving the viscosity bound for systems where both quantities are defined in the same physical regime. The comment notes that within nonrelativistic quantum mechanics (without relativistic completion) one can still evade the viscosity bound by increasing the number of particle species, as noted by Kovtun, Son, and Starinets (2004) and Cohen (2007).
