# Stylized features of single-nucleon momentum distributions

> Nuclear short-range correlations (SRC) typically manifest themselves in the tail parts of the single-nucleon momentum distributions. We propose an approximate practical method for computing those SRC contributions to the high-momentum parts. The framework adopted in this work is applicable throughout the nuclear mass table and corrects mean-field models for central, spin-isospin and tensor correlations by shifting the complexity induced by the SRC from the wave functions to the operators. It is argued that the expansion of these modified operators can be truncated to a low order. The proposed model can generate the SRC-related high-momentum tail of the single-nucleon momentum distribution. These are dominated by correlation operators acting on mean-field pairs with vanishing relative radial and angular-momentum quantum numbers. The proposed method explains the dominant role of proton-neutron pairs in generating the SRC and accounts for the magnitude and mass dependence of SRC as probed in inclusive electron scattering. It also provides predictions for the ratio of the amount of correlated proton-proton to proton-neutron pairs which are in line with the observations. In asymmetric nuclei, the correlations make the average kinetic energy for the minority nucleons larger than for the majority nucleons.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a Low-order Correlation Operator Approximation (LCA) that can compute short-range correlation contributions to single-nucleon momentum distributions throughout the nuclear mass table, with the caveat that the method truncates the effective operator expansion to terms linear and quadratic in the correlation operators, relying on the local character of SRC to justify this truncation.

## Method

The authors start from a mean-field (independent particle model) Slater determinant and apply a many-body correlation operator containing central, spin-isospin, and tensor correlation functions to construct correlated ground-state wave functions. They then shift the complexity from the wave functions to the operators by constructing effective one-body and two-body operators, truncating the expansion at the level of two-body operators (the LCA method). The single-nucleon momentum distributions are evaluated by computing expectation values of these effective operators in the uncorrelated harmonic oscillator basis, using a global mass-dependent parametrization of the oscillator frequency.

## Key numbers

- The normalization factor N (deviation from 1 measures total SRC effect) ranges from 1.128 for 2H to 1.745 for 197Au, with values for 4He (1.327), 12C (1.435), 16O (1.527), 40Ca (1.637), and 208Pb (1.741)
- The crossover between tensor and central correlated contributions to the tail of n[1](p) occurs at a momentum slightly larger than 3 fm^-1 for all nuclei studied
- For 12C, the experimentally measured pp to pn ratio of (1 ± 0.3%)/(18 ± 5%) is compatible with LCA predictions
- The fitted linear relation between EMC effect magnitude and R2(A/2H) is: -dR_EMC/dx = (0.033 ± 0.035) + (0.071 ± 0.009) * R2(A/2H)
- For 12C, removal of a proton with initial momentum 275-550 MeV/c is accompanied by a neutron 92+8-18% of the time

## Context

This work extends the correlated basis function approach of Alvioli (2013) and the cluster expansion methods to provide a computationally tractable framework for mid-heavy and heavy nuclei where ab-initio methods like the quantum Monte Carlo calculations of Wiringa (2013) are limited to A ≤ 12. The LCA predictions for the mass dependence of SRC magnitude (R2 ratios) are compared to experimentally extracted a2 coefficients from the analysis of Hen (2012), and the results confirm the linear correlation between SRC magnitude and the EMC effect suggested by Weinstein (2011). The framework enables systematic studies of the isospin dependence of SRC across the nuclear mass table, explaining the dominance of proton-neutron pairs in SRC-sensitive reactions observed in the Jefferson Lab measurements of Subedi (2008) and Korover (2014).
