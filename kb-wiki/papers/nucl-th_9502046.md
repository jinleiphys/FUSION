# Mean-field description of nuclear charge density distributions.

> We propose a method to extract nuclear charge distributions from elastic electron scattering data based upon a mean field approach. The nuclear charge distributions are generated by solving the Schroedinger equation with a mean-field potential expanded in terms of Hermite functions. The coefficients of the potential are changed in order to obtain the best fit with the cross section data. Applications to the $^{12}C$, $^{16}O$ $^{40}Ca$ and $^{208}Pb$ nuclei are presented.

## Key claim

The authors propose a method to extract nuclear charge distributions from elastic electron scattering data that is less general than model-independent techniques but produces more stable solutions, with the caveat that the resulting charge distributions are not claimed to be the most realistic ones because dispersion effects, meson exchange currents, and neutron charge distribution are ignored.

## Method

The nuclear charge distribution is obtained by folding nucleon densities (generated from single-particle wave functions of a mean-field potential) with the nucleon electromagnetic form factor. The mean-field potential is expanded on an orthonormal basis of Hermite functions, and the expansion coefficients are varied to minimize a merit function that combines the chi-squared of the cross section fit with a chi-squared of the single-particle energies and a penalty term for positive potential values. The method is applied to elastic electron scattering data for 12C, 16O, 40Ca, and 208Pb.

## Key numbers

- For 12C: reduced chi-squared of 1.42 (SMH model), 1.25 (SMHWS model), compared to 1.73 (Fourier-Bessel) and 1.11 (Hermite expansion)
- For 16O: reduced chi-squared of 4.44 (SMH model), 4.01 (SMHWS model), compared to 3.55 (Fourier-Bessel) and 3.38 (Hermite expansion)
- For 40Ca: reduced chi-squared of 1.35 (SMH model), 1.31 (SMHWS model), compared to 1.38 (Fourier-Bessel) and 1.00 (Hermite expansion)
- For 208Pb: reduced chi-squared of 1.59 (SMH model), 1.52 (SMHWS model), compared to 1.44 (Fourier-Bessel) and 1.39 (Hermite expansion)
- Single-particle energy for 1p3/2 in 12C: SMH model gives -15.53 MeV, SMHWS gives -16.00 MeV, experimental value is -15.96 MeV
- Single-particle energy for 1p3/2 in 16O: SMH model gives -24.17 MeV, SMHWS gives -18.45 MeV, experimental value is -18.44 MeV
- Single-particle energy for 1d5/2 in 40Ca: SMH model gives -12.02 MeV, SMHWS gives -15.75 MeV, experimental value is -14.73 MeV
- Single-particle energy for 1g7/2 in 208Pb: SMH model gives -11.88 MeV, SMHWS gives -12.78 MeV, experimental value is -11.51 MeV

## Context

This work extends the approach of Elton and Swift (1967), who used an energy-dependent mean-field to fit elastic electron scattering data, by employing an energy-independent mean-field expanded on a Hermite function basis to fit data over a much wider momentum transfer range. The method is presented as an alternative to model-independent techniques such as the Fourier-Bessel expansion of Dreher, Friedrich, Merle, Rothhaas, and Lurs (1974) and the Hermite expansion of Friar and Negele (1973), which suffer from instability in the charge distribution when the number of expansion coefficients is increased. The approach enables more stable extraction of charge distributions with physically reasonable exponential tails, though the authors caution that the mean fields obtained have only pragmatical origin and should not be connected to microscopic nuclear theories.
