# Bethe-Salpeter Equations with Instantaneous Confinement: Establishing Stability of Bound States

> Salpeter equations with potential functions rising to infinity in configuration space do not automatically predict stable bound states. For this to happen, also the Lorentz behaviour of the involved Bethe-Salpeter kernels is crucial. At least for interaction potentials of harmonic-oscillator form analytic scrutinies of Salpeter equations with confining interactions may identify those Bethe-Salpeter kernels which describe bound states free from the notorious instabilities encountered in numerical evaluations. Such truly confining kernels can be singled out systematically by requesting the resulting bound-state energy spectra to be both real and discrete.

# Wiki Page: Bethe-Salpeter Equations with Instantaneous Confinement: Establishing Stability of Bound States

## Key claim
Salpeter equations with confining potentials that rise to infinity in configuration space do not automatically yield stable bound states; the Lorentz structure of the Bethe-Salpeter kernel determines whether the resulting bound-state energy spectra are real and discrete. For harmonic-oscillator confining interactions, stability can be proven analytically only when the kernel is predominantly of time-component Lorentz-vector type, with the scalar component subdominant.

## Method
The author reduces the full Salpeter equation (the instantaneous three-dimensional reduction of the Bethe-Salpeter formalism) for fermion-antifermion bound states to a system of radial differential equations by assuming a harmonic-oscillator potential in configuration space and massless constituents. For pseudoscalar bound states (J^PC = 0^{-+}), the Salpeter amplitude involves only two independent components, and the integral equation becomes a set of second-order differential equations. The spectral analysis proceeds by constructing positive self-adjoint operators from the differential operators and applying the minimum-maximum principle to prove discreteness of the mass-squared spectrum.

## Key numbers
- For a pure time-component Lorentz-vector harmonic-oscillator kernel with positive coupling strength a > 0, the bound-state mass eigenvalues are proven to be real and discrete, with the spectrum bounded from below.
- For mixed kernels with time-component Lorentz-vector coupling ξ and Lorentz-scalar coupling η, stability requires α = aξ > 0 and |β/α| < 1, where β = aη.
- Lowest-lying positive mass eigenvalues (in units of ∛a) for the mixed kernel range from 2.93 (β/α = -1.00) to 4.71 (β/α = +0.50) for the ground state (level 0), and from 4.68 to 7.07 for the first excited state (level 1), across the stability-compatible range -1 ≤ β/α < +1.
- For β/α = 0 (pure time-component Lorentz-vector), the ground state mass eigenvalue is 4.60 in units of ∛a.

## Context
This work extends earlier numerical studies by StabSal and Kopaleishvili that reported instabilities in Salpeter equations with linear or harmonic-oscillator confining potentials, and builds on the author's prior analytic analyses of the reduced Salpeter equation (Lucha 2007) and its improvement with dressed propagators (Lucha 2005, 2007). The paper provides rigorous analytic proofs for the full Salpeter equation, complementing the numerical evidence that stability requires the kernel to be predominantly time-component Lorentz-vector. The results enable systematic identification of confining Bethe-Salpeter kernels that guarantee bound-state stability ab initio, and suggest that similar analyses could be extended to more general potentials such as linear confinement if the integral-equation challenges can be overcome.
