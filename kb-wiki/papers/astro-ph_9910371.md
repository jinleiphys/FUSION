# From Boson Condensation to Quark Deconfinement: The Many Faces of Neutron Star Interiors

> Gravity compresses the matter in the cores of neutron stars to densities which are significantly higher than the density of ordinary atomic nuclei, thus providing a high-pressure environment in which numerous particle processes - from the generation of new baryonic particles to quark deconfinement to the formation of Boson condensates and H-matter - may compete with each other. There are theoretical suggestions of even more `exotic' processes inside pulsars, such as the formation of absolutely stable strange quark matter, a configuration of matter even more stable than the most stable atomic nucleus, iron. In the latter event, neutron stars would be largely composed of pure quark matter, eventually enveloped in nuclear crust matter. No matter which physical processes are actually realized inside neutron stars, each one leads to fingerprints, some more pronounced than others though, in the observable stellar quantities. This feature combined with the tremendous recent progress in observational radio and X-ray astronomy, renders neutron stars to nearly ideal probes for a wide range of dense matter studies, complementing the quest of the behavior of superdense matter in terrestrial collider experiments.

# From Boson Condensation to Quark Deconfinement: The Many Faces of Neutron Star Interiors

## Key claim

The interior of neutron stars may host a wide range of competing phases of superdense matter, from hyperons and kaon condensates to H-dibaryons, deconfined quark matter, and color-superconducting diquark condensates, each leaving distinct fingerprints in observable stellar quantities such as the braking index and cooling behavior, but the actual realization of any given phase depends on uncertain in-medium properties and the stiffness of the equation of state, with heavy neutron stars near 2 solar masses potentially ruling out many exotic phases.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical models for the equation of state (EoS) of neutron star matter, derived from relativistic nuclear field theories solved at the mean-field level and from the MIT bag model for quark matter. Stellar structure is computed using the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation for non-rotating stars and the full Einstein field equations for rotating stars, including the effect of frame dragging and the Kepler frequency limit. The braking index of pulsars is derived from the frequency-dependent moment of inertia, and cooling curves are computed from neutrino emission processes.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³, energy density ε₀ = 140 MeV/fm³
- Nuclear matter bulk properties at saturation: binding energy E/A = −16.0 MeV, effective nucleon mass m*_N/m_N = 0.79, incompressibility K ≃ 265 MeV, symmetry energy a_s = 32.5 MeV
- Effective K⁻ mass at ρ ∼ 3 ρ₀: m*_K⁻ ≃ 200 MeV, with density dependence m*_K⁻(ρ) ≃ m_K⁻ (1 − 0.2 ρ/ρ₀) where m_K⁻ = 495 MeV
- Maximum mass of nucleon stars with K⁻ condensates: between 1.5 M_⊙ and 1.8 M_⊙
- Radius of neutron stars with K⁻ condensates: R ≲ 10 km, with candidate RXJ 185635–3754 possibly as small as ∼ 7 km
- H-dibaryon formation threshold: between 3 ε₀ and 6 ε₀, with vacuum mass ∼ 2.2 GeV and medium potential of about −30 MeV or +30 MeV
- H-dibaryon condensate possible in stars with mass M = 1.444 M_⊙ (PSR 1913+16) for attractive potential, or M ≳ 1.6 M_⊙ for repulsive potential
- Bag constants for absolutely stable strange quark matter: B = 57.5 MeV/fm³ (B^(1/4) = 145 MeV) giving E/A = 829 MeV; B = 85.3 MeV/fm³ (B^(1/4) = 160 MeV) giving E/A = 915 MeV, compared to 930.4 MeV for ⁵⁶Fe
- Maximum mass of non-rotating neutron stars: 2.2 M_⊙; rapid rotation increases this by at most 25%, giving upper limit ∼ 2.5 M_⊙
- Mass of Hulse-Taylor pulsar PSR 1913+16: M = 1.444 M_⊙
- Lower bound on maximum mass from 4U 0900–40: ∼ 1.56 M_⊙; possible upper bound ∼ 1.98 M_⊙ from QPOs
- QPO frequency observed in 4U 1636–536: 1220 Hz
- Kepler period for 1.4 M_⊙ neutron stars: excludes half-millisecond periods
- Braking index anomaly occurs at Ω ∼ 400 s⁻¹ for one model star, and at Ω ∼ 1370 s⁻¹ for another
- Duration of braking index anomaly: ΔT ≃ 10⁸ years for millisecond pulsars with Ṗ ≃ 10⁻¹⁹
- Color superconductivity gap and critical temperature: ∼ 100 MeV
- Neutron star crust density at neutron drip: 4 × 10¹¹ g/cm³

## Context

This review paper, presented at the XXIII School of Theoretical Physics in Ustron, Poland in 1999, synthesizes two decades of theoretical work on dense matter in neutron stars, extending earlier studies by Baym (1976), Witten (1984), and Alcock (1986) on quark matter, and by Brown (1996) on kaon condensation. It builds on the author's own book (Weber, 1999) and prior work by Glendenning (1997, 1998) on EoS models. The paper enables future observational tests: the predicted braking index anomalies could be searched for in millisecond pulsar timing data, and the cooling curves provide templates for interpreting X-ray observations of neutron star surfaces. The discussion of sub-millisecond pulsars as potential strange star signatures directly connects to the r-mode instability work by Madsen (1998) and to ongoing searches for strangelets at CERN and Brookhaven.
