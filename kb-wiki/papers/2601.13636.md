# Superfluid Band Theory for the Rod Phase in the Magnetized Inner Crust Matter: Entrainment, Spin-orbit Coupling, Spin-triplet Pairing

> The inner crust of neutron stars hosts a rich variety of nuclear phenomena and provides a unique environment for exploring microscopic nuclear properties relevant to diverse astrophysical observations. Particularly magnetars, which possess extremely strong magnetic-fields, have attracted increasing attention in connection with nuclear spin dynamics and unconventional pairing correlations. This work is dedicated to develop a comprehensive theoretical framework to describe the structures and properties of two-dimensional (rod-phase) matter in the neutron star inner crust, incorporating band-structure effects, neutron spin-triplet pairing, and strong magnetic-fields on an equal footing. The main results of this study can be summarized as follows. In the first place, the magnetic-fields of the order of $10^{16}\,$G are found to substantially enhance the neutron effective mass by a factor of approximately $1.5$, indicating a significant modification of entrainment properties in strongly magnetized crustal matter. In the second place, while the overall behavior of pairing phase transitions is qualitatively similar to that observed in one-dimensional systems studied previously, the present two-dimensional calculations reveal a nontrivial role of the spin-orbit interaction in inducing spin-polarization under magnetic fields. In the third place, concerning spin-triplet superfluidity, the rank-0 component is shown to emerge as a consequence of magnetic-field-induced spin-polarization, irrespective of the presence of spin-triplet pairing interactions, whereas the rank-2 component appears only when the corresponding interaction channel is included.

## Key claim

Magnetic fields of order 10^16 G enhance the neutron effective mass by a factor of approximately 1.5 in the rod phase of the inner crust, but this result is obtained within a Hartree-Fock framework without pairing correlations, and the full superfluid calculations are needed to assess the interplay with spin-triplet pairing.

## Method

The authors extend their superfluid band theory framework to two-dimensional rod-phase matter in the neutron star inner crust, combining nuclear density functional theory with the superfluid local density approximation. They generalize the anomalous density to include spin-triplet components and incorporate a spin-triplet pairing energy density functional, solving the Kohn-Sham and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov equations self-consistently with Bloch boundary conditions for a square lattice. The framework treats band-structure effects, neutron spin-triplet pairing, and strong magnetic fields on an equal footing, with the pairing spin-current tensor decomposed into rank-0, rank-1, and rank-2 components.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic fields of order 10^16 G enhance the neutron effective mass by a factor of approximately 1.5, indicating significant modification of entrainment properties in strongly magnetized crustal matter.
- The rank-0 spin-triplet superfluid component emerges as a consequence of magnetic-field-induced spin-polarization, irrespective of the presence of spin-triplet pairing interactions.
- The rank-2 spin-triplet component appears only when the corresponding interaction channel is included in the calculation.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous superfluid band theory framework (Yoshimura 2024a) from one-dimensional to two-dimensional rod-phase matter, building on earlier band-theoretical calculations of neutron effective mass in the inner crust (Carter 2005, Chamel 2005, Chamel 2012, Chamel 2017). It also connects to studies of spin-triplet pairing in neutron stars (Schwenk 2004, Page 2004, Page 2009) and recent work showing that spin-orbit interactions can induce 3P1 superfluid components in open-shell nuclei (Hinohara 2024) and that spin-polarized neutron matter may produce 3P0 pairing (Tajima 2023). The framework enables future microscopic studies of magnetar crust properties and their astrophysical implications.
