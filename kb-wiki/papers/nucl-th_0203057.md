# Selfconsistent calculations of fission barriers in the Fm region

> The fission barriers of the nuclei 254Fm, 256Fm, 258Fm, 258No and 260Rf are investigated in a fully microscopic way up to the scission point. The analysis is based on the constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov theory and Gogny's D1S force. The quadrupole, octupole and hexadecapole moments as well as the number of nucleons in the neck region are used as constraints. Two fission paths, corresponding to the bimodal fission, are found. The decrease with isotope mass of the half-life times of heavy Fm isotopes is also explained.

# Selfconsistent calculations of fission barriers in the Fm region

## Key claim

The constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations with Gogny's D1S force find two fission paths (compact and elongated) for nuclei in the Fm region, but the static potential energy surfaces alone cannot fully reproduce the experimental mass asymmetry for 256Fm, suggesting that dynamical effects may play a role. The rapid decrease of spontaneous fission half-lives from 256Fm to 258Fm is qualitatively explained by the second fission barrier falling below the ground state energy, though the calculated half-life for 256Fm is three orders of magnitude shorter than the measured value.

## Method

The authors performed constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the Gogny D1S effective interaction, expanding quasiparticle operators on axially symmetric deformed harmonic oscillator bases with up to N0=17 shells. They applied constraints on the axial quadrupole (Q2), octupole (Q3), and hexadecapole (Q4) moments, as well as the number of nucleons in the neck region (QN), to map the potential energy surfaces for 254Fm, 256Fm, 258Fm, 258No, and 260Rf. The rotational energy correction was estimated using the cranking approximation to the Yoccoz moment of inertia, and spontaneous fission half-lives were computed in the WKB framework with the ATDHFB collective inertia.

## Key numbers

- The first fission barrier in axially symmetric calculations is approximately 10 MeV high for all nuclei considered, reduced by a few MeV when triaxial shapes (gamma between 0 and 8 degrees) are included.
- For 256Fm, the superdeformed minimum at Q2=70 b lies 2 MeV below the ground state energy, separated from scission by a barrier only 2 MeV high.
- For 258Fm, the second hump on the compact fission path rises only 0.5 MeV above the superdeformed minimum, and its top sits a few MeV below the ground state energy.
- The ridge between the compact and elongated fission paths in 258Fm exceeds 1.5 MeV along the entire path to fission.
- The deformed 129Sn fragment in the elongated fission path of 258Fm has a superdeformed state approximately 23 MeV above the ground state, corresponding to the expected difference in total kinetic energy between the two fission modes.
- For 258No, the mass ratio of fragments in the third compact path is M_H/M_L = 132.5/125.5; for 260Rf, M_H/M_L = 136/124.
- The calculated spontaneous fission half-life for 254Fm is 11 orders of magnitude larger than for 256Fm when only the symmetric compact path is considered, but becomes much shorter when left-right asymmetry is included.
- The calculated half-life for 256Fm is three orders of magnitude shorter than the experimental value.

## Context

This work extends earlier macroscopic-microscopic calculations of bimodal fission in the Fm region by Cwiok (1989) and Moeller (1987, 1989, 1992, 2000, 2001), who used Woods-Saxon or folded-Yukawa potentials with Strutinsky shell corrections, by performing fully self-consistent HFB calculations with the Gogny D1S force. The results confirm the existence of compact and elongated fission valleys found in those prior studies, but the static calculations fail to reproduce the mass asymmetry observed experimentally for 256Fm by Hoffman (1973). The explanation of the half-life decrease for heavy Fm isotopes addresses a problem noted by Staszczak (1999) as lacking satisfactory theoretical description. The findings enable future dynamical calculations that could better account for the mass distributions and half-lives of these nuclei.
