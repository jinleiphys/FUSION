# Quark-hadron duality: resonances and the onset of scaling

> We examine the origin of Bloom-Gilman duality and the relationship between resonances and scaling in deep-inelastic scattering. A simple quantum mechanical model is used to illustrate the essential features of Bloom-Gilman duality at low momentum transfer. As an application of local duality, we discuss model-independent relations between structure functions at x ~ 1 and elastic electromagnetic form factors.

## Key claim
Bloom-Gilman duality, the observation that the average of resonance-region structure functions follows the high-energy scaling curve, can be reproduced in a simple quantum mechanical model with confined quarks and correct kinematics, provided the density of excited states is Regge-like rather than linear.

## Method
The author uses a relativistic scalar quark confined to an infinitely massive core by an oscillator-like potential, which yields exact solutions for the complete spectrum of excited states. The structure function is computed as a sum over squares of transition form factors for infinitely narrow resonances, with the energy-dependent delta-function smoothed by a Breit-Wigner shape. The scaling variable \(u\) is defined with respect to the quark mass to account for nonzero mass effects at finite \(Q^2\).

## Key numbers
- At \(Q^2 = 1\) GeV\(^2\), around 2/3 of the total deep-inelastic cross section comes from the resonance region (\(W < 2\) GeV).
- The resonances and deep-inelastic continuum together produce only about a 10% correction to the lowest moment of the scaling \(F_2\) structure function at \(Q^2 = 1\) GeV\(^2\).
- The elastic magnetic form factor of the proton extracted from Jefferson Lab data agrees with the experimental form factor to within 30% for \(Q^2 < 5\) GeV\(^2\).
- The model structure function is computed at \(Q^2 = 0.5\), 1, 2, and 5 GeV\(^2\).
- The transition form factor falls as \(F_{0N} \sim \vec q^{\,N} \exp(-\vec q^{\,2} / 4 \beta^2)\).

## Context
This work reviews and extends the Bloom-Gilman duality observed by Bloom and Gilman (1970) in inclusive electron scattering, and reformulated in QCD using the operator product expansion by De Rujula et al. (1975). The simple quantum mechanical model follows the approach of Isgur et al. (1996) and demonstrates that confinement and Regge-like level spacing (\(E_N \sim \sqrt{N}\)) are sufficient to produce duality. The local duality relations connect structure functions at \(x \sim 1\) to elastic electromagnetic form factors, enabling predictions for the polarization asymmetry \(A_1\) and neutron-to-proton ratios that can be tested at Jefferson Lab.
