# Quantitative description of the $^{20}$Ne($p$,$pα$)$^{16}$O cross section as a means of probing the surface $α$ amplitude

> The proton-induced $α$ knockout reaction has been utilized for decades to investigate the $α$ cluster states of nuclei, of the ground state in particular. However, even in recent years, it is reported that the deduced $α$ spectroscopic factors from $α$ knockout experiments and reaction analyses with a phenomenological $α$ cluster wave function diverge depending on the kinematical condition of the reaction. In the present study we examine the theoretical description of the $^{20}$Ne($p$,$pα$)$^{16}$O cross section based on the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics and the distorted wave impulse approximation by comparing with existing experimental data. We also investigate the correspondence between the $α$ cluster wave function and the $α$ knockout cross section. The existing $^{20}$Ne($p$,$pα$)$^{16}$O data at 101.5 MeV is well reproduced by the present framework. Due to the peripherality of the reaction, the surface region of the cluster wave function is selectively reflected to the knockout cross section. A quantitatively reliable $α$ cluster wave function, $p$-$α$ cross section, and distorting potentials between scattering particles, $α$-$^{16}$O in particular, are crucial for the quantitative description of the ($p$,$pα$) cross section. Due to the peripherality of the reaction, the ($p$,$pα$) cross section is a good probe for the surface $α$ amplitude.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the distorted wave impulse approximation (DWIA) can quantitatively reproduce the $^{20}$Ne($p$,$p\alpha$)$^{16}$O cross section at 101.5 MeV without free parameters, provided that reliable inputs are used: the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) cluster wave function, a tuned $p$-$\alpha$ cross section, and the $\alpha$-$^{16}$O optical potential from Michel et al. (1983). The abstract's claim of "well reproduced" depends on the $p$-$\alpha$ cross section being tuned by a factor of 2.0 to match experimental data.

## Method

The $^{20}$Ne($p$,$p\alpha$)$^{16}$O reaction at 101.5 MeV was described using the factorized form of the distorted wave impulse approximation (DWIA). The $\alpha$-$^{16}$O reduced width amplitude (RWA) for the $^{20}$Ne ground state was obtained from antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) with the Gogny D1S interaction, using the Laplace expansion method. The DWIA calculation combined this RWA with the EDAD1 Dirac optical potential for protons, the Michel et al. (1983) optical potential for $\alpha$-$^{16}$O scattering, and a $p$-$\alpha$ differential cross section from a single-folding model that was tuned by a factor of 2.0 to reproduce data at $\theta_{p\alpha}=84^\circ$-$86^\circ$.

## Key numbers

- The $\alpha$ spectroscopic factor from the AMD-RWA is $S_\alpha = 0.26$.
- The incident proton energy is 101.5 MeV; the emitted proton angle is fixed at $-70^\circ$ and the $\alpha$ angle at $46.3^\circ$.
- The $p$-$\alpha$ differential cross section required a tuning factor of 2.0 to match experimental data at $\theta_{p\alpha}=84^\circ$-$86^\circ$ and $E_{p\alpha}=75$-$100$ MeV.
- The recoilless condition occurs at $T_p = 67$ MeV (corresponding to $T_\alpha = 30$ MeV).
- The $p$-$\alpha$ scattering angle range is $\theta_{p\alpha}=84^\circ$-$86^\circ$ in the center-of-mass frame.
- The three Brink-Bloch test RWAs had $S_\alpha$ values of 0.24 (original), 0.18 (smaller peak), and 0.21 (shorter tail).

## Context

This work resolves a long-standing inconsistency reported by Carey et al. (1984), where $\alpha$ spectroscopic factors from $^{20}$Ne($p$,$p\alpha$)$^{16}$O experiments were 2.4-3.0 times larger than those from structure theories, and by Mabiala et al. (2009), where $S_\alpha$ for $^{12}$C varied from 0.19 to 1.68 depending on kinematics. The present framework demonstrates that using a reliable AMD cluster wave function, proper optical potentials (especially the $\alpha$-$^{16}$O potential from Michel et al. (1983)), and a correctly tuned $p$-$\alpha$ cross section enables quantitative ($p$,$p\alpha$) reaction analysis. This enables future application to $\alpha$ clustering studies in unstable nuclei and suggests that the ($p$,$p\alpha$) reaction selectively probes the surface $\alpha$ amplitude due to reaction peripherality.
