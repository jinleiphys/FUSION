# Suddenly shortened half-lives beyond $^{78}$Ni: $N=50$ magic number and high-energy non-unique first-forbidden transitions

> $β$-decay rates play a decisive role in understanding the nucleosynthesis of heavy elements and are governed by microscopic nuclear-structure information. A sudden shortening of the half-lives of Ni isotopes beyond $N=50$ was observed at the RIKEN-RIBF. This is considered due to the persistence of the neutron magic number $N=50$ in the very neutron-rich Ni isotopes. By systematically studying the $β$-decay rates and strength distributions in the neutron-rich Ni isotopes around $N=50$, I try to understand the microscopic mechanism for the observed sudden shortening of the half-lives. The $β$-strength distributions in the neutron-rich nuclei are described in the framework of nuclear density-functional theory. I employ the Skyrme energy-density functionals (EDF) in the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculation for the ground states and in the proton-neutron Quasiparticle Random-Phase Approximation (pnQRPA) for the transitions. Not only the allowed but the first-forbidden (FF) transitions are considered. The experimentally observed sudden shortening of the half-lives beyond $N=50$ is reproduced well by the calculations employing the Skyrme SkM* and SLy4 functionals. The sudden shortening of the half-lives is due to the shell gap at $N=50$ and cooperatively with the high-energy transitions to the low-lying $0^-$ and $1^-$ states in the daughter nuclei. The onset of FF transitions pointed out around $N=82$ and 126 is preserved in the lower-mass nuclei around $N=50$. This study suggests that needed is a microscopic calculation where the shell structure in neutron-rich nuclei and its associated effects on the FF transitions are selfconsistenly taken into account for predicting $β$-decay rates of exotic nuclei in unknown region.

## Key claim

The experimentally observed sudden shortening of β-decay half-lives in Ni isotopes beyond N=50 is reproduced by calculations using the SkM* and SLy4 functionals, and is caused by the onset of high-energy non-unique first-forbidden transitions to low-lying 0⁻ and 1⁻ states in the daughter nuclei, acting cooperatively with the persistence of the N=50 shell gap.

## Method

The author performed fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations for ground states of neutron-rich Ni isotopes using Skyrme energy-density functionals (SkM* and SLy4), then applied proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation (pnQRPA) to compute β-strength distributions including both allowed and first-forbidden transitions. The coordinate-space HFB equations were solved in cylindrical coordinates with a mesh size of Δρ=Δz=0.6 fm and a box boundary condition at (ρ_max, z_max) = (14.7, 14.4) fm, with quasiparticle states truncated at 60 MeV energy cutoff and magnetic quantum number up to Ω=23/2.

## Key numbers

- The constant D used in the β-decay rate formula is 6147 s.
- The ratio of weak axial and vector coupling constants λ = -(g_A/g_V) = 1.2701(25), with a quenching factor q = (1/1.27)² = 0.62 applied for allowed Gamow-Teller transitions.
- The mass difference ΔM_(n-H) = 0.782 MeV between a neutron and a hydrogen atom.
- The nuclear radius is calculated as R = 1.2 × A^(1/3) fm.
- The FF transitions at N=52 gain a factor of approximately (10/5)⁵ = 32 from the phase space factor relative to allowed transitions, based on the energy difference of about 5 MeV between the prominent allowed peak (E_T ∼ -5 MeV) and the FF transitions.
- In the SkM* calculation, the πf_(5/2) level is located above the πp_(3/2) level by 1.5 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the experimental findings of Xu et al. (2014), who measured β-decay half-lives of 20 neutron-rich nuclei in the ⁷⁸Ni region at RIKEN-RIBF and observed a sudden shortening of half-lives beyond N=50. The study contradicts the earlier prediction by Borzov (2003) that first-forbidden transitions play only a minor role in ⁷⁹,⁸⁰Ni, and differs from the calculation of Mustonen and Engel (2016) using the SkO' functional, which produced only monotonic shortening. The results enable improved predictions of β-decay rates for exotic nuclei far from stability, relevant for understanding the r-process nucleosynthesis path around the N=50 waiting point.
