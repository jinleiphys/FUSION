# The physics of helical electron beam in a uniform magnetic field as a testing ground of gauge principle

> According to Bliokh et al., allowing free propagation along the direction of a uniform magnetic field, the familiar Landau electron state can be regarded as a non-diffracting version of the helical electron beam propagating along the magnetic field. Based on this observation, they argued that, while propagating along the magnetic field, the Landau electrons receive characteristic rotation with three different angular velocities, depending on the eigen-value $m$ of the canonical OAM operator, which is generally gauge-variant, and this splitting was in fact experimentally confirmed. Through complete analyses of highly mysterious $m$-dependent rotational dynamics of the quantum Landau states, we try to make clear how and why their observation does not contradict the widely-believed gauge principle.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the experimentally observed splitting of helical electron beams into three rotation regimes depending on the eigenvalue \(m\) of the canonical orbital angular momentum operator does not violate the gauge principle, because the observable rotation is determined by the gauge-invariant probability current rather than by the gauge-variant canonical OAM itself.

## Method

The authors analyze the Landau eigenstates in the symmetric gauge, expressing the Hamiltonian as a sum of a 2D harmonic oscillator and a Zeeman term. They decompose the gauge-invariant probability current into canonical (paramagnetic) and gauge (diamagnetic) contributions, and evaluate the expectation value of the electron's angular velocity \(\langle \omega(r) \rangle = \langle j_\phi(r)/r \rangle\). They then reinterpret the \(m\)-dependent rotation using the quantum mechanical guiding center formalism of Johnson and Lippmann (1949), relating the canonical OAM to the difference between the squared cyclotron radius and the squared guiding-center distance.

## Key numbers

* The three rotation regimes are: \(\langle \omega \rangle = 0\) for \(m < 0\), \(\langle \omega \rangle = \omega_L\) (Larmor frequency) for \(m = 0\), and \(\langle \omega \rangle = \omega_c\) (cyclotron frequency) for \(m > 0\), where \(\omega_c = eB/m_e\) and \(\omega_L = \omega_c/2\).
* The expectation value of the mechanical (kinetic) OAM in any Landau state is \(\langle l_z^{\text{mech}} \rangle = 2n + 1\), independent of \(m\), where \(n\) is the Landau quantum number.
* For the state \((n=10, m=10)\), \(\sqrt{\langle r_c^2 \rangle} = \sqrt{21}\,l_B\) and \(\sqrt{\langle R^2 \rangle} = l_B\); for the state \((n=0, m=-10)\), \(\sqrt{\langle r_c^2 \rangle} = l_B\) and \(\sqrt{\langle R^2 \rangle} = \sqrt{21}\,l_B\), where \(l_B = 1/\sqrt{eB}\) is the magnetic length.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Bliokh et al. (2012) and the experimental confirmation by Schattschneider et al., who observed the \(m\)-dependent rotation of electron helical beams in a uniform magnetic field. The authors provide a clearer physical explanation using the guiding center concept from Johnson and Lippmann (1949), which was also discussed in recent papers by Kitadono et al. (2019) and Enk (2019). The analysis clarifies why the gauge-variant canonical OAM can appear in an observable splitting without contradicting the gauge principle, and it connects to the nucleon spin decomposition problem in quantum chromodynamics.
