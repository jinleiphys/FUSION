# Radiative and exchange corrections for two-neutrino double-beta decay

> We investigate the impact of radiative and atomic exchange corrections in the two-neutrino double-beta ($2־½־²־²$)-decay of $^{100}$Mo. In the calculation of the exchange correction, the electron wave functions are obtained from a modified Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater self-consistent framework that ensures orthogonality between continuum and bound states. The atomic exchange correction causes a steep increase in the low-energy region of the single-electron spectrum, consistent with previous studies on $־²$-decay, while the radiative correction primarily accounts for a 5\% increase in the decay rate of $^{100}$Mo. When combined, the radiative and exchange effects cause a leftward shift of approximately 10 keV in the maximum of the summed electron spectrum. This shift may impact current constraints on parameters governing potential new physics scenarios in $2־½־²־²$-decay. The exchange and radiative corrections are introduced on top of our previous description of $2־½־²־²$-decay, where we used a Taylor expansion for the lepton energy parameters within the nuclear matrix elements denominators. This approach results in multiple components for each observable, controlled by the measurable $־¾_{31}$ and $־¾_{51}$ parameters. We explore the effects of different $־¾_{31}$ and $־¾_{51}$ values, including their experimental measurements, on the total corrected spectra. These refined theoretical predictions can serve as precise inputs for double-beta decay experiments investigating standard and new physics scenarios within $2־½־²־²$-decay.

## Key claim

The radiative and exchange corrections together cause a leftward shift of approximately 10 keV in the maximum of the summed electron spectrum for the two-neutrino double-beta decay of 100Mo, and the atomic exchange correction produces a steep increase in the low-energy region of the single-electron spectrum, but the abstract's claim of a 5% increase in the decay rate is primarily due to the radiative correction rather than the exchange correction.

## Method

The authors calculated the atomic exchange correction using electron wave functions obtained from a modified Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater self-consistent framework that ensures orthogonality between continuum and bound states, extending the exchange correction formalism from beta decay to double-beta decay where the initial nucleus undergoes a charge change of two units. The radiative correction was included using the first-order Sirlin function for virtual photon exchange and real photon emission. These corrections were incorporated into the phase-space factors of the Taylor expansion formalism for the 2־½־²־²-decay rate, which decomposes observables into components controlled by the ־¾31 and ־¾51 parameters.

- The combined radiative and exchange effects cause a leftward shift of approximately 10 keV in the maximum of the summed electron spectrum.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Taylor expansion formalism for 2־½־²־²-decay (Simkovic 2018, Nitescu 2021) by incorporating radiative and atomic exchange corrections that were previously studied only for single beta decay (Harston 1992, Pyper 1998, Nitescu 2023, Nitescu 2024). The corrected spectra can serve as precise inputs for double-beta decay experiments such as KamLAND-Zen and CUPID-Mo that are investigating standard and new physics scenarios, including constraints on beyond-the-Standard-Model parameters and searches for weakly interacting massive particles in liquid Xenon experiments.
