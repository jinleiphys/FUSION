# Physics of Core-Collapse Supernovae in Three Dimensions: a Sneak Preview

> Nonspherical mass motions are a generic feature of core-collapse supernovae, and hydrodynamic instabilities play a crucial role for the explosion mechanism. First successful neutrino-driven explosions could be obtained with self-consistent, first-principle simulations in three spatial dimensions (3D). But 3D models tend to be less prone to explosion than corresponding axisymmetric (2D) ones. This has been explained by 3D turbulence leading to energy cascading from large to small spatial scales, inversely to the 2D case, thus disfavoring the growth of buoyant plumes on the largest scales. Unless the inertia to explode simply reflects a lack of sufficient resolution in relevant regions, it suggests that some important aspect may still be missing for robust and sufficiently energetic neutrino-powered explosions. Such deficits could be associated with progenitor properties like rotation, magnetic fields or pre-collapse perturbations, or with microphysics that could lead to an enhancement of neutrino heating behind the shock. 3D simulations have also revealed new phenomena that are not present in 2D, for example spiral modes of the standing accretion shock instability (SASI) and a stunning dipolar lepton-emission self-sustained asymmetry (LESA). Both impose time- and direction-dependent variations on the detectable neutrino signal. The understanding of these effects and of their consequences is still in its infancy.

## Method

The authors review the status of 3D core-collapse supernova modeling using grid-based hydrodynamics schemes with energy-dependent, three-flavor neutrino transport, comparing results from different groups that employ various transport approximations (flux-limited diffusion, two-moment transport with Boltzmann closure, M1 transport) and different hydrodynamic codes. They analyze turbulent energy spectra from spherical harmonics decomposition of lateral velocities in the gain layer, and examine the growth rates of convection and the standing accretion shock instability (SASI) through the advective-acoustic cycle and the chi parameter criterion for convective onset.

## Key numbers

- Explosion energies from 2D simulations range from 0.34 to 0.88 bethe (1 bethe = 10^51 erg), still increasing at simulation end
- Typical supernova explosion energies range from less than ~10^50 erg to roughly 2.5 × 10^51 erg
- Hypernova explosions can reach several 10^52 erg for progenitor stars above ~20 solar masses
- The chi parameter threshold for convective growth is chi ≳ 2...3, where chi = tau_adv / tau_conv
- Initial density perturbations in the nonlinear regime require delta_rho ≳ O(1%) to rise against the accretion flow
- Parametric 3D simulations found explosions requiring ~15-25% lower driving neutrino luminosity than in 2D
- SASI-dominated explosions in 3D can be obtained with up to ~20% lower driving luminosity than in 2D due to spiral modes
- The convective engine in parametric 3D models was found to be 4% more efficient than in 2D
- Shock revival in some 2D models occurs after only ~200 ms of post-bounce accretion

## Context

This review extends the report given by Janka (2012) on the neutrino-driven mechanism and updates developments in 3D modeling. It contrasts results from multiple groups including the Oak Ridge group (Bruenn et al., 2013, 2016), the Garching group (Summa et al., 2016), O'Connor and Couch (2016), the Princeton group (Dolence et al., 2015; Skinner et al., 2016), and Japanese groups (Suwa et al., 2016; Nakamura et al., 2015). The work enables future systematic code comparisons and coordinated tests among competing groups, which are currently in preparation, and highlights open questions about the role of rotation, magnetic fields, pre-collapse perturbations, and microphysics in achieving robust explosions.
