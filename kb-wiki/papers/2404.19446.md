# Exploring the potential of synthesizing unknown superheavy isotopes via cold-fusion reactions based on the dinuclear system model

> To assess the potential of cold-fusion for synthesizing superheavy nuclei (SHN) with proton numbers 104-113, we systematically calculated 145 naturally occurring projectile-target combinations within the DNS model. Reactions predominantly show maximum cross-sections in the 1n to 2n channels, peaking near the Coulomb barrier with a sum of barrier and Q-value within 30 MeV. The maximum cross-section occurs below the Bass barrier, suggesting either the Bass model's limitation or significant deformation reducing the effective Coulomb barrier. Our calculations align well with experimental data, revealing that more neutron-rich projectiles slightly enhance fusion, though the effect is minor. For fixed targets (Pb, Bi), evaporation residue cross-sections decrease linearly with increasing projectile proton number, attributed to reduced fusion probability and lower fission barriers in heavier SHN. The touching potential $V_{\rm in}$ shows a linear trend with the product of projectile-target proton numbers, with neutron-rich systems exhibiting lower $V_{\rm in}$. Some reactions with $V_{\rm in} < V_{\rm S}$ may involve nucleon transfer before capture. Based on the DNS model, we identified optimal combinations and collision energies for synthesizing SHN with significant cross-sections. Collectively, our findings indicate that cold fusion is a promising avenue for creating proton-rich SHN around the drip line in the Z=104-113 region, offering distinct advantages over alternative mechanisms.

## Key claim

The dinuclear system (DNS) model, when calibrated with an optimal contact potential energy of 186.5 MeV for the benchmark reaction, can systematically predict cold-fusion evaporation residue cross sections for superheavy nuclei with proton numbers 104-113, with the caveat that the maximum cross section occurs below the Bass barrier, suggesting either a limitation of the Bass model or that significant deformation reduces the effective Coulomb barrier.

## Method

The authors employed the dinuclear system (DNS) model to calculate capture, fusion, and survival probabilities for 145 naturally occurring projectile-target combinations. The capture probability was evaluated using the Hill-Wheeler formula with an asymmetric Gaussian barrier distribution, the fusion probability was determined by solving a master equation for nucleon transfer across the potential energy surface, and the survival probability was calculated using a statistical evaporation model based on Weisskopf's theory and the Bohr-Wheeler fission width. The contact potential energy was systematically varied to identify the optimal value that reproduces experimental excitation functions.

## Key numbers

- Optimal contact potential energy identified as V_in = 186.5 MeV for the reaction 58Fe + 208Pb → 266Hs*
- For reactions producing 259Db: maximum 1n ERCS of 2.05 nb for 51V + 208Pb versus 4.36 nb for 50Ti + 209Bi, giving a ratio of 0.47
- For reactions producing 260Sg: maximum 1n ERCS of 0.32 nb for 52Cr + 208Pb versus 0.98 nb for 54Cr + 206Pb, giving a ratio of 0.32
- The 3n evaporation channel ERCS is 1-2 orders of magnitude smaller than the 1n or 2n channels
- The sum of Coulomb barrier and Q-value for reactions showing maximum cross sections is within 30 MeV

## Context

This work extends the DNS model framework previously developed by Feng (2006, 2007) and Adamian (1997, 1998) to systematically predict cold-fusion cross sections for superheavy nuclei in the Z=104-113 region. The calculations are benchmarked against experimental data from GSI (Hofmann 1995, 2000), RIKEN (2004 synthesis of element 113), and other laboratories. The findings enable experimental guidance for synthesizing proton-rich superheavy nuclei around the drip line, with the authors identifying optimal projectile-target combinations and collision energies for future synthesis attempts.
