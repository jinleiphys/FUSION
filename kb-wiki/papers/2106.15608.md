# Fast & accurate emulation of two-body scattering observables without wave functions

> We combine Newton's variational method with ideas from eigenvector continuation to construct a fast & accurate emulator for two-body scattering observables. The emulator will facilitate the application of rigorous statistical methods for interactions that depend smoothly on a set of free parameters. Our approach begins with a trial $K$ or $T$ matrix constructed from a small number of exact solutions to the Lippmann--Schwinger equation. Subsequent emulation only requires operations on small matrices. We provide several applications to short-range potentials with and without the Coulomb interaction and partial-wave coupling. It is shown that the emulator can accurately extrapolate far from the support of the training data. When used to emulate the neutron-proton cross section with a modern chiral interaction as a function of 26 free parameters, it reproduces the exact calculation with negligible error and provides an over 300x improvement in CPU time.

# Fast & Accurate Emulation of Two-Body Scattering Observables Without Wave Functions

## Key claim

The authors combine Newton's variational method with eigenvector continuation to construct an emulator for two-body scattering observables that, when applied to a modern chiral interaction with 26 free parameters, reproduces the exact neutron-proton cross section with negligible error while providing over a 300x improvement in CPU time, though the approach is demonstrated primarily for short-range potentials and requires the potential to depend smoothly on its parameters.

## Method

The emulator constructs a trial K or T matrix as a linear combination of exact solutions to the Lippmann-Schwinger equation computed at a small number of training points in parameter space. Newton's variational method provides a stationary functional that determines the optimal coefficients for the trial matrix at any new parameter value, requiring only operations on small matrices of dimension equal to the number of training points. For coupled channels, the method extends naturally by treating the coupled partial-wave components as a special case of the same formalism. The Coulomb interaction is included via the Vincent-Phatak method, cutting off the Coulomb potential at a finite radius and matching logarithmic derivatives to recover the correct asymptotic behavior.

## Key numbers

- The emulator for the neutron-proton total cross section using the SMS chiral potential at N4LO with cutoff Lambda = 450 MeV provides over 300x improvement in CPU time relative to exact calculation; when the momentum mesh increases from 80 to 160 quadrature points, the speedup factor becomes over 1000x.
- For the coupled 3S1-3D1 channel of np scattering using 12 training points (2 x 6 parameters), the residuals in the on-shell T matrix elements are on the order of 10^{-12} except near singularities.
- The emulated np total cross section has error less than 10^{-10} mb for laboratory energies above 50 MeV, and less than 10^{-8} mb below 50 MeV except near the singular T matrix in the 3S1-3D1 channel.
- For 500 random samples of the NN low-energy constants in the range [-15, 15] (extrapolating 10 units beyond the training range), the average absolute emulator error is less than 10^{-7} mb.
- The proton-alpha 1S0 phase shift emulator uses 4 training points and achieves negligible absolute residuals across center-of-mass energies up to 60 MeV.

## Context

This work extends eigenvector continuation, previously applied to bound-state properties such as binding energies and charge radii by Konig (2019) and Ekstrom (2019), and to two-body scattering via the Kohn variational principle by Furnstahl (2020). The approach differs from the wave-packet continuum discretization method of Miller (2021), which reported averaged errors in the total cross section on the order of 1 mb for the NNLO_opt chiral interaction. The emulator enables rigorous Bayesian parameter estimation, model comparison, and sensitivity analysis for chiral effective field theory interactions, with potential extension to three- and higher-body scattering systems.
