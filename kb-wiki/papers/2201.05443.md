# Emerging concepts in nuclear structure based on the shell model

> Some emerging concepts of nuclear structure are overviewed. (1) Background: the many-body quantum structure of atomic nucleus, a complex system comprising protons and neutrons (called nucleons collectively), has been studied largely based on the idea of the quantum liquid (a la Landau), where nucleons are quasiparticles moving in a (mean) potential well, with weak "residual" interactions between nucleons. The potential is rigid in general, although it can be anisotropic. While this view was a good starting point, it is time to look into kaleidoscopic aspects of the nuclear structure brought in by underlying dynamics and nuclear forces. (2) Methods: exotic features as well as classical issues are investigated from fresh viewpoints based on the shell model and nucleon-nucleon interactions. The 70-year progress of the shell-model approach, including effective nucleon-nucleon interactions, enables us to do this. (3) Results: we go beyond the picture of the solid potential well by activating the monopole interactions of the nuclear forces. This produces notable consequences in key features such as the shell/magic structure, the shape deformation, the dripline, etc. These consequences are understood with emerging concepts such as shell evolution (incl. type-II), T-plot, self-organization (for collective bands), triaxial-shape dominance, new dripline mechanism, etc. The resulting predictions and analyses agree with experiment. (4) Conclusion: atomic nuclei are surprisingly richer objects than initially thought.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the rigid mean-potential picture of nuclear structure must be replaced by a dynamic view where monopole interactions of nuclear forces actively reshape shell structure, deformation, and driplines, though the author acknowledges this is an overview of concepts in which he was personally involved rather than a comprehensive review.

## Method

The author uses the shell model with effective nucleon-nucleon interactions, decomposing the Hamiltonian into monopole and multipole components. Effective single-particle energies (ESPEs) are computed from monopole matrix elements of central, tensor, two-body spin-orbit, and three-nucleon forces, and their evolution with proton and neutron numbers is tracked across the nuclear chart using interactions such as the VMU interaction.

## Key numbers

- The spin-orbit strength parameter f is empirically about -20A^{-2/3} MeV (from eq. (2-132) of Bohr and Mottelson).
- First 2+ excitation energies show spikes at N=16, 32, and 34 in addition to conventional magic numbers, with values about a factor two higher than the overall trend for nuclei such as 24O16, 52Ca32, and 54Ca34.
- The N=34 magic number was predicted in 2001 and experimentally verified 12 years later by Steppenbeck et al. (2013) via measured 2+ energy levels.
- The loose-binding contribution to the 2p3/2-2p1/2 splitting in the Si-S region is 0.06 MeV against a 1.5 MeV splitting itself.

## Context

This work extends the Mayer-Jensen shell model (1949) by incorporating monopole interactions that were absent in the original independent-particle picture, building on earlier monopole studies by Bansal (1964), Baranger (1970), Poves (1981), and others. It enables predictions of new magic numbers (e.g., N=34), explains shape coexistence via type-II shell evolution, and provides a new dripline mechanism based on monopole-quadrupole interplay, with implications for understanding exotic nuclei and nucleosynthesis paths.
