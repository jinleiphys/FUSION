# Properties of a relativistic equation of state for collapse-driven supernovae

> We study characteristics of the relativistic equation of state (EOS) for collapse-driven supernovae, which is derived by relativistic nuclear many body theory. Recently the relativistic EOS table has become available as a new complete set of physical EOS for numerical simulations of supernova explosion. We examine this EOS table by using general relativistic hydrodynamics of the gravitational collapse and bounce of supernova cores. In order to study dense matter in dynamical situation, we perform simplified calculations of core collapse and bounce by following adiabatic collapse with the fixed electron fraction for a series of progenitor models. This is intended to give us ``approximate models'' of prompt explosion. We investigate the profiles of thermodynamical quantities and the compositions during collapse and bounce. We also perform the calculations with the Lattimer-Swesty EOS to compare the properties of dense matter. As a measure of the stiffness of the EOS, we examine the explosion energy of the prompt explosion with electron capture totally suppressed. We study the derivative of the thermodynamical quantities obtained by the relativistic EOS to discuss the convective condition in neutron-rich environment, which may be important in the delayed explosion.

## Key claim

The relativistic equation of state (EOS) based on the relativistic mean field (RMF) theory with the TM1 parameter set, when used in simplified adiabatic collapse calculations with fixed electron fraction, produces prompt explosions for progenitor models with iron core masses below approximately 1.4 solar masses, but the explosion energies are generally smaller than those obtained with the Lattimer-Swesty EOS (180 MeV incompressibility), serving as a measure of the stiffness of the relativistic EOS rather than a realistic prediction of supernova outcomes.

## Method

The authors performed general relativistic, spherically symmetric hydrodynamical simulations of adiabatic core collapse and bounce, using the relativistic EOS table (RMF with TM1) and, for comparison, the Lattimer-Swesty EOS with 180 MeV incompressibility. They fixed the electron fraction at its initial value from presupernova models (Woosley-Weaver 1995 and Nomoto 1997) and suppressed electron capture and neutrino transfer entirely, creating "approximate models" of prompt explosion. The simulations tracked thermodynamic profiles and compositions during collapse and bounce for a series of progenitor masses from 11 to 20 solar masses.

## Key numbers

* The relativistic EOS (TM1) has an incompressibility of 281 MeV and a symmetry energy of 36.9 MeV at saturation density; the Lattimer-Swesty EOS (180 MeV) has a symmetry energy of 29.3 MeV.
* Explosion energies for successful prompt explosions with the relativistic EOS: 1.7 foe (foe = 10^51 erg) for 11 and 12 solar mass models (WW95), 1.5 foe for 15 solar mass (WW95), and 1.8, 1.7, and 1.7 foe for 13, 15, and 18 solar mass models (N97), respectively.
* For the 15 solar mass model (WW95) with the Lattimer-Swesty EOS, the explosion energy was 1.9 foe, larger than the 1.5 foe from the relativistic EOS.
* Peak central density at bounce for the 15 solar mass model (WW95): 4.0 x 10^14 g/cm^3 with the relativistic EOS, compared to 5.3 x 10^14 g/cm^3 with the Lattimer-Swesty EOS.
* The central electron fraction in the initial 15 solar mass model (WW95) was 0.42; the free proton fraction in the relativistic EOS was about one order of magnitude smaller than in the Lattimer-Swesty EOS.
* The maximum neutron star mass for cold matter in the RMF with TM1 is 2.2 solar masses.
* The relativistic EOS table covers density from 10^5.1 to 10^15.4 g/cm^3, electron fraction from 0.0 to 0.56, and temperature from 0 to 100 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the study of dense matter EOS for supernova simulations by examining the newly available relativistic EOS table from Shen et al. (1998a, 1998b), which is based on the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory and RMF theory, in contrast to the non-relativistic compressible liquid-drop model of Lattimer and Swesty (1991) that has been widely used. The simplified adiabatic collapse calculations provide a baseline for understanding the relativistic EOS behavior before proceeding to more realistic simulations with full neutrino transport, which the authors note are in preparation. The results highlight the role of the larger symmetry energy (36.9 MeV vs. 29.3 MeV) in reducing the free proton fraction, which could affect electron capture rates and lepton fraction evolution in more detailed studies, and they connect to ongoing work on weak-interaction rates for nuclei in the A=45-65 range by Caurier et al. (1999) and Langanke et al. (2000).
