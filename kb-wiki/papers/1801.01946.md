# Ground-state properties of light kaonic nuclei signaling symmetry energy at high densities

> A sensitive correlation between the ground-state properties of light kaonic nuclei and the symmetry energy at high densities is constructed under the framework of relativistic mean-field theory. Taking oxygen isotopes as an example, we see that a high-density core is produced in kaonic oxygen nuclei, due to the strongly attractive antikaon-nucleon interaction. It is found that the $1S_{1/2}$ state energy in the high-density core of kaonic nuclei can directly probe the variation of the symmetry energy at supranormal nuclear density, and a sensitive correlation between the neutron skin thickness and the symmetry energy at supranormal density is established directly. Meanwhile, the sensitivity of the neutron skin thickness to the low-density slope of the symmetry energy is greatly increased in the corresponding kaonic nuclei. These sensitive relationships are established upon the fact that the isovector potential in the central region of kaonic nuclei becomes very sensitive to the variation of the symmetry energy. These findings might provide another perspective to constrain high-density symmetry energy, and await experimental verification in the future.

# Ground-state properties of light kaonic nuclei signaling symmetry energy at high densities

## Key claim
The ground-state properties of light kaonic oxygen isotopes, particularly the neutron skin thickness and the $1S_{1/2}$ single-particle energy, show a sensitive correlation with the symmetry energy at supranormal nuclear density (around 1.5 times normal nuclear density), but only because the strongly attractive antikaon-nucleon interaction creates a high-density core in the nucleus that amplifies the isovector potential's sensitivity to symmetry energy variations. The authors explicitly state that these findings "await experimental verification in the future" and that the existence of kaonic nuclei "has not yet been verified."

## Method
The authors used relativistic mean-field (RMF) theory with the TM2 parameter set as a starting point, incorporating a coupling term $\Lambda_v$ between the isoscalar vector and isovector vector fields to simulate different density dependencies of the symmetry energy. They solved the coupled Dirac equation for nucleons and Klein-Gordon equation for the $K^-$ meson self-consistently, with the $K^-$ optical potential depth fixed at -100 MeV at saturation density, and adjusted $\Lambda_v$ and $g_\rho$ to vary the symmetry energy while keeping the symmetry energy fixed at 24.93 MeV at $0.7\rho_0$.

## Key numbers
- The symmetry energy at normal nuclear density ($\rho_0 = 0.132$ fm$^{-3}$) varies from 35.90 MeV (for $\Lambda_v = 0.0$) to 29.96 MeV (for $\Lambda_v = 0.030$) across the parameter sets.
- The density slope L at $0.7\rho_0$ ranges from 106.53 MeV (for $\Lambda_v = 0.0$) down to 53.31 MeV (for $\Lambda_v = 0.030$).
- The symmetry energy at $1.5\rho_0$ ranges from 55.19 MeV (for $\Lambda_v = 0.0$) down to 38.71 MeV (for $\Lambda_v = 0.030$).
- The isovector potential in the central region of $^{22}_{K^-}O$ shows a 55% variation as the density slope L changes from 53.31 MeV to 106.53 MeV.
- The $K^-$ optical potential depth at saturation density was taken as -100 MeV, based on heavy-ion collision data from ZQF (2014).

## Context
This work extends previous RMF studies of kaonic nuclei (Akaishi and Yamazaki, 2002; Dote et al., 2007; Yang et al., 2014) by establishing a direct connection between kaonic nuclei properties and the high-density symmetry energy, a connection that had "seldom been investigated" before. The approach builds on the method of Horowitz and Piekarewicz (2001) for adjusting the high-density symmetry energy via the $\Lambda_v$ coupling term. The findings suggest that kaonic nuclei could serve as a new probe for constraining the symmetry energy at supranormal densities, complementing existing constraints from neutron star observations (Watts, 2016; Lattimer, 2016) and heavy-ion collisions (Li, 2008; Xiao, 2009; Feng, 2010; Ru, 2011), and potentially enabling experimental verification through photo-nucleus or pion-nucleus reactions such as $\gamma + ^{22}$O $\rightarrow$ $K^+ + ^{22}_{K^-}$O or $\pi^- + ^{22}$F $\rightarrow$ $K^{*+} + ^{22}_{K^-}$O.
