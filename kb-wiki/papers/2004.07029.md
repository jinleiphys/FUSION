# Production of light nuclei at colliders -- coalescence vs. thermal model

> The production of light nuclei in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is well described by both the thermal model, where light nuclei are in equilibrium with hadrons of all species present in a fireball, and by the coalescence model, where light nuclei are formed due to final-state interactions after the fireball decays. We present and critically discuss the two models and further on we consider two proposals to falsify one of the models. The first proposal is to measure a yield of exotic nuclide $^4{\rm Li}$ and compare it to that of $^4{\rm He}$. The ratio of yields of the nuclides is quite different in the thermal and coalescence models. The second proposal is to measure a hadron-deuteron correlation function which carries information whether a deuteron is emitted from a fireball together with all other hadrons, as assumed in the thermal model, or a deuteron is formed only after nucleons are emitted, as in the coalescence model. The $p\! -\!^3{\rm He}$ correlation function is of interest in context of both proposals: it is needed to obtain the yield of $^4{\rm Li}$ which decays into $p$ and $^3{\rm He}$, but the correlation function can also tell us about an origin of $^3{\rm He}$.

## Key claim

The thermal model and coalescence model predict similar yields of light nuclei (differing by less than a factor of 2 for deuterons in Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV), but two specific measurements can falsify one model: the yield ratio of exotic nuclide \(^4{\rm Li}\) to \(^4{\rm He}\), and the hadron-deuteron correlation function which reveals whether deuterons are emitted directly from the fireball or formed later via final-state interactions.

## Method

The author analytically compares the deuteron-to-proton yield ratio predicted by the thermal model (using a classical ideal gas partition function with chemical freeze-out temperature \(T_{\rm chem}=156\) MeV and a parameter \(\lambda_p\) accounting for resonance decays) and by the coalescence model (using a quantum-mechanical formation rate with a Gaussian source function and deuteron wave function). The ratio \(Q = D_{\rm CM}/D_{\rm TM}\) is computed for four centrality bins of Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV, using experimental data for deuteron yields, mean transverse momenta, and proton source radii from femtoscopic measurements. Two falsification proposals are then presented: measuring the \(^4{\rm Li}/^4{\rm He}\) yield ratio, and measuring hadron-deuteron or \(p-^3{\rm He}\) correlation functions to infer the source radius.

## Key numbers

- The thermal model deuteron-to-proton ratio at \(T_{\rm chem}=156\) MeV reproduces the experimental value \(3.6 \times 10^{-3}\) from Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV when \(\lambda_p = 2.51\).
- The ratio \(Q = D_{\rm CM}/D_{\rm TM}\) ranges from 0.51 to 0.65 across four centrality bins (0-10%, 10-20%, 20-40%, 40-60%) for Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV, with parameters: \(V_{\rm chem}\) from 696 to 3590 fm\(^3\), \(T_{\rm kin}\) from 760 to 900 MeV, and \(R_{\rm kin}\) from 2.5 to 4.3 fm.
- The deuteron root-mean-square radius is \(R_D = 2\) fm.
- The thermal model predicts the \(^4{\rm Li}/^4{\rm He}\) yield ratio is about 5 due to five spin states of \(^4{\rm Li}\) versus one of \(^4{\rm He}\).
- The source radius for deuterons differs by the factor \(\sqrt{4/3}\) between the thermal and coalescence scenarios; for \(^3{\rm He}\) the factor is \(\sqrt{3/2}\).

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing observation by Das Gupta (1981) that coalescence and thermal models give similar light-nucleus yields, confirmed analytically by Mrowczynski (2016) for deuterons. It proposes two experimental tests to distinguish the models: measuring the \(^4{\rm Li}/^4{\rm He}\) ratio (first suggested by Mrowczynski (2016) and developed by Bazak (2018, 2020)) and measuring hadron-deuteron correlation functions (proposed by Mrowczynski (2019)). The work enables future experiments at RHIC and LHC to determine whether light nuclei are emitted directly from a fireball or formed via final-state coalescence, resolving a long-standing puzzle in heavy-ion physics.
