# Mapped interacting boson model for nuclear structure studies

> The present status of the mapped interacting boson model studies on nuclear structure is reviewed. With the assumption that the nuclear surface deformation induced by the multi-nucleon dynamics is simulated by bosonic degrees of freedom, the interacting-boson Hamiltonian that provides energy spectra and wave functions is determined by mapping the potential energy surface that is obtained from self-consistent mean-field calculations based on the energy density functional onto the corresponding energy surface of the boson system. This procedure has been shown to be valid in general cases of the quadrupole collective states, and has allowed for systematic studies on spectroscopic properties of medium-heavy and heavy nuclei, including those that are far from the line of $β$ stability. The method has been extended to study nuclear structure phenomena that include shape phase transitions and coexistence, octupole deformation and collectivity, and the coupling of the single-particle to collective degrees of freedom, which is crucial to describe structures of odd nuclei, and $β$ and $ββ$ decays.

## Key claim

The mapped interacting boson model (IBM) provides a systematic method for predicting spectroscopic properties of medium-heavy and heavy nuclei without fitting to experimental data, but the approach systematically underestimates moments of inertia in strongly deformed rotational nuclei due to the limited degrees of freedom of the $s$ and $d$ bosons.

## Method

The constrained self-consistent mean-field (SCMF) calculations using a nuclear energy density functional (EDF) and pairing interaction produce a potential energy surface (PES) in terms of quadrupole deformations $(\beta,\gamma)$. The expectation value of the IBM-2 Hamiltonian in the boson coherent state, which gives an analytical bosonic energy surface, is fitted to the SCMF PES near the global minimum (up to approximately 2-3 MeV above the minimum) to determine the IBM-2 parameters $\epsilon_d$, $\kappa$, $\chi_\nu$, $\chi_\pi$, and the proportionality constant $C_\beta$. The mapped IBM-2 Hamiltonian is then diagonalized numerically to obtain energy eigenvalues and wave functions with good symmetry quantum numbers.

## Key numbers

- The proportionality constant $C_\beta$ relating bosonic to fermionic $\beta$ deformation typically takes a value $C_\beta \approx 5$, indicating bosonic $\beta$ deformation is larger than the fermionic one.
- For $^{148}$Sm, the SCMF PES exhibits a weakly deformed minimum at $\beta \approx 0.15$; for $^{154}$Sm, a well-developed prolate minimum at $\beta \approx 0.35$ is obtained.
- The calculated energy ratio $R_{4/2}$ for $^{148}$Sm is approximately 2.61 (vibrational-like), while for Sm nuclei with $N \geq 92$ the ratio is approximately 3.3 (rotational-like).
- The derived $\kappa$ value in the EDF-mapped IBM-2 is generally larger in magnitude than phenomenological values by approximately a factor of 2-3.
- The $\chi_\nu$ parameter approaches the SU(3) limit value $-\sqrt{7}/2 \approx -1.32$ in the deformed region with $N > 90$.
- The $2^+_1$ energy level for $^{134}$Ba is 605 keV experimentally, used for normalization in the E(5) model comparison.

## Context

This review extends the earlier Otsuka-Arima-Iachello (OAI) mapping method (Otsuka, Arima, and Iachello, 1970s-1980s) by using nuclear EDF-based SCMF calculations instead of shell-model truncation, enabling systematic studies of nuclei far from stability where experimental data are unavailable. The method enables predictions for exotic nuclei such as $^{202-214}$W and $^{204-216}$Os in the region south-east of $^{208}$Pb, and allows studies of shape phase transitions, octupole deformation, and odd-nuclei structure including $\beta$ and $\beta\beta$ decays. The approach has been applied to shape transitions in Ru, Pd, Xe, Ba, Yb, Hf, W, Os, Pt, and Kr isotopes across multiple mass regions.
