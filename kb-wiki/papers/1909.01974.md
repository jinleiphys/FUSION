# Chiral Waves on the Fermi-Dirac Sea: Quantum Superfluidity and the Axial Anomaly

> We show that as a result of the axial anomaly, massless fermions at zero temperature define a relativistic quantum superfluid. The anomaly pole implies the existence of a gapless Chiral Density Wave (CDW), i.e. an axion-like acoustic mode of an irrotational and dissipationless Hamiltonian perfect fluid, that is a correlated fermion/anti-fermion pair excitation of the Fermi-Dirac sea. In $D\!=\!2$ dimensions the chiral superfluid effective action coincides with that of the Schwinger model as $e\rightarrow 0$, and the CDW acoustic mode is precisely the Schwinger boson. Since this identity holds also at zero chiral chemical potential, the Dirac vacuum itself may be viewed as a quantum superfluid state. The CDW collective boson is a $U(1)$ chiral phase field, which is gapless as a result of a novel, non-linear realization of Goldstone's theorem, extended to this case of symmetry breaking by an anomaly. A new local form of the axial anomaly bosonic effective action in any $D$ even spacetime is given, consistent with superfluidity, and its quantization is shown to be required by the anomalous Schwinger terms in fermion current commutators. In QED$_4$ this collective Goldstone mode appears as a massless pole in the axial anomaly triangle diagram, and is responsible for the macroscopic non-dissipative currents of the Chiral Magnetic and Chiral Separation Effects, as well as the Anomalous Hall Effect. In a constant uniform magnetic field an exact dimensional reduction from $D\!=\!4$ to $D\!=\!2$ occurs and the collective $e^+e^-$ CDW chiral pair excitation propagating along the magnetic field direction is a Chiral Magnetic Wave, which acquires a mass gap $M^2\!=\! e^{3}B/2π^{2}$. Possible realizations and tests of the theory of collective bosonic excitations due to the anomaly in Dirac/Weyl materials are briefly discussed.

## Key claim

Massless fermions at zero temperature define a relativistic quantum superfluid as a direct consequence of the axial anomaly, with the gapless Chiral Density Wave (CDW) collective mode existing even in the limit of vanishing chiral chemical potential, implying that the Dirac vacuum itself may be viewed as a quantum superfluid state.

## Method

The authors construct a variational action principle for an ideal chiral fluid incorporating the axial anomaly, using a Clebsch potential (scalar phase field) to describe the dissipationless, irrotational flow. They then show that in D=2 dimensions, the bosonized form of massless free fermions coincides exactly with this chiral fluid description, and extend the analysis to D=4 dimensions by demonstrating that the massless pole in the axial anomaly triangle diagram gives rise to the same collective bosonic excitation responsible for macroscopic non-dissipative currents such as the Chiral Magnetic Effect and Chiral Separation Effect.

## Key numbers

* In D=2 dimensions, the axial anomaly divergence is given by \(\partial_a \tilde{j}^a = \frac{1}{2\pi} \epsilon^{ab}F_{ab} = \frac{^*F}{\pi}\)
* In the Schwinger model in D=2, the Schwinger boson acquires a mass gap \(M^2 = e^2/\pi\) for finite coupling \(e\)
* In QED\(_4\) with a constant uniform magnetic field, the Chiral Magnetic Wave acquires a mass gap \(M^2 = e^3 B / 2\pi^2\)
* For free massless fermions at finite chiral density in QED\(_4\), the collective boson propagates at sound speed \(v_s^2 = dp/d\epsilon = 1/3\)

## Context

This work extends the connection between macroscopic superfluid behavior and microscopic quantum field theory, building on the recognition that the axial anomaly implies a massless boson pole first identified in the anomalous triangle diagram by Dolgov and Zakharov (1971) and later linked to fermion pairing by Giannotti and Mottola (2008) and Blaschke et al. (2014). The paper provides a rigorous derivation showing that the bosonized effective action of massless QED\(_2\) coincides with the chiral superfluid effective action, and extends this to D=4 dimensions where the collective mode is responsible for the Chiral Magnetic Effect, Chiral Separation Effect, and Anomalous Hall Effect in Weyl semimetals. The results enable the treatment of the Dirac vacuum as a quantum superfluid and provide a framework for testing these collective bosonic excitations in Dirac/Weyl materials.
