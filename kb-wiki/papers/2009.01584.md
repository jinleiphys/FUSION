# A fast centrality-meter for heavy-ion collisions at the CBM experiment

> A new method of event characterization based on Deep Learning is presented. The PointNet models can be used for fast, online event-by-event impact parameter determination at the CBM experiment. For this study, UrQMD and the CBM detector simulation are used to generate Au+Au collision events at 10 AGeV which are then used to train and evaluate PointNet based architectures. The models can be trained on features like the hit position of particles in the CBM detector planes, tracks reconstructed from the hits or combinations thereof. The Deep Learning models reconstruct impact parameters from 2-14 fm with a mean error varying from -0.33 to 0.22 fm. For impact parameters in the range of 5-14 fm, a model which uses the combination of hit and track information of particles has a relative precision of 4-9 % and a mean error of -0.33 to 0.13 fm. In the same range of impact parameters, a model with only track information has a relative precision of 4-10 % and a mean error of -0.18 to 0.22 fm. This new method of event-classification is shown to be more accurate and less model dependent than conventional methods and can utilize the performance boost of modern GPU processor units.

# A fast centrality-meter for heavy-ion collisions at the CBM experiment

## Key claim
PointNet-based deep learning models can determine the impact parameter of individual Au+Au collisions at 10 AGeV from raw detector hit or track information with a mean error of -0.33 to 0.22 fm for impact parameters from 2-14 fm, but the method's accuracy depends on the physics model used to generate training data and has not yet been validated against real experimental data or realistic detector noise.

## Method
The authors used the UrQMD 3.4 event generator to produce Au+Au collisions at 10 AGeV, then transported particles through a full Geant3 simulation of the CBM detector in its electron-hadron configuration (MVD and STS silicon detectors). Four PointNet architectures were trained on different input features: hit positions in the MVD only, hit positions in the STS only, reconstructed tracks from both detectors (12-dimensional features per track), and a combination of MVD hits plus tracks. The models were trained on 10^5 events with uniformly sampled impact parameters from 0-16 fm using mean squared error loss, then tested on separate datasets with fixed impact parameters and with a realistic bdb distribution.

## Key numbers
- Impact parameter reconstruction from 2-14 fm yields a mean error varying from -0.33 to 0.22 fm across all deep learning models
- For impact parameters 5-14 fm, the HT-combi model (hits + tracks) achieves a relative precision of 4-9% with mean error -0.33 to 0.13 fm
- For impact parameters 5-14 fm, the MS-tracks model achieves a relative precision of 4-10% with mean error -0.18 to 0.22 fm
- The MS-tracks model processes 1092 events per second on an Nvidia Geforce RTX 2080 Ti GPU
- All models achieved an R^2 value of approximately 0.98 on validation data
- The Polyfit baseline (third-order polynomial fit to track multiplicity) shows mean errors fluctuating between -0.7 and 0.4 fm for events in the 5-14 fm range
- When the physics model was modified (doubling pion production by reducing Delta-baryon absorption cross section), deep learning models showed a shift in mean error of up to 0.32 fm, compared to 0.53 fm for the polynomial fit

## Context
This work extends previous machine learning approaches to impact parameter determination in heavy-ion collisions, such as the feed-forward networks used by Bass (1993) and David (1994), and the neural network studies by Haddad (1996) and De Sanctis (2009). Unlike those earlier studies which used event generator output with simple acceptance filters, this work incorporates a full detector simulation (CbmRoot with Geant3) and uses raw detector hits as input, enabling online event selection before track reconstruction. The method enables fast, event-by-event centrality determination at the CBM experiment at FAIR, which is important for fluctuation and correlation studies sensitive to the QCD phase transition. The authors note that the approach can be generalized to other experiments such as ALICE at the LHC or HADES at SIS18, and could be extended to tasks beyond impact parameter determination such as identification of rare processes or detection of the QCD phase transition.
