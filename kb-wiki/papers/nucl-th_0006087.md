# Fourth Order Algorithms for Solving the Multivariable Langevin Equation and the Kramers Equation

> We develop a fourth order simulation algorithm for solving the stochastic Langevin equation. The method consists of identifying solvable operators in the Fokker-Planck equation, factorizing the evolution operator for small time steps to fourth order and implementing the factorization process numerically. A key contribution of this work is to show how certain double commutators in the factorization process can be simulated in practice. The method is general, applicable to the multivariable case, and systematic, with known procedures for doing fourth order factorizations. The fourth order convergence of the resulting algorithm allowed very large time steps to be used. In simulating the Brownian dynamics of 121 Yukawa particles in two dimensions, the converged result of a first order algorithm can be obtained by using time steps 50 times as large. To further demostrate the versatility of our method, we derive two new classes of fourth order algorithms for solving the simpler Kramers equation without requiring the derivative of the force. The convergence of many fourth order algorithms for solving this equation are compared.

## Key claim

Phase transitions can be sharply defined in "Small" systems (where the linear dimension is of the characteristic range of the interaction) without invoking the thermodynamic limit, by analyzing the topology of the curvature determinant of the micro-canonical entropy surface as a function of conserved quantities.

## Method

The author defines entropy via Boltzmann's formula as the logarithm of the volume of the energy-shell sub-manifold in 6N-dimensional phase space, then classifies phase transitions by the sign and topology of the determinant of the curvature matrix of the micro-canonical entropy surface s(e,n) with respect to energy density e and particle density n. The eigenvectors of this curvature matrix define the order parameter direction, and regions of negative determinant correspond to first-order phase transitions with convex intruders in the entropy surface.

## Key numbers

- The Potts lattice gas example uses a system of 50*50 lattice points with three spin components (q=3).
- The curvature determinant det(e,n) is defined as the product of eigenvalues λ1 and λ2, where λ2 is always negative and λ1 can be positive or negative.
- Regions with det(e,n) > 0 correspond to a single stable phase (concave entropy), while det(e,n) < 0 indicates phase separation and first-order transition.
- The multi-critical point Pm is identified where det(e,n) = 0 and the gradient of det(e,n) is zero in a two-dimensional neighborhood.

## Context

This work extends the micro-canonical approach to thermodynamics pioneered by Boltzmann, in contrast to the canonical ensemble approach of Gibbs (1902) and the information-theoretic entropy of Balian (1991). It directly contradicts the textbook claim that phase transitions exist only in the thermodynamic limit (van Hove, 1949) and builds on earlier work by Gross and collaborators on nuclear multifragmentation as a first-order phase transition in small systems (Gross 1990, 1995, 1997). The approach enables thermodynamic analysis of hot nuclei, atomic clusters, and astrophysical systems under self-gravity where the thermodynamic limit is inapplicable.
