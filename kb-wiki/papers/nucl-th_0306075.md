# Entropy puzzle in small exploding systems

> We use a simple hard-core gas model to study the dynamics of small exploding systems. The system is initially prepared in a thermalized state in a spherical container and then allowed to expand freely into the vacuum. We follow the expansion dynamics by recording the coordinates and velocities of all particles until their last collision points (freeze-out). We have found that the entropy per particle calculated for the ensemble of freeze-out points is very close to the initial value. This is in apparent contradiction with the Joule experiment in which the entropy grows when the gas expands irreversibly into a larger volume.

## Key claim

The entropy per particle calculated from the freeze-out field of a freely expanding hard-core gas is very close to the initial value, in apparent contradiction with the Joule experiment where entropy grows during irreversible expansion into a larger volume; the authors find that the entropy increase is at most about 20% for the smallest system (N=50) and approaches zero for larger systems (N=400), suggesting that free expansion into vacuum is approximately isentropic at freeze-out before particles hit any container wall.

## Method

The authors simulate a classical non-relativistic gas of identical hard-core balls (radius 0.5 fm, mass 940 MeV/c²) initially thermalized in a spherical container at density 0.14 fm⁻³ and temperature 78 MeV. After a cooking stage to ensure equilibration, the container wall is removed and the gas expands freely into vacuum; all particles are tracked until their last collision (freeze-out), recording coordinates and velocities. The freeze-out field is divided into spherical shells, and for each shell the collective velocity, temperature, and entropy per particle are calculated using ideal-gas formulae, then summed to obtain total freeze-out entropy and compared with the initial entropy.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature: 78 MeV; initial average energy per particle: 118 MeV; initial density: 0.14 fm⁻³.
- Sound velocity at initial temperature: approximately 0.186c.
- Systems simulated: N = 50, 100, 200, and 400 particles.
- Initial entropy per particle: 2.68 (N=50), 2.69 (N=100), 2.71 (N=200), 2.70 (N=400).
- Final freeze-out entropy per particle: 3.27 (N=50), 3.07 (N=100), 2.97 (N=200), 2.75 (N=400).
- Freeze-out fraction (fraction of particles that undergo at least one collision before freeze-out): 0.60 (N=50), 0.69 (N=100), 0.73 (N=200), 0.79 (N=400).
- Bulk freeze-out density: approximately 0.1 particles/fm³, nearly independent of system size.
- Peak collective velocity in tail region: 0.5–0.6 (in units of c).
- Average number of collisions per particle scales roughly as √N: about 1 for N=50, 2–3 for N=400.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic simulations of expanding matter (Bondorf et al., 2002) which used a different entropy definition and found incomplete equilibration, and contrasts with the traditional Joule experiment where entropy increases during fast irreversible expansion. The results justify the common assumption in thermal and hydrodynamic models of heavy-ion collisions that matter expansion is approximately isentropic, and enable the use of such models for medium and heavy nuclei collisions; the paper also clarifies that entropy production in the Joule experiment occurs not during free expansion but during subsequent re-equilibration in the larger volume.
