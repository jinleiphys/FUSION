# Coulomb breakup of $^6\rm{Li}$ into $α\,+\,\rm{d}$ in the field of ion $^{208}\rm{Pb}$

> The triple differential cross section of $^{208}\rm{Pb}(^6\rm{Li};α,\rm{d})^{208}\rm{Pb}$ elastic Coulomb breakup is calculated using the semiclassical method. We fit the parameters of the Woods-Saxon potential using the experimental $α-\rm{d}$ phase shifts for different states to describe the relative motion of $α$-particle and deuteron. In order to check the validity of the two particle approach for $α-d$ system we apply a potential model to describe the $^2\rm{H}(α,γ)^6\rm{Li}$ radiative capture. Our results for the Coulomb breakup of $^6\rm{Li}$ show large value of the forward-backward asymmetry of the $α$-particle and deuteron emission around zero energy in the $^6\rm{Li}$ center-of-mass (c.m.) system. Comparison of the results of our calculation with experimental data gives evidence for the dominance of the Coulomb dissociation mechanism at the $α-d$ relative energy larger than $E_{α\,d}>300\,\rm{keV}$ and sufficiently big contribution of nuclear distortion at $E_{α\,d}$ near zero energy, but essentially smaller than the value reported in Ref.[F. Hammache et al. Phys.Rev. C82, 065802 (2010)].

## Key claim

The Coulomb dissociation mechanism dominates the $^{208}\rm{Pb}(^6\rm{Li},\alpha d)^{208}\rm{Pb}$ breakup for $\alpha$-$d$ relative energies above 300 keV, while nuclear distortion contributes significantly near zero energy but is substantially smaller than the contribution reported by Hammache et al. (2010).

## Method

The authors calculated the triple differential cross section for elastic Coulomb breakup of $^6\rm{Li}$ on $^{208}\rm{Pb}$ at 156 MeV collision energy using semiclassical time-dependent perturbation theory for the Coulomb contribution and diffraction theory for the nuclear breakup contribution. They fitted Woods-Saxon potential parameters to experimental $\alpha$-$d$ elastic scattering phase shifts for $S$, $P$, and $D$ states, and validated the two-body $\alpha$-$d$ approach by calculating the $^2\rm{H}(\alpha,\gamma)^6\rm{Li}$ radiative capture cross section. The bound state wave function was adjusted via a phase-equivalent potential to yield an asymptotic normalization coefficient (ANC) of 2.3 fm$^{-1/2}$.

## Key numbers

- Collision energy of $^6\rm{Li}$ beam: 156 MeV; scattering angle range: $2^\circ$-$6^\circ$ (laboratory frame)
- Woods-Saxon potential depths: $V_0 = 60.73$ MeV for the $^3S_1$ state; $V_0 = 57.0$ MeV with $V_{sl} = 4.0$ MeV for $^0P_1$, $^1P_1$, $^2P_1$ states; $V_0 = 55.9$ MeV with $V_{sl} = 4.0$ MeV for $^1D_1$, $^2D_1$ states; $V_0 = 55.9$ MeV with $V_{sl} = 5.06$ MeV for the $^3D_1$ state
- ANC of the $^6\rm{Li}$ ground state used in calculations: $C = 2.3$ fm$^{-1/2}$ (obtained via phase-equivalent potential with $\lambda = 1.38$)
- Calculated astrophysical $S$ factor at zero energy: $S(0) = 1.22$ MeV nb, with $S_{E1}(0) = 1.01$ MeV nb and $S_{E2}(0) = 0.21$ MeV nb
- Black-sphere radii $R_{bl}$ fitted from $\chi^2$ minimization: 6.30 fm (at $2^\circ$), 6.94 fm (at $3^\circ$), 6.34 fm (at $4^\circ$), 6.25 fm (at $6^\circ$)
- At $E_{\alpha d} = 100$ keV, the $E1$ Coulomb breakup cross section is smaller than the $E2$ one by a factor of 70; at $E_{\alpha d} = 10$ keV, the ratio is 1/24

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Kiener et al. (1991) by calculating triple differential cross sections at multiple scattering angles ($2^\circ$, $3^\circ$, $4^\circ$, $6^\circ$) rather than only at $3^\circ$, and by including both Coulomb and nuclear breakup contributions. The results contradict the claim of Hammache et al. (2010) of a very large nuclear breakup contribution, finding instead that nuclear distortion increases the total cross section by at most a factor of 2 above $E_{\alpha d} > 0.8$ MeV. The study enables improved extraction of the astrophysical $S$ factor for the $d(\alpha,\gamma)^6\rm{Li}$ reaction from Coulomb breakup data, though the authors note that higher-order effects (three-body Coulomb effects in the final state) may be needed to resolve remaining discrepancies near zero relative energy.
