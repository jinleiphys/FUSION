# Abnormal dense and dilute nuclear systems

> Researchers have long been interested in exotic states of matter. In the early 1970s, Migdal proposed the existence of metastable or stable nuclei containing a pion condensate, and Bodmer posited the existence of collapsed nuclei with a quark core. Lee and Wick proposed that compressed nuclear matter could undergo a phase transition into a scalar condensate state with effectively very light baryons. In the mid-1980s, Witten pointed out that strange quark matter may be an absolutely stable form of matter that can exist in the form of quark strangelets. These hypotheses stimulated large experimental efforts to detect these states in heavy-ion collisions and in terrestrial samples. Additionally, the cosmos is the arena for a broad search for traces of exotic matter in compact objects and other energetic phenomena. We review various approaches and arguments for the possibility of abnormal states of matter, including metastable and stable dense objects with condensates, as well as dilute nuclear objects. Among these, we discuss scalar condensate and p-wave and s-wave pion condensations in dense nuclear systems and the formation of a $Δ$ resonance matter. Then we consider a scalar mode condensation triggered by the Pomeranchuk instability in dilute nuclear matter, and clustering. We also mention recent ideas that a relativistic rotation and a dark-matter component may stabilize abnormal nuclear systems. Finally, we list some observational anomalies that cannot yet be appropriately explained by conventional physics.

## Key claim

This review argues that various "abnormal" nuclear states, including metastable or stable superdense nuclei with pion or scalar condensates, dilute nuclear objects, strangelets, and nuclearites, may exist, but the authors emphasize that these remain hypothetical and that experimental evidence from heavy-ion collisions so far does not require their presence, partly because compressed nuclear matter is produced at temperatures higher than the binding energy of such states.

## Method

The authors provide a comprehensive literature review covering theoretical models of abnormal nuclear systems from the 1970s to the present, including the Lee-Wick scalar condensate model, Migdal's pion condensate model, Witten's strange quark matter hypothesis, and the Pomeranchuk instability mechanism for dilute nuclear matter. They present detailed calculations within the Lee-Wick model using a scalar field Lagrangian with spontaneous symmetry breaking, comparing it to relativistic mean-field (RMF) models, and discuss extensions including nucleon-nucleon repulsion terms, delta resonance matter, and the effects of rotation and dark matter.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- For the Lee-Wick model with parameters \(\mu = m_\phi = 800\) MeV, \(\lambda = 10\), \(\delta = 0.03\), the critical density estimate is \(n_c \sim 2 n_0\) using a simple estimate, while the more refined expression gives \(n_{c,0} = \frac{m_\phi^2 m_N}{g^2} \delta\)
- For the same Lee-Wick parameter set, the binding energy minimum occurs at \(\sim 12 n_0\) with binding energy \(-340\) MeV per nucleon; including a repulsive term with \(k = 49\) MeV shifts the minimum to \(\sim 6.4 n_0\) with depth \(-136\) MeV
- The RMF model example uses parameters yielding binding energy per nucleon \(\mathcal{E}_0 = 16\) MeV, compressibility modulus \(K = 275\) MeV, and effective nucleon mass \(m_N^*(n_0) = 0.805\) at saturation density
- Dilute nuclear matter shows spinodal instability in the density region \(0.3 n_0 \lesssim n \lesssim 0.7 n_0\)
- Quark pairing gaps in color superconductivity are estimated at \(\sim 100\) MeV
- Axion quark nuggets can release energy up to \(\sim 2\) GeV per baryon charge via matter-antimatter annihilation
- Strangelets are hypothesized for baryon numbers \(10^2 < A < 10^7\); gravity becomes significant for baryon number \(A \gtrsim 10^{56}\)

## Context

This review extends the theoretical program initiated by Migdal (1971) on pion condensate nuclei, Lee and Wick (1974) on scalar condensate abnormal states, and Witten (1984) on absolutely stable strange quark matter. It connects these ideas to modern developments including the Pomeranchuk instability mechanism for dilute nuclear matter (Kolomeitsev and Voskresensky, 2016), the possibility of rotating nuclear drops forming charged pion condensate giant vortices (Voskresensky, 2023), and axion quark nugget models (Zhitnitsky, 2002 onward). The review enables future experimental searches in heavy-ion collisions and astrophysical observations of compact objects, while noting that current data from heavy-ion collisions do not require the existence of abnormal nuclei.
