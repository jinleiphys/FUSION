# Bayesian Inference analysis of jet quenching using inclusive jet and hadron suppression measurements

> The JETSCAPE Collaboration reports a new determination of the jet transport parameter $\hat{q}$ in the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) using Bayesian Inference, incorporating all available inclusive hadron and jet yield suppression data measured in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC. This multi-observable analysis extends the previously published JETSCAPE Bayesian Inference determination of $\hat{q}$, which was based solely on a selection of inclusive hadron suppression data. JETSCAPE is a modular framework incorporating detailed dynamical models of QGP formation and evolution, and jet propagation and interaction in the QGP. Virtuality-dependent partonic energy loss in the QGP is modeled as a thermalized weakly-coupled plasma, with parameters determined from Bayesian calibration using soft-sector observables. This Bayesian calibration of $\hat{q}$ utilizes Active Learning, a machine--learning approach, for efficient exploitation of computing resources. The experimental data included in this analysis span a broad range in collision energy and centrality, and in transverse momentum. In order to explore the systematic dependence of the extracted parameter posterior distributions, several different calibrations are reported, based on combined jet and hadron data; on jet or hadron data separately; and on restricted kinematic or centrality ranges of the jet and hadron data. Tension is observed in comparison of these variations, providing new insights into the physics of jet transport in the QGP and its theoretical formulation.

## Key claim

The JETSCAPE Collaboration reports a new Bayesian determination of the jet transport parameter \(\hat{q}/T^3\) in the Quark-Gluon Plasma that incorporates both inclusive hadron and inclusive jet suppression data for the first time, but finds tension between posterior distributions extracted from hadron data alone versus jet data alone, indicating that \(\hat{q}/T^3\) may not be a universal property independent of the probe used.

## Method

The analysis uses the JETSCAPE v3.5 modular framework with 2+1D hydrodynamic bulk medium profiles calibrated to soft-sector observables, and simulates jet quenching using the MATTER model for high-virtuality parton evolution and the LBT model for low-virtuality evolution. Bayesian inference is performed with a Gaussian Process Emulator trained on 230 design points selected via an active learning scheme, incorporating 729 data points from inclusive hadron and jet \(R_{AA}\) measurements at RHIC (\(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=0.2\) TeV) and the LHC (\(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) and 5.02 TeV) published before February 2022.

## Key numbers

- 729 data points were included in the analysis, compared to 66 data points in the previous JETSCAPE Bayesian calibration that used only inclusive hadron \(R_{AA}\) data
- The prior for the coupling \(\alpha_s\) at the soft scale is uniform over \(0.1 \leq \alpha_s \leq 0.5\)
- The prior for the transition scale \(Q_0\) (interpreted as average \(\hat{q}\tau\)) is uniform over \(1\ \mathrm{GeV} \leq Q_0 \leq 10\ \mathrm{GeV}\)
- The prior for the start time of jet modification \(t_{start}\) is uniform over \(0 \leq t_{start} \leq 1.5\ \mathrm{fm}/c\)
- Simulations required approximately 5.5 million CPU core-hours total, with 5000-10000 core-hours per design point per collision energy
- The analysis used 20-40 pre-computed medium profiles per 1% interval in centrality
- Inclusive hadron \(R_{AA}\) data were restricted to \(p_T > 9\ \mathrm{GeV}/c\) and centrality 0-50%

## Context

This work extends the previous JETSCAPE Bayesian inference determination of \(\hat{q}\) (JETSCAPE, 2021) which was based solely on 66 inclusive hadron suppression data points, now incorporating jet \(R_{AA}\) measurements from STAR, ALICE, ATLAS, and CMS. The multi-observable approach reveals tension between hadron-only and jet-only extractions, pointing to needed improvements in theoretical formulations of jet transport; the analysis also demonstrates the utility of active learning for efficient sampling of expensive parameter spaces, enabling future studies with broader datasets including jet substructure observables.
