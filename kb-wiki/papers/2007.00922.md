# Nuclear spin-dependent parity-violating effects in light polyatomic molecules

> Measurements of nuclear spin-dependent parity-violating (NSD-PV) effects provide an excellent opportunity to test nuclear models and to search for physics beyond the Standard Model. Molecules possess closely-spaced states with opposite parity which may be easily tuned to degeneracy to greatly enhance the observed parity-violating effects. A high-sensitivity measurement of NSD-PV effects using light triatomic molecules is in preparation [E. B. Norrgard, et al., Commun. Phys. 2, 77 (2019)]. Importantly, by comparing these measurements in light nuclei with prior and ongoing measurements in heavier systems, the contribution to NSD-PV from $Z^0$-boson exchange between the electrons and the nuclei may be separated from the contribution of the nuclear anapole moment. Furthermore, light triatomic molecules offer the possibility to search for new particles, such as the postulated $Z^{\prime}$ boson. In this work, we detail a sensitive measurement scheme and present high-accuracy molecular and nuclear calculations needed for interpretation of NSD-PV experiments on triatomic molecules composed of light elements Be, Mg, N, and C. The ab initio nuclear structure calculations, performed within the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) provide a reliable prediction of the magnitude of different contributions to the NSD-PV effects in the four nuclei. These results differ significantly from the predictions of the standard single-particle model and highlight the importance of including many-body effects in such calculations. In order to extract the NSD-PV contributions from measurements, a parity-violating interaction parameter $W_{\text{PV}}$, which depends on molecular structure, needs to be known with high accuracy. We have calculated these parameters for the triatomic molecules of interest using the relativistic coupled-cluster approach.

## Key claim

The paper claims that high-accuracy nuclear and molecular calculations are essential for interpreting nuclear spin-dependent parity-violating (NSD-PV) measurements in light triatomic molecules, and that the nuclear anapole-moment contributions in these systems are typically 2 to 4 times larger than predicted by the standard single-particle model, while the contributions from Z0-boson exchange are typically reduced.

## Method

The authors performed ab initio nuclear structure calculations using the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) with chiral nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon interactions to compute anapole-moment coupling constants and spin operator matrix elements for 9Be, 13C, 14N, 15N, and 25Mg. They also carried out relativistic coupled-cluster calculations (CCSD and CCSD(T)) within the Dirac-Coulomb Hamiltonian to determine the parity-violating interaction parameter WPV for the triatomic molecules BeNC, BeCN, MgNC, and MgCN. The molecular calculations used the finite-field approach where the NSD-PV interaction was added as a perturbation.

## Key numbers

* The NCSM anapole-moment coupling constant κA for 9Be is 0.016, compared to the single-particle model value of 0.007.
* The NCSM κA for 13C is -0.028, compared to the single-particle model value of -0.007.
* The NCSM κA for 15N is 0.088, compared to the single-particle model value of 0.044.
* The NCSM κA for 25Mg is 0.035, compared to the single-particle model value of 0.014.
* The NCSM total NSD-PV coupling constant κ for 15N is 0.103, compared to the single-particle model value of 0.060.
* The equilibrium bond length R1 for MgNC is 1.931 Å from CCSD(T), in agreement with the experimental value of 1.925 Å.
* The equilibrium bond length R1 for BeNC is 1.528 Å from CCSD(T).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for NSD-PV effects from heavy atoms and diatomic molecules to light triatomic molecules, building on the experimental proposal by Norrgard et al. (2019) for measurements using laser-cooled polyatomic molecules. The NCSM results differ significantly from the single-particle model estimates of Flambaum and Khriplovich (1980, 1984), highlighting the importance of many-body effects even in light nuclei. The calculations enable future experiments to separate the Z0-boson exchange contribution from the nuclear anapole moment contribution by comparing measurements in light nuclei with prior measurements in heavier systems such as 133Cs (Wood et al., 1997) and proposed measurements in BaF (DeMille et al., 2008).
