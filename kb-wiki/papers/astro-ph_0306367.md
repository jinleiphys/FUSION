# Evaluation of Production Cross Sections of Li, Be, B in CR

> Accurate evaluation of production cross sections of light elements is important for models of CR propagation, galactic chemical evolution, and cosmological studies. However, experimental spallation cross section data are scarce and often unavailable to CR community while semi-empirical systematics are frequently wrong by a significant factor. We use all available data from LANL nuclear database together with modern nuclear codes to produce evaluated production cross sections of isotopes of Li, Be, B suitable for astrophysical applications.

## Key claim

The authors produce evaluated production cross sections for isotopes of Li, Be, B by combining all available experimental data from the LANL nuclear database with modern nuclear codes, but note that the accuracy of nuclear cross section calculations used in astrophysics is far behind the accuracy of recent cosmic-ray measurements and is becoming a factor restraining further progress.

## Method

The authors collected all available experimental spallation cross section data from the LANL nuclear database, including cumulative and isobaric reactions and reactions on natural samples of elements, and combined these with modern nuclear codes to produce evaluated cross sections. The individual cross section evaluations were tested against isobaric (A = 7, 10) and cumulative data where available. The results were compared against frequently used semi-empirical systematics from Silberberg et al. (1998) and Webber et al. (2003).

## Key numbers

* Production cross sections of B-11, B-10, Be-10, Be-9, Be-7, Li-7, and Li-6 are shown in figures with units of millibarns as a function of kinetic energy, with evaluated cross sections compared against data and semi-empirical systematics.
* Isobaric cross sections for A=10 (C-10 + B-10 + Be-10) and A=7 (Be-7 + Li-7 + He-7) are presented in millibarns as a function of kinetic energy.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to compile and evaluate nuclear cross section data for cosmic-ray propagation, galactic chemical evolution, and cosmological studies, addressing the problem that experimental spallation cross section data are scarce and semi-empirical systematics are frequently wrong by a significant factor. The authors note that the low-energy part of production cross sections is often resonance-shaped, which may be important for reacceleration models as discussed in Strong and Moskalenko (1998), but is usually ignored in propagation calculations. The evaluated cross sections enable more accurate cosmic-ray propagation models and build upon earlier work by Moskalenko et al. (2001, 2002, 2003).
