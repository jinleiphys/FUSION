# Heavy-ion collisions - hot QCD in a lab

> High-energy heavy-ion collisions provide a unique opportunity to study the properties of the hot and dense strongly-interacting system composed of deconfined quarks and gluons -- the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) -- in laboratory conditions. The formation of a QGP is predicted by lattice QCD calculations as a crossover transition from hadronic matter (at zero baryochemical potential) and is expected to take place once the system temperature reaches values above 155 MeV and/or the energy density above $0.5~\mathrm{GeV}/\mathrm{fm}^{3}$. The nature of such a strongly coupled QGP has been linked to the early Universe at some microseconds after the Big Bang. To characterize the physical properties of the short-lived matter (lifetime of about $10~\mathrm{fm}/c$) experimental studies at Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider and the Large Hadron collider use auto-generated probes, such as high-energy partons created early in the hadronic collisions, thermally emitted photons, and a set of particle correlations that are sensitive to the collective expansion and the dynamics of the system. The lectures briefly introduced some of the experimental techniques and provided a glimpse at some of the results.

## Key claim

High-energy heavy-ion collisions can create a quark-gluon plasma (QGP) in the laboratory, but the system is so short-lived (lifetime of about 10 fm/c) that its properties must be inferred from decay products and auto-generated probes rather than studied directly with external probes.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental techniques used at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC) and the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) to study QGP. These include measuring particle production and transverse momentum spectra, analyzing particle correlations (including azimuthal anisotropy and quantum correlations), detecting direct photons and di-leptons, studying jet quenching via high-energy parton interactions with the medium, and measuring heavy-quark and quarkonia production.

## Key numbers

- QGP formation is expected above a critical temperature of about 150 MeV at zero baryochemical potential, or an energy density above 0.5 GeV/fm^3
- The QGP lifetime is about 10 fm/c
- Initial scatterings in heavy-ion collisions take place in the first tenths of fm/c (tau ~ 0.3 fm/c)
- Thermal equilibrium is assumed to be reached at about tau ~ 1 fm/c
- The inverse slope parameter T from exponential fits to direct photon spectra gives T ~ 220 MeV at RHIC and T ~ 300 MeV at the LHC
- LHC collision energies are sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV and 5 TeV, almost a factor 30 higher than the maximum RHIC energy of sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV
- Earlier experiments at AGS reached sqrt(s_NN) < 5 GeV and at SPS up to sqrt(s_NN) = 17.3 GeV

## Context

This lecture-style review extends the discovery at SPS and RHIC that the QGP behaves as a strongly coupled liquid opaque to high-energy partons, as described by Heinz (2000) and Adams (2005). These findings were confirmed at the LHC by Aamodt (2010) and Aad (2010), with the interesting twist that proton-lead collisions at the LHC show particle correlations resembling heavy-ion collisions (Abelev, 2012) but no jet quenching signal (Acharya, 2017). The work enables further experimental characterization of QGP transport properties such as shear viscosity over entropy density ratio and the jet quenching coefficient.
