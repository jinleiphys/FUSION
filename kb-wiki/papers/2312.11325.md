# Directed flow and hyperon polarization at RHIC BES from multi-fluid dynamics

> We present directed flow of protons and pions, as well as mean polarization of $Λ$ and $\barΛ$ hyperons computed for Au-Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}}=5...19.6$ GeV in MUFFIN model. MUlti Fluid simulation for Fast IoN collisions, or MUFFIN, is a state-of-the-art 3-fluid dynamic model for simulating heavy-ion collisions in the region from a few to a hundred GeV center-of-mass energy. Whereas MUFFIN succeeds to reproduce basic observables in the collision energy range of interest, the slope of the directed flow at mid-rapidity is much steeper as compared to the data, it has unclear EoS dependence and final-state hadronic cascade affects this observable significantly. The excitation function of the $Λ$ polarization shows a significant splitting between polarizations of $Λ$ and $\barΛ$, which challenges a widespread interpretation that the splitting is affected mainly by the late-stage magnetic field.

# Directed flow and hyperon polarization at RHIC BES from multi-fluid dynamics

## Key claim
MUFFIN reproduces basic observables in Au-Au collisions at RHIC BES energies, but the slope of directed flow at mid-rapidity is much steeper than data, with unclear equation-of-state dependence and significant modification by the final-state hadronic cascade. The excitation function of Λ polarization shows a strong splitting between Λ and anti-Λ that has the opposite sign to the data, challenging the widespread interpretation that the splitting is mainly affected by the late-stage magnetic field.

## Method
The authors used the MUFFIN (MUlti Fluid simulation for Fast IoN collisions) 3-fluid dynamical model, where projectile and target nuclei are treated as two cold baryon-rich fluids that inter-penetrate and slow down via friction terms, with energy and momentum lost to friction channeled into a third fluid representing produced particles. Fluid-to-particle transition (particlization) was performed at a fixed effective energy density of 0.5 GeV/fm³ using the Cooper-Frye formula, and the sampled hadrons were passed to the SMASH hadronic cascade for final-state rescatterings and resonance decays. Directed flow slopes and mean (transverse-momentum-integrated) Λ and anti-Λ polarization were computed for Au-Au collisions at center-of-mass energies from 5 to 19.6 GeV, using an averaged initial state corresponding to 10-40% centrality.

## Key numbers
- Directed flow slope dv₁/dy for nucleons and pions was computed at √s_NN = 5, 7.7, 11.5, 14.5, and 19.6 GeV for Au-Au collisions at 10-40% centrality; the amplitudes are considerably larger than STAR data.
- For √s_NN > 7.7 GeV, the final-state hadronic cascade pulls nucleon dv₁/dy down to large negative values, driving it away from experimental data points.
- Λ and anti-Λ mean polarization was computed at mid-rapidity at the particlization hypersurface for the same energy range and centrality class; MUFFIN produces a strong splitting between Λ and anti-Λ polarizations at the lower end of RHIC BES energies, with sign opposite to STAR data.
- The particlization switching energy density was ε_sw = 0.5 GeV/fm³.

## Context
This work extends earlier fluid-dynamic studies of directed flow and hyperon polarization to the multi-fluid framework, which is better suited for intermediate-energy collisions where the Lorentz contraction is weak and nuclei take several fm/c to pass through each other. The directed flow results are compared to STAR measurements (STAR Collaboration, 2014) and earlier calculations by Konchakovski et al. (2014) and Steinheimer et al. (2014), the latter having shown that the predicted turn-around of baryon directed flow slope as a signature of a first-order phase transition does not hold in modern models. The polarization results are compared to STAR data (STAR Collaboration, 2017) and earlier 1-fluid simulations using vHLLE with UrQMD initial state by Karpenko (2016). The strong opposite-sign splitting between Λ and anti-Λ polarization at low energies challenges the interpretation that the splitting is dominated by the late-stage magnetic field, and opens the question of what other mechanisms contribute.
