# Ratio of photon anisotropic flow in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> The $p_T$ dependent elliptic and triangular flow parameters of direct photons are known to be dominated by thermal radiations. The non-thermal contributions dilute the photon anisotropic flow by adding extra weight factor in the $v_n$ calculation. The discrepancy between experimental photon anisotropic flow data and results from theoretical model calculations is not well understood even after significant developments in the model calculations as well as in the experimental analysis. We show that the ratio of photon $v_n$ can be a potential observable in this regard by minimizing the uncertainties arising due to the non-thermal contributions. We calculate the $v_2/v_3$ of photons as a function of $p_T$ from heavy ion collisions at RHIC and compare the results with available experimental data. The ratio does not change significantly $p_T$ in the region $p_T>2$ GeV. However, it rises towards smaller $p_T$ ($< 2$ GeV) values. The ratio is found to be larger for peripheral collisions than for central collisions. In addition, it is found to be sensitive to the initial formation time and the final freeze-out temperature at different $p_T$ regions unlike the individual anisotropic flow parameters. We show that the photon $v_1/v_2$ and $v_1/v_3$ along with the $v_2/v_3$ results may help us constraining the initial conditions.

# Ratio of photon anisotropic flow in relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

The ratio of photon elliptic to triangular flow ($v_2/v_3$) can serve as a cleaner observable than individual flow parameters because it cancels out the non-thermal photon yield in the denominator, thereby reducing uncertainties from prompt photon contamination; however, the ratio is found to be close to experimental data only in the $p_T$ region 2–4 GeV where thermal radiation dominates, while individual $v_2$ and $v_3$ still underpredict the data.

## Method

The authors use an event-by-event ideal hydrodynamical framework with a Monte Carlo Glauber model for initial energy density distributions, evolving the system from an initial formation time $\tau_0$ to a freeze-out temperature $T_f$. Photon emission rates from the quark-gluon plasma and hadronic matter phases are taken from established thermal rate calculations, and the anisotropic flow coefficients $v_1$, $v_2$, and $v_3$ are computed as functions of transverse momentum for Au+Au, Cu+Au, and Cu+Cu collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV at RHIC.

## Key numbers

- Initial formation time $\tau_0 = 0.17$ fm/$c$ for Au+Au collisions at RHIC; freeze-out temperature $T_f = 160$ MeV.
- The ratio $v_2/v_3$ does not change significantly for $p_T > 2$ GeV, but rises for $p_T < 2$ GeV.
- The ratio is larger for peripheral collisions than for central collisions.
- Changing $\tau_0$ from 0.17 fm/$c$ to 0.60 fm/$c$ affects the ratio mostly in the lower $p_T$ region; the ratio with smaller $\tau_0$ is closer to experimental data in the $p_T$ region 2–3.5 GeV.
- Lowering $T_f$ from 160 MeV to 120 MeV changes the ratio only marginally in the lower $p_T$ region, with a small change observable only for larger $p_T$.
- The $v_2/v_3$ ratio from Au+Au collisions is close to PHENIX experimental data in the $p_T$ region 2–4 GeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of photon anisotropic flow using hydrodynamical models (Chatterjee 2005, Srivastava 2008) and addresses the persistent "direct photon puzzle" where theoretical calculations underpredict experimental photon $v_2$ data from PHENIX (Adare 2015) and ALICE (Acharya 2018). The ratio approach builds on prior work showing that photon $v_2$ and $v_3$ have different sensitivities to initial conditions (Chatterjee 2013, Dasgupta 2017), and that $v_1$ is dominated by QGP radiation (Shen 2013). The results suggest that measuring $v_2/v_3$, $v_1/v_2$, and $v_1/v_3$ across different collision systems and beam energies could help constrain initial formation time and freeze-out temperature, and may clarify the role of viscosity effects shown in earlier studies (Gale 2018).
