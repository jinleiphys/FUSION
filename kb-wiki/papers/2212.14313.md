# Reduced basis emulation of pairing in finite systems

> In recent years, reduced basis methods (RBMs) have been adapted to the many-body eigenvalue problem and they have been used, largely in nuclear physics, as fast emulators able to bypass expensive direct computations while still providing highly accurate results. This work is meant to show that the RBM is an efficient and accurate emulator for the strong correlations induced by the pairing interaction in a variety of finite systems like ultrasmall superconducting grains, interacting topological superfluids and mesoscopic hybrid superconductor-semiconductor devices, all of which require an expensive, beyond-mean-field, particle-number conserving description. These systems are modelled by the number-conserving Richardson pairing Hamiltonian and its appropriate generalizations. Their ground state is solved for exactly using the Density Matrix Renormalization Group. The reduced basis is assembled iteratively from a small number of exact ground state vectors, well-chosen from across the relevant parameter space using a fast estimate of the emulation error and a greedy local optimization algorithm. The reduced basis emulation is found to accurately describe the weak-to-strong pairing cross-over in small grains, the third-order topological phase transition of the interacting Richardson-Kitaev chain, and the complex charge stability diagram of a hybrid quantum dot - superconductor device. RBMs are thus confirmed to be cheap and accurate emulators for the widely encountered superconducting phenomena. Capable of providing orders of magnitude computational speed-up with respect to approaches based only on traditional many-body solvers, they open new possibilities in building and solving models of interacting many-body systems and in better interfacing them with experimental design and data analysis.

# Reduced basis emulation of pairing in finite systems

## Key claim
The reduced basis method (RBM) is an efficient and accurate emulator for strong pairing correlations in finite systems, provided the parameter space is sampled using a greedy algorithm with residual-based error estimation; the emulator cannot reliably extrapolate across a topological phase transition, as demonstrated for the Richardson-Gaudin-Kitaev chain where training only in the topologically trivial phase fails to reproduce the nontrivial phase.

## Method
The authors model finite systems (ultrasmall superconducting grains, topological superfluids, and hybrid quantum dot–superconductor devices) using the number-conserving Richardson pairing Hamiltonian and its generalizations. Exact ground states are computed with the Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) in the Matrix Product State formulation using the Itensor library, with a $10^{-9}$ energy convergence tolerance and maximum bond dimension of 2000. A reduced basis is assembled iteratively by a greedy algorithm that selects new sampling points at the location of maximum residual error, and the emulated ground state is obtained by solving a generalized eigenvalue problem in the reduced basis.

## Key numbers
- For the Richardson pairing Hamiltonian at half-filling ($L = 2N_p$), the critical pairing strength varies from $G_{\text{cr}} \simeq 0.32\epsilon$ for $L = 12$ to $G_{\text{cr}} \simeq 0.16\epsilon$ for $L = 200$.
- The number of reduced basis vectors needed to reach a residual threshold of $\text{Res}/\epsilon = 10^{-2}$ grows approximately linearly with system size, requiring about one additional basis state per 10 extra levels.
- For the Richardson-Gaudin-Kitaev chain at quarter filling ($N/L = 1/4$) with $L = 256$ sites, no more than 13 sampling points are required in the interval $1.4 < g < 2.6$ to reach a residual maximum of 0.01 (in units of $t_1$), where $g = GL/2$ is the reduced pairing strength.
- The thermodynamic limit critical reduced strength for the third-order topological phase transition in the RGK chain is $g_c = 2$.

## Context
This work extends the Eigenvector Continuation approach of Frame (2018) and the broader reduced basis methodology recently applied to quantum many-body systems by Bonilla (2022) and Melendez (2022). It demonstrates that RBMs can accurately emulate the weak-to-strong pairing crossover in the Richardson model, the topological phase transition in the Richardson-Gaudin-Kitaev chain introduced by Ortiz (2014), and the charge stability diagram of hybrid quantum dot–superconductor devices studied by Pavesic (2021). The results enable fast emulation of pairing phenomena that would otherwise require expensive beyond-mean-field calculations, and open the possibility of interfacing many-body models with experimental design and data analysis.
