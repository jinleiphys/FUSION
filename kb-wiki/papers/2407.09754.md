# Influence of magnetic field-induced anisotropic gluon pressure during pre-equilibrium in heavy-ion collisions: A faster road towards isotropization

> Magnetic fields of a large intensity can be generated in peripheral high-energy heavy-ion collisions. Although their intensity drops fast and, moreover, it is not clear whether these fields last long enough to induce a magnetization during the quark-gluon plasma phase, most of the models and simulations predict a significant intensity that lasts up to proper times of order 1 fm after the beginning of the reaction, which is a typical time for the hydrodynamical phase to start. This interval of time is referred to as the pre-equilibrium stage. The evolution of the reaction during pre-equilibrium is thus likely to be influenced by these fields. In this work we adopt a strong field approximation to study the effects of the magnetic field-induced anisotropy on the gluon pressure. We include this anisotropy within the description obtained by means of Effective Kinetic Theory and explore the consequences to reach isotropization at proper times of order 1 fm. We show that when including the magnetic field effects, isotropization is achieved faster.

## Key claim

The paper shows that when the anisotropic gluon pressure induced by a strong magnetic field is added to the pre-equilibrium pressure evolution in heavy-ion collisions, isotropization (the ratio of transverse to longitudinal pressure approaching 1) is achieved faster than in the standard case without magnetic field effects. The authors emphasize that the calculation uses the strong field approximation and should be considered approximate, with a more complete calculation requiring relaxation of this approximation.

## Method

The authors adopt a strong field approximation where only the parallel component of the gluon polarization tensor contributes, computing the effective potential at one-loop order in Euclidean space. They calculate the magnetic field-induced parallel pressure from the effective potential and obtain the perpendicular pressure using the relation \(P_\perp = P_\parallel - \eta M|eB|\) with \(\eta = 0.5\), where the magnetization \(M\) is derived from the effective potential. The time evolution of the magnetic field is taken from UrQMD simulations for Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV for 60-80% centrality, parameterized as a function of proper time. These magnetic field-induced pressures are then added to the transverse and longitudinal pressures from the Effective Kinetic Theory framework of Kurkela (2015) to compute the total pressure ratio.

## Key numbers

- The saturation scale used for the initial pre-equilibrium time scale is \(Q_s \sim 1.4\) GeV.
- The maximum magnetic field for collision energies of order \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV is of order \(|eB|_{\max} \gtrsim m_\pi^2\); the hard cutoff for perpendicular momentum integration is taken as \(\Lambda = 4 m_\pi\).
- The renormalization scale is set to \(\mu = \Lambda_{QCD}/2 = 100\) MeV.
- The magnetic field time profile is parameterized as \(|eB|/m_\pi^2 = A e^{-B\tau} + C/\tau^D\) with \(A = 4.432\), \(B = 15.895\) fm\(^{-1}\), \(C = 0.003\) fm\(^D\), and \(D = 1.682\), with \(\tau\) in fm.
- The evolution starts at \(\tau_0 = 0.1\) fm and stops at \(\tau_f = 1\) fm; the parallel pressure \(P_\parallel\) is about 25% larger than \(P_\perp\) at the beginning of the time evolution.
- The analysis uses a t'Hooft coupling \(\lambda = 10\) and an initial anisotropy parameter \(\xi = 10\) from the EKT framework of Kurkela (2015).

## Context

This work extends the Effective Kinetic Theory framework for pre-equilibrium gluon dynamics developed by Kurkela (2015, 2018) by incorporating the anisotropic pressure induced by strong magnetic fields, which had not been previously addressed. The magnetic field profile and intensity are based on UrQMD simulations from Ayala (2019). The results enable a more complete description of the pre-equilibrium stage in peripheral heavy-ion collisions, with implications for photon production channels (gluon fusion and gluon splitting) that are relevant when gluon occupancy is large and quarks are scarce, as studied in Ayala (2024, 2022, 2019, 2017).
