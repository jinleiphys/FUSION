# Temperature-dependent nuclear partition functions and abundances in stellar interior

> We calculate temperature-dependent nuclear partition functions (TDNPFs) and nuclear abundances for $728$ nuclei assuming nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE). The theories of stellar evolution support NSE. Discrete nuclear energy levels have been calculated \textit{microscopically}, using the pn-QRPA theory, up to an excitation energy of $10$ MeV in the calculation of TDNPFs. This feature of our paper distinguishes it from previous calculations. Experimental data is also incorporated wherever available to ensure reliability of our results. Beyond 10 MeV we employ simple Fermi gas model and perform integration over the nuclear level densities to approximate the TDNPFs. We calculate nuclidic abundances, using the Saha equation, as a function of three parameters: stellar density, stellar temperature and lepton-to-baryon content of stellar matter. All these physical parameters are considered to be extremely important in stellar interior. Results obtained in this paper show that the equilibrium configuration of nuclei remains unaltered by increasing stellar density (only calculated nuclear abundances increases by roughly same order of magnitude). Increasing the stellar temperature smooths the equilibrium configuration showing peaks at neutron-number magic nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors calculate temperature-dependent nuclear partition functions (TDNPFs) using microscopically computed discrete energy levels up to 10 MeV excitation energy via the pn-QRPA model for 728 nuclei, but beyond 10 MeV they revert to a simple Fermi gas model with integration over nuclear level densities, and the equilibrium configuration of nuclei remains unaltered by increasing stellar density (only abundance magnitudes increase proportionally).

## Method

The authors computed discrete nuclear energy levels microscopically using the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) up to 10 MeV excitation energy for all 728 nuclei, incorporating experimental data where available. Beyond 10 MeV, they employed a simple Fermi gas model with saddle-point approximation for level density and integrated up to 25 MeV excitation energy. Nuclear abundances were calculated using the Saha equation under nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) assumptions, solved iteratively as functions of stellar density, stellar temperature, and lepton-to-baryon fraction.

## Key numbers

- 728 nuclei were included in the stellar pool, up to mass number 100
- Discrete energy levels were calculated microscopically up to 10 MeV excitation energy; integration was performed up to 25 MeV excitation energy
- Temperature range considered: approximately 10^7 K to 10^10 K
- At T_9 = 5 and ρ_st = 10^11 g/cm^3, the most abundant nuclei (³H and ⁴He) had abundances ~10^32 cm^-3; the least abundant (⁷⁵Co and ⁷⁸Ni) had abundances ~10^-39 cm^-3
- At T_9 = 10 and ρ_st = 10^11 g/cm^3, ³H and ⁴He had abundances ~10^33 cm^-3
- Mass fraction calculations were performed at four sets of conditions: ρ_st = 5.86×10^7 g/cm^3, T_9 = 3.40, Y_e = 0.47; ρ_st = 1.45×10^8 g/cm^3, T_9 = 3.80, Y_e = 0.45; ρ_st = 1.06×10^9 g/cm^3, T_9 = 4.93, Y_e = 0.43; ρ_st = 4.01×10^10 g/cm^3, T_9 = 7.33, Y_e = 0.41
- At low stellar density ρ_st = 3.86×10^5 g/cm^3 and T_9 = 5, the equilibrium configuration peaked at ⁵⁶Fe

## Context

This work extends previous NSE calculations by Rauscher and Thielemann (2000) and Rauscher (2003), who used Hauser-Feshbach formalism with two mass models (FRDM and ETFSI) for TDNPFs, and by Aufderheide et al. (1994), who used only measured ground-state spins with a backshifted Fermi gas level density. The authors' novel contribution is the microscopic pn-QRPA calculation of discrete states up to 10 MeV, which they compare against the open-source NSE code libnuceq and prior work by Seitenzahl et al. (2009) and Juodagalvis et al. (2010). The results enable improved abundance calculations for presupernova evolution and silicon burning phases of massive stars, with future plans to combine these abundances with weak decay rates to identify nuclei that significantly affect the lepton-to-baryon fraction during stellar evolution.
