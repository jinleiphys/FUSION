# Analytical determination of the structure of the outer crust of a cold nonaccreted neutron star: extension to strongly quantizing magnetic fields

> The iterative method recently proposed for determining the internal constitution of the outer crust of a nonaccreted neutron star is extended to magnetars by taking into account the Landau-Rabi quantization of electron motion induced by the presence of a very high magnetic field. It is shown that in the strongly quantizing regime, the method can be efficiently implemented using new analytical solutions for the transitions between adjacent crustal layers. Detailed numerical computations are performed to assess the performance and precision of the method.

# Analytical determination of the structure of the outer crust of a cold nonaccreted neutron star: extension to strongly quantizing magnetic fields

## Key claim

The iterative method previously developed for unmagnetized neutron stars can be extended to magnetars with strongly quantizing magnetic fields (B >> 4.4 × 10^13 G) using new analytical solutions for the transition pressure between adjacent crustal layers, but the analytical approximations show errors of a few percent for transitions in the shallow crust where electrons are only moderately relativistic and for transitions where the threshold electron chemical potential becomes negative.

## Method

The authors extended the iterative method of Chamel (2020) by deriving analytical solutions to the cubic polynomial equation that governs the transition between adjacent crustal layers in the strongly quantizing magnetic field regime, where electrons are confined to the lowest Landau-Rabi level. Starting with 56Fe at the stellar surface, the sequence of equilibrium nuclides was determined iteratively by finding the nuclide leading to the lowest transition pressure, using experimental nuclear masses from the 2016 Atomic Mass Evaluation supplemented with the HFB-27 nuclear mass table. The method was tested for a magnetar with B_star = 2000 by comparing analytical results against exact numerical solutions of the equilibrium conditions.

## Key numbers

- The critical magnetic field for strongly quantizing regime: B_rel = 4.4 × 10^13 G
- For B_star = 2000, the iterative method took about 0.07 seconds on an Intel Core i7-975 processor, while the standard approach using about 19000 pressure values took about 24 minutes (approximately 2 × 10^4 times longer)
- Relative deviations between analytical and exact numerical results for pressures and densities do not exceed 0.24% for most transitions
- The transition from 56Fe to 62Ni showed errors of -4.6% in pressure and -1.4% in electron parameter x_e
- The transition from 132Sn to 80Zn showed errors of 3.3% in pressure and 1.6% in x_e, with a threshold electron chemical potential of -17.0 MeV
- Analytical formulas for baryon chemical potentials show deviations below 8 × 10^{-3}% in all cases
- Depths are determined with error of 0.14% at most, except near the two problematic transitions
- The neutron-drip pressure for this magnetar model is 1.14 × 10^{-3} MeV fm^{-3}
- The surface density is approximately given by n_s ≈ (A_s/λ_e^3)[|C| α B_star^2 / (4π^4 Z_s)]^{3/5}

## Context

This work extends the iterative method of Chamel (2020) for unmagnetized neutron stars to magnetars by incorporating Landau-Rabi quantization of electron motion in strong magnetic fields. The traditional approach, following Lai (1991), minimized the Gibbs free energy per nucleon at zero temperature for a finite set of pressure values, which becomes computationally expensive for systematic calculations over a wide range of magnetic-field strengths. The new analytical solutions enable rapid computation of crustal structure for magnetar modeling, with computer codes made publicly available for both unmagnetized (Chamel 2020b) and strongly magnetized (Chamel 2020c) neutron stars. The method is particularly suited for systematic calculations of the equation of state of dense magnetized matter required for modeling magnetars, including studies of quasiperiodic oscillations and r-process nucleosynthesis from ejected crustal material.
