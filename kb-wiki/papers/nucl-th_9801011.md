# Parity Violating Elastic Electron Scattering and Coulomb Distortions

> Parity violating elastic electron-nucleus scattering provides an accurate and model independent measurement of neutron densities, because the $Z^0$ couples primarily to neutrons. Coulomb distortion corrections to the parity violating asymmetry $A_l$ are calculated exactly using a relativistic optical model. Distortions significantly reduce $A_l$ in a heavy nucleus. However even with distortions, an experiment to measure the neutron radius is feasible. This will aid the interpretation of future atomic parity violation measurements and provide fundamental nuclear structure information. Coulomb distortions and small differences between neutron and proton radii could be important for a standard model test on $^4$He, $^{12}$C or $^{16}$O.

# Parity Violating Elastic Electron Scattering and Coulomb Distortions

## Key claim

Parity violating elastic electron-nucleus scattering can provide an accurate and model independent measurement of neutron densities, but Coulomb distortion corrections significantly reduce the parity violating asymmetry in heavy nuclei and must be included exactly; even with these distortions, an experiment to measure the neutron radius is feasible, though Coulomb distortions and small differences between neutron and proton radii could be important for a standard model test on light nuclei.

## Method

The author numerically solves the Dirac equation for an electron moving in vector and axial vector potentials using a relativistic optical model code called ELASTIC. The positive helicity state scatters from a potential V+A while the negative helicity state scatters from V-A, and the parity violating asymmetry is calculated from the difference of the two scattering amplitudes. The calculation includes terms of all orders in Z-alpha and uses partial wave phase shifts summed to compute scattering amplitudes for various nuclei including 208Pb, 12C, 16O, 4He, 138Ba, and 48Ca.

## Key numbers

- For 208Pb at 502 MeV, elastic cross sections are reproduced out to 3.7 Fm^-1 with error of order a percent near that momentum transfer
- The plane wave test for the asymmetry shows numerical agreement better than 0.1 percent for A_l except in diffraction minima
- For 12C at 200 MeV, the full calculation including Coulomb distortions and mean field densities is about 3 percent above the original prediction assuming equal neutron and proton densities
- For 208Pb at 850 MeV, the logarithmic derivative of the asymmetry with respect to the neutron radius peaks around 3.2 near 7 degrees and around 10 near 14 degrees
- For 208Pb at 850 MeV, the product of figure of merit and logarithmic derivative at the first maximum near 4 degrees is about 20 times the second maximum near 12 degrees
- For 208Pb at a fixed laboratory angle of six degrees, the product of figure of merit times logarithmic derivative is a factor of 3.8 lower at 1720 MeV and 15 lower at 2600 MeV compared to 730 MeV
- Root mean square charge radius for 208Pb from three parameter Fermi density is 5.490 Fm; weak radius from mean field theory is 5.744 Fm
- Root mean square charge radius for 4He from three parameter Fermi density is 1.717 Fm

## Context

This work extends earlier plane wave calculations of parity violating asymmetries by providing the first exact calculation of Coulomb distortion corrections using a relativistic optical model. The paper addresses the need for accurate neutron density measurements to aid interpretation of atomic parity violation experiments, such as the Cs experiment by Wood et al. (1997) accurate to 0.3 percent, and to provide fundamental nuclear structure information that constrains isovector terms in the nuclear matter energy functional. The results enable future experiments at Jefferson Laboratory to measure neutron radii in heavy nuclei like 208Pb and 138Ba, and provide a path toward calibrating nuclear theory for predicting neutron radii across the periodic table.
