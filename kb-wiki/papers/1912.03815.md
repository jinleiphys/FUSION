# Neutron stars from crust to core within the Quark-meson coupling model

> Recent years continue to be an exciting time for the neutron star physics, providing many new observations and insights to these natural laboratories of cold dense matter. To describe them, there are many models on the market but still none that would reproduce all observed and experimental data. The quark-meson coupling model stands out with its natural inclusion of hyperons as dense matter building blocks, and fewer parameters necessary to obtain the nuclear matter equation of state. The latest advances of the QMC model and its application to the neutron star physics will be presented, within which we build the neutron star's outer crust from finite nuclei up to the neutron drip line. The appearance of different elements and their position in the crust of a neutron star is explored and compared to the predictions of various models, giving the same quality of the results for the QMC model as for the models when the nucleon structure is not taken into account.

# Neutron stars from crust to core within the Quark-meson coupling model

## Key claim
The Quark-meson coupling (QMC) model, which incorporates the internal quark structure of nucleons, can reproduce the sequence of nuclei in the neutron star outer crust with the same quality as other commonly used nuclear matter models (such as FRDM, HFB-31, and NL3*), despite having fewer free parameters. However, the composition of the deepest outer crust layers remains uncertain and depends on theoretical mass predictions for very neutron-rich nuclei that are not yet experimentally available.

## Method
The authors used the QMCπ-II model, an energy density functional that treats nucleons as confined MIT bags of three quarks interacting through effective σ, ω, and ρ meson exchange, with an additional cubic σ self-interaction term. They calculated the sequence of nuclei in the neutron star outer crust by minimizing the Gibbs free energy per nucleon as a function of pressure, using nuclear masses from the QMCπ-II model for neutron-rich nuclei where experimental data are unavailable, and compared the resulting crust composition to predictions from FRDM, HFB-31, and NL3* models.

## Key numbers
- The QMCπ-II model has 7 free parameters: G_σ = 9.046 fm², G_ω = 5.287 fm², G_ρ = 4.708 fm², m_σ = 494.7 MeV, V_p = 287.5 MeV, V_n = 275.001 MeV, and λ_3 = 0.049.
- The incompressibility K for QMCπ-II is 270 MeV, compared to the experimental range of 240 ± 20 MeV and the previous QMC-I-π value of 319 MeV.
- The slope of symmetry energy L for QMCπ-II is 70 MeV, compared to the experimental range of 58.7 ± 28.1 MeV and the previous QMC-I-π value of 17 MeV.
- The outer crust density range is 10⁻¹⁵ fm⁻³ < ρ < 10⁻² fm⁻³, with the transition to inner crust at ρ ∼ 10⁻⁵ fm⁻³.
- The heaviest observed neutron star has mass around M ∼ 2 M_⊙ (citing Antoniadis 2013 and Demorest 2010).

## Context
This work extends the QMC model framework (Guichon 2006, Guichon 2018 review) to the full neutron star structure, building on the QMCπ-II parameterization (Martinez 2018) that improved the incompressibility and symmetry energy compared to the earlier QMC-I-π model (Stone 2016). The outer crust composition is compared to predictions from the finite range droplet model FRDM (Moller 1995, 1997, 2016), the Skyrme-type HFB-31 model (Goriely 2016), and the relativistic mean-field NL3* model (Lalazissis 2014). The results enable future construction of a unified equation of state covering the full neutron star density range, with ongoing extensions including tensor terms (QMCπ-0 model) and temperature dependence for proto-neutron star applications (Stone 2019).
