# An introduction to computational complexity and statistical learning theory applied to nuclear models

> The fact that we can build models from data, and therefore refine our models with more data from experiments, is usually given for granted in scientific inquiry. However, how much information can we extract, and how precise can we expect our learned model to be, if we have only a finite amount of data at our disposal? Nuclear physics demands an high degree of precision from models that are inferred from the limited number of nuclei that can be possibly made in the laboratories. In manuscript I will introduce some concepts of computational science, such as statistical theory of learning and Hamiltonian complexity, and use them to contextualise the results concerning the amount of data necessary to extrapolate a mass model to a given precision.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the difficulty in substantially improving nuclear mass models beyond current ~MeV precision is not due to any specific model's shortcomings, but is a fundamental limitation imposed by the finite amount of available nuclear ground-state data, as quantified by probably approximately correct (PAC) learning bounds.

## Method
The author introduces concepts from computational complexity (Hamiltonian complexity classes such as QMA) and statistical learning theory (Vapnik-Chervonenkis dimension, PAC learning bounds). These tools are then applied to nuclear mass models, specifically the Weizsäcker mass formula and several nuclear density functionals, to derive the minimum number of data points required to achieve a given generalization precision. The derivation uses Hoeffding's inequality to relate the model class size, number of training data points, and the probability of generalization error.

## Key numbers
- The PAC generalization error bounds derived for the Weizsäcker mass formula and nuclear density functionals are ≥ 1 MeV, comparable to the present root mean square deviation of these models.
- The relation between data points m, precision ε, and model class size |Λ| is given by m ≥ (1/ε)[log(|Λ|) + log(1/δ)], where δ is the probability of a bad generalization.
- The 2-body local Hamiltonian problem is demonstrated to be QMA-complete (Kempe, 2006), meaning any other QMA problem (including general k-body Hamiltonians) can be reduced to it.

## Context
This work extends the author's previous analysis (Idini, 2020) of mass models and energy density functionals using statistical learning theory. It places nuclear modeling challenges in the broader context of Hamiltonian complexity, noting that both finding solutions to local Hamiltonians and deriving universal density functionals belong to the QMA complexity class (Schuch, 2009; Whitfield, 2013; O'Gorman, 2021). The paper suggests that while these complexity results indicate fundamental limitations, specific implementations for finite nuclear systems may still be feasible, and that density functional theory offers a promising framework for evaluating both computational and data complexity simultaneously.
