# Origin of the electric hexadecapole isomer in $^{93}$Mo

> We present a shell-model analysis of $^{93}$Mo to investigate the unusual behavior of its ${21/2}^+$ isomer -- a prominent candidate for nuclear excitation by electronic capture. This state is unique as its decay is dominated by a slow electric hexadecapole $E4$ transition, while the typically much faster electric quadrupole $E2$ decay path is energetically forbidden. We investigate the microscopic origin of this phenomenon by examining in detail the structure of the wave functions of the initial and final states, and the $E4$ transition matrix elements. This analysis of $^{93}$Mo is contrasted with that of its particle-hole conjugate, $^{99}$Cd, where such an $E4$ transition is absent.

# Origin of the electric hexadecapole isomer in $^{93}$Mo

## Key claim

The $21/2^+$ isomer in $^{93}$Mo exists because the strong attractive neutron-proton interaction in the maximally aligned configuration inverts the $21/2^+$ and $17/2^+$ states, energetically forbidding the fast $E2$ decay and forcing a slow $E4$ transition; this mechanism is confirmed by comparison with the particle-hole conjugate $^{99}$Cd, where the interaction becomes repulsive and no such isomerism occurs.

## Method

Shell-model calculations were performed within the $\nu 1d_{5/2} \otimes \pi 0g_{9/2}^2$ model space using two-body matrix elements derived empirically from the spectra of $^{92}$Mo (for like-particle $V_{\pi\pi}$) and $^{92}$Nb (for neutron-proton $V_{\nu\pi}$). The reduced $E2$ and $E4$ transition probabilities were computed from the diagonalized wave functions, with effective charges calibrated to experimental data: $e_\pi = 1.32$ for $E2$, $e_\pi = 1.20$ for $E4$, and three neutron effective charges (1.18, 1.73, and 1.48) adopted from neighboring nuclei. The calculations were carried out using the Mathematica code shell.m within the J-scheme using coefficients of fractional parentage.

## Key numbers

- The $21/2^+$ isomer in $^{93}$Mo has a half-life of 6.85 hours; the $17/2^+$ state has a half-life of 3.53 nanoseconds.
- The $21/2^+$ state lies at 2.424 MeV (experimental) and 2.437 MeV (calculated); the first $17/2^+$ state lies at 2.429 MeV (experimental) and 2.454 MeV (calculated), confirming the inversion.
- The $B(E2; 17/2^+ \rightarrow 21/2^+)$ value is calculated as 2.02 W.u. ($e_\nu=1.18$), 2.40 W.u. ($e_\nu=1.78$), or 2.21 W.u. ($e_\nu=1.48$), representing a roughly 40% reduction from the previous estimate of 3.5 W.u. by Hasegawa et al. (2011).
- The $B(E4; 21/2^+ \rightarrow 13/2^+)$ value is calculated as 1.25 W.u. ($e_\nu=1.18$), 1.50 W.u. ($e_\nu=1.78$), or 1.37 W.u. ($e_\nu=1.48$), in excellent agreement with the experimental value of 1.449(17) W.u.
- The $B(E2; 17/2^+ \rightarrow 13/2^+)$ value is calculated as 3.67 W.u. ($e_\nu=1.18$), 4.30 W.u. ($e_\nu=1.78$), or 3.98 W.u. ($e_\nu=1.48$), compared to the experimental value of 4.48(23) W.u.
- The two-body matrix elements for $V_{\pi\pi}$ range from 0.000 MeV ($J=0$) to 2.761 MeV ($J=8$); for $V_{\nu\pi}$ they range from 0.000 MeV ($J=7$) to 0.501 MeV ($J=6$).
- The single-particle radial integrals are $\langle \nu 1d_{5/2}|r^4| \nu 1d_{5/2} \rangle = 42.75 b^4$ and $\langle \pi 0g_{9/2}|r^4| \pi 0g_{9/2} \rangle = 35.75 b^4$, where $b$ is the harmonic oscillator length parameter.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis (Maheshwari et al., 2025) that quantified the role of neutron-proton interaction in the level inversion and provided a revised $B(E2)$ estimate relevant to nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC). The present study provides the first explicit microscopic decomposition of the $E4$ transition matrix elements in $^{93}$Mo, showing constructive interference between proton and neutron contributions. The results are contrasted with the particle-hole conjugate nucleus $^{99}$Cd, where the Pandya transformation (Pandya, 1956) makes the neutron-proton interaction repulsive, preventing level inversion and $E4$ isomerism. This work enables improved predictions for induced isomer depletion and NEEC processes, and identifies the $M4$ isomer in $^{84}$Rb as a candidate for future study.
