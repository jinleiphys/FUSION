# The nuclear force imprints revealed on the elastic scattering of protons with $^{10}$C

> How does nature hold together protons and neutrons to form the wide variety of complex nuclei in the universe? Describing many-nucleon systems from the fundamental theory of quantum chromodynamics has been the greatest challenge in answering this question. The chiral effective field theory description of the nuclear force now makes this possible but requires certain parameters that are not uniquely determined. Defining the nuclear force needs identification of observables sensitive to the different parametrizations. From a measurement of proton elastic scattering on $^{10}$C at TRIUMF and ab initio nuclear reaction calculations we show that the shape and magnitude of the measured differential cross section is strongly sensitive to the nuclear force prescription.

## Key claim

The shape and magnitude of the measured differential cross section for proton elastic scattering on $^{10}$C is strongly sensitive to the nuclear force prescription, but none of the available parameterizations of the chiral nuclear force is optimal in all aspects; the N$^2$LO$_{\rm sat}$ interaction provides an angular distribution shape consistent with the experiment but fails to reproduce its magnitude.

## Method

The experiment was performed in inverse kinematics at the ISAC rare isotope beam facility at TRIUMF, bombarding a solid hydrogen target with a $^{10}$C beam re-accelerated using the ISAC-II superconducting linear accelerator. The scattered protons were identified using the correlation between energy-loss in an annular array of segmented silicon detectors and remaining energy deposited in CsI(Tl) detectors covering angles $\theta_{lab}$ $\sim$ 26$^\circ$ - 52$^\circ$. The theoretical description used the ab initio no-core shell model with continuum (NCSMC), which describes the reacting system using a basis expansion with components for the $^{11}$N nucleus and for the separated proton and $^{10}$C clusters, with chiral two-nucleon (NN) and three-nucleon (3N) forces as input.

## Key numbers

- Beam energies at mid-target were 4.54$A$ MeV and 4.82$A$ MeV, corresponding to p+$^{10}$C center-of-mass energies of E$_{cm}$=4.15 MeV and 4.4 MeV, respectively.
- Average beam intensity was 2000 particles per second; average target thickness was approximately 80 $\mu$m.
- Systematic uncertainties: 5% from target thickness, 5% from detection efficiency, and 4% from beam contamination, added in quadrature.
- For E$_{cm}$=4.15 MeV, the best-fit chi-square values for scaled cross sections were 65.5 for the NN interaction, 25.9 for the NN+3N400 interaction, and 1.7 for the N$^2$LO$_{\rm sat}$ interaction (after scaling).
- The N$^2$LO$_{\rm sat}$ calculation overestimates the measured cross section magnitude; a scaling factor of 2.3 brings the calculation into agreement with the data shape.
- The $3/2^-$ resonance in $^{11}$N is placed at 4.35(3) MeV in the evaluation from Ref. Ke12, while conflicting data from Ref. Gu03 places it at 4.56(1) MeV.

## Context

This work extends ab initio reaction theory calculations based on chiral effective field theory to proton-rich drip-line nuclei, specifically testing the NN interaction from Entem and Machleidt (2003), the NN+3N400 force from Roth et al. (2012), and the N$^2$LO$_{\rm sat}$ interaction from Ekström et al. (2015). The study demonstrates that elastic scattering angular distributions provide stronger constraints on nuclear force parameterizations than static properties such as binding energies or resonance energies alone, as shown by the failure of the NN+3N400 interaction to reproduce the angular distribution despite good predictions of oxygen isotope binding energies. This work enables future efforts to identify which specific parameters and components of chiral interactions are responsible for the successful description of the $^{10}$C(p,p) data, and motivates more intensive ab initio calculations for extreme nuclear systems.
