# Positron production in collision of heavy nuclei

> We consider the electromagnetic production of positron in collision of heavy nuclei, with the simultaneously produced electron captured by one of the nuclei. This cross-section exceeds by about four orders of magnitude the cross-section of $e^+ e^-$ production.

# Positron production in collision of heavy nuclei

## Key claim
The cross-section for electromagnetic positron production accompanied by electron capture in heavy-nucleus collisions exceeds the cross-section for free $e^+e^-$ pair production by about four orders of magnitude, but this result is derived under the assumption of non-relativistic nuclear velocities ($v/c = 0.1$) and neglects positron rescattering off the nuclei.

## Method
The authors compute the cross-section from a Feynman diagram where a virtual photon exchanged between two heavy nuclei produces an electron-positron pair; the electron is captured by one nucleus to form a single-electron ion, while the positron propagates freely. They integrate over the phase space of the outgoing nuclei and positron using non-relativistic kinematics for the nuclei, a non-relativistic wave function for the captured electron in momentum representation, and neglect the positron's interaction with the nuclei.

## Key numbers
- For uranium nuclei, the cross-section is $\sigma \simeq 0.07 \, v^2 / m^2$, where $v = 0.1$ (in units where $c=1$) and $m$ is the electron mass.
- The absolute cross-section estimate for $v=0.1$ is about $3 \times 10^{-23}$ cm$^2$.
- The cross-section for free $e^+e^-$ production in heavy ion collisions is about $10^{-25}$ cm$^2$, making the positron-capture process larger by roughly a factor of 300.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of positron and pair production in heavy-ion collisions (Mueller et al.; Marinov et al.) by providing a simple analytic estimate for the specific channel where the electron is captured. The result enables estimates of single-electron ion yields in slow heavy-ion collisions and highlights a mechanism that dominates over free pair production at non-relativistic velocities. The authors note that the non-relativistic approximation for the bound electron wave function is standard in such problems and usually works with reasonable accuracy.
