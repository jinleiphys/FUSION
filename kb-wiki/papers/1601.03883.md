# How to retrieve additional information from the multiplicity distributions

> Multiplicity distributions $P(N)$ measured in multiparticle production processes are most frequently described by the Negative Binomial Distribution (NBD). However, with increasing collision energy some systematic discrepancies become more and more apparent. They are usually attributed to the possible multi-source structure of the production process and described using a multi-NBD form of the multiplicity distribution. We investigate the possibility of keeping a single NBD but with its parameters depending on the multiplicity $N$. This is done by modifying the widely known clan model of particle production leading to the NBD form of $P(N)$. This is then confronted with the approach based on the so-called cascade-stochastic formalism which is based on different types of recurrence relations defining $P(N)$. We demonstrate that a combination of both approaches allows the retrieval of additional valuable information from the multiplicity distributions, namely the oscillatory behavior of the counting statistics apparently visible in the high energy data.

## Key claim

The authors propose that multiplicity distributions measured in high-energy collisions can yield additional dynamical information, specifically, oscillatory behavior in the counting statistics, if analyzed via recurrence relations rather than by fitting a single Negative Binomial Distribution (NBD) or a superposition of NBDs. They show that this oscillatory structure is hidden in the coefficients \(C_j\) of a general recurrence relation and is not captured by standard NBD fits, but emerges when the NBD parameters are allowed to depend on multiplicity.

## Method

The authors start from the recurrence relation \((N+1)P(N+1) = \langle N\rangle \sum_{j=0}^N C_j P(N-j)\) and invert it to compute the coefficients \(C_j\) directly from experimental multiplicity distributions \(P(N)\) measured by CMS and ALICE at LHC energies. They then compare these coefficients to those obtained from a standard single NBD, from a two-component NBD, and from a modified NBD (MNBD) in which the mean multiplicity parameter \(m\) is allowed to depend on \(N\) via a non-monotonic exponential form. The MNBD is constructed to flatten the ratio \(R = P_{\text{data}}(N)/P_{\text{fit}}(N)\) across all multiplicities.

## Key numbers

* CMS charged hadron multiplicity distribution at \(\sqrt{s} = 7\) TeV for \(|\eta| < 2\): best single NBD fit parameters are \(m = 25.5\) and \(k = 1.45\).
* MNBD parametrization for CMS data: \(m(N) = c \exp[a_1 |N - b| + a_2 (N - b)^4]\) with \(a_1 = 0.044\), \(b = 11\), \(c = 20.252\), and \(a_2 = 1.04 \times 10^{-9}\).
* Oscillatory coefficients \(C_j\) from CMS data at \(\sqrt{s} = 7\) TeV, \(|\eta| < 2\): fitted by a damped triangular wave with period \(\omega = 16\), damping length \(\lambda = 25\), and amplitudes \(a_1 = 3.2\), \(a_2 = 0.6\), \(\delta = 1.67\).
* ALICE data at \(\sqrt{s} = 900\) GeV, \(|\eta| < 1.5\): \(P(0) = 0.059\) (ALICE) versus \(P(0) = 0.075\) (CMS) for the same energy and pseudorapidity window.
* For the MNBD fit to CMS data, the acceptance probability used for proper normalization of \(P(0)\) is \(\alpha = 0.965\), yielding \(P(0) = 0.045\).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing use of the Negative Binomial Distribution to describe multiplicity distributions in hadronic collisions, which has been applied from SPS through Tevatron to LHC energies (reviews by Grosse-Oetringhaus and others). The authors note that systematic discrepancies between single NBD fits and data grow with energy, motivating multi-NBD approaches (e.g., by Ghosh and others). Their novel contribution is to show that the recurrence-relation coefficients \(C_j\), related to the combinants introduced by Koba and collaborators, reveal oscillatory structure not visible in the multiplicity distribution itself, and that this structure is sensitive to the detailed shape of \(P(N)\) at low multiplicities. This enables a new diagnostic for models of particle production, requiring future models to simultaneously describe both \(P(N)\) and the \(C_j\) coefficients.
