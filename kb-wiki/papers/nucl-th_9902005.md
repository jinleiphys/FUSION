# Halflife of 56Ni in cosmic rays

> A measurement of the 56Ni cosmic ray abundance has been discussed as a possible tool to determine the acceleration time scale of relativistic particles in cosmic rays. This conjecture will depend on the halflife of totally ionized 56Ni which can only decay by higher-order forbidden transitions. We have calculated this halflife within large-scale shell model calculations and find t_{1/2} \approx 4 \times 10^4 years, only slightly larger than the currently available experimental lower limit, but too short for 56Ni to serve as a cosmic ray chronometer.

## Key claim

The halflife of totally ionized 56Ni is calculated to be approximately 4 × 10^4 years, which is only slightly larger than the current experimental lower limit of 2.9 × 10^4 years but too short for 56Ni to serve as a cosmic ray chronometer, contrary to earlier speculation that it might be as large as 5.4 × 10^6 years.

## Method

Large-scale shell model calculations were performed for the 56Ni ground state and the three lowest states in 56Co in a model space allowing up to 6 particles to be excited from the f7/2 orbit to the rest of the pf shell. The calculations used both the KB3 interaction and a modified version of this force, employing harmonic oscillator wave functions (with oscillator parameter b = 1.99 fm) and Woods-Saxon radial wave functions. The partial halflives for the various beta decay branches of fully stripped 56Ni were computed using experimental energies for the final states.

## Key numbers

- Total halflife of totally ionized 56Ni: approximately 4 × 10^4 years
- Experimental lower limit for the halflife: 2.9 × 10^4 years (Norman, 1990)
- Earlier systematic estimate for the halflife: up to 5.4 × 10^6 years (Norman, 1990)
- Partial halflife for decay to the 3+ state at 158 keV (dominant branch): 3.8 × 10^4 years (KB3, harmonic oscillator), 3.7 × 10^4 years (modified KB3, harmonic oscillator), 4.2 × 10^4 years (KB3, Woods-Saxon), 3.9 × 10^4 years (modified KB3, Woods-Saxon)
- Partial halflife for decay to the 4+ ground state: 2.6 × 10^12 years (KB3, harmonic oscillator) to 5.0 × 10^12 years (modified KB3, Woods-Saxon)
- Partial halflife for decay to the 2+ state at 970 keV: 5.6 × 10^7 years (KB3, harmonic oscillator) to 3.3 × 10^8 years (modified KB3, Woods-Saxon)
- Laboratory halflife of 56Ni (electron capture): 6.075 ± 0.020 days
- Calculated halflife with universal Gamow-Teller renormalization factor (0.74)^2: 7.3 × 10^4 years
- Cosmic ray escape time from the galaxy: 10-20 million years

## Context

This work extends earlier experimental lower limit measurements by Norman (1990) and provides a theoretical calculation that resolves the wide range of possible halflives previously estimated from systematics of similar decays. The result contradicts the possibility raised by Norman (1990) that 56Ni could serve as a cosmic ray chronometer, as the calculated halflife of 4 × 10^4 years is significantly shorter than the 10-20 million year timescale for cosmic ray escape from the galaxy. The calculation demonstrates the capability of modern large-scale shell model approaches to treat forbidden beta decay transitions in the pf shell, building on methods developed by Martinez-Pinedo and Vogel (1998) and Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo (1998).
