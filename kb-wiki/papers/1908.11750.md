# Dynamical generation of resonances in the P33 partial wave

> We investigate the formation of resonances in the P33 partial wave with the emphasis on possible emergence of dynamically generated quasi-bound states as a consequence of a strong $p$-wave pion attractive interaction in this partial wave, as well as their possible interaction with the genuine quark excited states. By using the Laurent-Pietarinen expansion we follow the evolution of the $S$-matrix poles in the complex energy plane as a function of the interaction strength. Already without introducing a genuine quark resonant state, two physically interesting resonances emerge with pole masses around 1200 MeV and 1400 MeV, with the dominant $πN$ and $πΔ$ component, respectively. The added genuine resonant state in the $(1s)^3$ quark configuration mixes with the lower dynamically generated resonance forming the physical $Δ(1232)$ resonance, and pushes the second dynamical resonance to around 1500 MeV, which allows it to be identified with the $Δ(1600)$ resonance. Adding a second resonant state with one quark promoted to the $2s$ orbit generates another pole whose evolution remains well separated from the lower two poles. We calculate the helicity amplitudes at the pole and suggest that their $Q^2$ dependence could be a decisive test to discriminate between different models of the $Δ(1600)$ resonance.

# Dynamical generation of resonances in the P33 partial wave

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that two resonances in the P33 partial wave can emerge purely from coupled-channel meson-baryon dynamics without any genuine three-quark resonant state, with pole masses around 1200 MeV and 1400 MeV; however, the physical Δ(1232) resonance requires mixing with a genuine quark state in the (1s)³ configuration, while the Δ(1600) resonance can be identified with the second dynamically generated state pushed to around 1500 MeV by this mixing, with the (1s)²2s quark configuration playing only a minor role.

## Method

The authors solve a coupled-channels Lippmann-Schwinger equation for πN and πΔ scattering in the P33 partial wave, using a separable kernel derived from u-channel N and Δ exchange with pion loops, and employ the Laurent-Pietarinen expansion to track S-matrix poles in the complex energy plane as a function of the bag radius (which controls the interaction strength). They first study the purely dynamical case without any bare baryon states, then introduce a genuine three-quark resonant state in the (1s)³ configuration to model the Δ(1232), and finally add a second resonant state with one quark promoted to the 2s orbit to represent the radial excitation.

## Key numbers

- Two dynamically generated resonances emerge without any genuine quark state: pole masses around 1200 MeV and 1400 MeV, with dominant πN and πΔ components respectively
- For the u-channel N-exchange kernel alone at R=0.123 fm, the Δ(1232) pole position is Re Wp = 1205 MeV with -2 Im Wp = 94 MeV (PDG: 1210 MeV, 100 MeV)
- For the N+Δ-exchange kernel at R=0.200 fm, the Δ(1232) pole is at Re Wp = 1203 MeV with -2 Im Wp = 98 MeV
- The second dynamically generated pole (progenitor of Δ(1600)) appears at Re Wp = 1379 MeV with -2 Im Wp = 219 MeV for R=0.800 fm in the πΔ channel
- With one resonant state included at R=0.800 fm, the Δ(1600) pole moves to Re Wp = 1449 MeV with -2 Im Wp = 350 MeV
- With one resonant state at R=0.600 fm, the Δ(1600) pole is at Re Wp = 1508 MeV with -2 Im Wp = 427 MeV
- Adding a second resonant state with bare mass 2.2 GeV at R=0.800 fm gives Δ(1600) pole at Re Wp = 1453 MeV with -2 Im Wp = 360 MeV
- Adding a second resonant state with bare mass 2.0 GeV at R=0.800 fm gives Δ(1600) pole at Re Wp = 1452 MeV with -2 Im Wp = 347 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous coupled-channels analysis of the N(1440) resonance in the P11 partial wave (Golli et al., 2018) to the P33 partial wave, where the Δ(1232) and Δ(1600) resonances reside. It contradicts the traditional quark-model interpretation of Δ(1600) as primarily a (1s)²2s radial excitation (analogous to the Roper resonance), instead supporting the dynamical origin found in semi-phenomenological studies by Roenchen et al. (2014) and the EBAC approach (Sato and Lee, 2010). The calculated Q² dependence of helicity amplitudes at the pole is suggested as a decisive test to discriminate between this dynamical model and quark-model calculations by Capstick (1995), Ramalho (2010), and Aznauryan (2015).
