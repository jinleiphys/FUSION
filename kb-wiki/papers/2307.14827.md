# Triaxial projected shell model approach for negative parity states in even-even nuclei

> The triaxial projected shell model (TPSM) approach is generalized to investigate the negative parity band structures in even-even systems. In the earlier version of the TPSM approach, the quasiparticle excitations were restricted to one major oscillator shell and it was possible to study only positive parity states in even-even systems. In the present extension, the excited quasiparticles are allowed to occupy two major oscillator shells, which makes it possible to generate the negative parity states. As a major application of this development, the extended approach is applied to elucidate the negative parity high-spin band structures in $^{102-112}$Ru and it is shown that energies obtained with neutron excitation are slightly lower than the energies calculated with proton excitation. However, the calculated aligned angular momentum ($i_x$) clearly separates the two spectra with neutron $i_x$ in reasonable agreement with the empirically evaluated $i_x$ from the experimental data, whereas proton $i_x$ shows large deviations. Furthermore, we have also deduced the transition quadrupole moments from the TPSM wavefunctions along the negative-parity yrast- and yrare- bands and it is shown that these quantities exhibit rapid changes in the bandcrossing region.

## Key claim

The triaxial projected shell model (TPSM) approach is extended to allow quasiparticle excitations from two major oscillator shells, enabling the study of negative parity states in even-even nuclei; for the Ru isotopes studied, neutron excitations produce energies slightly lower than proton excitations, and only the neutron-excitation aligned angular momentum agrees with experimental data, while the proton-excitation results show large deviations.

## Method

The authors generalized the TPSM basis by allowing valence neutrons and protons to occupy two different major oscillator shells (neutrons in N=4 and 5; protons in N=3 and 4), rather than the previous restriction to one major shell. They constructed multi-quasiparticle basis states including two-neutron, four-neutron, two-proton, four-proton, and mixed neutron-proton excitations, performed three-dimensional angular-momentum projection, and diagonalized a pairing plus quadrupole-quadrupole Hamiltonian. The approach was applied to the negative parity high-spin band structures in six Ru isotopes from A=102 to 112, using deformation parameters from the microscopic-macroscopic model of Moller (2008) with adjustments.

## Key numbers

* For the yrast negative parity band in 112Ru at the highest observed spin I=14, the TPSM calculation with neutron excitation deviates from experiment by 0.039 MeV.
* For the excited negative parity band in 112Ru at the highest observed spin I=15, the calculated value deviates from experiment by 0.704 MeV.
* The monopole pairing strengths used were G1=22.68 and G2=16.22, with quadrupole pairing strength set to 0.18 times the monopole strength.
* Effective charges of 0.5e for neutrons and 1.5e for protons were used in transition probability calculations.
* Axial deformation parameters epsilon ranged from 0.220 (102Ru) to 0.280 (106Ru), with non-axial gamma values of 25 to 30 degrees across the isotope chain.
* The pairing set that best reproduced experimental data for 112Ru used neutron and proton pair gaps of Delta_n=0.6 MeV and Delta_p=0.6 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the TPSM approach previously limited to positive parity states in even-even nuclei (Jehangir 2012, Nazira 2022) by incorporating two-oscillator-shell quasiparticle excitations. It addresses the limited theoretical calculations available for negative parity bands in the A~110 region, where prior work using D1S Gogny force (NPRU2) and self-consistent cranking Skyrme calculations (Dai 2019) had characterized observed bands as neutron excited configurations. The results enable systematic TPSM studies of negative parity band structures in deformed and transitional nuclei, and the authors note that future self-consistent mean-field calculations may help resolve remaining deviations for excited bands.
