# No Radial Excitations in Low Energy QCD. I. Diquarks and Classification of Mesons

> We propose a new schematic model for mesons in which the building blocks are quarks and flavor-antisymmetric diquarks. The outcome is a new classification of the entire meson spectrum into quark-antiquark and diquark-antidiquark states which does not give rise to a radial quantum number: all mesons which have so far been believed to be radially excited are orbitally excited diquark-antidiquark states; similarly, there are no radially excited baryons. Further, mesons that were previously viewed as "exotic" are no longer exotic as they are now naturally integrated into the classification as diquark-antidiquark states. The classification also leads to the introduction of isorons (iso-hadrons), which are analogs of atomic isotopes, and their magic quantum numbers, which are analogs of the magic numbers of the nuclear shell model. The magic quantum numbers of isorons match the quantum numbers expected for low-lying glueballs in lattice QCD. We observe that interquark forces in mesons behave substantially differently from those in baryons: qualitatively, they are color-magnetic in mesons but color-electrostatic in baryons. We comment on potential models and the hydrogen atom. The implications of our results for confinement, asymptotic freedom, and a new set of relations between two fundamental properties of hadrons - their size and their energy - are discussed in our companion paper [arXiv:0910.2231].

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the entire meson spectrum can be reclassified into quark-antiquark and diquark-antidiquark states without any radial quantum number, meaning that all mesons previously believed to be radially excited are actually orbitally excited diquark-antidiquark states; this claim is conditional on the assumption that only flavor-antisymmetric diquarks serve as building blocks, derived from the phenomenological requirement that all observed light meson multiplets are flavor nonets.

## Method

The author constructs an extended schematic model where mesons are built from quarks and the two flavor-antisymmetric diquark configurations (Q1 and Q2), which are selected because diquark-antidiquark combinations from these diquarks form only flavor nonets under SU(3)_f. The meson spectrum from the Particle Data Group listings is then reclassified by assigning each observed meson multiplet to either a quark-antiquark (qq), Q1-antidiquark (QQA), or Q2-antidiquark (QQB) configuration with specified orbital (L) and spin (S) quantum numbers, using an "orbital excitation rule" that each unit of L contributes approximately 0.5 GeV to the mass of a light meson.

## Key numbers

- Each unit of orbital angular momentum L contributes about 0.5 GeV to the mass of a light meson (the "orbital excitation rule")
- S-waves: mass less than or equal to 1 GeV
- P-waves: mass between 1 and 1.5 GeV
- D-waves: mass between 1.5 and 2 GeV
- F-waves: mass between 2 and 2.5 GeV
- The pi(1300) is assigned as a QQB state with ^3P_0, not as a radial excitation
- The rho(1450) is assigned as a QQA state with ^1P_1, not as a radial excitation
- The eta(1475) and eta(1295) are assigned as QQB ^3P_0 states
- The psi(2S) is assigned as a QQA ^1P_1 state, not as a radial excitation
- The Upsilon(2S) is assigned as a QQA ^1P_1 state, not as a radial excitation

## Context

This work extends the diquark approach previously applied to baryons by Wilczek (2004) and Selem (2006), and to specific mesons by Jaffe (1976) for light scalar mesons and by Rosenzweig (1975) for charmed mesons, but applies it systematically to the entire meson spectrum for the first time. The classification contradicts the standard quark model classification in the PDG (which includes a radial quantum number n since 1992) and challenges potential models that predict radial excitations by analogy with the hydrogen atom. The paper enables a reinterpretation of "exotic" mesons as natural diquark-antidiquark states and introduces the concept of isorons (iso-hadrons) with magic JPC quantum numbers that match lattice QCD predictions for low-lying glueballs from Morningstar (1999) and Chen (2005).
