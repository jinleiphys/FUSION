# Thermodynamics of magnetized dense neutron-rich matter

> A neutron star is one of the possible end states of a massive star. It is compressed by gravity and stabilized by the nuclear degeneracy pressure. Despite its name, the composition of these objects is not exactly known. However, from the inferred densities, neutrons will most likely compose a significant fraction of the star's interior. While all neutron stars are expected to have a magnetic field, some neutron stars (''magnetars'') are much more highly magnetized than others with inferred magnetar surface magnetic field is between $10^{14}$ to $10^{15}$ gauss. While neutron stars are macroscopic objects, due to the extreme value of the stars' energy, pressure, and magnetic field the thermodynamics on the microscopic scale can be imprinted on the star's large scale behaviour. This contribution focusses on describing the thermodynamics of magnetized dense neutron matter, its equation of state and to explore conditions of a possible ferromagnetic state, contributions from the magnetized vacuum, as well as possible observational implications.

## Key claim

The paper explores whether a ferromagnetic phase transition in dense neutron-rich matter could contribute to the superstrong magnetic fields of magnetars, but concludes that such a transition would require baryon dipole moments roughly 35 times larger than normal values, making it a high-density effect that is not widely considered the origin of magnetar fields.

## Method

The author models magnetized charge-neutral, beta-equilibrated nuclear matter using a relativistic mean-field Lagrangian that includes both the charge coupling and the anomalous magnetic moment (AMM) coupling of fermions to the magnetic field. The thermodynamic grand potential is computed at zero temperature for free and interacting systems, using four nuclear parameter sets (NL3, FSU, IU-FSU, FSU2), and the magnetization, pressure anisotropy, and ferromagnetic phase boundary are derived from the grand potential.

## Key numbers

- Magnetar surface magnetic fields are inferred to be between \(10^{14}\) and \(10^{15}\) gauss.
- The ferromagnetic phase boundary is reached at multiples of approximately 35 times the normal baryon dipole moment (\(g_b^{(0)}\)).
- When the ferromagnetic phase boundary is crossed, the magnetic field \(B\) is of the order of \(10^{17}\) G.
- A quick estimate shows an energy difference of about 1 MeV/fm\(^3\) corresponds to luminosities of more than \(10^{50}\) ergs/m\(^3\).
- One FRB has an observed energy release of approximately \(10^{35}\) erg.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of magnetized nuclear matter (DienerSpin and references therein) by including an additional coupling constant parameter set (FSU2) and discussing observational consequences for magnetars. The paper builds on prior investigations of nuclear ferromagnetism (summarized in DienerPhD and more recently by Koji) and connects to observations of magnetar glitches, X-ray and gamma-ray bursts, and fast radio bursts (FRBs) as reported in FRBmag. The results enable future multi-wavelength studies with telescopes like the Square Kilometre Array (SKA) to probe the equation of state of strongly magnetized neutron stars.
