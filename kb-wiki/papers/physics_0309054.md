# Violations of fundamental symmetries in atoms and tests of unification theories of elementary particles

> High-precision measurements of violations of fundamental symmetries in atoms are a very effective means of testing the standard model of elementary particles and searching for new physics beyond it. Such studies complement measurements at high energies. We review the recent progress in atomic parity nonconservation and atomic electric dipole moments (time reversal symmetry violation), with a particular focus on the atomic theory required to interpret the measurements.

## Key claim

Atomic parity nonconservation (PNC) measurements and atomic electric dipole moment (EDM) searches provide complementary low-energy tests of the standard model and probes of new physics beyond it, with the cesium PNC measurement now in excellent agreement with the standard model prediction after resolution of earlier apparent discrepancies through improved atomic theory.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework for parity violation in atoms, focusing on the nuclear spin-independent weak interaction (parameterized by the nuclear weak charge \(Q_W\)) and nuclear spin-dependent contributions (including the nuclear anapole moment). They describe high-precision relativistic many-body methods for atomic structure calculations in heavy alkali-metal atoms, starting from the relativistic Hartree-Fock (RHF) approximation, including correlation corrections via the correlation potential method, and treating external-field interactions with the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method equivalent to the random-phase approximation with exchange.

## Key numbers

* The nuclear weak charge for cesium predicted by the standard model: \(Q_W^{SM}(^{133}_{55}{\rm Cs}) = -73.10 \pm 0.03\)
* The most precise measurement of parity nonconservation in cesium achieved an accuracy of 0.35%
* The most accurate atomic calculation for the cesium PNC amplitude has an uncertainty of 0.5%
* The parity violating effects in atoms increase faster than \(Z^3\), with a relativistic factor \(R(Z) \approx 10\) when \(Z=80\)
* The nuclear anapole moment scales as \(\kappa_a \propto A^{2/3}\), with \(\kappa_a \sim \alpha A^{2/3} \sim 0.1-1\) in heavy atoms
* For the nuclear spin-dependent contribution from the weak charge in cesium: \(\kappa_Q = 0.017\)
* Measurement accuracies achieved: 9% for bismuth \(^4S_{3/2}-^2D_{5/2}\), 2% for bismuth \(^4S_{3/2}-^2D_{3/2}\), 1% for lead, 1% for thallium (optical rotation), and 20% for thallium (Stark-PNC method)
* Theoretical uncertainties: 2.5-3% for thallium \(6P_{1/2}-6P_{3/2}\), 6% for thallium \(6P_{1/2}-7P_{1/2}\), 8% for lead, 12% for bismuth \(876\) nm transition, and about 70% for bismuth \(648\) nm transition

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on atomic parity violation by the Bouchiats (1974) and Khriplovich (1974), and the nuclear anapole moment theory by Flambaum and Khriplovich (1980). It synthesizes the experimental progress from the Novosibirsk, Oxford, Seattle, Paris, and Boulder groups, and the theoretical advances culminating in the 0.5% accuracy calculation by Dzuba, Flambaum, and Ginges (2002). The work enables precise extraction of the weak nuclear charge from cesium measurements, placing constraints on new physics such as extra Z bosons that are tighter than those from the Tevatron, and sets the stage for future isotope-ratio PNC measurements in rare-earth atoms that would eliminate atomic theory dependence.
