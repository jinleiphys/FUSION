# Multinucleon transfer with time-dependent covariant density functional theory

> The microscopic framework of time-dependent covariant density functional theory is applied to study multinucleon transfer reactions, with transfer probabilities calculated using the particle number projection method. It is found that similar total cross sections are obtained with two different relativistic density functionals, PC-PK1 and DD-ME2, as well as with the Skyrme functional SLy5 in a previous study, for multinucleon transfer in the reactions: $^{40}{\rm Ca}+{}^{124}{\rm Sn}$ at $E_{\rm lab} = 170$ MeV, $^{40}{\rm Ca}+{}^{208}{\rm Pb}$ at $E_{\rm lab} = 249$ MeV, and $^{58}{\rm Ni}+{}^{208}{\rm Pb}$ at $E_{\rm lab} = 328.4$ MeV. We report the first microscopic calculation of total cross sections for the reactions: $^{40}{\rm Ar}+{}^{208}{\rm Pb}$ at $E_{\rm lab} = 256$ MeV and $^{206}{\rm Pb}+{}^{118}{\rm Sn}$ at $E_{\rm lab} = 1200$ MeV. Compared to the results obtained with the GRAZING model, the cross sections predicted by the time-dependent covariant density functional theory are in much better agreement with data, and demonstrate the potential of microscopic models based on relativistic density functionals for the description of reaction dynamics.

## Key claim
The time-dependent covariant density functional theory (TD-CDFT) with particle number projection produces multinucleon transfer cross sections that are consistent across three very different density functionals (PC-PK1, DD-ME2, and Skyrme SLy5) for benchmark reactions, and yields significantly better agreement with experimental data than the GRAZING model for two additional reactions, though the theoretical cross sections systematically underestimate neutron stripping channels and shift peak positions to larger neutron numbers due to the neglect of neutron evaporation.

## Method
The time-dependent Dirac equation was solved for the single-particle wave functions of colliding nuclei, with scalar and vector potentials determined self-consistently from time-dependent densities and currents. After collision, the total wave function was projected onto components with definite neutron and proton numbers using the particle number projection method, and total cross sections for each transfer channel were obtained by integrating the probabilities over impact parameters using trapezoidal integration.

## Key numbers
- For $^{40}\text{Ca}+{}^{124}\text{Sn}$ at $E_{\text{lab}} = 170$ MeV: maximum fusion impact parameter is 3.93 fm (PC-PK1), 4.24 fm (DD-ME2), and 3.95 fm (Skyrme SLy5 from Sekizawa (2013))
- For $^{40}\text{Ca}+{}^{208}\text{Pb}$ at $E_{\text{lab}} = 249$ MeV: maximum fusion impact parameter is 4.63 fm (PC-PK1), 4.89 fm (DD-ME2), and 4.55 fm (Skyrme SLy5)
- For $^{58}\text{Ni}+{}^{208}\text{Pb}$ at $E_{\text{lab}} = 328.4$ MeV: maximum fusion impact parameter is 1.5 fm (PC-PK1), 2 fm (DD-ME2), and 1.38 fm (Skyrme SLy5); time evolution traced up to 4000 fm/$c$
- For $^{40}\text{Ar}+{}^{208}\text{Pb}$ at $E_{\text{lab}} = 256$ MeV: maximum fusion impact parameter is 5.99 fm (PC-PK1) and 6.36 fm (DD-ME2)
- For $^{206}\text{Pb}+{}^{118}\text{Sn}$ at $E_{\text{lab}} = 1200$ MeV: peak position in the $(-2p)$ channel is at neutron number $N=67$, three neutrons more than the experimental data
- Static ground-state calculations used a grid of $26\times 26\times 26$ points with 0.8 fm spacing; dynamical calculations used $60\times 26\times 60$ points; time step was 0.2 fm/$c$

## Context
This work extends the time-dependent covariant density functional theory framework, previously applied to fusion (Ren 2020), alpha cluster scattering (Ren 2020), and fission (Ren 2022), to multinucleon transfer reactions. The results are compared to prior TDHF calculations using the Skyrme functional SLy5 by Sekizawa (2013) and to the GRAZING model by Winther (1994, 1995). The TD-CDFT predictions for $^{40}\text{Ar}+{}^{208}\text{Pb}$ and $^{206}\text{Pb}+{}^{118}\text{Sn}$ represent the first microscopic calculations of total cross sections for these reactions, and the agreement with experimental data from Mijatovic (2016) and Diklic (2023) demonstrates the potential of relativistic density functionals for describing reaction dynamics. Future work will include dynamical pairing correlations and applications to transuranium nuclei production.
