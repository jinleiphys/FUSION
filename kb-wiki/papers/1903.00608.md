# Dynamics of clusters and fragments in heavy-ion collisions

> A review is given on the studies of formation of light clusters and heavier fragments in heavy-ion collisions at incident energies from several tens of MeV/nucleon to several hundred MeV/nucleon, focusing on dynamical aspects and on microscopic theoretical descriptions. Existing experimental data already clarify basic characteristics of expanding and fragmenting systems typically in central collisions, where cluster correlations cannot be ignored. Cluster correlations appear almost everywhere in excited low-density nuclear many-body systems and nuclear matter in statistical equilibrium where the properties of a cluster may be influenced by the medium. On the other hand, transport models to solve the time evolution have been developed based on the single-nucleon distribution function. Different types of transport models are reviewed putting emphasis both on theoretical features and practical performances in the description of fragmentation. A key concept to distinguish different models is how to consistently handle single-nucleon motions in the mean field, fluctuation or branching induced by two-nucleon collisions, and localization of nucleons to form fragments and clusters. Some transport codes have been extended to treat light clusters explicitly. Results indicate that cluster correlations can have strong impacts on global collision dynamics and correlations between light clusters should also be taken into account.

## Key claim

This review argues that cluster correlations (formation of deuterons, tritons, helium-3, alpha particles, and heavier fragments) are not merely a byproduct of heavy-ion collisions but can have strong reverse impacts on the global collision dynamics, such as compression, expansion, and collective flows, through energy release and reduction of degrees of freedom.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental data from the INDRA and FOPI collaborations for central heavy-ion collisions at incident energies from 32 to 400 MeV/nucleon, focusing on charge distributions, particle yields, kinetic energies, and stopping observables. It then surveys theoretical transport models, including mean-field models, molecular dynamics models with Gaussian wave packets, and their extensions that incorporate fluctuations or treat light clusters explicitly, comparing their performance in describing fragment formation.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density is approximately 0.16 fm^{-3}, and the compressed system in heavy-ion collisions can reach about twice this density (2ρ₀).
- In Xe+Sn central collisions at 50 MeV/nucleon, only 10% of total protons are emitted as free protons; about 20% are bound in alpha particles, about 10% in clusters of mass 2 and 3, and 60% in heavier fragments.
- In Au+Au central collisions at 400 MeV/nucleon, the fraction of free protons is still 30-40%.
- The mean multiplicity of fragments with charge Z ≥ 3 is about 7 at 50 MeV/nucleon and 5.5 at 32 MeV/nucleon in the Xe+Sn system.
- The stopping observable R_E (ratio of transverse to longitudinal kinetic energies) is about 0.5-0.7 for incident energies above 30 MeV/nucleon, indicating partial stopping.
- The typical time scale of compression and expansion dynamics is of the order of 10-100 fm/c.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on nuclear multifragmentation and transport models, building on experimental data from the INDRA collaboration (Hudan 2003) and FOPI collaboration (Reisdorf 1997, 2010) as well as statistical model analyses (Borderie 2008). It connects to studies of nuclear liquid-gas phase transitions and to astrophysical applications such as core-collapse supernovae, where the equation of state requires treatment of clusters at low densities. The review enables future development of transport models that consistently handle single-nucleon motions, two-nucleon collisions, and nucleon localization to form fragments and clusters.
