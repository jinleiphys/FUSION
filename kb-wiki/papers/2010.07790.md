# Nuclear collective dynamics in transport model with the lattice Hamiltonian method

> We review the recent progress on studying the nuclear collective dynamics by solving the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) equation with the lattice Hamiltonian method treating the collision term by the full-ensemble stochastic collision approach. This lattice BUU (LBUU) method has recently been developed and implemented in a GPU parallel computing technique, and achieves a rather stable nuclear ground-state evolution and high accuracy in evaluating the nucleon-nucleon (NN) collision term. This new LBUU method has been applied to investigate the nuclear isoscalar giant monopole resonances and isovector giant dipole resonances. While the calculations with the LBUU method without the NN collision term (i.e., the lattice Hamiltonian Vlasov method) describe reasonably the excitation energies of nuclear giant resonances, the full LBUU calculations can well reproduce the width of the giant dipole resonance of $^{208}$Pb by including a collisional damping from NN scattering. The observed strong correlation between the width of nuclear giant dipole resonance and the NN elastic cross section suggests that the NN elastic scattering plays an important role in nuclear collective dynamics, and the width of nuclear giant dipole resonance provides a good probe of the in-medium NN elastic cross section.

## Key claim

The lattice BUU (LBUU) method, when including the nucleon-nucleon collision term via a full-ensemble stochastic approach, can reproduce the width of the giant dipole resonance of 208Pb through collisional damping from NN scattering, but only the Vlasov (no-collision) version describes the excitation energies reasonably; the strong correlation between GDR width and NN elastic cross section suggests the width serves as a probe of the in-medium NN elastic cross section.

## Method

The authors solve the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) equation using the lattice Hamiltonian (LH) method, where the phase-space distribution is represented by test nucleons on a spatial lattice with a triangular form factor. The mean field is derived from an N3LO Skyrme pseudopotential, and the collision integral is implemented via a full-ensemble stochastic collision approach with in-medium NN cross sections obtained by multiplying free-space parameterizations (Cugnon et al., 1996) by a medium-correction factor. The ground state is prepared by energy variation, and giant resonances are excited and their time evolution is tracked using GPU-parallelized computations.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacing used in simulations: \( l_x = l_y = l_z = 0.5~\mathrm{fm} \)
- Time step: \( \Delta t = 0.2~\mathrm{fm}/c \) for full LBUU calculations; \( 0.4~\mathrm{fm}/c \) for Vlasov (no-collision) calculations
- Free NN cross-section cutoffs: \( \sigma_{\mathrm{NN}}^{\mathrm{free}}(p_{\mathrm{lab}} \le 0.1~\mathrm{GeV}/c) = \sigma_{\mathrm{NN}}^{\mathrm{free}}(p_{\mathrm{lab}} = 0.1~\mathrm{GeV}/c) \) for nn/pp; \( \sigma_{\mathrm{NN}}^{\mathrm{free}}(p_{\mathrm{lab}} \le 0.05~\mathrm{GeV}/c) = \sigma_{\mathrm{NN}}^{\mathrm{free}}(p_{\mathrm{lab}} = 0.05~\mathrm{GeV}/c) \) for np
- Cutoff momenta correspond to incident kinetic energies of about 1.3 MeV (for \( p_{\mathrm{lab}} = 0.05~\mathrm{GeV}/c \)) and 5.3 MeV (for \( p_{\mathrm{lab}} = 0.1~\mathrm{GeV}/c \))
- Box test for collision accuracy: volume \( V = 10 \times 10 \times 10~\mathrm{fm}^3 \) with 80 neutrons

## Context

This work extends prior BUU-type transport studies of nuclear giant resonances (e.g., Gai et al., 1981; TCPRC88; WKPRC95) by implementing the lattice Hamiltonian method of Lenk and Pandharipande (1989) with a full-ensemble stochastic collision approach and GPU parallel computing. The LBUU method achieves more stable ground-state evolution and higher accuracy in Pauli blocking than earlier BUU or QMD approaches, as noted in the transport code comparison project (XJPRC93; ZYXPRC97; OnoPRC100). The results enable using the GDR width as a quantitative probe of the in-medium NN elastic cross section, and the framework can be applied to extract nuclear equation-of-state information from collective modes.
