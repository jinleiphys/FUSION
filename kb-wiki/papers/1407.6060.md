# Single photon events from neutral current interactions at MiniBooNE

> The MiniBooNE experiment has reported results from the analysis of $ν_e$ and $\bar ν_e$ appearance searches, which show an excess of signal-like events at low reconstructed neutrino energies, with respect to the expected background. A significant component of this background comes from photon emission induced by (anti)neutrino neutral current interactions with nucleons and nuclei. With an improved microscopic model for these reactions, we predict the number and distributions of photon events at the MiniBooNE detector. Our results are compared to the MiniBooNE in situ estimate and to other theoretical approaches. We find that, according to our model, neutral current photon emission from single-nucleon currents is insufficient to explain the events excess observed by MiniBooNE in both neutrino and antineutrino modes.

## Key claim
The authors find that, according to their improved microscopic model, neutral current single-photon emission from single-nucleon currents is insufficient to explain the low-energy excess of electron-like events observed by MiniBooNE in both neutrino and antineutrino modes, but they note that multinucleon mechanisms have not yet been investigated for this channel.

## Method
The authors developed a microscopic model for neutral current (NC) photon emission on nucleons and nuclei, including nucleon-pole terms, Delta(1232) resonance excitation, and contributions from second-resonance-region baryon states (N*(1440), N*(1520), N*(1535)), with vector form factors from MAID analysis and axial form factors constrained by BNL/ANL pion production data. For nuclear targets, they applied a local Fermi gas model with Fermi motion, Pauli blocking, and collisional broadening of the Delta resonance in the medium, and calculated both incoherent and coherent reaction channels on carbon-12. They then folded these cross sections with the MiniBooNE neutrino and antineutrino fluxes, detector mass and composition (mineral oil, CH2), and detection efficiency to predict event distributions.

## Key numbers
- Total number of protons on target: 6.46 × 10^20 in neutrino mode and 11.27 × 10^20 in antineutrino mode
- Detector mass: 8.06 × 10^8 grams (mineral oil, CH2)
- Photon detection threshold: E_gamma > 140 MeV at MiniBooNE
- Leading axial N-Delta coupling: C^A_5(0) = 1.00 ± 0.11, with axial mass M_{AΔ} = 0.93 ± 0.07 GeV
- Nucleon axial mass: M_A = 1.016 ± 0.026 GeV
- In neutrino mode, total predicted NCγ events in the three reconstructed energy bins: 15.54 events (0.2-0.3 GeV), 48.23 events (0.3-0.475 GeV), 29.98 events (0.475-1.3 GeV)
- In antineutrino mode, total predicted NCγ events: 6.58 events (0.2-0.3 GeV), 19.55 events (0.3-0.475 GeV), 10.16 events (0.475-1.3 GeV)
- The MiniBooNE in situ estimate gives 19.5, 47.4, and 19.9 events in the same three bins for neutrino mode, and 8.8, 16.9, and 6.9 events for antineutrino mode
- The ratio sigma(NCγ, W<1.6 GeV)/sigma(nu_mu A -> mu- X) ≈ 0.8 × 10^{-4} at E_nu = 25 GeV, safely below the NOMAD upper limit of 4.0 × 10^{-4}

## Context
This work extends the microscopic NCγ model of Wang, Alvarez-Ruso, and Nieves (2013) to predict event rates at MiniBooNE, improving upon the earlier approach of Hill (2009, 2010) which treated the detector material as free nucleons and assumed a constant detection efficiency. The results agree well with the MiniBooNE in situ estimate (which used a resonance model tuned to NCπ0 data) and are consistent with the lower bound of the chiral effective field theory calculation by Zhang and Serot (2012), but disagree with the larger predictions of Hill (2010). The authors conclude that single-nucleon NCγ emission cannot explain the MiniBooNE low-energy excess, and suggest that multinucleon mechanisms (analogous to those studied for CCQE by Martini (2009), Amaro (2010), and Nieves (2011)) should be investigated, while the upcoming MicroBooNE experiment, capable of distinguishing photons from electrons, may resolve the puzzle.
