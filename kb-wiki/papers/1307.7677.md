# Relativistic Viscous Hydrodynamics for High Energy Heavy Ion Collisions

> It has been over a decade since the first experimental data from gold nuclei collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider suggested hydrodynamic behavior. While early ideal hydrodynamical models were surprisingly accurate in their predictions, they ignored that the large longitudinal velocity gradient meant that even small shear viscosity would produce large corrections to the transverse dynamics. In addition, much less was known about the equation of state predicted by lattice calculations of quantum chromodynamics, which predicts a soft region as the degrees of freedom change from quarks to hadrons but no first-order phase transition. Furthermore, the effects of late, dilute stage rescattering were handled within the hydrodynamic framework to temperatures where local kinetic equilibrium is difficult to justify. This dissertation presents a three-dimensional viscous hydrodynamics code with a realistic equation of state coupled consistently to a hadron resonance gas calculation. The code presented here is capable of making significant comparisons to experimental data as part of an effort to learn about the structure of experimental constraints on the microscopic interactions of dense, hot quark matter.

## Key claim

This dissertation presents a three-dimensional viscous hydrodynamics code with a realistic equation of state coupled consistently to a hadron resonance gas calculation, capable of making significant comparisons to experimental data to constrain microscopic interactions of dense, hot quark matter. The work specifically addresses the unknown range of validity of the boost-invariant assumption in viscous hydrodynamic models for predicting midrapidity observables.

## Method

The author developed a relativistic viscous hydrodynamics code that models the high-temperature quark-gluon plasma phase, coupled to a hadron resonance gas model for the lower-temperature hadronic phase. The code incorporates a realistic equation of state from lattice QCD calculations and includes three-dimensional dynamics without assuming boost invariance, allowing investigation of longitudinal structure. The model uses the Landau frame for the collective velocity and ignores effects of non-zero baryon number, limiting experimental observables to midrapidity.

## Key numbers

- The width of the pion rapidity distribution at $\sqrt{s_{\text{NN}}} = 200$ GeV is approximately 2.3 units of rapidity, as measured by the BRAHMS experiment
- The average collective velocity in heavy ion collisions is $<\beta>_{AA} = 0.59 \pm 0.05$, compared to $<\beta>_{pp} = 0.24 \pm 0.08$ in proton-proton collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV from STAR
- Ideal hydrodynamic simulations show excellent agreement with experimental elliptic flow data up to $p_T = 2$ GeV for mid-central collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV
- The baryon chemical potential at midrapidity at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV is reduced by almost two orders of magnitude from that of stable nuclear matter

## Context

This work extends prior viscous hydrodynamic codes that assumed boost invariance (Song 2007, Luzum 2008, Dusling 2007) by implementing full three-dimensional dynamics, addressing the questionable assumption of boost invariance even at midrapidity as shown by BRAHMS data (Bearden 2004). The code enables systematic investigation of how non-trivial longitudinal dynamics and uncertainties in the initial condition affect midrapidity predictions, building on the success of ideal hydrodynamic models that showed agreement with elliptic flow data (Kolb 2003, Adams 2005) but overestimated collision duration (Heinz 2002).
