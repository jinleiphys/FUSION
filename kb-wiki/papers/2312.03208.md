# Successful $νp$-process in neutrino-driven outflows in core-collapse supernovae

> The origin of the solar system abundances of several proton-rich isotopes, especially $^{92,94}$Mo and $^{96,98}$Ru, has been an enduring mystery in nuclear astrophysics. An attractive proposal to solve this problem is the $νp$-process, which can operate in neutrino-driven outflows in a core-collapse supernova after the shock is launched. Years of detailed studies, however, have cast doubt over the ability of this process to generate sufficiently high absolute and relative amounts of various $p$-nuclei. The $νp$-process is also thought to be excluded by arguments based on the long-lived radionuclide $^{92}$Nb.Here, we present explicit calculations, in which both the abundance ratios and the absolute yields of the $p$-nuclei up to $A\lesssim 105$ are successfully reproduced, even when using the modern (medium enhanced) triple-$α$ reaction rates. The process is also shown to produce the necessary amounts of $^{92}$Nb. The models are characterized by subsonic outflows and by the protoneutron star masses in the $\gtrsim 1.7 M_\odot$ range. This suggests that the Mo and Ru $p$-nuclides observed in the Solar System were made in CCSN explosions characterized by an extended accretion stage.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the $\nu p$-process can successfully reproduce both the abundance ratios and absolute yields of proton-rich isotopes up to $A\lesssim 105$, including $^{92,94}$Mo and $^{96,98}$Ru, but only in subsonic outflows from core-collapse supernovae with protoneutron star masses in the $\gtrsim 1.7 M_\odot$ range, even when using modern medium-enhanced triple-$\alpha$ reaction rates.

## Method

The authors constructed a suite of physical 1D models of neutrino-driven outflows from core-collapse supernovae, varying progenitor masses (9.5, 13, and 18 $M_\odot$) and protoneutron star properties, and computed nucleosynthetic yields for each outflow trajectory using a nuclear reaction network. They integrated these yields over time, weighted by mass outflow rates, to obtain production factors for each nuclide relative to solar system abundances, and compared subsonic versus supersonic outflow regimes.

## Key numbers

- Protoneutron star masses $\gtrsim 1.7 M_\odot$ are required for successful $\nu p$-process yields; the successful 13 $M_\odot$ progenitor model uses $M_\text{PNS} = 1.8 M_\odot$
- The subsonic outflow model produces $p$-nuclei yields for $A > 90$ that are 1--2 orders of magnitude higher than supersonic cases
- The outflow mass for both 13 and 18 $M_\odot$ models is $M_\text{out} \approx 4.4\text{--}4.5 \times 10^{-4} M_\odot$
- The subsonic outflow produces 3--6 late-time neutrons per iron-group seed, which is optimal for producing $^{92}$Nb
- The $^{92}$Nb production factor is normalized to $3 \times 10^{-3}$ times the solar $^{92}$Mo abundance, consistent with the required $10^{-3}\text{--}10^{-2}$ range from meteoritic studies
- The $^{98}$Tc yield is predicted at $\sim 10^{-4}$ of the $^{98}$Ru abundance
- Protoneutron star radii at 1--2 s post-explosion are in the range 17--22 km from modern simulations

## Context

This work resolves a long-standing puzzle in nuclear astrophysics regarding the origin of $^{92,94}$Mo and $^{96,98}$Ru, which previous studies by Fisker (2009), Bliss (2014, 2018), and Jin (2020) found the $\nu p$-process could not adequately produce, especially when using modern medium-enhanced triple-$\alpha$ reaction rates from Beard (2017). The authors show that earlier studies focused on supersonic outflow regimes, while subsonic outflows (previously overlooked in the $\nu p$-process context) naturally produce the required yields. This finding enables targeted follow-up studies with detailed multi-dimensional simulations of core-collapse supernovae with extended accretion stages and massive protoneutron stars.
