# A time-dependent wave-packet approach to reactions for quantum computation

> We describe a method for obtaining the scattering matrix for nuclear or chemical reactions on a finite lattice. Aside from the preparation of the initial and final states as wave packets, the only other operation required is unitary time evolution, making this approach ideal for simulations on quantum hardware. The central quantity is a time-dependent overlap between incoming and outgoing wave packets whose Fourier transform corresponds to the scattering matrix at fixed energy, from which one can calculate elastic and inelastic cross sections for reactions involving two interacting clusters. Working in Cartesian coordinates enables an efficient encoding of the problem on quantum hardware via the first quantization mapping, with favorable qubit scaling for describing asymptotic scattering states. Within this framework, we describe a quantum algorithm for probing the scattering amplitude through different angles, including the forward direction, which provides access to the total cross section via the optical theorem. We demonstrate our methods through a series of numerical examples, for both elastic and inelastic processes, comparing against exact calculations. The techniques we describe can more readily be extended to a large number of constituent particles than other existing approaches, once fault-tolerant quantum hardware becomes available.

# A time-dependent wave-packet approach to reactions for quantum computation

## Key claim
The authors describe a method for obtaining the scattering matrix for nuclear or chemical reactions on a finite lattice that requires only wave-packet state preparation and unitary time evolution, making it suitable for quantum computation. The approach works for short-ranged interactions and can be extended to composite systems with internal structure, but the authors explicitly restrict their numerical demonstrations to two spatial dimensions and note that extension to include Coulomb interactions is necessary for charged-particle reactions.

## Method
The method computes the scattering matrix via a time-dependent overlap between incoming and outgoing wave packets, whose Fourier transform yields the S-matrix at fixed energy. Working in absolute Cartesian coordinates with a first-quantized encoding on a spatial lattice, the authors prepare initial and final states as Gaussian wave packets with transverse momentum components to probe scattering through different angles. The wave packets are evolved to asymptotic times using unitary time evolution, and the overlap function is extracted via modified Hadamard tests, with the Fourier transform performed classically.

## Key numbers
- Two-nucleon system with masses M1 = M2 = 938.92 MeV/c² interacting via Gaussian potential V0 = -10 MeV and range R0 = 2.63 fm, producing a single bound state at Ed = -2.225 MeV in two spatial dimensions
- Spatial lattice of 64² points with lattice constant a = 1.9 fm, requiring 12 logical qubits per particle in first quantization
- Wave-packet spatial width σ = 5.39 fm; initial and final wave-packet momenta |k0,y| = |k'0,y| = 0.644 fm⁻¹
- Asymptotic evolution time t∞ = 1.08 MeV⁻¹
- Total cross section computed at evenly spaced energies E = 1, 4, ..., 49 MeV, with wave packets having appreciable support for 5 ≲ E ≲ 40 MeV
- Relative error on total cross section typically much less than 1% within the wave-packet support region
- Inelastic scattering example: V0 = 10 MeV, coupling V̄0 = 14.24 MeV, threshold Δ = 13 MeV, with no Feshbach resonances

## Context
This work extends the time-dependent scattering formalism of Tannor and Weeks (1991) from Jacobi coordinates to absolute Cartesian coordinates, enabling efficient encoding on quantum hardware via first quantization. It contrasts with existing quantum algorithms for elastic scattering (Sharma 2023, Turro 2024, Wang 2024, Yusf 2024) that compute partial-wave phase shifts and require summation over many partial waves or solution of coupled equations for non-central potentials. The method enables calculation of both elastic and inelastic cross sections for reactions involving composite systems, and can be extended to charged-particle reactions and photo-induced reactions once fault-tolerant quantum hardware becomes available.
