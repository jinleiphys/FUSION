# A Monte-Carlo Approach to Zero Energy Quantum Scattering

> Monte-Carlo methods for zero energy quantum scattering are developed. Starting from path integral representations for scattering observables, we present results of numerical calculations for potential scattering and scattering off a schematic $^4 \rm He $ nucleus. The convergence properties of Monte-Carlo algorithms for scattering systems are analyzed using stochastic differential equation as a path sampling method.

## Key claim

Monte-Carlo methods can be applied to zero-energy quantum scattering problems by using path integral representations of scattering observables, but the convergence is fundamentally slower than for bound-state problems because scattering wave functions are not normalizable and the continuous spectrum causes autocorrelation times that diverge as \(\beta^2\).

## Method

The authors derived three path integral representations for zero-energy scattering observables: one relating the cross section to a matrix element of the imaginary-time propagator, one relating the s-wave scattering length to the difference of partition functions \(\Delta Z(\beta) = \Tr e^{-\beta H} - \Tr e^{-\beta H_0}\), and one based on a virial-type scale transformation. They evaluated these path integrals numerically using a Langevin simulation (stochastic differential equation) to sample paths, with a reference potential (square well) to construct ratio estimators that reduce variance. The method was tested on a Gaussian potential and on a schematic \(^4\)He nucleus.

## Key numbers

* For a Gaussian potential with \(b=1\), \(m=1\), and potential strength \(V_0 = 0.1\) (repulsive), the ratio of cross sections was predicted within 4 percent of the exact result for \(\beta \approx 10\) and above.
* For \(V_0 = -0.3\) (attractive, no bound state), the cross section ratio was also accurate within 4 percent for \(\beta \approx 10\) and above.
* For \(V_0 = -0.5\) (attractive, near the bound state at \(V_0 \approx -0.67\)), the initial reference potential gave results approximately 10 percent too large; an improved reference potential (with bound state at the same \(V_0\)) reduced the deviation to below 4 percent.
* The autocorrelation time for the Langevin simulation at \(\beta = 100\) was approximately \(t_{ac} \approx 2000\), corresponding to one independent sample every 25000 paths.
* The autocorrelation time diverges as \(\beta^2\) for fixed \(\epsilon = 0.5\) and potential width \(b\).
* For the virial-based method (equation \ref{eq:VV}) with \(V_0 = 0.1\), a simulation time of \(T = 100000\) (2.5 million samples) was needed to approach the analytical scattering length \(a = -0.21\).

## Context

This work extends path integral Monte-Carlo techniques, previously successful for bound-state problems in nuclear physics (Carl, 1980s; Lang, 1980s) and quantum liquids (Kalos, 1970s; Whitlock, 1970s), to scattering problems where the continuous spectrum prevents direct application of standard methods. The authors explicitly address the limitations of the optical model approach (Foldy, 1945; Dover, 1980s; Eis, 1980s) for antiproton-nucleus and \(K^-\)-nucleus scattering, where missing dynamical properties cause phenomenological difficulties (Friedman, 1990s). The method enables first-principles calculation of zero-energy scattering observables for many-body targets, with the schematic \(^4\)He nucleus serving as a demonstration that the approach can be extended beyond simple potential scattering.
