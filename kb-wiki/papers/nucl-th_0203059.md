# On pressure versus density dependence in statistical multifragmentation models

> We show that the statistical multifragmentation model with the standard parameterization of the free volume predicts a constant pressure as density approaches the normal nuclear density. This is contrary to the Raduta&Raduta result obtained by disregarding the center of mass constraint. It is demonstrated that in finite nuclear systems the partitions with small number of fragments play an important role.

# On pressure versus density dependence in statistical multifragmentation models

## Key claim
The statistical multifragmentation model with standard free volume parameterization predicts a constant pressure as density approaches normal nuclear density, contrary to the Raduta&Raduta (2001) result of diverging pressure, provided the center-of-mass constraint is properly implemented in finite nuclear systems.

## Method
The authors derived the pressure from the canonical free energy expression for a finite system of A₀=100 nucleons, using the excluded volume approximation V_f = V - V₀ and including the (N-1) exponent from center-of-mass constraints. They directly calculated all 190,569,292 possible partitions using a liquid-drop description for fragments with binding energy B(A) = a_V·A - a_S·A^(2/3) (a_V ≈ a_S ≈ 16 MeV), disregarding Coulomb interaction and internal excitation.

## Key numbers
- Normal nuclear density: ρ₀ ≈ 0.15 fm⁻³
- System size: A₀ = 100 nucleons
- Liquid-drop parameters: a_V ≈ a_S ≈ 16 MeV
- In the limit V → V₀, pressure approaches P → T · (C₂/C₁) = constant (not infinity)
- Without center-of-mass constraint, pressure scales as P ∼ 1/V_f, giving P → ∞ at V → V₀

## Context
This comment directly addresses the Raduta&Raduta (2001) Physical Review Letters paper, which claimed a Van der Waals-like phase diagram with diverging pressure at normal density. The work extends the standard statistical multifragmentation approach of Botvina and collaborators, showing that proper implementation of conservation laws (center-of-mass constraint) yields finite pressure even at high densities. The authors note that for densities above ρ > (1/2–1/3)ρ₀, new physics beyond the individual-fragment picture (such as bubbles of nuclear gas inside nuclear matter) would be needed for realistic modeling.
