# Open Shell Effects in a Microscopic Optical Potential for Elastic Scattering of $^{6(8)}$He

> Elastic scattering observables (differential cross section and analyzing power) are calculated for the reaction $^6$He(p,p)$^6$He at projectile energies starting at 71 MeV/nucleon. The optical potential needed to describe the reaction is based on a microscopic Watson first-order folding potential, which explicitly takes into account that the two neutrons outside the $^4$He-core occupy an open p-shell. The folding of the single-particle harmonic oscillator density matrix with the nucleon-nucleon t-matrix leads for this case to new terms not present in traditional folding optical potentials for closed shell nuclei. The effect of those new terms on the elastic scattering observables is investigated. Furthermore, the influence of an exponential tail of the p-shell wave functions on the scattering observables is studied, as well as the sensitivity of the observables to variations of matter and charge radius. Finally elastic scattering observables for the reaction $^8$He(p,p)$^8$He are presented at selected projectile energies.

## Key claim

The authors extend traditional microscopic folding optical potentials to explicitly account for the open p-shell structure of the valence neutrons in \(^{6}\)He and \(^{8}\)He, but find that the additional terms arising from this open-shell treatment have a very small effect on the elastic scattering observables (differential cross section and analyzing power) at the energies studied, contrary to the expectation that such terms might resolve the "\(A_y\) problem" observed in experiments.

## Method

The authors construct a Watson first-order folding optical potential using the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon interaction as input. They model the \(^{6}\)He ground state as a filled s-shell (\(^{4}\)He core) plus two valence neutrons in the \(p_{3/2}\) sub-shell, using harmonic oscillator single-particle wave functions. The single-particle density matrix is folded with the NN t-matrix, and the expectation values of spin-momentum operators for the struck target nucleon are evaluated explicitly for the open p-shell, leading to two new terms in the optical potential: a spin-independent term proportional to the Wolfenstein amplitude \(C\) and a spin-dependent term proportional to the Wolfenstein amplitude \(M\). The resulting optical potential is used to calculate differential cross sections and analyzing powers for \(^{6}\)He(p,p)\(^{6}\)He at 71, 100, and 200 MeV/nucleon, and for \(^{8}\)He(p,p)\(^{8}\)He at selected energies.

## Key numbers

- The charge radius of \(^{6}\)He used to determine the s-shell oscillator parameter is taken from Mueller (2008); the matter radius is taken from Wang (thesis).
- The s-shell oscillator parameter is \(\nu_s = 0.392\) fm\(^{-1}\) and the p-shell oscillator parameter is \(\nu_p = 0.289\) fm\(^{-1}\).
- For the exponential tail study, matching radii of \(R_m = 3.2\) fm and \(R_m = 3.5\) fm are chosen; at \(R_m = 3.2\) fm, about 69% of the valence neutron probability is described by the harmonic oscillator p-wave, and at \(R_m = 3.5\) fm, about 79% is described.
- The s-shell probability at \(R_m = 3.2\) fm is greater than 95%, meaning the alpha core is minimally affected by the matching procedure.
- Calculations are carried out for projectile energies of 71, 100, and 200 MeV per nucleon for \(^{6}\)He.

## Context

This work extends traditional microscopic folding models, which were developed for closed-shell nuclei like \(^{16}\)O, \(^{40}\)Ca, or \(^{208}\)Pb (Elster, 1989), to the open-shell halo nuclei \(^{6}\)He and \(^{8}\)He. It addresses the "\(A_y\) problem" identified in experiments by Uesaka (2010) and Sakaguchi (2013), where simple folding models (Weppner, 2000; Gupta, 2000) could reproduce differential cross sections but failed to predict the analyzing power for \(^{6}\)He. The authors compare their approach to cluster-folding calculations by Crespo (2006, 2007) and Weppner (2011) that also had limited success with the \(A_y\) problem. This work enables future studies to incorporate more realistic wave functions (e.g., from few-body models or no-core shell model calculations) into the microscopic folding framework to better describe scattering from halo nuclei.
