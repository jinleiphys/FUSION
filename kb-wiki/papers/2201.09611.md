# Formulation of the Generator Coordinate Method with arbitrary bases

## Key claim
The paper generalizes the existing formalism for computing operator overlaps between Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) wave functions to cases where each HFB state is expanded in different, non-equivalent finite bases that span different subspaces of the Hilbert space, proving that all required contractions can be expressed solely in terms of quantities defined within the original finite bases without reference to the complementary infinite-dimensional subspace.

## Method
The author extends the formalism of PhysRevC.50.2874 by introducing a block structure for the Bogoliubov amplitudes where the finite basis and its complement are separated, and uses the unitary transformation operator connecting the two complete bases. The generalized Wick theorem is applied to evaluate operator overlaps by transforming to quasiparticle representation and decomposing the transformation operator into left and right components, yielding expressions for the density matrix and pairing tensors that vanish outside the finite subspaces. The LU decomposition of the overlap matrix between the two bases is introduced to define bi-orthogonal basis sets, leading to simplified formulas that match the standard expressions for complete bases but are expressed in terms of transformed U and V matrices.

## Key numbers
- The overlap between two HFB states is given by ⟨φ₀|φ₁⟩ = √(det A det R), where A is an N×N matrix defined as A = Ū₀ᵀ(Rᵀ)⁻¹Ū₁* + V̄₀ᵀ R V̄₁* and R is the overlap matrix between the two bases.
- The density matrix contraction is ρₗₖ⁰¹ = [V̄₁* A⁻¹ V̄₀ᵀ]ₗₖ for indices belonging to the finite subspaces, and zero otherwise.
- The pairing tensor contractions are κ̄ₖ₁ₖ₂⁰¹ = -[(Rᵀ)⁻¹ Ū₁* A⁻¹ V̄₀ᵀ]ₖ₁ₖ₂ and κₗ₁ₗ₂¹⁰ = [V̄₁* A⁻¹ Ū₀ᵀ (Rᵀ)⁻¹]ₗ₁ₗ₂ for indices in the finite subspaces.
- Using LU decomposition R = L₀* L₁ᵀ, the overlap simplifies to ⟨φ₀|φ₁⟩ = √(det Ã) where Ã = Ũ₀ᵀ Ũ₁* + Ṽ₀ᵀ Ṽ₁*.

## Context
This work extends the formalism of PhysRevC.50.2874 (denoted as I in the paper) for computing operator overlaps with arbitrary bases, building on the generalized Wick theorem developed by Onishi (1966) and Balian (1969). The approach addresses practical limitations in nuclear structure calculations, particularly in fission studies where different optimized basis parameters are needed for each nuclear shape (Schunck 2016, Marevic 2020), and in symmetry restoration where the basis is not closed under the symmetry operation. The formalism enables GCM calculations using harmonic oscillator bases with different oscillator lengths or rotated and translated bases without requiring impractically large basis sets, with applications to be presented in a future publication.
