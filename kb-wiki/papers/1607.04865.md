# Quest for magicity in hypernuclei

> In present study, we search the lambda magic number in hypernuclei within the framework of relativistic mean field theory (RMF) with inclusion of hyperon-nucleon and hyperon-hyperon potentials. Based on one- and two-lambda separation energy and two-lambda shell gap, 2, 8, 14, 18, 20, 28, 34, 40, 50, 58, 68, 70 and 82 are suggested to be the $Λ$ magic number within the present approach. The weakening strength of $Λ$ spin-orbit interaction is responsible for emerging the new lambda shell closure other than the model scheme. The predicted $Λ$ magic numbers are in remarkable agreement with earlier predictions and hypernuclear magicity quite resembles with nuclear magicity. %Our results also support the nuclear magicity, Our results are supported by nuclear magicity, where neutron number N = 34 is experimentally observed as a magic which is one of the $Λ$ closed shell in our predictions. In addition, the stability of hypernuclei is also examined by calculating the binding energy per particle, where Ni hypernucleus is found to be most tightly bound triply magic system in considered hypernuclei. Nucleon and lambda density distributions are observed and it is found that introduced $Λ$'s have significant impact on total density and reduces the central depression of the core nucleus. Nucleon and lambda mean field potentials and spin-orbit interaction potentials are also observed for predicted triply magic hypernuclei and the addition of $Λ$'s affect the both the potentials to a large extent. The single-particle energy levels are also analyzed to explain the shell gaps for triply magic multi-$Λ$ hypernuclei.

## Key claim

Based on one- and two-lambda separation energies and two-lambda shell gaps calculated within the relativistic mean field (RMF) approach, the authors suggest that 2, 8, 14, 18, 20, 28, 34, 40, 50, 58, 68, 70, and 82 are lambda magic numbers in hypernuclei, with the caveat that lambda numbers 28 and 68 appear as feeble or semi-magic closures, and the weakening of the lambda spin-orbit interaction is responsible for the emergence of new shell closures beyond the harmonic oscillator scheme.

## Method

The authors used the relativistic mean field (RMF) theory with the NL3* parameter set, including hyperon-nucleon (YN) and hyperon-hyperon (YY) interactions mediated by additional strange scalar (sigma*) and vector (phi) mesons. They calculated binding energies, one-lambda and two-lambda separation energies, two-lambda shell gaps, and single-particle energy levels for a series of multi-lambda hypernuclei built on doubly magic cores ranging from light (16O) to superheavy (378120) nuclei. The coupling constants for the lambda-meson interactions were fixed using the ratios R_sigma = 0.621, R_omega = 2/3, R_sigma* = 0.69, and R_phi = -sqrt(2)/3.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per particle for 56+8Lambda Ni is 9.5 MeV, identified as the most tightly bound triply magic system among those considered.
- The lambda binding energy for the s-state in 16Lambda O is -12.09 MeV (experimental value 12.5 +/- 0.35 MeV given in parentheses).
- The lambda binding energy for the s-state in 208Lambda Pb is -23.56 MeV (experimental value 26.3 +/- 0.8 MeV given in parentheses).
- The total radius of 16O is 2.541 fm, which reduces to 2.536 fm upon addition of a single lambda (17Lambda O).
- The total radius of 208Pb is 5.624 fm, which reduces to 5.616 fm upon addition of a single lambda (209Lambda Pb).
- The lambda mean field potential depth is found to be around -30 MeV.
- The nucleonic potential depth in multi-lambda hypernuclei is approximately -80 to -90 MeV.
- For 48+18Lambda Ca, the neutron s_{1/2} level energy is -62.499 MeV, compared to -54.037 MeV for the core of Ca hypernucleus.

## Context

This work extends the search for magic numbers from normal nuclei to hypernuclei, building on earlier RMF studies of hypernuclei by Schaffner (1994, 1993) and others, and on the established nucleonic magic numbers (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126). The predicted lambda magic numbers show remarkable agreement with earlier predictions and resemble nuclear magicity, with the neutron number N=34 (experimentally observed as a magic number in normal nuclei) appearing as one of the lambda closed shells. The results enable further studies of triply magic multi-lambda hypernuclei and the impact of strangeness on nuclear structure, including bubble structure and the extension of the drip line.
