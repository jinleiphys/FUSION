# Global polarization and spin alignment in heavy-ion collisions: past, present and future

> We give a brief overview on global polarization and spin alignment in heavy ion collisions. The current theoretical understandings on global polarization of hyperons and the global spin alignment of vector mesons are summarized.

## Key claim

This paper provides a brief overview of global polarization and spin alignment in heavy-ion collisions, summarizing current theoretical understandings. The abstract glosses over the fact that the paper is a conference proceeding that reviews existing work rather than presenting new original results, and it explicitly identifies several open questions that remain unanswered.

## Method

The authors review theoretical frameworks for spin phenomena in heavy-ion collisions, including the Wigner function approach for spin-1/2 particles and spin Boltzmann equations derived from the Kadanoff-Baym equation. For vector meson spin alignment, they describe a coalescence model incorporating strong force field effects from the chiral quark model with SU_L(3) x SU_R(3) symmetry, where the interaction between constituent quarks and the surrounding medium is mediated by effective vector fields.

## Key numbers

- Global OAM in Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV can reach 10^5 hbar, while local OAM can be as large as 1 to 4 hbar, based on Woods-Saxon model and hard-sphere model simulations
- Thermal vorticity field in heavy-ion collisions is of order 10^-2, giving induced spin alignment proportional to omega^2 of order 10^-4, much smaller than observed rho_00 values
- The coefficients C1 and C2 for phi meson spin alignment depend on strange quark mass m_s and phi meson mass m_phi: C1 = (8m_s^4 + 16m_s^2 m_phi^2 + 3m_phi^4) / (120m_s^2 (m_phi^2 + 2m_s^2)) and C2 = (8m_s^4 - 14m_s^2 m_phi^2 + 3m_phi^4) / (120m_s^2 (m_phi^2 + 2m_s^2))

## Context

This review extends the line of work initiated by Liang and Wang (2004) who first proposed that orbital angular momentum in non-central collisions can polarize quarks via spin-orbit coupling. It summarizes theoretical progress including the Wigner function approach by Becattini and collaborators and the spin Boltzmann equation framework by Sheng and colleagues. The paper identifies open questions including the need for comprehensive simulations based on spin kinetic theories or spin hydrodynamics with non-equilibrium effects, and possible connections between spin alignment and gluon condensates or glasma fields in the initial stage of heavy-ion collisions.
