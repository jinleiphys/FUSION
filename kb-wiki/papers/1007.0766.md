# Non-equilibrium steady state of sparse systems

> A resistor-network picture of transitions is appropriate for the study of energy absorption by weakly chaotic or weakly interacting driven systems. Such "sparse" systems reach a novel non-equilibrium steady state (NESS) once coupled to a bath. In the stochastic case there is an analogy to the physics of percolating glassy systems, and an extension of the fluctuation-dissipation phenomenology is proposed. In the mesoscopic case the quantum NESS might differ enormously from the stochastic NESS, with saturation temperature determined by the sparsity. A toy model where the sparsity of the system is modeled using a log-normal random ensemble is analyzed.

## Key claim

For "sparse" driven systems (where transition rates have a log-wide distribution), the non-equilibrium steady state (NESS) exhibits a crossover from linear response theory (LRT) to semi-linear response theory (SLRT) as driving strength increases, and in the quantum case the NESS temperature saturates to a finite value determined by the sparsity, rather than diverging as in the classical stochastic case.

## Method

The authors model a system with energy levels {E_n} and transition rates consisting of a driving term (from a T_A=∞ source) and a bath term (at temperature T_B) satisfying detailed balance. For the stochastic case, they analyze a "Chain model" with nearest-neighbor transitions where the driving-induced rates w_n are log-normally distributed, solving the rate equation Wp=0 for the steady-state occupation probabilities. For the quantum case, they use a master equation that includes off-diagonal coherences, with the driving term ε²[V,[V,ρ]] and bath-induced dephasing, solving for the NESS density matrix.

## Key numbers

- Sparsity parameter s = exp(-σ²), where s≪1 indicates sparse systems and s∼1 indicates all elements are comparable
- For the Chain model: N=25 levels, level spacing Δ₀=1, bath temperature T_B=10, bath-induced transition rate w^β=0.1
- In the stochastic case, the microscopic temperature of the n-th transition is T_n = [(w_n + w^β)/w^β] × T_B
- In the LRT limit (weak driving): D[LRT] = w̄_n × Δ₀²
- In the SLRT limit (strong driving): D[SLRT] = [1/w̄_n]⁻¹ × Δ₀²
- For strong driving, both D and T_sys are proportional to [1/w̄_n]⁻¹, hence proportional to ε²
- In the classical stochastic case, the EAR saturates to Ẇ_∞ = D_B/T_B
- In the quantum case, the EAR saturates to Ẇ_∞ = D_B/T_B - D_B/T_∞, where T_∞ is a finite saturation temperature

## Context

This work extends the study of NESS in driven systems beyond the standard linear response theory (LRT) and fluctuation-dissipation relation (FDR) framework. It builds on prior work by the authors (Hurowitz and Cohen, 2010) on sparse systems in optical billiards with vibrating walls and small metallic grains absorbing low-frequency irradiation. The "sparse" NESS resembles that of glassy systems (as studied by various authors), and the resistor-network picture adapts methods from percolating disordered resistor-networks (Miller, 1969). The work enables experimental detection of classical-to-quantum transitions through non-equilibrium measurements of energy absorption rates.
