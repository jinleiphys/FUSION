# Electromagnetic Signals and Backgrounds in Heavy-Ion Collisions:

> Aspects of the dilepton spectrum in heavy-ion collisions are discussed, with special emphasis on using lattice computations to guide the phenomenology of finite temperature hadronic matter. The background rates for continuum dileptons expected in forthcoming experiments are summarised. Properly augmented by data from ongoing measurements at HERA, these rates will serve as a calibrating background for QGP searches. Recent results on the temperature dependence of the hadronic spectrum obtained in lattice computations below the deconfinement transition are summarised. Light vector meson masses are strongly temperature dependent. Accurate measurements of a resolved $ρ$-peak in dimuon spectra in present experiments are thus of fundamental importance. ## Key claim
Lattice QCD computations below the deconfinement transition show that light vector meson masses (particularly the rho meson) are strongly temperature dependent, with a large shift already at T ≈ 0.9Tc, even though the chiral sector (quark condensate, pion mass, pion decay constant) shows no temperature effect up to that same temperature; this suggests that non-chiral condensates (e.g., gluon condensates) may play a stronger dynamical role than usually assumed in phenomenological models.

## Method
The author summarizes lattice QCD results for quenched (Nτ=4 and Nτ=8) and 4-flavor simulations, measuring the temperature dependence of the quark condensate, pion decay constant, and pion and rho meson masses at temperatures up to 0.9Tc. Masses were extracted from global fits to vector and pseudoscalar correlation functions constructed from local sources, with local masses checked via jack-knife estimates. The author also reviews perturbative QCD Drell-Yan cross section computations (exponentiated O(αs) and full O(αs²)) and their extrapolation to RHIC and LHC energies, including structure function measurements from HERA.

## Key numbers
- The Drell-Yan cross section agrees extremely well with data in the energy range √S = 19.4 to 640 GeV.
- At RHIC energies, the rapidity plateau for Drell-Yan pairs is 3 units wide; at LHC energies it increases to about 5 units.
- The thermal dilepton signal vanishes below the extrapolated Drell-Yan cross section at M ≈ 2–2.5 GeV for an initial temperature of 250 MeV; for an initial temperature of about 1 GeV, the signal may be visible above background even for M ≈ 5 GeV.
- The uncertainty in extrapolating Drell-Yan results to the intermediate mass range at RHIC and LHC energies is estimated as a factor of three, yielding an error of less than 0.5 GeV in the cutoff mass where thermal signal dominates.
- At LHC, the range of masses of interest corresponds to x ≈ 5×10⁻⁴.
- The quark condensate shows a discontinuity at Tc, with relatively temperature-independent behavior up to T ≈ 0.9Tc.
- The pion decay constant fπ shows no change with temperature up to T = 0.9Tc.
- The rho meson mass shows no discernible shift at T = 0.75Tc, but a large temperature-dependent shift at T ≈ 0.9Tc.
- The magnitude of the thermal mass shift depends on quark mass mq, with maximum effect for the rho meson, somewhat less for omega and phi, and virtually none for heavier mesons at this temperature.

## Context
This paper extends earlier lattice work by the CERN group (1992) and the HLRZ group (ongoing) on finite-temperature hadronic properties, using quenched simulations on Nτ=4 and Nτ=8 lattices. It contrasts with phenomenological models that emphasize chiral aspects (e.g., temperature dependence of the quark condensate) while neglecting other condensates like the gluon condensate. The results enable future non-perturbative lattice estimates of QCD matrix elements at high temperature for dilepton and photon production rates, and motivate accurate measurements of a resolved rho peak in dimuon spectra at present experiments (e.g., NA38) to test the predicted temperature-dependent mass shift. The work also provides calibrating background rates for QGP searches using Drell-Yan continuum data from HERA and the Aachen Workshop (1990) computations.
