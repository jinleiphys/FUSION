# Estimation of electric conductivity of the quark gluon plasma via asymmetric heavy-ion collisions

> We show that in asymmetric heavy-ion collisions, especially off-central Cu+Au collisions, a sizable strength of electric field directed from Au nucleus to Cu nucleus is generated in the overlapping region, because of the difference in the number of electric charges between the two nuclei. This electric field would induce an electric current in the matter created after the collision, which result in a dipole deformation of the charge distribution. The directed flow parameters $v_1^{\pm}$ of charged particles turn out to be sensitive to the charge dipole and provide us with information about electric conductivity of the quark gluon plasma.

## Key claim

In off-central Cu+Au collisions, a sizable electric field directed from the Au nucleus to the Cu nucleus is generated in the overlapping region, which induces a charge dipole in the quark gluon plasma; the charge-dependent directed flow parameters \(v_1^\pm\) of charged particles are sensitive to this dipole and can provide an experimental estimate of the electric conductivity \(\sigma\) of the quark gluon plasma, though the actual magnitude of the effect depends on the unknown conductivity and may be obscured by later-stage dynamics.

## Method

The authors performed event-by-event calculations of the electromagnetic fields generated by protons in Cu+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV, using the Liénard-Wiechert potentials with proton positions sampled from a Woods-Saxon distribution. They then derived a parametric relation between the charge-dependent part of the directed flow, \(A d'_e\), and the electric conductivity \(\sigma\) by estimating the total charge transferred across a plane between the two nuclei, using the constitutive relation \(\vec J = \sigma \vec E\) and neglecting space-time dependence of \(\sigma\). The electric flux through the overlapping region was computed as a function of impact parameter \(b\) to show its centrality dependence.

## Key numbers

- The event-averaged electric field magnitude in Cu+Au collisions at \(b = 4\) fm is \(|e \vec E| \sim O(m_\pi^2)\), comparable to fields in Au+Au collisions at the same energy.
- At \(t = 1\) fm/\(c\), the electric field is considerably larger than the critical field for electrons, \(|e|E_c = m_e^2\).
- The total transferred charge \(Q\) is estimated as \(Q \sim 1.7\,|e| \times B\), where \(B\) is a coefficient relating conductivity to temperature: \(\sigma \sim B\,C_{\rm EM} T\), with \(C_{\rm EM} = 8\pi \alpha_{\rm EM}/3\) for \(u,d,s\) quarks, \(T \sim 200\) MeV, \(\tau \sim 1\) fm/\(c\), and \(\epsilon \sim 1\).
- The charge-dependent directed flow parameter is estimated as \(A d'_e \sim -B \times 10^{-3}\), assuming total charged particle multiplicity \(N_{\rm tot} \sim 10^3\) and transferred charge \(n \sim 1\).
- The electric flux through the overlapping region is zero for most central collisions, positive for \(1 \lesssim b \lesssim 5\) fm (field toward Au), and changes sign at larger \(b\) (field toward Cu).

## Context

This work proposes a new experimental method to estimate the electric conductivity of the quark gluon plasma, complementing existing theoretical approaches such as lattice QCD simulations (Gupta 2003, Aarts 2007, Ding 2010, Buividovich 2010) and perturbative QCD calculations (Arnold 2003), which have yielded significantly different values of \(\sigma\). The approach is more direct than the conventional method using the Kubo formula and thermal dilepton spectra (Akamatsu 2011). The study extends the discussion of transient electromagnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions, previously focused on the chiral magnetic effect (Fukushima 2008, Burnier 2011, Burnier 2012), and builds on the suggestion by Bzdak (2011) that electric fields could be used to estimate conductivity, but notes that in symmetric collisions the field direction is not correlated with the reaction plane. The recent Cu+Au collision data from the PHENIX Collaboration (Huang 2012) make this proposal experimentally testable. Future work requires event-by-event hydrodynamics and magnetohydrodynamic modeling to quantify uncertainties from later-stage evolution and field fluctuations.
