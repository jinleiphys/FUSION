# Heavy Quark Energy Loss in the Quark-Gluon Plasma in the Moller theory

> We study the energy loss of a heavy quark propagating in the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) in the framework of the Moller theory, including possible large Coulomb logarithms as a perturbation to BDMPSZ bremsstrahlung, described in the Harmonic Oscillator (HO) approximation. We derive the analytical expression that describes the energy loss in the entire emitted gluon frequency region. In the small frequencies region, for angles larger than the dead cone angle, the energy loss is controlled by the BDMPSZ mechanism, while for larger frequencies it is described by N=1 term in the GLV opacity expansion. We estimate corresponding quenching rates for different values of the heavy quark path and different $m/E$ ratios.

# Heavy Quark Energy Loss in the Quark-Gluon Plasma in the Moller Theory

## Key claim

The energy loss of a heavy quark in the quark-gluon plasma can be described by a unified analytical formula covering all emitted gluon frequencies, where the BDMPSZ mechanism controls the small-frequency region outside the dead cone and the N=1 GLV opacity expansion controls the large-frequency region inside the dead cone, with Coulomb logarithms treated as a perturbation to the harmonic oscillator approximation. The heavy quark quenching rate is approximately equal to that of light quarks up to a dead cone angle of about 0.05, but the results are qualitative and depend on the interpolation formula used for the typical transverse momentum scale Q.

## Method

The author generalizes the Mehtar-Tani and Salgado (2011) perturbative approach for light quarks to heavy quarks, splitting the effective Moller potential into a harmonic oscillator (HO) part and a perturbative Coulombic part. The heavy quark propagator in the HO approximation is derived including a mass-dependent oscillatory phase, and the Coulomb correction is computed by integrating the perturbation over the HO propagator, separating the calculation into bulk (inside the medium) and boundary (outside the medium) contributions. The final energy loss spectrum is the sum of the HO term and the Coulomb correction, with the typical momentum scale Q chosen via two interpolation formulas that switch between the LPM diffusion regime and the dead-cone regime at the critical frequency ω_DC = (q̂/θ⁴)^{1/3}.

## Key numbers

- The dead cone angle is defined as θ = m/E, where m is the heavy quark mass and E is the heavy quark energy.
- The critical frequency separating the LPM and GLV regimes is ω_DC = (q̂/θ⁴)^{1/3}.
- The Debye mass is m_D ~ 4πα_s T² (1 + N_f/6) = (3/2)g²T⁶ for N_f = 3 light quarks.
- The density of scattering centers in the Gyulassy-Wang model is n = (3/2)T³.
- The bare quenching coefficient is q̂ = 4πα_s² N_c n.
- Numerical calculations use T = 0.4 GeV, α_s = 0.3, leading to μ = m_D = 0.9 GeV and q̂ ~ 0.3 GeV³.
- For L = 2 fm and E = 25 GeV, the quenching coefficient S(E) (divided by α_s C_F) is 1.74 for light quarks and 0.75 for heavy b-quarks (m_b = 5 GeV).
- For L = 2 fm and E = 100 GeV, S(E) is 0.83 for light quarks and 0.77 for heavy b-quarks.
- For L = 5 fm and E = 25 GeV, S(E) is 4.74 for light quarks and 2.55 for heavy b-quarks.
- For L = 5 fm and E = 100 GeV, S(E) is 2.5 for light quarks and 2.32 for heavy b-quarks.

## Context

This work extends the perturbative treatment of Coulomb logarithms in the LPM effect from light quarks (Mehtar-Tani and Salgado, 2011) to heavy quarks, building on the BDMPSZ formalism (Baier et al., 1997; Dokshitzer et al., 2001; Zakharov, 1996) and the GLV opacity expansion (Gyulassy, Levai, and Vitev, 2000; Wiedemann, 2000). It addresses the puzzle that the dead cone effect predicted by Dokshitzer and Kharzeev (2001) leads to quenching rates smaller than observed, while later work by Armesto, Salgado, and Wiedemann (2004) and Zakharov (2007) found the dead cone effect is absent in both HO and GLV approximations. The unified formula enables calculation of quenching weights across all frequencies and shows that heavy and light quark energy losses become similar for θ up to about 0.05, consistent with Zakharov (2007).
