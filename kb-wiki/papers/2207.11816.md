# Colliding and Fixed Target Mode in a Single Experiment -- A Novel Approach to Study the Matter under New Extreme Conditions

> Here, we propose a novel approach to experimentally and theoretically study the properties of QCD matter under new extreme conditions, namely having an initial temperature over 300~MeV and baryonic charge density over three times the values of the normal nuclear density. According to contemporary theoretical knowledge, such conditions were not accessible during the early Universe evolution and are not accessible now in the known astrophysical phenomena. To achieve these new extreme conditions, we proposed performing high-luminosity experiments at LHC or other colliders by means of scattering the two colliding beams at the nuclei of a solid target that is fixed at their interaction region. Under plausible assumptions, we estimate the reaction rate for the p+C+p and Pb+Pb+Pb reactions and discuss the energy deposition into the target and possible types of fixed targets for such reactions. To simulate the triple nuclear collisions, we employed the well-known UrQMD 3.4 model for the beam center-of-mass collision energies 2.76 TeV. As a result of our modeling, we found that, in the most central and simultaneous triple nuclear collisions, the initial baryonic charge density is approximately three times higher than the one achieved in the ordinary binary nuclear collisions at this energy.

## Key claim

The authors propose that triple nuclear collisions (TNC), where two colliding beams simultaneously strike a fixed target nucleus, can create QCD matter with initial temperatures over 300 MeV and baryonic charge densities exceeding three times normal nuclear density, conditions they claim were not present during early Universe evolution and are not found in known astrophysical phenomena. The claim is hedged by the acknowledgment that these are "plausible assumptions" and that the results come from UrQMD 3.4 simulations, which may not fully capture the dynamics of hot and dense stages at LHC energies.

## Method

The authors used the UrQMD 3.4 microscopic transport model to simulate simultaneous central p+C+p and Pb+Pb+Pb triple nuclear collisions at a center-of-mass collision energy of 2.76 TeV per nucleon pair. They placed two colliding beam nuclei (or protons) with zero impact parameter along the z-axis, with a third fixed target nucleus positioned at the origin between them. The reaction rates for TNC were estimated analytically for both traditional and non-traditional target arrangements, and the energy deposition into the target was discussed for different fixed target options including graphene layers, rotating solid targets, and jet micro-powder targets.

## Key numbers

- For the traditional target arrangement, the estimated TNC rate for p+C+p reactions is 4.8 × 10⁻⁹ s⁻¹, and for Pb+Pb+Pb reactions is 2.4 × 10⁻¹¹ s⁻¹.
- For the non-traditional target arrangement, the estimated TNC rate improves to 2.0 × 10⁻⁴ s⁻¹ for p+C+p and 3.4 × 10⁻⁷ s⁻¹ for Pb+Pb+Pb.
- In central simultaneous Pb+Pb+Pb TNC at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV, the initial net baryonic charge density reaches 0.567 fm⁻³, compared to 0.146 fm⁻³ in binary Pb+Pb collisions at the same energy.
- At √s_NN = 200 GeV, the initial net baryonic charge density in Pb+Pb+Pb TNC is 0.913 fm⁻³ versus 0.321 fm⁻³ in binary collisions.
- The UrQMD simulations show deviations from ALICE data of approximately 10% for p+p collisions and approximately 17% for Pb+Pb binary collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV for pseudorapidity |η| ≤ 1.

## Context

This work extends the concept of fixed-target experiments at colliders, building on the LHCb SMOG system (2013) and discussions of combined fixed-target and collider modes at LHC and RHIC. The authors propose TNC as an independent and complementary source of information on the QCD phase diagram, distinct from binary collision experiments and astrophysical probes of compact stars. The approach enables future experimental searches for QCD phase transitions at extreme baryonic densities, potentially allowing studies of Bose-Einstein condensates of pions or kaons, and could be implemented using super-thin microstrip metal sensors or rotating solid targets at LHC or other colliders.
