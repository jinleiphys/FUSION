# What might we learn from a future supernova neutrino signal?

> Neutrinos from a future Galactic supernova will be detected by several large underground detectors, in particular by SuperKamiokande (SK) and the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO). If, as expected, the $ν_μ$ and $ν_τ$ neutrinos have somewhat higher energy on average that the electron neutrinos, they will dominate the neutral current response. The ways to separate the neutral and charged current signals will be discussed, and the best strategy to measure the possible time delay of the neutral current events will be outlined. Given the expected count rates, one will be able to measure in this way the $ν_τ$ mass down to about 30 eV in SNO and to 50 eV in SuperKamiokande. Another application to be discussed is the supernova localization by the neutrino signal, prior to or independently of the electromagnetic signal. The accuracy with which this can be accomplished using the angular distributions of the reactions will be estimated. With two or more detectors one can, in principle, attempt triangulation based on the arrival time of the neutrinos. It will be argued that for realistic parameters this method will be very difficult and likely leads only to crude localization.

## Key claim

If a future Galactic supernova occurs, the mass of the tau neutrino can be constrained to about 30 eV in SNO and 50 eV in SuperKamiokande by measuring the difference in mean arrival time between neutral-current and charged-current events, provided the neutrino luminosity decays with a time constant of about 3 seconds and the supernova is at a typical Galactic distance of 10 kpc.

## Method

The author models the supernova neutrino luminosity as an exponential decay with time constant τ = 3 s and assumes thermal neutrino spectra with distinct temperatures for each flavor (ν_e at ~3.5 MeV, ν̄_e at ~5 MeV, ν_μ and ν_τ at ~8 MeV). For mass determination, the mean arrival time of neutral-current events (the "Signal") is compared to that of charged-current events (the "Reference") using Monte Carlo simulations; the signal includes events from ν_τ and ν_μ neutrinos and their antiparticles, while background from other reactions is accounted for. For supernova localization, the angular distributions of neutrino-electron scattering and of positrons from ν̄_e + p → e⁺ + n are analyzed, and triangulation based on arrival-time differences between detectors is evaluated.

## Key numbers

- For a supernova at 10 kpc, the energy-dependent delay for a massive neutrino is Δt(E) = 0.515 (m/E)² D seconds, where m is in eV, E in MeV, and D in 10 kpc.
- Expected event counts in SuperKamiokande: 8300 ν̄_e + p → e⁺ + n events; 710 ν_x + ¹⁶O → ν_x + γ + X events; 200 ν_e + e⁻ → ν_e + e⁻ events; 120 ν_x + e⁻ → ν_x + e⁻ events.
- Expected event counts in SNO (1 kton D₂O + 1.4 kton H₂O): 365 ν̄_e + p → e⁺ + n events; 400 ν_x + d → ν_x + n + p events; 50 ν_x + ¹⁶O → ν_x + γ + X events; 15 ν_e + e⁻ → ν_e + e⁻ events; 10 ν_x + e⁻ → ν_x + e⁻ events.
- The smallest recognizable ν_τ mass is about 30 eV in SNO (using deuteron disintegration) and about 50 eV in SuperKamiokande (using γ from ¹⁶O excitation).
- The mass limit scales as m_lim ∼ √τ T^{3/4}, independent of distance D.
- For neutrino-electron scattering pointing, the one-sigma error is about 5° in SuperKamiokande and about 20° in SNO.
- For triangulation, the uncertainty in cosθ is δ(cosθ) ≈ 0.5 for existing detectors.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of supernova neutrino signals from SN 1987A observations and builds on prior studies of neutral-current reactions in water and heavy water, specifically the excitation of ¹⁶O (as suggested by Langanke, Vogel, and Kolbe) and deuteron disintegration in SNO. The paper enables future searches for tau neutrino mass in the 30-50 eV range, which would improve upon the existing laboratory limit of m_{ν_τ} < 18 MeV by about a factor of 10⁶, and provides realistic estimates for supernova localization that can guide the design of a supernova early-warning network.
