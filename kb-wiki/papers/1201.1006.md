# On the Mass Distribution and Birth Masses of Neutron Stars

> We investigate the distribution of neutron star masses in different populations of binaries, employing Bayesian statistical techniques. In particular, we explore the differences in neutron star masses between sources that have experienced distinct evolutionary paths and accretion episodes. We find that the distribution of neutron star masses in non-recycled eclipsing high-mass binaries as well as of slow pulsars, which are all believed to be near their birth masses, has a mean of 1.28 M_solar and a dispersion of 0.24 M_solar. These values are consistent with expectations for neutron star formation in core-collapse supernovae. On the other hand, double neutron stars, which are also believed to be near their birth masses, have a much narrower mass distribution, peaking at 1.33 M_solar but with a dispersion of only 0.05 M_solar. Such a small dispersion cannot easily be understood and perhaps points to a particular and rare formation channel. The mass distribution of neutron stars that have been recycled has a mean of 1.48 M_solar and a dispersion of 0.2 M_solar, consistent with the expectation that they have experienced extended mass accretion episodes. The fact that only a very small fraction of recycled neutron stars in the inferred distribution have masses that exceed ~2 M_solar suggests that only a few of these neutron stars cross the mass threshold to form low mass black holes.

## Key claim

The paper finds that the mass distribution of neutron stars in double neutron star systems has a mean of 1.33 solar masses with a dispersion of only 0.05 solar masses, which is much narrower than the distribution for other non-recycled neutron stars (mean 1.28 solar masses, dispersion 0.24 solar masses); the authors note that such a small dispersion in double neutron stars "cannot easily be understood" and "perhaps points to a particular and rare formation channel," contradicting the common assumption that double neutron stars are representative of all neutron star birth masses.

## Method

The authors compiled neutron star mass measurements from radio pulsar timing (using post-Keplerian parameters), eclipsing X-ray pulsar observations, optical observations of white dwarf companions, and X-ray burst spectroscopy. They divided the sample into subgroups based on evolutionary history (double neutron stars, non-recycled eclipsing high-mass binaries and slow pulsars, and recycled neutron stars) and applied Bayesian statistical inference using the full posterior likelihood of each mass measurement to estimate the parameters (mean and dispersion) of an assumed Gaussian intrinsic mass distribution for each subgroup.

## Key numbers

- Non-recycled eclipsing high-mass binaries and slow pulsars: mean mass = 1.28 solar masses, dispersion = 0.24 solar masses
- Double neutron stars: mean mass = 1.33 solar masses, dispersion = 0.05 solar masses (68% errors of 0.03 solar masses in both parameters)
- Recycled neutron stars: mean mass = 1.48 solar masses, dispersion = 0.2 solar masses
- For double neutron star pulsars specifically: mean = 1.35 solar masses, dispersion = 0.05 solar masses
- For double neutron star companions specifically: mean = 1.32 solar masses, dispersion = 0.05 solar masses
- The mass ratios of two of the six double neutron stars with well-determined masses are within one part in approximately 5 x 10^{-3} of unity

## Context

This work extends earlier statistical analyses by Thorsett and Chakrabarty (1999), who found a combined population mean of 1.35 solar masses with 0.04 solar masses dispersion, and by Kiziltan, Kottas, and Thorsett (2010), Valentim, Rangel, and Horvath (2011), and Zhang et al. (2011), who inferred mass distributions for different neutron star subgroups. The paper directly challenges the bimodal distribution proposed by Schwab, Podsiadlowski, and Rappaport (2010) for double neutron stars, showing that a single Gaussian provides a better fit to the observed mass ratios. The results enable future studies of how supernova explosion mechanisms and accretion histories shape neutron star masses, and they highlight the need to explain why double neutron stars have a much narrower mass distribution than other neutron stars believed to be near their birth masses.
