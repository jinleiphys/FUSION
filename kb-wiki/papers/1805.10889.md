# Correlated transitions in TKE and mass distributions of fission fragments described by 4-D Langevin equation

> We have decomposed to symmetric and asymmetric modes the mass-TKE fission fragment distributions calculated by 4-dimensional Langevin approach and observed how the dominant fission mode and symmetric mode change as functions of $Z^2/\sqrt[3]{A}$ of the fissioning system in the actinides and trans-actinide region. As a result, we found that the symmetric mode makes a sudden transition from super-long to super short fission mode around $^{254}$Es. The dominant fission modes on the other hand, are persistently asymmetric except for $^{258}$Fm, $^{259}$Fm and $^{260}$Md when the dominant fission mode suddenly becomes symmetric although it returns to the asymmetric mode around $^{256}$No. These correlated "twin transitions" have been known empirically by Darleane Hoffman and her group back in 1989, but for the first time we have given a clear explanation in terms of a dynamical model of nuclear fission. More specifically, since we kept the shape model parameters unchanged over the entire mass region, we conclude that the correlated twin transition emerge naturally from the dynamics in 4-D potential energy surface.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates for the first time that the correlated "twin transitions" in total kinetic energy (TKE) and mass distributions of fission fragments, where the symmetric fission mode suddenly switches from super-long to super-short around ²⁵⁴Es, and the dominant fission mode becomes symmetric only for ²⁵⁸Fm, ²⁵⁹Fm, and ²⁶⁰Md before returning to asymmetric, emerge naturally from the dynamics of a 4-dimensional Langevin model with shape parameters kept unchanged over the entire actinide and trans-actinide region.

## Method

The authors used a 4-dimensional Langevin approach with collective variables (z₀/R₀, δ₁, δ₂, α) representing elongation, right fragment tip deformation, left fragment tip deformation, and mass asymmetry, based on the two-center shell model shape parameterization. The potential energy was calculated as the sum of liquid drop deformation energy and shell corrections, with the collective inertia tensor from the Werner-Wheeler approximation and the friction tensor from the wall-window friction formulation. For each fissioning nucleus from Uranium to Rutherfordium, typically 500,000 Langevin trajectories were simulated starting from the second minimum until scission (R_neck = 0), and the resulting mass-TKE distributions were decomposed into symmetric and asymmetric fission modes.

## Key numbers

- The symmetric fission mode makes a sudden transition from super-long to super-short around ²⁵⁴Es.
- The dominant fission mode becomes symmetric only for ²⁵⁸Fm, ²⁵⁹Fm, and ²⁶⁰Md, then returns to asymmetric around ²⁵⁶No.
- For ²⁵⁴Es at 20 MeV excitation energy, the asymmetric TKE component (TKE_asy) is around 195.74 MeV, the average TKE is 197.19 MeV, and the super-short symmetric component averages around 225.82 MeV.
- The standard fission mode trend is given by TKE_STD = 0.1168 Z² A⁻¹/³ + 13.9 MeV.
- The super-long fission mode trend for fissioning systems with 1300 < Z² A⁻¹/³ < 1550 is TKE_SL = 0.1542 Z² A⁻¹/³ − 48.7 MeV.
- The super-short fission mode trend for Z² A⁻¹/³ > 1550 is TKE_SS = 0.0849 Z² A⁻¹/³ + 99.0 MeV.
- TKE_SL predicts 137.9 MeV for ²¹³At and 151.5 MeV for ²²⁷Ac, compared to Brosa (1990) values of 146 MeV and 153 MeV respectively.

## Context

This work extends the 3-D Langevin calculations by Usang et al. (2016, 2017) that used microscopic mass and friction tensors but failed to reproduce the transition from double-peak fission yield of ²⁵⁶Fm to single-peak yield of ²⁵⁸Fm, and gave poor TKE as a function of fragment mass. The correlated twin transitions were first observed experimentally by Hoffman et al. (1980) and further corroborated by Hulet et al. (1989), who proposed they arise from splitting into double magic fragments with super-short fission modes (Brosa 1990), but without a dynamical explanation. The 4-D Langevin approach with independent fragment tip deformations now provides a consistent dynamical explanation, and the analysis of δ distributions reveals multiple fission paths in δ-space that the 3-D model could not capture. Future work could adopt microscopic transport coefficients to improve the underprediction of average TKE for ²⁵⁸Fm, ²⁵⁹Fm, and ²⁶⁰Md.
