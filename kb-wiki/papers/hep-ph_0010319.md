# Transverse energy from minijets in ultrarelativistic nuclear collisions: a next-to-leading order analysis

> We compute in next-to-leading order (NLO) perturbative QCD the amount of transverse energy produced into a rapidity region $ΔY$ of a nuclear collision from partons created in the few-GeV subcollisions. The NLO formulation assumes collinear factorization and is based on the subtraction method. We first study the results as a function of the minimum transverse momentum scale and define and determine the associated $K$-factors. The dependence of the NLO results on the scale choice and on the size of $ΔY$ is also studied. The calculations are performed for GRV94 and CTEQ5 sets of parton distributions. Also the effect of nuclear shadowing to the NLO results is investigated. The main results are that the NLO results are stable relative to the leading-order (LO) ones even in the few-GeV domain and that there exists a new kinematical region not present in the LO, which is responsible for the magnitude of the $K$-factors. The dependence on the size of $ΔY$ is found to be practically linear within two central units of rapidity, and the scale dependence present at RHIC energies is seen to vanish at the LHC energies. Finally, the effect of NLO corrections to the determination of transverse energies and multiplicities in nuclear collisions is discussed within the framework of parton saturation.

## Key claim

The NLO pQCD results for transverse energy from minijets remain stable relative to leading-order (LO) results even when the minimum transverse momentum scale \(p_0\) is taken down to the few-GeV domain, but this stability depends on the existence of a new kinematical region in the NLO computation (where a single parton may have transverse momentum below \(p_0\) as long as the total transverse momentum released exceeds \(2p_0\)) that is not present in LO and is responsible for the magnitude of the \(K\)-factors.

## Method

The authors computed the first \(E_T\) moment \(\sigma_{\rm QCD}\langle E_T \rangle_{\Delta Y, p_0}\) of the minijet transverse energy distribution in a single proton-proton collision within collinear factorization at NLO, using the subtraction method of Ellis, Kunszt, and Soper (1992) to cancel soft and collinear singularities between the \(2\to2\) and \(2\to3\) contributions. Nuclear collision geometry was incorporated via the standard nuclear overlap function \(T_{AA}({\bf b})\), with the initial transverse energy in a central \(AA\) collision given by \(E_T = T_{AA}\,\sigma_{\rm QCD}\langle E_T \rangle\). The calculations used the GRV94 and CTEQ5 parton distribution functions, with the renormalization and factorization scales set equal, and included the effect of nuclear shadowing through the nuclear parton distributions of Eskola, Kolhinen, and Salgado (1998).

## Key numbers

* For central collisions of large nuclei (\(A\sim200\)), the nuclear overlap function \(T_{AA}(0) \approx 30/{\rm mb}\).
* The saturation scale \(p_{\rm sat}\) is approximately 1 GeV for RHIC energies \(\sqrt s = 56\dots 200\,A{\rm GeV}\) and approximately 2 GeV for the LHC energy \(\sqrt s = 5500\,A{\rm GeV}\).
* The dependence of the NLO results on the size of the rapidity acceptance window \(\Delta Y\) is found to be practically linear within two central units of rapidity.
* The scale dependence present at RHIC energies is seen to vanish at the LHC energies.
* The relative numerical error from the finite \(2\to3\) terms is estimated to be below four percent.

## Context

This work extends the leading-order minijet transverse energy calculations of Eskola, Kajantie, and Lindfors (1989) and the pQCD+saturation approach of Eskola, Kajantie, Ruuskanen, and Tuominen (1999) to next-to-leading order, reducing the uncertainty from higher-order contributions to partonic cross sections. The NLO formulation differs from that of another recent NLO computation by Li and Ochs (1998) in the definition of the perturbative condition for \(2\to3\) processes. The results enable more precise determination of initial conditions (energy densities, number densities, and formation times) for hydrodynamic and parton cascade models of heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, and allow a refined application of the parton saturation criterion for estimating initial gluon and quark production.
