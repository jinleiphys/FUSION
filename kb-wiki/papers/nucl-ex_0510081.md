# Status of the Standard Solar Model Prediction of Solar Neutrino Fluxes

> The Standard Solar Model (BP04) predicts a total 8B neutrino flux that is 17.2% larger than measured in the salt phase of the SNO detector (and if it were significant it will indicate oscillation to sterile neutrinos). Hence it is important to examine in details uncertainties (and values) of inputs to the SSM. Currently, the largest fractional uncertainty is due to the new evaluation of the surface composition of the sun. We examine the nuclear input on the formation of solar 8B [S17(0)] and demonstrate that it is still quite uncertain due to ill known slope of the measured astrophysical cross section factor and thus ill defined extrapolation to zero energy. This yields an additional reasonably estimated uncertainty due to extrapolation of +0.0 -3.0 eV-b (+0% -14%). Since a large discrepancy exists among measured as well as among predicted slopes, the value of S17(0) is dependent on the choice of data and theory used to extrapolate S17(0). This situation must be alleviated by new measurement(s). The "world average" is driven by the Seattle result due to the very small quoted uncertainty, which we however demonstrate it to be an over-estimated accuracy. We propose more realistic error bars for the Seattle results based on the published Seattle data.

## Key claim

The Standard Solar Model (BP04) predicts a total 8B neutrino flux 17.2% larger than measured by SNO, but this discrepancy may not be significant because the nuclear input S17(0) carries an additional extrapolation uncertainty of +0.0 -3.0 eV-b (+0% -14%) that is not captured in the quoted "world average" value.

## Method

The author re-examines the direct-capture (DC) data for the 7Be(p,γ)8B reaction, focusing on the slope of the astrophysical S-factor between 300 and 1400 keV, which is essential for subtracting the d-wave contribution and extrapolating to zero energy. He compares all available DC and Coulomb-dissociation (CD) slope measurements, identifies systematic disagreements of up to five sigma among modern DC data sets, and re-analyzes the Seattle group's calibration spectra and target-profile stability to propose more realistic error bars. The analysis uses the published Seattle data to evaluate systematic uncertainties from target-profile variation, finding a 7.5% yield difference at the lowest measured energy.

## Key numbers

- The SSM (BP04) predicts a total 8B neutrino flux of 5.79 × 10^6 cm^-2 sec^-1; the SNO salt-phase neutral-current measurement gives Φ_NC = 4.94 ± 0.21 (stat) +0.38/-0.34 (syst) × 10^6 cm^-2 sec^-1, a ratio of 1.172 (17.2% excess).
- The fractional uncertainty on the predicted 8B flux from S17 is listed as 4% in Bahcall and Serenelli (2005), but the author estimates an additional extrapolation uncertainty of +0.0 -3.0 eV-b (+0% -14%).
- The Seattle group's quoted S17 value is 21.4 eV-b with ±4% accuracy; the author finds that the target-profile variation between the middle and end of the experiment produces a 7.5% yield difference at the lowest measured energy, a factor of 3 larger than the quoted systematic uncertainty.
- The Seattle calibration spectra using 7Be(α,γ)11C show a resonance energy off by 9 keV from the known energy in 11C.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of nuclear uncertainties in the Standard Solar Model (SSM) following Bahcall and Serenelli (2005), who identified the surface composition (Z/X) as the largest fractional uncertainty (12%) for the 8B flux. The paper specifically addresses the S17(0) value, which was previously quoted as 19 +4 -2 eV-b by Adelberger et al. and updated to 21.4 eV-b by the Seattle group (2003). The author argues that the "world average" is driven by the Seattle result with overestimated accuracy, and that the discrepancy between the SSM and SNO measurement cannot be interpreted as evidence for sterile neutrino oscillation until the nuclear extrapolation uncertainty is resolved. The work calls for new measurements of the 7Be(p,γ)8B reaction to resolve systematic disagreements among the Seattle, Orsay (2001), Bochum (2001), and Weizmann groups.
