# The Resonance Theory of Proton and Alpha Decay from Hot Sources

> The consecutive microscopic solution is presented of the problem of tunneling of a particle through a potential barrier. The method is applied to the alpha and proton decay of compound systems formed in fusion reaction. Appearance of the peaks in the spectrum of emitted particles is predicted. The peaks correspond to quasistationary states inside the potential barrier.

## Key claim
The paper predicts that the energy spectrum of alpha and proton particles emitted from compound nuclei formed in fusion reactions will exhibit resonance peaks corresponding to quasistationary states inside the potential barrier, rather than a smooth distribution. This prediction depends on the existence of such quasistationary states and the specific energy of the emitted particle relative to the barrier.

## Method
The authors solve the Schrödinger equation for an alpha particle in a combined Saxon-Woods nuclear potential and Coulomb potential, using numerical integration with Runge-Kutta-Nyström and Shtermer methods. They compute the decay width by expressing the mother nucleus wavefunction in a channel basis and calculating the transition amplitude to the exit channel, then integrate over emission angles to obtain the energy-dependent decay probability.

## Key numbers
- The alpha-nucleus potential uses parameters: $V_0 = 100$ MeV, diffuseness $s = 2.3$ Fm, radius $c = 1.2 A^{1/3}$ Fm.
- A resonance eigenvalue is found at $E_\alpha = 10.79$ MeV for the system $^{131}$La + $\alpha$ with angular momentum $L = 0$.
- The calculated resonance line has a half-width of approximately 200 eV for the same system.
- Wavefunctions are shown for non-resonance energies of 11 MeV and 14 MeV, demonstrating much smaller amplitude inside the barrier compared to the resonance case.

## Context
This work extends the traditional theory of alpha and proton decay, which typically treats tunneling from a quasistationary state, to cases where the emitted particle is not in such a state, as in compound systems formed in fusion reactions. The approach contrasts with standard inverse-cross-section methods that assume time reversibility, which the authors argue may be violated due to effects such as back-transparency of the inner barrier slope or temperature effects on barrier distribution, as noted in prior work by the authors and others. The predicted resonance modulation in sub-barrier alpha spectra could provide a new experimental probe of nuclear structure and reaction dynamics, particularly for nuclei far from the drip line.
