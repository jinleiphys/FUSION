# The size of most massive neutron stars may reveal its exotic cores

> The recent high precision observation of the most massive pulsar J1614-2230 with $(1.97\pm0.04)$ solar mass ($M_\odot$) was reported with a suggestion that many nuclear models which consider exotic particles in the core could be ruled out. However, many recent calculations could explain this star with various exotic particles, rendering the precise mass measurements insufficient to conclude on exotic cores. We examine the sensitivity of the radius of such a star to the details of its core. With our calculations and analysis, here we show that, for the most massive neutron star, with a precise observation of its radius it is possible to ascertain the presence of exotic cores.

# The size of most massive neutron stars may reveal its exotic cores

## Key claim

A precise measurement of the radius of a neutron star with mass near 2 solar masses can distinguish whether its core contains exotic particles (such as antikaons, hyperons, or quarks), even though the mass measurement alone cannot rule out exotic cores. The authors show that a pure nucleonic star at this mass can only have a narrow range of radii (approximately 10.8 km to 11.3 km), and any radius outside this range would require an exotic core.

## Method

The authors calculate neutron star mass-radius relations using extended relativistic mean field (RMF) models with different parameter sets (NL3, G1, G2, FSU2.1) that include or exclude antikaon condensates, solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations. They supplement these calculations with a fiducial model using one or two parabolic equations of state to systematically explore the allowed radius range for a star of mass (1.97 ± 0.04) solar masses, and compile results from other models in the literature that include hyperons and quarks.

## Key numbers

- Pulsar J1614-2230 mass: (1.97 ± 0.04) solar masses (M⊙)
- Without exotic core (RMF model G2): radius = 11.03 km for maximum mass 2 M⊙
- With antikaons (RMF model G1, both K⁻ and K̄⁰): radius = 12.55 km for maximum mass 2 M⊙
- With antikaons (RMF model NL3): radius = 13.04 km for maximum mass 2 M⊙
- Compiled range without exotic core (from literature): 11.0 km ≤ R₂ᴺ ≤ 11.2 km
- Compiled range with exotic core (from literature): 10.6 km ≤ R₂ᴱ ≤ 13.2 km
- Fiducial model range without exotic core: 10.87 km ≤ R₂ᴺ ≤ 11.27 km
- Fiducial model range with exotic core: 9.79 km ≤ R₂ᴱ ≤ 13.22 km
- Threshold for exotic core presence: if R₂ ≲ 10.87 km, the neutron star must have an exotic core

## Context

This work responds to the claim following the Demorest (2010) observation of J1614-2230 that many nuclear models with exotic particles could be ruled out. The authors note that Lattimer (2012) had already argued that radius information is needed to rule out exotic models. The paper extends the authors' own prior work (Gupta and Arumugam, 2012) on antikaon condensation in neutron stars, and compiles results from models including those by Fattoyev (2010), Shen (2011), Alford (2005), Bauswein (2012), Lastowiecki (2011), and others. The analysis enables future observational campaigns to determine whether exotic matter exists in neutron star cores by targeting radius measurements of the most massive neutron stars.
