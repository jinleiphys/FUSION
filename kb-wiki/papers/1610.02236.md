# The properties of nuclear matter with lattice $NN$ potential in relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory

> We study the properties of nuclear matter with lattice nucleon-nucleon ($NN$) potential in the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) theory. To use this potential in such a microscopic many-body theory, we firstly have to construct a one-boson-exchange potential (OBEP) based on the latest lattice $NN$ potential. Three mesons, pion, $σ$ meson, and $ω$ meson, are considered. Their coupling constants and cut-off momenta are determined by fitting the on-shell behaviors and phase shifts of the lattice force, respectively. Therefore, we obtain two parameter sets of the OBEP potential (named as LOBEP1 and LOBEP2) with these two fitting ways. We calculate the properties of symmetric and pure neutron matter with LOBEP1 and LOBEP2. In non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock case, the binding energies of symmetric nuclear matter are around $-3$ and $-5$ MeV at saturation densities, while it becomes $-8$ and $-12$ MeV in relativistic framework with $^1S_0,~^3S_1,$ and $^3D_1$ channels using our two parameter sets. For the pure neutron matter, the equations of state in non-relativistic and relativistic cases are very similar due to only consideration $^1S_0$ channel with isospin $T=1$ case.

## Key claim
The paper constructs one-boson-exchange potentials (LOBEP1 and LOBEP2) from a lattice QCD nucleon-nucleon potential at a pion mass of 468.6 MeV and finds that, with only S- and D-wave channels available, the non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory yields more attractive binding energies than the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory, opposite to the usual pattern seen with realistic potentials, but that including predicted P-wave contributions reverses this ordering, restoring consistency with previous relativistic calculations.

## Method
The authors first transformed the lattice NN potential (La469, from the HAL QCD collaboration) from coordinate space to momentum space via Fourier transformation. They then fitted a one-boson-exchange potential with three mesons (pion, sigma, omega) to the La469 potential using two procedures: LOBEP1 was fitted to the on-shell matrix elements, while LOBEP2 was fitted to the non-relativistic phase shifts (using the Thompson equation for the relativistic OBEP phase shifts). These OBEPs were then used as input to solve the Bethe-Goldstone equation in both non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) and relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) theories for symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter, including only the available \(^1S_0\), \(^3S_1\), \(^3S_1-^3D_1\), and \(^3D_1\) channels.

## Key numbers
- For symmetric nuclear matter with LOBEP1 in BHF: binding energy \(E/A = -3.56\) MeV at saturation density \(\rho = 0.33\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- For symmetric nuclear matter with LOBEP2 in BHF: \(E/A = -5.47\) MeV at \(\rho = 0.39\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- For symmetric nuclear matter with LOBEP1 in RBHF: \(E/A = -8.67\) MeV at \(\rho = 0.54\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- For symmetric nuclear matter with LOBEP2 in RBHF: \(E/A = -12.34\) MeV at \(\rho = 0.63\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The LOBEP2 phase-shift fit achieved \(\chi^2/N_\text{data} \sim 0.2\) up to laboratory energy \(E_\text{lab} = 300\) MeV.
- The lattice potential used corresponds to pion mass \(m_\pi = 468.6\) MeV and nucleon mass \(M_N = 1161\) MeV, with lattice spacing \(a = 0.121(2)\) fm on a \(32^3 \times 32\) lattice.

## Context
This work extends the earlier BHF calculations of Inoue et al. (2013, 2015), who used the same lattice NN potential to obtain a saturation point (\(\rho_0 = 0.414\) fm\(^{-3}\), \(E/A = -5.4\) MeV) and a neutron star maximum mass of 0.53 solar masses. By applying the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory (Brockmann and Machleidt, 1990), the authors demonstrate that the missing P-wave components of the lattice potential are crucial for reproducing the repulsive relativistic effect seen with realistic potentials such as Bonn A (Machleidt, 1989). The results enable future ab initio calculations from QCD once lattice data for higher partial waves and lighter quark masses become available.
