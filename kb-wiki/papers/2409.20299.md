# Big Bang: a theory or fact

> The discovery and confirmation that some nuclides were formed soon after the Big Bang is one of the strongest arguments in favour of the Hot Big Bang theory. The process of combining protons and neutrons in a hot, expanding universe is known as Big Bang nucleosynthesis (or, occasionally, primordial nucleosynthesis). The only experiment that is currently constructed to be concurrently sensitive to all four known fundamental forces - gravitational, electromagnetic, strong and weak forces - is big bang nucleosynthesis, which offers our earliest test of cosmology. Combined, our theoretical comprehension of Big Bang nucleosynthesis and the measurement of primordial abundances constitute one of the most robust foundations for the conventional cosmological model. This deliberation provides modern calculations of Big Bang nucleosynthesis, help readers gain an intuitive knowledge of the process and give an overview of the most recent state-of-the-art measurements. Our trust in the current basic picture of cosmology is reinforced by the overall amazing agreement between Big Bang nucleosynthesis and many cosmological probes.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) provides one of the strongest arguments for the Hot Big Bang theory, but it acknowledges that the standard BBN model still overestimates the primordial abundance of lithium-7 by a factor of about 2.8 compared to observations, indicating that the lithium problem remains an unresolved challenge.

## Method

The author presents a pedagogical review of standard BBN theory based on the Friedmann-Robertson-Walker cosmological model, using the baryon-to-photon ratio from CMB measurements (WMAP) and the latest neutron lifetime to compute primordial abundances. Theoretical yields for light nuclides (D, He-3, He-4, Li-7) are calculated using a nuclear reaction network that folds Maxwell-Boltzmann energy distributions with energy-dependent nuclear cross sections, and these predictions are compared to observational abundance determinations from metal-poor stars, quasar absorption systems, and blue compact galaxies.

* Baryon-to-photon ratio from CMB: eta_10 = 6.0914 ± 0.0438 (corresponding to eta = 6.0914 × 10^{-10} ± 0.0438 × 10^{-10})
* Neutron lifetime: tau_n = 877.75 ± 0.28_stat +0.22/-0.16_syst seconds (Go2021)
* Predicted He-4 mass fraction: 0.2461 ± 0.0002; observed: 0.2453 ± 0.0034 (Aver et al. 2021)
* Predicted D/H ratio: (2.620 ± 0.031) × 10^{-5}; observed: (2.527 ± 0.030) × 10^{-5} (Cooke et al. 2018)
* Predicted He-3/H ratio: (1.066 ± 0.005) × 10^{-5}; observed: < (1.1 ± 0.2) × 10^{-5} (Bania et al. 2002)
* Predicted Li-7/H ratio: (4.421 ± 0.067) × 10^{-10}; observed: (1.58^{+0.35}_{-0.28}) × 10^{-10} (Sbordone et al. 2010)
* Planck time: t_Pl ≈ 5.39 × 10^{-44} seconds
* CMBR temperature: T = 2.7277 ± 0.002 K
* Deuterium binding energy: 2.2246 MeV
* He-4 binding energy: 28.3 MeV
* BBN duration: approximately 3 to 20 minutes after the start of space expansion

## Context

This review paper synthesizes the standard BBN framework established by Hoyle and Tayler (1964) and subsequent work by Wagoner (1967, 1969), updating theoretical predictions with the latest neutron lifetime measurement from Gonzalez et al. (2021) and CMB-derived baryon density from WMAP. The paper highlights the persistent lithium problem, where the predicted Li-7 abundance exceeds observations by roughly a factor of 2.8, a discrepancy that has been noted by previous authors including Fields (2020) and which motivates future work on nuclear reaction rates and possible nonstandard physics. The author anticipates that upcoming facilities such as the James Webb Space Telescope and CMB-Stage 4 experiments will help resolve open questions about first-generation stars, the equation of state of neutron stars, and the number of relativistic neutrino families.
