# Capture rate and neutron helicity asymmetry for ordinary muon capture on hydrogen

> Applying heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory to ordinary muon capture (OMC) on a proton, we calculate the capture rate and neutron helicity asymmetry up to next-to-next-to-leading order. For the singlet hyperfine state, we obtain the capture rate Gamma_0 = 695 sec^{-1} while, for the triplet hyperfine state, we obtain the capture rate Gamma_1 = 11.9 sec^{-1} and the neutron asymmetry alpha_1 = 0.93. If the existing formalism is used to relate these atomic capture rates to Gamma_{liq}, the OMC rate in liquid hydrogen, then Gamma_{liq} corresponding to our improved values of Gamma_0 and Gamma_1 is found to be significantly larger than the experimental value, primarily due to the updated larger value of g_A. We argue that this apparent difficulity may be correlated to the specious anomaly recently reported for mu^- + p to n + nu_mu + gamma, and we suggest a possibility to remove these two "problems" simply and simultaneously by reexamining the molecular physics input that underlies the conventional analysis of Gamma_{liq}.

## Key claim

The authors calculate the ordinary muon capture (OMC) rate and neutron helicity asymmetry on a proton using heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory (HBChPT) up to next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO), finding that the resulting capture rate in liquid hydrogen is significantly larger than the experimental value when using standard "atom-liquid" translation formulae, primarily due to the updated larger value of the axial coupling constant g_A. They argue this discrepancy may be correlated with a previously reported anomaly in radiative muon capture (RMC) and suggest both problems can be resolved simultaneously by reexamining the molecular physics input, specifically a molecular mixing parameter ξ.

## Method

The authors applied heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory (HBChPT) to calculate the nucleon weak current form factors for ordinary muon capture on a proton up to next-to-next-to-leading order (one-loop level). They computed the atomic capture rates for the singlet (Γ₀) and triplet (Γ₁) hyperfine states of the muonic hydrogen atom, as well as the neutron helicity asymmetry α₁, using the non-relativistic form factors evaluated at the fixed momentum transfer of OMC (q² = -0.88 m_μ²). The atomic rates were then related to the capture rate in liquid hydrogen (Γ_liq) using existing "atom-liquid" translation formulae that account for molecular formation and hyperfine transitions.

## Key numbers

- Singlet hyperfine state capture rate: Γ₀ = 695 sec⁻¹ (NNLO calculation with g_A = 1.267, g_πN = 13.4)
- Triplet hyperfine state capture rate: Γ₁ = 11.9 sec⁻¹ (NNLO calculation with g_A = 1.267, g_πN = 13.4)
- Neutron helicity asymmetry for triplet state: α₁ = 0.925 (NNLO calculation with g_A = 1.267, g_πN = 13.4)
- Experimental OMC rate in liquid hydrogen: Γ_liq^exp = 460 ± 20 sec⁻¹ (Bardin et al., 1981)
- Calculated Γ_liq using standard translation formulae with λ_op^th = 7.1 × 10⁴ sec⁻¹: 518 sec⁻¹ (with ξ = 1.0)
- Calculated Γ_liq with ξ = 0.85 and λ_op^th: 461 sec⁻¹ (consistent with experiment)
- Neutrino energy in OMC: E_ν = 99.15 MeV
- NNLO corrections decrease Γ₀ by 3.9% relative to NLO (722 sec⁻¹ → 695 sec⁻¹)

## Context

This work extends previous HBChPT calculations of OMC by Bernard et al. (1995, 2000) from next-to-leading order (NLO) to next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO), and provides explicit numerical results for capture rates that were not given in the earlier NNLO calculation by Fearing et al. (1999). The authors find that their updated Γ₀ and Γ₁ values, when combined with standard "atom-liquid" translation formulae from Bakalov et al. (1994), produce a Γ_liq significantly larger than the experimental value, contradicting the earlier agreement achieved using the phenomenological form factors of Primakoff (1959) and Opat (1964). This discrepancy is linked to a similar anomaly in radiative muon capture (RMC) reported by Jonkmans et al. (1996) and Wright et al. (1998), where the observed photon spectrum required g_P to be 50% above its PCAC value. The authors propose that both problems can be resolved by introducing a molecular mixing parameter ξ (originally discussed by Weinberg, 1960) in the range 0.8-0.9, which modifies the ortho molecular state capture rates. This work enables future comparison with the planned PSI experiment measuring Γ₀ with 1% accuracy using a hydrogen gas target, and highlights the need for NNLO corrections to match such precision.
