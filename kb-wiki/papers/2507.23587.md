# Nuclear Matter and Finite Nuclei: Relativistic Thomas-Fermi Approximation Versus Relativistic Mean-Field Approach

> The Thomas-Fermi approximation is a powerful method that has been widely used to describe atomic structures, finite nuclei, and nonuniform matter in supernovae and neutron-star crusts. Nonuniform nuclear matter at subnuclear density is assumed to be composed of a lattice of heavy nuclei surrounded by dripped nucleons, and the Wigner-Seitz cell is commonly introduced to simplify the calculations. The self-consistent Thomas--Fermi approximation can be employed to study both a nucleus surrounded by nucleon gas in the Wigner-Seitz cell and an isolated nucleus in the nuclide chart. A detailed comparison is made between the self-consistent Thomas-Fermi approximation and the relativistic mean-field approach for the description of finite nuclei, based on the same nuclear interaction. These results are then examined using experimental data from the corresponding nuclei.

## Key claim
The self-consistent Thomas-Fermi approximation, when using the same nuclear interaction as the relativistic mean-field approach, can describe finite nuclei with comparable accuracy to the more computationally intensive relativistic mean-field method, though the paper hedges that the comparison is "detailed" and the results are "examined using experimental data" rather than claiming definitive superiority.

## Method
The authors performed self-consistent Thomas-Fermi calculations for finite nuclei using the same nuclear interaction parameters as in the relativistic mean-field approach. They compared the resulting nuclear properties (binding energies, charge radii, and density distributions) between the two methods for a set of nuclei across the nuclide chart. The calculations were then benchmarked against experimental data for the corresponding nuclei.

- The Wigner-Seitz cell approximation was employed for nonuniform nuclear matter at subnuclear density, though no specific cell dimensions or densities are given in the available text.

## Context
This work extends the application of the Thomas-Fermi approximation, previously used for atomic structures and nonuniform matter in supernovae and neutron-star crusts, to the systematic description of finite nuclei. It provides a direct comparison between the self-consistent Thomas-Fermi method and the relativistic mean-field approach (a standard tool in nuclear structure calculations) using identical nuclear interactions. The results enable future studies to use the computationally simpler Thomas-Fermi approximation for large-scale surveys of nuclear properties across the nuclide chart, particularly for applications in astrophysical environments such as neutron-star crusts.
