# The impact of magnetic field on the thermal evolution of neutron stars

> The impact of strong magnetic fields B>10e13 G on the thermal evolution of neutron stars is investigated, including crustal heating by magnetic field decay. For this purpose, we perform 2D cooling simulations with anisotropic thermal conductivity considering all relevant neutrino emission processes for realistic neutron stars. The standard cooling models of neutron stars are called into question by showing that the magnetic field has relevant (and in many cases dominant) effects on the thermal evolution. The presence of the magnetic field significantly affects the thermal surface distribution and the cooling history of these objects during both, the early neutrino cooling era and the late photon cooling era. The minimal cooling scenario is thus more complex than generally assumed. A consistent magneto-thermal evolution of magnetized neutron stars is needed to explain the observations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the magnetic field has relevant and often dominant effects on neutron star thermal evolution, calling into question standard cooling models; the key qualifier is that this conclusion applies specifically to stars with magnetic fields above approximately 10^13 G, and that a fully consistent magneto-thermal evolution code (still in progress at the time of writing) is needed to properly disentangle interior properties.

## Method

The authors performed two-dimensional cooling simulations solving the energy balance equation with anisotropic thermal conductivity, including all relevant neutrino emission processes and crustal heating from Ohmic dissipation of the magnetic field. The magnetic field geometry was fixed during the evolution, with the field decay described by a phenomenological law combining an initial Hall stage and a later Ohmic stage, using characteristic timescales tau_Hall = 10^3 yr / B_0,15 and tau_Ohm = 10^6 yr. The resulting cooling curves were compared with a sample of neutron stars spanning about three orders of magnitude in magnetic field strength, using spin-down estimates for the dipolar field component and spin-down ages corrected for field decay.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field threshold for significant impact: B > 10^13 G
- Ohmic dissipation timescale used: tau_Ohm = 10^6 yr
- Hall timescale parameterization: tau_Hall = 10^3 yr / B_0,15, where B_0,15 is the initial field in units of 10^15 G
- For high-field neutron stars (B_0 > 5 x 10^14 G), the initial equilibrium temperature may be increased up to a factor of 5 compared to non-magnetized models, and remains nearly constant for up to 10^4 years
- The heat balance line (HBL) correlation: T_s,6^4 ~ C B_d,14^2, where C is a constant in the range 5-50, T_s,6 is surface temperature in units of 10^6 K, and B_d,14 is the dipolar field in units of 10^14 G
- The thermal and magnetic evolution proceeds on a similar timescale of about 10^6 yr

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of anisotropic temperature distributions in magnetized neutron star envelopes by Greenstein (1983) and Potekhin (2001), and builds on crustal confined magnetic field models by Geppert (2004) and Azorin (2006) that considered stationary solutions. The paper directly challenges the "minimal cooling" scenario reviewed by Page (2004) and Yakovlev (2004), showing that magnetic field effects must be included as a necessary ingredient. The results support the heat balance line correlation reported by Pons and Link (2007) and enable future fully coupled magneto-thermal evolution simulations to better interpret observations of magnetars, isolated neutron stars, and radio-pulsars.
