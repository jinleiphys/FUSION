# New inversion methods for the Lorentz Integral Transform

> The Lorentz Integral Transform approach allows microscopic calculations of electromagnetic reaction cross sections without explicit knowledge of final state wave functions. The necessary inversion of the transform has to be treated with great care, since it constitutes a so-called ill-posed problem. In this work new inversion techniques for the Lorentz Integral Transform are introduced. It is shown that they all contain a regularization scheme, which is necessary to overcome the ill-posed problem. In addition it is illustrated that the new techniques have a much broader range of application than the present standard inversion method of the Lorentz Integral Transform.

## Key claim

The paper introduces new inversion techniques for the Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) that incorporate regularization schemes to handle the ill-posed inversion problem, and claims these methods have a much broader range of application than the standard inversion method, particularly for response functions with more complicated structures than a single peak.

## Method

The authors test three new inversion approaches: a wavelet expansion using Mexican hat wavelets, an iterative method based on the Fridman approach for solving Fredholm integral equations, and an iterative method based on Banach's fixed point theorem. Each method is applied to two test cases: a realistic deuteron longitudinal response function from LIT calculations with non-negligible error margins, and a high-precision test case with an analytically known double-peak response function. The standard inversion method using basis functions of the form \(\chi_n(\omega,\alpha_i) = \omega^{\alpha_1} \exp(-\alpha_2 / (n\omega))\) is used as a reference.

## Key numbers

* For the deuteron test case at momentum transfer \(q^2 = 5\) fm\(^{-2}\), the LIT calculation used a Lorentzian width \(\sigma_{\rm I} = 10\) MeV and the elastic contribution had the form \(L_{\rm el} = 0.0436 / ((E_0 - \sigma_{\rm R})^2 + \sigma_{\rm I}^2)\) with binding energy \(E_{\rm B} = 2.2246\) MeV.
* The wavelet inversion with parameters \(M=0\) and \(N_0=15\) produced a smooth curve, while increasing to \(N_0=31\) already led to strong unrealistic oscillations.
* For the Fridman method, the iteration was stopped when the mean square error \({\cal E}_l\) was smaller than \(2.5 \times 10^{-16}\) or when errors or unphysical oscillations increased.
* The Banach method requires \(\sigma_{\rm I}\) sufficiently small, with the existence of a unique fixed point proven for \(0 < \sigma_{\rm I} < \epsilon\) for some \(\epsilon > 0\).

## Context

This work extends the LIT method originally proposed about a decade earlier (Leidemann et al., 1996) for ab initio calculations of electroweak reactions with nuclei from A=2-7, including inclusive electron scattering, total photoabsorption, exclusive reactions, photomeson production, and weak processes. The standard inversion method using basis functions had been successfully applied to simply structured responses but encountered difficulties with more complicated structures, such as the Coulomb monopole and quadrupole transitions in deuteron electron scattering that required separate inversions (Leidemann et al., 1996). The new methods enable inversion of total LITs with more complex structures in a single inversion step, which is important for future applications to nuclei with A>3 where bound-state methods are being developed (as illustrated by the seven-method comparison for \(^4\)He ground state solutions).
