# Elastic scattering of hadrons

> Colliding high energy hadrons either produce new particles or scatter elastically with their quantum numbers conserved and no other particles produced. We consider the latter case here. Although inelastic processes dominate at high energies, elastic scattering contributes considerably (18-25%) to the total cross section. Its share first decreases and then increases at higher energies. Small-angle scattering prevails at all energies. Some characteristic features are seen that provide informationon the geometrical structure of the colliding particles and the relevant dynamical mechanisms. The steep Gaussian peak at small angles is followed by the exponential (Orear) regime with some shoulders and dips, and then by a power-law drop. Results from various theoretical approaches are compared with experimental data. Phenomenological models claiming to describe this process are reviewed. The unitarity condition predicts an exponential fall for the differential cross section with an additional substructure to occur exactly between the low momentum transfer diffraction cone and a power-law, hard parton scattering regime under high momentum transfer. Data on the interference of the Coulomb and nuclear parts of amplitudes at extremely small angles provide the value of the real part of the forward scattering nuclear amplitude. The real part of the elastic scattering amplitude and the contribution of inelastic processes to the imaginary part of this amplitude (the so-called overlap function) at nonforward transferred momenta are also discussed. Problems related to the scaling behavior of the differential cross section are considered. The power-law regime at highest momentum transfer is briefly described.

## Key claim

Elastic scattering of hadrons contributes 18-25% to the total cross section at high energies, with its share first decreasing and then increasing at higher energies; the paper reviews phenomenological models and experimental data rather than presenting a single new measurement, noting that none of the asymptotic predictions of the black disk model have been observed in experiment.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental data on elastic hadron scattering across a wide energy range (from √s ≈ 4 GeV to 7 TeV), comparing results from proton-proton and proton-antiproton collisions. It examines the differential cross section as a function of squared momentum transfer t, analyzing three main angular regimes: the diffraction cone at small angles (Gaussian shape), the intermediate Orear regime (exponential fall-off with shoulders and dips), and the hard parton scattering regime at large angles (power-law drop). Various phenomenological models are compared, including geometrical approaches, Reggeon exchanges, QCD-inspired models, and the unitarity condition.

## Key numbers

- Elastic scattering contributes 18-25% to the total cross section at high energies
- Inelastic processes comprise 75-80% of all processes at high energies
- At low energies, the Orear regime occupies the interval between 0.8 and 2 GeV² in |t|; at LHC energies, it has moved to 0.4-1.5 GeV²
- The power-law decrease starts around |t| > 1.5-2 GeV² at the LHC
- The product γσ_t = 35.92 mb·GeV² is predicted for the black disk model, where γ is the |t| value at the first dip
- At √s = 7 TeV, the ratio X = σ_el/σ_t = 0.25, Z = 4πB/σ_t = 1.00, X/Z = 0.25, and XZ = 0.25
- The diffraction cone slope B changes by about 10% within the cone for |Δt| ≈ 0.3 GeV² at LHC energies
- The total cross section upper bound is σ_t ≤ (π/2m_π²) ln²(s/s₀)

## Context

This review extends the long line of work on elastic hadron scattering dating back to the 1960s, when first experimental data on pp and πp scattering were obtained at energies between 6.8 and 19.2 GeV (Foley, Cocconi, Krisch, and others). It builds on the geometric picture of two hadrons colliding as Lorentz-contracted black disks (Heisenberg, 1952; Cheng and Wu, 1970) and the Froissart-Martin bound on total cross section growth. The paper enables future work by cataloging the successes and failures of phenomenological models, noting that predictions often fail when new energy domains become available, and identifies open questions about whether the Orear regime and hard scattering will survive at ever higher energies.
