# Quantum time scales in alpha tunneling

> The theoretical treatment of alpha decay by Gamow is revisited by investigating the quantum time scales in tunneling. The time spent by an alpha particle in front of the barrier and traversing it before escape is evaluated using microscopic alpha nucleus potentials. The half-life of a nucleus is shown to correspond to the time spent by the alpha knocking in front of the barrier. Calculations for medium and super heavy nuclei show that from a multitude of available tunneling time definitions, the transmission dwell time gives the bulk of the lifetime of the decaying state, in most cases.

## Key claim

The half-life of a nucleus undergoing alpha decay corresponds to the transmission dwell time of the alpha particle in the region in front of the barrier (region I), not the time spent inside the barrier itself; this result is derived within a semiclassical WKB framework and holds for medium and super heavy nuclei, though the barrier dwell time is not negligible and must be added for a full account.

## Method

The authors used a semiclassical WKB approximation to evaluate dwell times for alpha tunneling through a spherically symmetric potential consisting of a nuclear part (double folding model with realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions), a Coulomb part (also double folded from charge densities), and a Langer-modified centrifugal barrier. They derived expressions for the transmission dwell time in the region between the inner and outer turning points (region I) and inside the barrier (region II), then connected these to the standard WKB decay width formula to relate the half-life to the transmission dwell time in region I. Calculations were performed for four medium-heavy nuclei, two super heavy nuclei, and the light nucleus ⁸Be, using experimental Q-values.

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁰⁸Te (Q = 3.445 MeV): ln2·τ_D,T^I = 1.506 s, ln2·τ_D,T^II = 0.261 s, experimental half-life = 4.286 s, P_α^full = 0.41.
- For ¹⁶⁹Ir (Q = 6.151 MeV): ln2·τ_D,T^I = 0.466 s, ln2·τ_D,T^II = 0.069 s, experimental half-life = 1.28 s, P_α^full = 0.42.
- For ¹⁷³Au (Q = 6.836 MeV): ln2·τ_D,T^I = 9.29 × 10⁻³ s, ln2·τ_D,T^II = 1.38 × 10⁻³ s, experimental half-life = 26.6 × 10⁻³ s, P_α^full = 0.40.
- For ¹⁸⁰W (Q = 2.508 MeV): ln2·τ_D,T^I = 2.69 × 10²⁵ s, ln2·τ_D,T^II = 3.74 × 10²⁴ s, experimental half-life = 5.68 × 10²⁵ s, P_α^full = 0.54.
- For ²⁷³Ds (Q = 11.368 MeV): ln2·τ_D,T^I = 2.038 × 10⁻⁵ s, ln2·τ_D,T^II = 2.76 × 10⁻⁶ s, experimental half-life = 17 × 10⁻⁵ s, P_α^full = 0.14.
- For ²⁷⁷112 (Q = 11.3 MeV): ln2·τ_D,T^I = 1.16 × 10⁻⁴ s, ln2·τ_D,T^II = 1.52 × 10⁻⁵ s, experimental half-life = 6.90 × 10⁻⁴ s, P_α^full = 0.19.
- For ⁸Be (l=2, Q = 3.1218 MeV): ln2·τ_D,T^I = 3.7 × 10⁻²² s, ln2·τ_D,T^II = 2.56 × 10⁻²² s, experimental half-life = 3.02 × 10⁻²² s, P_α^full = 2.1; the transmission coefficient |T|² = 0.86, and the alpha escapes after one knock.

## Context

This work revisits the original Gamow (1928) and Gurney-Condon (1928) treatment of alpha decay by introducing quantum tunneling time concepts, specifically the dwell time formalism of Smith (1960) and Buttiker (1983), and the traversal time of Buttiker and Landauer. It extends earlier work by Price (1993) on dwell times for transitions from quasilevels to continua, and by Baz (1966) and Rybachenko on Larmor clock methods. The results enable a direct connection between the half-life and the transmission dwell time in front of the barrier, providing a new interpretation of the assault frequency and number of assaults in the standard Gamow picture, and may be relevant to other tunneling problems involving bound states.
