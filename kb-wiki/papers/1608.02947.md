# Millihertz Quasi-periodic Oscillations in 4U 1636-536: Putting Possible Constraints on the Neutron Star Size

> Based on previous studies of quasi-periodic oscillations in neutron star LMXBs, mHz quasi-periodic oscillations (QPO) are believed to be related to `marginally stable' burning on the neutron star (NS) surface. Our study of phase resolved energy spectra of these oscillations in 4U 1636-53 shows that the oscillations are not caused by variations in the blackbody temperature of the neutron star, but reveals a correlation between the change of the count rate during the mHz QPO pulse and the spatial extend of a region emitting blackbody emission. The maximum size of the emission area $R^2_{\mathrm{BB}}=216.7^{+93.2}_{-86.4}$km$^2$, provides the direct evidence that the oscillations originate from a variable surface area constrained on the NS and are therefore not related to instabilities in the accretion disk. The obtained lower limit on the size of the neutron star (11.0 km) rules out equations of state that prefer small NS radii. Observations of mHz QPOs in NS LMXBs with NICER and eXTP will reduce the statistical uncertainty in the lower limit on the NS radius, which together with better estimates of the hardening factor and distance, will allow improving discrimination between different equations of state and compact star models. Furthermore, future missions will allow us to measure the peak blackbody emission area for a single mHz QPO pulse, which will push the lower limit to larger radii.

## Key claim
The mHz quasi-periodic oscillations in 4U 1636-53 originate from a variable surface area on the neutron star rather than from temperature variations of the whole star or from accretion disk instabilities, based on phase-resolved spectroscopy that reveals a correlation between count rate changes and the spatial extent of a blackbody-emitting region.

## Method
The authors analyzed two archival XMM-Newton observations of 4U 1636-53 taken in March and September 2009, extracting phase-resolved energy spectra across the mHz QPO pulse cycle. They fitted the quiescent-state spectra with an absorbed blackbody plus disk blackbody model, then tested whether the pulse-phase variations could be explained by temperature changes in the blackbody component or by an additional blackbody component with variable area. They also tested whether variations in the disk blackbody component could account for the oscillations.

## Key numbers
- Maximum blackbody emission area during the mHz QPO pulse: \(R^2_{\mathrm{BB}} = 216.7^{+93.2}_{-86.4}\) km\(^2\), derived from an apparent area of \(44.2^{+5.4}_{-4.8}\) km\(^2\) at a distance of 6.0 kpc, assuming a hardening factor of \(1.60^{+0.10}_{-0.15}\) and a compactness of \(M/R_{\mathrm{NS}} = 0.126\).
- Lower limit on the neutron star radius: 11.0 km (2\(\sigma\) lower limit), which rules out equations of state that prefer small NS radii such as strange quark matter.
- Blackbody temperature of the additional component during the pulse: \(0.65 \pm 0.05\) keV, constant within errors across the pulse phase.
- Apparent blackbody area during the pulse peak: \(44.2^{+5.4}_{-4.8}\) km\(^2\) (both observations combined, distance 6.0 kpc).
- Apparent blackbody area at the beginning of the pulse (\(0.3 \le \phi < 0.4\)): \(6.9^{+9.5}_{-3.5}\) km\(^2\); at the end (\(0.65 \le \phi < 0.8\)): \(9.9^{+3.6}_{-2.7}\) km\(^2\).
- Averaged mHz QPO centroid frequencies: 6.6–7.2 mHz (March observation) and 6.1–6.5 mHz (September observation).
- Quiescent-state blackbody temperature: \(1.81^{+0.03}_{-0.02}\) keV (March) and \(1.75^{+0.02}_{-0.03}\) keV (September).
- Quiescent-state blackbody area: \(10.9^{+0.7}_{-0.6}\) km\(^2\) (March) and \(8.9 \pm 0.5\) km\(^2\) (September), assuming distance 6.0 kpc and NS mass 1.4 M\(_\odot\).

## Context
This work extends studies of mHz QPOs in neutron star LMXBs, previously interpreted as marginally stable nuclear burning on the NS surface (Heger 2007) and observed in sources like 4U 1608-52 (Revnivtsev 2001) and 4U 1636-53 (Yu 2002, Altamirano 2008, Lyu 2014, 2015). The phase-resolved spectral analysis provides direct evidence that the oscillations originate from a variable surface area on the neutron star, ruling out the alternative interpretation of temperature oscillations across the whole star (Heger 2007) and disk instability models proposed for GRS 1915+105 (Taam 1997). The derived lower limit on the NS radius (11.0 km) constrains equations of state, and future observations with NICER and eXTP are expected to reduce statistical uncertainties to about 0.3 km and 0.15 km respectively, enabling improved discrimination between different compact star models.
