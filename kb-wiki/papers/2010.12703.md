# Machine learning-based inversion of nuclear responses

> A microscopic description of the interaction of atomic nuclei with external electroweak probes is required for elucidating aspects of short-range nuclear dynamics and for the correct interpretation of neutrino oscillation experiments. Nuclear quantum Monte Carlo methods infer the nuclear electroweak response functions from their Laplace transforms. Inverting the Laplace transform is a notoriously ill-posed problem; and Bayesian techniques, such as maximum entropy, are typically used to reconstruct the original response functions in the quasielastic region. In this work, we present a physics-informed artificial neural network architecture suitable for approximating the inverse of the Laplace transform. Utilizing simulated, albeit realistic, electromagnetic response functions, we show that this physics-informed artificial neural network outperforms maximum entropy in both the low-energy transfer and the quasielastic regions, thereby allowing for robust calculations of electron scattering and neutrino scattering on nuclei and inclusive muon capture rates.

## Key claim

The authors present a physics-informed artificial neural network (Phys-NN) that outperforms the standard maximum entropy (MaxEnt) method for inverting the Laplace transform of nuclear response functions, specifically in the low-energy transfer region and the quasielastic region, when tested on simulated but realistic electromagnetic response functions. The claim is conditional on the use of simulated data that mimic the statistical noise of Green's function Monte Carlo calculations, and the improvement is demonstrated for response functions with either a single quasielastic peak or an additional narrow elastic peak.

## Method

The authors constructed a neural network architecture that uses Gaussian kernel basis functions to model the reconstructed response function, with the output passed through an exponential function to enforce positivity and normalized to satisfy the sum rule. They trained the network on 500,000 simulated pairs of response functions and their Laplace transforms (Euclidean responses), where the responses were modeled as either a single asymmetric quasielastic peak (skew-normal distribution) or a combination of a narrow elastic peak (Gaussian) and a quasielastic peak, with parameters sampled to reflect realistic momentum-transfer dependence and nuclear binding. The training employed a two-phase optimization using a loss function that combines an entropy-based response cost and a reduced chi-squared Euclidean cost, with the network trained on NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPUs using TensorFlow.

## Key numbers

*   The training dataset consisted of 500,000 pairs of response functions and Euclidean responses, partitioned into training (80%), validation (20%), and test (1,000 pairs) datasets.
*   The momentum transfer \(q\) was sampled uniformly between 100 and 700 MeV, with the quasielastic peak width \(\sigma_{\rm QE}\) allowed 20% fluctuations around its central value \(2qk_F/m_N\) with Fermi momentum \(k_F=225\) MeV.
*   For the two-peak dataset, the elastic peak width \(\sigma_{\rm EL}\) was uniformly sampled between 5 and 10 MeV, and its strength was sampled proportional to \(e^{-\frac{\gamma}{2} q^2}\) with \(\gamma=400\) MeV.
*   The Phys-NN achieved a test metric \(1-\overline{R_R^{2}}\) of \(0.42 \times 10^{-4}\) for the one-peak dataset and \(9.04 \times 10^{-4}\) for the two-peak dataset, compared to MaxEnt values of \(29.7 \times 10^{-4}\) and \(84.8 \times 10^{-4}\), respectively.
*   The Phys-NN test entropy \(\overline{S_R}\) was \(0.72 \times 10^{-4}\) for the one-peak dataset and \(9.16 \times 10^{-4}\) for the two-peak dataset, compared to MaxEnt values of \(60.4 \times 10^{-4}\) and \(107 \times 10^{-4}\), respectively.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to invert the Laplace transform of nuclear response functions, which is a notoriously ill-posed problem typically addressed using the maximum entropy method (MaxEnt) as in Lovato (2016) and Lovato (2019). The authors note that MaxEnt has been shown to be inadequate for reconstructing the low-energy structure of nuclear response functions, requiring experimental input for low-lying transitions in carbon-12 as in Lovato (2016), and that it fails near the nuclear breakup threshold for muon capture rates as in Lovato (2019). The Phys-NN approach is inspired by recent machine learning techniques for inverting the Laplace transform in condensed matter physics, such as those of Yoon (2018) and Fournier (2020), which used artificial neural networks to recover electron spectral densities from imaginary-time Green's functions. This work enables more robust calculations of electron scattering, neutrino scattering, and inclusive muon capture rates on nuclei, particularly for larger nuclei like oxygen-16 and argon-40 where auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo calculations produce noisier Euclidean responses.
