# Orbital-free Density Functional Theory: differences and similarities between electronic and nuclear systems

> Orbital-free Density Functional Theory (OF-DFT) has been used when studying atoms, molecules and solids. In nuclear physics, there has been basically no application of OF-DFT so far, as the Density Functional Theory (DFT) has been widely applied to the study of many nuclear properties mostly within the Kohn-Sham (KS) scheme. There are many realizations of nuclear KS-DFT, but computations become very demanding for heavy systems, such as superheavy nuclei and the inner crust of neutron stars, and it is hard to describe exotic nuclear shapes using a finite basis made with a limited number of orbitals. These bottlenecks could, in principle, be overcome by an orbital-free formulation of DFT. This work is a first step towards the application of OF-DFT to nuclei. In particular, we have implemented possible choices for an orbital-free kinetic energy and solved the associated Schrödinger equation either with simple potentials or with simplified nuclear density functionals. While the former choice sheds light on the differences between electronic and nuclear systems, the latter choice allows us discussing the practical applications to nuclei and the open questions.

# Orbital-free Density Functional Theory: differences and similarities between electronic and nuclear systems

## Key claim
Orbital-free Density Functional Theory (OF-DFT) can produce reasonable results for magic nuclei when the kinetic energy is approximated with a mixed Thomas-Fermi and von Weizsäcker form, but the density tails are not well reproduced in either nuclear or electronic systems; the long-range character of the Coulomb force partially masks this deficiency in electronic systems compared to nuclear systems.

## Method
The authors solved the Euler equation of OF-DFT in spherical symmetry using a mixed kinetic energy functional combining Thomas-Fermi and von Weizsäcker terms with a parameter β, applied to non-interacting fermions in external potentials (Woods-Saxon for neutrons, Coulomb for electrons) and to self-consistent calculations with a simplified Skyrme energy density functional. For the nuclear case, they tested β values of 1/9, 1/5, and 1, and also implemented a single post-convergence Kohn-Sham iteration to recover shell effects in the density distributions.

## Key numbers
- For 8 neutrons in a Woods-Saxon potential mimicking ¹⁶O (V₀ = 50 MeV, R₀ = 1.2 × 16¹/³ fm, a = 0.65 fm): exact total energy is −142.27 MeV; OF-DFT with β = 1/9 gives −140.85 MeV, with β = 1/5 gives −135.19 MeV, with β = 1 gives −96.31 MeV.
- For the same Woods-Saxon system: exact r.m.s. radius is 2.575 fm; OF-DFT with β = 1/9 gives 2.500 fm, with β = 1/5 gives 2.562 fm, with β = 1 gives 3.12 fm.
- For 10 electrons in a Coulomb potential V(r) = −10e²/r: exact total energy is −200.0 Ha; OF-DFT with β = 1/9 gives −208.6 Ha, with β = 1/5 gives −196.1 Ha, with β = 1 gives −141.96 Ha.
- For the same Coulomb system: exact r.m.s. radius is 0.27 a.u.; OF-DFT with β = 1/9 gives 0.30 a.u., with β = 1/5 gives 0.318 a.u., with β = 1 gives 0.482 a.u.
- Single-particle energies for the Woods-Saxon potential: −32.6 MeV for the 1s state and −16.8 MeV for the 1p state.

## Context
This work is the first systematic attempt to apply OF-DFT to nuclear systems, extending prior OF-DFT applications in atomic and molecular physics (Chen 2008, Karasiev 2012, Witt 2018) and responding to a brief mention of nuclear OF-DFT by Bulgac (2018). The study connects to the Thomas-Fermi and extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) approximations in nuclear physics (Brack 1985, Centelles 1990), which have known limitations in reproducing density tails and deformation energies. The results enable future development of orbital-free functionals for superheavy nuclei, neutron star crusts, and shape-coexisting nuclei, and suggest that machine learning or Bayesian inference (Imoto 2021, Zhao 2022) could improve the kinetic energy functional beyond the ETF level.
