# Uncertainty quantification of optical models in fission fragment de-excitation

> We take the first step towards incorporating compound nuclear observables at astrophysically relevant energies into the experimental evidence used to constrain optical models, by propagating the uncertainty in two global optical potentials, one phenomenological and one microscopic, to correlated fission observables using the Monte Carlo Hauser-Feshbach formalism. We compare to a wide range of historic and recent experimental fission measurements, and discuss in detail regions of disagreement. We find that the parametric optical model uncertainty in neutron-fragment correlated observables involving neutron energy is significant. On the other hand, we observe that other experimental features, particularly neutron-fragment correlations near the $^{132}$Sn shell-closure and the high energy component of neutron spectra, are unlikely to be explained by the optical potential, and will require further experimental and theoretical effort to explain.

## Key claim
The parametric uncertainty in global optical potentials propagates to significant uncertainty in neutron-fragment correlated observables involving neutron energy and multiplicity, but certain experimental features, particularly neutron-fragment correlations near the \(^{132}\)Sn shell-closure and the high-energy component of neutron spectra, are unlikely to be explained by the optical potential alone and will require further experimental and theoretical effort.

## Method
The authors propagated uncertainty from two global optical potentials, the phenomenological KDUQ (Koning-Delaroche Uncertainty Quantified) and the microscopic WLH (Whitehead-Lovell-Holt), through the Monte Carlo Hauser-Feshbach fission code CGMF. For each potential, 300 posterior samples were drawn; for each sample, one million fission histories were generated for \(^{252}\)Cf spontaneous fission and \(^{235}\)U thermal neutron-induced fission. The resulting distributions of fission observables were compared to historic and recent experimental data.

## Key numbers
- Mean prompt neutron multiplicity \(\bar{\nu}\) for \(^{252}\)Cf(sf): KDUQ and WLH predictions disagreed with each other to statistical significance; WLH universally predicted too large multiplicities compared to experiment.
- Mean prompt neutron multiplicity \(\bar{\nu}\) for \(^{235}\)U(n\(_{\text{th}}\),f): KDUQ generally closest to experiment; WLH predicted larger values.
- Prompt fission neutron spectra (PFNS) for both systems: both models predicted spectra too soft compared to measurements; in the 1–10 MeV region, the uncertainty bands for the two optical potentials were well separated, especially for \(^{235}\)U(n\(_{\text{th}}\),f).
- Computational cost: one million CGMF histories per parameter sample required roughly 30 cpu-hours; 300 samples required roughly 9300 cpu-hours per potential per fissioning system.

## Context
This work extends efforts to propagate optical model uncertainties into non-elastic reaction channels, following work by Pruitt (2023) on Bayesian calibration of phenomenological optical potentials and Whitehead (2021) on microscopic global potentials. It takes the first step toward incorporating compound nuclear observables from fission, which provide experimental access to unstable nuclei at astrophysically relevant energies, into the experimental evidence used to constrain optical models. The results enable future global optimization of fission model inputs, including level densities and excitation energy sharing parameters, by identifying which observables are sensitive to the optical potential and which are not.
