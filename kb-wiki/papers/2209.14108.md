# Determination of the titanium spectral function from (e,e'p) data

> The E12-14-012 experiment, performed in Jefferson Lab Hall A, has measured the (e,e'p) cross section in parallel kinematics using a natural titanium target. Here, we report the full results of the analysis of the data set corresponding to beam energy 2.2 GeV, and spanning the missing momentum and missing energy range 15 <= pm <= 250 MeV/c and 12 <= Em <= 80 MeV. The reduced cross section has been measured with ~7% accuracy as function of both missing momentum and missing energy. We compared our data to the results of a Monte Carlo simulations performed using a model spectral function and including the effects of final state interactions. The overall agreement between data and simulations is quite good (chi2/d.o.f. = 0.9).

## Key claim

The paper reports the extraction of the proton spectral function for titanium from (e,e'p) data, with the explicit caveat that the derivation depends only on the model used to compute final-state interaction (FSI) corrections, and that the overall agreement between data and Monte Carlo simulation is good (chi2/d.o.f. = 0.9) but the spectral function determination is not model-independent.

## Method

The E12-14-012 experiment at Jefferson Lab Hall A measured the (e,e'p) cross section in parallel kinematics using a natural titanium target with a 2.2 GeV electron beam. The reduced cross section was obtained as a function of missing momentum (15 to 250 MeV/c) and missing energy (12 to 80 MeV) using the elementary electron-proton off-shell cross section of de Forest. A Monte Carlo simulation using the JLab SIMC spectrometer package, including a model spectral function, radiative corrections, Coulomb effects, and FSI corrections within the distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA), was fit to the data via chi2 minimization to extract spectroscopic factors, peak positions, and widths for each shell-model state.

## Key numbers

- Reduced cross section measured with ~7% accuracy as function of missing momentum and missing energy
- Total systematic uncertainty: 4.63% (kin1), 4.92% (kin2), 4.70% (kin3), 6.04% (kin4)
- Total statistical uncertainty: 0.78% (kin1), 0.60% (kin2), 0.82% (kin3), 1.24% (kin4)
- Best-fit chi2/d.o.f. = 0.49 for missing momentum fit with correlated spectral function included (675 degrees of freedom)
- Best-fit chi2/d.o.f. = 0.95 for missing energy fit with all priors (121 degrees of freedom)
- Total spectroscopic strength from missing energy fit with all priors: 20.32 +/- 0.65 (out of 22 protons)
- Correlated part strength from missing energy fit: 4.71 +/- 0.31 (accounting for 20% of total strength)
- Peak positions from missing energy fit: 1f7/2 at 11.32 +/- 0.10 MeV, 1d3/2 at 12.30 +/- 0.24 MeV, 2s1/2 at 12.77 +/- 0.25 MeV, 1d5/2 at 15.86 +/- 0.20 MeV, 1p1/2 at 33.33 +/- 0.60 MeV, 1p3/2 at 39.69 +/- 0.62 MeV, 1s1/2 at 53.84 +/- 1.86 MeV
- Correlated part threshold energy from missing energy fit: 25.20 +/- 0.02 MeV

## Context

This work extends the E12-14-012 collaboration's previous analysis of argon (Jiang 2022) to titanium, motivated by the isospin symmetry argument that the proton spectral function of titanium can serve as a proxy for the neutron spectral function of argon for neutrino interaction studies. The results support the earlier theoretical work of Barbieri (2019), who showed that replacing the neutron spectral function of argon with the proton spectral function of titanium in calculations of the Ar(nu_mu,mu-) cross section at 1 GeV has only a few-percent effect, though the authors caution this does not constitute clear-cut evidence. The extracted spectral function will enable improved modeling of neutrino-nucleus interactions for experiments like DUNE and the Fermilab short-baseline program.
