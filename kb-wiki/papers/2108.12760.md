# Inverse magneto-rotational catalysis and the phase diagram of a rotating hot and magnetized quark matter

> We study the properties of a hot and magnetized quark matter in a rotating cylinder in the presence of a constant magnetic field. To do this, we solve the corresponding Dirac equation using the Ritus eigenfunction method. This leads to the energy dispersion relation, Ritus eigenfunctions, and the quantization relation for magnetized fermions. To avoid causality-violating effects, we impose a certain global boundary condition, and study its effect, in particular, on the energy eigenmodes and the quantization relations of fermions. Using the fermion propagator arising from this method, we then solve the gap equation at zero and nonzero temperatures. At zero temperature, the dynamical mass $\bar{m}$ does not depend on the angular frequency, as expected. We thus study its dependence on the distance $r$ relative to the axis of rotation and the magnetic field $B$, and explore the corresponding finite size effect for various couplings $G$. We then consider the finite temperature case. The dependence of $\bar{m}$ on the temperature $T$, magnetic field $B$, angular frequency $Ω$, and distance $r$ for various $G$ is studied. We show that $\bar{m}$ decreases, in general, with $B$ and $Ω$. This is the ''inverse magneto-rotational catalysis (IMRC)'' or the ''rotational magnetic inhibition'', previously discussed in the literature. To explore the evidence of this effect in the phase diagrams of our model, we examine the phase portraits of the critical temperature $T_c$ as well as the critical angular frequency $Ω_c$ with respect to $G, B,Ω$, and $r$ as well as $G, B, T$, and $r$, respectively. We show that $T_{c}$ and $Ω_c$ decrease, in particular, with $B$. This is interpreted as clear evidence for IMRC.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in a hot and magnetized quark matter within a rotating cylinder, the dynamical mass decreases with both increasing magnetic field strength and angular frequency, confirming the "inverse magneto-rotational catalysis" (IMRC) effect; however, at zero temperature the dynamical mass does not depend on the angular frequency, contrary to some earlier claims in the literature.

## Method

The authors solve the Dirac equation for a one-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model in a rotating cylinder with a constant magnetic field using the Ritus eigenfunction method, imposing a global boundary condition to avoid causality-violating effects. They derive the fermion propagator from the resulting energy eigenmodes and quantization relations, then solve the gap equation numerically at both zero and nonzero temperatures to obtain the dynamical mass as a function of temperature T, magnetic field B, angular frequency Omega, distance r from the rotation axis, and coupling constant G.

## Key numbers

- The vorticity in heavy-ion collisions is estimated to be about 0.02 fm^{-1} ~ 10^{22} Hz, based on a velocity difference of 0.1 (in units of speed of light) and a transverse system size of about 5 fm.
- The normal nuclear density is given as rho_0 = 2.7 x 10^{14} gr/cm^3, with heavy-ion collisions reaching densities up to 5 rho_0.
- Electromagnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions are estimated in the range 10^{18} - 10^{20} G.
- The critical temperature T_c and critical angular frequency Omega_c both decrease with increasing magnetic field B, providing quantitative evidence for IMRC in the phase diagrams.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of inverse magnetic catalysis in dense quark matter by Fayazbakhsh et al. (2011) and of rotational suppression of chiral condensates by Fukushima et al. (2015) and Ebihara et al. (2017). It also builds on the systematic treatment of boundary conditions in rotating systems by Chernodub et al. (2016). The results enable further exploration of the QCD phase diagram under combined extreme conditions of temperature, magnetic field, and rotation relevant to heavy-ion collision experiments at RHIC and LHC.
