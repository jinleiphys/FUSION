# Azimuthal angular entanglement between decaying particles in ultra-peripheral ion collisions

> Ultra-peripheral collisions (UPCs) involving relativistic heavy ions are a unique laboratory to study quantum correlations. The intense electromagnetic fields generate high rates of photonuclear interactions, including events involving multiple photon exchange. Multiple photon exchange can result in the production of multiple vector mesons and/or nuclear excitations. These interactions share a common impact parameter, so the photons have the same linear polarization. The shared polarization entangles the particles, leading to unique quantum correlations. The decays of these vector excitations are sensitive to this polarization, allowing for the study of these correlations. This letter will compare classical and quantum calculations of the correlations between these azimuthal directions. The two approaches predict vert different angular correlations. The differences are akin to those seen with polarized photons in tests of Bells inequality. Uniquely, UPC photoproduction can produce final states containing three or more particles, all entangled with the same polarization. These more complex states exhibit additional unique phenomenology, allowing new tests of multi-particle entanglement.

# Azimuthal angular entanglement between decaying particles in ultra-peripheral ion collisions

## Key claim
Ultra-peripheral collisions (UPCs) of relativistic heavy ions can produce multiple vector mesons and/or nuclear excitations that share a common linear polarization because all exchanged photons originate from the same impact parameter, leading to quantum entanglement between the decay products. The quantum mechanical calculation predicts significantly stronger azimuthal angular correlations between decay products than a classical calculation, with differences analogous to those seen in tests of Bell's inequality, but the paper notes that the impact parameter is not directly observable and the initial wave function is azimuthally symmetric.

## Method
The paper compares classical and quantum mechanical calculations of azimuthal angular correlations between decay products of vector particles (vector mesons or giant dipole resonance excitations) produced via multiple photon exchange in UPCs. In the classical calculation, each vector particle decays following a cos²(θ) distribution relative to the impact parameter, and the two-particle angular correlation is obtained by convolution. In the quantum calculation, the wave function for the multi-particle state is written with a common polarization axis set by the impact parameter, and observation of the first decay fixes the polarization axis for subsequent particles. The formalism is extended to three or more particles, where the decay order determines the correlation strength between successive particles.

## Key numbers
- The typical photon transverse momentum from UPCs is of order ℏ/⟨|b|⟩ ≲ 10 MeV/c.
- For coherent vector meson production, the momentum transfer from scattering is roughly a few ℏ/R_A ≈ 30 MeV/c, where R_A is the nuclear radius.
- The cross-section for mutual GDR excitation at the LHC is about 550 mb.
- The classical two-particle angular correlation is P(θ₂₁) = 1 + cos(2θ₂₁)/2.
- The quantum two-particle angular correlation is P(θ₂₁) ∝ cos²(θ₂₁), giving P(π/2) = 0.
- In ALICE, charged particles from ρ→π⁺π⁻ decay are first observed in silicon detectors located about 2.3 cm from the interaction point.
- Neutrons from GDR decay are detected in Zero Degree Calorimeters ±116 m downstream from the interaction point.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of UPC interferometry, where single vector meson production exhibits destructive interference at low p_T due to the two-source nature of the collision (Klein, 1999; STAR, 2008; Brandenburg, 2025). The polarization correlations using self-analyzing decays have been studied in other systems such as e⁺e⁻→τ⁺τ⁻ (Privitera, 1991), φ→K⁰K̄⁰ (Bertlmann, 2001), e⁺e⁻→ΛΛ̄ (Tornqvist, 1980), and η_c→VV (Li, 2009; Chen, 2012), but unlike those systems, UPC-produced particles share no common point of origin. The paper enables experimental tests of multi-particle entanglement using existing data from STAR and LHC collaborations, and opens the possibility of studying wave function collapse order dependence in systems with three or more entangled particles.
