# Constraining the supersaturation density equation of state from core-collapse supernova simulations - Excluded volume extension of the baryons

> In this article the role of the supersaturation density equation of state (EOS) is explored in simulations of failed core-collapse supernova explosions. Therefore the nuclear EOS is extended via a one-parameter excluded volume description for baryons, taking into account their finite and increasing volume with increasing density in excess of saturation density. Parameters are selected such that the resulting supernova EOS represent extreme cases, with high pressure variations at supersaturation density which feature extreme stiff and soft EOS variants of the reference case, i.e. without excluded volume corrections. Unlike in the interior of neutron stars with central densities in excess of several times saturation density, central densities of core-collapse supernovae reach only slightly above saturation density. Hence, the impact of the supersaturation density EOS on the supernova dynamics as well as the neutrino signal is found to be negligible. It is mainly determined from the low- and intermediate-density domain, which is left unmodified within this generalized excluded volume approach.

## Key claim

The supersaturation density equation of state (EOS) has a negligible impact on core-collapse supernova dynamics and neutrino signal because central densities in these events reach only slightly above saturation density, unlike in neutron stars where central densities exceed several times saturation density.

## Method

The author extended the relativistic mean-field (RMF) EOS DD2 via a one-parameter excluded volume description for baryons, active only above nuclear saturation density. Two extreme parameter choices (v=+8.0 fm^3 and v=-3.0 fm^3) produced stiff and soft variants of the reference EOS. These were implemented in spherically symmetric, general relativistic neutrino radiation-hydrodynamics simulations (AGILE-BOLTZTRAN) of failed core-collapse supernovae from an 18 solar mass progenitor, tracking the early post-bounce phase.

## Key numbers

- Central density at core bounce: 2.69 x 10^14 g cm^-3 (stiff, v=+8.0 fm^3), 3.08 x 10^14 g cm^-3 (reference, v=0), 3.22 x 10^14 g cm^-3 (soft, v=-3.0 fm^3)
- Central density at 0.5 s post-bounce: 3.05 x 10^14 g cm^-3 (stiff), 3.71 x 10^14 g cm^-3 (reference), 4.17 x 10^14 g cm^-3 (soft, corresponding to 1.7 times saturation density)
- Central temperature at core bounce: 12.2 MeV (stiff), 13.0 MeV (reference), 14.0 MeV (soft)
- Incompressibility modulus: K = 541 MeV (stiff, v=+8.0 fm^3), K = 243 MeV (reference, v=0), K = 201 MeV (soft, v=-3.0 fm^3)
- Saturation density of the reference EOS: rho_0 = 0.149 fm^-3
- Symmetry energy: J = 31.67 MeV (unchanged across all variants)

## Context

This work extends prior studies of the EOS role in supernova simulations (e.g., Swesty 1994, Thompson 2003) which used the Lattimer & Swesty (1991) EOS with different incompressibility moduli (K=180/220/375 MeV) but could not isolate the high-density contribution because those EOS also differed at subsaturation density. The excluded volume approach (Typel 2015) allows the first direct identification of supersaturation density EOS effects. The results suggest that core-collapse supernovae cannot efficiently probe the supersaturation density EOS unless a first-order phase transition (e.g., to deconfined quark matter) introduces a discontinuity, which could be identified via neutrino or gravitational wave signals (Sagert 2008, Fischer 2011).
