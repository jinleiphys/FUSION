# Electromagnetic-radiation effects on alpha decay

> The effect of the electromagnetic radiation on the spontaneous charge emission from heavy atomic nuclei is estimated in a model which may be relevant for proton emission and alpha-particle decay in laser fields. Arguments are given that the electronic cloud in heavy atoms screens appreciably the electric field acting on the nucleus and the nucleus "sees" rather low fields. In these conditions, it is shown that the electromagnetic radiation brings second-order corrections in the electric field to the disintegration rate, with a slight anisotropy. These corrections give a small enhancement of the disintegration rate. The case of a static electric field is also discussed.

## Key claim
The paper argues that low-intensity electromagnetic radiation (including laser fields) produces only a small enhancement of the spontaneous alpha-decay or proton-emission rate, because the atomic electron cloud screens the external field so strongly that the nucleus experiences a much weaker field than the applied one. The enhancement is a second-order correction in the electric field, proportional to ξ², where ξ = qE₀/(mω²a) ≪ 1.

## Method
The author adopts a nuclear model with a preformed alpha particle (or proton) tunneling through a Coulomb potential barrier V(r) ≈ Zq²/r. The electromagnetic radiation is introduced adiabatically via a vector potential A ≈ A₀ cos ωt, and the Kramers-Henneberger transformation is applied to remove the time dependence from the kinetic term. The tunneling probability is computed by expanding the modified barrier limits in powers of the small parameter ξ = qE₀/(mω²a), averaging over time, and integrating over the emission angle θ. The screening of the external field by the atomic electron cloud is treated using a linearized Thomas-Fermi model, yielding a reduction factor F/E ≈ ω²/Ω² ≈ 10⁻³/Z² for the field acting on the nucleus.

## Key numbers
- For a laser frequency ω ≈ 10¹⁵ s⁻¹ (period T ≈ 10⁻¹⁵ s, wavelength λ ≈ 0.8 μm), the non-relativistic condition for protons gives a maximum field E₀ = 3×10¹³ V/cm and a corresponding laser intensity I = 10²⁴ W/cm².
- The Thomas-Fermi screening distance is R = a_H/Z^{1/3}, where a_H is the Bohr radius; the atomic eigenfrequency is ω₀ ≈ 4.5 Z × 10¹⁶ s⁻¹ (ℏω₀ ≈ 28 Z eV).
- For Z = 50, the screening reduces the field acting on the nucleus by a factor ≈ 4×10⁻⁷, from 3×10¹³ V/cm to about 10⁷ V/cm.
- The threshold field separating low-field from high-field regimes is E₀ ≈ 10⁵ V/cm (10² electrostatic units) for protons, derived from |q|E₀/(mω²a) = 1 with a ≈ 10⁻¹³ cm.
- The attempt frequency for tunneling corresponds to an energy-level spacing Δℰ ≈ 200 keV, giving t_a ≈ 3×10⁻²¹ s.
- For a static field with Z = 100 and ℰ = 1 MeV, the small parameter α = E r₂²/(Zq) = 10⁻⁴ E (with E in V/cm) is much less than unity for any usual static field.
- The maximum correction to the Geiger-Nuttall constant b₀ is of order unity, so the decay-rate enhancement factor is of order ξ² ≪ 1.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of laser-induced effects on nuclear decay, particularly those by Refs. [key-6]–[key-11] on alpha decay and Refs. [key-12, key-13] on proton emission, and contrasts with claims that electric fields either do not affect alpha decay (Ref. [key-8]) or greatly enhance it (Ref. [key-18]). The paper explicitly incorporates electron screening via the Thomas-Fermi model (Ref. [key-21]) and uses the Kramers-Henneberger transformation (Refs. [key-41]–[key-44]) to treat the time-dependent interaction. The results enable future calculations of decay-rate modifications in laser fields for heavy nuclei, and the formalism can be extended to general polarization and to intermediate-field regimes where ξ > 1.
