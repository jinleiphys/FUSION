# A Spectral Approach for Solving the Nonclassical Transport Equation

> This paper introduces a mathematical approach that allows one to numerically solve the nonclassical transport equation in a deterministic fashion using classical numerical procedures. The nonclassical transport equation describes particle transport for random statistically homogeneous systems in which the distribution function for free-paths between scattering centers is nonexponential. We use a spectral method to represent the nonclassical flux as a series of Laguerre polynomials in the free-path variable $s$, resulting in a nonclassical equation that has the form of a classical transport equation. We present numerical results that validate the spectral approach, considering transport in slab geometry for both classical and nonclassical problems in the discrete ordinates formulation.

# A Spectral Approach for Solving the Nonclassical Transport Equation

## Key claim
The paper introduces a spectral method that transforms the nonclassical transport equation, which includes the free-path variable *s* as an extra independent dimension, into a form identical to the classical transport equation, enabling solution by standard deterministic numerical procedures. The method is validated for slab geometry problems, but the authors note that its efficiency may be compromised for free-path distributions with algebraically decaying tails where high-order moments do not exist.

## Method
The authors represent the nonclassical angular flux as a series of Laguerre polynomials in the free-path variable *s*, then substitute this expansion into the initial-value form of the nonclassical transport equation. Using the orthogonality and derivative properties of Laguerre polynomials, they derive a system of equations for the expansion coefficients that has the same mathematical structure as the classical linear Boltzmann equation. The resulting equations are solved in slab geometry using the discrete ordinates (S_N) formulation with diamond difference spatial discretization and source iteration, where the improper integrals over *s* are approximated by Gauss-Legendre quadrature after truncating the integration range.

## Key numbers
- For validation of classical transport (homogeneous slab, Σ_t = 1 cm⁻¹, source Q = 1×10¹⁷ neutrons/cm³·s), the nonclassical spectral method with truncation order M = 10 reproduces the classical scalar flux to within close agreement for scattering ratios c = 0 and c = 0.9.
- For nonclassical problems in a random periodic slab (period ℓ = ℓ₁ + ℓ₂, material 1: Σ_t₁ = 1 cm⁻¹, material 2: void Σ_t₂ = 0 cm⁻¹), the best accuracy is obtained for purely absorbing systems (c = 0), where increasing M from 50 to 200 shows clear improvement in accuracy.
- For diffusive cases (c = 0.9), the nonclassical model underestimates the maximum scalar flux at x = 0, and increasing M provides virtually no improvement in accuracy.
- All numerical experiments used N = 20 discrete ordinates, G_ℓ = 300 Gauss-Legendre quadrature points, spatial cell width h_j = 0.005 cm, and stopping criterion ξ = 1×10⁻⁶.

## Context
This work extends the nonclassical transport theory originally proposed by Larsen (2007) for photon transport in cloudy atmospheres, which has since been applied to neutron transport in nuclear reactors (Vasques and Larsen, 2009, 2014), computer graphics (Deo, 2014; Jarabo), and the periodic Lorentz gas (Golse, 2012; Marklof and Strömbergsson, 2010-2015). Prior numerical results for the nonclassical transport equation existed only for rod geometry (Vasques and Krycki, 2015, 2017; Vasques and Slaybaugh, 2016); this paper provides the first deterministic numerical results for slab geometry. The method enables future work on multi-dimensional nonclassical systems, acceleration schemes, coarse-mesh methods, and convergence analysis, though the authors note that free-path distributions with algebraic tails (as in some applications by Davis and Xu, 2014; Xu and Davis, 2016) may require further investigation.
