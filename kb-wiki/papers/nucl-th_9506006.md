# Quantum-Statistical Correlations and Single Particle Distributions for Slowly Expanding Systems with Temperature Profile

> Competition among particle evaporation, temperature gradient and flow is investigated in a phenomenological manner, based on a simultaneous analysis of quantum statistical correlations and momentum distributions for a non-relativistic, spherically symmetric, three-dimensionally expanding, finite source. The parameters of the model emission function are constrained by fits to neutron and proton momentum distributions and correlation functions in intermediate energy heavy-ion collisions. The temperature gradient is related to the momentum dependence of the radius parameters of the two-particle correlation function, as well as to the momentum-dependent temperature parameter of the single particle spectrum, while a long duration of particle evaporation is found to be responsible for the low relative momentum behavior of the two-particle correlations.

# Quantum-Statistical Correlations and Single Particle Distributions for Slowly Expanding Systems with Temperature Profile

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a simultaneous description of single-particle momentum distributions and two-particle correlation functions for non-relativistic heavy-ion collisions requires including a temperature gradient, flow, and long emission times, but the model's six input parameters cannot be uniquely determined from the two measured functions unless the temperature gradient parameter \(a\) is non-zero; in the special case \(a = 0\), the same spectra and correlations can be obtained for different values of the input parameters.

## Method

The authors construct a phenomenological emission function for a non-relativistic, spherically symmetric, three-dimensionally expanding source, assuming local thermal equilibrium with a Gaussian spatial distribution, a Gaussian or gamma-distributed emission time distribution, a temperature profile decreasing with radius, and a scaling flow velocity. They derive analytic saddle-point approximations for the invariant momentum distribution and the quantum statistical correlation function, then numerically fit the model to neutron and proton momentum distributions and two-particle correlation functions from intermediate energy heavy-ion collisions, including Coulomb and strong final state interactions via the Wigner-function formalism with a Reid soft core potential.

## Key numbers

- The temperature profile is parameterized as \(T(\mathbf{r}) = T_0 / (1 + a^2 \mathbf{r}^2 / 2 t_0^2)\), where \(a\) controls the gradient and \(t_0\) is the mean freeze-out time.
- For the cooling profile, the authors use \(T(\mathbf{r},t) = T(\mathbf{r}) (\tau / t)^\alpha\) with \(\tau = 100\) fm/\(c\) and \(\alpha = 1/3\), motivated by isentropic three-dimensional expansion of an ideal gas.
- The gamma-distributed time distribution is \(H_{\Gamma}(t) = 2 t d \exp(-t^2 d) \theta(t)\), with mean emission time \(\langle t \rangle = (1/2) \sqrt{\pi / d}\) and variance \(\sigma^2(t) = (1/d)(1 - \pi/4)\).
- The effective temperature \(T_*\) is given by \(1/T_* = f/(T_0 + T_G) + (1-f)/T_0\) with \(f = b^2/(a^2 + b^2)\), where \(T_G = T_0 R_G^2 / R_T^2\) is the geometrical temperature.
- The effective radius parameter for the correlation function is \(R_*^2(\mathbf{K}) = R_T^2(\mathbf{K}) R_G^2 / (R_T^2(\mathbf{K}) + R_G^2)\), with \(R_T^2(\mathbf{p}) = t_0^2 T_0 / (a^2 E_k(\mathbf{p}) + b^2 m)\).
- The out-component radius is \(R_{out}^2(\mathbf{K}) = R_*^2(\mathbf{K}) + \mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{K}}^2 \Delta t^2\), where \(\mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{K}} = \mathbf{K}/m\).
- Example parameter values used in a figure: \(m = 938.3\) MeV/\(c^2\), \(R_G = 7.6\) fm, \(T_0 = 10.0\) MeV, \(t_0 = 45.0\) fm/\(c\), \(a = 1.0\), \(b = 0.7\), yielding an effective temperature \(T_* = 23.1\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the non-relativistic expanding source model of Helgesson et al. (1994) (Ref. [nr]) by introducing a temperature gradient and long emission times, motivated by deviations from purely exponential spectra observed in proton momentum distributions from intermediate energy heavy-ion reactions (Chitwood et al., Boal et al.). The model is structurally similar to the relativistic model of Csorgo and collaborators (1994-1996) (Refs. [3d, 3d:2]) that successfully described CERN SPS data, but addresses the qualitatively different regime of long-lived evaporative sources with lifetimes of a few 100 fm/\(c\) versus the short-lived systems at ultra-relativistic energies. The simultaneous analysis of neutron and proton spectra and correlations had not been performed prior to this work, and the model provides a framework applicable from intermediate energies up to the highest energies, with the extracted parameters (flow velocity, source radius) representing time averages when applied near the low energy limit.
