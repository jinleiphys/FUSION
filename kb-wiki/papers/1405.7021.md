# Equivalence of the effective Hamiltonian approach and the Siegert boundary condition for resonant states

> Two theoretical methods of finding resonant states in open quantum systems, namely the approach of the Siegert boundary condition and the Feshbach formalism, are reviewed and shown to be algebraically equivalent to each other for a simple model of the T-type quantum dot. It is stressed that the seemingly Hermitian Hamiltonian of an open quantum system is implicitly non-Hermitian outside the Hilbert space. The two theoretical approaches extract an explicitly non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian in a contracted space out of the seemingly Hermitian (but implicitly non-Hermitian) full Hamiltonian in the infinite-dimensional state space of an open quantum system.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Siegert boundary condition approach and the Feshbach formalism are algebraically equivalent for finding resonant states in a simple T-type quantum dot model, producing identical eigenstates and the same non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian. The key qualifier is that this equivalence is shown explicitly only for the specific tight-binding model of a T-type dot, though the author suggests generalization to more complex systems is possible.

## Method

The author solves the time-independent Schrodinger equation for a one-dimensional tight-binding model of a T-type quantum dot (a semi-infinite lead coupled to a single dot site) under two distinct theoretical frameworks. In the Siegert approach, the wavefunction is constrained to have only outgoing waves away from the scatterer, which yields a closed two-by-two eigenvalue problem for the amplitudes on the dot and the adjacent lead site. In the Feshbach formalism, the full infinite-dimensional Hilbert space is partitioned into a two-dimensional P subspace (dot and site 0) and the remaining Q subspace, and the effective Hamiltonian is constructed by integrating out the Q subspace through the Green's function of the lead Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- The dispersion relation for the lead is \(E = -2t \cos k\), where \(t\) is the nearest-neighbor hopping amplitude on the lead.
- For the case \(\varepsilon_d = 0\), the four solutions for the wave number \(k\) are given explicitly: bound states at \(k = 0 + i \log p\) and \(k = \pi + i \log p\), a resonant state at \(k = +\pi/2 - i \log p\), and an anti-resonant state at \(k = -\pi/2 - i \log p\), where \(p = \sqrt{(t_1^2 + \sqrt{4t^4 + t_1^4})/(2t^2)}\) and \(q = 1/p\) are dimensionless real numbers.
- The secular equation from the effective Hamiltonian yields a fourth-order polynomial in \(z = e^{ik}\): \(t^2 z^4 + t \varepsilon_d z^3 + t_1^2 z^2 - t \varepsilon_d z - t^2 = 0\).

## Context

This work extends the author's prior studies on resonant states and the Siegert boundary condition (Hatano 2008, Hatano 2009) and connects them to the Feshbach formalism (Feshbach 1958, Feshbach 1962) as applied to quantum dots by Sadreev (2003) and Rotter (2009). The paper clarifies that the full Hamiltonian of an open quantum system is Hermitian only within the Hilbert space spanned by bound and scattering states, but is implicitly non-Hermitian outside this space where resonant states reside; the effective Hamiltonian makes this non-Hermiticity explicit. This enables the use of either method interchangeably for computing resonance poles and wavefunctions in mesoscopic systems, molecules, and nuclear physics.
