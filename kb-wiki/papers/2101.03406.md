# Angular Momentum of Fission Fragments from Microscopic Theory

> During nuclear fission, a heavy nucleus splits into two rotating fragments. The associated angular momentum is large, yet the mechanism of its generation and its dependence on the mass of fragments remain poorly understood. In this Letter, we provide the first microscopic calculations of angular momentum distributions in fission fragments for a wide range of fragment masses. For the benchmark case of $^{239}$Pu($n_{\text{th}}$,f), we find that the angular momentum of the fragments is largely determined by the nuclear shell structure and deformation, and that the heavy fragments therefore typically carry less angular momentum than their light partners. We use the fission model $\tt{FREYA}$ to simulate the emission of neutrons and photons from the fragments. The dependence of the angular momenta on fragment mass after the emission of neutrons and statistical photons is linear for the heavy fragments and either constant or weakly linear for the light fragments, consistent with the universal sawtooth pattern suggested by recent experimental data. Finally, we observe that using microscopic angular momentum distributions modifies the number of emitted photons significantly.

# Angular Momentum of Fission Fragments from Microscopic Theory

## Key claim

The angular momentum of fission fragments in $^{239}$Pu($n_{\text{th}}$,f) is largely determined by nuclear shell structure and deformation at scission, with heavy fragments typically carrying less angular momentum than their light partners; however, the authors note that the associated uncertainty from the choice of energy density functional is likely of order $1\hbar$ and may be mass dependent, and the results are limited to states obeying the natural spin-parity rule $(-1)^J = \pi$.

## Method

The authors solved Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) equations with the SkM* Skyrme energy functional using the HFBTHO package, imposing constraints on quadrupole ($q_{20}$), octupole ($q_{30}$), and neck ($q_N$) operators to generate 1545 scission configurations in $^{240}$Pu. They adapted symmetry restoration techniques to extract angular momentum distributions for each fragment via integration over orientation angles with Wigner matrix elements, using Gaussian process interpolation to obtain results for integer nucleon numbers. The fission model FREYA was then employed to simulate neutron and photon emission from the primary fragments using the microscopic angular momentum distributions.

## Key numbers

- Average angular momentum of heavy primary fragments around the most likely fragmentation ($A_H:A_L \approx 136:104$) is in the range $5-9\hbar$
- Average angular momentum of light primary fragments is typically $10-13\hbar$
- For $^{132}_{50}$Sn at the doubly magic shell closure, $J_H \approx 2.5(0.1)\hbar$
- For $^{128}_{50}$Sn (four neutrons away from double shell closure), $J_H \approx 9.2(0.2)\hbar$
- For $^{138}_{54}$Xe (four nucleons beyond $^{132}$Sn), $J_H \approx 6.6(0.3)\hbar$
- Neutron evaporation reduces average angular momentum by less than $0.4\hbar$ in each fragment
- Photon multiplicity for $^{132}_{50}$Sn using microscopic distributions: $N_{\gamma} = 4.89$ versus default FREYA value of $6.62$
- Photon multiplicity for $^{144}_{56}$Ba using microscopic distributions: $N_{\gamma} = 12.46$ versus default FREYA value of $7.41$
- Quadrupole deformation parameters of fragments at scission: $\beta_2^F \leq 0.7$ and $|\beta_3^F| \leq 0.5$

## Context

This work provides the first microscopic calculations of angular momentum distributions across a wide range of fragment masses, extending beyond earlier DFT-based attempts that relied on restrictive approximations (Bonneau 2007, Bertsch 2019) or were limited to a single fragment pair (Bulgac 2020). The results contradict the outputs of phenomenological models such as FREYA (Randrup 2014, Vogt 2021) and CGMF (Talou 2011, Becker 2013, Stetcu 2016), which use generic moments of inertia that omit shell structure and deformation effects, leading to higher angular momentum for heavy fragments. The calculated post-emission angular momenta are consistent with the universal sawtooth pattern proposed by Wilson (2021) from measurements at the ALTO facility, though the authors find that statistical photon emission removes more than the constant $1\hbar$ shift assumed in that experimental work. The approach enables future full FREYA simulations based on microscopic distributions that can be compared with experimental photon multiplicities ranging from $N_{\gamma} = 6.88 \pm 0.35$ (Pleasonton 1973) to $N_{\gamma} = 7.23 \pm 0.3$ (Verbinski 1973).
