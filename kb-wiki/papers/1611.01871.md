# Symmetry Energy III: Isovector Skins

> Isoscalar density is a sum of neutron and proton densities and isovector is a normalized difference. Here, we report on the experimental evidence for the displacement of the isovector and isoscalar surfaces in nuclei, by $\sim$$0.9 \, \text{fm}$ from each other. We analyze data on quasielastic (QE) charge exchange (p,n) reactions, concurrently with proton and neutron elastic scattering data for the same target nuclei, following the concepts of the isoscalar and isovector potentials combined into Lane optical potential. The elastic data largely probe the geometry of the isoscalar potential and the (p,n) data largely probe a relation between the geometries of the isovector and isoscalar potentials. The targets include $^{48}$Ca, $^{90}$Zr, $^{120}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb and projectile incident energy values span the range of (10-50)$\,\text{MeV}$. In our fit to elastic and QE charge-exchange data, we allow the values of isoscalar and isovector radii, diffusivities and overall potential normalizations to float away from those in the popular Koning and Delaroche parametrization. We find that the best-fit isovector radii are consistently larger than isoscalar and the best-fit isovector surfaces are steeper. Upon identifying the displacement of the potential surfaces with the displacement of the surfaces for the densities in the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations, and by supplementing the results with those from analysing excitation energies to isobaric analog states in the past, we arrive at the slope and value of the symmetry energy at normal density of $70<L<101\, \text{MeV}$ and $33.5<a_a^V<36.4\, \text{MeV}$, respectively.

## Key claim

The authors report experimental evidence that the surfaces of the isovector and isoscalar densities in nuclei are displaced from each other by approximately 0.9 fm, with the isovector surfaces being steeper, and they use this displacement to constrain the slope and value of the symmetry energy at normal density, but only after correcting for Coulomb and shell effects in Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations and supplementing with past results from isobaric analog state excitation energies.

## Method

The authors analyzed quasielastic (p,n) charge-exchange reaction data concurrently with proton and neutron elastic scattering data for the target nuclei ⁴⁸Ca, ⁹⁰Zr, ¹²⁰Sn, and ²⁰⁸Pb, using the Lane optical potential formalism. They allowed the radii, diffusivities, and overall normalizations of the isoscalar and isovector potentials to float away from the Koning and Delaroche (KD) parametrization, fitting to 48 data sets across projectile incident energies from 10 to 50 MeV. The best-fit isovector radii were consistently larger than isoscalar radii, and the best-fit isovector surfaces were steeper; these potential-surface displacements were then identified with density-surface displacements in Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations to infer constraints on the symmetry energy.

## Key numbers

- Displacement between isovector and isoscalar surfaces in nuclei: approximately 0.9 fm.
- Best-fit isovector surfaces are sharper than isoscalar surfaces by approximately 0.1 fm in diffusivity.
- Slope of the symmetry energy at normal density: 70 < L < 101 MeV.
- Value of the symmetry energy at normal density: 33.5 < a_a^V < 36.4 MeV.
- Projectile incident energy range for the data: 10–50 MeV.
- Targets analyzed: ⁴⁸Ca, ⁹⁰Zr, ¹²⁰Sn, and ²⁰⁸Pb.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier half-infinite matter Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations (Danielewicz 2009), where a correlation between isovector skin size and the slope of the symmetry energy was observed, to finite spherical nuclei. It contrasts with earlier analyses by Carlson (1975) and Jon (1997, 2000), who adjusted isovector potential geometry on a nucleus-by-nucleus basis without self-consistency with elastic data, and with the Koning and Delaroche (2003) parametrization which assumed identical geometry for isoscalar and isovector potentials. The results enable tighter constraints on the symmetry energy and neutron matter energy as a function of density, and the authors note that only the stiffest chiral effective field theory predictions for neutron matter overlap with their credibility region.
