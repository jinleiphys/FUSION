# Event-by-event distribution of magnetic field energy over initial fluid energy density in $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}$= 200 GeV Au-Au collisions

> We estimate the event-by-event (e-by-e) distribution of the ratio ($σ$) of the magnetic field energy to the fluid energy density in the transverse plane of Au-Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}$ = 200 GeV. A Monte-Carlo (MC) Glauber model is used to calculate the $σ$ in the transverse plane for impact parameter b=0, 12 fm at time $τ_i\sim$0.5 fm. The fluid energy density is obtained by using Gaussian smoothing with two different smoothing parameter $σ_g$=0.25 , 0.5 fm. For $b=0~\rm fm$ collisions $σ$ is found to be $\ll$ 1 in the central region of the fireball and $σ\gtrsim$ 1 at the periphery. For b=12 fm collisions $σ\gtrsim$ 1. The e-by-e correlation between $σ$ and the fluid energy density ($\varepsilon$) is studied. We did not find strong correlation between $σ$ and $\varepsilon$ at the centre of the fireball, whereas they are mostly anti-correlated at the periphery of the fireball.

## Key claim

The ratio of magnetic field energy to fluid energy density (σ) is generally much less than 1 in the central region of the fireball for central collisions, but can reach σ ≳ 1 at the periphery for both central and peripheral collisions; however, the authors note that the true effect of large σ remains unclear without performing realistic magnetohydrodynamic simulations, and the results are obtained for a specific model (MC-Glauber) with free parameters that have not been fully explored.

## Method

A Monte-Carlo Glauber model was used to generate event-by-event nucleon positions for Au-Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV. Electromagnetic fields were computed from the Lienard-Wiechert potentials for all protons in the colliding nuclei, assuming Lorentz-contracted nuclei with γ ≈ 100, and the magnetic field was evolved to τ_i ≈ 0.5 fm under two scenarios: a "medium" scenario where the field was reduced to 0.1 times its initial value, and a "vacuum" scenario where it was reduced to 0.01 times. The fluid energy density was obtained by Gaussian smoothing of wounded nucleon positions with smoothing parameter σ_g = 0.5 fm (and 0.25 fm for sensitivity studies), with a normalization constant k = 6 (or k = 17) tuned to give a central energy density of ~40 GeV/fm³ for b = 0 fm.

## Key numbers

- For b = 0 fm collisions, σ ≪ 1 in the central region of the fireball and σ ≳ 1 at the periphery.
- For b = 12 fm collisions, σ ≳ 1 at both center and periphery.
- At the center of the collision zone (x = y = 0) for b = 0 fm, σ never exceeds 0.01.
- For b = 12 fm collisions, at the center (x = y = 0) most events have σ ≲ 0.01, but for a few events σ ∼ 1.
- At the periphery (x = 7, y = 0) for b = 0 fm, σ may reach ∼ 1 in some events.
- For b = 12 fm collisions at (x = 3, y = 0), a considerable number of events have σ ∼ 1.
- The initial central energy density was fixed to ∼ 40 GeV/fm³ at τ_i = 0.5 fm for b = 0 fm.
- The magnetic field energy density corresponding to a typical field of ∼10 m_π² is ∼ 5 GeV/fm³.
- The cutoff distance R_cut = 0.3 fm was used to remove singularities in the Lienard-Wiechert potentials.
- The Gaussian smoothing parameter σ_g was varied between 0.5 fm and 0.25 fm, with k adjusted to 17 for σ_g = 0.25 fm to maintain the same central energy density.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of electromagnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions by Bzdak and Skokov (2011) and Deng and Huang (2012), who calculated the intensity and energy dependence of magnetic fields, and by Voronyuk et al. (2011), who studied spatial distributions of electric and magnetic fields. The present study provides event-by-event distributions of the ratio σ, which is relevant for determining whether magnetohydrodynamic simulations are necessary for describing the space-time evolution of the quark-gluon plasma. The results suggest that while σ is small in the central region for most events, the large σ at the periphery and in peripheral collisions motivates future work with full 3+1 dimensional magnetohydrodynamic simulations and more realistic time evolution of electromagnetic fields in the pre-equilibrium phase, as described by Tuchin (2013).
