# Electromagnetic Signatures of Neutron Star Mergers in the Advanced LIGO Era

> The mergers of binaries containing neutron stars and stellar-mass black holes are the most promising sources for direct detection in gravitational waves by the interferometers Advanced LIGO and Virgo over the next few years. The concurrent detection of electromagnetic emission from these events would greatly enhance the scientific return of these discoveries. Here we review the state of the art in modeling the electromagnetic signal of neutron star binary mergers across different phases of the merger and multiple wavelengths. We focus on those observables which provide the most sensitive diagnostics of the merger physics and the contribution to the synthesis of rapid neutron capture ($r$-process) elements in the Galaxy. We also outline expected future developments on the observational and theoretical sides of this rapidly evolving field.

## Key claim
This review paper synthesizes the state of the art in modeling electromagnetic counterparts to neutron star binary mergers across multiple wavelengths and merger phases, with the caveat that the field was still rapidly evolving and many theoretical uncertainties remained unresolved as of 2015, particularly regarding the relative importance of different energy dissipation mechanisms and the role of magnetic fields.

## Method
The authors systematically review the predicted electromagnetic emission from neutron star-neutron star (NSNS) and neutron star-black hole (NSBH) mergers across three temporal phases: the inspiral phase (precursor emission from magnetospheric interaction and tidal crust shattering), the dynamical phase (numerical relativity simulations of merger dynamics, ejecta formation, and accretion disk formation), and the accretion phase (short gamma-ray burst production, relativistic jet formation, and late-time disk outflows). The review synthesizes results from both approximate-gravity simulations with realistic microphysics and full general relativistic simulations with approximate matter descriptions, comparing predictions with observational constraints from known short gamma-ray bursts and their afterglows.

## Key numbers
- aLIGO detection rate for NSNS mergers: 3-18 yr⁻¹ at design sensitivity, with orientation-averaged distance ~200 Mpc
- NSBH merger detection distance: approximately 2-3 times larger than NSNS mergers
- Dynamical ejecta masses for NSNS mergers: 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻² M⊙, with velocities 0.1-0.3c
- Dynamical ejecta masses for NSBH mergers: up to ~0.1 M⊙, with similar velocities
- Accretion disk masses: up to ~0.3 M⊙ for both NSNS and NSBH mergers
- Critical mass for prompt black hole formation in NSNS mergers: ~2.6-3.9 M⊙ depending on equation of state
- Viscous timescale for accretion torus: ~0.26 s for α=0.1, R=30 km, M_BH=3 M⊙
- Short GRB isotropic-equivalent luminosities: 10⁵⁰-10⁵² erg s⁻¹
- Short GRB jet energy range: 10⁴⁸-10⁵¹ erg (true energy after beaming correction)
- Millisecond magnetar rotational energy reservoir: ~3×10⁵² erg for P=1 ms
- Precursor emission energy from magnetospheric interaction: up to ~5×10⁴³ erg for B_d=10¹³ G at merger separation
- Tidal crust shattering energy release: ~10⁴⁶-10⁴⁷ erg up to tens of seconds before merger
- HMNS oscillation frequency range in GW spectrum: 2-4 kHz

## Context
This review extends earlier work on neutron star merger electromagnetic counterparts by Lee & Ramirez-Ruiz (2007), Berger (2014), and Rosswog (2015), while incorporating the most recent numerical relativity simulations and observational constraints from short GRB populations. The paper synthesizes results from multiple simulation groups including Hotokezaka et al. (2013), Bauswein et al. (2013), and Foucart et al. (2014), and connects merger physics to the open question of r-process nucleosynthesis sites, which had remained unresolved since the seminal work of Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler & Hoyle (1957). The review enables future coordinated electromagnetic-gravitational wave follow-up campaigns by identifying the most promising observational signatures and their characteristic timescales, particularly for the upcoming Advanced LIGO and Virgo observing runs.
