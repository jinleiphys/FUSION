# Comparison of SIA Defect Morphologies from Different Interatomic Potentials for Collision Cascades in W

> The morphology of defects formed in collision cascades is an essential aspect of the subsequent evolution of the microstructure. The morphological composition of a defect decides its stability, interaction, and migration properties. We compare the defect morphologies in the primary radiation damage caused by high energy collision cascades simulated using three different interatomic potentials in W. An automated method to identify morphologies of defects is used. While most defects form 1/2\3 dislocation loops, other specific morphologies include \1 dislocation loops, multiple loops clustered together, rings corresponding to C15 configuration and its constituent structures, and a combination of rings and dislocations. The analysis quantifies the distribution of defects among different morphologies and the size distribution of each morphology. We show that the disagreement between predictions of the different potentials regarding defect morphology is much stronger than the differences in predicted defect numbers.

## Key claim

The disagreement between three interatomic potentials regarding the morphology of self-interstitial atom (SIA) defect clusters in tungsten collision cascades is much stronger than the differences in predicted defect numbers, with each potential favoring different defect structures (ideal dislocation loops, multi-component loops, or C15-like rings) despite all producing broadly the same set of morphological types.

## Method

Molecular dynamics simulations of 100 keV and 200 keV collision cascades in tungsten were performed using the PARCAS code with three interatomic potentials: Finnis-Sinclair modified by Juslin et al. (JW), Derlet et al. with Bjorkas repulsive part (DND-BN), and Marinica et al. stiffened by Sand et al. (M-S). Defect morphology identification was carried out using Savi, a graph-based method that resolves each defect cluster into homogeneous constituent components (dumbbells/crowdions) and characterizes them by orientation, Burgers vector direction, and neighbor relationships to classify dislocation loops, C15-like rings, and mixed morphologies.

## Key numbers

- At 100 keV PKA energy, the JW potential produced 294 total point defects across 5 cascades, M-S produced 227, and DND-BN produced 2045 across 20 cascades
- At 200 keV PKA energy, JW produced 681 total point defects across 5 cascades, M-S produced 477, and DND-BN produced 2562 across 9 cascades
- For the M-S potential at 100 keV, 46 out of 227 in-cluster defects were in ring morphology (class @), compared to 8 out of 294 for JW and 43 out of 2045 for DND-BN
- For DND-BN at 200 keV, 1584 out of 2562 in-cluster defects were in multi-component dislocation loops (class ||//), compared to 202 for JW and 20 for M-S
- Less than 10% of all ring defects for each potential were C15-like 3D rings; the remainder were planar ring bases (hexagonal rings and triangle/tripod structures)
- The total number of defect clusters across all potentials and energies was 574
- The angular deviation from perfect crowdion orientation was normalized by dividing by 30 degrees (the maximum observed deviation)

## Context

This work extends earlier qualitative comparisons of defect morphologies in tungsten by Sand et al. (2013) and Setyawan et al. (2015), which reported differences in Burgers vector distributions and the presence of "3D clusters" without detailed classification. The automated graph-based method enables quantitative comparison beyond the conventional point defect count, addressing the need identified by Sand et al. (2016) for morphology-aware inputs to higher-scale models of radiation damage evolution. The results enable parameterization of multi-scale models that depend on defect stability, interaction, and mobility, and highlight the need for experimental validation through transmission electron microscopy, noting that most in-cascade clusters are below conventional TEM resolution limits as discussed by Sand et al. (2017).
