# Validity of the relativistic impulse approximation for elastic proton-nucleus scattering at energies lower than 200 MeV

> We present the first study to examine the validity of the relativistic impulse approximation (RIA) for describing elastic proton-nucleus scattering at incident laboratory kinetic energies lower than 200 MeV. For simplicity we choose a $^{208}$Pb target, which is a spin-saturated spherical nucleus for which reliable nuclear structure models exist. Microscopic scalar and vector optical potentials are generated by folding invariant scalar and vector scattering nucleon-nucleon (NN) amplitudes, based on our recently developed relativistic meson-exchange model, with Lorentz scalar and vector densities resulting from the accurately calibrated PK1 relativistic mean field model of nuclear structure. It is seen that phenomenological Pauli blocking (PB) effects and density-dependent corrections to $σ$N and $ω$N meson-nucleon coupling constants modify the RIA microscopic scalar and vector optical potentials so as to provide a consistent and quantitative description of all elastic scattering observables, namely total reaction cross sections, differential cross sections, analyzing powers and spin rotation functions. In particular, the effect of PB becomes more significant at energies lower than 200 MeV, whereas phenomenological density-dependent corrections to the NN interaction {\it also} play an increasingly important role at energies lower than 100 MeV.

# Validity of the relativistic impulse approximation for elastic proton-nucleus scattering at energies lower than 200 MeV

## Key claim
The relativistic impulse approximation (RIA) can describe elastic proton-nucleus scattering observables at energies as low as 30 MeV, but only when phenomenological Pauli blocking corrections and density-dependent modifications to the sigma-N and omega-N meson-nucleon coupling constants are included; the abstract's claim of "consistent and quantitative description" applies specifically to the combined effect of both corrections, not to the bare RIA.

## Method
Microscopic scalar and vector optical potentials were generated by folding invariant scalar and vector scattering nucleon-nucleon (NN) amplitudes from a relativistic meson-exchange model (Horowitz-Love-Franey type) with Lorentz scalar and vector densities from the PK1 relativistic mean field model for a 208Pb target. The resulting RIA optical potentials were then modified by phenomenological Pauli blocking correction factors and by renormalizing the real sigma-N and omega-N coupling constants, and the Dirac equation was solved to compute elastic scattering observables (differential cross sections, analyzing powers, spin rotation functions, and total reaction cross sections).

## Key numbers
- Incident laboratory kinetic energies studied: 30, 65, 98, 121, 152, and 200 MeV
- For 208Pb at 65 MeV, the real sigma-N coupling constant changed from -7.5701 to -11.1734 and the real omega-N coupling constant changed from 7.4511 to 12.9704 after density-dependent renormalization
- Phenomenological Pauli blocking correction factors at 65 MeV: scalar real 0.020, scalar imaginary 0.68, vector real 0.14, vector imaginary 0.85
- Phenomenological Pauli blocking correction factors at 200 MeV: scalar real 0.010, scalar imaginary 0.35, vector real 0.0605, vector imaginary 0.42
- The PK1 RMF model gave relative deviations of 0.0102 for masses of 19 spherical nuclei and 0.0178 for charge radii of 13 spherical nuclei
- The reference baryon density used for Pauli blocking was rho_0 = 0.1934 fm^{-3}

## Context
This work extends the RIA framework, previously validated for 200-400 MeV by Horowitz and Murdock (1987), down to 30-200 MeV where multiple scattering effects become important. It builds on the authors' earlier relativistic meson-exchange NN parameterization (Li, 2008) for 40-300 MeV and uses the PK1 relativistic mean field model. The study is motivated by the success of the nonrelativistic Melbourne group's g-folding model (Amos, 2000) at similar energies, and the results enable future development of fully microscopic relativistic dynamical models incorporating Pauli blocking and density-dependent corrections, analogous to the nonrelativistic approach.
