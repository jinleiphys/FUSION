# Modeling of critical experiments and its impact on integral covariance matrices and correlation coefficients

> In this manuscript we study the modeling of experimental data and its impact on the resulting integral experimental covariance and correlation matrices. By investigating a set of three low enriched and water moderated UO2 fuel rod arrays we found that modeling the same set of data with different, yet reasonable assumptions concerning the fuel rod composition and its geometric properties leads to significantly different covariance matrices or correlation coefficients. Following a Monte Carlo sampling approach, we show for nine different modeling assumptions the corresponding correlation coefficients and sensitivity profiles for each pair of the effective neutron multiplication factor keff. Within the 95% confidence interval the correlation coefficients vary from 0 to 1, depending on the modeling assumptions. Our findings show that the choice of modeling can have a huge impact on integral experimental covariance matrices. When the latter are used in a validation procedure to derive a bias, this procedure can be affected by the choice of modeling assumptions, too. The correct consideration of correlated data seems to be inevitable if the experimental data in a validation procedure is limited or one cannot rely on a sufficient number of uncorrelated data sets, e.g. from different laboratories using different setups etc.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that modeling the same set of three critical experiments with different, yet reasonable assumptions about fuel rod composition and geometry leads to correlation coefficients for the effective neutron multiplication factor that vary from 0 to 1 within the 95% confidence interval, directly affecting the covariance matrices used in validation procedures.

## Method

The authors used a Monte Carlo sampling approach with 250 samples per experiment and scenario, employing the SCALE 6.1.2 CSAS5 sequence with the KENO V.a criticality code and the continuous energy library ce_v7_endf (based on ENDF/B-VII). They defined nine different modeling scenarios (NoCor and A through H) that varied assumptions about whether fuel rods were identical or individual, whether grid hole displacements were included, and whether cladding parameters were shared or individual across experiments. The SUnCISTT code was used to steer and evaluate the calculations, and correlation coefficients were computed using Fisher's z-transformation to determine 95% confidence intervals.

## Key numbers

- Correlation coefficients between experiments LCT-39 6, 7, and 8 varied from 0 to 1 within the 95% confidence interval, depending on modeling assumptions
- The analysis required 6,750 SCALE inputs with up to 20,000 lines per input file, using 55,000 CPU-hours and 882 TByte-hours
- 250 Monte Carlo samples were generated for each experimental setup and scenario
- The experimental keff values were 1.0 with uncertainties of ±0.0012 for experiments 7 and 8, and ±0.0009 for experiment 6
- The Monte Carlo statistical uncertainty (sigma_MC) was set to 5×10^(-4) for scenarios A through D and 1×10^(-4) for scenarios E through H
- Scenarios A through D showed correlation coefficients close to 1; scenario E showed approximately 0.75 between experiments 6 and 7 and 0.7 between experiments 7 and 8; scenarios F and G showed approximately 0.6 and 0.5 respectively
- The experimental setups were 22×22 arrays with 363 fuel rods for experiment 6, 459 for experiment 7, and 448 for experiment 8

## Context

This work extends the discussion within the Expert Group on Uncertainty Analysis for Criticality Safety Assessment (UACSA) of the OECD/NEA Working Party on Nuclear Criticality Safety, specifically following parts of their benchmark proposal "Role of Integral Experiment Covariance Data for Criticality Safety Validation." The paper addresses questions raised by Ivanova, Hoefer (2015), and Sobes regarding how to treat sets of similar experimental data without knowing exact statistical dependencies. The findings show that the choice of modeling assumptions directly influences the bias and its uncertainty, and thus the resulting upper sub-critical limit, as discussed by Hoefer (2015) and Sobes. The authors note that a sufficient number of statistically independent data sets from different laboratories can circumvent the problem, but accurate consideration of correlated data is inevitable when experimental data in a validation procedure is limited.
