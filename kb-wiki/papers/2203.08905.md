# Probing confinement in a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ lattice gauge theory on a quantum computer

> Gauge theories describe the fundamental forces in the standard model of particle physics and play an important role in condensed matter physics. The constituents of gauge theories, for example charged matter and electric gauge field, are governed by local gauge constraints, which lead to key phenomena such as confinement of particles that are not fully understood. In this context, quantum simulators may address questions that are challenging for classical methods. While engineering gauge constraints is highly demanding, recent advances in quantum computing are beginning to enable digital quantum simulations of gauge theories. Here, we simulate confinement dynamics in a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ lattice gauge theory on a superconducting quantum processor. Tuning a term that couples only to the electric field produces confinement of charges, a manifestation of the tight bond that the gauge constraint generates between both. Moreover, we show how a modification of the gauge constraint from $\mathbb{Z}_2$ towards $\mathrm{U}(1)$ symmetry freezes the system dynamics. Our work illustrates the restriction that the underlying gauge constraint imposes on the dynamics of a lattice gauge theory, it showcases how gauge constraints can be modified and protected, and it promotes the study of other models governed by multi-body interactions.

# Probing confinement in a Z₂ lattice gauge theory on a quantum computer

## Key claim

Confinement of charged matter in a Z₂ lattice gauge theory can be observed on a superconducting quantum processor by tuning a background electric field term that couples only to the gauge field, producing confinement purely through the matter–gauge-field coupling. The authors also demonstrate that modifying the gauge constraint from Z₂ toward U(1) symmetry freezes the system dynamics, but these results are obtained at moderate Trotter step sizes (Δt = 0.2/J to 0.3/J) and rely on postselection for error mitigation, meaning the ideal gauge invariance is only approximately realized in the noisy hardware.

## Method

The authors implement a one-dimensional Z₂ lattice gauge theory on a superconducting Sycamore-class processor with up to 21 qubits, using a first-order Trotter–Suzuki decomposition of the Hamiltonian into matter–gauge-field three-qubit interactions (H_J), a background electric field term (H_f), and a matter rest mass term (H_m). The three-qubit gate for H_J is synthesized from 6 native √iSWAP† two-qubit gates and 3 layers of single-qubit z-rotations, compressed to a circuit depth of 8 two-qubit gate layers per Trotter step. To tune the gauge symmetry from Z₂ toward U(1), the authors add a protection term H_G = V Σ_i c_i G_i^{U(1)} composed of single-qubit operations that can be absorbed into existing rotations without increasing gate depth. Measurements are postselected on conserved total matter charge and Z₂ Gauss's law to mitigate device errors.

## Key numbers

- Up to 21 qubits used on a superconducting quantum processor of the Sycamore class
- Single Trotter step takes 256 ns, enabling experiments up to 25 Trotter steps for a total run time of up to 6.5 μs per shot
- Trotter step sizes of Δt = 0.2/J and 0.3/J used for the confinement dynamics
- Background field strengths f = 0.2J and f = 2.0J used to demonstrate deconfined versus confined regimes
- For the U(1) gauge protection experiment, a protection strength of V/J = 6 was sufficient to suppress U(1) gauge violations
- Data averaged over 50000 to 200000 shots for each configuration, and over 5–10 different qubit configurations on the chip
- For the confinement study with 16 qubits (8 matter sites plus 8 gauge fields), the average electric field ℰ(t) was measured with μ = 0.35J under periodic boundary conditions
- For the U(1) protection experiment with 12 qubits (6 matter plus 6 gauge fields), parameters were f = μ = 2.5J under periodic boundary conditions

## Context

This work extends prior quantum simulations of lattice gauge theories, which have mostly focused on continuous gauge groups such as U(1) (Martinez 2016, Bernien 2017, Mil 2020, Yang 2020, Klco 2018, Nguyen 2022) and pioneering laboratory demonstrations for discrete Z₂ gauge symmetry using building-block approaches (Schweizer 2019) and perturbative implementations (Wang 2021). The authors develop a non-perturbative, fully gauge-invariant decomposition that enables observation of confinement dynamics in few-tens-of-qubit systems and allows tuning the underlying gauge symmetry via an experimentally simple protection term. This work enables future studies of exotic out-of-equilibrium phenomena such as quantum many-body scars (Aramthottil 2022) and disorder-free localization (Disorder-Free Localization 2) at system sizes of L = 4–18 and times around Jt = 10, as well as testing advanced error-mitigation strategies on gauge-theory platforms.
