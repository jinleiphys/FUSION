# 2014 Update of the Discoveries of Nuclides

> The 2014 update of the discovery of nuclide project is presented. Only six new nuclides were observed for the first time in 2014 while the assignments of seventeen other nuclides were revised. In addition, for another fourteen nuclides the laboratories where they were discovered were reassigned.

## Key claim

The 2014 update of the nuclide discovery project reports only six new nuclides observed for the first time in refereed journals, while the assignments of seventeen other nuclides were revised and the laboratories for fourteen nuclides were reassigned; the abstract does not mention that the discovery of one of the six new nuclides ($^{164}$Ir) was not considered a new discovery by the original authors, who cited previous conference proceedings.

## Method

The author compiled and evaluated all reports of new nuclide discoveries published in refereed journals during 2014, using the established criteria from the earlier discovery-of-nuclide project (2009-2013). For each candidate nuclide, the original publication was examined for the production method (secondary beams, projectile fragmentation, or fusion evaporation), the laboratory, and the measured decay properties. Additionally, prior assignments were reevaluated based on feedback from other researchers and new results, leading to revisions of discovery credit for seventeen nuclides and reassignment of laboratories for fourteen nuclides.

## Key numbers

- Six new nuclides were observed for the first time in 2014: $^{15}$Ne, $^{77}$Co, $^{80}$Ni, $^{164}$Ir, $^{205}$Ac, and $^{266}$Lr.
- $^{15}$Ne ground state is unstable towards $^{13}$O + p + p decay by 2.522(66) MeV, corresponding to an atomic mass excess of 40.215(69) MeV.
- Half-lives measured for five new neutron-rich nuclei: $^{76}$Co (21.7$^{+6.5}_{-4.9}$ ms), $^{77}$Co (13.0$^{+7.2}_{-4.3}$ ms), $^{79}$Ni (43.0$^{+8.6}_{-7.5}$ ms), $^{80}$Ni (23.9$^{+26.0}_{-17.2}$ ms), and $^{81}$Cu (73.2$\pm$6.8 ms).
- $^{164}$Ir half-life determined as 70$\pm$10 μs from approximately 100 proton-decay events.
- $^{205}$Ac alpha decay energy 7.935(30) MeV and half-life 20$^{+97}_{-9}$ ms.
- Overall 3191 different nuclides have been discovered so far, reported by 901 different first authors in 1527 papers with 3546 different coauthors.
- The ten-year average discovery rate dropped to 29.5 nuclides/year; the rate for neutron-rich nuclides increased slightly to 22.1 nuclides/year, the largest ever.
- Total known neutron-rich nuclides (1199) remain fewer than proton-rich nuclides (1274).

## Context

This paper is the second annual update of the comprehensive nuclide discovery project originally published in a series of papers in Atomic Data and Nuclear Data Tables (2009-2013) and summarized in Nuclear Physics News (Thoennessen 2012) and Reports on Progress in Physics (Thoennessen 2013). The work extends the previous update (Thoennessen 2014) by adding 2014 data and correcting prior assignments, including the reassignment of $^{218}$At discovery to Hulubei and Cauchois (1939) rather than Karlik and Bernert (1943), and the reassignment of $^{12}$Li discovery to Hall et al. (2010) instead of Aksyutina et al. (2008). The continued revisions enable a consistent and accurate historical record of nuclide discoveries, and the author notes that further revisions are expected as the criteria are applied uniformly across all nuclides.
