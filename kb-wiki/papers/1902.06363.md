# Nuclear jets in heavy-ion collisions recall a stream of sand

> Head-on collisions between nuclei of different size at Fermi energies may give rise to extremely deformed dynamical regimes and patterns. Those latter, may suddenly turn into a stream of nuclear clusters, resembling collimated jets. Because the underlying instabilities are inadequately described by usual modelling approaches based on equilibrium approximations, this mechanism resulted rather unnoticed, even though it should be frequently registered in experiments. We employ the Boltzmann-Langevin equation to specifically address out-of-equilibrium conditions and handle dynamical fluctuations. An interesting interplay between surface and volume instabilities is discussed for the first time. Stable and rather regular patterns of streaming clusters arise from these conditions. Counterintuitively, we find that these clustered structures are not triggered by cohesive forces and they recall the granular flow of a stream of dry sand.

## Key claim

The authors claim that nuclear jets in heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies disassemble primarily through volume instabilities (spinodal decomposition) rather than through surface-tension-driven Rayleigh instabilities, with the mechanism resembling granular flow of dry sand rather than liquid breakup.

## Method

The authors employed the Boltzmann-Langevin equation solved in full three-dimensional phase space using the Boltzmann Langevin One Body (BLOB) approach, which includes nucleon-nucleon collisional correlations and Langevin-type fluctuations beyond mean-field dynamics. For comparison, they also used a collisionless stochastic mean-field approach where nucleon-nucleon collisions and their fluctuations were suppressed and replaced by coarse-grained noise. They simulated head-on collisions of asymmetric systems (36Ar+58Ni) and symmetric systems (36Ar+36Ar and 58Ni+58Ni) at bombarding energies from 40 to 95 MeV per nucleon, analyzing fragment multiplicities, size hierarchies, isotopic content, and dispersion relations extracted from cluster correlations.

## Key numbers

- The jet density drops to between 1/3 and 1/8 of nuclear saturation density (ρsat) along the longitudinal axis, as shown in the density profile for 36Ar+58Ni at 74 A MeV
- Jets arise above 40 A MeV and dominate the exit channel from 52 to 74 A MeV in BLOB calculations for 36Ar+58Ni
- The average density of potential ripples ⟨ρwell⟩ drops to around ρsat/4 during jet evolution at 74 A MeV
- Intermediate-mass fragments with A>4 form at temperatures around 3 MeV in a density region from ρsat/2 to ρsat/4
- Light clusters (d, t, 3He, α) are produced at smaller temperatures in density tails below ρsat/5
- The multiplicity continues to grow till around 280 fm/c due to light charged particle production in BLOB calculations
- The onset of instability growth is associated to the time interval from 110 to 120 fm/c

## Context

This work extends studies of deformed nuclear configurations beyond the traditional Rayleigh-instability picture used in nuclear fission models (Vandenbosch 1973, Griffin 1976, Blocki 1978) and neck fragmentation in dinuclear systems (Di Toro 2006, Baran 2012). The authors contrast their findings with the Plateau-Rayleigh surface instability commonly invoked for nuclear fission and neck breakup (Brosa 1990), and instead connect to spinodal volume instabilities previously studied in nuclear matter (Colonna 1994, Chomaz 2004, Napolitani 2017). The work enables new experimental searches for non-equilibrium features like fragment growth time in reactions with isotopic identification and particle correlations.
