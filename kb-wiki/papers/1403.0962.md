# Transport Coefficients of Bulk Viscous Pressure in the 14-moment approximation

> We compute the transport coefficients that appear in the fluid-dynamical equations for the bulk viscous pressure and shear-stress tensor using the 14-moment approximation in the limit of small, but finite, masses. In this limit, we are able to express all these coefficients in terms of known thermodynamic quantities, such as the thermodynamic pressure, energy density, and the velocity of sound. We explicitly demonstrate that the ratio of bulk viscosity to bulk relaxation time behaves very differently, as a function of temperature, than the ratio of shear viscosity to shear relaxation time. We further explicitly compute, for the first time, the transport coefficients that couple the bulk viscous pressure to the shear-stress tensor and vice versa. The coefficient that couples bulk viscous pressure to shear-stress tensor is found to be orders of magnitude larger than the bulk viscosity itself, suggesting that bulk viscous pressure production owes more to this coupling than to the expansion rate of the system.

# Transport Coefficients of Bulk Viscous Pressure in the 14-moment Approximation

## Key claim
The authors compute all transport coefficients for bulk viscous pressure and shear-stress tensor in the 14-moment approximation for a classical gas with small but finite mass (m/T << 1), finding that the coefficient coupling bulk viscous pressure to shear-stress tensor is orders of magnitude larger than bulk viscosity itself, which suggests that bulk viscous pressure production is driven more by this coupling than by the expansion rate of the system.

## Method
The authors start from the relativistic Boltzmann equation for a single-component gas of classical particles with zero chemical potential and use the 14-moment approximation (Israel-Stewart formalism) to derive closed fluid-dynamical equations for bulk viscous pressure and shear-stress tensor. They then expand all thermodynamic integrals and transport coefficients in a series of z = m/T using modified Bessel functions of the second kind, keeping terms up to leading non-vanishing order, and express the results in terms of known thermodynamic quantities such as energy density, thermodynamic pressure, and speed of sound.

## Key numbers
- Ratio of bulk viscosity to bulk relaxation time: ζ/τ_Π = 14.55 × (1/3 - c_s²)² (ε₀ + P₀) + O(z⁵)
- Ratio of coupling coefficient (bulk to shear) to bulk relaxation time: λ_Ππ/τ_Π = (8/5)(1/3 - c_s²) + O(z⁴)
- Ratio of shear viscosity to shear relaxation time: η/τ_π = (ε₀ + P₀)/5 + O(z²)
- Ratio of bulk relaxation coefficient to bulk relaxation time: δ_ΠΠ/τ_Π = 2/3 + O(z² ln z)
- Ratio of shear-shear coupling to shear relaxation time: τ_ππ/τ_π = 10/7 + O(z²)
- Ratio of shear-bulk coupling to shear relaxation time: λ_πΠ/τ_π = 6/5 + O(z² ln z)
- Relation between bulk and shear viscosity in the relaxation time approximation: ζ = 72.75 η (1/3 - c_s²)² + O(z⁵)

## Context
This work extends the formal transport coefficient calculations of Denicol, Koide, and Rischke (DKR) and Denicol, Niemi, Molnar, and Rischke (DNMR) by providing explicit approximate expressions valid for small masses, where previous results remained in formal integral form. The authors note that their proportionality factor relating ζ to η(1/3 - c_s²)² is almost five times larger than the classic result of Weinberg (1971), which was derived for a different physical system (material medium interacting with radiation in cosmology). These results enable more complete implementation of bulk viscosity in fluid-dynamical simulations of heavy ion collisions, particularly by providing simple parametric forms for the coupling terms between bulk and shear sectors that have been neglected in most previous simulations.
