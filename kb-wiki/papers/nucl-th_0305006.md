# Negative specific heat in a thermodynamic model of multifragmentation

> We consider a soluble model of multifragmentation which is similar in spirit to many models which have been used to fit intermediate energy heavy ion collision data. In this model $c_v$ is always positive but for finite nuclei $c_p$ can be negative for some temperatures and pressures. Furthermore, negative values of $c_p$ can be obtained in canonical treatment. One does not need to use the microcanonical ensemble. Negative values for $c_p$ can persist for systems as large as 200 paticles but this depends upon parameters used in the model calculation. As expected, negative specific heats are absent in the thermodynamic limit.

# Negative specific heat in a thermodynamic model of multifragmentation

## Key claim

In a soluble canonical thermodynamic model of multifragmentation, the heat capacity at constant volume ($c_v$) is always positive, but the heat capacity at constant pressure ($c_p$) can become negative for finite nuclei at certain temperatures and pressures. This negative $c_p$ appears in the canonical ensemble without requiring a microcanonical treatment, and can persist for systems as large as 200 particles depending on the model parameters, but disappears in the thermodynamic limit.

## Method

The authors use a canonical thermodynamic model where populations of different fragmentation channels are determined solely by phase space considerations, similar to the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) of Bondorf et al. (1995) and the Microcanonical Metropolis Monte Carlo (MMMC) model of Gross (1997). The partition function for A identical nucleons is computed via a recursion relation that allows rapid evaluation for systems up to A=3000. The internal partition function of composites includes a volume energy term ($W_0$=16 MeV), a temperature-dependent surface tension, and a Fermi-gas excitation term. The model is extended to the thermodynamic limit using the grand canonical ensemble, and calculations are performed for both one-species and two-species (proton-neutron) systems.

## Key numbers

- Negative $c_p$ values are obtained for systems as large as A=200 particles, but this depends on model parameters such as the maximum allowed cluster size.
- The volume energy parameter used is $W_0$ = 16 MeV, the surface tension parameter is $\sigma_0$ = 18 MeV, and the critical temperature for the surface tension is $T_c$ = 18 MeV.
- The Fermi-gas level density parameter $\epsilon_0$ is taken as 16 MeV.
- For a system of A=200 with maximum cluster size N=200, negative $c_p$ appears in regions where the isothermal compressibility is negative, corresponding to pressures around $p = 0.017$ MeV fm$^{-3}$.
- When the maximum cluster size is reduced to N=100 for a 200-particle system, the mechanical instability region (and thus negative $c_p$) disappears completely.
- In the grand canonical limit with N=2000, the coexistence region of a first-order phase transition becomes clearly visible, and no mechanical instability remains.
- For two-component systems with proton fraction y between 0.3 and 0.5 and densities $\rho/\rho_0$ up to 0.5, no chemical instability (negative derivative of chemical potential with respect to proton fraction) is observed.

## Context

This work extends the canonical thermodynamic model previously developed by Das Gupta and Mekjian (1998) and Bhattacharyya, Das Gupta, and Mekjian (1999), which has been used to fit intermediate energy heavy ion collision data. The finding that negative $c_p$ arises from mechanical instability regions in the equation of state is similar to results obtained by Chomaz, Duflot, and Gulminelli (2000) in the Lattice Gas Model, where $c_p$ can be negative while $c_v$ remains positive. The results are also similar to those of Moretto, Elliott, Phair, and Wozniak (2002), although the models differ. The work contrasts with the ten-dimensional Potts Model studied by Gross (1997), where negative specific heat appears in $c_v$ and only in the microcanonical ensemble. The study enables future investigations of how finite-size effects and parameter choices influence thermodynamic stability in nuclear multifragmentation models, though the authors note that direct experimental verification is difficult because nuclear disassembly in heavy ion collisions cannot be controlled to occur at constant volume or constant pressure.
