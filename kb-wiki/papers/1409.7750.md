# Electron-neutron scattering and transport properties of neutron stars

> We show that electrons can couple to the neutron excitations in neutron stars and find that this can limit their contribution to the transport properties of dense matter, especially the shear viscosity. The coupling between electrons and neutrons is induced by protons in the core, and by ions in the crust. We calculate the effective electron-neutron interaction for the kinematics of relevance to the scattering of degenerate electrons at high density. We use this interaction to calculate the electron thermal conductivity, electrical conductivity, and shear viscosity in the neutron star inner crust, and in the core where we consider both normal and superfluid phases of neutron-rich matter. In some cases, particularly when protons are superconducting and neutrons are in their normal phase, we find that electron-neutron scattering can be more important than the other scattering mechanisms considered previously.

# Electron-neutron scattering and transport properties of neutron stars

## Key claim
Electrons can couple to neutron excitations in neutron stars through an interaction *induced* by protons in the core and ions in the crust, and this scattering mechanism can limit electron contributions to transport properties, particularly shear viscosity, but only under specific conditions, especially when protons are superconducting and neutrons are in their normal phase.

## Method
The authors derived the effective electron-neutron interaction from the in-medium photon (plasmon) exchange diagram, where the plasmon couples to protons (or ions) which in turn couple to neutrons via the short-range nuclear potential. They calculated the static proton susceptibility and the neutron-proton interaction from two different equations of state (Akmal 1998 and Gandolfi 2009) for the core, and from the crust equation of state of Kobyakov (2013) for the inner crust. Using the Boltzmann equation with variational solutions, they computed the electron thermal conductivity, electrical conductivity, and shear viscosity from the induced electron-neutron scattering, considering both normal and superfluid neutron phases.

## Key numbers
- The nuclear saturation density is n₀ = 0.16 nucleons fm⁻³, and the baryon density in the core is expected in the range n = n₀/2 to 4 n₀.
- The Thomas-Fermi screening momentum is q_TF² = 4πα Σ_i ∂n_i/∂μ_i, approximately (4α/π)(m_p k_Fp + k_Fe² + k_Fμ √(m_μ² + k_Fμ²)).
- The electron Fermi momentum k_Fe ≈ 100 MeV for typical densities in the inner crust and core, much larger than the electron mass m_e.
- The neutron critical temperature for superfluidity in the crust is T_c^n ≈ 10⁸–10¹⁰ K; in the core, p-wave pairing gives T_c^n ≈ 10⁸ K.
- The proton superconducting critical temperature is T_c^p ≈ 10⁸–10¹⁰ K.
- The momentum-averaged effective coupling ⟨C_en²⟩_κ and ⟨C_en²⟩_η in the core are plotted as functions of density for two equation-of-state models (model A from Akmal 1998 and model B from Gandolfi 2009), with bands arising from varying the second-order momentum expansion coefficient V_np⁽²⁾ over the range 5×10⁻⁶ to 5×10⁻⁵ MeV⁻².
- In the crust, the ion static susceptibility χ_I(q) for q < π/a is χ_I = -n_I/(M_I v_l²), and for q ≫ π/a it scales as χ_I ∝ (q/q_D)² with the Debye wavenumber q_D = (6π² n_I)^(1/3).
- The inverse transport mean free paths scale as T², with explicit analytic forms given in terms of the functions Φ_κ, Φ_σ, and Φ_η of the ratio q_TF/k_Fe.

## Context
This work extends the long line of neutron star transport calculations by Baym, Pethick, and Pines (1969), Flowers and Itoh (1976), and Yakovlev and collaborators (1980, 2007, 2008), who studied electrical and thermal conductivity and shear viscosity but overlooked the induced electron-neutron coupling. The induced coupling mechanism is analogous to the plasmon-neutrino coupling described by Braaten (1993) and Altherr and Salati (1994). The results enable more accurate modeling of thermal evolution, magnetic field decay, and spin evolution of neutron stars, particularly in regimes where proton superconductivity suppresses electron-proton scattering, making the newly identified electron-neutron channel dominant.
