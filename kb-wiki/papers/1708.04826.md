# Tailoring laser-generated plasmas for efficient nuclear excitation by electron capture

> The optimal parameters for nuclear excitation by electron capture in plasma environments generated by the interaction of ultra-strong optical lasers with solid matter are investigated theoretically. As a case study we consider a 4.85 keV nuclear transition starting from the long-lived $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo isomer that can lead to the release of the stored 2.4 MeV excitation energy. We find that due to the complex plasma dynamics, the nuclear excitation rate and the actual number of excited nuclei do not reach their maximum at the same laser parameters. The nuclear excitation achievable with a high-power optical laser is up to twelve and up to six orders of magnitude larger than the values predicted for direct resonant and secondary plasma-mediated excitation at the x-ray free electron laser, respectively. Our results show that the experimental observation of the nuclear excitation of $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo and the subsequent release of stored energy should be possible at laser facilities available today.

# Tailoring laser-generated plasmas for efficient nuclear excitation by electron capture

## Key claim

Nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) in optical-laser-generated plasmas can achieve up to twelve orders of magnitude more nuclear excitations than direct resonant XFEL excitation, and up to six orders of magnitude more than secondary plasma-mediated XFEL excitation, but the laser parameters that maximize the NEEC rate are not the same as those that maximize the actual number of excited nuclei due to the complex plasma dynamics.

## Method

The authors model NEEC in plasmas generated by ultra-strong optical lasers interacting with solid targets containing a fraction of $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo isomers. They compute the NEEC rate as a convolution of the NEEC cross section with the free-electron flux, summed over charge states and capture channels, using the radiative-collisional code FLYCHK for charge state distributions and relativistic electronic wave functions. For low-density (underdense) plasmas, they apply scaling laws relating laser intensity to electron temperature and density; for high-density (solid-density) plasmas, they perform one-dimensional particle-in-cell (PIC) simulations with the EPOCH code to obtain electron temperature and density profiles as functions of target depth and time.

## Key numbers

* The 4.85 keV nuclear transition starting from the $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo isomer can release 2.4 MeV of stored excitation energy.
* For low-density plasmas, the optimal laser intensity for the NEEC rate is $I_{\mathrm{opt}} = 1.3 \times 10^{16}$ W/cm$^2$, yielding an electron temperature of 5.1 keV and density of $5.9 \times 10^{19}$ cm$^{-3}$.
* The optimal laser intensity for the total number of excited nuclei $N_{\mathrm{exc}}$ per pulse is $3.5 \times 10^{15}$ W/cm$^2$, independent of pulse duration in the range considered, producing an electron temperature of 1.4 keV and density of $6.6 \times 10^{19}$ cm$^{-3}$.
* For the PETAL laser (3500 J, 5000 fs pulse), the maximal $N_{\mathrm{exc}}$ reaches 1.9 excitations per pulse at the optimal intensity.
* For the high-density PIC simulation (100 J, 500 fs, $10^{18}$ W/cm$^2$), the integrated NEEC excitation number is 1.8 isomers per pulse.
* NEEC dominates over photoexcitation for $T_{\mathrm{e}} < 1.6$ keV at $n_{\mathrm{e}} = 10^{21}$ cm$^{-3}$ and up to 5 keV at $n_{\mathrm{e}} = 10^{22}$ cm$^{-3}$.
* The isomer fraction in the solid target is $f_{\mathrm{iso}} \approx 10^{-5}$, generated via the $^{93}_{41}$Nb(p,n)$^{93\mathrm{m}}_{\phantom{m} 42}$Mo reaction.

## Context

This work extends prior theoretical studies of NEEC in XFEL-generated cold plasmas by Gunst et al. (2014, 2015), where NEEC was found to exceed direct nuclear photoexcitation by six orders of magnitude for the same $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo system. The authors show that optical-laser-generated plasmas can provide a further six orders of magnitude increase over the XFEL-plasma case, bringing the number of depleted isomers per pulse to values that should be detectable at existing high-power laser facilities such as ELI-beamlines, PETAL, LULI, and VULCAN. The results enable experimental observation of NEEC-driven isomer depletion and represent a step toward controlled energy storage and release via nuclear isomers, though the authors note that only a $10^{-10}$ fraction of isomers in the plasma volume are depleted and practical energy storage applications remain far off.
