# Light-Front View of The Axial Anomaly

> Motivated by an apparent puzzle of the light-front vacua incompatible with the axial anomaly, we have considered the two-dimensional massless Schwinger model for an arbitrary interpolating angle of the quantization surface. By examining spectral deformation of the Dirac sea under an external electric field semiclassically, we have found that the axial anomaly is quantization angle independent. This indicates an intricate nontrivial vacuum structure present even in the light-front limit.

## Key claim

The axial anomaly in the two-dimensional massless Schwinger model is independent of the quantization angle, meaning it is correctly reproduced even in the light-front limit where half the fermion degrees of freedom naively decouple, provided one uses an angle-dependent regularization that preserves gauge invariance.

## Method

The authors study the massless Schwinger model quantized on an interpolating coordinate system that continuously connects equal-time quantization (θ = π) and light-front quantization (θ = π/2). They compute the axial anomaly using two independent methods: Schwinger's point-splitting regularization of the gauge currents, and a semiclassical spectral flow analysis of the Dirac vacuum under an external electric field, where the vacuum chiral charges are regularized with an angle-dependent exponential cutoff.

## Key numbers

- Axial charge production rate: d⟨Q₅⟩/dx⁺ = (2eL/π)E, independent of the interpolating angle θ.
- Dynamically generated photon mass: e/√π, arising from the Dirac vacuum deformation under the gauge field.
- The regularization parameter ε must be taken to zero before the light-front limit c → 0 is taken, to correctly regularize both left- and right-chiral charges.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of nontrivial vacuum structure in light-front quantization (Maskawa and Yamawaki, 1976; Lenz, 1990; Heinzl, Krusche and Werner, 1991) by addressing the specific puzzle of the axial anomaly. It resolves an apparent incompatibility between the light-front vacuum and the infrared interpretation of the anomaly via spectral flow (Callan, Dashen and Gross, 1978; Nielsen and Ninomiya, 1983), showing that the Dirac vacuum remains nontrivial at the light-front limit. The result enables further investigation of whether similar semiclassical interpretations can be made in four-dimensional QCD and provides insight into parton interpretations of spin-dependent deep inelastic scattering.
