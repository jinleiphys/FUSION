# An exact framework for uncertainty quantification in Monte Carlo simulation

> In the context of Monte Carlo (MC) simulation of particle transport Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) addresses the issue of predicting non statistical errors affecting the physical results, i.e. errors deriving mainly from uncertainties in the physics data and/or in the model they embed. In the case of a single uncertainty a simple analytical relation exists among its the Probability Density Function (PDF) and the corresponding PDF for the output of the simulation: this allows a complete statistical analysis of the results of the simulation. We examine the extension of this result to the multi-variate case, when more than one of the physical input parameters are affected by uncertainties: a typical scenario is the prediction of the dependence of the simulation on input cross section tabulations.

## Key claim
The paper presents an exact mathematical framework for uncertainty quantification (UQ) in Monte Carlo simulation of particle transport, but its practical applicability relies on three key assumptions: that input parameters and their probability distributions do not depend on the dynamical evolution of the system, that different input uncertainties are statistically independent, and that the response of the observable is linear in each parameter over its range of variability.

## Method
The authors derive an exact expression (Equation 4) for the probability density function (PDF) of a physical observable Y when input parameters have known PDFs, using the Central Limit Theorem in the limit of infinite events. For practical application, they assume independent input parameters and linear response, requiring only N+1 simulations to estimate the mean response and partial derivatives via finite differences. They then develop an exact analytical convolution formula (Equation 8) for weighted sums of independent stochastic variables with arbitrary polynomial distributions on bounded intervals, enabling approximation of arbitrary input PDFs to predetermined accuracy.

## Key numbers
- The linear approximation requires N+1 simulations: one at the nominal parameter values and one for each of the N parameters shifted by Delta.
- The statistical error in each simulation is of order sigma_y0 / sqrt(N_E), where N_E is the number of events.
- The condition for accurate derivative estimation is sigma_y0 / sqrt(N_E) much less than |y_MC(bar_x_j + Delta) - y_MC(bar_x_j)|.
- For polynomial approximations of input PDFs, the maximum error in the output distribution g(y) is bounded by epsilon * sum_j |a_j|, where epsilon is the maximum absolute difference between each input PDF and its polynomial approximation.
- The stable distribution family has four parameters (mu, c, alpha, beta) with alpha in (0,2] and beta in [-1,1]; special cases include normal (alpha=2), Cauchy (alpha=1, beta=0), and Levy (alpha=1/2, beta=1) distributions.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous results (Saracco and Pia, 2012, 2013) on single-uncertainty UQ to the multi-variate case, providing a mathematically rigorous alternative to brute-force statistical sampling that would require thousands of Monte Carlo runs. The framework generalizes the Irwin-Hall distribution (Irwin, 1927; Hall, 1927) and relies on stable distribution theory from Levy (1925) and Zolotarev (1980). The approach enables systematic UQ for particle transport simulations using cross section tabulations, with planned release of dedicated software to automate the convolution calculations.
