# Proton-neutron entanglement in the nuclear shell model

> We compute the proton-neutron entanglement entropy in the interacting nuclear shell model for a variety of nuclides and interactions. Some results make intuitive sense, for example that the shell structure, as governed by single-particle and monopole energies, strongly affects the energetically available space and thus the entanglement entropy. We also find a surprising result: that the entanglement entropy at low excitation energy tends to decrease for nuclides when $N \neq Z$. While we provide evidence this arises from the physical nuclear force by contrasting with random two-body interactions which shows no such decrease, the exact mechanism is unclear. Nonetheless, the low entanglement suggests that in models of neutron-rich nuclides, the coupling between protons and neutrons may be less computationally demanding than one might otherwise expect.

# Proton-neutron entanglement in the nuclear shell model

## Key claim

The entanglement entropy at low excitation energy tends to decrease for nuclides when N ≠ Z, and this suppression arises from the physical nuclear force rather than from generic two-body interactions, though the exact mechanism remains unclear.

## Method

The authors computed proton-neutron entanglement entropy from configuration-interaction shell-model wave functions in the M-scheme, using a bipartite factorization of the basis into proton and neutron components. They performed calculations in the sd-shell, pf-shell, and combined sd-pf valence spaces using empirical interactions (USDB, GX1A, mu-db), schematic interactions (quadrupole-quadrupole, isovector and isoscalar pairing), and random two-body interactions from the two-body random ensemble (TBRE), with systematic removal of single-particle energies and monopole terms to isolate shell-structure effects.

## Key numbers

- Ground state entanglement entropy for N=Z sd-shell nuclides with USDB interaction ranges from approximately 0.5 to 2.5, with maximum possible entropy (ln of subspace dimension) ranging from ln(640) ≈ 6.46 for 20Ne to ln(93710) ≈ 11.45 for 28Si.
- For the sd-shell triplet comparison (20Ne, 36Ar, 28Ne), the N≠Z nuclide 28Ne shows ground state entropy approximately 1 unit lower than the N=Z cases when using USDB; an entropy difference of 1 corresponds to a difference in effective dimensionality of e ≈ 2.71.
- Inflating USDB single-particle energies by a factor of 10 (ε(1s1/2) = -3.2079 MeV, ε(0d3/2) = 2.1117 MeV, ε(0d5/2) = -3.9257 MeV) reduces ground state entanglement entropy to zero at shell closures.
- The M=0 basis dimensions for N=Z sd-shell nuclides range from 640 (20Ne, 36Ar) to 93,710 (28Si).

## Context

This work extends recent studies of entanglement entropy in nuclei that addressed single-particle and seniority-mode entanglement (PhysRevC.103.034325, 2021; PhysRevC.106.024303, 2022) and orbital entanglement revealing shell closures (Tichai et al., 2022). It contrasts with earlier analyses using configuration information entropy (Zelevinsky et al., 1995) and invariant correlation entropy (Volya, 2003), which are either basis-dependent or computationally expensive. The finding of systematically lower entanglement for N≠Z nuclides suggests that reduced-basis methods such as the density matrix renormalization group, previously applied sporadically in nuclear structure (PhysRevC.65.054319, 2002; PhysRevLett.97.110603, 2006), may be particularly effective for neutron-rich nuclei where dimensional reduction is most needed.
