# Introduction to Nuclear-Reaction Theory

> These notes summarise the lectures I gave during the summer school "International Scientific Meeting on Nuclear Physics" at La Rábida in Spain in June 2018. They offer an introduction to nuclear-reaction theory, starting with the basics in quantum scattering theory followed by the main models used to describe breakup reactions: the Continuum Discretised Coupled Channel method (CDCC),the Time-Dependent approach (TD) and the eikonal approximation. These models are illustrated on the study of the exotic structure of halo nuclei.

## Key claim

These lecture notes provide an introductory overview of nuclear-reaction theory, covering quantum scattering basics and three main breakup models (CDCC, Time-Dependent approach, and eikonal approximation), with the caveat that the selection of recent articles on halo-nuclei structure reflects the author's personal biases and research activity rather than being an exhaustive review.

## Method

The notes begin with non-relativistic scattering theory for two particles interacting through a potential, introducing the cross section and its calculation via the partial-wave expansion of the wave function and the optical model to account for non-elastic reaction channels. The three main breakup models are then presented: the Continuum Discretised Coupled Channel method (CDCC), the Time-Dependent approach (TD), and the eikonal approximation, with a comparison of their advantages, drawbacks, and ranges of validity. The final section reviews what structural information about halo nuclei can and cannot be inferred from breakup measurements, drawing from recent literature.

## Key numbers

- 1 barn = 10^{-24} cm^2 = 100 fm^2 (definition of the cross-section unit)
- The proton-neutron phase shift in the ^3S_1 partial wave varies by 180 degrees, while in H and I partial waves it varies by less than 2 degrees (illustrating decreasing partial-wave influence with increasing L)
- A resonance in the ^3S_1 partial wave for p-^{13}C elastic scattering occurs at a proton energy of about 550 keV, causing a sharp increase in the radiative capture cross section for ^{13}C(p,gamma)^{14}N

## Context

These notes extend the pedagogical tradition of quantum scattering theory as presented in the textbook by Cohen-Tannoudji, Diu and Laloë (1977) and the more detailed reaction theory by Taylor (1972). The work enables students and researchers to understand the three main breakup models (CDCC, TD, eikonal) and their application to halo-nuclei studies, while also clarifying the limitations of what structural information can be extracted from breakup measurements. More advanced developments can be found in the references by Nunes and Thompson (2009) and Bertulani and Danielewicz (2004).
