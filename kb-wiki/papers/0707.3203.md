# Geo-neutrinos and Earth's interior

> The deepest hole that has ever been dug is about 12 km deep. Geochemists analyze samples from the Earth's crust and from the top of the mantle. Seismology can reconstruct the density profile throughout all Earth, but not its composition. In this respect, our planet is mainly unexplored. Geo-neutrinos, the antineutrinos from the progenies of U, Th and K40 decays in the Earth, bring to the surface information from the whole planet, concerning its content of natural radioactive elements. Their detection can shed light on the sources of the terrestrial heat flow, on the present composition, and on the origins of the Earth. Geo-neutrinos represent a new probe of our planet, which can be exploited as a consequence of two fundamental advances that occurred in the last few years: the development of extremely low background neutrino detectors and the progress on understanding neutrino propagation. We review the status and the prospects of the field.

## Key claim

Geo-neutrinos from uranium, thorium, and potassium decays in the Earth can provide a new observational probe of our planet's interior composition and heat budget, but the technique is limited by low event rates (hundreds per year in even the largest detectors), severe background from reactor antineutrinos, the inability to detect potassium geo-neutrinos via inverse beta decay, and the lack of directional information in current detectors.

## Method

The authors review the properties of geo-neutrinos from the decay chains of U-238, Th-232, and K-40, including their energy spectra, production rates, and detection via inverse beta decay on free protons (threshold 1.806 MeV). They calculate geo-neutrino fluxes and signal rates using nuclear data from Firestone (1996) and a reference Earth model based on the Bulk Silicate Earth (BSE) composition, and they analyze the contributions of individual beta transitions in the uranium and thorium chains to the detectable signal.

## Key numbers

- The deepest hole ever dug is about 12 km deep, a "mere dent in planetary terms."
- Earth's heat flow is estimated between 30 and 45 TW.
- Radiogenic heat from the Earth's crust accounts for at least 8 TW; the canonical BSE model provides about 20 TW of radiogenic heat.
- A natural fission reactor in the Earth's core producing more than about 20 TW is already excluded by antineutrino data (Dye 2006, Fogli 2005).
- The antineutrino production rate per unit mass for U-238 is 7.46 × 10^7 kg⁻¹ s⁻¹; for Th-232 it is 1.62 × 10^7 kg⁻¹ s⁻¹.
- The radiogenic heat production rate per unit mass for U-238 is 0.95 × 10⁻⁴ W/kg; for Th-232 it is 0.27 × 10⁻⁴ W/kg.
- The average survival probability for geo-neutrinos due to oscillations is ⟨P_ee⟩ ≈ 0.6.
- The arriving flux of geo-neutrinos from uranium and thorium is approximately 2 × 10⁶ cm⁻² s⁻¹.
- The signal rate in a 1-kton detector (≈10³² free protons) is about 30 events per year for uranium and thorium combined.
- The signal from uranium is 13 events per year per 10⁶ cm⁻² s⁻¹ flux per 10³² protons; from thorium it is 4.0 events per year under the same normalization.
- The ratio of thorium to uranium signal is S(Th)/S(U) ≈ 1/4 for a chondritic mass ratio m(Th)/m(U) ≈ 4.
- Only 0.38 antineutrinos per U-238 chain and 0.15 antineutrinos per Th-232 chain are above the 1.806 MeV detection threshold.
- 98% of the uranium signal comes from two transitions: Pa-234m (39.62% of U signal) and Bi-214 (58.21% of U signal).
- 99.8% of the thorium signal comes from two transitions: Bi-212 (94.15% of Th signal) and Ac-228 (5.66% of Th signal).
- Present errors on the intensity of the second Bi-214 transition (ΔI_k = 0.006) imply a 1.5% error on the total (U+Th) signal.
- Present errors on the intensity of the Ac-228 transition (ΔI_k = 0.06) imply a 0.9% error on the total (U+Th) signal.

## Context

This review extends the work of Araki (2005) at KamLAND, which provided the first evidence of a geo-neutrino signal, and builds on earlier proposals by Lagage (1985) regarding reactor antineutrino background. The paper systematizes the calculation of geo-neutrino spectra and fluxes, enabling future experiments to test the Bulk Silicate Earth model (McDonough 2003, Palme 2003) and alternative compositions such as enstatite chondrites (Javoy 1995), as well as to determine the radiogenic contribution to Earth's heat flow. The authors identify key open questions: the distribution of radio-elements in the core, discrimination among mantle circulation models, and detection of mantle plumes remain beyond current technology.
