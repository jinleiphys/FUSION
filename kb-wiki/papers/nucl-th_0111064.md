# Selected aspects of the classical molecular dynamics of N bodies in strong interaction

> Selected results of a classical simulation of N bodies in strong interaction are presented. The static properties of such classical systems are qualitatively similar to the known properties of atomic nuclei. The simulations of collisions show that all observed reaction mechanisms in nucleus-nucleus collisions are present in this numerical simulation. The first studies of such collisions are in qualitative agreement with experimental observations. This simulation could shed a new light on energy deposit in heavy ion collisions: the excitation energy of each cluster is found lower than the energy of the less bound body in the cluster. Finally, the similarities of fragmentation pattern with those obtained with a statistical code indicate that an unambiguous link can be established between the statistical and the dynamical descriptions of reaction mechanisms.

## Key claim

A classical N-body simulation with a simple two-body potential (finite-range attractive and short-range repulsive) reproduces all observed reaction mechanisms of nucleus-nucleus collisions qualitatively, and the excitation energy stored in clusters is bounded by the energy of the least bound body in the cluster, not by the total available energy.

## Method

The Classical N-Body Dynamics (CNBD) code solves Newtonian equations of motion using an adaptive-step fourth-order Runge-Kutta algorithm, with a third-degree polynomial two-body potential. Stable "ground state" clusters are prepared via Metropolis simulated annealing. Collisions are simulated for systems of 13+13, 34+34, 50+50, 100+100, and 18+50 bodies, with center-of-mass available energies ranging from 30 to 120 Energy Simulation Units (E.S.U.), using random orientations of cluster inertia axes and a flat impact parameter distribution up to the sum of cluster radii plus the interaction range.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per body for the fused N=68 system is approximately 60 E.S.U. (energy of the less bound body) and approximately 90 E.S.U. (binding energy per body).
- For N=34 + N=34 collisions, available energies in the center of mass are 30, 60, 90, and 120 E.S.U. per body.
- CPU time for a collision of two 50-body clusters on a Compaq DS20 ranges from approximately 30 to 400 seconds, depending on impact parameter.
- Energy conservation is maintained to better than 0.001% over the simulation.
- The "incompressibility modulus" K(N) becomes nearly independent of N for N above 20-30 bodies.
- The root mean square radius of N-body clusters follows a N^(1/3) dependence.

## Context

This work extends prior classical molecular dynamics studies of nuclear fragmentation (Pandharipande 1987, Dorso 1988, Latora 1994, Bondorf 1997, Sator 2001, Plagnol and Bormio, Dorso 1999) by using a simpler polynomial potential and an adaptive time-step algorithm. It directly connects the dynamical description of heavy-ion collisions (mean-field approaches at low energy, cascade models at high energy, transport models at intermediate energy) with statistical decay models (GEMINI, SIMON, SMM, MMMC, QSM, EES) by showing that fragmentation patterns from CNBD qualitatively match those from SMM calculations. This enables a consistent description of nucleus-nucleus collisions where statistical model parameters could be derived from the dynamical simulation's interaction parameters.
