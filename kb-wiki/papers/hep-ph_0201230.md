# Nuclear shadowing in deep inelastic scattering on nuclei: leading twist versus eikonal approaches

> We use several diverse parameterizations of diffractive parton distributions, extracted in leading twist QCD analyses of the HERA diffractive deep inelastic scattering (DIS) data, to make predictions for leading twist nuclear shadowing of nuclear quark and gluon distributions in DIS on nuclei. We find that the HERA diffractive data are sufficiently precise to allow us to predict large nuclear shadowing for gluons and quarks, unambiguously. We performed detailed studies of nuclear shadowing for up and charm sea quarks and gluons within several scenarios of shadowing and diffractive slopes, as well as at central impact parameters. We compare these leading twist results with those obtained from the eikonal approach to nuclear shadowing (which is based on a very different space-time picture) and observe sharply contrasting predictions for the size and Q^2-dependence of nuclear shadowing. The most striking differences arise for the interaction of small dipoles with nuclei, in particular for the longitudinal structure function F_{L}^{A}.

## Key claim

The leading twist approach to nuclear shadowing, which uses HERA diffractive parton distributions as input, predicts large nuclear shadowing for both quarks and gluons, with the magnitude and Q²-dependence sharply contrasting with predictions from the eikonal approach; the most striking differences appear for the longitudinal structure function F_L^A, which is sensitive to the mixing of qq̅ and qq̅g fluctuations of the virtual photon.

## Method

The authors used four different parameterizations of diffractive parton distribution functions (DPDFs) extracted from HERA diffractive DIS data (fits from Alvero-Collins-Terron-Whitmore, an improved version of that fit, the Hautmann-Kunszt-Soper light-cone model, and the H1 fit) as input to the leading twist master equation of Frankfurt and Strikman (1999), which relates nuclear shadowing corrections to nuclear parton distributions to the nucleon DPDFs via the Gribov relation generalized to individual parton species. They incorporated multiple scattering via a quasi-eikonal approximation for interactions with three or more nucleons, used these as initial conditions at Q₀² = 4 GeV², and evolved the nuclear parton distributions to higher Q² using DGLAP evolution. They compared these leading twist results with predictions from the eikonal approach, which assumes diagonal interactions of hadronic fluctuations with nucleons and ignores QCD mixing of parton configurations.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear shadowing for gluons is larger than for quarks by a factor of about two or three for the lightest nuclei at the initial scale Q₀² = 4 GeV².
- The probability of leading twist diffraction in gluon-induced processes reaches values comparable to the black-body limit of 50% for x ≤ 10⁻⁴ at Q² ∼ 4 GeV².
- The transition between shadowing and antishadowing for gluon distributions occurs for 0.023 ≤ x ≤ 0.035 at Q² ≈ 4 GeV², based on analysis of NMC data on the F₂ ratio of ¹¹⁸Sn and ¹²C.
- The upper limit of integration over x_{Pomeron} was set to 0.03 for gluons and 0.1 for sea quarks.
- The ratio of real to imaginary parts of the diffractive scattering amplitude was taken as η = 0.22 for the ACWT parameterization (α_Pomeron(0) = 1.14) and η = 0.32 for the H1 parameterization (α_Pomeron(0) = 1.20).

## Context

This work extends the leading twist approach to nuclear shadowing developed by Frankfurt and Strikman (1999), which generalized Gribov's theory of nuclear shadowing in hadron-nucleus scattering to individual quark and gluon nuclear parton densities. It contrasts with the eikonal approach, which assumes diagonal interactions of hadronic fluctuations and ignores QCD mixing of parton configurations. The predictions are relevant for interpreting future electron-ion collider experiments (EIC in the USA and HERA-based nuclear DIS at DESY), as well as for nucleus-nucleus collisions at RHIC and the LHC, where accurate nuclear parton distributions at small x are needed as boundary conditions for hard processes.
