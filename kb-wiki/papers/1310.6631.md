# Bound state techniques to solve the multiparticle scattering problem

> Solution of the scattering problem turns to be very difficult task both from the formal as well as from the computational point of view. If the last two decades have witnessed decisive progress in ab initio bound state calculations, rigorous solution of the scattering problem remains limited to A$\leq$4 case. Therefore there is a rising interest to apply bound-state-like methods to handle non-relativistic scattering problems. In this article the latest theoretical developments in this field are reviewed. Five fully rigorous methods will be discussed, which address the problem of nuclear collisions in full extent (including the break-up problem) at the same time avoiding treatment of the complicate boundary conditions or integral kernel singularities. These new developments allows to use modern bound-state techniques to advance significantly rigorous solution of the scattering problem.

## Key claim

This review argues that bound-state-like methods (Lorentz Integral Transform, complex scaling, complex energy, and continuum discretization) can rigorously solve multiparticle scattering problems, including breakup, without explicitly handling complicated asymptotic boundary conditions or integral kernel singularities, but the full many-body scattering problem may still be decades away from an exact numerical solution.

## Method

The paper reviews five fully rigorous methods that convert continuum scattering problems into bound-state-like calculations: the Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) method, techniques based on continuum-discretized states, the complex scaling method, complex energy methods in both configuration and momentum space, and the momentum lattice technique. For the complex energy method in momentum space specifically, the Alt-Grassberger-Sandhas (AGS) equations for four-nucleon scattering are solved at finite complex energies \(Z = E + i\varepsilon\) where kernels are nonsingular, then results are extrapolated to the physical \(\varepsilon \to +0\) limit using continued fraction analytic continuation, with special integration weights developed to handle quasi-singularities at small \(\varepsilon\).

## Key numbers

- For \(n\)-\(^3\)H scattering at 22.1 MeV neutron energy using the INOY04 potential, phase shifts in the \(^1S_0\) partial wave are 62.63 degrees (with inelasticity 0.990) using \(\varepsilon\) values from 1.0 to 2.0 MeV, with variations across different \(\varepsilon\) sets of less than 0.1 degrees.
- For \(p\)-\(^3\)He elastic scattering at 25 MeV proton energy, results using \(\varepsilon\) values from 2.0 to 4.0 MeV are indistinguishable from those using 2.4 to 3.6 MeV, confirming stability.
- Four different \(\varepsilon\) values are sufficient to obtain physical \(\varepsilon \to +0\) results with good accuracy.
- Calculations with standard integration weights (without special weights) fail at \(\varepsilon\) values from Table 1, with errors up to 10% for phase shifts and up to 25% for inelasticity parameters.
- At large \(\varepsilon > 4\) MeV, standard and special integration methods agree, but the \(\varepsilon \to +0\) extrapolation has at least one order of magnitude larger inaccuracies.
- 10 grid points are sufficient for all angular integrations, while 30 to 40 grid points are needed for discretization of Jacobi momenta (compared to 20 to 25 points needed below three-cluster breakup threshold).

## Context

This review extends earlier work on three-nucleon scattering using Faddeev equations (Gloeckle 1996, Kievsky 2001, Deltuva 2003) and four-nucleon calculations limited to single-channel reactions below breakup threshold (Viviani 2001, Lazauskas 2004). It enables rigorous treatment of four-nucleon scattering above breakup threshold with realistic interactions, as demonstrated by recent calculations of \(n\)-\(^3\)H and \(p\)-\(^3\)He elastic scattering up to 35 MeV lab energy (Deltuva 2012, Deltuva 2013). The methods can be combined with bound-state techniques like No-core shell model (Barrett 2013) or Coupled-cluster method (Hagen 2010) to handle many-body collisions, though the full many-body scattering problem remains a long-term challenge.
