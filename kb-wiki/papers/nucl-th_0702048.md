# Sensitivity of Nuclear Transition Frequencies to Temporal Variation of the Fine Structure Constant or the Strong Interaction

> There exist in nature a few nuclear isomers with very low (eV) excitation energies, and the combination of low energy and narrow width makes them possible candidates for laser-based investigations. The best candidate is the lowest-energy excited state known in nuclear physics, the 7.6(5) eV isomer of $^{229}$Th. A recent study suggests that a measurement of the temporal variation of the excitation energy of this isomer would have 5-6 orders of magnitude enhanced sensitivity to a variation of the fine structure constant ($α\cong 1/137.036$) or of a strong interaction parameter ($m_q/Λ_{QCD}$). We reexamine the physics involved in these arguments. By invoking the Feynman-Hellmann Theorem we argue that there is no expectation of significantly enhanced sensitivity to a variation in the fine structure constant (beyond that obtained from experimental considerations such as the low energy and narrow width of the isomer). A similar argument applies to the strong interaction, but evaluating the shift due to temporal variations of the underlying parameters of the strong interaction may be beyond current nuclear structure techniques.

## Key claim

The paper argues that there is no expectation of significantly enhanced sensitivity to a variation of the fine structure constant in the 7.6 eV isomer transition of ²²⁹Th beyond what follows from the low energy and narrow width of the state, contrary to a prior claim of 5–6 orders of magnitude enhancement. A similar argument applies to the strong interaction, but evaluating the shift due to temporal variations of strong interaction parameters may be beyond current nuclear structure techniques.

## Method

The authors apply the Feynman-Hellmann Theorem to relate the time derivative of a nuclear transition frequency to the difference in expectation values of the derivative of the Hamiltonian with respect to fundamental constants. For the fine structure constant, they compute the Coulomb energy difference between the ground state and isomer of ²²⁹Th using the Nilsson model with deformation parameters from Möller and Nix, diagonalizing the 3-D Nilsson Hamiltonian for the active neutron. For the strong interaction, they consider the dependence on nucleon mass and pion mass, computing the kinetic energy difference within the same Nilsson model framework.

## Key numbers

- The isomer of ²²⁹Th has an excitation energy of 7.6 ± 0.5 eV, the lowest-energy excited state known in nuclear physics.
- The lifetime of the isomer is estimated to be roughly 5 hours.
- Using the full Nilsson Hamiltonian with Möller and Nix deformation parameters, the kinetic energy difference ⟨⟨T⟩⟩ between the ground state and isomer is −0.49 MeV.
- The asymptotic Nilsson model predicts ⟨⟨T⟩⟩ = 0, while the full Nilsson model predicts values ranging from zero to a few MeV depending on deformation parameters.
- The asymptotic Nilsson orbits for the ground state and isomer are 5/2⁺[633] and 3/2⁺[631], respectively, each making up about 45% of the wave functions.

## Context

This work directly contradicts the claim of Flambaum (2006) that the ²²⁹Th isomer transition would provide 5–6 orders of magnitude enhanced sensitivity to temporal variation of the fine structure constant or strong interaction parameters. The authors show that within the Nilsson model and its extensions (including pairing), the Coulomb energy difference vanishes because the proton structure of the two states is identical, eliminating the claimed enhancement for α variation. For the strong interaction, the kinetic energy difference is difficult to predict accurately, and evaluating shifts due to variations of quark masses or QCD parameters is likely beyond current nuclear structure techniques. The paper enables future experimental work by clarifying that the main advantages of the ²²⁹Th isomer for laser-based studies remain its low energy and narrow width, not any enhanced fundamental sensitivity.
