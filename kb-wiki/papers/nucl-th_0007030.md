# Chiral Phase Transition within Effective Models with Constituent Quarks

> We investigate the chiral phase transition at nonzero temperature $T$ and baryon-chemical potential $μ_B$ within the framework of the linear sigma model and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model. For small bare quark masses we find in both models a smooth crossover transition for nonzero $T$ and $μ_B=0$ and a first order transition for T=0 and nonzero $μ_B$. We calculate explicitly the first order phase transition line and spinodal lines in the $(T,μ_B)$ plane. As expected they all end in a critical point. We find that, in the linear sigma model, the sigma mass goes to zero at the critical point. This is in contrast to the NJL model, where the sigma mass, as defined in the random phase approximation, does not vanish. We also compute the adiabatic lines in the $(T,μ_B)$ plane. Within the models studied here, the critical point does not serve as a ``focusing'' point in the adiabatic expansion.

## Key claim

For small bare quark masses, both the linear sigma model and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model predict a smooth crossover at nonzero temperature and zero baryon-chemical potential, and a first-order transition at zero temperature and nonzero baryon-chemical potential; however, the sigma mass vanishes at the critical point in the linear sigma model but does not vanish in the NJL model when defined via the random phase approximation.

## Method

The authors computed the grand canonical potential in the mean-field approximation for both the linear sigma model coupled to quarks and the NJL model, solving the gap equations for the order parameter (sigma field or constituent quark mass) across the (T, μ_B) plane. For the linear sigma model, meson masses were obtained from the curvature of the thermodynamic potential at the global minimum; for the NJL model, meson masses were computed from the poles of the quark-antiquark scattering amplitude in the random phase approximation. The first-order phase transition line, spinodal lines, and adiabatic lines (contours of constant entropy per baryon) were then mapped numerically.

## Key numbers

* Critical point in the linear sigma model: (T_c, μ_c) ≈ (99, 207) MeV.
* Critical point in the NJL model: (T_c, μ_c) ≈ (46, 332) MeV.
* Crossover temperature at μ = 0: approximately 140–150 MeV in the sigma model, and approximately 180–190 MeV in the NJL model.
* Effective bag constant (energy difference between global minimum and local maximum in vacuum): about 60 MeV/fm³ in the sigma model, and about 100 MeV/fm³ in the NJL model.
* NJL model parameters used: G = 5.496 GeV⁻², m₀ = 5.5 MeV, Λ = 631 MeV; sigma model parameters: f_π = 93 MeV, m_π = 138 MeV, m_σ = 600 MeV, g ≈ 3.3.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of the linear sigma model (Mocsy, 1999) to include nonzero baryon-chemical potential and compares results with the NJL model, which treats Dirac-sea contributions explicitly via a momentum cutoff. The paper contradicts the expectation from Stephanov, Rajagopal, and Shuryak (1998) that the critical point would serve as a "focusing" point for adiabatic expansion lines; instead, the adiabats bend away from the critical point when hitting the first-order transition line. The results enable dynamical hydrodynamical simulations to test observable signatures of the chiral phase transition, particularly the long-wavelength enhancement of the sigma field at the critical point in the sigma model.
