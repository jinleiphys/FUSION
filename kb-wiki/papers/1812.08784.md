# Cosmological Production of Dark Nuclei

> We study the formation of Dark Matter nuclei in scenarios where DM particles are baryons of a new confining gauge force. The dark nucleosynthesis is analogous to the formation of light elements in the SM and requires as a first step the formation of dark deuterium. We compute this process from first principles, using the formalism of pion-less effective theory for nucleon-nucleon interactions. This controlled effective field theory expansion allows us to systematically compute the cross sections for generic SM representations under the assumption of shallow bound states. In the context of vector-like confinement models we find that, for nucleon masses in the TeV range, baryonic DM made of electro-weak constituents can form a significant fraction of dark deuterium and a much smaller fraction of dark tritium. Formation of dark nuclei can also lead to monochromatic photon lines in indirect detection. Models with singlets do not undergo nucleosynthesis unless a dark photon is added to the theory.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for dark matter models where DM particles are baryons of a new confining gauge force, the formation of dark nuclei (dark nucleosynthesis) can be computed from first principles using pion-less effective field theory, but only under the assumption of shallow bound states (binding energy much smaller than nucleon mass). The success of nucleosynthesis depends critically on the formation of dark deuterium, and contrary to the Standard Model, the different densities and binding energies of DM require precise knowledge of the dark deuterium production cross-section.

## Method

The authors apply pion-less effective field theory (EFT) for non-relativistic nucleon-nucleon interactions, which reproduces the effective range expansion of quantum mechanics and allows computation of bound state formation cross-sections in terms of scattering lengths and binding energies alone. They compute the cross-sections for dark deuterium formation through electric (dipole) and magnetic transitions, then solve the relevant Boltzmann equations to determine the abundance of dark deuterium and heavier nuclei for different dark matter models, considering both electro-weak charged constituents and SM singlet constituents.

## Key numbers

- For models with electro-weak constituents, the dark deuterium mass fraction is estimated as: \(X_D = 5\% \times \left(\frac{3\ \mathrm{TeV}}{M}\right)^2 \left(\frac{E_B/M_N}{0.05}\right) \left(\frac{\langle \sigma_D v\rangle}{\alpha/M_N^2}\right) \left(\frac{g_\star}{106.75}\right)^{1/2} \left(\frac{25}{z_f}\right)\)
- For models with SM singlet constituents (no dark photon), the dark deuterium mass fraction is estimated as: \(X_D \sim 10^{-13} \left(\frac{g_\star}{10}\right)^{3/2} \left(\frac{E_B/M_N}{0.01}\right)^4 \left(\frac{20}{z_f}\right)^4 \left(\frac{\langle \sigma_{3\to 2} v^2\rangle}{1/M_N^5}\right) \left(\frac{1\ \mathrm{GeV}}{M_N}\right)^3\)
- The dark matter yield is given by: \(Y_{\mathrm{DM}} = n_{\mathrm{DM}}/s = 4.3 \times 10^{-13} \times (\mathrm{TeV}/M_N)\)
- Typical nuclear binding energies are in the range: \(0.001 < E_B/M_N < 0.1\)
- For an SU(2)_L triplet nucleon, the electro-weak splitting gives \(\Delta M_N = 165\ \mathrm{MeV}\)
- The magnetic cross-section scales as: \(\sigma v_{\mathrm{rel}}|_{\mathrm{magnetic}} = K_M \frac{2\pi\alpha}{v_{\mathrm{rel}}} \times \frac{\pi\alpha}{M_N^2} \left(\frac{E_B}{M_N}\right)^{3/2}\)
- The electric cross-section scales as: \(\sigma v_{\mathrm{rel}}|_{\mathrm{electric}} = K_E \frac{2\pi\alpha^3}{v_{\mathrm{rel}}^3} \times \frac{\pi\alpha}{M_N^2} \left(\frac{M_N}{E_B}\right)^{1/2} v_{\mathrm{rel}}^2\)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of cosmological bound state formation from perturbative bound states (von Harling 2014, Mitridate 2017) to strongly coupled nuclei, providing quantitative input for studies of asymptotic fusion of large nuclei (Hardy 2014, 2015) and for models where dark baryons couple to dark photons (Cline 2014). The paper builds on the vector-like confinement framework (Kilic 2009) and the observation that bound states with baryon number 2 exist in such scenarios (McCullough 2012). The results enable quantitative predictions for indirect detection experiments such as FERMI, which could observe monochromatic photon lines from dark nuclei formation, and clarify that for SM singlet models, nucleosynthesis cannot occur without additional light degrees of freedom such as a dark photon.
