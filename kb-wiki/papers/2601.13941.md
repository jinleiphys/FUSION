# Rotational enhancement and stability of protoquark stars during thermal evolution

> We present the first systematic study of rigidly rotating protoquark stars based on isentropic equations of state (EOS) within the density-dependent quark mass (DDQM) framework. Using a quasi-static equilibrium approach, we follow the Kelvin--Helmholtz evolution from hot, lepton-rich matter to a cold, catalyzed quark star (QS). Rotation substantially enhances the maximum stable mass (by up to $\sim 40\%$), equatorial radius, and key rotational observables, with the ratio of rotational kinetic to gravitational potential energy, $T_{\rm kin}/|W|$, reaching $0.18$--$0.19$ near the Keplerian limit, indicating a heightened susceptibility to gravitational-wave--emitting instabilities. Thermal evolution introduces a clear ordering: all stellar properties peak during the lepton-rich stages and decrease monotonically as the star cools. Compared to hadronic stars, rotating proto-QSs exhibit larger radii, higher moments of inertia, and stronger quadrupolar deformation, producing a distinct signature in the mass--radius--spin plane. The EOS parameters are constrained using current astrophysical observations, including mass--radius measurements from HESS~J1731--347 and PSR~J0030+0451, the high-mass constraint from PSR~J0740+6620, and mass-radius constraints inferred from GW170817. The results demonstrate that future multimessenger observations must account for both thermal history and rotation to identify quark matter (QM) in compact stars robustly.

# Rotational enhancement and stability of protoquark stars during thermal evolution

## Key claim

Rotation can increase the maximum stable mass of protoquark stars by up to ~40% near the Keplerian limit, but this enhancement is conditional on the specific evolutionary stage: it is largest for hot, lepton-rich configurations and decreases monotonically as the star cools toward the cold, catalyzed state. The ratio of rotational kinetic to gravitational potential energy reaches 0.18–0.19 near the mass-shedding limit, indicating heightened susceptibility to gravitational-wave-emitting instabilities.

## Method

The authors constructed rigidly rotating protoquark star models using the open-source rns code, which solves Einstein's field equations for stationary, axisymmetric spacetimes. They employed isentropic equations of state within the density-dependent quark mass (DDQM) framework, using two parameter sets (C = 0.8, √D = 127.4 MeV and C = 0.65, √D = 133.2 MeV) selected from Bayesian inference constrained by astrophysical observations. A quasi-static equilibrium approach was used to follow the Kelvin–Helmholtz evolution through four stages: hot lepton-rich (s_B = 1, Y_l = 0.4), intermediate (s_B = 2, Y_l = 0.2), neutrino-transparent (s_B = 2, Y_νe = 0), and cold catalyzed (T = 0).

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass enhancement ΔM_max reaches ~40% for hot, lepton-rich configurations at the Keplerian limit (r_p/r_e = 0.5), compared to ~39% for cold stars
- T_kin/|W| reaches 0.18–0.19 near the Keplerian limit across all evolutionary stages
- For the stiffer EOS (C = 0.8, √D = 127.4 MeV) at the Keplerian limit in the cold catalyzed stage: M_max = 3.05 M_⊙, R(M_max) = 21.33 km, I = 10.47 × 10^45 g cm^2, |Q| = 1985 × 10^42 g cm^3
- For the softer EOS (C = 0.65, √D = 133.2 MeV) at the Keplerian limit in the cold catalyzed stage: M_max = 2.85 M_⊙, R(M_max) = 19.57 km, I = 8.29 × 10^45 g cm^2, |Q| = 1566 × 10^42 g cm^3
- Spin frequencies at the Keplerian limit range from ~1000 Hz to ~1150 Hz depending on EOS and evolutionary stage
- Static (non-rotating) maximum masses range from 2.05 M_⊙ (cold, softer EOS) to 2.33 M_⊙ (hot lepton-rich, stiffer EOS)

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of rotating quark stars by Gourgoulhon (1999), Gondek-Rosinska (2000), and Zhou (2017) by incorporating finite-temperature effects and isentropic equations of state for the first time in a systematic study of protoquark stars. It contrasts with hadronic protoneutron star studies by Pons (1998) and Prakash (1996), where lepton-rich phases are more compact and expand during deleptonization, whereas quark matter contracts monotonically during cooling. The results provide a framework for distinguishing quark matter from hadronic matter in future multimessenger observations, particularly through combined mass–radius–spin constraints and gravitational-wave signatures from the Chandrasekhar–Friedman–Schutz instability.
