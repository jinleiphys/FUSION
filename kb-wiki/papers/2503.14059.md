# Delayed Thermal Relaxation of Rapidly Cooling Neutron Stars: Nucleon Superfluidity and Non-nucleon Particles

> The thermal relaxation time of neutron stars, typically defined by a sudden drop in surface temperature, is usually on the order of 10 to 100 years. In this study, we investigate neutron star thermal relaxation by incorporating nucleon superfluidity and non-nucleonic particles, specifically considering hyperons as a representative case. We find that rapidly cooling neutron stars driven by neutron superfluidity and direct Urca processes demonstrate delayed thermal relaxation under specific physical conditions. The former acquires that the neutron $^3P_2$ critical temperature is small enough, whereas the latter depends on the presence of a small core that permits direct Urca processes. To explore these scenarios, we propose simple theoretical frameworks to describe these delayed thermal relaxation behaviors and discuss how an recently-established enhanced modified Urca rate influences the relaxation time. By confronting the theoretical results with the observation of Cassiopeia A, we can effectively constrain the maximum neutron $^3P_2$ critical temperature.

## Key claim

Rapidly cooling neutron stars driven by neutron superfluidity (via the Cooper breaking and formation process) or direct Urca processes can exhibit delayed thermal relaxation, but only under specific conditions: the neutron \(^3P_2\) critical temperature must be sufficiently small for the superfluidity-driven case, and the star must have a small core that permits direct Urca processes for the latter case.

## Method

The authors constructed unified equations of state within the relativistic mean field framework using DD-ME2 and NL3 effective interactions with varying symmetry energy slopes, including hyperons (specifically \(\Lambda\) hyperons) as non-nucleonic particles. They performed neutron star cooling simulations using the NSCool code, incorporating nucleon superfluidity (neutron \(^1S_0\) and \(^3P_2\) pairing, proton \(^1S_0\) pairing) and varying the neutron \(^3P_2\) critical temperature via a rescaling coefficient \(R_{^3P_2}\). The thermal relaxation time was determined from the maximum of \(|\mathrm{d}\ln T_s/\mathrm{d}\ln t|\) on the cooling curve.

## Key numbers

- The typical thermal relaxation time of neutron stars is 10 to 100 years, depending on the cooling model.
- Superfluidity can shorten the thermal relaxation time by a factor of four (Gnedin 2001).
- For the DD-ME2 equation of state with \(L_0=51.26\) MeV, the fitted effective cooling timescale \(\tau_{\mathrm{mU}}^{\mathrm{eff}}e^{-\Phi}\) is \(3.15\pm0.04\) years.
- For the DD-ME2 equation of state with \(L_0=51.26\) MeV, the fitted normalized relaxation time \(t_1\) (for \(\beta=0.8\)) is \(34.62\pm0.48\) years.
- The threshold mass for the \(\Lambda p\) direct Urca process in hyperon stars is approximately \(1.3 M_\odot\) for DD-ME2 and \(1.4 M_\odot\) for NL3, with weak dependence on the equation of state.
- The maximum neutron \(^3P_2\) critical temperature used in the superfluid model is \(10^9\) K.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutron star thermal relaxation (Lattimer 1994; Gnedin 2001) by incorporating nucleon superfluidity and non-nucleonic particles (hyperons). It builds on the minimal cooling scenario (Page 2004, 2009) and enhanced cooling scenarios involving direct Urca processes (Prakash 1992; Lattimer 1991). The authors propose simple theoretical frameworks to describe delayed thermal relaxation and show that confronting their results with observations of Cassiopeia A can constrain the maximum neutron \(^3P_2\) critical temperature. This enables future work to use thermal relaxation observations as a probe of superfluid properties and composition in neutron star cores.
