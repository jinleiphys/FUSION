# Improved radial basis function approach with the odd-even corrections

> The radial basis function (RBF) approach has been used to improve the mass predictions of nuclear models. However, systematic deviations exist between the improved masses and the experimental data for nuclei with different odd-even parities of ($Z$, $N$), i.e., the (even $Z$, even $N$), (even $Z$, odd $N$), (odd $Z$, even $N$), and (odd $Z$, odd $N$). By separately training the RBF for these four different groups, it is found that the systematic odd-even deviations can be cured in a large extend and the predictive power of nuclear mass models can thus be further improved. Moreover, this new approach can better reproduce the single-nucleon separation energies. Based on the latest version of Weizsäcker-Skyrme model WS4, the root-mean-square deviation of the improved masses with respect to known data falls to $135$ keV, approaching the chaos-related unpredictability limit ($\sim 100$ keV).

## Key claim

By separately training the radial basis function (RBF) for four groups of nuclei classified by the odd-even parity of (Z, N), the systematic odd-even deviations that persist in the standard RBF approach can be largely cured, and the predictive power of nuclear mass models can be further improved. The root-mean-square deviation of the improved masses for the WS4 model falls to 135 keV, approaching the chaos-related unpredictability limit of about 100 keV.

## Method

The authors take the mass differences between experimental data (AME2012) and theoretical predictions from nine global mass models (e.g., RMF, HFB-27, WS4, FRDM) as samples for the RBF interpolation. Instead of training a single RBF on all nuclei, they train four separate RBFs on the four groups: even-even, even-odd, odd-even, and odd-odd nuclei. The reconstructed function S(Z,N) from each group-specific RBF is added to the original model prediction to obtain the corrected mass. The leave-one-out cross-validation method is used to evaluate predictive power.

## Key numbers

* For the WS4 model improved by the RBFoe approach, the total rms deviation with respect to known masses in AME2012 is 135 keV.
* For the RMF model, the total rms deviation improves from 0.488 MeV (RBF) to 0.244 MeV (RBFoe).
* For the ETFSI-2 model, the total rms deviation improves from 0.360 MeV (RBF) to 0.179 MeV (RBFoe).
* For the WS4+RBFoe model, the rms deviation for single-neutron separation energies (S_n) is 0.189 MeV, compared to 0.300 MeV for WS4+RBF.
* For the WS4+RBFoe model, the rms deviation for single-proton separation energies (S_p) is 0.192 MeV, compared to 0.290 MeV for WS4+RBF.
* The rms deviations for all nine models improved by RBFoe are below 300 keV.

## Context

This work extends the RBF approach previously applied to nuclear mass predictions by Wang (2011), Niu (2013), and Zheng (2014), which showed systematic odd-even staggering in the mass residuals. The new RBFoe method cures this problem and improves the description of single-nucleon separation energies, which are critical for astrophysical reaction rates. The best result (WS4+RBFoe, 135 keV) approaches the chaos-related unpredictability limit of about 100 keV identified by Barea (2005). The method can be applied to any global mass model, and the authors suggest wavelet transforms as a next step to map frequency components of mass differences to specific nuclear regions.
