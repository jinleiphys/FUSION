# Event-by-Event Fluctuations in Heavy Ion Collisions and the QCD Critical Point

> The event-by-event fluctuations of suitably chosen observables in heavy ion collisions at SPS, RHIC and LHC can tell us about the thermodynamic properties of the hadronic system at freeze-out. By studying these fluctuations as a function of varying control parameters, it is possible to learn much about the phase diagram of QCD. As a timely example, we stress the methods by which present experiments at the CERN SPS can locate the second-order critical endpoint of the first-order transition between quark-gluon plasma and hadron matter. Those event-by-event signatures which are characteristic of freeze-out in the vicinity of the critical point will exhibit nonmonotonic dependence on control parameters. We focus on observables constructed from the multiplicity and transverse momenta of charged pions. We first consider how the event-by-event fluctuations of such observables are affected by Bose-Einstein correlations, by resonances which decay after freeze-out and by fluctuations in the transverse flow velocity. We compare our thermodynamic predictions for such noncritical event-by-event fluctuations with NA49 data, finding broad agreement. We then focus on effects due to thermal contact between the observed pions and a heat bath with a given (possibly singular) specific heat, and due to the direct coupling between the critical fluctuations of the sigma field and the observed pions. We also discuss the effect of the pions produced in the decay of sigma particles just above threshold after freeze-out on the inclusive pion spectrum and on multiplicity fluctuations. We estimate the size of these nonmonotonic effects which appear near the critical point, including restrictions imposed by finite size and finite time, and conclude that they should be easily observable.

## Key claim

Event-by-event fluctuations of observables like charged pion multiplicity and mean transverse momentum can serve as a probe of the QCD critical point, provided that noncritical thermodynamic fluctuations (Bose enhancement, resonance decays, flow fluctuations) are understood and subtracted; the critical signatures appear as nonmonotonic dependence on control parameters such as collision energy or centrality, rising and falling by of order 10% near the critical point.

## Method

The authors compute event-by-event variances of extensive (multiplicity) and intensive (mean transverse momentum) observables for pions at thermal freeze-out, starting from an ideal Bose gas and then adding resonance decays, transverse flow fluctuations, and thermal contact with a heat bath. They estimate the magnitude of critical fluctuations by coupling the observed pions to the sigma field near the QCD critical endpoint, including finite-size and finite-time restrictions, and compare noncritical predictions to preliminary NA49 data on PbPb collisions at 160 AGeV.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out temperature for central PbPb collisions at SPS: T_f ≈ 120 MeV, with pion chemical potential μ_π ≈ 60 MeV
- Bose enhancement factor F_B = ⟨N⟩ v_ebe^2(p_T) / v_inc^2(p_T) increases v_ebe by a factor of √F_B, typically a few percent increase
- Critical signatures rise and fall by of order 10% near the critical point
- Multiplicity fluctuations from nonthermodynamic sources (geometrical, impact parameter, spectator effects) reproduce observed magnitude to within a few percent accuracy
- For N ~ 300 pions per event, 1/N corrections to variance estimators can reach a fraction of a percent
- Total number of events C can be made very large (say 10^6), so 1/C and 1/√C are much smaller than physical effects of order a few percent

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamic fluctuation framework of Shuryak (1998) and Mrowczynski (1998) by systematically separating noncritical from critical contributions to event-by-event fluctuations, and by providing explicit estimators for finite-sample corrections. It enables experimental searches for the QCD critical point at SPS, RHIC, and LHC by identifying observables (multiplicity and mean p_T fluctuations) whose nonmonotonic behavior as a function of control parameters would signal proximity to the critical endpoint. The analysis builds on the authors' earlier work (Stephanov, Rajagopal, Shuryak, 1998) on signatures of the tricritical point, and the finite-sample estimators correct systematic biases in the NA49 analysis procedure.
