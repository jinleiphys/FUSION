# Kinetic approach to a relativistic BEC with inelastic processes

> The phenomenon of Bose-Einstein condensation is investigated in the context of the Color-Glass-Condensate description of the initial state of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. For the first time, in this paper we study the influence of particle-number changing $2 \leftrightarrow 3$ processes on the transient formation of a Bose-Einstein Condensate within an isotropic system of scalar bosons by including $2 \leftrightarrow 3$ interactions of massive bosons with constant and isotropic cross sections, following a Boltzmann equation. The one-particle distribution function is decomposed in a condensate part and a non-zero momentum part of excited modes, leading to coupled integro-differential equations for the time evolution of the condensate and phase-space distribution function, which are then solved numerically. Our simulations converge to the expected equilibrium state, and only for $σ_{23}/σ_{22} \ll 1$ we find that a Bose-Einstein condensate emerges and decays within a finite lifetime in contrast to the case where only binary scattering processes are taken into account, and the condensate is stable due to particle-number conservation. Our calculations demonstrate that Bose-Einstein Condensates in the very early stage of heavy-ion collisions are highly unlikely, if inelastic collisions are significantly participating in the dynamical gluonic evolution.

## Key claim

For the first time, this paper studies the influence of particle-number changing 2↔3 processes on the transient formation of a Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC) in an isotropic system of scalar bosons, finding that a BEC emerges and decays within a finite lifetime only when the ratio of inelastic to elastic cross sections σ₂₃/σ₂₂ ≪ 1, in contrast to the stable condensate obtained when only binary scattering is considered.

## Method

The authors solve a Boltzmann equation for an isotropic, homogeneous system of massive scalar bosons (with degeneracy factor d_g=16 for gluons) by decomposing the one-particle distribution function into a condensate part and a nonzero-momentum part of excited modes. They include both elastic 2↔2 and inelastic 2↔3 scattering processes with constant and isotropic cross sections, leading to coupled integro-differential equations that are solved numerically using a high-order adaptive Runge-Kutta method (Cash-Karp) with Simpson quadrature for lower-dimensional integrals and Vegas Monte Carlo integration for three-dimensional integrals. The initial condition is a step-function-like distribution with saturation scale Q_s=1 GeV and step height f₀, and when the effective chemical potential μ_eff approaches the boson mass m, a small seed condensate n_c(t_onset)=10⁻⁶ n_init is manually inserted.

## Key numbers

- For boson mass m=100 MeV and initial step height f₀=0.45, a transient BEC forms and decays only when σ₂₃/σ₂₂ ≲ 0.005; for σ₂₃/σ₂₂ ≳ 0.01, no condensation is observed.
- For strongly overpopulated initial conditions (f₀=2) with masses m=100, 300, and 500 MeV, the effective chemical potential touches the mass limit only for σ₂₃/σ₂₂=0.0078 for m=300 and 500 MeV, but no condensation starts.
- The elastic cross section is fixed at σ₂₂=1 mb, and the boson masses studied are m=100, 300, and 500 MeV.
- The condensate onset time for σ₂₃/σ₂₂=0.0049 with m=100 MeV and f₀=0.45 occurs at approximately t≃0.9 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends previous kinetic studies of BEC formation in the Color-Glass-Condensate (CGC) framework that considered only elastic 2↔2 processes (Blaizot 2011, Xu 2014, and others), where a stable condensate forms due to particle-number conservation. It also builds on findings by Huang (2013) that inelastic collisions speed up thermalization in the infrared regime, and by Blaizot (2016) suggesting complete hindrance of BEC formation for massless gluons. The results enable quantitative assessment of whether transient BECs can occur in the early stage of heavy-ion collisions, concluding they are highly unlikely if inelastic collisions significantly participate in the dynamical gluonic evolution.
