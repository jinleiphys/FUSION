# Bubble nuclei: single-particle versus Coulomb interaction effects

> The detailed investigation of microscopic mechanisms leading to the formation of bubble structures in the nuclei has been performed in the framework of covariant density functional theory. The main emphasis of this study is on the role of single-particle degrees of freedom and Coulomb interaction. In general, the formation of bubbles lowers the Coulomb energy. However, in nuclei this trend is counteracted by the quantum nature of the single-particle states: only specific single-particle states with specific density profiles can be occupied with increasing proton and neutron numbers. A significant role of central classically forbidden region at the bottom of the wine bottle potentials in the formation of nuclear bubbles (via primarily the reduction of the densities of the $s$ states at $r=0$) has been revealed for the first time. Their formation also depends on the availability of low-$l$ single-particle states for occupation since single-particle densities represent the basic building blocks of total densities. Nucleonic potentials disfavor the occupation of such states in hyperheavy nuclei and this contributes to the formation of bubbles in such nuclei. Additivity rule for densities has been proposed for the first time. It was shown that the differences in the densities of bubble and flat density nuclei follow this rule in the $A\approx 40$ mass region and in superheavy nuclei with comparable accuracy. This strongly suggests the same mechanism of the formation of central depression in bubble nuclei of these two mass regions. Nuclear saturation mechanisms and self-consistency effects also affect the formation of bubble structures. The detailed analysis of different aspects of bubble physics strongly suggests that the formation of bubble structures in superheavy nuclei is dominated by single-particle effects.

## Key claim

The formation of bubble structures in superheavy nuclei is dominated by single-particle effects (specifically the occupation of low-angular-momentum states and the presence of classically forbidden regions in wine bottle potentials), not by Coulomb repulsion; the authors argue that the Coulomb interaction plays a secondary role even in hyperheavy nuclei, contrary to claims in prior work such as SNR (2017).

## Method

The authors performed calculations within covariant density functional theory (CDFT) using the NL3* energy density functional, restricted to spherical symmetry and neglecting pairing correlations (which collapse in all studied nuclei except 310126). They compared pairs of nuclei with and without central density depression: the light pair 34Si/36S and the superheavy pair 292120/310126, analyzing single-particle density contributions, nucleonic potentials, and Coulomb potentials to discriminate the roles of single-particle structure versus electrostatic repulsion.

## Key numbers

* In the 292120 nucleus, the difference between the proton potential at r=0 and at the surface peak is approximately 21 MeV, of which the Coulomb contribution (V_Coul(r=0) - V_Coul(r_surf) ≈ 6.8 MeV) accounts for less than one third.
* In the 592186 hyperheavy nucleus, V_π(r=1.7 fm) - V_π(r=8.03 fm) = 13.79 MeV, while V_Coul(r=1.7 fm) - V_Coul(r=8.03 fm) = 6.55 MeV.
* In the 466156 hyperheavy nucleus, V_π(r=1.81 fm) - V_π(r=6.84 fm) = 11.22 MeV, while V_Coul(r=1.81 fm) - V_Coul(r=6.84 fm) = 5.37 MeV.
* The rms proton radius of 34Si is 3.046 fm and of 36S is 3.171 fm; the rms neutron radius of 34Si is 3.304 fm and of 36S is 3.297 fm.
* The rms proton radius of 292120 is 6.223 fm and of 310126 is 6.302 fm; the rms neutron radius of 292120 is 6.386 fm and of 310126 is 6.519 fm.
* In the 292120 nucleus, to create the central depression up to r=3.0 fm with average density 0.041 fm^{-3}, one must move 0.061 particles from the inner sphere (r<1.0 fm), 0.44 particles from the first inner shell (1.0-2.0 fm), and 1.19 particles from the second inner shell (2.0-3.0 fm) to outer shells.

## Context

This work extends the debate on bubble nucleus formation mechanisms, directly contradicting the correlation analysis of SNR (2017) which claimed central depression in superheavy nuclei is driven by electrostatic repulsion, and instead supporting the earlier conclusion of AF (2005) that shell structure and specific subshell filling dominate. The paper introduces for the first time the role of classically forbidden regions at the bottom of wine bottle potentials in reducing s-state densities at r=0, and proposes an additivity rule for densities that holds comparably in the A≈40 mass region and in superheavy nuclei, suggesting a common mechanism. This enables future experimental searches for bubble nuclei at FRIB, FAIR, and RIKEN to be guided by single-particle structure considerations rather than Coulomb-driven expectations.
