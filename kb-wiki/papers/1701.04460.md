# Pascalutsa-Vanderhaeghen light-by-light sumrule from photon-photon collisions

> Light-by-light scattering sumrules based on general field theory principles relate cross-sections with different helicities. In this paper the simplest sumrule is tested for the $I=0$ and $2$ channels for "real" photon-photon collisions. Important contributions come from the long-lived pseudoscalar mesons and from di-meson intermediate states. The latest Amplitude Analysis of $γγ\toππ, \overline{K}K$ allows this contribution to be evaluated. However, we find that other multi-meson contributions up to 2.5 GeV are required to satisfy the sumrules. While data on three and four pion cross-sections exist, there is no information about their isospin and helicity decomposition. Nevertheless, we show the measured cross-sections are sufficiently large to ensure the sumrules for the helicity differences are likely fulfilled.

## Key claim

The Pascalutsa-Vanderhaeghen sumrule for real photon-photon scattering, which requires the weighted integral of the helicity-two minus helicity-zero cross-section to vanish, is not satisfied by the sum of single-particle and two-meson intermediate states alone; additional multi-meson contributions up to 2.5 GeV are required, and the authors argue that existing cross-section data are sufficiently large to likely fulfill the sumrule, but a definitive test is impossible without isospin and helicity decomposition of those multi-meson channels.

## Method

The authors evaluated the PV sumrule integral \(\int_{s_{th}}^{\infty} ds\,(\sigma_2-\sigma_0)/s\) for the \(I=0\) and \(I=2\) channels by combining: (1) single-particle contributions from \(\pi^0\), \(\eta\), \(\eta'\) using PDG decay widths; (2) two-meson contributions from \(\gamma\gamma\to\pi\pi\) and \(\gamma\gamma\to K\overline{K}\) obtained from a coupled-channel Amplitude Analysis of Belle data up to \(\sqrt{s}=1.44\) GeV, with higher-energy contributions estimated using the Born approximation; and (3) estimates for multi-pion channels (3\(\pi\), 4\(\pi\)) by scaling measured integrated cross-sections \(\Sigma\) with a ratio \(\mathcal{R}\) derived from the Born amplitude to convert from the measured angular range to the full angular range and from the sum of helicities to the helicity difference.

## Key numbers

* Single-particle contributions to the sumrule integral: \(\pi^0\) in \(I=1\) gives \(-190.9\pm4.0\) nb; \(\eta,\eta'\) in \(I=0\) give \(-497.7\pm19.3\) nb (from PDG decay widths).
* Two-meson contributions from Amplitude Analysis (integral up to 2 GeV\(^2\)): \(\gamma\gamma\to\pi\pi\) gives \(+231.3\pm31.2\) nb for \(I=0\) and \(-82.9\pm12.2\) nb for \(I=2\); \(\gamma\gamma\to K\overline{K}\) gives \(+6.2\pm2.0\) nb for \(I=0\) and \(+0.9\pm0.2\) nb for \(I=1\).
* Total sumrule integral including high-energy Born estimate (up to infinity): \(I=0\) sum = \(-166.0\pm46.4\) nb; \(I=1\) sum = \(-37.8\pm28.4\) nb; \(I=2\) sum = \(-44.2\pm6.1\) nb.
* Measured integrated cross-section \(\Sigma\) for \(\pi^+\pi^-\pi^+\pi^-\) from 1.1 to 2.5 GeV: \(215\pm11\pm21\) nb (data from reference [data6]).
* Measured integrated cross-section \(\Sigma\) for \(\pi^+\pi^-\pi^0\pi^0\) from 0.8 to 3.4 GeV: \(103\pm4\pm14\) nb (data from reference [data8]).

## Context

This work tests the Pascalutsa-Vanderhaeghen sumrule (Pascalutsa and Vanderhaeghen, 2010) for real photon-photon scattering, which is a key constraint for models of hadronic light-by-light scattering in the muon \(g-2\) calculation. The authors extend earlier narrow-resonance estimates by Pascalutsa and Vanderhaeghen (2010, 2011) by using a full Amplitude Analysis (Dai, Pennington, 2016) of Belle data on \(\gamma\gamma\to\pi\pi, K\overline{K}\) to accurately compute two-meson contributions. The results show that the sumrule is not saturated by these channels, and the authors identify four-pion production as the likely dominant missing contribution, urging dedicated experimental studies at BESIII and Belle to provide differential cross-sections for \(\rho^+\rho^-\) and \(\rho^0\rho^0\) production from threshold to 2.5 GeV.
