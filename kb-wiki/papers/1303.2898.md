# Interplay between ω-Nucleon Interaction and Nucleon Mass in Dense Baryonic Matter

> The dilaton-limit fixed point and the scaling properties of hadrons in the close vicinity of the fixed point in dense baryonic matter uncovered in hidden local symmetry implemented with spontaneously broken scale symmetry are shown to reveal a surprisingly intricate interplay, hitherto unsuspected, between the origin of the bulk of proton mass and the renormalization-group flow of the ω-nuclear interactions. This rends a theoretical support to the previous (phenomenologically) observed correlation between the dropping nucleon mass and the behavior of the ω-nuclear interactions in dense matter described in terms of half skyrmions that appear at a density denoted $n_{1/2}$ in skyrmion crystals. The role of the ω-meson degree of freedom in the source for nucleon mass observed in this paper is highly reminiscent of its important role in the skyrmion description of nucleon mass in hidden local symmetric theory. One of the most notable novel results found in this paper is that the nucleon mass in dense baryonic medium undergoes a drop roughly linear in density up to a density (denoted $\tilde{n}$) slightly above nuclear matter density ($n_0$) and then stays more or less constant up to the dilaton limit fixed point. The possibility that we entertain is that $\tilde{n}$ coincides with or at least close to $n_{1/2}$. We note that this feature can be economically captured by the parity-doublet model for nucleons with the chiral-invariant mass $m_0\sim (0.7-0.8) m_N$. It is found in one-loop renormalization-group analysis with the Lagrangian adopted that while the ρ-NN coupling "runs" in density, the ω-NN coupling does not scale: it will scale at two-loop or higher-loop order, but at a slower pace, so it is more appropriate to say it "walks" rather than runs.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in dense baryonic matter, the effective ω-nucleon coupling does not drop as fast as the ρ-nucleon coupling does, contrary to the expectation from U(2) symmetry; instead it "walks" rather than runs under renormalization-group flow, and this behavior is intricately linked to the nucleon mass dropping roughly linearly up to a density slightly above nuclear matter density and then staying constant.

## Method

The authors construct a hidden local symmetric (HLS) Lagrangian with parity-doublet nucleons, implementing spontaneously broken scale symmetry via a dilaton field, but breaking U(2) symmetry for the ρ and ω mesons by treating them under separate gauge groups [SU(2)_V × U(1)_V]_local. They perform a one-loop renormalization-group analysis of the coupling constants and analyze the dilaton-limit fixed point (DLFP) approached as the chiral condensate vanishes, using mean-field treatment of the Lagrangian with density-sliding parameters.

## Key numbers

- The nucleon mass drops roughly linearly in density up to a density denoted \(\tilde{n}\) slightly above nuclear matter density \(n_0\), then stays constant up to the dilaton-limit fixed point; the authors entertain that \(\tilde{n}\) coincides with or is close to \(n_{1/2} \sim 2n_0\).
- The chiral-invariant mass in the parity-doublet model is estimated as \(m_0 \sim (0.7-0.8) m_N\), where \(m_N\) is the free-space nucleon mass.
- Compact-star matter involves central densities of \(\sim (5-6)n_0\) where \(n_0\) is normal nuclear matter density.

## Context

This work extends the hidden local symmetry framework with dilaton-limit fixed point developed by Sasaki (2012) and Paeng (2012), and provides theoretical support for phenomenological correlations observed in skyrmion crystal studies of dense matter by Lee and Rho. The results enable a consistent description of compact-star equations of state, specifically supporting the stiffening of the symmetry energy needed to accommodate the 2-solar-mass neutron star discovered by Dong et al., while avoiding the collapse that would result from unmodified suppression of ω-exchange repulsion. The paper builds on the parity-doublet model of DeTar and Kunihiro (1989) and the dilaton-limit fixed point concept introduced by Beane and van Kolck.
