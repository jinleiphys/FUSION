# Solar models with accretion. I. Application to the solar abundance problem

> We generate new standard solar models using newly analyzed nuclear fusion cross sections and present results for helioseismic quantities and solar neutrino fluxes. We discuss the status of the solar abundance problem and investigate whether nonstandard solar models with accretion from the protoplanetary disk might alleviate the problem. We examine a broad range of possibilities, analyzing both metal-enriched and metal-depleted accretion models and exploring three scenarios for the timing of the accretion. Only partial solutions are found: one can bring either the depth of the convective zone or the surface helium abundance into agreement with helioseismic results, but not both simultaneously. In addition, detailed results for solar neutrino fluxes show that neutrinos are a competitive source of information about the solar core and can help constrain possible accretion histories of the Sun. Finally, we briefly discuss how measurements of solar neutrinos from the CN-cycle could shed light on the interaction between the early Sun and its protoplanetary disk.

## Key claim

Nonstandard solar models with accretion from the protoplanetary disk can only partially solve the solar abundance problem: they can bring either the depth of the convective zone or the surface helium abundance into agreement with helioseismic results, but not both simultaneously, regardless of whether the accreted material is metal-enriched or metal-depleted.

## Method

The authors constructed solar models using the GARSTEC stellar evolution code, incorporating newly evaluated nuclear reaction rates from Solar Fusion II (SFII) and the AGSS09 low-metallicity solar composition. They modified the standard solar model algorithm to include accretion of material with varying metal mass fractions (Z_ac from 0 to 0.03) and total accreted masses (M_ac from 3e-5 to 0.06 M_sun), exploring three timing scenarios: early accretion starting at 5 Myr, intermediate at 15 Myr, and late at 30 Myr, with a fixed accretion duration of 10 Myr.

## Key numbers

- GS98 standard solar model yields convective zone depth R_CZ/R_sun = 0.7124 and surface helium Y_S = 0.2429, compared to helioseismic values of 0.713 ± 0.001 and 0.2485 ± 0.0035 respectively
- AGSS09 standard solar model yields R_CZ/R_sun = 0.7231 and Y_S = 0.2319, differing from helioseismic values at 3σ for both quantities
- The average fractional rms sound speed deviation ⟨δc/c⟩ is 0.0009 for GS98 and 0.0037 for AGSS09, a factor of about four larger for the low-metallicity model
- The SFII recommended S-factor for ^7Be(p,γ)^8B is S_17(0) = 20.8 ± 0.7 (expt) ± 1.4 (theor) eV b, with 7.5% total uncertainty at solar Gamow peak energies
- The SFII recommended S-factor for ^14N(p,γ)^15O is S_114(0) = 1.66 ± 0.12 keV b, significantly reduced from the SFI value of 3.5^{+0.4}_{-1.6} keV b
- The SFII recommended S-factor for p(p,e^+ν_e)d is S_11(0) = (4.01 ± 0.04) × 10^{-22} keV b, with 0.9% uncertainty in the Gamow peak
- The SFII recommended S-factor for ^3He(^4He,γ)^7Be is S_34(0) = 0.56 ± 0.03 keV b
- The SFII recommended S-factor for ^3He(^3He,2p)^4He is S_33(0) = 5.21 ± 0.27 MeV b
- The SFII recommended S-factor for hep neutrinos is S_hep = (8.6 ± 2.6) × 10^{-20} keV b
- The SFII recommended S-factor for ^15N(p,γ)^16O is S_115^γ(0) = 36 ± 6 keV b (interim value)
- Predicted ^8B neutrino flux for GS98 is 5.58(1 ± 0.14) × 10^6 cm^{-2} s^{-1} and for AGSS09 is 4.59(1 ± 0.14) × 10^6 cm^{-2} s^{-1}
- Predicted ^7Be neutrino flux for GS98 is 5.00(1 ± 0.07) × 10^9 cm^{-2} s^{-1} and for AGSS09 is 4.56(1 ± 0.07) × 10^9 cm^{-2} s^{-1}
- χ^2 values comparing SSM predictions to inferred solar neutrino fluxes are 3.5 for GS98 and 3.4 for AGSS09, both giving 90% agreement probability
- The accreted mass range explored is from 3 × 10^{-5} to 0.06 M_sun (10 to 2 × 10^4 M_earth)
- The minimum-mass solar nebula estimates range from 0.013 to 0.17 M_sun from various authors (Weidenschilling 1977, Hayashi 1981, Desch 2007)

## Context

This work extends the standard solar model framework of Bahcall and Pinsonneault (2001) and Serenelli et al. (2009) by incorporating the updated Solar Fusion II nuclear reaction rates, and directly addresses the solar abundance problem identified when low-metallicity AGSS09 abundances (Asplund et al. 2009) are used. The accretion approach builds on earlier inhomogeneous solar models by Guzik et al. (2006, 2010) and Castro et al. (2007), who assumed metal-poor accretion onto the main-sequence Sun but found persistent discrepancies in sound speed profiles. The results enable future work connecting solar accretion histories to measurements of CN-cycle neutrinos, which could distinguish between different accretion scenarios and constrain the interaction between the early Sun and its protoplanetary disk.
