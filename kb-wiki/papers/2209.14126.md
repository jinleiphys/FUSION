# The dual King relation

> We introduce a new linear relation in the isotope shifts of atomic spectroscopy. While the famous King relation is the linear relation among the different transitions, the new one is the linear relation among the different isotope pairs. Since we obtain this relation by exchanging the roles of the electronic and the nuclear factors in the original relation, we call it the dual King relation. This relation shows us similar information to the original King relation without including new fit parameters when we measure the isotope shifts of many transitions. In the dual King relation, the fit coefficients consist of the nuclear factors. Then, the fit results give us constraints to the ratios of the isotope dependence independent of the electron wave functions. This helps us to test the origin of unknown higher order isotope shifts. We show that the dual King relation can also be employed to constrain the weakly interacting light new boson at the same level as the original King relation.

## Key claim
The paper introduces a new linear relation among isotope shifts, called the dual King relation, which is obtained by exchanging the roles of electronic and nuclear factors in the original King relation. The author claims this relation provides similar information to the original King relation but with fewer fit parameters when many transitions are measured, and can constrain weakly interacting light new bosons at the same level as the original relation.

## Method
The author formulates the dual King relation by rewriting the isotope shift equation as a matrix equation where the roles of electronic factors (transition-dependent) and nuclear factors (isotope-pair-dependent) are exchanged. For a set of isotope pairs, the relation shows that different transitions lie on a linear relation in the space spanned by distinct isotope pairs, with fit coefficients consisting of nuclear isotope dependences (inverse mass differences and mean square charge radius differences). The method is demonstrated using existing Ytterbium data and fake Calcium data generated from known electronic factors and nuclear masses.

## Key numbers
- For Ytterbium data, the 2D dual King fit gives a minimum chi-squared of 1.7 x 10^4; the 3D result is chi-squared = 26 with 9 degrees of freedom, corresponding to a 3.1 sigma deviation.
- For fake Calcium data with 4 isotope pairs and 7 transitions, the 2D dual King relation requires 4 parameters (compared to 12 for the original King relation), and the 3D relation requires 3 parameters (compared to 15).
- The ratios of inverse mass differences D(mu)_31 and D(mu)_21 are constrained with errors of about 10^-6 and 10^-7, respectively.
- The ratios of mean square charge radius differences D(<r^2>)_ij are constrained on a line with a width of O(10^-7), while their errors from direct measurements are O(10^-2).
- Current 95% confidence level upper bounds on the new boson coupling: |g_e g_n| < 6.9 x 10^-11 for Ca and 3.0 x 10^-12 for Yb.
- Expected 95% upper bound with 2D dual King relation at 1 Hz precision: |g_e g_n| < 8.8 x 10^-15.
- Expected 95% upper bound with 3D dual King relation at 10 mHz precision: |g_e g_n| < 9.7 x 10^-17.

## Context
This work extends the original King relation (King, 1963) and its generalized version (Ref. 1710.11443) used to constrain new physics from isotope shifts. The dual King relation provides a complementary approach that simplifies analysis when many transitions are measured, as is becoming possible with precision spectroscopy of Ytterbium (Refs. 2004.11383, 2110.13544, Figueroa 2022, 2201.03578) and planned measurements with highly charged Calcium ions (Ref. 2102.02309). The method enables direct constraints on nuclear isotope dependences independent of electron wave functions, and can test the origin of higher-order isotope shifts such as second-order perturbation of the field shift, second-order perturbation of the mass shift, and particle shifts from new bosons.
