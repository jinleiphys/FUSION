# Enhanced Neutrino Cooling from Parity-Doubled Nucleons in Neutron Star Cooling Simulations

> Although restoration of chiral symmetry is predicted by quantum chromodynamics to take place at high baryon density, most modeling of neutron star interiors disregards a chiral phase transition. We model neutron star cores with a parity doublet model, which allows for dynamical chiral symmetry restoration and predicts the appearance of the parity partners of nucleons and hyperons at large densities, as well as deconfined quark matter. We study the thermal evolution of neutron stars, focusing for the first time on the impact of Urca processes involving the parity partners in neutron star cooling simulations. We find that Urca processes for the parity partners of the nucleons significantly affect the thermal evolution of massive stars and allow for improved agreement with observed surface temperature and ages.

# Enhanced Neutrino Cooling from Parity-Doubled Nucleons in Neutron Star Cooling Simulations

## Key claim

The inclusion of direct Urca processes involving the parity partners of nucleons (specifically the N⁻(1535) and its isospin partners) in neutron star cooling simulations significantly accelerates the thermal evolution of massive stars (above approximately 2 M⊙), but only when those parity partners appear at densities above a weakly first-order chiral transition near n_b ≈ 0.6 fm⁻³; the effect is negligible for lower-mass stars where parity partners are absent.

## Method

The authors constructed neutron star models using the parity doublet chiral mean field (PD-CMF) model, an SU(3) Lagrangian with non-linear chiral symmetry realization that includes the full baryon octet, their negative-parity partners, and deconfined quark matter via a Polyakov loop. They solved the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff equations for hydrostatic equilibrium and then evolved the thermal structure using the general-relativistic cooling equations, incorporating neutrino emissivities from direct Urca, modified Urca, and bremsstrahlung processes for all baryon species and their parity partners, as well as quark Urca processes, with nucleon pairing (CCDK model for ¹S₀ and AO model for ³P₂) and quark pairing (CFL pattern with Δ = 10 MeV) included where specified.

## Key numbers

- The weakly first-order chiral transition occurs at baryon density n_b ≈ 0.6 fm⁻³, with a small energy density jump Δε/ε ≈ 4%
- The PD-CMF model predicts a maximum neutron star mass of approximately 2.1 M⊙
- The direct Urca neutrino emissivity for parity partners follows Q^dU ∝ T⁶, with the prefactor containing G_F² cos²θ_C (θ_C = 13.02°) and g_A = 1.267
- The modified Urca emissivity scales as Q^mU ∝ T⁸, with the neutron-branch constant A = 0.04656 and pion mass m_π = 139 MeV
- Nuclear matter saturation properties: n_b = 0.16 fm⁻³, binding energy E_0/B = −16 MeV, symmetry energy S_0 = 31 MeV, symmetry energy slope L = 53 MeV, incompressibility K_0 = 303 MeV
- The excluded volume parameter for baryons is v_b = 0.4 fm³
- Envelope masses considered: ΔM/M = 10⁻¹⁸ for heavy-element envelopes and ΔM/M = 10⁻⁸ for carbon-rich envelopes
- Quark pairing assumed with critical temperature T_c = 0.4Δ and gap Δ = 10 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous neutron star cooling studies within the SU(3) parity doublet model by Dexheimer (2012), Mukherjee (2017), and Marczenko (2018), which did not include neutrino processes involving parity partners. It builds directly on the recent calculation by Brodie (2025) that found nucleon parity partners enable rapid cooling in an isothermal approximation, now performing fully self-consistent cooling simulations of the entire star including crust, core, and atmosphere. The results enable discrimination between models with and without chiral symmetry restoration in dense matter, complementing constraints from mass-radius measurements and gravitational-wave signals from mergers (Hammond, 2025). The comparison with observed neutron star surface temperatures from the catalog of Beznogov (2014) shows that including parity-partner Urca processes with paired quark matter produces cooling bands broad enough to accommodate most observations, unlike models without parity partners.
