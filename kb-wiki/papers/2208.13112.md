# Toward Quantum Computing Phase Diagrams of Gauge Theories with Thermal Pure Quantum States

> The phase diagram of strong interactions in nature at finite temperature and chemical potential remains largely unexplored theoretically due to inadequacy of Monte-Carlo-based computational techniques in overcoming a sign problem. Quantum computing offers a sign-problem-free approach but evaluating thermal expectation values is generally resource intensive on quantum computers. To facilitate thermodynamic studies of gauge theories, we propose a generalization of thermal-pure-quantum-state formulation of statistical mechanics applied to constrained gauge-theory dynamics, and numerically demonstrate that the phase diagram of a simple low-dimensional gauge theory is robustly determined using this approach, including mapping a chiral phase transition in the model at finite temperature and chemical potential. Quantum algorithms, resource requirements, and algorithmic and hardware error analysis are further discussed to motivate future implementations. Thermal pure quantum states, therefore, may present a suitable candidate for efficient thermal-state preparation in gauge theories in the era of quantum computing.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a generalization of thermal-pure-quantum-state (TPQ) formulation to gauge theories by adding a penalty term to the Hamiltonian that suppresses unphysical components during imaginary-time evolution, and numerically demonstrates that this "physical TPQ" (PTPQ) approach can robustly determine the phase diagram of a simple low-dimensional gauge theory, including a chiral phase transition at finite temperature and chemical potential. The hedge is that the demonstration is performed on a small system (N=6 sites, 11 qubits) of a Z2 lattice gauge theory in 1+1 dimensions, and the method's utility for larger, more complex gauge theories like QCD remains to be tested on actual quantum hardware.

## Method

The authors construct physical thermal pure quantum (PTPQ) states by first preparing an approximate Haar-random state using a random circuit of single-qubit and entangling two-qubit gates, then applying a non-unitary imaginary-time evolution operator e^{-βH/2} combined with a Gauss's law penalty operator Q_G = e^{-(β/2) λ Σ_n (1-G_n)} that penalizes non-physical components. For the Z2 lattice gauge theory in 1+1 dimensions coupled to staggered fermions, they compute the chiral condensate order parameter as a function of temperature T and chemical potential μ using exact diagonalization on classical computers for N=6 sites, with random-circuit depth d=20 and averaging over r=10 PTPQ-state realizations.

## Key numbers

- System size: N=6 fermionic lattice sites, corresponding to 11 qubits
- Model parameters: lattice spacing a=1, fermion mass m=1/2, electric-field strength ε/m=1, penalty strength λ/m=4
- Random-circuit depth d=20 and averaging over r=10 PTPQ-state realizations used for the phase diagram
- For non-equal-time correlation functions: r=1 PTPQ sample at T/m=0.2 and r=20 samples at T/m=4, with μ/m=0.5
- Trotterization: N_T=10 steps (Δβ=1) reproduces chiral phase transition qualitatively; N_T≳100 steps (Δβ≲0.1) required for percent-level agreement at T/m=0.1
- Unitary errors: for σ^x_l errors at α/m=0.1, PTPQ states still capture phase transitions; at α/m=1, relative accuracy near transition drops to approximately 45%

## Context

This work extends the thermal-pure-quantum-state formulation of statistical mechanics introduced by Sugiura and Shimizu (2013) to gauge theories with local constraints, where naive TPQ states fail because random states may lie outside the physical Hilbert space. The approach builds on prior work using penalty terms to enforce Gauss's law in analog and digital quantum simulation of gauge theories by Banerjee et al. (2012), Hauke et al. (2013), and Halimeh et al. (2021, 2022). The results enable future quantum-computing studies of phase diagrams and transport properties in strongly interacting gauge theories, including potential applications to QCD at finite baryon density where classical Monte-Carlo methods suffer from the sign problem.
