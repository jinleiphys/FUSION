# Exact WKB method for radial Schrödinger equation

> We revisit exact WKB quantization for radial Schrödinger problems from the modern resurgence perspective, with emphasis on how ``physically meaningful'' quantization paths should be chosen and interpreted. Using connection formulae at simple turning points and at regular singular points, we show that the nontrivial-cycle data give the spectrum. In particular, for the $3$-dimensional harmonic oscillator and the $3$-dimensional Coulomb potential, we explicitly compute a closed contour which starts at $+\infty$, bulges into the $r<0$ sector to encircle the origin, and returns to $+\infty$. Also we propose that the appropriate slice of the closed path provides a physical local basis at $r=0$, which is used by an origin-to-$\infty$ open path. Via the change of variables $r=e^x$ ($x\in(-\infty,\infty)$), the origin data are pushed to the boundary condition of convergence at $x\to-\infty$, which renders the equivalence between open-connection and closed-cycle quantization transparent. The Maslov contribution from the regular singularity is incorporated either as a small-circle monodromy which is justified in terms of renormalization group, or, equivalently, as a boundary phase; we also develop an optimized/variational perturbation theory on exact WKB. Our analysis clarifies, in radial settings, how mathematical monodromy data and physical boundary conditions dovetail, thereby addressing recent debates on path choices in resurgence-based quantization.

# Exact WKB method for radial Schrödinger equation

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for radial Schrödinger problems, the choice of quantization path (closed cycle encircling the origin versus open path from origin to infinity) is a gauge freedom: both yield identical spectra once the correct local bases and connection data, including the Maslov contribution from the regular singularity at \(r=0\), are imposed, provided the nontrivial-cycle data (Voros periods and Stokes multipliers) are supplied.

## Method

The authors apply exact WKB analysis with Borel resummation to the radial Schrödinger equation, using connection formulae at simple turning points (Airy-type) and at the regular singular point \(r=0\) (small-circle monodromy). They compute the monodromy matrix for a closed contour that starts at \(+\infty\), bulges into the \(r<0\) sector, encircles the origin, and returns to \(+\infty\), and compare this to an open-path formulation starting from a local Frobenius basis at \(r=0\) and propagating to infinity. The equivalence is made explicit for the 3D harmonic oscillator and the 3D Coulomb potential, and the transformation \(r=e^x\) maps the origin to a boundary condition at \(x\to-\infty\).

## Key numbers

- For the 3D harmonic oscillator, the quantization condition yields \(E_n = \hbar\left(2n_r + l + \frac{3}{2}\right)\) with \(n_r\in\mathbb{Z}\), where the Maslov contribution from the origin is \(l+1/2\).
- For the 3D Coulomb potential, the quantization condition yields \(E_n = -\frac{e^4}{2\hbar^2 n^2}\) with \(n = n_r + l + 1\), where the leading-order contour integral gives \(\frac{1}{2\pi}\hbar^{-1}\oint_0^a dr\, S_{-1} = i\frac{e^2}{\hbar}\frac{1}{\sqrt{-2E}}\).
- The closed-cycle quantization condition takes the form \(\cosh\left(\frac{1}{\hbar}\oint dr\, S_{\mathrm{odd}}\right) + \cos(\pi(2l+1)) = 0\), where \(\sqrt{1+4Q_2} = 2l+1\).
- The small-circle monodromy around \(r=0\) yields \(e^{i\pi(2l+1)}\).

## Context

This work extends the exact WKB/resurgence framework of the Aoki–Kawai–Takei (AKT) school (Aoki, Kawai, Takei, 1991–2004) and earlier work by Voros (1983) and Delabaere (1999) to radial problems with a regular singularity at the origin. It addresses recent debates on path choices in resurgence-based quantization, particularly in the context of black-hole quasi-normal mode analyses by Andersson (1995), Hatsuda (2019–2023), and Miyachi (2025). The paper clarifies how mathematical monodromy data and physical boundary conditions dovetail in radial settings, enabling a unified treatment of bound-state and scattering problems.
