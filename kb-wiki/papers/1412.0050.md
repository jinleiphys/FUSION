# Toroidal high-spin isomers in light nuclei with N not equal to Z

> The combined considerations of both the bulk liquid-drop-type behavior and the quantized aligned rotation with cranked Skyrme-Hartree-Fock approach revealed previously [Phys. Lett. B 738 (2014) 401] that even-even, N=Z, toroidal high-spin isomeric states have general occurrences for light nuclei with A between 28 and 52. We find that in this mass region there are in addition N not equal to Z toroidal high-spin isomers when the single-particle shells for neutrons and protons occur at the same cranked frequency $\hbar ω$. Examples of N not equal to Z toroidal high-spin isomers, $^{36}_{16}$S$_{20}$($I$=74$\hbar$) and $^{40}_{18}$Ar$_{22}$($I$=80,102$\hbar$), are located and examined. The systematic properties of these N not equal to Z toroidal high-spin isomers fall into the same regular (muti-particle)-(muti-hole) patterns as other N=Z toroidal high-spin isomers.

# Toroidal high-spin isomers in light nuclei with N not equal to Z

## Key claim

The authors find that toroidal high-spin isomers exist not only for N=Z nuclei but also for N≠Z nuclei in the mass range A=28–52, provided that the single-particle shells for neutrons and protons occur at the same cranked frequency ħω. The systematic properties of these N≠Z toroidal isomers follow the same regular multi-particle–multi-hole patterns as their N=Z counterparts.

## Method

The authors used a cranked Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) approach with the SkM* force and a density-dependent mixed pairing interaction, solving the constrained HFB or cranked HF equations with the symmetry-unrestricted code HFODD in a three-dimensional Cartesian deformed harmonic oscillator basis of 1140 states. Starting from toroidal configurations obtained at I=0 under a large-magnitude negative quadrupole moment constraint Q20, they applied non-collective rotation about the symmetry axis by introducing a Lagrange multiplier ħω to reach quantized angular momentum states I=Iz, and searched for local energy minima as a function of Q20 and I. The single-particle Routhians from a radially displaced harmonic oscillator potential were used as a qualitative guide to locate the (N,I) and (Z,I) shells where neutrons and protons have shells at the same ħω.

## Key numbers

- Toroidal high-spin isomer 36S (I=74ħ) found at Q20 = -13.95 b, ħω = 1.85 MeV, excitation energy E* = 205.87 MeV, major radius R = 6.08 fm, minor radius d = 1.39 fm, R/d = 4.37, maximum density ρmax = 0.112 fm⁻³
- Toroidal high-spin isomer 40Ar (I=80ħ) found at Q20 = -17.83 b, ħω = 1.65 MeV, E* = 215.49 MeV, R = 6.56 fm, d = 1.38 fm, R/d = 4.75, ρmax = 0.116 fm⁻³
- Toroidal high-spin isomer 40Ar (I=102ħ) found at Q20 = -21.37 b, ħω = 1.85 MeV, E* = 253.42 MeV, R = 7.21 fm, d = 1.37 fm, R/d = 5.26, ρmax = 0.107 fm⁻³
- For 40Ar, increasing I from 80ħ to 102ħ decreases ρmax from 0.116 to 0.107 fm⁻³ and increases R from 6.56 to 7.21 fm, while d stays constant at about 1.37 fm
- The 36S(I=74ħ) isomer arises from 4p-4h excitations of both neutrons and protons at ħω ≈ 1.8 MeV; the 40Ar(I=80ħ) isomer also from 4p-4h excitations at ħω ≈ 1.8 MeV; the 40Ar(I=102ħ) isomer from 5p-5h excitations at ħω ≈ 2.3 MeV
- For the 52Fe(I=132ħ) isomer, the zero-point energy for the topmost occupied state [N,0,9]19/2 is 8.0 MeV for both neutrons and protons

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous finding (Staszczak and Wong, 2014) that even-even N=Z toroidal high-spin isomers occur generally for light nuclei with A between 28 and 52, by demonstrating that N≠Z toroidal isomers also exist in the same mass region. It confirms and complements the independent work of Ichikawa et al. (2012, 2014) who found toroidal high-spin isomers in 40Ca and other N=Z nuclei using cranked SHF starting from alpha-particle ring configurations. The results enable further systematic study of toroidal nuclear shapes and their stability, and raise the question of whether the observed bound states with positive total energy but negative transverse energy represent bound states in the continuum analogous to those described by von Neumann and Wigner (1929).
