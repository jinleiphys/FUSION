# Numerical solution of $Q^2$ evolution equations in a brute-force method

> We investigate numerical solution of $Q^2$ evolution equations for structure functions in the nucleon and in nuclei. (Dokshitzer-Gribov-Lipatov-)Altarelli-Parisi and Mueller-Qiu evolution equations are solved in a brute-force method. Spin-independent flavor-nonsinglet and singlet equations with next-to-leading-order $α_s$ corrections are studied. Dividing the variables $x$ and $Q^2$ into small steps, we simply solve the integrodifferential equations. Numerical results indicate that accuracy is better than 2\% in the region $10^{-4}<x<0.8$ if more than two-hundred $Q^2$ steps and more than one-thousand $x$ steps are taken. The numerical solution is discussed in detail, and evolution results are compared with $Q^2$ dependent data in CDHSW, SLAC, BCDMS, EMC, NMC, Fermilab-E665, ZEUS, and H1 experiments. We provide a FORTRAN program for Q$^2$ evolution (and ``devolution'') of nonsinglet-quark, singlet-quark, $q_i+\bar q_i$, and gluon distributions (and corresponding structure functions) in the nucleon and in nuclei. This is a very useful program for studying spin-independent structure functions.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a brute-force method for solving Q² evolution equations (Altarelli-Parisi and Mueller-Qiu) achieves accuracy better than 2% in the region 10⁻⁴ < x < 0.8, but only if more than two-hundred Q² steps and more than one-thousand x steps are taken, with the x steps spaced logarithmically rather than linearly.

## Method

The authors divide the variables x and Q² into small steps, using logarithmic spacing for x to handle the small-x region down to 10⁻⁴, and define differentiation and integration by finite differences and Riemann sums respectively. They then solve the integrodifferential DGLAP equations (both flavor-nonsinglet and singlet, with next-to-leading-order αs corrections) and the nonlinear Mueller-Qiu recombination equations step by step in Q², using the discretized forms given in Eqs. (3.1)-(3.4). Special handling is applied to the plus-distributions [1/(1-x)]₊ and [ln(1-x)/(1-x)]₊ in the splitting functions.

## Key numbers

- Accuracy better than 2% in the region 10⁻⁴ < x < 0.8 when using more than 200 Q² steps and more than 1000 x steps
- Typical running time approximately 5 minutes on AlphaServer 2100 4/200 for the nonsinglet case, 60 minutes for singlet-quark evolution, and 80 minutes for singlet evolution with recombination effects
- The FORTRAN program (BF1) contains 2439 lines of code
- Nuclear radius is taken as R_A = R₁ A^{1/3} with R₁ = 1.1 fm, and R₀ = R₁ = 1 fm is used with K = 9/8 so that nuclear correction terms vanish for A = 1

## Context

This work extends earlier numerical approaches to Q² evolution, particularly addressing limitations of the Laguerre-polynomial method of Herrod, Wada, and Webber (1983), which the authors found had unsatisfactory accuracy at small and large x in the nonsinglet case and could not handle the nonlinear recombination terms. The brute-force method was previously investigated in Ref. [BF] but without published details on accuracy. The paper provides a FORTRAN program (BF1) that enables evolution of nonsinglet-quark, singlet-quark, q_i + q̄_i, and gluon distributions in nucleons and nuclei, and compares results with data from CDHSW, SLAC, BCDMS, EMC, NMC, Fermilab-E665, ZEUS, and H1 experiments. This enables future studies of small-x physics at HERA and tests of higher-twist effects such as parton recombination contributions to nuclear shadowing.
