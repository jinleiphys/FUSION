# Nuclear prolate-shape dominance with the Woods-Saxon potential

> We study the prolate-shape predominance of the nuclear ground-state deformation by calculating the masses of more than two thousand even-even nuclei using the Strutinsky method, modified by Kruppa, and improved by us. The influences of the surface thickness of the single-particle potentials, the strength of the spin-orbit potential, and the pairing correlations are investigated by varying the parameters of the Woods-Saxon potential and the pairing interaction. The strong interference between the effects of the surface thickness and the spin-orbit potential is confirmed to persist for six sets of the Woods-Saxon potential parameters. The observed behavior of the ratios of prolate, oblate, and spherical nuclei versus potential parameters are rather different in different mass regions. It is also found that the ratio of spherical nuclei increases for weakly bound unstable nuclei. Differences of the results from the calculations with the Nilsson potential are described in detail.

## Key claim

The prolate-shape dominance of nuclear ground-state deformations arises from a strong interference between the effects of the surface thickness of the single-particle potential and the spin-orbit potential, but the amplitude of this interference is reduced by about a factor of 1/3 when the Woods-Saxon potential replaces the Nilsson potential, and the observed ratios of prolate, oblate, and spherical nuclei depend on the mass region and on whether nuclei are weakly bound.

## Method

The authors used the Strutinsky macroscopic-microscopic method, modified with Kruppa's prescription and their own reference density method to handle the continuum spectrum of the Woods-Saxon potential, to calculate ground-state shapes for 2148 even-even nuclei from Oxygen to superheavy elements. They varied the surface diffuseness multiplier \(f_a\) (0.2 to 2.0) and the spin-orbit strength multiplier \(f_{ls}\) (-1.5 to 1.5) for six parameter sets of the Woods-Saxon potential, and also varied the pairing strength multiplier \(f_\Delta\) (0, 0.7, 1.0, 1.2). For each potential, the total energy surface was computed on a grid of 51 × 21 points in the \((\beta_2, \beta_4)\) deformation plane.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of prolate-shape nuclei oscillates between approximately 40% (at ±40% strengths of the standard spin-orbit term) and ≥80% (at ±100% and 0% strengths) for the Nilsson potential with standard \(l^2\) strength; this oscillation amplitude is reduced by about a factor of 1/3 when the Woods-Saxon potential is used.
- The study covered 2148 even-even nuclei with \(8 \leq Z \leq 126\) and \(8 \leq N \leq 184\) between the proton and neutron drip lines.
- For the Woods-Saxon potential, 589 combinations of multipliers \((f_{ls}, f_a)\) were examined (31 × 19 grid with \(\Delta f_{ls} = \Delta f_a = 0.1\)); for the Nilsson potential, 496 combinations of \((f_{ls}, f_{ll})\) were examined (31 × 16 grid with \(\Delta f_{ls} = \Delta f_{ll} = 0.1\)).
- The pairing gap was set to \(\tilde{\Delta} = f_\Delta \times 13 A^{-1/2}\) MeV, with \(f_\Delta = 1\) for most calculations and three other values (0, 0.7, 1.2) tested in one section.
- The average spin-orbit splitting in \(^{208}\)Pb varies by about 10% for most parameter sets when \(f_a\) changes from 0.1 to 2.0, but for the universal parameter set the neutron splitting decreases by 25% while the proton splitting increases by 100% over the same range.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier study with the Nilsson potential (Takahara and Shimizu, 2001) by replacing the \(l^2\) term with a more realistic Woods-Saxon potential, confirming that the interference between surface diffuseness and spin-orbit effects persists but is weaker. The results contradict the expectation from the harmonic oscillator potential, which gives only 55% prolate nuclei among deformed nuclei (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975), and support the earlier findings of Frisk (1990) and Arita (2012) that the radial profile of the potential is a key origin of prolate-shape dominance. The method enables systematic study of how potential parameters affect shape distributions across the nuclear chart, including for weakly bound unstable nuclei where the ratio of spherical nuclei increases.
