# Systematic analysis of reaction cross sections of carbon isotopes

> We systematically analyze total reaction cross sections of carbon isotopes with N=6--16 on a $^{12}$C target for wide range of incident energy. The intrinsic structure of the carbon isotope is described by a Slater determinant generated from a phenomenological mean-field potential, which reasonably well reproduces the ground state properties for most of the even $N$ isotopes. We need separate studies not only for odd nuclei but also for $^{16}$C and $^{22}$C. The density of the carbon isotope is constructed by eliminating the effect of the center of mass motion. For the calculations of the cross sections, we take two schemes: one is the Glauber approximation, and the other is the eikonal model using a global optical potential. We find that both of the schemes successfully reproduce low and high incident energy data on the cross sections of $^{12}$C, $^{13}$C and $^{16}$C on $^{12}$C. The calculated reaction cross sections of $^{15}$C are found to be considerably smaller than the empirical values observed at low energy. We find a consistent parameterization of the nucleon-nucleon scattering amplitude, differently from previous ones. Finally, we predict the total reaction cross section of $^{22}$C on $^{12}$C.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that both the Glauber approximation and the eikonal model using a global optical potential successfully reproduce low and high incident energy reaction cross section data for 12C, 13C, and 16C on a 12C target, but the calculated reaction cross sections for 15C are considerably smaller than the empirical values observed at low energy, indicating that the simple mean-field description fails for this nucleus.

## Method

The intrinsic structure of each carbon isotope was described by a Slater determinant generated from a phenomenological mean-field potential with Woods-Saxon form, where the central potential strength was adjusted to reproduce the experimental neutron separation energy for the last nucleon. The center-of-mass motion was removed from the Slater determinant to obtain intrinsic densities, and reaction cross sections were calculated using two complementary schemes: the Glauber approximation (with a newly parameterized nucleon-nucleon profile function) and the eikonal model using a global optical potential for nucleon-12C scattering. For odd-N isotopes and for 16C and 22C, the density description was improved using core+n or core+n+n dynamical models beyond the single Slater determinant.

## Key numbers

- The 12C+12C reaction cross section calculated with the modified NN profile function parameters reproduces experimental data across the full energy range from 30 A MeV to 1000 A MeV; the parameter set includes sigma_NN_tot values ranging from 19.6 fm2 at 30 A MeV to 4.32 fm2 at 1000 A MeV
- The charge radii calculated from the mean-field model are 2.48 fm for 12C, 2.47 fm for 13C, and 2.52 fm for 14C, compared to experimental values of 2.4715, 2.4795, and 2.4962 fm respectively
- The overlap o(nu) between the Slater determinant and a harmonic-oscillator shell model wave function is larger than 0.98 for even N isotopes, but decreases to about 0.95 for odd N nuclei
- The proton radii of carbon isotopes remain nearly constant in the range of 2.3-2.4 fm across the isotopic chain

## Context

This work extends systematic reaction cross section analyses of carbon isotopes previously measured up to 20C at 700-960 A MeV incident energy by Ozawa and collaborators, and is motivated by an ongoing measurement of the reaction cross section of 22C by Tanaka and collaborators. The study provides a consistent parameterization of the nucleon-nucleon scattering amplitude that differs from previous ones by Lenzi and collaborators and by Ray and collaborators, and enables predictions for the 22C+12C reaction cross section that can be tested by future experiments. The paper also connects to three-body model calculations of 16C and 22C by Suzuki, Horiuchi, and collaborators, and to molecular model studies of 14C by Itagaki and collaborators.
