# Schematic model for induced fission in a configuration-interaction approach

> We model fission at barrier-top energies in a simplified model space that permits comparison of different components of the residual nucleon-nucleon interaction. The model space is built on particle-hole excitations of reference configurations. These are Slater determinants of uniformly spaced orbitals characterized only by their quantum numbers and orbital energies. The residual interaction in the Hamiltonian includes the diabatic interaction connecting similar orbitals at different deformations, the pairing interaction between like nucleons, and a schematic off-diagonal neutron-proton interaction. We find that the fission reaction probability is sensitive to the off-diagonal neutron-proton interaction much more than to the pairing and the diabatic interactions. In particular, the transmission coefficients become insensitive to th e strength of the pairing interaction when the neutron-proton interaction is large. We also find that the branching ratio is insensitive to the final-state scission dynamics, as is assumed in the well-known Bohr-Wheeler theory.

## Key claim

The fission reaction probability is sensitive to the off-diagonal neutron-proton interaction much more than to the pairing and diabatic interactions; in particular, transmission coefficients become insensitive to the strength of the pairing interaction when the neutron-proton interaction is large, and the branching ratio is insensitive to final-state scission dynamics as assumed in Bohr-Wheeler theory, but only within the simplified schematic model space with uniformly spaced orbitals.

## Method

The authors constructed a configuration-interaction (CI) model with three Q-blocks along a fission path, using uniformly spaced single-particle orbitals with spacing d for both protons and neutrons. The Hamiltonian included three types of residual interaction: a diabatic interaction connecting similar orbitals at different deformations (parameterized by h2), a monopole pairing interaction between identical nucleons (strength Gpair), and a schematic off-diagonal neutron-proton random interaction (strength vnp). Transmission coefficients and branching ratios were computed using the Datta S-matrix formula with Green's functions, averaging over ensembles of random interactions.

## Key numbers

- The single-particle level spacing d is estimated to be around 0.5 MeV for nuclei in the actinide region
- The baseline pairing strength is Gpair = 0.3d, with the pairing interaction strength in the actinide region around 0.1 MeV corresponding to Gpair ≈ 0.2d
- The baseline neutron-proton interaction strength is vnp = 0.03d
- The diabatic interaction parameter h2 is estimated as h2 = 3d, based on Gogny HFB calculations for 236U
- The barrier height is set to Bh = 4d
- The overlap parameter between neighboring Q-blocks is λ = 1.0, giving overlap N(q,q±1) = e^{-1}
- The mean decay widths are set to γn = 0.001d, γcap = 0.01d, and γfis = 0.1d
- With orthogonal basis and vnp = 0.06d, the energy-averaged transmission coefficient ⟨Tn,fis⟩ is 0.318 for Gpair = 0, 0.331 for Gpair = 0.1d, and 0.331 for Gpair = 0.2d
- With non-orthogonal basis, the base model (vnp = 0.03d, Gpair = 0.3d, h2 = 3d) gives ⟨Tn,fis⟩ = 0.417 and branching ratio α^{-1} = 1.62 at E = 4d
- With all interactions set to zero (model VI), the transmission coefficient is 0.000 within numerical error

## Context

This work extends the CI approach previously applied to semi-realistic calculations based on the Skyrme energy functional by Bertsch and Hagino (2023), which was restricted to neutron excitations only with seniority zero. The present schematic model introduces the off-diagonal neutron-proton interaction whose role in low-energy nuclear fission had not been clarified, and demonstrates that the Bohr-Wheeler theory's assumption of insensitivity to scission dynamics can be realized microscopically. The results indicate that realistic density-functional calculations for induced fission will require large model spaces including neutron-proton interactions, motivating development of efficient truncation schemes or numerical methods for inverting large matrices.
