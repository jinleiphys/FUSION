# Can weakly bound heavy nuclei with very large neutron excess exist?

> A realistic model is suggested based on the quasiparticle Lagrange version of the self-consistent Finite Fermi Systems theory supplemented with the microscopically calculated surface parameters of the Landau-Migdal interaction amplitude. The latter are expressed in terms of the off-shell T-matrix of free NN-scattering and show a strong dependence on the chemical potential of a nucleus under consideration in the drip line vicinity. This effect could result in shifting the neutron drip line position to very large values of the neutron excess.

## Key claim

The paper suggests that the neutron drip line for heavy nuclei may be shifted to much larger neutron excess than previously predicted, because the external (surface) part of the Landau-Migdal interaction amplitude depends strongly on the neutron chemical potential near the drip line, which in turn deepens the neutron potential well. The authors present this as a qualitative possibility rather than a definitive prediction, noting that the effect could be less pronounced after corrections to their approximate calculation.

## Method

The authors use the quasiparticle Lagrange version of self-consistent Finite Fermi Systems (FFS) theory, supplemented with microscopically calculated surface parameters of the Landau-Migdal amplitude derived from the off-shell T-matrix of free NN-scattering. They perform self-consistent calculations for a chain of tin isotopes, comparing results from a phenomenological Lagrangian (with energy-independent forces) against a semi-microscopic Lagrangian where the external neutron-neutron amplitude \(f^{\exx}_{nn}\) depends on the neutron chemical potential via \(E = 2\mu_n\). Pairing is treated in a simplified BCS-like scheme with a limited basis and volume pairing interaction.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{150}\)Sn: \(f^{\exx}_{nn} = -1.4\), \(V_0 = -15\) MeV, \(\mu_n = -3.4\) MeV, leading to \(\delta \mu_n = \delta \mu_n^0 / 3.2\).
- For \(^{200}\)Sn: \(f^{\exx}_{nn} = -1.66\), \(V_0 = -22.4\) MeV, \(\mu_n = -2.0\) MeV, leading to \(\delta \mu_n = \delta \mu_n^0 / 6.5\).
- The phenomenological calculations for tin isotopes give a maximum mass \(A_{\text{max}} = 176\), corresponding to asymmetry parameter \(y = 0.43\), which exceeds the critical value \(y_0 = 0.37\) predicted for asymmetric nuclear matter.
- The semi-microscopic calculation predicts the existence of tin isotopes beyond \(A = 176\); calculations were interrupted at \(A = 208\).
- The external amplitudes from the T-matrix calculation are \(f^{\exx} = -2.6\) and \(f'^{\exx} = 1.56\), compared to the phenomenological values \(\lambda_{00} = -3.25\) and \(\lambda'_{00} = 2.4\) from Khodel and Saperstein (1982).
- The adjustable parameter \(\gamma\) was set to 1.6 (instead of 3.2 in earlier work) to reproduce the single-particle spectrum of \(^{124}\)Sn.

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent Finite Fermi Systems theory of Khodel and Saperstein (1982) and the quasiparticle Lagrange method of Zverev and Saperstein (1998, 1999) by incorporating a microscopically calculated energy dependence of the Landau-Migdal amplitude. It contrasts with phenomenological approaches such as those of Dobaczewski et al. (1996) and Fayans et al. (2000), which use energy-independent forces and predict a maximum tin isotope at \(A = 176\). The paper suggests that the energy dependence of the interaction could shift the neutron drip line to much larger masses, but notes that a more consistent microscopic theory (based on Brueckner theory for finite nuclei, in progress) is needed to confirm the effect.
