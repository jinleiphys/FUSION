# Single-particle decomposition of nuclear surface diffuseness

> Nuclear surface diffuseness reflects spectroscopic information near the Fermi level. I propose a way to decompose the surface diffuseness into single-particle (s.p.) contributions in a quantitative way. Systematic behavior of the surface diffuseness of neutron-rich even-even O, Ca, Ni, Sn, and Pb isotopes is analyzed with a phenomenological mean-field approach. The role of the s.p. wave functions near the Fermi level is explored: The nodeless s.p. orbits form a sharp nuclear surface, while the nodal s.p. orbits contribute to diffusing the nuclear surface.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a quantitative decomposition of nuclear surface diffuseness into single-particle (s.p.) contributions, showing that nodeless s.p. orbits form a sharp nuclear surface while nodal s.p. orbits diffuse it, but the analysis is limited to spherical configurations from a phenomenological mean-field potential and does not include pairing correlations, which would smooth the kink structures at magic numbers.

## Method

The author generates single-particle density distributions from a Woods-Saxon mean-field potential for even-even O, Ca, Ni, Sn, and Pb isotopes, then decomposes the total surface diffuseness parameter \(a\) by relating the two-parameter Fermi (2pF) density to the sum of s.p. densities. The derivative of each s.p. density at the radius parameter \(R\) is evaluated as \(D_i = d\bar{\rho}_i/dr|_{r=R}\), and the total diffuseness is obtained from \(a = -\rho_0/4 (\sum_i D_i)^{-1}\). The validity of the density distributions is confirmed by comparing calculated total reaction cross sections and point-proton radii with experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The diffuseness parameter \(a\) for \(^{16}\)O is 0.460 fm, smaller than the standard empirical value of 0.54 fm.
- The average \(a\) value across all isotopes studied is 0.54 fm, consistent with the empirical value from Bohr and Mottelson (1969).
- The square root of the rms deviation between diffuseness parameters from the direct formula and the minimization method is 0.014 fm for all isotopes.
- The rms radius of the \(0f_{7/2}\) orbit in \(^{52}\)Ca is 4.33 fm, while in \(^{60}\)Ni it is 4.26 fm; for the \(1p_{3/2}\) orbit, the rms radii are 4.76 fm in \(^{52}\)Ca and 4.35 fm in \(^{60}\)Ni.
- The \(2s_{1/2}\) orbit in \(^{86}\)Ni has a s.p. energy of \(-0.888\) MeV and an rms s.p. radius of 7.60 fm, much larger than the matter radius of \(^{86}\)Ni (4.56 fm).
- The matter radius enhancement from \(^{22}\)O to \(^{24}\)O due to the \(1s_{1/2}\) orbit is 0.27 fm.

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of nuclear surface diffuseness by Hatakeyama (2018), who showed that diffuseness changes at major or subshell closures, and by Scamps (2013), who provided a systematic theoretical study. The decomposition method enables quantitative understanding of how specific s.p. orbits near the Fermi level shape the nuclear surface, which is relevant for interpreting interaction cross-section measurements of unstable nuclei such as those by Tanaka (2020) and for constraining the equation of state of nuclear matter from finite nuclear systems as discussed by Reinhard (2010) and Horiuchi (2017). The approach can be extended to include pairing correlations and deformation, which would smooth the kink structures seen in the present spherical mean-field results.
