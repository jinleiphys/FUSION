# Various Shades of Blue's Functions

> We discuss random matrix models in terms of elementary operations on Blue's functions (functional inverse of Green's functions). We show that such operations embody the essence of a number of physical phenomena whether at/or away from the critical points. We illustrate these assertions by borrowing on a number of recent results in effective QCD in vacuum and matter. We provide simple physical arguments in favor of the universality of the continuum QCD spectral oscillations, whether at zero virtuality, in the bulk of the spectrum or at the chiral critical points. We also discuss effective quantum systems of disorder with strong or weak dissipation (Hatano-Nelson localization).

## Key claim

The paper argues that Blue's functions (functional inverses of Green's functions) provide a unified and computationally simpler framework for analyzing random matrix models across diverse physical systems, but this approach relies on the validity of the 1/N expansion and breaks down in regimes where microscopic universality (e.g., near spectral edges or chiral critical points) requires reorganized scaling laws.

## Method

The authors develop elementary operations on Blue's functions, addition, multiplication, differentiation, and integration, for both hermitian and non-hermitian random matrix ensembles with arbitrary measures. They then apply these operations to effective QCD models (chiral random matrix models) and to quantum systems with disorder and dissipation (Hatano-Nelson localization), using the Blue's function formalism to derive eigenvalue distributions, spectral boundaries, and partition function zeros via conformal mapping techniques.

## Key numbers

- For the Gaussian hermitian ensemble, the Green's function is \( G(z) = \frac{1}{2}(z - \sqrt{z^2 - 4}) \), yielding Wigner's semicircle law \( 2\pi\nu(\lambda) = \sqrt{4 - \lambda^2} \) for the eigenvalue distribution.
- The addition law for Blue's functions reads \( B_{1+2}(z) = B_1(z) + B_2(z) - \frac{1}{z} \), with the Gaussian Blue's function being \( B_H(u) = u + 1/u \).
- The conformal mapping between hermitian and anti-hermitian Gaussian ensembles plus a deterministic matrix \( M \) is given by \( w = z - 2G_{H+M}(z) \), where \( w \) is a point in the complex plane for the non-hermitian ensemble.
- The density of Yang-Lee zeros along cusp lines is \( \varrho(z) = \frac{1}{4\pi} |G^{(i)} - G^{(j)}| \), normalized to 1 in the z-plane.
- For Gaussian randomness, the two-point correlator diverges at \( z^2 - 4 = 0 \) (Airy oscillations at spectral edges) and for chiral Gaussian randomness at \( z(z^2 - 4) = 0 \) (Bessel oscillations at zero virtuality).

## Context

This work extends Voiculescu's (1990s) free random variable formalism and Zee's (1990s) introduction of Blue's functions, providing a pedagogical synthesis with new applications. The paper connects random matrix techniques to effective QCD models (e.g., chiral random matrix models by Shuryak and Verbaarschot, 1990s) and to the Hatano-Nelson (1996) model of localization with dissipation. It enables systematic calculation of spectral properties in disordered systems and QCD-like theories, while identifying regimes where the 1/N expansion fails and microscopic universality (Airy, Bessel oscillations) takes over.
