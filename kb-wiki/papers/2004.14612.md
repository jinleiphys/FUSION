# Determining $B(E1)$ distributions of weakly bound nuclei from breakup cross sections using Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels calculations. Application to $^{11}$Be

> A novel method to extract the $B(E1)$ strength of a weakly bound nucleus from experimental Coulomb dissociation data is proposed. The method makes use of continuum discretized coupled channels (CDCC) calculations, in which both nuclear and Coulomb forces are taken into account to all orders. This is a crucial advantage with respect to the standard procedure based on the Equivalent Photon Method (EPM) which does not properly take into account nuclear distortion, higher order coupling effects, or Coulomb-nuclear interference terms. The procedure is applied to the $^{11}$Be nucleus using two sets of available experimental data at different energies, for which seemingly incompatible $B(E1)$ have been reported using the EPM. We show that the present procedure gives consistent $B(E1)$ strengths, thus solving the aforementioned long-standing discrepancy between the two measurements.

# Determining $B(E1)$ distributions of weakly bound nuclei from breakup cross sections using Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels calculations. Application to $^{11}$Be

## Key claim

The authors propose that the long-standing discrepancy between $B(E1)$ distributions of $^{11}$Be extracted from two different Coulomb dissociation experiments (at 69 MeV/u and 520 MeV/u) can be resolved by using Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels (CDCC) calculations instead of the standard Equivalent Photon Method (EPM), because the CDCC method accounts for nuclear distortion, higher-order coupling effects, and Coulomb-nuclear interference that the EPM neglects.

## Method

The authors use a particle-plus-rotor model (PRM) to describe $^{11}$Be as a neutron coupled to a $^{10}$Be core, including core excitation to the $2^+$ state. They perform extended Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels (XCDCC) calculations that incorporate both Coulomb and nuclear interactions to all orders, using optical model potentials for the $n$-$^{208}$Pb and $^{10}$Be-$^{208}$Pb systems appropriate for each incident energy. The model $B(E1)$ distribution is then adjusted by scaling the dipole Coulomb matrix elements by small factors $\delta(\varepsilon_i)$, and the resulting linear relationship between cross sections and $B(E1)$ values is used to extract experimental $B(E1)$ strengths from the measured breakup cross sections.

## Key numbers

- Two experimental datasets are analyzed: one at 69 MeV/u (RIKEN, Fukuda et al.) and one at 520 MeV/u (GSI, Palit et al.), both using the reaction $^{11}$Be on $^{208}$Pb.
- For the 69 MeV/u data, angular ranges considered are $0^\circ<\theta_\text{c.m.}<1.3^\circ$ ("safe Coulomb") and $0^\circ<\theta_\text{c.m.}<6^\circ$ (including nuclear effects).
- The deformation parameter $\beta_2 = 0.67$ is used for the $n$+$^{10}$Be central potential to account for coupling with the $2^+$ state of $^{10}$Be.
- Two structure model parameter sets (S3 and S5 from Sum07) are used, which produce different $B(E1)$ strengths.
- The XCDCC calculations for the 69 MeV/u data give significantly larger cross sections than EPM calculations, with the EPM angular distributions differing significantly from the data.
- For the 520 MeV/u data, the EPM distribution (with added nuclear breakup) largely overestimates the data, while XCDCC calculations show better agreement.

## Context

This work extends the standard Equivalent Photon Method (EPM) developed by Alder and Winther (1975) and Bertulani and Baur (1988), which has been the standard procedure for extracting $B(E1)$ distributions from Coulomb dissociation experiments. The method addresses a known discrepancy between $B(E1)$ distributions extracted from experiments by Palit et al. (2003) at GSI and Fukuda et al. (2004) at RIKEN, which were previously found to be incompatible, especially at low breakup energies. The authors note that a recent ab-initio calculation by Calci et al. (2016) using the no-core shell model with continuum (NCSMC) agrees with the RIKEN data, while an eikonal calculation by Moschini et al. (2019) for the GSI data overestimates the cross section at the peak. The procedure enables consistent extraction of $B(E1)$ distributions from experiments at different collision energies, resolving the previous discrepancy.
