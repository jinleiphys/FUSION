# Extracting the hadronization timescale in sqrt{s}=7 TeV proton-proton collisions from pion and kaon femtoscopy

> A hadronic rescattering model with the proper hadronization time width as the free parameter is compared with charged pion and charged and neutral kaon femtoscopy measurements from the LHC ALICE experiment for sqrt{s}=7 TeV proton-proton collisions. Comparisons between the model and measurements are made for one-dimensional source parameters in several charged multiplicity and transverse particle pair momentum bins. It is found that a reasonable description of the measured source parameters by the model is obtained for a hadronization proper time width of 0.4 +- 0.1 fm/c, which is in agreement with an estimate based on the uncertainty principle. The model calculations also suggest that 1) some form of collectivity is necessary to describe the multiplicity dependence of the measured radius parameters, and 2) the underlying physical size and timescale of the collision is significantly larger than what the extracted radius parameters and hadronization proper time width would imply.

## Key claim

A hadronic rescattering model with a Gaussian distribution of hadronization proper times can describe ALICE pion and kaon femtoscopy data from sqrt(s)=7 TeV proton-proton collisions, yielding a hadronization proper time width of 0.4 +/- 0.1 fm/c, but only if some form of collectivity (rescattering) is included to reproduce the multiplicity dependence of the measured radius parameters.

## Method

The author used PYTHIA 6.4 to generate initial particles and momenta for sqrt(s)=7 TeV proton-proton collisions, then assigned hadronization space-time points via Gaussian distributions for transverse radius (sigma_r = 1 fm) and proper time (width tau_had as free parameter). Particles were evolved in 0.25 fm/c time steps through two-body elastic and inelastic scatterings, decays, and nucleon-antinucleon annihilation until freeze-out, after which quantum statistics correlations were imposed on boson pairs. One-dimensional correlation functions in invariant momentum difference q_inv were constructed and fitted with a Gaussian to extract source radius R and lambda parameters, which were compared to ALICE measurements in several charged multiplicity and pair transverse momentum bins.

## Key numbers

* Hadronization proper time width tau_had = 0.4 +/- 0.1 fm/c, extracted from best agreement between model and ALICE data for sqrt(s)=7 TeV proton-proton collisions.
* Upper limit on hadronization proper time width from uncertainty principle: Delta tau <= 0.7 fm/c, based on pion rest mass as the lightest hadron.
* Model radius parameters R ~ 1 fm for charged pions, charged kaons, and neutral kaons in the N_ch = 1-11 multiplicity bin with tau_had = 0.4 fm/c.
* Initial energy density at midrapidity reaches approximately 2.4 GeV/fm^3 at the initial collision time, with energy density exceeding 1 GeV/fm^3 for t < 1.35 fm/c.
* Underlying physical freeze-out time widths are approximately 15 times larger than tau_had, and freeze-out radius widths are approximately 2 times larger than the extracted R parameters.

## Context

This work extends previous phenomenological studies of hadronization time in proton-proton collisions by Humanic (2006) and Truesdale (2010) at lower energies (sqrt(s)=0.2, 0.9, and 1.8 TeV), which extracted single hadronization proper times of 0.1-0.2 fm/c using delta-function distributions rather than Gaussian widths. It also improves upon a recent UrQMD model study that obtained a range of 0.3-0.8 fm/c for the single hadronization proper time in the same collision system. The finding that rescattering (collectivity) is needed to explain the multiplicity dependence of radius parameters supports suggestions of collective behavior in proton-proton collisions from other authors (Bozek 2009, Werner 2011, Kisiel 2010, Chajecki 2009). The results enable future comparisons with upgraded LHC data at sqrt(s)=14 TeV and motivate studies of penetrating probes such as direct photons and dileptons to characterize the early stages of these collisions.
