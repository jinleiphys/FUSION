# Temperature Dependence of Gluon and Ghost Propagators in a Dyson-Schwinger Equations context

> We investigate the finite-temperature structure of ghost and gluon propagators within an approach based on the rainbow truncated Dyson-Schwinger equations in Landau gauge. The method, early used for modeling the quark, ghost and gluon propagators in vacuum, is extended to finite temperatures. In Euclidean space, within the Matsubara imaginary-time formalism it is necessary to distinguish between the transversal and longitudinal, with respect to the heat bath, gluon dressing functions, for which the Dyson-Schwinger equation splits into a corresponding system of coupled equations. This system is considered within the rainbow approximation generalized to finite temperatures and solved numerically. The solutions for the ghost and gluon propagators are obtained as functions of temperature $T$, Matsubara frequency $Ω_n$ and three-momentum squared ${\bf k}^2$. It is found that, for zero Matsubara frequency, the dependence of the ghost and gluon dressing functions on ${\bf k}^2$ are not sensitive to the temperature $T$, while at ${\bf k}^2=0$ their dependence on $T$ is quite strong. Dependence on the Matsubara frequency $Ω_n$ is investigated as well.The performed numerical analysis of the solution of the Dyson-Schwinger equations shows that at certain value of the temperature $T_0\sim 150$ MeV the iteration procedure does not longer converge. In the vicinity of $T_0$ the longitudinal gluon propagator increases quite fastly, whereas the transversal propagator does not exhibit any irregularity. This in a qualitative agreement with results obtained within the QCD lattice calculations in this temperature interval.

## Key claim

The paper claims that, within a rainbow-truncated Dyson-Schwinger equation approach at finite temperature, the ghost and gluon dressing functions at zero Matsubara frequency show no sensitivity to temperature as a function of three-momentum squared, while at zero three-momentum their temperature dependence is strong; however, the iteration procedure fails to converge above a temperature of approximately 150 MeV, which the authors interpret as a qualitative signal of the deconfinement transition.

## Method

The authors extended a previously developed rainbow-truncated Dyson-Schwinger equation formalism, originally used for vacuum propagators, to finite temperatures using the Matsubara imaginary-time formalism in Euclidean space. They derived a coupled system of integral equations for the ghost dressing function and the transverse and longitudinal gluon dressing functions, replacing dressed vertices and propagators in the interaction kernels by bare quantities augmented with Gaussian form factors whose parameters were fixed by a fit to zero-temperature lattice QCD data. The resulting one-dimensional integral equations with summations over Matsubara frequencies were solved numerically via an iteration procedure on a Gaussian quadrature grid.

## Key numbers

- The iteration procedure ceases to converge at a temperature \(T_0 \sim 150\) MeV.
- The gluon renormalization constants used are \(Z_3 = \tilde Z_3 \approx 1.065\), taken from a previous fit to zero-temperature lattice data.
- The effective parameters for the three-gluon loop include \(D_{11}^T = D_{11}^L = 0.462\) GeV\(^2\), \(\omega_{11}^T = \omega_{11}^L = 1.095\) GeV, \(D_{12}^T = D_{12}^L = 0.116\) GeV\(^2\), and \(\omega_{12}^T = \omega_{12}^L = 2.15\) GeV.
- The ghost-loop parameters include \(D_{21}^T = D_{21}^L = 7.7\) GeV\(^2\), \(\omega_{21}^T = \omega_{21}^L = 0.58\) GeV, \(D_{22}^T = D_{22}^L = 0.25\) GeV\(^2\), and \(\omega_{22}^T = \omega_{22}^L = 4.5\) GeV.
- The ghost-gluon loop parameters include \(\omega_{31}^T = \omega_{31}^L = 0.73\) GeV, \(\omega_{32}^T = \omega_{32}^L = 2.16\) GeV, \(D_{31}^T = D_{31}^L = 0.39\pi\) GeV\(^2\), and \(D_{32}^T = D_{32}^L = 0.1\pi\) GeV\(^2\).

## Context

This work extends the rainbow-truncated Dyson-Schwinger approach previously applied to vacuum propagators (Kaptari, 2020) to finite temperatures, building on the Matsubara formalism used in earlier finite-temperature studies such as those by S.-X. Qin et al. (2011) and Blank and Krassnigg (2011). The observed loss of convergence near 150 MeV and the rapid rise of the longitudinal gluon propagator in that vicinity qualitatively agree with lattice QCD results for SU(2) gauge theory (Bornyakov et al., 2004; Ilgenfritz et al., 2017), suggesting the approach may capture the deconfinement transition. The method enables future studies of meson and glueball properties at finite temperature within a relatively simple phenomenological framework.
