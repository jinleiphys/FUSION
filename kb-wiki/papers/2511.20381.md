# Matrix approximations of operators

> The approximate representation of operators by finite matrices is analysed in terms of accuracy and convergence. The identity operator, for example, can be reconstructed using a basis of harmonic oscillator states leading to a narrow peak approximation of the $δ$ function, but this peak may be perturbed by small, residual, oscillations. The peak does not shrink nor grows quickly, and the oscillations only diminish slowly as the size of the matrix increases. For the kinetic energy operator, a triple peak (one positive, two negative) representation of $-δ''$ is obtained, but that is affected also by residual oscillations. Again, convergence is slow as the matrix dimension increases. We find compact formulas to explain such oscillations. Similar observations are found for representations of local interactions, while separable potentials are better represented. As a comparison, in the context of a toy model, the effects of choosing an alternative single particle basis are studied. A formal approach for the approximation of operators is considered for comparison. We conclude with a word of caution for (finite) matrix approximations of operators.

## Key claim

The paper argues that finite matrix approximations of operators using harmonic oscillator bases converge slowly and exhibit persistent residual oscillations, meaning that the accuracy of such approximations is fundamentally limited and should be treated with caution, especially for local operators like the identity and kinetic energy.

## Method

The authors constructed finite-rank kernels by projecting the identity operator, kinetic energy operator, and various potentials onto truncated harmonic oscillator bases of dimension N (typically N=50, 100, 150, 200). They analyzed the convergence of these kernels by examining crest profiles, cut shapes, and weight integrals as functions of the coordinate variables r and s, and compared results for different basis choices including shifted Gaussians and symmetrized Gaussian pairs. Compact formulas based on the Christoffel-Darboux relation were derived to explain the observed oscillatory behavior.

## Key numbers

- For N=50, the cut weight w_50(s) oscillates between approximately 0.92 and 1.08; for N=200, w_200(s) still oscillates between approximately 0.96 and 1.04
- For the kinetic energy operator reconstruction with N=50, the weight oscillates with amplitude approximately 4; for N=200, the amplitude grows to approximately 8
- For the moderate-range potential v(r) = -e^{-r^2}, the crest ratio error is at most approximately 6% for N=50 and reduces to 3% for N=200
- For the short-range potential v(r) = e^{-9r^2}, the crest ratio error is approximately 17% for N=50 and reduces to 8% for N=200
- The ground state eigenvalue of the toy Hamiltonian h = -d^2/dr^2 + e^{-9r^2} - e^{-r^2} is epsilon_ex = -0.1720763; the harmonic oscillator basis gives epsilon_50 = -0.171874 and epsilon_100 = -0.172071
- Using 25 pairs of symmetrically shifted Gaussians, the approximate ground state eigenvalue is epsilon_25 = -0.17194, better than the harmonic oscillator result at N=50
- The r^2 operator reconstruction at the origin gives a crest ratio of exactly 1/2 independent of N; at r=1,2,3 the crest ratios fluctuate about 1.5, 4.5, and 9.5 respectively

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Karataglidis and Amos (2008) on convergence problems in truncated basis expansions, and relates to the Feshbach projection formalism (Feshbach, 1958, 1962) for splitting Hilbert spaces into subspaces. The findings serve as a caution for practitioners using finite matrix approximations in nuclear structure calculations, particularly when representing local operators with continuous spectra. The paper suggests that alternative basis choices, such as shifted Gaussians or symmetrized Gaussian pairs, can improve convergence for specific problems, and that the full Hamiltonian may converge better than its individual operator components.
