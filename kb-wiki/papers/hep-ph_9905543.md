# Effective Field Theory Approach to Pionium

> The various dynamical scales below the pion mass involved in $π^{+}$ $π^{-}$ atoms are sequentially integrated out using non-relativistic effective field theory techniques. This allows us to systematically organise the corrections to the energy levels and decay width. We present our results in terms of a single unknown constant which may be obtained by matching to the Chiral Lagrangian with electromagnetic interactions at two loops.

# Effective Field Theory Approach to Pionium

## Key claim

The authors present a systematic non-relativistic effective field theory treatment of pionium (π⁺π⁻ atoms) by sequentially integrating out dynamical scales below the pion mass, organizing corrections to energy levels and decay width in terms of a single unknown constant that may be obtained by matching to the Chiral Lagrangian with electromagnetic interactions at two loops, but this two-loop matching calculation has not yet been carried out.

## Method

The authors start from the Chiral Lagrangian coupled to electromagnetism and sequentially integrate out three dynamical scales: first the pion mass m (~140 MeV), producing a non-relativistic EFT for pion pairs near threshold; then the charged-neutral pion mass difference Δm (~5 MeV), integrating out neutral pions to obtain a theory of charged pions only; finally the soft scale mα/2 (~0.5 MeV), integrating out electrons and transverse photons to obtain a potential non-relativistic theory equivalent to quantum mechanics with Coulomb and vacuum polarization potentials. The bound state energy and decay width are then calculated using perturbation theory on Coulomb wave functions, with dimensional regularization and MS scheme for UV divergences.

## Context

This work extends the effective field theory approach previously applied to QED bound states (NRQED, pNRQED) by Lepage (1992) and to positronium by Labelle (1998) to the hadronic atom pionium, where strong interactions play a crucial role. It improves upon earlier non-relativistic treatments by Rasche (1970s), Ravndal (1990s), and Holstein (1990s) by providing a systematic power-counting scheme that avoids the need for coupled-channel approaches and clarifies which resummations are justified. The results are directly relevant to the DIRAC experiment at CERN, which aims to measure the pionium decay width at 10% accuracy to extract pion-pion scattering lengths; the formalism enables error estimates and identifies the two-loop Chiral Lagrangian matching calculation (yet to be performed) as the necessary next step to determine the remaining unknown constant.
