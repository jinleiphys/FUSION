# $B_c$ enhancement with non-thermalized bottom quarks in nuclear collisions at Large Hadron Collider

> We study $B_c$ production in high-energy nuclear collisions in a transport approach with dissociation and regeneration at finite temperatures. Due to the rare production in p+p collisions and the strong combination of uncorrelated $c$ and $\bar b$ quarks in the quark-gluon plasma, the $B_c$ yield is significantly enhanced in the nuclear collisions at the Large Hadron Collider. Moreover, the centrality and momentum-dependent yield of $B_c$ sensitively reflect the thermalization degree of bottom quarks. And the newly observed experimental data favors a far from the thermal bottom quark distribution in the quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim

The $B_c$ meson yield in Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies is significantly enhanced relative to p+p collisions due to strong regeneration from uncorrelated charm and bottom quarks in the quark-gluon plasma, and the centrality and momentum dependence of this enhancement favors a bottom quark distribution that is far from thermal equilibrium, as described by a Langevin equation rather than a thermal distribution.

## Method

The authors solve a Boltzmann transport equation for $B_c$ mesons in a (2+1)-dimensional hydrodynamic background (MUSIC package) with an equation of state matching lattice QCD at high temperature and hadron resonance gas at low temperature, using a crossover at $T_c = 170$ MeV. The loss and gain terms in the transport equation are computed from gluon dissociation and its inverse process, with cross sections derived via operator-product-expansion (OPE) methods extended to D-wave states for the first time. The initial $B_c$ distribution in nuclear collisions is obtained from a geometrical superposition of p+p collisions (using the BCVEGPY2.2 generator) with cold nuclear matter effects (shadowing via EPS09 and Cronin broadening), while charm quarks are taken as thermally equilibrated and bottom quarks are modeled with three scenarios: thermal, pQCD (free-streaming), and Langevin dynamics with spatial diffusion coefficient $2\pi T D_s \approx 4$.

## Key numbers

- Melting temperature for $B_c(1S)$: $T_D \approx 2.1 T_c$ (with $T_c = 170$ MeV), comparable to $J/\psi$ melting temperature.
- Melting temperature for excited states $B_c(1P)$, $B_c(1D)$, $B_c(2S)$: $T_D \approx 1.3 T_c$.
- Wavefunction at origin for $B_c(1S)$: $R_{00}(0) = 1.163$ GeV$^{3/2}$; for $B_c(2S)$: $R_{20}(0) = -0.970$ GeV$^{3/2}$; first derivative for $B_c(1P)$: $R'_{01}(0) = 0.382$ GeV$^{5/2}$; second derivative for $B_c(1D)$: $R''_{02}(0) = 0.197$ GeV$^{7/2}$.
- Total $B_c(1S)$ cross section in p+p collisions at $\sqrt{s}=5.02$ TeV (with feeddown): $\sigma^{B_c(1S)}_{pp} \approx 140$ nb; central rapidity ($|y|<2$) cross section: $d\sigma^{B_c(1S)}_{pp}/dy \approx 26$ nb.
- Charm quark production cross section in p+p at $\sqrt{s}=5.02$ TeV: $d\sigma^{c\bar c}_\text{pp}/d\eta = 1.165$ mb.
- Bottom quark production cross section in p+p at $\sqrt{s}=5.02$ TeV: $d\sigma^{b\bar{b}}_\text{pp}/d\eta = 45$ $\mu$b.
- Decay branching ratio $B(B_c\to J/\psi\mu^+v_{\mu})$ used to fit CMS data: 6%.

## Context

This work extends prior transport studies of heavy quarkonium in nuclear collisions, such as those for $J/\psi$ and $\Upsilon$ by Yan et al. (2006) and Chen et al. (2017), to the $B_c$ system, which is unique because it requires two different heavy quark flavors. The authors compare their results to the first $B_c$ observation in Pb+Pb collisions by the CMS Collaboration (2022), finding that the $B_c$ nuclear modification factor $R_{AA}$ is generally larger than unity, in contrast to $J/\psi$ and $\Upsilon$ where $R_{AA}$ is typically less than or at most unity. The sensitivity of $B_c$ production to bottom quark thermalization enables a new probe of heavy quark energy loss in the quark-gluon plasma, and the authors conclude that bottom quarks are far from thermal equilibrium even in central collisions, consistent with the Langevin description used in previous work by Chen et al. (2017) and lattice results by Altenkort et al. (2023).
