# Synthesis mechanism of superheavy element 120: a dinuclear system model approach with microscopic inputs

> The dinuclear system model incorporates several essential input physical quantities, including nuclear mass, fission barrier, shell correction energy, level density parameter, and shell damping factor, etc., which are derived from diverse nuclear structure models. To achieve theoretical consistency, we try to generate these essential input physical quantities from the finite-temperature covariant density functional theory using PC-PK1 energy density functional, with pairing correlations treated via the BCS approach. With microscopically determined input parameters, the dinuclear system model can successfully reproduce experimental results for: (i) cold fusion reaction systems ($^{48}$Ca + $^{204,206-208}$Pb $\rightarrow$ $^{252,254-256}$No$^*$), and (ii) hot fusion reaction systems ($^{48}$Ca + $^{239,240,242,244}$Pu $\rightarrow$ $^{287,288,290,292}$Fl$^*$). Furthermore, we perform calculations for the fusion reactions $^{50}$Ti+$^{249}$Cf, $^{51}$V+$^{249}$Bk, $^{54}$Cr+$^{248}$Cm, and $^{55}$Mn+$^{243}$Am, targeting the synthesis of element 120. It is found that the maximum synthesis cross section for these four reactions are 48.20 fb, 12.33 fb, 5.25 fb, 0.47 fb corresponding to $^{50}$Ti($^{249}$Cf,4n)$^{295}$120 at $E^*_{\rm CN}$ = 41 MeV, $^{51}$V($^{249}$Bk,3n)$^{297}$120 at $E^*_{\rm CN}$ = 34 MeV, $^{54}$Cr($^{248}$Cm,3n)$^{299}$120 at $E^*_{\rm CN}$ = 32 MeV, $^{55}$Mn($^{243}$Am,5n)$^{293}$120 at $E^*_{\rm CN}$ = 53 MeV, respectively.

## Key claim

The dinuclear system model, when supplied with input physical quantities (masses, fission barriers, shell correction energies, level density parameters, and shell damping factors) derived consistently from finite-temperature covariant density functional theory using the PC-PK1 energy density functional, can reproduce experimental cross sections for cold and hot fusion reactions and predict synthesis cross sections for element 120; the predictive accuracy depends critically on using microscopically consistent inputs rather than inputs from disparate nuclear structure models.

## Method

The authors used finite-temperature covariant density functional theory with the PC-PK1 energy density functional and BCS pairing to compute free energy surfaces, excitation energies, entropies, and level density parameters for compound nuclei of No, Fl, and element 120 isotopes. They extracted asymptotic level density parameters and shell damping factors by fitting the phenomenological Ignatyuk formula to the calculated level density parameters, and obtained fission barriers and neutron separation energies from the same microscopic framework. These quantities were then used as inputs to the dinuclear system model, which solves master equations for nucleon transfer and calculates capture, fusion, and survival probabilities to obtain evaporation residue cross sections.

## Key numbers

* Maximum synthesis cross section for $^{50}$Ti($^{249}$Cf,4n)$^{295}$120: 48.20 fb at compound nucleus excitation energy $E^*_{\rm CN}$ = 41 MeV.
* Maximum synthesis cross section for $^{51}$V($^{249}$Bk,3n)$^{297}$120: 12.33 fb at $E^*_{\rm CN}$ = 34 MeV.
* Maximum synthesis cross section for $^{54}$Cr($^{248}$Cm,3n)$^{299}$120: 5.25 fb at $E^*_{\rm CN}$ = 32 MeV.
* Maximum synthesis cross section for $^{55}$Mn($^{243}$Am,5n)$^{293}$120: 0.47 fb at $E^*_{\rm CN}$ = 53 MeV.
* The model successfully reproduced experimental data for cold fusion reactions $^{48}$Ca + $^{204,206-208}$Pb $\rightarrow$ $^{252,254-256}$No$^*$ and hot fusion reactions $^{48}$Ca + $^{239,240,242,244}$Pu $\rightarrow$ $^{287,288,290,292}$Fl$^*$.

## Context

This work extends the dinuclear system model approach of Feng (2006, 2007) and Bao (2015) by replacing inputs from disparate models (masses from the finite-range droplet model of Moller (2016), fission barriers from the macroscopic-microscopic model of Myers (1974), and empirical level density parameters $a = A/12$) with microscopically consistent inputs from covariant density functional theory. The results enable experimental planning for synthesizing element 120 at facilities such as JINR, GSI, RIKEN, and LBNL, and move toward a self-consistent structure-based reaction framework that could be extended to other superheavy nuclei.
