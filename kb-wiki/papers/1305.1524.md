# Pseudospin symmetry in single particle resonances in spherical square wells

> The pseudospin symmetry (PSS) has been studied extensively for bound states. Recently we justified rigorously that the PSS in single particle resonant states is exactly conserved when the attractive scalar and repulsive vector potentials of the Dirac Hamiltonian have the same magnitude but opposite sign [PRL 109, 072501 (2012)]. To understand more deeply the PSS, we focus on several issues related to the exact conservation and breaking mechanism of the PSS in single particle resonances. In particular, we are interested in how the energy and width splittings of PS partners depend on the depth of the scalar and vector potentials. We investigate the asymptotic behaviors of radial Dirac wave functions. Spherical square well potentials are employed in which the PSS breaking part in the Jost function can be well isolated. By examining the zeros of Jost functions corresponding to small components of the radial Dirac wave functions, general properties of the PSS are analyzed. By examining the Jost function, the occurrence of intruder orbitals is explained and it is possible to trace continuously the PSS partners from the PSS limit to the case with a finite potential depth. The dependence of the PSS in resonances as well as in bound states on the potential depth is investigated systematically. We find a threshold effect in the energy splitting and an anomaly in the width splitting of pseudospin partners when the depth of the single particle potential varies from zero to a finite value. The conservation and the breaking of the PSS in resonant states and bound states share some similar properties. The appearance of intruder states can be explained by examining the zeros of Jost functions. Origins of the threshold effect in the energy splitting and the anomaly in the width splitting of PS partners, together with many other problems, are still open and should be further investigated.

## Key claim

The pseudospin symmetry (PSS) in single particle resonant states is exactly conserved under the same condition as for bound states (Σ(r)=0 or dΣ(r)/dr=0), but in realistic nuclei this condition cannot be satisfied and the PSS is always broken; the authors use spherical square well potentials to isolate the PSS-breaking part in the Jost function and study how energy and width splittings depend on potential depth.

## Method

The authors employed spherical square well potentials for the scalar potential Σ(r) and vector potential Δ(r) in the Dirac Hamiltonian, allowing the PSS-breaking term in the Jost function to be separated from the PSS-conserving term. By examining the zeros of Jost functions corresponding to the small components of radial Dirac wave functions, they analyzed the general properties of PSS for both bound states and resonant states. The dependence of PSS on potential depth was investigated systematically by varying the depth from zero to a finite value.

## Key numbers

- For 208Pb, the square well potentials have depths of approximately 650 MeV for Δ(r) and 66 MeV for Σ(r), with a radius around 7 fm
- For bound states with pseudo orbital angular momentum l̃=1 (p̃), there are two bound states for d3/2 (κ=2) and three bound states for s1/2 (κ=-1), demonstrating that there is always one bound state with negative κ that does not have a partner (intruder state)
- The Jost function analysis shows zero points at E=-66 MeV for all Jost functions except that with κ=-1 (J-1(E)), which do not correspond to physical states

## Context

This work extends the rigorous justification of PSS in single particle resonant states previously established by the same authors (Lu, Zhao, and Zhou, 2012) in Physical Review Letters 109, 072501. It builds on the foundational work of Ginocchio (1997) who showed PSS is a relativistic symmetry of the Dirac Hamiltonian, and Meng et al. (1998) who connected PSS to the condition dΣ(r)/dr=0. The study uses square well potentials as a solvable model to investigate open problems about the conservation and breaking mechanism of PSS in resonances, with the origins of the threshold effect in energy splitting and anomaly in width splitting remaining open for further investigation.
