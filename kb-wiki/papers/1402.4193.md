# Is gauge-invariant complete decomposition of the nucleon spin possible ?

> Is gauge-invariant complete decomposition of the nucleon spin possible? Although it is a difficult theoretical question which has not reached a complete consensus yet, a general agreement now is that there are at least two physically inequivalent gauge-invariant decompositions (I) and (II) of the nucleon. %The one is a nontrivial gauge-invariant %generalization of the Jaffe-Manohar decomposition. %The other is an extension of the Ji decomposition, which allows %a gauge-invariant decomposition of the total gluon angular %momentum into the intrinsic spin and orbital parts. In these two decompositions, the intrinsic spin parts of quarks and gluons are just common. What discriminate these two decompositions are the orbital angular momentum parts. The orbital angular momenta of quarks and gluons appearing in the decomposition (I) are the so-called "mechanical" orbital angular momenta, while those appearing in the decomposition (II) are the generalized (gauge-invariant) "canonical" ones. By this reason, these decompositions are also called the "mechanical" and "canonical" decompositions of the nucleon spin, respectively. A crucially important question is which decomposition is more favorable from the observational viewpoint. The main objective of this concise review is to try to answer this question with careful consideration of recent intensive researches on this problem.

## Key claim

The paper argues that there are at least two physically inequivalent gauge-invariant decompositions of the nucleon spin, the "mechanical" and "canonical" decompositions, and that the question of which is more favorable from an observational viewpoint can be answered by examining their relation to deep-inelastic-scattering observables, though a complete consensus has not yet been reached.

## Method

The author reviews the history of the nucleon spin decomposition problem, contrasting the Jaffe-Manohar decomposition (gauge-variant) with the Ji decomposition (gauge-invariant but not fully decomposed), then examines the gauge-invariant-extension (GIE) approach proposed by Chen et al. and the alternative decomposition proposed by Wakamatsu. The paper analyzes the "seemingly" covariant generalizations of these decompositions and establishes explicit relations between the mechanical and canonical orbital angular momenta and high-energy deep-inelastic-scattering observables such as generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and polarized parton distribution functions (PDFs).

## Key numbers

- The intrinsic quark spin portion of the nucleon spin is around 1/3, based on high-statistics measurements by COMPASS (2005, 2007) and HERMES (2007).
- The quark orbital angular momentum in the mechanical decomposition is given by L_q = (1/2) ∫ x [H^q(x,0,0) + E^q(x,0,0)] dx - (1/2) ∫ Δq(x) dx.
- The gluon orbital angular momentum in the mechanical decomposition is given by L_G = (1/2) ∫ x [H^G(x,0,0) + E^G(x,0,0)] dx - ∫ Δg(x) dx.

## Context

This review extends the long-standing debate on nucleon spin decomposition that began with the EMC Collaboration measurements in 1987 and the subsequent Jaffe-Manohar (1990) and Ji (1997, 1998) decompositions. It directly addresses the recent controversies sparked by Chen et al. (2008, 2009) and the subsequent work by Wakamatsu (2010, 2011), Leader and Lorce (2013), and others. The paper enables future work by clarifying that the quark orbital angular momentum extracted from combined GPD and polarized PDF analysis is the mechanical OAM, not the canonical OAM, and that the gluon OAM extracted similarly contains a potential angular momentum contribution.
