# Relaxation time for quark spin and thermal vorticity alignment in heavy-ion collisions

> We compute the relaxation time for quark/antiquark spin and thermal vorticity alignment in a quark-gluon plasma at finite temperature and quark chemical potential. We model the interaction of quark/antiquark spin with thermal vorticity as driven by a phenomenological modification of the elementary quark interaction with gluons. We find that in a scenario where the angular velocity of the quark-gluon plasma produced in a peripheral heavy-ion collision is small, quarks/antiquarks take a long time to align their spin with the vorticity. However, when the angular velocity created in the reaction is large, the alignment is efficient and well within the lifetime of the system created in the reaction. The relaxation time is larger for antiquarks which points out to a difference for the polarization of hadrons and antihadrons when this alignment is preserved during hadronization.

## Key claim

The relaxation time for quark/antiquark spin and thermal vorticity alignment depends critically on the magnitude of the angular velocity of the quark-gluon plasma: when the angular velocity is small (as estimated from Hijing and AMPT simulations), alignment takes longer than the system lifetime except at the highest temperatures and chemical potentials, but when the angular velocity is large (as in an extreme scenario where all initial angular momentum is retained), alignment is efficient and well within the system lifetime.

## Method

The authors compute the interaction rate for quark spin and thermal vorticity alignment from a one-loop quark self-energy diagram in the imaginary-time formalism of thermal field theory, using a phenomenological vertex that couples the quark spin operator to the thermal vorticity tensor. The effective gluon propagator is taken in the Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) approximation at finite temperature and quark chemical potential, and the interaction rate is integrated over phase space to obtain the total rate, whose inverse gives the relaxation time. The calculation is performed for massless quarks, with the angular velocity estimated from two scenarios: one using Hijing and AMPT simulations (small vorticity) and one using UrQMD simulations (large vorticity, assuming rigid rotation).

## Key numbers

- For semicentral Au+Au collisions at impact parameter b = 10 fm, the small-vorticity scenario gives angular velocities ω ≈ 0.12 fm⁻¹ at √s_NN = 10 GeV and ω ≈ 0.10 fm⁻¹ at √s_NN = 200 GeV (from Hijing/AMPT simulations at Δt = 0.4 fm after full overlap).
- For the large-vorticity scenario (UrQMD simulations at Δt = 0.4 fm after full overlap), ω ≈ 0.23 fm⁻¹ at √s_NN = 10 GeV and ω ≈ 0.46 fm⁻¹ at √s_NN = 200 GeV.
- In the small-vorticity scenario, the relaxation time τ ≲ 3 fm only for the largest temperature and chemical potential considered; for μ ∼ 0, τ exceeds the system lifetime of approximately 10 fm.
- In the large-vorticity scenario, τ ≲ 3 fm for all temperatures and chemical potentials considered, well within the system lifetime.
- For antiquarks in the small-vorticity scenario, τ ≲ 10 fm only for the largest temperatures; in the large-vorticity scenario, τ ≲ 10 fm for T above 120 MeV for all chemical potentials considered.
- The strong coupling constant is fixed at α_s = 0.3 throughout the analysis.

## Context

This work extends the recent estimate of the relaxation time for strange quark spin and vorticity alignment by Kapusta (2019), which found that the relaxation time was too large when driven by vorticity fluctuations or helicity flip from interactions with light quarks and gluons. The present calculation introduces a phenomenological vertex to model the direct coupling between quark spin and thermal vorticity, finding that alignment can be efficient if a large fraction of the initial angular momentum is retained by the quark-gluon plasma. The results motivate further study of the transfer of quark polarization to hadron polarization during hadronization, and suggest that a difference between hadron and antihadron polarization may be expected at large quark chemical potential, relevant for experiments such as NICA.
