# Transmission coefficients in compound-nucleus reaction theory

> A recent article (D.A. Brown, et al., Phys. Rev. C98 024616 (2018)) rproposed a modification of the cross section formula used in practical calculations of compound nucleus reactions. We discuss the main concepts and approximations of statistical reaction theory and conclude that the standard practical implementations of the cross section formula remain the preferred choice.

## Key claim

The standard Hauser-Feshbach plus width-fluctuation-correction formula, as implemented in practical codes, remains the preferred choice for compound-nucleus reaction cross sections; a recently proposed modification that replaces transmission coefficients \(T_c\) by \(T_c/\sqrt{1-T_c}\) deviates from the underlying GOE statistical theory at larger coupling parameters.

## Method

The authors benchmark the standard and modified cross-section formulas against the exact GOE (Gaussian orthogonal ensemble) theory for compound-nucleus reactions. They compute the ratio of the cross section from each formula to the GOE cross section for a two-channel reaction, varying the transmission coefficient in one channel while holding the other fixed, and then vice versa.

## Key numbers

- For a two-channel reaction with \(T_b = 0.26\), the ratio of the standard formula cross section to the GOE cross section remains close to 1 as \(x_a\) increases from 0.1; the modified formula using \(f_c = T_c/\sqrt{1-T_c}\) gives higher ratios, indicating deviations from GOE theory.
- For a two-channel reaction with \(T_a = 0.39\), the standard formula again gives very good agreement with GOE theory, while the modified formula leads to a reduction in the ratio at higher values of \(x_b\).
- The transmission coefficient \(T_c\) is related to the dimensionless coupling parameter \(x_c\) by \(T_c = 4x_c/(1+x_c)^2\), and this relation holds for any value of \(x_c\).

## Context

This work responds to a recent proposal by Brown et al. (2018) to modify the Hauser-Feshbach plus width-fluctuation-correction formula by replacing \(T_c\) with \(T_c/\sqrt{1-T_c}\). The authors extend the earlier benchmarking approach of Kawano et al. (2014, 2015) and show that the standard formula, based on Moldauer's parametrization (1980), agrees well with the exact GOE theory of Weidenmüller (1985) and Mitchell et al. (2010), while the modified formula does not. The results enable practitioners to retain the standard implementation in codes such as those following the historical development of statistical reaction theory.
