# Insights into the pi- p -> eta n reaction mechanism

> A dynamical coupled-channels formalism is used to investigate the $η-$meson production mechanism on the proton induced by pions, in the total center-of-mass energy region from threshold up to 2 GeV. We show how and why studying exclusively total cross section data might turn out to be misleading in pinning down the reaction mechanism.

## Key claim

The double-bump structure in the total cross section of the reaction π⁻p → ηn is dominated by the interference between the S₁₁(1535) and S₁₁(1650) resonances, but relying solely on total cross section data is misleading because those two S-wave resonances are insufficient to reproduce the differential cross section data.

## Method

A dynamical coupled-channels formalism derived from projection operator techniques was applied, with nonresonant interactions obtained from a unitary transformation method using phenomenological Lagrangians. The model included intermediate πN, ηN, πΔ, σN, and ρN channels and all three- and four-star resonances with mass ≤ 2 GeV: S₁₁(1535), S₁₁(1650), P₁₁(1440), P₁₁(1710), P₁₃(1720), D₁₃(1520), D₁₃(1700), D₁₅(1675), and F₁₅(1680). The model was fitted exclusively to differential cross section data for π⁻p → ηn up to W ≈ 2 GeV, yielding a reduced χ² of 1.96, with total cross section results being predictions from that fit.

## Key numbers

- Total cross section exhibits two bumps at approximately 1.560 GeV and 1.710 GeV, with a minimum at roughly 1.660 GeV in center-of-mass energy.
- The full model fit to differential cross section data achieved a reduced χ² = 1.96.
- Angular distributions were examined at three energies: W = 1.660, 1.710, and 1.880 GeV.
- The S₁₁(1650) resonance has a destructive effect on the differential cross section that is "very significant at the lowest energy depicted" (W = 1.660 GeV), explaining the minimum in total cross section.

## Context

This work extends earlier coupled-channels studies of πN → πN reactions by Julia-Diaz et al. (2007) and the dynamical coupled-channels formalism developed by Matsuyama et al. (2006). It directly addresses the long-standing puzzle of the second bump in the π⁻p → ηn total cross section, which previous approaches attributed to various resonances: Sauermann et al. (1994) found no second bump with core terms alone, requiring P₁₃(1720); Penner and Mosel (2002) attributed it to P₁₁-wave; and Gasparyan et al. (2003) obtained agreement via core terms plus P₁₃(1720) but with angular distributions deviating above W ≈ 1.65 GeV. The findings enable future work to properly constrain baryon resonance properties by requiring simultaneous fits to both total and differential cross section data.
