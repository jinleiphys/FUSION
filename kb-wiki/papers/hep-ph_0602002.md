# Phase transitions in the early and the present Universe

> The evolution of the Universe is the ultimate laboratory to study fundamental physics across energy scales that span about 25 orders of magnitude: from the grand unification scale through particle and nuclear physics scales down to the scale of atomic physics. The standard models of cosmology and particle physics provide the basic understanding of the early and present Universe and predict a series of phase transitions that occurred in succession during the expansion and cooling history of the Universe. We survey these phase transitions, highlighting the equilibrium and non-equilibrium effects as well as their observational and cosmological consequences. We discuss the current theoretical and experimental programs to study phase transitions in QCD and nuclear matter in accelerators along with the new results on novel states of matter as well as on multi- fragmentation in nuclear matter. A critical assessment of similarities and differences between the conditions in the early universe and those in ultra- relativistic heavy ion collisions is presented. Cosmological observations and accelerator experiments are converging towards an unprecedented understanding of the early and present Universe.

## Key claim

The paper surveys the series of phase transitions predicted by the standard models of cosmology and particle physics that occurred during the expansion and cooling history of the Universe, but emphasizes that the only phase transition realistically accessible to accelerator experiments in the foreseeable future is the QCD transition, with all earlier transitions (electroweak, grand unification) requiring indirect inference from observational aftermath rather than direct laboratory study.

## Method

The authors review the equilibrium and non-equilibrium aspects of phase transitions across energy scales spanning about 25 orders of magnitude, from the Planck scale through grand unification, electroweak, QCD, nuclear, and atomic physics scales. They present the theoretical framework of effective potentials and order parameters for first and second order transitions, compare cooling rates from cosmological expansion with equilibration rates from scattering processes to assess whether transitions occur in local thermodynamic equilibrium, and critically evaluate the similarities and differences between conditions in the early Universe and those in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions at SPS-CERN and RHIC-BNL.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework connecting particle physics phase transitions to cosmology, building on the standard models described in Kolb and Turner (1990) and other cosmology textbooks. The paper provides a critical assessment that contrasts with earlier optimistic expectations by emphasizing that only the QCD phase transition can be studied directly in accelerator experiments (at SPS-CERN, RHIC-BNL, and the forthcoming LHC), while the electroweak and grand unification transitions must be inferred indirectly from cosmological observations. The work enables future comparisons between lattice QCD results, heavy ion collision data from RHIC and LHC, and cosmological observations of the cosmic microwave background and light element abundances to test the standard model's predictions for the thermal history of the Universe.
