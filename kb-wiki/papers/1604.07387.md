# Origin of the mass splitting of azimuthal anisotropies in a multi-phase transport model

> Both hydrodynamics-based models and a multi-phase transport (AMPT) model can reproduce the mass splitting of azimuthal anisotropy ($v_n$) at low transverse momentum ($p_{\perp}$) as observed in heavy ion collisions. In the AMPT model, however, $v_n$ is mainly generated by the parton escape mechanism, not by the hydrodynamic flow. In this study we provide detailed results on the mass splitting of $v_n$ in this transport model, including $v_2$ and $v_3$ of various hadron species in d+Au and Au+Au collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and p+Pb collisions at the Large Hadron Collider. We show that the mass splitting of hadron $v_2$ and $v_3$ in AMPT first arises from the kinematics in the quark coalescence hadronization process, and then, more dominantly, comes from hadronic rescatterings, even though the contribution from the latter to the overall charged hadron $v_n$ is small. We further show that there is no qualitative difference between heavy ion collisions and small-system collisions or between elliptic ($v_2$) and triangular ($v_3$) anisotropies. Our studies thus demonstrate that the mass splitting of $v_2$ and $v_3$ at low-$p_{\perp}$ is not a unique signature of hydrodynamic collective flow but can be the interplay of several physics effects.

## Key claim

The mass splitting of hadron $v_2$ and $v_3$ at low transverse momentum in the AMPT model arises first from kinematics in the quark coalescence hadronization process and then, more dominantly, from hadronic rescatterings, even though the contribution from the latter to the overall charged hadron $v_n$ is small; this demonstrates that such mass splitting is not a unique signature of hydrodynamic collective flow but can result from the interplay of several physics effects.

## Method

The authors used the string melting version of the AMPT model (v2.26t5) with a strong coupling constant of 0.33 and a total parton scattering cross section of 3 mb to simulate Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV with impact parameter 6.6-8.1 fm, d+Au collisions at 200 GeV with impact parameter 0 fm, and p+Pb collisions at 5 TeV with impact parameter 0 fm. They isolated the contributions to mass splitting by examining primordial hadrons right after quark coalescence, including resonance decays, and varying the hadronic rescattering cutoff time from 0.6 fm/c to 30 fm/c, and also performed a test with randomized parton azimuthal directions after each scattering to remove collective flow effects.

## Key numbers

- Parton scattering cross section set to 3 mb and strong coupling constant alpha_s = 0.33 for all AMPT calculations.
- Au+Au collisions simulated at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV with impact parameter b = 6.6-8.1 fm (approximately 20%-30% centrality).
- d+Au collisions simulated at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV with b = 0 fm; p+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5 TeV with b = 0 fm.
- Hadronic interactions terminated at a default cutoff time t_max = 30 fm/c; hadronic rescatterings turned off by setting t_max = 0.6 fm/c (NTMAX=3).
- The decay pion momentum in the rho meson rest frame is about 0.36 GeV/c.
- Average hadronic eccentricity after hadronization is 0.11 in Au+Au and 0.42 in d+Au collisions, starting from initial average eccentricities of 0.29 and 0.53 respectively.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by He et al. (2015) and Lin et al. (2015) showing that azimuthal anisotropy in AMPT is mainly generated by anisotropic parton escape rather than hydrodynamic flow, and by Li et al. (2016) on v2 mass splitting in Au+Au and d+Au collisions. It contradicts the common interpretation that mass splitting of v_n at low pT is a hallmark of hydrodynamic collective flow as described by Heinz (2013) and others, and instead shows that transport models can produce the same effect through parton escape, coalescence kinematics, and hadronic rescatterings. The results enable further investigation of whether mass splitting can distinguish between hydrodynamic and transport descriptions of heavy ion collisions, particularly in small systems such as p+Pb and d+Au.
