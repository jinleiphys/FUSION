# Effective Hamiltonians with Relativistic Corrections I: The Foldy--Wouthuysen transformation versus the direct Pauli reduction

> Two different methods of obtaining ``effective $2\times 2$ Hamiltonians'' which include relativistic corrections to nonrelativistic calculations are discussed, the standard Foldy--Wouthuysen transformation and what we call the ``direct Pauli reduction''. We wish to investigate under which circumstances the two approaches yield the same result. Using a generic interaction with harmonic time dependence we show that differences in the corresponding effective S--matrices do arise beyond first--order perturbation theory. We attribute them to the fact that the use of the direct reduction effective Hamiltonian involves the additional approximation of neglecting contributions from the negative--energy intermediate states, an approximation which is unnecessary in the Foldy--Wouthuysen case as there the $4\times 4$ Hamiltonian does not connect positive-- and negative--energy states. We conclude that at least in the cases where the relativistic Hamiltonian is known, using the direct Pauli reduction effective Hamiltonian introduces spurious relativistic effects and therefore the Foldy--Wouthuysen reduction should be preferred.

## Key claim

The Foldy–Wouthuysen transformation and the direct Pauli reduction produce effective \(2\times2\) Hamiltonians whose corresponding effective S–matrices differ beyond first-order perturbation theory, because the direct Pauli reduction neglects contributions from negative-energy intermediate states; only the Foldy–Wouthuysen effective S–matrix reproduces the full relativistic S–matrix to a given order in \(1/M\).

## Method

The authors compared two methods for constructing effective \(2\times2\) Hamiltonians that include relativistic corrections: the standard Foldy–Wouthuysen transformation, which block-diagonalizes the \(4\times4\) Dirac Hamiltonian order by order in \(1/M\) via successive unitary transformations, and the direct Pauli reduction, which evaluates the matrix element of the interaction Hamiltonian between free positive-energy Dirac states and reduces it to two-component form. They analyzed the first-order matrix elements of both methods for a generic interaction with harmonic time dependence and then examined the second-order S–matrix in old-fashioned time-ordered perturbation theory, using a general unitary transformation formalism to track contributions from negative-energy intermediate states.

## Key numbers

- The difference between the Foldy–Wouthuysen and direct Pauli reduction Hamiltonians at order \(1/M^2\) is given by \(\Delta H_1(t) = -\frac{i}{8 M^2} [\mathcal{O}_0, \dot{\mathcal{O}}_I] + \frac{\beta}{16 M^3} [\mathcal{O}_0^2, [\mathcal{O}_0, \mathcal{O}_I]] + \text{odd terms} + [1/M^4]\).
- For a harmonic time dependence \(\dot{\mathcal{O}}_I = \mp i \omega \mathcal{O}_I\), the matrix element of \(\Delta H_1\) between free positive-energy Foldy–Wouthuysen states is \(\frac{1}{8 M^2} (E_f - E_i \mp \omega) \int d^3x \, \Psi^{FW\dagger}_{0f} [\mathcal{O}_0, \mathcal{O}_I] \Psi^{FW}_{0i} + [1/M^4]\).
- In a proton–proton bremsstrahlung calculation at incident laboratory energy \(T^{LAB} = 280\) MeV with outgoing protons at \(\Theta_3 = \Theta_4 = 6^\circ\) in a coplanar equal-angle geometry, the difference between using the Foldy–Wouthuysen Hamiltonian and the direct Pauli reduction Hamiltonian is of order 7%.

## Context

This work extends earlier discussions by Nieto (1977) and Goldman (1977), who assumed that the two reduction methods yield identical results based on the “Schrödinger statement” that \([\Delta, H_0^{FW}] + i\dot{\Delta} = 0\); the authors show this assumption fails when states are off-energy-shell. The paper clarifies that in first-order processes such as electron scattering from an on-shell nucleon (McVoy, 1962) the two methods agree because energy is conserved at the vertex, but in higher-order processes like proton–proton bremsstrahlung (Workman, 1992; Nakayama, 1991) the choice matters. The results enable practitioners to correctly select the Foldy–Wouthuysen reduction when the full relativistic Hamiltonian is known, and motivate a detailed comparison for Compton scattering in a companion paper.
