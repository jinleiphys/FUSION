# Closed-orbit theory for spatial density oscillations

> We briefly review a recently developed semiclassical theory for quantum oscillations in the spatial (particle and kinetic energy) densities of finite fermion systems and present some examples of its results. We then discuss the inclusion of correlations (finite temperatures, pairing correlations) in the semiclassical theory.

# Closed-orbit theory for spatial density oscillations

## Key claim

The paper presents a semiclassical theory for quantum oscillations in spatial particle and kinetic-energy densities of finite fermion systems, claiming that the oscillatory parts of these densities can be expressed as sums over closed orbits (not periodic orbits) of the corresponding classical system, valid to leading order in ℏ and for arbitrary local potentials in any dimension.

## Method

The authors separate the particle density ρ(r) and kinetic-energy densities τ(r) and τ₁(r) into smooth parts given by extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) theory and oscillatory parts expressed semiclassically as sums over closed orbits γ that start and end at the same point r. Each orbit contributes an amplitude A_γ(λ̃,r) and a phase Φ_γ(λ̃,r) containing the classical action S_γ, a Morse index μ_γ, and a dimension-dependent constant. The theory is then extended to finite temperatures and pairing correlations by convolving the zero-temperature semiclassical expressions with appropriate folding functions, yielding modulation factors f̃_T(τ) = πTτ/sinh(πTτ) for temperature and f̃_Δ(τ) = Δτ K₁(Δτ) for BCS pairing.

## Key numbers

- For the two-dimensional quartic oscillator V(x,y) = ½(x⁴ + y⁴) − κ x²y² with κ = 0.6 and N = 632 particles, the Thomas-Fermi kinetic-energy functional holds approximately for the exact quantum-mechanical densities including oscillations, tested along the diagonal x = y (units ℏ = m = 1).
- The Friedel oscillation contribution from the primitive "+" orbit in a D-dimensional spherical billiard is given by δρ₊(r) = −ρ_TF^(D) 2^ν Γ(ν+1) (R/r)^(ν−½) J_ν(z)/z^ν, where ν = D/2, z = 2(R−r)p_λ/ℏ, and p_λ = (2mλ̃)^(½).
- The integrated Friedel oscillation yields a surface term δN₊ = −[1/(2π^(D/2)ℏ^(D−1))] [Γ(D/2)/Γ(D)] p_λ^(D−1) S_D, where S_D is the hypersurface area of the D-dimensional sphere.
- The temperature modulation factor in the finite-temperature trace formula is f̃_T(τ) = πTτ / sinh(πTτ).
- The pairing modulation factor for BCS correlations is f̃_Δ(τ) = Δτ K₁(Δτ), where K₁ is a modified Bessel function.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previously developed semiclassical theory (Brack and Roccia, 2009) for spatial density oscillations in finite fermion systems, building on Gutzwiller's trace formula (Gutzwiller, 1971) for the oscillating level density and on extended Thomas-Fermi theory for smooth densities. The key result is a local virial theorem δτ(r) ≈ [λ̃ − V(r)] δρ(r) that holds for arbitrary potentials and dimensions, explaining why the Thomas-Fermi kinetic-energy functional (without gradient corrections) works surprisingly well even for oscillating exact densities, a fact noted earlier (Brack, 1997) but not previously understood. The inclusion of finite temperatures and pairing correlations via convolution methods (Brack and Quentin, 1995) enables applications to hot nuclei and superfluid fermion systems, with the anomalous density matrix for pair condensation identified as an open problem for future research.
