# Correlation Integral vs. second order Factorial Moments and an efficient computational technique

> We develop a mapping between the factorial moments of the second order $F_2$ and the correlation integral $C$. We formulate a fast computation technique for the evaluation of both, which is more efficient, compared to conventional methods, for data containing number of pairs per event which is lower than the estimation points. We find the effectiveness of the technique to be more prominent as the dimension of the embedding space increases. We are able to analyse large amount of data in short computation time and access very low scales in $C$ or extremely high partitions in $F_2$. The technique is an indispensable tool for detecting a very weak signal hidden in strong noise.

## Key claim

The authors establish a direct mapping between the second-order factorial moment \(F_2\) and the correlation integral \(C\), and develop a ring-based computational technique that is more efficient than conventional methods specifically when the number of pairs per event is lower than the number of estimation points, with the efficiency gain increasing as the embedding space dimension increases.

## Method

The authors derive an analytical mapping between \(F_2(M)\) and \(C(R)\) by equating the average number of point pairs counted at a given partition \(M\) (for \(F_2\)) with the average number of pairs counted at a corresponding scale \(R\) (for \(C\)), using the relation \(R = \beta_d R_w / M\) where \(\beta_d\) depends on the embedding dimension \(d\) (e.g., \(\beta_2 = 1/\sqrt{\pi}\) for two dimensions). They then implement a computational technique where all pairwise distances in the data set are computed once and assigned to rings (bins) of distance or partition, allowing the correlation integral or factorial moments at all scales to be obtained by cumulative summation rather than requiring separate data passes for each scale.

## Key numbers

- For two-dimensional embedding space, the scale-to-partition relation is \(R = R_w / \sqrt{\pi} \cdot 1/M\), where \(R_w\) is the side length of the analysis window.
- For three-dimensional embedding space, the relation is \(R = \sqrt[3]{3/(4\pi)} \cdot R_w / M\).
- The mapping between \(C\) and \(F_2\) for constant multiplicities is \(C(R) = [N_m/(N_m-1)] \cdot (1/M^d) \cdot F_2(M)\).
- The mapping for varying multiplicities uses \(a_m = \langle N_m(N_m-1)\rangle_e / \langle N_m\rangle_e^2\).
- The ring technique assigns pairs to bins using \(M_{CI} = [\ln(R_T/R) / \ln(\tilde{A}_b) + m_c]\) for correlation integral calculations, where \(\tilde{A}_b > 1\) and \(R_T\) is the maximum available distance in the data.

## Context

This work extends the factorial moment analysis introduced by Bialas and Peschanski (1986) for studying intermittency and critical correlations in particle physics, and the correlation integral method of Grassberger and Procaccia (1983) for fractal dimension determination. The mapping and ring technique enable access to very small scales (high partitions) that were previously computationally prohibitive, particularly relevant for detecting critical opalescence signals in heavy-ion collision data such as the proton transverse momentum analysis by the NA49 collaboration (2014). The method is applicable to general point set analysis beyond particle physics.
