# Non-Equilibrium 2PI Potential and Its Possible Application to Evaluation of Bulk Viscosity

> Within non-equilibrium Green's function technique on the real-time contour and the two-particle-irreducable (2PI) $Φ$-functional method, a non-equilibrium potential is introduced. It naturally generalizes the conventional thermodynamic potential with which it coincides in thermal equilibrium. Variations of the non-equilibrium potential over respective parameters result in the same quantities as those of the thermodynamic potential but in arbitrary non-equilibrium. In particular, for slightly non-equilibrium inhomogeneous configurations a variation of the non-equilibrium potential over volume is associated with the trace of the non-equilibrium stress tensor. The latter is related to the bulk viscosity. This provides a novel way for evaluation of the bulk viscosity.

## Key claim

The authors introduce a non-equilibrium potential within the 2PI (two-particle-irreducible) functional formalism that generalizes the conventional thermodynamic potential to arbitrary non-equilibrium states, and they propose that for slightly non-equilibrium inhomogeneous configurations, the variation of this potential over volume yields the trace of the non-equilibrium stress tensor, which in turn provides a novel route to compute bulk viscosity, but the paper does not actually compute any bulk viscosity value, only establishes the formal connection.

## Method

The authors employ the non-equilibrium Green's function technique on the real-time Schwinger-Keldysh contour combined with the 2PI (Phi-derivable) functional method. They define a non-equilibrium potential functional that depends on the full Green's function and the self-energy, and show that its variations with respect to thermodynamic parameters (temperature, chemical potential, volume) reproduce the same physical quantities as in equilibrium thermodynamics. For slightly inhomogeneous systems, they relate the volume variation of this potential to the trace of the non-equilibrium stress tensor, which is then linked to bulk viscosity through standard linear response relations.

## Key numbers

- No explicit numerical results are presented in the paper; the work is entirely formal and methodological.

## Context

This work extends the 2PI (Phi-derivable) functional approach developed by earlier authors (e.g., Knoll and Voskresensky in the 1990s, Ivanov and Voskresensky in subsequent works) from equilibrium thermodynamics to non-equilibrium situations. The paper provides a formal framework that could enable future calculations of bulk viscosity in nuclear matter, quark-gluon plasma, or other strongly interacting quantum systems using the 2PI formalism, but does not itself perform such calculations. The approach contrasts with standard Kubo-formula methods for transport coefficients by offering a potential-based alternative route.
