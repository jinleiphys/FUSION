# Filtering states with total spin on a quantum computer

> Starting from a general wave function described on a set of spins/qubits, we propose several quantum algorithms to extract the components of this state on eigenstates of the total spin ${\bf S}^2$ and its azimuthal projection $S_z$. The method plays the role of total spin projection and gives access to the amplitudes of the initial state on a total spin basis. The different algorithms have various degrees of sophistication depending on the requested tasks. They can either solely project onto the subspace with good total spin or completely uplift the degeneracy in this subspace. After each measurement, the state collapses to one of the spin eigenstates that could be used for post-processing. For this reason, we call the method Total Quantum Spin filtering (TQSf). Possible applications ranging from many-body physics to random number generators are discussed.

# Filtering states with total spin on a quantum computer

## Key claim
The authors propose quantum algorithms that can extract components of a general wave function on eigenstates of total spin **S**² and its azimuthal projection S_z, but the method's ability to completely resolve degeneracy in the total spin subspace requires additional measurements of intermediate spin operators **S**²_[j] for j = 2,...,n, not just the total spin operator alone.

## Method
The method, called Total Quantum Spin filtering (TQSf), applies Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE) using unitary operators U_S and U_z constructed from the total spin operator **S**² and the number operator N₁ (which counts qubits in state |1⟩). For systems with degenerate total spin subspaces, the authors extend the approach by performing QPE on the set of operators **S**²_[j] (or equivalently H_[j] = Σ_{i<j} P_{ij}) for each intermediate number of qubits, or by using a sequential measurement technique based on the operator G_[j] that encodes whether total spin increases or decreases when adding each successive qubit.

## Key numbers
- For n=4 qubits, the optimal number of ancillary qubits for total spin projection is n_S = 2 and for S_z projection is n_z = 3.
- The eigenvalues of **S**² for n qubits satisfy λ_S ≤ n(n+2)/4.
- The number of ancillary qubits n_S must satisfy n_S > ln[k(k+1)]/ln 2 − 1 for even n=2k, and n_S > ln[k(k+2)]/ln 2 for odd n=2k+1.
- For the operator H_[j], the number of ancillary qubits n_[j] must satisfy n_[j] > ln(j−1)/ln 2.
- The eigenvalues of H_[j] are integers in the interval [−1, j−1].
- For n=4 qubits, the Hilbert space ℋ_{0,0} contains 2 states, while ℋ_{1,M} with M=−1,0,1 contains 3 states each.

## Context
This work extends the permutational quantum computing (PQC) framework introduced by Marzuoli and Rasetti (2002, 2005) and further discussed by Jordan (2010), which uses the total spin basis connected to the permutation symmetry group S_n. The TQSf method relates to symmetry breaking/symmetry restoration problems previously discussed on quantum computers by Lacroix (2020) and others. The algorithms enable preparation of states with good total spin for use in variational approaches (projection after variation or projection before variation, as in Ring and Schuck (1980) or Blaizot and Ripka (1986)), and can generate random numbers from binomial distributions on an equidistant discretized mesh.
