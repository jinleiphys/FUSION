# Absence of hindrance in microscopic $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C fusion study

> Background: Studies of low-energy fusion of light nuclei are important in astrophysical modeling, with small variations in reaction rates having a large impact on nucleosynthesis yields. Due to the lack of experimental data at astrophysical energies, extrapolation and microscopic methods are needed to model fusion probabilities. Purpose: To investigate deep sub-barrier $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C fusion cross sections and establish trends for the $S$ factor. Method: Microscopic methods based on static Hartree-Fock (HF) and time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) mean-field theory are used to obtain $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C ion-ion fusion potentials. Fusion cross sections and astrophysical $S$ factors are then calculated using the incoming wave boundary condition (IWBC) method. Results: Both density-constrained frozen Hartree-Fock (DCFHF) and density-constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) predict a rising $S$ factor at low energies, with DC-TDHF predicting a slight damping in the deep sub-barrier region ($\approx1$~MeV). Comparison between DC-TDHF calculations and maximum experimental cross-sections in the resonance peaks are good. However the discrepancy in experimental low energy results inhibits interpretation of the trend. Conclusions: Using the fully microscopic DCFHF and DC-TDHF methods, no $S$ factor maximum is observed in the $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C fusion reaction. In addition, no extreme sub-barrier hindrance is predicted at low energies. The development of a microscopic theory of fusion including resonance effects, as well as further experiments at lower energies must be done before the deep sub-barrier behavior of the reaction can be established.

## Key claim

Both density-constrained frozen Hartree-Fock (DCFHF) and density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) methods predict no maximum in the astrophysical S factor and no extreme sub-barrier hindrance for 12C+12C fusion, but the authors note that the discrepancy in experimental low-energy results inhibits interpretation of the trend, and that a microscopic theory including resonance effects plus further experiments are needed before the deep sub-barrier behavior can be established.

## Method

The authors used static Hartree-Fock (HF) and time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) mean-field theory to compute 12C+12C ion-ion fusion potentials via the density-constrained frozen Hartree-Fock (DCFHF) and density-constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) methods. Fusion cross sections and astrophysical S factors were then calculated using the incoming wave boundary condition (IWBC) method to solve the two-body Schrodinger equation for transmission probabilities. The calculations used the UNEDF1 Skyrme functional as the default, with additional comparisons using SLy4d and QMC-I functionals, and were performed with a 3D code using a 72 fm box along the collision axis and 0.8 fm grid spacing.

## Key numbers

- DC-TDHF predicts a slight damping in the deep sub-barrier region at approximately 1 MeV center-of-mass energy
- The TDHF collision energy chosen for comparison to experiment was E_cm = 6.03 MeV
- The barrier peak occurs at R = 8.06 fm, where core contours of density 0.08 fm^-3 are well separated and the overlapping region density is 0.008 fm^-3
- Changing grid spacing from 1.0 fm to 0.8 fm changes barrier heights by about Delta V_peak = 0.5 keV
- The lowest experimental resonance discussed is at 2.14 MeV, which is subject to experimental controversy

## Context

This work extends microscopic mean-field studies of 12C+12C fusion (e.g., Bonche 1978, Lebhertz 2012, Simenel 2013a) to deep sub-barrier energies using parameter-free approaches, and contradicts the hindrance model of Jiang (2007) which predicts a sharp downturn in the S factor at low energies. The results enable future fully microscopic descriptions of many-body tunneling (e.g., following Levit 1980, Reinhardt 1980) and motivate further experiments at lower energies, as well as studies of asymmetric systems like 13C+12C where transfer effects may play a larger role.
