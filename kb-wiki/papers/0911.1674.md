# Microscopic Transport Theory of Nuclear Processes

> We formulate a microscopic theory of the decay of a compound nucleus through fission which generalizes earlier microscopic approaches of fission dynamics performed in the framework of the adiabatic hypothesis. It is based on the constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov procedure and the Generator Coordinate Method, and requires an effective nucleon-nucleon interaction as the only input quantity. The basic assumption is that the slow evolution of the nuclear shape must be treated explicitely, whereas the rapidly time-dependent intrinsic excitations can be treated by statistical approximations. More precisely, we introduce a reference density which represents the slow evolution of the nuclear shape by a reduced density matrix and the state of intrinsic excitations by a canonical distribution at each given shape of the nucleus. The shape of the nuclear density distribution is described by parameters ("generator coordinates"), not by "superabundant" degrees of freedom introduced in addition to the complete set of nucleonic degrees of freedom. We first derive a rigorous equation of motion for the reference density and, subsequently, simplify this equation on the basis of the Markov approximation. The temperature which appears in the canonical distribution is determined by the requirement that, at each time t, the reference density should correctly reproduce the mean excitation energy at given values of the shape parameters. The resulting equation for the "local" temperature must be solved together with the equations of motion obtained for the reduced density matrix.

## Key claim

The paper formulates a microscopic theory of compound-nucleus fission decay that generalizes earlier adiabatic approaches by explicitly including intrinsic excitations via a canonical distribution at each nuclear shape, under the assumption that the slow shape evolution can be treated explicitly while rapid intrinsic excitations are handled by statistical approximations and the Markov approximation.

## Method

The theory is based on the constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) procedure and the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM), using an effective nucleon-nucleon interaction as the only input. A reference density operator is introduced that represents the slow shape evolution via a reduced density matrix and the intrinsic excitation state via a canonical distribution at each shape, with the temperature determined by requiring the reference density to reproduce the mean excitation energy at given shape parameters. A rigorous equation of motion for the reference density is derived using the Nakajima-Zwanzig formalism, then simplified via the Markov approximation and perturbative treatment of the coupling Hamiltonian, leading to an integro-differential equation for the reduced density matrix that must be solved together with an equation for the local temperature.

## Key numbers

* The overlap parameter epsilon is defined as exp(-Delta q^2 / 2 sigma_0^2), where Delta q is the spacing between discrete generator coordinate values.
* The dimension of the Hill-Wheeler vector space is N = N_gc * N_intr, where N_gc is the number of discrete generator coordinate values and N_intr is the number of intrinsic excitations per shape.
* The lifetime of compound nuclear resonances is much larger than the formation time scale of less than 10^-22 s.
* The upper limit of applicability is given by excitation energies in excess of about 50 MeV, where neutron and proton evaporation has been described using the Thomas-Fermi approximation.

## Context

This work extends earlier microscopic fission dynamics approaches performed within the adiabatic hypothesis, such as those by Berger-Gogny, Goutte, and Dubray, which only superimposed HFB ground states in the GCM expansion and thus omitted intrinsic excitations. The theory enables description of fission and fusion-fission processes up to moderately high excitation energies, and the authors plan to apply it to describe neutron, proton, and photon emission from the fissioning nucleus in a subsequent paper. The approach contrasts with classical models such as those of Swiatecki-Blocki and with the Hamiltonian-based transport theories of Hofmann and Grossmann, by using GCM to avoid introducing "superabundant" collective degrees of freedom.
