# Charmonium Cross Sections and the QGP

> In this short review we summarize experimental information and theoretical results for the low-energy dissociation cross sections of charmonia by light hadrons. These cross sections are required for the simulation of charmonium absorption through collisions with comovers in heavy ion collisions, which competes with quark-gluon plasma production as a charmonium-suppression mechanism. If the cross sections are sufficiently large these dissociation reactions may be misinterpreted as an effect of quark-gluon plasma production. Theoretical predictions for these RHIC-related processes have used various methods, including a color-dipole scattering model, meson exchange models, constituent interchange models and QCD sum rules. As the results have been largely unconstrained by experiment, some of the predictions differ by orders of magnitude, notably in the near-threshold regime that is most relevant to QGP searches.

## Key claim
The dissociation cross sections of charmonia by light hadrons are required for simulating charmonium absorption in heavy ion collisions, and if these cross sections are sufficiently large, they may be misinterpreted as an effect of quark-gluon plasma production. The theoretical predictions differ by orders of magnitude, notably in the near-threshold regime most relevant to QGP searches, and are largely unconstrained by experiment.

## Method
The paper reviews four theoretical approaches applied to low-energy charmonium dissociation cross sections: a color-dipole scattering model, meson exchange models, constituent interchange models, and QCD sum rules. Experimental estimates are inferred indirectly from J/ψ photoproduction experiments and from the ratio of J/ψ to Drell-Yan lepton pairs in heavy ion collisions, using a classical survival probability formula.

## Key numbers
- Early Fermilab and SLAC photoproduction experiments gave rough estimates of ~1 mb for σ(J/ψ+N), assuming vector dominance, for photon energies from Eγ ≈ 13 to 200 GeV.
- A 1977 SLAC photoproduction experiment estimated σ(J/ψ+N) = 3.5 (0.8) mb at Eγ ≈ 17 GeV (√s ≈ 6 GeV).
- A "naive" interpretation of J/ψ production data from nuclear collisions gives σ(J/ψ+N)^abs ≈ 6 mb at √s ≈ 10 GeV.
- He, Hüfner and Kopeliovich inferred σ(J/ψ+N)^abs = 2.8 (0.3) mb and σ(ψ'+N)^abs = 10.5 (3.6) mb at √s ≈ 10 GeV from small and negative xF events.
- The Kharzeev-Satz color-dipole formula gives σ(J/ψ+N) = 2.5 mb × (1 − λ0/λ)^6.5, which falls precipitously as √s is decreased and gives a zero cross section near 4.5 GeV.
- Oh et al. note the color-dipole model predicts σ(ψ'+p)/σ(J/ψ+p) ~ 2000–5000 at 4.2 GeV.
- Hüfner et al. find J/ψ+p and ψ'+p cross sections of ≈ 3 mb and 10 mb respectively at √s = 10 GeV.
- Matinian and Müller's meson exchange calculation for J/ψ+π and J/ψ+ρ gives cross sections in the 0.5 to 1 mb range 500 MeV above threshold.
- Haglin's meson exchange calculation for J/ψ+N found a peak of about 7 mb near √s = 4.3 GeV.
- Sibirtsev et al. found a peak cross section of about 1 mb for J/ψ+N near √s = 4.6 GeV.
- Martins, Blaschke and Quack's constituent interchange calculation for J/ψ+π gives a maximum total cross section of about 7 mb at √s ≈ 4.1 GeV.
- Barnes et al. constituent interchange calculation for J/ψ+π peaks at about 1.4 mb just above 3.9 GeV.
- Black's constituent interchange calculation for J/ψ+p finds a peak cross section of ca. 12 mb for the final state D̅^0 Λ_c^+ just above threshold.

## Context
This review extends earlier work on charmonium suppression as a QGP signature, originally proposed by Matsui and Satz (1986), and summarizes the theoretical situation that Müller (1999) described as "embarrassing" due to predictions differing by at least two orders of magnitude. The paper enables future experimental measurements of low-energy charmonium dissociation cross sections, which would test the four theoretical approaches (color-dipole, meson exchange, constituent interchange, and QCD sum rules) and clarify whether comover absorption or QGP production is the dominant suppression mechanism at RHIC.
