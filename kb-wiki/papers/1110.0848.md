# Are present reaction theories for studying rare isotopes good enough?

> Rare isotopes are most often studied through nuclear reactions. Nuclear reactions can be used to obtain detailed structure information but also in connection to astrophysics to determine specific capture rates. In order to extract the desired information it is crucial to have a reliable framework that describes the reaction process accurately. A few recent developments for transfer and breakup reactions will be presented. These include recent studies on the reliability of existing theories as well as effort to reduce the ambiguities in the predicted observables.

## Key claim

Existing non-perturbative reaction theories (ADWA, CDCC) for transfer and breakup are reliable at around 10 A MeV but their agreement with exact Faddeev calculations deteriorates at higher beam energies, where ambiguities in the effective interactions play a significant role.

## Method

The authors benchmarked the adiabatic distorted wave approximation (ADWA) and the continuum discretized coupled channel method (CDCC) against exact Faddeev solutions for (d,p) transfer reactions on targets from A=10 to A=48 at energies E ≈ 5–40 A MeV. For breakup reactions, they compared the dynamical eikonal approximation (DEA) and the time-dependent (TD) semiclassical approach against CDCC for the breakup of ¹⁵C on Pb at 20 and 68 A MeV. They also applied dispersive optical model (DOM) potentials to (d,p) reactions on doubly magic nuclei and introduced a ratio method (breakup/elastic+inelastic+breakup) to reduce core-target interaction uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁰Be(d,p)¹¹Be at 21.4 MeV, ADWA, CDCC, and Faddeev all predict cross sections within a few percent of each other.
- Not including Coulomb in the ¹⁰Be(d,p)¹¹Be calculation introduces an error of 15%.
- For ¹³²Sn(d,p)¹³³Sn at 9.46 MeV, DOM provides an improvement in describing the angular distribution compared to the Chapel Hill parameterization.
- For the breakup of ¹⁵C on Pb at 20 A MeV, DEA produces too large a cross section compared to CDCC; at 68 A MeV, DEA performs well.
- The ratio method for ¹¹Be breakup on ¹²C at 68 A MeV shows strong sensitivity of the form factor to the projectile's neutron separation energy.

## Context

This work extends benchmarks of transfer reaction theories (Nunes 2011) and breakup reaction theories (Capel 2011) by comparing approximate methods to exact Faddeev solutions and CDCC. It also applies the dispersive optical model (Mahaux) to reduce ambiguities in (d,p) spectroscopic factors, building on prior studies of Ca isotopes (Mueller 2011) and ¹³³Sn (Jones 2011). The ratio method (Capel 2011) enables extraction of projectile structure independent of core-target interactions, which is the largest uncertainty in most breakup calculations. Future work requires extending these methods to handle two-particle states and mixed configurations.
