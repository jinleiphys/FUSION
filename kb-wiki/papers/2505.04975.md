# The candidates of 2$α$ condensate around the 16O nucleus studied by the real-time evolution method

> Background: Searching for alpha condensation around a core nucleus, a new class of nuclear clustering, is an interesting topic. Previous theoretical studies predicted 16O + 2$α$ condensed states. However, in those studies, the strong mixing with non-resonant states made the identification of true resonant states non-trivial. Purpose: To address this issue, we aim to provide a more robust theoretical verification of the 16O + 2$α$ condensation. Our goal is to clearly identify the resonant states and predict their properties, such as decay widths. Method: We employ the real-time evolution method (REM), which generates physically important basis states using the equation of motion, minimizing contamination from the continuum. The analytical continuation in the coupling constant (ACCC) was used to estimate the $α$-decay widths. Results: The present calculations show much better convergence of eigenstates, and the $0^+_3$ and $0^+_4$ states showed remarkable isoscalar monopole transition strengths, which were in good agreement with the predictions of Ichikawa et al. The small alpha-decay widths for these states suggest that experimental observation appears feasible. Conclusion: The present results show the reasonable agreement with a previous work, firmly establishing the $0^+_3$ and $0^+_4$ states approximately 5 and 1 MeV below the 16O + 2$α$ threshold as candidates for the 2$α$ condensation around the core nucleus 16O. The REM proved effective in identifying these states.

# The candidates of 2α condensate around the 16O nucleus studied by the real-time evolution method

## Key claim

The 0⁺₃ and 0⁺₄ states at approximately 5 and 1 MeV below the ¹⁶O + 2α threshold are firmly established as candidates for 2α condensation around ¹⁶O, but the identification is conditional on the real-time evolution method (REM) producing cleaner basis states with less continuum contamination than previous random-generation approaches. The 0⁺₅ state could not be reliably characterized because its energy lies too high above the α threshold for the analytical continuation method to extrapolate from the bound-state region.

## Method

The authors employed the real-time evolution method (REM) to generate basis wave functions for the ¹⁶O + 2α system by solving the equation of motion of Gaussian wave packets from the time-dependent variational principle, starting from an initial state excited by 20–50 MeV and evolving up to 4000 fm/c with a reflecting wall at radii R = 4–9 fm. The Hamiltonian used the Volkov No. 2 effective nuclear interaction with Majorana exchange parameter M = 0.63, and the model wave function was a Brink-Bloch wave function with a ¹⁶O core (approximated as a 4α tetrahedron) and two valence α particles. The analytical continuation in the coupling constant (ACCC) method, using a rank [6/6] Padé approximation, was applied to estimate α-decay widths by artificially varying the exchange term strength to bind the resonances and then extrapolating to the physical point.

## Key numbers

- 0⁺₁ state energy: -16.78 MeV (bound, below α-decay threshold)
- 0⁺₂ state energy: -8.16 MeV (bound)
- 0⁺₃ state energy: -4.95 MeV (below ¹⁶O + 2α threshold), α-decay width ≤ 0.01 MeV
- 0⁺₄ state energy: -1.26 MeV (below ¹⁶O + 2α threshold), α-decay width 0.17 MeV
- 0⁺₅ state energy: +0.37 MeV (above threshold, ACCC not applicable)
- Isoscalar monopole transition strength B(IS0) for 0⁺₃ state: 145.0 fm⁴
- Isoscalar monopole transition strength B(IS0) for 0⁺₄ state: 160.7 fm⁴
- Valence 2α distribution radius for 0⁺₃ and 0⁺₄ states: approximately 4.13 fm and 4.08 fm, respectively
- Total radius of 0⁺₃ state: 2.97 fm; 0⁺₄ state: 2.94 fm

## Context

This work extends the theoretical predictions of Ichikawa et al. (2011, 2012) who first proposed ¹⁶O + 2α condensates using randomly generated α-particle configurations within the same model space, but whose results suffered from strong mixing with non-resonant continuum states. The REM approach, previously applied to other cluster states by Imai et al. (2019), Zhou et al. (2020), and others, provides cleaner identification of resonances. The results enable experimental searches for these states, as the small α-decay widths suggest observability, though the proton decay channel (threshold at -5.09 MeV) may affect the 0⁺₄ state and requires future microscopic modeling.
