# The Photon in Dense Nuclear Matter I: Random Phase Approximation

> We present a comprehensive and pedagogic discussion of the properties of photons in cold and dense nuclear matter based on the resummed one-loop photon self energy. Correlations between electrons, muons, protons and neutrons in beta equilibrium that arise due to electromagnetic and strong interactions are consistently taken into account within the random phase approximation. Screening effects and damping as well as collective excitations are systematically studied in a fully relativistic setup. Our study is relevant to linear response theory of dense nuclear matter, calculations of transport properties of cold dense matter and to investigations of the production and propagation of hypothetical vector bosons such as the dark photons.

## Key claim

The paper provides a comprehensive, pedagogic treatment of photon propagation in cold, dense nuclear matter (relevant to neutron star cores) using the relativistic random phase approximation (RPA), and it emphasizes that induced electromagnetic interactions with neutrons, mediated by strongly interacting protons, are an important new aspect explored for the first time, going beyond the standard hard dense loop (HDL) approximation which fails for heavy fermions like nucleons.

## Method

The authors calculate the retarded one-loop photon self-energy in a degenerate plasma of electrons, muons, protons, and neutrons in beta equilibrium, using the real-time formalism at zero temperature. They then resum this self-energy within the RPA to obtain the dressed photon propagator in Coulomb gauge, from which they extract the longitudinal and transverse dispersion relations, screening masses, and spectral functions. Strong interactions among nucleons are incorporated via Skyrme-type effective interactions (specifically the NRAPR parametrization) to define quasiparticle properties (effective masses and chemical potentials) within Fermi liquid theory.

## Key numbers

* Plasma frequency for electrons at nuclear saturation density: \(\omega_0^2 = \frac{e^2}{3\pi^2} \frac{k_f^3}{\mu}\), with explicit values derived from the NRAPR interaction giving \(m_p^* = 575\ \text{MeV}\), \(\mu_p^* = 589\ \text{MeV}\), \(\mu_e = 122\ \text{MeV}\).
* Debye mass (static screening) for a single fermion species: \(m_D^2 = e^2 \mu k_f / \pi^2 = 3\omega_0^2 / v_f^2\), where \(v_f = k_f / \mu\) is the relativistic Fermi velocity.
* Slope of the gapless longitudinal branch (lower branch) in the small-momentum limit: determined by the universal equation \(\frac{1}{2} c \log\left|\frac{1+c}{1-c}\right| = 1\), yielding \(c \sim 0.83\).
* Low-momentum expansion of the transverse mode dispersion: \(\omega_\perp^2 = \omega_0^2 + \left(1 + \frac{1}{5} v_f^2\right) \boldsymbol{q}^2\).

## Context

This work extends prior studies of photon propagation in dense plasmas, which were largely based on the hard dense loop (HDL) approximation (Altherr 1992, Altherr 1992) or focused on deconfined quark matter (Litim 2001, Schmitt 2003), to the phenomenologically relevant regime of cold, dense nuclear matter found in neutron star cores. It also builds on earlier calculations of transport properties in neutron stars (Shternin 2007, Shternin 2008) and on the demonstration that induced interactions can substantially modify these results (Bertoni 2014). The derived dynamic photon propagator enables future consistent calculations of thermal and electrical conductivity, shear viscosity, and neutrino scattering rates in dense matter, and is also relevant to studies of dark photon production and propagation (Batell 2014, Chang 2016).
