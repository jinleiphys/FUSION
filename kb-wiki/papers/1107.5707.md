# The existence of a two-solar mass neutron star constrains the gravitational constant G_N at strong field

> In General Relativity there is a maximum mass allowed for neutron stars that, if exceeded, entails their collapse into a black hole. Its precise value depends on details of the nuclear matter equation of state about which we are much more certain thanks to recent progress in low-energy effective theories. The discovery of a two-solar mass neutron star, near that maximum mass, when analyzed with modern equations of state, implies that Newton's gravitational constant in the star cannot exceed its value on Earth by more than 8% at 95% confidence level. This is a remarkable leap of ten orders of magnitude in the gravitational field intensity at which the constant has been constrained.

## Key claim

The existence of a neutron star with mass 1.97(4) solar masses, when analyzed with modern chiral effective field theory equations of state, implies that Newton's gravitational constant in the star cannot exceed its value on Earth by more than 8% at 95% confidence level, but only if one adopts the stiffest equation of state allowed by causality; a more conservative cutoff yields a bound of 19% at the 2-sigma level.

## Method

The authors integrate the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation of hydrostatic equilibrium numerically using a Runge-Kutta algorithm, employing a pure neutron matter equation of state derived from chiral effective field theory with a new power counting that systematically includes multi-nucleon forces. They vary the gravitational constant G_N in the TOV equation to determine the maximum possible neutron star mass, and compare this to the observed 1.97(4) solar mass neutron star. To control systematic uncertainties at high energy densities, they substitute their equation of state with the stiffest possible equation of state allowed by causality (sound speed equal to light speed) above a maximum Fermi momentum of either 600 MeV or 450 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The observed neutron star mass is 1.97(4) solar masses (where one solar mass is about 2 × 10^30 kg)
- The gravitational constant G_N on Earth is 6.6738(8) N(m/kg)^2
- The acceleration of gravity at the surface of the neutron star is about 2 × 10^12 m/s^2
- For binary pulsar J0737-3039, the relevant acceleration is about 270 m/s^2, with G_N precision of 0.05%
- For binary pulsar PSR B1913+16, the relevant acceleration is about 330 m/s^2, with G_N precision of 0.2%
- The bound on G_N variation is 8% at 95% confidence level using the causality-limit equation of state with cutoff at 600 MeV Fermi momentum
- The relaxed bound is 19% at the 2-sigma level using the more conservative 450 MeV Fermi momentum cutoff
- The free neutron gas equation of state cannot support stars beyond about 0.6-0.7 solar masses
- Stars above 2.2-2.3 solar masses are not supported by the interacting equation of state

## Context

This work extends constraints on Newton's gravitational constant from laboratory measurements (precision 1 × 10^-4) and binary pulsar observations (Kramer 2006) at accelerations of a few hundred m/s^2, to the strong-field regime of neutron stars with surface gravity about 2 × 10^12 m/s^2, a leap of ten orders of magnitude in field intensity. The authors use a chiral effective field theory equation of state (Lacour 2009, Oller 2009) that is in broad agreement with vintage nuclear theory treatments (Akmal 1998) and with independent low-density calculations (Hebeler 2010). The result enables constraints on modified theories of gravity motivated by string theory and cosmology (de la Cruz-Dombriz 2009), and future work may examine f(R) theories where the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are already available (Kainulainen 2006).
