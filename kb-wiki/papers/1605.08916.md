# Alpha decay as a probe for the structure of neutron-deficient nuclei

> The advent of radioactive ion beam facilities and new detector technologies have opened up new possibilities to investigate the radioactive decays of highly unstable nuclei, in particular the proton emission, $α$ decay and heavy cluster decays from neutron-deficient (or proton-rich) nuclei around the proton drip line. It turns out that these decay measurements can serve as a unique probe for studying the structure of the nuclei involved. On the theoretical side, the development in nuclear many-body theories and supercomputing facilities have also made it possible to simulate the nuclear clusterization and decays from a microscopic and consistent perspective. In this article we would like to review the current status of these structure and decay studies in heavy nuclei, regarding both experimental and theoretical opportunities. We then discuss in detail the recent progress in our understanding of the nuclear $α$ formation probabilities in heavy nuclei and their indication on the underlying nuclear structure.

## Key claim

Alpha decay measurements can serve as a unique probe for nuclear structure in neutron-deficient nuclei, but the alpha formation probability extracted from experimental half-lives varies significantly across isotopic chains and is systematically suppressed near shell closures, particularly at N=126, meaning the Geiger-Nuttall law's success depends on the formation probability being roughly constant or linear within each isotopic chain.

## Method

The paper reviews the microscopic R-matrix description of alpha decay, where the decay half-life is expressed in terms of the Coulomb-Hankel function (penetrability) and the alpha formation amplitude at the nuclear surface. The formation amplitude is extracted from experimental half-lives by inverting the R-matrix formula, and the resulting formation probabilities are compared to empirical pairing gaps derived from experimental binding energies using the three-point formula. The review also examines shell-model calculations for 212Po using a four-particle state above the 208Pb core, and discusses generalizations of the Geiger-Nuttall law that incorporate the charge and mass dependence of the penetrability.

## Key numbers

- The alpha formation probability for 212Po calculated from shell-model wave functions that reproduce the experimental half-life is 0.025
- The root mean square radius of the alpha particle R_alpha is approximately 1.281 fm
- For 105Te, two alpha decay events were observed with branching ratios of 89% (4711 keV) and 11% (4880 keV)
- A prompt 171.7 keV gamma-ray transition was observed in 105Te and interpreted as the g7/2 to d5/2 orbital transition
- Early shell-model calculations using only one configuration underestimated alpha decay rates by 4-5 orders of magnitude compared to experimental values
- The alpha formation probabilities for nuclei 162W, 162Hf and 193At are systematically larger than those of neighboring nuclei

## Context

This review extends the long line of alpha decay studies originating with Gamow (1928) and Gurney and Condon (1928), and builds on the microscopic R-matrix formalism developed by Thomas (1954) and later applied by Delion (2006) and Qi (2009, 2012). The work connects alpha formation probabilities to pairing correlations, following the insight of Soloviev (1962) that pairing enhances alpha decay widths, and demonstrates that the extracted formation probabilities track empirical pairing gaps from Andreyev (2013). The review enables future microscopic calculations of alpha decay using density functional theory and ab initio methods, while identifying the need to understand why Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations still underestimate formation amplitudes by several orders of magnitude.
