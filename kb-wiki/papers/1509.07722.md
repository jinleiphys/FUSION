# A three-body model for the analysis of quasi-free scattering reactions in inverse kinematics

> A new method to calculate cross sections for $(p,pn)$ and $(p,2p)$ reactions measured under inverse kinematics conditions is proposed. The method uses the prior form of the scattering transition amplitude, and replaces the exact three-body wave function appearing in this expression by an expansion in terms of $p$-$n$ or $p$-$p$ states, covering the physically relevant excitation energies and partial waves. A procedure of discretization, similar to that used in continuum-discretized coupled-channels calculations, is applied in order to make this expansion finite and numerically tractable. The proposed formalism is non-relativistic but several relativistic kinematical corrections are applied to extend its applicability to energies of current interest. The underlying optical potentials for the entrance and exit channels are generated microscopically, by folding an effective density-dependent G-matrix with the density of the composite nucleus. Numerical calculations for $^{12}$C($p$,$2p$), $^{12}$C($p$,$pn$) and $^{23}$O($p$,$pn$) at $\sim$400~MeV/nucleon are presented to illustrate the method. The role of final-state interactions and Pauli principle between the outgoing nucleons is also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new non-relativistic three-body model for calculating $(p,pn)$ and $(p,2p)$ quasi-free scattering cross sections in inverse kinematics, which avoids the impulse approximation used in standard DWIA methods. The method relies on expanding the final three-body wave function in terms of discretized $p$-$n$ or $p$-$p$ states, but the author notes that the practical implementation requires an auxiliary potential in the incoming channel and that the result will depend on this choice when the three-body wave function is approximated.

## Method

The method starts from the prior form of the exact scattering transition amplitude and replaces the exact three-body wave function with an expansion in $p$-$N$ eigenstates (covering physically relevant excitation energies and partial waves), discretized using a continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) procedure. The nucleon-nucleon interaction is taken as the Reid93 parametrization, while the nucleon-nucleus optical potentials are generated microscopically by folding an effective density-dependent G-matrix with Hartree-Fock ground-state densities from the Skyrme Sk20 interaction. Relativistic kinematical corrections are applied to extend the non-relativistic formalism to energies of several hundred MeV per nucleon, and the transition amplitude is evaluated using a modified version of the coupled-channels code fresco.

## Key numbers

* $^{12}$C($p$,$2p$), $^{12}$C($p$,$pn$) and $^{23}$O($p$,$pn$) reactions are calculated at $\sim$400 MeV/nucleon to illustrate the method.
* The Reid93 NN potential reproduces proton-proton and proton-neutron phase-shifts up to 350 MeV with $\chi^2/N_\mathrm{data}=1.03$.
* The Woods-Saxon bound state wave functions use a diffuseness parameter $a_0=0.7$ fm, with the radius parameter $r_0$ adjusted so that the rms of the calculated orbital matches the HF value via $\sqrt{\langle r^2_\mathrm{sp} \rangle}= [A/(A-1)]^{1/2} \sqrt{\langle r^2_\mathrm{HF} \rangle}$.
* The deuteron ground state binding energy is given as $e_{pN}=-2.22$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the "transfer to the continuum" method previously developed by Moro (2006) and tested in benchmark calculations with the Faddeev/AGS method by Deluva (2007) for the $^{11}$Be($p$,$pn$) reaction at $\sim$35 MeV/u. The method is presented as an alternative to the distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) commonly used for quasi-free scattering analyses (Jacob and Maris, 1966), which relies on the impulse approximation and on-shell T-matrix substitutions that may need testing at the energies of current interest. The approach enables the analysis of inverse kinematics $(p,pN)$ experiments planned at facilities such as GSI, RIKEN and NSCL/MSU, and is expected to provide spectroscopic information complementary to that from heavy-ion knockout reactions (Navin et al., 1998; Aumann et al., 2000; Maddalena et al., 2001; Hansen et al., 2003; Gade et al., 2008) and from transfer reactions at lower energies.
