# Searching for observable effects induced by anomalous triangle singularities

> We investigate the anomalous triangle singularity (ATS) and its possible manifestations in various processes. We show that the ATS should have important impact on our understanding of the nature of some newly observed threshold states. Discussions on how to distinguish the ATS phenomena from genuine dynamic pole structures are presented.

# Searching for observable effects induced by anomalous triangle singularities

## Key claim

The authors argue that anomalous triangle singularities (ATS) can produce resonance-like structures in hadronic processes that mimic genuine dynamical poles, but that these ATS effects can be distinguished by their sensitivity to kinematic conditions and their energy-dependent behavior. The paper hedges that while ATS explains several observed threshold enhancements, the Schmid theorem may suppress ATS effects in elastic channels, making combined elastic and inelastic analyses essential.

## Method

The authors perform an analytic study of the scalar three-point function using dispersion relations and Landau equations to determine the kinematic conditions under which anomalous triangle singularities occur. They then apply these conditions to several physical processes involving triangle diagrams with intermediate meson loops (e.g., $K\bar{K}^*$, $D\bar{D}_1$, $D^*K$), computing invariant mass distributions to show how ATS produces narrow peaks near thresholds. The analysis uses heavy hadron chiral perturbation theory for coupling constants but focuses on the kinematic dependence of lineshapes rather than absolute rates.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of triangle singularities first developed in the 1960s by Peierls (1961), Goebel (1964), Landshoff (1962), and Aitchison (1969), applying it to modern observations of threshold states. The paper directly addresses the interpretation of the $Z_c(3900)$ observed by BESIII (2013) and Belle (2013), the $a_1(1420)$ observed by COMPASS (2015), and the $\eta(1405)/\eta(1475)$ puzzle from BESIII (2012). The analysis builds on the earlier proposal by Wu et al. (2011) that ATS explains isospin violations in $\eta(1405)/\eta(1475)$ decays, and on the method of Guo et al. (2014) for distinguishing poles from cusp effects. The work enables future experimental searches at BES-III, Belle-II, and LHCb to identify ATS signatures by tracking the energy dependence of peak positions in invariant mass spectra, particularly in bottomed meson decays $B \to K\bar{K}^*\bar{D}^{(*)} \to KD_s^{(*)-}\pi\pi$.
