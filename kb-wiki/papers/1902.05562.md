# Magnetic dipole excitation and its sum rule in nuclei with two valence nucleons

> Background: Magnetic dipole (M1) excitation is the leading mode of nuclear excitation by the magnetic field, which couples unnatural-parity states. Since the M1 excitation occurs mainly for open-shell nuclei, the nuclear pairing effect is expected to play a role. As expected from the form of operator, this mode may provide the information on the spin-related properties, including the spin component of dineutron and diproton correlations. In general, the sum rule for M1 transition strength has not been derived yet. Purpose: To investigate the M1 excitation of the systems with two valence nucleons above the closed-shell core, with pairing correlation included, and to establish the M1 sum rule that could be used to validate theoretical and experimental approaches. Possibility to utilize the M1 excitation as a tool to investigate the pairing correlation in medium is also discussed. Method: Three-body model, which consists of a rigid spherical core and two valence nucleons, is employed. Interactions for its two-body subsystems are phenomenologically determined in order to reproduce the two-body and three-body energies. We also derive the M1 sum rule within this three-body picture. Conclusion: The introduced M1 sum rule can be utilized as a benchmark for model calculations of M1 transitions in the systems with two valence nucleons. The total sum of the M1 transition strength is related with the coupled spin of valence nucleons in the open shell, where the pairing correlation is unnegligible. The three-body-model calculations for 18 O, 18 Ne, and 42 Ca nuclei demonstrate a significant effect of the pairing correlations on the low-lying M1 transitions. Therefore, further experimental studies of M1 transitions in those systems are on demand, in order to validate proposed sum rule, provide a suitable probe for the nuclear pairing in medium, as well as to optimize the pairing models.

## Key claim

The paper derives a sum rule for M1 transition strength in nuclei with two valence nucleons above a closed-shell core, showing that the total M1 strength is directly proportional to the spin-triplet component of the valence nucleon pair in the ground state, and that pairing correlations can significantly suppress this strength depending on the range of the pairing interaction.

## Method

A three-body model consisting of a rigid spherical core and two valence nucleons (both neutrons or both protons) was employed, with single-particle states solved using a Woods-Saxon potential and pairing interactions modeled by either a density-dependent contact (DDC) potential or the finite-range Minnesota interaction. The M1 sum rule was derived within this three-body picture, and numerical diagonalization of the anti-symmetrized two-particle basis was performed to obtain ground and excited states for 18O, 18Ne, and 42Ca.

## Key numbers

- For 18O with no pairing, the numerical sum-rule value is S_M1,cal. ≈ 0.799 g²_s, consistent with the analytic value of 4/5 g²_s = 0.8 g²_s
- For 18O with DDC pairing, the sum-rule value is S_M1,cal. ≈ 0.393 g²_s, corresponding to a spin-triplet component N_{S12=1} = 19.7%
- For 18O with Minnesota pairing, the sum-rule value is S_M1,cal. ≈ 0.696 g²_s, corresponding to N_{S12=1} = 34.9%
- The ground state energy of 18O with DDC pairing is -12.019 MeV, compared to the experimental value of -12.188 MeV
- For 18Ne with DDC pairing, the sum-rule value is S_M1,cal. ≈ 0.376 (g_l - g_s)², with N_{S12=1} = 18.9%
- For 18Ne with Minnesota pairing, the sum-rule value is S_M1,cal. ≈ 0.656 (g_l - g_s)², with N_{S12=1} = 33.0%
- The ground state energy of 18Ne with DDC pairing is -4.527 MeV, compared to the experimental value of -4.523 MeV

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of M1 excitations in open-shell nuclei, where previous studies by Kneissl (1996), Pietralla (2008), and Heyde (2010) reviewed M1 transitions, and where sum rules for electric multipole modes were established by Eisenberg (1970) and Ring (1980) but no general M1 sum rule existed. The three-body model builds on approaches developed by Hagino (2007), Bertulani (2007), and Oishi (2010, 2014) for dineutron and diproton correlations. The results enable experimental validation of the proposed M1 sum rule and suggest that M1 excitation data can serve as a probe for nuclear pairing correlations and help optimize pairing models, particularly by distinguishing between zero-range and finite-range pairing interactions.
