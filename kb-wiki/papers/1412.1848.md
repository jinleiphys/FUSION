# Stability of thin-shell interfaces inside compact stars

> We use the thin-shell Darmois-Israel formalism to model and assess the stability of the interfaces separating phases, e.g. the core and the crust, within compact stars. We exemplify the relevance and non-triviality of this treatment in the simplest case of an incompressible star, in constant pressure phase transitions, and in the case of strange quark stars with crust.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the Darmois-Israel thin-shell formalism is necessary for correctly modeling and assessing the stability of interfaces (e.g., core-crust boundaries) inside compact stars in general relativity, and that the stability condition depends nontrivially on the shell's equation of state (parameterized by the squared speed of sound \(\eta\)) and on general relativistic corrections, not merely on the sign of the density jump as in Newtonian theory.

## Method
The authors apply the Darmois-Israel thin-shell formalism to spherically symmetric spacetimes, deriving the effective potential for radial shell motion and the stability condition \(V''(R_0) > 0\) for a static shell at radius \(R_0\). They then specialize this condition to two cases: (1) constant-pressure phase transitions (e.g., Maxwell or Gibbs constructions) and (2) specific numerical examples of incompressible stars with two constant-density layers and of strange quark stars with a crust. The stability is analyzed as a function of the free parameter \(\eta = v_s^2\) (the squared speed of sound in the shell) and other system parameters.

## Key numbers
- For incompressible stars, the maximum density allowed for constant-density stars is \(\rho_{\rm max} \simeq 10^{-12}~\text{cm}^{-2} = 10^{16}~\text{g cm}^{-3}\), well above nuclear density \(\sim 10^{14}~\text{g cm}^{-3}\).
- Reasonable central pressure values for the stratified system are \(p_0^- \simeq 10^{35}~\text{dyn cm}^{-2} \simeq 10^{-13}~\text{cm}^{-2}\).
- For the constant-pressure phase-transition case (with negligible interfacial mass), the stability condition reduces to \([\rho]^+_- < 0\), i.e., the energy density must decrease outward.
- In the Newtonian limit, the stability condition for a shell with no surface mass density is \([\rho(R_0)]^+_- < 0\); for vacuum systems, stability requires \([m(R_0)]^+_- > 0\).
- For the incompressible star example with \(C_2 = 0.5\) (i.e., \(\rho^- = 0.5\,\rho^+\)), there exist stable solutions even when \([\rho]^+_- > 0\), contrary to the classical Newtonian expectation.

## Context
This work extends the general relativistic treatment of thin-shell interfaces (Darmois-Israel formalism) to compact star interiors, building on prior applications to wormholes, radiating spheres, and dynamic thin layers (e.g., Lobo 2005, Poisson 2004). It directly addresses the stability of core-crust interfaces in neutron stars and strange quark stars, where previous Newtonian analyses would simply require a negative density jump. The results enable future studies that include perturbations of the adjacent spacetimes and more realistic equations of state, and they highlight the need for a proper equation of state for the shell itself, a topic still open in material sciences (surface forces 2011). The paper also notes that for constant-pressure phase transitions (Maxwell or Gibbs constructions), the interface is always stable if the density decreases outward, but cautions that mixed phases in Gibbs constructions may be too thick for a thin-shell treatment.
