# Thermal shape fluctuation effects in the description of hot nuclei

> The behavior of several nuclear properties with temperature is analyzed within the framework of the Finite Temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (FTHFB) theory with the Gogny force and large configuration spaces. Thermal shape fluctuations in the quadrupole degree of freedom, around the mean field solution, are taken into account with the Landau prescription. As representative examples the nuclei $^{164}$Er, $^{152}$Dy and $^{192}$Hg are studied. Numerical results for the superfluid to normal and deformed to spherical shape transitions are presented. We found a substantial effect of the fluctuations on the average value of several observables. In particular, we get a decrease in the critical temperature ($T_c$) for the shape transition as compared with the plain FTHFB prediction as well as a washing out of the shape transition signatures. The new values of $T_c$ are closer to the ones found in Strutinsky calculations and with the Pairing Plus Quadrupole model Hamiltonian.

# Thermal shape fluctuation effects in the description of hot nuclei

## Key claim

The inclusion of thermal shape fluctuations in the quadrupole degree of freedom within the Finite Temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (FTHFB) theory with the Gogny force reduces the critical temperature for the deformed-to-spherical shape transition compared to plain mean-field predictions and washes out the sharp signatures of the transition, bringing the critical temperatures closer to those found in Strutinsky calculations and the Pairing Plus Quadrupole model.

## Method

The authors performed FTHFB calculations using the D1S parametrization of the Gogny force in large axially deformed harmonic oscillator configuration spaces (N0=15, with checks at N0=17) for the nuclei 164Er, 152Dy, and 192Hg. Thermal shape fluctuations in the quadrupole moment were included via the Landau prescription, where the probability of a given deformation q is proportional to exp(-F(q)/T), and ensemble averages of observables were computed by integrating over the quadrupole deformation degree of freedom. For comparison, calculations were also performed with the schematic Pairing Plus Quadrupole (PPQ) model Hamiltonian using the Baranger-Kumar parameter set in a two-shell configuration space.

## Key numbers

- For 164Er, the mean-field critical temperature for the shape transition is T_c ≈ 2.7 MeV with the Gogny force, compared to T_c ≈ 1.8 MeV with the PPQ model
- When shape fluctuations are included, the critical temperature for 164Er becomes T_c = 1.7 MeV with the Gogny force and T_c = 1.4 MeV with the PPQ model, as determined by the maximum of σ(β₂)
- The pairing collapse in 164Er occurs at T ≈ 0.7 MeV for both protons and neutrons
- For 152Dy, the neutron pairing collapses at T ≈ 0.5 MeV and the proton pairing at T ≈ 1.0 MeV, with the mean-field shape transition at T = 1.4 MeV
- For 192Hg, the neutron pairing vanishes at T = 0.8 MeV, the mean-field shape transition occurs at T ≈ 1.4 MeV, and the maximum of σ(β₂) indicates a shape transition at T = 0.9 MeV
- The ground state deformation of 192Hg is β₂ = -0.135 (oblate) in the mean-field approximation
- For 164Er, around 20 to 30 MeV excitation energy, the level density with fluctuations is almost two orders of magnitude larger than the mean-field prediction

## Context

This work extends the FTHFB calculations with the Gogny force from Egido, Martin, and Robledo (2000) by incorporating thermal shape fluctuations, addressing a discrepancy where mean-field approaches with effective forces (Skyrme, Gogny, relativistic mean field) predict sharp shape transitions at T_c ≈ 2.7 MeV for 164Er, while schematic models and Strutinsky calculations give T_c ≈ 1.7 MeV. The results confirm earlier findings from Pairing Plus Quadrupole calculations by Egido, Ring, and collaborators (1985, 1986) that fluctuations are essential for describing shape transitions in finite nuclear systems. The work enables more realistic predictions for hot nuclei properties including level densities, fission barriers, and the interpretation of Giant Dipole Resonance experiments.
