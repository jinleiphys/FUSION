# Use of the Discrete Variable Representation Basis in Nuclear Physics

> The discrete variable representation (DVR) basis is nearly optimal for numerically representing wave functions in nuclear physics: Suitable problems enjoy exponential convergence, yet the Hamiltonian remains sparse. We show that one can often use smaller basis sets than with the traditional harmonic oscillator basis, and still benefit from the simple analytic properties of the DVR bases which requires no overlap integrals, simply permit using various Jacobi coordinates, and admit straightforward analyses of the ultraviolet and infrared convergence properties.

## Key claim

The discrete variable representation (DVR) basis is nearly optimal for numerically representing wave functions in nuclear physics, offering exponential convergence and sparse Hamiltonians; the authors claim that one can often use smaller basis sets than with the traditional harmonic oscillator basis, while still benefiting from simple analytic properties that require no overlap integrals and permit straightforward analyses of ultraviolet and infrared convergence.

## Method

The authors compare the DVR basis (particularly the Fourier plane-wave basis and the Bessel-function DVR basis for spherical coordinates) against the traditional harmonic oscillator basis for solving the Schrödinger equation in nuclear physics. They demonstrate the method by solving one-dimensional harmonic oscillator problems, three-body and four-body bound state problems using Jacobi coordinates on Cartesian lattices, and reference applications to time-dependent superfluid dynamics and quantum Monte Carlo calculations.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of DVR basis size to harmonic oscillator basis size for a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions is 384/π³ ≈ 12.4 in the large-N limit.
- With Dirichlet boundary conditions, the ratio reduces to 48/π³ ≈ 1.5.
- For a cubic box with sides L = 40 fm and maximum momentum Λ = 300 MeV/c, the DVR basis requires approximately 10³ states.
- The spherical Bessel-function DVR basis achieves a ratio of 8/π² ≈ 0.8 relative to the harmonic oscillator basis, meaning it uses fewer states.
- For nuclear matter with Λ = 600 MeV/c, the spatial mesh size Δr ≈ 1 fm, giving N_s ≈ 20 radial mesh points in a spherical box of radius R ≈ 20 fm.
- The largest Hilbert spaces used were approximately 1.68×10⁷ for the triton (lattices 8⁶ to 16⁶) and 1.34×10⁸ for the alpha particle (lattices 4⁹ to 8⁹).
- Spatial mesh sizes a = 0.5 to 1.5 fm correspond to Λ ≈ 300 to 930 MeV/c.

## Context

This work extends the DVR approach previously discussed by Littlejohn (2002), Baye (1995, 2006), and others, and contrasts it with the harmonic oscillator basis whose convergence properties were recently analyzed by Furnstahl (2012) and Coon (2012). The DVR basis enables straightforward extrapolation to continuum and thermodynamic limits using techniques from Beth, Uhlenbeck, and Luscher (1937, 1986), and has been applied to density functional theory calculations for unitary Fermi gases (Bulgac 2007, 2008, 2009, 2011) and quantum Monte Carlo calculations for strongly interacting fermions (Wlazlowski 2013, Drut 2012).
