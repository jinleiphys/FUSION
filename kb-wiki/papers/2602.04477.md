# Neutron Dark Decay and Exotic Compact Objects

> Recent measurements of the compact star XTE J1814-338, with a mass of $M=1.2_{-0.05}^{+0.05}\ M_{\odot}$ and a radius of $R=7_{-0.4}^{+0.4} \ {\rm Km}$ alongside those of HESS J1731-347, which has a mass of $M=0.77_{-0.17}^{+0.20}\ M_{\odot}$ and a radius of $R=10.4_{-0.78}^{+0.86} \ {\rm Km}$, provide compelling evidence for the potential existence of exotic matter in neutron star cores. These observations offer important insights into the equation of state of dense nuclear matter. Recently, Fornal and Grinstein, in order to overcame the discrepancy between the neutron lifetime measured in beam and bottle experiments, proposed the existence of neutron dark decay. In the present work, an effort is made to connect the interpretation of the above exotic compact objects with the possible existence of dark particles, assumed to be products of neutron dark decay. Our hypothesis offers an advantage over comparable proposals, as the coexistence of dark matter and hadronic matter within neutron stars emerges from an intrinsic mechanism, thereby obviating the need to invoke external merger-related processes. It is still unclear to what extent the proposed dark decay of the neutron is affected by the extreme environment within neutron stars. Within this framework, we examined the case in which a mechanism suppressing the dark neutron decay becomes operative at densities few times above nuclear saturation density. We found that the proposed alternative explanation accommodates the simultaneous existence of neutron dark decay while consistently predicting both the two solar mass limit and the presence of compact objects with subsolar masses.

## Key claim
The authors propose that the recently observed exotic compact objects HESS J1731-347 and XTE J1814-338 can be explained by neutron dark decay into a dark fermion and a light dark boson, but only if a mechanism suppressing this decay becomes operative at densities a few times above nuclear saturation density, thereby allowing simultaneous prediction of both subsolar-mass objects and maximum masses exceeding two solar masses.

## Method
The authors constructed equations of state for mixed neutron and dark matter using the APR (Akmal et al., 1998) hadronic equation of state for pure neutron matter, combined with a self-interacting Fermi gas description for dark matter particles with Yukawa-type interactions. They considered three interaction scenarios: dark matter self-interaction only, neutron-dark matter interaction only, and both interactions simultaneously, solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to compute mass-radius relations and dark matter fractions for each case.

## Key numbers
- The compact object HESS J1731-347 has mass M = 0.77 (+0.20, -0.17) solar masses and radius R = 10.4 (+0.86, -0.78) km
- The compact object XTE J1814-338 has mass M = 1.2 (+0.05, -0.05) solar masses and radius R = 7 (+0.4, -0.4) km
- The dark particle mass is fixed at m_chi = 938 MeV, with the constraint 937.993 MeV < m_chi + m_phi < 939.565 MeV
- The self-interaction parameter z_chi was varied across values including 130, 220, 200, 250, 350, and 1500 MeV
- The neutron-dark matter interaction parameter z_chi n was varied across values including 10, 20, 30, 40, 150, 180, 200, and 300 MeV
- The cutoff density for suppressing dark neutron decay was specified in units of nuclear saturation density n_s = 0.16 fm^{-3}, with values including twice the saturation density

## Context
This work extends the neutron dark decay hypothesis proposed by Fornal and Grinstein (2018) to explain the beam-bottle neutron lifetime discrepancy, applying it to the interpretation of recently observed light compact objects. It builds upon prior studies by Baym et al. (2018), Grinstein et al. (2019), and McKeen et al. (2018) that examined dark matter effects on neutron star properties, but introduces the novel scenario of density-dependent suppression of dark neutron decay. The work enables observational constraints on dark matter interaction strengths and provides a framework for simultaneously explaining both subsolar-mass compact objects and two-solar-mass neutron stars without invoking external merger-related processes.
