# Radioactive decays at limits of nuclear stability

> The last decades brought an impressive progress in synthesizing and studying properties of nuclides located very far from the beta stability line. Among the most fundamental properties of such exotic nuclides, usually established first, is the half-life, possible radioactive decay modes, and their relative probabilities. When approaching limits of nuclear stability, new decay modes set in. First, beta decays become accompanied by emission of nucleons from highly excited states of daughter nuclei. Second, when the nucleon separation energy becomes negative, nucleons start to be emitted from the ground state. Here, we present a review of the decay modes occurring close to the limits of stability. The experimental methods used to produce, identify and detect new species and their radiation are discussed. The current theoretical understanding of these decay processes is overviewed. The theoretical description of the most recently discovered and most complex radioactive process - the two-proton radioactivity - is discussed in more detail.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that the limits of nuclear stability are not sharply defined by the single-nucleon drip lines alone, because the Coulomb and centrifugal barriers can prevent prompt nucleon emission even when separation energies become negative, allowing beta decay or other modes to compete.

## Method

The authors survey experimental production methods (fusion-evaporation, fragmentation, spallation, fission) and separation techniques used to access nuclides far from beta stability, then review the observed decay modes at the stability limits: beta-delayed particle emission, proton radioactivity, alpha emission, and two-proton radioactivity. They provide a theoretical overview of each decay process, with particular detail given to the two-proton radioactivity mechanism, including its description in terms of three-body models and the Gamow Shell Model.

## Key numbers

- More than 3000 unstable nuclides have been identified in laboratories, compared to 256 stable nuclides.
- The definition of radioactivity adopted uses a half-life much longer than the K-shell vacancy half-life in carbon, about 2 × 10^{-14} s, corresponding to a decay width of about 0.03 eV.
- Approximately 30 new nuclides have been identified per year on average, growing from about 2200 in 1981 to about 3000 in 2006.
- The neutron drip-line has been determined experimentally and unambiguously for even N only up to N=20 and for odd N up to N=27.
- Cluster radioactivity branching ratios are less probable than alpha decay by a factor of at least 10^9.
- The first half-life determination for doubly magic 78Ni was done with 11 atoms.
- The first insight into the structure of 45Fe resulted from decays of 75 atoms.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on nuclear stability limits by Thoennessen (2004) and on mass models by Lunney (2003), and it complements reviews of nuclear structure near the proton drip-line by Blank (2008) and of superheavy element synthesis by Hofmann (2000) and Oganessian (2007). The paper enables future experimental programs at next-generation radioactive beam facilities by cataloguing the decay modes expected at the proton and neutron drip lines, and it identifies open questions in the description of open quantum systems, particularly the need for unified treatments of nuclear structure and reactions such as the Gamow Shell Model and the Shell Model Embedded in the Continuum.
