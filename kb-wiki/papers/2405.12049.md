# Micro-cosmos model of a nucleon

> This study explores the age-old quest to construct a geometric model of a quantum particle. While static classical particle models have largely been dismissed, the focus has now shifted to intricate dynamic models that hold the promise of reconciling general relativity with quantum mechanics. We propose that matter particles can be described as radiation confined within dynamically curved spacetime regions, without the need for quantization of space and time, and using standard field equations and natural Planck units. Specifically, we investigate a cyclic or oscillating radiation-dominated micro cosmos undergoing repeated bouncing. Our methodology employs integration, with carefully defined initial conditions. The results include several observable properties characteristic of quantum particles. We calculate the total mass, revealing a compelling inverse proportionality between mass and radius identical with the de Broglie relationship. Applying this model to protons, we discover a profound and surprisingly simple relationship between the proton's radius and mass expressed in Planck units. This enables a definition of the proton radius that aligns remarkably well with the 2018 CODATA value. Furthermore, our analysis demonstrates that the radial density profile of the proton (or nucleon), averaged over a cycle time, increases toward the center. The problem of embedding the micro cosmos within a background spacetime is also described. These results underscore the relevance of general relativity in the domain of nuclear physics. Moreover, the model offers a fresh perspective that can stimulate new ideas in the ongoing quest to unify general relativity with quantum physics.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a nucleon can be modeled as a cyclic, radiation-dominated micro cosmos (a closed FLRW universe) that oscillates between a Planck-scale minimum and a maximum size, and that this classical general-relativistic model reproduces the proton's mass-radius relationship without modifying the gravitational coupling constant, provided one accepts a geometric factor of exactly 4 (or \(2/\pi\) in Planck units with \(h\)) linking the proton radius to its Compton wavelength.

## Method

The author solves the Friedmann equation for a closed, radiation-dominated universe with scale factor \(a(t)\) oscillating between a minimum \(a_0 = l_\mathrm{Pl}\) and a maximum \(a_\mathrm{max}\). The time-varying mass of the micro cosmos is obtained as \(m_\mathrm{mc}(t) = l_\mathrm{Pl} m_\mathrm{Pl} / a(t)\), and the total mass averaged over one cycle is computed by integration. The model is then calibrated to the proton by setting the averaged mass equal to the proton mass and identifying the de Broglie wavelength with the cycle-integrated size, yielding a definition of the proton radius. A radial density profile is derived by transforming the time-dependent density into a spatial coordinate using the scale-factor solution.

## Key numbers

* The proton radius is defined as \(r_\mathrm{p} \equiv 4\hbar/(m_\mathrm{p}c) = 0.84123564119 \times 10^{-15}\) m, which deviates from the 2018 CODATA value \(0.8414 \pm 0.0019\) fm by approximately 218 ppm.
* The ratio of the maximum scale factor to the Planck length is \(a_\mathrm{max}/a_0 = (\pi/2) \cdot (m_\mathrm{Pl}/m_\mathrm{p}) = (\pi/2) \cdot 1.3012 \times 10^{19}\), giving \(a_\mathrm{max} = 3.3035 \times 10^{-16}\) m.
* The cycle time is \(t_\mathrm{crunch} \approx 2 a_\mathrm{max}/c = 2.2 \times 10^{-24}\) s, comparable to the lifetime of the \(\rho\) meson (\(4.5 \times 10^{-24}\) s).
* The average nucleon density is \(\rho_\mathrm{nuc} = 6.703 \times 10^{17}\) kg/m\(^3\).
* The central radial density approaches \(4 \times 10^{39} \cdot \rho_\mathrm{nuc}\) as \(r \to a_0\), still below the Planck density.

## Context

This work extends the strong gravity approach of Salam (1972) and Sivaram and Sinha (1977, 1979), and the cyclic micro-universe models of Caldirola, Pavsic, Recami (1978) and Italiano and Recami (1984), but avoids introducing an ad hoc strong coupling constant by using standard Einstein equations with Planck units. The derived proton radius matches the result of Trinhammer and Bohr (2019) and is consistent with a heuristic QCD calculation by Ji (1995) relating the factor 4 to the trace anomaly. The model enables a geometric interpretation of the proton's radial density profile, which could be compared with structure functions from deep inelastic scattering, and offers a framework for describing nuclear binding and the mass defect without renormalizing coupling constants.
