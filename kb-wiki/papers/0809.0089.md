# Viscosity at RHIC: Theory and Practice

> Hydrodynamic behavior and the associated discussions of viscosity at RHIC has inspired a r enaissance in modeling viscous hydrodynamics. An explanation of Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics is presented here, with an emphasis on the tangible benefits compared to Navier Stokes.

## Key claim

Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics avoids the superluminal and unstable behavior of Navier-Stokes treatments for short-wavelength modes, and does not assume that anisotropy is determined by the instantaneous velocity gradient, making it more suitable for modeling heavy-ion collisions at RHIC where viscous effects are of order 10-20%.

## Method

The paper presents a pedagogical explanation of Israel-Stewart relativistic viscous hydrodynamics, contrasting it with the traditional Navier-Stokes approach. It derives the relaxation equations for the non-equilibrium parts of the stress-energy tensor, showing that the relaxation times can be expressed in terms of equal-time correlations calculable from lattice gauge theory. The author then applies these concepts to interpret femtoscopic measurements of source size, using a radially- and boost-symmetric Israel-Stewart hydrodynamic code coupled to a Boltzmann code for the hadronic stage, with color-glass initial conditions and an initial stress-energy tensor anisotropy of \(T_{xx}=T_{yy}=\epsilon/2\), \(T_{zz}=0\).

## Key numbers

- Viscous effects at RHIC are of the order of 10-20 percent.
- For non-interacting longitudinal classical fields, \(T_{xx}=T_{yy}=\epsilon\), three times higher than for an ultra-relativistic gas.
- Israel-Stewart relaxation times of 0.5 fm/\(c\) were used in the calculations.
- The viscous calculation reduced the \(R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}\) ratio by approximately 15% compared to ideal hydrodynamics.
- The mean \(p_t\) of protons was 10% higher in the viscous calculation compared to the non-viscous calculation.
- Relaxing the boost-invariant assumption for longitudinal expansion lowers \(R_{\rm long}\) by 5-10%.
- Shear viscosity and early anisotropy of the field-dominated phase reduces \(R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}\) by approximately 20%.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of viscous hydrodynamics at RHIC, building on the Israel-Stewart formalism developed by Israel and Stewart (1979) and later applied by Muronga (2004), Koide (2007), Baier and Romatschke (2006), Heinz and Chaudhuri (2006), Song and Heinz (2008), and Romatschke (2007). The paper contrasts the Israel-Stewart approach with traditional Navier-Stokes treatments and addresses the need to reconcile femtoscopic measurements of source size, particularly the \(R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}\) ratio, which had been overpredicted by earlier ideal hydrodynamic models. The work enables future global model/data analyses that simultaneously fit spectra, elliptic flow, and femtoscopic radii to extract quantitative conclusions about the bulk properties of matter created at RHIC.
