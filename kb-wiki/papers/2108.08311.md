# Resolving the fastest ejecta from binary Neutron Star mergers: implications for electromagnetic counterparts

> We examine the effect of spatial resolution on initial mass ejection in grid-based hydrodynamic simulations of binary neutron star mergers. The subset of the dynamical ejecta with velocities greater than $\sim 0.6$c can generate an ultraviolet precursor to the kilonova on $\sim$hr timescales and contribute to a years-long non-thermal afterglow. Previous work has found differing amounts of this fast ejecta, by one- to two orders of magnitude, when using particle-based or grid-based hydrodynamic methods. Here we carry out a numerical experiment that models the merger as an axisymmetric collision in a co-rotating frame, accounting for Newtonian self-gravity, inertial forces, and gravitational wave losses. The lower computational cost allows us to reach spatial resolutions as high as $4$m, or $\sim 3\times 10^{-4}$ of the stellar radius. We find that fast ejecta production converges to within $10\%$ for a cell size of $20$m. This suggests that fast ejecta quantities found in existing grid-based merger simulations are unlikely to increase to the level needed to match particle-based results upon further resolution increases. The resulting neutron-powered precursors are in principle detectable out to distances $\lesssim 200$Mpc with upcoming facilities. We also find that head-on collisions at the free-fall speed, relevant for eccentric mergers, yield fast and slow ejecta quantities of order $10^{-2}M_\odot$, with a kilonova signature distinct from that of quasi-circular mergers.

## Key claim

The authors find that fast ejecta (velocities greater than ~0.6c) production in grid-based hydrodynamic simulations of binary neutron star mergers converges to within 10% for a cell size of 20 meters, suggesting that existing grid-based simulations are unlikely to produce fast ejecta quantities matching particle-based results even with further resolution increases.

## Method

The authors performed axisymmetric Newtonian hydrodynamic simulations of binary neutron star mergers in a co-rotating frame using the FLASH code, accounting for self-gravity, inertial forces, and gravitational wave losses. They modeled the merger as a collision between two neutron stars placed along the symmetry axis, using a piecewise polytropic equation of state, and varied spatial resolution from 281 meters down to 4 meters to study convergence of fast ejecta production.

## Key numbers

- Fast ejecta production converges to within 10% for a cell size of 20 meters
- Finest spatial resolution reached was 4 meters, or approximately 3 x 10^-4 of the stellar radius
- Baseline model (APR4 EOS, 1.4 + 1.4 solar masses, 32 meter resolution) produced total fast ejecta mass of 1.4 x 10^-5 solar masses
- Head-on collisions at free-fall speed yield fast and slow ejecta quantities of order 10^-2 solar masses
- Neutron-powered precursors are detectable out to distances less than or equal to 200 Mpc with upcoming facilities
- Fast ejecta mass converges as (cell size)^0.46 over the entire resolution range explored

## Context

This work addresses discrepancies between particle-based (SPH) simulations by Bauswein (2013), which found fast ejecta masses around 10^-4 solar masses, and grid-based simulations by Ishii (2018) and Radice (2018b), which found much smaller quantities (10^-7 to 10^-5 solar masses). The results suggest that grid-based simulations are unlikely to produce fast ejecta at the level needed to match particle-based results, which has implications for predicting ultraviolet kilonova precursors on hour timescales and non-thermal afterglows lasting years. The study also examines head-on collisions relevant for eccentric mergers, finding distinct kilonova signatures compared to quasi-circular mergers.
