# Relativistic Quantum Transport Theory

> Relativistic quantum transport theory has begun to play an important role in the space-time description of matter under extreme conditions of high energy density in out-of-equilibrium situations. The following introductory lectures on some of its basic concepts and methods comprise the sections: 1. Introduction; 2. Aims of transport theory (classical); 3. Quantum mechanical distribution functions - the density matrix and the Wigner function; 4. Transport theory for quantum fields; 5. Particle production by classical fields; 6. Fluid dynamics of relativistic quantum dust.

## Key claim

Relativistic quantum transport theory provides a space-time description of matter under extreme conditions of high energy density in out-of-equilibrium situations, but the derivation of consistent transport equations and the computational work required to find realistic solutions remain significant difficulties that have rarely been explored in the microscopic or high energy density domain.

## Method

The lectures develop transport theory starting from classical relativistic kinetic theory, introducing the relativistic Vlasov and Boltzmann equations with the relaxation time collision term and the Jüttner distribution as an equilibrium solution. Quantum mechanical distribution functions are introduced via the density matrix formalism and the Wigner function, which is then extended to quantum field theory using the O(4) linear sigma model as an example. The Hartree approximation is employed to truncate the infinite hierarchy of Wightman function equations, and a Wigner operator is defined to express the two-point function equations in a form suitable for transport theory.

## Key numbers

- The vacuum expectation value of the sigma field is given by |σ₀| = √(-6μ²/λ), where μ² is the mass parameter (negative for spontaneous symmetry breaking) and λ is the coupling constant
- The effective pion mass m_π² = (λ/3!)(σ² + 2σ₀σ) approaches zero as σ → 0, while the effective sigma mass m_σ² = (λ/3!)(π² + 2σ₀²) yields one massive radial mode with m_σ² → λσ₀²/3 together with three massless Goldstone modes
- The relaxation time collision term is defined as C[f] ≡ -(1/τ_r)(f - f₀), where τ_r is the relaxation time parameter and f₀ is the equilibrium one-body density

## Context

This work extends the classical relativistic kinetic theory presented in the monograph by de Groot (1980) and the hydrodynamic approach to high-energy collisions originating from Fermi and Landau, which has been applied to study multiplicity distributions and thermal transverse momentum spectra. The lectures address the need for a quantum transport theory applicable to the quark-gluon plasma formed in heavy-ion collisions at CERN and RHIC, as well as to electro-weak interactions in supernova neutrino plasmas, building on recent progress by Cooper (1998) and Blaizot (1999) concerning selfinteracting scalar particles and the quark-gluon plasma respectively. The paper enables further development of transport equations for nonequilibrium quantum fields and the investigation of how quantum decoherence and thermalization lead to hydrodynamic behavior from underlying quantum field theory.
