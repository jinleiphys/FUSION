# Neutron star mass and radius measurements from atmospheric model fits to X-ray burst cooling tail spectra

> Observations of thermonuclear X-ray bursts from accreting neutron stars (NSs) in low-mass X-ray binary systems can be used to constrain NS masses and radii. Most previous work of this type has set these constraints using Planck function fits as a proxy: both the models and the data are fit with diluted blackbody functions to yield normalizations and temperatures which are then compared against each other. Here, for the first time, we fit atmosphere models of X-ray bursting NSs directly to the observed spectra. We present a hierarchical Bayesian fitting framework that uses state-of-the-art X-ray bursting NS atmosphere models with realistic opacities and relativistic exact Compton scattering kernels as a model for the surface emission. We test our approach against synthetic data, and find that for data that are well-described by our model we can obtain robust radius, mass, distance, and composition measurements. We then apply our technique to Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer observations of five hard-state X-ray bursts from 4U 1702-429. Our joint fit to all five bursts shows that the theoretical atmosphere models describe the data well but there are still some unmodeled features in the spectrum corresponding to a relative error of 1-5% of the energy flux. After marginalizing over this intrinsic scatter, we find that at 68% credibility the circumferential radius of the NS in 4U 1702-429 is R = 12.4+-0.4 km, the gravitational mass is M=1.9+-0.3 Msun, the distance is 5.1 < D/kpc < 6.2, and the hydrogen mass fraction is X < 0.09.

## Key claim

The paper claims that directly fitting detailed atmosphere models to X-ray burst cooling tail spectra, rather than using diluted blackbody fits as a proxy, can yield robust neutron star mass and radius measurements, but only for data that are well-described by the model; for real data from 4U 1702-429, unmodeled features remain at the 1-5% level of the energy flux, requiring marginalization over intrinsic scatter.

## Method

The authors constructed a hierarchical Bayesian fitting framework using state-of-the-art X-ray bursting neutron star atmosphere models with realistic opacities and relativistic exact Compton scattering kernels, computed with the ATLAS stellar modeling program modified for high temperatures. They applied this framework to Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer observations of five hard-state X-ray bursts from 4U 1702-429, fitting the atmosphere models directly to the observed spectra and sampling the posterior distributions using an affine-invariant ensemble Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampler. The model assumed a uniformly emitting, slowly rotating neutron star with plane-parallel atmosphere models in hydrostatic equilibrium, limited to the range of radiative acceleration to gravitational acceleration ratio g_rad/g = 0.2-0.98.

## Key numbers

* Circumferential radius of the neutron star in 4U 1702-429: R = 12.4 ± 0.4 km at 68% credibility
* Gravitational mass of the neutron star in 4U 1702-429: M = 1.9 ± 0.3 solar masses at 68% credibility
* Distance to 4U 1702-429: 5.1 < D/kpc < 6.2 at 68% credibility
* Hydrogen mass fraction upper limit: X < 0.09 at 68% credibility
* Unmodeled features in the spectrum correspond to a relative error of 1-5% of the energy flux
* Surface gravities considered: log10 g = 14.0, 14.3, and 14.6 (g in cgs units)
* Model parameter limits: g_rad/g = 0.2-0.98, log g = 13.7-14.9, hydrogen mass fraction X = 0-1

## Context

This work extends the cooling tail method previously applied to 4U 1702-429 by Nattila et al. (2016) and the touchdown method of Ozel et al. (2006, 2009, 2016), both of which used diluted blackbody fits as a proxy rather than direct atmosphere model fitting. The paper also builds on the atmosphere models of Suleimanov et al. (2011, 2012) and Nattila et al. (2015). The direct fitting approach enables more robust constraints on neutron star mass and radius, though the remaining unmodeled spectral features indicate that further improvements to atmosphere models are needed, particularly regarding the treatment of the burst environment and possible deviations from the assumed plane-parallel, hydrostatic equilibrium conditions.
