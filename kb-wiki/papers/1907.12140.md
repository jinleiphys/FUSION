# Smallest QCD Droplet and Multiparticle Correlations in pp Collisions

> The collective evolution of produced matter in heavy-ion collisions is effectively described by hydrodynamics from time scales greater than the inverse of the temperature, $τ\gtrsim 1/T$. In the context of the Gubser solution, I show that the hydrodynamization condition $τ\, T \gtrsim 1$ is translated into an allowed domain in the spatial system size and the final multiplicity for hydrodynamics applicability. It turns out that the flow measurements in pp collisions are inside the domain of validity. I predict that by approaching the boundaries of the allowed domain the hydrodynamic response to the initial ellipticity changes its sign. I follow a rather model-independent approach for the initial state where, instead of modeling the initial energy density of individual events, the initial system size and ellipticity event-by-event fluctuation are modeled. The model, initial state fluctuation+Gubser solution+Cooper-Frye freeze-out, describes the multiplicity and transverse momentum dependence of two-point and four-point correlation functions ($c_2\{2\}$ and $c_2\{4\}$) in an accurate agreement with pp collision experimental measurements. In particular, the sign of the four-point correlation function is the same as the observation, which failed to be described correctly in previous studies. I also predict a signal for the sign change in the hydrodynamic response that can be inspected in future experimental measurements of two-point and four-point correlation functions at lower multiplicities.

## Key claim

The paper claims that hydrodynamics can describe multiparticle correlations in pp collisions, but only within a specific domain of system size and final multiplicity; the hydrodynamic response to initial ellipticity changes sign near the boundaries of this domain, which the author predicts can be observed in future experimental measurements at lower multiplicities.

## Method

The author uses the analytical Gubser solution of relativistic ideal hydrodynamics for conformal fluids, perturbed to include an initial elliptical shape, combined with the Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription to compute final particle distributions. The free parameters of the Gubser solution are mapped to standard quantities (total transverse energy, rms radius, and ellipticity), and the hydrodynamization condition τT ≳ 1 is translated into an allowed domain in the (system size, final multiplicity) phase space. Event-by-event fluctuations of the initial system size and ellipticity are modeled independently, and the model predictions for two-point and four-point correlation functions are compared with experimental pp collision data from CMS and ATLAS.

## Key numbers

- The hydrodynamization condition leads to a lower bound on system size: r_rms ≥ r_crit, where r_crit = sqrt(4π/3) [τ_hyd² ε_fo^(1/2)] ε_tot^(-1/2), with τ_hyd = 0.9 fm/c and ε_fo = 0.18 GeV/fm³ used in the analysis.
- The model parameters extracted from data are: χσ_ε ≈ 0.097, Γ₂^ε ≈ -0.75, and σ_r ≈ 0.4 fm.
- The freeze-out energy density is fixed to ε_fo = 0.18 GeV/fm³, and the equation of state constant C₀ = 13 is used.
- The model predicts a valley in v₂{2} and v₂{4} around ⟨N_ch⟩ = 10, indicating an in-plane/out-of-plane transition in the hydrodynamic response.
- For the comparison with iEBE-VISHNU simulations, a scaling factor χ = 0.39 is used to match the ideal Gubser results to causal hydrodynamic simulations for Pb-Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV.

## Context

This work extends the application of the Gubser solution (Gubser 2010) from heavy-ion collisions to small systems like pp collisions, where the applicability of hydrodynamics has been debated. It contradicts earlier studies that failed to correctly describe the sign of the four-point correlation function c₂{4} in pp collisions. The model enables future experimental tests of the predicted sign change in hydrodynamic response at lower multiplicities, and the approach is compared with numerical hydrodynamic simulations using iEBE-VISHNU (Shen 2014) and initial state models such as AMPT (Lin 2004) and TᵣENTo (Moreland 2014).
