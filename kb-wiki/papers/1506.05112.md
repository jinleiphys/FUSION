# Understanding the nucleon as a Borromean bound-state

> Analyses of the three valence-quark bound-state problem in relativistic quantum field theory predict that the nucleon may be understood primarily as a Borromean bound-state, in which binding arises mainly from two separate effects. One originates in non-Abelian facets of QCD that are expressed in the strong running coupling and generate confined but strongly-correlated colour-antitriplet diquark clusters in both the scalar-isoscalar and pseudovector-isotriplet channels. That attraction is magnified by quark exchange associated with diquark breakup and reformation. Diquark clustering is driven by the same mechanism which dynamically breaks chiral symmetry in the Standard Model. It has numerous observable consequences, the complete elucidation of which requires a framework that also simultaneously expresses the running of the coupling and masses in the strong interaction. Planned experiments are capable of validating this picture.

# Understanding the nucleon as a Borromean bound-state

## Key claim

The nucleon can be understood as a Borromean bound-state in which binding arises from two separate effects: non-Abelian QCD dynamics that generate confined but strongly-correlated colour-antitriplet diquark clusters, and additional attraction from quark exchange associated with diquark breakup and reformation. This picture is presented as a prediction from analyses of the three valence-quark bound-state problem in relativistic quantum field theory, with the caveat that planned experiments are needed to validate it.

## Method

The authors solve a Poincaré-covariant Faddeev equation for the nucleon, which reduces the three valence-quark scattering problem to a quark+diquark system by exploiting the dominant role of diquark correlations in the two-body subsystems. The Faddeev kernel incorporates both scalar-isoscalar and pseudovector-isotriplet diquark correlations, with the interaction class derived from the renormalisation-group-invariant running coupling that has successfully described many hadron observables. The nucleon electromagnetic current is then evaluated using the complete Faddeev wave function and decomposed into contributions from different diquark components and orbital angular momentum states.

## Key numbers

- Scalar diquark mass-scale: $m_{[ud]_{0^+}} \approx 0.7-0.8$ GeV
- Pseudovector diquark mass-scale: $m_{\{uu\}_{1^+}} \approx 0.9-1.1$ GeV
- Scalar diquark strength in the nucleon: approximately 60%
- Pseudovector diquarks provide roughly 150 MeV of attraction in the Faddeev equation
- The length-scale for flux-tube dissolution in QCD: $r_{\not\sigma} \simeq (1/3)$ fm
- The dominant Faddeev amplitude component $u[ud]$ produces 62% of the proton's normalisation
- The ratio $R_{EM}(Q^2)=\mu_p G_E(Q^2)/G_M(Q^2)$ might become negative for $Q^2 \gtrsim 8$ GeV$^2$
- A zero in $F_1^d$ is predicted at $x \simeq 7$ (where $x=Q^2/M_N^2$)
- Neutron electric form factor data available out to $Q^2=3.4$ GeV$^2$

## Context

This work extends the continuum QCD approach to nucleon structure developed over two decades of studying two- and three-body bound-state problems (Cahill 1987, Bender 1996, Maris 2002, Cloet 2008, Eichmann 2009, Roberts 2011, Segovia 2014). It contrasts with the "Y-junction" flux-tube picture from lattice QCD with static sources (Bissey 2006, 2009), arguing that such a picture is not valid in real-world QCD with light dynamical quarks. The results enable tests of the diquark correlation picture through flavour-separated nucleon form factors, which can be extracted from combined proton and neutron electromagnetic form factor data (Cates 2011). The predicted zero in $F_1^d$ would provide definitive evidence against the interpretation of precocious scaling in $F_2^p/F_1^p$ as a perturbative QCD effect.
