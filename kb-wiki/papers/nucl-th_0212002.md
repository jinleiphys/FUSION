# Application of Absorbing Boundary Condition to Nuclear Breakup Reactions

> Absorbing boundary condition approach to nuclear breakup reactions is investigated. A key ingredient of the method is an absorbing potential outside the physical area, which simulates the outgoing boundary condition for scattered waves. After discretizing the radial variables, the problem results in a linear algebraic equation with a sparse coefficient matrix, to which efficient iterative methods can be applicable. No virtual state such as discretized continuum channel needs to be introduced in the method. Basic aspects of the method are discussed by considering a nuclear two-body scattering problem described with an optical potential. We then apply the method to the breakup reactions of deuterons described in a three-body direct reaction model. Results employing the absorbing boundary condition are found to accurately coincide with those of the existing method which utilizes discretized continuum channels.

# Application of Absorbing Boundary Condition to Nuclear Breakup Reactions

## Key claim

The absorbing boundary condition (ABC) method provides an alternative fully quantum mechanical description of nuclear breakup reactions that accurately reproduces results from the existing coupled discretized continuum channels (CDCC) method, without requiring the introduction of virtual discretized continuum states, provided the absorbing potential parameters satisfy a WKB-derived condition to minimize reflected waves.

## Method

The authors replace the standard outgoing-wave boundary condition by adding a finite, space-dependent absorbing potential outside the physical region, converting the scattering problem into a linear algebraic equation with a sparse coefficient matrix solved by the Bi-conjugate gradient method with preconditioning. For the three-body case, absorbing potentials are placed in both the projectile-target relative coordinate (R) and the core-neutron relative coordinate (r), with linear spatial dependence parameterized by strength, radius, and thickness. The radial coupled-channel equation is discretized using the discrete variable representation (DVR) on uniform grids, and the scattered wave function is obtained by imposing vanishing boundary conditions at the outer grid boundaries.

## Key numbers

- For the test case of $^{16}$O + $^{12}$C elastic scattering at $E_{\text{lab.}}$ = 139.2 MeV, the absorbing potential parameters were $r_c$ = 20 fm, $\Delta r$ = 10 fm, with grid spacing 0.2 fm; only $W_{\text{abs}}$ = 200 MeV satisfied the WKB condition of Eq. (Wcond).
- For the $d$ + $^{58}$Ni reaction at incident deuteron energy $E_d$ = 80 MeV, the radial regions were treated up to 50 fm, with absorbing potentials active at $R, r > 25$ fm; parameters were $W_{PT}$ = 50 MeV, $R_c$ = 25 fm, $\Delta R$ = 25 fm, $W_{nC}$ = 20 MeV, $r_c$ = 25 fm, $\Delta r$ = 25 fm.
- The deuteron binding energy from the Gaussian $n$-$p$ potential ($V_0$ = -72.15 MeV, $a_0$ = 1.484 fm) was 2.2177 MeV, reproduced to better than 0.0001 MeV accuracy with DVR grid spacing of 0.5 fm for the $p$-$n$ coordinate.
- The matrix dimension for the $d$ + $^{58}$Ni calculation was $N_{\text{dim}} = 10^5$, with $N_J = 4$ angular momentum channels, $N_R = 250$ grid points for $R$ (spacing 0.2 fm), and $N_r = 100$ grid points for $r$ (spacing 0.5 fm), for total angular momentum $J$ up to 100 including $l = 0$ and $l = 2$ for the $p$-$n$ relative angular momentum.

## Context

This work extends the absorbing boundary condition method, previously developed for chemical reactions (Kosloff, Neuhauser, Seideman), to nuclear breakup reactions described as three-body direct reactions. The ABC results for $d$ + $^{58}$Ni at 80 MeV are compared with the CDCC method of Yahiro (1986), showing accurate agreement. The approach avoids the discretized continuum channels required by the standard CDCC method (Yahiro, Austern, Sakuragi) and offers an alternative for describing reactions of weakly-bound projectiles including halo nuclei (Tanihata), with the potential to handle three-body continuum final states without explicit boundary condition treatment.
