# Neutron star properties from modern meson-exchange potential models

> We calculate the total mass, radius, moment of inertia and surface gravitational redshift for neutron stars using various equations of state. The latter are derived from recent meson-exchange potential models, employing both a relativistic and a non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach.

## Key claim

The paper calculates neutron star properties using equations of state derived from modern meson-exchange potential models, finding that while both non-relativistic and relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approaches yield acceptable values for moment of inertia and gravitational redshift, the relativistic equation of state produces too large mass and radius values, with the authors noting that the differences between potential models A, B, and C are small for neutron matter.

## Method

The authors derived equations of state for pure neutron matter using both non-relativistic and relativistic (Dirac-Brueckner) Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approaches, starting from the Bonn meson-exchange potential models (A, B, and C) as defined in table A.2 of Machleidt (1989). They employed the model-space Brueckner-Hartree-Fock method with a cut-off parameter a=2, solving the Bethe-Goldstone equation self-consistently, and then used the resulting equations of state as input to the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations to compute neutron star mass, radius, moment of inertia, and surface gravitational redshift.

## Key numbers

- The neutron drip density is approximately 4 × 10^11 g/cm^3
- The density at which nuclei begin to dissolve is approximately 3 × 10^14 g/cm^3
- The minimum stable neutron star mass is approximately 0.1 solar masses, with corresponding central density of approximately 10^14 g/cm^3 and radius of approximately 200 km
- All microscopic calculations give maximum neutron star mass below 2.7 solar masses
- Observations of binary pulsars give a common mass region of 1.2 to 1.8 solar masses
- The upper limit from general relativity and causality is approximately 3-5 solar masses
- Stiff equations of state predict maximum mass in the range 1.5-2.7 solar masses
- Rotation may increase maximum neutron star mass by less than 20%

## Context

This work extends the Dirac-Brueckner approach of Brockmann and Machleidt (1990) and Machleidt, Brockmann, and Müther (1990) to neutron star calculations, building on the Bonn meson-exchange potential models. The study aims to discriminate between non-relativistic and relativistic equations of state for neutron matter, addressing the long-standing problem that non-relativistic calculations fail to simultaneously reproduce the binding energy per nucleon (-16 ± 1 MeV) and saturation density (Fermi momentum 1.35 ± 0.05 fm^-1) of symmetric nuclear matter, a deficiency known as the "Coester band." The results enable comparison with astronomical observations of binary pulsars and help constrain the equation of state at high densities, though the authors note that at densities above 10^15 g/cm^3, hyperons and more complete relativistic treatments may be needed.
