# Unpolarized structure functions at Jefferson Lab

> Over the past decade measurements of unpolarized structure functions at Jefferson Lab with unprecedented precision have significantly advanced our knowledge of nucleon structure. These have for the first time allowed quantitative tests of the phenomenon of quark-hadron duality, and provided a deeper understanding of the transition from hadron to quark degrees of freedom in inclusive scattering. Dedicated Rosenbluth-separation experiments have yielded high-precision transverse and longitudinal structure functions in regions previously unexplored, and new techniques have enabled the first glimpses of the structure of the free neutron, without contamination from nuclear effects.

## Key claim

Jefferson Lab's high-precision measurements of unpolarized structure functions have enabled the first quantitative tests of quark-hadron duality and provided a deeper understanding of the transition from hadron to quark degrees of freedom, but these tests rely on accounting for target mass corrections and higher twist effects that become significant at the large-\(x\) and low-\(Q^2\) kinematics accessible at the lab.

## Method

The paper reviews inclusive electron-nucleon scattering measurements at Jefferson Lab using the CEBAF accelerator, where cross sections were measured with the HMS spectrometer in Hall C (with better than 1.6% systematic point-to-point uncertainties and typically less than 1.8% normalization uncertainties) and with the CLAS spectrometer in Hall B. Rosenbluth separation (longitudinal-transverse separation) was performed by measuring at multiple values of the virtual photon polarization parameter \(\epsilon\) at fixed \(x\) and \(Q^2\) to extract the transverse structure function \(F_1\) and the longitudinal structure function \(F_L\) separately. Structure function moments and truncated moments were computed from the data and compared to leading-twist parton distribution function (PDF) fits, with target mass corrections applied using the operator product expansion formalism.

## Key numbers

- Proton \(F_2^p\) data were taken over a range from \(Q^2\) as low as 0.1 GeV\(^2\) up to \(Q^2 = 8\) GeV\(^2\).
- LT-separated cross sections in Hall C achieved better than 1.6% systematic point-to-point uncertainties and typically less than 1.8% normalization uncertainties.
- The \(n=2\) moments of the proton \(F_2^p\), \(F_1^p\), and \(F_L^p\) structure functions show agreement with PDF fits down to \(Q^2 \approx 1\) GeV\(^2\) when target mass corrections are included.
- Truncated moment analysis at \(Q^2 = 1\) GeV\(^2\) shows that the leading twist moment differs from the data by approximately 15% for \(W_{\rm max} > 1.5\) GeV when target mass corrections are included.
- Higher twist contributions in the \(\Delta(1232)\) resonance region decrease from approximately \(-15\%\) of the data at \(Q^2 = 1\) GeV\(^2\) to values consistent with zero at larger \(Q^2\).
- Higher twist contributions in the \(S_{11}(1535)\) region vary between approximately \(-15\%\) of the data at \(Q^2 = 1\) GeV\(^2\) and 20–25% at \(Q^2 \sim 5\) GeV\(^2\).
- The combined higher twist contribution from the first two resonance regions is less than or about 15% in magnitude for all \(Q^2\).

## Context

This work extends the legacy of early SLAC measurements by Friedman, Kendall, and Taylor (1990 Nobel Prize) and the Bloom-Gilman duality observations from the 1970s, providing the first high-precision data in the resonance region up to \(Q^2 \approx 8\) GeV\(^2\). The results have motivated a re-evaluation of the applicability of perturbative QCD at low \(Q^2\) and have allowed a much larger body of data to be used in global PDF analyses such as CTEQ6X, which previously imposed cuts of \(Q^2 \gtorder 4\) GeV\(^2\) and \(W^2 \gtorder 12\) GeV\(^2\). The measurements pave the way toward the 12 GeV experimental program at Jefferson Lab, where structure functions will be measured to very high \(x\) in the DIS region to address long-standing questions about the behavior of PDFs as \(x \to 1\).
