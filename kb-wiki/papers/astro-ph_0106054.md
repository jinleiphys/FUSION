# Signal of Quark Deconfinement in Millisecond Pulsars and Reconfinement in Accreting X-ray Neutron Stars

> Theoretically, the phase transition between the confined and deconfined phases of quarks can have a remarkable effect on the spin properties of millisecond pulsars and on the spin distribution of the population of x-ray neutron stars in low-mass binaries. In the latter class of stars, the effect has already been observed---a strong clustering in the population in a narrow band of spins. The observed clustering cannot presently be uniquely assigned to the phase transition as cause. However, there is another possible signal---not so far observed---in millisecond pulsars that we also discuss. It would have the same origin, and its discovery would tend to confirm the interpretation in terms of a phase transition in the stellar core and that the high density phase exists in the higer mass canonical neutron stars.

## Key claim

The observed clustering of accreting X-ray neutron stars in a narrow spin-frequency band could be caused by the gradual disappearance (reconfinement) of a quark matter phase as the star is spun up by accretion, but this clustering cannot presently be uniquely assigned to the phase transition as the cause; a second, as-yet-unobserved signal, spontaneous spinup of isolated millisecond pulsars, would, if discovered, tend to confirm the phase-transition interpretation.

## Method

The authors compute the moment of inertia of rotating neutron stars in general relativity (to second order in angular velocity) using an equation of state for confined nuclear matter described by a covariant mean-field model with the baryon octet, and for quark matter described by the MIT bag model. For isolated millisecond pulsars, they integrate the magnetic dipole energy-loss equation including the dependence of moment of inertia on rotation frequency to find the spin evolution. For accreting X-ray neutron stars, they solve the spin-evolution equation including accretion torque, magnetic torque, and a time-dependent magnetic field decaying from 10¹² G to 10⁸ G over 10⁶ years.

## Key numbers

- The critical period for the growth of a pure quark matter core in the model is P = 2π/Ω ∼ 4.6 ms.
- The epoch of spinup for an isolated millisecond pulsar undergoing the phase transition is estimated to endure for 2 × 10⁷ years, compared to a spin-down timescale of ∼ 10⁹ years, implying about 1 in 50 isolated millisecond pulsars should be spinning up.
- The Crab pulsar has period P ∼ 1/30 s, period derivative Ṗ ∼ 4 × 10⁻¹³ s/s, inferred moment of inertia I ∼ 9 × 10⁴⁴ g cm², surface magnetic field B ∼ 4 × 10¹² G, and rotational energy E_rot ∼ 2 × 10⁴⁹ erg.
- PSR 1937+21 has period P = 1.5578064487275(3) ms and period derivative Ṗ ∼ 10⁻¹⁹ s/s; two measurements spaced 0.3 hours apart could detect the direction of period change.
- The Alfvén radius for a canonical accreter (Ṁ = 10⁻¹⁰ M⊙ yr⁻¹, B ∼ 10¹² G) is r_A = 7 × 10³ km; for a weak-field accreter (B ∼ 10⁸ G) it shrinks to ∼ 40 km.
- The initial mass of the star in the examples is 1.42 M⊙.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier studies (Glendenning 1997) of phase transitions in rotating neutron stars and applies the same mechanism to accreting X-ray neutron stars in low-mass binaries, which are believed to be the evolutionary link between canonical pulsars and millisecond pulsars (Alpar 1982, van den Heuvel 1991, van der Klis 1998, Chakrabarty 1998). The predicted clustering of accreting neutron stars in spin frequency was subsequently observed in data from the Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer (RXTE), reviewed by van der Klis (2000). The paper also draws an analogy to the backbending phenomenon in rotating atomic nuclei (Johnson 1972, Stephens 1972, Mottelson and Valatin 1960). The results enable a testable observational program: searching for isolated millisecond pulsars that are spinning up rather than down, which would provide a second signal of quark deconfinement in neutron star cores.
