# A chiral lagrangian with Broken Scale: testing the restoration of symmetries in astrophysics and in the laboratory

> We study matter at high density and temperature using a chiral lagrangian in which the breaking of scale invariance is regulated by the value of a scalar field, called dilaton \cite{Heide:1993yz,Carter:1995zi,Carter:1996rf,Carter:1997fn}. We provide a phase diagram describing the restoration of chiral and scale symmetries. We show that chiral symmetry is restored at large temperatures, but at low temperatures it remains broken at all densities. We also show that scale invariance is more easily restored at low rather than large baryon densities. The masses of vector mesons scale with the value of the dilaton and their values initially slightly decrease with the density but then they increase again for densities larger than $\sim 3 ρ_0$. The pion mass increases continuously with the density and at $ρ_0$ and T=0 its value is $\sim$ 30 MeV larger than in the vacuum. We show that the model is compatible with the bounds stemming from astrophysics, as e.g. the one associated with the maximum mass of a neutron star. The most striking feature of the model is a very significant softening at large densities, which manifests also as a strong reduction of the adiabatic index. While the softening has probably no consequence for Supernova explosion via the direct mechanism, it could modify the signal in gravitational waves associated with the merging of two neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in a chiral lagrangian with broken scale invariance, chiral symmetry is restored at large temperatures but remains broken at all densities at low temperatures, while scale invariance is more easily restored at low baryon densities than at large ones; these results depend on the inclusion of thermal fluctuations of vector meson fields, which reduce the critical temperature for scale restoration to a more realistic value compared to earlier work by Carter et al. (1996, 1997).

## Method

The authors study symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter using the chiral-dilaton model (CDM), a mean-field lagrangian containing chiral fields (sigma and pions), a dilaton field representing the gluon condensate, and vector meson fields (omega and rho). They compute the thermodynamic potential at finite temperature and density, including thermal fluctuations of the chiral and vector meson fields via a resummation technique developed by Carter et al. (1996, 1997), and minimize the thermodynamic quantities directly to obtain field values, effective masses, and the equation of state.

## Key numbers

- At zero baryon density, the chiral restoration temperature in the chiral limit is T_C = 165 MeV, close to the QCD critical temperature from lattice QCD.
- At nuclear saturation density rho_0 and T=0, the pion mass is approximately 30 MeV larger than in the vacuum.
- The masses of vector mesons initially decrease slightly with density but then increase again for densities larger than approximately 3 rho_0.
- The symmetry energy at saturation density rho_0 is set to E_sym = 35 MeV.
- The vacuum energy parameter |epsilon_vac|^(1/4) = 228 MeV.
- The critical temperature for chiral restoration in the chiral limit tends asymptotically to approximately 40 MeV at very large densities.
- The dilaton field drops to zero at temperatures greater than or approximately equal to 196 MeV, initially in the small density region.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the chiral-dilaton model developed by the University of Minnesota group (Heide et al. 1993; Carter et al. 1995, 1996, 1997) by providing detailed predictions for quantities measurable in heavy-ion collision experiments, such as compressibility and meson masses at large densities, and by testing the model at large baryon and isospin densities relevant for astrophysics. The authors show that including thermal fluctuations of vector meson fields reduces the critical temperature for scale restoration from the unrealistically large value obtained in Carter et al. (1996, 1997) to a more realistic one, and they provide a phase diagram in the energy-density versus baryon-density plane that can be compared with estimates from heavy-ion collision models. The results enable future work incorporating hyperonic and quark degrees of freedom, and suggest that the significant softening of the equation of state at large densities could modify gravitational wave signals from neutron star mergers.
