# Amplitude- and truncated partial-wave analyses combined: A novel, almost theory-independent single-channel method for extracting photoproduction multipoles directly from measured data

> Amplitude- and truncated partial-wave analyses are combined into a single procedure and a novel, almost theory-independent single-channel method for extracting multipoles directly from measured data is developed. In practice, we have created a two-step procedure which is fitted to the same data base: in the first step we perform an energy independent amplitude analysis where continuity is achieved by constraining the amplitude phase, and the result of this first step is then taken as a constraint for the second step where a constrained, energy independent, truncated partial-wave analysis is done. The method is tested on the world collection of data for $η$ photoproduction, and the obtained fit-results are very good. The sensitivity to different possible choices of amplitude phase is investigated and it is demonstrated that the present data base is insensitive to notable phase changes, due to an incomplete database. New measurements are recommended to remedy the problem.

## Key claim

The authors propose a novel two-step single-channel method that combines amplitude analysis and truncated partial-wave analysis to extract photoproduction multipoles directly from measured data with minimal model dependence, but they acknowledge that the method's phase constraint still relies on a theoretical model (Bonn-Gatchina BG2014-02) and that the existing data base is insensitive to notable phase changes due to incompleteness.

## Method

The method proceeds in two steps fitted to the same data base: first, an energy-independent amplitude analysis is performed where continuity is achieved by constraining the amplitude phase using analytic phases from the Bonn-Gatchina model (BG2014-02) or a smoothed version thereof; second, the resulting continuous amplitudes are used as a penalty function in a constrained, energy-independent truncated partial-wave analysis (TPWA) with angular momentum cutoff ℓmax = 5. The absolute values of the four transversity amplitudes are extracted from the single-spin observables σ0, Σ, T, and P, while the phases are fixed to analytic forms; a threshold penalty function enforces the q(W)^L behavior near threshold. The method is tested on the world collection of η photoproduction data from A2@MAMI, GRAAL, and CBELSA/TAPS.

## Key numbers

- Penalty coefficient λpen. = 10 was used, determined as the lower boundary of an estimated optimal window around λpen. ≃ 10 to 50.
- Threshold penalty coefficient λthr. = 2 was used, with the phenomenological function parameter b = mN (nucleon mass).
- The data base includes 2400 σ0 data points from A2@MAMI (2010) at 120 energies from 710 to 1395 MeV lab energy and 20 angles from 18° to 162° in the center-of-mass frame.
- The data base includes 150 Σ data points from GRAAL (2007) at 15 energies from 724 to 1472 MeV lab energy and 10 angles from 40° to 160°.
- The data base includes 144 T and 144 F data points from A2@MAMI (2016) at 12 energies from 725 to 1350 MeV lab energy and 12 angles from 24° to 156°.
- The data base includes 64 E, 66 P, 48 G, and 66 H data points from CBELSA/TAPS (2020) at 6-8 energies from 725 to 1450 MeV lab energy and 8-11 angles.
- The 2-dimensional Pietarinen expansion for the phase used N = 20 energy terms and M = 3 angular terms (Legendre polynomials up to l = 3) for the full fit, and N = 4 energy terms for the smoothed phase.

## Context

This work extends the Karlsruhe-Helsinki fixed-t analyticity approach of Hoehler (1984) for pion-nucleon elastic scattering, which was revived for η photoproduction by Osmanovic (2018), but replaces the complicated fixed-t representation with a simpler fixed-W amplitude analysis. The method addresses the continuum ambiguity problem identified by Atkinson (1973), Bowcock (1975), and Atkinson (1985), which causes discontinuous single-energy partial wave analyses. The results enable extraction of multipoles with reduced model dependence compared to direct penalization of partial waves as done by Workman (2011), but the authors recommend new measurements of double-polarization observables to resolve the phase ambiguity and improve the method's uniqueness.
