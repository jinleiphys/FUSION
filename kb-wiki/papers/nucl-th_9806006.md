# Anti-Lambda/Anti-Proton ratios at the AGS

> We attempt to explain the large ${\bar Λ}/\bar p$ ratios measured in heavy ion collisions at $12~{\rm A\cdot GeV}/c$ beam momentum within an hadronic framework. The ratios are large compared to corresponding ratios in $pp$ collisions, and to thermal fits. We show using a simple model and a detailed cascade calculation that different annihilation cross--sections of $\bar Λ$'s and $\bar p$'s, and the net conversion of $\bar p$'s to $\bar Λ$'s, do not account for the enhancement. Uncertainties in elementary cross--sections and formation times are also considered.

# Anti-Lambda/Anti-Proton Ratios at the AGS

## Key claim

The large $\bar{\Lambda}/\bar{p}$ ratios (around 3) measured in heavy ion collisions at 12 A·GeV/c beam momentum cannot be explained by hadronic mechanisms such as differential annihilation cross-sections or $\bar{p}$-to-$\bar{\Lambda}$ conversion, even when uncertainties in elementary cross-sections and formation times are considered; the authors conclude that a non-hadronic production mechanism, possibly QGP formation, may be required, but note that a more accurate measurement of the $\bar{\Lambda}$ annihilation cross-section is needed before a definite conclusion can be reached.

## Method

The authors performed thermal model fits to central Au+Au collisions at 11.6 A·GeV/c using four parameters (freeze-out temperature, electric, baryonic, and strange chemical potentials) constrained by measured particle ratios, then constructed a simple geometric model with rate equations for $\bar{p}$ and $\bar{\Lambda}$ survival probabilities through nuclear matter, and finally implemented a detailed cascade calculation where $\bar{p}$ and $\bar{\Lambda}$ particles were inserted into simulated Au+Au and Si+Pb collisions at AGS energies, using experimental cross-section data for $\bar{p}$ annihilation and a parameterized model for $\bar{\Lambda}$ annihilation with an adjustable exponent $\alpha$.

## Key numbers

* $\bar{\Lambda}/\bar{p}$ ratio in central Si+Pb collisions measured by E859: $3 \pm 1 \pm 1$ (corrected for acceptance, efficiencies, and feed-down)
* E864 lower bound for $\bar{\Lambda}/\bar{p}$ in Au+Pb at 11.6 A·GeV/c: $> 2.3$ at 98% confidence level, with most probable value 3.5
* $\bar{\Lambda}/\bar{p}$ ratio in $pp$ collisions at AGS energies ($\sqrt{s} \sim 5$ GeV): approximately 0.2-0.25
* Thermal model best-fit parameters for Au+Au at 11.6 A·GeV/c: $T_0 = 120 \pm 14$ MeV, $\mu_b = 556 \pm 19$ MeV, $\mu_s = 111 \pm 14$ MeV, $\mu_e = -14 \pm 2$ MeV
* Thermal model maximum $\bar{\Lambda}/\bar{p}$ ratio: $1.58 \pm 0.30$ (best fit), with upper limit $\sim 1.9$ under reasonable constraints
* $\bar{p}$ annihilation cross-section parameterization: $\sigma_{p\bar{p}}^{annih}(p_{lab}) = 67\, p_{lab}^{-0.7}$ mb
* $\bar{\Lambda}$ annihilation cross-section parameterization: $\sigma_{p\bar{\Lambda}}^{annih}(p_{lab}) = 15\, (p_{lab}/10)^{-\alpha}$ mb, with best-fit $\alpha = 0.5$
* Cascade calculation for Au+Au with $\alpha = 0$ and zero formation time: $\bar{\Lambda}/\bar{p} = 2.4 \pm 1.0$; with $\alpha = 0.5$ (realistic): $\bar{\Lambda}/\bar{p} \sim 1$
* $\bar{p}$ annihilation mean free path: $\lambda_a \sim 1.6$ fm (simple estimate) to 2.0 fm (detailed calculation)
* $\bar{\Lambda}$ annihilation mean free path: $\Lambda_a \approx 4.2\, (3.76)^{-\alpha}$ fm
* Net conversion mean free path for $\bar{p}$ to $\bar{\Lambda}$: $\lambda_c \approx 59$ fm
* $\bar{\Lambda}/\bar{p}$ ratio at SPS (200 A·GeV/c): 0.25 for $pp$, rising to 1.5 for heavy ion systems

## Context

This work extends the analysis of $\bar{\Lambda}/\bar{p}$ ratios reported by the E859 and E864 collaborations at the AGS and the NA35 collaboration at the SPS, challenging the interpretation by Letessier et al. (1995) and Sollfrank et al. (1994) that such ratios require QGP formation. The authors show that hadronic transport models (including RQMD simulations by E864) cannot reproduce the observed enhancement, and that thermal fits by Braun-Munzinger et al. (1995, 1996) and Schnedermann and Heinz (1994) also fail to reach the experimental values. The paper enables future work by identifying the poorly known $\bar{\Lambda}$ annihilation cross-section as the critical experimental input needed to resolve the puzzle, and by providing a simple geometric model that reproduces the cascade results.
