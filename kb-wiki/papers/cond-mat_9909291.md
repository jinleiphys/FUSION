# Stars and statistical physics: a teaching experience

> The physics of stars, their workings and their evolution, is a goldmine of problems in statistical mechanics and thermodynamics. We discuss many examples that illustrate the possibility of deepening student's knowledge of statistical mechanics by an introductory study of stars. The matter constituting the various stellar objects provides examples of equations of state for classical or quantal and relativistic or non-relativistic gases. Maximum entropy can be used to characterize thermodynamic and gravitational equilibrium which determines the structure of stars and predicts their instability above a certain mass. Contraction accompanying radiation induces either heating or cooling, which explains the formation of stars above a minimum mass. The characteristics of the emitted light are understood from black-body radiation and more precisely from the Boltzmann-Lorentz kinetic equation for photons. The luminosity is governed by the transport of heat by photons from the center to the surface. Heat production by thermonuclear fusion is determined by microscopic balance equations. The stability of the steady state of stars is controlled by the interplay of thermodynamics and gravitation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that stellar physics provides an exceptional pedagogical framework for deepening students' understanding of statistical mechanics and thermodynamics, using simple models that account for the main phenomena of stellar structure and evolution without requiring advanced astrophysical training.

## Method

The authors describe a second-year elective course at Ecole Polytechnique that systematically applies statistical mechanics concepts, including maximum entropy, chemical potential, equations of state for classical and quantum gases, and microscopic transport, to explain stellar properties. The course covers equations of state for different stellar objects (main sequence stars, white dwarfs, neutron stars), gravitational equilibrium via entropy maximization, black-body radiation and photon kinetics for light emission, heat transport by photons, and thermonuclear fusion balance equations, using order-of-magnitude estimates and semi-quantitative analyses.

## Context

This paper reports on a teaching experience inspired by the work of Nauenberg and Weisskopf, extending the pedagogical approach of using stellar physics to illustrate statistical mechanics concepts. The course builds on standard results such as the Saha ionization formula, the Chandrasekhar limit, and the Stefan-Boltzmann law, while showing how simple models can explain the existence of three distinct classes of stars (main sequence, white dwarfs, neutron stars) and their stability. The approach enables students to understand stellar evolution, the role of quantum mechanics in preventing gravitational collapse, and the stability of stars against perturbations in nuclear energy production.
