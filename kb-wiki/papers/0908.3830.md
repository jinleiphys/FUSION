# Femtoscopic signatures of collective behavior as a probe of the thermal nature of relativistic heavy ion collisions

> Femtoscopy measures space-time characteristics of the particle emitting source created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. It is argued that collective behavior of matter (radial flow) produces specific femtoscopic signatures. The one that is best known, the m_T dependence of the pion ``HBT radii'', can be explained by the alternative scenario of temperature gradients in an initial state thermal model. We identify others that can invalidate such alternatives, such as non-identical particle correlations and m_T scaling for particles of higher mass. Studies with a simple rescattering code show that as the interaction cross-section is increased the system develops collective behavior and becomes more thermalized at the same time, the two effects being the natural consequence of increased number of particle rescatterings. Repeating calculations with a more realistic rescattering model confirmed all of these conclusions and provided deeper insight into the mechanisms of collectivity buildup, showing a preference for a thermal model with uniform temperature.

# Femtoscopic signatures of collective behavior as a probe of the thermal nature of relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

The well-known m_T dependence of pion HBT radii can be explained either by collective radial flow or by temperature gradients in an initial-state thermal model, but non-identical particle correlations and m_T scaling for heavier particles can distinguish between these two scenarios. The authors show that as the interaction cross-section increases, the system simultaneously develops collective behavior and becomes more thermalized, with the two effects being natural consequences of increased particle rescatterings.

## Method

A simple Monte Carlo rescattering model was initialized with pions and kaons distributed in a Gaussian transverse profile (R = 4.0 fm, longitudinal extent 2.0 fm) with either uniform temperature (300 MeV) or a radial temperature gradient (T_max = 500 MeV, T_base = 100 MeV). Particles underwent elastic rescatterings with varying interaction distance d (0.1 to 10 fm, corresponding to average collisions per particle from 0.15 to 12.9), and the final emission points and momenta were analyzed to extract single-particle source distributions, two-particle HBT radii, and non-identical particle emission asymmetries. Calculations were repeated with a more realistic rescattering model including pions, kaons, nucleons, and low-lying resonances with elastic and inelastic scattering, coupled to three different initial state models including a PYTHIA-based p+p superposition.

## Key numbers

- System transverse Gaussian radius R = 4.0 fm, longitudinal extent z_max = 2.0 fm
- Initial temperatures: uniform case T_base = T_max = 300 MeV; gradient case T_max = 500 MeV, T_base = 100 MeV, R_T = 2.0 fm
- Interaction distances d = 0.1, 1, 2, 5, 10 fm correspond to average collisions per particle of 0.15, 2.2, 4.7, 9.2, and 12.9 respectively
- Each event contained 1000 pions and 100 kaons; 1000 events per parameter set
- Time step for evolution was 0.1 fm in the simple model, 0.5 fm/c in the realistic model
- Realistic rescattering model used proper time tau = 0.1 fm/c for the p+p superposition initial state
- Emission asymmetry between pions and kaons was found to be directly proportional to the average number of rescatterings per particle, with the scaled asymmetry following a fit of a*sqrt(b*<N_c>)

## Context

This work extends studies of collective behavior in relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, where hydrodynamic models (Hirano 2002, Zschiesche 2001, Heinz 2002) describe momentum observables but require specific initial conditions and equations of state to match space-time observables (Broniowski 2008, Pratt 2008). The authors build on earlier parton cascade studies (Zhang 1998, Cheng 2001, Molnar 2002) and blast-wave simulations (Retiere 2003, Kisiel 2006) by systematically varying the interaction cross-section to probe the transition from non-collective to collective behavior. The work enables using non-identical particle correlations, already measured at RHIC (Adams 2003), as a direct probe of the degree of collectivity and thermalization in heavy-ion collisions, with the emission asymmetry shown to be insensitive to temperature gradients and directly proportional to the number of rescatterings per particle.
