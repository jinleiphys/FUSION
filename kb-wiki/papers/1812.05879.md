# Estimating magnetar radii with an empirical meta-model

> The presence of strong magnetic fields in neutron stars, such as in magnetars, may significantly affect their crust-core transition properties and the crust size. This knowledge is crucial in the correct interpretation of astrophysical phenomena involving magnetars, such as glitches in observed rotation frequencies, cooling, bursts and possibly tidal polarizabilities. A recently developed meta-modelling technique allows exploring the model dependence of density functional theory equation of state calculations. In this work, we extend this meta-model to investigate the effect of strong magnetic fields on spinodal instabilities of neutron star matter and the associated crust-core properties. Both Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov and a full self-consistent numerical calculations are performed for the neutron star structure, the results being quantitatively different for strong magnetic fields.

## Key claim

The authors extend a meta-modelling technique for the nuclear equation of state to study how strong magnetic fields affect the crust-core transition in magnetars, finding that the effect is model-dependent and that the appearance of multiple stable/unstable layers predicted by some relativistic mean-field models is not a general feature.

## Method

The authors use a meta-modelling approach based on density functional theory, where the energy per particle of homogeneous nuclear matter is expressed as an expansion around saturation density in terms of empirical nuclear parameters. They introduce magnetic field effects by modifying the kinetic energy contributions of protons and electrons through Landau level quantization, while leaving neutrons unaffected. The crust-core transition point is determined from both thermodynamic spinodals (curvature matrix of the energy density) and dynamical spinodals (including finite-size surface and Coulomb terms), and the neutron star structure is computed using both the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) formalism and a full self-consistent numerical solution of the Einstein-Maxwell equations.

## Key numbers

- The critical quantizing field for electrons is \(B^e_c = 4.4 \times 10^{13}\) G; magnetic fields are expressed in units of \(B_* = B/B^e_c\)
- For the SLy5 functional, the thermodynamic spinodal shows negligible effect up to \(B_* = 10^2\) (i.e., \(4.4 \times 10^{15}\) G), but above \(B_* = 10^3\) the spinodal region splits into alternating stable and unstable regions
- For the dynamical spinodal of SLy5 at \(B_* = 10^4\), the transition density interval is \(\Delta \rho_{PT} = 0.034\) fm\(^{-3}\) (from \(\rho_1 = 0.043\) to \(\rho_2 = 0.077\) fm\(^{-3}\)) and the pressure interval is \(\Delta p_{PT} = 0.204\) MeV/fm\(^{-3}\)
- For the thermodynamic spinodal of SLy5 at \(B_* = 10^4\), the transition density interval is \(\Delta \rho_{PT} = 0.065\) fm\(^{-3}\) and the pressure interval is \(\Delta p_{PT} = 0.744\) MeV/fm\(^{-3}\)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of magnetar crust-core transitions that used relativistic mean-field models and the Vlasov formalism for dynamical spinodals (cp1, cp2, cp3), but finds that the appearance of multiple stable/unstable layers is model-dependent rather than universal. The meta-modelling technique, originally developed by Casali et al. (2014) and applied to neutron stars and nuclei, allows the authors to systematically explore the influence of poorly constrained empirical parameters like the slope of the symmetry energy (\(L_{sym}\)) and its curvature (\(K_{sym}\)), whose influence increases with magnetic field strength. The results enable more reliable predictions of magnetar radii and crust properties needed for interpreting glitches, cooling, bursts, and tidal polarizabilities from gravitational wave events like GW170817.
