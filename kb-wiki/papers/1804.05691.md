# Centrality dependence of freeze-out temperature fluctuations in Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC

> Many data in the High Energy Physics are, in fact, sample means. It is shown that when this exact meaning of the data is taken into account and the most weakly bound states are removed from the hadron resonance gas, the whole spectra of pions, kaons and protons measured at midrapidity in Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 2.76$ TeV can be fitted simultaneously. The invariant distributions are predicted with the help of the single-freeze-out model in the chemical equilibrium framework. The method is applied to the measurements in centrality bins of Pb-Pb collisions and gives acceptable fits for all but peripheral bins. The comparison with the results obtained in the framework of the original single-freeze-out model is also presented. Some more general, possible implications of this approach are pointed out.

## Key claim
The paper claims that the simultaneous fit of pion, kaon, and proton spectra in Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 2.76$ TeV can be achieved only when two conditions are applied together: the freeze-out temperature is treated as a random variable (rather than a fixed parameter) and the most weakly bound resonances (full width $\Gamma > 250$ MeV) are removed from the hadron resonance gas. The claim is hedged by the fact that acceptable fits are obtained for all centrality bins except the most peripheral ones (60-80%), and that in mid-central bins the simpler original Single-Freeze-Out Model (without randomization) also gives acceptable fits.

## Method
The authors generalize the Single-Freeze-Out Model (SFOM) by treating the freeze-out temperature $T_f$ (or equivalently $\beta_f = 1/T_f$) as a random variable with either a log-normal or triangular probability density function, then averaging the theoretical invariant distribution over this distribution. They also remove all resonances with full width $\Gamma > 250$ MeV and masses below 1600 MeV from the hadron gas. The model parameters (geometric: $\tau_f$, $\rho_{max}$; thermal: parameters of the p.d.f. for $\beta_f$) are estimated simultaneously by fitting the averaged distributions to the measured $p_T$ spectra of pions, kaons, and protons at midrapidity ($|y| < 0.5$) for nine centrality classes.

## Key numbers
- For the 0-5% most central bin with log-normal p.d.f.: $E[T_f] = 116.7 \pm 3.0$ MeV, $\sqrt{V[T_f]} = 20.7 \pm 1.6$ MeV, $\chi^2/n_{dof} = 1.048$, $p$-value = 29%.
- For the 0-5% bin with triangular p.d.f.: $E[T_f] = 111.6$ MeV, $\sqrt{V[T_f]} = 22.6$ MeV, $\chi^2/n_{dof} = 1.026$, $p$-value = 38%.
- For the 0-5% bin using the original SFOM (no randomization, all resonances included): $T_f = 147.0 \pm 0.5$ MeV, $\chi^2/n_{dof} = 1.739$, $p$-value = $1.6 \times 10^{-9}$.
- For the 10-20% bin with log-normal p.d.f.: $E[T_f] = 122.2 \pm 3.0$ MeV, $\sqrt{V[T_f]} = 19.6 \pm 1.7$ MeV, $\chi^2/n_{dof} = 0.589$, $p$-value = 100%.
- For the 50-60% bin with log-normal p.d.f.: $E[T_f] = 134.7 \pm 3.6$ MeV, $\sqrt{V[T_f]} = 16.7 \pm 2.3$ MeV, $\chi^2/n_{dof} = 1.373$, $p$-value = 0.013%.
- For the 70-80% bin with log-normal p.d.f.: $E[T_f] = 132.5 \pm 3.6$ MeV, $\sqrt{V[T_f]} = 17.6 \pm 2.2$ MeV, $\chi^2/n_{dof} = 4.360$, $p$-value = 0%.
- The removed resonances (with $\Gamma > 250$ MeV) include $f_0(500)$, $h_1(1170)$, $a_1(1260)$, $\pi(1300)$, $f_0(1370)$, $\pi_1(1400)$, $a_0(1450)$, $\rho(1450)$, $K^*_0(1430)$, and $N(1440)$.

## Context
This work extends the author's earlier Randomized Single-Freeze-Out Model (RSFOM) applied to the most central bin (Prorok 2015) to all centrality classes of Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 2.76$ TeV. It addresses the "proton/pion puzzle" where the Statistical Model overestimates the $p/\pi$ ratio by a factor of about 1.5 (Floris 2014). The approach offers an alternative to five existing explanations: undiscovered high-mass resonances, non-equilibrium thermal models, hadronic inelastic interactions after hadronization (especially baryon annihilation), flavor hierarchy at freeze-out, and inclusion of resonance spectral functions (Huovinen 2016, Vovchenko 2018). The results enable a classification of centrality bins into three groups: the two most central bins where freeze-out temperature fluctuates significantly, mid-central bins where the original SFOM (with $T_f \sim 150$ MeV) works as at RHIC (Prorok 2007), and peripheral bins where both models fail.
