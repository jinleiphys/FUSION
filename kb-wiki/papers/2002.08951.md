# Bayesian Inference of Dense Matter Equation of State within Relativistic Mean Field Models using Astrophysical Measurements

> We present a Bayesian analysis to constrain the equation of state of dense nucleonic matter by exploiting the available data from symmetric nuclear matter at saturation and from observations of compact X-ray sources and from the gravitational wave event GW170817. For the first time, such analysis is performed by using a class of models, the relativistic mean field models, which allow to consistently construct an equation of state in a wide range of densities, isospin asymmetries and temperatures. The selected class of models contains five nuclear physics empirical parameters at saturation for which we construct the joint posterior distributions. By exploring different types of priors, we find that the equations of state with the largest evidence are the ones featuring a strong reduction of the effective mass of the nucleons in dense matter which can be interpreted as an indication of a phase transition to a chiral symmetry restored phase. Those equations of state in turn predict $R_{1.4} \sim 12$ km. Finally, we present a preliminary investigation on the effect of including $Λ$ hyperons showing that they appear in stars more massive than about $1.6 M_{\odot}$ and lead to radii larger than about $R_{1.4} \sim 14$ km. Within the model here explored, the formation of such particles provide a poor agreement with the constraints from GW170817.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the equations of state with the largest Bayesian evidence are those featuring a strong reduction of the nucleon effective mass in dense matter, which the authors interpret as a possible indication of a phase transition to a chiral symmetry restored phase; however, this conclusion is conditional on the specific class of relativistic mean field models used and on the choice of priors, with the most probable values of the symmetry energy slope parameter L typically falling outside the currently accepted range of 40-60 MeV.

## Method

The authors performed a Bayesian analysis using relativistic mean field models with five nuclear empirical parameters at saturation (effective mass, incompressibility, saturation density, symmetry energy, and binding energy per nucleon), constructing joint posterior distributions via Markov-Chain Monte Carlo simulations. They used mass-radius distributions from fifteen astrophysical sources including six thermonuclear bursters, five quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries, the source 4U 1702-429, the NICER observation of PSR J0030+0451, and the gravitational wave event GW170817, applying five different prior types (Baseline, Marg_unif, Marg_Gauss, Wide_unif, Wide_Gauss) and also investigated the inclusion of Lambda hyperons.

## Key numbers

- The most probable radius for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star (R1.4) is approximately 12.58 km for the Baseline prior, with a 68% confidence interval range of 12.48-13.30 km
- For the Wide_unif prior, the most probable R1.4 is 11.70 km (first mode) and 12.15 km (second mode), with a 68% CI range of 11.16-12.78 km
- The most probable maximum mass for the Baseline prior is 2.01 solar masses, with a 68% CI range of 2.00-2.24 solar masses
- For the Wide_unif prior with Lambda hyperons, the most probable R1.4 is 12.80 km and the most probable maximum mass is 2.01 solar masses
- The median R1.4 for the Baseline prior is 12.95 km with uncertainties of -0.20 and +0.25 km
- The most probable symmetry energy slope parameter L is 81.2 MeV for the Baseline prior and 74.7 MeV (first mode) or 49.8 MeV (second mode) for the Wide_unif prior

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian inference approach pioneered by Steiner (2010) for constraining the neutron star equation of state, but differs from previous studies that used piecewise polytropic or spectral parametrizations by instead employing relativistic mean field models that can consistently describe matter across a wide range of densities, isospin asymmetries, and temperatures. The approach enables future studies of finite-temperature effects relevant for supernova and merger simulations, and the inclusion of hyperons and other baryonic degrees of freedom, though the authors note that the model cannot incorporate higher-order symmetry energy derivatives such as the slope L or curvature Ksym that have been shown to be relevant in work by Xie (2019).
