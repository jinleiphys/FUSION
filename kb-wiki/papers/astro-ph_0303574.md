# The first second of the Universe

> The history of the Universe after its first second is now tested by high quality observations of light element abundances and temperature anisotropies of the cosmic microwave background. The epoch of the first second itself has not been tested directly yet; however, it is constrained by experiments at particle and heavy ion accelerators. Here I attempt to describe the epoch between the electroweak transition and the primordial nucleosynthesis. The most dramatic event in that era is the quark--hadron transition at 10 $μ$s. Quarks and gluons condense to form a gas of nucleons and light mesons, the latter decay subsequently. At the end of the first second, neutrinos and neutrons decouple from the radiation fluid. The quark--hadron transition and dissipative processes during the first second prepare the initial conditions for the synthesis of the first nuclei. As for the cold dark matter (CDM), WIMPs (weakly interacting massive particles) -- the most popular candidates for the CDM -- decouple from the presently known forms of matter, chemically (freeze-out) at 10 ns and kinetically at 1 ms. The chemical decoupling fixes their present abundances and dissipative processes during and after thermal decoupling set the scale for the very first WIMP clouds.

## Key claim

The epoch of the first second of the Universe, between the electroweak transition and primordial nucleosynthesis, has not been tested directly by observations but is constrained by particle and heavy ion accelerator experiments; however, details of the QCD transition and the nature of cold dark matter remain unknown, so the conclusions rely on yet untested models.

## Method

The paper reviews the thermodynamic history of the early Universe from the electroweak transition (T ≈ 100–200 GeV) to the onset of primordial nucleosynthesis (T ≈ 1 MeV), comparing the Hubble expansion rate with interaction rates for weak, electromagnetic, and strong processes. It examines the effective number of relativistic helicity degrees of freedom as a function of temperature, the QCD transition at approximately 10 μs, and the chemical and kinetic decoupling of neutrinos, neutrons, and weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs), using results from lattice QCD, heavy ion experiments, and particle physics models.

## Key numbers

- The QCD transition occurs at approximately 10 μs, with a transition temperature T⋆ ≈ 160 MeV (selected value, with lattice QCD giving 171 ± 4 MeV for two-flavour QCD and 154 ± 8 MeV for three-flavour QCD).
- The Hubble radius at the QCD transition is about 10 km: 7.2 km before the transition (g = 61.75) and 12 km after it (g = 21.25), for T⋆ = 160 MeV.
- The mass inside a Hubble volume at the QCD transition is approximately 2.5 M⊙ (for g = 61.75, T⋆ = 160 MeV).
- The baryon number inside a Hubble volume at the QCD transition is approximately 2.1 × 10⁴⁸ (for η = 5.6 × 10⁻¹⁰, T⋆ = 160 MeV, g = 61.75).
- The typical bubble nucleation distance for a first-order QCD transition with homogeneous nucleation is about 1 cm, or 10⁻⁶ of the Hubble radius.
- The latent heat in quenched lattice QCD is l ≈ 1.4 T⋆⁴, giving a ratio R_L = 0.2 relative to the Stefan–Boltzmann value (bag model gives R_L = 1).
- The hadron chemical freeze-out temperature at RHIC is T = 174 ± 7 MeV at a baryon chemical potential μB = 46 ± 5 MeV (statistical errors only).
- The baryon-to-photon ratio from standard BBN and deuterium measurements is η = (5.6 ± 0.5) × 10⁻¹⁰.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on the early Universe that began with studies of the cosmological QCD transition in the early 1980s (Hogan 1983, Witten 1984, Applegate and Hogan 1985) and summarizes the understanding of the epoch between the electroweak transition and primordial nucleosynthesis. It connects particle physics results from lattice QCD (Karsch, Boyd, Iwasaki) and heavy ion experiments at CERN and RHIC (Braun-Munzinger) to cosmology, and discusses implications for big bang nucleosynthesis, dark matter (WIMPs, QCD balls, axions), and structure formation. The paper enables future work on the initial conditions for BBN, the formation of small CDM clumps, and the modification of primordial gravitational waves by the QCD transition.
