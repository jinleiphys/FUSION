# Dynamical coupled-channel models for hadron dynamics

> Dynamical coupled-channel (DCC) approaches parametrize the interactions and dynamics of two and more hadrons and their response to different electroweak probes. The inclusion of unitarity, three-body channels, and other properties from scattering theory allows for a reliable extraction of resonance spectra and their properties from data. We review the formalism and application of the ANL-Osaka, the Juelich-Bonn-Washington, and other DCC approaches in the context of light baryon resonances from meson, (virtual) photon, and neutrino-induced reactions, as well as production reactions, strange baryons, light mesons, heavy meson systems, exotics, and baryon-baryon interactions. Finally, we also provide a connection of the formalism to study finite-volume spectra obtained in Lattice QCD, and review applications involving modern statistical and machine learning tools.

## Key claim
Dynamical coupled-channel (DCC) approaches provide a reliable framework for extracting resonance spectra and properties from data by incorporating unitarity, three-body channels, and other scattering-theory constraints, but the practical implementations (ANL-Osaka and Juelich-Bonn-Washington) differ in how they treat energy dependence of interactions and the elimination of virtual processes through unitary transformations versus time-ordered perturbation theory.

## Method
The ANL-Osaka approach applies a unitary transformation to a field-theoretical Hamiltonian to eliminate virtual processes, yielding an energy-independent effective interaction in a Fock space of bare resonances, two-body meson-baryon states, and ππN three-body states. The Juelich-Bonn-Washington approach uses relativistic time-ordered perturbation theory with energy-dependent interactions derived from the Wess-Zumino Lagrangian, iterated in a Lippmann-Schwinger equation. Both approaches solve coupled-channel scattering equations along complex spectator-momentum contours to avoid three-body singularities, with analytic continuation to real momenta handled via the Cahill-Sloan method for triangle singularities.

## Key numbers
- The on-shell momentum for two-body channels is given by p_cm = (1/(2E)) sqrt((E^2 - (m_a - m_b)^2)(E^2 - (m_a + m_b)^2)), where E = sqrt(s) is the total energy and m_a, m_b are particle masses.
- The numerical T-matrix dimension for n_κ channels of two stable particles with n_SMC mesh points is n_κ · (n_SMC + 1).
- The self-energy for the πΔ channel involves an integral over loop momentum q with the Δ → πN coupling Γ_Δ,πN(q) and the πN mass M_πN(q) = ω_π(q) + E_N(q).
- The three-body singularity condition is given by sqrt(s) - E_{p'} - E_p - E_{p'+p} = 0, with solutions p_± expressed in terms of incoming/outgoing spectator masses m_1, m_3 and exchanged mass m_u.

## Context
This review consolidates the formalism of the ANL-Osaka model (developed by Sato, Lee, and collaborators) and the Juelich-Bonn-Washington model (developed by Haidenbauer, Meißner, and collaborators), extending earlier work on baryon spectroscopy from meson and photon-induced reactions. It connects DCC approaches to finite-volume spectra from Lattice QCD and to modern statistical and machine learning tools, enabling future extraction of resonance parameters from neutrino-induced reactions, heavy meson systems, and exotic hadrons.
