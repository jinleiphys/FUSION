# Big-Bang Nucleosynthesis verifies classical Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution

> We provide the most stringent constraint to date on possible deviations from the usually-assumed Maxwell-Boltzmann (MB) velocity distribution for nuclei in the Big-Bang plasma. The impact of non-extensive Tsallis statistics on thermonuclear reaction rates involved in standard models of Big-Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) has been investigated. We find that the non-extensive parameter $q$ may deviate by, at most, $|δq|$=6$\times$10$^{-4}$ from unity for BBN predictions to be consistent with observed primordial abundances; $q$=1 represents the classical Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics. This constraint arises primarily from the {\em super}sensitivity of endothermic rates on the value of $q$, which is found for the first time. As such, the implications of non-extensive statistics in other astrophysical environments should be explored. This may offer new insight into the nucleosynthesis of heavy elements.

# Big-Bang Nucleosynthesis verifies classical Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution

## Key claim

The classical Maxwell-Boltzmann (MB) velocity distribution for nuclei in the Big-Bang plasma is verified to within a deviation of at most |δq| = 6×10⁻⁴ from unity for the non-extensive Tsallis parameter q, where q=1 represents classical Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics. This constraint is derived from the supersensitivity of endothermic (reverse) reaction rates to q, a phenomenon reported for the first time, and is primarily driven by the deuterium abundance constraint rather than by lithium.

## Method

The authors numerically calculated forward and reverse thermonuclear reaction rates for 17 key BBN reactions using the non-extensive q-Gaussian velocity distribution (Tsallis statistics), with a coherent treatment where both forward and reverse rates were derived from the same q-distribution formalism (Equations 3 and 5). These rates were incorporated into a standard BBN reaction network of 34 reactions with up-to-date cosmological parameters (baryon-to-photon ratio from WMAP9, neutron lifetime 887.7 s, Nν=3), and the predicted primordial abundances of D, ³He, ⁴He, and ⁷Li were compared to observed abundances via χ² minimization over q values from 0.94 to 1.06.

## Key numbers

- The non-extensive parameter q may deviate from unity by at most |δq| = 6×10⁻⁴ for BBN predictions to be consistent with observed primordial abundances; this is the most stringent constraint to date, compared to previous estimates of (-0.34 to 0.34)% from Torres et al. and (-12 to 5)% from Bertulani et al. (2013).
- For the ²H(d,p)³H reaction at 0.2 GK, even a very small deviation q=0.999 yields a Tsallis reverse rate about 10¹⁰ times larger than the MB reverse rate.
- The predicted D/H abundance with MB distribution is 2.54×10⁻⁵, compared to the observed value of (2.53±0.04)×10⁻⁵ from Cooke et al. (2014).
- The predicted ⁷Li/H abundance with MB distribution is 5.34×10⁻¹⁰, compared to the Galactic halo star observation of (1.58±0.31)×10⁻¹⁰ from Li et al., and the SMC observation of (4.8±1.8)×10⁻¹⁰ from SMC.
- A deviation δq≈0.985% would reconcile BBN predictions with the ⁷Li abundance observed in Galactic halo stars, but this would make D, ³He, and ⁴He predictions deviate from observations by 43.5σ, 1.6σ, and 3.0σ, respectively.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of non-extensive statistics in astrophysical environments, including constraints from solar structure by Degl'Innocenti et al. (1998) who found δq between (-1.0 to 0.4)%, and earlier BBN work by Bertulani et al. (2013) which found δq between (-12 to 5)% but used an approximate treatment of reverse rates and restricted integration ranges. The present study provides a more rigorous treatment with coherent forward and reverse rates and unrestricted integrals, yielding a constraint two orders of magnitude tighter than Torres et al. The results rule out non-extensive statistics as a solution to the cosmological lithium problem, but the authors suggest that the supersensitivity of endothermic rates to q may have significant implications for nucleosynthesis in more extreme environments such as supernova explosions.
