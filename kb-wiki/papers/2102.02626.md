# Unperturbed inverse kinematics nucleon knockout measurements with a 48 GeV/c carbon beam

> From superconductors to atomic nuclei, strongly-interacting many-body systems are ubiquitous in nature. Measuring the microscopic structure of such systems is a formidable challenge, often met by particle knockout scattering experiments. While such measurements are fundamental for mapping the structure of atomic nuclei, their interpretation is often challenged by quantum mechanical initial- and final-state interactions (ISI/FSI) of the incoming and scattered particles. Here we overcome this fundamental limitation by measuring the quasi-free scattering of 48 GeV/c 12C ions from hydrogen. The distribution of single protons is studied by detecting two protons at large angles in coincidence with an intact 11B nucleus. The 11B detection is shown to select the transparent part of the reaction and exclude the otherwise large ISI/FSI that would break the 11B apart. By further detecting residual 10B and 10Be nuclei, we also identified short-range correlated (SRC) nucleon-nucleon pairs, and provide direct experimental evidence for the separation of the pair wave-function from that of the residual many-body nuclear system. All measured reactions are well described by theoretical calculations that do not contain ISI/FSI distortions. Our results thus showcase a new ability to study the short-distance structure of short-lived radioactive atomic nuclei at the forthcoming FAIR and FRIB facilities. These studies will be pivotal for developing a ground-breaking microscopic understanding of the structure and properties of nuclei far from stability and the formation of visible matter in the universe.

## Key claim
By detecting an intact residual nuclear fragment (e.g., 11B) in coincidence with knockout protons, the authors show that initial- and final-state interaction (ISI/FSI) distortions can be experimentally suppressed in high-energy inverse kinematics nucleon knockout measurements, provided the fragment remains bound. This post-selection method is demonstrated for both single-proton knockout and short-range correlated (SRC) pair breakup, with the caveat that the measured event rate is significantly reduced.

## Method
A 48 GeV/c 12C beam from the Nuclotron accelerator was directed at a stationary liquid-hydrogen target. The two-arm spectrometer detected two protons from the (p,2p) reaction at large angles (24°–37°), while downstream silicon planes, multi-wire proportional chambers, and a dipole magnet with drift chambers identified and measured the momenta of residual nuclear fragments (11B, 10B, 10Be). Exclusive events were selected by requiring coincidence between the two protons and a specific fragment, and theoretical calculations without ISI/FSI distortions were compared to the measured distributions.

## Key numbers
- The 12C(p,2p)11B quasielastic event yield accounts for (43.7 ± 2.4 (stat) +4.9 −5.8 (sys))% of the measured inclusive 12C(p,2p) quasielastic yield.
- 12C(p,2p)10B and 12C(p,2p)10Be quasielastic events account for an additional (7.8 ± 1.0 (stat) +1.3 −1.4 (sys))% and ≤ 2%, respectively.
- For SRC pair selection, 23 12C(p,2p)10B and 2 12C(p,2p)10Be events were measured, with a GCF-calculated 10B/10Be yield ratio of 12.1.
- The extracted SRC pair center-of-mass momentum distribution has a Gaussian width of σ_c.m. = (156 ± 27) MeV/c.
- SRC events were selected by requiring |p_miss| > 350 MeV/c, in-plane opening angle > ~63°, and missing energy between −110 and 240 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the method of quasi-free proton-induced nucleon knockout in inverse kinematics, previously applied to study single-particle structure of exotic nuclei by Kobayashi (2008) and Atar (2018), by adding direct detection of the residual fragment to suppress ISI/FSI distortions. It also provides the first experimental evidence for the factorization of SRC pair wave-functions from the residual nuclear system, a key prediction of theoretical SRC models by Ciofi degli Atti (2015) and Cruz-Torres (2019). The results enable future studies of short-distance nuclear structure in short-lived radioactive nuclei at FAIR and FRIB facilities.
