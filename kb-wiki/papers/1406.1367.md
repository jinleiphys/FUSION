# Inhomogeneous chiral condensates

> The chiral condensate, which is constant in vacuum, may become spatially modulated at moderately high densities where in the traditional picture of the QCD phase diagram a first-order chiral phase transition occurs. We review the current status of this idea, which originally dates back to Migdal's pion condensation, but recently received new momentum through studies on the nature of the chiral critical point and by the conjecture of a quarkyonic-matter phase. We discuss how these nonuniform phases emerge in generalized Ginzburg-Landau analyses as well as in specific calculations, both within effective models and in Dyson-Schwinger or large-$N_c$ approaches to QCD. Questions about the most favored shape of the modulations and its dimension, and about the effects of nonzero isospin chemical potential, strange quarks, color superconductivity, and external magnetic fields on these inhomogeneous phases will be addressed as well.

## Key claim

The chiral condensate, which is constant in vacuum, may become spatially modulated at moderately high densities where a first-order chiral phase transition was traditionally expected, but the exact properties of these inhomogeneous phases depend on the specific model and dimensionality, with the simple exact mapping found in 1+1 dimensions being lost in 3+1 dimensions due to transverse momenta acting as a gap that weakens the influence of the wave vector on the spectrum.

## Method

The authors review the formation of inhomogeneous chiral condensates using the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model and the Quark-Meson (QM) model as primary frameworks, employing mean-field approximations where the quark fields are linearized around scalar and pseudoscalar condensates that are allowed to vary spatially. They analyze the Ginzburg-Landau approach near second-order phase transitions, study specific one-dimensional modulations like the chiral density wave ansatz, and discuss higher-dimensional modulations as well as extensions including nonzero isospin chemical potential, strange quarks, color superconductivity, and external magnetic fields. The review also covers large-Nc approaches to QCD and Dyson-Schwinger equation methods for inhomogeneous phases.

## Key numbers

- In the 1+1 dimensional NJL model (NJL2), the chiral spiral solution has wave number |q| = 2μ, and the anomalous term in the thermodynamic potential is −μ²/(2π) per flavor
- In the 1+1 dimensional NJL2 model, the baryon density at μ > 0 is n = μ/π per flavor
- For the Chandrasekhar-Clogston limit in weak-coupling superconductors, BCS pairing is possible when the Fermi momentum difference δkF is less than ΔBCS/√2
- In the chiral density wave ansatz in 3+1 dimensions, the quark dispersion relation splits into two branches: E±(p) = √(p² + Δ² + q²/4 ± √(Δ²q² + (q·p)²))

## Context

This review extends the original idea of inhomogeneous pion condensation dating back to Migdal (1971, 1973) and the pioneering large-Nc study by Deryagin, Grigoriev and Rubakov (1992), as well as subsequent refinements by Shuster and Son, Park et al. (1999), and Rapp, Shuryak and Zahed (2000). It builds upon systematic model analyses by Kutschera et al. (1989), Nakano and Tatsumi (2004), and Nickel (2009), and connects to the conjecture of a quarkyonic-matter phase. The review enables future investigations into the most favored shape and dimension of modulations, and the effects of isospin chemical potential, strange quarks, color superconductivity, and external magnetic fields on these inhomogeneous phases.
