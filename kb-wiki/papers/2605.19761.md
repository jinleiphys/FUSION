# Magnetized neutron stars: perturbative versus fully-numerical approaches

> (1) Background: for the study of highly magnetized neutron stars observed as magnetars, and to quantify the effect of this intense magnetic field onto the star's structure and shape which can be particularly relevant for the study of emission of continuous gravitational waves, both numerical and perturbative approaches have been developed. (2) Methods: we compare these two approaches in General Relativity with the limitation to the case where the magnetic field has a purely poloidal structure. The perturbative one (Konno-99) assumes that the deformation induced by the magnetic field is small and that this field arises only from dipole currents. The full numerical one is based on the library LORENE. (3) Results: we have used both approaches to compute the magnetic field distribution and the deformation of the star, varying the value of the magnetic field at the pole, the compactness of the star and its equation of state. (4) Conclusions: whereas the perturbative approach breaks down for very high polar magnetic field values (typically above a few times $10^{16}$ G), it gives very good results for observed values, even in magnetars. On the contrary, the numerical code exhibits resolution problems for relatively low magnetic field values (typically $10^{10}$ G), which translates into imprecise computation of the star's deformation and mass quadrupole moment.

## Key claim

The perturbative approach (Konno-99) accurately models magnetized neutron stars for observed magnetar field strengths (up to a few times 10^16 G), but breaks down at higher fields; conversely, the fully numerical code (LORENE-based magstar) has resolution problems at relatively low fields (around 10^10 G), making it imprecise for computing deformation and mass quadrupole moment at those values.

## Method

The authors compared two general-relativistic approaches for non-rotating neutron stars with a purely poloidal magnetic field: a perturbative method (Konno-99) that assumes small deformations and dipole-only currents, and a fully numerical code (magstar, part of the LORENE library) that solves the Einstein-Maxwell equations using spectral methods with a fixed-point iteration. Both approaches computed the magnetic field distribution and stellar deformation for varying polar magnetic field strengths, stellar compactness, and equations of state.

## Key numbers

- Perturbative approach breaks down for polar magnetic field values above a few times 10^16 G.
- Numerical code exhibits resolution problems for polar magnetic field values around 10^10 G.
- For relatively low magnetic fields (B_pole less than about 10^16 G), the difference between perturbative and numerical results is very small; at the highest field values the star can support, the difference reaches about 100%.
- Virial test error indicators (GRV2 and GRV3) for numerical solutions with microphysical EoSs remained below 10^-4.

## Context

This work extends the perturbative formalism of Konno (1999) and the numerical approach of Bocquet et al. (1995) and Novak (2003) by directly comparing their accuracy for magnetized neutron stars. The comparison is motivated by the need for reliable models of magnetically deformed neutron stars as potential sources of continuous gravitational waves, relevant for LIGO-Virgo-Kagra searches and future detectors like DECIGO, Cosmic Explorer, and Einstein Telescope. The results enable researchers to choose the appropriate method based on the magnetic field strength of interest, with the perturbative approach being sufficient for observed magnetar fields and the numerical approach necessary for very high fields.
