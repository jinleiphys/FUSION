# Observational discrimination of Eddington-inspired Born-Infeld gravity from general relativity

> Direct observations of neutron stars could tell us an imprint of modified gravity. However, it is generally difficult to resolve the degeneracy due to the uncertainties in equation of state (EOS) of neutron star matter and in gravitational theories. In this paper, we are successful to find the observational possibility to distinguish Eddington-inspired Born-Infeld gravity (EiBI) from general relativity. We show that the radii of neutron stars with $0.5M_{sun}$ are strongly correlated with the neutron skin thickness of ${}^{208}$Pb independently of EOS, while this correlation depends on the coupling constant in EiBI. As a result, via the direct observations of radius of neutron star with $0.5M_{sun}$ and the measurements of neutron skin thickness of ${}^{208}$Pb by the terrestrial experiments, one could not only discriminate EiBI from general relativity but also estimate the coupling constant in EiBI.

## Key claim

The paper claims that one can observationally distinguish Eddington-inspired Born-Infeld gravity (EiBI) from general relativity by combining measurements of the radius of a neutron star with mass 0.5 solar masses and the neutron skin thickness of lead-208, provided both quantities can be measured with sufficient precision; the degeneracy between equation-of-state uncertainties and gravitational theory can be broken because the correlation between these two observables depends on the EiBI coupling constant while being nearly independent of the nuclear equation of state.

## Method

The author constructs neutron star models in EiBI gravity by solving the modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations derived from the EiBI action, using eleven realistic nuclear equations of state (phenomenological models, relativistic mean field models, and Skyrme-type effective interactions) that are all consistent with terrestrial experimental data for stable nuclei. For each equation of state and each value of the normalized EiBI coupling constant (8πϵ₀κ) in the range -0.02 to 0.04, the radius of a 0.5 solar mass neutron star is computed and plotted against the neutron skin thickness of lead-208, which is estimated from the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy L using the formula of Oyamatsu and Iida (2003). Linear fits to these correlations are obtained, and the dependence of the fit coefficients on the coupling constant is extracted.

## Key numbers

* The nuclear saturation density ϵ₀ is 2.68 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³ (or 1.99 × 10⁻⁴ km⁻² in geometric units).
* The linear fit R₀₅ = c₀ + c₁ ΔR (where R₀₅ is in km and ΔR in fm) has root mean fractional variation (RMFV) of 1.44% for 8πϵ₀κ = -0.02, 1.09% for 8πϵ₀κ = 0 (general relativity), and 0.90% for 8πϵ₀κ = 0.02.
* The coefficients in the linear fit depend on the coupling constant as: c₀/km = 8.21 + 60.3 × (8πϵ₀κ) and c₁/(km/fm) = 31.0 - 125.8 × (8πϵ₀κ).
* The empirical formula for the coupling constant is: 8πϵ₀κ = [(R₀₅/km) - 8.21 - 31.0(ΔR/fm)] / [60.3 - 125.8(ΔR/fm)].
* The current best measurement of the neutron skin thickness of lead-208 from the PREX experiment is ΔR = 0.33⁺⁰·¹⁶₋₀·₁₈ fm.
* The lowest observed neutron star mass is 0.87 ± 0.07 M⊙ (eccentric orbit) or 1.00 ± 0.10 M⊙ (circular orbit) from the binary system 4U1538-52.
* For a 0.7 M⊙ neutron star, the RMFV values are 2.00%, 1.44%, and 1.08% for 8πϵ₀κ = -0.02, 0, and 0.02 respectively; the relative deviation between R₀₅ and R₀₇ for ΔR = 0.1 fm ranges from 1.16% to 3.32% depending on κ.
* If R₀₅ and ΔR have ±10% observational uncertainties, the inferred 8πϵ₀κ varies by up to ±0.04 for R₀₅ = 12 km and ±0.06 for R₀₅ = 14 km.

## Context

This work extends the suggestion by Horowitz and Piekarewicz (2003) that neutron star radii correlate with neutron skin thickness in general relativity, now applied to the modified gravity theory EiBI proposed by Banados and Ferreira. It addresses the degeneracy problem noted in earlier studies of compact objects in EiBI by Pani et al. (2011), Pani et al. (2012), and others, where stellar structure depends on both the unknown equation of state and the gravitational coupling constant. The result enables a combined program of terrestrial nuclear experiments (such as PREX) and astronomical observations of low-mass neutron stars to test general relativity in the strong-field regime, and provides an empirical formula to estimate the EiBI coupling constant from these measurements.
