# Energy loss, hadronization and hadronic interactions of heavy flavors in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We construct a theoretical framework to describe the evolution of heavy flavors produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. The in-medium energy loss of heavy quarks is described using our modified Langevin equation that incorporates both quasi-elastic scatterings and the medium-induced gluon radiation. The space-time profiles of the fireball is described by a (2+1)-dimensional hydrodynamics simulation. A hybrid model of fragmentation and coalescence is utilized for heavy quark hadronization, after which the produced heavy mesons together with the soft hadrons produced from the bulk QGP are fed into the hadron cascade UrQMD model to simulate the subsequent hadronic interactions. We find that the medium-induced gluon radiation contributes significantly to heavy quark energy loss at high $p_\mathrm{T}$; heavy-light quark coalescence enhances heavy meson production at intermediate $p_\mathrm{T}$; and scatterings inside the hadron gas further suppress the $D$ meson $R_\mathrm{AA}$ at large $p_\mathrm{T}$ and enhance its $v_2$. Our calculations provide good descriptions of heavy meson suppression and elliptic flow observed at both the LHC and RHIC.

## Key claim

The paper constructs a comprehensive theoretical framework for heavy flavor evolution in heavy-ion collisions, claiming that medium-induced gluon radiation dominates heavy quark energy loss at high transverse momentum (above ~7 GeV for charm and ~18 GeV for bottom), while heavy-light quark coalescence enhances heavy meson production at intermediate $p_\mathrm{T}$ and hadronic rescattering further suppresses $D$ meson $R_\mathrm{AA}$ at large $p_\mathrm{T}$ and enhances $v_2$. The hedge is that the spatial diffusion coefficient $D(2\pi T)$ is chosen around 5–6 to best describe experimental data, making the framework tuned rather than predictive.

## Method

The authors couple a modified Langevin equation incorporating both quasi-elastic scatterings and medium-induced gluon radiation (via higher-twist formalism with a dead-cone factor) to a (2+1)-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic simulation of the QGP fireball. Heavy quark hadronization is treated with a hybrid model of fragmentation (via Pythia) and instantaneous coalescence (using Wigner functions for mesons and baryons), with the coalescence probability normalized to unity for zero-momentum heavy quarks in a static medium. After hadronization, heavy mesons and soft hadrons from the bulk are fed into the UrQMD hadron cascade to simulate hadronic rescattering.

## Key numbers

- Spatial diffusion coefficient of heavy quarks: $D(2\pi T) \approx 5\text{–}6$, equivalent to $\hat{q}/T^3 \approx 9.4\text{–}11.3$ for gluons (or $4.2\text{–}5.0$ for quarks)
- Crossing point where radiative energy loss exceeds collisional: ~7 GeV for charm quarks, ~18 GeV for bottom quarks
- Lower cut-off for radiated gluon energy: $\omega_0 = \pi T$
- Starting time of QGP evolution: $\tau_0 = 0.6$ fm/$c$
- Critical temperature for hadronization: $T_\mathrm{c} = 165$ MeV
- Oscillator frequency for Wigner functions: 0.215 GeV for all $c$-hadrons, 0.102 GeV for all $b$-hadrons
- Thermal masses: 300 MeV for $u$ and $d$ quarks, 475 MeV for $s$ quarks; heavy quark masses: 1.27 GeV for $c$, 4.19 GeV for $b$
- Shear-viscosity-to-entropy-density ratio: 0.08 for Glauber initial condition, 0.20 for KLN initial condition

## Context

This work extends earlier Langevin-based transport models (Svetitsky 1987, Moore 2004, He 2011) by incorporating radiative energy loss alongside collisional loss, addressing the "heavy flavor puzzle" where heavy meson $R_\mathrm{AA}$ and $v_2$ at LHC and RHIC are comparable to light hadrons. It builds on the authors' previous modified Langevin approach (Cao 2012, 2013) and the instantaneous coalescence model for light hadrons (Fries 2003, Greco 2003, Oh 2009). The framework enables systematic comparison of heavy meson suppression and elliptic flow data from ALICE and STAR, and can be extended to study initial-state fluctuations and pre-equilibrium effects.
