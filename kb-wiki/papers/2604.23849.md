# Weyl anomaly induced transport in hydrodynamics

> We show that the Weyl (trace) anomaly gives rise to a new non-dissipative vector current in accelerated relativistic fluids. The anomaly uniquely fixes the second-order transport coefficient governing the coupling between the electromagnetic field and the fluid acceleration. We derive this result by extending hydrodynamic anomaly matching to include the trace anomaly, and independently reproduce it in boundary quantum field theory by treating the Rindler horizon of an accelerated observer as an effective boundary. From the boundary perspective, the electric- and magnetic-field sectors correspond to screening and vacuum magnetization effects near the boundary. In the local rest frame, the electric-field contribution induces an additional charge density, while the magnetic-field contribution generates a transverse current with a Nernst-like, more generally thermomagnetic Hall-like, tensor structure. Our results reveal a new class of anomaly-induced transport governed by the trace anomaly.

## Key claim

The Weyl (trace) anomaly induces a new non-dissipative vector current in accelerated relativistic fluids, with the transport coefficient uniquely fixed by the anomaly coefficient \(C\), but this result is derived under the condition of global equilibrium where the system satisfies Killing conditions and the magnetic component of thermal vorticity is set to zero.

## Method

The authors extend hydrodynamic anomaly matching, originally developed for chiral anomalies by Son and Surowka (2009), to include the trace anomaly by imposing global equilibrium Killing conditions and the anomalous trace relation \(T^\mu_\mu = C F^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu}\) order by order in a gradient expansion of the current and energy-momentum tensor. They independently reproduce the same current in boundary quantum field theory by treating the Rindler horizon of an accelerated observer as an effective boundary, using the stretched horizon formalism to map the acceleration vector to the boundary normal.

## Key numbers

* The Weyl anomaly coefficient \(C\) appears in the trace relation \(T^\mu_\mu = C F^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu}\) (Eq. 1), with \(C\) fixed by the field content of the microscopic theory.
* The induced current in covariant form is \(j^\mu = -4C F^{\mu\nu} a_\nu\) (Eq. 8), where \(a_\nu\) is the fluid acceleration.
* In the local rest frame, the charge density is \(j^0 = -4C (\mathbf{E}\cdot\mathbf{a})\) and the spatial current is \(\mathbf{j} = 4C \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{B}\) (Eq. 11).
* At global equilibrium with \(\mathbf{a} = T^{-1}\boldsymbol{\nabla}T\) and \(\mathbf{E} = T\boldsymbol{\nabla}\overline{\mu}\), the charge density becomes \(j^0 = -4C \boldsymbol{\nabla}\overline{\mu}\cdot\boldsymbol{\nabla}T\) and the current becomes \(\mathbf{j} = 4C T^{-1} \boldsymbol{\nabla}T\times\mathbf{B}\) (Eq. 12).

## Context

This work extends the anomaly-matching logic of Son and Surowka (2009) and the equilibrium formulation of Yang (2022) from chiral anomalies to the Weyl (trace) anomaly, revealing a new class of anomaly-induced transport distinct from the chiral magnetic effect (CME) and chiral vortical effect (CVE) discovered by Vilenkin (1980), Fukushima (2008), and Kharzeev (2009). The result connects to boundary quantum field theory work by Chu (2018, 2019) on vacuum magnetization currents near boundaries, and establishes a bulk-boundary correspondence for Weyl-anomaly-driven transport. This enables new investigations into charge separation in heavy-ion collisions, transport in Weyl semimetals where Chernodub (2021) has discussed conformal-anomaly effects, and trace-anomaly-induced dynamics in early universe cosmology.
