# Identifying resonances with wave-packet dynamics

> A new method for the study of resonant behavior - using wave-packet dynamics - is presented, based on the powerful window operator technique. The method is illustrated and quantified by application to the astrophysically-important example of low-energy $^{12}$C + $^{12}$C collisions. For this selected, potential model test case, the technique is shown to provide both resonance energies and widths in agreement with alternative methods, such as complex-energy scattering-matrix pole searches and scattering phase-shift analyses. The method has a more general capability to study resonance phenomena across disciplines, that involve particles temporarily trapped by potential pockets.

# Identifying resonances with wave-packet dynamics

## Key claim

The paper presents a new method for determining potential resonance energies and widths using time-dependent wave-packet dynamics combined with the window operator technique, but the authors explicitly note that many alternative methods are actually more efficient for the one-dimensional test case studied, and the method's primary advantage lies in its applicability to multi-dimensional problems where stationary-state methods face unclear boundary conditions.

## Method

The method involves three steps: (i) defining an initial Gaussian wave-packet centered at a large internuclear distance and boosted toward the potential barriers, (ii) propagating the wave-packet in time using the Chebyshev propagator on a Fourier radial grid, and (iii) computing the effective energy spectrum of the part of the wave-packet inside the Coulomb barrier radius using the window operator method. The energy spectrum is normalized by the initial wave-packet spectrum to remove dependence on incident energy weights, and resonance energies and widths are extracted by fitting Lorentzian functions to the resulting probability density peaks.

## Key numbers

- Coulomb barrier heights for the 12C+12C system: 6.5 MeV (J=0), 6.8 MeV (J=2), and 7.6 MeV (J=4); barrier radii are 8 fm, 7.9 fm, and 7.8 fm respectively.
- For J=0 resonance: energy 4.431 MeV and width 1.10 +/- 0.07 keV (with Rmax=7000 fm grid), compared to S-matrix pole values of 4.437 MeV and 1.55 keV.
- For J=2 resonance: energy 5.082 MeV and width 5.91 +/- 0.29 keV (with Rmax=7000 fm grid), compared to S-matrix pole values of 5.088 MeV and 5.70 keV.
- For J=4 resonance: energy 6.535 MeV and width 49.72 +/- 0.30 keV (with Rmax=7000 fm grid), compared to S-matrix pole values of 6.538 MeV and 49.50 keV.
- The time-step used for propagation was 10^-22 s, preserving wave function normalization with accuracy of approximately 10^-14.
- The initial wave-packet parameters: R0=400 fm, sigma=10 fm, mean incident energy E0=6 MeV, on a radial grid from 0-1000 fm with 2048 evenly spaced points.

## Context

This work extends the wave-packet dynamics approach previously applied to nuclear collisions (Diaz-Torres and Wiescher, prior work) by incorporating the window operator method of Schafer for energy projection. The method is tested on a simplified one-dimensional potential model of 12C+12C collisions, allowing direct comparison with established techniques such as complex-energy S-matrix pole searches and scattering phase-shift analyses (Merzbacher). The calculated resonances account for some resonant structures observed in the astrophysical S-factor for stellar carbon burning, and the method is intended for future application to multi-dimensional problems like heavy-ion fission and fusion where intermediate nuclear molecular structures are formed.
