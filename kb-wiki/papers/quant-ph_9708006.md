# Two-mode squeezed states in high energy physics

> We discuss the possibility to observe hadron modification in hot and dense matter via the correlation of identical particles. We find that a modification of hadronic masses in medium leads to two-mode squeezing which signals itself in a back-to-back correlations of hadrons. This effect leads to a signal of a shift of $ϕ$-meson mass.

# Two-mode squeezed states in high energy physics

## Key claim
A modification of hadronic masses in hot and dense matter leads to two-mode squeezing that produces back-to-back correlations of identical particles, providing a signal of hadron mass modification. Although this effect can signal a shift in the φ-meson mass at RHIC, the authors note that larger signals are expected for the mass shift of lighter particles such as kaons or pions.

## Method
The authors model freeze-out as a sudden transition from a thermalized system of dressed quasi-particles (with shifted masses) to free particles in vacuum. Using a scalar field theory in the mean-field approximation, they diagonalize the in-medium Hamiltonian via a Bogoliubov transformation that mixes modes k and -k, then compute one- and two-particle distribution functions using the Glauber-Sudarshan representation of the thermal density matrix in the b-basis (in-medium quanta).

## Key numbers
- φ-meson vacuum mass: m_φ = 1020 MeV
- Kaon vacuum mass: m_K = 494 MeV
- Freeze-out temperature assumed: T = 140 MeV
- RHIC collision energy: √s = 40 TeV for Au + Au collisions
- A 10% decrease in m_φ produces a small back-to-back correlation signal; a 20% decrease produces a slightly larger but still small signal
- The intercept of the same-mode correlation function C₂(k, k) remains at the canonical value of 2

## Context
This work extends the authors' earlier studies (Asakawa and Csörgő, 1996) of hadron modification via one-mode squeezed states, correcting the earlier incorrect expression for the back-to-back correlation function. The paper contrasts with previous expectations (Asakawa and Csörgő, 1996; Asakawa, 1996) that the effect would be largest for heavier particles, finding instead that the back-to-back correlation signal is largest for the lightest particles (photons, pions, or kaons). The results enable experimental searches for in-medium hadron mass shifts using back-to-back correlations at large relative momenta, which are not contaminated by resonance decays unlike standard Bose-Einstein correlations at small relative momenta.
