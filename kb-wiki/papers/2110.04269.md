# Fast emulation of quantum three-body scattering

> We develop a class of emulators for solving quantum three-body scattering problems. They are based on combining the variational method for scattering observables and the recently proposed eigenvector continuation concept. The emulators are first trained by the exact scattering solutions of the governing Hamiltonian at a small number of points in its parameter space, and then employed to make interpolations and extrapolations in that space. Through a schematic nuclear-physics model with finite-range two and three-body interactions, we demonstrate the emulators to be extremely accurate and efficient. The computing time for emulation is on the scale of milliseconds (on a laptop), with relative errors ranging from $10^{-13}$ to $10^{-4}$ depending on the case. The emulators also require little memory. We argue that these emulators can be generalized to even more challenging scattering problems. Furthermore, this general strategy may be applicable for building the same type of emulators in other fields, wherever variational methods can be developed for evaluating physical models.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that eigenvector continuation (EC) combined with the Kohn variational principle (KVP) can produce extremely accurate and fast emulators for quantum three-body scattering, with relative errors ranging from $10^{-13}$ to $10^{-4}$ depending on the type of parameter variation, but the accuracy degrades when varying interaction operators (nonlinear cases) compared to varying linear coupling strengths.

## Method

The authors combine the Kohn variational principle for scattering observables with eigenvector continuation, constructing trial wave functions as linear combinations of exact scattering solutions computed at a small number of training points in the Hamiltonian parameter space. The emulators are tested on a schematic nuclear-physics model with finite-range two- and three-body interactions for spin-zero bosons with separable potentials, where the stationary functional is solved as a small generalized eigenvalue problem.

## Key numbers

- Computing time for emulation is on the scale of milliseconds on a laptop
- Relative errors for the "linear" case (varying coupling strengths) range from $10^{-14}$ to $10^{-13}$
- Relative errors for the "nonlinear-1" case (varying interaction ranges) range from $10^{-6}$ to $10^{-5}$
- Relative errors for the "nonlinear-2" case (varying subcluster structure) are $10^{-4}$
- Memory requirements are less than 1 MB for the linear case and tens of MB for nonlinear-2 case

## Context

This work extends the eigenvector continuation method, previously applied to bound-state calculations (Frame 2019) and two-body scattering (Furnstahl 2020, Melendez 2021, Drischler 2021), to the more challenging domain of three-body scattering. The emulators address a severe bottleneck in applications requiring many evaluations of three-body scattering in parameter space, such as Bayesian uncertainty quantification for chiral effective field theory (Epelbaum 2008, Hebeler 2020) and for phenomenological reaction models needed at rare isotope facilities (King 2019).
