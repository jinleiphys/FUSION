# Production of dark-matter bound states in the early universe by three-body recombination

> The small-scale structure problems of the universe can be solved by self-interacting dark matter that becomes strongly interacting at low energy. A particularly predictive model for the self-interactions is resonant short-range interactions with an S-wave scattering length that is much larger than the range. The velocity dependence of the cross section in such a model provides an excellent fit to self-interaction cross sections inferred from dark-matter halos of galaxies and clusters of galaxies if the dark-matter mass is about 19 GeV and the scattering length is about 17 fm. Such a model makes definite predictions for the few-body physics of weakly bound clusters of the dark-matter particles. The formation of the two-body bound cluster is a bottleneck for the formation of larger bound clusters. We calculate the production of two-body bound clusters by three-body recombination in the early universe under the assumption that the dark matter particles are identical bosons, which is the most favorable case. If the dark-matter mass is 19 GeV and the scattering length is 17 fm, the fraction of dark matter in the form of two-body bound clusters can increase by as much as 4 orders of magnitude when the dark-matter temperature falls below the binding energy, but its present value remains less than 10^(-6). The present fraction can be increased to as large as 10^(-3) by relaxing the constraints from small-scale structure and decreasing the mass of the dark matter particle.

## Key claim

The paper calculates the production of two-body bound clusters (dark deuterons) by three-body recombination in the early universe, finding that for the benchmark dark matter mass of 19 GeV and scattering length of 17 fm that solve small-scale structure problems, the present-day fraction of dark matter in dark deuterons remains less than 10^(-6), though this fraction can be increased to as large as 10^(-3) by relaxing the small-scale structure constraints and decreasing the dark matter particle mass.

## Method

The authors model dark matter as identical spin-0 bosons with short-range interactions and a large positive S-wave scattering length, using universal few-body physics to describe the system. They determine the mass and scattering length parameters by fitting the velocity-dependent elastic cross section to astrophysical data on self-interaction rates from dwarf galaxies, low-surface-brightness galaxies, and galaxy clusters compiled by Kaplinghat, Tulin, and Yu (2015). They then solve the Boltzmann equation for the dark deuteron abundance during Hubble expansion in the early universe, incorporating the three-body recombination rate and the competing dimer-breakup rate calculated from universal three-body physics.

## Key numbers

* Best-fit dark matter mass: m_chi = 19^(+3)_(-2) GeV, from fitting the elastic cross section to astrophysical data on self-interaction rates
* Best-fit scattering length: a = (17 +/- 3) fm, from the same fit
* Binding energy of the dark deuteron: E_2 = 7.1 keV, derived from E_2 = hbar^2/(m_chi * a^2)
* For the benchmark parameters (19 GeV, 17 fm), the present-day fraction of dark matter in dark deuterons is less than 10^(-6)
* By relaxing small-scale structure constraints and decreasing the dark matter mass, the present fraction can be increased to as large as 10^(-3)
* The fraction of dark matter in dark deuterons can increase by as much as 4 orders of magnitude when the dark-matter temperature falls below the binding energy

## Context

This work extends the universal few-body physics framework previously applied to atomic systems (such as He-4 atoms with large scattering lengths) to the dark matter sector, building on earlier studies of dark matter bound states by Braaten, Laha, and collaborators (2013, 2015) and by Kaplinghat, Tulin, and Yu (2015) who determined self-interaction cross sections from astrophysical data. The paper enables quantitative predictions for dark nucleosynthesis in the early universe, showing that three-body recombination alone cannot produce a significant population of dark deuterons for parameters that solve small-scale structure problems, which implies that larger dark nuclei will also not be formed through this channel.
