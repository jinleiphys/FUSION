# Existence of inelastic supernumerary nuclear rainbow in $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C scattering

> The existence of a supernumerary nuclear rainbow in inelastic scattering is reported. This is done by studying inelastic $^{16}$O scattering from $^{12}$C, exciting the $2^+$ (4.44 MeV) state of $^{12}$C and elastic scattering at the incident energies in the range 124 to 200 MeV, using the coupled channels method. An extended double folding potential is used. This is derived from realistic wave functions for $^{12}$C and $^{16}$O calculated with a microscopic $Î±$ cluster model and a finite-range density-dependent nucleon-nucleon force. Excitations to the $2^+$ (4.44 MeV), 3$^-$ (9.64 MeV) and $4^+$ (14.08 MeV) states of $^{12}$C, and the $3^-$ (6.13 MeV) and $2^+$ (6.92 MeV) states of $^{16}$O are included in the coupled channels calculations. The emergence of the supernumerary bow is understood by the properties of both the Luneburg-lens-like potential in the internal region and diffuse attraction in the outer region. The existence of a supernumerary rainbow for inelastic scattering in addition to the existence of a dynamically created secondary rainbow and a dynamically refracted primary rainbow for elastic scattering, which are not observed in meteorological rainbows, further deepens the understanding of nuclear rainbows.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first observation of a supernumerary nuclear rainbow (higher-order Airy minima beyond A1) in inelastic scattering, specifically for the $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C system exciting the $2^+$ (4.44 MeV) state of $^{12}$C, based on coupled channels calculations that reproduce experimental angular distributions at incident energies from 124 to 200 MeV.

## Method

The authors performed coupled channels calculations for $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C elastic and inelastic scattering using an extended double folding (EDF) potential derived from microscopic $\alpha$ cluster model wave functions for both nuclei and a finite-range density-dependent nucleon-nucleon force (DDM3Y-FR). The calculations included couplings to the $0^+_1$ (0.0 MeV), $2^+$ (4.44 MeV), $3^-$ (9.64 MeV), and $4^+$ (14.08 MeV) states of $^{12}$C and the $0_1^+$ (0.0 MeV), $3^-$ (6.13 MeV), and $2^+$ (6.92 MeV) states of $^{16}$O, with a phenomenological imaginary potential applied to all diagonal channels and Coulomb excitation included.

## Key numbers

- Incident laboratory energies studied: 124, 170, 181, and 200 MeV.
- Normalization factor for the real double folding potential: $N_R = 0.97$.
- Volume integral per nucleon pair for the elastic channel: $J_V = 306.1$ MeV fm$^3$ at 124 MeV, 296.9 MeV fm$^3$ at 170 MeV, 294.9 MeV fm$^3$ at 181 MeV, and 291.6 MeV fm$^3$ at 200 MeV.
- For inelastic scattering at 124 MeV with absorption switched off in the $2^+$ channel, the Airy minima $A2$, $A3$, and $A4$ appear at approximately 97.5$^\circ$, 67.5$^\circ$, and 46$^\circ$ in the center-of-mass frame, respectively.
- For elastic scattering at 124 MeV with $W=0$, the $A2$, $A3$, and $A4$ minima appear at 104$^\circ$, 62$^\circ$, and 42.5$^\circ$, respectively.
- The volume integral for the inelastic $^{16}$O(g.s.)-$^{12}$C($2^+$) channel at 124 MeV is $J_V = 303$ MeV fm$^3$, nearly indistinguishable from the elastic channel value of 306 MeV fm$^3$.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of nuclear rainbows in $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C scattering by Szilner et al. (2001) and Ogloblin et al. (2000), who observed supernumerary bows up to $A5$ in elastic scattering at lower energies (85-132 MeV) and $A2$/$A3$ at higher energies, but no such structure had been reported for inelastic scattering. The authors had previously identified the $A1$ Airy minimum for inelastic scattering to the $2^+$ state of $^{12}$C (Ohkubo et al., 2014C) and studied dynamically created secondary bows (Ohkubo et al., 2014) and dynamically refracted primary bows (Ohkubo et al., 2016) in elastic scattering. The existence of inelastic supernumerary rainbows enables further study of cluster structure with the $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C($2^+$) configuration at lower energies and deepens understanding of nuclear rainbows under strong interactions.
