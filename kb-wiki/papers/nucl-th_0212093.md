# Exotic nuclear phases in the inner crust of neutron stars in the light of Skyrme-Hartree-Fock theory

> The bottom part of the neutron star crust is investigated using the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock approach with the Coulomb interaction treated beyond the Wigner-Seitz approximation. A variety of nuclear phases is found to coexist in this region. Their stability and relative energies are governed by the Coulomb, surface and shell energies. We have also found that a substantial contribution is coming from the spin-orbit interaction.

## Key claim

The bottom of the neutron star crust contains a wider variety of coexisting nuclear phases than previously predicted, with their relative stability governed by Coulomb, surface, and shell energies, and with a substantial contribution from the spin-orbit interaction that the abstract does not quantify as being of the same order as the surface energy term.

## Method

The authors performed Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations for neutron-proton-electron matter using the SLy4 force, discretizing the Hartree-Fock equations in coordinate space within a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions. The Coulomb interaction was treated beyond the Wigner-Seitz approximation by solving the Poisson equation with periodic boundary conditions, and the Coulomb exchange term for both protons and electrons was handled in the Slater approximation. The total energy density was computed as a function of the axial proton quadrupole moment for two average total densities and proton-to-nucleon ratios satisfying beta stability.

## Key numbers

- Calculations were performed in cubic boxes with side lengths of 26 fm (left panel) and 20.8 fm (right panel).
- The nucleon density region studied ranges from 0.03 to 0.1 fm\(^{-3}\).
- The neutron star considered has a typical mass of 1.4 M\(_\odot\), with the crust containing only 1.2% of the total stellar mass.

## Context

This work extends earlier liquid-drop and Thomas-Fermi models (e.g., Pethick and Ravenhall, and references therein) that predicted only five phases (spherical nuclei, rods or "spaghetti," slabs or "lasagna," tubes, and bubbles) under restrictive symmetry conditions and the Wigner-Seitz approximation. The present approach removes those limitations and reveals additional nuclear shapes and lattice geometries, showing that shell effects from unbound neutrons cause different phases to coexist in the same density range. The authors note that pairing correlations were not included and require further examination for the inner edge of the crust.
