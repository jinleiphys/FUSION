# First Principle Predictions for Cold Fermionic Gases Near Criticality via Critical Boson Dominance and Anomaly Matching

> Recently the authors have developed an effective field theory formalism to systematically describe cold fermionic gases near the unitary limit. The theory has enhanced predictive power due to the fact that interactions are dominated by the exchange of a gapped critical boson whose couplings and mass are fixed by matching the dilatation anomaly between the UV and IR theories. We utilize this theory to give analytic predictions for the compressibility and magnetic susceptibility for fermions near unitarity with attractive interactions above the critical temperature $T_c$, with a well defined theoretical error. The inputs to the predictions are: the scattering length $a$, the effective mass $m^\star$ and contact parameter $\tilde C(a)$. We then compare our predictions to numerical simulations and find excellent agreement within the window of scattering lengths where the EFT is valid ($10\geq \mid \! k_F a \! \mid\geq 1$). Experimental corroboration of this theory supports critical point that can be describe by the inclusion of a scalar dilaton mode, whose action is fixed by symmetries.

# First Principle Predictions for Cold Fermionic Gases Near Criticality via Critical Boson Dominance and Anomaly Matching

## Key claim

The authors provide analytic predictions for the compressibility and magnetic susceptibility of cold fermionic gases near unitarity with attractive interactions above the critical temperature, but only within the specific window of scattering lengths where the effective field theory is valid: $10 \geq |k_F a| \geq 1$. The predictions rely on the assumption that interactions are dominated by exchange of a gapped critical boson (dilaton) whose mass is fixed by anomaly matching, and the results carry a well-defined theoretical error of approximately 20%.

## Method

The authors construct an effective field theory for fermions near the unitary limit where interactions are dominated by exchange of a gapped dilaton boson, whose mass is fixed by matching the dilatation anomaly between the UV theory of interacting fermions and the IR effective theory. This anomaly matching yields the dilaton mass in terms of the scattering length $a$, the effective mass $m^\star$, and the contact density $\mathcal{C}(a)$. The dilaton exchange localizes at energies below the dilaton mass, generating a predicted s-wave Landau parameter $f_d$, which is then used to compute the compressibility and spin susceptibility via standard Fermi liquid formulas.

## Key numbers

- The effective mass at unitarity was measured to be $m^\star/m = 1.13$ (Nir); the authors take $m^\star/m \approx 1.1$ for their calculations.
- The range of validity for the EFT is $10 \geq |k_F a| \geq 1$, corresponding to $1 \gtrsim (k_F |a|)^{-1} \gtrsim 0.1$.
- The critical temperature peaks at unitarity with $T_c/T_F \approx 0.15$.
- The ratio of the short-distance contribution $f_{FS}$ to the dilaton contribution $f_d$ is approximately $f_{FS}/f_d \sim 0.2$, setting the 20% error budget.
- The compressibility and susceptibility predictions are given as functions of $k_F a$ for various values of $T/T_f$, with the compressibility formula: $\kappa/\kappa_0 = [1 - (\pi^2/12)(T^2/T_F^2)] / [1 + (1 - (\pi^2/12)(T^2/T_F^2)) (m^\star k_F / (m \pi^2)) f_0]$.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previously developed effective field theory formalism (Pavaskar and Rothstein, 2024) for cold fermionic gases near the unitary limit, which itself builds on the non-relativistic symmetry breaking analysis of Vishwanath (2001) and Rothstein and Shrivastava (2018). The predictions are compared to numerical simulations (numerics, numerics2) and show excellent agreement within the EFT validity window. The results support the idea that the unitary Fermi gas at the critical point can be described by coupling fermions to a dilaton that leads to a non-Fermi liquid, and the authors claim this is the first evidence for a dilaton in nature. The anomaly matching relation used is analogous to the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner relation (Gell-Mann, Oakes, and Renner, 1968) connecting meson masses to the quark chiral condensate.
