# Alleviating the inconsistencies in modelling decay of fissile compound nuclei

> This work attempts to overcome the existing inconsistencies in modelling decay of fissile nucleus by inclusion of important physical effects in the model and through a systematic analysis of a large set of data over a wide range of CN mass (ACN). The model includes shell effect in the level density (LD) parameter, shell correction in the fission barrier, effect of the orientation degree of freedom of the CN spin (Kor), collective enhancement of level density (CELD) and dissipation in fission. Input parameters are not tuned to reproduce observables from specific reaction(s) and the reduced dissipation coefficient is treated as the only adjustable parameter. Calculated evaporation residue (ER) cross sections, fission cross sections and particle, i.e. neutron, proton and alpha-particle, multiplicities are compared with data covering ACN = 156-248. The model produces reasonable fits to ER and fission excitation functions for all the reactions considered in this work. Pre-scission neutron multiplicities are underestimated by the calculation beyond ACN~200. An increasingly higher value of pre-saddle dissipation strength is required to reproduce the data with increasing ACN. Proton and alpha-particle multiplicities, measured in coincidence with both ERs and fission fragments, are in qualitative agreement with model predictions. The present work mitigates the existing inconsistencies in modelling statistical decay of the fissile CN to a large extent.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a statistical model incorporating shell effects in level density and fission barrier, collective enhancement of level density, K-orientation effects, and dissipation can simultaneously reproduce evaporation residue cross sections, fission cross sections, and particle multiplicities across a wide range of compound nucleus masses (A_CN = 156-248) using only the reduced dissipation coefficient as an adjustable parameter, thereby resolving previous contradictions where fission enhancement was needed to fit ER cross sections while fission suppression was needed to fit pre-scission neutron multiplicities.

## Method

The authors performed Monte Carlo statistical model calculations using the code VECSTAT, following the time evolution of compound nuclei formed in heavy ion-induced fusion-fission reactions. The model included shell-corrected level density parameters following Ignatyuk (1975), shell corrections to the fission barrier from the finite-range liquid drop model of Sierk (1986), collective enhancement of level density using rotational and vibrational enhancement factors with damping, K-orientation effects on the fission barrier following Lestone (1999), and Kramers fission width with transient time and saddle-to-scission neutron emission. The reduced dissipation coefficient beta was the only adjustable parameter, with input parameters fixed by fitting s-wave neutron resonance spacings following Reisdorf (1981) and using coupled-channels calculations with CCFULL for compound nucleus spin distributions.

## Key numbers

- Reduced dissipation coefficient beta = 2 x 10^21 s^-1 required for compound nuclei up to A_CN ~ 200 (e.g., 156Er, 170Yb, 192Pt, 197Tl, 200Pb)
- Reduced dissipation coefficient beta = 3 x 10^21 s^-1 required for compound nuclei between 210Po and 216Ra
- Reduced dissipation coefficient beta = 4 x 10^21 s^-1 required for highly fissile compound nuclei 239Np and 248Cf
- Compound nucleus mass range covered: A_CN = 156 to 248
- Fissility range covered: chi_CN = 0.600 (156Er) to 0.826 (248Cf)
- Critical energy for damping of collective enhancement: E_cr = 40 MeV with Delta E = 10 MeV, from Junghans (1998)
- Parameters for K-orientation: beta_2^0 = 0.15, Delta beta_2 = 0.04 from Ohta (2001)

## Context

This work extends the statistical model framework by incorporating multiple physical effects that were previously applied separately, resolving contradictions noted by Brinkmann (1994) and Sagaidak (2009) where fission barrier reduction was needed for ER cross sections while dissipation was needed for pre-scission neutron multiplicities. The model enables systematic determination of pre-saddle dissipation coefficients across a wide mass range, addressing questions raised by Paul (1994), Dioszegi (2000), and Shaw (2000) about disentangling pre-saddle and saddle-to-scission dissipation. The approach builds on earlier work by Lestone (2009) and the authors' shorter report (Banerjee 2018) by extending the analysis to a larger set of systems and additional observables including proton and alpha-particle multiplicities.
