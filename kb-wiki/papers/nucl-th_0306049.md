# Residual correlations in liquid drop mass calculations

> A systematic study of correlations in the chart of calculated masses of Moller and Nix is presented. It is shown that the differences between the masses calculated by Moller at al and the measured ones have a well defined oscillatory component as function of N and Z, which can be removed with an appropriate fit, reducing significantly the error width, and concentrating the error distribution on a single peak around zero. The residual correlations can have important consequences in the errors as signaling the presence of chaos, as was recently proposed.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the differences between nuclear masses calculated with the Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM) of Moller et al. and experimentally measured masses contain systematic, oscillatory residual correlations as functions of proton number Z and neutron number N, which can be removed by fitting a few sinusoidal components, thereby reducing the error width and eliminating an unexpected double-peak structure in the error distribution.

## Method

The authors performed a systematic Fourier analysis of the mass differences ΔM(N,Z) = M_exp - M_th for 1654 nuclei with measured masses from the FRDM calculation of Moller et al. (1995). They analyzed cuts along isotopic chains (fixed Z), isotonic chains (fixed N), chains of fixed mass number A and isospin projection T_z, and a "boustrophedon" ordering of all nuclei into a single list. They then fitted sinusoidal functions of N and Z with two dominant frequencies each (six parameters total for each of N and Z) to remove the oscillatory components.

## Key numbers

- The FRDM mass model error for the entire region from ¹⁶O to ²⁶³106 is 0.669 MeV, with 9 constants adjusted to ground-state masses of 1654 nuclei.
- The r.m.s. mass error for all nuclei is reduced from 0.681 MeV to 0.597 MeV after removing the oscillatory components.
- For nuclei with A ≥ 65, the r.m.s. mass error is reduced from 0.534 MeV to 0.462 MeV after correction.
- The dominant Fourier frequency in isotopic chains (Z=46 to 56) is f ≈ 0.05, corresponding to a period ΔA ≈ 20.
- For fixed T_z chains (2T_z = 5,6,7,8), the prominent peak is at f ≈ 0.033, corresponding to a period ΔA ≈ 30.
- For the boustrophedon ordering, the dominant frequency has period Δi = 207.
- The fitted sinusoidal frequencies for N are 0.012 and 0.047 (Δ₁ fit for A ≥ 65), with amplitudes 0.30 MeV and 0.20 MeV respectively.
- The fitted sinusoidal frequencies for Z are 0.020 and 0.053 (Δ₁ fit for A ≥ 65), with amplitudes 0.23 MeV and 0.10 MeV respectively.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of mass model errors from Moller et al. (1995), who noted systematic increases in error for lighter nuclei and attributed them to limitations of the Strutinsky method. The paper shows that the double-peak error distribution first observed in the FRDM results is not statistical noise but reflects genuine oscillatory correlations. The findings suggest that the Strutinsky method for including microscopic fluctuations in nuclear mass calculations could be improved, and the authors indicate that a refinement of the Strutinsky method is being studied in a separate publication (Velazquez et al., 2003). The power-law behavior of the Fourier amplitudes at higher frequencies is noted as a potential signature of quantum chaos, following the random matrix context of Relano et al. (2002).
