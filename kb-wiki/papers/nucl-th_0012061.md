# Liquid-gas phase transition in nuclei in the relativistic Thomas-Fermi theory

> The equation of state (EOS) of finite nuclei is constructed in the relativistic Thomas-Fermi theory using the non-linear $σ-ω-ρ$ model. The caloric curves are calculated by confining the nuclei in the freeze-out volume taken to be a sphere of size about 4 to 8 times the normal nuclear volume. The results obtained from the relativistic theory are not significantly different from those obtained earlier in a non-relativistic framework. The nature of the EOS and the peaked structure of the specific heat $C_v$ obtained from the caloric curves show clear signals of a liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclei. The temperature evolution of the Gibbs potential and the entropy at constant pressure indicate that the characteristics of the transition are not too different from the first-order one.

# Liquid-gas phase transition in nuclei in the relativistic Thomas-Fermi theory

## Key claim

The relativistic Thomas-Fermi theory with the nonlinear σ-ω-ρ model produces equations of state and caloric curves for finite nuclei that show clear signals of a liquid-gas phase transition, but the results are not significantly different from those obtained earlier in a non-relativistic framework. The characteristics of the transition are "not too different" from a first-order one, though the authors note that precise statements about the order require exact calculations of partition functions that are not yet possible.

## Method

The authors constructed the equation of state of finite nuclei using the relativistic Thomas-Fermi (RTF) approximation with the nonlinear σ-ω-ρ Lagrangian density, solving the coupled Dirac and Klein-Gordon equations in a spherical confining volume. Caloric curves (excitation energy versus temperature) were calculated for the representative nuclei ⁴⁰Ca, ¹⁰⁹Ag, and ¹⁵⁰Sm by confining each nucleus in a freeze-out volume taken as a sphere of size 4 to 8 times the normal nuclear volume. Three parameter sets (NL1, NL3, and NLSH) with nuclear incompressibilities K∞ of 212, 272, and 356 MeV respectively were used to study the effects of different equation-of-state stiffness on the thermodynamic properties.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature Tc for symmetric infinite nuclear matter with NL3 parameter set: 14.2 MeV
- Critical temperature Tc for asymmetric infinite nuclear matter (asymmetry X = 0.2) with NL3 parameter set: 13.6 MeV
- Critical volume Vc for finite nuclei: approximately 5 to 6 times the normal nuclear volume V0
- Coulomb interaction lowers Tc for ¹⁵⁰Sm by about 1 MeV
- Freeze-out volume used for caloric curve calculations: 8V0
- Normal nuclear saturation density ρ0: 0.15 fm⁻³
- Radius parameter r0 for normal nuclear volume: 1.2 fm
- For finite nuclei, the difference in neutron or proton chemical potentials at the ends of the Maxwell line is typically about 0.2 MeV, which is 30-40 times smaller than for infinite nuclear matter with the same asymmetry
- The N/Z ratio throughout the nuclear volume varies by no more than 10% at all temperatures beyond T ∼ 3.0 MeV

## Context

This work extends earlier non-relativistic Thomas-Fermi calculations by De et al. (1999) to a relativistic framework, motivated by the success of relativistic mean-field theory in describing ground-state properties of nuclei across the periodic table. The authors compare their results with experimental caloric curves from the ALADIN collaboration (Pochodzalla et al., 1995) for Au+Au at 600 A MeV, which showed a plateau at T ∼ 5 MeV, and from the EOS collaboration (Hauger et al., 1996) for Au on C at 1 A GeV, which showed a plateau at T ∼ 6 MeV. The calculated caloric curves exhibit shoulders at temperatures significantly higher than those observed experimentally, which the authors attribute to neglect of fluctuations and collective flow. The study enables future work incorporating these effects and suggests that the liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclei has characteristics closer to a first-order transition, consistent with lattice gas model results (Pandharipande et al., 1998), though experimental data remain inconclusive.
