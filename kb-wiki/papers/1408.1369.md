# Coulomb effect in Au+Au and Pb+Pb collisions as a function of collision energy

> The subtle differences between positive and negative pion spectra can used be used to study the nature of the nuclear interaction region in heavy-ion collisions. Several large acceptance heavy ion experiments at facilities ranging from SIS, the AGS, the SPS, to RHIC have measured mid-rapidity $π^+$ and $π^-$ spectra for central Au+Au or Pb+Pb collisions. From these spectra one can create pion ratios as a function of $m_t-m_0$, which are used to determine the Coulomb potential, $V_C$, and the initial pion ratio, $R_i$, across a range of collision energies from 1 to 158 AGeV. The implications of the $V_C$ and $R_i$ trends with collision energy will be discussed.

## Key claim

The Coulomb potential extracted from mid-rapidity pion ratios in central Au+Au and Pb+Pb collisions decreases monotonically with beam energy from 1 to 158 AGeV, and this decrease is primarily driven by a reduction in the net charge of the interaction region due to decreased baryon stopping rather than by an increase in the emission radius.

## Method

The authors compiled published mid-rapidity π⁺ and π⁻ transverse mass spectra from central Au+Au and Pb+Pb collisions at beam energies from 1 to 158 AGeV, using data from SIS, AGS, SPS, and RHIC experiments. They fit the pion ratio as a function of m_t-m_0 using a model that assumes identical initial spectral shapes for both charges, a Bose-Einstein emission function, and a Coulomb modification that includes an effective potential accounting for the expanding source by integrating the proton emission function up to a kinetic energy corresponding to the pion velocity. The fits used fixed pion and proton slope parameters determined from the spectra, with the Coulomb potential V_C and the initial pion ratio R_i as the two free parameters.

## Key numbers

- At 1 AGeV: V_C = 27.8 ± 1.3 MeV, R_i = 0.469 ± 0.011
- At 2 AGeV: V_C = 24.8 ± 0.9 MeV, R_i = 0.515 ± 0.005
- At 4 AGeV: V_C = 21.9 ± 0.5 MeV, R_i = 0.639 ± 0.004
- At 6 AGeV: V_C = 19.3 ± 0.4 MeV, R_i = 0.694 ± 0.004
- At 8 AGeV: V_C = 17.5 ± 0.5 MeV, R_i = 0.710 ± 0.004
- At 10.8 AGeV: V_C = 16.5 ± 4.1 MeV, R_i = 0.749 ± 0.035
- At 20 AGeV: V_C = 13.3 ± 3.0 MeV, R_i = 0.834 ± 0.008
- At 30 AGeV: V_C = 8.8 ± 1.5 MeV, R_i = 0.871 ± 0.009
- At 42 AGeV: V_C = 8.2 ± 5.0 MeV, R_i = 0.950 ± 0.050
- At 158 AGeV: V_C = 7.9 ± 0.5 MeV, R_i = 0.930 ± 0.003
- The KaoS group previously reported R_i = 0.515 ± 0.05 and V_C greater than 20 MeV at 1 AGeV; the E866 analysis at 10.8 AGeV found R_i = 0.83 and V_C = 9 ± 3 MeV
- The sidewards pion source radius R_side from two-pion femtoscopy is approximately 5 fm for all sqrt(s_NN) from 2.5 to 200 GeV

## Context

This work extends earlier analyses by the KaoS group (Wagner 1998, Muntz 1998) at SIS energies and the E866 collaboration (Ahle 1996) at AGS energies, who used static-source models to extract Coulomb potentials and initial pion ratios from mid-rapidity data. The authors improve upon these studies by incorporating an effective Coulomb potential that accounts for the expanding source, finding that the extracted V_C values are higher than those reported by the original collaborations. The monotonic decrease in V_C with beam energy tracks the decrease in net-proton rapidity density, suggesting the emission radius remains approximately constant across the energy range, consistent with femtoscopy results (Anson 2014). The rise in R_i from about 0.47 at 1 AGeV toward unity at higher energies indicates a transition from pion production primarily through the Delta resonance channel to isospin-independent direct production.
