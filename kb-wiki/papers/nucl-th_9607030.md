# Analysis of Delta I =2 staggering in nuclear rotational spectra

> A method is proposed and tested for the analysis of Delta I=2 staggering observed in nuclear rotational bands. We examine six super- and hyper-deformed bands, among which that of 149Gd and possibly of 147Gd seem to exhibit real staggering. However, we emphasize that the presence of staggering may not necessarily imply the occurrence of bifurcation. It is also shown that a similar staggering seen in normally deformed bands is a manifestation of band crossings. A more extensive analysis is planned.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the \(\Delta I=2\) staggering observed in nuclear rotational spectra does not necessarily imply a \(\Delta I=4\) bifurcation; the staggering can arise from band crossings or from the mathematical properties of the multi-point formulas used to analyze the data, and only in a few cases (notably \(^{149}\)Gd and possibly \(^{147}\)Gd) does the staggering appear to be a real physical effect.

## Method

The authors introduce a 1-point formula that subtracts a cubic polynomial fit from the measured \(\Delta I=2\) transition energies \(E_2(I)\), yielding a residual \(\Delta^{(1)}E_2(I)\) that reveals deviations from smooth behavior. They apply this method to six super- and hyper-deformed bands (\(^{147}\)Gd, \(^{149}\)Gd, \(^{154}\)Er, \(^{191}\)Hg, \(^{194}\)Hg, \(^{195}\)Pb) and compare the results with the standard 5-point formula and with the dynamic moment of inertia. A schematic three-band crossing model is also constructed to demonstrate how band crossings produce staggering in multi-point formulas.

## Key numbers

* \(^{149}\)Gd: the 1-point formula shows regular oscillations in the spin region 16–36 (relative to an unknown reference spin \(I_{ref}\)), and the dynamic moment of inertia decreases globally with spin, indicating real staggering.
* \(^{194}\)Hg: the 1-point formula shows irregular kinks outside the error bars, and the dynamic moment of inertia increases with spin, so the staggering seen in the 5-point formula is not real.
* \(^{147}\)Gd: the dynamic moment of inertia oscillates about a certain value, and the 1-point formula shows a staggering that is less regular than in \(^{149}\)Gd, making it a possible but uncertain candidate for real staggering.
* \(^{154}\)Er: the dynamic moment of inertia decreases, but the 1-point formula shows only irregular kinks, so the staggering is not real.
* \(^{191}\)Hg and \(^{195}\)Pb: the 1-point formula shows irregular kinks, and the dynamic moment of inertia increases (for \(^{191}\)Hg) or oscillates (for \(^{195}\)Pb), so the staggering is not real.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of \(\Delta I=2\) staggering first reported for super- and hyper-deformed bands in references [1-7] and debated theoretically in references [8-14]. The authors explicitly contrast their interpretation with the usual view that staggering implies bifurcation, and they show that for normally deformed bands (e.g., \(^{160}\)Yb) the staggering is a manifestation of band crossings, as previously discussed in a two-band crossing model by Hara and Sun (no year given). The proposed 1-point formula provides a tool to filter out spurious staggering before theoretical analysis, and the authors plan a more extensive analysis of super- and hyper-deformed bands.
