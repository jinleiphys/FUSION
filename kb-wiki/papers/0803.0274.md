# Comment on "Pygmy dipole response of proton-rich argon nuclei in random-phase approximation and no-core shell model"

> In a recent article by C. Barbieri, E. Caurier, K. Langanke, and G. Martínez-Pinedo \cite{Bar.08}, low-energy dipole excitations were studied in proton-rich $^{32,34}$Ar with random-phase approximation (RPA) and no-core shell model (NCSM) using correlated realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions obtained by the unitary correlation operator method (UCOM) \cite{Fel.98}. The main objective of this Comment is to argue that the article \cite{Bar.08} contains an inconsistency with respect to previous study of excitations in the same UCOM-RPA framework using identical correlated Argonne V18 interaction \cite{Paa.06}, it does not provide any evidence that the low-lying state declared as pygmy dipole resonance in $^{32}$Ar indeed has the resonance-like structure, and that prior to studying exotic modes of excitation away from the valley of stability one should ensure that the model provides reliable description of available experimental data on nuclear ground state properties and excitations in nuclei. Although the authors aimed at testing the UCOM based theory at the proton drip line, available experimental data that are used as standard initial tests of theory frameworks at the proton drip line have not been considered in the UCOM case (e.g., binding energies, one-proton separation energies, two-proton separation energies).

## Key claim

The Comment argues that the UCOM-RPA and NCSM calculations in Barbieri et al. (2008) are inconsistent with prior systematic UCOM-RPA studies using the same correlated Argonne V18 interaction (Paar, 2006), and that the low-lying state in ³²Ar labeled as a pygmy dipole resonance lacks evidence for resonance-like collectivity, as pairing correlations were not properly included.

## Method

The Comment compares the UCOM-RPA and NCSM results for ³²,³⁴Ar from Barbieri et al. (2008) against earlier systematic UCOM HF+RPA calculations across nuclei from ¹⁶O to ²⁰⁸Pb (Paar, 2006). It also references a UCOM second-RPA study (Papakonstantinou, 2007) that includes 2p2h configurations, and notes that the NCSM calculations in Barbieri et al. (2008) include up to 2p2h spaces but were not benchmarked against stable nuclei. The Comment critiques the lack of pairing correlations in the HF+RPA ground state and residual interaction, and the absence of tests against experimental ground-state properties (binding energies, separation energies) at the proton drip line.

## Key numbers

- In Paar (2006), excitation energies of isovector giant dipole resonances (IVGDR) are approximately 3–8 MeV higher than experimental values across nuclei from ¹⁶O to ²⁰⁸Pb.
- In Papakonstantinou (2007), including 2p2h configurations in UCOM RPA lowers dipole transition strengths in ¹⁶O and ⁴⁰Ca by approximately 8 MeV.
- The UCOM RPA centroid energy for the low-lying state in ³²Ar reported in Barbieri et al. (2008) is 9.15 MeV; the Comment notes this would be shifted to significantly lower values if 2p2h effects were included.
- The NCSM centroid energies for ³²,³⁴Ar in Barbieri et al. (2008) differ from UCOM RPA by less than 1 MeV.
- In Hartree-Fock calculations using UCOM, binding energies across the nuclide chart are underestimated by up to 50%, and radii are smaller by 1–3 fm compared to experimental data (Roth, 2006).

## Context

This Comment extends the critique of UCOM-based approaches from Paar (2006), which showed that UCOM HF+RPA overestimates giant resonance energies across stable nuclei, and from Roth (2006), which demonstrated large underestimates of binding energies and radii in HF and FMD. It contradicts the claim in Barbieri et al. (2008) that a pygmy dipole resonance exists in ³²Ar, arguing that the transition densities shown could arise from single-particle transitions alone (Vretenar, 2001). The Comment enables future work by emphasizing that before studying exotic modes at the proton drip line, UCOM-based theories must first be validated against existing experimental data on ground-state properties (binding energies, one- and two-proton separation energies) and giant resonances in stable nuclei, as done in relativistic QRPA (Paar, 2005) and experimental studies of pygmy dipole resonances in ¹³²Sn (Adrich, 2005).
