# Nucleon-Nucleon Potentials in Comparison: Physics or Polemics?

> Guided by history, we review the major developments concerning realistic nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials since the pioneering work by Kuo and Brown on the effective nuclear interaction. Our main emphasis is on the physics underlying various models for the NN interaction developed over the past quarter-century. We comment briefly on how to test the quantitative nature of nuclear potentials properly. A correct calculation (performed by independent researchers) of the $χ^2$/datum for the fit of the world NN data yields 5.1, 3.7, and 1.9 for the Nijmegen, Paris, and Bonn potential, respectively. Finally, we also discuss in detail the relevance of the on- and off-shell properties of NN potentials for microscopic nuclear structure calculations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the quality of a nucleon-nucleon (NN) potential should not be judged solely by its chi-squared fit to scattering data, because a model with many free parameters can achieve a low chi-squared while offering little physical insight, whereas a model with a solid theoretical basis and few parameters may have a slightly higher chi-squared but be far more meaningful for nuclear structure calculations.

## Method

The authors review three major classes of meson-theoretic NN potentials developed over the preceding 25 years: the one-boson-exchange model (exemplified by the Nijmegen potential), the dispersion-theoretic approach with two-pion exchange (the Paris potential), and the field-theoretic approach including irreducible two-meson exchanges such as pi-rho diagrams (the Bonn potential). They compare the theoretical input, number of free parameters, and chi-squared per datum for the fit of the world NN data, and discuss the on-shell and off-shell properties of each potential.

## Key numbers

- The chi-squared per datum for the fit of the world NN data (10–300 MeV) is 5.1 for the Nijmegen potential, 3.7 for the Paris potential, and 1.9 for the Bonn full model.
- For the Bonn B potential, the chi-squared per datum is 2.1 for 2158 np data without total cross section and 2.3 for 2322 np data including total cross section in the energy range 10–300 MeV.
- For the 567 neutron-proton data in the energy range 300–350 MeV, the chi-squared per datum is 18.0, 7.2, and 6.4 for the Nijmegen, Paris, and Bonn potential, respectively.
- The Nijmegen potential predicts the 3S1 phase shift at 210 MeV as 14.91 degrees, which is 12 standard deviations below the phase shift analysis value of 19.0 ± 0.33 degrees.
- The Paris potential predicts the pp singlet scattering length aC_pp off by 0.07 fm, which is 26 standard deviations from the empirical value (error 0.0026 fm).
- The Nijmegen and Paris potentials reproduce the four S-wave effective range parameters with a chi-squared per datum of 95 and 169, respectively.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on realistic NN potentials that began with the Hamada-Johnston potential (1962) and the Reid potential (1968), and was motivated by the pioneering effective interaction work of Kuo and Brown (1966). The paper contrasts the dispersion-theoretic Paris potential (Vinh Mau, 1973; parametrized by Lacombe et al., 1980) with the field-theoretic Bonn potential (Machleidt, Holinde, and Elster, 1987), and shows that including irreducible pi-rho exchange diagrams provides the missing short-range repulsion without resorting to extensive phenomenology. The results enable a more reliable interpretation of microscopic nuclear structure calculations, since the off-shell behavior of the potential is determined by theory rather than by the parametrization chosen to fit on-shell data.
