# Revisiting the Gluon Spectrum in the Boost-Invariant Glasma from a Semi-Analytic Approach

> In high energy heavy-ion collisions, the degrees of freedom at the very early stage can be effectively represented by strong classical gluonic fields within the Color Glass Condensate framework. As the system expands, the strong gluonic fields eventually become weak such that an equivalent description using the gluonic particle degrees of freedom starts to become valid. We revisit the spectrum of these gluonic particles by solving the classical Yang-Mills equations semi-analytically with the solutions having the form of power series expansions in the proper time. We propose a different formula for the gluon spectrum which is consistent with energy density during the whole time evolution. We find that the chromo-electric fields have larger contributions to the gluon spectrum than the chromo-magnetic fields do. Furthermore, the large momentum modes take less time to reach the weak-field regime while smaller momentum modes take more time. The resulting functional form of the gluon spectrum is exponential in nature and the spectrum is close to a thermal distrubtion with effective temperatures around $0.6$ to $0.9\, Q_s$ late in the Glasma evolution. The sensitiveness of the gluon spectrum to the infrared and the ultraviolet cut-offs are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new gauge-invariant formula for the gluon spectrum in the boost-invariant Glasma that remains consistent with the energy density throughout the entire time evolution, unlike previous Abelian expressions. The claim is hedged by the caveat that the dispersion relation for gluonic quasiparticles is chosen as that of free massless particles (\(\omega = k_{\perp}\)) without rigorous derivation, and the results rely on a leading \(Q^2\) approximation that includes only minimal non-Abelian effects.

## Method

The author solves the classical Yang-Mills equations in Milne coordinates under boost invariance using power series expansions in proper time \(\tau\), retaining only the leading terms in a momentum scale separation \(Q^2 \gg Q_s^2 \gg m^2\) (the leading \(Q^2\) approximation). Four correlation functions of field strength components are computed semi-analytically, then Fourier transformed to momentum space to construct the gluon spectrum via the new formula. The spectrum is fitted to thermal and nonthermal functional forms at late times (\(\tau = 0.6\) fm/c).

## Key numbers

- Effective temperatures from thermal fits at \(\tau = 0.6\) fm/c: \(T_{\rm eff} = 0.843\) GeV (roughly \(0.7 Q_s\)) for the Bose-Einstein fit with effective mass \(M_{\rm eff} = 0.717\) GeV.
- Nonthermal fit (Eq. 11) yields \(\tilde{T}_{\rm eff} = 0.937\) GeV (roughly \(0.8 Q_s\)) and \(\tilde{M}_{\rm eff} = 0.831\) GeV for benchmark parameters \(Q = 4.0\) GeV, \(m = 0.2\) GeV, \(Q_s = 1.2\) GeV.
- Alternative nonthermal fit (Eq. 13) gives \(\tilde{T}_{\rm eff} = 0.776\) GeV (roughly \(0.65 Q_s\)) for the same benchmark parameters.
- The power series expansion converges up to \(\tau_c \sim 0.5\) fm/c, enhanced tenfold from the naive estimate \(\tau_c \sim 1/Q \approx 0.05\) fm/c due to rapidly decreasing coefficients.
- For varying \(Q_s\) with fixed \(Q = 4.0\) GeV and \(m = 0.2\) GeV: at \(Q_s = 1.0\) GeV, \(\tilde{T}_{\rm eff} = 0.875\) GeV; at \(Q_s = 1.5\) GeV, \(\tilde{T}_{\rm eff} = 1.087\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the semi-analytic approach of Fries (2006) and Chen (2015) for solving classical Yang-Mills equations in the Glasma, improving upon the Abelian approximation used in earlier studies. It contrasts with numerical simulations by Krasnitz (2000, 2001, 2003), Lappi (2003), and Blaizot (2010), which fitted the gluon distribution to a Bose-Einstein form at low momenta and a power law at high momenta, and with the effective kinetic theory bridging the Glasma to hydrodynamics by Kurkela (2015). The new gauge-invariant formula enables mode-by-mode tracking of when strong fields become weak, and the resulting spectrum can serve as an improved initial condition for kinetic theory approaches to early thermalization.
