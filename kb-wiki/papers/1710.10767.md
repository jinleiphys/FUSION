# Extrapolation of scattering data to the negative-energy region II

> A problem of analytical continuation of scattering data to the negative-energy region to obtain information about bound states is discussed within an exactly solvable potential model. This work is continuation of the previous one by the same authors [L. D. Blokhintsev et al., Phys. Rev. C 95, 044618 (2017)]. The goal of this paper is to determine the most effective way of analytic continuation for different systems. The $d+α$ and $α+^{12}$C systems are considered and, for comparison, an effective-range function approach and a recently suggested $Δ$-method [O. L. Ram\'ırez Suárez and J.-M. Sparenberg, Phys. Rev. C 96, 034601 (2017)] are applied. We conclude that the $Δ$-method is more effective for heavier systems with large values of the Coulomb parameter, whereas for light systems with small values of the Coulomb parameter the effective-range function method might be preferable.

# Extrapolation of scattering data to the negative-energy region II

## Key claim

The Delta-method (using the function Δ_l(E) instead of the effective-range function) is more effective for analytic continuation of scattering data to bound-state poles in heavier systems with large Coulomb parameter, whereas for light systems with small Coulomb parameter the effective-range function method might be preferable; this conclusion is drawn from an exactly solvable square-well plus Coulomb potential model, not from experimental data.

## Method

The authors calculate exact scattering phase shifts for the d+α and α+¹²C systems using an analytic solution of the Schrödinger equation with a square-well nuclear potential plus Coulomb interaction. They then approximate the effective-range function K_l(E) and the Δ-function Δ_l(E) by Taylor series expansions in energy at E=0, extrapolate these polynomials to negative energies where bound-state poles lie, and compare the resulting asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) and binding energies to the exact values from the model.

## Key numbers

- For the d+α system (one bound state, ε=1.474 MeV, exact ANC=2.29 fm⁻¹/²): approximating K_0(E) with a 4th-order polynomial in version 1 gives ε=1.4744 MeV and C=2.2917 fm⁻¹/²; version 2 (binding energy fixed) with N=4 gives C=2.28997 fm⁻¹/².
- For the d+α system using Δ_0(E): version 1 with N=3 gives ε=0.432 MeV and C=0.565 fm⁻¹/², far from the exact values; version 2 with N=2 gives C=0.799 fm⁻¹/², also poor.
- For the α+¹²C system (two bound 0⁺ states, ε₁=1.113 MeV, exact C₁=3218.46 fm⁻¹/²): approximating K_0(E) with N=6 in version 1 gives ε₁=1.1126 MeV and C₁=3215.71 fm⁻¹/²; version 2 with N=6 gives C₁=3216.71 fm⁻¹/².
- For the α+¹²C system using Δ_0(E) in version 3 (approximating ln[Δ_0(E)/(E+ε₁)]): N=4 gives C₁=3216.54 fm⁻¹/², N=6 gives C₁=3217.52 fm⁻¹/², close to the exact value.
- The Coulomb parameter α₂=2μ/α₁² is 7.53 MeV⁻¹ for d+α and 0.0933 MeV⁻¹ for α+¹²C, two orders of magnitude smaller for the heavier system.
- For the α+¹²C ground state (ε₂=7.162 MeV, exact C₂=3475.35 fm⁻¹/²): approximating Δ_0(E) with explicit inclusion of the pole at Eᵢ₂=-4.48135 MeV gives C₂ values ranging from 29.62 to 9520.82 fm⁻¹/² depending on N and version, all far from the exact value.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Blokhintsev et al., Phys. Rev. C 95, 044618, 2017) by comparing the effective-range function method with the Delta-method proposed by Ramírez Suárez and Sparenberg (Phys. Rev. C 96, 034601, 2017) for two systems with different Coulomb parameters. The results show that the Delta-method works well for the α+¹²C system (large Coulomb parameter) but fails for the d+α system (small Coulomb parameter), where the effective-range function method is preferable. The paper also demonstrates that extrapolation to the deeply bound ground state of ¹⁶O is unreliable with either method, and criticizes prior attempts by Orloff et al. and Orloff and Irgaziev that ignored the presence of the excited 0⁺ state or the negative-energy pole of Δ_0(E), leading to unphysical imaginary ANCs.
