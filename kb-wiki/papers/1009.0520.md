# Coupled-channels density-matrix approach to low-energy nuclear reaction dynamics

> Atomic nuclei are complex, quantum many-body systems whose structure manifests itself through intrinsic quantum states associated with different excitation modes or degrees of freedom. Collective modes (vibration and/or rotation) dominate at low energy (near the ground-state). The associated states are usually employed, within a truncated model space, as a basis in (coherent) coupled channels approaches to low-energy reaction dynamics. However, excluded states can be essential, and their effects on the open (nuclear) system dynamics are usually treated through complex potentials. Is this a complete description of open system dynamics? Does it include effects of quantum decoherence? Can decoherence be manifested in reaction observables? In this contribution, I discuss these issues and the main ideas of a coupled-channels density-matrix approach that makes it possible to quantify the role and importance of quantum decoherence in low-energy nuclear reaction dynamics. Topical applications, which refer to understanding the astrophysically important collision $^{12}$C + $^{12}$C and achieving a unified quantum dynamical description of relevant reaction processes of weakly-bound nuclei, are highlighted.

# Coupled-channels density-matrix approach to low-energy nuclear reaction dynamics

## Key claim
Standard coherent coupled-channels models that treat excluded degrees of freedom only through complex potentials (optical models) are incomplete because they describe absorption but not quantum decoherence. The coupled-channels density-matrix (CCDM) approach, based on the Liouville-von Neumann master equation with Lindblad dissipative terms, can quantify the role of quantum decoherence in low-energy nuclear reaction dynamics, which may explain systematic disagreements between coherent coupled-channels predictions and high-precision fusion measurements.

## Method
The approach projects a Liouville-von Neumann master equation with Lindblad terms onto a product-state basis, where one part describes internuclear separations on a grid and another part describes selected intrinsic collective states of the interacting nuclei. This yields a finite set of coupled equations for time-dependent density-matrix elements, with the initial density matrix determined when the two nuclei are well-separated at ground states and a Gaussian wave-packet describes the radial motion. The formalism has been further developed using a coupled-angular-momentum state basis to investigate decoherence effects on asymptotic observables such as angular distributions of inelastic excitations.

## Key numbers
- The Gamow peak for the astrophysically important collision $^{12}$C + $^{12}$C is approximately 1.5 MeV, and direct experiments are extremely difficult below 3 MeV incident energy.
- In a one-dimensional model of a wave-packet scattering off a potential barrier, the optical model preserves spatial coherence while Lindblad dynamics results in loss of coherence, and the impact of decoherence on the tunnelling probability is "quite substantial" (the paper shows this qualitatively in Figure 3 but provides no explicit numerical values for the tunnelling probability or coherence measure).

## Context
This work extends the author's prior suggestions (Diaz-Torres, 2008-2010) that quantum decoherence and energy dissipation should be simultaneously included in low-energy reaction dynamics. It directly challenges the standard coherent coupled-channels picture, which has shown systematic disagreement with precise fusion measurements (Nanda, 2008; David, 2009) and has failed to simultaneously describe elastic scattering, quasi-elastic scattering, and fusion (Newton, 2007). The approach aims to address long-standing problems including understanding fusion resonances in $^{12}$C + $^{12}$C collisions (Aguilera, 2006; Spillane, 2007) and achieving a unified quantum dynamical description of weakly-bound nuclei reaction processes, including no-capture breakup and incomplete fusion, which existing quantum models (Thompson, 2009) cannot calculate unambiguously.
