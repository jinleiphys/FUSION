# Quasiparticle properties of nonequilibrium gluon plasma

> We apply classical gluodynamics to early stages of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. We start by going through the space-time evolution of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions in the color glass condensate framework and the basics of real-time gluodynamics on the lattice in the temporal gauge. We study the plasmon mass scale in three- and two-dimensional systems by comparing three different methods to measure the mass scale. The methods are a formula which can be derived from Hard Thermal Loop effective theory at leading order (HTL), the effective dispersion relation (DR) and measurement of the plasma oscillation frequency triggered by the introduction of a uniform electric field (UE) into the system. We observe that in both systems the plasmon mass scale decreases like a power law after an occupation number dependent initial transient time. In both cases the UE and HTL methods are in rough agreement, and in the three-dimensional case the two agree in the continuum limit. As a second way to study the quasiparticle properties, we derive, implement and test an algorithm which can be used to simulate linearized fluctuations on top of the classical background. The algorithm is derived by requiring conservation of Gauss' law and gauge invariance. We then apply the algorithm to spectral properties of overoccupied gluodynamics using linear response theory. We establish the existence of transverse and longitudinal quasiparticles by extracting their spectral functions. We also extract the dispersion relation, effective mass, plasmon mass and damping rate of the quasiparticles. Our results are consistent with the HTL effective theory, but we also observe effects beyond leading order HTL.

## Key claim

The paper establishes that classical gluodynamics can be used to study quasiparticle properties of nonequilibrium gluon plasma, and that the results are mostly consistent with Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) effective theory at leading order, though effects beyond leading order HTL are also observed, particularly for longitudinal modes.

## Method

The author compared three methods to measure the plasmon mass scale in three- and two-dimensional classical nonequilibrium gauge systems: a formula derived from Hard Thermal Loop effective theory at leading order (HTL), the effective dispersion relation (DR), and measurement of plasma oscillation frequency triggered by a uniform electric field (UE). Separately, the author derived, implemented, and tested an algorithm to simulate linearized fluctuations on top of a classical Yang-Mills background, conserving Gauss' law and gauge invariance, then applied linear response theory to extract spectral functions, dispersion relations, effective masses, plasmon masses, and damping rates of transverse and longitudinal quasiparticles.

## Key numbers

* In three dimensions, the plasmon mass squared scales as \(\omega_{pl}^2 \sim t^{-2/7}\) after an initial transient time.
* In two dimensions, the plasmon mass squared scales as \(\omega_{pl}^2 \sim t^{-1/3}\) after an initial transient time.
* In HTL perturbation theory, the plasmon mass \(\omega_{pl}\) and the asymptotic mass \(m\) are related by \(\omega_{pl}^{\mathrm{HTL}} = \sqrt{2/3} \, m_{\mathrm{HTL}}\).
* In HTL perturbation theory, the Debye mass is related to the plasmon mass by \(m_D^2 = 3 \omega_{pl}^2\).

## Context

This work extends the application of classical gluodynamics to the early stages of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions within the color glass condensate framework, building on prior studies of nonequilibrium gauge theory. The linear response formalism developed here enables future studies of spectral properties in anisotropic and expanding geometries, which are more relevant to heavy-ion collisions, and also allows investigation of unstable quantum fluctuations that may contribute to pressure isotropization as discussed by Gelis (2013).
