# Impact of reactor neutrino uncertainties on coherent scattering's discovery potential

> Nuclear power reactors are the most intense man-made source of antineutrino's and have long been recognized as promising sources for coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering (CE$ν$NS) studies. Its observation and the spectral shape of the associated recoil spectrum is sensitive to a variety of exotic new physics scenarios and many experimental efforts are underway. Within the context of the reactor antineutrino anomaly, which initially indicated eV-scale sterile neutrino's, the modeling of the reactor antineutrino spectrum has seen a significant evolution in the last decade. Even so, uncertainties remain due to a variety of nuclear structure effects, incomplete information in nuclear databases and fission dynamics complexities. Here, we investigate the effects of these uncertainties on one's ability to accurately distinguish new physics signals. For the scenarios discussed here, we find that reactor spectral uncertainties are similar in magnitude to the projected sensitivities pointing towards a need for $β$ spectroscopy measurements below the inverse $β$ decay threshold.

## Key claim

Reactor antineutrino spectral uncertainties are similar in magnitude to the projected sensitivities for detecting new physics signals via coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering (CEνNS), indicating that β spectroscopy measurements below the inverse beta decay threshold of 1.8 MeV are needed before reliable discovery claims can be made.

## Method

The author calculates CEνNS differential cross sections for Standard Model and Beyond Standard Model scenarios (non-standard interactions, generalized neutrino interactions, and neutrino magnetic moment) using reactor antineutrino spectra. Spectral uncertainties are estimated bin-by-bin by taking the maximum of the model uncertainty and the discrepancy between the Daya Bay experimental data and theoretical predictions, then propagating these uncertainties through to the recoil energy spectrum for silicon target nuclei. The resulting spectra are fit for changes to the weak charge Q_W and neutrino magnetic moment using Gaussian noise sampling.

## Key numbers

- Reactor antineutrino spectral uncertainties below the IBD threshold (1.8 MeV) contribute approximately half of the recoil spectrum at kinetic energies around 30 eV for silicon targets
- A single β transition with a 2 MeV endpoint can produce a 15% lower detected total count rate depending on whether the transition is allowed or unique first-forbidden
- The crossing point where sub-IBD-threshold antineutrinos dominate the recoil spectrum occurs at approximately 30 eV recoil kinetic energy
- Theoretical uncertainty estimates for antineutrino spectra remain at a few percent down to about 800 keV
- The Dresden-II experiment operates with a threshold energy of approximately 200 eV
- Typical reactor thermal power of 1 GW produces approximately 10^20 antineutrinos per second
- Uncertainties on reactor thermal power are estimated at less than 2%, while uncertainties on effective thermal energy per fission are 0.25-0.5%

## Context

This work extends the analysis of reactor antineutrino spectral uncertainties beyond the inverse beta decay threshold, building on the reactor antineutrino anomaly first identified by Mention (2011) and the subsequent theoretical and experimental efforts by Huber (2011) and Mueller (2011). The study directly addresses the needs of current CEνNS experiments including Dresden-II (Colaresi 2021, 2022), CONNIE (Aguilar-Arevalo 2022), and νGEN (Alekseev 2022), showing that without improved β spectroscopy measurements below 1.8 MeV, distinguishing new physics signals from spectral uncertainties will be severely limited. The work enables future experimental campaigns to prioritize measurements of forbidden β transitions and total absorption gamma spectroscopy, as previously pursued by Fijakowska (2017) and Guadilla (2019).
