# Analysis of the Multi-component Relativistic Boltzmann Equation for Electron Scattering in Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

> Big-bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) is valuable as a means to constrain the physics of the early universe and it is the only probe of the radiation-dominated epoch. A fundamental assumption in BBN is that the nuclear velocity distributions obey Maxwell-Boltzmann (MB) statistics as they do in stars. Specifically, the BBN epoch is characterized by a dilute baryon plasma for which the velocity distribution of nuclei is mainly determined by the dominant Coulomb elastic scattering with mildly relativistic electrons. One must therefore deduce the momentum distribution for reacting nuclei from the multi-component relativistic Boltzmann equation. However, the full multi-component relativistic Boltzmann equation has only recently been analyzed and its solution has only been worked out in special cases. Moreover, a variety of schemes have been proposed that introduce non-thermal components into the BBN environment which can alter the thermal distribution of reacting nuclei. Here, we construct the relativistic Boltzmann equation in the context of BBN. We also derive a Langevin model and perform relativistic Monte-Carlo simulations which clarify the baryon distribution during BBN and can be used to analyze any relaxation from a non-thermal injection. We show by these analyses that the thermalization process leads to a nuclear distribution function that remains very close to MB statistics even during the most relativistic environment relevant to BBN. Hence, the predictions of standard BBN remain unchanged.

## Key claim

The authors show analytically and via Monte-Carlo simulation that the nuclear velocity distribution during Big Bang Nucleosynthesis remains very close to Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics even in the most relativistic environment relevant to BBN, provided the nuclei are in thermal equilibrium with the background electron-positron plasma. This conclusion holds independently of the specific form of the electron distribution function, contrary to suggestions that non-thermal components or relativistic corrections could significantly alter the standard BBN predictions.

## Method

The authors constructed the relativistic multi-component Boltzmann equation for the BBN environment, reduced to a two-component system of nuclei scattering elastically with relativistic electrons. They derived analytic solutions using the entropy flow and Gibbs relation to identify the temperature parameter, and performed relativistic Monte-Carlo simulations of proton thermalization in a bath of Fermi-Dirac distributed electrons and positrons, sampling electron momenta from the flux-weighted distribution to account for instantaneous viscosity effects.

## Key numbers

- Baryon-to-photon ratio during BBN is approximately $10^{-9}$; the ratio of baryons to electron-positron pairs is also $\sim 10^{-9}$ during much of BBN.
- At temperature $T_9 = 11.6$ (1 MeV), the ratio of proton elastic scattering rates from electrons to that from other protons is $10^9$.
- At $kT = 0.1$ MeV, the ratio of proton scattering rates from electrons to that from photons is $10^3$; at $kT = 0.01$ MeV, this ratio is $10^{14}$.
- The Monte-Carlo simulation used $\sim 10^7$ scattering events; the standard deviation of the proton kinetic energy distribution from a pure Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution is about one percent over the energy interval from $0.25kT$ to $3kT$.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the relativistic multi-component Boltzmann equation previously studied by Kremer (2012, 2013) and Cubero (2007) to the specific BBN environment, where nuclei are dominated by Coulomb elastic scattering with relativistic electrons rather than by nuclear collisions. The results contradict the approach of McDermott (2018) which started from a Fermi-Dirac distribution for baryons, and show that the kinetic equipartition assumption used in classical Langevin models (Debbasch 1997, Dunkel 2007) is inappropriate for the relativistic primordial plasma. The work enables future studies of non-thermal injection scenarios (Jedamzik 2004, Kawasaki 2005, Kusakabe 2009, Cyburt 2010) by providing a validated simulation framework for analyzing relaxation from non-thermal distributions.
