# Patterns of the ground states in the presence of random interactions: nucleon systems

> We present our results on properties of ground states for nucleonic systems in the presence of random two-body interactions. In particular we present probability distributions for parity, seniority, spectroscopic (i.e., in the laboratory framework) quadrupole moments and $α$ clustering in the ground states. We find that the probability distribution for the parity of the ground states obtained by a two-body random ensemble simulates that of realistic nuclei: positive parity is dominant in the ground states of even-even nuclei while for odd-odd nuclei and odd-mass nuclei we obtain with almost equal probability ground states with positive and negative parity. In addition we find that for the ground states, assuming pure random interactions, low seniority is not favored, no dominance of positive values of spectroscopic quadrupole deformation, and no sign of $α$-cluster correlations, all in sharp contrast to realistic nuclei. Considering a mixture of a random and a realistic interaction, we observe a second order phase transition for the $α$-cluster correlation probability.

## Key claim

Using a two-body random ensemble (TBRE) Hamiltonian, the parity distribution of ground states for nucleonic systems reproduces the pattern seen in realistic nuclei with mass number A >= 70 (positive parity dominant for even-even systems, roughly equal positive/negative parity for odd-mass and odd-odd systems), but the dynamic properties of those ground states, seniority, spectroscopic quadrupole moments, and alpha-clustering correlations, all disagree sharply with realistic nuclei.

## Method

The authors diagonalized a scalar two-body Hamiltonian with random Gaussian-distributed two-body matrix elements (a TBRE Hamiltonian) for fermion systems in several truncated shell-model spaces, including the sd shell, the f5/2 p1/2 g9/2 shell, and combinations of these with the g7/2 d5/2 and h11/2 s1/2 d3/2 shells. For each system, 1000 runs of calculations were performed to accumulate statistics on parity, seniority, spectroscopic quadrupole moments, and the expectation value of the Majorana operator (as a measure of alpha-clustering correlation) in the ground states. For the alpha-clustering study, a mixed Hamiltonian H = (1-lambda) H_TBRE + lambda H_real was used, with lambda varied from 0 to 1.

## Key numbers

* For even-even nuclei in basis A (f5/2 p1/2 g9/2 shell), positive parity probability in ground states ranges from 81.8% for (Np,Nn)=(2,4) to 93.1% for (2,2); for odd-mass and odd-odd systems in the same basis, positive parity probability ranges from 31.2% for (2,1) to 77.1% for (1,3).
* For the sd shell, the number of cases with negative spectroscopic quadrupole moment (prolate deformation) exceeds those with positive moment for most configurations, e.g., (Np,Nn)=(2,1) gives 418 negative vs 280 positive; exceptions include (4,3) with 434 positive vs 374 negative.
* For 1000 TBRE runs, 485 runs produced (I,T)=(0,0) ground states for 8Be and 365 runs for 20Ne.
* The average expectation value of the Majorana operator <P_M> for (I,T)=(0,0) ground states was -1.26 for 8Be (geometric value -1.20) and -1.66 for 20Ne (geometric value -1.05); for the sdg shell with two protons and two neutrons, <P_M> was -0.629 (geometric value -0.4).
* For the mixed Hamiltonian in the p-shell, the average overlap of the ground state with the realistic Cohen-Kurath ground state drops sharply for lambda values below approximately 0.6, exhibiting a second-order phase transition.

## Context

This work extends the discovery by Johnson et al. (1999) of spin-zero ground state dominance in TBRE Hamiltonians and the subsequent studies of robust phenomena in many-body systems with random interactions. The authors explicitly contrast their findings with realistic nuclei: low seniority is not favored in TBRE ground states (unlike realistic pairing-dominated nuclei), no overall dominance of prolate deformation is observed (unlike the prolate dominance in realistic nuclei discussed by Tajima (2003)), and alpha-clustering correlations are absent in pure TBRE Hamiltonians (unlike the strong alpha-clustering seen in 8Be and 20Ne with realistic interactions such as Cohen-Kurath). The observed second-order phase transition in alpha-clustering probability as a function of mixing between random and realistic interactions provides a new tool for understanding how much random noise the realistic nuclear structure can tolerate before breaking down.
