# Event-by-Event Fluctuations

> In this review, we systematically examine the principles and the practices of fluctuations such as the momentum and the charge fluctuations as applied to the heavy ion collisions. Main emphases are: (i) Fluctuations as signals of phase transition (ii) Relationship between correlation functions and fluctuations (iii) Qualitative difference between fluctuations in small systems and large systems. Whenever available, theoretical results are compared with data from RHIC and SPS.

## Key claim

This review argues that event-by-event fluctuations of conserved charges (such as electric charge or baryon number) and of transverse momentum can serve as signatures of the QCD phase transition and of the formation of a Quark-Gluon Plasma, but the observable signals depend critically on the choice of statistical ensemble (grand-canonical vs. canonical) and on the size of the phase-space window, with volume fluctuations and initial-state effects (such as KNO scaling) acting as confounding factors that must be disentangled.

## Method

The authors systematically derive fluctuation observables from the grand-canonical and canonical partition functions, expressing charge and energy fluctuations as second derivatives of the free energy (susceptibilities). They then compare these thermal predictions with a wounded-nucleon model that includes no secondary scatterings, and with data from RHIC and SPS, to isolate the effects of thermalization, phase transitions, and experimental acceptance.

## Key numbers

- For a classical ideal pion gas, the ratio of charge fluctuation to entropy is \(\frac{\langle\delta Q_\pi^2\rangle}{S_\pi} = \frac{1}{6}\); for a 2-flavor Quark-Gluon Plasma it is \(\frac{\langle\delta Q_q^2\rangle}{S_{\rm QGP}} = \frac{1}{24}\), a factor of four smaller.
- Hadronic resonances reduce the pion-gas result by about 30%, leaving a factor of 3 signal for the Quark-Gluon Plasma.
- For a pion gas at \(T = 170\ \text{MeV}\), the quantum-statistics correction factor is \(\omega_\pi = 1.13\).
- In the canonical ensemble for kaon production at low energy (\(\langle N_K\rangle \simeq 0.1\)), the second factorial moment is \(F_2^{\rm C} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\langle N_K\rangle}{6} + \ldots\), compared to \(F_2^{\rm G} = 1\) in the grand-canonical limit.
- If the system passes through the QCD critical endpoint, a correlation length of \(\xi \simeq 3\ \text{fm}\) would produce a \(5-10\%\) increase in fluctuations of the mean transverse momentum.
- For 6% most central Au+Au collisions (\(R\approx 7\ \text{fm}\), \(b_{\rm max}\approx 3.0\ \text{fm}\)), geometric volume fluctuations give \(\langle\delta V^2\rangle_{\rm geom} \approx \langle V\rangle_{\rm geom}^2 / 170\).
- At RHIC, with about 600 charged particles per unit rapidity and \(\langle Q\rangle/\langle N\rangle \approx 0.1\), the ratio \(\langle Q\rangle^2/\langle N\rangle \approx 6\) is much smaller than the geometric factor \(C = O(100)\), making charge fluctuations robust against volume fluctuations.
- In the wounded-nucleon model without rescattering, the scaled multiplicity fluctuation \(\omega_{\rm WNM}\) depends linearly on the acceptance fraction \(p\) and includes a term \(c_{\rm KNO}\langle n\rangle_N\) with \(c_{\rm KNO} \approx 0.35\) for proton-proton collisions; at RHIC energy \(c_{\rm KNO}\langle n\rangle_N \approx 7\).

## Context

This review systematizes the theoretical framework for fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions, extending earlier work on charge fluctuations as a QGP signature by Asakawa (2000) and Jeon (2000), and on critical-point fluctuations by Stephanov (1998, 1999) and Berdnikov (1999). It connects fluctuation observables to susceptibilities calculable in lattice QCD (Gottlieb, 1997; Gavai, 2002) and to two-particle correlation functions (Bialas, 1999; Pruneau, 2002). The treatment of canonical-ensemble effects for small systems builds on Cleymans (1991) and Ko (2000). The review enables experimental searches for the QCD critical point via excitation functions of fluctuations, and provides a baseline (wounded-nucleon model) against which thermalization can be tested.
