# The equation of state for nucleon matter and neutron star structure

> Properties of dense nucleon matter and the structure of neutron stars are studied using variational chain summation methods and the new Argonne v18 two-nucleon interaction. The neutron star gravitational mass limit obtained with this interaction is 1.67 M_{solar}. Boost corrections to the two-nucleon interaction, which give the leading relativistic effect of order (v/c)^2, as well as three-nucleon interactions, are also included in the nuclear Hamiltonian. Their successive addition increases the mass limit to 1.80 and 2.20 M_{solar}. Hamiltonians including a three-nucleon interaction predict a transition in neutron star matter to a phase with neutral pion condensation at a baryon number density of 0.2 fm^{-3}. We also investigate the possibility of dense nucleon matter having an admixture of quark matter, described using the bag model equation of state. Neutron stars of 1.4 M_{solar} do not appear to have quark matter admixtures in their cores. However, the heaviest stars are predicted to have cores consisting of a quark and nucleon matter mixture. These admixtures reduce the maximum mass of neutron stars from 2.20 to 2.02 (1.91) M_{solar} for bag constant B = 200 (122) MeV/fm^3. Stars with pure quark matter in their cores are found to be unstable. We also consider the possibility that matter is maximally incompressible above an assumed density, and show that realistic models of nuclear forces limit the maximum mass of neutron stars to be below 2.5 M_{solar}. The effects of the phase transitions on the composition of neutron star matter and its adiabatic index are discussed.

## Key claim

The maximum gravitational mass of neutron stars is limited to below 2.5 solar masses by realistic models of nuclear forces, with the specific maximum mass depending on whether boost corrections and three-nucleon interactions are included, and whether quark matter admixtures are allowed. The abstract's quoted mass limits (1.67, 1.80, 2.20 solar masses) are conditional on the successive inclusion of the Argonne v18 interaction alone, then with boost corrections, then with three-nucleon interactions, and these limits are further reduced when quark matter admixtures are considered.

## Method

Variational chain summation (VCS) methods were used to calculate the equation of state of symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter with the Argonne v18 two-nucleon interaction, including relativistic boost corrections of order (v/c)^2 and the Urbana IX three-nucleon interaction (and its modified version UIX*). The possibility of mixed phases of quark and nucleon matter was investigated using the bag model equation of state, and the structure of neutron stars was computed from the resulting equations of state.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star gravitational mass limit with Argonne v18 alone: 1.67 solar masses
- Mass limit after adding boost corrections: 1.80 solar masses
- Mass limit after adding three-nucleon interactions: 2.20 solar masses
- Transition to neutral pion condensed phase predicted at baryon number density of approximately 0.2 fm^{-3} with three-nucleon interactions
- Maximum mass reduced from 2.20 to 2.02 solar masses for bag constant B = 200 MeV/fm^3 when quark matter admixtures are included
- Maximum mass reduced from 2.20 to 1.91 solar masses for bag constant B = 122 MeV/fm^3 when quark matter admixtures are included
- Realistic models of nuclear forces limit maximum neutron star mass to below 2.5 solar masses
- Equilibrium density of nuclear matter rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}
- Energy of neutron matter at 5 rho_0 predicted by lowest order Brueckner method ranges from 80 to 93 MeV per nucleon across modern potentials

## Context

This work extends the variational chain summation calculations of Wiringa, Fiks and Fabrocini (1988) who used the older Argonne v14 and Urbana v14 interactions with the Urbana VII three-nucleon interaction, obtaining mass limits of 2.19 and 2.13 solar masses. The present study uses the modern Argonne v18 interaction which fits the Nijmegen database of nucleon-nucleon scattering data, and incorporates relativistic boost corrections that had been neglected in most previous dense matter studies. The results enable more reliable predictions for neutron star structure and composition, including the possibility of mixed quark-nucleon phases in the cores of the heaviest stars, and provide constraints on the maximum neutron star mass that can be tested against future observations.
