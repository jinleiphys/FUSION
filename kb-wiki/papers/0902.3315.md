# Exotic quantum holonomy induced by degeneracy hidden in complex parameter space

> An adiabatic change of a bound state along a closed circuit in the parameter space can induces holonomies not only in the phase of the state, but also in the associated eigenspace and eigenvalue. The former is the well-known Berry phase while the latter, namely the exotic holonomy, is found a decade ago and its origin has not been understood yet. By extending the parameter into the complex number, the correspondence of the exotic holonomies and the degeneracy of the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian, or the exceptional points, is revealed. We show that this explains all the known non-trivial characteristics of the exotic holonomies.

## Key claim

By extending the real parameter space of a periodically kicked spin-1/2 system into the complex plane, the exotic holonomies (eigenspace and eigenvalue holonomies) are shown to originate from exceptional points (EPs), degeneracies of the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian, that are hidden in the unphysical complex parameter domain, even though the physical system remains unitary and the parameter path contains no degeneracy on itself.

## Method

The authors analyze a periodically kicked spin-1/2 system described by a Floquet operator \(\hat{U}(\lambda)\) where \(\lambda\) is a real parameter varied from 0 to \(2\pi\) along a closed loop in \(S^1\). They extend \(\lambda\) into the complex plane and identify the exceptional points (EPs) as square-root branch points of the quasienergy gap \(\Delta(\lambda)\). The holonomy matrix \(M(C)\) is computed from the non-Abelian gauge connection \(\AF(\lambda)\) using path-ordered exponentials, and the integral \(\eta(C) = \oint_C (\partial\Theta/\partial\lambda) d\lambda\) is evaluated via the residue at the EP enclosed by the loop.

## Key numbers

* The quasienergy gap is \(\Delta(\lambda) = 2\cos^{-1}\left[\cos((\lambda-\lambdaR)/2) / \cosh(\lambdaI/2)\right]\), where \(\lambdaR = 2\tan^{-1}[-\cos\theta\tan(\mu/2)]\) and \(\lambdaI = 2\tanh^{-1}[\sin\theta\sin(\mu/2)]\).
* The exceptional points are located at \(\lambda_{\pm} = \lambdaR \pm i\lambdaI\).
* The holonomy matrix for a single loop is \(M(C) = \sgn(\beta) \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\), where \(\sgn(\beta) = \pm 1\) for \(\beta \neq 0\).
* For a double loop, the holonomy matrix is \(M(C^2) = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\), corresponding to a Berry phase of \(\pi\).

## Context

This work explains the origin of the exotic holonomies first reported by Cheon (1998) in a kicked spin system, which had remained mysterious because the parameter space is only a one-dimensional loop \(S^1\) with no room for the Johansson-Sjöqvist theorem to apply. The authors show that the exotic holonomies are governed by the same exceptional-point topology studied in open quantum systems by Heiss (2000) and Dembowski (2001, 2003), but with the key distinction that the degeneracy is hidden in the unphysical complex parameter space rather than arising from a non-Hermitian Hamiltonian. This enables a unified theory of quantum holonomies and suggests that similar hidden degeneracies may be ubiquitous in other systems.
