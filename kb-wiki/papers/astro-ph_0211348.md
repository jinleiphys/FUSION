# Strange quark stars - A review

> A pedagogical overview of strange quark matter and strange stars is presented. After a historical notation of the research and an introduction to quark matter, a major part is devoted to the physics and astrophysics of strange stars, with attention being paid to the possible ways by which neutron stars and strange stars can be distinguished in astrophysics. Recent possible evidence for bare strange stars is also discussed.

# Strange quark stars - A review

## Key claim

Strange stars could exist in nature if the Bodmer-Witten conjecture is correct (that 3-flavor quark matter has lower energy per baryon than normal nuclear matter), but this conjecture cannot be proven from first principles; the paper argues that pulsar-like compact stars believed to be neutron stars might actually be strange stars, and that bare strange stars (without crusts) may be viable candidates for radio pulsars.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of strange star theory, from the discovery of neutron stars through the quark model and the Bodmer-Witten conjecture. It presents the MIT bag model equation of state for strange matter (P = (rho - 4B)/3) and discusses the TOV equation solutions that yield mass-radius relations for strange stars. The review then compares crusted versus bare strange stars, evaluates potential observational discriminators (rotation periods, mass-radius relations, surface properties), and examines possible evidence for bare strange stars from pulsar emission models, soft gamma-ray repeaters, and featureless X-ray spectra.

## Key numbers

- Quark deconfinement occurs at baryon number density rho_c ~ 1.5 rho_0 (where rho_0 is ordinary nuclear matter density), corresponding to a nucleon radius r_n ~ 1 fm
- For rho = 1.5 rho_0, Fermi energy is 379 MeV for u quarks and 477 MeV for d quarks, compared to strange quark mass m_s ~ 150 MeV
- Strange stars have typical mass 1-2 solar masses and radius ~10 km
- Near the quark surface, electron number density n_e ~ 9.5 x 10^35 / (1.2 z_11 + 4)^3 cm^-3 and electric field E ~ 7.2 x 10^18 / (1.2 z_11 + 4)^2 V/cm, where z_11 = z/(10^-11 cm)
- Crust mass for crusted strange stars is ~10^-5 solar masses
- QCD critical temperature for deconfinement at zero chemical potential is T_c ~ 150-180 MeV
- A pulsar-like compact star with radius less than ~8 km could be a strange star (Drake et al. 2002)
- The 2.14 ms optical source in SN 1987A (Middleditch et al. 2000) would be a strange star if confirmed

## Context

This review extends the line of work initiated by Bodmer (1971) and Witten (1984) on stable strange quark matter, and the strange star models of Haensel, Zdunik and Schaeffer (1986) and Alcock, Farhi and Olinto (1986). It contrasts with the conventional neutron star interpretation of pulsar-like compact stars, and specifically addresses the bare strange star hypothesis proposed by Xu and Qiao (1998) as an alternative to crusted strange stars. The review enables future observational identification of strange stars using advanced X-ray missions, focusing on surface conditions rather than complex interior processes that remain poorly understood.
