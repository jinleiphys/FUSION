# Hadrons in the Nuclear Medium

> Quantum Chromodynamics, the microscopic theory of strong interactions, has not yet been applied to the calculation of nuclear wave functions. However, it certainly provokes a number of specific questions and suggests the existence of novel phenomena in nuclear physics which are not part of the the traditional framework of the meson-nucleon description of nuclei. Many of these phenomena are related to high nuclear densities and the role of color in nucleonic interactions. Quantum fluctuations in the spatial separation between nucleons may lead to local high density configurations of cold nuclear matter in nuclei, up to four times larger than typical nuclear densities. We argue here that experiments utilizing the higher energies available upon completion of the Jefferson Laboratory energy upgrade will be able to probe the quark-gluon structure of such high density configurations and therefore elucidate the fundamental nature of nuclear matter. We review three key experimental programs: quasi-elastic electro-disintegration of light nuclei, deep inelastic scattering from nuclei at $x>1$, and the measurement of tagged structure functions. These interrelated programs are all aimed at the exploration of the quark structure of high density nuclear configurations. The study of the QCD dynamics of elementary hard processes is another important research direction and nuclei provide a unique avenue to explore these dynamics. We argue that the use of nuclear targets and large values of momentum transfer at would allow us to determine whether the physics of the nucleon form factors is dominated by spatially small configurations of three quarks.

## Key claim
The paper argues that experiments using the Jefferson Laboratory energy upgrade (to 11 GeV electrons) will be able to probe the quark-gluon structure of high-density configurations in cold nuclear matter, where local densities may reach up to four times typical nuclear densities. The authors hedge that while QCD has not yet been applied to nuclear wave function calculations, such experiments could determine whether nucleon form factors are dominated by spatially small three-quark configurations and whether hard two-body processes involve small-size quark-antiquark configurations.

## Method
The paper reviews three key experimental programs: quasi-elastic electro-disintegration of light nuclei at momentum transfers between 1.5 and 4 GeV^2 to probe short-range correlations; deep inelastic scattering from nuclei at Bjorken-x greater than 1 to observe superfast quarks; and measurement of tagged structure functions using backward-going nucleons in coincidence with the outgoing electron to study non-nucleonic degrees of freedom. The authors analyze how final state interactions, meson exchange currents, and isobar current contributions can be suppressed or controlled at high momentum transfer, establishing that Q^2 above 1.5 GeV^2 is required for effective short-range correlation studies.

## Key numbers
- Local high-density configurations in nuclei may reach densities up to four times larger than typical nuclear densities (rho_0 = 0.17 nucleon/fm^3)
- The optimal range for probing short-range correlations is 1.5 ≤ Q^2 ≤ 4 GeV^2
- About 25% of nucleons in heavy nuclei have momenta above the Fermi surface (k_F ≈ 250 MeV/c), with about 80% of this probability related to two-nucleon short-range correlations
- The proton electromagnetic radius is approximately 0.86 fm, and the average center-to-center distance between nearby nucleons in infinite nuclear matter is about 1.7 fm
- The conservation of the light-cone momentum fraction alpha for slow nucleons during rescattering is accurate to better than 5% for propagating nucleon momenta starting from 3 GeV/c

## Context
This review extends the discussion of the nuclear EMC effect discovered nearly twenty years prior, which showed modifications in nuclear structure functions that could not be explained by a simple collection of nucleons. The paper builds on prior work on short-range correlations using realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials (Pieper et al., 1997) and earlier inclusive experiments at SLAC. The proposed program would enable studies of cold dense matter relevant to understanding neutron star interiors and possible transitions to quark stars, complementing high-temperature studies from heavy-ion collisions.
