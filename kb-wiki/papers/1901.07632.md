# Neutron drip line in the Ca region from Bayesian model averaging

> The region of heavy calcium isotopes forms the frontier of experimental and theoretical nuclear structure research where the basic concepts of nuclear physics are put to stringent test. The recent discovery of the extremely neutron-rich nuclei around $^{60}$Ca [Tarasov, 2018] and the experimental determination of masses for $^{55-57}$Ca (Michimasa, 2018] provide unique information about the binding energy surface in this region. To assess the impact of these experimental discoveries on the nuclear landscape's extent, we use global mass models and statistical machine learning to make predictions, with quantified levels of certainty, for bound nuclides between Si and Ti. Using a Bayesian model averaging analysis based on Gaussian-process-based extrapolations we introduce the posterior probability $p_{ex}$ for each nucleus to be bound to neutron emission. We find that extrapolations for drip-line locations, at which the nuclear binding ends, are consistent across the global mass models used, in spite of significant variations between their raw predictions. In particular, considering the current experimental information and current global mass models, we predict that $^{68}$Ca has an average posterior probability ${p_{ex}\approx76}$% to be bound to two-neutron emission while the nucleus $^{61}$Ca is likely to decay by emitting a neutron (${p_{ex}\approx 46}$ %).

# Neutron drip line in the Ca region from Bayesian model averaging

## Key claim
The authors predict that the two-neutron drip line in the Ca region extends to approximately $^{72}$Ca and $^{78}$Ti, with $^{68}$Ca having an average posterior probability of about 76% to be bound to two-neutron emission, but these predictions are conditional on the correctness of the density functional theory framework and the global mass models used.

## Method
The authors used nine global nuclear mass models based on Skyrme energy density functionals (SkM*, SkP, SLy4, SV-min, UNEDF0, UNEDF1, UNEDF2) and phenomenological models (FRDM-2012, HFB-24) to compute one-neutron and two-neutron separation energies. They applied Gaussian process emulators trained on experimental mass data (AME2003 and AME2016*) to correct model predictions and quantify uncertainties, then performed Bayesian model averaging to compute posterior existence probabilities $p_{ex}$ for each nucleus between Si and Ti.

## Key numbers
- $^{68}$Ca has an average posterior probability $p_{ex} \approx 76\%$ to be bound to two-neutron emission
- $^{61}$Ca has $p_{ex} \approx 46\%$, indicating it is likely to decay by emitting a neutron
- $^{60}$Ca is expected to be well bound with $S_{2n} \approx 5$ MeV
- The two-neutron drip line extends to $^{72}$Ca and $^{78}$Ti according to the averaged predictions
- Prediction improvements from the Gaussian process correction ranged from 20% to 40% for most models
- The error on $S_{1n}$ for odd-A and odd-odd nuclei obtained from even-even neighbors is expected to be 200-300 keV

## Context
This work extends the Bayesian model averaging methodology previously developed by Neufcourt (2018) to the Ca region, building on the recent experimental discovery of $^{60}$Ca by Tarasov (2018) and mass measurements of $^{55-57}$Ca by Michimasa (2018). The results address a discrepancy between A-body methods (which predict the two-neutron drip line around $^{60}$Ca, as in Hagen 2013 and Stroberg 2017) and DFT approaches (which locate it around $^{70}$Ca, as in Erler 2012 and Forssen 2013). The analysis enables quantified predictions for future experimental searches at rare-isotope facilities and identifies $^{49}$S, $^{52}$Cl, and $^{53}$Ar as marginally-bound threshold systems that challenge current mass models.
