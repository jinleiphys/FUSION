# Thermal Particle and Photon Production in Pb+Pb Collisions with Transverse Flow

> Particle and photon production is analyzed in the presence of transverse flow using two approximations to describe the properties of the hadronic medium, one containing only $π, ρ, ω$, and $η$ mesons (simplified equation of state) and the other containing hadrons and resonances from the particle data table. Both are considered with and without initial quark gluon plasma formation. In each case the initial temperature is fixed by requiring $dN_{ch}/dy \sim$ 550 in the final state. It is shown that most observables are very sensitive to the equation of state. This is particularly evident when comparing the results of the simplified equation of state in the scenarios with and without phase transition. The hadronic gas scenario leads to a substantially higher rate for the $p_T$-distribution of all particles. In the complete equation of state with several hundreds of hadronic resonances, the difference between the scenarios with and without phase transition is rather modest. Both photon and particle spectra, in a wide $p_T$ range, show very similar behavior. It is therefore concluded that from the $p_T$ spectra it will be hard to disentangle quark gluon plasma formation in the initial state. It is to be stressed however, that there are conceptual difficulties in applying a pure hadronic gas equation of state at SPS-energies. The phase transition scenario with a quark gluon plasma present in the initial state seems to be the more natural one.

# Thermal Particle and Photon Production in Pb+Pb Collisions with Transverse Flow

## Key claim

When using a complete equation of state containing all hadrons and resonances from the particle data table, the difference in particle and photon transverse momentum spectra between scenarios with and without initial quark-gluon plasma formation is very modest, making it hard to prove QGP presence from pT spectra alone; however, the authors stress that a pure hadronic gas equation of state at SPS energies faces conceptual difficulties due to unacceptably high particle densities, making the phase transition scenario the more natural one.

## Method

The authors solved relativistic hydrodynamic equations for longitudinally boost-invariant expansion with transverse flow in Pb+Pb collisions, using four combinations of equation of state: a simplified hadronic gas containing only pi, rho, omega, and eta mesons versus a complete gas with all hadrons and resonances from the particle data table, each considered with and without an initial quark-gluon plasma phase. The initial temperature in each scenario was fixed by requiring dNch/dy ~ 550 in the final state and assuming entropy conservation, with an initial thermalization time tau0 ~ 1 fm. Thermal photon rates were calculated using the Braaten-Pisarski resummed rate for the QGP phase and contributions from pi-rho-A1 interactions for the hadronic phase.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature required to reproduce final state entropy: T ~ 320 MeV for simplified equation of state without phase transition; T ~ 210 MeV for complete equation of state with phase transition
- Critical temperature assumed: Tc = 160 MeV
- Charged particle multiplicity used to fix initial conditions: dNch/dy ~ 550
- Initial thermalization time: tau0 ~ 1 fm
- Particle density at T ~ 200 MeV in pure hadronic gas: 2-3/fm^3
- For the simplified equation of state without phase transition, initial particle density exceeds 10 hadrons/fm^3 at tau_i = 1 fm/c
- For the complete equation of state without phase transition, initial particle density exceeds 3 hadrons/fm^3 at tau_i = 1 fm/c
- At the critical temperature, the effective number of degrees of freedom increases by more than a factor of two when going from the simplified to the complete particle input

## Context

This work extends hydrodynamic studies of transverse flow effects in relativistic heavy ion collisions, building on prior work by Rischke et al. (1995) on the influence of the equation of state on transverse expansion, and by Sollfrank et al. (1996) on particle spectra with flow. The paper systematically compares four equation-of-state scenarios to assess whether pT spectra can distinguish QGP formation, concluding that the complete resonance gas makes the difference between scenarios almost non-existent. This enables future work to focus on other observables or on the conceptual necessity of a phase transition at SPS energies, as the pure hadronic gas at T ~ 200 MeV leads to particle densities incompatible with finite hadron sizes.
