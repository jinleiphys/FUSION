# Phenomenology of Irreversible Processes from Gravity

> We propose that the space-time evolution of strongly coupled matter formed by ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions can be modelled by phenomenological equations involving the energy-momentum tensor and conserved currents alone. These equations can describe the late stage of local chemical and thermal equilibration of the matter formed after collisions, and its subsequent transition to hydrodynamic expansion in an unified framework. The full set of equations include local energy, momentum and charge conservation; but also additional equations for evolution of non-equilibrium variables. These equations with precisely determined phenomenological parameters can be obtained by the AdS/CFT correspondence. On the gravity side of this correspondence, for vanishing chemical potentials, these phenomenological equations give all solutions of pure gravity in AdS which have regular future horizons. We also discuss field-theoretic grounds for validity of these phenomenological equations.

# Phenomenology of Irreversible Processes from Gravity

## Key claim

The authors propose that the space-time evolution of strongly coupled matter formed in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions can be modelled by a closed set of phenomenological equations involving only the energy-momentum tensor and conserved currents, which unify the late stages of local thermal and chemical equilibration with subsequent hydrodynamic expansion. The hedge is that these equations are claimed to describe the transition to equilibrium and hydrodynamic expansion only for the late stage, and their validity relies on the AdS/CFT correspondence to determine the phenomenological parameters, with the full set of gravity solutions with regular future horizons reproduced only for vanishing chemical potentials.

## Method

The authors construct phenomenological equations by decomposing the energy-momentum tensor into hydrodynamic and non-hydrodynamic parts using the Landau-Lifshitz decomposition, where the non-equilibrium shear-stress tensor is split into a purely hydrodynamic part (given by a derivative expansion up to second order, with transport coefficients from AdS/CFT) and a non-hydrodynamic part. The non-hydrodynamic part is governed by a general Weyl and Lorentz covariant equation that sums over all convective time derivatives at each order in an amplitude expansion, with phenomenological parameters partially determined by requiring regularity of future horizons in dual gravity solutions describing homogeneous relaxation.

## Key numbers

- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio from AdS/CFT is given as η/s = 1/4π.
- Experimental data analysis suggests 1 < 4π(η/s) < 2.5 for temperatures probed at RHIC.
- The first few phenomenological parameters for the non-hydrodynamic equation are: D_R^(1,0) = -1, D_R^(1,1) = -(π/2) - (1/4) ln 2, and D_R^(2,0,0) = 1/2.
- The energy density is normalized as e(x) = (3/4)·(π T(x))^4.
- The hydrodynamic part of the shear-stress tensor up to second order in derivatives includes terms with coefficients (2 - ln 2) and ln 2 multiplying (π T)^2.

## Context

This work extends the program of using AdS/CFT to model strongly coupled matter in heavy ion collisions, building on the known result of η/s = 1/4π from Policastro (2001) and the systematic hydrodynamic corrections to Navier-Stokes reviewed by Janik. The authors propose that their phenomenological equations can be matched with early-stage models such as the parton cascade model of Geiger, and they provide field-theoretic justification by showing that the Boltzmann equation has special "conservative solutions" determined by the energy-momentum tensor alone, which become purely hydrodynamic "normal solutions" at late times as described by Chapman. The approach enables a unified framework for describing both the thermalization and hydrodynamic expansion phases, potentially addressing puzzles such as the RHIC HBT puzzle that remain unexplained by simple hydrodynamic models.
