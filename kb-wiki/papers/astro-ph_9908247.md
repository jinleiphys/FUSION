# Fine-tuning carbon-based life in the universe by the triple-alpha process in red giants

> Through the triple-alpha process occurring in red giant stars the bulk of the carbon existing in our universe is produced. We calculated the change of the triple-alpha reaction rate for slight variations of the nucleon-nucleon force using a microscopic 12-body model. Stellar model calculations for a low-mass, intermediate-mass and massive star using the different triple-alpha reaction rates obtained with different strengths of the N-N interaction have been performed. Even with a change of 0.4% in the strength of N-N force, carbon-based life appears to be impossible, since all the stars then would produce either almost solely carbon or oxygen, but could not produce both elements.

## Key claim

Even a 0.4% change in the strength of the nucleon-nucleon (N-N) force makes carbon-based life impossible because all stars would then produce almost solely carbon or oxygen, but not both elements; for smaller variations (e.g., 0.1%), the viability of life depends on the stellar population, as a 20 solar-mass star with a 0.1% weaker force produces no carbon while lower-mass stars still do.

## Method

The authors used a microscopic 12-body three-cluster model to calculate the resonance energy and radiative width of the second 0⁺ state in ¹²C for six variations (p = 0.996, 0.998, 0.999, 1.001, 1.002, 1.004) of the strength of three different effective N-N interactions (Minnesota, Volkov 1, Volkov 2). They then input these triple-alpha reaction rates into a stellar evolution code to follow the evolution of 1.3, 5, and 20 solar-mass stars from hydrogen burning through the first thermal pulses or until core temperature reached 10⁹ K.

## Key numbers

- The experimental resonance energy of the 0⁺₂ state in ¹²C is ε = 0.3796 MeV with full width Γ = 8.5 × 10⁻⁶ MeV (8.5 eV).
- For the Minnesota force at p = 1.004 (0.4% stronger), the resonance energy shifts to ε = 273.3 keV; at p = 0.996 (0.4% weaker), it shifts to ε = 481.4 keV.
- The radiative width Γ_γ for the 0⁺₂ → 2⁺₁ transition ranges from 2.85 meV (p = 1.004, MN) to 3.07 meV (p = 0.996, MN), compared to the experimental value of 3.7 ± 0.5 meV.
- A 0.4% change in N-N strength (p = 0.996 or 1.004) causes a 5 solar-mass star to produce almost no oxygen or almost no carbon, respectively.
- For a 20 solar-mass star, even p = 0.999 (0.1% weaker force) destroys carbon almost totally.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Livio et al. (1989) that only investigated hypothetical ad hoc shifts of the 0⁺₂ resonance energy, by instead starting from variations of the underlying N-N interaction. The results enable quantitative constraints on the fine-tuning of fundamental forces required for carbon-based life, and they connect nuclear-structure calculations of the Hoyle state to stellar nucleosynthesis across low-mass, intermediate-mass, and massive stars. The paper also addresses the ongoing debate about which stellar mass range dominates carbon production in the Galaxy, as discussed by Gustafsson and Ryde (1998).
