# Asymptotic normalization coefficients for $α+ {}^{12}{\rm C}$ synthesis and the $S$-factor for ${}^{12}{\rm C}(α, \,γ){}^{16}{\rm O}$ radiative capture

> The $^{12}{\rm C}(α,γ)^{16}$O reaction, determining the survival of carbon in red giants, is of interest for nuclear reaction theory and nuclear astrophysics. A specific feature of the $^{16}$O nuclear structure is the presence of two subthreshold bound states, (6.92 MeV, 2$^+$) and (7.12 MeV, 1$^-$), that dominate the behavior of the low-energy $S$-factor. The strength of these subthreshold states is determined by their asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs), which need to be known with high accuracy. Recently, using a model-independent extrapolation method, Blokhintsev {\it et al.} [Eur. Phys. J. A {\bf 59} (2023) 162] determined the ANCs for the $α$-particle removal taking into account three subthreshold states in $^{16}$O. The goal of this paper is to address four main problems elucidating the impact of the subthreshold ANCs on the low-energy $S$-factor. Firstly, we analyse the connection between variations of the subthreshold ANCs and the low-energy $S$-factor, in particular, at the most effective energy of $300$ keV. Secondly, we calculate contributions to the $S(300\,{\rm keV})$-factor from the subthreshold $1^{-}$ and $2^{+}$ resonances, that are controlled by the subthreshold ANCs. We also evaluate the contribution of the uncertainties of the subthreshold ANCs to the budget of the low-energy $S$-factor uncertainty, especially, the $S(300\,{\rm keV})$-factor. Thirdly, we analyse interference of the subthreshold resonances (SRs) with higher resonances and with the $E1$ and $E2$ direct captures to the ground state. Finally, we investigate a correlated effect of the subthreshold and ground-state ANCs on the low-energy $S$-factor and, in particular, on the $S(300\,{\rm keV})$-factor.

## Key claim

The subthreshold asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) for the 1⁻ (7.12 MeV) and 2⁺ (6.92 MeV) states in ¹⁶O dominate the low-energy S-factor for ¹²C(α,γ)¹⁶O, and their uncertainties are a major contributor to the uncertainty budget of S(300 keV); however, the ground-state ANC has negligible influence on the E1 component at low energies due to the very small effective charge for E1 transitions.

## Method

The authors performed R-matrix calculations using the AZURE2 code, separating the radiative capture amplitude into nonresonant (direct capture), subthreshold resonance (SR), and higher resonance contributions. They employed two sets of subthreshold ANCs: a high set from Blokhintsev et al. (2023) obtained via extrapolation of elastic scattering phase shifts to bound-state poles, and a low set from deBoer et al. (2017). The S-factors were computed including E1 and E2 transitions to the ground state of ¹⁶O, interference between SRs and higher resonances, interference with direct captures, and cascade radiative captures through four subthreshold states (0₂⁺, 3⁻, 2⁺, and 1⁻).

## Key numbers

- Binding energies of the two key subthreshold states: ε_s1 = 0.045 MeV (1⁻ state) and ε_s2 = 0.2449 MeV (2⁺ state)
- Ground-state binding energy of ¹⁶O: ε₀ = 7.162 MeV
- Ground-state ANC values explored: 58 fm⁻¹/² (from deBoer et al. 2017) and 337 fm⁻¹/² (from Shen et al.)
- Effective charge for E1 transition: Z_eff(1)/e = -0.00097, which suppresses the channel radiative width and direct capture for E1
- The nearest 1⁻ and 2⁺ resonances above threshold are located at 2.43 MeV and 2.68 MeV, respectively

## Context

This work extends the R-matrix analysis of the ¹²C(α,γ)¹⁶O reaction, which determines the carbon-to-oxygen ratio in red giant stars and has been studied for nearly 50 years (Rolfs and Rodney; deBoer et al. 2017). The paper directly builds on the extrapolation method of Blokhintsev et al. (2023) for determining subthreshold ANCs and compares with the lower ANC values used in the comprehensive review by deBoer et al. (2017). The results enable improved uncertainty quantification for the S(300 keV) factor, which is critical for stellar evolution models but cannot be measured directly due to the extremely small cross section at that energy (the lowest measured S-factor energy is approximately 1 MeV).
