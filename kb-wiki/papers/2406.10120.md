# Weak entanglement approximation for nuclear structure

> The interacting shell model, a configuration-interaction method, is a venerable approach for low-lying nuclear structure calculations; but it is hampered by the exponential growth of its basis dimension as one increases the single-particle space and/or the number of active particles. Recent, quantum-information-inspired work has demonstrated that the proton and neutron sectors of a nuclear wave function are weakly entangled. Furthermore, the entanglement is smaller for nuclides away from $N=Z$, such as heavy, neutron-rich nuclides. Here we implement a weak entanglement approximation to bipartite configuration-interaction wave functions, approximating low-lying levels by coupling a relatively small number of many-proton and many-neutron states. This truncation scheme, which we present in the context of past approaches, reduces the basis dimension by many orders of magnitude while preserving essential features of nuclear spectra.

## Key claim

The weak entanglement approximation, implemented as the Proton And Neutron Approximate Shell-model (PANASh), reduces the basis dimension by many orders of magnitude while preserving essential features of nuclear spectra, with the caveat that the approximation works best for nuclei with weaker proton-neutron entanglement, particularly neutron-rich nuclei away from N=Z.

## Method

The authors first solve the Hamiltonian in the zero-entanglement limit by setting the proton-neutron interaction to zero, obtaining uncoupled proton and neutron eigenstates. They then couple these eigenstates together in all combinations suitable to form basis states with good total angular momentum, truncating the number of factors from each subspace to reduce the overall basis dimension. Finally, the full Hamiltonian including the proton-neutron interaction is diagonalized in this truncated basis.

## Key numbers

- For $^{78}$Ge: using 1% of basis factors (four orders of magnitude reduction in dimension), ground state binding energy within 1.6 MeV of full configuration interaction (FCI) at 676 MeV total binding energy
- For $^{60}$Ni: using 1% of basis factors (four orders of magnitude reduction), ground state binding energy within 1 MeV of FCI; using 5% of basis factors (three orders of magnitude reduction), ground state binding energy within 160 keV and first $2^+$ state within 81 keV
- For $^{70}$As: using 1% of basis factors, ground state binding energy discrepancy of almost 3 MeV; using 9% of basis factors, $2^-$ ground state off by 0.75 MeV
- For $^{79}$Rb: using 1% of basis factors (four orders of magnitude reduction), ground state binding energy too high by 2.1 MeV; using 5% of basis factors, ground state binding energy error of 770 keV
- For $^{132}$Ce aspirational calculation: PANASh used 1000 proton and 1000 neutron levels (neutron basis fraction m=0.015 d_n), largest J-scheme dimension 432,000 for J=6 compared to FCI J=6 dimension of 133 billion; PANASh ground state energy 3.37 MeV lower than truncated shell model calculation
- For $^{128}$Xe: PANASh ground state energy 0.43 MeV above FCI ground state, while truncated shell model ground state was 1.53 MeV above FCI

## Context

This work extends the observation from Johnson (2023) that low-lying shell model states have weak proton-neutron entanglement, with nuclei having N greater than Z showing systematically lower entanglement than N=Z counterparts. The method relates to density matrix renormalization group (DMRG) approaches by White (1992, 1993) and Papenbrock (2005), as well as variational wavefunction factorization by Papenbrock (2003, 2004), but PANASh is more straightforward as it does not iterate to optimize the basis. The approach enables calculations in model spaces too large for standard shell model diagonalization, such as $^{132}$Ce with an FCI M-scheme dimension of 2.4 trillion, and can provide many more excited states than FCI for comparable computational cost.
