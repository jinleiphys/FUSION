# Time dependence of partition into spectators and participants in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> The process of formation of the participant system in heavy-ion collisions is investigated in the framework of a simplified analytic Glauber-like model, which is based on the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation. The key point lies in the time-dependent partition of the nucleon system into two groups: nucleons, which did not take part in any interaction before a given time and nucleons, which already have interacted. In the framework of the proposed model we introduce a natural energy-dependent temporal scale $t_c$, which allows us to remove all dependencies of the model on the collision energy except for the energy dependence of the nucleon-nucleon cross-section. By investigating the time dependence of the total number of participants we conclude that the formation process of the participant system becomes complete at $t\simeq1.5 t_c$. Time dependencies of participant total angular momentum and vorticity are also considered and used to describe the emergence of rotation in the reaction plane.

## Key claim
The paper claims that the formation of the participant system in relativistic heavy-ion collisions becomes complete at approximately \(t \simeq 1.5 t_c\), where \(t_c = R_0/(\gamma_0 v_0)\) is a natural energy-dependent temporal scale. This conclusion is hedged by the fact that the model is a simplified analytic Glauber-like approach based on the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation with only "loss" terms, and comparisons with UrQMD show that the number of participants continues to increase slightly beyond \(1.5 t_c\) in the more complex cascade model.

## Method
The authors developed an analytic model based on the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation, assuming a ballistic mode for nucleons and binary collisions with only "loss" terms to describe the time-dependent partition of nucleons into spectators (those that have not yet interacted) and participants (those that have interacted). The initial spatial distribution of nucleons was taken as a Woods-Saxon profile, and the momentum distribution was approximated as delta functions in the forward-backward direction, neglecting Fermi motion. The model was solved to obtain time-dependent spatial densities of spectator nucleons, from which the total number of participants, total angular momentum of participants, and vorticity in the reaction plane were calculated as functions of time.

## Key numbers
- The formation of the participant system becomes complete at \(t \simeq 1.5 t_c\).
- The time scale \(t_c\) lies in the range: \(t_c \simeq 1\) to \(2\) fm/\(c\) at SPS energies, \(t_c \simeq 0.1\) to \(0.8\) fm/\(c\) at RHIC energies, and \(t_c \sim 10^{-2}\) to \(10^{-3}\) fm/\(c\) at LHC energies.
- For Pb+Pb collisions at SPS and RHIC energies, the nucleon-nucleon cross section used was \(\sigma_{\rm NN} = 33\) mb; at LHC energy, \(\sigma_{\rm NN} = 70\) mb.
- The nuclear radius used was \(R_0 = 6.53\) fm, and the Woods-Saxon diffuseness parameter was \(a = 0.545\) fm.
- The total angular momentum of the participant system can reach \(L \approx 10^6 \hbar\) for LHC energies.
- The most significant increase in the total number of participants occurs in the time range \(t \simeq 0.5 t_c\) to \(1 t_c\), while the most significant increase in the total angular momentum of participants occurs in the time interval \(t \simeq 0.75 t_c\) to \(1.25 t_c\).

## Context
This work extends the standard Glauber model (Glauber and Sitenko, 1969; Bialas, Bleszynski, and Czyz, 1976) by introducing a time-dependent description of the spectator-participant partition, which is typically treated only as a final-state distribution. The model is validated by comparison with the UrQMD transport approach (Bass et al., 1998; Bleicher et al., 1999) for Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 17.3\) GeV, showing generally good agreement. The results enable future studies of the time evolution of initial angular momentum and vorticity in the reaction plane, which are relevant for understanding rotation and polarization phenomena in heavy-ion collisions, as previously explored by Becattini (2008), Gao et al. (2008), and Csernai et al. (2013).
