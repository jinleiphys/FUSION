# Observation of resonance trapping in an open microwave cavity

> The coupling of a quantum mechanical system to open decay channels has been theoretically studied in numerous works, mainly in the context of nuclear physics but also in atomic, molecular and mesoscopic physics. Theory predicts that with increasing coupling strength to the channels the resonance widths of all states should first increase but finally decrease again for most of the states. In this letter, the first direct experimental verification of this effect, known as resonance trapping, is presented. In the experiment a microwave Sinai cavity with an attached waveguide with variable slit width was used.

## Key claim

This paper presents the first direct experimental verification of resonance trapping, where the widths of most resonance states first increase with coupling strength to decay channels but then decrease again for most states, as predicted by theory. The authors hedge that this demonstration was possible by tracing the motion of resonance poles as a function of the slit opening, starting close to the real axis and following them into the region of overlapping resonances.

## Method

A microwave Sinai cavity (quarter billiard, 285x200 mm, radius 70 mm) was coupled to an attached waveguide (220x23.2 mm) with a variable slit width opening from 3 to 23.2 mm in steps of 0.2 mm, yielding 102 measurements. The complex reflection coefficient R(E) was measured as a function of energy in the range 2.95 to 6.85 cm^-2 (8.2 to 12.5 GHz), and the energy positions E_R and widths Γ_R of resonances were extracted using a centered time-delay analysis (CTDA) of the Argand diagram, fitting the time-delay function τ^c(E) to a sum of Breit-Wigner contributions.

## Key numbers

- 102 measurements were performed for different openings d from 3 to 23.2 mm in steps of 0.2 mm
- The frequency range of the waveguide was 8.2 to 12.5 GHz, corresponding to energies between 2.95 cm^-2 and 6.85 cm^-2
- Only one mode could propagate through the waveguide between thresholds at 1.83 cm^-2 and 7.33 cm^-2
- The cavity dimensions were 285x200 mm with a radius of 70 mm, and the waveguide was 220x23.2 mm
- At least 46% of the 127 resonance poles (47 resonances with decreasing width plus 11 that stopped increasing) were unambiguously identified as trapped
- At least 58% of all resonances were strongly affected by trapping
- The widths of 47 resonances decreased with d, while 11 resonances stopped increasing within the accuracy of the CTDA

## Context

This work extends theoretical studies of resonance trapping in open quantum systems that have been performed for over a decade using models such as the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian formalism (Moldauer, various groups). It provides the first unambiguous experimental verification of an effect previously studied theoretically in nuclear physics (doorway states), atomic physics, molecular physics, and mesoscopic physics, and which had been calculated for microwave resonators (PepiRose). The experiment enables future studies of controlled mesoscopic systems where tuning the coupling strength to decay channels can control system properties, and opens the door to further experimental and theoretical studies including conductance measurements.
