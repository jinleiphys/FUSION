# Lattice QCD at the physical point: light quark masses

> Ordinary matter is described by six fundamental parameters: three couplings (gravitational, electromagnetic and strong) and three masses: the electron's (m_e) and those of the up (m_u) and down (m_d) quarks. An additional mass enters through quantum fluctuations: the strange quark mass (m_s). The three couplings and m_e are known with an accuracy of better than a few per mil. Despite their importance, $m_u$, $m_d$ (their average m_{ud}) and m_s are relatively poorly known: e.g. the Particle Data Group quotes them with conservative errors close to 25%. Here we determine these quantities with a precision below 2% by performing ab initio lattice quantum chromodynamics (QCD) calculations, in which all systematics are controlled. We use pion and quark masses down to (and even below) their physical values, lattice sizes of up to 6 fm, and five lattice spacings to extrapolate to continuum spacetime. All necessary renormalizations are performed nonperturbatively.

## Key claim
The authors determine the average up/down quark mass ($m_{ud}$) and the strange quark mass ($m_s$) with a precision below 2% using ab initio lattice QCD, but only by fulfilling six specific conditions they argue are necessary for a fully controlled calculation: including all three light quarks in the fermion determinant with an exact algorithm, simulating at or below physical pion masses, using large volumes (up to 6 fm), performing continuum extrapolations from five lattice spacings, applying fully nonperturbative renormalization, and fixing parameters using only $M_\pi$, $M_K$, and $M_\Omega$ as inputs.

## Method
The authors generated $N_f=2+1$ gauge field ensembles using a Symanzik improved gauge action and 2-level HEX smeared clover fermions at five lattice spacings (0.116 to 0.054 fm), with pion masses ranging from approximately 120 to 380 MeV. They computed hadron masses ($M_\pi$, $M_K$, $M_\Omega$) and bare PCAC quark masses, then performed nonperturbative renormalization in the RI scheme at 4 GeV using the Rome-Southampton method with nonperturbative running, followed by combined continuum extrapolation and interpolation to the physical mass point using 288 different analysis variations to control systematic uncertainties.

## Key numbers
- $m_{ud} = 3.503(48)(49)$ MeV in the RI scheme at 4 GeV (first error statistical, second systematic)
- $m_s = 96.4(1.1)(1.5)$ MeV in the RI scheme at 4 GeV
- $m_s/m_{ud} = 27.53(20)(08)$, scheme-independent, with better than 1% precision
- Five lattice spacings used: $a \approx 0.116$, 0.093, 0.077, 0.065, and 0.054 fm
- Difference between finest lattice results and continuum limit is approximately 3%; between coarsest lattice and continuum limit is approximately 10%
- Finite volume corrections below 0.5% for all runs, and at the one per mil level for $M_\pi < 200$ MeV
- Individual quark masses from combining with dispersive information: $m_u = 2.17(04)(10)$ MeV and $m_d = 4.84(07)(12)$ MeV in the RI scheme at 4 GeV

## Context
This work extends the Budapest-Marseille-Wuppertal collaboration's previous lattice QCD calculations (Durr 2008) by reaching physical pion masses and using five lattice spacings, achieving significantly higher precision than prior $N_f=2+1$ results from MILC (Bazavov 2009), HPQCD (Davies 2009, McNeile 2010), and RBC-UKQCD (Aoki 2010), with uncertainties smaller than the world averages from Colangelo (2010) by more than a factor of 5. The results provide precise input for phenomenological calculations requiring light quark masses and demonstrate that lattice QCD can now bracket physical quark mass values, enabling future work on individual up and down quark masses through inclusion of QED effects as in Blum (2010).
