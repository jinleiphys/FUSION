# Resonance Statistics -Informed Fitting Applied to Automated Cross Section Evaluation

> This work investigates the use of resonance statistics for resonance evaluation to inform spin group assignment and an alternative fitting objective function beyond the commonly used chi-squared statistic. Resonance statistics -informed methods are applied to the automated resonance fitting framework, developed by N. Walton et al. In this automated framework, the utility of resonance statistics is largely unexplored. The new resonance statistics -informed spin group shuffling algorithm reduces spin group frequency bias seen in the base fitting algorithm. Although resonance statistics -informed optimization produces negligible changes in pointwise cross section agreement, it significantly improves consistency with Wigner level-spacing statistics and stabilizes the fitted resonance density in the presence of model imperfections.

## Key claim
Resonance statistics-informed fitting produces negligible changes in pointwise cross section agreement compared to standard chi-squared fitting, but significantly improves consistency with Wigner level-spacing statistics and stabilizes fitted resonance density when model imperfections are present.

## Method
The authors embedded a Monte Carlo spin group shuffling algorithm into an existing automated resonance fitting framework (Walton et al.), generating 5 candidate models per elimination step with spin assignments weighted by Porter-Thomas and Wigner likelihoods. They also constructed an alternative objective function that augments the chi-squared statistic with a Bayesian prior based on resonance statistics, using probability densities on reduced width amplitudes (rather than squared widths) to avoid divergent behavior near zero. The method was tested on synthetic and real tantalum-181 data across four progressively augmented configurations: no resonance statistics, statistics applied to variable selection only, to variable and model selection, and to variable selection, model selection, and gradient-descent optimization.

## Key numbers
- For tantalum-181, Monte Carlo spin group shuffling is only applied to models with 9 or fewer resonances within an 11.5 eV window, based on the probability of GOE-generated ladders.
- In the energy window from 200 eV to 220 eV for tantalum-181, there are approximately 5 resonances and 621 data points (over 100 data points per resonance); at 2000 eV, there are only around 25 data points per resonance.
- The external solver for resonance statistics-informed gradient-descent incurs an approximate 50% runtime penalty due to text file-based derivative handling.
- For the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, approximately 99% of random samples from distributions have a KS value less than 1.63 divided by the square root of N, where N is the number of parameters.
- For the Anderson-Darling and Cramér-von Mises tests at 1% significance, thresholds are 1.97 and 0.743 respectively.

## Context
This work extends the automated resonance fitting framework developed by Walton et al., which previously focused on improving goodness of fit while spin group assignment and resonance statistics remained secondary considerations. Prior work by Walton et al. used a rudimentary machine-learning spin group reassignment tool called BRR that was not embedded directly within the fitting routine. The new Monte Carlo spin group shuffling algorithm reduces spin group frequency bias seen in the base fitting algorithm, and the resonance statistics-informed objective function addresses a gap where using resonance statistics for optimization was largely unexplored in resonance evaluation. This enables more reproducible resonance evaluations with improved statistical consistency, particularly important for isotopes like tantalum-181 where data quality is critical for nonproliferation and criticality safety applications.
