# The modified astrophysical S-factor of the ${}^{12}$C+${}^{12}$C fusion reaction at sub-barrier energies

> The $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C fusion reaction plays a crucial role in stellar evolution and explosions. Its open reaction channels mainly include $α$, $p$, $n$, and ${}^{8}$Be. Despite more than a half century of efforts, large discrepancies remain among the experimental data measured using various techniques. In this work, we analyze the existing data using the statistical model. Our calculation shows: 1) the relative systematic uncertainties of the predicted branching ratios get smaller as the predicted ratios increase; 2) the total modified astrophysical S-factors (S$^*$ factors) of the $p$ and $α$ channels can each be obtained by summing the S$^*$ factors of their corresponding ground-state transitions and the characteristic $γ$ rays while taking into account the contributions of the missing channels to the latter. After applying corrections based on branching ratios predicted by the statistical model, an agreement is achieved among the different data sets at ${E}_{cm}>$4 MeV, while some discrepancies remain at lower energies suggesting the need for better measurements in the near future. We find that the recent S$^*$ factor obtained from an indirect measurement is inconsistent with the direct measurement at energies below 2.6 MeV. We recommend upper and lower limits for the ${}^{12}$C+${}^{12}$C S$^*$ factor based on the existing models. A new $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C reaction rate is also recommended.

## Key claim

After applying statistical-model branching-ratio corrections, the different experimental data sets for the $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C fusion S$^*$ factor agree at center-of-mass energies above 4 MeV, but discrepancies persist below that energy; the indirect Trojan Horse Method result is inconsistent with direct measurements below 2.6 MeV, and the paper recommends upper and lower limits for the S$^*$ factor based on existing models rather than a single definitive value.

## Method

The authors analyzed existing experimental data from particle-spectroscopy and characteristic-gamma-ray measurements of the $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C fusion reaction using the statistical model (Hauser-Feshbach formalism) to predict branching ratios for each decay channel of the $^{24}$Mg compound nucleus. They validated the predicted branching ratios against particle-spectroscopy data, then applied these branching ratios to convert the partial S$^*$ factors measured by different experiments into total S$^*$ factors for the proton and alpha channels, accounting for missing channels such as ground-state transitions and unobserved gamma-ray cascades.

## Key numbers

- The S$^*$ factors from particle spectroscopy by Patterson et al. and Becker et al. are about a factor of 2 higher than those from gamma-ray spectroscopy by Kettner et al. at $E_{\rm cm} > 3.6$ MeV, with differences rising to a factor of 4–9 around $E_{\rm cm} = 3$ MeV.
- The S$^*$ factors of Aguilera et al. are about 30% higher than those of Kettner et al., while those of Spillane et al. are 30–60% lower than Kettner et al.
- The mean ratios of the 440 keV gamma-ray line to the sum of all proton channels and the 1634 keV line to the sum of all alpha channels were estimated by Spillane et al. as $0.55 \pm 0.05$ and $0.48 \pm 0.05$, respectively.
- The summed S$^*$ factors of the proton channel from Becker et al., Patterson et al., and Mazarakis et al. agree within $\pm 20\%$ for $E_{\rm cm} > 4$ MeV, but Becker et al. are lower by a factor of up to 10 below $E_{\rm cm} = 3.4$ MeV.
- The neutron channel cross section was measured by Bucher et al. down to $E_{\rm cm} = 3.0$ MeV with a systematic uncertainty of about 40%.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to reconcile discrepancies among $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C fusion data measured over more than half a century using particle spectroscopy (Patterson 1969, Mazarakis and Stephens 1973, Becker 1981, Zickefoose 2018) and gamma-ray spectroscopy (Kettner 1980, Aguilera 2006, Spillane 2007). It directly addresses the controversy raised by the indirect Trojan Horse Method measurement of Tumino (2018), which claimed enhanced S$^*$ factors at low energies, and the critique by Mukhamedzhanov (2019) regarding the plane-wave approximation used in that work. The recommended upper and lower limits and the new reaction rate enable improved modeling of carbon burning in massive stars, type Ia supernova ignition, and superbursts, and highlight the need for better direct measurements below $E_{\rm cm} = 2.6$ MeV.
