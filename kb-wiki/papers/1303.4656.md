# Comment on 'Evidence for Stratification of Deuterium-Tritium Fuel in Inertial Confinement Fusion Implosions'

> Recent implosion experiments performed at the OMEGA laser facility reported by Casey et al.[1], displayed an anomalously low dd proton yield and a high tt neutron yield as compared to dt fusion reactions, explained as a stratification of the fuel in the implosion core. We suggest that in the com- pression stage the fuel is out of equilibrium. Ions are inward accelerated to a velocity v0 independent on the particle type. Yield ratios are simply given by the ratios of fusion cross-sections obtained at the same velocity. A 'Hubble' type model gives also a reasonable description of the data. These considerations might be relevant for implosion experiments at the National Ignition Facility as well.

## Key claim

The anomalously low dd proton yield and high tt neutron yield observed in OMEGA implosions, previously attributed to fuel stratification, can instead be explained by non-equilibrium effects where ions are accelerated to a common velocity \(v_0\) independent of particle type, making yield ratios simply equal to ratios of fusion cross sections at that same velocity rather than requiring modified ion concentrations.

## Method

The authors replace the standard thermal-equilibrium reaction-rate formula (which uses Maxwellian-averaged \(\langle \sigma v \rangle\)) with a delta-function velocity distribution \(\delta(v - v_0)\), assuming all ions are radially accelerated to the same velocity \(v_0\) during compression. They also test a "Hubble-type" implosion model where velocity is proportional to radius (\(v = -\beta r\)) combined with a Gaussian density distribution, which yields an effective temperature that depends on the reduced mass of the reacting pair. Yield ratios from both non-equilibrium models are compared to the OMEGA data and to the thermal-equilibrium predictions of Casey et al. (2012).

## Context

This comment directly challenges the stratification explanation proposed by Casey et al. (2012) for anomalous yield ratios in OMEGA implosions, and also references Amendt et al. for the stratification hypothesis. The work suggests that non-equilibrium velocity distributions during the compression stage, rather than spatial separation of fuel species, could account for the observed yields. The authors note that their considerations may be relevant for implosion experiments at the National Ignition Facility, and that experimental determination of the plasma velocity distribution is needed to distinguish between equilibrium and non-equilibrium interpretations.
