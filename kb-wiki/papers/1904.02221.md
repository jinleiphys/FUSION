# From the Standard Model towards a Theory of Matter: Quarks

> We follow the example of Cabibbo by revising the Standard Model (SM) to present a universal mass structure for fermions. A universal Higgs coupling for each species of fundamental fermions moves the SM towards a Theory of Matter, albeit without correctly describing the observed mass spectrum. It exposes a need for a complete Theory of Matter to include components from physics beyond the Standard Model (BSM). Describing the effect of these components phenomenologically provides a means to infer the nature of some of the BSM physics required. Our results also provide constraints on some BSM matrix elements. Here we apply this concept to quarks; the application to leptons will appear in a separate paper. An immediate benefit for theory is the reduction of the largest fine structure constant for the Higgs coupling to fermions by an order of magnitude, which improves the perturbative appearance of the weak interactions. The small mixing of the third generation of each fermion in the fermion families to the others is attributed to the small BSM perturbations that produce the small mass ratio of the lighter generations to the most massive one.

## Key claim

By imposing a universal Higgs coupling for each charge species of fermions (Higgs Universality), the Standard Model can be moved toward a Theory of Matter, but this universal starting point does not correctly describe the observed mass spectrum; the deviations from universality are attributed to perturbatively small corrections from physics beyond the Standard Model (BSM), which may involve interactions with dark matter.

## Method

The authors start from a "democratic" mass matrix (all entries equal) for each charge sector of quarks, which is diagonalized by the tri-bi-maximal (TBM) matrix. They then introduce a general Hermitian BSM correction matrix proportional to a small parameter epsilon, and diagonalize the combined mass matrix using a unitary transformation that factorizes into a TBM part, a block-diagonalizing part, and a 2x2 rotation. The resulting unitary matrices for up and down quarks are combined to produce the CKM matrix, whose elements are fit to PDG values to constrain the BSM parameters.

## Key numbers

- Up quark masses (in MeV/c^2): 2.3, 1275, 173500 (from PDG)
- Down quark masses (in MeV/c^2): 3.8, 95, 4150 (from PDG)
- Epsilon_u = 7.35 x 10^{-3} (ratio of intermediate up quark mass to top mass)
- Delta_u = 1.8 x 10^{-3} (ratio of up quark mass to charm mass)
- Epsilon_d = 2.29 x 10^{-2} (ratio of strange quark mass to bottom mass)
- Delta_d = 4.0 x 10^{-2} (ratio of down quark mass to strange mass)
- Deviations from universality are less than or equal to 1% in the up quark sector and less than 4% in the down quark sector
- Cabibbo angle Theta_C = 0.2270 (approximately 13.0 degrees)
- sin(theta_13) = 0.00357, sin(theta_23) = 0.04110, sin(theta_12) = 0.22506, sin(chi) = 0.94563 (extracted from PDG parametrization)

## Context

This work extends the concept of "democracy" in mass matrices previously explored by Kaus and Meshkov (1990) and Jarlskog (1980s), and follows the historical example of Cabibbo's universality principle. The authors reinterpret the small deviations from a democratic mass matrix as arising from BSM physics, potentially involving dark matter interactions, rather than requiring substructure or grand unification as attempted by Fritzsch and Planckl (1990s). The approach provides constraints on BSM matrix elements and reduces the largest Higgs fine structure constant by an order of magnitude, improving the perturbative appearance of weak interactions. The analysis of leptons is deferred to a separate paper.
