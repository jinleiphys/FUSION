# On the instanton-induced portion of the nucleon strangeness II: the MIT model beyond the linearized approximation

> If instantons are introduced into the MIT bag model in such a way that bag radii are allowed to vary, the MIT bag interior can accommodate instanton density which is by an order of magnitude larger than in the case when the radii are fixed (although it is still significantly smaller than in the nonperturbative QCD vacuum). The instanton contribution to baryon mass shifts is also correspondingly larger. The instanton-induced part of the scalar strangeness of the nucleon MIT bag is an order of magnitude larger than found previously, within the linearized approximation. The decrease of the model radii (which is associated with the increase of the instanton density) is very favorable from the standpoint of nuclear physics.

## Key claim

By allowing bag radii to vary in the MIT bag model (removing the "linearized approximation" that froze radii), the instanton density inside the bag becomes an order of magnitude larger than previously found, though still significantly smaller than in the nonperturbative QCD vacuum; this leads to a corresponding order-of-magnitude increase in the instanton-induced scalar strangeness of the nucleon.

## Method

The authors perform a full refitting of the MIT bag model parameters (zero-point energy parameter Z₀, strong coupling constant α_c, volume energy density B, and instanton density n) by minimizing a functional that simultaneously enforces agreement with experimental baryon masses and the pressure-balance condition dM_bag/dR = 0 for each baryon. The minimization uses the simplex method, allowing the bag radii of octet and decuplet baryons to vary as free parameters determined by equilibrium, unlike the earlier linearized approximation (Klabucar 1998) which kept radii frozen at the values from DeGrand et al. (1975). The instanton-induced interaction includes one-body and two-body terms from the Shifman-Vainshtein-Zakharov (SVZ) interaction, with the three-body term neglected as it contributes only negligibly to the Λ mass.

## Key numbers

- Instanton density inside the bag in the linearized approximation: n = 0.266 × 10⁻⁴ GeV⁴; in the present work, densities are an order of magnitude larger but still at least a factor of 3 below the nonperturbative QCD vacuum density n₀ ≈ 1.6 × 10⁻³ GeV⁴ = 1 fm⁻⁴
- Strong coupling constant α_c is reduced by 30% compared to the value in DeGrand et al. (1975) for the fit with the highest acceptable instanton density
- The functional F (sum of squared deviations from experimental masses plus pressure-balance penalty) reaches values around 1.2–1.3 × 10⁻³ GeV², with average deviation from experimental baryon masses of 11 MeV; the original MIT fit (DeGrand 1975) gave F_M = 3.2 × 10⁻³ GeV², and the linearized approximation gave F_M = 3.5 × 10⁻³ GeV²
- Fixed input parameters: nonstrange quark masses m_u = m_d = 0, strange quark mass m_s = 279 MeV, quark-antiquark condensate ⟨0|q̄q|0⟩ = −(240 MeV)³, average instanton radius ρ = 1/600 MeV⁻¹ ≈ 1/3 fm
- Empirical total scalar strangeness of the nucleon: ⟨N|s̄s|N⟩ ≈ 2.8

## Context

This work extends the instanton-extended MIT bag model of Klabucar (1998) by removing the linearized approximation that froze baryon radii, and amends the nucleon strangeness results of Klabucar (1998). It addresses a long-standing inconsistency noted by Brown et al. (1990s) between the MIT bag model and nuclear physics: the standard MIT nucleon radius (~1 fm) is too large for the picture of independent nucleons in nuclei. The decrease in bag radii associated with higher instanton densities alleviates this problem. The approach differs from that of Kochelev (1990s) and collaborators, who used a different bag model, and stays as close as possible to the original MIT bag model of DeGrand et al. (1975). The instanton-induced scalar strangeness is enhanced an order of magnitude over the linearized approximation, while the basic bag strangeness from Donoghue and Nappi (1985) still dominates but is reduced by the smaller bag radii.
