# Investigating High-Energy Proton-Induced Reactions on Spherical Nuclei: Implications for the Pre-Equilibrium Exciton Model

> A number of accelerator-based isotope production facilities utilize 100- to 200-MeV proton beams due to the high production rates enabled by high-intensity beam capabilities and the greater diversity of isotope production brought on by the long range of high-energy protons. However, nuclear reaction modeling at these energies can be challenging because of the interplay between different reaction modes and a lack of existing guiding cross section data. A Tri-lab collaboration has been formed among the Lawrence Berkeley, Los Alamos, and Brookhaven National Laboratories to address these complexities by characterizing charged-particle nuclear reactions relevant to the production of established and novel radioisotopes. In the inaugural collaboration experiments, stacked-targets of niobium foils were irradiated at the Brookhaven Linac Isotope Producer (E$_p$=200 MeV) and the Los Alamos Isotope Production Facility (E$_p$=100 MeV) to measure $^{93}$Nb(p,x) cross sections between 50 and 200 MeV. The measured cross-section results were compared with literature data as well as the default calculations of the nuclear model codes TALYS, CoH, EMPIRE, and ALICE. We developed a standardized procedure that determines the reaction model parameters that best reproduce the most prominent reaction channels in a physically justifiable manner. The primary focus of the procedure was to determine the best parametrization for the pre-equilibrium two-component exciton model. This modeling study revealed a trend toward a relative decrease for internal transition rates at intermediate proton energies (E$_p$=20-60 MeV) in the current exciton model as compared to the default values. The results of this work are instrumental for the planning, execution, and analysis essential to isotope production.

## Key claim

The default predictions of the nuclear model codes TALYS, CoH, EMPIRE, and ALICE largely failed to reproduce the measured niobium cross sections, consistently underestimating pre-equilibrium emission; the authors therefore developed a standardized procedure to determine reaction model parameters that best reproduce the most prominent reaction channels, revealing a trend toward a relative decrease for internal transition rates at intermediate proton energies (20–60 MeV) in the current exciton model compared to default values.

## Method

Stacked-target activation experiments were performed at the Brookhaven Linac Isotope Producer (200 MeV) and the Los Alamos Isotope Production Facility (100 MeV) using niobium foils to measure 93Nb(p,x) cross sections between 50 and 200 MeV. The induced activities of 23 residual products were measured via gamma spectroscopy using multiple HPGe detectors across three laboratories, with decay-curve fitting to extract end-of-bombardment activities. A standardized modeling procedure was then developed within the TALYS framework to optimize the two-component exciton model parameters by comparing calculated cross sections to the measured 93Nb(p,x) data and previously published 139La(p,x) cross sections.

## Key numbers

- Cross sections measured for 93Nb(p,x) reactions over the energy range 50–200 MeV, including first measurements of the 93Nb(p,4n)90Mo beam monitor reaction beyond 100 MeV
- LANL irradiation: 7203 seconds with a 100 nA H+ beam at 100.16 MeV (0.1% uncertainty)
- BNL irradiation: 3609 seconds with a 200 nA H+ beam at 200 MeV (0.2% uncertainty)
- The modeling study revealed a trend toward decreased internal transition rates in the exciton model for proton energies between 20 and 60 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Tri-lab collaboration's effort to characterize charged-particle nuclear reactions for isotope production, building on the stacked-target methodology of Voyles (2018) and Morrell (2020). The study addresses the failure of default nuclear model codes (TALYS, CoH, EMPIRE, ALICE) to reproduce pre-equilibrium emission for proton-induced reactions on spherical nuclei, which have been studied less than neutron-induced reactions as noted by Alhassan (2019). The results enable improved planning and analysis for isotope production at high-energy proton facilities and inform the physical basis of the two-component exciton model.
