# Towards a fully self-consistent spectral function of the nucleon in nuclear matter

> We present a calculation of nuclear matter which goes beyond the usual quasi-particle approximation in that it includes part of the off-shell dependence of the self-energy in the self-consistent solution of the single-particle spectrum. The spectral function is separated in contributions for energies above and below the chemical potential. For holes we approximate the spectral function for energies below the chemical potential by a $δ$-function at the quasi-particle peak and retain the standard form for energies above the chemical potential. For particles a similar procedure is followed. The approximated spectral function is consistently used at all levels of the calculation. Results for a model calculation are presented, the main conclusion is that although several observables are affected by the inclusion of the continuum contributions the physical consistency of the model does not improve with the improved self-consistency of the solution method. This in contrast to expectations based on the crucial role of self-consistency in the proofs of conservation laws.

# Towards a fully self-consistent spectral function of the nucleon in nuclear matter

## Key claim

The authors present a calculation of nuclear matter that includes part of the off-shell dependence of the self-energy beyond the quasi-particle approximation, but find that despite affecting several observables, the improved self-consistency does not actually improve the physical consistency of the model, contrary to expectations from conservation law proofs.

## Method

The authors approximate the spectral function separately for particles and holes: for holes, the spectral function below the chemical potential is replaced by a delta-function at the quasi-particle peak while retaining the standard form above the chemical potential, with the reverse procedure for particles. This approximated spectral function is used consistently at all levels of the calculation, including in the T-matrix and self-energy equations, using a non-relativistic version of the Bonn-C potential restricted to the \(^1S_0\) and coupled \(^3S_1\)-\(^3D_1\) partial waves (with the tensor force reduced by a factor 1/2 to remove a bound state).

## Key numbers

- At normal nuclear matter density (\(p_f = 0.27\) GeV/c), the real part of the self-energy in the continuum calculation is approximately 3 MeV less negative than in the quasi-particle approximation deep in the Fermi sea, with the difference reducing to about 2 MeV at the Fermi momentum.
- The imaginary part of the self-energy in the continuum calculation is 1.5 MeV smaller (less negative) than in the quasi-particle approximation for small momenta.
- Binding energies in the continuum method are approximately 1 MeV higher than those found in the quasi-particle approximation.
- For nuclear matter, energies up to about 2 GeV must be included to adequately fulfill the sum-rule.
- Depletions of the momentum distribution are on the order of 6%, requiring typically two iterations for convergence.

## Context

This work extends the T-matrix approximation approach to nuclear matter beyond the quasi-particle approximation, building on prior work by Baym (1962) on conserving approximations and by Ramos et al. (1989) on non-relativistic calculations including off-shell effects. The authors compare their results to the quasi-particle approximation and find that the saturation point shifts to lower densities when continuum contributions are included. The paper enables future investigations into whether fully self-consistent calculations (including the width of the quasi-particle peak) might restore the conservation laws that the present approximation still violates.
