# Mean-field approach in the multi-component gas of interacting particles applied to relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Generalized mean-field approach for thermodynamic description of relativistic single- and multi-component gas in the grand canonical ensemble is formulated. In the framework of the proposed approach different phenomenological excluded-volume procedures are presented and compared to the existing ones. The mean-field approach is then used to effectively include hard-core repulsion in hadron-resonance gas model for description of chemical freeze-out in heavy-ion collisions. We calculate the collision energy dependence of several quantities for different values of hard-core hadron radius and for different excluded-volume procedures such as van der Waals and Carnahan-Starling models. It is shown that a choice of the excluded-volume model becomes important for large particle densities. For large enough values of hadron radii ($r\gtrsim0.9$ fm) there can be a sizable difference between different excluded-volume procedures used to describe the chemical freeze-out in heavy-ion collisions. At the same time, for the smaller and more commonly used values of hard-core hadron radii ($r\lesssim0.5$ fm), the precision of the van der Waals excluded-volume procedure is shown to be sufficient.

## Key claim

The paper formulates a generalized mean-field approach for relativistic single- and multi-component gases that can incorporate various phenomenological excluded-volume procedures in a thermodynamically consistent way. The authors show that for the commonly used hard-core hadron radii (r less than or about 0.5 fm), the simpler van der Waals excluded-volume procedure is sufficiently precise, but for larger radii (r greater than or about 0.9 fm) the choice of excluded-volume model (van der Waals versus Carnahan-Starling) produces sizable differences in the description of chemical freeze-out in heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors derive a self-consistent mean-field theory starting from the density of free energy in the canonical ensemble, then perform a Legendre transform to the grand canonical ensemble where the mean field U(n,T) and excess pressure P_ex(n,T) are defined and related through a thermodynamic consistency condition. They connect this formalism to the virial expansion of the classical gas and generalize it to multi-component systems with conserved charges. The approach is then applied to the hadron-resonance gas model by implementing different excluded-volume procedures (van der Waals and Carnahan-Starling) as specific choices of the mean field and excess pressure, and the collision energy dependence of thermodynamic quantities is calculated for different values of the hard-core hadron radius.

## Key numbers

- For hard-core hadron radii r greater than or about 0.9 fm, there is a sizable difference between different excluded-volume procedures used to describe chemical freeze-out in heavy-ion collisions.
- For hard-core hadron radii r less than or about 0.5 fm, the precision of the van der Waals excluded-volume procedure is shown to be sufficient.
- The effective proper volume per particle is given by tilde_v_0(n) = (1/n) * [1 - exp(-tilde_U(n))], where tilde_U(n) = U(n,T)/T.

## Context

This work extends the generalized mean-field approach previously developed by Anchishkin and collaborators (Anchishkin, 1990s) and connects it to the hadron-resonance gas model used to describe chemical freeze-out in heavy-ion collisions, as employed by Cleymans, Redlich, Becattini, Braun-Munzinger, and others (1990s-2000s). The paper compares the van der Waals and Carnahan-Starling excluded-volume procedures within this framework, building on earlier excluded-volume models by Hagedorn, Cleymans and Suhonen, Rischke, and Uddin and Singh. The approach enables systematic inclusion of different classical equations of state for real gases into the relativistic mean-field description of strongly interacting matter, with potential applications to improving the equation of state used in fluid dynamical models of heavy-ion collisions.
