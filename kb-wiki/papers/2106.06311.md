# Tests of the Porter-Thomas Distribution for Reduced Partial Neutron Widths

> Given N data points drawn from a chi-square distribution, we use Bayesian inference to determine most likely values and N-dependent confidence intervals for the width sigma and the number k of degrees of freedom of that distribution. Using reduced partial neutron widths measured in a number of nuclei, a guessed value of sigma, and a maximum-likelihood approach (different from Bayesian inference), Koehler et al. and Koehler have determined the most likely k-values of chi-square distributions that fit the data. In all cases they find values for k that differ substantially from k = 1 (the value characterizing the Porter-Thomas distribution (PTD) predicted by random-matrix theory). The authors conclude that the validity of the PTD must be rejected with considerable statistical significance. We show that the value of sigma guessed in these papers lies far outside the Bayesian confidence interval for sigma, casting serious doubt on the results of and the conclusions drawn there. We also show that sigma and k must both be determined from the data. Comparison of the results with the Bayesian confidence intervals would then decide on acceptance or rejection of the PTD.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the rejection of the Porter-Thomas distribution (PTD) by Koehler et al. (2010) and Koehler (2011) is invalid because those works used a guessed value for the width parameter sigma that lies far outside the Bayesian confidence interval for sigma, and that both parameters sigma and k must be determined simultaneously from the data before any conclusion about the PTD can be drawn.

## Method

The authors applied Bayesian inference to chi-square distributions with and without a cutoff, deriving the normalized posterior probability distributions (likelihood functions) for the width parameter sigma (for fixed degrees of freedom k) and for k (for fixed sigma). They analytically determined the most likely values and N-dependent confidence intervals for both parameters, using the full chi-square distribution and, for k=2 and k=4, the cutoff distribution, then expanded the likelihood functions around their maxima to estimate the minimum number of data points required for reliable parameter estimation.

## Key numbers

- For the Porter-Thomas distribution (k=1) without cutoff, N must be at least 1800 to determine sigma^2 within a 10% interval (0.9 to 1.1 times the most likely value) with probability 0.99923.
- For N=400 (close to the 411 widths in 194Pt used by Koehler et al. 2010), sigma^2 is found with probability 0.99923 only in the interval 0.785 to 1.214 times the most likely value.
- For k=1, with N=400 the confidence interval for k is |epsilon| less than or equal to 0.11 (i.e., k between 0.89 and 1.11); for N=50 the interval is |epsilon| less than or equal to 0.31.
- For k=2 with cutoff, the most likely value of sigma^2 shifts to (M - y0)/2; for y0 = M/2 this differs by a factor of 1/2 from the value without cutoff.
- For k=4 with cutoff and y0 = M/2, the most likely sigma^2 is M*sqrt(2)/8, differing by a factor of approximately 0.7 from the value without cutoff.

## Context

This work directly challenges the conclusions of Koehler et al. (2010) and Koehler (2011), who claimed rejection of the Porter-Thomas distribution with statistical significance of at least 99.997% for 194Pt and 98.17% for the nuclear data ensemble. The authors show that those analyses used a guessed value of sigma rather than determining it from the data, and that the available data sets (411 widths in 194Pt, 158 in 192Pt) are too small to determine both sigma and k with sufficient accuracy. The paper enables a proper re-analysis of reduced partial neutron width data using Bayesian methods that simultaneously determine both parameters, which would then allow a valid test of the Porter-Thomas distribution predicted by random-matrix theory.
