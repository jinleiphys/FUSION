# Fast-reactor neutron sources in evaluated nuclear data library validation

> Two different neutron sources, based on $^{235}$U-fission neutrons with different average energies, provide integral benchmark data for validation of $γ$-ray production data in the evaluated nuclear data libraries corresponding to fast-neutron-induced inelastic-neutron scattering reactions. Firstly, we consider the IRT-M Research Reactor, formerly located at the Nuclear Research Institute just outside of Baghdad, Iraq, to demonstrate the validation methodology using the associated $γ$-ray data in the Evaluated Nuclear Data File, version VIII.0 (ENDF/B-VIII.0), for several $γ$-ray transitions over a wide range of nuclides including $^{28}$Si, $^{32}$S, $^{56}$Fe, and $^{186}$W. Using the characterized neutron flux of the Baghdad IRT-M Reactor, we find flux-weighted cross-sections deduced using the ENDF/B-VIII.0 $γ$-ray data to be in good agreement with the integral measurements performed at the Baghdad Research Reactor in addition to the corresponding results of different reaction-model calculations, {\tt CoH$_{3}$} and {\tt EMPIRE}. Given the excitation thresholds for the $γ$-ray transitions involved in this investigation, these observations lend further support to the characterization of the IRT-M flux in the fast-neutron energy region $0.862 \leq E_{n} \leq 5.0$ MeV. The additional detail devoted to the IRT-M source reflects the broader scope of the validation work carried out at that facility. A second neutron source considered for this validation work is the Forschungsreaktor M{ü}nich (FRM-II), Garching, Germany. Again, the flux-weighted $γ$-ray data from ENDF/B-VIII.0 for $^{56}$Fe compare well to the integral FRM-II measurement and reaction-model calculations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the neutron flux of the Baghdad IRT-M Research Reactor, previously claimed to be "not well-characterized," is in fact well characterized over the fast-neutron energy region 0.862 to 5.0 MeV and suitable for use as an integral benchmark, based on consistency between flux-weighted cross sections from ENDF/B-VIII.0 and integral measurements for multiple nuclides.

## Method

The authors convolved characterized neutron flux distributions from two 235U-fission-based sources (the Baghdad IRT-M Reactor and the FRM-II reactor in Garching, Germany) with differential gamma-ray production cross sections from the ENDF/B-VIII.0 evaluated library and from reaction-model calculations using the CoH3 and EMPIRE codes. They compared the resulting flux-weighted cross sections to integral gamma-ray production measurements for several gamma-ray transitions in 28Si, 32S, 56Fe, and 186W, using a covariance-based chi-squared minimization procedure to optimize the Baghdad flux parametrization.

## Key numbers

- The Baghdad IRT-M Reactor flux is characterized over the fast-neutron energy region 0.862 ≤ En ≤ 5.0 MeV
- For the 2231-keV 2+1 → 0+gs transition in 32S, the integral measurement from the Baghdad Atlas is 25.0(60) mb, compared to flux-weighted values of 26.57 mb (CoH3), 26.49 mb (EMPIRE), and 21.67 mb (ENDF/B-VIII.0 estimated)
- The ratio ⟨σlev⟩/⟨σtot⟩ for 32S agrees to within approximately 1% across all methods: 0.858 (CoH3), 0.890 (EMPIRE), and 0.878 (ENDF/B-VIII.0)
- For the 847-keV 2+1 → 0+gs transition in 56Fe at FRM-II, the integral measurement is 586(41) mb with angular correction and 715(50) mb without, compared to CoH3 values of 622.5 mb and 759.4 mb respectively
- The FRM-II neutron source has an average energy of 2.3 MeV and a neutron flux of 1.4 × 10^8 cm^-2 s^-1

## Context

This work extends the validation methodology developed by Hurst (2021) for the Baghdad Reactor flux characterization, addressing a claim by Kolos (2022) that the flux was not well-characterized. The paper demonstrates consistency across multiple nuclides (28Si, 32S, 56Fe, 186W) and two different neutron sources, supporting the use of these facilities as integral benchmarks for fast-neutron-induced inelastic-scattering gamma-ray production data. Future work will investigate additional transitions and improve characterization of the FRM-II flux, particularly its low-energy tail.
