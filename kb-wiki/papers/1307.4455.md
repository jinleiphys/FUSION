# Forecasting neutron star temperatures: predictability and variability

> It is now possible to model thermal relaxation of neutron stars after bouts of accretion during which the star is heated out of equilibrium by nuclear reactions in its crust. Major uncertainties in these models can be encapsulated in modest variations of a handful of fudge parameters that change the crustal thermal conductivity, specific heat, and heating rates. Observations of thermal relaxation constrain these fudge parameters and allow us to predict longer term variability in terms of the neutron star core temperature. We demonstrate this explicitly by modeling ongoing thermal relaxation in the neutron star XTE J1701-462. Its future cooling, over the next 5 to 30 years, is strongly constrained and depends mostly on its core temperature, uncertainties in crust physics having essentially been pinned down by fitting to the first three years of observations.

## Key claim

The future cooling of the neutron star XTE J1701-462 over the next 5 to 30 years is strongly constrained and depends mostly on its core temperature, because uncertainties in crust physics have essentially been pinned down by fitting to the first three years of observations.

## Method

The authors used the numerical code NSCool to model the thermal relaxation of the neutron star crust after an accretion outburst, solving the heat diffusion equation with density- and temperature-dependent inputs for specific heat, thermal conductivity, and heating rates. They performed an extensive search of parameter space, varying the impurity parameter, ion effective mass, and neutron superfluid transition temperature, to fit the observed cooling light curve of XTE J1701-462 and then project its future temperature evolution.

## Key numbers

- The accretion outburst of XTE J1701-462 lasted 1.6 years with a high accretion rate close to the Eddington limit, approximately 2 x 10^-8 solar masses per year.
- The observed cooling light curve of XTE J1701-462 shows an initial rapid cooling phase of a few hundred days followed by a two-year-long plateau.
- The thermal time scale of the outermost 200-meter-thick layer at a temperature of about 2 x 10^8 K is roughly 30 to 100 days.
- Models that fit the 12 data points of XTE J1701-462 (up to 1158 days) are separated into four bands according to initial core temperature: A: T0 = 10^8 K; B: T0 = 10^7.75 K; C: T0 = 10^7.5 K; D: T0 between 10^7.25 and 10^6 K.
- For a given core temperature, the uncertainty in future cooling time is smaller than a factor of two.

## Context

This work extends previous modeling of thermal relaxation in the quasi-persistent transients KS 1731-260 and MXB 1659-29 by Shternin (2007), Brown (2009), and Page (2012), which showed that these systems are unique laboratories for studying neutron star crust physics. The authors demonstrate that the same crust physics can describe the vastly different temporal behavior observed in XTE J1701-462, MXB 1659-29, and KS 1731-260, and they predict that continued monitoring of XTE J1701-462 will test the crust relaxation model and provide useful constraints for thermal and transport properties of the neutron star crust.
