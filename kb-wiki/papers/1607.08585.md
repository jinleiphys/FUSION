# Three-nucleon bound states and the Wigner-SU(4) limit

> We examine the extent to which the properties of three-nucleon bound states are well-reproduced in the limit that nuclear forces satisfy Wigner's SU(4) (spin-isospin) symmetry. To do this we compute the charge radii up to next-to-leading order (NLO) in an effective field theory (EFT) that is an expansion in powers of $R/a$, with $R$ the range of the nuclear force and $a$ the nucleon-nucleon ($N\!N$) scattering lengths. In the Wigner-SU(4) limit, the triton and Helium-3 point charge radii are equal. At NLO in the range expansion both are $1.66$ fm. Adding the first-order corrections due to the breaking of Wigner symmetry in the $N\!N$ scattering lengths gives a ${}^3\mathrm{H}$ point charge radius of $1.58$ fm, which is remarkably close to the experimental number, $1.5978\pm0.040$ fm (Angeli and Marinova in At Data Nucl Data Tables 99:69-95, 2013). For the ${}^3\mathrm{He}$ point charge radius we find $1.70$ fm, about 4% away from the experimental value of $1.77527\pm0.0054$ fm (Angeli and Marinova 2013). We also examine the Faddeev components that enter the tri-nucleon wave function and find that an expansion of them in powers of the symmetry-breaking parameter converges rapidly. Wigner's SU(4) symmetry is thus a useful starting point for understanding tri-nucleon bound-state properties.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that Wigner's SU(4) (spin-isospin) symmetry provides a useful starting point for understanding tri-nucleon bound-state properties, even though the nucleon-nucleon system appears far from this limit. The expansion around the Wigner-SU(4) limit converges rapidly, with the triton binding energy changing by only 0.8 MeV due to symmetry breaking and the SU(4)-odd component of the triton wave function being less than 10% the size of the SU(4)-even part.

## Method

The authors use pionless effective field theory (EFT(π̸)) to compute three-nucleon bound-state properties, expanding in powers of R/a where R is the range of the nuclear force and a the nucleon-nucleon scattering lengths. They treat Wigner-SU(4) symmetry breaking perturbatively by expanding the coupled integral equations for the triton vertex functions (Faddeev components) in powers of the symmetry-breaking parameter δ = (γ_t - γ_s)/2, where γ_t and γ_s are the pole momenta in the ³S₁ and ¹S₀ channels respectively. Range corrections are included at next-to-leading order (NLO) in the EFT expansion.

## Key numbers

- In the Wigner-SU(4) limit at NLO in the range expansion, both the triton and Helium-3 point charge radii are 1.66 fm.
- Adding first-order Wigner-symmetry-breaking corrections gives a ³H point charge radius of 1.58 fm, compared to the experimental value of 1.5978 ± 0.040 fm (Angeli and Marinova, 2013).
- For ³He, the point charge radius including first-order symmetry-breaking corrections is 1.70 fm, about 4% away from the experimental value of 1.77527 ± 0.0054 fm (Angeli and Marinova, 2013).
- The triton binding energy in the Wigner-SU(4) limit (δ=0) is 7.62 MeV, compared to the physical value of 8.48 MeV at δ = 26.80 MeV, an 11% difference.
- The LO triton point charge radius in the Wigner-SU(4) limit is 1.22 fm; the NLO value is 1.66 fm; the O(r+δ) value is 1.58 fm.
- The parameter governing Wigner-SU(4) breaking is δ = 26.80 MeV, defined as (1/a_{np}^{S=1} - 1/a_{np}^{S=0})/2.

## Context

This work extends the pionless EFT calculations of Vanasse (2015) for the triton charge radius to include both ³H and ³He in the context of Wigner-SU(4) symmetry. It builds on the observation by Kievsky and Gattobigio (2015) that the triton lies within the "Efimov window" of few-body universality, and on earlier work by Bedaque, Hammer, and van Kolck (1999) showing that the three-nucleon equations reduce to the bosonic case when the ³S₁ and ¹S₀ scattering lengths are equal. The results enable systematic calculations of tri-nucleon properties using an expansion around the Wigner-SU(4) limit, with the isovector combination of charge radii providing a clean test of symmetry-breaking effects.
