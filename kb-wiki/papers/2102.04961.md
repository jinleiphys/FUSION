# Morphology of three-body quantum states from machine learning

> The relative motion of three impenetrable particles on a ring, in our case two identical fermions and one impurity, is isomorphic to a triangular quantum billiard. Depending on the ratio $κ$ of the impurity and fermion masses, the billiards can be integrable or non-integrable (also referred to in the main text as chaotic). To set the stage, we first investigate the energy level distributions of the billiards as a function of $1/κ\in [0,1]$ and find no evidence of integrable cases beyond the limiting values $1/κ=1$ and $1/κ=0$. Then, we use machine learning tools to analyze properties of probability distributions of individual quantum states. We find that convolutional neural networks can correctly classify integrable and non-integrable states.The decisive features of the wave functions are the normalization and a large number of zero elements, corresponding to the existence of a nodal line. The network achieves typical accuracies of 97%, suggesting that machine learning tools can be used to analyze and classify the morphology of probability densities obtained in theory or experiment.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that convolutional neural networks can classify individual quantum states of a triangular billiard as integrable or non-integrable with typical accuracies of 97%, but the classification relies on coarse-grained features of the wave functions rather than detailed quantum properties, and the network's decision is driven primarily by the normalization and the presence of a large number of zero elements (nodal lines) rather than by the underlying classical dynamics.

## Method

The authors map the relative motion of three impenetrable particles on a ring (two identical fermions and one impurity) onto a family of isosceles triangular quantum billiards parametrized by the mass ratio κ = m_I/m. They perform exact diagonalization of the Hamiltonian H_P=0 using a basis of antisymmetrized sine functions with n_max = 130, generating approximately 4000 eigenstates for each mass ratio. They then discretize the probability densities |ψ(z_1,z_2)|^2 as 64×64 pixel images and train a convolutional neural network to classify states as integrable (κ=1 and 1/κ=0) or non-integrable (κ=2 and κ=5), using 1000 states per mass ratio from the 50th to 1050th excited states.

## Key numbers

- The neural network achieves typical accuracies of 97% in classifying integrable versus non-integrable states
- The mass ratio range studied is 1/κ ∈ [0,1], corresponding to impurity heavier than or equal to fermions
- The limiting cases are (90°,45°,45°) triangle for 1/κ=0 and (60°,60°,60°) equilateral triangle for κ=1
- The basis truncation uses n_max = 130, yielding about 4000 states with relative energy accuracy of order 10^{-3}
- The density of states follows ρ^(κ)(E→∞) = (L^2/4π)√(κ/(κ+2))
- The minimal distance between energy levels δ_min scales as 1/√N for large N when averaged over κ = 1.1, 1.2, ..., 5

## Context

This work extends the study of quantum-classical correspondence in integrable and chaotic systems using deep learning, following the approach of Kharkov (2020) who suggested that neural networks can learn the difference between wave functions of integrable and chaotic systems. The triangular billiard is one of the most-studied models in quantum chaology, with prior work by Berry (1984), Li (1985), Kaufman (1999), and Aquiar (2008) establishing its properties. The paper also addresses the question of whether additional integrable cases exist beyond the two limiting mass ratios, as suggested by Li (1995) and McGuire (2001), finding no numerical evidence for such cases. The results enable future use of machine learning tools to analyze and classify probability densities obtained in theory or experiment, particularly for three-body systems in ring geometries.
