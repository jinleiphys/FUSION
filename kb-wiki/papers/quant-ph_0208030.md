# Decay process accelerated by tunneling in its very early stage

> We examine a fast decay process that arises in the transition period between the Gaussian and exponential decay processes in quantum decay systems. It is usually expected that the decay is decelerated by a confinement potential barrier. However, we find a case where the decay in the transition period is accelerated by tunneling through a confinement potential barrier. We show that the acceleration gives rise to an appreciable effect on the time evolution of the nonescape probability of the decay system.

# Decay process accelerated by tunneling in its very early stage

## Key claim

The authors find that, contrary to the usual expectation that a confinement potential barrier always decelerates quantum decay, a sufficiently weak barrier (small height and narrow width) can accelerate the decay speed in the transition period between the Gaussian and exponential decay stages, producing a nonescape probability that falls below that of the barrier-free case while still at the ten-percent level.

## Method

The authors numerically solve the one-dimensional time-dependent Schrödinger equation for a particle initially confined by an infinite wall at x=0 and a repulsive square barrier of height h and width w located at 1 < x < 1+w. The initial wave function is the ground state of the infinite well (√2 sin πx), and the nonescape probability P(4,t) and its logarithmic derivative g(4,t) are computed for various barrier heights and widths using an implicit unitary time-evolution operator.

## Key numbers

- For h=10 and w=0.6, the transition period occurs in 0.3 ≲ t ≲ 2 (in units where ℏ=1 and 2m=1), with the maximum of |g(4,t)| at t ∼ 0.6.
- For a thin barrier with w=0.2 and h=10, the maximum decay speed at t ∼ 1.8 exceeds that for no potential barrier at t ∼ 0.7.
- For w=0.2 and h=10, the nonescape probability for the barrier case becomes smaller than that for no potential barrier at t ∼ 1.5, when the residual nonescape probability is still about ten percent.
- Acceleration by tunneling is remarkable for h ≲ 10 and w ≲ 0.2.
- For h=10 and w=0.2, g(4,t) takes positive values around t ∼ 3.5, indicating a negative probability current.

## Context

This work extends the one-dimensional model analysis of Winter (1961) and the reexamination by Dicus et al. (2002) of a particle tunneling through a delta-function potential barrier, where an irregular fast decay in the transition period was first noted. The authors use a square barrier of finite width to show that the acceleration can be controlled by barrier parameters, and they note that for highly unstable systems the pole analysis that gives the exponential lifetime may not be effective. The results also imply that negative probability currents, previously seen only at very late times after the exponential period, can occur in the initial stage after the Gaussian period.
