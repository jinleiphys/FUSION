# Scaling patterns for azimuthal anisotropy in Pb+Pb collisions at Root_s = 2.76 TeV: Further constraints on transport coefficients

> Azimuthal anisotropy measurements for charged hadrons, characterized by the second order Fourier coefficient $v_2$, are used to investigate the path length ($L$) and transverse momentum ($p_T$) dependent jet quenching patterns of the QCD medium produced in Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$\,TeV. $v_2$ shows a linear decrease as $1/\sqrt{p_T}$ and a linear increase with the medium path length difference ($ΔL$) in- and out of the $Ψ_2$ event plane. These patterns compliment a prior observation of the scaling of jet quenching ($R_{\rm AA}$) measurements. Together, they suggest that radiative parton energy loss is a dominant mechanism for jet suppression, and $v_2$ stems from the difference in the parton propagation length $ΔL$.An estimate of the transport coefficient $\hat{q}$, gives a value comparable to that obtained in a prior study of the scaling properties of $R_{\rm AA}$. These results suggest that high-$p_T$ azimuthal anisotropy measurements provide strong constraints for delineating the mechanism(s) for parton energy loss, as well as for reliable extraction of $\hat{q}$.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the azimuthal anisotropy \(v_2\) of high-\(p_T\) charged hadrons in Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV shows a linear decrease with \(1/\sqrt{p_T}\) and a linear increase with the path length difference \(\Delta L\) between the in-plane and out-of-plane directions, and that these scaling patterns confirm that radiative parton energy loss is the dominant mechanism for jet suppression, with the anisotropy arising from the difference in parton propagation length. The hedge is that this conclusion is drawn from scaling tests that are complementary to prior observations for \(R_{\rm AA}\), and the extracted transport coefficient \(\hat{q}\) is comparable to that obtained from those prior \(R_{\rm AA}\) studies.

## Method

The authors used published high-\(p_T\) \(v_2\) data from the CMS and ATLAS collaborations for charged hadrons with \(p_T \gtrsim 8\) GeV/\(c\) and \(1 < |\eta| < 2\) in Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV. They estimated the path length \(L\) and the path length difference \(\Delta L\) using a Monte-Carlo Glauber-based model, where the transverse size \(\bar{R}\) and eccentricity \(\varepsilon\) were derived from the distribution of participant nucleons, with \(\Delta L \equiv L_y - L_x = \varepsilon (L_x+L_y) \sim \varepsilon \bar{R}\). They then tested the scaling of \(v_2\) with \(1/\sqrt{p_T}\) and \(\Delta L\), and used the anisotropy factor \(R_{v_2}(p_T,\Delta L) = (1-2v_2)/(1+2v_2)\) together with the energy-loss formula of Dokshitzer and Kharzeev to extract an estimate of the transport coefficient \(\hat{q}\).

## Key numbers

*   The average value of the ratio \(S_{p_T}/\Delta L\) (the slope of \(\ln(R_{v_2})\) vs. \(1/\sqrt{p_T}\) divided by the path length difference) is \(3.0 \pm 0.3\) GeV\(^{1/2}\)/fm.
*   The extracted transport coefficient is \(\hat{q}_{\rm LHC} \approx 0.47 \pm 0.09\) GeV\(^2\)/fm, using \(\alpha_s = 0.3\), \(C_F = 9/4\), and \(\mathcal{L} = n = 6.7\).
*   This value is compared to a prior estimate from scaled \(R_{\rm AA}\) data of \(\hat{q}_{\rm LHC} \approx 0.56 \pm 0.05\) GeV\(^2\)/fm, evaluated with the same \(C_F\) and \(\alpha_s\).
*   An extrapolation of the scaling suggests that the anisotropy associated with jet quenching becomes negligible (\(v_2 \sim 0\)) for \(p_T \gtrsim 100\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the scaling analysis of jet quenching (\(R_{\rm AA}\)) performed by Lacey et al. (2009, 2012) to the azimuthal anisotropy \(v_2\), using the same theoretical framework of medium-induced gluon radiation proposed by Dokshitzer and Kharzeev. The observed complementary scaling patterns for \(v_2\) and \(R_{\rm AA}\) provide further constraints on the mechanism of parton energy loss and the extraction of the transport coefficient \(\hat{q}\), suggesting that high-\(p_T\) anisotropy measurements can be used alongside suppression measurements to delineate energy-loss mechanisms.
