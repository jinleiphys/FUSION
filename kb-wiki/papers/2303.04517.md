# Deep learning for flow observables in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We train a deep convolutional neural network to predict hydrodynamic results for flow coefficients, average transverse momenta and charged particle multiplicities in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions from the initial energy density profiles. We show that the neural network can be trained accurately enough so that it can reliably predict the hydrodynamic results for the flow coefficients and, remarkably, also their correlations like normalized symmetric cumulants, mixed harmonic cumulants and flow-transverse-momentum correlations. At the same time the required computational time decreases by several orders of magnitude. To demonstrate the advantage of the significantly reduced computation time, we generate 10M initial energy density profiles from which we predict the flow observables using the neural network, which is trained using 5k, and validated using 90k events per collision energy. We then show that increasing the number of collision events from 90k to 10M can have significant effects on certain statistics-expensive flow correlations, which should be taken into account when using these correlators as constraints in the determination of the QCD matter properties.

## Key claim

A deep convolutional neural network (DenseNet) can predict final-state flow observables and their correlations from initial energy density profiles with sufficient accuracy to replace full hydrodynamic simulations, but only for the specific viscosity parametrization used in the training data; the network cannot generalize to different transport coefficients without retraining.

## Method

The authors trained a DenseNet-BC convolutional neural network on 20,000 events from viscous relativistic hydrodynamic simulations (EKRT initial conditions) across four collision systems (200 GeV Au+Au, 2.76 TeV Pb+Pb, 5.023 TeV Pb+Pb, 5.44 TeV Xe+Xe), with 5,000 events per system. The network takes as input a 269x269 grid of the initial energy density in the transverse plane (0.07 fm resolution) and outputs event-by-event predictions for flow coefficients v_n (n=2-6), average transverse momentum [p_T], and charged particle multiplicity dN_ch/dη. Separate networks were trained for each observable, using mean squared error loss (except mean squared logarithmic error for multiplicity), with heavy data augmentation via random rotations, flips, and translations.

## Key numbers

- Training used 20,000 hydrodynamic events total (5,000 per collision system); validation used 90,000 events per collision energy
- Input grid size: 269x269 with 0.07 fm resolution
- Training time per network: approximately 80 minutes on Nvidia Tesla V100 GPU (32 GB VRAM)
- 10 million events generated using the neural network in approximately 20 GPU hours, compared to an estimated 5 million CPU hours for equivalent full hydrodynamic simulations
- The neural network predicts flow coefficients v_n{2} with near-exact agreement for v_2, with increasing relative errors for higher harmonics (v_5, v_6 show clear deviations in event-by-event comparisons)
- For normalized symmetric cumulants NSC(4,2), the 10M-event neural network prediction shows slight deviations from 90k-event hydrodynamic results only in most central and peripheral collisions
- For nMHC(v_2^2, v_3^6), increasing from 90k to 10M events removes sharp sign changes, yielding a result near zero except in most peripheral collisions

## Context

This work extends the approach of using linear estimators between initial eccentricities and flow coefficients (Gardim 2011, Niemi 2012, Niemi 2015) by replacing simple linear relations with a deep neural network that captures nonlinear hydrodynamic evolution. It builds on the EKRT saturation model (Eskola 1999, Paatelainen 2012) and the hydrodynamic framework of Hirvonen (2022). Prior neural network applications in heavy-ion collisions include impact parameter estimation, jet identification, and short-time hydrodynamic evolution modeling (Huang 2018), but this is the first successful training to predict full flow observables and their correlations from initial-state energy density. The method enables high-statistics (10M event) studies of statistics-expensive correlators like normalized symmetric cumulants and mixed harmonic cumulants, which are needed to match the precision of LHC measurements from ALICE and ATLAS for constraining QCD matter properties.
