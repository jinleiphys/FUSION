# Confronting electron- and neutrino-nucleus scattering

> The analysis of the sample of charged current quasi elastic events collected by the MiniBooNE Collaboration suggests that the scheme successfully employed to describe electron-nucleus scattering fails to explain neutrino-nucleus cross sections. I argue that, due to flux average, the double differential neutrino-nucleus cross section does not allow for a clearcut determination of the dominant reaction mechanism. A systematic study of the large body of electron scattering data may help to identify the processes, other than single nucleon knockout, contributing to the observed neutrino cross section.

# Confronting electron- and neutrino-nucleus scattering

## Key claim

The scheme that successfully describes electron-nucleus scattering fails to explain neutrino-nucleus cross sections, but this discrepancy may be largely due to flux averaging in neutrino experiments rather than a genuine failure of the nuclear model. The double differential neutrino-nucleus cross section, because it is flux-averaged, does not allow a clearcut determination of the dominant reaction mechanism, and contributions from processes other than single nucleon knockout (such as multinucleon knockout via two-body currents) may explain the apparent disagreement without requiring a modified axial mass.

## Method

The author compares inclusive electron-carbon scattering cross sections at fixed beam energy (730 MeV to 1.501 GeV) and scattering angle (37 degrees) with the flux-averaged double differential charged-current quasi-elastic (CCQE) neutrino-carbon cross section measured by MiniBooNE (mean neutrino energy 788 MeV). The same nuclear spectral functions and vector nucleon form factors are used in both calculations, with a dipole axial form factor using the world-average axial mass M_A = 1.03 MeV from deuterium data. The author then demonstrates that the flux integration inherent in neutrino experiments mixes contributions from different kinematic regimes (different Bjorken x values and reaction mechanisms), unlike fixed-energy electron scattering where the quasi-elastic peak is cleanly separated.

## Key numbers

- Mean neutrino energy of the MiniBooNE flux: ⟨E_ν⟩ = 788 MeV
- Electron beam energies used for comparison: 0.730, 1.501 GeV (and intermediate values) at θ_e = 37 degrees
- Axial mass used in calculations: M_A = 1.03 MeV (from world average of deuterium data)
- For the neutrino cross section at T_μ = 0.55 GeV and cos θ = 0.75: x = 1 corresponds to E_ν = 0.788 GeV, x = 0.5 corresponds to E_ν = 0.975 GeV; the MiniBooNE flux values at these energies are within less than 20% of one another
- Multinucleon knockout contribution from ground-state correlations: less than 10% of the integrated spectrum (constrained by semi-inclusive (e,e'p) data)

## Context

This work extends the long-standing approach to electron-nucleus scattering within the Impulse Approximation (IA), as reviewed in the literature (e.g., RMP), and confronts it with the MiniBooNE CCQE data (MiniBooNE Collaboration, 2008). The author argues against the interpretation of Refs. (MiniBooNE, K2K, BOONE) that a larger effective axial mass M_A is needed, and instead supports the view of Martini et al. (2009) and Nieves et al. (2011) that multinucleon knockout via two-body currents (2p2h final states) is the dominant competing mechanism. The analysis of electron scattering data at multiple beam energies (from Refs. 12C_ee and 12C_ee_2) shows that flux averaging mixes contributions from different x regimes, which explains why the NOMAD data at higher neutrino energies can be described without modifying M_A. The paper calls for consistent ab initio models that describe both electron and neutrino scattering across all kinematic regimes using the same Hamiltonian.
