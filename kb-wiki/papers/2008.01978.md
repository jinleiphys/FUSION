# The $\boldsymbol{^{29}}$F nucleus as a lighthouse on the coast of the island of inversion

> The exotic, neutron-rich and weakly-bound isotope $^{29}$F stands out as a waymarker on the southern shore of the island of inversion, a portion of the nuclear chart where the effects of nuclear forces lead to a reshuffling of the single particle levels and to a reorganization of the nuclear structure far from stability. This nucleus has become very popular, as new measurements allow to refine theoretical models. We review the latest developments and suggest how to further assess the structure by proposing predictions on electromagnetic transitions that new experiments of Relativistic Coulomb Excitation should soon become able to measure.

# The $^{29}$F nucleus as a lighthouse on the coast of the island of inversion

## Key claim
The authors propose that the newly measured dominant $\ell=1$ component in $^{28}$F, combined with their adjusted three-body model (scenario D$^\flat$), indicates that $^{29}$F has a ground state dominated by a $(2p_{3/2})^2$ intruder configuration (57.5%), which may signal a moderate halo structure; however, the predicted matter radius increase of $\Delta R = 0.192$ fm is smaller than the value of 0.35 fm reported in a contemporaneous measurement by Bagchi et al. (2020), leaving the extent of the halo structure still under debate.

## Method
The authors adjusted their existing three-body model ($^{27}$F + n + n) for $^{29}$F by tuning the $d$-wave resonance position in the $^{27}$F+n binary subsystem to match the new experimental data on $^{28}$F from Revel et al. (2020), producing an intermediate scenario D$^\flat$. They then computed the ground-state wave function composition, matter radius, and electric dipole ($E1$) strength function into the continuum, and estimated the Relativistic Coulomb Excitation (RCE) cross section using semiclassical theory with the same kinematic parameters as the RIKEN experiment.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state composition of $^{29}$F in scenario D$^\flat$: 57.5% $(2p_{3/2})^2$, 28.1% $(1d_{3/2})^2$, and 6% $(1f_{7/2})^2$.
- Matter radius increase relative to the $^{27}$F core: $\Delta R = 0.192$ fm.
- Integrated $E1$ strength up to 6 MeV: 1.59 e$^2$fm$^2$, out of a total cluster sum rule of 1.92 e$^2$fm$^2$.
- Prominent $E1$ peak at about 0.8 MeV above the three-body threshold, exhausting about 50% of the total strength.
- Estimated total RCE cross section for a $^{29}$F beam at 235 MeV/nucleon on a $^{208}$Pb target: about 600 mb integrated up to 15 MeV, with more than 90% below 5–6 MeV.
- Estimated nuclear contribution to the cross section: approximately 200 mb.
- Forward-peaked angular range for RCE: $\Delta\theta \sim 0.22^\circ$.
- The same calculations using scenario A (standard shell-model order) yield a reduction of the total $B(E1)$ by approximately 40%.

## Context
This work extends the three-body model of Fortunato et al. (2020) for $^{29}$F by incorporating new experimental data on $^{28}$F from Revel et al. (2020), which measured the ground state at 199(6) keV with a dominant $\ell=1$ component (79%) and a resonance at about 0.9 MeV with a strong $\ell=2$ component (72%). The results contradict earlier interpretations by Christian et al. (2016) that $pf$-shell intruder components play only a small role, and instead support the presence of intruder configurations from the $N=3$ shell, consistent with Monte Carlo shell model calculations by Falou et al. (2010). The predictions for $E1$ strength and RCE cross sections are intended to guide future experiments at RIKEN, and the authors note that a contemporaneous measurement by Bagchi et al. (2020) of reaction cross sections on carbon concludes that $^{29}$F is the heaviest Borromean two-neutron halo nucleus to date, with a larger $\Delta R = 0.35$ fm than predicted here.
