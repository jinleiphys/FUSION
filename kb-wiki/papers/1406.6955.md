# Excitation energy dependence of fission in the mercury region

> Background: Recent experiments on beta-delayed fission reported an asymmetric mass yield in the neutron-deficient nucleus 180Hg. Earlier experiments in the mass region A=190-200 close to the beta-stability line, using the (p,f) and (α,f) reactions, observed a more symmetric distribution of fission fragments. While the beta-delayed fission of 180Hg can be associated with relatively low excitation energy, this is not the case for light-ion reactions, which result in warm compound nuclei. Purpose: To elucidate the roles of proton and neutron numbers and excitation energy in determining symmetric and asymmetric fission yields, we compute and analyze the isentropic potential energy surfaces of 174,180,198Hg and 196,210Po. Methods: We use the finite-temperature superfluid nuclear density functional theory, for excitation energies up to E*=30MeV and zero angular momentum. For our theoretical framework, we consider the Skyrme energy density functional SkM* and a density-dependent pairing interaction. Results: For 174,180Hg, we predict fission pathways consistent with asymmetric fission at low excitation energies, with the symmetric fission pathway opening very gradually as excitation energy is increased. For 198Hg and 196Po, we expect the nearly-symmetric fission channel to dominate. 210Po shows a preference for a slightly asymmetric pathway at low energies, and a preference for a symmetric pathway at high energies. Conclusions: Our self-consistent theory suggests that excitation energy weakly affects the fission pattern of the nuclei considered. The transition from the asymmetric fission in the proton-rich nuclei to a more symmetric fission in the heavier isotopes is governed by the shell structure of pre-scission configurations.

## Key claim

The authors predict that excitation energy weakly affects the fission pattern of mercury and polonium isotopes, with asymmetric fission dominating in neutron-deficient isotopes (174,180Hg) up to at least 30 MeV, contrary to macroscopic-microscopic models that predicted symmetric fission at low energies for 174Hg.

## Method

The authors employed finite-temperature superfluid nuclear density functional theory (FT-DFT) with the Skyrme SkM* energy density functional and density-dependent pairing interaction to compute isentropic potential energy surfaces for 174,180,198Hg and 196,210Po at excitation energies up to 30 MeV and zero angular momentum. They constrained the quadrupole moment Q20 and octupole moment Q30 using the augmented Lagrangian method to map the fission pathways, then identified least-energy trajectories by scanning the two-dimensional potential energy surfaces.

## Key numbers

- For 180Hg, the asymmetric split corresponding to 100Ru/80Kr occurs at Q30 ≈ 30 b^(3/2) and Q20 ≈ 300 b
- The beta-delayed fission of 180Hg populates the nucleus with up to E* = 10.44 MeV excitation energy (limited by the electron-capture Q-value of the precursor 180Tl)
- At E* ≈ 30 MeV, the symmetric and asymmetric pathways in 174,180Hg are quite close in energy, with the symmetric pathway presenting a barrier about 1-2 MeV higher than the asymmetric pathway
- For 210Po at E* = 43 MeV, only the symmetric pathway remains, consistent with temperature damping of shell effects
- The macroscopic barriers for 174,180Hg are approximately 12-24 MeV, making the trend with excitation energy very gentle

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent study of Warda (2013) to include thermal effects on fission pathways in the mercury region, addressing the puzzle of asymmetric fission observed in 180Hg by Andreyev (2010) via beta-delayed fission, contrasted with earlier symmetric fission seen in (p,f) and (α,f) reactions by Mulgin (1998) and Itkis (1990, 1991). The results contradict the macroscopic-microscopic model of Moller (2012), which predicted 174Hg would favor symmetric fission at low energies, and the scission-point model of Andreev (2013), which predicted symmetric yields at all excitation energies. The study enables future dynamical finite-temperature calculations of collective inertia and guides ongoing experimental investigations in the neutron-deficient Hg-Pb region.
