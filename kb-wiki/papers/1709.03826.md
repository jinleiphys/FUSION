# Shear viscosity of a hadron gas and influence of resonance lifetimes on relaxation time

> We address a discrepancy between different computations of $η/s$ (shear viscosity over entropy density) of hadronic matter. Substantial deviations of this coefficient are found between transport approaches mainly based on resonance propagation with finite lifetime and other (semi-analytical) approaches with energy-dependent cross-sections, where interactions do not introduce a timescale. We provide an independent extraction of this coefficient by using the newly-developed SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly interacting Hadrons) transport code, which is an example of a mainly resonance-based approach. We compare the results from SMASH with numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation for simple systems using the Chapman-Enskog expansion, as well as previous results in the literature. Our conclusion is that the hadron interaction via resonance formation/decay strongly affects the transport properties of the system, resulting in significant differences in $η/s$ with respect to other approaches where binary collisions dominate. We argue that the relaxation time of the system ---which characterizes the shear viscosity--- is determined by the interplay between the mean-free time and the lifetime of resonances. We show how an artificial shortening of the resonance lifetimes, or the addition of a background elastic cross section nicely interpolate between the two discrepant results.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the discrepancy in computed values of shear viscosity over entropy density (η/s) for hadronic matter arises primarily from whether interactions are modeled via resonance formation with finite lifetimes or via point-like binary collisions; the relaxation time that determines shear viscosity is controlled by the interplay between the mean-free time and the resonance lifetime, not just by cross-section magnitude.

## Method

The authors used the SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly interacting Hadrons) transport code to simulate thermalized hadronic matter in a static box, extracting shear viscosity via the Green-Kubo formalism from the auto-correlation function of off-diagonal energy-momentum tensor components. They compared these results to numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation using the Chapman-Enskog expansion for simple systems, and systematically varied resonance lifetimes and added background elastic cross-sections to isolate the effect of resonance propagation on transport coefficients.

## Key numbers

* Shear viscosity over entropy density η/s for a full hadron gas at zero baryon chemical potential shows a decreasing profile at low temperatures, reaching a plateau around T = 110 MeV and remaining flat until approximately T = 170 MeV.
* The shear viscosity to enthalpy density ratio ηT/w (where w is enthalpy density) displays a difference when going to higher baryon chemical potential, unlike η/s which is relatively independent of μ_B up to approximately 600-650 MeV.
* For a single-species gas with constant isotropic cross-section σ = 20 mb at T = 200 MeV and mass m = 138 MeV, the SMASH Green-Kubo method reproduces analytical Chapman-Enskog results with maximum deviation less than 8%, establishing the systematic error.
* The relaxation time τ in a π-ρ gas reaches a plateau at high temperatures when resonances have finite lifetime, while the mean-free time τ_mft remains unaffected by changes in resonance lifetimes.
* Thermal equilibration times for the full hadron gas range from a couple hundred fm at higher temperatures (T = 150 MeV and higher) to several thousand fm at lower temperatures (T = 100 MeV and lower).

## Context

This work addresses a discrepancy between transport approaches like UrQMD (Demir, 2008) and semi-analytical approaches using energy-dependent cross-sections (Torres-Rincon, 2012), where results differed by up to a factor of ten; the authors also note that Pratt (2016) found considerable differences between UrQMD and the B3D transport approach. The study enables improved extraction of η/s(T, μ_B) for hydrodynamic simulations of heavy-ion collisions and clarifies that resonance lifetimes must be properly accounted for when comparing transport calculations to lattice QCD estimates (Meyer, 2007; Meyer, 2009) or to Bayesian analyses of experimental data (Bernhard, 2016).
