# Causal, stable first-order viscous relativistic hydrodynamics with ideal gas microphysics

> We present the first numerical analysis of causal, stable first-order relativistic hydrodynamics with ideal gas microphysics, based in the formalism developed by Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha, and Kovtun (BDNK theory). The BDNK approach provides definitions for the conserved stress-energy tensor and baryon current, and rigorously proves causality, local well-posedness, strong hyperbolicity, and linear stability (about equilibrium) for the equations of motion, subject to a set of coupled nonlinear inequalities involving the undetermined model coefficients (the choice for which defines the "hydrodynamic frame"). We present a class of hydrodynamic frames derived from the relativistic ideal gas "gamma-law" equation of state which satisfy the BDNK constraints, and explore the properties of the resulting model for a series of (0+1)D and (1+1)D tests in 4D Minkowski spacetime. These tests include a comparison of the dissipation mechanisms in Eckart, BDNK, and Muller-Israel-Stewart theories, as well as investigations of the impact of hydrodynamic frame on the causality and stability properties of Bjorken flow, planar shockwave, and heat flow solutions.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first numerical analysis of causal, stable first-order relativistic hydrodynamics for a non-conformal fluid with ideal gas microphysics, demonstrating that the BDNK formalism can be extended beyond the previously studied conformal case, provided the transport coefficients satisfy a set of coupled nonlinear inequalities derived from the theory.

## Method

The authors derive a class of hydrodynamic frames from the relativistic ideal gas "gamma-law" equation of state, specifying the eight transport coefficients (relaxation times tau_epsilon, tau_P, tau_Q; shear viscosity eta; bulk viscosity zeta; thermal conductivity sigma; and the heat flux coefficients beta_epsilon, beta_n) in terms of a single lengthscale L and dimensionless free parameters. They then solve the BDNK equations of motion in (0+1)D and (1+1)D in 4D Minkowski spacetime for Bjorken flow, planar shockwaves, and heat flow solutions, comparing dissipation mechanisms with Eckart and Muller-Israel-Stewart theories.

## Key numbers

- The adiabatic index Gamma is in the range (1, 2) for the gamma-law equation of state
- The sound speed squared is c_s^2 = Gamma P / rho, where rho = epsilon + P
- The causality constraint requires hat_tau >= ((Gamma - 1)(2 - c_s^2) + c_s^2) / (1 - c_s^2)
- The linear stability constraint requires hat_sigma <= 1/3
- For Bjorken flow tests, Gamma = 4/3, m = 1, hat_V = 1/10, hat_sigma = 0, with hat_tau values of 0.5, 1, and 2
- For shockwave profile tests, Gamma = 4/3, m = 0.1, hat_V = 2/15, hat_sigma = 0, hat_tau = 1.5
- For shock instability tests, Gamma = 4/3, m = 0.1, hat_V = 4/3, hat_sigma = 0, with hat_tau values of 1.5 and 3
- For acausal instability tests, Gamma = 4/3, m = 0.1, hat_V = 4/3, hat_sigma = 0, with hat_tau values of 0.25, 0.4, 0.5, and 1.5
- For heat flow tests, Gamma = 4/3, m = 0.1, hat_V = 2/15, hat_tau = 1.5, with hat_sigma values of 0 and 1/3
- For telegraph equation tests, Gamma = 4/3, m = 0.1, hat_V = 2/15, with hat_sigma values of 0.15, 1.5, 7.5 and hat_tau values of 1.5, 15, 75

## Context

This work extends the BDNK formalism beyond the conformal fluid model studied previously by Pandya (2021) and Pandya (2022), which assumed zero chemical potential and no bulk viscosity or heat conduction. The authors derive transport coefficients from the relativistic ideal gas microphysics, enabling the study of bulk viscosity and thermal conductivity effects absent in conformal models. The results enable future investigations of dissipative relativistic hydrodynamics for systems such as binary neutron star mergers and cosmological models, where non-conformal equations of state are essential. The paper compares its dissipation mechanism with Eckart (1940) theory and Muller-Israel-Stewart theory, and uses the BDNK constraints to study where and why pathologies arise when violating the theoretical bounds.
