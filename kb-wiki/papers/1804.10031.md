# Equilibrium of nuclear matter in QCD sum rules

> We calculate the nucleon parameters in symmetric nuclear matter employing the QCD sum rules approach. We focus on the average energy per nucleon and study the equilibrium states of the matter. We treat the matter as a relativistic system of interacting nucleons. Assuming the dependence of the nucleon mass on the light quark mass $m_q$ to be more important than that of nucleon interactions we find that the contribution of the relativistic nucleons to the scalar quark condensate can be expressed as that caused by free nucleons at rest multiplied by the density dependent factor $F(ρ)$. We demonstrate that there are no equilibrium states while we include only the condensates with dimension $d \leq 3$. There are equilibrium states if we include the lowest order radiative corrections and the condensates with $d \leq 4$. They manifest themselves for the nucleon sigma term $σ_N >60$ MeV. Including the condensates with $ d \leq 6$ we find equilibrium states of nuclear matter for $σ_N> 41$ MeV. In all cases the equilibrium states are due to influence of the relativistic motion of the nucleons composing the matter on the scalar quark condensate.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that equilibrium states of symmetric nuclear matter can be obtained from QCD sum rules only when condensates up to dimension \(d \leq 6\) are included, and only for nucleon sigma term values above a threshold (\(\sigma_N > 41\) MeV); the equilibrium arises specifically from the relativistic motion of nucleons modifying the scalar quark condensate, not from the nonrelativistic approximation.

## Method

The authors employed finite-density QCD sum rules to compute the nucleon effective mass \(m^*\) and vector self-energy \(\Sigma_V\) in symmetric nuclear matter, treating the matter as a relativistic system of interacting nucleons moving in self-consistent scalar and vector fields. They included the operator product expansion (OPE) terms up to dimension \(d=6\), incorporating the lowest-order radiative corrections (\(\alpha_s\) corrections beyond the logarithmic approximation) and contributions from the gluon condensate and nonlocality of the vector condensate. The average binding energy per nucleon \(\mathcal{B}(\rho)\) was then calculated from \(m^*\) and \(\Sigma_V\), and the condition \(d\mathcal{B}/d\rho = 0\) was checked for equilibrium.

## Key numbers

- Equilibrium states appear for nucleon sigma term \(\sigma_N > 60\) MeV when including condensates with \(d \leq 4\) and lowest-order radiative corrections.
- Equilibrium states appear for \(\sigma_N > 41\) MeV when including condensates with \(d \leq 6\).
- For \(\sigma_N = 64.5\) MeV, the equilibrium density \(\rho_{eq} = 0.95 \rho_0\) (with \(\rho_0 = 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\)) and the minimum binding energy \(\mathcal{B}_{min} = -15.8\) MeV.
- For \(\sigma_N = 65\) MeV, \(\rho_{eq} = 0.99 \rho_0\) and \(\mathcal{B}_{min} = -17.8\) MeV; at this point the scalar self-energy \(\Sigma_S = -418\) MeV and the vector self-energy \(\Sigma_V = 334\) MeV.
- The vacuum nucleon mass used is \(m = 928\) MeV, with vacuum residue \(\lambda_0^2 = 2.36\) GeV\(^6\) and vacuum continuum threshold \(W_0^2 = 2.13\) GeV\(^2\).
- The conventional value of the nucleon sigma term is \(\sigma_N = (45 \pm 8)\) MeV, but values up to \(\sigma_N = (66 \pm 6)\) MeV are considered.

## Context

This work extends the finite-density QCD sum rules approach previously developed by the same group (Drukarev et al., earlier papers) and others, treating nuclear matter as a relativistic system rather than a gas of free noninteracting nucleons. It contrasts with the Walecka model (mean-field scalar-vector model), finding similar self-energy values at equilibrium (\(\Sigma_V \approx 323\) MeV, \(\Sigma_S \approx -413\) MeV in the Walecka model). The results enable future studies of nuclear matter saturation using QCD sum rules, showing that equilibrium emerges only when relativistic corrections to the scalar condensate (via the density-dependent factor \(F(\rho)\)) are included, and that the sigma term must exceed a threshold that depends on the OPE truncation.
