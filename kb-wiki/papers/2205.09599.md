# Feasibility of studying astrophysically important charged-particle emission with the variable energy $γ$-ray system at the Extreme Light Infrastructure -- Nuclear Physics facility

> In the environment of a hot plasma, as achieved in stellar explosions, capture and photodisintegration reactions proceeding on excited states in the nucleus can considerably contribute to the astrophysical reaction rate. Such reaction rates including the excited-state contribution are obtained from theoretical calculations as the direct experimental determination of these astrophysical rates is currently unfeasible. In the present study, ($γ$,p) and ($γ$,$α$) reactions in the mass and energy range relevant to the astrophysical $p$ process are considered and the feasibility of measuring them with the ELISSA detector system at the future Variable Energy $γ$-ray (VEGA) facility at ELI-NP is investigated. The simulation results reveal that, for the ($γ$,p) reaction on twelve targets of $^{29}$Si, $^{56}$Fe, $^{74}$Se, $^{84}$Sr, $^{91}$Zr, $^{96,98}$Ru, $^{102}$Pd, $^{106}$Cd, and $^{115, 117, 119}$Sn, and the ($γ$,$α$) reaction on five targets of $^{50}$V, $^{87}$Sr, $^{123,125}$Te, and $^{149}$Sm, the yields of the reaction channels with the transitions to the excited states in the residual nucleus are relevant and even dominant. It is further found that for each considered reaction, the total yields of the charged-particle $X$ may be dominantly contributed from one, two or three ($γ$,$X_{i}$) channels within a specific, narrow energy range of the incident $γ$-beam. Furthermore, the energy spectra of the ($γ$,$X_{i}$) channels with $0\leq i\leq 10$ are simulated for each considered reaction, with the incident $γ$-beam energies in the respective energy range as derived before. It becomes evident that measurements of the photon-induced reactions with charged-particle emissions considered in this work are feasible with the VEGA+ELISSA system and will provide knowledge useful for nuclear astrophysics.

## Key claim

The paper claims that measuring photon-induced charged-particle emission to excited final states with the VEGA+ELISSA system at ELI-NP is feasible, but this feasibility is conditional on selecting specific narrow energy ranges of the incident gamma beam where the total charged-particle yield is dominated by only one, two, or three reaction channels, allowing the dominant channel yields to be approximated from the total measured yield within acceptable uncertainty.

## Method

The authors used the Hauser-Feshbach reaction model implemented in TALYS 1.9 to calculate cross sections for (gamma,p) and (gamma,alpha) reactions on 34 target nuclides, considering transitions to the ground state and first ten excited states in the residual nucleus. They then performed GEANT4 simulations of the VEGA gamma-ray source and ELISSA silicon strip detector system to estimate yields and determine the minimum gamma-beam energies required to achieve a detectable limit of 100 counts per day for total proton or alpha-particle yields.

## Key numbers

- The VEGA system delivers gamma-ray energies up to 19.5 MeV with a spectral density of 5x10^3 photons/s/eV and a bandwidth of 0.5%
- The most probable photon energy for the astrophysical gamma process is in the range of 260-390 keV, corresponding to plasma temperatures of 1.5-3.5 GK
- The relevant energy window for astrophysical contributions is 2-4 MeV for protons and 5-10 MeV for alpha-particles
- The highest (gamma,p_i) cross section for Fe-56, Ru-96, and Pd-102 reaches about 1 mb, while that for Sn-119 is only a few times 10^-3 mb
- The ELISSA detector system provides angular coverage of 20 to 160 degrees in the laboratory system, with energy resolution better than 1% and spatial resolution up to 1 mm
- The detectable limit used in the study is 100 counts per day for total proton or alpha-particle yields

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to constrain astrophysical reaction rates for the gamma process, which produces proton-rich p-nuclides in stellar explosions. The authors build on the formalism of Rauscher (2012) showing that excited-state contributions dominate photodisintegration rates, and on the suggestion by Rauscher (2013) that photodisintegration experiments can probe particle emission to excited final states. The study specifically addresses the limitations identified in earlier work by Lan (2018) regarding the sensitivity of calculated cross sections to nuclear potentials, and proposes measurements at the VEGA facility that could provide data to test optical model potentials and improve theoretical predictions of astrophysical capture rates.
