# Isoscalar g Factors of Even-Even and Odd-Odd Nuclei

> We consider T=0 states in even-even and odd-odd N=Z nuclei. The g factors that emerge are isoscalar. We find that the single j shell model gives simple expressions for these g factors which for even-even nuclei are suprisingly close to the collective values for K=0 bands. The g factors of many 2+ in even-even nuclei and 1+ and 3+ states in odd-odd nuclei have g factors close to 0.5.

# Isoscalar g Factors of Even-Even and Odd-Odd Nuclei

## Key claim

The measured isoscalar g factors of T=0 states in N=Z nuclei (both even-even and odd-odd) are close to 0.5, but this proximity cannot be taken as unambiguous evidence for collective rotational structure because the single-j shell model also predicts g ≈ 0.5 for large l, and both the LS and jj coupling limits yield similar values near 0.5 for heavier nuclei.

## Method

The authors compile experimental g factors from the literature for 2+ states in even-even N=Z nuclei (20Ne through 44Ti) and for 1+, 3+, and 5+ states in odd-odd N=Z nuclei (2H through 58Cu). They derive analytic expressions for isoscalar g factors in the single-j shell model for N=Z nuclei, showing that the second term in the general g-factor formula vanishes due to charge symmetry, leaving g = (g_j(p) + g_j(n))/2. They also perform a fit of isoscalar magnetic moments for five mirror-nuclei pairs using an effective operator with parameters δg_l, δg_s, and a [Y^2 σ]^1 term.

## Context

This work extends earlier discussions by Talmi (1971) on isoscalar magnetic moments and by Arima (1978) on the collective g = 0.5 value for 2+ states in light N=Z nuclei. The authors compile more recent experimental data including measurements by Speidel and Koller groups (2003-2006) and Stone (2008) for 58Cu. The paper clarifies that the single-j shell model of deShalit and Talmi (1963) and McCullen et al. (1964) also predicts g ≈ 0.5 for N=Z nuclei, so the proximity to 0.5 is not uniquely diagnostic of collective rotation. The analysis of mirror-nuclei pairs using effective operators follows the approach of Zamick (1977) and connects to renormalization calculations by Mavromatis et al. (1966), Towner (1987), and Shimizu et al. (1974). The results enable more careful interpretation of future g-factor measurements in N=Z nuclei, particularly for heavier odd-odd systems where the LS limit alone cannot explain the observed values.
