# Chiral Symmetry and Finite Pion Mean Field in Nuclei

> We introduce the chiral symmetry in the description of finite nuclei with the hope to unify the hadron and nuclear physics. This trial becomes possible by introducing finite pion mean field, which contributes largely to enhance the spin-orbit splitting so as to provide the j-j magic number. We discuss the qualitative consequence of the finite pion mean field for various observables.

## Key claim
The paper argues that introducing a finite pion mean field in finite nuclei, within a chiral sigma model framework, can enhance spin-orbit splitting to produce the j-j magic numbers, but this is a qualitative trial that requires parity and charge projection for quantitative results; the authors note the pionic phase transition is "not so strong" and needs special care.

## Method
The authors construct an extended chiral sigma model (linear sigma model with omega meson) and derive its non-linear representation. They apply the mean-field approximation to infinite nuclear matter, adjusting the sigma meson mass and omega-nucleon coupling to reproduce saturation properties, then calculate finite N=Z even-even nuclei (neutron number 16 to 34) without and with a finite pion mean field, using parity-mixed single-particle states with definite total spin.

## Key numbers
- In nuclear matter, the incompressibility from the extended chiral sigma model is K = 650 MeV, compared to the expected K ~ 300 MeV.
- The nucleon mass is set to M = 939 MeV, omega meson mass m_omega = 783 MeV, pion mass m_pi = 139 MeV, and pion decay constant f_pi = 93 MeV.
- The coupling constants are fixed as g_sigma = M/f_pi = 10.1 and tilde-g_omega = m_omega/f_pi = 8.42.
- For finite nuclei calculations, g_omega is set to 7.176 (instead of 7.033) for overall agreement with RMF(TM1) results.
- The extended chiral sigma model without pion mean field produces a magic number at N = Z = 18 instead of N = Z = 20; inclusion of the pion mean field provides the magic number at N = Z = 28.
- The nucleon mass is reduced by about 20% in finite nuclei with the chiral sigma model.

## Context
This work extends the authors' preliminary study of finite pion mean fields in nuclei (Toki 2002) and contrasts with the RMF(TM1) model (Sugahara 1994), which produces magic numbers at N=20 and 28 via large vector and scalar potentials. The paper connects to ab initio variational calculations of light nuclei (Pieper 2001) showing pion matrix elements are 70-80% of the total attraction, and to Kaiser (2002) who found large attraction from 2p-2h configurations due to pion exchange. The approach aims to unify hadron and nuclear physics through chiral symmetry, enabling future quantitative calculations of Gamow-Teller distributions, missing single-particle strength, and magnetic moments.
