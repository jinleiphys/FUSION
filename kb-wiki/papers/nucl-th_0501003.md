# Analysis of Low-Momentum Correlations with Cartesian Harmonics

> Exploiting final-state interactions and/or identity interference, analysis of anisotropic correlations of particles at low-relative velocities yields information on the anisotropy of emission sources in heavy-ion reactions. We show that the use of cartesian surface-spherical harmonics in such analysis allows for a systematic expansion of the correlations in terms of real angular-moment coefficients dependent on relative momentum. The coefficients are directly related to the analogous coefficients for emission sources. We illustrate the analysis with an example of correlations generated by classical Coulomb interaction.

## Key claim

The paper shows that using real-valued cartesian surface-spherical harmonics, rather than complex tesseral spherical harmonics, allows a systematic expansion of anisotropic two-particle correlation functions in terms of real angular-moment coefficients that are directly related to analogous coefficients for the emission source, with the caveat that the relation holds only when the kernel depends on the rank ℓ and not on the azimuthal index m.

## Method

The authors derive the mathematical formalism for expanding correlation functions and source functions in cartesian surface-spherical harmonics, showing that the expansion coefficients satisfy the same integral equation as the tesseral harmonic coefficients (Eq. 4). They illustrate the method using a Gaussian source model with anisotropic widths (6 fm in beam direction, 4 fm transverse) displaced 5 fm from the origin, and compute correlation functions using the classical limit of repulsive Coulomb interactions appropriate for intermediate mass fragments. They then demonstrate imaging of the source coefficients from the correlation coefficients using a basis of rectangle functions and χ² minimization.

## Context

This work extends the tesseral spherical harmonic expansion approach of Brown (1997) for analyzing anisotropic correlation functions in heavy-ion reactions. The cartesian harmonic formalism provides a more transparent connection between geometric features of the real source function and the expansion coefficients, avoiding the complexity of complex-valued projections. The method enables systematic quantification of source anisotropies up to arbitrary rank ℓ, with reflection symmetries manifesting explicitly, and can be applied to extract source shapes from correlation measurements using imaging techniques previously developed by Brown (2000), Panitkin (2001), and Verde (2001, 2003).
