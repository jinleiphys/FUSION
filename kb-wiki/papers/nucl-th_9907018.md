# Nonlinear waves of nuclear density

> Nonlinear excitations of nuclear density are considered in the framework of semiclassical nonlinear nuclear hydrodynamics. Possible types of stationary nonlinear waves in nuclear media are analysed using Nonlinear Schroedinger equation of fifth order and classified using a simple mechanical picture. It is shown that a rich spectrum of nonlinear oscillations in one-dimensional nuclear medium exist.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a rich spectrum of nonlinear stationary waves (including soliton-like solutions, periodic waves, and shock-like fronts) exists in one-dimensional nuclear matter, but this result is derived under the restrictive assumptions of irrotational flow, spinless and isospinless nucleons, and no Coulomb or spin-orbit effects.

## Method

Starting from a semiclassical nuclear hydrodynamics framework with Skyrme interactions, the authors derived a fifth-order nonlinear Schrödinger equation (NoSE3-5) for the nuclear density. They reduced the one-dimensional stationary problem to an ordinary differential equation and classified possible solutions using a mechanical analogy where the wavefunction amplitude is treated as a particle moving in a potential well defined by the parameter λ (chemical potential). Solutions were obtained both numerically and analytically for special cases.

## Key numbers

- The dimensionless nonlinear Schrödinger equation has the form: iψ_τ = -Δψ - 4|ψ|²ψ + 3|ψ|⁴ψ
- Soliton-like solutions exist for chemical potential values 0 < λ < 1
- The soliton solution has analytical form ψ(x) = (λ/(1 + (1-λ)^(1/2) cosh(2λ^(1/2) r)))^(1/2)
- The "diffuseness" parameter is D = 1/(2√λ)
- The "radius" parameter is R = (1/(2√λ)) arctanh(√λ)
- The particle number N = arctanh(√λ)
- Finite amplitude motion is possible for λ < 4/3; for λ > 4/3 any finite amplitude motion is impossible
- The potential V(ψ) = -λ/2 ψ² + ψ⁴ - 1/2 ψ⁶ vanishes at ψ = 0, ±√(1-√(1-λ)), ±√(1+√(1-λ))

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous nonlinear hydrodynamical approach (Kartavenko 1993, Kartavenko et al. 1996) based on currents and density algebra, which itself is an extension of the Bohr-Mottelson collective model (Eisenberg and Greiner). The approach enables classification of large-amplitude collective motions beyond traditional perturbation theory near equilibrium, and could describe cluster preformation on nuclear surfaces relevant to alpha decay and cluster radioactivity (Sandulescu and Greiner 1989). The one-dimensional analysis provides a foundation for future work on three-dimensional nonlinear waves and coupling between irrotational and rotational modes.
