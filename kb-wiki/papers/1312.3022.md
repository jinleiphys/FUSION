# Possible generation of anomalously soft quark excitations at nonzero temperature: Nonhyperbolic dispersion of parapion and van Hove singularity

> We study the quark spectrum at finite temperature near and above the pseudocritical temperature of the chiral phase transition incorporating the effects of the collective modes with the quantum number of the sigma (parasigma) and pion (parapion) in a chiral effective model with a nonzero current quark mass. Below the pion zero-binding temperature where the pionic modes are bound, the quark self-energy has van Hove singularity induced by the scattering of quarks with the composite bound pions with a nonhyperbolic dispersion curve. This singularity is found to cause a drastic change in the quark spectrum from that in the mean field picture near the pseudocritical temperature: The quark spectrum has an unexpected sharp peak at an energy considerably lower than the constituent quark mass, while the spectrum approaches the mean field one at high temperatures. We clarify that the emergence of this anomalous structure of the quark spectral function originates from the composite nature of the pionic modes with a non-Lorentz invariant dispersion relation in the medium at finite temperature.

## Key claim

The authors show that, contrary to expectations from earlier work with elementary particles, a nonzero current quark mass does *not* suppress the anomalous low-energy peak in the quark spectral function near the pseudocritical temperature. Instead, the composite nature of pionic modes with a nonhyperbolic dispersion relation at finite temperature produces a van Hove singularity in the quark self-energy, which generates a sharp peak at an energy considerably lower than the constituent quark mass.

## Method

The authors employ a two-flavor Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with a nonzero current quark mass (5.5 MeV) and parameters fitted to vacuum pion properties. They calculate the quark self-energy at zero momentum in the random phase approximation, incorporating the retarded propagators of the sigma and pionic modes obtained from the one-loop quark-antiquark polarization functions. The quark spectral function is then evaluated from the retarded propagator, with the self-energy decomposed into contributions from the sigma mode, the bound pionic pole, and the pionic continuum.

## Key numbers

- Constituent quark mass in vacuum: 337 MeV.
- Pseudocritical temperature defined by half the vacuum chiral condensate: approximately 192 MeV.
- Pseudocritical temperature defined by maximum dynamic chiral susceptibility at momentum 10 MeV: approximately 206 MeV.
- Pseudocritical temperature defined by maximum static chiral susceptibility: approximately 211 MeV.
- Pion zero-binding temperature T_ZB: 216 MeV.
- At T = 206 MeV, the quark spectral function has a sharp peak at p0 approximately 25 MeV, with spectral weight Z approximately 0.16.
- At T = 206 MeV, the constituent quark mass in the mean-field approximation is approximately 120 MeV, and the rest mass of the pionic mode is approximately 150 MeV.
- The van Hove singularities in the imaginary part of the self-energy occur at p0 approximately 47 MeV and p0 approximately 52 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Kitazawa, Kunihiro, and Nemoto (2006) from the chiral limit to the case of nonzero current quark mass, where the constituent quark mass remains nonzero at all temperatures. It contrasts with earlier Yukawa model studies by Kitazawa, Kunihiro, and Nemoto (2007) and Kitazawa, Kunihiro, and Nemoto (unpublished), which assumed elementary bosons with hyperbolic (relativistic) dispersion relations and found that a nonzero fermion mass suppresses the low-energy peak. The present results show that the composite nature of pionic modes and their nonhyperbolic dispersion, previously discussed by Shuryak (1990) and Pisarski (1996), produce van Hove singularities that generate a sharp low-energy peak even with a nonzero quark mass. This mechanism may enable new understanding of quark properties in the hot medium near the chiral crossover temperature.
