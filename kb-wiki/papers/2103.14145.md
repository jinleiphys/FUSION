# Memory effects in Langevin approach to the nuclear fission process

> We present the schematic calculations within the Langevin approach in order to investigate the dependence of fission width on the memory time and the excitation energy at low temperatures where the quantum fluctuations play an important role. For this we consider the simple one-dimensional case with the potential energy given by two parabolic potentials (Kramers potential). For friction and the mass parameters we use the deformation independent values fitted to the results obtained earlier within the microscopic linear response theory. We have found out that at small excitation energies (comparable with the fission barrier height) the memory effects in the friction and random force acts on the fission width in opposite direction. The total effect is not so large, but still quite noticeable (depending on the value of the relaxation time). The use of effective temperature in the diffusion coefficient turns out to be much more important compared with the memory effects. The calculated fission width at very low excitation energies is unrealistically too big.

## Key claim

At low excitation energies (comparable to the fission barrier height), memory effects in the friction and random force act on the fission width in opposite directions, nearly canceling each other; the total effect is noticeable but depends strongly on the damping parameter and relaxation time, while the use of an effective temperature in the diffusion coefficient produces a much larger (and likely unrealistic) enhancement of the fission width.

## Method

The authors solved one-dimensional non-Markovian Langevin equations with a two-parabolic (Kramers) potential, using deformation-independent mass and friction parameters fitted to results from microscopic linear response theory. They introduced memory effects via an exponentially decaying friction kernel and an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck noise process, converting the integro-differential equations into a set of Markovian equations for numerical integration. The fission width was extracted from the exponential decay of the survival probability inside the potential well, and results were compared to Kramers' high- and low-viscosity limits and their finite-relaxation-time generalizations.

## Key numbers

- Barrier height fixed at V_b = 6 MeV, close to the fission barrier of actinide nuclei.
- Harmonic oscillator frequency set to hbar * omega_0 = 1 MeV, close to collective vibrations calculated for Th-224.
- Friction coefficient gamma/M = 0.6 (T^2 + hbar^2 omega_0^2 / pi^2) / (1 + T^2/40) (T in MeV).
- Relaxation times used: tau = 0, tau = 5e-22 sec, and tau = 1e-21 sec.
- Damping parameter eta varied from 0.075 to 0.3 for the realistic friction model; turnover point in eta shifts from about 0.1 (tau=0) to about 0.5 (tau=1e-21 sec).
- At E* = 4 MeV (eta = 0.075), some dependence of Gamma_f on tau is seen; at higher E* the dependence is very weak.
- Effective temperature T* = (hbar omega_0 / 2) coth(hbar omega_0 / 2T) increases the fission width by up to two orders of magnitude at low excitation energies (e.g., at E* = V_b/2, T ~ 0.335 MeV, the width differs by two orders of magnitude compared to using T).
- Critical temperature for negative stiffness: T_c = hbar omega_b / pi.

## Context

This work extends earlier Langevin-based fission studies (Nix 1976, Wada 1993, Froebrich, Pomorski 1996, Aritomo, Mazurek, Sierk, and others) by explicitly incorporating memory effects at low excitation energies where quantum fluctuations are important. It addresses contradictory prior claims about the importance of non-Markovian effects: some studies (Korash, Aichelin 2004, Washiyama) found substantial influence, while others (Froebrich) found very small effects. The results show that memory effects in friction and random force nearly cancel, but the use of effective temperature (Hofmann and Kiderlen) produces a huge enhancement that the authors consider unrealistic, motivating future work with fully microscopic transport coefficients from linear response theory.
