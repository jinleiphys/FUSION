# Neutrinos and the Supernova Origin of the Elements

> Intense fluxes of neutrinos are emitted by the hot neutron star produced in a supernova. The electron neutrino and antineutrino capture reactions on neutrons and protons, respectively, provide heating to drive a wind from the hot neutron star. The same reactions also determine the neutron-richness of the wind material. Nucleosynthesis via rapid neutron capture, the r-process, may occur in the wind material as it expands away from the neutron star. The neutron-richness of the wind material, and hence, the r-process nucleosynthesis therein, are sensitive to mixing between the muon (or tauon) neutrino/antineutrino and the electron (or sterile) neutrino/antineutrino. Indirect arguments and direct tests for the supernova origin of the r-process elements are discussed with a goal to establish supernova r-process nucleosynthesis as an important probe for neutrino mixing.

## Key claim

The neutron-richness of the neutrino-driven wind material, and hence the occurrence of r-process nucleosynthesis in supernovae, is sensitive to mixing between muon/tauon neutrinos and electron (or sterile) neutrinos at the level of sin^2 2θ ≲ 10^{-4} for δm^2 ≳ 1 eV^2; this sensitivity can be used as a probe for neutrino mixing only if the supernova origin of the r-process elements is first proven.

## Method

The paper reviews the role of supernova neutrinos in driving a wind from the hot neutron star and in setting the neutron-to-proton ratio of that wind via ν_e + n → p + e^- and \barν_e + p → n + e^+ capture reactions. It then examines how matter-enhanced mixing between ν_{μ(τ)} and ν_e (or sterile neutrinos) would alter the electron fraction of the wind, and compares the mixing parameters incompatible with a neutron-rich wind to those reported by the LSND experiment. Indirect arguments for the supernova origin of r-process elements are drawn from observed europium abundances in metal-poor stars and Galactic chemical evolution calculations, and two direct tests (gamma-ray detection from r-process progenitor nuclei and abundance anomalies in binary companions) are outlined.

## Key numbers

* Average supernova neutrino energies: ⟨E_{ν_e}⟩ ≈ 11 MeV, ⟨E_{\barν_e}⟩ ≈ 16 MeV, ⟨E_{ν_{μ(τ)}}⟩ ≈ ⟨E_{\barν_{μ(τ)}}⟩ ≈ 25 MeV.
* Mixing parameters incompatible with supernova r-process nucleosynthesis: sin^2 2θ ∼ 3×10^{-3}–10^{-2} at δm^2 ≳ several eV^2 (LSND region).
* Sensitivity of supernova r-process nucleosynthesis to neutrino mixing: sin^2 2θ ≲ 10^{-4} for δm^2 ≳ 1 eV^2.
* Estimated Galactic supernova rate: ∼ (30 yr)^{-1}; neutron star merger rate: ∼ (3×10^4 yr)^{-1}.
* Eu abundance from a single supernova: log ε_SN(Eu) ∼ −2.5; from a single neutron star merger: log ε_NSM(Eu) ∼ 0.5.
* Expected gamma-ray flux from r-process progenitor nuclei in a supernova at 10 kpc or from the Vela SNR: ≳ 10^{-7} γ cm^{-2} s^{-1}.
* Sensitivity of Compton Gamma-Ray Observatory: ∼ 10^{-5} γ cm^{-2} s^{-1}; INTEGRAL: ∼ 10^{-6} γ cm^{-2} s^{-1}.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of neutrino-driven winds from supernovae (Duncan 1992, Qian & Woosley 1996) and the role of neutrino oscillations in setting the wind's neutron-richness (Qian et al. 1993). It directly addresses the LSND experiment's reported mixing parameters and shows that only those at low δm^2 are compatible with r-process nucleosynthesis, while higher δm^2 values would require sterile neutrinos (Caldwell 1999). The indirect arguments for supernovae over neutron star mergers as the r-process site build on abundance observations in metal-poor stars (Gratton 1997, McWilliam 1995, Sneden 1996, 1998) and Galactic chemical evolution models (Qian & Wasserburg 2000). The proposed direct tests, gamma-ray detection from r-process progenitor nuclei and abundance anomalies in binary companions, provide observational pathways to confirm the supernova origin, which would then enable using supernova r-process nucleosynthesis as a probe for neutrino mixing.
