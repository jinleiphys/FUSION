# Nuclear Excitation by Muon Capture

> Efficient excitation of nuclei via exchange of a real or virtual photon has a fundamental importance for nuclear science and technology development. Here, we present a new mechanism of nuclear excitation based on the capture of a free muon into the atomic orbits (NE$μ$C). The cross section of such a new process is evaluated using the Feshbach projection operator formalism and compared to other known excitation phenomena, i.e. photo-excitation and nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC), showing up to ten orders of magnitude increase in cross section. NE$μ$C is particularly interesting for MeV excitations that become accessible thanks to the stronger binding of muons to the nucleus. The binding energies of muonic atoms have been calculated introducing a state of the art modification to the Flexible Atomic Code (FAC). An analysis of an experimental scenarios in the context of modern muon production facilities shows that the effect can be detectable for selected isotopes. The total probability of NE$μ$C is predicted to be $ P \approx 10^{-6}$ per incident muon in a beam-based scenario. Given the high transition energy provided by muons, NE$μ$C can have important consequences for isomer feeding and particle-induced fission.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new nuclear excitation mechanism, nuclear excitation by muon capture (NEμC), claiming cross sections up to ten orders of magnitude larger than the analogous electronic process (NEEC) for certain transitions, but the actual probability per incident muon in a beam-based scenario is predicted to be only about \( P \approx 10^{-6} \), making detection feasible only for selected isotopes and with sufficiently high muon fluxes.

## Method

The authors use the Feshbach projection operator formalism, previously developed for NEEC by Pálffy, to calculate NEμC resonance strengths and cross sections for electric dipole (E1) and quadrupole (E2) transitions. Muonic binding energies for K and L shells are computed using a modified version of the Flexible Atomic Code (FAC) that solves the Dirac equation including finite nuclear size, vacuum polarization, and self-energy corrections. The resonance condition requires the nuclear transition energy to exceed the muon binding energy, with the free muon energy matching the difference.

## Key numbers

- NEμC integrated cross section (resonance strength) for \(^{68}\)Se (E2, capture in \(2p_{3/2}\)): \( S = 1.82 \times 10^{5} \) b·eV, the highest value reported
- NEμC resonance strength for \(^{48}\)Cr (E2, capture in \(2p_{3/2}\)): \( S = 3.58 \times 10^{3} \) b·eV
- NEμC resonance strength for \(^{93}\)Mo (E2, capture in \(2p_{3/2}\)): \( S = 1.27 \times 10^{4} \) b·eV
- NEμC resonance strength for \(^{73}\)Ge (E2, capture in \(2p_{1/2}\)): \( S = 9.85 \times 10^{3} \) b·eV
- NEμC resonance strength for \(^{207}\)Pb (E1, capture in \(2p_{1/2}\)): \( S = 447.50 \) b·eV
- NEμC resonance strength for \(^{138}\)Ba (E1, capture in \(1s_{1/2}\)): \( S = 146 \) b·eV
- Total probability per incident muon for \(^{73}\)Ge (capture in \(2p_{3/2}\) only): \( P = 1.54 \times 10^{-6} \)
- Total probability per incident muon for \(^{93}\)Mo (capture in \(2p_{3/2}\) only): \( P = 1.39 \times 10^{-6} \)
- Total probability per incident muon for \(^{68}\)Se (single channel): \( P = 1.02 \times 10^{-5} \)
- NEμC-induced fission probability for \(^{238}\)U: \( \sim 4.30 \times 10^{-5} \) per incident muon
- Comparison with NEEC: NEμC resonance strengths are 5 to 10 orders of magnitude larger than corresponding NEEC values (e.g., for \(^{68}\)Se, NEEC \( S = 1.44 \times 10^{-5} \) b·eV vs. NEμC \( S = 1.82 \times 10^{5} \) b·eV)
- Accuracy of FAC muonic binding energies: standard deviation of differences with reference calculations ranges from 0.36 keV for \(^{40}\)Zr to 0.87 keV for \(^{209}\)Bi

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC), developed by Pálffy (2006), to the muonic case, proposing a mechanism that avoids the stringent requirement of high ionization states needed for NEEC. The predicted NEμC cross sections are orders of magnitude larger than those for NEEC (Wu, 2019) and, for quadrupole transitions, substantially larger than direct photoexcitation. The process could enable isomer feeding (e.g., in \(^{113}\)In and \(^{87}\)Sr) and muon-induced fission, particularly for lighter isotopes where muon cascade excitation becomes non-resonant. The authors note that the disagreement between NEEC theory and experiment (nine orders of magnitude discrepancy reported by Wu, 2019) might be resolved by measuring NEμC rates.
