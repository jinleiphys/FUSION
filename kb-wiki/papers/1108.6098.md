# Deformation and shell effects in nuclear mass formulas

> We analyze the ability of the three different Liquid Drop Mass (LDM) formulas to describe nuclear masses for nuclei in various deformation regions. Separating the 2149 measured nuclear species in eight sets with similar quadrupole deformations, we show that the masses of prolate deformed nuclei are better described than those of spherical ones. In fact, the prolate deformed nuclei are fitted with an RMS smaller than 750 keV, while for spherical and semi-magic species the RMS is always larger than 2000 keV. These results are found to be independent of pairing. The macroscopic sector of the Duflo-Zuker (DZ) mass model reproduces shell effects, while most of the deformation dependence is lost and the RMS is larger than in any LDM. Adding to the LDM the microscopically motivated DZ master terms introduces the shell effects, allowing for a significant reduction in the RMS of the fit but still exhibiting a better description of prolate deformed nuclei. The inclusion of shell effects following the Interacting Boson Model's ideas produces similar results.

## Key claim

The authors show that Liquid Drop Mass (LDM) formulas describe the masses of prolate deformed nuclei significantly better than those of spherical or semi-magic nuclei, with RMS values below 750 keV for prolate deformed nuclei versus above 2000 keV for spherical and semi-magic species, contrary to the common expectation that LDM formulas are best suited for spherical nuclei.

## Method

The authors separated 2149 measured nuclear species into eight groups based on their quadrupole deformation (seven deformation ranges plus one semi-magic group) and performed independent least-squares fits for three different LDM formulas (LDM1, LDM2, LDM3) to each group. They then repeated the analysis using the macroscopic sector of the Duflo-Zuker (DZ) mass model and two hybrid models that add shell corrections (either DZ master terms or valence-nucleon terms) to the LDM1 formula.

## Key numbers

- RMS for prolate deformed nuclei (group 7) fitted with LDM3: 629 keV; for spherical nuclei (group 3) with LDM3: 2151 keV
- RMS for all 2149 nuclei with LDM1: 2387 keV; with LDM2: 2374 keV; with LDM3: 2422 keV
- RMS for semi-magic nuclei with LDM1: 2113 keV; with LDM2: 2056 keV; with LDM3: 1967 keV
- Global RMS for the hybrid LDM+DZ model (LDM1 plus DZ shell corrections): 1407 keV
- Global RMS for the hybrid LDM+val model (LDM1 plus valence-nucleon terms): 1075 keV
- RMS for the macroscopic DZ1 model alone: 2852 keV; for DZ2 model alone: 3443 keV

## Context

This work extends a preliminary study by the same authors (Hirsch et al., 2011) and challenges the conventional view that LDM formulas describe spherical nuclei best, showing instead that deformed nuclei are better fitted. The analysis connects to the Duflo-Zuker mass model (Duflo, 1994; Zuker, 1994; Duflo, 1995), which gives an RMS of 373 keV for all known masses, and to Interacting Boson Model (IBM) F-spin approaches (Dieperink and Van Isacker, 2007; 2009) that introduce shell effects through valence-nucleon counting. The results enable future work relating shell-effect parameters to nuclear deformation in a more systematic way.
