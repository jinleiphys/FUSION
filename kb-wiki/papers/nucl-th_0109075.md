# Probing Mechanical and Chemical Instabilities in Neutron-Rich Matter

> The isospin-dependence of mechanical and chemical instabilities is investigated within a thermal and nuclear transport model using a Skyrme-type phenomenological equation of state for neutron-rich matter. Respective roles of the nuclear mean field and the 2-body stochastic scattering on the evolution of density and isospin fluctuations in either mechanically or chemically unstable regions of neutron-rich matter are investigated. It is found that the mean field dominates overwhelmingly the fast growth of both fluctuations, while the 2-body scattering influences significantly the later growth of the isospin fluctuation only. The magnitude of both fluctuations decreases with the increasing isospin asymmetry because of the larger reduction of the attractive isoscalar mean field by the stronger repuslive neutron symmetry potential in the more neutron-rich matter. Moreover, it is shown that the isospin fractionation happens later, but grows faster in the more neutron-rich matter. Implications of these results to current experiments exploring properties of neutron-rich matter are discussed.

# Probing Mechanical and Chemical Instabilities in Neutron-Rich Matter

## Key claim

The isospin-dependence of mechanical and chemical instabilities in neutron-rich matter is investigated using a Skyrme-type phenomenological equation of state, with the finding that the nuclear mean field dominates the fast growth of both density and isospin fluctuations, while 2-body stochastic scattering only significantly influences the later growth of isospin fluctuations. The magnitude of both fluctuations decreases with increasing isospin asymmetry, meaning that more neutron-rich matter is actually more stable against the growth of these fluctuations, contrary to what one might expect from the lower binding energy of such systems.

## Method

The authors used a thermal model based on a Skyrme-type phenomenological equation of state with a parabolic approximation for the energy per nucleon in asymmetric nuclear matter, parameterized with a symmetry energy term S(ρ)=S₀(ρ₀)·u^γ where γ is a free parameter. They then employed the isospin-dependent transport model IBUU (Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck) with periodic boundary conditions in a cubic box, using 10⁴ test particles per nucleon in 1 fm³ cells, to simulate the dynamical evolution of density and isospin fluctuations in systems initialized in mechanically or chemically unstable regions of the phase diagram.

## Key numbers

- The symmetry energy at saturation density is S₀(ρ₀)=30 MeV, with γ values of 0.5 and 1 corresponding to K_sym values of -68 MeV and 0 MeV, respectively
- The critical temperature for mechanical instability decreases with increasing isospin asymmetry; for γ=0.5, the critical temperature is shown as a function of δ in Figure 5
- Chemical instability occurs over an envelope of pressures between 0.005 MeV/fm³ and 0.8 MeV/fm³ at T=5 MeV with γ=0.5
- Systems were initialized at T_i=5 MeV, ρ_i=0.05 fm⁻³ with isospin asymmetries δ_i=0.2, 0.6, and 0.9 for studying fluctuation growth
- The Pauli blocking rate increases from about 35% for δ_i=0.2 to about 50% for δ_i=0.9
- With δ_i=0.2 each nucleon makes about 4 scatterings up to 100 fm/c, while with δ_i=0.9 each nucleon makes only about 1 collision; the number of nuclear scatterings in matter with δ_i=0.9 is about 1/6 of that with δ_i=0.2
- The neutron-neutron cross section is only about 1/3 of that for neutron-proton collisions

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of mechanical instabilities in isospin-symmetric systems to the isospin-asymmetric case, and builds upon the theoretical framework for chemical (diffusive) instabilities established by Lattimer (1978), Barz (1980), Müller, and Likó (1997), as well as the transport model approach of Baran (1998, 2000). The results are consistent with those based on more microscopic many-body theories and with the linear response analysis of Baran (1998). The findings have implications for understanding multifragmentation mechanisms in reactions induced by neutron-rich nuclei at intermediate energies, for the structure and stability of neutron stars, and for the production of exotic beams via projectile fragmentation. The work enables predictions about the time scales of multifragmentation and isospin fractionation in neutron-rich nuclear reactions that can be tested experimentally through measurements of neutron-neutron, proton-proton, and fragment-fragment correlation functions.
