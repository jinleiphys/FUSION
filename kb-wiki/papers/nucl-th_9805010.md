# On the origin of the radial flow in low energy heavy ion reactions

> The average transverse energy of nucleons and intermediate mass fragments observed in the heavy ion reaction Xe(50A MeV)+Sn shows the same linear increase as a function of their mass as observed in heavy ion collisions up to the highest ene rgies available today and fits well into the systematics. At higher energies this observation has been interpreted as a sign of a strong radial flow in an otherwise thermalized system. Investigating the reaction with Quantum Molecular dynamics simulations we find in between 50A MeV and 200A MeV a change in the reaction mechanism. At 50A MeV the apparent radial flow is merely caused by an in-plane flow and Coulomb repulsion. The average transverse fragment energy does not change in the course of the reaction and is equal to the initial fragment energy due to the Fermi motion. At 200A MeV, there are two kinds of fragments: those formed from spectator matte r and those from the center of the reaction. There the transverse energy is caused by the pr essure from the compressed nuclear matter. In both cases we observe a binary event stru cture, even in central collisions. This demonstrates as well the non thermal character of the reaction. The actual process which leads to multifragmentation is rather complex and is discussed in detail.

# On the origin of the radial flow in low energy heavy ion reactions

## Key claim

The linear increase of average transverse energy with fragment mass observed in Xe(50A MeV)+Sn reactions is not evidence of genuine radial flow from a thermalized compressed system, but is instead caused by a combination of in-plane flow, Coulomb repulsion, and the initial Fermi motion of nucleons; the apparent radial flow at this energy is "not real" and the system remains non-thermal and semi-transparent even in central collisions.

## Method

The authors performed Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) simulations of the reaction Xe+Sn at beam energies of 50A MeV and 200A MeV, using Gaussian wave packets for nucleons with a constant width (4L = 4.33 fm²) and a density-dependent potential V = -124(ρ/ρ₀) + 70.5(ρ/ρ₀)² MeV. After 240 fm/c when the fragment distribution stabilizes, a Coulomb trajectory program was applied to account for Coulomb repulsion. The time evolution of density, transverse momentum, and fragment energy was tracked, and the motion of nucleons finally emitted as intermediate mass fragments (IMFs) was analyzed in the mean-field potential by projecting relative density and potential gradients onto the x-z plane.

## Key numbers

- At 50A MeV, the average transverse fragment energy initially is about 14 MeV and independent of fragment size, consistent with the Goldhaber formula for fast fragment formation from Fermi motion.
- The maximum density in the center of the reaction for Xe(50A MeV)+Sn at b=3 fm is reached at approximately 50 fm/c, with expansion to a low-density non-interacting phase by 120 fm/c.
- At 200A MeV, the radial flow velocity in units of the speed of light is in the range 0.05 ≤ β_r/c ≤ 0.2, depending on event selection.
- For the reaction at 50A MeV, the in-plane flow is negative (as in deep inelastic collisions), while at 200A MeV the in-plane flow becomes positive (bounce-off type).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of radial flow systematics previously observed across beam energies from 50A MeV to 200A GeV, where the linear increase of transverse energy with fragment mass was interpreted as evidence for strong radial flow in a thermalized system (Poghosyan et al., Marini et al., Reisdorf et al.). The authors use QMD simulations (Aichelin 1991) to show that at 50A MeV the apparent radial flow is an artifact of initial Fermi motion and Coulomb effects, contradicting the thermal interpretation. At 200A MeV, the mechanism changes toward a participant-spectator picture with genuine compression-driven radial flow for small fragments. The results enable future investigation of how the nuclear equation of state influences radial flow at higher energies (Harman et al., forthcoming).
