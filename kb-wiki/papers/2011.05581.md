# The subtle connection between shape coexistence and quantum phase transition. The Zr case

> Background: Zr region is characterized by very rapid changes in the ground state structure of the nuclei. In particular, the onset of deformation when passing from $^{98}$Zr to $^{100}$Zr is one of the fastest ever observed in the nuclear chart. It has been probed both experimental and theoretically that certain low-lying excited states of Zr isotopes own different shapes than the ground state. Purpose: We intend to disentangle the interplay between the sudden changes in the ground state shape, i.e., the existence of a quantum phase transition, and the presence in the spectra of coexisting states with very different deformation, i.e., the presence of shape coexistence. Method: We rely on a previous calculation using the Interacting Boson Model with Configuration Mixing (IBM-CM) which reproduces in detail the spectroscopic properties of $^{96-110}$Zr. This IBM-CM calculation allows to compute mean-field energy surfaces, wave functions and any other observable related with the presence of shape coexistence or with a quantum phase transition. Results: We obtain energy surfaces and the equilibrium value of the deformation parameter $β$, the U(5) decomposition of the wave functions and the density of states. Conclusions: We confirm that Zr is a clear example of quantum phase transition that originates from the crossing of two configurations with a very different degree of deformation. Moreover, we observe how the intruder configuration exhibits its own evolution which resembles a quantum phase transition too.

## Key claim

The paper confirms that the Zr isotopic chain exhibits a quantum phase transition (QPT) originating from the crossing of two configurations with very different deformation, and that the intruder configuration itself shows an evolution resembling its own QPT; however, the correspondence with the X(5) critical point symmetry is not crystal-clear, with only certain ratios agreeing while key ratios like E(0⁺₂,₃)/E(2⁺₁) in ¹⁰⁰Zr are too low.

## Method

The authors used the Interacting Boson Model with Configuration Mixing (IBM-CM) to compute mean-field energy surfaces, equilibrium deformation parameters β, U(5) decomposition of wave functions, and density of states for ⁹⁶–¹¹⁰Zr. The Hamiltonian includes a regular [N] boson space and an intruder [N+2] boson space (representing 2p-2h proton excitations across Z=40), with mixing terms between them. Parameters were obtained from a least-squares fit to experimental energy levels and B(E2) transition probabilities as detailed in García-Ramos and Heyde (2019).

## Key numbers

- The onset of deformation occurs between ⁹⁸Zr and ¹⁰⁰Zr, described as one of the fastest ever observed in the nuclear chart.
- For ¹⁰⁰Zr, the ratio E(4⁺₁)/E(2⁺₁) = 2.66 (X(5) prediction: 2.91); E(6⁺₁)/E(2⁺₁) = 5.00 (X(5): 5.45); E(0⁺₂,₃)/E(2⁺₁) = 3.91 (X(5): 5.67).
- For ¹⁰²Zr, the ratio E(4⁺₁)/E(2⁺₁) = 3.16 (X(5): 2.91); E(6⁺₁)/E(2⁺₁) = 6.39 (X(5): 5.45); E(0⁺₂,₃)/E(2⁺₁) = 5.92 (X(5): 5.67).
- The ρ²(E0:0⁺₂→0⁺₁) value for ¹⁰⁰Zr is one of the largest observed over the full nuclear mass table, indicating large mixing between regular and intruder configurations.
- The two-neutron separation energy and nuclear radii show a sudden increase at A=100, consistent with a first-order QPT.

## Context

This work extends previous IBM-CM calculations for Zr isotopes (García-Ramos and Heyde, 2018, 2019) and connects the rapid structural changes in the Zr region (also studied in Sr isotopes by Ansari et al., 2017 and Régis et al., 2017) to the broader phenomena of shape coexistence and QPT. The paper clarifies the distinction between shape coexistence (where two configurations coexist over a wide parameter range) and a first-order QPT (where two minima coexist only near the critical point), using the Zr chain as a case study. It enables future work to disentangle whether the observed rapid changes are driven by configuration crossing or by an intrinsic QPT within a single configuration, and suggests that 4p-4h excitations may need to be included for heavier Zr isotopes.
