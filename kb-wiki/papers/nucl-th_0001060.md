# Deeply bound levels in kaonic atoms

> Using a microscopic antikaon-nucleus optical potential recently developed by Ramos and Oset (nucl-th/9906016, in print in Nuclear Physics A) from a chiral model, we calculate strong interaction shifts and widths for $K^-$ atoms. This purely theoretical potential gives an acceptable description of the measured data ($χ^2/{\rm num.data}= 3.8$), though it turns out to be less attractive than what can be inferred from the existing kaon atomic data. We also use a modified potential, obtained by adding to the latter theoretical one a s-wave term which is fitted to known experimental kaonic data ($χ^2/{\rm degree of freedom}= 1.6$), to predict deeply bound $K^-$ atomic levels, not detected yet. This improved potential predicts, in general, states even narrower than those recently reported by Friedman and Gal. (Phys.Let.B459, 1999, 43). This reinforces the idea that these deeply atomic states can be detected and resolved by using suitable nuclear reactions. Besides, we also study $K^-$ and $\bar K^0$ nuclear bound states and compute binding energies and widths, for both species of antikaons, in $^{12}$C, $^{40}$Ca and $^{208}$Pb. Despite of restricting our study only to potentials obtained from best fits to the known kaonic atom data, the dynamics of these nuclear bound states depend dramatically on the particular optical potential used.

## Key claim

Using a modified optical potential that adds a fitted s-wave term to the microscopic chiral-model potential of Ramos and Oset (2000), the authors predict deeply bound kaonic atomic states that are generally narrower than those reported by Friedman and Gal (1999), reinforcing the possibility that these states can be detected and resolved via suitable nuclear reactions; however, the dynamics of nuclear bound states depends dramatically on which optical potential is used, even when all potentials are fitted to the same kaonic atom data.

## Method

The authors solved the Klein-Gordon equation with electromagnetic and strong optical potentials for K- atoms across the periodic table, using four different optical potentials: the purely theoretical self-consistent potential from Ramos and Oset (2000) (V_opt^(1)), that potential plus a fitted s-wave term linear in density (V_opt^(1m)), a simple t-rho phenomenological potential (V_opt^(2)), and a density-dependent phenomenological potential (V_opt^(2DD)). The fits to the 63 experimental energy shifts and widths were performed using chi-squared minimization, and the resulting potentials were then used to predict binding energies and widths of deeply bound atomic and nuclear states in 12C, 40Ca, and 208Pb.

## Key numbers

- The purely theoretical potential V_opt^(1) gives chi^2/num.data = 3.8 for 63 kaonic atom data points
- The modified potential V_opt^(1m) gives chi^2/dof = 1.6, with fitted parameter delta b_0 = (0.078 +/- 0.009) + i (-0.25 +/- 0.01) fm
- The simple t-rho potential V_opt^(2) gives chi^2/dof = 2.15, with b_0 = (0.52 +/- 0.03) + i (0.80 +/- 0.03) fm
- The density-dependent potential V_opt^(2DD) gives chi^2/dof = 1.83, with B_0 = (1.62 +/- 0.04) + i (-0.028 +/- 0.009) fm and alpha = 0.273 +/- 0.018
- For the 1s state in 208Pb, V_opt^(1m) predicts binding energy B = -7.20 MeV and width Gamma = 1.42 MeV; V_opt^(2) gives Gamma = 1.75 MeV; V_opt^(2DD) gives Gamma = 1.60 MeV
- For the 1s state in 12C, V_opt^(1m) predicts B = -0.274 MeV and Gamma = 0.036 MeV; V_opt^(2) gives Gamma = 0.064 MeV
- The ratio of the fitted correction to the phenomenological potential shows Re(delta b_0)/Re(b_0) = 0.15 and Im(delta b_0)/Im(b_0) = -0.32

## Context

This work extends the microscopic chiral-model approach of Ramos and Oset (2000) by quantifying its deficiencies against kaonic atom data and constructing an improved hybrid potential. It confirms and extends the predictions of Friedman and Gal (1999) that deeply bound kaonic atomic states are narrow enough to be resolved, with the new potential predicting even narrower widths (20-40% smaller than those from V_opt^(2)). The results enable experimental searches for these states using nuclear reactions, as suggested by earlier work, and highlight the strong model-dependence of predictions for nuclear kaonic bound states, which are deeper than atomic states and sensitive to the interior of the optical potential.
