# Using Equation of State Constraints to Classify Low-Mass Compact Binary Mergers

> Compact objects observed via gravitational waves are classified as black holes or neutron stars primarily based on their inferred mass with respect to stellar evolution expectations. However, astrophysical expectations for the lowest mass range, $\lesssim 1.2 \,M_\odot$, are uncertain. If such low-mass compact objects exist, ground-based gravitational wave detectors may observe them in binary mergers. Lacking astrophysical expectations for classifying such observations, we go beyond the mass and explore the role of tidal effects. We evaluate how combined mass and tidal inference can inform whether each binary component is a black hole or a neutron star based on consistency with the supranuclear-density equation of state. Low-mass neutron stars experience a large tidal deformation; its observational identification (or lack thereof) can therefore aid in determining the nature of the binary components. Using simulated data, we find that the presence of a sub-solar mass neutron star (black hole) can be established with odds $\sim 100:1$ when two neutron stars (black holes) merge and emit gravitational waves at signal-to-noise ratio $\sim 20$. For the same systems, the absence of a black hole (neutron star) can be established with odds $\sim 10:1$. For mixed neutron star-black hole binaries, we can establish that the system contains a neutron star with odds $\gtrsim 5:1$. Establishing the presence of a black hole in mixed neutron star-black hole binaries is more challenging, except for the case of a $\lesssim 1\,M_{\odot}$ black hole with a $\gtrsim 1\,M_{\odot}$ neutron star companion. On the other hand, classifying each individual binary component suffers from an inherent labeling ambiguity.

# Using Equation of State Constraints to Classify Low-Mass Compact Binary Mergers

## Key claim

The authors show that for low-mass compact binary mergers (component masses below approximately 1.2 solar masses), combined mass and tidal deformability measurements can classify binary components as neutron stars or black holes based on consistency with the supranuclear-density equation of state, but only when the signal-to-noise ratio is sufficiently high (approximately 20) and the classification of individual components suffers from an inherent labeling ambiguity.

## Method

The authors simulated gravitational-wave signals from 55 unique binary configurations with component masses between 0.8 and 1.2 solar masses, covering all combinations of neutron star-neutron star, black hole-neutron star, neutron star-black hole, and black hole-black hole systems. They performed parameter estimation using the IMRPhenomXAS_NRTidalv3 waveform model with zero noise realizations at signal-to-noise ratios of 20 and 12, then computed odds ratios by marginalizing over an equation of state posterior from Legred (2021) that incorporates radio pulsar, X-ray pulse-profile, and gravitational-wave observations.

## Key numbers

- The presence of a sub-solar mass neutron star (black hole) can be established with odds approximately 100:1 when two neutron stars (black holes) merge at signal-to-noise ratio approximately 20
- The absence of a black hole (neutron star) can be established with odds approximately 10:1 for the same systems at signal-to-noise ratio approximately 20
- For mixed neutron star-black hole binaries, the presence of a neutron star can be established with odds greater than or equal to 5:1
- Black hole-black hole signals show evidence for a black hole with odds greater than or equal to 10:1 for all masses at signal-to-noise ratio 20, increasing from 10:1 at 1.2-1.2 solar masses to 100:1 at 0.8-0.8 solar masses
- The tidal deformability of a 1 solar mass neutron star is approximately 10,000, compared to approximately 10 for a 2 solar mass neutron star

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to identify neutron stars through tidal effects in gravitational waves, such as the GW170817 classification by Essick (2019) which used equation of state consistency, and the work of Chen (2020) showing that absence of tidal signatures can identify black holes in the 1-2 solar mass range. The method goes beyond simple tidal detection by requiring consistency with the full equation of state prediction for neutron stars of a given mass, rather than just testing for nonzero tidal deformability. This enables classification of potential low-mass compact object mergers that lack astrophysical expectations for their nature, though the authors note that the true equation of state remains unknown and must be marginalized over using existing constraints.
