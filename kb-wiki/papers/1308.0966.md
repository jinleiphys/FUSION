# Bateman method for two-body scattering without partial-wave decomposition

> The use of Bateman method for solving the two-variable version of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation without recourse to partial-wave decomposition is investigated. Bateman method is based on a special kind of interpolation of the momentum representation of the potential on a multi-variate grid. A suitable scheme for the generation of a multi-variate Cartesian grid is described. The method is tested in the nucleon-nucleon scattering employing a model two-nucleon potential. Our results show that the Bateman method is capable of producing quite accurate solutions with relatively small number of grid points.

## Key claim

The Bateman method, based on a separable expansion of the potential via interpolation on a multi-variate Cartesian grid, can produce quite accurate solutions to the two-variable Lippmann-Schwinger equation for two-body scattering without partial-wave decomposition, but the paper's results show that 3-4 digit accuracy is achievable with relatively few grid points while reaching higher accuracy (e.g., 6 digits) requires much finer interpolation grids, especially for the forward scattering direction.

## Method

The momentum-space kernel of the potential is interpolated by a separable expansion constructed from its sections on a Cartesian grid formed by the product of a q-grid and an x-grid. The q-grid divides the domain into two intervals ([0, 2q0] and [2q0, ∞)) with the semi-infinite interval mapped via a transformation to allow finite-element partitioning, while the x-grid partitions [-1,1] into equal finite elements. The Bateman interpolate of the potential is then used to construct the transition operator, with singular integrals treated by separating principal-value and imaginary parts and using a subtraction procedure, and the method is tested on the Malfliet-Tjon III model two-nucleon potential.

## Key numbers

- For the Malfliet-Tjon III potential at E=150 MeV (q0=1.901860 fm⁻¹), the Nystrom reference on-shell T-matrix element at x=+1.0 has real part -6.092782 and imaginary part -1.937247 (units: MeV-fm³).
- At E=150 MeV, with N=41 q-grid points and M=41 x-grid points, the Bateman method gives on-shell T-matrix at x=+1.0 with real part -6.092758 and imaginary part -1.937226, agreeing with Nystrom to about 4-5 digits.
- At E=400 MeV (q0=3.105725 fm⁻¹), with N=41 and M=41, the Bateman method gives on-shell T-matrix at x=+1.0 with real part -6.163427 and imaginary part -1.937226 (the imaginary part value appears identical to the 150 MeV case in the table, though the text indicates separate calculations).
- When the quadrature grid is used as both interpolation and quadrature grid in the Bateman method, results agree with Nystrom calculations to at least 8 digits after the decimal point.
- Typical quadrature grids used Nq=160-200 points for 6-7 digit stability, with Nx=80 sufficient for 6-digit stability.

## Context

This work extends the Bateman method, originally proposed by Bateman (1922) for single-variable integral equations and previously applied to partial-wave Lippmann-Schwinger equations in the 1970s, to the two-variable version of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation without partial-wave decomposition. The only prior Bateman application avoiding partial-wave expansion was by Gianini and Lim (1978) for a Yukawa potential, but their interpolation grid was severely limited. The paper shows that the Bateman method is intimately related to the Nystrom method (Atkinson, 1976), with the Nystrom method being a special case where interpolation and quadrature grids coincide, and suggests the Bateman method could enable efficient generation of off-shell T-matrix elements for direct momentum-vector approaches to three-particle Faddeev equations without partial-wave decomposition, as pursued by Elster, Glockle, and collaborators (2005, 2009).
