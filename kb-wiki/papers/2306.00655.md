# Effects of sequential decay on collective flows and nuclear stopping power in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies

> In this study, the rapidity distribution, collective flows, and nuclear stopping power in $^{197}\mathrm{Au}+^{197}\mathrm{Au}$ collisions at intermediate energies were investigated using the ultrarelativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) model with GEMINI++ code. The UrQMD model was adopted to simulate the dynamic evolution of heavy-ion collisions, whereas the GEMINI++ code was used to simulate the decay of primary fragments produced by UrQMD. The calculated results were compared with the INDRA and FOPI experimental data. It was found that the rapidity distribution, collective flows, and nuclear stopping power were affected to a certain extent by the decay of primary fragments, especially at lower beam energies. Furthermore, the experimental data of the collective flows and nuclear stopping power at the investigated beam energies were better reproduced when the sequential decay effect was included.

## Key claim

The sequential decay of excited primary fragments, simulated by coupling the UrQMD transport model with the GEMINI++ statistical decay code, significantly affects the rapidity distribution, collective flows, and nuclear stopping power in Au+Au collisions at intermediate energies, with the effect being more pronounced at lower beam energies; the inclusion of sequential decay improves agreement with experimental data from the INDRA and FOPI collaborations, but the yields of light clusters such as He-3 and He-4 remain underestimated.

## Method

The dynamical evolution of Au+Au collisions was simulated using the Ultrarelativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model with a soft momentum-dependent equation of state (incompressibility K0 = 200 MeV) and an in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section. Primary fragments were constructed using an isospin-dependent minimum spanning tree (iso-MST) algorithm, and their excitation energies and angular momenta were calculated from the UrQMD output. The statistical decay of these excited primary fragments was then treated using the GEMINI++ code, which includes light particle evaporation, symmetric and asymmetric fission, and all possible binary-decay modes. Observables including rapidity distributions, directed flow v1, elliptic flow v2, and nuclear stopping power (RE and vartl) were computed with and without the sequential decay step and compared to INDRA and FOPI experimental data.

## Key numbers

- At Elab = 40 MeV/nucleon and impact parameter b = 5.5-7.5 fm, the yields of He-3 and He-4 from peripheral Au+Au collisions are approximately 1.5 and 6 times greater, respectively, when sequential decay is included compared to simulations without it.
- The directed flow slope v11 for Z=1 particles at Elab = 50 MeV/nucleon decreases and the elliptic flow v20 increases when sequential decay is included, bringing both closer to experimental data from the INDRA collaboration.
- The nuclear stopping power RE for free protons from central Au+Au collisions (b = 0-2 fm) at Elab = 40 MeV/nucleon is slightly decreased when sequential decay is included, with results saturating and becoming close to experimental data above a stopping time of 200 fm/c.
- At Elab = 400 MeV/nucleon, the effects of sequential decay on collective flows and nuclear stopping power are much weaker than at lower energies, with the difference in values gradually disappearing as beam energy increases.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Wang (2013), Li (2018), and Li (2022) that used the UrQMD model with the conventional Minimum Spanning Tree coalescence method and found that while collective flows of protons and deuterons could be reasonably reproduced, those of alpha particles differed from experimental data. The present study addresses this by incorporating the GEMINI++ statistical decay code (Charity, 1988; Charity, 2010) to treat the de-excitation of primary fragments, a step often omitted in transport model comparisons. The results enable more reliable extraction of nuclear matter properties, such as the nuclear equation of state and symmetry energy, by improving the agreement between transport model calculations and experimental data at intermediate energies, particularly for light cluster observables.
