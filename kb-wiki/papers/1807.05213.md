# Probing the transverse size of initial inhomogeneities with flow observables

> Disentangling the effect of initial conditions and medium properties is an open question in the field of relativistic heavy ion collisions. We argue that, while one can study the impact of initial inhomogeneities by varying their size, it is important to maintain the global properties fixed. We present a method to do this. We show that many observables are insensitive to the the hot spot sizes, including integrated vn, scaled distributions of vn, symmetric cumulants, eventplane correlations, and differential vn(pT ). We find however that the factorization breaking ratio rn and sub-leading component in a Principal Component Analysis are more sensitive to the initial granularity and can be used to probe short-scale features of the initial density.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while many standard flow observables are insensitive to the size of initial-state hot spots (small-scale inhomogeneities) when global properties like eccentricities are held fixed, the factorization breaking ratio \(r_n\) and the sub-leading component from a Principal Component Analysis do show sensitivity and can therefore be used to probe short-scale features of the initial density.

## Method

The authors systematically smooth the transverse energy density of initial conditions (NEXUS and MC-KLN) using a cubic spline filter with a smoothing parameter \(\lambda\) ranging from 0.3 to 1 fm, while ensuring that the integral of the energy density and the lowest-order cumulants (which encode large-scale structure) remain unchanged. They then feed these smoothed initial conditions into relativistic viscous hydrodynamic models (NeXSPheRIO and v-USPhydro) and compute a suite of final-state flow observables, comparing results across different \(\lambda\) values to isolate the effect of small-scale granularity.

## Key numbers

* The smoothing parameter \(\lambda\) is varied between 0.3 fm and 1 fm for both NEXUS and MC-KLN initial conditions.
* For the factorization breaking ratio \(r_n\) in 20-25% centrality Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV, with \(2.5\ \mathrm{GeV} < p_T^b < 3.0\ \mathrm{GeV}\), the effect of varying \(\lambda\) is up to approximately 15%.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Noronha-Hostler et al. (2015) and Petersen et al. (2010) that examined the effect of coarse-graining on spectra and differential flow, but it introduces a method that keeps global eccentricities fixed to isolate the role of hot-spot size. The finding that integrated \(v_n\), scaled \(v_n\) distributions, symmetric cumulants, and event-plane correlations are insensitive to granularity contradicts the naive expectation that these observables would be strongly affected. The paper identifies the factorization breaking ratio and sub-leading PCA components as promising discriminators, enabling future work to distinguish between different initial-condition models (e.g., NEXUS vs. MC-KLN) based on their short-scale structure.
