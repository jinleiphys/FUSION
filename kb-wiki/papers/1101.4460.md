# Fully microscopic shell-model calculations with realistic effective hamiltonians

> The advent of nucleon-nucleon potentials derived from chiral perturbation theory, as well as the so-called V-low-k approach to the renormalization of the strong short-range repulsion contained in the potentials, have brought renewed interest in realistic shell-model calculations. Here we focus on calculations where a fully microscopic approach is adopted. No phenomenological input is needed in these calculations, because single-particle energies, matrix elements of the two-body interaction, and matrix elements of the electromagnetic multipole operators are derived theoretically. This has been done within the framework of the time-dependent degenerate linked-diagram perturbation theory. We present results for some nuclei in different mass regions. These evidence the ability of realistic effective hamiltonians to provide an accurate description of nuclear structure properties.

# Fully microscopic shell-model calculations with realistic effective hamiltonians

## Key claim
Fully microscopic shell-model calculations using single-particle energies and two-body matrix elements both derived from realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials via perturbation theory can reproduce experimental nuclear structure properties without any phenomenological input, though systematic overestimates of ground-state energies in oxygen and calcium isotopes suggest the need for three-body forces.

## Method
The authors derive an effective shell-model hamiltonian using time-dependent degenerate linked-diagram perturbation theory, starting from either a low-momentum potential (V_low-k) obtained from the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential with a cutoff of 2.6 fm^-1, or from the N^3LOW chiral potential with a cutoff of 2.1 fm^-1. The effective hamiltonian is constructed via the Kuo-Lee-Ratcliff folded-diagram expansion, with the Q-box including one- and two-body Goldstone diagrams through third order, and the folded series summed to all orders using the Lee-Suzuki iteration method. Single-particle energies are obtained from the S-box calculation rather than taken from experiment, and self-consistency correction diagrams from the harmonic oscillator auxiliary potential are included up to third order.

## Key numbers
- Two-neutron separation energy S_2n for ^22C: calculated 601 keV, compared to experimental evaluation of 420 keV (Audi 2003)
- One-neutron separation energy S_n for ^21C: calculated -1.6 MeV, confirming particle instability
- Predicted excitation energy of the unbound J^pi = 2^+_1 state in ^22C: 4.661 MeV
- Upshift needed to reproduce ^17O ground-state energy relative to ^16O: 427 keV
- Upshift needed to reproduce ^41Ca binding energy relative to ^40Ca: approximately 1.6 MeV
- Discrepancy between theoretical and experimental ground-state energies for N=82 isotones relative to ^132Sn core: approximately 2.4 MeV

## Context
This work extends the realistic shell-model approach pioneered by Kuo and Brown (1966) for the sd-shell region, and builds on the V_low-k renormalization method of Bogner et al. (2002) and the N^3LOW chiral potential approach of Coraggio et al. (2007). The fully microscopic method removes the phenomenological fitting of single-particle energies that was typical in earlier realistic calculations reviewed by Coraggio et al. (2009). The results enable reliable predictions for neutron-rich nuclei near the dripline, such as the Borromean nucleus ^22C observed by Tanaka et al. (2010), and provide a framework for testing the role of three-body forces in nuclear structure.
