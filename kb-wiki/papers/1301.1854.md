# V_{ud} from neutron beta decay

> The experimental determination of V_{ud} from neutron beta decay requires accurate values of the neutron lifetime and the ratio of the weak axial-vector to vector coupling constants of the nucleon. The latter is derived from measurements of angular correlation coefficients in the differential decay probability, such as the beta asymmetry parameter A, the neutrino-electron angular correlation coefficient a, or the parameter C of the proton asymmetry. As a probe free from nuclear structure corrections the decay of the free neutron has the potential to provide the most accurate value of V_{ud}. Towards that end however, the experimental sensitivity still needs to be further improved to become competitive with superallowed nuclear beta decays. This contribution briefly reviews the current status of those neutron decay studies relevant for the determination of V_{ud}.

## Key claim

The free neutron beta decay can potentially provide the most accurate value of \(V_{\mathrm{ud}}\) because it is free from nuclear structure corrections, but experimental sensitivity still needs further improvement to become competitive with superallowed nuclear beta decays.

## Method

The paper reviews the experimental determination of \(V_{\mathrm{ud}}\) from neutron beta decay, which requires accurate measurements of the neutron lifetime \(\tau_{\mathrm{n}}\) and the ratio \(\lambda = g_{\mathrm{A}}/g_{\mathrm{V}}\) of the weak axial-vector to vector coupling constants. The value of \(\lambda\) is derived from angular correlation coefficients in the differential decay probability, primarily the beta asymmetry parameter \(A\), with \(V_{\mathrm{ud}}\) then obtained using the Marciano-Sirlin formula that incorporates radiative corrections.

## Key numbers

- The Marciano-Sirlin formula gives \(|V_{\mathrm{ud}}|^2 = 4908.7(1.9)\ \mathrm{s} / [\tau_{\mathrm{n}}(1+3\lambda^2)]\), where the uncertainty of 1.9 s corresponds to the radiative correction \(\Delta_R\) and limits accuracy to \(\delta|V_{\mathrm{ud}}| = 1.9 \times 10^{-4}\).
- To make experimental errors smaller than the radiative correction, \(\tau_{\mathrm{n}}\) must be measured with accuracy \(\delta\tau_{\mathrm{n}} \leq 0.34\ \mathrm{s}\) and \(\lambda\) with \(\delta\lambda \leq 3 \times 10^{-4}\).
- The most recent PDG average neutron lifetime is \(880.1 \pm 1.1\ \mathrm{s}\) with a scale factor \(S=1.8\).
- The latest PDG value for \(\lambda\) is \(-1.2701 \pm 0.0025\) (\(S=1.9\)); a newer result from PERKEO II gives \(\lambda = -1.2755 \pm 0.0013\).
- The UCNA experiment determined \(\lambda = -1.27590^{+0.00409}_{-0.00445}\).
- An accuracy of \(\delta\lambda = \pm 0.00067\) has been announced for a new PERKEO III measurement.

## Context

This review extends the discussion of \(V_{\mathrm{ud}}\) determination from neutron decay presented by Marciano and Sirlin (2006), who stated that neutron decay was not yet competitive with superallowed \(0^+ \rightarrow 0^+\) nuclear beta decays. The paper updates the experimental situation following the 2005 neutron lifetime discrepancy from Serebrov et al. and subsequent corrections, including the revised value from Arzumanov et al. (2012). It enables future progress by identifying the required precision targets for \(\tau_{\mathrm{n}}\) and \(\lambda\) and describing ongoing experiments with magnetic trapping and new spectrometers such as PERC and \(Nab\) that aim to surpass current theoretical limitations.
