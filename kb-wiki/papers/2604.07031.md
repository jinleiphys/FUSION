# How acausal equations emerge from causal dynamics

> We construct a causal and covariantly stable kinetic model whose spectrum at real wavenumbers $k$ reproduces any rest-frame stable dissipative dispersion relation $ω(k)$ via suitable initialization of the microscopic degrees of freedom. Macroscopic observables can therefore obey arbitrary linear evolution equations (including forms that would be acausal if taken as fundamental), while the underlying dynamics remains causal, and all apparent propagation is encoded in the initial data. This provides an explicit counterexample to the idea that microscopic causality alone constrains the analytic form of dispersion relations at real $k$. In particular, bounds on transport coefficients based solely on the analytic structure of $ω(k)$, such as the hydrohedron bounds, require additional assumptions about the region in the complex $k$-plane where $ω(k)$ corresponds to physical modes.

## Key claim

The paper constructs a causal and covariantly stable kinetic model whose spectrum at real wavenumbers \(k\) can reproduce any rest-frame stable dissipative dispersion relation \(\omega(k)\) through suitable initialization of microscopic degrees of freedom, providing an explicit counterexample to the idea that microscopic causality alone constrains the analytic form of dispersion relations at real \(k\).

## Method

The author constructs a kinetic toy model in (1+1)-dimensional Minkowski spacetime involving a linearized observable \(\psi(t,x,\varepsilon)\) that counts particles at spacetime point \((t,x)\) with energy \(\varepsilon\). The equation of motion \(\partial_t \psi = \partial_\varepsilon \psi\) describes particles that remain at fixed spatial positions while progressively losing energy, with general solution \(\psi(t,x,\varepsilon)=\psi(0,x,\varepsilon+t)\). This model is manifestly causal because its spacetime characteristics are the lines \(x=\mathrm{const}\), so no information propagates between different spatial points. By taking Fourier superpositions of the quasi-normal modes \(\psi^{\{k,\omega\}}(t,x,\varepsilon)= e^{ikx-i\omega(\varepsilon+t)}\), any convergent superposition solves the model and is governed by the dispersion relation \(\omega=f(k)\), allowing arbitrary rest-frame stable dispersion relations to be "carved out" through appropriate initial data.

## Key numbers

- The model violates the hydrohedron bound \(\mathfrak{D} \mathcal{R}\leq \frac{16}{3\pi}\) for pure diffusion \(\omega=-ik^2\) (with \(\mathfrak{D}=1\)), yielding \(\mathfrak{D}\mathcal{R}=+\infty\).
- An explicit example with finite radius of convergence gives \(\omega =\dfrac{-i(32 k)^2}{(3\pi)^2+(32 k)^2}\), yielding \(\mathfrak{D}\mathcal{R}=\frac{32}{3\pi}\), which violates the bound \(\mathfrak{D}\mathcal{R}\leq \frac{16}{3\pi}\).
- The model can reproduce a viscous sound mode propagating at speed \(2c\) via the dispersion relation \(\omega=2k-ik^2\).
- In the relativistic Fokker-Planck kinetic theory, the radius of validity is \(\mathcal{R}_v=1/(2\mathfrak{D})\), giving \(\mathfrak{D}\mathcal{R}_v=\frac{1}{2}\leq \frac{16}{3\pi}\), in agreement with the hydrohedron bounds.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous demonstrations that the hydrodynamic mode of relativistic Fokker-Planck kinetic theory (a causal and stable model) is exactly \(\omega=-i\mathfrak{D}k^2\) for all real \(k\) (Gavassino, 2026), which already contradicted the hydrohedron bounds proposed by Heller and collaborators (2022, 2023). The paper generalizes this counterexample by showing that any rest-frame stable dissipative dispersion relation can be embedded in a causal kinetic theory, using the "stadium wave" analogy where neighboring points do not exchange information but each point possesses sufficiently many relaxing and oscillatory degrees of freedom. The results show that the hydrohedron bounds can be salvaged by replacing the radius of convergence \(\mathcal{R}\) with a "radius of validity" \(\mathcal{R}_v\) that bounds the maximal disk in the complex \(k\)-plane where the dispersion relation admits a realization within the theory.
