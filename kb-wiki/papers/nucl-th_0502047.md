# Information entropy and nucleon correlations in nuclei

> The information entropies in coordinate and momentum spaces and their sum ($S_r$, $S_k$, $S$) are evaluated for many nuclei using "experimental" densities or/and momentum distributions. The results are compared with the harmonic oscillator model and with the short-range correlated distributions. It is found that $S_r$ depends strongly on $\ln A$ and does not depend very much on the model. The behaviour of $S_k$ is opposite. The various cases we consider can be classified according to either the quantity of the experimental data we use or by the values of $S$, i.e., the increase of the quality of the density and of the momentum distributions leads to an increase of the values of $S$. In all cases, apart from the linear relation $S=a+b\ln A$, the linear relation $S=a_V+b_V \ln V$ also holds. V is the mean volume of the nucleus. If $S$ is considered as an ensemble entropy, a relation between $A$ or $V$ and the ensemble volume can be found. Finally, comparing different electron scattering experiments for the same nucleus, it is found that the larger the momentum transfer ranges, the larger the information entropy is. It is concluded that $S$ could be used to compare different experiments for the same nucleus and to choose the most reliable one.

# Information entropy and nucleon correlations in nuclei

## Key claim

The information entropy sum S = S_r + S_k increases with the quality of the experimental density and momentum distributions used, following the maximum entropy principle, but this ordering holds only when the constraint dρ/dr ≤ 0 is satisfied and when deformed Fermi distributions are not required; three exceptions (²⁴Mg, ¹⁵⁰Nd, ²³⁸U) violate the rule that larger momentum-transfer ranges give larger S.

## Method

Shannon information entropies in coordinate space (S_r) and momentum space (S_k) were calculated from experimental charge density distributions (Fermi-type parameterizations from DeVries (1987) and Burov (1998)) and from experimental momentum distributions obtained via superscaling analysis of inclusive electron scattering (Donnelly (1999), Antonov (2004, 2005)). The coherence density fluctuation model (CDFM) was used to relate density distributions to momentum distributions and vice versa, enabling three approaches: (1) experimental densities with CDFM-derived momentum distributions, (2) experimental momentum distributions with CDFM-derived densities, and (3) both experimental densities and experimental momentum distributions directly.

## Key numbers

- For the harmonic oscillator model: S = 5.2391 + 0.8816 ln A (parameters from fit to s-, p-, and sd-shell nuclei)
- For approach 1 (experimental densities): S = 6.0011 + 0.7847 ln A (nuclei from ⁴He to ²³⁸U)
- For approach 2 (experimental momentum distributions): S = 6.8396 + 0.8919 ln A (five nuclei: ⁴He, ¹²C, ²⁷Al, ⁵⁶Fe, ¹⁹⁷Au)
- For approach 3 (both experimental densities and momentum distributions): S = 6.8845 + 0.9201 ln A (same five nuclei)
- The lower bound from the entropic uncertainty relation is S ≥ 3(1 + ln π) ≈ 6.434 nats, attained only for Gaussian wave functions (⁴He in the HO model)
- S_r varies by about 10% or less across models; S_k varies by about 50% across models
- For ¹⁴²Nd, S = 9.8762 nats (q-range 0.22–0.73 fm⁻¹) and S = 9.8978 nats (q-range 0.55–2.97 fm⁻¹)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of Shannon information entropy in fermion systems by Gadre (1987), Panos (1997), and Massen (1998, 2002), which established the linear relation S = a + b ln A for atoms, metallic clusters, and nuclei. It also builds on the maximum entropy principle criterion for nuclear density quality proposed by Lalazissis (1998) and the short-range correlation studies of Massen (2003) and Moustakidis (2004). The results enable using the information entropy sum S as a quantitative criterion to compare different electron scattering experiments for the same nucleus and to select the most reliable one, and suggest a connection between the ensemble volume (V_ensemble = e^S) and the mean nuclear volume V.
