# Semiclassical shell-structure micro-macroscopic approach for the level density

> Level density $ρ(E,A)$ is derived for a one-component nucleon system with a given energy $E$ and particle number $A$ within the mean-field semiclassical periodic-orbit theory beyond the saddle-point method of the Fermi gas model. We obtain $~~ρ\propto I_ν(S)/S^ν$, with $I_ν(S)$ being the modified Bessel function of the entropy $S$. Within the micro-macro-canonical approximation (MMA), for a small thermal excitation energy, $U$, with respect to rotational excitations, $E_{\rm rot}$, one obtains $ν=3/2$ for $ρ(E,A)$. In the case of excitation energy $U$ larger than $E_{\rm rot}$ but smaller than the neutron separation energy, one finds a larger value of $ν=5/2$. A role of the fixed spin variables for rotating nuclei is discussed. The MMA level density $ρ$ reaches the well-known grand-canonical ensemble limit (Fermi gas asymptotic) for large $S$ related to large excitation energies, and also reaches the finite micro-canonical limit for small combinatorial entropy $S$ at low excitation energies (the constant "temperature" model). Fitting the $ρ(E,A)$ of the MMA to the experimental data for low excitation energies, taking into account shell and, qualitatively, pairing effects, one obtains for the inverse level density parameter $K$ a value which differs essentially from that parameter derived from data on neutron resonances.

## Key claim

The paper derives a level density formula $\rho \propto I_\nu(S)/S^\nu$ (with $I_\nu$ the modified Bessel function of entropy $S$) that interpolates between the Fermi gas asymptotic limit at high excitation energies and the constant-temperature model at low excitation energies, but the exponent $\nu$ depends on whether the thermal excitation energy $U$ is smaller ($\nu=3/2$) or larger ($\nu=5/2$) than the rotational energy $E_{\rm rot}$, and the inverse level density parameter $K$ obtained from fitting low-energy data differs essentially from that derived from neutron resonance data.

## Method

The authors start from the micro-canonical expression for the level density as an inverse Laplace transform of the partition function, using the mean-field approximation with the single-particle level density decomposed into a smooth extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) part and an oscillating shell-correction part from periodic-orbit theory (POT). They perform the saddle-point integration over the chemical potential variable exactly, but go beyond the saddle-point method for the temperature variable by expanding the Jacobian factor in the integrand either for small shell effects (case i, leading to $\nu=3/2$) or for dominating shell effects (case ii, leading to $\nu=5/2$), then evaluating the resulting integrals analytically in terms of modified Bessel functions.

## Key numbers

* Inverse level density parameter $K = A/a$ is introduced, with typical values around $K \approx 10$ MeV and $K \approx 20$ MeV used in calculations for $A=200$ nuclei.
* The condition for neglecting higher-order terms in the expansion is $U \ll \sqrt{90/7} \, A^{1/3} \lambda^2 / (2\pi^4 K)$, which evaluates to approximately 20 MeV using $\lambda=40$ MeV, $A=200$, and $K\approx 10$ MeV.
* The distance between major shells is $D_{\rm sh} \approx \lambda / A^{1/3}$, with $\lambda \approx 40$ MeV for nuclear matter density $\rho_0 = 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$.
* The relative energy shell correction $\mathcal{E}_{\rm sh}$ takes values of 1.7 and 0.6 in the illustrative calculations shown in Figure 1.

## Context

This work extends the micro-macro-canonical approximation (MMA) previously developed by Magner and Kolomietz (1979) for the case of small excitation energies relative to rotational energy, now generalized to larger excitation energies up to the neutron separation energy. The approach uses periodic-orbit theory (POT) from Gutzwiller (1971) and Balian and Bloch (1972) to incorporate shell effects, and connects to the extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) theory at finite temperature used by Barranco and Buchinger (2003). The resulting level density formula bridges the Fermi gas model (Bethe 1936, Ericson 1960) at high excitation energies and the constant-temperature model (Gilbert and Cameron 1965) at low excitation energies, enabling improved fits to experimental level density data for heavy nuclei below the neutron resonance region.
