# The Similarity Renormalization Group with Novel Generators

> The choice of generator in the Similarity Renormalization Group (SRG) flow equation determines the evolution pattern of the Hamiltonian. The kinetic energy has been used in the generator for most prior applications to nuclear interactions, and other options have been largely unexplored. Here we show how variations of this standard choice can allow the evolution to proceed more efficiently without losing its advantages.

## Key claim

Variations of the standard kinetic-energy generator in the Similarity Renormalization Group flow equation can allow the evolution to proceed more efficiently without losing its advantages, provided the novel generator reduces to the kinetic energy for basis states with kinetic energy less than a scale parameter sigma.

## Method

The authors tested two alternative generators for the SRG flow equation: an "inverse" operator G_s = -sigma^2 / (1 + T_rel / sigma^2) and an "exponential" operator G_s = -sigma^2 exp(-T_rel / sigma^2), where T_rel is the relative kinetic energy and sigma is an adjustable scale parameter. They applied these generators to evolve realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials (Argonne v_18 and N^3LO 500 MeV chiral EFT) in two-body systems and a one-dimensional model Hamiltonian for few-body bound states, comparing computational time and decoupling properties against the standard G_s = T_rel choice.

## Key numbers

- For the Argonne v_18 ^1S_0 potential with sigma = 2 fm^-1, the exponential and inverse generators showed nearly an order of magnitude reduction in computational time at lambda_eq = 4 fm^-1 and two orders of magnitude at lambda_eq = 2 fm^-1 compared to G_s = T_rel.
- For the N^3LO 500 MeV chiral EFT potential, evolving with the exponential generator to lambda_eq = 2 fm^-1 was about 1.5 times as fast as with G_s = T_rel, and about 3 times as fast to lambda_eq = 1.5 fm^-1.
- In the one-dimensional model, the speedup for A=2, A=3, and A=4 particle systems with sigma = 2 ranged from 3.3 to 4.1 times faster than G_s = T_rel; with sigma = 3, speedup ranged from 2.6 to 2.8 times faster.

## Context

This work extends prior SRG applications to nuclear interactions that used the kinetic energy as the generator (Bogner 2006, Bogner 2007, Jurgenson 2009, Hebeler 2010). The novel generators address computational difficulties encountered when evolving large-cutoff effective field theory potentials, which could take weeks on a single processor (Wendt 2011). The results enable exploration of the transition between pionful and pionless EFT regions and may facilitate distributed computing schemes for evolving three- and four-body interactions, which are approaching memory limits on single nodes (Jurgenson 2010).
