# Astromers in the radioactive decay of r-process nuclei

> We study the impact of astrophysically relevant nuclear isomers (astromers) in the context of the rapid neutron capture process (r-process) nucleosynthesis. We compute thermally mediated transition rates between long-lived isomers and the corresponding ground states in neutron-rich nuclei. We calculate the temperature-dependent beta-decay feeding factors which represent the fraction of material going to each of the isomer and ground state daughter species from the beta-decay parent species. We simulate nucleosynthesis by including as separate species nuclear excited states with measured terrestrial half-lives greater than 100 microseconds. We find a variety of isomers throughout the chart of nuclides are populated, and we identify those most likely to be influential. We comment on the capacity of isomer production to alter radioactive heating in an r-process environment.

# Astromers in the radioactive decay of r-process nuclei

## Key claim
Nuclear isomers (long-lived excited nuclear states) are dynamically populated during r-process nucleosynthesis and can alter radioactive heating by up to 25% on timescales of 10-100 days, but the inclusion of temperature-dependent beta-decay rates and thermally mediated transitions tempers the dramatic effects suggested by earlier work (Fujimoto 2020).

## Method
The authors computed thermally mediated transition rates between long-lived isomers and ground states in neutron-rich nuclei using experimental level energies, half-lives, spin-parity assignments, and gamma intensities from the ENSDF database, with Weisskopf approximations for unmeasured gamma transitions. They calculated temperature-dependent beta-decay feeding factors representing the fraction of material going to isomer and ground state daughter species, then simulated nucleosynthesis using the Jade nuclear reaction network with a matrix exponential solver, treating as separate species all nuclear excited states with measured terrestrial half-lives greater than 100 microseconds. The simulation assumed an initial solar-like distribution of r-process material and a temperature evolution based on homologous expansion into free space, following the trajectory of Zhu (2018).

## Key numbers
- Isomers with half-lives greater than 100 microseconds were included; over 700 nuclei are known to possess such isomeric states
- 48 isotopes reached an isomer population ratio R >= 0.1 at some time in the simulation
- The isomer of 85Kr has the highest Astromer Importance Rating from approximately 0.5 to 1 day due to greater than 90% feeding of the isomer by 85Br
- 129Te remains important across all epochs from 0.01 days to 1 year
- The 195Pt isomer de-excites on a timescale of approximately 4 days
- A 25% increase in heating from inclusion of isomers was found between three and one hundred days
- The 115Cd isomer has a half-life of 3.85e6 seconds and slows beta-decay for 45 days
- The 121Sn isomer has a half-life of 1.39e9 seconds and slows beta-decay for 44 years
- The 128Sb isomer accelerates decay to an effective half-life of 11 minutes
- The 130Sb isomer accelerates decay to an effective half-life of 6.3 minutes

## Context
This work extends the study of astrophysically relevant nuclear isomers (astromers) from well-known cases in lighter nuclei (26Al studied by Gupta 2001 and Runkle 2001; 85Kr as an s-process branch point studied by Abia 2001) into the full r-process context for the first time. It directly contrasts with the approach of Fujimoto (2020), who compared no-isomer simulations against models that replaced ground-state properties with isomer properties for hand-picked nuclei, finding that the present dynamical treatment with temperature-dependent rates tempers those earlier dramatic effects. The results enable identification of specific isomers most likely to produce observable electromagnetic signals (x-rays and gamma-rays) from de-excitation or subsequent beta decay, providing targets for radioactive beam facilities and multi-messenger astronomy observations.
