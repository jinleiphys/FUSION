# Three loop HTL perturbation theory at finite temperature and chemical potential

> In this proceedings contribution we present a recent three-loop hard-thermal-loop perturbation theory (HTLpt) calculation of the thermodynamic potential for a finite temperature and chemical potential system of quarks and gluons. We compare the resulting pressure, trace anomaly, and diagonal/off-diagonal quark susceptibilities with lattice data. We show that there is good agreement between the three-loop HTLpt analytic result and available lattice data.

# Three loop HTL perturbation theory at finite temperature and chemical potential

## Key claim
Three-loop hard-thermal-loop perturbation theory (HTLpt) yields good agreement with lattice QCD data for the pressure, trace anomaly, and quark number susceptibilities at finite temperature and chemical potential, but only down to temperatures on the order of 300 MeV, not to arbitrarily low temperatures.

## Method
The authors computed the thermodynamic potential for QCD at finite temperature and chemical potential to three-loop (next-to-next-to-leading order, NNLO) in hard-thermal-loop perturbation theory, using one-loop running coupling with the scale fixed by requiring α_s(1.5 GeV) = 0.326. They used two separate renormalization scales, Λ_g = 2π T for purely-gluonic graphs and Λ_q = 2π √(T² + μ²/π²) for fermionic graphs, and varied both scales by a factor of two to estimate systematic uncertainties.

## Key numbers
- Scale fixing: α_s(1.5 GeV) = 0.326, giving Λ_MSbar = 176 MeV
- Number of colors N_c = 3, number of flavors N_f = 3
- Central renormalization scales: Λ_g = 2π T, Λ_q = 2π √(T² + μ²/π²)
- Scale variation range: π T ≤ Λ_g ≤ 4π T
- Comparisons shown for μ_B = 0 and μ_B = 400 MeV
- Agreement with lattice data down to temperatures on the order of 300 MeV

## Context
This work extends previous HTLpt calculations at one-loop (Andersen, Braaten, Strickland, 2000) and two-loop order (Andersen et al., 2011) to three-loop order at finite chemical potential, building on three-loop results at zero chemical potential (Andersen et al., 2011). The results are compared with lattice data from Borsanyi et al. (2010, 2012) for pressure and trace anomaly, and from multiple lattice collaborations (WB, BNL-BI, MILC, TIFR) for baryon number and quark number susceptibilities. The analytic thermodynamic potential enables computation of pressure, energy density, entropy density, trace anomaly, speed of sound, and various quark number susceptibilities, providing a gauge-invariant perturbative framework for QGP phenomenology at temperatures relevant to heavy-ion collisions.
