# Cluster formations in deformed states for $^{28}$Si and $^{32}$S

> We study cluster formation in strongly deformed states for $^{28}$Si and $^{32}$S using a macroscopic-microscopic model. The study is based on calculated total-energy surfaces, which are the sums of deformation-dependent macroscopic-microscopic potential-energy surfaces and rotational-energy contributions. We analyze the angular-momentum-dependent total-energy surfaces and identify the normal- and super-deformed states in $^{28}$Si and $^{32}$S, respectively. We show that at sufficiently high angular momenta strongly deformed minima appear. The corresponding microscopic density distributions show cluster structure that closely resemble the $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C and $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O configurations. At still higher deformations, beyond the minima, valleys develop in the calculated surfaces. These valleys lead to mass divisions that correspond to the target-projectile configurations for which molecular resonance states have been observed. We discuss the relation between the one-body deformed minima and the two-body molecular-resonance states.

## Key claim

The macroscopic-microscopic model, applied for the first time to very light nuclei, shows that normal-deformed (ND) and super-deformed (SD) minima in \(^{28}\)Si and \(^{32}\)S develop cluster structures resembling \(^{16}\)O+\(^{12}\)C and \(^{16}\)O+\(^{16}\)O configurations, but only at sufficiently high angular momenta (\(I=10\) for \(^{28}\)Si ND, \(I=8\) for \(^{32}\)S SD); the molecular-resonance states observed in two-body reactions are governed by the two-body freely-rotating limit rather than the one-body rigid-rotor moment of inertia.

## Method

The authors calculated total-energy surfaces for \(^{28}\)Si and \(^{32}\)S by summing a macroscopic-microscopic potential-energy surface (using the three-quadratic-surface parametrization in a five-dimensional deformation space) and a rotational-energy contribution based on the rigid-body moment of inertia. They analyzed the angular-momentum-dependent surfaces using the immersion technique to identify minima and valleys, and computed nuclear density distributions from single-particle wave functions to characterize cluster formation. The Coulomb-barrier heights for the \(^{16}\)O+\(^{12}\)C and \(^{16}\)O+\(^{16}\)O reactions were calculated in the Yukawa-plus-exponential model for the freely-rotating limit and compared to saddle-point energies from the one-body potential.

## Key numbers

* Ground-state shape of \(^{28}\)Si: oblate with quadrupole moment \(Q_2 = -0.59\) (\(e^2\)b).
* ND minimum in \(^{28}\)Si: flat region near \(Q_2 = 0.05\) (\(e^2\)b); SD minimum at \(Q_2 = 1.41\) (\(e^2\)b) corresponding to \(\beta = 0.68\).
* Ground-state shape of \(^{32}\)S: prolate with \(Q_2 = 0.39\) (\(e^2\)b) corresponding to \(\beta_2 = 0.24\); SD minimum at \(Q_2 = 1.58\) (\(e^2\)b) corresponding to \(\beta = 0.72\).
* Neck formation and cluster transition occur at \(I = 10\) for the ND minimum in \(^{28}\)Si and at \(I = 8\) for the SD minimum in \(^{32}\)S.
* Calculated rotational band of the ND state in \(^{28}\)Si corresponds to \(N = 18\) in prior work; the SD state in \(^{32}\)S corresponds to \(N = 24\) in prior work.
* Band-head energies normalized by shifting: ND minimum for \(^{28}\)Si by \(+4.8\) MeV, SD minima for both nuclei by \(+2.8\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic model, previously successful for heavy-mass fusion-fission systems (Nix 1969; Möller 2009), to light \(s\)-\(d\) shell nuclei. It complements self-consistent mean-field calculations by Kimura and Horiuchi (2004) and Taniguchi et al. (2009), who identified SD bands and cluster components in \(^{32}\)S and \(^{28}\)Si using antisymmetrized molecular dynamics. The results enable a unified description connecting one-body deformed states to two-body molecular-resonance states observed in \(^{16}\)O+\(^{12}\)C and \(^{16}\)O+\(^{16}\)O reactions, and suggest that triaxial deformations (Möller 2011) are needed to reproduce the moment of inertia of alpha-cluster states in \(^{32}\)S.
