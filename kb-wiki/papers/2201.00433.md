# Direct Nuclear Reactions

> In this brief review we discuss the basic theoretical concepts used in the experimental studies of the most common cases of direct reactions such as (a) elastic scattering, (b) inelastic scattering, (c) Coulomb excitation, (d) transfer reactions and (e) breakup reactions.

## Key claim
This review presents the basic theoretical concepts for direct nuclear reactions (elastic scattering, inelastic scattering, Coulomb excitation, transfer reactions, and breakup reactions) as a scope-limited overview, without claiming to be exhaustive or to present new numerical results.

## Method
The authors describe the theoretical framework for direct reactions using partial-wave expansion of the scattering wavefunction, the S-matrix formalism, and the optical potential model. They derive the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for the T-matrix and present the eikonal approximation for high-energy collisions. For inelastic scattering, they introduce the Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) and the continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) method, along with time-dependent and time-independent coupled-channels equations.

## Key numbers
- Direct nuclear reactions occur in a characteristic time of \(10^{-22}\) s, the time it takes a nucleon to cross the nucleus.
- The Sommerfeld parameter \(\eta = Z_1Z_2e^2/\hbar v\) marks the transition from classical to quantum scattering; around \(\eta \sim 10\) the scattering changes from Fresnel to Fraunhofer regime.
- The distance between dips in Fraunhofer angular distributions is given by \(\Delta \theta \sim 1/kR\), where \(R\) is the nuclear size.
- The exponential fall-off of the cross section in the Fraunhofer regime is \(\sigma(\theta) \propto \exp(-qa)\), where \(q\) is the momentum transfer and \(a\) is the nuclear surface diffuseness.
- CDCC calculations for \(^{11}\)Be + \(^{64}\)Zn at 24.5 MeV are compared to experimental data from PhysRevLett.105.022701.

## Context
This review extends the foundational work of Bethe and Butler (1950s) who first understood direct reactions as surface diffractive effects and established their use as spectroscopic tools. It builds upon standard references including Feshbach (1992), Satchler (1983), and Canto and Hussein (2013). The paper enables researchers to apply direct reaction methods to study nuclear structure, particularly for weakly-bound nuclei where continuum coupling (CDCC) is essential, and for developing microscopic optical potentials from nucleon-nucleon scattering and self-energy formalisms.
