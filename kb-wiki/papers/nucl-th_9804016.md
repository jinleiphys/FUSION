# Comparison of techniques for computing shell-model effective operators

> Different techniques for calculating effective operators within the framework of the shell model using the same effective interaction and the same excitation spaces are presented. Starting with the large-basis no-core approach, we compare the time-honored perturbation-expansion approach and a model-space truncation approach. Results for the electric quadrupole and magnetic dipole operators are presented for $^6$Li. The convergence trends and dependence of the effective operators on differing excitation spaces and Pauli Q-operators is studied. In addition, the dependence of the electric-quadrupole effective charge on the harmonic-oscillator frequency and the mass number, for A=5,6, is investigated in the model-space truncation approach.

## Key claim

The paper compares three techniques for computing shell-model effective operators (no-core, perturbation-expansion, and model-space truncation) using the same nucleon-nucleon interaction and excitation spaces, finding that the perturbation-expansion method does not show strong convergence for the electric quadrupole effective charges as the intermediate-state excitation space increases, while the model-space truncation approach yields effective charges that depend significantly on the harmonic-oscillator frequency and mass number.

## Method

The authors performed no-core shell-model calculations for ⁶Li in complete NℏΩ basis spaces up to 6ℏΩ, using G-matrices derived from the Nijmegen II and Reid93 nucleon-nucleon potentials. They then computed effective charges via a second-order perturbation expansion (including zeroth-order, first-order, and second-order diagrams) and via a model-space truncation method that projects large-space no-core results into the 0ℏΩ space using the Lee-Suzuki similarity transformation. The electric quadrupole and magnetic dipole operators were studied, and the dependence of the quadrupole effective charge on harmonic-oscillator frequency (ℏΩ = 8, 10, 14, 17.8, 22 MeV) and mass number (A=5,6) was investigated in the model-space truncation approach.

## Key numbers

- For ⁶Li in the 6ℏΩ no-core calculation with ℏΩ = 14 MeV and Δ = -25 MeV, the electric quadrupole moment, magnetic dipole moment, and root-mean-square proton radius are all within about 10% of their respective experimental values.
- The calculated root-mean-square proton radius is about 15% smaller than the experimental value.
- The experimental electric quadrupole moment of ⁶Li is -0.082 (units not specified but standard is e·b).
- Using calculated single-particle energies instead of multiples of ℏΩ for energy denominators results in about a 10% reduction in the T=0 and T=1 effective charges.
- The contribution from each successively larger excitation space decreases by roughly a factor of four for the TDA and RPA diagrams.
- The effective neutron charge from perturbation theory is of the order of 0.3, with about two-thirds coming from the first-order diagram.
- The effective proton charge from perturbation theory is slightly larger than one, with the major contribution coming from first-order diagrams.
- The model-space truncation calculations were performed for ℏΩ = 8, 10, 14, 17.8, and 22 MeV for A=5 and A=6 systems.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Thoresen, Navratil, Barrett 1997) on a truncation procedure for determining effective charges in small shell-model spaces using large-space no-core calculations, and compares with the perturbation-theory approach of Siegel and Zamick (1970). The paper shows that the model-space truncation method yields effective charges consistent with empirical values, while perturbation-theory calculations typically produce effective charges that are too small compared with empirical values. The results enable a better understanding of why microscopic perturbation-theory calculations have historically failed to reproduce the empirical effective charges (typically e_eff^p ≈ 1.5e and e_eff^n ≈ 0.5e) and suggest that the model-space truncation approach provides a more reliable method for deriving effective operators.
