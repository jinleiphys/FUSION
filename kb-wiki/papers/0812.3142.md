# Trial wave functions, molecular states, and ro-vibrational spectra in the lowest Landau level: A universal description for bosons and fermions

> Through the introduction of a class of appropriate translationally invariant trial wave functions, we show that the strong correlations in the lowest Landau level (LLL) reflect in finite systems the emergence of intrinsic point-group symmetries associated with rotations and vibrations of molecules formed through particle localization. This molecular description is universal, being valid for both bosons and fermions, for both the yrast and excited states of the LLL spectra, and for both low and high angular momenta. This physical picture is fundamentally different from the "quantum-fluid" one associated with Jastrow-type trial functions.

# Trial wave functions, molecular states, and ro-vibrational spectra in the lowest Landau level: A universal description for bosons and fermions

## Key claim
The strong correlations in the lowest Landau level (LLL) for finite systems reflect emergent intrinsic point-group symmetries associated with rotations and vibrations of molecules formed through particle localization, providing a universal description valid for both bosons and fermions, for both yrast and excited states, and for both low and high angular momenta. This molecular picture is fundamentally different from the quantum-fluid description associated with Jastrow-type trial functions, and the authors claim numerical results agree with exact diagonalization within machine precision.

## Method
The authors construct translationally invariant trial wave functions called rotating electron molecules (REM) for fermions and rotating boson molecules (RBM) for bosons, using a two-step method: first constructing a single Slater determinant (for fermions) or permanent (for bosons) from displaced Gaussian orbitals whose centers form regular polygonal ring configurations, then restoring rotational symmetry via angular momentum projection. Vibrational excitations are incorporated through symmetric polynomials of the form Q_λ = Σ_i (z_i - z_c)^λ, where z_c is the center-of-mass coordinate and λ > 1 is a prime number. The full LLL spectra are obtained by diagonalizing the interaction Hamiltonian in the translationally invariant subspace spanned by these rovibrational molecular trial functions.

## Key numbers
- For N=3 spinless bosons with contact interaction g δ(z_i - z_j): the yrast state at L=0 has energy 1.5000000 in units of g/(π Λ^2); at L=2, energy 0.7500000; at L=3, energy 0.3750000; at L=6, the ground state energy is 0.0000000 (the bosonic Laughlin function for ν=1/2).
- For N=4 spin-polarized electrons with Coulomb interaction e^2/(κ r_ij): the yrast state at L=6 has energy 2.22725097 in units of e^2/(κ l_B); at L=9, energy 1.93480798; at L=12, energy 1.68518201; at L=18 (ν=1/3), energy 1.30572905.
- For N=4 electrons at L=18 (ν=1/3), the yrast state's largest component is the pure Φ^REM_18(0,4) REM state with overlap 0.9294 with the exact-diagonalization solution.
- For N=4 electrons at L=30 (ν=1/5), the yrast energy per electron is 0.25084902 e^2/(κ l_B), agreeing with exact diagonalization within machine precision, compared to composite-fermion diagonalization result 0.250863(6) and composite-fermion crystal result 0.25101(4).

## Context
This work extends the rotating electron molecule (REM) approach introduced by Yannouleas and Landman (2002, 2007) for finite electron systems in quantum dots to a universal framework covering both bosons and fermions. It directly contradicts the quantum-fluid interpretation of Laughlin (1983) and the composite-fermion picture of Jain (1989), showing that the Jastrow-Laughlin ansatz for ν=1/3 and ν=1/5 can be expressed as superpositions of molecular trial functions. The authors demonstrate numerical superiority over composite-fermion diagonalization and composite-fermion crystal approaches of Chang and Jain (2006) and Jeon and Jain (2007) for N=4 electrons at L=30. This work enables a unified molecular description of LLL spectra for small trapped ultracold bosonic systems, which the authors note are becoming experimentally accessible following Gemelke et al. (2008).
