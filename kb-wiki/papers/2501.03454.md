# Right-handed weak currents in neutrinoless $ββ$ decays and ton scale $ββ$ detectors

> Right handed weak-currents (RHCs) in the left-right (L-R) symmetric model for neutrinoless double beta decays (DBDs) of both the $0^+~\to~0^+$ and $0^+~\to~2^+$ transitions are discussed from both theoretical and experimental view points. $<λ>$ and $<η>$-terms are related by $<λ>/<η> \approx \tanβ$, which is constrained in the regions of $1-60$ for SUSY grand unified theories (GUTs) and of $1-165$ for non-SUSY GUTs. The enhancement mechanisms of the $<η>$ term over the $<λ>$ term in the $0^+$ transition are shown, and the $Δ$ isobar contribution to the NME for the transition to the 2$^+$ state is found to be of the order of $20\%$ of the NME with the quenched weak coupling. The new and interesting RHC regions of $<λ>\approx 5\times10^{-8}$ and $<η>\approx 1.5\times 10^{-10}$ are shown to be exclusively explored by measuring both the $ββ$ and $γ$ rays associated with the ground and excited DBDs by means of the ton-scale DBD detectors for the IH (inverted hierarchy) $ν$-masses. The actual RHCs to be studied depend on the RHC NMEs.

## Key claim

The paper argues that if the inverted hierarchy (IH) neutrino mass region is disfavored by cosmological constraints, ton-scale neutrinoless double beta decay (DBD) detectors may find no neutrino-mass signals but could instead discover right-handed weak currents (RHCs) in the regions of \(<\lambda> \approx 5\times10^{-8}\) and \(<\eta> \approx 1.5\times10^{-10}\), provided that both ground-state and excited-state DBDs are measured with coincident \(\beta\beta\) and \(\gamma\) rays.

## Method

The authors analyze the left-right symmetric model for neutrinoless DBD, deriving the transition rates for both \(0^+ \to 0^+\) and \(0^+ \to 2^+\) decays in terms of the effective couplings \(<\lambda>\) and \(<\eta>\). They compute nuclear matrix elements (NMEs) using the Quasi-Particle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) model and the shell model for five DBD isotopes (\({}^{76}\)Ge, \({}^{82}\)Se, \({}^{100}\)Mo, \({}^{130}\)Te, \({}^{136}\)Xe), and evaluate the \(\Delta\) isobar contribution to the \(0^+ \to 2^+\) transition via a microscopic meson-exchange current approach. The RHC decay rates are then compared with experimental sensitivities achievable by ton-scale detectors that measure both \(\beta\beta\) and \(\gamma\) rays.

## Key numbers

* The ratio \(<\lambda>/<\eta> \approx \tan\beta\) is constrained to the range \(1-60\) for SUSY GUTs and \(1-165\) for non-SUSY GUTs.
* The \(\Delta\) isobar contribution to the NME for the \(0^+ \to 2^+\) transition is about \(20\%\) of the NME when using the quenched weak coupling \(g_{\rm A}^{eff} \approx 0.7\).
* The new RHC regions to be explored are \(<\lambda> \approx 5\times10^{-8}\) and \(<\eta> \approx 1.5\times10^{-10}\).
* The effective axial-vector coupling ratio is \(g_{\rm A}^{eff}/g_{\rm A} \approx 0.55\) from analysis of single beta decay rates.
* The \(\nu\)-mass region for the inverted hierarchy corresponds to \(<m> \approx 0.6 \times 10^{-7}\) in units of \(m_e\) (approximately 30 meV).
* The lower bound on the right-handed gauge boson mass is \(M_{W2} \geq 3.7\) TeV from CMS data.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical treatment of RHCs in neutrinoless DBD previously developed by Doi (1985), Tomoda (1988), and Suhonen (1998), and addresses the possibility that ton-scale detectors built for the IH neutrino-mass region may instead observe RHC signals if cosmological constraints from DESI, Planck, and ACT disfavor the inverted hierarchy. The paper specifically enables experimental searches that distinguish between \(<\lambda>\) and \(<\eta>\) contributions by measuring both ground-state and excited-state DBDs with coincident \(\gamma\) rays, building on the ELEGANT experimental approach of Ejiri (1987, 1996). The \(\Delta\) isobar mechanism for the \(0^+ \to 2^+\) transition is compared with earlier estimates by Doi (1985) and Vergados (1988), and the microscopic calculation using meson-exchange currents yields a smaller contribution than earlier phenomenological estimates.
