# On the Evidence for Postacceleration Effects in Breakup Reactions with Halo Nuclei

> We study the postacceleration of charged fragments in reactions with unstable nuclear beams. For elastic breakup processes we show that the postaceleration effect can be well understood in terms of closed analytical forms derived in a quantum mechanical formulation. This gives theoretical support to the effect experimentally observed in the breakup of Li-11 projectiles at intermediate energies.

# On the Evidence for Postacceleration Effects in Breakup Reactions with Halo Nuclei

## Key claim
The postacceleration effect observed in the breakup of 11Li projectiles at intermediate energies can be explained by a simple analytical quantum mechanical model, but only under the assumptions that Coulomb dissociation is the dominant inelastic process and that the fragments are focused at forward directions, and only for breakup that proceeds directly to the structureless continuum without a relevant resonance.

## Method
The authors derived a closed-form analytical expression for the parallel momentum distribution of fragments in elastic breakup reactions using the Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) with eikonal wave functions. They factorized the breakup amplitude into an excitation part and an elastic part, approximated the Coulomb interaction in the dipole limit, and used a Yukawa-type ground state wavefunction for the halo nucleus to obtain an analytic expression for the cross section.

## Key numbers
- For 11Li projectiles incident on lead at 30 MeV/nucleon, the longitudinal momentum shift is approximately (8 fm)^(-1).
- The relative velocity difference between 9Li and the two neutrons is approximately 0.08c, compared to the beam velocity of approximately 0.25c.
- The postacceleration effect is quite large at low energies and tends to disappear at E/A approximately 1000 MeV/nucleon and higher.

## Context
This work provides theoretical support for the postacceleration effect experimentally observed by Ieki et al. (1993) and Sackett et al. (1993) in the breakup of 11Li, which had been questioned by Canto et al. (1997), Shyam et al. (1992), and Typel and Baur (1994). The analytical derivation enables experimentalists to estimate the width of the parallel velocity distribution for planning experimental setups, and clarifies that the postacceleration arises from differences in Coulomb phases between the charged fragment and the neutrons.
