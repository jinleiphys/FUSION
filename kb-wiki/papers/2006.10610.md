# Green's functions techniques for extended nuclear systems

> I review the application of self-consistent Green's functions methods to study the properties of infinite nuclear systems. Improvements over the last decade, including the consistent treatment of three-nucleon forces and the development of extrapolation methods from finite to zero temperature, have allowed for realistic predictions of the equation of state of infinite symmetric, asymmetric and neutron matter based on chiral interactions. Microscopic properties, like momentum distributions or spectral functions, are also accessible. Using an indicative set of results based on a subset of chiral interactions, I summarise here the first-principles description of infinite nuclear system provided by Green's functions techniques, in the context of several issues of relevance for nuclear theory including, but not limited to, the role of short-range correlations in nuclear systems, nuclear phase transitions and the isospin dependence of nuclear observables.

## Key claim

Self-consistent Green's functions (SCGF) methods, when combined with chiral interactions and a finite-to-zero-temperature extrapolation procedure, can provide realistic predictions for the equation of state and microscopic properties of infinite nuclear systems, but the results depend on the specific chiral interaction cutoff used and represent only a subset of possible forces, not the full uncertainty from the strong interaction.

## Method

The author reviews SCGF calculations for infinite symmetric, asymmetric, and neutron matter using three chiral N3LO two-nucleon forces with cutoffs of 414, 450, and 500 MeV, supplemented by N2LO three-nucleon forces fitted to A=3 binding energies and the 3H-3He Gamow-Teller matrix elements. Finite-temperature ladder-resummation SCGF simulations are performed at several temperatures (typically 4-10 values from around 20 MeV downward), and the temperature dependence of self-energies and spectral functions is fitted to polynomials and extrapolated to zero temperature, avoiding the superfluid regime that prevents convergence at very low temperatures. The three-nucleon forces are included via a normal-ordering procedure that builds effective one- and two-body density-dependent forces using the correlated momentum distributions from the SCGF calculation.

## Key numbers

* At a density of ρ=0.16 fm⁻³ in symmetric matter, the quasi-particle peak width at k=0 and k=2k_F is approximately Γ_k ≈ 40 MeV, independent of the chiral interaction cutoff.
* The zero-temperature extrapolation procedure uses finite-temperature simulations from high temperatures of order 20 MeV down to values where the degeneracy parameter T/ε_F allows controlled extrapolation; for sufficiently large temperatures (typically T ≳ 2-5 MeV), the treatment avoids complications from nuclear superfluidity.
* The three chiral interactions considered have cutoffs in relative momentum of Λ=414 MeV, 450 MeV, and 500 MeV, with two-body matrix elements computed for partial waves up to J=9.
* The spectral function at k=k_F in zero-temperature symmetric matter shows a zero-width δ function at ω=ε_{k_F} combined with a broad energy-dependent background; the high-energy tails extend to approximately 500 MeV for the Λ=500 MeV interaction.

## Context

This review extends the SCGF treatment of infinite nuclear systems beyond earlier work that was limited to two-body interactions only (Rios 2005, Rios 2009) and to finite-temperature calculations without zero-temperature extrapolation (Bozek 2003, Soma 2009). The inclusion of three-nucleon forces via normal ordering (Carbone 2013, Carbone 2014) and the development of the finite-to-zero-temperature extrapolation procedure (Ding 2016, Rios 2017) enable comparisons with zero-temperature results from other many-body methods such as Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (Baldo 2012) and quantum Monte Carlo (Gandolfi 2015, Piarulli 2020). The work enables future studies of the density, isospin asymmetry, and temperature dependence of microscopic and macroscopic nuclear properties relevant for neutron-star astrophysics and core-collapse supernovae.
