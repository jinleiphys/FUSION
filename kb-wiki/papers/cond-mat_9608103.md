# Fragmentation phase transition in atomic clusters I --- Microcanonical thermodynamics

> Here we first develop the thermodynamics of microcanonical phase transitions of first and second order in systems which are thermodynamically stable in the sense of van Hove. We show how both kinds of phase transitions can unambiguously be identified in relatively small isolated systems of $\sim 100$ atoms by the shape of the microcanonical caloric equation of state $<T(E/N)>$ I.e. within microcanonical thermodynamics one does not need to go to the thermodynamic limit in order to identify phase transitions. In contrast to ordinary (canonical) thermodynamics of the bulk microcanonical thermodynamics (MT) gives an insight into the coexistence region. The essential three parameters which identify the transition to be of first order, the transition temperature $T_{tr}$, the latent heat $q_{lat}$, and the interphase surface entropy $Δs_{surf}$ can very well be determined in relatively small systems like clusters by MT. The phase transition towards fragmentation is introduced. The general features of MT as applied to the fragmentation of atomic clusters are discussed. The similarities and differences to the boiling of macrosystems are pointed out.

# Fragmentation Phase Transition in Atomic Clusters I, Microcanonical Thermodynamics

## Key claim

Phase transitions of first and second order can be unambiguously identified in isolated systems as small as ~100 atoms by the shape of the microcanonical caloric equation of state <T(E/N)>, without needing to reach the thermodynamic limit; a first-order transition is signaled by a backbending (S-bend) in this curve, whereas a second-order transition shows only a horizontal saddle point.

## Method

The authors directly calculate the volume W of the accessible N-body phase space and its dependence on total energy, using the Boltzmann relation S = k ln(W) to define microcanonical thermodynamics. They apply Microcanonical Metropolis Monte Carlo (MMMC) sampling to group quantum states by common qualities and weight each configuration by the size of its corresponding phase-space subset, treating internal degrees of freedom of fragments via known internal specific heats. The method is demonstrated on the 2-dimensional 10-states Potts model and on sodium clusters (Na²⁺₁₀₀ and Na²⁺₂₀₀) to extract transition temperature T_tr, latent heat q_lat, and interphase surface entropy Δs_surf.

## Key numbers

- For bulk sodium at normal pressure, the melting transition temperature is T_m = 371 K, with a latent heat q_lat appearing as a δ-function peak in the specific heat.
- For Na²⁺₁₀₀ clusters, the caloric equation of state shows a backbending at specific excitation energy ε ≈ 0.5 eV/atom, signaling a first-order phase transition from a regime with a single large evaporation residue to multifragmentation with fragments of similar size (≤ 10 atoms).
- The internal entropy s_int of atomic clusters can easily become larger than 10 per atom, whereas in nuclei it is rarely s_int > 2 per nucleon.
- In the canonical ensemble, inhomogeneous configurations with phase separation are exponentially suppressed by a factor exp(−Δσ_surf N^{2/3}/T) relative to homogeneous configurations.

## Context

This work extends the microcanonical thermodynamics approach previously applied to the 10-states Potts model (Gross et al., 1995) to atomic clusters, showing that the same three parameters (T_tr, q_lat, Δs_surf) can be determined in small systems. It contrasts with canonical treatments such as Nosé molecular dynamics used by Berry (1994) and others, which suppress phase coexistence in finite systems. The paper enables the study of fragmentation as a new phase transition in finite systems, distinct from the liquid-gas transition of bulk matter, and sets the stage for subsequent papers in the series addressing asymmetric fission (Paper II), Coulomb explosion (Paper III), and the connection to the bulk liquid-gas transition (Paper IV).
