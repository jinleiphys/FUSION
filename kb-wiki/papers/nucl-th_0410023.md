# Systematics of quadrupolar correlation energies

> We calculate correlation energies associated with the quadrupolar shape degrees of freedom with a view to improving the self-consistent mean-field theory of nuclear binding energies. The Generator Coordinate Method is employed using mean-field wave functions and the Skyrme SLy4 interaction. Systematic results are presented for 605 even-even nuclei of known binding energies, going from mass A=16 up to the heaviest known. The correlation energies range from 0.5 to 6.0 MeV in magnitude and are rather smooth except for large variations at magic numbers and in light nuclei. Inclusion of these correlation energies in the calculated binding energy is found to improve two deficiencies of the Skyrme mean field theory. The pure mean field theory has an exaggerated shell effect at neutron magic numbers and addition of the correlation energies reduce it. The correlations also explain the phenomenon of mutually enhanced magicity, an interaction between neutron and proton shell effects that is not explicable in mean field theory.

## Key claim

Quadrupolar correlation energies calculated with the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) reduce the exaggerated shell effect at magic numbers in Skyrme mean-field theory and explain the phenomenon of mutually enhanced magicity, but the authors note that a more complete theory requires refitting the energy functional to account for the correlation contribution and that residual shell effects remain.

## Method

The authors performed systematic GCM calculations for 605 even-even nuclei from mass A=16 to the heaviest known, using mean-field wave functions generated with the Skyrme SLy4 interaction on a triaxial 3-dimensional spatial grid. They constrained the quadrupole operator to generate configurations with different quadrupole moments, restricted to shapes within 5 MeV of the mean-field ground state, and included angular momentum projection to J=0 and particle-number projection. The correlation energy was obtained in two steps: first the energy gain from angular momentum projection of each configuration, then the additional energy from mixing configurations with different quadrupole moments via the Hill-Wheeler equations, using a topological Gaussian overlap approximation and other computational shortcuts to reduce the effort by 1-2 orders of magnitude.

## Key numbers

- Correlation energies range from 0.5 to 6.0 MeV in magnitude across the 605 even-even nuclei studied.
- For the doubly magic nucleus ²⁰⁸Pb, the total correlation energy is -1.7 MeV (E_J=0 = -1.7 MeV, E_HW = 0.0 MeV).
- For the deformed nucleus ¹⁷⁰Hf, the total correlation energy is -3.4 MeV (E_def = -12.2 MeV, E_J=0 = -2.9 MeV, E_HW = -0.4 MeV).
- For the soft nucleus ¹⁸⁰Hg, the total correlation energy is -3.1 MeV (E_def = -3.0 MeV, E_J=0 = -2.5 MeV, E_HW = -0.5 MeV).
- The configuration space for ²⁰⁸Pb required 4 configurations; for ¹⁸⁰Hg, 15 configurations ranging from -20 b to +32 b; for ¹⁷⁰Hf, 12 configurations.
- The computational effort for the entire table of 605 nuclei required on the order of 10¹⁷ floating point operations.
- The target accuracy for correlation energies was 0.2 MeV, achieved when overlap probabilities of neighboring configurations are about 0.5 or higher.
- The pairing interaction strength was set to v₀ = -1000 MeV fm³.

## Context

This work extends self-consistent mean-field theory (Skyrme SLy4 interaction) by including quadrupolar correlation effects that are beyond the mean-field approximation, addressing the systematic problem of exaggerated shell effects at magic numbers that produces rms errors of 1.5-2.0 MeV in pure mean-field binding energies (Goriely et al., 2004). The GCM approach improves upon the RPA theory of correlation energies applied by Baroni et al. (2004), which has convergence problems for short-range interactions, and provides a systematic method applicable to both spherical and deformed nuclei. The results enable future construction of effective interactions that incorporate correlation energies, and the authors note that further improvements should include octupole deformations, pairing vibrations, and neutron-proton pairing effects, particularly for light N=Z nuclei.
