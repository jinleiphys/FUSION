# Analytic Boosted Boson Discrimination at the Large Hadron Collider

> Jet substructure is playing a central role at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) probing the Standard Model in extreme regions of phase space and providing innovative ways to search for new physics. Analytic calculations of experimentally successful observables are a primary catalyst driving developments in jet substructure, allowing for a deeper understanding of observables and for the exploitation of increasingly subtle features of jets. In this paper we present a field theoretic framework enabling systematically improvable calculations of groomed multi-prong substructure observables, which builds on recent developments in multi-scale effective theories. We use this framework to compute for the first time the full spectrum for groomed tagging observables at the LHC, carefully treating both perturbative and non-perturbative contributions in all regions. Our analysis enables a precision understanding which we hope will improve the reach and sophistication of jet substructure techniques at the LHC.

## Key claim
The paper presents a field-theoretic framework enabling systematically improvable calculations of groomed multi-prong substructure observables, computing for the first time the full spectrum for groomed tagging observables at the LHC. The hedge is that the framework is demonstrated specifically for the groomed $D_2$ observable at next-to-leading logarithmic (NLL) and leading order (LO) accuracy, and the analysis is limited to the case where $m_J^2 \ll z_{\text{cut}} p_{TJ}^2 \ll p_{TJ}^2$, which is satisfied for situations of interest at the LHC.

## Method
The authors use soft-collinear effective theory (SCET) to factorize the multi-differential cross section for groomed $D_2$ into single-scale functions (jet functions, collinear-soft functions, and matching coefficients), with logarithms resummed via renormalization group evolution. They tile the multi-dimensional phase space with three distinct effective field theories (factorization I for unresolved substructure, IIa for two energetic subjets, and IIb for one subjet near the grooming threshold) and smoothly patch them together. Non-perturbative corrections from hadronization are implemented via a shape function $F(\epsilon)$ with a single parameter $\Omega_D$ extracted from $e^+e^-$ collisions in Pythia 8.226, and the framework is validated by comparison with the EVENT2 fixed-order code.

## Key numbers
- The mMDT grooming parameter $z_{\text{cut}}$ is typically taken to be around 10%.
- The one-loop anomalous dimension for the hard matching coefficient $H^{s}$ in factorization IIb includes a term $\frac{\alpha_s}{\pi}(C_F-C_A)\frac{\text{Cl}_2(\frac{\pi}{3})}{\pi}$, where $\text{Cl}_2(\frac{\pi}{3}) \approx 1.01494$ is the maximum value of the Clausen function.
- The leading-power non-perturbative scaling is $D_2^{\text{NP}} \sim \frac{\Omega_D}{z_{\text{cut}}^{3/2} m_J}$, where $\Omega_D \sim \Lambda_{\text{QCD}}$ is a non-perturbative scale independent of jet transverse momentum $p_{TJ}$.
- The shape function used for non-perturbative corrections is $F(\epsilon) = (4\epsilon/\Omega_D^2) e^{-2\epsilon/\Omega_D}$, following Stewart (2014).
- The mass cut applied for $Z$ boson identification is 80-100 GeV.

## Context
This work extends the analytic calculation program for jet substructure observables, building on prior developments in multi-scale effective field theories by Bauer (2011), Larkoski (2015), and Pietrulewicz (2016). It specifically computes the groomed $D_2$ observable, which was introduced by Larkoski (2014, 2015) and has been widely used at the LHC to identify hadronically decaying $W/Z/H$ bosons. The framework enables precision understanding of both perturbative and non-perturbative aspects, and the authors note that the non-perturbative parameter $\Omega_D$ is independent of the mechanism of jet production (up to suppressed flavor dependence), allowing it to be extracted from $e^+e^-$ collisions and applied to LHC processes. This enables next-generation jet substructure techniques to exploit the rich LHC dataset.
