# The interplay of nuclear and Coulomb effects in proton breakup from exotic nuclei

> This paper gives new insight to the study of dynamical effects in proton breakup as compared to neutron breakup from a weakly bound state in an exotic nucleus. Following our recent work [Ravinder Kumar and Angela Bonaccorso, Phys. Rev. C84 014613 (2011)] there has been some discussion in the literature [B. Paes, J. Lubiana, P.R.S. Gomes, V. Guimarães, Nucl. Phys. A890 1 (2012); Y. Kucuk and A. M. Moro, Phys. Rev. C86 034601 (2012)], thus in order to clarify and asses quantitatively which mechanism would dominate measured observables, we study here several reaction mechanisms separately but also their total including interference. These mechanisms are: the recoil effect of the core-target Coulomb potential which we distinguish from the direct proton-target Coulomb potential and nuclear breakup, which consists of stripping and diffraction. Direct Coulomb breakup typically gives cross sections about an order of magnitude larger than the recoil term and the amount of nuclear diffraction vs. Coulomb depends on the target. Thus for each mechanism the absolute values of breakup cross sections and parallel momentum distributions for 8B and 17F projectiles calculated on a light and a heavy target in a range of intermediate incident energies (40-80A.MeV) are presented. Furthermore we study in detail the interference among the two Coulomb effects and nuclear diffraction. The calculation of the direct and recoil Coulomb effects separately and of their interference is the new and most relevant aspect of this paper.

## Key claim

This paper provides a quantitative assessment of the relative magnitudes of nuclear and Coulomb breakup mechanisms for weakly bound protons, showing that direct Coulomb breakup cross sections are about an order of magnitude larger than the recoil term, and that interference effects between mechanisms can significantly modify the simple sum of cross sections, varying with target and incident energy.

## Method

The authors used a semiclassical method that treats the full Coulomb and nuclear interactions to all orders and all multipolarities, calculating breakup cross sections and parallel momentum distributions for 8B and 17F projectiles on 12C and 208Pb targets at incident energies of 40, 60, and 80 A.MeV. They separated the reaction mechanisms into nuclear stripping, nuclear diffraction, direct proton-target Coulomb breakup, and core-target Coulomb recoil, and computed their individual contributions as well as their total including interference effects.

## Key numbers

- For 8B on 208Pb at 40 A.MeV: direct Coulomb breakup cross section is 4562.66 mb, while recoil Coulomb is 534.18 mb, showing direct term is about an order of magnitude larger
- For 8B on 208Pb at 40 A.MeV: total Coulomb breakup (including interference) is 4129.47 mb, demonstrating destructive interference between direct and recoil terms
- For 8B on 12C at 40 A.MeV: nuclear stripping is 51.62 mb, diffraction is 31.72 mb, while direct Coulomb is only 2.09 mb and recoil is 0.10 mb
- For 8B on 12C at 40 A.MeV: the combined Coulomb and diffraction cross section is 60.29 mb, almost double the simple sum of diffraction (31.72 mb) and total Coulomb (2.51 mb) due to constructive interference
- For 17F on 208Pb at 40 A.MeV: direct Coulomb breakup is 1209.35 mb, recoil Coulomb is 65.98 mb, and total Coulomb is 1542.39 mb (constructive interference)
- For 17F on 12C at 40 A.MeV: nuclear stripping is 18.06 mb, diffraction is 8.19 mb, direct Coulomb is 0.58 mb, recoil Coulomb is 0.007 mb
- Initial binding energies: 8B has -0.14 MeV (1p3/2 state), 17F has -0.6 MeV (1d5/2 state)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous semiclassical study (Kumar and Bonaccorso, 2011) of dynamical effects in proton breakup, responding to discussions in the literature by Paes et al. (2012) and Kucuk and Moro (2012) regarding the relative importance of nuclear versus Coulomb breakup mechanisms. The paper provides the first quantitative assessment of direct and recoil Coulomb effects separately and their interference, which is essential for interpreting experimental data on proton-rich nuclei and for nuclear astrophysics applications where Coulomb breakup is considered the inverse of (p,gamma) capture reactions.
