# Transport of hard probes through glasma

> We calculate the transverse momentum broadening $\hat q$ and collisional energy loss $dE/dx$ of hard probes traversing an evolving glasma during the earliest phase of a relativistic heavy-ion collision. We use a Fokker-Planck equation and apply a proper time expansion to describe the temporal evolution of the glasma. The correlators of the chromodynamic fields that determine the Fokker-Planck collision terms, which in turn provide $\hat q$ and $dE/dx$, are computed to fifth order. Both transport coefficients are strongly dependent on time. The maximum values they acquire before the proper time expansion breaks down are large: $\hat q$ is of the order of a few ${\rm GeV^2/fm}$ and $dE/dx \sim 1~{\rm GeV/fm}$. Their precise values depend on the probe's velocity ${\bf v}$, the saturation momentum $Q_s$, and an IR regulator $m$ that is related to the confinement scale. We study the dependence of our results on these quantities. Different regularization procedures are analysed and shown to produce similar results. We also discuss the validity of the proper time expansion and the compatibility of the approximations that are inherent in the derivation of the Fokker-Planck equation. We show that hard probes lose a comparable amount of energy when they propagate through the short-lived glasma phase, and the long-lasting hydrodynamic phase. The conclusion is that the glasma plays an important role in jet quenching.

## Key claim

The transverse momentum broadening $\hat q$ and collisional energy loss $dE/dx$ of hard probes traversing the glasma phase are strongly time-dependent and can reach large values (a few GeV²/fm and ~1 GeV/fm respectively), but these maximum values are obtained only before the proper time expansion breaks down, and their precise values depend on the probe's velocity, the saturation momentum $Q_s$, and an IR regulator $m$ related to the confinement scale.

## Method

The authors use a Fokker-Planck equation to describe the transport of hard probes through the glasma, with collision terms determined by correlators of chromoelectric and chromomagnetic fields. These field correlators are computed using a Color Glass Condensate (CGC) approach combined with a proper time expansion of the classical Yang-Mills equations, carried out to fifth order in proper time $\tau$. The glasma potentials are obtained from the McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model, and the resulting field correlators are used to compute the tensor $X^{\alpha\beta}(\mathbf{v})$, from which $\hat q$ and $dE/dx$ are extracted via projections.

## Key numbers

- $\hat q$ reaches values of the order of a few GeV²/fm (maximum before proper time expansion breaks down)
- $dE/dx \sim 1$ GeV/fm (maximum before proper time expansion breaks down)
- The glasma phase lifetime is approximately 1 fm (natural units with $c = \hbar = k_B = 1$)
- The hydrodynamic phase lifetime is approximately 10 fm
- Hard probes lose a comparable amount of energy in the short-lived glasma phase and the long-lasting hydrodynamic phase

## Context

This work extends the analysis of heavy quark transport through glasma initiated in Mrowczynski (2017) and Carrington (2020), using a proper time expansion originally proposed by Fries (2006) and developed by Chen (2015). The results contradict earlier work by Aurenche (2012) which found synchrotron-like gluon emission from glasma fields to be much smaller than radiative energy loss from quark-gluon plasma. The paper enables quantitative inclusion of the glasma phase in multi-stage jet quenching models, showing that the glasma plays an important role in jet quenching and cannot be neglected.
