# Imaging nuclei by smashing them at high energies: how are their shapes revealed after destruction?

> High-energy nuclear collisions have recently emerged as a promising ``imaging-by-smashing'' approach that may reveal the intrinsic shapes of atomic nuclei. Here, I outline a conceptual framework for this technique, explaining how nuclear shapes are encoded during quark-gluon plasma formation and evolution, and how they can be decoded from final-state particle distributions. I highlight the method's potential to advance our understanding of both nuclear structure and quark-gluon plasma physics.

## Key claim
High-energy nuclear collisions can reveal the intrinsic shapes of atomic nuclei through an "imaging-by-smashing" approach, but the extracted shape parameters are effective quantities that reflect how the many-body wavefunction influences the dynamical collision process, not necessarily identical to shapes measured by low-energy techniques.

## Method
The method involves three steps: first, reconstructing the initial quark-gluon plasma (QGP) geometry from final-state particle distributions using hydrodynamic models; second, mapping these initial conditions back to the intrinsic nuclear shape parameters (quadrupole deformation β₂ and triaxiality γ); third, comparing collisions of isobaric or isobar-like nuclei (similar mass but different shapes) to separate global deformation effects from quantum fluctuations. The STAR Collaboration demonstrated this by comparing ²³⁸U+²³⁸U collisions (deformed) with ¹⁹⁷Au+¹⁹⁷Au collisions (nearly spherical), using three moments of flow observables: ⟨v₂²⟩, ⟨(δpT)²⟩, and ⟨v₂²δpT⟩.

## Key numbers
- The nuclear crossing timescale is τ = 2R₀/Γ < 0.1 fm/c, where Γ > 100 is the Lorentz contraction factor
- Rotational degrees of freedom in deformed heavy nuclei have excitation energies around 0.1 MeV, corresponding to timescales of τ ∼ 10³–10⁴ fm/c
- Quantum fluctuations induce eccentricities scaling roughly as 1/√A, where A is the mass number
- In head-on collisions of strongly-deformed uranium nuclei, the deformation contribution (bₙ terms) can exceed 50% of the baseline fluctuation contribution for ⟨v₂²⟩ and ⟨(δpT)²⟩, and up to three times for ⟨v₂²δpT⟩
- The Monte-Carlo Glauber model used R₀ = 6.81 fm, a = 0.55 fm, and A = 238 for uranium simulations

## Context
This work extends the concept demonstrated by the STAR Collaboration (2024) extracting the shape of ²³⁸U from collision data, building on decades of heavy-ion physics that has constrained QGP properties (Busza 2018). The method contrasts with traditional low-energy techniques such as electron scattering, laser spectroscopy, and Coulomb excitation (Yang 2022), which provide time-averaged images and cannot directly probe neutron distributions. The approach enables future studies of shape coexistence (Garrett 2021), subbarrier nuclear fusion (Hagino 2012), and connections to fundamental symmetries including neutrinoless double-beta decay searches (Agostini 2022) and nuclear electric dipole moments (Spevak 1996).
