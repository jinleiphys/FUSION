# Unified equations of state for cold non-accreting neutron stars with Brussels-Montreal functionals. I. Role of symmetry energy

> The theory of the nuclear energy-density functional is used to provide a unified and thermodynamically consistent treatment of all regions of cold non-accreting neutron stars. In order to assess the impact of our lack of complete knowledge of the density dependence of the symmetry energy on the constitution and the global structure of neutron stars, we employ four different functionals. All of them were precision fitted to essentially all the nuclear-mass data with the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method and two different neutron-matter equations of state based on realistic nuclear forces. For each functional, we calculate the composition, the pressure-density relation, and the chemical potentials throughout the star. We show that uncertainties in the symmetry energy can significantly affect the theoretical results for the composition and global structure of neutron stars. To facilitate astrophysical applications, we construct analytic fits to our numerical results.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that uncertainties in the density dependence of the symmetry energy, particularly the symmetry coefficient J, significantly affect theoretical predictions for neutron star composition and global structure, but the authors note that they were unable to find acceptable nuclear-mass fits outside the range 29 MeV ≤ J ≤ 32 MeV using their generalized Skyrme functionals.

## Method

The authors employed four Brussels-Montreal energy-density functionals (BSk22, BSk24, BSk25, BSk26) fitted to nuclear mass data from the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation using the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method, with two different neutron-matter equations of state (APR and LS2) as constraints. For the outer crust they minimized the Gibbs free energy per nucleon at fixed pressure using nuclear mass tables, while for the inner crust they applied the fourth-order extended Thomas-Fermi plus Strutinsky integral (ETFSI) method with proton shell corrections and BCS pairing, and for the core they used homogeneous nuclear matter calculations.

## Key numbers

- Symmetry coefficient J values: BSk22 = 32.0 MeV, BSk24 = 30.0 MeV, BSk25 = 29.0 MeV, BSk26 = 30.0 MeV
- Symmetry-slope coefficient L values: BSk22 = 68.5 MeV, BSk24 = 46.4 MeV, BSk25 = 36.9 MeV, BSk26 = 37.5 MeV
- Root-mean-square deviations from 2408 measured nuclear masses (AME 2016): HFB-22 = 0.648 MeV, HFB-24 = 0.565 MeV, HFB-25 = 0.556 MeV, HFB-26 = 0.580 MeV
- Root-mean-square deviations for 286 neutron-rich nuclei: HFB-22 = 0.904 MeV, HFB-24 = 0.781 MeV, HFB-25 = 0.829 MeV, HFB-26 = 0.811 MeV
- Neutron drip occurs at mean baryon number density approximately 2.6 × 10⁻⁴ nucleons fm⁻³ (mass-energy density approximately 4.3 × 10¹¹ g cm⁻³)
- Incompressibility K_v values: BSk22 = 245.9 MeV, BSk24 = 245.5 MeV, BSk25 = 236.0 MeV, BSk26 = 240.8 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous unified neutron star calculations using functionals BSk19-21 (Pearson et al. 2011, 2012) by incorporating new functionals with varying symmetry energies and improved inner-crust calculations including pairing and proton drip for the first time. The paper enables more reliable modeling of neutron star structure and composition, and the analytic fits provided facilitate astrophysical applications such as supernova-core collapse, neutron-star mergers, and r-process nucleosynthesis calculations. The results are constrained by chiral effective field theory calculations (Tews et al. 2013) and recent gravitational-wave observations, which the authors note corroborate that the real neutron-matter equation of state is not stiffer than LS2.
