# Does the Sun have a Dark Disk?

> The Sun is not quite a perfect sphere, and its oblateness, thought to be induced through its rotation, has been measured using optical observations of its radius. Its gravitational quadrupole moment can then be deduced using solar models, or through helioseismology, and it can also be determined from measurements of its gravitational effects on Mercury's orbit. The various assessments do not appear to agree, with the most complete and precise orbital assessments being in slight excess of other determinations. This may speak to the existence of a non-luminous disk or ring, where we also note evidence for a circumsolar dust ring within Mercury's orbit from the Solar TErrestrial RElations Observatory (STEREO) mission. Historically, too, a protoplanetary disk may have been key to reconciling the Sun's metallicity with its neutrino yield. The distribution of the non-luminous mass within Mercury's orbit can modify the relative size of the optical and orbital quadrupole moments in different ways. We develop how we can use these findings to limit the mass of a dark disk, ring, or halo in the immediate vicinity of the Sun, and we note how future observational studies of the inner solar system can not only refine these constraints but also help to identify and to assess the mass of its dark-matter component.

## Key claim

The paper suggests that the slight excess of the gravitational quadrupole moment \(J_2^{\rm Orb}\) determined from Mercury's orbit over the helioseismological determination \(J_2^{\rm Heli}\) could indicate the presence of non-luminous matter (a dark disk, ring, or halo) within Mercury's orbit, rather than being solely attributable to systematic errors or unaccounted solar physics.

## Method

The authors compare three independent determinations of the solar gravitational quadrupole moment \(J_2\): (1) optical measurements of solar oblateness from the RHESSI instrument, converted to \(J_2^{\rm Opt}\) using the equipotential condition; (2) helioseismological inferences \(J_2^{\rm Heli}\) from SDO/HMI and SoHO/MDI oscillation data interpreted with CESAM and ASTEC solar evolution models; and (3) orbital determinations \(J_2^{\rm Orb}\) from MESSENGER spacecraft radio tracking data of Mercury's perihelion precession, analyzed within a parametrized post-Newtonian (PPN) framework assuming general relativity (\(\beta=\gamma=1\)). The differences between these \(J_2\) values are then used to constrain the possible mass and distribution of non-luminous matter within Mercury's orbit, considering scenarios including a circumsolar dust ring, a dark-matter halo, and a dark disk.

## Key numbers

*   \(J_2^{\rm Orb} = 2.2709 \pm 0.0044\) (in units of \(10^{-7}\)) from Genova et al. (2018) MESSENGER analysis assuming GR (\(\beta=1; \eta=0\)).
*   \(J_2^{\rm Heli} = 2.214 \pm 0.002\) (in units of \(10^{-7}\)) from SDO/HMI data using CESAM and ASTEC models (Mecheri and Meftah, 2021).
*   The difference \(J_2^{\rm Orb}|_{\beta=1; \eta=0} - J_2^{\rm Heli} = 0.057 \pm 0.006\) (in units of \(10^{-7}\)), increasing to \(\pm 0.020\) when Earth orbit uncertainties are included (Konopliv et al., 2020).
*   The circumsolar dust ring at Mercury's orbit has an estimated mass of \((1.02 - 4.05) \times 10^{12\pm (\approx 1)} \,\rm kg\) (Pokorny et al., 2023).
*   The local dark matter density is \(\rho_{\rm dm} = 0.4\,\rm GeV/cm^3\), corresponding to a mass of \(6\times 10^{11} \, \rm kg\) within Mercury's orbit.
*   The solar mass loss rate from MESSENGER data is \(\dot{\mu}/\mu = (-6.13 \pm 1.47)\times 10^{-14}\,\rm yr^{-1}\) (Genova et al., 2018).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing comparison of solar quadrupole moment determinations, first motivated by Dicke (1964) in the context of testing general relativity, by using the modern precision of MESSENGER data (Park et al., 2017; Genova et al., 2018) and helioseismology (Mecheri et al., 2004; Mecheri and Meftah, 2021). It builds on earlier constraints on non-luminous matter in the solar system from planetary ephemerides (Anderson et al., 1988; Pitjev and Pitjeva, 2013) and on the discovery of a circumsolar dust ring from STEREO (Stenborg et al., 2018). The analysis enables future observational studies of the inner solar system to distinguish between conventional non-luminous matter (dust, asteroids) and exotic dark-matter components, and to refine tests of general relativity (Will, 2018).
