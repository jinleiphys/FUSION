# Do we need to use regularization on the thermal part in the NJL model?

> The Nambu--Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model is one of the most useful tools to study non-perturbative strong interaction matter. Because it is a nonrenormalizable model, the choosing of regularization is a subtle issue. In this paper, we discuss one of the general things of the regularization in the NJL model, which is whether we need to use the regularization on the thermal part by evaluating the quark chiral condensate and thermal properties in the two-flavor NJL model. The calculations in this work include three regularization schemes that contain both gauge covariant and invariant schemes. We found no matter which regularization scheme we choose, it is necessary to use the regularization on the thermal part when calculating the chiral condensate related physics quantities and do not use the regularization on the thermal part when calculating the grand potential related physical quantities.

# Do we need to use regularization on the thermal part in the NJL model?

## Key claim
Whether regularization must be applied to the thermal part in the Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model depends on which physical quantity is being calculated: regularization on the thermal part is necessary for chiral condensate related quantities, but must not be used for grand potential related quantities, regardless of which regularization scheme (gauge covariant or gauge invariant) is chosen.

## Method
The authors evaluate the quark chiral condensate (constituent quark mass) and grand-potential-derived thermodynamic quantities (pressure, energy density, specific heat, speed of sound) in the two-flavor NJL model at zero chemical potential, using three regularization schemes: three-momentum hard cutoff, three-momentum soft cutoff, and Pauli-Villars regularization. For each scheme, calculations are performed both with and without applying the regularization to the thermal part of the grand potential, and the results are compared.

## Key numbers
- Hard cutoff parameters: $\Lambda = 653$ MeV, $m = 6$ MeV, $G_S = 2.14/\Lambda^2$
- Soft cutoff parameters: $\Lambda = 626.76$ MeV, $m = 6$ MeV, $G_S = 2.02/\Lambda^2$
- Pauli-Villars parameters: $\Lambda = 859$ MeV, $m = 6$ MeV, $G_S = 2.84/\Lambda^2$
- Critical temperature $T_0$ with hard cutoff: 195 MeV (with regularization on thermal part), 178 MeV (without)
- Critical temperature $T_0$ with soft cutoff: 202 MeV (with regularization on thermal part), 179 MeV (without)
- Critical temperature $T_0$ with Pauli-Villars: 172 MeV (with regularization on thermal part), 138 MeV (without)
- Without regularization on the thermal part, the constituent quark mass becomes smaller than the current quark mass ($m = 6$ MeV) at high temperature, producing a positive chiral condensate, an unphysical result
- Without regularization on the thermal part, the square of the speed of sound approaches the free Fermi gas value $v_s^2 = 1/3$ at high temperature; with regularization on the thermal part, it exceeds the speed of light at high temperature

## Context
This work systematically addresses a long-standing ambiguity in NJL model calculations, extending earlier observations by Yu (2015) that the choice of whether to regularize the thermal part can reverse the dependence of critical temperature on chiral chemical potential. The results reconcile two competing viewpoints: that the convergent thermal integral should not be regularized (to retain its full contribution) versus that regularization should apply uniformly for consistency. The authors connect their findings to lattice QCD results from Braguta (2014) and Dyson-Schwinger Equation studies by Xu (2015), and note that for quantities like net baryon fluctuations (which depend on derivatives of the grand potential) regularization should be omitted, while for meson masses (related to the chiral condensate) it should be applied.
