# Discrepancy in tidal deformability of GW170817 between the Advanced LIGO twin detectors

> We find that the Hanford and Livingston detectors of Advanced LIGO derive a distinct posterior probability distribution of binary tidal deformability tilde{Lambda} of the first binary-neutron-star merger GW170817. By analyzing public data of GW170817 with a nested-sampling engine and the default TaylorF2 waveform provided by the LALInference package, the probability distribution of the binary tidal deformability derived by the LIGO-Virgo detector network turns out to be determined dominantly by the Hanford detector. Specifically, by imposing the flat prior on tidal deformability of individual stars, symmetric 90% credible intervals of tilde{Lambda} are estimated to be 527^{+619}_{-345} with the Hanford detector, 927^{+522}_{-619} with the Livingston detector, and 455^{+668}_{-281} with the LIGO-Virgo detector network. Furthermore, the distribution derived by the Livingston detector changes irregularly when we vary the maximum frequency of the data used in the analysis. This feature is not observed for the Hanford detector. While they are all consistent, the discrepancy and irregular behavior suggest that an in-depth study of noise properties might improve our understanding of GW170817 and future events.

## Key claim

The posterior probability distribution of the binary tidal deformability \(\tilde{\Lambda}\) of GW170817 derived from the Livingston detector data is distinct from that derived from the Hanford detector data, and the Livingston-derived distribution behaves irregularly when the maximum analysis frequency is varied, suggesting that the high-frequency Livingston data may have been corrupted by noise, even though the 90% credible intervals are nominally consistent between the two detectors.

## Method

The authors performed Bayesian parameter estimation of GW170817 using public data from the Hanford detector alone, the Livingston detector alone, and the combined Hanford-Livingston-Virgo (HLV) network. They employed the nested sampling engine in the LALInference package with the restricted post-Newtonian TaylorF2 waveform approximant, a flat prior on individual tidal deformabilities in \([0,5000]\), and a low-spin prior, varying the maximum frequency \(f_\mathrm{max}\) of the data used in the analysis from 800 Hz to 2048 Hz.

## Key numbers

- Symmetric 90% credible interval of \(\tilde{\Lambda}\) for the Hanford-only data: \(527^{+619}_{-345}\) at \(f_\mathrm{max} = 2048\) Hz.
- Symmetric 90% credible interval of \(\tilde{\Lambda}\) for the Livingston-only data: \(927^{+522}_{-619}\) at \(f_\mathrm{max} = 2048\) Hz.
- Symmetric 90% credible interval of \(\tilde{\Lambda}\) for the HLV network data: \(455^{+668}_{-281}\) at \(f_\mathrm{max} = 2048\) Hz.
- Signal-to-noise ratios: 18.8 for Hanford, 26.8 for Livingston, and 32.6 for HLV.
- The Livingston-only median \(\tilde{\Lambda}\) value turns from decreasing to increasing at \(f_\mathrm{max} \approx 1100\) Hz, while the Hanford-only median decreases monotonically with increasing \(f_\mathrm{max}\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of GW170817 by the LIGO-Virgo Collaboration (2017, 2019) and independent analyses such as De et al. (2018), which reported combined-detector results, by separately examining the posterior distributions from each Advanced LIGO detector. The discrepancy and irregular frequency dependence suggest that an in-depth study of noise properties, particularly a glitch in the Livingston data about half a second before merger noted by the LIGO-Virgo Collaboration (2017), may improve understanding of GW170817 and future events, and that secure parameter estimation will benefit from third detectors such as Advanced Virgo or KAGRA.
