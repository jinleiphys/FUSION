# Model reduction methods for nuclear emulators

> The field of model order reduction (MOR) is growing in importance due to its ability to extract the key insights from complex simulations while discarding computationally burdensome and superfluous information. We provide an overview of MOR methods for the creation of fast & accurate emulators of memory- and compute-intensive nuclear systems. As an example, we describe how "eigenvector continuation" is a special case of a much more general and well-studied MOR formalism for parameterized systems. We continue with an introduction to the Ritz and Galerkin projection methods that underpin many such emulators, while pointing to the relevant MOR theory and its successful applications along the way. We believe that this will open the door to broader applications in nuclear physics and facilitate communication with practitioners in other fields.

## Key claim

The paper argues that eigenvector continuation (EC), recently introduced to nuclear physics for fast emulation of bound-state and scattering calculations, is actually a special case of the much more general and well-studied parametric model order reduction (PMOR) formalism, specifically the reduced basis method, which has been known in applied mathematics and engineering for decades.

## Method

The authors provide a pedagogical overview of projection-based model order reduction methods, focusing on the Ritz (variational) and Galerkin projection techniques that underpin nuclear emulators. They derive reduced-order models for eigenvalue problems and differential equations by projecting high-dimensional operators onto low-dimensional subspaces spanned by "snapshot" solutions computed at selected parameter values, exploiting affine parameter dependence to enable efficient online-offline decomposition.

- The affine parameter decomposition H(θ) = Σ_n h_n(θ) H_n (Eq. 3) permits projecting each H_n once in the offline stage, enabling rapid reconstruction of the reduced Hamiltonian for each new parameter value in the online stage

## Context

This work extends the nuclear physics community's understanding of eigenvector continuation (Frame 2017, Konig 2019, Ekstrom 2019) by connecting it to the mature model order reduction literature, including the reduced basis method (Machiels 2000, Horger 2017) and projection-based PMOR techniques (Benner 2015, Quarteroni 2016). It enables nuclear physicists to leverage decades of MOR theory, error bounds, and algorithmic developments (such as greedy sampling and hyperreduction methods) for broader applications including density functional theory, scattering calculations, and uncertainty quantification.
