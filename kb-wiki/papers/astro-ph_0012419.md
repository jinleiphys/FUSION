# Possible evidence for a variable fine structure constant from QSO absorption lines: motivations, analysis and results

> An experimental search for variation in the fundamental coupling constants is strongly motivated by modern high-energy physics theories. Comparison of quasar absorption line spectra with laboratory spectra provides a sensitive probe for variability of the fine structure constant, alpha, over cosmological time-scales. We have previously developed and applied a new method providing an order of magnitude gain in precision over previous optical astrophysical constraints. Here we extend that work by including new quasar spectra of damped Lyman-alpha absorption systems. We also re-analyse our previous lower redshift data and confirm our initial results. The constraints on alpha come from simultaneous fitting of absorption lines of subsets of the following species: Mg I, Mg II, Al II, Al III, Si II, Cr II, Fe II, Ni II and Zn II. We present a detailed description of our methods and results based on an analysis of 49 quasar absorption systems (towards 28 QSOs) covering the redshift range 0.5 < z < 3.5. There is statistical evidence for a smaller alpha at earlier epochs: da/a = (-0.72 +/- 0.18) * 10^{-5}. The new and original samples are independent but separately yield consistent and significant non-zero values of da/a. We summarise the results of a thorough investigation of systematic effects published in a companion paper. The value we quote above is the raw value, not corrected for any of these systematic effects. The only significant systematic effects so far identified, if removed from our data, would lead to a more significant deviation of da/a from zero.

## Key claim
There is statistical evidence for a smaller fine structure constant at earlier epochs, with the raw (uncorrected for systematic effects) value of Δα/α = (-0.72 ± 0.18) × 10⁻⁵. The authors emphasize that this is the raw value and that the only significant systematic effects identified so far, if removed, would lead to a more significant deviation from zero.

## Method
The many-multiplet (MM) method was applied to 49 quasar absorption systems (towards 28 QSOs) covering redshifts 0.5 < z < 3.5. This method compares the wavelength separations between resonance transitions of different ionic species (Mg I, Mg II, Al II, Al III, Si II, Cr II, Fe II, Ni II, and Zn II) rather than relying solely on alkali-doublet (AD) spacings, providing an order of magnitude gain in precision. The analysis included new quasar spectra of damped Lyman-alpha absorption systems and a re-analysis of previous lower-redshift data from Webb et al. (1999).

## Key numbers
- Δα/α = (-0.72 ± 0.18) × 10⁻⁵, the raw value from the combined sample of 49 absorption systems, not corrected for systematic effects.
- The previous result from Webb et al. (1999), using 30 Keck/HIRES absorption spectra containing Mg II and Fe II, was Δα/α = (-1.09 ± 0.36) × 10⁻⁵ over redshifts ~0.6–1.6.
- The new and original samples are independent but separately yield consistent non-zero values of Δα/α.
- The redshift range covered is 0.5 < z < 3.5.

## Context
This work extends the many-multiplet method introduced by Dzuba, Flambaum & Webb (1999a,b) and Webb et al. (1999), which itself provided an order of magnitude improvement over the alkali-doublet method used by earlier authors such as Varshalovich, Panchuk & Ivanchik (1996) and Varshalovich, Potekhin & Ivanchik (2000). The results are consistent with constraints from radio spectra of QSO absorption clouds (Drinkwater et al. 1998; Murphy et al. 2001d) and from the Oklo natural reactor (Damour & Dyson 1996; Fujii et al. 2000), though those probe different redshifts and environments. This detection motivates further searches for variation in fundamental constants and provides constraints on theories of extra dimensions and scalar field models such as those of Bekenstein (1982) and Barrow & Magueijo (2000).
