# The relevance of nuclear reactions for Standard Solar Models construction

> The fundamental processes by which nuclear energy is generated in the Sun have been known for many years. However, continuous progress in areas such as neutrino experiments, stellar spectroscopy and helioseismic data and techniques requires ever more accurate and precise determination of nuclear reaction cross sections, a fundamental physical input for solar models. In this work, we review the current status of (standard) solar models and present a detailed discussion on the relevance of nuclear reactions for detailed predictions of solar properties. In addition, we also provide an analytical model that helps understanding the relation between nuclear cross sections, neutrino fluxes and the possibility they offer for determining physical characteristics of the solar interior. The latter is of particular relevance in the context of the conundrum posed by the solar composition, the solar abundance problem, and in the light of the first ever direct detection of solar CN neutrinos recently obtained by the Borexino collaboration. Finally, we present a short list of wishes about the precision with which nuclear reaction rates should be determined to allow for further progress in our understanding of the Sun.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while standard solar models (SSMs) are well-established, the persistent solar abundance problem, the failure of low-metallicity SSMs to match helioseismic constraints, can potentially be resolved by improved nuclear reaction rate determinations combined with better opacity calculations, but only if nuclear cross sections are known to approximately 5% precision or better.

## Method

The authors review the current status of SSMs constructed with the Barcelona 2016 (B16) framework, which uses the FreeEOS equation of state, OP atomic radiative opacities, and nuclear reaction rates from Solar Fusion II with updates for the p(p,e+nu_e)d, 7Be(p,gamma)8B, and 14N(p,gamma)15O reactions. They present an analytical model relating nuclear cross sections to neutrino fluxes and solar interior properties, and perform numerical SSM calculations comparing high-metallicity (GS98-based) and low-metallicity (AGSS09-based) compositions against helioseismic data.

## Key numbers

* Central temperature of the present Sun: Tc = 15.6 x 10^6 K, central density: rhoc = 150 g cm^-3
* Solar age: tausun = 4.57 Gyr; solar luminosity: Lsun = 3.8418 x 10^33 erg s^-1; solar radius: Rsun = 6.9598 x 10^10 cm
* Surface helium abundance Ysur = 0.2426 +/- 0.0059 (B16-HZ) versus helioseismic value 0.2485 +/- 0.0035; Ysur = 0.2317 +/- 0.0059 (B16-LZ)
* Convective envelope depth Rcz/Rsun = 0.7116 +/- 0.0048 (B16-HZ) versus helioseismic 0.713 +/- 0.001; Rcz/Rsun = 0.7223 +/- 0.0053 (B16-LZ)
* Surface metal-to-hydrogen ratio (Z/X)sun = 0.02292 (HZ) versus 0.01780 (LZ), a 29% difference
* Opacity uncertainty modeled as 2% at solar center and 7% at base of convective envelope
* Energy released per synthesized 4He nucleus: Q = 26.7 MeV; neutrinos carry away about 0.6 MeV on average
* Central helium mass fraction Ycen = 0.6328 +/- 0.0053 (B16-HZ) and 0.6217 +/- 0.0062 (B16-LZ)

## Context

This work extends the long history of SSM development from Bahcall (1982) through Solar Fusion I (Adelberger 1998) and Solar Fusion II (Adelberger 2011), and addresses the solar abundance problem identified by Basu (2004) and Bahcall (2005). The paper builds on the B16 SSMs of Vinyoles (2017) and discusses how future CN neutrino measurements by Borexino can determine the solar core C+N abundance, potentially resolving the composition dispute between GS98 and AGSS09 abundances. The analytical model enables understanding of how nuclear cross section uncertainties propagate to neutrino flux predictions, which is critical for interpreting the first direct detection of solar CN neutrinos by Borexino.
