# Diffusion model approach to simulating electron-proton scattering events

> Generative AI is a fast-growing area of research offering various avenues for exploration in high-energy nuclear physics. In this work, we explore the use of generative models for simulating electron-proton collisions relevant to experiments like CEBAF and the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC). These experiments play a critical role in advancing our understanding of nucleons and nuclei in terms of quark and gluon degrees of freedom. The use of generative models for simulating collider events faces several challenges such as the sparsity of the data, the presence of global or event-wide constraints, and steeply falling particle distributions. In this work, we focus on the implementation of diffusion models for the simulation of electron-proton scattering events at EIC energies. Our results demonstrate that diffusion models can accurately reproduce relevant observables such as momentum distributions and correlations of particles, momentum sum rules, and the leading electron kinematics, all of which are of particular interest in electron-proton collisions. Although the sampling process is relatively slow compared to other machine learning architectures, we find diffusion models can generate high-quality samples. We foresee various applications of our work including inference for nuclear structure, interpretable generative machine learning, and searches of physics beyond the Standard Model.

## Key claim
Diffusion models can generate high-quality simulated electron-proton scattering events that reproduce relevant observables including momentum distributions, particle correlations, momentum sum rules, and leading electron kinematics, but the agreement within 1-sigma statistical uncertainties was not attainable and the model has difficulties near phase-space edges.

## Method
The authors trained a diffusion model with a U-Net architecture on 10^6 Pythia8-simulated neutral-current electron-proton scattering events at sqrt(s)=105 GeV with Q^2>25 GeV^2, representing events as 64x64 pixel images with three color channels (pi+, K+, e-). They applied a custom bijective rescaling of the momentum variable z to handle steeply falling distributions near both kinematic endpoints, used a hybrid loss function combining simplified mean-squared error and variational lower bound terms, and employed a cosine variance schedule with 500 diffusion steps.

## Key numbers
- Center-of-mass energy: sqrt(s)=105 GeV, chosen as representative for the future Electron-Ion Collider
- Lower cut on photon virtuality: Q^2>25 GeV^2 to exclude photoproduction events
- Image resolution: 64x64 pixels (default choice)
- Average sparsity of images: 99.95 +/- 0.02% empty pixels for 64x64 images including all particle species
- Number of diffusion steps: 500
- Training dataset size: 10^6 images
- Batch size: 8
- Learning rate: 10^-4 (AdamW optimizer)
- Hybrid loss hyperparameter lambda: 0.001
- Time to generate 10^6 samples: O(hours) on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU

## Context
This work extends the application of diffusion models from calorimeter shower simulation (Mikuni 2022, Mikuni 2023, Amram 2023, Buhmann 2023) and jet simulation using point clouds (Leigh 2023, Butter 2023, Acosta 2023) to full collider events, specifically addressing the unique challenges of electron-proton collisions. The approach contrasts with earlier generative model work using GANs for collider event simulation (deOliveira 2017, Paganini 2017, Alanazi 2020) and normalizing flows (Krause 2021). The results enable future applications including inference for nuclear structure, interpretable generative machine learning, searches for physics beyond the Standard Model, and potential integration with parton shower development and QCD global analysis frameworks.
