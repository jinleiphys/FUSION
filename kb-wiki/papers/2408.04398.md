# Speed of sound and isothermal compressibility in a magnetized quark matter with anomalous magnetic moment of quarks

> We study the characteristics of quark matter under the influence of a background magnetic field with anomalous magnetic moment (AMM) of quarks at finite temperature and quark chemical potential in the framework of Polyakov loop extended Nambu Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) model. In presence of a magnetic field, the speed of sound and isothermal compressibility become anisotropic with respect to the direction of the background magnetic field, splitting into parallel and perpendicular directions with respect to the magnetic field. Though the qualitative nature of parallel and perpendicular components of squared speed of sound appear similar, they differ in magnitude at lower values of temperature. The parallel and perpendicular components of isothermal compressibility decrease with increasing temperature, indicating a trend towards increased incompressible strongly interacting matter. On inclusion of the AMM of quarks, the perpendicular component of isothermal compressibility becomes greater than the parallel component. Additionally, we investigate the quark number susceptibility normalized by its value at zero magnetic field, which may indicate the presence of magnetic fields in the system.

## Key claim

The speed of sound and isothermal compressibility in magnetized quark matter become anisotropic with respect to the direction of the background magnetic field, splitting into parallel and perpendicular components; the inclusion of the anomalous magnetic moment (AMM) of quarks causes the perpendicular component of isothermal compressibility to become greater than the parallel component, a result that depends on the specific values of temperature and magnetic field strength.

## Method

The authors employ the Polyakov loop extended Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) model with two quark flavors in a constant background magnetic field, incorporating the anomalous magnetic moment (AMM) of quarks. The thermodynamic potential is derived using the imaginary time formalism of finite-temperature field theory, with a smooth cutoff regularization to handle divergences. The constituent quark mass, Polyakov loop expectation values, and all thermodynamic quantities are obtained by solving the coupled gap equations self-consistently, and the speed of sound and isothermal compressibility are computed from derivatives of pressure, energy density, entropy density, quark number density, and magnetization with respect to temperature and chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- The chiral transition temperature at zero magnetic field and zero chemical potential is approximately \(T^{\rm ch} \sim 230\ \text{MeV}\).
- The model parameters are fixed using the pion decay constant \(f_\pi = 92.4\ \text{MeV}\), the pion mass \(m_\pi = 138\ \text{MeV}\), and the chiral condensate \(\langle \bar{u}u \rangle^{1/3} = -245.7\ \text{MeV}\) at zero temperature and density.
- The smooth cutoff parameter is \(\Lambda = 620\ \text{MeV}\) and the current quark mass is \(m = 5.5\ \text{MeV}\).
- The AMM values used are \(\kappa_u = 0.29016\ \text{GeV}^{-1}\) and \(\kappa_d = 0.35986\ \text{GeV}^{-1}\).
- Representative magnetic field strengths studied are \(eB = 0.05\ \text{GeV}^2\) and \(eB = 0.10\ \text{GeV}^2\).
- Up to 2000 Landau levels are included for convergence in the finite magnetic field calculations.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of magnetized quark matter using the PNJL model, particularly building on the authors' own earlier work on nuclear matter in a magnetic field (Mondal 2023) and on anisotropic pressure in magnetized quark matter with AMM (Chaudhuri 2022). It contrasts with lattice QCD results that show inverse magnetic catalysis (IMC) at higher temperatures (Bali 2011, Bali 2012), which the inclusion of AMM helps to reproduce in effective model calculations. The results enable further investigations of the equation of state for compact stars such as neutron stars and hybrid stars, where anisotropic pressure and the speed of sound affect mass-radius relations, tidal deformability, and gravitational wave frequencies.
