# Hyperon--anti-hyperon polarization asymmetry in relativistic heavy-ion collisions as an interplay between chiral and helical vortical effects

> We argue that the enhancement in the spin polarization of anti-hyperons compared to the polarization of the hyperons in noncentral relativistic heavy-ion collisions arises as a result of an interplay between the chiral and helical vortical effects. The chiral vortical effect generates the axial current of quarks along the vorticity axis while the recently found helical vortical effect generates the helicity flow -- the projection of the quark's polarization vector onto its momentum -- along the same axis. For massless fermions, the helical charge corresponds to a difference in the contributions of particles and anti-particles to the axial charge. Assuming that the spin of light quarks transfers to the strange quarks via the vector kaon states ("the spin-vector dominance"), we are able to describe the ratio of the (anti)hyperon spin polarizations, obtained by the STAR group, without fitting parameters. We also argue that the helical vortical effect dominates over the chiral vortical effect and the chiral magnetic effect in the generation of the electric current.

## Key claim

The observed enhancement of anti-hyperon spin polarization over hyperon spin polarization in noncentral relativistic heavy-ion collisions arises from the interplay between the chiral vortical effect and the helical vortical effect, without requiring any fitting parameters to describe the ratio of polarizations measured by the STAR collaboration.

## Method

The authors model the spin polarization of light quarks in a vortical quark-gluon plasma using the axial current from the chiral vortical effect and the helicity current from the helical vortical effect, with conductivities evaluated at chemical freeze-out. They assume that polarized light quarks transfer their spin to strange quarks via intermediate vector kaon resonances ("spin-vector dominance"), and that the strange quarks then determine the hyperon and anti-hyperon polarizations. The ratio of anti-hyperon to hyperon polarization is computed from the ratio of the sum and difference of the chiral and helical vortical conductivities, eliminating all unknown kinematic and dynamical factors.

## Key numbers

* The chiral vortical conductivity is given by σ_A = T²/6 + μ_B²/(18π²), where T is temperature and μ_B is baryon chemical potential.
* The helical vortical conductivity is given by σ_H = (2 ln 2)/(3π²) μ_B T + O(μ_B³/T) in the high-temperature limit.
* The freeze-out temperature is parameterized as T(μ_B) = 0.166(2) GeV - 0.139(16) GeV⁻¹ μ_B² - 0.053(21) GeV⁻³ μ_B⁴.
* The baryon chemical potential as a function of collision energy is μ_B(√s) = 1.308(28) GeV / (1 + 0.273(8) GeV⁻¹ √s).
* At √s_NN = 200 GeV, the predicted asymmetry in anti-hyperon/hyperon polarizations is less than 10%.
* The decay parameters for Λ and anti-Λ are α_Λ = -α_anti-Λ = 0.642 ± 0.013.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions beyond the chiral vortical effect alone, which was first identified by Vilenkin (1979) in the context of rotating black holes. The helical vortical effect was recently introduced by Ambrus (2019) and provides a mechanism for the polarization splitting that previous explanations attributed to magnetic fields (Becattini, 2016) or different freeze-out conditions (Vitiuk, 2019). The prediction enables experimental tests at RHIC and LHC energies, and the authors argue that at √s_NN = 200 GeV, the helical vortical effect dominates over both the chiral vortical effect and the chiral magnetic effect in generating electric currents.
