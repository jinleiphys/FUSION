# Effective-Range Corrections to the Proton-Proton Fusion Rate

## Key claim
When the renormalization mass is taken well above the characteristic energies of the system, the next-to-leading order effective-range correction gives a fusion rate eight percent below the standard value from nuclear potential models; however, an unknown counterterm at this order prevents an absolute prediction of the rate.

## Method
The authors use the effective field theory of Kaplan, Savage and Wise without explicit pions, including the dimension-eight derivative coupling \(C_2\) that determines the effective ranges of scattering amplitudes. Coulomb effects are included non-perturbatively using a functional equation for the Coulomb Green's function, and the PDS regularization scheme is employed to handle ultraviolet divergences. The reduced matrix element \(\Lambda(0)\) for proton-proton fusion at zero energy is computed to next-to-leading order by expanding the full result to first order in the deuteron effective-range coupling \(C_2^d\).

## Key numbers
- Deuteron bound-state momentum: \(\gamma = 45.7\) MeV.
- Spin-triplet \(pn\) effective range: \(\rho_d = 1.76\) fm.
- Proton-proton scattering length: \(a_p = -7.82\) fm.
- Proton-proton effective range: \(\rho_p = 2.79\) fm.
- Leading-order reduced matrix element: \(\Lambda_0(0) = 2.51\), giving \(\Lambda_0^2(0) = 6.30\), which is 10% below the accepted value \(\Lambda_{tot}^2(0) = 7.0 \pm 0.05\) from complete potential-model calculations.
- Next-to-leading order reduced matrix element (for \(\mu \gg \gamma\)): \(\Lambda(0) = 2.54\), a 1.4% addition to the leading-order result, corresponding to a rate 8% below the accepted value from nuclear potential models.
- Expansion parameter: \(\gamma\rho_d = 0.41\).

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous leading-order calculation of proton-proton fusion (Kong and Ravndal, 1999) using the effective field theory of Kaplan, Savage and Wise (1998). The result is compared to the effective-range approximation of Bahcall and May (1969) and to the most complete potential-model calculations of Bahcall and Kamionkowski (1995). The next-to-leading order correction is surprisingly small due to an almost total cancellation between the two effective-range contributions, and the authors note that reproducing higher-order effective-range effects would require including even higher-order interactions with new unknown counterterms, making it difficult for this effective theory to compete with the more accurate potential-model results.
