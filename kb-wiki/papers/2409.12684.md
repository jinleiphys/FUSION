# Detecting Hyperons in neutron stars -- a machine learning approach

> We present a neural network classification model for detecting the presence of hyperonic degrees of freedom in neutron stars. The models take radii and/or tidal deformabilities as input and give the probability for the presence of hyperons in the neutron star composition. Different numbers of observations and different levels of uncertainty in the neutron star properties are tested. The models have been trained on a dataset of well-calibrated microscopic equations of state of neutron star matter based on a relativistic mean-field formalism. Real data and data generated from a different description of hyperonic matter are used to test the performance of the models.

## Key claim

The authors present a neural network classification model that can detect the presence of hyperonic degrees of freedom in neutron stars using radii and/or tidal deformabilities as input, but the model's performance depends on the number of observations, the level of input noise, and the specific combination of observables used; the abstract does not specify that the model achieves only moderate classification accuracy under realistic noise conditions.

## Method

The authors trained feed-forward neural networks on a dataset of 17,810 nucleonic and 18,728 hyperonic equations of state generated within a relativistic mean-field formalism with Bayesian constraints from nuclear saturation properties, chiral effective field theory, and the requirement of supporting two-solar-mass neutron stars. Mock observational data were created by sampling mass-radius and mass-tidal deformability points from the TOV solutions with added Gaussian noise at three levels (noiseless, small noise, and realistic noise matching current observational uncertainties), using 5, 10, or 15 simulated neutron stars per equation of state. The models output a binary classification probability for the presence of hyperons, with training performed using binary cross-entropy loss and the sigmoid activation function.

## Key numbers

- Training dataset: 17,810 nucleonic equations of state and 18,728 hyperonic equations of state, balanced to 17,810 each for a total of 35,620 equations of state.
- Three noise levels tested: noiseless (sigma_R = 0 km, sigma_M = 0 solar masses); small noise (sigma_R = 0.2 km, sigma_M = 0.1 solar masses); realistic noise (sigma_R = 0.626 km, sigma_M = 0.136 solar masses).
- For tidal deformability noise: small noise used 0.5 times the training standard deviation at each mass (e.g., 130.05 at 1.4 solar masses); realistic noise used 2 times that standard deviation.
- Input dimensions: 10, 20, or 30 for radius-only or tidal-deformability-only datasets (corresponding to 5, 10, or 15 neutron stars); 20, 40, or 60 for combined radius and tidal deformability datasets.
- Training used 20 mock observations per equation of state (n_s = 20); testing used 1 mock observation per equation of state (n_s = 1).

## Context

This work extends the application of machine learning to neutron star composition inference, building on prior Bayesian inference frameworks for the equation of state (Landry 2018, Raaijmakers 2021, Huth 2021) and previous neural network studies mapping neutron star observations to properties like speed of sound and proton fraction (Carvalho 2023). The approach directly addresses the hyperon puzzle, where hyperon softening of the equation of state must be reconciled with two-solar-mass neutron star observations (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013, Fonseca 2021). The method enables future classification of neutron star composition using upcoming observational data from missions such as eXTP, STROBE-X, and the Square Kilometer Array.
