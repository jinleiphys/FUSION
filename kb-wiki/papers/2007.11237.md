# Comment on "Quasielastic lepton scattering and back-to-back nucleons in the short-time approximation", by S. Pastore et al

> The article of Pastore et al, while proposing an interesting and potentially useful approach for the generalisation of Quantum Monte Carlo techniques to the treatment of the nuclear electromagnetic response, features an incorrect and misleading discussion of y-scaling. # Comment on "Quasielastic lepton scattering and back-to-back nucleons in the short-time approximation", by S. Pastore et al.

## Key claim
Contrary to the assertion by Pastore et al., y-scaling in the transverse channel is accidental, not fundamental, because the response to transversely polarised virtual photons receives sizeable contributions from non-scaling processes in which the momentum transfer is shared between two nucleons. The authors' conclusion that y-scaling is preserved even in the presence of mechanisms other than single-nucleon knockout does not account for the fact that q-independence and y-scaling are distinct properties that do not necessarily imply one another.

## Method
The comment analyzes the definition of the scaling variable y from conservation of energy in the lab frame under the assumption that momentum transfer is absorbed by only one nucleon, and contrasts this with processes involving two-nucleon currents where the momentum transfer is shared. The author examines the formalism of the scaling function F(y) as the q → ∞ limit of the reduced nuclear cross section, and reviews the historical scaling analysis of longitudinal and transverse responses from Saclay data (Finn et al., 1980s) and more recent work on light nuclei (Carlson et al.).

## Key numbers
- The scaling variable y is defined from the relation ω + M_A = √(m² + (y+q)²) + √((M_A - m + E_thr)² + y²), where M_A is target mass, m is nucleon mass, and E_thr is the nucleon emission threshold.
- In deuteron, the relation between scaling function and momentum distribution takes the form n(k) = -(1/2π)(1/y)(dF(y)/dy) evaluated at |y| = k.
- The analysis of Finn et al. (1980s) of carbon responses revealed a significant excess of strength in the transverse channel that did not spoil the scaling behaviour at y < 0, leading to two distinct q-independent functions F_L(y) and F_T(y).

## Context
This comment corrects the discussion of y-scaling in Pastore et al. (2020), which proposed the Short Time Approximation (STA) for extending Quantum Monte Carlo techniques to nuclear electromagnetic response. The work extends the earlier scaling analyses of Finn et al. (1980s) on carbon and Carlson et al. on light nuclei, which already established that meaningful scaling functions providing information on initial-state dynamics can only be obtained from the longitudinal response. The author notes that accidental y-scaling is long known to occur in processes ranging from electron-nucleus scattering to neutron scattering from liquid helium (Negele). On the constructive side, after removal of excess transverse strength from two-nucleon currents, the longitudinal and transverse responses from Pastore et al. could enable a fully consistent study of scaling function universality, valuable for analyzing charged-current neutrino-nucleus interactions in accelerator-based neutrino oscillation searches.
