# Nuclear mass predictions with machine learning reaching the accuracy required by $r$-process studies

> Nuclear masses are predicted with the Bayesian neural networks by learning the mass surface of even-even nuclei and the correlation energies to their neighbouring nuclei. By keeping the known physics in various sophisticated mass models and performing the delicate design of neural networks, the proposed Bayesian machine learning (BML) mass model achieves an accuracy of $84$~keV, which crosses the accuracy threshold of the $100$~keV in the experimentally known region. It is also demonstrated the corresponding uncertainties of mass predictions are properly evaluated, while the uncertainties increase by about $50$~keV each step along the isotopic chains towards the unknown region. The shell structures in the known region are well described and several important features in the unknown region are predicted, such as the new magic numbers around $N = 40$, the robustness of $N = 82$ shell, the quenching of $N = 126$ shell, and the smooth separation energies around $N = 104$.

## Key claim

The proposed Bayesian machine learning (BML) mass model achieves an accuracy of 84 keV in the experimentally known region, crossing the 100 keV threshold required for r-process studies, but this result depends on the specific design choices of training on the smoother mass surface of even-even nuclei and separation energies to neighboring nuclei, and on using eight different mass models as inputs.

## Method

The authors trained nine separate Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) to cover the whole nuclear chart: one for the mass surface of even-even nuclei and eight for the correlation energies (separation energies) connecting even-even nuclei to their odd-A and odd-odd neighbors. The network outputs were the discrepancies between experimental masses and predictions from eight existing mass models (BW2, KTUY, FRDM12, WS4, RMF, HFB-31, Bhagwat, and DZ28), and the network inputs included Z, N, and the microscopic correction energy from each model. The final mass prediction was taken as the weighted mean of the eight individual BNN predictions, with weights given by the inverse square of each model's uncertainty.

## Key numbers

* Mass prediction accuracy (rms deviation) of 84 keV with respect to experimental data in AME2016, crossing the 100 keV threshold required for r-process studies.
* Uncertainties increase by about 50 keV each step along isotopic chains towards the unknown region.
* Rms deviations for separation and decay energies: 78 keV for S_n, 83 keV for S_p, 105 keV for S_{2n}, 111 keV for S_{2p}, and 99 keV for Q_beta.
* In a theory-to-theory validation using FRDM12 as pseudo-experimental data, the mass prediction accuracy in the learning region reached 93 keV.
* For the testing set of new nuclei in AME2020 with experimental uncertainties below 100 keV, the BML model achieved an rms deviation of 170 keV, compared to 245 keV for WS4+BNN-I4, 691 keV for FRDM2012, and 718 keV for HFB-31.
* The chi_nu factor for the BML model was 2.1 when comparing to AME2016 experimental data.

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian neural network approach for nuclear mass predictions previously developed by Niu (2018) and Neufcourt (2018), improving upon the earlier WS4+BNN-I4 model which achieved 184 keV accuracy. The BML model achieves the first global mass prediction accuracy below 100 keV, which is the threshold required for r-process studies as established by Mumpower (2016). The predictions enable quantitative r-process calculations by providing mass values for nuclei near N=82, N=126, and N=104, and the authors suggest that the smooth separation energies around N=104 favor the interpretation that the rare-earth peak originates from doubly asymmetric fission fragment distributions in the A=278 region, as proposed by Goriely (2013), rather than from a kink in separation energies.
