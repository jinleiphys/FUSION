# Discovering QCD-Coupled Axion Dark Matter with Polarization Haloscopes

> In the presence of QCD axion dark matter, atoms acquire time-dependent electric dipole moments. This effect gives rise to an oscillating current in a nuclear spin-polarized dielectric, which can resonantly excite an electromagnetic mode of a microwave cavity. We show that with existing technology such a "polarization haloscope" can explore orders of magnitude of new parameter space for QCD-coupled axions. If any cavity haloscope detects a signal from the axion-photon coupling, an upgraded polarization haloscope has the unique ability to test whether it arises from the QCD axion.

## Key claim

The authors show that a "polarization haloscope" using a nuclear spin-polarized dielectric inside a microwave cavity can probe orders of magnitude of new parameter space for QCD-coupled axion dark matter at GHz frequencies, but the projected sensitivity depends on uncertain nuclear, atomic, and material properties; the minimal QCD axion line is only reachable with a post-discovery setup that assumes significant technological enhancements.

## Method

The authors propose detecting QCD axion dark matter via the time-dependent electric dipole moments (EDMs) that axions induce in atoms, which produce an oscillating polarization current in a nuclear spin-polarized dielectric placed inside a microwave cavity. They estimate the signal power using the Dicke radiometer equation, considering the axion-induced atomic EDM amplitude, the density of polarized nuclei, the cavity geometry and quality factor, and the dielectric permittivity. The analysis identifies promising stable nuclei with octupole deformation (Dy-161, Eu-153, Gd-155) that yield atomic EDMs comparable to the neutron EDM, and evaluates material requirements including dielectric loss tangents and spin polarization techniques such as dynamic nuclear polarization.

## Key numbers

- Axion mass range motivated by standard misalignment production: m_a ~ (0.5 - 50) micro-eV, corresponding to frequencies m_a / 2 pi ~ (0.1 - 10) GHz
- Local axion field amplitude: theta_a ~ 4.3 x 10^{-19} cos(m_a t) for the QCD axion
- Neutron EDM: d_n ~ (2.4 x 10^{-3} e fm) x theta_a
- Atomic EDM for Dy-161: |d_A| ~ 1.2 x 10^{-3} e fm x theta_a
- Schiff moment estimates for Dy-161: <S_z> ~ 4.3 e fm^3 x theta_a; for Eu-153: 1.0 e fm^3 x theta_a; for Gd-155: 1.2 e fm^3 x theta_a
- Number density of rare earth atoms in candidate materials: 3 x 10^{22} cm^{-3}
- Dielectric permittivity assumed for projections: epsilon ~ 7
- Prototype setup: V = 100 L, Q = 10^5, B = 8 T, f_p ~ 5% for Eu-153
- Cubic meter setup: V = 1 m^3, Q = 10^6, f_p = 1
- Post-discovery setup: Q = 10^8, T = 10 mK, amplifier noise reduced by 3 dB via squeezing

## Context

This work extends the cavity haloscope concept (Sikivie 1983) by replacing the axion-photon coupling current with a current from axion-induced atomic EDMs, directly probing the axion-gluon coupling at GHz frequencies for the first time. It builds on proposals for detecting oscillating nuclear EDMs via nuclear magnetic resonance (Budker 2013, JacksonKimball 2017) and piezoelectric materials (Arvanitaki 2021), but targets higher frequencies relevant for standard misalignment production. The approach enables definitive discrimination between QCD axions and other axion-like particles if a signal is detected by conventional cavity haloscopes such as ADMX or CAPP.
