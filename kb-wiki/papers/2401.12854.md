# Optimization and Stabilization of Functional Renormalization Group Flows

> We revisit optimization of functional renormalization group flows by analyzing regularized loop integrals. This leads us to a principle, the Principle of Strongest Singularity, and a corresponding order relation which allows to order existing regularization schemes with respect to the stability of renormalization group flows. Moreover, the order relation can be used to construct new regulators in a systematic fashion. For studies of critical behavior, which require to follow renormalization group flows down to the deep infrared regime, such new regulators may turn out to be particularly useful. The general application of this principle is demonstrated with the aid of a scalar field theory which is solved over a wide range of scales with novel methods borrowed from numerical fluid dynamics.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the stability of functional renormalization group flows in theories with spontaneous symmetry breaking is governed by the strength of singularities in regularized loop integrals near the pole of the inverse propagator, formalized as the Principle of Strongest Singularity (POSS). This principle is conditional on regulators being comparable, meaning they share the same limiting value of the dimensionless inverse propagator minimum (normalized to -1).

## Method

The authors analyze the Wetterich equation within the Local Potential Approximation (LPA) for a scalar field theory, deriving an order relation based on the ratio of threshold functions \(l^{(d)}_1(\omega)\) as \(\omega \to -1\). They classify regulators by their shape functions \(r(y)\) and construct new regulators (e.g., the "strong divergent" SD regulator) designed to maximize the singularity strength, then test numerical stability by solving the flow equation for the effective potential down to deep infrared scales using numerical methods borrowed from fluid dynamics.

## Key numbers

- The normalization condition sets \(\min_{0 \leq y \leq \infty} P^2(y) = 1\), corresponding to \(\omega_{\mathrm{pole}} = -1\) for all comparable regulators.
- The Litim regulator \(r_{\mathrm{Litim}}(y) = (1/y - 1)\theta(1-y)\) is identified as the strongest singularity among regulators in set \(\mathcal{A}\) (where \(r' \leq 0\)).
- The exponential regulator \(r_{\mathrm{exp}}(y) = 1/(\exp(y)-1)\) does not yield divergent threshold functions at \(\omega_{\mathrm{pole}}\) in \(d > 2\) spacetime dimensions for \(m=1\), spoiling the self-healing property.
- The "strong divergent" (SD) regulator is constructed piecewise with parameters \(y_0\) and slope \(s\) to extend the region where \(P^2(y) = 1\) beyond the Litim regulator's support.

## Context

This work extends the optimization framework for functional RG flows pioneered by Litim (2001) and Pawlowski (2005), which focused on maximizing the gap in the propagator or minimizing the full two-point function. The POSS approach specifically addresses numerical stability in symmetry-breaking scenarios, complementing earlier PMS-based optimization by Canet (2002, 2003) and more recent conformal-constraint methods by Balog (2020) and Delamotte (2024). The new regulators enable solving RG flows to much smaller scales without numerical breakdown, which is particularly useful for studies of critical behavior in the deep infrared.
