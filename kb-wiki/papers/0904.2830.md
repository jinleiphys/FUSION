# New exact solutions of the standard pairing model for well-deformed nuclei

> A new step-by-step diagonalization procedure for evaluating exact solutions of the nuclear deformed mean-field plus pairing interaction model is proposed via a simple Bethe ansatz in each step from which the eigenvalues and corresponding eigenstates can be obtained progressively. This new approach draws upon an observation that the original one- plus two-body problem in a $k$-particle Hilbert subspace can be mapped unto a one-body grand hard-core boson picture that can be solved step by step with a simple Bethe ansatz known from earlier work. Based on this new procedure, it is further shown that the extended pairing model for deformed nuclei [Phys. Rev. Lett. 92, 112503 (2004) ] is similar to the standard pairing model with the first step approximation, in which only the lowest energy eigenstate of the standard pure pairing interaction part is taken into consideration. Our analysis show that the standard pairing model with the first step approximation displays similar pair structures of first few exact low-lying states of the model, which, therefore, provides a link between the two models.

# New exact solutions of the standard pairing model for well-deformed nuclei

## Key claim

The standard pairing model for deformed nuclei can be solved exactly through a step-by-step diagonalization procedure using a simple Bethe ansatz in each step, but the method is practical only for relatively small systems because it requires solving for all roots of a single-variable equation at each step. The extended pairing model (Pan et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 92, 112503, 2004) is shown to be similar to the standard pairing model under the first step approximation, where only the lowest quasi-spin term of the pairing interaction is retained.

## Method

The deformed mean-field plus standard pairing Hamiltonian is mapped onto a one-body grand hard-core boson problem in a k-pair Hilbert subspace. The Hamiltonian is then diagonalized step by step using a Bethe ansatz procedure that treats each quasi-spin component (labeled by quantum number q) of the pairing interaction sequentially, solving a single-variable nonlinear equation at each step rather than the coupled Richardson-Gaudin equations with k variables. For a system with p=10 levels and various numbers of pairs k, the authors compute eigenenergies and overlaps with exact solutions, comparing results at each step of the diagonalization.

## Key numbers

- For p=10 levels with pairing strength G=0.5 (arbitrary units) and single-particle energies ε₁=1.706, ε₂=2.754, ε₃=3.440, ε₄=4.349, ε₅=5.743, ε₆=6.604, ε₇=7.591, ε₈=8.959, ε₉=9.335, ε₁₀=10.125: the first step approximation yields ground state overlaps with exact eigenstates of 100% for k=1, 98% for k=2, 93% for k=3, 91% for k=4, 88% for k=5, 91% for k=6, 96% for k=7, 96% for k=8, 95% for k=9, and 100% for k=10.

- For the k=5 case at half-filling, after three diagonalization steps (including q=0,1,2 terms), the overlaps of the first four eigenstates with exact results reach 99%, with ground state energy 32.481 compared to exact 31.856.

- The relation between the extended pairing model coupling constant G_ex and the standard pairing strength G is G_ex = ((p-k)! k! (p-k+1) k / p!) G.

- For the sixth major shell (82-126) with G=0.2 MeV, the ground-state energy difference between the extended and standard pairing models in the first step approximation is largest at k=2 with a value of -36.3636 keV.

## Context

This work extends the Richardson-Gaudin exact solution method for the mean-field plus pairing Hamiltonian (Richardson and Gaudin, 1960s) by providing an alternative step-by-step diagonalization that avoids solving coupled nonlinear equations with k variables. The procedure builds on the extended pairing model of Pan et al. (2004), which introduced many-pair interaction terms and a simpler Bethe ansatz. The authors show that the extended pairing model reproduces the same excitation energies as the standard pairing model under the first step approximation, providing a link between the two approaches. This enables application to relatively large systems when only the first few low-lying states are needed, and suggests the method may generalize to other quantum many-body problems with non-commuting Hamiltonian terms.
