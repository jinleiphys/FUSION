# Bubble Nuclei, Neutron Stars and Quantum Billiards

> We briefly review the significance of quantum corrections in the total energy of systems with voids: bubble nuclei, atomic clusters and the inhomogeneous phase of neutron stars.

# Bubble Nuclei, Neutron Stars and Quantum Billiards

## Key claim
Quantum corrections (shell effects/Casimir energy), not Coulomb or surface energies, determine the optimal position of a bubble inside a finite Fermi system and give rise to long-range oscillatory interactions between multiple bubbles, with amplitudes comparable to the energy differences between competing phases in neutron star crust.

## Method
The authors use semiclassical methods (Gutzwiller trace formula) to compute shell correction energies for hard-wall potentials representing voids in Fermi systems, analyzing the dependence on bubble position, fractional volume, and inter-bubble distance. They evaluate the interaction energy between two spherical bubbles in homogeneous neutron matter as a function of separation, and extend the analysis to three or more bubbles to identify genuine many-body contributions.

## Key numbers
- Shell correction effects scale with particle number as ∝ N^(1/6), while surface deformation modes scale as ∝ N^(2/3).
- Energy differences between various pasta phases in neutron stars are of the order of keV per fm^3.
- The interaction energy between two bubbles (Eq. 1) contains terms proportional to cos[2k_F(a-2R)]/[2k_F(a-2R)] and sin[2k_F(a-2R)]/[4k_F^2(a-2R)^2], where R is bubble radius, a is center-to-center distance, and k_F is the Fermi wave number.

## Context
This work extends the liquid-drop and Thomas-Fermi treatments of bubble nuclei (Wheeler, 1950s) and the pasta phase of neutron stars (Pethick, 1990s) by incorporating quantum shell corrections previously noted as important by Wong and by Dietrich. It also connects to the Ruderman-Kittel interaction between impurities in a Fermi gas and to Sinai billiard models from quantum chaos (Bohigas). The results suggest that the inhomogeneous phase of neutron stars may have an extremely complicated, possibly disordered structure with multiple shape types coexisting, and that similar quantum corrections should be relevant for quark-gluon mixed phases at higher densities (Heiselberg, Glen).
