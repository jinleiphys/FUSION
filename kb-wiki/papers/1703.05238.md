# Non-exponential and oscillatory decays in quantum mechanics

> The quantum-mechanical theory of the decay of unstable states is revisited. We show that the decay is non-exponential both in the short-time and long-time limits using a more physical definition of the decay rate than the one usually used. We report results of numerical studies based on Winter's model that may elucidate qualitative features of exponential and non-exponential decay more generally. The main exponential stage is related to the formation of a radiating state that maintains the shape of its wave function with exponentially diminishing normalization. We discuss situations where the radioactive decay displays several exponents. The transient stages between different regimes are typically accompanied by interference of various contributions and resulting oscillations in the decay curve. The decay curve can be fully oscillatory in a two-flavor generalization of Winter's model with some values of the parameters. We consider the implications of that result for models of the oscillations reported by GSI.

## Key claim

The paper argues that quantum-mechanical decay is universally non-exponential at both short and long times, and that the exponential stage is only an approximation that holds when a "radiating state" with constant shape and exponentially diminishing normalization has formed; the authors use a more physical definition of survival probability (projection onto the parent state, not the initial wave function) to show that the decay rate must vanish at \(t=0\).

## Method

The authors revisit Winter's solvable one-dimensional delta-potential model, solving the time-dependent Schrödinger equation both via expansion in complex-energy poles and via direct numerical integration. They also employ the Feshbach projection formalism to construct an effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian that describes the intrinsic (parent) subspace coupled to continuum channels, and they analyze the analytic structure of the survival amplitude as a Fourier transform of the energy-dependent amplitude \(D(E)\).

## Key numbers

* In Winter's model with delta strength \(G=6\), the two lowest complex poles are at \(k_1 = 2.75794 - i\,0.140433\) (lifetime \(\tau_1 = 0.65\)) and \(k_2 = 5.71348 - i\,0.370148\) (lifetime \(\tau_2 = 0.12\)); the first pole dominates the exponential stage for any initial state.
* For the initial state \(n=2\) in Winter's model (\(G=6\)), the amplitude of the \(r=1\) pole component is \(M^{(1)}_{22}=0.0005\) and the \(r=2\) component is \(M^{(2)}_{22}=0.9917\), showing that the short-time decay follows \(\exp(-t/\tau_2)\) until the two modes interfere around \(t \approx 1\).
* The non-resonant (power-law) contribution to the survival amplitude at long times is \(M^{(NR)}_{nn'}(t) = \frac{1+i}{\pi^{5/2}\sqrt{2}} (1+G)^2 \frac{1}{n n'} \frac{1}{t^{3/2}}\) for Winter's model.
* The transition time to power-law decay is estimated as \(t_L \sim \tau \ln(E_0/\Gamma)\), where \(E_0\) is the resonance energy and \(\Gamma\) the width.
* For \(s\)-wave decay, the asymptotic survival probability scales as \(S(t) \propto 1/t^{3}\).

## Context

This work revisits the foundational quantum-mechanical theory of decay, extending the classic analysis of Winter (1961) and the general non-exponential decay theorems of Khalfin (1957, 1958), Newton (1961), and Fonda (1978). The authors connect their results to the effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian approach of Feshbach (1958) and to the super-radiance phenomenon described by Friedrich and Volya (2003). The paper is motivated in part by the reported observation of decay oscillations in neutrino emission at GSI, and it provides a two-flavor generalization of Winter's model that can produce fully oscillatory decay curves, offering a potential framework for interpreting those experimental claims.
