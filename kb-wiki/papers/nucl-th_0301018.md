# Complex Energy Method for Scattering Processes

> A method for solving few-body scattering equations is proposed and examined. The solution of the scattering equations at complex energies is analytically continued to get scattering T-matrix with real positive energy. Numerical examples document that the method works well for two-nucleon scattering and three-nucleon scattering, if the set of complex energies is properly chosen.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that solving few-body scattering equations at complex energies and then analytically continuing the T-matrix to real positive energies via the point method (a continued fraction expansion) yields accurate results for two- and three-nucleon scattering, provided the set of complex energies is properly chosen.

## Method

The authors solve the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for two-nucleon scattering and the Amado-Mitra-Faddeev-Lovelace (AMFL) type three-body equations at a discrete set of complex energies (E + iε). They then apply the point method (a continued fraction expansion) to analytically continue the resulting T-matrix elements from the complex energy points to the real energy axis (ε = 0). For the three-body case, complex ε values with imaginary parts larger than 1 MeV are used to ensure smooth integrands.

## Key numbers

- For two-nucleon scattering with the Yamaguchi separable force at 10.0 MeV, the analytically continued propagator τ(ε=0) = -0.1148323866513037 - 0.60608981409107898 i fm², agreeing with the exact value -0.11483238665130231 - 0.60608981409107832 i fm² (Table I).
- For the CD-Bonn potential in the ³S₁-³D₁ coupled channel at 10.0 MeV, the on-shell T-matrix element T₀₀ at ε=0 is 0.081860 - 0.36687 i fm², matching the exact value 0.081860 - 0.36687 i fm² (Table II).
- For three-nucleon scattering with the AV14 potential at 10.0 MeV laboratory energy and J^π = (1/2)^+, the analytically continued n-d elastic amplitude X_dd = 0.4248 - 1.5440 i fm⁵, compared to the contour deformation result 0.4244 - 1.5441 i fm⁵ (Table III).
- For J^π = (1/2)^-, the analytically continued X_dd = -4.5247×10⁻² - 0.3098 i fm⁵, compared to the contour deformation result -4.5393×10⁻² - 0.3098 i fm⁵ (Table IV).

## Context

This work extends the complex energy approach previously applied in atomic physics (Schlessinger, McDonald, Reinhardt, Doolen) to nuclear physics with realistic nucleon-nucleon forces. It contrasts with the contour deformation method (Lovelace, Karhill, Ehben, Koike) and the Lorentz transformation method used by the Trento group, which avoid singularities without performing analytical continuation to the real axis. The method enables future calculations for four-body break-up processes, where no calculations have been performed so far.
