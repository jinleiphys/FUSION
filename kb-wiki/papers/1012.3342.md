# The Cosmological QCD Phase Transition Revisited

> The QCD phase diagram might exhibit a first order phase transition for large baryochemical potentials. We explore the cosmological implications of such a QCD phase transition in the early universe. We propose that the large baryon-asymmetry is diluted by a little inflation where the universe is trapped in a false vacuum state of QCD. The little inflation is stopped by bubble nucleation which leads to primordial production of the seeds of extragalactic magnetic fields, primordial black holes and gravitational waves. In addition the power spectrum of cold dark matter can be affected up to mass scales of a billion solar masses. The imprints of the cosmological QCD phase transition on the gravitational wave background can be explored with the future gravitational wave detectors LISA and BBO and with pulsar timing.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a first-order QCD phase transition at high baryochemical potential could trap the early universe in a false vacuum, causing a brief inflationary period ("little inflation") of about seven e-folds that dilutes the baryon-to-photon ratio from order unity to the observed value of about \(10^{-9}\), contingent on the existence of such a first-order transition at large baryon densities.

## Method

The authors model a cosmological scenario where an initial large baryon asymmetry (from Affleck-Dine baryogenesis) leads to \(\mu/T \sim 1\), causing the universe to encounter a first-order QCD phase transition line. They describe the resulting de Sitter expansion from the false vacuum, followed by bubble nucleation that ends inflation and reheats the universe to \(T \sim T_c\). The gravitational wave background is calculated using lattice QCD parameterizations from Bazavov (2009) and Borsanyi (2010), compared with the MIT bag model, and the power spectrum of dark matter is analyzed for modes crossing the horizon during the brief inflation.

## Key numbers

- The QCD phase transition occurs at \(T_c \sim 150\) MeV, corresponding to a cosmic time of about \(10^{-5}\) seconds after the Big Bang.
- The little inflation requires about \(N = \ln(a_f/a_i) \sim 7\) e-folds to dilute the baryon-to-photon ratio from \(\mu/T \sim 1\) to the observed value of about \(10^{-9}\).
- The dark matter mass within the horizon at \(T_c\) is \(10^{-9} M_\odot\); after inflation, mass scales up to \(M_{\text{max}} \sim 10^6 M_\odot\) (for \(q=3\)) or up to \(10^9 M_\odot\) (for \(q=4\)) are affected.
- The characteristic gravitational wave frequency from the QCD phase transition is redshifted to \(\nu_{\text{peak}} \sim 10^{-8}\) Hz, with a maximum amplitude \(h \sim 10^{-12}\).
- The gravitational wave energy density ratio across the phase transition is \(\Omega_g(\nu \gg \nu^*)/\Omega_g(\nu \ll \nu^*) = (g_f/g_i)^{1/3} \sim 0.7\).
- The maximum black hole mass from bubble collapse is \(M_{bh} \sim 1 M_\odot\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier "little inflation" or "tepid inflation" concept at the QCD phase transition introduced by Kampfer et al. and by Borghini et al. (2000), and builds on the authors' own prior work (Boeckel 2009, Schettler 2010). It contrasts with the standard cosmological scenario where the QCD phase transition occurs at nearly zero baryochemical potential and is a crossover (Aoki 2006), producing no strong signals. The scenario enables testable predictions for gravitational wave detectors (LISA, BBO, pulsar timing arrays), dark matter structure formation on dwarf galaxy scales, and primordial magnetic field seeds, while also affecting WIMP dark matter detectability at the LHC.
