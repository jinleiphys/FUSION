# Thermodynamics of ultra-small metallic grains in the auxiliary-field Monte Carlo approach

> We use an auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) method to calculate thermodynamic properties (spin susceptibility and heat capacity) of ultra-small metallic grains in the presence of pairing correlations. This method allows us to study the crossover from bulk systems, where mean-field BCS theory is valid, to the fluctuation-dominated regime of ultra-small particles at finite temperature. The computational effort at low temperatures is significantly reduced by exploiting a simple renormalization method.

## Key claim

The auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) method can calculate thermodynamic properties (spin susceptibility and heat capacity) of ultra-small metallic grains across the full crossover from the BCS mean-field regime to the fluctuation-dominated regime, for arbitrary values of the ratio \(\Delta/\delta\) between the bulk BCS gap and the mean level spacing, and over the full temperature range. The abstract's claim of "significantly reduced" computational effort at low temperatures is qualified: the renormalization method works well only when the truncated band width \(N_r\) is sufficiently large, and the appropriate renormalization scheme depends on whether \(\Delta/\delta \geq 0.5\) or \(\Delta/\delta < 0.5\).

## Method

The authors model the metallic grain using the BCS pairing Hamiltonian with an equidistant single-particle spectrum at half filling. They apply a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to express the many-body propagator as a path integral over auxiliary fields, then sample the fields via Monte Carlo to compute thermal expectation values in the canonical ensemble with fixed electron number. The computational effort at low temperatures is reduced by truncating the single-particle model space to a smaller band and renormalizing the pairing coupling constant \(g\) to preserve either the discrete BCS gap (for \(\Delta/\delta \geq 0.5\)) or the perturbative renormalization of Halperin (for \(\Delta/\delta < 0.5\)).

## Key numbers

* For an ultra-small aluminum grain with \(\Delta/\delta = 1\), the bare cutoff is \(N_o \approx 89\) and the bare coupling constant is \(g/\delta \approx 0.193\), using \(\omega_D \approx 34\ \mathrm{meV}\) and \(\Delta \approx 0.38\ \mathrm{meV}\).
* For \(\Delta/\delta = 1\), AFMC spin susceptibility results used \(N_r = 10\) with \(M = 16000\) samples for \(T \le 0.8\,\delta\) (and \(M = 64000\) at \(T = 0.4\,\delta\)), \(N_r = 15\) with \(M = 4000\) for \(0.8\,\delta \le T \le 1.5\,\delta\), and \(N_r = 25\) with \(M = 4000\) at higher temperatures.
* For \(\Delta/\delta = 3\), AFMC spin susceptibility used \(N_r = 10\) with \(M = 64000\) samples for \(T \le 1.25\,\delta\) (and \(M = 384000\) at \(T = 0.67\,\delta\)), \(N_r = 15\) with \(M = 16000\) for \(1.25\,\delta \le T \le 1.6\,\delta\), and \(N_r = 25\) with \(M = 4000\) at higher temperatures.
* The spin susceptibility for an odd number of electrons shows a reentrant behavior with a minimum at a certain temperature; for \(\Delta/\delta = 1\), number-parity effects appear at temperatures \(T \le 1.5\,\delta\), compared to \(T \le 1.0\,\delta\) for the free Fermi gas.
* The heat capacity for an even number of electrons at \(\Delta/\delta = 3\) displays a shoulder around the BCS critical temperature, while for \(\Delta/\delta = 1\) the shoulder occurs at higher temperatures than the BCS critical temperature.

## Context

This work extends the auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) method, previously developed in nuclear physics as the shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method (Alhassid et al., 1990s), to metallic nanoparticles described by the BCS pairing Hamiltonian. It addresses the crossover regime where the ratio \(\Delta/\delta\) is of order unity, which was inaccessible to the Richardson exact solution (Richardson, 1963) used by Lorenzo et al. (2000) for \(\Delta/\delta \le 1\) and \(T \le 1.6\,\delta\), and to the static-path approximation (SPA) which ignores quantum fluctuations. The results enable systematic calculation of thermodynamic signatures of pairing correlations (spin susceptibility and heat capacity) for arbitrary \(\Delta/\delta\) and full temperature range, and can be compared with future experiments on ultra-small metallic grains.
