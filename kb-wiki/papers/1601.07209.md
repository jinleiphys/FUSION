# Sensitivities and correlations of nuclear structure observables emerging from chiral interactions

> Starting from a set of different two- and three-nucleon interactions from chiral effective field theory, we use the importance-truncated no-core shell model for ab initio calculations of excitation energies as well as electric quadrupole (E2) and magnetic dipole (M1) moments and transition strengths for selected p-shell nuclei. We explore the sensitivity of the excitation energies to the chiral interactions as a first step towards and systematic uncertainty propagation from chiral inputs to nuclear structure observables. The uncertainty band spanned by the different chiral interactions is typically in agreement with experimental excitation energies, but we also identify observables with notable discrepancies beyond the theoretical uncertainty that reveal insufficiencies in the chiral interactions. For electromagnetic observables we identify correlations among pairs of E2 or M1 observables based on the ab initio calculations for the different interactions. We find extremely robust correlations for E2 observables and illustrate how these correlations can be used to predict one observable based on an experimental datum for the second observable. In this way we circumvent convergence issues and arrive at far more accurate results than any direct ab initio calculation. A prime example for this approach is the quadrupole moment of the first 2^+ state in C-12, which is predicted with an drastically improved accuracy.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the variation of chiral interactions provides a diagnostic tool to estimate theoretical uncertainties in nuclear structure observables, but the uncertainty band from different chiral interactions does not always cover experimental values, revealing systematic insufficiencies in the chiral interactions themselves. The authors identify extremely robust correlations among electric quadrupole (E2) observables that can be exploited to predict one observable from another with drastically improved accuracy, circumventing convergence issues in direct ab initio calculations.

## Method
The authors used the importance-truncated no-core shell model (IT-NCSM) to perform ab initio calculations of excitation energies, electric quadrupole (E2) and magnetic dipole (M1) moments and transition strengths for selected p-shell nuclei (Li-6, B-10, C-12). They employed a set of different two- and three-nucleon interactions from chiral effective field theory at N2LO and N3LO, including the Entem-Machleidt (EM), N2LO_opt, and Epelbaum-Glockle-Meissner (EGM) interactions with various cutoff combinations, all evolved via similarity renormalization group (SRG) to soften the interactions.

## Key numbers
- Excitation energies for Li-6, B-10, and C-12 were computed with IT-NCSM up to N_max=10 for Li-6 and N_max=8 for C-12 and B-10, using SRG flow parameter alpha=0.08 fm^4 and harmonic oscillator energy hbar-Omega=16 MeV
- The threshold extrapolation uncertainty for excitation energies is approximately 50 keV for the largest model spaces
- C-12 ground-state energies from EGM interactions span a range of -96.8 to -80.5 MeV, compared to the experimental value of -92.16 MeV
- The EM interaction predicts a C-12 ground-state energy of -97.8 MeV, the largest binding energy among the interactions studied
- Changing the SRG flow parameter for the EM interaction from alpha=0.08 fm^4 to alpha=0.04 fm^4 reduces the C-12 binding energy by about 2.3 MeV
- All interactions underestimate the first 1+ state excitation energy in C-12 by more than 2 MeV compared to experiment

## Context
This work extends previous ab initio studies of p-shell nuclei using the no-core shell model (NCSM) by Navratil et al. (2007) and the IT-NCSM by Roth (2009), and complements recent efforts by the LENPIC collaboration to explore chiral interactions in few- and many-body calculations. The sensitivity analysis identifies the first 1+ state in C-12 as a benchmark for next-generation chiral three-nucleon interactions at N3LO, and the correlation method for E2 observables enables predictions that circumvent convergence issues that have limited direct ab initio calculations of electromagnetic observables in previous work.
