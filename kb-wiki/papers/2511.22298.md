# Relativistic recoil as a key to the fine-structure puzzle in muonic $^{90}\text{Zr}$

> The long-standing fine-structure anomaly in muonic $^{90}$Zr is resolved through a rigorous treatment of the relativistic-recoil effect. From a fit of ab initio QED calculations of the muonic $^{90}$Zr spectrum to precision measurements performed four decades ago, we extract a significantly more precise root-mean-square (rms) charge radius with 6-fold improvement in quality of the fit. A 2-parameter Fermi (2pF) distribution is assumed to model the nuclear charge density and yields a best-fit value of rms charge radius of $r_\text{rms}[^{90}\text{Zr}]=4.2732(7)$ fm ($χ^2 /{\text{DoF}} = 0.995$), in agreement with the previous muonic spectroscopy value, but a factor $6$ more precise, and 3$σ$ larger than the accepted literature value. Additionally, the same analysis has been performed for $^{120}$Sn, where the extracted value of $r_\text{rms}[^{120}\text{Sn}]=4.6518(34)$ fm ($χ^2 /{\text{DoF}} = 0.88$) is consistent with the accepted value. These results confirm our assumption that the muonic fine-structure puzzle arose from an incomplete treatment of QED effects and their uncertainties.

## Key claim
The long-standing fine-structure anomaly in muonic $^{90}$Zr is resolved by rigorously including the relativistic-recoil effect in the QED calculation, which had previously been treated inconsistently using a non-relativistic two-body approach. The resolution depends on the specific element: for $^{90}$Zr the relativistic recoil is the dominant correction, while for $^{208}$Pb the anomaly had a different origin.

## Method
The authors performed ab initio QED calculations of the muonic $^{90}$Zr and $^{120}$Sn spectra, including vacuum polarization (Uehling, Wichmann-Kroll, Kallen-Sabry, muonic and hadronic Uehling), self-energy, nuclear polarization, and relativistic recoil corrections. A 2-parameter Fermi (2pF) distribution was assumed for the nuclear charge density, and a least-squares fit was performed to precision measurements from Phan (1985) for zirconium and Piller (1990) for tin, with the covariance matrix including experimental and theoretical uncertainties from nuclear polarization, self-energy, and relativistic recoil.

## Key numbers
- Best-fit rms charge radius for $^{90}$Zr: $r_\text{rms} = 4.2732(7)$ fm with $\chi^2/\text{DoF} = 0.995$, a factor 6 more precise than the previous muonic spectroscopy value and $3\sigma$ larger than the accepted literature value.
- Best-fit rms charge radius for $^{120}$Sn: $r_\text{rms} = 4.6518(34)$ fm with $\chi^2/\text{DoF} = 0.88$, consistent with the accepted literature value but with nearly twice the uncertainty.
- For $^{90}$Zr, the 2pF best-fit parameters are $c = 4.9057(77)$ fm and $a = 0.5259(35)$ fm.
- For $^{120}$Sn, the 2pF best-fit parameters are $c = 5.51(4)$ fm and $a = 0.496(21)$ fm.
- The relativistic recoil correction to the $1s_{1/2}$ state in $^{90}$Zr is $-3.2866(90)$ keV; the nuclear polarization correction is $1.481(154)$ keV.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous reevaluation of QED corrections for muonic $^{208}$Pb (Beyer et al., 2025) to the remaining two elements where the fine-structure anomaly had been observed: $^{90}$Zr and $^{120}$Sn. The anomaly was originally reported by Yamazaki (1979), Phan (1985), Bergem (1988), and Piller (1990), and was previously attributed to nuclear polarization, but state-of-the-art nuclear polarization calculations by Valuev (2022) confirmed the discrepancy. The resolution enables more reliable extraction of nuclear charge radii from muonic atom spectroscopy and provides a baseline for precision tests of QED and searches for new physics. The authors note that the remaining tension in the fit quality, which evolves from $\chi^2/\text{DoF} \approx 1$ for Zr and Sn to 9.7 for Pb, likely originates from the need for a more realistic nuclear charge distribution than the conventional 2pF model.
