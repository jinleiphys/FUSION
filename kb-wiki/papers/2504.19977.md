# $^{208}$Pb nuclear charge radius revisited: closing the fine-structure-anomaly gap

> A comprehensive reevaluation of the root-mean-square nuclear charge radius is presented for the doubly magic $^{208}$Pb extracted from muonic spectroscopy measurements. By integrating rigorous theoretical quantum electrodynamics calculations, state-of-the-art numerical methods, and a systematic reanalysis of the uncertainties, we reduced the long-standing muonic fine-structure anomaly and improved the goodness of fit by a factor of twenty. The resulting value of 5.5062(5)~fm for a Fermi distribution is fairly consistent with the previously reported muonic spectroscopy value, and three standard deviations larger than the commonly used compilation data, which indicates that the current value and its uncertainty could be significantly underestimated. Attributing the remaining discrepancy to theory errors which can not be rigorously calculated we suggest the rms charge radius with reduced model dependence to be 5.5062(17) fm. This work sets an improved benchmark for charge radius extraction in heavy nuclei and paves a path for systematic reevaluations across the nuclear chart.

## Key claim
By incorporating fully rigorous quantum electrodynamics calculations and a systematic reanalysis of uncertainties, the authors reduce the long-standing muonic fine-structure anomaly in lead-208 and improve the goodness of fit by a factor of twenty, but the remaining high chi-squared per degree of freedom (9.5) is attributed to an inconsistency between fits of different energy groups of transitions, not solely to nuclear polarization as previously assumed.

## Method
The authors calculated all individual contributions to the muonic spectrum of lead-208 that contribute at least 10% of the experimental precision, including finite nuclear size effects, vacuum polarization (Uehling, Wichmann-Kroll, Kallen-Sabry, muonic Uehling, and hadronic Uehling), self energy, relativistic recoil, nuclear polarization, and electron screening. They performed a chi-squared minimization fit of the theoretical transition energies (computed as functions of two-parameter Fermi distribution parameters) to experimental measurements from Bergem (1988), treating theory uncertainties and experimental uncertainties separately, and analyzed results from individual runs as well as a combined fit.

## Key numbers
- Best-fit root-mean-square charge radius for a two-parameter Fermi distribution: 5.5062(5) fm
- Reduced model-dependent root-mean-square charge radius (normalizing to chi-squared per degree of freedom = 1): 5.5062(17) fm
- Best-fit two-parameter Fermi parameters: c = 6.6409(25) fm, a = 0.5283(18) fm
- Chi-squared per degree of freedom for the combined fit: 9.5 (compared to 187 in Bergem (1988))
- Fine-structure anomaly in Delta 2p reduced from 6 sigma to 2 sigma
- Experimental transition energies used: 2p3/2 - 1s1/2 at 5962.854(90) keV, 2p1/2 - 1s1/2 at 5778.058(100) keV, and seven other transitions ranging from 1030.543(27) keV to 2642.332(30) keV

## Context
This work extends the muonic spectroscopy measurements of Bergem (1988) by incorporating fully rigorous QED calculations that were previously unavailable, and it contradicts the combined muonic and electron-scattering compilations of Angeli (2013) and Fricke (2004), which give a significantly smaller radius. The analysis builds on microscopic nuclear polarization calculations by Valuev (2022) that showed nuclear polarization is likely not solely responsible for the fine-structure anomaly. This work enables systematic reevaluations of charge radii across the nuclear chart using muonic spectroscopy with modern QED theory, and it suggests that the currently accepted standard radius for lead-208 may be underestimated.
