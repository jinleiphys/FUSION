# Determination of pairing matrix elements from average single particle level densities

> A simple and efficient method to treat nuclear pairing correlations within a simple Hartree-Fock--plus-BCS description is proposed and discussed. It relies on the fact that the intensity of pairing correlations depends crucially on level densities around the Fermi surface ($ ρ(e_F)$) and that any fitting of nuclear energies as functions of the nucleon numbers is akin of a semi-classical average, smoothing out their quantal structure. A particular attention has been paid to two points generally ignored in previous similar approaches. One is a correction advocated by Möller and Nix [Nucl. Phys. A 536, 20 (1992)] taking into account the fact that the data included into the fit correspond to $ ρ(e_F)$ values systematically lower than average. The second is due to a systematic overestimation of the proton sp level density at the Fermi surface resulting from the local Slater approximation of the Coulomb terms in use in most microscopic descriptions. Our approach is validated by the agreement with data of corresponding calculated moments of inertia of well and rigidly deformed rare-earth nuclei, evaluated according to the Inglis-Belyaev ansatz with some crude Thouless-Valatin corrections. Indeed, the agreement which is found, is at least of the same quality as what results from a specific fit of the pairing intensities to these particular pieces of data. While this approach is currently limited to the very simple seniority force pairing treatment, it may serve as a starting point to define pairing residual interactions from averaged odd-even mass differences data, using merely average sp level densities associated to calculated canonical basis.

## Key claim

The authors propose that pairing matrix elements can be reliably determined from average single-particle level densities rather than from direct fits to spectroscopic data, provided two corrections are applied: a Möller-Nix correction for the systematically lower-than-average level density at equilibrium deformation, and a correction for the overestimation of proton level density caused by the Slater approximation for Coulomb exchange.

## Method

The authors used the uniform gap method with Strutinsky-averaged single-particle level densities from Hartree-Fock calculations with three Skyrme parametrizations (SIII, SkM*, SLy4) to extract constant pairing matrix elements via the BCS gap equation. They applied the Möller-Nix phenomenological formula for average pairing gaps and derived a linear correction factor for protons by comparing exact Coulomb exchange calculations with the Slater approximation across a sample of deformed nuclei. The resulting pairing matrix elements were validated by calculating moments of inertia using the Inglis-Belyaev ansatz with Thouless-Valatin corrections for 19 lanthanide nuclei, 3 hafnium isotopes, 1 tungsten isotope, and 22 actinide nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The Möller-Nix effective pairing gap formula uses r = 4.8 MeV with B_s = 1
- The proton gap ratio correction follows R_p = 0.0181 * E_cond^p + 0.781, where E_cond^p is the proton condensation energy in MeV
- The difference between exact integration and the asinh approximation for pairing matrix elements is mostly within ±1 keV for protons, with a maximum absolute difference of 4.3 keV for neutrons
- The smoothing width gamma is defined as gamma = beta * 41 / A^(1/3) MeV, with beta = 1.2 and polynomial order M = 2
- Calculated moments of inertia for nuclei like 156Sm (Z=62, N=94) range from 37.742 to 39.438 hbar^2/MeV across parametrizations, compared to experimental value 39.531 hbar^2/MeV
- For 176Yb, the average Fermi level density for neutrons is approximately 4.71 MeV^-1 and for protons approximately 3.62 MeV^-1, with fitted V_n ≈ 0.168 MeV and V_p ranging from 0.215 to 0.230 MeV depending on initial G_q

## Context

This work extends the approach of Hafiza (2019) who showed that independent fits of odd-even mass differences and moments of inertia yield similar pairing strength parameters in rare-earth nuclei. The authors build on the uniform gap method from the Strutinsky shell correction approach (Funny Hill) and the Möller-Nix (1992) correction for systematic bias in level densities at equilibrium deformation. The method enables determination of pairing residual interactions from averaged odd-even mass differences using only average single-particle level densities, potentially replacing direct fits to spectroscopic data.
