# Fermions with long and finite range interactions on a quantum ring

> Background: Idealised systems are commonly used in nuclear physics and condensed matter. For instance, the construction of nuclear energy density functionals involves properties of infinite matter, while neutron drops are used to test nuclear interactions and approximations to the nuclear many-body problem. In condensed matter, quantum rings are also used to study properties of electron systems. Purpose: To investigate the possibility to use quantum rings with systems of nucleons including many-body correlations. Methods: A quantum ring model of a finite number of same spin fermions is developed. Several attractive and repulsive interactions with finite and infinite ranges are considered. Quantum Monte Carlo calculations are used to provide exact ground-state energies. Comparisons with analytical Hartree-Fock solutions are used to get an insight into the role of correlations. Results: Hartree-Fock results with no breaking of space translational symmetry are able to describe many systems. However, additional spatial correlations are required in the case of dense systems with a strong short-range repulsion, or with attractive interactions in large rings. Conclusions: Self-bound systems of fermions with spatial correlations produced by basic features of the nuclear interactions can be described on a quantum ring, encouraging applications with realistic interactions, as well as investigations with higher dimensional geometries such as spherium.

## Key claim

Hartree-Fock solutions without breaking space translational symmetry can describe many fermion systems on a quantum ring, but additional spatial correlations are required for dense systems with strong short-range repulsion or for attractive interactions in large rings, where self-bound systems form.

## Method

The authors developed a quantum ring model for a finite number of same-spin fermions, using Quantum Monte Carlo (variational and diffusion Monte Carlo) calculations with the CASINO code to obtain exact ground-state energies. They compared these exact results with analytical Hartree-Fock solutions derived for Coulomb, Yukawa, and two model nuclear potentials (sums of attractive and repulsive Yukawa terms) to isolate the role of spatial correlations. The Jastrow factor in the trial wavefunction was optimized via VMC energy minimization to capture correlations beyond the mean-field Slater determinant.

## Key numbers

- For non-interacting fermions on a ring at Seitz radius \(r_s=1\), the kinetic energy per particle asymptotes to \(\pi^2/24\).
- For repulsive Coulomb (\(Z=+1\)) at \(r_s=1\), QMC results lie a few percentage points below the analytic HF expression \(\epsilon^{\mathrm{KE}}_{\mathrm{HF}}(n)+Z\epsilon^{\mathrm{PE}}_{\mathrm{HF}}(n)\).
- For 5 particles with the nuclear-like potential \(V_1^{\mathrm{nuc}}\) (strong short-range repulsion), QMC predicts negative binding energy above \(R \sim 1\) a.u., while the HF analytic expression suggests no radius at which the system is bound.
- For 5 particles on a ring of radius \(R=5\) a.u., the three attractive interactions (\(Z=-1\), \(V_1^{\mathrm{nuc}}\), \(V_2^{\mathrm{nuc}}\)) show large disagreement between QMC and HF results, with QMC predicting significantly more binding due to spatial correlations.
- The kinetic energy per particle in the HF approximation is \(\epsilon^{\mathrm{KE}}_{\mathrm{HF}}(n) = (n^2-1)/(24R^2)\).

## Context

This work extends the quantum ring model, previously applied to electron systems (Loos 2009, 2012, 2013; Rogers 2017) and bosons in annular traps (Bargi 2010, Kaminishi 2011), to fermion systems with nuclear-like interactions. It complements other idealised nuclear systems such as infinite nuclear matter (Oertel 2017, Leonhardt 2020) and neutron drops in harmonic potentials (Pudliner 1996, Bogner 2011, Gandolfi 2011, Maris 2013, Shen 2018, Zhao 2020). The results encourage applications with realistic nuclear interactions and extensions to higher-dimensional geometries such as spherium (Loos 2009), which could provide benchmarks for nuclear energy density functionals and test approximations to the nuclear many-body problem.
