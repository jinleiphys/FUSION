# $Λ_c N$ interaction from lattice QCD and its application to $Λ_c$ hypernuclei

> The interaction between $Λ_c$ and a nucleon ($N$) is investigated by employing the HAL QCD method in the (2+1)-flavor lattice QCD on a $(2.9~\mathrm{fm})^3$ volume at $m_π\simeq 410,~570,~700$ MeV. We study the central potential in $^1S_0$ channel as well as central and tensor potentials in $^3S_1 - $$^3D_1$ channel, and find that the tensor potential for $Λ_c N$ is negligibly weak and central potentials in both $^1S_0$ and $^3S_1 - $$^3D_1$ channels are almost identical with each other except at short distances. Phase shifts and scattering lengths calculated with these potentials show that the interaction of $Λ_c N$ system is attractive and has a similar strength in $^1S_0$ and $^3S_1$ channels at low energies (i.e. the kinetic energy less than about $40$ MeV). While the attractions are not strong enough to form two-body bound states, our results lead to a possibility to form $Λ_c$ hypernuclei for sufficiently large atomic numbers ($A$). To demonstrate this, we derive a single-folding potential for $Λ_c$ hypernuclei from the $Λ_c$-nucleon potential obtained in lattice QCD, and find that $Λ_c$ hypernuclei can exist for $A \ge 12$ with the binding energies of a few MeV. We also estimate the Coulomb effect for the $Λ_c$ hypernuclei.

## Key claim

The $\Lambda_c N$ interaction is attractive in both $^1S_0$ and $^3S_1$ channels at low energies (kinetic energy less than about 40 MeV), but the attraction is not strong enough to form two-body bound states; however, the authors find that $\Lambda_c$ hypernuclei can exist for atomic numbers $A \ge 12$ with binding energies of a few MeV, based on a single-folding potential derived from the lattice QCD results.

## Method

The authors employed the HAL QCD method to extract the $\Lambda_c N$ potential from (2+1)-flavor lattice QCD simulations on a $(2.9~\mathrm{fm})^3$ volume at three pion masses ($m_\pi \simeq 410, 570, 700$ MeV). They calculated the central potential in the $^1S_0$ channel and both central and tensor potentials in the $^3S_1 - ^3D_1$ channel, then derived phase shifts and scattering lengths from these potentials. Using the lattice-derived $\Lambda_c N$ potential, they constructed a single-folding potential to study $\Lambda_c$ hypernuclei and estimate binding energies for various atomic numbers.

## Key numbers

- Scattering lengths (particle physics convention) for $^1S_0$ channel: $0.49(18)(11)$ fm at $m_\pi = 412(2)$ MeV, $0.24(13)(3)$ fm at $m_\pi = 570(1)$ MeV, $0.13(11)(11)$ fm at $m_\pi = 702(2)$ MeV
- Scattering lengths (particle physics convention) for $^3S_1$ channel: $0.51(20)(9)$ fm at $m_\pi = 412(2)$ MeV, $0.29(16)(9)$ fm at $m_\pi = 570(1)$ MeV, $0.17(10)(12)$ fm at $m_\pi = 702(2)$ MeV
- $\Lambda_c$ hypernuclei can exist for atomic numbers $A \ge 12$ with binding energies of a few MeV
- Lattice volume: $(2.9~\mathrm{fm})^3$ with lattice spacing $a = 0.0907(13)$ fm
- Pion masses used: $702(2)$ MeV, $570(1)$ MeV, $412(2)$ MeV
- $\Lambda_c$ baryon masses: $2685(3)$ MeV at $m_\pi \simeq 700$ MeV, $2555(5)$ MeV at $m_\pi \simeq 570$ MeV, $2434(6)$ MeV at $m_\pi \simeq 410$ MeV

## Context

This work extends previous phenomenological studies of charmed hypernuclei, which began with Dover (1977) using one-boson-exchange potential models based on flavor $SU(4)$ symmetry, and later studies by Bando (1980s) comparing $\Lambda$ and $\Lambda_c$ hypernuclei. The authors note that flavor $SU(4)$ may not give a good description due to the heavy charm quark mass, and that previous constituent quark model studies (Huang, Gal, Maeda, Valcarce) and quark-meson coupling model calculations (Tsushima) had produced varying predictions. This lattice QCD calculation provides a first-principles determination of the $\Lambda_c N$ interaction, enabling future studies of charmed hypernuclei and hadronic matter with charm quarks. The weaker tensor potential compared to the $\Lambda N$ system (Nemura) is attributed to the suppression of $D$ meson exchange and the large $\Lambda_c N - \Sigma_c N$ mass difference.
