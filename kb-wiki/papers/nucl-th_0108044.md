# Hadron structure and the limitations of phenomenological models in electromagnetic reactions

> The description of electromagnetic reactions at intermediate energies, such as pion electroproduction or (virtual) Compton scattering, traditionally starts from covariant tree-level Feynman diagrams (Born or pole terms). Internal hadron structure is included by means of (on-shell) form factors in the vertices while free propagators are used. To overcome problems with gauge invariance, simple prescriptions such as choosing $F_1^V (q^2) = F_π (q^2)$ in pion electroproduction or the ``minimal substitution'' are used. We discuss the limitations of such approaches by comparing to the most general structure of electromagnetic vertices and propagators for pions and nucleons. The recipes to enforce gauge invariance are critically examined and contrasted with the exact treatment. The interplay between off-shell effects and irreducible ``contact'' terms is demonstrated for real Compton scattering on a pion. The need for a consistent microscopic treatment of reaction amplitudes is stressed. Shortcomings of minimal substitution are illustrated through an example in the framework of chiral perturbation theory.

## Key claim

The paper argues that commonly used phenomenological models for electromagnetic reactions at intermediate energies, which rely on on-shell form factors and free propagators with ad hoc prescriptions to restore gauge invariance, are fundamentally limited and cannot reliably extract hadron structure information; a consistent microscopic treatment such as chiral perturbation theory is necessary to properly account for off-shell effects and gauge invariance.

## Method

The authors analyze the general Lorentz- and gauge-invariant structure of electromagnetic vertices for spin-0 (pion) and spin-1/2 (nucleon) particles, deriving the constraints imposed by the Ward-Takahashi identity. They compare these general vertices with those obtained through the minimal substitution prescription and with the simplified form-factor assumptions commonly used in pion electroproduction models, and they examine the role of off-shell effects in real Compton scattering on a pion by decomposing the amplitude into class-A (pole) and class-B (irreducible) contributions.

## Key numbers

- The on-shell pion form factor is defined as \(F(q^2) \equiv F(q^2, m^2, m^2)\) at \(p^2 = p'^2 = m^2\)
- For real Compton scattering on a pion, the class-A tensor reduces to \(M^{\mu\nu}_{\rm A} = e^2[(2P^\nu+q^\nu)F(0,m^2,s) \Delta(p'+q')(2P^\mu+q'^\mu)F(0,s,m^2) + (2P^\mu-q'^\mu)F(0,m^2,u) \Delta(p'-q)(2P^\nu-q^\nu)F(0,u,m^2)]\)
- The pole term of the class-A tensor is \(M^{\mu\nu}_{\rm pole} = e^2[(2P^\nu+q^\nu)(2P^\mu+q'^\mu)/(s-m^2) + (2P^\mu-q'^\mu)(2P^\nu-q^\nu)/(u-m^2)]\)
- The off-shell contribution \(\Delta M^{\mu\nu}\) is proportional to \(h(z) = [F(0,m^2,z) - F(0,m^2,m^2)]/(z-m^2)\) with \(F(0,m^2,m^2)=1\)

## Context

This work extends the formal analysis of electromagnetic vertices by Bincer (1960), Naus (1987), and Tiemeijer (1990), and critically examines the phenomenological approaches used in analyses such as those by Dressler (1988), Nozawa (1990), Hanstein (1998), Guidal (1997), and Vanderhaeghen (1998) for pion electroproduction. The paper demonstrates that the common assumption \(F_1^V(q^2) = F_\pi(q^2)\) to enforce gauge invariance is unnecessary when vertices and propagators are treated consistently via the Ward-Takahashi identity, and it enables a proper framework for extracting hadron form factors from electroproduction experiments using chiral perturbation theory.
