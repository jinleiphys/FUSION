# Realistic Effective Intaractions and Nuclear Structure Calculations

> We address the two main questions relevant to microscopic nuclear structure calculations starting from a free NN potential. These concern the accuracy of these kinds of calculations and the extent to which they depend on the potential used as input. Regarding the first question, we present some results obtained for nuclei around doubly magic 132Sn and 208Pb by making use of an effective interaction derived from the Bonn A potential. Comparison shows that our results are in very good agreement with the experimental data. As for the second question, we present the results obtained for the nucleus 134Te by making use of four different NN potentials. They indicate that nuclear structure calculations may help in understanding the off-shell nature of the NN potential.

## Key claim

Realistic effective interactions derived from the Bonn A free NN potential can reproduce experimental spectra of nuclei around doubly magic 132Sn and 208Pb with rms deviations typically below 110 keV, but the quality of agreement depends on which NN potential is used as input; for 134Te, the Bonn A potential gives the best fit (rms 106 keV) while the Paris potential gives the worst (rms 346 keV), suggesting that potentials with weaker tensor force (lower deuteron D-state probability) may yield better nuclear structure descriptions.

## Method

The authors performed shell-model calculations using a G-matrix folded-diagram formalism to derive effective interactions from free NN potentials, including renormalizations from core polarization and folded diagrams. For nuclei around 132Sn (134Te, 135I, 136Xe), they used 132Sn as an inert core with valence protons occupying five single-particle orbits; for Pb isotopes (206Pb, 205Pb, 204Pb), they treated neutrons as valence holes relative to a 208Pb closed core with six single-hole orbits. They compared results from four different NN potentials (Bonn A, CD Bonn, Nijmegen93, Paris) for 134Te, using experimental single-particle energies from 133Sb and 207Pb where available.

## Key numbers

- rms deviation for 134Te using Bonn A potential: 106 keV
- rms deviation for 135I using Bonn A potential: 58 keV
- rms deviation for 136Xe using Bonn A potential: 107 keV (eight identified excited states)
- rms deviation for 206Pb using Bonn A potential: 207 keV
- rms deviation for 205Pb using Bonn A potential: 74 keV (excluding three unidentifiable states)
- rms deviation for 204Pb using Bonn A potential: 216 keV
- rms deviations for 134Te with different potentials: Bonn A 106 keV, CD Bonn 160 keV, Nijmegen93 211 keV, Paris 346 keV
- Deuteron D-state probabilities: Bonn A 4.4%, CD Bonn 4.83%, Nijmegen93 5.76%, Paris 5.8%
- Shell-model oscillator parameter: 7.88 MeV for N=82 isotones (A=132), 6.88 MeV for Pb isotopes (A=208)
- Single-hole energies for Pb isotopes (in MeV): p1/2=0, f5/2=0.570, p3/2=0.898, i13/2=1.633, f7/2=2.340, h9/2=3.414

## Context

This work extends the microscopic approach to nuclear structure calculations pioneered by Kuo and Brown (1966), who derived an sd-shell effective interaction from the Hamada-Johnston potential. The authors build on their own previous calculations for nuclei in the 100Sn and 132Sn regions (Andreozzi et al., 1996; Covello et al., 1997) and present new results for Pb isotopes. The comparison of four NN potentials for 134Te represents a systematic investigation of how nuclear structure calculations can distinguish between different potentials, which may help reveal off-shell properties of the NN interaction. The authors plan to extend this work to other nuclei and additional modern potentials such as Argonne v18 and Nijmegen I/II.
