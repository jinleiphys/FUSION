# A self-consistent treatment of damped motion for stable and unstable collective modes

> We address the dynamics of damped collective modes in terms of first and second moments. The modes are introduced in a self-consistent fashion with the help of a suitable application of linear response theory. Quantum effects in the fluctuations are governed by diffusion coefficients D_{μν}. The latter are obtained through a fluctuation dissipation theorem generalized to allow for a treatment of unstable modes. Numerical evaluations of the D_{μν} are presented. We discuss briefly how this picture may be used to describe global motion within a locally harmonic approximation. Relations to other methods are discussed, like "dissipative tunneling", RPA at finite temperature and generalizations of the "Static Path Approximation".

## Key claim

The paper presents a self-consistent linear response framework for damped collective modes that works for both stable and unstable modes, with the key qualifier that the treatment relies on a locally harmonic approximation (LHA) valid only within limited time intervals where the collective motion can be linearized, and that the diffusion coefficients for unstable modes require analytic continuation of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem.

## Method

The authors start from a Hamiltonian of the form H = H_intr + H_coupl + H_coll and use linear response theory to derive a collective response function χ_coll(ω) = χ(ω)/(1 + kχ(ω)), where χ(ω) is the intrinsic response and k is a coupling constant that depends on deformation and temperature. For unstable modes (negative stiffness C), they perform analytic continuations of integral representations for response functions, choosing contours that respect causality while handling poles in the upper half-plane. The diffusion coefficients D_{μν} governing quantum fluctuations are obtained from a generalized fluctuation-dissipation theorem extended to unstable modes, with numerical evaluations presented for the nuclear case using a self-energy parametrization Γ(ω,T) = (1/Γ_0) × [(ħω − μ)² + π²T²] / [1 + (1/c²)((ħω − μ)² + π²T²)] with Γ_0 = 33 MeV and c = 20 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The self-energy parametrization uses Γ_0 = 33 MeV and c = 20 MeV for the imaginary part of the nucleonic self-energy
- For nuclear physics, the static stiffness C(0) drops dramatically above k_B T = 2 MeV where shell structure diminishes
- The sum rule for inertia gives 1/M ≤ S^{(1)}_{qq} = (ik²/ħ)⟨[Ḟ, F]⟩ ≡ 1/m_0, where m_0 corresponds to the irrotational flow inertia of the liquid drop model
- The effective stiffness C satisfies C ≥ C(0), meaning at an instability the dynamics lowers the absolute value of the stiffness (weakens the instability)

## Context

This work extends the Kramers (1940) transport equation approach to include quantum effects for both stable and unstable collective modes, building on earlier treatments by Hofmann and collaborators (Hofmann and Sollacher, 1989; Hofmann, 1995) that introduced self-consistent collective variables via a Bohm-Pines type method. The formalism enables treatment of dissipative tunneling and large-scale collective motion (such as nuclear fission) within a locally harmonic approximation, where the intrinsic subsystem and its coupling to the collective coordinate change along the dynamical path. The paper contrasts its approach with finite-temperature RPA and Static Path Approximation methods (Attalha, 1993; Rossignoli and Canosa, 1994), arguing that those approaches miss the transition to macroscopic liquid-drop behavior at high temperatures where two-body collisions dominate.
