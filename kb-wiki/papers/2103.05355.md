# Particle yields in pp interactions at $\sqrt{s}=17.3$ GeV interpreted in the statistical hadronization model

> The unified set of yields of particles produced in proton-proton collisions at $\sqrt{s}$ = 17.3 GeV (laboratory beam momentum 158 GeV/c) is evaluated, combining the experimental results of the NA49 and NA61/SHINE collaborations at the CERN SPS. With the statistical hadronization code Thermal-Fist we confirm the unacceptably high value of $χ^2$, both in the canonical and grand canonical - strangeness canonical approach, and the common volume for all the hadrons. The use of the energy-dependent width of the Breit-Wigner parametrization for the mass distributions of unstable particles improves the quality of the description of particle yields only slightly. We confirm the observation that exclusion of the $ϕ$ meson yield makes the fit result acceptable. The complete experimental data set of particle yields can be reasonably fitted if the canonical volumes of hadrons without and with open strangeness are allowed to vary independently. The canonical volume of strangeness was found larger than that for non-strange hadrons, which is compatible with the femtoscopy measurements of p+p system at $\sqrt{s} = $ 27.4 MeV and 900 MeV. The model with the best-fit parameters allows to predict the yields of several not yet measured particles emitted from p+p at $\sqrt{s}$ = 17.3 GeV.

# Particle yields in pp interactions at $\sqrt{s}=17.3$ GeV interpreted in the statistical hadronization model

## Key claim

The complete set of particle yields from pp collisions at $\sqrt{s}=17.3$ GeV can be fitted acceptably by the statistical hadronization model only if either the phi meson yield is excluded from the fit, or if the canonical volumes for hadrons with and without open strangeness are allowed to vary independently, with the strangeness volume found larger than that for non-strange hadrons.

## Method

The authors compiled a unified data set of particle multiplicities from published NA49 and NA61/SHINE results for inelastic pp collisions at $\sqrt{s}=17.3$ GeV (158 GeV/c beam momentum), averaging yields where both experiments provided measurements. They performed fits using the Thermal-FIST statistical hadronization code in two formulations: grand canonical with strangeness canonical (SC) and full canonical ensemble (CE), both with and without energy-dependent Breit-Wigner (eBW) resonance mass distributions. They also tested scenarios where the phi meson was excluded and where the canonical volume for strange hadrons (radius R_C) was treated as a free parameter independent of the non-strange volume (radius R).

## Key numbers

- Temperature T = 173.7 ± 3.2 MeV, baryochemical potential μ_B = 248.5 ± 3.7 MeV, radius R = 1.35 ± 0.08 fm, strangeness radius R_C = 1.82 ± 0.13 fm, γ_S = 0.41 ± 0.01, χ²/NDF = 11.5/10 = 1.15 (SC approach with eBW, all particles included, R_C free)
- χ²/NDF = 145/11 = 13 (SC, BW, all particles, R_C = R) versus χ²/NDF = 12.5/10 = 1.3 (SC, BW, phi excluded, R_C = R)
- χ²/NDF = 143/12 = 12 (CE, BW, all particles, R_C = R) versus χ²/NDF = 16.0/11 = 1.5 (CE, BW, phi excluded, R_C = R)
- The eBW approach raised temperature by approximately 8 MeV and reduced radius by approximately 0.13 fm compared to standard BW treatment
- R_C found approximately 0.5 fm larger than R in the successful SC fit with free R_C
- Neutron yield predicted as 0.87, compared to experimental value 0.67 ± 0.10 (deviation of 1.98 sigma)
- Predicted deuteron yield: 2.58 × 10⁻²; predicted Omega yield: 7.6 × 10⁻⁵

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Kraus (2010) and Begun (2018) on statistical hadronization in pp collisions at SPS energies, using the updated Thermal-FIST code with eBW resonance shapes not previously applied to this system. The authors confirm the "phi meson puzzle", that including the phi meson yield produces unacceptable fits, previously noted by Begun (2018) for separate NA49 and NA61/SHINE data sets. The finding that R_C exceeds R is compatible with kaon femtoscopy measurements at sqrt(s) = 27.4 GeV (LEBC, 1992) and 900 GeV (CMS, 2014), which also indicate larger source sizes for kaons than for pions. The best-fit parameters enable predictions for unmeasured particles including light nuclei and charmed hadrons, which can be tested by future measurements.
