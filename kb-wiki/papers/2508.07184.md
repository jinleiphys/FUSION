# Characterizing radial flow fluctuations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions at top RHIC and LHC energies

> We present a systematic investigation of the transverse-momentum differential radial flow fluctuations observable $v_0(p_T)$ in relativistic heavy-ion collisions at top RHIC ($\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}}{\,=\,}200$ GeV) and LHC ($\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}}{\,=\,}2.76$ and 5.02 TeV) energies. Using a multistage hydrodynamic model, we assess the sensitivity of $v_0(p_T)$ to a wide range of physical effects, including bulk and shear viscosities, off-equilibrium corrections at particlization, the presence of a hadronic afterburner, and the nucleon size in the initial conditions. By employing complementary rescaling strategies, we demonstrate how different physical effects leave distinct imprints on the shape of $v_0(p_T)$. A combined double-rescaling of $v_0(p_T)/v_0$ versus $p_T/\langle p_T \rangle$ reveals a universality across a wide range of energies and model assumptions in the low-$p_T$ regime, a robust signature of collective behavior. This allows us to disentangle the universal dynamics of the bulk medium from model-specific features that emerge at higher $p_T$. Our results establish $v_0(p_T)$ as a powerful and complementary observable for constraining QGP transport properties and initial-state granularity, offering a unique probe of the created QCD medium.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the transverse-momentum differential radial flow fluctuation observable \(v_0(p_T)\) can serve as a complementary probe of QGP transport properties and initial-state granularity, but its sensitivity to bulk viscosity changes with collision energy: at top RHIC energy (200 GeV) the alternative rescaling \(v_0(p_T)\) versus \(p_T/\langle p_T \rangle\) provides better discrimination than the previously used \(v_0(p_T)/v_0\) versus \(p_T\) scaling, which works better at LHC energies.

## Method

The study uses a multistage hydrodynamic model within the JETSCAPE framework (v3.7) combining TRENTo initial conditions with free-streaming, MUSIC viscous hydrodynamics, iSS particlization, and SMASH hadronic afterburner. The model was previously calibrated via Bayesian inference on \(p_T\)-integrated observables from RHIC Au+Au at 200 GeV and LHC Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV, and the authors now compute \(v_0(p_T)\) using both covariance-based and fluctuation-based formulations with pseudorapidity-separated subevents to suppress nonflow correlations. They systematically vary bulk and shear viscosities, off-equilibrium corrections at particlization (Grad versus Chapman-Enskog), the presence of the hadronic afterburner, and the nucleon size in initial conditions across three beam energies.

## Key numbers

- Three beam energies studied: \(\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}} = 200\) GeV (RHIC Au+Au), 2.76 TeV (LHC Pb+Pb), and 5.02 TeV (LHC Pb+Pb)
- Model results compared to ALICE and ATLAS measurements for 30-40% centrality Pb+Pb at 5.02 TeV; agreement holds up to \(p_T \lesssim 2.5\) GeV for ALICE and \(p_T \lesssim 2.2\) GeV for ATLAS
- Low-\(p_T\) cuts applied: \(p_T > 0.2\) GeV for ALICE and \(p_T > 0.5\) GeV for ATLAS matching experimental acceptance
- The Bayesian analysis constrained viscosities in the crossover region \(T \sim 150-250\) MeV, with viscosities at \(T \gtrsim 250\) MeV remaining poorly constrained
- Switching temperature \(T_{\mathrm{sw}}\) used for transition to hadronic transport

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical studies of \(v_0(p_T)\) by Gardim (2019), Schenke (2020), and Parida (2024) from single LHC energies to a systematic comparison across RHIC and LHC energies, and builds upon the first experimental measurements by ALICE (2025) and ATLAS (2025) at 5.02 TeV. The study uses the same Bayesian-calibrated model from the JETSCAPE Collaboration (2020, 2022) that was previously validated against \(p_T\)-integrated observables, and now tests its predictive power for this new differential observable. The results enable future combined constraints on bulk viscosity, particlization models, and initial-state granularity by comparing the universal low-\(p_T\) scaling behavior against model-specific features at higher \(p_T\).
