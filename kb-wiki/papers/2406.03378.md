# Use of quantality in nuclei and many-body systems

> The use of quantality is discussed in the case of nuclei and other many-body systems such as atomic electrons. This dimensionless quantity is known to indicate when a many-body system behaves like a crystal or a quantum liquid. Its role is further analyzed by showing its relation to the scattering length. The emergence of a fundamental lengthscale, the limit radius, is also shown. It corresponds to the hard-core of the nucleon-nucleon interaction in the case of nucleons, and to a value close to the Bohr radius in the case of atomic electrons. The occurrence of a cluster phase in nuclei is analyzed using the quantality through its relation to the localization parameter, allowing for the identification of both the number of nucleons and the density as control parameters for the occurrence of this phase. The relation of the quantality to the magnitude of the interaction also exhibits a third dimensionless parameter, monitoring the magnitude of the spin-orbit effect in finite systems, through the realization of the pseudo-spin symmetry. The impact of quantality on the spin-orbit effect is compared in various many-body systems. The role of quantality in the relative effect of the binding energy and the shell one is also analyzed in nuclei. Finally, additional dimensionless quantities are proposed from the generalization of the quantality. Nuclei are found to be exceptional systems because all their dimensionless quantities are close to the order of unity, at variance with other many-body systems.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the quantality, a dimensionless ratio of zero-point energy to potential energy, can be used as a unifying tool to understand phase transitions (crystal, cluster, quantum liquid), the emergence of a fundamental length scale (the limit radius), and the magnitude of spin-orbit effects across different many-body systems, with the hedge that all results are semi-quantitative and should be considered within one order of magnitude.

## Method

The authors relate the quantality (Λ) to the scattering length via an analytical expression derived from a spherically symmetric finite well potential. They connect the quantality to the localization parameter (α_loc) using a harmonic oscillator approximation for the nucleonic wave function, and derive a relation between the quantality, the effective coupling constant (α), and a spin-orbit parameter (η) through the non-relativistic reduction of the Dirac equation. The analysis is applied to nuclei, atomic electrons, and helium systems using phenomenological values from the literature.

## Key numbers

- Nuclei: quantality Λ ≈ 0.4, using V₀ ≈ 100 MeV and r₀ ≈ 1 fm
- Atomic electrons: quantality Λ ≈ 3.1, using V₀ ≈ 10 eV and r₀ ≈ 0.05 nm
- ⁴He atom: quantality Λ ≈ 0.14
- ³He atom: quantality Λ ≈ 0.19
- H₂ molecule: quantality Λ ≈ 0.06 (crystal state)
- ²⁰Ne atom: quantality Λ ≈ 0.007 (crystal state)
- Limit radius in nuclei: r_l = 0.5 fm, corresponding to the hard-core size of the nucleon-nucleon interaction
- Packing density in nuclei: ρ_l ≈ 1.5 fm⁻³, about 10 times the saturation density ρ₀ ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³
- First unitarity limit for scattering length divergence: Λ ≈ 0.4
- Second unitarity limit: Λ ≈ 0.05
- Mott density for alpha-clusterization in dilute nuclei: ρ^α_Mott/ρ₀ ≈ 0.25
- Effective coupling constant for nuclei: α ≈ 0.51
- Spin-orbit parameter for nuclei: η ≈ 1.3
- Spin-orbit parameter for atomic electrons: η ≈ -5 × 10⁴

## Context

This work extends the use of quantality, originally introduced by de Boer (1948) and used by Mottelson (1996, 1999) to distinguish quantum liquids from crystals, to analyze cluster phases and spin-orbit effects in finite systems. It builds on prior studies of the localization parameter by Ebran et al. (2012, 2013, 2014a, 2018) and the spin-orbit parameter by Ebran et al. (2016). The results enable a unified understanding of clusterization as a transitional phase between crystal and quantum liquid states, and identify both nucleon number and density as control parameters for cluster occurrence in nuclei, consistent with the Ikeda conjecture (1968) and more recent EDF calculations by Schuck et al. (2013), Typel et al. (2014), and Ebran et al. (2020).
