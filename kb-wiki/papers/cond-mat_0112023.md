# Selected aspects of physics of fermionic bubbles

> We discuss properties of the Fermi system which contain one or more spherical (or almost spherical) objects. The interplay between various effects, such as shell correction and chaotic behavior is considered. We briefly mention the role of the temperature, pairing, and effects associated with bubble dynamics.

# Selected aspects of physics of fermionic bubbles

## Key claim

The paper argues that fermionic bubbles, spherical impurities or inhomogeneities in a fermionic system, exhibit generic properties across many physical systems, but the abstract's claim of "interplay between various effects" glosses over the key hedge: the analysis is largely semiclassical and qualitative, with the authors explicitly noting that many aspects (such as bubble dynamics and the relative importance of Anderson's orthogonality catastrophe versus recoil) remain unresolved and "far from being fully understood."

## Method

The authors perform a semiclassical analysis of fermionic bubbles using the liquid drop model with shell corrections, deriving the interaction energy between two spherical bubbles from periodic orbit theory. They examine the shell correction energy as a function of bubble position and separation, and extend the analysis to include temperature effects via the free energy expression involving the density of states. The study is theoretical and does not present new experimental or computational results.

## Key numbers

- The shell correction energy amplitude grows approximately as ∝ N^(1/6), where N is the fermion number.
- The interaction energy between two bubbles of radius R at large separation d is given by E_shell = (1/8π)(ħ² k_F / m)(R²/d³) cos(2k_F d), where k_F is the Fermi momentum and m the fermion mass.
- The density range for phase transitions in neutron star crust (nuclei → rods → plates → tubes → bubbles → uniform matter) is 0.04–0.1 fm⁻³.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical and shell-correction approaches of Strutinsky (1967) and Balian & Bloch (1970) to the specific case of fermionic bubbles, building on earlier studies of bubble nuclei by Yu (2000) and neutron star crust by Bulgac (2000). The paper enables future investigation of collective bubble dynamics, the role of pairing correlations, and the possibility of disordered or "foamy" phases in dense fermionic matter, particularly in neutron star crusts where the authors suggest the inhomogeneous phase may have an extremely complicated structure with multiple shape types coexisting.
