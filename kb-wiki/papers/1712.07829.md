# Shadowing in the low energy photonuclear reaction

> The photonuclear reaction in the multi-GeV region occurs because of the electromagnetic and hadronic interaction. The later originates due to the hadronic fluctuation, i.e., vector meson, of the photon. The total cross section of the reaction is shadowed because of the vector meson nucleus (hadronic) interaction. To estimate it quantitatively, the cross section of the photonuclear reaction was calculated in the low energy region ($\sim 1-3$ GeV) using simple vector meson dominance (SVMD) model, i.e., the low lying vector mesons ($ρ^0$, $ω$ and $ϕ$ mesons) were considered. The nuclear shadowing is reinvestigated using generalized vector meson (GVMD) model, where the higher $ρ$ meson effective state ($ρ^\prime$ meson) is taken into account along with the low lying vector mesons. Using GVMD model, the shadowing and the total cross section of the photonuclear reaction are calculated in the above mentioned energy region. The calculated results have been compared with the measured spectra.

## Key claim

The paper claims that including a higher effective \(\rho\) meson state (\(\rho^\prime\)) via the generalized vector meson dominance (GVMD) model reduces the nuclear shadowing predicted by the simple vector meson dominance (SVMD) model and reproduces the measured transparency bump near \(E_\gamma \sim 1.6\) GeV for heavier nuclei, a feature the SVMD model underestimates.

## Method

The total cross section of the photonuclear reaction was calculated using the optical theorem applied to forward Compton scattering amplitudes within the Glauber multiple-scattering formalism. The SVMD model considered only the low-lying vector mesons (\(\rho^0\), \(\omega\), \(\phi\)), while the GVMD model added an effective \(\rho^\prime\) meson state with mass \(m_{\rho^\prime} = \sqrt{3} m_\rho\) and coupling \(\gamma_{\gamma \rho^\prime} = (m_{\rho^\prime}/m_\rho) \gamma_{\gamma \rho}\). The vector meson-nucleon scattering amplitudes were taken from experimental data and phenomenological analyses (Kondratyuk et al. for \(\rho N\), additive quark model and Regge theory for \(\omega N\), and vector meson dominance for \(\phi N\)), and the nuclear density distributions were taken from electron scattering experiments.

## Key numbers

- The photon energy range studied is \(E_\gamma \sim 1-3\) GeV.
- The \(\rho\) meson mass is \(m_\rho = 775.26\) MeV; the \(\omega\) meson mass is \(m_\omega = 782.65\) MeV; the \(\phi\) meson mass is \(m_\phi \sim 1020\) MeV.
- The intrinsic width of the \(\rho\) meson is \(\Gamma_\rho \sim 150\) MeV; the widths of the \(\omega\) and \(\phi\) mesons are \(\Gamma_\omega \sim 8.5\) MeV and \(\Gamma_\phi \sim 4.3\) MeV, respectively.
- The photon-to-vector-meson coupling constants are \(\gamma_{\gamma \rho^0} / \gamma_{\gamma \omega} / \gamma_{\gamma \phi} = 2.48 / 8.53 / 6.72\).
- The two-body correlation function uses \(q_c = 0.78\) GeV.
- The \(\rho^\prime\) meson parameters are \(m_{\rho^\prime} = \sqrt{3} m_\rho\) and \(\epsilon = 0.18\) for the scattering amplitude relation.
- The fine structure constant is \(\alpha_{em} = 1/137.036\).
- The nuclear transparency \(T = \sigma_t^{\gamma A} / (A \sigma_t^{\gamma N})\) was calculated for \(^{12}\)C, \(^{27}\)Al, \(^{63}\)Cu, \(^{112}\)Sn, and \(^{208}\)Pb.
- A bump in the transparency for heavier nuclei appears at \(E_\gamma \sim 1.6\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the SVMD-based Glauber analysis of Falter et al. (2004) for photonuclear shadowing in the 1–3 GeV region by incorporating the GVMD model, following the approach of Pautz and Shaw (1998) for \(\rho\) meson photoproduction. The GVMD model had previously been used by Frankfurt et al. (1999) to study shadowing in deep inelastic scattering and by Ditsas and Shaw (1976) for photonuclear reactions above 3 GeV. The present calculation shows that the GVMD model, with an effective \(\rho^\prime\) meson, better reproduces the measured transparency for heavy nuclei, especially the bump near 1.6 GeV, while the SVMD model works well for lighter nuclei across the full energy range.
