# Measuring intensity interference in a low multiplicity system $ππx$ with a new observing method

> Prior proposed observing approaches using event mixing technique for Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) measurements in exclusive reactions with very low multiplicities are still unsatisfactory due to the problems of sample reduction and introducing extra and unnecessary fitting parameters. We here propose an event mixing method with a new mixing cut, named energy sum range (ESR) cut, to investigate two-pion Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) in reactions with only two identical pions among three final state particles. This mixing method employs two-pion energy sum characteristic to control the mixing procedures, with no requirement on eliminating any original events. Numerical simulations are performed to show the viability of this new BEC observing method.

## Key claim

The proposed event mixing method with the energy sum range (ESR) cut can measure two-pion Bose-Einstein correlations in exclusive reactions with only three final-state particles, but it still introduces systematic bias: the extracted source radius \(r_0\) is about 5% overestimated and the chaoticity parameter \(\lambda_2\) is about 17% underestimated compared to the input values, requiring correction in practical applications.

## Method

The authors generate pure phase-space Monte Carlo events for the reaction \(\gamma p \to \pi^{0} \pi^{0} p\) at six incident photon energies from 1.00 to 1.15 GeV using the ROOT TGenPhaseSpace utility. They construct a reference sample by mixing two original events subject to two constraints: the missing mass consistency (MMC) cut, which forces the mixed event's missing mass to match that of the original events, and the new energy sum range (ESR) cut, which requires that the two-pion energy sums of the two original events differ by less than \(E_{cut}\) and that the mixed event's energy sum lies between the two original values. The optimum \(E_{cut}\) is empirically set to \(0.32 E_{\gamma}\) by minimizing the slope of the correlation function for non-BEC samples. BEC-effect samples are generated by weighting the phase-space events according to a Gaussian correlation function with input parameters \(r_0 = 0.8\) fm and \(\lambda_2 = 1.0\), and the correlation function is fitted to extract the BEC parameters.

## Key numbers

- The optimum ESR cut window is \(E_{cut} = 0.32 E_{\gamma}\), determined from pure phase-space samples at six incident photon energies (1.00, 1.03, 1.06, 1.09, 1.12, 1.15 GeV).
- The weighted mean fitted source radius over the six energies is \(r_0 = 0.83 \pm 0.02\) fm, compared to the input mean of \(0.79 \pm 0.01\) fm (about 5% overestimation).
- The weighted mean fitted chaoticity parameter is \(\lambda_2 = 0.79 \pm 0.02\), compared to the input mean of \(0.95 \pm 0.02\) (about 17% underestimation).
- At \(E_{\gamma} = 1.15\) GeV, the fit yields \(r_0 = 0.81 \pm 0.04\) fm and \(\lambda_2 = 0.74 \pm 0.06\) with \(\chi^2/ndf = 31.5/13\).
- The input BEC parameters for the simulated BEC samples are typically set to \(r_0 = 0.8\) fm and \(\lambda_2 = 1.0\).

## Context

This work extends prior efforts by He et al. (2016, 2018) to develop event mixing methods for two-pion Bose-Einstein correlations in the low-multiplicity system \(\pi\pi X\), specifically for the reaction \(\gamma p \to \pi^{0} \pi^{0} p\) at incident photon energies around 1 GeV. Earlier methods using a pion energy (PE) cut required discarding about 40% of original events, while later approaches using energy sum order or invariant-mass/energy hierarchy cuts solved the sample reduction problem but introduced extra fitting parameters that worsened accuracy. The new ESR cut avoids both issues and yields smaller systematic bias than those prior methods, but the remaining bias (5% in \(r_0\), 17% in \(\lambda_2\)) still requires correction. This enables future experimental measurements of nucleon resonance sizes via BEC in exclusive photoproduction reactions, though detector acceptance effects such as cluster overlapping in electromagnetic calorimeters must also be addressed.
