# Planetary Impacts by Clustered Quark Matter Strangelets

> We propose a model of clustered u-d-s quark matter that leads to stable bulk strange quark matter. We discuss qualitatively consequences of impacts by sub-planetary mass strangelets on rocky solar system bodies.

# Planetary Impacts by Clustered Quark Matter Strangelets

## Key claim
Clustered quark matter incorporating a massive strange quark can produce an absolutely stable minimum in energy per baryon, meaning that strangelets of sufficient size, once formed, would never decay. The stability is sensitive to the bag constant and strange quark mass, with the model showing that a stable bound state exists for physically plausible parameter values.

## Method
The authors extend the clustered quark model (CQM) of Clark (1986) to include a massive strange quark, adding a third Fermi sea of free strange quarks and correlated 3-quark states corresponding to the four lowest mass strange baryons (Λ and Σ±,0). The thermodynamic potential is computed using the excluded volume method as a function of baryon density, with chemical equilibrium between quark flavors enforced by weak interactions and baryon number conservation.

## Key numbers
- Strangelet mass range considered for impacts: 10⁻¹² < M/M⊕ < 0.01, where M⊕ = 6×10²⁴ kg is Earth's mass
- Strange quark mass at 2 GeV: m_s = 104⁺²⁶₋₃₄ MeV, based on PDG (2010) and recent lattice results
- Bag constant values used: B¹/⁴ = 155 MeV (corresponding to 4B = 0.247 GeV/fm³) and B¹/⁴ = 171 MeV
- Strange quark mass varied from m_s = 90 MeV to m_s = 140 MeV in the calculations
- Clustered strange quark matter becomes energetically favored at densities above nuclear density, specifically for 1/n < 5 fm³
- Large strangelet mass range for impact features: 10⁻¹² < M/M⊕ < 10⁻⁵

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of strange quark matter stability originally developed by Madsen (1999) and the clustered quark model of Clark (1986). The authors apply their model to planetary impacts, building on prior seismic searches for strangelet passage through Earth by Herrin (2005) and the Moon by Banerdt (2006). The paper enables a new observational strategy: searching for persistent geologic impact features from large strangelets rather than relying on real-time seismic detection, and suggests that such impacts could explain several unresolved geologic puzzles including mantle plumes, the AD 536 dust veil, and volcanic activity on the Moon, Mercury, and Mars.
