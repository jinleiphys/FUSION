# Far-forward neutrinos at the Large Hadron Collider

> We present a new calculation of the energy distribution of high-energy neutrinos from the decay of charm and bottom hadrons produced at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). In the kinematical region of very forward rapidities, heavy-flavor production and decay is a source of tau neutrinos that leads to thousands of { charged-current} tau neutrino events in a 1 m long, 1 m radius lead neutrino detector at a distance of 480 m from the interaction region. In our computation, next-to-leading order QCD radiative corrections are accounted for in the production cross-sections. Non-perturbative intrinsic-$k_T$ effects are approximated by a simple phenomenological model introducing a Gaussian $k_T$-smearing of the parton distribution functions, which might also mimic perturbative effects due to multiple initial-state soft-gluon emissions. The transition from partonic to hadronic states is described by phenomenological fragmentation functions. To study the effect of various input parameters, theoretical predictions for $D_s^\pm$ production are compared with LHCb data on double-differential cross-sections in transverse momentum and rapidity. The uncertainties related to the choice of the input parameter values, ultimately affecting the predictions of the tau neutrino event distributions, are discussed. We consider a 3+1 neutrino mixing scenario to illustrate the potential for a neutrino experiment to constrain the 3+1 parameter space using tau neutrinos and antineutrinos. We find large theoretical uncertainties in the predictions of the neutrino fluxes in the far-forward region. Untangling the effects of tau neutrino oscillations into sterile neutrinos and distinguishing a 3+1 scenario from the standard scenario with three active neutrino flavours, will be challenging due to the large theoretical uncertainties from QCD.

## Key claim

The paper presents a next-to-leading order QCD calculation of tau neutrino fluxes from charm and bottom hadron decays at the LHC, finding that thousands of charged-current tau neutrino events could be detected in a 1 m long, 1 m radius lead detector at 480 m from the interaction point. However, the authors emphasize that large theoretical uncertainties from QCD scale choices and non-perturbative intrinsic-\(k_T\) effects make it challenging to distinguish a 3+1 sterile neutrino scenario from the standard three-active-neutrino scenario using these tau neutrinos.

## Method

The authors computed heavy-flavor hadron production at NLO in QCD using collinear factorization, with non-perturbative intrinsic-\(k_T\) effects approximated by a Gaussian smearing of parton distribution functions. Hadronization was described by phenomenological fragmentation functions, and the transition from partonic to hadronic states used the Peterson fragmentation function with \(\epsilon=0.335\). The theoretical predictions for \(D_s^\pm\) double-differential cross sections in transverse momentum and rapidity were compared to LHCb data to constrain the factorization scale parameter \(N_F\) and the average intrinsic transverse momentum \(\langle k_T\rangle\), with the renormalization scale fixed to \(N_R=1.0\) and the default factorization scale set to \(N_F=1.5\).

## Key numbers

- For a 1 m long, 1 m radius lead neutrino detector at 480 m from the LHC interaction region, the predicted number of charged-current tau neutrino events ranges from 1800 to 11200 depending on the QCD scale choice.
- The best fit to LHCb \(D_s^+\) data yields \(N_F = 1.44\) and \(\langle k_T\rangle = 2.23\) GeV with \(\chi^2/{\rm DOF} = 2.68\).
- The non-perturbative intrinsic \(\langle k_T\rangle\) is expected to be \(\sim 0.5\)–\(1\) GeV, while the phenomenological best-fit value is 2.2 GeV.
- The contribution from \(B\)-mesons to the \(\nu_\tau + \bar{\nu}_\tau\) energy distribution is one to two orders of magnitude smaller than the contribution from \(D\)-mesons, depending on energy.

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of prompt neutrino fluxes from atmospheric and accelerator sources by focusing on the far-forward region at the LHC, where heavy-flavor production and decay become a significant source of tau neutrinos. The authors compare their results to LHCb data on \(D_s\) production (LHCb Collaboration, 2018) and note that the FASER\(\nu\), XSEN, and SND@LHC proposals (all from 2019–2020) are the primary near-term experiments that could measure these neutrinos. The large QCD scale uncertainties identified here imply that future forward heavy-flavor measurements (e.g., by FASER\(\nu\)) will be essential to reduce theoretical uncertainties and enable searches for sterile neutrino oscillations.
