# The structure of superheavy elements newly discovered in the reaction of $^{86}$Kr with $^{208}$Pb

> The structure of superheavy elements newly discovered in the $^{208}$Pb($^{86}$Kr,n) reaction at Berkeley is systematically studied in the Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) approach. It is shown that various usually employed RMF forces, which give fair description of normal stable nuclei, give quite different predictions for superheavy elements. Among the effective forces we tested, TM1 is found to be the good candidate to describe superheavy elements. The binding energies of the $^{293}$118 nucleus and its $α-$decay daughter nuclei obtained using TM1 agree with those of FRDM within 2 MeV. Similar conclusion that TM1 is the good interaction is also drawn from the calculated binding energies for Pb isotopes with the Relativistic Continuum Hartree Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory. Using the pairing gaps obtained from RCHB, RMF calculations with pairing and deformation are carried out for the structure of superheavy elements. The binding energy, shape, single particle levels, and the Q values of the $α-$decay $Q_α$ are discussed, and it is shown that both pairing correlation and deformation are essential to properly understand the structure of superheavy elements. A good agreement is obtained with experimental data on $Q_α$. %Especially, the atomic number %dependence of $Q_α$ %seems to match with the experimental observation

## Key claim

Among several Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) forces that describe normal stable nuclei equally well, the TM1 parameter set is the best candidate for describing superheavy elements, but only when both pairing correlation and deformation are included simultaneously can the calculated alpha-decay Q-values agree with experimental data.

## Method

The authors performed systematic RMF calculations for the superheavy nucleus 293118 and its alpha-decay daughter nuclei using four different effective forces (TM1, NL1, NL3, NLSH) and compared the results with the macroscopic-microscopic FRDM approach. They then used the Relativistic Continuum Hartree Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory under spherical symmetry to obtain pairing gaps, and finally carried out deformed RMF+BCS calculations incorporating both deformation and the RCHB-derived pairing gaps.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy of 293118 with TM1: 2074.7 MeV; with FRDM: 2077.2 MeV (difference less than 3 MeV)
- Binding energy of 265104 with TM1: 1949.2 MeV; with FRDM: 1950.0 MeV (within 1 MeV)
- Q-value for alpha decay of 293118 with DRMF+BCS: 12.0 MeV; experimental data show a peak at Z=114
- Q-value for alpha decay of 285114 with DRMF+BCS: 13.2 MeV; FRDM gives 9.4 MeV for the same nucleus
- Deformation parameter beta for 293118 with TM1: 0.2340; with FRDM: 0.080
- Charge radius of 293118 with TM1: 6.360 fm; with NL1: 6.276 fm (within 1% across all forces)
- Binding energy of 208Pb with TM1 in RCHB: 1637.76 MeV; experimental value: 1636.45 MeV

## Context

This work extends the study of superheavy elements following the discovery of 293118 at Berkeley Lab's 88-Inch Cyclotron via the 208Pb(86Kr,n) reaction (Ninov 1999), which was motivated by the production rate predictions of Smolanczuk (1999). The authors contrast their fully microscopic RMF approach with the macroscopic-microscopic FRDM method of Moller (1995) and with the spherical calculations of Smolanczuk (1997). The results enable future investigations into the shape dependence of pairing gaps and the validity of the ground-state-to-ground-state assumption for the observed alpha-decay chain, as well as comparison with the work of Cwiok et al. (1999) who predicted systematically smaller deformations.
