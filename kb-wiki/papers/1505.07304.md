# Outer crust of a cold non-accreting magnetar

> The outer crust structure and composition of a cold, non-accreting magnetar is studied. We model the outer crust to be made of fully equilibrated matter where ionized nuclei form a Coulomb crystal embedded in an electron gas. The main effects of the strong magnetic field are those of quantizing the electron motion in Landau levels and of modifying the nuclear single particle levels producing, on average, an increased binding of nucleons in nuclei present in the Coulomb lattice. The effect of an homogeneous and constant magnetic field on nuclear masses has been predicted by using a covariant density functional, in which induced currents and axial deformation due to the presence of a magnetic field that breaks time-reversal symmetry have been included self-consistently in the nucleon and meson equations of motion. Although not yet observed, for $B\gtrsim 10^{16}$G both effects contribute to produce different compositions and to enlarge the range of pressures typically present in common neutron stars. Specifically, in such a regime, the magnetic field effects on nuclei favor the appearance of heavier nuclei at low pressures. As $B$ increases, such heavier nuclei are also preferred up to larger pressures. In the most extreme case, the whole outer crust is almost made of ${}_{40}^{92}$Zr$_{52}$.

# Outer crust of a cold non-accreting magnetar

## Key claim

For magnetic fields above approximately 10^16 G, both the quantizing effect on electrons and the magnetic-field-induced increase in nuclear binding energies (computed self-consistently via a covariant density functional) can change the composition of the outer crust and enlarge the range of pressures compared to typical neutron stars; however, these effects are model-dependent and become clearly distinguishable only at the most extreme fields considered (up to 10^18 G), where the entire outer crust may consist almost entirely of 92Zr.

## Method

The authors model the outer crust as fully equilibrated, zero-temperature matter composed of a body-centered cubic Coulomb lattice of fully ionized nuclei embedded in a uniform relativistic electron gas, subject to a constant, homogeneous magnetic field. The electron motion is quantized into Landau levels, and the nuclear binding energies are computed using two covariant density functionals (NL3 and DD-ME2) that include, self-consistently, the coupling of the external magnetic field to both the proton orbital motion and the intrinsic magnetic dipole moments of protons and neutrons, breaking time-reversal symmetry and inducing axial deformation. The equilibrium composition at each pressure is found by minimizing the Gibbs free energy per baryon, scanning over a large set of nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The critical magnetic field is defined as B_c = m_e^2/e ≈ 4.41 × 10^13 G.
- For B ≳ 10^16 G, both electron quantization and nuclear binding effects contribute to produce different compositions and enlarge the pressure range of the outer crust.
- At the most extreme field considered, B = 10^18 G, and for the studied models, the whole outer crust is almost made of 92Zr (Z=40, N=52).
- On average, the binding energy of nuclei present in the outer crust does not increase by more than 10% when magnetic fields of B ∼ 10^17-18 G are taken into account.
- The neutron-drip pressure P_drip is predicted to increase linearly with B_star (the field in units of B_c) and quadratically with the binding energy of the drip nucleus, leading to roughly one order of magnitude increase in P_drip between B = 10^16 G and B = 10^17 G.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutron star outer crust composition in the absence of magnetic fields (Baym 1971, Ruester 2006, Roca-Maza 2008) and in the presence of magnetic fields (Lai 1991, Chamel 2012, Pearson 2011, Wolf 2013, Kreim 2013) by including, for the first time, the self-consistent effect of the external magnetic field on nuclear binding energies via covariant density functional theory. The results enable predictions for the structure and composition of magnetar crusts at fields up to the theoretical maximum of 10^18 G, and provide a framework for identifying observable signatures of magnetic field effects on nuclear masses in future X-ray observations of soft gamma-ray repeaters and anomalous X-ray pulsars.
