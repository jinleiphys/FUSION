# Understanding the Optical Potential in HBT Interferometry

> The validity of using a pion optical potential to incorporate the effects of final state interactions on HBT interferometry is investigated. We find that if the optical potential is real, the standard formalism is modified as previously described in the literature. However, if the optical potential is complex, a new term involving pion emission from eliminated states must be included. The size of such effects in previous work by Cramer and Miller is assessed.

# Understanding the Optical Potential in HBT Interferometry

## Key claim

The standard distorted-wave emission function (DWEF) formalism for HBT interferometry is valid when the optical potential is real, but if the optical potential is complex, a new term involving pion emission from eliminated states must be included; however, in the specific prior work by Cramer and Miller (2004, 2005), the imaginary part of the optical potential is small enough that setting it to zero does not qualitatively change the description of the data.

## Method

The authors re-derive the correlation function for two-pion HBT interferometry starting from a Lagrangian with a random classical source, first using plane waves (the Pratt formalism), then using distorted waves with a real optical potential, and finally using a coupled-channels formalism to handle a complex optical potential. The coupled-channels approach explicitly includes the infinite number of medium states that are eliminated when constructing a complex optical potential, and the correction terms from states with n>1 are identified. The numerical impact of the imaginary part is assessed by performing DWEF fits to STAR data with the imaginary part set to a vanishingly small value and comparing the quality of the fit.

## Key numbers

- The imaginary part of the optical potential (represented by the term w2) is about one tenth of the real potential in the work of Miller (2005).
- When the imaginary part is removed, the temperature drops from about 160 MeV to about 121 MeV, and the emission duration Delta-tau drops to 0 fm/c.
- The chi-squared value for the fit with the full complex optical potential is 117, while the fit with vanishing imaginary part gives chi-squared = 300.
- The fit with no optical potential at all gives chi-squared = 1068, with the pion effective mass mu_pi = 83.5 MeV.
- The fit with vanishing imaginary part but emission duration fixed to 1.5 fm/c gives chi-squared = 451.

## Context

This work extends the DWEF formalism developed by Cramer and Miller (2004, 2005), which used distorted waves with a complex optical potential to describe HBT radii and pion spectra simultaneously. The paper addresses conceptual questions raised in the literature about the meaning of the imaginary part of the optical potential and the relationship between sources and the optical potential, following earlier work by Gyulassy (1979), Barz (1996, 1998), Kapusta (2005), and Wong (2003) on final state interactions in HBT interferometry. The results enable a clearer understanding of when the DWEF formalism is sufficient and when explicit treatment of eliminated medium states is needed, particularly for systems with strong quantum opacity.
