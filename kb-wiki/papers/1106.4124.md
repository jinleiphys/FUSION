# Towards thermodynamics of the quark quasi-particles

> Some features of hot and dense gas of quarks which are considered as the quasi-particles of the model Hamiltonian with four-fermion interaction are studied. Being adapted to the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model this approach allows us to accommodate a phase transition similar to the nuclear liquid-gas one at the proper scale and to argue an existence of the mixed phase of vacuum and normal baryonic matter as a plausible scenario of chiral symmetry (partial) restoration. Analyzing the transition layer between two phases we estimate the surface tension coefficient and discuss the possibility of quark droplet formation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that within the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, a hot and dense gas of quark quasi-particles can undergo a first-order phase transition analogous to nuclear liquid-gas transition, with a mixed phase of vacuum and normal baryonic matter representing a plausible scenario for partial chiral symmetry restoration; however, the authors caution that their quantitative estimates (e.g., critical temperature ~45.7 MeV) should not be directly compared to experimentally measured nuclear matter values (15-20 MeV) because they use a simplified one-flavor NJL model with a standard parameter set rather than aiming to reproduce nuclear matter properties.

## Method

The authors study quark ensembles at finite temperature and fixed baryonic chemical potential within the NJL model using a Bogolyubov-Hartree-Fock approximation, where the statistical operator is constructed from an approximating Hamiltonian quadratic in quasi-particle creation/annihilation operators. They minimize the thermodynamic potential (grand canonical ensemble) to obtain equilibrium densities of quarks and anti-quarks, and solve the gap equation for the dynamical quark mass. For the transition layer between gas and liquid phases at zero temperature, they employ a Thomas-Fermi approximation within an effective quark-meson Lagrangian (Walecka-type model) to compute surface tension and study spherically-symmetric quark droplet solutions.

## Key numbers

- Dynamical quark mass at zero temperature, zero density: M_q = 335 MeV (with cutoff Lambda = 631 MeV, current quark mass m = 5.5 MeV, and coupling G*Lambda^2/(2*pi^2) = 1.3)
- Critical temperature for the liquid-gas phase transition: T_c ~ 45.7 MeV, with critical charge density ~0.12 charge/fm^3
- Liquid phase density at zero temperature (in equilibrium with gas phase): rho_l = 3 * 0.185 ch/fm^3 (factor 3 relates quark to baryon density)
- Dynamical quark mass in the liquid phase at zero temperature: approximately 70 MeV
- Vacuum pressure estimate for the NJL model: 40-50 MeV/fm^3
- Gas component density at T=0: approximately 0.01 of normal nuclear density (normal nuclear density ~0.16/fm^3)
- Surface tension coefficient u_s (MeV) shown as function of parameter c for stable kinks with eta <= 1.2 (Fig. 4, numerical values not explicitly given in text)
- Droplet properties from Table 1 (N_f=1): for N_q = 15, central density ~0.34 ch/fm^3, radius R_0 = 1.84 fm, surface thickness t = 2.24 fm, r_0 = 0.74 fm; for N_q = 742, central density ~0.47 ch/fm^3, R_0 = 7.15 fm, t = 2.27 fm, r_0 = 0.79 fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Molodtsov and Zinovjev, 2009; Molodtsov, 2010) where quarks were treated as quasi-particles and the filling of the Fermi sphere was analyzed, revealing discontinuities in the mean energy functional and new solution branches for dynamical quark mass. The approach provides a microscopic foundation for the conventional bag model and suggests that quark-filled states could serve as "construction material" for baryons. The results enable further investigation of finite quark droplet properties and the transition layer between vacuum and baryonic matter, with open questions about the precise critical temperature (which differs from experimentally measured nuclear matter values of 15-20 MeV) and the treatment of non-uniform quark condensates using more advanced methods (e.g., Klevansky and Neise, 1996).
