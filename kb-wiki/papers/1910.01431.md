# Possibility of rapid neutron star cooling with the realistic equation of state

> Whether fast cooling processes occur or not is crucial for the thermal evolution of neutron stars. In particular, the threshold of the direct Urca process, which is one of the fast cooling processes, is determined by the interior proton fraction $Y_p$, or the nuclear symmetry energy. Since recent observations indicate the small radius of neutron stars, a low value is preferred for the symmetry energy. In this study, simulations of neutron star cooling are performed adopting three models for equation of state (EoS): Togashi, Shen, and LS220 EoSs. The Togashi EoS has been recently constructed with realistic nuclear potentials under finite temperature, and found to account for the small radius of neutron stars. As a result, we find that, since the direct Urca process is forbidden, the neutron star cooling is slow with use of the Togashi EoS. This is because symmetry energy of Togashi EoS is lower than those of other EoSs. Hence, in order to account for observed age and surface temperature of isolated neutron stars (INS) with use of the Togashi EoS, other fast cooling processes are needed regardless of the surface composition.

## Key claim

The Togashi equation of state (EoS), which is preferred by observations indicating a small neutron star radius (less than 13 km), forbids the direct Urca process due to its low symmetry energy; consequently, cooling curves with this EoS are too slow to match observed low-temperature isolated neutron stars, regardless of surface composition or superfluid models.

## Method

The authors performed spherically symmetric neutron star cooling simulations by solving the general relativistic stellar structure equations (TOV equations) for masses from 1.1 to 2.1 solar masses, adopting three finite-temperature EoS models: Togashi, Shen, and LS220. They included neutrino emission from modified Urca, bremsstrahlung, nucleon pair breaking formation (PBF), and direct Urca processes, and tested four combinations of superfluid models for protons (AO and CCDK for 1S0 state) and neutrons (BEEHS and EEHO for 3P2 state). Cooling curves were compared with observational data for 18 isolated neutron stars and Cassiopeia A.

## Key numbers

- Togashi EoS saturation parameters: symmetry energy S0 = 30.0 MeV, symmetry energy slope L = 35.0 MeV, incompressibility K = 245 MeV, saturation density rho0 = 2.66 x 10^14 g cm^-3
- Shen EoS: S0 = 36.9 MeV, L = 111 MeV, K = 281 MeV, rho0 = 2.41 x 10^14 g cm^-3
- LS220 EoS: S0 = 28.6 MeV, L = 73.8 MeV, K = 220 MeV, rho0 = 2.57 x 10^14 g cm^-3
- Maximum neutron star masses: Togashi 2.21 solar masses, Shen 2.17 solar masses, LS220 2.04 solar masses
- Direct Urca threshold proton fraction Yp_DU = 0.1477 when muons are present, or 1/9 when muons are absent
- Modified Urca neutrino loss rate approximately 10^19-21 erg cm^-3 s^-1; direct Urca emissivity around 10^27 erg cm^-3 s^-1
- For LS220 EoS, direct Urca turns on for neutron stars with mass greater than or approximately 1.4 solar masses, but not for 1.1 solar masses
- For Shen EoS, direct Urca turns on for all masses considered (1.1 to 2.1 solar masses)
- For Togashi EoS, direct Urca is disallowed even for the maximum-mass neutron star (2.21 solar masses)
- Critical symmetry energy for direct Urca in npe matter: S_DU(u) approximately 50.7 (u n0 / 0.155 fm^-3)^(1/3) MeV

## Context

This work extends previous neutron star cooling studies (Tsuruta 1964, Page 2004, Yakovlev 2004, Noda 2013) by focusing on the EoS dependence of cooling curves, particularly the Togashi EoS (Togashi 2017) which was constructed with realistic nuclear potentials and accounts for the small neutron star radius suggested by observations of GW170817 (Abbott 2018) and low-mass X-ray binaries (Steiner 2010). The results contradict the ability of the Togashi EoS to explain observed low-temperature isolated neutron stars, unlike the LS220 EoS which can match observations when combined with appropriate superfluid models (EEHO for 3P2 neutrons). This implies that if the symmetry energy is as low as required by radius constraints, other fast cooling processes involving exotic particles (hyperons, pions, kaons, quarks) may be necessary, as discussed in Lim (2019) and Raduta (2019).
