# Anisotropic multicluster model in light nuclei

> Multicluster models consider that the nucleons can be moving around different centers in the nuclei. These models have been widely used to describe light nuclei but always considering that the mean field is composed of isotropic harmonic oscillators with different centers. In this work, we propose an extension of these models by using anisotropic harmonic oscillators. The strenghts of these oscillators, the distance among the different centers and the disposition of the nucleons inside every cluster are free parameters which have been fixed using the variational criterion. We have used a hamiltonian with the kinetic energy terms and a phenomenological two-body potential like Volkov V2 potential. All the one-body and two-body matrix elements have been analytically calculated. Only a numerical integration on the Euler angles, it is needed to carry out the projection on the values of the total spin of the state and its third component. We have studied the ground state and the first excited states of 8 Be, 12 C and 10 Be getting good results for the energies. The disposition of the nucleons in the different clusters have been also analyzed by using projection on the different cartesian planes getting much more information than when the radial one-body density is used.

# Anisotropic multicluster model in light nuclei

## Key claim

The authors propose extending standard multicluster models, which have always used isotropic harmonic oscillators centered at different points, by allowing anisotropic (deformed) harmonic oscillators for each cluster, and they show that this yields improved ground-state binding energies and better agreement with experimental excitation energies for $^8$Be, $^{12}$C, and $^{10}$Be, though the improvement is modest for intrinsically deformed configurations.

## Method

The authors construct Slater determinants from single-particle wavefunctions that are eigenstates of anisotropic harmonic oscillators centered at different spatial points, with the oscillator strengths, inter-cluster distances, and nucleon distributions treated as variational parameters. They use the Volkov V2 nucleon-nucleon interaction with a spin-orbit term and Coulomb interaction, compute all one- and two-body matrix elements analytically, and perform a numerical integration over Euler angles to project onto states of definite total angular momentum, parity, and isospin. The method is applied to the ground and first excited states of $^8$Be, $^{12}$C, and $^{10}$Be using various cluster configurations.

## Key numbers

- $^8$Be ground state (one center, isotropic): -39.20 MeV; with anisotropic oscillators: -50.46 MeV (an increase of about 25% in binding energy)
- $^8$Be ground state (two $\alpha$ clusters, isotropic): -53.20 MeV; with anisotropic oscillators: -54.04 MeV (only 0.8 MeV improvement)
- $^8$Be $2^+$ excitation energy (two clusters, anisotropic): 3.49 MeV (experimental: 3.04 MeV)
- $^8$Be $4^+$ excitation energy (two clusters, anisotropic): 12.24 MeV (experimental: 11.40 MeV)
- $^{12}$C ground state (one center, isotropic): -76.06 MeV; with anisotropic oscillators: -84.06 MeV (about 10% increase)
- $^{12}$C ground state (three $\alpha$ clusters, equilateral triangle, isotropic): -86.56 MeV; with anisotropic oscillators: -89.17 MeV (about 2.6% improvement)
- $^{12}$C $3^-$ excitation energy (isosceles triangle, anisotropic): 9.55 MeV (experimental: 9.64 MeV)
- $^{10}$Be ground state (three centers $2\alpha+nn$, anisotropic): -58.78 MeV
- $^{10}$Be ground state (two centers $^6$He$+\alpha$, anisotropic): -59.23 MeV
- $^{10}$Be $2^+$ excitation energy (two centers $^6$He$+\alpha$, anisotropic): 4.35 MeV (experimental: 3.37 MeV)
- Volkov V2 potential parameters: $V_1 = -60.65$ MeV, $V_2 = 61.14$ MeV, $a_1 = 1.80$ fm, $a_2 = 1.01$ fm, $M = 0.6$
- Spin-orbit parameters: $S_0 = 30$ MeV fm$^5$, $r_0 = 0.1$ fm

## Context

This work extends the multicluster model tradition originating from Wheeler's Resonating Group Model (1937), Margenau's Slater determinant approach (1941), and Brink's Generator Coordinate Method (1967), which have all used isotropic harmonic oscillators. The results for $^{12}$C with isotropic oscillators agree with those of Dufour and Descouvemont (1996). The anisotropic extension allows the model to capture polarization effects induced by asymmetric cluster distributions, and the authors note that for $^{12}$C the anisotropic results fit quite well with those obtained using the Generator Coordinate Method. The work enables more flexible descriptions of light nuclei, particularly neutron-rich nuclei like $^{10}$Be where multiple cluster configurations compete, and suggests that the different configurations studied may have important linear dependence among them.
