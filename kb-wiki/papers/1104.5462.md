# Super-Radiant Dynamics, Doorways, and Resonances in Nuclei and Other Open Mesoscopic Systems

> The phenomenon of super-radiance (Dicke effect, coherent spontaneous radiation by a gas of atoms coupled through the common radiation field) is well known in quantum optics. The review discusses similar physics that emerges in open and marginally stable quantum many-body systems. In the presence of open decay channels, the intrinsic states are coupled through the continuum. At sufficiently strong continuum coupling, the spectrum of resonances undergoes the restructuring with segregation of very broad super-radiant states and trapping of remaining long-lived compound states. The appropriate formalism describing this phenomenon is based on the Feshbach projection method and effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian. A broader generalization is related to the idea of doorway states connecting quantum states of different structure. The method is explained in detail and the examples of applications are given to nuclear, atomic and particle physics. The interrelation of the collective dynamics through continuum and possible intrinsic many-body chaos is studied, including universal mesoscopic conductance fluctuations. The theory serves as a natural framework for general description of a quantum signal transmission through an open mesoscopic system.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the super-radiant transition, a sharp restructuring of overlapping resonances into very broad (short-lived) and very narrow (trapped) states, occurs generically in any open quantum system when the continuum coupling strength becomes comparable to or exceeds the typical level spacing, and that this phenomenon survives even in the presence of intrinsic many-body chaos.

## Method

The authors derive an effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian using the Feshbach projection method, where the Hilbert space is split into an intrinsic discrete subspace (Q) and a continuum subspace (P). By formally eliminating the continuum, they obtain an energy-dependent Hamiltonian whose anti-Hermitian part has a factorized structure proportional to the number of open channels. They then analyze the eigenvalue problem of this effective Hamiltonian in various regimes, from weak continuum coupling (isolated resonances with Porter-Thomas width distributions) to strong continuum coupling (super-radiant states and trapped states), and illustrate the formalism with examples from nuclear continuum shell model calculations, a one-dimensional lattice transport model, and doorway-state dynamics.

## Key numbers

* In the one-dimensional lattice model with N cells, hopping amplitude v, and edge decay widths γ_L and γ_R, the weak-coupling limit (γ_L,R ≪ v) gives widths Γ_q = γ_L|⟨1|q⟩|² + γ_R|⟨N|q⟩|² for each Bloch state q; in the strong-coupling limit (γ_L,R ≫ v), two super-radiant states appear at complex energies E_L,R = −(i/2)γ_L,R, while the remaining N−2 states acquire small widths ∝ (v/γ_L,R)².
* For the optical super-radiance of N identical two-level atoms, the maximum coherent radiation probability at M=0 is proportional to |A|² S(S+1) with S = N/2, scaling as N², compared to the incoherent spontaneous radiation probability ∝ N.
* The total width of all resonances in any system equals the trace of the anti-Hermitian matrix W, which for the lattice model is γ_L + γ_R.

## Context

This review extends the concept of super-radiance, originally discovered by Dicke (1954) in quantum optics, to nuclear, atomic, molecular, and mesoscopic systems. It builds on the effective Hamiltonian formalism developed from Feshbach (1958, 1962) and Fano (1961), and on earlier work by Sokolov, Zelevinsky, and collaborators (1988–1989) who first identified the analogy between overlapping nuclear resonances and optical super-radiance. The paper enables a unified treatment of open quantum systems, including the continuum shell model for nuclei far from stability, doorway-state descriptions of giant resonances, and the connection between Ericson fluctuations in nuclear reactions and universal conductance fluctuations in mesoscopic physics (Beenakker, 1997).
