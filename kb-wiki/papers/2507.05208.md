# Extraction of ground-state nuclear deformations from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions: Nuclear structure physics context

> The collective-flow-assisted nuclear shape-imaging method in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions has recently been used to characterize nuclear collective states. In this paper, we assess the foundations of the shape-imaging technique employed in these studies. We argue that some current UHIC nuclear imaging techniques neglect fundamental aspects of spontaneous symmetry-breaking and symmetry-restoration in colliding ions and incorrectly infer one-body multipole moments from studies of nucleonic correlations. Therefore, the impact of this approach on nuclear structure research has been overstated. Conversely, efforts to incorporate existing knowledge on nuclear shapes into analysis pipelines can be beneficial for benchmarking tools and calibrating models used to extract information from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion experiments.

# Extraction of ground-state nuclear deformations from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions: Nuclear structure physics context

## Key claim

The collective-flow-assisted nuclear shape-imaging method used in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions neglects fundamental aspects of spontaneous symmetry-breaking and symmetry-restoration in colliding ions and incorrectly infers one-body multipole moments from studies of nucleonic correlations; therefore, the impact of this approach on nuclear structure research has been overstated. Conversely, incorporating existing knowledge on nuclear shapes into analysis pipelines can be beneficial for benchmarking tools and calibrating models used to extract information from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion experiments.

## Method

The authors critically assess the theoretical foundations of the shape-imaging technique by examining symmetry-breaking and symmetry-restoration concepts in the context of two-body densities, the notion of "shape fluctuations," and the existing body of knowledge on nuclear deformations from low-energy measurements. They analyze the uncertainty quantification challenges in the multiscale modeling pipeline that connects nuclear structure models to final-state observables in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, and evaluate whether current approaches properly account for the quantum-mechanical nature of nuclear ground states.

## Key numbers

- The spectroscopic quadrupole moment of the $3/2^+$ ground state of $^{197}$Au is $Q = +0.547(16)$ b, obtained from muonic x-ray spectroscopy (Stone 2021).
- Reference Stone (2021) lists no less than 50 experimental techniques that form the basis for compiled quadrupole moments, with uncertainties of 1% or less for stable nuclei.
- The quadrupole moment of $^{28}$Na was determined from $\beta$-NMR spectroscopy with optical pumping via a collinear laser beam (Keim 2000).

## Context

This paper extends the critical discussion of shape-imaging methods used by the STAR collaboration (Abdulhamid 2024, STAR 2025) and others (Giacalone 2020, 2021, 2025; Bally 2022, 2023; Jia 2022, 2024) by pointing out fundamental quantum-mechanical flaws in their interpretation. The authors contrast the UHIC approach with decades of low-energy nuclear structure measurements, including quadrupole moments from hyperfine splitting in mesic atoms (Fitch 1953, Cooper 1953, Wheeler 1953), laser spectroscopy (Neyens 2003, Yang 2023), electron scattering (Hofstadter 1957, Donnely 1975), and Coulomb excitation (Cline 1986, Zielinska 2022), as well as theoretical predictions from mean-field theory (Moller 2008, 2016; Goriely 2016; Erler 2012). The paper enables a re-evaluation of whether UHIC experiments can meaningfully contribute to nuclear structure knowledge, and suggests that the proper use of many-body correlations from UHIC could open a new research avenue if combined with appropriate theoretical frameworks such as those in Ke (2025) and Giacalone (2025).
