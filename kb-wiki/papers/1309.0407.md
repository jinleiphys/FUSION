# Radial basis function approach in nuclear mass predictions

> The radial basis function (RBF) approach is applied in predicting nuclear masses for 8 widely used nuclear mass models, ranging from macroscopic-microscopic to microscopic types. A significantly improved accuracy in computing nuclear masses is obtained, and the corresponding rms deviations with respect to the known masses is reduced by up to 78%. Moreover, strong correlations are found between a target nucleus and the reference nuclei within about three unit in distance, which play critical roles in improving nuclear mass predictions. Based on the latest Weizsäcker-Skyrme mass model, the RBF approach can achieve an accuracy comparable with the extrapolation method used in atomic mass evaluation. In addition, the necessity of new high-precision experimental data to improve the mass predictions with the RBF approach is emphasized as well.

## Key claim

The radial basis function (RBF) approach reduces the rms deviation of nuclear mass predictions by up to 78% across eight mass models, but this improvement is effective only for nuclei within a distance of about three units on the nuclear chart from the training set; beyond that range the improvement ceases for the most accurate models.

## Method

The RBF approach reconstructs the mass difference between experimental data and theoretical predictions using a linear combination of radial basis functions, where the Euclidean distance between nuclei in the (Z,N) plane defines the basis. The weights are determined by solving a linear system using known masses, and the reconstructed function is then added to the theoretical mass for any target nucleus. The predictive power was tested by excluding the target nucleus from the training set (R_min ≥ 1) and by varying the maximum distance R_max of included reference nuclei.

## Key numbers

- RMF model: rms deviation reduced from 2.217 MeV to 0.488 MeV (78% reduction) for 2328 nuclei in AME12.
- WS3 model: rms deviation reduced from 0.335 MeV to 0.207 MeV (38% reduction) for 2353 nuclei in AME12.
- DZ10 model: rms deviation reduced from 0.591 MeV to 0.225 MeV (62% reduction) for 2353 nuclei in AME12.
- KTUY model: rms deviation reduced from 0.701 MeV to 0.210 MeV (70% reduction) for 2353 nuclei in AME12.
- For the 214 new masses in AME12, the best predictive accuracy with RBF was 0.277 MeV based on the WS3 model.
- For nuclei with evaluated masses in AME03, the RBF approach with WS3 achieved an rms deviation of 0.268 MeV using AME12 data, compared to 0.398 MeV from the AME extrapolation method.

## Context

This work extends the RBF approach previously applied by Wang (2011) to eight mass models, systematically investigating the range of mass correlations that the method exploits. The authors find that strong correlations exist only within about three units of distance on the nuclear chart, which explains why the method works best for nuclei near known masses. The study enables improved mass predictions for astrophysical calculations, particularly in regions close to experimentally known nuclei, and suggests the RBF approach could be applied to other nuclear properties such as beta-decay half-lives and fission barriers.
