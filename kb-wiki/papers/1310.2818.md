# Hydrodynamics of a quark droplet II: Implications of a non-zero baryon chemical potential

> We present an extended version of the dynamical model for a multi-quark droplet evolution described in our proceeding paper. The model includes collective expansion of the droplet, effects of the vacuum pressure and surface tension, and now a non-zero baryon number. The hadron emission from the droplet is described following Weisskopf's statistical model. We consider evolutions of droplets with different initial temperatures and net baryon number. It is found that the introduction of a non-zero net baryon number does not change the lifetime of the droplets significantly. Only when we consider an initially very baryon-rich, low-temperature droplets is the lifetime is decreased significantly. We have, furthermore, found a convergence of both baryon chemical potential and temperature toward the values $μ_{\rm B} \approx$ 450 MeV and $T \approx 150$ MeV. This convergence is linked to the competing emission of baryons versus mesons.

# Hydrodynamics of a quark droplet II: Implications of a non-zero baryon chemical potential

## Key claim

The introduction of a non-zero net baryon number does not change the lifetime of quark droplets significantly, except for initially very baryon-rich, low-temperature droplets where the lifetime is decreased significantly. Both the baryon chemical potential and temperature converge toward the values μB ≈ 450 MeV and T ≈ 150 MeV during the droplet evolution, linked to the competing emission of baryons versus mesons.

## Method

The authors extend a dynamical model for multi-quark droplet evolution (previously developed for baryon-free droplets in Bjerrum-Bohr et al. (2012)) to include non-zero baryon number, using the MIT bag model for confinement, hydrodynamic equations for a perfect fluid with a linear velocity profile, and Weisskopf's statistical model for hadron emission from the droplet surface. The evolution is governed by conservation equations for total energy, entropy, and net baryon number, which are solved numerically for the droplet radius R(t), temperature T(t), and baryon chemical potential μB(t) under close-to-equilibrium initial conditions where thermal pressure balances vacuum pressure and Laplace pressure. Five cases with different initial temperatures and net baryon numbers (all with the same initial bulk energy density of 0.75 GeV/fm³ and initial radius ≈2 fm) are studied until the radius reaches 0.8 fm.

## Key numbers

- Initial droplet total energy: E = 34.4 GeV, with plasma energy 25.1 GeV, vacuum energy 6.7 GeV, and surface energy 2.5 GeV (for initial energy density ε = 0.75 GeV/fm³)
- Bag constant: B = 200 MeV/fm³; surface tension coefficient: σ = 50 MeV/fm²
- For cases 1-3, radial shrinkage rate ≈ 0.075c; for case 4, ≈ 0.086c
- In case 5, inward surface velocities up to 0.4c are observed
- In case 4, almost 12 baryons are emitted during the first 5 fm/c; in case 5, almost 20 baryons are emitted in the same interval
- Convergence values: T ≈ 150 MeV and μB ≈ 450 MeV (corresponding to baryon density nB ≈ 0.45 fm⁻³)
- Lifetime decreased by approximately 3 fm/c in case 4 and 6 fm/c in case 5 compared to case 1
- Total energy drops by about 31 GeV (from 34 GeV to 3 GeV) during droplet evolution for all cases
- Estimated upper bound of recoil pressure for droplet radius R = 2 fm: 30 MeV/fm³, much smaller than vacuum pressure (200 MeV/fm³) and Laplace pressure (50 MeV/fm³)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous model for baryon-free droplets (Bjerrum-Bohr et al. (2012), denoted as (I)) to include finite baryon chemical potential, relevant for lower-energy heavy-ion collisions where net-baryon dynamics become important. The approach builds on earlier investigations by Biró et al. (1983) and Barz et al. (1990), who introduced schematic versions of hydrodynamical models for quark droplets. The convergence of temperature and chemical potential toward fixed values is linked to the competition between baryon and meson emission, similar to the constant-temperature behavior reported by Barz et al. (1990), though here the general trend shows increasing temperature rather than constant temperature. The shorter lifetimes found for baryon-rich droplets (about 10 fm/c at LHC and 7 fm/c at RHIC) are in better agreement with hadronization times extracted from HBT measurements (Aamodt et al. (2011)). The study could enable better understanding of phenomena such as cold strangelet formation and flavor distillation, and the authors plan to introduce strangeness degrees of freedom in future work.
