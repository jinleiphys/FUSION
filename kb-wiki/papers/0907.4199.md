# On the role of ground state correlations in hypernuclear non-mesonic weak decay

> The contribution of ground state correlations (GSC) to the non--mesonic weak decay of $^{12}_Λ$C and other medium to heavy hypernuclei is studied within a nuclear matter formalism implemented in a local density approximation. We adopt a weak transition potential including the exchange of the complete octets of pseudoscalar and vector mesons as well as a residual strong interaction modeled on the Bonn potential. Leading GSC contributions, at first order in the residual strong interaction, are introduced on the same footing for all isospin channels of one-- and two--nucleon induced decays. Together with fermion antisymmetrization, GSC turn out to be important for an accurate determination of the decay widths. Besides opening the two--nucleon stimulated decay channels, for $^{12}_Λ$C GSC are responsible for 14% of the rate $Γ_1$ while increasing the $Γ_{n}/Γ_{p}$ ratio by 4%. Our final results for $^{12}_Λ$C are: $Γ_{\rm NM}=0.98$, $Γ_{n}/Γ_{p}=0.34$ and $Γ_2/Γ_{\rm NM}=0.26$. The saturation property of $Γ_{\rm NM}$ with increasing hypernuclear mass number is clearly observed. The agreement with data of our predictions for $Γ_{\rm NM}$, $Γ_n/Γ_p$ and $Γ_2$ is rather good.

## Key claim

Ground state correlations (GSC) contribute 14% of the one-nucleon induced non-mesonic decay rate and increase the neutron-to-proton ratio by 4% for \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C, but these results depend on the authors' definition that excludes final state interaction diagrams from the decay widths, treating them instead as non-observable quantities.

## Method

The authors employed a nuclear matter formalism with local density approximation, using a weak transition potential that includes exchange of the full octets of pseudoscalar and vector mesons (\(\pi\), \(\eta\), \(K\), \(\rho\), \(\omega\), \(K^*\)) with coupling constants from the Nijmegen soft-core interaction NSC97f, and a residual strong interaction modeled on the Bonn potential (\(\pi\), \(\rho\), \(\sigma\), \(\omega\) exchange). Leading-order GSC contributions were introduced at first order in the residual strong interaction for all isospin channels of both one- and two-nucleon induced decays, evaluating Goldstone diagrams where the nucleon-nucleon interaction occurs before the weak transition, while excluding diagrams where it occurs after (classified as final state interactions). The finite hypernucleus results were obtained by averaging over the \(\Lambda\) momentum distribution and local Fermi momentum using harmonic oscillator wavefunctions.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C: total non-mesonic decay width \(\Gamma_{\rm NM} = 0.98\) (units not specified but standardly in units of the free \(\Lambda\) decay width)
- For \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C: neutron-to-proton ratio \(\Gamma_n/\Gamma_p = 0.34\)
- For \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C: two-nucleon induced fraction \(\Gamma_2/\Gamma_{\rm NM} = 0.26\)
- GSC are responsible for 14% of the one-nucleon induced rate \(\Gamma_1\) in \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C
- GSC increase the \(\Gamma_n/\Gamma_p\) ratio by 4% for \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C
- Harmonic oscillator frequency \(\hbar\omega = 10.8\) MeV used for \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C
- \(\Lambda\) binding energy \(V_\Lambda = -10.8\) MeV used for \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C

## Context

This work extends the microscopic calculations of Bauer and Garbarino (2003, 2004) by including leading-order GSC contributions on the same footing for one- and two-nucleon induced decays for the first time, going beyond previous calculations that only evaluated the uncorrelated (diagram a) contributions. The results address the long-standing puzzle of the \(\Gamma_n/\Gamma_p\) ratio by providing a more complete theoretical framework that includes GSC effects, and the saturation of \(\Gamma_{\rm NM}\) with increasing hypernuclear mass number is clearly observed. The approach enables future inclusion of additional GSC diagrams (such as those involving the \(\Delta(1232)\) resonance or \(\Lambda N\) strong interactions) and provides a basis for comparing with experimental data from KEK and other facilities, though the authors note that the non-mesonic partial decay rates are not direct observables and require theoretical input for extraction from nucleon coincidence measurements.
