# Pre-saddle neutron multiplicity for fission reactions induced by heavy ions and light particles

> Pre-saddle neutron multiplicity has been calculated for several fission reactions induced by heavy ions and light particles. Experimentally, it is impossible to determine the contribution of neutrons being emitted before the saddle point and those emitted between the saddle and the scission points. Determination of the pre-saddle neutron multiplicity in our research is based on the comparison between the experimental anisotropies and those predicted by the standard saddle-point statistical model. Analysis of the results shows that the pre-saddle neutron multiplicity depends on the fission barrier height and stability of the compound nucleus. In heavy ion induced fission, the number of pre-saddle neutrons decreases with increasing the excitation energy of the compound nucleus. A main cause of this behavior is due to a reduction in the ground state-to-saddle point transition time with increasing the excitation energy of the compound nucleus. Whereas in induced fission by light particles, the number of pre-saddle neutrons increases with increasing the excitation energy of the compound nucleus.

## Key claim

The number of pre-saddle neutrons (neutrons emitted between the ground state and the saddle point of the fissioning nucleus) can be determined by comparing experimental fission-fragment angular anisotropies with predictions of the standard saddle-point statistical model (SSPSM), a method that works only for reactions where the angular anisotropy shows "normal" behavior (i.e., good agreement with SSPSM predictions).

## Method

The authors calculated pre-saddle neutron multiplicity by iteratively adjusting the number of pre-saddle neutrons in the SSPSM angular anisotropy formula until the predicted anisotropy matched the experimental value. They used the rotating finite range model (RFRM) to compute the effective moment of inertia, spin-dependent fission barrier height, and rotational energy, and assumed a level density parameter of A_CN/8. The average excitation energy lost per evaporated neutron was taken as B_n + 2T, where T is the nuclear temperature at the saddle point.

## Key numbers

- For the 16O+208Pb→224Th reaction system at E_c.m. = 76.9 MeV (E_ex = 22.7 MeV), the calculated pre-saddle neutron multiplicity is 1.81, compared to an experimental pre-scission multiplicity of 1.50 (ratio 1.21).
- For the same system at E_c.m. = 119.0 MeV (E_ex = 55.0 MeV), the calculated pre-saddle neutron multiplicity is 0.00, compared to an experimental pre-scission multiplicity of 3.40 (ratio 0.00).
- For the 16O+208Pb→224Th system at B_f/T = 1, the ratio of calculated pre-saddle to experimental pre-scission neutron multiplicity is approximately 1/4.1, consistent with a ground-to-saddle to saddle-to-scission time ratio of about 1/3.7.
- For 11B+197Au over projectile energy range E_c.m./V_b = 1.4-1.9, the pre-saddle neutron multiplicity ranges from 3.1 to 1.4, with an average of 2.0.
- For 16O+209Bi over projectile energy range E_c.m./V_b = 1.0-1.6, the pre-saddle neutron multiplicity ranges from 1.7 to 0.9, with an average of 1.4.
- For p+209Bi and α+206Pb reactions forming 210Po, and for α+182W and p+185Re reactions forming 186Os, pre-saddle neutron multiplicity increases with excitation energy (specific numerical values shown in Fig. 4 of the paper).

## Context

This work extends the standard saddle-point statistical model approach by providing a method to isolate the pre-saddle component of pre-scission neutron emission, which is experimentally inseparable from saddle-to-scission neutrons. The results contradict the common assumption that all pre-scission neutrons are emitted before the saddle point, and instead show that the fraction of pre-saddle neutrons varies strongly with excitation energy and projectile type. The method enables future studies of ground-state-to-saddle and saddle-to-scission transition times, and builds on prior work by Hinde (1989) on viscosity effects, Sierk (1986) on the rotating finite range model, and Frobrich (1994) on theoretical pre-scission neutron multiplicities.
