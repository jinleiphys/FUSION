# Nuclear shadowing

> The phenomenon of shadowing of nuclear structure functions at small values of Bjorken-$x$ is analyzed. First, multiple scattering is discussed as the underlying physical mechanism. In this context three different but related approaches are presented: Glauber-like rescatterings, Gribov inelastic shadowing and ideas based on high-density Quantum Chromodynamics. Next, different parametrizations of nuclear partonic distributions based on fit analysis to existing data combined with Dokshitzer-Gribov-Lipatov-Altarelli-Parisi evolution, are reviewed. Finally, a comparison of the different approaches is shown, and a few phenomenological consequences of nuclear shadowing in high-energy nuclear collisions are presented.

## Key claim

Nuclear shadowing at small Bjorken‑x is explained by multiple scattering of the hadronic component of the virtual photon, but the review notes that existing experimental data are confined to a limited region of not very low x and small or moderate Q², with a strong kinematical correlation between small x and small Q², so the behaviour at the smallest available x is compatible with either saturation or a mild decrease.

## Method

The paper reviews three theoretical approaches to nuclear shadowing: Glauber‑like rescatterings (dipole model), Gribov inelastic shadowing (relating diffractive dissociation to the two‑scattering contribution), and high‑density QCD (saturation physics, including the McLerran‑Venugopalan model and the Balitsky‑Kovchegov equation). It also reviews parametrizations of nuclear partonic distributions based on fits to data combined with DGLAP evolution, and compares the different models against experimental data on nuclear structure‑function ratios.

## Key numbers

* Shadowing sets in for x ≲ 0.1, with the transition from antishadowing to shadowing occurring at x ≲ 0.1 A⁻¹/³ (using typical nuclear radii R_A ∼ A¹/³ fm).
* The coherence length l_c = 2p₊/k_T²; in the totally coherent limit (p₊ → ∞) the two‑scattering contribution to the nuclear cross section is σ_A² = –(A(A–1)/2) ∫ d²x_T [T_A(x_T)σ]².
* In the Gribov approach, the first shadowing correction is σ_A² = –4π A(A–1) ∫ d²b T_A²(b) ∫_{M²_min}^{M²_max} dM² (dσ^D_{γ*–p}/dM²dt)|_{t=0} F_A²(t_min), with M²_min ≃ 4m_π² and M²_max = Q²(x_Pmax/x – 1) with x_Pmax ∼ 0.1.
* In the McLerran‑Venugopalan model, the unintegrated gluon density is proportional to (1/α_s) ∫ (d²x_T / x_T²) e^{-i x_T·k_T} (1 – e^{-x_T² Q_s²/4}), where Q_s² ∝ A T_A(b) xg_nucleon is the saturation scale.
* The nuclear structure function at very small x is predicted to increase as ln² x with decreasing x.

## Context

This review extends earlier reviews of nuclear structure‑function modifications (Arneodo 1992, Geesaman 1995) by focusing specifically on the small‑x shadowing region and by incorporating recent developments in high‑density QCD and DGLAP‑based parametrizations. It enables future experimental tests at RHIC, the LHC, and a possible Electron‑Ion Collider, and provides a framework for comparing models that address the origin of shadowing (multiple scattering, diffraction, saturation) with those that parametrize its evolution.
