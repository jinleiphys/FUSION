# Neutron stars and supernova explosions in the framework of Landau's theory

> A general formula of the symmetry energy for many-body interaction is proposed and the commonly used two-body interaction symmetry energy is recovered. Within Landau's theory (Lt), we generalize two equations of state (EoS) CCS$δ$3 and CCS$δ$5 to asymmetric nuclear matter. We assume that the density and density difference between protons and neutrons divided by their sum are order parameters. We use different EoS to study neutron stars by solving the TOV equations. We demonstrate that different EoS give different mass and radius relation for neutron stars even when they have exactly the same ground state (gs) properties ($E/A$, $ρ_0$, $K$, $S$, $L$ and $K_{sym}$). Furthermore, for one EoS we change $K_{sym}$ and fix all the other gs parameters. We find that for some $K_{sym}$ the EoS becomes unstable at high density even for neutron matter. This suggests that a neutron star (NS) can exist below and above the instability region but in different states: a quark gluon plasma (QGP) at high density and baryonic matter at low density. If the star's central density is in the instability region, then we associate these conditions to the occurrence of Supernovae (SN).

## Key claim

The paper proposes that different equations of state (EoS) can produce different mass-radius relations for neutron stars even when they share identical ground state properties (energy per nucleon, saturation density, incompressibility, symmetry energy, slope of symmetry energy, and symmetry incompressibility). Furthermore, for a specific EoS (CCSδ5), varying only the symmetry incompressibility Ksym can produce an instability region at high density that the authors associate with supernova explosions, suggesting neutron stars can exist in either a baryonic phase or a quark-gluon plasma phase depending on whether their central density lies outside or inside this instability region.

## Method

The authors generalized two equations of state derived from Landau's theory (CCSδ3 and CCSδ5) to asymmetric nuclear matter by treating density and the relative density difference between protons and neutrons as order parameters. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for pure neutron matter using these EoS along with two Skyrme-type EoS (CK225 and CK225₁), fixing ground state properties of symmetric nuclear matter to common values (K=225 MeV, S=28.5 MeV, L=73 MeV, Ksym=-25 MeV). For the CCSδ5 EoS, they independently varied Ksym while keeping S and L fixed, and applied Maxwell construction when a first-order phase transition appeared.

## Key numbers

- Ground state density ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³; energy per nucleon at saturation E/A = -15 MeV; incompressibility K = 225 MeV
- For CCSδ3, critical density for second-order phase transition in symmetric nuclear matter: ρc = 2.9354 ρ₀
- For CCSδ5, critical density for second-order phase transition in symmetric nuclear matter: ρc = 5.2795 ρ₀
- For CCSδ5 with S=28.5 MeV and L=50 MeV, a bound state for pure neutron matter appears at Ksym = -222.5 MeV, with ground state density ρn = 3.91 ρ₀
- For CCSδ5 with S=28.5 MeV and L=50 MeV, a first-order phase transition appears at Ksym = -215 MeV
- Observed neutron star mass PSR J1614-2230: 1.97 ± 0.04 M⊙ (from Shapiro delay)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous development of Landau-theory-based EoS (Zheng et al., 2014, "Virial expansion of the nuclear equation of state") and their systematic study of 159 Skyrme EoS correlating neutron star maximum mass with Ksym (Zheng et al., 2013). It contrasts with the systematic survey by Dutra et al. (2012) of 240 Skyrme EoS, which found only five passing all observational criteria. The paper demonstrates that fixing ground state properties alone is insufficient to determine neutron star structure, and that the high-density behavior of the EoS, including possible phase transitions to quark-gluon plasma, is crucial. This enables future work using the instability region as a criterion for supernova conditions and suggests that very massive neutron stars may exist in a quark phase while lighter ones remain baryonic.
