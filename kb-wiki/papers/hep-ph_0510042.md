# Dark matter search by exclusive studies of X-rays following WIMPs nuclear interactions

> It is shown that weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs), which are possible cold dark matter candidates, can be studied by exclusive measurements of X-rays following WIMPs nuclear interactions. Inner-shell atomic electrons are ionized through WIMP-nuclear interaction, and then mono-energetic X-rays are emitted when they are filled by outer-shell electrons. The number of inner-shell holes amounts to as large as one per five nuclear recoils for K-shell and several per recoil for L-shell in the case of medium heavy target nuclei interacting with 100-300 GeV WIMPs. Then the K and L X-ray peaks show up in the 5-50 keV region. Consequently exclusive studies of the X-rays in coincidence with the nuclear recoils and the ionization electrons are found to provide excellent opportunities to detect WIMPs such as the Lightest Super Symmetric Particles (LSP)

## Key claim

The paper proposes that WIMPs can be detected via exclusive measurements of X-rays emitted after inner-shell atomic electrons are ionized by WIMP-nuclear interactions, claiming that the X-ray production rate is large enough to make such detection realistic. The key hedge is that this claim is evaluated specifically for medium-heavy target nuclei (e.g., $^{131}$Xe) interacting with WIMPs in the 30-300 GeV mass range, and the rates depend on the WIMP mass.

## Method

The authors calculate the ratio of inner-shell electron ionization cross-sections to nuclear recoil cross-sections for $^{131}$Xe, using realistic atomic wave functions and a Gaussian WIMP velocity distribution with respect to the galactic center. The ionization rates are evaluated for the 1s, 2s, 2p, 3p, and 3d shells, and then converted to X-ray production rates using fluorescence branching ratios. The analysis considers WIMP masses of 30, 100, and 300 GeV, and the resulting K and L X-ray energies fall in the 5-50 keV region.

## Key numbers

- For $^{131}$Xe with a 100 GeV WIMP, the K-shell (1s) hole production rate per nuclear recoil is 0.221 (when multiplied by Z=54 electrons per atom), corresponding to approximately one K X-ray per 5 recoils.
- For $^{131}$Xe with a 100 GeV WIMP, the L-shell (2s+2p) hole production rate per nuclear recoil is about 5-7 (sum of 1.461 for 2s and 4.506 for 2p, when multiplied by Z).
- The K X-ray cross-section relative to nuclear recoil for $^{131}$Xe with a 100 GeV WIMP is 0.20 (sum of all K lines, multiplied by Z).
- The K X-ray cross-section relative to nuclear recoil for $^{131}$Xe with a 30 GeV WIMP is 0.03, and with a 300 GeV WIMP is 0.23.
- K X-ray energies for $^{131}$Xe range from 29.5 keV (K$_{\alpha 2}$) to 34.4 keV (K$_{\beta 2}$).
- The K-shell binding energy for Xe is 34.56 keV.
- The 1s hole production rate changes less than 3% when the electron detection threshold varies from 0 to 0.2 keV for a 100 GeV WIMP.
- Relativistic effects reduce the 1s hole production by less than 30%.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous proposals for detecting WIMPs via gamma-rays following inelastic scattering (Ejiri 1993, Vergados 2005) and via ionization electrons (Vergados 2005, Moustakidis 2005). The X-ray method is presented as complementary to existing direct detection experiments such as DAMA (Bernabei 1998), EDELWEISS (Benoit 2002), and CDMS (Akerib 2003), which measure only nuclear recoils. The proposed exclusive measurements of X-rays in coincidence with nuclear recoils and ionization electrons could enable sensitivity to WIMP-proton cross-sections down to $10^{-7}$ pb, and the K-to-L X-ray ratio could be used to estimate the WIMP mass.
