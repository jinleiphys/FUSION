# Constraints on neutron skin thickness in 208Pb and density-dependent symmetry energy

> Accurate knowledge about the neutron skin thickness $ΔR_{np}$ in $^{208}$Pb has far-reaching implications for different communities of nuclear physics and astrophysics. Yet, the novel Lead Radius Experiment (PREX) did not yield stringent constraint on the $ΔR_{np}$ recently. We employ a more practicable strategy currently to probe the neutron skin thickness of $^{208}$Pb based on a high linear correlation between the $ΔR_{np}$ and $J-a_{\text{sym}}$, where $J$ and $a_{\text{sym}}$ are the symmetry energy (coefficient) of nuclear matter at saturation density and of $^{208}$Pb. An accurate $J-a_{\text{sym}}$ thus places a strong constraint on the $ΔR_{np}$. Compared with the parity-violating asymmetry $A_{\text{PV}}$ in the PREX, the reliably experimental information on the $J-a_{\text{sym}}$ is much more easily available attributed to a wealth of measured data on nuclear masses and on decay energies. The density dependence of the symmetry energy is also well constrained with the $J-a_{\text{sym}}$. Finally, with a `tomoscan' method, we find that one just needs to measure the nucleon densities in $^{208}$Pb starting from $R_{m} = 7.61\pm0.04$ fm to obtain the $ΔR_{np}$ in hadron scattering experiments, regardless of its interior profile that is hampered by the strong absorption.

## Key claim

The neutron skin thickness of 208Pb can be constrained more effectively by the quantity J - a_sym (the difference between the symmetry energy at saturation density and the symmetry energy coefficient of 208Pb) than by the parity-violating asymmetry APV measured in the PREX experiment, because J - a_sym can be determined from widely available nuclear mass and beta-decay data rather than requiring a high-precision electron scattering measurement.

## Method

The authors established a linear correlation between the neutron skin thickness ΔRnp of 208Pb and the quantity J - a_sym using 26 different Skyrme and relativistic mean-field models, with a correlation coefficient of 0.989. They extracted a_sym for 208Pb from experimental beta-decay energies of heavy odd-A nuclei and from experimental mass differences between mirror nuclei, obtaining a_sym = 22.4 ± 0.4 MeV. Using a previously determined value of J = 32.4 ± 0.6 MeV, they derived J - a_sym = 10.0 ± 1.0 MeV and then used the linear correlation to predict ΔRnp. They also introduced a "tomoscan" method that expresses ΔRnp as an integral of a radial distribution function f(r) to identify which radial region contributes to the neutron skin.

## Key numbers

- ΔRnp = 0.176 ± 0.021 fm for 208Pb, predicted from J - a_sym = 10.0 ± 1.0 MeV
- a_sym = 22.4 ± 0.4 MeV for 208Pb, extracted from experimental beta-decay energies and mass differences
- J = 32.4 ± 0.6 MeV, adopted from the union of two recent determinations (32.5 ± 0.5 MeV from mass systematics and 32.10 ± 0.31 MeV from double differences of experimental symmetry energies)
- Slope parameter L = 54 ± 16 MeV at saturation density ρ0 = 0.16 fm^-3
- Slope parameter L at ρ = 0.11 fm^-3: L_0.11 = 48 ± 6 MeV
- Curvature parameter K_sym = -152 ± 70 MeV
- Symmetry energy at 2ρ0: S(2ρ0) = 42 ± 10 MeV
- Reference density ρA = 0.55 ρ0 = 0.088 fm^-3, where a_sym equals the nuclear matter symmetry energy
- Rm = 7.61 ± 0.04 fm, the radius from which nucleon densities must be measured to determine ΔRnp
- Transition density from solid neutron star crust to liquid interior: ρc = 0.091 ± 0.008 fm^-3, derived from ΔRnp

## Context

This work extends the approach of Centelles et al. (2009), who showed that ΔRnp correlates linearly with J - a_sym using asymmetric semi-infinite nuclear matter calculations, by instead extracting a_sym directly from experimental data. The predicted ΔRnp = 0.176 ± 0.021 fm is significantly smaller than the PREX result of 0.33+0.16-0.18 fm (Abrahamyan et al., 2012) and is consistent with other measurements and analyses. The results enable improved constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy, which is crucial for neutron star physics including crust-core transition properties relevant to pulsar glitch phenomena. The "tomoscan" method provides a practical simplification for future hadron scattering experiments measuring neutron skin thickness.
