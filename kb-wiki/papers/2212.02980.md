# Event-by-event investigation of the two-particle source function in $\sqrt{s_{_{\text{NN}}}}=2.76$ TeV PbPb collisions with EPOS

> The investigation of the two-particle source function in lead-lead collisions simulated by the EPOS model at a center of mass energy per nucleon pair of $\sqrt{s_{_{\text{NN}}}}=2.76$ TeV is presented. The two-particle source functions are reconstructed directly on an event-by-event basis for pions, kaons and protons separately, using the final stage of EPOS. A Lévy source shape is observed for all three particle species in the individual events, deviating significantly from a Gaussian shape. The source parameters are extracted as functions of collision centrality and pair average transverse mass ($m_{\text{T}}$). The Lévy exponent is found to be ordered accordingly to particle mass. The Lévy scale parameter is found to scale for all particle species with $m_{\text{T}}$ according to Gaussian hydrodynamical predictions; however, there is no $m_{\text{T}}$-scaling found across these species. In case of pions, the effects of the decay products and hadronic rescattering are also investigated. The Lévy exponent is decreased when decay products are also included in the analysis. Without hadronic rescattering and decay products, the source shape is close to a Gaussian.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the two-particle source function in EPOS-simulated PbPb collisions follows a Lévy shape on an event-by-event basis for pions, kaons, and protons, not just after event averaging; however, this result is conditional on the inclusion of the hadronic afterburner (UrQMD), because without hadronic rescattering and decay products the source shape is close to a Gaussian.

## Method

The two-particle source function was reconstructed directly from the final-stage particle coordinates in EPOS-simulated PbPb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{_{\text{NN}}}}=2.76$ TeV, using approximately $8.0\times10^5$ events. The one-dimensional distribution of the pair separation vector magnitude in the longitudinally co-moving system (LCMS) was fitted event-by-event with a three-dimensional spherically symmetric Lévy alpha-stable distribution for pions, kaons, and protons separately, in bins of collision centrality and pair average transverse momentum ($k_{\text{T}}$). For pions, four separate cases were investigated: with and without decay products, and with and without the UrQMD hadronic afterburner.

## Key numbers

- Lévy exponent $\alpha$ values: pions in the range 1.4–1.5, kaons in the range 1.4–1.6, protons in the range 1.6–2.0, for events including both primordial and decay particles with UrQMD.
- The $\left<R\right>$ values for pions are about 20–30% larger than lower-energy EPOS results and about twice as large as experimentally measured values at CMS.
- The $\left<\alpha\right>$ values of 1.4–1.5 for pions (primordial and decay) are slightly smaller than lower-energy EPOS values of 1.5–1.6 and significantly lower than experimental values of 1.7–1.9.
- 10 $k_{\text{T}}$ classes were defined for pions in the range 250–1000 MeV/$c$, 5 classes for kaons in the range 200–1000 MeV/$c$, and 3 classes for protons in the range 200–1400 MeV/$c$.
- 4 centrality classes were investigated: 0–5%, 5–10%, 10–20%, and 20–30%.
- The lower fit limit was 2 fm for pions, 3 fm for kaons, and 5 fm for protons; the upper fit limit was varied between 70 fm and 100 fm.

## Context

This work extends previous femtoscopy studies that found Lévy-shaped sources in experimental data from RHIC (Adare 2018) and LHC (CMS 2023) by demonstrating that the Lévy shape appears already at the single-event level in the EPOS model, ruling out event averaging as the sole cause. It also extends an earlier event-by-event EPOS analysis for pions in AuAu collisions at $\sqrt{s_{_{\text{NN}}}}=200$ GeV (Kincses 2022) to PbPb at LHC energies and to kaons and protons. The results enable future comparisons with experimental measurements of Lévy parameters for kaons and protons, which are not yet published, and challenge the anomalous diffusion explanation for the Lévy shape because the ordering of $\alpha$ values (protons > pions > kaons) does not match the predicted ordering from cross-section arguments (Csanad 2007).
