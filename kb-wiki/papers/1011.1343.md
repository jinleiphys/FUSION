# Chiral symmetry, nuclear forces and all that

> These are personal recollections of how Gerry Brown's work and thinking influenced the development of the chiral effective Lagrangian based theory of nuclear forces.

## Key claim

The paper presents personal recollections of how Gerry Brown's work and thinking influenced the development of the chiral effective Lagrangian based theory of nuclear forces, arguing that Brown's early insights into the role of chiral symmetry in nuclear interactions anticipated the modern effective field theory (EFT) approach, though the early work lacked a systematic power counting.

## Method

The author traces the historical development of chiral EFT for nuclear forces from the early 1980s through the late 2000s, describing key calculations in pion-nucleon scattering using heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory, the construction of nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials using Weinberg's power counting with unitary transformations and spectral function regularization, and the derivation of three-nucleon forces (3NF) at next-to-next-to-leading order (N$^2$LO). The paper also discusses applications to nuclear matter using G-matrix and R-matrix methods, and to nuclear lattice simulations combining chiral EFT with Monte Carlo techniques.

## Context

This paper extends the historical narrative of chiral EFT for nuclear forces, building on Weinberg's seminal papers (Weinberg, 1990, 1991) that established the power counting for nuclear potentials, and on the early numerical work by van Kolck and collaborators (Ordonez, 1993). The author connects Brown's earlier work on the chiral bag model and meson-exchange currents (Rho and Brown, 1981) to modern EFT developments, showing how the systematic approach resolves the lack of power counting that plagued earlier phenomenological models. The work enables precision calculations of few-nucleon systems and nuclear matter, and opens the path to nuclear lattice simulations for nuclei up to $A \simeq 40$ using petascale computing.
