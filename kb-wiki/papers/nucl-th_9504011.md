# Charge Symmetry Breaking in 500 MeV Nucleon-Trinucleon Scattering

> Elastic nucleon scattering from the 3He and 3H mirror nuclei is examined as a test of charge symmetry violation. The differential cross-sections are calculated at 500 MeV using a microsopic, momentum-space optical potential including the full coupling of two spin 1/2 particles and an exact treatment of the Coulomb force. The charge-symmetry-breaking effects investigated arise from a violation within the nuclear structure, from the p-nucleus Coulomb force, and from the mass-differences of the charge symmetric states. Measurements likely to reveal reliable information are noted.

## Key claim

The forward-hemisphere superratio of nucleon-trinucleon cross sections at 500 MeV provides a reliable signal of charge symmetry breaking, but the backward-hemisphere ratios are too sensitive to Coulomb-nuclear interference and numerical uncertainties to yield trustworthy information.

## Method

The authors solved the Lippmann-Schwinger equation with a microscopic, nonlocal, momentum-space optical potential that includes full spin-1/2 × spin-1/2 coupling and an exact treatment of the Coulomb potential. They used analytic form factors for the trinucleon matter and spin distributions, varied the nuclear root-mean-square radii to break charge symmetry, and discretized the coupled-channels equation over up to 64 grid points with 64 partial waves including NN partial waves up to l=4.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear RMS radii used: charge-symmetric values are Rp(³H)=1.700 fm, Rn(³H)=1.880 fm, Rp(³He)=1.880 fm, Rn(³He)=1.700 fm; charge-symmetry-breaking values include Rp(³H)=1.760 fm from a recent measurement by Just (1985)
- The difference Rn(³H) - Rp(³He) = -0.30 ± 0.008 fm and Rn(³He) - Rp(³H) = 0.035 ± 0.007 fm, as found by Gibbs and Gibson (1991) from pion scattering data
- The calculation is performed at 500 MeV nucleon energy
- The Coulomb cutoff radius used was 7 fm

## Context

This work extends the study of charge symmetry breaking from the pion-trinucleon system, where Pillai et al. (1991) found large deviations from unity in cross-section ratios, to the nucleon-trinucleon system. The analysis by Kim et al. (1987) showed that π-trinucleon Coulomb force largely explains those deviations, while Gibbs and Gibson (1991) attributed the remaining effect to nuclear structure differences. The present calculation enables a complementary test of charge symmetry breaking using nucleon probes, predicting that most violation arises from nuclear structure with about one-third from the proton-nucleus Coulomb interaction, and that a 10% measurement in the forward hemisphere would be valuable.
