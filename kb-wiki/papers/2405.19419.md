# Supernova electron-neutrino interactions with xenon in the nEXO detector

> Electron-neutrino charged-current interactions with xenon nuclei were modeled in the nEXO neutrinoless double-$β$ decay detector (~5 metric ton, 90% ${}^{136}$Xe, 10% ${}^{134}$Xe) to evaluate its sensitivity to supernova neutrinos. Predictions for event rates and detectable signatures were modeled using the Model of Argon Reaction Low Energy Yields (MARLEY) event generator. We find good agreement between MARLEY's predictions and existing theoretical calculations of the inclusive cross sections at supernova neutrino energies. The interactions modeled by MARLEY were simulated within the nEXO simulation framework and were run through an example reconstruction algorithm to determine the detector's efficiency for reconstructing these events. The simulated data, incorporating the detector response, were used to study the ability of nEXO to reconstruct the incident electron-neutrino spectrum and these results were extended to a larger xenon detector of the same isotope enrichment. We estimate that nEXO will be able to observe electron-neutrino interactions with xenon from supernovae as far as 5-8 kpc from Earth, while the ability to reconstruct incident electron-neutrino spectrum parameters from observed interactions in nEXO is limited to closer supernovae.

## Key claim
The nEXO detector, primarily designed for neutrinoless double-beta decay, can observe electron-neutrino charged-current interactions with xenon from supernovae as far as 5-8 kpc from Earth, but the ability to reconstruct the incident electron-neutrino spectrum parameters is limited to closer supernovae, depending on the supernova neutrino flux model and parameters of the core-collapse supernova.

## Method
The authors modeled electron-neutrino charged-current interactions with xenon nuclei using the MARLEY event generator, adapted from argon to xenon targets by supplying experimentally measured Gamow-Teller strength distributions for 136Xe and theoretical calculations for 134Xe. The interactions were simulated within the nEXO simulation framework and run through an example reconstruction algorithm to determine detector efficiency, using a conservative scintillation light threshold corresponding to approximately 100% efficiency for 500 keV energy depositions.

## Key numbers
- nEXO detector contains approximately 5 metric tons of xenon, enriched to 90% 136Xe and 10% 134Xe
- nEXO will be able to observe electron-neutrino interactions from supernovae as far as 5-8 kpc from Earth
- A 300-metric-ton detector of similar enrichment would observe approximately 15-41 events at a distance of 10 kpc, depending on the supernova electron-neutrino flux model
- The inclusive cross section for 136Xe using the GKVM model is 3.10 x 10^-40 cm^2 (MARLEY) compared to 3.15 x 10^-40 cm^2 from Pirinen (2019)
- The inclusive cross section for 134Xe using the GKVM model is 2.68 x 10^-40 cm^2 (MARLEY) compared to 2.49 x 10^-40 cm^2 from Pirinen (2019)
- The isobaric analog state in 136Cs is measured at 13.386 MeV excitation energy relative to the 136Cs ground state
- The calculated location of the isobaric analog state in 134Cs is 12.189 MeV
- The detector design energy resolution is less than 1% at the 136Xe Q value of 2.458 MeV
- The cosmic muon rate at the SNOLAB Cryopit location is 0.27 muons per square meter per day

## Context
This work extends previous calculations of electron-neutrino charged-current interactions with xenon by Divari (2013), Pirinen (2019), and Bhattacharjee (2022), which focused on interaction rates but not on how many interactions would be detectable. The study uses the MARLEY event generator, originally designed for argon targets by Gardiner (2021), and adapts it for xenon using Gamow-Teller strength measurements from Frekers (2018) and theoretical calculations from Moreno (2006). The results enable future studies of supernova neutrino detection in large xenon detectors, including potential 300-metric-ton detectors, and could be extended to model inelastic neutral-current neutrino-nucleus scattering and other interaction channels in nEXO.
