# Comment on the relation between the collinear tripartition and the collective model of the atomic nuclei

## Key claim
The paper argues that a triangular (non-axial) configuration of fragments in spontaneous fission of ²⁵²Cf is strictly forbidden by the symmetry principles of the collective Bohr model, because such a configuration requires a nonzero projection of angular momentum K > 0, which is impossible for a spinless nucleus. However, even a co-axial initial configuration does not guarantee final collinearity, as transverse velocities from fragment spins and Coulomb repulsion can produce a small divergence of up to 2°.

## Method
The author applies the Bohr collective model formalism, using the wave function expansion in Wigner D-functions with quantum number K (projection of angular momentum on the nuclear symmetry axis), to analyze the allowed configurations for spontaneous fission of spinless ²⁵²Cf. The analysis considers three cases from prior work by Tash: collinear alignment, a 0.5 fm transverse shift of the middle fragment, and a transverse initial velocity of the middle fragment. The author then evaluates how the Coulomb repulsion between fragments and the initial transverse velocities arising from wriggling vibrations and fragment spins affect the final collinearity.

## Key numbers
- The middle Ca fragment shifted by 0.5 fm from the fission axis is considered as the threshold for a triangular-like configuration.
- The final divergence between heavy and light fragments does not exceed 2°, which is below the resolution of the experimental setup of Piat.
- The paper discusses spontaneous fission of ²⁵²Cf into three fragments: Zn, Ca, and Sn.

## Context
This comment responds to work by Tash (2016) on collinear tripartition dynamics in ²⁵²Cf fission, and extends the author's own prior publications (Karpeshin, 2015; Karpeshin, 2016) which first showed that displaced middle-fragment configurations are forbidden by symmetry. The analysis uses the Bohr-Mottelson collective model (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975) and incorporates the wriggling vibration mechanism from Nix (1969) to explain fragment spins. The result enables experimentalists to understand why only co-axial configurations are possible in spontaneous fission of spinless nuclei, while also explaining why observed collinearity may be imperfect at the level of a few degrees.
