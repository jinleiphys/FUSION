# Indications of a spatial variation of the fine structure constant

> We previously reported Keck telescope observations suggesting a smaller value of the fine structure constant, alpha, at high redshift. New Very Large Telescope (VLT) data, probing a different direction in the universe, shows an inverse evolution; alpha increases at high redshift. Although the pattern could be due to as yet undetected systematic effects, with the systematics as presently understood the combined dataset fits a spatial dipole, significant at the 4.2-sigma level, in the direction right ascension 17.5 +/- 0.9 hours, declination -58 +/- 9 degrees. The independent VLT and Keck samples give consistent dipole directions and amplitudes, as do high and low redshift samples. A search for systematics, using observations duplicated at both telescopes, reveals none so far which emulate this result.

## Key claim

The combined Keck and Very Large Telescope (VLT) datasets, probing different directions in the universe, show opposite redshift trends for the fine structure constant, which together fit a spatial dipole model at the 4.2-sigma level; however, the authors explicitly state that the pattern could still be due to as yet undetected systematic effects.

## Method

The authors used the Many Multiplet method to measure the relative variation of the fine structure constant, Δα/α, from quasar absorption spectra. They analyzed 153 absorption systems from 60 VLT quasar spectra and combined these with 143 earlier Keck measurements, modeling absorption profiles with the Voigt profile fitting code vpfit. They fitted dipole and dipole+monopole spatial models to the combined dataset and assessed significance via bootstrap resampling, and performed an empirical systematic test using 6 quasars observed at both telescopes to map relative velocity shifts between the two instruments.

## Key numbers

- Keck sample: ⟨Δα/α⟩ for z<1.8 = −0.54 ± 0.12 × 10⁻⁵; for z>1.8 = −0.74 ± 0.17 × 10⁻⁵.
- VLT sample: ⟨Δα/α⟩ for z<1.8 = −0.06 ± 0.16 × 10⁻⁵; for z>1.8 = +0.61 ± 0.20 × 10⁻⁵.
- Best-fit dipole direction: right ascension 17.5 ± 0.9 hours, declination −58 ± 9 degrees.
- Dipole amplitude for the no-monopole model: A = (1.02 ± 0.21) × 10⁻⁵.
- Spatial gradient model amplitude: A = (1.1 ± 0.25) × 10⁻⁶ Glyr⁻¹.
- Dipole detection significance: 4.1–4.2σ across all three models.
- Empirical systematic test: linear fit to composite velocity shift gave slope a = (−7 ± 14) × 10⁻⁵ km s⁻¹ Å⁻¹, statistically insignificant.
- After applying the 6-quasar systematic correction, dipole+monopole significance reduced to 3.1σ.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier Keck-only detections of a smaller α at high redshift (Webb 2001, Murphy 2003, Murphy 2004) by adding VLT data that show the opposite trend, suggesting a spatial rather than purely temporal variation. The result contradicts a simple monotonic time-evolution of α and instead supports a dipole model, which the authors note could violate the equivalence principle and imply a very large or infinite universe. The paper enables future targeted observations toward the dipole pole and antipole directions to maximize detection sensitivity, and calls for an independent technique to check the results.
