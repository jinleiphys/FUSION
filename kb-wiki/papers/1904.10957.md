# R-mode instability in compact stars

> R-mode oscillations have been identified as viable and promising targets for continuous gravitational wave searches, meanwhile, it would allow us to probe the interior of compact stars directly. As well as emitting gravitational wave, r-modes would strongly affect the thermal and spin evolution of compact stars. In this paper, we reviewed the theory behind the gravitational wave driven r-mode instability in a rapidly rotating compact star. In particular, we will focus on r-mode instability window, r-mode evolution and detectability of r-mode.

# R-mode instability in compact stars

## Key claim
The r-mode instability in rapidly rotating compact stars is driven by gravitational radiation via the Chandrasekhar-Friedman-Schutz mechanism, but the instability only occurs above a critical rotation frequency and is suppressed by viscous damping; the actual instability window depends sensitively on the equation of state, the presence of exotic matter such as deconfined quarks, and additional damping mechanisms like surface rubbing, which the abstract does not detail.

## Method
The paper reviews the theoretical framework for the gravitational wave driven r-mode instability, focusing on the l=m=2 mode that couples most strongly to gravitational waves. It derives the instability window by balancing the gravitational radiation driving timescale against the shear viscosity and bulk viscosity damping timescales, and discusses the coupled evolution equations for the star's rotation frequency, r-mode amplitude, and temperature based on angular momentum and energy conservation. The review also examines the detectability of gravitational waves from r-modes using the characteristic strain amplitude formula and compares predictions to observational data from young pulsars and low-mass X-ray binaries.

## Key numbers
- The r-mode frequency in the co-rotating frame, to first order in rotation frequency Omega, is given by omega = 2mOmega / [l(l+1)] + O(Omega^3), with the l=m=2 mode dominating gravitational wave emission.
- For strange stars with M=1.4 solar masses and R=10 km, the instability window spans temperatures from 10^8 K to 10^10 K where bulk viscosity damping in quark matter is small.
- For color-flavor-locked phase strange stars, the instability occurs at temperatures around 10^10 K to 10^11 K.
- The viscous timescale for shear due to surface rubbing is tau_sr proportional to 10^9 T_9 (with nu=20 Hz), and the viscous timescale for shear due to electron-electron scattering is tau_sv^ee proportional to 10^12 T_9^(5/3).
- The most sensitive r-mode fractional amplitude limit from LIGO searches is less than 4 x 10^(-5) for Vela Jr.
- Constraints from millisecond pulsar and low-mass X-ray binary data imply a very small r-mode amplitude alpha <= 10^(-7).
- Saturation amplitudes from theoretical calculations range from order 10^(-4) to 10^(-2) dimensionless units in some models, while others give amplitudes equal to or larger than 10^(-1).

## Context
This review extends the foundational work on r-mode instability by Lindblom (1998), Owen et al. (1998), and Andersson (2001), and incorporates recent developments on the effects of exotic matter and additional damping mechanisms such as those studied by Wang et al. (2019). The paper discusses how the instability window can be constrained by comparing with observational data from young pulsars like PSR J0537-6910 and the Crab pulsar, and it enables future multi-messenger studies combining gravitational wave detections from aLIGO and next-generation observatories with electromagnetic observations from facilities like FAST, SKA, NICER, and eXTP.
