# Electric quadrupole transitions of the Bohr Hamiltonian with Manning-Rosen potential

> Analytical expressions of the wave functions are derived for a Bohr Hamiltonian with the Manning{Rosen potential in the cases of γ-unstable nuclei and axially symmetric prolate deformed ones with γ=0. By exploiting the results we have obtained in a recent work on the same theme Ref. [1], we have calculated the B(E2) transition rates for 34 γ-unstable and 38 rotational nuclei and compared to experimental data, revealing a qualitative agreement with the experiment and phase transitions within the ground state band and showing also that the Manning-Rosen potential is more appropriate for such calculations than other potentials.

## Key claim

The Manning-Rosen potential yields B(E2) transition rates that are in qualitative agreement with experiment for both γ-unstable and axially symmetric prolate deformed nuclei, and the authors claim it is "more appropriate" than the Morse, Kratzer, or Davidson potentials because its shape is flatter for large β, which avoids overestimating β-band spacings. The hedge is that the agreement is qualitative, and for γ-unstable nuclei some inter-band transitions are systematically underestimated by all models, while for rotational nuclei some inter-band transitions are systematically overestimated.

## Method

The authors solve the Bohr Hamiltonian with a Manning-Rosen potential for the β-coordinate, using a harmonic oscillator potential for the γ-part in the axially symmetric case. They derive analytical radial wave functions in terms of Jacobi polynomials (γ-unstable) and hypergeometric functions (rotational), then compute B(E2) transition rates via the quadrupole operator for 34 γ-unstable and 38 axially symmetric prolate deformed nuclei. The potential parameters (β₀, α, and c) are taken from a prior fit to energy spectra (Chabab et al. 2015) and are not refitted to transition data.

## Key numbers

- 34 γ-unstable nuclei and 38 axially symmetric prolate deformed nuclei were studied.
- Nearly 60% of the studied nuclei are "very well reproduced" by the Manning-Rosen potential in both cases, based on root-mean-square (rms) deviation comparisons.
- The rms deviation is defined as: rms = sqrt( (1/N) Σⱼ [ (B(E2, Lᵢ→L_f)_j^(exp) / B(E2, 2_g→0_g)^(exp) ) - (B(E2, Lᵢ→L_f)_j^(th) / B(E2, 2_g→0_g)^(th) ) ]² ).
- For the (n'_γ=1, K'=2) → (n_γ=0, K=0) transition in the rotational case, the γ-integral coefficient C_{0,0;1,2} = 1/√(3c).
- Normalization constants for γ-wave functions: N²_{0,0} = 2c and N²_{1,2} = 6c², giving N²_{1,2} / N²_{0,0} = 3c.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior calculation of energy spectra and bandhead ratios for the same nuclei using the Manning-Rosen potential (Chabab et al. 2015) by now computing electromagnetic transition rates. It directly compares with results from Bonatsos et al. using the Morse potential (Inci 2011), the Kratzer potential (Bonatsos 2013), and the Davidson potential (Bonatsos 2011), finding that the Manning-Rosen potential reproduces nearly 60% of the studied nuclei better than these alternatives, which succeed for less than 30% in the best case. The work enables systematic testing of the Manning-Rosen potential as a viable alternative to infinite square well, Morse, Kratzer, and Davidson potentials within the Bohr-Mottelson framework for describing collective nuclear structure away from critical point symmetries E(5) and X(5).
