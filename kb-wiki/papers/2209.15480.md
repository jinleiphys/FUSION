# Applications of Persistent Homology in Nuclear Collisions

> We introduce a novel set of observables associated to the rapidly developing field of persistent homology for the quantitative characterization of nuclear collisions and their evolution. Persistent homology allows for the identification of topological and homological characteristics of distributions in multi-dimensional spaces. We demonstrate here how to apply the toolset of persistent homology to the extraction of novel clustering signatures and the identification of long-range flow correlations in the particle production process of nuclear collisions.

## Key claim

Persistent homology can be applied to momentum-space point clouds from nuclear collisions to extract novel clustering signatures and identify long-range flow correlations, but the method is demonstrated only as a proof-of-concept using simulated Pb+Pb collisions at a single beam energy, with the caveat that the pipeline requires a rapidity cut and periodic boundary conditions in azimuthal angle.

## Method

The authors generated 10,000 Pb+Pb collision events at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV using the iEBE-VISHNU framework with T_R_ENTo initial conditions, a pre-hydrodynamic free-streaming phase, boost-invariant hydrodynamics with the DNMR formalism, and a hadronic afterburner (UrQMD). They applied a Delaunay triangulation field estimation (DTFE) to the particle point clouds in (phi, y) coordinates, performed a superlevel set filtration of the resulting density field, and computed persistence diagrams, Betti curves, fractal dimensions, cluster entropy, and local clustering statistics from the dendrogram structure. A mixed-event background was constructed by combining all events in the same centrality class with random azimuthal rotations and uniform resampling.

## Key numbers

* 10,000 Pb+Pb collision events at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV were simulated using the Duke Bayesian tune of iEBE-VISHNU.
* The hydrodynamic evolution was terminated at a freeze-out temperature T_FO = 150 MeV, corresponding to an energy density e_FO approximately 0.265 MeV/fm^3.
* A rapidity cut |y| <= 2 was imposed to avoid complications from periodic boundary conditions in the phi direction.
* Events with output multiplicity less than 50 particles were excluded from the analysis.
* The fractal dimension is defined as dim PH_i = alpha/(1 - beta), where beta = lim_{n to infinity} log( <E^i_alpha> ) / log(n), with E^i_alpha = sum_{(b,d) in PD_i} (d - b)^alpha.

## Context

This work extends the application of persistent homology, previously used in cosmology for galactic morphology (Pranav et al.) and in condensed matter for phase diagrams (PhysRevE.93.052138), to the phenomenology of relativistic nuclear collisions. The approach complements recent machine learning techniques in high-energy physics, particularly graph neural networks (Thais 2022), and provides a topological alternative to traditional observables such as anisotropic flow coefficients, particle multiplicities, and n-point correlation functions. The method enables new ways to quantify clustering and collective flow in momentum-space point clouds, with potential applications to identifying the QCD critical point and characterizing jet structure.
