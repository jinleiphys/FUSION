# Hadron production from resonance decay in relativistic collisions

> A statistical model for decay and formation of heavy hadronic resonances is formulated. The resonance properties become increasingly uncertain with increasing resonance mass. Drawing on analogy with the situation in low-energy nuclear physics, we employ the Weisskopf approach to the resonance processes. In the large-mass limit, the density of resonance states in mass is governed by a universal Hagedorn-like temperature T_H. As resonances decay, progressively more and more numerous lighter states get populated. For T_H \simeq 170 MeV, the model describes data for the hadron yield ratios at the RHIC and SPS energies under the extreme assumption of a single heavy resonance giving rise to measured yields.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that hadron yield ratios at RHIC and SPS energies can be described by a statistical model of sequential binary decays of heavy resonances, under the extreme assumption that a single heavy resonance populates a given rapidity region, with the Hagedorn temperature of the resonance mass spectrum set to approximately 170 MeV.

## Method

The authors formulate a Weisskopf-type statistical model for heavy hadronic resonance decay and formation, using a continuum density of states governed by a Hagedorn temperature. They implement Monte-Carlo simulations of sequential binary decays and inverse fusion reactions for a single heavy initial resonance, tracking baryon number, strangeness, isospin, and isospin projection, with 55 baryonic and 34 mesonic states treated as discrete below a threshold mass of 2 GeV. The model is applied to central Au+Au collisions at RHIC (sqrt(s)=130A GeV) and Pb+Pb collisions at SPS (158A GeV), comparing calculated yield ratios and transverse mass spectra to experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Hagedorn temperature: T_H = 170 MeV (optimal value for describing RHIC and SPS data)
- Threshold mass separating discrete from continuum states: m_th^c = 2 GeV
- Ground state mass parameters: a_Q = 0.387 GeV, a_S = 0.459 GeV
- Characteristic radius parameter: r_0 = 1 fm for a m_d = 1 GeV hadron
- Prefactor power for T_H = 170 MeV: alpha = 2.82
- Mass parameter in density of states: m_r = 0.5 GeV
- For RHIC data: optimal B_0/m_0 ratio approximately 0.030 GeV^{-1}
- For RHIC data: initial resonance mass m_0 = 100 GeV, baryon number B_0 = 3
- For SPS data: initial baryon number B_0 = 26, mass m_0 = 100 GeV
- For RHIC transverse spectra: average collective flow velocity <beta_t> = 0.27
- For SPS transverse spectra: average collective flow velocity <beta_t> = 0.14
- Pion slope temperature increase from fusion: 4% relative to version without fusion
- Elastic cross section assumed: sigma_el = 5 mb
- Hagedorn temperature range for reasonable data agreement: T_H = 165-175 MeV
- Antiproton-to-pion ratio at RHIC: model gives 0.06 (T_H=170 MeV) vs data 0.07 +/- 0.01
- Antiproton-to-proton ratio at RHIC: model gives 0.65 (T_H=170 MeV) vs data 0.65 +/- 0.07
- Kaon-to-pion ratio at RHIC: model gives 0.175 (T_H=170 MeV) vs data 0.146 +/- 0.024
- Lambda-to-hadron ratio at RHIC: model gives 0.054 (T_H=170 MeV) vs data 0.054 +/- 0.015

## Context

This work extends the Hagedorn statistical bootstrap approach (Hagedorn, 1965) to resonance decay chains in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, drawing analogy with the Weisskopf compound-nucleus model from low-energy nuclear physics. The model provides an alternative to grand-canonical thermodynamic models (Braun-Munzinger et al., 1999; Redlich et al., 2000; Becattini et al., 2001) that require cut-offs on primary resonance mass, and demonstrates that apparent chemical equilibrium can emerge from sequential decays without assuming thermal equilibrium. The results enable future investigations of strangeness production mechanisms, the role of non-resonant processes, and the connection between the Hagedorn temperature and the QCD critical temperature from lattice calculations (Karsch, 2002).
