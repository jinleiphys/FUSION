# Quark mass dependence of the nucleon axial-vector coupling constant

> We study the quark mass expansion of the axial-vector coupling constant g_A of the nucleon. The aim is to explore the feasibility of chiral effective field theory methods for extrapolation of lattice QCD results - so far determined at relatively large quark masses corresponding to pion masses larger than 0.6 GeV - down to the physical value of the pion mass. We compare two versions of non-relativistic chiral effective field theory: One scheme restricted to pion and nucleon degrees of freedom only, and an alternative approach which incorporates explicit Delta(1230) resonance degrees of freedom. It turns out that, in order to approach the physical value of g_A in a leading-one-loop calculation, the inclusion of the explicit Delta(1230) degrees of freedom is crucial. With information on important higher order couplings constrained from analyses of inelastic pion production processes, a chiral extrapolation function for g_A is obtained, which works well from the chiral limit across the physical point into the region of present lattice data. The resulting enhancement of our extrapolation function near the physical pion mass is found to arise from an interplay between long- and short- distance physics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a leading-one-loop chiral effective field theory calculation can successfully extrapolate lattice QCD results for the nucleon axial-vector coupling constant g_A from pion masses above 0.6 GeV down to the physical pion mass, but only if explicit Delta(1230) resonance degrees of freedom are included and higher-order couplings are constrained by independent experimental data from πN → ππN reactions.

## Method

The authors performed leading-one-loop calculations of g_A(m_π) using two versions of non-relativistic chiral effective field theory: heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory (HBChPT) with only pion and nucleon degrees of freedom, and the small scale expansion (SSE) which additionally includes explicit Delta(1230) resonance fields. They fitted the resulting analytic expressions to five quenched lattice QCD data points from the QCDSF collaboration in the pion mass range 0.3 GeV² < m_π² < 0.6 GeV², using three different fitting strategies: free fits, fits constrained by SU(6) symmetry for the axial ΔΔ coupling g_1, and fits constrained by information on the counterterm B_9 from analyses of πN → ππN scattering.

## Key numbers

- The physical value of g_A is 1.2670(30) from neutron beta decay, used as a constraint in some fits.
- The lattice data used span pion masses from approximately 0.55 GeV to 0.77 GeV (m_π² from 0.3 to 0.6 GeV²), with five data points from the QCDSF collaboration.
- The free SSE fit (Fit Ib) yielded g_A^0 = 0.78 ± 0.04, C(λ=1 GeV) = +1.06 ± 0.08 GeV⁻², and g_1 = 0.0 ± 0.1, with χ²/d.o.f. = 0.55.
- The SU(6)-constrained SSE fit (Fit II) with g_1 = 9/5 g_A^0 gave g_A^0 = 0.94 ± 0.04 and C(λ=1 GeV) = -0.25 ± 0.04 GeV⁻², with χ²/d.o.f. = 0.39.
- The fit constrained by πN → ππN data (Fit III) gave g_A^0 in the range 1.12 to 1.26, C(λ=1 GeV) between -2.2 and -4.6 GeV⁻², and g_1 between 4.3 and 6.6, with χ²/d.o.f. = 0.48 to 0.60.
- The counterterm B_9^r at λ = m_π^phys = 0.138 GeV was determined from πN → ππN analysis as (-1.4 ± 1.2) GeV⁻².

## Context

This work extends earlier chiral extrapolation attempts for g_A by Leinweber et al. (using Padé approximants for magnetic moments) and Detmold et al. (using a chiral quark model with SU(6) symmetry for Δ couplings), which failed to produce the enhancement of g_A near the physical pion mass. The paper demonstrates that a systematic perturbative calculation including all short- and long-distance contributions to leading-one-loop order in the small scale expansion, with the Δ(1230) as an explicit degree of freedom, can succeed when higher-order couplings are constrained by independent data from πN → ππN scattering. This enables reliable chiral extrapolation of lattice QCD results for g_A from the region of present lattice simulations down to the physical point.
