# Evidence against nuclear polarization as source of fine-structure anomalies in muonic atoms

> A long-standing problem of fine-structure anomalies in muonic atoms is revisited by considering the $Δ2p$ splitting in muonic $^{90}\mathrm{Zr}$, $^{120}\mathrm{Sn}$ and $^{208}\mathrm{Pb}$ and the $Δ3p$ splitting in muonic $^{208}\mathrm{Pb}$. State-of-the-art techniques from both nuclear and atomic physics are brought together in order to perform the most comprehensive to date calculations of nuclear-polarization energy shifts. Barring the more subtle case of muonic $^{208}\mathrm{Pb}$, the results suggest that the dominant calculation uncertainty is much smaller than the persisting discrepancies between theory and experiment. We conclude that the resolution to the anomalies is likely to be rooted in refined QED corrections or even some other previously unaccounted-for contributions.

## Key claim
The paper argues that nuclear polarization (NP) is unlikely to be the source of the long-standing fine-structure anomalies in muonic atoms, because the dominant nuclear model uncertainty is much smaller than the gap between theoretical predictions and experimental data for muonic ⁹⁰Zr, ¹²⁰Sn, and ²⁰⁸Pb, barring the more subtle case of muonic ²⁰⁸Pb where muon-nuclear resonances may complicate the picture.

## Method
The authors computed nuclear-polarization energy shifts for muonic ⁹⁰Zr, ¹²⁰Sn, and ²⁰⁸Pb using a field-theoretical framework that includes the full electromagnetic muon-nucleus interaction via ladder, cross, and seagull Feynman diagrams. The muonic Dirac spectrum was treated with finite basis-set expansions using B-splines within the dual-kinetic-balance approach, while the nuclear spectrum was computed using Hartree-Fock plus random-phase approximation (RPA) with nine different Skyrme parametrizations to assess model dependence.

## Key numbers
- For muonic ⁹⁰Zr, the NP contribution to the fine-structure splitting Δ2pᴺᴾ ranges from 5.3 eV (KDE0) to 6.0 eV (SkP and SGII), all positive and far from the experimentally allowed region.
- For muonic ¹²⁰Sn, Δ2pᴺᴾ is persistently positive across all Skyrme parametrizations, ranging from 17.0 eV (BSk14) to 21.1 eV (SGII), while the experimental analysis requires Δ2pᴺᴾ < 0 to resolve the anomaly.
- For muonic ²⁰⁸Pb, Δ2pᴺᴾ ranges from 48.1 eV (BSk14) to 69.3 eV (SkP), and Δ3pᴺᴾ ranges from -44.1 eV (SkP) to -29.5 eV (KDE0), both showing a persistent gap from experimental values.
- The calculations in Feynman and Coulomb gauges agree within 0.1–0.3%, demonstrating excellent gauge invariance.
- The completeness of the RPA nuclear spectra was numerically controlled using the energy-weighted sum rule, fulfilled at the level of at least 99.8% in most cases.

## Context
This work extends the theoretical efforts of Tanaka (1994), Haga (2002, 2005, 2007), and Rinker (1975) who previously attempted to explain fine-structure anomalies in muonic atoms using nuclear-polarization calculations with various approximations. The present study improves upon these by using a complete muonic Dirac spectrum via B-spline expansions and a fully self-consistent RPA treatment of nuclear excitations with multiple Skyrme interactions, enabling a systematic assessment of nuclear model dependence. The results redirect attention toward refined QED corrections (such as self-energy, previously estimated only with simple prescriptions by Borie and Rinker) as the likely resolution to the anomalies, and enable future work on muonic ⁹⁰Zr and tin isotopes before tackling the more complicated case of muonic ²⁰⁸Pb.
