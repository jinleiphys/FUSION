# Weak Polarized Electron Scattering

> Scattering polarized electrons provides an important probe of the weak interactions. Precisely measuring the parity-violating left-right cross section asymmetry is the goal of a number of experiments recently completed or in progress. The experiments are challenging, since A_{LR} is small, typically between 10^(-4) and 10^(-8). By carefully choosing appropriate targets and kinematics, various pieces of the weak Lagrangian can be isolated, providing a search for physics beyond the Standard Model. For other choices, unique features of the strong interaction are studied, including the radius of the neutron density in heavy nuclei, charge symmetry violation, and higher twist terms. This article reviews the theory behind the experiments, as well as the general techniques used in the experimental program.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that parity-violating electron scattering (PVES) provides a versatile probe of both electroweak physics and hadronic/nuclear structure, but the interpretation of asymmetries requires careful control of hadronic effects such as charge symmetry violation, higher twist terms, and nuclear structure uncertainties that the abstract does not detail.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework for parity-violating electron scattering, including the effective Lagrangian for neutral-current interactions and the definition of electroweak couplings \(g_{AV}^{eq}\) and \(g_{VA}^{eq}\) with radiative corrections. They survey experimental techniques across multiple facilities (SLAC, Mainz, MIT-Bates, Jefferson Lab), describing spectrometer designs, detector choices (integrating vs. counting), beam helicity reversal methods, and systematic error control. The paper also examines how different targets and kinematics isolate either electroweak parameters or hadronic effects such as neutron densities, charge symmetry violation, and higher twist contributions.

## Key numbers

- The parity-violating left-right asymmetry \(A_{LR}\) is typically between \(10^{-4}\) and \(10^{-8}\)
- The Qweak experiment measured \(A_{LR}^{ep} = -0.280 \pm 0.035\ \text{(stat)} \pm 0.031\ \text{(syst)}\) ppm, yielding the constraint \(2 g_{AV}^{eu} + g_{AV}^{ed} = -0.032 \pm 0.006\) (SM prediction: \(-0.0355\))
- The SLAC-E158 experiment measured \(A_{LR}^{ee} = -0.131 \pm 0.014\ \text{(stat)} \pm 0.010\ \text{(syst)}\) ppm, extracting \(g_{AV}^{ee} = 0.0190 \pm 0.0027\) (SM prediction: \(0.0225\))
- The PREX experiment measured \(A_{LR} = 0.656 \pm 0.060\ \text{(stat)} \pm 0.014\ \text{(syst)}\) ppm for elastic scattering from lead
- The proposed MOLLER experiment aims for \(\delta A_{LR}/A_{LR} = 2.3\%\) with \(A_{LR} = 0.035\) ppm
- The proposed SoLID experiment aims for \(\delta A_{LR}/A_{LR} = 0.6\%\) with \(A_{LR} = -800\) ppm
- The proposed Mainz-P2 experiment aims for \(\delta A_{LR}/A_{LR} = 1.7\%\) with \(A_{LR} = -0.020\) ppm

## Context

This review extends earlier reviews of strange elastic form factors (Armstrong, 2012) and the weak mixing angle at low energies (Kumar, 2013) by focusing on neutron density measurements in heavy nuclei, charge symmetry violation, and higher twist effects in deep inelastic scattering. The paper connects PVES results to atomic parity violation measurements in cesium (Wood, 1997) and describes how future experiments at Jefferson Lab (MOLLER, SoLID), the proposed Electron Ion Collider (Accardi, 2012), and the MESA facility in Mainz will improve precision on electroweak couplings by factors of 2-5 and probe neutron radii with implications for nuclear structure and neutron star physics.
