# Three-body decays: structure, decay mechanism and fragment properties

> We discuss the three-body decay mechanisms of many-body resonances. R-matrix sequential description is compared with full Faddeev computation. The role of the angular momentum and boson symmetries is also studied. As an illustration we show the computed $α$-particle energy distribution after the decay of 12C(1^+) resonance at 12.7 MeV.

# Three-body decays: structure, decay mechanism and fragment properties

## Key claim
The same measured three-body decay momentum distributions can be described by two apparently opposite mechanisms, sequential decay via an intermediate two-body state or direct decay into the three-body continuum, because the decay path is not an observable, making unique extraction of structure and decay mechanism ambiguous without model interpretation.

## Method
The authors compare R-matrix sequential decay simulations with full Faddeev computations using the complex scaled hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method to solve the three-body Faddeev equations. Short-range and Coulomb potentials are included, and a structureless three-body interaction is adjusted to fit the resonance position. The single-particle energy distributions are obtained by integrating the squared wave function over hyperangles.

## Key numbers
- The $^{12}$C($1^+$) resonance at 12.7 MeV excitation energy is used as the illustrative example for three-alpha decay.
- For the Faddeev computation, the $^8$Be($2^+$) two-body energy is fixed at $E_{2r} = 2.7$ MeV, and three-body energies $E_{3r}$ of 3.5, 5.4, and 7.8 MeV are tested.
- The sequential decay probability $P_{seq}$ at large distances ranges from 0.001 at $E_{3r}=3.5$ MeV to 0.89 at $E_{3r}=7.8$ MeV, with corresponding three-body widths $\Gamma_{3r}$ of 0.005, 0.09, and 1.15 MeV.
- The ratio $E_{2r}/E_{3r}$ takes values 0.86, 0.56, and 0.39 for the three cases.

## Context
This work extends earlier conflicting descriptions of the $^{12}$C($1^+$) decay: Fynbo (2003) successfully described it as sequential via $^8$Be($2^+$), while Alvarez-Rodriguez (2007) described it as direct three-body decay. The paper shows that both R-matrix and Faddeev approaches can reproduce the same measured distributions, implying that the decay mechanism is not uniquely determined by the data. The results enable future work to recognize that full Faddeev computations are needed to reliably extract structure information from three-body decays, and that R-matrix analyses alone may be insufficient.
