# Nuclear single-particle states: dynamical shell model and energy density functional methods

> We discuss different approaches to the problem of reproducing the observed features of nuclear single-particle (s.p.) spectra. In particular, we analyze the dominant energy peaks, and the single-particle strength fragmentation, using the example of neutron states in 208Pb. Our main emphasis is the interpretation of that fragmentation as due to particle-vibration coupling (PVC). We compare with recent Energy Density Functional (EDF) approaches, and try to present a critical perspective.

# Nuclear single-particle states: dynamical shell model and energy density functional methods

## Key claim
The paper argues that despite decades of effort, no consistent theoretical calculation exists that accurately reproduces both the dominant single-particle energies and spectroscopic factors for neutron states in 208Pb, even for this benchmark nucleus. The authors contend that particle-vibration coupling (PVC) is the primary mechanism for single-particle strength fragmentation, but that current implementations in both dynamical shell model and Energy Density Functional (EDF) approaches remain insufficient.

## Method
The authors compare three distinct theoretical approaches for neutron states in 208Pb: relativistic mean field (RMF) plus PVC using the NL3 effective Lagrangian with relativistic RPA phonons; Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus PVC using the SLy5 interaction with fully self-consistent RPA phonons; and a static EDF approach using SLy4_L where dressed energies are obtained from total energy differences of even-even and odd systems. The particle-vibration coupling is treated within second-order perturbation theory for the mass operator, and the resulting shifts, dressed energies, and spectroscopic factors are compared with experimental data.

## Key numbers
- Skyrme-HFB calculations reproduce nuclear masses with accuracy of about 0.6 MeV (citing Goriely)
- Average m_omega values from 1968-1983 calculations: 1.2-1.4 for neutron valence shells in 208Pb, 1.3-1.6 for proton case
- Shifts (Delta epsilon) from RMF+PVC (Ref. PR) range from -1.36 MeV (1j15/2) to +1.40 MeV (1h9/2 and 2f7/2)
- Shifts from Skyrme+PVC (Ref. Dubrovnik) range from -0.55 MeV (1j15/2) to +0.63 MeV (2f7/2)
- Dressed energies from RMF+PVC for 3d3/2: -0.63 MeV (experiment: -1.40 MeV); from Skyrme+PVC: +0.01 MeV
- Dressed energies from RMF+PVC for 2f7/2: -9.71 MeV (experiment: -9.71 MeV); from Skyrme+PVC: -11.44 MeV
- Spectroscopic factors from RMF+PVC range from 0.36 (1h9/2) to 0.92 (4s1/2); from Skyrme+PVC range from 0.75 (2f7/2) to 0.95 (4s1/2)
- Experimental spectroscopic factors for neutron states in 208Pb have uncertainties of +/-0.1 to +/-0.2
- Short-range correlations explain only up to about 10% of deviations of spectroscopic factors from independent single-particle values (citing Barbieri)

## Context
This work extends the dynamical shell model formalism originally developed by Bertsch and Kuo (1970s) and reviewed by Mahaux and collaborators (1980s), which demonstrated the enhancement of the effective mass near the Fermi energy in finite nuclei. The paper directly compares modern PVC calculations (Refs. PR, Dubrovnik) with EDF approaches (Ref. Zalewski using SLy4_L) and finds that neither reproduces experimental data for 208Pb consistently. The authors note that tensor force studies by Lesinski and collaborators cannot remedy deficiencies of central terms in Skyrme functionals, while gradient corrections up to N3LO (Carlsson) show promise for improving single-particle energies. The work identifies the need for merging dynamical theories (necessary for spectroscopic factors) with EDF approaches as the future direction for nuclear structure research.
