# Strangelets in Terrestrial Atmosphere

> A new dynamical model for the propagation of strangelets through the terrestrial atmosphere is proposed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that strangelets observed at mountain altitudes (around 3.6 km) can originate from initially small lumps of strange quark matter (baryon number as low as 64 amu) that grow in mass by absorbing neutrons during collisions with atmospheric nuclei, rather than from initially large strangelets that fragment as previously assumed.

## Method

The authors model the propagation of a strangelet through the Earth's atmosphere by solving the equations of motion (including gravity and the terrestrial magnetic field) using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method, with the strangelet absorbing neutrons from air molecules (mainly N₂) during collisions. The mass increase is governed by a differential equation that depends on the mean free path of the strangelet (which varies with altitude and instantaneous mass) and a geometric factor determining the fraction of neutrons absorbed per collision. Initial conditions are set at 25 km altitude with a strangelet mass of 64 amu, charge +2, and speed 6.6×10⁷ m/s, corresponding to a geomagnetic latitude of about 30° (representative of northeastern India).

## Key numbers

- Initial strangelet mass at 25 km altitude: 64 amu, with charge +2 and speed 6.6×10⁷ m/s
- Final strangelet mass at 3.6 km altitude (height of Sandakphu peak in northeastern India): approximately 340 amu
- Earth's magnetic dipole moment used in calculations: 8.1×10²² J/T
- Geomagnetic latitude of observation point: approximately 30°

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of strangelet propagation by Wilk et al. (1990s), who proposed that strangelets lose mass in collisions and have a critical mass below which they evaporate into neutrons. The present paper contradicts that picture by arguing that strangelets instead absorb neutrons and grow in mass, based on the expectation that strange quark matter is more tightly bound than ordinary nuclei. The model enables predictions for the flux of strangelets detectable by large-area detectors at mountain altitudes, such as the experiment being set up at Sandakphu (Saha, year not specified).
