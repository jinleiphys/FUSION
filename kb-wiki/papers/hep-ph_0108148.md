# The luminosity constraint on solar neutrino fluxes

> A specific linear combination of the total solar neutrino fluxes must equal the measured solar photon luminosity if nuclear fusion reactions among light elements are responsible for solar energy generation. This luminosity constraint, previously used in a limited form in testing the no neutrino oscillation hypothesis, is derived in a generality that includes all of the relevant solar neutrino fluxes and which is suitable for analyzing the results of many different solar neutrino experiments. With or without allowing for neutrino oscillations, the generalized luminosity constraint can be used in future analyses of solar neutrino data. Accurate numerical values for the linear coefficients are provided.

## Key claim

If nuclear fusion reactions among light elements are responsible for the solar luminosity, a specific linear combination of all relevant solar neutrino fluxes must equal the measured solar photon luminosity; the coefficients in this constraint are determined by nuclear mass differences and are independent of solar model details to an accuracy of one part in 10^4 or better, though the derivation assumes local kinetic equilibrium for deuterium and helium-3.

## Method

The author derives the luminosity constraint by writing the solar luminosity as a sum over neutrino fluxes multiplied by energy coefficients alpha_i, where each alpha_i represents the thermal energy delivered to the star from the nuclear reactions associated with that neutrino flux minus the average neutrino energy loss. For the pp chain, the derivation uses the local equilibrium conditions for deuterium and helium-3 to eliminate the fictitious helium-3-helium-3 reaction flux, then expresses each alpha coefficient in terms of atomic mass differences and computed average neutrino energies. For CNO neutrinos, the coefficients are derived directly from the relevant reaction chains. The constraint is presented in both dimensional form and a dimensionless form using the BP2000 standard solar model as a reference.

## Key numbers

- The solar luminosity used is 3.842 x 10^33 erg s^-1, derived from satellite data
- The characteristic solar photon flux used for normalization is 8.5272 x 10^10 cm^-2 s^-1 (corresponding to 10 MeV)
- The alpha coefficient for pp neutrinos is 1.30987 (in units of 10 MeV), with a dimensionless coefficient a = 0.6978
- The alpha coefficient for pep neutrinos is 1.19193 (10 MeV), with a = 0.001642
- The alpha coefficient for ^7Be neutrinos is 1.26008 (10 MeV), with a = 0.05594
- The alpha coefficient for ^8B neutrinos is 0.66305 (10 MeV), with a = 0.0000592
- The alpha coefficient for ^13N neutrinos is 0.34577 (10 MeV), with a = 0.006426
- The alpha coefficient for ^15O neutrinos is 2.15706 (10 MeV), with a = 0.005629
- The alpha coefficient for hep neutrinos is 0.37370 (10 MeV), with a = 1.09E-07
- Thermal corrections to the alpha values are less than or of order 0.01% for all important solar neutrino sources

## Context

This work generalizes the luminosity constraint previously used in a limited form by Spiro and Vignaud (1990) and later authors including Hata, Bludman, and Langacker (1994), Parke (1995), and Heeger and Robertson (1996), who grouped neutrinos into only three sets (low, intermediate, and high energy). The generalized formulation includes all seven important solar neutrino fluxes and corrects errors in published coefficients, including an error in the author's own prior work with Krastev (1996) regarding the hep coefficient. This enables future analyses of solar neutrino data from six or more experiments (chlorine, Kamiokande, SAGE, GALLEX/GNO, Super-Kamiokande, SNO, and upcoming BOREXINO and ICARUS) to incorporate the luminosity constraint both for testing the no-oscillation hypothesis and for determining neutrino oscillation parameters.
