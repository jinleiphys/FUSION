# A possible solution to the gallium anomaly moving beyond the leptonic wave function factorization

> For over thirty years, a $\sim20\%$ deficit, now exceeding $5σ$, has persisted between measured and predicted neutrino capture rates on $^{71}$Ga, as observed in radioactive source experiments (namely GALLEX, SAGE, and more recently BEST) using $^{51}$Cr and $^{37}$Ar. This long-standing discrepancy, referred to as the gallium anomaly, has posed a significant challenge to our understanding of both experimental methods and theoretical predictions. In this work, we revisit the theoretical calculation of the neutrino capture cross-section by moving beyond the standard treatment of the leptonic wave functions, revealing limitations in the commonly used factorization approach based on the detailed balance principle. Incorporating phenomenologically constrained Gamow-Teller transition densities, able to correctly reproduce the precisely measured half-life of $^{71}{\textrm{Ge}}$, we find that the revised cross-section can be significantly reduced, potentially resolving the gallium anomaly without invoking new physics.

## Key claim

The gallium anomaly, a persistent ~20% deficit between measured and predicted neutrino capture rates on 71Ga that now exceeds 5σ significance, can potentially be resolved without invoking sterile neutrinos or other new physics by abandoning the standard factorization of leptonic and nuclear wave functions in the theoretical cross-section calculation, provided that phenomenologically constrained Gamow-Teller transition densities are used.

## Method

The authors recalculated the ground-state inverse beta decay cross section for νe + 71Ga → 71Ge + e− by moving beyond the detailed-balance approximation that factorizes leptonic and nuclear matrix elements. They employed exact Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater (DHFS) radial solutions for electron wave functions and introduced data-driven phenomenological parametrizations (Single Gaussian, Sum of Gaussians, and Double Gaussian forms) for the weak transition density ρTD(r), which were constrained by a combined fit to both the experimental 71Ge half-life and the measured neutrino capture rates from 51Cr and 37Ar sources in the GALLEX, SAGE, and BEST experiments.

## Key numbers

- Experimental ground-state IBD cross section for 51Cr source: σexp_gs,51Cr = (4.44 ± 0.33) × 10−45 cm²
- Experimental ground-state IBD cross section for 37Ar source: σexp_gs,37Ar = (5.21 ± 0.72) × 10−45 cm²
- Experimental 71Ge half-life: t1/2exp = 11.465 ± 0.003 d
- Best-fit SOG parametrization yields σgs,51Cr = 4.45 × 10−45 cm² and σgs,37Ar = 5.17 × 10−45 cm², with t1/2 = 11.463 d (χ²IBD = 0.005, χ²EC = 0.0003), fully resolving the anomaly
- Best-fit DG parametrization yields σgs,51Cr = 4.44 × 10−45 cm² and σgs,37Ar = 5.15 × 10−45 cm², with t1/2 = 11.456 d (χ²IBD = 0.007, χ²EC = 0.007), fully resolving the anomaly
- SG parametrization fails to resolve the anomaly, yielding σgs,51Cr = 5.27 × 10−45 cm² and σgs,37Ar = 6.30 × 10−45 cm², with t1/2 = 11.515 d (χ²IBD = 8.68, χ²EC = 0.24)

## Context

This work extends the framework developed in Cadeddu et al. (2025) by critically reassessing the detailed-balance principle that has been used since the foundational work of Bahcall (1997) to relate neutrino capture on 71Ga to electron capture on 71Ge. The authors show that the standard factorization approach, which assumes leptonic wave functions are constant over the nuclear volume, introduces significant bias. The revised calculation, using transition densities that can exhibit sign-changing behavior (as seen in ab-initio studies by King et al. (2020) and Ney et al. (2021)), reduces the cross section by ~20% and resolves the anomaly without sterile neutrinos, which are now disfavored by MicroBooNE and KATRIN results. This work calls for improved theoretical and experimental determination of weak transition densities in medium-mass nuclei.
