# Matching Stages of Heavy Ion Collision Models

> Heavy ion reactions and other collective dynamical processes are frequently described by different theoretical approaches for the different stages of the process, like initial equilibration stage, intermediate locally equilibrated fluid dynamical stage and final freeze-out stage. For the last stage the best known is the Cooper-Frye description used to generate the phase space distribution of emitted, non-interacting, particles from a fluid dynamical expansion/explosion, assuming a final ideal gas distribution, or (less frequently) an out of equilibrium distribution. In this work we do not want to replace the Cooper-Frye description, rather clarify the ways how to use it and how to choose the parameters of the distribution, eventually how to choose the form of the phase space distribution used in the Cooper-Frye formula. Moreover, the Cooper-Frye formula is used in connection with the freeze-out problem, while the discussion of transition between different stages of the collision is applicable to other transitions also. More recently hadronization and molecular dynamics models are matched to the end of a fluid dynamical stage to describe hadronization and freeze-out. The stages of the model description can be matched to each other on spacetime hypersurfaces (just like through the frequently used freeze-out hypersurface). This work presents a generalized description of how to match the stages of the description of a reaction to each other, extending the methodology used at freeze-out, in simple covariant form which is easily applicable in its simplest version for most applications.

# Matching Stages of Heavy Ion Collision Models

## Key claim

This work does not replace the Cooper-Frye freeze-out description but clarifies how to choose its parameters and the form of the phase space distribution, extending the matching methodology between different stages of heavy ion collisions (initial, fluid dynamical, and freeze-out stages) to a simple covariant form applicable even when the matter before the transition hypersurface is not in thermal equilibrium.

## Method

The authors derive covariant scalar equations from the conservation laws for baryon current and energy-momentum tensor across a spacetime hypersurface, solving for the final state parameters (energy density, pressure, baryon density, and flow velocity) when the post-transition matter is in thermal equilibrium with a known equation of state. They then extend the formalism to cases where the final state is out of thermal equilibrium, using anisotropic distribution functions such as the cut-Jüttner and cancelling-Jüttner distributions, and discuss the matching of fluid dynamics to molecular dynamics models via the Cooper-Frye formula with enforcement of conservation laws through iterative correction.

## Key numbers

- For an ideal gas of massless particles after the front with equation of state P = e/3, the covariant equation reduces to a quadratic: dσ̂ᵝ dσ̂_β e² + 2 aᵝ dσ̂_β e − 3 aᵝ a_β = 0, where aᵝ ≡ A₀ᵝ/D.
- When flow velocity is normal to the freeze-out hypersurface and the initial fluid is in its local rest frame, the quadratic simplifies to e² + 2 e₀ e − 3 e₀² = 0, yielding physical root e = e₀ (energy density conserved) and unphysical root e = −3 e₀.
- The generalized specific volume X = (e+P)/n² appears in the equation j² (w/n²) = A₀ᵝ dσ_β + P (dσᵝ dσ_β), connecting to relativistic shock and detonation theory.

## Context

This work extends the freeze-out matching methodology originally developed by Cooper and Frye (1974) and the generalized conservation law treatment of Csernai (1987, 1994) to cover transitions where the initial state is not in thermal equilibrium, going beyond the Rayleigh line and Taub adiabat approach. It also addresses the anisotropic freeze-out distributions identified by Bugaev (1996) using cut-Jüttner distributions and by Csernai and Tamosiunas (2007) using cancelling-Jüttner distributions, and provides a framework for matching fluid dynamics to molecular dynamics models as practiced in hybrid models combining fluid dynamics with microscopic transport.
