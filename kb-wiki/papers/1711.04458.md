# Benchmarking theoretical formalisms for $(p,pn)$ reactions: the $^{15}$C($p,pn$)$^{14}$C case

> [Background] Proton-induced knockout reactions of the form $(p,pN)$ have experienced a renewed interest in recent years due to the possibility of performing these measurements with rare isotopes, using inverse kinematics. Several theoretical models are being used for the interpretation of these new data, such as the distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA), the transition amplitude formulation of the Faddeev equations due to Alt, Grassberger and Sandhas (FAGS) and, more recently, a coupled-channels method here referred to as transfer-to-the-continuum (TC). [Purpose] Our goal is to compare the momentum distributions calculated with the DWIA and TC models for the same reactions, using whenever possible the same inputs (e.g. distorting potential). A comparison with already published results for the FAGS formalism is performed as well. [Method] We choose the $^{15}$C($p$,$pn$)$^{14}$C reaction at an incident energy of 420 MeV/u, which has been previously studied with the FAGS formalism. The knocked-out neutron is assumed to be in a $2s$ single-particle orbital. Longitudinal and transverse momentum distributions are calculated for different assumed separation energies. [Results] For all cases considered, we find a very good agreement between DWIA and TC results. The energy dependence of the distorting optical potentials is found to affect in a modest way the shape and magnitude of the momentum distributions. Moreover, when relativistic kinematics corrections are omitted, our calculations reproduce remarkably well the FAGS result. [Conclusions] The results found in this work provide confidence on the consistency and accuracy of the DWIA and TC models for analyzing momentum distributions for $(p,pn)$ reactions at intermediate energies.

## Key claim

The distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) and transfer-to-the-continuum (TC) models produce fully consistent momentum distributions for the 15C(p,pn)14C reaction at 420 MeV/u, but only when the same input ingredients (distorting potentials, binding potentials) are used; the agreement with the more sophisticated Faddeev-AGS (FAGS) formalism requires omitting relativistic kinematics corrections.

## Method

The authors calculated longitudinal and transverse momentum distributions for the 15C(p,pn)14C knockout reaction at an incident energy of 420 MeV/u using the DWIA and TC formalisms, assuming the knocked-out neutron occupies a 2s single-particle orbital. The calculations employed the Reid93 nucleon-nucleon potential, Woods-Saxon binding potentials with depth adjusted to three different separation energies (1.22, 5, and 18 MeV), and the EDAD2 Dirac phenomenology optical potential for nucleon-nucleus distortions. For comparison with published FAGS results, the Koning-Delaroche optical potential at 200 MeV was used with non-relativistic kinematics.

## Key numbers

* The longitudinal momentum distribution (LMD) peak from DWIA is reduced by 8.0% when energy-dependent optical potentials for outgoing nucleons are used instead of energy-independent potentials, for the case with Sn = 1.22 MeV.
* The LMD peak reduction from energy-dependent potentials is 9.3% for Sn = 5 MeV and 4.9% for Sn = 18 MeV.
* The difference between TC and energy-independent DWIA at the LMD peak is only 0.3% for Sn = 1.22 MeV, 0.8% for Sn = 5 MeV, and 1.4% for Sn = 18 MeV.
* Including relativistic kinematics corrections in the TC calculation increases the transverse momentum distribution magnitude by approximately 30% compared to the non-relativistic calculation.

## Context

This work benchmarks three theoretical formalisms (DWIA, TC, and FAGS) used to analyze proton-induced knockout reactions, which have become important for studying single-particle structure in rare isotopes via inverse kinematics. The comparison extends earlier work by Crespo et al. (2008, 2009) showing that DWIA can be recovered from a truncated Faddeev multiple-scattering series, and directly compares with FAGS calculations published by Cravo et al. (2016) for the same reaction. The results enable reliable use of the computationally simpler DWIA and TC methods for analyzing ongoing experimental campaigns measuring (p,pn) cross sections, with the caveat that relativistic corrections must be included for accurate spectroscopic factor extraction.
