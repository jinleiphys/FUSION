# Characterization of the scission point from fission-fragment velocities

> The isotopic-yield distributions and kinematic properties of fragments produced in transfer-induced fission of 240Pu and fusion-induced fission of 250Cf, with 9 MeV and 45 MeV of excitation energy respectively, were measured in inverse kinematics with the spectrometer VAMOS. The kinematic properties of identified fission fragments allow to derive properties of the scission configuration such as the distance between fragments, the total kinetic energy, the neutron multiplicity, the total excitation energy, and, for the first time, the proton- and neutron-number sharing during the emergence of the fragments. These properties of the scission point are studied as functions of the fragment atomic number. The correlation between these observables, gathered in one single experiment and for two different fissioning systems at different excitation energies, give valuable information for the understanding and modeling of the fission process.

## Key claim

The paper derives properties of the scission configuration, including fragment distance, total kinetic energy, neutron multiplicity, total excitation energy, and, for the first time, proton- and neutron-number sharing during fragment emergence, from fission-fragment velocities measured in inverse kinematics, but the reconstruction relies on the assumption that no neutron evaporation occurs from saddle to scission or from the neck at scission, and that the average momentum relation can be approximated by products of averages with a relative error smaller than 0.4%.

## Method

Transfer-induced fission of 240Pu (excitation energy ~9 MeV) and fusion-induced fission of 250Cf (excitation energy ~45 MeV) were produced using a 238U beam at 6.1 AMeV on a 12C target, with fragment identification via the VAMOS spectrometer in inverse kinematics. Fragment velocity vectors in the fissioning-nucleus reference frame were reconstructed from time-of-flight and trajectory measurements, corrected for energy loss in the target and for transmission cuts. From the velocities, the average fragment masses at scission were deduced using momentum conservation, and the neutron excess, neutron multiplicity, and total kinetic energy at scission were derived as functions of fragment atomic number.

## Key numbers

- 240Pu fissioning system produced with excitation energy centered on E* ~ 9 MeV with a width of 6 MeV; 250Cf produced with initial excitation energy of 45 MeV, leading to an average fissioning system mass of A_FS = 249.6 after pre-scission neutron evaporation.
- Resolution on the resulting fission velocity is better than 2%, based on a 0.4% velocity measurement resolution and 5 mrad angular resolution.
- For 240Pu, the neck distance parameter set to d = 1.9 fm to match average velocities at symmetric splits; for 250Cf, d = 2.2 fm.
- The total kinetic energy at scission for 240Pu shows deviations around Z ~ 50 and Z ~ 44, higher by about 10 MeV compared to calculations with fixed deformation and neck parameters.
- For 250Cf, the measured average total kinetic energy at scission differs from fixed-parameter calculations by up to ~4 MeV towards asymmetric splits; the GEF code shows an overall underestimation from about ~4 MeV at symmetry to ~10 MeV for asymmetric splits.
- Average total neutron multiplicity for 240Pu is around <ν_{1+2}> ≈ 3.

## Context

This work extends the inverse-kinematics approach of Caamaño et al. (2013) by improving the fission-velocity determination (including target energy-loss corrections) and deriving scission-point properties, particularly the neutron excess at scission, for two fissioning systems at different excitation energies. The results are compared with the scission-point model of Wilkins et al. (1976) and with calculations from the GEF code, revealing shell-structure effects in low-energy fission of 240Pu that are suppressed at the higher excitation energy of 250Cf. The data enable more stringent tests of fission models, especially regarding the sharing of neutrons and protons at scission and the partition of excitation energy between fragments.
