# Statistical models of nuclear level densities

> We present calculations of nuclear level densities that are based upon the detailed microphysics of the interacting shell model yet are also computationally tractable. To do this, we combine in a novel fashion several previously disparate ideas from statistical spectroscopy, namely partitioning of the model space into subspaces, analytic calculations of moments up to fourth order directly from the two-body interaction, and Zuker's binomial distribution. We get excellent agreement with full scale interacting shell model calculations. We also calculate ``ab initio'' the level densities for $^{29}$Si and $^{57}$Co and get reasonable agreement with experiment.

# Statistical models of nuclear level densities

## Key claim
The authors combine analytic moment calculations (up to fourth order), configuration partitioning, and Zuker's binomial distribution to produce nuclear level densities that are computationally tractable while retaining microscopic detail from the interacting shell model. The method achieves "excellent agreement" with full shell model calculations and "reasonable agreement" with experimental data for ²⁹Si and ⁵⁷Co, but the authors note that the treatment of particle-hole excitations is "ad hoc" and that results are limited more by uncertainty in the effective interaction than by the statistical approximations themselves.

## Method
The method computes configuration moments (centroids, widths, third and fourth moments) analytically from two-body matrix elements for each subspace of the model space, without diagonalization. These moments are used to parameterize a continuous binomial distribution for each configuration, following Zuker's combinatorial argument that binomials handle asymmetric distributions better than Gaussians. The total level density is obtained by summing the configuration-level binomial distributions, with the option to fix the binomial order N either by the model space dimension (standard binomial) or by the fourth moment (fourth-moment scaled or FMS binomial).

## Key numbers
- Third moments for a 0ℏω mid-pf-shell valence space can be computed in "a few hours" and fourth moments in "a day or two" on a modest workstation, corresponding to roughly 10⁸ levels, which is less demanding than Monte Carlo path integration by a factor of 10 or more in CPU time.
- For most binomials and shell model diagonalization, the scaled fourth moment m₄ is less than 3, with a typical value around 2.8; the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble semicircle distribution has m₄ = 2.
- The difference between the best description (sum of partitioned binomials) and other approaches is, at low energy, a factor of two or more.
- For ⁵⁴Fe and ⁴⁸Cr in the pf-shell, the sum of binomials is "clearly superior" to either a Gaussian or a single standard binomial.

## Context
This work extends the statistical spectroscopy approach of Mon and French (1975) and Wong (1986), who showed that level densities in finite spaces tend toward Gaussians described by first and second moments. The authors improve upon earlier attempts by Grimes et al. (1979), who computed higher moments via the representative vector method, and by path-integral Monte Carlo techniques (Johnson 1992, Dean 1995, Ormand 1997), which are limited by the sign problem and require supercomputer time. The method enables microscopic level density calculations for nuclides where full diagonalization is impossible due to large model space dimensions, and is intended for application to nuclear reaction rate calculations in nucleosynthesis, particularly neutron-capture cross sections for the s and r processes.
