# Neutron stars with hyperon cores: stellar radii and EOS near nuclear density

> The existence of 2 Msun pulsars puts very strong constraints on the equation of state (EOS) of neutron stars (NSs) with hyperon cores, which can be satisfied only by special models of hadronic matter. The radius-mass relation for these models is sufficiently specific that it could be subjected to an observational test with future X-ray observatories. We want to study the impact of the presence of hyperon cores on the radius-mass relation for NS. We aim to find out how, and for which particular stellar mass range, a specific relation R(M), where M is the gravitational mass, and R is the circumferential radius, is associated with the presence of a hyperon core. We consider a set of 14 theoretical EOS of dense matter, based on the relativistic mean-field (RMF) approximation, allowing for the presence of hyperons in NSs. We seek correlations between R(M) and the stiffness of the EOS below the hyperon threshold needed to pass the 2 Msun test. For NS masses 1.0<M/Msun<1.6, we get R>13km, because of a very stiff pre-hyperon segment of the EOS. At nuclear density, the pressure is significantly higher than a robust upper bound obtained recently using chiral effective field theory. If massive NSs do have a sizable hyperon core, then according to current models the radii for M=1.0-1.6 Msun are necessarily >13km. If, on the contrary, a NS with a radius R<12 km is observed in this mass domain, then sizable hyperon cores in NSs, as we model them now, are ruled out. Future X-ray missions with <5% precision for a simultaneous M and R measurement will have the potential to solve the problem with observations of NSs. Irrespective of this observational test, present EOS allowing for hyperons that fulfill condition M_max>2 Msun yield a pressure at nuclear density that is too high relative to up-to-date microscopic calculations of this quantity.

## Key claim

If massive neutron stars (NSs) do have a sizable hyperon core, then according to current models the radii for masses M = 1.0-1.6 solar masses are necessarily greater than 13 km; if, on the contrary, a NS with a radius less than 12 km is observed in this mass domain, then sizable hyperon cores in NSs, as they are modeled now, are ruled out.

## Method

The authors considered a set of 14 theoretical equations of state (EOS) of dense matter based on the relativistic mean-field (RMF) approximation, allowing for the presence of hyperons in NSs, plus one recent EOS based on non-relativistic G-matrix theory. They sought correlations between the radius-mass relation R(M) and the stiffness of the EOS below the hyperon threshold needed to pass the 2 solar mass test, and compared these hyperonic EOS models to a reference set of purely nucleonic EOS.

## Key numbers

- For NS masses 1.0 < M/M_sun < 1.6, the hyperonic EOS models yield R > 13 km.
- The pressure at nuclear density (n_0 = 0.16 fm^-3) for hyperonic EOS is 8 +/- 2.5 in units of 10^33 dyn cm^-2, while for nucleonic reference EOS it is 3.3 +/- 0.3 in the same units.
- The constraint from chiral effective field theory (Hebeler 2013) gives 2.7 < P_33(n_0) < 4.4 in units of 10^33 dyn cm^-2, which is violated by all hyperonic EOS models.
- For a 1.4 solar mass NS, the rotational increase of equatorial radius follows B_H = 3.1 km/kHz^2 for hyperonic EOS and B_N = 1.6 km/kHz^2 for nucleonic EOS.
- The central baryon density in a 2 solar mass NS is typically n_c/n_0 = 4-5.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of EOS models that satisfy the 2 solar mass constraint from pulsars J1614-2230 (Demorest 2010) and J0348+0432 (Antoniadis 2013), focusing specifically on models with hyperon cores. The authors find that hyperonic EOS models that pass the 2 solar mass test produce pressures at nuclear density that are too high relative to the microscopic calculations of Hebeler (2013) and Gandolfi (2012). The paper enables future X-ray missions with less than 5% precision for simultaneous mass and radius measurements to observationally test whether sizable hyperon cores exist in neutron stars.
