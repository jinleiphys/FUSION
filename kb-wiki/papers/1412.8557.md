# The emergent dynamical symmetry at the triple point of nuclear deformations

> Based on the boson realization of the Euclidean algebras, it is shown that the five-dimensional Euclidean dynamical symmetry may emerge at the triple point of the shape phase diagram of the interacting boson model, which thus offers a symmetry-based understanding of this isolated point. It is further shown that the low-lying dynamics in $^{108}$Pd, $^{134}$Ba, $^{64}$Zn, and $^{114}$Cd may be dominated by the Euclidean dynamical symmetry.

## Key claim

The five-dimensional Euclidean dynamical symmetry (Eu(5) DS) emerges approximately at the triple point of the interacting boson model (IBM) shape phase diagram, but only in the limit where the number of d-bosons is much smaller than the total boson number (n_d/N << 1); this symmetry is almost exact for the ground state in the large-N limit but only approximate for excited states.

## Method

The authors constructed a boson realization of the Euclidean algebra Eu(5) using d-boson operators and expressed the Eu(5) Casimir operator in terms of an SU(1,1) algebra. They then examined commutation relations between the generators of Eu(5) and the IBM consistent-Q Hamiltonian, finding that the commutators vanish only at the parameter values (η=0.5, χ=0), which corresponds to the triple point of the IBM shape phase diagram. The Hamiltonian at this point was shown to reduce to the Eu(5) Casimir operator in the n_d/N << 1 limit. The authors diagonalized the Eu(5) Hamiltonian within a finite Hilbert subspace with m=100 truncation to calculate energy levels and B(E2) transition strengths for candidate nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposal of Eu(5) dynamical symmetry by Zhang et al. (2014) and establishes its connection to the IBM, which was previously missing. The triple point in the IBM was identified by Jolie et al. (2002) and Warner (2002), and the E(5) critical point symmetry was introduced by Iachello (2000). The results show that the Eu(5) DS provides an algebraic equivalent description of the E(5) CPS, revealing a more intimate relationship between the E(5) CPS and the IBM beyond the mean-field approximation. This enables a symmetry-based understanding of nuclei at the triple point and suggests that previously identified E(5) candidates (¹⁰⁸Pd, ¹³⁴Ba, ⁶⁴Zn, ¹¹⁴Cd) may be better described by the Eu(5) dynamical symmetry.
