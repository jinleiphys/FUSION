# Entrance-channel dependence of fission transients

> Fission transients describe the fission rate as it evolves towards the quasistationary value given by Kramers' formula. The nature of fission transients is dependent on the assumed initial distribution of the compound nuclei along the fission coordinate. Although the standard initial assumption of a near-spherical object leads to a transient suppression of the fission rate (fission delay), a moderate initial fissionlike deformation can reduce the magnitude of this suppression. For still larger initial deformations, transient fission enhancements are possible. Examples of this behavior are illustrated via a one-dimensional Langevin simulation. It is argued that the initial conditions are determined by the fusion dynamics and thus are entrance-channel dependent. Transient fission may be considered intermediate between statistical fission and quasifission as the rapid time scale of transient fission may not lead to an equilibrium of the angular and mass-asymmetry coordinates. The relationship between the mean first passage time and the transients are discussed. For temperatures much smaller than the fission barrier, the mean first passage time is independent of the nature of the fission transients if there is no strong competition from evaporation. Thus, fission transients are most important when the evaporation time scale is smaller than, or of the order of, the transient time.

## Key claim

Fission transients are strongly sensitive to the initial deformation of the compound nucleus along the fission coordinate; while the standard assumption of a near-spherical initial shape produces a transient suppression (fission delay), moderate initial fissionlike deformation reduces this suppression, and larger initial deformations can produce a transient fission enhancement, meaning the very existence of a fission delay depends on the appropriate initial condition.

## Method

One-dimensional Langevin simulations in the high-friction limit were performed for a schematic potential with a fission barrier of B=4 MeV, appropriate for a compound nucleus with A=248. The transient fission rate was determined by tracking the last passage of each trajectory through the saddle point, and results were compared for three initial deformations (x_min, x_1, and x_2) at temperatures of T=0.5 and 1 MeV. Additional simulations explored weak friction and the effect of including a competing evaporative decay mode.

## Key numbers

- Fission barrier B = 4 MeV, used for a compound nucleus with A=248.
- Friction parameter γ = 3.4 zs MeV/fm² in the high-friction simulations.
- Temperatures studied: T = 0.5 MeV and T = 1 MeV for the main transient comparisons.
- Initial deformations: V(x₁)-V(x_min) = B/4 and V(x₂)-V(x_min) = B/2.
- For the ¹⁹F+¹⁸¹Ta reaction producing a compound nucleus with 80 MeV excitation energy, the first-chance statistical-fission time t_f = 70 zs at J=45 ħ and drops to t_f = 10 zs at J=63 ħ.
- In the weak-friction simulation, the reduced friction γ/M = 0.023 zs⁻¹ and the oscillation frequency ω = 1.87 zs⁻¹.
- For the HICOL simulation of ⁶⁴Ni+¹⁰⁰Mo at E/A=5 MeV (J=0), thermal excitation energy is dissipated in 0.5 zs.

## Context

This work extends the foundational treatment of fission transients by Grangé and Weidenmüller (1980) and subsequent studies (Grangé 1983, Weidenmüller and Jing-Shang 1984) that assumed near-spherical initial conditions, by demonstrating that the initial deformation is determined by fusion dynamics and is thus entrance-channel dependent. The paper argues that transient fission may be considered intermediate between statistical fission and quasifission, and that calculations invoking fission delays to explain prescission neutron multiplicities (e.g., Hinde 1986) should be reexamined. The results enable future work to couple fusion dynamics with fission transient calculations, particularly for understanding the suppression of residue yields and broadening of fission-fragment mass distributions for more symmetric entrance channels.
