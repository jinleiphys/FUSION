# Some properties of the resonant state in quantum mechanics and its computation

> The resonant state of the open quantum system is studied from the viewpoint of the outgoing momentum flux. We show that the number of particles is conserved for a resonant state, if we use an expanding volume of integration in order to take account of the outgoing momentum flux; the number of particles would decay exponentially in a fixed volume of integration. Moreover, we introduce new numerical methods of treating the resonant state with the use of the effective potential. We first give a numerical method of finding a resonance pole in the complex energy plane. The method seeks an energy eigenvalue iteratively. We found that our method leads to a super-convergence, the convergence exponential with respect to the iteration step. The present method is completely independent of commonly used complex scaling. We also give a numerical trick for computing the time evolution of the resonant state in a limited spatial area. Since the wave function of the resonant state is diverging away from the scattering potential, it has been previously difficult to follow its time evolution numerically in a finite area.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the number of particles in a resonant state is conserved when counted in an expanding volume that tracks the outgoing momentum flux, even though the particle number decays exponentially in a fixed volume. The authors also introduce numerical methods for finding resonance poles and computing time evolution that are independent of the commonly used complex scaling technique.

## Method

The authors derive a general relation showing that the imaginary part of the Hamiltonian expectation value for arbitrary wave functions is proportional to the momentum flux out of the integration volume. They then define the resonant state as an eigenfunction of the Schrödinger equation under the Siegert boundary condition (outgoing waves only) and solve analytically for a double-delta potential. For numerical computation, they introduce an iterative method using an effective potential as an energy-dependent boundary condition to find resonance poles in the complex energy plane, and a numerical trick for computing time evolution of the diverging resonant wave function in a limited spatial area.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of resonant states in open quantum systems by providing a general flux-based interpretation of the complex energy eigenvalue, previously derived only for individual resonant states by Berggren (1987), Moiseyev (1990), and Masui (1999). The numerical method using effective potentials offers an alternative to the complex scaling approach of Aguilar (1971), Balslev (1971), and Simon (1972), enabling computation of resonance poles and time evolution in finite spatial domains. The results enable further studies of quasi-stable resonant states with very long lifetimes in systems such as ladder lattices, to be reported by Nakamura (2007).
