# Nuclear deformation effects on charge radius measurements of the proton and deuteron

> Up to now, all charge radius measurements of the proton and deuteron assumed uniform spheroidal charge distribution. We investigate the nuclear deformation effects on these charge radius measurements by assuming a uniform prolate charge distribution for the proton and deuteron. We solve the energy levels of the corresponding muonic and electric atoms with such deformed nucleus and present how the purely quadruple deformation of proton and deuteron affects their Lamb shifts. The numerical results suggest that the deformation of proton and deuteron leads to that the charge radius extracted from the electronic measurement should be smaller than the corresponding one in the muonic measurement which assumed uniform spheroidal charge distribution. If the central values of newest measurements for the proton are adopted, the proton would have a prolate structure with the 0.91 $\mathrm{fm}$ long axis and 0.73 $\mathrm{fm}$ short axis. Further improved precise charge radius measurements of the proton and deuteron will help us to pin down their shape deformation.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether assuming a uniform prolate (rather than spheroidal) charge distribution for the proton and deuteron can resolve the proton radius puzzle, but finds that the deformation correction to the Lamb shift is negative for both electronic and muonic systems, meaning deformation would require the charge radius extracted from electronic measurements to be *smaller* than that from muonic measurements, the opposite of what the puzzle requires. The authors conclude that nuclear deformation alone cannot explain the discrepancy between muonic and electronic proton radius measurements.

## Method

The authors model the proton and deuteron as uniformly charged prolate spheroids using a geometric collective model with a quadrupole deformation parameter β, solving the Schrödinger equation for both muonic and electronic hydrogen (and deuterium) via the Gaussian Expansion Method with a variational principle. They compute the energy levels of the 1S, 2S, and 2P states for various effective radii r₀ and deformation parameters β, then subtract the energy shifts from the uniform spheroidal case to isolate the deformation contribution ΔE_def to the Lamb shift. The constraint equations relating experimental Lamb shifts to the deformed-nucleus parameters are solved numerically to find consistent (r_p, β_p) pairs for the proton and (r_d, β_d) for the deuteron.

## Key numbers

- For the proton, the effective ratio √⟨r²⟩/r₀ = 0.774 (adopted value from fitting 1S and 2S states of both muonic and electronic hydrogen).
- The experimental proton charge radii used as inputs: r_{p,m;exp} = 0.842 fm (muonic hydrogen) and r_{p,e;exp} = 0.875 fm (electronic hydrogen).
- The experimental deuteron charge radii used: r_{d,m;exp} = 2.12562 fm (muonic deuterium) and r_{d,e;exp} = 2.1424 fm (electronic deuterium).
- The deformation parameter β is varied from 0.0 to 0.6 for both proton and deuteron, with r_p varied from 0.78 fm to 0.83 fm and r_d from 2.06 fm to 2.11 fm.
- For muonic hydrogen with r_p = 0.80 fm, the 2S-2P energy interval increases from 3.319 meV (β=0) to 6.117 meV (β=0.6).
- For electronic hydrogen with r_p = 0.80 fm, the 2S-2P energy interval increases from 4.9×10⁻⁷ meV (β=0) to 8.9×10⁻⁷ meV (β=0.6).

## Context

This work extends the line of theoretical attempts to resolve the proton radius puzzle first reported by Pohl (2010), which showed a 5.6σ discrepancy between the muonic hydrogen radius (0.84087 fm) and the previously accepted CODATA value (0.8751 fm). The authors note that all prior charge radius measurements assumed uniform spheroidal charge distributions, while earlier work by Buchmann (2001) and Bernstein (2002) had suggested intrinsic deformation of the nucleon. The paper finds that deformation corrections cannot explain the puzzle, consistent with the conclusion that the solution may come from experimental side as suggested by recent electronic hydrogen measurements by Beyer (2017) and Bezginov (2019) that support the smaller radius value.
