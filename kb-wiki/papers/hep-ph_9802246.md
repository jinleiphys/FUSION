# Multiple Scattering Expansion of the Self-Energy at Finite Temperature

> An often used rule that the thermal correction to the self-energy is the thermal phase-space times the forward scattering amplitude from target particles is shown to be the leading term in an exact multiple scattering expansion. Starting from imaginary-time finite-temperature field theory, a rigorous expansion for the retarded self-energy is derived. The relationship to the thermodynamic potential is briefly discussed.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the common rule that the thermal correction to the self-energy equals the thermal phase-space integral of the forward scattering amplitude is only the leading term in an exact multiple scattering expansion, valid when temperature and density are low enough that the expansion can be truncated.

## Method

Starting from the imaginary-time (Matsubara) formulation of finite-temperature field theory, the authors derive diagrammatic rules for calculating arbitrary N-point retarded functions by analytic continuation of Euclidean correlation functions. They then expand the retarded self-energy in powers of the thermal phase-space factor \(\Gamma_\zeta(k)\), relating the coefficients to zero-temperature Feynman and Cutkosky diagrams that correspond to physical scattering amplitudes.

## Key numbers

- The thermal phase-space factor for scalar bosons is \(\Gamma_{\rm B}(k) = n_B(|k^0 -\mu|) \,2\pi\,\delta(k^2 - m^2)\).
- The thermal phase-space factor for spin-1/2 fermions is \(\Gamma_{\rm F}(k) = -n_{\rm F}(|k^0 -\mu|)\, (k^\mu\gamma_\mu + m) \,2\pi\,\delta(k^2 - m^2)\).
- The expansion is controlled when \(T \ll |m_a \pm \mu_a|\) for all particle species \(a\), so that terms are ordered by the relative strengths of \(e^{-(m_a -\mu_a)/T}\) and \(e^{-(m_a +\mu_a)/T}\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier diagrammatic method of Jeon (1995) for calculating spectral densities of two-point functions in scalar theory to general N-point retarded functions and to the self-energy. It provides a rigorous foundation for the approximate formula used by Shuryak, Ashida, and Zahed, and enables systematic higher-order corrections beyond the leading forward-scattering approximation. The results connect finite-temperature field theory to zero-temperature scattering amplitudes, allowing experimental scattering data to be used in thermal calculations.
