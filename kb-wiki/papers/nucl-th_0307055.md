# Oriented collisions for cold synthesis of superheavy nuclei

> The conditions of optimum orientations (lowest barrier and largest interaction radius) for deformed colliding nuclei are introduced in "cold" fusion of superheavy nuclei. Also, the role of (octupole and) hexadecupole deformations is studied. We have used the proximity potential and applied our method to Ca-induced reactions.

## Key claim

The paper introduces the concept of "optimum orientations" for deformed colliding nuclei in cold fusion reactions, defined as the orientations that give the lowest fusion barrier and largest interaction radius. The authors show that the inclusion of higher multipole deformations (octupole and hexadecupole) is not universally favorable for fusion, as it lowers barriers only for some orientations while raising them for others, depending on the signs of the quadrupole deformations.

## Method

The authors extend the quantum mechanical fragmentation theory (QMFT) to include higher multipole deformations (λ=3 and 4) and two orientation angles for each nucleus. They calculate the total interaction potential as the sum of binding energies, a Coulomb potential with multipole expansion, and a proximity potential, then apply this to Ca-induced reactions forming compound systems from Z=110 to Z=116. The fragmentation potentials and mass distribution yields are computed for different target-projectile combinations at fixed orientations, using the stationary Schrödinger equation in the mass asymmetry coordinate.

## Key numbers

- For the compound system $^{286}112^*$, the excitation energy for Ca minima is reduced from ~35 MeV for spherical nuclei to <20 MeV for deformed and oriented collisions.
- The proximity potential uses surface tension coefficient γ = 0.9517[1-1.7826((N-Z)/A)²] MeV fm⁻² and surface thickness b = 0.99 fm.
- The compound nucleus excitation energies for the studied reactions are E* ~30-35 MeV, between cold fusion (10-20 MeV) and hot fusion (40-50 MeV).
- The universal function Φ(s₀) for the proximity potential has two forms: for s₀ ≤ 1.2511 and s₀ ≥ 1.2511.

## Context

This work extends the QMFT approach developed by Gupta and collaborators (1977) for cold fusion valleys, which identified optimum target-projectile combinations based on shell closure effects. It builds on earlier studies of oriented collisions by Greiner (1981) and Malhotra (1985), and addresses the recent suggestion by Nörenberg (1994) regarding prolate-oblate configurations. The method is applied to the neutron-rich $^{48}$Ca beam experiments on actinide targets by Oganessian (1999), and the results show that oriented collisions can reduce compound nucleus excitation energies, potentially allowing "warm" and "hot" fusion reactions to be reached in cold fusion conditions.
