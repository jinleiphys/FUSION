# Feasibility Studies for Single Transverse-Spin Asymmetry Measurements at a Fixed-Target Experiment Using the LHC Proton and Lead Beams (AFTER@LHC)

> The measurement of Single Transverse-Spin Asymmetries, $A_N$, for various quarkonium states and Drell-Yan lepton pairs can shed light on the orbital angular momentum of quarks and gluons, a fundamental ingredient of the proton-spin puzzle. The AFTER@LHC proposal combines a unique kinematic coverage and large luminosities thanks to the Large Hadron Collider beams to deliver precise measurements, complementary to the knowledge provided by collider experiments such as at RHIC. In this paper, we report on sensitivity studies for $J/ψ$, $Υ$ and Drell-Yan $A_N$ done using the performance of LHCb-like or ALICE-like detectors, combined with polarised gaseous hydrogen and helium-3 targets. In particular, such analyses will provide us with new insights and knowledge about transverse-momentum-dependent parton distribution functions for quarks and gluons and on twist-3 collinear matrix elements in the proton and the neutron.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that AFTER@LHC, a fixed-target experiment using LHC proton and lead beams with a polarised gas target, can measure single transverse-spin asymmetries (A_N) for quarkonium states and Drell-Yan lepton pairs with sufficient statistical precision to probe gluon and quark Sivers functions, provided that detector configurations similar to LHCb or ALICE are used and that systematic uncertainties cancel in the asymmetry ratio.

## Method

The authors performed Monte Carlo simulations of p+p collisions at sqrt(s) = 115 GeV using LHCb-like and ALICE-like detector acceptances, with a polarised gaseous hydrogen or helium-3 target. They generated invariant mass distributions of dimuon pairs from J/psi, Upsilon, and Drell-Yan production, subtracted combinatorial background using the like-sign technique, and assumed removal of correlated background from charm and bottom decays via microvertexing. Statistical uncertainties on A_N were computed from the expected signal and background yields for one year of data taking at integrated luminosities of 10 fb^-1 (LHCb-like) or 0.25 fb^-1 (ALICE-like), assuming an effective target polarisation of 80% for hydrogen and 23% for helium-3.

## Key numbers

- J/psi yields expected with LHCb-like detector per LHC year at sqrt(s) = 115 GeV and L_int = 10 fb^-1: 1.69 x 10^7 (2 < y < 3), 1.04 x 10^8 (3 < y < 4), 1.01 x 10^8 (4 < y < 5)
- Upsilon(1S) yields: 4.85 x 10^4 (3 < y < 4), 8.85 x 10^4 (4 < y < 5), 1.37 x 10^5 (3 < y < 5)
- Drell-Yan yields for 4 < M_mumu < 9 GeV/c^2: 4.32 x 10^5 (2 < y < 3), 9.32 x 10^5 (3 < y < 4), 4.98 x 10^5 (4 < y < 5)
- Drell-Yan signal-to-background ratios: 0.74 (2 < y < 3), 0.05 (3 < y < 4), 0.11 (4 < y < 5)
- Double J/psi yield: 780 events per year for 2 < y < 5
- Effective polarisation for p+p^uparrow: P = 80%; for p+^3He^uparrow: P_eff = 23%
- Single muon pT threshold: pT > 1.2 GeV/c for LHCb-like detector; pT > 1 GeV/c for ALICE
- LHCb-like detector acceptance: 2 < eta < 5; ALICE muon spectrometer acceptance: 2.5 < eta < 4 (IP_Z = 0) or 3.2 < eta < 4.2 (IP_Z = -5 m)
- Spin figure of merit for AFTER@LHC with p+p^uparrow: F = 6.4 x 10^32 cm^-2 s^-1

## Context

This work extends the feasibility studies for the AFTER@LHC proposal (Brodsky et al., 2012) by providing quantitative sensitivity projections for single transverse-spin asymmetry measurements using realistic detector simulations. It complements existing measurements from PHENIX at RHIC (Adare et al., 2010), which measured J/psi A_N with limited precision, and COMPASS at CERN, which observed a non-zero asymmetry hinting at non-zero gluon orbital angular momentum (Kurek, 2016; Adolph et al., 2017). The projected precision would enable first measurements of the transverse-momentum dependence of the gluon Sivers function via double J/psi production and constrain twist-3 collinear matrix elements in both protons and neutrons, advancing understanding of the proton spin puzzle beyond current capabilities at RHIC, COMPASS, and planned experiments such as E1039 at Fermilab.
