# An equation-of-state-meter for CBM using PointNet

> A novel method for identifying the nature of QCD transitions in heavy-ion collision experiments is introduced. PointNet based Deep Learning (DL) models are developed to classify the equation of state (EoS) that drives the hydrodynamic evolution of the system created in Au-Au collisions at 10 AGeV. The DL models were trained and evaluated in different hypothetical experimental situations. A decreased performance is observed when more realistic experimental effects (acceptance cuts and decreased resolutions) are taken into account. It is shown that the performance can be improved by combining multiple events to make predictions. The PointNet based models trained on the reconstructed tracks of charged particles from the CBM detector simulation discriminate a crossover transition from a first order phase transition with an accuracy of up to 99.8%. The models were subjected to several tests to evaluate the dependence of its performance on the centrality of the collisions and physical parameters of fluid dynamic simulations. The models are shown to work in a broad range of centralities (b=0-7 fm). However, the performance is found to improve for central collisions (b=0-3 fm). There is a drop in the performance when the model parameters lead to reduced duration of the fluid dynamic evolution or when less fraction of the medium undergoes the transition. These effects are due to the limitations of the underlying physics and the DL models are shown to be superior in its discrimination performance in comparison to conventional mean observables.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a PointNet-based deep learning model can classify whether a heavy-ion collision at 10 AGeV was driven by a crossover or first-order phase transition equation of state, using reconstructed charged-particle tracks from a simulated CBM detector. However, the 99.8% accuracy claimed in the abstract is only achieved for central collisions (impact parameter 0-3 fm) with 40 combined events; for the full centrality range (b=0-7 fm) the accuracy drops to 96.6%, and for peripheral events (b=3-7 fm) it falls to 81.27%.

## Method

The authors generated Au-Au collision events at 10 AGeV using the UrQMD-hybrid model with two different equations of state: a Maxwell-constructed first-order phase transition and a chiral mean-field crossover. The final-state particles were passed through the CBM detector simulation (CbmRoot with GEANT3) to produce realistic reconstructed tracks from the Silicon Tracking System and Micro Vertex Detector. A PointNet architecture with three 1D-convolution layers (128, 256, 512 feature maps), average pooling, and a three-layer fully connected network was trained on point clouds of reconstructed track parameters (x, y, track direction, charge/momentum) to classify the underlying equation of state.

## Key numbers

- Validation accuracy of 99.8% for central collisions (b=0-3 fm) when combining 40 reconstructed-track events (Model-2)
- Validation accuracy of 96.6% for the full centrality range (b=0-7 fm) with 40 combined events (Model-1)
- Validation accuracy of 81.27% for peripheral events (b=3-7 fm) when trained separately with centrality bin information (Model-3)
- Single-event accuracy on reconstructed tracks was only 62.4% for b=0-7 fm
- With ideal final-state particles (no detector effects), 15 combined events achieved 99.7% accuracy
- With acceptance cuts only (2-25 degree polar angle), 20 combined events achieved 99.5% accuracy
- Testing accuracy dropped when freeze-out energy density was increased by 40%, causing about 50% of particles to be emitted before hydrodynamic evolution began (for b=0 fm)

## Context

This work extends prior deep-learning approaches for classifying QCD transitions, specifically the CNN-based studies by Pang (2016) on hydrodynamic output and Du (2019) on hadronic cascade afterburner output, by introducing a PointNet architecture that operates directly on reconstructed detector tracks rather than pre-processed spectra. The study demonstrates for the first time that deep learning can classify equations of state from realistic detector-simulated data including acceptance cuts, finite resolution, and reconstruction inefficiencies, which is a necessary step toward online event analysis at the CBM experiment at FAIR. The results enable future integration of this EoS-meter with other PointNet-based tools such as centrality meters for real-time event selection at CBM's 10 MHz interaction rate.
