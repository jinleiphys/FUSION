# Monte-Carlo Study of Bound States in a Few-Nucleon System - Method of Continued Fractions -

## Key claim
The authors propose a new Monte-Carlo method that combines the method of continued fractions (MCF) with a random walk technique to study bound states, including excited states, of few-nucleon systems. The method avoids the restriction to ground states inherent in imaginary-time projection methods like Green's function Monte-Carlo, but requires a specific weight-counting procedure to handle the "sign problem" arising from destructive interference between MCF wave functions.

## Method
The authors modify the original MCF iteration formula so that higher-order wave functions are generated using only the free Green's function \(G_0(E)\), enabling Monte-Carlo random walk techniques. They develop a new weight-counting method where the weight and sign of each new sampling point are computed by summing contributions from all old points through the free Green's function, implementing a "multiple cancellation" mechanism to handle sign cancellations. The bound state energy is determined by searching for the energy \(E\) that satisfies \(\Omega(E)=0\), where \(\Omega(E)\) is expressed as a continued fraction of matrix elements \(x_i = \langle f|V|F_i \rangle\).

## Key numbers
- One-dimensional model: three bound states at \(-38.7\), \(-10.6\), and \(-2.01\) MeV (exact values), with Monte-Carlo results in good agreement; potential parameters \(V_0 = -69.3\) MeV, \(a = 0.711\) fm\(^{-1}\)
- One-dimensional model: 20,000 sampling points for ground state, 70,000 for first excited state, 100,000 for second excited state
- Four-nucleon (\(^4\)He) bound state with Volkov potential: \(E = -29.88 \pm 0.59\) MeV using 200,000 sampling points
- Volkov potential parameters: \(V_1 = -83.34\) MeV with \(a_1 = 1.60^{-2}\) fm\(^{-2}\), \(V_2 = 144.86\) MeV with \(a_2 = 0.82^{-2}\) fm\(^{-2}\)
- Three iterations of MCF were sufficient for convergence in the four-nucleon calculation

## Context
This work extends the method of continued fractions originally developed by Horáček and Sasakawa for scattering problems to bound state calculations using Monte-Carlo integration. It contrasts with variational Monte-Carlo (VMC) and Green's function Monte-Carlo (GFMC) methods, which are restricted to ground states due to their imaginary-time projection \(e^{-\tau H}\). The authors note that GFMC has been applied to nuclei with \(A \leq 7\) using realistic potentials (Carlson et al., various years), and that excited states have been studied in shell-model calculations with auxiliary-field Monte-Carlo (Koonin et al.) and in one- and two-dimensional GFMC examples (Cheon, 1992). The new method enables systematic study of excited states in few-nucleon systems without prior knowledge of wave function nodes.
