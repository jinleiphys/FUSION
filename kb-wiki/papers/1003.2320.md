# Strange quark matter in explosive astrophysical systems

> Explosive astrophysical systems, such as supernovae or compact star binary mergers, provide conditions where strange quark matter can appear. The high degree of isospin asymmetry and temperatures of several MeV in such systems may cause a transition to the quark phase already around saturation density. Observable signals from the appearance of quark matter can be predicted and studied in astrophysical simulations. As input in such simulations, an equation of state with an integrated quark matter phase transition for a large temperature, density and proton fraction range is required. Additionally, restrictions from heavy ion data and pulsar observation must be considered. In this work we present such an approach. We implement a quark matter phase transition in a hadronic equation of state widely used for astrophysical simulations and discuss its compatibility with heavy ion collisions and pulsar data. Furthermore, we review the recently studied implications of the QCD phase transition during the early post-bounce evolution of core-collapse supernovae and introduce the effects from strong interactions to increase the maximum mass of hybrid stars. In the MIT bag model, together with the strange quark mass and the bag constant, the strong coupling constant $α_s$ provides a parameter to set the beginning and extension of the quark phase and with this the mass and radius of hybrid stars.

# Strange quark matter in explosive astrophysical systems

## Key claim

The paper presents a hybrid equation of state (EoS) incorporating a quark matter phase transition that is compatible with both heavy ion collision data and pulsar mass constraints, but only under the condition that the different proton fractions and dynamical timescales of supernovae versus heavy ion collisions are properly accounted for. The low critical densities for quark matter onset in supernovae (around saturation density) do not contradict the higher critical densities required by heavy ion data because supernova matter has low proton fraction ($Y_p \leq 0.3$) and allows strangeness equilibration via weak interactions, whereas heavy ion collisions have isospin-symmetric matter ($Y_p \sim 0.5$) and timescales too short for strangeness production.

## Method

The authors implement a quark matter phase transition using the MIT bag model for quark matter and the relativistic mean field approach of Shen (1998) for hadronic matter, employing the Gibbs construction for a mixed phase of quarks and hadrons. They apply this hybrid EoS to core-collapse supernova simulations in spherical symmetry using general relativistic radiation hydrodynamics with three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport, testing two bag constant values ($B^{1/4}=165$ MeV and $B^{1/4}=162$ MeV) with zero strong coupling constant, and additionally explore the effect of including first-order strong interaction corrections via $\alpha_s=0.3$ with a reduced bag constant $B^{1/4}=155$ MeV.

## Key numbers

- Bag constant values used: $B^{1/4}=165$ MeV and $B^{1/4}=162$ MeV for the baseline MIT bag model without strong coupling corrections
- Strange quark mass fixed at $m_s=100$ MeV, within limits set by the Particle Data Group
- For $B^{1/4}=165$ MeV and proton fraction $Y_p=0.3$, the onset of strange quark matter occurs around saturation density $n_0$
- For heavy ion collisions with $Y_p \sim 0.5$ and $B^{1/4}=165$ MeV, the critical density for the onset of quark matter is up to $5n_0$ at small temperatures
- The Hulse-Taylor pulsar mass is $1.4414 \pm 0.0002$ solar masses M$_\odot$
- The millisecond pulsar J1903+0327 has a mass of $1.67 \pm 0.01$ M$_\odot$
- Hybrid stars with $B^{1/4}=165$ MeV and $B^{1/4}=162$ MeV (both with $\alpha_s=0$) exceed the Hulse-Taylor constraint of 1.44 M$_\odot$ but have maximum masses below $1.67 \pm 0.01$ M$_\odot$
- With the parameter set $B^{1/4}=155$ MeV and $\alpha_s=0.3$, the hybrid star maximum mass reaches $\sim 1.67$ M$_\odot$

## Context

This work extends the study of Sagert (2009) on quark matter phase transitions in core-collapse supernovae by providing a more detailed analysis of the hybrid EoS and its compatibility with constraints from heavy ion data and pulsar observations. The approach uses the hadronic EoS of Shen (1998) and the Gibbs construction for mixed phases as developed by Glendenning (1992) and Hempel (2009). The paper enables future simulations of supernovae and binary mergers with a consistent quark-hadron EoS, and predicts that a second neutrino burst dominated by anti-neutrinos could be observed by present and future neutrino detectors, providing correlated information about the progenitor mass and the critical density for quark matter onset.
