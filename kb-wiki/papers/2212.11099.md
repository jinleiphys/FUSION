# Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay

> This White Paper, prepared for the Fundamental Symmetries, Neutrons, and Neutrinos Town Meeting related to the 2023 Nuclear Physics Long Range Plan, makes the case for double beta decay as a critical component of the future nuclear physics program. The major experimental collaborations and many theorists have endorsed this white paper.

## Key claim

This White Paper argues that ton-scale neutrinoless double beta decay experiments using multiple isotopes should be the highest priority for the future US nuclear physics program, but this recommendation is conditional on the assumption that such experiments can cover the entire inverted mass ordering region corresponding to an effective Majorana mass greater than approximately 18 meV, and that discovery would only be guaranteed if the neutrino mass spectrum is inverted or the lightest neutrino mass exceeds 50 meV.

## Method

The paper reviews the current status and future plans for neutrinoless double beta decay searches, synthesizing input from major experimental collaborations (CUPID, KamLAND-Zen, LEGEND, Majorana, nEXO, NEXT, SNO+, SuperNEMO, Theia) and theorists. It evaluates three leading ton-scale next-generation experiments using different isotopes and technologies: large bolometer arrays (CUPID), high-resolution germanium detectors (LEGEND), and a large-volume liquid xenon time projection chamber (nEXO), all of which were examined in a 2021 DOE portfolio review. The paper also discusses advanced techniques beyond the ton scale, including high-pressure xenon gas TPCs with barium tagging (NEXT) and hybrid Cherenkov/scintillation detectors (Theia).

## Key numbers

- The effective Majorana mass parameter for the inverted ordering region is m_beta_beta > (18.4 ± 1.3) meV
- Discovery of neutrinoless double beta decay would be possible if the lightest neutrino mass m_lightest > 50 meV, irrespective of mass ordering
- Ton-scale experiments are expected to extend sensitivity to neutrinoless double beta decay half-lives by as much as two orders of magnitude
- Future detectors aim for half-life sensitivities in excess of 10^28 years

## Context

This White Paper was prepared for the Fundamental Symmetries, Neutrons, and Neutrinos Town Meeting related to the 2023 Nuclear Physics Long Range Plan, updating the 2015 Long Range Plan which considered a ton-scale experiment the highest priority for new experiments in nuclear physics. It builds on progress since 2015 including an order of magnitude improvement in half-life sensitivity of existing experiments and advances in theoretical understanding of nuclear matrix elements. The paper extends the work of ongoing experiments (CUORE, KamLAND-Zen, LEGEND-200, SNO+, NEXT-100, SuperNEMO Demonstrator) and addresses R&D challenges previously identified by a 2015 NSAC sub-committee that have now been resolved. The paper enables a coordinated US-led program of discovery science in partnership with international collaborators.
