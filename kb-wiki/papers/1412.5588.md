# The interplay of disk wind and dynamical ejecta in the aftermath of neutron star - black hole mergers

> We explore the evolution of the different ejecta components generated during the merger of a neutron star (NS) and a black hole (BH). Our focus is the interplay between material ejected dynamically during the merger, and the wind launched on a viscous timescale by the remnant accretion disk. These components are expected to contribute to an electromagnetic transient and to produce r-process elements, each with a different signature when considered separately. Here we introduce a two-step approach to investigate their combined evolution, using two- and three-dimensional hydrodynamic simulations. Starting from the output of a merger simulation, we identify each component in the initial condition based on its phase space distribution, and evolve the accretion disk in axisymmetry. The wind blown from this disk is injected into a three-dimensional computational domain where the dynamical ejecta is evolved. We find that the wind can suppress fallback accretion on timescales longer than ~100 ms. Due to self-similar viscous evolution, the disk accretion at late times nevertheless approaches a power-law time dependence $\propto t^{-2.2}$. This can power some late-time GRB engine activity, although the available energy is significantly less than in traditional fallback models. Inclusion of radioactive heating due to the r-process does not significantly affect the fallback accretion rate or the disk wind. We do not find any significant modification to the wind properties at large radius due to interaction with the dynamical ejecta. This is a consequence of the different expansion velocities of the two components.

# The interplay of disk wind and dynamical ejecta in the aftermath of neutron star - black hole mergers

## Key claim
The disk wind launched from the remnant accretion disk can suppress fallback accretion on timescales longer than ~100 ms, but the wind properties at large radius are not significantly modified by interaction with the dynamical ejecta because the two components have different expansion velocities. The authors qualify that this conclusion depends on the specific merger parameters used (a 10 solar mass black hole with a 1.4 solar mass neutron star) and that their two-step modeling approach involves approximations that future studies should refine.

## Method
The authors developed a two-step hydrodynamic simulation approach: first, they evolved the remnant accretion disk in axisymmetry (2D) including shear viscosity and neutrino source terms using the FLASH3.2 code, measuring the disk wind properties at a radius of 800 km. Second, they injected this sampled wind into a three-dimensional (3D) computational domain containing the dynamical ejecta, evolving only with gravity and radioactive heating as source terms. The initial conditions were taken from a Newtonian SPH merger simulation of a 10 solar mass black hole with a 1.4 solar mass neutron star at 139 ms after merger, with components separated based on total specific energy and the ratio of rotational kinetic energy to internal energy.

## Key numbers
- Disk wind mass ejection: approximately 0.04 solar masses (20 percent of initial disk mass) over 10 seconds of evolution
- Unbound wind fraction: between 50 and 60 percent of the ejected material has positive specific energy
- Mean velocity of unbound wind: approximately 0.04c
- Disk accretion at late times follows a power-law time dependence proportional to t^{-2.2}
- Wind suppression of fallback accretion occurs on timescales longer than ~100 ms
- Expansion time at 10^8 cm: approximately 0.1 seconds for disk wind (v_r ~ 0.03c)
- Viscous time at 10^8 cm: approximately 1 second for alpha = 0.03
- Radioactive heating leads to ~15 percent enhancement in unbound mass ejection
- Resolution uncertainty in mass ejection: ~10 percent
- Initial disk mass: 0.2 solar masses
- Tidal tail mass: 0.06 solar masses
- Fallback component mass: 0.02 solar masses
- Wind injection radius (r_cut): 800 km
- Black hole mass: 11.1 solar masses with spin parameter a = 0.8

## Context
This work extends previous studies of neutron star-black hole merger ejecta by examining the interaction between the dynamical ejecta (studied by Bauswein (2013), Kyutoku (2013), and Foucart (2012)) and the disk wind (studied by Metzger (2009), Lee (2009), Fernandez and Metzger (2013), and Just (2014)). The authors build on their own prior work (FKMQ14) on disk outflows and on the radioactive heating parameterization of Korobkin (2012). The results enable more realistic predictions for kilonova transients (Kasen (2013), Barnes and Kasen (2013)) and for late-time gamma-ray burst engine activity, although the available energy from fallback is significantly less than in traditional fallback models proposed by Faber (2006) and Rosswog (2007). The finding that radioactive heating does not significantly affect fallback accretion or the disk wind contradicts earlier suggestions by Metzger (2010) that such heating could suppress fallback.
