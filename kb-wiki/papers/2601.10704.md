# The Effective Theory of Muon-to-Electron Conversion

> We summarize recent work to develop an effective theory of muon-to-electron conversion, based on a complete set of low-energy effective operators that are developed from a systematic expansion in velocities and momenta. The expansion effectively factors rates into sums of particle physics and nuclear physics terms, where the former are expressed as bilinears in the LECs (the low-energy constants of the effective theory) and the latter are the associated nuclear responses. One can view the nuclear responses as ``dials" that can be adjusted -- for example, by selection of targets with specific properties -- in order to isolate the former. We show that an important dial, in the case of Mu2e and COMET, will be inelastic transitions to certain low-energy nuclear states that are resolvable in 27Al. If these transitions are exploited, the experiments have the potential not only to discover charged lepton flavor violation (CLFV), but to determine the operators responsible for the CLFV. We also discuss how such low-energy results can be ``ported" to higher energies through a tower of matched EFTs, so they can be combined with other experimental limits to further constrain CLFV

## Key claim

The paper claims that by exploiting inelastic transitions to low-energy nuclear states in 27Al, the Mu2e and COMET experiments have the potential not only to discover charged lepton flavor violation (CLFV), but also to determine which operators are responsible for the CLFV, provided the transitions are resolvable under a shape analysis.

## Method

The authors developed a nonrelativistic effective theory (NRET) for muon-to-electron conversion, constructing all 16 independent Galilean-invariant single-nucleon operators from available Hermitian operators (intrinsic velocities, spin, and momentum transfer). They embedded these operators in a nucleus using a partial-wave expansion with distorted electron waves, factoring the conversion rate into leptonic response functions (bilinears in low-energy constants, or LECs) and nuclear response functions that act as experimental "dials." The formalism was extended to include inelastic transitions to excited nuclear states, and the expected electron signals for various CLFV scenarios were simulated for Mu2e Run I using only about 6% of the expected total data set.

## Key numbers

* The current best limit on the CLFV branching ratio for muon-to-electron conversion is B(mu- + Ti -> e- + Ti) < 6.1 x 10^{-13} at 90% C.L. from SINDRUM II.
* The current best limit for muon-to-electron conversion on gold is B(mu- + Au -> e- + Au) < 7 x 10^{-13} at 90% C.L. from SINDRUM II.
* Mu2e and COMET are expected to reach branching ratio sensitivities of about 10^{-17}, probing new physics scales in excess of 10^4 TeV.
* For the 27Al target, the dimensionless weak-scale coupling limit for the isoscalar charge operator (i=1,11, tau=0) at a branching ratio of 10^{-17} is |c_i^tau| = 4.0 x 10^{-10}, corresponding to an energy scale Lambda_i^tau = 1.2 x 10^4 TeV.
* For the 27Al target, the dimensionless weak-scale coupling limit for the isoscalar transverse spin-velocity operator (i=3,15, tau=0) at a branching ratio of 10^{-17} is |c_i^tau| = 1.6 x 10^{-8}, corresponding to an energy scale Lambda_i^tau = 1.9 x 10^3 TeV.
* The three excited states of 27Al of interest are at 0.844, 1.015, and 2.212 MeV; the electron background from radiative muon capture does not contribute below 3.62 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the effective theory formalism previously developed for dark matter direct detection (Fitzpatrick 2012) to muon-to-electron conversion, completing a tower of matched effective field theories that connects low-energy experimental results to higher-energy BSM theories. The formalism generalizes earlier work on coherent muon-to-electron conversion (Kitano 2002) that focused only on the scalar, isoscalar operator, by including all 16 NRET operators and six nuclear response functions for elastic conversion, plus additional operators and responses for inelastic transitions. This enables experiments like Mu2e and COMET to not only set limits but potentially identify the CLFV mechanism, and allows results to be "ported" to higher energies for combination with other experimental limits such as those from MEG II and Mu3e.
