# From Quark Gluon Plasma to a Perfect Fluid of Quarks and Beyond

> With high energy heavy ion collisions one tries to create a new forms of matter that is similar to the one present at the birth of our Universe. Recent development on flow pattern, initial energy-density and freeze-out temperature shows that most likely this new form of matter is in a deconfined state, has colored degrees of freedom and is more fluid-like than gas-like. In present paper we calculate estimations on the physical properties of this new-old matter.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the matter created in relativistic Au+Au collisions at RHIC behaves as a nearly perfect fluid of deconfined quarks and gluons, but this conclusion is hedged by the fact that the perfect fluid description is validated only for soft particle production up to transverse momenta of about 1–1.5 GeV and for pseudorapidities up to η_beam − 0.5.

## Method

The authors apply exact solutions of relativistic perfect fluid hydrodynamics (the Buda-Lund model) to fit experimental data on single-particle spectra, elliptic flow, and HBT radii from RHIC Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 130 and 200 GeV. They extract the acceleration parameter λ from measured rapidity distributions to compute a correction to the Bjorken energy-density estimate, and they fit the Buda-Lund model to data from PHOBOS, PHENIX, and STAR to test universal scaling of elliptic flow.

## Key numbers

- The correction factor to the Bjorken energy-density estimate for RHIC data from BRAHMS can be as large as ε/ε_Bj ≈ 2.2.
- The lattice QCD critical temperature for the deconfinement transition is T_c = 175 ± 2 MeV (Fodor and Katz, 2006).
- The universal scaling of elliptic flow v_2 is described by the function I_1(w)/I_0(w) and is validated for Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 130 and 200 GeV.
- The perfect fluid description holds for soft particle production up to transverse momenta of about 1–1.5 GeV and pseudorapidities up to η_beam − 0.5.

## Context

This work extends the Bjorken (1983) estimate of initial energy density by incorporating acceleration effects from exact relativistic hydrodynamic solutions (Csörgő et al., 2006). It provides quantitative evidence for the perfect fluid picture of soft particle production at RHIC, consistent with earlier findings of strong collective flow and jet quenching (Adler et al., 2003; Adcox et al., 2004). The results enable future exploration of the full QCD phase diagram by comparing higher-energy data with lower-energy measurements from SPS, where central temperatures were found below the critical value (Ster et al., 1999; Csörgő et al., 1999).
