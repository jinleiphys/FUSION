# Cold quantum gases: coherent quantum phenomena from Bose-Einstein condensation to BCS pairing of fermions

> Studies of trapped quantum gases of bosons and of fermions have opened up a new range of many-body problems, having a strong overlap with nuclear and neutron star physics. Topics discussed here include: the Bose yrast problem -- how many-particle Bose systems carry extreme amounts of angular momentum; the infrared divergent structure of the transition to Bose condensation in a weakly interacting system; and the physics of extremely strongly interacting Bose and Fermi systems, in the scale-free regime where the two body s-wave scattering lengths are large compared with the interparticle spacing. Such a regime is realized experimentally through use of atomic Feshbach resonances. Finally we discuss creation of BCS-paired states in trapped Fermi gases.

# Cold quantum gases: coherent quantum phenomena from Bose-Einstein condensation to BCS pairing of fermions

## Key claim

The paper argues that trapped ultracold atomic gases provide a controllable laboratory for studying many-body quantum phenomena that have direct parallels in nuclear and neutron star physics, including Bose-Einstein condensation, vortex formation, and BCS pairing. The key hedge is that while the systems exhibit strong overlap with nuclear physics, the atomic systems allow parameter variation (via Feshbach resonances) over ranges inconceivable in nuclear systems, making them complementary rather than identical analogues.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical and experimental developments in ultracold trapped quantum gases, covering Bose-Einstein condensates in rapidly rotating traps, the infrared-divergent structure of the BEC transition temperature in weakly interacting systems, and the physics of strongly interacting Bose and Fermi systems near Feshbach resonances. The analysis uses mean-field Gross-Pitaevskii theory for condensates, finite-temperature field theory and renormalization group methods for the transition temperature problem, and fixed-node Monte Carlo and variational calculations for the unitary regime.

## Key numbers

- Typical atomic clouds contain ~10^6 atoms with density ~10^14/cm^3, cooled to temperatures ~10^-8 K
- For 87Rb, half-life T1/2 = 4.75 × 10^10 years; for 40K, T1/2 = 1.3 × 10^9 years
- Vortex core radius in weakly interacting condensates is ξ0 = 1/√(8π n a_s), typically ~0.2 μm
- Cores begin to touch at Ω_c1 ~ 8π^2 n a ħ/m ~ 10^3 - 10^5 rad/sec
- Current experiments have reached Ω/ω_⊥ ≈ 0.995 in harmonic traps
- The shift in BEC transition temperature is ΔT_c/T_c^0 = c a_s n^(1/3), with c ranging from 0.34 ± 0.06 to 2.33 ± 0.25 across different calculations
- The next-to-leading order term includes a logarithmic correction: d (a_s n^(1/3))^2 ln(a_s n^(1/3)) with d ≈ 19.7518
- In the unitary regime, the ground state energy per particle for fermions is E/N = (3/5) E_f^0 (1+β), with β = -0.56 from fixed-node Monte Carlo (Carlson et al.) and β = -0.33 from variational calculations (Heiselberg)
- For 40K experiments at JILA, initial temperature was T/T_f ~ 0.08

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework connecting ultracold atomic physics to nuclear and neutron star physics, building on earlier work by Leggett (2001) on the Gross-Pitaevskii equation and by Eagles (1969), Nozieres and Schmitt-Rink (1985), and others on the BEC-BCS crossover. The paper discusses how Feshbach resonances enable experimental access to the unitary regime where the scattering length exceeds the interparticle spacing, a regime relevant to understanding strongly coupled quark-gluon plasmas produced at RHIC. The work enables future studies of vortex lattice melting, fractional quantum Hall-like states in rotating condensates, and direct detection of superfluidity in paired Fermi gases through vortex production or the Josephson effect.
