# Probing Earth-Bound Dark Matter with Nuclear Reactors

> Strongly-interacting dark matter can be accumulated in large quantities inside the Earth, and for dark matter particles in a few GeV mass range, it can exist in large quantities near the Earth's surface. We investigate the constraints imposed on such dark matter properties by its upscattering by fast neutrons in nuclear reactors with subsequent scattering in nearby well-shielded dark matter detectors, schemes which are already used for searches of the coherent reactor neutrino scattering. We find that the existing experiments cover new parameter space on the spin-dependent interaction between dark matter and the nucleon. Similar experiments performed with research reactors, and lesser amount of shielding, may provide additional sensitivity to strongly-interacting dark matter.

# Probing Earth-Bound Dark Matter with Nuclear Reactors

## Key claim
Existing CEνNS experiments at commercial nuclear reactors can probe new parameter space for spin-dependent dark matter-nucleon interactions, but only for dark matter that constitutes a subdominant fraction of the total dark matter density (fχ ≤ 1) and has sufficiently large cross sections to be captured and thermalized inside the Earth. The sensitivity is limited by shielding at commercial reactors, while research reactors with less shielding could provide additional reach despite their lower power.

## Method
The authors calculate the accumulation of strongly-interacting dark matter inside the Earth by solving the Boltzmann equation for capture and evaporation, obtaining the dark matter number density at the Earth's surface. They then model the upscattering of these thermalized dark matter particles by fast neutrons from nuclear fission (using the Watt spectrum for 235U) in a water moderator, treating the reactor as a neutron beam dump. The accelerated dark matter flux is propagated through concrete shielding using Monte Carlo simulations that account for multiple scatterings, and the event rate is computed for a germanium detector (modeled after the CONUS experiment) via nuclear recoils with a quenching factor of 0.18.

## Key numbers
- Thermal power of the Brokdorf reactor (CONUS experiment): P = 3.9 GW
- Distance from reactor core to detector: l_det = 17.1 m
- Shielding thickness: l_s = 5 m of concrete
- CONUS detector exposure: 248.7 kg×day, corresponding to n_t V_det t_det = 1.75×10^32 sec (SI case) and 1.4×10^31 sec (SD case)
- Neutron production rate: dN_n/dt ≈ 7.8×10^19 sec^-1 × (P/1 GW)
- Energy threshold for analysis: 0.3 keV_ee to 1 keV_ee, corresponding to nuclear recoil energies 1.7 keV to 5.6 keV
- Maximum event limit: 85 events in the energy region of interest
- Dark matter mass range where surface density peaks: m_χ ∼ 2 GeV
- Evaporation becomes negligible for m_χ ≳ 1 GeV; surface density drops for m_χ ≳ 3 GeV due to sinking to Earth's center
- Quenching factor: k = 0.18 (assumed energy-independent)

## Context
This work extends the idea of using laboratory sources to accelerate thermalized dark matter, previously explored for underground accelerators by Pospelov (2020) and McKeen (2022). It directly applies the experimental infrastructure already built for coherent reactor neutrino scattering (CEνNS) detection, specifically the CONUS experiment. The results are compared against existing direct detection constraints from CRESST-III, CRESST (surface), Darkside-50, EDELWEISS (surface), the XQC rocket experiment, and the balloon-based RRS experiment. The paper enables future searches using research reactors with less shielding, which could probe larger cross sections where commercial reactor experiments lose sensitivity due to dark matter attenuation in shielding.
