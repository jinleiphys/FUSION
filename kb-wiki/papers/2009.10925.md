# Screening Effects on Electron Capture Rates and Type Ia Supernova Nucleosynthesis

> Type Ia supernovae (SNe Ia) are believed to be a thermonuclear explosion of a white dwarf, but the mass of their progenitors is still an open problem. In near-Chandrasekhar-mass (near-M_Ch) models of SNe Ia, the central density reaches >10^9 g cm^{-3}. The electron chemical potential becomes higher than the Q-values of electron capture (EC) transitions between fp-shell nuclei, so a portion of the available electrons is captured by iron group elements and thus neutron-rich isotopes are formed. Since EC reaction rates are sensitive to the density, the degree of neutronization is a key to distinguish near- and sub-M_Ch models. In order to compare observations and theoretical models, an accurate treatment of EC reactions is necessary. In previous theoretical works, however, effects of electron screening on ECs are ignored. Screening lowers EC rates and thus leads to a higher electron fraction. We implement electron screening on ECs to calculate explosive SN Ia nucleosynthesis in a near-M_Ch single degenerate model. It is found that some of neutron-rich nuclear abundances, namely those of 46,48Ca, 50Ti, 54Cr, 58Fe, 64Ni and 67,70Zn, decrease when screening effects on ECs are considered. Of these, 50Ti, 54Cr and 58Fe are particularly interesting because a significant portion of the solar abundance of these nuclei is presumed to originate from SNe Ia. We conclude that implementing the screening effect on ECs in modern SN Ia models is desirable to precisely calculate abundances of neutron-rich nuclides.

## Key claim

Electron screening on electron capture (EC) reactions in near-Chandrasekhar-mass Type Ia supernova models lowers EC rates and leads to a higher electron fraction, which reduces the abundances of certain neutron-rich nuclei; the effect is most significant for 50Ti, 54Cr, and 58Fe, for which a significant portion of the solar abundance is presumed to originate from SNe Ia.

## Method

The authors calculated EC rates for fp-shell nuclei using the GXPF1J shell-model Hamiltonian, incorporating electron screening effects via the relativistic random phase approximation dielectric function and ion screening via the Coulomb chemical potential. They then performed post-process nuclear reaction network calculations using the one-dimensional W7 deflagration model and WDD2 delayed-detonation model, with screened EC rates applied to fp-shell nuclei and thermonuclear reaction screening based on the standard approach of Wallace, Woosley, and Weaver (1982).

## Key numbers

- Electron fraction at 10 s in W7: Y_e = 0.45747 with EC screening versus Y_e = 0.45682 without EC screening
- Electron fraction at 10 s in WDD2: Y_e = 0.45031 with EC screening versus Y_e = 0.44967 without EC screening
- EC rates for 56Ni reduced by about 20-40% with screening effects compared to without
- Abundances of 48Ca, 50Ti, 54Cr, 58Fe, 64Ni, and 70Zn are 30-50% smaller in W7 when EC screening is included
- Abundances of 46Ca, 48Ca, 50Ti, 54Cr, 58Fe, 64Ni, 67Zn, and 70Zn are 10-30% smaller in WDD2 when EC screening is included
- SN Ia contribution to solar 54Cr: 8.5% (W7 with EC screening) versus 12% (W7 without EC screening)
- SN Ia contribution to solar 58Fe: 5.7% (W7 with EC screening) versus 7.6% (W7 without EC screening)
- SN Ia contribution to solar 50Ti: 45% (WDD2 with EC screening) versus 51% (WDD2 without EC screening)
- SN Ia contribution to solar 54Cr: 150% (WDD2 with EC screening) versus 165% (WDD2 without EC screening)
- SN Ia contribution to solar 58Fe: 54% (WDD2 with EC screening) versus 58% (WDD2 without EC screening)
- Effect on nickel and manganese elemental abundances in SN remnants: less than 0.4% for nickel and less than 0.5% for manganese

## Context

This work extends previous SN Ia nucleosynthesis calculations by Iwamoto et al. (1999), Brachwitz et al. (2000), and others that used bare EC rates without screening, and is consistent with earlier studies by Brachwitz et al. (2000) and Boos et al. (2019) that noted strong sensitivity of neutron-rich nuclei to EC rates. The results enable more precise comparison between SN Ia models and observed solar abundance patterns of titanium, chromium, and iron, and motivate implementation of screened EC rates in modern multi-dimensional SN Ia models such as those by Maeda et al. (2010), Jordan et al. (2012), and Seitenzahl et al. (2013).
