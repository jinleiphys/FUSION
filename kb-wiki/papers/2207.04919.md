# Nuclear fission and fusion in a random-walk model

> This dissertation deals with theoretical descriptions of nuclear fission and synthesis of superheavy elements via fusion. The associated shape evolutions are treated using a random-walk approach where both the potential energy and the nuclear level density influence the dynamics. The work in this thesis extends the random-walk model by, in addition to the previous description of fragment mass yields, also simulating how much kinetic energy the fission-fragments obtain and the number of neutrons they emit, as well as how these two quantities are correlated. The thesis also presents studies of how different ways of fissioning, called fission modes, are present in different nuclei and how the presence of these modes depends on the energy of the system. The model is furthermore applied to the description of the shape evolution in fusion for production of superheavy elements.

## Key claim

The dissertation extends a random-walk model for nuclear shape evolution to simulate not only fission-fragment mass yields but also the kinetic energy of fission fragments, the number of neutrons they emit, and the correlation between these two quantities, with the dynamics influenced by both potential energy and nuclear level density.

## Method

The shape evolution of fissioning and fusing nuclei is treated as a Brownian random-walk process on a multi-dimensional potential-energy surface, where the transition probabilities depend on both the potential energy and the microscopic nuclear level density. The model is applied to calculate fission-fragment mass distributions, total kinetic energies, and neutron multiplicities for various actinide and superheavy nuclei, and is also used to study the competition between fusion and quasifission in superheavy element synthesis.

## Key numbers

* For 235U(n,f), fission-fragment neutron multiplicities were calculated using an energy partition based on shape-dependent microscopic level densities (Paper I).
* Fission-fragment mass and total-kinetic-energy distributions were calculated for the fermium isotopes 256Fm, 258Fm, and 260Fm at low excitation energies, showing a transition from asymmetric fission in 256Fm to symmetric fission in 258Fm with a correlated large change in total kinetic energy (Paper III).
* Fission-fragment mass yields and average total kinetic energies were calculated for even-even nuclei in the region 74 ≤ Z ≤ 126 and 92 ≤ N ≤ 230, revealing an island of asymmetric fission in the superheavy region where the heavy fragment is close to 208Pb (Paper IV).
* Neutron multiplicities from fission fragments with specified mass numbers for events having a specified total fragment kinetic energy were calculated for 235U(n,f); with increasing neutron energy a superlong fission mode becomes increasingly prominent (Paper V).
* The persistence of the symmetric super-short fission mode versus both particle number and excitation energy was studied for even fermium isotopes 254-268Fm (Paper VI).

## Context

This work extends the random-walk (Brownian shape-motion) model previously used for fission-fragment mass yields by adding predictions for kinetic energy and neutron emission, enabling direct comparison with experimental data on correlations between these observables. The studies of fission modes in fermium isotopes (Papers III and VI) build on known experimental observations of a transition from asymmetric to symmetric fission, while the survey of superheavy nuclei (Paper IV) predicts new asymmetric fission patterns analogous to the standard fission mode around 132Sn. The fusion-quasifission study (Paper VII) applies the same random-walk framework to the production of superheavy elements, a field where experimental synthesis has reached element 118 (oganesson, discovered by Yuri Oganessian and collaborators).
