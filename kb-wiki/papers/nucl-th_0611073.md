# Bound Electron Screening Corrections to Reactions in Hydrogen Burning Processes

> How important would be a precise assessment of the electron screening effect, on determining the bare astrophysical $S$-factor ($S_b(E)$) from experimental data? We compare the $S_b(E)$ obtained using different screening potentials, (1) in the adiabatic limit, (2) without screening corrections, and (3) larger than the adiabatic screening potential in the PP-chain reactions. We employ two kinds of fitting procedures: the first is by the conventional polynomial expression and the second includes explicitly the contribution of the nuclear interaction and based on a statistical model. Comparing bare $S$-factors that are obtained by using different screening potentials, all $S_b(E)$ are found to be in accord within the standard errors for most of reactions investigated, as long as the same fitting procedure is employed. $S_b(E)$ is, practically, insensitive to the magnitude of the screening potential.

# Bound Electron Screening Corrections to Reactions in Hydrogen Burning Processes

## Key claim

The bare astrophysical S-factor $S_b(E)$ is practically insensitive to the magnitude of the screening potential used to correct experimental data, as long as the same fitting procedure is employed; all $S_b(E)$ values obtained using different screening potentials (adiabatic limit, no screening, or larger than adiabatic) agree within standard errors for most PP-chain reactions investigated.

## Method

The authors compared bare S-factors extracted from experimental data using two fitting procedures: (1) a conventional polynomial expansion of the S-factor, and (2) an expression based on a statistical compound-nucleus model that explicitly includes the nuclear interaction via penetration factors and partial-wave contributions. For each reaction, they applied three different screening potentials, the adiabatic limit, zero screening, and a potential larger than the adiabatic limit, and compared the resulting $S_b(0)$ values.

## Key numbers

- $^3$He($^3$He,2$p$)$^4$He: $S_b(0) = 5.23 \pm 0.06$ MeVb (adiabatic limit, $U_e = 246.8$ eV, quadratic polynomial), compared to $S_b(0) = 5.56 \pm 0.07$ MeVb (no screening) and $S_b(0) = 5.18$ MeVb (NACRE with $U_e = 330$ eV)
- $^3$He($^3$He,2$p$)$^4$He: fitting $U_e$ as a free parameter gave $U_e = 371 \pm 46$ eV and $S_b(0) = 5.06 \pm 0.09$ MeVb (polynomial fit)
- $^3$He($^3$He,2$p$)$^4$He: using the statistical model fit with $U_e = 246.8$ eV gave $S_b(0) = 5.4$ MeVb; with $U_e = 0$ gave $S_b(0) = 6.5$ MeVb; with $U_e$ free gave $U_e = 299 \pm 102$ eV and $S_b(0) = 5.2$ MeVb
- $^7$Li($p,\alpha$)$^4$He: $S_b(0) = 0.0620 \pm 0.0006$ MeVb (adiabatic limit, $U_e = 175$ eV, cubic polynomial), compared to $S_b(0) = 0.0673 \pm 0.0008$ MeVb (no screening) and $S_b(0) = 0.0593$ MeVb (NACRE with $U_e = 300$ eV)
- $^7$Li($p,\alpha$)$^4$He: fitting $U_e$ as a free parameter gave $U_e = 195 \pm 28$ eV and $S_b(0) = 0.061 \pm 0.001$ MeVb (polynomial fit)
- $^7$Li($p,\alpha$)$^4$He: using the statistical model fit with $U_e$ free gave $U_e = 495 \pm 41$ eV and $S_b(0) = 0.031$ MeVb
- Minimum energies measured: D($p,\gamma$)$^3$He at 2.52 keV; $^3$He($^3$He,2$p$)$^4$He at 20.76 keV; $^7$Li($p,\alpha$)$^4$He at 12.7 keV; $^3$He($\alpha$,$\gamma$)$^7$Be at 93 keV (prompt-$\gamma$) and 127 keV (activation); $^7$Be($p,\gamma$)$^8$B at 115.6 keV

## Context

This work systematically examines the sensitivity of bare S-factor extraction to the choice of electron screening potential across all PP-chain reactions, extending prior studies that focused on individual reactions such as $^3$He($^3$He,2$p$)$^4$He by Junker et al. (1998) and $^7$Li($p,\alpha$)$^4$He by Engstler et al. (1992). The authors compare their results with the NACRE compilation (Angulo et al., 1999), which used screening potentials larger than the adiabatic limit, and with R-matrix analyses by Descouvemont et al. The finding that $S_b(0)$ is insensitive to the screening potential magnitude enables simpler treatment of electron screening in extrapolations of low-energy nuclear reaction data for stellar nucleosynthesis, though the authors caution that polynomial fits lack physical meaning and propose the statistical model as a more physically motivated alternative.
