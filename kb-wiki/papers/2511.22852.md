# NuclearConfectionery: Multi-stage Simulation Framework for Modeling Relativistic Heavy-ion Collisions

> We present the NuclearConfectionery, a modular framework for simulating the full dynamical evolution of relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Its core hydrodynamic module, CCAKE 2.0, represents a major advance over previous SPH-based relativistic hydrodynamic codes. CCAKE 2.0 simultaneously evolves energy-momentum and multiple conserved charges (B, S, Q) with a four-dimensional equation of state, and can be run in either Cartesian or hyperbolic coordinates, enabling consistent simulations from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan to LHC energies. We have implemented a particlization module that supports global BSQ charge conservation on the freeze-out surface; the resulting hadron ensemble is then propagated through a hadronic transport afterburner. A source term is included in the equations of motion to couple jets to the fluid, allowing simultaneous bulk and hard-probe evolution or, alternatively, for stopped baryons at low beam energies. The framework offers flexible choices of equations of motion (Israel-Stewart, DNMR, ADNH) and transport coefficients, along with GPU-ready performance via Kokkos/Cabana, offline equation of state inversion for 4D tables, and containerized portability. We validate the code with semi-analytical benchmarks (including BSQ Gubser and Landau-Khalatnikov solutions) and extensive convergence studies. The NuclearConfectionery provides a user-friendly, high-performance, open-source tool for event-by-event simulations across collision energies, offering flexibility to study QCD matter at both vanishing and finite densities.

## Key claim

The NuclearConfectionery framework, with its core hydrodynamic module CCAKE 2.0, provides a modular, GPU-ready, open-source tool for simulating relativistic heavy-ion collisions across the full range of beam energies from RHIC Beam Energy Scan to LHC, but the abstract's claim of enabling "consistent simulations" is qualified by the need to choose between Cartesian coordinates (for low energies) and hyperbolic coordinates (for high energies), and by the fact that the code has been validated only against semi-analytical benchmarks (BSQ Gubser and Landau-Khalatnikov solutions) and convergence studies, not against full experimental data.

## Method

The framework uses Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) to solve the relativistic viscous hydrodynamic equations of motion, evolving energy-momentum and three conserved charges (baryon number B, strangeness S, electric charge Q) simultaneously with a four-dimensional equation of state. The hydrodynamic evolution is coupled to a particlization module that enforces global BSQ charge conservation on the freeze-out surface via a Cooper-Frye sampler, after which the hadron ensemble is propagated through the SMASH hadronic transport afterburner. A source term in the equations of motion allows coupling of jets to the fluid for high-energy collisions or dynamical initialization for stopped baryons at low beam energies.

## Key numbers

* Total runtime for a single central Pb+Pb event at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV is 5.62 hours, with (2+1)D hydrodynamics taking 0.3 hours and hadronic transport taking 5.3 hours.
* Total runtime for a single central Au+Au event at 200 GeV is 22.25 hours, with (3+1)D hydrodynamics taking 19 hours using an online equation of state.
* Total runtime for a single central Au+Au event at 19.6 GeV with offline equation of state inversion and Delta eta = 0.3 is 2 hours, with (3+1)D hydrodynamics taking 1.8 hours.
* Typical (3+1)D SPH simulations require approximately 1-48 GB of RAM depending on resolution, centrality, and neighbor count.
* The QCD crossover temperature used as reference is approximately T_had ~ 155 MeV, from lattice QCD calculations by Aoki (2006) and Borsanyi (2010, 2013).

## Context

This work extends the SPH-based relativistic hydrodynamic approach from the earlier vUSPhydro code by Noronha-Hostler (2013, 2014) and CCAKE 1.0 by Plumberg (2024), which introduced BSQ conserved charges with a 4D equation of state. The framework contrasts with grid-based codes such as MUSIC by Denicol (2018), vHLLE by Karpenko (2013), VISHNU by Du (2019), and CLVisc by Wu (2021), which have dominated the field but lack the Lagrangian advantages of SPH for handling expanding dilute media and exact local charge conservation. The framework enables event-by-event simulations across collision energies from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program by STAR (2020, 2025) to LHC energies, allowing studies of QCD matter at both vanishing and finite baryon densities, including the search for a critical endpoint predicted by Stephanov (1998) and a first-order phase transition line at finite n_B.
