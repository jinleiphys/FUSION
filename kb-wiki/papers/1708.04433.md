# The symmetry energy in nucleon and quark matter

> The symmetry energy characterizes the isospin dependent part of the equation of state of isospin asymmetric strong interaction matter and it plays a critical role in many issues of nuclear physics and astrophysics. In this talk, we briefly review the current status on the determination of the symmetry energy in nucleon (nuclear) and quark matter. For nuclear matter, while the subsaturation density behaviors of the symmetry energy are relatively well-determined and significant progress has been made on the symmetry energy around saturation density, the determination of the suprasaturation density behaviors of the symmetry energy remains a big challenge. For quark matter, which is expected to appear in dense matter at high baryon densities, we briefly review the recent work about the effects of quark matter symmetry energy on the properties of quark stars and the implication of possible existence of heavy quark stars on quark matter symmetry energy. The results indicate that the $u$ and $d$ quarks could feel very different interactions in isospin asymmetric quark matter, which may have important implications on the isospin effects of partonic dynamics in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim
The symmetry energy in nuclear matter around saturation density is constrained to \(E_{\text{sym}}(\rho_0) = 32.5 \pm 2.5\) MeV and \(L = 55 \pm 25\) MeV, but these values come from a consensus of many constraints that are not all mutually overlapping; the supra-saturation density behavior remains largely controversial and model-dependent.

## Method
The paper reviews constraints on the nuclear matter symmetry energy from terrestrial experiments (heavy-ion collisions, nuclear structure measurements such as neutron skin thickness, electric dipole polarizability, and isobaric analog states) and astrophysical observations (neutron star mass-radius data). For quark matter, the confined-isospin-density-dependent-mass (CIDDM) model is used to relate the quark matter symmetry energy to the maximum mass of quark stars, with parameters tuned to reproduce the masses of heavy pulsars.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear matter symmetry energy at saturation density: \(E_{\text{sym}}(\rho_0) = 32.5 \pm 2.5\) MeV and \(L = 55 \pm 25\) MeV from a consensus of over 30 constraints.
- A more precise consensus from six experimental constraints gives \(E_{\text{sym}}(\rho_0) = 31.5 \pm 1.0\) MeV and \(L = 55 \pm 11\) MeV.
- For supra-saturation densities, the ASY-EOS experiment yields a symmetry energy potential proportional to \((\rho/\rho_0)^\gamma\) with \(\gamma = 0.72 \pm 0.19\).
- In the CIDDM model, to obtain a quark star mass larger than \(1.93 M_\odot\), the minimum isovector parameter is \(D_I = 70\) MeV\(\cdot\)fm\(^{3\alpha}\) (with \(\alpha=0.7\), \(\beta=0.1\) fm\(^3\), \(D=24.181\) MeV\(\cdot\)fm\(^{-3z}\), \(z=1.8\)).
- The DI-70 parameter set predicts a quark matter symmetry energy at least twice that of a free quark gas (with \(m_{u0}=m_{d0}=5.5\) MeV).

## Context
This review extends the work of Lattimer and Steiner (2014) on nuclear matter symmetry energy constraints and the CIDDM model of Chu et al. (2014) for quark matter. It synthesizes constraints from heavy-ion collision analyses (e.g., Tsang et al. 2009, Russotto et al. 2011, 2016) and nuclear structure studies (e.g., Tamii et al. 2011 on dipole polarizability, Danielewicz and Lee 2014 on isobaric analog states). The results enable future work to refine the supra-saturation density behavior of nuclear matter symmetry energy and to test whether heavy pulsars such as PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0348+0432 can be quark stars, which would impose strong constraints on quark matter interactions.
