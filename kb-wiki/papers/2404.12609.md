# Nuclear charge radius predictions by kernel ridge regression with odd-even effects

> The extended kernel ridge regression (EKRR) method with odd-even effects was adopted to improve the description of the nuclear charge radius using five commonly used nuclear models. These are: (i) the isospin dependent $A^{1/3}$ formula, (ii) relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory, (iii) Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) model HFB25, (iv) the Weizsäcker-Skyrme (WS) model WS$^\ast$, and (v) HFB25$^\ast$ model. In the last two models, the charge radii were calculated using a five-parameter formula with the nuclear shell corrections and deformations obtained from the WS and HFB25 models, respectively. For each model, the resultant root-mean-square deviation for the 1014 nuclei with proton number $Z \geq 8$ can be significantly reduced to 0.009-0.013~fm after considering the modification with the EKRR method. The best among them was the RCHB model, with a root-mean-square deviation of 0.0092~fm. The extrapolation abilities of the KRR and EKRR methods for the neutron-rich region were examined and it was found that after considering the odd-even effects, the extrapolation power was improved compared with that of the original KRR method. The strong odd-even staggering of nuclear charge radii of Ca and Cu isotopes and the abrupt kinks across the neutron $N=126$ and 82 shell closures were also calculated and could be reproduced quite well by calculations using the EKRR method.

## Key claim

The extended kernel ridge regression (EKRR) method, which incorporates odd-even effects through a remodulated kernel function, can reduce the root-mean-square deviation of nuclear charge radius predictions from five commonly used nuclear models to 0.009-0.013 fm for 1014 nuclei with proton number Z >= 8, with the best result of 0.0092 fm achieved for the relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) model; however, the extrapolation power of the EKRR method is limited to extrapolation distances of less than 3 for models that already have small RMS deviations.

## Method

The EKRR method was trained on the residuals between experimental charge radii and predictions from five nuclear models: the isospin-dependent A^(1/3) formula, RCHB theory, the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov model HFB25, the Weizsacker-Skyrme model WS*, and the HFB25* model (the last two using a five-parameter formula with shell corrections and deformations from the WS and HFB25 models). The kernel function was extended by adding an odd-even kernel term K_oe that enhances correlations between nuclei with the same number parity of protons and neutrons, and the hyperparameters (sigma, lambda, sigma_oe, lambda_oe) were optimized using leave-one-out cross-validation on 1014 experimental data points.

## Key numbers

- RMS deviation for the RCHB model after EKRR correction: 0.0092 fm (best among the five models)
- RMS deviation range for all five models after EKRR correction: 0.009-0.013 fm (for 1014 nuclei with Z >= 8)
- RMS deviation for the A^(1/3) formula after EKRR correction: 0.0100 fm
- RMS deviation for the WS* model after EKRR correction: 0.0096 fm
- RMS deviation for the HFB25* model after EKRR correction: 0.0120 fm
- RMS deviation for the HFB25 model after EKRR correction: 0.0130 fm

- RMS deviation for the HFB25 model after KRR correction: 0.0177 fm
- RMS deviation for the WS* model after KRR correction: 0.0155 fm
- RMS deviation for the HFB25* model after KRR correction: 0.0182 fm

## Context

This work extends the kernel ridge regression approach previously applied to nuclear charge radii by Ma et al. (2022) and the EKRR method developed for nuclear masses by Wu et al. (2021). The results improve upon other machine learning approaches for charge radii, including artificial neural networks (0.028 fm RMS deviation, Wu et al. 2020), Bayesian neural networks (0.014 fm, Dong et al. 2023), and radial basis function methods (0.017 fm, Li et al. 2023). The method enables more accurate reproduction of odd-even staggering in Ca and Cu isotopes and kinks at neutron shell closures N=126 and N=82, which are challenging for traditional nuclear models.
