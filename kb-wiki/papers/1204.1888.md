# Equation-of-state dependence of the gravitational-wave signal from the ring-down phase of neutron-star mergers

> Neutron-star (NS) merger simulations are conducted for 38 representative microphysical descriptions of high-density matter in order to explore the equation-of-state dependence of the postmerger ring-down phase. The formation of a deformed, oscillating, differentially rotating very massive NS is the typical outcome of the coalescence of two stars with 1.35 $M_{\odot}$ for most candidate EoSs. The oscillations of this object imprint a pronounced peak in the gravitational-wave (GW) spectra, which is used to characterize the emission for a given model. The peak frequency of this postmerger GW signal correlates very well with the radii of nonrotating NSs, and thus allows to constrain the high-density EoS by a GW detection. In the case of 1.35-1.35 $M_{\odot}$ mergers the peak frequency scales particularly well with the radius of a NS with 1.6 $M_{\odot}$, where the maximum deviation from this correlation is only 60 meters for fully microphysical EoSs which are compatible with NS observations. Combined with the uncertainty in the determination of the peak frequency it appears likely that a GW detection can measure the radius of a 1.6 $M_{\odot}$ NS with an accuracy of about 100 to 200 meters. We also uncover relations of the peak frequency with the radii of nonrotating NSs with 1.35 $M_{\odot}$ or 1.8 $M_{\odot}$, with the radius or the central energy density of the maximum-mass Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff configuration, and with the pressure or sound speed at a fiducial rest-mass density of about twice nuclear saturation density. Furthermore, it is found that a determination of the dominant postmerger GW frequency can provide an upper limit for the maximum mass of nonrotating NSs. The prospects for a detection of the postmerger GW signal and a determination of the dominant GW frequency are estimated to be in the range of 0.015 to 1.2 events per year with the upcoming Advanced LIGO detector.

## Key claim

The dominant gravitational-wave frequency from the postmerger ring-down phase of neutron-star mergers correlates strongly with the radius of a nonrotating neutron star of 1.6 solar masses, with a maximum deviation of only 60 meters for fully microphysical equations of state compatible with neutron-star observations, potentially allowing radius measurements to an accuracy of about 100 to 200 meters from a gravitational-wave detection.

## Method

The authors conducted 38 three-dimensional relativistic smoothed particle hydrodynamics simulations of neutron-star mergers, using the conformal flatness approximation for general relativity and implementing 38 different microphysical equations of state grouped into four technical categories (full temperature-dependent EoSs, barotropic zero-temperature EoSs with thermal ideal-gas extension, piecewise polytrope fits, and chiral effective field theory based EoSs). The simulations started from quasi-equilibrium orbits about two to three revolutions before merger, with initial nonrotating (irrotational) velocity profiles and zero-temperature stars in neutrinoless beta-equilibrium, using approximately 340,000 SPH particles per binary. The gravitational-wave signal was extracted from the postmerger phase, and the dominant peak frequency was correlated with various neutron-star properties obtained from solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for each equation of state.

## Key numbers

- 38 representative microphysical descriptions of high-density matter were simulated for 1.35-1.35 solar mass mergers
- Maximum deviation from the correlation between peak frequency and radius of a 1.6 solar mass neutron star is only 60 meters for fully microphysical EoSs compatible with neutron-star observations
- Gravitational-wave detection can measure the radius of a 1.6 solar mass neutron star with an accuracy of about 100 to 200 meters
- The pressure at 1.85 times nuclear saturation density ranges from 1.18 x 10^34 dyne/cm^2 to 9.56 x 10^34 dyne/cm^2 across the EoS sample
- Sound speed at 1.85 times nuclear saturation density varies between 0.29 and 0.70 times the speed of light
- Maximum masses of nonrotating neutron stars range from 1.79 to 3.00 solar masses, with corresponding radii from 8.65 km to 14.30 km
- Central energy densities of maximum-mass configurations range from 1.35 x 10^15 g/cm^3 to 3.80 x 10^15 g/cm^3
- Detection prospects for the postmerger gravitational-wave signal are estimated at 0.015 to 1.2 events per year with Advanced LIGO

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier study (Bauswein et al. 2012) by enlarging the set of equations of state from a limited sample to 38 microphysical models, investigating the influence of total binary mass and initial mass ratio, and quantifying the scatter in correlations between postmerger gravitational-wave frequency and neutron-star radii. The approach provides a complementary method to inspiral-based radius measurements (Flanagan and Hinderer 2008, Read et al. 2009) and enables future gravitational-wave detections from neutron-star mergers to constrain the high-density equation of state, potentially determining neutron-star radii with sub-kilometer precision and providing upper limits on the maximum mass of nonrotating neutron stars.
