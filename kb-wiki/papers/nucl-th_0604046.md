# Reconstructing supernova-neutrino spectra using low-energy beta-beams

> Only weakly interacting, neutrinos are the principal messengers reaching us from the center of a supernova. Terrestrial neutrino telescopes, such as SNO and SuperKamiokande, can provide precious information about the processes in the core of a collapsing and exploding star. But the information about the supernova that a neutrino detector can supply, is restricted by the fact that little experimental data on the neutrino-nucleus cross sections exists and by the uncertainties in theoretical calculations. In this letter, we propose a novel procedure that determines the response of a target nucleus in a supernova-neutrino detector directly, by using low-energy beta-beams. We show that fitting 'synthetic' spectra, constructed by taking linear combinations of beta-beam spectra, to the original supernova-neutrino spectra reproduces the folded differential cross sections very accurately. Comparing the response in a terrestrial detector to these synthetic responses provides a direct way to determine the main parameters of the supernova-neutrino energy-distribution.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that by fitting linear combinations of low-energy beta-beam neutrino spectra to supernova-neutrino spectra, one can accurately reconstruct the folded differential cross sections for neutrino-nucleus scattering, thereby determining the supernova-neutrino energy distribution without relying on theoretical cross-section calculations. The method is shown to work for a small number of beta-beam components (N=3 to 5), but its accuracy is ultimately limited by detector uncertainties rather than theoretical ambiguities.

## Method

The authors represent supernova-neutrino spectra as linear combinations of beta-beam spectra with different boost factors (gamma values), then fit these synthetic spectra to the original supernova-neutrino spectra by minimizing the integral difference between them. They apply this technique to compute folded differential cross sections for neutrino-nucleus scattering on various target nuclei (16O, deuteron, 208Pb) using both Fermi-Dirac and power-law parameterizations of supernova-neutrino spectra, and test the reconstruction of incident neutrino spectra from synthetic detector responses with added noise.

## Key numbers

- Average energies for supernova neutrinos vary roughly between 12 and 18 MeV for electron neutrinos and antineutrinos, and between 16 and 22 MeV for muon and tau neutrinos
- Beta-beam gamma factors were restricted to integer values between 5 and 15 for most fits, with some fits including gamma=3 for lower-energy spectra
- The reconstruction procedure was tested with 5% and 10% uncertainties on the expansion parameters, showing stability at the 10% level
- For the 16O target, N=3 and N=5 beta-beam components in the linear combination reproduced the differential cross sections convincingly

## Context

This work addresses the problem that theoretical neutrino-nucleus cross-section calculations at supernova-relevant energies are subject to large nuclear structure uncertainties and model dependencies, as shown by the spread in predictions for 208Pb from Kolbe and Langanke (2001), Jachowicz et al. (2002), and Engel et al. (2003). The proposed method extends the low-energy beta-beam concept from Volpe (2004) and Serreau and Volpe (2004) to directly determine supernova-neutrino spectra without relying on theoretical cross sections. This enables future neutrino detectors like SNO, SuperKamiokande, LAND, and OMNIS to extract supernova parameters from the observed signal, and the technique can also be extended to study neutrino oscillation effects by comparing charged-current and neutral-current responses.
