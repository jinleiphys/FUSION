# Almost-Entirely Empirical Estimation for Chemical Potential

> Based on statistical thermal approaches, the transverse momentum distribution of the well-identified produced particles, $π^+$, $π^-$, $K^+$, $K^-$, $p$, $\bar{p}$, is studied. From the partition function of grand-canonical ensemble, we propose a generic expression for the dependence of the full chemical potential $μ$ on rapidity $y$. Then, by fitting this expression with the experimental results of most central $p_{\perp}$ and $d^2 N/2 πp_{\bot} dp_{\bot} dy$, at $7.7$, $11.5$, $19.6$, $27$, $39$, $130$, $200~$GeV, we introduce a generic expression for the rapidity dependence of $μ$, at different energies and particle types, $μ=a+b y^2$. The resulting energy dependence reads $\sqrt{s_{\mathtt{NN}}}=c[(μ-a)/b]^{d/2}$. As a validation check, the proposed approach reproduces, excellently, the rapidity spectra measured at different energies.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the full chemical potential μ of identified hadrons can be expressed as a universal quadratic function of rapidity y, μ = a + b y², where the parameters a and b depend on particle type but not on collision energy, and this expression is derived from an almost-entirely empirical procedure using experimental transverse momentum distributions rather than from thermal model fitting.

## Method

The authors start from the grand-canonical partition function and derive an expression (Eq. 9) that relates the chemical potential μ to the measured transverse momentum p⊥ and the double-differential yield d²N/(2π p⊥ dp⊥ dy) for each particle species. They input experimental STAR data for π⁺, π⁻, K⁺, K⁻, p, and p̄ at √sNN = 7.7, 11.5, 19.6, 27, 39, 130, and 200 GeV, assuming a fixed freezeout temperature T = 0.165 GeV, and then fit the resulting μ(y) values to a quadratic function. The energy dependence is then expressed as √sNN = c[(μ−a)/b]^(d/2), and the approach is validated by using the extracted μ(y) to compute rapidity spectra dN/dy via Eq. 10 and comparing with experimental data from multiple experiments.

## Key numbers

- For π⁺ and π⁻: μ = (0.06 ± 0.001) + (0.109 ± 0.01) y² (MeV)
- For K⁺ and K⁻: μ = (0.07 ± 0.001) + (0.13 ± 0.001) y² (MeV)
- For p and p̄: μ = (0.28 ± 0.001) + (0.17 ± 0.001) y² (MeV)
- For π⁺ at √sNN = 7.7 GeV: p⊥ = 0.6796 GeV, d²N/(2π p⊥ dp⊥ dy) = 12.023 GeV⁻², fit parameters a = (0.062 ± 0.014) MeV, b = (0.109 ± 0.003) MeV
- For p at √sNN = 7.7 GeV: p⊥ = 0.668 GeV, d²N/(2π p⊥ dp⊥ dy) = 11.926 GeV⁻², fit parameters a = (0.295 ± 0.024) MeV, b = (0.174 ± 0.005) MeV
- For p̄ at √sNN = 7.7 GeV: p⊥ = 0.63 GeV, d²N/(2π p⊥ dp⊥ dy) = 0.098 GeV⁻², fit parameters a = (0.162 ± 0.024) MeV, b = (0.171 ± 0.0055) MeV
- Energy dependence exponent d/2 is approximately −1.165 for all particles
- Fixed freezeout temperature assumed: T = 0.165 GeV

## Context

This work extends previous studies of baryon chemical potential dependence on beam energy by Tawfik (2013) and Andronic (2005), which were based on thermal model fits to bulk thermodynamics, by instead deriving a generic chemical potential (including baryon, strangeness, and other quantum numbers) directly from single-particle rapidity distributions. The approach builds on earlier work by Becattini (2007) and Uddin (2011) on rapidity-dependent freezeout parameters, but claims to be more universal by requiring no thermal model for the rapidity dependence itself. The validation against experimental dN/dy data from multiple experiments (Bearden 2003, Baechler 1994, Bartke 1990, Back 2000, Arsene 2009, Lin 2000, Bachler 1999, Biedron 2006) suggests the method could enable direct extraction of chemical potentials from measured rapidity spectra across a wide range of collision energies.
