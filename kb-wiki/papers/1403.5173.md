# Complementarity of Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay and Cosmology

> Neutrinoless double beta decay experiments constrain one combination of neutrino parameters, while cosmic surveys constrain another. This complementarity opens up an exciting range of possibilities. If neutrinos are Majorana particles, and the neutrino masses follow an inverted hierarchy, then the upcoming sets of both experiments will detect signals. The combined constraints will pin down not only the neutrino masses but also constrain one of the Majorana phases. If the hierarchy is normal, then a beta decay detection with the upcoming generation of experiments is unlikely, but cosmic surveys could constrain the sum of the masses to be relatively heavy, thereby producing a lower bound for the neutrinoless double beta decay rate, and therefore an argument for a next generation beta decay experiment. In this case as well, a combination of the phases will be constrained.

## Key claim
If neutrinos are Majorana particles and the mass hierarchy is inverted, upcoming neutrinoless double beta decay experiments and cosmic surveys will both detect signals, and their combined constraints can pin down one of the Majorana phases. If the hierarchy is normal, a detection by the upcoming generation of double beta decay experiments is unlikely, but cosmic surveys could produce a lower bound on the neutrinoless double beta decay rate, motivating a next-generation experiment.

## Method
The authors project combined constraints from future neutrinoless double beta decay experiments and cosmic surveys by constructing a Fisher matrix for the two observables: the effective Majorana mass \(m_{\beta\beta}\) and the sum of neutrino masses \(S \equiv \sum m_\nu\). They transform this Fisher matrix to a new parameter set consisting of \(\cos(2\lambda_2)\) (where \(\lambda_2\) is a Majorana phase) and \(S\), using the partial derivatives of \(m_{\beta\beta}\) with respect to these parameters, and then marginalize over \(S\) to obtain the projected error on the phase. For the normal hierarchy, they compute the minimum possible value of \(m_{\beta\beta}\) as a function of a lower limit on \(S\) from cosmic surveys.

## Key numbers
- The projected 1-sigma uncertainty on the effective Majorana mass from upcoming double beta decay experiments is taken as \(\Delta_{\beta} = 10\) meV.
- The projected 1-sigma uncertainty on the sum of neutrino masses from upcoming cosmic surveys is taken as \(\Delta_S = 20\) meV.
- For the inverted hierarchy, with \(S\) small and \(\cos(2\lambda_2) = -1\), the projected 1-sigma error on \(\cos(2\lambda_2)\) is 0.35, corresponding to a 6-sigma separation from the \(\lambda_2 = 0\) value.
- The cosmic neutrino energy density fraction is \(f_\nu = 0.008 \, (\sum m_\nu / 100\ \mathrm{meV})\).
- The power spectrum of the gravitational potential is reduced by 5% if \(\sum m_\nu = 100\) meV.
- Planck has mapped the gravitational potential with 27-sigma significance (Ade 2013).
- Upcoming small-scale CMB polarization and galaxy surveys can constrain \(\sum m_\nu\) at the 16 meV level (Font-Ribera 2013).

## Context
This work extends the relationship between \(m_{\beta\beta}\) and the sum of neutrino masses identified by Pascoli (2005), showing how upcoming cosmic surveys (e.g., Planck, South Pole Telescope, Atacama Cosmology Telescope) and neutrinoless double beta decay experiments (e.g., KamLAND-Zen, GERDA, Majorana) can together measure a Majorana phase. It complements the work of Minakata (2014) on phase measurement. The results enable a strategy for future experiments: if the normal hierarchy holds and cosmic surveys find a large sum of masses, a next-generation double beta decay experiment would have a guaranteed lower bound on the decay rate, ensuring a definitive test of the Majorana nature of neutrinos.
