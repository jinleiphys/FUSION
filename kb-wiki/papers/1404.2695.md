# Thermal properties of the nuclear surface

> The thermal evolution of a few thermodynamic properties of the nuclear surface like its thermodynamic potential energy, entropy and the symmetry free energy are examined for both semi-infinite nuclear matter and finite nuclei. The Thomas-Fermi model is employed. Three Skyrme interactions, namely, SkM$^*$, SLy4 and SK255 are used for the calculations to gauge the dependence of the nuclear surface properties on the energy density functionals. For finite nuclei, the surface observables are computed from a global liquid-drop inspired fit of the energies and free energies of a host of nuclei covering the entire periodic table. The hot nuclear system is modelled in a subtracted Thomas-Fermi framework. Compared to semi-infinite nuclear matter, substantial changes in the surface symmetry energy of finite nuclei are indicated; surface thermodynamic potential energies for the two systems are, however, not too different. Analytic expressions to fit the temperature and asymmetry dependence of the surface thermodynamic potential of semi-infinite nuclear matter and the temperature dependence of the surface free energy of finite nuclei are given.

## Key claim

The paper finds that the thermal evolution of the surface thermodynamic potential for semi-infinite nuclear matter follows an exponent α₁ ≈ 0.9 in the temperature range up to about 7.5 MeV, rather than the canonical value of 1.26 that holds only in a narrow window near the critical temperature; for finite nuclei, the exponent is significantly larger (α₁ ≈ 1.4–1.5) and the surface symmetry free energy coefficient is substantially smaller than for semi-infinite matter.

## Method

The authors employ the finite-temperature Thomas-Fermi (FTTF) model with a subtracted density prescription to obtain equilibrium density profiles for both semi-infinite nuclear matter and finite nuclei, using three Skyrme interactions (SkM*, SLy4, and SK255). For semi-infinite matter, the surface thermodynamic potential per unit area σ_μ is computed directly from the density profiles as a function of temperature and isospin asymmetry. For finite nuclei, the free energies of 69 spherical or near-spherical nuclei (A = 34–218, Z = 14–92) are calculated in the subtracted FTTF scheme and then fitted to a liquid-drop formula to extract the temperature-dependent surface free energy coefficient f_s and surface symmetry free energy coefficient f_s^sym.

## Key numbers

- For symmetric semi-infinite nuclear matter, the surface thermodynamic potential at zero temperature σ_μ(0,0) = 1.055 MeV fm⁻² (SkM*), 1.135 MeV fm⁻² (SLy4), and 1.060 MeV fm⁻² (SK255).
- The critical temperature for symmetric semi-infinite matter T_c(X=0) = 14.61 MeV (SkM*), 14.53 MeV (SLy4), and 15.98 MeV (SK255).
- The exponent α₁ for symmetric semi-infinite matter in the temperature range T = 0 to 7.5 MeV is 0.966 (SkM*), 0.919 (SLy4), and 1.019 (SK255); for finite nuclei in the same range, α₁ = 1.486 (SkM*), 1.404 (SLy4), and 1.427 (SK255).
- The surface symmetry free energy coefficient at zero temperature for semi-infinite matter is approximately twice as large for SK255 as for SkM* or SLy4, reflecting the larger density slope parameter L (≈95 MeV for SK255 versus ≈45 MeV for SLy4 or SkM*).
- The empirical value of f_s^sym(T=0) from experimental data is 58.91 ± 1.08 MeV, which is best matched by the SK255 interaction among those tested.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering study of Ravenhall, Pethick, and Lattimer (1983) on the thermodynamic evolution of surface properties in semi-infinite nuclear matter, who suggested a temperature dependence of the form [g(T,T_c)]^α with α = 1.25. The present calculations show that in the temperature range accessible to finite nuclei (up to the limiting temperature ≈7.5 MeV), the exponent is significantly lower (≈0.9) for semi-infinite matter and higher (≈1.4) for finite nuclei. The results also contradict the finding of Papakonstantinou et al. (2013) that surface energy increases with asymmetry for cold nuclei, a discrepancy attributed to different definitions of the surface energy and asymmetry parameter. The work enables improved modeling of hot nuclei in multifragmentation reactions and of nuclear matter in neutron star and supernova environments, where temperature-dependent surface properties are crucial for determining nuclear masses, electron capture rates, and level densities.
