# Disentangling random thermal motion of particles and collective expansion of source from transverse momentum spectra in high energy collisions

> In the framework of a multisource thermal model, we describe experimental results of the transverse momentum spectra of final-state light flavour particles produced in gold-gold (Au-Au), copper-copper (Cu-Cu), lead-lead (Pb-Pb), proton-lead ($p$-Pb), and proton-proton ($p$-$p$) collisions at various energies, measured by the PHENIX, STAR, ALICE, and CMS Collaborations, by using the Tsallis-standard (Tsallis form of Fermi-Dirac or Bose-Einstein), Tsallis, and two- or three-component standard distributions which can be in fact regarded as different types of "thermometers" or "thermometric scales" and "speedometers". A central parameter in the three distributions is the effective temperature which contains information on the kinetic freeze-out temperature of the emitting source and reflects the effects of random thermal motion of particles as well as collective expansion of the source. To disentangle both effects, we extract the kinetic freeze-out temperature from the intercept of the effective temperature ($T$) curve as a function of particle's rest mass ($m_0$) when plotting $T$ versus $m_0$, and the mean transverse flow velocity from the slope of the mean transverse momentum ($\langle p_T \rangle$) curve as a function of mean moving mass ($\overline{m}$) when plotting $\langle p_T \rangle$ versus $\overline{m}$.

## Key claim

The effective temperature extracted from transverse momentum spectra contains both random thermal motion and collective expansion effects; the authors disentangle these by extracting the kinetic freeze-out temperature from the intercept of the linear T versus m0 relation and the mean transverse flow velocity from the slope of the linear ⟨pT⟩ versus mean moving mass relation, using three different distribution functions as "thermometers" that yield consistent results.

## Method

Within a multisource thermal model framework, the authors fitted transverse momentum spectra of light-flavor hadrons (pions, kaons, protons, and strange baryons) from Au-Au, Cu-Cu, Pb-Pb, p-Pb, and p-p collisions at center-of-mass energies from 0.2 to 7 TeV using three distribution functions: the Tsallis-standard distribution, the Tsallis distribution, and two- or three-component standard (Boltzmann, Fermi-Dirac, or Bose-Einstein) distributions. For each particle species and centrality bin, they extracted the effective temperature T from the fits, then plotted T versus particle rest mass m0 to obtain the kinetic freeze-out temperature T0 as the intercept at m0=0. Separately, they calculated the mean transverse momentum ⟨pT⟩ and mean moving mass from the same fit parameters, plotted ⟨pT⟩ versus mean moving mass, and extracted the mean transverse flow velocity ⟨uT⟩ as the slope of this linear relation.

## Key numbers

- Kinetic freeze-out temperature from the standard distribution in central collisions: T_S0 = (156 ± 6) MeV
- Mean transverse flow velocity for central collisions: ⟨uT⟩ = (0.443 ± 0.071)c
- Mean transverse flow velocity for peripheral collisions: ⟨uT⟩ = (0.403 ± 0.079)c
- The relative difference in mean transverse flow velocity between central and peripheral collisions is about 10%
- The central collision ⟨uT⟩ value (0.443c) is comparable to the radial flow velocity range (0.426-0.472)c from the blast-wave model
- The peripheral collision ⟨uT⟩ value (0.403c) differs from the blast-wave model radial flow velocity (≈0) for peripheral collisions
- The kinetic freeze-out temperature T_S0 (156 MeV) equals the chemical freeze-out temperature (156 MeV) extracted from particle ratios in a thermal and statistical model
- The kinetic freeze-out temperature T_S0 (156 MeV) is less than the chemical freeze-out temperature (170 MeV) used in some QCD critical point estimations

## Context

This work extends the methodology of Wei, Liu, and Lacey (2015) for disentangling thermal motion from collective flow using linear relations between effective temperature and mass, and between mean transverse momentum and mean moving mass. The results are compared with the blast-wave model of Schnedermann, Sollfrank, and Heinz (1993) and its improved versions that include anisotropic flow parameters, as well as with the quark recombination model. The extracted kinetic freeze-out temperatures show no obvious dependence on collision energy or system size in the 0.2-7 TeV range, suggesting that different interacting systems reach the same excitation degree at kinetic freeze-out. The methodology provides an alternative to the blast-wave model for extracting flow velocities, with the key difference that the present work's transverse flow velocity includes both isotropic radial flow and anisotropic collective flow components.
