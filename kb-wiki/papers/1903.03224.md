# 2018 Update of the Discoveries of Nuclides

> The 2018 update of the discovery of nuclide project is presented. 50 new nuclides were observed for the first time in 2018. A large number of isotopes is still only published in conference proceedings or internal reports.

## Key claim

The 2018 update of the discovery of nuclide project reports that 50 new nuclides were observed for the first time in refereed journals, but a large number of isotopes remain published only in conference proceedings or internal reports, and the positive trend in new discoveries is unlikely to continue in 2019 unless those unpublished results appear in refereed literature.

## Method

The author compiled and updated the ongoing discovery of nuclide project by surveying refereed journal publications from 2018 that reported first observations of new isotopes. For each new nuclide, the production method (projectile fragmentation, fusion-evaporation, or secondary beams), laboratory, and first author were recorded. The author also tracked which isotopes from previous years remained unpublished in refereed journals, listing them in a separate table of conference proceedings and internal reports.

## Key numbers

- 50 new nuclides were observed for the first time in 2018, the most since 2012 when 68 new isotopes were reported.
- 43 of the new nuclides were very neutron-rich isotopes discovered at RIKEN in Japan.
- The total number of observed isotopes reached 3302 by the end of 2018.
- The ten-year average discovery rate was 35.7 nuclides per year, the highest since 2000.
- The ten-year average for neutron-rich isotopes reached an all-time high of 27.8 per year.
- Known proton-rich nuclides numbered 1298, while neutron-rich nuclides numbered 1274, a difference of only 24.
- The ten-year average for transuranium isotopes dropped to 3.7 per year, its lowest since 2000 (3.4).
- 916 different first authors in 1551 papers reported discoveries, with 3762 different co-authors total.

## Context

This work is the sixth annual update of the isotope discovery project originally published in a series of papers in Atomic Data and Nuclear Data Tables from 2009 through 2013 (see for example the first paper by Ginepro (2009) and the last by Fry (2013)), with summary papers in Nuclear Physics News (Thoennessen, 2012) and Reports on Progress in Physics (Thoennessen, 2013). The update enables tracking of which newly discovered nuclides remain unpublished in refereed journals, highlighting that 33 neutron-rich nuclides reported from RIKEN in conference proceedings need to be published before the positive discovery trend can continue.
