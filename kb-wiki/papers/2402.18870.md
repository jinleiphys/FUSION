# B-mesons as essential probes of hot QCD matter

> This article elucidates the pivotal role of b-mesons and bottomonium states in exploring the existence and properties of hot QCD matter (commonly known as quark-gluon-plasma (QGP) produced within the crucible heavy-ion collision experiments). Owing to the complex and confounding nature of strong interaction force the direct detection of probing the hot QCD matter is not feasible. In light of this, investigating the dynamics of b-quarks and anti-quarks within the hot QCD medium emerges as an invaluable indirect probe. The impact of b-quarks and the mesons spans a spectrum of interesting domains regarding the physics of QCD at finite temperature, encompassing the QCD phase transition, color screening, quarkonia dissociation, heavy quark energy loss and collective flow, anisotropic aspects, and strongly coupled nature of hot QCD medium. These aspects underscore the indispensable nature of B-mesons in the quest to create and explore the complex nature of strong interaction force through the QGP/hot QCD matter. In this context, we mainly focus on works related to transport studies of b-mesons in hot QCD medium, lattice QCD, and effective field theory studies on bottomonium states, and finally, open quantum system frameworks to quarkonia to explore the properties of hot QCD medium in relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments.

## Key claim

The paper argues that bottom quarks and B-mesons are essential probes of hot QCD matter because, unlike charm quarks, bottom quarks exhibit Brownian motion in the quark-gluon plasma at experimentally relevant temperatures, making the Langevin approach a suitable approximation for their dynamics; however, this claim is qualified by the condition that the approximation holds only when the ratio M_b/T is approximately 10, and the authors note that the Fokker-Planck/Langevin framework may not be valid for charm quarks at the same temperatures.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework for heavy quark dynamics in hot QCD matter, starting from the Boltzmann transport equation and deriving the Fokker-Planck equation through a momentum expansion that yields drag and diffusion coefficients. They compare Langevin (stochastic) and Boltzmann (full collision integral) approaches for bottom and charm quarks at T=0.4 GeV with an initial delta distribution at p=10 GeV, using perturbative QCD to compute the drag and diffusion coefficients with Debye mass m_D=gT for regularization. For bottomonium suppression, they discuss the open quantum system framework using the Lindblad equation derived from potential non-relativistic QCD (pNRQCD) with lattice QCD inputs, and they review lattice NRQCD studies using extended meson operators to probe in-medium modifications of bottomonium states.

## Key numbers

* Bottom quark mass to temperature ratio M_b/T is approximately 10 at T=0.4 GeV, which justifies the Langevin approximation for bottom quarks but not for charm quarks at the same temperature.
* The spatial diffusion coefficient D_x for b quarks, anti-B mesons, and Lambda_b baryons is shown as a function of temperature, with a minimum near the quark-hadron transition temperature T_c, indicating maximum interaction strength in that region.
* The thermalization time for heavy quarks is given by tau_th = M D_x / T = 1.8 * (2 pi T D_x) / (T/T_c)^2 fm/c, where T_c is the quark-hadron transition temperature.
* The bottomonium nuclear suppression factor R_AA computed within the open quantum system framework with lattice QCD inputs agrees with LHC data for 5.02 TeV Pb-Pb collisions, and NLO corrections to this framework show good agreement with LHC data but some disagreements with RHIC data.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on heavy quark probes of the quark-gluon plasma, building on foundational studies by Svetitsky (1987) on the Fokker-Planck approach and by Moore and Teaney (2004) on heavy quark thermalization. It contrasts the Brownian motion of bottom quarks with the non-Brownian behavior of charm quarks, as previously shown by Das et al. (2013), and discusses the open quantum system framework for quarkonia suppression originally proposed by Akamatsu (2014) and developed by Brambilla et al. (2020) using pNRQCD. The work enables future studies of B-meson observables such as the nuclear suppression factor R_AA and elliptic flow v_2, as well as the Lambda_b/B ratio for understanding in-medium hadronization mechanisms, with ongoing experimental efforts at both RHIC and LHC.
