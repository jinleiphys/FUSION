# Covariant energy density functionals: the assessment of global performance across the nuclear landscape

> The assessment of the global performance of the state-of-the-art covariant energy density functionals and related theoretical uncertainties in the description of ground state observables has recently been performed. Based on these results, the correlations between global description of binding energies and nuclear matter properties of covariant energy density functionals have been studied in this contribution.

# Covariant energy density functionals: the assessment of global performance across the nuclear landscape

## Key claim
The correlations between global description of binding energies and nuclear matter properties of covariant energy density functionals are not straightforward; functionals with good nuclear matter properties do not necessarily provide good descriptions of finite nuclei, and strict enforcement of nuclear matter constraints will not necessarily reduce uncertainties in mass predictions for neutron-rich systems.

## Method
The author compared binding energy predictions from four state-of-the-art covariant energy density functionals (NL3*, DD-ME2, DD-MEδ, and DD-PC1) across the nuclear landscape of even-even nuclei, using experimental data from the AME2012 compilation. The spreads in predicted binding energies were mapped as functions of proton and neutron number, and these were compared with the nuclear matter properties (saturation density, energy per particle, incompressibility, symmetry energy and its slope, and Lorentz effective mass) of each functional. The analysis also examined how differences in fitting protocols and nuclear matter properties contribute to systematic uncertainties in mass predictions.

## Key numbers
- Binding energy spreads for known nuclei stay within 5-6 MeV; spreads are typically around 3 MeV for nuclei in the valley of beta-stability.
- Theoretical systematic uncertainties in masses reach 15 MeV for some nuclei approaching the neutron-drip line.
- For the DD-ME2/DD-MEδ pair, predictions differ by less than 1.5 MeV for almost half of the Z ≤ 104 nuclear landscape, and only in a few points does the difference exceed 5 MeV.
- The rms deviations between calculated and experimental binding energies (ΔE_rms) for the four functionals are: NL3* = 2.96 MeV, DD-ME2 = 2.39 MeV, DD-MEδ = 2.29 MeV, DD-PC1 = 2.01 MeV (based on 640 measured masses from AME2012).
- Nuclear matter properties at saturation for the functionals include: saturation density ρ0 from 0.150 to 0.152 fm^{-3}; energy per particle E/A from -16.31 to -16.06 MeV; incompressibility K∞ from 219 to 258 MeV; symmetry energy J from 32.35 to 38.68 MeV; slope L from 49.4 to 122.6 MeV; Lorentz effective mass m*/m from 0.61 to 0.67.
- Among 263 relativistic functionals analyzed in Ref. RMF-nm, only 4 and 3 satisfy nuclear matter constraint sets SET2a and SET2b, respectively; removing the isospin incompressibility constraint increases these numbers to 35 and 30.
- FSUGold has the largest rms deviation from experiment for binding energies at 6.5 MeV among all CEDFs with known global performance.

## Context
This work extends the systematic assessment of covariant energy density functionals presented in Afanasjev (2013, 2014, 2015), which for the first time investigated theoretical systematic uncertainties in ground state observables on a global scale for relativistic functionals. The analysis contradicts the expectation that functionals satisfying nuclear matter constraints from Ref. RMF-nm will automatically provide good descriptions of finite nuclei; for example, FSUGold satisfies nuclear matter constraints but has the worst binding energy rms deviation (6.5 MeV), while NL3* and DD-ME2 fail some nuclear matter constraints yet provide better descriptions of finite nuclei. The findings enable future work to focus on resolving the mismatch between nuclear matter physics and finite-nuclei physics in functional development, particularly regarding shell effects that affect neutron-rich systems.
