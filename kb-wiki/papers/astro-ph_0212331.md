# Does the Sun Shine by pp or CNO Fusion Reactions?

> We show that solar neutrino experiments set an upper limit of 7.8% (7.3% including the recent KamLAND measurements) to the fraction of energy that the Sun produces via the CNO fusion cycle, which is an order of magnitude improvement upon the previous limit. New experiments are required to detect CNO neutrinos corresponding to the 1.5% of the solar luminosity that the standard solar model predicts is generated by the CNO cycle.

## Key claim

The authors show that solar neutrino experiments set an upper limit of 7.8% (7.3% including KamLAND data) on the fraction of the Sun's luminosity produced by the CNO cycle, but the global fit is essentially flat for CNO luminosity fractions below 5%, meaning current experiments are not sensitive to CNO fluxes corresponding to less than 5% of solar luminosity.

## Method

The authors performed a global chi-squared analysis of 80 measurements from the chlorine, SAGE, GALLEX, GNO, Super-Kamiokande, and SNO solar neutrino experiments, plus KamLAND reactor data, treating the neutrino oscillation parameters (Delta m^2 and tan^2 theta) and all eight solar neutrino fluxes as free parameters. They imposed the luminosity constraint requiring that the sum of thermal energy generation rates from all neutrino fluxes equal the solar luminosity, then marginalized over oscillation parameters and non-CNO fluxes to determine the 3-sigma upper limit on the CNO luminosity fraction.

## Key numbers

- Upper limit on CNO luminosity fraction: 7.8% at 3-sigma using only solar neutrino data; 7.3% at 3-sigma including KamLAND data
- Standard solar model predicts 1.5% of solar luminosity from CNO cycle
- Previous best limit on CNO contribution was 99.95% (from Bahcall 2002)
- Maximum allowed CNO neutrino flux: phi_CNO_max < 3.41 x 10^10 cm^-2 s^-1 times (L_CNO/L_sun)
- For the best-fit non-LMA solution at large CNO values: Delta m^2 = 7.9 x 10^-11 eV^2, tan^2 theta = 0.22
- Expected KamLAND events without oscillations: 86.8 events above 2.6 MeV visible energy
- With a simulated BOREXINO measurement at 10% (5%) precision, the limit would improve to 5.6% (4.9%)

## Context

This work extends the earlier analysis of Bahcall (2002) which found that 99.95% of solar luminosity could come from CNO, by using new Super-Kamiokande and SNO measurements that ruled out the small-mixing-angle solutions that allowed large CNO contributions. The order-of-magnitude improvement comes from the flat recoil energy spectrum measured by Super-Kamiokande and the total event rates from both Super-Kamiokande and SNO. The authors note that measuring CNO neutrinos at the 1.5% level predicted by standard solar models (Bahcall 2000) will require new low-energy experiments such as BOREXINO or KamLAND with sufficient energy resolution to distinguish continuum CNO neutrinos from the Be-7 and pep neutrino lines.
