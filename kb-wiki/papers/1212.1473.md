# Derivation of transient relativistic fluid dynamics from the Boltzmann equation for a multi-component system

> We derive the non-equilibrium single-particle momentum distribution function of a hadron resonance gas. We then study the effects that this newly derived expression can have in the freeze-out description of fluid-dynamical models of heavy ion collisions and compare it with the method traditionally employed, the 14-moment approximation.

## Key claim
The paper derives a non-equilibrium momentum distribution function for a multi-component hadron resonance gas that goes beyond the traditional Israel-Stewart (IS) 14-moment approximation, but finds that the particle-species dependence of the expansion coefficients has only a small effect on differential elliptic flow in heavy-ion collisions, at least within the simplified hadronic model considered.

## Method
The authors use the method of moments (Denicol 2012) to expand the non-equilibrium part of the single-particle distribution function for each hadron species in a complete orthogonal basis of irreducible tensors and energy-dependent polynomials. They truncate the expansion to include only the shear-stress tensor contribution (ℓ=2, n=0) and then relate the individual species shear-stress tensors to the total shear-stress tensor and shear tensor via a matrix diagonalization of the collision integral for elastic 2-to-2 collisions of classical particles. The coefficients α_i and β_i in this relation are computed using a simple hadronic model with constant cross sections (30 mb) for all hadrons up to 1.8 GeV mass.

## Key numbers
- Constant hadronic cross section assumed: σ_ij = 30 mb for all elastic collisions between hadrons.
- All hadrons up to a mass of 1.8 GeV are included in the model.
- The differential elliptic flow v2(pT) is computed for pions, kaons, and protons in three centrality classes: 20-30%, 30-40%, and 40-50%.

## Context
This work extends the method-of-moments derivation of transient relativistic fluid dynamics from the Boltzmann equation (Denicol 2012) from single-component to multi-component systems, addressing the freeze-out problem in heavy-ion collisions. It directly tests the common Israel-Stewart ansatz (Teaney 2003) where the non-equilibrium correction δf is assumed to have a quadratic momentum dependence with a species-independent coefficient matched to the total shear stress. The results show that the particle-species dependence of the shear-stress coefficients has a modest effect on elliptic flow, but the authors note this may be due to the oversimplified constant-cross-section model and plan future work with more realistic hadronic cross sections and inelastic collisions.
