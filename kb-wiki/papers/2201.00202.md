# Possible early universe signals in proton collisions at the Large Hadron Collider

> Our universe was born about 13.8 billion years ago from an extremely hot and dense singular point, in a process known as the Big Bang. The hot and dense matter which dominated the system within a few microseconds of its birth was in the form of a soup of elementary quarks and gluons, known as the quark-gluon plasma (QGP). Signatures compatible with the formation of the QGP matter have experimentally been observed in heavy-ion (such as Au or Pb) collisions at ultra-relativistic energies. Recently, experimental data of proton-proton (pp) collisions at the CERN Large Hadron Collider (LHC) have also shown signals resembling those of the QGP formation, which made these studies quite stimulating as to how the collision of small systems features in producing the early universe signals. In this article, we report on some of the compelling experimental results and give an account of the present understanding. We review the pp physics program at the LHC and discuss future prospects in the context of exploring the nature of the primordial matter in the early universe.

# Possible early universe signals in proton collisions at the Large Hadron Collider

## Key claim
Proton-proton collisions at the LHC, specifically in high-multiplicity events, exhibit multiple signatures resembling those of quark-gluon plasma formation in heavy-ion collisions, suggesting the possible formation of QGP droplets in small collision systems. The authors review these observations but note that a definitive conclusion about QGP droplet formation in pp collisions requires further investigation of additional signatures such as jet quenching and quarkonia suppression.

## Method
The authors review experimental results from the ALICE and CMS experiments at the LHC, comparing observables across pp, p-Pb, and Pb-Pb collision systems at center-of-mass energies up to 13 TeV for pp and 5.02 TeV per nucleon for Pb-Pb. They examine transverse momentum spectra fitted with the Blast-wave model to extract kinetic freeze-out temperature and radial flow velocity, particle yield ratios for strangeness enhancement, two-particle azimuthal correlations for ridge structures, and elliptic flow harmonics from multi-particle correlations.

## Key numbers
- Critical energy density for deconfinement transition: $\epsilon_c$ = 1 GeV/fm³, with critical temperature $T_c \approx$ 150-170 MeV, compared to normal nuclear matter energy density $\rho_N$ = 0.17 GeV/fm³ and nucleon energy density $\rho_p$ = 0.5 GeV/fm³
- For pp collisions at $\sqrt{s}$ = 7 TeV, Blast-wave analysis yields kinetic freeze-out temperature $T_{\rm kin} = 163\pm10$ MeV and average radial flow velocity $<\beta_T> = 0.49 \pm 0.02$
- LHC pp collisions at 13 TeV produce about 5-10 particles in central rapidity on average, with high-multiplicity events reaching approximately 10 times that number
- QGP lifetime is approximately $10^{-23}$ second (about 3 fm/c)

## Context
This review extends the established program of QGP research from heavy-ion collisions at CERN SPS, RHIC, and LHC into the unexpected domain of small collision systems. The observation of QGP-like signatures in high-multiplicity pp collisions challenges the traditional use of pp collisions as a baseline for heavy-ion measurements and opens questions about the minimum system size required for QGP formation. The authors discuss future prospects with LHC detector upgrades through 2036 and the planned Future Circular Collider (FCC) at 100 TeV pp and 39 TeV per nucleon heavy-ion collisions, which would enable further study of early universe signals.
