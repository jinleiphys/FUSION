# Topological effects and particle-physics analogies beyond the massless Dirac-Weyl fermion in graphene nanorings

> Armchair and zigzag edge terminations in planar hexagonal and trigonal graphene nanorings are shown to underlie one-dimensional topological states associated with distinctive energy gaps and patterns (e.g., linear dispersion of the energy of an hexagonal ring with an armchair termination versus parabolic dispersion for a zigzag terminated one) in the bands of the tight-binding spectra as a function of the magnetic field. A relativistic Dirac-Kronig-Penney model analysis of the tight-binding Aharonov-Bohm behavior reveals that the graphene quasiparticle in an armchair hexagonal ring is a condensed-matter realization of an ultrarelativistic fermion with a position-dependent mass term, akin to the zero-energy fermionic solitons with fractional charge familiar from quantum field theory and from the theory of polyacetylene. The topological origins of the above behavior are highlighted by contrasting it with the case of a trigonal armchair ring, where we find that the quasiparticle excitations behave as familiar Dirac fermions with a constant mass. Furthermore, the spectra of a zigzag hexagonal ring correspond to the low-kinetic-energy nonrelativistic regime of a leptonlike massive fermion. A onedimensional relativistic Lagrangian formalism coupling a fermionic and a scalar bosonic field via a Yukawa interaction, in conjunction with the breaking of the Z2 reflectional symmetry of the scalar field, is shown to unify the above dissimilar behaviors.

## Key claim

The paper shows that graphene nanorings can realize a richer variety of relativistic quantum field behaviors beyond the massless Dirac-Weyl fermion, including position-dependent mass terms and fermionic solitons, but only for specific combinations of ring shape (hexagonal versus trigonal) and edge termination (armchair versus zigzag). The abstract's claim of "topological effects and particle-physics analogies" is qualified by the finding that the armchair hexagonal ring specifically yields an ultrarelativistic fermion with a position-dependent mass term, while the trigonal armchair ring yields familiar constant-mass Dirac fermions and the zigzag hexagonal ring yields nonrelativistic massive fermions.

## Method

The authors performed tight-binding calculations of single-particle spectra and Aharonov-Bohm magnetization for planar graphene nanorings with three configurations: hexagonal armchair, trigonal armchair, and hexagonal zigzag. They then modeled these spectra using a one-dimensional relativistic Dirac-Kronig-Penney superlattice approach, where each side of the polygonal ring was divided into square potential steps with assigned electrostatic potentials and position-dependent scalar mass terms, solving the generalized Dirac equation with a bosonic scalar field.

## Key numbers

- The hexagonal armchair ring contained 2718 carbon atoms; the trigonal armchair ring contained 2142 carbon atoms; the hexagonal zigzag ring contained 2688 carbon atoms.
- The tight-binding hopping parameter was set to t = 2.7 eV.
- The band gap δ₁ in the hexagonal armchair ring was approximately 80 K, described as easily detectable experimentally.
- The armchair nanoribbon width parameter was N_W = 14 for both hexagonal and trigonal armchair rings.
- In the DKP model for the hexagonal armchair ring, the position-dependent mass parameter was m₀ = 0.01 t/v_F², with segment lengths a = 8a₀ and b = 15a₀ (where a₀ = 0.246 nm is the graphene lattice constant).
- For strong soliton localization in the hexagonal armchair ring, the DKP model used m₀ = 0.30 t/v_F².
- For the trigonal armchair ring DKP model, the mass parameter was m₀ = 0.02 t/v_F².
- The Fermi velocity of graphene v_F replaces the speed of light, with v_F/c ≈ 1/300.

## Context

This work extends prior tight-binding studies of polygonal graphene rings by Rech (2007), Bahamon (2009), Luo (2009), and others, as well as continuum Dirac-Weyl studies by Recher (2007) and Akhmerov (2008), which did not address analogies to one-dimensional quantum field theoretical models. The paper introduces a Dirac-Kronig-Penney superlattice model for the Aharonov-Bohm effect, enabling connections to the Jackiw-Rebbi fermionic soliton model (1976) and the theory of trans-polyacetylene by Heeger (1988) and Jackiw (1981). The findings enable experimental testing of regime-crossover signatures in graphene systems prepared with atomic precision, and exploration of similar topological behavior in other designer-Dirac-fermion artificial systems such as those by Manoharan (2012) or nanopatterned artificial graphene.
