# Population synthesis of helium white dwarf--red giant star mergers and the formation of lithium-rich giants and carbon stars

> The formation histories of lithium-rich and carbon-rich red giants are not yet understood. It has been proposed that the merger of a helium-core white dwarf with a red giant branch star might provide a solution. We have computed an extended grid of post-merger evolution models and combined these with predictions of binary star population synthesis. The results strongly support the proposal that the merger of a helium white dwarf with a red giant branch star can provide the progenitors of both lithium-rich red clump stars and early-R carbon stars. The distribution of post-merger models in $T_{\rm eff}$, $\log g$, $\log L$, the surface abundances of lithium and carbon, and the predicted space densities agree well with the observed distributions of these parameters for Li-rich and early-R stars in the Galaxy.

## Key claim

The merger of a helium white dwarf with a red giant branch star can provide progenitors for both lithium-rich red clump stars and early-R carbon stars, but only for specific ranges of helium white dwarf mass: less massive HeWDs (0.35-0.40 solar masses) produce lithium enrichment, while more massive HeWDs produce carbon enrichment, and the surface abundance of lithium is very sensitive to the pre-merger HeWD mass and hence the core mass of the remnant.

## Method

The authors computed an extended grid of 259 post-merger evolution models using the MESA stellar evolution code (version V8118) for four metallicities (Z=0.03, 0.02, 0.01, 0.004), simulating the merger as two separate fast-accretion steps: first accretion of helium-rich material onto the HeWD, then accretion of hydrogen-rich material to represent assimilation of the RGB envelope. They combined these evolutionary tracks with binary star population synthesis using the BSE code, evolving 10^7 binary systems over 14 Gyr to obtain the mass distribution of merger progenitors and calculate the predicted space densities and surface abundances of post-merger stars.

## Key numbers

- HeWD masses chosen for the grid: 0.350, 0.375, 0.400, 0.425, 0.450, and 0.475 solar masses, in steps of 0.025 solar masses
- RGB helium core masses ranged from 0.150 to 0.25 solar masses in steps of 0.05 solar masses
- Final masses of merged models ranged from 0.80 to 2.0 solar masses in steps of 0.1 solar masses
- Accretion rate during helium accretion phase: 10^{-3} solar masses per year, similar to the average from SPH simulations by Piersanti (2010)
- Accretion rate for hydrogen-rich envelope: 1 solar mass per year, as used by Zhang (2017)
- Li-rich giants form through HeWD+RGB mergers at rates of 8.6, 7.0, 5.9, and 6.7 x 10^{-4} per year for the Galactic disc at Z=0.03, 0.02, 0.01, and 0.004 respectively
- Predicted Galactic disc volume density of Li-rich giants: 0.8-1.2 x 10^{-6} per cubic parsec, corresponding to approximately 1% of total red clump stars (total red clump space density: 1.14 x 10^{-4} per cubic parsec from Knapp 2001)
- Li-rich giants defined as having A(Li) > 1.5 may live for up to 1.0 x 10^8 years
- Core masses of Li-rich merger remnants range from 0.4-0.65 solar masses, with peak at 0.45-0.5 solar masses
- Final masses of Li-rich mergers range from 0.8 to 1.8 solar masses, with peak at approximately 1.2 solar masses
- For stars with A(Li) >= 3.2, core masses are approximately in the range 0.44-0.51 solar masses
- From 10^7 binary systems: 3931, 3233, 2707, and 3093 undergo HeWD+RGB mergers producing enriched lithium surfaces for Z=0.03, 0.02, 0.01, and 0.004 respectively
- Carbon-rich models obtained only for Z=0.03, 0.02, and 0.01, not for Z=0.004

## Context

This work extends the earlier study by Zhang (2013), which investigated only five merger models with a much higher accretion rate than indicated by 3D SPH simulations, by surveying a larger parameter space of 259 models and adopting accretion rates consistent with the SPH simulation by Piersanti (2010). The binary population synthesis approach follows the methodology used by Izzard (2007) to model R stars, and the results support the proposal that HeWD+RGB mergers can explain both Li-rich red clump stars and early-R carbon stars, with the predicted space densities matching the observed fraction of Li-rich giants (approximately 1% of red clump stars). The work enables future asteroseismic identification of merger products and can be tested against the 30 Li-rich giants already confirmed as red clump stars by asteroseismology from studies by Jofre (2015), Carlberg (2015), Kumar (2018), and Singh (2019a, 2019b).
