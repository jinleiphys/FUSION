# Progenitor neutron stars of the lightest and heaviest millisecond pulsars

> The recent mass measurements of two binary millisecond pulsars, PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0751+1807 with a mass M=1.97+/-0.04 Msun and M= 1.26 +/- 0.14 Msun, respectively, indicate a wide range of masses for such objects and possibly also a broad spectrum of masses of neutron stars born in core-collapse supernovae. Starting from the zero-age main sequence binary stage, we aim at inferring the birth masses of PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0751+1807 by taking the differences in the evolutionary stages preceding their formation into account. Using simulations for the evolution of binary stars, we reconstruct the evolutionary tracks leading to the formation of PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0751+1807. We analyze in detail the spin evolution due to the accretion of matter from a disk in the intermediate-mass/low-mass X-ray binary. We consider two equations of state of dense matter, one for purely nucleonic matter and the other one including a high-density softening due to the appearance of hyperons. Stationary and axisymmetric stellar configurations in general relativity are used, together with a recent magnetic torque model and observationally-motivated laws for the decay of magnetic field. The estimated birth mass of the neutron stars PSR J0751+1807 and PSR J1614-2230 could be as low as 1.0 Msun and as high as 1.9 Msun, respectively. These values depend weakly on the equation of state and the assumed model for the magnetic field and its accretion-induced decay. The masses of progenitor neutron stars of recycled pulsars span a broad interval from 1.0 Msun to 1.9 Msun. Including the effect of a slow Roche-lobe detachment phase, which could be relevant for PSR J0751+1807, would make the lower mass limit even lower. A realistic theory for core-collapse supernovae should account for this wide range of mass.

## Key claim

The birth masses of the progenitor neutron stars of millisecond pulsars span a broad interval from 1.0 solar masses to 1.9 solar masses, depending weakly on the equation of state and the assumed model for magnetic field decay; including a slow Roche-lobe detachment phase could make the lower mass limit even lower.

## Method

The authors used binary star evolution simulations to reconstruct evolutionary tracks from the zero-age main sequence stage to the formation of PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0751+1807. They modeled the spin evolution due to disk accretion in intermediate-mass and low-mass X-ray binaries using stationary, axisymmetric general relativistic stellar configurations, two equations of state (purely nucleonic DH and hyperonic BM), a magnetic torque model, and observationally-motivated laws for accretion-induced magnetic field decay.

## Key numbers

- PSR J1614-2230 has a measured mass of 1.97 +/- 0.04 solar masses, a spin period of 3.15 ms, and a spin frequency of 317 Hz
- PSR J0751+1807 has a measured mass of 1.26 +/- 0.14 solar masses, a spin period of 3.48 ms, and a spin frequency of 287 Hz
- The estimated birth mass of the progenitor neutron star of PSR J0751+1807 could be as low as 1.0 solar masses
- The estimated birth mass of the progenitor neutron star of PSR J1614-2230 could be as high as 1.9 solar masses
- The DH equation of state yields a maximum mass of 2.05 solar masses and a radius at maximum mass of 10.0 km for non-rotating configurations
- The BM equation of state yields a maximum mass of 2.03 solar masses and a radius at maximum mass of 10.7 km for non-rotating configurations
- The white dwarf companion of PSR J1614-2230 has a mass of 0.500 +/- 0.006 solar masses
- The white dwarf companion of PSR J0751+1807 has a mass of 0.12 +/- 0.02 solar masses

## Context

This work extends the evolutionary scenarios for PSR J1614-2230 developed by Tauris et al. (2011) and Lin and Rappaport (2011) by adding a detailed disk-accretion spin-up model, and constructs a new evolutionary scenario for PSR J0751+1807 using elements from Tauris and van den Heuvel (2003) and Istrate (2014). The results show that progenitor neutron star masses span a much wider range than previously assumed, which challenges core-collapse supernova theories to account for birth masses from 1.0 to 1.9 solar masses. The findings enable future work on the relation between supernova explosion mechanisms and the resulting neutron star mass distribution.
