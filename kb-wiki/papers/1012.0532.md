# The influence of collective neutrino oscillations on a supernova r-process

> Recently, it has been demonstrated that neutrinos in a supernova oscillate collectively. This process occurs much deeper than the conventional matter-induced MSW effect and hence may have an impact on nucleosynthesis. In this paper we explore the effects of collective neutrino oscillations on the r-process, using representative late-time neutrino spectra and outflow models. We find that accurate modeling of the collective oscillations is essential for this analysis. As an illustration, the often-used "single-angle" approximation makes grossly inaccurate predictions for the yields in our setup. With the proper multiangle treatment, the effect of the oscillations is found to be less dramatic, but still significant. Since the oscillation patterns are sensitive to the details of the emitted fluxes and the sign of the neutrino mass hierarchy, so are the r-process yields. The magnitude of the effect also depends sensitively on the astrophysical conditions - in particular on the interplay between the time when nuclei begin to exist in significant numbers and the time when the collective oscillation begins. A more definitive understanding of the astrophysical conditions, and accurate modeling of the collective oscillations for those conditions, is necessary.

## Key claim

Collective neutrino oscillations can significantly impact supernova r-process nucleosynthesis, but the magnitude of this effect depends sensitively on the interplay between the timing of nuclear assembly and the onset of collective oscillations, as well as on the sign of the neutrino mass hierarchy and the details of the emitted neutrino fluxes.

## Method

The authors coupled multiangle collective neutrino oscillation calculations (including all three flavors) to a nuclear reaction network that follows composition from nuclear statistical equilibrium through intermediate-mass seed formation to final r-process abundances. They used parameterized neutrino-driven wind trajectories based on the Panov and Janka (2009) parameterization, with two density profiles (early-type and late-type) and a range of entropies (s/k = 100, 200, 300) and initial timescales (1 ms to 50 ms), employing late-time neutrino spectra from Keil et al. (2003).

## Key numbers

- Neutrino temperatures used: T_nu_e = 2.6 MeV, T_nu_e_bar = 4.0 MeV, T_nu_mu = 5.0 MeV; degeneracy parameters eta_nu_e = 3.0, eta_nu_e_bar = 2.8, eta_nu_mu = 1.8
- Neutrino luminosities: L_nu_e = 6.6 x 10^51 erg/s, L_nu_e_bar = 8.8 x 10^51 erg/s, L_nu_mu = 12.7 x 10^51 erg/s
- Initial electron fraction set by these neutrino parameters: Y_e = 0.41
- Multiangle calculations predicted oscillations starting at approximately 75 km for the q=3.0 spectra (compared to approximately 125 km in Duan et al. (2010) for q=3.5 spectra)
- With L_x suppressed by a factor of 0.7, the starting radius became 70 km for inverted hierarchy, while collective oscillations disappeared for normal hierarchy
- Neutrino capture rates increased by up to factors of two to three due to collective oscillations
- The atmospheric mass splitting used: Delta m^2 = 2.7 x 10^{-3} eV^2; the solar mass splitting: approximately 7.7 x 10^{-5} eV^2
- The unknown mixing angle theta_13 was set to 0.01

## Context

This work extends the collective neutrino oscillation calculations of Duan et al. (2006, 2010) by coupling them to r-process nucleosynthesis for the first time with proper multiangle treatment, going beyond the earlier equilibrium electron fraction estimates of Balantekin and Yuksel (2004) and Chakraborty et al. (2009). The results show that the single-angle approximation used in many previous studies makes grossly inaccurate predictions for r-process yields, and that accurate modeling requires supercomputer calculations of multiangle collective oscillations. The framework established here enables future work to incorporate more realistic supernova conditions and to apply similar methods to other hot outflow environments such as neutron star mergers.
