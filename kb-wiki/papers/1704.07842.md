# Probing the spacetime evolution of heavy ion collisions with photons and dileptons

> Photons and dileptons are emitted throughout the evolution of the deconfined nuclear medium produced in heavy ion collisions. As such they can provide valuable information about the different phases of the medium, and complement hadronic measurements and other observables. In this work, recent developments related to electromagnetic emissions at early time, in the cross-over region, and at late times are reviewed. The spacetime description of the nuclear medium from a hydrodynamic model of heavy ion collisions is used to provide context and guide the discussion.

## Key claim

Photons and dileptons can provide direct information about the different phases of the deconfined nuclear medium produced in heavy ion collisions, complementing hadronic measurements, but their interpretation depends critically on the assumption that the medium is close to local thermal equilibrium for most of its evolution, an assumption that becomes uncertain during the confinement transition.

## Method

The paper reviews recent theoretical developments in electromagnetic emissions from heavy ion collisions, using a viscous relativistic hydrodynamic model of the medium's spacetime evolution as a framework. The author examines photon and dilepton production at three stages: early times (pre-hydrodynamic phase, tau less than about 0.1-1 fm), during the hydrodynamic evolution through the cross-over region (temperatures roughly 150-400 MeV), and at late times after confinement (below about 150 MeV). The review connects these emission sources to specific challenges in understanding the quark-gluon plasma, including thermalization, flow development, and the nature of the QCD cross-over.

## Key numbers

* The hydrodynamic evolution of the medium spans from tau ~ 0.1 fm to ~10 fm, with the initial time for the simulation shown being 0.4 fm.
* Confinement occurs progressively in a range of temperatures of order 150-200 MeV for an equilibrated QCD medium without net baryon density.
* The thermal photon v2(EP) grows rapidly with time and is roughly saturated after about 5-6 fm of evolution.
* By tau ~ 2-3 fm, most of the nuclear medium is below a temperature of 300 MeV; by tau ~ 5-6 fm, most of it is below 200 MeV.
* Prompt photons dominate photon measurements at transverse momenta above about 4-5 GeV once hadronic decays are subtracted.
* The dielectron invariant mass range of interest for studying the deconfined medium is 0.3 GeV less than M(e+e-) less than 1 GeV.

## Context

This review extends the line of work using electromagnetic probes to study heavy ion collisions, building on hydrodynamic models that have been successful in describing soft hadron measurements (Gale 2013, Heinz 2013, deSouza 2015). It discusses recent investigations of early-time electromagnetic emissions (Greif 2017, Berges 2017, Oliva 2017) and compares with non-equilibrium descriptions (Linnyk 2015). The paper highlights ongoing challenges in evaluating thermal electromagnetic emission rates at moderate temperatures (150-400 MeV) where the strong coupling is not small, referencing lattice calculations in the quenched limit (Ghiglieri 2016), holographic calculations (Finazzo 2015, Iatrakis 2016), and effective models (Gale 2014, Lee 2014). The work enables future systematic comparisons between electromagnetic observables across different collision energies and system sizes, from SPS and HADES through RHIC beam energy scan to LHC energies.
