# The nuclear matter phase diagram, multifragmentation, Zipf's law

> Some of the progress in determining the phase boundaries of the nuclear phase diagram, the location of the critical point of the nuclear fragmentation phase transition, and the values of the critical exponents of this transition is reviewed. The recently postulated connection of the size of the largest fragments as a function of their rank with Zipf's law known from linguistics is discussed, and an exact formula is derived. We show that Zipf's law is not strictly observed in nuclear fragmentation, but that one can derive useful approximations.

## Key claim
The paper shows that Zipf's law is not strictly observed in nuclear fragmentation, but that the cluster size distributions instead follow the more general Zipf-Mandelbrot distribution, with the exponent λ approximated as a function of the critical exponent τ.

## Method
The authors derive exact analytical expressions for the average size of the largest clusters in a fragment distribution at the critical point, assuming Poissonian probabilities for cluster sizes generated by a scaling theory. They then compute the ratio of the average size of the largest cluster to that of the cluster of rank r for different values of the critical exponent τ and a system size of 10,000 constituents, comparing these results to the expectation from Zipf's law.

## Key numbers
- Critical parameters obtained from 10.8 GeV pion-induced gold fragmentation data: σ = 0.5 ± 0.1, τ = 2.35 ± 0.05, and Tc = 8.3 ± 0.2 MeV.
- The exponent λ in the Zipf-Mandelbrot distribution is asymptotically approximated as λ(τ) = 1/(τ−1).
- System size used in the exact simulations: 10,000 constituents.

## Context
This work reviews progress in determining the nuclear matter phase diagram, the critical point of the nuclear fragmentation phase transition, and the critical exponents of this transition. It extends the percolation model of nuclear fragmentation introduced by Bauer (1984-1988) and the scaling analysis of experimental fragment size distributions performed by Kbb (2002). The paper enables future use of the Zipf-Mandelbrot distribution as a more accurate tool for detecting critical behavior in nuclear fragmentation experiments, rather than the simpler Zipf's law proposed by Watanabe and Ma (2005).
