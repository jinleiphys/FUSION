# Open bottom states and the anti-B meson propagation in hadronic matter

> The interaction and propagation of anti-B mesons with light mesons, N and Delta baryons is studied within a unitarized approach based on effective models that are compatible with chiral and heavy-quark symmetries. We find several heavy-quark spin doublets in the open-bottom sectors, where anti-B and anti-B* mesons are present. In the meson sector we find several resonant states, among them, a B0 and a B1 with masses 5530 MeV and 5579 MeV as well as Bs0* and Bs1* narrow states at 5748 MeV and 5799 MeV, respectively. They form two doublets with no experimental identification yet, the first one being the bottom counterpart of the D0(2400) and D1(2430) states, and the second bottom doublet associated to the ubiquitous Ds0* (2317) and the Ds1 (2460). In the baryon sector, several Lambda_b and Sigma_b doublets are identified, among them the one given by the experimental Lambda_b(5910) and Lambda*_b(5921). Moreover, one of our states, the Sigma_b*(5904), turns out to be the bottom counterpart of the Sigma*(1670) and Sigma_c*(2549), which is a case for discovery. We finally analyze different transport coefficients for the anti-B meson in hot matter, such as formed in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. For RHIC/LHC energies, the main contribution to the coefficients comes from the interaction of anti-B mesons with pions. However, we also include the effects of baryonic density which might be sizable at temperatures T < 100 MeV, as the chemical potential is expected to increase in the last stages of the expansion. We conclude that although the relaxation time decreases with larger baryonic densities, the anti-B meson does not thermalize at RHIC/LHC energies, representing an ideal probe for the initial bottom distribution.

## Key claim

The paper claims that several predicted open-bottom meson and baryon resonances form heavy-quark spin symmetry doublets, and that the anti-B meson does not thermalize in the hadronic matter created at RHIC and LHC energies, even when baryonic density effects are included at temperatures below 100 MeV.

## Method

The authors use a unitarized coupled-channel Bethe-Salpeter approach with effective interactions that respect both chiral symmetry and heavy-quark spin symmetry (HQSS). For the meson sector, they employ a leading-order (LO) heavy-quark expansion with next-to-leading order (NLO) chiral terms, while for the baryon sector they use an SU(6)×HQSS Weinberg-Tomozawa type interaction. The scattering amplitudes are solved on-shell, and poles are extracted from the first and second Riemann sheets to identify dynamically-generated resonances. Transport coefficients (drag, diffusion, relaxation time) for the anti-B meson are then computed from these unitarized scattering amplitudes in a hot hadronic medium.

## Key numbers

- B0 resonance mass: 5530.3 MeV with width 238.5 MeV (J=0, S=0, I=1/2)
- B1 resonance mass: 5579.2 MeV with width 251.9 MeV (J=1, S=0, I=1/2)
- Bs0* narrow state mass: 5747.6 MeV with width 0.0 MeV (J=0, S=1, I=0)
- Bs1* narrow state mass: 5798.7 MeV with width 0.0 MeV (J=1, S=1, I=0)
- Lambda_b(5910) state mass: 5910.1 MeV with width 0.0 MeV (J=1/2, I=0)
- Lambda*_b(5921) state mass: 5921.5 MeV with width 0.0 MeV (J=3/2, I=0)
- Sigma_b*(5904) state mass: 5904 MeV (identified as bottom counterpart of Sigma*(1670) and Sigma_c*(2549))
- Subtraction constant for meson-meson loop: a(mu) = -3.38 at mu = 1 GeV with cutoff Lambda = 770 MeV
- Renormalization parameter for baryon sector: alpha = 0.967

## Context

This work extends previous unitarized studies of D meson propagation in hadronic matter (Abreu 2011, Tolos 2013, Torres-Rincon 2013) to the bottom sector, and continues the baryon resonance analysis of Garcia-Recio (2012) for open-bottom states. The predicted B0 and B1 states are identified as the bottom counterparts of the experimental D0(2400) and D1(2430), while the Bs0* and Bs1* states correspond to the Ds0*(2317) and Ds1(2460). The finding that the anti-B meson does not thermalize at RHIC/LHC energies enables its use as a probe of the initial bottom quark distribution in heavy-ion collisions, in contrast to lighter heavy-flavor mesons.
