# Search for the chiral magnetic effect in collisions between two isobars with deformed and neutron-rich nuclear structures

> Isobar collisions which were thought to have the same background and different magnetic fields provide an opportunity to verify the chiral magnetic effect (CME) in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. However, the first result from the RHIC-STAR isobar experiment did not observe the CME signal, but discovered that the backgrounds are different between $_{44}^{96}\textrm{Ru}+_{44}^{96}\textrm{Ru}$ and $_{40}^{96}\textrm{Zr}+_{40}^{96}\textrm{Zr}$ collisions. We test eighteen cases of Woods-Saxon parameter settings resulting from different nuclear deformation or nuclear structure effects using a mutiphase transport model. We find out that seven cases can reasonably reproduce three reference ratios measured by the STAR experiment. Considering both the halo-type neutron skin structure and CME-like charge separation, we demonstrate that it is difficult for the CME observables ($Δδ$, $Δδ$ ratio, $Δγ$ and $Δγ$ ratio) to distinguish the presence or absence of the CME, if the CME strength is weak in isobar collisions. It is because the final state interactions significantly weaken the initial CME signal, resulting in non-linear sensitivities of the CME observables to the CME strength. Therefore, more sensitive observables are required to search for the possible small CME signal in isobar collisions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that even after accounting for nuclear deformation and neutron-skin effects in isobar collisions, the chiral magnetic effect (CME) observables cannot distinguish the presence or absence of a weak CME signal because final-state interactions significantly weaken the initial signal, resulting in non-linear sensitivities of the observables.

## Method

The authors used a multiphase transport (AMPT) model to simulate collisions between two isobars, ⁹⁶Ru and ⁹⁶Zr, at √s = 200 GeV. They tested eighteen different Woods-Saxon parameter settings incorporating various nuclear deformation parameters (quadrupole β₂ and octupole β₃) and neutron-skin structures, then selected the best-performing configuration (halo-type neutron skin) via chi-square tests against STAR experimental data. They introduced a CME-like charge separation into the initial parton cascade stage by tuning the percentage f of quarks participating in charge separation, with f for Ru+Ru set to 1.15 times that for Zr+Zr, and compared the resulting Δδ, Δγ, and their ratios to STAR measurements.

## Key numbers

- Seven of eighteen Woods-Saxon parameter cases (Case 3, Case 4, Case 5, Case 7, Case 9, Case 10, and the halo-type case) could reasonably reproduce the three reference ratios measured by STAR.
- The halo-type neutron skin case had the minimum chi-square value of 0.047 among all eighteen cases.
- The ratio of charge separation percentages f_Ru+Ru / f_Zr+Zr was set to 1.15 for all centrality bins; for example, f=10% means f_Ru+Ru=10% and f_Zr+Zr=8.7%.
- The AMPT model with CME strength f=2% or f=5% produced Δγ results almost identical to the no-CME case (f=0), all consistent with STAR data, while f=7.5% or f=10% gave ratios too large for the 20-50% centrality bin.
- The AMPT simulation would require at least about 1.8×10⁹ events to distinguish a small CME intensity at the level of the STAR statistical errors.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the RHIC-STAR isobar experiment (STAR, 2021), which found that the ratio of CME observables between Ru+Ru and Zr+Zr collisions was less than unity, contrary to expectations. The study builds on prior work by Xu (2021) and Jia (2021) that introduced nuclear deformation and neutron-skin effects to explain the observed differences in multiplicity and flow between the two isobar systems. The results are consistent with simulations from event-by-event anomalous-viscous fluid dynamics (EBE-AVFD) by Kharzeev (2022), which suggested a limited CME signal contribution of about 2%. The paper concludes that more sensitive observables are needed to search for possible small CME signals in isobar collisions.
