# Thermal photons as a quark-gluon plasma thermometer revisited

> Photons are a penetrating probe of the hot medium formed in heavy-ion collisions, but they are emitted from all collision stages. At photon energies below 2-3 GeV, the measured photon spectra are approximately exponential and can be characterized by their inverse logarithmic slope, often called "effective temperature" $T_\mathrm{eff}$. Modelling the evolution of the radiating medium hydrodynamically, we analyze the factors controlling the value of $T_\mathrm{eff}$ and how it is related to the evolving true temperature $T$ of the fireball. We find that at RHIC and LHC energies most photons are emitted from fireball regions with $T{\,\sim\,}T_\mathrm{c}$ near the quark-hadron phase transition, but that their effective temperature is significantly enhanced by strong radial flow. Although a very hot, high pressure early collision stage is required for generating this radial flow, we demonstrate that the experimentally measured large effective photon temperatures $T_\mathrm{eff}{\,>\,}T_\mathrm{c}$, taken alone, do not prove that any electromagnetic radiation was actually emitted from regions with true temperatures well above $T_\mathrm{c}$. We explore tools that can help to provide additional evidence for the relative weight of photon emission from the early quark-gluon and late hadronic phases. We find that the recently measured centrality dependence of the total thermal photon yield requires a larger contribution from late emission than presently encoded in our hydrodynamic model.

## Key claim
The large effective temperatures of thermal photons measured in heavy-ion collisions do not directly prove emission from quark-gluon plasma with true temperatures well above the critical temperature \(T_\mathrm{c}\); instead, these high effective temperatures are significantly enhanced by strong radial flow, and most photons are emitted from fireball regions with temperatures near \(T_\mathrm{c}\).

## Method
The authors used the boost-invariant viscous hydrodynamic code VISH2+1 with ensemble-averaged Monte-Carlo Glauber initial conditions, a shear viscosity to entropy density ratio \(\eta/s = 0.08\), and a lattice-based equation of state (s95p-PCE-v0) with chemical freeze-out at \(T_\mathrm{chem} = 165\) MeV. Photon emission rates included viscous corrections linear in the shear stress tensor for \(2\to2\) scattering processes in the quark-gluon plasma and a meson gas description for the hadronic phase, with linear interpolation between the two rate sets in the temperature window 184–220 MeV. The hydrodynamic evolution started at \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm/\(c\) and ended on an isothermal freeze-out surface at \(T_\mathrm{dec} = 120\) MeV.

## Key numbers
- Peak initial temperature in the fireball center: \(T_0 = 452\) MeV (energy density \(e_0 = 62\) GeV/fm\(^3\)) for LHC Pb+Pb at 0–40% centrality; \(T_0 = 370\) MeV (\(e_0 = 35\) GeV/fm\(^3\)) for RHIC Au+Au at 0–20% centrality.
- Experimental effective temperatures: PHENIX reported \(T_\mathrm{eff} = 221 \pm 19 \pm 19\) MeV for Au+Au 0–20% centrality at RHIC; ALICE reported \(T_\mathrm{eff} = 304 \pm 51^\mathrm{sys.+stat.}\) MeV for Pb+Pb 0–40% centrality at the LHC.
- Critical temperature range for chiral restoration and color deconfinement: \(T_\mathrm{c} \simeq 155\)–170 MeV.
- Fraction of total photon yield emitted from cells with temperature \(T > 250\) MeV: 21% for RHIC Au+Au 0–20% centrality; 32% for LHC Pb+Pb 0–40% centrality.
- Fraction of total photon yield emitted from times \(\tau < 2.0\) fm/\(c\): 28.5% for RHIC; 26% for LHC.
- Power-law scaling of thermal photon yield (above \(p_T > 0.6\) GeV) with number of participants \(N_\mathrm{part}\): exponent 1.7 from the model, compared to the experimentally measured value of \(1.48 \pm 0.08 \pm 0.04\) from PHENIX.
- Scaling powers for hadronic gas (HG) photons above \(p_T > 0.4\) GeV: \(N_\mathrm{part}\) exponent 1.46, charged multiplicity exponent 1.23; for QGP photons: \(N_\mathrm{part}\) exponent 2.05, charged multiplicity exponent 1.83.

## Context
This work extends earlier hydrodynamic modeling of thermal photon production (Turbide 2007, Qin 2009, Chatterjee 2013) and directly addresses the interpretation of effective temperatures measured by PHENIX (Adare 2008) and ALICE (Wilde 2012). The finding that most photons are emitted from temperatures near \(T_\mathrm{c}\) and that radial flow boosts their effective temperature is consistent with earlier arguments by van Hees (2011) and Rapp (2011) based on simpler fireball models. The result that the centrality dependence of thermal photon yields is closer to that of hadronic gas photons than QGP photons suggests that current hydrodynamic models underestimate late-stage photon emission, which also connects to the unexpectedly large photon elliptic flow measured by PHENIX (Adare 2011) and ALICE (Lohner 2012). The study enables future work using transverse momentum cuts to isolate photon emission from different temperature regions and to test scenarios such as delayed chemical equilibration of quarks.
