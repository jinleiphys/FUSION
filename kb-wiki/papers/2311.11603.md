# Shakhov-type extension of the relaxation time approximation in relativistic kinetic theory and second-order fluid dynamics

> We present a relativistic Shakhov-type generalization of the Anderson-Witting relaxation time model for the Boltzmann collision integral to modify the ratio of momentum diffusivity to thermal diffusivity. This is achieved by modifying the path on which the single particle distribution function $f_{\mathbf{k}}$ approaches local equilibrium $f_{0\mathbf{k}}$ by constructing an intermediate Shakhov-type distribution $f_{\mathrm{S}\mathbf{k}}$ similar to the 14-moment approximation of Israel and Stewart. We illustrate the effectiveness of this model in case of the Bjorken expansion of an ideal gas of massive particles and the damping of longitudinal waves through an ultrarelativistic ideal gas.

# Shakhov-type extension of the relaxation time approximation in relativistic kinetic theory and second-order fluid dynamics

## Key claim

The paper introduces a relativistic generalization of the Shakhov model that extends the Anderson-Witting relaxation time approximation (RTA) by allowing independent control of the first-order transport coefficients (bulk viscosity, particle diffusion, and shear viscosity) through additional relaxation times, while maintaining a momentum-independent relaxation time. The model modifies the path by which the distribution function approaches local equilibrium using an intermediate Shakhov-type distribution constructed similarly to the 14-moment approximation of Israel and Stewart.

## Method

The authors replace the Anderson-Witting collision term with a Shakhov-type term where the distribution function relaxes toward an intermediate Shakhov distribution f_{S k} rather than directly toward the local equilibrium distribution f_{0 k}. The Shakhov distribution is constructed using a near-equilibrium expansion analogous to the Grad and Israel-Stewart 14-moment approximation, with polynomials H^{(ℓ)}_{k 0} in energy chosen to satisfy matching conditions for particle density, energy density, and the Landau frame definition. The Chapman-Enskog method is applied to derive first-order constitutive relations, and the method of moments is used to obtain second-order fluid-dynamical relaxation equations. The model is validated through numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation for two test cases: the (0+1)-dimensional Bjorken expansion of massive particles and the damping of longitudinal waves in an ultrarelativistic gas.

## Key numbers

- For the Bjorken flow simulations: particle rest mass m₀ = 1 GeV/c², initial proper time τ₀ = 0.5 fm/c, initial temperature β₀⁻¹ = 0.5 GeV, relaxation times τ_π = 0.5 fm/c and τ_Π = 0.5 fm/c for the reference Anderson-Witting case, giving 4π η/s ≈ 2.6 at initial time
- For the Bjorken flow: the ratio τ_Π/τ_π is varied among values 3, 1, and 1/3, with τ_π = 0.5 fm/c fixed in one set and τ_Π = 0.5 fm/c fixed in another set
- For longitudinal waves: initial temperature β₀⁻¹ = 0.6 GeV, wave number k = 2π/L with L = 6.4 fm, initial perturbations δn₀/n₀ = δv₀/c = 10⁻³
- For longitudinal waves: fixed kτ_π = 0.4 gives 4π η/s ≈ 3.11; τ_V/τ_π varied among values 3, 1, and 1/3 with kτ_π = 0.4 fixed, and τ_π/τ_V varied with kτ_V = 0.4 fixed

## Context

This work extends the non-relativistic Shakhov model (Shakhov 1968) to the relativistic domain, building upon the Anderson-Witting relaxation time approximation (Anderson and Witting 1974) which is the relativistic generalization of the Bhatnagar-Gross-Krook (BGK) model (Bhatnagar 1954). The standard Anderson-Witting model fixes the Prandtl number at 1, while most ideal gases have Pr = 2/3; the Shakhov extension remedies this by introducing additional parameters. The model provides an alternative to energy-dependent relaxation time approaches (Rocha 2021, Dash 2021) for tuning transport coefficients, and enables systematic comparison with second-order fluid dynamics (Israel and Stewart 1979, Denicol 2012) in heavy-ion collision phenomenology, particularly for studying baryon diffusion (Denicol 2018) and bulk and shear viscosity effects (Gardim 2020, Hirvonen 2022).
