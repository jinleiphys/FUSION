# Non-symmetrized hyperspherical harmonic basis for $A$-bodies

> The use of the hyperspherical harmonic (HH) basis in the description of bound states in an $A$-body system composed by identical particles is normally preceded by a symmetrization procedure in which the statistic of the system is taken into account. This preliminary step is not strictly necessary; the direct use of the HH basis is possible, even if the basis has not a well defined behavior under particle permutations. In fact, after the diagonalization of the Hamiltonian matrix, the eigenvectors reflect the symmetries present in it. They have well defined symmetry under particle permutation and the identification of the physical states is possible, as it will be shown in specific cases. The problem related to the large degeneration of the basis is circumvented by constructing the Hamiltonian matrix as a sum of products of sparse matrices. This particular representation of the Hamiltonian is well suited for a numerical iterative diagonalization, where only the action of the matrix on a vector is needed. As an example we compute bound states for systems with $A=3-6$ particles interacting through a short-range central interaction. We also consider the case in which the potential is restricted to act in relative s-waves with and without the inclusion of the Coulomb potential. This very simple model predicts results in qualitative good agreement with the experimental data and it represents a first step in a project dedicated to the use of the HH basis to describe bound and low energy scattering states in light nuclei.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that a non-symmetrized hyperspherical harmonic (HH) basis can be used directly for A-body bound-state calculations without prior symmetrization, because the Hamiltonian diagonalization automatically yields eigenvectors with the correct permutation symmetry. The method is feasible for A=3–6 systems despite the large basis degeneracy, provided the Hamiltonian matrix is constructed as a sum of products of sparse matrices suitable for iterative diagonalization.

## Method
The Hamiltonian matrix is built using a non-symmetrized HH basis in which basis elements do not have well-defined behavior under particle permutations. The potential energy matrix for each pair interaction V(i,j) is expressed by rotating the reference HH basis to a coordinate system where particles i and j have well-defined quantum numbers, using rotation matrices that factor into products of sparse matrices (the A-coefficients). The kinetic energy is treated analytically, and the full Hamiltonian matrix is diagonalized using iterative methods (e.g., symmetry-adapted Lanczos) to extract eigenvectors with the desired permutation symmetry.

## Key numbers
- The Volkov potential parameters: V_R = 144.86 MeV, R_1 = 0.82 fm, V_A = -83.34 MeV, R_2 = 1.6 fm; with ℏ²/m = 41.47 MeV·fm⁻².
- The two-nucleon system using this potential has binding energy E_2N = 0.54592 MeV and scattering length a_2N = 10.082 fm.
- Bound states are computed for systems with A = 3, 4, 5, and 6 particles interacting through a short-range central interaction, with and without the Coulomb potential and with s-wave projection.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous use of non-symmetrized HH bases for three- and four-particle systems (Gattobigio et al., 2009) to A > 4 systems. It contrasts with standard approaches that require explicit symmetrization of the HH basis (e.g., Barnea et al., 1997; Novoselsky et al., 1994) and with methods like the Green function Monte Carlo (GFMC) and no-core shell model (NCSM) used for light nuclei up to A=12. The technique enables future application to bound and low-energy scattering states in light nuclei using realistic interactions, including charge-symmetry-breaking terms that mix different isospin components.
