# Statistical Nuclear Spectroscopy with $q$-normal and bivariate $q$-normal distributions and $q$-Hermite polynomials

> Statistical nuclear spectroscopy (also called spectral distribution method), introduced by J.B. French in late 60's and developed in detail in the later years by his group and many other groups, is based on the Gaussian forms for the state (eigenvalue) and transition strength densities in shell model spaces with their extension to partial densities defined over shell model subspaces. The Gaussian forms have their basis in embedded random matrix ensembles with nuclear Hamiltonians consisting of a mean-field one-body part and a residual two-body part. However, following the recent random matrix results for the so called Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev model due to Verbaaarschot et al, embedded random matrix ensembles with $k$-body interactions are re-examined and it is shown that the density of states, transition strength densities and strength functions (partial densities) in fact follow more closely the $q$-normal distribution (the parameter $q$ is related to the fourth moment of these distributions with $q=1$ giving Gaussian and $q=0$ giving semi-circle form). The $q$-normal has the important property that it is bounded for $0 \le q < 1$. The $q$-normal (also its bivariate and general multi-variate extensions) and the associated $q$-Hermite polynomials are studied for their properties by Bryc, Szabowski and others [P.J. Szabowski, Electronic Journal of Probability {\bf 15}, 1296 (2010)]. Following these, in the present article developed is statistical nuclear spectroscopy based on $q$-normal (univariate and bivariate) distributions and the associated $q$-Hermite polynomials. In particular, formulation is presented for nuclear level densities, shell model orbit occupancies, transition strengths (for electromagnetic and $β$ and double $β$-decay type operators) and strength sums.

## Key claim

The paper argues that statistical nuclear spectroscopy, traditionally based on Gaussian distributions for eigenvalue and transition strength densities, should instead be reformulated using $q$-normal distributions and $q$-Hermite polynomials, because embedded random matrix ensembles with $k$-body interactions generate densities that follow the $q$-normal form more closely than the Gaussian, with the parameter $q$ (related to the fourth moment) ranging from $q=1$ (Gaussian) to $q=0$ (semi-circle) and providing a natural bounded support for the distributions.

## Method

The authors develop a formulation for statistical nuclear spectroscopy using univariate and bivariate $q$-normal distributions and the associated $q$-Hermite polynomials, building on recent results from the Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev model and embedded random matrix ensembles. They present explicit formulas for nuclear level densities by decomposing the total state density into partial densities over proton-neutron configurations, approximating each partial density by a $q$-normal distribution with centroid and variance computed from the Hamiltonian moments, and optionally adding Gram-Charlier corrections using $q$-Hermite polynomials. For shell model orbit occupancies, they derive expansions using $q$-Hermite polynomials and the Poisson-Mehler formula, and for transition strengths they employ the bivariate $q$-normal distribution with a correlation coefficient $\xi$.

## Key numbers

- The $q$-normal distribution $f_{qN}(x|q)$ is defined over the interval $(-2/\sqrt{1-q}, +2/\sqrt{1-q})$; for $q=1$ it reduces to the Gaussian $(1/\sqrt{2\pi})\exp(-x^2/2)$ and for $q=0$ to the semi-circle $(1/2\pi)\sqrt{4-x^2}$.
- The fourth reduced central moment of the $q$-normal distribution is $\mu_4(q) = 2+q$, the sixth is $\mu_6(q) = 5+6q+3q^2+q^3$, and the eighth is $\mu_8(q) = 14+28q+28q^2+20q^3+10q^4+4q^5+q^6$.
- For the conditional $q$-normal distribution, the centroid is $\epsilon(y:\xi,q) = \xi y$ and the variance is $\sigma^2(y:\xi,q) = 1-\xi^2$, with skewness $\gamma_1(y:\xi,q) = -(1-q)\xi y/\sqrt{1-\xi^2}$ and excess $\gamma_2(y:\xi,q) = (q-1) + (1-q)^2[\xi y/\sqrt{1-\xi^2}]^2 + (1-q^2)\xi^2/(1-\xi^2)$.
- For a typical value of excess $\gamma_2 = -0.3$, the $q$-normal distribution range is $-3.65 \le \hat{e} \le 3.65$; for $\gamma_2 = -0.2$ the range is $-4.47 \le \hat{e} \le 4.47$; for $\gamma_2 = -0.4$ the range is $-3.16 \le \hat{e} \le 3.16$.
- Numerical examples use a 1000-member EGOE(1+2+3) ensemble with $N=12$ single-particle states and $m=6$ fermions, with interaction strengths $\lambda_2 = 0.2, 0.3$ and $\lambda_3 = 0.05, 0.1, 0.2$.

## Context

This work extends the spectral distribution method (SDM) originally introduced by J.B. French in the late 1960s and developed by the Rochester group and others, which was based on Gaussian forms justified by embedded random matrix ensembles. The authors build on recent results by Verbaarschot and collaborators showing that the Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev model yields $q$-normal eigenvalue densities, and on mathematical properties of $q$-normal distributions and $q$-Hermite polynomials studied by Bryc, Szabowski, and Ismail. The new formulation enables natural incorporation of the fourth moment and boundedness of densities into SDM, addressing limitations of Gaussian approximations that required artificial cut-offs in prior work by Zelevinsky, Horoi, and Sen'kov. Future work should investigate the dependence of $q$ on the configuration and on the one-body and two-body parts of the Hamiltonian.
