# Soft and Hard scale QCD Dynamics in Mesons

> Using a ladder-rainbow kernel previously established for the soft scale of light quark hadrons, we explore the extension to masses and electroweak decay constants of ground state pseudoscalar and vector quarkonia and heavy-light mesons in the c- and b-quark regions. We make a systematic study of the effectiveness of a constituent mass concept as a replacement for a heavy quark dressed propagator. The difference between vector and axial vector current correlators is examined to estimate the four quark chiral condensate. The valence quark distributions, in the pion and kaon, defined in deep inelastic scattering, and measured in the Drell Yan process, are investigated with the same ladder-rainbow truncation of the Dyson-Schwinger and Bethe-Salpeter equations.

## Key claim

The ladder-rainbow truncation of Dyson-Schwinger and Bethe-Salpeter equations, previously successful for light quark hadrons, can be extended to heavy-light mesons and quarkonia in the c- and b-quark regions, but only if the infrared strength of the effective kernel is systematically reduced as quark mass increases; a constituent mass approximation for heavy quarks fails to reproduce electroweak decay constants, underestimating them by 30-70%.

## Method

The authors employ a ladder-rainbow (LR) truncation of the Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) and Bethe-Salpeter equations (BSEs) with an effective running coupling that combines an infrared enhancement term fitted to light quark observables and an ultraviolet term preserving one-loop renormalization group behavior with N_f=4 and Lambda_QCD = 0.234 GeV. For heavy quarks, they compare a constituent mass approximation (with M_c^cons = 2.0 GeV and M_b^cons = 5.3 GeV fitted to the lightest pseudoscalar) against fully dressed quark propagators from the DSE, and also investigate a schematic infrared suppression of the kernel proportional to quark mass. The valence quark distributions in pion and kaon are computed from the same LR truncation using a Minkowski-space formulation of the parton distribution function, with numerical solutions for dressed propagators and Bethe-Salpeter amplitudes evolved via leading-order DGLAP evolution.

## Key numbers

- Constituent masses fitted: M_c^cons = 2.0 GeV for c-quark, M_b^cons = 5.3 GeV for b-quark
- Quark current masses at mu = 2 GeV: m_c = 1.2 +/- 0.2 GeV, m_b = 4.2 +/- 0.2 GeV
- For heavy-light mesons with constituent mass approximation, decay constants are 30-50% below experimental values (e.g., calculated f_D = 0.154 GeV vs. experimental 0.222 GeV; calculated f_B = 0.105 GeV vs. experimental 0.176 GeV)
- For quarkonia with dynamical dressing, decay constants improve: f_eta_c = 0.387 GeV (experiment 0.340 GeV), f_J/psi = 0.415 GeV (experiment 0.411 GeV), f_eta_b = 0.692 GeV (experiment unknown), f_Upsilon = 0.682 GeV (experiment 0.708 GeV)
- With constituent mass approximation, quarkonia decay constants are 30-70% too low (e.g., f_eta_c = 0.239 GeV, f_J/psi = 0.198 GeV, f_eta_b = 0.244 GeV, f_Upsilon = 0.210 GeV)
- Four-quark condensate extracted at mu = 19 GeV is 65% greater than the vacuum saturation assumption <q qbar>^2
- Pion mass difference from Das-Guralnik-Mathur-Low-Young sum rule: calculated 4.86 MeV vs. experimental 4.43 +/- 0.03 MeV
- Model scale for parton distributions: Q_0 = 0.57 GeV, determined by matching x^n moments for n=1,2,3
- Momentum sum rule at Q_0: integral_0^1 dx x(u_pi + dbar_pi) = 0.74 for pion, 0.76 for kaon

## Context

This work extends the ladder-rainbow DSE/BSE approach previously established for light quarks by Maris and Tandy (1999, 2000) into the heavy quark domain, addressing the need to characterize the kernel's performance beyond its fitted light-quark region. The results contradict the adequacy of a simple constituent mass approximation for heavy quarks, even for b-quarks, and suggest that implicit quark-gluon vertex dressing must decrease with quark mass, consistent with the analysis of Brodsky and Shrock (2008) regarding a universal maximum wavelength from confinement. The valence quark distribution calculations significantly extend the exploratory study of Hecht et al. (2000) by using full numerical DSE solutions rather than phenomenological parameterizations, and enable future systematic inclusion of higher-order truncation effects beyond ladder-rainbow.
