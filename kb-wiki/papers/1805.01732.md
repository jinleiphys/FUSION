# Hindered alpha decays of heaviest high-K isomers

> To find candidates for long-lived high-K isomers in even-even Z=106-112 superheavy nuclei we study dominant alpha-decay channel of two- and four-quasi-particle configurations at a low excitation. Energies are calculated within the microscopic - macroscopic approach with the deformed Woods-Saxon potential. Configurations are fixed by a standard blocking procedure and their energy found by a subsequent minimization over deformations. Different excitation energies of a high-K configuration in parent and daughter nucleus seem particularly important for a hindrance of the alpha-decay. A strong hindrance is found for some four-quasi-particle states, particularly $K^π = 20^{+}$ and/or $19^{+}$ states in $^{264-270}$Ds. Contrary to what was suggested in experimental papers, it is rather a proton configuration that leads to this strong hindrance. If not shortened by the electromagnetic decay, alpha half-lives of $\sim$ 1 s could open new possibilities for studies of chemical/atomic properties of related elements.

# Hindered alpha decays of heaviest high-K isomers

## Key claim
Strong hindrance of alpha decay in four-quasi-particle states of certain superheavy nuclei (particularly $^{264-270}$Ds) arises primarily from the proton two-quasi-particle component, contrary to earlier experimental suggestions that attributed it to neutron configurations. The hindrance depends critically on the difference in excitation energies of the same high-K configuration between parent and daughter nuclei.

## Method
The authors used a microscopic-macroscopic approach with a deformed Woods-Saxon potential, performing four-dimensional minimizations over deformations $\beta_{20}$ through $\beta_{80}$ for each blocked configuration. They calculated excitation energies of two- and four-quasi-particle states in even-even superheavy nuclei with Z=106-112, then computed alpha-decay hindrance factors by separating contributions from changes in $Q_{\alpha}$, angular momentum (centrifugal barrier via WKB approximation), and structural overlap (using values from Delion et al.). The analysis focused on configuration-preserving transitions between identical multi-quasiparticle states in parent and daughter nuclei.

## Key numbers
- Excitation energy of the four-quasi-particle $K^{\pi}=20^{+}$ state in $^{264-270}$Ds is 1.4 MeV (sum of individual single-particle excitations with nearly vanishing pairing gap)
- For the two-proton $K^{\pi}=10^{-}\pi$ configuration in $^{270}$Ds: $Log_{10}[HF]_{Q}=5.44$ (hindrance from $Q_{\alpha}$ change alone), total $Log_{10}[HF]_{TOT}=5.44$ for $ex\rightarrow ex$ transitions
- For the two-neutron $K^{\pi}=10^{-}\nu$ configuration in $^{270}$Ds: $Log_{10}[HF]_{Q}=0$ (no hindrance from $Q_{\alpha}$ change), total $Log_{10}[HF]_{TOT}=0$ for $ex\rightarrow ex$ transitions
- Centrifugal barrier for $\Delta L=20\hbar$ (four-quasi-particle state): $V_{L}=4.5$ MeV, giving $HF_{L}=10^{12}$
- For $^{270}$Ds four-quasi-particle state: $Q_{\alpha}=9.36$ MeV, $\Delta Q_{\alpha}=-2.02$ MeV relative to ground-state transition, $Log_{10}[HF]=5.13$ to 6.75 depending on method
- For $^{270}$Cn four-quasi-particle state: $Q_{\alpha}=13.06$ MeV, $\Delta Q_{\alpha}=+0.48$ MeV, $Log_{10}[HF]=-0.87$ to -0.92 (decay faster than ground state)
- Predicted alpha half-life of four-quasi-particle state in $^{264-270}$Ds: approximately 1 second (compared to ground-state half-life of ~0.1 $\mu$s for $^{264}$Ds and $^{270}$Ds)
- Maximum hindrance factor for four-quasi-particle state: $10^{8}$ predicted for $^{264}$Ds

## Context
This work extends theoretical studies of high-K isomerism in superheavy nuclei, following prior predictions by Liu et al. (2011) who identified candidates in Ds and Cn isotopes using the Nilsson-Strutinsky approach with Lipkin-Nogami pairing. The authors contradict the experimental assignment by Hofmann et al. (2001) for the $^{270}$Ds isomer, which proposed a two-neutron $10^{-}$ configuration; instead, they find that proton configurations drive the hindrance. The results also disagree with Liu et al. (2011) regarding Cn isotopes, where the present model predicts no hindrance (or faster decay) for $^{268}$Cn and $^{270}$Cn. The predicted ~1 s alpha half-lives for four-quasi-particle states in $^{264-270}$Ds, if not shortened by electromagnetic decay, could enable chemical and atomic studies of these elements that are impractical for their short-lived ground states.
