# Applications of baryon chiral perturbation theory. A topical example: The nucleon sigma terms

> We present an overview of modern approaches to low-energy baryon structure based on baryon chiral perturbation theory. These are driven by the emergence of Lorentz covariant schemes and the systematic consideration of the effects of the lowest-lying decuplet resonances. In order to illustrate the progress recently achieved in this field, we present the last developments on our understanding of the nucleon sigma terms along these lines. In particular, we will show how these methods, in SU(2) or SU(3) settings, are reliable tools to process and maximize the information on the physical structure of the nucleon one can obtain from either experimental data or lattice QCD results.

# Applications of baryon chiral perturbation theory. A topical example: The nucleon sigma terms

## Key claim
Lorentz covariant baryon chiral perturbation theory (B$\chi$PT) schemes, when combined with explicit inclusion of the $\Delta(1232)$ resonance in a $\delta$-counting, can reliably extract the nucleon sigma terms from either experimental $\pi N$ scattering data or lattice QCD results, but the extracted values depend on which partial-wave analysis is used as input and on whether decuplet contributions are included.

## Method
The author reviews modern B$\chi$PT approaches using the extended-on-mass-shell (EOMS) renormalization scheme, which preserves the analytic structure of baryon propagators while restoring power counting. For $\pi N$ scattering, the $\Delta(1232)$ is included as an explicit degree of freedom in a $\delta$-counting ($\delta \sim M_\Delta - M_N \sim 300$ MeV counted as $\mathcal{O}(p^{1/2})$), and the amplitude is computed up to $\mathcal{O}(p^3)$. For the baryon-octet mass splittings, $SU(3)_F$ B$\chi$PT is applied at $\mathcal{O}(p^3)$ and $\mathcal{O}(p^4)$ in the EOMS scheme, with and without decuplet contributions, to determine $\sigma_0$ and the strangeness content $y$.

## Key numbers
- $\sigma_{\pi N} = 59(7)$ MeV from the chiral analysis of GW and Matsinos partial-wave analyses, including theoretical uncertainty from higher-order graphs and dispersion of values.
- $\sigma_{\pi N} = 43(5)$ MeV from the Karlsruhe-Helsinki (KH) partial-wave analysis.
- $\sigma_0 = 46(8)$ MeV from covariant $SU(3)$ B$\chi$PT at $\mathcal{O}(p^3)$ with octet contributions only; $\sigma_0 = 58(8)$ MeV with octet plus decuplet contributions.
- Strangeness content $y = 0.02(13)$ obtained by combining $\sigma_{\pi N} = 59(7)$ MeV with the covariant $\sigma_0$ result including decuplet.
- From lattice QCD fits (PACS-CS data) in covariant $SU(3)$ B$\chi$PT at $\mathcal{O}(p^3)$: $\sigma_{\pi N} = 59(2)$ MeV with decuplet, $\sigma_{\pi N} = 46(2)$ MeV without decuplet; $\sigma_{sN} = -7(23)$ MeV with decuplet, $\sigma_{sN} = 28(23)$ MeV without decuplet.
- From lattice QCD fits (LHPC data) at $\mathcal{O}(p^3)$: $\sigma_{\pi N} = 61(2)$ MeV with decuplet, $\sigma_{\pi N} = 43(2)$ MeV without decuplet.
- At $\mathcal{O}(p^4)$ without decuplet: $\sigma_{\pi N} = 43(1)(6)$ MeV and $\sigma_{sN} = 126(24)(54)$ MeV (Ren et al.); Semke et al. report $\sigma_{\pi N} = 32(2)$ MeV and $\sigma_{sN} = 22(20)$ MeV at the same order.

## Context
This work reviews and extends the line of baryon chiral perturbation theory calculations of nucleon sigma terms, which began with Gasser (1982) using early B$\chi$PT with form factors ($\sigma_0 = 35(5)$ MeV) and continued with heavy-baryon calculations by Borasoy and Meissner (1996) ($\sigma_0 = 36(7)$ MeV). The present review emphasizes that Lorentz covariant schemes (EOMS) resolve convergence problems seen in heavy-baryon expansions, particularly in $SU(3)$ applications where the HB approach showed poor convergence (Geng et al., 2008). The results enable reliable extraction of sigma terms from lattice QCD data and resolve the apparent tension between a $\sigma_{\pi N} \sim 60$ MeV and a small strangeness content of the nucleon. Open questions remain at $\mathcal{O}(p^4)$, where different groups report inconsistent values for $\sigma_{\pi N}$ and $\sigma_{sN}$, requiring further lattice data and cross-checks with cutoff regularization approaches (Young et al., 2009).
