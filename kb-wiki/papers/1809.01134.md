# Neutron star cooling with a dynamic stellar structure

> The observations combined with theory of neutron star (NS) cooling play a crucial role in achieving the intriguing information of the stellar interior, such as the equation of state (EOS), composition and superfluidity of dense matter. The traditional NS cooling theory is based on the assumption that the stellar structure does not change with time. The validity of such a static description has not yet been confirmed. We generalize the theory to a dynamic treatment; that is, continuous change of the NS structure (rearrangement of the stellar density distribution with the total baryon number fixed) as the decrease of temperature during the thermal evolution, is taken into account. It is found that the practical thermal energy used for the cooling is slightly lower than that is estimated in static situation, and hence the cooling of NSs is accelerated correspondingly but the effect is rather weak. Therefore, the static treatment is a good approximation in the calculations of NS cooling.

# Neutron star cooling with a dynamic stellar structure

## Key claim

The traditional assumption that neutron star structure does not change during cooling is a good approximation, because the dynamic treatment (where the stellar density distribution continuously rearranges with decreasing temperature) shows that the practical thermal energy available for cooling is only slightly lower than the static estimate, leading to a rather weak acceleration of cooling.

## Method

The authors generalized the standard neutron star cooling theory from a static to a dynamic treatment by solving the TOV equations with finite-temperature equations of state derived from relativistic mean field theory (IU-FSU interaction), imposing conservation of total baryon number as the star cools. They computed the static thermal energy as the integral of thermal energy density over the stellar volume, and the dynamic thermal energy as the total gravitational mass difference between finite-temperature and zero-temperature configurations, then compared the two to define an efficiency factor.

## Key numbers

- For a canonical 1.4 solar mass neutron star with the IU-FSU interaction, the efficiency factor eta = 0.87, meaning only 87% of the static thermal energy is available for cooling.
- For a low-mass neutron star of 0.5 solar masses, the efficiency factor is eta = 0.94.
- For a high-mass neutron star of 1.9 solar masses, the efficiency factor drops to eta = 0.78, meaning 22% of the static thermal energy is not available.
- Both the static and dynamic thermal energies are proportional to T^2, and the efficiency factor eta is independent of temperature.

## Context

This work extends the standard neutron star cooling theory (Page et al. 2004, Yakovlev and Pethick 2004) by relaxing the assumption that stellar structure is fixed during thermal evolution, which had not been previously validated. The dynamic treatment builds on finite-temperature EOS calculations using the IU-FSU interaction (Fattoyev et al. 2010) and the Haensel-Zdunik-Dobaczewski and Negele-Vautherin EOS for the crust. The results confirm that the static approximation is adequate for current cooling simulations, but the efficiency factor formalism introduced here could be incorporated into future cooling codes for higher precision, particularly for massive neutron stars where the correction reaches 22%.
