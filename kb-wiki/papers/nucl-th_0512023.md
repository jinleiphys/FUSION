# Charge Radii of beta-Stable Nuclei

> In previous work it was shown that the radius of nucleus R is determined by the alpha-cluster structure and can be estimated on the number of alpha-clusters disregarding to the number of excess neutrons. A hypothesis also was made that the radius R_m of a beta-stable isotope, which is actually measured at electron scattering experiments, is determined by the volume occupied by the matter of the core plus the volume occupied by the peripheral alpha-clusters. In this paper it is shown that the condition R_m = R restricts the number of excess neutrons filling the core to provide the beta-stability. The number of peripheral clusters can vary from 1 to 5 and the value of R for heavy nuclei almost do not change, whereas the number of excess neutrons should change with the number of peripheral clusters to get the value of R_m close to R. It can explain the path of the beta-stability and its width. The radii R_m of the stable isotopes with 12 =< Z =< 83 and the alpha-decay isotopes with 84 =< Z =< 116 that are stable to beta-decay have been calculated.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the path and width of beta-stability in nuclei can be explained by the condition that the measured matter radius R_m of a beta-stable isotope equals the radius R determined by alpha-cluster structure, which restricts the number of excess neutrons filling the core.

## Method

The author calculates nuclear charge radii R_m for beta-stable isotopes with atomic numbers Z = 12 to 83 and for alpha-decay isotopes with Z = 84 to 116 that are stable to beta-decay, using a model where the nuclear radius is determined by alpha-cluster structure. The model assumes that the measured radius R_m is the sum of the volume occupied by the core matter plus the volume occupied by peripheral alpha-clusters, and the condition R_m = R is imposed to determine the number of excess neutrons that provide beta-stability.

## Key numbers

* Radii R_m were calculated for stable isotopes with Z = 12 to 83 and alpha-decay isotopes with Z = 84 to 116 that are stable to beta-decay.
* The number of peripheral clusters can vary from 1 to 5 for heavy nuclei.
* The value of R for heavy nuclei almost does not change as the number of peripheral clusters varies from 1 to 5.

## Context

This work extends a previous study (Nie, 2005) which showed that the nuclear radius R is determined by alpha-cluster structure and can be estimated from the number of alpha-clusters regardless of excess neutrons. The present paper adds the hypothesis that the measured matter radius R_m of beta-stable isotopes is determined by the core volume plus peripheral alpha-cluster volume, and uses this to explain the beta-stability path and its width. The approach enables predictions of which isotopes are beta-stable based on geometric constraints from alpha-clustering.
