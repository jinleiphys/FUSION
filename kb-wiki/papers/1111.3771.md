# The QCD Phase Diagram in Chiral Fluid Dynamics

> We give a general overview about the approaches to study the phase diagram of QCD. Thereafter, we examine the evolution of a fireball in a chiral fluid dynamic model including nonequilibrium effects.

# The QCD Phase Diagram in Chiral Fluid Dynamics

## Key claim
The paper presents a chiral fluid dynamic model that couples the chiral phase transition order parameter (sigma field) to a fluid dynamic expansion of quarks, including nonequilibrium effects such as dissipation and noise. The authors show that supercooling and reheating occur during a first-order phase transition but are absent in a critical point scenario, though these results are based on model calculations rather than direct experimental observation.

## Method
The authors start from the linear sigma model with constituent quarks and derive coupled dynamics of the sigma mean-field (the order parameter for chiral symmetry) and relativistic fluid dynamics of the quark fluid. The sigma field evolution is governed by a Langevin equation with a damping term and stochastic noise satisfying the dissipation-fluctuation theorem, while the quark fluid expansion is described by relativistic fluid dynamic equations with a source term allowing energy dissipation. The system is initialized in an ellipsoidal shape at a maximal temperature of 160 MeV, with the sigma field initially in equilibrium with the quark fluid, and the radial expansion profiles are computed for both a first-order phase transition scenario and a critical point scenario.

## Key numbers
- Initial maximal temperature: T = 160 MeV in the inner region of the fireball
- Critical temperature for critical point scenario: T_c^cp = 139.88 MeV
- Critical temperature for first-order phase transition scenario: T_c^fopt = 123.27 MeV
- Damping coefficient value for the regime 2m_q > m_sigma > 2m_pi: 2.2/fm
- Time scales shown in radial profiles: t = 4.8 fm, t = 6.4-8.0 fm, t = 9.6 fm

## Context
This work extends earlier chiral fluid dynamic models by Mishustin (1998) and Paech (2003) through the selfconsistent inclusion of dissipation and noise, as previously developed by the authors in Nahrgang (2011). The study builds on lattice QCD results showing a crossover at zero baryon density (Aoki, 2006) and on model calculations that suggest a first-order phase transition at higher baryonic densities ending in a critical point. The results address the question of how nonequilibrium dynamics, including critical slowing down (Berdnikov, 1999), may diminish critical phenomena at the critical point while producing interesting effects at the first-order phase transition, which is relevant for interpreting heavy-ion collision data from experiments searching for the conjectured critical point (Stephanov, 1998; Stephanov, 1999).
