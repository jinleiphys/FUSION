# Time dynamics in chaotic many-body systems: can chaos destroy a quantum computer?

> Highly excited many-particle states in quantum systems (nuclei, atoms, quantum dots, spin systems, quantum computers) can be ``chaotic'' superpositions of mean-field basis states (Slater determinants, products of spin or qubit states). This is a result of the very high energy level density of many-body states which can be easily mixed by a residual interaction between particles. We consider the time dynamics of wave functions and increase of entropy in such chaotic systems. As an example we present the time evolution in a closed quantum computer. A time scale for the entropy S(t) increase is t_c =t_0/(n log_2{n}), where t_0 is the qubit ``lifetime'', n is the number of qubits, S(0)=0 and S(t_c)=1. At t << t_c the entropy is small: S= n t^2 J^2 log_2(1/t^2 J^2), where J is the inter-qubit interaction strength. At t > t_c the number of ``wrong'' states increases exponentially as 2^{S(t)} . Therefore, t_c may be interpreted as a maximal time for operation of a quantum computer, since at t > t_c one has to struggle against the second law of thermodynamics. At t >>t_c the system entropy approaches that for chaotic eigenstates.

# Time dynamics in chaotic many-body systems: can chaos destroy a quantum computer?

## Key claim
Chaos in a closed quantum computer does not destroy operability immediately when eigenstates become chaotic; instead, there is a finite operational time window $t_c = \tau_0/(n \log_2 n)$ during which the entropy remains small, after which the exponential proliferation of "wrong" states makes further computation effectively impossible due to the second law of thermodynamics.

## Method
The author models a quantum computer as a system of $n$ qubits with a random Hamiltonian $H = \sum_i \epsilon_i \sigma_i^z + \sum_{i<j} J_{ij} \sigma_i^x \sigma_j^x$, where $\epsilon_i$ are uniformly distributed in $[0.5\Delta_0, 1.5\Delta_0]$ and $J_{ij}$ are nearest-neighbor interactions. The time evolution of the wave function is computed starting from a pure basis state (all qubits in a definite spin configuration), and the entropy $S(t) = -\sum_s W_s \log_2 W_s$ is tracked as a function of time using the strength function formalism and Breit-Wigner-type local spectral densities.

## Key numbers
- The maximal operational time for a quantum computer is $t_c \simeq \tau_0/(n \log_2 n)$, where $\tau_0$ is the single-qubit "lifetime" and $n$ is the number of qubits.
- At $t \ll t_c$, the entropy grows as $S \simeq n t^2 J^2 \log_2(1/t^2 J^2)$, where $J$ is the inter-qubit interaction strength.
- The entropy reaches $S(t_c) = 1$ at the melting time $t_c$.
- For 1000 qubits with $\Delta_0 \sim 1$ K, the average interval between multi-qubit eigenstates is $D \sim 10^{-298}$ K.
- The boundary of chaos in eigenstates is $J_c \simeq 0.4 \Delta_0/n$ (from numerical simulations in prior work).
- The asymptotic entropy at $t \gg t_c$ is $S^{(k)} + 1$, where $S^{(k)}$ is the entropy of a chaotic eigenstate, and is smaller than the maximal possible entropy $S_{\text{max}} = n$.

## Context
This work extends the eigenstate-based analysis of quantum computer "melting" by Georgeot and Shepelyansky (1999) to the time domain, showing that the relevant timescale for loss of coherence is set by the spreading width rather than the level spacing. The author draws on the statistical theory of chaotic many-body eigenstates developed for nuclei and atoms (Flambaum and collaborators, 1997-1999), where the number of principal basis components $N_p \sim \Gamma/D$ and the enhancement of weak perturbations by $\sqrt{N_p}$ are well-established. The result enables a practical bound on quantum computer operation times in the chaotic regime, and suggests similar entropy-growth timescales for other many-body systems such as quantum dots and disordered systems.
