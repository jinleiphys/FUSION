# Quantum effects on plasma screening for thermonuclear reactions in laser-generated plasmas

> A quantum plasma screening model based on the density matrix formalism is used to investigate theoretically the thermonuclear reactions $^{13}$C($α$, $n$)$^{16}$O and $^2$H($d$, $n$)$^3$He in laser-generated plasmas over a large range of densities and temperatures. For cold and dense (solid-state density) plasmas, our results show that quantum effects can enhance the plasma screening for thermonuclear reactions up to one order of magnitude compared to the classical case. This result can have impact on nuclear astrophysics predictions, and also may play a role for fusion energy gain prospects. Our simulations allow us to identify the laser-generated plasma experimental setting in which the quantum effects on plasma screening could be confirmed at existing high-intensity laser facilities.

## Key claim

For cold and dense (solid-state density) plasmas, the quantum density matrix formalism (DMF) predicts plasma screening enhancement factors up to one order of magnitude higher than classical models, but this enhancement is restricted to the intermediate screening regime of low temperature (around 200 eV) and high density (above 10^23 cm^-3), and is not present in the weak screening regime accessible with gas-jet targets.

## Method

The authors solved the density matrix equation from quantum statistical mechanics self-consistently with a modified Poisson-Boltzmann equation to obtain the electron density distribution and screening potential around nuclear charges. The free energy was then computed from the electrostatic energy via integration over inverse temperature, and the plasma screening enhancement factor was obtained from the difference in free energies between the combined nuclear system and the individual reactants. The numerical approach combined an implicit Crank-Nicolson step size with the Runge-Kutta-Fehlberg method on a non-linear two-dimensional cylindrical grid, with the potential approximated by the Mitler weak screening potential at large distances.

## Key numbers

- For the 13C(alpha,n)16O reaction in a helium plasma at density 10^24 cm^-3 and temperature 200 eV, the DMF quantum model predicts a screening enhancement factor approximately one order of magnitude higher than the Salpeter weak screening model (which gives a factor of about 1.10 at those parameters)
- For the 2H(d,n)3He reaction at deuterium ion density 10^24 cm^-3 and temperature 200 eV, the DMF model gives g_scr = 1.12, compared to 1.10 for weak screening and 1.079 for the Mitler formula
- For the 2H(d,n)3He reaction at density 10^21 cm^-3 and temperature 200 eV, all three models agree at g_scr = 1.0030-1.0031
- In Setup III (optical laser plus XFEL at HIBEF), the calculated reaction rates for 13C(alpha,n)16O are 1.31 x 10^7 s^-1 for DMF, 3.65 x 10^6 s^-1 for weak screening, and 1.02 x 10^6 s^-1 for the Mitler model, assuming 10 kHz repetition rate
- In Setup II (ELI-NP), calculated events per laser pulse for 13C(alpha,n)16O are 4.4 x 10^5 for DMF, 1.2 x 10^5 for weak screening, and 3.4 x 10^4 for the Mitler model

## Context

This work extends the density matrix formalism originally introduced by Gruzinov (1998) for solar plasma parameters to the intermediate screening regime of low temperature and high density accessible in laser-generated plasmas. The results contradict classical models including the Salpeter weak screening model (Salpeter, 1954), the Mitler formula (Mitler, 1977) based on the Stewart-Pyatt ionization potential depression model, and the Graboske et al. (1973) intermediate screening formula. The predicted enhancement could impact nuclear astrophysics predictions for low mass stars, brown dwarfs, and pre-main-sequence stars, and may help resolve the ongoing debate about plasma-induced ionization potential depression measurements (Ciricosta, 2012) versus the Stewart-Pyatt model (Stewart, 1966). The work identifies three experimental setups at existing facilities (NIF, ELI-NP, and HIBEF) that could test the quantum effects, with the combined optical laser and XFEL setup at HIBEF identified as the most promising.
