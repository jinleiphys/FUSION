# An EFT for the weak $ΛN$ interaction

> The nonleptonic weak $|ΔS|=1$ $ΛN$ interaction, responsible for the dominant, nonmesonic decay of all but the lightest hypernuclei, is studied in the framework of an effective field theory. The long-range physics is described through tree-level exchange of the SU(3) Goldstone bosons ($π$ and $K$), while the short-range potential is parametrized in terms of lowest-order contact terms obtained from the most general non-derivative local four-fermion interaction. Fitting to available weak hypernuclear decay rates for $^5_Λ{\rm He}$, $^{11}_Λ{\rm B}$ and $^{12}_Λ{\rm C}$ yields reasonable values for the low-energy constants.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a low-order effective field theory (EFT) combining long-range pion and kaon exchange with leading-order contact terms can describe the available hypernuclear weak decay data for \(^5_\Lambda\)He, \(^{11}_\Lambda\)B, and \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C, but the fit quality depends on which asymmetry data are included; the two present experimental asymmetry values for A=5 and A=11 cannot be fitted simultaneously with this set of contact terms.

## Method

The long-range part of the weak \(\Lambda N \to NN\) transition was described through tree-level exchange of the SU(3) Goldstone bosons (\(\pi\) and \(K\)), while the short-range part was parametrized using leading-order contact terms from the most general non-derivative local four-fermion interaction, with operators up to order \(q/M_N\). The low-energy constants (LECs) were fitted to experimental total decay rates \(\Gamma\), neutron-to-proton ratios \(\Gamma_n/\Gamma_p\), and asymmetries \(\mathcal{A}\) for \(^5_\Lambda\)He, \(^{11}_\Lambda\)B, and \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C, using shell-model wave functions for the initial hypernucleus and correlated \(\Lambda N\) wave functions from a \(G\)-matrix calculation based on the Nijmegen NSC97f strong interaction model.

## Key numbers

- The \(\Lambda N \to NN\) reaction releases approximately 177 MeV at threshold, corresponding to a momentum \(| \vec{p} | \approx 417\) MeV.
- The weak coupling constant is \(G_F m_\pi^2 = 2.21 \times 10^{-7}\).
- The strong \(NN\pi\) coupling constant is \(g_{NN\pi} = 13.16\); the empirical constants for the weak \(\Lambda N\pi\) vertex are \(A_\pi = 1.05\) (parity-violating) and \(B_\pi = -7.15\) (parity-conserving).
- The strong \(\Lambda N K\) coupling constant is taken as \(g_{\Lambda N K} = -17.66\) from the Nijmegen Soft-Core model f (NSC97f).
- For \(^5_\Lambda\)He, the total decay rate from OPE+OKE+LO PC+LO PV is 0.44 (units not specified but consistent with experimental values in the table), compared to experimental values of \(0.41 \pm 0.14\) (Szymanski 1991) and \(0.50 \pm 0.07\) (Noumi 1995).
- The \(n/p\) ratio for \(^5_\Lambda\)He from the full calculation is 0.54, compared to experimental values of \(0.93 \pm 0.55\) (Szymanski 1991) and \(0.50 \pm 0.10\) (KEK 2002).
- The asymmetry \(\alpha_\Lambda\) for \(^5_\Lambda\)He from the full calculation including the asymmetry in the fit is 0.24, matching the experimental value \(0.24 \pm 0.22\) (Ajimura 2000).
- For \(^{12}_\Lambda\)C, the total decay rate from the full calculation is 0.93, compared to experimental values of \(1.14 \pm 0.2\) (Szymanski 1991), \(0.89 \pm 0.15\) (Noumi 1995), and \(0.83 \pm 0.11\) (Bhang).
- The fitted LEC \(C_{IS}\) (isoscalar, spin-independent central operator) is \(5.97 \pm 0.71\) when the \(^5_\Lambda\)He asymmetry is included in the fit.
- The reduced chi-squared \(\hat{\chi}^2\) for the full fit including the \(^5_\Lambda\)He asymmetry is 1.12.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the weak \(\Lambda N\) interaction that used one-pion exchange (OPE) alone or added a Fermi (V-A) contact interaction (Jun), and a previous version of this manuscript (Parreno, Bennhold, Holstein 2004). It contrasts with more model-dependent approaches that used heavier meson exchange (Parreno, Ramos, Bennhold 1997; Dubach, Feldman, Holstein, Tabakin 1996), an effective quark Hamiltonian (Iso 2000), correlated \(2\pi\)-exchanges (Itonaga), or \(K\)-exchange plus correlated and uncorrelated \(2\pi\)-exchanges (Jido, Oset, Palomar 2001). The EFT approach enables a less model-dependent parametrization of the short-range physics, with the next generation of high-precision weak decay experiments expected to provide much improved constraints.
