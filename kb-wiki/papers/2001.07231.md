# Reexamining the relation between the binding energy of finite nuclei and the equation of state of infinite nuclear matter

> The energy density is calculated in coordinate space for $^{12}$C, $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca, and $^{208}$Pb using a dispersive optical model constrained by all relevant data including the corresponding energy of the ground state. The energy density of $^{8}$Be is also calculated using the Green's function Monte-Carlo method employing the Argonne/Urbana two and three-body interactions. The nuclear interior minimally contributes to the total binding energy due to the 4$πr^2$ phase space factor. Thus, the volume contribution to the energy in the interior is not well constrained. The dispersive-optical-model energy densities are in good agreement with \textit{ab initio} self-consistent Green's function calculations of infinite nuclear matter restricted to treat only short-range and tensor correlations. These results call into question the degree to which the equation of state for nuclear matter is constrained by the empirical mass formula. In particular, the results in this paper indicate that saturated nuclear matter does not require the canonical value of 16 MeV binding per particle but only about 13-14 MeV when the interior of $^{208}$Pb is considered.

## Key claim

The canonical value of 16 MeV per particle for the saturation energy of symmetric nuclear matter, derived from the volume term of the empirical mass formula, is not required by the data; the interior of finite nuclei like $^{208}$Pb instead suggests a saturation energy of only about 13-14 MeV per particle when the volume contribution is properly evaluated.

## Method

The authors calculated the coordinate-space energy density for $^{12}$C, $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca, and $^{208}$Pb using a dispersive optical model (DOM) constrained by scattering data, ground-state energies, charge densities, and quasihole energies, with the total binding energy obtained from the Migdal-Galitski sum rule. For $^{8}$Be, the energy density was computed using the Green's function Monte Carlo method with Argonne/Urbana two- and three-body interactions. The DOM energy densities were compared to self-consistent Green's function (SCGF) calculations of infinite nuclear matter that include only short-range and tensor correlations, using three different nucleon-nucleon interactions (CD-Bonn, AV18, and N3LO chiral).

## Key numbers

- The DOM binding energy per particle for $^{208}$Pb is -7.76 MeV, compared to the experimental value of -7.87 MeV and the mass-formula value of -7.81 MeV.
- The DOM binding energy per particle for $^{40}$Ca is -8.46 MeV, compared to the experimental value of -8.55 MeV.
- The DOM binding energy per particle for $^{48}$Ca is -8.66 MeV, matching the experimental value of -8.66 MeV.
- The DOM binding energy per particle for $^{12}$C is -7.85 MeV, compared to the experimental value of -7.68 MeV.
- The AV18 interaction in SCGF calculations of nuclear matter saturates at about -11.5 MeV per particle, while the APR equation of state (AV18 + Urbana-IX) gives a minimum energy of -12.6 MeV per particle.
- The interior of $^{208}$Pb indicates saturated nuclear matter requires only about 13-14 MeV binding per particle, not the canonical 16 MeV.
- The empirical mass formula parameters used are $a_V = 15.6$ MeV, $a_S = 17.2$ MeV, $a_C = 0.697$ MeV, and $a_A = 46.6$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the argument of Dewulf (2003) that the link between finite nuclei and nuclear matter should be confined to short-range correlations, and challenges the traditional extrapolation from the Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula to the nuclear matter saturation energy. The results contradict the assumption that the volume coefficient $a_V$ of the liquid drop model directly gives the saturation energy of infinite nuclear matter, a connection that has been standard since Bethe (1971) and Myers (1996). The paper enables a re-evaluation of nuclear matter equations of state used in neutron star calculations, such as the APR equation of state from Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall (1998), which already gives a saturation energy of -12.6 MeV rather than -16 MeV.
