# High-energy neutrino-nucleus interactions

> High-energy neutrino-nucleus interactions are discussed by considering neutrino-oscillation experiments and ultra-high-energy cosmic neutrino interactions. The largest systematic error for the current neutrino oscillation measurements comes from the neutrino-nucleus interaction part, and its accurate understanding is essential for high-precision neutrino physics, namely for studying CP violation in the lepton sector. Depending on neutrino beam energies, quasi-elastic, resonance, Regge, or/and deep inelastic scattering (DIS) processes contribute to the neutrino cross section. It is desirable to have a code to calculate the neutrino-nucleus cross section in any kinematical range by combining various theoretical descriptions. On the other hand, the IceCube collaboration started obtaining cross section data up to the $10^{15}$ eV range, so that it became necessary to understand ultra-high-energy neutrino interactions beyond the artificial lepton-accelerator energy range. For future precise neutrino physics including the CP measurement, it is also necessary to understand accurate nuclear corrections. The current status is explained for nuclear corrections in DIS structure functions. The possibility is also discussed to find gravitational sources within nucleons and nuclei, namely matrix elements of quark-gluon energy-momentum tensor. They could be probed by neutrino interactions without replying on direct ultra-weak "gravitational interactions" with high-intensity neutrino beams, possibly at a future neutrino factory, by using techniques of hadron tomography.

## Key claim

Accurate neutrino-nucleus cross sections are essential for future high-precision neutrino oscillation measurements (especially CP violation studies) and for interpreting ultra-high-energy cosmic neutrino data from IceCube, but current theoretical descriptions must combine multiple interaction regimes (quasi-elastic, resonance, Regge, DIS) and include nuclear corrections at the 5% accuracy level, which is not yet achieved.

## Method

The paper reviews the kinematical regions of neutrino-nucleus scattering (quasi-elastic, resonance, deep inelastic scattering, and Regge) as functions of neutrino energy and momentum transfer, focusing on the DIS regime. It presents the charged-current and neutral-current cross section formulas in terms of structure functions \(F_1, F_2, F_3\) and parton distribution functions (PDFs), discusses nuclear modifications of structure functions and nuclear PDFs (NPDFs) using data from charged-lepton DIS, Drell-Yan processes, and neutrino experiments, and describes how generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and generalized distribution amplitudes (GDAs) can probe gravitational form factors of hadrons via neutrino scattering or two-photon processes.

## Key numbers

- The accuracy required for theoretical neutrino-nucleus cross sections in future oscillation measurements is roughly 5%.
- IceCube reported the first neutrino cross section measurements in the energy range \(10^{13}\)–\(10^{15}\) eV (2017).
- The DIS region is defined by \(W^2 = (p+q)^2 \ge 4\) GeV\(^2\) and \(Q^2 \ge 1\) GeV\(^2\); the resonance region is \(M_N^2 < W^2 < 4\) GeV\(^2\).
- Nuclear modifications of structure functions and PDFs are generally 20–30% effects for medium and large nuclei.
- The strange-to-light-antiquark ratio from neutrino dimuon events is \(s+\bar s \simeq 0.4 (\bar u + \bar d)\) at relatively small \(Q^2\).
- From Belle two-photon data on \(\gamma^* \gamma \to \pi^0 \pi^0\), the pion gravitational-mass radius is \(\sqrt{\langle r^2 \rangle_{\text{mass}}} = 0.56 \sim 0.69\) fm and the mechanical radius is \(\sqrt{\langle r^2 \rangle_{\text{mech}}} = 1.45 \sim 1.56\) fm; the pion charge radius is \(0.672 \pm 0.008\) fm.

## Context

This review extends the discussion of neutrino-nucleus interactions motivated by the T2K experiment (which uses water targets and requires accurate neutrino-oxygen cross sections) and by the IceCube collaboration's first cross section measurements in 2017. It builds on prior work in neutrino DIS (e.g., NuTeV), charged-lepton DIS at HERA, and nuclear PDF analyses such as nCTEQ (2016). The paper also connects to hadron tomography studies of GPDs at JLab and CERN-COMPASS, and to Belle's 2016 measurement of \(\gamma^* \gamma \to \pi^0 \pi^0\) for determining GDAs and gravitational form factors. Future experiments like the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), KM3NeT, Baikal-GVD, and a possible neutrino factory could provide improved nuclear corrections and probe gravitational sources within hadrons.
