# The resistible effects of Coulomb interaction on nucleus-vapor phase coexistence

> We explore the effects of Coulomb interaction upon the nuclear liquid vapor phase transition. Because large nuclei (A>60) are metastable objects, phases, phase coexistence, and phase transitions cannot be defined with any generality and the analogy to liquid vapor is ill-posed for these heavy systems. However, it is possible to account for the Coulomb interaction in the decay rates and obtain the coexistence phase diagram for the corresponding uncharged system.

# The resistible effects of Coulomb interaction on nucleus-vapor phase coexistence

## Key claim

For nuclei with A > 60, the Coulomb interaction reverses the surface-energy trend that produces negative heat capacities in smaller systems, making negative heat capacities impossible for these larger nuclei; however, by correcting experimental decay rates for Coulomb effects, one can extract the coexistence phase diagram for the corresponding uncharged system, bypassing the fundamental difficulty that large nuclei (A > 60) are metastable objects for which phases, phase coexistence, and phase transitions cannot be defined with any generality.

## Method

The authors analyze the three components of Coulomb energy (drop self-energy, drop-vapor interaction, and vapor self-energy) and show that for nuclei above A ≈ 60, the binding energy decreases with increasing size due to Coulomb repulsion, reversing the surface-dominated trend. They then apply transition state theory to nuclear decay, treating the saddle-point barriers for fragment emission as conditional saddles in statistical equilibrium with the droplet; by dividing away the Coulomb contribution to these barriers, they recover rates that correspond to the decay of an uncharged drop, from which the Fisher droplet model yields the coexistence diagram for the Coulomb-free system.

## Key numbers

- For A > 60, the binding energy (and thus the molar enthalpy of vaporization ΔH_m) progressively decreases with increasing A, reversing the surface trend that dominates at smaller A values.
- For a gold-like nucleus, the estimated energy difference ΔE between the metastable state and the true ground state (three fragments of approximately 60 nucleons each) is approximately 135 MeV; at a temperature of 4 MeV, the Boltzmann factor for the metastable configuration is P ≈ e^{-34} or approximately 2 × 10^{-15}.
- The upper limit for observing negative heat capacities is A ≈ 60 for systems where monomer emission dominates; this limit would be displaced upward only if large fragments dominate the breakup, but the average cluster size in the gas phase rarely rises above unity in both Ising models and experimental data.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous thermodynamic treatment of finite nuclear systems (Moretto 2002, on negative heat capacities) by explicitly addressing the Coulomb interaction, which had been treated as a troublesome inessential divergence in earlier nuclear matter calculations. The paper directly contradicts claims of negative heat capacities in large nuclei (D'Agostino 2000, Chomaz 2001) and provides a method to recover the phase diagram of chargeless nuclear matter from experimental decay data, analogous to how cold nuclear matter properties are extracted by setting liquid-drop Coulomb terms to zero. The approach enables the use of the Fisher droplet model (Fisher 1967) to describe first-chance fragment abundances after Coulomb correction, following the same spirit as nuclear matter calculations that treat neutrons and protons as distinct particles without Coulomb interaction.
