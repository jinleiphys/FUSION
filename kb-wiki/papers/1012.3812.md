# Evidence for a Bound H-dibaryon from Lattice QCD

> We present evidence for the existence of a bound H-dibaryon, an I=0, J=0, s=-2 state with valence quark structure uuddss, at a pion mass of m_pi ~ 389 MeV. Using the results of Lattice QCD calculations performed on four ensembles of anisotropic clover gauge-field configurations, with spatial extents of L ~ 2.0, 2.5, 3.0 and 3.9 fm at a spatial lattice spacing of b ~ 0.123 fm, we find an H-dibaryon bound by B = 16.6 +- 2.1 +- 4.6 MeV at a pion mass of m_pi ~ 389 MeV.

## Key claim

The paper presents strong evidence for a bound H-dibaryon (a six-quark state with valence structure uuddss) from Lattice QCD calculations, but only at a pion mass of approximately 389 MeV, and the authors note that the quark-mass dependence of the binding energy is unknown, so a direct comparison with experiment is not yet possible.

## Method

The NPLQCD collaboration performed Lattice QCD calculations on four ensembles of n_f=2+1 anisotropic clover gauge-field configurations with spatial extents of 2.0, 2.5, 3.0, and 3.9 fm at a spatial lattice spacing of approximately 0.123 fm. Using Luscher's method, they extracted two-particle scattering amplitudes from the energy eigenvalues of the Lambda-Lambda system in finite volume, and extrapolated to infinite volume using only the two largest volumes (3.0 and 3.9 fm) to avoid contamination from finite-volume modifications to the Lambda mass.

## Key numbers

- H-dibaryon binding energy at m_pi ~ 389 MeV: B = 16.6 +/- 2.1 +/- 4.6 MeV (statistical and systematic uncertainties combined in quadrature)
- Probability greater than 0.98 that the H-dibaryon is bound at this pion mass
- Finite-volume binding energies: 12.3(1.1)(4.0) MeV on L=2.0 fm, 4.5(1.1)(1.3) MeV on L=2.5 fm, 16.3(1.2)(1.4) MeV on L=3.0 fm, and 16.6(1.4)(3.1) MeV on L=3.9 fm
- Spatial lattice spacing b_s ~ 0.123(1) fm, anisotropy xi = 3.50(3)
- Pion mass m_pi ~ 389 MeV

## Context

This work extends earlier quenched Lattice QCD calculations by the NPLQCD collaboration (Beane 2006) and others that found hints of a bound H-dibaryon, and contrasts with earlier quenched calculations (Wetzorke 2002) that concluded the H-dibaryon does not exist as a stable hadron. The results also differ from the HALQCD collaboration (Inoue 2010), who found a hint of an H-dibaryon in the SU(3) limit that evaporated when SU(3)-breaking was included. The paper enables future Lattice QCD calculations in larger volumes, at smaller lattice spacings, and over a range of light-quark masses including the physical point, demonstrating that the study of light hypernuclei directly from QCD is feasible.
