# Phenomenology of the little bang

> I review recent selected developments in the theory and modeling of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. I explain why relativistic viscous hydrodynamics is now used to model the expansion of the matter formed in these collisions. I give examples of first quantitative predictions, and I discuss remaining open questions associated with the description of the freeze-out process. I argue that while the expansion process is now well understood, our knowledge of initial conditions is still poor. Recent analyses of two-particle correlations have revealed fine structures known as ridge and shoulder, which extend over a long range in rapidity. These correlations are thought to originate from initial state fluctuations, whose modeling is still crude. I discuss triangular flow, a simple mechanism recently put forward, through which fluctuations generate the observed correlation pattern.

# Phenomenology of the little bang

## Key claim

Relativistic viscous hydrodynamics now provides quantitative predictions for ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions, but our knowledge of initial conditions remains poor and the freeze-out process still has open questions. The ridge and shoulder structures observed in two-particle correlations are thought to originate from initial state fluctuations, with triangular flow recently proposed as a mechanism through which these fluctuations generate the observed correlation pattern.

## Method

The paper reviews the application of relativistic viscous hydrodynamics to model the expansion of strongly interacting quark-gluon matter formed in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, using a systematic gradient expansion in the Knudsen number (ratio of mean free path to system size). The author compares hydrodynamic calculations with experimental data on particle multiplicities, average transverse momentum, elliptic flow, and higher harmonic flow, and discusses the interpretation of two-particle correlation measurements using the concept of triangular flow arising from initial-state fluctuations in the positions of participant nucleons.

## Key numbers

- Lorentz contraction factor of 100 at the top RHIC energy for colliding nuclei
- Agreement between hydrodynamics and kinetic theory holds within 10% up to Knudsen number K = 1/2
- Elliptic flow v2 increases up to 7% for more peripheral collisions at RHIC
- Viscosity to entropy ratio η/s = 0.08 is close to an absolute lower bound and produces sizable effects on elliptic flow
- Viscous correction with CGC initial conditions is roughly 20% for central collisions
- Viscous hydrodynamics with η/s = 0.16 matches elliptic flow data up to transverse momentum pt ~ 2 GeV
- Chemical freeze-out temperature of approximately 165 MeV where particle abundance ratios become constant

## Context

This review extends the earlier work on ideal hydrodynamics for RHIC phenomenology (Kolb 2003) and the theoretical lower bound on viscosity (Kovtun 2004), building on the realization that viscous corrections were important (Teaney 2003, Bhalerao 2005). The paper discusses recent full viscous hydrodynamic calculations (Romatschke 2007, Song 2007) and the proposal of triangular flow by Alver and Roland (2010) as a mechanism to explain the ridge and shoulder structures in two-particle correlations. The work enables improved extraction of QCD transport coefficients from experimental data and sets the stage for LHC heavy-ion physics, while identifying the need for better modeling of initial-state fluctuations and freeze-out processes.
