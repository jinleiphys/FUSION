# Gamma Ray Heating and Neutrino Cooling Rates due to Weak Interaction Processes on sd-shell Nuclei in Stellar Cores

> Gamma ray heating and neutrino cooling rates, due to weak interaction processes, on $sd$-shell nuclei in stellar core are calculated using the proton neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation theory. The recent extensive experimental mass compilation of \citep{Wang12}, other improved model input parameters including nuclear quadrupole deformation \citep{Ram01}, \citep{Mol16} and physical constants are taken into account in the current calculation. The purpose of this work is two fold, one is to improve the earlier calculation of weak rates performed by \citep{Nabi99} using the same theory. We further compare our results with previous calculations. The selected $sd$-shell nuclei, considered in this work, are of special interest for the evolution of O-Ne-Mg core in 8-10 M$_\odot$ stars due to competitive gamma ray heating rates and cooling by URCA processes. The outcome of these competitions is to determine, whether the stars end up as a white dwarf \citep{Nabi08}, an electron-capture supernova \citep{Jones13} or Fe core-collapse supernova \citep{Suz16}. The selected $sd$-shell nuclei for calculation of associated weak-interaction rates include $^{20,23}$O, $^{20,23}$F, $^{20,23,24}$Ne, $^{20,23-25}$Na, and $^{23-25}$Mg. The cooling and heating rates are calculated for density range ($10 \leq ρ($\;g.cm$^{-3}) \leq $ 10$^{11}$) and temperature range ($0.01\times10^{9}$ $\leq$ $\;T(K)$ $\leq$ $30\times10^{9}$). The calculated gamma heating rates are orders of magnitude bigger than the shell model rates (except for $^{25}$Mg at low densities). At high temperatures the gamma heating rates are in reasonable agreement. The calculated cooling rates are up to an order of magnitude bigger for odd-A nuclei.

## Key claim

The gamma ray heating rates calculated using the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory are orders of magnitude larger than shell model rates for most sd-shell nuclei (except for 25Mg at low densities), while the neutrino cooling rates are up to an order of magnitude larger for odd-A nuclei, but only under the specific conditions of the density range (10 to 10^11 g.cm^-3) and temperature range (0.01x10^9 to 30x10^9 K) relevant to O-Ne-Mg cores in 8-10 solar mass stars.

## Method

The authors used the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory with a Nilsson model basis (axially deformed) and BCS pairing to calculate Gamow-Teller strength distributions, weak interaction rates, gamma ray heating rates, and neutrino cooling rates for sd-shell nuclei (20,23O, 20,23F, 20,23,24Ne, 20,23-25Na, and 23-25Mg). The calculation incorporated recent experimental mass data from Wang and collaborators (2012), nuclear quadrupole deformation parameters from Raman (2001) and Moller (2016), and updated physical constants, with the Hamiltonian tuned to reproduce the Ikeda Sum Rule and terrestrial half-lives within about 10 percent deviation.

## Key numbers

* Gamma heating rates for electron capture on 23Na are up to an order of magnitude bigger than the earlier pn-QRPA calculation (Nabi 1999) at high temperatures.
* The density range covered is 10 to 10^11 g.cm^-3, and the temperature range is 0.01x10^9 to 30x10^9 K.
* The pairing gaps were set as Delta_p = Delta_n = 12/sqrt(A) MeV.
* The Nilsson oscillator constant was chosen as hbar*omega = 41*A^(-1/3) MeV.
* The value of D in the rate formula was taken as 6143 s.
* The ratio of axial to vector coupling constants (g_A/g_V) was taken as -1.2694.
* The model space used was 5*hbar*omega.
* For 25Mg at low densities, the gamma heating rates are the exception where they are not orders of magnitude larger than shell model rates.

## Context

This work extends the earlier pn-QRPA calculation of weak rates by Nabi and Klapdor (1999) for sd-shell nuclei (A=18-39, Z=10-20) by incorporating updated experimental mass data, improved nuclear quadrupole deformation parameters, and new physical constants, while also calculating rates for nuclei with Z=8 and 9 not included in that earlier work. The results are compared with shell model calculations by Oda and collaborators (1994) using the Wildenthal interaction and by Suzuki and collaborators (2016) using the USDB Hamiltonian. The calculated heating and cooling rates are intended for use in stellar simulation codes to determine the evolutionary fate of 8-10 solar mass stars, specifically whether they end as white dwarfs, electron-capture supernovae, or iron core-collapse supernovae.
