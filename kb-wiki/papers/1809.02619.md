# Study of various few-body systems using Gaussian expansion method (GEM)

> We review our calculation method, Gaussian expansion method (GEM), and its applications to various few-body (3- to 5-body) systems such as 1) few-nucleon systems, 2) few-body structure of hypernuclei, 3) clustering structure of light nuclei and unstable nuclei, 4) exotic atoms/molecules, 5) cold atoms, 6) nuclear astrophysics and 7) structure of exotic hadrons. Showing examples in our published papers, we explain i) high accuracy of GEM calculations and its reason, ii) wide applicability of GEM and iii) successful predictions by GEM calculations before measurements. GEM was proposed 30 years ago and has been applied to a variety of subjects. To solve few-body Schroedinger equations accurately, use is made of the Rayleigh-Ritz variational method for bound states, the complex-scaling method for resonant states and the Kohn-type variational principle to S-matrix for scattering states. The total wave function is expanded in terms of few-body Gaussian basis functions spanned over all the sets of rearrangement Jacobi coordinates. Gaussians with ranges in geometric progression work very well both for short-range and long-range behavior of the few-body wave functions. Use of Gaussians with complex ranges gives much more accurate solution when the wave function has many oscillations.

## Key claim

The Gaussian expansion method (GEM) provides high-accuracy solutions to few-body Schroedinger equations for bound, resonant, and scattering states by expanding the total wave function in Gaussian basis functions with ranges in geometric progression, spanned over all sets of rearrangement Jacobi coordinates; the authors claim that this approach achieves precision comparable to or better than other methods while requiring significantly less computation time, but note that the accuracy depends on the employed interactions being well-established.

## Method

The few-body Schroedinger equation is solved using the Rayleigh-Ritz variational method for bound states, the complex-scaling method for resonant states, and the Kohn-type variational principle to the S-matrix for scattering states. The total wave function is expanded in terms of few-body Gaussian basis functions constructed on all sets of rearrangement Jacobi coordinates, with radial ranges taken in geometric progression (r_n = r_1 a^{n-1}) to describe both short-range and long-range behavior. For highly oscillatory wave functions, Gaussians with complex ranges (eta_n = (1 +/- i omega) nu_n) are employed, and for 4-body systems, infinitesimally-shifted Gaussian lobe basis functions are used to avoid tedious angular-momentum algebra.

## Key numbers

- The dtmu- molecule J=v=1 state energy was calculated as -0.660 eV from the (tmu)_{1s}+d threshold, requiring 7 significant figures accuracy relative to the -2711.242 eV 3-body breakup threshold (Kamimura 1988)
- GEM calculation of the dtmu- molecule required only 3 minutes computation time on 1980s supercomputers, compared to ~10 hours for methods using elliptic or Slater geminal basis sets (Korobov 1987, Alexander 1988)
- For 3-nucleon bound states (3H and 3He) using the AV14 potential, GEM with 3600 basis functions achieved binding energy convergence with 1-keV accuracy using only 3 round-number parameter sets (r1=0.05, r_nmax=15.0, R1=0.3, R_nmax=9.0 fm; r1=0.1, r_nmax=15.0, R1=0.3, R_nmax=9.0 fm; r1=0.1, r_nmax=10.0, R1=0.3, R_nmax=6.0 fm) (Kameyama 1989)
- The antiproton mass determination from antiprotonic helium atom studies used GEM Coulomb 3-body calculations with accuracy of 10 significant figures in level energies, leading to the Particle Data Group 2000 recommended value |m_antiproton - m_proton|/m_proton < 5 x 10^{-7}

## Context

This review extends the authors' three decades of work developing GEM, first proposed in Kamimura (1988) for the muonic molecule problem, and applied to few-nucleon systems, hypernuclei, clustering nuclei, exotic atoms/molecules, cold atoms, nuclear astrophysics, and exotic hadrons. The method enables precision calculations that have successfully predicted experimental results before measurement, such as energy levels of antiprotonic helium atoms and shrinkage of hypernuclei, and provides a framework for studying systems where interactions are not fully established by first improving phenomenological interactions to reproduce subsystem data.
