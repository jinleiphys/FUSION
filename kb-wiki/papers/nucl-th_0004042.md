# Discussing the possibility of observation of parity violation in heavy ion collisions

> It was recently argued that in heavy ion collision the parity could be broken. This Note addresses the question of possibility of the experimental detection of the effect. We discuss how parity violating effects would modify the final particle distributions and how one could construct variables sensitive to the effect, and which measurement would be the (most) conclusive. Discussing different observables we also discuss the question if the ``signals'' can be faked by ``conventional'' effects (such as anisotropic flow, etc.) and make estimates of the signals.

## Key claim

Parity-violating effects in heavy ion collisions could be detected via event-by-event observables, but the signals are small and can be faked by conventional effects such as anisotropic flow, Coulomb interactions, or resonance decays; the most robust observable must correlate both electric and magnetic field effects to avoid confusion.

## Method

The author simulates parity-odd bubbles by bubbles with parallel real electric and magnetic fields randomly oriented in space, following Chikanian and Sandweiss (1999). The effect on particle momentum distributions is split into transverse and longitudinal field components. For the transverse case, the magnetic field rotates the distribution about the y-axis while the electric field shifts it along the y-axis; the variable V is constructed from the sum and difference of average transverse momenta in forward and backward hemispheres to measure the product of the fields. For the longitudinal case, the magnetic field rotates the distribution about the z-axis in opposite directions for positive and negative particles, which can be detected by comparing reaction planes reconstructed separately from positive and negative pions.

## Key numbers

- The impulse acting on a particle crossing the bubble boundary is estimated to be about 30 MeV for both electric and magnetic fields.
- In the STAR acceptance for central Au+Au collisions, about 2000 charged particles are expected; subdividing into forward and backward hemispheres gives about 1000 particles in each part.
- The mean squared transverse momentum is estimated as ⟨p_x²⟩ ≈ (350 MeV)² from RQMD.
- The signal-to-background ratio for a quantity like ⟨p_x⟩ in one hemisphere is approximately 1.5α, where α is the fraction of particles crossing the bubble boundary.
- The mean rotation angle due to the magnetic field is ⟨θ_H⟩ ≈ 0.05α.
- For the anisotropic flow parameters v_n ≈ 0.02–0.06 at SPS energies, the non-statistical variance of the sine of the azimuthal angle difference between positive and negative pions is expected to be σ_{sin(Δφ),non-stat} ≈ α · (1–3)·10⁻³.
- The NA49 Collaboration (1999) reported a preliminary upper limit on this quantity of < 10⁻³.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical proposal of Kharzeev, Pisarski, and Tytgat (1998) that parity-odd bubbles with non-zero ⟨B·E⟩ could form in heavy ion collisions, and responds to the observable proposed by Gyulassy (1999) using a twist tensor. It provides practical event-by-event analysis techniques for the STAR experiment at RHIC, showing that the variable V and reaction-plane correlations between positive and negative pions can distinguish parity violation from conventional directed flow. The results enable experimental searches for parity violation in strong interactions, with the NA49 Collaboration (1999) already providing an upper limit that constrains the possible signal size.
