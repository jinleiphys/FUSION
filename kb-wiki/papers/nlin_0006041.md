# Rate of energy absorption for a driven chaotic cavity

> We consider the response of a chaotic cavity in d dimensions to periodic driving. We are motivated by older studies of one-body dissipation in nuclei, and also by anticipated mesoscopic applications. For calculating the rate of energy absorption due to time-dependent deformation of the confining potential, we introduce an improved version of the wall formula. Our formulation takes into account that a special class of deformations causes no heating in the zero-frequency limit. We also derive a mesoscopic version of the Drude formula, and explain that it can be regarded as a special example of our calculations. Specifically we consider a quantum dot driven by electro-motive force which is induced by a time-dependent homogeneous magnetic field.

# Rate of energy absorption for a driven chaotic cavity

## Key claim

The paper introduces an improved version of the wall formula for calculating the rate of energy absorption in a chaotic cavity under periodic driving, showing that a special class of deformations (dilations, translations, and rotations) causes no heating in the zero-frequency limit, contrary to the naive white-noise approximation. The dissipation coefficient vanishes for these "special" deformations when the driving frequency approaches zero, provided the cavity exhibits hard chaos with no mixed phase space.

## Method

The authors use linear response theory (LRT) to relate the dissipation coefficient μ(Ω) to the noise power spectrum of the generalized force associated with a deformation parameter x, via a fluctuation-dissipation relation. They compute the autocorrelation function C_E(τ) of the fluctuating force for a single classical particle in a chaotic billiard, treating collisions as impulses in the hard-wall limit, and evaluate the power spectrum numerically by Fourier transforming a long trajectory sample (∼10⁶ consecutive collisions) with smoothing in ω-space. For quantum-classical correspondence tests, they compute boundary overlap integrals of eigenfunctions for 451 states in a stadium billiard between wavenumbers 398 < k < 402.

## Key numbers

- For the generalized 2D Sinai billiard with parameters a=2, b=1, θ₁=0.2, θ₂=0.5, the average collision rate with the wall is (1/τ_bl) ≈ 0.63.
- The RMS estimation error for classical calculations of the power spectrum is 3%, and for quantum calculations is 10%.
- The mean level spacing in the quantum stadium calculation is Δ ≈ 8.8 × 10⁻³ in ω units.
- For the Drude conductance in a 2D quantum dot, the small-frequency result gives C̃_ℐ(ω) = (1/4) e² (⟨r²⟩/A²) v_F² × τ_col, while the large-frequency WNA result gives C̃_ℐ(ω) proportional to 1/ω² with prefactor (2/(3π)) (e²/A³) v_F³ ∮ |n × r|² ds.

## Context

This work extends older studies of one-body dissipation in nuclei using the wall formula (Wall 1970, Koonin 1977, Jarzynski 1992, 1993) by providing a corrected version that accounts for the vanishing dissipation of special deformations. The authors show that the mesoscopic Drude formula for conductance in a quantum dot driven by a time-dependent magnetic field reduces to a special case of their rotation deformation calculation. The results enable improved predictions for energy absorption in mesoscopic systems such as quantum dots, and the dilation case plays a vital role in a numerical billiard diagonalization method introduced by Vergini and Saraceno (1999).
