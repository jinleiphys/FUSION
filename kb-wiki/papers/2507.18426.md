# Three-qubit encoding in ytterbium-171 atoms for simulating 1+1D QCD

> Simulating nuclear matter described by quantum chromodynamics using quantum computers is notoriously inefficient because of the assortment of quark degrees of freedom such as matter/antimatter, flavor, color, and spin. Here, we propose to address this resource efficiency challenge by encoding three qubits within individual ytterbium-171 atoms of a neutral atom quantum processor. The three qubits are encoded in three distinct sectors: an electronic "clock" transition, the spin-1/2 nucleus, and the lowest two motional states in one radial direction of the harmonic trapping potential. We develop a family of composite sideband pulses and demonstrate a universal gate set and readout protocol for this three-qubit system. We then apply it to single-flavor quantum chromodynamics in 1+1D axial gauge for which the three qubits directly represent the occupancy of quarks in the three colors. We show that two atoms are sufficient to simulate both vacuum persistence oscillations and string breaking. We consider resource requirements and connections to error detection/correction. Our work is a step towards resource-efficient digital simulation of nuclear matter and opens new opportunities for versatile qubit encoding in neutral atom quantum processors.

## Key claim

The authors propose encoding three qubits within individual ytterbium-171 atoms, using electronic clock, nuclear spin, and motional degrees of freedom, to simulate 1+1D quantum chromodynamics, claiming that two atoms suffice to simulate vacuum persistence oscillations and string breaking, but only in the axial gauge where gauge bosons are eliminated and with the caveat that the motional qubit readout requires up to four measurement rounds due to motional scrambling during photon scattering.

## Method

The authors encode three qubits in each ytterbium-171 atom: an electronic "clock" qubit (the ¹S₀–³P₀ transition), a nuclear spin-1/2 qubit, and a motional qubit using the lowest two harmonic oscillator states along one radial direction of the optical tweezer trap. They develop composite sideband pulse sequences (optimized via gradient ascent) to implement intra-quoct gates including SWAPₑₘ, CZₑₘ, and CCZ gates, and inter-quoct Rydberg-mediated C\bar{C}Z gates. They construct Trotterized circuits for the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian in 1+1D axial gauge with open boundary conditions, mapping the three quark colors directly onto the three qubits per atom, using one row of atoms for quarks and another for antiquarks.

## Key numbers

* Gate fidelity for SWAPₑₘ: ℱ ≈ 0.9925, achieved at a Rabi frequency chosen for maximal fidelity at minimal gate time with trap frequency ω = 2π × 100 kHz.
* Gate fidelity for CZₑₘ: ℱ ≈ 0.9929, under the same trap frequency condition.
* Gate fidelity for CCZ (e-n-m doubly controlled Z): ℱ ≈ 0.9814.
* Gate fidelity for the e-m shelving operation used in readout: ℱ ≈ 0.9907.
* Motion-preserving pulses (MPPs) operate on general quoct states with fidelity ≳ 0.999, and Hadamard gates preserve motional superpositions with fidelity ≳ 0.999.
* The motional qubit coherence time is T₂* ≈ 100 ms, limited primarily by intensity noise.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previously proposed two-qubit "ququart" architecture based on electronic clock and nuclear spin degrees of freedom in ytterbium-171 (Jia 2024) by adding a motional qubit. It builds on recent progress in cooling optically trapped atoms to their motional ground state (Scholl 2023) and on qudit approaches for gauge bosons in quantum electrodynamics (Meth 2023). The paper enables resource-efficient digital simulation of nuclear matter on neutral atom quantum processors, with the specific application to 1+1D QCD in axial gauge following the quoct formalism developed by Illa (2024).
