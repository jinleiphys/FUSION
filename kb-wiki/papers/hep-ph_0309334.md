# Chiral symmetry restoration in excited hadrons

> 5 pp., presented at Hadron '03 (X Int. Conf. on Hadron Spectroscopy, August 31 - September 6, 2003, Aschaffenburg, Germany

# Chiral symmetry restoration in excited hadrons

## Key claim

Parity doubling observed in high-lying nucleon and delta spectra (starting around 1.7 GeV) reflects effective chiral symmetry restoration, meaning that the role of quark condensates that break chiral symmetry in the vacuum becomes progressively less important high in the spectrum, even though the vacuum itself remains chirally broken.

## Method

The author analyzes experimental baryon and meson spectra from partial wave analyses (proton-antiproton annihilation at LEAR, tau-lepton decay data from ALEPH and OPAL) and compares spectral patterns of states connected by chiral transformations. The argument uses operator product expansion to show that at large space-like momenta, correlation functions become chirally symmetric because condensate contributions are suppressed by inverse powers of momentum, and dispersion relations connect this to spectral density at large masses.

## Key numbers

- Parity doubling in nucleon and delta spectra begins starting from the 1.7 GeV region, where excited nucleons of the same spin but opposite parity become approximately degenerate
- The difference between vector and axial-vector spectral densities from tau-lepton decays is compatible with zero at masses of 1.7 GeV
- Pion and $n\bar n$ $f_0$ meson spectra show a pattern of chiral symmetry restoration starting from 1.5 GeV mass

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier proposal (Glozman 2000) that parity doubling reflects effective chiral restoration, and provides a systematic QCD-based formulation developed in Cohen and Glozman (2001, 2002). The results contradict nonrelativistic constituent quark models (such as those by Capstick and Isgur, Godfrey and Isgur, and others) which predict many unobserved baryon states near 2 GeV and cannot produce systematic parity degeneracy. The findings also rule out scalar confinement potentials but are compatible with Lorentz-vector confinement and string models with bare quarks of definite chirality at the ends (Glozman 2002). Lattice calculations by DeGrand (2003) provide supporting evidence that high-lying mesons decouple from low-lying Dirac eigenmodes that determine the quark condensate. The work enables experimental programs at ELSA, JLAB, the future antiproton ring at GSI, and the new Japanese hadron facility to systematically study high-lying hadrons and understand QCD in the confining regime.
