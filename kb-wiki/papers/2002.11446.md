# Measurement of the charge separation along the magnetic field with Signed Balance Function in 200 GeV Au + Au collisions at STAR

> Experimental searches for Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) in heavy-ion collisions have been going on for a decade, and so far there is no conclusive evidence for its existence. Recently, the Signed Balance Function (SBF), based on the idea of examining the momentum ordering of charged pairs along the in- and out-of-plane directions, has been proposed as a probe of CME. In this approach, a pair of observables is invoked: one is $r_{\mathrm{rest}}$, the out-of-plane to in-plane ratio of $ΔB$ measured in pair's rest frame, where $ΔB$ is the difference between signed balance functions; The other is a double ratio, $R_{\mathrm{B}} = r_{\mathrm{rest}}/r_{\mathrm{lab}}$, where $r_{\mathrm{lab}}$ is a measurement similar to $r_{\mathrm{rest}}$ but measured in the laboratory frame. These two observables give opposite responses to the CME-driven charge separation compared to the background correlations arising from resonance flow and global spin alignment. Both $r_{\mathrm{rest}}$ and $R_{\mathrm{B}}$ being larger than unity can be regarded as a case in favor of the existence of CME. It is found experimentally that $r_{\mathrm{rest}}$, $r_{\mathrm{lab}}$ and $R_{\mathrm{B}}$ are larger than unity in Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV, and larger than realistic model calculations with no CME implemented. These findings are difficult to be explained by a background-only scenario.

## Key claim

The Signed Balance Function (SBF) observables \(r_{\mathrm{rest}}\) and \(R_{\mathrm{B}}\) are found to be larger than unity in 200 GeV Au+Au collisions at STAR, and larger than realistic model calculations with no Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) implemented; the paper states these findings are "difficult to be explained by a background-only scenario," but does not claim conclusive evidence for CME.

## Method

The SBF method examines the momentum ordering of charged pairs along the in-plane and out-of-plane directions relative to the reaction plane. Two observables are constructed: \(r_{\mathrm{rest}}\), the out-of-plane to in-plane ratio of the difference between signed balance functions measured in the pair's rest frame, and \(R_{\mathrm{B}} = r_{\mathrm{rest}}/r_{\mathrm{lab}}\), where \(r_{\mathrm{lab}}\) is the same ratio measured in the laboratory frame. The analysis uses about one billion minimum-bias events from 200 GeV Au+Au collisions recorded by STAR in 2016, with pions identified using the Time Projection Chamber and Time-Of-Flight detector within \(|\eta| < 0.5\) and \(0.2 < p_T < 2\) GeV/\(c\), and the second-order event plane reconstructed from TPC tracks with \(0.5 < \eta < 1.0\).

## Key numbers

* \(r_{\mathrm{rest}}\), \(r_{\mathrm{lab}}\), and \(R_{\mathrm{B}}\) are all larger than unity for all centralities in 200 GeV Au+Au collisions at STAR.
* In the 30-40% centrality bin, both \(r_{\mathrm{rest}}\) and \(R_{\mathrm{B}}\) from data are larger than the AVFD model calculation without CME (the case of \(a_1 = 0\)).
* For AMPT events (no CME implemented) and AVFD events with \(n_5/s = 0\), \(r_{\mathrm{rest}}\) and \(R_{\mathrm{B}}\) are consistent with unity within statistical uncertainties.
* The analysis uses about one billion minimum-bias events and particles in the kinematic range \(|\eta| < 1\) and \(0.2 < p_T < 2\) GeV/\(c\).

## Context

This work extends the SBF method proposed by Tang (2019) as a probe of the Chiral Magnetic Effect, which has been searched for experimentally for a decade without conclusive evidence. The results are compared to two realistic models: AMPT (Lin, 2004) with no CME signal, serving as a background baseline, and AVFD (authors cited as "avfdref") which implements CME via an anomalous transport current with finite axial charge over entropy ratio \(n_5/s\). The findings suggest that background-only explanations from resonance flow and global spin alignment are insufficient, motivating further studies with isobar collisions and more detailed investigations of Local Charge Conservation effects.
