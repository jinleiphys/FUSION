# Yield ratio of neutrons to protons in $^{12}C(d,n)^{13}$N and $^{12}C(d,p)^{13}$C from 0.6 MeV to 3 MeV

> The neutron yield in $^{12}$C(d,n)$^{13}$N and the proton yield in $^{12}C(d,p)^{13}$C have been measured by deuteron beam from 0.6 MeV to 3 MeV which is delivered from a 4-MeV electro static accelerator bombarding on the thick carbon target. The neutrons are detected at $0\degree$, $24\degree$, $48\degree$ and the protons at $135\degree$ in the lab frame. The ratios of the neutron yield to the proton one have been calculated and can be used as an effective probe to pin down the resonances. The resonances are found at 1.4 MeV, 1.7 MeV, 2.5 MeV in $^{12}C(d,p)^{13}$C and at 1.6 MeV, 2.7 MeV in $^{12}$C(d,n)$^{13}$N. This method provides a way to reduce the systematic uncertainty and helps to confirm more resonances in compound nuclei.

## Key claim

The ratio of neutron yield in $^{12}$C(d,n)$^{13}$N to proton yield in $^{12}$C(d,p)$^{13}$C, measured using a thick carbon target, can identify resonances in both reactions while eliminating systematic uncertainties from the incident beam intensity; however, the method becomes less sensitive at higher incident energies, so only the strongest resonances are observable.

## Method

A deuteron beam from a 4-MeV electrostatic accelerator, with energies from 0.6 MeV to 3 MeV, bombarded a thick natural graphite target (1 mm thickness, much larger than the deuteron range of several micrometers). Neutrons were detected at 0°, 24°, and 48° in the lab frame using three EJ-301 liquid scintillation detectors (with pulse shape discrimination for neutron-gamma separation), while protons were detected at 135° using an Au-Si surface barrier detector. The ratio of the neutron yield to the proton yield was calculated as a function of incident deuteron energy, and resonances were identified as maxima or minima in the ratio curves.

## Key numbers

- Neutron detectors (EJ-301) placed 112.9 cm from target, with solid angle 1.2×10⁻² sr; detector diameter 12.7 cm; efficiency approximately 0.4 to 0.5 from 0.6 to 3 MeV.
- Proton detector (Au-Si surface barrier) placed 135 mm from target at 135°, with diameter 7.5 mm and solid angle 2.42×10⁻³ sr; efficiency 100% for proton detection.
- Resonances found in $^{12}$C(d,p)$^{13}$C at incident deuteron energies of 1.4 MeV, 1.7 MeV, and 2.5 MeV.
- Resonances found in $^{12}$C(d,n)$^{13}$N at incident deuteron energies of 1.6 MeV and 2.7 MeV.
- Statistical errors on yields below 4.2% (each measurement had counts above 560).
- Abundance of $^{12}$C in natural graphite target was 98.93%; contribution from $^{13}$C(d,n)$^{14}$N was within 3.3% of total neutron yield.
- Vacuum in target chamber reached 6.2×10⁻⁵ Pa; beam voltage fluctuation was lower than 2 keV.

## Context

This work extends prior measurements of $^{12}$C(d,n)$^{13}$N and $^{12}$C(d,p)$^{13}$C cross sections, which showed discrepancies in resonance energies around 1.4 MeV among different studies (Jaszczak 1969; Michelmann et al.; Papillon 1997). The ratio method provides a way to reduce systematic uncertainties from beam intensity variations and target sparking, enabling resonance identification without precise beam current measurement. The results are relevant for the Trojan Horse Method applied to $^{12}$C(p,γ)$^{13}$N, for ion beam analysis in metallurgy, and for clarifying neutron yields in laser-induced plasma experiments where $^{12}$C(d,n)$^{13}$N may interfere with d(d,n)$^{3}$He measurements.
