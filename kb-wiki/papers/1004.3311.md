# The Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background

> The Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB) is the weak glow of MeV neutrinos and antineutrinos from distant core-collapse supernovae. The DSNB has not been detected yet, but the Super-Kamiokande (SK) 2003 upper limit on the electron antineutrino flux is close to predictions, now quite precise, based on astrophysical data. If SK is modified with dissolved gadolinium to reduce detector backgrounds and increase the energy range for analysis, then it should detect the DSNB at a rate of a few events per year, providing a new probe of supernova neutrino emission and the cosmic core-collapse rate. If the DSNB is not detected, then new physics will be required. Neutrino astronomy, while uniquely powerful, has proven extremely difficult -- only the Sun and the nearby Supernova 1987A have been detected to date -- so the promise of detecting new sources soon is exciting indeed.

## Key claim

The Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB) has not yet been detected, but the Super-Kamiokande (SK) 2003 upper limit on the electron antineutrino flux is close to predictions that are now quite precise based on astrophysical data; if SK is modified with dissolved gadolinium to reduce detector backgrounds and increase the energy range for analysis, it should detect the DSNB at a rate of a few events per year, and if the DSNB is not detected, then new physics will be required.

## Method

The DSNB flux spectrum at Earth is calculated as a line-of-sight integral over redshift of the product of three terms: the supernova neutrino emission spectrum per supernova (characterized by total energy and average energy, approximated as a Fermi-Dirac spectrum), the evolving core-collapse supernova rate density derived from star formation rate measurements, and the differential cosmological distance element. The predicted flux is compared to the Super-Kamiokande 2003 upper limit on the electron antineutrino flux, which was obtained using inverse beta decay detection in a water Cherenkov detector with a positron energy threshold of 18 MeV.

## Key numbers

* The cosmic energy density in neutrinos from core-collapse supernovae is comparable to that in photons from stars, approximately 0.01 eV cm$^{-3}$, and is about 10 times less than that of the cosmic microwave background.
* The local supernova rate density is (1.25 plus or minus 0.25) times 10$^{-4}$ Mpc$^{-3}$ year$^{-1}$, and the rate grows by a factor of about 10 by redshift z = 1.
* The SK 2003 upper limit on the electron antineutrino flux is less than or approximately 1.2 cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$ for energies integrated above 19.3 MeV.
* The estimated DSNB detection rate in Super-Kamiokande is approximately 3 events per year.
* The total gravitational potential energy released in core collapse is approximately 3 times 10$^{53}$ erg.
* Nominal values for the electron antineutrino emission per supernova are a total energy of about 5 times 10$^{52}$ erg (3 times 10$^{58}$ MeV) and an average energy of about 15 MeV.
* The minimum mass star that leads to core collapse is (8.5 plus or minus 1.5) solar masses.

## Context

This review extends the long history of DSNB predictions dating back to Zel'dovich and Guseinov (1965) and Ruderman (1965), and builds on detailed calculations from the early 1980s by Berezinsky (1982), Dar (1984), and others. The work synthesizes recent precise measurements of the cosmic star formation rate and direct progenitor observations by Smartt (2008) to produce predictions with much smaller uncertainties than earlier work, and it enables future detection by Super-Kamiokande with gadolinium loading, which would provide a new probe of supernova neutrino emission and the cosmic core-collapse rate.
