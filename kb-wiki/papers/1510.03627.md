# Open heavy-flavor production and suppression in heavy-ion collisions

> Heavy-flavor observables are valuable probes of the quark-gluon plasma, which is expected to be produced in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. These experiments offer the unique opportunity to study strongly interacting matter at high temperatures and densities in the laboratory. In this overview talk I will summarize the current theoretical status of heavy-flavor production and suppression in heavy-ion collisions and discuss open challenges.

## Key claim
Heavy-flavor observables in heavy-ion collisions can probe quark-gluon plasma properties, but current theoretical models cannot yet reliably extract transport coefficients because the discriminative power is limited by large experimental error bars and by the fact that many different models based on pQCD-inspired interactions, nonperturbative scatterings, or strong-coupling approaches all reproduce the qualitative features of the nuclear modification factor and elliptic flow.

## Method
The paper reviews the theoretical status of heavy-flavor production and suppression, covering initial heavy-quark production via FONLL and event generators (POWHEG, MC@NLO), propagation through the QGP using either the Fokker-Planck/Langevin equation or the full Boltzmann equation, and hadronization via coalescence at low pT and fragmentation at high pT. The medium evolution is modeled either by a parton cascade or by fluid dynamics, with scattering partners sampled from thermal distributions using local temperature and fluid velocity fields.

## Key numbers
- Charm quark mass: mc ~ 1.3-1.5 GeV; bottom quark mass: mb ~ 4.5-5.1 GeV
- LHC beam energy: sqrt(s) = 2.76 TeV (soon 5.1 TeV); RHIC beam energy: sqrt(s) = 200 GeV
- QGP formation time: tau_QGP ~ 0.3-1.0 fm/c
- Hadronic final-state interactions contribute 10-20% depending on the observable
- The spatial diffusion coefficient from lattice QCD is shown with large uncertainty; the continuum extrapolated value is referenced from Kaczmarek (no single number given for the coefficient itself)

## Context
This overview talk extends the theoretical framework for heavy-flavor probes discussed in prior work by Jacak (2012) on QGP formation and by Song (2010) and Schenke (2011) on shear viscosity. It summarizes the current status of models including the MC@sHQ+EPOS2 approach by Nahrgang (2013, 2014) and compares to data from ALICE. The paper identifies open challenges including the need for higher-statistics data from upcoming RHIC runs with the heavy-flavor tracker at STAR and from LHC runs, the study of asymmetric systems like p+Pb to distinguish cold nuclear matter effects from hot medium effects, and the use of new observables such as azimuthal correlations between heavy-flavor pairs and higher-order flow harmonics.
