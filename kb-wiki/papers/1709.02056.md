# High cumulants of conserved charges and their statistical uncertainties

> We study the influence of measured high cumulants of conserved charges on their associated statistical uncertainties in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. With a given number of events, the measured cumulants randomly fluctuate with an approximately normal distribution, while the estimated statistical uncertainties are found to be correlated with corresponding values of the obtained cumulants. Generally, with a given number of events, the larger the cumulants we measure, the larger the statistical uncertainties that are estimated. The error-weighted averaged cumulants are dependent on statistics. Despite this effect, however, it is found that the three sigma rule of thumb is still applicable when the statistics are above one million.

# High cumulants of conserved charges and their statistical uncertainties

## Key claim

The estimated statistical uncertainty of the cumulant ratio $\kappa\sigma^2$ is correlated with the measured value itself: larger measured cumulants yield larger estimated errors, and this correlation cannot be eliminated as long as the same data are used for both the measurement and the error estimation. Despite this correlation, the three-sigma rule of thumb remains applicable when the event statistics exceed one million.

## Method

The authors performed Monte Carlo simulations of net-particle multiplicity distributions using a Skellam distribution with parameters $m_1=14.5$ and $m_2=0.6$ (modeling net-proton distributions at RHIC/STAR low energies) and $m_1=79.0$ and $m_2=65.0$ (modeling net-charge distributions). They generated 5 million independent random samples for each of several event multiplicities (0.01M, 0.1M, 1M events) and computed $\kappa\sigma^2 \equiv C_4/C_2$ and its statistical error from each sample, then examined the correlation between the measured value and its estimated error, as well as the behavior of event-weighted versus error-weighted averages.

## Key numbers

- For 0.01M events, the probability distribution of $\kappa\sigma^2$ has mean 0.9895 and width 0.91; for 0.1M events, mean 0.9989 and width 0.29; for 1M events, mean 0.9998 and width 0.092.
- When $\kappa\sigma^2$ equals unity, the averaged estimated error $\left<error\right>$ is 0.91 for 0.01M events, 0.29 for 0.1M events, and 0.092 for 1M events.
- The probability that $|\kappa\sigma^2 - \left<\kappa\sigma^2\right>|/error$ exceeds 3.0 is 0.74% for 0.01M events, 0.34% for 0.1M events, and 0.28% for 1M events (compared to 0.3% for a normal distribution).
- The probability that $|\kappa\sigma^2 - \left<\kappa\sigma^2\right>|/error$ exceeds 4.0 is 0.079% for 0.01M events, 0.015% for 0.1M events, and 0.0077% for 1M events (compared to 0.007% for a normal distribution).
- At $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 11.5$ GeV in Au+Au collisions, the analyzed statistics for net-proton cumulants is 6.6M events; within 0-5% centrality with 100 multiplicity bins, each $N_{ch}$ bin contains approximately 4100 events.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of centrality bin width correction (CBWC) by Luo (2013) and by Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which had indicated that estimated errors depend on measured cumulant values. The paper directly addresses the practical issue that at RHIC Beam Energy Scan I statistics, particularly at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} \le 11.5$ GeV, the event-weighted CBWC method may still underestimate $\kappa\sigma^2$ because the number of events per multiplicity bin falls below the required 0.01M threshold. The results enable experimentalists to assess the reliability of significance claims for cumulant measurements and to choose appropriate averaging schemes when comparing to lattice QCD predictions or searching for the QCD critical point.
