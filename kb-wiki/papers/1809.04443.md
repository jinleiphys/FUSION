# Two-neutrino double electron capture on $^{124}$Xe based on an effective theory and the nuclear shell model

> We study the two-neutrino double electron capture on $^{124}$Xe based on an effective theory (ET) and large-scale shell model calculations, two modern nuclear structure approaches that have been tested against Gamow-Teller and double-beta decay data. In the ET, the low-energy constants are fit to electron capture and $β^{-}$ transitions around xenon. For the nuclear shell model, we use an interaction in a large configuration space that reproduces the spectroscopy of nuclei in this mass region. For the dominant transition to the $^{124}$Te ground state, we find half-lives $T^{2ν{\rm ECEC}}_{1/2}=(1.3-18)\times 10^{22}$ y for the ET and $T^{2ν{\rm ECEC}}_{1/2} = (0.43-2.9)\times 10^{22}$ y for the shell model. The ET uncertainty leads to a half-life almost entirely consistent with present experimental limits and largely within the reach of ongoing experiments. The shell model half-life range overlaps with the ET, but extends less beyond current limits. Our findings thus suggest that the two-neutrino double electron capture on $^{124}$Xe has a good chance to be discovered by ongoing or future experiments. In addition, we present results for the two-neutrino double electron capture to excited states of $^{124}$Te.

# Two-neutrino double electron capture on $^{124}$Xe based on an effective theory and the nuclear shell model

## Key claim
The two-neutrino double electron capture on $^{124}$Xe has a good chance to be discovered by ongoing or future experiments, but the effective theory predicts a half-life range that is almost entirely consistent with present experimental limits, while the shell model range extends less beyond current limits and is partially excluded by the most recent XMASS limit.

## Method
The authors calculate nuclear matrix elements for $2\nu$ECEC on $^{124}$Xe using two complementary approaches: an effective theory (ET) that describes the initial and final nuclei as spherical collective cores and uses the single-state dominance approximation, and large-scale nuclear shell model calculations with the GCN5082 interaction in a truncated configuration space comprising the $0g_{7/2}$, $1d_{5/2}$, $1d_{3/2}$, $2s_{1/2}$, and $0h_{11/2}$ orbitals. The ET low-energy constants are fitted to electron capture and $\beta^-$ transitions on neighboring iodine isotopes, while the shell model uses three different quenching factors ($q=0.57$, $0.48$, $0.42$) for the spin-isospin operator to account for missing correlations.

## Key numbers
- Half-life for $^{124}$Xe $2\nu$ECEC to $^{124}$Te ground state (two K-shell electrons): ET predicts $T^{2\nu\text{ECEC}}_{1/2} = (1.3-18) \times 10^{22}$ y; shell model predicts $T^{2\nu\text{ECEC}}_{1/2} = (0.43-2.9) \times 10^{22}$ y
- $Q$-value of $^{124}$Xe: 2857 keV
- Phase-space factor for ground-state transition: $G^{2\nu\text{ECEC}} = 1.72 \times 10^{-20}$ y$^{-1}$
- ET nuclear matrix element for ground-state transition: $M^{2\nu\text{ECEC}} = 0.011-0.041$
- Shell model nuclear matrix element for ground-state transition: $M^{2\nu\text{ECEC}} = 0.028-0.072$
- Excitation energy of lowest $1^+_1$ state in $^{124}$I set to 105-170 keV
- $\log(ft)^{\text{EC}} = 5.00(10)$ for EC on the lowest $1^+_1$ state in $^{124}$I, based on iodine isotopes $A=122-128$
- Collective mode excitation energy: $\omega = 478.3$ keV

## Context
This work extends the effective theory approach previously applied to Gamow-Teller and two-neutrino $\beta\beta$ decays of heavy nuclei including $^{128,130}$Te (Coello Perez 2017) to the $2\nu$ECEC mode on $^{124}$Xe, and presents the first large-scale nuclear shell model calculation for this process. The results are compared with quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) calculations by Suhonen (2013) and Pirinen (2015), and with experimental limits from XMASS (Abe 2016, 2018), XENON100 (Aprile 2017), and Gavrilyuk (2018). The findings enable experimental searches to refine their sensitivity expectations and suggest that the $^{124}$Xe $2\nu$ECEC could be discovered in ongoing dark matter detectors such as XMASS, XENON100, and LUX.
