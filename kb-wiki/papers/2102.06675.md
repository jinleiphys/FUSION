# The role of the electric Bond number in the stability of pasta phases

> The stability of pasta phases in cylindrical and spherical Wigner-Seitz (W-S) cells is examined. The electric Bond number is introduced as the ratio of electric and surface energies. In the case of a charged rod in vacuum, other kinds of instabilities appear in addition to the well known Plateau- Rayleigh mode. For the case of a rod confined in a W-S cell the variety of unstable modes is reduced. It comes from the virial theorem, which bounds the value of the Bond number from above and reduces the role played by electric forces. A similar analysis is done for the spherical W-S cell, where it appears that the inclusion of the virial theorem stabilizes all of the modes.

# The role of the electric Bond number in the stability of pasta phases

## Key claim

The stability of pasta phases (cylindrical rods and spherical balls) in Wigner-Seitz cells depends critically on the electric Bond number, which the virial theorem constrains to a bounded range (0 to 4 for cylinders, 5/6 to 5 for spheres); this bound eliminates most unstable modes, leaving only the Plateau-Rayleigh hourglass mode unstable for very small volume fractions in the cylindrical case, and stabilizing all modes in the spherical case, contrary to the vacuum case where a charged rod is always unstable regardless of the Bond number.

## Method

The authors performed a second-order energy variation analysis for shape perturbations of charged proton clusters in cylindrical and spherical Wigner-Seitz cells, using the generalized Green's function with Neumann boundary conditions to enforce cell neutrality. They examined three specific deformation modes (flattening, hourglass, and gyroid) for the cylindrical rod and Legendre polynomial modes for the spherical ball, computing stability functions that depend on the dimensionless wavelength, the electric Bond number (ratio of electric to surface energy), and the volume fraction occupied by the cluster. The virial theorem was then applied to eliminate the Bond number as an independent parameter, yielding a stability condition expressed solely in terms of the volume fraction.

## Key numbers

- For a charged rod in vacuum, the Plateau-Rayleigh instability occurs at wavelength L = 2πR, corresponding to x = 1 in the dimensionless parameter x = L/(2πR).
- For the cylindrical Wigner-Seitz cell, the virial theorem constrains the electric Bond number to the range 0 < Bo_e^{vir} < 4, given by the formula ζ(w) = 2(w-1)²/(w - ln w - 1).
- For the spherical Wigner-Seitz cell, the virial theorem constrains the Bond number to the range 5/6 < Bo_e^{vir} < 5, given by ζ(w) = 5(w-1)²/[3(w - 3w^{1/3} + 2)].
- The Bohr-Wheeler instability condition for an isolated nucleus (spherical case, w → 0) corresponds to a Bond number of ζ = 10/3.
- The stability function for the spherical cell quadrupole mode (l=2) is f(w,ζ) = (1/3)(9w^{5/3} + 10w - 4) + (40/9ζ)(w-1)².

## Context

This work extends the stability analysis of pasta phases beyond the elastic constant approach of Pethick and Potekhin (1998) and the single-mode analysis of Iida et al. (2001), providing a complete treatment of all perturbation modes for rods and balls in Wigner-Seitz cells. The authors previously showed that lasagna (slab) phases are stable for all modes and volume fractions (Kubis, 2017). The present results demonstrate that the virial theorem, which enforces the relation E_s = 2E_Coul from cell-size minimization, bounds the electric Bond number and thereby stabilizes most pasta-phase deformations, though the cylindrical case retains a narrow instability region for the hourglass mode at very small volume fractions. A more realistic treatment would require introducing true periodicity of the lattice rather than isolated Wigner-Seitz cells.
