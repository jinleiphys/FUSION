# Quarkonium Contribution to Meson Molecules

> Starting from a molecular picture for the X(3872) resonance, this state and its J^{PC}=2++ HQSS partner [X2(4012)] are analyzed within a model which incorporates possible mixings with 2P charmonium states. Since it is reasonable to expect the bare chi_{c1}(2P) to be located above the D\bar D* threshold, but relatively close to it, the presence of the charmonium state provides an effective attraction that will contribute to bind the X(3872), but it will not appear in the 2++ sector. Indeed in this latter sector, the chi_{c2}(2P) should provide an effective small repulsion, because it is placed well below the D*\bar D* threshold. We show how the 1++ and 2++ bare charmonium poles are modified due to the D(*)\bar D(*) loop effects, and the first one is moved to the complex plane. The meson loops produce, besides some shifts in the masses of the charmonia, a finite width for the 1++ dressed charmonium state. On the other hand, the X(3872) and X2(4012) start developing some charmonium content, which is estimated by means of the compositeness Weinberg sum-rule. We also show that for X(3872) molecular probabilities of around 70-90 %, the X2 resonance destabilizes and disappears from the spectrum, becoming either a virtual state or being located deep into the complex plane, with decreasing influence in the D* \bar D* scattering line.

## Key claim

The X(3872) resonance can be described as a predominantly molecular state (70-90% molecular probability) with a 10-30% charmonium admixture from the χc1(2P) state, where the charmonium component provides effective attraction that contributes to binding the X(3872) but does not appear in the 2++ sector, where the χc2(2P) instead provides effective small repulsion because it lies well below the D*D̅* threshold.

## Method

The authors extend a heavy quark spin symmetry (HQSS) effective field theory approach, previously used to describe D(*)D̅(*) molecules via contact-range potentials, by incorporating 2P charmonium degrees of freedom and their mixings with molecular components. They solve renormalized Lippmann-Schwinger equations for coupled-channel amplitudes in the 1++ and 2++ sectors, using the experimental ratio of radiative decays Rψγ = Br(X→ψ(2S)γ)/Br(X→J/ψγ) to constrain the charmonium-molecular coupling. The compositeness Weinberg sum-rule is employed to quantify molecular versus charmonium content in the resulting states.

## Key numbers

- X(3872) averaged mass: 3871.69 ± 0.17 MeV, which is 0.16 MeV below the D0D̅*0 threshold, with full width less than 1.2 MeV
- Ratio of branching fractions Rψγ = Br(X→ψ(2S)γ)/Br(X→J/ψγ) = 2.46 ± 0.64 ± 0.29
- For X(3872) molecular probabilities of 70-90%, the X2 resonance destabilizes and disappears from the spectrum, becoming either a virtual state or located deep in the complex plane
- The X2 mass is predicted to satisfy MX2 - MX(3872) ≈ MD* - MD ≈ 140 MeV
- In the bottom sector, MXb2 - MXb ≈ MB* - MB ≈ 46 MeV

## Context

This work extends the HQSS EFT approach of Nieves (2012) and Hidalgo Duque (2012), which predicted X2, Xb, and Xb2 states as purely molecular D(*)D̅(*) bound states, by incorporating charmonium-molecular mixing that was not considered in those earlier works. The study is motivated by lattice QCD simulations by Prelovsek (2013) that required both charmonium and molecular interpolating fields to find a signature of X(3872), and by phenomenological studies by Dong (2009) and Takizawa (2012) suggesting that a charmonium admixture of 5-12% could explain the radiative decay ratio Rψγ. The results enable predictions for the observability of X2, Xb, and Xb2 states in experiments such as BESIII, LHCb, CMS, Belle-II, and PANDA, suggesting that even small charmonium mixings might explain why X2 has not been observed yet, while Xb and Xb2 should be real QCD states that might be observed in the near future.
