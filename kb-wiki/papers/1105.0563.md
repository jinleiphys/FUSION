# Density and Temperature of Bosons from Quantum Fluctuations

> A method to determine the density and temperature of a system is proposed based on quantum fluctuations typical of Bosons in the limit where the reached temperature T is close to the critical temperature $T_c$ for a Bose condensate at a given density $ρ$. Quadrupole and particle multiplicity fluctuations relations are derived in terms of $\frac{T}{T_c}$. This method is valid for weakly interacting infinite and finite Boson systems. As an example, we apply it to heavy ion collisions using the Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) approach which includes the Fermi statistics. The model shows some clusterization into deuteron and $α$ clusters which could suggest a Bose condensate. However, our approach demonstrates that in the model there is no Bose condensate but it gives useful informations to be tested experimentally. We stress the differences with methods based on classical approximations. The derived 'quantum' temperatures are systematically higher than the corresponding 'classical' ones. The role of the Coulomb charge of fragments is discussed.

## Key claim

A method to determine density and temperature from quadrupole and multiplicity fluctuations is derived for weakly interacting Bose systems near the critical temperature for Bose-Einstein condensation, but when applied to CoMD simulations of heavy ion collisions, the approach demonstrates that no Bose condensate actually forms in the model despite clusterization into deuteron and alpha clusters.

## Method

Quadrupole fluctuations (variance of \(p_x^2 - p_y^2\)) and multiplicity fluctuations (normalized variance of particle number) were derived analytically using a Bose-Einstein momentum distribution expanded near the critical temperature \(T_c\). These relations were then applied to event-by-event data from Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) simulations of \(^{40}\text{Ca}+^{40}\text{Ca}\) collisions at fixed impact parameter \(b=1\) fm and beam energies from 4 MeV/A to 100 MeV/A, with over 200,000 events per beam energy.

## Key numbers

- Beam energies simulated: 4 MeV/A up to 100 MeV/A for \(^{40}\text{Ca}+^{40}\text{Ca}\) collisions at impact parameter \(b=1\) fm
- Maximum simulation time: \(t=1000\) fm/c
- Over 200,000 events per beam energy were calculated
- For \(T < T_c\), the quadrupole fluctuation formula gives \(\sigma^2_{xy} = (2mT)^2 \frac{g_{7/2}(0)}{g_{3/2}(0)}\) where the ratio is a constant
- For \(T > T_c\), the multiplicity fluctuation formula is \(\frac{\langle(\Delta N)^2\rangle}{\bar N} = 0.921 \frac{(T/T_c)^3}{[1-(T/T_c)^{3/2}]^2}\)
- The classical Maxwell-Boltzmann result gives \(\sigma^2_{xy} = \bar N 4m^2 T_{cl}^2\)

## Context

This work extends the fluctuation-based approach previously applied to trapped Fermi gases (ref. prl) and Bose condensates (ref. bose) to subatomic systems produced in heavy ion collisions, building on an earlier method for fermions by the same authors (Zheng and Bonasera, plb1). The approach contrasts with classical approximations used in many heavy ion collision analyses near the Fermi energy (Albergo, Pochodzalla, and others) by incorporating quantum statistics. The method enables determination of both density and temperature from event-by-event fragment measurements, and could be applied to experimental data to search for signatures of Bose-Einstein condensation of alpha clusters, Mott transitions, and pairing in low-density nuclear matter.
