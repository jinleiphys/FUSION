# Collectivity, chaos, and computers

> Two important pieces of nuclear structure are many-body collective deformations and single-particle spin-orbit splitting. The former can be well-described microscopically by simple SU(3) irreps, but the latter mixes SU(3) irreps, which presents a challenge for large-scale, ab initio calculations on fast modern computers. Nonetheless, SU(3)-like phenomenology remains even in the face of strong mixing. The robustness of band structure is reminiscent of robust, pairing collectivity that arises from random two-body interactions.

# Collectivity, chaos, and computers

## Key claim
Despite strong mixing of SU(3) irreps by single-particle spin-orbit splitting, SU(3)-like phenomenology (rotational band structure) remains robust in nuclear spectra; this robustness is reminiscent of pairing collectivity that arises from random two-body interactions, but a general theory explaining how collective behavior emerges generically from messy interactions is still lacking.

## Method
The author computed exact wavefunctions for realistic interactions (Wildenthal's USD interaction in the sd-shell and the monopole-modified KB3 interaction in the pf-shell) and used a Lanczos moment method to project the distribution of these wavefunctions onto SU(3) irreps without computing all SU(3) eigenstates. The mixing of SU(3) irreps was compared with and without spin-orbit splitting to isolate its effect. Computational efficiency was assessed by counting the number of Slater determinants needed to represent states in spherical, cylindrical (SU(3)), and Hartree-Fock bases.

## Key numbers
- In the sd-shell and lower pf-shell, single-particle spin-orbit splitting is "by far the most important source of mixing of SU(3) irreps" (no explicit numerical fragmentation fraction given).
- For ²⁴Mg in the sd-shell, the leading (8,4) SU(3) irrep outperforms the simplest spherical configuration (d₅/₂)⁸ in terms of binding energy and B(E2) values (no explicit numerical values given).
- In full 0ħω shell-model calculations, spherical-basis codes compute ²⁴Mg roughly ten times faster than SU(3)-basis codes for the full space including all configurations.
- Projection of good angular momentum in spherical shell-model space requires "a few tens or a few hundred Slater determinants"; in SU(3) codes it requires "several hundred or even more than a thousand Slater determinants."

## Context
This paper extends the work of Gueorguiev et al. (2002) on hybrid SU(3)-spherical bases and Bahri and Rowe (2000) on quasi-dynamical symmetry, which explains how fragmented wavefunctions can still produce SU(3)-like spectra. It contrasts with the spherical interacting shell model codes (Glasgow code, OXBASH, ANTOINE, REDSTICK) that treat spin-orbit splitting trivially but handle deformation poorly. The work enables future development of "optimal basis states" combining symplectic states with generator coordinate methods (Carroll 2002) for large-scale ab initio calculations that must simultaneously account for deformation and spin-orbit splitting.
