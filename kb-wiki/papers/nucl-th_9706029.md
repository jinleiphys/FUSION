# How to Renormalize the Schrodinger Equation

> These lectures illustrate the key ideas of modern renormalization theory and effective field theories in the context of simple nonrelativistic quantum mechanics and the Schrödinger equation. They also discuss problems in QED, QCD and nuclear physics for which rigorous potential models can be derived using renormalization techniques. They end with an analysis of nucleon-nucleon scattering based effective theory.

## Key claim

Modern renormalization theory allows one to construct effective theories that reproduce arbitrary low-energy data with arbitrary precision, even when the true short-distance potential is unknown and the naive delta-function approximation leads to infinities beyond first-order perturbation theory.

## Method

The author generates synthetic low-energy data (binding energies, S-wave phase shifts, and matrix elements) by numerically solving the Schrodinger equation for a Coulomb potential plus an undisclosed short-range potential V_s(r) with m=1 and alpha=1. He then constructs an effective theory by: (1) incorporating the correct long-range Coulomb behavior, (2) introducing an ultraviolet cutoff a (with Lambda = 1/a) that regulates the Coulomb potential via the error function and smears delta functions as Gaussians of width a, and (3) adding local contact terms (c a^2 delta_a^3(r), d_1 a^4 nabla^2 delta_a^3(r), d_2 a^4 nabla dot delta_a^3(r) nabla, and higher orders) whose dimensionless couplings are tuned to reproduce the data. The method is illustrated with both nonperturbative numerical solutions and one-loop perturbation theory.

## Context

These lectures extend the modern renormalization theory and effective field theory approach, previously applied to QED and QCD, into the context of nonrelativistic quantum mechanics and the Schrodinger equation. The work directly contradicts the conventional wisdom (seen in textbooks and conference proceedings) that approximations must be abandoned once infinities appear and that only knowledge of the true potential can resolve such difficulties. The approach enables systematic analysis of low-energy nucleon-nucleon interactions using effective potential theory, and provides a framework for deriving rigorous potential models for problems in QED, QCD, and nuclear physics where the short-distance structure is unknown or complicated.
