# Cavitation from bulk viscosity in neutron stars and quark stars

> The bulk viscosity in quark matter is sufficiently high to reduce the effective pressure below the corresponding vapor pressure during density perturbations in neutron stars and strange stars. This leads to mechanical instability where the quark matter breaks apart into fragments comparable to cavitation scenarios discussed for ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Similar phenomena may take place in kaon-condensed stellar cores. Possible applications to compact star phenomenology include a new mechanism for damping oscillations and instabilities, triggering of phase transitions, changes in gravitational wave signatures of binary star inspiral, and astrophysical formation of strangelets. At a more fundamental level it points to the possible inadequacy of a hydrodynamical treatment of these processes in compact stars.

# Cavitation from bulk viscosity in neutron stars and quark stars

## Key claim

Bulk viscosity in dense quark matter can be so high that the effective pressure during density perturbations in compact stars drops below the vapor pressure, causing mechanical instability and cavitation (fragmentation of the fluid), but this effect is conditional on specific parameter ranges of oscillation frequency, amplitude, and microscopic relaxation timescales, and does not occur for ordinary nuclear matter.

## Method

The author derives the condition for cavitation using the relativistic hydrodynamic expression for effective pressure, \(P_{\text{eff}} = P - \zeta \Theta\), where \(\zeta\) is bulk viscosity and \(\Theta\) is the expansion scalar. The bulk viscosity is expressed in terms of the difference between adiabatic indices for frozen and equilibrium composition, \(\Delta \equiv \Gamma_{\text{Fr}} - \Gamma_{\text{Eq}}\), and the microscopic relaxation time \(\tau\). Using the MIT bag model for quark matter with strange quark mass and bag constant as parameters, the author calculates \(\Gamma_{\text{Fr}}\) and \(\Gamma_{\text{Eq}}\) as functions of baryon density to determine where the necessary condition for cavitation, \(\Delta > \Delta_{\text{C}}\), is satisfied.

## Key numbers

- For a pressure of \(10^{35}\) dyn/cm\(^2\), bulk viscosity of \(10^{31}\) g/cm·s, and expansion scalar \(\Theta > 10^{4}\) s\(^{-1}\), the condition \(P_{\text{eff}} < 0\) occurs.
- For a typical compact star mass of 1.4 solar masses, \(\Delta > 10\) in a surface layer stretching to a depth of 100 meters and \(\Delta > 100\) in the outer few meters of a strange star.
- The microscopic relaxation time \(\tau\) for quark matter can take values from \(10^{-10}\) to \(10\) seconds depending on temperature and strange quark mass.
- For nuclear matter with kaon condensates, the highest published value for \(\Delta\) is around 5 (Chatterjee, 2007).
- For nuclear matter without kaon condensates, the values of \(\Gamma_{\text{Fr}}\) and \(\Gamma_{\text{Eq}}\) differ at most by 0.8 (Haensel, 2002).
- The bag constant range for stable strange stars is \(145\) MeV \(< B^{1/4} < 165\) MeV depending on strange quark mass.

## Context

This work extends the concept of cavitation from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions (Torrieri, 2008; Rajagopal, 2009) to the high-chemical-potential, low-temperature regime of compact stars. It builds on prior calculations of bulk viscosity in dense nuclear and quark matter (Sawyer, 1989; Madsen, 1992; Alford, 2006) and on the formalism linking bulk viscosity to adiabatic indices (Lindblom, 2001). The paper enables new investigations of damping mechanisms for stellar oscillations and r-mode instabilities (Andersson, 1997), modifications to gravitational wave signatures from binary inspiral (Bauswein, 2008), triggering of phase transitions, and astrophysical formation of strangelets (Madsen, 2004).
