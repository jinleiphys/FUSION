# The NDL Equation of State for Supernova Simulations

> We present an updated and improved equation of state (which we call the NDL EoS) for use in neutron-star structure and supernova simulations. This EoS is based upon a framework originally developed by Bowers & Wilson, but there are numerous changes. Among them are: (1) a reformulation in the context of density functional theory; (2) the possibility of the formation of material with a net proton excess (Ye > 0.5); (3) an improved treatment of the nuclear statistical equilibrium and the transition to heavy nuclei as the density approaches nuclear matter density; (4) an improved treatment of the effects of pions in the regime above nuclear matter density including the incorporation of all the known mesonic and baryonic states at high temperature; (5) the effects of 3-body nuclear forces at high densities; and (6) the possibility of a first-order or crossover transition to a QCD chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement phase at densities above nuclear matter density. This paper details the physics of, and constraints on, this new EoS and describes its implementation in numerical simulations. We show comparisons of this EoS with other equations of state commonly used in supernova collapse simulations.

## Key claim

The NDL Equation of State updates the Bowers & Wilson framework with six specific improvements, but the paper's central hedge is that the EoS is "consistent with known experimental nuclear matter constraints and recent mass and radius measurements of neutron stars" rather than claiming definitive agreement with all supernova observations.

## Method

The authors construct a tabular equation of state covering four regimes: baryons below saturation not in nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE), baryons below saturation in NSE, hadronic matter above saturation density including pions, and a possible phase transition to quark-gluon plasma. Below saturation and above T ≈ 0.5 MeV, NSE is imposed using a four-component fluid of free neutrons, protons, alphas, and an average heavy nucleus; below that temperature a nine-element reaction network is evolved dynamically. Above saturation density, a parameterized Skyrme energy density functional with a modified zero-range 3-body interaction is employed, with coefficients determined by fixing saturation density, binding energy, compressibility, and skewness from literature values while requiring neutron star masses ≥ 1.97 ± 0.04 M⊙.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density n₀ = 0.16 ± 0.01 fm⁻³ (adopted constraint)
- Binding energy per nucleon E₀ = −16 ± 1 MeV (adopted constraint)
- Compressibility K₀ = 240 ± 10 MeV (adopted constraint)
- Skewness coefficient Q₀ = −390 ± 90 MeV (determined in this work)
- Maximum neutron star mass constraint: ≥ 1.97 ± 0.04 M⊙ (from Demorest)
- Energy per nucleon to dissociate ⁵⁶Fe into free nucleons: εₚ₀ = 8.37 MeV for protons, εₙ₀ = 9.15 MeV for neutrons
- Transition density from subnuclear to uniform nuclear matter: ρ_N = 2.66 × 10¹⁴ [1 − (1 − 2Y_e)⁵/²] g cm⁻³
- Average heavy nucleus mass formula: ⟨A⟩ = 194.0 (1 − Y_e)² (1 + X + 2X² + 3X³) with X ≡ (ρ / 7.6 × 10¹³ g cm⁻³)¹/³
- Symmetry energy constant S_E = 120 MeV, derived for a symmetry energy of 30.4 MeV per nucleon
- Statistical weight temperature scale: T_S = (8 MeV) (1 + 2ρ/ρ_N)

## Context

This work extends the original Livermore equation of state framework of Bowers & Wilson (1982) and Wilson & Mathews, reformulating it in the context of density functional theory. It contrasts with the two most commonly used supernova EoS tables: Lattimer & Swesty (1991), which uses a non-relativistic compressible liquid drop model, and Shen et al. (1998), which is based on Relativistic Mean Field theory with the TM1 parameter set. The NDL EoS also differs from more recent tables such as those of Hempel et al. (2010) and Steiner et al. (2012) by including pions, all known mesonic and baryonic states at high temperature, and the possibility of a transition to quark-gluon plasma. The publicly available tabular form enables direct implementation in core-collapse supernova and neutron-star simulations.
