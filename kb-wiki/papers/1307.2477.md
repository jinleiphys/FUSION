# Bivelocity picture in the nonrelativistic limit of relativistic hydrodynamics

> We discuss the nonrelativistic limit of the relativistic Navier-Fourier-Stokes (NFS) theory. The next-to-leading order relativistic corrections to the NFS theory for the Landau-Lifshitz fluid are obtained. While the lowest order truncation of the velocity expansion leads to the usual NFS equations of nonrelativistic fluids, we show that when the next-to-leading order relativistic corrections are included, the equations can be expressed concurrently with two different fluid velocities. One of the fluid velocities is parallel to the conserved charge current (which follows the Eckart definition) and the other one is parallel to the energy current (which follows the Landau-Lifshitz definition). We compare this next-to-leading order relativistic hydrodynamics with bivelocity hydrodynamics, which is one of the generalizations of the NFS theory and is formulated in such a way to include the usual mass velocity and also a new velocity, called the volume velocity. We find that the volume velocity can be identified with the velocity obtained in the Landau-Lifshitz definition. Then, the structure of bivelocity hydrodynamics, which is derived using various nontrivial assumptions, is reproduced in the NFS theory including the next-to-leading order relativistic corrections.

# Bivelocity picture in the nonrelativistic limit of relativistic hydrodynamics

## Key claim

The next-to-leading order relativistic corrections to the Navier-Fourier-Stokes (NFS) theory for the Landau-Lifshitz fluid can be expressed using two distinct fluid velocities concurrently, one parallel to the conserved charge current (Eckart definition) and one parallel to the energy current (Landau-Lifshitz definition), and this structure reproduces bivelocity hydrodynamics, which was previously derived using various nontrivial assumptions.

## Method

The authors start from the relativistic Landau-Lifshitz formulation of hydrodynamics and perform a systematic expansion of the hydrodynamic variables in powers of v_L/c, where v_L is the Landau-Lifshitz fluid velocity. They truncate at leading order to recover the standard NFS equations, then keep terms up to O(v_L^2/c^2) to obtain the next-to-leading order (NLO) relativistic corrections. The resulting NLO equations are rewritten using both the Landau-Lifshitz velocity and the Eckart velocity, and the structure is compared to the bivelocity hydrodynamics formalism of Brenner.

## Key numbers

- The difference between the Eckart and Landau-Lifshitz fluid velocities is v_E^i - v_L^i = (m c)/(q rho_m) nu^i + O(v_L^4/c^4), where nu^i is the diffusion current which is proportional to O(v_L^3/c^3)
- The proper scalar energy density expanded to O(v_L^2/c^2) is epsilon_L = rho_m (c^2 + hat{u}) + (1/2) rho_m v_L^2 - (1/c^2)[(1/2) rho_m v_L^2 hat{u} + (1/8) rho_m (v_L^2)^2] + O(v_L^4/c^4)
- The conserved charge density n_L expanded to O(v_L^2/c^2) is n_L = (q/m) rho_m - (q/(2m)) rho_m (v_L^2/c^2) + O(v_L^4/c^4)
- The shear viscosity tensor pi^{ij}_L and bulk viscous pressure Pi_L are both proportional to O(v_L^0/c^0) at leading order, while the diffusion current nu^i is proportional to O(v_L^3/c^3)

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Chandrasekhar (1958) and Greenberg (1971) on the nonrelativistic limit of relativistic hydrodynamics, which only considered the Eckart definition of fluid velocity and did not discuss the role of two fluid velocities. The authors compare their NLO results with the bivelocity hydrodynamics proposed by Brenner (2005-2013), which introduces a mass velocity and a volume velocity. The key finding is that the volume velocity in bivelocity hydrodynamics can be identified with the Landau-Lifshitz velocity, and the structure of bivelocity hydrodynamics emerges naturally from relativistic NFS theory including NLO corrections, without requiring the nontrivial assumptions of the original bivelocity formulation. This enables a deeper theoretical foundation for bivelocity hydrodynamics and clarifies the physical meaning of the two velocities in terms of well-established relativistic fluid definitions.
