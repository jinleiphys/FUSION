# Dissipative reactions with intermediate-energy beams -- a novel approach to populate complex-structure states in rare isotopes

> A novel pathway for the formation of multi-particle-multi-hole (np-mh) excited states in rare isotopes is reported from highly energy- and momentum-dissipative inelastic-scattering events measured in reactions of an intermediate-energy beam of 38Ca on a Be target. The negative-parity,complex-structure final states in 38Ca were observed following the in-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy of events in the 9Be(38Ca,38Ca+gamma)X reaction in which the scattered projectile lost longitudinal momentum of order p = 700 MeV/c. The characteristics of the observed final states are discussed and found to be consistent with the formation of excited states involving the rearrangement of multiple nucleons in a single, highly-energetic projectile-target collision. Unlike the far-less dissipative, surface-grazing reactions usually exploited for the in-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy of rare isotopes, these more energetic collisions appear to offer a practical pathway to nuclear-structure studies of more complex multi-particle configurations in rare isotopes - final states conventionally thought to be out of reach with high-luminosity fast-beam-induced reactions.

## Key claim

The paper reports that highly energy- and momentum-dissipative inelastic-scattering events of an intermediate-energy beam of 38Ca on a Be target can populate multi-particle-multi-hole (np-mh) excited states in rare isotopes, specifically negative-parity, complex-structure final states in 38Ca, which are conventionally thought to be out of reach with high-luminosity fast-beam-induced reactions. The claim is hedged by the observation that the specific reaction dynamics are unclear and remain a challenge for future, more complete and exclusive measurements.

## Method

The experiment used a 38Ca rare-isotope beam produced by fragmentation of a stable 40Ca beam accelerated to 140 MeV/nucleon, impinging on a 188-mg/cm2-thick 9Be foil at the target position of the S800 spectrograph. The magnetic rigidity of the S800 spectrograph was tuned for 36Ca, transmitting only the low-momentum tail of the reacted 38Ca distribution, and prompt gamma-ray detection was performed using the GRETINA array of 48 high-purity germanium crystals to tag final states via event-by-event Doppler reconstruction. Gamma-gamma coincidences were used to construct the level scheme, and shell-model calculations using the FSU cross-shell effective interaction were compared to the observed states.

## Key numbers

- The scattered projectile lost longitudinal momentum of order p = 700 MeV/c (approximately 18 MeV/c per nucleon or 5.4 MeV/nucleon in energy).
- The cross section for finding 38Ca with such large momentum loss was extracted to be sigma(p0 +/- 330 MeV/c) = 3.8(4) mb.
- The mid-target energy of 38Ca in the 9Be reaction target was 60.9 MeV/nucleon.
- The S800 momentum acceptance at the setting was p0 +/- 330 MeV/c, with p0 = 11.222 GeV/c for the reaction setting and p0 = 11.932 GeV/c for unreacted 38Ca.
- Gamma-ray transitions observed include those at 2213(5), 1489(5), 489(4), and 3684(8) keV, with new transitions reported at 214(4), 1048(6), 2417(7), 2537(6), 2688(7), and 2758(7) keV.
- The proton separation energy of 38Ca is Sp = 4.54727(22) MeV.
- The experiment ran for less than 40 hours with 160,000 38Ca/s impinging on the target.

## Context

This work extends the approach of in-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy of rare isotopes beyond the far-less-dissipative, surface-grazing reactions usually exploited, such as one- and two-nucleon transfer and knockout reactions (Gade 2008, Hansen 2003) or inelastic scattering (Obertelli 2016). The observed population of complex-structure states in the low-momentum tail is reminiscent of observations by Podolyak (2016) in two-neutron knockout from 56Fe, where a 10+ isomer was populated at similar absolute momentum loss, though the present work attributes the mechanism to simultaneous multi-nucleon rearrangement rather than Delta(1232) resonance excitation. The approach enables nuclear-structure studies of multi-particle configurations in rare isotopes that are complementary to low-energy multi-step Coulomb excitation (Hadynska-Klek 2018) and multi-nucleon transfer reactions (Corradi 2013), with the advantage of thick targets and strong forward focusing for fast beams.
