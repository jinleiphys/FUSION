# Various cluster radioactivities above magic nuclei

> We present parameter-free tunneling calculations for various cluster radioactivities including the diproton decays of atomic nuclei. An uniform folded cluster potential has been suggested that is based on a self-consistent mean-field model, with the folding factor determined using the quantization conditions of the quasibound cluster state. We have investigated the $α$-particle and heavier-cluster decays of trans-$^{100}$Sn and trans-$^{208}$Pb nuclei, and the observed diproton emission from the proton drip-line nucleus $^{16}$Ne, showing the overall reasonable descriptions of cluster radioactivities with calculated half-lives agreeing well with experimental data. We have also predicted the properties of yet unobserved cluster decays of the exotic nuclei $^{112,114}$Ba, $^{104}$Te and $^{38}$Ti.

# Various Cluster Radioactivities above Magic Nuclei

## Key claim

The authors present a parameter-free tunneling model for cluster radioactivities (including diproton decays) using a folded cluster potential derived from self-consistent mean-field calculations, with the folding factor determined by quantization conditions rather than fitted to decay data. The calculated half-lives agree with experimental data within a factor of approximately 2 for alpha decays and within one order of magnitude for heavier-cluster and diproton decays, though the results depend exponentially on the Q value, which for unknown nuclei must be taken from theoretical mass predictions.

## Method

The authors use the Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin (WKB) tunneling approach with L=0 ground-state-to-ground-state decays in even-even nuclei. The nuclear potential is constructed by folding neutron and proton potentials from Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations with the SkI4 force, using a folding factor lambda determined by the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition combined with the Wildermuth condition for the oscillator quantum number G. The Coulomb potential is taken directly from the mean-field calculation without folding, and a preformation factor of P=1 is assumed for all decays.

## Key numbers

- For the test case of alpha decay in 212Po, the calculated half-life is 89 ns versus the experimental value of 299 +/- 2 ns, giving a preformation factor of 0.3
- The folding factor for the alpha-nucleus potential in 212Po is lambda = 0.595, yielding a volume integral of J_v = 325 MeV fm^3, consistent with the experimental range of 300-350 MeV fm^3 from alpha-particle scattering
- For the 12C decay of 114Ba with Q = 19.00 +/- 0.04 MeV, the calculated partial half-life is 1.5 x 10^10 s with a branching ratio of 4.7 x 10^-8
- For the 12C decay of 112Ba using the theoretical Q = 23.17 MeV from Moller et al. (1997), the calculated partial half-life is 67 s; using Q = 21.46 MeV from Kumar et al. (1994) gives 9.5 x 10^4 s
- For the diproton decay of 16Ne with Q_2p = 1411 +/- 20 keV, the calculated half-life is 6.5 x 10^-20 s versus the observed 9 x 10^-21 s
- For the diproton decay of 38Ti with Q_2p = 960 +/- 260 keV, the calculated half-life is 120 ns; considering the error in Q gives a range from 0.26 ns to 1.7 x 10^6 ns
- Folding factors for alpha decays are stable at lambda ~ 0.5 for trans-100Sn nuclei and lambda ~ 0.6 for trans-208Pb nuclei, with volume integrals of J_v ~ 290 and ~ 330 MeV fm^3 respectively

## Context

This work extends the WKB tunneling approach of Buck et al. (1993) for cluster decays by replacing phenomenological potentials with self-consistent mean-field potentials from Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations, removing the need for adjustable parameters. It builds on earlier studies of alpha-nucleus potentials using effective nucleon-nucleon interactions by Ohkubo (1995), Mohr (2000), and Rauscher (2005), and on the diproton decay calculations of Nazarewicz et al. (1996) around 48Ni. The method enables predictions for yet-unobserved cluster decays in exotic nuclei such as 112,114Ba, 104Te, and 38Ti, where experimental data are lacking, though the authors note that deformation effects have not been included and will be addressed in future work.
