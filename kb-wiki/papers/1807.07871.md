# Geometrically confined thermal field theory: Finite size corrections and phase transitions

> Motivated by the recent shocking results from RHIC and LHC that show quark-gluon plasma signatures in small systems, we study a simple model of a massless, noninteracting scalar field confined with Dirichlet boundary conditions. We use this system to investigate the finite size corrections to thermal field theoretically derived quantities compared to the usual Stefan-Boltzmann limit of an ideal gas not confined in any direction. Two equivalent expressions with different numerical convergence properties are found for the free energy in $D$ rectilinear spacetime dimensions with $c\le D-1$ spatial dimensions of finite extent. We find that the First Law of Thermodynamics generalizes such that the pressure depends on direction but that the Third Law is respected. For systems with finite dimension(s) but infinite volumes, such as a field constrained between two parallel plates or a rectangular tube, the relative fluctuations in energy are zero, and hence the canonical and microcanonical ensembles are equivalent. We present precise numerical results for the free energy, total internal energy, pressure, entropy, and heat capacity of our field between parallel plates, in a tube, and in finite volume boxes of various sizes in 4 spacetime dimensions. For temperatures and system sizes relevant for heavy ion phenomenology, we find large deviations from the Stefan-Boltzmann limit for these quantities, especially for the pressure. Further investigation of an isolated system of fields constrained between parallel plates reveals a divergent isoenergetic compressibility at a critical length $L_c\sim1/T$. We have thus discovered a new second order phase transition via a first principles calculation, a transition that is driven by the size of the system.

## Key claim

For an isolated, noninteracting massless scalar field confined between parallel plates with Dirichlet boundary conditions, the isoenergetic compressibility diverges at a critical plate separation $L_c \sim 1/T$, revealing a second-order phase transition driven solely by system size rather than temperature.

## Method

The authors compute the canonical partition function for a massless, noninteracting scalar field in $D$ rectilinear spacetime dimensions with $c \le D-1$ compactified spatial dimensions under Dirichlet boundary conditions, using the Matsubara imaginary-time formalism. Two independent analytic expressions for the free energy are derived, one converging exponentially fast for small $T \times L_i$ and one for large $T \times L_i$, and are evaluated numerically for the cases of two parallel plates, a rectangular tube, and a finite box in 4 spacetime dimensions. The isoenergetic compressibility is then examined for the isolated (microcanonical) system between parallel plates.

## Key numbers

- For a system between parallel plates, the critical length for the phase transition is $L_c \sim 1/T$ (no exact numerical prefactor given).
- The free energy density for two parallel plates includes a zero-temperature Casimir term $-\pi^2/(1440 L_1^4)$.
- The Stefan-Boltzmann limit for the free energy density of an infinite system is $-\pi^2 T^4/90$.
- Large deviations from the Stefan-Boltzmann limit persist out to system sizes $L \sim 10/T$ for the pressure.

## Context

This work extends the study of finite-size corrections to thermal field theory, motivated by quark-gluon plasma signatures observed in small collision systems at RHIC and LHC. It contrasts with prior studies using periodic boundary conditions (Meyer 2009, Giusti 2011, Braun 2011) by employing Dirichlet boundary conditions, which the authors argue are more physically realistic for a finite QGP droplet. The phase transition discovered is distinct from the usual temperature-driven transitions and from the first-order transition in finite-volume Bose-Einstein condensation (Biswas 2007, Park 2008); it is second-order and also exists for fermionic fields. The work enables future investigation of ensemble equivalence and phase transitions in finite-size QGP systems.
