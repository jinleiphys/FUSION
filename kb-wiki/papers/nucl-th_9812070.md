# Modeling Fission in the Cascade-Exciton Model

> Recent developments of the Cascade-Exciton Model (CEM) of nuclear reactions to describe high energy particle induced fission are briefly described. The increased accuracy and predictive power of the CEM are shown by several examples. Further necessary work is outlined.

## Key claim

The authors report that modifying the Cascade-Exciton Model (CEM) to use an energy-independent level density parameter at the saddle point, rather than one scaled from the neutron-emission channel, significantly improves agreement with experimental fission cross sections for preactinide targets like Pb and Bi, but only after adjusting the surface-area parameter \(B_s\) to a physically reasonable value; the model still requires an additional input parameter (\(B_s\) or \(a_f/a_n\)) and does not yet provide a fully predictive, parameter-free description.

## Method

The authors extended the CEM code (version CEM95) to include fission by calculating particle-emission widths \(\Gamma_j\) and fission widths \(\Gamma_f\) using standard statistical-model formulas with level densities approximated by \(\rho(U) \propto \exp(2\sqrt{aU})\). They varied the level-density parameter at the saddle point \(a_f\) by either scaling it as a constant ratio \(a_f/a_n\) (original approach) or by setting \(a_f\) to an energy-independent value derived from the asymptotic Fermi-gas formula with a fitted surface-area parameter \(B_s\) (modified approach). They also incorporated updated mass tables, shell corrections, pairing gaps, and an empirical thermal-damping term for ground-state shell corrections in the fission barrier.

## Key numbers

* For neutron-induced fission of \(^{238}\)U, calculations with \(a_f/a_n = 1.02\) reproduce experimental cross sections from 10 MeV to 1 GeV (Fig. 1).
* For \(^{209}\)Bi(p,f) and \(^{208}\)Pb(p,f), the optimal \(B_s\) values are 1.18 and 1.15 respectively; for neutron-induced fission of both \(^{209}\)Bi and \(^{208}\)Pb, the optimal \(B_s\) is 1.12.
* The ratio \(a_f / \widetilde{a_n}\) (asymptotic level density parameter for neutron emission) for the modified calculations on Pb and Bi lies in the range 1.045–1.070.
* The statistical weight method yields calculated fission cross sections with statistical uncertainties of 5–10% using only 3000 inelastic events per calculation.
* For neutron-induced fission of gold, the improved model (CEM97) reproduces experimental data (Staples) well, while standard CEM95 with \(a_f/a_n = 1.10\) shows large discrepancies (Fig. 4).

## Context

This work extends the Cascade-Exciton Model (CEM) of Mashnik and Sierk (1998) to include fission, building on earlier fission models such as the Fong statistical model (used at JINR, ORNL, BNL, and PINP), the thermodynamical model of Stepanov (ITEP), and the diffusion model of Gonchar (INR, Moscow). The authors identify that standard CEM95 fails for preactinides (Prokofiev, 1997) and show that modifying the saddle-point level density treatment improves agreement. The work enables future development of a fully predictive CEM that can describe fission fragment distributions without an adjustable parameter, and it motivates consistent modeling of barriers, masses, and level densities.
