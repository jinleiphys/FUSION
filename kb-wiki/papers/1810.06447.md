# Identifying structures in the continuum: Application to $^{16}$Be

> The population and decay of two-nucleon resonances offer exciting new opportunities to explore dripline phenomena. The understanding of these systems requires a solid description of the three-body (core+N+N) continuum. The identification of a state with resonant character from the background of non-resonant continuum states in the same energy range poses a theoretical challenge. It is the purpose of this work to establish a robust theoretical framework to identify and characterize three-body resonances in a discrete basis. A resonance operator is proposed, which describes the sensitivity to changes in the potential. Resonances are then identified from the lowest eigenstates of the resonance operator. The operator is diagonalized in a basis of Hamiltonian pseudostates, built within the hyperspherical harmonics formalism using the analytical THO basis. The energy and width of the resonance are determined from its time dependence. The method is applied to 16Be in a 14Be+n+n model. An effective core+n potential, fitted to the available information on the subsystem 15Be, is employed. The 0+ ground state resonance of 16Be presents a strong dineutron configuration, which favors the picture of a correlated two-neutron emission. Fitting the three body interaction to the experimental two-neutron separation energy |S2n|=1.35(10) MeV, the computed width is Gamma(0+)=0.16 MeV. From the same Hamiltonian, a 2+ resonance is also predicted with E_r(2+)=2.42 MeV and Gamma(2+)=0.40 MeV. The dineutron configuration and the computed 0+ width are consistent with previous R-matrix calculations for the true three-body continuum. The extracted values of the resonance energy and width converge with the size of the pseudostate basis and are robust under changes in the basis parameters. This supports the reliability of the method in describing the properties of unbound core+N+N systems in a discrete basis.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a resonance operator method to identify and characterize three-body resonances in a discrete basis, where the resonance is identified from the lowest eigenstate of the operator \(\widehat{M}=\widehat{H}^{-1/2}\widehat{V}\widehat{H}^{-1/2}\) rather than from the Hamiltonian eigenstates directly, and the resonance parameters are extracted from the time evolution of that state.

## Method

The authors diagonalize the three-body Hamiltonian for \(^{16}\)Be in a \(^{14}\)Be\(+n+n\) model using the hyperspherical harmonics formalism with an analytical transformed harmonic oscillator (THO) basis, producing a set of positive-energy pseudostates representing the continuum. They then construct and diagonalize the resonance operator \(\widehat{M}\) in the basis of these Hamiltonian pseudostates, and identify the lowest eigenstate as the candidate resonance. The resonance energy \(\varepsilon_r\) and width \(\Gamma\) are determined by minimizing a quality parameter that compares the time-dependent amplitude of this state to an ideal exponential decay form, using a time profile \(W(t)=e^{-xt}\) and extrapolating to small \(x\) values.

## Key numbers

- The experimental two-neutron separation energy used to fit the three-body interaction is \(|S_{2n}| = 1.35(10)\) MeV.
- The computed width for the \(0^+\) ground state resonance of \(^{16}\)Be is \(\Gamma(0^+) = 0.16\) MeV.
- A \(2^+\) resonance is predicted with energy \(\varepsilon_r(2^+) = 2.42\) MeV and width \(\Gamma(2^+) = 0.40\) MeV.
- The resonance energy extracted from the method for the \(0^+\) state is \(\varepsilon_r(0^+) = 1.34\) MeV.
- The computed \(0^+\) width of 0.16 MeV is close to the 0.17 MeV obtained from previous \(R\)-matrix calculations by Lovell et al. (2017) for the true three-body continuum.

## Context

This work extends the pseudostate approach of Casal et al. (2018) by introducing a resonance operator that overcomes the difficulty of identifying resonances from a dense spectrum of Hamiltonian pseudostates, and provides a method to extract both energy and width. The results for \(^{16}\)Be are consistent with the hyperspherical \(R\)-matrix calculations of Lovell et al. (2017) using the same interactions, and confirm the dominant dineutron configuration in the ground-state resonance. The method enables the study of unbound core\(+N+N\) systems in a discrete basis without solving the full three-body scattering problem, and can be applied to other two-nucleon emitters and halo nuclei.
