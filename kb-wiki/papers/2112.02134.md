# New approach to lattice QCD at finite density: reweighting without an overlap problem

> Approaches to finite baryon density lattice QCD usually suffer from uncontrolled systematic uncertainties in addition to the well-known sign problem. We test a method - sign reweighting - that works directly at finite chemical potential and is yet free from any such uncontrolled systematics: with this approach the only problem is the sign problem itself. In practice the approach involves the generation of configurations with the positive fermionic weights given by the absolute value of the real part of the quark determinant, and a reweighting by a sign. There are only two sectors, +1 and -1 and as long as the average $\left\langle \pm \right\rangle \neq 0$ (with respect to the positive weight) this discrete reweighting has no overlap problem - unlike reweighting from $μ=0$ - and the results are reliable. We also present results based on this algorithm on the phase diagram of lattice QCD with two different actions: as a first test, we apply the method to calculate the position of the critical endpoint with unimproved staggered fermions at $N_τ=4$; as a second application, we study the phase diagram with 2stout improved staggered fermions at $N_τ=6$. This second one is already a reasonably fine lattice - relevant for phenomenology. We demonstrate that the method penetrates the region of the phase diagram where the Taylor and imaginary chemical potential methods lose predictive power.

# New approach to lattice QCD at finite density: reweighting without an overlap problem

## Key claim

Sign reweighting, generating configurations with the absolute value of the real part of the quark determinant and reweighting by a sign, works directly at finite chemical potential with no uncontrolled systematic uncertainties beyond the sign problem itself, provided the average sign remains nonzero; unlike reweighting from zero chemical potential, this discrete reweighting has no overlap problem because the reweighting factor takes only two values (+1 and -1).

## Method

The authors generate gauge configurations using a modified RHMC algorithm at zero chemical potential, with an extra accept/reject step that incorporates the factor |Re det M_ud(μ)^(1/2)| / det M_ud(0). The quark determinant is computed exactly via the reduced matrix formalism with dense linear algebra, avoiding stochastic estimators. Observables are then reweighted by the sign factor ε = sign(cos Arg det M_ud(μ)^(1/2)), which is valid for observables satisfying O(U)=O(U*) or those defined as real derivatives of the partition function with respect to real parameters. The method is tested on two discretizations: unimproved staggered fermions at N_τ=4 on volumes 6³×4 through 12³×4, and 2stout improved staggered fermions at N_τ=6 on 16³×6 lattices.

## Key numbers

- For unimproved staggered fermions at N_τ=4, the leading Fisher zero extrapolates to the real axis at μ_B/T ≈ 2.4, consistent with the critical endpoint prediction of Fodor and Katz (2004).
- For 2stout improved staggered fermions at N_τ=6, simulations were performed at fixed T = 140 MeV with μ_B/T values of 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.2, 2.5, and 2.7.
- The chiral condensate at μ_B=0 crossover temperature is crossed at μ_B/T ≈ 2.2 on the 2stout improved N_τ=6 lattices.
- The asymptotic ratio of needed statistics between sign quenched and phase quenched simulations is (π/2)² ≈ 2.5 in the large-μ or large-volume limit.
- The small-μ asymptotics of the average sign in the sign quenched ensemble behaves as 1 − (4/π)^(5/2) (σ²(μ)/2)^(3/2) exp(−π²/(8σ²(μ))), approaching 1 faster than any polynomial.

## Context

This work extends the sign reweighting approach introduced by de Forcrand and Philipsen (2002) and Alexandru et al. (2005), and directly addresses the overlap problem that limits conventional multiparameter reweighting from zero chemical potential, as demonstrated by Giordano et al. (2020). The method enables first-principles studies of the QCD phase diagram in the region where Taylor expansion (Allton et al., 2002; Bazavov et al., 2017) and analytic continuation from imaginary chemical potentials (de Forcrand and Philipsen, 2002; D'Elia and Lombardo, 2003) lose predictive power, covering the range relevant for the RHIC Beam Energy Scan. The strength of the sign problem can be estimated from zero-chemical-potential simulations alone, making the method practical for planning future projects.
