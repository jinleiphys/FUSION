# Strong charmonium decays in a microscopic model

> Although the spectra of heavy quarkonium systems have been successfully explained by certain QCD motivated potential models, their strong decays are still an open problem. We perform a microscopic calculation of vector charmonium strong decays into open-charm mesons where the $q\bar{q}$ pairs are created from the same interquark interactions acting in the quark model that has been used to describe its spectrum, and also its leptonic and radiative decays. We compare the numerical results with those predicted by the $^{3}P_{0}$ decay model and with the available experimental data, and discuss the possible influence on the strong widths of the different terms of the potential. A comparison with other predictions from similar microscopic decay models is also included.

## Key claim

The authors perform a microscopic calculation of vector charmonium strong decays into open-charm mesons where the quark-antiquark pairs are created from the same interquark interactions used to describe the spectrum, leptonic, and radiative decays, but the total decay widths obtained are generally lower than experimental data, and the ratios between different decay channels are not reproduced by any model, indicating that strong decays into charmed mesons remain an open problem.

## Method

The authors solve the Schrodinger equation using the Gaussian Expansion Method to find quark-antiquark bound states with a potential model that includes one-gluon exchange plus a mixture of scalar and vector confinement (with scalar fraction a_s = 0.81). The strong decay amplitudes are calculated from a transition operator derived from the full quark-quark interaction Hamiltonian, where the quark-antiquark pair creation vertex comes from the same interquark interactions (one-gluon exchange, scalar confinement, and vector confinement) rather than from a phenomenological pair creation model. The decay widths are computed for vector charmonium states (1-- c-cbar resonances) decaying into open-charm meson pairs, and results are compared with the 3P0 decay model and experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The confinement scalar fraction parameter is a_s = 0.81, indicating dominantly scalar confinement
- For psi(3770) (1^3D_1 state), the microscopic model predicts a total decay width of 18.97 MeV into DD, compared to the experimental total width of 27.5 +/- 0.9 MeV
- For psi(4040) (3^3S_1 state), the microscopic model predicts a total decay width of 39.12 MeV, compared to the experimental total width of 80 +/- 10 MeV
- For psi(4160) (2^3D_1 state), the microscopic model predicts a total decay width of 32.70 MeV, compared to the experimental total width of 103 +/- 8 MeV
- For X(4360) (4^3S_1 state), the microscopic model predicts a total decay width of 102.15 MeV, compared to the experimental total width of 74 +/- 15 +/- 10 MeV
- For psi(4415) (3^3D_1 state), the microscopic model predicts a total decay width of 42.72 MeV, compared to the experimental total width of 62 +/- 20 MeV
- The 3P0 model predicts total widths of 26.47 MeV for psi(3770), 111.27 MeV for psi(4040), 115.95 MeV for psi(4160), 113.92 MeV for X(4360), and 159.01 MeV for psi(4415)
- The quark model predicts masses for vector charmonium states: 3096 MeV (1S), 3703 MeV (2S), 3796 MeV (1D), 4097 MeV (3S), 4153 MeV (2D), 4389 MeV (4S), 4426 MeV (3D), 4614 MeV (5S), 4641 MeV (4D)

## Context

This work extends the microscopic decay model approach of Eichten (1978, 2006), who assumed strong decays are driven by the time-like component of the confining interaction, and of Ackleh and Swanson (1996) and Bao and Fei (2011), who assumed pair production by one-gluon exchange and scalar confining interactions. The authors use the quark interaction of Vijande (2005) which includes a mixture of scalar and vector confinement, allowing study of how the Lorentz structure of confinement influences strong decay widths. The results show that considering only the confinement time-like components improves agreement with experimental data, consistent with the conclusions of PhysRevD.55.1578 that confinement should have a time-like Dirac structure, but the failure to reproduce decay channel ratios suggests that additional Fock components of the wave function may be needed, as discussed by Entem (2011) regarding molecular components.
