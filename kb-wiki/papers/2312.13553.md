# Relativistic heat conduction in the large-flux regime

> We propose a general procedure for evaluating, directly from microphysics, the constitutive relations of heat-conducting fluids in regimes of large fluxes of heat. Our choice of hydrodynamic formalism is Carter's two-fluid theory, which happens to coincide with Öttinger's GENERIC theory for relativistic heat conduction. This is a natural framework, as it should correctly describe the relativistic ``inertia of heat'' as well as the subtle interplay between reversible and irreversible couplings. We provide two concrete applications of our procedure, where the constitutive relations are evaluated respectively from maximum entropy hydrodynamics and Chapman-Enskog theory.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a general procedure for computing the non-equilibrium equation of state for heat-conducting fluids at arbitrarily large heat fluxes, but the author explicitly acknowledges that the applicability of the GENERIC-Multifluid (GM) theory outside the Israel-Stewart regime requires three strong assumptions that "are quite strong assumptions to digest."

## Method

The author uses Carter's two-fluid theory (which coincides with Öttinger's GENERIC theory for relativistic heat conduction) to define a non-equilibrium free-energy density that depends on the thermal momentum covector. The constitutive relations are expressed in terms of physical observables (heat flux magnitude, longitudinal and transverse pressures, relaxation time, and entropy production rate) that can be computed unambiguously from microscopic kinetic models, allowing reconstruction of the non-equilibrium free energy via integration. Two concrete applications are provided: one using Minerbo's maximum entropy closure and another using Levermore's Chapman-Enskog closure for flux-limited diffusion.

## Key numbers

* For the Minerbo model, the polynomial fit for the transport coefficient is given by \(T^2 \sigma / (\mathfrak{R} v_{\text{ch}}^2) = 5/3 - (3/2)(v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^2 + (v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^4 - (1/6)(v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^6\), accurate up to \(v_{\text{ch}} w/T \sim 0.5\) with approximately 5% error at larger fluxes.
* For the Levermore model, the polynomial fit is \(T^2 \sigma / (\mathfrak{R} v_{\text{ch}}^2) = 5/6 + (1/6)(v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^4\), with the same accuracy range.
* The non-equilibrium free energy for the Minerbo model is \(\F = \F_{\text{eq}} + [ (5/6)(v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^2 - (3/8)(v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^4 + (1/6)(v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^6 - (1/48)(v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^8 ] \mathfrak{R}\).
* The non-equilibrium free energy for the Levermore model is \(\F = \F_{\text{eq}} + [ (5/12)(v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^2 + (1/36)(v_{\text{ch}} w/T)^6 ] \mathfrak{R}\).

## Context

This work extends the Israel-Stewart theory (Israel and Stewart, 1979) beyond the second-order expansion in dissipative fluxes, which is the standard framework for relativistic dissipation. It builds on the demonstration by Gavassino (2023) that Carter's multifluid theory and Öttinger's GENERIC theory are mathematically equivalent for heat conduction. The procedure enables falsifiable predictions for large-flux regimes relevant to neutron-star matter and quark matter, where quasi-particle descriptions (Chamel and Haensel, 2006; Nakano, 2020) are commonly used, and provides a method to compute non-equilibrium equations of state before fitting hydrodynamic data.
