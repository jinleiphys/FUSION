# Lattice Calculation of Thermal Properties of Low-Density Neutron Matter with Pionless NN Effective Field Theory

> Thermal properties of low-density neutron matter are investigated by determinantal quantum Monte Carlo lattice calculations on 3+1 dimensional cubic lattices. Nuclear effective field theory (EFT) is applied using the pionless single- and two-parameter neutron-neutron interactions, determined from the $^1S_0$ scattering length and effective range. The determination of the interactions and the calculations of neutron matter are carried out consistently by applying EFT power counting rules. The thermodynamic limit is taken by the method of finite-size scaling, and the continuum limit is examined in the vanishing lattice filling limit. The $^1S_0$ pairing gap at $T \approx 0$ is computed directly from the off-diagonal long-range order of the spin pair-pair correlation function, and is found to be approximately 30% smaller than BCS calculations with the conventional nucleon-nucleon potentials. The critical temperature $T_c$ of the normal-to-superfluid phase transition and the pairing temperature scale $T^\ast$ are determined, and the temperature-density phase diagram is constructed. The physics of low-density neutron matter is clearly identified as being a BCS-Bose-Einstein condensation crossover.

## Key claim

The authors find that the $^1S_0$ pairing gap in low-density neutron matter is approximately 30% smaller than BCS calculations with conventional nucleon-nucleon potentials, but this result is obtained specifically using a pionless effective field theory interaction limited to Fermi momenta below 0.6 fm$^{-1}$ and depends on taking both the thermodynamic and continuum limits through finite-size scaling and vanishing lattice filling.

## Method

Determinantal quantum Monte Carlo (DQMC) lattice calculations were performed on 3+1 dimensional cubic lattices using a grand canonical ensemble formulation, with the neutron-neutron interaction described by a pionless effective field theory Hamiltonian at leading order (LO) and next-to-leading order (NLO). The interaction parameters $c_0(a)$ and $c_2(a)$ were determined from the $^1S_0$ scattering length and effective range by applying EFT power counting rules, with $c_2$ treated perturbatively in both the two-body and many-body calculations. The thermodynamic limit was taken by finite-size scaling, and the continuum limit was examined in the vanishing lattice filling limit ($n \rightarrow 0$), with a standard parameter set using $n = 1/4$ as a practical compromise.

## Key numbers

- The $^1S_0$ pairing gap at $T \approx 0$ is approximately 30% smaller than BCS calculations with conventional nucleon-nucleon potentials
- The scattering length $a_0$ is approximately 20 fm, with effective range $r_0$ approximately 2.8 fm
- The matching density between LO and NLO calculations is at $k_F = 0.3041$ fm$^{-1}$
- The range of $1/(k_F a_0)$ in the low-density region investigated is $-0.8 \lesssim 1/(k_F a_0) \lesssim -0.1$
- The lattice filling fraction $n = 1/4$ (1/8 of full filling) was used as the standard parameter set
- The standard parameter set covers Fermi momenta from $k_F = 0.07602$ fm$^{-1}$ (15 MeV) to $k_F = 0.6081$ fm$^{-1}$ (120 MeV), corresponding to densities from $9 \times 10^{-5} \rho_0$ to $5 \times 10^{-2} \rho_0$ where $\rho_0 = 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$

## Context

This work extends previous quantum Monte Carlo calculations of neutron matter pairing gaps, specifically contrasting with Green's function Monte Carlo (GFMC) results by Carlson and Gezerlis (2007) which found smaller gaps than BCS, and auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) results by Fabrocini and Gandolfi (2005, 2008) which gave gaps close to BCS values. The authors use a different finite-temperature, grand canonical formulation rather than the zero-temperature approaches of GFMC and AFDMC, and introduce a pionless EFT interaction determined consistently with EFT power counting rules. This enables construction of the temperature-density phase diagram for low-density neutron matter and identification of the BCS-Bose-Einstein condensation crossover regime, with implications for understanding neutron star crust structure and the surface structure of neutron-rich unstable nuclei.
