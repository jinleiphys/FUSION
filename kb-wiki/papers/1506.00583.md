# The Possible Origin and Implications of the Shoulder in Reactor Neutrino Spectra

> We analyze within a nuclear database framework the shoulder observed in the antineutrino spectra in current reactor experiments. We find that the ENDF/B-VII.1 database predicts that the antineutrino shoulder arises from an analogous shoulder in the aggregate fission beta spectra. In contrast, the JEFF-3.1.1 database does not predict a shoulder for two out of three of the modern reactor neutrino experiments, and the shoulder that is predicted by JEFF-3.1.1 arises from $^{238}$U. We consider several possible origins of the shoulder, and find possible explanations. For example, there could be a problem with the measured aggregate beta spectra, or the harder neutron spectrum at a light-water power reactor could affect the distribution of beta-decaying isotopes. In addition to the fissile actinides, we find that $^{238}$U could also play a significant role in distorting the total antineutrino spectrum. Distinguishing these and quantifying whether there is an anomaly associated with measured reactor neutrino signals will require new short-baseline experiments, both at thermal reactors and at reactors with a sizable epithermal neutron component.

## Key claim

The shoulder observed in reactor antineutrino spectra at 4-6 MeV prompt energy can be reproduced by the ENDF/B-VII.1 nuclear database but not by the JEFF-3.1.1 database for two of the three modern experiments, and the discrepancy arises primarily from differences in evaluated fission-fragment yields rather than from new physics such as sterile neutrinos.

## Method

The authors used a summation method to compute aggregate antineutrino spectra for the Daya Bay, RENO, and Double Chooz fission splits, combining cumulative fission yields from either the ENDF/B-VII.1 or JEFF-3.1.1 libraries with an updated beta-decay library that incorporates total absorption gamma-ray spectroscopic (TAGS) data from Greenwood and Tengblad. They compared the resulting spectra to the Huber-Mueller and Huber-Haag model predictions, and examined five possible origins of the shoulder: non-fission antineutrino sources, forbidden beta-decay transitions, contributions from 238U, effects of a harder neutron spectrum in pressurized water reactors (PWRs), and errors in the original ILL aggregate beta-spectra measurements.

## Key numbers

* The shoulder region corresponds to prompt energies 4.0-6.5 MeV, representing approximately 2% of the total prompt-energy spectrum as reported by RENO.
* The deviation of the measured spectra from the Huber-Mueller model in the 4-6 MeV shoulder region represents an approximately 4 sigma deviation.
* For Daya Bay, the fission split is 0.586 235U : 0.076 238U : 0.288 239Pu : 0.050 241Pu; for RENO it is 0.62 : 0.12 : 0.21 : 0.05; for Double Chooz it is 0.496 : 0.087 : 0.351 : 0.066.
* In the 4-6 MeV prompt-energy region, the 238U antineutrino spectrum is almost twice as large as that of 235U, contributing about 24% (15%) to the total shoulder-region spectrum for RENO (Daya Bay).
* The JEFF-3.1.1 (ENDF/B-VII.1) database suggests 238U could account for 25% (50%) of the observed shoulder in RENO and Daya Bay.
* For 96Y, the ENDF/B-VII.1 evaluation assigns 90% of independent fission yield to the isomer and 10% to the ground state, while JEFF-3.1.1 assigns 64% to the isomer and 36% to the ground state, giving a 96Y yield range of 3.75-6.05% for 235U and 1.86-4.35% for 239Pu.
* The uncertainty in converting measured aggregate beta spectra to antineutrino spectra is about 4%, while the database summation uncertainty is appreciably larger.

## Context

This work extends the database analysis of Dwyer and Langford (2014), who first noted that the shoulder appears in the ENDF/B-VII.1 library, and of Sonzogni et al. (2014), who included TAGS data updates. The authors find that the two major fission-yield libraries (ENDF/B-VII.1 and JEFF-3.1.1) give qualitatively different predictions for the shoulder, with the ENDF/B-VII.1 prediction correlating with no reactor anomaly and the JEFF-3.1.1 prediction correlating with an anomaly. The paper enables future short-baseline experiments at both thermal reactors and reactors with sizable epithermal neutron components to distinguish between possible origins of the shoulder and to quantify whether a reactor neutrino anomaly exists.
