# Signatures of shape transition in odd-A neutron-rich Rubidium isotopes

> The isotopic evolution of the ground-state nuclear shapes and the systematics of one-quasiproton configurations are studied in odd-A Rubidium isotopes. We use a selfconsistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov formalism based on the Gogny energy density functional with two parametrizations, D1S and D1M, and implemented with the equal filling approximation. We find clear signatures of a sharp shape transition at N=60 in both charge radii and spin-parity of the ground states, which are robust, consistent to each other, and in agreement with experiment. We point out that the combined analysis of these two observables could be used to predict unambiguously new regions where shape transitions might develop.

## Key claim

The paper finds clear signatures of a sharp shape transition at N=60 in odd-A Rubidium isotopes, visible in both charge radii and ground-state spin-parity, but notes that the two-neutron separation energies do not signal any clear shape change in these isotopes, contrary to what might be expected.

## Method

The authors performed selfconsistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the Gogny energy density functional with two parametrizations (D1S and D1M), implemented with the equal filling approximation (EFA) to treat the odd nucleon. Axial symmetry was assumed based on previous studies suggesting triaxiality plays no significant role in these isotopes. The one-quasiproton configurations, charge radii differences, and two-neutron separation energies were computed and compared to experimental data from laser spectroscopy and mass measurements.

## Key numbers

- The shape transition occurs at N=60 (A=97), where the ground state jumps from 5/2- to 3/2+.
- In 89Rb, the 1/2- and 5/2- states are practically degenerate; in 91Rb (93Rb) they are separated by less than 0.2 (0.5) MeV.
- The charge radii difference δ⟨r²_c⟩ shows a jump between N=58 and N=60, correlating with the spin-parity change.
- For heavier isotopes (N≥60), the spherical barriers separating oblate and prolate minima are about 3 MeV, with minima about 1 MeV apart.
- D1M produces slightly lower excited states than D1S due to its larger effective mass making the single-particle spectrum more dense.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by the same group on shape transitions in even and odd neutron-rich Sr, Zr, and Mo isotopes (Rodriguez-Guzman 2009, 2010) to the odd-A Rb isotopes, which have received recent experimental attention (Lhersonneau 2001, Bucurescu 2007, Hwang 2009, Simpson 2010). The results are consistent with the Federman-Pittel mechanism (Federman 1979) pointing to the T=0 neutron-proton interaction as the driving force toward quadrupole deformation, and the Jahn-Teller picture where nuclei avoid high single-particle level densities. The work enables future searches for shape transitions in other regions of the nuclear chart using the combined analysis of charge radii and spin-parity, and calls for extended experimental programs at facilities like ISOLTRAP at ISOLDE/CERN and IGISOL at the University of Jyvaskyla.
