# Threshold 3He and 3H Transverse Electron Scattering Response Functions

> The threshold transverse response functions R_T(q,omega) for 3He and 3H are calculated using the AV18 nucleon-nucleon potential, the UrbanaIX three-body force, and the Coulomb potential. Final states are completely taken into account via the Lorentz integral transform technique. Consistent two-body pi- and rho-meson exchange currents as deduced using the Arenhövel-Schwamb technique are included. The convergence of the method is shown and a comparison of the corresponding MEC contribution is made to that of a consistent MEC for the meson theoretical r-space BonnA potential. The response R_T is calculated in the threshold region at q=174, 324, and 487 MeV/c and compared with available data. The strong MEC contributions in the threshold region are nicely confirmed by the data at q=324 and 487 MeV/c although some differences between theoretical and experimental results remain. A comparison is also made with other calculations, where the same theoretical input is used. The agreement is generally rather good, but leaves also some space for further improvement.

# Threshold ³He and ³H Transverse Electron Scattering Response Functions

## Key claim

The transverse response functions R_T(q,ω) for ³He and ³H calculated with the AV18+UrbanaIX potential and consistent meson-exchange currents (MEC) via the Arenhövel-Schwamb method show strong MEC contributions that are nicely confirmed by data at q=324 and 487 MeV/c, although some differences between theoretical and experimental results remain, and the agreement is generally rather good but leaves space for further improvement.

## Method

The authors compute the transverse response R_T(q,ω) for ³He and ³H using the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) technique, which fully accounts for final-state interactions. The nuclear Hamiltonian includes the AV18 nucleon-nucleon potential, the UrbanaIX three-body force, and the Coulomb potential. Consistent two-body π- and ρ-meson exchange currents are constructed using the Arenhövel-Schwamb method, which parametrizes the isovector part of the AV18 potential as a superposition of fictitious meson masses. The response is decomposed into electric and magnetic multipole transitions to final states with total angular momentum J_f up to 5/2 (and 7/2 for T_f=1/2), and the LITs are inverted separately for dominant M1 and M2 contributions and for summed remaining multipoles.

## Key numbers

- Momentum transfers studied: q = 174, 324, and 487 MeV/c
- For the AV18 MEC strength relative to BonnA MEC: reduction of about 12% (T_f=1/2) and 9% (T_f=3/2) at q=324 MeV/c; at q=174 and 487 MeV/c reductions of 9% and 15% (T_f=1/2) and 4% and 13% (T_f=3/2), respectively
- Convergence of the MEC expansion demonstrated: N=14 and N=16 Gaussian integration points give almost identical results, while N=12 does not converge
- Resolution parameter σ_I = 10 MeV for most transitions, reduced to σ_I = 2.5 MeV for dominant M1 and M2 threshold contributions
- For ³He at q=174 MeV/c, theoretical R_T underestimates data below 11 MeV
- For ³He results compared to Golak et al. (2005): differences below three-body break-up threshold are about 20% smaller near threshold, reducing to 9% (q=174 MeV/c), 3% (q=323 MeV/c), and 0% (q=487 MeV/c) close to three-body break-up threshold
- At q=487 MeV/c, nuclear recoil energy is about 50 MeV, leading to a relative reduction of R_T in Golak et al. (2005) by a little more than 1% due to use of three-momentum transfer squared versus four-momentum transfer squared for nucleon form factors

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous calculation using the BonnA potential (Leidemann et al., 2008) to the AV18+UrbanaIX combination, enabling direct comparison with the calculations of Golak et al. (2005) and Deltuva et al. (2008). The results confirm the essential role of meson-exchange currents in the threshold region, as previously shown with BonnA, but with quantitative differences in MEC strength that vary with momentum transfer and final isospin channel. The comparison with experimental data from Retzlaff et al. shows that while MEC contributions greatly improve agreement, discrepancies remain, particularly for ³H and at the lowest q=174 MeV/c, suggesting that additional currents involving the Δ resonance or systematic experimental uncertainties may need to be considered.
