# Numerical modelling of quantum statistics in high-energy physics

> Numerical modelling of quantum effects caused by bosonic or fermionic character of secondaries produced in high energy collisions of different sorts is at the moment still far from being established. In what follows we propose novel numerical method of modelling Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) observed among identical (bosonic) particles produced in such reactions. We argue that the most natural approach is to work directly in the momentum space of produced secondaries in which the Bose statistics reveals itself in their tendency to bunch in a specific way in the available phase space. Fermionic particles can also be treated in similar fashion.

## Key claim

The authors propose a novel numerical method for modelling Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) from the very beginning of a Monte Carlo event generation, rather than imposing them as an afterburner; the method works by grouping identical bosons into "elementary emitting cells" (EECs) whose particles share nearly the same energy and charge, and the observed BEC signal reflects the size of these cells rather than the size of the overall hadronizing source.

## Method

The algorithm proceeds event-by-event: first, a seed particle is selected with energy drawn from a Boltzmann distribution \(f(E) = \exp(-E/T)\); then additional particles of the same charge are added to form an EEC, with each new particle's energy drawn from a distribution peaked at the seed energy, and the number of particles in the cell follows a geometrical (Bose-Einstein) distribution controlled by a parameter \(P_0\) (acting as a chemical potential). This process is repeated until the available energy is exhausted, with charge neutrality enforced. The resulting clusters of same-charge, nearly same-energy particles are called "quantum clans," and their multiplicity distribution is a Pólya-Aeppli distribution, in contrast to the negative binomial distribution of the classical clan model.

## Key numbers

- The two-particle correlation function \(C_2(Q=|p_1-p_2|)\) is defined as \(C_2(Q) = N(p_1,p_2)/[N(p_1)\cdot N(p_2)]\).
- The energy distribution for seed particles is \(f(E) = \exp(-E/T)\), where \(T\) is a parameter playing the role of temperature.
- The number of particles in an EEC follows a geometrical distribution with parameter \(P_0\), related to chemical potential by \(\mu = T \cdot \ln P_0\).
- The spread of energies within an EEC is controlled by a distribution \(G(E^{(1)}_1 - E^{(1)}_i)\) with width parameter \(\sigma\).
- The model reproduces the characteristic BEC shape: \(C_2(Q) > 1\) at \(Q=0\), decreasing toward \(C_2=1\) for large \(Q\).
- The size of the emitting cell is \(R \sim 1/\delta y\), where \(\delta y\) is the cell size in rapidity space from the information-theory approach of OMT (2004).

## Context

This work extends the information-theory approach of OMT (2004) and the afterburner method of UWW (2004) by building quantum statistics directly into the event generator, avoiding violations of energy-momentum conservation and changes to multiparticle spectra that plague afterburner approaches (modBEC, 2004). It introduces a "quantum clan model" that differs from the classical clan model of NBD (1990) by using geometrical (rather than logarithmic) particle distributions within clans, thereby accounting for bosonic character. The approach enables a natural explanation of the puzzling observation that BEC-derived source sizes vary little with collision energy or projectile size (Quark Matter 2004 proceedings): BEC measures the correlation length (EEC size, parameter \(\sigma\)) rather than the fireball size, which depends on \(T\) and \(\mu\).
