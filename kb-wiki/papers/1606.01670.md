# Point kinetic model of the early phase of a spherically symmetric nuclear explosion

> A concise point kinetic model of the explosion of a prompt supercritical sphere driven by a nuclear fission chain reaction is presented. The findings are in good agreement with the data available for Trinity, the first detonation of a nuclear weapon conducted by the United States Army as part of the Manhattan project. Results are presented for an implosion device containing pure plutonium-239, although the model can be easily applied to, e.g., uranium-235. The fizzle probability and corresponding yield of a fission bomb containing plutonium recovered from reactor fuel and therefore containing significant amounts of spontaneously fissioning plutonium-240 which can induce a predetonation of the device is illustrated by adding a corresponding source term in the presented model. Related questions whether a bomb could be made by developing countries or terrorist organizations can be tackled this way. Although the information needed to answer such questions is in the public domain, it is difficult to extract a consistent picture of the subject for members of organizations who are concerned about the proliferation of nuclear explosives.

# Point kinetic model of the early phase of a spherically symmetric nuclear explosion

## Key claim

The point kinetic model reproduces the Trinity test yield of 15 kt from the plutonium core alone to within reasonable accuracy, but only after adjusting the extrapolation length constant from 0.71045 to 0.633 to gauge the model; the model does not account for the additional 6 kt estimated from the uranium tamper, and the total accepted Trinity yield of 21 kt is not reproduced by the core-only calculation.

## Method

The author develops a spatially homogenized point kinetic model combining neutron diffusion theory with a quasi-static approximation for the neutron flux distribution inside an expanding plutonium sphere. The model couples the time-dependent neutron population, governed by the inverse bomb period (Rossi alpha), to the radiation pressure-driven expansion of a fireball containing black body radiation, where the kinetic energy of the expanding matter shell is computed from the work done by radiation pressure. The dynamics are solved numerically using Euler discretization with adaptive timesteps of order 10^-12 s, starting from maximum core compression.

## Key numbers

- Critical mass of pure alpha-phase plutonium-239: 10.0 kg (critical radius 4.933 cm)
- Critical mass of pure uranium-235: 41.9 kg (critical radius 8.092 cm)
- For Trinity simulation with compression factor c=2.63, yield is 15.1 kilotons from the plutonium core
- For compression factor c=2.5 with adjusted extrapolation constant, yield is 15 kt (gauged value)
- Only about 13% of plutonium nuclei in the core underwent fission during the Trinity test
- Plutonium-240 spontaneous fission rate: 479.1 ± 5.3 fissions per gram-second, releasing neutrons at (1032 ± 14) g^-1 s^-1
- Fireball radius at 0.016 s after ignition matches Trinity test measurements
- Radiation temperature reaches order 10^8 K (corresponding to 8.62 keV)
- Approximate yield formula: Y = 128 kt kg^-1 m_0^49 ρ_max^3

## Context

This work extends the point kinetics approach familiar from nuclear reactor theory (Weinberg and Wigner, 1958) to the prompt supercritical regime of a nuclear explosion, building on earlier critical mass calculations by Reed and others. The model enables quantitative estimates of fission bomb yields and fizzle probabilities for proliferation assessment, particularly for devices using reactor-grade plutonium containing plutonium-240. The results confirm the approximate proportionality between yield and the cube of the inserted reactivity anticipated by Serber, and the fireball expansion follows the classic blast wave scaling R(t) ~ Y_B^(1/5) t^(2/5) for large radii.
