# Probing hadron-quark phase transition in twin stars using $f$-modes

> Although it is conjectured that a phase transition from hadronic to deconfined quark matter in the ultrahigh-density environment of Neutron Stars (NS), the nature of phase transition remains an unresolved mystery. Furthermore, recent efforts reveal that the finite surface tension effects can lead to a mixed phase with different geometric shapes (so-called "pasta" phases), leading to a smooth phase transition from hadronic to quark matter in the NS interior. Depending on whether there is a strong or a pasta-induced smooth first-order phase transition, one may expect a third family of stable, compact stars or "twin stars" to appear, with the same mass but different radii compared to NSs. The possibility of identifying twin stars using astrophysical observations has been a subject of interest. This study investigates the potential of probing the nature of the hadron-quark phase transition through future gravitational wave (GW) detections from fundamental ($f$-) mode oscillations in Neutron Stars. Using a newly developed model that parametrizes the hadron-quark phase transition with ``pasta phases," we calculate $f$-mode characteristics within a full general relativistic framework. We then use Universal Relations in GW asteroseismology to derive stellar properties from the detected mode parameters. Our findings suggest that detecting GWs from $f$-modes with third-generation GW detectors offers a promising scenario for the existence of twin stars. However, we also estimate various uncertainties in determining the mode parameters and conclude that these uncertainties make it more challenging to identify the nature of the hadron-quark phase transition.

## Key claim

The paper suggests that detecting gravitational waves from fundamental-mode oscillations with third-generation detectors offers a promising scenario for confirming the existence of twin stars, but the uncertainties in determining mode parameters make it more challenging to identify the nature of the hadron-quark phase transition than the abstract alone implies.

## Method

The authors used a four-parameter polytropic equation of state model (ACB4) that parametrizes the hadron-quark phase transition via a mixed phase with "pasta phases" using a single parameter, the pressure increment at the critical chemical potential. They solved the general relativistic Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for stellar structure and the full linearized perturbation equations for quadrupolar f-mode oscillations using direct integration, then applied universal relations in gravitational wave asteroseismology to recover stellar properties from mode parameters.

## Key numbers

- The ACB4 EOS model uses four polytropic regions with indices Gamma_i = 4.921, 0.0, 4.00, and 2.80, with transition pressure P_c = 63.178 MeV fm^{-3} at critical chemical potential
- The mixed phase pressure increment Delta p ranges from 0% to 8% of the critical pressure; above Delta p = 5%, the third family branch disappears and all compact stars lie in a single connected branch
- The universal relation fit parameters for Re(M omega) are a_0 = -0.024 +/- 7 x 10^{-4}, a_1 = 0.589 +/- 0.006, a_2 = 0.089 +/- 0.011
- The universal relation fit parameters for Im(M omega) are b_0 = (10.39 +/- 0.005) x 10^{-2}, b_1 = (-4.87 +/- 0.004) x 10^{-1}, b_2 = (5.686 +/- 0.0415) x 10^{-1}
- The Cowling approximation can overestimate the f-mode frequency by up to approximately 30% compared to full general relativistic treatment

## Context

This work extends previous studies on twin stars and hadron-quark phase transitions by Ayriyan, Blaschke, and collaborators (2018-2021) and by Alvarez-Castillo and Paschalidis (2017-2018), who developed the ACB4 parametrization. It builds on universal relations for f-mode asteroseismology established by Andersson and Kokkotas (1996, 1998) and later refined by Tsui and Leung (2005) and Lioutas and Stergioulas (2018). The study enables future gravitational wave observations with third-generation detectors such as the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer to potentially distinguish between sharp and smooth hadron-quark phase transitions in neutron stars.
