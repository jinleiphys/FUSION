# Equation of state and thickness of the inner crust of neutron stars

> The cell structure of $β$-stable clusters in the inner crust of cold and warm neutron stars is studied within the Thomas-Fermi approach using relativistic mean field nuclear models. The relative size of the inner crust and the pasta phase of neutron stars is calculated, and the effect of the symmetry energy slope parameter, $L$, on the profile of the neutron star crust is discussed. It is shown that while the size of the total crust is mainly determined by the incompressibility modulus, the relative size of the inner crust depends on $L$. It is found that the inner crust represents a larger fraction of the total crust for smaller values of $L$. Finally, it is shown that at finite temperature the pasta phase in $β$-equilibrium matter essentially melts above $5-6$ MeV, and that the onset density of the rodlike and slablike structures does not depend on the temperature.

## Key claim

The relative size of the inner crust compared to the total crust depends on the symmetry energy slope parameter L, with smaller L values producing a larger inner crust fraction, while the total crust size is mainly determined by the incompressibility modulus. At finite temperature, the pasta phase in beta-equilibrium matter melts above 5-6 MeV, but the onset densities of rodlike and slablike structures do not depend on temperature, and the quoted melting temperatures should be interpreted as upper limits because thermal fluctuations are not included.

## Method

The authors performed Thomas-Fermi calculations within the Wigner-Seitz approximation using seven relativistic mean-field nuclear models (NL3, TM1, FSU, NL3_omega_rho, DD-ME2, DD-ME_delta, and IU-FSU) to describe the non-uniform beta-equilibrium matter in the inner crust of neutron stars. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations were integrated to determine the density profiles and crust thicknesses for stars of masses 1.0, 1.44, and 1.6 solar masses, as well as the maximum mass configuration for each model. Finite temperature effects were studied using both the Thomas-Fermi approach and a dynamical spinodal calculation within the relativistic Vlasov formalism.

## Key numbers

- The symmetry energy slope parameter L ranges from 47.2 MeV (IU-FSU) to 118.3 MeV (NL3) across the models considered.
- For a 1 solar mass star, the total crust thickness lies between 1.5 and 2 km for all models.
- For maximum mass configurations, the crust thickness is mainly below 0.5 km.
- The inner crust occupies at least 55% of the total crust size for most models, reducing to approximately 50% for NL3 and TM1.
- In a 1 solar mass star, the non-droplet pasta phase size is smaller than 200 m.
- The slab phase fraction of the non-droplet pasta phase is approximately 35-40% for most models, except IU-FSU where it is almost 60% and DD-ME2 with approximately 30-35%.
- The correlation between inner crust thickness and the ratio E_sym/K_0 has a slope of approximately -9.5 +/- 15% km.
- The pasta phase in beta-equilibrium matter melts above 5-6 MeV, with slab structures melting at temperatures below 3 MeV and rods melting approximately 1 MeV higher for most models.
- The transition densities between droplet-rod and rod-slab phases do not depend on temperature.

## Context

This work extends previous Thomas-Fermi studies of the pasta phase (Grill 2012, Pais 2012) and provides a systematic comparison of seven relativistic mean-field models to isolate the effects of the symmetry energy slope L and incompressibility K_0 on crust structure. The results are consistent with earlier dynamical spinodal calculations (Providencia 2006, Brito 2006) and with Skyrme-force calculations by Ducoin (2008), while also connecting to studies of pasta phase effects on crustal shear phenomena (Newton 2011). The findings enable more accurate modeling of neutron star crust properties and have implications for supernova and proto-neutron star evolution, particularly neutrino diffusion.
