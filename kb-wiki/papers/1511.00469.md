# Validity of the generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis in $^{238}$Np

> We have analyzed primary $γ$-ray spectra of the odd-odd $^{238}$Np nucleus extracted from $^{237}$Np($d,pγ$)$^{238}$Np coincidence data measured at the Oslo Cyclotron Laboratory. The primary $γ$ spectra cover an excitation-energy region of $0 \leq E_i \leq 5.4$ MeV, and allowed us to perform a detailed study of the $γ$-ray strength as function of excitation energy. Hence, we could test the validity of the generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis, which, in its strictest form, claims no excitation-energy dependence on the $γ$ strength. In this work, using the available high-quality $^{238}$Np data, we show that the $γ$-ray strength function is to a very large extent independent on the initial and final states. Thus, for the first time, the generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis has been experimentally verified for $γ$ transitions between states in the quasi-continuum region, not only for specific collective resonances, but also for the full strength below the neutron separation energy. Based on our findings, the necessary criteria for the generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis to be fulfilled are outlined.

## Key claim

The generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis, which states that the gamma-ray strength function is independent of the initial and final excitation energy, is verified for the first time in the nuclear quasi-continuum region for the full strength below the neutron separation energy in the odd-odd nucleus 238Np, with the caveat that the hypothesis requires a sufficiently high level density to average out Porter-Thomas fluctuations.

## Method

Primary gamma-ray spectra were extracted from 237Np(d,pγ)238Np coincidence data measured at the Oslo Cyclotron Laboratory, covering an initial excitation energy region of 0 to 5.4 MeV. The Oslo method was used to simultaneously extract the level density and gamma-ray transmission coefficient from the two-dimensional probability matrix, assuming factorization into level density at the final energy and a transmission coefficient dependent only on gamma-ray energy. The gamma-ray strength function was then derived separately as a function of initial excitation energy and final excitation energy by dividing the normalized decay probability by the known level density, and these excitation-energy-dependent strength functions were compared to the average strength function obtained from the standard Oslo method.

## Key numbers

- Level density at a few hundred keV above ground state: approximately 200 MeV^{-1}
- Level density at the neutron separation energy Sn = 5.488 MeV: approximately 43 × 10^6 MeV^{-1}
- The primary gamma spectra cover an excitation-energy region of 0 ≤ Ei ≤ 5.4 MeV
- The Oslo method fitting was performed for the region 3.0 ≤ Ei ≤ 5.4 MeV and Eγ > 0.84 MeV, with a reduced chi-squared of 0.81
- Excitation-energy bins for the analysis were 121 keV wide
- At 2.0 MeV excitation energy, approximately 1200 levels exist within a 121-keV bin; at 4.0 MeV, approximately 120000 levels exist within the same bin width
- Relative Porter-Thomas fluctuations are 4.1% at 2.0 MeV and 0.4% at 4.0 MeV, compared to typical data error bars of 10%
- Systems with fewer than approximately 200 levels per bin will have Porter-Thomas fluctuations exceeding the 10% experimental statistical error

## Context

This work extends previous tests of the Brink-Axel hypothesis, which had primarily been examined at very high excitation energies or with only a few states included, such as in (n,γ) reactions (Stefanon 1977, Raman 1981, Kahane 1984, Islam 1991, Kopecky 1990) and the 89Y(p,γ)90Zr reaction (Netterdon 2015), which showed deviations. The high level density of the odd-odd 238Np nucleus provides ideal conditions where Porter-Thomas fluctuations are negligible, enabling the first experimental verification of the generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis in the quasi-continuum. The findings enable more confident application of the hypothesis in calculations of (n,γ) cross sections for r-process nucleosynthesis and nuclear reactor applications, and establish the necessary criterion that the level density must be high enough to average out Porter-Thomas fluctuations relative to experimental errors.
