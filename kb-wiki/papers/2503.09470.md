# Impact of the Center of Mass Fluctuations on the Ground State Properties of Nuclei

> Ground state properties across the entire nuclear chart are described predominantly and rather accurately within the density functional theory (DFT). DFT however breaks many symmetries, among them the most important being the translational, rotational, and gauge symmetries. The translational symmetry breaking is special, since it is broken for all nuclei, unlike the rotational and gauge symmetries. The center-of-mass (CoM) correction most commonly used in the literature [see Vautherin and Brink, Phys. Rev. C {\bf 5}, 626 (1972) and Bender {\it et al.}, Rev. Mod. Phys. {\bf 75}, 121 (2003)] leads to a gain of 15,...,19 MeV, which varies rather weakly for medium and heavy mass nuclei. A better approximation to the CoM correction was suggested by Butler {\it et al.}, Nu cl. Phys. A {\bf 422}, 157 (1984) and its magnitude varies between 10 and 5 MeV from light to heavy nuclei, a correction which is also significantly larger than the RMS energy error in the Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula, initially proposed by Gamow, Proc. Phys. Soc. A {\bf 126}, 157 (1930), which is at most 3.5 MeV, and which for heavy nuclei corresponds to about 0.2\% of their mass. ....

## Key claim

The widely used center-of-mass (CoM) energy correction formulas for nuclear ground states are contaminated by contributions from excited states and are intrinsically inaccurate; the Peierls-Yoccoz (PY) projection method yields CoM energy corrections that are consistently larger and more reliable, but even the PY framework does not reproduce the correct relativistic nuclear inertia, which requires a relativistic density functional theory.

## Method

The authors apply the Peierls-Yoccoz (PY) translational symmetry restoration method (Peierls and Yoccoz, 1957) to project mean-field many-body wave functions onto translationally invariant intrinsic states with zero total momentum. They evaluate the CoM energy correction as the difference between the projected ground state energy and the mean-field energy, using the SeaLL1 energy density functional (Bulgac, 2018) for a range of nuclei. The PY projected wave functions are constructed by integrating the mean-field wave function over all possible center-of-mass displacements, yielding intrinsic wave functions that depend only on relative coordinates.

## Key numbers

* The commonly used Hartree-Fock CoM correction (Vautherin and Brink, 1972) gives approximately -22 MeV, largely independent of mass number, with a fitted formula E_CoM^HF = -17.46 - 15.17/A^(1/6) + 28.01/A^(2/6) ± 0.42 MeV.
* The Butler et al. (1984) prescription gives CoM corrections varying from 12.20 MeV for 16O to 5.19 MeV for 208Pb.
* The PY CoM energy correction for 208Pb is larger than the value obtained from the simple expectation value -⟨ψ|K_CoM|ψ⟩, with the difference increasing for nuclei with A > 90 to at least 1 MeV.
* The SeaLL1 EDF predicts a nuclear mass ratio M_A(DFT)/(N m_n + Z m_p) = 0.991624 for 208Pb, a 0.84% violation of Galilean invariance.
* Dobaczewski (2009) reports PY nuclear inertia scaling as M_A(DFT)/(N m_n + Z m_p) = 1.311(14) A^(-0.060(3)), ranging from about 1.10 for A=16 to about 0.95 for A=208.
* The CoM spatial fluctuation volume is 3.86 fm^3 for 16O and 0.17 fm^3 for 208Pb, corresponding to cubes of side 1.57 fm and 0.55 fm respectively.

## Context

This work extends the PY projection method (Peierls and Yoccoz, 1957) to heavy nuclei beyond A=40, where previous applications were limited (Marcos, 1984; Schmid, 1990, 1991, 2002a; Rodriguez-Guzman, 2004a). The results contradict the A^(-1) scaling predicted by Sheikh (2021) and show significant disagreement with the CoM corrections from Da-Costa (2024) and Dobaczewski (2009). The authors argue that current non-relativistic DFT approaches cannot reproduce the correct nuclear inertia (which differs from Am by about 1% due to binding energy), and that a relativistic DFT formulation is needed to achieve the sub-MeV accuracy required for astrophysical r-process and rp-process simulations.
