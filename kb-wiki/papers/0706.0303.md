# A hybrid model for fusion at deep sub-barrier energies

> A hybrid model where the tunneling probability is estimated based on both sudden and adiabatic approaches has been proposed to understand the heavy ion fusion phenomena at deep sub-barrier energies. It is shown that under certain approximations, it amounts to tunneling through two barriers: one while overcoming the normal Coulomb barrier (which is of sudden nature) along the radial direction until the repulsive core is reached and thereafter through an adiabatic barrier along the neck degree of freedom while making transition from a di-nuclear to a mono-nuclear regime through shape relaxation. A general feature of this hybrid model is a steep fall-off of the fusion cross section, sharp increase of logarithmic derivative L(E) with decreasing energy and the astrophysical S-factor showing a maxima at deep sub-barrier energies particularly for near symmetric systems. The model can explain the experimental fusion measurements for several systems ranging from near symmetric systems like $^{58}Ni+^{64}Ni, ^{58}Ni+^{58}Ni$ and $ ^{58}Ni+^{69}Y$ to asymmetric one like $^{16}O+^{208}Pb$ where the experimental findings are very surprising. Since the second tunneling is along the neck co-ordinate, it is further conjectured that deep sub-barrier fusion supression may not be observed for the fusion of highly asymmetric projectile target combinations where adiabatic transition occurs automatically without any hindrance. The recent deep sub-barrier fusion cross section measurements of $^{6}Li+^{198}Pt$ system supports this conjecture.

# A hybrid model for fusion at deep sub-barrier energies

## Key claim

The paper proposes that heavy-ion fusion at deep sub-barrier energies involves two sequential tunneling steps: first through the sudden Coulomb barrier along the radial direction, and then through an adiabatic barrier along the neck degree of freedom during the transition from a di-nuclear to a mono-nuclear configuration. The model suggests that deep sub-barrier fusion suppression may not occur for highly asymmetric projectile-target combinations because the adiabatic transition happens automatically without requiring neck tunneling.

## Method

The author constructs a hybrid model combining sudden and adiabatic approaches, where the fusion cross section is factorized as the product of two tunneling probabilities: one through the sudden Coulomb barrier at the barrier radius R_b, and another through an adiabatic neck barrier at the pocket minimum R_m. The neck barrier is estimated using a macroscopic model of two nuclei connected by a cylindrical neck, with the neck potential V_neck(r,n) derived from surface energy considerations. The tunneling probabilities are calculated using the Hill-Wheeler approximation, and the model is applied to both symmetric systems (e.g., 58Ni+64Ni) and asymmetric systems (e.g., 16O+208Pb).

## Key numbers

- For the 64Ni+64Ni system, the minimum value of the M3Y+Repulsion potential pocket V_m is approximately 85 MeV, which is about 9 MeV below the Coulomb barrier.
- For 64Ni+64Ni, the neck barrier V_neck(R_m,nu_b) is approximately 2.7 MeV (assuming surface tension gamma=1.0 MeV/fm^2), giving an adiabatic barrier height V_n of about 87.7 MeV.
- For 16O+16O, the neck potential V_neck(R_m,nu_b) is about 1 MeV, with an assumed V_m of 2.5 MeV, yielding an adiabatic neck barrier of approximately 3.5 MeV.
- The compound nucleus for 64Ni+64Ni can in principle be formed down to a threshold energy of E_cm approximately 49 MeV (from the condition that E_cm+Q is positive).
- The geometrical neck radius nu_g for symmetric systems at rho=0.25 is approximately 0.13, which is less than the barrier position nu_b=1/6.
- For 6Li+198Pt, nu_g is approximately 0.19, which lies to the right of nu_b=1/6.

## Context

This work extends the two-step model of Takigawa and collaborators (2004) by explicitly calculating the second tunneling along the neck coordinate, rather than treating it as a capture in a potential pocket. It addresses the same deep sub-barrier fusion suppression phenomena that motivated the repulsive-core potential model of Misicu and Esbensen (2003, 2004), but avoids the unphysical cut-off in fusion cross section at energies below the potential pocket minimum that occurs in that model. The hybrid model can explain the different suppression patterns observed for systems like 16O+208Pb (where the logarithmic slope saturates) versus Ni-induced reactions (where it increases with decreasing energy), and it predicts that highly asymmetric systems such as 6Li+198Pt should show no fusion hindrance, consistent with measurements by Arai and collaborators (2006).
