# Precision Thermodynamics of the Fermi polaron at strong coupling

> The Fermi polaron problem, which describes a mobile impurity that interacts with a spin-polarized Fermi sea, is a paradigmatic system in quantum many-body physics and has been challenging to address quantitatively in its strong coupling regime. We present the first controlled thermodynamic calculations for the Fermi polaron at strong coupling using finite-temperature auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFMC) methods in the framework of the canonical ensemble. Modeled as a spin-imbalanced system, the Fermi polaron has a Monte Carlo sign problem, but we show that it is moderate over a wide range of temperatures and coupling strengths beyond the unitary limit of the BCS-BEC crossover. We calculate the contact, a quantity which measures the strength of the short-range correlations, as a function of temperature at unitarity and as a function of the coupling strength at fixed temperature and find good agreement with a variational approach based on one particle-hole excitation of the Fermi sea. We compare our results for the contact with recent experiments and find good agreement at unitarity (within error bars) but discrepancies away from unitarity on the BEC side of the crossover. We also calculate the thermal energy gap at unitarity as a function of temperature.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first controlled finite-temperature thermodynamic calculations for the Fermi polaron at strong coupling, but the method is limited to regimes where the Monte Carlo sign problem is moderate; the authors cannot probe the polaron-molecule transition directly because the sign problem becomes severe above a coupling strength of approximately \((k_F a)^{-1} \approx 0.7\).

## Method

The authors use finite-temperature auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFMC) in the canonical ensemble, projecting onto fixed numbers of spin-up and spin-down particles to isolate a single impurity (\(N_\downarrow=1\)) interacting with a spin-polarized Fermi sea of \(N_\uparrow\) particles. They discretize the continuum Hamiltonian on a cubic lattice with periodic boundary conditions, apply a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to linearize the two-body interaction, and sample auxiliary-field configurations using the Metropolis-Hastings-Rosenbluth algorithm. Systematic errors are removed by extrapolating to continuous time (\(\Delta\beta \rightarrow 0\)) and to the continuum limit (filling factor \(\nu \rightarrow 0\)).

## Key numbers

* Contact \(C\) (in units of \(k_F\)) at unitarity increases monotonically with temperature below \(0.5\,T_F\); the low-temperature AFMC results are significantly larger than the ground-state diffusion Monte Carlo result of Pessoa (2021).
* At \(T = 0.2\,T_F\) and coupling \((k_F a)^{-1} \approx 0.9\), the average Monte Carlo sign is \(\sim 0.2\) on a \(9^3\) lattice, which still allows meaningful calculations but prevents probing the polaron-molecule transition.
* The thermal energy gap at unitarity at low temperatures is consistent with the zero-temperature Chevy ansatz value of \(\Delta E = -0.61\,E_F\) from Chevy (2006).
* The average Monte Carlo sign at unitarity for the \(20+1\) system declines rapidly below \(T \sim 0.1\,T_F\) and is \(\sim 0.6\) at \(T \sim 0.05\,T_F\).

## Context

This work extends prior theoretical studies of the Fermi polaron that were limited to ground-state properties (diffusion Monte Carlo by Pessoa (2021); diagrammatic Monte Carlo by Prokof'ev (2008) and Goulko (2016); functional renormalization group by Schmidt (2011); Chevy's one particle-hole ansatz (2006)) or to finite-temperature variational calculations (Parish (2021); Liu (2020A)). It provides the first controlled finite-temperature benchmark for the contact and thermal energy gap, enabling direct comparison with ultracold-atom experiments by Yan (2019) and Ness (2020). The results show good agreement with the variational one particle-hole approximation at unitarity but reveal significant discrepancies with experiment away from unitarity on the BEC side, motivating future work on the polaron spectral function and improved experimental measurements.
