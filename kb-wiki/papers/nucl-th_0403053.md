# Viscosity and Thermalization

> I solve the Relativistic Navier Stokes Equations assuming boost invariance and rotational symmetry. I compare the resulting numerical solutions for two limiting models of the shear viscosity. In the first model the shear viscosity is made proportional to the temperature. Thus, $η\propto T/σ_0$ where $σ_0$ is some fixed cross section (perhaps $σ_0 \sim Λ_{QCD}^{-2}$) . This viscosity model is typical of the classical Boltzmann simulations of Gyulassy and Molnar. In the second model the shear viscosity is made proportional to $T^3$. This model is typical of high temperature QCD. When the initial mean free path of the $T^3$ model is four times larger than the $T/σ_0$ model, the two models of viscosity produce the same radial flow. This result can be understood with simple scaling arguments. Thus, the large transport opacity needed in classical Boltzmann simulations is in part an artifact of the fixed scale $σ_0$ in these models.

## Key claim

When the initial mean free path of a viscosity model proportional to \(T^3\) (typical of high-temperature QCD) is four times larger than that of a model with \(\eta \propto T/\sigma_0\) (typical of classical Boltzmann simulations), the two models produce the same radial flow; thus the large transport opacity needed in classical Boltzmann simulations is in part an artifact of the fixed scale \(\sigma_0\) in those models.

## Method

The author solved the Relativistic Navier Stokes Equations (using the Ottinger model) assuming boost invariance and rotational symmetry, with initial conditions from P. Kolb's inviscid hydrodynamic calculations. Two models of shear viscosity were compared: one with \(\eta = 1.264\, T/\sigma_0\) (fixed cross section \(\sigma_0 = 10\,\text{mb}\)) and a "Minimal Model" where \(\eta = 1.264\, T/\sigma_0\) for energy density below \(e_c = 1\,\text{GeV/fm}^3\) and \(\eta = s/5\) (giving \(\eta \propto T^3\)) above that threshold. The viscous solutions were compared to inviscid (Euler) solutions, and freezeout was defined where the viscous correction reached half the hydrodynamic pressure.

## Key numbers

- The shear viscosity in the classical gas model is \(\eta = 1.264\, T/\sigma_0\) with \(\sigma_0 = 10\,\text{mb}\).
- The Minimal Model uses a critical energy density \(e_c = 1\,\text{GeV/fm}^3\) to switch between viscosity regimes.
- The viscosity in the Minimal Model is initially four times larger than the fixed cross section model, yet both produce approximately the same radial flow.
- For a Bjorken expansion with \(\eta \propto T^3\), the ratio \(\Gamma_s/\tau \sim 1/\tau^{2/3}\) (system comes closer to equilibrium over time); for \(\eta \propto T/\sigma_0\), \(\Gamma_s/\tau \sim \text{Const}\) (degree of thermalization remains constant).
- The viscous and inviscid solutions become similar by a time of approximately \(6\,\text{fm}\).

## Context

This work extends the classical Boltzmann simulations of Gyulassy and Molnar (2001) by showing that the large cross sections (\(\approx 10\,\text{mb}\)) needed in those simulations to reproduce elliptic flow are partly an artifact of using a fixed cross section \(\sigma_0\) rather than the temperature-dependent viscosity of high-temperature QCD. The paper enables more realistic viscous hydrodynamic modeling of heavy ion collisions by demonstrating that a viscosity proportional to \(T^3\) (as in perturbative QCD) leads to better thermalization during expansion than models with a fixed scale.
