# Comment on "Pionic decay of a possible d' dibaryon and the short-range NN interaction"

## Key claim
The authors argue that the d' decay amplitude for the s-wave process d' → NNπ is proportional to the pion 4-momentum k_μ (and thus does not vanish at k → 0), contrary to the claim in Obukhovsky et al. (1997) that the amplitude is proportional to k². The authors assert that the result of Obukhovsky et al. is erroneous.

## Method
The authors analyze the d' → NNπ decay within a quark shell model using both pseudo-vector and pseudo-scalar forms of the qqπ vertex. They examine the transition (s⁵p¹) → (s⁶) + π, writing the quark wave functions for s₁/₂ and p₁/₂ shells as bispinors, and evaluate the transition amplitude integral to determine its dependence on the pion momentum. They also incorporate NN final state interaction (FSI) effects using a Yamaguchi potential with Coulomb corrections.

## Key numbers
- The difference in decay rates between d' → nnπ⁺ and d' → ppπ⁻ is approximately 10–15% due to Coulomb effects.
- The parameter β in the Yamaguchi potential is approximately 230 MeV.
- The numerical result obtained with the FSI formula (including Coulomb) differs from the "exact" solution by only 20–30%.
- The d' mass in the potential model of Obukhovsky et al. (1997) is stated to be too large (no specific numerical value given for the correct mass).

## Context
This comment directly contradicts the calculation of Obukhovsky, Itonaga, Wagner, Buchmann, and Faessler (1997) regarding the d' dibaryon decay width. The authors extend their own earlier work (Samsonov and Schepkin, 1993) which showed that the d' decay amplitude satisfies the Adler self-consistency condition and is proportional to k_μ. They also discuss the suppression of the radiative decay d' → dγ, noting that E1 transitions with ΔT = 0 between N = Z nuclei are forbidden (Radicati, 1952), and that this suppression can be avoided in flux-tube models with anomalously light diquarks. The work enables improved experimental searches for the d' resonance, particularly through cuts on NN invariant masses in double pion production experiments at ITEP (Moscow).
