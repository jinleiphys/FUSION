# Nuclear Dominated Accretion Flows in Two Dimensions. II. Ejecta dynamics and nucleosynthesis for CO and ONe white dwarfs

> We study mass ejection from accretion disks formed in the merger of a white dwarf with a neutron star or black hole. These disks are mostly radiatively-inefficient and support nuclear fusion reactions, with ensuing outflows and electromagnetic transients. Here we perform time-dependent, axisymmetric hydrodynamic simulations of these disks including a physical equation of state, viscous angular momentum transport, a coupled $19$-isotope nuclear network, and self-gravity. We find no detonations in any of the configurations studied. Our global models extend from the central object to radii much larger than the disk. We evolve these global models for several orbits, as well as alternate versions with an excised inner boundary to much longer times. We obtain robust outflows, with a broad velocity distribution in the range $10^2-10^4$ km s$^{-1}$. The outflow composition is mostly that of the initial white dwarf, with burning products mixed in at the $\lesssim 10-30\%$ level by mass, including up to $\sim 10^{-2}M_\odot$ of ${}^{56}$Ni. These heavier elements (plus ${}^{4}$He) are ejected within $\lesssim 40^\circ$ of the rotation axis, and should have higher average velocities than the lighter elements that make up the white dwarf. These results are in broad agreement with previous one- and two-dimensional studies, and point to these systems as progenitors of rapidly-rising ($\sim $ few day) transients. If accretion onto the central BH/NS powers a relativistic jet, these events could be accompanied by high energy transients with peak luminosities $\sim 10^{47}-10^{50}$ erg s$^{-1}$ and peak durations of up to several minutes, possibly accounting for events like CDF-S XT2.

## Key claim

No detonations occur in any of the simulated configurations of accretion disks formed from white dwarf mergers with neutron stars or black holes, contrary to some earlier studies that used parametric nuclear reactions and an ideal gas equation of state; the inclusion of radiation pressure from a physical equation of state prevents thermonuclear runaways.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent, axisymmetric hydrodynamic simulations of accretion disks formed from CO and ONe white dwarfs merging with neutron stars or black holes, using the FLASH3 code with a physical Helmholtz equation of state, a coupled 19-isotope nuclear reaction network, viscous angular momentum transport via an alpha-viscosity prescription, and self-gravity. They evolved two sets of models: "large inner boundary" models that excised the innermost region to follow the outer disk for up to 100 orbital periods (several hours), and "small inner boundary" models that resolved down to the central compact object surface but were evolved for only several orbital periods.

## Key numbers

- Outflow velocities span a broad range of 10^2 to 10^4 km s^-1 across all models
- Up to approximately 10^-2 solar masses of 56Ni are produced in the outflows
- Burning products are mixed into the outflow at the less than or equal to 10-30% level by mass
- Heavier elements and 4He are ejected within less than 40 degrees of the rotation axis
- For the CO+NS large boundary model, the asymptotic ejecta mass extrapolated to infinite time is 0.44 solar masses, representing 60-70% of the initial white dwarf mass
- The accreted mass at the compact object is approximately 3 x 10^-3 solar masses for WD+NS models and 0.12 solar masses for the ONe+BH model
- Maximum outflow velocities reach approximately 6,000 km s^-1 for CO+NS models and approximately 15,000 km s^-1 for the ONe+BH model
- The mass outflow rate decays as a power law with exponents delta approximately 1.3, 1.2, and 1.8 for the CO+NS, CO/He+NS, and ONe+BH large boundary models respectively
- For small boundary models resolving the neutron star, the 56Ni mass fraction in the ejecta at the torus radius is 2 x 10^-2 for the CO+NS model

## Context

This work extends the one-dimensional height-integrated models of Margalit and Metzger (2016) and the two-dimensional simulations of Fernandez and Metzger (2013, Paper I) by including a physical equation of state, a fully-coupled nuclear network, and self-gravity. The absence of detonations contradicts some results from Paper I that used parametric nuclear reactions and an ideal gas equation of state, and also differs from the detonations reported by Zenati (2019) in similar two-dimensional simulations. The results point to these systems as progenitors of rapidly-rising transients lasting a few days, and if the central object powers a relativistic jet, the events could produce high-energy transients with peak luminosities of 10^47 to 10^50 erg s^-1 lasting up to several minutes, potentially explaining events like CDF-S XT2.
