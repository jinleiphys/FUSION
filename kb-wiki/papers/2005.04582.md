# Manifestation of the divergence between antisymmetrized-molecular-dynamics and container pictures of $^{9}$Be via ${}^{9}$Be($p,pn$)${}^{8}$Be knockout reaction

> We propose a new approach to probe the spatial extension of the valence neutron orbital in the $^{9}$Be nucleus via the ${}^{9}$Be($p,pn$)${}^{8}$Be knockout reaction. This property of the nuclear molecular orbital has not been established in previous experimental studies and divergence exists between the theoretical descriptions of ${}^{9}$Be from different perspectives, \textit{i.e.}, the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics and the container pictures of cluster dynamics. These pictures are represented by two different well-proven microscopic models, the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) and Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Röpke (THSR) wave functions. The corresponding reduced width amplitudes (RWAs) in the $^{8}$Be$+n$ channel are extracted from both the AMD and THSR wave functions, and they are found to describe drastically different valence-nucleon motion, which shows the theoretical ambiguity in describing the $π$-orbitals in $^{9}$Be. Using the RWAs as input, the physical observables of the ${}^{9}$Be($p,pn$)${}^{8}$Be knockout reaction are predicted by the distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) framework. The magnitudes of the triple-differential cross sections (TDX) are found to be highly sensitive to the RWA input. It is concluded that the ${}^{9}$Be($p,pn$)${}^{8}$Be knockout reaction could provide a feasible probing for the subtle differences between several structure models manifesting through the spatial extension of the $π$-orbital in the $^{9}$Be nucleus.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that the $^{9}$Be($p,pn$)${}^{8}$Be knockout reaction can distinguish between two competing microscopic descriptions of the valence neutron $\pi$-orbital in $^{9}$Be, the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) picture and the Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Röpke (THSR) container picture, by measuring the triple-differential cross section (TDX), which is predicted to differ by nearly a factor of two between the two models.

## Method
The authors extracted reduced width amplitudes (RWAs) in the $^{8}$Be$+n$ channel from both AMD and THSR wave functions of the $^{9}$Be ground state. These RWAs were used as input to the distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) framework to calculate the triple-differential cross sections (TDX) of the $^{9}$Be($p,pn$)${}^{8}$Be knockout reaction at 392 MeV laboratory energy. Two sets of distorting potentials (EDAD1 Dirac-phenomenology and microscopic single-folding with Melbourne $g$-matrix) were employed to estimate optical potential uncertainties.

## Key numbers
- The TDX predicted with the THSR wave function is larger than that with the AMD wave function by nearly a factor of 2.
- The neutron spectroscopic factors are 0.259 for THSR and 0.182 for AMD.
- The reaction energy is 392 MeV for the incoming proton, with the outgoing proton at 251 MeV and emission angle ($\theta_1^L, \phi_1^L$) = ($34^\circ, 0^\circ$); the quasifree condition is fulfilled at $\theta_2^L=50.5^\circ$.
- The AMD calculation gives the $^{9}$Be ground state energy $E = -59.2$ MeV, excitation energy of the first $5/2^{-}$ state $\Delta E = 1.8$ MeV, and matter root-mean-square radius $R_{\text{rms}} = 2.52$ fm; the THSR calculation gives $E = -55.4$ MeV, $\Delta E = 2.4$ MeV, and $R_{\text{rms}} = 2.55$ fm; experimental values are $E = -58.2$ MeV, $\Delta E = 2.4$ MeV, and $R_{\text{rms}} = 2.45$ fm.

## Context
This work extends previous theoretical studies of molecular orbitals in Be isotopes using AMD (Enyo, 1995; Kimura, 2016) and THSR (Lyu, 2015; Lyu, 2016) wave functions, which had shown divergent descriptions of the valence neutron spatial extension but lacked experimental validation. The approach builds on the established DWIA framework for ($p,pN$) knockout reactions (Wakasa, 2017; Phuc, 2019) and earlier predictions for ($p,p\alpha$) reactions on Be isotopes (Lyu, 2018; Lyu, 2019). The proposed reaction enables direct experimental probing of the $\pi$-orbital size, which electron scattering cannot provide, and can be extended to exotic nuclei via inverse kinematics ($p,pN$) experiments.
