# Dynamical onset of superconductivity and retention of magnetic fields in cooling neutron stars

> A superconductor of paired protons is thought to form in the core of neutron stars soon after their birth. Minimum energy conditions suggest magnetic flux is expelled from the superconducting region due to the Meissner effect, such that the neutron star core is largely devoid of magnetic fields for some nuclear equation of state and proton pairing models. We show via neutron star cooling simulations that the superconducting region expands faster than flux is expected to be expelled because cooling timescales are much shorter than timescales of magnetic field diffusion. Thus magnetic fields remain in the bulk of the neutron star core for at least 10^6-10^7 yr. We estimate the size of flux free regions at 10^7 yr to be <~ 100 m for a magnetic field of 10^11 G and possibly smaller for stronger field strengths. For proton pairing models that are narrow, magnetic flux may be completely expelled from a thin shell of approximately the above size after 10^5 yr. This shell may insulate lower conductivity outer layers, where magnetic fields can diffuse and decay faster, from fields maintained in the highly conducting deep core.

## Key claim

The paper shows via neutron star cooling simulations that the superconducting region of paired protons expands faster than magnetic flux can be expelled by the Meissner effect, because cooling timescales are much shorter than magnetic field diffusion timescales. This means magnetic fields remain in the bulk of the neutron star core for at least 10^6-10^7 yr, contrary to the minimum-energy expectation of complete flux expulsion.

## Method

The authors performed neutron star cooling simulations using a relativistic code solving energy balance and heat flux equations, considering three nuclear equation of state models (APR, BSk20, BSk21) and three proton pairing gap models (AO, BS, CCDK). They compared the cooling timescale (the rate at which the superconducting region expands) against two magnetic field diffusion timescales: a modified Ohmic timescale and a superconducting dissipation timescale. The cooling lengthscale (radial growth of the superconducting region per logarithmic time decade) was compared to the magnetic field diffusion lengthscale at each epoch.

## Key numbers

- Cooling timescale is ~1 yr (T/10^9 K)^(-6) for modified Urca processes, while flux diffusion timescales are ~10^6-10^7 yr for km-scale lengthscales; the ratio t_cool/t_Ohm and t_cool/t_sc are both ~10^(-8) for l_mag ~ 1 km
- At 10^7 yr, flux-free regions are <~ 100 m for a magnetic field of 10^11 G, and possibly smaller for stronger field strengths
- For narrow proton pairing models (BS gap), magnetic flux may be completely expelled from a thin shell of approximately the above size after 10^5 yr
- The modified Ohmic diffusion timescale is 4.4 x 10^6 yr (sigma_c/10^29 s^(-1)) (l_mag/1 km)^2 (H/2H_c2 / 10^(-5))
- The superconducting dissipation timescale is 3.9 x 10^7 yr (n_nuc/n_p)^(1/6) (l_mag/1 km)^2
- For a 1.9 M_sun NS with APR EOS and CCDK gap, the superconducting region grows by hundreds of meters per logarithmic time decade
- For a 1.4 M_sun NS with APR EOS and CCDK gap, the cooling lengthscale is ~1 km and flux diffusion timescales exceed 10^7 yr
- For the BS gap model with BSk21 EOS, the superconducting region has radial width <~ 2 km and flux expulsion could occur in ~10^4 yr for ~10^10 G fields

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Baym et al. (1969) and Baym (1970) who first considered Meissner flux expulsion in neutron stars but did not quantitatively examine whether expulsion could occur fast enough during cooling. It contradicts the assumption that minimum-energy Meissner states are dynamically achieved, showing instead that rapid cooling prevents flux expulsion. The results enable understanding of magnetic field retention in young neutron stars and magnetars (age < 10^5 yr), limiting the amount of field decay that can occur if crustal fields are anchored in the core. The work uses the flux diffusion formulation of Graber et al. (2015) and Graber (2016), and complements the study of Elfritz et al. (2016) who considered fluxtube motion from core to crust.
