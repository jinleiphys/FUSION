# Improved predictions of nuclear reaction rates with the TALYS reaction code for astrophysical applications

> Nuclear reaction rates of astrophysical applications are traditionally determined on the basis of Hauser-Feshbach reaction codes. These codes adopt a number of approximations that have never been tested, such as a simplified width fluctuation correction, the neglect of delayed or multiple-particle emission during the electromagnetic decay cascade, or the absence of the pre-equilibrium contribution at increasing incident energies. The reaction code TALYS has been recently updated to estimate the Maxwellian-averaged reaction rates that are of astrophysical relevance. These new developments enable the reaction rates to be calculated with increased accuracy and reliability and the approximations of previous codes to be investigated. The TALYS predictions for the thermonuclear rates of relevance to astrophysics are detailed and compared with those derived by widely-used codes for the same nuclear ingredients. It is shown that TALYS predictions may differ significantly from those of previous codes, in particular for nuclei for which no or little nuclear data is available. The pre-equilibrium process is shown to influence the astrophysics rates of exotic neutron-rich nuclei significantly. For the first time, the Maxwellian-averaged (n,2n) reaction rate is calculated for all nuclei and its competition with the radiative capture rate is discussed. The TALYS code provides a new tool to estimate all nuclear reaction rates of relevance to astrophysics with improved accuracy and reliability.

## Key claim

The TALYS reaction code, updated for astrophysical applications, provides Maxwellian-averaged reaction rates with increased accuracy and reliability compared to traditional Hauser-Feshbach codes, but the improvements are most significant for exotic neutron-rich nuclei and specific cases like the Re-Os chronometer, while for stable nuclei the differences are often within a factor of 2-3 and comparable to uncertainties in nuclear input models.

## Method

The authors updated the TALYS reaction code to calculate Maxwellian-averaged thermonuclear reaction rates using the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model with full jls coupling, including width fluctuation corrections via the Moldauer (1980) or GOE (Verbaarschot et al. 1985) prescriptions, pre-equilibrium emission via the exciton model, multi-particle emission channels, and parity-dependent level densities. They compared TALYS predictions with those from the widely-used MOST code using identical nuclear input models (HFB masses, HFB plus combinatorial level densities, QRPA gamma-ray strengths, and global optical potentials) for nuclei between Z=16 and Z=83 from the valley of stability to the neutron drip line.

## Key numbers

* The stellar $^{187}$Os(n,$\gamma$)$^{188}$Os rate at temperatures of 1-3 $\times$ 10$^8$ K differs by approximately 40% between the Hofmann et al. (1980) width fluctuation correction (used in previous codes) and the exact GOE correction implemented in TALYS.
* For stable nuclei above Ca, both TALYS and MOST reproduce experimental radiative neutron capture rates at T=3 $\times$ 10$^8$ K to within roughly a factor of 2-3.
* For exotic neutron-rich Sn and Pb isotopes at T=10$^9$ K, the ratio of MOST to TALYS radiative neutron capture rates deviates by factors as large as 10 (e.g., $^{211}$Pb shows a factor of approximately 7 difference).
* The TALYS code is optimized for incident projectile energies from 1 keV up to 200 MeV on target nuclei with mass numbers between 12 and 339.

## Context

This work extends the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model calculations previously performed by codes such as MOST (Aikawa et al. 2005; Arnould and Goriely 2006), SMOKER (Thielemann et al. 1987), and NON-SMOKER (Rauscher and Thielemann 2001), which neglected pre-equilibrium contributions, used simplified width fluctuation corrections (Hofmann et al. 1980), and omitted multi-particle emission and parity-dependent level densities. The TALYS code enables testing these approximations for the first time and provides a new tool for calculating reaction rates for r-process nucleosynthesis and the Re-Os nucleocosmochronology, where the improved width fluctuation correction significantly affects the age determination of the Galaxy. The paper also presents the first calculation of Maxwellian-averaged (n,2n) reaction rates for all nuclei and discusses their competition with radiative capture.
