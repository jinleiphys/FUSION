# Current Status of r-Process Nucleosynthesis

> The rapid neutron capture process (r process) is believed to be responsible for about half of the production of the elements heavier than iron and contributes to abundances of some lighter nuclides as well. A universal pattern of r-process element abundances is observed in some metal-poor stars of the Galactic halo. This suggests that a well-regulated combination of astrophysical conditions and nuclear physics conspires to produce such a universal abundance pattern. The search for the astrophysical site for r-process nucleosynthesis has stimulated interdisciplinary research for more than six decades. There is currently much enthusiasm surrounding evidence for r-process nucleosynthesis in binary neutron star mergers in the multi-wavelength follow-up observations of kilonova/gravitational-wave GRB170807A/GW170817. Nevertheless, there remain questions as to the contribution over the history of the Galaxy to the current solar-system r-process abundances from other sites such as neutrino-driven winds or magnetohydrodynamical ejection of material from core-collapse supernovae. In this review we highlight some current issues surrounding the nuclear physics input, astronomical observations, galactic chemical evolution, and theoretical simulations of r-process astrophysical environments with the goal of outlining a path toward resolving the remaining mysteries of the r process.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while binary neutron star mergers (as confirmed by GW170817) are now the leading candidate for the main/heavy r-process, a multi-component origin is likely required, with neutrino-driven winds from core-collapse supernovae possibly contributing the light/weak r-process component, though the relative contributions over Galactic history remain uncertain.

## Method

This is a comprehensive review paper that synthesizes constraints from nuclear theory (masses, beta-decay rates, neutron capture rates, fission barriers, neutrino physics), experimental nuclear data, astronomical observations of metal-poor halo stars and kilonova/GW170817, galactic chemical evolution models, and theoretical simulations of candidate astrophysical sites (neutron star mergers, core-collapse supernovae with neutrino-driven winds or magnetohydrodynamical ejection). The authors evaluate how each piece of evidence supports or challenges different r-process site scenarios.

## Key numbers

- The r-process is believed responsible for about half of the production of elements heavier than iron
- Absolute abundances of r-process elements are about seven orders of magnitude below those of abundant elements like C and O
- Total amount of r-process material in the Galaxy is of order a few hundreds of M_sun, based on a galactic total interstellar gas mass estimate of 10^9 M_sun
- Nucleosynthesis events at occurrence rates of 10^-4 per year or below, ejecting amounts of 10^-4 M_sun or more are sufficient sources for the r-process
- Mass variations of ±0.5 MeV in nuclear masses can result in up to an order of magnitude change in the final abundance pattern
- The r-process path can proceed until neutron-induced or beta-induced fission terminates at A ~ 300
- In one model (Shibagaki 2016), only about 10% of the final yield comes from termination of the r-process path at N=212 and Z=111, while almost 90% of A=160 came from fission of more than 200 different parent nuclei mostly via beta-delayed fission

## Context

This review extends decades of work following the foundational papers by Burbidge (1957) and Cameron (1957) that first identified the r-process, and builds on the observational discovery of a universal r-process abundance pattern in metal-poor halo stars (Sneden 1995, 2002). The paper synthesizes the dramatic advance from the 2017 detection of GW170817 (Abbott 2017) and its kilonova counterpart (Metzger 2017), which provided the first direct evidence for r-process nucleosynthesis in neutron star mergers. The review enables future work by identifying key nuclear physics uncertainties (masses, beta-decay rates, fission fragment distributions) that need experimental measurement, and by framing the open question of whether a single site or multiple sites (including core-collapse supernovae as proposed by Woosley 1994 and Shibagaki 2016) are needed to explain the full solar-system r-process abundance pattern.
