# Quarkonium dissociation in quark-gluon plasma via ionization in magnetic field

> We study the impact of magnetic fields generated in relativistic heavy ion collisions on the decay probability of quarkonium produced in the central rapidity region. The quark and anti-quark components are subject to mutually orthogonal electric and magnetic fields in the quarkonium comoving frame. In the presence of an electric field, quarkonium has finite dissociation probability. We use the WKB approximation to derive the dissociation probability. We found that quarkonium dissociation energy, i.e. the binding energy at which dissociation probability is of order unity, increases with the magnetic field strength. It also increases with quarkonium momentum in the laboratory frame due to Lorentz boost of electric field in the comoving frame. As a consequence, J/Psi in plasma dissociates at lower temperature then it would be in the absence of a magnetic field. We argue that J/Psi's produced in heavy-ion collisions at LHC with P_T>9GeV would dissociate even in vacuum. In plasma, J/Psi dissociation in magnetic field is much stronger due to decrease of its binding energy with temperature. We discuss the phenomenological implications of our results.

## Key claim

Quarkonium dissociation in a magnetic field occurs via Lorentz ionization, where the quark and antiquark tunnel through a potential barrier in the comoving frame; the dissociation probability is of order unity when the binding energy drops below a threshold that increases with magnetic field strength and quarkonium momentum, meaning that J/Psi in plasma dissociates at a lower temperature than it would without a magnetic field, and J/Psi produced at LHC with transverse momentum above 9 GeV would dissociate even in vacuum.

## Method

The authors used the WKB (quasi-classical) approximation to calculate the tunneling probability of a quarkonium state in the presence of mutually orthogonal electric and magnetic fields in the quarkonium comoving frame, treating the binding force as short-range. They derived the dissociation probability with exponential accuracy, independent of the precise form of the quarkonium wave function, and then solved for the dissociation energy (the binding energy at which the dissociation probability is of order unity) as a function of magnetic field strength and quarkonium velocity. The non-relativistic limit was shown to provide a good approximation to the full relativistic calculation, and the contribution of quark spin was included via a replacement of the effective binding energy.

## Key numbers

*   At LHC, the estimated magnetic field strength is \(eB_0 \approx 15 m_\pi^2\) (Kharzeev 2007, Skokov 2009).
*   At RHIC, the estimated magnetic field strength is \(eB_0 \approx m_\pi^2\) (Kharzeev 2007).
*   The vacuum binding energy of J/Psi is \(\epsilon_b = 0.64\) GeV (derived from \(M=3.1\) GeV, \(m=1.87\) GeV).
*   For J/Psi moving perpendicular to the magnetic field at LHC (\(eB_0=15 m_\pi^2\)), the dissociation energy equals the vacuum binding energy at velocity \(V=0.94\), corresponding to transverse momentum \(P_\bot > 9\) GeV.
*   For J/Psi moving perpendicular to the magnetic field at LHC with half the vacuum binding energy, dissociation occurs at \(V>0.71\) (\(P_\bot > 3.1\) GeV) for \(eB_0=15 m_\pi^2\) and at \(V>0.92\) (\(P_\bot > 7.3\) GeV) for \(eB_0=7 m_\pi^2\).
*   The Zeeman splitting for J/Psi (\(S=1, L=0, J=1\)) in a field of \(eB_0=15 m_\pi^2\) is \(\Delta M = 0.15\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the study of quarkonium dissociation in quark-gluon plasma, originally proposed by Matsui and Satz (1986) as a probe of QGP formation via Debye screening, by incorporating the effect of strong magnetic fields generated in relativistic heavy-ion collisions (Kharzeev 2007). It introduces Lorentz ionization as a new dissociation mechanism, which is distinct from thermal screening and cold nuclear matter effects. The results enable predictions for J/Psi suppression at LHC, particularly a strong dependence on transverse momentum and azimuthal angle, and suggest that the magnetic field contributes to azimuthal anisotropy of J/Psi production, a subject for future investigation.
