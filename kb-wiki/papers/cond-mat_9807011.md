# Pair-correlations and magnetic susceptibility of small Al-grains

> Paircorrelations and the magnetic susceptibility of electrons in a spherical cavity are studied both for grand canonical and the canonical ensemble. The coupling constant of the $BCS$ Hamiltonian is adjusted to experimental values of the gap parameter. The gap parameter is found to increase for small grains as a consequence of the pronounced shell structure in the spectrum of the spherical cavity. The sharp phase transition at $T_c$ is smeared out for the canonical ensemble. The strong paramagnetic susceptibility of the normal electrons in the cavity is reduced by the superconductivity, but it remains positive.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the experimentally observed increase of the superconducting gap and transition temperature in small aluminum grains can be explained by the electronic shell structure of a spherical cavity, without requiring an increase of the effective pairing coupling constant; in fact, the strong level bunching in the spherical model over-accentuates the shell structure, so the coupling constant must be *attenuated* relative to the bulk to match the more modest increase seen in experiment.

## Method

The authors model electrons confined in a spherical cavity with infinite walls, using a BCS Hamiltonian with pairing interaction restricted to the Debye window around the Fermi level. They apply the single-shell approximation, where only the highly degenerate Fermi level participates in pairing, and solve the BCS equations numerically for the grand canonical ensemble; for the canonical ensemble, they use the exact quasi-spin solution of the pairing Hamiltonian. The coupling constant \(G(N)\) is adjusted to reproduce experimental two-quasiparticle energies and transition temperatures for Al grains with \(10^3 < N < 10^5\) electrons.

## Key numbers

- The measured ratio \(f = T_c(N)/T_c(\text{bulk})\) for Al grains is \(f \approx 1.5\) for \(N \sim 10^5\) and \(f \approx 3\) for \(N \sim 10^3\).
- The pairing gap \(\Delta\) in grains of radius \(R \sim (5\text{--}10)\) nm is measured to be as large as \(\sim 2\) times the bulk value.
- The fitted coupling constant for the spherical model is \(G = g N^{-\alpha}\) with \(g = 1.94\ \text{meV}\) and \(\alpha = 0.47\).
- An alternative fit gives \(G = g N^{-1} \exp[-\alpha N^{-\beta}]\) with \(g = 3.21\ \text{eV}\), \(\alpha = 25\), and \(\beta = 0.26\).
- The zero-temperature two-quasiparticle energy in the single-shell model is \(2E(0) = GM\), where \(M = 2l+1\) is the degeneracy of the Fermi level.
- The bulk BCS ratio \(2\Delta(0)/T_c = 3.52\) is recovered for the averaged values in the spherical model.
- For the canonical ensemble at half filling, the zero-temperature canonical gap is \(\Delta_{\text{can}}(0) = \Delta(0) \sqrt{1 + 2/(2M-1)}\), which approaches the BCS value for large \(M\).
- At \(T = 3\ \text{K}\), the paramagnetic susceptibility of normal grains \(\chi(G=0)\) is about 10 times higher than that of superconducting grains \(\chi(G>0)\).

## Context

This work extends studies of pairing in confined electron systems such as slabs (Ref. 9) and cubes (Ref. 11), and contrasts with the extended Landau-Ginzburg model for grains (Ref. 22) that ignores shell structure. The spherical model and the equidistant-level model are presented as two limiting cases of maximal and no shell structure, respectively; real grains lie somewhere in between. The results enable interpretation of experimental data on transition temperatures and magnetic susceptibility in small Al grains, and suggest that measurements of the \(N\)-dependence of susceptibility at low temperatures could distinguish between different forms of the coupling constant \(G(N)\).
