# s-process nucleosynthesis in low-mass AGB stars by the $^{13}$C($α$,n)$^{16}$O neutron source

> In this review we outline our knowledge on slow neutron captures, concentrating on its main part occurring during the final stages of stellar evolution for low or intermediate-mass stars when they evolve during the Asymptotic Giant Branch, or AGB, stars. We focus our attention on how, in this field, studies passed from a first era of inquiries based on nuclear systematics, to numerical nucleosynthesis computations performed in stellar codes. We then discuss how these last were forced, by observational constraints, to almost abandon, for the synthesis of nuclei between Sr and Pb, the rather naturally activated Ne22 neutron source (operating efficiently at T > 30 keV, and producing a neutron density N_n > 5 10^8 cm^-3). This implied considering the alternative reaction 13C(alpha,n)16O, that can be activated locally after each of the TDU. The mentioned crucial reaction occurs at T< 8 keV, in the time intervals separating two subsequent thermal pulses (TP). The layers where 13C(alpha,n)16O operates are characterized by a radiative equilibrium and their low temperature also yields low values for the neutron density (N_n < 10^7 cm^-3).

## Key claim

This review argues that the s-process nucleosynthesis of nuclei between Sr and Pb (the "main component") in low-mass AGB stars is driven by the $^{13}$C($\alpha$,n)$^{16}$O neutron source, not the $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n)$^{25}$Mg source, because the latter requires temperatures above 30 keV that are not reached in the thermal pulses of low-mass stars; however, the activation of the $^{13}$C source is "not straightforward" and requires specific mixing mechanisms to form a $^{13}$C pocket, a process whose details remain a "longstanding debate, partly still open."

## Method

The paper is a review that traces the historical development of s-process nucleosynthesis models, from the early phenomenological approach using exponential distributions of neutron exposures (Seeger 1965) to numerical stellar evolution codes. It describes how stellar models of TP-AGB stars incorporate convective boundary mixing, often parameterized by an exponential velocity profile with a tunable parameter $\beta$, to simulate the formation of a $^{13}$C pocket after each third dredge-up episode. The review then discusses how rotation, magnetic fields, and other non-convective mixing processes have been explored to improve agreement between model predictions and observations of AGB stars, post-AGB sources, and presolar grains.

## Key numbers

* The $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n)$^{25}$Mg neutron source operates efficiently at temperatures $T \gtrsim 3.5 \times 10^8$ K (30 keV) and produces a neutron density $n_n \gtrsim 5 \times 10^8$ cm$^{-3}$.
* The $^{13}$C($\alpha$,n)$^{16}$O neutron source operates at low temperatures ($T \simeq 8-9 \times 10^7$ K, less than 8 keV) and yields low neutron densities $n_n \lesssim 10^7$ cm$^{-3}$.
* At about 300 MK, the reaction rate ratio $<\sigma v>_{^{22}Ne(\alpha,n)}/<\sigma v>_{^{22}Ne(\alpha,\gamma)}$ is approximately 3.
* A typical $^{13}$C pocket extends for about $10^{-3}$ M$_\odot$ and contains a few $10^{-5}$ M$_\odot$ of $^{13}$C, corresponding to more than $10^{51}$ nuclei of $^{13}$C.
* The temperature at the base of the intermediate convective zone during thermal pulses ranges between 2.8 and $3.5 \times 10^8$ K, depending on stellar mass.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on s-process phenomenology by Seeger (1965) and the CFHZ paper, and the early stellar modeling by Schwarzschild and Harm (1965) and Icko Iben (1975, 1981). It describes the paradigm shift from the $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n)$^{25}$Mg neutron source to the $^{13}$C($\alpha$,n)$^{16}$O source, motivated by observations that s-process-rich carbon stars are of low mass, as first noted by Iben (1981). The review enables future work by outlining the open questions regarding the physical mechanisms that shape the hydrogen profile after third dredge-up, including convective overshoot (parameterized by the $\beta$ factor), rotation, and magnetic fields, which are needed to reproduce the complex isotopic patterns observed in presolar grains.
