# Range corrections for two-neutron halo nuclei in effective theory

> The range corrections to the universal properties and structure of two-neutron halo nuclei are investigated within an effective quantum mechanics framework. Treating the nucleus as an effective three-body system, we make a systematic improvement upon previous calculations by calculating the linear range corrections at next-to-leading order. Since the effective ranges for the neutron-core interactions are not known, we estimate the effective range to be set by the inverse of the pion mass. We investigate the possibility of excited Efimov states in two-neutron halo nuclei and calculate their mean square radii to next-to-leading order. We find that the effective range corrections are generally small and the leading order predictions are very robust.

## Key claim

The paper systematically improves upon leading-order calculations of two-neutron halo nuclei by including linear effective-range corrections at next-to-leading order, finding that these corrections are generally small and that the leading-order predictions are very robust; however, the existence of an excited Efimov state in $^{20}$C depends on the uncertain $n$-$^{18}$C bound state energy, with the NLO threshold shifting from 165 keV to 161 keV.

## Method

The authors treat two-neutron halo nuclei as effective three-body systems (core plus two valence neutrons) and solve coupled Faddeev integral equations for the spectator functions using a separable effective potential with contact interactions up to next-to-leading order. The two-body T-matrices incorporate both scattering lengths and effective ranges for the neutron-neutron and neutron-core interactions, with the effective range estimated as the inverse of the pion mass (1.4 fm) where experimental values are unknown. A three-body force term is tuned to reproduce the experimental ground state binding energy, and the cutoff is chosen consistent with the Wigner bound.

## Key numbers

- The NLO threshold for an excited Efimov state in $^{20}$C is $E_{nc} < 161$ keV, compared to the LO threshold of $E_{nc} < 165$ keV.
- The shift in the excited state binding energy due to range corrections is no greater than approximately 0.5 keV over the range of possible $E_{nc}$ values.
- For $^{11}$Li with $E_{nc} = -25$ keV and $r_0 = 1.4$ fm, the NLO radii are: $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2 \rangle} = 8.80 \pm 0.07$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_{nc}^2 \rangle} = 7.21 \pm 0.06$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_n^2 \rangle} = 6.51 \pm 0.05$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_c^2 \rangle} = 1.040 \pm 0.008$ fm.
- For $^{14}$Be with $E_{nc} = -200$ keV and $r_0 = 1.4$ fm, the NLO radii are: $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2 \rangle} = 3.86 \pm 0.09$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_{nc}^2 \rangle} = 3.29 \pm 0.08$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_n^2 \rangle} = 3.02 \pm 0.07$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_c^2 \rangle} = 0.384 \pm 0.009$ fm.
- For $^{20}$C with $E_{nc} = 530$ keV and $r_0 = 1.4$ fm, the NLO radii are: $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2 \rangle} = 3.38 \pm 0.18$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_{nc}^2 \rangle} = 2.75 \pm 0.15$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_n^2 \rangle} = 2.60 \pm 0.14$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_c^2 \rangle} = 0.21 \pm 0.01$ fm.
- For the excited Efimov state of $^{20}$C ($^{20}$C*) with $E_{nc} = 60$ keV and $r_0 = 1.4$ fm, the NLO radii are: $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2 \rangle} = 43.2 \pm 0.5$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_{nc}^2 \rangle} = 38.7 \pm 0.4$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_n^2 \rangle} = 42.9 \pm 0.5$ fm, $\sqrt{\langle r_c^2 \rangle} = 2.26 \pm 0.02$ fm.
- The neutron-neutron effective range is taken as $r_{nn} = (2.75 \pm 0.11)$ fm from experiment (Miller 1990), but the authors use $r_{nn} \approx r_{nc} \approx 1.4$ fm for consistency.
- The cutoff used in calculations is $\Lambda = 580$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the leading-order effective theory calculations of Canham and Hammer (2008) for two-neutron halo nuclei by including next-to-leading order effective range corrections, following similar studies of range corrections in the three-nucleon system (Hammer 2001; Bedaque 2002; Griesshammer 2004; Platter 2006) and in the Efimov effect for three identical bosons (Thogersen 2008; Platter, Ji, and Phillips 2008). The results confirm earlier model studies by Fedorov (1994) and Amorim (1996) that $^{20}$C is the only candidate for an excited Efimov state, but the existence depends on the uncertain $n$-$^{18}$C binding energy, with the TUNL evaluation (1995) giving $E_{nc} = (162 \pm 112)$ keV leaving the possibility open, while the AME2003 evaluation gives $E_{nc} = (580 \pm 100)$ keV which excludes it. The NLO corrections enable more precise predictions for halo nucleus radii and binding energies, though the authors note that their error estimates do not include the uncertainty in the effective range estimate itself.
