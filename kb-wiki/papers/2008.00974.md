# Nuclear Currents in Chiral Effective Field Theory

> In this article, we review the status of the calculation of nuclear currents within chiral effective field theory. After formal discussion of the unitary transformation technique and its application to nuclear currents we will give all available expressions for vector, axial-vector currents. Vector and axial-vector currents will be discussed up to order $Q$ with leading-order contribution starting at order $Q^{-3}$. Pseudoscalar and scalar currents will be discussed up to order $Q^0$ with leading-order contribution starting at order $Q^{-4}$. This is a complete set of expressions in next-to-next-to-next-to-leading-order (N$^3$LO) analysis for nuclear scalar, pseudoscalar, vector and axial-vector current operators. Differences between vector and axial-vector currents calculated via transfer-matrix inversion and unitary transformation techniques are discussed. The importance of consistent regularization is an additional point which is emphasized: lack of consistent regularization of axial-vector current operators is shown to lead to a violation of the chiral symmetry in the chiral limit at order $Q$. For this reason, a hybrid approach at order $Q$, discussed in various publications, is non-applicable. To respect the chiral symmetry the same regularization procedure needs to be used in the construction of nuclear forces and current operators. Although full expressions of consistently regularized current operators are not yet available an isoscalar part of the electromagnetic charge operator up to order $Q$ has a very simple form and can be easily regularized in a consistent way. As an application, we review our recent high accuracy calculation of the deuteron charge form factor with a quantified error estimate.

## Key claim

This review paper claims that a complete set of nuclear current operators (scalar, pseudoscalar, vector, and axial-vector) can be derived up to N³LO in chiral effective field theory using the unitary transformation technique, but the abstract's claim of completeness is qualified by the fact that consistently regularized current operators are not yet available; a mismatch between dimensional regularization used in current construction and cutoff regularization used in practical calculations leads to chiral symmetry violation at one-loop order, making hybrid approaches at order Q non-applicable.

## Method

The authors apply the unitary transformation technique to block-diagonalize the chiral perturbation theory Hamiltonian, first for the strong interaction to obtain energy-independent nuclear forces, then extend the method to construct nuclear currents by introducing a time-dependent unitary transformation that depends explicitly on external sources (scalar, pseudoscalar, vector, axial-vector). The current operators are obtained by functional differentiation of the transformed Hamiltonian with respect to the corresponding external source, yielding energy-transfer-dependent momentum-space currents. The approach is compared with the alternative time-ordered perturbation theory combined with transfer-matrix inversion used by the JLab-Pisa group.

## Key numbers

- Vector and axial-vector currents are discussed up to order Q with leading-order contribution starting at order Q⁻³; pseudoscalar and scalar currents are discussed up to order Q⁰ with leading-order contribution starting at order Q⁻⁴
- For the radiative capture n + p → γ + d, the impulse approximation showed a 10% discrepancy with experiment, which was explained by including pion exchange currents (Riska 1972)
- At N³LO, meson-exchange currents make a 4% effect on the proton-proton fusion rate compared to the leading single-particle Gamow-Teller matrix element (Park 1998)
- Two-body currents contribute at the 2-3% level for weak transitions in A ≤ 10 nuclei, except for ⁸Li, ⁸B, and ⁸He β-decays where they provide 20-30% corrections (King 2020)
- For ¹⁰C β-decay, using phenomenological AV18+IL7 wave functions gave a ~10% discrepancy with data, which decreased to 4% when chiral EFT wave functions were used (Pastore 2017)

## Context

This review extends the line of work on nuclear currents that began with meson-exchange models (Riska 1972, Villars 1947) and the application of chiral perturbation theory to nuclear forces pioneered by Weinberg (1990, 1991). It builds upon first-generation chiral EFT currents by Park et al. (1993, 1995) and second-generation currents developed by the JLab-Pisa group using time-ordered perturbation theory (Pastore 2008, 2009) and by the author's group using unitary transformation techniques (Kolling 2009, 2011). The work enables future calculations of nuclear observables with consistent regularization that respects chiral symmetry, with a first application to the deuteron charge form factor providing a high-accuracy determination with quantified error estimates.
