# A Tale of Tails: Photon Rates and Flow in Ultra-Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> We consider the possibility that quark and gluon distributions in the medium created in high energy heavy ion collisions may be modified by a power law tail at energies much higher than the temperature. We parametrize such a tail by Tsallis distributions with an exponent motivated by phenomenology. These distributions are characterized by an effective temperature scale that we assume to evolve in time like the temperature for thermal distributions. We find that including such a tail increases the rates for photon production and significantly delays the emission times for photons of a fixed energy. We argue that these effects are sufficiently large that they should be able to account for photon yields and flow patterns seen in LHC and RHIC experiments.

# A Tale of Tails: Photon Rates and Flow in Ultra-Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The authors propose that power-law tails in quark and gluon momentum distributions, parametrized by Tsallis distributions with exponent a ≈ 6, can simultaneously increase photon production rates and delay photon emission times enough to resolve the discrepancy between theoretical calculations and experimental photon yields and elliptic flow at RHIC and LHC. The claim is conditional on the assumption that the Tsallis distributions scale with a single temperature-like parameter that evolves in time like the thermal temperature, and that the exponent a ≈ 6 is motivated by phenomenology from proton-proton collisions.

## Method

The authors compute photon production rates from a quark-gluon plasma by replacing the usual Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac distribution functions for quarks and gluons with Tsallis distributions of the form f(E) = [1 + E/(aT)]^{-a}. They evaluate the hard (p > p*) contributions to the Compton scattering and annihilation processes using Monte Carlo integration, and multiply the full thermal rate (including hard thermal loop resummation for soft contributions) by the ratio r(E_γ/T) extracted from the hard part. The photon yield is then integrated over a 1+1 dimensional Bjorken-expanding plasma with time-dependent temperature T(t) = T_0 (t_0/t)^{1/3}, with the initial temperature in the Tsallis case rescaled to maintain equal parton number density.

## Key numbers

- For a = 6, the ratio of Tsallis to thermal photon rate is well approximated by r(E_γ/T) = 2.06 + 0.238 exp(0.608 E_γ/T)
- The initial temperature rescaling factor for equal parton number is N_T(6) ≈ 0.65 (Boltzmann approximation), with N_T(6) = 0.67 for Bose and N_T(6) = 0.64 for Fermi distributions
- Mean photon emission times for thermal case (T_0 = 500 MeV): 3.9 fm/c at p_T = 1 GeV, 1.7 fm/c at p_T = 2 GeV, 0.9 fm/c at p_T = 3 GeV
- Mean photon emission times for Tsallis case (T_0 = 325 MeV, a = 6): 4.6 fm/c at p_T = 1 GeV, 3.5 fm/c at p_T = 2 GeV, 2.5 fm/c at p_T = 3 GeV
- The stationary phase point for the Tsallis distribution integral gives E_γ/T = 4a/(a-4), which equals 12 for a = 6
- The infrared cutoff used is p* = 10^{-0.25} ≈ 0.56

## Context

This work addresses the "thermal photon puzzle" where calculations using thermal distributions (Shuryak 1978, Kapusta 1991, Baier 1991) produce photon yields approximately a factor of 4 too small and elliptic flow v_2 significantly lower than observed at RHIC (Adare 2008, 2011) and LHC (Wilde 2012, Lohner 2012). Previous attempts to resolve this include work by Liu (2009), Bzdak (2012), Muller (2013), Linnyk (2013, 2015), Monnai (2014), and Gale (2014). The authors' mechanism differs from most others by simultaneously increasing yields and delaying emission times, potentially allowing photons to be produced after anisotropic flow has developed. The work enables future incorporation of these modified rates into full 3+1 dimensional hydrodynamic simulations, and suggests that first-principles computations of parton distributions from classical field methods (Blaizot 2011, Gelis 2013, Epelbaum 2014, Berges 2013, 2014) could test the assumptions about scaling behavior.
