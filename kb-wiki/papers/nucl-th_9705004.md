# Analysis of Photoreaction in the Delta Energy Region by the Quantum Molecular Dynamics Approach

> We study the photoreaction in the delta energy region using the QMD approach. The proton and pion cross-sections are calculated and compared with experimental data. Through this work we examine the multistep contributions in the cross-sections and the {$π- Δ$} dynamics.

## Key claim

The Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) approach, previously applied to nucleon-induced reactions, can be extended to photoreactions in the delta energy region, but the model's semi-classical treatment of pion absorption and the lack of a pure two-nucleon pion absorption process lead to systematic discrepancies with experimental data, particularly an overestimate of pion photoproduction cross-sections and a dip in the proton spectrum between the quasi-free peak and the two-nucleon absorption peak that is not observed experimentally.

## Method

The authors applied the QMD plus Statistical Decay Model (SDM) framework, using the same parameters as in their previous nucleon-induced reaction studies (Niita et al., 1995), to simulate photoreactions on various nuclear targets (C, Al, Ti, Cu, He) at photon momenta of 213-390 MeV/c. The initial photoabsorption was modeled via three channels: delta resonance production, N*(1440) resonance production, and direct pion-nucleon pair production, with cross-sections fitted to experimental gamma-proton data. The simulation tracked the step-wise collisional evolution of the system, decomposing contributions by the number of collisions (step number) responsible for particle emission, and compared calculated proton and pion double-differential cross-sections with experimental data from Kanazawa (1987), Arends (1991), and others.

## Key numbers

- The QMD parameters include: saturation density rho_0 = 0.168 fm^-3, Skyrme parameters A = -124 MeV, B = 70.5 MeV, tau = 4/3, giving binding energy/nucleon of 16 MeV at saturation and compressibility of 237.7 MeV (soft EOS); width parameter L = 2 fm^2
- For the gamma (375 MeV/c) + C reaction at theta = 30 degrees, the calculated quasi-free peak position almost coincides with the experimental peak from Kanazawa (1987), but the absolute value overestimates the data
- For gamma (390 MeV/c) + C and + Ti reactions at theta = 52 degrees, the QMD+SDM results underestimate experimental data around the quasi-free peak and two-nucleon photoabsorption energy regions
- The pion mean-free path lambda used in test calculations was estimated to be 5 fm, based on Cassing et al. (1990)
- For the total pi0 production cross-sections on C, Al, and Cu targets, the QMD results overestimate experimental data from Arends (1986) at the delta resonance peak energy, with the overestimate increasing with mass number A
- In the test calculation QMD/T1 (increasing the N+Delta -> N+N cross-section by four times), the pi0 cross-section at q = 375 MeV/c is slightly reduced but still overestimates experimental data
- For gamma (213 MeV/c) + Ca reaction, the pion spectra with and without a delta potential show no significant difference; without the delta potential the quasi-free peaks are shifted to slightly higher energy at theta = 81 and 109 degrees

## Context

This work extends the QMD+SDM framework previously applied to nucleon-induced reactions (Niita et al., 1995; Chadwick et al., 1995; Chiba et al., 1996) to photoreactions, aiming for a unified description of different reaction types. The study contrasts with other approaches such as the PICA code (Gabriel and Alsmiller, 1969), which lacks explicit delta degrees of freedom; the Monte Carlo model of Carrasco et al. (1992), which operates only in momentum space; and the BUU approach (Cassing et al., 1990; Hombach et al., 1995), which describes only one-body dynamics and cannot treat clusters or coincident observables. The results enable future improvements by introducing a pure two-nucleon pion absorption process (three-body collisions) and more realistic elementary photoabsorption processes, as suggested by Engel et al. (1994) and Nozawa et al. (1990), to simultaneously describe proton-induced reactions, heavy-ion collisions, and photoreactions.
