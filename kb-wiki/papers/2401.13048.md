# Quantum error mitigation for Fourier moment computation

> Hamiltonian moments in Fourier space - expectation values of the unitary evolution operator under a Hamiltonian at different times - provide a convenient framework to understand quantum systems. They offer insights into the energy distribution, higher-order dynamics, response functions, correlation information and physical properties. This paper focuses on the computation of Fourier moments within the context of a nuclear effective field theory on superconducting quantum hardware. The study integrates echo verification and noise renormalization into Hadamard tests using control reversal gates. These techniques, combined with purification and error suppression methods, effectively address quantum hardware decoherence. The analysis, conducted using noise models, reveals a significant reduction in noise strength by two orders of magnitude. Moreover, quantum circuits involving up to 266 CNOT gates over five qubits demonstrate high accuracy under these methodologies when run on IBM superconducting quantum devices.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that combining echo verification (EV) and operator decoherence renormalization (ODR) with control reversal gates (CRG) can reduce noise strength by two orders of magnitude in the computation of Fourier moments for a nuclear effective field theory on superconducting quantum hardware, but only under the condition that the noise is sufficiently depolarizing (achieved via randomized compiling and Pauli twirling) and that the post-selection probability remains above 0.08.

## Method

The authors computed Fourier moments \( m(j\tau) = \langle \Psi_0 | \hat{O}^\dagger e^{-iHj\tau} \hat{O} | \Psi_0 \rangle \) for a triton-inspired nuclear lattice model (2D Fermi-Hubbard with 2 dynamical nucleons plus a static nucleon on a \( L \times L \) lattice) using Hadamard tests on IBM superconducting quantum devices. They integrated echo verification (EV) with control reversal gates (CRG) to mitigate errors, where CRG toggles forward/backward time evolution using Pauli operators that anticommute with the Hamiltonian, and applied operator decoherence renormalization (ODR) to estimate and correct depolarizing noise parameters. Error suppression techniques including dynamical decoupling, pulse-efficient transpilation, and randomized compiling were used to make the hardware noise more depolarizing.

## Key numbers

* Noise strength was reduced by two orders of magnitude using the combined error mitigation techniques (EV + ODR + CRG) compared to unmitigated runs.
* Quantum circuits involved up to 266 CNOT gates over five qubits and demonstrated high accuracy on IBM superconducting quantum devices.
* The post-selection probability \( P_0 \) remained above 0.08 for all experiments performed, ensuring the echo verification protocol remained viable.
* For the CRG implementation with second-order Trotter steps and linear connectivity, the total CNOT overhead was \( 14 + 2r \) gates (where \( r \) is the number of Trotter steps), compared to \( 2\Gamma \) CNOT gates for a naive controlled implementation (with \( \Gamma \) the number of Pauli strings in the Hamiltonian).
* The number of CRG groups required for the Hamiltonian studied was \( M = 3 \), compared to the worst-case bound of \( M = 2n \) for an \( n \)-qubit system.

## Context

This work extends the Fourier moment approach for computing response functions in nuclear physics (Roggero 2020, faster_spectral_roggero) by implementing it on noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) hardware, specifically IBM superconducting devices. It adapts echo verification (OBrien 2021) and operator decoherence renormalization (self-mitigation, Farrell 2023) to Hadamard tests with control reversal gates (QETU), building on prior nuclear lattice EFT models for triton response (neutrio_nucleus_roggero, two_point_roggero). The results enable practical computation of spectral properties from Fourier moments on current quantum hardware, with the CRG technique providing a general advantage for Hamiltonians with more than \( 2n \) Pauli terms.
