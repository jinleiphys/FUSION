# Statistical Theory of Neutron-Induced Nuclear Fission and of Heavy-Ion Fusion

> For both reactions we use an approach similar to that of compound-nucleus reaction theory. For neutron-induced fission, we describe the compound system generated by absorption of the neutron and the nuclear system near the scission point as two statistically independent systems governed by random-matrix theory. The systems are connected either by a barrier penetration factor or by a set of transition states above the barrier. Each system is coupled to a different set of channels. An analogous model is used for heavy-ion fusion. Assuming that (seen from the entrance channel) the system on the other side of the barrier is in the regime of strongly overlapping resonances, we obtain for fixed spin and parity closed-form analytical expressions for the total probability for fission and for fusion. Parts of these expressions can be calculated reliably within existing compound-nucleus reaction theory. The remaining parts are the probabilities for passage through or over the barrier. These may be determined theoretically from the liquid-drop model or experimentally from total fission or fusion cross sections.

# Statistical Theory of Neutron-Induced Nuclear Fission and of Heavy-Ion Fusion

## Key claim

The paper proposes that neutron-induced fission and heavy-ion fusion can be described by a statistical model where the compound nucleus (subsystem 1) and the nuclear system near the scission or merger point (subsystem 2) are treated as two statistically independent random-matrix systems governed by the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE), connected only by barrier penetration or a small set of transition states; the analytical results hold only under the condition that at least one of the two subsystems is in the regime of strongly overlapping resonances.

## Method

The author constructs a Hamiltonian consisting of two independent GOE random matrices H1 and H2 representing the two subsystems, coupled either by a rank-one tunneling matrix element (for energies below the barrier) or by a small number k of transition states (for energies near or above the barrier). Each subsystem is coupled to its own distinct set of open channels, and the average transition probability between channels on opposite sides of the barrier is computed using supersymmetry techniques, with asymptotic expansions in inverse powers of the sum of transmission coefficients when resonances strongly overlap. The total fission or fusion probability for fixed spin and parity is obtained by summing over all exit channels on the opposite side, which yields a factorized expression separating compound-nucleus formation from barrier transmission.

## Key numbers

- For first-order perturbation theory (tunneling far below barrier) to apply, the tunneling probability P_tun(E) must be less than 0.1 or so, because the correction term is of order 1/(1 + P_tun(E))^2
- The regime of isolated compound-nucleus resonances in subsystem 1 corresponds to neutron energies of up to 10 keV or so
- The condition for strongly overlapping resonances in subsystem 1 or 2 is formally defined by the inequality sum_a T_{1,a} >> 1 or sum_b T_{2,b} >> 1, respectively
- The average GOE level spacing is given by d = 2 pi lambda / N, where lambda defines the spectral range and N is the matrix dimension
- For tunneling below the barrier with strongly overlapping resonances in both subsystems, the total fission probability is P_{fission,1a}(E) = T_{1,a} * P_tun(E) / (1 + P_tun(E))^2

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier statistical transition-state theory developed in Weidenmuller (2022) and Weidenmuller (2024), which itself builds on a suggestion by Hagino (2021) and Bertsch (2021). The approach contrasts with the traditional time-dependent liquid-drop model of Bohr and Wheeler (1939) and the generator-coordinate method of Bertsch (2022), which require extensive numerical computation. The factorization result enables the total fission or fusion cross section to be expressed as the product of the standard compound-nucleus formation cross section (calculable from existing reaction theory) and a barrier transmission factor that can be determined either from the liquid-drop model or extracted from experimental data, providing a test for collective models of the fission barrier.
