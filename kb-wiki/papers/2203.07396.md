# Heavy Elements -- They came out of the blue

> How are the heavy elements formed? This has been a key open question in physics for decades. Recent direct detections of neutron star mergers and observations of evolved stars show signatures of chemical elements in the blue range of their spectra that bear witness of recent nuclear processes that led to heavy element production. The formation of heavy elements typically takes place through neutron-capture reactions creating radioactive isotopes, which following beta-decay turn into the stable isotopes we today can measure indirectly in the surfaces of cool, low-mass stars or meteoritic grains. The conditions (such as the neutron density or entropy) of these n-capture reactions remains to date poorly constrained, and only through a multidisciplinary effort can we, by combining and comparing observations, experiments, and theoretical predictions, improve on one of the top 10 most important open physics questions posed at the turn of the century. This emphasises the need for detailed observations of the near-UV to blue wavelength region. The shortage of spectrographs and hence spectra covering this range with high-resolution and high signal-to-noise has for decades played a limiting factor in our understanding of how heavy elements form in the nuclear reactions as well as how they behave in the stellar surfaces. With CUBES we can finally improve the observations, by covering the crucial blue range in more remote stars and also achieve a higher signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). This is much needed to detect and accurately deblend the absorption lines and in turn derive more accurate and precise abundances of the heavy elements.

## Key claim

The formation of heavy elements through neutron-capture reactions remains poorly constrained because the conditions (such as neutron density or entropy) of these reactions are not well understood, and only through a multidisciplinary effort combining observations, experiments, and theoretical predictions can progress be made on this open question. The shortage of high-resolution, high-signal-to-noise spectra covering the near-UV to blue wavelength region has been a limiting factor, and the CUBES instrument can improve observations by covering this crucial blue range in more remote stars with higher signal-to-noise ratio.

## Method

The paper reviews observational studies of neutron-capture processes (s-process, r-process, and i-process) in cool, low-mass stars, focusing on heavy element abundance measurements from stellar spectra in the blue wavelength range (below 4300 Angstrom). The author discusses how abundance patterns of elements such as Sr, Ba, Eu, Th, and Ag are derived from high-resolution spectroscopy and used to trace different nucleosynthesis channels, comparing observations from instruments like UVES/VLT with simulated performance of the proposed CUBES spectrograph.

## Key numbers

- Heavy element information is typically known only in hundreds or even tens of stars for some elements, while alpha-element abundances have been measured in thousands of stars
- The extreme scatter in barium abundances exceeds three orders of magnitude
- The Th line is located around 4000 Angstrom and is only spectroscopically detected in tens of stars
- The Pb line at 4058 Angstrom is notoriously hard to derive owing to weak and blended lines
- For silver (Ag), the strongest transitions are around 3300 Angstrom, and a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of 50 per pixel for giants and 100 per pixel for dwarfs is needed with UVES/VLT to derive Ag abundances
- For the metal-poor dwarf star HD121004 (Vmag=9), 1-hour integration with UVES resulted in SNR approximately 120 around the Ag lines
- With CUBES, 1-hour integration would yield SNR of 100 in a 16-magnitude star
- Abundance recovery of two blended lines could be perfectly recovered if core-to-core separation was at least 0.6 times the full width at half maximum (FWHM), while plus or minus 0.1 dex uncertainty could be recovered at 0.3 times FWHM
- Uncertain radial velocity shifts of approximately 1-2 km/s could lead to uncertainties as large as plus or minus 0.3-0.4 dex

## Context

This paper extends the observational work on heavy element abundances in metal-poor stars from studies such as Kobayashi (2006), Francois (2007), Cescutti (2008), Hansen (2012), Roederer (2013), and others, which showed large star-to-star abundance scatter in heavy elements like Ba compared to alpha-elements like Mg. The work enables future observations with the CUBES spectrograph to map neutron-capture elements in dwarf galaxies and more remote stars, addressing open questions about the nature of the s-process, r-process, and i-process formation channels, particularly in the context of upcoming facilities like FAIR and the ELT.
