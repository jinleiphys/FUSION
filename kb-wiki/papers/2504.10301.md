# Quantum three-body problem for nuclear physics

> A brief excursion into the three-body problem in quantum mechanics is presented for graduate students or researchers in nuclear physics. Starting from single-particle coordinates, the three-body Schrödinger equation is systematically transformed into a representation in Jacobi coordinates. Gradient, Laplacian, and kinetic energy operators are explicitly derived using the multivariable chain rule. Faddeev equations are reformulated in hyperspherical coordinates. In all transformations (from single-particle coordinates to Jacobi coordinates, rotation between Jacobi coordinates and from Jacobi coordinates to hyperspherical coordinates) the determinant of the Jacobian matrix is computed to ensure correct transformation of volume elements. The Faddeev equations in hyperspherical coordinates are projected onto a hyperspherical harmonics basis, leading to the coupled hyperradial equations that define the hyperspherical harmonics method.

## Key claim

This paper provides a pedagogical, fully explicit derivation of the quantum-mechanical three-body Schrödinger and Faddeev formalisms in Jacobi and hyperspherical coordinates, with particular emphasis on the rigorous computation of Jacobian determinants and the transformation of differential operators, steps that the author claims are often omitted or presented only schematically in existing reviews and pedagogical treatments.

## Method

Starting from the three-body Schrödinger equation in single-particle coordinates, the author systematically transforms the kinetic energy operator into mass-scaled Jacobi coordinates using the multivariable chain rule, explicitly verifying that all off-diagonal mixed-derivative terms cancel to yield a diagonalized kinetic energy operator. The Faddeev equations are then reformulated in hyperspherical coordinates, and the Jacobian determinant is computed for each coordinate transformation (single-particle to Jacobi, rotation between Jacobi sets, and Jacobi to hyperspherical) to ensure correct transformation of volume elements. Finally, the Faddeev equations in hyperspherical coordinates are projected onto a hyperspherical harmonics basis, leading to the coupled hyperradial equations that define the hyperspherical harmonics method.

## Key numbers

* The kinetic energy operator in mass-scaled Jacobi coordinates takes the diagonal form \(T = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla_{\vec{\eta}_i}^2 - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla_{\vec{\lambda}_i}^2 - \frac{\hbar^2}{2M}\nabla_{\vec{R}}^2\), where \(m\) is an arbitrary reference mass and \(M = m_i + m_j + m_k\) is the total mass.
* For a nuclear physics example with reference mass \(m = 939.6\ \text{MeV}/c^2\) (neutron mass), the factor \(\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} = 20.7\ \text{MeV}\cdot\text{fm}^2\), using \(\hbar c = 197.33\ \text{MeV}\cdot\text{fm}\).
* The Jacobian determinant for the transformation from single-particle coordinates \((\vec{r}_i, \vec{r}_j, \vec{r}_k)\) to Jacobi coordinates \((\vec{\eta}_i, \vec{\lambda}_i, \vec{R})\) is \(|\det(J_1)| = \left( \frac{m_i m_j m_k}{m^2 M} \right)^{-3/2}\).

## Context

This work extends pedagogical treatments of the three-body problem such as those by Thompson (2009) and Forrey (2010), who discussed the diagonalization of the kinetic energy operator and the separation of internal and center-of-mass motion. The paper enables graduate students and researchers to follow the complete derivation of the hyperspherical harmonics method from first principles, including the explicit computation of Jacobian determinants and operator transformations that are often glossed over in the literature.
