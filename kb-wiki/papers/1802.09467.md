# Different manifestations of S-matrix poles

> Making use of the analytical properties of the $S$-matrix and a theorem of Mittag-Leffler, model independent non-relativistic expressions for cross sections in single channel elastic scattering, scattering phase shifts and survival probabilities of resonances are derived. Provided certain conditions are satisfied by the poles, the residues can also be estimated analytically. Considerations of the low energy behaviour of the $S$-matrix and cross sections reveal additional conditions on the residues of the poles appearing in the Mittag-Leffler expansions. The exact expressions for the resonant cross section and phase shift are shown to reduce to the commonly used Breit-Wigner formula plus corrections. The latter is shown to approach the exact result with the example of a meson and a baryon resonance. Finally, a comparison of the exact expressions with some realistic examples is presented. The calculations of survival probabilities in particular reveal the reason behind the non-observability of non-exponential decay.

## Key claim

The paper derives model-independent non-relativistic expressions for cross sections, phase shifts, and survival probabilities from the analytic properties of the S-matrix using a Mittag-Leffler expansion, showing that the commonly used Breit-Wigner formula is an approximation that requires correction terms whose importance grows with the ratio of the resonance width to its energy; the exact expressions reduce to Breit-Wigner plus corrections only when the condition $|2\Im(\sum_{p\neq n} 1/k_{lp}) - (a + \sum_m 1/\zeta_{lm})| \ll 1/(2|k_{ln}|)$ is satisfied for the residue estimate.

## Method

The authors used the Mittag-Leffler theorem to expand the S-matrix as a meromorphic function in terms of its poles (bound, virtual, and resonant) in the complex momentum plane, imposing analyticity conditions including reality on the imaginary axis, unimodularity on the real axis, and the zero structure required by S-matrix symmetry properties. They derived expressions for the residues of the S-matrix poles analytically in terms of the pole positions and other poles, then obtained the total cross section via the optical theorem and the phase shift from the S-matrix expansion. The survival probability was calculated using the Fock-Krylov method from the energy derivative of the phase shift (density of states), with special attention to threshold behavior requiring additional conditions on the poles and residues.

## Key numbers

- For the $\sigma$ meson resonance: mass $\approx 500$ MeV, width $\Gamma_r \approx 400$ MeV, giving $\epsilon_r = M_\sigma - 2M_\pi = 220$ MeV and $x = \Gamma_r/(2\epsilon_r) \approx 0.9$; five correction terms were needed to match the exact cross section expression.
- For the $\Delta(1232)$ baryon resonance: $x = \Gamma_r/(2\epsilon_r) = 0.375$; only two correction terms sufficed to match the exact expression.
- The exact resonant cross section formula: $\sigma^{(l)} = -4\pi(2l+1) \frac{2\tan(\Arg(k_{lr}))}{\Gamma_{lr}/2} \frac{1}{1 + ((k^2 - \epsilon_{lr})/(\Gamma_{lr}/2))^2}$, where $k_{lr}^2 = \epsilon_{lr} - i\Gamma_{lr}/2$.
- The Breit-Wigner cross section plus corrections: $\sigma^{(l)} = \frac{4\pi(2l+1)}{\epsilon_{lr}} \frac{(\Gamma_{lr}/2)^2}{(k^2 - \epsilon_{lr})^2 + (\Gamma_{lr}/2)^2} [1 - \frac{1}{4}(\frac{\Gamma_{lr}}{2\epsilon_{lr}})^2 + \frac{1}{8}(\frac{\Gamma_{lr}}{2\epsilon_{lr}})^4 - \frac{5}{64}(\frac{\Gamma_{lr}}{2\epsilon_{lr}})^6 + \dots]$.
- The error bound for the N-term expansion: $\eta_N \leq \frac{1}{N+1} \frac{(2N)!}{(N!)^2} (\frac{x_{lr}}{2})^{2N}$.

## Context

This work extends the analytic S-matrix approach to resonance phenomenology, providing model-independent expressions that go beyond the standard Breit-Wigner approximation from Breit and Wigner (1936). The Mittag-Leffler expansion method connects to prior work on S-matrix pole expansions by Newton and others, and the threshold behavior analysis relates to the Beth-Uhlenbeck formula for the density of states. The results enable systematic corrections to Breit-Wigner fits in partial wave analyses, such as those by Workman et al. (2016), and provide analytic criteria for when single-pole approximations are valid. The survival probability calculations explain the non-observability of non-exponential decay in realistic resonances.
