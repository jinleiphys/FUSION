# Randomized measurement protocols for lattice gauge theories

> Randomized measurement protocols, including classical shadows, entanglement tomography, and randomized benchmarking are powerful techniques to estimate observables, perform state tomography, or extract the entanglement properties of quantum states. While unraveling the intricate structure of quantum states is generally difficult and resource-intensive, quantum systems in nature are often tightly constrained by symmetries. This can be leveraged by the symmetry-conscious randomized measurement schemes we propose, yielding clear advantages over symmetry-blind randomization such as reducing measurement costs, enabling symmetry-based error mitigation in experiments, allowing differentiated measurement of (lattice) gauge theory entanglement structure, and, potentially, the verification of topologically ordered states in existing and near-term experiments. Crucially, unlike symmetry-blind randomized measurement protocols, these latter tasks can be performed without relearning symmetries via full reconstruction of the density matrix.

## Key claim

The authors propose symmetry-conscious randomized measurement protocols that preserve the symmetry structure of quantum states, claiming that these protocols enable the simultaneous measurement of both distillable and symmetry components of entanglement in lattice gauge theories, a capability that symmetry-blind protocols lack, without requiring full state tomography to relearn the symmetries.

## Method

The authors construct symmetry-preserving random unitary circuits using local Hamiltonian terms (e.g., hopping and density-density interactions) as generators, ensuring that each unitary commutes with the relevant symmetry operators (e.g., particle number or Gauss law constraints). They then apply these circuits to measure k-purities via stochastic moments of measurement probabilities, and reconstruct entanglement entropies, symmetry-resolved Schmidt spectra, and classical shadows from the randomized measurement data. For the Z2 lattice gauge theory in 1+1 dimensions, they implement 3-qubit gauge-invariant unitaries as a near-term feasible strategy.

## Key numbers

- For particle number symmetry, the sampling complexity to approximate a 2-design scales as N_ℰ ∼ d_s^ξ with ξ = 2.1 ± 0.2, where d_s is the dimension of a symmetry sector.
- The relative sampling cost reduction compared to symmetry-blind schemes is exponential in system size n, scaling as r_s ∼ (d_s/d_ℋ)^ξ ≈ [2 (s/n)^(s/n) (1−s/n)^(1−s/n)]^(−nξ) / (2π n (s/n)(1−s/n))^(ξ/2) for sectors far from half filling.
- For n=10 qubits with N_ℰ=8192 random unitaries, the difference between circuit sampling and direct Haar random sampling becomes very small within ℓ ≈ 15 layers for all symmetry sectors.
- The required number of measurement shots N_M to measure second Rényi entropies with precision better than ε=0.05 scales approximately linearly with the sector dimension d_s, for fixed N_ℰ=1428 and ℓ=128 layers.
- For the Z2 lattice gauge theory ground state with N=10 sites (N_A=5), m·a=0.1, e/m=8, and ℓ=32 layers, k-purities up to k=4 are reconstructed as a function of N_ℰ up to 2^14.

## Context

This work extends randomized measurement protocols such as classical shadows (Huang 2020) and entanglement tomography (Elben 2018, Vermersch 2018, Brydges 2019) by incorporating symmetry constraints, building on prior symmetry-conscious approaches for particle number symmetry (Hao 2022, Zhao 2021, Wan 2022). The protocols enable experimental detection of topological order via entanglement spectrum gap measurements, as conjectured by Li and Haldane (2008), and are applicable to near-term quantum simulation experiments of lattice gauge theories (Martinez 2016, Klco 2018, Nguyen 2022).
