# Discovery of a new long-lived isomer in $^{114}$Rh via Penning-trap mass spectrometry

> We report on mass measurements of three long-lived states in $^{114}$Rh performed with the JYFLTRAP Penning-trap mass spectrometer: the ground state and two isomers with estimated half-lives of about one second. The used Phase-Imaging Ion-Cyclotron-Resonance technique allowed for the discovery of a so far unknown second long-lived isomer. All three states were produced directly in proton-induced fission on a uranium target, whereas only the isomeric states were populated in the $β$ decay of the $^{114}$Ru ground state with spin-parity $0^+$. We propose spin-parity assignments of $(6^-)$ for the ground state, and $(3^+)$ and $(0^-)$ for the isomers. They resolve the puzzle of anomalous fission yields of this isotope despite the existing literature assigning a low angular momentum to the ground state. The experimental evidence is further supported by a detailed analysis based on mean-field calculations with the BSkG3 model. As for many other nuclei in this mass region, considering triaxial shapes is decisive for the interpretation of low-lying states of this nucleus. The discovery of a new isomer in $^{114}$Rh and our theoretical work challenge the currently adopted spin-parity assignments in this and several other odd-odd neutron-rich Rh isotopes.

## Key claim

The paper reports the discovery of a previously unknown second long-lived isomer in \(^{114}\)Rh, resolving the puzzle of anomalous fission yields that had suggested this nucleus was the sole exception in the region where fission favors high-spin states. The authors propose that the ground state has spin-parity \((6^-)\) rather than the low-spin assignment in the literature, with the two isomers assigned as \((0^-)\) and \((3^+)\).

## Method

Mass measurements of three long-lived states in \(^{114}\)Rh were performed using the JYFLTRAP double Penning-trap mass spectrometer at the IGISOL facility, employing the Phase-Imaging Ion-Cyclotron-Resonance (PI-ICR) technique. The states were produced either directly in 25-MeV-proton-induced fission of a \(^{nat}\)U target or via the \(\beta\) decay of the \(^{114}\)Ru ground state (spin-parity \(0^+\)) using in-trap decay. Frequency ratios relative to \(^{85}\)Rb\(^+\) reference ions were measured to determine mass-excess values, and the relative production yields from the two production methods were used to infer spin ordering. Mean-field calculations with the BSkG3 energy density functional, which allows for triaxial deformation, were performed to interpret the structure of the low-lying states.

## Key numbers

- Mass-excess of the \(^{114}\)Rh ground state: \(-75662.5(19)\) keV, measured against \(^{85}\)Rb\(^+\) with \(t_{acc} = 644\) ms.
- Excitation energy of the first isomer (\(^{114}\)Rh\(^{m1}\), proposed \((0^-)\)): \(94.5(23)\) keV above the ground state.
- Excitation energy of the second isomer (\(^{114}\)Rh\(^{m2}\), proposed \((3^+)\)): \(117.2(21)\) keV above the ground state.
- Half-life of the \(^{114}\)Rh ground state: \(1.85(6)\) s, adopted from a weighted average of partial half-lives reported in Lhersonneau (2003) for high-spin states.
- Half-lives of both isomers estimated to be about one second, based on constant ion ratios for accumulation times between 220 and 620 ms.
- Frequency ratio between \(^{114}\)Rh\(^{m2}\) and \(^{114}\)Rh\(^{m1}\) (both \(2^+\) charge state): \(1.000000218(9)\), corresponding to an energy difference of \(22.4(9)\) keV.
- Ratio of registered ions in fission: \(^{114}\)Rh\(^{gs}\) : \(^{114}\)Rh\(^{m1}\) : \(^{114}\)Rh\(^{m2}\) = \(15 : 1 : 2.6\).
- Ratio of \(^{114}\)Rh\(^{m1}\) : \(^{114}\)Rh\(^{m2}\) from in-trap decay of \(^{114}\)Ru: about \(2 : 1\).

## Context

This work extends the study of neutron-rich odd-odd Rh isotopes by Hukkanen (2023), which had reported a puzzling anomaly in the fission yields of \(^{114}\)Rh. The discovery of a second isomer and the revised spin-parity assignments (ground state \((6^-)\), isomers \((0^-)\) and \((3^+)\)) resolve that anomaly and challenge the currently adopted assignments in this and several other odd-odd neutron-rich Rh isotopes. The experimental results are supported by mean-field calculations with the BSkG3 model, which, like earlier work on \(^{112}\)Rh by Hukkanen (2023) and on \(^{115}\)Ru by Hukkanen (2023a), demonstrates that considering triaxial shapes is decisive for interpreting low-lying states in this mass region. The findings enable future spectroscopic studies of the newly identified isomer and may require re-evaluation of spin-parity assignments in neighboring odd-odd Rh isotopes.
