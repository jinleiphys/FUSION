# Khon-Sham Density Functional Inspired Approach to Nuclear Binding

> A non-relativisitic nuclear density functional theory is constructed, not as usual, from an effective density dependent nucleon-nucleon force but directly introducing in the functional results from microscopic nuclear and neutron matter Bruckner G-matrix calculations at various densities. A purely phenomenological finite range part to account for surface properties is added. The striking result is that only four to five adjustable parameters, spin-orbit included, suffice to reproduce nuclear binding energies and radii with the same quality as obtained with the most performant effective forces, containing on the order of ten parameters. In this pilot work, for the pairing correlations, simply a density dependent zero range force is adopted from the literature. Possible future extensions of this approach are pointed out.

## Key claim

The authors construct a nuclear energy density functional whose bulk part is taken directly from microscopic Brueckner G-matrix calculations of infinite nuclear and neutron matter, adding only a phenomenological finite-range surface term and a spin-orbit term. With only four to five adjustable parameters, this functional reproduces binding energies and charge radii of spherical nuclei with quality comparable to Skyrme, Gogny, and relativistic mean-field forces that typically contain around ten parameters.

## Method

The functional is built as E = T₀ + E^{s.o.} + E_{int}^{∞} + E_{int}^{FR} + E_C, where T₀ is the uncorrelated kinetic energy, E^{s.o.} is a Skyrme-like spin-orbit term, E_C is the Coulomb energy (direct plus Slater exchange), and E_{int}^{∞} is the bulk correlation energy taken from microscopic infinite matter calculations using a converged hole-line expansion with a realistic bare force. The bulk part is implemented via local density approximation using polynomial fits to the symmetric and neutron matter equations of state, with the symmetric matter fit constrained to give E/A = -16 MeV at saturation density ρ₀ = 0.17 fm⁻³. The surface term E_{int}^{FR} uses a Gaussian finite-range form with three adjustable parameters (V_L, V_U, r₀), and the spin-orbit strength W₀ is a fourth parameter; these are fitted to binding energies of 11 spherical nuclei (BCP1 set) or to binding energies plus charge radii of 8 nuclei (BCP2 set). Pairing is added via a density-dependent zero-range force taken from the literature.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per particle at saturation: E/A = -16.00 MeV, at ρ₀ = 0.17 fm⁻³
- RMS deviation in binding energy over 161 even-even spherical nuclei: 1.775 MeV (BCP1), 2.057 MeV (BCP2), compared to 2.414 MeV (D1S), 3.582 MeV (NL3), 1.711 MeV (SLy4)
- RMS deviation in charge radius over 161 even-even spherical nuclei: 0.031 fm (BCP1), 0.028 fm (BCP2), compared to 0.020 fm (D1S), 0.020 fm (NL3), 0.024 fm (SLy4)
- Two-neutron separation energy S_{2n} for ²⁰⁸Pb: 14.77 MeV (BCP1), 14.79 MeV (BCP2), experimental 14.11 MeV
- Infinite nuclear matter properties: symmetry energy J = 33.55 MeV, symmetry energy slope L = 56.39 MeV, incompressibility K_∞ = 249 MeV, effective mass ratio m*/m = 1.00
- Average pairing gap in ¹¹⁶Sn: 1.5 MeV
- Binding energy per particle for drip-line nucleus ¹⁷⁶Sn: -6.57 MeV (BCP1), compared to -6.44 MeV (D1S) and -6.67 MeV (NL3)

## Context

This work extends the approach of using microscopic nuclear matter equations of state as input for nuclear functionals, similar in spirit to the SLy forces (Saclay-Lyon, 1998) which used neutron matter EOS at high density, but here the microscopic EOS is used for the entire bulk part rather than as a constraint. The method follows the Kohn-Sham DFT strategy used in condensed matter and quantum chemistry, where the bulk correlation energy is taken from accurate ab initio calculations (e.g., Quantum Monte Carlo for electrons), and contrasts with the traditional nuclear physics approach of constructing effective forces like Skyrme (Vautherin and Brink, 1972) or Gogny (Gogny, 1975) with globally fitted parameters. The results support the applicability of KS-DFT to self-bound systems, a topic under debate (Kreis et al., 2001; Engel, 2007; Dobaczewski, 2007; Giraud, 2007), and enable future extensions to deformed nuclei and improved pairing treatments.
