# From SPS to RHIC: Maurice and the CERN heavy-ion programme

> Maurice Jacob played a key role in bringing together different groups from the experimental and theoretical nuclear and particle physics communities to initiate an ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collision program at the CERN SPS, in order to search for the quark-gluon plasma. I review the history of this program from its beginnings to the time when the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) at Brookhaven National Laboratory (BNL) started operation. I close by providing a glimpse of the important discoveries made at RHIC and giving an outlook towards heavy-ion collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). During Maurice's life and not least through his perpetually strong influence, relativistic heavy-ion physics has matured and led to discoveries that radiate into many other fields of physics. Heavy-ion physicists owe a great deal to Maurice Jacob.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the CERN SPS heavy-ion program produced "compelling evidence" for a new state of matter with many characteristics of the quark-gluon plasma, but explicitly states this evidence is "indirect" and based on "circumstantial evidence" because the SPS beam energy was just barely enough to reach the QGP phase, causing the QGP to convert to hadrons almost immediately and camouflage its signatures.

## Method

The author reviews the history of the CERN SPS ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collision program from its inception in 1986 through its lead beam phase ending in 2002, describing the experimental campaigns with oxygen, sulfur, and lead beams at various energies. He then discusses the process by which the heavy-ion community, coordinated by Maurice Jacob and the author, produced a "White Paper" assessing the scientific achievements of the program, culminating in a CERN press release on February 10, 2000 announcing the discovery of a new state of matter. The paper closes with a comparison to later RHIC results, focusing on elliptic flow measurements and their interpretation via ideal hydrodynamics and quark coalescence.

## Key numbers

- SPS delivered 16O beams at 60 A GeV in 1986, followed by 32S beams at 60 and 200 A GeV from 1986 to 1993
- Lead beam program operated with 207Pb at 160 A GeV starting in 1994, finishing in 2002 with low energy runs at 40, 30, and 20 GeV/nucleon
- The White Paper reported initial energy density of about 3-4 GeV/fm^3 with mean particle momenta corresponding to T approximately 240 MeV
- Hadronization occurred at energy density of 1 GeV/fm^3 (T approximately 170 MeV)
- Freeze-out occurred at energy density of order 50 MeV/fm^3 (T = 100-120 MeV)
- At RHIC, more than 99% of all final hadrons have pT less than 1.5 GeV/c
- At SPS, elliptic flow in Pb+Pb collisions was about half of what ideal fluid dynamics predicts
- At RHIC, experimental data exhaust the ideal fluid prediction for elliptic flow
- The conjectured lower limit for shear viscosity to entropy ratio is eta/s = hbar/(4 pi kB)

## Context

This invited talk was presented at the "Maurice Jacob Memorial Meeting" at CERN on 11 September 2007, reviewing the history of the CERN heavy-ion program that Jacob helped initiate. The paper extends the narrative from the SPS program through the RHIC era, where discoveries such as "jet quenching" and "perfect QGP liquid" behavior were made, and provides an outlook toward heavy-ion collisions at the LHC. The author notes that the SPS White Paper (Heinz 2000) built on earlier theoretical work by Lee (1974) and Collins (1974) on QCD phase transitions, and that RHIC results confirmed and extended the SPS picture, enabling connections to condensed matter physics of ultracold atoms, string theory via AdS/CFT duality (Maldacena 1997), and viscous hydrodynamic calculations (Romatschke 2007, Song 2007).
