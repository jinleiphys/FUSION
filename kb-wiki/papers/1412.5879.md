# Impact of the energy loss spatial profile and shear viscosity to entropy density ratio for the Mach cone vs. head shock signals produced by a fast moving parton in a quark-gluon plasma

> We compute the energy and momentum deposited by a fast moving parton in a quark-gluon plasma using linear viscous hydrodynamics with an energy loss per unit length profile proportional to the path length and with different values of the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio. We show that when varying these parameters, the transverse modes still dominate over the longitudinal ones and thus energy and momentum is preferentially deposited along the head-shock, as in the case of a constant energy loss per unit length profile and the lowest value for the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio.

## Key claim
The paper shows that even when the energy loss per unit length is taken to be proportional to the path length (rather than constant) and the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio is varied above its conjectured lower bound, the transverse hydrodynamic modes still dominate over the longitudinal ones, so energy and momentum are preferentially deposited along the head-shock direction rather than producing a clear Mach cone signal.

## Method
The authors solve the linearized viscous hydrodynamic equations in Fourier space for a source term representing a fast moving parton, using an energy loss per unit length profile proportional to the traveled path length \(dE/dx = C z\). They compute the energy density \(\delta\epsilon\) and momentum density \(\mathbf{g}\) deposited into the medium, decomposing \(\mathbf{g}\) into longitudinal and transverse components. The resulting integrals are evaluated numerically for different values of the dimensionless parameter \(\kappa\) (related to the parton travel time in units of sound attenuation length) and for three values of the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi,\ 1.5/4\pi,\ 2/4\pi\).

## Key numbers
- For \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\) and a traveled path length \(L\) in the range 7 fm \(< L <\) 10 fm, the dimensionless parameter \(\kappa\) lies in the range \(75 < \kappa < 100\).
- For \(\eta/s = 1.5/4\pi\), the allowed \(\kappa\) values are 50, 60, and 70; for \(\eta/s = 2/4\pi\), the allowed \(\kappa\) values are 35, 45, and 55.
- The energy deposition for \(\eta/s = 1.5/4\pi\) is 30% smaller than for \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\); for \(\eta/s = 2/4\pi\) it is 60% smaller than for \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\).
- A restriction \(\kappa > 30\) is derived from requiring \(\delta\epsilon > 0\), which translates to \(\eta/s < 2.5 \times (1/4\pi)\) for a minimum travel time \(t_{\text{min}} = 7\) fm.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study Ayala et al. (2014) which assumed a constant energy loss per unit length and the lowest value \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\), finding that the head-shock signal dominates over the Mach cone. Here they test the robustness of that conclusion by adopting a linear path-length dependence for \(dE/dx\), motivated by studies of radiative and collisional energy loss mechanisms (e.g., Eloss1, lineal, Neufeld82, Neufeld103). The results contradict the idea that a single fast parton can produce a double-peak structure in azimuthal correlations via Mach cone emission, supporting instead the interpretation that such structures arise from two partons depositing energy, as argued in Ayala et al. (2014). The work also provides constraints on the allowed range of \(\eta/s\) for which the linear hydrodynamic description remains valid.
