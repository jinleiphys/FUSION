# Correlations and the relativistic structure of the nucleon self-energy

> A key point of Dirac Brueckner Hartree Fock calculations for nuclear matter is to decompose the self energy of the nucleons into Lorentz scalar and vector components. A new method is introduced for this decomposition. It is based on the dependence of the single-particle energy on the small component in the Dirac spinors used to calculate the matrix elements of the underlying NN interaction. The resulting Dirac components of the self-energy depend on the momentum of the nucleons. At densities around and below the nuclear matter saturation density this momentum dependence is dominated by the non-locality of the Brueckner G matrix. At higher densities these correlation effects are suppressed and the momentum dependence due to the Fock exchange terms is getting more important. Differences between symmetric nuclear matter and neutron matter are discussed. Various versions of the Bonn potential are considered.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a new method for decomposing the nucleon self-energy into Lorentz scalar and vector components in Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) calculations, which reveals that the momentum dependence of these components is significant (variation up to 20 percent) and arises from competing contributions of Fock exchange terms and correlation effects, contrary to the common assumption that these components are essentially constant.

## Method

The authors perform DBHF calculations for symmetric nuclear matter and neutron matter using the Bonn A and Bonn C one-boson-exchange potentials. They evaluate the single-particle potential U(k, m_D*) for various fixed values of the effective Dirac mass m_D*, then fit this dependence to a Dirac-Hartree expression with two effective coupling constants Γ_σ(k) and Γ_ω(k) at each nucleon momentum k. This allows extraction of momentum-dependent scalar and vector self-energy components Σ_s(k) and Σ_0(k) by solving a self-consistency condition for m_D*(k). The method is tested against direct Dirac-Hartree-Fock calculations and then applied to separate the contributions from Fock exchange terms and correlation effects from the Brueckner G-matrix.

## Key numbers

- At empirical saturation density (k_F = 1.35 fm⁻¹) for Bonn C potential, Σ_s ranges between -245 MeV and -270 MeV and Σ_0 between -165 MeV and -200 MeV for nucleon momenta from k=0 to the Fermi momentum, showing variation as large as 20 percent
- The DBHF single-particle potential U(k) ranges between -80 MeV and -60 MeV for nucleon momenta below k_F for both Bonn A and Bonn C potentials
- The Dirac-Hartree-Fock (Born approximation) contribution to U(k) for Bonn A ranges between -20 MeV and +10 MeV, while for Bonn C it ranges between +10 MeV and +40 MeV
- The d-state probability in the deuteron is 4.47 percent for Bonn A and 5.53 percent for Bonn C
- At densities around and below saturation density (k_F = 1.35 fm⁻¹), the effective mass m* from the simplified Dirac-Hartree scheme underestimates both m_D* and m_BG* by a considerable amount; the three values merge only at densities above twice the empirical saturation density

## Context

This work extends the DBHF approach of Brockmann and Machleidt (1990) and Engvik et al. (1994), who assumed constant (momentum-independent) scalar and vector self-energy components, and addresses the ambiguity in extracting the Dirac structure from G-matrix elements noted by Horowitz and Serot (1987), Sehn et al. (1995), and de Jong and Lenske (1996). The new method enables separation of Fock exchange, correlation, and relativistic contributions to the momentum dependence, which is essential for deriving effective density-dependent NN interactions for use in Dirac-Hartree-Fock calculations of finite nuclei, as pursued by Fritz et al. (1993) and Toki et al. (1991). The results show that correlation effects dominate the momentum dependence at low densities but are suppressed at high densities, and that the improved scheme yields weaker and less density-dependent effective coupling constants, promising more reliable results for asymmetric nuclear systems.
