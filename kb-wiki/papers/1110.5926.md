# Probing strongly interacting atomic gases with energetic atoms

> We investigate properties of an energetic atom propagating through strongly interacting atomic gases. The operator product expansion is used to systematically compute a quasiparticle energy and its scattering rate both in a spin-1/2 Fermi gas and in a spinless Bose gas. Reasonable agreement with recent quantum Monte Carlo simulations even at a relatively small momentum k/kF>1.5 indicates that our large-momentum expansions are valid in a wide range of momentum. We also study a differential scattering rate when a probe atom is shot into atomic gases. Because the number density and current density of the target atomic gas contribute to the forward scattering only, its contact density (measure of short-range pair correlation) gives the leading contribution to the backward scattering. Therefore, such an experiment can be used to measure the contact density and thus provides a new local probe of strongly interacting atomic gases.

## Key claim

The operator product expansion (OPE) can systematically compute quasiparticle energies and scattering rates for energetic atoms propagating through strongly interacting atomic gases, with the expansions valid for momenta as low as \(k/k_F \gtrsim 1.5\) rather than only in the asymptotic large-momentum limit, as demonstrated by reasonable agreement with quantum Monte Carlo simulations.

## Method

The author applies the operator product expansion to the single-particle Green's function of an energetic atom in the large-momentum limit, expanding the product of field operators into a series of local operators with Wilson coefficients computed from few-body scattering problems. The quasiparticle energy and scattering rate are extracted from the self-energy for both a spin-1/2 Fermi gas and a spinless Bose gas, with contributions organized by inverse powers of momentum \(|\mathbf{k}|\). The differential scattering rate for a probe atom shot into the gas is similarly computed, identifying contributions from number density, current density, and contact density of the target gas.

## Key numbers

- For a spin-1/2 Fermi gas, the quasiparticle energy expansion: \(E_\up(\mathbf{k}) = \left[1 + 32\pi \frac{n_\downarrow}{a_f |\mathbf{k}|^4} - 7.54 \frac{\mathcal{C}_f}{|\mathbf{k}|^4} + O(\mathbf{k}^{-6})\right] \frac{\mathbf{k}^2}{2m}\).
- For a spin-1/2 Fermi gas, the scattering rate expansion: \(\Gamma_\up(\mathbf{k}) = \left[32\pi\left(1 - \frac{4}{a_f^2 |\mathbf{k}|^2}\right) \frac{n_\downarrow}{|\mathbf{k}|^3} + 44.2 \frac{\mathcal{C}_f}{a_f |\mathbf{k}|^5} + O(\mathbf{k}^{-6})\right] \frac{\mathbf{k}^2}{2m}\).
- For a spinless Bose gas, the quasiparticle energy expansion: \(E_b(\mathbf{k}) = \left[1 + 64\pi \frac{n_b}{a_b |\mathbf{k}|^4} - 2\,\Re f\!\left(\frac{|\mathbf{k}|}{\kappa_*}\right) \frac{\mathcal{C}_b}{|\mathbf{k}|^4} + O(\mathbf{k}^{-5})\right] \frac{\mathbf{k}^2}{2m}\).
- For a spinless Bose gas, the scattering rate expansion: \(\Gamma_b(\mathbf{k}) = \left[64\pi \frac{n_b}{|\mathbf{k}|^3} + 4\,\Im f\!\left(\frac{|\mathbf{k}|}{\kappa_*}\right) \frac{\mathcal{C}_b}{|\mathbf{k}|^4} + O(\mathbf{k}^{-5})\right] \frac{\mathbf{k}^2}{2m}\), where \(\Im f\) ranges from \(-13.3\) to \(-11.2\).
- Comparison with quantum Monte Carlo data at \((a k_F)^{-1}=0\) and \(T/\epsilon_F=0.15\) uses contact density \(\mathcal{C}/k_F^4 = 0.1102(11)\) from Goulko (2010), giving self-energy correction \(-0.831 (k_F/|\mathbf{k}|)^2\).
- Comparison at \((a k_F)^{-1}=0.2\) and \(T/\epsilon_F=0.19\) uses contact density \(\mathcal{C}/k_F^4 = 0.156(2)\) from Gandolfi (2011) at zero temperature, giving self-energy correction \(-0.839 (k_F/|\mathbf{k}|)^2\).
- The quasiparticle width in the Fermi gas at unitarity: \(\Gamma(\mathbf{k})/\epsilon_F = \frac{16}{3\pi} \frac{k_F}{|\mathbf{k}|} + O(\mathbf{k}^{-4})\), with breakdown at \((|\mathbf{k}|/k_F)^2 \approx 2.1\).

## Context

This work extends the "hard probe" concept from nuclear and particle physics (e.g., jet quenching in quark-gluon plasmas at RHIC and LHC, neutron-deuteron scattering for three-nucleon forces) to ultracold atomic gases, building a bridge between these strongly interacting systems. The OPE approach generalizes earlier work on the contact by Tan (2005) and Braaten (2008, 2010) and provides a systematic framework for computing quasiparticle properties that complements quantum Monte Carlo simulations by Magierski (2011). The proposed scattering experiment enables local measurement of the contact density, which previously could only be measured integrated over the whole volume in experiments by Kuhnle (2010, 2011) and Navon (2010).
