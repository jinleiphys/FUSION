# On the Analysis of Intermediate-Energy Coulomb Excitation Experiments

> In a recent publication (Bertulani et al., PLB 650 (2007) 233 and arXiv:0704.0060v2) the validity of analysis methods used for intermediate-energy Coulomb excitation experiments was called into question. Applying a refined theory large corrections of results in the literature seemed needed. We show that this is not the case and that the large deviations observed are due to the use of the wrong experimental parameters. We furthermore show that an approximate expression derived by Bertulani et al. is in fact equivalent to the theory of Winther and Alder (NPA 319 (1979) 518), an analysis method often used in the literature.

# On the Analysis of Intermediate-Energy Coulomb Excitation Experiments

## Key claim
The large corrections (10-30%) to previously published Coulomb excitation cross sections claimed by Bertulani et al. (2007) are not due to deficiencies in the standard analysis methods, but rather result from that work using the wrong experimental parameters, specifically, interchanging center-of-mass and laboratory scattering angles and using incident beam energy instead of mid-target beam energy.

## Method
The authors recalculated Coulomb excitation cross sections for the same data sets (isotopes 11Be, 38S, 40S, 42S, 44Ar, 46Ar) using Eq. (5) from Bertulani et al. (2007), first with the parameters as listed in that work and then with corrected parameters (mid-target beam energy and center-of-mass scattering angle). They also performed an analytical comparison showing that the approximate expression derived by Bertulani et al. is algebraically identical to the semi-classical theory of Winther and Alder (1979) for both E1 and E2 excitations.

## Key numbers
- For 11Be (data set 3): experimental cross section 244 mb; Bertulani et al. reported 168.0 mb; calculation with correct parameters gave 240.7 mb (using mid-target energy 57.6 MeV/u and center-of-mass angle 4.02 degrees)
- For 38S (data set 7): experimental cross section 59 mb; Bertulani et al. reported 45.0 mb; calculation with correct parameters (mid-target energy 34.6 MeV/u, center-of-mass angle 4.92 degrees) gave 59.3 mb
- For 40S (data set 8): experimental cross section 94 mb; Bertulani et al. reported 70.0 mb; calculation with correct parameters gave 95.0 mb
- For 42S (data set 9): experimental cross section 128 mb; Bertulani et al. reported 94.3 mb; calculation with correct parameters gave 128.9 mb
- For 44Ar (data set 10): experimental cross section 81 mb; Bertulani et al. reported 58.3 mb; calculation with correct parameters gave 81.6 mb
- For 46Ar (data set 11): experimental cross section 53 mb; Bertulani et al. reported 38.2 mb; calculation with correct parameters gave 52.8 mb
- For 46Ar (data set 12): experimental cross section 68 mb; Bertulani et al. reported 47.4 mb; calculation with correct parameters (mid-target energy 73.2 MeV/u, center-of-mass angle 3.62 degrees) gave 71.8 mb
- For E2 excitations, the Winther and Alder theory deviates by less than 5% from the exact treatment for beam energies above 30 MeV/u (as shown in Bertulani et al. 2003)

## Context
This work responds to Bertulani et al. (2007), which claimed that standard analysis methods for intermediate-energy Coulomb excitation experiments required corrections of up to 30%. The authors show that the semi-classical theory of Winther and Alder (1979), used in prior work such as Scheit (1996), Gade (2003), and Fauerbach (1997), is analytically equivalent to the approximate expression derived by Bertulani et al. (2007). The paper enables continued confidence in the existing body of B(E2) and B(E1) values extracted from intermediate-energy Coulomb excitation, while acknowledging that refined relativistic treatments may become necessary for higher-precision measurements at next-generation radioactive beam facilities.
