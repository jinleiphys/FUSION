# Extended time-dependent generator coordinate method study of induced fission (II): total kinetic energy distribution

> A model is developed to calculate the total kinetic energy (TKE) distribution of fission fragments in the framework of the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM), extended to include dissipation effects in the description of induced fission dynamics. Starting from an expression for the dissipative term in the GCM Hamiltonian that determines the time evolution of a statistical collective wave function, derived in the first part of this work, the integrated flux of the probability current through the scission hyper-surface can be computed at different temperatures. The kinetic energy at scission for a specific pair of fragments at a given temperature is determined by the energy balance formula. By folding the kinetic energies of the fragments with the flux of the probability current, the TKE distribution is calculated. The method is illustrated with a calculation of the TKE distribution for induced fission of $^{228}$Th, in the 3D space of collective coordinates: axially-symmetric quadrupole and octupole deformations, and nuclear temperature.

# Extended time-dependent generator coordinate method study of induced fission (II): total kinetic energy distribution

## Key claim

The authors develop a method to calculate total kinetic energy (TKE) distributions of fission fragments within a dissipation-extended time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) framework, and show that including a dissipative term reduces the systematically too-large TKE values from standard TDGCM to quantitative agreement with data for photo-induced fission of ²²⁸Th. The method relies on an adjustable dissipation strength parameter γ = 0.01, and the results represent mean values from four calculations with different random matrices for the dissipation function.

## Method

The authors extend the TDGCM by including a dissipation term proportional to the collective momentum in the time-dependent Schrödinger-like equation for a statistical collective wave function, with the nuclear temperature treated as a third collective coordinate alongside axially-symmetric quadrupole (β₂) and octupole (β₃) deformations. Self-consistent finite-temperature relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations with the DD-PC1 functional and BCS pairing are performed in the (β₂, β₃, T) space to generate the collective potential (Helmholtz free energy), mass tensor, and density profiles. The TKE for each fragment pair at a given temperature is computed from an energy balance formula at scission, and the final TKE distribution is obtained by folding these kinetic energies with the time-integrated flux of the probability current through the scission hyper-surface.

## Key numbers

- The calculation is performed for induced fission of ²²⁸Th in a 3D collective space with β₂ from -1 to 7 (step 0.04), β₃ from 0 to 3.5 (step 0.05), and temperature T from 0 to 2.0 MeV (step 0.1 MeV).
- The time step is δt = 5 × 10⁻⁴ zs (1 zs = 10⁻²¹ s), and charge and mass distributions are calculated after 4 × 10⁴ time steps (20 zs).
- The dissipation strength parameter is γ = 0.01, the same value used in the companion paper for charge yields of ²²⁸Th.
- The average excitation energy of the initial collective state is E*_coll = 11 MeV, corresponding to the peak photon energy in the photo-induced fission data (photon energy interval 8–14 MeV).
- Close to 2 × 10⁴ pairs of fission fragments were calculated for the scission surface.
- The absorption rate for the imaginary potential is r = 20 × 10²² s⁻¹ with absorption band width w = 1.0.
- The scission surface is defined by the Gaussian neck operator threshold ⟨Q_N⟩ > 3, with neck width a_N = 1 fm.
- The root-mean-square value of the Gaussian distribution of dissipation random variables is γ √[log(ρ(T)) log(ρ(T′))], where ρ(T) is the intrinsic nuclear level density.

## Context

This work extends the dissipation-extended TDGCM introduced in the first part of the study (Zhao 2022) to calculate TKE distributions, addressing the known deficiency that standard TDGCM systematically overestimates TKEs because it lacks dissipation and converts all potential energy into collective kinetic energy (Ren 2022). The method is applied to ²²⁸Th and compared with photo-induced fission data from Schmidt (2000); the results show that including dissipation reduces TKEs to quantitative agreement with experiment, whereas the Coulomb repulsion formula (Goutte 2005, Tao 2017, Li 2022) cannot capture dissipation effects. The approach enables future tests on different fissioning systems and excitation energies, though the authors note the method is computationally intensive and that a fully microscopic dissipation term without adjustable parameters would require a dynamical GCM extension.
