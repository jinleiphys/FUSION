# Deep learning for flow observables in high energy heavy-ion collisions

> We demonstrate how deep convolutional neural networks can be trained to predict 2+1 D hydrodynamic simulation results for flow coefficients, mean-transverse-momentum and charged particle multiplicity from the initial energy density profile. We show that this method provides results that are accurate enough, so that one can use neural networks to reliably estimate multi-particle flow correlators. Additionally, we train networks that can take any model parameter as an additional input and demonstrate with a few examples that the accuracy remains good. The usage of neural networks can reduce the computation time needed in performing Bayesian analyses with multi-particle flow correlators by many orders of magnitude.

## Key claim

Deep convolutional neural networks can predict 2+1 D hydrodynamic simulation results for flow coefficients, mean transverse momentum, and charged particle multiplicity from initial energy density profiles with sufficient accuracy to reliably estimate multi-particle flow correlators, provided the networks are trained on adequate data. The method reduces computation time for Bayesian analyses by many orders of magnitude, but the accuracy depends on the number of training events and the specific observable and centrality region.

## Method

The authors trained DenseNet convolutional neural networks, one per observable, on 20,000 EKRT model initial energy density profiles and corresponding 2+1 D hydrodynamic simulation results for four collision systems (200 GeV Au+Au, 2.76 TeV Pb+Pb, 5.023 TeV Pb+Pb, 5.44 TeV Xe+Xe). They also trained extended networks (NN_p) that accept model parameters (such as viscosities) as additional inputs, using 160,000 training events distributed across 2,000 parameter points sampled from a Latin hypercube, with only 80 events per parameter point.

## Key numbers

- Training data consisted of 20,000 EKRT initial energy density profiles and corresponding final state observables, distributed evenly across four collision systems.
- Training one network for a single observable took approximately 1 hour.
- With a set of trained networks, generating 1 million events took approximately 20 hours.
- For NN_p networks, 160,000 training events were used across 4 collision systems and 2,000 parameter points, yielding only 80 events per parameter point (250 times more efficient than the single-parameter training).
- Validation used 90,000 independent events for single-parameter networks and 20,000 events for NN_p networks.
- The authors demonstrated generation of 10 million events with neural networks to reduce statistical errors.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior development of neural network emulators for heavy-ion collisions (Hirvonen 2023) by adding the capability to include model parameters as additional inputs, enabling parameter variation without retraining. The approach directly addresses the computational bottleneck in Bayesian analyses of QCD matter properties, which require approximately 10^6 to 10^9 simulated collision events and 10^5 to 10^8 CPU hours when using full hydrodynamic simulations. The method enables inclusion of multi-particle flow correlators (such as normalized symmetric cumulants NSC(m,n)) in Bayesian analyses, which were previously impractical due to computational cost. The results show that neural networks can reproduce hydrodynamic results accurately except in peripheral regions with extremely high viscosities where hydrodynamic numerical errors themselves become significant.
