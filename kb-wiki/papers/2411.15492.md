# Reduced Basis Method for Few-body Bound State Emulation

> Recent advances in both theoretical and computational methods have enabled large-scale, precision calculations of the properties of atomic nuclei. With the growing complexity of modern nuclear theory, however, also comes the need for novel methods to perform systematic studies and quantify the uncertainties of models when confronted with experimental data. This study presents an application of such an approach, the reduced basis method, to substantially lower computational costs by constructing a significantly smaller Hamiltonian subspace informed by previous solutions. Our method shows comparable efficiency and accuracy to other dimensionality reduction techniques on an artificial three-body bound system while providing a richer representation of physical information in its projection and training subspace. This methodological advancement can be applied in other contexts and has the potential to greatly improve our ability to systematically explore theoretical models and thus enhance our understanding of the fundamental properties of nuclear systems.

## Key claim

The reduced basis method (RBM) using free-particle wave functions in momentum space as a projection basis achieves comparable efficiency and accuracy to eigenvector continuation (EC) for three-body bound state emulation, while offering greater flexibility for handling systems near the dripline where discontinuities (Wigner cusps) challenge traditional Galerkin projection methods.

## Method

The authors constructed an artificial three-body system (frozen alpha core with two valence protons, representing 6Be) using the Gamow coupled-channel (GCC) method with a Woods-Saxon potential and Berggren basis. They trained the RBM by computing high-fidelity wave functions at several potential strength parameters c in the range [1.5, 3.5], then projected the Hamiltonian onto a subspace of free-particle wave functions with momenta randomly selected from the interval [0.5, 2.5] fm^-1. The reduced eigenvalue problem was solved by diagonalizing the projected Hamiltonian matrix of dimension 5-14, compared to the full GCC space of dimension 3900.

## Key numbers

- Over 90% of evaluated target points showed relative error epsilon(c) less than 10^-4 when using a training subspace dimension of only 5
- Emulator solution time approximately 10^-3 seconds per target point, while GCC required four orders of magnitude longer (approximately 10 seconds per point)
- The RBM subspace dimension stabilized at 14 regardless of increasing training wave functions to 30, 40, or 50, due to principal component analysis truncation at singular value precision 10^-12
- The optimal momentum filter interval Re(k) in [0.5, 1.0] fm^-1 corresponded to energies approximately between 5 and 20 MeV
- The full GCC model space had dimension 3900, with maximum hyperspherical quantum number K_max = 16 and max(ell_x, ell_y) <= 8

## Context

This work extends the eigenvector continuation (EC) method of Frame (2018) and the broader reduced basis method framework described by Quarteroni (2016) and Melendez (2022), applying it specifically to the Gamow coupled-channel approach developed by Wang (2017). The method addresses limitations of standard Galerkin projection near the dripline where Wigner cusps (Wigner, 1948) create discontinuities between bound and scattering spaces. This enables future emulation of resonance problems and open quantum systems, including two-proton decay and non-exponential decay phenomena studied by Wang (2019, 2021, 2023).
