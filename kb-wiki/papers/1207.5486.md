# Heavy Quarkonium in the Quark Gluon Plasma from Effective Field Theories and Potentials

> The measurements of heavy quarkonium suppression at RHIC and LHC urge theory to develop intuitive as well as quantitative methods for the description of $Q\bar{Q}$ melting in the quark-gluon plasma. Here I will present a brief sketch on the effective field theory strategies underlying the definition of the heavy quark static potential and report on two recent advances in the extraction and interpretation of such a potential. On the one side, progress has been made in obtaining its values from lattice QCD, which promises to make possible investigating its real and imaginary part non-perturbatively. On the other side, the existence of an imaginary part emphasizes the dynamical nature of the melting process and invites us to make a direct connection to the framework of open quantum systems.

# Heavy Quarkonium in the Quark Gluon Plasma from Effective Field Theories and Potentials

## Key claim
The static heavy quark-antiquark potential in the quark-gluon plasma can be rigorously defined from QCD via effective field theories (pNRQCD) or quantum mechanical path integrals, and its real and imaginary parts can be extracted non-perturbatively from lattice QCD using the Maximum Entropy Method. However, the extracted potential at the highest temperature studied (2.33 T_C) shows a puzzling steep rise in both real and imaginary parts that may be an artifact of the spectral reconstruction or fitting procedure rather than genuine physics.

## Method
The author derives the static Q-Qbar potential from QCD using two complementary non-relativistic frameworks: potential Non-Relativistic QCD (pNRQCD), which treats heavy quark pairs as singlet and octet fields, and a quantum mechanical path integral approach that expresses the heavy quark forward correlator in terms of particle trajectories. The potential is defined via the late-time limit of the real-time Wilson loop, V(r) = lim_{t->infinity} i ∂_t W_square(r,t) / W_square(r,t). To extract the potential non-perturbatively, the Euclidean Wilson loop from lattice QCD is analytically continued by expressing it as a Laplace transform of a positive-definite spectral function ρ_square(r,ω), which is reconstructed using Bayesian inference (Maximum Entropy Method). The complex potential is then read off from the position and width of the lowest-lying positive-frequency peak in the spectrum.

## Key numbers
- Bottomonium constituent quark mass: m_b ≈ 4.6 GeV
- QCD scale and deconfinement temperature: Λ_QCD ∼ 200 MeV, T_C ∼ 200 MeV
- Lattice simulation used anisotropic 20^3 × 12, 24, 36 lattices at β = 6.1 (a_x = 0.097 fm) with bare anisotropy ξ_b = 3.2108
- Temperatures studied: T = 2.33 T_C, 1.17 T_C, and 0.78 T_C
- HTL-resummed perturbative result for the static potential: V_HTL(r) = -(g^2/(3π))[m_D + e^{-m_D r}/r] - i(g^2 T/(3π)) φ(m_D r), with Debye mass m_D^2 = g^2 T^2 (N_c/3 + N_f/6)
- RHIC collision energy: √s_NN = 200 GeV (gold nuclei)
- LHC collision energy: √s_NN = 2.76 TeV (lead nuclei)

## Context
This work extends the program of defining heavy quarkonium melting in the quark-gluon plasma using effective field theories, building on the foundational pNRQCD framework of Brambilla (1999) and the quantum mechanical path integral approach of Barchielli (1986, 1988). It directly addresses the perturbative finding by Laine (2006) and Beraudo (2007) that the in-medium potential acquires an imaginary part from Landau damping, and provides a non-perturbative lattice QCD method to extract both real and imaginary parts. The results challenge earlier model-based approaches that identified the color singlet free energies F^1(r) or internal energies U^1(r) (Satz, 2005) with the potential, since those quantities are purely real and show screening behavior already at T_C, whereas the extracted real part appears to freeze near T_C. The imaginary part motivates interpreting heavy quarkonium as an open quantum system (Akamatsu, 2011; Young, 2010; Borghini, 2011), where thermal fluctuations cause spatial decoherence. Future work requires larger lattices and improved spectral reconstruction to resolve whether the steep rise at 2.33 T_C is physical or an artifact.
