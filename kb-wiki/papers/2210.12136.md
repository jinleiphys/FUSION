# Nuclear data activities for medium mass and heavy nuclei at Los Alamos

> Nuclear data is critical for many modern applications from stockpile stewardship to cutting edge scientific research. Central to these pursuits is a robust pipeline for nuclear modeling as well as data assimilation and dissemination. We summarize a small portion of the ongoing nuclear data efforts at Los Alamos for medium mass to heavy nuclei. We begin with an overview of the NEXUS framework and show how one of its modules can be used for model parameter optimization using Bayesian techniques. The mathematical framework affords the combination of different measured data in determining model parameters and their associated correlations. It also has the advantage of being able to quantify outliers in data. We exemplify the power of this procedure by highlighting the recently evaluated 239-Pu cross section. We further showcase the success of our tools and pipeline by covering the insight gained from incorporating the latest nuclear modeling and data in astrophysical simulations as part of the Fission In R-process Elements (FIRE) collaboration.

## Key claim

The NEXUS framework provides a data-agnostic pipeline for nuclear data evaluation and dissemination, demonstrated through a Bayesian optimization of the 239Pu total cross section that achieves results nearly identical to the ENDF/B-VIII.0 evaluation, with slight modifications below 30 keV where reported dataset uncertainties pull the fit downward.

## Method

The authors used the NEXUS Python package to access experimental cross section data from EXFOR and theoretical calculations from the Los Alamos statistical model code CoH. A Metropolis random-walk algorithm performed Bayesian hyperparameter optimization on six optical model parameters (potential depths, diffuseness, radii) from the Soukhovitskii potential, holding deformation fixed, to minimize chi-square goodness of fit against weighted experimental data for the 239Pu total cross section.

## Key numbers

- The six optical model parameters were standardized to unity; all optimal values were within 10% of the baseline Soukhovitskii parameters, with diffuseness parameters changing the most.
- The optimized total cross section fit shows "slight modifications" relative to ENDF/B-VIII.0 below 30 keV and a smaller effect between 1 and 2 MeV near the local minimum of sigma_tot.
- The FIRE collaboration calculated fission fragment mass yields for all fissioning systems using the Finite-Range Liquid-Drop Model (FRLDM), using millions of computational hours on the Los Alamos high-performance computing network.
- The single mass measurement of 123Pd adjusted the r-process abundance pattern by a "relatively significant amount" (shown in Figure 5, red curve relative to baseline).
- Kiss et al. measured 28 new beta-decay neutron emission probabilities and half-lives for lanthanide species; final abundances were most sensitive to the half-life of 168Sm followed by several Gd isotopes.

## Context

This work extends the nuclear data pipeline efforts at Los Alamos, building on the ENDF/B-VIII.0 evaluation (ENDF, 2018) and the Reference Input Parameter Library (Capote, 2009). The Bayesian optimization approach follows the hierarchical evaluation method of Hobson (2002) and uses the Soukhovitskii optical model potential (Soukhovitskii, 2005; Capote, 2005). The FIRE collaboration results extend prior r-process studies by Mumpower (2016) and Sprouse (2020), and the URSA framework aims to enable real-time incorporation of new nuclear data from upcoming facilities like FRIB (Horowitz, 2019) into astrophysical simulations, addressing the need identified by Surman (2018) for rapid data-to-application pipelines.
