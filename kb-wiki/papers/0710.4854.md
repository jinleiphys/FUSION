# Big bang nucleosynthesis as a probe of fundamental "constants"

> Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) is the earliest sensitive probe of the values of many fundamental particle physics parameters. We have found the leading linear dependences of primordial abundances on all relevant parameters of the standard BBN code, including binding energies and nuclear reaction rates. This enables us to set limits on possible variations of fundamental parameters. We find that 7Li is expected to be significantly more sensitive than other species to many fundamental parameters, a result which also holds for variations of coupling strengths in grand unified (GUT) models. Our work also indicates which areas of nuclear theory need further development if the values of ``constants'' are to be more accurately probed.

## Key claim

The paper systematically computes the leading linear dependences of primordial light-element abundances (D, ³He, ⁴He, ⁶Li, ⁷Li) on variations of fundamental particle physics parameters, finding that ⁷Li is significantly more sensitive than other species to many such parameters; this sensitivity means that a fractional variation of the fine-structure constant α by about −2.5×10⁻⁴ or +4×10⁻⁴ (depending on the grand-unified model) could bring the predicted ⁷Li abundance into agreement with observation, potentially ameliorating the "lithium problem."

## Method

The authors modified a standard BBN reaction-integration code to independently vary thirteen "nuclear" parameters (gravitational constant, neutron lifetime, fine-structure constant, electron mass, average nucleon mass, neutron-proton mass difference, and the binding energies of D, T, ³He, ⁴He, ⁶Li, ⁷Li, and ⁷Be) and extracted the response matrix C of logarithmic abundance derivatives. They then constructed a second response matrix F relating these nuclear parameters to six fundamental Standard Model parameters (G_N, α, Higgs vacuum expectation value, m_e, light quark mass difference δ_q, and average light quark mass m̂) using theoretical estimates for the dependence of binding energies on pion mass. The overall abundance variations with respect to fundamental parameters are given by the matrix product R = C F, and constraints on individual parameter variations are derived from observed D and ⁴He abundances.

## Key numbers

- Response matrix entry for ⁷Li abundance with respect to ⁴He binding energy: ∂ln Y(⁷Li)/∂ln B(⁴He) = −57
- Response matrix entry for ⁷Li abundance with respect to ⁷Be binding energy: ∂ln Y(⁷Li)/∂ln B(⁷Be) = 81
- Response matrix entry for ⁷Li abundance with respect to average nucleon mass: ∂ln Y(⁷Li)/∂ln m_N = −12
- Response matrix entry for ⁷Li abundance with respect to fine-structure constant: ∂ln Y(⁷Li)/∂ln α = −8.1
- Allowed individual variation of fine-structure constant from D and ⁴He bounds: −3.6% ≤ Δln α ≤ +1.9%
- Allowed individual variation of average light quark mass m̂: −1.3% ≤ Δln m̂ ≤ +1.7%
- In a GUT model with Higgs v.e.v. proportional to unification scale (γ=0): Δln Y(⁷Li) ≈ 1960 Δln α, so a fractional variation of α by about −2.5×10⁻⁴ brings abundances within 2σ bounds
- In a GUT model with γ=1.5: Δln Y(⁷Li) ≈ −1420 Δln α, so a fractional variation of α by about +4×10⁻⁴ brings theory and observation into agreement within 1σ bounds

## Context

This work extends the systematic approach of varying fundamental constants in BBN pioneered by the "response matrix" method of Muller, Sharma, and Wetterich (2004), and provides the first comprehensive tabulation of linear dependences for all relevant nuclear parameters. The results enable quantitative constraints on time-varying couplings from BBN, complementing observational searches for variations of α and the proton-electron mass ratio at redshifts 0.5–4 from absorption spectra (Murphy 2003, Reinhold 2006, Levshakov 2006). The finding that ⁷Li is exceptionally sensitive to many fundamental parameters suggests that the "lithium problem" (the factor 2–3 discrepancy between standard BBN predictions and observed primordial ⁷Li) might be resolved by allowing small variations of constants, while also identifying the need for improved nuclear theory of binding-energy dependence on quark masses as the main source of uncertainty.
