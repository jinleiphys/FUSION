# Photoexcitation of the $^{229}$Th nuclear clock transition using twisted light

> The $^{229}$Th nucleus has a unique transition at only 8 eV which could be used for a novel nuclear clock. We investigate theoretically the prospects of driving this transition with vortex light beams carrying orbital angular momentum. Numerical results are presented for two experimental configurations which are promising for the design of the planned nuclear clock: a trapped ion setup and a large ensemble of nuclei doped into CaF$_2$ crystals which are transparent in the frequency range of the nuclear transition. We discuss the feasibility of the vortex beam nuclear excitation and compare the excitation features with the case of plane wave beams.

## Key claim
The authors theoretically investigate whether vortex (twisted) light beams carrying orbital angular momentum can drive the 8 eV nuclear clock transition in $^{229}$Th, finding that while on-axis excitation can spatially separate the $\mathcal{E}2$ channel from the $\mathcal{M}1$ channel, the overall excitation probability for macroscopic samples averaged over all crystal orientations is similar to plane wave excitation.

## Method
The authors derive the twisted light-nucleus interaction matrix element for Bessel beams using semiclassical theory, expressing it in terms of nuclear reduced transition probabilities $B(\mathcal{M}1)$ and $B(\mathcal{E}2)$ and the driving field intensity. They then calculate nuclear excitation probabilities using the density matrix formalism for two configurations: a single trapped $^{229}$Th ion (including hyperfine and Zeeman splitting) and a macroscopic ensemble of $^{229}$Th nuclei doped into CaF$_2$ crystals (including nuclear quadrupole splitting from crystal fields). For the crystal case, they use an analytical solution of multilevel Bloch equations and average over the three possible quantization axes from cubic symmetry.

## Key numbers
- The $^{229}$Th nuclear clock transition energy is approximately 8 eV, corresponding to a magnetic dipole ($\mathcal{M}1$) and electric quadrupole ($\mathcal{E}2$) multipole mixture.
- For the $\mathcal{M}1$ channel, the reduced transition probability used is $B(\mathcal{M}1, I_g \rightarrow I_e) = 0.017$ W.u. (Weisskopf units), derived from experimental values in Kraemer (2023).
- For the $\mathcal{E}2$ channel, the reduced transition probability used is $B(\mathcal{E}2, I_g \rightarrow I_e) = 27.04$ W.u., taken from theoretical predictions in Minkov (2017).
- The radiative decay rate of the nuclear excited state is $\Gamma_\gamma = 3.14 \times 10^{-4}$ per second.
- The nuclear quadrupole splitting in CaF$_2$ crystals is on the order of 100 MHz.
- For a single $^{229}$Th$^{35+}$ ion on-axis with a vortex beam ($m_\gamma = 2$, $\Lambda = +1$) at intensity $\mathcal{I} = 15$ W/cm$^2$, the maximum $\mathcal{E}2$ excitation probability reaches approximately 27% after a few hours of interrogation time for a perfectly coherent laser ($\Gamma_\ell = 0$ Hz).
- The doped crystal approach involves $N \approx 10^{14}$ to $10^{16}$ nuclei.

## Context
This work extends theoretical studies of vortex beam interactions with nuclei, previously applied to giant dipole resonances (Lu et al.), multipolar nuclear transitions (Kazinski 2023), and delta baryon photoproduction (Afanasev 2022), to the specific case of the $^{229}$Th nuclear clock transition. The study follows the two experimental approaches for building a nuclear clock described by Peik (2015): a single-ion trap setup (Campbell 2012, Peik 2003) and a solid-state clock using Th-doped VUV-transparent crystals (Kazakov 2012, Dessovic 2014). The first direct VUV laser excitation of the $^{229}$Th isomer using plane wave beams was reported in PRL (2024). The results enable assessment of whether vortex beams offer advantages over plane wave excitation for nuclear clock development, showing that while on-axis geometry can separate multipole channels, the macroscopic crystal approach yields similar overall excitation to plane waves.
