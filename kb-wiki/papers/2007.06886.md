# Shear viscosity of classical Yang-Mills field

> We investigate the shear viscosity $η$ of the classical Yang-Mills (CYM) field on a lattice by using the Green-Kubo formula, where the shear viscosity is calculated from the time-correlation function of the energy-momentum tensor in equilibrium. Dependence of the shear viscosity $η(g,T)$ on the coupling $g$ and temperature $T$ is represented by a scaling function $f_η(g^2T)$ as $η(g,T)=Tf_η(g^2T)$ due to the scaling-invariant property of the CYM. The explicit functional form of $f_η(g^2T)$ is successfully determined from the calculated shear viscosity: It turns out that $η(g,T)$ of the CYM field is proportional to $1/g^{1.10-1.88}$ at weak coupling, which is a weaker dependence on $g$ than that in the leading-order perturbation theory but consistent with that of the "anomalous viscosity" $η\propto 1/g^{1.5}$ under the strong disordered field. The obtained shear viscosity is also found to be roughly consistent with that estimated through the analysis of the anisotropy of the pressure of the CYM dynamics in the expanding geometry with recourse to a hydrodynamic equation.

## Key claim

The shear viscosity of the classical Yang-Mills field is proportional to \(1/g^{1.10-1.88}\) at weak coupling, which is a weaker dependence on the coupling \(g\) than predicted by leading-order perturbation theory but consistent with the "anomalous viscosity" \(\eta \propto 1/g^{1.5}\) expected under a strong disordered field.

## Method

The authors performed real-time lattice simulations of SU(2) classical Yang-Mills theory on \(16^3\), \(24^3\), and \(32^3\) lattices with periodic boundary conditions, using the non-compact formalism in the temporal gauge. They prepared thermally equilibrated field configurations by evolving random initial conditions until equipartition of the electric field energy was achieved, then computed the shear viscosity via the Green-Kubo formula from the time-correlation function of the off-diagonal elements of the energy-momentum tensor. The coupling constant was varied over the range \(g = 0.15\) to \(20\) while keeping the average energy per degree of freedom at unity, corresponding to temperature \(T \approx 1.14-1.25\) in lattice units.

## Key numbers

* Shear viscosity normalized by temperature, \(\eta/T\), ranges from \(1.89 \pm 0.23\) at \(g^2T = 0.0256\) to \(0.105 \pm 0.006\) at \(g^2T = 499\) on the \(32^3\) lattice.
* The scaling function \(f_\eta(g^2T) = \eta/T\) was fitted to \(F(x) = \alpha x^{-\beta/2} + \gamma x^{-\delta/2}\) with parameters \(\alpha = 0.09 \pm 0.07\), \(\beta = 1.49 \pm 0.39\), \(\gamma = 0.33 \pm 0.06\), and \(\delta = 0.35 \pm 0.07\) over the range \(0.0256 < g^2T < 499\).
* The temperature extracted from the equipartition fit at \(g=1\) on the \(32^3\) lattice is \(T_{\rm fit} = 1.16\) with reduced chi-squared \(\chi^2_{\rm r} = 1.00\).
* The shear viscosity in physical units at lattice spacing \(a = (2\ {\rm GeV})^{-1}\) for physical temperatures \(T_{\rm phys} = 0.2, 0.4, 0.8\ {\rm GeV}\) is smaller than the value obtained by Epelbaum and Gelis (2013) at \(g=0.5\) by about 30-40%.

## Context

This work extends the Green-Kubo approach previously applied to classical scalar field theory (Homor 2014; Kyoto group 2015) to the classical Yang-Mills field, providing the first direct equilibrium calculation of its shear viscosity. The results are compared with the nonequilibrium estimate of Epelbaum and Gelis (2013), who deduced the shear viscosity from the pressure anisotropy of the CYM field in an expanding geometry using viscous hydrodynamics; the equilibrium values are found to be in the same numerical range but 30-40% smaller. The weak coupling dependence observed is consistent with the "anomalous viscosity" mechanism proposed by Asakawa, Bass, and Muller (2006) for systems with strong disordered fields, and is much weaker than the leading-order perturbative QCD result \(\eta \propto 1/[g^4 \ln(g^{-1})]\) (Arnold 2000). This work enables more reliable extraction of transport coefficients for the glasma phase in heavy-ion collisions and supports the interpretation that the near-minimal shear viscosity of the quark-gluon plasma may originate from classical Yang-Mills dynamics.
