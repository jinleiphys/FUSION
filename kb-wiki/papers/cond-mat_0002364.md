# Formation and control of electron molecules in artificial atoms: Impurity and magnetic-field effects

> Interelectron interactions and correlations in quantum dots can lead to spontaneous symmetry breaking of the self-consistent mean field resulting in formation of Wigner molecules. With the use of spin-and-space unrestricted Hartree-Fock (sS-UHF) calculations, such symmetry breaking is discussed for field-free conditions, as well as under the influence of an external magnetic field. Using as paradigms impurity-doped (as well as the limiting case of clean) two-electron quantum dots (which are analogs to helium-like atoms), it is shown that the interplay between the interelectron repulsion and the electronic zero-point kinetic energy leads, for a broad range of impurity parameters, to formation of a singlet ground-state electron molecule, reminiscent of the molecular picture of doubly-excited helium. Comparative analysis of the conditional probability distributions for the sS-UHF and the exact solutions for the ground state of two interacting electrons in a clean parabolic quantum dot reveals that both of them describe formation of an electron molecule with similar characteristics. The self-consistent field associated with the triplet excited state of the two-electron quantum dot (clean as well as impurity-doped) exhibits symmetry breaking of the Jahn-Teller type, similar to that underlying formation of nonspherical open-shell nuclei and metal clusters. Furthermore, impurity and/or magnetic-field effects can be used to achieve controlled manipulation of the formation and pinning of the discrete orientations of the Wigner molecules. Impurity effects are futher illustrated for the case of a quantum dot with more than two electrons.

## Key claim

The paper shows that spontaneous symmetry breaking of the self-consistent mean field, leading to formation of Wigner molecules, occurs in two-electron quantum dots for a broad range of impurity parameters, not only in the triplet excited state but also in the singlet ground state, a phenomenon absent in the ground state of natural helium atoms. The authors demonstrate that impurity and magnetic-field effects can control the orientation and formation of these electron molecules, potentially enabling switching between discrete states.

## Method

The authors performed spin-and-space unrestricted Hartree-Fock (sS-UHF) calculations for two-electron quantum dots with parabolic confinement, both clean and with Coulombic impurities, at zero and finite magnetic fields. They compared these results with exact solutions for the clean two-electron quantum dot, using conditional probability distributions (CPDs) to analyze electron localization. The study examined three representative cases: a Thomson quantum dot with two off-centered impurities, a Rutherford quantum dot with a central attractive impurity under magnetic fields, and an eight-electron quantum dot with a single impurity.

## Key numbers

- The Wigner molecule bond length in the singlet ground state of the Thomson quantum dot with two attractive impurities is 29 nm.
- The energy gain of the symmetry-broken sS-UHF singlet ground state relative to the symmetry-adapted solution is 1.62 meV for the Thomson quantum dot with pinning impurities, and 1.32 meV for the clean Thomson quantum dot.
- The critical magnetic field at which the triplet state becomes the ground state in the Rutherford quantum dot is B_c = 2.8 T.
- For the clean two-electron quantum dot at B=0, the exact ground-state energy is 19.80 meV, the sS-UHF ground-state energy is 21.45 meV, and the RHF ground-state energy is 22.77 meV, giving a correlation energy of 2.97 meV of which the sS-UHF captures 44%.
- The pair-correlation function for the exact ground state shows the electrons keep apart at a distance 2r_0 = 22.92 nm.
- The Zeeman splitting energy for the system is 0.026 meV/T.

## Context

This work extends the concept of spontaneous symmetry breaking and Wigner molecule formation from nuclear physics (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975) and metal clusters (Yannouleas and Landman, 1999; Ekardt, 1984) to two-dimensional quantum dots, unifying these phenomena across finite fermionic systems. The paper builds on prior studies of Wigner crystallization in quantum dots (Maksym, 1996; Matveev, 1995; Yannouleas and Landman, 1999) and connects to the molecular picture of doubly-excited helium atoms developed in the 1970s. The ability to control electron molecule orientation via impurities and magnetic fields opens possibilities for nanoscale logic gates as proposed by Lent (1993).
