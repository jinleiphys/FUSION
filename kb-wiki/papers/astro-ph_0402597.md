# Structure of Stars and Nuclei

> The properties of dense hadronic and quark matter and its relation to compact stars are discussed. In a bottom-up approach one starts with nuclear and hypernuclear physics at low density and extrapolates hadronic matter to large densities. The matching to the quark matter phase is performed in a top-down approach starting at asymptotically large densities. Implications for the mass-radius relation of compact stars and the existence of a new family of solutions will be outlined.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a strong first-order phase transition in dense hadronic matter, specifically to hyperon-dominated matter or to quark matter, can produce a new stable family of compact stars ("hypercompact stars" or "neutron star twins") with radii of only 7–8 km, but the existence and properties of this branch depend sensitively on the poorly constrained hyperon–hyperon interaction strength and the nature of the matching between hadronic and quark matter equations of state.

## Method

The author uses a relativistic mean-field model with baryons from the octet (nucleons, Λ, Σ, Ξ) and meson exchanges (σ, ω, ρ, plus hidden-strange σ* and φ for hyperon–hyperon interactions), fixing coupling constants to nuclear matter properties and hypernuclear data at saturation density n₀. For the quark matter phase, a top-down approach employs perturbative QCD up to order α_s² for massless quarks, matched to the low-density hadronic equation of state via the Gibbs construction for first-order phase transitions. The Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff (TOV) equations then yield mass–radius relations for compact stars.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear matter density: n₀ = 3 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³
- Hulse-Taylor pulsar mass: M = (1.4411 ± 0.00035) M_⊙
- Shortest known pulsar rotation period: 1.557 ms (PSR 1937+21)
- Temperature of isolated neutron star RX J1856: 49 eV (optical), 60 eV (X-ray, Chandra)
- Distance to RX J1856: D = 117 ± 12 parsec
- Apparent black-body radius of RX J1856: R_∞ = 4–8 km; with atmosphere corrections: R_∞ = 15 ± 3 km
- Λ hyperon potential depth at n₀: U_Λ = −30 MeV
- Ξ hyperon relativistic potential at n₀: about −28 MeV (older events), about −14 MeV (more recent estimate from final-state interactions)
- Binding energy of multi-hyperonic nuclei: −13 MeV/A to −21 MeV/A; purely hyperonic nuclei up to −8 MeV/A
- Onset density for hyperons in neutron star matter: about 2n₀ (Λ or Σ⁻)
- Maximum mass for a purely nucleonic star: about 2 M_⊙; for a hyperonic star: about 1.5 M_⊙ (depending on parameter set)
- Maximum mass of the new hypercompact star branch: about 1.5 M_⊙ with radius 7–8 km
- For strong hyperon–hyperon interaction (g_{σ*Λ}/g_{σN} = 1.0): maximum mass about 1.6 M_⊙, with masses below 0.2 M_⊙ down to radii of 7–8 km
- Critical endpoint in QCD phase diagram: μ_end = 400–700 MeV (from lattice calculations)
- Chiral phase transition temperature at zero chemical potential: T_c ≈ 170 MeV
- Matching density for strong first-order phase transition to quark matter: n_c = 2.5n₀ (one example shown)

## Context

This lecture-style paper extends the line of work on dense matter in compact stars that began with Oppenheimer and Volkoff (1939) and includes studies of hyperonic matter by Glendenning (1985), Balberg (1997), and Vidana (2000), as well as quark matter studies by Witten (1984) and Farhi (1984). The specific prediction of a third family of compact stars (neutron star twins) from a strong first-order phase transition builds on the criterion established by Gerlach (1968) and has been explored for transitions to strange quark matter by Schertler (2000) and to kaon condensates by Banik (2001). The work enables observational tests via mass–radius measurements of compact stars, potentially distinguishing between ordinary neutron stars, hypercompact stars, and hybrid stars with quark cores.
