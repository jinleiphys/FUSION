# Dimuon production from in-medium rho decays from QCD sum rules

> We compute the dimuon-excess invariant mass distribution at the rho-meson peak in the context of relativistic heavy-ion collisions at SPS energies. The temperature, T, and chemical potential, mu, dependent parameters describing the rho-meson --its width, mass and leptonic decay constant-- are determined from finite energy QCD sum rules. Results show that the rho-meson width increases whereas its mass and leptonic decay constant decrease near the (chemical potential-dependent) critical temperature T_c(mu) for chiral symmetry restoration/quark-gluon deconfinement. As a consequence, starting from T_c(mu), for a short lived cooling the main effect is a broadening of the dimuon distribution. However, when the evolution brings the system to a lower freeze-out temperature, with the rho-meson parameters approaching their vacuum values, the dimuon distribution shows a broad peak centered at the vacuum rho-meson mass. For even lower freeze-out temperatures the peak becomes less prominent since the thermal phase space factor suppresses the distribution for larger values of the invariant dimuon mass, given that the average temperature is smaller. The dimuon distribution exhibits a non-trivial behavior with mu. For small mu values the distribution broadens with increasing mu becoming a bit steeper at low invariant masses for larger values of mu. Our results are in very good agreement with data from the NA60 Collaboration.

## Key claim

The authors use finite-energy QCD sum rules (FESR) to compute the temperature- and chemical-potential-dependent rho-meson parameters (width, mass, leptonic decay constant) and show that the resulting dimuon invariant mass distribution around the rho peak agrees very well with NA60 data, provided the system evolves from a critical temperature down to a freeze-out temperature of about 0.1 GeV; the shape of the distribution is sensitive to the ratio of initial to freeze-out temperature and to the chemical potential, but the agreement holds for average chemical potentials up to 0.07 GeV.

## Method

The authors start from finite-energy QCD sum rules (FESR) for the light-quark vector current correlator, extending previous zero-chemical-potential results to finite baryon chemical potential by modifying the Fermi-Dirac distributions in the perturbative QCD sector and by using a parametrization of the crossover transition line T_c(mu) from a Schwinger-Dyson approach. They solve the FESR to obtain the temperature- and chemical-potential-dependent rho-meson mass, width, and leptonic decay constant, then use these parameters in a Breit-Wigner spectral function to compute the dimuon thermal rate from pion annihilation into rho mesons that decay into muon pairs, assuming longitudinal expansion with a cooling law and integrating over the fireball evolution from an initial proper time of 5 fm down to a freeze-out temperature.

## Key numbers

- Vacuum rho-meson mass: M_rho(0) = 0.776 GeV; vacuum width: Gamma_rho(0) = 0.145 GeV.
- Critical temperature at zero chemical potential: T_c(mu=0) = 0.197 GeV.
- For mu = 0.03 GeV, the parametrization of the crossover line is T_c(mu) = T_c(0) - 0.218 mu - 0.139 mu^2 (with mu in GeV).
- The rho-meson mass at finite T and mu = 0.03 GeV follows M_rho(T,mu) = M_rho(0)[1 - 0.5597 (T/T_c)^12.18].
- The rho-meson width at finite T and mu = 0.03 GeV follows Gamma_rho(T,mu) = Gamma_rho(0)[1 - 1.0717 (T/T_c)^2.763]^{-1}.
- The initial proper time is taken as tau_0 = 5 fm; the freeze-out temperature used for comparison with NA60 data is T_f = 0.1 GeV.
- The ratio T_0/T_f used in the calculation is about a factor of 2.

## Context

This work extends the finite-temperature QCD sum rule approach of the same group (Ayala et al., 2013, Ref. [nos]) to finite chemical potential, providing a complementary description to hadronic many-body and transport calculations (Rapp, 2000; Hees and Rapp, 2006; Cassing, 2007; Weil, 2012; Rapp, 2013) that have successfully described SPS data but face challenges at RHIC and LHC energies. The results enable future studies of the dimuon excess at collider energies where baryon density is smaller, and the authors note that additional processes (pion-rho scattering, other vector resonances, quark-gluon plasma contributions, transverse expansion) need to be included for a complete description away from the rho peak.
