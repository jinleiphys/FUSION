# Accelerating lattice quantum field theory calculations via interpolator optimization using NISQ-era quantum computing

> The only known way to study quantum field theories in non-perturbative regimes is using numerical calculations regulated on discrete space-time lattices. Such computations, however, are often faced with exponential signal-to-noise challenges that render key physics studies untenable even with next generation classical computing. Here, a method is presented by which the output of small-scale quantum computations on Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum era hardware can be used to accelerate larger-scale classical field theory calculations through the construction of optimized interpolating operators. The method is implemented and studied in the context of the 1+1-dimensional Schwinger model, a simple field theory which shares key features with the standard model of nuclear and particle physics.

# Accelerating lattice quantum field theory calculations via interpolator optimization using NISQ-era quantum computing

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that small-scale quantum computations on NISQ-era hardware can construct optimized interpolating operators for classical lattice quantum field theory calculations, but only if the quantum computation provides approximate wavefunctions with sufficient fidelity, the method works even with imperfect quantum computations, as shown by introducing a 0.05 radian error on variational parameters corresponding to approximately 15% error on expansion coefficients.

## Method

The authors use variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) calculations to obtain approximate wavefunctions of both the vacuum and the target excited state (the lightest vector meson) in the 1+1-dimensional Schwinger model. They then optimize a linear combination of interpolating operators to maximize the overlap between the vacuum and the target state, using the matrix elements computed from the VQE wavefunctions. The resulting quantum-improved interpolating operator is then used in a classical Euclidean Monte Carlo computation of correlation functions and effective masses, with the method tested on systems of 4 and 6 spatial sites with various electric flux truncations.

## Key numbers

- The Schwinger model parameters are set to J/ω = 5/3 and m/ω = 1/6, matching recent quantum device studies (Klco et al., 2018)
- The temporal lattice spacing is Δt = a/5 based on previous Monte Carlo studies (Schiller, 1983)
- Classical Monte Carlo computations use 10^7 configurations on a system with 4 spatial sites (8 staggered sites) and 40 temporal sites (80 staggered sites)
- Electric flux truncation parameters {Λ², Λ̃²} = {1, 8} are used for the exact diagonalization benchmark
- A 0.05 radian error on variational parameters produces approximately 15% error on the coefficients of expansion, matching fidelities from recent Schwinger model studies on quantum hardware (Kokail et al., 2018)
- The truncated Hilbert space with L=4 and Λ̃²=4 has dimensionality 10 in the zero-momentum odd-parity subspace, compared to 100 for the Λ̃²=12, L=6 system
- The quantum-improved operator optimization on a 4 spatial-site system with flux truncations Λ̃² = {2, 4, 8} all perform significantly better than the naive operator when applied to a 6 spatial-site system

## Context

This work extends the variational approach to interpolating operator construction pioneered by Michael (1985) and Luscher (1990), which has been highly successful in meson spectroscopy (Wilson, 2015) but scales quadratically with basis size and remains intractable for nuclear systems (Joo, 2019). The method builds on recent digital quantum simulations of the Schwinger model on trapped-ion devices (Martinez, 2016) and IBM superconducting hardware (Klco, 2018), and offers a hybrid approach that exploits the strengths of both quantum and classical computation. This enables near-term exploitation of NISQ-era quantum devices to accelerate classical lattice field theory calculations of nuclear physics processes, including those relevant to dark matter searches and neutrino physics.
