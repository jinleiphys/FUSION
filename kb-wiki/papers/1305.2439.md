# Statistical multifragmentation model with discretized energy and the generalized Fermi breakup. I. Formulation of the model

> The Generalized Fermi Breakup recently demonstrated to be formally equivalent to the Statistical Multifragmentation Model, if the contribution of excited states are included in the state densities of the former, is implemented. Since this treatment requires the application of the Statistical Multifragmentation Model repeatedly on the hot fragments until they have decayed to their ground states, it becomes extremely computational demanding, making its application to the systems of interest extremely difficult. Based on exact recursion formulae previously developed by Chase and Mekjian to calculate the statistical weights very efficiently, we present an implementation which is efficient enough to allow it to be applied to large systems at high excitation energies. Comparison with the GEMINI++ sequential decay code shows that the predictions obtained with our treatment are fairly similar to those obtained with this more traditional model.

## Key claim

The paper presents an implementation of the Generalized Fermi Breakup model (GFBM) that is computationally efficient enough to be applied to large nuclear systems at high excitation energies, by using exact recursion formulae previously developed by Chase and Mekjian (1995) and Pratt and Das Gupta (2000) to calculate statistical weights. The authors claim that the predictions of their treatment are "fairly similar" to those obtained with the GEMINI++ sequential decay code, with the agreement systematically improving as the excitation energy of the source increases.

## Method

The authors implement the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) using a discretized energy approach (SMM-DE) based on recursion relations that treat energy as a conserved discrete quantity alongside mass and charge. They then apply this SMM-DE repeatedly to hot primary fragments until they decay to their ground states, using a binning procedure for average excitation energies to speed up calculations. The primary fragment distributions are obtained from the SMM-DE, and the secondary decay is treated by iterating the same formalism on each fragment, summing contributions from successive decay steps to infinity using a geometric series.

## Key numbers

- The caloric curve comparison for $^{40}$Ca shows that the SMM and SMM-DE predict very similar results, with the temperature rising slower than a Fermi gas for $E^*/A \gtrsim 2$ MeV due to increased heat capacity.
- The energy bin parameter $\Delta_Q = 0.2$ MeV is used in the calculations, with tests showing that $\Delta_Q = 1.0$ MeV gives similar results.
- The average excitation energy bin $\Delta\overline{\epsilon}^* = 1$ MeV is used for the secondary decay stage, with tests showing that values of 1.0, 5.0, and 10.0 MeV produce weakly affected charge distributions.
- The primary total multiplicity $M$ for $^{40}$Ca is close to unity for $E^*/A \lesssim 2$ MeV, then rises steadily for $E^*/A \gtrsim 2$ MeV.
- The primary Intermediate Mass Fragment (IMF) multiplicity ($3 \le Z \le 15$) for $^{40}$Ca is zero for $E^*/A \lesssim 2$ MeV, then rises steadily; the final IMF multiplicity first rises and then falls off as excitation energy increases.

## Context

This work extends the Generalized Fermi Breakup model introduced in Ref. fbk2012, which was shown to be formally equivalent to the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) developed by Bondorf and collaborators (1995) and others. The implementation uses recursion formulae originally developed by Chase and Mekjian (1995) and further developed by Pratt and Das Gupta (2000), which have been successfully applied to nuclear breakup descriptions over the previous decade. The results are compared with the GEMINI++ sequential decay code, showing good agreement that improves at higher excitation energies, suggesting the treatment is at least as good as more traditional approaches. The work enables efficient application of the GFBM to large systems at high excitation energies, with applications to other systems and comparisons with experimental data stated to be in progress.
