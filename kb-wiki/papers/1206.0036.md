# Real-Space Imaginary-Time Propagators for Non-Local Nucleon-Nucleon Potentials

> Nuclear structure quantum Monte Carlo methods such as Green's function or auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo have used phenomenological local real-space potentials containing as few derivatives as possible, such as the Argonne-Urbana family of interactions, to make sampling simple and efficient. Basis set methods such as no-core shell model and coupled-cluster techniques typically use softer non-local potentials because of their more rapid convergence with basis set size. These non-local potentials are usually defined in momentum space and are often based on effective field theory. Comparisons of the results of the two types of methods can be difficult when different potentials are used. We show methods for evaluating the real-space imaginary-time propagators needed to perform quantum Monte Carlo calculations using such non-local potentials. We explore the universality of the large imaginary time propagators for different potentials and discuss how non-local potentials can be used in quantum Monte Carlo calculations.

## Key claim

The paper shows that real-space imaginary-time pair propagators for non-local nucleon-nucleon potentials can be computed via diagonalization of the momentum-space Hamiltonian, and that these propagators become universal at large imaginary times for phase-shift-equivalent potentials, but the central propagators for N³LO potentials exhibit small negative "ringing" regions that may complicate quantum Monte Carlo sampling.

## Method

The authors construct the momentum-space Hamiltonian for two nucleons in a spherical box with Dirichlet boundary conditions, discretizing the momentum spectrum at zeros of spherical Bessel functions. They diagonalize this Hamiltonian to obtain the imaginary-time propagator in momentum space, then transform to a 3D real-space basis using Clebsch-Gordan coefficients and spherical harmonics. The central part of the propagator is defined as the trace over all spins and isospins, and is evaluated for the Argonne v₁₈, N³LO, and N³LO(600) potentials.

## Key numbers

- For the propagator calculations, the authors use \(k_{\text{max}} = 40\ \text{fm}^{-1}\) with \(N_k \sim 80\) discrete momentum states, yielding truncation errors less than \(10^{-10}\).
- The typical Green's function Monte Carlo time step is \(\tau = (2000\ \text{MeV})^{-1}\).
- At \(\tau = (50\ \text{MeV})^{-1}\), energies of 175 MeV and above are suppressed by a factor of \(1/e^3 \approx 0.0498\).
- The quantum potentials show good agreement only up to \(k \approx 2\ \text{fm}^{-1}\), corresponding to a kinetic energy of 175 MeV.
- For N³LO with an initial separation of 1.0 fm, the fraction of walkers with negative weights is \(f \sim \mathcal{O}(10^{-2})\).
- The negative parts of the propagator are of order \(10^{-1}\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), while the peak is of order \(10^2\ \text{fm}^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends quantum Monte Carlo methods, which previously used local potentials like the Argonne-Urbana family (Wiringa 1995, Carlson 1983, Pieper 2001), to handle non-local potentials from chiral effective field theory such as the N³LO interaction of Entem and Machleidt (2003). The universal behavior at large imaginary times is analogous to the renormalization group results leading to the \(V_{\text{low }k}\) potential of Bogner (2001) and the similarity renormalization group results of Bogner (2006). The method enables direct comparison of quantum Monte Carlo results with basis-set methods like no-core shell model (Navratil 2000, 2009) and coupled-cluster techniques (Hagen 2010, Jansen 2011, Hagen 2008) using the same non-local potentials.
