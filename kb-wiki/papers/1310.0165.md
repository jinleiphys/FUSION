# Benchmarking the Bayesian reconstruction of the non-perturbative heavy $Q\bar{Q}$ potential

> The extraction of the finite temperature heavy quark potential from lattice QCD relies on a spectral analysis of the real-time Wilson loop. Through its position and shape, the lowest lying spectral peak encodes the real and imaginary part of this complex potential. We benchmark this extraction strategy using leading order hard-thermal loop (HTL) calculations. I.e. we analytically calculate the Wilson loop and determine the corresponding spectrum. By fitting its lowest lying peak we obtain the real- and imaginary part and confirm that the knowledge of the lowest peak alone is sufficient for obtaining the potential. We deploy a novel Bayesian approach to the reconstruction of spectral functions to HTL correlators in Euclidean time and observe how well the known spectral function and values for the real and imaginary part are reproduced. Finally we apply the method to quenched lattice QCD data and perform an improved estimate of both real and imaginary part of the non-perturbative heavy $Q\bar{Q}$ potential.

## Key claim

The paper benchmarks a procedure for extracting the complex heavy quark potential from lattice QCD by testing it against analytically known leading-order hard-thermal loop (HTL) calculations, and finds that a novel Bayesian reconstruction method can reproduce the real part of the potential with high precision, but the imaginary part requires extremely precise data (relative errors of \(10^{-5}\)) for a reliable determination.

## Method

The authors first analytically compute the Euclidean Wilson loop and Wilson line correlator in leading-order HTL perturbation theory, then numerically Fourier transform the real-time correlator to obtain the exact spectral function. They test the extraction procedure by (1) fitting the lowest-lying spectral peak with a skewed Lorentzian form to extract the potential, and (2) performing a Bayesian reconstruction of the spectral function from Euclidean correlator data using a new method with an unrestricted search space and a modified entropy functional, comparing results to the standard extended Maximum Entropy Method (MEM). Finally, they apply the full procedure to quenched lattice QCD data from Rothkopf (2011).

## Key numbers

* The HTL test was performed at temperature \(T = 2.33 \times 270\) MeV with a momentum cutoff \(\Lambda = 5\pi\) GeV.
* The Euclidean HTL correlators were calculated for 32 to 256 points to the tenth digit; relative Gaussian errors of \(10^{-2}\) to \(10^{-5}\) were added to test reconstruction accuracy.
* For the Wilson line correlator, the real part of the potential was reproduced with below one percent error already with data of \(10^{-2}\) relative precision.
* A reliable extraction of the imaginary part of the potential required data with relative errors of \(10^{-5}\).
* The quenched lattice QCD data used had \(N_\tau = 32\) and lattice spacing \(a = 0.39\) fm at \(T = 2.33 T_C\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier extraction strategy of Rothkopf (2011) and Burnier (2012) by identifying that the naive Lorentzian fit used previously overestimates the potential at large \(r\), and that the extended MEM cannot resolve the width of the lowest spectral peak, leading to inaccurate imaginary parts. The new Bayesian method corrects these issues and enables reliable extraction of the real part of the potential for phenomenology, while showing that the imaginary part remains challenging. The results are compatible with Debye screening, in contrast to the counterintuitive rising potential found in Rothkopf (2011).
