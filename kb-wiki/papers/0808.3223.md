# Isoscaling and the high Temperature limit

> This study shows that isoscaling, usually studied in nuclear reactions, is a phenomenon common to all cases of fair sampling. Exact expressions for the yield ratio $R_{21}$ and approximate expressions for the isoscaling parameters $α$ and $β$ are obtained and compared to experimental results. It is concluded that nuclear isoscaling is bound to contain a component due to sampling and, thus, a words of caution is issued to those interested in extracting information about the nuclear equation of state from isoscaling.

## Key claim

Isoscaling, the exponential dependence of isotope yield ratios on neutron and proton numbers, is not unique to nuclear reactions but arises from any fair sampling process; consequently, nuclear isoscaling always contains a combinatorial component that complicates extracting the symmetry energy of the nuclear equation of state, especially at high temperatures where the sampling contribution dominates.

## Method

The authors derived exact expressions for the isoscaling ratio \(R_{21}\) using hypergeometric probabilities for sampling without replacement from two urns with different isotopic compositions, and obtained approximate exponential forms using the binomial approximation. They compared the sampling-derived isoscaling parameter \(\alpha(S) = \ln(N_2 A_1 / N_1 A_2)\) to experimental \(\alpha(E)\) values from four reaction pairs measured at the Texas A&M Cyclotron Institute across multiple beam energies.

## Key numbers

- The sampling isoscaling parameter is given by \(\alpha(S) = \ln(N_2 A_1 / N_1 A_2)\), derived from the binomial approximation to the hypergeometric distribution.
- At all energies studied, \(\alpha(E) > \alpha(S)\), and for large energies \(\alpha(E) \rightarrow \alpha(S)\).
- The ratio \(\alpha(E)/\alpha(S)\) collapses the four scattered experimental curves into a single group when plotted as a function of beam energy.

## Context

This work extends earlier findings that isoscaling appears in classical molecular dynamics simulations before thermalization (Dorso 2006) and in percolation models (Davila 2006, Dorso 2007), by showing it arises from pure combinatorics in an urn model. It contradicts the assumption that isoscaling directly reflects the symmetry energy via equation \(\alpha = (4C_{sym}/T)[(Z_1/A_1)^2 - (Z_2/A_2)^2]\) (Botvina 2002, Tsang 2001). The results enable future experiments to subtract the sampling baseline to isolate nuclear equation-of-state effects, particularly at lower energies where nuclear binding modifies the fair sampling assumption.
