# New phase space calculations for beta-decay half-lives

> We revisit the computation of the phase space factors (PSF) involved in the positron decay and electron capture (EC) processes for a large number of nuclei of experimental interest. To obtain the electron/positron wave functions needed in computation, we develop a code for solving accurately the Dirac equation with a nuclear potential derived from a realistic proton density distribution in the nucleus. The finite nuclear size (FNS) and screening effects are included through recipes which differ from those used in previous calculations. Comparing our results with former calculations employing approximate methods but computed with the same Q-values, we find a close agreement for positron decays, while for the EC process there are relevant differences. For the EC process we also find that the screening effect has a notable influence on the computed PSF values specially for light nuclei. Further, we re-computed the same PSF values but using the most recent Q-values reported in literature. In several cases these new Q-values differ significantly from the older ones, which results in large differences in the PSF values as compared with previous results. These new PSF values proposed here, can contribute to a more reliable calculation of the beta decay rates, which are key quantities in the study of nuclei far from the stability line, as well as to better understanding of the stellar evolution.

## Key claim

The paper claims that new phase space factor (PSF) calculations for positron decay and electron capture, using a Dirac equation solver with a realistic proton density distribution and updated Q-values, yield close agreement with prior results for positron decays but show differences up to 35% for electron capture processes, with screening effects notably influencing PSF values for light nuclei.

## Method

The authors developed a code to solve the Dirac equation numerically using a Coulomb potential derived from a realistic proton density distribution (obtained from a Woods-Saxon potential), incorporating finite nuclear size (FNS) and screening effects via a Thomas-Fermi screening function computed by the Majorana method. For positron decay, continuum positron wave functions were computed and normalized to asymptotic Coulomb functions; for electron capture, bound-state electron wave functions for the 1s1/2 and 2s1/2 orbitals were obtained by matching inward and outward numerical solutions. The PSF integrals were evaluated using Gauss-Legendre quadrature with 32 points, and results were compared with those from the approximate methods of Gov71 and Mar70 using the same Q-values, then recomputed with updated Q-values from Aud12.

## Key numbers

- For positron decays of 16 nuclei of astrophysical interest, agreement between the new method (TW) and previous results from TH73 and Wil74 is generally under 1%, except for the two heaviest nuclei where differences reach approximately 3%.
- For electron capture transitions, differences between the new method and the recipe of Mar70 range from a few percent to about 35%.
- Screening effects in electron capture PSF values for light nuclei lead to a decrease of up to 10-15% compared to unscreened calculations; for heavier nuclei, screening differences are only up to 2-3%.
- For the nucleus 105Ag, the positron decay PSF difference between the new method and the Gov71 recipe is large (approximately a factor of 10), attributed to a very small W0 value of 0.325 MeV.

## Context

This work extends prior phase space factor calculations for beta decay and electron capture by Behrens (1969), Martin and Blichert-Toft (1970), and Gove and Martin (1971), which used approximate Fermi functions and corrections for finite nuclear size and screening. The authors also note similar recent improvements in double-beta decay PSF calculations by Kotila and Iachello (2012) and Mirea et al. (2015). The new PSF values, computed with updated Q-values from Audi et al. (2012), are intended to enable more reliable beta-decay rate calculations for nuclei far from stability and for stellar evolution modeling.
