# First Observation of Large Missing-Momentum (e,e'p) Cross-Section Scaling and the onset of Correlated-Pair Dominance in Nuclei

> We report the first measurement of $x_B$-scaling in $(e,e'p)$ cross-section ratios off nuclei relative to deuterium at large missing-momentum of $350 \leq p_{miss} \leq 600$ MeV/c. The observed scaling extends over a kinematic range of $0.7 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$, which is significantly wider than $1.4 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$ previously observed for inclusive $(e,e')$ cross-section ratios. The $x_B$-integrated cross-section ratios become constant (i.e., scale) beginning at $p_{miss}\approx k_F$, the nuclear Fermi momentum. Comparing with theoretical calculations we find good agreement with Generalized Contact Formalism calculations for high missing-momentum ($> 375$ MeV/c), suggesting the observed scaling results from interacting with nucleons in short-range correlated (SRC) pairs. For low missing-momenta, mean-field calculations show good agreement with the data for $p_{miss}\le k_F$, and suggest that contributions to the measured cross-section ratios from scattering off single, un-correlated, nucleons are non-negligible up to $p_{miss}\approx 350$ MeV/c. Therefore, SRCs become dominant in nuclei at $p_{miss}\approx 350$ MeV/c, well above the nuclear Fermi Surface of $k_F \approx 250$ MeV/c.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first observation of $x_B$-scaling in semi-inclusive $(e,e'p)$ cross-section ratios off nuclei relative to deuterium at large missing-momentum, extending the scaling region to $0.7 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$ (wider than the previously observed $1.4 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$ for inclusive $(e,e')$ reactions). However, the abstract's claim that short-range correlated (SRC) pairs become dominant at $p_{miss}\approx 350$ MeV/c is qualified by the finding that mean-field (single-nucleon) contributions are non-negligible up to that momentum, and the scaling onset at $p_{miss}\approx k_F$ (the nuclear Fermi momentum) reflects the combined effect of both SRC and residual mean-field contributions.

## Method

The experiment used a 5.01 GeV electron beam at Jefferson Lab incident on deuterium and solid targets (carbon, aluminum, iron, lead), with scattered electrons and knocked-out protons detected using the CLAS spectrometer. Events were selected with $Q^2 \geq 1.5$ (GeV/c)$^2$, missing momentum $350 \leq p_{miss} \leq 600$ MeV/c, and the proton emitted within $25^\circ$ of the momentum transfer, with additional cuts on missing mass ($0.8 \leq M_{miss} \leq 1.08$ GeV/c$^2$) and the angle between $\vec{p}_{miss}$ and $\vec{q}$ to suppress inelastic events. Cross-section ratios relative to deuterium were extracted after correcting for proton attenuation, electron radiation effects, and CLAS acceptance differences.

## Key numbers

- Missing-momentum range for scaling study: $350 \leq p_{miss} \leq 600$ MeV/c
- Bjorken scaling variable range for observed scaling: $0.7 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$ (compared to $1.4 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$ for inclusive $(e,e')$)
- $x_B$-integrated cross-section ratios become constant beginning at $p_{miss}\approx k_F$, where $k_F \approx 220-260$ MeV/c (carbon: $\approx 220$ MeV/c, aluminum: $\approx 235$ MeV/c, iron: $\approx 260$ MeV/c)
- Good agreement with Generalized Contact Formalism (GCF) calculations for high missing-momentum $> 375$ MeV/c
- Fitted SRC pair center-of-mass momentum distribution width $\sigma_{CM} = 160-210$ MeV/c at 68% confidence
- Fitted residual $A-2$ system excitation energy $E^* = 20-55$ MeV at 68% confidence
- SRC dominance onset at $p_{miss}\approx 350$ MeV/c, above the nuclear Fermi surface of $k_F \approx 250$ MeV/c

## Context

This work extends previous inclusive $(e,e')$ scaling measurements by Schmookler et al. (2019) and earlier studies by Frankfurt, Egiyan, Fomin, and others, which observed scaling only for $x_B \geq 1.5$ and attributed it to scattering off nucleons in deuteron-like proton-neutron SRC pairs. The new semi-inclusive $(e,e'p)$ measurements extend the scaling plateau down to $x_B=0.7$, providing a tool to study the transition from mean-field to SRC dominance. The results enable future detailed theoretical calculations to quantify the mean-field to SRC transition region and obtain effective high-resolution descriptions of heavy nuclei, building on the Generalized Contact Formalism framework developed by Weiss, Cruz-Torres, and others.
