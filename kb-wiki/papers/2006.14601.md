# A lower bound on the maximum mass if the secondary in GW190814 was once a rapidly spinning neutron star

> The recent detection of GW190814 featured the merger of a binary with a primary having a mass of $\sim 23\,M_{\odot}$ and a secondary with a mass of $\sim 2.6\,M_{\odot}$. While the primary was most likely a black hole, the secondary could be interpreted as either the lightest black hole or the most massive neutron star ever observed, but also as the indication of a novel class of exotic compact objects. We here argue that the secondary in GW190814 needs not be an ab-initio black hole nor an exotic object; rather, based on our current understanding of the nuclear-matter equation of state, it can be a rapidly rotating neutron star that collapsed to a rotating black hole at some point before merger. Using universal relations connecting the masses and spins of uniformly rotating neutron stars, we estimate the spin, $0.49 \lesssim χ\lesssim 0.68$, of the secondary -- a quantity not constrained so far by the detection -- and a novel strict lower bound on the maximum mass, $M_{\rm TOV} > 2.08^{+0.04}_{-0.04}\, \,M_{\odot}$, of nonrotating neutron stars, consistent with recent observations of a very massive pulsar. The new lower bound also remains valid even in the less likely scenario in which the secondary neutron star never collapsed to a black hole.

## Key claim

The secondary object in GW190814 (mass ~2.6 solar masses) could have been a rapidly rotating neutron star that collapsed to a black hole before merger, rather than an ab-initio black hole or exotic compact object; this scenario yields a lower bound on the maximum mass of nonrotating neutron stars that is independent of the equation of state and remains valid even if the neutron star never collapsed.

## Method

The authors apply universal relations from Breu (2016) connecting the critical mass of uniformly rotating neutron stars on the stability line to their dimensionless spin normalized by the Keplerian (mass-shedding) limit. Using the measured mass of the GW190814 secondary (2.59 solar masses, with lower limit 2.51 solar masses) and the known maximum mass enhancement factor for rotating stars (1.203), they derive a lower bound on the nonrotating maximum mass M_TOV. They then combine this with upper limits on M_TOV from GW170817 (Rezzolla 2017, Shibata 2019) and compactness-dependent fits for the Keplerian spin to estimate the secondary's spin range.

## Key numbers

- Lower bound on maximum mass of nonrotating neutron stars: M_TOV > 2.08^{+0.04}_{-0.04} solar masses (conservative, using m2 = 2.51 solar masses)
- Optimal lower bound: M_TOV > 2.15^{+0.04}_{-0.04} solar masses (using most likely m2 = 2.59 solar masses)
- Secondary dimensionless spin range: 0.49_{-0.05}^{+0.08} less than or similar to chi_2 less than or similar to 0.68_{-0.05}^{+0.11} (assuming M_TOV less than or similar to 2.3 solar masses)
- Maximum mass enhancement factor for uniformly rotating stars: xi_max = 1.203 plus or minus 0.022
- For the highest M_TOV estimate of 2.51 solar masses, the required spin to support a 2.6 solar mass neutron star is chi_2 > 0.31

## Context

This work extends the line of astrophysical constraints on the neutron-star maximum mass derived from GW170817 by Margalit (2017), Rezzolla (2017), Ruiz (2017), and Shibata (2019), and complements the pulsar mass measurements of Antoniadis (2013) for PSR J0348+0432 (2.01 solar masses) and Cromartie (2019) for PSR J0740+6620 (2.14 solar masses). The new lower bound provides an independent constraint on the nuclear-matter equation of state at high densities, and the spin estimate for the secondary enables future searches for electromagnetic counterparts such as fast radio bursts associated with rotational collapse to a black hole.
