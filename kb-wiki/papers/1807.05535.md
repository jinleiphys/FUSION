# High precision nuclear mass predictions towards a hundred kilo-electron-volt accuracy

> Mass is a fundamental property and an important fingerprint of atomic nucleus. It provides an extremely useful test ground for nuclear models and is crucial to understand energy generation in stars as well as the heavy elements synthesized in stellar explosions. Nuclear physicists have been attempting at developing a precise, reliable, and predictive nuclear model that is suitable for the whole nuclear chart, while this still remains a great challenge even in recent days. Here we employ the Fourier spectral analysis to examine the deviations of nuclear mass predictions to the experimental data and to present a novel way for accurate nuclear mass predictions. In this analysis, we map the mass deviations from the space of nucleon number to its conjugate space of frequency, and are able to pin down the main contributions to the model deficiencies. By using the radial basis function approach we can further isolate and quantify the sources. Taking a pedagogical mass model as an example, we examine explicitly the correlation between nuclear effective interactions and the distributions of mass deviations in the frequency domain. The method presented in this work, therefore, opens up a new way for improving the nuclear mass predictions towards a hundred kilo-electron-volt accuracy, which is argued to be the chaos-related limit for the nuclear mass predictions.

## Key claim

The authors claim that by combining Fourier spectral analysis with the radial basis function (RBF) approach, nuclear mass model predictions can be systematically improved toward a hundred kilo-electron-volt accuracy, which is argued to be the chaos-related limit for nuclear mass predictions. The hedge is that this accuracy is approached only for nuclei with mass number A greater than about 120, and that the method is demonstrated on a pedagogical mass model (BW2) as an example.

## Method

The authors performed a two-dimensional discrete Fourier transform of the mass differences between experimental data (from AME2016) and theoretical predictions for several global mass models (BW, BW2, FRDM, WS4, HFB-27, RMF). They then applied the radial basis function (RBF) approach to remove smooth (low-frequency) deviations, and a modified RBFoe approach that treats nuclei with different odd-even parities of (Z, N) separately to remove high-frequency (pairing-related) oscillations. The method was tested by removing individual terms (pairing, shell-correction, isospin-dependent, Coulomb, A-dependent) from the BW2 model and examining the resulting rms deviations after RBF and RBFoe corrections.

## Key numbers

* The rms deviation of the BW2 model is 1.666 MeV; after RBF correction (BW2+RBF) it drops to 0.346 MeV; after RBFoe correction (BW2+RBFoe) it drops further to 0.213 MeV.
* The rms deviation of the WS4 model is 0.302 MeV; after RBF correction (WS4+RBF) it drops to 0.199 MeV; after RBFoe correction (WS4+RBFoe) it drops to 0.138 MeV.
* For the WS4+RBFoe model, the rms deviation crosses the 100-keV accuracy threshold when mass number A is greater than about 120.
* The rms deviation of the BW model (Bethe-Weizsacker) is about 3 MeV; the WS4 model achieves the best accuracy at about 300 keV.
* The rms deviation of the RMF model is reduced to about 500 keV after RBF correction.

## Context

This work extends the long line of nuclear mass model development from the Bethe-Weizsacker model (1935) through the finite-range droplet model (FRDM) of Moller (1995), the Weizsacker-Skyrme (WS4) model of Wang (2014), and the Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB-27) model of Goriely (2013). The authors use the Fourier spectral analysis, previously applied to nuclear masses by Velazquez (2004), combined with the RBF interpolation method previously used in nuclear mass predictions by Wang (2011) and Niu (2013). The work enables a systematic strategy for improving mass models toward the chaos-related accuracy limit estimated by Molinari (2004) and Velazquez (2005), which is crucial for understanding the r-process and the origin of heavy elements.
