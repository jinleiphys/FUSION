# Distinguishing between Dirac and Majorana neutrinos with two-particle interferometry

> Two-particle interferometry, a second-order interference effect, is explored as another possible tool to distinguish between massive Dirac and Majorana neutrinos. A simple theoretical framework is discussed in the context of several gedanken experiments. The method can in principle provide both the mass scale and the quantum nature of the neutrino for a certain class of incoherent left-handed source currents.

## Key claim

Two-particle intensity interferometry can in principle determine both the mass scale and the Dirac or Majorana nature of the neutrino, but only for a specific class of sources and detector configurations (case D in the paper: two distinguishable sources emitting massive neutrinos, with helicity-averaged detection). The method fails to distinguish Dirac from Majorana neutrinos in the massless limit or when sources are identical.

## Method

The author derives the two-particle correlation function C₂ using the Koonin-Pratt equation, which relates C₂ to the spatial pair source distribution and the two-particle wave function. For free fermions from point sources, C₂ reduces to 1 − ξ cos(Δθ d/λ), where ξ encodes the quantum statistics and spin/helicity configuration. Four gedanken experiments (cases A-D) are analyzed by varying whether neutrinos are massless or massive, whether the two sources are identical or distinguishable, and whether detectors filter on same, opposite, or averaged helicities.

## Key numbers

- For massive neutrinos with m ≪ E from identical sources (case C), helicity-averaged C₂ gives ξ ∼ 1 − m²/E²
- For massive neutrinos from distinguishable sources (case D), Majorana neutrinos give ξ ∼ m²/E² under helicity averaging, while Dirac neutrinos give C₂ = 1
- For massless neutrinos from identical sources (case A), C₂ = 1 − cos(Δθ d/λ) with ξ = 1 for both Dirac and Majorana cases
- For massless neutrinos from distinguishable sources (case B), C₂ = 1 for both Dirac and Majorana cases
- Current experiments such as KamLAND and SNO achieve about one event per day, while the required time resolution for the proposed measurement is on the order of femto- to attoseconds

## Context

This work extends the Hanbury Brown-Twiss (HBT) intensity interferometry technique, originally developed for radio astronomy and later applied in quantum optics and high-energy particle femtoscopy, to the problem of neutrino quantum nature. The method is presented as an alternative to neutrinoless double beta decay (ββ(0ν)), which can only identify Majorana neutrinos and whose mass extraction is model-dependent. The paper notes that in the massless limit the Practical Dirac-Majorana Confusion Theorem (nuconf1) prevents distinction, and that the experimental requirements (extremely high neutrino flux and attosecond timing resolution) currently render the method prohibitive.
