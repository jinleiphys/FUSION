# Greedy Emulators for Nuclear Two-Body Scattering

> Applications of reduced basis method emulators are increasing in low-energy nuclear physics because they enable fast and accurate sampling of high-fidelity calculations, enabling robust uncertainty quantification. In this paper, we develop, implement, and test two model-driven emulators based on (Petrov-)Galerkin projection using the prototypical test case of two-body scattering with the Minnesota potential and a more realistic local chiral potential. The high-fidelity scattering equations are solved with the matrix Numerov method, a reformulation of the popular Numerov recurrence relation for solving special second-order differential equations as a linear system of coupled equations. A novel error estimator based on reduced-space residuals is applied to an active learning approach (a greedy algorithm) to choosing training samples ("snapshots") for the emulator and contrasted with a proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) approach. Both approaches allow for computationally efficient offline-online decompositions, but the greedy approach requires much fewer snapshot calculations. These developments set the groundwork for emulating scattering observables based on chiral nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon interactions and optical models, where computational speed-ups are necessary for Bayesian uncertainty quantification. Our emulators and error estimators are widely applicable to linear systems.

## Key claim

The paper develops and tests two projection-based reduced-order model emulators (Galerkin and least-squares Petrov-Galerkin) for nuclear two-body scattering that, when combined with a novel error estimator based on reduced-space residuals and a greedy algorithm for snapshot selection, require much fewer high-fidelity calculations than the proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) approach while maintaining comparable accuracy.

## Method

The authors solve the radial Schrödinger equation for neutron-proton scattering using the matrix Numerov method as the high-fidelity full-order model (FOM). They construct reduced-order model emulators via (Petrov-)Galerkin projection onto a reduced basis, using either a proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) with truncated singular value decomposition or a greedy algorithm that iteratively selects training snapshots based on a novel error estimator computed from reduced-space residuals. The emulators are tested on the Minnesota potential and a local chiral potential, with an efficient offline-online decomposition enabling computational speed-ups.

## Key numbers

* The greedy algorithm uses only 6 FOM samples (snapshots) to construct the emulator basis, compared to 100 FOM samples required by the POD approach for the same basis size of \(n_b = 6\).
* The emulator basis size is \(n_b = 6\) for both the greedy and POD approaches in the illustrative comparison.
* The FOM uses a grid with \(N+1\) points (where \(N\) is the number of grid intervals) with step size \(h\) for the radial coordinate \(r\) sampled at equidistant grid points \(r_n\).

## Context

This work extends previous applications of reduced basis method emulators in nuclear physics, particularly the POD-based approaches implemented in the ROSE software package by Giuliani (2022) and Odell (2023), and the greedy algorithm work by Sarkar and Lee (2021) and Bonilla (2022) for one-dimensional parameter spaces. The developments enable future Bayesian uncertainty quantification for scattering observables based on chiral nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon interactions and optical models, where computational speed-ups are necessary.
