# Higher moments of net-proton multiplicity distributions in a heavy-ion event pile-up scenario

> High-luminosity modern accelerators, like the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) at BNL and Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN, inherently have event pile-up scenarios which significantly contribute to physics events as a background. While state-of-the-art tracking algorithms and detector concepts take care of these event pile-up scenarios, several offline analytical techniques are used to remove such events from the physics analysis. It is still difficult to identify the remaining pile-up events in an event sample for physics analysis. Since the fraction of these events is significantly small, it may not be as serious of an issue for other analysis as it would be for an event-by-event analysis. Particularly, when the characteristics of the multiplicity distribution are observable, one needs to be very careful. In the present work, we demonstrate how a small fraction of residual pile-up events can change the moments and their ratios of an event-by-event net-proton multiplicity distribution, which are sensitive to the dynamical fluctuations due to the QCD critical point. For this study we assume that the individual event-by-event proton and antiproton multiplicity distributions follow Poisson, negative binomial or binomial distributions. We observe a significant effect in cumulants and their ratios of net-proton multiplicity distributions due to pile-up events, particularly at lower energies. It might be crucial to estimate the fraction of pile-up events in the data sample while interpreting the experimental observable for the critical point.

## Key claim

Even a very small fraction (0.01–2%) of residual pile-up events, which cannot be fully removed by experimental techniques, can significantly alter the higher cumulants and their ratios (especially C₄₂ = κσ²) of net-proton multiplicity distributions, particularly at lower collision energies where the asymmetry between proton and antiproton multiplicities is large. This effect may mimic or obscure the dynamical fluctuations expected from the QCD critical point.

## Method

The authors used a simple Monte Carlo approach to simulate event pile-up by generating independent proton and antiproton multiplicity distributions (Poisson, negative binomial, or binomial) using mean values from 0–5% central Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 7.7, 11.5, 19.6, 27, 39, 62.4, and 200 GeV measured by STAR. They artificially added a small fraction (0.01–2%) of extra protons and antiprotons from either central or minimum-bias events to the original central-event distributions, then constructed the net-proton (Np – Np̄) distribution on an event-by-event basis and calculated cumulants C₁ through C₄ and their ratios.

## Key numbers

- Collision rates at RHIC: 1.25 kHz at √sNN = 19.6 GeV and ~60 kHz at √sNN = 200 GeV, leading to in-time pile-up fractions of 1.36×10⁻⁴ and ~6.5×10⁻³ respectively.
- Out-of-time pile-up events from STAR TPC drift time (40 μs): ~0.05 events at √sNN = 19.6 GeV and ~2.4 events at √sNN = 200 GeV.
- Mean proton multiplicities for 0–5% central Au+Au: 18.918 ± 0.009 at √sNN = 7.7 GeV down to 5.664 ± 0.001 at √sNN = 200 GeV.
- Mean antiproton multiplicities for 0–5% central Au+Au: 0.165 ± 0.001 at √sNN = 7.7 GeV up to 4.116 ± 0.001 at √sNN = 200 GeV.
- C₄₂ values increase by an order of magnitude at √sNN = 7.7 GeV with only 0.1% pile-up events (Poisson case, central-central mixing).
- Pile-up fractions studied: 0.01%, 0.05%, 0.1%, 0.5%, 1%, and 2%.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of background effects on fluctuation observables used in the search for the QCD critical point, following the non-monotonic behavior of κσ² in net-proton distributions reported by STAR (Adamczyk 2014). It complements prior studies of systematic effects such as kinematic acceptance (Garg 2013), resonance decays (Mishra 2016), and charge conservation (Bzdak 2012). The results indicate that residual pile-up must be quantified before interpreting higher-moment data from current RHIC BES and future experiments like CBM at FAIR, which will have collision rates up to 10 MHz.
