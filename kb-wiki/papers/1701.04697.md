# On thermodynamic self-consistency of generic axiomatic-nonextensive statistics

> Generic axiomatic-nonextensive statistics characterized by two asymptotic properties, to each of them a scaling function is assigned, characterized by c and d for first and second scaling property, respectively, is formulated in a grand-canonical ensemble with finite volume in the thermodynamic limit. The thermodynamic properties of a relativistic ideal gas of hadron resonances are studied. It is found that this generic statistics satisfies the requirements of the equilibrium thermodynamics. Essential aspects of thermodynamic self-consistency are clarified. Analytical expressions are proposed for the statistical fits of various transverse momentum distributions measured in most-central collisions at different collision energies and colliding systems. Estimations for the freezeout temperature and chemical potential and both c and d are determined. The earlier are found compatible with the parameters deduced from Boltzmann-Gibbs (BG) statistics (extensive), while the latter refer to generic nonextensivity. The resulting equivalence class (c,d) is associated to stretched exponentials, where Lambert function reaches its asymptotic stability. In some measurements, the resulting nonextensive entropy is linearly composed of extensive entropies. Apart from power-scaling, the particle ratios and yields are excellent quantities to highlighting whether the particle production takes place, (non)extensively. Various particle ratios and yields measured by the STAR experiment in central collisions at 200, 62.4 and 7.7 GeV are fitted to this novel approach. We found that both c and d<1, i.e. referring to neither BG- nor Tsallis-type statistics, but to (c,d)-entropy, where Lambert functions exponentially raise. We conclude that the particle production at STAR energies is likely a nonextensive process but not necessarily BG or Tsallis type.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a generic axiomatic-nonextensive statistics, characterized by two scaling exponents (c,d), satisfies the requirements of equilibrium thermodynamics for a relativistic ideal gas of hadron resonances, and that particle production at STAR energies is a nonextensive process but not necessarily of the Boltzmann-Gibbs or Tsallis type, with both c and d found to be less than 1.

## Method

The authors formulated the generic axiomatic-nonextensive statistics in a grand-canonical ensemble with finite volume in the thermodynamic limit, using the Hanel-Thurner entropy characterized by scaling exponents c and d. They derived thermodynamic quantities (pressure, number density, energy density, entropy density) for both classical Boltzmann-Gibbs and quantum (Fermi-Dirac and Bose-Einstein) statistics, then verified the first and second laws of thermodynamics by checking the standard thermodynamic relations (e.g., ∂p/∂μ = n, ∂p/∂T = s, ∂ε/∂n = μ). They fitted analytical expressions for transverse momentum distributions and particle ratios measured by the STAR experiment in most-central collisions at 200, 62.4, and 7.7 GeV to extract freezeout temperature, baryon chemical potential, and the exponents c and d.

## Key numbers

- Freezeout temperature (T_ch) and baryon chemical potential (μ_b) extracted from fits to STAR data at 200, 62.4, and 7.7 GeV were found compatible with parameters deduced from Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics.
- Both exponents c and d were found to be less than 1, indicating neither Boltzmann-Gibbs (c=1, d=1) nor Tsallis-type (c=q, d=0) statistics, but rather (c,d)-entropy where Lambert functions exponentially raise.
- The equivalence class (c,d) was associated with stretched exponentials, where the Lambert function reaches its asymptotic stability.

## Context

This work extends the Hanel-Thurner entropy framework (Thurner and Hanel, 2010) to high-energy nuclear physics, building on earlier applications of nonextensive Tsallis statistics to particle production (Tawfik, 2010; Cleymans, 2011; Tripathy, 2016). It addresses the criticism by Bialas (2015) that Tsallis fits may be incomplete and that statistical cluster decay could explain power-law distributions. The paper enables a more general classification of nonextensivity in heavy-ion collisions beyond the Boltzmann-Gibbs and Tsallis paradigms, using the (c,d) exponents to characterize the degree and type of nonextensivity.
