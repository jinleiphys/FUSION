# Propagating Uncertainties from Nuclear Physics to Gamma-rays in Core Collapse Supernovae

> Nuclear yields are powerful probes of supernova explosions, their engines and their progenitors. In addition, as we improve our understanding of these explosions, we can use nuclear yields to probe dense matter and neutrino physics, both of which play a critical role in the central supernova engine. Especially with upcoming gamma-ray detectors that can directly detect radioactive isotopes out to increasing distances from gamma-rays emitted during their decay, nuclear yields have the potential to provide some of the most direct probes of supernova engines and stellar burning. To utilize these probes, we must understand and limit the uncertainties in their production. Uncertainties in the nuclear physics can be minimized by combining both laboratory experiments and nuclear theory. Similarly, astrophysical uncertainties caused by simplified explosion trajectories can be minimized by higher-fidelity stellar-evolution and supernova-engine models. This paper reviews the physics and astrophysics uncertainties in modeling nucleosynthetic yields, identifying the key areas of study needed to maximize the potential of supernova yields as probes of astrophysical transients and dense-matter physics.

# Propagating Uncertainties from Nuclear Physics to Gamma-rays in Core Collapse Supernovae

## Key claim
Nuclear yields from core-collapse supernovae can serve as direct probes of supernova engines and stellar burning, but only if both nuclear physics uncertainties (from cross sections and rates) and astrophysical uncertainties (from progenitor modeling, explosion trajectories, and multi-dimensional effects) are systematically identified and constrained through a coupled theory and experimental program.

## Method
The paper reviews astrophysical uncertainties in progenitor modeling (stellar mixing, rotation, mass loss, radiation transport) and explosive trajectories (shock deceleration, continuous engine outflows, auxiliary shocks, turbulence), alongside nuclear physics uncertainties in reaction rates. It presents focused case studies on O-C shell merger nucleosynthesis using post-processed models that implement 3D macro-physical mixing prescriptions into 1D stellar models, and parameterized trajectory models for innermost ejecta.

## Key numbers
- The gray (single-group) radiation transport approximation can cause up to 20% errors in energy transport in stellar models.
- For the O-C shell merger, the 3D mixing uncertainty for radioactive isotopes is 2.2 dex on average, with $^{44}$Ti featuring the largest uncertainty of 4.8 dex.
- 20% of massive star models feature an O-C shell merger.
- The Rayleigh-Taylor instability growth timescale is approximately 0.8 seconds for the Si/O shell interface and approximately 30 seconds for the CO/He shell interface.
- The Kelvin-Helmholtz instability growth timescale for Mach numbers of 0.1 or higher is greater than 5 seconds for a normalized wave number of a few tenths.

## Context
This review extends prior work on supernova nucleosynthesis by Fryer (2002), the NuGrid collaboration (2018), and multi-dimensional simulations of stellar convection by Jones (2017), Andrassy (2020), and Rizzuti (2024). It builds on studies of O-C shell mergers by Roberti (2024, 2025) and 3D mixing effects by Issa (2025). The paper identifies key areas for future work including improved stellar mixing models, multi-group radiation transport, and coupled engine-plus-nucleosynthesis simulations to enable gamma-ray observations from upcoming detectors to constrain supernova engine physics and dense matter properties.
