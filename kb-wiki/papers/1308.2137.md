# The cooling of the Cassiopeia A neutron star as a probe of the nuclear symmetry energy and nuclear pasta

> X-ray observations of the neutron star in the Cas A supernova remnant over the past decade suggest the star is undergoing a rapid drop in surface temperature of $\approx$ $2-5.5\%$. One explanation suggests the rapid cooling is triggered by the onset of neutron superfluidity in the core of the star, causing enhanced neutrino emission from neutron Cooper pair breaking and formation (PBF). Using consistent neutron star crust and core equations of state (EOSs) and compositions, we explore the sensitivity of this interpretation to the density dependence of the symmetry energy $L$ of the EOS used, and to the presence of enhanced neutrino cooling in the bubble phases of crustal "nuclear pasta". Modeling cooling over a conservative range of neutron star masses and envelope compositions, we find $L\lesssim70$ MeV, competitive with terrestrial experimental constraints and other astrophysical observations. For masses near the most likely mass of $M\gtrsim 1.65 M_{\odot}$, the constraint becomes more restrictive $35\lesssim L\lesssim 55$ MeV. The inclusion of the bubble cooling processes decreases the cooling rate of the star during the PBF phase, matching the observed rate only when $L\lesssim45$ MeV, taking all masses into consideration, corresponding to neutron star radii $\lesssim 11$km.

# The cooling of the Cassiopeia A neutron star as a probe of the nuclear symmetry energy and nuclear pasta

## Key claim

The rapid cooling of the Cassiopeia A neutron star, interpreted within the minimal cooling paradigm, constrains the slope of the nuclear symmetry energy L to L less than or approximately 70 MeV under conservative assumptions about neutron star mass and envelope composition; however, these constraints depend on whether enhanced neutrino cooling mechanisms in nuclear pasta phases are active, and on the assumption that the carbon atmosphere model used to infer the surface temperature is correct.

## Method

The authors constructed consistent neutron star crust and core equations of state using Skyrme nuclear matter models with systematically varied symmetry energy slope L from 30 to 80 MeV, while keeping symmetric nuclear matter properties fixed. They performed thermal evolution calculations using the NSCool code, incorporating neutron and proton pairing gaps, the Cooper pair breaking and formation (PBF) neutrino emission mechanism, and two bubble cooling processes (neutrino-antineutrino pair emission and direct Urca) in nuclear pasta phases. Cooling trajectories were computed for neutron star masses of 1.25, 1.4, 1.6, and 1.8 solar masses, envelope light element masses corresponding to eta values of -8 and -13, and with and without bubble cooling processes.

## Key numbers

- The observed surface temperature decline of the Cas A neutron star is approximately 2-5.5% over the past decade, with a best estimate of approximately 4% between 2000 and 2009
- The age of the Cas A neutron star in 2005 is taken as approximately 335 years, based on the estimated supernova date of approximately 1680 plus or minus 20 years
- For masses near the most likely value of M greater than or approximately 1.65 solar masses, the constraint becomes 35 less than or approximately L less than or approximately 55 MeV without bubble cooling processes
- With bubble cooling processes active and considering all masses, the constraint becomes L less than or approximately 45 MeV, corresponding to neutron star radii less than or approximately 11 km
- The maximum neutron 3P2 critical temperature values obtained range from 5.1-5.7 times 10^8 K with bubble cooling processes and 5.6-9 times 10^8 K without them
- The neutrino luminosity from bubble cooling processes is approximately 10^40 times T_9^6, where T_9 is the core temperature in units of 10^9 K, compared to the modified Urca luminosity of approximately 10^40 times T_9^8

## Context

This work extends the interpretation of Cas A neutron star cooling by Shternin (2011) and Page (2011), who used the APR equation of state and found that neutron superfluidity with critical temperatures of approximately 5-9 times 10^8 K could explain the rapid cooling. The present study systematically varies the symmetry energy slope L, which determines neutron star radii and crust properties, and for the first time examines the observable effect of enhanced neutrino cooling in nuclear pasta bubble phases. The constraints obtained on L (30-80 MeV from terrestrial experiments, 35-55 MeV from this work for likely masses) are competitive with constraints from nuclear neutron skin measurements, electric dipole polarizability, and heavy ion collision experiments as reviewed by Li (2008), Tsang (2012), and Lattimer (2013). The results enable future monitoring of the Cas A neutron star temperature to further constrain the symmetry energy and test whether bubble cooling processes are active in neutron star crusts.
