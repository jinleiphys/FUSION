# Neutron rich matter, neutron stars, and their crusts

> Neutron rich matter is at the heart of many fundamental questions in Nuclear Physics and Astrophysics. What are the high density phases of QCD? Where did the chemical elements come from? What is the structure of many compact and energetic objects in the heavens, and what determines their electromagnetic, neutrino, and gravitational-wave radiations? Moreover, neutron rich matter is being studied with an extraordinary variety of new tools such as Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) and the Laser Interferometer Gravitational Wave Observatory (LIGO). We describe the Lead Radius Experiment (PREX) that is using parity violation to measure the neutron radius in 208Pb. This has important implications for neutron stars and their crusts. Using large scale molecular dynamics, we model the formation of solids in both white dwarfs and neutron stars. We find neutron star crust to be the strongest material known, some 10 billion times stronger than steel. It can support mountains on rotating neutron stars large enough to generate detectable gravitational waves. Finally, we describe a new equation of state for supernova and neutron star merger simulations based on the Virial expansion at low densities, and large scale relativistic mean field calculations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that measuring the neutron radius of 208Pb via the PREX experiment can determine the equation of state of neutron matter at slightly subnuclear densities, which in turn correlates with neutron star radii and crust properties, but the author notes that the Steiner et al. (2010) results are potentially controversial because they depend on the model assumed for X-ray bursts.

## Method

The author describes the PREX experiment, which measures the parity-violating asymmetry A_pv for 1.05 GeV electrons elastically scattered from 208Pb at laboratory angles near five degrees, using a 0.5 mm thick enriched Pb foil target. Large-scale molecular dynamics simulations were performed to model the freezing of carbon/oxygen mixtures in white dwarfs (27648 ions) and the crystallization of rp-process ash in accreting neutron stars (up to 12 million ions), as well as to simulate crust breaking under strain. A new equation of state for supernova and neutron star merger simulations was constructed using a Virial expansion at low densities and relativistic mean field calculations at high densities, covering temperatures from 0 to 80 MeV, densities from 10^-8 to 1.6 fm^-3, and various proton fractions.

## Key numbers

- The parity-violating asymmetry A_pv for 208Pb is of order 0.5 ppm, and the experiment aims to measure it to 3%, allowing determination of the neutron root mean square radius to 1% (plus or minus 0.5 fm).
- The Steiner et al. (2010) analysis predicts the neutron minus proton root mean square radii in 208Pb to be R_n minus R_p equals 0.15 plus or minus 0.02 fm.
- The breaking stress of neutron star crust is 10 billion times larger than that for steel.
- The oxygen concentration (by number) x_O in the core of white dwarfs in NGC 6397 is constrained to x_O less than 0.57, corresponding to an effective astrophysical S factor at 300 keV of S_300 less than or equal to 170 keV barns.
- The new equation of state table generation required over 100,000 CPU hours.

## Context

This work extends the line of research connecting laboratory measurements of neutron-rich nuclei to astrophysical objects, specifically building on the relationship between the neutron radius of 208Pb and neutron star radii established by Horowitz and Piekarewicz (2001). The molecular dynamics simulations of white dwarf crystallization provide a new method to constrain the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction rate, complementing earlier astroseismology approaches by Winget et al. (2009). The finding that neutron star crust is the strongest known material enables future searches for continuous gravitational waves from rotating neutron stars at LIGO and VIRGO, as discussed by Bildsten (1998) and others.
