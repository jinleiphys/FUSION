# $T$-matrix Approach to Quark-Gluon Plasma

> A selfconsistent thermodynamic $T$-matrix approach is deployed to study the microscopic properties of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP), encompassing both light- and heavy-parton degrees of freedom in a unified framework. The starting point is a relativistic effective Hamiltonian with a universal color force. The input in-medium potential is quantitatively constrained by computing the heavy-quark (HQ) free energy from the static $T$-matrix and fitting it to pertinent lattice-QCD (lQCD) data. The corresponding $T$-matrix is then applied to compute the equation of state (EoS) of the QGP in a two-particle irreducible formalism including the full off-shell properties of the selfconsistent single-parton spectral functions and their two-body interaction. In particular, the skeleton diagram functional is fully resummed to account for emerging bound and scattering states as the critical temperature is approached from above. We find that the solution satisfying three sets of lQCD data (EoS, HQ free energy and quarkonium correlator ratios) is not unique. As limiting cases we discuss a weakly-coupled solution (WCS) which features color-potentials close to the free energy, relatively sharp quasiparticle spectral functions and weak hadronic resonances near $T_{\rm c}$, and a strongly-coupled solution (SCS) with a strong color potential (much larger than the free energy) resulting in broad non-quasiparticle parton spectral functions and strong hadronic resonance states which dominate the EoS when approaching $T_{\rm c}$.

## Key claim

The selfconsistent thermodynamic \(T\)-matrix approach, constrained by three sets of lattice-QCD data (equation of state, heavy-quark free energy, and quarkonium correlator ratios), does not yield a unique solution; instead, two limiting scenarios emerge: a weakly-coupled solution (WCS) with color potentials close to the free energy and sharp quasiparticle spectral functions, and a strongly-coupled solution (SCS) with a strong color potential much larger than the free energy and broad non-quasiparticle parton spectral functions.

## Method

The authors deploy a selfconsistent thermodynamic \(T\)-matrix approach based on a relativistic effective Hamiltonian with a universal color force (Cornell potential type including relativistic corrections) for light quarks, gluons, and heavy quarks. They compute the heavy-quark free energy from the static \(T\)-matrix and fit it to lattice-QCD data to constrain the in-medium potential, then apply the \(T\)-matrix to compute the equation of state in a two-particle irreducible Luttinger-Ward-Baym formalism with full off-shell single-parton spectral functions. The skeleton diagram functional is fully resummed using a matrix-logarithm technique to account for emerging bound and scattering states as the critical temperature is approached from above.

## Key numbers

* The static heavy-quark free energy is computed from the \(T\)-matrix and fitted to lattice-QCD data; the SCS features a color potential "much larger than the free energy" while the WCS has potentials "close to the free energy."
* The equation of state is computed using the Luttinger-Ward functional formalism with a matrix-log resummation technique, fitting to lattice-QCD results from Borsanyi (2010) and Bazavov (2014).
* Euclidean quarkonium correlator ratios are computed for charmonia and bottomonia and compared to lattice-QCD data from Datta (2003), Jakovac (2006), Aarts (2007), and Aarts (2011).
* The selfconsistent solution is constrained by three sets of lattice-QCD data simultaneously, but the solution is found to be "not unique."

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Liu 2015, Liu 2016) by setting up a unified Luttinger-Ward formalism to investigate light, heavy, and static degrees of freedom in the quark-gluon plasma, firmly rooted in thermal lattice-QCD information. It builds on earlier thermodynamic \(T\)-matrix approaches for heavy quarks (Cabrera 2006, van Hees 2007, Riek 2010, Huggins 2012) and the bound-state QGP concept (Shuryak 2004, Mannarelli 2005). The results enable future computation of transport coefficients such as the shear viscosity-to-entropy density ratio and heavy-flavor diffusion coefficients, as well as photon and dilepton production rates, directly from the real-time formalism.
