# Production mechanism of neutron-deficient actinide isotopes in complete fusion reactions and multinucleon transfer reactions

> Within the dinuclear system model, unknown neutron-deficient isotopes Np, Pu, Am, Cm, Bk, Cf, Es, Fm are investigated in $^{40}$Ca, $^{36,40}$Ar, $^{32}$S, $^{28}$Si,$^{24}$Mg induced fusion-evaporation reactions and multinucleon transfer reactions with radioactive beams $^{59}$Cu,$^{69}$As,$^{90}$Nb,$^{91}$Tc, $^{94}$Rh, $^{105,110}$Sn, $^{118}$Xe induced with $^{238}$U near Coulomb barrier energies. The production cross sections of compound nuclei in the fusion-evaporation reactions and fragments yields in the multinucleon transfer reactions are calculated within the model. A statistical approach is used to evaluate the survival probability of excited nuclei via the both reaction mechanisms. A dynamical deformation is implemented into the model in the dissipation process. It is found that charge particle channels (alpha and proton) dominate in the decay process of proton-rich nuclides and the fusion-evaporation reactions are favorable to produce the new neutron-deficient actinide isotopes. The total kinetic energies and angular spectra of primary fragments are strongly dependent on colliding orientations.

## Key claim

Within the dinuclear system model, fusion-evaporation reactions with light projectiles (e.g., $^{40}$Ca, $^{36}$Ar, $^{32}$S) are more favorable than multinucleon transfer reactions with radioactive beams for producing new unknown neutron-deficient actinide isotopes (Np through Fm), although the charged-particle channels (alpha and proton) dominate the decay of these proton-rich nuclides.

## Method

The authors used the dinuclear system (DNS) model, solving master equations for the time evolution of the distribution probability of fragment proton number, neutron number, excitation energy, and quadrupole deformation. A statistical approach based on Weisskopf evaporation theory evaluated the survival probability of excited primary fragments against evaporation of neutrons, protons, alpha particles, and gamma rays in competition with binary fission. Dynamical deformation was implemented in the dissipation process, and the total kinetic energies and angular spectra of primary fragments were calculated as functions of colliding orientations (tip-tip, side-side, no-deformation).

## Key numbers

* For the MNT reaction $^{105}$Sn + $^{238}$U at $E_{lab}$ = 6 MeV/nucleon, the half-width value of relaxation time is $50 \times 10^{-22}$ s; for the fusion-evaporation reaction $^{36}$Ar + $^{196}$Hg it is $300 \times 10^{-22}$ s.
* The relaxation time of radial motion $\tau_r$ = $5 \times 10^{-22}$ s; the relaxation time of angular momentum $\tau_J$ = $15 \times 10^{-22}$ s; the deformation relaxation time $\tau_{\beta}$ = $40 \times 10^{-22}$ s.
* In the MNT reaction $^{118}$Xe + $^{238}$U, the predicted production cross section for $^{236}$Cf is $9 \times 10^{-4}$ mb; for $^{240}$Es it is $3 \times 10^{-6}$ mb; for $^{240}$Fm it is $2 \times 10^{-6}$ mb.
* In the fusion-evaporation reaction $^{40}$Ca + $^{181}$Ta, the predicted cross section for $^{219}$Np via the 2n channel is $1 \times 10^{-6}$ mb; for $^{218}$Np via the 3n channel it is $3 \times 10^{-6}$ mb; for $^{217}$Np via the 4n channel it is $4 \times 10^{-6}$ mb.

## Context

This work extends the DNS model approach previously applied to superheavy nucleus formation (Adamian et al., 1997, 1998; Lanzhou Group, 2003, 2005, 2006) and to quasifission and fusion-fission dynamics (Feng et al., 2009, 2010). It builds on experimental efforts at IMP Lanzhou, FLNR Dubna, and LBNL Berkeley that synthesized new proton-rich actinide isotopes via fusion-evaporation reactions (Yang et al., 2015, 2018; Ma et al., 2015) and on MNT experiments such as $^{48}$Ca + $^{248}$Cm that identified $^{216}$U, $^{219}$Np, $^{223}$Am, $^{229}$Am, and $^{233}$Bk (Devaraja et al., 2015). The results enable predictions of production cross sections for unknown neutron-deficient actinide isotopes that could be targeted at radioactive beam facilities such as BRIF, BISOL, and RIKEN.
