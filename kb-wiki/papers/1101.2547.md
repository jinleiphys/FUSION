# Ab initio calculation of the Hoyle state

> The Hoyle state plays a crucial role in the hydrogen burning of stars heavier than our sun and in the production of carbon and other elements necessary for life. This excited state of the carbon-12 nucleus was postulated by Hoyle [1] as a necessary ingredient for the fusion of three alpha particles to produce carbon at stellar temperatures. Although the Hoyle state was seen experimentally more than a half century ago [2,3], nuclear theorists have not yet uncovered the nature of this state from first principles. In this letter we report the first ab initio calculation of the low-lying states of carbon-12 using supercomputer lattice simulations and a theoretical framework known as effective field theory. In addition to the ground state and excited spin-2 state, we find a resonance at -85(3) MeV with all of the properties of the Hoyle state and in agreement with the experimentally observed energy. These lattice simulations provide insight into the structure of this unique state and new clues as to the amount of fine-tuning needed in nature for the production of carbon in stars.

## Key claim

The authors report the first ab initio calculation of the Hoyle state of carbon-12, finding a resonance at -85(3) MeV that agrees with the experimentally observed energy, but only after including isospin-breaking and electromagnetic corrections at next-to-leading order; the result is a prediction without free parameters for beryllium-8 and carbon-12, though the helium-4 ground state energy was used to set one three-nucleon interaction coefficient.

## Method

The authors use chiral effective field theory up to next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO, O(Q^3)) combined with Monte Carlo lattice simulations on a periodic cubic lattice with spacing 1.97 fm and time step 1.32 fm. They compute ground state energies of helium-4, beryllium-8, and carbon-12, and extract low-lying excited states of carbon-12 using Euclidean time projection on 24 single-nucleon standing waves to build initial states of 6 protons and 6 neutrons. Higher-order corrections are computed using perturbation theory, and the experimentally observed helium-4 energy is used to set one three-nucleon interaction coefficient at NNLO.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy of carbon-12 at NNLO: -91(3) MeV (experiment: -92.16 MeV)
- Hoyle state (0_2^+) energy at NNLO: -85(3) MeV (experiment: -84.51 MeV)
- Spin-2 state (2_1^+) energy at NNLO: -88(3) MeV for J_z=0 projection and -90(4) MeV for J_z=2 projection (experiment: -87.72 MeV)
- Beryllium-8 ground state energy at NNLO: -58(2) MeV (experiment: -56.50 MeV)
- Beryllium-8-alpha threshold from lattice at NNLO: -86(2) MeV (experiment: -84.80 MeV)
- Charge radius of ground state at leading order: 2.04(2) fm (experiment: 2.47(2) fm)
- Charge radius of Hoyle state at leading order: 2.4(1) fm

## Context

This work extends prior ab initio approaches such as the no-core shell model (Navratil, 2007; Roth, 2009), constrained-path Green's function Monte Carlo (Pieper, 2001; Wiringa, 2002), auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (Gandolfi, 2009), and coupled cluster methods (Hagen, 2010), none of which had reproduced the Hoyle state from first principles. It also goes beyond empirical alpha-cluster models (Tohsaki, 2001; Funaki, 2003; Feldmeier, 2011) by providing a parameter-free calculation. The results enable future studies of the fine-tuning of fundamental parameters (such as up and down quark masses) that set the Hoyle state energy near the beryllium-8-alpha threshold, and open the door to ab initio investigations of other nuclear reactions relevant to stellar nucleosynthesis.
