# Introducing the Random Phase Approximation Theory

> Random Phase Approximation (RPA) is the theory most commonly used to describe the excitations of many-body systems. In this article, the secular equations of the theory are obtained by using three different approaches: the equation of motion method, the Green's function perturbation theory and the time-dependent Hartree--Fock theory. Each approach emphasizes specific aspects of the theory overlooked by the other methods. Extensions of the RPA secular equations to treat the continuum part of the excitation spectrum and also the pairing between the particles composing the system are presented. Theoretical approaches which overcome the intrinsic approximations of RPA are outlined.

## Key claim

The Random Phase Approximation (RPA) is presented as the most commonly used theory for describing excitations of many-body systems, but its validity is restricted to situations where excitation energies are relatively small compared to the global binding energies of the system, meaning RPA is not suitable for describing deep structural modifications such as fission in nuclei or phase transitions in fluids.

## Method

The paper derives the RPA secular equations using three distinct approaches: the equation of motion method, the Green's function perturbation theory, and the time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory. Extensions of the RPA equations are presented to treat the continuum part of the excitation spectrum (continuum RPA) and to include pairing effects between particles (quasiparticle RPA). The paper also outlines theoretical approaches that overcome the intrinsic approximations of RPA, such as higher-order RPA formulations and methods for handling microscopic interactions with strongly repulsive cores.

- The neutron transition in 208Pb yields excitation energies of 3.431 MeV with negative parity and angular momentum 4 or 5

## Context

This paper provides a pedagogical introduction to RPA theory, extending the foundational work of Bohm and Pines (1951-1953) who originally formulated the theory for describing collective oscillations of electron gas. The work contrasts RPA with the Independent Particle Model (IPM) and Hartree-Fock theory, showing how the residual interaction neglected in mean-field approaches plays a crucial role in describing excitations. The paper enables practitioners to understand the assumptions, merits, and faults of RPA, and indicates how to improve upon it through extensions such as continuum RPA and quasiparticle RPA, as well as higher-order approaches that go beyond the standard RPA framework.
