# Combined Garvey Kelson Relations for Mass Determinations and Machine Learning

> Simple Garvey Kelson mass relations applied in two regions are often used as an evaluation metric for machine learning based mass models. These relations have also been used in the training of some machine learning based models. Unfortunately, these Garvey Kelson relations do not broadly sum to zero as is sometimes assumed. In this manuscript, we generate three Garvey Kelson based mass relations that have been optimized with the goal of predicting nuclear masses the most accurately. These three relations have each been optimized for specific tasks. One relation has been optimized to predict the masses on the corner of a 5-by-5 grid. One has been optimized to predict the central mass on that grid, and the last has been optimized to work over the entire grid. Using these relations with the AME 2020 N & Z > 7 data, the central nucleus can be determined with a 129 keV standard deviation, any of the four corner masses with a 472 keV standard deviation, and the overall measure finds a 35 keV standard deviation for a per difference metric. We have compared these results with those from theoretical mass models and have tested the prediction and extrapolation capabilities of the relation that predicts corner masses. We also discuss how these relations can be implemented in machine learning based approaches.

## Key claim
Simple Garvey-Kelson mass relations do not broadly sum to zero as is sometimes assumed, particularly near N=Z, and the authors generate three optimized combined GK relations that are closer to zero and can be applied universally across the chart of nuclides without regional dependence.

## Method
The authors generated all 18 possible GK configurations on a 5-by-5 grid of neighboring nuclei and combined them by adding or subtracting each configuration, producing 3^18 possible combinations. They selected the three optimal combinations that minimize specific metrics: the standard deviation per difference (σ_PD), the standard deviation for predicting the central mass (σ_13), and the standard deviation for predicting corner masses (σ_1, σ_5, σ_21, σ_25). These relations were tested against experimental masses from AME 2020 and compared with theoretical mass models including Duflo-Zuker (DZ), Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM) 2012, Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) 31, Weizsäcker-Skyrme (WS) 4, WS with Radial Basis Function (WSRBF), and the Four Model Tree Ensemble (FMTE).

## Key numbers
- Using AME 2020 data with N and Z greater than 7 (N_C=1365 nuclei), the central nucleus can be determined with a 129 keV standard deviation using the ∑M_4GKM relation
- Any of the four corner masses can be predicted with a 472 keV standard deviation using the ∑M_6GKC relation
- The overall per-difference metric (σ_PD) finds a 35 keV standard deviation using the ∑M_8GKPD relation
- The two-region GK relations give σ_PD = 78 keV and σ_13 = 234 keV; the three-region GK relations give σ_PD = 77 keV
- The ∑M_12GK relation from Barea et al. gives σ_PD = 60 keV and σ_13 = 140 keV
- For 13 newly measured masses, the ∑M_6GKC relation achieved a mean absolute error of 308 keV, compared to 81 keV for FMTE and 234 keV for WSRBF
- The minimum achievable σ_PD with more complex combinations is 33 keV, and the minimum σ_13 drops to 119 keV

## Context
This work extends the approach of combining GK relations pioneered by Barea et al. (2008) and further explored by Cheng et al. (2014) and Bao et al. (2013), who found that subsets of the 12 GK configurations can yield lower standard deviations than using all expressions. The authors improve upon these prior efforts by including all 18 GK configurations (both transverse and longitudinal orientations) rather than only those with non-zero central coefficients. The resulting optimized relations provide a physically constrained regularization framework for machine learning based mass models, building on earlier work by Morales et al. (2009) on iterative mass predictions and by recent studies that incorporated two-region GK relations into machine learning loss functions. The three new relations enable more accurate evaluation metrics for mass models and can be used to predict unknown masses at the boundaries of known experimental data.
