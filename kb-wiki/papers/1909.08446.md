# Eigenvector Continuation as an Efficient and Accurate Emulator for Uncertainty Quantification

> First principles calculations of atomic nuclei based on microscopic nuclear forces derived from chiral effective field theory (EFT) have blossomed in the past years. A key element of such ab initio studies is the understanding and quantification of systematic and statistical errors arising from the omission of higher-order terms in the chiral expansion as well as the model calibration. While there has been significant progress in analyzing theoretical uncertainties for nucleon-nucleon scattering observables, the generalization to multi-nucleon systems has not been feasible yet due to the high computational cost of evaluating observables for a large set of low-energy couplings. In this Letter we show that a new method called eigenvector continuation (EC) can be used for constructing an efficient and accurate emulator for nuclear many-body observables, thereby enabling uncertainty quantification in multi-nucleon systems. We demonstrate the power of EC emulation with a proof-of-principle calculation that lays out all correlations between bulk ground-state observables in the few-nucleon sector. On the basis of ab initio calculations for the ground-state energy and radius in 4He, we demonstrate that EC is more accurate and efficient compared to established methods like Gaussian processes.

## Key claim

Eigenvector continuation (EC) can serve as an efficient and accurate emulator for nuclear many-body observables, enabling uncertainty quantification in multi-nucleon systems where the high computational cost of evaluating observables for a large set of low-energy couplings has previously made such analyses infeasible. The method is demonstrated to be more accurate and efficient than established methods like Gaussian processes, particularly for extrapolation in multi-dimensional parameter domains.

## Method

The authors construct an EC emulator by first obtaining exact eigenvectors (wavefunctions) from no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations for a set of training points in the 16-dimensional parameter space of low-energy constants (LECs) of a chiral nuclear potential. For each new parameter value, the ground state of the Hamiltonian is found by solving a generalized eigenvalue problem in the low-dimensional subspace spanned by these training eigenvectors, rather than performing a full diagonalization in the original 2775-dimensional harmonic-oscillator basis. The method is benchmarked for the ground-state energy and radius of 4He, with training points selected using a space-filling Latin Hypercube design and validation points drawn from a uniform distribution.

## Key numbers

- The NCSM model space for 4He calculations was truncated at Nmax=16 with a harmonic-oscillator frequency of hbar-Omega=36 MeV, yielding a basis of 2775 antisymmetric and translationally invariant four-body states
- For emulation with 12 training data points varying 3 LECs, EC achieved sub-percent accuracy while polynomial interpolation and Gaussian processes struggled even for interpolation within the convex hull of training points
- With 64 training data points varying all 16 LECs, EC still provided accurate results while polynomial interpolation and Gaussian processes failed completely
- The asymptotic speedup factor for EC emulation compared to direct calculation was 614 for the 4He benchmark case, limited by the relatively small model space
- A proof-of-principle application correlated observables in 2H and 4He across 10,000 LEC samples, with the entire set of EC evaluations taking less than one minute on a standard laptop

## Context

This work extends eigenvector continuation, introduced by Frame (2017) for extrapolation in cases where direct calculation was not possible due to computational issues like the Monte Carlo sign problem, to both interpolation and extrapolation in multi-dimensional parameter spaces. The method enables large-scale sampling for uncertainty quantification in nuclear many-body systems, which has previously been feasible only for nucleon-nucleon scattering observables. The approach is particularly relevant for Bayesian inference analyses of chiral effective field theory parameters, as pursued by Furnstahl (2014, 2015), Wesolowski (2015, 2018), and Ekstrom (2019), where the required large number of exact calculations would be prohibitively expensive.
