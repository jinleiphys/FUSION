# From the liquid drop model to lattice QCD

> The present article aims to give a concise account of the main developments in nuclear structure theory, from its origin in the 1930s to date, taking the modelling of inter-nucleon interactions as guideline.

## Key claim

This paper provides a concise historical overview of nuclear structure theory from the 1930s to 2018, with the explicit hedge that the modelling of inter-nucleon interactions remains "the most uncertain piece of the nuclear puzzle" despite eighty years of development, and that a full QCD treatment of bound states is only becoming feasible "nowadays" with quantitative results "expected in the near future."

## Method

The paper traces the evolution of nuclear structure theory by following the modelling of interactions between nucleons, from the liquid drop model and semi-empirical mass formula of Weizsäcker and Bethe (1935) through the independent-particle shell model of Goeppert-Mayer and Jensen (1949), to the interacting shell model and energy density functionals (Skyrme, Gogny, and relativistic approaches from the 1970s-1980s). It then discusses modern ab initio approaches that aim to connect nuclear structure to quantum chromodynamics via lattice simulations, noting that ab initio techniques remain limited to systems with mass number A ~ 100.

## Key numbers

* 253 stable and approximately 3100 unstable isotopes are known as of 2017, with theoretical predictions suggesting 6000 to 9000 isotopes may exist in total.
* The heaviest synthesised element has 118 protons, while the heaviest stable nucleus is 208Pb with 82 protons.
* The neutron drip line has been established experimentally only for elements with atomic number Z ≤ 8.
* Magic numbers observed in stable nuclei are N = 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126, corresponding to particularly stable configurations in binding energy deviations from the semi-empirical mass formula.
* The inter-nucleon distance in nuclei is roughly 2 femtometers, comparable to the range of nuclear interactions.
* Energy scales in nuclei span from nucleon momenta of order 10^8 eV down to rotational excitations as low as 10^4 eV.
* Modern energy density functionals contain about 15 parameters fitted at the single-reference level to experimental binding energies and charge radii.

## Context

This review extends the narrative from the liquid drop model of Gamow, Bohr, and Wheeler through the Weizsäcker-Bethe semi-empirical mass formula (1935) and the shell model of Goeppert-Mayer and Jensen (1949), to modern Skyrme, Gogny, and relativistic density functionals developed since the 1970s-1980s. It contrasts these effective approaches with ab initio methods that have recently enabled valence-space calculations (Bogner 2014, Jansen 2014) and discusses how the non-observable nature of shell structure was illustrated in ab initio calculations (Duguet 2015). The paper enables readers to understand the current landscape where single-reference energy density functionals remain the only microscopic method able to address the entire Segrè chart, while ab initio techniques are pushing toward A ~ 100 and lattice QCD is beginning to describe light nuclei qualitatively.
