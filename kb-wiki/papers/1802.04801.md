# Heavy Ion Collisions: The Big Picture, and the Big Questions

> Heavy ion collisions quickly form a droplet of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) with a remarkably small viscosity. We give an accessible introduction to how to study this smallest and hottest droplet of liquid made on earth and why it is so interesting. The physics of heavy ions ranges from highly energetic quarks and gluons described by perturbative QCD to a bath of strongly interacting gluons at lower energy scales. These gluons quickly thermalize and form QGP, while the energetic partons traverse this plasma and end in a shower of particles called jets. Analyzing the final particles in a variety of different ways allows us to study the properties of QGP and the complex dynamics of multi-scale processes in QCD which govern its formation and evolution, providing what is perhaps the simplest form of complex quantum matter that we know of. Much remains to be understood, and throughout the review big open questions will be encountered.

# Heavy Ion Collisions: The Big Picture, and the Big Questions

## Key claim
Heavy ion collisions produce a droplet of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) that behaves as a strongly coupled liquid with remarkably small specific viscosity, but the review emphasizes that much remains unknown about how this liquid forms within 1 fm/c, what the smallest droplet size is that behaves hydrodynamically, and how a strongly coupled liquid emerges from an asymptotically free gauge theory.

## Method
The authors provide a qualitative review of the sequence of events in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions, from the initial Lorentz-contracted nuclei through the formation of QGP, its hydrodynamic expansion, and eventual hadronization. They synthesize experimental data on particle distributions, flow anisotropies, and jet quenching across multiple collision systems (AA, pA, pp, and e+e-) and energy scales, connecting these observations to theoretical frameworks including perturbative QCD, relativistic hydrodynamics, lattice QCD thermodynamics, and holographic calculations.

## Key numbers
- The specific viscosity of QGP is close to 1/4π, the value for infinitely strongly coupled gauge theories with a dual gravitational description
- QGP forms within a time of order 1 fm/c after the collision
- At LHC energies (√sNN = 2.76 TeV), the average energy density 1 fm/c after a head-on PbPb collision is greater than 12 GeV/fm³, about twenty times the energy density inside a typical hadron (500 MeV/fm³)
- The relativistic γ factors at RHIC and LHC are approximately 100 and 2500 respectively, corresponding to beam rapidities of y = 5.3 and 8.5
- Lattice QCD calculations show a continuous crossover from hadron gas to QGP around T ~ 150 MeV
- The diameter of a Pb or Au nucleus disc is about 14 fm, with thickness about 14/γ fm
- The total transverse energy in particles with pseudorapidity between -0.5 and 0.5 at LHC is 1.65 ± 0.1 TeV
- A head-on LHC collision can produce as many as 30,000 final-state particles
- Matter in thermal equilibrium at 300 MeV has an energy density ≈ 12 T⁴ = 12.7 GeV/fm³
- QGP forms at rapidities extending between y = -6.5 and y = 6.5 in LHC collisions
- The discs lose about 85% of their energy during the collision process

## Context
This review extends and synthesizes the understanding developed over 50 years of ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions, building on the foundational work of Heisenberg and Heitler (1930s-1940s), Fermi and Landau (1940s-1950s), and Feynman (1960s) on particle production in high energy collisions. It connects to lattice QCD calculations by Borsanyi (2013) and Bazavov (2014) showing the crossover transition, and to the discovery that QGP has η/s near 1/4π as found by Heinz (2013) and Romatschke (2017). The review enables future investigations into the limits of hydrodynamics for small systems, the nature of hydrodynamization, the role of the QCD critical point (to be explored by the RHIC Beam Energy Scan II), and the connection between heavy ion physics and neutron star interiors via binary neutron star mergers.
