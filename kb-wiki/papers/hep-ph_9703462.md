# Physics at ELFE

> I review some central physics opportunities at the 15 ... 30 GeV continuous beam electron accelerator ELFE, proposed to be built in conjunction with the DESY linear collider. Our present detailed knowledge of single parton distributions in hadrons and nuclei needs to be supplemented by measurements of compact valence quark configurations, accessible through hard exclusive scattering, and of compact multiparton subsystems which contribute to semi-inclusive processes. Cumulative (x>1, x_F>1) processes in nuclei measure short-range correlations between partons belonging to different nucleons in the same nucleus. The same configurations may give rise to subthreshold production of light hadrons and charm. The challenges of understanding high energy charmonium production indicate that charmonium will be a sensitive probe of color dynamics at ELFE. At low energies, charmonium forms inside the target nucleus, allowing a determination of $c\bar c$ bound state interactions in nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the proposed ELFE accelerator (15-30 GeV continuous electron beam) will enable measurements of rare, compact parton configurations in hadrons and nuclei that are inaccessible to standard inclusive deep inelastic scattering, but the author notes that the theoretical framework for such measurements is still incomplete and that factorization for exclusive and semi-inclusive processes has not been fully proven in QCD.

## Method

The author reviews three categories of physics opportunities for ELFE: hard exclusive scattering (e.g., elastic ep scattering at large Q²) which selects compact valence quark configurations; semi-inclusive processes where a pion carries a large fraction z of the photon energy, selecting compact quark-antiquark pairs; and cumulative processes (x>1, x_F>1) in nuclei that probe short-range correlations between partons from different nucleons. The analysis of charmonium production near threshold (E_γ ≈ 9 GeV) is presented as a sensitive probe of color dynamics, with the author discussing both the Color Evaporation Model and two-gluon exchange approaches.

## Key numbers

- ELFE beam energy range: 15 to 30 GeV
- Luminosity range: L = 10³⁵ to 10³⁸ cm⁻²s⁻¹
- Energy resolution: ΔE/E ≈ 10⁻³
- Duty factor: approximately 90%
- Resolution scale: order 0.1 fm (corresponding to Q² ≈ 10 GeV²)
- Charm threshold for real photons: E_γ^th ≈ 8 to 12 GeV for J/ψ and D\bar{D} production on free nucleons
- Cross section σ(γN → J/ψ N) near threshold: approximately 1 nb
- Expected J/ψ dimuon decay rate at ELFE: about 5 per second
- Proton form factor data available up to Q² ≤ 30 GeV², consistent with 1/Q⁴ scaling for Q² ≥ 5 GeV²
- Pion transition form factor data in range 1 < Q² < 8 GeV²
- Cumulative proton production in nuclei: ⟨p_⊥²⟩ = 2 GeV² at x_F = 4
- Subthreshold antiproton production observed on ⁶³Cu down to E_lab ≈ 3 GeV (kinematic limit for pA → \bar{p}+X is 2.8 GeV)
- Elastic J/ψ photoproduction data from SLAC and Cornell (1975) in the ELFE energy range

## Context

This paper extends the physics program of earlier ELFE proposals (Brodsky, 1995; ELFE physics reviews) and discusses how ELFE would complement existing facilities: compared to muon beams at CERN and Fermilab, ELFE offers higher luminosity; compared to SLAC, higher duty factor; compared to TJNAF, higher energy. The HERMES experiment at DESY works in the same energy range but at lower luminosity and duty factor. The paper discusses how ELFE would enable tests of factorization in exclusive reactions (Brodsky and Lepage, 1980; Collins, Frankfurt and Strikman, 1997) and measurements of off-forward parton distributions (Ji, 1997; Radyushkin, 1996). The charmonium production puzzles discussed build on work by Schuler (1994), Sansoni (1995), and Mangano (1995), and the subthreshold production observations reference earlier work by Stavinsky (1979) and Geaga (1977).
