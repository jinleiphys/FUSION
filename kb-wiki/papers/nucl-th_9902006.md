# Multifragmentation and the Renormalization Group

> The possible usefulness of the renormalization group method in Nuclear Physics is pointed out in this talk in the context of the nuclear multifragmentation. The presentation is rather superficial and sketchy, to indicate the main lines only. But I believe that the idea is warrant of further, more careful studies.

## Key claim

The renormalization group (RG) method may be useful for describing nuclear multifragmentation, but the author explicitly states the presentation is "rather superficial and sketchy" and that the idea "is warrant of further, more careful studies" rather than being a fully developed or validated approach.

## Method

The paper outlines a conceptual framework for applying RG techniques to nuclear multifragmentation by eliminating high-momentum degrees of freedom through blocking steps, constructing effective Hamiltonians with cutoff-dependent coupling constants. For the spinodal phase separation problem, the method uses infinitesimal RG steps (Wegner-Houghton equations) and tree-level renormalization via saddle-point solutions of the blocking relation, where the effective action becomes degenerate in the mixed phase.

## Key numbers

- The initial cutoff for the microscopic Hamiltonian is set at \(k=\Lambda\approx\) few GeV/c single particle momentum.
- The spinodal instability onset is defined by \(k_{cr}\), where \(\Phi_0(k)>0\) for \(k<k_{cr}\).
- The tree-level renormalization example uses a potential \(V(\phi)=-0.05\phi^2+0.2\phi^4/4!\).
- The effective action in the mixed phase is given by \(S_k^{mixed}=\frac12 Z_k^{stable}(\Phi_0(k))\int d^dx[(\partial_\mu\phi(x))^2-k^2\phi^2(x)]+O(\dk/k)\), with corrections of order \(\dk/k\) that can be arbitrarily small.

## Context

This talk extends the RG methodology from quantum field theory and condensed matter physics (Wegner-Houghton equations, 1973) into nuclear multifragmentation, drawing analogies to the treatment of d, f, and g electrons in quantum chemistry and to the dynamical RG method developed for glassy materials. The work suggests that the RG approach could provide a systematic description of statistical aspects of multifragmentation and enable the Maxwell construction for the mixed phase, but notes that nuclear physics lacks the long scaling regimes of QCD and QED, making the suppression of non-universal interactions more difficult.
