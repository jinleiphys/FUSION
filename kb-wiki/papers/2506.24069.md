# Recurring region for neutron-star observables

> In this letter, we report a new phenomena of recurring regions when relating observables for hybrid neutron stars and hybrid neutron-star mergers. To describe dense matter within hybrid stars, we introduce a percolation to vary the size and characteristics of the deconfinement phase transition to quark matter. Before and after the percolation, we keep the hadronic and quark phases the same, described by different realistic models for the equation of state of beta-equilibrated, charge-neutral, zero-temperature matter. When solving spherical and deformed equations for neutron stars in general relativity, we find that: no matter the size or characteristics of the percolation region, or the order of the phase transition on either side (hadronic and quark), as long as we minimize the average sound speed from the beginning of the percolation region to the central density for a given star, we can produce equations of state that cross through the same, small recurring region within mass-radius and mass-tidal deformability diagrams. Our findings provide a new way to produce hybrid equations of state for dense matter that match a given observation of neutron stars or neutron star mergers.

## Key claim

The authors report a new "recurring region" phenomenon where different hybrid neutron-star equations of state (EoSs) cross through the same small region in mass-radius and mass-tidal deformability diagrams, provided the average speed of sound from the beginning of the percolation region to the central density is minimized for a given star, regardless of the size or characteristics of the percolation region or the order of the phase transition.

## Method

The authors constructed hybrid equations of state by inserting a fifth-order polynomial percolation region between hadronic and quark phases, replacing first-order phase transitions with second- or third-order transitions. They used two hadronic models (CMF-7 and DD2F) and two quark models (CMF quark phase and NJL with string flip model), then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations and perturbed Einstein field equations to compute neutron-star masses, radii, and tidal deformabilities. The average speed of sound squared from the percolation onset to the stellar center was minimized by varying second-order baryon susceptibilities at the percolation boundaries.

## Key numbers

* Recurring region appears at a stellar mass near 1.5 solar masses (specifically 1.554 solar masses) in both mass-radius and mass-tidal deformability diagrams.
* For the blue curve (CMF-7, third-order transition, boundaries n_B,H=0.15 fm^-3 and n_B,Q=1.20 fm^-3), the susceptibilities at the recurring region are chi_2^B,H=0.00226 fm^-3/MeV and chi_2^B,Q=0.00106 fm^-3/MeV.
* Stellar radii corresponding to minima in average speed of sound vary within a range of approximately 0.8 km for one parameter variation and approximately 0.2 km for another, with the smaller range producing radii compatible with astrophysical constraints (below about 13.5 km).
* For the red curve (CMF-7, boundaries n_B,H=0.30 fm^-1 and n_B,Q=1.20 fm^-1), the recurring region spans a significantly smaller range of radii of 0.1 km.
* A recurring region was also found for a larger stellar mass of 1.8 solar masses, appearing at larger radii than for the 1.554 solar mass case.

## Context

This work extends the percolation method for describing quark deconfinement in hybrid stars, originally motivated by the quarkyonic matter picture from McLerran (2007, 2020) and the percolation prescription from Kojo (2014). The recurring region phenomenon provides a new way to produce hybrid equations of state that match given neutron-star observations, independent of the specific percolation parameters or phase transition order. This enables future work where different EoSs with similar macroscopic properties can be compared in neutron-star merger simulations, potentially improving understanding of dense matter and providing predictions for gravitational wave detectors searching for evidence of stable quark matter.
