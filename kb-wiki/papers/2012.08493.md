# Hadronization of correlated gluon fields

> Following an explicit example, we present the chain of steps required for an event-by-event description of hadron production in high energy hadronic and nuclear collisions. We start from incoming nuclei, described in the Color Glass Condensate effective theory, whose collision creates the gluon fields of the glasma. Individual gluons are then sampled from the gluon fields' Husimi (smeared Wigner) distributions, and clustered using a new spacetime based algorithm. Clusters are fed into the Herwig event generator, which performs the hadronization, conserving energy and momentum. We discuss the physical implications of smearing and problems with the quasi particle picture for the studied processes. We compute spectra of charged hadrons and identified particles and their azimuthal momentum anisotropies, and address systematic uncertainties on observables, resulting from the general lack of detailed knowledge of the hadronization mechanism.

## Key claim

The paper presents a complete event-by-event chain from the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) initial state to hadron production in p+Pb collisions, but the quasi-particle picture underlying the hadronization is pushed to its limits of validity because spatial variations occur on the same scale as the de-Broglie wavelength of typical excitations, requiring a coarse-graining (smearing) procedure that washes out fluctuations on short distance and momentum scales.

## Method

The authors start with an IP-Glasma calculation of gluon fields in 5.02 TeV p+Pb collisions, solving classical Yang-Mills equations to evolve the fields to tau = 0.2 fm/c. They compute the gluon Wigner distribution and then apply a Gaussian smearing with sigma_x = 0.197 fm and sigma_p = 1 GeV to obtain a positive-definite Husimi distribution, from which individual gluons are sampled. These gluons are clustered using a new spacetime-based algorithm (SAHARA) that groups gluons by their distance of closest approach, with the clustering controlled by a parameter alpha and a minimum gluon number N_min. Each cluster is then hadronized independently using Herwig 7, with a democratic color assignment for the gluons.

## Key numbers

*   Smearing parameters: sigma_x = 0.197 fm and sigma_p = 1 GeV, chosen as a compromise between spatial and momentum resolution.
*   The strong coupling constant is set to g = 2.75 to reproduce correct charged particle multiplicities after hadronization.
*   Gluons are sampled over a spacetime rapidity range determined by eta_max = 2.
*   The Wigner distribution is computed at tau = 0.2 fm/c, which corresponds to approximately a time scale of 1/Q_s.
*   The infrared regulator is set to m = 0.4 GeV, and the width parameter of the normalization fluctuation is sigma = 0.5.
*   For the charged hadron p_T spectrum, the IP-Glasma+SAHARA+Herwig result agrees well with the IP-Glasma+KKP result for 5 GeV < p_T < 8 GeV, with increasing differences below p_T ~ 0.5 GeV.
*   The charged hadron v_2{2}(p_T) from the full calculation is approximately a factor of three smaller than experimental data (e.g., at p_T = 2 GeV).
*   The charged hadron v_3{2}(p_T) is also approximately three times smaller than experimental data.

## Context

This work extends CGC-based calculations beyond parton-level results or independent fragmentation (which is only valid at high p_T > 1-2 GeV, as in Kniehl (2000)), providing the first event-by-event CGC calculation with a sophisticated hadronization description for p+Pb collisions. It contrasts with hydrodynamic calculations (e.g., Bozek (2011), Schenke (2020)) that assume thermal equilibrium and encode hadronization in the equation of state. The framework enables direct comparison to experimental data from ALICE and CMS, and the authors note that the discrepancy in azimuthal anisotropies likely stems from neglected final state effects that could be implemented via partonic transport (Greif (2017)) or a hydrodynamic phase (Schenke (2020)).
