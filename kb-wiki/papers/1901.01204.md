# A brief history of the pion-nucleon coupling constant

> This work provides a brief history of determinations of the pion-nucleon ($πN$) coupling constant from $πN$ and $N N$ data. From robust analyses of twenty reported values of the charged-pion coupling constant, exhibiting sizeable fluctuation, the result $f_c^2 = 762.9^{+6.5}_{-6.2} \cdot 10^{-4}$ is obtained. Similar values are extracted for the other two $πN$ coupling constants, $f_0^2$ and $f_p^2$, from fewer data. The average values of the various $πN$ coupling constants, extracted in this work, suggest no splitting, in agreement with the thesis of the Nijmegen group. Additional analysis of the $f_c^2$ and $f_0^2$ values, both reported in four studies, turned to be inconclusive: one of these studies suggests that $f_0<f_c$, whereas another slightly favours $f_0>f_c$; no significant splitting effects are observed in the other two studies. The analysis of the low-energy $πN$ data with the ETH model indicates significant splitting and, under certain conditions, it implies that $f_0>f_c$. Also discussed in the paper are the electromagnetic corrections, which need to be applied to the strong shift and to the total decay width of the ground state of pionic hydrogen in order that estimates for the hadronic $s$-wave $πN$ scattering lengths be obtained; this is a relevant subject as $f_c^2$ may be extracted from the isovector scattering length by use of the Goldberger-Miyazawa-Oehme sum rule. Regarding the removal of the electromagnetic effects in the $πN$ system at threshold, my opinion is that Theory must find a way to provide reliable and accurate corrections, matching the level of accuracy of the experimental results.

# A brief history of the pion-nucleon coupling constant

## Key claim

The paper argues that the charged-pion coupling constant has been overestimated in canonical pre-meson-factory determinations, and that robust statistical analysis of twenty reported values yields a best estimate of $f_c^2 = 762.9^{+6.5}_{-6.2} \cdot 10^{-4}$, with no evidence for splitting between the charged, neutral, and proton coupling constants, though the author notes that the analysis of low-energy $\pi N$ data with the ETH model indicates significant splitting and, under certain conditions, implies $f_0 > f_c$.

## Method

The author collected published values of the pion-nucleon coupling constants from $\pi N$ and $N N$ scattering data, applying five selection criteria: each value must have a meaningful uncertainty, be a new result (not a compilation average), be a final result within a given methodology, appear in a peer-reviewed journal, and have no definite proof of being incorrect. For the charged-pion coupling constant $f_c^2$, twenty values meeting these criteria were subjected to robust statistical analysis to obtain an average and its uncertainty. The same procedure was applied to the neutral-pion coupling constant $f_0^2$ and the proton coupling constant $f_p^2$ from fewer available determinations. The paper also discusses electromagnetic corrections needed to extract hadronic $s$-wave $\pi N$ scattering lengths from pionic hydrogen measurements, which feed into the Goldberger-Miyazawa-Oehme sum rule determination of $f_c^2$.

## Key numbers

- $f_c^2 = 762.9^{+6.5}_{-6.2} \cdot 10^{-4}$: robust average from twenty reported values of the charged-pion coupling constant
- $f_c^2 = 0.0790(10)$: the "canonical" value from Bugg et al. (1973), obtained from fixed-$t$ dispersion relations applied to low-energy $\pi N$ cross sections
- $f_c^2 = 0.079(1)$: the KH80 result from Koch and Pietarinen (1980), obtained from an energy-independent partial-wave analysis of $\pi N$ data below 500 MeV/$c$
- $f_c^2 = 0.0735(15)$: the SM90 result from Arndt et al. (1990), obtained from fixed-$t$ dispersion relations on $\pi N$ elastic scattering data below 2 GeV
- $f_c^2 = 0.0748(3)$: the final Nijmegen group result from Stoks et al. (1993), obtained from $N N$ partial-wave analyses
- $f_c^2 = 0.0756(9)$: result from Timmermans et al. (1997), obtained from $\pi N$ data below 410 MeV
- $f_c^2 = 0.0765(14)$: result from Matsinos (1997), obtained from a robust analysis of low-energy $\pi N$ measurements
- $f_c^2 = 0.0756(7)$: result from Gibbs et al. (1998), obtained from modern low-energy $\pi^\pm p$ elastic scattering data using the GMO sum rule
- $f_c^2 = 0.07756(94)$: result from Ericson et al. (2004), obtained from pionic hydrogen data with their own electromagnetic corrections
- $f_p^2 = 0.0755(7)$: result from Rentmeester et al. (1999), the most recent Nijmegen determination from $p p$ partial-wave analysis
- $f_0^2 = 0.0752(8)$: result from Klomp et al. (1991), the neutral-pion coupling constant from Nijmegen $N N$ analyses
- $f_c^2 = 0.0803(14)$: result from Rahm et al. (1998), from $n p$ differential cross sections at 162 MeV
- $f_c^2 = 0.0814(18)$: result from Rahm et al. (2001), from $n p$ differential cross sections at 96 MeV

## Context

This work provides a historical review of determinations of the pion-nucleon coupling constant, extending from early efforts in the 1950s through the pre-meson-factory era (up to 1980), the meson-factory era (1981–1997), and modern determinations after 1998. The paper contrasts the "canonical value" of Bugg et al. (1973) and Koch and Pietarinen (1980), centred around $f_c^2 \approx 0.079$, with the lower values obtained by the Nijmegen group (Stoks et al., 1993; Klomp et al., 1991) and the VPI/GWU group (Arndt et al., 1990, 1994), which cluster around $f_c^2 \approx 0.075$. The author notes that the Nijmegen group found no significant splitting between the charged, neutral, and proton coupling constants, in agreement with the thesis of that group. The paper also discusses the ongoing controversy over electromagnetic corrections in pionic hydrogen measurements, comparing the approaches of Sigg et al. (1996), Oades et al. (2007), and Ericson et al. (2004), which affect the extraction of $f_c^2$ via the Goldberger-Miyazawa-Oehme sum rule. The work enables future determinations to be placed in historical context and highlights the need for reliable theoretical electromagnetic corrections matching experimental precision.
