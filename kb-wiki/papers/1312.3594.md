# Wavelets in field theory

> We discuss the use of Daubechies wavelets in discretizing quantum field theories.

## Key claim

Daubechies wavelets provide a basis for discretizing quantum field theories that is natural for multiscale problems, but the exact Hamiltonian expressed as a sum of well-defined smeared-field operators does not converge; only finite-volume, finite-resolution truncations yield well-defined theories.

## Method

The authors expand local quantum fields in 1+1 dimensions in an orthonormal basis of Daubechies scaling functions and wavelets, which are related by dyadic scale transformations and form a multiresolution decomposition of \(L^2(\mathbb{R})\). They define discrete field operators by smearing the canonical fields with these basis functions, derive the equal-time commutation relations for the discrete fields, and construct truncated Hamiltonians (e.g., for a \(:\phi^4:\) interaction) by discarding degrees of freedom below a chosen resolution scale. The scaling properties of the overlap integrals are used to relate Hamiltonians at different resolutions, and the renormalization group structure of the scaling equation is exploited to systematically eliminate fine-scale degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

* The scaling function support is \([n, 2K-1+n]\) on scale \(1/2^k\), where \(K\) is an integer defining the Daubechies type.
* The scaling coefficients \(h_l\) satisfy \(\sum_{n=0}^{2K-1} h_n = \sqrt{2}\) and \(\sum_{n=0}^{2K-1} h_n h_{n-2m} = \delta_{m0}\).
* The overlap integrals scale as \(D^k_{mn} = 2^k D^0_{mn}\) and \(\Gamma^{k}_{n_1 \cdots n_m} = 2^{k(m-2)/2} \Gamma^{0}_{n_1 \cdots n_m}\).
* Exact solutions for the \(h_l\) coefficients exist for \(K=1,2,3\) (values given in the paper's Table 1, not reproduced in the text).

## Context

This work extends prior discussions of wavelet methods in quantum field theory from both computational (e.g., refs. 4-8 in the paper) and theoretical (e.g., refs. 9-13) perspectives. The multiscale structure of the wavelet basis provides a natural framework for eliminating short-distance degrees of freedom, leading to effective models amenable to computation. The approach enables systematic restoration of eliminated degrees of freedom and can be combined with similarity renormalization group methods to decouple coarse and fine scales, potentially yielding finite renormalized parameters in the limit of arbitrarily fine scales.
