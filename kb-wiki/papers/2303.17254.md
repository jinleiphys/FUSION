# Hot QCD White Paper

> Hot QCD physics studies the nuclear strong force under extreme temperature and densities. Experimentally these conditions are achieved via high-energy collisions of heavy ions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) and the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). In the past decade, a unique and substantial suite of data was collected at RHIC and the LHC, probing hydrodynamics at the nucleon scale, the temperature dependence of the transport properties of quark-gluon plasma, the phase diagram of nuclear matter, the interaction of quarks and gluons at different scales and much more. This document, as part of the 2023 nuclear science long range planning process, was written to review the progress in hot QCD since the 2015 Long Range Plan for Nuclear Science, as well as highlight the realization of previous recommendations, and present opportunities for the next decade, building on the accomplishments and investments made in theoretical developments and the construction of new detectors. Furthermore, this document provides additional context to support the recommendations voted on at the Joint Hot and Cold QCD Town Hall Meeting, which are reported in a separate document.

## Key claim

This white paper reviews progress in hot QCD since the 2015 Long Range Plan and presents opportunities for the next decade, but it is a community planning document rather than a presentation of new experimental results; the specific claims about jet quenching and heavy flavor energy loss are drawn from previously published measurements and simulations, not from new data reported here.

## Method

The document synthesizes experimental results from RHIC and LHC collaborations (STAR, PHENIX, ALICE, ATLAS, CMS) on heavy flavor tagged jets, jet substructure, and global parameter extraction using Bayesian analysis. It describes multi-stage energy loss simulations combining the MATTER higher-twist formalism with the LBT linearized Boltzmann transport approach, and discusses Bayesian inference methods that use Gaussian process emulators and Markov-Chain Monte-Carlo to extract transport coefficients such as the jet quenching parameter \(\hat{q}\) and the charm diffusion coefficient \(D_s\).

## Key numbers

* \(b\)-jet nuclear modification factor ratio to inclusive jets is consistently above 1 in 0-20% central PbPb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\) = 5.02 TeV, indicating less suppression of \(b\)-jets than inclusive jets (ATLAS 2022)
* Dijet momentum imbalance \(\langle x_J \rangle\) shift in 0-10% central PbPb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\) = 5.02 TeV is compatible between \(b\)-dijets and inclusive dijets (CMS 2018)
* At RHIC, the ratio of \(D^0\) meson radial profiles in central collisions is consistent with unity within uncertainties for \(p_{T,D^0} > 5\) GeV/\(c\) and \(p_{T,jet} > 5\) GeV/\(c\) (STAR 2022)
* At the LHC, a hint of \(D^0\) meson diffusion away from the jet axis is seen for \(4 < p_{T,D^0} < 20\) GeV/\(c\) and \(p_{T,jet} > 60\) GeV/\(c\) (CMS 2019)
* Jet \(R_{AA}\) shows no strong dependence on jet distance parameter from 0.2 to 1.0 at high \(p_T\) in CMS data, while ALICE reports larger suppression of wide jets using machine-learning based reconstruction (CMS 2021, ALICE 2023)
* Soft-drop groomed momentum sharing \(z_g\) shows little modification in PbPb compared to pp for \(z_{cut} = 0.2\) at the LHC (ALICE 2021)
* Nuclear modification factor as a function of groomed jet radius \(R_g\) shows variation of quenching effects at angular scales smaller than 0.1 (ATLAS 2022, ALICE 2022)

## Context

This white paper extends the 2015 Long Range Plan for Nuclear Science (Akiba 2015) by reviewing progress in jet quenching, heavy flavor energy loss, and global parameter extraction. It highlights the need for multi-scale energy loss approaches combining formalisms such as MATTER and LBT (JETSCAPE 2021, Fan 2022), and emphasizes that Bayesian analysis methods (JETSCAPE 2021, Xie 2022) are now essential for extracting transport properties like \(\hat{q}\) and \(D_s\). The document enables future precision measurements at sPHENIX and LHC Runs 3-4, and calls for improved experimental uncertainty reporting and continued methodology development for global parameter extraction.
