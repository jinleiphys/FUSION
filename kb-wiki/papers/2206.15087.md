# Sub-barrier fusion hindrance and absence of neutron transfer channels

> The sub-barrier fusion hindrance has been observed in the domain of very low energies of astrophysical relevance. This phenomenon can be analyzed effectively using an uncomplicated straightforward elegant mathematical formula gleaned presuming diffused barrier with a Gaussian distribution. The mathematical formula for cross section of nuclear fusion reaction has been obtained by folding together a Gaussian function representing the fusion barrier height distribution and the expression for classical cross section of fusion assuming a fixed barrier. The variation of fusion cross section as a function of energy, thus obtained, describes well the existing data on sub-barrier heavy-ion fusion for lighter systems of astrophysical interest. Employing this elegant formula, cross sections of interacting nuclei from $^{16}$O + $^{18}$O to $^{12}$C + $^{198}$Pt, all of which were measured down to $<$ 10 $μ$b have been analyzed. The agreement of the present analysis with the measured values is comparable, if not better, than those calculated from more sophisticated calculations. The three parameters of this formula varies rather smoothly implying its usage in estimating the excitation function or extrapolating cross sections for pairs of interacting nuclei which are yet to be measured. Possible effects of neutron transfers on the hindrance in heavy-ion fusion have been explored.

## Key claim

The paper claims that sub-barrier fusion hindrance in lighter heavy-ion systems can be effectively described by a simple three-parameter Gaussian barrier distribution formula, with agreement comparable to more sophisticated coupled-channel calculations. The key qualifier is that this hindrance persists even for reactions with positive fusion Q-values because the width of the barrier distribution remains small, and the authors explicitly state that neutron transfer channels are absent (all one- and two-neutron transfer Q-values are negative) for the systems studied, so transfer effects do not contribute to the observed hindrance.

## Method

The authors derived a fusion cross-section formula by folding a Gaussian distribution for the fusion barrier height (with mean height h0 and width σh) together with the classical expression for fusion cross section assuming a fixed barrier. They treated the effective barrier radius Rh as a third free parameter and extracted all three parameters by least-square fitting to measured excitation functions for seven reactions ranging from 16O+18O to 12C+198Pt, all measured down to cross sections below 10 μb. The width σh was further decomposed into contributions from static quadrupole deformations (σ1, σ2) and an adjustable parameter σ0 accounting for nuclear vibrations and quantum tunneling.

## Key numbers

- Cross sections were analyzed for systems measured down to **< 10 μb** (stated in abstract and text).
- For 12C+24Mg: h0 = **11.483 MeV**, σh = **0.815 MeV**, Rh = **6.646 fm** (Table I).
- For 12C+30Si: h0 = **13.540 MeV**, σh = **1.090 MeV**, Rh = **8.300 fm** (Table I).
- For 12C+198Pt: h0 = **55.140 MeV**, σh = **1.749 MeV**, Rh = **11.179 fm** (Table I).
- For 16O+18O: h0 = **9.797 MeV**, σh = **0.859 MeV**, Rh = **7.743 fm** (Table I).
- For 28Si+64Ni: h0 = **50.403 MeV**, σh = **1.402 MeV**, Rh = **7.182 fm** (Table I).
- For 58Ni+58Ni: h0 = **98.278 MeV**, σh = **2.275 MeV**, Rh = **8.550 fm** (Table I).
- For 64Ni+64Ni: h0 = **92.646 MeV**, σh = **1.466 MeV**, Rh = **8.862 fm** (Table I).
- All one-neutron and two-neutron transfer Q-values from projectile to target (and from target to projectile) are **negative** for all seven reactions studied (Table II), confirming absence of neutron transfer channels.

## Context

This work extends the diffused-barrier approach of Winther (2004) and Capurro et al. (2011) to a broader set of lighter systems of astrophysical interest, including reactions relevant to carbon and oxygen burning in heavy stars. The authors compare their results to more sophisticated coupled-channel calculations (citing work by Rowley et al. (1991), Stelson et al. (1995, 2006, 2007), and others) and find comparable or better agreement with fewer parameters. The extracted parameters vary smoothly with Coulomb parameter and mass number, enabling extrapolation to unmeasured systems. The finding that fusion hindrance persists even with positive fusion Q-values due to small σh has implications for thermonuclear reaction rates in stellar evolution, and the formula can be used as an ingredient in the sticking-diffusion-survival model for superheavy element production (citing Adamian et al. (1993)).
