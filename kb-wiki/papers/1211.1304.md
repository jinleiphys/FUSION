# Synthesis of thin-long heavy nuclei in ternary collisions

> We illustrate the formation of a thin-long structure of heavy nuclei by three-nucleus simultaneous collisions within time-dependent density functional theory. The impact parameter dependence for such formation is systematically demonstrated through clarifications of the difference between binary and ternary collision events. A new method for producing thin-long heavy nuclei in the laboratory is suggested, as well as the possible formation of the thin-long structure in hot dense matter such as that encountered in core collapse supernovae.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that simultaneous three-nucleus collisions can produce thin-long heavy nuclei (length 25-30 fm) that are stabilized by rotation, and they suggest this ternary fusion mechanism may be more efficient than binary collisions for synthesizing superheavy elements, provided the incident energy is at an optimal value.

## Method

The authors performed three-dimensional time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) calculations using Skyrme-type effective nucleon-nucleon interactions (SLy6 and SKI3 parameter sets) to simulate collisions of three identical ⁵⁶Fe nuclei. They systematically varied the impact parameter (|b| = 1, 3, 5, 7, 9 fm) and compared the resulting fusion products with binary collisions of the same nuclei, analyzing the length and rotational stabilization of the compound nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Incident energy for ternary collisions: 1.47 MeV per nucleon in the center-of-mass frame
- Incident energy for binary collisions: 2.20 MeV per nucleon in the center-of-mass frame
- Fusion product for ternary collisions: ¹⁶⁸Pt (from three ⁵⁶Fe nuclei)
- Fusion product for binary collisions: ¹¹²Te (from two ⁵⁶Fe nuclei)
- Length parameter R/A¹/³ for ternary products (n=3): 4.98 fm, compared to 3.63 fm for binary products (n=2) and approximately 2.40 fm for a spherical nucleus
- Rotational energy for |b| = 7 fm ternary collision: approximately 2.10 MeV, with rotation up to π rad achieved in a few 10⁻²¹ s
- Critical mass number for stability transition from n=1 to n≥2: A₀(κ) = 212(κ/2)², where κ = A/Z
- For A=400 and κ=2.5: E(2.5,3,400) = 3.28 GeV, E(2.5,2,400) = 3.30 GeV, E(2.5,1,400) = 3.32 GeV
- For A=500 and κ=2.5: E(2.5,3,500) = 4.38 GeV, E(2.5,2,500) = 4.45 GeV, E(2.5,1,500) = 4.55 GeV

## Context

This work extends the study of nuclear fusion beyond the standard binary collision approach used in superheavy element synthesis, which typically relies on two-nucleus fusion or multi-nucleon transfer reactions. The authors connect their findings to the triple-alpha reaction mechanism in astrophysics (Clayton) and to earlier work on exotic clustering in light nuclei (Ichikawa 2011). The results suggest a new experimental pathway for superheavy synthesis using simultaneous three-nucleus collisions, and they open the question of whether such thin-long structures could form in hot dense matter such as core-collapse supernova environments, where plasma effects (electron screening and many-body Coulomb correlations) might lower the optimal incident energy and enhance the fusion rate.
