# First order and stable relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics

> Relativistic thermodynamics is derived from kinetic equilibrium in a general frame. Based on a novel interpretation of Lagrange multipliers in the equilibrium state we obtain a generic stable but first order relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics. Although this was believed to be impossible, we circumvent this difficulty by a specific handling of the heat flow.

# First order and stable relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics

## Key claim

The authors derive a first-order relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics that is generically stable, contrary to the long-held belief that such a theory is impossible. This is achieved by a specific handling of the heat flow through a generalized Gibbs relation that includes the rest-frame momentum density as an additional thermodynamic variable.

## Method

The authors start from kinetic theory for a single-component Boltzmann gas and reinterpret the Lagrange multipliers in the equilibrium distribution function, decomposing the vectorial invariant βᵃ into components parallel and perpendicular to the velocity field. From this they derive a generalized Gibbs relation de + (qₐ/h)dqᵃ = Tds + μdn, where h = e+p is the enthalpy density, and then compute the entropy production to obtain linear Onsager constitutive relations. The generic stability of the resulting first-order equations is proved via linear perturbation analysis in the Eckart frame using Routh-Hurwitz criteria.

## Key numbers

- The condition for stability by missing accelerating term requires ∂e/∂μ|_T > 0, which may be violated for Van der Waals gases.
- The determinant of matrix R gives the condition (h + k²(η̃λ̃T/h))Γ + η̃k² = 0, yielding negative real Γ solutions for all wave numbers k.
- The Routh-Hurwitz criteria are fulfilled if transport coefficients λ, η, ηᵥ are nonnegative and thermodynamic stability inequalities hold: ∂ₑT > 0, ∂ₙ(μ/T) > 0, and ∂ₑT ∂ₙ(μ/T) − (∂ₙT/T)² ≥ 0.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous generalization of relativistic thermodynamics (Ván 2008, Ván and Biró 2008) and directly addresses the instability and acausality problems of Eckart theory identified by Hiscock and Lindblom (1985). It contrasts with the Israel-Stewart theory (Israel 1976, Israel and Stewart 1976, 1979), which is only conditionally stable with complicated conditions lacking simple physical interpretation. The approach differs from that of García-Colín and Sandoval-Villalbazo (2006) and Tsumura, Kunihiro and Onoshi (2006, 2008, 2011) by not omitting the acceleration-related term but instead compensating its destabilizing effect with other terms. The work enables a first-order relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics that preserves generic stability under only natural thermodynamic requirements.
