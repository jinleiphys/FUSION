# Nuclear Physics Confronts Relativistic Collisions Of Isobars

> High-energy collisions involving the $A=96$ isobars $^{96}$Zr and $^{96}$Ru have been performed in 2018 at Brookhaven National Laboratory's Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) as a means to search for the chiral magnetic effect in QCD. This would manifest itself as specific deviations from unity in the ratio of observables taken between $^{96}$Zr+$^{96}$Zr and $^{96}$Ru+$^{96}$Ru collisions. Measurements of such ratios (released at the end of 2021) indeed reveal deviations from unity, but these are primarily caused by the two collided isobars having different radial profiles and intrinsic deformations. To make progress in understanding RHIC data, nuclear physicists across the energy spectrum gathered in Heidelberg in 2022 as part of an EMMI Rapid Reaction Task Force (RRTF) to address the following question. Does the combined effort of low-energy nuclear structure physics and high-energy heavy-ion physics enable us to understand the observations made in isobar collisions at RHIC?

## Key claim

The deviations from unity observed in ratios of observables between $^{96}$Ru+$^{96}$Ru and $^{96}$Zr+$^{96}$Zr collisions at RHIC are primarily caused by the two isobars having different radial profiles and intrinsic deformations, not by the chiral magnetic effect; any quantitative conclusion about the impact of the strong magnetic field on measured collective flow is premature without proper uncertainty quantification on the knowledge of the nuclear geometries.

## Method

The EMMI Rapid Reaction Task Force gathered nuclear physicists from low-energy nuclear structure and high-energy heavy-ion communities in Heidelberg in 2022 to assess whether combined expertise could explain the STAR collaboration's isobar collision data. The approach involved comparing predictions from energy density functional calculations of nuclear structure with initial-state and hydrodynamic computations of heavy-ion collisions, using the Glauber Monte Carlo framework where nucleon positions are the relevant degrees of freedom for high-energy nuclear collisions.

## Key numbers

* The STAR collaboration obtained ratios of observables between Ru+Ru and Zr+Zr collisions with a relative precision of 0.4% or lower
* The $v_3$ of Zr+Zr collisions is higher by about 10% in the central limit compared to Ru+Ru collisions
* Changing the nuclear skin thickness parameter $a$ by 0.18 fm (from 0.60 fm to 0.42 fm) caused eccentricities to vary by more than 10% in uranium-uranium collisions
* The $A=96$ isobars differ by 4 units in electric charge ($^{96}_{44}$Ru and $^{96}_{40}$Zr)

## Context

This work extends the earlier BNL Task Force from 2016 (Koch et al., 2016) which concluded that nuclear structure effects would not challenge the isobar program, but did not consider octupole deformation or skin thickness differences. The paper contradicts the assumption that collective flow would be identical in the two collision systems. The findings enable unprecedented precision tests of paradigms underlying nuclear collisions around and above the GeV scale, and the task force recommends using the $^{136}_{54}$Xe-$^{136}_{58}$Ce pair for future isobar runs due to the near-spherical shape of $^{136}$Xe.
