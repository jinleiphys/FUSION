# Nuclear fusion in dense matter: Reaction rate and carbon burning

> In this paper we analyze the nuclear fusion rate between equal nuclei for all five different nuclear burning regimes in dense matter (two thermonuclear regimes, two pycnonuclear ones, and the intermediate regime). The rate is determined by Coulomb barrier penetration in dense environments and by the astrophysical S-factor at low energies. We evaluate previous studies of the Coulomb barrier problem and propose a simple phenomenological formula for the reaction rate which covers all cases. The parameters of this formula can be varied, taking into account current theoretical uncertainties in the reaction rate. The results are illustrated for the example of the ^{12}C+^{12}C fusion reaction. This reaction is very important for the understanding of nuclear burning in evolved stars, in exploding white dwarfs producing type Ia supernovae, and in accreting neutron stars. The S-factor at stellar energies depends on a reliable fit and extrapolation of the experimental data. We calculate the energy dependence of the S-factor using a recently developed parameter-free model for the nuclear interaction, taking into account the effects of the Pauli nonlocality. For illustration, we analyze the efficiency of carbon burning in a wide range of densities and temperatures of stellar matter with the emphasis on carbon ignition at densities rho > 10^9 g/cc.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a simple phenomenological formula for the nuclear fusion reaction rate that covers all five burning regimes in dense matter (two thermonuclear, two pycnonuclear, and the intermediate regime), but the authors note that the theoretical reaction rates remain rather uncertain, especially at high densities, due to both nuclear physics uncertainties in the S-factor (within a factor of about 3.5) and plasma physics uncertainties in Coulomb barrier penetration that are even larger.

## Method

The authors calculated the astrophysical S-factor for 12C+12C fusion using the Sao Paulo potential, a parameter-free model for the nuclear interaction based on nonlocal quantum effects, with nuclear density distributions obtained from relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory using the NL2 effective interaction. They then analyzed the Coulomb barrier penetration problem for identical nuclei in dense matter by evaluating previous studies and constructing a phenomenological reaction rate formula that accounts for plasma screening effects using a mean-force plasma potential approach, with the screening enhancement factor derived from classical Monte Carlo simulations of strongly coupled Coulomb systems.

## Key numbers

- The S-factor for 12C+12C at energies E ≤ 19.8 MeV is fitted by S(E) = 5.15 × 10^16 exp{-0.428E - 3E^0.308/[1+exp(0.613(8-E))]} MeV barn, with a formal maximum fit error of 16% at E=5.8 MeV
- The S-factor values from the NL2 model are uncertain within a factor of approximately 3.5 in the limit E → 0
- The screening enhancement factor h0 for a Coulomb liquid at 1 ≤ Γ ≤ 170 is given by h0 = 1.0563 Γ + 1.0208 Γ^0.3231 - 0.2748 ln Γ - 1.0843
- The enhancement factor e^h0 reaches values as high as approximately 10^74 at Γ ∼ 170
- The difference between the enhancement factor from Eq. (6) of Ogata et al. (1991) and the authors' fit reaches a factor of approximately 40 for Γ ∼ 170
- The Ogata (1997) PIMC result for e^h0 differs from the authors' fit by a maximum factor of approximately 6 at Γ=170
- Carbon burning in evolved massive stars occurs at typical densities ρ ∼ 10^5 g cm^-3 and temperatures T ∼ (6-8) × 10^8 K
- Carbon ignition in type Ia supernovae occurs at T ∼ (1.5-7) × 10^8 K and ρ ∼ (2-5) × 10^9 g cm^-3
- Carbon ignition in accreting neutron star crusts requires ρ ∼ 1.3 × 10^9 g cm^-3 and initial temperature T ≳ 10^9 K

## Context

This work extends the seminal study of Salpeter and Van Horn (1969) on nuclear fusion in dense matter by providing updated calculations of the astrophysical S-factor using the parameter-free Sao Paulo potential model and by proposing a unified phenomenological reaction rate formula. The paper enables improved modeling of carbon burning in evolved massive stars, type Ia supernova explosions in accreting white dwarfs, and superburst ignition in accreting neutron star crusts, while also identifying that plasma physics uncertainties in Coulomb barrier penetration are currently larger than nuclear physics uncertainties in the S-factor.
