# Resonance contributions to HBT correlation radii

> We study the effect of resonance decays on intensity interferometry for heavy ion collisions. Collective expansion of the source leads to a dependence of the two-particle correlation function on the pair momentum K. This opens the possibility to reconstruct the dynamics of the source from the K-dependence of the measured HBT radii. Here we address the question to what extent resonance decays can fake such a flow signal. Within a simple parametrization for the emission function we present a comprehensive analysis of the interplay of flow and resonance decays on the one- and two-particle spectra. We discuss in detail the non-Gaussian features of the correlation function introduced by long-lived resonances and the resulting problems in extracting meaningful HBT radii. We propose to define them in terms of the second order q-moments of the correlator C(q, K). We show that this yields a more reliable characterisation of the correlator in terms of its width and the correlation strength `lambda' than other commonly used fit procedures. The normalized fourth-order q-moments (kurtosis) provide a quantitative measure for the non-Gaussian features of the correlator. At least for the class of models studied here, the kurtosis helps separating effects from expansion flow and resonance decays, and provides the cleanest signal to distinguish between scenarios with and without transverse flow.

## Key claim

Resonance decays can introduce non-Gaussian features into the two-pion correlation function that fake or mask the momentum-dependent signal from collective expansion flow; the authors propose that using second-order q-moments to define HBT radii, together with the fourth-order kurtosis, provides a cleaner separation of flow and resonance decay effects than standard Gaussian fitting procedures, at least within the class of models studied.

## Method

The authors constructed a simple analytical model for the emission function assuming local thermalization at freeze-out with longitudinal and transverse collective expansion, a finite duration of particle emission, and thermal excitation of hadronic resonances. They computed the one- and two-particle spectra by summing direct pion emission with contributions from 18 resonance decay channels (including rho, omega, eta, Delta, K*, Sigma*, Lambda, and others), treating the decay kinematics exactly via an exponential proper-time distribution along the resonance trajectories. The two-particle correlation function was then analyzed using both standard Gaussian fits and an alternative method based on second-order q-moments (to define HBT radii and intercept parameter) and normalized fourth-order q-moments (kurtosis) to quantify non-Gaussian features.

## Key numbers

- The omega meson has a lifetime of 23.4 fm/c and contributes up to about 10% of all pions at low transverse momentum (f_omega(K=0) approximately 0.1).
- The model uses a freeze-out temperature T = 150 MeV, transverse source radius R = 5 fm, longitudinal rapidity width Delta-eta = 1.2, proper freeze-out time tau_0 = 5 fm/c, and emission duration Delta-tau = 1 fm/c.
- The omega meson has mass 782 MeV, decay width 8.43 MeV, and branching ratio 0.89 to pi- pi+ pi0.
- The rho meson has mass 770 MeV, decay width 150 MeV, and spin J=1.
- The Delta(1232) has mass 1232 MeV, decay width 115 MeV, and spin J=3/2.
- The K*(892) has mass 892 MeV, decay width 50 MeV, and spin J=1.
- The eta meson has mass 547 MeV, decay width 1.2 x 10^{-3} MeV, and branching ratio 0.24 to pi- pi+ pi0.
- The K_S^0 has mass 498 MeV and branching ratio 0.69 to pi+ pi-.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of HBT interferometry for dynamically expanding sources (Pratt 1984, 1986; Makhlin and Sinyukov 1988; Csorgo and collaborators 1994-1996; Wiedemann, Heinz, and collaborators 1996) by systematically analyzing how resonance decays contaminate the flow signal in two-pion correlations. It complements hydrodynamic simulations with sharp freeze-out hypersurfaces (Marb) by using a flexible analytical model that allows independent variation of temperature and transverse flow velocity. The paper enables future experimental analyses to use the kurtosis as a diagnostic tool to distinguish expansion flow from resonance decay effects, and provides a more reliable definition of HBT radii via q-moments rather than Gaussian fits when the correlation function is non-Gaussian.
