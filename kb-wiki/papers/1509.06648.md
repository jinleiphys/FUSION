# FRIGA, A New Approach To Identify Isotopes and Hypernuclei In N-Body Transport Models

> We present a new algorithm to identify fragments in computer simulations of relativistic heavy ion collisions. It is based on the simulated annealing technique and can be applied to n-body transport models like the Quantum Molecular Dynamics. This new approach is able to predict isotope yields as well as hyper-nucleus production. In order to illustrate its predicting power, we confront this new method to experimental data, and show the sensitivity on the parameters which govern the cluster formation.

# FRIGA, A New Approach To Identify Isotopes and Hypernuclei In N-Body Transport Models

## Key claim

The FRIGA algorithm can predict absolute isotope and hypernucleus yields from transport model simulations, but only when the cluster formation is evaluated at early times (t=60 fm/c) and the fragments are produced at sub-saturation densities (around ρ₀/2 for intermediate mass fragments), making the predictions sensitive to the low-density behavior of the nuclear potentials rather than the full equation of state.

## Method

The authors extend the Simulated Annealing Clusterisation Algorithm (SACA) by adding asymmetry energy, pairing, and nuclear structure contributions to the binding energy used in the annealing optimization. The algorithm identifies the combination of fragments and free nucleons that maximizes total binding energy at a given time during the reaction, using a Skyrme interaction supplemented by Yukawa potential for nucleon-nucleon interactions. For hypernuclei, the ΛN potential is taken as 2/3 of the nucleon-nucleon potential, and excited primary fragments may undergo sequential secondary decay via the GEMINI algorithm.

## Key numbers

- Asymmetry energy parameter: E₀ = 23.3 MeV with γ = 1 ("stiff" asymmetry potential)
- Pairing energy for light isotopes: Δ_pairing = 12 MeV for ⁴He and 6.9 MeV for ³He; for heavy nuclei Δ_pairing = 12/√A MeV
- Primary fragment internal density: ρ = ρ₀/2 for intermediate mass fragments, ρ = ρ₀/5 for light Z<3 isotopes
- Mean excitation energy of intermediate mass fragments: between 0 and 3 A.MeV for beam energies 50 A.MeV to 1 A.GeV
- Yield ratio Y(³_ΛH)/Y(⁴_ΛH) from IQMD-FRIGA: 1.3 ± 0.2 (without asymmetry energy), 1.9 ± 0.4 (with asymmetry energy), compared to experimental value 1.4 ± 0.8 from HyPHI Collaboration
- Hypernucleus yield reduction from V_ΛN = (2/3)V_nN factor: approximately 20 percent on average

## Context

This work extends the SACA approach developed by Dorso (1993) and Puri (2000), which had been successfully applied to understand fragment charge distributions and bimodality in heavy ion collisions. The FRIGA algorithm enables predictions for isotope-specific yields and hypernucleus production that were previously beyond the scope of transport models using simple coalescence or minimum spanning tree methods. The authors compare their results to experimental data from the HyPHI Collaboration for ⁶Li+¹²C collisions at 2 A.GeV, and the approach can be applied to any n-body transport model such as QMD or PHSD for studying fragment formation mechanisms and the density dependence of the asymmetry energy.
