# Initialization of hydrodynamics in relativistic heavy ion collisions with an energy-momentum transport model

> A key ingredient of hydrodynamical modeling of relativistic heavy ion collisions is thermal initial conditions, an input that is the consequence of a pre-thermal dynamics which is not completely understood yet. In the paper we employ a recently developed energy-momentum transport model of the pre-thermal stage to study influence of the alternative initial states in nucleus-nucleus collisions on flow and energy density distributions of the matter at the starting time of hydrodynamics. In particular, the dependence of the results on isotropic and anisotropic initial states is analyzed. It is found that at the thermalization time the transverse flow is larger and the maximal energy density is higher for the longitudinally squeezed initial momentum distributions. The results are also sensitive to the relaxation time parameter, equation of state at the thermalization time, and transverse profile of initial energy density distribution: Gaussian approximation, Glauber Monte Carlo profiles, etc. Also, test results ensure that the numerical code based on the energy-momentum transport model is capable of providing both averaged and fluctuating initial conditions for the hydrodynamic simulations of relativistic nuclear collisions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the choice of isotropic versus anisotropic initial momentum-space distributions significantly affects the energy density and transverse flow at the thermalization time, with longitudinally squeezed initial distributions producing larger transverse flow and higher maximal energy density; however, these results are conditional on the specific values of the relaxation time parameter, equation of state, and transverse profile of the initial energy density distribution.

## Method

The authors employ an energy-momentum transport model that interpolates between free-streaming and hydrodynamic evolution using a relaxation-time parameter, solving the energy-momentum conservation equations with a source term derived from the relaxation dynamics. They modify a 2+1 dimensional viscous hydrodynamic code to include these source terms, assuming longitudinal boost invariance, and evolve the system from an initial time of 0.1 fm/c to a thermalization time of 1 fm/c. The initial phase-space density is parametrized with a Gaussian transverse spatial profile and a momentum-space anisotropy parameter λ = λ⊥/λ∥, with values of 1 (isotropic), 0.01 (large longitudinal pressure), and 100 (small longitudinal pressure, similar to Color Glass Condensate initial conditions).

## Key numbers

- Initial energy density at the center of the system is normalized to 1000 GeV fm⁻³ at τ₀ = 0.1 fm/c
- Default relaxation time parameter τ_rel = 0.5 fm/c
- Initial time τ₀ = 0.1 fm/c and thermalization time τ_th = 1 fm/c
- Gaussian transverse radius parameters R_x = R_y = R = 5.33 fm
- Fixed transverse momentum scale λ⊥ = 1.4 GeV for all calculations
- For λ = 1/3, the hydrodynamic and free-streaming energy densities at τ_th are approximately equal: ε_HM(τ_th) ≈ ε_FS(τ_th)
- For λ = 1 (isotropic case), the final energy density in the relaxation model lies between the hydrodynamic model (minimal values) and free-streaming model (maximal values)
- For λ = 0.01 (large longitudinal pressure), the smallest energy density values occur in free-streaming and the maximal ones in hydrodynamic expansion
- For λ = 100 (small longitudinal pressure, CGC-like), the maximal energy density at thermalization time is the highest among the three cases

## Context

This work extends the energy-momentum transport model proposed in a prior paper (Preth, 2014) to study how different initial momentum-space anisotropies affect the initial conditions for hydrodynamic simulations of relativistic heavy ion collisions. The approach contrasts with anisotropic hydrodynamics methods (Martinez and Strickland, 2010; Florkowski and Ryblewski, 2011) that rely on the Romatschke-Strickland ansatz, which the authors argue is not well justified beyond 0+1 dimensions, especially for event-by-event calculations with inhomogeneous transverse profiles. The results enable more realistic initialization of hydrodynamic codes such as those used by Karpenko (2014) for modeling RHIC and LHC collisions, allowing systematic studies of pre-equilibrium dynamics on final observables.
