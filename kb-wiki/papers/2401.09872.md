# A pulsar in a binary with a compact object in the mass gap between neutron stars and black holes

> Among the compact objects observed in gravitational wave merger events a few have masses in the gap between the most massive neutron stars (NSs) and least massive black holes (BHs) known. Their nature and the formation of their merging binaries are not well understood. We report on pulsar timing observations using the Karoo Array Telescope (MeerKAT) of PSR J0514-4002E, an eccentric binary millisecond pulsar in the globular cluster NGC 1851 with a total binary mass of $3.887 \pm 0.004$ solar masses. The companion to the pulsar is a compact object and its mass (between $2.09$ and $2.71$ solar masses, 95% confidence interval) is in the mass gap, so it either is a very massive NS or a low-mass BH. We propose the companion was formed by a merger between two earlier NSs.

## Key claim

The companion of PSR J0514-4002E has a mass between 2.09 and 2.71 solar masses (95% confidence interval), placing it in the mass gap between known neutron stars and black holes; the authors propose this companion was formed by a merger between two earlier neutron stars, but cannot determine whether it is a very massive neutron star or a low-mass black hole.

## Method

The authors conducted 24 pulsar timing observations of PSR J0514-4002E using the MeerKAT radio telescope between January 2021 and August 2022, combined with six archival observations from the Green Bank Telescope between 2005 and 2006. They measured the rate of periastron advance from the timing model to derive the total system mass, and used Bayesian analysis of the mass function, Shapiro delay upper limits, and Einstein delay upper limits to constrain the individual component masses. Near-ultraviolet and optical observations with the Hubble Space Telescope were used to search for and exclude a luminous stellar companion.

## Key numbers

- Total binary mass: 3.887 ± 0.004 solar masses, derived from the relativistic periastron advance rate of 0.034676 ± 0.000031 degrees per year
- Companion mass: between 2.09 and 2.71 solar masses (95% confidence interval), with Bayesian median of 2.35 (+0.20, -0.18) solar masses
- Pulsar mass: Bayesian median of 1.53 (+0.18, -0.20) solar masses
- Orbital inclination: Bayesian median of 52 (+6, -5) degrees, with 95% upper limit of 62 degrees
- Orbital period: 7.4478966582 ± 0.0000000072 days
- Orbital eccentricity: 0.70793232 ± 0.00000085
- Pulsar spin period: 5.595947418100 ± 0.000000000027 milliseconds
- 95% confidence upper limits: Shapiro delay orthometric amplitude h3 < 1.48 microseconds, Einstein delay gamma_E < 25 milliseconds

## Context

This work extends the population of compact objects in the mass gap between neutron stars and black holes, previously observed only in gravitational wave merger events such as GW190814 (Abbott et al., 2020), GW190917, and GW200210 (Abbott et al., 2021). The total system mass of 3.887 solar masses exceeds that of the most massive double neutron star known in the Milky Way, PSR J1913+1102 (Ferdman et al., 2020), and the heaviest DNS merger detected in gravitational waves, GW190425 (Abbott et al., 2020). The detection enables future tests of general relativity and studies of binary evolution in globular clusters, particularly the formation of massive compact objects through neutron star mergers in dense stellar environments.
