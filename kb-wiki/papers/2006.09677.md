# Study of Charge Radii with Neural Networks

> A feed-forward neural network model is trained to calculate the nuclear charge radii. The model trained with input data set of proton and neutron number $Z,N$, the electric quadrupole transition strength $B(E2)$ from the first excited 2$^+$ state to the ground state, together with the symmetry energy. The model reproduces well not only the isotope dependence of charge radii, but also the kinks of charge radii at the neutron magic numbers $N=82$ for Sn and Sm isotopes, and also $N=126$ for Pb isotopes. The important role of $B(E2)$ value is pointed out to reproduce the kink of the isotope dependence of charge radii in these nuclei. Moreover, with the inclusion of the symmetry energy term in the inputs, the charge radii of Ca isotopes are well reproduced. This result suggests a new correlation between the symmetry energy and charge radii of Ca isotopes. The Skyrme HFB calculation is performed to confirm the existence of this correlation in a microscopic model.

# Study of Charge Radii with Neural Networks

## Key claim
A feed-forward neural network trained on proton number, neutron number, and a preprocessed combination of B(E2) values and symmetry energy can reproduce both the overall isotope dependence and the kinks at N=82 and N=126 for heavy isotopes, but the inclusion of the symmetry energy term is critical specifically for reproducing the charge radii of Ca isotopes, which the model without symmetry energy fails to describe qualitatively.

## Method
A fully connected feed-forward neural network with structure [3,40,1] (44 neurons, 201 parameters) was trained on 347 nuclei using RMSProp optimization. The three input features were proton number Z, neutron number N, and a transformed function g(B(E2),δ_S) that combines the electric quadrupole transition strength B(E2) (calculated from the Grodzin's formula using experimental E(2₁⁺) energies) with a symmetry-energy-related factor derived from the Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula. The network was trained 10 times with random training/testing splits, and for each training the output was averaged over 50 random initializations.

## Key numbers
- Total data set: 347 nuclei with both experimental E(2₁⁺) and charge radii available
- Network structure: [3,40,1] with 201 trainable parameters
- Best model with symmetry energy: RMSD = 0.0286 fm (training set), 0.0280 fm (testing set)
- Best model without symmetry energy: RMSD = 0.0266 fm (training set), 0.0231 fm (testing set)
- Lorentzian smoothing width Γ = 7.0 for preprocessing E(2₁⁺) values
- Skyrme-HFB calculations used SAMi-J family with J values of 27, 28, 28.16, and 31 MeV, with fixed incompressibility K∞ = 245 MeV and effective mass m*/m = 0.675

## Context
This work extends prior phenomenological charge radius models (Duflo liquid-drop model, Garvey-Kelson relation) and machine learning approaches (Ma 2019, Utama 2016) by introducing B(E2) and symmetry energy as explicit inputs. The neural network reproduces kinks at N=82 for Sn and Sm isotopes and N=126 for Pb isotopes that were previously studied experimentally by Gorges (2019), Anselment (1986), and Cocolios (2011). The finding that symmetry energy correlates with Ca isotope charge radii is new; the authors confirm this correlation microscopically using Skyrme-HFB calculations with the SAMi-J family of interactions (Roca-Maza 2012). The ML model successfully reproduces the Ca isotope trend including the kink at N=28 and peak at N=24, but the HFB calculations, while showing sensitivity to the symmetry energy parameter J, do not reproduce the N=28 kink structure, leaving the physical mechanism as an open question.
