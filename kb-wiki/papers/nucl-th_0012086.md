# Relativistic NN scattering equations without partial-wave decomposition

## Key claim

Within the spectator (Gross) quasi-potential formalism, an almost perfect cancellation occurs between retardation effects and negative-energy state contributions, causing the spectator amplitude with retardation to nearly coincide with the non-relativistic limit; this agreement is not perfect and deteriorates with increasing energy.

## Method

The authors solved three-dimensional reductions of the Bethe-Salpeter equation, the Gross (spectator), Blankenbecler-Sugar (BbS), and Equal-Time equations, for nucleon-nucleon scattering using the Malfliet-Tjon potential, without performing a partial-wave decomposition. Instead, they solved the integral equation in two dimensions (momentum and scattering angle) after factoring out the azimuthal angle, and compared the resulting scattering amplitudes at 300 MeV and 800 MeV to non-relativistic Lippmann-Schwinger results.

## Key numbers

- At 300 MeV (low energy) and 800 MeV (high energy), relativistic effects are quantified for the BbS, spectator/Gross, and Equal-Time equations, with deviations from non-relativistic results increasing with energy.
- The spectator/Gross amplitude with retardation effects included becomes closer to the non-relativistic limit than the Equal-Time amplitude at both energies.
- The decomposition of the Gross propagator shows that negative-energy effects (Gross +-) increase with energy, while retardation contributions have the opposite effect, leading to an almost perfect cancellation.

## Context

This work extends the non-relativistic two-nucleon calculations of Elster (1998) for scalar particles interacting via the Malfliet-Tjon potential, which were solved without partial-wave decomposition and used in three-nucleon system calculations. The authors reproduced those non-relativistic results and then compared them with three different relativistic quasi-potential equations. The approach enables future covariant two-nucleon calculations that incorporate the full Dirac structure of the nucleon, as pursued by Elster (2000) with realistic NN interactions.
