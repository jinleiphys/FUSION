# Hadron physics with functional methods

> We give a pedagogical introduction to hadron spectroscopy and structure studies using functional methods. We explain the basic features of Dyson-Schwinger, Bethe-Salpeter and Faddeev equations, which are employed to calculate the spectra of mesons, baryons and four-quark states. We discuss dynamical mass generation as a consequence of the spontaneous breaking of chiral symmetry, which is intertwined with the emergence of the light pions as Goldstone bosons of QCD. We highlight the importance of diquark correlations in the baryon sector, while for four-quark states such as the light scalar mesons and heavy exotics the dominant two-body clusters are typically mesons. We conclude with a brief discussion of hadron matrix elements like electromagnetic form factors and how vector-meson dominance is an automatic outcome of functional equations.

## Key claim

This pedagogical review argues that functional methods (Dyson-Schwinger, Bethe-Salpeter, and Faddeev equations) provide a viable nonperturbative framework for calculating hadron spectra and structure, complementing lattice QCD, though the approach relies on truncations and model-dependent effective interactions for the gluon propagator and quark-gluon vertex rather than being fully first-principles.

## Method

The paper derives and solves Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) for the quark propagator, which are coupled integral equations relating QCD's n-point correlation functions, using a momentum-subtraction renormalization scheme. Bethe-Salpeter equations (BSEs) are then employed as eigenvalue equations to extract meson masses and amplitudes from the quark-antiquark four-point function at its poles, while Faddeev equations handle baryons and four-quark states through diquark or meson cluster decompositions.

## Key numbers

- Light up and down current-quark masses are only about 2–5 MeV from the Higgs mechanism, yet the proton mass is 938 MeV, demonstrating dynamical mass generation.
- The pion mass is about 140 MeV, much smaller than other hadron masses of order 1 GeV, reflecting its role as a pseudo-Goldstone boson.
- In the Munczek-Nemirovsky model, the chiral-symmetry breaking solution yields a quark mass function M(p^2) = sqrt(Lambda^2 - p^2) with A(p^2) = 2, where Lambda is the model scale.

## Context

This work extends the functional methods approach reviewed in Eichmann (2016) and Eichmann (2020), contrasting with lattice QCD (Gattringer, 2010) which computes correlation functions directly from the path integral. The pedagogical treatment enables future calculations of hadron matrix elements like electromagnetic form factors and parton distributions, and highlights that vector-meson dominance emerges automatically from the functional equations rather than being imposed externally.
