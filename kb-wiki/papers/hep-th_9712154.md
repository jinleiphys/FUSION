# About Symmetries in Physics

> The goal of this introduction to symmetries is to present some general ideas, to outline the fundamental concepts and results of the subject and to situate a bit the following lectures of this school. [These notes represent the write-up of a lecture presented at the fifth ``Seminaire Rhodanien de Physique: Sur les Symetries en Physique" held at Dolomieu (France), 17-21 March 1997. Up to the appendix and the graphics, it is to be published in "Symmetries in Physics", F.Gieres, M.Kibler,C.Lucchesi and O.Piguet, eds. (Editions Frontieres, 1998).]

## Key claim

This lecture-note introduction argues that symmetries are fundamental to the structure of physical theories, but the presentation is pedagogical and qualitative rather than advancing a specific new result; the abstract's claim to "present some general ideas" and "outline fundamental concepts" is accurate, with the caveat that the paper is a school lecture write-up and therefore contains no original research findings.

## Method

The paper surveys the classification of symmetries in physics, distinguishing geometric (space-time) symmetries from internal symmetries, and reviews the mathematical description using Lie groups and Lie algebras. It discusses the implementation of symmetries through representations in quantum mechanics, the implications of Noether's theorem for conservation laws, and different manifestations such as broken and approximate symmetries, with illustrative examples drawn from classical mechanics, quantum mechanics, and relativistic field theory.

## Key numbers

- No numerical results are presented; the paper is a pedagogical review with no original experimental or theoretical numerics.

## Context

This paper extends the tradition of pedagogical introductions to symmetries in physics exemplified by Weyl (1952) and Feynman (1965), and it was written to situate the other lectures at the fifth "Seminaire Rhodanien de Physique: Sur les Symetries en Physique" held in Dolomieu, France in March 1997. The notes enable students and non-specialists to follow the more specialized lectures in the same volume, covering topics from conformal field theories (relevant to string theory and critical phenomena) to supersymmetry and the super Poincare algebra.
