# Model-independent determination of nuclear weak form factors and implications for Standard Model precision tests

> We analyze the recoil corrections in superallowed beta decays of $T=1$, $J^P=0^+$ nuclei by fixing the mean square charge weak radius model-independently using the data of multiple charge radii across the nuclear isotriplet. By comparing to model estimations, we argue that the existing theory uncertainty in the statistical rate function $f$ might have been substantially underestimated. We discuss the implications of our proposed strategy for precision tests of Standard Model, including a potential alleviation of the first-row CKM unitarity deficit, and motivate new experiments for charge radii measurements.

# Model-independent determination of nuclear weak form factors and implications for Standard Model precision tests

## Key claim
The existing theory uncertainty in the statistical rate function \(f\) for superallowed beta decays may have been substantially underestimated because all prior analyses used simplified nuclear models to handle the charged weak form factor, rather than a model-independent determination from measured charge radii across the nuclear isotriplet. The author argues that this systematic error could reach \(10^{-3}\) for medium and heavy nuclei, and that correcting it could potentially alleviate the first-row CKM unitarity deficit.

## Method
The author derives an isospin relation (Eq. 8) that expresses the mean square charged weak radius \(R_\text{CW}^2\) of the parent-daughter transition in terms of the measured charge radii of two nuclei within the same \(T=1\) isotriplet, using the Wigner-Eckart theorem and neglecting isospin symmetry breaking corrections. This model-independent \(R_\text{CW}^2\) is then inserted into the shape factor \(S\) of the differential decay rate to compute the total decay rate \(\Gamma\) for 13 superallowed transitions, and the results are compared to those obtained using two model-dependent approaches: setting \(R_\text{CW}=R_\text{Ch,1}\) (the charge radius of the most stable \(T_z=+1\) isotope) and using Wilkinson's shell-model estimate (Eq. 9).

## Key numbers
- The recoil correction to the total decay rate is negative and ranges from \(-0.06\%\) (for \(^{18}\text{Ne}\)) to \(-1.17\%\) (for \(^{74}\text{Rb}\)).
- The systematic error from naively replacing \(R_\text{CW}\) by \(R_\text{Ch,1}\) ranges from \(-0.03\%\) (for \(^{18}\text{Ne}\)) to \(-0.35\%\) (for \(^{50}\text{Fe}\)).
- The systematic error from using Wilkinson's shell-model estimate (Eq. 9) ranges from \(-0.02\%\) (for \(^{18}\text{Ne}\)) to \(-0.29\%\) (for \(^{50}\text{Fe}\)).
- For \(^{38}\text{Ca}\), the model-independent \(R_\text{CW}^2\) is \(15.99(28)\ \text{fm}^2\), compared to \(R_\text{Ch,1}^2 = 11.579(13)\ \text{fm}^2\) for \(^{38}\text{Ar}\).
- For \(^{74}\text{Rb}\), the model-independent \(R_\text{CW}^2\) is \(19.5(5.5)\ \text{fm}^2\), compared to \(R_\text{Ch,1}^2 = 17.531(34)\ \text{fm}^2\) for \(^{74}\text{Kr}\).
- The current quoted relative uncertainty of \(f\) in Hardy and Towner (2020) is \(0.13\%\) for \(^{18}\text{Ne}\), \(0.01\%\) for \(^{34}\text{Ar}\), \(0.40\%\) for \(^{50}\text{Fe}\), and \(0.20\%\) for \(^{74}\text{Rb}\); in most cases the central values of the systematic error from modeling exceed these quoted uncertainties.

## Context
This work extends the idea first pointed out by Holstein (1974) to use charge radii from an isotriplet to determine the charged weak radius model-independently, but which was not seriously implemented in subsequent analyses. It directly challenges the assumption in Hardy and Towner (2020) and earlier reviews that the statistical rate function \(f\) is well under control, showing that the recoil correction from the charged weak form factor introduces systematic errors that may have been underestimated. The method enables a potential resolution of the first-row CKM unitarity deficit: if the overall \(\mathcal{F}t\)-value were reduced by the average of the central values (0.11%), the central value of \(|V_{ud}|_{0^+}\) would increase from 0.97367 to 0.97421, largely restoring unitarity. The work also motivates new experiments for charge radii measurements of rare isotopes at facilities such as BECOLA at FRIB and the muX experiment at PSI, particularly for isotriplets with \(A = 10, 14, 26, 30, 46, 62\) where one additional charge radius measurement would enable model-independent \(R_\text{CW}\) determination for eight more superallowed transitions.
