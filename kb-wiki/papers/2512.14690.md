# Drell-Yan at the Electron-Ion Collider

> The photon is arguably the most universally important particle across all fields of physics. Despite its status as a fundamental particle, at high energies the photon can be seen as a hadronic source of partons. The partonic content of the photon is very poorly constrained compared to that of the proton, with photon PDF uncertainties typically one or two orders of magnitude larger than their proton counterparts, despite the fact that its source, the $γ\to q\bar{q}$ splitting, is perturbatively calculable. The high luminosity, excellent particle identification, and far-backward electron tagging capabilities of the Electron-Ion Collider make it an ideal environment for studying photon parton distribution functions. Similar to the $p+p$ or $π+p$ systems, photoproduction at the EIC can be thought of as two parton distributions colliding. One of the most powerful processes in such collisions is production of lepton pairs, i.e. $h+p\rightarrow l^+l^-+X$, known as the Drell--Yan process. This process has the ability to access for the first time the transverse-momentum-dependent parton distributions of the photon. The transversely polarized proton beam of the EIC additionally provides a possible means of accessing the transversity distribution of the proton without relying on fragmentation functions.

## Key claim

The Electron-Ion Collider can measure the Drell-Yan process in photoproduction for the first time, providing access to transverse-momentum-dependent parton distributions of the photon and, with a transversely polarized proton beam, a novel means of accessing the proton transversity distribution without relying on fragmentation functions; however, the feasibility depends on the final implementation of the streaming readout data acquisition system for the dimuon channel.

## Method

The authors simulate the resolved photoproduction Drell-Yan process $\gamma p \rightarrow \ell^+\ell^- + X$ for the EIC 10x275 GeV beam configuration using the Sherpa 3.0.1 event generator with NLO resolved photoproduction, employing the Weizsäcker-Williams equivalent photon approximation with $Q^2_{\mathrm{max}} = 1$ GeV$^2$ and collinear initial-state photons. Detector effects are implemented by applying resolutions and acceptances approximating the ePIC detector performance, including the low-$Q^2$ tagger acceptance for tagged photoproduction. The analysis focuses on tagged photoproduction where the scattered electron is measured, providing sub-percent resolution on the inelasticity $y$, and uses the dilepton mass, rapidity, and transverse momentum to map the phase space of $x_\gamma$ and $x_p$.

## Key numbers

* Total cross section for Drell-Yan dimuon production at 10x275 GeV with $M_{\ell\ell} > 1$ GeV: 5.43$^{+1.34}_{-1.25}$ pb using the SAS1M photon PDF set, and 8.10$^{+0.47}_{-1.13}$ pb using the SAS2M set, with uncertainties from 7-point scale variations
* The SAS2M photon PDF set produces $\chi^2/\mathrm{ndf} = 1.01$ compared to $\chi^2/\mathrm{ndf} = 1.50$ for SAS1M when fit to world data on $F_2^\gamma$
* The design luminosity for the 10x275 GeV configuration is 100 fb$^{-1}$ per year, approximately a factor of 7 higher than the 18x275 configuration
* The resolution on $y_h$ in untagged photoproduction is $\sigma(y) \simeq 0.18$, roughly independent of $y$, with a non-negligible bias from acceptance losses
* The resolution on the momentum of the hadronic final state in the remnant hemisphere after detector effects is approximately 300 MeV, roughly constant with energy

## Context

This work extends the theoretical and phenomenological discussions of photoproduction Drell-Yan dating back to Jaffe (1971) and subsequent studies by Busenitz and Sullivan (1981), who described the cross section as "depressingly small" and measurement as "very difficult and perhaps impossible." The paper builds on recent progress in precise predictions for resolved photoproduction using Sherpa (Hoeche 2023), Pythia (Helenius 2017), and MadGraph (Manna 2024), which have identified the photon PDF as the dominant source of uncertainty in photoproduction cross section predictions. The measurement would complement dijet photoproduction studies at the EIC (Chu 2017) and provide the first experimental constraints on photon TMDs, while the transversity measurement would be complementary to semi-inclusive DIS (Pire 2009) without relying on fragmentation functions.
