# Characterizing SASI- and Convection-Dominated Core-Collapse Supernova Explosions in Two Dimensions

> The success of the neutrino mechanism of core-collapse supernovae relies on the supporting action of two hydrodynamic instabilities: neutrino-driven convection and the Standing Accretion Shock Instability (SASI). Depending on the structure of the stellar progenitor, each of these instabilities can dominate the evolution of the gain region prior to the onset of explosion, with implications for the ensuing asymmetries. Here we examine the flow dynamics in the neighborhood of explosion by means of parametric two-dimensional, time-dependent hydrodynamic simulations for which the linear stability properties are well understood. We find that systems for which the convection parameter is sub-critical (SASI-dominated) develop explosions once large-scale, high-entropy bubbles are able to survive for several SASI oscillation cycles. These long-lived structures are seeded by the SASI during shock expansions. Finite-amplitude initial perturbations do not alter this outcome qualitatively, though they can lead to significant differences in explosion times. Supercritical systems (convection-dominated) also explode by developing large-scale bubbles, though the formation of these structures is due to buoyant activity. Non-exploding systems achieve a quasi-steady state in which the time-averaged flow adjusts itself to be convectively sub-critical. We characterize the turbulent flow using a spherical Fourier-Bessel decomposition, identifying the relevant scalings and connecting temporal and spatial components. Finally, we verify the applicability of these principles on the general relativistic, radiation-hydrodynamic simulations of Mueller, Janka, & Heger (2012), and discuss implications for the three-dimensional case.

## Key claim

Systems where the convection parameter χ is sub-critical (SASI-dominated) develop explosions once large-scale, high-entropy bubbles survive for several SASI oscillation cycles, while supercritical systems (convection-dominated) also explode by developing large-scale bubbles, but through buoyant activity rather than SASI seeding.

## Method

The authors performed parametric two-dimensional, time-dependent hydrodynamic simulations using FLASH3.2, solving conservation equations in spherical polar coordinates with parameterized neutrino heating and cooling, an ideal gas equation of state (γ=4/3), and a point-mass gravity. They evolved sequences of models with varying heating rates and dissociation parameters to probe SASI-dominated (χ<3) and convection-dominated (χ>3) regimes, and analyzed the turbulent flow using a spherical Fourier-Bessel decomposition. They also verified their principles against the general relativistic, radiation-hydrodynamic simulations of Müller, Janka, and Heger (2012).

## Key numbers

- The convection parameter χ is defined as the integral of Im(ω_BV)/|v_r| from gain radius to shock radius; the critical value for transition from SASI- to convection-dominated behavior is χ ≈ 3
- The initial shock radius without heating r_s0 ≈ 150 km, with free-fall speed v_ff0 ≈ 5×10^9 cm/s, dynamical time t_ff0 ≈ 3 ms, and upstream density ρ_1 ≈ 10^8 g/cm^3
- For the e0 sequence (SASI-dominated), model e0B10 with B=0.010 explodes at t_exp = 336 t_ff0; for the e3 sequence (convection-dominated), model e3B08 with B=0.008 explodes at t_exp = 223 t_ff0
- The minimum amplitude for a perturbation to rise buoyantly against accretion flow when χ<3 is (Δρ/ρ)_c ≈ C_D v_2^2 / (2 l_v g_s), with drag coefficient C_D ≈ 0.5 for a sphere
- For the e0 sequence, the advection time t_adv ranges from 8.9 to 13.1 t_ff0 as B increases from 0 to 0.008; for the e3 sequence, t_adv ranges from 19.8 to 34.8 t_ff0

## Context

This work extends the linear stability analysis of Foglizzo (2006) and the parametric simulations of Fernández and Thompson (2009a, 2009b) by examining the nonlinear transition to explosion in both SASI- and convection-dominated regimes. It connects to the two-dimensional simulations of Müller, Janka, and Heger (2012), who found that an 8.1 M_sun progenitor becomes convection-dominated while a 27 M_sun progenitor develops strong SASI activity. The findings enable interpretation of three-dimensional simulations (e.g., Hanke et al. 2013, Burrows et al. 2012) where SASI amplitudes are smaller than in two dimensions, suggesting that progenitor structure and initial perturbations may determine which instability dominates.
