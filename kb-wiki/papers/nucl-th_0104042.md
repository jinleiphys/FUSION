# Strange hadrons and their resonances: a diagnostic tool of QGP freeze-out dynamics

> We update our chemical analysis of (strange) hadrons produced at the SPS in Pb--Pb collisions at 158A GeV and and present a first chemical analysis of RHIC results. We report that the shape of (anti)hyperon m_T-spectra in a thermal freeze-out analysis leads to freeze-out conditions found in chemical analysis, implying sudden strange hyperon production. We discuss how a combined analysis of several strange hadron resonances of differing lifespan can be used to understand the dynamical process present during chemical and thermal freeze-outs. In medium resonance quenching is considered.

## Key claim

The coincidence of chemical freeze-out (particle yields) and thermal freeze-out (spectral shapes) for strange hyperons in Pb–Pb collisions at 158A GeV implies a sudden, explosive breakup of a deconfined quark-gluon plasma fireball, rather than a gradual hadronization process. The authors hedge that this conclusion depends on the assumption that resonance decay products do not re-equilibrate in rescattering, and that the analysis excludes the anomalous production pattern of \(\Omega+\overline\Omega\).

## Method

The authors performed a statistical model fit to the yields of strange hadrons (chemical freeze-out analysis) using Fermi–Bose statistics and an extensive resonance cascade, updating earlier fits with new experimental data from the SPS (WA97, NA49) and first RHIC (STAR) results. Separately, they fitted the transverse mass (\(m_\bot\)) spectra of \(\Lambda,\overline\Lambda,\Xi,\overline\Xi,K_s\) in four centrality bins (thermal freeze-out analysis) using a model with two velocities: a local flow velocity \(v\) and a hadronization surface (breakup) velocity \(v_f\). They then compared the freeze-out temperatures from both analyses and studied the observable yields of strange resonances (\(\Lambda(1520)\), \(K^*(892)\), \(\Sigma^*(1385)\)) as a function of freeze-out temperature, fireball lifespan, and possible in-medium quenching of the resonance width.

## Key numbers

* Chemical freeze-out temperature for Pb–Pb at 158A GeV: \(T = 145 \pm 3.5\) MeV (without imposed strangeness balance) or \(T = 150 \pm 3\) MeV (with imposed strangeness balance).
* Expansion velocity from chemical analysis: \(v = 0.52 \pm 0.055\) (without strangeness balance) or \(v = 0.57 \pm 0.04\) (with strangeness balance).
* From first RHIC data at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=130\) GeV: \(\bar p/p = 0.6 \pm 0.02\) gives \(\lambda_q = 1.09\) and \(\mu_B = 38\) MeV (at \(T=150\) MeV).
* RHIC strangeness yield: \(dN_{K^+}/dy|_{y=0} = 35 \pm 3.5\), \(dN_{K^-}/dy|_{y=0} = 30 \pm 3\), leading to total \(\bar s\) yield \(d\bar s/dy|_{y=0} > 85 \pm 9\).
* Observed \(\Lambda(1520)/(\text{all }\Lambda) = 0.025 \pm 0.008\) from NA49; this is compatible with a sudden hadronization (fireball lifespan ~1 fm/c) only if the resonance width is quenched from its natural value of 15.6 MeV to about 150 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors’ earlier chemical analysis (Letessier et al., 2000) and the sudden QGP breakup hypothesis (Rafelski et al., 2000). It confirms the coincidence of chemical and thermal freeze-out seen in SPS data, consistent with a first-order phase transition and deep supercooling. The resonance analysis builds on earlier proposals to use \(\phi\) meson quenching (Birse, 1991; Ko, 1992; Klingl et al., 1998) and extends them to strange baryon resonances as a tool to measure the fireball lifespan. The results enable future combined analyses of multiple strange resonances (e.g., \(\Sigma^*(1385)\) and \(K^*(892)\)) to distinguish between sudden hadronization and a long-lived hadronic phase, and to test for in-medium resonance quenching.
