# Fusion and Breakup of Halo Nuclei

> We discuss the effect of the coupling to the break-up channel on the total fusion involving a halo nucleus with a heavy target. We show that there is a competition between the hindrance arising from this coupling, mostly at above barrier energies, and the enhancement which ensues at sub-barrier energies owing to the static effect of the extended matter distribution and the coupling to the soft modes.

## Key claim

The coupling to the break-up channel in fusion of halo nuclei with heavy targets produces a competition between hindrance at above-barrier energies and enhancement at sub-barrier energies, where the sub-barrier enhancement arises from the static effect of the extended matter distribution and coupling to soft modes (pygmy resonances), not from break-up itself.

## Method

The authors develop a model for complete fusion cross-section σ_CF = (π/k²) Σ_ℓ (2ℓ+1) T_ℓ,i(E) P_ℓ,i(E), where T_ℓ,i(E) is the fusion transmission coefficient and P_ℓ,i(E) is the break-up survival probability. They review experimental data from four measurements on ¹¹Be and ⁶He fusion with heavy targets (²⁰⁹Bi and ²³⁸U) at near-barrier energies, and critically assess theoretical approaches including Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels (CDCC) calculations by Diaz-Torres and Thompson (2002) and Hagino et al (2000).

## Key numbers

- The break-up threshold for halo nuclei is less than 1 MeV.
- For ⁹Be+²⁰⁸Pb at near-barrier energies, the one-dimensional barrier calculation must be multiplied by 0.68 to account for the observed complete fusion suppression (Hinde et al, 2002).
- The incomplete fusion probability for ⁹Be+²⁰⁸Pb is 0.32 ± 0.07 (Hinde et al, 2002).
- For ⁷Li+²⁰⁹Bi at near-barrier energies, the break-up survival probability effect on fusion is 75% (Dasgupta et al, 2002).
- The ratio (Z_C/Z_Bi)² ≈ 0.005 for ⁷Li+¹²C, implying essentially no reduction in σ_CF for light systems.

## Context

This review extends the coupled-channels theory of sub-barrier fusion (Dasgupta et al, 1998) to halo nuclei by incorporating break-up channel coupling, building on earlier work by Hussein et al (1992) and Canto et al (1995). The paper critically compares with CDCC calculations by Hagino et al (2000) and Diaz-Torres and Thompson (2002), and with experimental measurements by Signorini et al (1999), Trotta et al (2000), and Kolata et al (1998). It identifies that current CDCC calculations overestimate incomplete fusion by including neutron removal cross-section, and suggests that three- or four-body Faddeev-type treatments are needed for accurate calculations of complete and incomplete fusion cross-sections.
