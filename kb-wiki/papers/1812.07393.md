# Hyperons in thermal QCD: A lattice view

> The hadron resonance gas (HRG) is a widely used description of matter under extreme conditions, e.g. in the context of heavy-ion phenomenology. Commonly used implementations of the HRG employ vacuum hadron masses throughout the hadronic phase and hence do not include possible in-medium effects. Here we investigate this issue, using nonperturbative lattice simulations employing the FASTSUM anisotropic Nf=2+1 ensembles. We study the fate of octet and decuplet baryons as the temperature increases, focussing in particular on the positive- and negative-parity groundstates. While the positive-parity groundstate masses are indeed seen to be temperature independent, within the error, a strong temperature dependence is observed in the negative-parity channels. We give a simple parametrisation of this and formulate an in-medium HRG, which is particularly effective for hyperons. Parity doubling is seen to emerge in the deconfined phase at the level of correlators, with a noticeable effect of the heavier s quark. Channel dependence of this transition is analysed.

# Hyperons in thermal QCD: A lattice view

## Key claim

The positive-parity groundstate masses of octet and decuplet baryons remain temperature independent within error throughout the hadronic phase, while the negative-parity groundstate masses drop significantly as temperature increases, becoming nearly degenerate with their positive-parity partners near the deconfinement transition; this temperature dependence of negative-parity masses can be parametrized and incorporated into an in-medium hadron resonance gas that improves agreement with lattice data for hyperon fluctuations.

## Method

The authors performed nonperturbative lattice QCD simulations using the FASTSUM anisotropic Nf=2+1 ensembles with a fixed-scale approach, varying temperature by changing the number of time slices Nτ across eight ensembles from T=44 MeV to T=352 MeV. They computed all octet (spin 1/2) and decuplet (spin 3/2) baryon correlators for both positive and negative parity, applying Gaussian smearing at source and sink, and extracted groundstate masses via exponential fits. The crossover temperature Tc=185(4) MeV was determined from the inflection point of the renormalised Polyakov loop.

## Key numbers

- Crossover temperature: Tc=185(4) MeV, determined via inflection point of renormalised Polyakov loop
- Spatial lattice spacing: as=0.1227(8) fm; inverse temporal lattice spacing: aτ^{-1}=5.63(4) GeV; renormalised anisotropy ξ=as/aτ=3.5
- Pion mass: mπ=384(4) MeV (light quarks not at physical mass); strange quark mass at physical value
- Negative-parity groundstate masses at lowest temperature (T=44 MeV): N(1/2^-) 1778(52) MeV, Δ(3/2^-) 2138(117) MeV, Σ(1/2^-) 1823(35) MeV, Λ(0^-) 1899(66) MeV, Σ*(3/2^-) 2131(62) MeV, Ξ(1/2^-) 1917(27) MeV, Ξ*(3/2^-) 2164(42) MeV, Ω(0^-) 2193(30) MeV
- Negative-parity masses at T/Tc=0.95: N(1/2^-) 1348(83) MeV, Δ(3/2^-) 1526(74) MeV, Σ(1/2^-) 1431(51) MeV, Λ(0^-) 1286(75) MeV, Σ*(3/2^-) 1542(60) MeV, Ξ(1/2^-) 1415(50) MeV, Ξ*(3/2^-) 1578(48) MeV, Ω(0^-) 1576(66) MeV
- Fit parameter range for transition width: 0.22 ≤ γ ≤ 0.35; ratio m-(Tc)/m+(0) range: 0.85 to 1.1
- Inflection point temperatures from R ratio lie 9 to 15 MeV below the Polyakov loop Tc=185(4) MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous lattice studies of nucleon, Delta and Omega baryons (Aarts 2017) to all octet and decuplet hyperons, providing the first comprehensive lattice determination of temperature-dependent baryon masses across the hadronic phase. The results challenge the standard hadron resonance gas (HRG) approach, which uses vacuum masses throughout (Dashen 1969, Venugopalan 1992, Karsch 2003), and instead support an in-medium HRG that incorporates the observed temperature dependence of negative-parity states. The findings can constrain parity-doublet models (Detar 1988, Jido 1999, Jido 2001) and provide benchmarks for effective approaches such as the Polyakov-Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model (Torres-Rincon 2015) and QCD sum rules (Azizi 2016). The work enables improved modelling of heavy-ion phenomenology and suggests the need for similar studies of mesonic in-medium effects.
