# Symmetry breaking and quantum correlations in finite systems: Studies of quantum dots and ultracold Bose gases and related nuclear and chemical methods

> Investigations of emergent symmetry breaking phenomena occurring in small finite-size systems are reviewed, with a focus on the strongly correlated regime of electrons in two-dimensional semicoductor quantum dots and trapped ultracold bosonic atoms in harmonic traps. Throughout the review we emphasize universal aspects and similarities of symmetry breaking found in these systems, as well as in more traditional fields like nuclear physics and quantum chemistry, which are characterized by very different interparticle forces. A unified description of strongly correlated phenomena in finite systems of repelling particles (whether fermions or bosons) is presented through the development of a two-step method of symmetry breaking at the unrestricted Hartree-Fock level and of subsequent symmetry restoration via post Hartree-Fock projection techniques. Quantitative and qualitative aspects of the two-step method are treated and validated by exact diagonalization calculations. Strongly-correlated phenomena emerging from symmetry breaking include: (I) Chemical bonding, dissociation, and entanglement (at zero and finite magnetic fields) in quantum dot molecules and in pinned electron molecular dimers formed within a single anisotropic quantum dot. (II) Electron crystallization, with particle localization on the vertices of concentric polygonal rings, and formation of rotating electron molecules (REMs) in circular quantum dots. (III) At high magnetic fields, the REMs are described by parameter-free analytic wave functions, which are an alternative to the Laughlin and composite-fermion approaches. (IV) Crystalline phases of strongly repelling bosons. In rotating traps and in analogy with the REMs, such repelling bosons form rotating boson molecules (RBMs).

## Key claim

The paper argues that spontaneous symmetry breaking in small finite systems (such as quantum dots and ultracold Bose gases) is not an artifact but a legitimate mean-field description that must be followed by a post-Hartree-Fock symmetry restoration step to recover the exact ground state, with the broken-symmetry effects surviving as hidden or intrinsic correlations.

## Method

The authors develop a two-step method: first, symmetry breaking at the unrestricted Hartree-Fock (UHF) level, which yields lower-energy Slater determinants that violate rotational or other symmetries of the Hamiltonian; second, restoration of the broken symmetries via post-Hartree-Fock projection techniques (following Peierls and Yoccoz in nuclear physics and Lowdin in quantum chemistry). The method is validated by comparing its results with exact diagonalization (EXD) calculations and by analyzing conditional probability distributions (CPDs) to reveal hidden molecular structures.

## Key numbers

* Wigner parameter \(R_W\) (for charged particles) and \(R_\delta\) (for neutral bosons) quantify the relative strength of two-body repulsion to one-particle kinetic energy; larger values correspond to stronger correlation effects.
* For ultracold bosons in quasi-linear harmonic traps, experimental tunability of \(R_\delta\) over two orders of magnitude (from 1 to 5 and from 5 to 200) has been reported, enabling access to the Tonks-Girardeau regime.
* In vertical quantum dots, addition energy measurements at zero magnetic field showed maxima at closed electronic shells (\(N=2,6,12,\ldots\)) and mid-shells (\(N=4,9,\ldots\)), consistent with a 2D-harmonic-oscillator central-mean-field model.
* For two-electron lateral quantum dots, excitation spectra measurements at zero and low magnetic fields (Ellegaard et al., 2006; Zumbuhl et al., 2004) provide unambiguous signatures of strong correlations and Wigner molecule formation.

## Context

This review extends the concepts of spontaneous symmetry breaking and symmetry restoration from nuclear physics (Peierls and Yoccoz, 1957; Bohr and Mottelson) and quantum chemistry (Lowdin, 1955; Fukutome) to condensed-matter nanosystems, specifically two-dimensional semiconductor quantum dots and ultracold bosonic atoms in harmonic traps. It contrasts with earlier reviews on quantum dots (Kouwenhoven et al., 2001; Reimann and Manninen, 2002) and Bose-Einstein condensates (Dalfovo et al., 1999; Leggett, 2001) that focused on the electronic-shell-structure or BEC regimes, and it enables a unified description of strongly correlated phenomena across fermionic and bosonic finite systems, with implications for solid-state quantum computing and the fractional quantum Hall regime.
