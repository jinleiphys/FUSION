# Deeply Virtual Compton Scattering with Positron Beams at Jefferson Lab

> A brief discussion of the DVCS program at the Jefferson Lab 12 GeV energy upgrade is given. Emphasis is on what can be learned from using both polarized electron and polarized positron beams in conjunction with polarized nucleon targets.

## Key claim

Using both polarized electron and polarized positron beams at the Jefferson Lab 12 GeV upgrade would allow separation of the real and imaginary parts of the DVCS amplitude without relying on the Rosenbluth technique, which requires measurements at two different beam energies and reduces kinematic coverage.

## Method

The paper analyzes the differential cross section structure for deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS) with polarized lepton beams and polarized proton targets, deriving the interference terms that isolate the generalized parton distributions (GPDs) at the specific kinematics x=ξ. It presents quantitative estimates of charge asymmetries and cross sections using the CLAS12 detector acceptance and luminosity, assuming a 10 cm liquid hydrogen target with 40 nA electron current and 8 nA positron current for 1000 hours of beam time, with predictions based on the dual model parameterization of GPDs H and E.

## Key numbers

- Electron beam current of 40 nA corresponds to an operating luminosity of 10^35 cm^-2 sec^-1
- Positron beam current assumed at 8 nA, five times lower than the electron beam
- 1000 hours of beam time used for rate projections for both electron and positron beams
- Beam energy of 11 GeV assumed for the JLab upgrade
- Polarized proton targets based on ammonia have a dilution factor of approximately 0.18
- Positron currents in excess of 1 μA would be required for programs with high resolution magnetic spectrometers

## Context

This work extends the existing DVCS program at Jefferson Lab, where experiments with 4 to 6 GeV electron beams have already demonstrated feasibility of measuring beam spin asymmetries (Stepanyan 2001, Munoz 2006, Girod 2008, Gavalian 2009) and longitudinal target asymmetries (Chen 2006). The paper builds on the theoretical framework of generalized parton distributions reviewed by Diehl and Radyushkin, and uses the VGG model for projections. It enables a complete separation of all four leading twist GPDs (H, H̃, E, Ẽ) at the Jefferson Lab 12 GeV upgrade without kinematic limitations imposed by the Rosenbluth separation technique.
