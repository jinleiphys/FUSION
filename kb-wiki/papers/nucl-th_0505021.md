# Enhancement Mechanisms of Low Energy Nuclear Reactions

> The review of possible stimulation mechanisms of LENR (low energy nuclear reaction) is represented. We have concluded that transmutation of nuclei at low energies and excess heat are possible in the framework of the modern physical theory -- the universal resonance synchronization principle [1] and based on its different enhancement mechanisms of reaction rates are responsible for these processes [2]. The excitation and ionization of atoms may play role as a trigger for LENR. Superlow energy of external fields may stimulate LENR [3]. Investigation of this phenomenon requires knowledge of different branches of science: nuclear and atomic physics, chemistry and electrochemistry, condensed matter and solid state physics,... The results of this research field can provide a new source of energy, substances and technologies. The puzzle of poor reproducibility of experimental data is due to the fact that LENR occurs in open systems and it is extremely sensitive to parameters of external fields and systems. Classical reproducibility principle should be reconsidered for LENR experiments. Poor reproducibility and unexplained results do not means that the experiment is wrong.

# Enhancement Mechanisms of Low Energy Nuclear Reactions

## Key claim

Low energy nuclear reactions (LENR) are possible within modern physical theory, specifically the universal resonance synchronization principle, but experimental results are poorly reproducible because LENR occurs in open systems that are extremely sensitive to parameters of external fields and system conditions, meaning poor reproducibility does not necessarily indicate experimental error.

## Method

The authors review experimental evidence for various enhancement mechanisms of nuclear reaction rates at low energies, including bound-state beta decay in fully ionized atoms, environmental effects on electron capture decay rates, controlled gamma-decay using resonant screens, the Okorokov effect for coherent excitation in periodic crystal fields, and resonance synchronization between nuclear, atomic, and external field frequencies. They analyze how ionization of atoms, chemical environments, and low-energy external fields such as lasers, ultrasound, and electromagnetic fields can alter nuclear decay rates and induce transmutations.

## Key numbers

- The half-life of neutral $^{187}Re$ is 42 Gyr, while bare $^{187}Re^{75+}$ has a half-life of $32.9 \pm 2.0$ yr, representing a decrease by a factor of about one billion (9 orders of magnitude)
- Fully ionized $^{163}Dy^{66+}$ (stable as neutral atom with $Q_{\beta} = -2.565$ keV) decays via bound-state beta decay to $^{163}Ho^{66+}$ with $Q^{K}_{\beta b} = +50.3$ keV and a half-life of 47 days
- The half-life of $^{7}Be$ in endohedral fullerene $^{7}Be@C_{60}$ is $52.68 \pm 0.05$ days versus $53.12 \pm 0.05$ days in $^{7}Be$ metal, a $0.83\%$ difference
- The decay rate of $^{7}Be$ in gold is lower than in graphite by $(0.38 \pm 0.09)\%$ and lower than in $Al_{2}O_{3}$ by $(0.72 \pm 0.07)\%$
- The radiative lifetime of an excited nucleus can be increased by $10-40\%$ and total lifetime by $1\%$ using a thin resonant screen with $^{57}Co(^{57*}Fe)$ source and $^{57}Fe$ absorber
- The acceleration of decay time in H-like ions of $^{229m}Th$ may be up to $10^{5}$ (five orders of magnitude)
- The enhancement factor for resonance transfer of energy by electrons using laser compensation may be $10^{3}$ in some cases
- The half-life of ionized radiophosphorus is approximately 1-2 minutes, comparable to the lifetime of natural ball lightning

## Context

This review extends the long-standing debate about the constancy of nuclear decay rates, beginning with the historical assumption from Rutherford, Chadwick and Ellis (1930) that decay constants are independent of physical and chemical conditions, through the discovery by Segre (1947) that electron-capture decay rates depend on chemical environment. The authors build on experimental observations of bound-state beta decay in fully ionized atoms by Jung et al. (1992) for $^{163}Dy$ and Bosch et al. (1996) for $^{187}Re$, and on the Okorokov effect for coherent excitation in crystals (1965). The work enables further investigation of controlled nuclear transmutations using low-energy external fields, with potential applications in energy production and understanding of geo- and biotransmutations as described by Vysotsky and Kornilova (2003).
