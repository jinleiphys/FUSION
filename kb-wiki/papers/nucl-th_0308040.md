# Contribution of the nucleon-hyperon reaction channels to K$^-$ production in proton-nucleus collisions

> The cross sections for producing K$^-$ mesons in nucleon-hyperon elementary processes are estimated assuming one-pion exchange and using the experimentally known pion-hyperon cross sections. The results are implemented in a transport model which is applied to calculation of proton-nucleus collisions. In significant difference to earlier estimates for heavy-ion collisions the inclusion of the nucleon-hyperon cross section roughly doubles the K$^-$ production in near-threshold proton-nucleus collisions.

## Key claim

The nucleon-hyperon (NY → NNK⁻) reaction channel, previously considered unimportant for heavy-ion collisions, roughly doubles the K⁻ production yield in near-threshold proton-nucleus collisions on heavy targets, contrary to earlier estimates.

## Method

The authors estimated the NY → NNK⁻ cross sections using a one-pion exchange model, where the unknown pion-baryon vertex is replaced by experimentally measured πY → NK⁻ cross sections (extracted from inverse K⁻p reactions). These cross sections were then implemented into a Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck transport model to calculate K⁻ production in proton collisions on carbon and gold targets at beam energies of 2.5 and 3.5 GeV.

## Key numbers

- The NY → NNK⁻ cross section reaches nearly 1 mb in the relevant kinetic energy region, about 50 times larger than the related NN → NYK⁺ cross sections.
- Including the NY channel roughly doubles the K⁻ yield in p+Au collisions at 2.5 GeV; the cross section diminishes by about 50% when the NY channel is excluded.
- For p+Au at 2.5 GeV, the calculated K⁻/K⁺ ratio is 0.010 compared to the experimental value of 0.0074 (with about 20% error).
- For p+C at 2.5 GeV, the calculated K⁻/K⁺ ratio is 0.013 compared to the experimental value of 0.0085.
- For p+Au at 3.5 GeV, the calculated K⁻/K⁺ ratio is 0.024 compared to the experimental value of 0.027.
- For p+C at 3.5 GeV, the calculated K⁻/K⁺ ratio is 0.024 compared to the experimental value of 0.028.

## Context

This work extends earlier one-boson exchange calculations by Ko (1981) and Cassing et al. (1990s), which found the NY channel unimportant for heavy-ion collisions, by showing its significance in proton-nucleus collisions. The results enable more reliable extraction of the in-medium K⁻ potential from comparisons with KaoS collaboration data (2000s), as the inclusion of the NY channel affects predictions for the attractive antikaon potential (here taken as V_K⁻ = -0.08 GeV × n/n₀).
