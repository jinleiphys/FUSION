# Microscopic Theory of Nuclear Fission

> Nuclear fission represents the ultimate test for microscopic theories of nuclear structure and reactions. Fission is a large-amplitude, time-dependent phenomenon taking place in a self-bound, strongly-interacting many-body system. It should, at least in principle, emerge from the complex interactions of nucleons within the nucleus. The goal of microscopic theories is to build a consistent and predictive theory of nuclear fission by using as only ingredients protons and neutrons, nuclear forces and quantum many-body methods. Thanks to a constant increase in computing power, such a goal has never seemed more within reach. This chapter gives an overview both of the set of techniques used in microscopic theory to describe the fission process and of some recent successes achieved by this class of methods.

# Microscopic Theory of Nuclear Fission

## Key claim

Microscopic theories of nuclear fission, based on energy density functional (EDF) methods, can provide a consistent and predictive description of the fission process using only protons, neutrons, nuclear forces, and quantum many-body methods, but only if one accepts a "rather narrow definition" of microscopic that excludes true *ab initio* approaches, which remain unable to reach the actinide and superheavy nuclei where fission is relevant and are still focused on static properties with insufficient precision.

## Method

The chapter reviews the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) theory as the basic building block of the EDF approach, where the many-body wave function is approximated as a product state of quasiparticle operators and the total energy is minimized with respect to variations of the one-body density matrix and pairing tensor. Solutions are obtained by imposing constraints on multipole moment operators to map the potential energy surface as a function of nuclear deformation, with spontaneous symmetry breaking (of particle number, rotational invariance, and other symmetries) being a central feature that enables the description of extreme deformation. Multi-reference EDF methods using projection techniques (particle-number projection and angular-momentum projection) are then applied to restore the quantum numbers lost through symmetry breaking.

## Key numbers

- The time scale of the fission process is approximately τ_f ≈ 10^{-19} s, which makes direct measurement of primary fragment properties impossible.
- Spontaneous fission half-lives τ_SF are measurable observables that encode the probability of fission from the ground state.
- The collective mass tensor (not a scalar but a rank-2 tensor) plays a critical role in determining tunneling probabilities for spontaneous fission.
- The Skyrme Hamiltonian uses a local zero-range potential of the form V(r₁,r₂)δ(r₁−r₂), while the Gogny Hamiltonian uses a finite-range potential proportional to exp[(r₁−r₂)²/μ²] with a typical length scale μ.

## Context

This chapter extends the long line of fission theory beginning with Bohr and Wheeler (1939) and the critical insight by Meitner (1939), and builds on the EDF framework reviewed in Schunck (2019). It contrasts microscopic EDF methods with phenomenological approaches such as the liquid drop model and macroscopic-microscopic methods, and notes that *ab initio* methods (e.g., those reviewed by Hagen (2014), Carlson (2015), Hergert (2016), and Stroberg (2019)) cannot yet reach the actinide region. The work enables future calculations of fission observables, including fragment distributions Y(Z,A), excitation energies, spin distributions, and prompt neutron and gamma emission, by providing the theoretical foundation for computing these quantities as inputs to statistical reaction codes.
