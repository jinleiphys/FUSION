# What do mesons have to do with nuclear structure?

> The theory of nuclear structure (binding, low energy spectra, transitions, etc.) depends on nucleon-nucleon (NN) interactions. The meson theory of NN interactions has predictive power for NN scattering, and partial success when applied to the theory of nuclear structure. Is it possible to test this theoretical picture -- by direct experimental interaction with mesons in nuclei? Some experimental searches for the `pion excess' in nuclei have ambiguous results. How sensitive are such experiments to the mesonic aspects of nuclear structure? These questions are addressed in this talk. More details and references are given in Reference [1].

# What do mesons have to do with nuclear structure?

## Key claim

The conventional meson-exchange theory of nuclear structure predicts that correlation-induced changes to nuclear response functions (and thus the "pion excess") are small, on the order of a few percent of the quasifree peak, and spread over a large range of excitation energy; these changes are within the uncertainties of existing (p,n) experiments and may be kinematically suppressed in deep inelastic scattering, so there is no conflict with data but also no positive confirmation.

## Method

The author derives the connection between meson-exchange potentials (one-pion exchange and one-rho exchange) and nuclear response functions R_L (longitudinal) and R_T (transverse), which are measured in inelastic scattering experiments such as (p,n). Using sum rules for the response functions and a two-component model that separates shell-model response from correlation-driven response, the author estimates the magnitude of correlation contributions to R_L and R_T for 16O and compares these estimates to experimental data from (p,n) reactions and to kinematic constraints in deep inelastic scattering.

## Key numbers

- For 16O, the one-pion exchange contribution to binding is <V_π>_A/A = -30.7 MeV, a major part of nuclear binding.
- The integrated excess pion number per nucleon is δn_A/A ≃ 0.03 in the conventional theory.
- For the correlation response model at 200 MeV/c ≤ q ≤ 400 MeV/c, the centroid ω_L = 293 MeV.
- The constant model gives ΔR_L/3Aq^2 = 2.6 × 10^{-4} MeV^{-1}; the exponential model gives an upper bound of 3.8 × 10^{-4} MeV^{-1}.
- The transverse correlation model gives ΔR_T/3Aq^2 = -4.5 × 10^{-4} MeV^{-1} for ω ≤ ω_L.
- Experimental (p,n) peak values are max R_α/3Aq^2 ≃ 1.2 - 1.7 × 10^{-2} MeV^{-1}, dropping to ≃ 0.5 × 10^{-2} MeV^{-1} at the ends of the energy-loss range.
- Correlation contributions are a few percent at the peak and less than 10% over ω ≤ 150 MeV, smaller than experimental uncertainties (counting ≤ 10%, systematic 6%-8%, model uncertainties 20% and 10%).
- For q = 400 MeV/c, S_L(q) = 1.11 is a maximum; the integral J(q,y) falls below 1.0 for y ≃ 0.3.

## Context

This talk extends the meson-exchange theory of nuclear structure, particularly the work of the Argonne-Urbana group (1990s) who calculated ground-state correlation functions and sum rules using realistic NN potentials. The author develops a model for response functions that uses sum-rule information from those calculations, and compares the results to (p,n) experiments analyzed by various groups. The work addresses whether the "pion excess" predicted by meson theory can be observed experimentally, concluding that current experiments lack the precision needed and that kinematic constraints in DIS may further suppress observable effects. The analysis connects to earlier work by Sullivan on pion contributions to scattering and to RPA calculations with pion and rho exchange.
