# A Bayesian Approach to Detection of Small Low Emission Sources

> The article addresses the problem of detecting presence and location of a small low emission source inside of an object, when the background noise dominates. This problem arises, for instance, in some homeland security applications. The goal is to reach the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) levels on the order of $10^{-3}$. A Bayesian approach to this problem is implemented in 2D. The method allows inference not only about the existence of the source, but also about its location. We derive Bayes factors for model selection and estimation of location based on Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) simulation. A simulation study shows that with sufficiently high total emission level, our method can effectively locate the source.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a Bayesian approach using Bayes factors and MCMC sampling can detect the presence and location of a small low-emission source even when ballistic particles from the source account for only about 0.1% of total detections (signal-to-noise ratio on the order of 10^{-3}), provided the total number of detected particles is sufficiently large.

## Method

The authors model the detection problem as a choice between two models: no source (M1) where all detected particles come from isotropic uniform background, and source present (M2) where a fraction p of particles originate ballistically from a small source of known radius d at unknown location L = (l1, l2). They compute Bayes factors for model selection using the harmonic mean of likelihoods from MCMC samples, employing parallel tempering with 6 chains to handle the highly multimodal posterior distribution. The Gibbs sampler updates the emission rate p (on a discrete grid), and Metropolis-Hastings updates the polar coordinates (r, u) of the source location.

## Key numbers

- The target signal-to-noise ratio is on the order of 10^{-3}, corresponding to ballistic particles accounting for about 0.1% of total emission.
- Source radius is assumed to be d = 0.01 (1% of the object radius, which is the unit disk).
- With n = 2 x 10^5 detected particles and true emission rate p = 0.001, the proportion of Bayes factors exceeding 3 (positive evidence) was 0.6 for source at (0.6, 0.3) and 0.4 for source at (0.96, -0.1).
- With n = 5 x 10^5 detected particles and p = 0.001, the proportion of Bayes factors exceeding 3 was 1.0 for both source locations, with median Bayes factors of 1.41 x 10^9 and 2.35 x 10^9 respectively.
- For p = 0 (no source) with n = 2 x 10^5, the median Bayes factor was 1.06 and only 0.1 of simulations produced BF > 3; with n = 5 x 10^5, the median BF was 1.16 and none exceeded 3.

## Context

This work extends the analytic detection techniques of Xun, Mallick, Carroll, and Kuchment (2011) by introducing a Bayesian framework that provides both model selection (source presence) and location estimation, whereas prior work used more analytic approaches. The method addresses a regime considered impossible in medical emission tomographic imaging (as noted by Herman) due to the overwhelming background noise. The approach enables practical detection using Compton-type cameras (as described by Xun, Mallick, Carroll, and Kuchment (2011) and references therein) which can determine directional information without collimation, and future work is planned to address scattering, inhomogeneous background, and 3D situations.
