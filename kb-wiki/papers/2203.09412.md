# Study on nuclear $α$-decay energy by an artificial neural network with pairing and shell effects

> We build and train the artificial neural network model (ANN) based on the experimental $α$-decay energy ($Q_α$) data. Besides decays between the ground states of parent and daughter nuclei, decays from the ground state of parent nuclei to the excited state of daughter nuclei are also included. By this way, the number of samples are increased dramatically. The results calculated by ANN model reproduce the experimental data with a good accuracy. The root-mean-square (rms) relative to the experiment data is 0.105 MeV. The influence of different input is investigated. It is found that either the shell effect or the pairing effect results in an obvious improvement of the predictive power of ANN model, and the shell effect plays a more important role. The optimal result can be obtained as both the shell and pairing effects are considered simultaneously. Application of ANN model in prediction of the $α$-decay energy shows the neutron magic number at $N=184$, and a possible sub-shell gap around $N=174$ or 176 in the superheavy nuclei region.

## Key claim

The artificial neural network (ANN) model reproduces experimental alpha-decay energies with a root-mean-square deviation of 0.105 MeV when both shell and pairing effects are included as inputs, but the abstract's claim of "good accuracy" glosses over the fact that this best result is achieved only when the model is trained on a dataset that includes decays to excited states of the daughter nucleus (2131 data points), and that the test-set performance degrades to 0.303 MeV when only mass number and proton number are used as inputs.

## Method

The authors constructed a multilayer perceptron (MLP) with three hidden layers of 64 units each, using the ReLU activation function and the Adam optimizer, trained on 2131 experimental alpha-decay energies from Te (Z=52) to Og (Z=118). The input features were combinations of mass number A, proton number Z, a pairing term delta = [(-1)^Z + (-1)^N]/2, and a shell-effect term P = nu_p * nu_n / (nu_p + nu_n), where nu_p(n) is the difference between the nucleon number and the nearest magic number (Z=8,20,28,50,82,126; N=8,20,28,50,82,126,184). The data were split 80% for training and 20% for testing, and the model was trained for 1000 epochs with a learning-rate reduction callback.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square deviation for the best ANN model (inputs: A, Z, delta, P) is 0.105 MeV on the test set.
- With only A and Z as inputs, the test-set root-mean-square deviation is 0.303 MeV.
- Adding only the pairing term delta reduces the test-set root-mean-square to 0.220 MeV (a 50% improvement relative to the A,Z-only case).
- Adding only the shell term P reduces the test-set root-mean-square to 0.176 MeV.
- The ANN model with inputs (A, Z, P) achieves a test-set root-mean-square of 0.176 MeV, compared to 0.274 MeV for the Duflo-Zuker plus Bayesian neural network (DZ+BNN) model from 2019Alpha and 0.310 MeV for the XGBoost model from SaxenaG2021.
- For the superheavy nuclei region (Z=104-118, 87 data points), the overall root-mean-square deviation is 0.204 MeV, with individual element values ranging from 0.091 MeV (Ts) to 0.310 MeV (Mt).

## Context

This work extends machine-learning approaches to alpha-decay energy prediction, specifically improving upon the Bayesian neural network (BNN) method applied to the Duflo-Zuker mass model by 2019Alpha and the XGBoost, Random Forest, Decision Trees, and MLP models used by SaxenaG2021 (2021). Unlike those prior studies, which only considered ground-state-to-ground-state decays, the present work includes decays to excited states of the daughter nucleus, substantially increasing the training dataset. The trained ANN model is then used to predict alpha-decay energies in the superheavy nuclei region, suggesting a neutron magic number at N=184 and a possible sub-shell gap around N=174 or 176, which provides guidance for future experimental searches and theoretical studies of superheavy element stability.
