# Systematics of supernumerary nuclear rainbow in inelastic $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C scattering

> We perform a systematic study of inelastic nuclear rainbow scattering for the \oc system to the 2$^+$ (4.44 MeV) state of $^{12}$C at incident energies of 100--608 MeV with the coupled-channels method. The recently generalized nearside-farside decomposition for inelastic scattering was applied in combination with the multichannel deflection function analysis to elucidate the origin of the nuclear rainbow phenomenon and the suppression of the primary and supernumerary Airy minima in the inelastic scattering cross section. The systematic evolution of the Airy minima for the excited 2$^+$ (4.44 MeV) state of $^{12}$C was unambiguously determined. Our work suggests that there is no clear shift in the positions of the first Airy minima and a small shift at low energies for the second and third Airy minima between the inelastic and elastic scattering cross sections. Using the $K$-subamplitudes splitting technique combined with the generalized nearside-farside decomposition and deflection function, the distinct refractive pattern commonly suppressed in the inelastic heavy-ion scattering can be interpreted and provides new insights into the relationship between elastic and inelastic nuclear rainbow scattering.

## Key claim

The paper systematically determines the energy evolution of Airy minima in inelastic $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C scattering to the 2$^+$ (4.44 MeV) state of $^{12}$C, finding that there is no clear shift in the positions of the first Airy minima and only a small shift at low energies for the second and third Airy minima between inelastic and elastic scattering cross sections, contrary to earlier suggestions of a backward shift.

## Method

The authors performed coupled-channels (CC) calculations using double-folding potentials with the CDM3Y3 density-dependent interaction and Woods-Saxon imaginary potentials, fitting elastic and inelastic scattering data at nine incident energies from 100 to 608 MeV. They applied the recently generalized nearside-farside (NF) decomposition for inelastic scattering combined with multichannel deflection function analysis and the $K$-subamplitudes splitting technique to separate contributions from different transferred angular momentum substates. The CC calculations were performed using the ECIS code, and the $S$-matrix elements were used for the $K$-subamplitude and NF analysis.

## Key numbers

* Incident energies studied: 100, 115.9, 124, 170, 181, 200, 260, 281, and 608 MeV for the $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C system.
* Elastic scattering Airy minima at 170 MeV: A1 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.}\approx 84^\circ$, A2 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.} \approx 58^\circ$, A3 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.}\approx 37^\circ$.
* Elastic scattering Airy minima at 181 MeV: A1 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.} \approx 78^\circ$, A2 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.} \approx 52^\circ$, A3 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.}\approx 35^\circ$.
* Elastic scattering Airy minima at 200 MeV: A1 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.}\approx 66^\circ$, A2 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.}\approx 47^\circ$.
* Elastic scattering Airy minima at 260 MeV: A1 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.}\approx 49^\circ$, A2 at $\theta_{\rm c.m.}\approx 31^\circ$.
* Real potential renormalization factor $N_{\rm R}$ ranges from 0.915 to 0.930 across all energies.
* Volume integral of real potential $J_{\rm R}$ ranges from 247.7 to 303.7 MeV fm$^3$ across the energy range.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of nuclear rainbow scattering in the $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C system, which has been extensively investigated for elastic scattering by Brandan and Satchler (1997), Khoa et al. (2007), and Ogloblin et al. (1998, 2000). It directly addresses the earlier analysis by Ohkubo and Hirabayashi (2017) who suggested a backward shift of Airy minima in inelastic scattering based on CC calculations without the proper $K$-subamplitude decomposition. The new NF decomposition technique developed by Phuc et al. (2021) enables unambiguous identification of Airy minima in the suppressed inelastic cross section, providing a systematic comparison between elastic and inelastic nuclear rainbow scattering across the full refractive energy region.
