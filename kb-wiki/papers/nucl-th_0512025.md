# Do halos exist on the dripline of deformed nuclei?

> A study of the effect of deformation and pairing on the development of halo nuclei is presented. Exploratory three-body $core+n+n$ calculations show that both the NN interaction and the deformation/excitation of the core hinder the formation of the halo. Preliminary self-consistent mean-field calculations are used to search for regions in the nuclear chart where halos could potentially develop. These are also briefly discussed.

# Do halos exist on the dripline of deformed nuclei?

## Key claim
Both the neutron-neutron interaction and the deformation/excitation of the core hinder halo formation in three-body core+n+n systems, and preliminary self-consistent mean-field calculations suggest that deformation in heavy nuclei strongly opposes halo development, contrary to earlier claims by Hamamoto (2004) that deformation does not hinder halo formation.

## Method
The authors performed exploratory three-body Faddeev calculations using the hyperspherical method for a core+n+n system, artificially decreasing the core-n interaction to simulate approach to the neutron dripline. They also carried out self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB) calculations on a 3D mesh using the SLy4 Skyrme force for the particle-hole channel and a zero-range density-dependent pairing force, searching for nuclei where the 3s1/2 neutron orbital (spherical case) or the 1/2+ Nilsson orbital from the 4s1/2 shell (deformed case) intercepts the Fermi level at the dripline.

## Key numbers
- For the three-body model based on 12Be, when both the NN interaction and core deformation (quadrupole deformation β2=0.67) are included, the probability of the K=0 hyperspherical component in the ground state wavefunction remains well below 50% as the two-neutron separation energy approaches zero, indicating no halo formation.
- In the HFB search for spherical nuclei, the neutron dripline for Cr isotopes is reached at 80Cr.
- For deformed Dy isotopes, the analysis based on Helm and geometrical radii showed no significant difference between the neutron's Helm and geometrical radii, indicating no halo develops.

## Context
This work extends the debate on whether halos can exist for nuclei with A>40, following the pairing anti-halo effect demonstrated by Bennaceur (2000) for Carbon isotopes using HFB, and contradicting the claims of Hamamoto (2004) that deformation does not hinder halo formation and that s-wave orbitals decouple from the mean field at the dripline. The three-body model results confirm the earlier findings of Nunes (2005) and the HFB results confirm Bennaceur (2000), while the self-consistent calculations disagree with the non-self-consistent results of Hamamoto (2004). The study enables future investigations into the dependence on the microscopic pairing force, particularly its low-density behavior and finite-range effects.
