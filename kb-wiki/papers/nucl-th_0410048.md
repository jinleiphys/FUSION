# Entrance channels and alpha decay half-lives of the heaviest elements

> The barriers standing against the formation of superheavy elements and their consecutive $α$ decay have been determined in the quasimolecular shape path within a Generalized Liquid Drop Model including the proximity effects between nucleons in a neck, the mass and charge asymmetry, a precise nuclear radius and the shell effects given by the Droplet Model. For moderately asymmetric reactions double-hump potential barriers stand and fast fission of compact shapes in the outer well is possible. Very asymmetric reactions lead to one hump barriers which can be passed only with a high energy relatively to the superheavy element energy. Then, only the emission of several neutrons or an $α$ particle can allow to reach an eventual ground state. For almost symmetric heavy-ion reactions, there is no more external well and the inner barrier is higher than the outer one.

# Entrance channels and alpha decay half-lives of the heaviest elements

## Key claim
The barriers against superheavy element formation and subsequent alpha decay depend critically on the asymmetry of the entrance channel, with only very asymmetric reactions allowing the compound nucleus to be reached via neutron or alpha emission, while almost symmetric reactions make ground-state formation unlikely even when shell effects are included.

## Method
The authors used a Generalized Liquid Drop Model (GLDM) with quasimolecular shapes (two joined elliptic lemniscatoids) to calculate potential barriers for heavy-ion fusion reactions and alpha decay, incorporating proximity effects between nucleons in a neck, mass and charge asymmetry, a precise nuclear radius formula, and shell effects from the Droplet Model. For the entrance channel, they studied the dependence of barrier shapes on reaction asymmetry and proton magic number (114, 120, or 126), and for alpha decay they applied a unified fission model treating alpha emission as very asymmetric spontaneous fission.

## Key numbers
- The nuclear radius formula used is R_i = (1.28A_i^{1/3} - 0.76 + 0.8A_i^{-1/3}) fm, giving r_0 = 1.13 fm for 48Ca and r_0 = 1.18 fm for 248Cm
- The proximity surface width parameter b = 0.99 fm
- The surface parameter gamma = 0.9517 sqrt((1-2.6I_1^2)(1-2.6I_2^2)) MeV fm^{-2}
- The assault frequency for alpha decay is taken as nu_0 = 10^20 s^{-1}
- For frontal collisions, the fusion radius formula is R_fus,l=0 (fm) = (A_1^{1/3}+A_2^{1/3})[1.908 - 0.0857 ln(Z_1Z_2) + 3.94/(Z_1Z_2)]
- The external barrier peak energy relative to sphere energy is positive for Z_1Z_2 below approximately 2500, and there is no real external macroscopic barrier for Z_1Z_2 above approximately 3200
- For the heaviest elements, predicted alpha decay half-lives range from microseconds to some days, except for nuclei 319,320126 and 317124 which have very high alpha decay half-lives due to very low Q_alpha
- The fission barrier height can reach 10 MeV when neutron and proton shell effects add together

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous GLDM studies of fusion (Royer and Remaud, 1985), fission (Royer and Zbiri, 2002), and alpha emission (Royer, 2000) to superheavy element formation, building on experimental synthesis of heavy elements using reactions such as Ni on Bi and Zn on Pb (Hofmann, 1995, 1996) and Ca on U, Pu, and Cm (Oganessian, 1999, 2000). The predictions enable evaluation of proposed new reactions using other isotopes, inverse kinematics, and radioactive beams (Gupta, 2001; Petrov, 2001; Kumar, 2003), and the alpha decay half-life formulas provide testable predictions for future experiments on elements up to Z=126.
