# Neutrino-nucleus reactions and nuclear structure

> The methods used in the evaluation of the neutrino-nucleus cross section are reviewed. Results are shown for a variety of targets of practical importance. Many of the described reactions are accessible in future experiments with neutrino sources from the pion and muon decays at rest, which might be available at the neutron spallation facilities. Detailed comparison between the experimental and theoretical results would establish benchmarks needed for verification and/or parameter adjustment of the nuclear models. Having a reliable tool for such calculation is of great importance in a variety of applications, e.g. the neutrino oscillation studies, detection of supernova neutrinos, description of the neutrino transport in supernovae, and description of the r-process nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

The paper reviews theoretical methods for calculating neutrino-nucleus cross sections across a wide energy range (few MeV to few GeV), arguing that no single nuclear model works for all energies; instead, the shell model is appropriate below about 50 MeV, the continuum random phase approximation (CRPA) is suitable from about 100 to 500 MeV, and the relativistic Fermi gas model works above roughly 300 MeV, with overlapping regions where different methods give essentially identical results.

## Method

The authors derive cross sections using the standard current-current form of the weak interaction Hamiltonian, performing a multipole expansion of the weak nuclear current and applying the extreme relativistic limit for the final lepton. They calculate cross sections for charged-current and neutral-current reactions using three distinct approaches: the nuclear shell model (complete diagonalization in model spaces up to 4 ħω for light nuclei), the continuum random phase approximation (CRPA) with finite-range residual forces based on the Bonn potential, and the relativistic Fermi gas model. For reactions leading to particle emission, they use a two-step process: first calculating the primary cross section as a function of excitation energy, then computing branching ratios into decay channels (proton, neutron, alpha, and gamma emission) using the statistical model code SMOKER.

## Context

This review extends and systematizes earlier work on neutrino-nucleus reactions, building on the formalism of Walecka (1975) and Donnelly and Haxton (1979). It compares three theoretical approaches: the nuclear shell model (exemplified by Haxton (1987) for ¹⁶O), the continuum random phase approximation (CRPA) developed by Kolbe (1992) and others, and the relativistic Fermi gas model of Moniz (1971). The paper identifies a persistent discrepancy between CRPA calculations and the LSND measurement of the inclusive ¹²C(ν_μ,μ)¹²N* cross section, which remains unresolved and motivates future large-scale shell model calculations including up to 3ħω excitations. The results enable benchmark tests at spallation neutron sources and provide cross sections needed for supernova neutrino detection, neutrino oscillation studies, and r-process nucleosynthesis calculations.
