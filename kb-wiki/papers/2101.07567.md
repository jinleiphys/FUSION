# Alpha-Cluster formation in heavy alpha-emitters within a multistep model

> $α$-decay always has enormous impetuses to the development of physics and chemistry, in particular due to its indispensable role in the research of new elements. Although it has been observed in laboratories for more than a century, it remains a difficult problem to calculate accurately the formation probability $S_α$ microscopically. To this end, we establish a new model, i.e., multistep model, and the corresponding formation probability $S_α$ values of some typical $α$-emitters are calculated without adjustable parameters. The experimental half-lives, in particular their irregular behavior around a shell closure, are remarkably well reproduced by half-life laws combined with these $S_α$. In our strategy, the cluster formation is a gradual process in heavy nuclei, different from the situation that cluster pre-exists in light nuclei. The present study may pave the way to a fully understanding of $α$-decay from the perspective of nuclear structure.

# Alpha-Cluster formation in heavy alpha-emitters within a multistep model

## Key claim

The authors propose a nucleonic self-assembly model for alpha-cluster formation in heavy nuclei, where the alpha particle does not pre-exist inside the parent nucleus but is generated gradually during the decay process through a spontaneous assembly of four nucleons. The model reproduces experimental half-lives remarkably well when combined with half-life laws, but the authors acknowledge that computing the formation probability with very high accuracy is out of reach at present because single-particle levels are not well-defined in the mean-field approximation, especially for well-deformed nuclei.

## Method

The authors introduce an intermediate configuration (IC) where a single-neutron level and a single-proton level in the parent nucleus are fully occupied, providing exactly four nucleons to form an alpha particle. The formation probability is calculated as a sum over all possible pathways (different ICs) using overlap integrals between parent, intermediate, and daughter wavefunctions within a BCS formulation, with single-particle properties determined from a covariant density functional approach using the NL3 interaction for the mean-field and the D1S Gogny force for pairing. The calculated formation probabilities are then inserted into the Viola-Seaborg formula and the universal decay law to compute half-lives, which are compared with experimental data for even-even Po, Rn, and Ra isotopes with spherical or near-spherical shapes.

## Key numbers

- The root-mean-square deviation for the Viola-Seaborg formula with S_alpha=1 is 0.360, which reduces to 0.109 when the calculated S_alpha is included.
- The root-mean-square deviation for the universal decay law with S_alpha=1 is 0.316, which reduces to 0.0888 when the calculated S_alpha is included.
- The universal decay law with S_alpha reproduces experimental half-lives within approximately 20% (average deviation 0.0812).
- The correlation coefficient for the universal decay law fitting with S_alpha is r=0.9998.
- The S_alpha value for 212Po is about six times larger than that of its neighbor 210Po.
- For the superheavy nuclei, the calculated S_alpha is 0.66 for 292Lv and 0.45 for 294Og, giving a ratio S_alpha(292Lv)/S_alpha(294Og)=1.5.
- The extracted ratio from experimental data is 3.2^{+4.3}_{-1.8} (using data from Dubna) or 4.5^{+5.8}_{-2.2} (using data from GSI).
- The predicted S_alpha for 104Te is 0.24.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to calculate alpha-formation probability microscopically, which has been pursued since Mang (1960) using shell models, BCS models, and Skyrme energy density functionals. The authors distinguish their approach from the quartetting wave function approach (QWFA) used for Po isotopes, which does not employ overlap integrals between initial and final states. The model enables predictions for superheavy nuclei, suggesting the probable proton-magic nature of Z=120 based on comparison with experimental data for 292Lv and 294Og, and could help clarify the role of isoscalar pairing in self-conjugate nuclei like 104Te when combined with precise alpha-decay measurements.
