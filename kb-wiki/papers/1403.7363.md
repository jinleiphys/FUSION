# Effects of medium modification of pion production threshold in heavy ion collisions and the nuclear symmetry energy

> Using the relativistic Vlasov-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (RVUU) equation based on mean fields from the nonlinear relativistic models, we study the effect of medium modification of pion production threshold on the total pion yield and the $π^-/π^+$ ratio in Au+Au collisions. We find that the in-medium threshold effect enhances both the total pion yield and the $π^-/π^+$ ratio, compared to those without this effect. Furthermore, including the medium modification of the pion production threshold in asymmetric nuclear matter leads to a larger $π^-/π^+$ ratio for the $NLρδ$ model with a stiffer symmetry energy than the $NLρ$ model with a softer symmetry energy, opposite to that found without the in-medium threshold effect. Experimental data from the FOPI Collaboration are reproduced after including a density-dependent cross section for $Δ$ baryon production from nucleon-nucleon collisions, which suppresses the total pion yield but hardly changes the $π^-/π^+$ ratio. The large errors in the experimentally measured $π^-/π^+$ ratio prevent, however, the distinction between the predictions from the $NLρ$ and $NLρδ$ models.

## Key claim

The in-medium modification of the pion production threshold reverses the effect of nuclear symmetry energy on the \(\pi^-/\pi^+\) ratio in Au+Au collisions, making the ratio larger for the stiffer symmetry energy (\(NL\rho\delta\) model) than for the softer one (\(NL\rho\) model), opposite to the trend found without this threshold effect. However, the large experimental errors in the measured \(\pi^-/\pi^+\) ratio prevent distinguishing between the two models.

## Method

The authors used the relativistic Vlasov-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (RVUU) transport model, based on nonlinear relativistic mean-field models (\(NL\rho\) and \(NL\rho\delta\)), to simulate Au+Au collisions at beam energies from 0.3 AGeV to 0.7 AGeV. They included the covariant in-medium modification of the threshold energy for \(\Delta\) resonance production in nucleon-nucleon collisions, as well as a density-dependent cross section for \(\Delta\) production, and compared the resulting total pion yields and \(\pi^-/\pi^+\) ratios to experimental data from the FOPI Collaboration.

## Key numbers

- The binding energy per nucleon of symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density \(\rho_0 = 0.16\ \mathrm{fm^{-3}}\) is 16 MeV in both the \(NL\rho\) and \(NL\rho\delta\) models.
- The incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density is about 240 MeV in both models.
- The symmetry energy at saturation density is about 30 MeV in both models.
- In nuclear matter at \(\rho_N = 0.3\ \mathrm{fm^{-3}}\) and isospin asymmetry \(\alpha = 0.5\), the difference in the vector mean-field potential (\(\Sigma^0\)) between initial and final states for \(\Delta\) production is 373 MeV in the \(NL\rho\delta\) model and 112 MeV in the \(NL\rho\) model; the scalar mean-field difference (\(\Sigma^s\)) is \(-122\) MeV in the \(NL\rho\delta\) model.
- The total pion yield from the FOPI Collaboration is reproduced by introducing a density-dependent \(\Delta\) production cross section with a fitting parameter \(A = 1.2\) in the exponential suppression factor \(\exp(-A\rho_N/\rho_0)\) for both models.

## Context

This work extends the study of Ferrini et al. (2005), which first pointed out the in-medium threshold effect on pion production but did not compare with experimental data and did not treat the effect in a fully covariant way. The present study improves on that treatment and compares results to Au+Au collision data from the FOPI Collaboration (Reisdorf et al., 2006). The paper shows that the threshold effect must be included when extracting the high-density behavior of the nuclear symmetry energy from the \(\pi^-/\pi^+\) ratio, as it can cancel or reverse the sensitivity to the symmetry energy stiffness. The results enable future work to use more precise experimental data to distinguish between soft and stiff symmetry energy models.
