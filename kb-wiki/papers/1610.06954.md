# Phonon-particle coupling effects in odd-even double mass differences of semi-magic nuclei

> A method is developed to consider the particle-phonon coupling (PC) effects in the calculation of the odd-even double mass differences (DMD) in semi-magic nuclei starting from the free $NN$-potential. The PC correction $δΣ^{\rm PC}$ to the mass operator $Σ$ is found in $g_L^2$-approximation, $g_L$ being the vertex of creating the $L$-phonon. The tadpole term of the operator $δΣ^{\rm PC}$ is taken into account. The method is based on a direct, without any use of the perturbation theory, solution of the Dyson equation with the mass operator $Σ(ε){=}Σ_0{+}δΣ^{\rm PC}(ε)$ for finding the single-particle energies and $Z$-factors. In its turn, they are used as an input for finding different PC corrections to the DMD values. Results for a chain of even semi-magic nuclei $^{200-206}$Pb show that the inclusion of the PC corrections makes agreement with the experimental data significantly better.

## Key claim

The paper develops a method to include particle-phonon coupling (PC) corrections in calculations of odd-even double mass differences (DMD) for semi-magic nuclei, where the standard perturbation theory fails due to low-lying collective phonons. The method solves the Dyson equation directly without perturbation theory, and the inclusion of PC corrections significantly improves agreement with experimental data for the chain of even semi-magic nuclei 200-206Pb.

## Method

The method starts from the free NN-potential (Argonne v18) and uses a two-step renormalization approach splitting the two-particle Hilbert space into a model subspace (states with energies below 40 MeV) and a complementary subspace. For the valence subspace containing two shells near the Fermi level, the Dyson equation is solved directly (without perturbation theory) to find single-particle energies and Z-factors, which are then used to compute PC corrections to DMD values including the phonon-induced interaction and tadpole terms. The calculations use the DF3-a version of the Fayans energy density functional to generate the self-consistent basis and include two L-phonons (2+1 and 3-1).

## Key numbers

- The separation energy E0 used to split the model subspace is 40 MeV
- The normalization factor C0 in the semi-microscopic model is 300 MeV·fm3
- The phenomenological parameter gamma is 0.06 without PC corrections, reduced to 0.03 with PC corrections
- In 204Pb, the 2+ phonon energy omega2 is 0.88 MeV and the energy difference eps(3s1/2)-eps(2d3/2) is 0.79 MeV, leading to energy denominators of approximately 0.1 MeV
- For 200Pb: D2- (experimental) = 1.0878(675) MeV, D2+ (experimental) = -1.345(56) MeV; PC-corrected with gamma=0.03 gives D2- = 1.166 MeV, D2+ = -1.219 MeV
- For 202Pb: D2- (experimental) = 1.0856(324) MeV, D2+ (experimental) = -1.233(41) MeV; PC-corrected with gamma=0.03 gives D2- = 1.117 MeV, D2+ = -1.225 MeV
- For 204Pb: D2- (experimental) = 0.9333(44) MeV, D2+ (experimental) = -1.1694(152) MeV; PC-corrected with gamma=0.03 gives D2- = 0.824 MeV, D2+ = -1.110 MeV
- For 206Pb: D2- (experimental) = 0.8347(43) MeV, D2+ (experimental) = -1.1461(78) MeV; PC-corrected with gamma=0.03 gives D2- = 0.856 MeV, D2+ = -1.136 MeV
- The rms difference between theory and experiment is 0.849 MeV for the pure free-potential calculation, 0.216 MeV for the semi-microscopic model with gamma=0.06, 0.167 MeV for PC-corrected with gamma=0, and 0.087 MeV for PC-corrected with gamma=0.03

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous method for PC corrections in magic nuclei (Saperstein et al. 2015, 2016) to semi-magic nuclei, where the perturbation theory used previously fails due to low-lying collective 2+ phonons. The approach builds on the semi-microscopic model developed by Pankratov et al. (2011, 2012) for the pairing problem and the DMD calculations without PC corrections by Gnezdilov et al. (2014). The method enables more accurate calculations of nuclear masses and pairing gaps in semi-magic nuclei starting from a free NN-potential, and the results suggest that PC corrections account for a significant portion of the many-body effects previously absorbed into the phenomenological parameter gamma.
