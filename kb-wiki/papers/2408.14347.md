# Analysis of flow factorization and event-plane correlations based on a maximum likelihood estimator

> In this study, we use the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) to explore factorization and event-plane correlations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Our analyses incorporate both numerical simulations and publicly available data from the CMS Collaboration. We focus on Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV and Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV. The differential flows obtained for various centrality windows and momentum cuts are consistent with conventional methodologies such as multi-particle cumulants and event-plane methods. Leveraging these findings, we proceed to undertake further analysis of flow factorization and event-plane correlations. These quantities are relevant because of their sensitivity to initial-state fluctuations. While higher-order correlators might provide different implementations of factorization ratio, the MLE estimator is readily applied to these scenarios. Moreover, MLE's unique capabilities allow us to compute specific correlators that are typically inaccessible by other means. As an asymptotically normal and unbiased estimator, MLE provides a valuable alternative tool for flow and correlation analysis.

## Key claim

The maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) provides a viable alternative to conventional flow analysis methods (multi-particle cumulants and event-plane methods) for extracting flow harmonics and event-plane correlations, with the specific advantage that it can compute correlators typically inaccessible by other means, though the agreement with conventional methods is primarily demonstrated for differential flows and factorization ratios under the assumption of independent particle emission.

## Method

The authors apply the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) to extract flow harmonics \(v_n\) and event planes \(\Psi_n\) by maximizing the log-likelihood function constructed from the one-particle azimuthal distribution, assuming independent and identically distributed particle emission. They compare MLE results with two-particle cumulants, four-particle cumulants, and the event-plane method using both hydrodynamic simulations (NeXSPheRIO code) and CMS Open Data for Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV and Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV. The analysis covers differential flow as a function of transverse momentum, integrated flow for various momentum intervals, and the factorization ratio \(r_n(p_{\rm T}^{\rm a},p_{\rm T}^{\rm t})\) evaluated from event-by-event flow vectors.

## Key numbers

* For Au+Au at 200 GeV, 0-5% centrality, integrated elliptic flow \(v_2\) in the momentum interval \(0.1 < p_{\rm T} < 0.5\) GeV: MLE gives \(0.025 \pm 0.020\), event-plane method gives \(0.027 \pm 0.014\), two-particle cumulant gives \(0.027 \pm 0.015\), and four-particle cumulant gives \(0.028 \pm 0.009\).
* For Au+Au at 200 GeV, 0-5% centrality, integrated triangular flow \(v_3\) in the momentum interval \(0.1 < p_{\rm T} < 0.5\) GeV: MLE gives \(0.024 \pm 0.019\), event-plane method gives \(0.025 \pm 0.013\), two-particle cumulant gives \(0.025 \pm 0.013\), and four-particle cumulant gives \(0.028 \pm 0.007\).
* For Au+Au at 200 GeV, 0-5% centrality, the standard deviation of elliptic flow \(\sigma_{v_2}\) from MLE is approximately 0.0155, stable across 200 to 1900 events; the standard deviation of the event plane \(\sigma_{\Psi_2}\) is approximately 0.842 for 200 events and 0.899 for 1900 events.
* For Au+Au at 200 GeV, 0-5% centrality, the factorization ratio \(r_2\) shows a relative deviation of about 20% at the largest transverse momentum difference \(p_{\rm T}^{\rm a} - p_{\rm T}^{\rm t} \sim 2\) GeV using particle cumulants; for 10-20% centrality, the maximal difference in \(r_2\) is about 10%.
* The differential flow analysis for Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV uses CMS Open Data with 2956 events, focusing on the transverse momentum interval \(0.9 < p_{\rm T} < 1.7\) GeV; only 720 events contain more than 500 charged hadrons.

## Context

This work extends the MLE-based flow analysis previously introduced by some of the authors (sph-vn-10) from integrated flow to differential flow and multi-particle correlators. It builds on the established framework of flow harmonics extraction using particle correlations (Borghini 2000, Bilandzic 2010) and event-plane methods (Poskanzer 1998), and addresses the factorization breakdown phenomenon studied by ALICE (2011), Gardim (2012), and Heinz (2013) as a probe of initial-state fluctuations. The results enable further investigation of event-plane decorrelation across transverse momentum and higher-order mixed harmonic factorization, particularly for correlators that are not straightforward to compute with particle-correlation methods unless event planes are perfectly correlated.
