# A compact star rotating at 1122 Hz and the r-mode instability

> We show that r-mode instabilities severely constraint the composition of a compact star rotating at a submillisecond period. In particular, the only viable astrophysical scenario for such an object, present inside the Low Mass X-ray Binary associated with the x-ray transient XTE J1739-285, is that it has a strangeness content. Since previous analysis indicate that hyperonic stars or stars containing a kaon condensate are not good candidates, the only remaining possibility is that such an object is either a strange quark star or a hybrid quark-hadron star. We also discuss under which conditions sub-millisecond pulsars are rare.

# A compact star rotating at 1122 Hz and the r-mode instability

## Key claim
The paper argues that a compact star rotating at 1122 Hz, as possibly observed in the Low Mass X-ray Binary (LMXB) associated with XTE J1739-285, can only be a strange quark star or a hybrid quark-hadron star, because r-mode instabilities exclude neutron stars, hyperonic stars, and stars with kaon condensates from reaching such a high rotation frequency. The viability of this scenario depends critically on the value of the strange quark mass and on whether the star is spinning up by accretion or spinning down from birth.

## Method
The authors analyze the r-mode instability window in the angular velocity–temperature (Ω–T) plane for compact stars of various compositions, using the standard equations for the temporal evolution of angular velocity, temperature, and r-mode amplitude from Andersson (2001), with the addition of reheating from bulk viscosity dissipation. They compare the instability windows for neutron stars, hyperonic stars, hybrid stars, and strange quark stars, and compute the time evolution of a star rotating at 1122 Hz under different assumptions for the strange quark mass (100 MeV and 300 MeV) and cooling rates (fast vs. slow).

## Key numbers
- The observed pulsation frequency of the X-ray transient XTE J1739-285 is 1122 ± 0.3 Hz.
- For a strange quark mass of 300 MeV, the time spent by a star rotating above 1122 Hz is of order 10^8 years, compatible with the typical age of LMXBs (10^8–10^9 years).
- For a strange quark mass of 100 MeV, the time spent above 1122 Hz is of order 100 years, incompatible with LMXB ages.
- The High Temperature Instability Window (HTIW) starts at temperatures larger than (7 ± 3) × 10^9 K.
- The Low Temperature Instability Window (LTIW) is at temperatures smaller than (5 ± 4) × 10^8 K.
- For fast cooling with direct URCA processes, the inner temperature of an accreting star is ~5 × 10^7 K for a mass accretion rate of 10^−10 M⊙/year, scaling as T ∝ Ṁ^{1/6}.
- To reach T ≥ 10^8 K via accretion reheating with ms = 300 MeV, a mass accretion rate of ~10^−8 M⊙/year is needed.
- Neutron stars cannot spin up to rotational periods shorter than ~1.5 ms based on temperature and accretion rate estimates.
- Hybrid or hyperonic stars cannot recycle to frequencies significantly exceeding 700–800 Hz if they are to the left of the LTIW.

## Context
This work extends the analysis of r-mode instabilities in compact stars, building on the foundational work of Andersson (1998) and Friedman (1998) on the instability window, and on Bildsten (1999) on viscous boundary layers. It directly addresses the observational claim by Kaaret (2006) of a 1122 Hz pulsar in XTE J1739-285, and the subsequent equation-of-state constraints from Lavagetto (2006) and Bejger (2006). The paper shows that previous conclusions ruling out hyperonic stars and kaon condensates (Ozel 2006) are consistent, and that only quark or hybrid stars with a sufficiently large strange quark mass (~300 MeV) can satisfy both the r-mode stability constraints and the age of LMXBs. The inclusion of reheating from bulk viscosity, neglected in earlier work by Wagoner (2002) and Reisenegger (2003), is crucial for obtaining long spin-down times. Future observations of XTE J1739-285 could test whether it is indeed a quark or hybrid star.
