# Improved Mass and Radius Constraints for Quiescent Neutron Stars in Omega Cen and NGC 6397

> We use Chandra and XMM observations of the globular clusters $ω$ Cen and NGC 6397 to measure the spectrum of their quiescent neutron stars (NSs), and thus to constrain the allowed ranges of mass and radius for each. We also use Hubble Space Telescope photometry of NGC 6397 to identify a potential optical companion to the quiescent NS, and find evidence that the companion lacks hydrogen. We carefully consider a number of systematic problems, and show that the choices of atmospheric composition, interstellar medium abundances, and cluster distances can have important effects on the inferred NS mass and radius. We find that for typical NS masses, the radii of both NSs are consistent with the 10-13 km range favored by recent nuclear physics experiments. This removes the evidence suggested by Guillot and collaborators for an unusually small NS radius, which relied upon the small inferred radius of the NGC 6397 NS.

## Key claim

For typical neutron star masses, the radii of both quiescent neutron stars in Omega Cen and NGC 6397 are consistent with the 10-13 km range favored by recent nuclear physics experiments, removing the evidence previously suggested by Guillot and collaborators for an unusually small neutron star radius that relied upon the small inferred radius of the NGC 6397 neutron star.

## Method

The authors analyzed Chandra and XMM-Newton X-ray spectra of quiescent neutron stars in the globular clusters Omega Cen and NGC 6397, fitting hydrogen and helium atmosphere models to constrain mass and radius. They also performed Hubble Space Telescope photometry of NGC 6397 to identify a potential optical companion to the quiescent neutron star U24, measuring its R-band magnitude and placing upper limits on B-band and H-alpha flux to constrain the companion's nature and hydrogen content.

## Key numbers

- For the Omega Cen neutron star with mass fixed to 1.4 solar masses, distance 5.3 kpc, and using the tbabs ISM model, the best-fit radius is 16.3 km with 90% confidence range 11.3-23.0 km
- For the Omega Cen neutron star using the same assumptions as Guillot et al. (distance 4.8 kpc, wabs ISM model, mass 1.4 solar masses), the best-fit radius is 20.6 km with 90% confidence range 18.1-23.5 km
- The possible optical counterpart to U24 in NGC 6397 has R magnitude 26.2 plus or minus 0.3, corresponding to M_R = 13.7 and a maximum companion mass of approximately 0.089 solar masses if a main-sequence star
- Upper limits on the U24 counterpart: B greater than 26.8, H-alpha greater than 25.5, giving B-R greater than 0.6 and H-alpha-R greater than -0.7
- The upper limit on the equivalent width of H-alpha for the putative U24 counterpart is 19 Angstroms
- Adopted distance for Omega Cen: 5.30 plus or minus 0.17 kpc
- Adopted distance for NGC 6397: 2.51 plus or minus 0.07 kpc

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Guillot et al. (2013), who conducted a Bayesian multi-object spectral analysis of five quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries in globular clusters and found an extremely small radius for the NGC 6397 neutron star (R = 6.6 km) and an extremely large radius for the Omega Cen neutron star (R = 20.1 km). The authors demonstrate that systematic uncertainties in interstellar medium modeling, atmospheric composition, and cluster distances significantly affect inferred neutron star radii, and that using updated ISM models (Wilms et al. 2000 instead of Morrison and McCammon 1983) and revised cluster distances reconciles the measurements with the 10-13 km radius range from nuclear physics. The H-alpha non-detection for the NGC 6397 neutron star companion suggests a hydrogen-deficient (likely white dwarf) donor, motivating the use of helium atmosphere models which would give larger inferred radii.
