# Exact Treatment of Continuum Couplings in Nuclear Optical Potentials via Feshbach Theory

> We present the first numerically exact implementation of full-coupling Feshbach theory for deriving effective optical potentials in nuclear reactions, overcoming long-standing computational barriers that previously necessitated weak-coupling approximations. By integrating Feshbach projection operators with the Continuum-Discretized Coupled-Channels (CDCC) method, we rigorously incorporate all continuum-continuum interactions, previously considered intractable, to extract dynamic polarization potentials that capture virtual excitations and absorption in reactions with weakly bound nuclei. Application to deuteron-induced reactions on $^{58}$Ni demonstrates that our full-coupling effective potentials reproduce CDCC observables exactly, while weak-coupling and folding-model approaches show significant deviations. The method uniquely separates elastic breakup from breakup-fusion processes through the optical theorem, enabling precise determination of incomplete and complete fusion cross sections, critical for interpreting rare-isotope beam experiments at facilities like FRIB. The non-local polarization potentials provide insights into the energy-dependent interplay between reaction mechanisms, with our analysis suggesting an increasing relative importance of elastic breakup at higher energies while continuum absorption shows more complex behavior. This exact treatment eliminates systematic uncertainties from coupling approximations, providing predictive power for unstable nuclei far from stability where experimental data are scarce.

# Exact Treatment of Continuum Couplings in Nuclear Optical Potentials via Feshbach Theory

## Key claim

The authors present the first numerically exact implementation of full-coupling Feshbach theory for deriving effective optical potentials, eliminating the weak-coupling approximations that prior studies relied upon; the method reproduces CDCC observables exactly for deuteron-induced reactions on ⁵⁸Ni, but the demonstration is limited to a simplified model where particle spins are ignored and closed channels are not included.

## Method

The authors integrate Feshbach projection operators with the Continuum-Discretized Coupled-Channels (CDCC) method, dividing the projectile's model space into a bound-state subspace (P) and a discretized continuum subspace (Q). They construct the coupled-channel Green's function within the Q-space from regular and irregular solutions of the QHQ Hamiltonian, yielding a non-diagonal Green's function matrix that fully incorporates continuum-continuum couplings. The resulting dynamic polarization potential is obtained as a partial-wave-dependent nonlocal effective optical potential, and the optical theorem is applied within the P-space to separate elastic breakup from breakup-fusion absorption.

## Key numbers

- Elastic scattering cross sections for d + ⁵⁸Ni are computed at incident energies of 20 MeV, 30 MeV, 40 MeV, and 56 MeV, with the full-coupling effective potential reproducing CDCC results exactly across all four energies.
- The total angular momentum is truncated at J_max = 120 for the d + ⁵⁸Ni calculations.
- The p-n continuum states are included up to ℓ ≤ 2 partial waves.
- The dynamic polarization potential for d + ⁵⁸Ni at 56 MeV with J = 0 is spatially confined within 10 fm.
- The elastic breakup cross section increases gradually with incident energy, while the absorption cross section from the dynamic polarization potential exhibits a bell-shaped behavior: increasing, plateauing, then slightly decreasing at higher energies.

## Context

This work extends the Feshbach projection formalism (Feshbach, 1958; Feshbach, 1962) by overcoming computational barriers that previously forced weak-coupling approximations, as used by Sargsyan (2024) and Rao (1973). The method directly addresses the need for microscopic optical potentials for exotic, weakly bound nuclei at facilities like FRIB, RAON, and HIAF, where traditional phenomenological potentials parameterized from stable nuclei (CH89; KD02) prove inadequate. The approach enables precise determination of incomplete and complete fusion cross sections and naturally extends to other strongly-coupled quantum systems such as ultracold atomic gases and molecular dynamics where perturbative approaches fail.
