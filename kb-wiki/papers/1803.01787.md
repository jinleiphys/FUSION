# Impact of Fission Neutron Energies on Reactor Antineutrino Spectra

> Recent measurements of reactor-produced antineutrino fluxes and energy spectra are inconsistent with models based on measured thermal fission beta spectra. In this paper, we examine the dependence of antineutrino production on fission neutron energy. In particular, the variation of fission product yields with neutron energy has been considered as a possible source of the discrepancies between antineutrino observations and models. In simulations of low-enriched and highly-enriched reactor core designs, we find a substantial fraction of fissions (from 5% to more than 40%) are caused by non-thermal neutrons. Using tabulated evaluations of nuclear fission and decay, we estimate the variation in antineutrino emission by the prominent fission parents U-235, Pu-239, and Pu-241 versus neutron energy. The differences in fission neutron energy are found to produce less than 1% variation in detected antineutrino rate per fission of U-235, Pu-239, and Pu-241. Corresponding variations in the antineutrino spectrum are found to be less than 10% below 7 MeV antineutrino energy, smaller than current model uncertainties. We conclude that insufficient modeling of fission neutron energy is unlikely to be the cause of the various reactor anomalies. Our results also suggest that comparisons of antineutrino measurements at low-enriched and highly-enriched reactors can safely neglect the differences in the distributions of their fission neutron energies.

## Key claim

The variation in antineutrino production due to differences in fission neutron energy produces less than 1% variation in detected antineutrino rate per fission and less than 10% variation in the antineutrino spectrum below 7 MeV, which is smaller than current model uncertainties; therefore, insufficient modeling of fission neutron energy is unlikely to be the cause of the reactor antineutrino anomalies.

## Method

The authors used MCNP5 reactor simulations of a Westinghouse pressurized water reactor (PWR), the High Flux Isotope Reactor (HFIR), and the National Bureau of Standards Reactor (NBSR) to estimate the fraction of fissions induced by thermal, resonant, and fast neutrons for each fissioning isotope. They then applied the summation method using the Oklo toolkit with JEFF-3.1.1 fission yields and ENSDF nuclear structure data to calculate antineutrino spectra for each isotope under different neutron energy conditions, comparing the purely thermal case (as in the ILL beamline measurements) to the mixed-energy case representative of actual reactor cores.

## Key numbers

- In the PWR model, thermal neutrons account for 82.43% of U-235 fissions, 29.66% of Pu-239 fissions, and 37.79% of Pu-241 fissions at middle-of-cycle; resonant neutrons account for 15.34%, 69.44%, and 61.42% respectively
- The fraction of fissions caused by non-thermal neutrons ranges from 5% to more than 40% across the reactor designs studied
- For U-235, the LEU/thermal antineutrino spectrum ratio is within approximately 2.5% of unity across the entire energy spectrum
- For Pu-239 and Pu-241, deviations from unity in the LEU/thermal ratio reach as large as 8% in the 5-7 MeV energy range
- For a PWR-like reactor with fission fractions of 0.561 (U-235), 0.076 (U-238), 0.307 (Pu-239), and 0.056 (Pu-241), overall deviations from the pure-thermal spectrum prediction are no greater than 3.5% in the 5-7 MeV energy range

## Context

This work addresses the hypothesis raised by Hayes (2014) that differing fission neutron energies between reactor antineutrino measurements and the ILL thermal beamline beta spectrum measurements (Huber, 2011) could explain the reactor antineutrino flux anomaly and spectrum anomaly observed by Daya Bay (2016) and other experiments. The study extends prior work by Leconte (2012) and Gook (2017) on energy-dependent fission yields, and uses summation calculations similar to those of Dwyer (2015) and Sonzogni (2015) to quantify the effect. The results enable future comparisons of antineutrino measurements at low-enriched and highly-enriched reactors to safely neglect differences in fission neutron energy distributions.
