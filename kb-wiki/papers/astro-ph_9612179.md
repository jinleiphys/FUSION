# Constraining dense-matter superfluidity through thermal emission from millisecond pulsars

> As a neutron star spins down, the gradual decrease of the centrifugal force produces a progressive increase of the density of any given fluid element in its interior. Since the ``chemical'' (or ``beta'') equilibrium state is determined by the local density, this process leads to a chemical imbalance quantified by a chemical potential difference, e.g., δμ=μ_n-μ_p-μ_e, where n, p, and e denote neutrons, protons, and electrons. In the presence of superfluid energy gaps, in this case Δ_n and Δ_p, reactions are strongly inhibited as long as both δμand kT are much smaller than the gaps. Thus, no restoring mechanism is available, and the imbalance will grow unimpeded until δμ=δμ_{thr}=Δ_n+Δ_p. At this threshold, the reaction rate increases dramatically, preventing further growth of δμ, and converting the excess chemical energy into heat. The thermal luminosity resulting from this ``rotochemical heating'' process is $L\sim 2\times 10^{-4}(δμ_{thr}/0.1\MeV)\dot E_{rot}$, similar to the typical x-ray luminosity of pulsars with spin-down power \dot E_{rot}. The threshold imbalance, and therefore the luminous stage, are only reached by millisecond pulsars. A preliminary study of eleven millisecond pulsars with reported ROSAT observations shows that the latter can already be used to start constraining superfluid energy gaps in the theoretically interesting range, ~ 0.1 - 1 MeV.

## Key claim

Rotochemical heating, driven by the gradual spin-down of neutron stars, can produce thermal luminosities comparable to observed X-ray emission from millisecond pulsars only if the chemical imbalance reaches a threshold set by the sum of the neutron and proton superfluid energy gaps (δμ_thr ≈ Δ_n + Δ_p); this threshold is reached exclusively by stars with initial rotation periods P_i ≲ 12 (δμ_thr/0.1 MeV)^(1/2) ms, i.e., millisecond pulsars.

## Method

The author models the growth of chemical imbalance δμ = μ_n − μ_p − μ_e in a spinning-down neutron star core where direct Urca reactions (n → p + e + ν̄_e and p + e → n + ν_e) are suppressed by superfluid energy gaps Δ_n and Δ_p. The imbalance grows unimpeded until δμ reaches the threshold δμ_thr ≈ Δ_n + Δ_p, at which point reactions turn on rapidly, converting the excess chemical energy into heat at a steady rate. The resulting thermal luminosity is expressed as a fraction of the spin-down power Ė_rot, and the model is applied to ROSAT X-ray observations of eleven millisecond pulsars to derive upper limits on δμ_thr from their observed (or upper-limit) count rates, assuming blackbody spectra and estimated distances and hydrogen column densities.

## Key numbers

- Thermal luminosity from rotochemical heating: L ∼ 2 × 10^(−4) (δμ_thr / 0.1 MeV) Ė_rot
- Rotational energy loss required to reach threshold: ΔE_rot ≈ 1.4 × 10^50 (δμ_thr / 0.1 MeV) erg (assuming I = 10^45 g cm^2)
- Initial rotation period required to reach threshold: P_i ≲ 12 (δμ_thr / 0.1 MeV)^(−1/2) ms
- For pulsar B1937+21 (P = 1.6 ms, t_s = 0.2 Gyr): δμ_1 = 7.0 MeV, δμ_2 = 0.36 MeV
- For pulsar B1957+20 (P = 1.6 ms, t_s = 2.1 Gyr): δμ_1 = 7.0 MeV, δμ_2 = 0.44 MeV
- For pulsar B1821-24 (P = 3.1 ms, t_s = 0.03 Gyr): δμ_1 = 1.9 MeV, δμ_2 = 0.42 MeV
- For pulsar J0437-4715 (P = 5.7 ms, t_s = 5 Gyr): δμ_1 = 0.55 MeV, δμ_2 = 1.4 MeV
- For pulsar B1257+12 (P = 6.2 ms, t_s = 3.2 Gyr): δμ_1 = 0.47 MeV, δμ_2 = 0.33 MeV
- Theoretically interesting superfluid energy gap range: ∼ 0.1 – 1 MeV

## Context

This work extends the rotochemical heating mechanism introduced by Reisenegger (1995) to include the effects of nucleon superfluidity, which delays the onset of chemical equilibration and enhances the resulting thermal emission. It provides a new observational method to constrain superfluid energy gaps in neutron star cores, complementing earlier attempts using cooling neutron star observations by Page and Applegate (1992) and Page (1994, 1995, 1996). The preliminary analysis of eleven millisecond pulsars observed with ROSAT demonstrates that existing X-ray data can already probe the theoretically predicted gap range, and deeper observations with better spectral resolution combined with realistic atmosphere models (Zavlin, Pavlov, and Shibanov 1996) could yield tighter constraints.
