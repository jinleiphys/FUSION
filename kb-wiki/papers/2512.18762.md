# Quantum-inspired Bayesian probability algorithm for nuclear mass predictions

> In this study, a novel quantum-inspired Bayesian probability (QIBP) algorithm, informed by quantum dynamics, is proposed to improve the predictions of nuclear mass from theoretical models. Within the QIBP framework, residuals between the theoretical and experimental mass values are mapped into wave functions in Hilbert space. The corresponding potentials are obtained by solving the Schrödinger equation. Assuming that the residuals follow a Boltzmann distribution, the prior and likelihood probability density functions (PDFs) can be obtained from potentials. Finally, the Bayesian theorem is applied to derive the posterior PDF for estimating the target nuclear mass residuals. In global optimization, after employing the QIBP algorithm, the standard deviations of the WS4 model and the HFB model with the SLy4 parameter set are reduced from 0.273 MeV and 5.250 MeV to 0.149 MeV and 0.324 MeV, respectively. In extrapolation analysis, the QIBP algorithm also effectively improves both models, indicating robust extrapolation capability. In addition, extrapolation based on the synthetic experimental set shows that the QIBP algorithm performs well near the known region and remains effective for most nuclides toward the drip lines. Furthermore, the QIBP algorithm is applied to predict $α$-decay energies of Ra and Es isotopes, and the shell effects manifested in these isotopes are analyzed. This study validates the feasibility of quantum machine learning in nuclear mass research and demonstrates that the proposed algorithm can accurately describe nuclear masses, with potential applications in other areas of nuclear physics.

## Key claim

The quantum-inspired Bayesian probability (QIBP) algorithm reduces the standard deviation of nuclear mass predictions from theoretical models by mapping residuals into wave functions in Hilbert space and solving the Schrödinger equation, but its performance depends on the assumption that residuals follow a Boltzmann distribution and on the choice of smoothing parameters (β, β_Z, β_N) where β must be smaller than β_Z and β_N for stability.

## Method

The QIBP algorithm maps residuals between theoretical and experimental nuclear masses into wave functions using Gaussian kernels with a bandwidth parameter λ set to the standard deviation of the residuals, then solves the Schrödinger equation to obtain potentials. Assuming the residuals follow a Boltzmann distribution, the algorithm derives prior and likelihood probability density functions from these potentials and applies the multivariate Bayesian theorem to obtain a posterior PDF, whose expectation value gives the estimated residual for each target nucleus. Leave-one-out cross-validation was performed on 2253 nuclei with Z ≥ 20 and N ≥ 20 from AME2020, using the WS4 macroscopic-microscopic model and the HFB model with the SLy4 parameter set.

## Key numbers

- WS4 model standard deviation reduced from 0.273 MeV to 0.149 MeV (45.4% improvement) after QIBP refinement in global optimization
- HFB model with SLy4 parameter set standard deviation reduced from 5.250 MeV to 0.324 MeV (93.8% improvement) after QIBP refinement in global optimization
- For extrapolation to 70 newly added nuclei in AME2020, WS4 test set standard deviation reduced from 0.355 MeV to 0.216 MeV (39.2% improvement)
- For extrapolation to 70 newly added nuclei in AME2020, HFB test set standard deviation reduced from 6.367 MeV to 0.676 MeV (89.4% improvement)
- In synthetic extrapolation using WS4 as experimental set and HFB as theoretical model across 2297 nuclei, standard deviation reduced from 10.660 MeV to 4.532 MeV (57.5% improvement)
- Classical Bayesian (CBP) estimator achieved WS4 standard deviation of 0.187 MeV and HFB standard deviation of 0.509 MeV in global optimization, compared to QIBP's 0.149 MeV and 0.324 MeV

## Context

This work extends Bayesian inference approaches to nuclear mass predictions, specifically improving upon the continuous Bayesian probability (CBP) estimator (Liu et al., prior work) by replacing kernel density estimation with Schrödinger equation-derived potentials. The QIBP algorithm captures subtle patterns such as odd-even staggering and deformation effects that the WS4 model and the HFB model with SLy4 parameter set fail to incorporate, and it enables more accurate predictions of α-decay energies for Ra and Es isotopes as well as one-neutron separation energies for Pb isotopes.
