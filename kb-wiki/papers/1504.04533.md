# Exploring effects of magnetic field on the Hadron Resonance Gas

> We present a study of the effects of magnetic fields on fluctuations and correlations in hadron resonance gas model. We find significant changes in the fluctuations of net baryon number, electric charge and strangeness. This is also reflected in various fluctuation ratios along the freezeout curve.

# Exploring effects of magnetic field on the Hadron Resonance Gas

## Key claim
The Hadron Resonance Gas (HRG) model predicts that magnetic fields significantly enhance fluctuations of net baryon number, electric charge, and strangeness, as well as their correlations, but the study sets an upper limit for these effects because it assumes non-interacting hadrons with point-like masses unaffected by temperature, chemical potential, or magnetic field, and that the magnetic field survives long enough to affect hadrons.

## Method
The authors extended the standard HRG model to include a constant external magnetic field B_z, modifying single-particle energy levels for charged hadrons via Landau levels and for neutral hadrons via spin-field coupling, including anomalous magnetic moments for baryons. They computed second-order and fourth-order susceptibilities (diagonal fluctuations) and off-diagonal correlations of baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge at zero chemical potential as functions of temperature, and then evaluated experimentally accessible moment products (σ²/M, Sσ, κσ²) for net-proton, net-charge, and net-kaon along the freeze-out curve parameterized as a function of center-of-mass energy √s.

## Key numbers
- Magnetic field strengths considered: up to 15 m_π² (approximately 3 × 10¹⁹ G), corresponding to the predicted field for non-central heavy-ion collisions at LHC energies.
- The HRG model included all hadrons listed in the Particle Data Book (2014 edition) up to a mass of 3 GeV.
- For baryon number susceptibilities, the dominant contributors at low temperature are protons and neutrons; at high temperature, Δ⁺⁺(1232) particles (spin 3/2, doubly charged) cause susceptibilities to increase faster with magnetic field due to effective mass decrease and degeneracy effects.
- For strangeness susceptibilities, the magnitudes of χ_S² and χ_S⁴ are similar up to T ≈ 0.1 GeV; above T ≈ 0.12 GeV, magnetic field effects become noticeable as K*± (spin 1) populations increase.
- The magnetic diffusion time τ_mag in heavy-ion collisions can vary from 0.3 fm to 150 fm depending on the electrical conductivity of the medium.

## Context
This work extends the HRG model studies of fluctuations and correlations (previously done at zero magnetic field by Cleymans et al. (2005), Karsch et al. (2004), and Samanta et al. (2014)) to include magnetic fields, building on the hadronic equation of state for non-zero magnetic fields developed by Endrodi (2013). The results are relevant for interpreting experimental data from STAR (2014) on net-proton and net-charge fluctuations, and suggest that magnetic field should be considered as a freeze-out parameter in future analyses, particularly for peripheral collisions where spectator particles generate large fields. The study also connects to lattice QCD findings by Bali et al. (2012) that the critical temperature may increase with magnetic field, and to predictions of magnetic catalysis and inverse magnetic catalysis by Shovkovy (2013) and Preis et al. (2011).
