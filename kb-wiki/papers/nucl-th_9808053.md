# Coefficients of Fractional Parentage for Nuclear Shell Model

> A new procedure for the calculation of shell model coefficients of fractional parentage has been developed. This procedure is free of numerical diagonalization, orthogonalization, and even of grop theoretical classification of antisymmetric states.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new procedure for calculating coefficients of fractional parentage (CFP) that completely avoids numerical diagonalization, orthogonalization, and group-theoretical classification of antisymmetric states, instead relying on the spectral decomposition of the antisymmetrization operator matrix into a lower-triangular form.

## Method

The method constructs the matrix **A** of the antisymmetrization operator in a basis of vector-coupled parent states with lower antisymmetry, then performs its spectral decomposition **A** = **F****F**⁺ by imposing an upper-triangular zero condition on **F**. The matrix elements of **F** are the CFP, obtained by solving sequentially for each row using only the diagonal elements and the condition that **A** is a projection matrix (eigenvalues 0 or 1). The calculation uses exact arithmetic with numbers represented as n/m/√k where n, m, k are integers, avoiding floating-point approximations.

## Key numbers

- For the j = 5/2 nuclear shell, the complete set contains 3359 CFP total.
- The method imposes m(m-1)/2 zeros by zeroing the upper right corner of the **F** matrix, giving 141 zeros in the complete CFP set for j = 5/2.
- The actual number of CFP equal to zero is 328 due to possible non-accounted symmetries.
- For the N=4, J=6, T=0 state in the j=5/2 shell, the **A** matrix has dimension 5 and rank 2.
- Calculation of CFP for the j = 5/2 nuclear shell on a 25 MHz AT-386 computer took about 20 hours.

## Context

This work extends the iterative CFP procedure developed in classical works (Racah, 1943; Redmond, 1954) by eliminating the need for group-theoretical classification via Casimir operators (Racah, 1949) and avoiding numerical Schmidt orthogonalization or Grammian matrix diagonalization (Redmond, 1954). The method enables calculation of CFP for highly excited states and arbitrary configurations, including those where center-of-mass motion must be controlled, and is applicable to neutron-rich nuclei and neutron halo phenomena requiring high-quality wave functions beyond simple shell-model approximations.
