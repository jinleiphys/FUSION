# A study of randomness, correlations and collectivity in the nuclear shell model

> A variable combination of realistic and random two-body interactions allows the study of collective properties, such as the energy spectra and B(E2) transition strengths in 44Ti, 48Cr and 24Mg. It is found that the average energies of the yrast band states maintain the ordering for any degree of randomness, but the B(E2) values lose their quadrupole collectivity when randomness dominates the Hamiltonian. The high probability of the yrast band to be ordered in the presence of pure random forces exhibits the strong correlations between the different members of the band.

## Key claim

The average energies of yrast band states maintain their ordering for any degree of randomness in the Hamiltonian, but the B(E2) values lose their quadrupole collectivity when randomness dominates; the high probability of the yrast band to be ordered under pure random forces (25-45%) is far larger than the product of individual state probabilities, revealing strong correlations between band members that are not associated with quadrupole coherence.

## Method

The authors constructed Hamiltonians as a linear combination H = a H_C + b H_R, where H_C is a realistic interaction (Wildenthal for the sd-shell, KB3 for the fp-shell) and H_R is a two-body random ensemble (TBRE) with normally distributed matrix elements. They diagonalized these Hamiltonians for 960 samples each in 44Ti, 48Cr, and 24Mg using the Antoine shell-model code, varying the mixing parameter b from 0 to 1. They computed average energies, ordering probabilities, B(E2) transition strengths, energy ratios R = E(4+1)/E(2+1), and energy correlations between neighboring states.

## Key numbers

- For 48Cr with b=0.4, each state in the ground state band has at least 90% probability to occupy its physically expected place (e.g., J=0 at 96.56%, J=2 at 91.88%).
- For 48Cr with pure random forces (b=1.0), the probability of occupying the expected place ranges from 44% (J=2) to 83% (J=16); for 44Ti with b=1.0, probabilities range from 44% (J=2) to 95% (J=12).
- The experimental B(E2,2+ -> 0+) for 48Cr is 230 e^2b^2; for b=0.2 the distribution concentrates around this value, while for b=1.0 the distribution is strongly concentrated at very small values.
- The experimental B(E2,2+ -> 0+) for 44Ti is 147 e^2b^2; for b=0.2 the distribution is narrow around this value, and for b=1.0 collectivity is completely lost.
- Energy widths for 48Cr increase from 1.5 MeV at b=0.2 to 7-9 MeV at b=1.0.
- The probability for the entire ground state band to be ordered decreases to about 35% for a fully random Hamiltonian across all three nuclei.
- For 48Cr with a displaced TBRE (DTBRE) centered at c=-1.0 and width sigma=0.6, the probability for each state to occupy its expected place at b=1.0 is larger than 88% (e.g., J=0 at 88.12%, J=2 at 80.31%).
- Energy correlations r between neighboring states in 48Cr remain above 0.98 for all mixing parameters b from 0.2 to 1.0.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Coraggio et al. (1982) on the transition between random and collective behavior in 20Ne, and by Bijker et al. (2000) and others showing that bosonic spaces exhibit vibrational and rotational spectra even with random interactions. The authors use realistic interactions (Wildenthal for sd-shell, KB3 for fp-shell) and systematically vary the mixing with random forces, finding that while energy ordering persists, quadrupole collectivity is lost for pure random forces. The results enable further investigation into the geometric constraints imposed by two-body Hamiltonians, as suggested by Chau et al., and complement studies of displaced random ensembles by Velazquez et al. (2002) which preserve rotor patterns.
