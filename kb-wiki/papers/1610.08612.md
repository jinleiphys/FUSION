# Multiresolution decomposition of quantum field theories using wavelet bases

> We investigate both theoretical and computational aspects of using wavelet bases to decouple physics on different scales in quantum field theory.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that flow equation methods can be used to block-diagonalize a free scalar field Hamiltonian in a Daubechies wavelet basis, thereby decoupling short-distance degrees of freedom from long-distance ones, but only for the free field case where the operators generated by the flow remain quadratic and thus tractable.

## Method

The authors expand a free scalar field in 1+1 dimensions using Daubechies K=3 scaling functions and wavelets, which provide an orthonormal basis of compactly supported functions that decompose the Hilbert space by spatial resolution. They then apply Wegner-type flow equations, with a generator chosen as the commutator of the Hamiltonian with its scale-diagonal part, to continuously evolve the Hamiltonian toward a form where the scaling-function and wavelet degrees of freedom are decoupled. The constant coupling coefficients (the derivative matrix elements) are computed exactly using the renormalization group equation for the scaling function, reducing to nine non-zero rational numbers for the K=3 basis.

## Key numbers

* The Daubechies K=3 scaling function has support on the interval [0, 2K-1] = [0, 5]; the six scaling coefficients h_0 through h_5 are given explicitly as algebraic numbers involving sqrt(10) and sqrt(5+2*sqrt(10)).
* The derivative coupling matrix D^0_{s;0m} for the scaling functions has exactly nine non-zero coefficients, for m from -4 to 4, and these are rational numbers (computed in the appendix).
* The scaling property D^k_{s;mn} = 2^{2k} D^0_{s;mn} relates the derivative matrix at resolution k to the base resolution matrix.

## Context

This work extends the program of using wavelet bases for quantum field theory, following earlier work by Best (1994), Federbush (1995), and Battle (1999), by adding flow equation methods previously developed by Wegner, Glazek and Wilson (1993), and Perry. The wavelet approach provides an alternative to lattice truncations, with the advantage that large-scale degrees of freedom do not need to be generated from many small-scale ones. The paper tests the method only on a free scalar field; the authors note that extending to interacting theories would require managing an infinite set of generated effective operators, which is left as an open problem.
