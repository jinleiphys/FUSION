# Two-Pion Exchange in Proton-Proton Scattering

> The contribution of the box and crossed two-pion-exchange diagrams to proton-proton scattering at 90$^{\circ}_{c.m.}$ is calculated in the laboratory momentum range up to 12 GeV/c. Relativistic form factors related to the nucleon and pion size and representing the pion source distribution based on the quark structure of the hadronic core are included at each vertex of the pion-nucleon interaction. These form factors depend on the four-momenta of the exchanged pions and scattering nucleons. Feynman-diagram amplitudes calculated without form factors are checked against those derived from dispersion relations. In this comparison, one notices that a very short-range part of the crossed diagram, neglected in dispersion-relation calculations of the two-pion-exchange nucleon-nucleon potential, gives a sizable contribution. In the Feynman-diagram calculation with form factors the agreement with measured spin-separated cross sections, as well as amplitudes in the lower part of the energy range considered, is much better for pion-nucleon pseudo-vector vis à vis pseudo-scalar coupling. While strengths of the box and crossed diagrams are comparable for laboratory momenta below 2 GeV/c, the crossed diagram dominates for larger momenta, largely due to the kinematics of the crossed diagram allowing a smaller momentum transfer in the nucleon center of mass. An important contribution arises from the principal-value part of the integrals which is non-zero when form factors are included. It seems that the importance of the exchange of color singlets may extend higher in energy than expected.

## Key claim

The box and crossed two-pion-exchange diagrams, when calculated with relativistic form factors representing the quark structure of the nucleon core, can account for the magnitude of the proton-proton scattering cross section at 90° center-of-mass angle for laboratory momenta up to 12 GeV/c, suggesting that two-pion exchange may be the dominant mechanism in this intermediate energy region rather than being limited to low energies.

## Method

The authors calculated the Feynman amplitudes for the box and crossed two-pion-exchange diagrams in proton-proton scattering at 90° center-of-mass angle, using both pseudo-scalar (PS) and pseudo-vector (PV) pion-nucleon coupling. Relativistic form factors depending on the four-momenta of the exchanged pions and scattering nucleons were included at each vertex to represent the finite size of the nucleon and pion based on quark structure. The amplitudes were projected onto the Saclay decomposition (amplitudes a, b, c, d, e) and compared with experimental spin-separated cross sections and the spin correlation observable C_NN extracted from data by Lin, Bhatia, Crosbie, Akerlof, Kammerud, and Abe.

## Key numbers

- Laboratory momentum range studied: 0 to 12 GeV/c
- Pseudo-scalar coupling constant normalization: g²/4π = 13.75
- The spin correlation observable C_NN for proton-proton scattering at 90° center-of-mass decreases from near unity at low energies to a constant value of around 0.07 from 4 GeV/c to 8 GeV/c, then increases to values around 0.5
- One-pion exchange alone predicts a constant value of 1/3 for C_NN
- The dipole form factor range used for one-pion exchange comparison: Λ = 1.4 GeV/c
- Box and crossed diagram strengths are comparable for laboratory momenta below 2 GeV/c; the crossed diagram dominates for larger momenta

## Context

This work extends the long history of two-pion-exchange studies in nucleon-nucleon interactions, beginning with the work of Taketani, Nakamura, and Sasaki, and continuing through the Paris potential group (Cottingham 1973, Lacombe 1981, Richard 1975) and more recent chiral symmetry studies by Friar and Coon, Robilotta, and Rentmeester et al. (who found strong evidence for two-pion-exchange importance). Unlike previous approaches that used dispersion relations to derive non-relativistic potentials, this calculation uses full Feynman diagrams with form factors and finds that a very short-range part of the crossed diagram neglected in dispersion-relation calculations gives a sizable contribution. The results enable future work comparing two-pion exchange with quark-exchange models (Brodsky, Farrar) that also predict specific energy dependence for the cross section.
