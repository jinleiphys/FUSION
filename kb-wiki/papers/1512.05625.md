# Heavy quark diffusion from coherent color fields in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> The diffusion coefficients of heavy quarks from the coherent color electromagnetic fields which are generated in the early stage of relativistic heavy-ion collisions are calculated at midrapidity, and compared with those obtained from collisions within a thermalized quark-gluon plasma. The coherent color fields are modeled such that they are initially longitudinal and then become isotropic. We found that the diffusion coefficients from the coherent color fields are larger than those from collisions except for very fast heavy quarks, and the color fields are less effective for heavy-quark energy loss. The importance of coherent color fields for heavy-quark diffusion decreases as energy density decreases.

## Key claim

The diffusion coefficients of heavy quarks from coherent color fields are larger than those from collisions within a thermalized quark-gluon plasma except for very fast heavy quarks, and the coherent color fields are less effective for heavy-quark energy loss; the importance of these fields for heavy-quark diffusion decreases as energy density decreases.

## Method

The authors modeled the coherent color electromagnetic fields generated in the early stage of relativistic heavy-ion collisions using the color glass condensate (CGC) framework, with fields initially longitudinal and then evolving toward isotropy. They calculated the longitudinal and transverse momentum diffusion coefficients (κ_L and κ_T) for mid-rapidity heavy quarks by substituting the color Lorentz force into the Langevin diffusion tensor, using a Gaussian correlation function for the color fields with a memory time determined by the saturation momentum and heavy-quark velocity. The time evolution of the pressure anisotropy and energy density was parameterized from lattice calculations of classical Yang-Mills equations combined with Bjorken's law, and the results were compared with leading-order perturbative QCD calculations of diffusion from collisions in a thermalized quark-gluon plasma.

## Key numbers

- For central Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV with Q_s = 1.0 GeV and α_s = 0.3 at τ = 0.5 fm/c: κ_T from color fields is larger than from collisions up to v = 0.74 for bottom quarks; κ_L from color fields is larger up to v = 0.74.
- For central Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV at τ = 1.0 fm/c: κ_T from color fields is larger than from collisions up to v = 0.92; κ_L from color fields is larger up to v = 0.72.
- For central Pb+Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV with Q_s = 2.0 GeV and α_s = 0.3 at τ = 0.5 fm/c: κ_T from color fields is larger than from collisions up to v = 0.89; κ_L from color fields is larger up to v = 0.69.
- For central Pb+Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV at τ = 1.0 fm/c: κ_T from color fields is larger than from collisions up to v = 0.79; κ_L from color fields is larger up to v = 0.64.
- The average transverse velocities of mid-rapidity charm quarks in p+p collisions are v_T = 0.7 at √s_NN = 200 GeV and v_T = 0.85 at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV; for bottom quarks, v_T = 0.58 and 0.7 respectively.
- The production times of heavy quark pairs are less than 0.07 fm/c for charm (mass ~1.5 GeV) and less than 0.02 fm/c for bottom (mass ~5.0 GeV).
- The transverse correlation length of color fields is approximately the inverse saturation momentum, σ_T ≈ 1/Q_s, which is around 0.1–0.2 fm at RHIC and LHC energies.

## Context

This work extends the study of heavy-quark interactions in heavy-ion collisions beyond the thermalized quark-gluon plasma phase, which has been extensively modeled by authors such as Moore (2004) and others using collisional and radiative energy loss mechanisms. By calculating diffusion from the coherent color fields of the glasma phase, the authors provide a first quantitative comparison showing that these fields dominate heavy-quark diffusion at early times (τ ~ 0.5–1.0 fm/c) but become less important as the system expands and energy density decreases. This enables future studies to incorporate both coherent field and collisional contributions in a unified framework for heavy-quark transport, and suggests that extending the calculation to slow heavy quarks and forward/backward rapidities would require knowledge of the correlation lengths in the τ and η directions.
