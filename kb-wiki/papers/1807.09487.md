# Evolution of magnetic fields from the 3+1 dimensional self-similar and Gubser flows in ideal relativistic magnetohydrodynamics

> Motivated by the recently found realization of the $1+1$ dimensional Bjorken flow in ideal and nonideal relativistic magnetohydrodynamics (MHD), we use appropriate symmetry arguments, and determine the evolution of magnetic fields arising from the $3+1$ dimensional self-similar and Gubser flows in an infinitely conductive relativistic fluid (ideal MHD). In the case of the $3+1$ dimensional self-similar flow, we arrive at a family of solutions, that are related through a differential equation arising from the corresponding Euler equation. To find the magnetic field evolution from the Gubser flow, we solve the MHD equations of a stationary fluid in a conformally flat $dS^{3}\times E^{1}$ spacetime. The results are then Weyl transformed back into the Minkowski spacetime. In this case, the temporal evolution of the resulting magnetic field is shown to exhibit a transition between an early time $1/t$ decay to a $1/t^{3}$ decay at a late time. Here, $t$ is the time coordinate. Transverse and longitudinal components of the magnetic fields arising from these flows are also found. The latter turns out to be sensitive to the transverse size of the fluid. In contrast to the result arising from the Gubser flow, the radial domain of validity of the magnetic field arising from the self-similar flow is highly restricted. A comparison of the results suggests that the (conformal) Gubser MHD may give a more appropriate qualitative picture of the magnetic field decay in the plasma of quarks and gluons created in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the temporal evolution of magnetic fields in ideal relativistic magnetohydrodynamics (MHD) depends on the chosen flow profile: for the Gubser flow, the magnetic field exhibits a transition from an early-time \(1/t\) decay to a late-time \(1/t^3\) decay, while for the self-similar flow the radial domain of validity is highly restricted, suggesting that the conformal Gubser MHD provides a more appropriate qualitative picture of magnetic field decay in quark-gluon plasma created in heavy ion collisions.

## Method

The authors applied symmetry arguments to the ideal MHD equations, generalizing the Bekenstein and Oron treatment to two spatial symmetries, and solved for the magnetic field evolution from two distinct flow profiles: the \(3+1\) dimensional self-similar flow (using a cylindrical similarity variable) and the Gubser flow (using a conformally flat \(dS^{3}\times E^{1}\) spacetime with subsequent Weyl transformation back to Minkowski spacetime). For the self-similar flow, a family of solutions was obtained from the Euler equation; for the Gubser flow, the MHD equations were solved for a stationary fluid in the curved spacetime and then transformed.

## Key numbers

- For the Gubser flow, the magnetic field decays as \(1/t\) at early times and transitions to a \(1/t^{3}\) decay at late times, where \(t\) is the time coordinate.
- The magnetic field from the Bjorken flow (transverse MHD) decays as \(B = B_0 \frac{\tau_0}{\tau}\), where \(\tau\) is the proper time coordinate.
- In heavy ion collisions, electromagnetic fields are of order \(eB \sim 1\)-\(10 m_{\pi}^2\) in the early stage, with \(m_{\pi} \sim 140\) MeV.
- The timescale for spectators to leave the collision region is about \(0.065\) fm/c at RHIC and \(0.005\) fm/c at LHC.
- The medium reaches local thermal equilibrium in a timescale of roughly \(0.5\) fm/c.

## Context

This work extends the earlier analytical realization of magnetic fields in MHD with the \(1+1\) dimensional Bjorken flow by Rischke (2015) and the authors' own previous work (Shokri and Sadooghi, 2017) that relaxed the infinite conductivity assumption. It addresses the known limitation of the Bjorken flow, which lacks transverse expansion, by implementing the \(3+1\) dimensional self-similar flow from Csorgo (2002) and the Gubser flow from Gubser (2010). The results enable more realistic modeling of magnetic field evolution in the quark-gluon plasma created in heavy ion collisions, particularly by incorporating transverse expansion effects that are absent in the Bjorken flow.
