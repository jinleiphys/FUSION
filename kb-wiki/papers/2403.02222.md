# Neutron star cooling and mass distributions

> We study the cooling of isolated neutron stars, employing different nuclear equations of state with or without active direct Urca process, and investigate the interplay with the nuclear pairing gaps. We find that a consistent description of all current cooling data requires fast direct Urca cooling and reasonable proton 1S0 gaps, but no neutron 3P2 pairing. We then deduce the neutron star mass distributions compatible with the cooling analysis and compare with current theoretical models. Reduced 1S0 gaps and unimodal mass distributions are preferred by the analysis. The importance of statistical and systematic errors is also investigated.

## Key claim

A consistent description of all current neutron star cooling data requires an equation of state that allows fast direct Urca cooling for a wide enough mass range, combined with partial quenching by the proton 1S0 BCS pairing gap, but no neutron 3P2 pairing; the analysis prefers reduced 1S0 gaps and unimodal mass distributions, though the authors note that statistical and systematic errors are important.

## Method

The authors employed three nuclear equations of state (EOS): a microscopic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) EOS with the Argonne V18 potential (which allows direct Urca cooling) and two relativistic-mean-field (RMF) models, DD2 and TW99 (which do not allow direct Urca). They performed one-dimensional cooling simulations using the NSCool code, solving general-relativistic energy balance and transport equations, and varied the proton 1S0 pairing gap by applying global scaling factors \(s_x\) and \(s_y\) on both magnitude and density extension of the BCS gap. They then derived neutron star mass distributions by counting how many cooling data points fall between adjacent fixed-mass cooling curves, and compared these histograms with theoretical mass distributions from the literature.

## Key numbers

- The V18 EOS has a direct Urca threshold density \(\rho_{\mathrm{DU}} \approx 0.37\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\) and corresponding threshold mass \(M_{\mathrm{DU}} = 1.01\ M_\odot\).
- For the V18 EOS with unscaled BCS p1S0 gap (\(s_x = s_y = 1\)), the mass at which the p1S0 gap vanishes in the center is \(M_{1S0} = 1.92\ M_\odot\).
- The V18 EOS yields a maximum mass \(M_{\mathrm{max}} = 2.36\ M_\odot\), radius \(R_{1.4} = 12.3\ \mathrm{km}\), and tidal deformability \(\Lambda_{1.4} = 440\) for a \(1.4\ M_\odot\) star.
- The DD2 EOS gives \(M_{\mathrm{max}} = 2.42\ M_\odot\), \(R_{1.4} = 13.2\ \mathrm{km}\), \(\Lambda_{1.4} = 680\); the TW99 EOS gives \(M_{\mathrm{max}} = 2.08\ M_\odot\), \(R_{1.4} = 12.3\ \mathrm{km}\), \(\Lambda_{1.4} = 400\).
- There are 57 cooling data points in total, of which 51 are used (excluding 6 upper limits).

## Context

This work extends previous cooling studies by the same group (Taranto 2016, Fortin 2018, Wei 2019, Wei 2020) and others (Beznogov 2015, Potekhin 2020), updating the calculations to the latest observational data and more constrained equations of state. The analysis enables a combined inference of neutron star mass distributions and superfluid properties from cooling data, and the authors compare their derived mass histograms with theoretical mass distributions from Zhang (2011), Ozel (2012), Antoniadis (2016), Alsing (2018), and Rocha (2019). The finding that neutron 3P2 pairing is disfavored agrees with earlier work by Grigorian (2005) and Wei (2020).
