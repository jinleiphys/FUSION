# Symmetry verification for noisy quantum simulations of non-Abelian lattice gauge theories

> Non-Abelian gauge theories underlie our understanding of fundamental forces of modern physics. Simulating them on quantum hardware is an outstanding challenge in the rapidly evolving field of quantum simulation. A key prerequisite is the protection of local gauge symmetries against errors that, if unchecked, would lead to unphysical results. While an extensive toolkit devoted to identifying, mitigating, and ultimately correcting such errors has been developed for Abelian groups, non-commuting symmetry operators complicate the implementation of similar schemes in non-Abelian theories. Here, we discuss two techniques for error mitigation through symmetry verification, tailored for non-Abelian lattice gauge theories implemented in noisy qudit hardware: dynamical post-selection (DPS), based on mid-circuit measurements without active feedback, and post-processed symmetry verification (PSV), which combines measurements of correlations between target observables and gauge transformations. We illustrate both approaches for the discrete non-Abelian group $D_3$ in 2+1 dimensions, explaining their usefulness for current NISQ devices even in the presence of fast fluctuating noise. Our results open new avenues for robust quantum simulation of non-Abelian gauge theories, for further development of error-mitigation techniques, and for measurement-based control methods in qudit platforms.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that two error-mitigation techniques, dynamical post-selection (DPS) and post-processed symmetry verification (PSV), can protect gauge invariance in noisy quantum simulations of non-Abelian lattice gauge theories, even though the non-commuting nature of the gauge transformations prevents the simple direct post-selection methods used for Abelian groups. The methods are shown to work for the discrete non-Abelian group \(D_3\) in 2+1 dimensions, with the caveat that both require exponential overhead: DPS in the number of shots satisfying all Gauss' laws and PSV in the number of operators to be measured.

## Method

The authors simulate a digital (Trotterized) real-time evolution of a pure-gauge Kogut–Susskind Hamiltonian for the dihedral group \(D_3\) on a 2+1 dimensional square lattice with two plaquettes and periodic boundary conditions, using exact diagonalization in the full Hilbert space of dimension \(6^4\). They model noise by applying randomly drawn unitary operations at each Trotter step and compare two protection schemes: DPS, which uses mid-circuit measurements on auxiliary qudits to project onto gauge-invariant subspaces after each noisy step, and PSV, which reconstructs gauge-invariant expectation values from post-processed correlations between target observables and gauge transformation operators measured on the noisy state.

## Key numbers

- Trotter step \(\Delta t/a = 0.25\) and magnetic coupling \(1/g^2 = 0.5\) used for all numerical benchmarks.
- The dihedral group \(D_3\) has order \(|D_3| = 6\), with two one-dimensional irreducible representations (\(j=e,p\)) and one two-dimensional faithful irreducible representation (\(j=\tau\)).
- For the DPS protocol with the given system size and gauge group, every local symmetry is checked on average every \(t^{*} = 10\Delta t\).
- Ensemble averages over 5000 trajectories with noise strength \(\gamma = 0.2\) are shown in the summary figure.

## Context

This work extends prior error-mitigation techniques developed for Abelian lattice gauge theories, such as energy penalty terms (Zohar 2012, Banerjee 2012, Halimeh 2020), stochastic phase-destruction methods (Stannigel 2014, Kasper 2023), and direct post-selection (Mildenberger 2022, Nguyen 2022), to the more challenging non-Abelian case where gauge transformations do not commute. The DPS method builds on earlier work on symmetry protection through mid-circuit measurements (Wauters 2024, Schmale 2024), while PSV generalizes symmetry-verification approaches (Bonet-Monroig 2018, McClean 2020) to lattice gauge theories with discrete symmetry groups. The results enable robust quantum simulations of non-Abelian gauge theories on current NISQ qudit devices and open avenues for further development of error-mitigation techniques and measurement-based control methods.
