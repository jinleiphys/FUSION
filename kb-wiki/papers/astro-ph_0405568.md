# Stars as thermonuclear reactors: their fuels and ashes

> Atomic nuclei are transformed into each other in the cosmos by nuclear reactions inside stars: -- the process of nucleosynthesis. The basic concepts of determining nuclear reaction rates inside stars and how they manage to burn their fuel so slowly most of the time are discussed. Thermonuclear reactions involving protons in the hydrostatic burning of hydrogen in stars are discussed first. This is followed by triple alpha reactions in the helium burning stage and the issues of survival of carbon and oxygen in red giant stars connected with nuclear structure of oxygen and neon. Advanced stages of nuclear burning in quiescent reactions involving carbon, neon, oxygen and silicon are discussed. The role of neutron induced reactions in nucleosynthesis beyond iron is discussed briefly, as also the experimental detection of neutrinos from SN 1987A which confirmed broadly the ideas concerning gravitational collapse leading to a supernova.

## Key claim

Stars achieve extremely long lifetimes because their thermonuclear reactions occur at energies far below the Coulomb barrier, with the Gamow peak lying well below the barrier height; the slow burning is further enforced for the p+p reaction by the weakness of the weak interaction, which converts a proton into a neutron during the brief encounter of a scattering event.

## Method

The paper derives the thermally averaged reaction rate per particle pair by convolving the Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution with the energy-dependent cross-section, factoring out the rapidly varying Gamow transmission factor into the astrophysical S-factor. For the specific case of deuterium formation via p+p → d + e⁺ + νₑ, the cross-section is calculated using first-order perturbation theory with Fermi's Golden Rule, separating the nuclear and weak-interaction Hamiltonians and applying the super-allowed Gamow-Teller transition selection rules.

## Key numbers

- The p+p Coulomb barrier height is 0.55 MeV, while the Gamow peak E₀ at T₆ = 15 is only 5.9 keV, with I_max = 1.1 × 10⁻⁶
- For α + ¹²C at T₆ = 15, the Coulomb barrier is 3.43 MeV, Gamow peak E₀ = 56 keV, and I_max = 3 × 10⁻⁵⁷
- For ¹⁶O + ¹⁶O at T₆ = 15, the Coulomb barrier is 14.07 MeV, Gamow peak E₀ = 237 keV, and I_max = 6.2 × 10⁻²³⁹
- The p+p reaction releases 0.42 MeV of kinetic energy, with the total energy including positron annihilation being 1.442 MeV; the average neutrino energy from this reaction is 0.26 MeV
- The sun's central temperature is 15.6 × 10⁶ K (T₆ = 15.6); the CNO cycle contributes approximately 1.5% of solar luminosity in the standard solar model, with an upper limit of 7.3% from neutrino experiments

## Context

This lecture-note paper reviews the foundational framework of stellar nucleosynthesis established by Bethe (1937), Bethe and Critchfield (1938), and von Weizsäcker (1937-1938), extending the treatment of Gamow-peak reaction rates and the astrophysical S-factor formalism from Rolfs and Rodney (1988) and Bahcall (1989). It enables students and researchers to connect the nuclear-physics inputs (cross-sections, S-factors, screening corrections) to stellar evolution models and to understand the mapping between the Hertzsprung-Russel diagram and the chart of nuclides, as described by Reeves (1968).
