# Phase Transition in Pulsar Timing

> A phase transition in the nature of matter in the core of a neutron star, such as quark deconfinement or Bose condensation, can cause the spontaneous spin-up of a solitary millisecond pulsar. The spin-up epoch for our model lasts for $2\times 10^7$ years or 1/50 of the spin-down time (Glendenning, Pei and Weber in Ref. \cite{glen97:a}). The possibility exists also for future measurements on X-ray neutron stars with low-mass companions for mapping out the tell-tale ``backbending'' behavior of the moment of inertia. Properties of phase transitions in substances such as neutron star matter, which have more than one conserved charge, are reviewed.

## Key claim

A first-order phase transition in the core of a solitary millisecond pulsar, such as quark deconfinement, can cause a spontaneous spin-up epoch lasting for 2×10^7 years or 1/50 of the spin-down time, but this signal requires that the transition occurs at or near the limiting mass star and is accompanied by a sufficient softening of the equation of state.

## Method

The author solves the General Relativistic equations for a rotating neutron star with fixed baryon number across a sequence of angular velocities, using a relativistic covariant theory for nuclear matter and the MIT bag model for quark matter. The moment of inertia is computed using an expression that incorporates changes in composition, centrifugal stretching, and frame dragging, going beyond the Hartle approximation. The spin-up era duration is estimated by solving the magnetic dipole radiation deceleration equation with a frequency-dependent moment of inertia.

## Key numbers

- Spin-up epoch lasts for 2×10^7 years, which is 1/50 of the spin-down time for the model pulsar
- The anomalous braking index endures for 10^8 years, corresponding to the slow spin-down and gradual envelopment of the core by the new phase
- The quark matter core radius is 5 km when the star is not rotating, with a mixed phase extending to 8 km
- Global charge neutrality requires Z_net/A ≤ (m/e)^2 ≈ 10^-36

## Context

This work extends the concept of "backbending" in moment of inertia, originally predicted for rotating nuclei by Mottelson and Valatin (1960) and discovered in the 1970s by Johnson and Stephens, to neutron star astrophysics. The calculation improves upon the Hartle (1967) expression for moment of inertia by using the author's own later derivation (Glendenning 1992, 1993) that accounts for composition changes and frame dragging. The paper also reviews the Gibbs equilibrium treatment for phase transitions in substances with multiple conserved charges, which was developed by Glendenning (1991) and contrasts with the earlier Maxwell construction approach used in pre-1990 studies of deconfinement in neutron stars. Future measurements of X-ray neutron stars in low-mass binaries could map the "backbending" behavior of the moment of inertia and potentially identify distinct branches corresponding to different phases.
