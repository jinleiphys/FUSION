# Neutron Star Structure and the Equation of State

> The structure of neutron stars is considered from theoretical and observational perspectives. We demonstrate an important aspect of neutron star structure: the neutron star radius is primarily determined by the behavior of the pressure of matter in the vicinity of nuclear matter equilibrium density. In the event that extreme softening does not occur at these densities, the radius is virtually independent of the mass and is determined by the magnitude of the pressure. For equations of state with extreme softening, or those that are self-bound, the radius is more sensitive to the mass. Our results show that in the absence of extreme softening, a measurement of the radius of a neutron star more accurate than about 1 km will usefully constrain the equation of state. We also show that the pressure near nuclear matter density is primarily a function of the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy, while the nuclear incompressibility and skewness parameters play secondary roles. In addition, we show that the moment of inertia and the binding energy of neutron stars, for a large class of equations of state, are nearly universal functions of the star's compactness. These features can be understood by considering two analytic, yet realistic, solutions of Einstein's equations, due, respectively, to Buchdahl and Tolman. We deduce useful approximations for the fraction of the moment of inertia residing in the crust, which is a function of the stellar compactness and, in addition, the presssure at the core-crust interface.

# Neutron Star Structure and the Equation of State

## Key claim

The neutron star radius is primarily determined by the pressure of matter near nuclear matter equilibrium density, and in the absence of extreme softening, a radius measurement more accurate than about 1 km will usefully constrain the equation of state, with the radius being virtually independent of the stellar mass. This correlation holds for cold, catalyzed neutron stars but not for protoneutron stars with finite entropies or trapped neutrinos.

## Method

The authors examined a wide class of equations of state (EOS) for dense matter, including nonrelativistic potential models, relativistic field theoretical models, and self-bound strange quark matter models, solving Einstein's equations for non-rotating, non-magnetized neutron stars at zero temperature. They established empirical power-law correlations between neutron star radii and the pressure evaluated at fiducial densities of 1, 1.5, and 2 times nuclear saturation density, and explained these correlations using analytic solutions due to Buchdahl (1967) and Tolman. The pressure near nuclear saturation density was related to the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy, with nuclear incompressibility and skewness parameters playing secondary roles.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear equilibrium density: $\rho_s \cong 2.7 \times 10^{14}$ g cm$^{-3}$
- For a 1.4 M$_\odot$ star, the causality constraint gives a lower radius limit of about 4.5 km
- The maximum QPO frequency measured is $\nu_{max} = 1230$ Hz, leading to $M \simle 1.78$ M$_\odot$ and $R \simle 8.86 (M/{\rm M}_\odot)$ km for a non-rotating star
- With rotation corrections, the mass limit increases to about 2.1 M$_\odot$
- The Stella and Vietri (1999) model fits QPO observations only if $1.9 \simle M/{\rm M}_\odot \simle 2.1$
- For RX J185635-3754, the X-ray blackbody fit yields $R_\infty \approx 7.3 (D/120 {\rm pc})$ km
- An et al. (2000) determined for non-magnetized heavy element atmospheres that $R_\infty/D \cong 0.18 \pm 0.05$ km pc$^{-1}$
- The radius-pressure correlation has the form $R_M \simeq C(n,M) [P(n)]^{0.23-0.26}$
- For MIT-model strange quark stars, the maximum mass relation is $R \cong 1.85 R_s$, and with $M > 1$ M$_\odot$ constraint, $R < 8.5$ km or $R_\infty < 10.5$ km
- The symmetry energy at saturation density is estimated as $S_v \approx 27-36$ MeV
- The equilibrium proton fraction at saturation density is $x_s \simeq 0.04$

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Rhoades and Ruffini (1974) on causality constraints for maximum mass, and by Lattimer et al. (1990) and Glendenning (1992) on radius lower limits. It contrasts with Lindblom (1992), who suggested that multiple mass and radius measurements would be necessary to constrain the EOS; the authors instead show that a single accurate radius measurement can provide useful constraints. The results enable future observational programs to constrain the dense matter EOS through radius measurements from thermal emission sources like RX J185635-3754, and through interpretation of pulsar glitches via the moment of inertia distribution in the crust, as discussed by Link, Epstein and Lattimer (1999).
