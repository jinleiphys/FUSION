# The SuSAv2 model for inelastic neutrino-nucleus scattering

> The susperscaling model SuSAv2, already available for charged-current neutrino-nucleus cross sections in the quasielastic region, is extended to the full inelastic regime. In the model the resonance production and deep inelastic reactions are described through the extension to the neutrino sector of the SuSAv2 inelastic model developed for ($e,e'$) reactions, which combines phenomenological structure functions with a nuclear scaling function. This work also compares two different descriptions of the $Δ$ resonance region, one based on a global scaling function for the full inelastic spectrum and the other on a semi-phenomenological $Δ$ scaling function extracted from ($e,e'$) data for this specific region and updated with respect to previous work. The results of the model are tested against ($e,e'$) data on $^{12}$C, $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca and $^{40}$Ar and applied to the study of the charged current inclusive neutrino cross-section on $^{12}$C and $^{40}$Ar measured by the T2K, MicroBooNE, ArgoNEUT and MINERvA experiments, thus covering several kinematical regions.

## Key claim

The SuSAv2 model, previously limited to the quasielastic region for charged-current neutrino-nucleus scattering, is extended to the full inelastic regime by combining phenomenological structure functions with a nuclear scaling function; the paper compares two descriptions of the Delta resonance region, one using a global scaling function for the full inelastic spectrum and the other using a semi-phenomenological Delta scaling function extracted from electron scattering data, with the latter updated relative to prior work.

## Method

The authors extended the SuSAv2 superscaling model, originally developed for quasielastic electron and neutrino scattering, to the inelastic regime by folding a nuclear scaling function with phenomenological inelastic structure functions for the resonance and deep inelastic scattering regions. For the Delta resonance region specifically, they extracted a semi-phenomenological scaling function from inclusive electron scattering data on carbon-12 by subtracting the quasielastic, two-particle-two-hole, higher resonance, and deep inelastic scattering contributions computed within the SuSAv2 framework. The model was then tested against electron scattering data on carbon-12, oxygen-16, calcium-40, and argon-40, and applied to charged-current inclusive neutrino cross-section measurements from T2K, MicroBooNE, ArgoNEUT, and MINERvA.

## Key numbers

* The Delta scaling function was extracted from electron scattering data on carbon-12 using the scaling variable psi_Delta, which vanishes when the energy transfer corresponds to excitation of the Delta(1232) resonance on a free nucleon at rest (omega = sqrt(q^2 + m_Delta^2) - m_N).
* The model was tested against electron scattering data on four nuclei: carbon-12, oxygen-16, calcium-40, and argon-40.
* Neutrino cross-section comparisons covered experiments with neutrino energies peaked around 0.6 GeV (T2K, MicroBooNE) and at energies higher than 3 GeV (MINERvA, ArgoNEUT).
* The Delta scaling function was shown to be approximately valid only for psi_Delta less than or equal to 0, while scaling is broken for positive psi_Delta values due to opening of higher inelastic channels.

## Context

This work extends the SuSAv2 model, previously applied to quasielastic neutrino scattering (Megias 2016, Gonzalez-Jimenez 2014), to the full inelastic regime, building on prior inelastic electron-scattering models (Barbaro 2004, Maieron 2009) and a semi-phenomenological Delta model (Ivanov 2016). The updated Delta scaling function improves upon earlier work by using the latest SuSAv2 quasielastic and two-particle-two-hole models and by subtracting higher resonance and deep inelastic scattering contributions to better isolate the Delta resonance peak. This enables more accurate predictions for neutrino oscillation experiments operating in the 0.5-10 GeV region, particularly for charged-current inclusive measurements where inelastic channels are significant.
