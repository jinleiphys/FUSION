# Azimuthally sensitive correlations in nucleus-nucleus collisions

> We define a set of model-independent observables which generally characterize the azimuthal dependence of two-particle correlations in nucleus-nucleus collisions. We explain how they can be analyzed, and show to what extent such analyses are model dependent. We discuss specific applications to the anisotropic flow of decaying particles, azimuthally sensitive HBT, and correlations between particles at large transverse momentum. A quantitative prediction is made for jet quenching with respect to the reaction plane.

# Azimuthally sensitive correlations in nucleus-nucleus collisions

## Key claim
The paper introduces a set of model-independent observables, the pair-flow Fourier coefficients \(v_{c,n}^{\rm pair}\) and \(v_{s,n}^{\rm pair}\), to characterize the azimuthal dependence of two-particle correlations in nucleus-nucleus collisions, but the authors explicitly note that isolating the "true" correlated part from the uncorrelated background is always model-dependent and involves some degree of arbitrariness, typically in the form of a free normalization constant.

## Method
The authors define the pair-flow coefficients via a Fourier expansion of the azimuthal distribution of the pair angle \(\phi_{\rm pair}\) (a weighted combination of the two particle azimuthal angles) with respect to the reaction plane, generalizing the single-particle flow coefficients \(v_n\). They show that any method used to measure single-particle anisotropic flow (event-plane, two-particle correlations, cumulants, Lee-Yang zeroes) can be adapted to extract these pair-flow coefficients, with minor modifications needed for the sine terms \(v_{s,n}^{\rm pair}\). The paper then demonstrates how to apply this formalism to three specific cases: anisotropic flow of decaying particles (e.g., \(\Lambda\) baryons), azimuthally sensitive Hanbury-Brown Twiss (HBT) interferometry, and two-particle correlations at large transverse momentum (jet quenching).

## Key numbers
- For the mixed-event method in azimuthally sensitive HBT at RHIC (STAR experiment), typical correction factors are \(\langle \cos 2\Delta\Psi_R \rangle^2 \simeq 0.6\) and \(\langle \cos 2\Delta\Psi_R \rangle^2 / \langle \cos 4\Delta\Psi_R \rangle \simeq 1.3\), where \(\Delta\Psi_R\) is the difference between the estimated event plane and the true reaction plane.
- The error on the reconstructed azimuthal distribution at midrapidity at RHIC is likely dominated by the error on the fourth harmonic \(v_4\), with an estimated uncertainty of \(\frac{1}{\pi}\delta v_4\) on the probability distribution \(p(\phi-\Phi_R)\).
- The paper derives a quantitative prediction for jet quenching: if only the second harmonic \(v_2\) is nonvanishing, the sine term must satisfy \(v_{s,2}^{\rm pair}(\Delta\phi) = (v_{c,2}^{\rm pair}(\Delta\phi) - v_2^{(1)}) \tan 2\Delta\phi\), where \(v_2^{(1)}\) is the elliptic flow of the trigger particle.

## Context
This work provides a unified, model-independent framework for azimuthally sensitive two-particle correlations, extending the single-particle flow formalism of Voloshin (1994) and the event-plane method of Danielewicz (1985) and Poskanzer (1998). It addresses inconsistencies in prior analyses that used the event-plane method (which assumes all correlations are due to flow) to study nonflow correlations such as quantum statistics or jet fragmentation. The paper specifically builds on earlier work on azimuthally sensitive HBT by Heinz (2002) and on jet quenching with respect to the reaction plane by Wang (2000) and Lokhtin, and it enables quantitative comparisons between different experiments by replacing event-plane-based observables with detector-independent Fourier coefficients.
