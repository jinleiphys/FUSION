# Non-perturbative thermal QCD at all temperatures: the case of mesonic screening masses

> We present a strategy based on the step-scaling technique to study non-perturbatively thermal QCD up to very high temperatures. As a first concrete application, we compute the flavour non-singlet meson screening masses at 12 temperatures covering the range from $T \sim 1$ GeV up to $\sim 160$ GeV in the theory with three massless quarks. The calculation is carried out by Monte Carlo simulations on the lattice by considering large spatial extensions in order to have negligible finite volume effects. For each temperature we have simulated 3 or 4 values of the lattice spacing, so as to perform the continuum limit extrapolation with confidence at a few permille accuracy. Chiral symmetry restoration manifests itself in our results through the degeneracy of the vector and the axial vector channels and of the scalar and the pseudoscalar ones. In the entire range of temperatures explored, the meson screening masses deviate from the free theory result, $2 πT$, by at most a few percent. These deviations, however, cannot be explained by the known leading term in the QCD coupling constant $g$ up to the highest temperature, where other contributions are still very relevant. In particular the vector-pseudoscalar mass splitting turns out to be of $O(g^4)$ in the entire range explored, and it remains clearly visible up to the highest temperature, where the two screening masses are still significantly different within our numerical precision. The pattern of different contributions that we have found explains why it has been difficult in the past to match non-perturbative lattice results at $T \sim 1$ GeV with the analytic behaviour at asymptotically high temperatures.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that mesonic screening masses in thermal QCD with three massless quarks deviate from the free theory prediction of \(2\pi T\) by at most a few percent across a temperature range from about 1 GeV to 160 GeV, but these small deviations cannot be explained by the known leading-order term in the QCD coupling constant \(g\) even at the highest temperatures, where other contributions remain significant.

## Method

The authors performed lattice Monte Carlo simulations of three-flavour QCD using both the Wilson plaquette gauge action (for the nine highest temperatures) and the tree-level Symanzik improved gauge action (for the three lowest temperatures), with \(O(a)\)-improved Wilson fermions and shifted boundary conditions. They employed a step-scaling technique to define lines of constant physics at 12 temperatures, simulating 3 or 4 lattice spacings per temperature to enable continuum limit extrapolations at few-permille precision. Meson screening masses were extracted from two-point correlation functions computed with a preconditioned Dirac solver.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range covered: from \(T_{11} = 1.167(23)\) GeV up to \(T_0 = 164.6(5.6)\) GeV, with 12 temperatures total
- Pseudoscalar screening mass at \(T_0\) (164.6 GeV): \(m_P/(2\pi T) = 0.9934(14)\) for \(L_0/a = 6\) (finest lattice spacing at that temperature)
- Vector screening mass at \(T_0\) (164.6 GeV): \(m_V/(2\pi T) = 0.9996(12)\) for \(L_0/a = 6\)
- Vector-pseudoscalar mass splitting at \(T_0\): \((m_V - m_P)/(2\pi T) = 0.0065(4)\)
- Vector-pseudoscalar mass splitting at \(T_8\) (3.040 GeV): \((m_V - m_P)/(2\pi T) = 0.0252(14)\)
- Vector-pseudoscalar mass splitting at \(T_{11}\) (1.167 GeV): \((m_V - m_P)/(2\pi T) = 0.0171(19)\) (from \(L_0/a = 10\) data)
- Pseudoscalar screening mass at \(T_1\) (82.3 GeV): \(m_P/(2\pi T) = 1.0090(25)\) for \(L_0/a = 10\)
- Vector screening mass at \(T_1\) (82.3 GeV): \(m_V/(2\pi T) = 1.0160(27)\) for \(L_0/a = 10\)

## Context

This work extends non-perturbative lattice QCD calculations of mesonic screening masses to much higher temperatures than previously achieved, covering the range from about 1 GeV to 160 GeV. Prior lattice studies had been limited to temperatures around 1 GeV, where matching to asymptotic high-temperature analytic predictions proved difficult. The results explain this difficulty by showing that the vector-pseudoscalar mass splitting remains of \(O(g^4)\) across the entire range and that deviations from free theory persist up to the highest temperatures. This enables future studies of thermal QCD properties at arbitrarily high temperatures using the step-scaling methodology, and provides benchmark non-perturbative results for comparison with analytic approaches such as those based on dimensional reduction.
