# Collision dynamics of two $^{238}$U atomic nuclei

> Collisions of actinide nuclei form, during very short times of few $10^{-21}$ s, the heaviest ensembles of interacting nucleons available on Earth. Such very heavy ions collisions have been proposed as an alternative way to produce heavy and superheavy elements. These collisions are also used to produce super-strong electric fields by the huge number of interacting protons to test spontaneous positron-electron ($e^+e^-$) pair emission predicted by the quantum electrodynamics theory. The time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory which is a fully microscopic quantum approach is used to study collision dynamics of two $^{238}$U atomic nuclei. In particular, the role of nuclear deformation on collision time and on reaction mechanisms such as nucleon transfer is emphasized. These calculations are pessimistic in terms of transfermium elements ($Z>100$) production. However, the highest collision times ($\sim4\times10^{-21}$ s at 1200 MeV) should allow experimental signature of spontaneous $e^+e^-$ emission. Surprisingly, we also observe ternary fission due to purely dynamical effects.

## Key claim
The time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations predict that the collision time for bare $^{238}$U+$^{238}$U central collisions can reach up to $\sim4\times10^{-21}$ s at 1200 MeV, which should allow experimental observation of spontaneous positron-electron pair emission, but only for certain initial orientations of the deformed nuclei, and only at center-of-mass energies above 1000 MeV.

## Method
The authors performed fully microscopic TDHF calculations using the SLy4$d$ Skyrme energy density functional on a three-dimensional spatial grid with 0.8 fm lattice spacing and $1.5\times10^{-24}$ s time steps. They simulated central collisions of two $^{238}$U nuclei in four different initial orientations of their prolate deformation axes ($xx$, $yx$, $yy$, and $yz$ configurations) at center-of-mass energies ranging from 700 to 1600 MeV, analyzing nucleon transfer, collision times, and fragment formation.

## Key numbers
- Maximum collision time of $\sim4\times10^{-21}$ s at $E_{CM}=1200$ MeV for the $yx$, $yy$, and $yz$ configurations
- Threshold for experimental observation of spontaneous positron emission: sticking time of at least $2\times10^{-21}$ s (from prior work cited as Reinhardt et al. (1981) and Ackermann et al. (2008))
- In the $yx$ configuration at $E_{CM}=900$ MeV, average transfer of $\sim6$ protons and $\sim11$ neutrons from the right to the left nucleus
- At $E_{CM}\leq1000$ MeV, standard transfer produces nuclei up to $^{253}$Cf from average nucleon flux
- At $E_{CM}\sim1500$ MeV, superheavy fragments up to $Z\sim130$ and $N\sim205$ could be produced, but are expected to fission due to high temperature
- Saturation density $\rho_0=0.16$ fm$^{-3}$ is reached in the neck only at $E_{CM}\sim1000$ MeV
- In the $xx$ configuration at $E_{CM}=900$ MeV, density in the neck reaches a maximum of $0.166$ fm$^{-3}$, exceeding saturation density and leading to ternary fission with a $^{16}$C-like fragment
- Ternary fission in the $yy$ configuration occurs at $E_{CM}=1500$ and 1600 MeV, producing a neutron-rich boron isotope

## Context
This work extends earlier Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) calculations by Tian et al. (2008) by including the strong ground-state deformation of $^{238}$U and using fully antisymmetrized independent-particle wave functions with exact Pauli principle treatment. The results are pessimistic for producing transfermium elements ($Z>100$) via this reaction, consistent with experimental findings by Schädel et al. (1978) that no such nuclei have been observed. The study enables future systematic investigations of other actinide collisions to optimize channels for superheavy element production, and predicts ternary fission as a new probe of nuclear dynamics that should be tested experimentally.
