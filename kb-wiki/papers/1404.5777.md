# The suggested presence of the tetrahedral-symmetry in the ground-state configuration of the $^{96}$Zr nucleus

> We discuss the predictions of the large scale calculations using the realistic realisation of the phenomenological nuclear mean-field theory. Calculations indicate that certain Zirconium nuclei are tetrahedral-symmetric in their ground-states. After a short overview of the research of the nuclear tetrahedral symmetry in the past we analyse the predictive capacities of the method and focus on the $^{96}$Zr nucleus expected to be tetrahedral in its ground-state.

## Key claim

Large-scale calculations using a realistic phenomenological nuclear mean-field theory (Woods-Saxon with universal parameterization) indicate that the ground-state of \(^{96}\)Zr has tetrahedral (\(T_d\)) symmetry, but the authors hedge that this is a "suggested presence" and that the static symmetry described by the mean-field Hamiltonian represents only the leading-order symmetry due to zero-point motion.

## Method

The authors employed the macroscopic-microscopic method with a deformed Woods-Saxon Hamiltonian using the "universal parameterization" (fixed depth, radius, and diffusivity for all nuclei). They calculated total energy surfaces as functions of the tetrahedral deformation parameter \(\alpha_{32}\) and the axial octupole deformation \(\alpha_{30}\), minimizing over the octahedral deformation, and compared single-particle level diagrams for protons in \(^{90}\)Zr to identify shell gaps and degeneracies characteristic of the tetrahedral point group.

## Key numbers

- The ground-state minimum of \(^{96}\)Zr corresponds to tetrahedral deformation \(\alpha_{32} \approx 0.15\) with a small octahedral deformation, lying about 1300 keV below the spherical minimum.
- The measured \(B(\text{E3})\) reduced transition probability for \(^{96}\)Zr is \(57 \pm 4\) Weisskopf units (W.u.), the largest such value ever measured according to the authors.
- For neighboring nuclei: \(^{98}\)Mo has \(B(\text{E3}) = 35 \pm 3\) W.u., \(^{96}\)Mo has \(31 \pm 4\) W.u., \(^{100}\)Ru has \(14 \pm 3\) W.u., \(^{102}\)Pd has \(29 \pm 10\) W.u., and \(^{88}\)Sr has \(18.3 \pm 11\) W.u.
- The proton shell gap at \(Z=40\) persists up to tetrahedral deformations of about \(\alpha_{32} \approx 0.3\), while the same gap decreases continuously as a function of axial octupole deformation \(\alpha_{30}\).

## Context

This work extends earlier mean-field predictions of tetrahedral symmetry in Zr isotopes by Li and Dudek (1994), who first showed that tetrahedral magic numbers (56, 64, 70, 90, 112, 136) could be generated, and by Dudek et al. (2002), who extended the magic gaps to lighter nuclei. The calculations contradict purely axial octupole interpretations of the large \(B(\text{E3})\) values in the Zr region, suggesting instead that tetrahedral (\(T_d\)) symmetry dominates over axial (\(C_\infty\)) octupole symmetry. The results enable experimental searches for tetrahedral rotational bands with characteristic E3 transitions and new selection rules, as well as potential implications for nucleosynthesis waiting-point nuclei.
