# The Shape of the Correlation Function

> The two-particle correlation function employed in Hanbury-Brown Twiss interferometry and femtoscopy is traditionally parameterized by a Gaussian form. Other forms, however, have also been used, including the somewhat more general Lévy form. Here we consider a variety of effects present in realistic femtoscopic studies which may modify the shape of the correlation function and thereby influence the physical interpretation of a given parameterization.

## Key claim

The paper argues that much of the non-Gaussian deviation observed in experimental Lévy parametrizations of the two-pion correlation function can be explained by relatively mundane, non-critical sources such as one-dimensional projection and resonance decays, rather than by exotic phenomena like critical fluctuations near a QCD critical point.

## Method

The authors used two complementary models to generate pion-emitting sources: a blast-wave model and a (2+1)-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic model (iEBE-VISHNU) with boost-invariant symmetry, specific shear viscosity η/s = 0.08, and no hadronic afterburner. They computed two-pion correlation functions from these sources, then fitted the resulting correlation functions with a Lévy-stable parametrization to extract the Lévy exponent α, systematically varying whether the correlation function was treated in full three-dimensional momentum space or projected onto one-dimensional scalar relative momentum variables (Q_inv or Q_LCMS), and whether resonance decays were included or only direct pions were considered.

## Key numbers

* The Lévy exponent α extracted from one-dimensional projections of the correlation function generically falls in the range 1.3–1.6 for a wide range of transverse pair momentum K_T values, even when the underlying three-dimensional correlation function is well described by α = 2.
* Including resonance decays reduces the extracted value of α by an additional 0.1–0.2 compared to models with only direct pion production.
* At low K_T, the three-dimensional fit to the correlation function from thermal pions alone gives α near 2, while the one-dimensional projection of the same correlation function suppresses α considerably.

## Context

This work extends the study of Lévy-stable parametrizations of HBT correlation functions, which were previously applied to experimental data by the PHENIX Collaboration (Adare et al., 2018) and proposed as a probe of critical fluctuations by Csörgő (2009). The paper systematically quantifies how non-critical effects, specifically one-dimensional projection, resonance decays, ensemble averaging, and bin-averaging in pair momentum, can produce sub-Gaussian Lévy exponents, building on earlier studies of resonance contributions to HBT radii by Wiedemann and Heinz (1997) and of event-by-event HBT by Plumberg and Heinz (2018). The results establish a baseline for interpreting future measurements of the Lévy index, suggesting that quantitative control over these mundane sources of non-Gaussianity is essential before any definitive claims about critical phenomena can be drawn from such measurements.
