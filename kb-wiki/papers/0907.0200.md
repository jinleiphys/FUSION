# Quark matter in neutron stars

> According to quantum chromodynamics, matter at ultra-high density and low temperature is a quark liquid, with a condensate of Cooper pairs of quarks near the Fermi surface ("color superconductivity"). This paper reviews the physics of color superconductivity, and discusses some of the proposed signatures by which we might detect quark matter in neutron stars.

# Quark matter in neutron stars

## Key claim
Color-superconducting quark matter may exist in the cold dense cores of neutron stars, but it remains an open question whether such phases persist down to the densities actually achieved in neutron star cores, because the densities at which the strong interaction becomes perturbatively weak are extraordinarily high.

## Method
The paper reviews the physics of color superconductivity in dense QCD matter, describing the BCS-type Cooper pairing of quarks near the Fermi surface. It surveys the expected phase diagram including the color-flavor locked (CFL) phase, two-flavor color superconductivity (2SC), and crystalline (LOFF) pairing, then evaluates proposed astrophysical signatures for detecting quark matter in neutron stars: mass-radius relations, r-mode spindown, gravitational wave emission, pulsar glitches, and neutrino cooling.

## Key numbers
- The crossover temperature from hadronic gas to quark-gluon plasma is about 170 MeV.
- The separation between Fermi momenta of different quark species due to strange quark mass is δp_F = M_s^2/(4μ), to lowest order in the strange quark mass.
- The CFL phase remains favored over the unpaired phase as long as Δ_CFL > M_s^2/(4μ), and becomes unstable against unpairing when Δ_CFL ≳ M_s^2/(2μ).
- The surface tension threshold for a sharp quark-nuclear interface versus a mixed phase is about 40 MeV/fm^2 = 0.2 × (200 MeV)^3.
- The shear modulus of the crystalline LOFF phase is ν ∼ 0.5–20 MeV/fm^3, compared to nuclear matter at ν ∼ 10^−4–10^−2 MeV/fm^3.
- Hybrid stars with quark matter cores can have masses up to 2 M_⊙ when strong interaction corrections are included.

## Context
This review extends the line of work on color superconductivity initiated by Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1998) and summarized in the detailed review by Alford (2007). It discusses how the CFL phase may connect smoothly to hypernuclear matter as suggested by Schafer (1999) and Alford (1999). The paper enables future observational tests: comparing predicted r-mode spindown curves from Jaikumar (2008) against LMXB observations, searching for gravitational wave signatures from crystalline quark cores using LIGO, and interpreting the rapid cooling of some neutron stars reported by Page (2009) as possible evidence for non-CFL quark matter undergoing direct Urca neutrino emission.
