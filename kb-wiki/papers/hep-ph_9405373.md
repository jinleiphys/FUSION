# Source Dimensions in Ultrarelativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> Recent experiments on pion correlations, interpreted as interferometric measurements of the collision zone, are compared with models that distinguish a prehadronic phase and a hadronic phase. The models include prehadronic longitudinal expansion, conversion to hadrons in local kinetic equilibrium, and rescattering of the produced hadrons. We find that the longitudinal and outward radii are surprisingly sensitive to the algorithm used for two-body collisions. The longitudinal radius measured in collisions of 200 GeV/u sulfur nuclei on a heavy target requires the existence of a prehadronic phase which converts to the hadronic phase at densities around 0.8-1.0 GeV/fm$^3$. The transverse radii cannot be reproduced without introducing more complex dynamics into the transverse expansion.

## Key claim

The longitudinal and outward source radii extracted from pion interferometry are surprisingly sensitive to the algorithm used for two-body collisions in the hadronic phase, and the longitudinal radius measured in 200 GeV/u sulfur-nucleus collisions requires a prehadronic phase converting to hadrons at densities around 0.8-1.0 GeV/fm³, while the transverse radii cannot be reproduced without introducing more complex dynamics into the transverse expansion.

## Method

The authors modeled heavy ion collisions in two stages: a prehadronic phase with only longitudinal expansion, followed by conversion to a hadronic gas phase (pions, rho mesons, and omega mesons) in local kinetic equilibrium with subsequent rescattering. They used a Monte Carlo cascade program to simulate the space-time evolution, sampling initial hadron distributions from a parameterized density function and tracking particle propagation, pairwise collisions, and resonance decays to produce final-state pions with their last interaction coordinates. The simulated correlation functions were then compared to experimental data from the NA35 and NA44 collaborations at CERN SPS.

## Key numbers

- Hadronization density required to fit the longitudinal radius: 0.8-1.0 GeV/fm³ (corresponding to approximately 1.0 pions/fm³)
- Average hadronization proper time for the baseline model: τ_h = 2.3 fm with standard deviation σ = 1.5 fm
- Temperature parameter used to fit single-particle spectra: T = 150 MeV
- Spatial rapidity dispersion parameter: Δη = 1.2
- Total number of pions in final state: 700, reproducing measured dN/dy ≈ 65 for S+Au at mid-rapidity
- Ratio of rho mesons to pions at hadronization: w_ρ/π = 0.12-0.21 (from isospin degeneracy arguments)
- Longitudinal radius increased by approximately 50% when switching from repulsive to attractive collision algorithm
- Longitudinal radius increased by approximately 15% when using isotropic (uniform in solid angle) scattering instead of repulsive uniform-in-θ scattering

## Context

This work extends a study by Welke et al. (1994) which considered earlier data with more detailed model assumptions, and contrasts with a study by Sullivan et al. (1993, 1994) that found good agreement with available data using conventional physics. The paper enables future work by identifying that the longitudinal radius constrains the hadronization density, while the failure to reproduce transverse radii points to the need for explicit transverse expansion dynamics in the prehadronic phase, and it highlights the inconsistency between NA35 and NA44 experimental results that must be resolved before general conclusions can be drawn.
