# Semiclassical origin of asymmetric nuclear fission: Nascent-fragment shell effect in periodic-orbit theory

> The origin of the asymmetry in the fragment mass distribution of low-energy nuclear fission is considered from the semiclassical point of view. Using the semiclassical periodic-orbit theory, one can define and quantify the shell effect associated with spatially localized nascent-fragment (prefragment) part of the potential. We investigate the roles of prefragments in the deformed shell effect using a simple cavity potential model but with realistic shape degrees of freedom for describing the fission processes. The results suggest that the prefragment magic numbers play essential roles in determining the shapes at the fission saddles, which should have a close relation to the fragment mass distribution.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the shell effect associated with spatially localized nascent fragments ("prefragments") embedded in the fissioning nucleus, as quantified by semiclassical periodic-orbit theory (POT), plays an essential role in determining the shapes at fission saddles and thus the fragment mass distribution; this claim is conditional on the use of a simplified cavity potential model with fixed prefragment deformations and neck parameter, and the authors note that the agreement between quantum and semiclassical results is not as good for asymmetric shapes as for symmetric ones.

## Method

The authors applied semiclassical periodic-orbit theory (POT) to a three-quadratic-surfaces (3QS) cavity potential model, which describes the nuclear surface as three smoothly connected quadratic surfaces with parameters for elongation, fragment mass asymmetry, neck curvature, and prefragment deformations. They fixed the prefragment deformations to spherical (epsilon_j=0) and the neck parameter to sigma_2=-0.6, then computed shell energies as functions of particle number for varying elongation and asymmetry parameters. The shell energy was decomposed into contributions from classical periodic orbits confined in each prefragment, and the results were compared with exact quantum mechanical calculations and with the sum of shell energies of independent spherical cavities.

## Key numbers

* The neck parameter was fixed at sigma_2 = -0.6, which is close to its values for some actinide nuclei along fission paths from realistic macroscopic-microscopic calculations.
* For the symmetric 3QS cavity with elongation q_2=4.0, the triangle orbit family (3,1) has length L_31 = 3*sqrt(3) ≈ 5.20 in units of R_0, and the square orbit family (4,1) has length L_41 = 4*sqrt(2) ≈ 5.66 in units of R_0.
* The diameter orbit (2,1) has length L_21 = 4 in units of R_0.
* For the symmetric 3QS cavity with q_2=4.0, deep shell energy minima occur around particle number N=200, caused by constructive contributions of diameter and triangle orbits.
* For asymmetric shapes with q_2=4.0 and alpha_g=0.25, constructive contributions from prefragment magic numbers give deep shell energy minima around N=100 and N=160.
* The spherical cavity model has magic numbers at N = 34, 58, 92, 138, etc.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical approach to fission proposed by Strutinsky and Magner (1976) by applying periodic-orbit theory to a deformed cavity model with realistic fission shape degrees of freedom, specifically the three-quadratic-surfaces parametrization of Nix (1969). It builds on earlier studies using the two-center shell model (Mosel, 1971) and the (c-h-alpha) parametrization (Brack, 1997), and it addresses the need to extract prefragment shell effects that cannot be isolated in standard quantum mean-field calculations. The results enable future studies that include prefragment deformations and more realistic potentials, and they provide a semiclassical framework for understanding why asymmetric fission occurs even in systems like Hg-180 (Andreyev, 2010) where symmetric products are very stable.
