# On the extension of the Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula to light nuclei

> Some general features of the Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula recently extended to light nuclei have been explored. Though this formula improves fits to the properties of light nuclei and it does seem to work well in delineating the positions of all old and new magic numbers found in that region, yet it is not well tuned for predicting finer details. The mass predictions have also been found to be less accurate compared to those by the macroscopic-microscopic calculations. It is concluded that such semi-empirical mass formulae can not be a substitute for more fundamental mass formulae having its origin based upon the basic nucleon-nucleon effective interaction.

# On the extension of the Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula to light nuclei

## Key claim
The modified Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula improves fits to binding energy curves for light nuclei and identifies new magic numbers, but it is not well tuned for predicting finer details and its mass predictions are less accurate than those from macroscopic-microscopic calculations. The author concludes that such semi-empirical mass formulae cannot substitute for more fundamental mass formulae based on the basic nucleon-nucleon effective interaction.

## Method
The author applied a previously published modified Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula (Samanta and Adhikari, 2002) that includes an altered asymmetry term with a fitted constant k=17 and a modified pairing term with constant c=30, while keeping the volume, surface, Coulomb, symmetry, and pairing coefficients unchanged from the original formula. One and two proton separation energies, one and two neutron separation energies, and atomic mass excesses were calculated using this formula. The chi-square per degree of freedom was computed for 1742 experimental atomic mass excesses using the modified BW formula and compared to the macroscopic-microscopic model using 1727 experimental atomic mass excesses.

## Key numbers
- The modified BW formula uses asymmetry term modifying constant k=17 and pairing term modifying constant c=30.
- The volume coefficient a_v=15.79 MeV, surface coefficient a_s=18.34 MeV, Coulomb coefficient a_c=0.71 MeV, symmetry coefficient a_sym=23.21 MeV, and pairing coefficient a_p=12 MeV remain unchanged from the old BW formula.
- The chi-square per degree of freedom for the modified BW formula using 1742 experimental atomic mass excesses is 12.936.
- The chi-square per degree of freedom for the macroscopic-microscopic model using 1727 experimental atomic mass excesses is 1.821.
- The formula predicts S_{1p}=3.37 MeV for ^5Li, S_{1p}=1.12 MeV for ^6Be, S_{1p}=0.11 MeV for ^15F, S_{1p}=0.65 MeV for ^16Ne, S_{1n}=1.00 MeV for ^17B, S_{1n}=1.03 MeV for ^20C, S_{1n}=0.95 MeV for ^26O, and S_{1n}=-0.38 MeV for ^31F.
- The electronic binding energy constant a_el = 1.433 x 10^{-5} MeV, with Delta m_H = 7.289034 MeV and Delta m_n = 8.071431 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the analysis of a modified Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula originally proposed by Samanta and Adhikari (2002) for light nuclei, which identified new shell closures at neutron numbers N=16, N=14, and N=6. The author compares the modified formula unfavorably to the macroscopic-microscopic model of Myers and Swiatecki (1994, 1996), which uses a Seyler-Blanchard type effective interaction and provides better mass estimates. The paper suggests that future improvements should focus on density dependent effective interactions that can provide unified descriptions of nuclear scattering, cluster radioactivity, and nuclear matter, rather than further modifying semi-empirical mass formulae.
