# Impact of Pycnonuclear Fusion Uncertainties on the Cooling of Accreting Neutron Star Crusts

> The observation of X-rays during quiescence from transiently accreting neutron stars provides unique clues about the nature of dense matter. This, however, requires extensive modeling of the crusts and matching the results to observations. The pycnonuclear fusion reaction rates implemented in these models are theoretically calculated by extending phenomenological expressions and have large uncertainties spanning many orders of magnitude. We present the first sensitivity studies of these pycnonuclear fusion reactions in realistic network calculations. We also couple the reaction network with the thermal evolution code dStar to further study their impact on the neutron star cooling curves in quiescence. Varying the pycnonuclear fusion reaction rates alters the depth at which nuclear heat is deposited although the total heating remains constant. The enhancement of the pycnonuclear fusion reaction rates leads to an overall shallower deposition of nuclear heat. The impurity factors are also altered depending on the type of ashes deposited on the crust. These total changes correspond to a variation of up to 9 eV in the modeled cooling curves. While this is not sufficient to explain the shallow heat source, it is comparable to the observational uncertainties and can still be important for modeling the neutron star crust.

## Key claim

Varying pycnonuclear fusion reaction rates by up to a factor of 10^6 alters the depth at which nuclear heat is deposited in accreting neutron star crusts, but the total heating remains constant; the resulting changes in modeled cooling curves are at most 9 eV, which is insufficient to explain the shallow heat source required by observations but is comparable to observational uncertainties.

## Method

The authors performed the first sensitivity studies of pycnonuclear fusion reactions in realistic nuclear reaction network calculations, using the same network as Lau (2018) with 4844 reactions from Be to Si. They coupled the reaction network with the thermal evolution code dStar to model the thermal structure of the crust during accretion and the cooling curves during quiescence, varying all pycnonuclear fusion rates simultaneously by factors of 10^2 and 10^6 in both directions. Calculations were performed for three initial compositions: pure 56Fe, superburst ashes from Keek (2012), and rp-process X-ray burst ashes from Woosley (2004) and Cyburt (2016), with a fixed crust temperature of 0.5 GK and an accretion rate of 2.64 x 10^4 g cm^-2 s^-1.

## Key numbers

- Pycnonuclear fusion reaction rates have total uncertainties spanning 8 to 10 orders of magnitude, as shown by Yakovlev (2006); the authors adopt an uncertainty factor of 10^6 in either direction
- The variation in modeled cooling curves corresponds to up to 9 eV in photon energy
- For 56Fe initial composition, enhanced rates shift 40Mg + 40Mg fusion onset to a depth of P = 2 x 10^30 dyn cm^-2 (density 8.8 x 10^11 g cm^-3)
- For rp-process ashes with rates enhanced by 10^6, heating already occurs at P = 4 x 10^29 dyn cm^-2 (density 2.3 x 10^11 g cm^-3)
- The largest temperature difference in the crust at 4,383 days of accretion is 4.4 MK (2%) at P = 5 x 10^29 dyn cm^-2
- The most important reaction for 56Fe composition is 40Mg + 40Mg with integrated flow 9.142 x 10^-3 mole/g
- For rp-process ashes, the most important reaction is 32Ne + 32Ne with integrated flow 1.691 x 10^-3 mole/g

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Sato (1979), Haensel (1990), and Haensel (2008) that identified pycnonuclear fusion as the main heat source in accreting neutron star crusts, and directly addresses the large theoretical uncertainties of 8-10 orders of magnitude noted by Yakovlev (2006). The results show that pycnonuclear fusion uncertainties cannot explain the shallow heat source required by observations (typically 0.5-2 MeV/u as found by Brown (2009), Degenaar (2015), and others), but the 9 eV variation is comparable to observational uncertainties and will be important for precision modeling with upcoming surveys.
