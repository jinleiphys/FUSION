# Exploring high-density baryonic matter: Maximum freeze-out density

> The hadronic freeze-out line is calculated in terms of the net baryon density and the energy density instead of the usual T and mu_B. This analysis makes it apparent that the freeze-out density exhibits a maximum as the collision energy is decreased. This maximum freeze-out density is well within the parameters of the proposed NICA accelerator.

## Key claim

The hadronic freeze-out line, when expressed in terms of net baryon density and energy density rather than temperature and chemical potential, reveals a maximum in freeze-out density that occurs at a baryon chemical potential of 400–500 MeV, which is above the critical value for a first-order phase transition.

## Method

The authors used the THERMUS statistical model code with an updated hadronic resonance list from the Particle Data Group to calculate freeze-out conditions along the experimentally determined freeze-out curve. They represented the freeze-out line in the plane of net baryon density ρ_B and excitation energy density ε* (total energy density minus the rest-mass contribution m_N ρ_B), and also included calculations with an excluded-volume correction using hard-core radii of c = 0.3 fm and c = 0.6 fm to approximate hadronic interactions.

## Key numbers

- The maximum net baryon density at freeze-out amounts to about three quarters of the nuclear saturation density ρ_0 ≈ 0.16 fm^{-3}
- The maximum freeze-out density occurs at μ_B = 400–500 MeV and T = 140–130 MeV
- The optimal collider energy for exploring compressed baryonic matter is √s_{NN} = 5.6–7.8 GeV
- The corresponding optimal fixed-target beam kinetic energy is 15–30 GeV per nucleon
- At the highest collision energies, freeze-out occurs at an energy density of nearly one half GeV/fm^3 with negligible net baryon density

## Context

This work extends the earlier analysis by Randrup (2006) that first proposed representing the freeze-out line in terms of mechanical densities rather than chemical potential and temperature, using updated THERMUS code and hadronic resonance data. The results provide guidance for the planned NICA accelerator facility at JINR, suggesting that freeze-out densities up to the maximum value could be explored at a collider delivering beam kinetic energies up to approximately 2.4 GeV per nucleon. The inclusion of excluded-volume effects, following the approach of Gorenstein (2008), shows that while interactions quantitatively affect the results, the qualitative feature of a maximum freeze-out density remains robust.
