# The mass of odd-odd nuclei in microscopic mass models

> Accurate estimates of the binding energy of nuclei far from stability that cannot be produced in the laboratory are crucial to our understanding of nuclear processes in astrophysical scenarios. Models based on energy density functionals have shown that they are capable of reproducing all known masses with root-mean-square error better than 800 keV, while retaining a firm microscopic foundation. However, it was recently pointed out in [M. Hukkanen et al., arXiv:2210.10674] that the recent BSkG1 model fails to account for a contribution to the binding energy that is specific to odd-odd nuclei, and which can be studied by using appropriate mass difference formulas. We analyse here the (lacking) performance of three recent microscopic mass models with respect to such formulas and examine possibilities to remedy this deficiency in the future.

## Key claim

Microscopic mass models BSkG1, BSkG2, and BSk31 fail to reproduce the systematically enhanced binding energy of odd-odd nuclei, as quantified by the \(\Delta^{(3)}_{np}\) mass difference; the abstract's claim of sub-800 keV rms accuracy glosses over this specific systematic deficiency that is not visible in the global rms error.

## Method

The authors computed the three-point mass difference \(\Delta^{(3)}_{np}\) from experimental masses in the AME20 compilation and from the calculated masses of three Skyrme-EDF models (BSkG1, BSkG2, BSk31) and the macroscopic-microscopic FRDM(2012) model. They compared the average values and scatter of \(\Delta^{(3)}_{np}\) across all nuclei with \(Z \geq 8\) to assess whether each model reproduces the empirical trend of additional binding in odd-odd nuclei. They also examined the rms deviation for odd-odd nuclei separately and discussed possible mechanisms (time-odd terms, alternating pairing strengths, spin-spin interactions) that could generate the missing binding energy.

## Key numbers

- Experimental \(\Delta^{(3)}_{np}\) values are systematically positive: of 1993 \((N,Z)\) pairs, only 28 yield negative values; the average experimental value is 305 keV.
- BSkG1 and BSkG2 produce average \(\Delta^{(3)}_{np}\) values of almost precisely 0 keV; BSk31 gives about 100 keV.
- FRDM(2012) reproduces the experimental trend with an average \(\Delta^{(3)}_{np}\) of about 306 keV.
- Global rms deviations on all known masses: BSkG1 741 keV, BSkG2 678 keV, BSk31 588 keV, FRDM(2012) 606 keV.
- For odd-odd nuclei specifically, BSkG2 rms is 704 keV and BSk31 rms is 616 keV, each about 40 keV above their respective global rms.

## Context

This work extends the critique by Hukkanen et al. (2022) that the BSkG1 model fails to account for the odd-odd binding energy contribution, and confirms the same deficiency in BSkG2 and BSk31. It contrasts with the macroscopic-microscopic FRDM(2012) model (Moller et al., 2016), which includes an explicit phenomenological \(A^{-2/3}\) term for odd-odd nuclei and reproduces the effect. The paper identifies that none of the microscopic models included observables sensitive to this small effect in their fitting protocols, and suggests that tuning time-odd spin-spin terms in the Skyrme EDF or adopting phenomenological corrections could remedy the deficiency in future models.
