# Improving PWR core simulations by Monte Carlo uncertainty analysis and Bayesian inference

> A Monte Carlo-based Bayesian inference model is applied to the prediction of reactor operation parameters of a PWR nuclear power plant. In this non-perturbative framework, high-dimensional covariance information describing the uncertainty of microscopic nuclear data is combined with measured reactor operation data in order to provide statistically sound, well founded uncertainty estimates of integral parameters, such as the boron letdown curve and the burnup-dependent reactor power distribution. The performance of this methodology is assessed in a blind test approach, where we use measurements of a given reactor cycle to improve the prediction of the subsequent cycle. As it turns out, the resulting improvement of the prediction quality is impressive. In particular, the prediction uncertainty of the boron letdown curve, which is of utmost importance for the planning of the reactor cycle length, can be reduced by one order of magnitude by including the boron concentration measurement information of the previous cycle in the analysis. Additionally, we present first results of non-perturbative nuclear-data updating and show that predictions obtained with the updated libraries are consistent with those induced by Bayesian inference applied directly to the integral observables.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a Monte Carlo-based Bayesian inference framework (MOCABA) can reduce the prediction uncertainty of the boron letdown curve for a PWR reactor cycle by one order of magnitude when measurements from the previous cycle are included, but this impressive improvement depends on the high correlation (0.92-0.99) between cycles and is less pronounced for power distribution predictions due to lower measurement precision.

## Method

The authors generated 200 random nuclear data libraries using the NUDUNA Monte Carlo code with ENDF/B-VII.1 covariance data for six key isotopes (²³⁵U, ²³⁸U, ²³⁹Pu, ¹H, ¹⁶O, ¹⁰B), then performed SEANAP core simulations for two consecutive PWR cycles (Cycle A and Cycle B) with each random library. The MOCABA Bayesian updating procedure was applied using measurements from Cycle A to improve predictions for Cycle B, with the predictive power assessed by comparing posterior predictions to actual Cycle B measurements.

## Key numbers

- Prior boron concentration uncertainty for Cycle B: 46 ppm at beginning of cycle (BOC), decreasing to 40 ppm at end of cycle (EOC)
- Posterior boron concentration uncertainty after Bayesian updating: approximately 3 ppm, representing a reduction factor of 11-19 across burnup points
- ²³⁸U contributed 36 ppm uncertainty at BOC, decreasing to 25 ppm at EOC; ²³⁵U contributed 22 ppm at BOC, decreasing to 14 ppm at EOC; ²³⁹Pu contributed 19 ppm at BOC, increasing to 29 ppm at EOC
- Correlation between Cycle A and Cycle B boron values ranged from 0.92 to 0.99
- Maximum uncertainty reduction for power per fuel assembly: approximately 50%; average reduction: 47% at 2842 MWd/t and 29% at 10284 MWd/t
- Boron concentration measurement uncertainty: 6 ppm (0.6% at BOC); power measurement uncertainty: 5%
- Reactor rated thermal power: 2775 MWt, with 157 fuel assemblies and fresh fuel enrichment of 3.6 w/o

## Context

This work extends the MOCABA methodology (developed by AREVA GmbH) from a theoretical framework to practical reactor cycle prediction, comparing favorably to the Generalized Linear Least Squares (GLLS) method which relies on first-order perturbation theory. The authors compare their ENDF/B-VII.1/NUDUNA uncertainty results to prior work by SeanapUncert using TENDL-2012/TMC, finding that different nuclear data evaluations produce significantly different uncertainty magnitudes and isotope importance rankings. The methodology enables improved reactor operation planning through reduced prediction uncertainties, and the authors note that future work should verify whether updated nuclear data libraries derived from one reactor also improve predictions for other reactors.
