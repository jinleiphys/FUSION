# Hot neutron stars and their equation of state

> A set of microscopic, covariant density-functional, and non-relativistic Skyrme-type equations of state is employed to study the structure of purely nucleonic neutron stars at finite temperature. After examining the agreement with presently available astrophysical observational constraints, we find that the magnitude of thermal effects depends on the nucleon effective mass as well as on the stiffness of the cold equation of state. We evidence a fairly small but model-dependent effect of finite temperature on stellar stability that is correlated with the relative thermal pressure inside the star.

## Key claim

The magnitude of thermal effects on neutron star structure depends on the nucleon effective mass and the stiffness of the cold equation of state, but the effect of finite temperature on stellar stability is fairly small and model-dependent, correlated with the relative thermal pressure inside the star.

## Method

The authors employed a set of microscopic (Brueckner-Hartree-Fock), covariant density-functional (relativistic mean field), and non-relativistic Skyrme-type equations of state extended to finite temperature, studying purely nucleonic neutron stars under the assumption of beta-equilibrium and isentropic matter with entropy per baryon S/A=2. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for both cold and hot configurations to obtain mass-radius relations and maximum gravitational masses, then correlated the relative change in maximum mass with the ratio of thermal pressure to cold pressure at the stellar center.

## Key numbers

- Maximum gravitational masses for cold neutron stars range from 1.84 solar masses (LS180) to 2.72 solar masses (LS375), with realistic EOSs satisfying 2.1 < M_max/M_sun < 2.4
- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star (R_1.4) ranges from 11.3 km (SRO(APR)) to 14.5 km (Shen11); observational constraints give 12.45 ± 0.65 km (Miller21), 11.94^{+0.76}_{-0.87} km (Pang21), and 12.33^{+0.76}_{-0.81} km (Raaijmakers21)
- Radius of a 2.08 solar mass neutron star (R_2.08) ranges from 10.7 km (SRO(APR)) to 13.6 km (Shen11); observational constraint gives 12.35 ± 0.75 km (Miller21)
- Tidal deformability Lambda_1.4 ranges from 250 (SRO(APR)) to 1170 (Shen11); observational constraint is 70–580 (Abbott18)
- Thermal pressure ratio at maximum-mass central density is below 3 percent for V18, N93, and LS375, and up to 10 percent for SRO(APR) and TNTYST
- Relative change of maximum gravitational mass due to finite temperature is limited to less than 4 percent for the subset of realistic EOSs
- Temperatures at high density reach up to 100 MeV for SRO(APR) and TNTYST, with lowest temperatures for BHF and LS EOSs

## Context

This work extends previous studies of cold neutron star equations of state constrained by the GW170817 merger event (Abbott 2018) and NICER observations (Riley 2019, Miller 2019, Riley 2021, Miller 2021) to the finite-temperature regime, which remains practically unconstrained by current astrophysical data. The authors compare microscopic BHF EOSs (V18, N93), variational EOSs (TNTYST, SRO(APR)), relativistic mean-field models (Shen11, Shen20, HS(DD2), SFH(SFHx), FSU2H), and Skyrme-type LS EOSs (LS180, LS220, LS375), finding that BHF models with large symmetry energies predict no increase of stellar stability with temperature, contrary to most other models. The results enable future comparisons with next-generation gravitational-wave detectors (Maggiore 2020) that will observe hot neutron star merger remnants.
