# Propagator poles and an emergent stable state below threshold: general discussion and the E(38) state

> In the framework of a simple quantum field theory describing the decay of a scalar state into two (pseudo)scalar ones we study the pole(s) motion(s) of its propagator: besides the expected pole on the second Riemann sheet, we find -- for a large enough coupling constant -- a second, additional pole on the first Riemann sheet below threshold, which corresponds to a stable state. We then perform a numerical study for a hadronic system in which a scalar particle couples to pions. We investigate under which conditions a stable state below the two-pion threshold can emerge. In particular, we study the case in which this stable state has a mass of 38 MeV, which corresponds to the recently claimed novel scalar state E(38). Moreover, we also show that the resonance $f_{0}(500)$ and the stable state E(38) could be two different manifestation of the same `object'. Finally, we also estimate the order of magnitude of its coupling to photons.

## Key claim

For a sufficiently large coupling constant, the propagator of a scalar state decaying into two pseudoscalars develops an additional pole on the first Riemann sheet below threshold, corresponding to a stable state; this stable state at 38 MeV and the broad resonance f0(500) could be two different manifestations of the same underlying object, though the existence of the E(38) state remains unconfirmed.

## Context

This work extends the study of additional propagator poles in scalar field theories, previously discussed by Dullemond, Tornqvist, Boglione, and Rupp/Bugg, but finds a qualitatively different pole trajectory: the pole moves on the real axis of the second sheet before slipping through the branch cut onto the first sheet. The numerical application connects to the controversial E(38) state claimed by van Beveren and Rupp (2012) from reanalysis of BABAR data, and to the well-established f0(500) resonance. The results suggest that a unified description of both states is possible within a simple quantum field theory, and provide a new theoretical perspective distinct from the original E(38) interpretation.
