# Introduction to the transverse-momentum-weighted technique in the twist-3 collinear factorization approach

> The twist-3 collinear factorization framework has drawn much attention in recent decades as a successful approach in describing the data for single spin asymmetries (SSAs). Many SSAs data have been experimentally accumulated in a variety of energies since the first measurement was done in late 70s and it is expected that the future experiments like Electron-Ion collider will provide us with more data. In order to perform a consistent and precise description of the data taken in different kinematic regimes, the scale evolution of the collinear twist-3 functions and the perturbative higher order hard part coefficients are mandatory. In this paper, we introduce the techniques for next-to-leading order (NLO) calculation of transverse-momentum-weighted SSAs, which can be served as a useful tool to derive the QCD evolution equation for twist-3 functions, and to verify the QCD collinear factorization for twist-3 observables at NLO, as well as to obtain the finite NLO hard part coefficients.

## Key claim

The transverse-momentum-weighted technique provides a systematic method for next-to-leading order (NLO) calculations of single spin asymmetries (SSAs) within the twist-3 collinear factorization framework, enabling derivation of QCD evolution equations for twist-3 functions and verification of collinear factorization at NLO, but the consistency check between the Ward-Takahashi identity approach and direct diagrammatic calculation remains a future task for general scattering processes.

## Method

The authors take semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering (SIDIS) as an example process, performing a transverse-momentum-weighted integration of the differential cross section for a transversely polarized proton target. They compute the leading-order (LO) contribution by collinear expansion of the hard part around the parton momentum, identifying the result as proportional to the first moment of the TMD Sivers function (the Qiu-Sterman function). For the NLO calculation, they separate virtual corrections (one-loop diagrams using dimensional regularization) and real corrections (2→2 partonic scattering), treating the latter by decomposing propagators into principal value and imaginary parts to isolate the pole contributions required for the single spin asymmetry.

## Key numbers

- LO cross section: \( \frac{d\langle P_{h\perp}\Delta\sigma\rangle^{\rm LO}}{dx_B dy dz_h} = \tilde\sigma_0 \sum_q e_q^2 G_{q,F}(x_B,x_B) D_{q\to h}(z_h) \), where \( \tilde\sigma_0 = -\pi M_N \sigma_0 \) with \( \sigma_0 = \frac{2\pi\alpha_{em}^2}{Q^2}\frac{1+(1-y)^2}{y} \)
- NLO virtual correction: \( \frac{d\langle P_{h\perp}\Delta\sigma\rangle^{\rm virtual}}{dx_B dy dz_h} = \tilde\sigma_0 \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \sum_q e_q^2 G_{q,F}(x_B,x_B) D_{q\to h}(z_h) C_F \left(\frac{4\pi\mu^2}{Q^2}\right)^\epsilon \frac{1}{\Gamma(1-\epsilon)} \left(-\frac{2}{\epsilon^2} - \frac{3}{\epsilon} - 8\right) \)
- Mandelstam variables for real corrections: \( \hat{s} = \frac{1-\hat{x}}{\hat{x}}Q^2 \), \( \hat{t} = -\frac{1-\hat{z}}{\hat{x}}Q^2 \), \( \hat{u} = -\frac{\hat{z}}{\hat{x}}Q^2 \), with \( \hat{x}=x_B/x \), \( \hat{z}=z_h/z \)
- Phase space constraint from real emission: \( \delta\left(p^2_{c\perp} - \frac{(1-\hat{x})(1-\hat{z})\hat{z}}{\hat{x}}Q^2\right) \)

## Context

This work extends the twist-3 collinear factorization framework developed by Efremov, Qiu, and Koike (1990s-2000s) for describing single spin asymmetries, which were first observed experimentally by Klem (1976) and Bunce (1976). The transverse-momentum-weighted technique was previously applied to Drell-Yan production by Vogelsang (2009) and Chen (2016), and the NLO SIDIS calculation builds on the leading-order results of Koike (2006-2011) and the NLO work of Kang (2012). The technique enables derivation of evolution equations for twist-3 functions (such as the Qiu-Sterman function \( G_{q,F}(x,x) \)) and provides NLO hard coefficients needed for precision extraction of these functions from data at COMPASS and the future Electron-Ion Collider.
