# Are small radii of compact stars ruled out by GW170817/AT2017gfo?

> The detection of GW170817 and its electromagnetic counterparts allows to constrain the equation of state of dense matter in new and complementary ways. Very stiff equations of state are ruled out by the upper limit on the average tidal deformability, $\tildeΛ\lesssim 800$, imposed by the detected gravitational wave signal. A lower limit, $\tildeΛ\gtrsim 400$, can also be extracted by considering the large amount of ejected matter which powers the kilonova AT2017gfo. By using several microscopic nucleonic equations of state, we first confirm the existence of a monotonic relation between $R_{1.5}$ (the radius of the $1.5M_{\odot}$ configuration) and $\tildeΛ$. This translates the limits on $\tildeΛ$ into limits on the radius: $11.8\,\text{km} \lesssim R_{1.5} \lesssim 13.1\,$km. We then show that the monotonic relation is violated, if a second branch of compact stars composed of quark matter exists, as in the two-families or the twin-stars scenarios. In particular, it is possible to fulfill the limits on $\tildeΛ$ while having $R_{1.5}$ significantly smaller than $12\,$km. In both those scenarios the event GW170817/AT2017gfo originates from the merger of a hadronic star and a star containing quark matter.

# Are small radii of compact stars ruled out by GW170817/AT2017gfo?

## Key claim

The lower limit on the average tidal deformability (Λ̃ ≳ 400) from the kilonova AT2017gfo does **not** rule out compact star radii significantly smaller than 12 km, provided that a second branch of compact stars composed of quark matter exists, either in the two-families scenario or the twin-stars scenario. The monotonic relation between R₁.₅ and Λ̃ that holds for purely nucleonic equations of state is violated when quark matter appears.

## Method

The authors computed mass-radius relations and tidal deformabilities for several microscopic nucleonic equations of state (BHF, APR, DBHF, and relativistic mean-field models LS220 and SFHo), including versions with hyperons and delta resonances. They then constructed two classes of quark-matter scenarios: (i) a two-families scenario combining hadronic stars (SFHo+HD, SFHo+HD2) with strange quark matter stars (QS, QS2); and (ii) a twin-stars scenario using a constant-speed-of-sound hybrid EOS (DBHF+CS) with a first-order phase transition to quark matter. For each EOS they solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations together with the tidal Love number equation, fixing the chirp mass to M_c = 1.188 M⊙ and varying the mass asymmetry q = M₂/M₁ between 0.7 and 1.

## Key numbers

- The upper limit on the average tidal deformability from GW170817 is Λ̃ ≲ 800; the lower limit from the kilonova AT2017gfo is Λ̃ ≳ 400.
- For purely nucleonic equations of state, the limits on Λ̃ translate to 11.8 km ≲ R₁.₅ ≲ 13.1 km.
- In the two-families scenario, the hadronic EOS SFHo+HD gives R₁.₅ = 10.6 km while still satisfying Λ̃ > 400; SFHo+HD2 gives R₁.₅ = 11.2 km.
- In the twin-stars scenario with causal speed of sound (c²_q = 1), R₁.₅ = 10.7 km is possible; with c²_q = 2/3, R₁.₅ = 11.6 km.
- The twin-stars configuration at maximum asymmetry q = 0.7 has component radii (R₁, R₂) = (10.7, 13.0) km and Λ̃ ≈ 600.
- The lower limit from prompt-collapse arguments gives R₁.₆ > 10.7 km (Bauswein 2017).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Annala (2017), who used a polytropic parametrization to derive R₁.₄ < 13.4 km from Λ₁.₄ < 800, and of Radice (2017), who inferred Λ̃ ≳ 400 from the kilonova ejecta. It directly addresses the tension between small radii (9.9–11.2 km) suggested by X-ray spectral modeling of low-mass X-ray binaries (Ozel 2016) and the tidal deformability constraints. The two-families scenario builds on prior work by Drago (2013, 2015, 2017) and Wiktorowicz (2017); the twin-stars scenario follows Alford (2015) and Paschalidis (2017). The results enable future tests: mergers of two light compact stars (e.g., 1.2 M⊙ + 1.2 M⊙) would produce very different Λ̃ values in the two scenarios, and upcoming NICER radius measurements and SKA moment-of-inertia measurements can distinguish between the one-family and two-family interpretations.
