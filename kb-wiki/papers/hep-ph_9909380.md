# Oscillations of Cumulant Moments - Universality of Amplitudes

> We demonstrate on simple examples that oscillatory behaviour of moments of multiplicity distributions P(n) observed in e^+e^- annihilations, in hadronic pp collisions and in collisions on nuclei, p+A, is to a large extend caused by the experimental artifact of measuring only limited range of P(n). In particular we show that by applying a suitable universal cutt-off procedure to the measured P(n) one gets for reactions mentioned before oscillations of similar magnitude. The location of zeros of oscillations as a function of the rank of moments and their shapes remain, however, distinctively different for different types of reactions considered. This applies to some extend also to collisions of nuclei, which otherwise follow their own pattern of behaviour.

# Oscillations of Cumulant Moments - Universality of Amplitudes

## Key claim
The oscillatory behavior of cumulant moments of multiplicity distributions observed across different collision systems (e+e-, pp, pA) is largely an experimental artifact caused by the limited range over which the multiplicity distribution P(n) is measured, rather than a purely dynamical QCD effect. When a universal cut-off parameter u = (n_max - ⟨n⟩)/σ_n is applied to compare different reactions at the same effective truncation level, the amplitudes of oscillations become comparable in magnitude, though the shape and zero locations remain distinct for different reaction types.

## Method
The authors calculated normalized cumulant moments H_q = K_q/F_q from multiplicity distributions P(n) for e+e- annihilations, pp collisions, and pA reactions, using the same distributions and parameters as in prior works (Suzuki 1996, Nakagawa 1996, Suzuki 1998). They applied a universal truncation procedure defined by the variable u = (n_max - ⟨n⟩)/σ_n, which measures the cut-off point in terms of standard deviations from the mean, and compared the resulting oscillation patterns across reactions at identical u values. For heavy ion collisions (AB), they used a Monte Carlo model with Saxon-Woods nuclear distributions and MNBD elementary distributions to generate P(n) and compute H_q moments.

## Key numbers
- Amplitudes of oscillations increase from 10^{-3} for e+e- annihilations, to 10^{-2} for pp collisions, up to 10^{-1} for pA and heavy ion (AB) reactions, before applying the universal cut-off.
- The universal cut-off variable u is defined as u = (n_max - ⟨n⟩)/σ_n, where n_max is the maximum observed multiplicity, ⟨n⟩ is the mean multiplicity, and σ_n is the standard deviation of P(n).
- For the Monte Carlo simulation of nuclear collisions, the nucleon diffusiveness parameter was 0.49 fm for S nuclei and 0.545 fm for Pb nuclei, with nuclear radii given by r[fm] = 1.12 A^{1/3} - 0.86/A^{1/3}, and the total inelastic cross section was σ = 32 mb.
- Central collisions in heavy ion systems were defined as the 1% of collisions with smallest impact parameter.

## Context
This work extends the debate on whether oscillations of cumulant moments originate from QCD dynamics (Dremin 1993, 1994) or from truncation effects in measured multiplicity distributions (Ugoc 1995). It builds on prior phenomenological studies using negative binomial distributions (NBD) and modified negative binomial distributions (MNBD) by Suzuki (1991, 1996), Nakagawa (1996), and Biyajima (1984), which showed that truncation of NBD induces oscillations. The paper demonstrates that comparing reactions at the same universal cut-off parameter u reveals approximate universality of oscillation amplitudes, but the shape and frequency of oscillations in rank q remain sensitive to dynamical details and can discriminate between models. For heavy ion collisions, the geometry of the collision (via the distribution of emitting sources) dominates the oscillation pattern, as shown by the difference between central collisions (gaussian-like P(n), weak oscillations) and minimum bias collisions (Saxon-Woods-like P(n), strong oscillations), which is a separate mechanism from simple truncation.
