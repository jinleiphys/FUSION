# Benchmark on neutron capture extracted from $(d,p)$ reactions

> Direct neutron capture reactions play an important role in nuclear astrophysics and applied physics. Since for most unstable short-lived nuclei it is not possible to measure the $(n, γ)$ cross sections, $(d,p)$ reactions have been used as an alternative indirect tool. We analyze simultaneously $^{48}{\rm Ca}(d,p)^{49}{\rm Ca}$ at deuteron energies $2, 13, 19$ and 56 MeV and the thermal $(n,γ)$ reaction at 25 meV. We include results for the ground state and the first excited state of $^{49}$Ca. From the low-energy $(d,p)$ reaction, the neutron asymptotic normalization coefficient (ANC) is determined. Using this ANC, we extract the spectroscopic factor (SF) from the higher energy $(d,p)$ data and the $(n, γ)$ data. The SF obtained through the 56 MeV $(d,p)$ data are less accurate but consistent with those from the thermal capture. We show that to have a similar dependence on the single particle parameters as in the $(n, γ)$, the (d,p) reaction should be measured at 30 MeV.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that spectroscopic factors (SFs) extracted from higher-energy (d,p) reactions and from thermal neutron capture are consistent for the benchmark case of \(^{48}\)Ca, but only when the asymptotic normalization coefficient (ANC) from sub-Coulomb (d,p) data is used to constrain the bound state. The authors show that (d,p) reactions at around 30 MeV have a similar sensitivity to the nuclear interior as the (n,γ) reaction, making them a better indirect tool than higher-energy data.

## Method

The authors simultaneously analyzed \(^{48}\)Ca(d,p)\(^{49}\)Ca angular distributions at deuteron energies of 2, 13, 19, and 56 MeV, together with thermal (n,γ) data at 25 meV, for both the ground state and first excited state of \(^{49}\)Ca. They extracted the ANC from the peripheral sub-Coulomb (2 MeV) (d,p) data, then used that ANC to extract spectroscopic factors from the higher-energy (d,p) data and the thermal capture data by varying the single-particle Woods-Saxon radius over a range of 1.05–1.65 fm with fixed diffuseness 0.65 fm. The (d,p) calculations used the adiabatic distorted wave approximation (ADWA) with finite-range effects, while the (n,γ) calculations used first-order electromagnetic transition operators in the long-wavelength limit.

## Key numbers

- ANC for \(^{49}\)Ca ground state (\(p_{3/2}\)) from sub-Coulomb (d,p) at 1.99 MeV: \(C^2_{p_{3/2}} = 32.1 \pm 3.2\) fm\(^{-1}\)
- ANC for \(^{49}\)Ca first excited state (\(p_{1/2}\)) from sub-Coulomb (d,p): \(C^2_{p_{1/2}} = 9.30 \pm 0.93\) fm\(^{-1}\)
- Spectroscopic factor for ground state from joint analysis of 2 MeV (d,p) and 56 MeV (d,p): \(S_{exp} = 0.55 \pm 0.25\)
- Spectroscopic factor for ground state from joint analysis of sub-Coulomb (d,p) and thermal (n,γ): \(S_{exp} = 0.53 \pm 0.11\), with single-particle ANC \(b_0 = 7.80 \pm 1.2\) fm\(^{-1/2}\) corresponding to \(r_0 \approx 1.45\) fm
- Spectroscopic factor for first excited state from joint analysis: \(S_{exp} = 0.71^{+0.20}_{-0.12}\), with \(b_0 = 3.63^{+0.54}_{-0.58}\) fm\(^{-1/2}\) corresponding to \(r_0 \approx 1.45\) fm
- Thermal (n,γ) data at 25 meV measured with 6% accuracy; sub-Coulomb (d,p) at 1.992 MeV measured with 10% accuracy
- Neutron scattering length for \(^{48}\)Ca–n: \(a = 0.356 \pm 0.088\) fm

## Context

This work extends the methodology of Mukhamedzhanov (2005) and Pang (2006) for combining ANCs and SFs from transfer reactions, and applies it for the first time to simultaneously analyze (d,p) and (n,γ) reactions on the same nucleus. The benchmark on \(^{48}\)Ca was chosen because of high-quality thermal capture data (Cranston 1971, Beer 1996), well-known neutron scattering length (Sears 1992), and multiple (d,p) data sets (Rapaport 1972, Metz 1975, Uozumi 1994). The results confirm the reduction of SFs relative to shell model predictions seen by Brown (2002) and Lee (2006), and the large bound-state radii (\(r_0 \approx 1.45\) fm) provide evidence for a neutron skin in neutron-rich calcium isotopes. The work enables a validated methodology for extracting (n,γ) cross sections on unstable nuclei using (d,p) reactions, with the recommendation that experiments at around 30 MeV would be optimal.
