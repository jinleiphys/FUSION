# The neutron distribution in nuclei as measured with parity violating electron scattering

## Key claim

Parity-violating elastic electron scattering can provide an almost direct measurement of the Fourier transform of the neutron density in nuclei, but this interpretation relies on the factorization of single-nucleon physics and the plane-wave approximation for the electron, both of which become questionable at higher momentum transfers and in heavy nuclei.

## Method

The authors review the formalism of parity-violating electron scattering from nuclei, expressing the helicity asymmetry in terms of electromagnetic and weak neutral response functions decomposed into Coulomb, electric, and magnetic multipoles. They apply a two-state mixing model for isospin breaking, where the ground state is a superposition of isoscalar and isovector monopole states, and compute the nuclear-structure-dependent correction term Gamma(q) for light N=Z nuclei (C-12, Si-28) and for N≠Z nuclei (C-14, Si-30, Ca-40, Ca-48, Pb-208) using phenomenological density parameterizations from Donnelly et al.

## Key numbers

- The scale of the asymmetry is set by A0 ≈ 6.5×10⁻⁴ τ, where τ = |Q²|/(4m_N²)
- The Weinberg angle was known with precision sin²θ_W = 0.23055 ± 0.00041 (0.18% accuracy) at the time of writing
- For light N=Z nuclei like C-12, the isospin-breaking correction Γ(q) remains below 1% over the momentum transfer range 1.75 ≤ q ≤ 3.5 fm⁻¹
- For N≠Z nuclei like C-14 and Si-30, the structure-dependent term Γ̃(q) takes values between 10% and 50% in the same momentum range
- For Pb-208 at q ≈ 0.5 fm⁻¹, the asymmetry is A ≈ 8×10⁻⁸, and a 1% change in the neutron radius produces about a 6% change in the asymmetry
- The vector couplings in the Standard Model are β_V^p = 0.038 and β_V^n = -0.5

## Context

This paper reviews and extends the work of Donnelly et al. on using parity-violating electron scattering to probe neutron distributions, and builds on earlier proposals by Feinberg and Walecka for testing the Standard Model with spin-zero, isospin-zero nuclei. The authors show that while such experiments were originally conceived to test electroweak theory, the improved precision of the Weinberg angle now makes them better suited for measuring neutron densities, which are needed for interpreting atomic parity-violation experiments in cesium. The work also connects to calculations by Ramavataram et al. and Pandharipande et al. for He-4, and to the authors' own prior work on quasi-elastic polarized electron scattering.
