# Equivalent Representations of Collective Hamiltonian and Implication on Generalized Density Matrix Method

> We discuss equivalent representations of the collective/bosonic Hamiltonian in the form of Taylor expansion over collective coordinate and momentum. Different expansions are equivalent if they are related by a transformation of collective variables. The independent parameters in the collective Hamiltonian are identified, which are much less in number than it appears. In this sense, the microscopic generalized density matrix method fixes the collective Hamiltonian completely, which seems to solve the old problem of microscopic calculation of the collective Hamiltonian.

# Equivalent Representations of Collective Hamiltonian and Implication on Generalized Density Matrix Method

## Key claim

The collective Hamiltonian, when expressed as a Taylor expansion in collective coordinate and momentum, contains far fewer independent parameters than the number of expansion coefficients suggests, because many different expansions are equivalent under transformations of the collective variables. The generalized density matrix (GDM) method provides exactly enough constraints to fix all independent parameters, thereby solving the long-standing problem of microscopically determining the collective Hamiltonian, but only if the assumed form of the collective Hamiltonian (e.g., which terms are nonzero) is correct.

## Method

The authors start from a Taylor expansion of the collective Hamiltonian in powers of the collective coordinate α and momentum π, keeping all symmetry-allowed terms. They then consider general nonlinear transformations of the collective variables (α, π) → (ᾱ, π̄) that preserve the canonical commutation relation [α, π] = i. By requiring that the transformed Hamiltonian be equivalent to the original, they derive constraints on the transformation coefficients. Counting the number of free transformation parameters and the number of constraints at each order of anharmonicity reveals the number of truly independent parameters in the collective Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- The collectivity factor Ω governs the scaling of expansion coefficients: Λ^(mn) ∼ Ω^−(m+n−2)/2, and transformation coefficients scale as x^(mn) ∼ Ω^−(m+n−1)/2, y^(mn) ∼ Ω^−(m+n−1)/2.
- In the harmonic order, there is one independent parameter: ω².
- In the cubic order, there are zero independent parameters (Λ^(30) and Λ^(12) can be set to zero).
- In the quartic order, there is one independent parameter (e.g., Λ^(40)), with Λ^(22) and Λ^(04) set to zero.
- In each even order of anharmonicity (quartic, sextic, octic, ...), there is exactly one independent parameter; odd orders have none.
- In the Lipkin model, only three terms are nonzero: ω², Λ^(40), and Λ^(04); the GDM method must go up to sixth order to fix them, and constraints from eighth order and higher should be satisfied identically.

## Context

This work extends the authors’ own generalized density matrix method (Jia, 2011) by showing that the constraints previously found in each even order of anharmonicity are sufficient to completely determine the collective Hamiltonian, contrary to the earlier impression that the Hamiltonian was not fully fixed. The analysis uses estimates from Zelevinsky (prior work) showing the scaling of coefficients with the collectivity factor Ω, and the Lipkin model and quadrupole-plus-pairing model are cited as confirming these estimates. The result enables a practical microscopic calculation scheme: one assumes a finite set of nonzero terms in the collective Hamiltonian, solves the GDM equations of motion up to twice the anharmonicity order, and checks that higher-order constraints are approximately satisfied.
