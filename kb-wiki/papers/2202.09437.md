# The need for a local nuclear physics feature in the neutron-rich rare-earths to explain solar $r$-process abundances

> We apply Markov Chain Monte Carlo to predict the masses required to form the observed solar $r$-process rare-earth abundance peak. Given highly distinct astrophysical outflows and nuclear inputs, we find that results are most sensitive to the $r$-process dynamics (i.e. overall competition between reactions and decays), with similar mass trends predicted given similar dynamics. We show that regardless of whether fission deposits into the rare-earths or not, our algorithm consistently predicts the need for a local nuclear physics feature of enhanced stability in the neutron-rich lanthanides.

# The need for a local nuclear physics feature in the neutron-rich rare-earths to explain solar $r$-process abundances

## Key claim
The authors use Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) to predict that a local nuclear physics feature of enhanced stability in neutron-rich lanthanides is required to form the solar $r$-process rare-earth abundance peak, regardless of whether fission deposits material into the rare-earth region or not. This conclusion holds across highly distinct astrophysical outflows and nuclear inputs, with the key qualifier that the required mass trends are most sensitive to the overall $r$-process dynamics (hot vs. cold competition between reactions and decays) rather than the exact hydrodynamic properties.

## Method
The authors apply an MCMC procedure to derive mass adjustments to the Duflo-Zuker (DZ) mass model, parameterized as $M(Z,N) = M_{DZ}(Z,N) + a_N e^{-(Z-C)^2/2f}$, where the adjustments are centered in the neutron-rich region. At each MCMC step, neutron capture rates and beta decay properties are updated according to the test masses, a nucleosynthesis calculation is performed, and the resulting abundances are compared to solar data in the $A=150-180$ region to set the evolution of the Markov chain. The method is applied to ten distinct astrophysical outflows (including both hot and cold dynamics from accretion disk winds and neutron star merger dynamical ejecta) with various fission yield treatments (symmetric 50/50 splits, GEF16, and GEF18).

## Key numbers
- Baseline abundances for hot outflows had $\chi^2$ values of 200, 121, 236, 212, and 211; after MCMC, all hot cases achieved $\chi^2 \le 40$, with most around 35.
- Baseline abundances for cold outflows had $\chi^2$ values of 286, 361, 211, 499, and 488; after MCMC, cold cases achieved $\chi^2$ values of 22, 106, 68, 185, and 148.
- The hot dynamical ejecta cases used initial conditions at 8 GK with electron fraction $Y_e = 0.01$, initial entropy of 9 $s/k_B$, and initial density of $1.1 \times 10^9$ g/cm$^3$.
- The cold dynamical ejecta cases used initial conditions at 8 GK with $Y_e = 0.01$ to 0.02, initial entropies of 4 to 22 $s/k_B$, and initial densities of $2.8 \times 10^7$ to $7.0 \times 10^{10}$ g/cm$^3$.
- For hot outflows, the persistent feature requiring enhanced stability is at $N=104$, just outside the latest measurements.
- For cold outflows, the primary feature responsible for peak formation is at $N=101$, which is in tension with latest precision mass measurements.

## Context
This work extends the MCMC methodology previously applied to moderately neutron-rich outflows by Vassh et al. (2018, 2020) to very neutron-rich conditions ($Y_e \sim 0.01$) that significantly populate fissioning nuclei, including dynamical ejecta from neutron star mergers. It directly addresses speculation by Goriely (1999) that late-time fission deposition could be the exclusive origin of the rare-earth peak. The results show that even with fission deposition from GEF16 and GEF18 yields, local lanthanide nuclear features are still required, ruling out fission-only explanations. The work enables future experimental campaigns at ARIEL at TRIUMF, FRIB, and the N=126 Factory at ANL to target neutron-rich lanthanide properties near $N=104$, and could be used alongside simulation advancements to infer the astrophysical site where solar lanthanides were dominantly formed.
