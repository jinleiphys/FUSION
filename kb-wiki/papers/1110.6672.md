# Three dimensional structure of low-density nuclear matter

> We numerically explore the pasta structures and properties of low-density nuclear matter without any assumption on the geometry. We observe conventional pasta structures, while a mixture of the pasta structures appears as a metastable state at some transient densities. We also discuss the lattice structure of droplets.

## Key claim

The authors perform fully three-dimensional calculations of nuclear pasta structures in large cubic cells without assuming any particular geometry, and find that while conventional pasta phases (droplet, rod, slab, tube, bubble) appear as ground states, the face-centered cubic (fcc) lattice is energetically more favorable for droplets than the body-centered cubic (bcc) lattice assumed in most prior work, contrary to previous expectations.

## Method

The authors employ a relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with the Thomas-Fermi approximation, solving the coupled equations of motion for meson fields (sigma, omega, rho) and the Coulomb potential on a three-dimensional grid in a cubic cell with periodic boundary conditions. They use a cell size of about 60 fm and grid width of about 0.8 fm, and search for equilibrium by iteratively adjusting local chemical potentials until they become uniform across the cell, starting from randomly prepared initial density distributions. Calculations are performed for fixed proton fractions Y_p = 0.1, 0.3, and 0.5.

## Key numbers

- Droplet-to-rod structural change occurs around a volume fraction u = 0.2, corresponding to baryon density approximately 0.02 fm^{-3} for symmetric matter (Y_p = 0.5).
- Droplet radii for fcc lattice range from 6.86 fm at baryon density 0.012 fm^{-3} to 7.79 fm at 0.020 fm^{-3}; for bcc lattice they range from 6.99 fm to 7.92 fm over the same density range.
- The Coulomb energy difference between bcc and fcc lattices amounts to about 0.2 to 0.8 MeV, with the ratio of Coulomb energy to total energy difference being about 20%.
- Typical pasta phases observed at specific baryon densities for Y_p = 0.5: spherical droplets (fcc) at 0.01 fm^{-3}, cylindrical rods (honeycomb) at 0.024 fm^{-3}, slabs at 0.05 fm^{-3}, cylindrical tubes (honeycomb) at 0.08 fm^{-3}, and spherical bubbles (fcc) at 0.09 fm^{-3}.
- A typical calculation requires about 10 CPU days on the PRIMERGY BX900 system.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of nuclear pasta that employed the Wigner-Seitz cell approximation with assumed geometrical symmetry, such as those by Ravenhall (1983) and Hashimoto (1984), as well as Thomas-Fermi calculations by Williams and Koonin and Hartree-Fock calculations by Newton that used smaller cubic cells. The key advance is the use of sufficiently large cells (about 60 fm) to include several periods of pasta structures, avoiding the implicit restriction to simple periodicities. The finding that fcc is favored over bcc for droplet lattices contradicts prior results from Oyamatsu and Nakazato, and also differs from quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) calculations that found bcc structures; this discrepancy remains an open question. The work enables more realistic studies of neutron star crust and supernova matter, particularly for beta-equilibrium conditions and finite temperatures where complex metastable structures may contribute to transport properties and cooling processes.
