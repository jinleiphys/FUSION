# A Spallation Model for the Titanium-rich Supernova Remnant Cassiopeia A

> Titanium-rich subluminous supernovae are rare and challenge current SN nucleosynthesis models. We present a model in which ejecta from a standard Supernova is impacted by a second explosion of the neutron star (a Quark-nova), resulting in spallation reactions that lead to 56Ni destruction and 44Ti creation under the right conditions. Basic calculations of the spallation products shows that a delay between the two explosions of ~ 5 days reproduces the observed abundance of 44Ti in Cas A and explains its low luminosity as a result of the destruction of 56Ni. Our results could have important implications for lightcurves of subluminous as well as superluminous supernovae.

# A Spallation Model for the Titanium-rich Supernova Remnant Cassiopeia A

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the titanium-rich but subluminous nature of Cassiopeia A can be explained if a standard supernova explosion is followed approximately 5 days later by a second explosion (a Quark-nova) whose relativistic ejecta spallate the inner supernova ejecta, destroying 56Ni and creating 44Ti; the model is presented as an alternative to explanations invoking extinction by dust or asymmetric explosions, and the authors note that the rarity of such events follows naturally from the required time delay between the two explosions.

## Method

The authors model the Quark-nova as providing a relativistic beam of neutrons (average Lorentz factor ~10, energy ~10 GeV) that strikes the innermost 56Ni layer of the expanding supernova ejecta, which serves as the target. They divide the Ni layer into sub-layers of thickness equal to the neutron spallation mean free path, then use semi-empirical formulas for neutron and proton multiplicities from spallation reactions to compute the distribution of product nuclei via a Poissonian cascade through successive sub-layers, iterating until the projectile energy drops below 73 MeV where spallation effectively ceases. The calculation is performed for different time delays between the supernova and Quark-nova explosions, ranging from 2 to 8 days.

## Key numbers

- The observed 44Ti mass in Cas A is (0.8-2.5) x 10^{-4} solar masses, which would imply an ejected 56Ni mass of at least 0.05 solar masses if produced by standard nucleosynthesis.
- The Quark-nova ejecta mass is about 10^{-3} solar masses, with total ejected nucleons N^0 ~ 1.2 x 10^{54} for M_QN,-3 = 1.
- The neutron energy is E_0 ~ 10 GeV, and the average number of collisions per neutron in the Ni layer is N_coll ~ 2.75 x (M_A,0.1) / ((A_56)^0.3 x (v_5000 km/s x t_delay,5 days)^2).
- For fiducial values, the time delay must be less than 8.3 days for at least one collision per neutron.
- Spallation effectively ceases when projectile energy drops below 73 MeV.
- The neutron-capture timescale is ~10.2 hours for fiducial values, so significant neutron capture requires t_delay < 16 hours.
- The total hydrogen mass that could form from spallation products is M_H ~ 0.1 solar masses x M_QN,-3.
- Hydrogen recombination radiation escapes when the innermost ejecta reaches radius ~1.8 x 10^{15} cm x (M_A0,0.1)^{1/2}, corresponding to ~41 days x (M_A0,0.1)^{1/2} / v_in,5000 after explosion.
- For t_delay = 5 days, only 57% of the original 56Ni remains to experience spallation (the rest having decayed to 56Co).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous dual-shock Quark-nova (dsQN) model, which was introduced as an explanation for superluminous supernovae (Leahy 2008) and studied for photometric and spectroscopic signatures (Ouyed 2010). The paper presents the first exploration of nuclear processing of inner supernova ejecta by Quark-nova ejecta, offering an alternative to the dust-extinction and asymmetric-explosion explanations for Cas A's low luminosity despite high 44Ti content (Maeda 2003). The model predicts delayed hydrogen signatures weeks after the second explosion and modified supernova light curves, and it suggests that the carbon-rich atmosphere of the compact object in Cas A (Ho 2009) could be explained as fallback material from the spallation process.
