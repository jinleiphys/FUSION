# Effects of chiral symmetry restoration on meson and dilepton production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We include effects of chiral symmetry and its restoration in the kinetic equations for baryon propagation and explore the consequences for $η$, $π^0$, $ρ$ and dilepton production in heavy-ion collisions at 1-2A GeV. Numerical calculations are performed using the GiBUU microscopic transport model supplemented by the parity-doublet model for the mean fields of the nucleon and the $N^*(1535)$ resonance. In this chiral model, a strong dropping of the Dirac mass of the $N^*(1535)$ in the high-density stage of a collision leads to a considerable enhancement in the production of this resonance as compared to the standard (non-linear) Walecka model. As the system expands, the Dirac masses of these abundant soft $N^*(1535)$ resonances gradually increase and ultimately cross the $N η$ decay threshold. As a result, an enhanced low-energy $η$ production is observed in the calculations with chiral mean fields. Comparing with TAPS data on $η$ and $π^0$ production we find that the chiral model improves the agreement for the $m_t$-spectra of $η$'s at small $m_t$ in heavy colliding systems. A similar enhancement is also observed in the soft $ρ$ production due to chiral symmetry and its partial restoration. The resulting dilepton yields at low and intermediate invariant masses are slightly enhanced due to these chiral effects which further improve the agreement between GiBUU transport simulations and HADES data for C+C at 1A GeV.

## Key claim

The inclusion of chiral symmetry restoration via the parity-doublet model (PDM) in transport simulations of heavy-ion collisions at 1-2A GeV leads to enhanced low-energy η and soft ρ production, but the improvement over the standard Walecka model is conditional: it is observed primarily for heavy colliding systems (Ar+Ca and Au+Au) at low transverse mass, while for lighter systems (C+C) the chiral model overestimates the low-m_t η yields.

## Method

The authors extended the GiBUU microscopic transport model by incorporating baryonic mean fields from the parity-doublet model with mirror assignment, where the nucleon and its negative-parity partner N*(1535) have Dirac masses that evolve with density. The in-medium production thresholds were modified to account for the stronger dropping of the N*(1535) Dirac mass compared to the nucleon, and the kinetic equations for baryon propagation were solved including elastic and inelastic collisions, resonance decays, and off-shell ρ-meson propagation with collisional broadening. Calculations were performed for various colliding systems (C+C, Ar+Ca, Au+Au) at beam energies of 0.6-2.0A GeV and compared with TAPS and HADES experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The N*(1535) Dirac mass drops strongly at high density, with the N* → ηN decay channel closing at ρ_B ∼ 0.4 ρ_0 according to prior PDM studies (Jido 2008)
- Two parameter sets were used: Set P3 (Zschiesche 2006) with incompressibility K = 510.57 MeV, and Set 2 (Shin 2018) with K = 215 MeV; both reproduce saturation density with binding energy ∼ -16 MeV per nucleon
- The chiral model improves agreement with TAPS data for η m_t-spectra at small m_t in Ar+Ca and Au+Au at 0.8A GeV, but overestimates low-m_t η yields for C+C at 0.8 and 1.0A GeV
- Dilepton yields at low and intermediate invariant masses from C+C at 1A GeV are slightly enhanced by chiral effects, improving agreement with HADES data

## Context

This work extends prior studies of chiral symmetry restoration in dense baryonic matter using the parity-doublet model (Zschiesche 2006, Sasaki 2010, Weyrich 2015) by implementing the PDM mean fields into a full transport model (GiBUU) for heavy-ion collisions. It builds on earlier predictions that the N*(1535) mass drops considerably across the chiral transition (Tripolt 2021) and that this could produce signals in dilepton spectra. The results enable future searches for chiral symmetry restoration signals in low-energy heavy-ion collisions, particularly through η and dilepton measurements at facilities like FAIR, and suggest that the PDM dynamics must be tested against systematic data across different system sizes and beam energies.
