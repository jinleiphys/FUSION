# Four-nucleon force using the method of unitary transformation

> We discuss in detail the derivation of the leading four-nucleon force in chiral effective field theory using the method of unitary transformation. The resulting four-nucleon force is given in both momentum and configuration space. It does not contain any unknown parameters and can be used in few- and many-nucleon studies.

## Key claim

The leading four-nucleon force in chiral effective field theory, derived using the method of unitary transformation, is local and contains no unknown parameters; however, the effective Hamiltonian at N³LO is defined only up to additional unitary transformations on the nucleon subspace, introducing ambiguities that affect the form of the four-nucleon force.

## Method

The derivation uses the method of unitary transformation to project the full pion-nucleon Hamiltonian onto the purely nucleonic subspace, employing a new formulation of the power counting based on canonical field dimensions rather than topological diagram properties. The effective chiral Lagrangian for pions and nucleons is expanded to order κ=4 in the canonical field dimension, and the decoupling equation for the operator A is solved perturbatively to obtain the effective potential. The resulting four-nucleon force is evaluated from all contributing operator sequences at order ν=4, and the effects of additional unitary transformations on the nucleon subspace are studied by introducing generators S₁ and S₂ with arbitrary parameters α₁ and α₂.

## Key numbers

- Pion decay constant: Fπ = 92.4 MeV
- Nucleon axial-vector coupling: gA = 1.267
- The leading four-nucleon force contributes at chiral order ν = 4 (next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order, N³LO)
- The four-nucleon force is parameter-free: it contains no unknown parameters beyond those already present in the two-nucleon and three-nucleon forces at lower orders

## Context

This work extends the chiral effective field theory program for nuclear forces, where two-nucleon forces have been worked out to N³LO by Entem and Machleidt (2003) and Epelbaum et al. (2004), and three-nucleon forces are available at N²LO following van Kolck (1994) and Epelbaum et al. (2002). The leading four-nucleon force derived here is required for complete N³LO calculations in few-nucleon systems, which are needed to test the convergence of the chiral expansion and to address puzzles such as large deviations in deuteron breakup cross sections reported by Ley et al. (2006). The paper also resolves ambiguities from additional unitary transformations by showing that a specific choice of parameters α₁ and α₂ is preferable based on the structure of three-nucleon force contributions.
