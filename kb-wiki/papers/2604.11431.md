# Impact of Effective Nucleon Mass and Multineutron States on the Equation of State for Core-Collapse Supernovae

> In this study, we investigate the impact of effective nucleon mass and the existence of the dineutron $(\mathrm{^{2}n})$ and the tetraneutron $(\mathrm{^{4}n})$ on the thermodynamic properties and nuclear compositions by constructing new equations of state. Our results indicate that the model with a larger effective nucleon mass slightly alters the nuclear composition in neutron-rich environments primarily due to differences in the symmetry energy: the mass fractions of unbound neutrons, protons, and heavy nuclei increase. The impact on the thermodynamic properties is negligible, except for the chemical potentials. On the other hand, multineutron states become prominent at high densities in neutron-rich environments, leading to a substantial reduction in the unbound neutron fraction. This depletion lowers the chemical potential of unbound neutrons, which in turn reduces the abundance of neutron-rich nuclei. Consequently, the number of unbound protons increases, leading to a corresponding rise in proton chemical potential. These shifts in chemical potentials promote the formation of heavy nuclei with larger mass and atomic numbers. Ultimately, this compositional shift results in a lower free energy, primarily driven by the emergence of these heavy nuclei.

# Impact of Effective Nucleon Mass and Multineutron States on the Equation of State for Core-Collapse Supernovae

## Key claim
The model with a larger effective nucleon mass (TM1m) slightly alters nuclear composition in neutron-rich environments primarily due to differences in the symmetry energy, but the impact on thermodynamic properties is negligible except for chemical potentials. The inclusion of dineutron (²n) and tetraneutron (⁴n) states becomes prominent at high densities in neutron-rich environments, leading to substantial reduction in the unbound neutron fraction and ultimately resulting in lower free energy driven by the emergence of heavy nuclei.

## Method
The authors constructed new equations of state within an extended nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) formalism, using the TM1e and TM1m relativistic mean-field models to describe uniform nuclear matter and unbound nucleon interactions. They incorporated a wide range of nuclear species from experimental mass data and the Finite-Range Droplet Model, and included dineutron and tetraneutron states with binding energies of -0.066 MeV and -2.37 MeV respectively. The models were compared against the Thomas-Fermi single-nucleus approximation (SNA) from Shen (2020).

## Key numbers
- TM1m effective nucleon mass ratio: M*/M = 0.793; TM1e: M*/M = 0.634
- Saturation density for both models: n₀ = 0.145 fm⁻³
- Symmetry energy at saturation for both models: E_sym = 31.4 MeV; symmetry energy slope: L = 40 MeV
- TM1m predicts neutron star radius of 12.4 km for 1.4 M☉; TM1e predicts 13.1 km
- Dineutron binding energy: B_²n = -0.066 MeV; tetraneutron binding energy: B_⁴n = -2.37 MeV
- Multineutron states emerge prominently at densities around 10⁻³ fm⁻³ at Y_p = 0.1

## Context
This work extends the TM1e-based equation of state framework of Shen (2020) and the TM1m model of Li (2024) by incorporating them into an NSE formalism rather than the Thomas-Fermi single-nucleus approximation. It also builds on the authors' previous study (Matsuki 2024) that investigated multineutron states in supernova post-bounce conditions but did not account for nuclear interactions of unbound nucleons. The results enable future supernova and proto-neutron star cooling simulations to systematically test how effective nucleon mass and multineutron states influence dynamical evolution, particularly neutrino emission and scattering.
