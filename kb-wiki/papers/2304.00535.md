# Polaronic Proton and Diproton Clustering in Neutron-Rich Matter

> We show that strong spin-triplet neutron-proton interaction causes polaronic protons to occur in neutron matter at subnuclear densities and nonzero temperature. As the neutron density increases, proton spectra exhibit a smooth crossover from a bare impurity to a repulsive polaron branch; this branch coexists with an attractive polaron branch. With the neutron density increased further, the attractive polarons become stable with respect to deuteron formation. For two adjacent protons, we find that the polaron effects and the neutron-mediated attraction are sufficient to induce a bound diproton, which leads possibly to diproton formation in the surface region of neutron-rich nuclei in laboratories as well as in neutron stars.

# Polaronic Proton and Diproton Clustering in Neutron-Rich Matter

## Key claim

Strong spin-triplet neutron-proton interactions cause protons immersed in neutron matter at subnuclear densities and nonzero temperature to form polaronic quasiparticles; at sufficiently high neutron densities where the Fermi energy exceeds the deuteron binding energy, attractive polarons become stable against deuteron formation, and the combination of polaron effects and neutron-mediated attraction can bind two adjacent protons into a diproton even though diprotons are unbound in vacuum.

## Method

The authors employ a many-body T-matrix approach (TMA), originally developed for atomic Fermi polarons, to describe a single proton impurity in a spin-balanced ideal neutron gas at finite temperature, taking the limit of zero proton fraction. The spin-triplet neutron-proton interaction is modeled using a Yamaguchi-type separable potential fitted to the empirical scattering length (5.42 fm), effective range (1.76 fm), and deuteron binding energy (2.2 MeV). The proton self-energy is computed from the in-medium neutron-proton T-matrix, and the diproton bound-state condition is obtained from the pole of the proton-proton scattering T-matrix, incorporating the polaron residue, effective mass, and a leading-order neutron-mediated interaction evaluated at zero temperature.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron binding energy: \(E_{\rm d} = 2.2\) MeV, used as the reference energy for polaron stability.
- Spin-triplet scattering length: \(a_{\rm t} = 5.42\) fm; effective range: \(r_{\rm t} = 1.76\) fm (empirical values for the neutron-proton interaction).
- Proton-proton scattering length: \(a_{\rm s} = -17.164\) fm; effective range: \(r_{\rm s} = 2.865\) fm (electromagnetic-subtracted values for the direct proton-proton interaction).
- Temperature fixed at \(T = 2.0\) MeV for the main spectral calculations; the dimensionless temperature \(T/T_{\rm F}\) ranges from \(\sim 10\) at \(k_{\rm F} \simeq 0.1\) fm\(^{-1}\) to \(\sim 0.1\) at \(k_{\rm F} \simeq 0.8\) fm\(^{-1}\).
- Critical neutron Fermi momentum for diproton binding: \(k_{\rm F} \simeq 0.3\) fm\(^{-1}\) (dependent on polaron parameters).
- Polaron residue \(Z\) ranges from approximately 0.6 to 1.0 over the densities studied; effective mass ratio \(M_{\rm eff}/M\) exceeds 1 at low densities and falls below 1 at \(k_{\rm F} \gtrsim 0.8\) fm\(^{-1}\).

## Context

This work extends the concept of Fermi polarons from cold-atom physics (Massignan et al., 2014; Kohstall et al., 2012) to nuclear systems, specifically addressing a proton impurity in neutron matter relevant to neutron star crusts and neutron-rich nuclei. It builds on prior studies of alpha particles in dilute neutron matter by some of the same authors (Tajima et al., 2020) and on the Lee-Low-Pines treatment of a proton-phonon system (PhysRevC.47.1077, 1993). The finding that unbound clusters can become bound due to medium effects parallels earlier work on the Hoyle state and 8Be in neutron matter (PhysRevC.104.065801, 2021; PhysRevC.106.045807, 2022). The results enable future investigation of diproton Bose-Einstein condensation and possible modifications to the standard BCS-type proton superconductivity scenario in neutron star cooling (Chamel & Haensel, 2017), as well as novel alpha-clustering structures in neutron-rich nuclei.
