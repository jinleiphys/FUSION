# Canonical Ensemble vs. Grand Canonical Ensemble in the Description of Multicomponent Bosonic Systems

> The thermodynamics of a system of interacting bosonic particles and antiparticles in the presence of the Bose-Einstein condensate is studied in the framework of the Skyrme-like mean-field model. It is assumed that the total charge density (isospin density) is conserved at all temperatures. Two cases are explicitly considered: zero and nonzero isospin charge of the system. A comparative analysis is carried out using Canonical Ensemble and Grand Canonical Ensemble. It is shown that the Grand Canonical Ensemble is not suitable for describing bosonic systems of particles and antiparticles in the presence of condensate.

## Key claim

The Grand Canonical Ensemble is not suitable for describing multicomponent bosonic systems (particles and antiparticles) in the presence of a Bose-Einstein condensate because the chemical potential becomes fixed by the condensate formation condition and is no longer a free variable; an adequate description requires the Canonical Ensemble, where the chemical potential is a thermodynamic function of the conserved charge density and temperature.

## Method

The authors study a relativistic system of interacting bosonic particles and antiparticles (modeled as pi-mesons) using a Skyrme-like mean-field model where the mean field depends on the total particle density as \(U(n) = -A n + B n^2\). They solve self-consistent equations for the total particle density \(n\) and isospin (charge) density \(n_I\) at finite temperature, considering three regimes: (a) both components in the thermal phase, (b) particles in the condensate phase with antiparticles thermal, and (c) both components in the condensate phase. The phase structure is classified by the parameter \(\kappa = A/(2\sqrt{mB})\), and the Gibbs criterion (pressure comparison) determines which phase is thermodynamically realized.

## Key numbers

- The critical attraction coefficient is \(A_c = 2\sqrt{mB}\), corresponding to \(\kappa_c = 1\); for \(\kappa > 1\) (strong attraction) two real roots of \(U(n) + m = 0\) exist.
- For the numerical example, parameters are: \(B = 10 m v_0^2\), \(m = 140\) MeV, \(v_0 = 0.45\) fm\(^3\), with \(\kappa = 1.1\).
- The finite charge density case uses \(n_I = 0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The critical curve for thermal particles is \(n_{\rm lim}(T)\), defined by the Bose-Einstein distribution with \(\mu_I = m\).
- In the condensate phase for both components, the chemical potential \(\mu_I = 0\) regardless of the finite charge density \(n_I = 0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Bose-Einstein condensation in relativistic bosonic systems with conserved charge, first studied by Haber and Weldon (1981) and Kapusta (1981), who noted that below \(T_c\) the chemical potential determines only the charge density of excited states. The authors build on their own mean-field model (Anchishkin 1992, 1995) and show that the Grand Canonical Ensemble fails in the condensate phase for interacting systems, consistent with Kerson Huang's (1987) statement that Bose-Einstein condensation requires particle number conservation. The results enable a proper thermodynamic description of pion condensates in relativistic heavy-ion collisions and neutron star matter, where charge conservation is essential.
