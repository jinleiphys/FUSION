# Comparison of Statistical Multifragmentation Model simulations with Canonical Thermodynamical Model results: a few representative cases

> The statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) has been widely used to explain experimental data of intermediate energy heavy ion collisions. A later entrant in the field is the canonical thermodynamic model (CTM) which is also being used to fit experimental data. The basic physics of both the models is the same, namely that fragments are produced according to their statistical weights in the available phase space. However, they are based on different statistical ensembles, and the methods of calculation are different: while the SMM uses Monte-Carlo simulations, the CTM solves recursion relations. In this paper we compare the predictions of the two models for a few representative cases.

# Comparison of Statistical Multifragmentation Model simulations with Canonical Thermodynamical Model results: a few representative cases

## Key claim
The statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) and the canonical thermodynamic model (CTM) produce very similar predictions for fragment yields and caloric curves across a range of systems and excitation energies, despite being based on different statistical ensembles (microcanonical/grand-canonical versus canonical) and using different computational methods (Monte-Carlo simulations versus recursion relations). However, the agreement holds primarily for channels with significant yields, and systematic differences appear in isotope distributions for large fragments due to the broader phase space sampled by the grand-canonical ensemble used in SMM at higher temperatures.

## Method
The authors compared fragment mass distributions, isotope yields, and caloric curves from the CTM (which solves recursion relations for the canonical partition function at fixed temperature, mass number A0, charge Z0, and freeze-out volume V=6V0) with SMM simulations using either a microcanonical treatment (for low excitation energy, E0=1 MeV/nucleon, restricting multiplicity M≤3) or a grand-canonical Monte-Carlo sampling (for medium and high temperatures of 5 and 8 MeV). Both models used the same liquid-drop description for fragments with mass number a≥5, the same treatment of light nuclei (A<5) with experimental ground-state properties, and the Wigner-Seitz approximation for Coulomb interactions. Three systems were studied: A0=73, Z0=32; A0=146, Z0=64; and A0=146, Z0=56.

## Key numbers
- At low excitation energy E0=1 MeV/nucleon, the average temperature Tp from the microcanonical SMM is 3.13 MeV; CTM calculations were performed at this same temperature.
- For medium and high excitation energies, comparisons were made at fixed temperatures of 5 MeV and 8 MeV for all three systems.
- The caloric curves from the two models agree to within ΔT ∼ 0.1 MeV, which the authors state is likely experimentally indistinguishable.
- In the multifragmentation region (T > 4 MeV), a larger system (A0=146, Z0=64) has a slightly larger excitation energy per nucleon than a smaller system (A0=73, Z0=32), attributed to stronger Coulomb effects.
- At temperatures T ≈ 3.5-5 MeV, the neutron-rich system (A0=146, Z0=56) shows a slightly lower excitation energy compared to the symmetric system (A0=146, Z0=64), indicating an isospin effect.

## Context
This work provides a systematic quantitative comparison between two widely used statistical models for nuclear multifragmentation: the SMM, developed by Bondorf and collaborators (Bondorf, 1995), and the CTM, introduced by Das Gupta and collaborators (Das Gupta, 2001). The SMM has been successfully applied to describe experimental data from intermediate-energy heavy ion collisions (Xi, 1997; Dagostino, 1999; Scharenberg, 2001), while the CTM has also been used to fit experimental fragment yields (Das Gupta, 2001; Tsang, 2001). The comparison is long overdue and enables researchers to understand which ensemble is most appropriate for analyzing specific experimental observables, particularly for characteristics sensitive to ensemble choice such as isoscaling. The authors note that subsequent de-excitation of primary hot fragments must be accounted for when interpreting experimental data, and that experimental uncertainty in thermal source parameters may have a greater impact on results than the choice of statistical ensemble.
