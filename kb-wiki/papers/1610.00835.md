# A fast algorithm for radiative transport in isotropic media

> We propose in this work a fast numerical algorithm for solving the equation of radiative transfer (ERT) in isotropic media. The algorithm has two steps. In the first step, we derive an integral equation for the angularly averaged ERT solution by taking advantage of the isotropy of the scattering kernel, and solve the integral equation with a fast multipole method (FMM). In the second step, we solve a scattering-free transport equation to recover the original ERT solution. Numerical simulations are presented to demonstrate the performance of the algorithm for both homogeneous and inhomogeneous media.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a fast numerical algorithm for solving the equation of radiative transfer (ERT) in isotropic media that reduces the computational complexity by avoiding explicit angular discretization, but the method is specifically limited to media where the scattering kernel and physical coefficients do not depend on the angular variable.

## Method

The algorithm first derives a Fredholm integral equation of the second type for the angularly averaged ERT solution (the local density U(x)) by integrating the characteristic-based integral formulation of the ERT over all directions. This integral equation is solved using a Krylov subspace method (GMRES) accelerated with an interpolation-based fast multipole method (FMM). Once the angularly averaged solution is obtained, a scattering-free transport equation is solved to recover the full angular-dependent ERT solution.

## Key numbers

* The integral kernel for the volume integral equation takes the form K(x,y) = (1/|S^{d-1}|) * mu_s(y) * E(x,y) / |x-y|^{d-1}, where |S^{d-1}| = 2pi for d=2 and |S^{d-1}| = 4pi for d=3
* For piecewise constant collocation discretization, the numerical error is O(h^2(1+log|h|)) when using analytical integration (M-i) or the approximation method (M-ii), and O(h) when using the simplest approximation (M-iii)
* The number of discretization cells N scales as O(h^{-d}) where d is the spatial dimension (2 or 3)

## Context

This work extends the extensive literature on numerical methods for the equation of radiative transfer, which includes Monte Carlo methods (Bhuiyan and Spanier 2007, Deaconu et al. 2012, Edstrom 2005) and various deterministic discretization schemes (Adams and Nowak 1998, Asadzadeh 1998, Gao and Zhao 2009). The key innovation is reformulating the ERT into a volume integral equation that can be solved with FMM acceleration, enabling computational complexity that does not scale with angular discretization size. This enables efficient computation of the local density U(x) for applications in nuclear engineering, astrophysics, biomedical optics, and radiation therapy where the angularly averaged solution is often the primary quantity of interest.
