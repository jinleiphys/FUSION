# Electromagnetic excitation of the Delta(1232)-resonance

> We review the description of the lowest-energy nucleon excitation -- the $Δ$(1232)-resonance. Much of the recent experimental effort has been focused on the precision measurements of the nucleon-to-$Δ$ transition by means of electromagnetic probes. We confront the results of these measuremnts with the state-of-the-art calculations based on chiral effective-field theories (EFT), lattice QCD, large-$N_c$ relations, perturbative QCD, and QCD-inspired models. We also discuss the link of the nucleon-to-$Δ$ form factors to generalized parton distributions (GPDs). Some of the theoretical approaches are reviewed in detail, in particular, recent dynamical and unitary-isobar models of pion electroproduction, which are extensively used in the interpretation of experiments. Also, the novel extension of chiral EFTs to the energy domain of the $Δ$-resonance is described in detail. The two-photon exchange effects in the electroexcitation of the $\De$-resonance are addressed here as well.

## Key claim

This review confronts precision measurements of the nucleon-to-Delta(1232) electromagnetic transition with state-of-the-art calculations from chiral effective-field theories, lattice QCD, large-Nc relations, perturbative QCD, and QCD-inspired models, but the abstract's claim of a comprehensive confrontation is hedged by the paper's focus on describing the theoretical frameworks in detail rather than presenting a single definitive comparison or resolving all discrepancies between experiment and theory.

## Method

The paper reviews the general analysis of pion photo- and electroproduction on the nucleon, presenting the kinematics, Lorentz-covariant decompositions into invariant amplitudes, the CGLN decomposition into Pauli-spinor amplitudes, helicity amplitudes, multipole decompositions, and isospin decompositions. It then surveys multiple theoretical approaches including dynamical and unitary-isobar models (such as the MAID model and the Sato-Lee model), chiral effective-field theories extended to the Delta-resonance energy domain, lattice QCD calculations, large-Nc relations, and QCD-inspired models (constituent quark models, chiral bag models, Skyrme models, and chiral quark-soliton models), and discusses the link of nucleon-to-Delta form factors to generalized parton distributions.

## Key numbers

- The Delta(1232) resonance is the lowest-energy nucleon excitation with a mass of approximately 1232 MeV (from the resonance name itself).
- The E2/M1 ratio for the gamma N to Delta(1232) transition has been measured in experiments such as Beck et al. (1997) and Beck et al. (2000) using the reactions p(gamma(pol.),p)pi0 and p(gamma(pol.),pi+)n.
- The C2/M1 ratio for the electroexcitation of the Delta(1232) has been measured by the CLAS Collaboration (Joo et al., 2002) and the OOPS Collaboration (Sparveris et al., 2005) at various Q^2 values.
- Lattice QCD calculations by Alexandrou et al. (2004, 2005) provide the N to Delta electromagnetic transition form factors at multiple Q^2 values.
- The MAID unitary isobar model (Drechsel et al., 1999) analyzes pion photo- and electroproduction data up to 1 GeV.
- The Sato-Lee dynamical model (Sato and Lee, 1996, 2001) provides a coupled-channels approach for pion electroproduction.
- Chiral effective-field theory calculations by Pascalutsa and Vanderhaeghen (2005, 2006) predict the electromagnetic nucleon to Delta transition at next-to-leading order.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on the Delta(1232) resonance, including the reviews by Brown and Weise (1975) on pion scattering and isobars in nuclei, by Cattapan and Ferreira (2002) on the role of the Delta in nuclear physics, and by Burkert and Lee (2004) on electromagnetic meson production in the nucleon resonance region. It also builds on the dynamical and unitary-isobar models of Drechsel and Tiator (1992), the MAID model (Drechsel et al., 1999), and the Sato-Lee model (Sato and Lee, 1996, 2001), as well as chiral perturbation theory developments by Bernard, Kaiser, and Meissner (1995) and the extension to the Delta-resonance region by Pascalutsa and Vanderhaeghen (2005, 2006). The review enables future work by providing a comprehensive theoretical framework for interpreting ongoing and future precision measurements of the nucleon-to-Delta transition at facilities such as Jefferson Lab, and by establishing connections between nucleon-to-Delta form factors and generalized parton distributions that can be explored in deeply virtual Compton scattering and hard exclusive reactions.
