# Causality in relativistic many body theory

> The stability of the nuclear matter system with respect to density fluctuations is examined exploring in detail the pole structure of the electro-nuclear response functions. Making extensive use of the method of dispersion integrals we calculate the full polarization propagator not only for real energies in the spacelike and timelike regime but also in the whole complex energy plane. The latter proved to be necessary in order to identify unphysical causality violating poles which are the consequence of a neglection of vacuum polarization. On the contrary it is shown that Dirac sea effects stabilize the nuclear matter system shifting the unphysical pole from the upper energy plane back to the real axis. The exchange of strength between these real timelike collective excitations and the spacelike energy regime is shown to lead to a reduction of the quasielastic peak as it is seen in electron scattering experiments. Neglecting vacuum polarization one also obtains a reduction of the quasielastic peak but in this case the strength is partly shifted to the causality violating pole mentioned above which consequently cannot be considered as a physical reliable result. Our investigation of the response function in the energy region above the threshold of nucleon anti-nucleon production leads to another remarkable result. Treating the nucleons as point-like Dirac particles we show that for any isospin independent NN-interaction RPA-correlations provide a reduction of the production amplitude for $p\bar p$-pairs by a factor 2.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in relativistic many-body calculations of nuclear matter response functions, neglecting Dirac sea (vacuum polarization) effects introduces unphysical, causality-violating poles in the upper complex energy plane, making the system unstable; only calculations that include vacuum polarization preserve causality and produce physically reliable collective excitations.

## Method

The authors analytically continue the longitudinal and transverse electro-nuclear response functions of nuclear matter (described by the Walecka sigma-omega model) from real energies into the full complex energy plane using dispersion integrals. They compute the full polarization propagator via spectral representations of the imaginary parts, performing subtractions in the dispersion integrals to renormalize the vacuum contributions, and then examine the pole structure of the RPA response functions for both the spacelike (quasielastic) and timelike (collective excitation and particle-antiparticle production) regimes.

## Key numbers

* Saturation density of nuclear matter corresponds to a Fermi momentum of \(k_{\mathrm F} = 280\,\mathrm{MeV}/c\) and an energy of \(-16\,\mathrm{MeV}\) per nucleon.
* The scalar meson mass is \(m_s = 550\,\mathrm{MeV}\) and the vector meson mass is \(m_v = 783\,\mathrm{MeV}\).
* In the Hartree approximation (including vacuum polarization), the effective nucleon mass is \(m^* = 0.78\,m\); in the mean field approximation (neglecting vacuum polarization), it is \(m^* = 0.56\,m\).
* For any isospin-independent NN-interaction, RPA correlations reduce the production amplitude for \(p\bar{p}\)-pairs by a factor of 2 at high energies.
* The Landau ghost in meson propagators appears only for momentum transfers \(|\vec q| \geq 2.55\,m\) (where \(m\) is the nucleon mass), which is higher than the momenta considered.

## Context

This work extends earlier relativistic nuclear matter calculations of response functions by Kurasawa, Wehrberger, Horowitz, and others, which were performed only for spacelike kinematics and gave conflicting treatments of vacuum polarization. The paper shows that calculations neglecting Dirac sea effects (as in some earlier RPA studies) produce unphysical poles in the upper complex energy plane, whereas including vacuum polarization stabilizes the system and yields physically consistent collective excitations. This enables a unified understanding of how strength is transferred from the quasielastic peak to timelike collective modes, and it provides a general result for the halving of the nucleon-antinucleon production amplitude that can be tested in heavy-ion collision experiments.
