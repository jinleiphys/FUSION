# Tensor Polarized Gamma-Deuteron Compton Scattering in Effective Field Theory

> The differential cross section for gamma-deuteron Compton scattering from a tensor polarized deuteron is computed in an effective field theory. The first non-vanishing contributions to this differential cross section are the interference terms between the leading electric coupling diagrams and the subleading single potential pion exchange diagrams or the subleading magnetic moment coupling diagrams. At 90 degree photon scattering angle, only the pion term contributes at this order to the tensor polarized differential cross section. This provides a clean way to study the photon pion dynamics in the two nucleon sector. The effect is measurable for photon energies between 40 and 80 MeV provided the uncertainty in the measured cross sections are less than 7%.

## Key claim

The first non-vanishing contributions to the tensor polarized gamma-deuteron Compton scattering differential cross section are interference terms between leading electric coupling diagrams and subleading single potential pion exchange diagrams or subleading magnetic moment coupling diagrams; at a 90-degree photon scattering angle, only the pion term contributes at this order, providing a clean way to study photon-pion dynamics in the two-nucleon sector, but the effect is measurable only for photon energies between 40 and 80 MeV provided the uncertainty in measured cross sections is less than 7%.

## Method

The differential cross section for tensor polarized gamma-deuteron Compton scattering was computed analytically using a nucleon-nucleon effective field theory (EFT) expansion. The scalar form factor S was calculated at leading order (LO) from electric coupling diagrams, while the tensor form factor T was calculated at next-to-leading order (NLO) from one-potential-pion exchange diagrams and magnetic moment coupling diagrams, with the tensor polarized differential cross section dσ₂ formed from the S-T interference term. The calculation was performed in the deuteron rest frame (lab frame) with the incident photon direction set as the (0,0,1) axis, and the pion propagators were expanded to first order in photon momentum, neglecting terms suppressed by k²/mπ².

## Key numbers

- The pion decay constant used is f = 132 MeV.
- The isoscalar nucleon magnetic moment is κ⁽⁰⁾ = (κp + κn)/2 and isovector is κ⁽¹⁾ = (κp - κn)/2, with κp = 2.79285 and κn = -1.91304 in nuclear magnetons.
- The four-nucleon operator coefficients are C₀⁽¹S₀⁾ = -3.34 fm² and C₀⁽³S₁⁾ = -5.51 fm² at renormalization scale μ = mπ.
- The tensor polarized differential cross section dσ₂ vanishes at LO and is given at NLO by dσ₂^NLO/dΩ = (α²/(2M_N²)) [Re(F₀^LO F₂^NLO*)(1+cos²θ) + 2 Re(F₀^LO G₂^NLO*) cosθ].
- At 90 degrees scattering angle, the magnetic interference term vanishes, leaving only the pion contribution.
- The effect is measurable for photon energies between 40 and 80 MeV if experimental uncertainties are less than 7%.
- At backward angles, the effect could be seen if measurement uncertainties are less than 10%.
- The effective field theory expansion is expected to break down at photon energy of about 100 MeV, with neglected NLO terms suppressed by k²/mπ² contributing a 30% effect at 80 MeV.
- The NLO results for G₂ agree with potential model calculations of Weyrauch and Arenhovel (1983) to about 10%, while agreement on F₂ is about 30-40%.

## Context

This work extends the effective field theory treatment of unpolarized gamma-deuteron Compton scattering previously computed by Chen, Griesshammer, Savage, and Springer (1998) to the tensor polarized case, providing the first analytic calculation of this observable. The results enable experimental tests of photon-pion dynamics in the two-nucleon sector using tensor polarized deuteron targets, complementing planned vector polarized experiments at TUNL for the Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule, and the tensor polarized cross section at 90 degrees isolates the pion contribution from magnetic contributions. The calculations agree with potential model results of Weyrauch and Arenhovel (1983) to within 10-40% depending on the form factor, and the approach can be extended to next-next-to-leading order to reduce theoretical uncertainty from the current estimated 30% to a few percent.
