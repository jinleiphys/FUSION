# Non-Symmetrized Hyperspherical Harmonics Method for Non-Equal Mass Three-Body Systems

> The non-symmetrized hyperspherical harmonics method for a three-body system, composed by two particles having equal masses, but different from the mass of the third particle, is reviewed and applied to the $^3$H, $^3$He nuclei and $^3_Λ$H hyper-nucleus, seen respectively as $nnp$, $ppn$ and $NNΛ$ three-body systems. The convergence of the method is first tested in order to estimate its accuracy. Then, the difference of binding energy between $^3$H and $^3$He due to the difference of the proton and the neutron masses is studied using several central spin-independent and spin-dependent potentials. Finally, the $^3_Λ$H hypernucleus binding energy is calculated using different $NN$ and $ΛN$ potential models. The results have been compared with those present in the literature, finding a very nice agreement.

## Key claim

The non-symmetrized hyperspherical harmonics (NSHH) method, which avoids the preliminary antisymmetrization of the wave function, can be straightforwardly extended to three-body systems where two particles have equal masses different from the third, and yields binding energies for $^3$H, $^3$He, and $^3_\Lambda$H that agree well with literature values, provided the expansion is pushed to sufficiently large values of the grand-angular momentum $G^{max}$ and the maximum $j$ value $j_{max}$.

## Method

The authors expand the three-body wave function in a basis of hyperspherical harmonics (HH) functions using a fixed permutation (p=3) without prior symmetrization, and expand the hyperradial part in generalized Laguerre polynomials. The Rayleigh-Ritz variational principle reduces the problem to a generalized eigenvalue problem solved via the Lanczos algorithm, with potential matrix elements evaluated by transforming HH functions between different Jacobi coordinate permutations using Raynal-Revai coefficients. The method is applied to $^3$H ($nnp$), $^3$He ($ppn$), and $^3_\Lambda$H ($NN\Lambda$) using central spin-independent potentials (Volkov, Afnan-Tang, Malfliet-Tjon) and spin-dependent potentials (Minnesota, Argonne AV4$'$) for the nuclear systems, and three potential models for the hypernucleus.

## Key numbers

- $^3$H binding energy with AV4$'$ potential (equal masses): 8.991 MeV, converging to 8.992 MeV from the symmetrized HH method (Kievsky 2008)
- Binding energy difference $\Delta B = B_{^3\text{H}} - B_{^3\text{He}}$ (without Coulomb): 14 keV for Afnan-Tang, Malfliet-Tjon, and AV4$'$ potentials; 11 keV for Volkov; 8 keV for Minnesota
- $^3$He binding energy including point Coulomb interaction with AV4$'$ potential: 8.272 MeV
- $^3$H binding energy with Volkov potential (equal masses): 8.465 MeV, matching the literature value of 8.465 MeV (Kievsky 2008)
- $^3$H binding energy with Malfliet-Tjon potential (equal masses): 8.250 MeV, compared to 8.253 MeV from literature (Kievsky 2008)
- $^3$H binding energy with Afnan-Tang potential (equal masses): 6.697 MeV, compared to 6.698 MeV from the symmetrized HH method (Kievsky 2008)
- $^3$H binding energy with Minnesota potential (equal masses): 8.385 MeV, compared to 8.386 MeV from literature (Kievsky 2008)
- Convergence for AV4$'$ required $G^{max}=80$, $N_{max}=24$, $j_{max}=20$ to reach approximately 3 keV accuracy
- Convergence for Malfliet-Tjon required $G^{max}=90$, $j_{max}=22$ to reach approximately 3 keV uncertainty

## Context

This work extends the non-symmetrized hyperspherical harmonics method, previously demonstrated for equal-mass systems by Gattobigio (2009, 2011) and Deflorian (2013, 2014), to non-equal mass three-body systems, making the treatment of $^3$H, $^3$He, and hypernuclei more straightforward than in the standard symmetrized HH approach of Kievsky (2008). The calculated binding energy difference $\Delta B = 14$ keV for the AV4$'$ potential agrees with the Faddeev equation result of Perez (2014) using the realistic Argonne AV18 potential, validating the method. The approach enables systematic studies of mass-asymmetric three-body systems and hypernuclei without the computational overhead of symmetrization, though it requires larger basis sizes (higher $G^{max}$ and $j_{max}$) than the symmetrized method.
