# Constraint fitting of experimental data with a jet quenching model embedded in a hydrodynamical bulk medium

> We present a global fit to single- and double-inclusive suppression data of high-$p_T$ particles in central Au+Au collisions at top RHIC energy. We also include in this analysis data on heavy quarks via their D and B meson semi-leptonic decays (i.e. non-photonic electrons). The analysis is based on the parton quenching weights for medium-induced gluon radiation computed in the BDMPS approximation then embedded in a hydrodynamical description of the bulk medium. Our results indicate that values of the transport coefficient $\hat q$ more than four times larger than perturbative estimates are preferred by experimental data. This confirms previous calculations based on simpler implementations of the medium geometry or only the single-inclusive suppression. We also comment on the statistical compatibility of the heavy quark data within a radiative only energy loss scenario, and on the sensitivity of the results to nuclear modification of the parton distribution functions (PDFs) and to assumptions on the energy loss during times in the collision prior to the hydrodynamical behavior.}

## Key claim

The authors find that a global fit to single-inclusive pion suppression, dihadron suppression, and heavy-flavor electron data in central Au+Au collisions at RHIC requires a transport coefficient $\hat q$ more than four times larger than perturbative QCD estimates, but they caution that the precise interpretation of this magnitude is uncertain due to subleading terms in the high-energy approximation and unknown higher-order corrections.

## Method

The authors embed parton quenching weights for medium-induced gluon radiation in the BDMPS approximation into a full three-dimensional ideal hydrodynamical description of the bulk medium, using the Salgado-Wiedemann quenching weights with effective scaling relations for a dynamical medium. They perform a modified $\chi^2$ analysis that treats statistical, correlated systematic, and normalization uncertainties consistently, fitting the $K$ parameter that scales the local transport coefficient $\hat q(\xi) = K \cdot 2 \epsilon^{3/4}(\xi)$ relative to the energy density from hydrodynamics. The analysis includes three different extrapolations for the transport coefficient at times before the hydrodynamical thermalization time $\tau_0 = 0.6$ fm/$c$, and considers three different nuclear parton distribution function sets (EKS98, EPS08, and no nuclear modification).

## Key numbers

* Global fit to light hadron data ($R_{AA}$ for $\pi^0$ and $I_{AA}$ for dihadrons) yields $K = 4.1 \pm 0.6$ for the canonical calculation (EKS98 nPDFs, Case ii for early times).
* The average transport coefficient at the minimum $K=4.1$ is $\langle \hat q \rangle \simeq 6.2$, 8.7, and 138.5 GeV$^2$/fm for Cases i, ii, and iii respectively, showing large ambiguity in the averaged value for an evolving medium.
* The hydrodynamical model uses initial time $\tau_0 = 0.6$ fm/$c$, critical temperature $T_c = 170$ MeV, bag constant $B^{1/4} = 247$ MeV, and kinetic decoupling temperature $T^{\rm dec} = 100$ MeV.
* The analysis uses 14 data points for PHENIX $\pi^0$ $R_{AA}$ ($p_T^{\pi^0} > 5$ GeV, 0-5% centrality), 6 data points for STAR $I_{AA}$ ($p_T^{\rm trig}=8-15$ GeV, 0-5% centrality), and 20 data points for non-photonic electron $R_{AA}$ (11 from PHENIX, 9 from STAR, $p_T^e > 3$ GeV).
* For the heavy quark case, an 8% theoretical uncertainty from scale and mass variation is included, and the $K$ range is extended up to $K^\prime = 40$.

## Context

This work extends previous global fits by Zhang et al. (2007) and Nagle et al. (2008) by including a full hydrodynamical description of the medium (following Hirano et al., 2003-2004) and simultaneously fitting light hadron single-inclusive suppression, dihadron suppression, and heavy quark suppression via non-photonic electrons. The results confirm earlier findings by Dainese et al. (2004), Eskola et al. (2004), and Renk et al. (2006) that the required parton-medium interaction strength exceeds perturbative estimates, and the study enables more reliable constraints on the transport coefficient by demonstrating that the extracted $K$ factor is relatively insensitive to assumptions about energy loss before thermalization and to nuclear PDF choices, though the heavy quark data show poor compatibility with a purely radiative energy loss scenario.
