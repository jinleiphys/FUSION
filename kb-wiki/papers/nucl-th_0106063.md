# Physics of Antiproton Nuclear Interactions near Threshold

> Antiproton-nucleus optical potentials fitted to $\bar p$-atom level shifts and widths are used to calculate the recently reported very low energy ($p_{L}<100$ MeV/c) $\bar p$ cross sections for annihilation on light nuclei. The apparent suppression of annihilation upon increasing the atomic charge $Z$ and mass number $A$ is resolved as due to the strong effective repulsion produced by the very absorptive optical potential which keeps the $\bar p$-nucleus wavefunction substantially outside the nuclear surface, so that the resulting reaction cross section saturates as function of the strength of Im $V_{\rm opt}$. This feature, for $E >0$, parallels the recent prediction, for $E < 0$, that the level widths of $\bar p$ atoms saturate and, hence, that $\bar p$ deeply bound atomic states are relatively narrow. Predictions are made for $\bar p$ annihilation cross sections over the entire periodic table at these very low energies and the systematics of the calculated cross sections as function of $A$, $Z$ and $E$ are discussed and explained in terms of a Coulomb-modified strong-absorption model. Finally, optical potentials which fit simultaneously low-energy $\bar p - ^4$He observables for $E < 0$ as well as for $E > 0$ are used to assess the reliability of extracting Coulomb modified $\bar p$ nuclear scattering lengths directly from the data.

# Physics of Antiproton Nuclear Interactions near Threshold

## Key claim

The apparent suppression of antiproton annihilation on light nuclei with increasing atomic charge Z and mass number A is resolved as due to strong effective repulsion from the very absorptive optical potential, which keeps the antiproton-nucleus wavefunction substantially outside the nuclear surface, causing the reaction cross section to saturate as a function of the strength of the imaginary part of the optical potential.

## Method

Optical potentials of the `tρ` form, fitted to antiproton-atom level shifts and widths, were used to calculate total reaction (annihilation) cross sections for antiprotons on nuclei at very low momenta (p_L < 100 MeV/c). The calculations employed density-folded potentials with complex strength parameters, and the results were compared to experimental data from the OBELIX collaboration for light nuclei. A Coulomb-modified strong absorption model was developed to explain the systematics of the calculated cross sections as functions of A, Z, and energy.

## Key numbers

- For antiprotons at 57 MeV/c on ^4He, the calculated annihilation cross section using potential (a) is 901 mb, in excellent agreement with the reported value of 915 ± 39 mb.
- For antiprotons at 57 MeV/c on Ne, the experimental annihilation cross section is 2210 ± 1105 mb.
- The calculated antiproton atomic level widths in Pb saturate at about 2 MeV.
- For antiproton-nucleus scattering lengths for A > 10, the real part follows Re a_0 = (1.54 ± 0.03) A^{0.311 ± 0.005} fm, while the imaginary part is roughly constant at Im a_0 = -1.00 ± 0.04 fm across the periodic table.
- For the antiproton-helium system, the optical potential (c) yields a Coulomb-modified s-wave scattering length with Re ã_0 = 1.851 fm and Im ã_0 = -0.630 fm.
- The effective radius parameterization for A > 12 is R = (7/6)(1.840 + 1.120 A^{1/3}) fm, valid to a few percent for p_L up to 100 MeV/c.

## Context

This work extends the optical model approach previously applied to hadronic atoms by Batty, Friedman, and Gal (1997) and to antiproton atoms by Batty, Friedman, and Gal (1995), connecting the bound-state (E < 0) and scattering (E > 0) regimes across threshold. The saturation phenomenon in antiproton atomic widths was predicted by Friedman and Gal (1999). The analysis challenges the model-independent extraction of Coulomb-modified scattering lengths claimed by Protasov et al. (2000) for the antiproton-helium system, showing that p-wave contributions depend on effective range terms beyond the scattering length approximation. The results enable systematic predictions of antiproton annihilation cross sections across the periodic table and provide a framework for interpreting future low-energy antiproton-nucleus measurements.
