# An isospin dependent global nucleon-nucleus optical model at intermediate energies

> A global nucleon-nucleus optical potential for elastic scattering has been produced which replicates experimental data to high accuracy and compares well with other recently formulated potentials. The calculation that has been developed describes proton and neutron scattering from target nuclei ranging from carbon to nickel and is applicable for projectile energies from 30 to 160 MeV. With these ranges it is suitable for calculations associated with experiments performed by exotic beam accelerators. The potential is also isospin dependent and has both real and imaginary isovector asymmetry terms to better describe the dynamics of chains of isotopes and mirror nuclei. An analysis of the validity and strength of the asymmetry term is included with connections established to other optical potentials and charge-exchange reaction data. An on-line observable calculator is available for this optical potential.

## Key claim

The paper presents a global nucleon-nucleus optical potential (WP OMP) that reproduces experimental elastic scattering data with high accuracy for targets from carbon to nickel at projectile energies from 30 to 160 MeV, but the authors acknowledge that the potential uses the highest number of adjustable parameters among three contemporary global potentials and that the fitting procedure required discarding the highest 5% of chi-squared values after each iteration to approach a global minimum.

## Method

The authors constructed a phenomenological optical model potential using Woods-Saxon form factors with volume, surface, and spin-orbit terms, parameterized by 23 distinct parameters expressed as separable polynomials in target mass number A and projectile energy E. They performed a weighted chi-squared minimization on over 300 nucleon-nucleus experimental datasets using a Monte Carlo preliminary search with a Sobol number generator followed by steepest-descent local minimization, fitting parameters in three sequential steps: first on N=Z targets and mixed-isotope sets, then on datasets with non-zero N-Z dependence, and finally on magic-number terms. The fitting code solved the non-relativistic Schrodinger equation in a Coulomb basis with a relativistic correction, running on a multi-processor parallel system where each processor handled one nucleon-nucleus experimental dataset at fixed energy and nucleon number.

## Key numbers

- Projectile energy range: 30 MeV to 160 MeV
- Target mass number range: 12 ≤ A ≤ 60 (carbon to nickel)
- 23 distinct parameters used to describe 13 Woods-Saxon potential terms
- Over 300 different nucleon-nucleus experiments used in the fitting
- 115 data sets of fixed E and A used for the first fitting step (non-asymmetric parameters)
- 90 experiment data sets with N-Z dependence used for the second fitting step
- 40 data sets containing at least one magic nuclei experiment used for the third fitting step
- Monte Carlo preliminary search included up to 3 × 10^5 vectors in the parameter space
- Differential cross sections favored over polarizations by a factor of 1.5 in the weighting
- Forward angle weighting: the 50th forward angle point weighted approximately one seventh that of the extreme forward point

## Context

This work extends the tradition of global optical model potentials established by Beccetti and Greenlees (1969) and more recently by Koning and Delaroche (KD OMP) and Madland (MD OMP), with the KD OMP covering 1 keV to 200 MeV and setting an accuracy benchmark. The WP OMP is specifically designed for the next generation of radioactive beam accelerators, targeting lighter nuclei far from stability, and includes both real and imaginary isovector asymmetry terms that the other two contemporary potentials lack. The potential enables consistent multichannel calculations across chains of isotopes and mirror nuclei, and the authors provide an online optical potential calculator to facilitate its use by the research community.
