# Time evolution of gluon coherent state and its von Neumann entropy in heavy-ion collisions

> We propose a new prescription for evaluating a von Neumann entropy in the initial stage of high-energy heavy-ion collisions utilizing the time evolution of classical Yang-Mills (CYM) field: The von Neumann entropy is computed for the quantum coherent states constructed so as to give the classical gluon fields as the expectation values. The entropy is to be liberated when the complete decoherence is achieved. As a demonstration, the time evolution of the CYM dynamics is solved with an initial condition which mimics the Glasma state, though in a non-expanding geometry; the Glasma state is characterized by the longitudinal color-electric and -magnetic fields with gluon fields' fluctuations around it. We find that the initial longitudinal fluctuations of the fields play essential roles for the entropy production in two ways: First, the field fluctuations at $t=0$ themselves act as a source of the von Neumann entropy prepared before the time evolution. Second, the initial fluctuations triggers field instabilities, and hence the larger the strength of them, the more the entropy production at later time.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the von Neumann entropy in the initial stage of heavy-ion collisions can be computed from classical Yang-Mills dynamics by constructing quantum coherent states that reproduce the classical gluon fields as expectation values, but only after assuming that complete decoherence occurs at some time, which gives an upper limit on the entropy produced through decoherence rather than a direct calculation of entropy from the unitary evolution alone.

## Method

The authors solve classical Yang-Mills equations on a lattice in the non-compact (A, E) formalism for SU(2) gauge theory, using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method. They construct quantum coherent states from the classical gauge and electric fields in momentum space, then compute the von Neumann entropy by assuming complete decoherence at a given time, which diagonalizes the density matrix in the Fock state basis. The initial conditions mimic the Glasma state in a non-expanding geometry, with longitudinal fluctuations added to the color-electric and magnetic fields.

## Key numbers

- With zero longitudinal fluctuations (Δ=0), the entropy density S_dec/Volume remains approximately constant at 2.3 for the calculation time up to g^2 μ t < 100
- With Δ=5.0×10^{-4}, the entropy density remains nearly identical to the Δ=0 case for g^2 μ t < 18, then increases and saturates around g^2 μ t = 80
- For Δ=5.0×10^{-3}, the squared fluctuation-background electric field ratio is 8.194×10^{-4} and the initial energy density is 0.1712 (g^2 μ)^4
- For Δ=1.0×10^{-1}, the squared fluctuation-background electric field ratio is 3.278×10^{-1} and the initial energy density is 0.21 (g^2 μ)^4
- The entropy with finite Δ is approximately 16 times larger than that with Δ=0, on a 20^3 lattice where N_z = 20
- With Δ=0.1, the squared electric fields in perpendicular and longitudinal directions nearly coincide after g^2 μ t ≈ 35, indicating isotropization
- With Δ=0.05, isotropization occurs at g^2 μ t ≈ 40
- The physical scale is estimated as g^2 μ ≈ 0.32 GeV ≈ (0.63 fm)^{-1}, obtained by matching the transverse lattice area to that of an Au nucleus with radius R_Au ≈ 7 fm

## Context

This work extends previous studies of Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy in classical Yang-Mills dynamics by Kunihiro and Iida, where positive Lyapunov exponents indicated entropy production from chaotic behavior. The authors build on the Glasma model from McLerran and Venugopalan (1994) and earlier estimates of decoherence time by Muller and collaborators, who argued that τ_dec ∼ Q_s^{-1} and found produced entropy roughly one-third of that measured in final hadron distributions. The new method enables direct computation of von Neumann entropy from classical dynamics, and the results suggest that initial longitudinal fluctuations are crucial for entropy production, potentially explaining the early thermalization observed in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC.
