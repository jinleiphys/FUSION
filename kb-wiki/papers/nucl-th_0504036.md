# Dynamical aspects of isoscaling

> The origin and dynamical evolution of isoscaling was studied using classical molecular dynamics simulations of ${}^{40}$Ca + ${}^{40}$Ca, ${}^{48}$Ca + ${}^{48}$Ca, and ${}^{52}$Ca + ${}^{52}$Ca, at beam energies ranging from $20 \ MeV/A$ to $85 MeV/A$. The analysis included a study of the time evolution of this effect. Isoscaling was observed to exist in these reactions from the very early primary isotope distributions (produced by highly {\it non-equilibrated} systems) all the way to asymptotic times. This indicates that isoscaling is independent of quantum effects and thermodynamical equilibrium. In summary, collision-produced isoscaling appears to be due more to the mere partitioning of the proton-neutron content of the participant nuclei, than to specific details of the reaction dynamics.

## Key claim

Isoscaling is observed in classical molecular dynamics simulations of Ca+Ca collisions from the earliest non-equilibrated stages through asymptotic times, indicating the effect is independent of quantum effects and thermodynamic equilibrium, and arises primarily from the partitioning of proton-neutron content rather than specific reaction dynamics.

## Method

The authors performed classical molecular dynamics (MD) simulations using the "Latino" code with a two-body Yukawa potential (Coulomb plus nuclear) for collisions of ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca, ⁴⁸Ca+⁴⁸Ca, and ⁵²Ca+⁵²Ca at beam energies from 20 to 85 MeV/A. Fragment recognition was done using the Minimum Spanning Tree (MSE) cluster-detection algorithm, and yield matrices Y(N,Z) were constructed at multiple times from impact to 5000 fm/c. The ratio R₂₁(N,Z) = Y₂(N,Z)/Y₁(N,Z) was fitted to the exponential form exp(αN + βZ) using least squares for each reaction pair and energy.

## Key numbers

- Beam energies studied: 20, 25, 35, 45, 65, and 85 MeV/A for each of the three Ca+Ca systems
- Asymptotic time for analysis: 5000 fm/c; early-time analysis at 125 fm/c
- For the ⁴⁰Ca-⁴⁸Ca comparison at 35 MeV/A and 1250 fm/c: α = 1.07, β = 1.22 (matching AMD-V results of α = 1.03, β = 1.22)
- Maximum temperatures of the largest fragment: between 2.5 and 3.5 MeV, with average temperatures differing by up to 30% between the two reactions at early times
- 2000 collisions performed per energy per system

## Context

This work extends the study of isoscaling beyond equilibrium statistical models (e.g., microcanonical, canonical, grand canonical ensembles used by Tsang (2001) and Das) by using a fully dynamical classical MD approach that includes non-equilibrium dynamics. The results agree with quantal AMD-V simulations by Ono (2004) but demonstrate that quantum effects (such as β-decay or the Pauli principle) are not necessary for isoscaling. The findings challenge the interpretation linking α and β to differences in chemical potentials and a common temperature, as proposed by Xu and Tsang, and point toward purely geometrical and sampling factors as the origin, to be explored in a follow-up study by Davila.
