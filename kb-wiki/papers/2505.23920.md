# AI-Assisted analysis of $^{28}$Si$^*$ $\rightarrow$ 7$α$ break-up data

> Mid-weight $α$-conjugate nuclei are predicted to possess exotic toroid-like resonances with high angular momenta. The search for these states in $^{28}$Si$^*$ is the main point of two published experimental investigations of the peripheral $^{28}$Si + $^{12}$C reaction by Cao and collaborators and by Hannaman and collaborators. In this work, we develop a novel Artificial Intelligence (AI)-based machine learning method utilizing the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) to analyze available experimental and theoretical data. We additionally study the reaction with the Hybrid $α$-Cluster (H$α$C) model. In all the examined data our results suggest the presence of underlying structure which is close to that predicted for toroidal states.

# AI-Assisted analysis of $^{28}$Si$^*$ $\rightarrow$ 7$\alpha$ break-up data

## Key claim
The Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) machine learning method reveals underlying structure in both experimental and theoretical $^{28}$Si$^*$ $\rightarrow$ 7$\alpha$ break-up data that is close to that predicted for toroidal states, but the method's accuracy is limited in regions of low statistics and for peaks with small mixing coefficients. The authors do not claim definitive detection of toroidal states, only that the data "suggest the presence of underlying structure" consistent with such predictions.

## Method
The authors developed an unsupervised machine learning method using the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) from the Scikit-learn Python library to decompose excitation energy spectra into a linear combination of Gaussian components. They first trained the method on known Hybrid alpha-Cluster (HalphaC) model results for rotating $^{28}$Si, then applied it to experimental data from two previous experiments (Cao et al. and Hannaman et al.) and to new collision HalphaC (cHalphaC) model calculations of the $^{28}$Si + $^{12}$C reaction at 35 MeV/A beam energy. The optimal number of Gaussians was determined by requiring the reduced chi-square to be close to 1, and the statistical significance was tested by dividing the Hannaman data into eight time-ordered partitions and analyzing each separately.

## Key numbers
- Beam energy: 35 MeV/A for the $^{28}$Si + $^{12}$C reaction in inverse kinematics (294 MeV in the center-of-mass frame)
- Maximum initial angular momentum for 7$\alpha$ production in cHalphaC calculations: 48$\hbar$
- Number of events in cHalphaC calculations: 40,400,000 events with $0 \le l \le 50$
- Bin width for excitation energy spectra: 2 MeV (cHalphaC) and 1.25 MeV (Hannaman data)
- Number of random points generated for GMM: $N_p = 10^6$
- Excitation energy range of interest for toroidal states: $E^* \sim 60-150$ MeV
- Cao et al. reported possible toroid resonances at 114, 126, and 138 MeV
- GMM analysis of Cao data yielded six peaks, approximately reproducing the 114 MeV and 138 MeV resonances; the 126 MeV peak was not reproduced independently but fell within a wide fourth Gaussian
- GMM analysis of Hannaman data also yielded six peaks, with resonances close to those found by Cao et al.
- Variation of excitation energies across time-ordered partitions: approximately 2.6%
- Lowest toroidal state predicted in Ren (2020): approximately 72 MeV (0$\hbar$ state)
- Electrostatic energy between fragments (E_C) found to be less than 1 MeV in total
- Q-value for 7$\alpha$ break-up calculated within 2% error of the HalphaC model

## Context
This work extends the search for exotic toroid-like resonances in $^{28}$Si$^*$, which were first predicted theoretically by Wong (1972, 1973, 1978) and later by Stasczak and Wong (2014). It directly addresses the conflicting conclusions from two experimental investigations: Cao et al. reported possible toroid resonances at 114, 126, and 138 MeV using the NIMROD-ISiS detector system, while Hannaman et al. concluded there were no statistically significant peaks using the FAUST detector array. The authors argue that the polynomial fit method used by Hannaman et al. has drawbacks, including the ability to fit any peak with enough polynomial terms and the introduction of artificial structure from derivative roots. The new GMM-based method enables extraction of underlying structure without these limitations, and the results suggest that both experimental datasets contain consistent features near the predicted toroidal state energies. The cHalphaC model calculations provide an upper limit to experimental cross sections, as many non-alpha-conjugate exit channels are not included in the model.
