# Complex fission phenomena

> Complex fission phenomena are studied in a unified way. Very general reflection asymmetrical equilibrium (saddle point) nuclear shapes are obtained by solving an integro-differential equation without being necessary to specify a certain parametrization. The mass asymmetry in binary cold fission of Th and U isotopes is explained as the result of adding a phenomenological shell correction to the liquid drop model deformation energy. Applications to binary, ternary, and quaternary fission are outlined.

## Key claim

The paper claims that reflection-asymmetric saddle point shapes can be obtained as solutions of an integro-differential equation without imposing a specific shape parametrization a priori, and that adding a phenomenological shell correction to the liquid drop model deformation energy produces minima in the saddle point energy at nonzero mass asymmetry, thereby explaining mass asymmetry in binary cold fission of Th and U isotopes. The authors acknowledge that their calculated heavy fragment mass numbers (around 125) disagree with experimental values (134–140) by 6.7–10.7%, attributing this to the simplicity of the phenomenological shell correction.

## Method

The authors derived an Euler-Lagrange equation from the variational problem of minimizing the liquid drop model (LDM) deformation energy (surface plus Coulomb terms) under constraints of constant volume and a given deformation parameter, obtaining an integro-differential equation for the axially symmetric nuclear surface shape ρ(z). They solved this equation numerically using an iterative Runge-Kutta method, first for reflection-symmetric shapes within pure LDM, then for reflection-asymmetric shapes by introducing a mass-asymmetry parameter η and adding a phenomenological shell correction adapted from Myers and Swiatecki (1966). The shell correction was computed from the volumes of the left and right fragments at each deformation, using the fragment proton and neutron numbers to evaluate a shell correction energy that depends on the distance from magic numbers.

## Key numbers

- For binary fission of a nucleus with fissility X = 0.6 (e.g., ¹⁷⁰Yb), the saddle point occurs at d_L = 1.91, corresponding to deformation α = 2.304.
- For nuclei with X = 0.60, 0.70, and 0.82 (¹⁷⁰Yb, ²⁰⁴Pb, and ²⁵²Cf), the saddle point elongations α are 2.304, 1.822, and 1.165, respectively.
- For binary cold fission of ²²⁶–²³⁸Th and ²³⁰–²³⁸U, the minima of saddle point energy occur at mass asymmetry parameters d_L − d_R in the range 0.04–0.08, corresponding to η ≈ 0.050–0.095 and heavy fragment mass A₁ ≈ 125.
- For ternary fission shapes of ¹⁷⁰Yb (X = 0.60) with three fragments and two necks, increasing d_L = d_R from 2.25 to 7.00 changes the deformation α from 1.650 to 2.730 and the energy in units of E_s⁰ from 0.165 to 0.134; the configuration with E/E_s⁰ = 0.134 approaches true ternary fission into ⁵⁶V + ⁵⁶V + ⁵⁸Cr with Q = 83.639 MeV.
- For quaternary fission shapes of ¹⁷⁰Yb with four fragments and three necks, increasing d_L = d_R from 2.30 to 4.00 changes α from 2.144 to 3.233 and E/E_s⁰ from 0.188 to 0.214; the configuration with E/E_s⁰ = 0.214 approaches fission into ⁴²Cl + ⁴²Cl + ⁴³Ar + ⁴³Ar.
- The linear approximation for the Q-value of fission into n equal fragments gives Q_n/E_s⁰ ≈ 1 − n^{1/3} + 2X(1 − n^{−2/3}); tripartition becomes exothermic at X ≥ 0.426, and Q₃ exceeds Q₂ at X ≥ 0.611.

## Context

This work extends the unified approach to cold binary fission, cluster radioactivity, and alpha decay developed by Poenaru et al. (1995, 1996) to include ternary and quaternary (multicluster) fission, and it builds on the liquid drop model saddle point shapes of Cohen and Swiatecki (1963) by solving for shapes without a fixed parametrization. The method reproduces the well-known LDM saddle point shapes for symmetric fission and, with the addition of shell corrections, qualitatively accounts for mass asymmetry in fission, as previously discussed by Wilkins et al. (1976). The authors suggest that using a more realistic shell correction, such as from the two-center shell model of Raduta et al. (2003), could improve agreement with experimental mass distributions, and they note that the predicted enhanced yield of two-alpha accompanied fission was experimentally confirmed by Gönnenwein et al. (2002, 2003).
