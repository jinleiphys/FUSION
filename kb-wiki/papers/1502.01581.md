# Non-extensive statistics effects in transverse momentum spectra of hadrons

> The Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac statistics of the identified hadrons were verified on the basis of the transverse momentum distributions of bosons and fermions created in the $pp$ collisions at high energies using the Tsallis-factorized statistics and the two-component distributions of the Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics. The main differences between the parameters of the Tsallis-factorized statistics and the Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics were identified. The results of the Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics are reasonable and suggest that the soft and hard hadrons may be produced from the two different macroscopic states of the dynamical system. It was revealed that the volume of the system obtained in the Tsallis-factorized statistics is unusually large in comparison with the geometrical volume of two protons. The main formulas for the Tsallis statistics in the grand canonical ensemble were formulated.

# Non-extensive statistics effects in transverse momentum spectra of hadrons

## Key claim
The Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac statistics of identified hadrons produced in pp collisions at LHC energies are verified using both Tsallis-factorized statistics and two-component Boltzmann-Gibbs distributions, but the Tsallis-factorized statistics yields an unusually large system volume compared to the geometrical volume of two protons, while the two-component Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics suggests that soft and hard hadrons originate from two different macroscopic states of the dynamical system.

## Method
The author fitted experimental transverse momentum spectra of identified hadrons (pions, kaons, protons, antiprotons, K_S^0, Lambda, anti-Lambda, phi, Xi- + anti-Xi+) from ALICE Collaboration data at sqrt(s) = 0.9 TeV using two approaches: (1) the Tsallis-factorized statistics with Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac distributions, and (2) two-component Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac distributions from ordinary Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics. The fits were performed at rapidity y = 0, and the extracted parameters (temperature T, radius R, and Tsallis parameter q) were compared across hadron types and between the two statistical frameworks.

## Key numbers
- Mean Tsallis-factorized temperature: T = 70.65 ± 4.27 MeV
- Mean Tsallis-factorized radius: R = 4.871 ± 1.151 fm
- Mean Tsallis-factorized parameter: q = 1.138 ± 0.003
- Mean first-component Boltzmann-Gibbs temperature: T1 = 148.38 ± 4.59 MeV
- Mean first-component Boltzmann-Gibbs radius: R1 = 1.799 ± 0.091 fm
- Mean second-component Boltzmann-Gibbs temperature: T2 = 332.25 ± 12.15 MeV
- Mean second-component Boltzmann-Gibbs radius: R2 = 0.419 ± 0.016 fm
- For pions at sqrt(s) = 0.9 TeV: Tsallis-factorized temperature T = 75.94 ± 0.57 MeV (pi+), R = 4.397 ± 0.032 fm, q = 1.142 ± 0.001
- For protons at sqrt(s) = 0.9 TeV: Tsallis-factorized temperature T = 33.44 ± 13.2 MeV, R = 16.365 ± 12.103 fm, q = 1.147 ± 0.009

## Context
This work extends previous analyses of transverse momentum spectra using Tsallis-like distributions (Rybczynski 2014, Cleymans 2013, Azmi 2014) and two-source Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics (Liu 2014, Li 2014), which had primarily used classical Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions rather than quantum Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac statistics. The paper enables a direct comparison between the Tsallis-factorized approach and a two-component equilibrium picture, suggesting that the two-component Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics provides physically reasonable volumes (R1 consistent with two-proton geometry) while the Tsallis-factorized statistics yields nonphysical volumes. The failure of the two-component Boltzmann-Gibbs fit for high-pT pions indicates that additional production mechanisms may be needed for pions.
