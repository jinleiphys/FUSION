# Probing the Structure of Nucleons in the Resonance Region

> Status, open questions, and future prospects of the physics of excited nucleons are discussed. Emphasis is on the study of the structure of nucleons via measurements of their electromagnetic transition form factors, the search for "missing" resonances, the spin structure of the nucleon in the resonance region, and connections between the resonance and the deep-inelastic regimes.

# Probing the Structure of Nucleons in the Resonance Region

## Key claim
The paper argues that studying nucleon structure through electromagnetic transition form factors in the resonance region can bridge the gap between the perturbative QCD regime at short distances and the hadronic regime at large distances, but this connection remains poorly established and requires both high-quality experimental data and significant theoretical development to achieve a unified description.

## Method
The author reviews the status of experimental programs at Jefferson Lab (JLab), particularly with the CLAS detector, which measures exclusive meson electroproduction channels (such as single pion, Δ++π−, pω, pη, pη′, and KΛ/KΣ) across a large kinematic range in momentum transfer Q² and invariant mass W. The paper discusses how these measurements of transition form factors, polarization observables, and inclusive scattering data are used to test quark model predictions, search for "missing" resonances, and connect the resonance region to deep inelastic scattering via sum rules and quark-hadron duality.

## Key numbers
- The ratio E₁₊/M₁₊ for the Δ(1232) transition was measured at Q² = 4 GeV², yielding E₁₊/M₁₊ ≈ −0.02 (Hall C experiment at JLab).
- CLAS collected approximately 3 × 10⁵ pη′ events in photoproduction.
- The Bjorken integral Γ₁^{pn} at finite Q² is related to the Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule at Q² = 0, with the slope given by I^{pn}_{GDH}(Q² → 0) = 2(M²/Q²) Γ₁^{pn}(Q² → 0).
- The generalized GDH integral is being measured at JLab in the low Q² range Q² = 0.1 – 2.0 GeV².
- Chiral perturbation theory may extend the GDH sum rule to Q² ≈ 0.2 GeV², while higher twist expansions may be valid down to Q² = 0.5 GeV², leaving a gap from Q² = 0.2 – 0.5 GeV² to be bridged.

## Context
This paper reviews the experimental program at Jefferson Lab that extends earlier work on nucleon resonance structure from DESY and SLAC (such as the E143 experiment) and addresses open questions raised by quark model predictions from Isgur (1980s) and others. The work enables tests of the single-quark transition model (Hey, 1970s), searches for hybrid baryons and missing quark model states, and connects to the Bjorken sum rule and Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule framework. The paper also builds on the observation of quark-hadron duality by Bloom and Gilman (1970s), which was reexamined by the Keppel experiment at JLab.
