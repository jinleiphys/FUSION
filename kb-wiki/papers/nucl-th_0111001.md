# Boundary of Nuclear Physics and QCD

> Recent progress in lattice QCD, combined with the imminent advent of a new generation of dedicated supercomputers and advances in chiral extrapolation mean that the next few years will bring quite novel insights into hadron structure. We review some of the recent highlights in this field, the questions which might be addressed and the experiments which may be expected to stretch that understanding to its limits. Only with a sound understanding of hadron structure can one hope to explore the fundamental issue of how that structure may change at finite density (or temperature). We explore potential future insights from lattice QCD into the phenomenon of nuclear saturation and a very important hint from recent data of a change in the structure of a bound nucleon.

## Key claim

Recent progress in lattice QCD, combined with advances in chiral extrapolation, will soon yield novel insights into hadron structure, but the most sophisticated lattice calculations with dynamical fermions are only just becoming feasible at pion masses around 400-500 MeV, requiring controlled extrapolation to the physical pion mass of 140 MeV.

## Method

The paper reviews lattice QCD simulations of hadron properties (masses, magnetic moments, charge radii, and moments of parton distribution functions) performed at quark masses typically above 60 MeV, then applies chiral perturbation theory to extrapolate these results to the physical quark mass. The extrapolation uses functional forms that incorporate the correct leading non-analytic (LNA) behavior from Goldstone boson loops, such as terms proportional to m_pi^3 for the nucleon mass and m_pi^2 log m_pi for moments of structure functions, with a cutoff parameter that reflects the finite size of the pion source.

## Key numbers

- String tension: sigma = (440 MeV)^2, corresponding to a confining force of order 10 tonnes between objects less than 10^-18 m in size.
- Vacuum energy density of pure gluonic QCD: epsilon_vac = -0.5 GeV/fm^3.
- Lattice QCD shows the gluon propagator is non-perturbative for q^2 less than 4 GeV^2.
- For a current quark mass of order 110 MeV, the quark mass function M(0) is approximately 400 MeV, decreasing to around 300 MeV in the chiral limit.
- Lattice simulations suggest the lowest mass glueball (0++) has a mass of 1611 +/- 30 +/- 160 MeV.
- The leading non-analytic contribution to the nucleon mass from pion loops is sigma_NN^LNA = -5.6 m_pi^3; at the physical pion mass this is -17 MeV, but at m_pi = 420 MeV it is -460 MeV.
- The sigma commutator of the nucleon extracted from chiral extrapolation of lattice data lies in the range 45-55 MeV.
- Extrapolated values of proton and neutron magnetic moments from non-perturbative QCD are 2.85 +/- 0.22 mu_N and -1.90 +/- 0.15 mu_N respectively.
- Linear extrapolation of lattice data for u-d moments of structure functions overestimates experimental values by about 50 percent.

## Context

This review extends the line of work connecting lattice QCD to hadron structure through chiral perturbation theory, building on the framework of Brown and Rho (1990s) regarding in-medium changes of hadron properties and on earlier lattice calculations by groups such as CP-PACS, UKQCD, QCDSF, and MIT. The paper enables future calculations of accurate hadronic properties at the physical quark mass within a few years using next-generation supercomputers with speeds exceeding 10 tera-flops, and it identifies the need for lattice data at pion masses of 200-300 MeV to constrain chiral extrapolation parameters. The work also highlights the importance of experimental programs at JLab, Mainz, and MIT-Bates for testing these predictions, and it points to the recent determination of G_E/G_M for a proton bound in He-4 as the first firm evidence for a change in the structure of a bound nucleon.
