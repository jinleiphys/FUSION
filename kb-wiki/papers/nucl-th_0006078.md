# The Liquid-Gas Phase Transitions in a Multicomponent Nuclear System with Coulomb and Surface Effects

> The liquid-gas phase transition is studied in a multi-component nuclear system using a local Skyrme interaction with Coulomb and surface effects. Some features are qualitatively the same as the results of Muller and Serot which uses relativistic mean field without Coulomb and surface effects. Surface tension brings the coexistance binodal surface to lower pressure. The Coulomb interaction makes the binodal surface smaller and cause another pair of binodal points at low pressure and large proton fraction with less protons in liquid phase and more protons in gas phase.

## Key claim

The liquid-gas phase transition in a multi-component nuclear system is studied using a local Skyrme interaction with Coulomb and surface effects, which are neglected in the relativistic mean field approach of Müller and Serot. The authors find that surface tension lowers the coexistence binodal surface to lower pressure, while the Coulomb interaction makes the binodal surface smaller and introduces an additional pair of binodal points at low pressure and large proton fraction, where the liquid phase has fewer protons and the gas phase has more protons.

## Method

The authors use a nonrelativistic zero-range Skyrme interaction to derive the potential energy density for a two-component system of protons and neutrons, including Coulomb and surface terms approximated for a uniform spherical finite nucleus. They compute the pressure, chemical potentials, energy density, and entropy as functions of density, temperature, and proton fraction using Fermi integrals in the nearly non-degenerate (classical ideal gas) limit, and then determine phase coexistence by requiring equal pressure and equal chemical potentials for each component between phases.

## Key numbers

- The Coulomb term is parameterized as \(C \rho^\beta \rho_p^2\) with \(\frac{4\pi}{5} e^2 R^2\) and the surface term as \(C_s \rho^\eta\) with \(\frac{4\pi r_0^2 \sigma}{V^{1/3}} \rho^{2/3}\), where \(R = r_0 A^{1/3}\) and the total charge is \(Z\) (from \(U_C = \frac{3}{5}\frac{e^2 Z^2}{R V}\)).
- For a symmetric nucleus (\(N=Z\), \(\rho_q = \rho/2\)), the potential energy density reduces to \(U(\rho) = \frac{3}{8} t_0 \rho^2 + \frac{3}{48} t_3 \rho^{\alpha+2} + C \rho^\beta \rho_p^2 + C_s \rho^\eta\).
- The pressure for a symmetric system (\(y=1/2\)) at fixed \(T\) is given by \(P = T\rho + \frac{3}{8} t_0 \rho^2 + \frac{(\alpha+1)}{16} t_3 \rho^{\alpha+2} + \frac{T}{2\sqrt{2}} \left(\frac{\lambda^3}{\gamma}\right) \left(\frac{\rho}{2}\right)^2 + \frac{(\beta+1)}{4} C \rho^{\beta+2} + (\eta - 1) C_s \rho^\eta\).
- The critical point conditions are \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial\rho_q} = \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial\rho_q^2} = 0\) and \(\frac{\partial\mu_q}{\partial\rho_q} = \frac{\partial^2\mu_q}{\partial\rho_q^2} = 0\).

## Context

This work extends the initial study of the liquid-gas phase transition using a Skyrme interaction (Mekjian and Lee, earlier work) and parallels the detailed study by Müller and Serot (1995) using relativistic mean field theory without Coulomb or surface effects. The inclusion of Coulomb and surface terms introduces new features not present in Müller and Serot's results, such as a back-bending in the proton fraction as a function of density and an additional pair of binodal points, which are relevant for understanding phase transitions in finite nuclei and in astrophysical contexts such as neutron stars.
