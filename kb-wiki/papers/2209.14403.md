# A novel Machine-Learning method for spin classification of neutron resonances

> The performance of nuclear reactors and other nuclear systems depends on a precise understanding of the neutron interaction cross sections for materials used in these systems. These cross sections exhibit resonant structure whose shape is determined in part by the angular momentum quantum numbers of the resonances. The correct assignment of the quantum numbers of neutron resonances is, therefore, paramount. In this project, we apply machine learning to automate the quantum number assignments using only the resonances' energies and widths and not relying on detailed transmission or capture measurements. The classifier used for quantum number assignment is trained using stochastically generated resonance sequences whose distributions mimic those of real data. We explore the use of several physics-motivated features for training our classifier. These features amount to out-of-distribution tests of a given resonance's widths and resonance-pair spacings. We pay special attention to situations where either capture widths cannot be trusted for classification purposes or where there is insufficient information to classify resonances by the total spin $J$. We demonstrate the efficacy of our classification approach using simulated and actual $^{52}$Cr resonance data.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that machine-learning classifiers trained on stochastically generated resonance sequences can automate spin and orbital angular momentum assignments for neutron resonances using only resonance energies and widths, without requiring detailed transmission or capture measurements; however, the method's efficacy is demonstrated specifically for simulated and actual $^{52}$Cr resonance data, and the authors note that classification by total angular momentum $J$ may not be feasible when there is insufficient information.

## Method
The authors recast the spin classification problem as a supervised machine-learning task using classifiers from the scikit-learn Python module. They train the classifiers on stochastically generated resonance sequences whose statistical properties (nearest neighbor spacing distributions, spacing-spacing correlations, and Porter-Thomas width distributions) mimic those of real neutron resonances, using physics-motivated features that amount to out-of-distribution tests of a given resonance's widths and resonance-pair spacings. The trained classifiers are then applied to assign either the orbital angular momentum $L$ alone or the full spingroup quantum numbers ($L$, $J$) to each resonance in a sequence.

## Key numbers
- The mean spacing-spacing correlation for resonances within a spingroup is $\overline{\rho} \approx -0.27$, indicating an average anticorrelation between adjacent spacings.
- For medium mass nuclei, the average level spacing $\overline{D} \sim$ keV, providing enough resonances for robust classification by $L$ and potential classification by $J$.
- For heavy nuclei, $\overline{D} \sim$ eV, representing the ideal situation for the classification code.
- The Wigner surmise distribution for nearest neighbor spacings is given by $P_w(x) = (\pi x/2) \exp(-\pi x^2/4)$ where $x = D/\overline{D}$.
- The Porter-Thomas distribution of resonance widths is a $\chi^2$ distribution with $\nu$ degrees of freedom, where for elastic scattering $\nu_{\mathrm{el}} = 1$ and for capture $\nu_{\gamma}$ is assumed very large ($\nu_{\gamma} \rightarrow \infty$).

## Context
This work extends earlier information-theoretic approaches to resonance spin classification, including the suggestion by Georgopulos (1981) to use random matrix theory metrics for determining missing level fractions, the probabilistic assignment method described by Frohner (2000), and the work of Mitchell et al. (2004) on width distribution analysis. The method also builds on the series of papers by Mulhall et al. (2007-2011) examining the $\Delta_3$ statistic for spin sequence purity, and the reports by Mitchell and Shriner (2009, 2011) estimating missing or misclassified resonances using RMT-inspired metrics. The approach enables automated, reproducible spin classification that could replace the current subjective, visual inspection method used by resonance evaluators, potentially reducing unquantified uncertainty in nuclear data evaluations.
