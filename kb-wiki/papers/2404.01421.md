# Transport Coefficients of relativistic matter: A detailed formalism with a gross knowledge of their magnitude

> The present review article has attempted a compact formalism description of transport coefficient calculations for relativistic fluid, which is expected in heavy ion collision experiments. Here, we first address the macroscopic description of relativistic fluid dynamics and then its microscopic description based on the kinetic theory framework. We also address different relaxation time approximation-based models in Boltzmann transport equations, which make a sandwich between Macro and Micro frameworks of relativistic fluid dynamics and finally provide different microscopic expressions of transport coefficients like the fluid's shear viscosity and bulk viscosity. In the numeric part of this review article, we put stress on the two gross components of transport coefficient expressions: relaxation time and thermodynamic phase-space part. Then, we try to tune the relaxation time component to cover earlier theoretical estimations and experimental data-driven estimations for RHIC and LHC matter. By this way of numerical understanding, we provide the final comments on the values of transport coefficients and relaxation time in the context of the (nearly) perfect fluid nature of the RHIC or LHC matter.

## Key claim

This review argues that the magnitude of transport coefficients such as shear viscosity and bulk viscosity for relativistic matter produced in heavy ion collisions can be understood by separating the calculation into two gross components, relaxation time and thermodynamic phase-space part, and that tuning the relaxation time component alone can cover both earlier theoretical estimations and experimental data-driven estimations for RHIC and LHC matter, though the authors provide only "final comments" rather than a single definitive numerical prediction.

## Method

The authors present a compact formalism of relativistic fluid dynamics starting from macroscopic conservation laws and then derive microscopic expressions for transport coefficients using the Boltzmann transport equation with relaxation time approximation (RTA) models, specifically the Anderson–Witting, Marle, and BGK models. They separate the transport coefficient expressions into a relaxation time component and a thermodynamic phase-space component, then numerically tune the relaxation time to match existing theoretical calculations and experimental data from RHIC and LHC for shear viscosity to entropy density, bulk viscosity to entropy density, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity.

## Key numbers

- The KSS bound for shear viscosity to entropy density is given as \(1/(4\pi) \approx 0.08\), described as a possible lower bound for a wide class of systems.
- The medium formed in heavy ion collisions lasts for approximately \(10^{-23}\) s (about 1 fm), with a temperature around \(10^{12}\) K (about 100 MeV).
- The authors state that RHIC experiments created QGP with a value of \(\eta/s\) nearly equal to the lower bound of 0.08.
- The review reports that a cold fluid of lithium atoms at Duke University in 2002 had \(\eta/s < 0.5\), close to its lower bound.
- The authors provide a range of relaxation time values in the QGP temperature range and a range of radius for a hard sphere scattering model in the hadronic temperature domain such that the Anderson–Witting model can cover existing theoretical calculations, but no specific numerical values for these ranges are given in the truncated text.

## Context

This review extends the long line of work on relativistic fluid dynamics and transport coefficients that began with Eckart (1940) and Landau–Lifshitz (1959), and later developed through the Israel–Stewart transient theory (1976, 1979) and the causal first-order hydrodynamics formulations by Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha, and Kovtun (2017–2022). It builds on the relaxation time approximation models of Bhatnagar–Gross–Krook (1954), Marle (1969), and Anderson–Witting (1974), and aims to provide a pedagogical entry point for beginning-level researchers. The review enables future work by identifying three possible sources for the low \(\eta/s\) of RHIC/LHC matter, resonance-type interactions, finite-size effects, and magnetic field effects, and by providing a framework to compare different model calculations of transport coefficients.
