# Symmetry energy: nuclear masses and neutron stars

> We describe the main features of our most recent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov nuclear mass models, based on 16-parameter generalized Skyrme forces. They have been fitted to the data of the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation, and favour a value of 30 MeV for the symmetry coefficient J, the corresponding root-mean square deviation being 0.549 MeV. We find that this conclusion is compatible with measurements of neutron-skin thickness. By constraining the underlying interactions to fit various equations of state of neutron matter calculated {\it ab initio} our models are well adapted to a realistic and unified treatment of all regions of neutron stars. We use our models to calculate the composition, the equation of state, the mass-radius relation and the maximum mass. Comparison with observations of neutron stars again favours a value of J = 30 MeV.

## Key claim

The authors' Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov mass models fitted to the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation favor a symmetry coefficient J = 30 MeV, but this conclusion comes with the qualification that J = 29 MeV also performs well globally while J = 31 MeV is less favored and J = 32 MeV is ruled out; the preference for J = 30 MeV is further supported by neutron-skin measurements and neutron-star observations, though the authors express some caution regarding the J = 29 MeV model.

## Method

The authors constructed 16-parameter generalized Skyrme forces and used the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method to fit nuclear masses from the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation, simultaneously constraining the forces to fit ab initio equations of state of pure neutron matter (FP, APR, or LS2). They generated five new parameter sets (BSk22 to BSk26) with different imposed values of the symmetry coefficient J (29, 30, 31, and 32 MeV) and different high-density constraints, then applied these models to calculate the composition, equation of state, mass-radius relation, and maximum mass of neutron stars by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations.

## Key numbers

- The root-mean-square deviation for the best global fit (BSk24, J = 30 MeV) to 2353 measured masses is 0.549 MeV
- For the subset of 257 neutron-rich nuclei (Sn ≤ 5.0 MeV), BSk24 gives σ = 0.702 MeV while BSk25 (J = 29 MeV) gives σ = 0.791 MeV
- Maximum neutron-star masses range from 1.86 solar masses (BSk19, FP constraint) to 2.28 solar masses (BSk21 and BSk24, LS2 constraint)
- The L parameter varies from 36.9 MeV (BSk25, J = 29 MeV) to 68.5 MeV (BSk22, J = 32 MeV)
- The Ksym parameter varies from -135.6 MeV (BSk26, APR constraint) to 13.0 MeV (BSk22, LS2 constraint)
- For neutron-skin thickness measurements on 26 nuclei, BSk24 gives σ_rms = 0.0437 fm and BSk26 gives σ_rms = 0.0415 fm
- The liquid-drop model fit to the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation yields J = 27.30 MeV with rms deviation 3.03 MeV for 2353 nuclei

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous HFB mass models (BSk19-21, 2010) by fitting to the updated 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation and exploring different values of the symmetry coefficient J, while also incorporating constraints from ab initio neutron-matter calculations by Friedman and Pandharipande (1981), Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall (1998), and Li and Schulze (2008). The models enable a unified treatment of all regions of neutron stars (outer crust, inner crust, and core) and allow comparison with observations of massive neutron stars PSR J1614-2230 (1.97 ± 0.04 solar masses) and PSR J0348+0432 (2.01 ± 0.04 solar masses), as well as constraints from X-ray bursters and low-mass X-ray binaries studied by Steiner et al. (2010).
