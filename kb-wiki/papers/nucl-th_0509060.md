# Beyond the Quasi-Particle picture in Nuclear Matter calculations using Green's function techniques

> Widths of low-lying states in nuclei are of the order of 30 MeV. These large widths are a consequence of the strong interactions leading to a strongly correlated many body system at the typical densities of nuclear matter. Nevertheless "traditional" Brueckner calculations treat these states as quasiparticles i.e. with spectral functions of zero widths. The width is related to the imaginary part of the selfenergy and is included selfconsistently in an extension of the Brueckner theory using T-matrix and Green's function techniques. A more general formulation applicable also to non-equilibrium systems is contained in the Kadanoff-Baym (KB) equations while still maintaining the basic many-body techniques of Bruecknet theory. In the present work the two-time KB-equations are time-stepped along the imaginary time-axis to calculate the binding energy of nuclear matter as a function of density, including the spectral widths self-consistently. These zero temperature calculations are compared with quasi-particle calculations. The inclusion of the self-consistent widths are found to add several MeV to the binding. The spectral widths are due to the long-ranged correlations. Short-ranged correlations decrease rather than increase the binding. The metod is easily extended to non-zero temperatures where the importance of the widths are expected to increase.

# Beyond the Quasi-Particle picture in Nuclear Matter calculations using Green's function techniques

## Key claim

The inclusion of self-consistent spectral widths (imaginary parts of the self-energy) in nuclear matter calculations adds several MeV to the binding energy relative to quasi-particle (Brueckner) calculations, but this effect is approximately canceled by the depletion of occupation numbers due to short-ranged correlations, which reduces binding by about 2 MeV. The net result is that full T-matrix calculations show decreased rather than increased binding compared to the quasi-particle result.

## Method

The author time-stepped the two-time Kadanoff-Baym (KB) equations along the imaginary time axis to calculate the correlated Green's functions at zero temperature, using a separable NN-potential derived by inverse scattering techniques that reproduces the V_low-k interaction with a momentum cutoff of Lambda = 2 fm^{-1}. Only S-states were included as these are the major contributors to the widths, and the self-energy was calculated from the long-ranged part of the NN-interaction in a second-order Born approximation. The binding energy was then compared with a quasi-particle calculation performed by replacing the correlated Green's functions with free ones having zero widths.

## Key numbers

- Widths of low-lying states in nuclei are of the order of 30 MeV.
- The correlated medium shows a depletion of occupation numbers of about 20 percent at normal nuclear matter density.
- Earlier results using a local gaussian interaction and second-order selfenergy showed the effect of the widths to increase binding by 0.5 MeV at k_F = 1.4 fm^{-1} and by 1.5 MeV at k_F = 1.8 fm^{-1}.
- The present calculation shows an increase in binding of about 2 MeV due to the widths of the spectral function relative to the quasi-particle result.
- A previous calculation with spectral functions gave a binding of 17.4 MeV while the Brueckner (quasi-particle) calculation gave 20.0 MeV.
- The depletion factor Z' is approximately 0.8, so the binding due to this correction is reduced by about 20 percent, i.e., about 3 MeV.
- The total effect associated with the depletion would be a binding decreased by approximately 2 MeV, essentially canceling the width effect.

## Context

This work extends earlier equilibrium Green's function calculations by Kohler (2004) that used a local gaussian interaction and second-order selfenergy, now employing a more realistic separable interaction consistent with V_low-k approaches (Bogner 2003). The results contrast with full T-matrix calculations by Muther (2003) and Bozek (1999, 2002) which showed decreased rather than increased binding when including spectral functions. The method enables future extension to non-zero temperatures and non-equilibrium systems, and the author notes that the imaginary time-stepping technique has the numerical advantage over conventional T-matrix calculations of not requiring explicit integration over sharply peaked spectral functions as a function of omega.
