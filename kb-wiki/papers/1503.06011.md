# Reappraisal of the limit on the variation in $α$ implied by Oklo

> We reconsider the analysis of the sensitivity of neutron resonance energies $E_i$ to changes in $α$ with a view to resolving uncertainties that plague earlier treatments. We point out that, with more appropriate choices of nuclear parameters, the standard estimate (due to Damour and Dyson) of the sensitivity for resonances in ${}^{150}$Sm is increased by a factor of 2.5. We go on to identify and compute excitation, Coulomb and deformation corrections. To this end, we use deformed Fermi density distributions fitted to the output of Hartree-Fock (HF) + BCS calculations (with both the SLy4 and SkM$^*$ Skyrme functionals), the energetics of the surface diffuseness of nuclei, and thermal properties of their deformation. We also invoke the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis, performing the requisite microcanonical averages with two phenomenological level densities which, via the leptodermous expansion of the level density parameter, include the effect of increased surface diffuseness. Theoretical uncertainties are assessed with the \emph{inter-model} prescription of Dobaczewski et al. [J. Phys. G: Nucl. Part. Phys. {\bf 41}, 074001 (2014)]. The corrections diminish the revised ${}^{150}$Sm sensitivity but not by more than 25\%. Subject to a weak and testable restriction on the change in $m_q/Λ$ (relative to the change in $α$) since the time when the Oklo reactors were active ($m_q$ is the average of the $\text{u}$ and $\text{d}$ current quark masses, and $Λ$ is the mass scale of quantum chromodynamics), we deduce that $|α_{\text{Oklo}}-α_{\text{now}}|<1.1\times 10^{-8}α_{\text{now}}$ (95\% confidence level). The corresponding bound on the present-day time variation of $α$ is tighter than the best limit to date from atomic clock experiments.

## Key claim

The authors revise the Damour-Dyson estimate of the sensitivity of neutron resonance energies in 150Sm to changes in the fine structure constant α, finding that the standard estimate is increased by a factor of 2.5 with more appropriate nuclear parameters, but that excitation, Coulomb, and deformation corrections diminish this revised sensitivity by no more than 25%. Subject to a weak and testable restriction on the change in m_q/Λ relative to the change in α, they deduce that |α_Oklo − α_now| < 1.1 × 10⁻⁸ α_now at 95% confidence level.

## Method

The authors compute corrections to the Damour-Dyson estimate of k_α (the sensitivity coefficient linking neutron resonance energy shifts to changes in α) for the 150Sm compound nucleus formed by neutron capture on 149Sm. They use deformed Fermi density distributions fitted to Hartree-Fock + BCS calculations with both the SLy4 and SkM* Skyrme functionals, incorporate the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis to perform microcanonical averages with back-shifted Fermi gas and constant temperature level densities, and assess theoretical uncertainties using the inter-model prescription of Dobaczewski et al. (2014).

## Key numbers

- The revised Damour-Dyson estimate for k_α^DD in 150Sm is −2.51 ± 0.20 MeV, a factor of 2.5 larger than the standard estimate of −1.1 MeV
- The difference in mean-square charge radii δ_gs⟨r²⟩ for the isotope pair 149,150Sm is 0.250(20) fm², about 20% larger than the value adopted by Damour and Dyson
- The equivalent rms radius Q_i for the 150Sm compound nucleus is 6.50 ± 0.01 fm, revised from the Damour-Dyson value of 8.11 fm
- The net correction to k_α^DD is always less than 25% of the revised k_α^DD value
- The 95% confidence level upper bound on |ΔE_i| for the 150Sm resonance is 23 meV
- The final bound on α variation is |α_Oklo − α_now| < 1.1 × 10⁻⁸ α_now (95% confidence level)

## Context

This work extends the foundational analysis of Damour and Dyson (1996) on Oklo-based constraints on α variation, addressing uncertainties that had led some researchers to dismiss Oklo bounds as strongly model-dependent. The authors' bound is one order of magnitude lower than the Oklo-based bound most commonly adopted in earlier attempts to identify phenomenologically successful models of α-variation, and the corresponding bound on the present-day time variation of α is tighter than the best limit from atomic clock experiments. The results enable more reliable constraints on dynamical dark energy models that admit variation in α over cosmological time scales.
