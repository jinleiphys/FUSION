# Physics with ions at the Future Circular Collider

> The unique physics opportunities accessible with nuclear collisions at the CERN Future Circular Collider (FCC) are summarized. Lead-lead (PbPb) and proton-lead (pPb) collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 39 and 63 TeV respectively with $\mathcal{L}_{int}$ = 33 nb$^{-1}$ and 8 pb$^{-1}$ monthly integrated luminosities, will provide unprecedented experimental conditions to study quark-gluon matter at temperatures ${\cal O}$(1 GeV). The following topics are succinctly discussed: (i) charm-quark densities thrice larger than at the LHC, leading to direct heavy-quark impact in the bulk QGP properties, (ii) quarkonia, including $Υ(1S)$, melting at temperatures up to five times above the QCD critical temperature, (iii) access to initial-state nuclear parton distributions (nPDF) at fractional momenta as low as $x\approx 10^{-7}$, (iv) availability of $5\cdot 10^5$ top-quark pairs per run to study the high-$x$ gluon nPDF and the energy loss properties of boosted colour-antennas, (v) study of possible Higgs boson suppression in the QGP, and (vi) high-luminosity $γγ$ (ultraperipheral) collisions at c.m. energies up to 1 TeV.

## Key claim

The paper argues that nuclear collisions at the Future Circular Collider (FCC) will provide unprecedented conditions to study quark-gluon matter at temperatures of order 1 GeV, but this claim is conditional on achieving the projected monthly integrated luminosities of 33 nb$^{-1}$ for PbPb and 8 pb$^{-1}$ for pPb, which are factors of 10 above LHC values.

## Method

The authors extrapolate from LHC heavy-ion data to FCC energies using scaling laws (e.g., charged-particle multiplicity scaling as $\propto(\sqrt{s_{NN}})^{0.3}$) and present NLO cross-section calculations for hard probes including charm, bottom, top quarks, and Higgs boson production. They evaluate the expected yields and experimental sensitivities for key observables such as quarkonium melting, nuclear parton distribution functions (nPDF) constraints, and ultraperipheral photon-photon collisions, using simulations and existing theoretical frameworks.

## Key numbers

- PbPb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 39 TeV and pPb at 63 TeV, with monthly integrated luminosities of 33 nb$^{-1}$ and 8 pb$^{-1}$ respectively
- Central PbPb collisions produce dN$_{ch}$/d$\eta|_{\eta=0}$ $\approx$ 3600 charged particles per unit rapidity, with energy density 35-40 GeV/fm$^3$ at $\tau_0$ = 1 fm/c
- Medium temperatures reach T $\approx$ 1 GeV, with $\Upsilon$(1S) melting expected at T = (4-5)T$_c$
- Charm-quark densities thrice larger than at the LHC, with $\sigma(\ttbar)$ $\approx$ 3.2 $\mu$b in pPb and 300 $\mu$b in PbPb
- About 5 $\times$ 10$^5$ top-quark pairs per run in the fully leptonic decay channel
- Higgs boson production cross sections $\sigma(gg\to H)$ $\approx$ 0.12 $\mu$b in pPb and 12 $\mu$b in PbPb, yielding about 500 and 1000 Higgs events per month in PbPb and pPb respectively, with diphoton significance of 5.5$\sigma$
- Access to nPDF at fractional momenta as low as $x \approx 10^{-7}$
- Ultraperipheral $\gamma\gamma$ collisions at c.m. energies up to 1 TeV

## Context

This paper extends the physics program of the LHC heavy-ion program (e.g., ALICE, ATLAS, CMS) to the FCC, building on prior work by Eskola (2009) on nPDF constraints and by Laine (2006) on finite-T QCD calculations. It enables new studies of QGP properties at higher temperatures, including direct measurement of $\Upsilon$(1S) melting predicted by lattice-QCD calculations (Aarts, 2014), and provides the first opportunity to use top quarks and Higgs bosons as probes of hot QCD matter, as well as access to the saturation regime where DGLAP factorization is expected to break down (Gelis, 2010).
