# Inferring three-nucleon couplings from multi-messenger neutron-star observations

> Understanding the interactions between nucleons in dense matter is an important challenge in theoretical physics. Effective field theories have emerged as the dominant approach to address this problem at low energies, with many successful applications to the structure of nuclei and the properties of dense nucleonic matter. However, how far into the interior of neutron stars these interactions can describe dense matter is an open question. Here, we develop a framework that enables the inference of three-nucleon couplings in dense matter directly from astrophysical neutron star observations. We apply this formalism to the LIGO/Virgo gravitational-wave event GW170817 and the X-ray measurements from NASA's Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer and establish direct constraints for the couplings that govern three-nucleon interactions in chiral effective field theory. Furthermore, we demonstrate how next-generation observations of a population of neutron star mergers can offer stringent constraints on three-nucleon couplings, potentially at a level comparable to those from laboratory data. Our work directly connects the microscopic couplings in quantum field theories to macroscopic observations of neutron stars, providing a way to test the consistency between low-energy couplings inferred from terrestrial and astrophysical data.

## Key claim

The paper develops a Bayesian inference framework that directly constrains the low-energy couplings (LECs) \(c_1\) and \(c_3\) governing three-nucleon (3N) interactions in chiral effective field theory from multi-messenger neutron-star observations, but the current astrophysical constraints are consistent with laboratory values only at the 90% confidence level and carry large statistical uncertainties.

## Method

The authors construct a Hamiltonian at next-to-next-to-leading order (N\(^2\)LO) in chiral EFT, fixing the two-nucleon part to the Entem-Machleidt-Nosyk interaction with cutoff 450 MeV, and parameterizing the leading 3N forces by the LECs \(c_1\) and \(c_3\). They compute the neutron-matter equation of state (EOS) using third-order many-body perturbation theory (MBPT) and train a parametric matrix model (PMM) emulator on 30 MBPT calculations to rapidly predict the EOS for arbitrary \(c_1, c_3\) values. The neutron-star EOS is extended above \(2 n_{\rm sat}\) using speed-of-sound parameterizations with 2, 5, or 7 total parameters, and an ensemble of neural networks emulates the tidal deformability-mass relation from the EOS. Posterior distributions for \(c_1\) and \(c_3\) are obtained via Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling, conditioning on the gravitational-wave event GW170817 and X-ray observations of three pulsars from NICER (PSR J0030+0451, PSR J0740+6620, PSR J0437-4715).

## Key numbers

* The laboratory values from pion-nucleon scattering are \(c_1 = -0.74 \pm 0.02\) GeV\(^{-1}\) and \(c_3 = -3.61 \pm 0.05\) GeV\(^{-1}\) at one-sigma level.
* The posterior median for \(c_3\) from current astrophysical data is \(-2.52\) GeV\(^{-1}\), consistent with the laboratory value at the 90% confidence level.
* The PMM emulator predicts the neutron-matter EOS at nuclear saturation density \(n_{\rm sat} = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\) with an average deviation of 0.04% from high-fidelity MBPT calculations.
* The neural-network emulator predicts the tidal deformability at \(1.4 M_\odot\) with an average uncertainty of 0.02% and a maximum of 2.5%.
* For next-generation detectors, analyzing the 20 loudest events from a simulated population of \(\approx 400\) events per year yields constraints on \(c_3\) that converge to the injected value with uncertainties decreasing as \(\sim 1/\sqrt{N}\).
* The average (maximum) size of the third-order MBPT correction across all densities and LEC combinations is 252 (790) keV.

## Context

This work extends efforts to connect chiral EFT interactions to neutron-star observables, building on prior EOS calculations by Hebeler et al. (2013), Drischler et al. (2017), and Keller et al. (2022). It improves upon earlier attempts to constrain nuclear interactions from astrophysical data by Maselli et al. (2020), Sabatucci et al. (2022), and Rose et al. (2023), which did not directly investigate LECs or fully model EOS variations from low to high densities. The framework enables consistency checks between LECs inferred from neutron-star observations and those from terrestrial nuclear experiments (e.g., Hoferichter et al. 2015, Siemens et al. 2016), and with next-generation gravitational-wave detectors (Einstein Telescope, Cosmic Explorer) it can provide constraints competitive with laboratory data.
