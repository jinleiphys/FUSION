# High precision analytical description of the allowed $β$ spectrum shape

> A fully analytical description of the allowed $β$ spectrum shape is given in view of ongoing and planned measurements. Its study forms an invaluable tool in the search for physics beyond the standard electroweak model and the weak magnetism recoil term. Contributions stemming from finite size corrections, mass effects, and radiative corrections are reviewed. A particular focus is placed on atomic and chemical effects, where the existing description is extended and analytically provided. The effects of QCD-induced recoil terms are discussed, and cross-checks were performed for different theoretical formalisms. Special attention was given to a comparison of the treatment of nuclear structure effects in different formalisms. Corrections were derived for both Fermi and Gamow-Teller transitions, and methods of analytical evaluation thoroughly discussed. In its integrated form, calculated $f$ values were in agreement with the most precise numerical results within the aimed for precision. We stress the need for an accurate evaluation of weak magnetism contributions, and note the possible significance of the oft-neglected induced pseudoscalar interaction. Together with improved atomic corrections, we then present an analytical description of the allowed $β$ spectrum shape accurate to a few parts in $10^{-4}$ down to 1\,keV for low to medium $Z$ nuclei, thereby extending the work by previous authors by nearly an order of magnitude.

## Key claim

The authors present a fully analytical description of the allowed beta spectrum shape that is accurate to a few parts in 10^4 down to 1 keV for low to medium Z nuclei, extending the work of previous authors by nearly an order of magnitude, but this precision is conditional on the accurate evaluation of weak magnetism contributions and the inclusion of the often-neglected induced pseudoscalar interaction.

## Method

The authors derived analytical expressions for all corrections to the allowed beta spectrum shape, including finite nuclear size effects, mass effects, radiative corrections, atomic screening, atomic exchange, shake-up and shake-off effects, and nuclear structure-dependent corrections. They combined the transparency of the Holstein formalism with the rigor of the Behrens-Buhring formalism for nuclear structure effects, and provided analytical fits for atomic exchange based on state-of-the-art numerical calculations. All formulae were implemented in a custom C++ program that calculates the allowed beta spectrum shape and corresponding (anti)neutrino spectrum from configuration files describing decay parameters and nuclear properties.

## Key numbers

- The analytical description is accurate to a few parts in 10^4 down to 1 keV for low to medium Z nuclei
- The atomic exchange effect can exceed 20% in the lowest energy regions for higher Z
- The L0(Z,W) parametrization yields accuracy to 1 part in 10^4 for p ≤ 45 and for |Z| ≤ 60
- The effect of L0(Z,W) ranges from a few 0.1% up to several percent
- A precision of typically 0.5% is required when determining the Fierz interference term b_Fierz to improve existing limits on scalar or tensor coupling constants
- The factor (1+γ)/2 absent in some expansions introduces an error of about 0.5% for Z=20

## Context

This work extends the analytical parametrizations of Wilkinson (1982-1997) by nearly an order of magnitude in precision, while building on the rigorous numerical framework of Behrens and collaborators (1963-1982) and the formalism of Holstein (1971-1974). The improved description enables more sensitive searches for beyond-Standard-Model scalar and tensor interactions via the Fierz interference term, and allows for precision tests of QCD-induced form factors such as weak magnetism, which is required at the 10^-4 level to avoid other Standard Model effects mimicking non-zero Fierz terms.
