# On the Calculation of Pressure Derivatives in Mean-Field Thermal Field Theories

> Accurate determination of higher-order pressure derivatives with respect to temperature $T$ and chemical potential $μ$ is essential for analyzing critical phenomena, transport properties, and phase transitions in strongly interacting matter. However, standard numerical differentiation methods often suffer from large numerical instabilities, especially in more complex mean-field thermal field theories. In this work, we present an approach that systematically derives symbolic expressions for these higher-order derivatives, bypassing the numerical instabilities commonly encountered in conventional methods. Our formalism is based on a Jacobian technique, which ensures that the dependence of internal mean-field parameters is fully incorporated into the final symbolic expressions. We illustrate the effectiveness of this method using the two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model as an example and show that it is particularly advantageous near phase transitions and at low temperatures, where numerical differentiation becomes highly sensitive.

## Key claim

The paper presents a Jacobian-based formalism that derives symbolic expressions for higher-order pressure derivatives with respect to temperature and chemical potential in mean-field thermal field theories, bypassing the numerical instabilities of finite-difference methods. The method is claimed to be particularly advantageous near phase transitions and at low temperatures, where numerical differentiation becomes highly sensitive, but the authors only illustrate it using the two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model as an example rather than proving general applicability.

## Method

The formalism expresses higher-order pressure expansion coefficients \(c_{m,n} = \partial^{m+n}P/\partial T^m \partial \mu^n\) as ratios of Jacobian determinants involving partial derivatives of the effective potential \(\Omega^\text{eff}(T,\mu,\boldsymbol{\chi})\) with respect to temperature, chemical potential, and internal mean-field parameters \(\boldsymbol{\chi}\). By taking derivatives under the constraint that \(\partial_{\chi_i}\Omega^\text{eff}=0\) (the gap equations), the method avoids explicitly computing derivatives of the internal parameters \(\bar{\boldsymbol{\chi}}(T,\mu)\) with respect to external variables. The symbolic expressions are evaluated by inserting the physical solutions \(\bar{\boldsymbol{\chi}}(T,\mu)\) obtained from numerically solving the gap equations, requiring only a single evaluation at the minimizing configuration rather than multiple closely-spaced evaluations.

## Key numbers

* The speed of sound squared \(c_s^2\) is expressed in terms of second-order pressure coefficients as \(c_s^2 = \frac{1}{T s + \mu n} \frac{2 n s (\partial s/\partial \mu|_T) - s^2 (\partial n/\partial \mu|_T) - n^2 (\partial s/\partial T|_\mu)}{(\partial s/\partial \mu|_T)^2 - (\partial s/\partial T|_\mu)(\partial n/\partial \mu|_T)}\), where the denominator involves delicate cancellations between quantities of order one.
* For a single internal variable \(\chi\), the second temperature derivative is \(c_{2,0} = -\left[ \frac{\partial^2 \Omega^\text{eff}}{\partial T^2} - \frac{(\partial^2 \Omega^\text{eff}/\partial T \partial \chi)^2}{\partial^2 \Omega^\text{eff}/\partial \chi^2} \right]_{\chi=\bar{\chi}}\).
* The mixed second derivative is \(c_{1,1} = -\left[ \frac{\partial^2 \Omega^\text{eff}}{\partial T \partial \mu} - \frac{(\partial_\mu \partial_\chi \Omega^\text{eff})(\partial_T \partial_\chi \Omega^\text{eff})}{\partial_\chi^2 \Omega^\text{eff}} \right]_{\chi=\bar{\chi}}\).
* The second chemical potential derivative is \(c_{0,2} = -\left[ \frac{\partial^2 \Omega^\text{eff}}{\partial \mu^2} - \frac{(\partial_\mu \partial_\chi \Omega^\text{eff})^2}{\partial_\chi^2 \Omega^\text{eff}} \right]_{\chi=\bar{\chi}}\).

## Context

This work extends the Jacobian technique for constrained derivatives (Cox 1965) to systematically compute higher-order thermodynamic derivatives in mean-field thermal field theories, addressing numerical instabilities previously encountered in calculations of the speed of sound squared in color-superconducting phases (Gholami 2024) and in finite-temperature expansions of the dense-matter equation of state near critical points (Mroczek 2024). The method enables more reliable computation of thermodynamic quantities such as heat capacity, particle number susceptibility, and shock adiabats (Rischke 1996) in models like the NJL model and its extensions, where internal parameters are not available in closed form and numerical differentiation near phase transitions is particularly problematic.
