# Calibrating global behaviour of equation of state by combining nuclear and astrophysics inputs in a machine learning approach

> We implemented symbolic regression techniques to identify suitable analytical functions that map various properties of neutron stars (NSs), obtained by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations, to a few key parameters of the equation of state (EoS). These symbolic regression models (SRMs) are then employed to perform Bayesian inference with a comprehensive dataset from nuclear physics experiments and astrophysical observations. The posterior distributions of EoS parameters obtained from Bayesian inference using SRMs closely match those obtained directly from the solutions of TOV equations. Our SRM-based approach is approximately 100 times faster, enabling efficient Bayesian analyses across different combinations of data to explore their sensitivity to various EoS parameters within a reasonably short time.

# Calibrating global behaviour of equation of state by combining nuclear and astrophysics inputs in a machine learning approach

## Key claim

Symbolic regression models (SRMs) can map neutron star properties (radii, tidal deformabilities, maximum mass) to nuclear matter parameters with sufficient accuracy that Bayesian inference using SRMs yields posterior distributions closely matching those obtained from direct solutions of the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations, while being approximately 100 times faster. The SRM approach is validated only for EoSs where the symmetry energy parameters L0 and Ksym0 lie within commonly accepted ranges; large relative errors (greater than 30%) occur for tidal deformability when L0 is below 40 MeV or Ksym0 is below -200 MeV.

## Method

The authors constructed a large ensemble (approximately 10^4) of neutron star equations of state using a density expansion in powers of n^(1/3) over the range 0.5 to 8 times nuclear saturation density, with crust EoSs from Baym (1971) and Carriere (2003). They applied symbolic regression to derive analytical expressions (quadratic polynomials in standardized nuclear matter parameters) that map eight nuclear matter parameters (K0, Q0, Z0, J0, L0, Ksym0, Qsym0, Zsym0) to neutron star radii and tidal deformabilities for masses from 1.1 to 2.15 solar masses in steps of 0.05 solar masses, and to the maximum mass. These SRMs were then used within a Bayesian inference framework to constrain the EoS using combinations of pseudo data and realistic data from nuclear experiments and astrophysical observations.

## Key numbers

- SRM predictions achieve R² values of approximately 0.99 for radii, 0.93 for tidal deformabilities, and 0.92 for maximum mass
- Root mean squared errors for the SRM predictions at 1.425 solar masses: 0.33 km for radius, 64 for dimensionless tidal deformability, and 0.03 solar masses for maximum mass
- The SRM-based Bayesian inference is approximately 100 times faster than the TOV-based approach, delivering results within 20 minutes on the same computational setup
- The prior ranges for nuclear matter parameters include: K0 from 180 to 330 MeV, J0 from 25 to 40 MeV, L0 from 20 to 120 MeV, Ksym0 from -300 to 300 MeV
- The reference values used to generate pseudo data include: K0 = 266 MeV, J0 = 33.17 MeV, L0 = 67 MeV, Ksym0 = -47 MeV
- For the scenario combining all data (S5), the 68% confidence intervals from SRM-based inference yield: K0 = 262 (+21, -23) MeV, J0 = 32.97 (+1.29, -1.26) MeV, L0 = 65 (+14, -14) MeV, Ksym0 = -26 (+79, -80) MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous correlation study (Imam 2024) that mapped only tidal deformability at specific masses, by now providing analytical expressions for radii, tidal deformabilities, and maximum mass across a broad mass range (1.1 to 2.15 solar masses). It builds on efforts by Richter (2023) who used LASSO regression and neural networks to identify key EoS parameters affecting the radius of canonical neutron stars. The SRM approach enables rapid Bayesian analyses across different data combinations, allowing systematic assessment of how nuclear physics inputs (symmetry energy, pure neutron matter energy, incompressibility slope) and astrophysical observations (radii, tidal deformabilities, maximum mass) jointly constrain the equation of state. The results demonstrate that low-density nuclear data (from Brown 2013 and Khan 2013) primarily constrain the symmetric nuclear matter sector, while pseudo experimental data on symmetry energy and its pressure constrain the density-dependent symmetry energy sector.
