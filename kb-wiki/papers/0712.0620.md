# Progress in methods to solve the Faddeev and Yakubovsky differential equations

> We shortly recall the derivation of the Faddeev-Yakubovsky differential equations and point out their main advantages. Then we give a review of the numerical approaches used to solve the bound-state and scattering problems for the three- and four-body systems based on these equations. A particular attention is payed to the latest developments.

## Key claim

The Faddeev-Yakubovsky differential equations offer a numerically advantageous formulation for few-body quantum systems because their asymptotic boundary conditions are simpler than those for the total wave function, and the discretized matrices have a band structure for local potentials; however, the paper reviews methods rather than presenting a single new result, and the advantages are conditional on the use of short-range, smooth potentials.

## Method

The paper first derives the Faddeev differential equations for an arbitrary number of components by starting from the Lippmann-Schwinger equation and introducing Faddeev components, then generalizes to the four-body case by introducing Yakubovsky components and the chain-of-partitions formalism. It then reviews numerical approaches including partial-wave decomposition, finite-difference and spline discretization, tensor factorization tricks, and hard-core boundary conditions to handle strongly repulsive short-range potentials.

## Key numbers

- The four-body Yakubovsky differential equations form an 18×18 block operator matrix for the 18 distinct chains of partitions (12 from 3+1 partitions and 6 from 2+2 partitions).
- For four identical bosons, the Yakubovsky equations reduce to a system of only two equations.
- The three-body Faddeev equations reduce to dimension 3 after partial-wave decomposition and separation of Eulerian angles, first solved numerically in 1992 (HKM1992).
- Hard-core boundary conditions for the three-body case require \(\sum_{\beta=1}^3 \psi_\beta\bigr|_{x_\alpha=c_\alpha}=0\) for each pair \(\alpha\), where \(x_\alpha\) is the interparticle distance and \(c_\alpha\) the sum of core radii.

## Context

This review extends the line of work beginning with Faddeev (1960) on integral equations for three-body scattering and Yakubovsky (1967) for N-body systems, and specifically the differential-equation formulation developed by Noyes and Fiedeldey (1968) and Merkuriev, Gignoux and Laverne (1976). It enables further calculations of bound states, resonances, and scattering observables for systems with hard-core interactions, as demonstrated by recent calculations for three and four helium atoms (MSSK2001, KMS2007, Lazauskas and Carbonell 2006). Open challenges remain for scattering problems involving triple collisions and Coulomb interactions.
