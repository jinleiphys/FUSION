# Progress in Nuclear Astrophysics of East and Southeast Asia

> Nuclear astrophysics is an interdisciplinary research field of nuclear physics and astrophysics, seeking for the answer to a question, how to understand the evolution of the Universe with the nuclear processes which we learn. We review the research activities of nuclear astrophysics in east and southeast Asia which includes astronomy, experimental and theoretical nuclear physics and astrophysics. Several hot topics such as the Li problems, critical nuclear reactions and properties in stars, properties of dense matter, r-process nucleosynthesis and $ν$-process nucleosynthesis are chosen and discussed in further details. Some future Asian facilities, together with physics perspectives, are introduced.

# Progress in Nuclear Astrophysics of East and Southeast Asia

## Key claim
This review paper surveys nuclear astrophysics research activities across Japan, Korea, mainland China, Taiwan, and Malaysia, claiming that the region has made tremendous progress since the 1980s through coordinated efforts in experimental nuclear physics, astronomical observation, and theoretical modeling, but the paper is a review that summarizes existing work rather than presenting a single new result, and the claimed progress is qualified by noting that many critical nuclear reaction rates and nuclear properties remain experimentally inaccessible and must be supplemented by theoretical predictions.

## Method
The authors compiled a review of nuclear astrophysics activities in five Asian countries/regions by summarizing experimental programs at facilities including RIKEN RIBF (Japan), RAON (Korea, under construction), JUNA and HIAF (China), and CRIB (Japan); astronomical surveys including LAMOST (China) and Subaru Telescope (Japan); and theoretical approaches including shell model calculations, relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov theory, and chiral perturbation theory. The review covers five specific hot topics: the lithium problems, critical nuclear reactions in stars, properties of dense matter, r-process nucleosynthesis, and neutrino-process nucleosynthesis.

## Key numbers
- 226 new isotopes have been synthesized and 84 nuclear masses have been measured at Asian facilities and adopted in the Atomic Mass Evaluation by 2020
- LAMOST survey has obtained over 10 million low-resolution spectra and six million medium-resolution spectra, with seven million stars released in DR7 with reliable stellar parameters
- The most Li-rich K giant TYC 429-2097-1 has a Li abundance over 3,000 times higher than the normal amount
- Over 10,000 metal-poor star candidates with metallicities less than 1% of the sun have been discovered through LAMOST
- The RAON facility stable beam commissioning is expected to begin in 2021
- The Subaru Telescope Prime Focus Spectrometer (PFS) is planned to begin operation from 2023
- KAGRA in Japan started operation in 2020

## Context
This review extends the tradition of the bi-annual international conference "Origin of Matter and Evolution of Galaxies" (OMEG), which started in 1988, and complements similar reviews from other regions. It summarizes work that builds on earlier studies such as the discovery of neutron halos by Tanihata and collaborators, the detection of SN1987A neutrinos in KAMIOKANDE, and the GW170817 neutron star merger detection. The review enables future coordinated use of new Asian facilities including RAON, HIAF, JUNA, and the upgraded RIBF, and identifies open questions including the lithium abundance problem in Big Bang nucleosynthesis, the need for direct measurements of the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction at Gamow energies, and the identification of r-process nucleosynthesis sites.
