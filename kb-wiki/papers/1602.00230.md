# Electron-neutrino scattering off nuclei from two different theoretical perspectives

> We analyze charged-current electron-neutrino cross sections on Carbon. We consider two different theoretical approaches, on one hand the Continuum Random Phase Approximation (CRPA) which allows a description of giant resonances and quasielastic excitations, on the other hand the RPA-based calculations which are able to describe multinucleon emission and coherent and incoherent pion production as well as quasielastic excitations. We compare the two approaches in the genuine quasielastic channel, and find a satisfactory agreement between them at large energies while at low energies the collective giant resonances show up only in the CRPA approach. We also compare electron-neutrino cross sections with the corresponding muon-neutrino ones in order to investigate the impact of the different charged-lepton masses. Finally, restricting to the RPA-based approach we compare the sum of quasielastic, multinucleon emission, coherent and incoherent one-pion production cross sections (folded with the electron-neutrino T2K flux) with the charged-current inclusive electron-neutrino differential cross sections on Carbon measured by T2K. We find a good agreement with the data. The multinucleon component is needed in order to reproduce the T2K electron-neutrino inclusive cross sections.

## Key claim

The two theoretical approaches (CRPA and RPA-based) agree satisfactorily for quasielastic electron-neutrino scattering on Carbon at large neutrino energies, but at low energies (around 200 MeV) the CRPA approach uniquely captures collective giant resonance structures that are absent in the RPA-based model; the multinucleon component is required to reproduce the T2K inclusive electron-neutrino cross section data.

## Method

The authors compared two theoretical frameworks for charged-current electron-neutrino scattering on Carbon-12: (1) a Continuum Random Phase Approximation (CRPA) built on Hartree-Fock (HF) single-particle wave functions, which describes giant resonances and quasielastic excitations; and (2) an RPA-based approach using a local relativistic Fermi gas (LRFG) with semiclassical finite-size corrections, which additionally includes multinucleon emission (np-nh) and coherent/incoherent pion production. They computed double-differential cross sections for fixed kinematics, then flux-folded the results with the T2K and MiniBooNE electron-neutrino fluxes, and finally compared the RPA-based model's sum of all channels (quasielastic, multinucleon, and pion production) against the T2K inclusive charged-current electron-neutrino data on Carbon.

## Key numbers

- The threshold energy in the HF+CRPA approach is about 18 MeV, reflecting the nucleon separation energy, which is ignored in the semiclassical LRFG approximation.
- The maximum multipole number for CRPA calculations is set to J=16, sufficient to reach convergence of the cross sections.
- For the RPA-based model, the Landau-Migdal parameter f' = 0.6, spin-isospin parameters g'_NN = 0.7, g'_NΔ = g'_ΔΔ = 0.5, and C_ρ = 1.5; the pion-nucleon form factor cutoff Λ_π = 1 GeV and rho-nucleon cutoff Λ_ρ = 1.5 GeV.
- The ratio dσ(ν_e)/d cosθ / dσ(ν_μ)/d cosθ at E_ν = 200 MeV in the CRPA 1p-1h channel varies from about 1.5 to 0.3 as cosθ increases from 0 to 1.
- The multinucleon (np-nh) contribution dominates the genuine quasielastic one for electron momenta p_e less than about 0.2 GeV in the T2K flux-folded cross sections.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical comparison of neutrino-nucleus scattering models, building on the RPA-based approach of Martini (2009) and the CRPA approach of Jachowicz (2002) and Pandey (2013, 2014). It addresses the need for precise electron-neutrino cross sections motivated by ν_μ → ν_e oscillation experiments (e.g., T2K, MINERvA), following earlier muon-neutrino studies that revealed the axial mass anomaly resolved by multinucleon contributions (Martini 2009, Nieves 2011). The comparison with T2K data (Abe 2014) enables validation of the theoretical models for electron-neutrino scattering, and the authors note that a future comparison with MINERvA results (Wolcott 2015) is planned.
