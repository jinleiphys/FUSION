# Longitudinal and Transverse Spin Responses in Relativistic Many Body Theory

> Spin longitudinal and transverse response function are studied by means of the relativistic many-body theory. The spin response functions in the relativistic theory are largely reduced from those in the non-relativistic theory. The local density approximation with the eikonal approximation is applied to the the nuclear absorption in the $(\vec{p},\vec{n})$ reactions on C and Ca. We compare the calculated results with the recent experimental data.

# Longitudinal and Transverse Spin Responses in Relativistic Many Body Theory

## Key claim
The relativistic many-body theory, using pseudovector pion-nucleon coupling and the resulting reduction of the effective nucleon mass in the nuclear medium, can bring the ratio of longitudinal to transverse spin response functions close to unity in agreement with experimental data from (p,n) reactions on C and Ca, at least in the low energy transfer region (omega < 60 MeV). The authors qualify that the theoretical responses underestimate experimental data above omega > 60 MeV, where contributions from Delta isobar excitations and multiple scattering processes are expected.

## Method
The authors calculate spin longitudinal and transverse response functions using a relativistic version of the pi + rho + g' model, with pseudovector coupling for the pi-NN vertex and vector-tensor coupling for the rho-NN vertex. They compute the free polarization functions in nuclear matter using the relativistic mean field theory with the TM1 parameter set, then incorporate random phase approximation (RPA) correlations with relativistic Landau-Migdal parameters (g'_pi = 0.7, g'_rho = 0.3). For finite nuclei (C-12 and Ca-40), they apply the local density approximation with an eikonal attenuation factor for nuclear absorption, using a nucleon-nucleon cross section of 30 mb.

## Key numbers
- Momentum transfer: |q| = 1.7 fm^-1 (the value of special interest measured by experiments)
- Effective nucleon mass at saturation density (k_F = 1.36 fm^-1): M* = 0.59 M
- Effective nucleon mass at surface density (k_F = 0.97 fm^-1, rho = 0.37 rho_0): M* = 0.83 M
- Typical Fermi momentum from LDA: k_bar_F = 0.98 fm^-1 for C-12, 0.97 fm^-1 for Ca-40
- Effective neutron numbers: N_eff = 2.0 for C-12, N_eff = 3.7 for Ca-40 (with sigma_NN = 30 mb)
- Experimental effective neutron numbers: N_eff^exp = 2.7 for C-12, 6.0 for Ca-40
- Free response ratio at saturation density: R_L/R_T ≈ 0.8 (little dependence on omega)
- Free response ratio at surface density: R_L/R_T ≈ 0.94
- RPA response ratio at surface density: R_L^RPA/R_T^RPA = 1 to 1.2 at low omega
- Landau-Migdal parameters used: g'_pi = 0.7, g'_rho = 0.3
- rho-NN tensor-to-vector ratio: kappa = 6.6
- rho-NN coupling constant: g_rho = 2.6
- Ratio of couplings: C_rho = 2.18

## Context
This work extends the relativistic approach of Horowitz and collaborators (1990s), who first applied the pseudovector coupling idea to spin observables in nuclear matter, by incorporating finite nuclear geometry and distortion effects through the local density approximation with eikonal absorption. The paper addresses the long-standing puzzle from experiments at LAMPF and RCNP (Wakasa, 1990s) where the ratio of longitudinal to transverse spin responses was found close to or less than unity, contradicting non-relativistic theoretical predictions from Esbensen et al. and Ichimura et al. that gave ratios much larger than one. The results enable future work on two-step and multiple scattering processes at higher excitation energies (omega > 60 MeV) and the inclusion of Delta isobar excitations, which the authors identify as necessary for a complete understanding of the spin response functions.
