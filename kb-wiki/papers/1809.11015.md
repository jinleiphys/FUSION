# Impact of the ionization of the atomic shell on the lifetime of the 229mTh isomer

> Recent experimental data are analyzed, concerning the half-lives of the 229mTh isomer in neutral atoms and various ions. Calculation is performed on the united platform of interplay of traditional and subthreshold resonance conversion. General agreement with experiment is obtained in the cases of Th I and Th III, a prediction is made concerning half-life in Th IV. Most critical is the case of Th II, where experimental data can be explained by interplay of various factors. A new physics is proposed, based on dependence of the nuclear lifetime on the ambient conditions, such as atmospheric pressure. This must be taken into account in future experiments and their interpretation.

# Impact of the ionization of the atomic shell on the lifetime of the 229mTh isomer

## Key claim

The half-life of the 229mTh isomer in singly charged ions (Th II) can be reconciled with the experimentally observed value of 10 ms or shorter only if one accounts for a combination of accidental resonance coincidence between the nuclear transition energy and a strong electronic level, plus collisional broadening of atomic lines due to the helium buffer gas at 40 mbar pressure in the stopping cell. Without these effects, the calculated half-life of about 1–6 seconds is 2–3 orders of magnitude longer than experiment.

## Method

The authors calculated the bound internal conversion (BIC) factor R for the 229mTh isomer decay in Th I, Th II, Th III, and Th IV using the multiconfiguration Dirac-Fock (MCDF) method with configuration mixing. They computed the discrete internal conversion coefficient analogues α_d and the resonance Breit-Wigner factors for each intermediate electronic state, summing over all contributing electronic bridges to obtain the total decay width. For Th II, they performed a statistical analysis assuming a uniform distribution of the nuclear isomer energy within 7.6 ± 0.5 eV to estimate the probability of accidental resonance with strong electronic levels, and included the effect of collisional broadening from the buffer gas.

## Key numbers

- In neutral Th atoms (Th I), the internal conversion coefficient is α(M1) = 1.1 × 10^9, giving a radiative nuclear half-life of approximately 2 hours.
- For Th II with the ground state configuration (7s)(6d_{3/2})^2 and isomer energy 7.6 eV, the calculated BIC factor is R = 1334, yielding a half-life of about 6 seconds.
- For Th II with the alternative ground state configuration (7s)^2(6d_{3/2}), the calculated BIC factor is R = 6933, yielding a half-life of about 1 second.
- For Th II, when the nuclear energy is assumed to be 8.28 eV (close to the 7.995 eV electronic level with Δ = 0.015 eV), the calculated half-life is 0.01 seconds.
- For Th III, the calculated BIC factor is R = 2.8, giving an expected half-life of about 40 minutes, consistent with the experimental lower bound of > 100 seconds.
- For Th IV, the calculated BIC factor is R = 0.08, so the expected half-life approaches the bare-nucleus value of about 2 hours.
- The probability that R > 7.2 × 10^5 (sufficient for half-life < 0.01 s) is 10% for the (7s)(6d_{3/2})^2 ground state and 13% for the (7s)^2(6d_{3/2}) ground state, assuming uniform distribution of nuclear energy within 7.6 ± 0.5 eV.
- Including collisional broadening, the required threshold becomes R_c = 7.2 × 10^4, with probabilities of 32% and 41% for the two ground state configurations respectively.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous calculations for neutral 229Th atoms (Karpeshin and Trzhaskovskaya, 2017) and the electronic bridge mechanism first applied to the 76-eV 235U isomer (Karpeshin et al., 1996) and to 229Th under a 3.5 eV isomer energy assumption (Karpeshin et al., 1999). It directly addresses the experimental measurements of the isomer half-life in neutral atoms and ions (Seiferle et al., 2017; von der Wense et al., 2016). The paper enables future experiments to account for environmental effects on nuclear decay rates in the BIC regime, and motivates more precise determination of the isomer energy and electronic spectroscopy of the 8s and 9s levels in Th II.
