# Scattering phase shift in quantum mechanics on quantum computers: non-Hermitian systems and imaginary-time simulations

> To overcome the fast oscillatory behavior of correlation functions for extracting scattering phase shift in real-time quantum simulations encountered in Ref.\cite{Guo:2026qkx}, we propose and test two solutions in the present work. One is to simulate Hermitian systems in imaginary time, the other is to simulate non-Hermitian systems in real time. We demonstrate that both approaches lead to the problem of non-unitary quantum evolution which can be solved by combining two quantum algorithms: block encoding and Hadamard test. The combined quantum algorithm does not require mid-circuit measurements or adjustment of the input parameters of the Hamiltonian, and can be easily implemented on quantum computers. Both the size and length of quantum circuits grow linearly with evolution time. Numerical tests on quantum simulators show that both approaches agree with exact solutions for a sufficiently long time before the signal is lost in statistical fluctuations. The results bode well for using non-Hermitian and imaginary-time simulations to circumvent oscillations inherent in real-time simulation of other quantum systems.

# Scattering phase shift in quantum mechanics on quantum computers: non-Hermitian systems and imaginary-time simulations

## Key claim

The fast oscillatory behavior of correlation functions for extracting scattering phase shift in real-time quantum simulations can be circumvented by either simulating Hermitian systems in imaginary time or non-Hermitian systems in real time, provided that the resulting non-unitary quantum evolution is handled by combining block encoding with the Hadamard test algorithm; however, both approaches lose signal to statistical fluctuations after a finite number of time steps (approximately 10-20 steps for the systems tested).

## Method

The authors discretize a one-dimensional quantum mechanical Hamiltonian with a contact interaction on a periodic lattice, encoding it in Pauli operator strings for 1-qubit and 2-qubit systems. They implement time evolution via trotterization, where non-unitary operators (either $e^{-H\tau}$ for imaginary-time evolution or $e^{H_2 t}$ for the anti-Hermitian part of the $L \to iL$-rotated Hamiltonian) are block-encoded into unitary operations using ancillary qubits, and the trace of the resulting operator is evaluated via the Hadamard test algorithm. Numerical tests are performed on quantum simulators with up to 100,000 shots per trial.

## Key numbers

- For the 1-qubit imaginary-time simulation with parameters $m=1$, $a=4$, $V_0=2$, and time step $\delta\tau=0.2$, agreement with the exact solution extends to over 10 time steps before signal is lost in statistical fluctuations (100 trials of 100,000 shots each).
- For the 2-qubit imaginary-time simulation with parameters $m=1$, $a=4/3$, $V_0=2$, and $\delta\tau=0.1$, agreement extends to about 5 time steps before being spoiled by statistical errors.
- For the 2-qubit non-Hermitian real-time simulation with parameters $m=1$, $a=4/3$, $V_0=2$, and $\delta t=0.2$, agreement extends to around 15 time steps in the real part and about 10 steps in the imaginary part (1,000,000 shots).
- The 2-qubit imaginary-time simulation requires $3N+1$ ancillary qubits for $N$ time steps, while the 2-qubit non-Hermitian simulation requires only $N+1$ ancillary qubits.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Guo 2026) on extracting scattering phase shifts via the integrated correlation function formalism in 1D quantum mechanics, which encountered fast oscillatory behavior in real-time simulations. The present paper implements two strategies to suppress oscillations: the $L \to iL$ rotation (which produces a non-Hermitian Hamiltonian) and Wick rotation to imaginary time. The block encoding technique follows approaches developed for imaginary time evolution by previous authors (PhysRevA.109.052414, Leadbeater 2024, Yi 2025). The results enable future application of these non-unitary simulation methods to more complex quantum systems, though the authors note that the computational demand on classical simulators becomes prohibitive beyond 10-20 time steps, and that fault-free quantum hardware would be needed to realize the full advantage.
