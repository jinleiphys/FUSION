# MOCABA: a general Monte Carlo-Bayes procedure for improved predictions of integral functions of nuclear data

> MOCABA is a combination of Monte Carlo sampling and Bayesian updating algorithms for the prediction of integral functions of nuclear data, such as reactor power distributions or neutron multiplication factors. Similarly to the established Generalized Linear Least Squares (GLLS) methodology, MOCABA offers the capability to utilize integral experimental data to reduce the prior uncertainty of integral observables. The MOCABA approach, however, does not involve any series expansions and, therefore, does not suffer from the breakdown of first-order perturbation theory for large nuclear data uncertainties. This is related to the fact that, in contrast to the GLLS method, the updating mechanism within MOCABA is applied directly to the integral observables without having to "adjust" any nuclear data. A central part of MOCABA is the nuclear data Monte Carlo program NUDUNA, which performs random sampling of nuclear data evaluations according to their covariance information and converts them into libraries for transport code systems like MCNP or SCALE. What is special about MOCABA is that it can be applied to any integral function of nuclear data, and any integral measurement can be taken into account to improve the prediction of an integral observable of interest. In this paper we present two example applications of the MOCABA framework: the prediction of the neutron multiplication factor of a water-moderated PWR fuel assembly based on 21 criticality safety benchmark experiments and the prediction of the power distribution within a toy model reactor containing 100 fuel assemblies.

# MOCABA: a general Monte Carlo-Bayes procedure for improved predictions of integral functions of nuclear data

## Key claim

MOCABA provides a Bayesian updating mechanism for Monte Carlo predictions of integral nuclear observables that avoids the first-order perturbation theory breakdown of the Generalized Linear Least Squares (GLLS) method, but its effectiveness depends on the degree of physical similarity between benchmark experiments and the application case, as measured by the correlation submatrix of the prior covariance.

## Method

The procedure first performs random sampling of nuclear data evaluations using the NUDUNA Monte Carlo program, which generates random ENDF-6-formatted files according to covariance information and converts them into libraries for transport codes such as MCNP or SCALE. For each nuclear data sample, a random sample of application-case system parameters is drawn, and transport calculations are performed for both the application case and benchmark observables to produce a multivariate data set. The prior mean vector and covariance matrix are estimated from this Monte Carlo data set, and Bayesian updating is then applied using a likelihood function that incorporates benchmark measurements and their system-parameter uncertainties, yielding posterior estimates and uncertainties for the integral observables of interest.

## Key numbers

- 1000 Monte Carlo samples of nuclear data for 235U, 238U, 1H and 16O were used, with covariance information from the ENDF/B-VII.1 evaluation, for the fuel assembly reactivity prediction test case.
- 21 criticality safety benchmark experiments were used: 4 from LEU-COMP-THERM-007 (Cases 1-4) and 17 from LEU-COMP-THERM-039 (all 17 cases), all involving water-moderated UO2 fuel rod arrays.
- Pearson correlations due to system parameter uncertainties between benchmark keff values were higher than 0.98 for all experiments except Case 3 and Case 4 of LCT-007.
- The application case was a 16x16 PWR fuel assembly, with uncertainties specified for 6 system parameters defining fuel rod dimensions, guide thimbles, and fuel column height.
- The benchmark system parameter vector had 3 components (fuel rod inner diameter, fuel rod thickness, and mean linear density of the fuel column), with a 3x3 covariance matrix derived from ICSBEP uncertainty specifications.

## Context

This work extends the Monte Carlo nuclear data uncertainty propagation approach of NUDUNA (Hoefer et al., earlier work) by adding a Bayesian updating capability that addresses the limitations of the GLLS method, which breaks down for large nuclear data uncertainties as shown by Rochman et al. (earlier work). The method enables the use of integral benchmark measurements to improve predictions of any integral function of nuclear data, including reactor power distributions and neutron multiplication factors, without requiring adjoint transport calculations or sensitivity matrices. The approach is demonstrated on a UACSA benchmark exercise from the OECD/NEA Working Party on Nuclear Criticality Safety, and enables future applications to reactor core design, safety analysis, and depletion analysis where first-order perturbation theory would be inadequate.
