# The use of Kolmogorov-Smirnov test in event-by-event analysis

> We propose to use the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test to uncover non-statistical differences between events created in heavy ion collisions within the same centrality class. The advantage of the method over other approaches which are currently in use, is that it is sensitive to any difference between the events and is not restricted to simple moments of the distribution of hadrons. The particular application examined here is the identification of the fireball decay due to spinodal fragmentation and/or sudden rise of the bulk viscosity.

## Key claim

The Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test can uncover non-statistical differences between heavy-ion collision events within the same centrality class by comparing complete event shapes rather than just moments of hadron distributions; the method is proposed specifically to identify fireball decay due to spinodal fragmentation or a sudden rise of bulk viscosity, but the authors note that resonance decays and finite-multiplicity effects can produce spurious signals that must be controlled.

## Method

The two-sample two-sided KS test is applied to pairs of events: for each event, an empirical cumulative distribution function is constructed from the rapidities of hadrons, and the maximum vertical distance \(D\) between the staircases of two events is computed. The quantity \(Q\) is then derived from the known cumulative distribution function of \(\sqrt{n}D\) (where \(n\) is the harmonic mean of the two event multiplicities), and the distribution of \(Q\) over many event pairs is examined. A uniform distribution of \(Q\) indicates that all events come from the same underlying probability distribution with independent particle emission; any departure signals non-statistical differences or intra-event correlations. The method is tested on toy events with momentum conservation and on events generated with the DRAGON Monte Carlo event generator, which simulates fragmented fireballs using a blast-wave model with fragment emission.

## Key numbers

- Toy events: mean multiplicity of 1000 particles, distributed into 100 groups (average 10 particles per group), with momenta generated from a uniform spherical distribution and total momentum within each group set to zero.
- DRAGON simulations: sets of \(10^4\) events generated, from which \(10^5\) pairs are randomly chosen for the KS test.
- Fragmentation simulation for central Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV (RHIC) uses fragments with mean volume of 5 fm\(^3\).
- Comparison simulations without fragmentation are performed for RHIC and for FAIR at \(E_{beam} = 30A\) GeV.
- The parameter \(R\) is defined as \(R = (N_0 - N_{\text{tot}}/B) / \sqrt{N_{\text{tot}}/B}\), where \(N_0\) is the number of pairs in the first bin, \(N_{\text{tot}}\) is the total number of pairs, and \(B\) is the number of bins of the \(Q\)-histogram; momentum conservation yields negative \(R\), while fireball fragmentation causes large positive \(R\).

## Context

This work extends prior event-by-event analysis techniques such as rapidity correlations (Pratt, 1994), correlations in azimuthal angle and pseudorapidity (Alver, 2009), multiplicity fluctuations (Baym, 1999), and mean \(p_t\) fluctuations (Baym, 1999; Broniowski, 2005), which focus only on specific moments of the momentum distribution. The KS test offers sensitivity to any difference between events, including those from spinodal decomposition (previously studied in lower-energy nuclear collisions by Chomaz, 2003) or from a sudden rise in bulk viscosity near the critical temperature \(T_c\) (suggested by Torrieri, 2007). The method enables future searches for fireball fragmentation at RHIC and FAIR, though the authors note that the influence of other processes (beyond momentum and charge conservation) still requires study.
