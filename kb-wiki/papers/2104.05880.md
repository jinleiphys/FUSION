# Properties of a quantum vortex in neutron matter

> We have studied systematically microscopic properties of a quantum vortex in neutron matter at finite temperatures and densities corresponding to different layers of the inner crust of a neutron star. To this end and in preparation of future simulations of the vortex dynamics, we have carried out fully self-consistent 3D Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations, using one of the latest nuclear energy-density functionals from the Brussels-Montreal family, which has been developed specifically for applications to neutron superfluidity in neutron-star crusts. By analyzing the flow around the vortex, we have determined the effective radius relevant for the vortex filament model. We have also calculated the specific heat in the presence of the quantum vortex and have shown that it is substantially larger than for a uniform system at low temperatures. The low temperature limit of the specific heat has been identified as being determined by Andreev states inside the vortex core. We have shown that the specific heat in this limit does not scale linearly with temperature. The typical energy scale associated with Andreev states is defined by the minigap, which we have extracted for various neutron-matter densities. Our results suggest that vortices may be spin-polarized in the crust of magnetars. Finally, we have obtained a lower bound for the specific heat of a collection of vortices with given surface density, taking into account both the contributions from the vortex core states and from the hydrodynamic flow.

## Key claim

The paper systematically characterizes the microscopic structure of a single quantum vortex in neutron matter at finite temperatures, showing that the specific heat in the presence of a vortex is substantially larger than for a uniform system at low temperatures and does not scale linearly with temperature, with the low-temperature limit determined by Andreev states inside the vortex core.

## Method

Fully self-consistent three-dimensional Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations were performed using the Brussels-Montreal energy-density functional BSk31, which was specifically fitted to microscopic calculations of the equation of state, effective masses, and 1S0 pairing gaps of pure neutron matter. The HFB equations were solved in a cubic box with an axially symmetric external potential to confine the neutron superfluid, imposing a phase constraint on the pairing field to generate a single vortex with winding number 1. Calculations included contributions from time-odd mean fields (current-current couplings) for the first time in this context, and were carried out at densities corresponding to different layers of the inner crust of a neutron star.

## Key numbers

- Pairing gaps in uniform neutron matter were recalculated with cutoff energies up to approximately 180 MeV; the overall precision of self-consistent calculations lies within 5% in the vicinity of the maximum pairing gap.
- The vortex core radius R_core (distance at which the pairing field reaches 90% of the bulk value) was computed for six densities; at zero temperature and density 0.0059 fm^-3, the bulk pairing gap is 1.334 MeV.
- The effective radius for the vortex filament model R_VFM was determined to be approximately 10.5 fm for density 0.0059 fm^-3, typically of order a few coherence lengths.
- The critical temperature for vortex disappearance is given by T_c = |Delta|/1.76.
- The simulation box for specific heat calculations had volume 180 x 180 x 36 fm^3 with external potential parameters r_1 = 80 fm and r_2 = 87.5 fm.
- The cutoff energy for the main calculations was approximately 90 MeV, corresponding to a spatial resolution of 1.5 fm in each direction.

## Context

This work extends previous zero-temperature studies of neutron vortices using Bogoliubov-de Gennes equations (de 1999) and HFB calculations with the FaNDF^0 functional (Yu 2003) that focused on very dilute neutron matter, as well as later HFB studies using the Skyrme SII functional (Avogadro 2007). The finite-temperature calculations using the BSk31 functional, which was specifically constructed for astrophysical applications (Chamel 2016), enable future time-dependent simulations of vortex dynamics in neutron-star crusts. The results provide microscopic input for effective Vortex Filament Models (VFM) similar to those used for superfluid helium (Schwarz), and suggest that vortices may be spin-polarized in the crust of magnetars, with implications for understanding pulsar glitches.
