# Specific features and symmetries for magnetic and chiral bands in nuclei

> Magnetic and chiral bands have been a hot subject for more than twenty years. Therefore, quite large volumes of experimental data as well as theoretical descriptions have been accumulated. Although some of the formalisms are not so easy to handle, the results agree impressively well with the data. The objective of this paper is to review the actual status of both experimental and theoretical investigations. Aiming at making this material accessible to a large variety of readers, including young students and researchers, I gave some details on the schematic models which are able to unveil the main features of chirality in nuclei. Also, since most formalisms use a rigid triaxial rotor for the nuclear system's core, I devoted some space to the semi-classical description of the rigid triaxial as well as of the tilted triaxial rotor. In order to answer the question whether the chiral phenomenon is spread over the whole nuclear chart and whether it is specific only to a certain type of nuclei, odd-odd, odd-even or even-even, the current results in the mass regions of $A\sim 60,80,100,130,180,200$ are briefly described for all kinds of odd/even-odd/even systems. The chiral geometry is a sufficient condition for a system of proton-particle, neutron-hole and a triaxial rotor to have the electromagnetic properties of chiral bands. In order to prove that such geometry is not unique for generating magnetic bands with chiral features, I presented a mechanism for a new type of chiral bands. One tries to underline the fact that this rapidly developing field is very successful in pushing forward nuclear structure studies.

# Specific features and symmetries for magnetic and chiral bands in nuclei

## Key claim

This review argues that chiral geometry is a sufficient but not unique condition for generating magnetic bands with chiral features, and presents a mechanism for a new type of chiral motion in even-even nuclei. The paper emphasizes that while chiral symmetry breaking explains the near-degenerate doublet bands observed in nuclei like 134Pr, the phenomenon may be more widespread across the nuclear chart than previously recognized, appearing in odd-odd, odd-mass, and even-even systems across mass regions A ~ 60, 80, 100, 130, 180, and 200.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental data and theoretical descriptions of magnetic and chiral bands accumulated over more than twenty years, including schematic models that couple proton-particles and neutron-holes to a rigid triaxial rotor core. It presents the semi-classical description of the rigid triaxial rotor and the tilted triaxial rotor, the tilted axis cranking (TAC) approach, the triaxial projected shell model, and a collective Hamiltonian for the chiral coordinate. The author also introduces an extension of the generalized coherent state model (GCSM) to particle-core systems as a new mechanism for chiral motion in even-even nuclei.

## Key numbers

- M1 transition rates in band 1 of 198Pb range from 1.85 μN² to 5.90 μN², and in band 3 from 2.33 μN² to 5.82 μN²
- M1 transition rates in 199Pb range from 1.66 μN² to 4.82 μN² for band 1 and from 1.51 μN² to 2.59 μN² for band 2
- Magnetic bands exhibit B(M1) values of 0.5-2 μN² and very small B(E2) values of about 0.1 (eb)², compared with typical values for well deformed nuclei
- The ratio J(2)/B(E2) for magnetic bands is about an order of magnitude larger than for normal or super-deformed bands, taking values of order 100 (eb)⁻² MeV⁻¹
- In the 134Pr nucleus, a pair of almost degenerate ΔI=1 bands was observed, interpreted as chiral symmetry violation
- The shears mechanism generates a band length proportional to 2j, with the minimum potential energy attained at θ = 90° corresponding to Î = √2/2
- The effective moment of inertia from the shears mechanism is I = j²/(3V₂)
- More than 140 bands in more than 60 nuclides exhibiting magnetic band characteristics have been observed (Amita, 2001)

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Frauendorf and Meng (1997) who first pointed out that chiral transformation in nuclear structure takes place in the space of angular momentum, and builds on the experimental observation by Petrache et al. (1996) of a pair of almost degenerate ΔI=1 bands in 134Pr. The paper also draws on the earlier result of Frisk and Bengtsson (1987) showing that in triaxial nuclei a mean-field cranking solution may exist with angular momentum having non-vanishing components on all three principal axes. The schematic model of Machiavelli et al. (1999) describing the competition between shears and core rotation mechanisms is used extensively. The review enables future work by surveying chiral candidates across multiple mass regions and proposing a new type of chiral motion in even-even nuclei, while noting that the TAC-covariant density functional theory (CDFT) approach of Meng (2013) has been successfully applied to nuclei in the A ~ 60 and A ~ 80 regions.
