# Current without bias and diode effect in shuttling transport of nanoshafts

> A row of parallely ordered and coupled molecular nanoshafts is shown to develop a shuttling transport of charges at finite temperature. The appearance of a cu rrent without applying an external bias voltage is reported as well as a natura l diode effect allowing unidirectional charge transport along one field directi on while blocking the opposite direction. The zero-bias voltage current appears above a threshold of initial thermal and/or dislocation energy.

## Key claim

A row of coupled, flexible molecular nanoshafts can produce a net electrical current without any applied external bias voltage, and also exhibit a diode effect where current flows preferentially in one direction. However, this zero-bias current only appears above a threshold of initial thermal energy (temperature) or initial mechanical deformation (dislocation energy) that breaks the symmetry of the system.

## Method

The authors model a linear chain of N elastically coupled nanoshafts (treated as cantilevers with spring constant k = 3EI/L³) whose top ends obey coupled harmonic oscillator equations. The shafts are placed between two charged contacts; when adjacent shaft tops touch, charge is transferred, and when a shaft touches a contact, it contributes to the measured current. The analytical solution for the oscillator chain is combined with a discrete recharging rule: the time evolution is solved analytically between contact events, and each event resets the initial conditions and charge distribution. Simulations are performed for 11 nanoshafts with parameters typical of quarterthiophene.

## Key numbers

- For quarterthiophene, the spring constant is k = 1 N/m and the mass per nanoshaft is m = 5.49 × 10⁻²² g, giving a characteristic frequency ω = 1.35/ps.
- The length unit is l₀ = 0.1 nm; the field unit is 6.2 × 10⁸ V/m; the current unit is 21.6 mA (assuming single charge q = e); the energy unit is 31.2 meV (equivalent to 362 K).
- The threshold potential energy for initiating shuttling transport in the quarterthiophene example is m ω² Δx²/2 = 281 meV.
- The total current without bias increases quadratically with the initial velocity (i.e., proportionally to the initial kinetic energy) of the leftmost nanoshaft.

## Context

This work extends the concept of Brownian motors or ratchet effects (Hanggi and Bartussek, 1996; Julicher et al., 1997; Astumian and Hanggi, 2002) to a coupled mechanical chain of molecular shafts, providing a new mechanism for charge transport in organic field-effect transistors (OFETs). The shuttling mode is analogous to single-dot shuttling by mechanical cantilevers (Erbe et al., 2001; Scheible et al., 2002) but achieved here with a row of elastically coupled molecular tubes. The results offer an alternative explanation for the threshold and saturation behavior observed in OFETs based on oligothiophene self-assembled films (Habenicht et al., 2008), and suggest that such systems can function as nanoscale diodes or zero-bias current sources.
