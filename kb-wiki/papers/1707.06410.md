# A new equation of state for core-collapse supernovae based on realistic nuclear forces and including a full nuclear ensemble

> We have constructed a nuclear equation of state (EOS) that includes a full nuclear ensemble for use in core-collapse supernova simulations. It is based on the EOS for uniform nuclear matter that two of the authors derived recently, applying a variational method to realistic two- and there-body nuclear forces. We utilize an extended liquid drop model of heavy nuclei and a quantum-theoretical mass evaluation for light nuclei. In addition to realistic nuclear forces, the inclusion of in-medium effects on the full ensemble of nuclei makes the new EOS one of the most realistic EOS's for supernova simulations. We make comparisons with the FYSS EOS, which is based on the same formulation for the nuclear ensemble but adopts the relativistic mean field (RMF) theory with the TM1 parameter set for uniform nuclear matter. The new EOS is softer than the FYSS EOS around and above nuclear saturation densities. We find that neutron-rich nuclei with small mass numbers are more abundant in the new EOS than in the FYSS EOS because of the larger saturation densities and smaller symmetry energy of nuclei in the former. We apply the two EOS's to 1D supernova simulations and find that the new EOS gives lower electron fractions and higher temperatures in the collapse phase owing to the smaller symmetry energy. As a result, the inner core has smaller masses for the new EOS. It is more compact, on the other hand, due to the softness of the new EOS and bounces at higher densities. The ensuing outward propagations of the shock wave in the outer core are very similar in the two simulations, which may be an artifact, though, caused by the use of the same tabulated electron capture rates for heavy nuclei ignoring differences in the nuclear composition between the two EOS's in these computations.

## Key claim

The new equation of state (EOS) is one of the most realistic for supernova simulations because it combines realistic nuclear forces (Argonne v18 two-body and UIX three-body potentials via a variational method) with a full nuclear ensemble including in-medium effects, but the similarity of shock propagation between the new EOS and the FYSS EOS in 1D simulations may be an artifact caused by using the same tabulated electron capture rates for heavy nuclei, ignoring differences in nuclear composition between the two EOSs.

## Method

The authors constructed a multi-nucleus EOS by combining a variational-method EOS for uniform nuclear matter (based on realistic Argonne v18 and UIX three-body forces) with an extended liquid drop model for heavy nuclei and a quantum-theoretical mass evaluation for light nuclei that includes Pauli-energy shifts and self-energy shifts. They minimized the free energy density with respect to nuclear abundances under baryon and charge conservation, then applied the resulting EOS to 1D neutrino radiation-hydrodynamics simulations of an 11.2 solar mass progenitor, comparing results with the FYSS EOS based on relativistic mean field theory with the TM1 parameter set.

## Key numbers

- Symmetry energy: ~30.0 MeV for the new EOS (variational method) versus ~36.9 MeV for the FYSS EOS (RMF with TM1)
- Radii of 1.4 solar mass neutron stars: ~14.2 km for the RMF EOS and ~11.5 km for the variational EOS
- Critical temperature for surface energy reduction: T_cs = 18 MeV
- Surface tension constants: sigma_0 = 1.15 MeV/fm^3 and S_s = 45.8 MeV
- Shell effects washout temperature scale: approximately 2.0-3.0 MeV
- Progenitor mass for 1D simulations: 11.2 solar masses
- Simulation duration: 150 ms after core bounce

## Context

This work extends the FYSS EOS framework (Furusawa 2011, 2013, 2017) by replacing the relativistic mean field treatment of uniform nuclear matter with a variational method based on realistic nuclear forces from Togashi (2013, 2017), who constructed the first such EOS for supernova simulations. The new EOS is softer than the FYSS EOS around and above nuclear saturation densities, and its smaller symmetry energy leads to lower electron fractions and higher temperatures during collapse. The approach enables more realistic supernova simulations by combining realistic nuclear forces with a full nuclear ensemble, though the authors note that consistent electron capture rates accounting for the different nuclear compositions are needed to fully assess differences in shock dynamics.
