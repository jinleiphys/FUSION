# On the nature of some SGRs and AXPs as rotation-powered neutron stars

> We investigate the possibility that some SGRs/AXPs could be canonical rotation-powered pulsars using realistic NS structure parameters instead of fiducial values. We show that realistic NS parameters lowers the estimated value of the magnetic field and radiation efficiency, $L_X/\dot{E}_{\rm rot}$, with respect to estimates based on fiducial NS parameters. We show that nine SGRs/AXPs can be described as canonical pulsars driven by the NS rotational energy, for $L_X$ computed in the soft (2--10~keV) X-ray band. We compute the range of NS masses for which $L_X/\dot{E}_{\rm rot}<1$. We discuss the observed hard X-ray emission in three sources of the group of nine potentially rotation-powered NSs. This additional hard X-ray component dominates over the soft one leading to $L_X/\dot{E}_{\rm rot}>1$ in two of them. We show that 9 SGRs/AXPs can be rotation-powered NSs if we analyze their X-ray luminosity in the soft 2--10~keV band. Interestingly, four of them show radio emission and six have been associated with supernova remnants (including Swift J1834.9-0846 the first SGR observed with a surrounding wind nebula). These observations give additional support to our results of a natural explanation of these sources in terms of ordinary pulsars. Including the hard X-ray emission observed in three sources of the group of potential rotation-powered NSs, this number of sources with $L_X/\dot{E}_{\rm rot}<1$ becomes seven. It remains open to verification 1) the accuracy of the estimated distances and 2) the possible contribution of the associated supernova remnants to the hard X-ray emission.

## Key claim

Nine SGRs/AXPs can be described as canonical rotation-powered pulsars driven by neutron star rotational energy, but only when their X-ray luminosity is computed in the soft 2-10 keV band and when realistic neutron star structure parameters are used instead of fiducial values (mass 1.4 solar masses, radius 10 km, moment of inertia 10^45 g cm^2).

## Method

The authors computed neutron star mass, radius, moment of inertia, and angular momentum by numerical integration of the axisymmetric general relativistic equations of equilibrium (Hartle's formalism) for three nuclear equations of state (NL3, TM1, GM1) under both global and local charge neutrality conditions. They then calculated the rotational energy loss for observed rotation periods and spin-down rates, and estimated surface magnetic fields using a general relativistic model of a rotating magnetic dipole, comparing the resulting radiation efficiency (observed X-ray luminosity divided by rotational energy loss) against the condition for rotation-powered pulsars.

## Key numbers

- Nine SGRs/AXPs satisfy L_X / E_dot_rot < 1 for soft X-ray band (2-10 keV): Swift J1834.9-0846, PSR J1846-0258, 1E 1547.0-5408, SGR J1745-2900, XTE J1810-197, PSR J1622-4950, SGR 1627-41, SGR 0501+4516, CXOU J171405.7-381031
- Including hard X-ray emission (20-150 keV) for three sources reduces the count to seven sources with L_X / E_dot_rot < 1
- For SGR 1900+14 at 15 kpc distance: blackbody component has k_B T_BB = 0.47 keV, emitting radius R_BB = 4.0 km; power-law component contributes 72% of total flux, giving L_PL_X = 9.3 x 10^34 erg/s
- For SGR 1806-20 at 15 kpc: k_B T_BB = 0.55 keV, R_BB = 3.7 km; power-law component contributes 84% of total flux, giving L_PL_X = 4.1 x 10^35 erg/s; at revised distance 8.7 kpc, L_PL_X = 1.4 x 10^35 erg/s
- For PSR J1846-0258: a glitch with fractional period change |Delta P|/P ~ (2-4.4) x 10^-6 is associated with the 2006 outburst of energy (3.8-4.8) x 10^41 erg
- Minimum mass for PSR J1846-0258 from glitch energetics: M_min = 0.72 solar masses (global neutrality) and M_min = 0.61 solar masses (local neutrality)
- Hard X-ray luminosity (20-150 keV) exceeds soft X-ray contribution by factors: 50 for SGR 0501+4516, 149 for 1E 1547.0-5408, 527 for SGR J1745-2900

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Coelho 2012, 2013, 2014, 2015) that identified four SGRs/AXPs as potential rotation-powered neutron stars, now expanding the set to nine sources. It challenges the magnetar model (Duncan and Thompson 1992, Thompson and Duncan 1995) and other proposed scenarios (fallback accretion, quark stars, quark-nova remnants, massive magnetized white dwarfs) by showing that realistic neutron star parameters can reconcile the rotational energy loss with observed X-ray luminosities. The results enable future verification of source distances and disentanglement of supernova remnant contributions to hard X-ray emission, and predict that glitch-outburst connections should be observable in these sources as in ordinary pulsars.
