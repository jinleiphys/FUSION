# First principles description of the giant dipole resonance in 16O

> We present an ab-initio calculation of the giant dipole resonance in 16O based on a nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction from chiral effective field theory that reproduces NN scattering data with high accuracy. By merging the Lorentz integral transform and the coupled-cluster methods, we extend the previous theoretical limits for break-up observables in light nuclei with mass numbers (A<=7), and address the collective giant dipole resonance of 16O. We successfully benchmark the new approach against virtually exact results from the hyper-spherical harmonics method in 4He. Our results for 16O reproduce the position and the total strength (bremsstrahlung sum rule) of the dipole response very well. When compared to the cross section from photo-absorption experiments the theoretical curve exhibits a smeared form of the peak. The tail region between 40 and 100 MeV is reproduced within uncertainties.

## Key claim
The authors present an ab-initio calculation of the giant dipole resonance in 16O using only a nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction from chiral effective field theory, omitting three-nucleon forces. The theoretical curve reproduces the experimental centroid and total dipole strength well, but exhibits a smeared form of the peak compared to photo-absorption data, and the tail region between 40 and 100 MeV is reproduced within uncertainties.

## Method
The authors merged the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method with the coupled-cluster (CC) method at the singles-and-doubles (CCSD) level to compute the dipole response function of 16O. They employed a nucleon-nucleon interaction from chiral effective field theory at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N3LO) with a point Coulomb force, omitting three-nucleon forces. The LIT of the dipole response was computed by solving Schrodinger-like equations for the similarity-transformed Hamiltonian in model spaces up to Nmax=18 with oscillator frequencies between 20 and 26 MeV, and the response function was obtained by numerical inversion of the LIT.

## Key numbers
- The CCSD calculation for 4He used model spaces with Nmax=18 and hbar-Omega=20 MeV, achieving convergence; the binding energies were 23.97 MeV for 4He and 7.37 MeV for 3H within CCSD, compared to 25.39 MeV and 7.85 MeV with the exact EIHH method.
- For 16O, the CCSD binding energy was 107.24 MeV (experiment: 127.72 MeV) and the threshold energy was 14.25 MeV (experiment: 12.13 MeV).
- The theoretical photo-absorption cross section integrated up to 100 MeV yields an enhancement kappa=0.57-0.58 of the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule [59.74*(NZ/A) MeV mb (1+kappa)].
- The LIT comparison used a resolution parameter Gamma=10 MeV; the inversion employed basis functions with N varying from 5 to 9.

## Context
This work extends the LIT method, previously limited to light nuclei with mass numbers A<=7 (e.g., 4He, 6Li, 6He, 7Li), to the medium-mass nucleus 16O by merging it with the coupled-cluster method. The authors benchmarked their approach against virtually exact hyperspherical harmonics (EIHH) results for 4He. This enables future ab-initio studies of electromagnetic responses in medium-heavy stable and rare isotopes, such as neutron-rich Oxygen isotopes where fragmented low-lying dipole strengths are expected, and opens perspectives for continuum problems in other fields like quantum chemistry.
