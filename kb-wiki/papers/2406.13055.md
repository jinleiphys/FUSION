# Self-consistent strong screening applied to thermonuclear reactions

> Self-consistent strong plasma screening around light nuclei is implemented in the Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) epoch to determine the short-range screening potential, $eϕ(r)/T \geq 1$, relevant for thermonuclear reactions. We numerically solve the non-linear Poisson-Boltzmann equation incorporating Fermi-Dirac statistics adopting a generalized screening mass to find the electric potential in the cosmic BBN electron-positron plasma for finite-sized $^4$He nuclei as an example. Although the plasma follows Boltzmann statistics at large distances, Fermi-Dirac statistics is necessary when work performed by ions on electrons is comparable to their rest mass energy. While strong screening effects are generally minor due to the high BBN temperatures, they can enhance the fusion rates of high-$Z>2$ elements while leaving fusion rates of lower-$Z\le 2$ elements relatively unaffected. Our results also reveal a pronounced spatial dependence of the strong screening potential near the nuclear surface. These findings about the electron-positron plasma's role refine BBN theory predictions and offer broader applications for studying weakly coupled plasmas in diverse cosmic and laboratory settings.

# Self-consistent strong screening applied to thermonuclear reactions

## Key claim

While strong screening effects are generally minor during Big Bang nucleosynthesis due to the high temperatures (86 keV > T > 20 keV), they can enhance fusion rates of high-Z (Z>2) elements while leaving fusion rates of lower-Z (Z≤2) elements relatively unaffected; however, the overall screening effect diminishes with decreasing temperature even though the relative enhancement of strong over weak screening grows as temperature decreases.

## Method

The authors numerically solve the nonlinear Poisson-Boltzmann equation incorporating Fermi-Dirac statistics with a generalized screening mass to determine the electric potential in the cosmic BBN electron-positron plasma for finite-sized ⁴He nuclei (modeled as Gaussian charge distributions with root-mean-squared charge radius R = √(2/3) × 1.67824 fm). They integrate inward from large distances (starting at 1 Å) using the weak-field analytic solution as a boundary condition, employing a shooting algorithm to determine the effective charge parameter Q_eff that yields a convergent solution at r=0. The induced charge density for Fermi-Dirac statistics is parameterized numerically from the momentum integral and then used in the Poisson equation, avoiding the Boltzmann approximation which would over-predict screening by up to a factor of 10³.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range studied: 86 keV > T > 20 keV, corresponding to the BBN epoch
- Debye screening mass m_D ≈ 2.7 keV for the BBN plasma
- For ⁴He at T = 86 keV, the re-scaled potential at the origin eφ(0)/T ≈ 27
- The strong screening mass m²_s,FD/m²_D is between 10 and 100 times larger than the weak screening mass in the region between the Gamow energy (E_G ≈ 390 keV) and the potential at the origin (eφ(0) ≈ 2300 keV)
- The Boltzmann screening potential over-predicts screening effects by a factor of up to 10³ compared to Fermi-Dirac strong screening
- The induced screening potential eφ_ind for ⁴He at T = 86 keV levels off near the nuclear surface at eφ_ind = 170 keV for Boltzmann statistics
- The charge radius of ⁴He used: R_α = 1.67824 fm (Krauth 2021)
- The static conductivity of the early universe σ_0 ≈ 0.23 keV
- Thermal velocity of ions during BBN: β < 10⁻²

## Context

This work extends the Salpeter (1954) approach to plasma screening in nuclear reactions by moving beyond the weak-field Debye-Hückel limit to treat self-consistent strong screening where eφ/T ≥ 1 near the nuclear surface. It addresses the singularity problem noted by Gruzinov (1998) for point-like charges by introducing finite-sized nuclei, and incorporates Fermi-Dirac statistics following the approach of Bruggen (2000) but with direct numerical solution rather than analytic matching conditions. The results refine BBN theory predictions at the 0.1% precision level and offer broader applications for studying weakly coupled plasmas in cosmic and laboratory settings, including potential development of a dynamic theory of strong screening.
