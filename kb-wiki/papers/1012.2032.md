# Modeling neutrino-nucleus interactions. Do we need a new paradigm?

> The availability of the double-differential charged-current neutrino cross section, measured by the MiniBooNE collaboration using a carbon target, allows for a systematic comparison of nuclear effects in quasi-elastic electron and neutrino scattering. The results of theoretical studies based on the impulse approximation scheme and state-of-the-art models of the nuclear spectral functions suggest that the electron cross section and the flux averaged neutrino cross sections corresponding to the same target and seemingly comparable kinematical conditions can not be described within the same theoretical scheme using the value ofthe nucleon axial mass obtained from deuterium measurements. I analyze the assumptions underlying the treatment of electron scattering data, and argue that the development of a new {\em paradigm}, suitable for application to processes in which the lepton kinematics is not fully determined, will be required.

## Key claim

The electron-nucleus cross section and the flux-averaged neutrino-nucleus cross section for the same carbon target and seemingly comparable kinematics cannot be described within the same theoretical scheme using the nucleon axial mass value obtained from deuterium measurements (M_A = 1.03 GeV). The author argues that the paradigm successfully applied to electron scattering, where lepton kinematics is fully determined, is not suitable for neutrino interactions where the incoming neutrino energy is uncertain and must be significantly modified.

## Method

The author compares theoretical calculations of the inclusive electron-carbon cross section at fixed beam energy (730 MeV) and scattering angle (37 degrees) with flux-averaged charged-current quasi-elastic (CCQE) neutrino-carbon cross sections from MiniBooNE. Both calculations use the impulse approximation scheme with state-of-the-art nuclear spectral functions and the same vector form factors, while the neutrino calculation employs a dipole parametrization of the axial form factor with M_A = 1.03 GeV. The author then examines the flux-unfolded total cross section and the reconstructed Q^2 distribution to assess the consistency between electron and neutrino scattering descriptions.

## Key numbers

- The world average axial mass from deuterium measurements is M_A = 1.03 ± 0.02 GeV
- The K2K and MiniBooNE collaborations using oxygen and carbon targets yield M_A ~ 1.2–1.35 GeV
- The electron-carbon cross section measurement was performed at beam energy E_e = 730 MeV and scattering angle θ_e = 37 degrees, with three-momentum transfer |q| ~ 450 MeV
- The mean energy of the MiniBooNE neutrino flux is ⟨E_ν⟩ = 788 MeV
- Within the author's approach, the axial mass yielding the best χ²-fit to the flux-integrated MiniBooNE Q²-distribution is M_A = 1.6 GeV
- The ratio of the MiniBooNE flux at E_ν = 975 MeV to that at E_ν = 788 MeV is approximately 0.83

## Context

This work extends the comparison of nuclear effects in quasi-elastic electron and neutrino scattering enabled by the MiniBooNE double-differential charged-current cross section data. The author contrasts the successful description of electron-nucleus scattering using nuclear many-body theory and realistic hamiltonians (reviewed in Benhar et al., Reviews of Modern Physics, 2008) with the failure of the same approach for neutrino scattering. The model of Martini et al. (2009) is noted as a prominent exception that reproduces the MiniBooNE flux-unfolded total cross section without increasing M_A, by including multi-nucleon knockout contributions. The paper argues that a new paradigm is needed that includes single- and multi-nucleon knockout, meson exchange currents, and inelastic processes leading to nucleon resonance excitation and pion production, which could help reconcile the different axial mass values obtained by different experiments.
