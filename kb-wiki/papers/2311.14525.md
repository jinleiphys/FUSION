# Systematics of the low-energy electric dipole strength in the Sn isotopic chain

> We present a systematic study of the mass dependence of the low-energy electric dipole strength (LEDS) in Sn isotopes in the range $ A = 111 - 124$ based on data obtained with the Oslo method and with relativistic Coulomb excitation in forward-angle ($p,p^\prime$) scattering. The combined data cover an energy range of $2 - 20$ MeV which permits, with minimal assumptions, a decomposition of the total strength into the contribution from the low-energy tail of the isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR) and possible resonance-like structures on top of it. In all cases, a resonance peaked at about 8.3 MeV is observed, exhausting an approximately constant fraction of the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn (TRK) sum rule with a local maximum at $^{120}$Sn which might be related to shell structure effects. For heavier isotopes ($A \geq 118$) a consistent description of the data requires the inclusion of a second resonance centered at 6.5 MeV, representing the isovector response of the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR). Its strength corresponds to a small fraction of the total LEDS only and shows an approximately linear dependence on mass number. The experimental results are also compared to ab initio-based microscopic calculations to investigate the importance of an inclusion of quasiparticle vibration coupling (qPVC) for a realistic description of the LEDS. A possible interpretation of the experimentally observed two-bump structure is given.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the low-energy electric dipole strength (LEDS) in Sn isotopes can be decomposed into a constant contribution from the tail of the isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR) and one or two resonance-like structures on top, but the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) component is much smaller than most theoretical predictions, exhausting at most about 15% of the total LEDS rather than representing the full low-energy strength.

## Method

The authors combined gamma strength functions (GSFs) extracted via the Oslo method from light-ion-induced compound reactions for 111-113,116-122,124Sn with GSFs from relativistic Coulomb excitation in forward-angle (p,p') scattering for even-mass isotopes 112-120,124Sn, covering an energy range of 2-20 MeV. They performed a simultaneous fit to both data sets decomposing the total strength into a generalized Lorentzian (GLO) model for the IVGDR tail, a Lorentzian for the M1 spin-flip resonance, exponential functions for the low-energy upbend, and one or two Gaussian resonances on top of the IVGDR tail. The experimental results were compared to relativistic quasiparticle random phase approximation (RQRPA) and relativistic quasiparticle time-blocking approximation (RQTBA) calculations using the NL3* effective interaction.

## Key numbers

- Total LEDS integrated over 4-10 MeV ranges from 3.5% to 4% of the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn (TRK) sum rule across Sn isotopes A=111-124
- The IVGDR tail contribution is approximately constant at about 1.5% of the TRK sum rule
- The dominant resonance is centered at about 8.3 MeV with average strength of 2% of the TRK value
- A second resonance at about 6.5 MeV appears for isotopes with A >= 118, with strength ranging from 0.1% of TRK in 118Sn to 0.5% of TRK in 124Sn
- The PDR strength shows an approximately linear dependence on mass number (neutron excess)

## Context

This work extends systematic studies of the PDR in Sn isotopes by Paar (2007), Piekarewicz (2006), and others, but challenges the common interpretation that QRPA-level calculations predict the PDR strength directly. The authors show that QRPA predictions correspond to the total LEDS rather than the PDR alone, and that including quasiparticle vibration coupling (qPVC) via RQTBA is essential for reproducing the experimental two-bump structure. The results enable future investigations of the toroidal nature of low-energy E1 transitions, as recently demonstrated by Neumann-Cosel (2023) in a N=Z nucleus, and provide constraints for calculations of (n,gamma) reaction cross sections relevant to nucleosynthesis.
