# Atomki anomaly and dark matter in a radiative seesaw model with gauged $B-L$ symmetry

> Motivated by recently reported anomalies in a decay of an excited state of beryllium by the Atomki collaboration, we study a radiative seesaw model with gauged $B-L$ symmetry and a $Z_2$ parity. Assuming that the anomalies originate from the decay of the $B-L$ gauge boson followed by the nuclear decay, the mass of the lightest right-handed neutrino or the dark matter candidate can be determined below $10$ GeV. We show that for this mass range, the model can explain the anomalies in the beryllium decay and the relic dark matter abundance consistent with neutrino masses. We also predict its spin-independent cross section in direct detection experiments for this mass range.

## Key claim

Assuming the Atomki anomalies originate from the decay of the $B-L$ gauge boson followed by nuclear decay, the mass of the lightest right-handed neutrino (the dark matter candidate) can be determined below 10 GeV, and the model can simultaneously explain the beryllium decay anomalies, the relic dark matter abundance, and nonvanishing neutrino masses.

## Method

The authors study a radiative seesaw model with gauged $U(1)_{B-L}$ symmetry and a $Z_2$ parity, extending the Standard Model with two scalar particles (a singlet $S$ responsible for $B-L$ breaking and an inert doublet $\eta$) and three right-handed neutrinos $N_R$. They impose the $X$ gauge boson mass to be 16.70 MeV to match the Atomki anomaly and derive the vacuum expectation value of $S$, then compute the thermal relic abundance of the lightest $Z_2$-odd right-handed neutrino via the Boltzmann equation, including annihilation channels into $XX$ and $HH$ pairs. The spin-independent dark matter-nucleon scattering cross section is calculated through Higgs boson exchange.

## Key numbers

- $m_X = 16.70 \pm 0.35(\text{stat}) \pm 0.5(\text{syst})$ MeV, the mass of the $B-L$ gauge boson required to explain the Atomki anomaly
- $v_s = 13.78 \left( \frac{2 \times 10^{-3}}{|\varepsilon_X|} \right)$ GeV, the vacuum expectation value of $S$ for the central $m_X$ value
- $|\varepsilon_n| = |\varepsilon_X| = (2-10) \times 10^{-3}$, the required neutron coupling to the $X$ boson
- $|\varepsilon_p| \lesssim 1.2 \times 10^{-3}$, the required proton coupling constraint
- $m_H = 19.5 \left( \frac{\lambda_s}{1} \right)^{1/2} \left( \frac{2 \times 10^{-3}}{|\varepsilon_X|} \right)$ GeV, the mass of the lighter $Z_2$-even scalar
- $m_{N_R} = 9.75 \left( \frac{Y_R}{1} \right) \left( \frac{2 \times 10^{-3}}{|\varepsilon_X|} \right)$ GeV, the mass of the right-handed neutrino dark matter candidate
- $m_{\nu_L} \sim 0.10 \left( \frac{\lambda_5 g_{i\alpha}^2}{5.7 \times 10^{-9}} \right) \left( \frac{Y_R}{1} \right) \left( \frac{v/m_\eta}{0.1} \right) \left( \frac{2 \times 10^{-3}}{|\varepsilon_X|} \right)$ eV, the left-handed neutrino mass scale
- $m_{DM} \simeq 3.4$ GeV, the maximum dark matter mass in the model for heavy $H$ ($m_H \gtrsim 9$ GeV)
- $m_{DM} = 2$ GeV, the dark matter mass for $m_H = 2$ GeV at the $HH$ pair annihilation threshold
- $m_{DM}^\pm = 0.5$ or $1.5$ GeV, the dark matter masses for $m_H = 2$ GeV at the $H$ resonance
- $\Gamma(h \rightarrow N_R N_R) = 6.21 \times 10^{-7}$ MeV, the Higgs invisible decay width into right-handed neutrinos
- $\Gamma(h \rightarrow H H) = 1.03 \times 10^{-3}$ MeV, the Higgs decay width into $H$ pairs
- $\Gamma(h \rightarrow X X) = 1.02 \times 10^{-3}$ MeV, the Higgs decay width into $X$ boson pairs

## Context

This work extends the radiative seesaw model with gauged $B-L$ symmetry proposed by Kanemura, Seto, and Shimomura (2011) to address the Atomki anomalies reported by Krasznahorkay et al. (2015). It builds on the interpretation by Feng et al. (2016) that the anomalies can be explained by a gauge boson in models with gauged baryon or $B-L$ symmetry. The paper shows that the same framework can simultaneously accommodate neutrino masses, dark matter relic abundance, and the Atomki signal, and predicts spin-independent dark matter cross sections within reach of upcoming direct detection experiments such as SuperCDMS SNOLAB.
