# Hindrance of the excitation of the Hoyle state and the ghost of the $2^+_2$ state in $^{12}$C

> While the Hoyle state (the isoscalar $0^+_2$ excitation at 7.65 MeV in $^{12}$C) has been observed in almost all the electron and $α$ inelastic scattering experiments, the second $2^+$ excited state of $^{12}$C at $E_{\rm x}\approx 10$ MeV, believed to be an excitation of the Hoyle state, has not been clearly observed in these measurements excepting the high-precision \aap experiments at $E_α=240$ and 386 MeV. Given the (spin and isospin zero) $α$-particle as a good probe for the nuclear isoscalar excitations, it remains a puzzle why the peak of the $2^+_2$ state could not be clearly identified in the measured \aap spectra. To investigate this effect, we have performed a microscopic folding model analysis of the \ac scattering data at 240 and 386 MeV in both the Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) and coupled-channel (CC) formalism, using the nuclear transition densities given by the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) approach and a complex CDM3Y6 density dependent interaction. Although AMD predicts a very weak transition strength for the direct $(0^+_1\to 2^+_2)$ excitation, our detailed analysis has shown evidence that a weak \emph{ghost} of the $2^+_2$ state could be identified in the 240 MeV \aap data for the $0^+_3$ state at 10.3 MeV, when the CC effects by the indirect excitation of the $2^+_2$ state are taken into account. Based on the same AMD structure input and preliminary \aap data at 386 MeV, we have estimated relative contributions from the $2^+_2$ and $0^+_3$ states to the excitation of $^{12}$C at $E_{\rm x}\approx 10$ MeV as well as possible contamination by $3^-_1$ state.

## Key claim

While the Hoyle state (0⁺₂ at 7.65 MeV in ¹²C) is readily observed in electron and α inelastic scattering, the second 2⁺ state at E_x ≈ 10 MeV, believed to be a rotational excitation of the Hoyle state, has remained elusive in most measurements; the authors show that a weak "ghost" of the 2⁺₂ state can be identified in the 240 MeV (α,α') data for the 0⁺₃ state at 10.3 MeV only when coupled-channel effects from indirect excitation of the 2⁺₂ state are included, and that at 386 MeV the 2⁺₂ strength is partially masked by contamination from the 3⁻₁ state.

## Method

The authors performed a microscopic folding model analysis of inelastic α+¹²C scattering data at E_α = 240 and 386 MeV using both Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) and coupled-channel (CC) formalisms. Nuclear transition densities for all relevant states (0⁺₁, 2⁺₁, 0⁺₂, 2⁺₂, 0⁺₃, 3⁻₁, 4⁺₁, 4⁺₂, 1⁻₁) were taken from antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) calculations, and the complex CDM3Y6 density-dependent nucleon-nucleon interaction was used to generate the double-folded optical potentials and inelastic form factors.

## Key numbers

- The AMD calculation predicts B(E2;0⁺₁→2⁺₂) ≈ 2 e²fm⁴ for the direct ground-state to 2⁺₂ transition, which is very weak compared to B(E2;0⁺₁→2⁺₁) ≈ 42.5 e²fm⁴.
- The predicted B(E2;0⁺₂→2⁺₂) ≈ 511 e²fm⁴ and B(E2;2⁺₂→4⁺₂) ≈ 1071 e²fm⁴ are much stronger than the ground-state band transitions, supporting the interpretation of the 2⁺₂ and 4⁺₂ states as members of a rotational band built on the Hoyle state.
- The ratio of integrated α+¹²C cross sections at 240 MeV is σ(2⁺₂)/σ(3⁻₁) ≈ 12.8% in DWBA and ≈ 14.8% in CC, consistent with the upper limit of about 15% from the ¹²C+¹²C experiment at 101.5 MeV.
- At 240 MeV, the 2⁺₂ cross section must be reduced to about 16% of its predicted strength (scaling the AMD transition density by a factor of 0.4) to keep the centroid of the combined 0⁺₃+2⁺₂ peak within the experimental value of 10.3 ± 0.3 MeV.
- At 386 MeV, the DWBA integrated cross section for the 2⁺₂ state at Θ_c.m. = 5.1° is dσ/dΩ ≈ 6.6 mb/sr, compared to the experimental value of about 13 ± 2 mb/sr; CC enhances this to 7.3 mb/sr, still below the data.
- The measured 386 MeV angular distribution for E_x ≈ 10 MeV requires up to 13% contamination from the 3⁻₁ state to match the CC prediction.

## Context

This work extends earlier folding model analyses of inelastic α scattering on ¹²C that used Resonating Group Method (RGM) wave functions (Kamimura 1981) to study the Hoyle state and other isoscalar excitations (Khoa 2008). It addresses the long-standing puzzle of why the 2⁺₂ state, predicted by cluster models (Funaki 2005, Kanada-En'yo 2007) and three-body calculations (Kato) as a rotational excitation of the Hoyle state, has been difficult to observe experimentally, despite high-precision measurements by the Texas A and M group (John 2003) at 240 MeV and Itoh et al. (2004, 2008) at 386 MeV. The results enable future experiments to better disentangle the 2⁺₂ state from the nearby 3⁻₁ and 0⁺₃ states, and suggest that the 2⁺₂ peak observed at 11.46 MeV in the 240 MeV data may correspond to the adopted 2⁺ level at 11.16 MeV seen only once in a ¹¹B(³He,d) reaction (Reynolds 1971).
