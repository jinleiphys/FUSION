# Nuclear structure properties and weak interaction rates of even-even Fe isotopes

> Nuclear structure properties and weak interaction rates of neutron rich even even iron (Fe) isotopes (A = 50 70) are investigated using the Interacting Boson Model1 (IBM1) and the proton neutron Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (pnQRPA) model. The IBM1 is used for the calculation of energy levels and the B(E2) values of neutron rich Fe isotopes. Later their geometry was predicted within the potential energy formalism of the IBM1 model. Weak interaction rates on neutron rich nuclei are needed for the modeling and simulation of presupernova evolution of massive stars. In the current study, we investigate the possible effect of nuclear deformation on stellar rates of even even Fe isotopes. The pnQRPA model is applied to calculate the weak interaction rates of selected Fe isotopes using three different values of deformation parameter. It is noted that, in general, bigger deformation values led to smaller total strength and larger centroid values of the resulting Gamow Teller distributions. This later translated to smaller computed weak interaction rates. The current finding warrants further investigation before it may be generalized.The reported stellar rates are up to 4 orders of magnitude smaller than previous calculations and may bear astrophysical significance.

# Nuclear structure properties and weak interaction rates of even-even Fe isotopes

## Key claim
The reported stellar weak interaction rates for even-even Fe isotopes are up to 4 orders of magnitude smaller than previous calculations, but the authors caution that this finding warrants further investigation before it may be generalized, as the correlation between deformation and rates was studied for only a limited set of nuclei.

## Method
The Interacting Boson Model-1 (IBM-1) was used to calculate energy levels, B(E2) values, and geometric shapes of even-even Fe isotopes (A = 50-70) by fitting Hamiltonian parameters to experimental data from the National Nuclear Data Center. The proton-neutron Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (pn-QRPA) model, with single-particle energies from the Nilsson model, was then applied to compute Gamow-Teller strength distributions and weak interaction rates (electron capture, beta decay, positron capture, and positron decay) using three different values of the nuclear deformation parameter: from IBM-1, from the Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM), and from measured B(E2) values.

## Key numbers
- The calculated stellar rates are up to 4 orders of magnitude smaller than previous calculations (Fuller et al. 1982, Langanke et al. 2001) at high core temperatures for even-even Fe isotopes.
- For $^{60}$Fe, the Large-Scale Shell Model (LSSM) calculated beta decay rates are up to 3 orders of magnitude bigger than the pn-QRPA results, and the difference with FFN rates at high T$_9$ values is up to 4 orders of magnitude.
- The Ikeda sum rule was satisfied to around 95% in the pn-QRPA calculation.
- The oscillator constant for nucleons was computed using $\hbar\omega = (45A^{-1/3} - 25A^{-2/3})$ MeV.
- The ratio $g_A/g_V$ was taken as -1.254 and the physical constant $F$ as 6295 s.

## Context
This work extends previous investigations of weak interaction rates on fp-shell nuclei using the pn-QRPA model, following studies by Nabi et al. (2020) on even-even chromium isotopes and earlier fine-grid stellar rate calculations by Nabi et al. (1999, 2004). The results contradict the larger rates reported by Fuller et al. (1982, 1985), Langanke et al. (1998, 2000), and the Large-Scale Shell Model calculations of Langanke et al. (2001), with differences attributed to the pn-QRPA treatment of excited states GT strength distributions versus the Brink-Axel hypothesis used in prior work. The findings enable improved modeling of presupernova evolution of massive stars, particularly for the electron capture and beta decay rates of Fe isotopes that Heger et al. (2001) rated among the most important nuclei for determining the lepton-to-baryon fraction in 15, 25, and 40 solar mass stars.
