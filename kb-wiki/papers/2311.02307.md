# Coalescence sum rule and the electric charge- and strangeness-dependences of directed flow in heavy ion collisions

> The rapidity-odd directed flows ($v_{\rm 1}$) of identified hadrons are expected to follow the coalescence sum rule when the created matter is initially in parton degrees of freedom and then hadronizes through quark coalescence. A recent study has considered the $v_{\rm 1}$ of produced hadrons that do not contain $u$ or $d$ constituent quarks. It has constructed multiple hadron sets with a small mass difference but given difference in electric charge $Δq$ and strangeness $ΔS$ between the two sides, where a nonzero and increasing $Δv_{\rm 1}$ with $Δq$ has been proposed to be a consequence of electromagnetic fields. In this study, we examine the consequence of coalescence sum rule on the $Δv_{\rm 1}$ of the hadron sets in the absence of electromagnetic fields. We find that in general $Δv_{\rm 1} \neq 0$ for a hadron set with nonzero $Δq$ and/or $ΔS$ due to potential $v_{\rm 1}$ differences between $\bar u$ and $\bar d$ and between $s$ and $\bar s$ quarks. We further propose methods to extract the coefficients for the $Δq$- and $ΔS$-dependences of the direct flow difference, where a nonzero constant term would indicate the breaking of the coalescence sum rule. The extraction methods are then demonstrated with transport model results.

# Coalescence sum rule and the electric charge- and strangeness-dependences of directed flow in heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

The coalescence sum rule predicts that the directed flow difference between two sides of a hadron set is generally nonzero when the electric charge difference and/or strangeness difference between the sides is nonzero, even in the complete absence of electromagnetic fields. A nonzero intercept term in the fit of the directed flow slope difference versus charge and strangeness differences would indicate breaking of the coalescence sum rule, not simply a nonzero slope versus charge difference as previously proposed.

## Method

The authors derived analytic relations from the coalescence sum rule connecting the directed flow difference of hadron sets to the constituent quark directed flows, using seven produced hadron species (K−, φ, p̅, Λ̅, Ξ̅⁺, Ω⁻, Ω̅⁺) that contain only u̅, d̅, s, and s̅ quarks. They constructed five independent hadron sets where the two sides have equal total numbers of u̅+d̅ quarks and equal total numbers of s+s̅ quarks, then proposed two fitting methods (a 5-set method and a 3-set method) to extract coefficients for the Δq and ΔS dependences of the directed flow slope difference. They demonstrated these methods using the default AMPT model for mid-central Au+Au collisions at five beam energies from 7.7 to 200 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Five independent hadron sets exist under the constraint of equal total u̅+d̅ and s+s̅ quark numbers between the two sides.
- The AMPT model simulations used mid-central (10-50%) Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 7.7, 14.5, 27, 54.4, and 200 GeV.
- The pt range was chosen as [0,2] GeV/c for mesons and [0,3] GeV/c for (anti)baryons for the model analysis.
- At 7.7 GeV, the nonzero intercept c0 from the AMPT results indicates breaking of the coalescence sum rule.
- The directed flow slope difference Δv1' was fit within |y| < 1.5 at each energy.

## Context

This work extends the study by referAshik (2023) and STAR (2023) which proposed that a nonzero directed flow difference increasing with electric charge difference Δq would be a consequence of electromagnetic fields. The present paper shows that the coalescence sum rule itself predicts nonzero Δv1 for hadron sets with nonzero Δq and/or ΔS due to potential v1 differences between u̅ and d̅ quarks and between s and s̅ quarks, providing a baseline for future experimental tests. The analysis builds on the coalescence sum rule formalism developed by Molnar (2003), Lin (2002, 2003, 2009), and the NCQ scaling approach, and uses the AMPT model of Lin (2005) for demonstration. This work enables experimental extraction of quark-level directed flow differences and identification of coalescence sum rule breaking, which could then be attributed to electromagnetic field effects in future studies.
