# Chemical potential (in)dependence of hadron scatterings in the hadronic phase of QCD-like theories and its applications

> We formulate a method for calculating the hadron-hadron scattering amplitudes at nonzero chemical potential ($μ$) in the hadronic phase at zero temperature, where the baryon number symmetry remains to be violated. Although it is widely believed that the physical quantities do not change even if we turn on a small $μ$ at zero temperature, the shape of correlation functions for a single hadron depends on $μ$. Then, the dispersion relation of the single hadron is modified to $E({\bf p},μ) = \sqrt{{\bf p}^2+m^2}-μn_{O}$. Here, $m$ and $n_O$ denote the hadron mass at $μ=0$ and the quantum number, respectively. From this relation, it is possible that the effective mass of the hadron depends on $μ$. We extend the HAL QCD method at $μ=0$ to the case of $μ\ne 0$, which allows us to extract the scattering phase shifts via the interaction potential. We have found that the interaction potential can depend on $μ$ only through the effective mass while the scattering phase shifts, obtained by solving the Schrödinger equation with the interaction potential, are independent of $μ$. We also numerically analyze the S-wave scatterings of two pions with isospin $I=2$ and two scalar diquarks within the framework of QC$_{2}$D at nonzero quark chemical potential. While the lattice is not exactly set to zero temperature, the $μ$-independence can be observed. Furthermore, we improve the results for the S-wave scatterings of two hadrons obtained above by taking the $μ$-independence for granted. Thanks to the asymmetric property of the correlation functions for diquarks at $μ\neq0$, we can access a long-$τ$ regime and can reduce the systematic error coming from inelastic contributions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that while single-hadron correlation functions and dispersion relations depend on chemical potential (μ) in the hadronic phase at zero temperature, the scattering phase shifts extracted via the HAL QCD method remain independent of μ as long as the system stays below the critical chemical potential where baryon number conservation is spontaneously broken.

## Method

The authors extend the time-dependent HAL QCD method, originally formulated at μ=0, to nonzero chemical potential by deriving the μ-dependent dispersion relation E(p,μ) = √(p²+m²) − μ n_O for single hadrons and showing that the R-correlator (ratio of four-point to two-point correlation functions) becomes μ-independent. They then perform lattice Monte Carlo simulations in two-colour QCD (QC₂D) with two flavours of Wilson fermions and Iwasaki gauge action, analyzing S-wave scatterings of two pions with isospin I=2 and two scalar diquarks at nonzero quark chemical potential, extracting interaction potentials via the leading-order local approximation and solving the Schrödinger equation to obtain phase shifts.

## Key numbers

- The critical chemical potential for QC₂D is μ_c = m_π/2, where m_π is the pion mass at μ=0 and equals the nucleon (diquark) mass m_N due to Pauli-Gürsey symmetry.
- The numerical simulation is performed at a temperature T ≈ 0.19 T_c, where T_c is the pseudo-critical temperature at μ_q=0.
- The dispersion relation for a single hadron at nonzero μ is E(p,μ) = √(p²+m²) − μ n_O, where n_O = 0 for mesons and n_O = N_c for baryons (or n_O = 1 for baryons when using baryon chemical potential).

## Context

This work extends the HAL QCD method (Ishii 2006, Aoki 2009) from zero chemical potential to finite μ, building on analytical studies of the Silver Blaze phenomenon (Cohen 2004) and lattice proofs of μ-independence of quark number density for 0 < μ_q < m_π/2 (Nagata 2012). The numerical demonstration in QC₂D, which is sign-problem-free at finite density, provides a testbed for understanding dense QCD and enables future applications to three-colour QCD if the sign problem can be overcome. The method also allows improvement of scattering parameter extraction by exploiting the asymmetric correlation functions for baryons at nonzero μ to access longer Euclidean times and reduce systematic errors from inelastic contributions.
