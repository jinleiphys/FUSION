# A note on the canonical approach to hydrodynamics and linear response theory

> This note provides a comprehensive examination of the various approaches to formulating relativistic hydrodynamics, with a particular emphasis on the canonical approach. Relativistic hydrodynamics plays a crucial role in understanding the behavior of fluids in high-energy astrophysical phenomena and heavy-ion collisions. The canonical approach is explored in detail, highlighting its foundational principles, mathematical formulations, and practical implications in modeling relativistic fluid dynamics. Following this, we delve into the linear response theory, elucidating its relevance in the context of hydrodynamics. We analyze the response of relativistic fluids to external perturbations, discussing the theoretical framework and key results. This dual focus aims to bridge the gap between theoretical foundations and practical applications, offering a robust perspective on the dynamic interplay between relativistic hydrodynamics and linear response theory.

## Key claim

This note argues that the canonical (gradient-expansion) approach to relativistic hydrodynamics, while foundational, faces fundamental problems with causality, stability, and the systematic inclusion of thermal fluctuations; these issues are not relevant for linear response theory in the hydrodynamic regime (ω, k → 0), which remains well-defined.

## Method

The authors review the canonical formulation of relativistic hydrodynamics as a perturbative expansion in the Knudsen number (Kn = l/L ≪ 1), where the stress-energy tensor and U(1) current are expressed as derivative expansions in the hydrodynamic fields (temperature T, chemical potential μ, and fluid four-velocity u^μ). They then examine the linear response of relativistic fluids to small perturbations around global thermodynamic equilibrium, focusing on the ω, k → 0 limit where causality and stability problems do not arise. The paper also surveys alternative formulations including kinetic theory, the Schwinger-Keldysh effective action, and fluid-gravity duality.

## Key numbers

* The Knudsen number condition for hydrodynamics is Kn = l/L ≪ 1, where l is the microscopic length scale and L is the macroscopic system size.
* In 3+1 dimensions, mode interactions due to thermal fluctuations introduce non-analytic corrections scaling as O(ω^(3/2)), which are larger than first-order (O(ω)) but smaller than second-order (O(ω^2)) corrections.
* In 2+1 dimensions, the fluctuation-induced correction is logarithmic rather than power-law.
* The KSS bound from holography gives η/s ≥ 1/(4π) for the ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density.

## Context

This note extends the pedagogical treatment of relativistic hydrodynamics found in Kovtun (2012) and the effective field theory approach of Glorioso and Liu (2018). It contrasts the canonical gradient-expansion method with the MIS (Müller-Israel-Stewart) second-order formulation, which introduces relaxation times τ_π, τ_Π, τ_ν to restore causality, and with the more recent BDNK frame approach (Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha, Kovtun, 2018-2022) that achieves causal first-order hydrodynamics. The paper enables early graduate students to understand why linear response theory remains valid despite foundational issues in the full nonlinear hydrodynamic equations.
