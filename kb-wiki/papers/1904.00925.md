# Comment on "Is a Trineutron Resonance Lower in Energy than a Tetraneutron Resonance?" [arXiv:1612.01502]

> The quantum Monte Carlo study [S. Gandolfi, H.-W. Hammer, P. Klos, J. E. Lynn, and A. Schwenk, Phys. Rev. Lett. {\bf 118}, 232501 (2017), arXiv:1612.01502] of few-neutron resonant states provided results incompatible with rigorous few-body calculations. In this Comment we point out serious shortcomings in the work by Gandolfi et al, leading to misinterpretation of unbound few-body systems.

## Key claim

The quantum Monte Carlo study by Gandolfi et al. (2017) claiming to find tetraneutron and trineutron resonances is invalid because its extrapolation procedure from bound to unbound systems ignores threshold effects and misidentifies discretized continuum states as bound states that would evolve into resonances.

## Method

Deltuva and Lazauskas analyzed the two-step strategy used by Gandolfi et al. (2017): (i) making the few-neutron system bound by adding an external trap potential or enhancing the nn interaction, then (ii) extrapolating the binding energy to the physical limit at zero added attraction. The authors examined the threshold for dineutron formation in these artificially bound systems and traced the pole trajectories of the 1S0 dineutron state in Wood-Saxon traps for both a realistic potential and the two-Gaussian potential used by Gandolfi et al. (2017).

## Key numbers

- A bound dineutron emerges in a trap with radius R_WS = 6 fm and potential depth V_0 ≈ -0.09 MeV only, or when the enhancement factor α in the 1S0 wave exceeds ≈ 1.1 (values slightly depend on the underlying nn potential).
- The 4n states declared to be bound tetraneutrons with E_4n → 0 in Gandolfi et al. (2017) were found only at significantly larger absolute values of V_0 ≈ -1.2 MeV and α ≈ 1.3, where dineutrons are already well bound.
- For a realistic potential, the 1S0 pole trajectory yields a virtual state energy of -0.12 MeV, in sharp contrast with the positive 0.1 MeV value reported by Gandolfi et al. (2017).

## Context

This Comment directly challenges the quantum Monte Carlo study by Gandolfi et al. (2017) on few-neutron resonant states, arguing its results are incompatible with rigorous few-body calculations by Lazauskas (2011) and Deltuva (2018). The work highlights that polynomial extrapolation procedures neglect branching-point discontinuities at thresholds, as previously discussed by Kukulin, and therefore are conceptually incorrect for extracting resonance energies from artificially bound systems.
