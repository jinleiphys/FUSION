# Examination of the calorimetric spectrum to determine the neutrino mass in low-energy electron capture decay

> The standard kinematic method for determining neutrino mass from the beta decay of tritium or other isotope is to measure the shape of the electron spectrum near the endpoint. It has been known for 30 years that a similar distortion of the "visible energy" remaining after electron capture is caused by neutrino mass. There has been a resurgence of interest in using this method with 163-Ho. Recent theoretical analyses offer reassurance that there are no significant theoretical uncertainties. We show that the situation is, however, more complicated, and that the spectrum shape is presently not well enough understood to permit a sensitive determination of the neutrino mass in this way. The theoretical analyses consider only single vacancy states in the daughter 163-Dy atom. It is necessary to consider configurations with more than one vacancy that can be populated owing to the change in nuclear charge. The shakeup and shakeoff theory of Carlson and Nestor is used as a basis for estimating the population of double-vacancy states. A spectrum of satellites associated with each primary vacancy created by electron capture is presented. The theory of the calorimetric spectrum is more complicated than has been described heretofore. There are numerous shakeup and shakeoff satellites present across the spectrum, and some may be very near the endpoint.

## Key claim

The calorimetric spectrum from electron capture in 163Ho is significantly more complicated than previously assumed, and the spectrum shape is presently not well enough understood to permit a sensitive determination of the neutrino mass in this way, contrary to recent theoretical analyses that offer reassurance that there are no significant theoretical uncertainties.

## Method

The author uses the shakeup and shakeoff theory of Carlson and Nestor (1973) as a basis for estimating the population of double-vacancy states in the daughter 163Dy atom following electron capture in 163Ho. Single-vacancy capture probabilities are adopted from Lusignoli and Vignati (2011), and the Carlson-Nestor probabilities for Xe are rescaled by a factor (54/66)^(2(2j+1)) to account for the difference in nuclear charge between Xe (Z=54) and Dy (Z=66). A spectrum of satellites associated with each primary vacancy created by electron capture is presented, considering configurations with one or two vacancies and an extra (4f7/2) electron.

## Key numbers

* The ground-state Q-value for 163Ho decay is in the vicinity of 2.5 keV, with recent studies indicating a value near 2.8 keV.
* The primary (3s)-1 vacancy line has an energy of 2041.8 eV with a width of 13.2 eV and relative intensity 100%.
* The (3s)-1(4s)-1 double vacancy satellite has an energy of 2474.2 eV with a width of 13.2 eV and relative intensity 0.034%.
* The (4s)-1(5s)-1 double vacancy satellite has an energy of 458.3 eV with a width of 5.4 eV and relative intensity 0.114%.
* The ECHo project has achieved 8.3-eV energy resolution with metallic magnetic calorimeters.
* If one were unaware of the spectral distortion and attempted to fit the spectrum near the endpoint with the standard shape, the result would be Q=2499 eV and m_nu^2 = -16 eV^2, far from the correct values (using Q=2500 eV and m_nu^2 = 1 eV^2 as input).

## Context

This work extends the original proposal by De Rujula and Lusignoli (1981, 1982) for using electron capture in 163Ho to measure neutrino mass, and directly challenges the reassurance offered by recent theoretical analyses that there are no significant theoretical uncertainties. The paper shows that the standard single-vacancy approximation used in prior work is insufficient, and that multiple-vacancy configurations (shakeup and shakeoff satellites) must be considered. This work enables future experimental programs (ECHo, NuMECS, HOLMES) to develop more sophisticated fitting procedures that account for the complex satellite structure, and calls for new, more quantitative relativistic theoretical calculations to match the improving experimental sensitivity.
