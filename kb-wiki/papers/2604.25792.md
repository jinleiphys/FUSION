# Exact emulation of few-body systems at low cost

> Effective field theories have established themselves as key pillars of modern nuclear physics. They enable a quantitative understanding of the strong nuclear force, provided low-energy constants that parametrize short-distance physics can be determined from experimental data. This, however, often becomes prohibitively expensive due to a significant computational cost of solving the A-body problem. The computational challenge is particularly severe for three-body forces, which are at the frontier of nuclear and atomic physics and play an important role in the equation of state of neutron stars. Here we prove that for a parametric low-rank update of a Hamiltonian, the A-body problem at a fixed energy exactly reduces to a low-dimensional matrix equation regardless of the size of the Hilbert space. As a proof-of-principle, we present exact and computationally cheap snapshot-based emulators for few-body scattering and bound states. Unlike alternatives, our emulators can be used far away from the snapshot region without loss of precision and yield accurate results for parameter values not accessible using conventional solution techniques. Our approach is not restricted by the interaction type, number of particles, and methods for generating snapshots and can be applied to mitigate the computational burden of the A-body problem to a broad class of problems in nuclear, atomic, and molecular physics.

## Key claim

The paper proves that for a parametric low-rank update of a Hamiltonian, the A-body problem at a fixed energy exactly reduces to a low-dimensional matrix equation regardless of the Hilbert space size, but this exact reduction for bound states is only valid for updates that preserve the binding energy.

## Method

The authors derive a Woodbury identity for the Lippmann-Schwinger equation (LSE) that shows a low-rank update of the potential leads to a low-rank update of the T-matrix. For scattering, they build snapshot-based emulators by computing r+1 linearly independent snapshots, orthonormalizing them, and projecting the Faddeev equation onto this reduced basis. For bound states, they perform singular value decomposition (SVD) of the Hamiltonian update into rank-1 terms, tune each rank-1 term to preserve the binding energy, and build a reduced basis from the resulting eigenvectors.

## Key numbers

- Speed-up of approximately 3000 times for chiral EFT two-nucleon potentials compared to direct solution of the LSE using 60 mesh points
- For three-body scattering with the full three-nucleon force depending on c_D and c_E, the achieved speed-up amounts to approximately 10^6
- The three-body Faddeev equation matrix size is approximately 10^5 x 10^5 in a partial-wave basis
- For the rank-1 update of the three-nucleon force with c_E, the original 10^5 x 10^5 matrix equation reduces to a 2 x 2 matrix equation
- For the two-parameter emulator with c_D and c_E, the Faddeev equation reduces from 10^5 x 10^5 to 30 x 30 without accuracy loss
- The c_D-interaction has rank 28 for the employed discretization setup
- The energy for three-body scattering is fixed to E_lab = 70 MeV
- The binding energy for the deuteron example is set to bar E = -2.22 MeV

## Context

This work extends the eigenvector continuation (EC) method introduced by Frame (2017) and other reduced basis method emulators developed by Witala (2021), Wesolowski (2021), Bonilla (2022), Garcia (2023), Sun (2024), Armstrong (2025), and Gnech (2025). Unlike these previous approaches that trade accuracy for speed and require sophisticated snapshot selection algorithms as in Sarkar (2021) and Maldonado (2025), the present method provides exact emulation without accuracy loss even far from the snapshot region. The approach enables efficient determination of low-energy constants in chiral effective field theory, particularly for three-nucleon forces at fifth chiral order which involve a large number of LECs as discussed by Girlanda (2011) and Huesmann (2026).
