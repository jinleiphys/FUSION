# Continuum Shell Model

> The Continuum Shell Model is an old but recently revived method that traverses the boundary between nuclear many-body structure and nuclear reactions. The method is based on the non-Hermitian energy-dependent effective Hamiltonian. The formalism, interpretation of solutions and practical implementation of calculations are discussed in detail. The results of the traditional shell model are fully reproduced for bound states; resonance parameters and cross section calculations are presented for decaying states. Particular attention is given to one- and two-nucleon reaction channels including sequential and direct two-body decay modes. New calculations of reaction cross sections and comparisons with experimental data for helium and oxygen isotope chains are presented.

## Key claim

The Continuum Shell Model (CSM) unifies nuclear many-body structure and reactions through a non-Hermitian energy-dependent effective Hamiltonian, fully reproducing traditional shell model results for bound states while correctly describing resonance parameters and cross sections for decaying states; however, the method requires an iterative Breit-Wigner definition for resonances rather than a direct eigenvalue condition, and the effective interaction parameters from the conventional shell model are used without consistent readjustment for the energy-dependent Hermitian part.

## Method

The authors decompose the many-body Hilbert space into an internal space of bound Slater determinants (P-space) and an external continuum space (Q-space) containing one or more nucleons in continuum orbitals. They eliminate the Q-space via Feshbach projection, producing a non-Hermitian energy-dependent effective Hamiltonian in the P-space whose anti-Hermitian part has a factorized form that guarantees unitarity of the scattering matrix. The decay amplitudes for one-body channels are computed from single-particle wave functions in a mean-field potential using regular Coulomb functions, while the many-body amplitudes combine these with spectroscopic overlaps from the shell model; two-body decay channels are treated similarly with antisymmetrized products of continuum states and residual nucleus eigenstates.

## Key numbers

- For neutral particles, the one-body decay width scales as \(\gamma \sim \epsilon^{l+1/2}\) above threshold, where \(\epsilon\) is the decay energy and \(l\) is the orbital angular momentum.
- For charged particles, the energy behavior of the decay amplitude follows from the regular Coulomb function, with the low-energy limit \(F_l \propto (kr)^{l+1} \propto \epsilon^{(l+1)/2}\).
- The self-energy term below threshold for neutral particles contains a contribution \(\pi \ae^2 \Theta(-\epsilon) \epsilon^l \sqrt{-\epsilon}\), where \(\ae\) is a constant determined by the potential and wave function overlap.
- The s.p. decay width from the outgoing flux normalization is \(\gamma = 2\pi a^2 = (k/\mu) |\mathcal{N}|^2\), where \(\mathcal{N}\) is the asymptotic normalization of the Siegert state.

## Context

This work extends the Feshbach projection formalism (Feshbach 1958, 1962) and the Weisskopf-Wigner approach (Weisskopf 1930) to modern nuclear structure calculations, building on earlier CSM developments by Volya (2003, 2004) and alternative formulations by Michel (2002, 2003) and Okolowicz (2003). The method enables self-consistent treatment of isotope chains with correct threshold behavior of resonance widths and cross sections, and is applied to helium and oxygen isotope chains with comparisons to experimental data; future work requires consistent readjustment of shell model interaction parameters to account for the energy dependence of the Hermitian self-energy term.
