# Quarks in the universe

> Matter in its present form was formed when our Universe emerged from the quark-gluon phase (QGP) at about 30mus into its evolution. To explore this early period in the laboratory, we study highly excited matter formed in relativistic heavy ion collision experiments: heavy nuclei crash into each other, and form compressed and energetically excited nuclear matter, resembling in its key features the stuff which filled the early Universe. In these experiments we further explore the physics of the vacuum structure of strongly interacting gauge theory, Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD). The common beginning for both, heavy ion collisions, and vacuum structure investigations, is the physics of the quantum electrodynamic (QED) vacuum in the presence of the supercritical external field that is formed when two highly charged heavy ions are brought together near to the Coulomb barrier in a considerably lower reaction energy collision.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) formed in relativistic heavy ion collisions provides a laboratory analog for the early Universe at about 30 microseconds after the Big Bang, and that studying the hadronization of QGP reveals how matter formed from energy in the expanding Universe. The hedge is that the experimental environment differs from the early Universe in several ways, particularly in baryon asymmetry, time constants, and unknown dark matter properties, which must be bridged by extrapolation.

## Method

The author reviews the theoretical framework of statistical hadronization models (SHM) applied to QGP formed in heavy ion collisions, where quark and gluon degrees of freedom in the deconfined phase combine into final-state hadrons. The method involves tracking chemical non-equilibrium parameters (γ_s, γ_q) alongside conserved quantum number chemical potentials (μ_B, μ_Q, μ_Y) to describe particle yields, and comparing strangeness-to-entropy ratios and strange antibaryon enhancements as signatures of QGP formation. The analysis draws on experimental data from AGS, SPS, and RHIC facilities, and extrapolates laboratory results to early Universe conditions using thermodynamic and cosmological equations.

## Key numbers

- QGP hadronization temperature: T_h = 160 MeV (early Universe) and T_h ≈ 170 ± 20 MeV (laboratory)
- Strange quark mass: 2m_s ≈ 190 ± 30 MeV, with m_s/T_h ≈ 0.7
- Strangeness-to-entropy ratio in QGP: N_s/S|_QGP ≈ 0.03
- Baryon-to-photon ratio in the Universe: η ≡ B/N_γ = (6.1 ± 0.15) × 10^{-10}
- Entropy per baryon in the Universe: S/B|_Universe = (1.3 ± 0.1) × 10^{10}
- Baryochemical potential at RHIC: μ_B = 24 MeV; in early Universe: μ_B = 1.1 eV
- Baryochemical potential to temperature ratio at hadronization in early Universe: μ_B/3T ≈ 2 × 10^{-9}
- QGP lifetime in laboratory: τ ≈ 3 × 10^{-23} s (based on nuclear size R = 6 fm and expansion velocity v = 0.6c)
- Universe expansion time constant at hadronization: τ_U ≈ 32 μs for energy density scale ε_0 = 1 GeV/fm^3
- γ_q hadronization value for equilibrated QGP: γ_q^HG ≈ e^{m_π/2T} ≈ 1.6
- γ_s hadronization value for equilibrated strange QGP: γ_s^HG ≈ 2.5

## Context

This paper extends the QED strong-field vacuum program initiated by Greiner (1969) into QCD vacuum modification, building on earlier theoretical work by Rafelski and collaborators (1980–1983) that proposed QGP formation and observability in heavy ion collisions. It connects to the statistical hadronization model reviewed by Torrieri (2004) and to experimental results compiled by the NA49 collaboration (Alt, 2006). The work enables further investigation of the QCD phase boundary under chemical non-equilibrium conditions, and opens questions about how dark matter (e.g., sterile neutrinos with mass m_νs < 15 keV as discussed by Biermann, 2006, or MeV-scale dark matter as discussed by Boehm, 2003) influenced the dynamics of the quark-hadron transition in the early Universe.
