# Dissipative corrections to particle spectra and anisotropic flow from a saddle-point approximation to kinetic freeze out

> A significant fraction of the changes in momentum distributions induced by dissipative phenomena in the description of the fluid fireball created in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions actually take place when the fluid turns into individual particles. We study these corrections in the limit of a low freeze-out temperature of the flowing medium, and we show that they mostly affect particles with a higher velocity than the fluid. For these, we derive relations between different flow harmonics, from which the functional form of the dissipative corrections could ultimately be reconstructed from experimental data.

## Key claim

The dissipative corrections to particle spectra and anisotropic flow that occur at the kinetic freeze-out stage (when the fluid turns into particles) can be studied analytically using a saddle-point approximation, and these corrections mostly affect particles with a higher velocity than the fluid. The authors derive relations between different flow harmonics from which the functional form of the dissipative corrections could be reconstructed from experimental data, but only in the limit of a low freeze-out temperature and for specific transverse momentum ranges where the saddle-point approximation holds.

## Method

The authors compute the Cooper-Frye integral for particle emission from a freeze-out hypersurface using a saddle-point approximation, which selects the fluid cell on the freeze-out surface where the product of particle four-momentum and fluid four-velocity is minimized. They classify particles as "slow" (transverse velocity \(p_t/m\) smaller than the maximum fluid transverse velocity \(u_{\max}\)) or "fast" (transverse velocity larger than \(u_{\max}\)), and analyze the dissipative corrections to the phase space distribution for each class separately. For fast particles, they expand the maximum transverse flow velocity in a Fourier series and derive analytic expressions for the flow harmonics \(v_1\) through \(v_5\) in terms of a dissipative function \(\mathcal{D}(p_t)\) and the flow anisotropy coefficients \(V_n\).

## Key numbers

- Typical value of \(\bar{u}_{\max}\) at midrapidity in heavy-ion collisions at maximum RHIC energy or at the LHC is about 1, which corresponds to \(\bar{v}_{\max} \simeq 0.7\).
- The Fourier coefficients \(V_n\) of the maximum transverse flow velocity are assumed to be small, of order 0.05 or smaller, with hierarchy \(V_2 \gtrsim V_3 \gg V_1, V_4, V_5\).
- For the numerical toy model: freeze-out temperature \(T_0 = 160\) MeV, cylinder radius \(R = 7.5\) fm, proper time \(\tau_{\text{f.o.}} = 5.25\) fm/\(c\), \(\bar{u}_{\max} = 0.55\), \(V_2 = 0.05\), \(V_3 = 0.05\), and ratio \(\eta/s = 0.16\).
- The saddle-point approximation for fast particles becomes reasonable above about 3 GeV/\(c\) and much better above 5 GeV/\(c\) for pions.
- The fast particle region for pions in the numerical model starts at \(p_t \gtrsim 0.6\) GeV/\(c\).

## Context

This work extends the saddle-point approach of Borghini (2005) for ideal fluid freeze-out to include dissipative corrections from shear and bulk viscosity, as well as conformal second-order effects. It bridges the gap between ideal fluid dynamics and Boltzmann transport calculations with few scatterings per particle, as studied by Borghini (2010). The results are compared to numerical computations using a blast-wave-like flow profile similar to that of Teaney (2003) and Huovinen (2001). The derived relations between flow harmonics could enable experimental constraints on the functional form of dissipative corrections at freeze-out, complementing earlier attempts by Luzum (2010) to extract such corrections from data.
