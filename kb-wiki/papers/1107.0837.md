# Proton-nucleus total reaction cross sections in the optical limit Glauber theory: Subtle dependence on the equation of state of nuclear matter

> We calculate the proton-nucleus total reaction cross sections at different energies of incident protons within the optical limit approximation of the Glauber theory. The isospin effect has been taken into account. The nucleon distribution is obtained in the framework of macroscopic nuclear models in a way depending on the equation of state of uniform nuclear matter near the saturation density. We find that at an energy of order 40 MeV, the reaction cross section calculated for neutron- rich isotopes significantly increases as the parameter L characterizing the density dependence of the symmetry energy increases, while at energies of order 300 and 800 MeV, it is almost independent of L. This is a feature of the optical limit Glauber theory in which an exponential dependence of the reaction cross section on the neutron skin thickness remains when the total proton-neutron cross section is small enough.

# Proton-nucleus total reaction cross sections in the optical limit Glauber theory: Subtle dependence on the equation of state of nuclear matter

## Key claim

The proton-nucleus total reaction cross section for neutron-rich isotopes calculated within the optical limit approximation of Glauber theory shows a significant increase with the symmetry-energy density-dependence parameter L at incident energies around 40 MeV, but is almost independent of L at 300 and 800 MeV. This behavior arises from an exponential dependence of the reaction cross section on the neutron skin thickness that persists when the total proton-neutron cross section is sufficiently small.

## Method

The authors used the optical limit approximation of Glauber theory to calculate proton-nucleus total reaction cross sections, incorporating isospin effects through separate proton and neutron profile functions. Nucleon density distributions were obtained from a macroscopic nuclear model based on an extended Thomas-Fermi approach, where the energy per nucleon of nuclear matter was expanded around saturation density in terms of the EOS parameters (saturation density n0, saturation energy w0, incompressibility K0, symmetry energy S0, and density symmetry coefficient L). The density distributions were parameterized as a function of radius with adjustable radii, diffuseness parameters, and central densities, then fitted to reproduce empirical masses and charge radii of stable nuclei for various combinations of L and K0. Reaction cross sections were computed via Monte Carlo integration of the phase-shift function for p-63Cu, p-80Ni, p-112Sn, p-124Sn, and p-208Pb at incident energies of 40, 300, and 800 MeV.

## Key numbers

- For p-80Ni at 40 MeV, the total reaction cross section for (L, K0) = (12.4, 350) MeV is 1248 mb, while for (L, K0) = (171.3, 360) MeV it is 1346 mb, a difference of about 7%.
- The neutron radius parameter for 80Ni is approximately an ≈ 5.4 + 0.3(L/170 MeV) fm, with the proton radius parameter ap ≈ 5.1 fm.
- The EOS parameters L and K0 were varied over ranges of 0 MeV < L < 175 MeV and 180 MeV ≤ K0 ≤ 360 MeV.
- The parameter b3 controlling the EOS at high density and large neutron excess was fixed at 1.58632 fm3 throughout the fitting.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Iida et al., 2007) that connected the EOS of nuclear matter to proton-nucleus elastic differential cross sections at 800 MeV, where a strong correlation between the peak angle in small momentum transfer and L was found but no strong correlation with total reaction cross sections. The present study examines the reaction cross section at multiple energies and reveals a significant L dependence at 40 MeV for neutron-rich isotopes, suggesting that low-energy proton reaction cross sections could constrain L if experimental uncertainties are below about 3.5%. The authors note that the validity of the optical limit Glauber approximation at these energies is not obvious, and that Coulomb distortion of the proton trajectory at the lowest energy has been ignored, pointing to alternative empirical approaches (Ishihara and Lee, 2008; Iida et al., 2007) as potentially more reliable for describing the energy and size dependence.
