# Towards First Detection of the Solar MSW Transition With JUNO

> Matter-induced neutrino flavor mixing (the Mikheyev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein, or MSW, effect) is a central prediction of the neutrino mixing framework, but it has not been conclusively observed. Direct observation of the energy-dependent MSW transition in the solar electron-neutrino survival probability would solve this, but backgrounds have been prohibitive. We show that our new technique for suppressing muon-induced spallation backgrounds will allow JUNO to measure the MSW transition at $>$4$σ$ significance in 10 years. This would strongly support upcoming multi-\$1B next-generation long-baseline experiments and their goals in cementing the neutrino mixing framework.

## Key claim
The authors show that a new neutron-tagging technique for suppressing muon-induced spallation backgrounds will allow JUNO to measure the energy-dependent MSW transition in solar neutrinos at >4σ significance in 10 years, provided the analysis threshold is raised to 2.3 MeV to account for previously underestimated ¹³N production.

## Method
The authors simulate JUNO's detection of solar ⁸B neutrinos via elastic scattering on electrons, using a 20-kton liquid scintillator target with 3%/√(E/MeV) energy resolution. They apply a new background rejection strategy that tags hadronic showers by detecting secondary neutrons (average yield ~14 neutrons per shower, at least one neutron 98% of the time) via their 2.2 MeV capture gamma rays, using cylindrical cuts of up to 6 m radius and 40 s for hadronic showers versus 3 m and 5 s for electromagnetic showers. Sensitivity is evaluated using a χ² test comparing the hypothesis of a constant survival probability against the MSW prediction, with systematic uncertainties on spallation yields conservatively doubled relative to FLUKA simulations.

## Key numbers
- JUNO will detect ⁸B solar neutrinos at a rate of ~80 events per day with a 20-kton target mass
- The new neutron-tagging method reduces deadtime from ~50% to ~10%, yielding a factor of ~2 gain in exposure compared to JUNO's baseline approach
- JUNO can reject the constant survival probability hypothesis at >4σ significance in 10 years
- JUNO will surpass the current combined SK+SNO result (2.1σ) in about two years
- The analysis threshold is set at 2.3 MeV (raised from 2.0 MeV) to remove ¹³N background with beta-decay endpoint at 2.2 MeV
- The detector energy resolution is 3% at 1 MeV with an energy-scale uncertainty of ~1%
- For hadronic showers, the average neutron yield is ~14 neutrons, with at least one neutron detected 98% of the time
- The spallation background rate is ~4000 events per day before cuts
- JUNO's measurement would restrict the effective NSI coupling ε_ee to the range (-0.2, 0.2) at 1σ and (-0.3, 0.4) at 3σ

## Context
This work extends the search for the MSW transition in solar neutrinos, which has remained inconclusive despite measurements from Borexino (2018) at low energies, Super-Kamiokande (2023) at high energies (1.2σ favoring an upturn), and SNO (2011) (0.7σ favoring a downturn), with combined significance of only 2.1σ. The new neutron-tagging technique builds on earlier work by Li et al. (2014, 2015) for Super-Kamiokande, adapted to JUNO's geometry and scintillator properties. A precise measurement of the MSW transition would break degeneracies that could otherwise limit DUNE's sensitivity to the neutrino mass ordering, which falls below 5σ for most values of δ_CP when NSI within currently allowed bounds are considered, but would be restored to >5σ for all δ_CP with JUNO's constraint.
