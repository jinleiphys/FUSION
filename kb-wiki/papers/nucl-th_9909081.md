# Selected Topics in Astro-Hadron Physics: Going from a Proton to Nuclei to Neutron Stars

> The effort currently in vogue in some small circle of physicists to go from a proton to nuclei to compact stars involves various aspects of particle and nuclear physics that require input from laboratory experiments, transcending narrow specialization in diverse sub-fields. Several topics on this matter are discussed in this note. The notion of Cheshire Cat Principle is introduced for nucleons, nuclei and dense hadronic matter and is confronted with experimental data on the proton, two-nucleon systems and heavy-ion experiments, with a leaping extrapolation to the structure of neutron stars. The matter discussed here illustrates that a close contact with experiments, indispensable in the present case, is essential for significant progress in {\it any} field of physics. I suggest that this is a field that has a tremendous potential for breakthrough in the Asia Pacific countries, particularly in Korea, where some of the seminal works in this area have been done by young theorists, both pre- and post-graduate.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a "Cheshire Cat Principle" provides an approximate equivalence between descriptions of hadronic matter in terms of quarks and gluons (QCD variables) and in terms of macroscopic hadronic variables (pions, nucleons, mesons), and that this duality extends from single protons through nuclei to neutron star matter. The author explicitly notes that this is an embryonic stage with "false starts, pitfalls, wrong tracks and controversies," and that much work will be needed to validate or invalidate the claims.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical developments in astro-hadron physics, focusing on the Cheshire Cat Principle applied to the proton (including the flavor-singlet axial charge), effective field theory (EFT) for two-nucleon systems using the "Lambda counting" scheme of Weinberg, and Brown-Rho (BR) scaling for dense hadronic matter. The BR scaling approach assumes that nucleons and mesons in nuclear matter behave as quasiparticles with masses that scale with density according to the in-medium quark condensate, and the author tests this against experimental data from heavy nuclei, heavy-ion collisions, and neutron star observations.

## Context

This paper extends the Cheshire Cat concept from earlier work by Rho, Brown, and collaborators (e.g., NRZ, 1980s) to connect hadron physics with astrophysics, specifically neutron star structure. It builds on effective field theory approaches for two-nucleon systems developed by Weinberg (1990s), Park, and collaborators (PKMR, 1990s), and on the BR scaling introduced by Brown and Rho. The work enables a unified framework for interpreting heavy-ion collision data (e.g., CERES dilepton measurements at CERN) and for predicting kaon condensation in neutron stars, linking laboratory experiments to compact object physics.
