# Microscopic three-cluster study of light exotic nuclei

> I develop a microscopic three-cluster model for exotic light nuclei. I use the hyperspherical formalism, associated with the Generator Coordinate Method. This model is well adapted to halo nuclei, since the long-range part of the radial wave functions is accurately reproduced. The core wave functions are described in the shell model, including excited states. This technique provides large bases, expressed in terms of projected Slater determinants. Matrix elements involve seven-dimension integrals, and therefore require long calculation times. I apply the model to 11Li, 14Be, 15B, and 17N described by two neutrons surrounding a 9Li, 12Be, 13B and 15N core, respectively. The 17Ne (as 15O+p+p) and 15Ne (as 13O+p+p) mirror nuclei are briefly discussed. I present the spectra and some spectroscopic properties, such as r.m.s. radii or E2 transition probabilities. I also analyze the importance of core excitations.

## Key claim

The paper develops a microscopic three-cluster model using the hyperspherical formalism and Generator Coordinate Method that can be applied to heavier exotic nuclei beyond those with an alpha core, but the calculations are extremely time-consuming and require parallelization, with the model's success depending on tuning the Majorana parameter of the Volkov interaction to reproduce experimental two-neutron separation energies.

## Method

The author constructs a fully antisymmetrized A-body wave function as a product of three cluster internal wave functions (a p-shell core and two neutrons) multiplied by a radial function depending on relative coordinates, using hyperspherical coordinates to reduce the degrees of freedom to a single hyperradius. The core wave functions are described in the shell model including excited states, with up to 90 Slater determinants for 9Li, and matrix elements involve seven-dimensional numerical integrals computed via a double projection over hyperangles. The Volkov V2 nucleon-nucleon interaction with a zero-range spin-orbit force is used, and the generator coordinate R is varied from 1.5 to 15 fm with a step of 1.5 fm, with maximum hypermomentum Kmax=16.

## Key numbers

- Two-neutron separation energies reproduced by tuning the Majorana parameter M: 0.370 MeV for 11Li (M=0.7750), 1.27 MeV for 14Be (M=0.6119), 3.75 MeV for 15B (M=0.5945), and 8.37 MeV for 17N (M=0.6177)
- 14Be 2+ resonance energy: 0.25 MeV theoretical, compared to experimental 0.28 ± 0.01 MeV
- 14Be ground-state matter radius: 2.96 fm theoretical, compared to experimental 3.16 ± 0.38 fm
- 11Li ground-state matter radius: 2.85 fm theoretical, compared to experimental 3.27 ± 0.24 fm
- 17Ne binding energy: -1.05 MeV theoretical, compared to experimental -0.93 MeV
- B(E2,1/2- → 5/2-) in 17Ne: 92.9 e²·fm⁴ theoretical, compared to 90 ± 18 e²·fm⁴ experimental (Marganiec et al. 2016)
- B(E2,1/2- → 3/2-) in 17Ne: 68.0 e²·fm⁴ theoretical, compared to 68+19-25 e²·fm⁴ experimental (Chromik et al. 1997)
- Number of Slater determinants for cores: 90 for 9Li, 15 for 12Be, 20 for 13B, and 6 for 15N
- Oscillator parameter b = 1.60 fm used for all calculations
- Spin-orbit amplitude S0 = 50 MeV·fm⁵ for 11Li and 40 MeV·fm⁵ for other nuclei

## Context

This work extends microscopic three-cluster models beyond previous applications limited to alpha-particle cores (such as 6He studied by Koremov and Descouvemont in 2004 and 8Li studied by Descouvemont and Piraux in 2019) to heavier p-shell core nuclei including 9Li, 12Be, 13B, and 15N. The model enables systematic study of halo nuclei with two-neutron halos, and the results show that non-microscopic three-body models (such as those by Descouvemont, Tursunov, and Baye in 2006 for 14Be) fail to reproduce both ground and excited state energies simultaneously because they neglect core excitations. The calculations also address the question of whether 17Ne has a proton halo, confirming the earlier conclusion of Tursunov, Descouvemont, and Baye (1996) that there is no evidence for such a halo.
