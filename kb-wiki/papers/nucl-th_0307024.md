# Determination of $S_{17}$ from systematic analyses on $^8$B Coulomb breakup with the Eikonal-CDCC method

> A new version of the method of Continuum-Discretized Coupled-Channels method (CDCC) is proposed, that is, the Eikonal-CDCC method (E-CDCC). The E-CDCC equation, for Coulomb dissociation in particular, can easily and safely be solved, since it is a first-order differential equation and contains no huge angular momentum in contrast to the CDCC one. The scattering amplitude calculated by E-CDCC has a similar form to that by CDCC. Then one can construct hybrid amplitude in an intuitive way, i.e., CDCC amplitude is adopted for a smaller angular momentum $L$ and E-CDCC one for a larger $L$ related to an impact parameter $b$. The hybrid calculation is found to perfectly reproduce the quantum mechanical result for $^{58}$Ni($^8$B,$^7$Be$+p$)$^{58}$Ni at 240 MeV, which shows its applicability to systematic analysis of $^8$B dissociation to extract the astrophysical factor $S_{17}$ with high accuracy.

# Determination of $S_{17}$ from systematic analyses on $^8$B Coulomb breakup with the Eikonal-CDCC method

## Key claim

The hybrid calculation combining standard CDCC for small angular momenta and the new Eikonal-CDCC (E-CDCC) for large angular momenta reproduces the full quantum-mechanical result for $^{58}$Ni($^8$B,$^7$Be$+p$)$^{58}$Ni at 240 MeV with less than 1% error, enabling systematic analysis of $^8$B dissociation to extract the astrophysical factor $S_{17}(0)$ with the required 5% accuracy. The method is specifically intended to overcome the practical impossibility of applying standard CDCC to high-energy data such as the GSI measurement at 250 MeV/nucleon, where more than 100,000 partial waves would be required.

## Method

The authors propose the Eikonal-CDCC method (E-CDCC), which treats the center-of-mass motion between projectile and target using a straight-line (eikonal) approximation while explicitly treating projectile excitation via discretized-continuum states as in standard CDCC. The E-CDCC equation is a first-order differential equation containing no huge angular momentum, making it easy and safe to solve. A hybrid scattering amplitude is constructed by using standard CDCC amplitudes for smaller angular momentum $L$ and E-CDCC amplitudes for larger $L$ (larger impact parameter $b$), with the connection point $L_{\rm C}$ chosen so that the two coincide for $L > L_{\rm C}$; Coulomb distortion is included by using Coulomb wave functions instead of plane waves in the eikonal approximation.

## Key numbers

- The hybrid calculation reproduces the quantum-mechanical result for $^{58}$Ni($^8$B,$^7$Be$+p$)$^{58}$Ni at 240 MeV with an error of less than 1% for both elastic and total breakup cross sections.
- The standard CDCC calculation for the MSU data requires approximately 15,000 partial waves; for the GSI data at 250 MeV/nucleon, more than 100,000 partial waves would be required.
- The modelspace for the test calculation used 16, 8, and 8 bin-states for s-, p-, and d-states of $^8$B respectively, with maximum excitation energy 10 MeV, $r_{\rm max}=100$ fm, $R_{\rm max}=500$ fm, and $L_{\rm max}=1000$ (compared to the convergence value of $L_{\rm max}\sim 4000$).
- The extracted $S_{17}(0)$ from a previous CDCC+ANC analysis of $^{58}$Ni($^8$B,$^7$Be$+p$)$^{58}$Ni at 25.8 MeV (Notre Dame data) was 22.83 $\pm$ 0.51 (theo) $\pm$ 2.28 (expt) eVb.

## Context

This work extends the CDCC method developed by the Kyushu group (CDCC, 1986; CDCC-foundation) to high-energy Coulomb breakup where standard CDCC becomes computationally prohibitive. The hybrid approach is designed to enable systematic analysis of $^8$B dissociation data from RIKEN (Motobayashi et al.), GSI, and MSU, which are used to extract the astrophysical factor $S_{17}(0)$ for the $^7$Be($p,\gamma$)$^8$B reaction relevant to the solar neutrino problem. The method is intended to be combined with the ANC method previously proposed by Yamashita et al. (2003) to determine $S_{17}(0)$ free from uncertainties in the detailed balance principle, and will enable quantitative evaluation of theoretical errors from the choice of $^8$B single-particle wave functions.
