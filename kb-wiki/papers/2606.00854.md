# Three- and four-boson systems expanded around the unitarity limit: Application to $^4$He

> The three- and four-boson systems with a large scattering length and a short effective range in the two-body sector are studied in the framework of Short-Range Effective Field Theory. The starting point (leading order) of the EFT is taken to be the universal unitarity limit, where the two-body sector is parameter-free and only one three-body parameter enters. In this limit, physical systems manifests discrete scale invariance. Deviations from universality arising from finite scattering-length and effective-range corrections, as well as a four-body force required by renormalization, are included perturbatively at next-to-leading order. The three-body ground state and its associated four-body ground and first-excited states are studied using the Faddeev-Yakubovsky formalism and a complementary diagrammatic approach. By employing techniques to remove contributions from deep trimers in tetramer calculations, we extend our analysis to larger cutoffs than previously accessible within the FY approach. Our results for binding energies and radii of $^4$He three- and four-atom systems converge well to results obtained with sophisticated phenomenological potentials. These successes suggest that the physics of $^4$He atomic clusters is governed by only small deviations from discrete scale invariance.

## Key claim

The authors show that the three- and four-boson systems of \(^4\)He atoms can be accurately described by a perturbative expansion around the unitarity limit in Short-Range Effective Field Theory, with only small deviations from discrete scale invariance governing the physics, provided that finite scattering-length, effective-range, and four-body-force corrections are included at next-to-leading order.

## Method

The authors solve the Faddeev-Yakubovsky (FY) equations and a complementary diagrammatic approach with auxiliary fields for three- and four-boson systems in Short-Range Effective Field Theory. At leading order, the two-body system is tuned to the unitarity limit (infinite scattering length, zero effective range) with a single three-body parameter; at next-to-leading order, finite scattering length and effective range corrections plus a four-body force are included perturbatively using distorted-wave perturbation theory. To access larger momentum cutoffs than previously possible, the authors introduce a pseudopotential projection method to remove unphysical deep trimers from the three-body spectrum in tetramer calculations.

## Key numbers

- The \(^4\)He dimer binding energy is approximately \(B_2 \simeq 1/(2 m a_2^2)\), where \(a_2\) is the large scattering length and \(m\) the atom mass.
- The ratio of binding energies between successive Efimov trimers is approximately 515, corresponding to a truncated geometric tower.
- The four-body excited state lies approximately 4.6 times more deeply bound than the corresponding Efimov trimer threshold (referencing Hammer (2006) and Deltuva (2010)).
- The LO three-body LEC \(C_0^{(0)}\) is fixed as \(C_0^{(0)} = -4\pi/(m \theta_1 \Lambda)\), where \(\theta_1\) is a regulator-dependent constant and \(\Lambda\) the momentum cutoff.
- The NLO two-body LECs are given by \(C_2^{(1)}/C_0^{(0)} = (m C_0^{(0)}/(4\pi)) (r_2/2 + \theta_{-1}/\Lambda)\) and \(C_0^{(1)}/C_0^{(0)} = (m C_0^{(0)}/(4\pi)) (\theta_3 \Lambda^3 C_2^{(1)}/C_0^{(0)} - 1/a_2)\), where \(a_2\) is the scattering length and \(r_2\) the effective range.

## Context

This work extends the perturbative expansion around the unitarity limit previously applied to nuclear few-body systems by Konig (2015, 2016, 2019) to \(^4\)He atomic clusters, which are an ideal testbed due to their proximity to two-body unitarity. It builds on prior EFT studies of \(^4\)He clusters by Bedaque (1998), Platter (2004), Bazak (2016, 2018), and Lin (2023), and on four-body calculations by Hammer (2006) and Deltuva (2010). The results enable systematic predictions for binding energies and radii of three- and four-body \(^4\)He systems with controlled theoretical uncertainties, and establish a framework for studying larger clusters and other systems near unitarity.
