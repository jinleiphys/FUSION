# 2013 Update of the discoveries of nuclides

> The 2013 update of the discovery of nuclide project is presented. Details of the 12 new nuclides observed for the first time in 2013 are described. In addition, the discovery of 266Db has been included and the previous assignments of 6 other nuclides were changed. Overview tables of where and how nuclides were discovered have also been updated and are discussed.

## Key claim

The 2013 update documents 12 new nuclides observed for the first time in refereed journals during 2013, plus the inclusion of 266Db and reassignment of 6 other nuclides, but the author notes that the criteria for discovery assignments are "certainly debatable" and the definition of what constitutes a nucleus (nuclides existing for more than about 10^-22 s) is "not well defined."

## Method

The project applies consistent criteria for nuclide discovery: clear identification through decay curves, particle or gamma-ray spectra, or unique mass and element identification, and publication in a refereed journal. For the 2013 update, the author compiled newly reported nuclides from projectile fragmentation, fusion-evaporation, and secondary beam reactions at facilities including MSU, RIKEN, GSI, Jyvaskyla, and Dubna, and re-evaluated prior assignments based on a systematic review of historical literature.

## Key numbers

- 12 new nuclides were reported in refereed journals in 2013
- 266Db was accepted as discovered, with a spontaneous fission half-life of 22^(+105)_(-10) min
- 3195 different nuclides have been discovered overall as of end of 2013
- 1271 proton-rich nuclides and 1197 neutron-rich nuclides have been discovered
- The current 2013 discovery rate is 32.9 nuclides/year, the largest since 2001
- 22.0 neutron-rich nuclides per year were discovered, equaling the largest rate first reached in 1998
- 205Ac was synthesized with alpha-decay energy 7.935(30) MeV and half-life 20^(+97)_(-9) ms
- 15Be decays by neutron emission to 14Be with a decay energy of 1.8(1) MeV
- 197Fr was identified with E_alpha = 7728(15) keV and T_1/2 = 0.6^(+3.0)_(-0.3) ms
- 198Fr showed two alpha-particle activities with E_alpha = 7613(15) keV and T_1/2 = (15^(+12)_(-5)) ms and E_alpha = 7684(15) keV and T_1/2 = (16^(+13)_(-5)) ms

## Context

This work continues the systematic documentation of all nuclide discoveries initiated by Thoennessen (2004) and expanded through a series of papers on individual elements completed in 2011. The update extends the timeline figures from the previous review (Thoennessen, 2013) and provides revised demographic tables of discoveries by country, laboratory, researcher, and method. The project enables tracking of discovery trends and identifies that next-generation fragmentation facilities (FAIR at GSI and FRIB at MSU) will not be ready until at least the end of the decade, with RIKEN's RIBF expected to produce most new nuclides in the near term.
