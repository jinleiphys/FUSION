# Three Lectures on Hadron Physics

> These lectures explain that comparisons between experiment and theory can expose the impact of running couplings and masses on hadron observables and thereby aid materially in charting the momentum dependence of the interaction that underlies strong-interaction dynamics. The series begins with a primer on continuum QCD, which introduces some of the basic ideas necessary in order to understand the use of Schwinger functions as a nonperturbative tool in hadron physics. It continues with a discussion of confinement and dynamical symmetry breaking (DCSB) in the Standard Model, and the impact of these phenomena on our understanding of condensates, the parton structure of hadrons, and the pion electromagnetic form factor. The final lecture treats the problem of grand unification; namely, the contemporary use of Schwinger functions as a symmetry-preserving tool for the unified explanation and prediction of the properties of both mesons and baryons. It reveals that DCSB drives the formation of diquark clusters in baryons and sketches a picture of baryons as bound-states with Borromean character. Planned experiments are capable of validating the perspectives outlined in these lectures.

## Key claim

Comparisons between experiment and theory can expose the impact of running couplings and masses on hadron observables, thereby aiding in charting the momentum dependence of the strong interaction; however, QCD is not currently known to be a rigorously well-defined theory, and the task of hadron physics is to determine whether QCD is truly the theory of the strong interaction.

## Method

The lectures use Schwinger functions (Green functions) as a nonperturbative tool in continuum QCD, deriving Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) from the field equations of quantum field theory via functional integral methods. The derivation is illustrated for the photon vacuum polarisation in QED, showing how the generating functional and Legendre transformation yield one-particle-irreducible (1PI) Green functions, whose functional derivatives define propagators and their inverses. The approach is then applied to QCD to study confinement, dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB), condensates, parton structure, and the pion electromagnetic form factor, and extended to baryons where DCSB drives diquark cluster formation.

## Key numbers

* The QCD running coupling at one-loop order is given by α_QCD(Q) = 6π / [(33 - 2N_f) ln(Q/Λ_QCD)], where Λ_QCD ∼ 300 MeV is the natural mass-scale of QCD.
* The QED running coupling at one-loop order is α_QED(Q) = α / [1 - (2α/3π) ln(Q/m_e)], with α and m_e renormalised at the physical electron mass Q = m_e.
* On the same momentum domain, the QCD coupling changes 500,000 times more than the QED coupling and runs in the opposite direction.
* The coupling is found to be huge at a separation r ≃ 1/4 r_p, where r_p is the proton's charge radius.
* No confirmed breakdown of QCD exists over the energy domain 0 < E < 8 TeV.

## Context

These lectures extend the continuum QCD approach using Schwinger functions and Dyson-Schwinger equations, building on the foundational work of Dyson (1949) and Schwinger (1951) and the development of perturbative QCD by Politzer (1973, 1974) and Gross (1973). The perspective enables a unified treatment of mesons and baryons, showing that DCSB drives diquark clustering in baryons and produces a Borromean bound-state picture, which can be tested by planned experiments at facilities such as JLab (upgrade expected 2017), RHIC, and a future electron ion collider (EIC).
