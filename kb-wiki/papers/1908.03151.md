# Real-Time Detection of Gravitational Waves from Binary Neutron Stars using Artificial Neural Networks

> The groundbreaking discoveries of gravitational waves from binary black-hole mergers and, most recently, coalescing neutron stars started a new era of Multi-Messenger Astrophysics and revolutionized our understanding of the Cosmos. Machine learning techniques such as artificial neural networks are already transforming many technological fields and have also proven successful in gravitational-wave astrophysics for detection and characterization of gravitational-wave signals from binary black holes. Here we use a deep-learning approach to rapidly identify transient gravitational-wave signals from binary neutron star mergers in noisy time series representative of typical gravitational-wave detector data. Specifically, we show that a deep convolution neural network trained on 100,000 data samples can rapidly identify binary neutron star gravitational-wave signals and distinguish them from noise and signals from merging black hole binaries. These results demonstrate the potential of artificial neural networks for real-time detection of gravitational-wave signals from binary neutron star mergers, which is critical for a prompt follow-up and detailed observation of the electromagnetic and astro-particle counterparts accompanying these important transients.

## Key claim
A deep convolutional neural network trained on 100,000 simulated data samples can rapidly identify binary neutron star gravitational-wave signals and distinguish them from detector noise and signals from merging black hole binaries, but only for signals with optimal signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of at least 18 for complete detection of both classes.

## Method
The author generated simulated gravitational-wave time series of 10-second duration sampled at 4096 Hz using the LIGO Algorithm Library (LALSuite). Binary neutron star (BNS) signals used the PhenomPNRT waveform model with component masses from 1 to 2 solar masses and tidal deformabilities computed with the APR equation of state, while binary black hole (BBH) signals used the SEOBNRv2 model with component masses from 5 to 50 solar masses and zero spin. A one-dimensional convolutional neural network with 4 convolutional layers, 4 pooling layers, and 2 dense layers was trained on 100,000 samples (one-third each BNS+noise, BBH+noise, and noise only) using curriculum learning where SNR was gradually decreased from 20 down to 3.

## Key numbers
- Training set: 100,000 independent time series; validation and testing sets each: 5,000 independent samples
- BNS component mass range: 1 to 2 solar masses; BBH component mass range: 5 to 50 solar masses
- Time series duration: 10 seconds sampled at 4096 Hz (input vector dimension: 40,960)
- Network achieves maximal true alarm probability for BNS signals at optimal SNR of 18
- All signals (both BNS and BBH) are detected for optimal SNR of 18 or greater
- Trained model size: approximately 83 MB, compressing about 6.6 × 10^4 waveforms (total size about 10.4 GB)
- Processing 10 seconds of gravitational-wave data takes milliseconds on both CPUs and GPUs after training

## Context
This work extends the application of deep learning for gravitational-wave detection from binary black holes (pioneered by George and Huerta (2017) and Gabbard et al. (2017)) to the specific case of binary neutron star mergers. The approach addresses the computational limitations of traditional template matched-filtering techniques, which become computationally prohibitive when extended to the full 9D parameter space of compact binary sources (including spins and orbital eccentricity) as noted by Huerta et al. (2019). The results enable rapid real-time detection that could trigger prompt electromagnetic follow-up observations of kilonovae and other multi-messenger counterparts, which require localization within hours of the merger due to their fast decay rates.
