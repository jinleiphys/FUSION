# The rigidity of crystalline color superconducting quark matter

> We calculate the shear modulus of crystalline color superconducting quark matter, showing that this phase of dense, but not asymptotically dense, three-flavor quark matter responds to shear stress like a very rigid solid. To evaluate the shear modulus, we derive the low energy effective Lagrangian that describes the phonons that originate from the spontaneous breaking of translation invariance by the spatial modulation of the gap parameter $Δ$. These massless bosons describe space- and time-dependent fluctuations of the crystal structure and are analogous to the phonons in ordinary crystals. The coefficients of the spatial derivative terms of the phonon effective Lagrangian are related to the elastic moduli of the crystal; the coefficients that encode the linear response of the crystal to a shearing stress define the shear modulus. We analyze the two particular crystal structures which are energetically favored over a wide range of densities, in each case evaluating the phonon effective action and the shear modulus up to order $Δ^2$ in a Ginzburg-Landau expansion, finding shear moduli which are 20 to 1000 times larger than those of neutron star crusts. The crystalline color superconducting phase has long been known to be a superfluid -- by picking a phase its order parameter breaks the quark-number $U(1)_B$ symmetry spontaneously. Our results demonstrate that this superfluid phase of matter is at the same time a rigid solid. We close with a rough estimate of the pinning force on the rotational vortices which would be formed embedded within this rigid superfluid upon rotation. Our results raise the possibility that (some) pulsar glitches could originate within a quark matter core deep within a neutron star.

## Key claim

The crystalline color superconducting phase of dense three-flavor quark matter is simultaneously a superfluid and a rigid solid, with shear moduli 20 to 1000 times larger than those of neutron star crusts, but this result is obtained within a Ginzburg-Landau expansion where the control parameter Δ/(M_s²/8μ) is about 1/2, meaning the expansion is pushed to or beyond the boundary of its regime of quantitative validity.

## Method

The authors derive a low-energy effective Lagrangian for the phonon modes that arise from spontaneous breaking of translation invariance by the spatially modulated diquark condensate, using a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with a point-like four-fermion interaction. They evaluate the shear modulus up to order Δ² in a Ginzburg-Landau expansion for two specific crystal structures (CubeX and 2Cube45z) by relating the coefficients of spatial derivative terms in the phonon effective action to the elastic moduli, integrating out the fermions to obtain the effective action for displacement fields.

## Key numbers

- The crystalline phases are favored (lower free energy than unpaired quark matter or uniform paired phases) for 2.9 Δ₀ < M_s²/μ < 10.4 Δ₀, where Δ₀ is the gap parameter in the CFL phase with M_s=0 and is expected to lie between 10 and 100 MeV
- The shear moduli of the crystalline phases are 20 to 1000 times larger than those of conventional neutron star crusts
- The quark number chemical potential μ in neutron star cores is expected to be in the range 350-500 MeV
- The Fermi momenta of the three quark flavors are split by δμ = M_s²/(8μ), with the u quark Fermi momentum at μ - M_s²/(6μ), the d quark at μ + M_s²/(12μ), and the s quark at μ - 5M_s²/(12μ)
- The lattice spacing for these crystal structures is many tens of fm

## Context

This work extends the analysis of crystalline color superconducting phases first explored for three-flavor quark matter by Rajagopal (2006), who identified the CubeX and 2Cube45z structures as having particularly low free energy. The paper demonstrates that these phases, previously known to be superfluids due to spontaneous breaking of U(1)_B symmetry, also behave as rigid solids, enabling the possibility that pulsar glitches could originate within a quark matter core deep inside a neutron star rather than only in the inner crust. The authors provide a rough estimate of the pinning force on rotational vortices, finding it comparable to that for neutron vortices in a neutron star crust, and note that the cooling properties of these phases were first studied by Anglani (2006).
