# Transfer reactions with the Lagrange-mesh method

> We apply the $R$-matrix method in Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) calculations. The internal wave functions are expanded over a Lagrange mesh, which provides an efficient and fast technique to compute matrix elements. We first present an outline of the theory, by emphasizing the $R$-matrix aspects. The model is applied to the 16O(d,p)7Li and 12C(7Li,t)16O reactions, typical of nucleon and of $α$ transfer, respectively. We illustrate the sensitivity of the cross sections with respect to the $R$-matrix parameters, and show that an excellent convergence can be achieved with relatively small bases. We also discuss the effects of the remnant term in DWBA calculations, and address the question of the peripherality in transfer reactions. We suggest that uncertainties on spectroscopic factors could be underestimated in the literature.

## Key claim

The Lagrange-mesh R-matrix method provides an efficient and fast technique for DWBA transfer-reaction calculations, achieving excellent convergence with relatively small bases (N~30-40 points), but the authors caution that uncertainties on spectroscopic factors in the literature could be underestimated, particularly due to remnant-term effects and peripherality questions.

## Method

The authors apply the R-matrix method within the Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA), expanding internal wave functions over a Lagrange mesh associated with Gauss quadrature. The configuration space is divided into internal and external regions separated by a channel radius a; in the internal region, scattering wave functions are expanded over Legendre-based Lagrange functions, while bound-state wave functions use Laguerre-based Lagrange functions. The method is applied to the 16O(d,p)17O reaction (neutron transfer) at 25.4 and 36 MeV and the 12C(7Li,t)16O reaction (alpha transfer) at 28 and 34 MeV, with sensitivity tests on R-matrix parameters and remnant-term effects.

## Key numbers

- For the 16O(d,p)17O reaction, convergence of the transfer cross section is achieved for channel radius a >= 15 fm with N ~ 40 basis functions, compared to "several hundreds" of points in finite-difference methods like Numerov
- At Ed = 25.4 MeV, the 16O(d,p)17O cross section difference with and without the remnant term reaches up to 30% at large angles
- For the 12C(7Li,t)16O reaction, spectroscopic factors used are 0.13 for the 0+2 state (Ex = 6.05 MeV) and 0.15 for the 2+1 state (Ex = 6.92 MeV) of 16O
- The deuteron ground-state wave function uses a Gaussian potential: Vnp(r) = -72.66 exp[-(r/1.484)2] MeV
- The constant hbar2/2mN = 20.9 MeV.fm2 is used throughout

## Context

This work extends the R-matrix method combined with Lagrange-mesh theory (Descouvemont 2010, Baye 2015) to transfer reactions, providing an alternative to finite-difference methods used in codes like FRESCO (Thompson 1988). The 16O(d,p)17O reaction was previously studied by Childs et al. (1974) and more recently by Moro et al. (2019), while the 12C(7Li,t)16O reaction has been used in indirect measurements of the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O cross section (Barker et al. 1999, Oulebsir et al. 2012), which is crucial for stellar models of carbon and oxygen abundances after helium burning. The method enables faster calculations than traditional approaches and can be extended to more complex coupled-channel calculations such as CDCC.
