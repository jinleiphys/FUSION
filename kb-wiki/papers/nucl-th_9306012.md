# Dirac sea effects in $K^+$ scattering from nuclei

> The ratio $R_T$ of $K^+-^{12}C$ to $K^+-d$ cross sections has been calculated microscopically using a boson-exchange $KN$ amplitude in which the $σ$ and $ω$ mesons are dressed by the modifications of the Dirac sea in nuclear matter. In spite of the fact that this dressing leads to a scaling of the mesons effective mass in nuclear matter, the effect on the $R_T$ ratio is found to be weak.

## Key claim
The authors find that dressing the σ and ω mesons by modifications of the Dirac sea in nuclear matter produces a scaling of the mesons' effective masses, but the effect on the ratio R_T of K⁺–¹²C to K⁺–d total cross sections is weak; the improvement in agreement with experiment is not appreciable.

## Method
The K⁺–nucleus optical potential was built by folding a density-dependent K⁺–nucleon t-matrix (calculated using the Bonn boson-exchange model) with nuclear densities deduced from electron scattering. The dressing of the σ and ω mesons was included at the one-loop approximation by modifying the nucleon effective mass in nuclear matter (linearly decreasing with density, with a 15% reduction at saturation density ρ₀), and the free-space Dirac sea polarization was subtracted via renormalization. The total K⁺–nucleus cross section was obtained from the forward elastic scattering amplitude using the optical theorem, and the ratio R_T was computed for kaon momenta from 400 MeV/c to 900 MeV/c.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies (e.g., K. Saito and A. W. Thomas, 1992; G. E. Brown and M. Rho, 1991) that suggested modifications of the Dirac sea could explain discrepancies in K⁺–nucleus scattering, and it directly tests the scaling law proposed by Brown and Rho. The authors show that while the effective masses of σ and ω mesons scale with the nucleon effective mass, the forward-angle dominance of the K⁺–nucleus interaction means the dressing has little impact on the R_T ratio, contradicting the expectation that such scaling would improve agreement with experiment. This result enables future work to focus on other in-medium effects (e.g., changes in the nucleon radius or meson-nucleon couplings) to resolve the remaining discrepancies.
