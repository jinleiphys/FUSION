# Polarized fermions in one dimension: density and polarization from complex Langevin calculations, perturbation theory, and the virial expansion

> We calculate the finite-temperature density and polarization equations of state of one-dimensional fermions with a zero-range interaction, considering both attractive and repulsive regimes. In the path-integral formulation of the grand-canonical ensemble, a finite chemical potential asymmetry makes these systems intractable for standard Monte Carlo approaches due to the sign problem. Although the latter can be removed in one spatial dimension, we consider the one-dimensional situation in the present work to provide an efficient test for studies of the higher-dimensional counterparts. To overcome the sign problem, we use the complex Langevin approach, which we compare here with other approaches: imaginary-polarization studies, third-order perturbation theory, and the third-order virial expansion. We find very good qualitative and quantitative agreement across all methods in the regimes studied, which supports their validity.

## Key claim
The complex Langevin (CL) method produces density and polarization equations of state for one-dimensional polarized fermions that agree very well with third-order perturbation theory, imaginary-polarization hybrid Monte Carlo (iHMC), and third-order virial expansion, but the CL results for repulsive couplings exhibit distributions with non-finite variance, and the CL method requires careful control of the fictitious-time step to avoid systematic deviations.

## Method
The authors placed the one-dimensional Fermi gas with zero-range interaction on a spacetime lattice and used a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to obtain a path-integral representation of the grand-canonical partition function. They applied complex Langevin (CL) stochastic quantization with a dynamical stabilization term to handle the sign problem arising from finite chemical potential asymmetry, and compared those results against lattice perturbation theory at third order (N3LO), the third-order virial expansion, and imaginary-polarization hybrid Monte Carlo (iHMC) with analytic continuation (for attractive interactions only). The CL calculations used lattices up to N_x = 61 spatial sites and N_tau = 160 temporal sites with temporal spacing tau = 0.05, evolved for 10^5 iterations with the first 10% discarded for thermalization.

## Key numbers
- Density equation of state at dimensionless coupling |lambda| = 1 (attractive and repulsive) shows agreement between CL, perturbation theory, iHMC, and virial expansion across beta mu from approximately -4 to +4 and beta h from 0 to 2.0.
- For attractive coupling lambda = 1, the CL density results agree with iHMC data from Loheac (2015) and with third-order perturbation theory within uncertainties shown in the figures.
- At stronger coupling |lambda| = 2, agreement between CL, perturbation theory, and virial expansion deteriorates compared to |lambda| = 1, but the overall comparison is described as satisfactory.
- The CL time-step sensitivity study at |lambda| = 1 and beta h = 2.0 shows that as dt_CL approaches zero, the remaining difference between CL and third-order perturbation theory is reduced by a factor of 3 relative to the largest dt_CL shown.
- Virial coefficients b_2 and b_3 used in the expansion are tabulated for lambda = -2.0, -1.0, 0, 1.0, 2.0; for example, at lambda = -1.0 (repulsive), b_2 = -0.490(5) and b_3 = 0.394(5); at lambda = 1.0 (attractive), b_2 = -0.0375(5) and b_3 = -0.240(5).

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous CL studies of unpolarized one-dimensional fermions (Loheac 2017) and their iHMC studies of polarized attractively interacting fermions (Loheac 2015) to include both attractive and repulsive polarized cases with a broader set of validation methods. The one-dimensional system serves as a testbed for methods that are applicable to higher-dimensional polarized Fermi gases (such as the unitary Fermi gas in three dimensions), where the sign problem prevents standard Monte Carlo approaches and where inhomogeneous superfluid phases remain poorly understood beyond mean-field approximations (as reviewed by Casalbuoni 2004 and Gubbels 2013). The results enable future CL calculations in higher dimensions and for non-trivial coupling strengths, though the non-finite variance issue for repulsive couplings requires further investigation.
