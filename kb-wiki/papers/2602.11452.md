# A study of charged-particle multiplicity distribution in high energy p-O collisions

> This study investigates the multiplicity distribution of charged particles generated in $p$-O collisions, employing Pythia (Angantyr) and $k_T$-factorization approach. Oxygen nucleus configurations are sampled using a $α$-cluster model to evaluate both formalisms and assess how initial nucleus configuration influences the properties of the produced final states. Results obtained through clustering are systematically compared to those derived from the Woods-Saxon nuclear distribution. The analysis encompasses various pseudorapidity intervals ($|η|<$ 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0) and center-of-mass energies ($\sqrt{s}=$ 2.36, 5.02, 7.0, 13.0 TeV). Based on the resulting distributions, we examine the KNO scaling effect and fit the distributions with the double NBD model for parameterization, aiming to accurately characterize the observed results and elucidate contributions from both soft and semi-hard processes. Our results indicate that different geometric descriptions of the oxygen nucleus project significantly different multiplicities of charged particles, especially for large multiplicities and higher pseudorapidity. We also observed that multiplicity of charged particles calculated with Pythia reveals significantly different behavior from that calculated with $k_T$-factorization.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the geometric description of the oxygen nucleus (alpha-cluster versus Woods-Saxon) produces significantly different charged-particle multiplicity distributions in p-O collisions, particularly at high multiplicities and larger pseudorapidity intervals, but this conclusion is conditional on using Pythia (Angantyr) simulations; the kT-factorization approach yields substantially different results from Pythia, so the geometric sensitivity claim applies primarily within the Pythia framework.

## Method

The authors simulated p-O collisions using Pythia (Angantyr) version 8.313 and a kT-factorization approach based on the Color Glass Condensate effective field theory, with the unintegrated gluon distributions obtained from the running-coupling Balitsky-Kovchegov equation. Oxygen nucleus configurations were sampled using two descriptions: an alpha-cluster model with four tetrahedrally arranged clusters (edge 3.42 fm) each described by a three-parameter Fermi distribution, and a Woods-Saxon distribution with parameters R=2.608 fm and a=0.513 fm. Collisions were simulated at center-of-mass energies of 2.36, 5.02, 7.0, and 13.0 TeV across pseudorapidity intervals |eta| < 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, and 3.0, and the resulting distributions were analyzed for KNO scaling and fitted with a double Negative Binomial Distribution model.

## Key numbers

- Alpha-cluster tetrahedral geometry: edge length of 3.42 fm between cluster centers, obtained via Extended Quantum Molecular Dynamics
- Woods-Saxon parameters for oxygen: R = 2.608 fm, a = 0.513 fm, w = 0
- Alpha-cluster three-parameter Fermi distribution parameters: R = 0.964 fm, a = 0.322 fm, w = 0.517
- Minimum distance of 0.9 fm maintained between nucleons within the same oxygen nucleus to avoid overlap
- Center-of-mass energies simulated: 2.36, 5.02, 7.0, and 13.0 TeV
- Pseudorapidity intervals analyzed: |eta| < 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0
- Saturation scale fluctuation parameter sigma = 0.5 used in kT-factorization calculations, following McLerran (2016)

## Context

This work extends phenomenological studies of multiplicity distributions in small collision systems, building on prior investigations of p-Pb and pp collisions by ALICE (2017, 2025), CMS (2011, 2018), and ATLAS (2011, 2016). The study directly compares two nuclear geometry descriptions for oxygen, following recent work by Ding (2023) and Sharma (2025) on alpha-clustering effects, and contrasts two theoretical formalisms (collinear factorization via Pythia/Angantyr and kT-factorization from the CGC framework). The results enable future comparisons with upcoming LHC Run 3 p-O collision data to discriminate between nuclear structure models and between different QCD evolution formalisms for small systems.
