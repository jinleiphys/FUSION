# Influence of fusion dynamics on fission observables: A multi-dimensional analysis

> An attempt to unfold the respective influence of the fusion and fission stages on typical fission observables, and namely the neutron pre-scission multiplicity, is proposed. A four-dimensional dynamical stochastic Langevin model is used to calculate the decay by fission of excited compound nuclei produced in a wide set of heavy-ion collisions. The comparison of the results from such a calculation and experimental data is discussed, guided by predictions of the dynamical deterministic HICOL code for the compound-nucleus formation time. While the dependence of the latter on the entrance-channel properties can straigthforwardly explain some observations, a complex interplay between the various parameters of the reaction can occur in other cases. A multi-dimensional analysis of the respective role of these parameters, including entrance-channel asymmetry, bombarding energy, compound-nucleus fissility, angular momentum and excitation energy, is proposed. It is shown that, depending on the size of the system, apparent unconsistencies may be deduced when projecting onto specific ordering parameters. The work suggests the possibility of delicate compensation effects in governing the measured fission observables, thereby highlighting the necessity of a multi-dimensional discussion.

## Key claim
The paper attempts to disentangle the influence of the fusion stage from the fission stage on measured neutron pre-scission multiplicities, but the authors emphasize that their work does not provide a complete unified dynamical model of fusion-fission; the dynamical codes are run separately for the entrance and exit channels, and the outcome of the fusion code is not explicitly fed into the subsequent treatment of compound-nucleus decay, making the investigation qualitative at some level.

## Method
The fusion dynamics was calculated using the deterministic HICOL code, which solves classical equations of motion with one-body dissipation in a three-dimensional shape space to estimate compound-nucleus formation times. The fission stage was modeled using a four-dimensional dynamical stochastic Langevin code that treats shape coordinates (elongation, neck constriction, left-right asymmetry) and the projection of angular momentum onto the symmetry axis, with particle evaporation included via Monte Carlo methods. The HICOL formation-time predictions were used to interpret discrepancies between the Langevin calculations (which assume Bohr's hypothesis of compound-nucleus decay independent of formation) and experimental neutron pre-scission multiplicities for a wide set of heavy-ion reactions.

## Key numbers
- For the reaction couple leading to $^{216}$Ra, the difference in formation time $t_{fo}$ between $^{19}$F+$^{197}$Au ($\alpha/\alpha_{BG} < 1$) and $^{12}$C+$^{204}$Pb ($\alpha/\alpha_{BG} > 1$) at $E^* \approx 61$ MeV varies from 20 to 50 $\times 10^{-22}$ s over the angular momentum range populated.
- The difference in experimental neutron pre-scission multiplicity between $^{19}$F+$^{197}$Au and $^{12}$C+$^{204}$Pb leading to $^{216}$Ra is about 0.5 neutron.
- For $^{248}$Cf formed in $^{11}$B+$^{237}$Np and $^{16}$O+$^{232}$Th collisions, the calculated initial compound-nucleus angular momentum distributions show "quite some difference" between the two projectile-target combinations, but the calculated $M_n^{pre}$ is not sensitive to angular momentum due to the high fissility of the CN.
- The compound nuclei studied have excitation energies exceeding about 40 MeV, with angular momentum populations ranging from about 10 to 80 $\hbar$.

## Context
This work extends the statistical-model-based systematics of Saxena (1993) and Shareef (2016), who attempted to separate fusion and fission time scales from neutron pre-scission multiplicities but could not achieve complete separation. By using dynamical models for both the entrance channel (HICOL) and the fission decay (stochastic Langevin), the authors aim to move beyond the one-dimensional projections used in prior work and demonstrate that apparent inconsistencies in measured neutron multiplicities can arise from compensation effects among entrance-channel asymmetry, bombarding energy, compound-nucleus fissility, angular momentum, and excitation energy. The study enables a multi-dimensional framework for interpreting fission observables and highlights the need for caution when extrapolating conclusions from one mass region to another.
