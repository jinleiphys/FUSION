# Exact results on the two-particle Green's function of a Bose-Einstein condensate

## Key claim

The two-particle Green's function for an interacting Bose-Einstein condensate can be expressed exactly in terms of unambiguously defined self-energies and vertices, and its poles are not shared with (i.e., are shifted from) those of the single-particle Green's function, contrary to the long-standing conclusion of Gavoret and Nozières (1964) and subsequent dielectric formalism studies.

## Method

Starting from the Dyson-Beliaev equation and the generalized Gross-Pitaevskii equation with an extra nonlocal potential, the author applies the functional-differentiation method to generate the two-particle Green's function from the single-particle Green's function. By introducing irreducible vertices derived from a single functional Phi, the linear responses of the Green's function and condensate wave function to the external potential are computed, yielding a closed-form expression for the two-particle Green's function in terms of the quasiparticle propagator chi^(q) and the condensate propagator chi^(c).

## Key numbers

- The two-particle Green's function K is expressed as: K = chi^(q) + chi^(4) Gamma^(3) chi^(c) Psi^(3) + Psi^(3) chi^(c) Gamma^(3) chi^(4) - Psi^(3) chi^(c) Psi^(3) (Equation ucalk)
- In the weak-coupling limit, Gamma^(2)_ii = 0 and Gamma^(2)_12(1,2) = -Gamma^(2)*_21(1,2) = -2V(r1 - r2) Psi(r1) Psi(r2)
- At the mean-field level, Sigma_12 + Gamma_12 = V(r1 - r2)[-Psi(r1) Psi(r2) - <phi(1) phi(2)>]

## Context

This work extends the functional-differentiation method previously applied to normal systems by Martin and Schwinger (1959), Baym and Kadanoff (1961), and Baym (1962) to the case of a Bose-Einstein condensate with broken U(1) symmetry. It directly contradicts the conclusion of Gavoret and Nozières (1964) that poles of the single-particle and two-particle Green's functions coincide, a result that had been reproduced by the dielectric formalism of Szépfalusy and Kondor (1974), Wong and Gould (1974), and Griffin (1993). The formalism enables practical calculations of the two-particle Green's function using the self-consistent Phi-derivable approximation developed by Kita (2009), and is applicable to trapped atomic gases through its coordinate-space formulation.
