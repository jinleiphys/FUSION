# Neutron Resonance Data Exclude Random Matrix Theory

> Almost since the time it was formulated, the overwhelming consensus has been that random matrix theory (RMT) is in excellent agreement with neutron resonance data. However, over the past few years, we have obtained new neutron-width data at Oak Ridge and Los Alamos National Laboratories that are in stark disagreement with this theory. We also have reanalyzed neutron widths in the most famous data set, the nuclear data ensemble (NDE), and found that it is seriously flawed, and, when analyzed carefully, excludes RMT with high confidence. More recently, we carefully examined energy spacings for these same resonances in the NDE using the $Δ_{3}$ statistic. We conclude that the data can be found to either confirm or refute the theory depending on which nuclides and whether known or suspected p-wave resonances are included in the analysis, in essence confirming results of our neutron-width analysis of the NDE. We also have examined radiation widths resulting from our Oak Ridge and Los Alamos measurements, and find that in some cases they do not agree with RMT. Although these disagreements presently are not understood, they could have broad impact on basic and applied nuclear physics, from nuclear astrophysics to nuclear criticality safety.

## Key claim

New neutron-width data from Oak Ridge and Los Alamos, as well as a reanalysis of the nuclear data ensemble (NDE), show that neutron resonance data exclude random matrix theory (RMT) with high confidence, contradicting the long-standing consensus that the two are in excellent agreement. The authors conclude that the NDE is seriously flawed and that, depending on which nuclides and whether known or suspected p-wave resonances are included, the data can either confirm or refute the theory.

## Method

The authors performed new neutron resonance measurements at ORELA (Oak Ridge) and LANSCE (Los Alamos) on isotopes of Pt and Sm, extracting reduced neutron widths and total radiation widths via R-matrix analyses of capture and total cross sections. They applied maximum-likelihood (ML) analyses using an energy-dependent threshold of the form T = a*E_n to separate s-wave from p-wave resonances, and tested whether the reduced neutron widths follow the Porter-Thomas distribution (a chi-squared distribution with one degree of freedom). They also reanalyzed the NDE by performing separate ML analyses for each nuclide with individual thresholds, and examined the Delta_3 statistic for resonance energy spacings using Monte Carlo simulations based on GOE matrices.

## Key numbers

- For 147Sm resonances below 350 eV, the ML estimate of the chi-squared degrees of freedom was nu_n = 0.91 ± 0.32 (consistent with PTD); for resonances between 350 and 700 eV, nu_n = 3.19 ± 0.83 (significant disagreement).
- For 192Pt, nu_n = 0.57 ± 0.16; for 194Pt, nu_n = 0.47 ± 0.19; both exclude the PTD by nearly three standard deviations.
- Combined 192,194Pt data exclude the PTD with at least 99.997% confidence.
- For 196Pt, nu_n = 0.60 ± 0.28 (consistent with 192,194Pt but with reduced statistical precision).
- Weighted average of ML results for the 24 NDE nuclides at minimum thresholds: nu_n = 0.801 ± 0.052, which is 3.8 standard deviations below the PTD value of 1.0 (99.98% confidence).
- After removing p-wave contamination from the NDE, weighted average nu_n = 1.217 ± 0.092, excluding the PTD at 98.17% confidence.
- For 147Sm Gamma_gamma distributions: lower-energy region (below ~300 eV) had mean = 52.0 ± 1.1 meV and standard deviation = 4.67 ± 0.81 meV; upper-energy region had mean = 59.6 ± 2.0 meV and standard deviation = 11.7 ± 1.5 meV.
- Translated to chi-squared degrees of freedom for Gamma_gamma: nu_gamma = 248 ± 87 (lower region) and 52 ± 14 (upper region).
- Median test rejects null hypothesis that medians are equal at 99.8% confidence; variance test rejects at 99.9% confidence for the two 147Sm energy regions.

## Context

This work directly contradicts the long-standing consensus that neutron resonance data agree with RMT, which was established primarily through analyses of the NDE by Haq et al. (1982) and Bohigas et al. (1983). The authors show that the NDE contains significant p-wave contamination and that previous analyses failed to account for experimental limitations properly. The results challenge the universality of the Porter-Thomas distribution and RMT predictions for nuclear resonances, with potential implications for nuclear astrophysics and nuclear criticality safety. The findings have already stimulated new theoretical work by Weidenmuller (2010), Celardo et al. (2011), Volya (2011), and Sokolov and Zelevinsky (2012), some of which relates to earlier work by Kleinwachter and Rotter (1985) on non-Hermitian Hamiltonians.
