# A Bayesian Inference of Hybrid Stars with Large Quark Cores

> Neutron stars (NSs) are interesting objects capable of reaching densities unattainable on Earth. The properties of matter under these conditions remain a mystery. Exotic matter, including quark matter, may be present in the NS core. In this work, we explore the possible compositions of NS cores, in particular, the possible existence of large quark cores. We use the Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) model with nonlinear terms for the hadron phase and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model and Mean Field Theory of Quantum Chromodynamics (MFTQCD) for the quark phase. Through Bayesian inference, we obtain different sets of equations: four sets with hybrid equations and one set with only the hadron phase. We impose constraints regarding the properties of nuclear matter, X-ray observational data from NICER, gravitational wave data from the binary neutron star merger GW170817, perturbative QCD (pQCD) calculations, and causality. The MFTQCD allows for a phase transition to quark matter at low densities, just above saturation density, while for the NJL sets, the phase transition occurs above twice the saturation density. As a result, the MFTQCD model predicts the presence of quark matter in the inner core of 1.4 M$_\odot$ NSs, while NJL models suggest a low probability of quark matter in the interior of a 1.4 M$_\odot$ NS. Both models predict the existence of quark matter in 2 M$_\odot$ NSs. The slope of the mass-radius curve has been shown to carry information about the presence of quark matter. In particular, a positive slope at 1.8 M$_\odot$ indicates the presence of non-nucleonic matter. A hybrid star with a stiff quark equation of state could explain a larger radius in more massive stars, such as two solar mass stars, compared to canonical NSs.

## Key claim

The paper claims that hybrid stars with large quark cores are possible under current observational constraints, but the existence and onset density of quark matter depend strongly on the quark model used: the MFTQCD model allows phase transitions just above saturation density (enabling quark cores in 1.4 solar mass neutron stars), while the NJL model requires densities above twice saturation density (making quark matter unlikely in canonical 1.4 solar mass neutron stars).

## Method

The authors performed Bayesian inference to sample parameters of the Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) model with nonlinear terms for the hadron phase, combined with either the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model or the Mean Field Theory of Quantum Chromodynamics (MFTQCD) for the quark phase. They constructed hybrid equations of state using Maxwell construction for the phase transition and imposed constraints from nuclear matter properties, NICER X-ray observations, gravitational wave data from GW170817, perturbative QCD calculations at 7 times saturation density, and causality. Five equation-of-state sets were generated: four hybrid sets (three using NJL with different priors and one using MFTQCD) and one purely hadronic set.

## Key numbers

- Phase transition density for MFTQCD: approximately 0.170 fm^-3 (minimum of 90% credible interval), very close to nuclear saturation density (rho_0 approximately 2.7 x 10^14 g cm^-3)
- Phase transition density for NJL sets: approximately twice the saturation density
- MFTQCD model predicts quark matter in the inner core of 1.4 solar mass neutron stars
- NJL models suggest low probability of quark matter in 1.4 solar mass neutron stars
- Both models predict quark matter in 2 solar mass neutron stars
- A positive slope of the mass-radius curve at 1.8 solar masses indicates the presence of non-nucleonic matter
- 1,000 live points used in nested sampling; obtained 6514, 4879, 6037, 7521, and 5327 samples for NJL, MFTQCD, RMF, NJL-GW, and r-NJL sets respectively

## Context

This work extends previous Bayesian analyses of hybrid stars by simultaneously sampling both hadron and quark phase parameters, building on earlier studies such as Albino (2024) where the hadron phase was described by a fixed RMF equation. The results enable future identification of quark matter signatures through mass-radius curve features, particularly the slope at high masses, and provide testable predictions for multi-messenger observations from NICER and gravitational wave detectors. The approach contrasts with purely hadronic models and earlier hybrid star studies that used fixed hadronic equations of state.
