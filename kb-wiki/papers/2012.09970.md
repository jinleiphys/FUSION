# CFNS Ad-Hoc meeting on Radiative Corrections Whitepaper

> Current precision scattering experiments and even more so many experiments planed for the Electron Ion Collider will be limited by systematics. From the theory side, a fundamental source of systematic uncertainty is the correct treatment of radiative effects. To gauge the current state of technique and knowledge, help the cross-pollination between different direction of nuclear physics, and to give input to the yellow report process, the community met in an ad-hoc workshop hosted by the Center for Frontiers in Nuclear Science, Stony Brook University. This whitepaper is a collection of contributions to this workshop.

## Key claim
The community whitepaper argues that while progress is being made on theoretical radiative corrections for lepton-nucleon scattering, the correct treatment of these effects remains a fundamental source of systematic uncertainty that will limit current precision experiments and especially those planned for the Electron Ion Collider, and that radiative corrections are often only an afterthought in detector design.

## Method
The paper collects contributions from an ad-hoc workshop hosted by the Center for Frontiers in Nuclear Science at Stony Brook University, covering multiple aspects of radiative corrections: development of Monte Carlo generators for lepton-proton scattering (ELRADGEN), dispersive calculations of two-photon exchange contributions using hadronic intermediate states, exact calculations of radiative corrections to semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering (SIDIS) cross sections, and discussions of software needs for the Electron Ion Collider.

## Key numbers
- The total two-photon exchange correction δ reaches approximately 6%-7% at low ε for Q² = 5 GeV² when including nucleon and all spin-1/2± and spin-3/2± resonances with mass below 1.8 GeV
- The radiative correction factor for SIDIS can change from 1.05 to 0.85 between z = 0.2 and 0.8 for the same x and Q²
- The radiative correction factor for SIDIS is 0.95 without and 1.4 with the exclusive radiative tail for M_x² = 1.5 GeV², and 1.05 without and 1.3 with for M_x² = 3.0 GeV²
- The estimate of the cos(3φ_h) azimuthal moment from radiative corrections in SIDIS is about 5% for E_beam = 10 GeV, Q² = 2.5 GeV², x = 0.25, z = 0.2, and p_t = 0.7 GeV
- The OLYMPUS experiment measured the ratio R_{2γ} = σ(e⁺p)/σ(e⁻p) over a range of ε from approximately 0.46 to 0.9 at electron energy E ≈ 2 GeV, with Q² ranging up to approximately 2 GeV²

## Context
This whitepaper extends the long-standing work on radiative corrections in electron scattering dating back to Mo and Tsai (1969) and the infrared catastrophe treatment by Bloch and Nordsieck (1937). It addresses the form factor ratio discrepancy between Rosenbluth separation experiments (Walker 1994, Andivahis 1994, Qattan 2005) and polarization transfer measurements (Jones 2000, Gayou 2002, Punjabi 2005, Puckett 2010, 2012), which motivated studies of two-photon exchange corrections by Blunden (2003) and Guichon (2003). The paper enables future precision measurements at the Electron Ion Collider by identifying needed developments in Monte Carlo generators, exact radiative correction formulae for SIDIS, and iteration procedures for extracting structure functions from data.
