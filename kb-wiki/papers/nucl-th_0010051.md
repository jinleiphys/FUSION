# At the edge of nuclear stability: nonlinear quantum amplifiers

## Key claim

Nuclei at the edge of stability can act as nonlinear quantum amplifiers, where a tiny change in the nucleon-nucleon interaction (on the order of 0.4% or less) produces a much larger change in the binding energy relative to the few-cluster breakup threshold. The effect is demonstrated specifically for the $0^+_2$ state of $^{12}$C, where the amplification arises because the residual interaction between clusters vanishes more slowly than the binding energy as the system approaches the breakup threshold.

## Method

The authors used a 12-body, $3\alpha$-cluster model of $^{12}$C with translationally invariant harmonic-oscillator shell-model states for the alpha clusters and intercluster Jacobi coordinates. They performed calculations with four different effective nucleon-nucleon interactions (Minnesota, Volkov 1, Volkov 2, and modified Hasegawa-Nagata), first adjusting the exchange mixture of each to reproduce the experimental $0^+_2$ resonance energy, then multiplying the strength of each force by a factor $p$ ranging from 0.996 to 1.004 and recomputing the resonance energies.

## Key numbers

- The $0^+_2$ state of $^{12}$C lies 380 keV above the $3\alpha$ threshold.
- For the MHN interaction, varying the strength factor $p$ from 0.996 to 1.004 changes the resonance energy from 517.0 keV to 235.6 keV (a change of 281.4 keV for a 0.8% change in interaction strength).
- For the MN interaction over the same $p$ range, the resonance energy changes from 481.4 keV to 273.3 keV.
- For the V1 interaction, the resonance energy ranges from 460.7 keV ($p=0.996$) to 294.4 keV ($p=1.004$).
- For the V2 interaction, the resonance energy ranges from 450.0 keV ($p=0.996$) to 306.0 keV ($p=1.004$).
- A 0.5% change in the nucleon-nucleon force would lead to virtually no carbon or oxygen production, making carbon-based life impossible.

## Context

This work extends the study of nuclei far from stability, particularly those with halos and skins that result from very small binding energies relative to cluster breakup thresholds. The authors build on the $3\alpha$-cluster model of $^{12}$C developed by Pichler and collaborators, which gave a good overall description of low-lying $^{12}$C states. The results connect to nuclear astrophysics through the triple-alpha process described by Rolfs, and the authors use a contemporary stellar model code to compute carbon and oxygen abundances. The findings can in principle constrain fundamental parameters of the Standard Model, as the nucleon-nucleon force strength is connected through the pion mass to quark masses and the Higgs field vacuum expectation value, as discussed by Jeltema.
