# Microscopic effective reaction theory for deuteron-induced reactions

> The microscopic effective reaction theory is applied to deuteron-induced reactions. A reaction model-space characterized by a $p+n+{\rm A}$ three-body model is adopted, where A is the target nucleus, and the nucleon-target potential is described by a microscopic folding model based on an effective nucleon-nucleon interaction in nuclear medium and a one-body nuclear density of A. The three-body scattering wave function in the model space is obtained with the continuum-discretized coupled-channels method (CDCC), and the eikonal reaction theory (ERT), an extension of CDCC, is applied to the calculation of neutron removal cross sections. Elastic scattering cross sections of deuteron on $^{58}$Ni and $^{208}$Pb target nuclei at several energies are compared with experimental data. The total reaction cross sections and the neutron removal cross sections at 56 MeV on 14 target nuclei are calculated and compared with experimental values.

## Key claim

The microscopic effective reaction theory (microscopic CDCC with ERT) can describe deuteron elastic scattering, total reaction, and neutron removal cross sections across a wide energy range without free parameters, but the microscopic optical potentials become invalid at low energies (around 10 MeV for nucleon energy) where the folding model treatment loses justification.

## Method

The authors adopt a p+n+A three-body model space and construct nucleon-target optical potentials by folding the Melbourne g-matrix interaction with target densities obtained from Hartree-Fock calculations using the Gogny D1S force. The three-body scattering wave function is solved with the continuum-discretized coupled-channels method (CDCC), and the eikonal reaction theory (ERT) is applied to calculate neutron removal cross sections by separating the scattering matrix into proton and neutron parts.

## Key numbers

- Elastic scattering cross sections for deuteron on $^{58}$Ni at 80 MeV, 56 MeV, 21.6 MeV, 200 MeV, and 400 MeV, and on $^{208}$Pb at 56 MeV, compared with experimental data
- Total elastic breakup cross section $\sigma_{\text{EB}}$ on $^{58}$Ni at 21.6 MeV: 72.82 mb; at 56 MeV: 149.01 mb; at 80 MeV: 157.54 mb; at 200 MeV: 111.73 mb; at 400 MeV: 53.21 mb
- Total elastic breakup cross section on $^{208}$Pb at 56 MeV: 287.50 mb
- Total reaction cross sections $\sigma_{\text{R}}$ for 56 MeV deuteron on 14 target nuclei, ranging from 832 mb ($^{12}$C) to 2978 mb ($^{209}$Bi), compared with experimental data
- Neutron removal cross sections $\sigma_{-n}$ for 56 MeV deuteron on 14 target nuclei, ranging from 347 mb ($^{12}$C) to 810 mb ($^{209}$Bi), compared with experimental data
- Convergence of $\sigma_{\text{EB}}$ at $\ell_{\text{max}}=6$ within a few percent error; at 56 MeV on $^{208}$Pb, $\sigma_{\text{EB}}$ at $\ell_{\text{max}}=2$ differs from $\ell_{\text{max}}=6$ by about 8%, while at 21.6 MeV on $^{58}$Ni the difference is about 26%
- Coulomb breakup contributes 18% to $\sigma_{\text{EB}}$ at 56 MeV on $^{12}$C and 30% on $^{58}$Ni

## Context

This work extends the microscopic reaction theory approach of Yahiro et al. (2012) and the CDCC method of Kamimura (1986) and Austern (1987) by implementing microscopic nucleon-target optical potentials from the Melbourne g-matrix interaction (Amos et al., 2000) rather than phenomenological potentials. The study improves upon earlier CDCC calculations by Yahiro et al. (1986) and Piyadasa et al. (1989, 1999) by including odd-partial p-n breakup waves and clarifying the role of higher partial waves (l>2). The success enables future application of microscopic CDCC to deuteron-induced reactions involving unstable nuclei.
