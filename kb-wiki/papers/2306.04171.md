# Nuclear mass predictions based on deep neural network and finite-range droplet model (2012)

> A neural network with two hidden layers is developed for nuclear mass prediction, based on the finite-range droplet model (FRDM12). Different hyperparameters, including the number of hidden units, the choice of activation functions, the initializers, and the learning rates, are adjusted explicitly and systematically. The resulting mass predictions are achieved by averaging the predictions given by several different sets of hyperparameters with different regularizers and seed numbers. It can provide us not only the average values of mass predictions but also reliable estimations in the mass prediction uncertainties. The overall root-mean-square deviations of nuclear mass have been reduced from $0.603$ MeV for the FRDM12 model to $0.200$ MeV and $0.232$ MeV for the training set and validation set, respectively.

## Key claim

A deep neural network with two hidden layers reduces the root-mean-square deviation of nuclear mass predictions from the finite-range droplet model (FRDM12) from 0.603 MeV to 0.200 MeV for the training set and 0.232 MeV for the validation set, but only when the predictions from seven selected hyperparameter sets (with different regularizers and seed numbers) are averaged; individual hyperparameter sets show validation-set RMS deviations ranging from 0.218 MeV to 0.238 MeV.

## Method

A deep neural network with four inputs (proton number Z, neutron number N, pairing term delta, and shell-effect term P), two hidden layers of 22 units each (sigmoid and softmax activation functions, respectively), and one output was trained on the mass residuals between FRDM12 predictions and experimental masses from AME2020. The training used 1966 nuclei (80% of 2457 nuclei with Z,N >= 8) and the Adam optimizer with learning rate 0.01; 33 sets of hyperparameters (varying regularizer type and seed number) were trained for 20000 epochs each, and the seven sets achieving a validation-set RMS deviation below 0.228 MeV were averaged to produce the final mass predictions and uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- Overall RMS deviation reduced from 0.603 MeV (FRDM12 alone) to 0.200 MeV for the training set and 0.232 MeV for the validation set (averaged over seven selected hyperparameter sets).
- Individual validation-set RMS deviations for the seven selected sets range from 0.218 MeV (orthogonal regularizer, lambda=0.1, seed=2) to 0.238 MeV (orthogonal regularizer, lambda=0.01, seed=1; and lambda=0.001, seeds 1 and 2).
- The best single set (no regularizer, seed=3) gives training-set RMS 0.175 MeV and validation-set RMS 0.228 MeV, corresponding to reductions of 71.0% and 62.2% from the FRDM12 baseline.
- For the two worst-performing nuclei in the FRDM12 model, Cr-41 (N=17) and Cr-42 (N=18), the neural network improved the mass difference from -2.5 MeV to -2.0 MeV and from -2.1 MeV to -0.6 MeV, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the line of neural-network mass predictions built on macroscopic-microscopic models, following earlier studies such as Utama (2017), Niu (2018), and Niu (2022) who applied neural networks to improve mass predictions. The explicit and systematic tuning of hyperparameters (activation functions, learning rate, hidden units, initializers) distinguishes this study from prior work; the authors recommend sigmoid+softmax activation functions (or alternatively sigmoid+tanh) and 22 hidden units per layer. The improved FRDM12+DNN masses can be used to refine models of beta-decay properties (e.g., the FRDM+QRPA model of Mumpower 2016) and other nuclear quantities such as beta-decay half-lives and beta-delayed neutron emission probabilities.
