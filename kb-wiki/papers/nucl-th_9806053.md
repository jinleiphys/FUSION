# Generalized entropy and temperature in nuclear multifragmentation

> In the framework of a 2D Vlasov model, we study the time evolution of the "coarse-grained" Generalized Entropy (GE) in a nuclear system which undergoes a multifragmentation (MF) phase transition. We investigate the GE both for the gas and the fragments (surface and bulk part respectively). We find that the formation of the surface causes the growth of the GE during the process of fragmentation. This quantity then characterizes the MF and confirms the crucial role of deterministic chaos in filling the new available phase-space: at variance with the exact time evolution, no entropy change is found when the linear response is applied. Numerical simulations were used also to extract information about final temperatures of the fragments. From a fitting of the momentum distribution with a Fermi-Dirac function we extract the temperature of the fragments at the end of the process. We calculate also the gas temperature by averaging over the available phase space. The latter is a few times larger than the former, indicating a gas not in equilibrium. Though the model is very schematic, this fact seems to be very general and could explain the discrepancy found in experimental data when using the slope of light particles spectra instead of the double ratio of isotope yields method in order to extract the nuclear caloric curve.

## Key claim

The coarse-grained Generalized Entropy (GE) increases during nuclear multifragmentation due to the formation of fragment surfaces, confirming that deterministic chaos, not two-body collisions, is the primary mechanism for filling the newly available phase space; this entropy growth is absent when the linear response approximation is applied, indicating that chaotic dynamics is essential for the entropy production observed in the exact time evolution.

## Method

The authors solved the Vlasov equation (collisionless mean-field dynamics) on a two-dimensional lattice for a fermion gas initialized inside the mechanically unstable spinodal region of the nuclear phase diagram, with a small random noise on the initial density profile to trigger fragment formation. They computed the coarse-grained Generalized Entropy (GE) from the discretized one-body phase-space distribution, separating contributions from the bulk, surface, and gas components of the fragments based on density thresholds. Fragment temperatures were extracted by fitting the final momentum distributions with a Fermi-Dirac function for the bulk, while the gas temperature was obtained by averaging over the available phase space.

## Key numbers

- Initial average density: \(0.55\,\rho_0\) and \(0.3\,\rho_0\), where \(\rho_0 = 0.55~\text{fm}^{-2}\) (two-dimensional saturation density corresponding to a three-dimensional Fermi momentum of \(P_F = 260~\text{MeV}/c\)).
- Initial temperature: \(T = 3~\text{MeV}\).
- Fragmentation time \(\tau_{frag} \simeq 160~\text{fm}/c\) for initial density \(0.55\,\rho_0\) and \(\tau_{frag} \simeq 80~\text{fm}/c\) for initial density \(0.3\,\rho_0\).
- Increase in entropy per particle at fragmentation: \(\Delta\sigma \sim 0.4\), independent of initial average density.
- Surface entropy per particle rises from zero to approximately 0.9 at fragmentation, accounting for essentially the entire total entropy per particle.
- Gas temperature is a factor of 2–3 larger than the fragment temperature, indicating the gas is not in equilibrium with the fragments.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of multifragmentation using the same 2D Vlasov model (Bur98, BBR1, BBR2, Jac) by quantifying entropy production via the coarse-grained Generalized Entropy and linking it to deterministic chaos rather than two-body collisions. The finding that the gas temperature is 2–3 times higher than the fragment temperature offers a possible explanation for the experimental discrepancy between caloric curves extracted from isotope yields (Albergo method) versus those from the slopes of light particle spectra (EOS2, INDRA). The results suggest that the non-equilibrated gas component, which would be emitted early in realistic reactions, does not carry information about the equilibrated fragmenting system.
