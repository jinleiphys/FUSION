# Anomalous Behavior of Giant Monopole Resonance Energy with Nuclear Matter Incompressibility in the framework of Relativistic Mean Field Formalism and Coherent Density Fluctuation Model

> The finite nucleus incompressibility $K^A$ is evaluated using the coherent density fluctuation model with the extended relativistic mean field density. The relativistic energy density functional for nuclear matter is replaced by the local density approximation for finite nuclei. The equation is used to calculate the finite nuclear incompressibility, which is further utilized to evaluate the isoscalar giant monopole excitation (ISGMR) energy $E_M$. This excitation energy is compared with other theoretical calculations and experimental data, wherever available. The results are comparable to the data. In contrast to the general understanding, the $E_M$ of finite nucleus is found to be maximum for the lowest nuclear matter incompressibility $K_{\infty}$, whereas it is minimum for the maximum $K_{\infty}$. These reverse results may be due to the self- and cross-interactions of the vector mesons in the nuclear potential.

## Key claim

The authors find that, contrary to the conventional expectation that higher nuclear matter incompressibility \(K_{\infty}\) leads to larger finite-nucleus incompressibility \(K^A\) and isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR) energy \(E_M\), their calculations show an inverse trend: the NL3 parameter set, which has the highest \(K_{\infty}\) (271.38 MeV), produces the lowest \(K^A\) and \(E_M\), while the IOPB-I set, with the lowest \(K_{\infty}\) (222.65 MeV), yields the highest values. This anomaly is attributed to the self- and cross-couplings of vector mesons in the nuclear potential.

## Method

The authors use the extended relativistic mean field (E-RMF) formalism to obtain nuclear matter energy density functionals, which are then translated to coordinate space via the local density approximation (LDA). The coherent density fluctuation model (CDFM) is applied, using a weight function \(|F(x)|^2\) derived from the E-RMF density distributions of finite nuclei, to compute the finite-nucleus incompressibility \(K^A\) as a surface-weighted average of the nuclear matter incompressibility \(K_{\infty}(\rho(x))\). The ISGMR energy \(E_M\) is then calculated from \(K^A\) using the relation \(E_M = \hbar \sqrt{K^A / (m \langle r_{rms}^2 \rangle)}\), where \(\langle r_{rms}^2 \rangle\) is the ground-state mean-square radius. Calculations are performed with three parameter sets (NL3, IOPB-I, G3) using both the relativistic energy density functional (REDF) and the Brückner energy density functional (BRUCK).

## Key numbers

- Nuclear matter incompressibility \(K_{\infty}\) values: G3 = 243.96 MeV, IOPB-I = 222.65 MeV, NL3 = 271.38 MeV.
- For \(^{40}\)Ca, the finite-nucleus incompressibility \(K^A\) from REDF-G3 is 221.33 MeV, while from BRUCK-G3 it is 122.88 MeV.
- For \(^{208}\)Pb, the ISGMR energy \(E_M\) from REDF is 17.39 MeV (G3), 18.11 MeV (IOPB-I), and 17.72 MeV (NL3); the experimental value is 13.96 \(\pm\) 0.2 MeV.
- For \(^{48}\)Ca, the calculated \(E_M\) from REDF is 28.69 MeV (G3), 29.84 MeV (IOPB-I), and 29.36 MeV (NL3); the experimental value is 19.88 \(\pm\) 0.16 MeV.
- For \(^{116}\)Sn, the calculated \(E_M\) from REDF is 21.70 MeV (G3), 22.65 MeV (IOPB-I), and 22.30 MeV (NL3); the experimental value is 15.8 \(\pm\) 0.1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the use of the coherent density fluctuation model (CDFM) combined with extended relativistic mean field (E-RMF) densities, previously applied by Pattnaik et al. (2024) and Yadav et al. (2025), to study finite-nucleus incompressibility and ISGMR energies. The results contradict the standard correlation between \(K_{\infty}\) and \(E_M\) established in earlier RPA-based studies by Patra et al. (2001, 2002), which found that higher \(K_{\infty}\) leads to larger \(E_M\). The present findings highlight the critical role of nonlinear vector-meson self- and cross-couplings (present in G3 and IOPB-I but not in NL3) in modifying the density distributions and stiffness of finite nuclei, suggesting that the traditional interpretation of ISGMR energies must account for these interaction-specific effects.
