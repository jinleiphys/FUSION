# Bridges from Lattice QCD to Nuclear Physics

> A review is given of attempts to bridge the gap between everyday particle and nuclear physics - involving many quarks - and the basic underlying theory of QCD that can only be evaluated exactly for few quark systems. Even the latter requires the original theory of QCD to be discretised to give Lattice QCD - but this modification can still yield exact results for the original theory. These LQCD results can then be considered on a similar footing to experimental data - namely as cornerstones that must be fitted by phenomenological models. In this way, the hope is that "QCD inspired" models can become more and more "QCD based" models, by fixing - in the few-quark case where LQCD can be carried out - the form of these models in such a way that they can be extended to multi-quark systems.

## Key claim

This review argues that lattice QCD results for few-quark systems can serve as "cornerstones" that must be fitted by phenomenological Effective Potential Theories (EPTs), enabling a progression from "QCD inspired" models toward "QCD based" models that can be extended to multi-quark systems, but the author notes that this bridging step is "mainly phenomenological" and that the magnitude of the step "should not be underestimated."

## Method

The paper reviews two broad categories of approximation to QCD: numerical Lattice QCD (LQCD) and effective theories, distinguishing between rigorous Effective Field Theories (EFTs) and phenomenological Effective Potential Theories (EPTs). It examines how LQCD results for few-quark systems, obtained via discretization on a 4-dimensional lattice with spacing \(a\) and extrapolated to the continuum limit, can be compared with EPT predictions using wave equations (Schrödinger, Dirac, Bethe-Salpeter, or Resonating Group), interquark potentials of the form \(V_{Q\bar{Q}}(r) = -e/r + b_s r + c\), and effective quark masses. The "bridge" is constructed by matching dimensionless LQCD quantities to their continuum counterparts, using the string tension or Sommer's prescription to set the physical scale.

## Key numbers

- The experimental string tension extracted from heavy-quark meson spectra is \(\sqrt{b_s} \approx 440\) MeV, equivalent to \(b_s \approx 1\) GeV/fm or \(\approx 5\) fm\(^{-2}\).
- A typical dimensionless lattice string tension is \(b'_s \approx 0.05\), yielding a lattice spacing \(a \approx \sqrt{0.05/5} \approx 0.1\) fm.
- The Regge slope for mesons and baryons is about 0.9 GeV\(^{-2}\), giving a simple classical estimate \(\sqrt{b_s} \approx 380\) MeV.
- The computational cost estimate for generating a new lattice configuration is approximately \(0.157 (L/a)^{3.41} (T/a)^{1.14} (1/am_\pi)^{2.77}\) Gflops per configuration.
- Lattice sizes of \(32^3 \times 60\) are used in scattering length studies, with \(64^3 \times 128\) becoming feasible; many calculations use \(16^3 \times 24\).
- Typical lattice spacings are \(a \approx 0.1\) fm, but improved actions allow \(a \approx 0.5\) fm in some cases.
- The ratio \(R_{\pi\rho} = m_\pi/m_\rho\) is 0.2 experimentally, but \(\approx 0.7\) when light quark masses are set near the strange quark mass.
- Light quark masses are \(\sim 10\) MeV for \(u,d\); the strange quark mass is \(m_s \sim 100\) MeV; the \(b\)-quark mass is \(m_Q \approx 5\) GeV.
- Effective quark masses in EPTs are typically \(\approx 300\)–400 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the long-standing effort to connect LQCD results to nuclear phenomenology, building on the work of Wilson (1974) on lattice gauge theory and the development of effective potentials by Richardson and Cornell for heavy-quark systems. It contrasts with rigorous EFT approaches such as Chiral Perturbation Theory (Gasser and Leutwyler), Heavy-Quark Effective Theory, and Non-relativistic QCD, which are integral to extracting continuum results from LQCD but are not directly applicable to multi-quark systems. The paper enables future work by identifying specific quantities, such as the string tension and interquark potentials, that can serve as bridges, while noting that the extension to many-body systems remains an open challenge, with van Kolck and others questioning whether the gap between non-relativistic nucleon-based descriptions and relativistic QCD can be bridged systematically.
