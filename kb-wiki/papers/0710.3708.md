# What we have learned so far from 3-fluid hydrodynamics

> Available data on heavy-ion collisions at AGS and SPS energies are analyzed using a 3-fluid dynamical model within a purely hadronic scenario. We investigate the problems met in reproducing these data within this scheme. In particular, we try to indicate those data which could point towards the occurrence of a phase transition into the quark-gluon phase. We also discuss the success of the model in reproducing the transverse-mass spectra of various hadrons. We argue that the simultaneous reproduction of the inverse-slope parameters of all considered particles may imply that these particles belong to the same hydrodynamic flow at the instant of their freeze-out rather than that it signals the onset of a phase transition.

# What we have learned so far from 3-fluid hydrodynamics

## Key claim
The simultaneous reproduction of inverse-slope parameters of transverse-mass spectra for pions, kaons, and protons within a purely hadronic 3-fluid dynamical model suggests these particles belong to the same hydrodynamic flow at freeze-out, rather than signaling the onset of a phase transition into the quark-gluon phase. However, the authors note that directed flow data and net-baryon rapidity spectra at certain energies favor a softer equation of state, which could indicate deconfinement.

## Method
The authors applied a 3-fluid dynamical (3FD) model to simulate heavy-ion collisions at AGS and SPS incident energies (1–160 A GeV), using a purely hadronic equation of state (H-EoS) with incompressibility K = 210 MeV. The model represents the nonequilibrium distribution of baryon-rich matter as two distinct fluids (projectile and target) plus a fireball fluid for newly produced particles, with a formation time of τ = 2 fm/c and freeze-out energy density ε_frz = 0.4 GeV/fm³. They compared calculated rapidity distributions, transverse-mass spectra, directed flow, elliptic flow, and hadron ratios against experimental data, and tested the effect of EoS stiffness by varying the incompressibility (K = 100, 150, 210, and 380 MeV).

## Key numbers
- Freeze-out energy density: ε_frz = 0.4 GeV/fm³
- Fireball fluid formation time: τ = 2 fm/c
- Incident energy range studied: E_lab ≈ 1–160 A GeV
- Hadronic EoS incompressibility values tested: K = 210 MeV (standard H-EoS), K = 150 MeV (soft), K = 100 MeV (extra-soft), and K = 380 MeV (hard)
- Inverse-slope parameters of transverse-mass spectra at midrapidity show a "step-like" behavior: they increase across the AGS energy domain and then saturate at SPS energies
- Net-baryon rapidity spectrum at 40 A GeV is almost perfectly reproduced with soft H-EoS (K = 150 MeV), while extra-soft H-EoS (K = 100 MeV) is required at 20 A and 30 A GeV
- At 80 A and 158 A GeV, the standard H-EoS (K = 210 MeV) gives the best result for net-baryon rapidity spectra
- Proton elliptic flow at 40 A GeV is strongly overestimated by the model even accounting for a post-hydro cascade that approximately halves the v₂ values at SPS energies

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous 3FD model simulations (Ivanov and Russkikh, 2006) and compares against transport models HSD and UrQMD (Bratkovskaya, 2004) and GiBUU (Wagner, 2007; Larionov, 2007), which failed to reproduce kaon inverse slopes. The saturation of inverse-slope parameters had been interpreted as a deconfinement signal by Gorenstein (2003) and Mohanty (2003). The directed flow predictions follow earlier work by Rischke (1995), Brachmann, and Paech (2001) showing that a first-order phase transition reduces directed flow. The elliptic flow "collapse" at 40 A GeV was previously attributed to the critical point by Shuryak (2005). The K⁺/π⁺ ratio maximum had been interpreted as an onset of deconfinement by Gazdzicki (1999, 2004), Braun-Munzinger (2001), and others. The authors note that 3FD simulations with a two-phase EoS (Toneev, 2006) involving a first-order phase transition are in progress.
