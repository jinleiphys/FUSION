# Non-Fermi Liquid Behaviour, the BRST Identity in the Dense Quark-Gluon Plasma and Color Superconductivity

> At sufficiently high baryon densities, the physics of a dense quark-gluon plasma may be investigated through the tools of perturbative QCD. This approach has recently been successfully applied to the study of color superconductivity, where the dominant di-quark pairing interaction arises from one gluon exchange. Screening in the plasma leads to novel behaviour, including a remarkable non-BCS scaling of T_C, the transition temperature to the color superconducting phase. Radiative corrections to one gluon exchange were previously considered and found to affect T_C. In particular, the quark self-energy in a plasma leads to non-Fermi liquid behaviour and suppresses T_C. However, at the same time, the quark-gluon vertex was shown not to modify the result at leading order. This dichotomy between the effects of the radiative corrections at first appears rather surprising, as the BRST identity connects the self-energy to the vertex corrections. Nevertheless, as we demonstrate, there is in fact no contradiction with the BRST identity, at least to leading log order. This clarifies some of the previous statements on the importance of the higher order corrections to the determination of T_C and the zero temperature gap in color superconductivity.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the apparent contradiction between the non-Fermi liquid behavior of the quark self-energy (which suppresses the transition temperature for color superconductivity) and the BRST identity (which connects the self-energy to vertex corrections) is resolved because the vertex function's infrared limit depends on the order in which the energy and momentum limits are taken, so the vertex does not contribute to the leading-order suppression of the critical temperature.

## Method

The authors evaluate the quark self-energy and the abelian quark-gluon vertex function in a dense quark-gluon plasma at high baryon density, using hard dense loop (HDL) resummed gluon propagators in Euclidean space. They analyze the infrared behavior of the self-energy and vertex by separating loop integrals into infrared-sensitive and infrared-regular regions, and they examine the two distinct orderings of the limits (energy first versus momentum first) in the Ward identity to reconcile the logarithmic singularity in the self-energy with the finite behavior of the magnetic vertex. The full non-abelian BRST identity is then verified by computing the contributions from the tri-gluon vertex diagram and the triangular vertex diagram to leading logarithmic order.

## Key numbers

- The quark self-energy at the Fermi surface exhibits non-analytic behavior: \(\Sigma(P)|_{p=\mu} = -\frac{ig^2}{12\pi^2} C_f \gamma_4 \, \nu \ln \frac{4l_c^3}{\pi m_D^2 |\nu|}\), where \(C_f = (N_c^2-1)/(2N_c)\) and \(m_D^2 \simeq N_f g^2 \mu^2/(2\pi^2)\).
- The pre-exponential factor for the critical temperature is suppressed by a factor \(c = c_0 e^{-\frac{1}{16}(\pi^2+4)(N_c-1)} \simeq 0.176\,c_0\) for \(N_c=3\), where \(c_0\) is the factor without radiative corrections.
- The Debye mass is given by \(m_D^2 \simeq \frac{N_f g^2 \mu^2}{2 \pi^2}\).
- The critical temperature scales as \(k_B T_C = c \frac{\mu}{g^5} e^{-\sqrt{\frac{6N_c}{N_c+1}}\frac{\pi^2}{g}}\).

## Context

This work extends the perturbative QCD treatment of color superconductivity initiated by Son (1998), Schafer and Wilczek (1999), Pisarski and Rischke (1999), and Hong (2000), who identified non-BCS scaling of the transition temperature due to magnetic gluon exchange and Landau damping. The authors previously showed (Brown, Liu, and Ren, 2000) that the non-Fermi liquid self-energy suppresses the pre-exponential factor, while the vertex corrections appeared not to contribute at leading order. The present paper resolves the apparent paradox with the BRST identity by demonstrating that the vertex function's infrared behavior depends on the order of limits, thereby confirming that the vertex does not modify the leading-order suppression. This clarifies the role of higher-order radiative corrections in determining the critical temperature and zero-temperature gap in color superconductivity, and the analysis also applies to relativistic electron plasmas in astrophysical contexts such as white dwarfs and supernovae.
