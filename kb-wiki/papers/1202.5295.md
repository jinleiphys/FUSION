# Calculating the Jet Quenching Parameter $\hat{q}$ in Lattice Gauge Theory

> We present a framework where first principles calculations of jet modification may be carried out in a non-perturbative thermal environment. As an example of this approach, we compute the leading order contribution to the transverse momentum broadening of a high energy (near on-shell) quark in a thermal medium. This involves a factorization of a non-perturbative operator product from the perturbative process of scattering of the quark. An operator product expansion of the non-perturbative operator product is carried out and related via dispersion relations to the expectation of local operators. These local operators are then evaluated in quenched SU(2) lattice gauge theory.

## Key claim

The paper presents a framework for first-principles calculation of the jet quenching parameter $\hat{q}$ using lattice gauge theory, but explicitly states this is a "proof of principle" calculation in quenched SU(2) gauge theory, with issues of renormalization ignored, and that a completely first-principles calculation "can never be directly compared with data" but will instead constrain transport coefficients used in phenomenological analyses.

## Method

The author factorizes the transverse momentum broadening of a near on-shell quark in a thermal medium into a perturbative scattering part and a non-perturbative operator product, then performs an operator product expansion (OPE) of the non-perturbative part to express it as a series of local operators suppressed by powers of the hard scale $Q^2$. These local operators are related via dispersion relations to the physical transport coefficient $\hat{q}$, and the leading local operators are evaluated in quenched SU(2) lattice gauge theory, working in $A^- = 0$ gauge.

## Key numbers

- The transport coefficient $\hat{q}$ is defined as $\hat{q} = \sum_k k_\perp^2 \frac{W(k)}{t}$, where $W(k)$ is the transition probability and $t$ is the time spent by the hard quark in the thermal volume
- The OPE expansion for $\hat{Q}$ (the analytically continued object whose discontinuity gives $\hat{q}$) is given as $\hat{Q} = \frac{4\sqrt{2} \pi^2 \alpha_s q^-}{N_c Q^2} \langle M | F^{+ \mu}_\perp \sum_{n=0}^\infty \left( \frac{-q \cdot i\mathcal{D} - \mathcal{D}_\perp^2}{Q^2} \right)^n F^+_{\perp, \mu} | M \rangle$
- The on-shell limit uses the delta function $\delta[(q+k)^2] \simeq \frac{1}{2q^-} \delta\left( k^+ - \frac{k_\perp^2}{2q^-} \right)$
- The dispersion relation contour integral $I_1$ is evaluated with $Q_0$ large and positive, where $Q_0 \sim Q$ is much larger than $q^+ \sim \lambda Q$

## Context

This work extends the higher-twist (HT) approach to jet quenching, where transport coefficients like $\hat{q}$ have previously been treated as fit parameters set by comparison to data, and contrasts with the AMY formalism which also varies $\alpha_s$ to fit data. The approach is inspired by the method of operator product expansions used to evaluate parton distribution functions on the lattice (Kronfeld 1984, Martinelli 1987-1988). The paper enables future calculations of jet transport coefficients in full SU(3) lattice gauge theory and in other gauges, and could constrain the number, structure, and normalization of transport coefficients used in phenomenological analyses of RHIC and LHC heavy-ion data.
