# On the experimental description of neutron resonances

> We present a collection of simple derivations for the neutron-induced resonance cross-sections. These formulae are commonly used to experimentally describe the fundamental properties of resonances for neutron-rich nuclei far from stability and to describe unbound nuclei. The main goal of this article is to illustrate their dependencies with basic observables in order to discuss the pertinence of experimental approaches in the derivation of their properties, especially for "N-body" resonances.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard Breit-Wigner parametrization used to extract resonance properties from experimental data has intrinsic limitations that can lead to misunderstanding when compared with theoretical descriptions, especially for "N-body" resonances where the two-body formalism is applied without justification.

## Method

The author derives the neutron-induced resonance cross-section formulae starting from a plane wave expansion into spherical harmonics, introducing a complex factor ηℓ for the outgoing wave to account for scattering on the nuclear potential. The derivation connects the logarithmic derivative fℓ of the radial wave function at the nuclear surface to the penetrability sℓ and energy shift Δℓ, leading to the Breit-Wigner form with energy-dependent width and shift expressed through Riccati-Bessel functions.

## Key numbers

- For systematic studies, the reference resonance has E0 = 1 MeV and Γ0 = 1 MeV, using a ¹⁷B+n system
- The width Γ evolves as k^(2ℓ+1) for ℓ>0, compared to energy varying as k², meaning for ℓ>0 the width decreases faster than the energy
- For ℓ=0, the width decreases only as the square root of the energy, causing threshold overlap that produces "virtual states"
- The single-particle width formula from Bohr and Mottelson gives Γ_sp = (2ħ²/μR²) × kR for ℓ=0, and Γ_sp = (2ħ²/μR²) × kR × vℓ × (2ℓ-1)/(2ℓ+1) for ℓ>0 when kR < ℓ^(1/2)

## Context

This work extends the standard R-matrix formalism from Blatt and Weisskopf (1952) and Satchler (1983), and the Breit-Wigner parametrization commonly used in experimental nuclear physics. The paper specifically addresses the misuse of two-body resonance parametrization for N-body resonances near the neutron drip line, noting that attempts by Barker (2003) and Fynbo (2009) have been made for sequential decays. The author recommends using ℓ=0 parametrization for multineutron resonances because it shows no energy dependence on position or width, and no dependence on the number of clustered neutrons or the nuclear radius parameter r0.
