# The Casimir Energy in a Separable Potential

> The Casimir energy is the first-order-in-\hbar correction to the energy of a time-independent field configuration in a quantum field theory. We study the Casimir energy in a toy model, where the classical field is replaced by a separable potential. In this model the exact answer is trivial to compute, making it a good place to examine subtleties of the problem. We construct two traditional representations of the Casimir energy, one from the Greens function, the other from the phase shifts, and apply them to this case. We show that the two representations are correct and equivalent in this model. We study the convergence of the Born approximation to the Casimir energy and relate our findings to computational issues that arise in more realistic models.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in a separable potential toy model, the Casimir energy is exactly \(-\frac{1}{2}\lambda\) and that two traditional representations (Greens function and phase shift) are equivalent and reproduce this result, but only under the specific assumptions that the eigenfrequencies are proportional to the eigenvalues of a Schrodinger-like operator and that the potential is nonlocal and separable.

## Method

The authors replace the local potential in a scalar field theory with a nonlocal but separable potential \(V(x,x') = -\lambda u(x)u(x')\), where \(\int dx\, u^2(x) = 1\), and assume the Casimir energy can be written as \(\mathcal{E}_c = \frac{1}{2}\mathrm{Tr}(\mathcal{H} - \mathcal{H}_0)\). They then construct the Greens function representation by solving the Lippmann-Schwinger equation iteratively and the phase shift representation by relating the modification of the continuum density of states to the s-wave phase shift. For a specific Yukawa choice of \(u(r)\), they study the convergence of the Born approximation to the phase shift and its relation to the Casimir energy calculation.

## Key numbers

* The Casimir energy for the separable potential model is exactly \(\mathcal{E}_c = -\frac{1}{2}\lambda\), independent of the form of \(u(x)\).
* For the Yukawa separable potential with \(u(r) = \sqrt{\alpha/(2\pi)} e^{-\alpha r}/r\), the scattering amplitude is \(f(g,z) = 2gz / [(1+iz)^2((1-iz)^2 - g)]\) where \(g = \lambda/\alpha^2\) and \(z = k/\alpha\).
* A bound state appears when \(g > 1\) (i.e., \(\lambda > \alpha^2\)), with binding energy \(B = (\sqrt{\lambda} - \alpha)^2\).
* For \(\lambda < \alpha^2\) (no bound state), the entire Casimir energy is given by the first Born approximation: \(\mathcal{E}_c = -\frac{1}{2}\lambda = -\frac{\lambda}{2\pi}\int_0^\infty d\omega\, \delta^{(1)}(\omega)\), and all higher Born approximations integrate to zero: \(\int_0^\infty d\omega\, \delta^{(n)}(\omega) = 0\) for all \(n > 1\).
* For \(\lambda > \alpha^2\) (with a bound state), the integral of the Born-subtracted phase shift gives \(-\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^\infty d\omega\, \bar\delta^N(\omega) = \frac{1}{2}(\sqrt{\lambda} - \alpha)^2\).

## Context

This work extends the study of Casimir energy calculations beyond perturbation theory by introducing a toy model where the exact result is trivial, allowing examination of subtleties that arise in more realistic quantum field theories. The authors relate their findings to the computational method introduced by Farhi, Graham, Haagensen and Jaffe (1998), where the first few Born approximations are subtracted from the phase shift and added back as Feynman diagrams for renormalization. The model also connects to work by Bashinsky on collective coordinate quantization in soliton theories, where separable-potential-like terms appear in the small oscillations Hamiltonian.
