# The $A_y$ Puzzle and the Nuclear Force

> The nucleon-deuteron analyzing power $A_y$ in elastic nucleon-deuteron scattering poses a longstanding puzzle. At energies $E_{lab}$ below approximately 30 MeV $A_y$ cannot be described by any realistic NN force. The inclusion of existing three-nucleon forces does not improve the situation. Because of recent questions about the $^3P_J$ NN phases, we examine whether reasonable changes in the NN force can resolve the puzzle. In order to do this we investigate the effect on the $^3P_J$ waves produced by changes in different parts of the potential (viz., the central force, tensor force, etc.), as well as on the 2-body observables and on $A_y$. We find that it is not possible with reasonable changes in the NN potential to increase the 3-body $A_y$ and at the same time to keep the 2-body observables unchanged. We therefore conclude that the $A_y$ puzzle is likely to be solved by new three-nucleon forces, such as those of spin-orbit type, which have not yet been taken into account.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that no reasonable modification of the two-nucleon (NN) potential can simultaneously keep two-body observables unchanged and increase the three-body analyzing power \(A_y\) by the 30% needed to match experimental data; they conclude that the puzzle must be resolved by new three-nucleon forces of spin-orbit type that have not yet been included in calculations.

## Method

The authors used the AV18 NN potential and applied separate 10% increases and decreases to five operator components (central, tensor, spin-orbit, \(l^2\), and \((l\cdot s)^2\)) in the \(^3P_J\) partial waves only. They then computed the resulting changes in the two-body analyzing power \(A_2\) and the three-body analyzing power \(A_y\) for elastic nucleon-deuteron scattering, and derived linear equations relating the percentage changes in each potential component to the percentage changes in the analyzing powers. They also separated the one-pion-exchange (OPEP) part from the short-range part of the tensor and central forces to isolate the origin of the sensitivity.

## Key numbers

* At \(E_{lab}=3\) MeV, the AV18 prediction for nd \(A_y\) underestimates the experimental data near the maximum by about 30%.
* A 1% change in the central force \(V_C\) causes roughly a \(\pm 0.3\%\) change in \(A_2\) at low energies; a 1% change in the tensor force \(V_T\) causes roughly a \(\pm 1.2\%\) change in \(A_2\) and a \(\pm 0.6\%\) change in \(A_y\).
* Solving the linear equations to increase \(A_y\) by 30% while keeping \(A_2\) unchanged requires changes in the tensor force of at least \(\delta_T = 56.7\%\) (when \(\delta_C = \delta_{l^2} = 0\)), and in the spin-orbit force of at least \(\delta_{ls} = 40\%\).
* A refit of the AV18 potential to modified \(^3P_J\) phases (with up to 5% weakening at 1 MeV) produced a \(\chi^2\) per phase-shift datum of 23 and improved \(A_y\) by only about 3% at \(E_{lab}=3\) MeV, far short of the needed 30%.

## Context

This work extends the longstanding \(A_y\) puzzle first identified in rigorous nd scattering calculations (Cornelius, 1980s) and subsequent studies that showed all realistic NN potentials and existing three-nucleon forces (3NFs) fail to describe the vector analyzing power below 30 MeV. The authors directly contradict the suggestion by Witala (1998) that low-energy \(^3P_J\) phase shifts might be modified within Fermi-Yang ambiguities, showing that such modifications cannot produce the needed increase in \(A_y\) and that refitting the potential to modified phases is not possible without violating OPEP constraints. The paper enables future work to focus on developing three-nucleon forces with spin-orbit components, which had been omitted from earlier 3NF models.
