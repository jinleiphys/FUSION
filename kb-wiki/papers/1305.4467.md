# Energy uncertainty of the final state of a decay process

> We derive the expression for the energy uncertainty of the final state of a decay of an unstable quantum state prepared at the initial time $t=0$. This expression is function of the time $t$ at which a measurement is performed to determine if the state has decayed and, if yes, in which one of the infinitely many possible final states. For large times the energy spread is, as expected, given by the decay width $Γ$ of the initial unstable state. However, if the measurement of the final state is performed at a time $t$ comparable to (or smaller than) the mean lifetime of the state $1/Γ$, then the uncertainty on the energy of the final state is much larger than the decay width $Γ$. Namely, for short times an uncertainty of the type $1/t$ dominates, while at large times the usual spread $Γ$ is recovered. Then, we turn to a generic two-body decay process and describe the energy uncertainty of each one of the two outgoing particles. We apply these formulas to the two-body decays of the neutral and charged pions and to the spontaneous emission process of an excited atom. As a last step, we study a case in which the non-exponential decay is realized ad show that for short times eventual asymmetric terms are enhanced in the spectrum.

## Key claim

The energy uncertainty of the final state of a decay process depends on the time at which a measurement is performed: for measurement times comparable to or smaller than the mean lifetime, the energy spread is much larger than the decay width Gamma, with a 1/t behavior dominating at short times, while the usual spread Gamma is recovered only at large times.

## Method

The author uses a Lee Hamiltonian approach in which an unstable state |S> is coupled to a continuum of final states |k>. The probability density eta(t,omega) for the energy of the final state at measurement time t is derived analytically in the Breit-Wigner limit, and the width at half-maximum delta-omega(t) is computed as a function of time. The formalism is then extended to two-body decays by transforming the energy distribution from the total energy omega to the individual particle energies omega_1 and omega_2, and applied to specific decay processes including neutral and charged pions and atomic spontaneous emission.

## Key numbers

- For short times (t less than or approximately 3 tau, where tau = 1/Gamma), the energy uncertainty delta-omega is approximately 5.56/t, where the numerical coefficient 2.78 is the nonzero solution of the transcendental equation y = sqrt(2) |1 - e^{iy}|.
- For large times, delta-omega approaches Gamma, the natural decay width of the initial unstable state.
- The neutral pion lifetime is tau_pi0 = (8.52 +/- 0.18) x 10^{-17} s, and each photon from pi0 decay has energy uncertainty delta-E_gamma = delta-omega/2.
- The charged pion lifetime is tau_pi+ = (2.6033 +/- 0.0005) x 10^{-8} s, with the muon energy spread delta-E_mu approximately 0.213 delta-omega and the neutrino energy spread delta-E_nu approximately 0.787 delta-omega.
- For the non-exponential decay model with parameters g = 0.95 sqrt(Gamma), M - E_0 = 2.52 Gamma, and alpha = 0.0396 tau, the survival probability shows deviations from exponential decay at short times.

## Context

This work extends the classic treatment of natural line broadening by Weisskopf and Wigner (1930) and Breit (1932), who studied the long-time limit where the final-state energy distribution equals the initial spectral function. It generalizes the analysis of Ford (1997) by providing the full time-dependent energy distribution of the final state, not just the infinite-time limit. The formalism is related to the continuous monitoring study of Elattari (2000) for electron tunneling, but differs in that the broadening here is an intrinsic feature of short-time evolution rather than being induced by the measurement process. The results enable experimental tests of energy uncertainty broadening in short-time measurements of decays, such as muon detection from pion decay or photon emission from excited atoms, and connect to the ongoing debate about non-exponential weak decay of ions measured by GSI (2008).
