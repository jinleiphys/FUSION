# The Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay, Physics beyond the Standard Model and the Neutrino Mass

> The Neutrinoless double beta Decay allows to determine the effectice Majorana electron neutrino mass. For this the following conditions have to be satisfied: (i) The neutrino must be a Majorana particle, i. e. identical to the antiparticle. (ii) The half life has to be measured. (iii)The transition matrix element must be reliably calculated. (iv) The leading mechanism must be the light Majorana neutrino exchange. The present contribution studies the accuracy with which one can calculate by different methods: (1) Quasi-Particle Random Phase Approach (QRPA), (2) the Shell Model (SM), (3) the (before the variation) angular momentum projected Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method (PHFB)and the (4) Interacting Boson Approach (IBA). In the second part we investigate how to determine experimentally the leading mechanism for the Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay. Is it (a) the light Majorana neutrino exchange as one assumes to determine the effective Majorana neutrino mass, ist it the heavy left (b) or right handed (c) Majorana neutrino exchange allowed by left-right symmetric Grand Unified Theories (GUT's). Is it a mechanism due to Supersymmetry e.g. with gluino exchange and R-parity and lepton number violating terms. At the end we assume, that Klapdor et al. have indeed measured the Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay(, although contested,)and that the light Majorana neutrino exchange is the leading mechanism. With our matrix elements we obtain then an effective Majorana neutrino mass of: <m(nu)> = 0.24 [eV], exp (pm) 0.02; theor. (pm) 0.01 [eV]

# The Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay, Physics beyond the Standard Model and the Neutrino Mass

## Key claim

Assuming that Klapdor et al. have indeed measured the neutrinoless double beta decay in 76Ge (although this result is heavily contested) and that the light Majorana neutrino exchange is the leading mechanism, the effective Majorana neutrino mass is determined to be 0.24 eV with an experimental uncertainty of ±0.02 eV and a theoretical uncertainty of ±0.01 eV.

## Method

The paper compares four many-body methods for calculating nuclear matrix elements for neutrinoless double beta decay: the Quasi-Particle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA), the Shell Model (SM), the angular momentum projected Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method (PHFB), and the Interacting Boson Model (IBM2). The second part investigates how to experimentally identify the leading mechanism among light Majorana neutrino exchange, heavy left or right handed Majorana neutrino exchange from left-right symmetric Grand Unified Theories, and supersymmetric mechanisms with gluino exchange and R-parity violation, using ratios of half-lives across different decay systems.

## Key numbers

- Effective Majorana neutrino mass: <m_nu> = 0.24 eV (exp ±0.02; theor. ±0.01) [eV], derived from the Klapdor et al. half-life of 76Ge
- Klapdor et al. experimental half-life for 76Ge: T^{0nu}_{1/2} = (2.23 +0.44 -0.31) × 10^25 years
- QRPA matrix element for 76Ge with deformation: M^{0nu}_nu = 4.69 (with g_A = 1.25); without deformation: M^{0nu}_nu = 5.30
- QRPA matrix element for 150Nd: 6.12 (spherical) reduced to 3.34 (deformed) with g_A = 1.25
- QRPA matrix element for 160Gd: 3.76 (deformed) with g_A = 1.25
- Predicted half-life ratio intervals for light neutrino exchange: T^{0nu}_{1/2}(100Mo)/T^{0nu}_{1/2}(76Ge) between 0.15 and 0.18; T^{0nu}_{1/2}(130Te)/T^{0nu}_{1/2}(76Ge) between 0.17 and 0.22; T^{0nu}_{1/2}(130Te)/T^{0nu}_{1/2}(100Mo) between 1.14 and 1.24
- Shell Model basis for A=76 and A=82 is restricted to four single-particle levels (1p_{3/2}, 0f_{5/2}, 1p_{1/2}, 0g_{9/2}), violating the Ikeda Sum Rule by 34 to 50 percent
- QRPA with the same four-level basis gives matrix element 1.12 for 82Se; expanding to six levels (including both spin-orbit partners 0f_{7/2} and 0g_{7/2}) increases the matrix element to 4.07; expanding to nine levels gives 4.27

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to extract the effective Majorana neutrino mass from neutrinoless double beta decay, comparing the QRPA approach of the Tubingen group (Rodin et al., 2005; Fang et al., 2011) with the Shell Model calculations of the Strasbourg-Madrid group (Poves et al., 2009), the projected HFB method of Rath et al. (2010), and the Interacting Boson Model of Iachello and Barea (2009). The paper enables experimental identification of the leading decay mechanism by using half-life ratios across multiple isotopes, building on earlier work by Simkovic, Vergados, and Faessler (2008, 2010). The key open question is whether the Klapdor result for 76Ge can be confirmed by other experiments, and whether the light Majorana neutrino exchange is indeed the dominant mechanism or whether heavy neutrino exchange or supersymmetric contributions must be considered.
