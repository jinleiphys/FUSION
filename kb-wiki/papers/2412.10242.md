# Equation of State Independent Determination on the Radius of a 1.4 $M_{\odot}$ Neutron Star Using Mass-Radius Measurements

> Traditional methods for determining the radius of a 1.4 $M_{\odot}$ neutron star ($R_{1.4}$) rely on specific equations of state (EOS) models that describe various types of dense nuclear matter. This dependence on EOS models can introduce substantial systematic uncertainties, which may exceed the measurement uncertainties when constraining $R_{1.4}$. In this study, we explore a novel approach to constraining $R_{1.4}$ using data from NICER observations of PSR J0030+0451 (J0030) and PSR J0437-4715 (J0437). However, this work presents a more data-driven analysis framework, substantially decreasing the need for EOS assumptions. By analyzing the Mass-Radius measurements of these two neutron stars, we infer $R_{1.4}$ using statistical methods based mostly on observational data. We examine various hotspot configurations for J0030, along with new J0437 observations, and their effects on the inferred radius. Our results are consistent with X-ray timing, gravitational wave, and nuclear physics constraints, while avoiding EOS-related biases. The same method has also been applied to a simulated mass-radius dataset, based on our knowledge of future X-ray telescopes, demonstrating the model's ability to recover the injected $R_{1.4}$ value in certain cases. This method provides a data-driven pathway for extracting neutron star properties and offers a new approach for future observational efforts in neutron star astrophysics.

## Key claim

The paper presents a data-driven method to determine the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star (R1.4) using NICER observations of PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0437-4715, substantially decreasing the need for equation-of-state (EOS) model assumptions, though some residual EOS dependence remains from assumptions about the neutron star surface shape and the exclusion of twin-star scenarios.

## Method

The author analyzed mass-radius posterior samples from NICER observations of PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0437-4715 under two scenarios: (1) treating the two neutron stars as having different masses and applying linear interpolation between randomly sampled mass-radius pairs to estimate R1.4, and (2) assuming both stars have exactly 1.4 solar masses and performing Bayesian inference with uniform priors on radii from 6 km to 16 km using the nested-sampling package UltraNest. For J0030, three different hotspot configuration models (ST+PST, ST+PDT, and PDT-U) were examined to assess their impact on the inferred radius.

## Key numbers

* Scenario 1 with ST+PST hotspot model: R1.4 = 11.66 km with 16% quantile at 10.81 km and 84% quantile at 12.54 km
* Scenario 1 with ST+PDT hotspot model: R1.4 = 11.67 km with 95% credible interval bounds of -1.50 km and +1.42 km
* Scenario 1 with PDT-U hotspot model: R1.4 = 11.99 km with 95% credible interval bounds of -1.57 km and +1.62 km
* Scenario 2 with ST+PST hotspot model: R1.4 = 12.67 km with 95% credible interval bounds of -0.86 km and +1.03 km
* Scenario 2 with ST+PDT hotspot model: R1.4 = 12.09 km with 95% credible interval bounds of -0.63 km and +0.81 km
* Scenario 2 with PDT-U hotspot model: R1.4 = 12.29 km with 95% credible interval bounds of -0.77 km and +1.02 km
* J0437 mass from radio observations: 1.44 +/- 0.07 solar masses (Reardon 2024)
* J0030 mass from Riley (2019) under ST+PST: 1.34 solar masses with -0.16 and +0.15 uncertainty
* J0030 radius from Riley (2019) under ST+PST: 12.71 km with -1.19 and +1.14 uncertainty

## Context

This work extends the NICER observational program (Miller 2019, Riley 2019, Vinciguerra 2024, Choudhury 2024) by providing a method to extract R1.4 directly from mass-radius measurements of neutron stars near 1.4 solar masses, avoiding the systematic uncertainties from EOS model selection that affect traditional approaches (Raaijmakers 2019, Raaijmakers 2020, Raaijmakers 2021, Huang 2023, Huang 2024, Rutherford 2024). The method enables future X-ray telescopes to constrain R1.4 in a data-driven manner and can help resolve tensions between nuclear experiments such as C-REX and P-REX regarding neutron skin thickness measurements.
