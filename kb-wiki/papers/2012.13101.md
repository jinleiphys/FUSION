# Detection and Parameter Estimation of Gravitational Waves from Binary Neutron-Star Mergers in Real LIGO Data using Deep Learning

> One of the key challenges of real-time detection and parameter estimation of gravitational waves from compact binary mergers is the computational cost of conventional matched-filtering and Bayesian inference approaches. In particular, the application of these methods to the full signal parameter space available to the gravitational-wave detectors, and/or real-time parameter estimation is computationally prohibitive. On the other hand, rapid detection and inference are critical for prompt follow-up of the electromagnetic and astro-particle counterparts accompanying important transients, such as binary neutron-star and black-hole neutron-star mergers. Training deep neural networks to identify specific signals and learn a computationally efficient representation of the mapping between gravitational-wave signals and their parameters allows both detection and inference to be done quickly and reliably, with high sensitivity and accuracy. In this work we apply a deep-learning approach to rapidly identify and characterize transient gravitational-wave signals from binary neutron-star mergers in real LIGO data. We show for the first time that artificial neural networks can promptly detect and characterize binary neutron star gravitational-wave signals in real LIGO data, and distinguish them from noise and signals from coalescing black-hole binaries. We illustrate this key result by demonstrating that our deep-learning framework classifies correctly all gravitational-wave events from the Gravitational-Wave Transient Catalog, GWTC-1 [Phys. Rev. X 9 (2019), 031040]. These results emphasize the importance of using realistic gravitational-wave detector data in machine learning approaches, and represent a step towards achieving real-time detection and inference of gravitational waves.

# Detection and Parameter Estimation of Gravitational Waves from Binary Neutron-Star Mergers in Real LIGO Data using Deep Learning

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate for the first time that deep neural networks can detect and characterize binary neutron-star gravitational-wave signals in real LIGO data (not just simulated Gaussian noise), and distinguish them from noise and binary black-hole signals, but the parameter estimation results are presented as deterministic point-wise estimates that should be considered a "proof-of-concept" study rather than a complete probabilistic inference framework.

## Method
The authors trained two convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with similar architecture: a detection CNN for classifying binary neutron-star (BNS) signals, binary black-hole (BBH) signals, and detector noise; and a regression CNN for estimating BNS component masses. They used real LIGO O2 data from the Livingston detector (L1) sampled at 4096 Hz, injected simulated gravitational-wave signals generated with LALSuite (TaylorF2 waveform model for BNS with component masses 1-2 solar masses and APR equation of state; SEOBNRv2 waveform model for BBH with component masses 5-50 solar masses), and whitened both signals and noise separately using power spectral density computed by Welch's method. The detection CNN was trained with 144,000 time series samples using curriculum learning starting at high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR greater than 50) and gradually decreasing to SNR range 3-20, while the regression CNN was trained on approximately 151,000 samples with BNS component masses from 1 to 2 solar masses.

## Key numbers
- The detection CNN correctly classified all 11 gravitational-wave events from GWTC-1 with probability greater than 99.5%
- For BNS signals, the CNN achieved maximal sensitivity at optimal SNR of 18 (for false alarm probability at or above 10^{-3})
- For BBH signals, the CNN achieved maximal sensitivity at optimal SNR of 10 (for false alarm probability at or above 10^{-3})
- At SNR greater than or equal to 11, the regression CNN estimated BNS component masses with mean absolute error smaller than 0.1 solar masses
- Processing 10 seconds of gravitational-wave data took approximately 3 milliseconds on GPUs with both CNNs
- The neural network models were 83 MB in size each, encoding information for approximately 150,000 templates (about 45 GB of data)
- When using the model trained on Gaussian noise instead of real LIGO noise, the detection CNN misclassified about 27% of real gravitational-wave events (3 BBH events misclassified, 2 more identified with lower detection probabilities)

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous deep-learning approach for detecting BNS signals in simulated Gaussian noise (Krastev, 2019) to real LIGO data, and builds upon pioneering applications of convolutional neural networks to gravitational-wave astrophysics by George and Huerta (2016) and Gabbard et al. (2017), who demonstrated CNNs could detect simulated BBH signals. The results demonstrate that using realistic detector noise in training is critical, as models trained on Gaussian noise misclassified approximately 27% of real events. This work enables future extensions to full 9-dimensional parameter space estimation (including sky location, distance, inclination, spins, and tidal deformabilities) and real-time multi-messenger follow-up of BNS and black-hole neutron-star mergers, though the authors note that glitches must be explicitly included in training for realistic searches.
