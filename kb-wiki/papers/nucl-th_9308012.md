# Low-energy M1 and E3 excitations in the proton-rich Kr-Zr region

> Low-energy intrinsic $K^π$=1$^+$, $0^-$, $1^-$, $2^-$, and $3^-$ states in the even-even proton-rich Sr, Kr, and Zr nuclei are investigated using the quasiparticle random phase approximation. In the Z$\simeq$N nuclei the lowest-lying 1$^+$ states are found to carry unusually large $B(M1)$ strength. It is demonstrated that, unlike in the heavier nuclei, the octupole collectivity in the light zirconium region is small and, thus, is not directly correlated with the systematics of the lowest negative parity states.

# Low-energy M1 and E3 excitations in the proton-rich Kr-Zr region

## Key claim
The lowest-lying 1⁺ states in Z≈N nuclei such as ⁷⁶Sr carry unusually large B(M1) strength (around 1–2 μ_N²), but this strength arises from (g₉/₂)² two-quasiparticle excitations rather than from a collective "scissors mode", the overlap between the QRPA wave function of the lowest 1⁺ state in ⁷⁶Sr and the synthetic orbital scissors state is only about 12%. The octupole collectivity in the light zirconium region is small and not directly correlated with the systematics of the lowest negative-parity states, contrary to what had been suggested based on energy systematics alone.

## Method
The authors performed quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) calculations using a deformed Woods-Saxon single-particle Hamiltonian with monopole-pairing fields, a long-ranged residual interaction (mainly quadrupole-quadrupole type), and a spin-spin residual interaction. For octupole correlations, they used a doubly-stretched octupole-dipole interaction within a Nilsson basis spanning 7 major shells, with coupling strengths determined by self-consistency conditions for the harmonic oscillator model. Two variants of pairing strength were considered: "standard" pairing gaps from Table 1, and "weak pairing" with Δₚ and Δₙ reduced by 50% relative to standard values.

## Key numbers
- In ⁷⁶Sr with weak pairing, the lowest 1⁺ state is predicted at 2.2 MeV with B(M1; g.s. → 1⁺) = 2.16 μ_N².
- The B(M1) values calculated in the "weak pairing" variant are approximately twice as large as those obtained in the "standard pairing" variant.
- The K^π=1⁺ isovector giant quadrupole resonance in ⁷⁶Sr lies at E_ex ~ 32 MeV and carries ~4 μ_N² of M1 strength, with about 50% scissors-mode component.
- In ⁸²Sr, the superdeformed configuration yields two 1⁺ states around 3 MeV and 4 MeV carrying large M1 strength.
- The most collective octupole state in the Kr isotopes is the K^π=3⁻ excitation in ⁷²Kr at E_ex ~ 3.2 MeV with B(E3) ~ 10 s.p.u., built upon the oblate minimum.
- In ⁸²Sr, the K^π=1⁻ and 2⁻ states in the oblate configuration appear at E_ex ~ 2.7 MeV with B(E3) ~ 7 s.p.u.
- The K^π=2⁻ modes in prolate ⁷⁸,⁸⁰,⁸²Sr are expected at about E_ex = 2.7 MeV carrying E3 strength around 6 s.p.u.
- In the superdeformed configuration of ⁸²Sr, a low-lying K^π=1⁻ excitation is predicted at E_ex ~ 2.3 MeV with B(E3) ~ 10 s.p.u.
- In the "weak pairing" variant for ⁷⁶Sr, the B(E3; g.s. → 1⁻) rate is reduced by a factor of ~3 compared to standard pairing.
- The ground-state deformation of ⁷⁶Sr and ⁸⁰Zr is around β₂ = 0.4.
- The deformed-to-spherical shape transition is expected to occur around N ~ 44.

## Context
This work extends earlier mean-field calculations for the A~80 mass region by Nazarewicz (1985) and Bengtsson (1988), which suggested shape coexistence and large deformations in Sr, Kr, and Zr isotopes. The study addresses the long-standing question of octupole collectivity in the light zirconium region, where earlier work by Cottle (1989) had argued that some negative-parity bands could be interpreted as collective octupole bands based on energy systematics. The present QRPA results contradict that interpretation, showing that the low-lying negative-parity states in well-deformed prolate nuclei have a dominant two-quasiparticle character. The predictions for large M1 strength in ⁷⁶Sr and neighboring nuclei are intended to stimulate experimental investigations using radioactive ion beam facilities and new-generation multidetector arrays.
