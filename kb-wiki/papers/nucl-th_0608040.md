# A relativistic framework to determine the nuclear transparency from A(p,2p) reactions

> A relativistic framework for computing the nuclear transparency extracted from A(p,2p) scattering processes is presented. The model accounts for the initial- and final-state interactions (IFSI) within the relativistic multiple-scattering Glauber approximation (RMSGA). For the description of color transparency, two existing models are used. The nuclear filtering mechanism is implemented as a possible explanation for the oscillatory energy dependence of the transparency. Results are presented for the target nuclei 7Li, 12C, 27Al, and 63Cu. An approximated, computationally less intensive version of the RMSGA framework is found to be sufficiently accurate for the calculation of the nuclear transparency. After including the nuclear filtering and color transparency mechanisms, our calculations are in acceptable agreement with the data.

## Key claim
The relativistic multiple-scattering Glauber approximation (RMSGA) framework, when combined with nuclear filtering and color transparency models, yields acceptable qualitative agreement with measured A(p,2p) nuclear transparencies, but the quantitative description is far from perfect and the parameters of the color transparency models cannot be unambiguously constrained.

## Method
The authors extended a previously developed relativistic and cross-section factorized framework for A(p,2p) reactions to incorporate the Ralston-Pire model for the pp scattering amplitude, which separates the amplitude into a quark-counting (point-like configuration) component and a Landshoff (normal-sized) component. Initial- and final-state interactions were treated using the relativistic multiple-scattering Glauber approximation (RMSGA), with color transparency implemented via two models: the quantum diffusion model of Farrar et al. (FLFS) and the hadronic model of Jennings and Miller (JM). Nuclear filtering was implemented by applying different attenuation treatments to the two components of the pp amplitude, with the quark-counting component experiencing reduced attenuation due to color transparency while the Landshoff component experienced standard Glauber attenuation.

## Key numbers
- For 12C(p,2p), the standard RMSGA calculations (without CT or NF) produce a nuclear transparency of approximately 0.15, nearly independent of beam momentum.
- The approximated RMSGA' framework agrees with the full RMSGA calculations at the 2-3% level while reducing computational cost by a factor of about 10^3.
- The incident lab momentum range studied is 5.9 to 14.4 GeV/c, with |t| values from 4.7 to 12.7 (GeV/c)^2.
- For the FLFS color transparency model, the squared mass difference parameter ΔM^2 was varied between 0.7 and 1.1 (GeV/c^2)^2.
- For the JM color transparency model, the complex mass M* was tested at (1232 + 150i) MeV and (1440 + 150i) MeV.
- The RMSGA+CT+NF calculations reproduce the maximum in 12C(p,2p) transparency at about 9.5 GeV/c.
- Inclusion of color screening effects would decrease the transparency by 6-12%, with the largest effect at higher p1.
- At 6 GeV/c, the FLFS calculations with ΔM^2 = 0.7 (GeV/c^2)^2 give T ∝ A^(-0.83); at 10 GeV/c, T ∝ A^(-0.70).

## Context
This work extends the relativistic multiple-scattering Glauber approximation framework previously developed by the same authors for A(e,e'p) reactions (Ryckebusch 2003, Lava 2004) to A(p,2p) reactions, incorporating the Ralston-Pire model (Ralston 1988) for the pp scattering amplitude and the nuclear filtering mechanism (Jain 1996). The calculations confirm earlier results by Jennings and Miller (1992, 1993) and Lee (1992) regarding the need for color transparency to increase calculated transparencies to the level of experimental data. The framework enables systematic comparison of two different color transparency models (Farrar 1988 and Jennings 1991) and provides a computationally efficient approximation suitable for calculating integrated quantities like nuclear transparency, while identifying that improved data at higher energies (such as from the planned J-PARC 50 GeV synchrotron) are essential to resolve remaining uncertainties.
