# Measuring Mass and Radius of the Maximum-mass Nonrotating Neutron Star

> The mass ($M_{\rm TOV}$) and radius ($R_{\rm TOV}$) of the maximum-mass nonrotating neutron star (NS) play a crucial role in constraining the elusive equation of state of cold dense matter and in predicting the fate of remnants from binary neutron star (BNS) mergers. In this study, we introduce a novel method to deduce these parameters by examining the mergers of second-generation (2G) black holes (BHs) with NSs. These 2G BHs are assumed to originate from supramassive neutron stars (SMNSs) formed in BNS mergers. Since the properties of the remnant BHs arising from the collapse of SMNSs follow a universal relation governed by $M_{\rm TOV}$ and $R_{\rm TOV}$, we anticipate that by analyzing a series ($\sim 100$ detections) of mass and spin measurements of the 2G BHs using the third-generation ground-based gravitational-wave detectors, $M_{\rm TOV}$ and $R_{\rm TOV}$ can be determined with a precision of $\sim 0.01M_\odot$ and $\sim 0.6$ km, respectively.

# Measuring Mass and Radius of the Maximum-mass Nonrotating Neutron Star

## Key claim
By analyzing approximately 100 detections of second-generation black hole mergers with neutron stars using third-generation gravitational-wave detectors, the mass and radius of the maximum-mass nonrotating neutron star can be determined with a precision of about 0.01 solar masses and 0.6 km, respectively, provided that the second-generation black holes originate from supramassive neutron stars formed in binary neutron star mergers and that the universal relation between critical mass and spin holds.

## Method
The authors first compute the stability line (critical mass versus dimensionless spin) for supramassive neutron stars using the Rapidly Rotating Neutron Star (RNS) code with the BSk21 equation of state. They then simulate a population of binary neutron star mergers to determine which produce supramassive neutron stars that collapse into second-generation black holes, using empirical fitting formulas for ejecta and disk masses. For the subsequent black hole-neutron star mergers detectable by future detectors (Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer), they estimate measurement uncertainties for black hole mass and spin using the Fisher-matrix code GWFAST with the IMRPhenomXP_NRTidalv2 waveform template. Finally, they apply orthogonal distance regression fitting to mock measurements to recover the maximum-mass nonrotating neutron star parameters.

## Key numbers
- Precision of M_TOV determination: approximately 0.01 solar masses with 100 detections including electromagnetic counterpart information
- Precision of R_TOV determination: approximately 0.6 km with 100 detections including electromagnetic counterpart information
- For the "GW only" case with 100 detections: M_TOV precision of 0.014 solar masses and R_TOV precision of 0.9 km
- Black hole mass measurement uncertainty from gravitational waves alone: 0.093 solar masses (median, with 68.3% credible interval -0.049/+0.060)
- Black hole mass measurement uncertainty with electromagnetic counterpart assistance: 0.026 solar masses (median, with 68.3% credible interval -0.015/+0.026)
- Universal relation coefficients: c2 = 0.0902(2), c4 = 0.0193(2)
- BSk21 equation of state parameters: M_b,TOV = 2.72 solar masses, M_b,kep = 3.22 solar masses, chi_kep = 0.7

## Context
This work extends the universal relation between critical mass and spin for supramassive neutron stars previously established by Shao et al. (2020) and applies it to a novel observational channel: second-generation black hole-neutron star mergers. The method provides an independent measurement of M_TOV and R_TOV that complements constraints from pulsar timing (e.g., PSR J0740+6620 from Fonseca et al. 2021), NICER observations (Riley et al. 2019, Miller et al. 2019), and multimessenger analyses of GW170817 (Abbott et al. 2019). If successful, this approach could enable precise equation of state constraints and potentially distinguish between neutron stars and quark stars, as the universal relation differs between these compact object types (Bhattacharyya et al. 2018, Zhou et al. 2019).
