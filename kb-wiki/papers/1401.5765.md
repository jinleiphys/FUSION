# Beyond the constant-mass Dirac physics: Solitons, charge fractionization, and the emergence of topological insulators in graphene rings

> The doubly-connected polygonal geometry of planar graphene rings is found to bring forth topological configurations for accessing nontrivial relativistic quantum field (RQF) theory models that carry beyond the constant-mass Dirac-fermion theory. These include generation of sign-alternating masses, solitonic excitations, and charge fractionization. The work integrates a RQF Lagrangian formulation with numerical tight-binding Aharonov-Bohm electronic spectra and the generalized position-dependent-mass Dirac equation. In contrast to armchair graphene rings (aGRGs) with pure metallic arms, certain classes of aGRGs with semiconducting arms, as well as with mixed metallic-semiconducting ones, are shown to exhibit properties of one-dimensional nontrivial topological insulators. This further reveals an alternative direction for realizing a graphene-based nontrivial topological insulator through the manipulation of the honeycomb lattice geometry, without a spin-orbit contribution.

# Beyond the constant-mass Dirac physics: Solitons, charge fractionization, and the emergence of topological insulators in graphene rings

## Key claim

Armchair graphene rings (aGRGs) with semiconducting arms (classes I and II) or mixed metallic-semiconducting arms can exhibit properties of one-dimensional nontrivial topological insulators through manipulation of the honeycomb lattice geometry alone, without requiring spin-orbit coupling, but only certain classes of aGRGs show this behavior, while pure metallic-arm rings (class III) do not.

## Method

The authors combine a relativistic quantum field (RQF) Lagrangian formulation with numerical tight-binding (TB) calculations of Aharonov-Bohm electronic spectra for hexagonal armchair graphene nanorings, and a generalized position-dependent-mass Dirac equation implemented via a Dirac-Kronig-Penney (DKP) superlattice model. The DKP model uses a scalar Higgs field φ(x) with a double-well φ⁴ potential, where the mass parameter along each ring arm is determined by the hopping parameters t₁ and t₂ derived from the effective 1D tight-binding problem for armchair graphene nanoribbons of width N_W. The TB spectra are computed using a nearest-neighbor tight-binding Hamiltonian with a Peierls phase factor for the magnetic field, and the DKP transfer-matrix method reproduces these spectra by assigning piecewise-constant mass values m_i^(n) to segments of each arm.

## Key numbers

- Hopping parameter for 2D graphene: t = 2.7 eV
- Armchair nanoribbon classes: N_W = 3l (semiconducting, Δ > 0), N_W = 3l+1 (semiconducting, Δ > 0), N_W = 3l+2 (metallic, Δ = 0), for l = 1,2,3,...
- For class-II ring (N_W = 16): DKP model uses alternating masses m₂^(n) = (-1)^n m₀ with m₀ = 0.18 t/v_F², arm segments a = 7 a₀ and b = 15 a₀, where a₀ = 0.246 nm is the graphene lattice constant
- For class-I ring (N_W = 15): DKP model uses m₁^(n) = m₃^(n) = 0.06 t/v_F², m₂^(n) = 0.13 t/v_F², with a = 2 a₀, b = 28 a₀
- For mixed ring (four class-III arms N_W = 17, two class-I arms N_W = 15): DKP parameters m₀,₁ = 0.01 t/v_F², m₀,₂ = 0.20 t/v_F², a = 10 a₀, b = 10 a₀
- Forbidden band levels cross zero energy at magnetic flux values Φ = (±j + 1/2) Φ₀, j = 1,2,3,..., where Φ₀ = hc/e is the flux quantum
- TB state energy in forbidden band of class-II ring at Φ = Φ₀/3: ε = 0.12507 × 10⁻² t
- TB state energy in mixed ring at Φ = Φ₀/3: ε = 0.1995 × 10⁻² t

## Context

This work extends the Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) model for polyacetylene (Heeger et al., 1988) and its Jackiw-Rebbi relativistic quantum field counterpart (Jackiw and Rebbi, 1976) to graphene ring geometries, showing that the doubly-connected polygonal structure enables topological configurations beyond constant-mass Dirac physics. The findings provide an alternative direction for realizing graphene-based topological insulators without spin-orbit coupling, which is negligible in planar graphene and currently being pursued through adatom deposition (Weeks et al., 2011). The results enable experimental exploration of solitonic excitations, charge fractionization (including e/6 and e/2 fractional charges), and 1D nontrivial topological insulator states in atomically precise graphene nanostructures (Cai et al., 2010), artificial graphene (Manoharan et al., 2012), and ultracold-atom optical lattice mimics (Tarruell et al., 2012).
