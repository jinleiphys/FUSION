# Testing the collectivity in large and small colliding systems with test particles

> We propose a test-particle method to probe the transport dynamics of the establishment and development of collective flow in large and small systems of heavy-ion collisions. We place test particles as passengers into the partonic medium created by Au$+$Au midcentral collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 200 GeV and $p$ $+$ Pb central collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 5.02 TeV, using a multiphase transport model. With the help of test particles in two extreme test cases, we demonstrate that parton collisions play an important role in establishing and developing collectivity in large and small colliding systems. The collectivity established by final state parton collisions is much stronger in large colliding systems compared to small colliding systems. The collectivity from the initial state can persist or survive more easily in small colliding systems than in large colliding systems due to fewer parton collisions. Our study provides a new method to understand the origin of collectivity in large and small colliding systems at the BNL Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and the CERN Large Hadron Collider.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a test-particle method to probe the transport dynamics of collective flow, demonstrating that parton collisions play a crucial role in establishing collectivity in both large and small colliding systems, but the collectivity from the initial state can persist more easily in small systems due to fewer parton collisions.

## Method

The authors used the string melting version of the AMPT (a multiphase transport) model to simulate Au+Au midcentral collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV and p+Pb central collisions at √sNN = 5.02 TeV. They placed four test particles as passengers into the partonic medium at specific positions and times, with two extreme initial velocity configurations: case 1 with isotropic velocities (zero initial collectivity) and case 2 with a common velocity (strongest initial collectivity). The time evolution of eight defined observables measuring dispersities and collectivity of the test particles was calculated for different parton interaction cross sections.

## Key numbers

- Au+Au midcentral collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV with impact parameter b = 8 fm; p+Pb central collisions at √sNN = 5.02 TeV with b = 0 fm
- Test particles placed at position r0 = (3, 0, 0 fm) for Au+Au and r0 = (2, 0, 0 fm) for p+Pb at time t0 = 1 fm/c
- Test particle mass set to 0.05 GeV, with initial velocity β giving transverse momentum pT ≈ 0.1 GeV/c
- Parton interaction cross section σ = 3 mb used as default, with comparisons to σ = 2 mb, 1 mb, and 0 mb
- 10^4 events simulated for each system and case
- Effective screening mass μ = 2.265 fm^-1 leading to total cross section of about 3 mb for elastic scattering
- Strong coupling constant αs = 0.33

## Context

This work extends previous studies using the AMPT model to describe collective flow in both large and small systems (Lin 2014, OrjuelaKoop 2015, Ma 2016). It builds on the parton escape mechanism proposed by He (2015) and Lin (2015) for small systems, and the demonstration that parton collisions are crucial for generating anisotropic flow (Ma 2016, Ma 2021). The test-particle method provides a new tool to distinguish between initial-state collectivity (e.g., from color glass condensate as studied by Dumitru 2010, Dusling 2013) and final-state collectivity from parton collisions, enabling systematic studies of how collectivity emerges across different system sizes.
