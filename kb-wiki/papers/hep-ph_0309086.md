# Baryon Asymmetry, Dark Matter and Quantum Chromodynamics

> We propose a novel scenario to explain the observed cosmological asymmetry between matter and antimatter, based on nonperturbative QCD physics. This scenario relies on a mechanism of separation of quarks and antiquarks in two coexisting phases at the end of the cosmological QCD phase transition: ordinary hadrons (and antihadrons), along with massive lumps (and antilumps) of novel color superconducting phase. The latter would serve as the cosmological cold dark matter. In certain conditions the separation of charge is C and CP asymmetric and can leave a net excess of hadrons over antihadrons in the conventional phase, even if the visible universe is globally baryon symmetric $B = 0$. In this case an equal, but negative, overall baryon charge must be hidden in the lumps of novel phase. Due to the small volume occupied by these dense lumps/antilumps of color superconducting phase and the specific features of their interaction with "normal" matter in hadronic phase, this scenario does not contradict the current phenomenological constrains on presence of antimatter in the visible universe. Moreover, in this scenario the observed cosmological ratio $Ω_{DM}\simΩ_{B}$ within an order of magnitude finds a natural explanation, as both contributions to $Ω$ originated from the same physics during the QCD phase transition. The baryon to entropy ratio $n_{B}/n_γ\sim 10^{-10}$ would also be a natural outcome, fixed by the temperature $T_f \simlt T_{QCD}$ at which the separation of phases is completed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the observed cosmological baryon asymmetry and dark matter abundance can both arise from nonperturbative QCD physics during the cosmological QCD phase transition, without requiring a net global baryon charge (B = 0) or new fundamental particles, provided that large C and CP asymmetries existed homogeneously in the quark-gluon plasma prior to the transition.

## Method

The authors develop a scenario in which quarks and antiquarks separate into two coexisting phases at the end of the QCD phase transition: ordinary hadrons and antihadrons in the conventional phase, and massive lumps and antilumps of color superconducting (CS) phase made of condensed quarks or antiquarks. They analyze the phenomenological viability of this scenario by studying the interaction of hadronic matter with CS lumps and antilumps, calculating reflection and transmission coefficients for quarks at the CS interface, and deriving constraints from observational data on antimatter in the present universe. The authors also estimate the baryon-to-photon ratio and the dark matter-to-baryon density ratio that would naturally emerge from this mechanism.

## Key numbers

- The observed baryon-to-photon ratio is η ≡ n_B/n_γ ≃ 6 × 10^{-10}, measured with high accuracy from cosmic microwave background data (Spergel 2003).
- The QCD phase transition temperature is T_QCD ≃ 160 MeV.
- The color superconducting gap is Δ ∼ 100 MeV, and lumps of CS phase become absolutely stable against decay into ordinary nuclear matter at temperatures T ≲ 0.57Δ ∼ 60 MeV.
- The observed cosmological ratio is Ω_B ∼ (1/6)Ω_DM, with baryonic matter contributing only a fraction of the total matter content.
- The nucleosynthesis temperature is T_nc ∼ 1 MeV, and the matter-radiation equality temperature is T_eq ∼ 1 eV.
- The nucleon mass is m_N ∼ 1 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the original proposal by Witten (1984) that massive lumps of ordinary quark matter in a novel state could form at the end of the QCD phase transition and serve as dark matter, but adds the crucial new element that antilumps of condensed antiquarks can also form, allowing the universe to have zero net baryon charge while still producing the observed hadronic excess. The scenario resembles an earlier idea by Widrow (1989) of a baryon-asymmetric universe with zero overall baryon charge, but replaces hypothetical new scalar fields with ordinary QCD matter in the color superconducting phase. The paper builds on recent observations by Madsen (2001) and Lugones (2003) that chunks of condensed quarks in CS phase can become absolutely stable objects, and on the frustrated collapse of axion domain walls mechanism (QCD-balls) that could form such lumps even if the QCD phase transition is not first order. The scenario enables a natural explanation for why Ω_DM and Ω_B are within an order of magnitude of each other, as both originate from the same QCD physics during the phase transition.
