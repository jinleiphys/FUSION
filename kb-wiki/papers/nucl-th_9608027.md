# The Effects of Bose-Condensates on Single Inclusive Spectra and Bose-Einstein Correlations

> The implications of the formation of a Bose condensate on one- and two-particle spectra are studied for ultrarelativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions in the framework of a hydrodynamic description. It is found that single particle spectra are considerably enhanced at low momenta. The Bose-Einstein correlation function has an intercept below two. For pion pairs in the central region a two-component structure may appear in the correlation function, which is different from that found in quantum optics. The chaoticity parameter is strongly momentum dependent.

## Key claim

The formation of a pionic Bose condensate in ultrarelativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions can produce a strong enhancement of single inclusive spectra at low momenta and a reduction of the Bose-Einstein correlation function intercept below two, but only over a limited momentum range set by the maximum fluid velocity at freeze-out; the effect is most pronounced for condensate fractions as small as 1% of the thermal density.

## Method

The authors model the expanding pion source using two hydrodynamic scenarios: a spherically expanding source and a longitudinally scaling (Bjorken-type) expanding source, both with a space-like freeze-out hypersurface. They decompose the pion emission current into a thermal component (described by a Bose-Einstein distribution with chemical potential equal to the pion mass) and a condensate component (where each fluid cell emits pions only at the momentum corresponding to its collective velocity). The single inclusive spectrum and the two-particle Bose-Einstein correlation function are then computed from the correlators of these currents, assuming the condensate phases in different fluid cells are completely uncorrelated.

## Key numbers

- For S+S collisions at 200 AGeV, the maximum fluid velocity at freeze-out is \(u_{max}=0.494\,c\), yielding a maximum condensate-contributed momentum \(k_{max}=68.7\ \mathrm{MeV}/c\) and a maximum momentum difference for BEC reduction \(q_{max}=137.4\ \mathrm{MeV}/c\).
- A condensate number density of only \(1\%\) of the thermal density (\(\kappa = n_{co} = 0.01\,n_{th}\)) reduces the intercept of the two-particle correlation function to 1.83 for the spherical source and 1.86 for the longitudinally expanding source.
- The absolute theoretical maximum momentum from a pionic Bose condensate is \(k_{max}=m_\pi c = 139.6\ \mathrm{MeV}/c\), with a corresponding \(q_{max}=279.2\ \mathrm{MeV}/c\).
- The freeze-out temperature is taken as \(T_f = m_\pi = 0.139\ \mathrm{GeV}\), the transverse radius as \(R_\perp = 3.8\ \mathrm{fm}\), and the parameters \(\tau_0 = \alpha_0 = 4.5\) (in units of c/fm and /fm·c respectively) with \(u_0 = 0.13\ \mathrm{fm}/c\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier quantum-statistical treatment of Bose condensates in heavy-ion collisions by Fowler and Weiner (1980s), which used a Landau-Ginzburg approach and found that interactions affect the shape but not the intercept of the correlation function. The present hydrodynamic approach explicitly includes source expansion and the correlation between position and momentum, enabling a momentum-dependent chaoticity parameter for the first time. The results suggest that future experiments at RHIC and LHC could detect condensate signatures through a bumpy structure in the BEC at low relative momentum, though the observability depends on source size, freeze-out velocity, resonance decay contributions, and experimental acceptance.
