# Isospin diffusion from $^{40,48}$Ca$+^{40,48}$Ca experimental data at Fermi energies: Direct comparisons with transport model calculations

> This article presents an investigation of isospin equilibration in cross-bombarding $^{40,48}$Ca$+^{40,48}$Ca reactions at 35 MeV/nucleon, by comparing experimental data with filtered transport model calculations. Isospin diffusion is studied using the evolution of the isospin transport ratio with centrality. The asymmetry parameter $δ=(N-Z)/A$ of the quasiprojectile (QP) residue is used as isospin-sensitive observable, while a recent method for impact parameter reconstruction is used for centrality sorting. A benchmark of global observables is proposed to assess the relevance of the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) model, coupled to GEMINI++, in the study of dissipative collisions. Our results demonstrate the importance of considering cluster formation to reproduce observables used for isospin transport and centrality studies. Within the AMD model, we prove the applicability of the impact parameter reconstruction method, enabling a direct comparison to the experimental data for the investigation of isospin diffusion. For both, we evidence a tendency to isospin equilibration with an impact parameter decreasing from 9 to 3 fm, while the full equilibration is not reached. A weak sensitivity to the stiffness of the equation of state employed in the model is also observed, with a better reproduction of the experimental trend for the neutron-rich reactions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that isospin equilibration in Ca+Ca reactions at 35 MeV/nucleon increases as the impact parameter decreases from 9 to 3 fm, but full equilibration is not reached; the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) model with cluster correlations reproduces experimental data better than the version without clusters, though both models show only weak sensitivity to the stiffness of the symmetry energy.

## Method

The authors measured cross-bombarding reactions of 40,48Ca+40,48Ca at 35 MeV/nucleon using the INDRA 4π charged-particle array coupled to the VAMOS high-acceptance spectrometer at GANIL. They compared experimental data with filtered transport model calculations using two versions of the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) model (AMD-nc without cluster correlations and AMD-CC with cluster correlations), both coupled to the GEMINI++ statistical decay code. Isospin diffusion was studied via the isospin transport ratio as a function of centrality, using the asymmetry parameter δ=(N−Z)/A of the quasiprojectile (QP) residue as the isospin-sensitive observable, with impact parameter reconstruction performed using a recent method.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 35 MeV/nucleon for all 40,48Ca+40,48Ca reactions
- Impact parameter range studied: from 9 fm down to 3 fm, where isospin equilibration increases but full equilibration is not reached
- Grazing impact parameter: about 9.7 fm for 40Ca+40Ca and 10.4 fm for 48Ca+48Ca
- AMD-nc simulations: approximately 100,000 primary events per system, followed up to t_lim=300 fm/c
- AMD-CC simulations: approximately 40,000 primary events per system, followed up to t_lim=500 fm/c
- Filtered simulated events represent about 22% (AMD-CC) and 18% (AMD-nc) of total statistics after applying experimental filter and offline selections
- About 75% of simulated events are discarded due to spectrometer angular acceptance and trigger condition
- VAMOS mass resolution: ΔA/A ≈ 1/165
- INDRA fragment identification thresholds: about 0.5 MeV/nucleon for lightest fragments (Z≲10) and 1.5 MeV/nucleon for heaviest fragments
- Data selection criteria retain about 90% and 87% of total experimental statistics for 40Ca and 48Ca projectile reactions, respectively

## Context

This work extends previous studies of isospin transport in heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies, particularly building on earlier investigations by the same collaboration using the INDRA-VAMOS setup (Fable 2023) and on prior AMD model comparisons for Ca+Ca systems (Wada 1998, Camaiani 2020). The study enables direct comparisons between experimental data and transport model calculations using identical observables and impact parameter reconstruction, addressing the need for improved interpretation of experimental observables to refine knowledge of the nuclear symmetry energy as highlighted by Huth (2022) and Lynch (2022).
