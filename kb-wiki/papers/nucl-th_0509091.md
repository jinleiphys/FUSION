# Critical End Point and Its Consequences

> Recently a lot of evidence that there exists a critical end point (CEP) in the QCD phase diagram has been accumulating. However, so far, no reliable equation of state with the CEP has been employed in hydrodynamical calculations. In this article, we construct the equations of state with the CEP on the basis of the universality hypothesis and show that the CEP acts as an attractor of isentropic trajectories. We also consider the time evolution in the case with the CEP and discuss how the CEP affects the final state observables.

# Critical End Point and Its Consequences

## Key claim
The critical end point (CEP) in the QCD phase diagram acts as an attractor of isentropic trajectories, meaning that hydrodynamical evolution near the CEP does not require fine-tuning of collision energy to make the system pass near the CEP; however, the authors caution that even if the system passes through the CEP before freezeout, the correlation length at freezeout does not necessarily show substantial enhancement due to critical slowing down and final state interactions in the hadron phase.

## Method
The authors constructed equations of state with the CEP by combining a singular part derived from the three-dimensional Ising model (based on the universality hypothesis that the QCD CEP belongs to the same universality class) with non-singular entropy densities from the hadron phase (excluded volume approximation) and the QGP phase (Bag model). They mapped the Ising model variables (r, h) onto the QCD variables (T, μ_B) and matched the singular entropy density near the CEP with the non-singular entropy densities away from the CEP. The time evolution of the correlation length was then computed assuming Bjorken scaling with an initial temperature of 200 MeV and proper time of 1 fm/c.

## Key numbers
- The CEP is located at (T_E, μ_BE) = (154.7 MeV, 367.8 MeV).
- The critical region parameters used are ΔT_crit = 100 MeV, Δμ_Bcrit = 200 MeV, and D = 0.15.
- Isentropic trajectories are shown for n_B/s values of 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, and 0.04 (with CEP) and 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04, and 0.05 (bag plus excluded volume model).
- The initial temperature for the time evolution calculation is 200 MeV and the initial proper time is 1 fm/c.
- The equilibrium correlation length ξ_eq along the trajectory with n_B/s = 0.008 is larger than that along n_B/s = 0.01, because the former approaches the CEP more closely.

## Context
This work extends earlier predictions of a CEP in the QCD phase diagram from effective theory analyses (Asakawa and Yazaki, 1989) and lattice calculations (Fodor and Katz, 2002), while also addressing questions raised about the lattice procedure (Ejiri, 2005). The authors construct the first equations of state incorporating the CEP for use in hydrodynamical calculations, which previously relied on the bag plus excluded volume model that shows no focusing of isentropic trajectories. The paper enables future hydrodynamical simulations to study how the CEP affects final state observables in heavy ion collisions, and suggests that the low kinetic freezeout temperature observed at RHIC may be anticipated on the left side of the CEP.
