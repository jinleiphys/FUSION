# Quasi-Particle Theory of Shear and Bulk Viscosities of Hadronic Matter

> A theoretical framework for the calculation of shear and bulk viscosities of hadronic matter at finite temperature is presented. The framework is based on the quasi-particle picture. It allows for an arbitrary number of hadron species with point-like interactions, and allows for both elastic and inelastic collisions. Detailed balance is ensured. The particles have temperature dependent masses arising from mean field or potential effects, which maintains self-consistency between the equation of state and the transport coefficients. As an example, we calculate the shear and bulk viscosity in the linear $σ$ model. The ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density shows a minimum in the vicinity of a rapid crossover transition, while the ratio of bulk viscosity to entropy density shows a maximum.

## Key claim

The paper presents a theoretical framework for calculating shear and bulk viscosities of hadronic matter that is self-consistent between the equation of state and transport coefficients, but the framework relies on the assumptions that quasi-particles are well-defined and that elementary interactions are local (point-like), which limits its applicability to systems where these approximations hold.

## Method

The authors derive integral equations for shear and bulk viscosities from the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation, allowing for an arbitrary number of hadron species with both elastic and inelastic collisions, including resonance formation and decay, while respecting detailed balance. They incorporate temperature-dependent effective masses from mean field or potential effects, and apply the framework to the linear sigma model with massive pions as a concrete example, using classical statistics and the relaxation time approximation.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density $\eta/s$ shows a minimum in the vicinity of a rapid crossover transition, while the ratio of bulk viscosity to entropy density $\zeta/s$ shows a maximum there.
- The conjectured lower bound from AdS/CFT correspondence is $\eta/s = 1/4\pi$ (in natural units with $\hbar = k_B = c = 1$).
- The intermediate temperature region of interest is loosely defined as $100 < T < 400$ MeV.
- For lattice QCD simulations, the lattice spacing must satisfy $a < 0.05$ fm while the box size must satisfy $L > 10$ fm, requiring at least 200 spatial lattice sites in each direction.
- The bulk viscosity formula in the relaxation time approximation is $\zeta = \frac{1}{9T} \sum_a \int \frac{d^3p}{(2\pi)^3} \frac{\tau_a(E_a)}{E_a^2} \left[ \left( 1 - 3v_s^2 \right) E_a^2 - m_a^2 \right]^2 f_a^{\rm eq}(E_a/T)$.
- The shear viscosity formula in the relaxation time approximation is $\eta = \frac{1}{15T} \sum_a \int \frac{d^3p}{(2\pi)^3} \frac{|{\bf p}|^4}{E_a^2} \tau_a(E_a) f_a^{\rm eq}(E_a/T)$.

## Context

This work extends earlier relativistic kinetic theory calculations of viscosities by de Groot (1980) and Prakash (1993) which considered only elastic collisions in dilute gases, by including inelastic collisions, resonances, mean fields, and temperature-dependent masses that are essential for accurate bulk viscosity determination as argued by Paech (2005). The framework enables systematic computation of $\eta(T)/s(T)$ and $\zeta(T)/s(T)$ for comparison with relativistic viscous fluid dynamics simulations of heavy ion collisions, complementing lattice QCD approaches by Nakamura (2004) and Meyer (2007, 2008) that are computationally expensive in the intermediate temperature region.
