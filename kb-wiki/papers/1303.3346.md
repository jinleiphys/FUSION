# Nuclear superfluidity for antimagnetic rotation in $^{105}$Cd and $^{106}$Cd

> The effect of nuclear superfluidity on antimagnetic rotation bands in $^{105}$Cd and $^{106}$Cd are investigated by the cranked shell model with the pairing correlations and the blocking effects treated by a particle-number conserving method. The experimental moments of inertia and the reduced $B(E2)$ transition values are excellently reproduced. The nuclear superfluidity is essential to reproduce the experimental moments of inertia. The two-shears-like mechanism for the antimagnetic rotation is investigated by examining the shears angle, i.e., the closing of the two proton hole angular momenta, and its sensitive dependence on the nuclear superfluidity is revealed.

# Nuclear superfluidity for antimagnetic rotation in $^{105}$Cd and $106}$Cd

## Key claim
Nuclear superfluidity (pairing correlations) is essential for reproducing the experimental moments of inertia of antimagnetic rotation bands in $^{105}$Cd and $^{106}$Cd, but the decreasing $B(E2)$ values with spin are primarily governed by deformation rather than superfluidity; the two-shears-like mechanism for antimagnetic rotation shows a sensitive dependence on nuclear superfluidity, particularly in delaying neutron alignment and making the closing of the two proton-hole angular momenta more obvious.

## Method
The cranked shell model (CSM) with a Nilsson Hamiltonian and monopole pairing interaction was solved using a particle-number conserving (PNC) method, which treats pairing correlations and blocking effects exactly by diagonalizing the Hamiltonian in a truncated cranked many-particle configuration (CMPC) space of about 1000 dimensions for both protons and neutrons. The kinematic moments of inertia $J^{(1)}$ and reduced $B(E2)$ transition probabilities were calculated from the expectation values of $J_x$ and the proton quadrupole moment $Q_{20}^\text{p}$, respectively, for the $\nu 1/2^-[550]$ band in $^{105}$Cd and the yrast band in $^{106}$Cd. The two-shears-like mechanism was examined by extracting the approximate $J_z$ component of the two $\pi g_{9/2}$ proton-hole angular momenta via $J_z = \sqrt{\langle \Psi | J_z^2 | \Psi \rangle}$.

## Key numbers
- Quadrupole deformation parameters: $\varepsilon_2 = 0.12$ for $^{105}$Cd and $\varepsilon_2 = 0.14$ for $^{106}$Cd.
- Effective pairing strengths for $^{105}$Cd: $G_p = 0.45$ MeV (protons) and $G_n = 0.80$ MeV (neutrons); for $^{106}$Cd: $G_p = 0.45$ MeV and $G_n = 0.45$ MeV.
- First backbending in $^{105}$Cd occurs at $\hbar\omega \approx 0.4$ MeV, caused by aligning one neutron pair $\nu g_{7/2}$.
- First backbending in $^{106}$Cd occurs at $\hbar\omega \approx 0.3$ MeV, caused by one pair of neutrons jumping from $\nu g_{7/2}$ to $\nu h_{11/2}$.
- For $^{105}$Cd with pairing, the expectation value of $Q_{20}$ decreases from 0.55 eb to 0.41 eb as $\hbar\omega$ increases from 0.45 MeV to 0.75 MeV; the $Q_{20}$ value and corresponding $B(E2)$ value are reduced to about 75% and 55%, respectively.
- For $^{106}$Cd, a deformation change from $\varepsilon_2 = 0.14$ to $\varepsilon_2 = 0.12$ is needed to reproduce the $B(E2)$ behavior from $\hbar\omega = 0.45$ MeV to 0.75 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the application of the particle-number conserving cranked shell model (PNC-CSM), previously successful for describing moments of inertia and identical bands in rare-earth and actinide nuclei (Zeng 1994, Liu 2002, Zeng 2001), to antimagnetic rotation in the $A=110$ mass region. It builds on experimental evidence for antimagnetic rotation in Cd isotopes (Simons 2003 for $^{106}$Cd, Choudhury 2010 for $^{105}$Cd) and prior theoretical treatments using the tilted axis cranking (TAC) model with microscopic-macroscopic models (Simons 2003) and covariant density functional theory (Zhao 2011). The results demonstrate that the PNC-CSM method, which conserves particle number exactly, can reproduce the experimental moments of inertia and $B(E2)$ values for antimagnetic rotation bands, and reveal that the two-shears-like mechanism is sensitive to nuclear superfluidity. This enables future microscopic studies of antimagnetic rotation in other mass regions and of the interplay between pairing and deformation in weakly deformed rotating nuclei.
