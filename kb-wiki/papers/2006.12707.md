# Large-scale Simulations of Antihelium Production in Cosmic-ray Interactions

> The possibility of antihelium production in interaction of cosmic rays with the interstellar gas is studied using large-scale Monte Carlo simulations. For this purpose, an energy-dependent coalescence mechanism developed previously is extended to estimate the production of light antinuclei (${}^3\overline{\text{He}}$ and ${}^4\overline{\text{He}}$). The uncertainty in the coalescence parameter and its effect on the expected antiparticle flux is also investigated. The simulated background antihelium fluxes are found to be lower than the fluxes predicted by simplified models using numerical scaling techniques.

## Key claim
The simulated background antihelium fluxes from cosmic-ray interactions with interstellar gas are lower than fluxes predicted by simplified numerical scaling models, particularly for ${}^3\overline{\text{He}}$ which is lower by almost an order of magnitude in the 1-10 GeV kinetic energy region. The coalescence parameter uncertainty produces flux bands that span from the $p_{0,G}$ value to 130% of $p_{0,G}$.

## Method
The authors used the EPOS-LHC Monte Carlo event generator to simulate over 25 trillion proton-proton collisions at 27 energy bins between 31 GeV and 12.5 TeV in the laboratory frame. An event-by-event coalescence afterburner extended from previous antideuteron work (Gomez-Coral et al., 2018) was applied to produce ${}^3\overline{\text{He}}$ and ${}^4\overline{\text{He}}$ via simultaneous and iterated coalescence mechanisms, with the coalescence momentum parameter $p_0$ varied from 70% to 130% of the best-fit value $p_{0,G}$ from antideuteron studies. The resulting production cross sections were used to calculate local source terms and propagated through the Galaxy using the MED propagation model with solar modulation to produce top-of-atmosphere fluxes.

## Key numbers
- Energy thresholds for antinuclei in p-p interactions: 17 GeV for $\overline{\text{d}}$, 31 GeV for ${}^3\overline{\text{He}}$, and 49 GeV for ${}^4\overline{\text{He}}$ in the target frame; 5.7, 7.5, and 9.7 GeV respectively in the center-of-mass frame
- Total computing power: approximately 5,000 years of CPU time, simulating more than 25 trillion proton-proton collisions
- Coalescence parameter parametrization: $p_0(T) = A / [1 + \exp(B - \ln(T/C))]$ with $A = 89.6 \pm 3.0$ MeV/$c$, $B = 6.6 \pm 0.88$, $C = 0.73 \pm 0.10$, where $T$ is collision kinetic energy in GeV
- ${}^3\overline{\text{He}}$ production per event at 100 GeV: $2.73 \times 10^{-12}$; at 158 GeV: $1.74 \times 10^{-10}$; at 600 GeV: $1.72 \times 10^{-8}$
- ${}^4\overline{\text{He}}$ production per event at 158 GeV: $5.36 \times 10^{-13}$; at 400 GeV: $1.66 \times 10^{-11}$; at 6000 GeV: $4.47 \times 10^{-10}$
- Antideuteron production per event at 31 GeV: $6.60 \times 10^{-11}$; at 158 GeV: $5.02 \times 10^{-6}$; at 12500 GeV: $2.04 \times 10^{-4}$
- The predicted $\overline{\text{p}}$ flux exceeds AMS-02 data by 20%-30% in the 1-5 GeV kinetic energy region
- The predicted ${}^3\overline{\text{He}}$ flux is lower than predictions from Poulin et al. (2019) and Korsmeier et al. (2018) by almost an order of magnitude in the 1-10 GeV kinetic energy region
- ALICE ${}^3\overline{\text{He}}$ and $\overline{\text{t}}$ production yields at $\sqrt{s} = 7$ TeV are within 10%-30% of the yield predicted using $p_{0,G}$ (90 MeV/$c$)
- ALICE $\overline{\text{d}}$ production yield at $\sqrt{s} = 13$ TeV is within 10%-20% of the yield predicted using $p_{0,G}$ (90 MeV/$c$)

## Context
This work extends the energy-dependent coalescence approach developed by Gomez-Coral et al. (2018) for antideuterons to predict ${}^3\overline{\text{He}}$ and ${}^4\overline{\text{He}}$ production in cosmic-ray interactions, using event-by-event Monte Carlo simulations rather than the simplified numerical scaling techniques employed by Poulin et al. (2019) and Korsmeier et al. (2018). The simulated background fluxes are lower than those earlier predictions, which is significant given that AMS-02 has reported several antihelium candidate events while no strong antideuteron candidates have been observed. The results enable more accurate background estimates for dark matter searches using antinuclei, and the model can be validated against future accelerator data from NA61/SHINE at CERN-SPS and higher-statistics LHC measurements.
