# Multiconfigurational time-dependent density functional theory for atomic nuclei: Technical and numerical aspects

> The nuclear time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) is a tool of choice for describing various dynamical phenomena in atomic nuclei. In a recent study, we reported an extension of the framework - the multiconfigurational TDDFT (MC-TDDFT) model - that takes into account quantum fluctuations in the collective space by mixing several TDDFT trajectories. In this article, we focus on technical and numerical aspects of the model. We outline the properties of the time-dependent variational principle that is employed to obtain the equation of motion for the mixing function. Furthermore, we discuss evaluation of various ingredients of the equation of motion, including the Hamiltonian kernel, norm kernel, and kernels with explicit time derivatives. We detail the numerical methods for resolving the equation of motion and outline the major assumptions underpinning the model. A technical discussion is supplemented with numerical examples that consider collective quadrupole vibrations in $^{40}$Ca, particularly focusing on the issues of convergence, treatment of linearly dependent bases, energy conservation, and prescriptions for the density-dependent part of an interaction.

## Key claim

The paper presents the technical and numerical implementation of the multiconfigurational time-dependent density functional theory (MC-TDDFT) for atomic nuclei, where the mixing function is treated variationally while the generating states follow independent TDHF trajectories; the authors explicitly note that this simplification differs from a fully variational treatment of both the mixing function and generating states, which would yield a more complicated set of coupled equations.

## Method

The MC-TDDFT state is constructed as a continuous superposition of time-dependent Slater determinants weighted by a complex mixing function, with the generating states evolving independently according to the time-dependent Hartree-Fock equations derived from a Skyrme energy density functional. The equation of motion for the mixing function is obtained from a time-dependent variational principle with Lagrange multipliers enforcing normalization and projection onto the image subspace of the norm kernel, then transformed into an equation for the collective wave function using the square root of the norm kernel. The Hamiltonian kernel, norm kernel, and time derivative kernel are evaluated using the generalized Wick theorem and transition densities, with the density-dependent part of the interaction treated via an average density prescription.

## Key numbers

- Collective quadrupole vibrations in $^{40}$Ca are used as numerical examples, focusing on convergence, treatment of linearly dependent bases, energy conservation, and prescriptions for the density-dependent part of an interaction
- The norm kernel for Slater determinants is computed as the determinant of the overlap matrix of single-particle states, with the total overlap factorizing into a product over neutrons and protons
- The Coulomb exchange contribution is calculated at the Slater approximation: $\mathcal{E}_{\bm{q}\bm{q'}}^{\rm{Cou,Exc}} (\bm{r};t) = - \frac{3}{4}\frac{e^2}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \big(\frac{3}{\pi}\big)^{1/3} \big[\rho_{D}^{(p)}(\bm{r};t)\big]^{4/3}$
- The average density prescription for the density-dependent term uses $\rho_D^{\alpha}(\bm{r};t) = \big[\frac{1}{2}\big(\rho_{\bm{q} \bm{q}}(\bm{r};t) + \rho_{\bm{q'} \bm{q'}}(\bm{r};t)\big)\big]^\alpha$

## Context

This work extends the recent MC-TDDFT framework reported by Marevic et al. (2023) for atomic nuclei, which demonstrated that collective multiphonon states emerge at high excitation energies when quantum fluctuations are included beyond the independent particle approximation. The approach differs from the adiabatic time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) of Berger (1984), Goutte (2004), and Younes (2019) by using time-dependent generating states rather than static configurations, thereby incorporating dissipation of collective motion into internal single-particle motion. The technical details provided here enable future applications to a wide range of nuclear phenomena including collective vibrations, heavy-ion reactions, and fission, building on the theoretical foundations laid by Reinhard, Cusson, and Goeke (1983, 1987).
