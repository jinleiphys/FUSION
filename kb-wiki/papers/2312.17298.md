# Empirical fits to inclusive electron-carbon scattering data obtained by deep-learning methods

> Employing the neural network framework, we obtain empirical fits to the electron-scattering cross sections for carbon over a broad kinematic region, extending from the quasielastic peak through resonance excitation to the onset of deep-inelastic scattering. We consider two different methods of obtaining such model-independent parametrizations and the corresponding uncertainties: based on the bootstrap approach and the Monte Carlo dropout approach. In our analysis, the $χ^2$ defines the loss function, including point-to-point and normalization uncertainties for each independent set of measurements. Our statistical approaches lead to fits of comparable quality and similar uncertainties of the order of $7$%. To test these models, we compare their predictions to test datasets excluded from the training process and theoretical predictions obtained within the spectral function approach. The predictions of both models agree with experimental measurements and theoretical calculations. We also perform a comparison to a dataset lying beyond the covered kinematic region, and find that the bootstrap approach shows better interpolation and extrapolation abilities than the one based on the dropout algorithm.

## Key claim
The paper claims that deep neural network (DNN) fits to inclusive electron-carbon scattering data, using either bootstrap or Monte Carlo dropout methods, can produce model-independent parametrizations with comparable quality and uncertainties of about 7%, but the bootstrap approach shows better interpolation and extrapolation abilities than the dropout algorithm when tested on data beyond the covered kinematic region.

## Method
The authors trained DNNs with ten hidden layers (300 units each) and batch normalization on 3265 experimental double-differential cross-section data points from 11 datasets for carbon targets, covering quasielastic, resonance, and deep-inelastic regimes. They employed two uncertainty estimation methods: model A used a bootstrap approach generating 50 resampled datasets from the original data, while model B used Monte Carlo dropout with a dropout rate of 0.01, both using a χ² loss function that included point-to-point and normalization uncertainties for each dataset.

## Key numbers
- Uncertainties of the order of 7% for both statistical approaches
- 3265 total data points from 11 datasets used for training and testing
- 50 bootstrap datasets generated for model A; 50 dropout iterations used for model B
- Dropout rate p = 0.01 selected for model B based on lowest test χ²/N = 1.15
- Normalization parameters for model A typically agree with unity to about 2%; for model B to about 4%
- The Whitney et al. (1974) dataset normalization parameter was 0.93 in both models, suggesting about 7% tension

## Context
This work extends the approach of Al Hammal et al. (2023), who used DNNs with 35 hidden layers and residual mechanisms for electron-nucleus scattering but did not account for experimental uncertainties in their loss function. The present analysis improves upon that work by incorporating normalization uncertainties and using χ² as the loss function, and it provides a direct comparison of bootstrap and Monte Carlo dropout methods for uncertainty estimation. The resulting parametrizations can be used to constrain neutrino-nucleus cross-section predictions for long-baseline oscillation experiments such as DUNE and Hyper-Kamiokande, leveraging the similarity between electron and neutrino interactions as advocated by Ankowski (2022).
