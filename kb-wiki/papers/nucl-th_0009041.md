# Critical Phenomena in Finite Systems

> We discuss the dynamics of finite systems within molecular dynamics models. Signatures of a critical behavior are analyzed and compared to experimental data both in nucleus-nucleus and metallic cluster collisions. We suggest the possibility to explore the instability region via tunneling. In this way we can obtain fragments at very low temperatures and densities. We call these fragments quantum drops.

# Critical Phenomena in Finite Systems

## Key claim

The authors suggest that finite nuclear systems can undergo fragmentation at very low temperatures and densities via quantum tunneling through a collective potential barrier, producing "quantum drops", but this is presented as a qualitative proposal requiring experimental confirmation, not a demonstrated result.

## Method

Classical Molecular Dynamics (CMD) simulations of 100 particles with Yukawa-type interactions were used to study the time evolution of finite excited systems in the density-temperature plane, with results normalized to ground-state density (0.12 fm⁻³) and binding energy (−10 MeV). For the quantum tunneling proposal, the authors combined an imaginary-time prescription with Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) to simulate expansion of ²³⁰U at excitation energies of 5–8 MeV/nucleon, allowing the system to tunnel through the collective potential when the collective momentum reaches zero.

## Key numbers

- Critical behavior occurs at initial temperature T = 5 MeV in CMD simulations, where the system enters the instability region near the critical point
- Excitation energy at the critical event: E*/E_b = 0.71, which coincides with the infinite case limit value at critical density and temperature
- Power-law exponent from mass distribution at T = 5 MeV: τ = 2.23
- Maximum Lyapunov exponent occurs at normalized excitation energy E* = 0.5 in both CMD and BNV calculations
- Ground-state density used for normalization: 0.12 fm⁻³ (finite system), 0.15 fm⁻³ (infinite case)
- Binding energy used for normalization: −10 MeV (finite system), −16 MeV (infinite case)
- Excitation energy range for ²³⁰U tunneling simulations: 5 to 8 MeV/nucleon
- Tunneling begins at t = 100 fm/c and exits at t = 600 fm/c in the example event

## Context

This work extends earlier CMD studies of finite-system phase transitions (ref.1) and Fisher model predictions for mass yields in nuclear fragmentation (Fisher). The authors compare their CMD results to experimental data from Au+Au collisions at 35 MeV/A performed at MSU using Multics-Miniball detectors (Mastinu, Belkacem). The Lyapunov exponent calculations are compared to BNV mean-field calculations (Burgio). The quantum tunneling proposal is analogous to spontaneous fission (fission) and is intended to motivate experimental searches for low-temperature fragmentation (qdrop).
