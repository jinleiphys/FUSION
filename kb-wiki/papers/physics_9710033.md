# First Order Static Excitation Potential: Scheme for Excitation Energies and Transition Moments

> We present an approximation scheme for the calculation of the principal excitation energies and transition moments of finite many-body systems. The scheme is derived from a first order approximation to the self energy of a recently proposed extended particle-hole Green's function. A hermitian eigenvalue problem is encountered of the same size as the well-known Random Phase Approximation (RPA). We find that it yields a size consistent description of the excitation properties and removes an inconsistent treatment of the ground state correlation by the RPA. By presenting a hermitian eigenvalue problem the new scheme avoids the instabilities of the RPA and should be well suited for large scale numerical calculations. These and additional properties of the new approximation scheme are illuminated by a very simple exactly solvable model.

# First Order Static Excitation Potential: Scheme for Excitation Energies and Transition Moments

## Key claim

The First Order Static Excitation Potential (FOSEP) scheme provides a hermitian eigenvalue problem of the same size as the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) that yields size-consistent excitation energies and transition moments while removing the inconsistent treatment of ground state correlation present in the RPA; however, the scheme is a first-order approximation derived from a specific choice of extended particle-hole Green's function states, and not all eigenvalues correspond to "physical" excitations.

## Method

The authors derive FOSEP from a first-order approximation to the static (energy-independent) part of the self-energy of an extended particle-hole Green's function, which satisfies a Dyson equation. The resulting hermitian eigenvalue problem involves a secular matrix of dimension equal to the combined particle-hole (ph) and hole-particle (hp) configuration spaces, with matrix elements constructed from Hartree-Fock orbital energies and antisymmetrized two-body interaction matrix elements. The method is applied to a simple exactly solvable model system to compare its performance against the Tamm-Dancoff Approximation (TDA) and the Random Phase Approximation (RPA).

## Key numbers

- The FOSEP secular matrix has the same size as the RPA matrix, comprising both ph-ph and hp-hp blocks, with the ph-ph block containing the TDA matrix (ε^ph + V) and the coupling block W given by -V_{pp'[hh']} for occupied orbitals h, h' and virtual orbitals p, p'.
- For a model system where the ph-ph block has dimension one, the two eigenvalues of the FOSEP matrix are given by ±√(ε^ph² + W²) + V, yielding one positive and one negative eigenvalue provided V² < ε^ph² + W².
- The zeroth-order excitation energies in the FOSEP matrix are given by ε_r - ε_s (orbital energy differences from Hartree-Fock), and the first-order self-energy matrix elements are V_{rs'[sr']} (n̄_r n_s - n_r n̄_s)(n̄_r' n_s' - n_r' n̄_s') in the Møller-Plesset partitioning.

## Context

This work extends the theory of extended particle-hole Green's functions developed by Brand and Cederbaum (1997, unpublished) and Brand (1996), which was previously applied to the particle-particle propagator as an optical potential for elastic scattering. The FOSEP scheme addresses known deficiencies of the RPA, which has been observed to give worse excitation energies than the TDA in certain cases (Koch 1995, Bauernschmitt 1996), by providing a hermitian eigenvalue problem that avoids RPA instabilities and complex eigenvalues. The method enables large-scale numerical calculations of excitation energies and transition moments in finite many-body systems (atoms, molecules, and atomic nuclei) while maintaining size consistency and invariance under unitary transformations of occupied or virtual Hartree-Fock orbitals.
