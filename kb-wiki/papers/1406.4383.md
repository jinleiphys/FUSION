# Error Analysis in Nuclear Density Functional Theory

> Nuclear density functional theory (DFT) is the only microscopic, global approach to the structure of atomic nuclei. It is used in numerous applications, from determining the limits of stability to gaining a deep understanding of the formation of elements in the universe or the mechanisms that power stars and reactors. The predictive power of the theory depends on the amount of physics embedded in the energy density functional as well as on efficient ways to determine a small number of free parameters and solve the DFT equations. In this article, we discuss the various sources of uncertainties and errors encountered in DFT and possible methods to quantify these uncertainties in a rigorous manner.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while nuclear density functional theory (DFT) is the only global microscopic approach to nuclear structure, its predictive power is limited by multiple sources of uncertainty that are often not rigorously quantified; the authors emphasize that model errors, fitting biases, and numerical implementation errors must be systematically addressed before theoretical predictions can be trusted, especially for nuclei where no experimental data exist.

## Method

The authors categorize uncertainties in nuclear DFT into three main sources: model error (from the choice of energy density functional form), fitting bias (from parameter optimization against experimental data), and numerical implementation error (from basis truncation and discretization). They illustrate these sources using the UNEDF1 optimization protocol for Skyrme functionals, comparing results obtained with and without the Lipkin-Nogami approximate particle number restoration, and examining sensitivity of optimized parameters to the choice of standard deviations assigned to different data types in the chi-squared objective function.

## Key numbers

* The UNEDF1 parametrization (with Lipkin-Nogami) gives deformed mass r.m.s. deviation of 0.721 MeV, spherical masses 1.461 MeV, proton radii 0.016 fm, neutron OES 0.023 MeV, proton OES 0.080 MeV, and fission isomer 0.208 MeV; the UNEDF1-HFB version (without Lipkin-Nogami) gives 0.776 MeV, 1.836 MeV, 0.022 fm, 0.051 MeV, 0.075 MeV, and 0.558 MeV respectively.
* Varying the standard deviation of OES data from 0.025 MeV to 0.100 MeV changes deformed mass r.m.s. from 0.960 MeV to 0.775 MeV and fission isomer r.m.s. from 0.830 MeV to 0.441 MeV.
* Varying the standard deviation of fission isomer excitation energy from 0.25 MeV to 1.00 MeV changes deformed mass r.m.s. from 1.057 MeV to 0.730 MeV and fission isomer r.m.s. from 0.279 MeV to 0.903 MeV.
* In harmonic oscillator basis calculations, total energy varies by about 0.7 MeV in Ca-40 and about 7 MeV in Pu-240 across the range of basis parameters shown.
* For mass A greater than 200, ground-state calculations have numerical truncation error greater than 1 MeV even with N=20 full oscillator shells, reaching 3-4 MeV near the scission point.
* To match the precision of coordinate-space calculations with mesh size h=0.05 fm, nearly 60 full harmonic oscillator shells are needed.

## Context

This work extends the efforts of the UNEDF collaboration (Bogner 2013) to bring rigorous uncertainty quantification to nuclear DFT, building on earlier sensitivity studies by Bertsch (2005) that showed nuclear binding energies are sensitive to only 3 of 8 parameters in a standard Skyrme functional. The paper contrasts the EDF approach (where all correlations are assumed encapsulated in the energy density functional) with the SCMF approach (where a hierarchy of approximations exists for a given Hamiltonian), noting that fits performed at one approximation level are not applicable at another. The authors identify Bayesian approaches as a promising direction for providing alternative estimates of the standard deviations assigned to different data types, which they show can significantly affect optimization results.
