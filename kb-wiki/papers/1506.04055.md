# A Study of Shell Model Neutron States in $^{207,209}Pb$ Using the Generalized Woods-Saxon plus Spin-Orbit Potential

> The experimental binding energies of single-particle and single-hole neutron states belonging to neutron shells that extend from N = 126 to 184 and 82 to 126 respectively, have been reproduced by solving the Schrödinger equation with a potential that has two components: the generalized Woods-Saxon (GWS) potential and the spin-orbit (SO) coupling term. The GWS potential contains the traditional WS potential plus a term (SU) whose intensity reaches a maximum in the nuclear surface. Our results indicate the existence of a explicit relationship between the strength of the SU potential and the orbital angular momentum quantum number $\ell$ of the state. This dependence has been used to make reasonable predictions for the excitation energy centroids of states located inside and outside the neutron shells investigated. Comparisons are made with results reported in previous investigations.

## Key claim

The authors claim that adding a surface (SU) term to the traditional Woods-Saxon potential, forming a generalized Woods-Saxon (GWS) potential, allows reproduction of experimental binding energies for single-particle and single-hole neutron states in the lead region, but only after establishing an explicit relationship between the SU potential strength and the orbital angular momentum quantum number \(\ell\). The hedge is that this relationship holds for most orbitals studied (\(\ell = 0, 2, 4, 6\)) but fails for the p and f orbitals (\(\ell = 1, 3\)), which require an empirical shift to the derived formulas.

## Method

The authors solved the Schrödinger equation using the GAMOW computer code with a potential consisting of a generalized Woods-Saxon term (traditional Woods-Saxon plus a surface term \(V_{SU}\)) and a spin-orbit coupling term. For each single-particle state in \(^{209}\text{Pb}\) and single-hole state in \(^{207}\text{Pb}\), they adjusted the strength parameter \(C_{\ell j}\) of the surface potential until the theoretical binding energy matched the experimental value extracted from previous transfer reaction studies. The Woods-Saxon and spin-orbit parameter values were taken from the parameterization of Schwierz et al. (2007).

## Key numbers

- The experimental binding energy of the \(2g_{9/2}\) orbital in \(^{209}\text{Pb}\) is \(-3.937\) MeV; the theoretical value with WS+SO alone is \(-3.822\) MeV.
- The experimental binding energy of the \(4s_{1/2}\) orbital in \(^{209}\text{Pb}\) is \(-1.904\) MeV; the theoretical value with WS+SO alone is \(-1.382\) MeV.
- The experimental binding energy of the \(3p_{1/2}\) orbital in \(^{207}\text{Pb}\) is \(-7.368\) MeV; the theoretical value with WS+SO alone is \(-7.687\) MeV.
- The experimental binding energy of the \(1i_{13/2}\) orbital in \(^{207}\text{Pb}\) is \(-9.237\) MeV; the theoretical value with WS+SO alone is \(-9.332\) MeV.
- The surface potential strength \(C_{\ell j}\) for the \(4s_{1/2}\) orbital (\(\ell=0\)) is 7.509 MeV.
- The surface potential strength \(C_{\ell j}\) for the \(3p_{1/2}\) orbital (\(\ell=1\)) is \(-3.307\) MeV.
- The surface potential strength \(C_{\ell j}\) for the \(2g_{9/2}\) orbital (\(\ell=4\)) is 1.096 MeV.
- The surface potential strength \(C_{\ell j}\) for the \(1j_{15/2}\) orbital (\(\ell=7\)) is 1.004 MeV (fragmentation scheme 1).
- The predicted excitation energy centroid for the \(1j_{13/2}\) orbital in \(^{209}\text{Pb}\) is 8.410 MeV, with a binding energy of 4.473 MeV (unbound state).
- The predicted excitation energy centroid for the \(1h_{9/2}\) orbital in \(^{207}\text{Pb}\) is 3.590 MeV, with a binding energy of \(-10.958\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous efforts by Blomqvist (1970), Rost (1968), Dudek (1979, 1987), and Schwierz et al. (2007) to describe single-particle states around \(^{208}\text{Pb}\) using Woods-Saxon potentials, none of which achieved overall agreement with experimental binding energies. The authors also compare their WS+SO results to those of Wang et al. (2014), who used a multi-step potential approximation. The study enables predictions for states outside the investigated neutron shells (N=82-126 and N=126-184), such as the \(1j_{13/2}\) orbital, and suggests the need for new measurements of the h-orbital fragmentations in \(^{207}\text{Pb}\) to resolve discrepancies with the work of Galès et al. (1983).
