# Ab Initio Symmetry-Adapted Emulator for Studying Emergent Collectivity and Clustering in Nuclei

> We discuss emulators from the ab initio symmetry-adapted no-core shell-model framework for studying the formation of alpha clustering and collective properties without effective charges. We present a new type of an emulator, one that utilizes the eigenvector continuation technique but is based on the use of symplectic symmetry considerations. This is achieved by using physically relevant degrees of freedom, namely, the symmetry-adapted basis, which exploits the almost perfect symplectic symmetry in nuclei. Specifically, we study excitation energies, point-proton root-mean-square radii, along with electric quadrupole moments and transitions for 6Li and 12C. We show that the set of parameterizations of the chiral potential used to train the emulators has no significant effect on predictions of dominant nuclear features, such as shape and the associated symplectic symmetry, along with cluster formation, but slightly varies details that affect collective quadrupole moments, asymptotic normalization coefficients, and alpha partial widths up to a factor of two. This makes these types of emulators important for further constraining the nuclear force for high-precision nuclear structure and reaction observables.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new type of emulator that combines eigenvector continuation with symplectic symmetry considerations from the symmetry-adapted no-core shell model, showing that the set of chiral potential parameterizations used to train the emulators has no significant effect on predictions of dominant nuclear features such as shape and cluster formation, but slightly varies details that affect collective quadrupole moments, asymptotic normalization coefficients, and alpha partial widths up to a factor of two.

## Method

The authors constructed symmetry-adapted eigenvector continuation (SA-EVC) emulators using the ab initio symmetry-adapted no-core shell model (SA-NCSM) framework with a symplectic Sp(3,R)-adapted basis. They trained the emulators on 32 training points within a 14-dimensional parameter space for a chiral nucleon-nucleon interaction truncated at next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO), with low-energy constants varied within plus or minus 10% of their NNLO_opt values. The emulators were validated against 256 validation points for excitation energies, point-proton root-mean-square radii, electric quadrupole moments, and B(E2) transition strengths for 6Li and 12C.

## Key numbers

- SA model spaces reduced Hamiltonian matrix sizes by up to four orders of magnitude compared to complete N_max model spaces, or equivalently by more than 97%
- Application of EVC to SA spaces resulted in an additional reduction of up to three orders of magnitude, or as much as 99%
- Final matrix sizes were as much as 10^{-5} times smaller than corresponding N_max complete spaces
- For NNLO_opt, collectivity-driven observables agreed within 0.3-2.9% between SA and complete model spaces, and radii agreed at the sub-percent level
- For 6Li 1+ ground state, the (2 0) symplectic irrep accounted for 83-88% of each training wave function across different LECs sets
- For 12C ground state, the (0 4) irrep probability was between 80-88% across training wave functions
- Asymptotic normalization coefficients for 6Li 3S1 partial wave varied from 1.45 to 2.07 fm^{-1/2} (about 20% variation) with NNLO_opt value of 1.77 fm^{-1/2}
- Alpha widths for the 3+ state in 6Li ranged from 6.34 keV to 14.05 keV (about plus or minus 40% from NNLO_opt value of 9.81 keV)
- Average relative errors over 256 validation LECs sets for 6Li: 6.91 x 10^{-2} for 1+ ground state quadrupole moment, 7.70 x 10^{-4} for 3+ state quadrupole moment, and 1.20 x 10^{-4} for 3+ excitation energy

## Context

This work extends the eigenvector continuation method (Ekstrom 2019, Konig 2020) by combining it with the symmetry-adapted no-core shell model framework (Dytrych 2020, Launey 2021) that exploits the nearly perfect symplectic symmetry observed in nuclei from first principles. The approach enables computationally intensive statistical analyses such as sensitivity studies and uncertainty quantification for collective and clustering observables that would otherwise be infeasible, and opens the door to using these emulators to further constrain nuclear forces for high-precision nuclear structure and reaction calculations.
