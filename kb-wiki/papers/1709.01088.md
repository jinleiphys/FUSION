# Double Gamow-Teller transitions and its relation to neutrinoless $ββ$ decay

> We study the double Gamow-Teller (DGT) strength distribution of $^{48}$Ca with state-of-the-art large-scale nuclear shell model calculations. Our analysis shows that the centroid energy of the DGT giant resonance depends mostly on the isovector pairing interaction, while the resonance width is more sensitive to isoscalar pairing. Pairing correlations are also key in neutrinoless $ββ$ ($0νββ$) decay. We find a simple relation between the centroid energy and width of the $^{48}$Ca DGT giant resonance and %the value of the $0νββ$ decay nuclear matrix element. More generally, we observe a very good linear correlation between the DGT transition to the ground state of the final nucleus and the $0νββ$ decay matrix element. The correlation, which originates on the dominant short-range character of both transitions, extends to heavier systems including several $ββ$ emitters, and also holds in energy-density functional results. Our findings suggest that DGT experiments can be a very valuable tool to obtain information on the value of $0νββ$ decay nuclear matrix elements.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the centroid energy of the double Gamow-Teller (DGT) giant resonance in ⁴⁸Ca is correlated with the neutrinoless double-beta decay nuclear matrix element, and that a linear correlation exists between the DGT transition to the ground state of the final nucleus and the 0νββ decay matrix element across many isotopes; however, the correlation is conditional on the shell model calculations reproducing upcoming DGT data, and the ground-state DGT transition accounts for only about 0.03 per mil of the total DGT strength, making its measurement a formidable challenge.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale nuclear shell model calculations using the Lanczos strength function method with the KSHELL code, computing the DGT strength distribution of ⁴⁸Ca in the pf-shell (with GXPF1B and KB3G interactions) and in the sd-pf two-shell space (with SDPFMU-DB interaction). They probed the dependence on pairing correlations by adding isovector (J=0,T=1) and isoscalar (J=1,T=0) pairing terms to the nuclear interaction, and then compared the resulting DGT centroid energies and widths to the 0νββ decay nuclear matrix elements computed with the same interactions. The correlation between DGT and 0νββ decay matrix elements was extended to 26 pairs of initial and final nuclei using various one-major-shell interactions, and compared with energy-density functional and quasiparticle random-phase approximation results.

## Key numbers

- The DGT giant resonance centroid energy differs by only 1.6 MeV between the two one-major-shell calculations (GXPF1B vs KB3G) for ⁴⁸Ca.
- The DGT transition to the ⁴⁸Ti ground state accounts for only about 0.03 per mil of the total DGT strength.
- The linear correlation between DGT and 0νββ decay matrix elements is valid up to M⁰ν ≈ 5 for the full shell model calculations, and extends to M⁰ν ≈ 10 when using seniority-truncated states.
- The 0νββ decay NME is dominated by the GT component, with the ratio g_A/g_V = 1.27 used for the Fermi component.
- The DGT strength distributions were smeared with Lorentzians of Γ = 1 MeV width to simulate experimental energy resolution.

## Context

This work extends theoretical predictions of the DGT giant resonance first made three decades ago by Auerbach, Vogel, Muto, and Zheng, and connects to modern searches using heavy-ion double charge-exchange reactions at RNCP Osaka and planned at RIBF RIKEN and LNS Catania. The correlation found between DGT and 0νββ decay matrix elements provides a potential experimental tool to constrain nuclear matrix elements for 0νββ decay, which currently disagree among nuclear many-body approaches (shell model, EDF, QRPA) by more than a factor of two. The paper suggests that upcoming DGT experiments can test the shell model predictions and, if successful, inform the value of 0νββ decay NMEs, complementing previous proposals using two-nucleon transfer reactions (Brown 2014) and two-neutrino ββ decay (Barabash 2015).
