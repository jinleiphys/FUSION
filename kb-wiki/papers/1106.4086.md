# A Euclidean formulation of relativistic quantum mechanics

> In this paper we discuss a formulation of relativistic quantum mechanics that uses Euclidean Green functions or generating functionals as input. This formalism has a close relation to quantum field theory, but as a theory of linear operators on a Hilbert space, it has many of the advantages of quantum mechanics. One interesting feature of this approach is that matrix elements of operators in normalizable states on the physical Hilbert space can be calculated directly using the Euclidean Green functions without performing an analytic continuation. The formalism is summarized in this paper. We discuss the motivation, advantages and difficulties in using this formalism. We discuss how to compute bound states, scattering cross sections, and finite Poincare transformations without using analytic continuation. A toy model is used to demonstrate how matrix elements of exp(-beta H) in normalizable states can be used to construct-sharp momentum transition matrix elements.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a formulation of relativistic quantum mechanics that uses Euclidean Green functions or generating functionals as the dynamical input, claiming that matrix elements of operators in normalizable states on the physical Hilbert space can be calculated directly without performing an analytic continuation. The hedge is that this framework is presented as a model-building strategy rather than a complete solution: the authors note that constructing model Euclidean Green functions or generating functionals with the required properties (especially reflection positivity) remains a challenge, and the formalism is illustrated with a toy non-relativistic model rather than a fully relativistic interacting example.

## Method

The authors construct the physical Hilbert space from a Euclidean generating functional \(Z[f]\) using the Osterwalder-Schrader reconstruction theorem as simplified by Fröhlich, where wave functionals are built from positive-time test functions and the inner product is given directly by \(Z[g - \Theta f]\). Poincaré generators (Hamiltonian \(H\), momentum \(\mathbf{P}\), angular momentum \(\mathbf{J}\), and boost \(\mathbf{K}\)) are defined as infinitesimal generators of Euclidean transformations on these wave functionals, with matrix elements expressed directly in terms of \(Z[f]\) and Euclidean test functions. Single-particle states are obtained as eigenstates of the mass-squared Casimir operator \(M^2 = H^2 - \mathbf{P}^2\), and scattering states are formulated using a two-Hilbert space version of Haag-Ruelle scattering theory, where matrix elements of \(e^{-\beta H}\) in normalizable states are used to construct sharp-momentum transition matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- The toy model used to demonstrate the method is an exactly solvable non-relativistic model (no specific numerical results from this model are given in the truncated text).
- The energy scale of interest is stated as "the few GeV energy scale" (from the Motivation section).
- The angle \(\chi\) defining the cone support for test functions used in boost generators satisfies \(0 \leq \chi < \pi/2\), and rotations in the \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}-\tau\) plane are restricted to angles \(\xi < \pi/2 - \chi\).
- The mass Casimir operator is defined as \(M^2 = H^2 - \mathbf{P}^2\) (equation c.18).

## Context

This work extends the program of Poincaré-invariant quantum mechanics (Keister and Polyzou, 1991) by providing a framework that has a more direct relation to Lagrangian field theory, using Euclidean Green functions as input rather than constructing dynamical unitary representations of the Poincaré group directly on a few-particle Hilbert space. The formalism builds on the Euclidean reconstruction theorem of Osterwalder and Schrader (1973, 1974) and Fröhlich's (1974) construction of the physical Hilbert space from generating functionals, and it enables the computation of bound states, scattering cross sections, and finite Poincaré transformations without analytic continuation, potentially allowing QCD-inspired constraints on few-body models. The approach is intended to address the difficulty that previous Poincaré-invariant quantum mechanical models lacked a straightforward connection to Lagrangian field theory, making it hard to use QCD to systematically improve or constrain the models.
