# Theory of competition between fusion and quasi-fission in a heavy fusing system

> A theory of the competition between fusion and quasi-fission in a heavy fusing system is proposed, which is based on a master equation and the two-center shell model. Fusion and quasi-fission arise from a diffusion process in an ensemble of nuclear shapes, each of which evolves towards the thermal equilibrium. The master equation describes the diffusion of the nuclear shapes due to quantum and thermal fluctuations. Other crucial physical effects like dissipation, ground-state shell effects, diabatic effects and rotational effects are also incorporated into the theory. The fusing system moves in a dynamical (time-dependent) collective potential energy surface which is initially diabatic and gradually becomes adiabatic. The microscopic ingredients of the theory are obtained with a realistic two-center shell model based on Woods-Saxon potentials. Numerical calculations for several reactions leading to $^{256}$No are discussed. Among other important conclusions, the results indicate that (i) the diabatic effects play a very important role in the onset of fusion hindrance for heavy systems, and (ii) very asymmetric reactions induced by closed shell nuclei seem to be the best suited to synthesize the heaviest compound nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that fusion and quasi-fission in heavy systems arise from a diffusion process in an ensemble of nuclear shapes, where the system moves on a dynamical (time-dependent) collective potential energy surface that is initially diabatic and gradually becomes adiabatic, rather than assuming thermal equilibrium as most prior models do.

## Method

The theory uses a master equation derived via the Nakajima-Zwanzig projection technique to describe the time evolution of the probability distribution of nuclear shapes, with the microscopic ingredients obtained from a realistic two-center shell model based on Woods-Saxon potentials. The Markov limit of the master equation is taken, assuming the memory time (coherence time) is the shortest time scale, and the transition probability rates between macrostates are expressed in terms of the level densities of the nuclear shapes. The formalism incorporates quantum and thermal fluctuations, dissipation, ground-state shell effects, diabatic effects, and rotational effects, with the single-particle motion evolving from diabatic (coherent) to adiabatic (thermalized) due to residual two-body collisions.

## Key numbers

- Memory time estimated as less than \(8 \cdot 10^{-21}/A\) seconds for systems with mass number \(A\), e.g., \(4 \cdot 10^{-23}\) s for \(A \sim 200\)
- Relaxation times for macroscopic probabilities estimated to be of order \(10^{-21}\) s in peripheral deep-inelastic heavy ion collisions
- Life-time of highly excited single-particle states estimated as \(\sim 10^{-22}\) s for systems like \(^{48}\)Ca + \(^{208}\)Pb, with particle states up to 10 MeV above the Fermi surface appearing due to diabatic effects
- Calculations performed for several reactions leading to \(^{256}\)No

## Context

This work aims to reconcile two conflicting approaches to modeling compound nucleus formation: the first type uses adiabatic potential energy surfaces with Langevin trajectories (e.g., Greiner, Swiatecki, Abe, Aritomo) but neglects quantum effects and diabaticity, while the second type (dinuclear system model by Adamian, Nasirov, and others) assumes a sudden potential and avoids the physics of large overlapping nuclei. The present theory extends the exploratory calculations of Diaz-Torres (2000, 2001) by incorporating quantum fluctuations and rotation, and provides a quantum-statistical foundation that includes diabatic effects, which the author argues play a crucial role in fusion hindrance for heavy systems. The approach enables more realistic predictions for optimizing superheavy element production, particularly suggesting that very asymmetric reactions induced by closed shell nuclei are best suited for synthesizing the heaviest compound nuclei.
