# Calculating the energy loss of leading jets

> The energy loss mechanism of jets plays a central role in nuclear and high energy physics. We propose direct measurements of the energy loss of leading jets and perform a calculation at next-to-leading logarithmic (NLL$'$) accuracy in the vacuum. The formation of leading jets can be described by jet functions which constitute probability densities and thus allow for a perturbative calculation of the average the energy loss. We identify the following three criteria for a direct measurement of jet energy loss at the cross section level. $i)$ We measure a well defined object, the leading jet, where the formation process can be expressed in terms of a probability density. $ii)$ In addition, we need a measurement of a hard reference scale with respect to which jet energy loss is defined. $iii)$ At leading logarithmic accuracy, we require that the jet energy loss can be identified with parton energy loss. We discuss suitable observables and present numerical results including threshold corrections by making use of a parton shower Monte Carlo approach.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the energy loss of leading jets can be directly measured at the cross-section level, provided three specific criteria are met: the object measured is a well-defined leading jet whose formation is described by a probability density; a hard reference scale is available to define the energy loss; and at leading logarithmic accuracy the jet energy loss can be identified with parton energy loss. The calculation is performed at next-to-leading logarithmic (NLL') accuracy in the vacuum, not in a medium.

## Method

The authors formulate the leading jet cross section using factorization into hard-scattering functions and leading jet functions, where the leading jet function constitutes a probability density for the jet carrying the largest momentum fraction. They solve the non-linear DGLAP-type evolution equations for the leading jet functions using a parton shower Monte Carlo approach, including threshold resummation at NLL' accuracy for both the hard and jet functions. The average energy loss is obtained by taking the first moment of the leading jet function.

## Key numbers

- For anti-k_T jets at NLO, the average energy loss of a quark jet is given by: ⟨z^{k_T}_{q,loss}⟩ = (α_s/(2π)) C_F ln(1/R²) (2 ln 2 - 3/8) + (α_s/(2π)) C_F (4 ln² 2 + (3/2) ln 2 - 19/8 + π²/3)
- For anti-k_T jets at NLO, the average energy loss of a gluon jet is given by: ⟨z^{k_T}_{g,loss}⟩ = (α_s/(2π)) ln(1/R²) [C_A (2 ln 2 - 43/96) + N_f T_F (7/48)] + (α_s/(2π)) [C_A (4 ln² 2 + (15/8) ln 2 - 793/288 + π²/3) + N_f T_F (-(3/4) ln 2 + 65/72)]
- The ratio of quark to gluon jet energy loss after higher-order corrections is approximately 0.6-0.7, compared to a leading-order estimate of C_F/C_A ≈ 0.44 based on color factors alone
- Numerical results are presented for a hard scale Q = 91 GeV and jet radius R = 0.5

## Context

This work extends the factorization formalism for inclusive jets developed by Catani (2013), Dasgupta (2014), and others to the case of leading jets, and builds upon the non-linear evolution equations for leading jet functions from Waalewijn (2012) and Scott (2019). The proposed observables enable direct experimental measurements of jet energy loss in proton-proton collisions, heavy-ion collisions, and at the future Electron-Ion Collider, using reference scales such as photon-jet balancing in hadronic collisions or the photon virtuality in Semi-Inclusive Deep Inelastic Scattering as proposed by Arratia (2020).
