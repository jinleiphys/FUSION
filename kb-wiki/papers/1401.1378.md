# Shell Evolutions and Nuclear Forces

> During the last 30 years, and more specifically during the last 10 years, many experiments have been carried out worldwide using different techniques to study the shell evolution of nuclei far from stability. What seemed not conceivable some decades ago became rather common: all known magic numbers that are present in the valley of stability disappear far from stability and are replaced by new ones at the drip line. By gathering selected experimental results, beautifully consistent pictures emerge, that very likely take root in the properties of the nuclear forces.The present manuscript describes some of these discoveries and proposes an intuitive understanding of these shell evolutions derived from observations. Extrapolations to yet unstudied regions, as where the explosive r-process nucleosynthesis occurs, are proposed. Some remaining challenges and puzzling questions are also addressed.

# Shell Evolutions and Nuclear Forces

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a universal hierarchy of proton-neutron nuclear forces, based on radial wave function overlap, angular momentum, and spin alignment, drives the systematic disappearance of harmonic oscillator magic numbers (N=8, 20, 40) and the emergence of new shell gaps far from stability, though the author notes this framework is semi-quantitative and requires detailed theoretical confirmation.

## Method

The author synthesizes experimental results from transfer reactions (d,p), β-decay studies, and γ-ray spectroscopy across multiple isotopic chains (Be, Ne, Mg, Si, Ca, Cr, Fe, Ni) to track the evolution of neutron single-particle energies and shell gaps. The analysis focuses on the N=17 isotones (specifically ²⁵Ne, ²⁷Ne, ²⁹Si, ³¹Si) and the N=20 isotones (³²Mg, ³⁴Si) to extract the energy spacings between neutron d₃/₂, f₇/₂, and p₃/₂ orbitals as a function of proton occupancy in the d₅/₂, f₇/₂, and g₉/₂ orbits.

## Key numbers

- The N=14, 28, and 50 spin-orbit shell gaps each increase by the same value of about 2.7 MeV when the neutron d₅/₂, f₇/₂, and g₉/₂ orbits are filled, respectively.
- The 0⁺₂ state in ³²Mg lies at 1058 keV, identified via the ³⁰Mg(t,p)³²Mg reaction at Rex-Isolde.
- The 0⁺₂ state in ³⁴Si lies at 2.72 MeV, with a half-life of 19.4(7) ns, decaying by pair creation; the mixing between the two 0⁺ states is about 20%.
- The 2⁺₁ state in ³⁴Si is at 3327 keV.
- The spacing between the 3/2⁺ and 7/2⁻ states (forming the N=20 gap) increases by about 1.2 MeV when adding four protons in the 1d₅/₂ orbit from Z=10 to Z=14.
- The spacing between the 7/2⁻ and 3/2⁻ states (forming the N=28 gap) increases by 1 MeV with filling of the d₃/₂ orbit and 2 MeV with filling of the d₅/₂ orbit.
- In ²⁶F, the experimental binding energy of the πd₅/₂ ⊗ νd₃/₂ multiplet is globally weaker than shell model predictions by about 17%, and the multiplet is compressed by about 25%.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering discoveries of the N=20 "Island of Inversion" by Thibault (1975), Détraz (1979), and Guillemaud-Mueller (1984), and the theoretical framework of Campi, Poves, and Warburton (1975-1990) describing intruder configurations. The paper synthesizes these findings with recent transfer reaction studies (Catford, Brown) and β-decay studies (Rotaru) to propose a universal mechanism based on the hierarchy of nuclear forces, building on the earlier insights of Elliott (1954) and Talmi and Unna (1960) about the role of two-body forces in shell evolution. The framework enables predictions for shell evolution in unexplored regions relevant to the r-process nucleosynthesis, particularly around N=82 below ¹³²Sn, and raises open questions about why valence mirror nuclei (²⁰C, ⁴²Si, ¹³²Sn) behave differently despite similar forces.
