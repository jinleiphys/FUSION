# Mean-field instabilities and cluster formation in nuclear reactions

> We review recent results on intermediate mass cluster production in heavy ion collisions at Fermi energy and in spallation reactions. Our studies are based on modern transport theories, employing effective interactions for the nuclear mean-field and incorporating two-body correlations and fluctuations. Namely we will consider the Stochastic Mean Field (SMF) approach and the recently developed Boltzmann-Langevin One Body (BLOB) model. We focus on cluster production emerging from the possible occurrence of low-density mean-field instabilities in heavy ion reactions. Within such a framework, the respective role of one and two-body effects, in the two models considered, will be carefully analysed. We will discuss, in particular, fragment production in central and semi-peripheral heavy ion collisions, which is the object of many recent experimental investigations. Moreover, in the context of spallation reactions, we will show how thermal expansion may trigger the development of mean-field instabilities, leading to a cluster formation process which competes with important re-aggregation effects.

# Mean-field instabilities and cluster formation in nuclear reactions

## Key claim

Cluster production in heavy ion collisions at Fermi energies and in spallation reactions emerges primarily from the growth of density inhomogeneities driven by mean-field instabilities in low-density nuclear matter, but many-body correlations (provided by stochastic fluctuations) are essential as seeds for nucleon assembly into clusters; the relative roles of one-body and two-body effects differ between the Stochastic Mean Field (SMF) approach and the Boltzmann-Langevin One Body (BLOB) model.

## Method

The authors employ two stochastic transport models: the Stochastic Mean Field (SMF) model, which projects fluctuations of the distribution function onto coordinate space under a local thermal equilibrium assumption, and the Boltzmann-Langevin One Body (BLOB) model, which solves the full Boltzmann-Langevin equation in phase space by replacing conventional binary test-particle collisions with collisions involving extended phase-space agglomerates of test particles. Both models use a soft isoscalar equation of state (compressibility K = 200 MeV) with a Skyrme-like effective interaction (SKM*), and test three different density dependencies of the symmetry energy (asysoft, asystiff, asysuperstiff). The nucleon-nucleon cross section uses free isospin-, angle-, and energy-dependent values or in-medium modified parameterizations.

## Key numbers

- Compressibility of the isoscalar EOS: K = 200 MeV (soft EOS)
- Symmetry energy at saturation: 30 MeV for asysoft, 28 MeV for asystiff and asysuperstiff
- Slope parameter L: 14 MeV for asysoft, 73 MeV for asystiff, 97 MeV for asysuperstiff
- For the SKM* interaction: A = -356 MeV, B = 303 MeV, sigma = 7/6
- Surface term coefficient: C_surf = -6/rho_0^(5/3) MeV fm^5
- Spinodal-like fragmentation in 136Xe+124Sn central collisions at 25 and 32 A MeV occurs at around 80-100 fm/c, followed by recombination at later times
- The leading wavelength of the dispersion relation corresponds to fragment sizes in the region of oxygen and neon

## Context

This review extends prior work on stochastic mean-field approaches (Ayik 1990, Randrup 1994) and the Boltzmann-Langevin equation, comparing the SMF model (Salvo) with the more recently developed BLOB model (Napolitani 2013). The work builds on earlier studies of spinodal instabilities and liquid-gas phase transitions in nuclear matter (Frankland, Moretto, Dagostino, EPJA, rep, Ono) and addresses the competition between mean-field instabilities and re-aggregation effects in spallation reactions. The results enable systematic comparison with experimental data from INDRA and CHIMERA collaborations on fragment production in central and semi-peripheral heavy ion collisions, and provide a framework for testing the density dependence of the symmetry energy through isospin distillation effects.
