# Ground-state properties of unitary bosons: from clusters to matter

> The properties of cold Bose gases at unitarity have been extensively investigated in the last few years both theoretically and experimentally. In this paper we use a family of interactions tuned to two-body unitarity and very weak three-body binding to demonstrate the universal properties of both clusters and matter. We determine the universal properties of finite clusters up to 60 particles and, for the first time, explicitly demonstrate the saturation of energy and density with particle number and compare with bulk properties. At saturation in the bulk we determine the energy, density, two- and three-body contacts and the condensate fraction. We find that uniform matter is more bound than three-body clusters by nearly two orders of magnitude, the two-body contact is very large in absolute terms, and yet the condensate fraction is also very large, greater than 90%. Equilibrium properties of these systems may be experimentally accessible through rapid quenching of weakly-interacting boson superfluids.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that large clusters (up to 60 particles) and bulk matter of unitary bosons are stable and exhibit universal behavior when interactions are tuned to two-body unitarity and very weak three-body binding, with the caveat that universality holds only when the interaction range is sufficiently small compared to the trimer size (specifically, for \(\mu_2 {\bar R}_3 = 65\) the results for different range ratios agree within statistical errors, while for \(\mu_2 {\bar R}_3 = 46\) non-universal effects appear).

## Method

The authors use Variational and Diffusion Monte Carlo (VMC and DMC) methods to solve the Schrodinger equation for a Hamiltonian with Gaussian two-body interactions tuned to infinite scattering length and repulsive Gaussian three-body interactions tuned to produce a weakly-bound trimer. They study finite clusters of up to 60 bosons and bulk matter with periodic boundary conditions, using trial wave functions of product form with one-, two-, and three-body correlation factors optimized variationally. The interaction ranges are chosen such that \(\mu_2 {\bar R}_3 = 46\) and \(65\) with three different ratios of two- to three-body ranges \(X_\mu = \mu_3 / \mu_2 = 0.5, 0.75, 1.0\).

## Key numbers

- For \(N=4\) clusters with \(\mu_2 {\bar R}_3 = 65\) and \(X_\mu = 1.0\), \(3E_4/(4E_3) = 3.5(1)\), agreeing with the precise calculation of Deltuva (2010) giving 3.46.
- For a 60-particle cluster, the binding per particle is approximately 50 times that of the trimer.
- Bulk equilibrium ground-state energy per particle is \(87 \pm 5\) times that of the trimer.
- Saturation density \(\rho_0 4 \pi {\bar R}_3^3 /3 = 275 \pm 20\).
- Dimensionless compressibility \(\kappa = 0.42(5)\).
- Liquid-drop extrapolation of cluster energies gives universal energy parameter \(\xi_B (N \rightarrow \infty) = 90 \pm 10\) and scaled surface energy \(\eta = -1.7 \pm 0.3\).
- Interparticle separation at equilibrium \([3/(4\pi \rho_0)]^{1/3} \simeq {\bar R}_3/6.4\).
- Two-body contact \(\alpha_2 = 17(3)\).
- Three-body contact \(\beta_3 = 0.9(1)\).
- Condensate fraction at equilibrium density \(n(k=0) = 0.93(1)\).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of small bosonic clusters (up to \(N \leq 15\)) by von Stecher (2010), Blume (2015), and others that found universal behavior for small N but non-universal effects attributed to finite-range interactions for larger clusters. The authors provide the first explicit demonstration of saturation of energy and density with particle number for unitary bosons, analogous to the fermionic calculations of Carlson (2003) and Chang (2004). The results enable experimental access to equilibrium properties through rapid quenching of weakly-interacting Bose superfluids, as studied by Makotyn (2014) and Fletcher (2017), and provide a benchmark for comparison with liquid helium-4, which has a binding energy per particle about 180 times its trimer energy (Pandharipande 1983) and a condensate fraction of 0.0725(75) (Glyde 2011).
