# Smearing of causality by compositeness divides dispersive approaches into exact ones and precision-limited ones

> Scattering off the edge of a composite particle or finite-range interaction can precede that off its center. An effective theory treatment with pointlike particles and contact interactions must find that the scattered experimental wave is slightly advanced, in violation of causality (the fundamental underlying theory being causal). In practice, partial-wave or other projections of multivariate amplitudes exponentially grow with imaginary E, so that upper complex-plane analyticity is not sufficient to obtain a dispersion relation for them, but only for a slightly modified function (the modified relations additionally connect different J). This limits the maximum precision of certain dispersive approaches to compositeness based on Cauchy's theorem leading to partial-wave dispersion relations. This may be of interest to some dispersive tests of the Standard Model with hadrons, and to unitarization methods used to extend electroweak effective theories. Interestingly, the Inverse Amplitude Method is safe (as the inverse amplitude has the opposite, convergent behavior allowing contour closure). Generically, one-dimensional sum rules such as for the photon vacuum polarization, one-dimensional form factors or the Adler function, to name a few, are not affected by this uncertainty. Likewise, fixed-t dispersion relations were cleverly constructed to avoid it and consequences therefrom are solid.

## Key claim
Compositeness or finite-range interactions cause a smearing of causality that introduces an exponential growth of partial-wave amplitudes in the upper complex energy plane, making standard Cauchy-contour dispersion relations invalid for these amplitudes unless a modified auxiliary function is used; this limits the maximum precision of certain dispersive approaches but does not affect one-dimensional sum rules or fixed-t dispersion relations.

## Method
The authors analyze the effect of finite target size on the time-domain scattering amplitude, showing that scattering from the edge of a composite object can produce a phase advance relative to scattering from the center, causing the amplitude to be non-zero for small negative times. They derive modified dispersion relations for partial-wave amplitudes that include an exponential factor to compensate for this behavior, and numerically evaluate the resulting uncertainty using the known scalar ππ partial-wave amplitude from Garcia-Martin et al. (2011) with an effective interaction range R ≈ 2 GeV⁻¹.

## Key numbers
- The phase advance for off-center scattering is given by 2R sin(θ/2) cos(α - θ/2), with maximum value 2R sin(θ/2)
- The effective range adopted for numerical estimates is R ≈ 2 GeV⁻¹ (compared to 0.79 fm ≈ 4 GeV⁻¹ for the pion scalar radius from Oller (2007) and 1/m_ρ = 0.26 fm for the vector radius)
- The diagonal matrix elements A_{JL} coupling partial waves are between 0.6 and 2/3, while off-diagonal elements fall quickly (e.g., A_{02} ≈ -0.095)
- The numerical uncertainty from the right-cut integral grows quickly with energy above threshold, as shown in figures for one-subtraction and two-subtraction dispersion relations

## Context
This work extends the classic observation by Gell-Mann, Goldberger, and Thirring (1954) that fixed-angle dispersion relations require modification for composite objects, and the discussion by Newton (1982) and Nussenzveig (1972) on causality violations from finite-size targets. The paper distinguishes between safe approaches (one-dimensional sum rules for the muon g-2, fixed-t dispersion relations, and the Inverse Amplitude Method from Dobado (1989)) and precision-limited ones (partial-wave dispersion relations). It enables future work on quantifying theoretical uncertainties in dispersive tests of the Standard Model with hadrons and in unitarization methods for electroweak effective theories, while noting that the Inverse Amplitude Method remains safe because the inverse amplitude has convergent behavior allowing contour closure.
