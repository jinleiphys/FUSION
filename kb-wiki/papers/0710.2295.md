# Solar Neutrinos: Models, Observations, and New Opportunities

> I discuss the development and resolution of the solar neutrino problem, as well as opportunities now open to us to extend our knowledge of main-sequence stellar evolution and neutrino astrophysics.

## Key claim

The solar neutrino problem was resolved not by errors in the standard solar model (SSM) but by the discovery that neutrinos have mass and undergo flavor conversion, with approximately two-thirds of solar electron neutrinos arriving at Earth as heavy-flavor neutrinos. The paper argues that this resolution now opens opportunities to use the Sun as a calibrated laboratory for testing stellar evolution and neutrino properties, provided that remaining open questions about neutrino mixing parameters and solar core metallicity are addressed.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of solar neutrino physics, from the early predictions of the SSM through the radiochemical chlorine (Homestake) and gallium (Gallex/GNO/SAGE) experiments, the water Cherenkov detectors Kamiokande and Super-Kamiokande, and the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO). SNO uniquely combined charged-current (CC) reactions on deuterium sensitive only to electron neutrinos, neutral-current (NC) reactions sensitive equally to all active flavors, and elastic scattering (ES) with flavor-dependent cross sections, allowing a model-independent determination of the total solar neutrino flux and its flavor composition.

## Key numbers

* The S-factor for the pp-chain reaction $^7$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^8$B was determined to $S_{17}(20~\mathrm{keV}) = 20.6 \pm 0.5 \pm 0.6$ eV-b, with an accuracy of $\lesssim$ 5%.
* The atmospheric neutrino mass-squared difference is $\delta m_{23}^2 \sim (2.2 \pm 0.8) \times 10^{-3}$ eV$^2$.
* The current upper bound on the reactor mixing angle from Chooz is $\sin^2{2\theta_{13}} \lesssim 0.19$ (at $\delta m^2 = 2.0 \times 10^{-3}$ eV).
* The absolute neutrino mass scale is bounded by tritium beta decay to $\lesssim 2.2$ eV.
* Cosmological constraints give $\sum_i m_\nu(i) \lesssim 0.7$ eV for the sum of neutrino masses.
* The $^8$B neutrino flux is predicted to grow with a time constant $\tau_0 \sim 0.9$ b.y., $\phi(^8\mathrm{B}) \sim \phi_0 e^{-t/\tau_0}$.
* The predicted CNO neutrino fluxes are $\phi(^{13}\mathrm{N}) \sim 5.7 \times 10^8/\mathrm{cm}^2\mathrm{s}$ and $\phi(^{15}\mathrm{O}) \sim 5.0 \times 10^8/\mathrm{cm}^2\mathrm{s}$.
* The SNO+ detector could detect about 2300 CNO neutrinos/year/kton above a threshold of about 0.8 MeV, with a flux measurement accurate to $\sim$ 10% possible.

## Context

This paper extends the line of work by Bahcall, Fowler, Iben, and Sears (1963) on SSM predictions and by Davis, Harmer, and Hoffman (1968) on the first solar neutrino measurements, which together established the solar neutrino problem. It builds on the resolution provided by SNO (2001-2002) and Super-Kamiokande, which demonstrated neutrino flavor conversion via the MSW effect as predicted by Mikheyev and Smirnov (1985) and Wolfenstein (1978). The paper enables future measurements of CNO neutrinos (e.g., by SNO+) to test the SSM assumption equating surface and core metallicity, and to probe the CNO cycle that dominates energy generation in massive main-sequence stars.
