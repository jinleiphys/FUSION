# Proxy-$SU(4)$ symmetry in A=60-90 region

> Applications of the proxy-$SU(3)$ model of Bonatsos and collaborators to nuclei in A=60-90 region introduces proxy-$SU(4)$ symmetry. Shell model spaces with single particle (sp) orbits $^1p_{3/2}$, $^1p_{1/2}$, $^0f_{5/2}$ and $^0g_{9/2}$ are essential for these nuclei and also protons and neutrons in this region occupy the same sp orbits. With this and applying the "proxy scheme", the $^0g_{9/2}$ changes to $^0f_{7/2}$ giving the SGA $U(40) \supset [U(10) \supset G \supset SO(3)] \otimes [SU(4) \supset SU_S(2) \otimes SU_T(2)]$. With $G=SU(3)$, we have the proxy-$SU(3)$ model. It is easy to see that proxy-$SU(3)$ symmetry implies goodness of the $SU(4)$ symmetry appearing above, i.e. proxy-$SU(4)$ symmetry. Shell model calculations pointing out the need for $^0g_{9/2}$ orbit, ground state masses, shape changes and shape co-existence in A=60-90 region and GT distributions clearly show the importance of proxy-$SU(4)$ in this mass region. Besides presenting this evidence, new proxy schemes with $G=SU(5)$, $SO(6)$ and $SO(10)$ that are generated by good proxy-$SU(4)$ symmetry are described in some detail. An important feature is that the four proxy symmetries $SU(3)$, $SO(6)$, $SU(5)$ and $SO(10)$ appear twice.

## Key claim

The paper argues that applying the proxy-SU(3) model to nuclei in the A=60-90 region necessarily introduces proxy-SU(4) symmetry, and that this symmetry is supported by indirect evidence from shell model calculations, ground state masses, shape changes, and Gamow-Teller distributions, though the evidence is circumstantial rather than direct.

## Method

The authors construct the group chain U(40) ⊃ [U(10) ⊃ G ⊃ SO(3)] ⊗ [SU(4) ⊃ SU_S(2) ⊗ SU_T(2)] for the proxy η=3 shell, where the active single-particle orbits ¹p₃/₂, ¹p₁/₂, ⁰f₅/₂, and ⁰g₉/₂ are reduced by replacing ⁰g₉/₂ with proxy ⁰f₇/₂. They enumerate the lowest SU(3) irreducible representations for all allowed nucleon numbers m and isospin T in the proxy η=3 shell using formulas connecting U(10) and SU(4) irreps, and then extend the analysis to new symmetry limits with G = SO(6), SU(5), and SO(10), providing their generators and quadratic Casimir invariants.

## Key numbers

- For N=Z nuclei around A=80, the ratio R(A) = Δ₂,₀(A)/⟨Δ₁,₀(A)⟩ = 2.45 ± 0.08, compared to R=3 for isospin symmetry (x=1) and R=2.4 for SU(4) symmetry (x=4)
- The proxy η=3 shell contains 40 single-particle states (U(40) algebra) with m ranging from 0 to 40 nucleons
- For m=12, the lowest SU(3) irrep for T=0 is (24,0); for m=14, T=0 it is (22,4); for m=16, T=0 it is (20,8)
- The quadratic Casimir invariant eigenvalue for SU(3) is ⟨C₂(SU(3))⟩^(λ,μ) = [λ² + μ² + λμ + 3(λ + μ)]
- The quadratic Casimir invariant eigenvalue for SO(6) is ⟨C₂(SO(6))⟩^[σ₁,σ₂,σ₃] = [σ₁(σ₁+4) + σ₂(σ₂+2) + σ₃²]

## Context

This work extends the proxy-SU(3) model of Bonatsos and collaborators (Bonatsos, Casten, and others, reviewed in Bonat-rev) to the A=60-90 mass region, where protons and neutrons occupy the same single-particle orbits, introducing spin-isospin SU(4) symmetry. The paper contrasts this approach with pseudo-SU(3) calculations in the r3 space (¹p₃/₂, ¹p₁/₂, ⁰f₅/₂) plus proton-neutron pairing SO(5) in ⁰g₉/₂ space, and with deformed shell model calculations using the Kuo interaction. The new proxy symmetries with G=SO(6), SU(5), and SO(10) enable future calculations of ground state structure and spectra for nuclei in this region, and the authors note that each of the four proxy symmetries (SU(3), SO(6), SU(5), SO(10)) appears twice, corresponding to α=+1 and α=-1 in the generator definitions.
