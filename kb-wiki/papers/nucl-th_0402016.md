# Shell effect in Pb isotopes near the proton drip line

> A mass formula (BWM) without shell effect is employed to study the variation of the shell effect in Pb isotopes through comparison with the experimental data. Unlike other macroscopic formulae, the BWM reproduces the general trend of the binding energy versus neutron number curves of all the nuclei from Li to Bi. The shell effect in Pb-isotopes reduces to ~56 keV at N=106 but, increases gradually for N<106, indicating increasing shell effect in Pb near the proton drip line.

## Key claim

The shell effect in Pb isotopes, extracted by comparing experimental binding energies to the Bethe-Weizsäcker modified (BWM) mass formula that contains no shell effects, reduces to approximately 56 keV at N=106 but then increases gradually for N<106, indicating an increasing shell effect in Pb near the proton drip line rather than a monotonic quenching.

## Method

The authors used the BWM mass formula, which modifies the asymmetry and pairing energy terms of the standard Bethe-Weizsäcker formula and was fitted to binding energy curves from Li to Bi, to compute binding energies for Pb isotopes without any shell effect. They then compared these BWM predictions to experimental mass data to extract the "shell effect" ΔB (the difference between experimental and BWM binding energies), and also computed the two-proton separation energy G₂p and single-proton separation energy Sₚ for Pb and nearby nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The shell effect in Pb isotopes reduces to approximately 56 keV at N=106, the minimum value observed.
- The BWM mass formula parameters include: volume term 15.777 A, surface term 18.34 A^(2/3), Coulomb term 0.71 Z(Z-1)/A^(1/3), asymmetry term 23.21 (A-2Z)²/[A×(1+e^(-A/17))], and pairing term (1-e^(-A/30))δ where δ = +12 A^(-1/2) for even-even nuclei, -12 A^(-1/2) for odd-odd nuclei, and 0 for odd-A nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the search for shell quenching effects in Pb isotopes, following earlier suggestions from alpha-decay experiments that the Z=82 shell closure might disappear near N=112-114 (Toth 1984, Brown 1992, Schmorak 1979), and observations of large variation in hindrance factors indicating persistence of the Z=82 shell gap on the neutron-deficient side (Wauters 1994). The results contradict the "unquenched" FRDM model of Möller (1995) which showed similar deviations for both Cd and Pb isotopes, and instead support the self-consistent calculation of Bender (2002) which found an increase of the Z=82 shell gap approaching the proton drip line. The work enables future experimental measurements of knockout and pickup cross sections from light Pb isotopes to extract spectroscopic factors and confirm the behavior of the Z=82 shell gap.
