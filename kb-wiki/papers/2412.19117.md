# Deformation and core$+n$ decoupling in the spectrum of $^{17}$C

> The coexistence of various structures, such as diverse shapes and cluster structures, is a fundamental property of atomic nuclei. In neutron-rich nuclei, a core$+n$ structure can compete with nuclear deformation due to the small neutron separation energy. A neutron-rich carbon isotope, $^{17}$C, exemplifies the appearance of the deformation and the core+$n$ decoupling in its spectrum, which is desirable for a deeper understanding of the coexistence phenomena in neutron-rich nuclei. We aim to describe and understand this coexistence phenomenon in the low-lying levels of $^{17}$C in a unified manner considering explicitly the degrees of freedom of both the quadrupole deformation and the relative motion between a $^{16}$C core and a valence neutron. We adopt the generator coordinate method (GCM) with the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) to describe various configurations. We superpose various basis wave functions generated by the energy variation by imposing two types of constraints: one incorporating the degree of the quadrupole deformation and the other taking care of the relative motion between a $^{16}$C core and a valence neutron. We find that the experimental energy level is well reproduced by the present method, including both deformed and $^{16}$C+$n$ configurations. The ground $3/2^{+}$ and second excited $5/2^{+}$ states exhibit a triaxially deformed shape, while the main component of the first excited $1/2^{+}$ state is a $^{16}$C($0^{+}$) core plus an $s$-wave neutron configuration. The tail of the valence neutron is significantly improved by including the $^{16}$C+$n$ basis functions explicitly. The explicit inclusion of both the quadrupole deformation and the relative motion between a core and a valence neutron is essential to describe the coexistence phenomena observed in neutron-rich nuclei in the AMD+GCM framework.

## Key claim

The coexistence of quadrupole deformation and core+neutron decoupling in the low-lying states of 17C can be described in a unified manner only when both degrees of freedom are explicitly included in the AMD+GCM framework; the experimental level ordering is reproduced only in the full model space that superposes basis functions from both the β-γ constraint method and the d constraint method.

## Method

The authors performed generator coordinate method (GCM) calculations with antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) basis wave functions for 17C, using two types of constraints: the β-γ constraint to control quadrupole deformation parameters, and the d constraint to control the distance between a 16C core and a valence neutron. They superposed 66 basis wave functions on the β-γ plane (β ≤ 0.5, 0° ≤ γ ≤ 60°) and 15 basis wave functions for d from 1 to 15 fm, then projected onto good angular momentum and parity. The Hamiltonian used the Volkov No. 2 central force with Majorana exchange parameter M = 0.579 and the G3RS spin-orbit force with V_LS = 800 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The ground 3/2+ state and second excited 5/2+ state both have maximum squared overlap with the same deformed basis at (β, γ) = (0.26, 19°), with values 87.3% and 84.3% respectively, indicating triaxial deformation.
- The spectroscopic factor for the 1/2+ state in the full model space is S = 0.521 for the 0+ ⊗ s1/2 configuration, compared to 0.481 in the β-γ only model space.
- The calculated B(M1; 1/2+ → 3/2+) in the full model space is 3.72 × 10^(-1) μ_N^2, compared to the experimental value of 1.04^(+0.03)_(-0.12) × 10^(-2) μ_N^2.
- The calculated B(M1; 5/2+ → 3/2+) in the full model space is 9.11 × 10^(-3) μ_N^2, compared to the experimental value of 7.12^(+1.27)_(-0.96) × 10^(-2) μ_N^2.
- The calculated neutron separation energy for the 1/2+ state is 0.80 MeV, while the experimental value is 0.52 MeV.
- The calculated root-mean-square radius of 16C is 2.64 fm, close to the empirical value 2.70 ± 0.03 fm.

## Context

This work extends the AMD+GCM approach previously applied to deformed nuclei (Suhara 2009) and cluster structures (Taniguchi 2004) by combining both β-γ and d constraint methods in a single framework. It addresses the long-standing puzzle of the 17C spectrum, where the ground 3/2+ state was understood via the Nilsson model with prolate deformation (Nilsson 1955) and the first excited 1/2+ state was suggested to be a one-neutron halo from Coulomb breakup measurements (Datta Pramanik 2003) and lifetime measurements (Suzuki 2008, Smalley 2015). The method enables future application to other neutron-rich carbon isotopes such as 19C and 21C where similar coexistence phenomena may occur.
