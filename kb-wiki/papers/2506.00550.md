# Learning about neutron star composition from the slope of the mass-radius diagram

> The slope of the neutron star mass-radius curve, $dM/dR$, is studied to understand the information it may carry about the composition of neutron stars, particularly with regard to the presence of non-nucleonic degrees of freedom. This study uses two large sets of relativistic mean-field equations of state with either nucleonic or nucleonic and hyperonic degrees of freedom, and imposes constraints obtained from GW170817 and the pulsars PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620. It is shown that: i) some mass-radius curves are characterized by a negative slope from one solar mass up to the maximum mass; ii) other equations of state (EoS) have a positive slope for a given range of masses below the maximum star mass. Within the set of models considered, the first set includes only a very small number of hyperonic EoS: less than 0.5\% of the total number of hyperonic stars and approximately one third of the nucleonic EoS. We have also analyzed the sign of the slope for neutron star masses of 1.2, 1.4 and 1.8$M_\odot$. Only approximately 1\% of hyperonic equations of state (EoS) predict a negative slope for 1.4$M_\odot$ stars, whereas over 90\% of nucleonic stars have a negative slope at this mass. Finally, almost all stars have a negative slope at 1.8$M_\odot$. A positive slope at 1.4$M_\odot$ may indicate the presence of non-nucleonic degrees of freedom within neutron stars. The nuclear matter property that distinguishes the different scenarios most clearly is the curvature of the symmetry energy. Nucleonic EoSs with a positive slope $dM/dR$ predict the highest values, which can exceed 100 MeV.

## Key claim

The sign of the slope dM/dR of the neutron star mass-radius curve at specific masses, particularly a positive slope at 1.4 solar masses, may indicate the presence of non-nucleonic degrees of freedom (hyperons) within neutron stars, but this is a probabilistic indicator rather than a definitive signature, as approximately one quarter of nucleonic stars at 1.2 solar masses also show a positive slope.

## Method

The authors generated two large sets of relativistic mean-field equations of state (17,537 nucleonic and 16,146 hyperonic EoS) through Bayesian inference constrained by nuclear matter saturation properties and the requirement of maximum mass above 2.0 solar masses. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for each EoS to obtain mass-radius sequences, then classified each EoS by whether the global slope dM/dR was negative (monotonically decreasing radius with mass) or not negative (back-bending present), and also evaluated the local slope sign at fixed masses of 1.2, 1.4, and 1.8 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- Less than 0.5% of hyperonic EoS (77 out of 16,146) satisfy dM/dR < 0 globally, compared to approximately one third of nucleonic EoS (11,495 out of 17,537)
- Only approximately 1% of hyperonic EoS predict a negative slope at 1.4 solar masses, whereas over 90% of nucleonic stars have a negative slope at this mass
- For nucleonic EoS with dM/dR < 0, the median maximum mass is 2.05 solar masses (5% quantile: 2.01, 95% quantile: 2.17); for hyperonic EoS with dM/dR < 0, the median maximum mass is 2.01 solar masses (5%: 2.00, 95%: 2.04)
- The median radius of a 1.4 solar mass star for hyperonic EoS with dM/dR < 0 is 13.39 km (5%: 13.26, 95%: 13.55); for nucleonic EoS with dM/dR < 0 it is 12.40 km (5%: 12.09, 95%: 12.87)
- Nucleonic EoS with a positive slope dM/dR predict the highest values of the curvature of the symmetry energy, which can exceed 100 MeV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Ferreira (2024) on the slope of the mass-radius curve as a diagnostic for neutron star composition, using the Bayesian-inferred EoS sets from Malik (2023). It builds on earlier studies by Cavagnoli (2011) and Dexheimer (2018) showing that the mass-radius slope for low to medium mass stars may be influenced by symmetry energy properties, and by Tan (2021) discussing how the slope of binary Love relations reflects the speed of sound in nuclear matter. The results suggest that future precise measurements of the mass-radius slope at 1.4 solar masses could help distinguish between purely nucleonic and hyperonic compositions, complementing constraints from gravitational wave events like GW170817 and NICER observations of PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620.
