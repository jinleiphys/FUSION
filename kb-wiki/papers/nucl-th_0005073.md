# The halo of the exotic nucleus 11Li: a single Cooper pair

> If neutrons are progressively added to a normal nucleus, the Pauli principle forces them into states of higher momentum. When the core becomes neutron-saturated, the nucleus expels most of the wavefunction of the last neutrons outside to form a halo, which because of its large size can have lower momentum. It is an open question how nature stabilizes such a fragile system and provides the glue needed to bind the halo neutrons to the core. Here we show that this problem is similar to that of the instability of the normal state of an electron system at zero temperature solved by Cooper, solution which is at the basis of BCS theory of superconductivity. By mimicking this approach using, aside from the bare nucleon-nucleon interaction, the long wavelength vibrations of the nucleus $^{11}$Li, the paradigm of halo nuclei, as tailored glues of the least bound neutrons, we are able to obtain a unified and quantitative picture of the observed properties of $^{11}$Li.

# The halo of the exotic nucleus 11Li: a single Cooper pair

## Key claim
The halo of 11Li is stabilized by pairing correlations induced by the exchange of long-wavelength collective vibrations between the two least-bound neutrons, acting as a tailored glue that supplements the bare nucleon-nucleon interaction; without this induced pairing, the bare Argonne potential alone cannot bind the two neutrons into a halo.

## Method
The authors calculated the single-particle spectrum of 10Li by coupling s1/2 and p1/2 orbitals to monopole, dipole, and quadrupole vibrations of the 9Li core, using random phase approximation (RPA) vibrational states built from a multipole-multipole separable interaction with strengths adjusted to reproduce 10Be data. They then diagonalized the two-neutron Hamiltonian for 11Li including both the bare Argonne nucleon-nucleon potential and the induced interaction from phonon exchange, using particle-vibration coupling vertices and self-energy corrections, and solved the resulting secular matrix for the ground state of the Cooper pair.

## Key numbers
- Two-neutron separation energy of 11Li: S2n = 0.294 ± 0.03 MeV (experimental value)
- Mean square radius of 11Li: ⟨r²⟩^{1/2} = 3.55 ± 0.10 fm, compared to 2.32 ± 0.02 fm for the 9Li core
- FWHM of perpendicular momentum distribution of halo neutrons from breakup on 12C: σ⊥ = 48 ± 10 MeV/c (experimental); calculated value σ⊥ = 56 MeV/c
- Ground-state energy of the Cooper pair from induced interaction alone: Egs = -0.270 MeV; adding Argonne potential gives Egs = -0.330 MeV
- Pairing correlation energy: Eo = 0.730 MeV (measured from unperturbed 2Es1/2 = 400 keV)
- Ground-state occupation probabilities: s1/2² = 0.40, p1/2² = 0.58, d5/2² = 0.02
- Mean square radius of the center of mass of the two halo neutrons: ⟨rcm²⟩^{1/2} = 5.4 fm
- Average mean square distance between halo neutrons: ⟨r12²⟩^{1/2} ≈ 9.2 fm
- Radius of the single-particle distribution of the halo: 7.1 fm
- Calculated total mean square radius of 11Li: 3.9 fm (slightly larger than experimental value)
- p1/2 resonance centroid in 10Li: Eres ≈ 0.5 MeV; s1/2 virtual state: Evirt = 0.2 MeV

## Context
This work extends the Cooper pair concept from condensed-matter physics (Cooper 1956, Bardeen 1957) to nuclear halo systems, showing that the pairing mechanism in 11Li is analogous to electron pairing via phonons in superconductors. It contradicts the expectation that the bare nucleon-nucleon interaction (Argonne potential) alone can bind the halo neutrons, and instead demonstrates that induced pairing from collective vibrations is essential. The results enable a strategy for predicting new neutron-rich nuclei near the drip line by identifying systems with soft long-wavelength response, building on earlier work by Barranco (1999) and experimental studies of 11Li by Hansen (1987), Kobayashi (1988), and Tanihata (1996).
