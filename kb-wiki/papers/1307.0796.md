# Symmetry Tests in Photo-Pion Production

> Small angle electron scattering with intense electron beams opens up the possibility of performing almost real photon induced reactions with thin, polarized hydrogen and few body targets, allowing for the detection of low energy charged particles.This promises to be much more effective than conventional photon tagging techniques. For photo-pion reactions some fundamental new possibilities include: tests of charge symmetry in the N-N system by measurement of the neutron-neutron scattering length $a_{nn}$ in the $γD \rightarrow π^{+} nn$ reaction; tests of isospin breaking due to the mass difference of the up and down quarks; measurements with polarized targets are sensitive to $π$N phase shifts and will test the validity of the Fermi-Watson (final state interaction) theorem. All of these experiments will test the accuracy and energy region of validity of chiral effective theories.

## Key claim

Small-angle electron scattering (virtual photon tagging) with high-intensity electron beams can enable photo-pion experiments that are significantly more effective than conventional photon tagging, particularly for tests of charge symmetry, isospin breaking, and chiral effective theories, provided that the Moller electron background can be eliminated by magnetic analysis at sufficiently large electron angles (approximately 4 degrees for 300 MeV or 2 degrees for 500 MeV incident electron energy).

## Method

The paper proposes using forward-angle electron scattering at very low four-momentum transfer (Q^2) to produce nearly real photons, where the scattered electrons are detected in a magnetic spectrometer that measures energy loss and eliminates Moller scattering background. The virtual photon luminosity is calculated by integrating over an electron angular ring (4 to 14 degrees in theta, half the ring in phi) for a 300 MeV beam with 1 mA current on a thin target of density 10^17 cm^-2, yielding a photon luminosity comparable to conventional tagging at Mainz. The method is applied to specific photo-pion reactions including gamma D -> pi+ nn for measuring the neutron-neutron scattering length, gamma D -> pi0 D for coherent pion production, and polarized target asymmetry measurements for testing the Fermi-Watson theorem.

## Key numbers

- Photon luminosity for virtual tagging: L_gamma^vTag = 8x10^27 cm^-2 sec^-1, achieved with 300 MeV beam energy, 1 mA current, target density 10^17 cm^-2, and electron angular integration from 4 to 14 degrees over half the phi ring
- Conventional tagging luminosity at Mainz: L_gamma^tag = 8x10^27 cm^-2 sec^-1, with N_e^tag = 5x10^5 Hz, N_T^tag = 8x10^22 cm^-2, and tagging efficiency epsilon_Tag = 0.2
- Moller background elimination requires electron angles of approximately 4 degrees for 300 MeV or 2 degrees for 500 MeV incident energy for energy loss Delta E ~ 200 MeV needed for pion production
- Theoretical cross sections for gamma D -> nn pi+ are calculated in units of 10^-5 microbarn sr^-2 MeV^-2 at 5 MeV above threshold and pion CM production angle theta_pi = 135 degrees, with sensitivity to a_nn values of 18, 19, and 20 fm
- BLAST experiment achieved polarized target density of approximately 10^14 protons(deuterons) per cm^2 with 1.5 cm diameter target tube
- Longitudinal contribution to the cross section is estimated to be less than approximately 2% for low Q^2 values

## Context

This paper extends the experimental approach demonstrated by the BLAST experiment at Bates (Hasell et al., 2011) and builds on the planned POLITE project that was terminated due to funding cessation. The proposed virtual tagging method offers advantages over conventional photon tagging techniques used by the A2 collaboration at Mainz and in experiments by Hornidge et al. (2012). The specific reaction for measuring a_nn from gamma D -> pi+ nn is based on chiral perturbation theory calculations by Lensky et al. (2007), and the isospin breaking tests relate to pi0-eta-eta' mixing corrections calculated by Bernstein and Holstein (2013). The work enables future tests of chiral perturbation theory and its connection to QCD, with potential extension to measurements at higher photon virtualities to determine the spatial extent (RMS radius) of transition matrix elements.
