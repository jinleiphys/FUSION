# Nuclear Physics from Lattice Quantum Chromodynamics

> Quantum Chromodynamics and Quantum Electrodynamics, both renormalizable quantum field theories with a small number of precisely constrained input parameters, dominate the dynamics of the quarks and gluons - the underlying building blocks of protons, neutrons, and nuclei. While the analytic techniques of quantum field theory have played a key role in understanding the dynamics of matter in high energy processes, they encounter difficulties when applied to low-energy nuclear structure and reactions, and dense systems. Expected increases in computational resources into the exascale during the next decade will provide the ability to determine a range of important strong interaction processes directly from QCD using the numerical technique of Lattice QCD. This will complement the nuclear physics experimental program, and in partnership with new thrusts in nuclear many-body theory, will enable unprecedented understanding and refinement of nuclear forces and, more generally, the visible matter in our universe. In this presentation, I will discuss the state-of-the-art Lattice QCD calculations of quantities of interest in nuclear physics, progress that is expected in the near future, and the anticipated impact.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD is now positioned to calculate properties and interactions of simple nuclear systems at physical light quark masses with dynamical QED included, but the full program requires extrapolations from finite-volume, discretized Euclidean-space calculations to infinite-volume Minkowski-space observables using effective field theories, and the anticipated exascale computing resources over the next decade are necessary to realize this capability broadly.

## Method

The paper reviews the LQCD methodology: gauge-field configurations are generated using variants of the HMC algorithm on leadership-class supercomputers, quark propagators are computed iteratively from these configurations using capacity computing resources, and hadronic correlation functions are constructed from multiple sources and sinks to extract the lowest-lying eigenstates in the lattice volume. For nuclear systems, the spectra of light nuclei and nucleon-nucleon scattering phase shifts are calculated at multiple pion masses and lattice volumes, then matched to nuclear effective field theories (NEFTs) such as pionless EFT to extend predictions to heavier nuclei and to determine counterterms directly from QCD.

## Key numbers

* Light hadron spectra postdicted by LQCD successfully reproduce experimental values, with the pion, kaon, and Omega- masses used to fix input parameters in the isospin limit without QED.
* The rho resonance was mapped out at pion masses of m_pi ~ 240 MeV and m_pi ~ 390 MeV using coupled-channel analysis (Wilson 2015).
* Strange form factors G_E^(s) and G_M^(s) were calculated at Q^2 = 0.26 GeV^2 and Q^2 = 0.17 GeV^2, with LQCD results more precise than experimental constraints at Q^2 = 0.23 GeV^2 (Shanahan 2014).
* The lowest-lying states in S-shell nuclei and hypernuclei were calculated at a pion mass of m_pi ~ 805 MeV (Beane 2012).
* The radiative capture cross section np -> d gamma was calculated as sigma^lqcd = 334.9 (+5.4/-4.7) mb at incident neutron speed v = 2200 m/s, consistent with the experimental value of 334.2 +/- 0.5 mb (Beane 2015).
* Magnetic moments of the lightest nuclei at m_pi ~ 805 MeV, expressed in natural nuclear magnetons defined with the nucleon mass at that quark mass, are very close to their physical values (Beane 2014, Chang 2015).
* A binding energy of charmonia to nuclear matter of ~ 40 MeV at physical light-quark masses was estimated from LQCD calculations at a heavy pion mass (Beane 2014).

## Context

This review extends the program of matching LQCD calculations to chiral nuclear forces, originally formulated by Weinberg (1990, 1991) and developed by Ordonez (1992), Kaplan (1996-1998), and Beane (2001), by demonstrating that LQCD can now determine the counterterms in NEFTs directly from QCD rather than solely from experimental data. The work enables predictions for nuclei beyond the reach of direct LQCD calculations, as demonstrated by Barnea (2013) who used LQCD results at m_pi ~ 805 MeV to predict binding energies of He-5, Li-5, and Li-6 via pionless EFT, and establishes a path toward refining nuclear forces at the physical point. The calculations also provide inputs for dark matter detection (nuclear sigma-terms), tests of the standard model through neutron beta-decay constraints, and predictions for charmonia-nucleus interactions relevant to the JLab 12 GeV upgrade program.
