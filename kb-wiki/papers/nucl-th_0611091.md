# Relativistic Dynamics of Non-ideal Fluids: Viscous and heat-conducting fluids II. Transport properties and microscopic description of relativistic nuclear matter

> In the causal theory of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics, there are conditions on the equation of state and other thermodynamic properties such as the second-order coefficients of a fluid that need to be satisfied to guarantee that the fluid perturbations propagate causally and obey hyperbolic equations. The second-order coefficients in the causal theory, which are the relaxation times for the dissipative degrees of freedom and coupling constants between different forms of dissipation (relaxation lengths), are presented for partonic and hadronic systems. These coefficients involves relativistic thermodynamic integrals. The integrals are presented for general case and also for different regimes in the temperature--chemical potential plane. It is shown that for a given equation of state these second-order coefficients are not additional parameters but they are determined by the equation of state. We also present the prescription on the calculation of the freeze-out particle spectra from the dynamics of relativistic non-ideal fluids.

## Key claim

The second-order coefficients in causal relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics (relaxation times and coupling constants between dissipative processes) are not free parameters but are fully determined by the equation of state of the nuclear matter, provided the equation of state satisfies conditions that guarantee causal, hyperbolic propagation of fluid perturbations.

## Method

The author employs Grad's 14-moment method to solve the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation for small deviations from local equilibrium, expressing the non-equilibrium distribution function in terms of dissipative fluxes (shear stress, bulk viscous pressure, and heat flux). The second-order coefficients are derived from relativistic thermodynamic integrals of the equilibrium distribution function, which are presented for general cases and for specific regimes in the temperature-chemical potential plane, using the Walecka model for hadronic matter and the MIT Bag model for the quark-gluon plasma phase.

## Key numbers

- The shear viscosity relaxation time coefficient involves the thermodynamic integral combination $\beta_2 = \frac{1}{2} \frac{\cj_{52}}{\beta \cj_{42}^2}$, where $\cj_{nk}$ are relativistic thermodynamic integrals
- The bulk viscosity relaxation time coefficient is given by $\beta_0 = \frac{3}{\beta\cj_{42}^2\Omega^2} \left\{5 \cj_{52} - \frac{3}{D_{20}}[\cj_{31}(\cj_{31}\cj_{30}-\cj_{41}\cj_{20})+\cj_{41}(\cj_{41}\cj_{10}-\cj_{31}\cj_{20})]\right\}$
- The heat flux relaxation time coefficient is $\beta_1 = \frac{D_{41}}{\beta \Lambda^2 \cj_{21}^2 \cj_{31}}$
- The coupling coefficient between heat flux and bulk viscous pressure is $\alpha_0 = \frac{D_{41} D_{20} - D_{31} D_{30}}{\beta \Lambda \Omega \cj_{42} \cj_{21}\cj_{31} D_{20}}$
- The coupling coefficient between heat flux and shear stress is $\alpha_1 = \frac{\cj_{31} \cj_{52} - \cj_{41} \cj_{42}}{\beta\Lambda \cj_{42} \cj_{21} \cj_{31}}$

## Context

This work extends the causal relativistic fluid dynamics formalism developed by Israel and Stewart (1970s) and the earlier work by Muller (1966) on non-relativistic fluids, providing explicit microscopic expressions for the second-order transport coefficients that were previously left as unspecified phenomenological parameters. The paper enables quantitative calculations of dissipative effects in heavy-ion collision simulations and astrophysical scenarios (supernova explosions, neutron star formation) by showing how these coefficients follow from the chosen equation of state, whether from the Walecka model for hadronic matter or the MIT Bag model for quark-gluon plasma. The freeze-out prescription for particle spectra from non-ideal fluids is also presented for direct application to experimental observables.
