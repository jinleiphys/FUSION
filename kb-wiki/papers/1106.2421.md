# Proton inelastic diffraction by a black nucleus and the size of excited nuclei

> We systematically derive a length scale characterizing the size of a low-lying, beta stable nucleus from empirical data for the diffraction peak angle in the proton inelastic differential cross section of incident energy of \sim 1 GeV. In doing so, we assume that the target nucleus in the ground state is a completely absorptive "black" sphere of radius a. The cross section πa^2, where a is determined in such a way as to reproduce the empirical proton diffraction peak angle in the elastic channel, is known to agree with empirical total reaction cross sections for incident protons to within error bars. By comparing the inelastic diffraction patterns obtained in the Fraunhofer approximation with the experimental ones, one can likewise derive the black sphere radius a_l for the excited state with spin l. We find that for ^{12}C, ^{58,60,62,64}Ni, and ^{208}Pb, the value of a_l obtained from the inelastic channel is generally larger than the value of a from the elastic channel and tends to increase with the excitation energy. This increase is remarkable for the Hoyle state. Finally, we discuss the relation between a_l and the size of excited nuclei.

## Key claim

The black sphere radius derived from proton inelastic diffraction peak angles for low-lying excited states of even-even nuclei is generally larger than the radius from elastic scattering and tends to increase with excitation energy, but this radius does not directly give the size of the excited nucleus, it is related to the transition density and expected to lie between the ground-state and excited-state sizes.

## Method

The authors treat the target nucleus as a completely absorptive "black" sphere and apply the Fraunhofer diffraction approximation to proton inelastic scattering at incident energies of about 1 GeV. They determine the black sphere radius \(a_l\) for each excited state by matching the calculated diffraction peak angle (from the inelastic Fraunhofer formula) to the empirical peak angle measured in proton inelastic differential cross sections, using specific numerical constants for each spin \(l\) (0, 2, 3, 4). The peak angles are extracted from experimental data for \(^{12}\)C, \(^{58,60,62,64}\)Ni, and \(^{208}\)Pb taken from the EXFOR database, with systematic errors estimated from the spacing of neighboring data points.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{12}\)C ground state: \(a = 2.75 \pm 0.06\) fm at \(T_p = 1040\) MeV; for the Hoyle state (\(0^+\), \(E_{\text{ex}} = 7.65\) MeV): \(a_0 = 3.20 \pm 0.07\) fm, giving \(a_0/a \simeq 1.16\)
- For \(^{58}\)Ni: ground state \(a = 4.79 \pm 0.18\) fm; \(2^+\) state (1.45 MeV) \(a_2 = 4.91 \pm 0.14\) fm; \(4^+\) state (2.46 MeV) \(a_4 = 5.22 \pm 0.11\) fm; \(3^-\) state (4.47 MeV) \(a_3 = 5.09 \pm 0.13\) fm at \(T_p = 1047\) MeV
- For \(^{208}\)Pb: ground state \(a = 7.49 \pm 0.35\) fm; \(3^-\) state (2.62 MeV) \(a_3 = 7.29 \pm 0.35\) fm at \(T_p = 1040\) MeV
- The elastic diffraction peak condition: \(2 p a \sin(\theta_M/2) = 5.1356\)
- The inelastic diffraction peak conditions: for \(l=0\), \(2 p a_0 \sin(\theta_{M0}/2) = 7.015\); for \(l=2\), \(2 p a_2 \sin(\theta_{M2}/2) = 6.783\); for \(l=3\), \(2 p a_3 \sin(\theta_{M3}/2) = 8.209\); for \(l=4\), \(2 p a_4 \sin(\theta_{M4}/2) = 9.617\)

## Context

This work extends the black sphere approach previously developed by Kohama, Iida, and Oyamatsu (2004, 2005) for proton elastic scattering to the inelastic channel, following the formalism of Blair (1959) for alpha-particle inelastic diffraction. The method provides a systematic way to extract a length scale from inelastic scattering data that correlates with excited-state nuclear sizes, complementing more microscopic approaches based on optical potentials and Glauber theory. The results enable future systematic studies of the size of isomers and other characteristic excited states across the nuclide chart, and the notable increase for the Hoyle state in \(^{12}\)C supports the alpha-clustering picture discussed by Takashina (2008).
