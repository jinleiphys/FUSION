# Geometry and entanglement in the scattering matrix

> A formulation of nucleon-nucleon scattering is developed in which the S-matrix, rather than an effective-field theory (EFT) action, is the fundamental object. Spacetime plays no role in this description: the S-matrix is a trajectory that moves between RG fixed points in a compact theory space defined by unitarity. This theory space has a natural operator definition, and a geometric embedding of the unitarity constraints in four-dimensional Euclidean space yields a flat torus, which serves as the stage on which the S-matrix propagates. Trajectories with vanishing entanglement are special geodesics between RG fixed points on the flat torus, while entanglement is driven by an external potential. The system of equations describing S-matrix trajectories is in general complicated, however the very-low-energy S-matrix -- that appears at leading-order in the EFT description -- possesses a UV/IR conformal invariance which renders the system of equations integrable, and completely determines the potential. In this geometric viewpoint, inelasticity is in correspondence with the radius of a three-dimensional hyperbolic space whose two-dimensional boundary is the flat torus. This space has a singularity at vanishing radius, corresponding to maximal violation of unitarity. The trajectory on the flat torus boundary can be explicitly constructed from a bulk trajectory with a quantifiable error, providing a simple example of a holographic quantum error correcting code.

## Key claim

The paper develops a geometric formulation of nucleon-nucleon scattering in which the S-matrix, rather than an effective field theory action, is the fundamental object; the very-low-energy S-matrix that appears at leading order in the EFT description possesses a UV/IR conformal invariance that renders the system of equations integrable and completely determines the entangling potential, though this symmetry is generally broken by higher-order corrections.

## Method

The authors parameterize the nucleon-nucleon S-matrix in a basis of the identity and spin-spin operators, imposing unitarity constraints that embed the S-matrix on a flat torus in four-dimensional Euclidean space. They define the entanglement power (EP) as a state-independent measure of quantum entanglement for the S-matrix, and show that trajectories with vanishing EP correspond to geodesics between renormalization group fixed points on the flat torus. The momentum flow of the S-matrix is described by beta functions, and the leading-order effective range expansion (ERE) is recovered by introducing an external entangling potential that drives the S-matrix away from geodesic motion.

## Key numbers

- The singlet scattering length is \(a_0 = -23.714 \pm 0.013\) fm and the triplet scattering length is \(a_1 = 5.425 \pm 0.002\) fm, determined experimentally for nucleon-nucleon scattering.
- The entanglement power of the nucleon-nucleon S-matrix is given by \(\mathcal{E}(\hat{\mathbf{S}}) = N_{\mathcal{P}} \sin^2(2(\delta_1 - \delta_0))\), with \(N_{\mathcal{P}} = 1/6\) when the probability distribution \(\mathcal{P} = 1\).
- The leading-order ERE approximation using only scattering lengths provides a good description of the physical nucleon-nucleon S-matrix for center-of-mass momenta \(p \leq 10\) MeV.
- The ratio of effective field theory couplings in the PDS scheme at the renormalization scale of the pion mass is \(C_T^{\mathrm{PDS}}/C_S^{\mathrm{PDS}} = 0.0824\).

## Context

This work extends the geometric approach to scattering developed in earlier studies of entanglement in free scalar field theory by Klco (2020) and builds on the effective field theory framework for nucleon-nucleon scattering established by Kaplan, Savage, and Wise (1998) and van Kolck (1998). The paper provides a new geometric interpretation of the renormalization group fixed points and entanglement in nuclear scattering, and suggests that inelasticity can be understood through a holographic duality relating the flat torus boundary to a three-dimensional hyperbolic bulk space, offering a concrete example of a quantum error correcting code.
