# The Systematics of Fundamental Particles and Unstable Nuclear Systems Using the Concept of Continuity and Discreteness

> In this review, a new method is proposed based on Cantor's theory of Cardinality to analyze the experimental data on unstable nuclear systems which includes fundamental particles and their flavours, $β$-decay including spin and parity, $α$-decay and the systematics of the energy levels of light nuclei. The method also derives a formula for the Binding Energies of unstable nuclei. All this is based on the theory of discreteness and continuity.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the ratio of a particle's width (or inverse lifetime) to its mass can be related to the mathematical expression \(n/2^n\) from Cantor's set theory, and that the integer \(n\) obtained from this relation serves as a universal index for classifying all unstable nuclear and particle systems by interaction strength. The authors hedge that this is a phenomenological approach that works for systems with at least two isospin flavours and does not apply to kaons, bottom and charmed mesons, or non-isospin states.

## Method

The authors take experimental masses and lifetimes (or half-lives) for elementary particles, \(\beta\)-emitting nuclei, and \(\alpha\)-emitting nuclei, and compute the integer \(n\) from the equation \(\frac{n}{2^n} = \frac{\hbar}{MT}\), where \(M\) is the mass (or neutron mass for \(\beta\)-emitters, or binding energy for \(\alpha\)-emitters) and \(T\) is the mean lifetime or half-life. They then plot and analyze the distribution of \(n\)-values across different decay types, study regularities in \(n\)-differences for isospin-related particles, and derive formulas for binding energies of \(\beta\)-emitters and for predicting lifetimes of isospin partners.

## Key numbers

- For elementary particles, the range of \(n\) values is: strong interactions \(n < 10\), electromagnetic interactions \(10 \le n \le 40\), weak interactions \(n > 40\).
- For the top quark with mass 174 GeV, the predicted lifetime lies between \(10^{-16}\) s and \(10^{-9}\) s.
- For a hypothetical toponium (ttbar) with mass \(\sim 350\) GeV, the predicted lifetime range is \(2 \times 10^{-25}\) s to \(2.5 \times 10^{-21}\) s.
- The predicted proton lifetime from the \(n\)-theory is \(5.33 \times 10^{33}\) years, which does not contradict the experimental limit of \(> 10^{31} - 5 \times 10^{32}\) years.
- For \(\beta\)-emitters, the \(n\) values mostly range from 70 to 150, with minimum \(n_0 = 70\) for \(\beta^-\) emitters and \(n_0 = 75\) for \(\beta^+\) emitters.
- The binding energy formula for \(\beta\)-emitters uses a normalising factor \(\alpha = 8.45\) and was tested against approximately 1300 nuclei.
- The \(\alpha\)-decay frequency factor \(f\) is constant over a wide range of \(n\)-values at about \(10^{21}\) to \(10^{22}\) per second.

## Context

This work extends earlier attempts to find discreteness in particle properties, notably Nambu (1952) who suggested a discrete mass spectrum based on the electron mass, and Mac Gregor (1970s) who found quantization of particle lifetimes in terms of the muon lifetime. The present approach claims to be more general by incorporating both mass and lifetime together in a unified framework without choosing a basis dependent on electron masses or muon lifetimes. The formalism enables predictions of lifetimes for isospin partners and for the proton, and suggests that excited nuclear levels can be associated with specific virtual mesons identified through the same \(n\)-index.
