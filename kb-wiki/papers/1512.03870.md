# Impacts of the Nuclear Symmetry Energy on Neutron Star Crusts

> Using the relativistic mean-field theory, we adopt two different methods, namely, the coexisting phase method and the self-consistent Thomas-Fermi approximation, to study the impacts of the nuclear symmetry energy on properties of neutron star crusts within a wide range of densities. It is found that the nuclear symmetry energy and its density slope play an important role in determining the pasta phases and the crust-core transition.

# Impacts of the Nuclear Symmetry Energy on Neutron Star Crusts

## Key claim
The nuclear symmetry energy and its density slope L strongly influence the pasta phase structure and crust-core transition density in neutron star crusts, but the effect is method-dependent: a smaller slope L produces more complex pasta phases (including bubble phases in the Thomas-Fermi method), while a larger L suppresses pasta phases and lowers the crust-core transition density.

## Method
The authors used relativistic mean-field (RMF) theory with two complementary approaches: the coexisting phases (CP) method and the self-consistent Thomas-Fermi (TF) approximation, both within the Wigner-Seitz cell approximation. They employed parameter sets from Bao et al. (2015) that share the same isoscalar properties and fixed symmetry energy but differ in the symmetry energy slope L, generated from the TM1 and IUFSU models. Calculations were performed at zero temperature for cold neutron star crusts under charge neutrality and beta equilibrium.

## Key numbers
- The symmetry energy at saturation density is constrained to 30 ± 4 MeV from experiments.
- The density slope L of the symmetry energy is uncertain, varying from about 20 to 115 MeV.
- The crust-core transition density decreases with increasing L (no specific numerical values for transition densities are given in the truncated text).

## Context
This work extends prior studies of neutron star crust structure (e.g., Refs. 6 and 7 in the paper) by systematically linking the symmetry energy slope L to pasta phase complexity and crust-core transition. It builds on the finding that pasta phases can reduce shear mode frequencies and explain quasi-periodic oscillations from soft gamma repeaters (Ref. 4). The results enable future finite-temperature studies of pasta phases and provide constraints for the uncertain symmetry energy slope L, which is important for interpreting neutron star oscillations and pulsar glitches (Ref. 5).
