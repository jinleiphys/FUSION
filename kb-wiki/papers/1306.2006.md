# Strange freezeout

## Key claim

The authors argue that known systematics of hadron cross sections may cause different particles to freeze out of the fireball produced in heavy-ion collisions at different times, and that a simple model with two freezeout points (2CFO) is a better description of data than the standard single freezeout (1CFO) model, while still remaining predictive. The resulting fits present constraints on the late stage evolution of the fireball, including the tantalizing possibility that the QCD chiral transition influences the yields at sqrt(S)=2700 GeV and the QCD critical point those at sqrt(S)=17.3 GeV.

## Method

The authors fit hadron yield data from multiple experiments across a range of collision energies using a multi-component hadron resonance gas model with one common temperature, baryon chemical potential, and volume for non-strange hadrons (except phi), and a different but common temperature, baryon chemical potential, and volume for strange hadrons and phi. They compare the chi-squared values of this 2CFO model against the standard 1CFO model, using discrimination and relative error metrics for each hadron species.

## Key numbers

- At sqrt(S)=17.3 GeV, the 2CFO fit gives T_S=157(3) MeV, T_NS=142(3) MeV, mu_S=214(14) MeV, mu_NS=208(8) MeV, with chi^2/N_df=15/7
- At sqrt(S)=2700 GeV (LHC), the 2CFO fit gives T_S=162(3) MeV, T_NS=146(3) MeV, mu_S=14(12) MeV, mu_NS=-2(7) MeV, with chi^2/N_df=4.4/6
- At sqrt(S)=130 GeV (RHIC), the 2CFO fit gives T_S=169(6) MeV, T_NS=157(8) MeV, mu_S=35(23) MeV, mu_NS=25(20) MeV, with chi^2/N_df=4.4/5
- For the 130 GeV data set from RHIC, the 1CFO model yields chi^2=12, close to the quoted value of chi^2=15 from more detailed models
- The ratio zeta=V_NS/V_c is around unity at the two lowest energies and rises with sqrt(S)

## Context

This work extends the standard single chemical freezeout (1CFO) paradigm established by Andronic et al., Yen et al., and Cleymans et al., which had explained hadron yields across a large range of collision energies with a single temperature and baryon chemical potential. The authors show that substructure in chemical freezeout is not a small correction but a significant effect, analogous to how decoupling in the early universe gives different freezeout for photons and neutrinos. The results enable using different hadron species as probes of the late-stage fireball evolution, potentially revealing signals of the QCD chiral transition at LHC energies and the QCD critical point at energies near where STAR sees anomalies in fluctuations of conserved quantities.
