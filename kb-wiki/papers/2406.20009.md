# Lanczos, the transfer matrix, and the signal-to-noise problem

> This work introduces a method for determining the energy spectrum of lattice quantum chromodynamics (LQCD) by applying the Lanczos algorithm to the transfer matrix and using a bootstrap generalization of the Cullum-Willoughby method to filter out spurious eigenvalues. Proof-of-principle analyses of the simple harmonic oscillator and the LQCD proton mass demonstrate that this method provides faster ground-state convergence than the "effective mass," which is related to the power-iteration algorithm. Lanczos provides more accurate energy estimates than multi-state fits to correlation functions with small imaginary times while achieving comparable statistical precision. Two-sided error bounds are computed for Lanczos results and guarantee that excited-state effects cannot shift Lanczos results far outside their statistical uncertainties.

# Lanczos, the transfer matrix, and the signal-to-noise problem

## Key claim

The Lanczos algorithm can be applied to the infinite-dimensional LQCD transfer matrix using recursive formulae whose inputs are only Euclidean correlation functions, providing faster ground-state convergence than the effective mass (power-iteration method) and more accurate energy estimates than multi-state fits to correlation functions with small imaginary times, while achieving comparable statistical precision. Two-sided error bounds guarantee that excited-state effects cannot shift Lanczos results far outside their statistical uncertainties, though the method requires a bootstrap generalization of the Cullum-Willoughby method to filter spurious eigenvalues arising from statistical noise.

## Method

The Lanczos algorithm is applied to the LQCD transfer matrix by constructing a tridiagonal matrix whose elements are obtained from Euclidean correlation functions through recursion relations for normalized states and matrix elements. The Lanczos vectors are defined by a three-term recurrence, and the matrix elements of the transfer matrix in this basis form a tridiagonal matrix whose eigenvalues (Ritz values) provide optimal Krylov-space approximations to transfer matrix eigenvalues. Spurious eigenvalues arising from statistical fluctuations are filtered using a bootstrap generalization of the Cullum-Willoughby method, where eigenvalues whose distance to eigenvalues of the reduced matrix falls below a threshold determined from bootstrap histograms are discarded, and bootstrap median estimators are used for the final energy estimate.

## Key numbers

- For the simple harmonic oscillator (SHO) with aM = 0.1 and beta/a = 100: Lanczos converges within uncertainties to the exact result aE0 ≈ 0.09996 after m=3 iterations (t/a=5), while effective mass reaches similar convergence for t/a ~ 10.
- SHO Lanczos result for full m_max = 49 iterations: E0^(m_max) = 0.0987(40), with two-sided residual-bound window [0.065(12), 0.128(10)].
- SHO multi-state fit weighted average: 0.1016(52), with Lanczos uncertainties approximately 30% smaller.
- LQCD proton mass with m_pi ≈ 170 MeV and a ≈ 0.091(1) fm: Lanczos gives E0^(m_max) = 0.429(20) for m_max = 47, with two-sided residual-bound window [0.350(31), 0.523(26)].
- High-statistics benchmark proton mass from NPLQCD with N_cfg = 670: aM_N^big = 0.4244(44), agreeing with Lanczos within 1 sigma.
- Two-state fits to proton correlation functions with t_min/a = 11 give 0.4383(86); weighted average of multi-state fits with t_min/a ≥ 2 gives 0.443(12), both 1.5-1.6 sigma larger than the benchmark.
- Lanczos variance approaches an O(10^-2) constant for t/a ≳ 20, in contrast to power-iteration/effective-mass results whose variance grows exponentially with Parisi-Lepage scaling ∝ e^{(M_N - 3/2 m_pi)t}.

## Context

This work extends the application of the Lanczos algorithm, widely used in computational linear algebra and quantum Monte Carlo (Caffarel 1991, Sorella 2001), to LQCD spectroscopy by showing it can be applied to infinite-dimensional transfer matrices using only Euclidean correlation functions. The method improves upon the effective mass (power-iteration method, Von Mises 1929) and generalized eigenvalue problem (GEVP) approaches (Fox 1981, Michael 1982, Luscher 1990, Blossier 2009) by providing two-sided error bounds rather than one-sided variational bounds, and by avoiding the exponential signal-to-noise problem predicted by Parisi (1983) and Lepage (1989). The approach is related to Prony's method (Fleming 2004, Lin 2007, Beane 2009) but provides additional theoretical guarantees through the Kaniel-Paige-Saad bound and residual bounds, and the variance analysis is inspired by Della Morte and Giusti (2007, 2008, 2010). This enables more reliable spectroscopy for nucleon, nuclear, and highly boosted systems where isolating ground states is challenging.
