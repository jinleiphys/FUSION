# Lattice Gauge Theory Sum Rule for the Shear Channel

> An exact expression is derived for the $(ω,p)=0$ thermal correlator of shear stress in SU($N_c$) lattice gauge theory. I remove a logarithmic divergence by taking a suitable linear combination of the shear correlator and the correlator of the energy density. The operator product expansion shows that the same linear combination has a finite limit when $ω\to\infty$. It follows that the vacuum-subtracted shear spectral function vanishes at large frequencies at least as fast as $α_s^2(ω)$ and obeys a sum rule. The trace anomaly makes a potential contribution to the spectral sum rule which remains to be fully calculated, but which I estimate to be numerically small for $T\gtrsim 3T_c$. By contrast with the bulk channel, the shear channel spectral density is then overall enhanced as compared to the spectral density in vacuo.

## Key claim

An exact sum rule for the shear channel spectral function in SU(\(N_c\)) lattice gauge theory is derived, but the trace anomaly contributes a term whose coefficient remains to be fully calculated; the author estimates this term to be numerically small (at most a 5% correction) for temperatures \(T \gtrsim 3T_c\).

## Method

The author derives a lattice sum rule for the zero-momentum Euclidean correlator of the shear stress operator \(\theta_{11}\) by applying a renormalization-group identity to an anisotropic lattice, then taking the isotropic limit. The resulting lattice expression is converted into a continuum spectral sum rule via a dispersion relation, and the operator product expansion (OPE) is used to show that the same linear combination of correlators that removes a logarithmic divergence also has a finite large-frequency limit. The finite part of the contact term from the energy-density correlator is extracted using known one-loop results for the anisotropy renormalization from Karsch (1982).

## Key numbers

* The leading-order OPE result for the shear-stress correlator at large \(\omega\) gives \(\int d^4x\, e^{i\omega x_0} \langle T_{12}(x)T_{12}(0)\rangle_{T-0} \sim -\frac{1}{3b_0 g_0^2}(e-3p) - \frac{1}{2}(e+p)\), where \(b_0 = 11N_c/(3(4\pi)^2)\) for SU(\(N_c\)).
* For SU(3) at \(T > 2.5T_c\), \((e-3p)/T^4 \lesssim 0.5\) while \(e/T^4 \approx 4.5\), leading to an estimated \(\lesssim 5\%\) correction from the trace anomaly term in the sum rule.
* The lightest tensor glueball mass is \(m_2/T_c \approx 7.9\) (or \(m_2 \approx 2.4\) GeV using the Sommer parameter \(r_0^{-1} = 0.41\) GeV), and its contribution to the shear sum rule at \(T = 3T_c\) is \(\approx 2\%\).
* In the free theory, the shear spectral function is \(\rho^{\text{free}}_{12,12}(\omega,T) = \frac{d_A}{10(4\pi)^2} \frac{\omega^4}{\tanh(\omega/4T)} + \left(\frac{2\pi}{15}\right)^2 d_A T^4 \omega \delta(\omega)\), with \(d_A = N_c^2 - 1\).

## Context

This work extends the sum-rule approach previously applied to the bulk channel by Meyer (2010) to the shear channel, and builds on a shear sum rule for conformal field theories proposed by Romatschke and Son (2009). The derived sum rule provides a global constraint on the shear spectral density that, when combined with Euclidean lattice correlators from Meyer (2007, 2008), can help determine the shear viscosity via the Kubo formula. At high temperatures, the sum rule receives an approximately 40% contribution from the transport peak, suggesting that determining the area under the transport peak from Euclidean data is realistic.
