# Limits on the neutron-antineutron oscillation time from the stability of nuclei

> We refute a recent claim by Nazaruk that the limits placed on the free--space neutron--antineutron oscillation time $τ_{n\bar n}$ can be improved by many orders of magnitude with respect to the estimate $τ_{n\bar n}>2(T_0/Γ)^{1/2}$, where $T_0$ is a measured limit on the annihilation lifetime of a nucleus and $Γ\sim 100$ MeV is a typical antineutron-nucleus annihilation width.

## Key claim
The paper refutes Nazaruk's claim that limits on the free-space neutron-antineutron oscillation time can be improved by 31 orders of magnitude from nuclear stability data. The authors show that Nazaruk's derivation contains a specific error, and that the correct limit remains of the same order as the standard estimate tau_{n nbar} > 2 (T_0 / Gamma)^{1/2}, where Gamma is about 100 MeV.

## Method
The authors re-derive the probability for neutron-antineutron oscillations in a nucleus using the S-matrix diagrammatic approach, correcting a missing factor of 2 in Nazaruk's expression for the antineutron-nuclear width factor. They then compare this corrected result with the optical potential model, showing that both approaches yield identical time evolution when the antineutron-nuclear dynamics is reduced to a width factor H = -i Gamma/2. A simple coupled Schrodinger equation derivation is also presented to illustrate the correct order-of-magnitude estimate.

## Key numbers
- The antineutron-nucleus annihilation width is Gamma ~ 100 MeV, corresponding to a characteristic annihilation time Gamma^{-1} ~ 10^{-23} seconds.
- The corrected nuclear annihilation lifetime T_0 is given by T_0 = (Gamma/4) tau_{n nbar}^2, leading to the limit tau_{n nbar} > 2 (T_0 / Gamma)^{1/2}.
- The neutron disappearance rate in the nucleus is gamma_< ~ 4 epsilon^2 / Gamma, where epsilon = 1 / tau_{n nbar}.

## Context
This work refutes the claim of Nazaruk (1994) that nuclear stability limits on neutron-antineutron oscillation could be improved by 31 orders of magnitude. It restores the standard order-of-magnitude estimate previously derived by Dover, Gal, and Richard (1983) and Alberico et al. (1982) using potential models. The paper enables continued use of the standard bound and notes that more sophisticated dynamical effects are unlikely to change the order of magnitude of the optical potential results.
