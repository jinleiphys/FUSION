# Atom in a $q$-Analog Harmonic Oscillator Trap

> We study the population inversion and Q-function of a two-level atom, interacting with single-mode laser light field, in a $q$-analog harmonic oscillator trap for increasing $q$. For $τ=.003(q=e^τ)$ the collapses and revivals of population inversion become well defined facilitating experimental observation but for large $τ\sim 0.1$ the time dependence of population inversion is completely wiped out.

## Key claim

For a small deformation parameter \(\tau = 0.003\) (where \(q = e^\tau\)), the collapses and revivals of population inversion in a two-level atom interacting with a single-mode laser field inside a \(q\)-analog harmonic oscillator trap become much more pronounced and well-defined than in the undeformed case, facilitating experimental observation; however, for large deformation \(\tau \sim 0.1\), the time dependence of the population inversion is completely wiped out.

## Method

The authors model a two-level atom with transition frequency \(\omega_a\) confined in a \(q\)-deformed harmonic oscillator trap, interacting with a classical single-mode travelling light field of frequency \(\omega_l\). The system Hamiltonian includes the \(q\)-analog oscillator energy, the atomic internal energy with detuning \(\Delta = \omega_a - \omega_l\), and the atom-light interaction term with Rabi frequency \(\Omega\) and an operator \(F = \exp[i\epsilon(a^\dagger + a)]\) where \(\epsilon\) depends on the recoil energy and trap quantum energy. The time-dependent Schrödinger equation is solved numerically for the probability amplitudes of the ground and excited atomic states coupled to the trap states, with the initial center-of-mass motion of the atom taken as a \(q\)-analog Glauber coherent state. The population inversion and the Q-function (quasi-probability distribution) are computed as functions of time for various values of the deformation parameter \(\tau\).

## Key numbers

- For \(\tau = 0.0\) (undeformed harmonic oscillator trap), the results of Blockley et al. are reproduced with mean number of trap quanta \(\overline{m} = 16\) and maximum trap state \(m = 32\).
- For \(\tau = 0.002\), the first revival peaks earlier than for \(\tau = 0.0\), and a well-defined collapse appears between the first and second revival.
- For \(\tau = 0.003\), the collapse and revival pattern becomes much more pronounced (well defined) compared to the undeformed case.
- For \(\tau = 0.004\) and \(0.006\), the pattern becomes diffuse after the first revival.
- For large deformation \(\tau \geq 0.01\), the time dependence of population inversion is considerably washed away; for \(\tau = 0.1\), no time dependence is seen.
- The effective Rabi frequency for the \(q\)-deformed oscillator trap, to first order in \(\epsilon\), is given by \(\mu(m) = \sqrt{ [ \frac{\omega}{2}[\cosh(2\tau(m+1)) + 1] + \Delta ]^2 + \Omega^2 \epsilon^2 [m+1]_q }\).
- The Q-function for \(\tau = 0.003\) shows as many as five peaks at \(t_1 = 30\) (in units of \(\Omega t / 2\pi\)), and up to eight distinct peaks at \(t = 15\), with a revival occurring when all peaks collide into a single peak at \(t_2 = 129.6\).

## Context

This work extends the study of collapses and revivals of population inversion in a two-level atom constrained in a harmonic oscillator trap by Blockley et al. (1992) to the case of a \(q\)-deformed harmonic oscillator trap, where the energy spacing between trap states is not constant but depends on the deformation parameter. The authors previously applied \(q\)-deformation to model nonlinearities in nuclear pairing (Sharma and Sharma, 1992, 1994). The results differ from those of Chai (1990), who studied the Jaynes-Cummings model with an intensity-dependent coupling using a \(q\)-analog oscillator for the radiation field and found collapses and revivals become more diffuse with increasing deformation; here, the explicit inclusion of the \(q\)-analog oscillator zero-order spectrum leads to well-defined patterns for small deformation. The work suggests that small nonlinearities in the trapping potential can be beneficial for experimental observation of collapses and revivals, while large nonlinearities wash out the pattern entirely.
