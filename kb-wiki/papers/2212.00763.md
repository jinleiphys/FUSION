# A Strong-QCD Regime Measurement of the Proton's Spin Structure

> The theory of the strong force, Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) remains one of the most important ways to understand the fundamental properties of ordinary matter. However, at low momentum transfer Q2, in the regime where the strong force becomes extremely strong, our understanding of QCD for ordinary nucleons becomes hazy. Several cutting edge theories such as Chiral Perturbation Theory and Lattice QCD have provided valuable predictions in this regime, but Lattice QCD has not yet extended predictions of many important quantities to this kinematic region, and Chiral Perturbation Theory has faced several important disagreements with experimental data for the neutron over the last several decades. It is therefore of extreme importance to have a benchmark of experimental data in the low energy regime for the proton's behavior, as a test of leading theories for the behavior of QCD in this regime. The E08-027 (g2p) experiment ran at Jefferson Lab in 2012 with the goal of collecting this valuable data. Full details of the g2p experiment and my experimental work at the University of New Hampshire are presented in this thesis, as well as a detailed description of the analysis process and the exciting benchmark results, which serve as a direct test of all current and future theories of QCD in the low-Q2 regime.

## Key claim

The E08-027 (g2p) experiment provides benchmark experimental data on the proton's spin structure at low momentum transfer (Q2) in the regime where Quantum Chromodynamics becomes non-perturbative, serving as a direct test of Chiral Perturbation Theory and Lattice QCD predictions. The results are presented as a thesis with the caveat that the lowest kinematic setting (2.5 T field, 1.1 GeV beam energy) was taken primarily for radiative corrections and is not used in the final results.

## Method

The experiment used inclusive electron scattering of polarized electrons off a spin-polarized solid ammonia (NH3) target in Jefferson Lab Hall A, with the target polarized via Dynamic Nuclear Polarization (DNP) at temperatures near 1 K. Scattered electrons were measured using the Hall A high resolution spectrometers (HRS) with septa magnets to achieve the low scattering angle of approximately 6 degrees, across six kinematic settings defined by two target magnetic fields (2.5 T and 5 T) and four beam energies (1.1, 1.7, 2.2, and 3.3 GeV), with five transverse and one longitudinal target field configuration.

## Key numbers

- Beam energies used: 1.1 GeV, 1.7 GeV, 2.2 GeV, and 3.3 GeV
- Target magnetic fields: 2.5 T and 5.0 T
- Beam current limited to 50 nA to prevent target depolarization
- Electron beam polarization measured between 79.89% and 89.72% across nine measurements, with systematic errors of 1.7%
- Target polarization for 5 T settings averaged between 60% and 80%
- Target polarization for 2.5 T settings was between 40% and 50%, later found to be underestimated by a factor of 2 due to incorrect calibration constant
- Target cup dimensions: 2.72 cm diameter and 2.83 cm length (1.3 cm length for 1.2 GeV runs)
- Beam raster pattern: 4 mm x 4 mm square from fast raster at 25 kHz, expanded to 2.5 cm circular pattern by slow raster at 99.412 Hz
- Chicane magnet configurations included beam entry angles from 4.09 degrees to 11.96 degrees depending on energy and field setting

## Context

This work extends experimental tests of Chiral Perturbation Theory and Lattice QCD predictions for nucleon spin structure into the low-Q2 regime where the strong coupling becomes large, addressing longstanding disagreements between Chiral Perturbation Theory and experimental data for the neutron. The results provide a benchmark for testing current and future theories of QCD in the non-perturbative regime, and the experimental techniques developed for low-current beam monitoring and target polarization at low energies enable future measurements in this kinematic region. The analysis builds on prior work by Zielinski (2017), Slifer, Chen, Badman, and Gu, and uses the Jefferson Lab CEBAF accelerator and Hall A equipment.
