# CLAS12 and its initial Science Program at the Jefferson Lab Upgrade

> An overview of the CLAS12 detector is presented and the initial physics program after the energy-doubling of the Jefferson Lab electron accelerator. Construction of the 12 GeV upgrade project has started October 2008. A broad program has been developed to map the nucleon's 3-dimensional spin and flavor content through the measurement of deeply exclusive and semi-inclusive processes. Other programs include forward distribution function to large $x_{B} \le 0.85$ and of the quark and gluon polarized distribution functions, as well as nucleon ground state and transition form factors at high $Q^2$. The 12 GeV electron beam and the large acceptance of CLAS12 are also well suited to explore hadronization properties using the nucleus as a laboratory

## Key claim

The CLAS12 detector and the 12 GeV Jefferson Lab upgrade will enable a comprehensive program to map the nucleon's three-dimensional spin and flavor content through deeply exclusive and semi-inclusive processes, but the extraction of generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and transverse momentum dependent distributions (TMDs) depends on establishing the dominance of the handbag mechanism at the upgrade energy, which must first be verified experimentally.

## Method

The paper describes the CLAS12 detector system, consisting of a Forward Detector (FD) covering scattering angles from 5° to 40° with a toroidal magnet, drift chambers, Cherenkov counters, time-of-flight scintillators, and electromagnetic calorimeters, plus a Central Detector (CD) with a 5 Tesla solenoid magnet, silicon vertex tracker, and central time-of-flight array. The planned physics program uses the 12 GeV continuous-wave electron beam to measure deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS), deeply virtual meson production, semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering (SIDIS), inclusive structure functions, elastic and transition form factors, and hadronization in nuclear targets.

## Key numbers

- Luminosity of 10^35 cm^-2 sec^-1, an order of magnitude increase over previous CLAS
- Forward detector covers polar angles from 5° to 40°; central detector covers angles from 35° to 135°
- Central solenoid magnetic field of 5 Tesla with uniformity ΔB/B < 10^-4 for polarized target operation
- High threshold Cherenkov counter (HTCC) pion momentum threshold of 4.9 GeV/c
- Low threshold Cherenkov counter (LTCC) provides pion identification for momenta greater than 3 GeV/c
- Time-of-flight resolution of 80 psec enables 4σ π/K separation up to 3 GeV/c and K/p separation up to 4.5 GeV/c
- Forward electromagnetic calorimeter with preshower (PCAL) provides factor 2.5 better spatial resolution for photon separation up to momenta greater than 10 GeV/c
- Lead-tungstate forward calorimeter with 420 crystals, 15mm x 15mm cross section, 18 radiation lengths thick, for photon and π0 identification up to 10 GeV/c
- Central detector particle identification range up to 1.2 GeV/c for pion-proton separation and 0.65 GeV/c for pion-kaon separation
- Projected measurement of F2^n/F2^p and d/u ratio to x values approaching 0.85
- Projected precision on Γ1^p moments up to Q^2 = 6 GeV^2 with statistical accuracy improved several-fold over existing world data
- Total point-to-point uncorrelated uncertainty on twist-4 term for Bjorken sum f2^(p-n) decreases by factor of 5-6 compared to results from Deur (2004)
- Projected extension of neutron magnetic form factor G_M^n measurements to higher Q^2
- Projected extension of N(1520)D13 resonance transition amplitude A_1/2 measurements to higher Q^2

## Context

This paper extends the nucleon structure program from the 6 GeV CLAS era (with results from experiments such as CLAS DVCS measurements, EG1 spin structure, and BONUS spectator-tagged measurements) to the 12 GeV energy regime. The work builds on the theoretical framework of GPDs developed by Ji (1996), Radyushkin (1996-1997), and others, and on TMD phenomenology, enabling three-dimensional imaging of the nucleon in transverse impact parameter space and extraction of gravitational form factors. The program enables next steps including separation of GPDs H, H̃, and E through combined beam, target, and transverse target asymmetries, determination of the d/u quark ratio at extreme x, and measurement of color transparency and hadronization times using nuclear targets.
