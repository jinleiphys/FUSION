# What can we learn from a second phi meson peak in ultrarelativistic nuclear collisions?

> The decay width of a phi meson is reduced from its vacuum value as its mass decreases in hot hadronic matter as a result of the partial restoration of chiral symmetry. This reduction is, however, cancelled by collisional broadening through the reactions $ϕπ\to KK^*$, $ϕK\toϕK$, $ϕρ\to KK$, and $ϕϕ\to KK$. The resulting phi meson width in hot hadronic matter is found to be less than about 10 MeV for temperatures below 200 MeV. If hadronic matter has a strong first-order phase transition, this narrow phi meson with reduced mass will appear as a second peak in the dilepton spectrum in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions. We discuss use of this second phi peak to determine the transition temperature and the lifetime of the two-phase coexistence region in the case of a strong first-order phase transition. We also discuss using the peak to determine the range of temperatures over which the transition occurs in the case of a smooth but fast change in the entropy density.

# What can we learn from a second phi meson peak in ultrarelativistic nuclear collisions?

## Key claim

The phi meson width in hot hadronic matter remains narrow (less than about 10 MeV for temperatures below 200 MeV) because collisional broadening from reactions such as phi-pion to K-K* cancels the reduction in decay width that would otherwise result from the phi mass dropping in a chirally restored environment; this narrow width makes a second phi peak in the dilepton spectrum a viable observable, but only if the hadronic matter undergoes a strong first-order phase transition or a sufficiently sharp crossover.

## Method

The authors calculated the collisional width of the phi meson in hot baryon-free hadronic matter by evaluating the thermally averaged scattering rates for four reactions: phi + pion -> K + K*, phi + K -> phi + K, phi + rho -> K + K, and phi + phi -> K + K. They used effective Lagrangians with coupling constants determined from measured decay widths (phi -> K K, K* -> K pi, rho -> pi pi) and included a monopole form factor with cutoff Lambda ~ 1.8 GeV at the vertices. The in-medium phi mass was taken from QCD sum-rule results by Asakawa and Ko (1993), and the kaon propagator was given an imaginary part corresponding to a kaon width of 10-30 MeV to regulate singularities when the phi mass exceeds twice the kaon mass.

## Key numbers

* The vacuum phi meson decay width is about 4 MeV; the measured width for phi -> K K is about 3.7 MeV.
* The total phi meson width in hot hadronic matter is less than 10 MeV for temperatures between 150 and 190 MeV when vertex form factors are included.
* The coupling constants used are g_phiKK^2/(4 pi) ~ 1.82, g_K*Kpi^2/(4 pi) ~ 0.86, and g_rhoKK^2/(4 pi) ~ 0.735.
* The kaon width in the medium is taken as 20 MeV for the central calculation, with values of 10 MeV and 30 MeV tested for sensitivity.
* The derivative of the phi mass with temperature is estimated as d m_phi/dT ~ -3 for T = 150-190 MeV.
* The transition temperature range delta T must be less than about 20-30 MeV for the second phi peak to be separable from nearby dilepton peaks.

## Context

This work extends the proposal by Asakawa and Ko (1993) that a double phi peak structure in dilepton spectra could signal a first-order quark-gluon plasma to hadronic matter transition, by explicitly calculating the phi meson width that was neglected in that earlier study. It contradicts earlier estimates by Lissauer and Shuryak (1991) and by Bi and Rafelski (1991) that the phi width would approximately double in hot matter, showing instead that the width remains small when the in-medium mass reduction is included. The results enable experimental searches for the second phi peak to extract the transition temperature, the lifetime of the mixed phase (for a first-order transition), and the temperature range of the transition (for a smooth but fast crossover), using methods analogous to those proposed by Seibert (1992) for rho meson dileptons but with advantages of less contamination and smaller flow distortion.
