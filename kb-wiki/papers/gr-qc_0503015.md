# Slowly rotating proto strange stars in Quark mass density- and temperature- dependent model

> Employing the Quark Mass Denisity- and temperature- dependent model and the Hartle's method, We have studied the slowly rotating strange star with uniform angular velocity. The mass-radius relation, the moment of inertia and the frame dragging for different frequencies are given. We found that we cannot use the strange star to solve the challenges of Stella and Vietri for the horizontal branch oscillations and the moment of inertia $I_{45}/(M/M_ s)>2.3$. Furthermore, we extended the Hartle's method to study the differential rotating strange star and found that the differential rotation is an effective way to get massive strange star.

## Key claim

Using the Quark Mass Density- and Temperature-Dependent (QMDTD) model and Hartle's method, the authors find that a uniformly rotating strange star cannot satisfy the condition \(I_{45}/(M/M_\odot) > 2.3\) required by the Stella and Vietri model for horizontal-branch oscillations, unless the temperature exceeds about 120 MeV, which challenges the viability of strange stars as an explanation for those observations.

## Method

The authors employed the QMDTD model, where quark masses depend on baryon number density and temperature via a temperature-dependent bag constant \(B(T) = B_0[1 - (T/T_c)^2]\), with \(T_c = 170\) MeV. They used Hartle's perturbative method to solve the Einstein equations for slowly rotating stars (angular velocity much smaller than the Keplerian limit), computing the mass-radius relation, moment of inertia, and frame dragging for both uniform rotation and differential rotation with an exponential radial profile \(\Omega(r) = \Omega_c e^{-\beta r}\).

## Key numbers

- Keplerian frequency for the model is \(\Omega_k \approx 1.5 \times 10^4\) Hz, much larger than the rotation frequencies used in the calculations.
- The increase of maximum mass scales as \(\delta M_{\max} / M_\odot \propto \Omega^2\), consistent with Hartle's perturbative approach.
- For uniform rotation at \(\Omega = 200\) Hz, the moment of inertia \(I_{45}\) increases with temperature (shown for \(T = 0, 20, 50, 80\) MeV).
- The ratio \(I_{45}/(M/M_\odot) > 2.3\) requires a temperature over 120 MeV, making the strange star explanation for the five bright sources (GX 17+2, Cyg X-2, GX 5-1, Sco X-1, GX 340+0) problematic.
- For differential rotation with surface angular velocity \(\Omega_e = 10346\) Hz, the maximum mass increase \(\delta M_{\max}/M_{\max}\) depends strongly on the ratio \(\Omega_c/\Omega_e\) and temperature.

## Context

This work extends the non-rotating proto strange star study of Gupta et al. (2004) to slowly rotating configurations using the QMDTD model, which itself improves upon the earlier Quark Mass Density-Dependent (QMDD) model by incorporating temperature-dependent quark confinement. The authors show that the challenge posed by Stella and Vietri (1999) and Psaltis et al. (1999) for neutron stars, that the moment of inertia condition \(I_{45}/(M/M_\odot) > 2.3\) cannot be met, also applies to strange stars, ruling out both neutron star and strange star interpretations for those five sources. The extension to differential rotation demonstrates a mechanism to produce more massive strange stars, which could be relevant for understanding newly born, hot proto strange stars.
