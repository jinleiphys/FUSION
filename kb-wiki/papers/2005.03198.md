# Quantifying Uncertainties on Fission Fragment Mass Yields With Mixture Density Networks

> Probabilistic machine learning techniques can learn both complex relations between input features and output quantities of interest as well as take into account stochasticity or uncertainty within a data set. In this initial work, we explore the use of one such probabilistic network, the Mixture Density Network (MDN), to reproduce fission yields and their uncertainties. We study mass yields for the spontaneous fission of $^{252}$Cf, exploring the number of training samples needed for converged predictions, how different levels of uncertainty propagate from the training set to the MDN predictions, and how well physical constraints of the yields - such as normalization and symmetry - are upheld by the algorithm. Finally, we test the ability of the MDN to interpolate between and extrapolate beyond samples in the training set using energy-dependent mass yields for the neutron-induced fission on $^{235}$U. The MDN provides a reliable way to include and predict uncertainties and is a promising path forward for supplementing sparse sets of nuclear data.

## Key claim

The Mixture Density Network (MDN) can reliably reproduce fission fragment mass yields and propagate uncertainties from training data to predictions, but the method shows limitations when training set uncertainties are very small (1%) and does not automatically enforce physical constraints like normalization and symmetry without explicit feature engineering.

## Method

The authors implemented a Mixture Density Network in PyTorch that maps input features (fragment mass, and optionally incident neutron energy) to a probabilistic output described as a sum of Gaussian distributions, with weights, means, and standard deviations determined by a feed-forward neural network. For spontaneous fission of ²⁵²Cf, training sets were constructed by sampling from Gaussian distributions around either a three-Gaussian parameterization (with uncertainties from 1% to 20%) or four experimental data sets, using 50 samples per mass value. For energy-dependent studies on ²³⁵U(n,f), the MDN was trained on mass yields at specific incident neutron energies and tested on interpolation and extrapolation to other energies.

## Key numbers

- Training set convergence was achieved with at least 50 samples per mass value for uncertainties of 5% to 20%; the ratio ε_MDN/ε_training flattened above this threshold.
- For 1% uncertainty on the training set, the MDN average uncertainty was 7% higher than the training set uncertainty; for uncertainties of 5% and above, the discrepancy dropped below 2%.
- The normalization of MDN predictions for ²⁵²Cf(sf) was within about 1.5% of the expected value of 2.0, compared to 0.5% for the training set.
- When symmetry constraints were built into the training set, the mean ratio Y(A_L)/Y(A_H) improved from 0.98 to 1.00, and the deviation from unity decreased from 0.06 to 0.02.

## Context

This work extends the application of probabilistic machine learning to nuclear data, specifically fission fragment yields, where prior approaches have used Gaussian processes (Neufcourt 2018, 2019) and Bayesian Neural Networks (Utama 2016, Wang 2019) for uncertainty quantification. The MDN offers advantages over Gaussian processes (which return to mean values outside training regions and cannot reliably extrapolate) and Bayesian Neural Networks (which are computationally demanding). The study enables future work on supplementing sparse nuclear data sets, particularly for neutron-induced fission at higher incident energies where experimental measurements are scarce, and suggests that incorporating covariance matrices and physical constraints directly into training sets could further improve predictions.
