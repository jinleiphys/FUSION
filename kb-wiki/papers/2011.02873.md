# Superconducting phases in a two-component microscale model of neutron star cores

> We identify the possible ground states for a mixture of two superfluid condensates (one neutral, the other electrically charged) using a phenomenological Ginzburg-Landau model. While this framework is applicable to any interacting condensed-matter mixture of a charged and a neutral component, we focus on nuclear matter in neutron star cores, where proton and neutron condensates are coupled via non-dissipative entrainment. We employ the Skyrme interaction to determine the neutron star's equilibrium composition, and hence obtain realistic coefficients for our Ginzburg-Landau model at each depth within the star's core. We then use the Ginzburg-Landau model to determine the ground state in the presence of a magnetic field. In this way, we obtain superconducting phase diagrams for six representative Skyrme models, revealing the microphysical magnetic flux distribution throughout the neutron star core. The phase diagrams are rather complex and the locations of most of the phase transitions can only be determined through numerical calculations. Nonetheless, we find that for all equations of state considered in this work, much of the outer core exhibits type-1.5 superconductivity, rather than type-II superconductivity as is generally assumed. For local magnetic field strengths $\lesssim 10^{14} \, {\rm G}$, the magnetic flux is distributed inhomogeneously, with bundles of magnetic fluxtubes separated by flux-free Meissner regions. We provide an approximate criterion to determine the transition between this type-1.5 phase and the type-I region in the inner core.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for all six Skyrme equations of state considered, much of the outer neutron star core exhibits type-1.5 superconductivity rather than the generally assumed type-II superconductivity, but this conclusion is hedged by the fact that the locations of most phase transitions can only be determined through numerical calculations and the phase diagrams are rather complex.

## Method

The authors constructed a phenomenological Ginzburg-Landau model for two coupled superfluid condensates (neutral neutrons and charged protons) that includes entrainment and density-gradient coupling terms consistent with Galilean invariance. They used the Skyrme interaction to determine the neutron star's equilibrium composition and obtained realistic coefficients for the Ginzburg-Landau model at each depth within the star's core, then minimized the free energy to determine the ground state in the presence of a magnetic field for six representative Skyrme models.

## Key numbers

- Local magnetic field strengths less than approximately 10^14 G produce inhomogeneous magnetic flux distributions with bundles of fluxtubes separated by flux-free Meissner regions
- Mass densities in neutron star cores range from approximately 10^14 g/cm^3 at the crust-core interface to approximately 10^15 g/cm^3 at the star's center
- Baryon number densities in the core range from approximately 0.06 to 0.6 fm^-3
- Saturation density is approximately 2.82 x 10^14 g/cm^3, corresponding to n_0 approximately 0.17 fm^-3
- Proton energy gaps reach maxima of approximately 0.4 to 1 MeV in the range k_Fp approximately 0.4 to 0.7 fm^-1
- Neutron energy gaps reach maxima of approximately 0.1 to 0.6 MeV at k_Fn approximately 1.4 to 2.5 fm^-1
- The CCDK proton gap fit uses Delta_0 = 102.0 MeV, k_1 = 0.0 fm^-1, k_2 = 9.0 fm^-2, k_3 = 1.3 fm^-1, k_4 = 1.5 fm^-2
- The TToa neutron gap fit uses Delta_0 = 2.1 MeV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Baym, Pethick, and Piker (1969) who first established the conventional picture of neutron star superconductivity, and improves upon the Ginzburg-Landau models of Alford and Good (2008) and Haber and Schmitt (2017) by including a consistent treatment of entrainment that satisfies Galilean invariance. The results challenge the standard assumption of type-II superconductivity in the outer core and suggest that type-1.5 behavior, previously observed in terrestrial experiments with MgB2, may dominate in neutron star cores, which has implications for magnetic field dynamics and rotational evolution through fluxtube-neutron vortex interactions.
