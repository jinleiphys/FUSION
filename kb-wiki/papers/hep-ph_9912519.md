# Color superconductivity and its electromagnetic manifestation

> A collection of the physical observables, related to the electromagnetic properties of a nucleon, to investigate the non--perturbative quantum fluctuations in the strong interaction vacuum state under the influence of at least one close by (in energy density) color superconducting phase found in several QCD motivated model calculations, are studied. It is shown that the spontaneous breaking of the electromagnetic gauge symmetry in the color superconducting phase of strong interaction can result in relatively clean signals in high energy processes, especially in the semi-leptonic deep inelastic scattering ones, due to a kind of electromagnetic induced strong interaction. A new type of mechanism, which is a generalization of the Higgs one, through which the local electromagnetic gauge symmetry is spontaneously broken by a spontaneous breaking of the global baryon (nucleon) number conservation, is revealed. A model independent assessment of the question of how far is the color superconducting phase of the strong interaction from its vacuum phase is made by studying currently available experimental data on the electromagnetic responses of a nucleon at high energies. It is shown that based on our current knowledge about a nucleon, it is quite likely that there is at least one color superconducting phase for the strong interaction that is close enough to the vacuum state so that its effects can even be seen in high energy processes besides heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a color superconducting phase of the strong interaction vacuum may be close enough in energy density to the normal vacuum state that its effects can be observed in high-energy semi-leptonic deep inelastic scattering processes, not just in heavy ion collisions, through a mechanism where the electromagnetic gauge symmetry is partially broken by spontaneous breaking of global baryon number conservation.

## Method

The author develops a theoretical framework for spontaneous partial breaking of the local electromagnetic U(1) gauge symmetry in a multi-charge system, generalizing the Higgs mechanism to cases where only certain components of the electric charge (such as baryon number contributions) are spontaneously broken. The formalism is applied to analyze how such symmetry breaking would modify the photon propagator and fermion-fermion scattering amplitudes, producing an effective massive vector boson with mass determined by the order parameter of the symmetry-breaking phase. The study then examines how these modifications would manifest in the electromagnetic responses of a nucleon, particularly in the small-x region of deep inelastic scattering structure functions.

## Key numbers

- The soft Pomeron intercept is given as approximately α_P ≈ 1.0, while the hard Pomeron intercept needed to reproduce small-x data is approximately α'_P ≈ 1.4
- The photon acquires an effective mass m_γ^2 = 4πα_em χ A, where α_em is the fine structure constant, χ is the order parameter, and A is defined by a loop diagram
- The transition region in photon energy where the hard Pomeron becomes relevant is of order 1 GeV
- The Goldstone boson-fermion coupling constant is given by g = √(χ/A), which is of order 1 due to strong interaction effects

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by the same author (Ying, 1999) on the electromagnetic properties of a "superconducting nucleon" and builds on model calculations of color superconductivity using four-fermion interaction models (cspc1, cspc2, scalar-spc, cspc3, cspc4 from the early 1990s) and instanton-motivated models (Diak, Wilczek, Shuryak). The paper aims to provide a model-independent assessment of whether a color superconducting phase exists close to the normal vacuum phase, using experimental data on nucleon electromagnetic responses. It addresses anomalies in nucleon structure functions at small Bjorken x, including the rapid rise of F2 observed by H1, ZEUS, and E665 collaborations, the violation of the Gottfried sum rule, and discrepancies between charged lepton and neutrino DIS measurements, offering a complementary perspective to perturbative QCD explanations based on Regge theory.
