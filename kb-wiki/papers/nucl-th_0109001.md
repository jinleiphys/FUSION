# Analytical solution of the dynamical spherical MIT bag

> We prove that when the bag surface is allowed to move radially, the equations of motion derived from the MIT bag Lagrangian with massless quarks and a spherical boundary admit only one solution, which corresponds to a bag expanding at the speed of light. This result implies that some new physics ingredients, such as coupling to meson fields, are needed to make the dynamical bag a consistent model of hadrons.

## Key claim
The authors prove that the MIT bag model with a radially moving spherical boundary and massless quarks admits only one solution: a bag expanding at exactly the speed of light. This result implies that the dynamical MIT bag model, without additional physics such as coupling to meson fields, cannot produce physically meaningful hadron states.

## Method
The authors start from the MIT bag Lagrangian with massless fermions and a spherical boundary, derive the Dirac equation inside the bag and the linear boundary condition on the moving surface, and show that for a non-static boundary the boundary condition forces the fermion field to vanish at the surface. They then solve the resulting spherical Dirac equations analytically, obtaining the general solution for the radial functions, and impose the vanishing boundary condition to derive constraints that force the bag wall velocity to equal the speed of light.

## Key numbers
- The only allowed bag wall velocity is \(\dot{R}(t) = 1\) (in units where \(c=1\)), i.e., expansion at the speed of light.
- After a time \(t = R_0 + t_0\), the solution describes an expanding spherical shell with inner radius \(R_{\text{in}} = t - t_0 - R_0\) and outer radius \(R_{\text{out}} = t - t_0 + R_0\).
- The bag radius evolves as \(R(t) = R_0 + t - t_0\) for \(t \geq t_0\).

## Context
This work extends earlier attempts to allow a dynamical bag boundary, such as those by Rebbi (1975), Kuti (1976), Nogami (1984), Brown (1986), and Meissner (1987), which focused on reproducing nucleon parity ordering but employed approximations. The authors show that the exact, unapproximated dynamical MIT bag model fails to produce any confined, non-trivial solution, contradicting the expectation that a moving boundary could yield stable hadron states. This result motivates the development of more complete models, such as the dynamical chiral bag model with meson coupling, which the authors indicate will be presented in a forthcoming work (Colanero and Chu, 2001).
