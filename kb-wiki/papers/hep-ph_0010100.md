# When can long-range charge fluctuations serve as a QGP signal?

> We critically discuss recent suggestion to use long-range modes of charge (electric or baryon) fluctuations as a signal for the presence of Quark-Gluon Plasma at the early stages of a heavy ion collision. We evaluate the rate of diffusion in rapidity for different secondaries, and argue that for conditions of the SPS experiments, it is strong enough to relax the magnitude of those fluctuations almost to its equilibrium values, given by hadronic ``resonance gas''. We further argue that experimental data from SPS agree with this conclusion. We evaluate the detector acceptance needed to measure such ``primordial'' long-range fluctuations at RHIC conditions. We conclude with an application of the charge fluctuation analysis to the search for the QCD critical point.

# When can long-range charge fluctuations serve as a QGP signal?

## Key claim

Long-range charge fluctuations can only serve as a signal for Quark-Gluon Plasma if the rapidity acceptance window is sufficiently wide to counter the relaxation of those fluctuations by diffusion in the hadronic gas phase; for SPS conditions with a typical acceptance of Δy=2, diffusion is strong enough to relax the fluctuations nearly to their equilibrium hadronic resonance gas values, making the QGP signal unobservable there.

## Method

The authors model the time evolution of conserved net charge density fluctuations in rapidity space using a stochastic diffusion (Langevin) equation with a noise term whose correlation is fixed by equilibrium thermodynamics. They solve the corresponding Fokker-Planck equation in Fourier space to obtain the time-dependent variance of each harmonic, then compute the event-by-event fluctuation of total charge in a rapidity window as a function of the diffusion distance Δy_diff. The diffusion coefficient is estimated from hadronic cascade codes (RQMD and UrQMD) for AuAu collisions, using the mean rapidity change per collision and the number of rescatterings per particle for pions, nucleons, and kaons.

## Key numbers

- Mean rapidity change per collision for pions: δy_coll^π ≈ 0.8; for nucleons: δy_coll^N ≈ 0.2
- At RHIC (100+100 GeV AuAu): Δy_diff^π ≈ 2.2, Δy_diff^N ≈ 0.9
- At SPS: Δy_diff^π ≈ 1.8, Δy_diff^N ≈ 0.77
- For SPS with Δy=2, the fraction F of equilibrium fluctuation reached is 0.70 for pions and 0.42 for nucleons
- Starting from a factor 1/3 suppression of electric charge fluctuations at QGP-hadron transition, the surviving suppression at SPS freeze-out is only 20% (ratio 0.80 of equilibrium value); for baryon number the surviving suppression gives ratio 0.62
- For RHIC with Δy=4, the primordial factor 1/3 suppression of electric charge fluctuations survives as a factor 0.70 suppression relative to equilibrium
- Equilibrium charge fluctuation in hadronic resonance gas: ⟨(ΔQ)^2⟩/⟨N_ch⟩ ≈ 0.7
- Estimated contribution of σ decays near the critical point: reduction of π+/π- fluctuations by about 20%

## Context

This paper critically evaluates the proposal by Asakawa, Heinz and Muller (1999) and Jeon and Koch (1999) that equilibrium charge fluctuations in QGP, which are factor 2-3 smaller than in hadron gas, could survive as a signal due to kinetic slowdown of fluctuation relaxation for long-range harmonics. The authors show that for SPS conditions, diffusion in the hadronic phase is too strong, consistent with the resonance gas model of Koch (1999) and the experimental data from NA49. The work extends the fluctuation analysis framework of Shuryak (1999) and provides quantitative criteria for detector acceptance needed at RHIC to observe primordial fluctuations. The paper also proposes a new signature for the QCD critical point E, based on π+/π- fluctuations from σ decays, building on the critical point phenomenology of Stephanov, Rajagopal and Shuryak (1998).
