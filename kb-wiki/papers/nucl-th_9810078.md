# Photon-Photon and Photon-Hadron Interactions at Relativistic Heavy Ion Colliders

> In central collisions at relativistic heavy ion colliders like the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider RHIC/Brookhaven and the Large Hadron Collider LHC (in its heavy ion mode) at CERN/Geneva, one aims at detecting a new form of hadronic matter --- the Quark Gluon Plasma. We discuss here a complementary aspect of these collisions, the very peripheral ones. Due to coherence, there are strong electromagnetic fields of short duration in such collisions. They give rise to photon-photon and photon-nucleus collisions with high flux up to an invariant mass region hitherto unexplored experimentally. After a general survey photon-photon luminosities in relativistic heavy ion collisions are discussed. Then photon-photon physics at various gamma-gamma-invariant mass scales is discussed. The region of several GeV, relevant for RHIC is dominated by QCD phenomena (meson and vector meson pair production). Invariant masses of up to about 100 GeV can be reached at LHC, and the potential for new physics is discussed. Lepton-pair production, especially electron-positron pair production is copious. Due to the strong fields there will be new phenomena, especially multiple e+e- pair production.

# Photon-Photon and Photon-Hadron Interactions at Relativistic Heavy Ion Colliders

## Key claim

Peripheral collisions at RHIC and LHC provide strong, coherent electromagnetic fields that enable photon-photon and photon-nucleus interactions at invariant masses up to about 100 GeV at LHC, but the practical utility of these collisions for clean physics signals depends on avoiding strong-interaction backgrounds from grazing collisions and on the challenge of overlapping events at high luminosities.

## Method

The authors use the equivalent photon (Weizsacker-Williams) approximation to compute impact-parameter-dependent photon spectra from relativistic heavy ions, folding them to obtain photon-photon luminosities for various ion species (Pb-Pb, Ca-Ca, p-p) at RHIC and LHC. They then factorize the production cross section for a final state f into a luminosity function and the elementary cross section for real photon-photon fusion, applying this to meson spectroscopy, vector meson pair production, lepton pair production, and searches for new physics including Higgs bosons and supersymmetric particles.

## Key numbers

- Maximum photon energy at RHIC: approximately several GeV, corresponding to Lorentz factor gamma ≈ 100
- Maximum photon energy at LHC: up to about 100 GeV, corresponding to Lorentz factor gamma ≈ 3000 (for Pb-Pb, gamma = 2950)
- Effective gamma-gamma luminosity comparison: LEP200 (E_el = 100 GeV, L = 10^32 cm^-2 s^-1), Pb-Pb at LHC (gamma = 2950, L = 10^26 cm^-2 s^-1), Ca-Ca at LHC (gamma = 3750, L = 4 × 10^30 cm^-2 s^-1), p-p at LHC (gamma = 7450, L = 10^30 cm^-2 s^-1)
- Cross section for giant dipole resonance excitation: of the order of 100 barns for heavy systems, scaling approximately with Z^(10/3)
- Invariant mass range for total hadronic cross section in photon-photon collisions measured by L3: 5 GeV < W_GG < 75 GeV
- For a light Higgs boson of mass 10 GeV in a two-Higgs-doublet model, the two-photon width is about 0.1 keV
- At LHC pp collisions with L = 10^34 cm^-2 s^-1, about 16 minimum bias events per bunch crossing are expected; clean elastic events are detectable at luminosities below 10^33 cm^-2 s^-1

## Context

This work extends the study of photon-photon collisions from e+e- colliders (reviewed by Budnev et al., 1975) to relativistic heavy ion colliders, building on earlier applications of the equivalent photon method in nuclear physics (Bertulani and Baur, 1988), nuclear astrophysics (Baur and Rebel, 1994, 1996), and the Primakoff effect (Primakoff, 1951). The paper provides a survey for planned experiments at RHIC (Klein and Nystrand, 1997, 1998) and LHC (FELIX collaboration, 1997; Hencken et al., 1996) and enables future searches for standard model Higgs bosons, supersymmetric particles, and glueballs via two-photon production, while noting that the strong interaction in grazing collisions and overlapping events at high luminosity remain difficult to quantify.
