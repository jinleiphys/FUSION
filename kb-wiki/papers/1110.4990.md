# Analyzing the contribution of individual resonance poles of the S-matrix to the two-channel scattering

> A two-channel problem is considered within a method based on first order differential equations that are equivalent to the corresponding Schrödinger equation but are more convenient for dealing with resonant phenomena. Using these equations, it is possible to directly calculate the Jost matrix for practically any complex value of the energy. The spectral points (bound and resonant states) can therefore be located in a rigorous way, namely, as zeros of the Jost matrix determinant. When calculating the Jost matrix, the differential equations are solved and thus, at the same time, the wave function is obtained with the correct asymptotic behavior that is embedded in the solution analytically. The method offers very accurate way of calculating not only total widths of resonances but their partial widths as well. For each pole of the S-matrix, its residue can be calculated rather accurately, which makes it possible to obtain the Mittag-Leffler type expansion of the S-matrix as a sum of the singular terms (representing the resonances) and the background term (contour integral). As an example, the two-channel model by Noro and Taylor is considered. It is demonstrated how the contributions of individual resonance poles to the scattering cross section can be analyzed using the Mittag-Leffler expansion and the Argand plot technique. This example shows that even poles situated far away from the physical real axis may give significant contributions to the cross section.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that even resonance poles located far from the physical real axis in the complex energy plane can contribute significantly to the scattering cross section, contrary to the common assumption that only narrow (long-lived) resonances near the real axis matter. The authors use a mathematically rigorous definition of a resonance as a zero of the Jost matrix determinant, which includes both narrow and broad states without requiring observable cross-section irregularities.

## Method

The authors transform the two-channel Schrodinger equation into a set of first-order differential equations for the Jost matrices, which are solved numerically from the origin to a large distance where the potential vanishes. This yields the Jost matrices and the S-matrix for complex energies, allowing precise location of spectral points as zeros of the Jost matrix determinant. The residues of the S-matrix at resonance poles are then calculated to construct a Mittag-Leffler expansion, decomposing the S-matrix into singular terms (resonances) and a background contour integral, with the contributions of individual poles analyzed using Argand plots.

## Context

This work extends the Mittag-Leffler expansion approach previously developed by Elander and coworkers (Rittby et al., 1984; Krylninni, 1989; Alferova et al., 2002, 2004) for analyzing resonance contributions to scattering. It provides a numerically accurate alternative to the complex variational Kohn method (Nuttal and Cohen, 1969; Rescigno and McCurdy, 1985) and the Siegert pseudo-state method (TON, 1998), which have convergence and completeness issues. The method enables detailed analysis of how individual resonance poles influence cross sections in multi-channel systems, which is relevant to understanding barrier states in chemical reactions (Friedman and Truhlar, 1991; Truhlar and Garrett, 1996) and charge-transfer reactions in ion-atom collisions.
