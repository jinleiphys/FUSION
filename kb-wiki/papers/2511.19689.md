# Relaxation of a single-particle excitation in a Fermi system within the diffusion approximation of kinetic theory

> The time evolution of the Wigner distribution function for a single-particle excitation in a Fermi system was studied within the framework of the diffusion approximation of kinetic theory by numerically solving a nonlinear diffusion equation with constant kinetic coefficients. A method was proposed to separate the dissipative processes into contributions from the relaxation of the single-particle excitation and from the relaxation of the nuclear core, with a distinct relaxation time introduced for each process. The influence of the diffusion and drift coefficients on the characteristic relaxation time scale was analyzed. It was found that the resulting relaxation times exhibit a discrepancy relative to the kinetic coefficient estimates known from previous studies.

# Relaxation of a single-particle excitation in a Fermi system within the diffusion approximation of kinetic theory

## Key claim

The relaxation time of a single-particle excitation in a Fermi system, calculated within the diffusion approximation using constant kinetic coefficients, is of order 10⁻²⁴ s, approximately one order of magnitude shorter than the typical two-particle relaxation time in nuclei (10⁻²³–10⁻²² s), and this discrepancy persists even after separating the single-particle contribution from the core relaxation, suggesting that the kinetic coefficient values themselves require refinement rather than the method being the primary source of disagreement.

## Method

The time evolution of the Wigner distribution function for a single-particle excitation in a Fermi system was studied by numerically solving a nonlinear diffusion equation with constant diffusion and drift coefficients, derived from the diffusion approximation to the Landau–Vlasov kinetic equation. The initial distribution was decomposed into a steplike core component and a single-particle peak, and the relaxation times for the total system, the core, and the isolated single-particle excitation were each computed by integrating the normalized root-mean-square deviation of the distribution function over time. The calculations used constant kinetic coefficients based on previous estimates at the Fermi surface, with the drift coefficient renormalized by a factor of 3 to yield an equilibrium temperature of 4 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Diffusion coefficient at the Fermi surface: \( D_{p}^{(0)}(p_\text{F}) \approx 3.38 \times 10^{-22} \) MeV² fm⁻² s
- Drift coefficient at the Fermi surface: \( K_{p}^{(0)}(p_\text{F}) \approx -2.76 \times 10^{-23} \) MeV fm⁻² s
- Equilibrium temperature from the ratio of kinetic coefficients: \( T_\text{eq} \approx 12 \) MeV (using unrenormalized coefficients); calculations instead used \( T_\text{eq} = 4 \) MeV with drift coefficient renormalized by factor 3
- Relaxation time of the steplike core distribution: \( \tau_{0} \approx 8.9 \times 10^{-24} \) s
- Equilibrium relaxation time from Wolschin (1982): \( \tau_\text{equ} = 3.05 \times 10^{-23} \) s (approximately three times larger than \( \tau_0 \))
- Typical two-particle relaxation time in nuclei: \( \tau_\text{coll} \approx 10^{-23} \)–\( 10^{-22} \) s
- Fermi energy for steplike distribution: \( E_\text{F} \approx 37.1 \) MeV; equilibrium Fermi energy: \( E_{\text{F,eq}} = 36.7 \) MeV
- Nuclear matter density: \( \rho = 0.16 \) fm⁻³
- Critical temperature of nuclear matter: \( T_\text{C} = 18 \) MeV (Ravenhall et al., 1983)
- With a 20-fold reduction of kinetic coefficients (\( y = 20 \)), total relaxation time \( \tau \) reaches approximately \( 10^{-22} \) s; with 200-fold reduction, single-particle relaxation time \( \tau_1 \) reaches order \( 5 \times 10^{-23} \) s

## Context

This work extends the diffusion approximation approach to kinetic theory previously developed by Kolomietz and Lukyanov (2014, 2015), who obtained explicit expressions for diffusion and drift coefficients within the low-temperature approximation using a Gaussian interparticle potential. The relaxation time definition follows the phenomenological method of Lukyanov (2025), where the relaxation time is defined as the area under the equivalent exponential time dependence of the normalized root-mean-square deviation. The results contradict the analytical asymptotic solution of Wolschin (1982), who obtained \( \tau_\text{equ} = 4D/v^2 \) as the \( t \to \infty \) limit, and also disagree with the typical two-particle relaxation times for giant multipole resonances reported by Wegmann (1974), Bertsch (1978), Kolomietz (1982), and Shevchenko and Kolomietz (2004). The discrepancy between the effective relaxation time and the asymptotic equilibrium time is attributed to the nonexponential behavior at early times and to the need for refined kinetic coefficient values, which the author suggests may require reexamination of the assumptions underlying the diffusion approximation and possible quantum effects.
