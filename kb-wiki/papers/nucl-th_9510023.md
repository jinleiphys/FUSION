# The Non-Local Nature of the Nuclear Force and its Impact on Nuclear Structure

> We calculate the triton binding energy with a non-local NN potential that fits the world NN data below 350 MeV with the almost perfect $χ^2$/datum of 1.03. The non-locality is derived from relativistic meson field theory. The result obtained in a 34-channel, charge-dependent Faddeev calculation is 8.00 MeV, which is 0.4 MeV above the predictions by local NN potentials. The increase in binding energy can be clearly attributed to the off-shell behavior of the non-local potential. Our result cuts in half the discrepancy between theory and experiment established from local NN potentials. Implications for other areas of microscopic nuclear structure, in which underbinding is a traditional problem, are discussed.

## Key claim

A non-local nucleon-nucleon (NN) potential derived from relativistic meson field theory predicts 0.4 MeV more triton binding energy than local NN potentials, cutting in half the 0.86 MeV discrepancy between local-potential predictions and the experimental value of 8.48 MeV. The authors attribute this increase specifically to the off-shell behavior of the non-local potential, but note that this represents only the most "primitive" source of non-locality, with short-range hadron structure effects potentially providing much larger contributions.

## Method

The authors constructed a relativistic one-boson-exchange (OBE) potential (CD-Bonn) using invariant Feynman amplitudes from pseudoscalar, scalar, and vector meson-nucleon Lagrangians, including square-root factors that cast the relativistic Blankenbecler-Sugar equation into Lippmann-Schwinger form. They fitted the 4301 proton-proton and neutron-proton data below 350 MeV laboratory energy with a chi-squared per datum of 1.03, adjusting the fictitious sigma boson individually in each partial wave to achieve this precision. The triton binding energy was then computed in a 34-channel, charge-dependent Faddeev calculation using both non-relativistic and relativistic three-body formalisms.

## Key numbers

- Triton binding energy from non-relativistic Faddeev calculation with CD-Bonn potential: 8.00 MeV, which is 0.38 MeV above the local potential predictions of 7.62 ± 0.01 MeV (Friar et al., 1993)
- Experimental triton binding energy: 8.48 MeV; the gap between local potential predictions and experiment is 0.86 MeV, of which the non-locality explains 44%
- Relativistic Faddeev calculation with CD-Bonn yields 8.19 MeV triton binding
- Deuteron D-state probability: 4.83% for CD-Bonn versus approximately 5.7% for local potentials (Nijm-II, Reid'93, V18)
- Chi-squared per datum for CD-Bonn: 1.03, matching Nijm-II and Reid'93, compared to 1.09 for Argonne V18
- Half off-shell tensor force shown at on-shell momentum q' = 153 MeV (equivalent to 50 MeV laboratory energy), with off-shell momentum ranging from 0 to 1400 MeV

## Context

This work extends the program of constructing high-precision NN potentials that reproduce scattering data with chi-squared per datum near unity, following the Nijmegen phase-shift analysis (Stoks et al., 1993) and the construction of local potentials by the Nijmegen group (Stoks et al., 1994) and Argonne group (Wiringa et al., 1995). The key advance is that previous phase-equivalent potential studies (Haftel and Tabakin, 1970; Coester et al., 1970) either used potentials not exactly identical on-shell or constructed off-shell behavior by mathematical methods without physical basis. The CD-Bonn potential enables systematic investigation of off-shell effects in nuclear structure, with implications for nuclear matter and finite nuclei where underbinding is a traditional problem (Jiang et al., 1992). The results leave little room for three-nucleon force contributions, though the authors note that consistent calculations treating two- and three-nucleon systems on equal footing (Weinberg, 1992; Picklesimer et al., 1992) show large cancellations can occur between genuine three-nucleon forces and medium effects.
