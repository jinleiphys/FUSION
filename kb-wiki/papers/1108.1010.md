# Neutrino and antineutrino energy loss rates in massive stars due to isotopes of titanium

> Weak interaction rates on titanium isotopes are important during the late phases of evolution of massive stars. A search was made for key titanium isotopes from available literature and a microscopic calculation of weak rates of these nuclei were performed using the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory. Earlier the author presented the stellar electron capture rates on titanium isotopes. In this paper I present the neutrino and antineutrino energy loss rates due to capture and decay rates on isotopes of titanium in stellar environment. Accurate estimate of neutrino energy loss rates are needed for the study of the late stages of the stellar evolution, in particular for cooling of neutron stars and white dwarfs. The results are also compared against previous calculations. At high stellar temperatures the calculated neutrino and antineutrino energy loss rates are bigger by more than two orders of magnitude as compared to the large scale shell model results and favor stellar cores with lower entropies. This study can prove useful for core-collapse simulators.

# Neutrino and antineutrino energy loss rates in massive stars due to isotopes of titanium

## Key claim
The pn-QRPA calculated neutrino and antineutrino energy loss rates for seven key titanium isotopes are systematically larger than previous large-scale shell model (LSSM) results at high stellar temperatures (T9 ~ 30), often by more than two orders of magnitude, which would favor stellar cores with lower entropies during presupernova evolution. This claim is conditional on the assumption that neutrinos and antineutrinos are transparent to stellar matter at presupernova densities, which does not hold at extremely high densities where neutrinos become trapped in neutrinospheres.

## Method
The author used the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory to perform a microscopic state-by-state calculation of weak interaction transition probabilities for seven titanium isotopes (49,51,52,53,54,55,56Ti). Neutrino and antineutrino energy loss rates were computed from four weak-interaction channels (electron capture, positron decay, beta decay, and positron capture) using phase space integrals that depend on stellar temperature, density, and electron Fermi energy, with the effective ratio of axial to vector coupling constants taken as -1.254 and the D-value as 6295 s.

## Key numbers
- At log(ρYe) = 1.0 g/cm³ and T9 = 30, the neutrino energy loss rate for 49Ti is 10^2.887 MeV s⁻¹ and the antineutrino rate is 10^2.888 MeV s⁻¹.
- At ρYe = 10 g/cm³ and T9 = 30, the neutrino energy loss per unit time per 51Ti nucleus is 1161.4 MeV s⁻¹ and the antineutrino energy loss is 4570.9 MeV s⁻¹.
- For 49Ti at ρYe = 10¹ g/cm³ and T9 = 1, the pn-QRPA neutrino rates are bigger than LSSM rates by at least a factor of 40.
- For 49Ti at ρYe = 10⁷ g/cm³ and T9 = 7, the pn-QRPA rates are bigger than FFN rates by as much as a factor of 44.
- For 51Ti antineutrino rates at T9 = 30 and low densities, the pn-QRPA rates are bigger than LSSM rates by around a factor of 50.
- For 55Ti antineutrino rates at low temperatures and densities, FFN rates are larger than pn-QRPA rates by 8 orders of magnitude, though these rates are very small numbers (~10⁻⁸⁰ MeV s⁻¹).
- For 56Ti antineutrino rates at T9 = 1, FFN rates are smaller than pn-QRPA rates by around 6 orders of magnitude.

## Context
This work extends the author's previous calculation of stellar electron capture rates on titanium isotopes (Nabi, 2007) and the broader pn-QRPA weak interaction rate calculations for fp/fpg-shell nuclei (Nabi and Klapdor-Kleingrothaus, 2004). The seven titanium isotopes studied were identified as astrophysically important by Aufderheide and collaborators (1994) in their search for key weak interaction nuclei in presupernova evolution. The results are compared against the pioneering FFN calculations (Fuller, Fowler, and Newman, 1982) used in the KEPLER stellar evolution code and the large-scale shell model (LSSM) calculations (Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo, 2000) used in recent presupernova simulations of massive stars in the 11-40 solar mass range (Heger and collaborators, 2001). The calculated rates are intended for use by core-collapse simulators to study the late stages of stellar evolution, particularly the cooling of neutron stars and white dwarfs.
