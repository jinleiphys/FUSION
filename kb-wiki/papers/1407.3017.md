# A Bayesian Approach for Parameter Estimation and Prediction using a Computationally Intensive Model

> Bayesian methods have been very successful in quantifying uncertainty in physics-based problems in parameter estimation and prediction. In these cases, physical measurements y are modeled as the best fit of a physics-based model $η(θ)$ where $θ$ denotes the uncertain, best input setting. Hence the statistical model is of the form $y = η(θ) + ε$, where $ε$ accounts for measurement, and possibly other error sources. When non-linearity is present in $η(\cdot)$, the resulting posterior distribution for the unknown parameters in the Bayesian formulation is typically complex and non-standard, requiring computationally demanding computational approaches such as Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) to produce multivariate draws from the posterior. While quite generally applicable, MCMC requires thousands, or even millions of evaluations of the physics model $η(\cdot)$. This is problematic if the model takes hours or days to evaluate. To overcome this computational bottleneck, we present an approach adapted from Bayesian model calibration. This approach combines output from an ensemble of computational model runs with physical measurements, within a statistical formulation, to carry out inference. A key component of this approach is a statistical response surface, or emulator, estimated from the ensemble of model runs. We demonstrate this approach with a case study in estimating parameters for a density functional theory (DFT) model, using experimental mass/binding energy measurements from a collection of atomic nuclei. We also demonstrate how this approach produces uncertainties in predictions for recent mass measurements obtained at Argonne National Laboratory (ANL).

## Key claim

Bayesian model calibration using a Gaussian process emulator can estimate parameter uncertainties and produce predictions for computationally intensive nuclear density functional theory models, but the approach requires constructing the emulator from an initial ensemble of model runs and assumes the model output changes smoothly as a function of the input parameters.

## Method

The authors built a Gaussian process emulator from 183 DFT model runs at different parameter settings for 75 nuclei (28 spherical and 47 deformed), using a basis representation with 9 empirical orthogonal functions to handle multiple outputs simultaneously. They then applied a Bayesian calibration framework that combines the emulator with experimental mass measurements to estimate posterior distributions for the 12 UNEDF1 model parameters and to produce predictions with uncertainties for 17 newly measured neutron-rich nuclei from the CARIBU facility at Argonne National Laboratory.

## Key numbers

- The DFT model requires 5-10 minutes to compute the mass for a given nucleus
- The emulator was built from m=183 DFT model runs for each of n=92 nuclei (28 spherical, 47 deformed, 17 neutron-rich), totaling 16,836 DFT runs
- q=9 basis functions were used, explaining over 99.9% of the variation in the simulation ensemble
- The emulator achieved root mean square error of 0.14 MeV for spherical masses and 0.17 MeV for deformed masses when tested against holdout DFT runs
- The 12 UNEDF1 model parameters have prior ranges specified in Table 1, including for example: $\rho_c$ from 0.155 to 0.165, $K^{NM}$ from 200 to 240, and $L^{NM}_{sym}$ from 15.0 to 65.0

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian model calibration approach of Kennedy and O'Hagan (2001) to nuclear density functional theory, specifically the UNEDF1 parametrization of the Skyrme functional developed by Kortelainen et al. (2012). The method enables parameter estimation and uncertainty quantification for computationally expensive nuclear models where direct Markov chain Monte Carlo would require thousands to millions of model evaluations. The approach can be applied to predict masses for newly measured neutron-rich nuclei and could be extended to other nuclear observables and to determine quantities such as the two-neutron drip line.
