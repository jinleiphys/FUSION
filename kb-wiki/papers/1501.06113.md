# Recent Advances in the Application of the Shell Model Monte Carlo Approach to Nuclei

> The shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method is a powerful technique for calculating the statistical and collective properties of nuclei in the presence of correlations in model spaces that are many orders of magnitude larger than those that can be treated by conventional diagonalization methods. We review recent advances in the development and application of SMMC to mid-mass and heavy nuclei.

## Key claim

The Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method can describe statistical and collective properties of mid-mass and heavy nuclei in model spaces far beyond conventional diagonalization, but its application to odd-particle systems has been limited by a sign problem that the authors have now circumvented using scalar imaginary-time single-particle Green's functions.

## Method

The authors use the auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) representation of the imaginary-time propagator, where the Hamiltonian is transformed into a functional integral over auxiliary fields. For odd-particle systems, they compute scalar imaginary-time single-particle Green's functions and extract ground-state energies of odd-mass nuclei from the asymptotic slope of these Green's functions relative to the known even-even ground-state energy. For collective properties, they calculate the thermal expectation value of total angular momentum squared ⟨J²⟩_T and compare its low-temperature behavior to rotational and vibrational model formulas.

## Context

This work extends the SMMC method beyond earlier applications to even-even nuclei (Nakada 1997, Alhassid 1999, 2007, 2008, Ozen 2013) by solving the odd-particle sign problem via Green's functions (Mukherjee 2012). It enables microscopic shell-model calculations of pairing gaps and level densities in odd-mass mid-mass nuclei, and demonstrates that a truncated spherical shell-model approach can describe the crossover from vibrational to rotational collectivity in heavy rare-earth nuclei (¹⁴⁴–¹⁵²Nd, ¹⁴⁸–¹⁵⁴Sm). The collective enhancement factors of level densities computed here provide a microscopic foundation for empirical formulas used in nuclear reaction modeling (Capote 2009).
