# On the Collisional Damping of Giant Dipole Resonance

## Key claim
The collisional damping width of the giant dipole resonance (GDR) calculated using the microscopic in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross-sections of Li and Machleidt accounts for only about 25-35% of the experimentally observed total width in $^{120}Sn$ and $^{208}Pb$ at finite temperatures, leaving the dominant contribution to be explained by coherent damping mechanisms such as coupling with surface fluctuations.

## Method
The authors calculate the collisional damping width of GDR excitations within a non-Markovian transport approach, linearizing the extended time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory around a finite-temperature equilibrium state. They evaluate the 2p-2h self-energy in the Thomas-Fermi approximation, replacing occupation numbers with Fermi-Dirac phase-space densities and summations over states with phase-space integrals, and compute the damping width using the scaling picture distortion factor $\chi_D({\bf p})={\bf p \cdot e}$ for the momentum distribution. The momentum integrals are performed exactly after transforming to relative and total momentum coordinates, and the nuclear density is determined in Thomas-Fermi approximation using a Woods-Saxon potential.

## Key numbers
- The collisional damping widths account for 25-35% of the observed GDR widths in $^{120}Sn$ and $^{208}Pb$ at finite temperatures.
- The GDR energy is taken as $\hbar\omega = 80 A^{-1/3}$ MeV.
- The Woods-Saxon potential parameters used are depth $V_0 = -44$ MeV, thickness $a = 0.67$ fm, and sharp radius $R_0 = 1.27 A^{1/3}$ fm.
- The normal nuclear matter density is taken as $\rho_0 = 0.18$ fm$^{-3}$.
- The microscopic in-medium cross-section parameterization is $\sigma_{pn}^{LM} = [31.5 + 0.092 |20.2 - E_{lab}^{0.53}|^{2.9}] \cdot (1.0 + 0.0034 E_{lab}^{1.51} \rho^2) / (1.0 + 21.55 \rho^{1.34})$, where $E_{lab}$ is the bombarding energy in MeV.

## Context
This work extends earlier calculations of collisional damping by Ayik et al. (1998) that used approximate momentum integrals and phenomenological Skyrme or Gogny forces, which the authors show produce unreliable cross-sections that deviate strongly from microscopic results at low densities and low energies. The calculations are compared with experimental GDR data from Baumann et al. (1998) and revised data from Kusnezov et al. (1998). The finding that collisional damping accounts for only about one-third of the spreading width implies that coherent damping mechanisms, such as the coupling of the dipole mode with surface fluctuations as studied by Bertsch et al. (1983) and Lauritzen et al. (1995), must provide the dominant contribution to the total GDR width.
