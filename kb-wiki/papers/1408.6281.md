# Nuclear pairing from microscopic forces: singlet channels and higher-partial waves

> Background: An accurate description of nuclear pairing gaps is extremely important for understanding static and dynamic properties of the inner crusts of neutron stars and to explain their cooling process. Purpose: We plan to study the behavior of the pairing gaps $Δ_F$ as a function of the Fermi momentum $k_F$ for neutron and nuclear matter in all relevant angular momentum channels where superfluidity is believed to naturally emerge. The calculations will employ realistic chiral nucleon-nucleon potentials with the inclusion of three-body forces and self-energy effects. Methods: The superfluid states of neutron and nuclear matter are studied by solving the BCS gap equation for chiral nuclear potentials using the method suggested by Khodel et al., where the original gap equation is replaced by a coupled set of equations for the dimensionless gap function $χ(p)$ defined by $Δ(p) = Δ_F χ(p)$ and a non-linear algebraic equation for the gap magnitude $Δ_F = Δ(p_F)$ at the Fermi surface. This method is numerically stable even for small pairing gaps, such as that encountered in the coupled $^3PF_2$ partial wave. Results: We have successfully applied Khodel's method to singlet ($S$) and coupled channel ($SD$ and $PF$) cases in neutron and nuclear matter. Our calculations agree with other ab-initio approaches, where available, and provide crucial inputs for future applications in superfluid systems.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that modern chiral nucleon-nucleon potentials, combined with three-body forces and self-energy effects, can reproduce pairing gaps in neutron and nuclear matter across multiple angular momentum channels, but the results show significant model dependence at high densities, particularly for the coupled \(^3PF_2\) channel where the gap magnitude is sensitive to the choice of cutoff scale and regulating functions.

## Method

The authors solved the BCS gap equation for infinite neutron and nuclear matter using the method of Khodel et al., which replaces the original gap equation with a coupled set of equations for a dimensionless gap function and a nonlinear algebraic equation for the gap magnitude at the Fermi surface. They employed the chiral N3LO nucleon-nucleon potential from Entem and Machleidt (2011) and the Juelich chiral potentials from Epelbaum et al. (2008), with three-body forces approximated as a density-dependent two-body potential from Holt et al. (2009, 2010) and self-energy effects included via a density-dependent effective mass. The calculations were performed for singlet \(^1S_0\) and coupled \(^3SD_1\) and \(^3PF_2\) partial waves, using both bare potentials and renormalization-group-evolved interactions (\(V_{\text{lowk}}\) and SRG).

## Key numbers

- The \(^1S_0\) pairing gap in nuclear matter reaches a maximum of approximately 3.5 MeV at Fermi momentum \(k_F \simeq 0.85\) fm\(^{-1}\) when using the bare chiral N3LO interaction; using \(V_{\text{lowk}}\) reduces the gap by almost 0.5 MeV.
- In the \(^3SD_1\) channel for nuclear matter, the pairing gap magnitude is about 10 MeV with conventional realistic potentials; including three-body forces and effective masses substantially reduces the gap and shifts its maximum to \(k_F \approx 1\) fm\(^{-1}\).
- For the \(^3PF_2\) channel in neutron matter, the peak in the gap consistently occurs between \(2.2 \leq k_F \leq 2.6\) fm\(^{-1}\), with all reasonable interactions giving a gap of magnitude approximately 1 MeV.
- SRG-evolved interactions in the \(^3SD_1\) channel reduce the maximum gap to approximately 5 MeV (a factor of 2 smaller than the bare potential), with variation on the order of 0.5 MeV or less for \(\lambda\) between 1.3 fm\(^{-1}\) and 2.2 fm\(^{-1}\).

## Context

This work extends previous BCS calculations of nuclear pairing gaps by employing modern chiral effective field theory potentials, which provide a systematic framework for including three-body forces and estimating theoretical uncertainties. The results agree with earlier ab-initio approaches such as Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (Gandolfi et al., 2009), Quantum Monte Carlo (Gezerlis et al.), and Correlated Basis Functions (CBF) at low densities, but show significant differences at higher densities, particularly compared to calculations using Argonne potentials. The study enables future applications to pairing gaps in finite-temperature neutron matter and provides crucial inputs for understanding neutron star cooling processes.
