# From Cosmic Matter to the Laboratory

> The recent discovery of binary neutron star mergers has opened a new and exciting venue of research into hot and dense strongly interacting matter. For the first time this elusive state of matter, described by the theory of quantum chromo dynamics, can be studied in two very different environments. On the macroscopic scale in the collisions of neutron stars and on the microscopic scale in collisions of heavy ions at particle collider facilities. We will discuss the conditions that are created in these mergers and the corresponding high energy nuclear collisions. This includes the properties of QCD matter, i.e. the expected equation of state as well as expected chemical and thermodynamic properties of this exotic matter. To explore this matter in the laboratory - a new research prospect is available at the Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research, FAIR. The new facility is being constructed adjacent to the existing accelerator complex of the GSI Helmholtz Center for Heavy Ion Research at Darmstadt/Germany, expanding the research goals and technical possibilities substantially. The worldwide unique accelerator and experimental facilities of FAIR will open the way for a broad spectrum of unprecedented research supplying a variety of experiments in hadron, nuclear, atomic and plasma physics as well as biomedical and material science which will be briefly described.

## Key claim

The paper argues that matter created in binary neutron star mergers and in heavy-ion collisions at the SIS accelerator at GSI/FAIR can be described by the same QCD equation of state, with both systems reaching similar specific entropy per baryon of approximately 2, but the abstract omits that this similarity holds only under the condition that the specific entropy is the key connecting parameter and that significant differences exist in strangeness content between the two scenarios.

## Method

The authors employ the Frankfurt Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model, a three-flavor chiral Lagrangian that includes baryonic and quark degrees of freedom with parity doubling, to construct a unified equation of state for QCD matter. They then use relativistic ideal fluid dynamics simulations of Pb+Pb collisions at SIS beam energies (0.6 A GeV shown) and apply the Rankine-Hugoniot-Taub adiabat solution to relate beam energy to initial compression, density, and temperature. For neutron star mergers, they assume isentropic evolution along trajectories of fixed specific entropy per baryon (S/A ≈ 2) and compare thermodynamic properties such as chiral condensate, speed of sound, and quark fraction between the two systems.

## Key numbers

- Pseudo-critical temperature at vanishing chemical potential from lattice QCD: ≈ 155 MeV
- Nuclear saturation density: n₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³
- Nuclear matter ground state binding energy: ε₀/n₀ - m_N = -16 MeV
- Critical temperature of nuclear liquid-vapor phase transition in CMF model: T_CP ≈ 17 MeV
- Critical temperature of chiral first order phase transition in CMF model: T_CP ≈ 17 MeV
- Beam energy range for CBM experiment at SIS100/300: 2–35 AGeV (light nuclei up to 44 AGeV)
- SIS100 and SIS300 synchrotron circumference: 1100 meters each
- Magnetic rigidity of SIS100: 100 Tm; SIS300: 300 Tm
- Beam intensity increase at FAIR: factor of 100 for primary beams, up to factor of 10000 for secondary radioactive beams
- Chiral crossover temperature in neutron star merger matter at S/A=2: T ≈ 42 MeV
- Maximum quark fraction in hottest neutron star merger matter: at most 10%
- Specific entropy per baryon range for SIS collisions: between 2 and 10
- Maximum compression in SIS range: up to approximately twenty times nuclear saturation energy density and up to ten times nuclear saturation density
- Total collision duration for Pb+Pb at E_lab = 0.6 A GeV: about 25 fm/c

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework connecting heavy-ion collision physics and neutron star merger observations, building on prior lattice QCD calculations by Aoki (2006), Borsanyi (2013), and Bazavov (2014) at vanishing chemical potential, and on the discovery of the first binary neutron star merger by LIGO/Virgo (2017). The CMF model used here was developed by Papazoglou (1998) and Steinheimer (2011), and the fluid dynamic approach follows the work of Stoecker (1980, 1981) and Rischke (1995). The paper enables future comparisons between experimental data from the CBM and HADES experiments at FAIR and gravitational wave observations from neutron star mergers, particularly to study the chiral transition and deconfinement at high baryon densities.
