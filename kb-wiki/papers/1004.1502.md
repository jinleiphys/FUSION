# Nucleon semimagic numbers and low-energy neutron scattering

> It is shown that experimental values of the cross sections of inelastic low-energy neutron scattering on even-even nuclei together with the description of these cross sections in the framework of the coupled channel optical model may be considered as a reliable method for finding nuclei with a semimagic number (or numbers) of nucleons. Some examples of the application of this method are considered.

## Key claim

Experimental values of inelastic low-energy neutron scattering cross sections on even-even nuclei, combined with coupled channel optical model (CCOM) descriptions, can serve as a reliable method for identifying nuclei with semimagic numbers of nucleons, but the method is most efficient when neutron inelastic cross sections for the nucleus and its neighbors are known with appropriate accuracy and becomes less certain when such data are absent or of poor precision.

## Method

The authors analyzed experimental total and elastic scattering cross sections, inelastic scattering cross sections for excitation of the 2⁺₁ level, and angular distributions for neutron energies Eₙ ≤ 3 MeV on even-even nuclei with A ≥ 56. They employed a two-phonon (five-channel) CCOM approximation for spherical nuclei and a three-channel rotational approximation for nonspherical nuclei, using a nonspherical optical potential with a Woods-Saxon real part including spin-orbit and symmetry terms, and an absorptive part taken as the derivative of the real form factor. The depth parameters V₀ and W were fitted as free parameters, while the quadrupole deformation parameter β₂ was taken from a compilation, and the diffuseness parameter a was adjusted (reduced for magic/semimagic nuclei, increased for nonspherical nuclei) to maintain consistent V₀ and W values across all nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Optimal CCOM parameters for all even-even nuclei studied: V₀ = 52.5 ± 1.5 MeV and W = 2.5 ± 0.5 MeV.
- Potential radius fixed as R = r₀A¹/³ with r₀ = 1.22 fm; initial nuclear diffuseness parameter a = 0.65 fm.
- For nonspherical nuclei, diffuseness parameter a was increased to 0.70–0.75 fm; for magic and semimagic nuclei, a was reduced to 0.55–0.60 fm; for double-magic nuclei, a ≈ 0.50 fm.
- The NₚNₙ-systematics curve for inelastic cross section at 300 keV over threshold is approximated by σ_inel = A + B√(NₚNₙ) with A = 0.5 b and B = 0.14 b (not valid for NₚNₙ > 120).
- For ⁷⁰Ge and ⁷²Ge, neutron inelastic cross sections differ by a factor of 1.7; the NₚNₙ-values are 0 and 40 respectively.
- For ¹⁴⁰Ce, inelastic cross section = 0.60 b; for ¹³⁶Ba, = 0.66 b.
- For ¹⁴⁶Gd, diffuseness parameter a = 0.50 fm; for ¹¹⁴Sn, a = 0.50 fm.
- For ²³²Th, two experimental inelastic cross section values: 1.13 ± 0.21 b and 1.22 ± 0.22 b (NₚNₙ = 128).
- s-neutron strength function S₀ for ²⁴⁶Cm = 0.45 ± 0.15; for ²⁴⁴Cm, S₀ = 1.00 ± 0.20; for ²⁴⁸Cm, S₀ = 1.10 ± 0.12.

## Context

This work extends the NₚNₙ-systematics approach previously developed by the authors (Zaikin and Surkova, 1999, 2002) and applies it to identify semimagic numbers, building on the concept that valence proton-neutron interactions can create gaps in single-nucleon level schemes, as proposed by De Shalit and Goldhaber and by Federman and Pittel. The method confirms the existence of neutron semimagic numbers Nₛ = 38, 56, 64 and proton semimagic numbers Zₛ = 40, 58, 64, and suggests possible semimagic numbers Z = 96 and N = 150, enabling future experimental verification of these candidates and extension to heavier nuclei where data are scarce.
