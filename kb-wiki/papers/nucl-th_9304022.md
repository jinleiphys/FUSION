# Comment on "Comparison of Potential Models with the PP Scattering Data Below 350 Mev"

> We point out two flaws in the recent test of nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials conducted by Stoks and de Swart. First, in some cases, the neutron-proton ($np$) version of an NN potential was compared to the proton-proton ($pp$) data, which is improper and yields (large) $χ^2$ that are essentially meaningless. Second, for a proper test of the quantitative nature of a NN potential, it is insufficient to compare to $pp$ data only, since this leaves the T=0 potential untested. Thus, it can happen that the $pp$ version of a potential predicts the $pp$ data accurately, while the $np$ version of that same potential is poor in $np$ (where also the T=0 potential is involved). An example for this is the Nijmegen potential, which predicts the $pp$ data well with a $χ^2$/datum of 2.0, but yields a $χ^2$/datum of 6.5 in $np$.

# Comment on "Comparison of Potential Models with the PP Scattering Data Below 350 MeV"

## Key claim
The test of nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials conducted by Stoks and de Swart contains two flaws: in some cases, the neutron-proton (np) version of a potential was improperly compared to proton-proton (pp) data, yielding meaningless χ² values; and restricting the test to pp data alone leaves the T=0 potential untested, which can mask a poor np prediction even when the pp version appears accurate.

## Method
The authors re-examined the χ²/datum values reported by Stoks and de Swart for several NN potentials (Argonne, Bonn87, Bonn89, Nijmegen, Paris) when fitted to pp and np scattering data below 350 MeV. They compared the χ²/datum obtained when np potentials were improperly applied to pp data versus when the correct pp versions were used, and they contrasted the χ²/datum for pp-only fits with those for np fits to demonstrate the role of the T=0 isospin channel.

## Key numbers
- The np version of the Argonne potential, when improperly confronted with pp data, yields a χ²/datum of 824 for 0–350 MeV; for 2–350 MeV the χ²/datum is 7.1.
- The np version of the Bonn87 potential, when improperly confronted with pp data, yields a χ²/datum of 641 for 0–350 MeV; for 2–350 MeV the χ²/datum is 13.
- The pp version of the Bonn potential (Bonn89) properly fitted to pp data yields a χ²/datum of 1.9.
- The Nijmegen potential predicts pp data with a χ²/datum of 2.0 but yields a χ²/datum of 6.5 in np.
- In nuclear matter, the binding energy per nucleon at normal density is 0.61 MeV smaller for a pp potential compared to an np potential; the correct charge-dependent calculation is 0.27 MeV above the pp value, against a total binding energy of 16 MeV per nucleon.
- In the triton, np potentials predict about 0.3 MeV more binding energy than pp potentials; the correct charge-dependent calculation is 0.1 MeV above the pp result, and the experimental triton binding energy is 8.48 MeV.

## Context
This comment directly addresses the work of Stoks and de Swart (1993), who compared NN potential models to pp scattering data. The authors argue that such a restricted test is insufficient because it ignores the T=0 isospin channel, which is tested only in np scattering. They show that the Paris and Nijmegen potentials, which fit pp data well (χ²/datum ≈ 2), produce substantially larger χ²/datum in np (factor 2–3 larger), primarily due to discrepancies in T=0 phase shifts such as ³D₂ and ¹P₁. This work enables more careful evaluation of NN potentials for few-nucleon calculations, where charge-dependence and T=0 contributions are crucial, as demonstrated by the Bochum group (Witala et al., 1991; Witala and Glöckle, 1991) for three-nucleon scattering.
