# Low-density homogeneous symmetric nuclear matter: disclosing dinucleons in coexisting phases

> The effect of in-medium dinucleon bound states on self-consistent single-particle fields in Brueckner, Bethe and Goldstone theory is investigated in symmetric nuclear matter at zero temperature. To this end, dinucleon bound state occurences in the $^1S_0$ and ${}^3SD_1$ channels are explicitly accounted for -within the continuous choice for the auxiliary fields- while imposing self-consistency in Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approximation calculations. Searches are carried out at Fermi momenta in the range $0<k_F\leq1.75$~fm$^{-1}$, using the Argonne $v_{18}$ bare nucleon-nucleon potential without resorting to the effective mass approximation. As a result, two distinct solutions meeting the self-consistency requirement are found with overlapping domains in the interval $0.130\;\textrm{fm}^{-1} \leq k_F \leq 0.285\;\textrm{fm}^{-1}$, corresponding to mass densities between $10^{11.4}$ and $10^{12.4}$ g cm$^{-3}$. Effective masses as high as three times the nucleon mass are found in the coexistence domain. The emergence of superfluidity in relationship with BCS pairing gap solutions is discussed.

# Low-density homogeneous symmetric nuclear matter: disclosing dinucleons in coexisting phases

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that within Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) theory using the continuous choice for the auxiliary field, two distinct self-consistent single-particle potentials coexist for symmetric nuclear matter at zero temperature in the Fermi momentum range 0.130 fm⁻¹ ≤ k_F ≤ 0.285 fm⁻¹, corresponding to mass densities between 10¹¹·⁴ and 10¹²·⁴ g cm⁻³. This coexistence is driven by dinucleon bound states in the ¹S₀ and ³SD₁ channels, with the deuteron channel identified as the primary constituent responsible for the second phase.

## Method

The authors solve the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone equations self-consistently for symmetric nuclear matter at zero temperature using the Argonne v₁₈ bare nucleon-nucleon potential, including all partial waves up to total angular momentum J=7ħ. They explicitly account for dinucleon bound state poles in the ¹S₀ and ³SD₁ channels of the g-matrix, using a regularization procedure with η=0.1 MeV to control singularities, and employ adaptive trapezoidal quadrature for Fermi-motion integrals with self-consistency achieved when maximum fluctuations of U(k) over three consecutive cycles do not exceed 0.04 MeV. Searches are performed for Fermi momenta in the range 0 < k_F ≤ 1.75 fm⁻¹ without resorting to the effective mass approximation.

## Key numbers

- Coexisting solutions found in the interval 0.130 fm⁻¹ ≤ k_F ≤ 0.285 fm⁻¹, corresponding to mass densities between 10¹¹·⁴ and 10¹²·⁴ g cm⁻³
- Effective masses as high as three times the nucleon mass found in the coexistence domain
- The critical Fermi momentum for the upper boundary of coexistence (k_β) is 0.285 fm⁻¹, resolved with steps as small as 0.001 fm⁻¹
- The critical Fermi momentum for the lower boundary of coexistence (k_α) is 0.130 fm⁻¹, resolved with steps of 0.01 fm⁻¹
- Difference between Fermi energies of solutions I and II is 1.6 MeV at k_α and 5.6 MeV at k_β
- Self-consistent solutions obtained at Fermi momenta k_F = 0.35(0.05)1.75 fm⁻¹ for the upper density range
- Regularization parameter η = 0.1 MeV used to control bound-state singularities
- Self-consistency convergence criterion: maximum fluctuations of U(k_i) over three consecutive cycles not exceeding 0.04 MeV

## Context

This work extends Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations of symmetric nuclear matter to very low densities where dinucleon bound states become important, a regime relevant for nuclear halos and envelopes of core-collapse supernovae. The authors build on the continuous choice for the auxiliary potential, which was shown by Song (1998) to provide better convergence than the standard choice, and on earlier BHF studies by Baldo (1999, 2000). The finding of two coexisting self-consistent solutions challenges the assumption of unique mean-field solutions at low densities and provides a baseline for future studies incorporating hole-hole propagation beyond Brueckner ladder diagrams, as well as for investigating superfluidity through BCS pairing gap equations.
