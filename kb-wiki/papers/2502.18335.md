# On the momentum space structure of the quark propagator

> The structure of the quark propagator in momentum space is explored taking into account non-perturbative QCD dynamics constraints for the quark spectral densities derived previously. We assume that the scalar and vector component of the quark propagator share a simple pole but not its residuum, together with other structures. Furthermore, a connection between the poles of the quark propagator and the zeros of the quark wave function $Z(p^2)$ is established. Asymptotic scaling laws for the representation of the quark propagator, after removing the shared pole, are also derived. The confrontation of our results with lattice data for the full QCD quark propagator data are in good agreement. Exploring the link with the lattice data and looking at the Bethe-Salpeter vertex and amplitude, in the chiral limit, we are able to provide estimations for these quantities, for $f_π$ and for the shared pole mass. The pole mass reproduces the constituent quark mass used in the quark models.

## Key claim

The quark propagator's scalar and vector components share a common simple pole at timelike momenta (not just any pole), but the residua at that pole differ between the two components; this shared pole is not present in the running quark mass but appears in the quark wave function, and the pole mass reproduces the constituent quark mass used in quark models.

## Method

The authors assumed a spectral representation for the quark propagator where the scalar and vector form factors each contain a shared simple pole plus additional structures parametrized as series of higher-order poles. They derived asymptotic scaling laws for the remaining parts after removing the shared pole, then fitted functional forms motivated by these scaling laws to lattice QCD data for the Landau gauge quark propagator from Oliveira (2018). Using the axial-vector Ward-Takahashi identity in the chiral limit, they related the fitted quark propagator to the pion Bethe-Salpeter vertex and computed the pion decay constant.

## Key numbers

- Running quark mass fit parameters: $m_0 = 0.007003(42)$ GeV (current quark mass), $a_1 = 0.2403(40)$ GeV$^3$, $b_1 = 10.04(41)$ GeV$^2$, $a_2 = 0.506(11)$ GeV$^5$, $b_2 = 1.227(14)$ GeV$^2$; $\chi^2/d.o.f. = 0.77$
- Quark wave function fit parameters (up to $p = 2.5$ GeV): $z_0 = 1.1500(15)$, $z_1 = -0.1639(69)$ GeV$^2$, $m_1^2 = 0.672(35)$ GeV$^2$; $\chi^2/d.o.f. = 0.49$
- Estimated shared pole mass $m \sim 377$ MeV (using $f_\pi = 130$ MeV, $M(0) = 350$ MeV, $Z_V(1 + m^2 \Sigma_V(0)) \sim 1$)
- Computed pion decay constant $f_\pi = 95$ MeV (compared to experimental $92$ MeV in the convention used)
- $E_\pi(0) = 3.12$ from the fits

## Context

This work extends the spectral density constraints derived by Lowdon (2017) for the quark propagator, which suggested a shared simple pole between scalar and vector components. It builds on Pad\'e analyses by Falcao (2022) that identified a timelike pole in the quark propagator correlated with the pion mass. The results enable future calculations of Minkowski-space pion observables relevant to the Electron-Ion Collider program, and motivate searching for Dyson-Schwinger equation kernels compatible with the integral representation containing higher-order poles.
