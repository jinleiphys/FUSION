# Cross section of $^{36}S(n,γ)^{37}S$

> At the Karlsruhe pulsed 3.75 MV Van de Graaff accelerator the $^{36}S(n,γ)^{37}S(5.05 min)$ cross section was measured by the fast cyclic activation technique via the 3.103 MeV $γ$-ray line of the $^{37}$S-decay. Samples of elemental sulfur enriched in $^{36}$S by 5.933 % were irradiated between two gold foils which served as capture standards. The capture cross section was measured at the neutron energies 25, 151, 176, and 218 keV, respectively. The $^{36}S(n,γ)^{37}S$-cross section in the thermonuclear and thermal energy range has been calculated using the direct-capture (DC) model combined with the folding procedure used for the determination of the potentials. The non-resonant experimental data for this reaction can be reproduced excellently using this method. The input parameters of the DC-calculation (masses, Q-values, nuclear density distributions, spectroscopic factors, spin-parity assignments and excitation energies of the low-lying states of the residual nucleus) have been taken from the available experimental data.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first experimental measurement of the $^{36}S(n,\gamma)^{37}S$ cross section and shows that direct-capture (DC) calculations using the folding procedure can reproduce the non-resonant experimental data excellently, though an enhancement at 176 keV indicates resonant contributions not captured by the DC model alone.

## Method

The cross section was measured at the Karlsruhe pulsed 3.75 MV Van de Graaff accelerator using the fast cyclic activation technique, irradiating elemental sulfur samples enriched to 5.933% in $^{36}S$ between two gold foils serving as capture standards. The induced $^{37}S$ activity was counted via its 3.103 MeV gamma-ray line, and cross sections were determined relative to the well-known $^{197}Au(n,\gamma)$ standard at neutron energies of 25, 151, 176, and 218 keV. Theoretical cross sections from thermal (25.3 meV) to thermonuclear energies were calculated using the direct-capture model combined with a folding procedure for the nucleon-nucleus potentials, with spectroscopic factors taken from $^{36}S(d,p)^{37}S$ experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Average experimental cross section at 25 keV: $187 \pm 14$ microbarn
- Average experimental cross section at $151 \pm 15$ keV: $81 \pm 7$ microbarn
- Average experimental cross section at $176 \pm 20$ keV: $125 \pm 11$ microbarn
- Average experimental cross section at $218 \pm 23$ keV: $78 \pm 7$ microbarn
- Thermal (25.3 meV) experimental cross section: $150 \pm 30$ microbarn (from Sears 1992)
- Thermonuclear reaction rate factor: $N_A \langle \sigma v \rangle = 2.56 \times 10^4$ cm$^3$ mole$^{-1}$ s$^{-1}$
- Measured cross section is a factor of 1.8 smaller than the earlier estimate of 300 microbarn at 30 keV by Woosley et al. (1978)
- $^{37}S$ half-life: 5.05 minutes; gamma-ray energy: 3103 keV with 94.0% intensity per decay

## Context

This work provides the first experimental measurement of the $^{36}S(n,\gamma)^{37}S$ cross section, which was previously only estimated at 300 microbarn at 30 keV using the statistical model by Woosley et al. (1978). The measured cross section being 1.8 times smaller than this estimate implies enhanced s-process production of $^{36}S$ in nucleosynthesis models. The reaction serves as a benchmark for nuclear-structure models (Shell Model, Relativistic Mean Field Theory, Quasi Particle Random Phase Approximation, Hartree Fock Bogoliubov Theory) for calculating neutron-capture cross sections by neutron-rich nuclei in the alpha- and r-processes, as discussed by Oberhummer et al. (1995).
