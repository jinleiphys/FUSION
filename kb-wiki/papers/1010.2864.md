# Quasi-Nambu-Goldstone Modes in Bose-Einstein Condensates

> We show that quasi-Nambu-Goldstone (NG) modes, which play prominent roles in high energy physics but have been elusive experimentally, can be realized with atomic Bose-Einstein condensates. The quasi-NG modes emerge when the symmetry of a ground state is larger than that of the Hamiltonian. When they appear, the conventional vacuum manifold should be enlarged. Consequently topological defects that are stable within the conventional vacuum manifold become unstable and decay by emitting the quasi-NG modes. Contrary to conventional wisdom, however, we show that the topological defects are stabilized by quantum fluctuations that make the quasi-NG modes massive, thereby suppressing their emission.

## Key claim

Quasi-Nambu-Goldstone modes, which are massless at tree level but acquire mass from quantum corrections, can be realized in spin-2 atomic Bose-Einstein condensates in the nematic phase; the degeneracy of three nematic phases (uniaxial, biaxial, and dihedral-2) at zeroth order corresponds to the vacuum alignment problem in particle physics, and quantum fluctuations break this degeneracy to select a unique ground state.

## Method

The authors analyze a spin-2 Bose-Einstein condensate described by a Hamiltonian with U(1)×SO(3) symmetry and three interaction terms (c0, c1, c2). They show that the zeroth-order solution for the nematic phase has an enlarged U(1)×SO(5) symmetry, leading to an enlarged vacuum manifold \(\tilde{M} \cong [\text{U(1)}\times\text{S}^4]/\mathbb{Z}_2\). They compute the ground-state energy at one-loop level to determine how quantum corrections break the enlarged symmetry, and perform modified Gross-Pitaevskii simulations to study the stability of a \(\mathbb{Z}_2\) vortex under conditions where the quantum symmetry-breaking contribution is either present or switched off.

## Key numbers

* The spin-2 condensate density used in numerical simulations is \(n = 1.5 \times 10^{14} \text{cm}^{-3}\) for typical experimental conditions with \(^{87}\text{Rb}\).
* The ground-state energy per particle at one-loop level contains a symmetry-breaking term \(v(\eta) = \omega \sum_{j=0}^2 (1 + X \cos(2\eta + 2\pi j/3))^{5/2}\), where \(\omega = 8\sqrt{M^3}[n(2c_1 - c_2)]^{5/2} / 15n\pi^2\hbar^3\) and \(X = -2c_1/(2c_1 - c_2)\).
* The uniaxial nematic phase has 3 NG modes and 2 quasi-NG modes; the biaxial nematic and dihedral-2 phases each have 4 NG modes and 1 quasi-NG mode, for a total of 5 soft modes in all phases.
* The enlarged vacuum manifold has dimension \(\text{dim}(\tilde{M}) = 5\), and the number of quasi-NG modes is given by \(n = \text{dim}(\tilde{M}) - \text{dim}(M)\).

## Context

This work extends the concept of quasi-Nambu-Goldstone modes, originally introduced by Weinberg (1976) in gauge theories and by Georgi and Pais (1977) for cases where ground-state symmetry exceeds Hamiltonian symmetry, into the experimentally accessible domain of cold atomic gases. The authors connect the vacuum degeneracy in spin-2 nematic condensates, previously studied by Barnett et al. (2006), to the vacuum alignment problem in technicolor models. The results enable experimental tests of quasi-NG mode physics by controlling quantum fluctuations in atomic Bose-Einstein condensates, and demonstrate that topological defects predicted from the conventional vacuum manifold can be stabilized or destabilized by these fluctuations.
