# Background subtraction and jet quenching on jet reconstruction

> In order to assess the ability of jet observables to constrain the characteristics of the medium produced in heavy-ion collisions at the LHC, we investigate the influence of background subtraction and jet quenching on jet reconstruction, with focus on the dijet asymmetry as currently studied by ATLAS and CMS. Using a toy model, we examine the influence of different background subtraction methods on dijet momentum imbalance and azimuthal distributions. We compare the usual jet-area based background subtraction technique and a variant of the noise-pedestal subtraction method used by CMS. The purpose of this work is to understand what are the differences between the two techniques, given the same event configuration. We analyze the influence of the quenching effect using the Q-PYTHIA Monte Carlo on the previous observables and to what extent Q-PYTHIA is able to reproduce the CMS data for the average missing transverse momentum that seems to indicate the presence of large angle emission of soft particles.

## Key claim

Using a toy model with Q-PYTHIA jets embedded in a simulated background, the authors find that a "standard" jet quenching model where energy loss occurs within a close range of the parent parton can explain the CMS dijet asymmetry, azimuthal correlation, and average missing transverse momentum data without requiring a large-angle emission mechanism, but only qualitatively because the toy background lacks realistic spatial correlations.

## Method

The authors generated Q-PYTHIA Monte Carlo events with varying quenching parameter \(\hat{q}\) (0, 4, and 8 GeV\(^2\) fm\(^{-1}\)) and embedded them in a toy background consisting of a thermal spectrum (with inverse slope \(T\) = 0.7, 0.9, or 1.2 GeV) plus a power-law \(p_T^{-6}\) component, with fixed multiplicity of 2100 particles per pseudorapidity unit. Jets were reconstructed using the anti-\(k_t\) algorithm with radius \(R = 0.3\), and two background subtraction methods were compared: the FastJet jet-area based method (using \(k_t\) algorithm with \(R = 0.4\) over \(|\eta| < 2\)) and a variant of the CMS noise-pedestal subtraction method with optimized \(E_{Tjets}\) thresholds (40, 60, or 70 GeV depending on temperature). The dijet asymmetry, dijet azimuthal correlation, and average missing transverse momentum outside a cone of \(\Delta R = 0.8\) were computed and compared to CMS data and CMS Monte Carlo simulations.

## Key numbers

* Background fluctuations of 7.69, 10.75, and 15.14 GeV correspond to thermal inverse slope parameters \(T = 0.7\), 0.9, and 1.2 GeV, respectively, with multiplicity fixed to \(2100 \pm 5\%\) particles per pseudorapidity unit.
* Optimal \(E_{Tjets}\) thresholds for the CMS-like method were 40 GeV for \(T = 0.7\), 60 GeV for \(T = 0.9\), and 70 GeV for \(T = 1.2\) GeV.
* A quenching parameter \(\hat{q} = 8\) GeV\(^2\) fm\(^{-1}\) in Q-PYTHIA was sufficient to induce a dijet asymmetry comparable to CMS data, with the background fixed to \(\sigma = 10.75\) GeV.
* The average missing transverse momentum outside a cone of \(\Delta R = 0.8\) around the leading or subleading jet axis, computed with \(\hat{q} = 8\) GeV\(^2\) fm\(^{-1}\), qualitatively reproduced the CMS data without requiring large-angle emission mechanisms.

## Context

This work extends the study of jet quenching in heavy-ion collisions at the LHC, specifically addressing the dijet asymmetry measurements by ATLAS (Aad, 2010) and CMS (Chatrchyan, 2011; Chatrchyan, 2012). It compares two background subtraction techniques (the FastJet jet-area method of Cacciari (2011) and the CMS noise-pedestal method of Kodolova (2007)) and evaluates the Q-PYTHIA Monte Carlo model of Armesto (2009) against CMS data. The results suggest that a standard quenching model can describe the main features of the data, but the authors note that more realistic background models with proper spatial correlations are needed for definitive conclusions, particularly for the missing transverse momentum observable.
