# Nuclear binding energies in artificial neural networks

> The binding energy (BE) or mass is one of the most fundamental properties of an atomic nucleus. Precise binding energies are vital inputs for many nuclear physics and nuclear astrophysics studies. However, due to the complexity of atomic nuclei and of the non-perturbative strong interaction, up to now, no conventional physical model can describe nuclear binding energies with a precision below 0.1 MeV, the accuracy needed by nuclear astrophysical studies. In this work, artificial neural networks (ANNs), the so called ``universal approximators", are used to calculate nuclear binding energies. We show that the ANN can describe all the nuclei in AME2020 with a root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) around 0.2 MeV, which is better than the best macroscopic-microscopic models, such as FRDM and WS4. The success of the ANN is mainly due to the proper and essential input features we identify, which contain the most relevant physical information, i.e., shell, paring, and isospin-asymmetry effects. We show that the well-trained ANN has excellent extrapolation ability and can predict binding energies for those nuclei so far inaccessible experimentally. In particular, we highlight the important role played by ``feature engineering'' for physical systems where data are relatively scarce, such as nuclear binding energies.

## Key claim
The authors claim that an artificial neural network (ANN) with seven carefully engineered input features can describe nuclear binding energies from AME2020 with a root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) around 0.2 MeV, which is better than the best macroscopic-microscopic models such as FRDM and WS4. The key qualifier is that this performance depends critically on "feature engineering" that encodes shell, pairing, and isospin-asymmetry effects, rather than on network complexity alone.

## Method
The authors constructed a fully connected feed-forward neural network with one input layer (seven features), two hidden layers, and one output layer, using the GeLU activation function and the Adam optimizer. The training set consisted of 3434 nuclei from AME2016, while the test set contained 122 nuclei newly added in AME2020. The seven input features were proton number Z, neutron number N, pairing indicators Z_EO and N_EO (1 for odd, 0 otherwise), shell-effect features Delta Z and Delta N (distance to nearest magic numbers 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126, 184), and an isospin-asymmetry feature ASY derived from the WS4 model.

## Key numbers
- ANN7 achieved an RMSD of 0.190 MeV on the training set (3434 nuclei) and 0.340 MeV on the test set (122 nuclei), with an overall RMSD of 0.197 MeV for the entire AME2020 dataset.
- For nuclei with Z >= 8 and N >= 8 (3336 training, 120 test), ANN7 achieved RMSDs of 0.149 MeV (training), 0.336 MeV (test), and 0.159 MeV (entire set), compared to WS4's 0.415 MeV, 1.295 MeV, and 0.474 MeV respectively.
- For the 2353 nuclei studied in the original WS4 paper, ANN7 gave an RMSD of 0.154 MeV versus WS4's 0.293 MeV.
- The RMSD decreased systematically with added features: ANN2 (Z,N only) gave 1.178 MeV, ANN4 (adding pairing) gave 0.551 MeV, ANN6 (adding shell effects) gave 0.299 MeV, and ANN7 (adding isospin asymmetry) gave 0.197 MeV for the entire set.

## Context
This work extends the direct application of neural networks to nuclear binding energies, building on earlier studies that used Bayesian neural networks on residuals (Utama 2015, Niu 2018) or direct fitting with fewer features (Yuksel 2021, Bahtiyar 2022). The results are comparable to the mixture density network approach of Mumpower (2022) and Lovell (2022), which achieved RMSDs of 0.316-0.336 MeV using 8-12 features, but the present work shows that only six of those twelve features are essential. The success demonstrates that feature engineering can compensate for limited data in nuclear physics applications, and the approach enables predictions for nuclei not yet experimentally accessible.
