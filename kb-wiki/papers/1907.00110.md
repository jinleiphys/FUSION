# Confinement in Nuclei and the Expanding Proton

> High-precision knowledge of electromagnetic form factors of nuclei is a subject of much current experimental and theoretical activity in nuclear and atomic physics. Such precision mandates that effects of the non-zero spatial extent of the constituent nucleons be handled in a manner that goes beyond the usual impulse approximation. A series of simple, Poincare-invariant, composite-proton models that respect the Ward-Takahashi identity and in which quarks are confined are used to study the validity of this approximation. The result of all of the models is a general theorem showing that medium modification of proton structure must occur. Combining this result with lattice QCD calculations leads to a conclusion that a bound proton must be larger than a free one.

# Confinement in Nuclei and the Expanding Proton

## Key claim

The paper proves a general theorem showing that medium modification of proton structure must occur when a proton is bound in a nucleus, and combining this result with lattice QCD calculations leads to the conclusion that a bound proton must be larger than a free one. The hedge is that this conclusion depends on a first-order expansion in the virtuality V = p² − M², which is small (about −0.1 M² in typical nuclear centers) but not negligible.

## Method

The author constructs five different Poincaré-invariant, composite-proton models (quark-diquark with scalar or vector vertices, and proton fluctuations into neutron-π⁺ or Δπ components) that respect the Ward-Takahashi identity and implement quark confinement via complex-conjugate-pair propagators. For each model, the Feynman diagrams for elastic electron-proton scattering are evaluated for off-mass-shell kinematics appropriate to a bound nucleon, and the first-order expansion in the virtuality V = p² − M² is performed. The result is a universal relation ΔF₁,₂ = V ∂F₁,₂/∂M², which is then combined with lattice QCD calculations of the proton charge radius as a function of pion mass to estimate the size change.

## Key numbers

- The average virtuality in typical nuclear centers is V/M² ≈ −0.1, derived from a nuclear Hartree-Fock potential of about −50 MeV.
- For A=3 nuclei, the average virtuality is V/M² = −0.073, as reported in Ciofi degli Atti (2007).
- From lattice QCD data (Jang 2018, Jang 2019), the derivative ∂r_E/∂m_π² ≈ −2.6 ± 0.3 fm GeV².
- Using the nucleon mass dependence M ≈ M₀ + 1.14 GeV⁻¹ m_π² from Walker-Loud (2008), the resulting shift in proton radius is δr_E = −(V/M²) × (1.1 ± 0.1) fm.
- For V/M² = −0.1, this gives an increase in the proton radius of about 0.11 fm, or approximately 12%.
- For A=3 nuclei with V/M² = −0.073, the shift is about 0.08 fm, corresponding to a 2% increase in the ³H charge radius.
- The MIT bag model gives (M²/r_E²) ∂r_E²/∂M² = −1; the non-relativistic quark model gives −1/2.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing study of medium modifications of nucleon structure, which began with the EMC effect (Aubert 1983, Arnold 1983) and kaon-nucleus scattering (Mardor 1990). It directly addresses the factorization approximation (product ansatz for nuclear form factors) dating to Amaldi (1950) and Villi (1959), which assumes free-nucleon form factors are valid for bound nucleons. The paper enables improved treatment of elastic electron-nucleus scattering at the precision level required by modern experiments, such as Jefferson Laboratory measurements of ³He-³H charge radius differences to ±0.02 fm (Gomez 2017) and muonic atom measurements (Antognini 2015). The result strengthens the connection between lattice QCD calculations and nuclear physics by providing a framework to compute medium modifications from the quark-mass dependence of nucleon properties.
