# To the modification of methods of nuclear chronometry in astrophysics and geophysics

> In practically all known till now methods of nuclear chronometry there were usually taken into account the life-times of only fundamental states of $α$-radioactive nuclei. But in the processes of nuclear synthesis in stars and under the influence of the constant cosmic radiation on surfaces of planets the excitations of the $α$-radioactive nuclei are going on. Between them there are the states with the excited $α$-particles inside the parent nuclei and so with much smaller life-times. And inside the large masses of stellar, terrestrial and meteoric substances the transitions between different internal conditions of radioactive nuclei are accompanied by infinite chains of the $γ$-radiations with the subsequent $γ$-absorptions, the further $γ$-radiations etc. For the description of the $α$-decay evolution with considering of such excited states and multiple $γ$-radiations and $γ$-absorptions inside stars and under the influence of the cosmic radiation on the earth surface we present the quantum-mechanical approach, which is based on the generalized Krylov-Fock theorem. Some simple estimations are also presented. They bring to the conclusion that the usual (non-corrected) "nuclear clocks" do really indicate not to realistic values but to the \emph{upper limits} of the durations of the $α$-decay stellar and planet processes.

## Key claim

Standard nuclear chronometry, which only considers ground-state lifetimes of α-radioactive nuclei, systematically overestimates the ages of stellar and planetary processes; the usual "nuclear clocks" actually indicate only the *upper limits* of the durations of α-decay processes, because excited states with much shorter lifetimes and chains of γ-radiation and γ-absorption inside large masses are neglected.

## Method

The authors generalize the Krylov-Fock theorem to describe α-decay evolution including excited states of parent nuclei (with excited internal α-particles) and multiple γ-emission and γ-absorption chains inside stellar and terrestrial matter. They derive modified decay equations that incorporate the abundances of both ground and excited states, the partial α- and γ-decay widths, and the propagation of γ-quanta through matter with resonant absorption and recoil energy losses. For terrestrial surface layers, they additionally model the effect of constant cosmic radiation using a flux-dependent correction factor that accounts for proton-induced removal and acceleration of α-decay.

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposals of Olkhovsky and collaborators (2000s) that excited states of α-radioactive nuclei can be maintained in stellar interiors via γ-emission-absorption chains, and that the Geiger-Nuttall law implies drastically reduced lifetimes for excited states. It also references the experimental result that the β-decay lifetime of ¹⁸⁷Re in bare atoms is 10⁹ times shorter than in neutral atoms (about 30 years versus 3 × 10¹⁰ years). The paper calls for revision of standard nuclear chronometry methods used in astrophysics and geophysics, and highlights the need to account for unknown initial daughter-nucleus abundances and cosmic radiation effects when dating terrestrial and meteoritic samples.
