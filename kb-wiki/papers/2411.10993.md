# Evidence of non-Solar elemental composition in the clocked X-ray burster SRGA J144459.2$-$604207

> In February and March 2024, a series of many Type I X-ray bursts from the accreting neutron star SRGA J144459.2$-$604207, which has been identified by multiple X-ray satellites, with the first reports coming from INTEGRAL and NinjaSat. These observations reveal that after exhibiting very regular behavior as a ``clocked'' burster, the peak luminosity of the SRGA J144459.2$-$604207 X-ray bursts shows a gradual decline. The observed light curves exhibit a short plateau feature, potentially with a double peak, followed by a rapid decay in the tail-features unlike those seen in previously observed clocked bursters. In this study, we calculate a series of multizone X-ray burst models with various compositions of accreted matter, specifically varying the mass fractions of hydrogen ($X$), helium ($Y$), and heavier CNO elements or metallicity ($Z_{\rm CNO}$). We demonstrate that a model with higher $Z_{\rm CNO}$ and/or lower $X/Y$ compared to the solar values can reproduce the observed behavior of SRGA J144459.2$-$604207. Therefore, we propose that this new X-ray burster is likely the first clocked burster with non-solar elemental compositions. Moreover, based on the X-ray burst light curve morphology in the decline phase observed by NinjaSat, a He-enhanced model with $X/Y \approx 1.5$ seems preferred over high-metallicity cases. We also give a brief discussion on the implications for the neutron star mass, binary star evolution, inclination angle, and the potential for a high-metallicity scenario, the last of which is closely related to the properties of the hot CNO cycle.

## Key claim

The authors propose that SRGA J144459.2-604207 is the first "clocked" X-ray burster with non-solar elemental composition, specifically a helium-enhanced model with hydrogen-to-helium ratio \(X/Y \approx 1.5\) is preferred over high-metallicity cases based on light curve morphology in the decline phase observed by NinjaSat, though the high-metallicity scenario is not entirely ruled out due to nuclear reaction rate uncertainties.

## Method

The authors used the 1D spherically symmetric general relativistic stellar evolution code HERES to calculate multizone X-ray burst models, solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation with thermal transport, nuclear burning, and neutrino cooling. They varied the mass fractions of hydrogen (\(X\)), helium (\(Y\)), and CNO metallicity (\(Z_{\rm CNO}\)) in the accreted matter, comparing models with solar composition (\(X/Y = 2.9\), \(Z_{\rm CNO} = 0.015\)), a helium-enhanced case (\(X/Y = 1.5\), \(Z_{\rm CNO} = Z_\odot\)), and a CNO-enhanced case (\(X/Y = (X/Y)_\odot\), \(Z_{\rm CNO} = 4 Z_\odot\)), using mass accretion rates \(\dot{M}_{-9} = 3\) and \(4\) for the clocked phase and \(\dot{M}_{-9} = 0.8\) and \(0.9\) for the decline phase.

## Key numbers

- Recurrence time in the clocked phase: \(\Delta t = 1.69\) h (observed by INTEGRAL), with exponential decay timescale \(\tau_e \sim 9\) s.
- Recurrence time in the decline phase: \(\Delta t = 7.909\) h for the last two bursts observed by NinjaSat.
- Helium-enhanced model with \(X/Y = 1.5\) and \(\dot{M}_{-9} = 0.9\) yields \(\Delta t = 7.76\) h, close to the NinjaSat observation of 7.909 h.
- CNO-enhanced model (\(Z_{\rm CNO} = 4 Z_\odot\)) in the decline phase predicts \(\Delta t = 25\) h for \(\dot{M}_{-9} = 0.8\) and \(\Delta t = 12\) h for \(\dot{M}_{-9} = 0.9\), both substantially longer than the observed 7.9 h.
- The accretion rate ratio between the decline phase and clocked phase is estimated as \(\dot{M}_{-9,{\rm NinjaSat}} / \dot{M}_{-9,{\rm clocked}} \simeq 0.2\) to 0.3.
- The helium-enhanced model corresponds to \(X \sim 0.6\) and \(Y \sim 0.4\), compared to solar helium abundance \(Y \sim 0.23\) to 0.28.

## Context

This work extends previous modeling of clocked bursters such as GS 1826-24 (Heger 2007) and GS 0836-429 (in't Zand 2016), which typically assumed hydrogen-rich compositions. The study contradicts the expectation that clocked bursters have solar-like compositions and instead supports an intermediate mass X-ray binary descendant scenario (Podsiadlowski 2002) where the donor star's outer layers have been stripped to expose helium-enriched material. The results enable constraints on the neutron star mass, binary evolution, and donor star properties, with the current donor mass estimated at \(M_\mathrm{don} \sim 0.3\) to \(0.4 M_\odot\) and an initial donor mass of \(M_\mathrm{don,0} \sim 2\) to \(2.5 M_\odot\).
