# Thermal evolution of hybrid stars within the framework of a nonlocal Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model

> We study the thermal evolution of neutron stars containing deconfined quark matter in their core. Such objects are generally referred to as quark-hybrid stars. The confined hadronic matter in their core is described in the framework of non-linear relativistic nuclear field theory. For the quark phase we use a non-local extension of the SU(3) Nambu Jona-Lasinio model with vector interactions. The Gibbs condition is used to model phase equilibrium between confined hadronic matter and deconfined quark matter. Our study indicates that high-mass neutron stars may contain between 35 and 40 % deconfined quark-hybrid matter in their cores. Neutron stars with canonical masses of around $1.4\, M_\odot$ would not contain deconfined quark matter. The central proton fractions of the stars are found to be high, enabling them to cool rapidly. Very good agreement with the temperature evolution established for the neutron star in Cassiopeia A (Cas A) is obtained for one of our models (based on the popular NL3 nuclear parametrization), if the protons in the core of our stellar models are strongly paired, the repulsion among the quarks is mildly repulsive, and the mass of Cas A has a canonical value of $1.4\, M_\odot$.

## Key claim

High-mass neutron stars may contain between 35 and 40% deconfined quark-hybrid matter in their cores, but neutron stars with canonical masses around 1.4 solar masses would not contain deconfined quark matter. Agreement with the temperature evolution of the neutron star in Cassiopeia A is obtained only for one specific model combination: the NL3 nuclear parametrization with strong proton pairing, mildly repulsive quark interactions, and assuming Cas A has a canonical mass of 1.4 solar masses.

## Method

The authors constructed hybrid star equations of state by combining a non-local SU(3) Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model with vector interactions for the quark phase and a non-linear relativistic mean-field model (parametrized as GM1 or NL3) for the hadronic phase. The Gibbs condition with global electric charge neutrality was used to model the mixed phase of confined hadronic matter and deconfined quark matter. The thermal evolution was computed by solving general relativistic energy balance and transport equations, including neutrino emission processes and three phenomenological models of proton pairing (shallow, medium, and deep).

## Key numbers

- High-mass neutron stars contain between 35% (GM1) and 40% (NL3) deconfined quark-hybrid matter in their cores
- Maximum neutron star masses reach up to 2.4 solar masses for the NL3 parametrization
- The neutron star in Cassiopeia A shows a surface temperature drop from 2.12 to 2.04 x 10^6 K between 2000 and 2009 (a 4% decrease)
- For the GM1 parametrization with zero vector coupling, maximum neutron star mass is about 1.8 solar masses, below the observed masses of J1614-2230 (1.97 +/- 0.04 solar masses) and J0348+0432 (2.01 +/- 0.04 solar masses)
- Canonical 1.4 solar mass stars have radii between 13.84 km (GM1, G_V=0) and 14.68 km (NL3, G_V=0.09 G_S)
- The surface tension threshold for favoring Gibbs construction over Maxwell construction is less than 40 MeV fm^-2

## Context

This work extends previous studies of hybrid stars by Orsaria (2013, 2014) and others by incorporating a non-local NJL model with vector interactions and detailed thermal evolution calculations. The finding that only the NL3 parametrization with deep proton pairing and mild quark repulsion matches Cas A data supports the interpretation by Page (2011) and Yakovlev (2011) that rapid cooling in Cas A is driven by the onset of neutron superfluidity. The results contradict simpler MIT bag model treatments (Glendenning, 1992, 2001) which do not predict the occurrence of Delta states in stable neutron stars, and rule out the GM1 parametrization with zero vector coupling because it cannot reach 2 solar masses.
