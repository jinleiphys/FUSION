# The Nuclear Central Force in the Chiral Limit

> Chiral perturbation theory supplemented by the Omnes function is employed to study the strength of the isoscalar central nuclear interaction, G_S, in the chiral limit vs the physical case. A very large modification is seen, i.e. eta_s = G_S ~ chiral /G_S ~ physical = 1.37 +- 0.10 . This large effect is seen to arise dominantly at low energy from the extra contributions made by massless pions at energies near the physical threshold where the physical spectral function must vanish kinematically. The slope away from the chiral limit, d_S, is also calculated and is correspondingly large. I also explain why this large variation is to be expected.

## Key claim

The isoscalar central nuclear interaction strength G_S is 37% larger in the chiral limit (massless pions) than in the physical case, with the dominant contribution arising from low-energy effects near the physical threshold where the spectral function must vanish kinematically.

## Method

The author employs a dispersive representation for the scalar-isoscalar interaction, relating the contact interaction strength G_S to a dispersion integral over a spectral function ρ_S(μ) constructed from two-pion exchange intermediate states. Chiral perturbation theory amplitudes are combined with the Omnes function for pion rescattering, using the inverse amplitude method (IAM) to model the ππ phase shifts and their dependence on the pion mass.

## Key numbers

- η_S = G_S(chiral) / G_S(physical) = 1.37 ± 0.10
- d_S = (0.31 ± 0.08) / m_π^2 (the chiral slope parameter)
- The box and cross box diagrams contribute a shift ΔG_S = 0.42 GeV^{-2}, compared to the total G_S ∼ -400 GeV^{-2}

## Context

This work extends the dispersive treatment of the scalar-isoscalar nuclear interaction developed in Donoghue (2006) using the Omnes formalism, building on prior work by Vinh Mau, Durso, and others on dispersion relations for the nuclear force. The results are relevant for understanding the quark mass dependence of nuclear binding, and connect to lattice QCD studies of nuclear forces in the chiral limit as well as to the chiral effective field theory approach of Epelbaum, Gloeckle, and Meissner (2005). The large variation found is analogous to the 100% violation of Dashen's theorem seen in pion and kaon electromagnetic mass splittings, where low-energy dynamical effects produce unexpectedly large mass dependence.
