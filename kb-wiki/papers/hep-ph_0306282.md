# Neutrino Physics: an Update

> We update our recent didactic survey of neutrino physics, including new results from the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory and KamLAND experiments, and recent constraints from WMAP and other cosmological probes.

## Key claim

The solar neutrino puzzle is resolved by neutrino oscillations, with approximately two-thirds of the solar electron neutrinos arriving at Earth as muon or tau neutrinos, confirming physics beyond the Standard Model; however, the absolute scale of neutrino masses and the detailed mass pattern (including the sign of the atmospheric mass-squared difference) remain unknown, with the most stringent bound on the sum of neutrino masses coming from cosmology rather than laboratory experiments.

## Method

The authors review the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO) experiment, which used a heavy-water Cherenkov detector to measure the charged-current (CC) reaction on deuterium (sensitive only to electron neutrinos) and the neutral-current (NC) breakup of deuterium (sensitive to all active neutrino flavors), along with neutrino-electron elastic scattering (ES). They also describe the KamLAND experiment, which detected reactor antineutrinos via inverse beta decay in a liquid scintillator detector located approximately 180 km from Japanese power reactors, using the disappearance technique to probe oscillation parameters. Cosmological constraints from the Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe (WMAP) and the 2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey are combined to bound the sum of neutrino masses.

## Key numbers

- SNO CC flux (electron neutrinos only): \(1.75 \pm 0.07 (\text{stat}) \pm 0.12 (\text{sys}) \pm 0.05 (\text{theory}) \times 10^{6} \, \text{cm}^{-2} \text{s}^{-1}\)
- SNO NC flux (all flavors): \(5.09 \pm 0.44 (\text{stat}) \pm 0.45 (\text{sys}) \times 10^{6} \, \text{cm}^{-2} \text{s}^{-1}\)
- SNO ES flux: \(2.39 \pm 0.34 (\text{stat}) \pm 0.15 (\text{sys}) \times 10^{6} \, \text{cm}^{-2} \text{s}^{-1}\), in agreement with SuperKamiokande ES flux \(2.32 \pm 0.03 (\text{stat}) \pm 0.06 (\text{sys}) \times 10^{6} \, \text{cm}^{-2} \text{s}^{-1}\)
- Heavy-flavor solar neutrino flux deduced from SNO: \(3.41 \pm 0.45 (\text{stat}) \pm 0.46 (\text{sys}) \times 10^{6} \, \text{cm}^{-2} \text{s}^{-1}\)
- Presence of heavy-flavor solar neutrinos confirmed at the 5.3 sigma level
- Atmospheric neutrino mass-squared difference: \(\delta m_{23}^2 \sim 2 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{eV}^2\) with maximal mixing \(\theta_{23} \sim \pi/4\)
- Solar neutrino mass-squared difference (LMA solution): \(\delta m_{12}^2 \sim 8 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{eV}^2\) with mixing angle \(\theta_{12} \sim 30\) degrees
- KamLAND observed 54 events versus \(86.8 \pm 5.6\) expected without oscillations (61% of expectation); best-fit \(\delta m_{12}^2 \sim 6.9 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{eV}^2\) and \(\sin^2 2\theta_{12} \sim 1.0\)
- Cosmological bound on sum of neutrino masses: \(\sum m_\nu \lesssim 1.0 \, \text{eV}\) (from WMAP and 2dF combined)
- Tritium beta decay upper bound on \(\bar{\nu}_e\) mass: 2.2 eV
- Claimed neutrinoless double beta decay signal corresponds to Majorana neutrino mass \(\sim 0.4 \, \text{eV}\) (disputed)

## Context

This paper updates the earlier didactic survey by Haxton and Holstein (2000) with results from SNO, KamLAND, and WMAP. The SNO results resolve the solar neutrino puzzle first uncovered by Davis (1968) and confirmed by SuperKamiokande, showing that the deficit is due to neutrino oscillations rather than problems with the standard solar model of Bahcall (2001). The KamLAND results provide the first terrestrial confirmation of the large-mixing-angle (LMA) solution and narrow the allowed range of \(\delta m_{12}^2\). The cosmological constraints from WMAP and the 2dF survey (2003) provide the tightest bound on absolute neutrino masses, superseding laboratory limits and enabling future searches for neutrinoless double beta decay and improved tritium experiments to probe masses down to \(\sim 0.3\) eV.
