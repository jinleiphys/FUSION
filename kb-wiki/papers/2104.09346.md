# Toward simulating quantum field theories with controlled phonon-ion dynamics: A hybrid analog-digital approach

> Quantum field theories are the cornerstones of modern physics, providing relativistic and quantum mechanical descriptions of physical systems at the most fundamental level. Simulating real-time dynamics within these theories remains elusive in classical computing. This provides a unique opportunity for quantum simulators, which hold the promise of revolutionizing our simulation capabilities. Trapped-ion systems are successful quantum-simulator platforms for quantum many-body physics and can operate in digital, or gate-based, and analog modes. Inspired by the progress in proposing and realizing quantum simulations of a number of relativistic quantum field theories using trapped-ion systems, and by the hybrid analog-digital proposals for simulating interacting boson-fermion models, we propose hybrid analog-digital quantum simulations of selected quantum field theories, taking recent developments to the next level. On one hand, the semi-digital nature of this proposal offers more flexibility in engineering generic model interactions compared with a fully-analog approach. On the other hand, encoding the bosonic fields onto the phonon degrees of freedom of the trapped-ion system allows a more efficient usage of simulator resources, and a more natural implementation of intrinsic quantum operations in such platforms. This opens up new ways for simulating complex dynamics of e.g., Abelian and non-Abelian gauge theories, by combining the benefits of digital and analog schemes.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that hybrid analog-digital quantum simulations of selected quantum field theories (including Yukawa theory and U(1) lattice gauge theory) can be implemented on trapped-ion platforms by encoding bosonic fields onto phonon degrees of freedom, offering more efficient resource usage than fully-digital approaches while providing more flexibility than fully-analog ones, though the feasibility depends on controlling both normal and local phonon modes and suppressing unwanted phonon hopping.

## Method

The authors develop an expanded gate set for trapped-ion quantum simulators that includes single-spin rotations, spin-spin entangling gates (Mølmer-Sørensen type), spin-phonon couplings via red and blue sideband transitions, and phonon-phonon interactions via standing-wave AC Stark shifts. They construct explicit quantum circuits for digitized time-evolution operators of a 1+1 dimensional Yukawa theory (scalar fields coupled to staggered fermions) and a 1+1 dimensional U(1) lattice gauge theory (Schwinger model), using normal modes of motion for the Yukawa case and local modes for the gauge theory case to accommodate non-trivial boson dynamics.

## Key numbers

* For a typical trapped-ion system with tens of Ytterbium ions, transverse trap frequency ω^x ~ 2π × 5 MHz and ion spacing d0 of order a few microns, the transverse confinement parameter β^x ~ 10^{-3}–10^{-4}, giving phonon hopping strength β^x ω^x ~ 2π × 1 kHz, which is negligible compared to spin-phonon coupling strength (~100 kHz) and spin-spin coupling strength (~10 kHz)
* The Lamb-Dicke parameters η_{m,j} are defined as η_{m,j} = sqrt((Δk)^2/(2M_ion ω_m)) b_{m,j}, where b_{m,j} are normalized normal-mode eigenvector components
* The ratio of coefficients for phonon-phonon interactions from standing-wave potentials is fixed to χ^{(1)}/χ^{(2)} = (-1 + η̃^2)/η̃^2, where η̃ is the Lamb-Dicke parameter for the standing-wave beam

## Context

This work extends prior hybrid analog-digital proposals for simulating interacting boson-fermion models such as the Holstein model (Mezzacapo et al., 2012) and the first experimental implementation of one-site boson-fermion dynamics (Zhang et al., 2018), taking them to relativistic quantum field theories including gauge theories. It contrasts with fully-analog trapped-ion proposals for the lattice Schwinger model (Yang et al., 2016) that are not yet experimentally feasible, and with fully-digital approaches where bosonic degrees of freedom require logarithmic qubit encoding and polynomially growing entangling gates. The work enables future simulations of Abelian and non-Abelian gauge theories by combining digital flexibility with analog efficiency, though challenges remain for higher-dimensional lattice gauge theories.
