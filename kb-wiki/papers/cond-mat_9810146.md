# Fixed-N Superconductivity: The Crossover from the Bulk to the Few-Electron Limit

> We present a truly canonical theory of superconductivity in ultrasmall metallic grains by variationally optimizing fixed-N projected BCS wave-functions, which yields the first full description of the entire crossover from the bulk BCS regime (mean level spacing $d \ll$ bulk gap $\tildeΔ$) to the ``fluctuation-dominated'' few-electron regime ($d\gg\tildeΔ$). A wave-function analysis shows in detail how the BCS limit is recovered for $d\ll \tilde Δ$, and how for $d \gg \tilde Δ$ pairing correlations become delocalized in energy space. An earlier grand-canonical prediction for an observable parity effect in the spectral gaps is found to survive the fixed-N projection.

# Fixed-N Superconductivity: The Crossover from the Bulk to the Few-Electron Limit

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a variationally optimized fixed-N projected BCS (PBCS) wavefunction provides the first full canonical description of the entire crossover from bulk BCS superconductivity to the fluctuation-dominated few-electron regime in ultrasmall metallic grains, showing that the grand-canonical prediction for an observable parity effect in the spectral gaps survives the fixed-N projection. However, the "breakdown of superconductivity" found in grand-canonical studies is not as complete in the canonical case, the system can always gain energy from pairing correlations even for arbitrarily large level spacing, though a remnant of the breakdown survives as a marked change in behavior at a characteristic level spacing.

## Method

The authors model the superconducting grain using a reduced BCS Hamiltonian with uniformly spaced single-particle levels and a pairing interaction within the Debye cutoff. They construct variational ground states by projecting BCS-type wavefunctions onto fixed electron number N (up to N ≤ 600) using an integral over a phase variable, following the approach of Dietrich, Mang and Pradal (1964) developed for shell-model nuclei. The resulting coupled variational equations are solved numerically using fast Fourier transform routines, and the method is extended to states with unpaired electrons by blocking specific levels. The PBCS results are compared with grand-canonical BCS calculations and with exact numerical diagonalization results of Mastellone, Falci and Fazio (1998) for small systems.

## Key numbers

- The PBCS results for ground state energies of even and odd grains agree with exact numerical diagonalization results of Mastellone, Falci and Fazio (1998) to within 1% for n₀ ≤ 12 (few-electron regime)
- The characteristic level spacing for the even case is dᶜ₀ = 0.5 Δ̃ (where Δ̃ is the bulk gap)
- The characteristic level spacing for the odd case is dᶜ₁ = 0.25 Δ̃
- The dimensionless coupling strength used is λ = 0.22, close to that of aluminum
- The number of electrons within the Debye cutoff is N = 2ω_D/d, with calculations performed for N ≤ 600
- In the bulk BCS limit, the condensation energy approaches the standard result -Δ̃²/(2d)
- The parity parameter Δ_ML in the large-d limit follows Δ_ML = d/(2 log(α d/Δ̃)) with fitting parameter α = 1.35

## Context

This work extends the theoretical treatment of ultrasmall superconducting grains beyond the grand-canonical approaches of Golubev and Zaikin (1994), von Delft et al. (1996), and Braun et al. (1997, 1998), which could not properly describe the crossover regime where mean level spacing d is comparable to the bulk gap Δ̃. It also goes beyond the fixed-N exact diagonalization study of Mastellone, Falci and Fazio (1998), which was limited to N ≤ 25 and could only partially probe the crossover via parameter rescaling. The PBCS approach enables a full description of the crossover and shows that the qualitative difference between "superconductivity" (d < dᶜ) and "fluctuations" (d > dᶜ) is that for the former but not the latter, adding more particles gives a different condensation energy, as Anderson put it, "more is different." The results are directly relevant to interpreting the experiments of Ralph, Black and Tinkham (1997) on individual ultrasmall superconducting grains.
