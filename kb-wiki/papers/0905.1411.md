# Hamiltonian light-front field theory in a basis function approach

> Hamiltonian light-front quantum field theory constitutes a framework for the non-perturbative solution of invariant masses and correlated parton amplitudes of self-bound systems. By choosing the light-front gauge and adopting a basis function representation, we obtain a large, sparse, Hamiltonian matrix for mass eigenstates of gauge theories that is solvable by adapting the ab initio no-core methods of nuclear many-body theory. Full covariance is recovered in the continuum limit, the infinite matrix limit. There is considerable freedom in the choice of the orthonormal and complete set of basis functions with convenience and convergence rates providing key considerations. Here, we use a two-dimensional harmonic oscillator basis for transverse modes that corresponds with eigensolutions of the soft-wall AdS/QCD model obtained from light-front holography. We outline our approach and present illustrative features of some non-interacting systems in a cavity. We illustrate the first steps towards solving QED by obtaining the mass eigenstates of an electron in a cavity in small basis spaces and discuss the computational challenges.

## Key claim

The authors propose a "Basis Light Front Quantized" (BLFQ) approach that combines a two-dimensional harmonic oscillator basis for transverse modes with a discretized longitudinal momentum basis, enabling the non-perturbative solution of light-front Hamiltonian field theories by adapting ab initio no-core methods from nuclear many-body theory, with the caveat that full covariance is only recovered in the infinite matrix (continuum) limit.

## Method

The authors construct single-particle basis states as products of two-dimensional harmonic oscillator wavefunctions for transverse modes (characterized by principal quantum number n and orbital quantum number m) and discretized plane-wave longitudinal modes with periodic or antiperiodic boundary conditions. They impose cutoffs on the total light-front momentum K and the maximum total transverse oscillator quanta N_max, and apply symmetry constraints including total charge Z, total azimuthal quantum number M_t, and total spin projection S. The Hamiltonian matrix for mass eigenstates is then solved using techniques adapted from the No Core Shell Model (NCSM) and No Core Full Configuration (NCFC) methods of nuclear physics.

## Key numbers

* Ground state energy of 12C calculated with JISP16 interaction: extrapolated value agrees with experiment within approximately 2 MeV uncertainty, obtained from matrices up to 600 million basis states at N_max=8 and near 8 billion at N_max=10
* For non-interacting QED in a cavity with K=6 and N_max=8-23, the total many-parton basis dimensions range from 1,400 states (N_max=8, Z=0) to 142,013 states (N_max=23, Z=0)
* For non-interacting QED with Z=0 and K=N_max=12, the total many-parton basis contains 8,422,971 states, with the largest Fock sector having 2,806,624 states (2 fermion-antifermion pairs and 6 bosons)
* The meson mass spectrum from the soft-wall AdS/QCD model follows M^2 = 4 kappa^2 (n + L) for spin-0 constituents and M^2 = 4 kappa^2 (n + L + 1/2) for spin-1 constituents

## Context

This work extends the Hamiltonian light-front field theory approach previously developed in discretized momentum basis by Brodsky, Pauli, and Pinsky (1998) and in transverse lattice approaches by Burkardt and Dalley (2002). The authors adapt the ab initio no-core methods of nuclear many-body theory (NCSM and NCFC) that have successfully solved light nuclei with realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions, as demonstrated for 12C by Maris et al. (2008). The choice of two-dimensional harmonic oscillator basis is motivated by its correspondence with eigensolutions of the soft-wall AdS/QCD model from light-front holography, as developed by de Teramond and Brodsky (2008). This approach enables future non-perturbative calculations of hadron masses, form factors, and parton distributions in QCD, with the immediate next step being the inclusion of interactions and renormalization to demonstrate convergence with respect to cutoffs.
