# Nuclear matrix elements calculation for $0νββ$ decay of $^{124}$Sn using nonclosure approach in nuclear shell model

> In this study, we calculate the nuclear matrix elements (NMEs) for the light neutrino-exchange mechanism of neutrinoless double beta $0νββ$) decay of $^{124}$Sn within the framework of the interacting nuclear shell model using the effective shell model Hamiltonian GCN5082. A novel method based on a nonclosure approach is employed, wherein for the intermediate nucleus $^{124}$Sb, effects of energy of 100 states for each $J_{k}^π$=$0^{+}$ to $11^{+}$ and $2^{-}$ to $9^{-}$ ($ΔJ_{k}$=1) are explicitly included in the NMEs calculation. Other common effects such as the finite size of nucleons, higher-order effects of nucleon currents, and short-range correlations (SRC) of nucleons are also taken into account. The extracted optimal closure energy is 2.9 MeV for a total NME of $^{124}$Sn $0νββ$ decay, which is independent of different forms of SRC parametrizations. A comparison of NMEs and half-lives with some of the recent calculations is presented. Further, to gain a comprehensive understanding of the role of nuclear structure on the $0νββ$ decay, the dependence of NMEs on spin-parity of the intermediate states, coupled spin-parity of neutrons and protons, and the number of intermediate states, is explored. It is observed that the inclusion of the effects of excitation energies of the intermediate nucleus yields more reliable NMEs. The present findings provide valuable insights for experimental investigations of $0νββ$ decay of $^{124}$Sn in India and elsewhere.

## Key claim

The nonclosure approach yields a total nuclear matrix element (NME) of 2.38 for the light neutrino-exchange mechanism of $0\nu\beta\beta$ decay in $^{124}$Sn using the GCN5082 Hamiltonian and CD-Bonn short-range correlations, which is about 10% larger than the earlier shell-model closure result of 2.17 from PhysRevC.93.024308 but about 10% smaller than the 2.62 from Menendez (2008); the extracted optimal closure energy of 2.9 MeV is independent of the short-range correlation parametrization.

## Method

The authors performed nuclear shell-model calculations using the GCN5082 effective Hamiltonian in the jj55 model space, diagonalizing with the KSHELL code to obtain wave functions and energies for the initial nucleus $^{124}$Sn, the intermediate nucleus $^{124}$Sb, and the final nucleus $^{124}$Te. They employed a running nonclosure method that explicitly includes the excitation energies of 100 intermediate states for each spin-parity $J_k^\pi$ from $0^+$ to $11^+$ and $2^-$ to $9^-$ (with $\Delta J_k = 1$) in the neutrino potential, and incorporated finite nucleon size, higher-order currents, and short-range correlations via Jastrow functions with three parametrizations (Miller-Spencer, CD-Bonn, AV18).

## Key numbers

- Total NME $M^{0\nu} = 2.38$ for CD-Bonn SRC with nonclosure approach, using $g_A = 1.270$.
- Predicted half-life lower bound: $T_{1/2}^{0\nu} = 7.49 \times 10^{26}$ years (for $\langle m_{\beta\beta}\rangle / m_e = 10^{-7}$).
- Optimal closure energy for total NME: 2.9 MeV, independent of SRC parametrization.
- Fermi NME optimal closure energy: 3.1 MeV; Gamow-Teller NME optimal closure energy: 2.7 MeV; tensor NME optimal closure energy: 0 MeV.
- For CD-Bonn SRC with nonclosure: $M_F = -0.564$, $M_{GT} = 2.012$, $M_T = 0.015$.
- $2\nu\beta\beta$ decay NME $M_{GT}^{2\nu} = 0.054$ MeV$^{-1}$, giving half-life $T_{1/2}^{2\nu} = 0.95 \times 10^{21}$ years.

## Context

This work extends the nonclosure approach, previously applied to lower-mass nuclei such as $^{48}$Ca (PhysRevC.88.064312; Sarkar 2020), $^{76}$Ge, and $^{82}$Se, to the higher-mass isotope $^{124}$Sn for the first time in the shell model. The results are compared with earlier shell-model closure calculations by PhysRevC.93.024308 and Menendez (2008), as well as with GCM, IBM-2, QRPA, EDF, and REDF models; the NMEs from different many-body methods vary widely (2.15 to 5.30), and the present work aims to improve reliability for the TIN.TIN experiment at the India-based Neutrino Observatory.
