# Interpretation of the nonextensitivity parameter $q$ in some applications of Tsallis statistics and Lévy distributions

## Key claim

For the case $q>1$, the nonextensitivity parameter $q$ in Tsallis statistics (also the index of the corresponding Lévy distribution) is entirely determined by the relative variance of fluctuations of the parameter $1/\lambda$ in the underlying exponential distribution, specifically $q-1 = \omega$, where $\omega$ is the relative variance of those fluctuations.

## Method

The authors start from the observation that a Lévy distribution $G_q(x)$ can be represented as an integral over an exponential distribution $g(x;\lambda) \sim \exp(-x/\lambda)$ weighted by a distribution $f(1/\lambda)$ of the inverse scale parameter. Using the Euler gamma function representation, they show that $f(1/\lambda)$ must be a gamma distribution. They then derive this gamma distribution from a Langevin equation with white Gaussian noise, solving the corresponding Fokker-Planck equation to obtain the stationary distribution of the fluctuating variable.

## Key numbers

- For cosmic ray hadron interaction depths, the Lévy distribution fit gives $q = 1.3$, corresponding to a relative variance of cross-section fluctuations $\omega = 0.3$ when using a gamma distribution for fluctuations.
- For the same cosmic ray data, a Gaussian fluctuation model gave $\omega' = 0.2$, which lies within the inequality bounds $0.18 < \omega' < 0.3$ derived for $q=1.3$.
- For transverse momentum distributions in heavy ion collisions, the nonextensive parameter is $q \simeq 1.015$, which implies relative temperature fluctuations $\Delta T/T \simeq 0.12$.
- The relative variance $\omega$ of the gamma distribution is given by $\omega = 1/\alpha = q - 1$, where $\alpha = 1/(q-1)$.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis of cosmic ray cascades (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 1999) where Lévy distributions with $q=1.3$ described the "long flying component," and their earlier analysis (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 1997) which used cross-section fluctuations. It provides a physical interpretation for the $q$ parameter in Tsallis statistics as arising from fluctuations of inverse temperature or cross-section, relevant to ongoing discussions of temperature fluctuations in nuclear collisions (Landau and Lifshitz, 1980; Stodolsky, 1995). This interpretation is important for understanding systematics in heavy ion collision data and the search for the Quark Gluon Plasma.
