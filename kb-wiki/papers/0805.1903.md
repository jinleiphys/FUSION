# Coupled-channels calculations of $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O fusion

> Fusion data for $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O are analyzed by coupled-channels calculations. It is shown that the calculated cross sections are sensitive to the couplings to the $2^+$ and $3^-$ excitation channels even at low energies, where these channels are closed. The sensitivity to the ion-ion potential is investigated by applying a conventional Woods-Saxon potential and the M3Y+repulsion potential, consisting of the M3Y double-folding potential and a repulsive term that simulates the effect of the nuclear incompressibility. The best overall fit to the data is obtained with a M3Y+repulsion potential which produces a shallow potential in the entrance channel. The stepwise increase in measured fusion cross sections at high energies is also consistent with such a shallow potential. The steps are correlated with overcoming the barriers for the angular momenta $L$ = 12, 14, 16, and 18. To improve the fit to the low-energy data requires a shallower potential and this causes a even stronger hindrance of fusion at low energies. It is therefore difficult, based on the existing fusion data, to make an accurate extrapolation to energies that are of interest to astrophysics.

## Key claim

Coupled-channels calculations of 16O+16O fusion are sensitive to couplings to the 2+ and 3- excitation channels even at low energies where these channels are closed, but the overall quality of fit to the existing fusion data is not very sensitive to the ion-ion potential at short distances, making accurate extrapolation to astrophysical energies difficult.

## Method

Coupled-channels calculations were performed in the rotating frame approximation using ingoing-wave boundary conditions (IWBC) imposed at the minimum of the pocket in the entrance channel potential. Two types of ion-ion potentials were compared: a standard Woods-Saxon potential with parameters adjusted to fit the fusion data, and an M3Y+repulsion potential consisting of the M3Y double-folding potential plus a repulsive term that simulates the effect of nuclear incompressibility. The calculations included couplings to the 2+ and 3- excited states of 16O, and at high energies a weak short-ranged imaginary potential was added near the minimum of the potential pocket.

## Key numbers

- The best-fit Woods-Saxon potential parameters: V0 = -42.14 MeV, Rpot = 6.083 fm, a = 0.602 fm, producing a Coulomb barrier of 10.10 MeV
- The best-fit M3Y+repulsion potential (with arep = 0.41 fm) produces a pocket at 2.4 MeV and a Coulomb barrier of 10.01 MeV
- The coupled-channels calculation with the Woods-Saxon potential yields chi-squared per data point (chi2/N) = 1.5; the no-coupling limit with adjusted radius (Rpot = 6.133 fm) yields chi2/N = 4.2
- The M3Y+repulsion potential with arep = 0.41 fm yields chi2/N = 1.3
- The nuclear incompressibility used was K = 234 MeV
- The measured RMS charge radius of 16O used for density input: 2.737(8) fm
- The stepwise increase in high-energy fusion cross sections is correlated with overcoming barriers for angular momenta L = 12, 14, 16, and 18
- The S factor from the best-fit M3Y+repulsion calculation develops a maximum near 4 MeV center-of-mass energy

## Context

This work extends the coupled-channels approach previously applied to medium-heavy systems (such as 64Ni+64Ni, 28Si+64Ni, and 16O+208Pb) to the light 16O+16O system, using the M3Y+repulsion potential developed by the author and collaborators (Esbensen and Misicu, 2007). The analysis contradicts the earlier adiabatic TDHF calculation by Reinhard et al. (1984), which predicted an S factor that keeps increasing with decreasing energy, while the present calculations show a maximum near 4 MeV. The results are in qualitative agreement with the empirical extrapolation method proposed by Jiang et al. (2007) for low-energy fusion hindrance. The work enables future experimental measurements down to 5-6 MeV to confirm the predicted hindrance, and suggests the need for model extensions (as proposed by Ichikawa, 2007) to describe fusion at even lower energies where the ground state of 32S is accessible.
