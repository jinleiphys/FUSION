# Solution of the Nuclear Shell Model by Symmetry-Dictated Truncation

> The dynamical symmetries of the Fermion Dynamical Symmetry Model are used as a principle of truncation for the spherical shell model. Utilizing the usual principle of energy-dictated truncation to select a valence space, and symmetry-dictated truncation to select a collective subspace of that valence space, we are able to reduce the full shell model space to one of manageable dimensions with modern supercomputers, even for the heaviest nuclei. The resulting shell model then consists of diagonalizing an effective Hamiltonian within the restricted subspace. This theory is not confined to any symmetry limits, and represents a full solution of the original shell model if the appropriate effective interaction of the truncated space can be determined. As a first step in constructing that interaction, we present an empirical determination of its matrix elements for the collective subspace with no broken pairs in a representative set of nuclei with $130\le A \le 250$. We demonstrate that this effective interaction can be parameterized in terms of a few quantities varying slowly with particle number, and is capable of describing a broad range of low-energy observables for these nuclei. Finally we give a brief discussion of extending these methods to include a single broken collective pair.

## Key claim
The Fermion Dynamical Symmetry Model (FDSM) uses dynamical symmetries as a truncation principle to reduce the full nuclear shell model space to manageable dimensions for heavy nuclei, but the authors hedge that this represents a full solution of the original shell model only if the appropriate effective interaction of the truncated space can be determined.

## Method
The authors impose a symmetry-dictated truncation within a valence space selected by energy-dictated truncation, using the generalized Ginocchio coupling scheme to define collective S and D pair subspaces. They diagonalize an effective Hamiltonian within the heritage-0 (no broken pairs) subspace using the FDU0 code, which treats the most general interaction consistent with the highest shell symmetry (SO8 or Sp6) of the valence space. The effective interaction matrix elements are determined empirically by fitting to experimental data for nuclei with mass numbers between 130 and 250.

## Key numbers
- The most general Sp6 x Sp6 Hamiltonian of the FDSM requires at most about 100 effective interaction parameters for the actinide region, compared to approximately 2500 matrix elements needed for a literal transcription of the empirical sd shell approach to the same region.
- For spectra depending only on energy differences, there are at most 11 parameters for SO8 x SO8, 8 for Sp6 x Sp6, and 9 for SO8 x Sp6; in practice the authors use a simplified Hamiltonian with 5 or fewer free parameters.
- The FDSM mass calculations for the heaviest elements (Z=110-111) were published before their discovery and represent a pure prediction of the theory.
- The FDSM predicts a superheavy island of stability shifted to lower neutron numbers than classical calculations, with maximal shell stabilization at a different location than the traditional predicted island.

## Context
This work extends the Fermion Dynamical Symmetry Model developed by Wu et al. (1994) and the Ginocchio S-D pair coupling scheme (1980) beyond dynamical symmetry limits toward quantitative shell model calculations. It contrasts with energy-dictated truncation approaches and mean-field methods, and offers a microscopic alternative to the Interacting Boson Model and nuclear supersymmetry for unifying even and odd nuclei. The results enable systematic shell model calculations for heavy nuclei including superheavy elements, with the authors predicting that the traditional island of superheavy elements does not exist and that the r-process path may run closer to the stability valley than previously assumed.
