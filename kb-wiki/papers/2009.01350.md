# Jet fragmentation function in heavy-ion collisions

> We study the fragmentation function of jets propagating through a dense quark-gluon plasma within perturbative QCD. Our results for its nuclear modification factor are in qualitative agreement with the experimental data in Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC. In particular, we reproduce the enhancements seen in the data at both relatively soft and relatively large transverse momenta, with clear physical interpretations. The perturbative predictions however are quite sensitive to the value of the infrared cutoff mimicking the confinement scale, due to the fact that the fragmentation function is not an infrared safe quantity. To remedy this, we propose a new observable -- the (primary) subjet fragmentation function -- which is infrared safe and has features similar to the fragmentation function. We provide predictions for this observable in the vacuum and in heavy-ion collisions that could be tested against the experimental data.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the nuclear modification factor for the standard jet fragmentation function in Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC can be reproduced qualitatively within perturbative QCD, but the predictions are highly sensitive to the infrared cutoff that mimics the confinement scale because the fragmentation function is not infrared safe. To address this, the authors propose a new infrared-safe observable, the primary subjet fragmentation function, which retains sensitivity to medium effects while being under better perturbative control.

## Method

The authors use a Monte Carlo parton shower that implements a factorized picture of jet evolution in a quark-gluon plasma, separating vacuum-like emissions (VLEs) and medium-induced emissions (MIEs). The evolution is divided into three steps: a cascade of VLEs inside the medium satisfying specific kinematic constraints, propagation of each parton through the medium sourcing MIEs, and a final cascade of VLEs outside the medium. The standard fragmentation function is defined from hadrons inside jets, while the new subjet fragmentation function is constructed by declustering jets with the Cambridge/Aachen algorithm along the hardest branch and counting subjets with transverse momentum relative to the branch above a chosen cutoff \(k_{\perp,\rm cut}\).

## Key numbers

- The quenching parameter \(\hat{q}\) and the jet path length \(L\) are varied across 4 sets of values that all reproduce the ATLAS data for the nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\) for jets (no explicit numerical values for \(\hat{q}\) or \(L\) are given in the text).
- The infrared cutoff \(k_{\perp,\rm min}\) used to regulate collinear divergences in the parton-level calculation is varied; the envelope of variations in the ratio \(\mathcal{R}(x)\) is large, with the text showing that \(k_{\perp,\rm min}\) variations dominate the uncertainty.
- For the subjet fragmentation function, the cutoff is set to \(k_{\perp,\rm cut}=2\) GeV, with unphysical parameters fixed to \(\theta_{\rm max}=1\) and \(k_{\perp,\rm min}=250\) MeV.
- The multiple branching scale \(\omega_{\rm br} \equiv \alpha_s^2 \hat{q} L^2\) is the typical scale for energy loss at large angles.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous framework for jet evolution in a quark-gluon plasma (Caucal et al., 2018; Caucal et al., 2019) and provides a perturbative QCD interpretation of the enhancements seen in the ATLAS data (Aaboud, 2018) for the fragmentation function at both small \(x\) and \(x\) close to 1. The small-\(x\) enhancement is attributed to amplified soft gluon production from angular ordering violation and semi-hard MIEs, differing from explanations involving medium response (Casalderrey-Solana et al., 2016; Tachibana et al., 2017; Kunnawalkam Elayavalli et al., 2017). The large-\(x\) enhancement is explained as a bias toward hard-fragmenting jets induced by the steeply falling jet spectrum, a mechanism that also correlates with the jet nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\). The new subjet fragmentation function, being infrared safe, enables cleaner comparisons with data and can be used to constrain theoretical models beyond what is possible with \(R_{AA}\) alone.
