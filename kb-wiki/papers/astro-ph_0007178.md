# Stellar production rates of carbon and its abundance in the universe

> The bulk of the carbon in our universe is produced in the triple-alpha process in helium-burning red giant stars. We calculated the change of the triple-alpha reaction rate in a microscopic 12-nucleon model of the C-12 nucleus and looked for the effects of minimal variations of the strengths of the underlying interactions. Stellar model calculations were performed with the alternative reaction rates. Here, we show that outside a narrow window of 0.5 and 4% of the values of the strong and Coulomb forces, respectively, the stellar production of carbon or oxygen is reduced by factors of 30 to 1000.

## Key claim

The stellar production of carbon or oxygen is reduced by factors of 30 to 1000 when the strength of the strong interaction varies by more than 0.5% or the Coulomb force varies by more than 4% from their standard values, based on microscopic calculations of the triple-alpha reaction rate and stellar evolution models.

## Method

The authors calculated the resonance energy and radiative width of the Hoyle state (0₂⁺ resonance in ¹²C) using a microscopic 12-nucleon, three-alpha cluster model with several effective nucleon-nucleon interactions (Minnesota and modified Hasegawa-Nagata forces). They varied the overall strength of the strong interaction by a factor p between 0.994 and 1.006 and the fine structure constant for the Coulomb force, then used the resulting triple-alpha reaction rates in stellar evolution calculations for stars of 1.3, 5, and 20 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- The Hoyle state resonance energy is ε = (379.47 ± 0.18) keV, total width Γ = (8.3 ± 1) eV, and radiative width Γ_γ = (3.7 ± 0.5) meV.
- Outside a window of 0.5% variation in the strong interaction strength and 4% variation in the Coulomb force strength, stellar carbon or oxygen production is reduced by factors of 30 to 1000.
- Stellar model calculations were performed for stars of masses 1.3, 5, and 20 solar masses (M_⊙).
- The ⁸Be ground state has a lifetime of about 10⁻¹⁶ s.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Livio, Hollowell, Weiss, and Truran (1989) that only considered hypothetical ad hoc shifts of the Hoyle state resonance energy, by instead calculating the resonance energy from first-principles variations of the fundamental forces. The results constrain the allowed strengths of the strong and electromagnetic forces relevant to the anthropic cosmological principle and provide tighter constraints on quark masses than those from light nuclei, as discussed by Hogan (1999) and Jeltema and Sher (2000).
