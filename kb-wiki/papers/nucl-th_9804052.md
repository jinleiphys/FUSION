# Pre-Equilibrium Evolution of QCD Plasma :An Appraisal

> We discuss, in this review, the existing models for the production of QGP in Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions. We shall try to give a brief description of the models available for the pre-equilibrium production of QGP, ranging from soft process dominated QCD regime to the pQCD dominated regime. We also present the estimates for the initial energy density, the temperature and the thermalisation time -- available from each of these studies. Finally, we shall address the questions regarding thermalisation, color equilibration and chemical equilibration of the produced QGP in different experimental facilities.

# Pre-Equilibrium Evolution of QCD Plasma: An Appraisal

## Key claim

This review argues that the pre-equilibrium phase of Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) production in relativistic heavy ion collisions must be understood through a combination of non-perturbative (flux tube) and perturbative (parton cascade) mechanisms, with the dominant production process depending on the collision energy scale. The author emphasizes that while the abstract presents a survey of available models, the actual estimates for initial energy density, temperature, and thermalisation time vary significantly across approaches and depend critically on assumptions about quark masses and field strengths.

## Method

The paper reviews three complementary frameworks for QGP production: the non-perturbative flux tube model based on Schwinger pair production in strong chromo-electric fields, the perturbative parton cascade model using jet cross sections with saturation criteria, and the McLerran-Venugopalan model for classical gluon fields from color charge fluctuations. For each framework, the author presents the relevant kinetic equations (Boltzmann-Vlasov with source terms, color hydrodynamic equations, and classical Yang-Mills equations) and discusses numerical solutions for the space-time evolution of the plasma.

## Key numbers

- For the flux tube model with constituent quark mass (0.5 GeV), the peak initial temperature reaches approximately 800 MeV, well above the critical energy density required for plasma formation
- For the flux tube model with constituent quark mass, the half-life of the electric field is approximately 4 to 5 fm; for current quark mass, the half-life is approximately 240 fm
- In the Boltzmann-Vlasov description with Schwinger-like source term and initial field strength E₀² = 20 GeV/fm³, the initial temperature at thermalisation varies between 225 and 250 MeV
- For the Self Screened Parton Cascade Model at RHIC (Gold on Gold): initial time τᵢ = 0.25 fm/c, initial temperature Tᵢ = 0.668 GeV, initial energy density εᵢ = 61.4 GeV/fm³
- For the Self Screened Parton Cascade Model at LHC: initial time τᵢ = 0.25 fm/c, initial temperature Tᵢ = 1.02 GeV, initial energy density εᵢ = 425 GeV/fm³
- For RHIC (LHC) gluons, initial temperature estimated at 500 MeV (660 MeV); for quarks at RHIC (LHC), 200 MeV (260 MeV); thermalisation time for gluons varies from 0.3 to 0.25 fm/c
- The saturation criteria gives p₀ ≈ 2 GeV for LHC and p₀ ≈ 1 GeV for RHIC for Pb on Pb collision at zero impact parameter
- The mean transverse energy of gluons from the parton cascade model is approximately 3 GeV with initial temperature approximately 1.1 GeV

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes earlier work on QGP production mechanisms, including the flux tube model of Low (1975) and Nussinov (1975), the Schwinger mechanism studies by Ganguly, Kaw, and Parikh (1993), and the parton cascade approach of Eskola and collaborators. The paper also discusses the McLerran-Venugopalan model (1994) for classical gluon fields and the color kinetic equations developed by Elze, Gyulassy, and Vasak. The review enables future work by identifying open questions about color equilibration versus thermal equilibration, the role of chaotic dynamics in equilibration, and the need for fully nonlinear solutions of the color kinetic equations. The author notes that at RHIC energies the system is not expected to reach chemical equilibrium, while at LHC energies it may come close.
