# Ground State and Saddle Point: masses and deformations for even-even superheavy nuclei with 98 < Z < 126 and 134< N < 192

> We determine ground-state and saddle-point shapes and masses of even-even superheavy nuclei in the range of proton numbers $98\leq Z \leq 126$ and neutron numbers $134\leq N \leq 192$. Our study is performed within the microscopic-macroscopic method. The Strutinsky shell and pairing correction is calculated for the deformed Woods-Saxon single-particle potential and the Yukawa-plus-exponential energy is taken as a smooth part. We use parameters of the model that were fitted previously to this region of nuclei. A high-dimensional deformation space, including nonaxial and reflection-asymmetric shapes, is used in the search for saddle points. Both ground-state and saddle-point shapes are found with the aid of the minimization procedure, with dynamical programming technique of search for saddle points. The results are collected in two tables. Calculated ground-state mass-excess, $Q_{α$ energies, total and macroscopic energies normalized to the macroscopic energy at the spherical shape, shell corrections (including pairing) and deformations are given for each nucleus in the table one. The second table gives the same properties, but at the saddle-point configuration. The obtained results are discussed and compared with available experimental data for alpha-decay energies ($Q_α$) and ground-state masses.

## Key claim

The paper provides systematic predictions of ground-state and saddle-point masses and deformations for even-even superheavy nuclei across a wide range (Z=98-126, N=134-192) using the microscopic-macroscopic method, but the authors acknowledge that they cannot be absolutely certain that the minimization procedure in the second step of their saddle-point-search does not lead to errors, and that the results depend on the assumption that the initial deformation net may be sufficient.

## Method

The total nuclear binding energy was calculated as a sum of a macroscopic part (Yukawa-plus-exponential liquid-drop energy) and a microscopic part (Strutinsky shell correction plus BCS pairing correction based on a deformed Woods-Saxon single-particle potential). Ground-state energies were minimized in a 7-dimensional axial deformation space (β20, β30, β40, β50, β60, β70, β80) using a conjugate gradient method, while saddle points were found via a two-step procedure: first on a 3D grid (β20, β22, β40) using dynamic programming, then minimizing over the remaining 7 deformation degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

- The biggest shell effect of approximately 9 MeV is observed for 270Hs (Z=108, N=162), the semi-magic nucleus
- A second minimum of the shell correction of approximately 7 MeV is located around Z=114 and N=184
- The macroscopic energy parameters fitted to experimental masses of even-even heaviest nuclei with Z≥84 are: av = 16.0643, κv = 1.9261, a0 = 17.926
- The surface energy parameters are as = 21.13 MeV and κs = 2.30
- The r.m.s. deviation of ground-state masses from the best available theoretical global mass calculations is approximately 500-800 keV
- The difference in barrier height between the dynamic programming method and the imaginary water flow method does not exceed 110 keV (tested for Z=108, N=166 and Z=112, N=174)
- Superdeformed oblate (SDO) shapes with β20 ≈ -0.45 appear for some Z≈120 nuclei

## Context

This work extends previous microscopic-macroscopic calculations of ground-state masses by Moller et al. (1995) and the macroscopic-microscopic approach of Munian et al. (2001) to a systematic study of both ground-state and saddle-point properties for superheavy nuclei. The results provide necessary theoretical saddle-point data for estimating cross sections and survival probabilities in the synthesis of superheavy nuclei, which were previously much rarer than ground-state predictions. The calculations enable future comparisons with experimental alpha-decay energies and ground-state masses from Dubna, GSI, and LBL Berkeley experiments, and can help interpret experiments on elements Z=107-118.
