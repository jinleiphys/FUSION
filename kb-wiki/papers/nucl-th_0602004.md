# Uncertainties in the $0νββ$--decay nuclear matrix elements

> The nuclear matrix elements $M^{0ν}$ of the neutrinoless double beta decay ($0νββ$) of most nuclei with known $2νββ$-decay rates are systematically evaluated using the Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) and Renormalized QRPA (RQRPA). The experimental $2νββ$-decay rate is used to adjust the most relevant parameter, the strength of the particle-particle interaction. With such procedure the $M^{0ν}$ values become essentially independent on single-particle basis size, the axial vector quenching factor, etc. Theoretical arguments in favor of the adopted way of determining the interaction parameters are presented. It is suggested that most of the spread among the published $M^{0ν}$'s can be ascribed to the choices of implicit and explicit parameters, inherent to the QRPA method.

# Uncertainties in the $0νββ$--decay nuclear matrix elements

## Key claim

By adjusting the particle-particle interaction strength $g_{pp}$ to reproduce the experimental $2νββ$-decay rate, the calculated $0νββ$ nuclear matrix elements become essentially independent of the single-particle basis size, the choice of nucleon-nucleon potential, and the axial vector quenching factor, contrary to the common claim that these matrix elements vary substantially across QRPA calculations.

## Method

The authors used the Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) and its Renormalized QRPA (RQRPA) variant to compute $0νββ$ nuclear matrix elements for nine nuclei with known $2νββ$-decay rates. For each nucleus, three single-particle bases of different sizes (from $1\hbar\omega$ to about $4\hbar\omega$ excitations) were employed, with single-particle energies from a Woods-Saxon potential and the Bonn-CD $G$-matrix as the residual interaction. The critical parameter $g_{pp}$ was adjusted separately for each basis so that the calculated $2νββ$ half-life matched the experimental value, and the resulting $0νββ$ matrix elements were averaged over the three basis choices with their variances computed.

## Key numbers

- For most nuclei, the ratio of matrix elements calculated with $g_A = 1.25$ versus $g_A = 1.0$ differs by only about 20%, rather than the naive factor of $(1.25/1.0)^2 = 1.56$ expected from the $g_A^2$ scaling.
- Neglecting short-range correlations (s.r.c.) would lead to a twofold increase in $M^{0ν}$ for realistic $g_{pp}$ values.
- The higher-order terms of the nucleon current (induced pseudoscalar and weak magnetism) cause about a 30% reduction in $M^{0ν}$.
- For $^{100}$Mo, the $1^-$ multipole becomes the strongest contributor when all effects (s.r.c., induced currents) are included, while the $2^-$ multipole dominates when they are neglected.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier studies (Rodin 2003, Rodin 2006) by systematically applying the $g_{pp}$-adjustment procedure to all nuclei with known $2νββ$ rates, and provides theoretical justification for why this method removes the parameter dependence that plagued earlier QRPA calculations. It directly contradicts the conclusions of Civitarese and Suhonen (2003) and Suhonen (2004), who argued that $0νββ$ matrix elements vary substantially with model choices; the authors attribute those differences to the neglect of short-range correlations and higher-order nucleon currents, and to the alternative choice of fixing $g_{pp}$ from single $β$-decay rather than $2νββ$-decay. The work enables more reliable extraction of the effective Majorana neutrino mass $\langle m_{ββ} \rangle$ from future $0νββ$ decay measurements by providing matrix elements with quantified uncertainties.
