# Number of Particles in Fission Fragments

> In current simulations of fission, the number of protons and neutrons in a given fission fragment is almost always obtained by integrating the total density of particles in the sector of space that contains the fragment. Because of the antisymmetry of the many-body wave function of the whole nucleus, this procedure systematically gives noninteger numbers of particles in the fragments. We introduce a novel sampling method to estimate rigorously the probability of finding $Z$ protons and $N$ neutrons in a fission fragment without resorting to projectors, which can sometimes give unwieldy results. When applied on standard Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov many-body states, we show that our approach reproduces indeed the results of full particle-number projection. We then estimate the charge and mass number dispersion of several scission configurations in $^{240}$Pu with and without pairing correlations included. We show that odd-even effects in the charge probability naturally occur within our approach, which could explain the well-known odd-even staggering of charge distributions. Our method is applicable either in static calculations of scission configurations such as, e.g., in the macroscopic-microscopic approach or energy density functional theory, but also in explicitly time-dependent density functional theory simulations of fission.

## Key claim

The authors introduce a novel Monte Carlo sampling method to rigorously estimate the probability of finding integer numbers of protons and neutrons in fission fragments, which reproduces the results of full particle-number projection but also provides a many-body basis for each prefragment; however, the method only works exactly when the two fragments are well separated and the single-particle bases in each spatial sector are approximately orthonormal.

## Method

Given a spatial partition of the full space into two sectors corresponding to the two prefragments, the authors decompose each single-particle wavefunction into components localized in each sector. For the Monte Carlo approach, they sample occupation probabilities from the canonical basis of a Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov state, then for each sampled Slater determinant they draw random assignments of each single-particle state to either the left or right fragment based on the integrated density in each sector. They compare this sampling method against standard particle-number projection techniques extended to arbitrary spatial partitions, using Pfaffian methods to compute norm overlaps.

## Key numbers

- For validation, the authors test on a nucleus with Z=60 and N=70 using the code HFBTHO 3
- The Monte Carlo sampling requires on the order of a few thousand iterations for the probability distributions to converge
- The method is applied to scission configurations in $^{240}$Pu using both macroscopic-microscopic and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations

## Context

This work extends the projection techniques originally introduced by Simenel (2010) for Slater determinants and later extended by Scamps (2013) to superfluid systems, applying them to fission fragments. The method addresses the long-standing problem that integrating the one-body density over a spatial sector systematically gives noninteger particle numbers due to the antisymmetry of the many-body wavefunction. The approach enables estimation of fragment properties at scission beyond just charge and mass yields, such as level densities, and naturally produces odd-even staggering effects in charge distributions that could explain experimental observations.
