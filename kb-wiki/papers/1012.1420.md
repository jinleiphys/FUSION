# Magnetars From Magnetized Cores Created by a Strong Interaction Phase Transition

> We consider a model where the strong magnetic fields of magnetars arise from a high baryon density, magnetized core. In this framework magnetars are distinguished from pulsars by their higher masses and central density. For magnetars, as core densities exceed a threshold, the strong interaction induces a phase transition to a neutral pion condensate that aligns all magnetic moments. The core magnetic field is initially shielded by the ambient high conductivity plasma. With time the shielding currents dissipate transporting the core field out, first to the crust and then breaking through the crust to the surface of the star. Recent observations provide strong support for this model which accounts for several properties of magnetars and also enables us to identify new magnetars.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that magnetars are distinguished from pulsars by having higher masses and central densities that exceed a threshold, triggering a strong interaction phase transition to a neutral pion condensate that aligns all magnetic moments; the model requires that the core magnetic field is initially shielded by plasma and only emerges to the surface after a delay of order 10^4 years via dissipation of shielding currents.

## Method

The authors model the creation of a magnetized core via a neutral pion condensate phase transition in high-density neutron or quark matter, using the chiral sigma model framework of Dautry and Nyman (1978) and Baym (1977). They then apply ambipolar diffusion theory from Goldreich and Reisenegger (1992) to describe the transport of the shielded core magnetic field to the crust over timescales of 10^4-10^5 years, followed by crustal breakout that powers quiescent X-ray emission and flares. Observational support is drawn from timing data of magnetars and high magnetic field pulsars, including analysis of spin-down rates, supernova remnant ages, and post-glitch behavior of PSR J1846-0258.

## Key numbers

- Surface magnetic fields of magnetars are 10^14-10^15 G, compared to pulsar fields of 10^10-10^12 G
- Magnetar periods fall in a narrow window of 5-12 seconds
- Quiescent radiative luminosity of magnetars is 10^35-10^36 erg/s; flares release 10^42-10^44 erg, sometimes much higher
- Core magnetic fields can reach 10^16-10^17 G from aligned neutron magnetic moments
- For a core of 3 km radius with field 10^16 G, the surface magnetic field is approximately 10^15 G
- Energy released in the phase transition is approximately 30 MeV (4.5 x 10^-5 erg) per baryon
- Ambipolar diffusion timescale is 10^4 x B_16^-2 x T_8.5^-6 years, where B_16 is field in units of 10^16 G and T_8.5 is temperature in units of 10^8.5 K
- For PSR J1846-0258 (P=0.326 s, B=5 x 10^13 G), the frequency derivative changed from -6.3 x 10^-11 s^-2 pre-glitch to -7.55 x 10^-11 s^-2 immediately after, with an asymptotic post-glitch value of -6.65 x 10^-11 s^-2, corresponding to a surface magnetic field increase of 0.13 x 10^13 G over 2.5 years
- The braking index for J1846-0258 post-outburst is 2.16 +/- 0.13, compared to pre-outburst value of 2.65 +/- 0.01

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier proposal (Dass and Soni, 2009) that magnetars possess cores with large magnetic moment density created by strong interactions, and provides a detailed two-stage model for field emergence. It directly contradicts the dynamo model of Duncan and Thompson (1992) which requires millisecond initial periods for field amplification, and addresses observational discrepancies noted by Leahy and Ouyed (2008) regarding the systematic age mismatch between magnetar spin-down ages and supernova remnant ages. The model enables identification of new magnetar candidates among high magnetic field pulsars using a crossover criterion where X-ray luminosity exceeds rotational energy loss, and predicts additional neutrino emission during the ambipolar diffusion stage as a testable signature.
