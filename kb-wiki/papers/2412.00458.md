# Extension of the integrated hydrokinetic model to nuclear collision energies relevant for the RHIC Beam-Energy Scan program and the research program at GSI-FAIR

> The present work is devoted to developing the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) for relativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions. While the previous cycle of works on this topic focused on ultra-relativistic collisions at the top RHIC and different LHC energies, the current work addresses relativistic collisions at the lower energies, specifically ranging from approximately 2 to 50 GeV per nucleon pair in the center-of-mass colliding system. In such collisions, the formation times for the initial state of dense matter can be up to three orders of magnitude longer than those in ultra-relativistic collisions. This difference reflects a fundamentally distinct nature and formation process, particularly regarding the possible stages of initial state evolution, including thermalization (which may be only partial at very low collision energies), subsequent hydrodynamic expansion, and the final transition of matter evolution into a hadronic cascade. These stages, which are fully realized in ultra-relativistic reactions, can also occur within the energy range of BES RHIC, albeit with distinct time scales. This publication not only advances the theoretical development of iHKM (referred to, if necessary, as the {\it{extended}} version of the integrated Hydrokinetic Model, iHKM{\it{e}}), but also provides examples of model applications for calculating observables. A systematic description across a wide range of experimental energies, which is preliminary yet quite satisfactory, for spectra, flow, and femtoscopy, will follow this study.

## Key claim

The paper extends the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) to relativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions at lower energies (approximately 2 to 50 GeV per nucleon pair in the center-of-mass system), where formation times for the initial state of dense matter can be up to three orders of magnitude longer than in ultra-relativistic collisions, and where thermalization may be only partial at very low collision energies.

## Method

The authors modify the iHKM framework by replacing the high-energy initial condition model (based on MC-GLAUBER and Color Glass Condensate) with quasiclassical UrQMD hadron-string cascade simulations for the initial pre-equilibrium dynamics at low and intermediate energies. The model implements a gradual thermalization stage where the total system is decomposed into a hydrodynamically evolving component and a non-thermalized UrQMD component, with a weight function P(tau) controlling the transition between them. The hydrodynamic evolution uses the vHLLE code with Israel-Stewart viscous hydrodynamics, and particlization (transition from fluid to particles) occurs at a fixed energy density using the Cornelius routine.

## Key numbers

- The energy range covered is approximately 2 to 50 GeV per nucleon pair in the center-of-mass colliding system.
- At BES RHIC energy of 14.5 AGeV, the overlapping time of colliding nuclei is 1.6 fm/c, compared to around 10^{-3} fm/c at 5.02 ATeV (LHC energy).
- The grid cell sizes used for the smearing kernel are 0.3 fm in transverse directions and 0.05 in longitudinal rapidity spacing.
- The smearing kernel stores 400 to 1000 particles (depending on collision energy sqrt(s_NN)) whose trajectories satisfy the selection criteria.
- The relaxation time parameter tau_rel is set at its minimum value tau_rel = tau_th - tau_0 in this paper.
- The equation of state used includes the chiral EOS with crossover-type transition and an EoS with first-order phase transition proposed in Kolb (2003).

## Context

This work extends the iHKM framework previously applied to ultra-relativistic collisions at top RHIC and LHC energies (Naboka 2014, Shapoval 2020) to the lower energy range relevant for the RHIC Beam-Energy Scan program, the HADES experiment at GSI, and the future CBM experiment at GSI-FAIR. The approach builds on earlier hybrid model work by Petersen (2008) that first used UrQMD as an initial stage for hydrodynamic simulations, and addresses the challenge that at these lower energies the system may not achieve complete thermalization. The model enables systematic investigation of whether quark-gluon plasma is created, where phase transitions occur, and whether a critical endpoint exists at certain collision energies, with subsequent publications planned for systematic comparisons with experimental data on spectra, flow, and femtoscopy.
