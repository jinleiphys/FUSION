# Femtoscopy for exotic hadrons and nuclei

> In high energy collision experiments with multiple hadron productions, the momentum distribution of the measured hadron pair shows a correlation due to the final state hadron interactions and the quantum statistics. In the past, this femtoscopy technique has been developed to extract the information of the emission source from the momentum correlation functions. Recently, correlation function measurement is utilized also as a new method to determine the hadron interactions. In fact, the ALICE collaboration at LHC measures the correlation functions with various hadron pairs, for which the standard scattering experiment is difficult, providing remarkable progress in the study of the hadron interactions. In this contribution, we introduce the theoretical method to calculate the correlation functions, and present recent results on the study of the antikaon-nucleon interactions and hyperon-nucleus interactions.

# Femtoscopy for exotic hadrons and nuclei

## Key claim

Femtoscopy, using momentum correlation functions of hadron pairs produced in high-energy collisions, can determine hadron-hadron interactions for systems inaccessible to traditional scattering experiments, provided the emission source function can be independently estimated; the technique achieves high precision at low relative momenta even for channels involving heavy quarks or unstable particles.

## Method

The Koonin-Pratt formula is used to compute the correlation function C(q) as an integral of the source function S(r) multiplied by the squared scattering wave function of the hadron pair. For coupled-channel systems such as K−p, an extended Koonin-Pratt formula is employed that sums over channel components with relative weight factors ωj, and the source function is calibrated using the well-known K+p interaction. Theoretical calculations incorporate channel coupling, isospin symmetry breaking, and Coulomb interactions, with the source size extracted from experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The K−p correlation function measured by ALICE in pp collisions at √s = 13 TeV shows a non-monotonic feature at relative momentum q ∼ 58 MeV, corresponding to the K̅0n threshold cusp.
- The theoretical K−p correlation function using the Kyoto potential with source size R = 0.9 fm agrees well with ALICE data; increasing the source size to R = 1.6 fm suppresses the correlation at low momenta.
- For Λα correlation functions calculated with a Gaussian source of R = 1 fm, the ordering of potentials by strength at r = 0 (U_Isle > U_Chi3 > U_LY-IV > U_SG) produces the opposite ordering in correlation functions (C_Isle < C_Chi3 < C_LY-IV < C_SG), demonstrating sensitivity to the repulsive core.
- The Λ(1520) resonance contributes a small peak around q ∼ 240 MeV in the K−p correlation function, parametrized by a Breit-Wigner form with PDG mass and width values.

## Context

This work extends the femtoscopy technique from its original use in source size extraction (Goldhaber 1960, Lisa 2005) to a method for determining hadron interactions, as pioneered by the ALICE collaboration for systems where scattering experiments are impractical. The K−p correlation analysis builds on chiral SU(3) dynamics approaches to the Λ(1405) resonance (Kaiser 1995, Oset 1997, Hyodo 2007) and uses the Kyoto potential (Ikeda 2011, Miyahara 2018) to test the K̅N interaction. The Λα correlation study addresses the hyperon puzzle in neutron star equations of state (Gal 2016, Burgio 2021) by probing the repulsive core predicted by chiral EFT three-body forces (Gerstung 2020). Future femtoscopic experiments at high-energy colliders can refine understanding of exotic hadrons and hypernuclei.
