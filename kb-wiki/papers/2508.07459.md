# Particle-hole origin of thermal beating in dipole-compression modes of a 1D Bose gas

> Using generalized hydrodynamics, we study the thermal behavior of dipole-compression collective oscillations in a harmonically trapped one-dimensional (1D) Bose gas across the crossover from weak to strong repulsive contact interactions. A key scale controlling this behavior is the temperature of the hole-induced anomaly, associated with the thermal population of hole excitations. In contrast to classical hydrodynamics, which predicts a single oscillation mode, we find a beating signal composed of two frequencies. As the temperature increases, both frequencies evolve from the low-temperature phononic hydrodynamic regime toward the collisionless limit around the anomaly temperature, without saturating at the values expected in the high-temperature collisional hydrodynamic regime. The lower frequency originates from hole excitations and is associated to low-energy oscillations, while the higher frequency emerges from particle excitations and corresponds to the dipole-compression mode. The thermal evolution of the relative excitation strengths of the two frequencies reflects the changing population imbalance between particle and hole spectral states across the anomaly. Our results reveal direct connections between excitations, thermodynamics, correlations, dynamics, and interparticle collisions, and may prove relevant to other atomic, nuclear, solid-state, electronic, and spin systems exhibiting similar anomalies or thermal second-order phase transitions.

# Particle-hole origin of thermal beating in dipole-compression modes of a 1D Bose gas

## Key claim
Using generalized hydrodynamics, the authors show that dipole-compression oscillations in a harmonically trapped 1D Bose gas exhibit a two-frequency beating signal across the crossover from weak to strong repulsive interactions, with the thermal evolution governed by the hole-induced anomaly temperature rather than by the classical hydrodynamic-to-collisionless crossover expected from collision rate arguments. The lower frequency originates from hole excitations and the higher frequency from particle excitations, and the classical hydrodynamic regime is restricted to temperatures below the anomaly temperature.

## Method
The authors perform generalized hydrodynamics (GHD) simulations of a harmonically trapped 1D Bose gas described by the Lieb-Liniger model with an external harmonic trap, using the exact thermal Bethe ansatz within the local density approximation for initial equilibrium states. They excite dipole-compression modes by applying a cubic perturbation to the harmonic trap potential at time zero, then compute the time evolution of the density using the iFluid package and extract oscillation frequencies from the Fourier spectrum of the skewness of the density profile.

## Key numbers
- For weak interactions at low temperature, the higher frequency component ω₂ recovers the phononic hydrodynamic limit of √6 ωₓ (where ωₓ is the axial harmonic trap frequency).
- For strong interactions at low temperature, ω₁ recovers the lowest breathing mode values of √3 ωₓ (regime I) and 2 ωₓ (regime VI).
- At temperatures above the anomaly (γ₀ > γ₀ᴬ), the two frequency components approach the collisionless limits of ω₁ ≈ 1 ωₓ and ω₂ ≈ 3 ωₓ.
- The anomaly temperature values for datasets (a)-(d) are: γ₀ᴬ = 2.86 × 10² (dataset a), 6.57 (dataset b), 1.51 (dataset c), 1.23 × 10⁻² (dataset d).
- The high-temperature collisional hydrodynamic prediction of √7 ωₓ is not reached; instead ω₂ passes through this value without saturating and continues to 3 ωₓ.

## Context
This work extends the study of collective modes in 1D Bose gases beyond classical hydrodynamics (CHD) predictions by Hu (2014) and earlier work by De Rosi (2015, 2016), which predicted a single oscillation mode and a high-temperature collisional hydrodynamic regime. The results connect to the hole-induced anomaly identified by De Rosi (2022) in the uniform 1D Bose gas, where a peak in the specific heat arises from thermal population of hole excitations. The findings enable prediction of collective dynamics from excitation spectra across finite-temperature systems and suggest similar anomaly-driven behavior may occur in other 1D systems such as the super Tonks-Girardeau gas, dipolar ensembles, spin chains, and nuclear matter in neutron stars.
