# Response of bare strange stars to energy input onto their surfaces

> We study numerically the thermal emission of $e^+e^-$ pairs from a bare strange star heated by energy input onto its surface; heating starts at some moment, and is steady afterwards. The thermal luminosity in $e^+e^-$ pairs increases to some constant value. The rise time and the steady thermal luminosity are evaluated. Both normal and colour superconducting states of strange quark matter are considered. The results are used to test the magnetar model of soft gamma-ray repeaters where the bursting activity is explained by fast decay of superstrong magnetic fields and heating of the strange star surface. It is shown that the rise times observed in typical bursts may be explained in this model only if strange quark matter is a superconductor with an energy gap of more that 1 MeV.

## Key claim

The rise times observed in typical soft gamma-ray repeater bursts (∼10⁻¹–10⁻³ s) can be explained by the magnetar model of strange stars only if strange quark matter is a colour superconductor with an energy gap of more than 1 MeV; for normal (non-superconducting) strange quark matter, the calculated rise times are too long to match the observations.

## Method

The author numerically solves the one-dimensional heat transfer equation for the surface layers of a bare strange star, with a boundary condition that balances the steady energy input onto the surface against the energy flux carried away by thermally emitted e⁺e⁻ pairs. Both normal (non-superconducting) and colour-superconducting states of strange quark matter are considered, using standard expressions for specific heat, thermal conductivity, and neutrino emissivity modified by an exponential suppression factor for the superconducting case. The rise time of the e⁺e⁻ pair luminosity is extracted from the numerical solutions as the time from the start of heating until the luminosity reaches half its maximum value.

## Key numbers

- For normal strange quark matter, the critical input luminosity at which the pair luminosity becomes comparable to the input is ∼10⁴⁰ erg s⁻¹; for superconducting strange quark matter with Δ₀ ≳ 0.3 MeV, the critical luminosity is ∼3×10³⁸ erg s⁻¹.
- The rise time τ₊ varies from ∼10⁶ s at L_input ≲ 10⁴⁰ erg s⁻¹ and Δ₀ ≲ 0.1 MeV, down to ∼10⁻⁸ s at L_input ∼ 10⁴⁵ erg s⁻¹ and Δ₀ ≳ 1 MeV.
- For the two giant bursts (peak luminosities ∼10⁴⁵ erg s⁻¹), the model predicts rise times ≲ 10⁻³ s, consistent with observations regardless of whether strange quark matter is superconducting.
- For typical bursts with luminosities ∼10⁴¹–10⁴² erg s⁻¹, the observed rise times (∼10⁻¹–10⁻³ s) require strange quark matter to be a superconductor with an energy gap Δ₀ ≳ 1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the author’s earlier studies of thermal emission from bare strange star surfaces (Usov 1998, 2001a) and of accretion-driven bursts (Usov 2001b). It tests the magnetar model of soft gamma-ray repeaters, where bursting activity is attributed to fast decay of superstrong magnetic fields (Duncan and Thompson 1992; Thompson and Duncan 1995). The results show that the observed rise times of typical bursts can discriminate between normal and superconducting states of strange quark matter, enabling future observational tests of colour superconductivity in compact stars.
