# Asymmetric semi-infinite nuclear matter: surface and curvature properties in relativistic and non-relativistic models

> Surface and curvature properties of asymmetric nuclear matter are studied beyond the proton drip. Using the semiclassical extended Thomas-Fermi method, the calculations are performed in the non-relativistic and relativistic meson field theories (Skyrme forces and non linear sigma-omega parametrizations). First we discuss the bulk equilibrium between the nuclear and drip phases. Next we analyze the asymmetric surface as a function of the bulk neutron excess. We examine local quantities related to the density profiles and, for two definitions of the bulk reference energy, the surface and curvature energy coefficients. The calculation of the curvature energy is carefully treated. The sensitivity of the nuclear surface to the relativistic effects is investigated. Mass formulae useful for arbitray neutron excess are discussed, and their limit at small asymmetries is compared with the liquid droplet model mass formula.

## Key claim

The surface and curvature energy coefficients of asymmetric semi-infinite nuclear matter are computed beyond the proton drip using semiclassical extended Thomas-Fermi methods, but the two definitions of the bulk reference energy (the "e-definition" and the "Gibbs definition") yield different surface and curvature coefficients; the Gibbs dynamical curvature energy vanishes identically when the variational equations are satisfied, so the full Gibbs curvature energy is purely geometrical.

## Method

The authors solved the coupled Euler-Lagrange equations for neutron and proton density profiles in a semi-infinite slab geometry, using the extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) method for non-relativistic Skyrme forces and the relativistic extended Thomas-Fermi (RETF) method for non-linear sigma-omega parametrizations. The asymptotic boundary conditions at infinity were fixed by solving the bulk coexistence equations (equal chemical potentials and equal pressure) between a dense nuclear phase and a dilute drip phase for a given bulk neutron excess. From the self-consistent density profiles, the surface tension was computed under two definitions of the reference energy (the "e-definition" and the "Gibbs definition"), and the curvature energy was separated into geometrical and dynamical contributions.

## Key numbers

- For the NL1 parametrization, the neutron drip occurs at a bulk neutron excess of approximately \(\bar\delta_{\rm nd} \simeq 0.24\) and the proton drip at \(\bar\delta_{\rm pd} \simeq 0.64\).
- The nuclear radius constant \(r_0\) is defined by \(\frac{4}{3}\pi r_0^3(\rho_0 - \rho_{\rm d}) = 1\).
- The surface energy coefficients are given by \(E_{\rm s,e} = 4\pi r_0^2 \sigma_{\rm e}\) and \(E_{\rm s,\mu} = 4\pi r_0^2 \sigma_{\mu}\).
- The curvature energy coefficient is decomposed as \(E_{\rm c} = E_{\rm c}^{\rm geo} + E_{\rm c}^{\rm dyn}\), with the geometrical part involving an integral of \((z - z_0)\) times the surface energy density and the dynamical part involving derivatives with respect to curvature \(\kappa\).
- The Gibbs dynamical curvature energy is zero (\(E_{\rm c,\mu}^{\rm dyn} = 0\)), so the full Gibbs curvature energy equals its geometrical part.

## Context

This work extends the earlier Thomas-Fermi study of surface energies in multicomponent systems by Myers et al. (1985) and the ETF analysis of neutron-rich nuclei by Kolehmainen et al. (1985) to include both non-relativistic Skyrme forces and relativistic mean-field models, with a careful treatment of the curvature energy that can only be fully obtained semiclassically. The results enable improved mass formulae for arbitrary neutron excess and are relevant for describing nuclei in neutron star crusts at densities between \(4 \times 10^{11}\) g/cm\(^3\) and normal nuclear values (\(2.7 \times 10^{14}\) g/cm\(^3\)).
