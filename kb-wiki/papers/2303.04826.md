# Exciting Ions: a Systematic Treatment of Ultraperipheral Heavy Ion Collisions with Nuclear Breakup

> We present an updated theoretical treatment of ultraperipheral collisions (UPCs) of heavy ions, within the SuperChic Monte Carlo generator. This in particular accounts for mutual ion excitation through additional photon exchanges between the colliding ions. This effect occurs frequently in UPCs, and indeed can be (and has been) measured in data through the use of zero degree calorimeter (ZDC) detectors installed in the far forward region. The theoretical approach presented here accounts for the non-trivial and non-negligible impact such ion dissociation has on the measured cross sections and distributions of the produced particles in the central detectors. This builds on previous work, whereby the survival factor probability of no additional inelastic ion-ion scattering due to the strong interaction, and its kinematic impact, are also accounted for within the same overall framework. We compare to data from ATLAS and CMS at the LHC, and STAR at RHIC, and find in general encouraging agreement for a range of observables and ZDC neutron tags, with some room for further improvement, suggesting the inclusion of higher order QED effects and/or tuning of the the $γA \to A^*$ cross section may be desirable. Overall, this gives confidence in the approach considered here and for applications to new phenomena within and beyond the Standard Model.

## Key claim

The paper presents an updated theoretical treatment of ultraperipheral heavy ion collisions that accounts for mutual ion excitation through additional photon exchanges, finding generally encouraging agreement with ATLAS, CMS, and STAR data but noting that inclusion of higher order QED effects and/or tuning of the gamma-A to A* cross section may be desirable for further improvement.

## Method

The author extends the SuperChic Monte Carlo generator to include mutual ion dissociation via additional photon exchanges between colliding ions, where the nuclear excitation is assumed to occur independently of the gamma-gamma to X production process. The breakup probability for each ion is computed as a convolution of the photon emission flux from the other ion and the measured gamma-A to A* cross section, with the survival factor for no additional inelastic ion-ion scattering also accounted for within the same framework. The calculation uses impact parameter space formulation where the dissociation probability factorizes into independent breakup probabilities for each ion, and the photon absorption cross section is taken from experimental data across multiple energy regions from GDR resonance up to high energies.

## Key numbers

- For Pb ions, the Woods-Saxon parameters used are: proton radius R_p = 6.680 fm, skin thickness d_p = 0.447 fm; neutron radius R_n = (6.67 +/- 0.03) fm, skin thickness d_n = (0.55 +/- 0.01) fm
- The integrated single neutron emission cross section at the LHC gives 260 mb for the GDR contribution at a representative impact parameter b_perp = 3 R_A
- The high-energy contribution above 80 GeV accounts for roughly 25% of the total contribution to the breakup probability at the LHC for b_perp = 3 R_A
- The photon energy cutoff for the high-energy contribution is set at omega = 500 GeV, corresponding to |y_gamma n| ~ 5 for PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_nn) = 5.02 TeV
- The GDR resonance peak lies in the omega ~ 10-20 MeV region, with single neutron emission data taken up to omega = 23.4 MeV

## Context

This work extends the SuperChic Monte Carlo generator framework previously developed by Harland-Lang (2018) for ultraperipheral collisions, building on the survival factor treatment that accounts for no additional inelastic ion-ion scattering. The treatment of ion dissociation effects was found to be incomplete in the Starlight MC generator implementation, as shown by CMS data (2020) on the impact of ion dissociation on dimuon acoplanarity, while theoretical calculations by Brandenburg (2020) described the trend but without providing a full MC treatment. This extension enables predictions for UPC production to be compared more directly with experimental measurements using zero degree calorimeter tags, and has applications to new phenomena within and beyond the Standard Model including light-by-light scattering, axion-like particle searches, and tau lepton anomalous magnetic moment measurements.
