# Comparison of hadronic rescattering calculations of elliptic flow and HBT with measurements from RHIC

> Results from the data obtained in the first physics run of the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) have shown suprisingly large elliptic flow and suprisingly small HBT radii. Attempts to explain both results in a consistant picture have so far been unsuccessful. The present work shows that a simple thermal-like initial state model coupled to a hadronic rescattering calculation can explain reasonably well both elliptic flow and HBT results from RHIC. The calculation suggests a very early hadronization time of about 1 fm/c after the initial collision of the nuclei.

# Comparison of hadronic rescattering calculations of elliptic flow and HBT with measurements from RHIC

## Key claim
A simple thermal-like initial state model with no initial flow, coupled to a hadronic rescattering calculation, can explain both the large elliptic flow and small HBT radii measured at RHIC, provided hadronization occurs very early, at about 1 fm/c after the initial nuclear collision. The model is uniquely determined by the HBT parameter R_Long, which cannot be compensated by introducing an ad hoc initial flow velocity.

## Method
The author uses a semi-classical Monte Carlo calculation with strong binary collisions between hadrons, carried out in three stages: initialization and hadronization, rescattering and freezeout, and calculation of experimental observables. The initial state is parameterized with a thermal transverse momentum distribution (temperature T = 300 MeV), a Gaussian rapidity distribution (width sigma_y = 2.4), and a hadronization proper time tau_had = 1 fm/c, with particle multiplicities constrained by PHOBOS charged hadron measurements. Rescattering is simulated in time steps of 0.1 fm/c using isospin-averaged s-wave and p-wave cross sections from Prakash et al. (1993), running to 100 fm/c.

## Key numbers
- Hadronization proper time: tau_had = 1 fm/c, required to match both RHIC elliptic flow and HBT results; same value was needed in previous SPS Pb+Pb rescattering calculations (Humanic, 1998).
- Initial temperature parameter: T = 300 MeV in the thermal transverse mass distribution.
- Rapidity width: sigma_y = 2.4 for the Gaussian rapidity distribution.
- Particle multiplicities at freezeout: approximately 5000 pions, 500 kaons, and 650 nucleons per event (isospin-summed).
- Maximum hadron number density at mid-rapidity at t = 0 fm/c: 6.8 fm^{-3}, dropping to about 1 fm^{-3} at t = 4 fm/c.
- Effective pion volume estimated from pi-pi scattering cross section (0.8 fm^2): 0.065 fm^3, giving a maximum particle occupancy of space less than 50%.
- Slope parameters from m_T distributions: close to preliminary STAR measurements of 190 +/- 10 MeV for pi^-, 300 +/- 30 MeV for K^-, and 565 +/- 50 MeV for anti-protons.
- Elliptic flow v_2 calculations for pions and nucleons at b = 8 fm show flattening for p_T > 2 GeV/c, consistent with STAR and PHENIX minimum-bias results.
- HBT parameters R_Tside, R_Tout, R_Long, and lambda from rescattering calculations at b = 0, 5, and 8 fm show trends consistent with STAR measurements at three centrality bins and as a function of m_T.

## Context
This work addresses the "first big mystery from RHIC": hydrodynamical models (Kolb, 1999) agreed with large elliptic flow but disagreed with HBT radii (Rischke, 1996), while relativistic quantum molecular dynamics calculations such as RQMD v2.4 (Sorge, 1989) underpredicted elliptic flow (Sorge, 1995) but gave HBT radii comparable to data (Hardtke, 1999). A hydrodynamical model with a hadronic afterburner (Soff, 2000) produced HBT radii significantly larger than measurements. The present approach differs from RQMD by using a simple thermal-like initial state rather than a color string picture. The calculation enables a physically motivated picture where the Color Glass Condensate (McLerran, 2002; Kovner, 1995) might "shatter" directly into hadronic fragments on a timescale of about 0.3 fm/c, rather than melting into quarks and gluons. The Hagedorn thermodynamic model (Hagedorn, 1970) with limiting temperature T_0 = 160 MeV is noted as potentially contradictory to the T = 300 MeV used here, but the author argues the thermal functional form is merely convenient and an exponential form exp(-m_T/T_e) would work equally well.
