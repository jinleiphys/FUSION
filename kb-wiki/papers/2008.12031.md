# Heating triangle singularities in heavy ion collisions

> We predict that triangle singularities of hadron spectroscopy can be strongly affected in heavy ion collisions. To do it we examine various effects on the singularity-inducing triangle loop of finite temperature in the terminal hadron phase. It appears that peaks seen in central heavy ion collisions are more likely to be hadrons than rescattering effects under two conditions. First, the flight-time of the intermediate hadron state must be comparable to the lifetime of the equilibrated fireball (else, the reaction mostly happens in vacuo after freeze out). Second, the medium effect over the triangle-loop particle mass or width must be sizeable. When these (easily checked) conditions are met, the medium quickly reduces the singularity: at T about 150 MeV, even by two orders of magnitude, acting then as a spectroscopic filter.

## Key claim

The authors predict that triangle singularities in hadron spectroscopy can be strongly suppressed in heavy ion collisions, but only under two specific conditions: the flight time of the intermediate hadron state must be comparable to the lifetime of the equilibrated fireball, and the medium effect on the triangle-loop particle mass or width must be sizeable. When these conditions are met, the medium reduces the singularity by up to two orders of magnitude at temperatures around 150 MeV, acting as a spectroscopic filter that makes observed peaks more likely to be genuine hadrons rather than rescattering effects.

## Method

The authors computed the scalar three-point triangle loop integral using the Matsubara formalism at finite temperature, replacing the continuous energy integration with a sum over Matsubara frequencies and analytically continuing back to Minkowski space. They examined five specific triangle diagrams (involving K*Σπ, K*KK, D*D*D, D*DD, and Δ⁺pn loops) by evaluating the squared modulus of the loop integral as a function of invariant mass at various temperatures, first including temperature only in the loop variable and then incorporating thermal corrections to the intermediate particle masses and widths from the literature.

## Key numbers

- At T ≈ 150 MeV, the triangle singularity is reduced by two orders of magnitude when thermal corrections to masses and widths are included
- For the K*KK triangle relevant to a₁(1420) production, the K⁺ mass drops from 0.49367 GeV at T=0 to 0.37 GeV at T=0.15 GeV; the K*⁺ mass drops from 0.89166 GeV to 0.508 GeV
- For the D*D*D triangle relevant to X(3872) production, the D*⁰ mass drops from 2.00685 GeV at T=0 to 1.868 GeV at T=0.15 GeV; the D⁰ mass drops from 1.86483 GeV to 1.776 GeV
- For the Δ⁺pn triangle relevant to deuteron production, the Δ⁺ width increases from 0.1311 GeV at T=0 to 0.159 GeV at T=0.15 GeV, while the Δ⁺ mass remains at 1.2349 GeV
- The invariant mass of K*⁰Σ⁰ is fixed at 2140 MeV for the Λ(1405) production example
- The invariant mass of K*⁺K⁻ is fixed at 1420 MeV for the a₁(1420) example
- The invariant mass of the initial c-cbar pair is fixed at 4.0172 GeV for the X(3872) production example
- The invariant mass of Δ⁺p is fixed at 2188.68 MeV for the deuteron example

## Context

This work extends the study of triangle singularities in hadron spectroscopy, which has been actively discussed in the last decade by Bugg (2011), Wang (2013), Szczepaniak (2015), and Guo (2019), among others. The authors apply thermal field theory methods to the specific problem of triangle singularities in heavy ion collisions, building on prior work by Cleven (2017), Gu (2018), and Montana (2020) who computed thermal masses and widths for mesons. The paper enables experimental discrimination between genuine hadron resonances and kinematic singularities in heavy ion collision data, particularly relevant for interpreting structures like the a₁(1420) (which Ketzer (2015) and Aceti (2016) argued is a triangle singularity rather than a resonance) and the X(3872).
