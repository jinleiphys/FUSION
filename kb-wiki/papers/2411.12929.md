# Non-Newtonian corrections to radiative viscosity: Israel-Stewart theory as a viscosity limiter

> Radiation is a universal friction-increasing agent. When two fluid layers are in relative motion, the inevitable exchange of radiation between such layers gives rise to an effective force, which tries to prevent the layers from sliding. This friction is often modeled as a Navier-Stokes shear viscosity. However, non-Newtonian corrections are expected to appear at distances of about one optical depth from the layers' interface. Such corrections prevent the viscous stress from becoming too large. Here, we set the foundations of a rigorous theory for these corrections, valid along incompressible flows. We show that, in the linear regime, the infinite Chapman-Enskog series can be computed analytically, leading to universal formulas for all transport coefficients, which apply to any fluid, with any composition, with radiation of any type (also neutrinos), and with nearly any type of radiative process. We then show that, with an appropriate shear-heat coupling coefficient, Israel-Stewart theory can correctly describe most non-Newtonian features of radiative shear stresses.

## Key claim

The paper shows that non-Newtonian corrections to radiative shear viscosity appear at distances of about one optical depth from a fluid interface, and that these corrections prevent the viscous stress from becoming arbitrarily large. The author demonstrates that Israel-Stewart theory with an appropriate shear-heat coupling coefficient can correctly describe most non-Newtonian features of radiative shear stresses, providing a viscosity limiter in Fourier space rather than in real space.

## Method

The author solves the linearized radiative Boltzmann equation coupled with momentum conservation for transversal (incompressible) shear waves in a fluid interacting with radiation. The infinite Chapman-Enskog gradient series is computed analytically by expanding the exact dispersion relation in powers of wavenumber, yielding universal formulas for all transport coefficients that apply to any fluid composition and any type of radiation. The non-Newtonian model is then derived by interpolating between the small-wavenumber and large-wavenumber limits of the exact dispersion relation, and is shown to correspond to the large-inertia limit of Israel-Stewart theory with a specific shear-heat coupling coefficient.

## Key numbers

* The shear stress gradient expansion coefficients are given by the exact formula η^(2a-1) = 2/[(1+2a)(3+2a)] ∫_0^∞ Σ_n ρ_n τ_n^(2a) dE, where τ_n are mean free paths and ρ_n are emissivity functions.
* For photons with absorption and isotropic scattering, the transport coefficients are η^(2a-1) = [4a_R T^4]/[(1+2a)(3+2a)] × 1/⟨(σ_A+σ_S)^(2a-1)⟩, where T is temperature, a_R is the radiation constant, and ⟨...⟩ is the Rosseland mean.
* The non-Newtonian model dispersion relation ω(k) = -(i/W) × [η^(-1) η^(1) k^2]/[η^(-1)+η^(1) k^2] reproduces the exact limits ω ≈ -(i/W) η^(1) k^2 for kτ → 0 and ω ≈ -(i/W) η^(-1) for kτ → ∞.
* The Israel-Stewart shear-heat coupling coefficient required to match the model is α_1 = ± (T κ_q η^(-1))^(-1/2), where κ_q is the radiative heat conductivity.

## Context

This work extends the foundational studies of radiative viscosity by Thomas (1930), Misner (1968), and Weinberg (1971), who suggested Navier-Stokes theory applies only when the radiation mean free path is infinitesimal compared to hydrodynamic gradients. The paper addresses the problem noted by Shibata and collaborators (2011) that radiative Navier-Stokes stress can exceed radiative pressure, requiring ad-hoc viscosity limiters. The author provides a rigorous derivation showing that Israel-Stewart theory, previously argued for by Udey and Israel (1982) based on stability arguments, can correctly describe non-Newtonian radiative shear stresses. This enables future simulations to include radiative shear viscosity rather than setting it to zero as is currently done in codes by Farris (2008), Sadowski (2013), O'Connor (2014), and Radice (2022).
