# Microscopic optical potential from the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory: Proton-nucleus scattering

> A relativistic microscopic optical model potential for nucleon-nucleus scattering is developed based on the \emph{ab initio} relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) theory with the improved local density approximation, which is abbreviated as the RBOM potential. Both real and imaginary parts of the single-particle potentials in symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter at various densities are determined uniquely in the full Dirac space. The density distributions of the target nuclei are calculated by the covariant energy density functional theory with the density functional PC-PK1. The central and spin-orbit terms of the optical potentials are quantitatively consistent with the relativistic phenomenological optical potentials. The performance of the RBOM potential is evaluated by considering proton scattering with incident energy $E\leq 200$ MeV on five target nuclei, $\prescript{208}{}{\text{Pb}}$, $\prescript{120}{}{\text{Sn}}$, $\prescript{90}{}{\text{Zr}}$, $\prescript{48}{}{\text{Ca}}$, and $\prescript{40}{}{\text{Ca}}$. Scattering observables including the elastic scattering angular distributions, analyzing powers, spin rotation functions, and reaction cross sections are analyzed. Theoretical predictions show good agreements with the experimental data and the results derived from phenomenological optical potentials. We anticipate that the RBOM potential can provide reference for other phenomenological and microscopic optical model potentials, as well as reliable descriptions for nucleon scattering on exotic nuclei in the era of rare-isotope beams.

## Key claim

The paper constructs a relativistic microscopic optical model potential (RBOM) from *ab initio* relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) theory in the full Dirac space, using the improved local density approximation (ILDA) with only one adjustable parameter (the range parameter \(t = 1.3\) fm), and shows that it reproduces proton-nucleus scattering observables for five stable nuclei up to 200 MeV without the ambiguities that plagued earlier RBHF-based potentials.

## Method

The authors first solved the RBHF equations self-consistently in the full Dirac space for symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter, obtaining complex scalar and vector single-particle potentials uniquely by including both positive-energy and negative-energy states. They then applied the improved local density approximation (ILDA) with a Gaussian form factor (range \(t = 1.3\) fm) to map these nuclear-matter potentials onto finite nuclei, using density distributions from covariant density functional theory (PC-PK1). Finally, they solved the Schrödinger equivalent equation for proton-nucleus scattering and extracted elastic scattering angular distributions, analyzing powers, spin rotation functions, and reaction cross sections for five target nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The single-particle potentials are calculated at the empirical saturation density \(\rho = 0.16\ \text{fm}^{-3}\) for asymmetry parameters \(\alpha\) from 0 to 0.8; real parts are of order several hundred MeV, while imaginary parts are one order smaller.
- The range parameter in the ILDA is fixed at \(t = 1.3\) fm for all calculations.
- Proton scattering is considered for incident energies \(E \leq 200\) MeV on five target nuclei: \(^{208}\text{Pb}\), \(^{120}\text{Sn}\), \(^{90}\text{Zr}\), \(^{48}\text{Ca}\), and \(^{40}\text{Ca}\).
- The imaginary parts of the single-particle potentials vanish for states below the Fermi surface.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic microscopic optical potential approach by using the RBHF theory solved in the full Dirac space, which resolves the long-standing ambiguity in determining single-particle potentials that affected earlier potentials such as CTOM (Xu et al., 2016). It builds on the improved local density approximation framework previously applied with non-relativistic BHF theory (Bauge et al., 1998, 2001) and with the projection method in RBHF. The RBOM potential enables reliable predictions for nucleon scattering on exotic nuclei at rare-isotope beam facilities, where phenomenological potentials fitted to stable targets cannot be safely extrapolated.
