# 40 years of the Nobel prize in physics: then and now

> The findings for which Aage Bohr and Ben R. Mottelson became co-winners of the 1975 Nobel prize in physics provided the basis for a comprehensive and operative answer to the central problem in the study of the nuclear structure, namely the identification of the appropriate concepts and degrees of freedom that are suitable for describing the phenomena encountered. To do so they produced a breathtaking unification of a number of well established concepts, namely liquid drop and shell models, elementary modes of excitation, superconductivity and quantum electrodynamics, resulting eventually in the paradigm of broken symmetry restoration to determine the nuclear collective variables (CV, elementary modes of excitation): violation of translation invariance by the mean field and by scattering states (single-particle motion), of rotational invariance in the variety of spaces, in particular in 3D- and in gauge-space, leading to surface vibrations and to quadrupole rotations, as well as to pairing vibrations and rotations, with associated emergent properties of generalized rigidity in these spaces, resulting from the coupling to single-particle degrees of freedom.

# 40 years of the Nobel prize in physics: then and now

## Key claim

The paper argues that the 1975 Nobel Prize awarded to Aage Bohr and Ben R. Mottelson recognized their unification of single-particle and collective motion in nuclei through the paradigm of broken symmetry restoration, but the author qualifies that this paradigm was not fully articulated in their 1953 paper nor in Bohr's 1964 Paris Congress contribution, only becoming embodied in the discussion of pairing rotations and vibrations by 1968.

## Method

The paper is an invited lecture delivered on December 14, 2015 at the Niels Bohr Institute, celebrating the 40th anniversary of the Nobel Prize. It reviews the historical development of the Bohr-Mottelson framework, including the unification of the liquid drop model (Niels Bohr and Fritz Kalckar), the shell model (Marie Goeppert Mayer and Hans Jensen), and concepts from superconductivity and quantum electrodynamics. The author then presents modern applications of the Nuclear Field Theory (NFT) based on particle-vibration coupling (PVC), including calculations of absolute two-nucleon transfer cross sections and self-energy effects in exotic nuclei.

## Context

This lecture extends the historical assessment of Bohr and Mottelson's work, which Phil Anderson (1972) described as fundamental for recognizing that nuclei can become football-shaped or plate-shaped with observable consequences in reactions and excitation spectra. The paper connects the 1975 Nobel work to modern developments including Cooper pair transfer as a quantitative probe of pairing correlations (Potel et al., 2013, Rep. Prog. Phys. 76, 106301), and the complete characterization of tin isotopes ¹¹⁸,¹¹⁹,¹²⁰,¹²¹,¹²²Sn using NFT (Idini et al., 2015, Phys. Rev. C 92, 031304(R)). The author notes that the paradigm of broken symmetry restoration is not found in the 1953 Bohr-Mottelson paper but was developed later, and that the language of nuclear field theory remains a challenge for connecting with the broader many-body physics community.
