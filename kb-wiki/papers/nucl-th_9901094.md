# Particle Interferometry for Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> In this report we give a detailed account on Hanbury Brown/Twiss (HBT) particle interferometric methods for relativistic heavy-ion collisions. These exploit identical two-particle correlations to gain access to the space-time geometry and dynamics of the final freeze-out stage. The connection between the measured correlations in momentum space and the phase-space structure of the particle emitter is established, both with and without final state interactions. Suitable Gaussian parametrizations for the two-particle correlation function are derived and the physical interpretation of their parameters is explained. After reviewing various model studies, we show how a combined analysis of single- and two-particle spectra allows to reconstruct the final state of relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

This review establishes the theoretical and phenomenological framework for extracting the space-time geometry and dynamics of the particle-emitting source created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions from Hanbury Brown/Twiss (HBT) two-particle correlations, but the reconstruction of the emission function from measured correlations is not unique due to the mass-shell constraint \(K \cdot q = 0\) which limits the accessible Fourier components.

## Method

The authors derive the two-particle correlation function \(C(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{K})\) from the single-particle Wigner phase-space density \(S(x,K)\) of the emitting source, using the classical current parametrization where the nuclear current is approximated by a classical commuting space-time function. They present both the smoothness and on-shell approximations that relate the correlator to a Fourier transform of the emission function, and develop Gaussian parametrizations (the Cartesian Pratt-Bertsch parametrization and the Yano-Koonin-Podgoretski\u\i\ parametrization) to connect theory with experiment. The framework is extended to include final state interactions, multiparticle symmetrization effects, and applications to non-central collisions with azimuthal dependence of the HBT radius parameters.

## Key numbers

* The QCD lattice simulations predict the quark-gluon plasma transition to occur at a temperature of approximately 150 MeV.
* The first relativistic heavy-ion collider RHIC at BNL will operate at \(\sqrt{s} = 200\) \(A\) GeV, and the LHC program at CERN will explore collisions at \(\sqrt{s} = 5.5\, A\) TeV.
* Hanbury Brown and Twiss measured in 1950 the diameter of the sun using two radio telescopes operating at 2.4 m wavelength.
* The mixing factor for reference pairs in experimental construction of the correlator is typically between 10 and 50.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on HBT interferometry from quantum optics (Hanbury Brown and Twiss, 1950s) and its independent discovery in particle physics by Goldhaber, Goldhaber, Lee and Pais (1960, the "GGLP-effect") to the rapidly expanding systems created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. It builds upon the seminal contributions of Shuryak (1973), Cocconi (1974), Kopylov and Podgoretski\u\i\ (1971-1974), and the key paper of Gyulassy, Kauffmann and Wilson (1979), as well as later developments in parametrization by Pratt (1983-1986) and Bertsch. The work enables a combined analysis of single- and two-particle spectra to reconstruct the final freeze-out state, and provides the technical tools for interpreting data from experiments at the AGS, SPS, RHIC, and LHC.
