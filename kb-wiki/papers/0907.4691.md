# Jet Quenching in Evolving QGP Medium

> A short summary of different approaches to the parton energy loss problem is given. A particular attention is paid to the differences between various models. A possible solution to the problem of distinguishing competing approaches is discussed.

## Key claim

Different theoretical approaches to parton energy loss (jet quenching) can all describe the experimental nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\) equally well with reasonable assumptions, but this success does not directly reveal the medium's temperature because temperature appears as an explicit parameter only in some models, while others use the transport coefficient \(\hat{q}\) with independent free parameters.

## Method

The paper reviews the McGill-AMY approach as an illustrative example, which calculates the leading-order gluon radiation rate in thermal QCD by resumming generalized ladder diagrams using a Schwinger-Dyson integral equation, then evolves the hard parton momentum distribution via Fokker-Planck equations that include both radiative and collisional energy loss. The resulting parton distributions are convoluted with the collision geometry and vacuum fragmentation functions to obtain the medium-modified fragmentation function and \(R_{AA}\). The approach is compared to other methods (BDMPS-Z, GLV, ASW, HT, DGLV, WHDG) which differ in how they treat scattering centers (static vs. dynamic), resummation schemes (diagram sum, path integral, opacity expansion), and evolution schemes (Poisson ansatz, Fokker-Planck, modified DGLAP).

## Key numbers

- The phase transition (cross-over) temperature between hadronic phase and QGP is roughly 200 MeV when three light quark species (\(u, d, s\)) are considered.
- At \(T = 200\) MeV, the energy density of QGP exceeds \(2\) GeV/fm\(^3\).
- Systems at RHIC can reach a maximum temperature of about 500 MeV, where the energy density can be as high as \(100\) GeV/fm\(^3\).
- The lifetime of the QGP created in a heavy ion collision is about 5 fm/c; the system size is about 10 fm; the hydrodynamic time scale is about \(\tau_0 \approx 0.5\) fm.
- The nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\) has a surprisingly small value of about 0.2 for all measured high \(p_T\) range in central collisions (PHENIX data, Adare 2008).
- In the McGill-AMY calculation shown, \(\alpha_s = 0.27\) is used; the coupling \(g \approx 2\) implies the perturbative treatment becomes a phenomenological model.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on parton energy loss in QGP that began with the BDMPS-Z approach (Baier 1996, Zakharov 1997, Baier 1998), which assumed random static scatterers and used the Poisson ansatz for multiple emissions. The paper contrasts the McGill-AMY approach, which uses fully dynamic thermal quarks and gluons with a Fokker-Planck evolution equation, against other methods such as GLV (Gyulassy 2003), ASW (Armesto 2003), DGLV (Djordjevic 2003), WHDG (Wicks 2005), and HT (Majumder 2008). The TEC-HQM collaboration has initiated standardized tests to identify where models differ, and the paper suggests that future tests will come from coherent calculations of other observables such as the hard photon spectrum (Qin 2009, Vitev 2008) and from Monte-Carlo event generators like YaJEM (Renk 2008), JEWEL (Zapp 2008), Q-PYTHIA (Armesto 2009), and MARTINI (McGill-AMY team).
