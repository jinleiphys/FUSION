# Shell effects and multi-chance fission in the sub-lead region

> Within the recently developed five-dimensional Langevin approach for the description of fission of heavy nuclei, we have calculated the fission fragments mass and kinetic energy distributions for the fission of $^{180}$Hg and $^{190}$Hg formed in the reactions $^{36}$Ar +$^{144}$Sm $\Rightarrow$ $^{180}$Hg and $^{36}$Ar +$^{154}$Sm $\Rightarrow$ $^{190}$Hg at few excitation energies and found very good agreement between the calculated and experimental results. Special attention was paid to the accurate description of the dependence of shell effects on the excitation energy. It was shown that the effect of multi-chance fission on the mass distribution is noticeable only at small excitation energies. The kinetic energy distributions are more sensitive to pre-scission neutron emission.

## Key claim

The five-dimensional Langevin approach, using a temperature-dependent shell correction that decreases much more slowly with excitation energy than the popular exponential approximation (Ignatyuk damping factor ~20 MeV), reproduces the double-humped mass distributions in sub-lead mercury isotopes without any parameter fitting, and shows that multi-chance fission noticeably affects the total kinetic energy distributions but only weakly influences the mass distributions except at the lowest excitation energies.

## Method

The authors solved five-dimensional Langevin equations for the time evolution of collective shape variables (two-center shell model parameters: elongation, left and right deformations, mass asymmetry, and neck radius) starting from the ground state deformation with zero initial momenta. The potential energy was calculated within a macroscopic-microscopic model using a folded Yukawa macroscopic part and a temperature-dependent shell correction computed from Woods-Saxon single-particle energies with BCS pairing at finite temperature, rather than using the common exponential damping approximation. The collective inertia tensor was computed in the Werner-Wheeler approximation, the friction tensor used the wall-and-window formula with a reduction factor of 0.27, and trajectories were evolved until the neck radius reached 2 fm, with multi-chance fission contributions from neutron emission calculated using the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model via the TALYS code.

## Key numbers

- Critical neck radius used as scission condition: \( r_{neck}^{crit} = 2 \) fm
- Reduction factor for wall friction: \( k_s = 0.27 \)
- Local collective vibration frequency: \( \hbar\varpi = 2 \) MeV (deformation-independent)
- Excitation energies studied for \( ^{180}\text{Hg} \) and \( ^{190}\text{Hg} \): approximately 30–70 MeV
- For \( ^{180}\text{Hg} \) at \( E^* = 33.4 \) MeV, the calculated mass distribution showed somewhat more shell effects compared with experimental results
- The shell correction \( \delta F = \delta F_{shell} + \delta F_{pair} \) at the ground state of \( ^{180}\text{Hg} \) decreases much slower with excitation energy than the exponential approximation \( \delta F(E^*) = \delta F(0) \exp(-E^*/\gamma) \) with \( \gamma \approx 20 \) MeV
- Shell effects observed in fission at excitation energies \( E^* = 40 \div 50 \) MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous four-dimensional Langevin approach (Ivanyuk et al., 2017; Scientific Reports) to five dimensions, which had previously described triple-humped structures in the thorium region. It directly addresses the surprising double-humped mass distribution in \( ^{180}\text{Hg} \) first reported by Andreyev et al. (2010) and experimentally studied by Nishio et al. (2015). Unlike prior theoretical work on sub-lead fission, which was restricted to static potential energy surface analyses (Moller, 2012; Ichikawa, 2012; Warda, 2012), scission point models, or TDHF restricted to the final saddle-to-scission descent, this is the first fully dynamical model applied to the sub-lead region. The results enable systematic dynamical studies of fission across the nuclear chart, particularly in regions where shell effects persist to unexpectedly high excitation energies.
