# The Freezeout Hypersurface at LHC from particle spectra: Flavor and Centrality Dependence

> We extract the freezeout hypersurface in Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=$ 2760 GeV at the CERN Large Hadron Collider by analysing the data on transverse momentum spectra within a unified model for chemical and kinetic freezeout. The study has been done within two different schemes of freezeout, single freezeout where all the hadrons freezeout together versus double freezeout where those hadrons with non-zero strangeness content have different freezeout parameters compared to the non-strange ones. We demonstrate that the data is better described within the latter scenario. We obtain a strange freezeout hypersurface which is smaller in volume and hotter compared to the non-strange freezeout hypersurface for all centralities with a reduction in $χ^2/N_{df}$ around $40\%$. We observe from the extracted parameters that the ratio of the transverse size to the freezeout proper time is invariant under expansion from the strange to the non-strange freezeout surfaces across all centralities. Moreover, except for the most peripheral bins, the ratio of the non-strange and strange freezeout proper times is close to $1.3$.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a double freezeout scheme (2FO), where hadrons with non-zero strangeness freeze out earlier and separately from non-strange hadrons, provides a better description of transverse momentum spectra in Pb-Pb collisions at LHC than the standard single freezeout (1FO) scheme, with a reduction in chi-squared per degree of freedom of around 40%. This improvement is conditional on using six free parameters (three for each freezeout hypersurface) versus three for 1FO, though the number can be reduced to four or five using observed correlations between geometric parameters.

## Method

The authors analyzed transverse momentum spectra data for pions, kaons, protons, Lambdas, Xi, Omega, K*, and phi mesons from Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2760 GeV across six centrality bins (0-5%, 5-10%, 10-20%, 20-40%, 40-60%, 60-80%). They used the Cooper-Frye formalism with a boost-invariant, fixed proper-time freezeout hypersurface parameterized by temperature T, maximum transverse radius rho_max, and freezeout proper time tau_f, implemented via the THERMINATOR Monte Carlo event generator. In the 2FO scheme, separate sets of these parameters were extracted for strange and non-strange hadrons by fitting to both particle yields and pT spectra up to pT = 3 GeV/c.

## Key numbers

- The strange freezeout temperature T_s ranges from 158(3) MeV (60-80% centrality) to 166(6) MeV (5-10% centrality), while the non-strange temperature T_ns ranges from 146(4) MeV (10-20% centrality) to 151(6) MeV (60-80% centrality).
- The 1FO temperature T ranges from 154(3) MeV (60-80% centrality) to 162(3) MeV (20-40% centrality).
- For the most central bin (0-5%), the geometric parameters are: rho_max = 13.4 fm, tau_f = 9.5 fm (1FO); rho_s = 11.4 fm, tau_s = 7.7 fm (strange); rho_ns = 14.5 fm, tau_ns = 10.2 fm (non-strange).
- The ratio tau_ns/tau_s is close to 1.3 for centralities from 0-5% through 20-40%, but this does not hold for the 40-60% and 60-80% bins.
- The ratio rho/tau is invariant between strange and non-strange freezeout surfaces across all centralities.
- The best fit in 2FO is for the 40-60% centrality bin with chi-squared per degree of freedom approximately 1.5.
- The reduction in chi-squared per degree of freedom for 2FO compared to 1FO is about 2.5 units for the most central bin and about 0.7 units for the 40-60% bin.

## Context

This work extends the double freezeout approach previously applied at RHIC energies (Chatterjee 2013) to LHC energies, and contrasts with the nonequilibrium freezeout (NFO) scenario studied by Begun (2014) which also requires six parameters. The 2FO scheme provides an equilibrium-based alternative to the NFO picture for describing the "proton anomaly" at LHC first reported by Rybczynski (2012). The results enable future studies of freezeout substructure, such as possible separate freezeout of pions and protons, and early freezeout of multi-strange baryons, though the authors caution that contributions from yet-unobserved heavier resonances must first be accounted for.
