# Analytical comparison between X(3) and X(5) models of the Bohr Hamiltonian

> The 3-D Bohr-Mottelson Hamiltonian for $γ$-rigid prolate isotopes, known as $X(3)$, is solved via inverse square potential having only one free parameter, $β_{0}$. The exact form of the wave functions and the energy spectra are obtained as a function of the free parameter of the potential that determines the changes in the spectra ratios and the $B(E2)$. Since $X(3)$ is an exactly separable $γ$-rigid version of $X(5)$, the solutions are compared with the $X(5)$ model and some new set of equations that show the relationships between the two models are stated. In other to show the dynamical symmetry nature of the solutions, the entire solutions from $β_{0}=0$ to $β_{0}=\infty$ are compared with $U(5)$, $X(5)$ and $SU(3)$. The solutions spread from the region around $U(5)$ over $X(5)$ and approach $SU(3)$ at $β_{0}=\infty$. The exact solutions obtained via variational procedure are compared favourably with some existing $X(3)$ models found in the literature. The strong agreement between the present model and $X(3)$ via infinite square well potential is discussed. Twelve best critical point isotopes, $^{102}$Mo, $^{104-108}$Ru, $^{120-126}$Xe, $^{148}$Nd, $^{184-188}$Pt are chosen for experimental realization of the model and moderate agreements are recorded. An excellent agreement which appears in the first $β$-excited state in the comparison of the present model with three $N=90$ isotones: $^{150}$Nd, $^{154}$Gd, and $^{156}$Dy, known to be $X(5)$ candidates, suggests that the present model compensates the $X(5)$ models whose predictions are excellent in the ground states but moderately bad in the first $β$-excited states.

## Key claim

The paper presents an analytical comparison between the X(3) and X(5) models of the Bohr Hamiltonian, showing that the X(3) model solved with an inverse square potential can compensate for the poor predictions of X(5) models in the first beta-excited states, while maintaining the dynamical symmetry nature from U(5) through X(5) to SU(3) as the free parameter beta_0 varies from 0 to infinity.

## Method

The authors solve the Davydov-Chaban Hamiltonian (the 3-D gamma-rigid version of the Bohr-Mottelson Hamiltonian) using an inverse square potential V(beta) = beta_0/beta^2 for 0 ≤ beta ≤ beta_0 and infinite for beta > beta_0. The radial Schrodinger equation is solved using MAPLE software to obtain Bessel function solutions, with the critical order nu^(X(3)) = sqrt(L(L+1)/3 + beta_0 + 1/4). Energy eigenvalues, spectra ratios, and B(E2) transition probabilities are computed as functions of the single free parameter beta_0, and a variational optimization procedure (beta_0-maximization) is applied to maximize the rate of change of spectra ratios for each angular momentum.

## Key numbers

- The critical order for X(3) is nu^(X(3)) = sqrt(L(L+1)/3 + beta_0 + 1/4), while for X(5) it is nu^(X(5)) = sqrt(L(L+1)/3 + beta_0 + 9/4)
- The relation nu^(X(3))(beta_0 = c+2) = nu^(X(5))(beta_0 = c) holds for c = 0, 1, 2, ...
- At beta_0 = infinity, both X(3) and X(5) give R_(4/2) = 3.296, approaching the SU(3) rotational limit value of R_(4/2) = 3.333
- At beta_0 = 0, the solutions approach the U(5) vibrational limit with the relation R_(L/2)(gsb) = 2 + R_(L/2)(beta_1) = 4 + R_(L/2)(beta_2)
- The relation R_(L/2)^(X(3))(beta_0 = c+2) = R_(L/2)^(X(5))(beta_0 = c) holds for c = 0, 1, 2, ...
- For the X(5) candidate Sm-152, the fitted beta_0 = 1.052 with quality factor sigma = 0.357
- Twelve isotopes are studied: Mo-102, Ru-104-108, Xe-120-126, Nd-148, Pt-184-188, plus three N=90 isotones: Nd-150, Gd-154, and Dy-156

## Context

This work extends the X(3) model first proposed by Bonatsos (2006) and builds on previous X(3) solutions using different potentials including the Davidson potential (Yigitoglu and Gokbulut, 2017) and sextic anharmonicity (Budaca, 2014). The paper provides the first analytical comparison between X(3) and X(5) models, deriving explicit relations between their critical orders and spectra ratios. The results enable improved description of the U(5) to SU(3) shape phase transition region of the Casten triangle, particularly for beta-excited states where X(5) models perform poorly, and can be applied to experimental data for critical point isotopes across the nuclear chart.
