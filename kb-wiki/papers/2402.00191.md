# The development of the concept of exchange forces in the 1930s: close encounters between Europe and Japan and the birth of nuclear theory

> The onset and the development of the concept of exchange force in quantum physics are historically reconstructed, starting from Heisenberg's seminal contributions in 1926 and going through the great developments in nuclear physics, which allowed the emergence of the idea of force mediating virtual quanta. Although most of such work was performed in Europe, the last and decisive effort in this long path was carried out by Japanese scientists in the 1930s. This is the main focus of the present work, which retraces the achievements of Yukawa and Tomonaga, whose results and mutual interactions are carefully analyzed and related to those of European physicists.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the concept of exchange forces, which originated in Heisenberg's 1926 work on quantum resonance and was applied to nuclear physics after Chadwick's 1932 discovery of the neutron, reached its decisive formulation through the work of Japanese physicists Yukawa and Tomonaga in the 1930s, who built upon and refined the earlier European contributions of Heisenberg, Majorana, and Fermi.

## Method

The authors provide a historical reconstruction of the development of exchange force concepts by analyzing the original papers of Heisenberg (1926-1933), Majorana, Fermi, and the Japanese physicists Yukawa and Tomonaga. They trace the conceptual evolution from Heisenberg's introduction of exchange interactions in atomic physics, through his application to nuclear forces using a Hamiltonian with charge coordinates and exchange integrals, to Majorana's refinement and Fermi's theory of beta-decay, and finally to Yukawa's meson hypothesis and Tomonaga's work on the range of proton-neutron interactions in Nishina's laboratory.

## Key numbers

* The nuclear radius is of order 10^{-13} cm, as inferred from proton-nuclei scattering experiments and the requirement that nuclear forces bind protons and neutrons within this region.
* Heisenberg's uncertainty principle gives for the mass defect of the neutron an energy of order 137 mc^2, while the observed mass defect is about a hundred times smaller and has the opposite sign.
* The binding energy of nuclei is proportional to the mass number A rather than A^2, indicating the saturation property of nuclear forces.
* The Compton wavelength of an electron is 10^{-11} cm, compared to the nuclear radius of 10^{-13} cm, which Bohr cited as evidence that electrons lose their individuality inside the nucleus.

## Context

This work extends the historical analyses of Carson (1996) and Carson (1997) on the development of exchange interactions, and of Miller (1985, 1994) on the role of visualization and intuition in nuclear physics. It provides a detailed account of how Japanese physicists, particularly Yukawa and Tomonaga, who were exposed to the new quantum mechanics through lectures by Heisenberg and Dirac in Japan in 1929 organized by Nishina, completed the European work on exchange forces by developing the meson theory and the understanding of proton-neutron interaction range. The paper enables future historical studies of the cross-cultural transmission of quantum concepts between Europe and Japan in the 1930s.
