# Microscopic theory of angular momentum distributions across the full range of fission fragments

> Modern nuclear theory provides qualitative insights into the fundamental mechanisms of nuclear fission and is increasingly capable of making reliable quantitative predictions. Most quantities of interest pertain to the primary fission fragments, whose subsequent decay is typically modeled using statistical reaction models. Consequently, a key objective of fission theory is to inform these models by predicting the initial conditions of the primary fragments. In this work, we employ a framework that combines joint angular momentum and particle number projection with time-dependent configuration mixing to calculate the angular momentum distributions of primary fragments. Focusing on the benchmark cases of neutron-induced fission of $^{235}$U and $^{239}$Pu, we predict - for the first time - microscopic angular momentum distributions for all fragments observed in experiments. Our results reveal a pronounced sawtooth pattern in the average angular momentum as a function of fragment mass, consistent with recent measurements. Additionally, we observe substantial variations in angular momentum distributions along isobaric chains, indicating that commonly used empirical formulas lack sufficient accuracy. We also quantify a strong correlation between the angular momentum and the deformation of the fragments at scission, and a weak correlation in the magnitude of the angular momentum between fragment partners. The generated data will enable estimation of the impact of microscopic distributions on fission spectra, paving the way toward fission modeling based on microscopic inputs.

## Key claim

The authors predict, for the first time, microscopic angular momentum distributions for all fragments observed in experiments for neutron-induced fission of 235U and 239Pu, but the results are conditional on the assumption that the population probability of scission configurations with different neck sizes is uniformly partitioned for fixed quadrupole and octupole moments.

## Method

The framework combines joint angular momentum and particle number projection with time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) under the Gaussian overlap approximation to calculate angular momentum distributions of primary fission fragments. Scission configurations are determined by solving constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov equations on a two-dimensional potential energy surface in quadrupole (q20) and octupole (q30) moments, then extended by constraining the neck operator to values between 1 and 3 nucleons. The dynamical population of these configurations is obtained by integrating the flux density from TDGCM calculations, and the final distributions are obtained by folding the projected probabilities with the population probabilities.

## Key numbers

- For neutron-induced fission of 235U and 239Pu, the neck operator is constrained to values with 1 ≤ qN ≤ 3, using step size ΔqN = 0.5
- The scission line is defined using topological balls of radius R where x% of neighbors within the ball have qN ≤ qN^sciss, with x, R, and qN^sciss as free parameters
- The initial collective wave packet energy is set using the approximate relation E0 ≈ EB + En, where EB is the fission barrier height and En is an equivalent incident neutron energy
- The total probability distribution for fragments is normalized to two, reflecting binary fission

## Context

This work extends the framework of Marevic (2021) by including simultaneous angular momentum and particle number projection in fission fragments, particle number projection in the whole nucleus, and configuration mixing with the time-dependent generator coordinate method. It provides microscopic angular momentum distributions across the full range of fission fragments for the first time, enabling estimation of the impact of microscopic distributions on fission spectra and paving the way toward fission modeling based on microscopic inputs rather than phenomenological formulas used in codes such as FREYA or CGMF.
