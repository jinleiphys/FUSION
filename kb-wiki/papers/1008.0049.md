# Weakly-bound rare isotopes with a coupled-channel approach that includes resonant levels

> The question of how the scattering cross section changes when the spectra of the colliding nuclei have low-excitation particle-emitting resonances is explored using a multi-channel algebraic scattering (MCAS) method. As a test case, the light-mass nuclear target 8Be, being particle-unstable, has been considered. Nucleon-nucleus scattering cross sections, as well as the spectra of the compound nuclei formed, have been determined from calculations that do, and do not, consider particle emission widths of the target nuclear states. The resonant character of the unstable excited states introduces a problem because the low-energy tails of these resonances can intrude into the sub-threshold, bound-state region. This unphysical behaviour needs to be corrected by modifying, in an energy-dependent way, the shape of the target resonances from the usual Lorentzian one. The resonance function must smoothly reach zero at the elastic threshold. Ways of achieving this condition are explored in this paper.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that including the finite particle-decay widths of excited target states in coupled-channel scattering calculations significantly improves the agreement between theoretical and experimental resonance widths in the compound nucleus, but the unphysical intrusion of Lorentzian resonance tails into the sub-threshold region requires an energy-dependent modification of the width shape that smoothly reaches zero at the elastic threshold.

## Method

The authors extended the Multi-Channel Algebraic Scattering (MCAS) method, which solves coupled Lippmann-Schwinger equations in momentum space using a finite-rank separable representation with sturmian functions, to incorporate complex channel energies reflecting the decay widths of target states. They applied this formalism to neutron scattering from the particle-unstable target 8Be, comparing calculations with target-state widths set to zero, with constant widths multiplied by a Heaviside step function, and with an energy-dependent scaling function for the widths. The energy-dependent scaling function was chosen to be unity at the resonance centroid energy and zero at the elastic threshold, with a parameter A controlling the shape.

## Key numbers

- The 8Be ground state (0+) has a width of 6 × 10⁻⁶ MeV; the 2+ resonance has centroid 3.03 MeV and width 1.5 MeV; the 4+ resonance has centroid 11.35 MeV and width ~3.5 MeV.
- For the 7/2⁻ resonance in 9Be, the no-width calculation gave a width of 2.08 × 10⁻⁵ MeV (ratio to experiment 1.72 × 10⁻⁵), while the width calculation gave 1.641 MeV (ratio 1.356, within a factor of 3 of the experimental value 1.210 ± 0.230 MeV).
- For the 1/2⁻ resonance in 9Be, the no-width calculation gave 0.495 MeV (ratio 0.458), while the width calculation gave 1.686 MeV (ratio 1.561, experimental value 1.080 ± 0.110 MeV).
- For the 5/2⁻ resonance in 9Be (experimental width 7.8 × 10⁻⁴ MeV), the no-width calculation gave 0.060 MeV (ratio 76.74) and the width calculation gave 2.772 MeV (ratio 3554), the only case where widths worsened agreement.
- The reaction cross section in the no-width case is zero until 3.4 MeV laboratory energy (3.03 MeV centre-of-mass), while with widths it shows a broad peak at ~1.5 MeV corresponding to the 5/2⁺ resonance.

## Context

This work extends the MCAS formalism (Amos 2003) to handle unstable target states, building on earlier developments for Pauli principle treatment via orthogonalizing pseudo-potentials (Canton 2005) and initial studies of unstable targets (Fraser 2008). The test case of n + 8Be scattering follows a rotor-model interaction (Fraser 2010). The energy-dependent width correction addresses the problem that Lorentzian resonance tails produce unphysical bound-state behaviour below threshold; the proposed rational-function scaling form allows analytical dispersion-relation shifts (Canton 1991) to preserve causality, which will be explored in future work alongside applications to other light nuclear systems.
