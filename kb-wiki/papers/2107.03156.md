# Generalized seniority isomers in and around $Z=82$ closed shell: a survey of Hg, Pb and Po isotopes

> In this paper, we investigate the generalized seniority scheme and the validity of Generalized Seniority Schmidt Model in and around the $Z=82$ semi-magic region. A consistently same multi-j configuration is used to explain all the nuclear spectroscopic properties such as $g-$factors, $Q-$moments and $B(E2)$ trends for the ${13/2}^+$, ${12}^+$ and ${33/2}^+$ isomers in all the three Hg, Pb and Po isotopic chains. The inverted parabolic $B(E2)$ trends for the first $2^+$ states in Hg, Pb and Po isotopes are also explained using the generalized seniority scheme. A comparison with the experimental data is presented, wherever possible, and future possibilities are suggested.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the generalized seniority (GS) scheme, using a consistent multi-j neutron configuration of {i₁₃/₂ ⊗ f₇/₂ ⊗ p₃/₂} with pair degeneracy Ω=13, can explain the g-factors, Q-moments, and B(E2) trends for the 13/2⁺, 12⁺, and 33/2⁺ isomers in Hg, Pb, and Po isotopes, as well as the inverted parabolic B(E2) trends for the first 2⁺ states, with the caveat that the model is phenomenological and does not include higher-order microscopic effects such as core polarization or meson exchange currents.

## Method

The authors applied the Generalized Seniority Schmidt Model (GSSM), which merges the generalized seniority scheme with the Schmidt model, to calculate g-factor trends for isomers in Hg (Z=80), Pb (Z=82), and Po (Z=84) isotopes using a multi-j neutron configuration of {i₁₃/₂ ⊗ f₇/₂ ⊗ p₃/₂} with Ω=13. They also used generalized seniority reduction formulas to calculate Q-moment and B(E2) trends, fitting one experimental data point to account for structural effects like single-particle matrix elements and radial integrals. The results were compared with experimental data from Stone’s compilations (2014, 2019) and the ENSDF database.

## Key numbers

*   The GSSM-calculated g-factor for the 13/2⁺ isomers in Hg, Pb, and Po isotopes is approximately -0.15 n.m., compared to the pure Schmidt value for a neutron i₁₃/₂ orbital of -0.2946 n.m., showing a deviation explained by multi-j occupancy.
*   The measured g-factors for the 13/2⁺ isomers are nearly constant across the isotopic chains, changing by not more than 10% around an average value.
*   For the 12⁺ isomers, the GSSM-calculated g-factor trend is nearly constant for all three isotopic chains, with measured values for ¹⁹⁸,²⁰⁰Po at N=114,116 matching the GSSM line.
*   The GS-calculated Q-moment for the 13/2⁺ isomers increases linearly with neutron number, reaching zero at the middle of the valence space (N=105), with measured values for Hg and Pb isotopes falling within experimental error bars for N=105,107,109.
*   The GS-calculated B(E2) trend for the 12⁺ isomers in Hg and Pb isotopes explains measured values up to N=116, after which a new parabola appears due to a change in multi-j configuration.
*   For the 33/2⁺ isomers in Pb isotopes, the GS-calculated B(E2) trend explains measured values up to N=115, after which values decrease, similar to the behavior of 12⁺ isomers.

## Context

This work extends the generalized seniority scheme and the GSSM, previously applied to Sn and Pb isotopes and N=82 isotones by Maheshwari (2019), to the Hg and Po isotopic chains, which have two proton holes or particles relative to the Z=82 closed shell. The study contradicts the interpretation of Wouters et al. (1991), who attributed g-factor deviations from the Schmidt value to core polarization, instead explaining them via multi-j occupancy. The results enable future predictions for the 33/2⁺ isomers in Hg and Po isotopes and suggest that new experimental measurements of moments and B(E2) values are needed to confirm the generalized seniority assignments, particularly for neutron-deficient isotopes and for Po isotopes where data are scarce.
