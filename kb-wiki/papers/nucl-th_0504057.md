# Angular Moment Analysis of Correlations

> For analyzing anisotropic low relative-velocity correlation-functions and the associated emission sources, we propose an expansion in terms of cartesian spherical harmonics. The expansion coefficients represent angular moments of the investigated functions. The respective coefficients for the correlation and source are directly related to each other via one-dimensional integral transforms. The shape features of the source may be partly read off from the respective features of the correlation function and can be, otherwise, imaged.

# Angular Moment Analysis of Low Relative Velocity Correlations

## Key claim
The paper proposes that anisotropic low-relative-velocity correlation functions and their associated emission sources can be systematically analyzed using an expansion in cartesian spherical harmonics, where the expansion coefficients represent angular moments that are directly related via one-dimensional integral transforms. The authors demonstrate that shape features of the source may be partly read off from the correlation function features and can otherwise be imaged, but note that this approach is most practical for weak anisotropies where only low-ℓ coefficients are significant.

## Method
The authors expand the correlation function R(q) and source function S(r) in terms of cartesian tensor components derived from traceless symmetric rank-ℓ tensors, which are constructed by applying a projection operator D to products of unit direction vectors. The three-dimensional integral relation linking correlation and source is reduced to a set of one-dimensional integral transforms for each ℓ, where the kernel Kℓ(q,r) comes from the Legendre expansion of the relative wavefunction squared. The method is illustrated using an anisotropic Gaussian source with repulsive Coulomb interactions in the classical limit, and imaging is tested by restoring source coefficients from simulated correlation measurements with assumed errors.

## Key numbers
- Source imaging was tested at 80 values of r_c^(-1/2) (inverse square root of head-on return radius), with a constant r.m.s. error of 0.015 assumed for the correlation coefficient measurements.
- Source coefficients were restored within the region r < 17 fm, using 5 points for ℓ=0, 4 points for ℓ=1, and 3 points for ℓ=2.
- The restored source normalization 4π ∫ dr r² S^(0) was 0.99 ± 0.05, compared to the original value of 1.00.
- Restored mean positions: ⟨x⟩ = 2.47 ± 0.11 fm (original 2.45 fm), ⟨z⟩ = 4.25 ± 0.13 fm (original 3.90 fm).
- Restored root-mean-square widths: ⟨(x-⟨x⟩)²⟩^(1/2) = 3.80 ± 0.24 fm (original 3.90 fm), ⟨y²⟩^(1/2) = 3.81 ± 0.22 fm (original 3.91 fm), ⟨(z-⟨z⟩)²⟩^(1/2) = 5.54 ± 0.19 fm (original 5.60 fm).
- The sample Gaussian source was elongated along the beam axis and displaced along the total pair momentum P at an angle of 30° relative to the beam axis.

## Context
This work extends the intensity interferometry technique from astronomy to nuclear physics, where prior approaches by Adler (2001) and Ghetti (2003) had only fitted experimental data with models parameterizing coarse shape features of particle-emitting regions. The method builds on earlier harmonic decomposition work by Brown (1997) and imaging techniques by Brown (1997, 1998), but replaces complex spherical harmonics with real cartesian tensor components that have clearer geometric interpretation. The approach enables systematic model-independent determination of emission source shapes in reactions with multi-particle final states, which could reveal reaction history including differences in emission times and stalling during quark-gluon phase transitions.
