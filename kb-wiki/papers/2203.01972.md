# Analyzing rotational bands in odd-mass nuclei using Effective Field Theory and Bayesian methods

> We recently developed an Effective Field Theory (EFT) for rotational bands in odd-mass nuclei. Here we use EFT expressions to perform a Bayesian analysis of data on the rotational energy levels of $^{99}$Tc, ${}^{155,157}$Gd, ${}^{159}$Dy, ${}^{167, 169}$Er, ${}^{167, 169}$Tm, ${}^{183}$W, ${}^{235}$U and ${}^{239}$Pu. The error model in our Bayesian analysis includes both experimental and EFT truncation uncertainties. It also accounts for the fact that low-energy constants (LECs) at even and odd orders are expected to have different sizes. We use Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling to explore the joint posterior of the EFT and error-model parameters and show both the LECs and the expansion parameter, $Q$, can be reliably determined. We extract the LECs up to fourth order in the EFT and find that, provided we correctly account for EFT truncation errors in our likelihood, results for lower-order LECs are stable as we go to higher orders. LEC results are also stable with respect to the addition of higher-energy data. We extract the expansion parameter for all the nuclei listed above and find a clear correlation between the extracted and the expected $Q$ based on the single-particle and vibrational energy scales. However, the $Q$ that actually determines the convergence of the EFT expansion is markedly smaller than would be naively expected based on those scales.

## Key claim

The Effective Field Theory (EFT) expansion parameter \(Q\) that actually determines the convergence of the rotational-band energy expansion is markedly smaller than would be naively expected based on single-particle and vibrational energy scales, provided the Bayesian analysis correctly accounts for EFT truncation errors in the likelihood.

## Method

The authors applied a Bayesian statistical model to rotational energy-level data for \(K=1/2\) bands in \(^{99}\)Tc, \(^{167,169}\)Er, \(^{167,169}\)Tm, \(^{183}\)W, \(^{235}\)U, and \(^{239}\)Pu, and \(K=3/2\) bands in \(^{155,157}\)Gd and \(^{159}\)Dy. The likelihood included both experimental uncertainties and EFT truncation errors modeled by summing omitted terms up to a cutoff order, with the theory covariance matrix constructed as an outer product of the theory-error vector across energy levels. Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling using the emcee toolkit was employed to explore the joint posterior of the low-energy constants (LECs) up to fourth order, the expansion parameter \(Q\), and separate characteristic sizes for even-order and odd-order dimensionless coefficients \(\bar{c}_{even}\) and \(\bar{c}_{odd}\).

## Key numbers

* For \(^{169}\)Er, the posterior for the LEC \(A_1\) was extracted at multiple EFT orders and showed consistent median values within 68% credible intervals as a function of the maximum spin \(I_{max}\) included, with error bands encompassing the 16th and 84th percentiles.
* For \(^{239}\)Pu, the posteriors for the LECs \(B_1\) and \(B\) were extracted at N\(^4\)LO with \(k_{max}=10\) (six omitted terms), showing stable central values and narrowing distributions as \(I_{max}\) increased.
* For \(^{167}\)Er at N\(^4\)LO, the posterior distributions for all LECs and error-model parameters were fully converged with \(Q_{cut}=0.16\) and \(\bar{c}_{cut}=22\) for both \(\bar{c}_{even}\) and \(\bar{c}_{odd}\).
* For \(^{99}\)Tc, the LECs and \(Q\) could only be extracted at \(I_{max}=11.5\); for \(^{183}\)W, two levels from the upper end of the data set had to be removed for numerical stability.

## Context

This work extends the EFT for rotational bands in odd-mass nuclei developed by Alnamlah (2020) by applying Bayesian parameter-estimation methods previously developed by the BUQEYE collaboration (Wesolowski 2016, 2019, 2021) to simultaneously determine LECs, the expansion parameter, and truncation uncertainties. The analysis builds on the even-even rotor EFT of Papenbrock (2010) and Coello Perez (2015), and enables reliable extraction of LECs with uncertainty quantification across multiple nuclei, showing that lower-order LEC results are stable as higher-order terms and additional data are included.
