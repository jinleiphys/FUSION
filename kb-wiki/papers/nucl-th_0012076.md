# Model-independent definition and determination of electromagnetic formfactors and multipole moments

> A theoretical method for the systematic definition and determination of Cartesian and spherical electromagnetic (onshell) formfactors and multipole moments for particles or composite systems from electromagnetic Breit-frame current distributions is presented. The method presented is free of sign ambiguities and is not based on the underlying analytical substructure of the electromagnetic current distributions. By construction the method contains all higher order momentum derivative terms within the definition of electromagnetic formfactors and multipole moments, which are not taken into account in a lot of existing theoretical calculations.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method for defining and determining electromagnetic formfactors and multipole moments that is free of sign ambiguities and includes all higher-order momentum derivative terms, which are often omitted in existing theoretical calculations; the author argues that these omitted derivative terms yield substantial quantitative uncertainties in the interpretation of theoretical results compared with experimental data, especially at high momentum transfers.

## Method

The author defines Cartesian and spherical electromagnetic multipole moments in configuration space using expansions of the static potential in terms of polynomials \(c^{i_1\ldots i_\ell}(\vec{r})\) and \(b^{\ell m}(\vec{r})\), then performs a Fourier transform to obtain the corresponding momentum-space definitions involving momentum derivatives acting on the Breit-frame current distribution \(j_B^\mu(\vec{q};S;S'_z,S_z)\). These momentum-space definitions are applied to quantum-field-theoretic matrix elements of the current operator in the Breit-frame (where energy transfer \(q^0=0\)), and the resulting expressions are expanded in spherical polarization matrices \(T_{LM}(S;S'_z,S_z)\) to extract generalized electric and magnetic formfactors \(F_E^{\ell m}(q^2;S;S'_z,S_z)\) and \(F_M^{\ell m}(q^2;S;S'_z,S_z)\).

## Key numbers

* For a vector particle (\(S=1\)) of mass \(M\), the electric charge, magnetic dipole moment, and electric quadrupole moment are given by \(e\,F_C(0)\), \(\frac{e}{M}\,F_M(0)\), and \(\frac{e}{M^2}\,F_Q(0)\) respectively, where the formfactors are defined via the Breit-frame current.
* For the deuteron at small \(q^2\), the approximate proportionality of the formfactors is \(|F_1(0)| : |F_2(0)| : |G_1(0)| \simeq 1 : 25 : 1.7\) (citing Garcon 1995).
* The Rosenbluth formfactors are given by \(A(q^2) = F_C^2(q^2) + \frac{8}{9}\,\eta^2\,F_Q^2(q^2) + \frac{2}{3}\,\eta\,F_M^2(q^2)\) and \(B(q^2) = \frac{4}{3}\,\eta\,(\eta+1)\,F_M^2(q^2)\), with \(\eta := -q^2/(2M)^2\).

## Context

This work extends the formalism of electromagnetic formfactors for particles and composite systems, addressing ambiguities in sign conventions and the omission of momentum derivative terms that appear in the exact Fourier transform from configuration to momentum space. The author critiques prior definitions by authors such as Gourdin (1974), Brodsky and Hiller (1992), and Zuilhof and Tjon (1980) for using incomplete identifying equations that neglect intrinsic contributions, and notes that a first application to the deuteron within a Bethe-Salpeter framework (Kleefeld 2000) shows sizable effects from these intrinsic contributions. The method enables unambiguous comparison of theoretical calculations with experimental data, particularly at high momentum transfers where derivative terms become quantitatively relevant.
