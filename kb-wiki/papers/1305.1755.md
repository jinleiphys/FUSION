# Nucleosynthesis in the accretion disks of Type II collapsars

> We investigate nucleosynthesis inside the gamma-ray burst (GRB) accretion disks formed by the Type II collapsars. In these collapsars, the core collapse of massive stars first leads to the formation of a proto-neutron star and a mild supernova explosion is driven. However, this supernova ejecta lack momentum and falls back onto the neutron star which gets transformed to a stellar mass black hole. In order to study the hydrodynamics and nucleosynthesis of such an accretion disk formed from the fallback material of the supernova ejecta, we use the well established hydrodynamic models. In such a disk neutrino cooling becomes important in the inner disk where the temperature and density are higher. Higher the accretion rate (dot{M}), higher is the density and temperature in the disks. In this work we deal with accretion disks with relatively low accretion rates: 0.001 M_sun s^{-1} \lesssim dot{M} \lesssim 0.01 M_sun s^{-1} and hence these disks are predominantly advection dominated. We use He-rich and Si-rich abundances as the initial condition of nucleosynthesis at the outer disk, and being equipped with the disk hydrodynamics and the nuclear network code, we study the abundance evolution as matter inflows and falls into the central object. We investigate the variation in the nucleosynthesis products in the disk with the change in the initial abundance at the outer disk and also with the change in the mass accretion rate. We report the synthesis of several unusual nuclei like {31}P, {39}K, {43}Sc, {35}Cl, and various isotopes of titanium, vanadium, chromium, manganese and copper. We also confirm that isotopes of iron, cobalt, nickel, argon, calcium, sulphur and silicon get synthesized in the disk, as shown by previous authors. Much of these heavy elements thus synthesized are ejected from the disk via outflows and hence they should leave their signature in observed data.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that in Type II collapsar accretion disks with relatively low accretion rates (0.001 to 0.01 solar masses per second), nucleosynthesis of heavy elements occurs predominantly in the advection-dominated outer regions, and the synthesis of several unusual nuclei is reported for the first time in this context, though the authors note that Swift observations have not yet confirmed the emission lines predicted from these elements.

## Method
The authors constructed a steady, axisymmetric, height-averaged 1.5-dimensional hydrodynamic model of an accretion disk around a 3 solar mass Schwarzschild black hole, using a pseudo-Newtonian potential and including neutrino cooling, radiation pressure, baryon pressure, electron degeneracy pressure, and neutrino pressure. They coupled this disk model with a nuclear reaction network code (modified from Chakrabarti et al. 1987 and Mukhopadhyay & Chakrabarti 2000, with reaction rates from the JINA Reaclib Database) to follow abundance evolution as matter inflows from the outer disk toward the black hole, using either He-rich or Si-rich initial abundances at the outer boundary.

## Key numbers
- Accretion rate range: 0.001 solar masses per second to 0.01 solar masses per second (the characteristic range for Type II collapsar disks)
- Black hole mass: 3 solar masses (Schwarzschild, Kerr parameter a = 0)
- Alpha viscosity parameter: 0.01
- Density and temperature reach values of order 10^10 g/cm^3 and 10^10 K near the inner edge of the disk
- For the low accretion rate case (0.001 solar masses per second), the outermost zone from approximately 1000 to 800 gravitational radii is dominated by calcium-40
- The helium-rich zone extends from approximately 80 gravitational radii inward to the inner edge for the low accretion rate case
- For the higher accretion rate case (0.01 solar masses per second), the zones shift outward and the outermost zone becomes rich in iron-54 and nickel-56

## Context
This work extends the collapsar model of MacFadyen and Woosley (1999) and the nucleosynthesis studies of Matsuba et al. (2004) and Kohri et al. (2005) by focusing specifically on Type II (fallback) collapsars with lower accretion rates, where heavy element synthesis is possible unlike in Type I collapsars where temperatures exceed 10^10 K and only protons and neutrons survive. The results enable predictions for the chemical signatures that should be observable in GRB afterglows via outflows from these disks, and the authors plan to study nucleosynthesis in the outflow in a subsequent work (Banerjee and Mukhopadhyay, submitted).
