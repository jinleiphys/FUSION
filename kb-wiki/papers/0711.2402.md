# Neutron star versus heavy-ion data: is the nuclear equation of state hard or soft?

> Recent astrophysical observations of neutron stars and heavy-ion data are confronted with our present understanding of the equation of state of dense hadronic matter. Emphasis is put on the possible role of the presence of hyperons in the interior of compact stars. We argue that data from low-mass pulsars provide an important cross-check between high-density astrophysics and heavy-ion physics.

## Key claim

The apparent conflict between a soft nuclear equation of state (EoS) extracted from heavy-ion kaon production data and a hard EoS inferred from high-mass neutron star measurements is resolved because the two data sets probe different density regimes: heavy-ion data constrain the EoS up to about 2–3 times nuclear saturation density (n₀), while neutron star maximum masses are determined by the EoS above 5n₀, where unknown high-density physics (hyperons, quark matter) dominates.

## Method

The authors model nucleonic matter using a Skyrme-type energy-per-nucleon ansatz with parameters fixed by saturation properties (density, binding energy, compression modulus K, asymmetry energy S₀ with density-dependence exponent α). They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation to compute neutron star mass-radius relations for various compression moduli and asymmetry parameters. Separately, they use relativistic mean-field (RMF) models with different effective nucleon masses and vector selfinteractions to compare the Schrödinger equivalent nucleon potential against the potential required by KaoS heavy-ion data (K ≈ 200 MeV). They then add hyperons to the RMF models, fixing hyperon couplings via SU(6) symmetry and hypernuclear data, and recompute the mass-radius relations.

## Key numbers

- For nucleonic matter with compression modulus K > 160 MeV and asymmetry exponent α = 1.0 or 1.1, the maximum neutron star mass exceeds 2 M⊙; for α = 0.7, M > 1.6 M⊙ for K > 160 MeV and M > 2 M⊙ for K > 220 MeV.
- Changing the asymmetry energy S₀ between 28 and 32 MeV shifts the maximum mass by at most ΔM = ±0.1 M⊙ for low K.
- The maximum central density for nucleonic matter is n_c = (7–8)n₀ for α = 1.0, 1.1 and up to 10n₀ for α = 0.7.
- The EoS remains causal up to K = 340 MeV (maximum mass 2.6 M⊙) for α = 1.0, 1.1 and up to K = 280 MeV for α = 0.7.
- For RMF models with a soft nucleon potential (consistent with KaoS data), maximum masses for nucleonic matter are: gl78 → 2.04 M⊙, djm-c → 1.98 M⊙, bodz0 → 1.66 M⊙, bm-a → 1.54 M⊙.
- Including hyperons reduces maximum masses substantially: gl78 → 1.53 M⊙, djm-c → 1.46 M⊙, bodz0 → 1.30 M⊙, bm-a → 1.27 M⊙.
- The lightest known pulsar J1756-2251 has mass M = (1.18 ± 0.02) M⊙; the Hulse-Taylor pulsar mass is M = (1.4414 ± 0.0002) M⊙.
- A 1.2 M⊙ neutron star has a maximum density of n = 2n₀ in the non-relativistic models.

## Context

This work reconciles the soft EoS (K ≈ 200 MeV) required by KaoS heavy-ion kaon production data (Sturm 2000, Fuchs 2000, Hartnack 2005, Forster 2007) with the stiff EoS implied by high-mass pulsar measurements such as EXO 0748–676 (Ozel 2006) and pulsars in Terzan 5 (Ransom 2005, Freire 2007). The authors extend earlier analyses by Sagert (2007) and show that the two data sets probe different density regimes, enabling future cross-checks via low-mass pulsar radius measurements. The inclusion of hyperons via SU(6) symmetry and hypernuclear potentials (Schaffner-Bielich 2000) demonstrates that a confirmed heavy pulsar above about 1.6 M⊙ would severely constrain hyperonic degrees of freedom in neutron star cores, potentially creating a tension with hypernuclear data.
