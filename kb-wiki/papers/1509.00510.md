# Random-matrix approach to the statistical compound nuclear reaction at low energies using the Monte-Carlo technique

> Using a random-matrix approach and Monte-Carlo simulations, we generate scattering matrices and cross sections for compound-nucleus reactions. In the absence of direct reactions we compare the average cross sections with the analytic solution given by the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) triple integral, and with predictions of statistical approaches such as the ones due to Moldauer, to Hofmann, Richert, Tepel, and Weidenmüller, and to Kawai, Kerman, and McVoy. We find perfect agreement with the GOE triple integral and display the limits of validity of the latter approaches. We establish a criterion for the width of the energy-averaging interval such that the relative difference between the ensemble-averaged and the energy-averaged scattering matrices lies below a given bound. Direct reactions are simulated in terms of an energy-independent background matrix. In that case, cross sections averaged over the ensemble of Monte-Carlo simulations fully agree with results from the Engelbrecht-Weidenmüller transformation. The limits of other approximate approaches are displayed.

## Key claim

Using Monte-Carlo simulations of the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) scattering matrix, the authors demonstrate that the exact GOE triple-integral formula of Verbaarschot, Weidenmüller, and Zirnbauer (1985) provides a perfect benchmark for compound-nucleus cross sections, while approximate statistical models (Moldauer, HRTW, KKM) show systematic deviations that become significant for weak absorption or small transmission coefficients.

## Method

The authors generate an ensemble of scattering matrices by drawing random elements of the GOE Hamiltonian \(H^{({\rm GOE})}_{\mu\nu}\) of dimension \(N\) (typically \(N=20\) to \(100\)) and constructing the \(S\)-matrix via Eqs. (SGOE) and (DGOE), with coupling strengths \(W_{\mu a}\) fixed by the transmission coefficients \(T_a\). Ensemble averages over typically 10,000 to 1,000,000 realizations are compared with the analytic GOE triple-integral of Verbaarschot et al. (1985) and with the approximate formulas of Moldauer (1980), Hofmann-Richert-Tepel-Weidenmüller (HRTW, 1975), and Kawai-Kerman-McVoy (KKM, 1973). For cases with direct reactions, an energy-independent background matrix is introduced and results are compared with the Engelbrecht-Weidenmüller transformation.

## Key numbers

* For \(T_a = T_b = 0.99\) with two channels, the Monte-Carlo ensemble average of the fluctuating elastic cross section \(\overline{|S_{aa}^{\rm fl}|^2}\) is 0.660, while the Hauser-Feshbach formula gives 0.495 (a 25% difference); the GOE triple-integral gives 0.661.
* For \(T_a = T_b = 0.5\), the MC elastic average is 0.351, the GOE result is 0.351, and the Hauser-Feshbach value is 0.250.
* For \(T_a = T_b = 0.1\), the MC elastic average is 0.0733, the GOE result is 0.0734, and the Hauser-Feshbach value is 0.0500.
* The HRTW model gives elastic averages of 0.0737, 0.352, and 0.661 for \(T=0.1, 0.5, 0.99\) respectively; Moldauer gives 0.0734, 0.349, and 0.665.
* The GOE triple-integral remains accurate even for as few as \(N=3\) resonances, though this is described as "extreme" and "not realistic."
* The convergence criterion for MC averages required \(|\Delta \overline{S}_{ab}| < 10^{-5}\).

## Context

This work extends the exact GOE scattering theory of Verbaarschot, Weidenmüller, and Zirnbauer (1985) by providing the first direct Monte-Carlo implementation of the full GOE \(S\)-matrix, avoiding the phenomenological assumptions about level and width distributions used in earlier statistical approaches by Moldauer (1975, 1980) and Hofmann et al. (1975, 1980). The results enable systematic validation of approximate width-fluctuation correction formulas used in nuclear data evaluation, and establish a criterion for when energy-averaged cross sections can be replaced by ensemble averages. The study also clarifies the role of direct reactions via the Engelbrecht-Weidenmüller transformation, which had not been tested against full GOE simulations before.
