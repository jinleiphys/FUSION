# Transient photon production in a QGP

> We discuss the shortcomings of a formula that has been used in the literature to compute the number of photons emitted by a hot or dense system during a finite time, and show that the transient effects it predicts for the photon rate are unphysical.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a previously published formula for computing the number of photons emitted by a hot or dense quark-gluon system during a finite time (Boyanovsky et al., 2004) is based on unphysical assumptions, specifically that the electromagnetic interactions are turned on and off at finite initial and final times, and that the resulting transient effects predicted for the photon rate are therefore unphysical.

## Method

The authors derive the photon yield formula from Boyanovsky et al. (2004) using the standard S-matrix formalism of quantum field theory, but explicitly assuming that the electromagnetic interactions are turned off at a finite final time t_f and turned on at a finite initial time t_i. They then analyze the consequences of keeping t_f finite, showing that the resulting expression for the photon production rate contains unphysical vacuum contributions and leads to an infinite total radiated energy per unit time, violating energy conservation.

## Key numbers

- The photon production rate formula derived is: 2ω dN/(dt d³x d³k) = -e² ∫_{-∞}^{+∞} (dE/2π) [sin((E-ω)(t_f-t_i))/(E-ω)] Π^μ_μ_{-+}(E,k), where t_f and t_i are the finite measurement and initial times respectively.
- The paper states that the r.h.s. of this formula is "infinite at any fixed photon energy ω" due to unphysical vacuum contributions.
- The remaining terms after vacuum subtraction lead to an energy dependence "too hard for being integrable," such that the total energy radiated as photons per unit time by a finite volume is infinite.

## Context

This work extends the critique of the real-time approach to photon production in a quark-gluon plasma originally proposed by Boyanovsky et al. (2004), which claimed large transient effects. The authors build on their own prior work (Fraga, Gelis, and Schiff, 2004) to provide a simpler derivation showing the formula's illegitimate character. The paper contradicts the claim of Boyanovsky et al. (2004) that the transient effects are physical, and it enables future work to avoid using this flawed formula for computing photon yields from finite-lifetime systems.
