# Uncertainty quantification in $(p,n)$ reactions

> Charge-exchange reactions are versatile probes for nuclear structure. In particular, when populating isobaric analog states, these reactions are used to study isovector nuclear densities and neutron skins. The quality of the information extracted from charge-exchange data depends on the accuracy of the reaction models and their inputs; this work addresses these two points. First, we quantify the uncertainties due to effective nucleon-nucleus interactions by propagating the parameter posterior distributions of the recent global optical model KDUQ [1] to $(p,n)$ reaction observables populating the isobaric analogue state, at beam energies in the range of $25-160$ MeV. Our analysis, focusing on $^{48}$Ca, shows that the total parametric uncertainties on the cross sections are around 60-100%. The source of this uncertainty is mainly the transition operator as the uncertainties from the distorted waves alone are less than about 15%. Second, we perform a comparison between two- and three-body models that both describe the dynamics of the reaction within the DWBA. The predictions from these two models are similar and generally agree with the available data, suggesting that 1-step DWBA is sufficient to describe the reaction process. Only at a beam energy of 25 MeV there are possibly signs that a 1-step assumption is not fully correct. This work provides motivation for the quantification of uncertainties associated with the transition operator in three-body model. It also suggests that further constraint of the optical potential parameters is needed for increased model precision.

## Key claim

The total parametric uncertainties on charge-exchange cross sections populating the isobaric analogue state in $^{48}$Ca are around 60-100%, with the dominant source being the transition operator rather than the distorted waves (which contribute less than about 15% uncertainty); the authors note that these uncertainties represent a lower bound as model uncertainties from the simplified DWBA treatment have not been quantified.

## Method

The authors propagated the 416 parameter posterior samples from the global optical model KDUQ (a Bayesian extension of the Koning-Delaroche potential) to compute $(p,n)$ charge-exchange cross sections for $^{48}$Ca populating the isobaric analogue state in $^{48}$Sc, at beam energies of 25, 35, 45, 135, and 160 MeV. They performed calculations within both a two-body (macroscopic) framework using the code CHEX and a three-body (microscopic) framework using the code DW81 with Love-Franey $T_{NN}$ interactions and shell-model transition densities from the $pf$ shell with the gx1a interaction.

## Key numbers

- Total parametric uncertainties on cross sections at the peak of the angular distribution range from 62% (at 25 MeV) to 99% (at 160 MeV) for the two-body model with full uncertainty quantification.
- Uncertainties from distorted waves alone are 10-15% across all beam energies studied (25-160 MeV), as shown in Table 1 for both two-body and three-body models.
- At 25 MeV, the absolute half-width of the 68% credible interval at the peak is 0.7 mb/sr (full UQ) versus 0.3 mb/sr (UQ-DW) for the two-body model.
- At 160 MeV, the absolute half-width of the 68% credible interval at the peak is 17.8 mb/sr (full UQ) versus 0.5 mb/sr (UQ-DW) for the two-body model.

## Context

This work extends the study of Whitehead et al. (2022) by using the global KDUQ optical potential (which intrinsically includes correlations between neutron and proton optical potentials, unlike the independent calibrations in that prior work) and by expanding the energy range to 25-160 MeV. It also provides the first direct comparison between two-body and three-body models for charge-exchange reactions including uncertainty quantification, building on preliminary steps in a thesis by Poxon-Pearon. The results motivate future inclusion of charge-exchange data in optical potential calibrations to reduce uncertainties, and suggest that further constraint of optical potential parameters is needed for increased model precision.
