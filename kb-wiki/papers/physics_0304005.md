# Pairing of fermions in atomic traps and nuclei

> Pairing gaps for fermionic atoms in harmonic oscillator traps are calculated for a wide range of interaction strengths and particle number, and compared to pairing in nuclei. Especially systems, where the pairing gap exceeds the level spacing but is smaller than the shell splitting $\hbarω$, are studied which applies to most trapped Fermi atomic systems as well as to finite nuclei. When solving the gap equation for a large trap with such multi-level pairing, one finds that the matrix elements between nearby harmonic oscillator levels and the quasi-particle energies lead to a double logarithm of the gap, and a pronounced shell structure at magic numbers. It is argued that neutron and proton pairing in nuclei belongs to the class of multi-level pairing, that their shell structure follows naturally and that the gaps scale as $\sim A^{-1/3}$ - all in qualitative agreement with odd-even staggering of nuclear binding energies. Pairing in large systems are related to that in the bulk limit. For large nuclei the neutron and proton superfluid gaps approach the asymptotic value in infinite nuclear matter: $Δ\simeq 1.1$ MeV.

# Pairing of fermions in atomic traps and nuclei

## Key claim

Pairing gaps in finite fermionic systems such as trapped atomic gases and nuclei can be understood within a unified framework of "multi-level pairing," where the pairing gap exceeds the single level spacing but remains smaller than the shell splitting. The paper argues that neutron and proton pairing in nuclei belongs to this multi-level pairing class, and that the observed shell structure and A^{-1/3} scaling of nuclear pairing gaps follow naturally from this description, though the comparison to experimental odd-even staggering data remains qualitative rather than quantitative.

## Method

The author solves the Bogoliubov-de Gennes gap equation for fermions in a three-dimensional spherical harmonic oscillator potential with an attractive delta-function interaction, using WKB approximations for the radial wave functions and numerical integration for the overlap integrals. The pairing gap is calculated for half-filled levels across a range of particle numbers and interaction strengths, and the results are compared to experimental nuclear binding energy data using the three-point filter method for odd-even staggering. The effective coupling constant for nuclei is treated as a single adjustable parameter, with the harmonic oscillator frequency scaled as 41 MeV·A^{-1/3} and the level splitting parameterized to account for spin-orbit splitting and magic numbers.

## Key numbers

- For large nuclei, the neutron and proton superfluid gaps approach the asymptotic value in infinite nuclear matter: Δ ≃ 1.1 MeV.
- The harmonic oscillator frequency for nuclei scales as ħω ≃ 41 MeV · A^{-1/3}.
- For single-level pairing at the top of a shell (l = n_F), the gap is E_{n_F l=n_F} = (|a|/√π a_osc) ħω.
- For single-level pairing at the bottom of a shell (l = 0), the gap is E_{n_F l=0} = (√2/π√n_F) (|a|/a_osc) ħω.
- For single-shell pairing, the supergap G = (32√(2n_F+3)/15π²) (|a|/a_osc) ħω.
- In the dense strongly interacting limit (k_F|a| ≥ 1), the pairing gap in bulk is Δ ≃ 0.54 E_F.
- The condition for single-shell pairing in atomic traps is n_F ≲ 64/5, corresponding to N ≲ 10³ trapped particles.
- The critical interaction for closed shells is G_c = ħω / 2 ln(4γ n_F), where γ = e^C and C = 0.577.

## Context

This work extends earlier pairing calculations for harmonic oscillator traps by Heiselberg and Mottelson (2002) and by Bruun and Heiselberg (2002) to the intermediate "multi-level" regime where the pairing gap lies between the single-level spacing and the shell splitting. The paper connects these results to nuclear pairing, showing that nuclei fall into this multi-level regime, and provides a framework for understanding the shell structure and A^{-1/3} scaling observed in odd-even staggering of nuclear binding energies. The results enable extrapolation of pairing gaps to very large nuclei and nuclear matter, with implications for neutron star superfluidity. The double-logarithmic structure of the gap equation in this regime is noted to be analogous to that appearing in color superconductivity in quark matter as studied by Son (1999), though the physical mechanism differs.
