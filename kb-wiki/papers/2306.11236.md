# Constraining the Woods-Saxon potential in fusion reactions based on the neural network

> The accurate determination of the nuclear interaction potential is essential for predicting the fusion cross sections and understanding the reaction mechanism, which plays an important role in the synthesis of superheavy elements. In this work, the neural network, which combines with the calculations of the fusion cross sections via the Hill-Wheeler formula, is developed to optimize the parameters of the Woods-Saxon potential by comparing the experimental values. The correlations between the parameters of Woods-Saxon potential and the reaction partners, which can be quantitatively fitted to a sigmoid-like function with the mass numbers, have been displayed manifestly for the first time. This study could promote the accurate estimation of nucleus-nucleus interaction potential in low energy heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The neural network, combined with the Hill-Wheeler formula for fusion cross-section calculations, can constrain the three parameters of the Woods-Saxon potential (depth \(V_0\), radius parameter \(r_0\), and diffuseness \(a\)) from experimental fusion data; the correlations between these parameters and the mass numbers of the projectile and target can be quantitatively fitted to a sigmoid-like function for the first time, but the method is limited to reactions above the Coulomb barrier and to relatively light projectile-target combinations where quasi-fission is minimal.

## Method

The authors constructed a neural network that takes the proton number \(Z\) and mass number \(A\) of the projectile and target as input features and outputs the three Woods-Saxon potential parameters (\(V_0\), \(r_0\), \(a\)). These parameters are then used in the Hill-Wheeler formula to compute fusion cross sections, which are compared with experimental values to define a loss function; because the loss is on cross sections rather than the potential parameters directly, weight coefficients (1, 0.001, and 0.01 for \(V_0\), \(r_0\), and \(a\), respectively) were assigned to propagate the loss back to the parameters. The network was trained on 283 projectile-target combinations (with 30 each for validation and testing) from an online dataset, using three hidden layers (32, 64, 128 neurons), Tanh activation, and the Adam optimizer with a learning rate decreasing from \(10^{-4}\) to \(10^{-6}\), with training truncated at epoch 500 to prevent overfitting.

## Key numbers

- The neural network achieved a loss of 1.26 on the testing set for the Hill-Wheeler calculation (\(\sigma_{\rm NN}\)), compared to 4.47 for the fixed-parameter model \(\sigma_{\rm Wang}\) (Ref. Wang 2017 systematics with \(V_0 = 80\) MeV, \(r_0 = 1.16\) fm, and a specific formula for \(a\)).
- The fitted sigmoid-like function for the diffuseness parameter \(a\) has coefficients \(c_1 = 1.0259\) fm, \(c_2 = 1.3603\), \(c_3 = -0.0877\), and \(c_4 = -0.0352\), with a coefficient of determination \(R^2 > 0.96\).
- The fitted sigmoid-like function for the radius parameter \(r_0\) has coefficients \(d_1 = 19.7344\) fm, \(d_2 = 16.5655\), and \(d_3 = -0.0343\), with \(R^2 > 0.96\).
- The linear fit for the depth parameter \(V_0\) uses coefficients \(b_1 = 0.4341\) MeV, \(b_2 = -0.1449\) MeV, \(b_3 = -0.2446\) MeV, \(b_4 = 0.0955\) MeV, and \(b_5 = 78.6446\) MeV.
- The constrained values of \(r_0\) range from about 1.14 fm to approximately 1.19 fm; \(V_0\) falls within roughly 77–83 MeV; \(a\) ranges from about 0.4 to 0.8 fm.
- The dataset included 343 reactions with \(Z_{\rm P} Z_{\rm T} \leq 1600\) and at least three experimental data points above the Coulomb barrier, where the barrier was estimated using a formula from Wen (2022).

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to constrain Woods-Saxon potential parameters for specific systems, such as those focusing on \(^{12}\)C or \(^{16}\)O induced reactions (Luo 2022, Evers 2008), by providing a global, data-driven optimization using neural networks. It introduces a physics-informed approach that integrates the Hill-Wheeler formula directly into the network training, contrasting with purely data-driven machine learning applications in nuclear physics (e.g., Gao 2021 for nuclear masses, Niu 2018 for masses, Wu 2020 for charge radii). The resulting analytical sigmoid-like fits for \(a\) and \(r_0\) as functions of mass number enable convenient modeling of heavy-ion fusion reactions and could promote more accurate estimation of nucleus-nucleus interaction potentials for superheavy element synthesis.
