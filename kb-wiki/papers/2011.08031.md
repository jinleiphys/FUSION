# Transmutation of protons in a strong electromagnetic field

> The process of turning a proton into a neutron, positron and electron-neutrino in a strong plane-wave electromagnetic field is studied. This process is forbidden in vacuum and is seen to feature an exponential suppression factor which is non-perturbative in the field amplitude. The suppression is alleviated when the proton experiences a field strength of about ten times the Schwinger critical field in its rest frame or larger. Around this threshold the lifetime of the proton, in its rest frame, is comparable to the conventional neutron decay lifetime. As the field strength is increased, the proton lifetime becomes increasingly short. We investigate possible scenarios where this process may be observed in the laboratory using an ultra-intense laser and a high-energy proton beam with the conclusion, however, that it would be very challenging to observe this effect in the near future.

# Transmutation of protons in a strong electromagnetic field

## Key claim

Protons can transmute into neutrons, positrons, and electron-neutrinos in a strong plane-wave electromagnetic field, a process forbidden in vacuum, but the effect is exponentially suppressed and only becomes appreciable when the proton experiences a field strength of about ten times the Schwinger critical field in its rest frame. Even under optimistic future laser and accelerator scenarios, the probability per proton per collision remains extremely small (below 10⁻¹⁵), making experimental observation very challenging in the near future.

## Method

The authors treat the weak-interaction process using the Vector-Axial Vector (V-A) point interaction with the Fermi constant G_F, describing the proton and positron via Volkov states (exact solutions of the Dirac equation in a plane-wave field) and the neutron and neutrino via free particle wave functions. They apply the locally constant field approximation (LCFA) valid for high-intensity fields (classical nonlinearity parameter ξ ≫ 1), expand the phase to cubic order in the relative phase variable, and evaluate the resulting integrals analytically in terms of modified Bessel functions of the second kind. The probability per unit proton proper time is then computed for a constant crossed field and integrated over a Gaussian laser pulse shape to obtain total probabilities for specific experimental configurations.

## Key numbers

- The proton transmutation process turns on at χ_P ≈ 10, where χ_P is the ratio of the field strength experienced by the proton in its rest frame to the Schwinger critical field E_cr ≈ 1.3 × 10¹⁶ V/cm.
- The asymptotic probability per unit proton proper time at χ_P ≪ 1 is dP/dτ ≈ 5.0 × 10⁻⁶ χ_P⁴ exp(−9.4/χ_P) s⁻¹.
- The proton lifetime at threshold (χ_P ∼ 10) is comparable to the conventional neutron decay lifetime.
- For a 7 TeV proton colliding head-on with a laser of peak intensity 1.0 × 10²⁶ W/cm² (χ_P = 203), the probability per proton is 3.8 × 10⁻¹⁷, and per collision (with 2.2 × 10¹¹ protons) is 8.3 × 10⁻⁶.
- For a 50 TeV proton (Future Circular Collider) with the same laser intensity (χ_P = 724), the probability per proton is 1.4 × 10⁻¹⁵, and per collision (with 1.0 × 10¹¹ protons) is 1.4 × 10⁻⁴.
- The energy gap to be overcome is (m_N + m_e − m_P)c² ≈ 1.8 MeV, comparable to 2m_e c² ≈ 1 MeV for electron-positron pair production.
- The stationary-phase analysis yields ζ* ≈ 0.32 for the positron variable at threshold.

## Context

This work extends earlier semi-quantitative estimates by Ritus (who studied modification of vacuum-allowed processes and made an estimate of proton transmutation probability via analytical continuation in a constant crossed field) and calculations by Lyulka (1985) who computed the probability in a constant crossed field. The authors improve upon prior treatments by using spin-1/2 particles with the full V-A weak interaction rather than spin-0 approximations or parity-preserving interactions used in earlier work by Ginzburg (1965), Blasone (2020), and others. The results enable future experimental searches at the intersection of ultra-intense laser facilities and high-energy proton accelerators, though the calculated probabilities indicate that observation would require substantial advances beyond currently planned facilities.
