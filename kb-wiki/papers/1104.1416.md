# Relativistic viscoelastic fluid mechanics

> A detailed study is carried out for the relativistic theory of viscoelasticity which was recently constructed on the basis of Onsager's linear nonequilibrium thermodynamics. After rederiving the theory using a local argument with the entropy current, we show that this theory universally reduces to the standard relativistic Navier-Stokes fluid mechanics in the long time limit. Since effects of elasticity are taken into account, the dynamics at short time scales is modified from that given by the Navier-Stokes equations, so that acausal problems intrinsic to relativistic Navier-Stokes fluids are significantly remedied. We in particular show that the wave equations for the propagation of disturbance around a hydrostatic equilibrium in Minkowski spacetime become symmetric hyperbolic for some range of parameters, so that the model is free of acausality problems. This observation suggests that the relativistic viscoelastic model with such parameters can be regarded as a causal completion of relativistic Navier-Stokes fluid mechanics. By adjusting parameters to various values, this theory can treat a wide variety of materials including elastic materials, Maxwell materials, Kelvin-Voigt materials, and (a nonlinearly generalized version of) simplified Israel-Stewart fluids, and thus we expect the theory to be the most universal description of single-component relativistic continuum materials. We also show that the presence of strains and the corresponding change in temperature are naturally unified through the Tolman law in a generally covariant description of continuum mechanics.

## Key claim

The relativistic viscoelastic model, constructed from Onsager's linear nonequilibrium thermodynamics, universally reduces to standard relativistic Navier-Stokes fluid mechanics in the long time limit, while for some range of parameters the wave equations for disturbances around hydrostatic equilibrium become symmetric hyperbolic, thereby remedying the acausality problems intrinsic to relativistic Navier-Stokes fluids.

## Method

The authors rederive their previously proposed relativistic viscoelastic theory using a local argument with the entropy current, expanding the entropy density to second order in the strain tensor components (spatial strain tensor ε_μν, vector strain ε_μ, and scalar strain θ). They then impose the second law of thermodynamics by requiring positive definiteness of the entropy production rate, which yields coupled linear relations between thermodynamic fluxes (stress tensor, diffusion current, and Lie derivatives of strains) and thermodynamic forces (strains, extrinsic curvature, and gradients of chemical potential over temperature). These relations produce two sets of equations: conservation laws for energy-momentum and particle number with constitutive equations, and rheology equations governing the time evolution of the strain variables.

## Key numbers

- The relaxation time for shear modes is τ_s ≡ λ_1/(η_1 T), where λ_1 is the coefficient in the entropy expansion for ε_⟨μν⟩ and η_1 is a positive semidefinite transport coefficient.
- The relaxation time for vector strain modes is τ_σ ≡ λ_2/(σ_1 T), where λ_2 is the coefficient for ε_μ and σ_1 is a positive semidefinite transport coefficient.
- The relaxation times for scalar modes are τ_± ≡ 2 det γ / [T (P_ζγ ∓ √(P_ζγ² − 4 det γ (det ζ_s + K′²)))], where γ is the 2×2 matrix of coefficients for tr ε and θ, ζ_s is the principal submatrix of the 3×3 symmetric matrix ζ, and K′ is an antisymmetric coupling parameter.
- The matrix γ = ((γ_1, γ_2), (γ_2, γ_3)) is positive semidefinite, and the symmetric matrices η, σ, and ζ are positive semidefinite.

## Context

This work extends the nonrelativistic theory of elasticity and anelasticity by Eckart (1948), which was recently reformulated in a covariant framework by Arroja, Fukuma, and collaborators, into a fully relativistic generally covariant viscoelastic theory. The model generalizes the Israel-Stewart approach (Müller, 1967; Israel, 1976; Israel and Stewart, 1979) by treating strains as additional thermodynamic variables, and it contains as special limiting cases elastic materials, Maxwell materials, Kelvin-Voigt materials, and a nonlinearly generalized version of simplified Israel-Stewart fluids. The theory enables causal numerical simulations of relativistic viscous fluids and provides a unified description of single-component relativistic continuum materials where strains and temperature changes are naturally connected through the Tolman law.
