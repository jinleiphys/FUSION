# Surface Delta Interaction and g factors

> Using an attractive surface delta interaction we obtain wave functions for 2 neutrons (or neutron holes) in the model space of 2 orbits (l=4, j=7/2) and (l=2, j=5/2). If we take the single particle energies to be degenerate we find that the g factors for I=2, 4 and 6 are all the same -namely the orbital g factor of the single nucleon. For a free neutron this quantity zero all 2particle or 2 hole g factors are equal to zero as well.. Only the orbital part of the g -factors contribute - the spin part cancels out. We then consider the effects of introducing a single energy splititng between the 2 orbits. We speculate that g factors for all n can be obtained by a linear approximation.

## Key claim

Using an attractive surface delta interaction (SDI) in the \(g_{7/2}-d_{5/2}\) model space for two neutrons or two neutron holes, the authors find that with degenerate single-particle energies the g factors for \(I=2,4,6\) are all equal to the orbital g factor \(g_l\) (zero for a free neutron), but that introducing a single-particle energy splitting between the two orbits causes the g factors to become strongly dependent on the splitting and on the spin.

## Method

The authors diagonalize the surface delta interaction (SDI) of Green and Moszkowski in a model space consisting of the \(g_{7/2}\) and \(d_{5/2}\) neutron orbits, using a strength \(C_0 = -0.2\) MeV. They compute the g factors \(G(J)\) for two-particle and two-hole states as a function of the single-particle splitting \(E = \epsilon_{g_{7/2}} - \epsilon_{d_{5/2}}\), using both free nucleon g factors (\(g_l=0\), \(g_s=-3.826\)) and quenched effective g factors (\(g_l=-0.1\), \(g_s=-2.678\)). They then propose a linear interpolation to estimate g factors for intermediate numbers of valence neutrons.

## Key numbers

* For degenerate single-particle energies (\(E=0\)) with free neutron g factors, \(G(2^+) = G(4^+) = G(6^+) = 0\) (all equal to \(g_l=0\)).
* With free g factors and \(E = -0.4\) MeV, \(G(2^+) = 0.386\), \(G(4^+) = 0.390\), \(G(6^+) = 0.323\); with \(E = +0.4\) MeV, \(G(2^+) = -0.669\), \(G(4^+) = -0.708\), \(G(6^+) = -0.056\).
* With quenched g factors and \(E = -0.4\) MeV, \(G(2^+) = 0.160\), \(G(4^+) = 0.162\), \(G(6^+) = 0.118\); with \(E = +0.4\) MeV, \(G(2^+) = -0.551\), \(G(4^+) = -0.577\), \(G(6^+) = -0.138\).
* For the nucleus \({}^{113}\)Sn, the \(J=7/2^+ - J=5/2^+\) splitting is 0.33 MeV; using \(E = -0.33\) MeV with free g factors gives \(G(2^+) = 0.371\), \(G(4^+) = 0.375\), \(G(6^+) = 0.274\).
* For \({}^{126}\)Sn with \(E=0\), the model predicts \(G(2) = -0.1096\), \(G(4) = -0.5812\), \(G(6) = -0.2758\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous SDI study of two-proton holes in \({}^{86}\)Kr (Zamick, 2015) to neutrons in the \(g_{7/2}-d_{5/2}\) space, and is motivated by experimental g-factor measurements in tin isotopes by Allmond (2015) and Kumbartzki (2012). The vanishing of the spin contribution at degeneracy is explained by pseudo-spin symmetry ideas of Hecht (1970) and Arima (1970), and by the quasi-spin formulation of Kerman (1961) and Talmi's generalized seniority scheme. The results enable a simple benchmark for more sophisticated calculations and highlight the extreme sensitivity of g factors to single-particle splitting in this region, which the authors note makes it difficult to pin down optimum values.
