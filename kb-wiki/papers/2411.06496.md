# Heavy Flavor Production at the Large Hadron Collider: A Machine Learning Approach

> Charmonia suppression has been considered as a smoking gun signature of quark-gluon plasma. However, the Large Hadron Collider has observed a lower degree of suppression as compared to the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider energies, due to regeneration effects in heavy-ion collisions. Though proton collisions are considered to be the baseline measurements to characterize a hot and dense medium formation in heavy-ion collisions, LHC proton collisions with its new physics of heavy-ion-like QGP signatures have created new challenges. To understand this, the inclusive charmonia production at the forward rapidities in the dimuon channel is compared with the corresponding measurements in the dielectron channel at the midrapidity as a function of final state charged particle multiplicity. None of the theoretical models quantitatively reproduce the experimental findings leaving out a lot of room for theory. To circumvent this and find a reasonable understanding, we use machine learning tools to separate prompt and nonprompt charmonia and open charm mesons using the decay daughter track properties and the decay topologies of the mother particles. Using PYTHIA8 data, we train the machine learning models and successfully separate prompt and nonprompt charm hadrons from the inclusive sample to study various directions of their production dynamics. This study enables a domain of using machine learning techniques, which can be used in the experimental analysis to better understand charm hadron production and build possible theoretical understanding.

## Key claim
The paper argues that while inclusive charmonia suppression is a key signature of quark-gluon plasma, none of the existing theoretical models quantitatively reproduce the experimental measurements of multiplicity-dependent J/ψ production in proton-proton collisions at LHC energies, motivating the use of machine learning to separate prompt and nonprompt charm hadrons for better understanding of production dynamics.

## Method
The author compares forward-rapidity (2.5 < y < 4) inclusive J/ψ production in the dimuon channel measured by ALICE at √s = 5.02, 7, and 13 TeV with midrapidity measurements in the dielectron channel, as a function of final-state charged particle multiplicity. Using PYTHIA8 simulated data for pp collisions at √s = 13 TeV and 5.02 TeV, gradient-boosting-decision-tree-based machine learning models (XGBoost and LightGBM) are trained on track-level properties and decay topologies, including pseudoproper decay length, invariant mass, transverse momentum, and pseudorapidity, to separate prompt from nonprompt J/ψ and D⁰ mesons.

## Key numbers
- Forward rapidity J/ψ yields are measured at √s = 5.02, 7, and 13 TeV in the dimuon channel (2.5 < y < 4), showing a nearly linear rise with midrapidity charged particle relative multiplicity.
- The nonprompt-to-prompt D⁰ ratio at midrapidity (|y| < 0.5) for minimum-bias pp collisions at √s = 5.02 TeV shows a linear increase with pT up to pT ≈ 12 GeV/c.
- The J/ψ fraction from b-hadron decay (f_B) as a function of pT at midrapidity for minimum-bias pp collisions at √s = 13 TeV is estimated using XGBoost and LightGBM, with trends similar to ALICE experimental data.

## Context
This work extends the longstanding study of charmonia suppression as a quark-gluon plasma signature, which was first proposed at RHIC energies and later observed with lower suppression at LHC due to regeneration effects (ALICE 2015). The paper directly addresses the failure of existing theoretical models, including Coherent Particle Production, CGC with improved color evaporation model, 3-Pomeron CGC, Percolation, EPOS3, and PYTHIA8, to quantitatively describe multiplicity-dependent J/ψ production across rapidity and production channels. The machine learning approach enables future experimental analyses to separate prompt and nonprompt charm hadrons without relying solely on statistical template fitting or dedicated secondary vertex detectors, opening the door to better theoretical understanding of charm hadron production dynamics in both pp and heavy-ion collisions.
