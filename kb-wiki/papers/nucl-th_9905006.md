# Prompt muon-induced fission: a probe for nuclear energy dissipation

> We solve the time-dependent Dirac equation for a muon which is initially bound to a fissioning actinide nucleus. The computations are carried out on a 3-D cartesian lattice utilizing the Basis-Spline collocation method. The muon dynamics is sensitive to the nuclear energy dissipation between the outer fission barrier and the scission point. From a comparison with experimental data we find a dissipated energy of about 10 MeV and a fission time delay due to friction of order $2 \times 10^{-21}$ s.

# Prompt muon-induced fission: a probe for nuclear energy dissipation

## Key claim
By solving the time-dependent Dirac equation for a muon initially bound to a fissioning actinide nucleus and comparing with experimental data, the author extracts a dissipated energy of about 10 MeV and a fission time delay of order 2 × 10^{-21} s, but notes that the weak dependence of the muon attachment probability on dissipated energy and the size of experimental error bars prevent extraction of very precise information about the amount of energy dissipation.

## Method
The time-dependent Dirac equation for the muonic spinor wavefunction is solved on a 3-D Cartesian lattice using the Basis-Spline collocation method, with the fission dynamics treated classically via a single collective coordinate R, a coordinate-dependent mass parameter, an empirical double-humped fission potential, and a linear friction force that accounts for nuclear energy dissipation between the outer fission barrier and the scission point. The muon attachment probability to the light fission fragment P_L is computed for 237Np at 9.5 MeV excitation energy from the 3d → 1s muonic transition, and these theoretical values are weighted by the measured relative mass distribution and integrated over experimental mass bins for comparison with data from Risse et al. (1991).

## Key numbers
- Dissipated energy extracted from comparison with experiment: about 10 MeV for 237Np (first mass bin A_L = 118.5 → 111.5)
- Fission time delay from saddle to scission due to friction: order 2 × 10^{-21} s
- Muon attachment probability to light fragment for mass bin A_L = 118.5 → 111.5: experimental (25.5 ± 8.5) × 10^{-2}; theoretical 26.0 × 10^{-2} at E_diss = 0 MeV and 22.3 × 10^{-2} at E_diss = 22 MeV
- Muon attachment probability to light fragment for mass bin A_L = 111.5 → 104.5: experimental (9.7 ± 2.6) × 10^{-2}; theoretical 6.62 × 10^{-2} at E_diss = 0 MeV and 3.51 × 10^{-2} at E_diss = 22 MeV
- Upper limit on muon ionization probability: P_ion < 10^{-4}
- Lattice dimensions: L_x = L_y = 67 fm, L_z = 146 fm with 25 × 25 × 53 lattice points and uniform spacing Δx = 2.8 fm
- Time step size: Δt = 9.3 × 10^{-24} s; between 1,200 and 1,900 time steps per run
- For mass asymmetry ξ = 1.10 with zero friction, muon attachment probability to light fragment is 20 percent

## Context
This work extends earlier theoretical studies of muon-induced fission by Davies, Sierk and Nix (1976), who extracted a viscosity coefficient from two-body collision friction and found a saddle-to-scission time delay of order 1 × 10^{-21} s, and by the author's own prior calculations (Oberacker and Umar, 1992, 1993) which used a smaller cubic lattice and a different derivative operator prescription. The extracted dissipated energy of about 10 MeV is compatible with results from other low-energy fission measurements based on the odd-even effect in charge yields (Wagemans, 1991). The approach enables future comparison with one-body dissipation models, which predict time delays an order of magnitude larger than the two-body collision result.
