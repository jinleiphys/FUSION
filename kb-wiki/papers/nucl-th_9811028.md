# Fermionic and Bosonic Bubbles and Foam

> The positioning of one or more bubbles inside a many fermion or boson system, does not affect the volume, surface or curvature terms in the liquid drop expansion of the total energy. If Coulomb effects are irrelevant, the only contribution to the ground state energy of a system with one or more voids arises from quantum effects, which is similar to the Casimir effect in vacuum or in critical phenomena. We discuss the characteristics of such systems, the interplay among various effects, such as shell corrections and chaotic behavior, and briefly mention the role of the temperature and pairing.

# Fermionic and Bosonic Bubbles and Foam

## Key claim

The positioning of one or more bubbles inside a many-fermion or many-boson system does not affect the volume, surface, or curvature terms in the liquid drop expansion of the total energy; when Coulomb effects are irrelevant, the only contribution to the ground state energy arises from quantum effects analogous to the Casimir effect, but the authors show that this quantum shell correction energy can make off-center bubble configurations energetically favorable for certain particle numbers, contrary to the tacit assumption that bubble position is determined solely by symmetry.

## Method

The authors developed two numerical methods to compute single-particle spectra for quantum billiards of arbitrary shapes: a conformal mapping method that transforms multiply connected 2D and 3D regions (e.g., a circular cavity with a circular hole) into concentric annuli, and a boundary overlap method (BOM) that analytically continues the boundary overlap matrix into the complex k-plane to count eigenvalues. They then applied the Strutinsky shell correction method to compute the ground state energy of noninteracting fermions in these geometries as a function of bubble displacement, bubble size, and particle number, for both 2D circular systems and 3D spherical systems.

## Key numbers

- For a 2D system with bubble radius a = R/2 (half the system radius), the unfolded single-particle spectrum was computed as a function of bubble displacement d from 0 to R, using the Weyl formula for the average cumulative number of states: N₀(k) = (R² - a²)k²/4 - (R + a)k/2.
- For two identical bubbles in an infinite medium, the single-particle eigenvalues follow a power law k²ₙ(d) ∝ εₙ(d)/d^α with α ≈ 2/5, where d is the minimum distance between bubble surfaces, determined from calculations with basis sets up to 4000 states.
- The asymptotic bubble-bubble interaction at large separations is predicted to be E₁₂(d) ∝ cos(2k_F d)/d³, where k_F is the Fermi momentum.
- For two bubbles at very small separations (much smaller than bubble radii), the interaction energy is a repulsive power law U(d) ∝ d^(-α) with α = 2 or α = 1 depending on whether particle number or density is held constant.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing nuclear physics problem of bubble nuclei, first considered by Wheeler and Pomorski (1950s onward), and more recent ideas about voids in highly charged alkali metal clusters (Dietrich, 1990s). The authors connect their quantum shell correction analysis to the Casimir effect in quantum field theory (Casimir, 1948) and critical Casimir effects in binary mixtures (Fisher, 1970s). The work enables future study of bubble dynamics in neutron star crusts (where similar "exotic" structures like plates, rods, and bubbles have been predicted by Ravenhall, Pethick, and Wilson, 1983), and suggests experiments with fullerenes in liquid metals or metallic balls in microwave resonators.
