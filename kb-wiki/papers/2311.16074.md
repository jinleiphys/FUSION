# A solution for infinite variance problem of fermionic observables

> Fermionic Monte Carlo calculations with continuous auxiliary fields often encounter infinite variance problem from fermionic observables. This issue renders the estimation of observables unreliable, even with an infinite number of samples. In this work, we show that the infinite variance problem stems from the fermionic determinant. Also, we propose an approach to address this problem by employing a reweighting method that utilizes the distribution from an extra time-slice. Two strategies to compute the reweighting factor are explored: one involves truncating and analytically calculating the reweighting factor, while the other employs a secondary Monte Carlo estimation. With Hubbard model as a testbed, we demonstrate that utilizing the sub-Monte Carlo estimation, coupled with an unbiased estimator, offers a solution that effectively mitigates the infinite variance problem at a minimal additional cost.

## Key claim
The infinite variance problem in fermionic Monte Carlo calculations with continuous auxiliary fields originates from the fermion determinant having zero points (exceptional configurations), and the proposed extra time-slice reweighting method with a sub-Monte Carlo unbiased estimator can remove the diverging variance without additional errors, but only when the reweighting factor is estimated without bias.

## Method
The authors used the Hubbard model at half-filling (μ=0) on a 4×4 lattice as a testbed, employing a compact continuous Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to convert the fermionic path integral into a bosonic one. They introduced an extra time-slice to define a new probability distribution for Monte Carlo sampling, then computed the reweighting factor R(φ) = det M_N(φ) / F(φ) using two strategies: an analytic truncation method based on the BSS formula expanded in ε, and a sub-Monte Carlo estimation of F(φ) using an unbiased estimator for 1/F(φ) from Moka (2019). The unbiased estimator uses a geometric distribution q_n and a parameter w chosen to minimize variance.

## Key numbers
- Double occupancy on a 4×4 lattice with U=8 and β=2: the standard Monte Carlo shows large negative peaks in the distribution, while the extra time-slice sub-MC method mitigates this abnormal behavior.
- The analytic method requires small ε (trotterization step) to avoid increasing error; the sub-MC method does not have truncation error from the ε expansion.
- The biased estimator for 1/F(φ) has a 1/n_sub behavior, where n_sub is the number of sub-Monte Carlo samples; the unbiased estimator converges to the unbiased value with lower cost, using an average n_sub ≈ 2k (where k is the cost for estimating w and p).

## Context
This work extends the extra time-slice reweighting approach suggested by Shi (2015) for curing infinite variance in fermionic observables, which previously relied on analytic integration using the BSS formula. The authors improve upon that method by introducing a sub-Monte Carlo estimation with an unbiased estimator from Moka (2019), avoiding the perturbative truncation errors and the need for very small ε. This enables reliable fermionic observable estimation in continuous auxiliary field calculations, which is relevant for hybrid Monte Carlo methods and contour deformation techniques used to address the sign problem in nuclear and condensed matter physics.
