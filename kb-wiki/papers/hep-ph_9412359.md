# Boson Interferometry after SPS and before RHIC

> We re--examine the connection between interferometry and the Wigner representation for source freeze--out, and continuous emission. At the operator level, two equivalent representations of the two--particle spectrum are found, which contradict the standard expression of kinetic theory. The discrepancy is resolved using two toy models. Further, we revisit interferometry in scale--invariant one--dimensional hydrodynamics, and argue that recent experimental results are evidence for a short kaon emission time. Using two exactly calculable models of two-- and three--dimensional flow, it is shown that the saddle point approximation, which is reasonable for one--dimensional flow, is no longer adequate. In these models the scaling law is altered, and we argue that such qualitative trends, together with other observables, are vital if one is to draw conclusions about the unknown source parameters.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard kinetic-theory expression for the two-particle interferometry spectrum is not uniquely derivable from the operator-level Wigner representation; two equivalent but contradictory forms exist, and the discrepancy is resolved only by specifying the physical initial state (density matrix) rather than relying on semiclassical phase-space distributions.

## Method

The authors derive operator-level expressions for one- and two-boson spectra using the Wigner representation for both freeze-out (initial data) and continuous emission (photon) cases, obtaining two equivalent forms for the two-particle spectrum that contradict the standard kinetic-theory formula. They resolve the paradox using two toy models: emission from a single one-dimensional cavity and from two separated one-dimensional cavities, where the averaging over the density matrix of the initial state reproduces the correct interference terms. They then apply the formalism to scale-invariant one-dimensional hydrodynamics and to exactly calculable two- and three-dimensional flow models (transverse explosion of a long filament and spherical explosion of a point-like source) to examine the saddle-point approximation and scaling laws.

## Key numbers

- For the one-dimensional cavity model, the single-particle spectrum shows an infinite sequence of alternating zeros and peaks with spacing determined by cavity size \(2a\), where allowed momenta are \(p_n = n\pi/a\) for \(n = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots\).
- In the two-cavity model, the interference term oscillates with period \(\Delta k \sim 1/(2L)\), where \(2L\) is the separation between cavities, and the condition \(|k_1 - k_2| L \gg 1\) corresponds to the Rayleigh criterion for resolving emission points.
- For the two-cavity model, the condition \(|k_1 - k_2| a \ll 1\) (with \(L \gg a\)) ensures that level spacing is large enough that analyzers do not detect particles from two different levels simultaneously.

## Context

This work extends the covariant theory of interferometry developed by Makhlin and Sinyukov (1988) and later by Andreev, Makhlin, and Sinyukov (1993), which showed \(m_\perp\) scaling of longitudinal correlation widths in one-dimensional hydrodynamics. It contradicts the standard kinetic-theory expression for two-particle spectra (as derived in Zajc, 1987) by showing that the Wigner representation yields two equivalent operator forms rather than a unique semiclassical formula. The paper enables a more careful treatment of interferometry for expanding sources with multi-dimensional flow, arguing that the saddle-point approximation (adequate for one-dimensional flow) fails for two- and three-dimensional flow, altering the scaling law and requiring qualitative trends alongside other observables to constrain source parameters.
