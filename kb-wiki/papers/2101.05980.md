# The effect of the quantization of the centrifugal stretching on the analysis of rotational spectra of even-even deformed nuclei in rare earth and actinide regions

> Deviation from the I(I+1) rule in the even-even isotopes 62Sm, 64Gd, 66Dy, 68Er, 70Y b, 72Hf, 74W and 76Os nuclei have been studied with a new approach based on the idea that the rotational nucleus being stretched out along the symmetry axis, where such stretching is treated according to quantum mechanics. This approach led to a new formula that describes the dependence of the moment of inertia on the angular momentum, and such formula works well for all even-even nuclei in the actinide and rare-earth region in the range 2.9<R<3.3. The calculations were carried out with the stretched model where the stretching of the nucleus with the rotation is quantized. The obtained results with a new derived formula which gives a reasonable agreement with the experimental date for all I up to 16.

# Wiki Page: Quantized Centrifugal Stretching Model for Rotational Spectra

## Key claim
The deviation from the I(I+1) rule in rotational spectra of even-even deformed nuclei can be explained by treating centrifugal stretching as a quantized phenomenon, leading to a new formula for the moment of inertia that works for both well-deformed and transitional nuclei in the range 2.9 < R = E4/E2 < 3.33, up to spin I = 16. The authors claim that the effect of microscopic features like Coriolis forces and pairing is so small it can be neglected in many cases, contrary to the assumptions of earlier models.

## Method
The authors derived a quantized beta-stretching formula by starting from the hydrodynamical expression for the moment of inertia and expanding the nuclear surface deformation in spherical harmonics, treating the deformation parameters as phonon creation and annihilation operators. They replaced the phonon energy with the rotational energy ħ²I(I+1)/(2ℑ_I) to obtain a recursion relation for the moment of inertia, then approximated to second order to yield a closed-form expression E(I) = A I(I+1) / [1 + B I(I+1) / (1 + B I(I+1))], where A = ħ²/(2ℑ₀) and B = 5ħ²/(8πℑ₀C). The two parameters A and B were determined by least-squares fitting to the first three experimental energy levels (I = 2, 4, 6) for each nucleus.

## Key numbers
- The model was applied to 40 even-even nuclei with atomic numbers from 152 to 190 and neutron numbers from 90 to 114, covering the ratio range 2.9 < R = E4/E2 < 3.33.
- Energy levels were calculated up to spin I = 16 for all nuclei.
- For most nuclei, the agreement with experimental data shows errors not more than 0.5%; very few cases display an error of 5%.
- For ¹⁵²Sm: experimental E(2) = 121.8 keV, current work gives 114.0 keV; experimental E(16) = 3365.0 keV, current work gives 3356.9 keV.
- For ¹⁵⁴Sm: experimental E(2) = 82.0 keV, current work gives 82.8 keV; experimental E(16) = 2968.2 keV, current work gives 3026.0 keV.
- For ¹⁶⁰Gd: experimental E(2) = 75.3 keV, current work gives 75.8 keV; experimental E(16) = 3007.1 keV, current work gives 3058.9 keV.
- For ¹⁶⁸Er: experimental E(2) = 79.8 keV, current work gives 80.6 keV; experimental E(16) = 3259.5 keV, current work gives 3315.8 keV.
- For ¹⁸⁸Os: experimental E(2) = 155.0 keV, current work gives 154.3 keV; experimental E(16) = 4236.5 keV, current work gives 4500.3 keV.

## Context
This work extends the centrifugal stretching idea of Diamond, Stephens, and Swiatecki (1964) by quantizing the stretching degree of freedom, whereas the original classical treatment gave good fits only for strongly deformed nuclei. The model improves upon the Variable Moment of Inertia (VMI) model of Mariscotti, Scharff-Goldhaber, and Buck (1969), which treated the moment of inertia as a general variable without clarifying its dependence on deformation, and upon the two-parameter formula of Sood (1967), which introduced an unphysical parameter N. The authors show that their formula outperforms the Gupta (1967, 1969) and Bohr-Mottelson two-parameter models, which only work in the narrow range 3.25 < R < 3.33, and matches the experimental data across the full range 2.9 < R < 3.33. The work enables systematic calculation of ground-state band energies for transitional nuclei without invoking Coriolis or pairing corrections.
