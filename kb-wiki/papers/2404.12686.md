# The MONUMENT Experiment: Ordinary Muon Capture studies for 0$νββ$ decay

> The MONUMENT experiment measures ordinary muon capture (OMC) on isotopes relevant for neutrinoless double-beta (0$νββ$) decay and nuclear astrophysics. OMC is a particularly attractive tool for improving the theoretical description of 0$νββ$ decay. It involves similar momentum transfers and allows testing the virtual transitions involved in 0$νββ$ decay against experimental data. During the 2021 campaign, MONUMENT measured OMC on $^{76}$Se and $^{136}$Ba, the isotopes relevant for next-generation 0$νββ$ decay searches, like LEGEND and nEXO. The experimental setup has been designed to accurately extract the total and partial muon capture rates, which requires precise reconstruction of energies and time-dependent intensities of the OMC-related $γ$ rays. The setup also includes a veto counter system to allow selecting a clean sample of OMC events. This work provides a detailed description of the MONUMENT setup operated during the 2021 campaign, its two DAQ systems, calibration and analysis approaches, and summarises the achieved detector performance. Future improvements are also discussed.

# The MONUMENT Experiment: Ordinary Muon Capture studies for 0νββ decay

## Key claim
The MONUMENT experiment measures ordinary muon capture (OMC) on isotopes relevant for neutrinoless double-beta decay to benchmark nuclear matrix element calculations, with the 2021 campaign successfully acquiring data on ⁷⁶Se and ¹³⁶Ba targets. The authors state that the results of the total and partial OMC capture rates will be reported elsewhere, meaning this paper only describes the experimental setup, calibration, and analysis approaches without presenting the actual physics results.

## Method
The experiment was conducted at the πE1 beam line of the Paul Scherrer Institute using negatively charged muons with momentum of approximately 40 MeV/c. The setup consisted of four scintillator counters (C₀, C₁, C₂, C₃) to tag muons stopped in the target, with the trigger condition μ_stop = ¬C₀ ∧ C₁ ∧ C₂ ∧ ¬C₃, and an array of eight HPGe detectors surrounding the target chamber to measure the time and energy distribution of γ rays and muonic X rays. Two independent data acquisition systems (MIDAS and ALPACA) operated in parallel, with MIDAS using online digital signal processing and ALPACA storing full waveforms for offline reconstruction via an optimized trapezoidal filter with MWD size of 8 μs and MWA size of 5 μs.

## Key numbers
- Muon beam momentum: approximately 40 MeV/c with a beam dispersion of roughly 2%
- Beam intensity: estimated at around 10⁴ muons per second
- Target masses: 2.0 g for all targets (⁷⁶Se, natSe, ¹³⁶Ba, natBa)
- ⁷⁶Se enrichment: 99.97%; ¹³⁶Ba enrichment: 95.27%
- ⁷⁶Se target thickness: 1.8 mm; ¹³⁶Ba target thickness: 2.4 mm
- HPGe detectors placed at a distance of approximately 15 cm from the target
- MIDAS dead time: 1.4 μs following each trigger
- ALPACA recorded HPGe traces of 20 μs at 15 MHz (low frequency) and 2 μs at 125 MHz (high frequency)
- PMT traces recorded: 2 μs at 125 MHz and approximately 7 μs at 30 MHz
- Offline measurements lasted 21 days after approximately one week of beam exposure

## Context
This work extends the approach demonstrated by earlier work (Dubna OMC, 2019) and builds upon the suggestion by Kortelainen (2002, 2003) that ordinary muon capture is advantageous for benchmarking neutrinoless double-beta decay nuclear matrix elements due to similar momentum transfers of 50-100 MeV. The MONUMENT experiment provides input for systematic studies of the sensitivity of OMC strength functions to effective weak axial couplings, as proposed by Suhonen (2020), and targets isotopes relevant for next-generation experiments such as LEGEND and nEXO. The detailed description of the setup, calibration procedures, and DAQ systems enables future measurement campaigns and the eventual extraction of total and partial muon capture rates for ⁷⁶Se and ¹³⁶Ba.
