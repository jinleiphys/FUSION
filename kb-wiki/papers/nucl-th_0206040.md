# Many-body Systems Interacting via a Two-body Random Ensemble (I): Angular Momentum distribution in the ground states

> In this paper, we discuss the angular momentum distribution in the ground states of many-body systems interacting via a two-body random ensemble. Beginning with a few simple examples, a simple approach to predict P(I)'s, angular momenta I ground state (g.s.) probabilities, of a few solvable cases, such as fermions in a small single-j shell and d boson systems, is given. This method is generalized to predict P(I)'s of more complicated cases, such as even or odd number of fermions in a large single-j shell or a many-j shell, d-boson, sd-boson or sdg-boson systems, etc. By this method we are able to tell which interactions are essential to produce a sizable P(I) in a many-body system. The g.s. probability of maximum angular momentum $I_{max}$ is discussed. An argument on the microscopic foundation of our approach, and certain matrix elements which are useful to understand the observed regularities, are also given or addressed in detail. The low seniority chain of 0 g.s. by using the same set of two-body interactions is confirmed but it is noted that contribution to the total 0 g.s. probability beyond this chain may be more important for even fermions in a single-j shell. Preliminary results by taking a displaced two-body random ensemble are presented for the I g.s. probabilities.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the angular momentum distribution of ground states in many-body systems with two-body random interactions can be predicted by a simple counting method: for each independent two-body matrix element set to -1 with all others zero, one finds which angular momentum gives the lowest eigenvalue, and the ground-state probability for each angular momentum is approximately the fraction of such runs that yield that angular momentum. The authors hedge that this approach works well but note that "quasi-degeneracy" problems arise in some cases, particularly for odd-fermion systems in many-j shells, where the lowest eigenvalue is close to the second lowest.

## Method

The authors set each independent two-body matrix element to -1 in turn while setting all others to zero, then diagonalize the Hamiltonian to find which angular momentum I yields the lowest eigenvalue among all states in the full shell-model space. They count the number of times each angular momentum I gives the lowest eigenvalue (denoted N_I) and estimate the ground-state probability as P(I) = N_I / N_m × 100%, where N_m equals the number of independent two-body matrix elements (or 2N-1 for cases where both largest and smallest coefficients matter). This method is applied to fermions in single-j and many-j shells, and to d-boson, sd-boson, and sdg-boson systems, with results compared to full diagonalization of a two-body random ensemble (TBRE) Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- For 4 fermions in a j=7/2 shell, the 0 ground-state probability from TBRE diagonalization is approximately 60.7% and the I_max=8 ground-state probability is approximately 39.3%, with all other I ground-state probabilities zero.
- For d-boson systems with boson number n_d=4 to 48, the predicted P(I_max) is approximately 40% for all n_d, while P(0) and P(2) are periodic with period delta(n_d)=6 and take values near 0%, 20%, 40%, or 60%.
- For fermions in a single-j shell, the I_max ground-state probability is predicted as 1/(j+1/2) × 100%, which decreases gradually with j and vanishes at large j.
- For 4 fermions in a single-j shell, the 0 ground-state probability can be fitted by P(0) = [(2j+1)/6] + k divided by (j+1/2) × 100%, where k = 1 if 2j = 3m, k = 0 if 2j+1 = 3m, and k = -1 if 2j-1 = 3m.
- For 6 fermions in a single-j shell, the 0 ground-state probability can be fitted by P(0) = [(2j)/3] divided by (j-1/2) × 100%.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Johnson, Bertsch, and Dean (2000) who discovered 0+ ground-state dominance in even fermion systems with two-body random ensembles, and by Bijker, Casten, and collaborators who confirmed this in sd-boson systems. The paper also builds on the authors' own prior work (Zhao, Arima, 2001) which used coefficients alpha^J to explain ground-state probabilities in small single-j shells. The present approach provides a universal method applicable to fermions in large single-j shells, many-j shells, and various boson systems, enabling prediction of all angular-momentum ground-state probabilities without full TBRE diagonalization. The authors also disprove the popular idea that 0 ground-state dominance comes intrinsically from the two-body nature of interactions, showing instead that specific interactions (certain G_J values) are essential.
