# On the Transport Properties of a Quark-Hadron Coulomb Lattice in the Cores of Neutron Stars

> Already more that 40 years ago, it has been suggested that because of the enormous mass densities in the cores of neutron stars, the hadrons in the centers of neutron stars may undergo a phase transition to deconfined quark matter. In this picture, neutron stars could contain cores made of pure (up, down, strange) quark matter which are surrounded by a mixed phase of quarks and hadrons. More than that, because of the competition between the Coulomb and the surface energies associated with the positively charged regions of nuclear matter and negatively charged regions of quark matter, the mixed phase may develop geometrical structures, similarly to what is expected of the sub-nuclear liquid-gas phase transition. In this paper we restrict ourselves to considering the formation of rare phase blobs in the mixed quark-hadron phase. The influence of rare phase blobs on the thermal and transport properties of neutron star matter is investigated. The total specific heat, $c_V$, thermal conductivity, $κ$, and electron-blob Bremsstrahlung neutrino emissivities, $ε_{ν,\text{BR}}$, of quark-hybrid matter are computed and the results are compared with the associated thermal and transport properties of standard neutron star matter. Our results show that the contribution of rare phase blobs to the specific heat is negligibly small. This is different for the neutrino emissivity from electron-blob Bremsstrahlung scattering, which turns out to be of the same order of magnitude as the total contributions from other Bremsstrahlung processes for temperatures below about $10^8$ K.

## Key claim

The contribution of rare phase blobs to the specific heat of quark-hybrid matter is negligibly small, but the neutrino emissivity from electron-blob Bremsstrahlung scattering is of the same order of magnitude as the total contributions from other Bremsstrahlung processes for temperatures below about 10^8 K, contrary to what one might expect from the introduction of new geometric degrees of freedom.

## Method

The authors model hadronic matter using a relativistic mean-field approximation with two parametrizations (G300 and HV) and quark matter using the MIT bag model with first-order perturbative corrections in the strong coupling constant. They compute the equilibrium radius of spherical rare-phase blobs by minimizing the sum of Coulomb and surface energy densities for a Wigner-Seitz cell, then calculate the specific heat, thermal conductivity, and electron-blob Bremsstrahlung neutrino emissivity using phonon sums following the method of Mochkovitch (1979) rather than the analytic fits used for neutron star crusts.

## Key numbers

- Maximum masses of quark-hybrid star models: 1.47 solar masses (HV1), 1.61 solar masses (HV2), 1.58 solar masses (G300I), and 1.69 solar masses (G300II)
- Radii of spherical blobs of rare phase range from 10 to 30 fm
- Charge numbers Z of blobs can exceed 10^3, and mass numbers A are one to two orders of magnitude greater than the heaviest stable nuclei
- Effective charge number Z_eff can fall near and below 10 as the quark volume fraction approaches 1
- The melting temperature of the Coulomb crystal is given by T_m = Z^2 e^2 / (a k_B * 172), where 172 is the melting point parameter Gamma_m
- The plasma temperature T_p = hbar omega_p / k_B, with omega_p = (4 pi Z^2 e^2 n_b / m_b)^(1/2)
- For temperatures below about 10^8 K, the electron-blob Bremsstrahlung neutrino emissivity is of the same order as other Bremsstrahlung processes

## Context

This work extends the study of geometrical structures in the quark-hadron mixed phase first proposed by Glendenning (1992) and applies methods developed for neutron star crusts by Kaminker (1999) and Potekhin (1999) to the core environment. The key difference from crustal calculations is that electric charge neutrality in the mixed phase is established between the rare phase, the dominant phase, and leptons present in both, rather than the simple relation n_e = Z n_i used for crusts. The results enable thermal evolution simulations of neutron stars with hypothetical quark-hadron cores and may help identify astrophysical signatures of such matter, though the maximum masses obtained (below 1.7 solar masses) cannot accommodate the 1.97 solar mass pulsar PSR J1614-2230 discovered by Demorest (2010).
