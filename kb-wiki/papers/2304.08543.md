# Alpha matter revisited

> We examine in detail two alternative descriptions of a system of $α$ particles interacting via local interactions of different character, highlighting the fact that a faithful microscopic description of such systems demands a consistent treatment of both short- and long-range correlations. In preparation, we examine four different versions of modern microscopic many-body theory and conclude by emphasizing that these approaches, although {\it a priori} very different, actually lead to the same equations for their efficient application. The only quantity that depends on the formulation of many-body theory chosen is an {\it irreducible} interaction correction. In the language of Green's functions and Feynman diagrams, it is the set of both particle-particle and particle-hole irreducible diagrams, and in variational Jastrow-Feenberg theory it is determined by {\it multipartite correlations} and {\it elementary diagrams}. We apply these theoretical methods to the calculation of the energetics, structure, thermodynamics, and dynamics of $α$ matter, as well as its condensate fraction. In dimensionless units, $α$ matter appears to be remarkably similar to the much-studied $^4$He quantum fluid, its low-temperature properties now basically solved in the Jastrow-Feenberg framework. Accordingly, one can have confidence in the results of application of the same procedure to $α$ matter. Even so, closer examination reveals significant differences between the physics of the two systems. Within an infinite nuclear medium, alpha matter is subject to a spinoidal instability. Extended mixtures of nucleons and alpha particles are yet to be given rigorous consideration in a corresponding theoretical framework.

## Key claim

Alpha matter (a hypothetical infinite system of alpha particles) is remarkably similar to liquid $^4$He when compared in dimensionless units, and its low-temperature properties are now basically solved within the Jastrow-Feenberg framework; however, closer examination reveals significant differences, including that alpha matter is subject to a spinodal instability within an infinite nuclear medium, and extended mixtures of nucleons and alpha particles remain to be treated rigorously.

## Method

The authors apply four versions of microscopic many-body theory, Jastrow-Feenberg variational theory, parquet-diagram summation, coupled-cluster method, and pair density functional theory, to a system of bosonic alpha particles interacting via local two-body potentials fitted to alpha-alpha scattering data, primarily the Ali-Bodmer potential (using only its $L=0$ component acting in all states). They solve the resulting self-consistent equations for the pair distribution function, static structure function, and related quantities, using coupling-constant integration to obtain the ground-state energy and extending the formalism to finite temperatures and dynamics via the Feynman dispersion relation and a time-dependent variational approach.

## Key numbers

- The Ali-Bodmer potential's repulsive strength drops from 475 MeV for $L=0$ to 320 MeV for $L=2$ to 10 MeV for $L=4$ (fitted to scattering data up to 24 MeV).
- The deBoer parameter $\Lambda$ values: 2.65 for Ali-Bodmer, 2.51 for Aziz ($^4$He), 3.35 for Gogny-D1 derived, and 2.97 for Gogny-D1N derived alpha-alpha interactions.
- Range parameter $\sigma$: 2.214 (Ali-Bodmer), 2.65 (Aziz), 2.819 (D1), 2.544 (D1N) in unspecified units.
- Depth parameter $\epsilon$: 11.975 (Ali-Bodmer), 10.8 (Aziz), 4.620 (D1), 7.220 (D1N) in unspecified units.

## Context

This work revisits and extends the original concept of alpha matter introduced by Tamagaki (1962), Nagasaki (1963), Harada (1963), and Clark and Wang (1966), applying modern many-body methods that have been developed for liquid $^4$He and shown to be equivalent across different formalisms (Jastrow-Feenberg, parquet, coupled-cluster). The paper demonstrates that the same generic many-body theory successfully applied to $^4$He can be applied to alpha matter, but identifies a spinodal instability that limits the density regime of stability; this enables future rigorous treatment of extended mixtures of nucleons and alpha particles, analogous to BCS-like pairing theory for neutron matter.
