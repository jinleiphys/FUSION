# Time-odd mean fields in the rotating frame: microscopic nature of nuclear magnetism

> The microscopic role of nuclear magnetism in rotating frame is investigated for the first time in the framework of the cranked relativistic mean field theory. It is shown that nuclear magnetism modifies the expectation values of single-particle spin, orbital and total angular momenta along the rotational axis effectively creating additional angular momentum. This effect leads to the increase of kinematic and dynamic moments of inertia at given rotational frequency and has an impact on effective alignments.

## Key claim

Nuclear magnetism, arising from the space-like components of vector mesons in the cranked relativistic mean field theory, modifies the expectation values of single-particle spin, orbital, and total angular momenta along the rotational axis, effectively creating additional angular momentum beyond that generated by the Coriolis force alone; this effect is state-dependent and rotational-frequency-dependent, and its net contribution to the total angular momentum depends on the shell filling and the specific single-particle configuration.

## Method

The authors performed cranked relativistic mean field (CRMF) calculations for the doubly magic superdeformed configuration in \(^{152}\)Dy (\(\pi 6^4 \nu 7^2\)), comparing results with and without the inclusion of nuclear magnetism (the space-like components of the vector \(\omega\) and \(\rho\) meson fields). They analyzed the expectation values of the single-particle angular momentum \(\langle \hat{j}_x \rangle_i\) and its decomposition into orbital \(\langle \hat{l}_x \rangle_i\) and spin \(\langle \hat{s}_x \rangle_i\) parts for the orbitals of the \(N=1\) neutron shell, and extended the analysis to higher shells and to proton orbitals. The calculations were performed in the one-dimensional cranking approximation, neglecting pairing correlations.

## Key numbers

* The inclusion of nuclear magnetism increases the kinematic (\(J^{(1)}\)) and dynamic (\(J^{(2)}\)) moments of inertia by \(\sim 20-30\%\) compared to calculations without it.
* For the \([110]1/2^+\) and \([110]1/2^-\) orbitals in the \(N=1\) shell, the expectation values of the spin angular momentum \(\langle \hat{s}_x \rangle_i\) are \(\sim 0.49\hbar\) and \(\sim -0.49\hbar\) respectively, at all rotational frequencies employed, and nuclear magnetism induces no change in these spin values (\(\Delta \langle \hat{s}_x \rangle_i \approx 0 \hbar\)).
* The contribution of the 'closed' \(N=1\) shell to the total angular momentum \(J\) accounts for only a few percent of \(J\).

## Context

This work extends the understanding of time-odd mean fields in rotating nuclei, which had been studied in non-relativistic theories using Skyrme forces (Dobaczewski and Dudek, 1995) and in earlier CRMF calculations (Koenig and Ring, 1993; Afanasjev, Konig, and Ring, 1996) that showed the importance of currents for renormalizing moments of inertia. The paper provides the first microscopic explanation of how nuclear magnetism creates additional angular momentum, and it enables future studies of its impact on signature splitting, single-particle energies, and effective alignments in rotating nuclei, as well as investigations of nuclear magnetism in non-rotating systems.
