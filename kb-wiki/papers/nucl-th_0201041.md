# On the Theory of Nuclear Anapole Moments

## Key claim

Nuclear anapole moments, induced by parity-nonconserving nuclear forces, dominate the nuclear-spin-dependent parity-violating effects in heavy atoms despite being electromagnetic in nature, because the anapole interaction is enhanced by a factor of approximately A^(2/3) (close to 30 in heavy nuclei) which compensates for the smallness of the fine-structure constant. The experimental result for cesium-137 indicates that the "best values" of parity-odd nuclear forces from DDH (1980) somewhat underestimate the magnitude of these forces, though the thallium measurement does not comply with this conclusion.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework for nuclear anapole moments, starting from the analytical constant-density approximation of Flambaum, Khriplovich, and Sushkov (1984) which yields a simple A^(2/3) scaling formula. They then compare various independent-particle model calculations using Woods-Saxon and oscillator potentials with many-body calculations including random-phase approximation (RPA) core polarization and large-basis shell-model calculations, summarizing the predicted anapole constants for cesium-133, thallium-203/205, and bismuth-209.

## Key numbers

- Experimental total effective constant for nuclear-spin-dependent parity nonconservation in Cs-137: kappa_tot = 0.44(6) (Wood et al., 1997)
- Extracted anapole constant for Cs-137 after removing neutral current and hyperfine contributions: kappa = 0.37(6)
- Fermi constant: G = 1.027 x 10^(-5) m^(-2) (in units where hbar = 1, c = 1; m is proton mass)
- Fine-structure constant: alpha = 1/137
- Nuclear radius parameter: r_0 = 1.2 fm
- Best-value proton P-odd coupling constant: g_p = 4.5 (from DDH, 1980)
- Theoretical predictions for anapole constants at best values: kappa(Cs-133) = 0.15-0.21, kappa(Tl-203/205) = 0.24, kappa(Bi-209) = 0.15
- Experimental result for thallium anapole moment: kappa = -0.22 +/- 0.30 (Vetter et al.)

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Flambaum, Khriplovich, and Sushkov (1984) and Flambaum and Khriplovich (1980) on nuclear anapole moments, which were first proposed by Zeldovich (1957). The paper reviews and compares subsequent calculations by Haxton, Henley, and Musolf (1989), Bouchiat and Piketty (1991), Dmitriev, Khriplovich, and Telitsin (1994), Dmitriev and Telitsin (1997), Auerbach and Brown (1999), and others. The experimental discovery of the cesium anapole moment by Wood et al. (1997) provides a new probe of parity-nonconserving nuclear forces, enabling tests of the DDH (1980) "best values" for weak nucleon-nucleon interactions. The disagreement between the cesium and thallium measurements remains an open problem requiring further experimental clarification.
