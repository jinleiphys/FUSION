# Nuclear collectivity and the harmonic spectrum of two-body correlations

> High-energy nuclear collisions have opened a new experimental method to reveal collective behavior in nuclear ground states through the lens of many-body correlations of nucleons. Using ab initio lattice and variational calculations of $^{20}$Ne and $^{16}$O, we study how emergent phenomena such as deformation or clustering can be identified in these systems from the dependence of their two-body density distributions on the relative azimuthal angle of nucleon pairs. A harmonic analysis of the correlation functions reveals in particular a dominant quadrupole component in $^{20}$Ne, consistent with a bowling-pin picture, and a prominent triangular modulation in $^{16}$O, possibly indicative of alpha-cluster correlations. Given that such structures can be accurately identified in high-energy collider experiments, these findings open a new paradigm for analyzing emergent collective behavior in atomic nuclei, relating their intrinsic shapes to the harmonic spectrum of microscopic correlations.

# Nuclear collectivity and the harmonic spectrum of two-body correlations

## Key claim

The intrinsic shapes of nuclear ground states can be directly identified from the harmonic decomposition of two-body correlation functions, specifically through the dependence of two-body density distributions on the relative azimuthal angle of nucleon pairs, without requiring spectroscopic information from excited states.

## Method

Using ab initio Nuclear Lattice Effective Field Theory (NLEFT) and quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) calculations, the authors sampled ground-state nucleon coordinates for $^{16}$O and $^{20}$Ne and computed two-body density distributions projected onto the transverse plane. They performed a harmonic analysis of the azimuthal correlation functions $\rho^{(2)}_\perp(r_\perp,\Delta\phi) / \rho^{(1)}_\perp(r_\perp)^2 - 1$ as a function of the relative azimuthal angle $\Delta\phi = \phi_1 - \phi_2$ for radial slices at varying distances from the nuclear center, and extracted Fourier coefficients and deformation parameters $\mathfrak{B}_n$ from the two-body density.

## Key numbers

* $^{20}$Ne (NLEFT, SU(4) Hamiltonian): $\mathfrak{B}_2 = 0.450$, $\mathfrak{B}_3 = 0.318$, with $\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_2 \rangle = 4.39$ fm$^4$ and $\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_3 \rangle = 25.2$ fm$^6$ at $R_A = 3.17$ fm
* $^{16}$O (NLEFT, SU(4) Hamiltonian): $\mathfrak{B}_2 = 0.202$, $\mathfrak{B}_3 = 0.347$, with $\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_2 \rangle = 0.521$ fm$^4$ and $\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_3 \rangle = 13.5$ fm$^6$ at $R_A = 2.78$ fm
* $^{16}$O (QMC, AV6P+UIX potential): $\mathfrak{B}_2 = -0.111$, $\mathfrak{B}_3 = 0.465$, with $\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_2 \rangle = -0.125$ fm$^4$ and $\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_3 \rangle = 14.9$ fm$^6$ at $R_A = 2.62$ fm
* $^{16}$O (QMC, pion-less EFT): $\mathfrak{B}_2 = -0.169$, $\mathfrak{B}_3 = 0.305$, with $\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_2 \rangle = -0.230$ fm$^4$ and $\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_3 \rangle = 5.11$ fm$^6$ at $R_A = 2.48$ fm
* $^{16}$O (mean-field Woods-Saxon): $\mathfrak{B}_2 = -0.249$, $\mathfrak{B}_3 \approx 0$, with $\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_2 \rangle = -0.763$ fm$^4$ at $R_A = 2.76$ fm
* Fourier decomposition of $^{20}$Ne correlation at $r_\perp = R_A$: $a_0 = -0.0289$, $a_1 = -0.0130$, $a_2 = 0.147$, $a_3 = 0.0322$, $a_4 = 0.0389$
* For $^{20}$Ne, $(A-1)\langle \hat{\mathcal{E}}_3 \rangle / \langle \hat{R}_3 \rangle^2 = 0.94$, giving a 30% correction to the mean squared triangularity from two-body correlations

## Context

This work extends the classical rigid-rotor framework of Blaizot (2025) to ab initio calculations, demonstrating that the harmonic spectrum predicted for deformed intrinsic shapes appears in realistic nuclear ground states. It complements the traditional spectroscopic approach of Bohr and Mottelson by providing a "horizontal route" based solely on ground-state correlations, as opposed to the "vertical route" through excitation spectra reviewed by Verney (2025). The results enable direct connections between ab initio nuclear structure calculations and experimental data from high-energy nuclear collisions at the LHC, particularly from the recent runs of O+O and Ne+Ne collisions reported by ATLAS (2025), ALICE (2025), and CMS (2025), and could inform benchmarks for neutrinoless double beta decay matrix elements as discussed by Yao (2021) and Agostini (2022).
