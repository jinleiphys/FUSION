# Nonequilibrium Phenomenology of Identified Particle Spectra in Heavy-Ion Collisions at LHC Energies

> We employ the Zubarev approach of the non-equilibrium statistical operator to investigate the enhancement of the low-$p_T$ region of pion spectra, introducing an effective pion chemical potential to describe the overpopulation of low-energy pion states. We test a corresponding freeze-out approach by analyzing the transverse-momentum spectra of identified particles measured recently with high precision by the ALICE Collaboration in Pb+Pb collisions at CERN LHC. A blast-wave model and a blast-wave-based particle generator, coupled to a hadronic transport model, are utilized. Bayesian inference methods are applied to extract the most probable sets of thermodynamic parameters at the chemical freeze-out hypersurface. Both models for the overpopulated pion states, the hadronic transport model and the thermal model with a nonzero pion chemical potential, provide a satisfactory description of the observed pion spectra. However, both approaches contain approximations which can be improved within a systematic nonequilibrium approach. We demonstrate that the introduction of a nonequilibrium pion chemical potential offers an efficient alternative to the conventional explanation of the low-$p_T$ enhancement, typically attributed to resonance decays with subsequent thermalization. A similar discussion holds also for the kaon spectra.

# Nonequilibrium Phenomenology of Identified Particle Spectra in Heavy-Ion Collisions at LHC Energies

## Key claim

The introduction of a nonequilibrium pion chemical potential within the Zubarev approach offers an efficient alternative to the conventional explanation of low-pT enhancement in pion spectra, which is typically attributed to resonance decays with subsequent thermalization; however, both the hadronic transport model and the thermal model with nonzero pion chemical potential provide satisfactory descriptions of the observed pion spectra, and both contain approximations that can be improved within a systematic nonequilibrium approach.

## Method

The authors employ the Zubarev method of the nonequilibrium statistical operator (NSO) to derive a generalized blast-wave model with effective pion and kaon chemical potentials as Lagrange multipliers for long-living fluctuations. They fit this model to high-precision ALICE data for pion, kaon, and proton transverse-momentum spectra from Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV, using chi-squared minimization for kinetic freeze-out models and Bayesian inference with Gaussian process emulators and Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling for chemical freeze-out models coupled to the SMASH hadronic transport afterburner.

## Key numbers

- Model B (kinetic freeze-out with nonequilibrium chemical potentials) yields freeze-out temperature T = (118.1 ± 3.7) MeV, surface velocity beta_S = 0.858 ± 0.005, pion chemical potential mu_pi = (126.2 ± 3.8) MeV, and kaon chemical potential mu_K = (402.9 ± 22.6) MeV for 0-5% central Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV
- Model A (standard blast-wave without chemical potentials) gives T = (97.7 ± 3.9) MeV and beta_S = 0.879 ± 0.005, with chi-squared* = 0.956 compared to 0.143 for model B, representing a factor of 6.7 improvement in fit quality over the pT range [0,2] GeV/c
- Model C (chemical freeze-out with SMASH afterburner, full chemical equilibrium) gives MAP estimates: T = 154.02 MeV, beta_S = 0.786, tau = 8.88 fm/c, R = 11.54 fm
- Model D (chemical freeze-out with SMASH afterburner, nonzero pion chemical potential) gives MAP estimates: T = 155.98 MeV, beta_S = 0.783, mu_pi = 9.52 MeV, tau = 8.08 fm/c, R = 11.5 fm
- Bayes factor B^D_C = 0.434, indicating model D does not significantly improve over model C

## Context

This work extends the long-standing puzzle of low-pT pion enhancement first observed at CERN-SPS (Strobele, 1988; Kataja, 1990) and revisited with high-precision ALICE data (ALICE, 2013; ALICE, 2019) that modern hydrodynamic codes systematically underestimate. The authors apply Zubarev's NSO formalism (Zubarev) to provide a systematic nonequilibrium framework, contrasting with earlier approaches using quark fugacity factors (Begun, 2014) or critical fluctuations at the chiral transition (Grossi, 2021). The results enable future work incorporating in-medium effects such as the Mott effect (Blaschke, 2024) and extending the NSO approach to include flow of particle species, potentially justifying the success of thermal statistical models and their ad hoc fugacity parameters (Petran, 2013).
