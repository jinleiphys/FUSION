# Nuclear magnetization distribution and hyperfine splitting in Bi$^{82+}$ ion

> Hyperfine splitting in Bi$^{82+}$ and Pb$^{81+}$ ions was calculated using continuum RPA approach with effective residual forces. To fix the parameters of the theory the nuclear magnetic dipole moments of two one- particle and two one-hole nuclei around $^{208}$Pb were calculated using the same approach. The contribution from velocity dependent two-body spin- orbit residual interaction was calculated explicitly. Additionally, the octupole moment of $^{209}$Bi and the hfs in muonic bismuth atom were calculated as well in the same approach. All the calculated observables, except the electronic hfs in $^{209}$Bi, are in good agreement with the data. We argue for more accurate measurement of the octupole moment and the muonic hfs for $^{209}$Bi.

## Key claim

The continuum RPA approach with effective residual forces, including an explicit velocity-dependent two-body spin-orbit interaction, reproduces the hyperfine splitting in Pb$^{81+}$ and the octupole moment and muonic hfs in $^{209}$Bi, but the calculated electronic hyperfine splitting in $^{209}$Bi$^{82+}$ remains 0.027 eV above the experimental value, a discrepancy of about four standard deviations in the derived Bohr-Weisskopf correction $\epsilon$.

## Method

The authors used the continuum random phase approximation (RPA) within Migdal's theory of finite Fermi systems (FFST) to calculate the nuclear magnetization distribution and hyperfine splitting. The parameters of the effective spin-spin and spin-orbit residual interactions were fixed by fitting the calculated magnetic dipole moments of four one-particle and one-hole nuclei around $^{208}$Pb ($^{207}$Tl, $^{207}$Pb, $^{209}$Bi, $^{209}$Pb) to experimental values, using effective charges $\zeta_s$ and $\zeta_l$ determined by a least-squares minimization. The single-particle basis included the continuum, and the correction to the electromagnetic current from the velocity-dependent spin-orbit interaction was computed explicitly.

## Key numbers

- Experimental ground-state hyperfine splitting in $^{209}$Bi$^{82+}$: $\Delta E_{hfs}^{exper.} = 5.0840(8)$ eV.
- Calculated hyperfine splitting in $^{209}$Bi$^{82+}$: $E_{hfs}^{theory} = 5.111^{+0.020}_{-0.003}(5)$ eV, where the first uncertainty is from the Bohr-Weisskopf effect and the second from QED corrections.
- Calculated hyperfine splitting in $^{207}$Pb$^{81+}$: $E_{hfs}^{theory} = 1.222^{+0.004}_{-0.021}(3)$ eV, compared to the experimental value $1.2159(2)$ eV.
- Bohr-Weisskopf correction $\epsilon$ for $^{209}$Bi$^{82+}$: $0.0095^{+0.0007}_{-0.0038}$ (this work), compared to the experimental value $0.0147(11)$.
- Calculated magnetization rms radius for $^{209}$Bi: $\langle r_m^2 \rangle^{1/2} = 5.86$ fm.
- Calculated octupole moment of $^{209}$Bi: $-55^{+16}_{-2}$ $\mu_N \cdot$fm$^2$, in agreement with the experimental value $-55(3)$ $\mu_N \cdot$fm$^2$.
- Calculated hyperfine splitting in muonic $^{209}$Bi: $4.63^{+0.064}_{-0.010}(6)$ keV, compared to the experimental value $4.44(15)$ keV.

## Context

This work extends the continuum RPA approach previously applied to muonic bismuth by Dmitriev (2000) to hydrogen-like ions, and it explicitly includes the velocity-dependent spin-orbit correction absent in the dynamical correlation model (DCM) calculations of Tomaselli et al. (1998, 1999). The results contradict the independent-particle calculations of Shabaev et al. (1997) and Gustavsson et al. (1999) for the Bohr-Weisskopf effect in $^{209}$Bi$^{82+}$, and they disagree with the DCM result on the opposite side of the experimental value. The work enables more precise tests of nuclear magnetization models by calling for improved measurements of the octupole moment and muonic hyperfine splitting in $^{209}$Bi.
