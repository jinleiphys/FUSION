# Nucleon-Nucleus Optical Potential computed with the Gogny interaction

> The ability of the Gogny forces of the D1 family to describe the nucleon-nucleus scattering is studied. To this end, we use an optical model potential built up using a semi-microscopic nuclear matter approach. The real and imaginary parts of the optical model are provided by the first and second-order terms, respectively, of the Taylor expansion of the mass operator calculated within the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock method using the reaction G-matrix built up with the effective Gogny force instead of a microscopic interaction. The optical potential in finite nuclei is obtained through the Local Density Approximation using the neutron and proton densities provided by a quasi-local Hartree-Fock calculation with the same Gogny force for the sake of consistency. A reasonable good agreement is found between the theoretical differential cross-sections and the analyzing powers of the elastic neutron and proton scattering along the periodic table from Ca to Pb calculated with the Gogny forces and the corresponding values predicted by the global phenomenological potential of Koning and Delaroche. To investigate the limits of the approximations used in this work, comparisons with the results of nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering in $^{40}$Ca and $^{48}$Ca obtained using the Nuclear Structure Model are also performed.

# Nucleon-Nucleus Optical Potential computed with the Gogny interaction

## Key claim

The Gogny D1S effective interaction, without any additional free parameters fitted to scattering data, can provide a reasonable global description of nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering from Ca to Pb when used in a semi-microscopic nuclear matter approach, but the agreement is limited by the Local Density Approximation's neglect of surface diffuseness corrections and by the omission of rearrangement terms in the real potential.

## Method

The authors compute a microscopic optical potential using the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock method in nuclear matter, where the first-order term of the mass operator expansion provides the real part and the second-order term provides the imaginary part, both evaluated with the Gogny D1S effective force. The optical potential is applied to finite nuclei through the Local Density Approximation using neutron and proton densities from a quasilocal Hartree-Fock calculation with the same Gogny force, and the real part is also computed in a Hartree-Fock prescription that includes rearrangement terms and uses the finite-nucleus mean field. The resulting differential cross sections and analyzing powers are compared with the global phenomenological potential of Koning and Delaroche and with Nuclear Structure Model results for ⁴⁰Ca and ⁴⁸Ca.

## Key numbers

- The D1S Gogny force parameters include: range parameters μ₁ = 0.7 fm and μ₂ = 1.2 fm; spin-orbit strength W_LS = 130 MeV·fm⁵; density-dependent strength t₃ = 1390 MeV·fm⁴.
- The real part of the MOP is tested for neutron scattering on ²⁰⁸Pb at projectile energies of 5, 15, and 30 MeV, showing that the parabolic approximation to the momentum dependence agrees with the exact calculation at the surface but differs slightly in the bulk region.
- The study covers targets from Ca to Pb across the periodic table, with comparisons to the POP of Koning and Delaroche and to NSM results for ⁴⁰Ca and ⁴⁸Ca.
- The imaginary part of the MOP is computed using the Gogny force appearing quadratically, with contributions from the density-dependent term, finite-range Gaussian terms, and spin-orbit term.

## Context

This work extends the nuclear matter approach to optical potentials, previously developed with Skyrme forces by Shen (2009), Xu (2014), and Pilipenko (2010, 2012), to the Gogny D1 family of effective interactions. It contrasts with the Nuclear Structure Model calculations of Blanchon (2015a, 2015b, 2017) for ⁴⁰Ca and ⁴⁸Ca, which explicitly include polarization terms from particle-vibration coupling and RPA excitations. The results enable future improvements by incorporating Gaussian folding corrections to the Local Density Approximation and by testing other Gogny parametrizations such as D1N and D1M.
