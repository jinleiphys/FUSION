# Small system studies: A theory overview

> There is now a substantial body of evidence that a deconfined quark-gluon plasma is created in ultrarelativistic collisions of heavy nuclei. Some key observables which are used to gauge the production of the quark-gluon plasma are the hadronic spectra and multiplicities across many species, azimuthal anisotropies in the production of various hadrons, jet quenching, photon/dilepton production, and heavy quarkonium suppression. A key question in the study of the quark-gluon plasma is what happens to these observables as one changes the system size. In particular, it is very interesting to study collisions of `small systems' such as pp, dA, pA, and $e^+ e^-$ since naively one does not expect to produce a QGP in these cases. One of the surprises from such studies was the existence of sizable azimuthal anisotropies in the hadron spectra in the highest multiplicity classes, which led to speculations that one can generate a QGP in such relatively rare events. This interpretation, however, is complicated by the fact that there can be multiple sources of azimuthal anisotropy. I will discuss our current understanding of the different sources of azimuthal anisotropies and the multiplicity windows in which each mechanism is expected to dominate. I will also provide a critical discussion of the reliability of hydrodynamic models applied to small systems. Finally, I will briefly discuss jet quenching and heavy quarkonium suppression in small systems and discuss whether or not these observables show any indication of the production of a small QGP droplet.

## Key claim
Azimuthal anisotropies observed in small-system collisions are consistent with strong final-state interactions and true collective flow only for charged-particle multiplicities \(N_{\rm trk} \gtrsim 100\); at lower multiplicities, multiple sources of anisotropy (initial-state correlations, parton escape, bremsstrahlung) dominate, and the existence of a quark-gluon plasma droplet in such events remains unconfirmed by hard probes.

## Method
The author reviews theoretical mechanisms for azimuthal anisotropy in pp, pA, dA, and \(e^+ e^-\) collisions, comparing hydrodynamic models (second-order viscous hydrodynamics, anisotropic hydrodynamics), kinetic-theory approaches (parton escape, BAMPS, AMPT), and initial-state correlation models (Glasma graphs, saturation corrections). The reliability of hydrodynamics is assessed via analytic structure of retarded correlators and estimates of gradient and inverse-Reynolds-number violations. Jet quenching and bottomonium suppression data from ALICE and CMS are examined for signs of QGP production.

## Key numbers
- Pseudo-critical QCD crossover temperature: \(T_c \sim 155\) MeV (lattice QCD).
- QGP lifetime in Pb-Pb central collisions at LHC: \(\tau \sim 10-15\) fm/c.
- Hydrodynamic models explain observed \(v_n\) down to \(N_{\rm trk} \sim 70-100\).
- Minimum hotspot size for reliable hydrodynamics: \(\sim 0.15\) fm (from kinetic theory and strong-coupling analyses).
- In BAMPS transport with Glasma initial conditions, at high multiplicity (\(\langle dN_g/dy \rangle = 26\)) final-state interactions dominate; at low multiplicity (\(\langle dN_g/dy \rangle = 6\)) initial-state correlations survive with small modification.
- For \(N_{\rm trk} \gtrsim 100\), azimuthal anisotropies are dominated by final-state interactions.
- Estimated maximal deviation of \(R_{pA}\) from unity in 0-1% centrality: 25% at \(p_T = 10\) GeV (Shen et al., 2016).
- ALICE \(R_{CP}\) for jets in pA 0-10% centrality at \(p_{T,\rm jet} = 57\) GeV: \(1.09 \pm 0.02{\rm (stat)} \pm 0.06{\rm (sys)}\).
- Bottomonium suppression in pA collisions: \(R_{AA} \sim 0.7-0.8\) (cold nuclear matter); in AA central collisions: \(R_{AA} \sim 0.35\) (strong final-state interactions).

## Context
This theory overview extends the debate on whether small-system azimuthal anisotropies require QGP formation, following experimental surprises from ALICE, CMS, and PHENIX (2010-2015). It contrasts hydrodynamic approaches (pioneered by Bozek and the Krakow group, 2011-2013) with initial-state correlation models (Dusling, Schenke, Kovchegov, 2012-2018) and parton escape mechanisms (Bzdak, 2014). The work enables quantitative cross-comparison of anisotropy sources across multiplicity windows and identifies open questions: the need for higher-order gradient resummations, the challenge of large viscous corrections in short-lived systems, and the statistical limitations of hard-probe measurements in high-multiplicity small-system events.
