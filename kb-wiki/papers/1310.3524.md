# What the Timing of Millisecond Pulsars Can Teach us about Their Interior

> The cores of compact stars reach the highest densities in nature and therefore could consist of novel phases of matter. We demonstrate via a detailed analysis of pulsar evolution that precise pulsar timing data can constrain the star's composition, through unstable global oscillations (r-modes) whose damping is determined by microscopic properties of the interior. If not efficiently damped, these modes emit gravitational waves that quickly spin down a millisecond pulsar. As a first application of this general method, we find that ungapped interacting quark matter is consistent with both the observed radio and x-ray data, whereas for ordinary nuclear matter some additional enhanced damping mechanism is required.

# What the Timing of Millisecond Pulsars Can Teach us about Their Interior

## Key claim

Precise pulsar timing data can constrain the interior composition of compact stars through r-mode oscillations, but the constraints are conditional: ungapped interacting quark matter is consistent with both radio and x-ray data, whereas ordinary nuclear matter requires some additional enhanced damping mechanism beyond standard viscous damping to explain the observations.

## Method

The authors analyze the evolution of millisecond pulsars using conservation equations that couple the star's angular velocity, core temperature, and r-mode amplitude, with energy loss rates from gravitational wave emission, viscous dissipation, and thermal cooling. They derive static instability boundaries in temperature-frequency space and novel dynamic instability boundaries in spin-down-frequency space, then compare these boundaries to observed radio timing data from the Manchester (2004) catalog and x-ray data from low-mass x-ray binaries (LMXBs) compiled by Haskell (2012).

## Key numbers

- For hadronic matter with standard viscous damping, the static instability boundary leaves most LMXB data points above the line, indicating r-modes would be unsuppressed; adding maximum Ekman layer damping still leaves some points below the boundary
- The saturated r-mode scenario for hadronic matter requires saturation amplitude α_sat ≲ 10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁶ to explain LMXB data, which is lower than any proposed saturation mechanism provides
- Old millisecond radio pulsars cluster at spin-down rates |ḟ| ∼ 10⁻¹⁵ s⁻², which lies within the dynamic instability region for the minimal hadronic matter model
- The only millisecond radio pulsar with a temperature estimate, J0437-4715, has a spin frequency low enough that it could have cooled out of the instability region for any α_sat ≲ 10⁻⁶
- If hadronic matter stars undergo r-mode spin-down, predicted surface temperatures at infinity would be (0.2–1) × 10⁶ K, significantly hotter than standard cooling estimates

## Context

This work extends the r-mode instability framework of Andersson (1997) and Friedman (1978) by introducing dynamic instability boundaries in timing parameter space, complementing the static temperature-frequency boundaries used in prior studies. The analysis builds on detailed viscosity calculations for nuclear matter by Shternin (2008) and Sawyer (1989), and for quark matter by Schwenzer (2012), including non-Fermi liquid effects. The results enable future discrimination between no-r-mode, saturated-r-mode, and boundary-straddling scenarios using combined radio, x-ray, and gravitational wave data, and motivate temperature measurements for millisecond radio pulsars above 300 Hz to test the hadronic matter model.
