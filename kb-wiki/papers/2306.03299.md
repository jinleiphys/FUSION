# The Gallium Anomaly

> In order to test the end-to-end operations of gallium solar neutrino experiments, intense electron-capture sources were fabricated to measure the responses of the radiochemical SAGE and GALLEX/GNO detectors to known fluxes of low-energy neutrinos. Such tests were viewed at the time as a cross-check, given the many tests of $^{71}$Ge recovery and counting that had been routinely performed, with excellent results. However, the four $^{51}$Cr and $^{37}$Ar source experiments yielded rates below expectations, a result commonly known as the Ga anomaly. As the intensity of the electron-capture sources can be measured to high precision, the neutrino lines they produce are fixed by known atomic and nuclear rates, and the neutrino absorption cross section on $^{71}$Ga is tightly constrained by the lifetime of $^{71}$Ge, no simple explanation for the anomaly has been found. To check these calibration experiments, a dedicated experiment BEST was performed, utilizing a neutrino source of unprecedented intensity and a detector optimized to increase statistics while providing some information on counting rate as a function of distance from the source. The results BEST obtained are consistent with the earlier solar neutrino calibration experiments, and when combined with those measurements, yield a Ga anomaly with a significance of approximately $4σ$, under conservative assumptions. But BEST found no evidence of distance dependence and thus no explicit indication of new physics. In this review we describe the extensive campaigns carried out by SAGE, GALLEX/GNO, and BEST to demonstrate the reliability and precision of their experimental procedures, including $^{71}$Ge recovery, counting, and analysis. We also describe efforts to define uncertainties in the neutrino capture cross section. With the results from BEST, an anomaly remains.

## Key claim

The gallium anomaly, a persistent discrepancy between measured and expected neutrino capture rates from radioactive sources on ⁷¹Ga, has been confirmed with approximately 4σ significance when BEST results are combined with earlier SAGE and GALLEX/GNO calibration experiments, but BEST found no evidence of distance-dependent variations that would indicate new physics such as sterile neutrino oscillations.

## Method

The authors review the end-to-end calibration campaigns of the radiochemical SAGE, GALLEX/GNO, and BEST experiments, which exposed gallium targets to intense electron-capture sources (⁵¹Cr and ³⁷Ar) producing monoenergetic neutrino lines. The produced ⁷¹Ge was extracted from the gallium targets via chemical procedures (as GeCl₄ from GaCl₃ solution in GALLEX, or from metallic gallium in SAGE and BEST), purified, converted into GeH₄, and counted in miniaturized gas proportional counters that recorded the x-rays and Auger electrons from ⁷¹Ge electron-capture decay. The BEST experiment additionally employed a nested two-zone target volume to provide sensitivity to oscillation baselines, using a ⁵¹Cr source of unprecedented intensity.

## Key numbers

- The four original SAGE and GALLEX/GNO source experiments (three with ⁵¹Cr, one with ³⁷Ar) yielded a combined ratio of observed to expected counts of R = 0.87 ± 0.05, a deviation of approximately 2.5σ from unity
- The BEST results combined with earlier measurements yield a gallium anomaly with significance of approximately 4σ under conservative assumptions
- The ³⁷Ar source used by SAGE had an activity of 409 ± 2 kCi, determined with 0.5% uncertainty from six distinct calibration procedures
- The ⁵¹Cr source half-life is 27.7 days; the ³⁷Ar source half-life is 35.0 days
- The ⁷¹Ge half-life is 11.43 days; exposures for ⁷¹Ga(νₑ,e⁻) were typically 5-10 days (5 d for GALLEX, 10 d for BEST)
- The SAGE special reactor held 13 t of gallium compared to the 7 t it would have held with the stirring mechanism in place
- The GALLEX target contained 101 tons of GaCl₃ solution with 30.3 t of natural gallium (12 t of ⁷¹Ga)
- The ⁷¹Ge electron-capture decay has 88% K-shell capture, 10.3% L-shell capture, and 1.7% M-shell capture

## Context

This review extends the history of the gallium anomaly that emerged from the SAGE and GALLEX/GNO calibration experiments in the 1990s and early 2000s, which was previously interpreted as possible evidence for sterile neutrino oscillations (Acero 2022, Adhikari 2016, Abazajian 2012). The BEST results deepen the anomaly but fail to provide the distance-dependent signal that would explicitly indicate new physics, and the sterile neutrino solution generates tension with null experiments such as LSND (Athanassopoulos 1996, Aguilar 2001) due to the large mixing angle required. The work enables future investigations using low-energy electron-capture sources to resolve the anomaly and its relationship to other low-energy neutrino anomalies.
