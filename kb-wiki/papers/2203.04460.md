# Model for independent particle motion

> Independent particle model in nuclear physics assumes that the nucleon in the nucleus moves in the average (mean field) potential generated by all other nucleons. This chapter gives a short overview of basic features of the independent particle motion in atomic nuclei and its theoretical realization in the framework of shell models for spherical, deformed and rotating nuclei as well as in more sophisticated approaches such as microscopic+macroscopic model and density functional theories. Independent particle motion of nucleons leads to global and single-particle consequences. The global ones manifest themselves in the shell structure and its consequences for global structure of nuclear landscape, the existence of superheavy nuclei and the superdeformation at high spin are briefly reviewed. The latter shows itself in the single-particle properties such as energies, alignments and densities; their manifestations are illustrated on specific examples.

## Key claim

This chapter provides a pedagogical overview of the independent particle model in nuclear physics, asserting that the model's success relies on the fact that the mean free path between nucleon collisions is large compared to the average inter-nucleon distance, and that nucleons occupy only approximately 1% of the nuclear volume, making nuclear matter behave close to a free gas with rare head-on collisions.

## Method

The chapter reviews the theoretical framework of the independent particle model, starting from the many-body nuclear Hamiltonian and its reduction to a sum of single-particle Hamiltonians via the mean-field approximation. It then describes the construction of phenomenological single-particle potentials (harmonic oscillator, modified oscillator/Nilsson, Woods-Saxon, and folded Yukawa) for spherical, deformed, and rotating nuclei, and discusses their implementation in the spherical shell model, deformed shell model (including Nilsson diagrams), and the cranked shell model for rotating systems. The chapter also examines spatial densities of single-particle states and their role in understanding clusterization and nuclear shapes.

## Key numbers

- Nucleons occupy approximately 1% of the volume of the nucleus, based on treating nucleons as hard spheres of radius c ≈ 0.5 fm (the half of the closest distance two nucleons can approach due to infinite repulsion).
- The Woods-Saxon potential uses a nuclear radius parameter r₀ ≈ 1.2 fm in the expression R = r₀ A^(1/3).
- Experimentally observed spherical shell closures occur at particle numbers 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, and 126.
- The single-particle density plots in the chapter use a contour starting at ρ = 0.001 fm⁻³ for visualization.
- In the cranked shell model example for superdeformed bands in ¹⁵²Dy, deformation parameters β₂ = 0.61 and β₄ = 0.11 are used, and a large N = 85 gap forms at ω ≈ 0.3 MeV that is absent at ω = 0 MeV.

## Context

This chapter extends the pedagogical tradition of nuclear structure textbooks such as Bohr and Mottelson (1975), Rowe (1980), and Casten (1990), and the Nilsson and Ragnarsson (1995) monograph. It provides a foundation for understanding more sophisticated approaches including microscopic+macroscopic models and density functional theories, and enables the interpretation of phenomena such as superheavy nuclei, superdeformation at high spin, and α-clusterization in light nuclei. The discussion of spatial densities and their nodal structure builds on recent work by Afanasjev (2018) on rod-shaped configurations and nuclear molecules.
