# Reverse engineering nuclear properties from rare earth abundances in the $r$ process

> The bulk of the rare earth elements are believed to be synthesized in the rapid neutron capture process or $r$ process of nucleosynthesis. The solar $r$-process residuals show a small peak in the rare earths around $A\sim 160$, which is proposed to be formed dynamically during the end phase of the $r$ process by a pileup of material. This abundance feature is of particular importance as it is sensitive to both the nuclear physics inputs and the astrophysical conditions of the main $r$ process. We explore the formation of the rare earth peak from the perspective of an inverse problem, using Monte Carlo studies of nuclear masses to investigate the unknown nuclear properties required to best match rare earth abundance sector of the solar isotopic residuals. When nuclear masses are changed, we recalculate the relevant $β$-decay properties and neutron capture rates in the rare earth region. The feedback provided by this observational constraint allows for the reverse engineering of nuclear properties far from stability where no experimental information exists. We investigate a range of astrophysical conditions with this method and show how these lead to different predictions in the nuclear properties influential to the formation of the rare earth peak. We conclude that targeted experimental campaigns in this region will help to resolve the type of conditions responsible for the production of the rare earth nuclei, and will provide new insights into the longstanding problem of the astrophysical site(s) of the $r$ process.

## Key claim

The rare earth peak in solar $r$-process residuals can be used to reverse engineer unknown nuclear masses far from stability, but the specific mass surface features required depend strongly on the assumed astrophysical conditions (hot, cold, or very neutron-rich cold trajectories), meaning that targeted nuclear measurements could distinguish between possible $r$-process sites.

## Method

The authors used a Monte Carlo Metropolis algorithm to vary a parameterized modification to the Duflo-Zuker mass model in the rare earth region (neutron numbers 95 to 115, atomic mass numbers approximately 150 to 180), recalculating beta-decay properties and neutron capture rates at each step. They coupled these nuclear inputs to a fast $r$-process network and ran simulations for nine different astrophysical trajectories representing hot, cold, and very neutron-rich cold conditions. The likelihood function combined a chi-squared comparison of the network output to solar isotopic $r$-process residuals (atomic mass numbers 150 to 180) with a chi-squared comparison of the modified masses to measured masses from the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation.

## Key numbers

* The mass modifications were applied to roughly 500 nuclei in the range $A\sim150$ to $A\sim180$.
* The Monte Carlo parameters for the $a_N$ coefficients were varied using Gaussian distributions with width 25 keV; the center parameter $C$ with width 0.1; and the fall-off parameter $f$ with width 0.5.
* The observational uncertainty in rare earth abundances was taken as $\Delta Y = 0.1$ dex.
* The average root-mean-square value for Duflo-Zuker masses compared to the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation was taken as $\Delta M \sim 400$ keV.
* A change in one-neutron separation energy of $\Delta S_n = 500$ keV yields roughly a factor of 2 (or less) change in neutron capture rate, and roughly up to a factor of 2 change in beta-decay half-life.

## Context

This work extends the dynamical mechanism for rare earth peak formation proposed by Surman et al. (1997) and Mumpower et al. (2012a, 2012b, 2012c), which requires a feature in the mass surface (such as a deformation-induced kink in neutron separation energies) to cause a pileup of material during freeze-out. The reverse engineering approach contrasts with forward calculations using specific mass models like FRDM (1995) or HFB-21, and it enables future experimental campaigns to measure masses and beta-decay properties of neutron-rich rare earth nuclei, which could help determine whether the $r$ process occurs in hot environments (e.g., neutrino-driven winds) or cold environments (e.g., neutron star mergers).
