# Studying Exotic Hadrons in Heavy Ion Collisions

> We investigate the possibilities of using measurements in present and future experiments on heavy ion collisions to answer some longstanding problems in hadronic physics, namely identifying hadronic molecular states and exotic hadrons with multiquark components. The yields of a selected set of exotic hadron candidates in relativistic heavy ion collisions are discussed in the coalescence model in comparison with the statistical model. We find that the yield of a hadron is typically an order of magnitude smaller when it is a compact multiquark state, compared to that of an excited hadronic state with normal quark numbers. We also find that some loosely bound hadronic molecules are formed more abundantly than the statistical model prediction by a factor of two or more. Moreover, due to the significant numbers of charm and bottom quarks produced at RHIC and even larger numbers expected at LHC, some of the proposed heavy exotic hadrons could be produced with sufficient abundance for detection, making it possible to study these new exotic hadrons in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The yield of an exotic hadron in relativistic heavy ion collisions is typically an order of magnitude smaller when it is a compact multiquark state compared to an excited hadronic state with normal quark numbers, while some loosely bound hadronic molecules are formed more abundantly than the statistical model prediction by a factor of two or more, provided that the coalescence model correctly captures the internal structure of the hadron.

## Method

The authors used a schematic model of isentropic boost-invariant longitudinal and accelerated transverse expansion to describe the time evolution of the hot dense matter formed in central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV (RHIC) and central Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=5 TeV (LHC). They calculated hadron yields using both the statistical model (assuming thermal and chemical equilibrium at hadronization temperature T_H=175 MeV) and the coalescence model, where hadron production is computed from the overlap of constituent phase-space distributions with Wigner functions of the produced hadrons. The oscillator frequencies for light, strange, charmed, and bottom hadrons were fixed by reproducing the statistical model yields of reference normal hadrons (Lambda(1115), Lambda_c(2286), and Lambda_b(5620)) in the coalescence model.

## Key numbers

- At RHIC, the charm quark number is N_c=3 and bottom quark number N_b=0.02 per central Au+Au collision; at LHC, N_c=20 and N_b=0.8 per central Pb+Pb collision.
- The hadronization temperature is T_C=T_H=175 MeV for both RHIC and LHC; the kinetic freeze-out temperature is T_F=125 MeV for both.
- The oscillator frequencies used in the coalescence model are omega=550 MeV for light quarks, omega_s=519 MeV for strange quarks, omega_c=385 MeV for charm quarks, and omega_b=338 MeV for bottom quarks.
- The coalescence factor for adding an s-wave quark is approximately 0.360, for a p-wave quark approximately 0.093, and for a d-wave quark approximately 0.029.
- The yield of Lambda(1520) in the coalescence model at RHIC is 1.6, compared to 1.9 in the statistical model; at LHC the values are 4.4 and 4.8 respectively.

## Context

This work extends the approach introduced by Cho et al. (2010) and Ohnishi et al. (2011) for studying exotic hadrons in heavy ion collisions, building on the coalescence model framework previously applied to light nuclei production (Chen 2003) and to the observed suppression of Lambda(1520) yields at RHIC (Kanada-En'yo 2006). The paper provides a comprehensive survey of proposed exotic hadrons including tetraquarks, pentaquarks, and dibaryons, and enables experimental discrimination between compact multiquark states and hadronic molecules by comparing their predicted yields from the coalescence model versus the statistical model in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC.
