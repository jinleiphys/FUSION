# Neutrino Emission from Supernovae

> Supernovae are the most powerful cosmic sources of MeV neutrinos. These elementary particles play a crucial role when the evolution of a massive star is terminated by the collapse of its core to a neutron star or a black hole and the star explodes as supernova. The release of electron neutrinos, which are abundantly produced by electron captures, accelerates the catastrophic infall and causes a gradual neutronization of the stellar plasma by converting protons to neutrons as dominant constituents of neutron star matter. The emission of neutrinos and antineutrinos of all flavors carries away the gravitational binding energy of the compact remnant and drives its evolution from the hot initial to the cold final state. The absorption of electron neutrinos and antineutrinos in the surroundings of the newly formed neutron star can power the supernova explosion and determines the conditions in the innermost supernova ejecta, making them an interesting site for the nucleosynthesis of iron-group elements and trans-iron nuclei. In this Chapter the basic neutrino physics in supernova cores and nascent neutron stars will be discussed. This includes the most relevant neutrino production, absorption, and scattering processes, elementary aspects of neutrino transport in dense environments, the characteristic neutrino emission phases with their typical signal features, and the perspectives connected to a measurement of the neutrino signal from a future galactic supernova.

## Key claim

Supernovae are the most powerful cosmic sources of MeV neutrinos, and these neutrinos carry away the gravitational binding energy of the compact remnant while potentially powering the supernova explosion through absorption in the surroundings of the newly formed neutron star; however, the actual explosion mechanism remains dependent on the efficiency of neutrino energy deposition, which is described as something that "can power" the explosion rather than a guaranteed process.

## Method

The paper reviews the basic weak interaction theory for neutrino production, absorption, and scattering in supernova cores and proto-neutron star matter, including charged-current beta processes and neutral-current reactions. It describes neutrino transport via the Boltzmann transport equation, with discussion of the diffusion approximation, two-moment transport, and the energy-dependent decoupling of different neutrino flavors at their respective neutrinospheres. The flavor-dependent differences between electron neutrinos and heavy-lepton neutrinos are analyzed in terms of their opacities and the locations of their transport and energy spheres.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star binding energy estimate: approximately 3.6 × 10^53 erg for a 1.5 solar mass neutron star with 10 km radius
- Nuclear saturation density: about 2.7 × 10^14 g cm^-3
- Neutrino trapping occurs when density exceeds a few times 10^11 g cm^-3
- Fermi coupling constant: G_F = 1.16637 × 10^-5 GeV^-2 = 1.43588 × 10^-49 erg cm^3
- Reference cross section σ_0 = 1.761 × 10^-44 cm^2
- Reference reaction rate R_0 = 3.297 × 10^39 σ_0 cm^-2 s^-1
- SN 1987A neutrino events were recorded over approximately 12 seconds with individual energies up to 40 MeV
- SN 1987A distance: roughly 50 kpc
- Neutrino energies in supernova cores are in the MeV range, much larger than the experimental rest-mass limit for active flavors (m_ν c^2 < 1 eV)
- Proto-neutron star temperatures: up to several 10^11 K (corresponding to several 10 MeV)
- Central temperature of neutrino-transparent neutron star: below about 1 MeV (roughly 10^10 K)

## Context

This chapter extends the foundational work by Colgate (1966) and Arnett (1966), who first recognized that neutrinos carry away the gravitational binding energy of neutron stars, and the revision of the theoretical picture after experimental confirmation of weak neutral currents in the early 1970s. The review covers the neutrino emission phases from core collapse through proto-neutron star cooling, building on prior work by Bethe (1990), Burrows (1986, 1990), and Raffelt (1996, 2001, 2012), and it enables predictions for neutrino signal detection from a future galactic supernova.
