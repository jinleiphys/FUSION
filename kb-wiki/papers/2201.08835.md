# Controlling extrapolations of nuclear properties with feature selection

> Predictions of nuclear properties far from measured data are inherently imprecise because of uncertainties in our knowledge of nuclear forces and in our treatment of quantum many-body effects in strongly-interacting systems. While the model bias can be directly calculated when experimental data is available, only an estimate can be made in the absence of such measurements. Current approaches to compute the estimated bias quickly lose predictive power when input variables such as proton or neutron number are extrapolated, resulting in uncontrolled uncertainties in applications such as nucleosynthesis simulations. In this letter, we present a novel technique to identify the input variables of machine learning algorithms that can provide robust estimates of model bias. Our process is based on selecting input variables, or features, based on their probability distribution functions across the entire nuclear chart. We illustrate our approach on the problem of quantifying the model bias in nuclear binding energies calculated with Density Functional Theory (DFT). We show that feature selection can systematically improve theoretical predictions without increasing uncertainties.

## Key claim

The authors propose that selecting machine learning features based on the similarity of their probability distributions between training and prediction regions (quantified by the Bhattacharyya coefficient) can eliminate the need for extrapolation when predicting nuclear properties, and that this feature selection is the only approach that consistently improves predictions without increasing uncertainties across all tested energy density functionals and data splits.

## Method

The authors used random forest regression to estimate the model bias (difference between DFT and experimental binding energies) for even-even nuclei calculated with the Skyrme energy density functionals SLY4 and UNEDF0. They compared three feature selection approaches: using only proton number Z and neutron number N (direct approach), using all available DFT properties as features (all-properties approach), and selecting only those properties whose probability distributions in the training and prediction regions have a Bhattacharyya coefficient above a chosen cutoff (selected-properties approach). The performance was evaluated across three different splits of the AME2020 data into training, testing, and prediction regions: a time-wise split, a large-N split, and a large-Z split.

## Key numbers

* The Bhattacharyya coefficient for neutron number N as a feature was 0.56, while for the deformation parameter beta_2 it was 0.94, indicating that N requires extrapolation while beta_2 does not.
* Cutoff values for the Bhattacharyya coefficient were set at 0.95 for the time-wise split, 0.90 for the large-N split, and 0.75 for the large-Z split.
* In the training region, the improvement index eta (ratio of root mean square error after bias removal to original root mean square error) was much less than 1 for all methods.
* In the testing region, eta generally remained lower than 0.5 for all methods.
* For the UNEDF0 functional with the direct approach and all-properties approach in the large-Z split (predictions far from training data), the improvement index eta took values greater than 1, meaning bias removal degraded predictive power.
* State-of-the-art mass models predict nuclear binding energies to within about 500 keV, while Penning-trap mass measurements yield results within a few eV.

## Context

This work extends the application of machine learning to nuclear mass predictions, building on prior efforts using Bayesian approaches (Neufcourt 2018, 2019), neural networks (Utama 2016, 2017, 2018), and Gaussian processes (Jiang 2019) to correct nuclear models. The authors specifically address the problem of uncontrolled uncertainties when extrapolating to neutron-rich nuclei needed for r-process simulations, which was identified as a limitation in previous approaches. The feature selection technique based on Bhattacharyya coefficients enables more reliable predictions for nuclei far from measured data, particularly relevant for next-generation radioactive ion beam facilities and nucleosynthesis simulations.
