# Adding Corrections to Global Spherical Potentials for Use in a Coupled-Channel Formulation

> The coupled-channel technique augments a non-relativistic distorted wave born approximation scattering calculation to include a coupling to virtual states from the negative energy region. It has been found to be important in low energy nucleon-nucleus scattering. We modify the nucleon-nucleus standard optical potentials, not designed for a coupled-channel space, so they can be used in that setting. The changes are small and systematic. We use a standard scattering code to adjust a variety of optical potentials and targets such that the original fit to scattering observables are maintained as we incorporate the coupled-channel environment. Overall over forty target nuclei were tested from $A=12$ to $A=205$ and nucleon projectile energies from 1 MeV to 200 MeV. There is excellent improvement in fitting the scattering observables, especially for low energy neutron scattering.The corrections were found to be unimportant for projectile energies greater than 200 MeV. The largest changes are to the surface amplitudes while the real radii and the real central amplitude are modified by only a few percent, every other parameter is unchanged. This technique is general enough to be applied to a variety of inelastic theoretical calculations.

# Adding Corrections to Global Spherical Potentials for Use in a Coupled-Channel Formulation

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that standard spherical DWBA optical potentials, which are not designed for coupled-channel calculations, can be systematically modified with small adjustments to six parameters (real radii, real central amplitude, real spin-orbit radius, and surface amplitudes) so that they reproduce the original elastic scattering observables when used in a coupled-channel environment, with the caveat that the prescription works best for projectile energies below 200 MeV and is most effective for low-energy neutron scattering.

## Method

The authors used the ECIS scattering code to take global spherical optical potentials (primarily Koning and Delaroche (2003) for A>26 and Weppner (2009, 2014) for A<27) and apply analytical corrections to the real volume, surface, and spin-orbit radii using a deformation-dependent energy scaling factor, while also adjusting the real central amplitude via volume integral conservation. The real and imaginary surface amplitudes were then fitted directly to maintain agreement with the original DWBA-calculated elastic differential cross-sections, total reaction cross-sections, and for neutrons the total cross-sections, using a weighted chi-squared metric that prioritized forward-angle elastic data and total cross-sections over backward-angle and polarization data. Over forty target nuclei from A=12 to A=205 were tested with nucleon projectile energies from 1 MeV to 200 MeV, using either vibrational models (for A<130 or odd-A nuclei) or rotational models (for A>130 even-even nuclei) with deformation parameters taken from literature sources.

## Key numbers

- Over 40 target nuclei were tested, ranging from A=12 to A=205, with nucleon projectile energies from 1 MeV to 200 MeV
- The weighted chi-squared improvement ratio averaged 16 across all cases, with the best case showing a factor of 400 improvement and the worst case (at high energy) showing a factor of 1.25 improvement
- For neutron projectiles at 1.0 MeV, the average chi-squared dropped from 2.1 x 10^4 (unmodified) to 1.5 x 10^2 (modified); at 200.0 MeV for neutrons, the chi-squared dropped from 4.5 x 10^1 to 4.0 x 10^1
- For proton projectiles at 5.0 MeV, the average chi-squared dropped from 7.3 x 10^1 to 1.1 x 10^0; at 200.0 MeV for protons, from 4.4 x 10^1 to 3.6 x 10^1
- The surface amplitudes were changed by at most 6 MeV (real surface increased, imaginary surface decreased)
- The real radii and real central amplitude were modified by only a few percent
- The energy scaling factor used was 60^4 / (E^2 + 60^2)^2, which at 240 MeV gives a suppression factor of about 300 relative to the 0 MeV baseline
- Corrections were found to be unimportant for projectile energies greater than 200 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous efforts by Bang and Vaagen (1980), who modified radius terms to conserve volume during deformation, and by Nobre et al. (2015), who applied similar corrections to rare-earth nuclei, as well as the TALYS code's manual prescription of reducing the imaginary surface term by 15% for coupled-channel calculations. The authors generalize these corrections to a much wider range of target masses (A=12-205) and projectile energies (1-200 MeV) for both neutrons and protons, and additionally adjust the spin-orbit radius and real central amplitude analytically while fitting surface amplitudes to observables. This enables researchers to use standard global spherical optical potentials in coupled-channel calculations without refitting to experimental data, and the authors note that their modified potentials show increased sensitivity to deformation parameters, which could help resolve discrepancies between theoretical and experimental deformation values.
