# Peculiarity of the $^{12}$C$(0^+)$ and $^{12}$C$(2^+)$ energy spectrum in a 3$α$ model

> Lowest energy spectrum of the $^{12}$C nucleus is analyzed in the 3$α$ cluster model with a deep $αα$-potential of Buck, Friedrich and Wheatley with Pauli forbidden states in the $S$ and $D$ waves. The direct orthogonalization method is applied for the elimination of the 3$α$-Pauli forbidden states. The effects of possible first order quantum phase transition are shown in the lowest $^{12}$C($0_1^+)$ and $^{12}$C($2_1^+)$ states from weakly bound phase to a deep phase. The ground and lowest $2^+$ states of the $^{12}$C nucleus in the deep phase are created by the critical eigen states of the Pauli projector for the $0^+$ and $2^+$ three-alpha functional spaces, respectively.

## Key claim

The paper suggests that the non-analytical behavior of the lowest \(^{12}\text{C}(0_1^+)\) and \(^{12}\text{C}(2_1^+)\) energy levels, observed when varying the treatment of Pauli forbidden states in the 3\(\alpha\) model, can be interpreted as a possible first-order quantum phase transition between a weakly bound phase and a deep phase, rather than being an artifact of the orthogonalization method.

## Method

The authors employed a variational method on a symmetrized Gaussian basis to solve the three-body Schrödinger equation for the 3\(\alpha\) system, using the deep Buck-Friedrich-Wheatley (BFW) \(\alpha\alpha\)-potential. The direct orthogonalization method was applied to separate the complete Hilbert space into allowed (\(L_Q\)) and forbidden (\(L_P\)) subspaces by diagonalizing the three-body Pauli projector \(\hat{P}\) and selecting eigenstates with eigenvalues below a threshold \(\epsilon\). The energy spectrum of the lowest \(0^+\) and \(2^+\) states was then calculated as a function of the maximal allowed eigenvalue \(\epsilon\) of \(\hat{P}\).

## Key numbers

- The BFW potential parameters are \(V_0 = -122.6225\) MeV, \(\eta = 0.22\) fm\(^{-2}\) for the nuclear part, and \(b = 0.75\) fm\(^{-1}\) for the Coulomb part; it contains two Pauli forbidden states in the \(S\) wave with energies \(E_1 = -72.6257\) MeV and \(E_2 = -25.6186\) MeV, and one forbidden state in the \(D\) wave with \(E_3 = -22.0005\) MeV.
- For the \(0^+\) spectrum, two almost forbidden states (AFS) have eigenvalues \(\epsilon_1 = 1.35333 \times 10^{-5}\) and \(\epsilon_2 = 1.07152 \times 10^{-3}\); including the second AFS in the allowed subspace creates a deep phase ground state at \(E = -19.897\) MeV, while excluding it yields a weakly bound state at \(E = -0.627\) MeV.
- For the \(2^+\) spectrum, the two AFS have eigenvalues \(\epsilon_3 = 6.74419 \times 10^{-6}\) and \(\epsilon_4 = 3.83029 \times 10^{-4}\); including the second AFS creates a deep phase state at \(E = -16.572\) MeV, while excluding it yields a weakly bound state at \(E = 1.873\) MeV.
- The experimental ground state energy of \(^{12}\text{C}\) is \(E_{exp} = -7.274\) MeV; the experimental Hoyle state energy is \(E_{exp}(0_2^+) = 0.380\) MeV; the experimental \(2_2^+\) energy is \(E_{exp}(2_2^+) = 2.596\) MeV.
- The energy shift for the possible QPT is about 19.27 MeV for the \(0^+\) spectrum and about 18.45 MeV for the \(2^+\) spectrum.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Fujii et al. (2006), who first identified the "almost forbidden states" in the 3\(\alpha\) system using the direct orthogonalization method but did not interpret the resulting non-analytical behavior as a quantum phase transition. The present study also clarifies the failure of the orthogonalizing pseudopotential (OPP) method applied earlier by Tursunov et al. (2001), where increasing the projecting constant \(\lambda_f\) from \(10^4\) MeV to \(10^6\) MeV caused the ground state energy to jump from -16.106 MeV to -0.435 MeV, a behavior now explained by the presence of AFS. The results are compared with ab-initio calculations by Serdar et al. (2016) that suggested a "nature near a quantum phase transition" in \(^{12}\text{C}\). The findings imply that similar QPT effects may appear in other multi-alpha cluster systems (e.g., \(^{16}\text{O}\), \(^{20}\text{Ne}\)) when the Pauli principle is treated exactly.
