# Prompt, pre-equilibrium, and thermal photons in relativistic nuclear collisions

> The direct photon emission model in relativistic nuclear colliders has been improved in recent years for reducing the discrepancy between theoretical estimations and experimental data and for understanding the properties of the QCD matter. In this study, the contribution of pre-equilibrium photons are investigated in addition to those of prompt and thermal photons in the framework of a relativistic hydrodynamic model. The numerical simulations at an LHC energy suggest that the pre-equilibrium photons may be relevant at intermediate transverse momentum near the saturation momentum scale, increasing particle spectra and reducing elliptic flow of direct photons.

## Key claim
Pre-equilibrium photons, modeled using self-similar scaling solutions for parton distributions in the glasma stage, may be relevant at intermediate transverse momentum near the saturation momentum scale in relativistic nuclear collisions, increasing particle spectra and reducing elliptic flow of direct photons; however, the study is a proof-of-principle and a full comparison to experimental data is beyond its scope.

## Method
The author introduces pre-equilibrium photon emission into a relativistic hydrodynamic model for the first time, using self-similar scaling laws for quark and gluon phase-space distributions motivated by turbulent thermalization. The pre-equilibrium stage is divided into three phases following the bottom-up thermalization scenario, and the photon emission rate is integrated over space-time and combined with prompt photons (from a scaled proton-proton parametrization) and thermal photons (from an interpolation of hadronic and QGP emission rates). The initial energy density distribution for the pre-equilibrium stage is given by the Monte-Carlo Glauber model, and the hydrodynamic evolution uses a lattice QCD-based equation of state with vanishing viscosity.

## Key numbers
- Pre-equilibrium photons are 47.8% of direct photons at \(p_T = Q_s = 2.5\) GeV for \(\sigma_z = 0.05\) GeV, 29.8% for \(\sigma_z = 0.1\) GeV, 18.0% for \(\sigma_z = 0.2\) GeV, and 14.5% for \(\sigma_z = 0.4\) GeV in Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 2.76\) TeV with impact parameter \(b = 4.6\) fm.
- The saturation momentum scale \(Q_s\) is set to 2.5 GeV for the central demonstration; the average \(Q_s\) at LHC energies is estimated to be around 2-3 GeV.
- The hydrodynamization time is chosen as \(\tau_\mathrm{hyd} = 0.6\) fm/\(c\), and the initial time is \(\tau_\mathrm{ini} = 1/Q_s\).
- The freeze-out temperature is \(T_f = 140\) MeV, and the crossover temperature is \(T_c = 170\) MeV.
- The inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross section is \(\sigma_{pp}^\mathrm{in} = 65\) mb, and the averaged number of collisions is \(N_\mathrm{coll} = 1256\) for 0-20% centrality.

## Context
This work extends prior efforts to resolve the "photon puzzle", the discrepancy between observed direct photon elliptic flow and hydrodynamic model predictions, by adding pre-equilibrium photon emission, which had been neglected in most previous models (e.g., Turbide (2003), Chatterjee (2005), Gale (2014)). It builds on the turbulent thermalization approach of Berges (2013, 2014, 2017) and the bottom-up thermalization scenario of Baier (2000). The results suggest that direct photon spectra and elliptic flow can be used to experimentally constrain early-time dynamics, and the approach can be extended to proton-proton collisions where glasma may also be produced.
