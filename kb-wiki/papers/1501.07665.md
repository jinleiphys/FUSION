# Neutrino induced decoherence and variation in nuclear decay rates

> Recent work has proposed that the interaction between ordinary matter and a stochastic gravitational background can lead to the decoherence of large aggregates of ordinary matter. In this work we point out that these arguments can be carried over to a stochastic neutrino background but with the Planck scale of the gravitational decoherence replaced by the weak scale. This implies that it might be possible to observe such neutrino induced decoherence on a small, microscopic system rather than a macroscopic system as is the case for gravitationally induced decoherence. In particular we suggest that neutrino decoherence could be linked with observed variations in the decay rates of certain nuclei. Finally we point out that this proposed neutrino induced decoherence can be considered the complement of the Mikheev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein (MSW) effect.

# Neutrino induced decoherence and variation in nuclear decay rates

## Key claim

The authors argue that the decoherence mechanism previously proposed for a stochastic gravitational background (Blencowe, 2013) can be carried over to a stochastic neutrino background, with the Planck scale replaced by the weak scale, potentially making neutrino-induced decoherence observable in individual nuclear systems rather than requiring macroscopic aggregates of matter. The claim is hedged by the caveat that the high-temperature limit used in the decoherence rate derivation may not be justified for cosmological neutrinos if any neutrino flavor has a mass near the current upper limit of 2 eV, though solar neutrinos would satisfy this condition.

## Method

The authors take the general system-bath Hamiltonian and decoherence rate formalism from Blencowe (2013), originally applied to a gravitational bath, and replace the gravitational coupling (Newton's constant) with the weak interaction coupling (Fermi constant). They use the effective field theory of the weak interaction, the Fermi four-fermion interaction, to derive the neutrino-induced decoherence rate, and then compute numerical estimates for atomic and nuclear systems using the weak energy scale E_weak = sqrt(1/G_F) ≈ 300 GeV. The weak charge of nuclei is calculated from the quark weak charges using the Weinberg angle, showing that the neutron contribution dominates.

## Key numbers

- Fermi coupling G_F ≈ 1.17 × 10^{-5} GeV^{-2} compared to Newton's constant G_N ≈ 6.71 × 10^{-39} GeV^{-2}, both in units of GeV^{-2} with hbar = c = 1
- Weak energy scale E_weak = sqrt(1/G_F) ≈ 300 GeV compared to Planck energy scale E_Pl ≈ 1.22 × 10^{19} GeV
- Neutrino-induced decoherence rate is (E_Pl / E_weak)^2 ≈ 10^{33} times larger than gravitational decoherence rate
- For a single atomic system with energy difference 1 eV: Gamma_weak(atomic) ≈ 10^{-12} Z_e (1 - 4 sin^2 theta_W) Hz; t_weak(atomic) ≈ 10^{12} / [Z_e (1 - 4 sin^2 theta_W)] sec
- For individual nuclei with energy difference 1 MeV: Gamma_weak(nuclear) ≈ 2 |Z(1 - 4 sin^2 theta_W) - N| Hz; t_weak(nuclear) ≈ 0.5 / |Z(1 - 4 sin^2 theta_W) - N| sec
- For Si-32 (N=18, decay energy 0.2 MeV): Gamma_weak ≈ 0.72 Hz; t_weak ≈ 1.39 sec
- For Ra-226 (N=138, decay energy 4.9 MeV): Gamma_weak ≈ 3700 Hz; t_weak ≈ 2.7 × 10^{-4} sec
- Cosmological neutrino background temperature T_nu ≈ 2 K; graviton background temperature T_g ≈ 1 K; photon CMB temperature T_gamma ≈ 2.7 K
- Upper limit on neutrino mass m_nu < 2 eV; mass squared differences Delta m_21^2 ≈ 8 × 10^{-5} eV^2 and Delta m_32^2 ≈ 2 × 10^{-3} eV^2
- Weak charge of proton Q^p_weak ≈ +0.08; neutron Q^n_weak = -1; electron Q^e_weak ≈ -0.08 (using sin^2 theta_W ≈ 0.23)

## Context

This work extends the gravitational decoherence mechanism of Blencowe (2013) to the neutrino sector, noting that both gravitons and neutrinos are difficult to shield and couple to bulk matter. The authors suggest that neutrino-induced decoherence could explain the observed seasonal variation in decay rates of Si-32 and Ra-226 reported by Fischbach (2012), where decay rates show a maximum in January (perihelion) and minimum in July (aphelion), consistent with the anti-quantum Zeno effect. The proposed mechanism is identified as the complement or inverse of the Mikheev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein (MSW) effect (Mikheev and Smirnov, 1985; Wolfenstein, 1978), explaining why the decoherence rate scales as G_F (first order) rather than G_F^2 (second order). The paper enables experimental tests using neutron-rich radioactive nuclei and suggests placing such materials on spacecraft with highly elliptical orbits to amplify the predicted variation.
