# Power Counting and Wilsonian Renormalization in Nuclear Effective Field Theory

> Effective field theories are the most general tool for the description of low energy phenomena. They are universal and systematic: they can be formulated for any low energy systems we can think of and offer a clear guide on how to calculate predictions with reliable error estimates, a feature that is called power counting. These properties can be easily understood in Wilsonian renormalization, in which effective field theories are the low energy renormalization group evolution of a more fundamental ---perhaps unknown or unsolvable--- high energy theory. In nuclear physics they provide the possibility of a theoretically sound derivation of nuclear forces without having to solve quantum chromodynamics explicitly. However there is the problem of how to organize calculations within nuclear effective field theory: the traditional knowledge about power counting is perturbative but nuclear physics is not. Yet power counting can be derived in Wilsonian renormalization and there is already a fairly good understanding of how to apply these ideas to non-perturbative phenomena and in particular to nuclear physics. Here we review a few of these ideas, explain power counting in two-nucleon scattering and reactions with external probes and hint at how to extend the present analysis beyond the two-body problem.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Wilsonian renormalization provides a clearer interpretation of power counting in nuclear effective field theory than the standard approach of removing ultraviolet divergences, but this clarity depends on the assumption that the hard scale M is well separated from the soft scale Q, and the analysis is primarily demonstrated for two-nucleon s-wave scattering rather than fully extended to many-body systems.

## Method

The author derives renormalization group equations (RGEs) for contact-range couplings by introducing a cut-off radius r_c in the Schrödinger equation for non-relativistic s-wave scattering, then requiring that observables remain unchanged by adding a delta-shell potential at r_c. The running of the couplings C_{2n}(r_c) is obtained by expanding the RGE in powers of k^2 and matching to the effective range expansion, distinguishing between natural scattering lengths (M a_0 ~ 1) and unnaturally large scattering lengths (Q a_0 ~ 1). The analysis is extended to include a long-range one-pion exchange potential, where the strength scale Λ_NN determines whether the potential is perturbative or non-perturbative.

## Key numbers

- The hard scale M characterizing the range of the underlying potential satisfies M r_0 ~ 1 and M^{2n+1} v_n ~ 1 for the effective range r_0 and shape coefficients v_n.
- For natural scattering length, the leading contact coupling scales as C_0 ~ 1/M^2, specifically C_0(r_c) = (2π/μ) a_0 [1 + a_0/r_c + O(a_0^2/r_c^2)].
- For unnaturally large scattering length (Q a_0 ~ 1), C_0 ~ 1/(M Q), given by C_0(r_c) = (2π/μ) r_c a_0/(r_c - a_0), representing an enhancement of one power of M/Q.
- The one-pion exchange potential strength scale Λ_NN is approximately 300 MeV, and the pion mass m is the soft scale Q in that context.
- The average distance of nucleons in a nucleus is about 1-2 fm, while the size of the nucleon is about 0.5 fm.

## Context

This review extends the Wilsonian renormalization group analysis of Birse, McGovern and Richardson (1998) from momentum space to coordinate space, making the interpretation of power counting clearer for nuclear effective field theory. It addresses the inconsistency in the Weinberg proposal discovered by Nogga, Timmermans and van Kolck (2005), where the proposal was not renormalizable in some P- and D-waves at lowest order, and builds on subsequent developments by Birse (2005) and Pavon Valderrama (2007) that made a consistent nuclear EFT possible. The work enables a systematic framework for organizing calculations in non-perturbative nuclear physics, with hints toward extending the analysis beyond the two-body problem to three-body systems and electroweak reactions.
