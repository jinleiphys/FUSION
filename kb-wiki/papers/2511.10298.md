# Shedding Light on (Anti-)nuclei Production with Pion-Nucleus Femtoscopy

> High-energy nuclear collisions provide a unique environment for synthesizing both nuclei and antinuclei (such as $\bar{d}$ and $\overline{^4\text{He}}$) at temperatures ($k_BT\sim100$ MeV) nearly two orders of magnitude above their binding energies of a few MeV. The underlying production mechanism, whether through statistical hadronization, nucleon coalescence, or dynamical regeneration and disintegration, remains unsettled. Here we address this question using a novel tool of pion-nucleus femtoscopy. By solving relativistic kinetic equations for pion-catalyzed reactions ($πNN \leftrightarrow πd$) for deuteron production and including a $70~\mathrm{MeV}/c^2$ downward shift of the in-medium $Δ(1232)$ mass, we successfully reproduce the resonance peaks observed by the ALICE Collaboration in both $π^+-p$ and $π^+-d$ femtoscopic correlation functions in high-multiplicity $pp$ collisions at $\sqrt{s} = 13~\mathrm{TeV}$. We further find that the nucleon coalescence model reproduces only about half of the observed peak strength, while the statistical hadronization model predicts no resonance feature. These results provide compelling evidence that pion-catalyzed reactions play a dominant role in the production of light (anti-)nuclei in high-energy nuclear collisions and cosmic rays.

## Key claim

By solving relativistic kinetic equations for pion-catalyzed reactions and including a 70 MeV/c² downward shift of the in-medium Δ(1232) mass, the authors reproduce the resonance peaks observed by ALICE in both π⁺-p and π⁺-d femtoscopic correlation functions; the nucleon coalescence model reproduces only about half of the observed peak strength, while the statistical hadronization model predicts no resonance feature.

## Method

The authors employed a multi-phase transport (AMPT) model to simulate the partonic and hadronic evolution of high-multiplicity pp collisions at √s = 13 TeV, including pion-catalyzed deuteron formation and dissociation reactions (πNN ↔ πd) via relativistic kinetic equations solved with a stochastic test-particle method. They computed two-particle femtoscopic correlation functions C(k*) using the Bethe-Salpeter amplitude for final-state interactions, comparing results from the pion-catalysis framework against predictions from the nucleon coalescence model and the statistical hadronization model (blast-wave parameterization). An in-medium Δ mass shift of δm_Δ = -70 MeV was introduced to account for the observed peak positions in the correlation functions.

## Key numbers

- The in-medium Δ(1232) mass was reduced by 70 MeV/c² from its vacuum value of 1.232 GeV/c² to 1.162 GeV/c² to match the observed correlation peak positions.
- The π⁺-p and π⁺-d correlation functions exhibit resonance peaks at k* ≈ 0.22 GeV/c with nominal Δ mass, shifting to k* ≈ 0.15-0.17 GeV/c with the 70 MeV/c² downward mass shift.
- The ALICE data were taken from high-multiplicity pp collisions at √s = 13 TeV with kinematic cuts: |η| ≤ 0.8, 0.14 < p_T < 4.0 GeV/c for pions, and 0.5 < p_T < 2.4 GeV/c for deuterons.
- The effective interaction range parameter l was set to 1.2 fm, and the deuteron Wigner function width parameter σ_d was taken as approximately 3.2 fm.
- The charged particle multiplicity was ⟨dN_ch/dη⟩ ≈ 26 for the theoretical calculations.

## Context

This work extends the femtoscopic approach beyond conventional hadron-hadron pairs (Fabbietti 2020) to hadron-nucleus systems, building on recent ALICE measurements of π⁺-p and π⁺-d correlations (ALICE 2025). It directly tests three competing scenarios for light (anti-)nuclei production: statistical hadronization (Andronic 2017), nucleon coalescence at kinetic freeze-out (Scheibl 1998, Bellini 2020), and continuous disintegration/regeneration driven by pion-catalyzed reactions (Oliinychenko 2018, Sun 2022). The results demonstrate that pion-nucleus femtoscopy can distinguish these mechanisms, with the pion-catalyzed framework providing the best description, and the approach can be extended to larger collision systems and heavier light nuclei such as π-³He, π-³H, π-⁴He, and π-³_ΛH pairs.
