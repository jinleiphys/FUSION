# Hybrid stars that masquerade as neutron stars

> We show that a hybrid (nuclear + quark matter) star can have a mass-radius relationship very similar to that predicted for a star made of purely nucleonic matter. We show this for a generic parameterization of the quark matter equation of state, and also for an MIT bag model, each including a phenomenological correction based on gluonic corrections to the equation of state. We obtain hybrid stars as heavy as 2 M_solar for reasonable values of the bag model parameters. For nuclear matter, we use the equation of state calculated by Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall using many-body techniques. Both mixed and homogeneous phases of nuclear and quark matter are considered.

## Key claim

Hybrid stars containing a quark matter core can have mass-radius relationships nearly identical to those of purely nucleonic stars, making them difficult to distinguish observationally. This similarity occurs specifically when the quark matter equation of state includes perturbative QCD corrections parameterized by a coefficient \(a_4 \approx 0.7\) (corresponding to \(c \approx 0.3\)), and the result is robust across different quark matter models and the inclusion of mixed phases.

## Method

The authors constructed hybrid star models by combining the Akmal-Pandharipande-Ravenhall (APR) equation of state for nuclear matter with a phenomenological parameterization of the quark matter equation of state: \(\Omega_{\rm QM} = -\frac{3}{4\pi^2} a_4 \mu^4 + \frac{3}{4\pi^2} a_2 \mu^2 + B_{\rm eff}\), where \(a_4 = 1-c\) incorporates QCD corrections. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to obtain mass-radius relations, tuning the bag constant \(B_{\rm eff}\) to fix the nuclear-to-quark matter transition density \(\rho_{\rm crit}\). They then extended the analysis to an MIT bag model with color-flavor-locked (CFL) superconducting quark matter, including both homogeneous and mixed phases of nuclear and quark matter.

## Key numbers

- Maximum hybrid star mass of approximately \(2~M_\odot\) is achievable for \(a_4 \approx 0.7\) (\(c \approx 0.3\)) with \(\sqrt{a_2}\) in the range \(150~\text{MeV}\) to \(250~\text{MeV}\).
- For free quark matter (\(c=0\)), the maximum hybrid star mass is about \(1.6~M_\odot\), consistent with previous studies.
- The QCD correction parameter \(c \approx 0.37\) was obtained by Fraga, Pisarski, and Schaffner-Bielich from matching to \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s^2)\) perturbative calculations in the interval \(\mu \simeq 300-600~\text{MeV}\).
- For \(c=0.3\) and \(a_2 = (180~\text{MeV})^2\), hybrid stars have masses approaching \(2~M_\odot\) and radii around \(11~\text{km}\).
- The transition density \(\rho_{\rm crit}\) was varied between \(2\) and \(4~n_{\rm sat}\), where \(n_{\rm sat} = 0.16~\text{fm}^{-3}\).
- For \(a_4=0.7\) and \(a_2=(150~\text{MeV})^2\), the bag constant \(B_{\rm eff}^{1/4} = 141.2~\text{MeV}\) gives a first transition at \(\mu = 324.4~\text{MeV}\) (\(\rho_{\rm crit} = 1.5~n_{\rm sat}\)), while \(B_{\rm eff}^{1/4} = 141.5~\text{MeV}\) causes the transition to jump to \(\mu = 458~\text{MeV}\) (\(\rho = 4.3~n_{\rm sat}\)).
- The APR equation of state exhibits a transition from low-density phase to high-density phase in pure neutron matter at \(\sim 0.20~\text{fm}^{-3}\) and in symmetric nuclear matter at \(\sim 0.32~\text{fm}^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Alford and Reddy (2003) that used a simple MIT bag model without QCD corrections and found maximum hybrid star masses of about \(1.6~M_\odot\). It contrasts with the general conclusion from prior work by Lattimer and Prakash (2000), Maieron et al. (2004), Buballa (2003), and others that quark matter softens the equation of state, yielding lower maximum masses (\(M \lesssim 1.7~M_\odot\)) than nuclear matter stars (\(M \lesssim 2.2~M_\odot\)). The key advance is showing that including perturbative QCD corrections (parameterized by \(a_4 \approx 0.7\)) allows hybrid stars to reach \(2~M_\odot\) while masquerading as purely nucleonic stars, making it harder to rule out quark matter cores via mass-radius observations. The results enable future work to focus on transport properties and other non-static observables to distinguish hybrid from nucleonic stars.
