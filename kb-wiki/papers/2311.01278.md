# Rotating Bose-Einstein Condensate Stars at finite temperature

> We study the effect of temperature on the global properties of static and slowly rotating self-gravitating Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC) stars within general relativity. We employ a recently developed temperature dependent BEC equation of state (EoS) to describe the stellar matter by assuming that the condensate can be described by a non-relativistic EoS. Stellar profiles are obtained using general relativistic Hartle-Thorne slow rotation approximation equations. We find that with increasing temperatures mass-radius values are found to be decreasing for the static and rotating cases; though presence of temperature supports high mass values at lower central densities. Countering effects of rotation and temperature on the BEC stellar structure have been analysed and quantified. We report that inclusion of temperature has significant effect on the rotating stellar profiles but negligible effect on the maximum mass, as in the case of static system. We have also studied the effect of EoS parameters -- boson mass and strength of the self-interaction -- on global properties of static and rotating BEC stars, in presence of temperature.

## Key claim

Temperature has a significant effect on the mass-radius profiles of both static and slowly rotating Bose-Einstein condensate stars, but the maximum mass remains nearly unchanged across the temperature range studied (1-4 × 10¹¹ K), with only a 0.01 M⊙ decrease at the highest temperature.

## Method

The authors solved the general relativistic Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for static configurations and the Hartle-Thorne slow rotation approximation equations for rotating configurations, using a recently developed finite-temperature BEC equation of state derived from the generalized Gross-Pitaevskii equation with repulsive contact and attractive gravitational interactions under the Thomas-Fermi and static Popov approximations. The stellar matter was treated as a non-relativistic condensate composed of bosons with mass m = 2m_n (twice the nucleon mass) and s-wave scattering length a = 1 fm, with temperatures ranging from 1 × 10¹¹ K to 4 × 10¹¹ K. Rotating configurations were computed at the Keplerian angular velocity Ω_K = √(GM/R³).

## Key numbers

- Static BEC star at T = 0 K: maximum mass M = 0.71 M⊙, radius R = 3.97 km, central density ρ_c = 2.03 × 10¹⁶ g/cm³
- Static BEC star at T = 4 × 10¹¹ K: maximum mass M = 0.70 M⊙ (negligible change from zero temperature)
- Rotating BEC star at T = 0 K (Keplerian rotation): maximum mass M = 0.85 M⊙, radius R = 4.47 km, central density ρ_c = 1.6 × 10¹⁶ g/cm³
- Rotating BEC star at T = 4 × 10¹¹ K (Keplerian rotation): maximum mass M = 0.84 M⊙
- Radii for a 0.65 M⊙ static star: 4.71 km (T = 1 × 10¹¹ K), 4.56 km (T = 2 × 10¹¹ K), 4.40 km (T = 3 × 10¹¹ K), 4.24 km (T = 4 × 10¹¹ K)
- Radii for a 0.8 M⊙ rotating star: 5.28 km (T = 1 × 10¹¹ K), 5.08 km (T = 2 × 10¹¹ K), 4.87 km (T = 3 × 10¹¹ K), 4.66 km (T = 4 × 10¹¹ K)
- Keplerian angular velocities for maximum mass configurations: (3.89, 3.96, 4.07, 4.19, 4.37) × 10⁴ s⁻¹ for T = (0, 1, 2, 3, 4) × 10¹¹ K respectively
- At ρ = 2 × 10¹⁶ g/cm³, pressure deviation from zero temperature: ∼0.27% at T = 4 × 10¹¹ K, ∼0.008% at T = 1 × 10¹¹ K

## Context

This work extends the general relativistic treatment of BEC stars by Chavanis (2011) and the finite-temperature BEC equation of state developed by Gruber (2014) to include slow rotation via the Hartle-Thorne approximation, which had not been applied to BEC stars before. The results show that temperature softens the equation of state and reduces mass-radius values, but the maximum mass remains robust, similar to findings by Latifah (2014) using a different finite-temperature EoS. This enables future studies of BEC stars at finite temperature with more realistic rotation profiles and could inform models of dark matter halos or exotic compact object candidates.
