# The Nuclear Symmetry Energy

> The nuclear symmetry energy characterizes the variation of the binding energy as the neutron to proton ratio of a nuclear system is varied. This is one of the most important features of nuclear physics in general, since it is just related to the two component nature of the nuclear systems. As such it is one of the most relevant physical parameters that affect the physics of many phenomena and nuclear processes. This review paper presents a survey of the role and relevance of the nuclear symmetry energy in different fields of research and of the accuracy of its determination from the phenomenology and from the microscopic many-body theory. In recent years, a great interest was devoted not only to the Nuclear Matter symmetry energy at saturation density but also to its whole density dependence, which is an essential ingredient for our understanding of many phenomena. We analyze the nuclear symmetry energy in different realms of nuclear physics and astrophysics. In particular we consider the nuclear symmetry energy in relation to nuclear structure, astrophysics of Neutron Stars and supernovae, and heavy ion collision experiments, trying to elucidate the connections of these different fields on the basis of the symmetry energy peculiarities. The interplay between experimental and observational data and theoretical developments is stressed. The expected future developments and improvements are schematically addressed, together with most demanded experimental and theoretical advances for the next few years.

## Key claim

This review paper surveys the role and relevance of the nuclear symmetry energy across nuclear structure, astrophysics of neutron stars and supernovae, and heavy ion collisions, emphasizing that while the symmetry energy at saturation density is reasonably well constrained, its density dependence above saturation remains poorly determined and strongly model-dependent.

## Method

The authors review and compare multiple approaches to determining the nuclear symmetry energy and its density dependence: semi-classical methods (liquid drop model, droplet model, Thomas-Fermi approximation), quantal energy density functional methods (notably Skyrme forces), microscopic many-body calculations of nuclear matter, and phenomenological constraints from nuclear binding energies, neutron skin measurements, isovector excitations, heavy ion collision data, and neutron star observations. They analyze the consistency between theoretical predictions and experimental/observational constraints across these different realms.

## Key numbers

- The free Fermi gas symmetry energy at saturation density is approximately 12 MeV, while the interacting nuclear matter value is estimated to be more than twice larger.
- A fit of the liquid drop model over 3100 nuclei with A > 10 gives the finite-nuclei symmetry energy S_N = 22.5 MeV, with an average error of 3.6 MeV for total binding energies up to 2000 MeV.
- The finite range droplet model fit yields S = 32 ± 0.5 MeV and L = 70 ± 15 MeV, with a standard deviation from experimental nuclear masses of 0.57 MeV.
- Most liquid drop model fits give a symmetry energy S centered around 30 MeV with a spread of ±3-4 MeV.

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes prior work on the nuclear symmetry energy, including the reviews by Tsang, Lattimer, and Horowitz, and the microscopic many-body calculations of Baldo and collaborators. It connects the liquid drop model tradition (dating to the 1950s), the droplet model of Myers and Swiatecki (1966-1969), and the Strutinsky shell correction method with modern energy density functional approaches and astrophysical constraints from neutron stars and supernovae. The paper enables a systematic comparison between microscopic theory and phenomenological constraints, identifying where agreement is satisfactory (up to saturation density) and where further work is needed (supra-saturation density).
