# Strange Hadron Spectroscopy with a Secondary KL Beam at GlueX

> We propose to create a secondary beam of neutral kaons in Hall D at Jefferson Lab to be used with the GlueX experimental setup for strange hadron spectroscopy. A flux on the order of 3 x 10^4 KL/s will allow a broad range of measurements to be made by improving the statistics of previous data obtained on hydrogen targets by three orders of magnitude. Use of a deuteron target will provide first measurements on the neutron which is {\it terra incognita}. The experiment will measure both differential cross sections and self-analyzed polarizations of the produced Λ, Σ, Ξ, and Ω hyperons using the GlueX detector at the Jefferson Lab Hall D. The measurements will span c.m. cosθ from -0.95 to 0.95 in the c.m. range above W = 1490 MeV and up to 3500 MeV. These new GlueX data will greatly constrain partial-wave analyses and reduce model-dependent uncertainties in the extraction of strange resonance properties (including pole positions), and provide a new benchmark for comparisons with QCD-inspired models and lattice QCD calculations. The proposed facility will also have an impact in the strange meson sector by providing measurements of the final-state Kπ system from threshold up to 2 GeV invariant mass to establish and improve on the pole positions and widths of all K*(Kπ) P-wave states as well as for the S-wave scalar meson κ(800).

## Key claim
The authors propose that a secondary KL beam with a flux of approximately 3 × 10^4 KL/s, used with the GlueX detector at Jefferson Lab Hall D, can improve statistics of previous hydrogen-target hyperon data by three orders of magnitude and provide first measurements on neutron targets, but this improvement is contingent on completing the current GlueX physics program first and requires a coupled-channel partial-wave analysis alongside J-PARC K- data when available.

## Method
The experiment will generate a secondary KL beam by directing a high-energy, high-intensity photon beam onto a beryllium target upstream of the GlueX detector. The KL beam will then scatter on liquid hydrogen or liquid deuterium cryotargets inside the GlueX detector, which has large acceptance for both charged and neutral particles. The program will measure differential cross sections and self-analyzed polarizations of Λ, Σ, Ξ, and Ω hyperons in two-body reactions (e.g., KL p → KS p, KL p → π+ Λ, KL p → K+ Ξ0) and three-body reactions (e.g., KL p → K+ K+ Ω-), spanning center-of-mass cosθ from -0.95 to 0.95 and W from 1490 MeV to 3500 MeV.

## Key numbers
- KL beam flux: 3 × 10^4 KL/s on target
- Statistics improvement: three orders of magnitude over previous hydrogen-target data
- Center-of-mass energy range: W = 1490 MeV up to 3500 MeV
- Center-of-mass angular coverage: cosθ from -0.95 to 0.95
- Strange meson invariant mass range: Kπ system from threshold up to 2 GeV
- Hyperon resonance spectroscopy target: up to 2400 MeV in Λ, Σ, Ξ, and Ω spectra
- PDG2016 baryon count: 109 baryons listed, with only 58 rated 4-star or 3-star
- Predicted states: constituent quark models predict no fewer than 64 N* and 22 Δ* states with mass less than 3 GeV
- Missing states to complete SU(3)F multiplets: 17 Λ*, 41 Σ*, 41 Ξ*, and 24 Ω*
- Λ(1405)-Λ(1520) doublet splitting: 115 MeV
- Ratio of π-decay widths for decuplets Δ(1232):Σ(1385):Ξ(1530) = 13:4:1
- Ratio of 1/2- to 3/2- splittings in Λ, Λc, Λb doublets: 8.2:2.1:1
- N(1535) ηN decay branch: 32-52%
- Λ(1670) ηΛ decay branch: 10-25%

## Context
This proposal extends the hyperon spectroscopy program from 1970s bubble chamber kaon beam experiments (PDG 2016 compilation) and recent CLAS photoproduction measurements (Moriya 2013) by providing three orders of magnitude higher statistics with a KL beam. It is complementary to the CLAS12 baryon spectroscopy experiments (VSP, KY programs) and will operate in Hall D at Jefferson Lab after the current GlueX physics program. The measurements will enable coupled-channel partial-wave analyses to determine hyperon resonance pole positions, providing benchmarks for lattice QCD calculations (Edwards 2013, Kamleh 2016) and QCD-inspired models, and will also constrain the Kπ vector and scalar form factors for CKM matrix element Vus determination and CP violation studies in B and D meson decays.
