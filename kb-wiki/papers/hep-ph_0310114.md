# Strongly Coupled Plasmas in High-Energy Physics

> One of the main activities in high-energy and nuclear physics is the search for the so-called quark-gluon plasma, a new state of matter which should have existed a few microseconds after the Big Bang. A quark-gluon plasma consists of free color charges, i.e. quarks and gluons, interacting by the strong (instead of electromagnetic) force. Theoretical considerations predict that the critical temperature for the phase transition from nuclear matter to a quark-gluon plasma is about 150 - 200 MeV. In the laboratory such a temperature can be reached in a so-called relativistic heavy-ion collision in accelerator experiments. Using the color charge instead of the electric charge, the Coulomb coupling parameter of such a system is of the order 10 - 30. Hence the quark-gluon plasma is a strongly coupled, relativistic plasma, in which also quantum effects are important. In the present work the experimental and theoretical status of the quark-gluon plasma physics will be reviewed, emphasizing the similarities and differences with usual plasma physics. Furthermore, the mixed phase consisting of free quarks and gluons together with hadrons (e.g. pions) will be discussed, which can be regarded as a complex plasma due to the finite extent of the hadrons.

# Strongly Coupled Plasmas in High-Energy Physics

## Key claim
The quark-gluon plasma (QGP) created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is a strongly coupled, relativistic plasma with a Coulomb coupling parameter of order 10–30, but its unambiguous detection remains elusive because no single signature has been proposed that can uniquely identify it; identification will likely require circumstantial evidence from a combination of all possible signatures.

## Method
The paper reviews the theoretical and experimental status of QGP physics, comparing it to conventional plasma physics. It derives the dielectric tensor for the QGP from the semiclassical Vlasov equation in the high-temperature limit, obtaining longitudinal and transverse dielectric functions that depend on an effective gluon mass. It also generalizes kinetic theory of thermophoretic forces to relativistic plasmas to model hadron flow in a hypothetical mixed phase of QGP and hadrons.

## Key numbers
- Critical temperature for the deconfinement phase transition: 150–200 MeV (1.8–2.4 × 10¹² K)
- Critical baryon density: about 10 times normal nuclear matter density, ρ₀ = 0.125 GeV/fm³ (2.2 × 10¹⁷ kg/m³)
- Coulomb coupling parameter Γ for the QGP at achievable temperatures: 10–30
- Strong coupling constant g at T ≈ 200 MeV: 1.5–2.5 in natural units
- Maximum temperature predicted at RHIC: T_max > 230 MeV
- Maximum fireball volume: about 3000 fm³, containing about 10000 partons
- QGP lifetime: 5–10 fm/c (1 fm/c = 3 × 10⁻²⁴ s)
- Pre-equilibrium phase duration: about 1 fm/c
- Interparton distance at T = 200 MeV: Δ = 0.5 fm
- Thermophoretic flow velocity for pions in the mixed phase: 0.8 c
- Thermophoretic flow velocity for protons in the mixed phase: 0.4 c
- Typical observed flow velocities at SPS: 0.5 c

## Context
This review extends the analogy between QGP and conventional plasma physics, noting that non-abelian effects are negligible in the high-temperature limit so the results also apply to ultrarelativistic QED plasmas such as those in supernovae explosions (temperatures up to 30 MeV). The work builds on lattice QCD simulations by Karsch (2002) for determining critical temperature and phase transition order, and on perturbative resummation techniques by Thoma (2000) for dynamical quantities. The thermophoretic analysis extends the kinetic derivation of Rothermel (2002) to relativistic systems, and the resulting large flow velocities suggest either a continuous phase transition or absence of QGP at SPS energies. The paper enables future comparison of QGP signatures from RHIC and LHC experiments with theoretical predictions.
