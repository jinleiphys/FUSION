# Quark-hadron pasta phase in neutron stars: the role of medium-dependent surface and curvature tensions

> We investigate the properties of the hadron-quark mixed phase, often termed the \textit{pasta} phase, expected to exist in the cores of massive neutron stars. To construct the equations of state (EoS), we combine an analytical representation based on the APR EoS for hadronic matter with the MIT bag model featuring vector interactions for quark matter. For modeling the mixed phase, we utilize the compressible liquid drop model that consistently accounts for finite-size and Coulomb effects. Unlike most previous analyses that treated surface tension as a constant free parameter and neglected curvature tension, we employ microphysical calculations using the multiple reflection expansion formalism to determine these parameters, while also ensuring their self-consistency with the EoS. We construct an extensive set of mixed hybrid EoSs by varying model parameters, solve the stellar structure equations to obtain neutron star mass-radius relationships, and select the models that satisfy current astrophysical constraints. Our findings closely align with calculations using a constant surface tension in terms of EoS stiffness and resulting stellar structure. However, they reveal significant differences in the types of geometric structures and their prevalence ranges within the mixed phase. Specifically, curvature effects enhance the emergence of tubes and bubbles at high densities despite the large value of surface tension, while suppressing the existence of drops and rods at low densities.

# Quark-hadron pasta phase in neutron stars: the role of medium-dependent surface and curvature tensions

## Key claim

The authors show that using microphysically calculated, density-dependent surface and curvature tensions (rather than constant free parameters) does not significantly change the stiffness of the equation of state or the resulting neutron star mass-radius relations, but it does substantially alter which geometric pasta structures appear and at what densities they exist, specifically, curvature effects enhance tubes and bubbles at high densities while suppressing drops and rods at low densities.

## Method

The authors combine an analytic representation of the APR equation of state for hadronic matter with the MIT bag model including vector interactions for quark matter. They model the hadron-quark mixed phase using the compressible liquid drop model within the Wigner-Seitz approximation, incorporating surface, curvature, and Coulomb energy contributions. Unlike previous work that treated surface tension as a constant free parameter, they employ the multiple reflection expansion formalism to compute medium-dependent surface and curvature tensions self-consistently with the equation of state, then solve the stellar structure equations to obtain mass-radius relations and select models satisfying current astrophysical constraints.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear incompressibility values considered: K0 = 258 MeV (δ = 0.1), K0 = 247 MeV (δ = 0.15), and K0 = 237 MeV (δ = 0.2), all within the experimental range K0 = 240 ± 20 MeV
- Symmetry energy parameters: S0 = 32 MeV and L0 = 58 MeV at saturation (using ζ = 0.6)
- Nuclear saturation density: n0 = 0.16 fm^-3
- Quark masses used: mu = 2.16 MeV, md = 4.67 MeV, ms = 93.4 MeV
- Vector meson mass: mV = 780 MeV
- Maximum vector coupling considered: GV = 0.3 fm^2
- Speed of sound in quark matter ranges between (cs/c)^2 = 1/3 (for GV = 0) and (cs/c)^2 ≈ 0.6 (for GV = 0.3 fm^2 at nB/n0 ~ 11)
- The hadronic EoS becomes superluminal at densities ~11 n0 for δ = 0.10; for δ = 0.15 and 0.2 the EoS remains subluminal at all relevant densities

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the quark-hadron pasta phase that treated surface tension as a constant free parameter and neglected curvature tension entirely, such as those by Yasutake (2014), Maslov (2018), Wu (2019), and Ju (2021). The authors build on microphysical calculations of surface and curvature tensions using the multiple reflection expansion formalism from Lugones (2013, 2020, 2021). The results enable more realistic modeling of the mixed phase in neutron star cores, though the authors note that the qualitative conclusions about the role of density-dependent surface and curvature tensions are robust despite uncertainties in the specific equation of state models used.
