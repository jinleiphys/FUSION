# Nuclear Physics under the low-energy, high intensity frontier

> Despite numerous achievements and recent progress, nuclear physics is often (wrongly) considered an old field of research nowadays. However, developments in theoretical frameworks and reliable experimental techniques have made the field mature enough to explore many new frontiers. In this regard, extending existing knowledge to an emerging field of physics -- where particles interact with a relatively low-energy but high intensity field (intense enough so that multi-particle processes become comparable or more important than one-to-one processes) -- can lead to exciting discoveries. Investigations can be realized under a highly time-compressed beam source (e.g., particle sources generated by laser-matter interaction using high-power laser systems). Here we focus on a new scheme, where high-power laser systems are exploited as a driver to generate energetic ($γ$-ray) photons. Together with additional low-energy photons provided by a second, less intense laser, a multi-photon absorption scheme enables a very attainable manipulation of nuclear transitions including isomer pumping and depletion.

## Key claim

The paper argues that multi-photon absorption processes in nuclei, which are normally suppressed as higher-order quantum effects, can become dominant over single-photon processes when a sufficiently intense secondary laser provides optical photons, enabling a "very attainable manipulation of nuclear transitions including isomer pumping and depletion." The key hedge is that this enhancement requires both a high-power laser system to generate gamma-ray photons and a second laser supplying optical photons at intensities around 10^22 W/cm^2, which is at the frontier of current capabilities.

## Method

The authors propose a scheme where a petawatt-class laser (HPLS1) generates a continuous spectrum of gamma-ray photons (10 keV to 10 MeV) via laser-plasma interaction, while a second laser (HPLS2) supplies optical (omega) photons at approximately 1 eV. The multi-photon absorption yield is calculated using the Goppert-Mayer formula for two-photon absorption, where the yield depends on the product of the intensities of both photon categories. For a concrete demonstration, the authors calculate the two-photon absorption cross section for the 77.389 keV isomer state of 113Sn, using experimentally known transition rates and Weisskopf estimates for the optical photon transition.

## Key numbers

- The effective two-photon absorption cross section for the 113Sn isomer transition is calculated as sigma_eff^(2PA) ≈ 5.2 × 10^(-48) * P_omega cm^2, where P_omega is the laser power density in W/cm^2.
- With a petawatt-class laser supplying P_omega ≈ 10^22 W/cm^2, the effective cross section becomes sigma_eff^(2PP) ≈ 10^(-25) cm^2 (0.1 barn).
- The gamma-ray flash from laser-plasma interaction has a duration of approximately 15-50 femtoseconds and occupies an area of about 100 μm^2.
- The 113Sn isomer state at 77.389 keV has a half-life of 21.4 minutes, while the target 3/2+ state at 1013.94 keV has a half-life of 0.2 picoseconds.
- A 10^10-fold improvement is predicted against all previous proposals in isomer pumping/depletion for isomers separated from their ground state within E4 transitions.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of multi-photon absorption, first developed by Goppert-Mayer (1931) for atomic systems and later applied to nuclear transitions by Refs. [PhysRevLett.42.1397, PhysRevC.20.1942], to the regime of high-power laser systems. The paper builds on recent particle-in-cell simulations showing that petawatt-class lasers can generate intense gamma-ray flashes via laser-plasma interaction (PhysRevLett.108.165006, 2012; Ji et al., 2014). The scheme enables experimental investigation of nonlinear nuclear photonics, including isomer pumping and depletion, and potentially the realization of a gamma-ray laser (graser) that circumvents the "graser dilemma" from Einstein's detailed balancing argument (1916).
