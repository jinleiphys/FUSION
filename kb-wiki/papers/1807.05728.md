# Applications of deep learning to relativistic hydrodynamics

> In this proceeding, we will briefly review our recent progress on implementing deep learning to relativistic hydrodynamics. We will demonstrate that a successfully designed and trained deep neural network, called {\tt stacked U-net}, can capture the main features of the non-linear evolution of hydrodynamics, which could also rapidly predict the final profiles for various testing initial conditions.

## Key claim

A deep neural network called "stacked U-net" can capture the main features of the non-linear evolution of relativistic hydrodynamics and rapidly predict final profiles for various testing initial conditions, but prediction accuracy decreases with longer evolution time and accumulates errors when multiple networks are chained together.

## Method

The authors generated training and testing data from (2+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamic simulations using the VISH2+1 code with an ideal equation of state p = e/3 and zero viscosity. They trained three individual stacked U-net neural networks, each covering a 2.0 fm/c time interval, to predict the energy-momentum tensor profiles T^{ττ}, T^{τx}, and T^{τy} at evolution times τ-τ₀ = 2.0, 4.0, and 6.0 fm/c (with τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c). The networks were trained on 10000 initial and final profiles from MC-Glauber initial conditions and then tested on profiles from MC-Glauber, MC-KLN, AMPT, and TRENTo initial conditions.

## Key numbers

- Training used 10000 initial and final profiles from VISH2+1 simulations with MC-Glauber initial conditions
- Grid size: dx = dy = 0.1 fm within a fixed transverse area of 13 fm × 13 fm
- Time step: dτ = 0.04 fm/c
- Three evolution times: τ-τ₀ = 2.0, 4.0, 6.0 fm/c (τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c)
- Batch size: 16; learning rate exponentially decaying from 10⁻³ to 10⁻⁵
- Kernel size of all layers: 3 × 3
- Tested on 100000 initial profiles for eccentricity distribution calculations

## Context

This work extends the application of deep learning techniques to relativistic hydrodynamics for heavy ion collisions, following recent successes in applying neural networks to other physics problems such as gravitational lens searches (Hezaveh 2017), phase identification in the Ising model (Carrasquilla 2017), and jet structure classification (Komiske 2016). The approach enables rapid prediction of hydrodynamic evolution without solving the full non-linear transport equations, though the authors note that prediction accuracy degrades at longer evolution times and that further improvements require new network structures and more powerful GPU servers.
