# Exploration of Nuclear Matter Properties and Related Thermodynamical Aspects

> In this study, my main goal is to examine the nuclear matter properties across a wide range of conditions, such as temperature, density, asymmetry, pressure, and magnetic field. Understanding the effect of these factors on nuclear matter is essential, given their relevance in various phenomena such as heavy-ion collisions, neutron stars, and supernovae. However, due to the absence of a fundamental nuclear theory, we must rely on models to describe the nuclear matter. Predicted properties like neutron star mass-radius relationships, tidal deformability, structure, critical points in the nuclear matter phase diagram etc. depend on the chosen model. This dependence arises because key parameters characterizing any nuclear model or equation of state (EoS) are not precisely known. Therefore, it is crucial to investigate how nuclear matter properties behave under various conditions and in relation to different EoS parameters. To accomplish this, I have examined three distinct forms of nuclear matter: infinite nuclear matter, finite nuclei, and neutron stars, using the effective relativistic mean field model (E-RMF).

## Key claim

The paper argues that nuclear matter properties such as neutron star mass-radius relationships, tidal deformability, and critical points in the nuclear matter phase diagram are model-dependent because key parameters characterizing any nuclear model or equation of state (EoS) are not precisely known; therefore, investigating how these properties behave under various conditions and in relation to different EoS parameters is crucial, using the effective relativistic mean field model (E-RMF) across three distinct forms of nuclear matter: infinite nuclear matter, finite nuclei, and neutron stars.

## Method

The author employs the effective relativistic mean field (E-RMF) model to examine nuclear matter properties across a wide range of conditions including temperature, density, asymmetry, pressure, and magnetic field. The study covers three distinct forms of nuclear matter: infinite nuclear matter, finite nuclei, and neutron stars. For neutron star structure, the analysis incorporates the cold catalyzed matter hypothesis and considers the outer crust using the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland (BPS) variational formalism with the single-nucleus approximation (SNA), while the inner crust is treated using the compressible liquid drop model (CLDM) and the core is described using nuclear many-body theories under beta-equilibrium.

## Key numbers

- The density of the neutron star core reaches 8-10 times the nuclear saturation density
- The limiting temperature T_l for multi-fragmentation was found to be approximately 5.6 MeV for the mass region A ≈ 90 in experiments using the ALADIN forward spectrometer at GSI
- The shell melting temperature T_m is approximately 1-2 MeV
- The critical temperature T_c of infinite symmetric nuclear matter from ab-initio calculations is T_c = 15.80^{+0.32}_{-1.60} MeV (Lu, 2020)
- The crossover temperature at zero chemical potential from LQCD calculations is T_c(μ_b=0) = 154 ± 9 MeV (Bazavov, 2017)
- The critical point is not expected to occur for μ_b/T ≤ 2 and T/T_c(μ_b=0) ≥ 2 (Bazavov, 2014)
- The highest measured pulsar spin frequency is 716 Hz
- The transition from crust to homogeneous core occurs at densities above 0.1-0.12 fm^{-3}
- The nuclear saturation density ρ_0 is approximately 0.16 fm^{-3}
- Magnetars possess magnetic fields ≥ 10^{15} G, while pulsars have magnetic fields in the range 10^{12}-10^{14} G
- The Chandrashekar mass limit for core collapse is 1.44 M_⊙
- Neutron stars form from progenitor stars with mass 8-20 M_⊙
- The protoneutron star cools to catalyzed ground state at approximately 10^8 K

## Context

This work extends the long line of nuclear matter investigations that began with the discovery of the neutron by Chadwick (1932) and the hypothesis of neutron stars by Baade and Zwicky (1934). It builds upon prior theoretical frameworks including the Skyrme effective NN interaction (PhysRevC.44.2505, PhysRevC.69.014602), the microscopic EoS of Friedman and Pandharipande, the relativistic mean-field framework (Malheiro, 1998), and ab-initio calculations using self-consistent Green's functions and Monte Carlo methods (Holt, 2013; Lu, 2020). The study is motivated by recent observational advances including the first detection of gravitational waves by LIGO and Virgo (Abbott, 2016, 2017, 2018) and data from NICER (Miller, 2021, 2019), and aims to enable more stringent constraints on the nuclear EoS, a more exact picture of neutron star interiors, and the development of unified EoS to describe nuclear matter across all regimes.
