# Revisiting the stability of strange-dwarf stars and strange planets

> The dynamical stability of strange-dwarf hybrid stars and strange planets, constituted by strange-quark-matter cores and dilute-nuclear-matter crusts, is revisited by analyzing the fundamental mode eigenfrequencies of the radial oscillation equations with boundary conditions for slow (rapid) conversions originating at the density-discontinuous interface characterizing extremely large (small) microscopic timescales compared to the oscillation periods. For the hadronic crust we used an analytic fit of the BPS results matched to the massless MIT bag model. For the rapid case, our calculations indicate that the zero mode is the so-called {\it reaction mode} whose frequency is a complex number, thus ruling out the existence of strange dwarfs (planets) in nature. On the other hand, slow conversions still provide a sizeable stability window which, interestingly, also reproduces the Glendenning-Kettner-Weber results. The robustness of our findings is demonstrated for different transition densities and using an equation of state from perturbative QCD for the ultra-dense core.

## Key claim

The dynamical stability of strange-dwarf stars and strange planets depends critically on the speed of microscopic phase conversions at the quark-hadron interface: rapid conversions produce a complex-frequency reaction mode that rules out these objects in nature, while slow conversions still provide a sizeable stability window that reproduces the earlier Glendenning-Kettner-Weber results.

## Method

The authors solved the general-relativistic Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations for hydrostatic equilibrium and Gondek's first-order radial oscillation equations with boundary conditions for slow and rapid phase conversions at the density-discontinuous interface between a strange-quark-matter core and a dilute-nuclear-matter crust. The hadronic crust was modeled using an analytic fit of the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland (BPS) equation of state, while the quark core was described by the massless MIT bag model (bag constant B^{1/4} = 145 MeV) and, for robustness, by a perturbative QCD equation of state from Kurkela (2009) with renormalization scale X in [3,4]. Three values of the crust-core transition density were used: 10^9, 10^10, and 4x10^11 g/cm^3 (the neutron drip point).

## Key numbers

- The MIT bag model uses B^{1/4} = 145 MeV, which satisfies epsilon/n_B < 930 MeV at zero pressure for absolute stability of strange quark matter.
- Transition densities studied: 10^9, 10^10, and 4x10^11 g/cm^3, the last being the neutron drip point (with an implicit maximum of 8.3x10^10 g/cm^3 for mechanical stability).
- Strange dwarfs have masses in the range ~0.8 to ~0.1 solar masses; strange planets have masses from ~9x10^-2 down to ~10^-3 solar masses.
- The auxiliary function uses Omega = 6.6x10^-22 MeV as a constant for displaying squared eigenfrequencies.
- For the pQCD equation of state, the renormalization scale X is restricted to [3,4] to satisfy the strange-quark-matter hypothesis.

## Context

This work resolves a long-standing disagreement between Glendenning (1994), who found strange dwarfs stable, and Alford (2017), who found them unstable, by showing that the discrepancy arises from implicit assumptions about phase conversion speed: Glendenning (1994) effectively assumed slow conversions (continuous Lagrangian variables), while Alford (2017) used a smoothed equation of state that effectively corresponds to rapid conversions. The analysis extends the formalism of Haensel (1989) and Pereira (2017) for radial oscillations with phase-splitting boundary conditions to the strange-dwarf regime, where the reaction mode becomes the fundamental mode and determines stability. The results enable future work on whether strange dwarfs could exist as a population explaining white-dwarf observations reported by Kurban (2022) that do not match standard white-dwarf masses and radii.
