# Pre-scission neutron multiplicity associated with the dynamical process in superheavy mass region

> The fusion-fission process accompanied by neutron emission is studied in the superheavy-mass region on the basis of the fluctuation-dissipation model combined with a statistical model. The calculation of the trajectory or the shape evolution in the deformation space of the nucleus with neutron emission is performed. Each process (quasi-fission, fusion-fission, and deep quasi-fission processes) has a characteristic travelling time from the point of contact of colliding nuclei to the scission point. These dynamical aspects of the whole process are discussed in terms of the pre-scission neutron multiplicity, which depends on the time spent on each process. We have presented the details of the characteristics of our model calculation in the reactions $^{48}$Ca+$^{208}$Pb and $^{48}$Ca+$^{244}$Pu, and shown how the structure of the distribution of pre-scission neutron multiplicity depends on the incident energy.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in the superheavy-mass region at low incident energy (excitation energy E* = 50 MeV), the pre-scission neutron multiplicity does not show a clear characteristic structure corresponding to each reaction process (quasi-fission, deep quasi-fission, fusion-fission) when using the standard classification based solely on fission fragment mass; instead, a classification method that adds a condition on the nuclear shape coordinate z (whether the trajectory enters the region z < 0.4, indicating mono-nuclear shape) is needed to distinguish the processes.

## Method

The authors combined a three-dimensional Langevin equation (using two-center parameterization with coordinates z0, delta, alpha) with a statistical model for neutron evaporation to simulate the fusion-fission dynamics in the reactions 48Ca+208Pb and 48Ca+244Pu at excitation energy E* = 50 MeV for angular momentum l = 0. They calculated 1,000 trajectories for 48Ca+208Pb and 10,000 trajectories for 48Ca+244Pu, each starting from the point of contact, and classified trajectories into quasi-fission (QF), deep quasi-fission (DQF), and fusion-fission (FF) processes using both the standard mass-based classification and a new classification that adds a condition on whether the trajectory enters the region z < 0.4.

## Key numbers

- In 48Ca+208Pb at E* = 50 MeV for l = 0: FF trajectories make up 96% of all trajectories, and 95% of FF trajectories escape from the spherical region within 5.0 x 10^-19 sec.
- In 48Ca+208Pb at E* = 50 MeV for l = 0: the average travelling time of the FF process is 15.3 x 10^-20 sec, with a peak at 1.2 x 10^-20 sec.
- In 48Ca+244Pu at E* = 50 MeV for l = 0, using standard classification: QF process accounts for 72%, DQF for 25%, and FF for 3% of 10,000 trajectories.
- In 48Ca+244Pu at E* = 50 MeV for l = 0: average travelling times are 1.11 x 10^-20 sec (QF), 1.73 x 10^-20 sec (DQF), and 4.83 x 10^-20 sec (FF).
- In 48Ca+244Pu at E* = 50 MeV for l = 0: the peak of travelling time for the QF process is at approximately 7.5 x 10^-21 sec.
- In 48Ca+244Pu at E* = 50 MeV for l = 0: the maximum number of evaporated neutrons is approximately 4 to 5.
- In 48Ca+244Pu at E* = 50 MeV for l = 0: the MSQF (mass symmetric quasi-fission) process occupies 1.21% of all trajectories and 4.55% of mass symmetric fission events.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous dynamical model (Aritomo 2004) that used Langevin calculations to classify fusion-fission paths in the superheavy-mass region, by adding neutron emission to the calculation. The study was motivated by experimental work from the DeMoN group (Donangelo 1999) and measurements by Itkis (2001) and Mate (2004) suggesting that pre-scission neutron multiplicity could help distinguish reaction processes. The paper shows that at low excitation energy (E* = 50 MeV), the standard classification by fission fragment mass is insufficient to separate QF, DQF, and FF processes, and proposes a new classification based on the trajectory's penetration into the small-z region (z < 0.4). This work enables more precise estimation of fusion probability in hot fusion reactions used for superheavy element synthesis, particularly those performed at FLNR (Oganessian 2004).
