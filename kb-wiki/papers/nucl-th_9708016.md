# What QCD sum rules tell about the rho meson

> Using a simple parametrization of Breit-Wigner type for the hadronic side of the QCD sum rule for $ρ$ mesons in vacuum as well as in a nuclear medium we explore the range of values for the mass and the width of the $ρ$ meson which are compatible with the operator product expansion.

## Key claim

Using a simple Breit-Wigner parametrization for the rho meson spectral function, the authors show that QCD sum rules do not uniquely determine the rho meson mass and width in vacuum or nuclear matter; instead, a range of parameter pairs (mass and width) are compatible with the operator product expansion, and the predictive power of the sum rule decreases as the width increases.

## Method

The authors parametrize the rho meson spectral function as a Breit-Wigner with an energy-dependent width that respects the correct threshold behavior (two-pion threshold in vacuum, one-pion threshold in medium). They treat the pole mass m_rho and on-shell width gamma as free parameters, then compute the relative deviation between the left-hand side and right-hand side of the Borel-transformed QCD sum rule (including condensates up to dimension 6) averaged over a Borel window. The continuum threshold s_0 is fixed by a finite energy sum rule, and the residue F is determined accordingly. Parameter pairs are considered acceptable if the average deviation d is below 0.2% or 1% and the Borel window width Delta M^2 exceeds 0.9 GeV^2 (vacuum) or 0.6 GeV^2 (saturation density).

## Key numbers

- For vacuum with kappa = 2.36, the experimental values m_rho = 0.77 GeV and gamma = 0.15 GeV lie inside the parameter range with d <= 0.2%.
- For vacuum with kappa = 2.36, the optimal parameter pair (minimal d) is m_rho = 0.71 GeV, gamma = 0.01 GeV, with s_0 = 1.1 GeV^2, F = 0.008 GeV^4, and d = 0.0011.
- For nuclear saturation density with kappa = 2.36, the optimal parameter pair is m_rho = 0.66 GeV, gamma = 0.22 GeV, with s_0 = 0.88 GeV^2, F = 0.006 GeV^4, and d = 0.00085.
- For nuclear saturation density with kappa = 2.36 and vanishing width (gamma = 0.01 GeV), the optimal mass is m_rho = 0.57 GeV, with s_0 = 0.78 GeV^2 and d = 0.0019.
- For kappa = 2.36 and d <= 1%, the allowed mass range for small width is about 0.1 GeV, while for large width it grows to 0.3-0.4 GeV.
- The Borel window for vacuum with vanishing width is 1.4 GeV^2; at nuclear saturation density it shrinks to 0.9 GeV^2.

## Context

This work extends the QCD sum rule analysis of Hatsuda, Lee, and Shiomi (1995) and Jin and Leinweber (1995), who used a delta-function spectral function and found a dropping rho mass in medium, and addresses the conflicting results of Asakawa and Ko (1993), who found a mass drop with a more sophisticated spectral function, versus Klingl, Kaiser, and Weise (1997), who found no mass shift but only broadening. The authors show that both scenarios can be compatible with QCD sum rules depending on the width, and that the sum rule approach loses predictive power for large widths. This enables future hadronic model builders to quantify agreement with QCD sum rules using the deviation d and Borel window criteria presented here.
