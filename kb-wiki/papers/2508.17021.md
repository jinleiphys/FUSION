# Baryon Electric Charge Correlation as QCD Magnetometer

> The detection of strong magnetic fields in peripheral heavy-ion collisions is crucial for observing effects such as the chiral magnetic effect but has proven exceptionally difficult. To address this, we propose the baryon electric charge correlation $χ^{\rm BQ}_{11}$ and the chemical potential ratio $μ_{\rm Q}/μ_{\rm B}$ as sensitive probes of magnetic fields, based on (2+1)-flavor lattice QCD simulations at the physical pion mass. Along the transition line, $χ^{\rm BQ}_{11}$ and $(μ_{\rm Q}/μ_{\rm B})_{\rm LO}$ in Pb-Pb collisions increase by factors of 2.1 and 2.4 at $eB \simeq 8M_π^2$, respectively. To bridge theoretical predictions with experimental observables, we implement systematic kinematic cuts that emulate detector acceptances of the STAR and ALICE experiments within the hadron resonance gas model. This allows us to construct experimentally relevant proxy observables. Furthermore, we demonstrate that $(μ_{\rm Q}/μ_{\rm B})_{\rm LO}$ is also sensitive to the collision system, showing a $1.5$-fold increase from Zr-Zr to Ru-Ru isobar collisions. Our findings offer new insights into thermo-magnetic effects and provide experimentally relevant guidance for the detection of magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions.

# Baryon Electric Charge Correlation as QCD Magnetometer

## Key claim

The baryon electric charge correlation χBQ₁₁ and the chemical potential ratio μQ/μB serve as sensitive probes of magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions, with enhancements of factors 2.1 and 2.4 respectively at eB ≃ 8Mπ² along the transition line, but these results depend on lattice QCD simulations at physical pion mass and the construction of proxy observables through the hadron resonance gas model with kinematic cuts emulating STAR and ALICE detector acceptances.

## Method

The authors performed (2+1)-flavor lattice QCD simulations with the HISQ action at physical pion mass on 32³×8 and 48³×12 lattices, computing second-order fluctuations and correlations of baryon number, electric charge, and strangeness in nonzero magnetic fields up to eB ≃ 8Mπ². They then constructed experimentally accessible proxy observables within the hadron resonance gas model by applying systematic kinematic cuts on transverse momentum and pseudo-rapidity that emulate the detector acceptances of the STAR and ALICE experiments, and defined central-peripheral (Rcp-like) double ratios to isolate magnetic field effects while mitigating volume-dependent effects.

## Key numbers

- χBQ₁₁/χQ₂ shows enhancement of approximately 2 at eB ≃ 8Mπ² along the transition line (lattice continuum estimate)
- χBQ₁₁/χQS₁₁ shows enhancement of approximately 2.25 at eB ≃ 8Mπ² along the transition line (lattice continuum estimate)
- (μQ/μB)LO in Pb-Pb collisions increases by a factor of 2.4 at eB ≃ 8Mπ² along the transition line
- (μQ/μB)LO shows a 1.5-fold increase from Zr-Zr to Ru-Ru isobar collisions at eB ≃ 8Mπ²
- The Ru system reaches approximately 4 for R((μQ/μB)LO) at eB ≃ 8Mπ², compared to approximately 2.4 for Pb/Au systems
- HRG-based proxies with kinematic cuts retain at least 80% of the magnetic sensitivity predicted by lattice QCD
- Kinematic cuts produce enhancements up to 25% at eB ≃ 8Mπ² for χBQ₁₁/χQ₂ and up to 60% for χBQ₁₁/χQS₁₁

## Context

This work extends earlier lattice QCD studies of conserved charge fluctuations in magnetic fields that used larger-than-physical pion mass (Mπ ≃ 220 MeV) at a single lattice spacing (Ding 2021) to physical pion mass calculations (Ding 2023, Ding 2025). It provides first-principles benchmarks that complement effective model studies (Adhikari 2024) and connects to ongoing experimental efforts by the ALICE collaboration, which has already reported centrality-dependent enhancements in χBQ₁₁/χQ₂ (ALICE 2025) that are qualitatively consistent with these theoretical predictions. The results enable experimental searches for magnetic field signatures in heavy-ion collisions through accessible fluctuation observables, with the ratio χBQ₁₁/χQS₁₁ predicted to be significantly more sensitive than previously measured observables.
