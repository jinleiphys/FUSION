# Fission of super-heavy elements: $^{132}$Sn-plus-the-rest, or $^{208}$Pb-plus-the-rest ?

> In this work we try to settle down the controversial predictions on the effect of doubly magic nuclei $^{132}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb on the mass distributions of fission fragments of super-heavy nuclei. For this we have calculated the mass distribution of super-heavy nuclei from $^{286}$Cn to $^{306}$122 within the dynamical 4-dimensional Langevin approach. We have found that in "light" super-heavies the influence of $^{208}$Pb on the mass distributions is present but negligible small. In "heavy" super-heavies, Z=120-122, the (quasi)symmetric peaks and strongly asymmetric peaks at fragment mass $A_F$ close to $A_F$=208 are of comparable magnitude.

## Key claim
The paper claims that both doubly magic nuclei $^{132}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb influence the fission fragment mass distributions of super-heavy elements, but the relative importance depends on the compound nucleus: in "light" super-heavies the $^{208}$Pb influence is negligible, while in "heavy" super-heavies (Z=120-122) the asymmetric peak near $A_F=208$ becomes comparable in magnitude to the symmetric and $^{132}$Sn-related peaks.

## Method
The authors performed four-dimensional Langevin dynamical calculations for super-heavy nuclei from $^{274}$Hs to $^{306}$122, using a two-center shell model shape parametrization with four deformation parameters (elongation, two fragment deformations, and mass asymmetry). The free energy was computed as the sum of liquid-drop deformation energy and temperature-dependent shell correction from a deformed Woods-Saxon potential, with collective inertia from the Werner-Wheeler approximation and friction from the wall-and-window formula. Trajectories were evolved from the ground state until the neck radius reached 1 fm (scission point).

## Key numbers
- For $^{286}$Cn at excitation energy $E_x=10$ MeV, the local minima corresponding to $A_F=208$ and its paired fragment $A_F=78$ are 20 MeV higher than the minimum around $A_F=132-154$.
- For $^{306}$122 at $E_x=10$ MeV, the difference between almost symmetric and strongly mass asymmetric minima is only 14 MeV.
- The $Z/A$ ratio for $^{132}$Sn is 0.379, while for $^{208}$Pb it is 0.394; the ratios for $^{286}$Cn and $^{306}$122 are 0.392 and 0.397 respectively.
- At $E_x=30$ MeV, shell effects are washed out and all nuclei from $^{274}$Hs to $^{306}$122 undergo symmetric fission.
- For $^{306}$122, the symmetric and mass asymmetric peaks (near $A_F=208$) are of the same magnitude.

## Context
This work resolves a controversy between predictions that fission of super-heavy elements would be dominated by $^{132}$Sn-like fragments (as seen in experiments by the Itkis group (2000s) and scission-point model calculations) versus predictions that $^{208}$Pb-like fragments would be the main fission mode (as in publications by other groups (2010s)). The authors note that in hot-fusion experiments, the $A=208$ peak is typically assigned to quasi-fission, but their calculations suggest a small fusion-fission component could be identified if prescission neutron emission cools the residue to excitation energies around 10 MeV. The results enable experimental searches for this superasymmetric fusion-fission component using coincidence measurements of prescission neutrons and fission fragments.
