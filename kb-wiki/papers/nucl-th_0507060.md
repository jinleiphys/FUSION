# Few-body physics in effective field theory

> Effective Field Theory (EFT) provides a powerful framework that exploits a separation of scales in physical systems to perform systematically improvable, model-independent calculations. Particularly interesting are few-body systems with short-range interactions and large two-body scattering length. Such systems display remarkable universal features. In systems with more than two particles, a three-body force with limit cycle behavior is required for consistent renormalization already at leading order. We will review this EFT and some of its applications in the physics of cold atoms and nuclear physics. In particular, we will discuss the possibility of an infrared limit cycle in QCD. Recent extensions of the EFT approach to the four-body system and N-boson droplets in two spatial dimensions will also be addressed.

# Few-body physics in effective field theory

## Key claim

Effective Field Theory (EFT) provides a systematically improvable, model-independent framework for few-body systems with short-range interactions and large two-body scattering length, but the three-body system requires a three-body force with limit cycle behavior for consistent renormalization already at leading order, introducing a three-body parameter that cannot be predicted from two-body data alone.

## Method

The author reviews the EFT for non-relativistic bosons with large S-wave scattering length, starting from a Lagrangian with contact interactions and summing bubble diagrams to obtain the exact two-body amplitude. For the three-body system, an integral equation for the atom-dimer scattering amplitude is solved after S-wave projection, introducing an ultraviolet cutoff and a three-body force term whose coupling runs on a limit cycle. The approach is extended to four-body systems using effective quantum mechanics based on Yakubovsky equations, and to N-boson droplets in two spatial dimensions using variational methods with a running coupling constant.

## Key numbers

- The limit cycle period: when the cutoff Lambda is increased by a factor of exp(pi/s_0) approximately 22.7, the three-body coupling H(Lambda) returns to its original value, where s_0 = 1.00624 is a transcendental number.
- For N-boson droplets in 2D at large N: R_{N+1}/R_N approximately 0.3417 and B_{N+1}/B_N approximately 8.567.
- The three-body ground state energy in 2D for zero-range potential: B_3^{(0)} = 16.522688(1) B_2, with one excited state at B_3^{(1)} = 1.2704091(1) B_2.
- The four-body ground state energy in 2D for zero-range potential: B_4^{(0)} = 197.3(1) B_2, with one excited state at B_4^{(1)} = 25.5(1) B_2.
- The critical pion mass range where the triplet scattering length diverges: 170 MeV less than m_pi less than 210 MeV.
- The atom-dimer scattering length expression for a greater than 0: a_{12} = a (1.46 - 2.15 tan[s_0 ln(a Lambda_*) + 0.09]) (1 + O(l/a)).
- The critical number of bosons in 2D for universality: N_crit approximately 0.931 ln(R_2/l) + O(1).

## Context

This review extends the universal predictions for three-body systems first derived by Efimov (1971), confirming and generalizing the Efimov effect and scaling relations using EFT methods. The work builds on the pionless EFT approach to few-nucleon problems developed by Bedaque, Hammer, and van Kolck (1999), and extends it to four-body systems (Platter, 2004) and N-boson droplets in 2D (Hammer, 2004). The discussion of an infrared limit cycle in QCD follows the conjecture by Braaten, Hammer, and Meissner (2003) that tuning up and down quark masses could produce an exact Efimov effect in the triton system. The scaling functions relating three- and four-body observables enable model-independent tests of universality in atomic and nuclear systems, with the Tjon line correlation between triton and alpha-particle binding energies serving as a nuclear physics example.
