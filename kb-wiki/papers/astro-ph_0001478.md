# Conversion of neutron stars to strange stars as the central engine of gamma-ray bursts

> We study the conversion of a neutron star to a strange star as a possible energy source for gamma-ray bursts. We use different recent models for the equation of state of neutron star matter and strange quark matter. We show that the total amount of energy liberated in the conversion is in the range of (1-4) 10^{53} ergs (one order of magnitude larger than previous estimates) and is in agreement with the energy required to power gamma-ray burst sources at cosmological distances.

# Conversion of neutron stars to strange stars as the central engine of gamma-ray bursts

## Key claim

The total energy released in the conversion of a neutron star to a strange star is in the range (1–4) × 10^53 erg, which is one order of magnitude larger than previous estimates and sufficient to power gamma-ray bursts at cosmological distances, but only under the assumption that the baryonic mass of the compact object is conserved during the conversion and that the specific equations of state for neutron star matter and strange quark matter are chosen appropriately.

## Method

The authors computed the total conversion energy as the difference between the binding energies of the initial neutron star and the final strange star, assuming baryonic mass conservation. They solved the Oppenheimer–Volkoff structural equations for non-rotating compact objects using multiple combinations of equations of state: for neutron star matter they used the microscopic BBB1 equation of state (Baldo et al. 1997) based on the Argonne v14 nucleon-nucleon interaction with three-body forces, and a hyperonic matter equation of state from Prakash et al. (1997); for strange quark matter they used the MIT bag model with various bag constants and strange quark masses, as well as the density-dependent quark model of Dey et al. (1998) with two parameter choices (SS1 and SS2).

## Key numbers

- Total conversion energy range: (1–4) × 10^53 erg, one order of magnitude larger than previous estimates of ~10^52 erg from Olinto (1987) and Cheng and Dai (1996)
- For the BBB1→B60_0 conversion model with a neutron star of baryonic mass 1.574 M_sun: initial gravitational mass 1.409 M_sun, radius 11.0 km, gravitational binding energy 4.497 × 10^53 erg; final strange star gravitational mass 1.254 M_sun, radius 10.5 km, gravitational binding energy 3.061 × 10^53 erg
- The conversion energy is reduced by a factor of 2–3 when the strange quark mass is set to 200 MeV (B60_{200} model) compared to the massless case
- Maximum gravitational masses for strange stars: 1.60 M_sun (B90_0), 1.75 M_sun (B60_{200}), 1.44 M_sun (SS1), 1.33 M_sun (SS2)
- The conversion rate for accretion-induced conversion in binary systems is estimated at (3–30) × 10^{-10} conversions per day per galaxy, consistent with the observed GRB rate

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposal by Alcock et al. (1986) and Olinto (1987) that neutron star to strange star conversion could power gamma-ray bursts, and corrects previous energy estimates by Cheng and Dai (1996) and Ma and Xie (1996) which gave only ~10^52 erg. The authors use more recent and accurate equations of state for both neutron star matter and strange quark matter, including the microscopic BBB1 equation of state from Baldo et al. (1997) and the quark model of Dey et al. (1998). The results show that the conversion energy is compatible with the ~3 × 10^53 erg required for cosmological GRBs such as GRB 971214 at redshift 3.42, and the conversion timescale of 1 ms to 1 s from Horvath and Benvenuto (1988) matches typical GRB durations. Future work requires detailed simulations of the conversion process itself and the mechanism by which gamma-rays are produced.
