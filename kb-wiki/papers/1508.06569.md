# Nuclear Reactions For Nucleosynthesis Beyond Fe

> Many more nuclear transitions have to be known in the determination of stellar reactivities for trans-iron nucleosynthesis than for reactions of light nuclei. This requires different theoretical and experimental approaches. Some of the issues specific for trans-iron nucleosynthesis are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that determining stellar reaction rates for nucleosynthesis beyond iron requires knowledge of many more nuclear transitions than for light-element reactions, which necessitates different theoretical and experimental approaches; the abstract glosses over the specific condition that most measurements can only test models for single transitions and cannot independently constrain a stellar rate due to pronounced thermal plasma effects.

## Method

The paper discusses the theoretical framework for stellar reaction rates, emphasizing the Hauser-Feshbach model for compound-nucleus reactions, which is applicable when nuclear level densities are high. It explains how stellar cross sections depend on plasma temperature through the population of excited target states, and introduces the ground-state contribution \(X_0\) as a metric for quantifying the importance of excited-state transitions. The author also describes a systematic sensitivity study using the sensitivity parameter \(s\) to relate changes in input quantities (such as particle widths, gamma widths, and level densities) to changes in output reactivities.

## Key numbers

- Effective interaction energies for neutron captures range from 8–30 keV for the main s-process, up to 90 keV for the weak s-process component, and 80–120 keV for the r-process.
- For charged particle reactions, effective energies are shifted to a few MeV, up to approximately 9 MeV for alpha-reactions in the production of p-nuclei.
- The ground-state contribution \(X_0\) for astrophysically relevant photodisintegration rates is less than \(5 \times 10^{-5}\).
- The peak energy \(E_\gamma^\mathrm{peak}\) for gamma-transitions contributing to astrophysical neutron and charged particle captures is approximately 2–4 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of nuclear reaction rates for nucleosynthesis beyond iron, building on prior approaches such as the s-process and r-process models described in Advances (2011) and Kapgall (2008). It addresses the specific challenges of high plasma temperatures, high nuclear level densities, and high Coulomb barriers for heavy nuclei, and it enables improved guidance for experiments by providing systematic sensitivity studies and ground-state contributions to stellar rates, as published in Sensis (2011) and Xs (2012). The paper also highlights the need for better nuclear structure models and for combining direct, resonant, and Hauser-Feshbach reaction mechanisms near magic numbers and driplines.
