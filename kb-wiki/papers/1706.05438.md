# Numerical Simulation of the Hydrodynamical Combustion to Strange Quark Matter in the Trapped Neutrino Regime

> We simulate and study the microphysics of combustion (flame burning) of two flavored quark matter (u, d) to three flavoured quark matter (u,d,s) in a trapped neutrino regime applicable to conditions prevailing in a hot proto-neutron star. The reaction-diffusion-advection equations for (u,d) to (u,d,s) combustion are coupled with neutrino transport, which is modelled through a flux-limited diffusion scheme. The flame speed is proportional to initial lepton fraction because of the release of electron chemical potential as heat, and reaches a steady-state burning speed of (0.001-0.008)c. We find that the burning speed is ultimately driven by the neutrino pressure gradient, given that the pressure gradient induced by quarks is opposed by the pressure gradients induced by electrons. This suggests, somewhat counter-intuitively, that the pressure gradients that drive the interface are controlled primarily by leptonic weak decays rather than by the quark Equation of State (EOS). In other words, the effects of the leptonic weak interaction, including the corresponding weak decay rates and the EOS of electrons and neutrinos, are at least as important as the uncertainties related to the EOS of high density matter. We find that for baryon number densities nB <= 0.35 fm-3, strong pressure gradients induced by leptonic weak decays drastically slow down the burning speed, which is thereafter controlled by the much slower burning process driven by backflowing downstream matter. We discuss the implications of our findings to proto-neutron stars.

# Numerical Simulation of the Hydrodynamical Combustion to Strange Quark Matter in the Trapped Neutrino Regime

## Key claim
The burning speed of two-flavor (u,d) to three-flavor (u,d,s) quark matter in a proto-neutron star is ultimately driven by the neutrino pressure gradient rather than by the quark equation of state, because the pressure gradient induced by quarks is opposed by the pressure gradient induced by electrons. The effects of leptonic weak interactions, including weak decay rates and the equation of state of electrons and neutrinos, are at least as important as the uncertainties related to the high-density quark equation of state.

## Method
The authors numerically solved the one-dimensional reaction-diffusion-advection equations for (u,d) to (u,d,s) combustion coupled with neutrino transport modeled through a flux-limited diffusion scheme, using an updated version of the Burn-UD code. The system included leptonic weak interactions (electron capture on up quarks producing strange or down quarks plus electron neutrinos) and non-leptonic weak interactions (conversion of down to strange quarks), with neutrino transport treated implicitly while hydrodynamics was solved explicitly using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta algorithm. The simulation used 48000 zones on a 600 cm grid with a zone length of 0.0125 cm, for conditions typical of proto-neutron stars: initial temperature 20 MeV and lepton fraction Y_L >= 0.2.

## Key numbers
- Steady-state burning speed: (0.001-0.008)c, proportional to initial lepton fraction
- For baryon number densities n_B <= 0.35 fm^{-3}, strong pressure gradients from leptonic weak decays drastically slow down the burning speed
- The total pressure gradient is about four orders of magnitude smaller than the pressure gradient of quarks or leptons individually
- The non-leptonic weak interaction timescale tau ~ 10^{-8} s
- The reaction zone width is approximately 1 cm, compared to 10^{-5} to 10^{-4} cm predicted by a pure diffusion formula
- The semi-analytic model shows quenching solutions exist when Delta_mu_e >= 10 MeV (difference in electron chemical potential between upstream and downstream)
- Burning speeds are bounded by the lepton fraction to less than approximately 0.01c

## Context
This work extends the microphysical approach of Niebergal et al. (2010, Paper I) by including full neutrino transport beyond the leakage prescription used in that earlier study, and applies it to the trapped lepton-rich regime of hot proto-neutron stars. The results contradict the assumption that the quark equation of state dominates combustion dynamics, as previously assumed in studies such as Olinto (1987) and Furusawa et al. (2016). The finding that for n_B < 0.35 fm^{-3} the flame is halted suggests a contained quark core of a few kilometers in the proto-neutron star, though the core can grow slowly through backflow advection of fuel. The quenching mechanism differs from the Coll's condition invoked by Drago et al. (2015) and Herzog et al. (2011), as the authors argue that mechanical equilibrium does not apply to this combustion process. Future work requires global hydrodynamic simulations of the whole compact star to quantify the conversion timescale, and exploration of alternate quark equations of state including chiral symmetry breaking.
