# $β$-Decay Half-Lives Serve as Novel Evidence for the New Magic Number \(N=32\)

> Conventional signatures of nuclear magic number, including low-lying quadrupole collectivity and mass systematics, face significant challenges when probing emergent shell closures near the drip line. However, $β$-decay half-lives are among the first experimental observables measurable following the discovery of neutron-rich isotopes. This letter demonstrates that $β$-decay half-lives provide evidence for the emergent magic number $N=32$. The observed half-life pattern around the $N=32$ can be attributed to the occupation probabilities of orbitals above this shell gap, which directly reflect the gap's magnitude. Our results reveal a pronounced $N=32$ shell gap in Ca isotopes and a weaker yet apparent gap in K isotopes, consistent with mass and electromagnetic transition data. Furthermore, the analysis indicates no prominent closed-shell signature at $N=32$ in Ar and Cl isotopes.

## Key claim
Beta-decay half-lives can serve as a novel signature for the emergent magic number N=32, but the correlation is mediated by occupation probabilities of orbitals above the shell gap rather than by the gap itself; this mechanism differs from traditional magic numbers where such orbital occupation effects are absent.

## Method
The authors performed self-consistent proton-neutron quasi-particle random phase approximation (pnQRPA) calculations based on relativistic Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (RHFB) theory using the PKA1 energy density functional, which includes tensor coupling. They calculated Gamow-Teller transition strengths and beta-decay half-lives for Ca, K, Ar, and Cl isotopes with N=30, 32, and 34, and varied the isovector pairing strength to mimic changes in the shell gap magnitude.

## Key numbers
- Experimental half-lives show a gradual decrease from N=30 to N=32, followed by a more rapid decline from N=32 to N=34 for both Ca and K isotopes; the reduction is more pronounced in Ca than in K.
- For Ca isotopes, the dominant GT transition is ν1f5/2 → π1f7/2; the transition strength Bm(GT) drops from 0.64 in 50Ca to 0.10 in 52Ca, then rises to 0.58 in 54Ca.
- The beta-decay transition energy Em increases from 3.43 MeV in 50Ca to 6.73 MeV in 52Ca and 9.90 MeV in 54Ca.
- For Ca isotopes, the PKA1 interaction reproduces experimental half-lives with isovector pairing strength 0.995; for K isotopes, strength 1.02 is needed.
- For Ar isotopes, PKA1 reproduces experimental half-lives with pairing strength 1.04; for Cl isotopes, strength 1.04 reproduces N=30 and N=32 data.

## Context
This work extends studies of emergent shell closures near the drip line, where conventional signatures like low-lying quadrupole collectivity and mass systematics face challenges (Huck 1985, Gade 2006, Wienholtz 2013, Rosenbusch 2015). It contrasts with prior work by Yoshida (2019) on N=50 and by Zeng (2025) on Cl isotopes, where the N=32 shell closure was suggested primarily from shell model calculations rather than direct half-life patterns. The results enable using beta-decay half-life measurements as a complementary probe for testing magicity at N=32 in neutron-rich pf-shell nuclei, particularly for Ar and Cl isotopes where experimental data remain sparse.
