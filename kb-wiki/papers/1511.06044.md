# QCD Evolution of Superfast Quarks

> Recent high-precision measurements of nuclear deep inelastic scattering at high x and moderate 6 < Q$^2$ < 9GeV$^2$ give a rare opportunity to reach the quark distributions in the {\it superfast} region, in which the momentum fraction of the nucleon carried by its constituent quark is larger than the total fraction of the nucleon at rest, x>1. We derive the leading-order QCD evolution equation for such quarks with the goal of relating the moderate-Q$^2$ data to the two earlier measurements of superfast quark distributions at large 60 < Q$^2$ < 200~GeV$^2$. Since the high-Q$^2$ measurements gave strongly contradictory estimates of the nuclear effects that generate superfast quarks, relating them to the high-precision, moderate-Q$^2$ data through QCD evolution allows us to clarify this longstanding issue. Our calculations indicate that the moderate-Q$^2$ data at $x\lesssim 1.05$ are in better agreement with the high-Q$^2$ data measured in (anti)neutrino-nuclear reactions which require substantial high-momentum nuclear effects in the generation of superfast quarks. Our prediction for the high-Q$^2$ and x>1.1 region is somewhat in the middle of the neutrino-nuclear and muon-nuclear scattering data.

# QCD Evolution of Superfast Quarks

## Key claim

The authors derive the leading-order QCD evolution equation for superfast quarks (x>1) in nuclei and find that the moderate-Q² JLab data at x≲1.05 are in better agreement with the high-Q² CCFR (anti)neutrino-nuclear data than with the BCDMS muon-nuclear data, while their prediction for x>1.1 lies between the two contradictory high-Q² measurements.

## Method

The authors derive the leading-order DGLAP evolution equation for nuclear parton distributions, extending the integration upper limit to the nuclear mass number A to account for superfast quarks. They apply this evolution equation to the JLab F₂A structure function data for ¹²C at moderate Q² (6-9 GeV²), using two different parameterizations: one from Fomin et al. (2010) and a new fit using the Nachtmann variable ξ as the scaling variable with negligible higher-twist corrections. The evolved structure functions are then compared to the BCDMS and CCFR data at high Q² (60-200 GeV²).

## Key numbers

- The JLab data were measured at moderate Q² values of 6 < Q² < 9 GeV², while the BCDMS data covered 52 ≤ Q² ≤ 200 GeV² and the CCFR data had ⟨Q²⟩ = 125 GeV²
- The BCDMS experiment on ¹²C obtained a slope factor s = 16.5 ± 0.6 for the x-dependence of F₂A at high Q²
- The CCFR experiment on ⁵⁶Fe obtained a slope factor s = 8.3 ± 0.7 ± 0.7, in clear contradiction with the BCDMS result
- QCD evolution of the JLab data yields a slope factor s = 13.0 ± 1.1 for x ≥ 0.75 at Q² = 125 GeV², between the CCFR and BCDMS values
- The new fit parameters for F₂A at Q₀² = √18 GeV² are p₀ = 0.235 ± 0.005, p₁ = 4.45 ± 0.01, p₂ = -9.17 ± 0.01 (from bootstrap method)
- NLO corrections at Q² ∼ 125 GeV² are as much as 11% for the evolved structure function

## Context

This work extends the study of superfast quarks (x>1) in nuclei, which were first probed experimentally by the BCDMS collaboration (Benvenuti et al., 1994) on ¹²C and the CCFR collaboration (Vakili et al., 1999) on ⁵⁶Fe, with contradictory results regarding the strength of high-momentum nuclear components. The JLab experiment (Fomin et al., 2010) provided high-precision data at moderate Q² that the authors now connect to the earlier measurements through proper QCD evolution rather than phenomenological extrapolation. This enables clarification of the longstanding discrepancy between the two high-Q² experiments and provides a framework for analyzing future measurements at the 12 GeV JLab upgrade and the Electron-Ion Collider.
