# Atomic ionization by sterile-to-active neutrino conversion and constraints on dark matter sterile neutrinos with germanium detectors

> The transition magnetic moment of a sterile-to-active neutrino conversion gives rise to not only radiative decay of a sterile neutrino, but also its non-standard interaction (NSI) with matter. For sterile neutrinos of keV-mass as dark matter candidates, their decay signals are actively searched for in cosmic X-ray spectra. In this work, we consider the NSI that leads to atomic ionization, which can be detected by direct dark matter experiments. It is found that this inelastic scattering process for a nonrelativistic sterile neutrino has a pronounced enhancement in the differential cross section at energy transfer about half of its mass, manifesting experimentally as peaks in the measurable energy spectra. The enhancement effects gradually smear out as the sterile neutrino becomes relativistic. Using data taken with germanium detectors that have fine energy resolution in keV and sub-keV regimes, constraints on sterile neutrino mass and its transition magnetic moment are derived and compared with those from astrophysical observations.

## Key claim

The paper claims that atomic ionization via sterile-to-active neutrino conversion, mediated by a transition magnetic moment, can produce detectable signals in direct dark matter experiments, with a pronounced enhancement in the differential cross section at energy transfer about half the sterile neutrino mass, but only for nonrelativistic sterile neutrinos; the enhancement smears out as the neutrino becomes relativistic.

## Method

The authors computed the differential cross section for atomic ionization by sterile-to-active neutrino conversion using an effective Lagrangian with a transition magnetic moment coupling, regularizing the photon propagator pole by adding an imaginary part from photoabsorption in the detector medium. They applied the multiconfiguration relativistic random phase approximation (MCRRPA) to calculate atomic response functions for germanium, and used equivalent photon approximation (EPA), longitudinal photon approximation (LPA), and free electron approximation (FEA) for various kinematic regimes. Experimental bounds were derived from a 139.3 kg-days dataset taken with a 500 g n-type point contact germanium detector at the Kuo-Sheng Reactor Neutrino Laboratory, using a minimal chi-squared analysis with a locally smooth background model.

## Key numbers

- For a nonrelativistic sterile neutrino with mass \(m_s = 7.1\,\text{keV}\) and velocity \(v_s = 10^{-3}\), the differential cross section peak occurs at energy transfer \(T \approx m_s/2 = 3.5\,\text{keV}\) for germanium targets.
- For \(m_s = 20\,\text{keV}\) at \(v_s = 10^{-3}\), the double-pole peak shifts to \(T = 10\,\text{keV}\) and the peak value is larger than the \(m_s = 7.1\,\text{keV}\) case by about one order of magnitude, consistent with the EPA scaling \(d\bar{\sigma}/dT \propto m_s^2\) giving \((20/7.1)^2 \approx 10\).
- The local dark matter density adopted is \(\rho = 0.4\,\text{GeV}\,\text{cm}^{-3}\), with mean velocity \(v_0 = 220\,\text{km/s}\) and escape velocity \(v_{\text{esc}} = 533\,\text{km/s}\) in Earth's reference frame.
- At \(m_s = 7.1\,\text{keV}\), the upper limit on the transition magnetic moment is \(\mu_{\nu_{\text{sa}}} < 2.5 \times 10^{-14}\,\mu_B\) at 90% confidence level.
- The germanium atom number density in detectors is \(n_{\text{Ge}} = 4.42 \times 10^{22}/\text{cm}^3\), giving a regulator \(|\vec{q}| n_{\text{Ge}} \sigma_{\gamma}(\text{Ge}, |\vec{q}|) \lesssim 1200\,\text{eV}^2\) in the allowed range of \(|\vec{q}|\).
- For hydrogen targets with number density \(n_{\text{H}} = 6.6 \times 10^{22}/\text{cm}^3\) (water density), the regulator is \(|\vec{q}| n_{\text{H}} \sigma_{\gamma}(\text{H}, |\vec{q}|) \lesssim 120\,\text{eV}^2\).

## Context

This work extends the formalism of neutrino magnetic moment scattering from active neutrinos (Chen 2013) to sterile-to-active neutrino conversion, and provides direct-detection constraints complementary to astrophysical X-ray searches for sterile neutrino decay, such as the 3.55 keV line reported by Bulbul (2014) and Boyarsky (2014) that suggested a sterile neutrino mass of 7.1 keV. The results enable future direct dark matter experiments with germanium detectors to probe sterile neutrino properties independently of cosmic X-ray observations, and the method can be applied to other detector materials and boosted sterile neutrino scenarios.
