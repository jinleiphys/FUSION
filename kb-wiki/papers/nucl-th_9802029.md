# Ground state correlations and mean-field in $^{16}$O

> We use the coupled cluster expansion ($\exp(S)$ method) to generate the complete ground state correlations due to the NN interaction. Part of this procedure is the calculation of the two-body G matrix inside the nucleus in which it is being used. This formalism is being applied to $^{16}O$ in a configuration space of 50 $\hbarω$. The resulting ground state wave function is used to calculate the binding energy and one- and two-body densities for the ground state of $^{16}O$.

## Key claim

The coupled cluster expansion (exp(S) method) can generate the complete ground state correlations in 16O due to the NN interaction, but the resulting binding energy is no longer an exact upper bound to the true energy because some couplings back into S2 are ignored in the truncation of the expansion.

## Method

The authors solved the coupled cluster equations in a configuration space of 50 ℏω using a harmonic oscillator basis with length parameter b=0.8 fm, excluding orbits with l ≥ 13. They computed the two-body G-matrix inside the nucleus by solving the Bethe-Goldstone equation self-consistently, including corrections for 3p3h correlations in a reduced space of 30 ℏω and l ≤ 6, while 4p4h correlations were included in the full space. The Argonne v18 potential was used to generate matrix elements, with comparisons also made for the Argonne v8 and v14 potentials.

## Context

This work extends the coupled cluster formulation of the Bochum group (Kummel et al.) by solving the equations entirely in configuration space rather than in nuclear matter, where correlation functions extend to infinity and cannot be applied to finite nuclei. The approach contrasts with real-space correlation function methods used by Pieper et al. for 16O and by Pudliner et al. for smaller nuclei. The results enable subsequent calculations of excited states and electron scattering observables, where correlations are expected to modify the strength of transitions by roughly a factor of two compared to mean-field predictions, as previously observed in 208Pb by Lichtenstadt et al. and in knockout reactions by Leuschner et al.
