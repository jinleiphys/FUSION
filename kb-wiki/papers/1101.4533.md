# Comment on "Distribution of Partial Neutron Widths for Nuclei Close to a Maximum of the Neutron Strength Function"

> A recent Letter attempted to reconcile the disagreement between neutron resonance data and random matrix theory (RMT). To this end, a new formula was derived for transforming measured (Γ_{λn}) to reduced (Γ_{λn}^0) neutron widths for s-wave resonances (λ=1,2,...) in nuclides near peaks of the s-wave neutron strength function. In this Comment, we show that such a rescaling would not, in general, be expected to reconcile the type of disagreement observed, and demonstrate that indeed it does not for the specific cases in question. Hence, the disagreements between RMT and these data remain.

## Key claim

The rescaling formula proposed by Weidenmüller (2010) to transform measured neutron widths into reduced widths for s-wave resonances near peaks of the neutron strength function does not reconcile the disagreement between platinum resonance data and the Porter-Thomas distribution predicted by random matrix theory; in fact, the new transformation generally broadens the distribution further, increasing the discrepancy.

## Method

The authors applied the maximum-likelihood (ML) method to extract the number of degrees of freedom ν for the χ² distribution of reduced neutron widths from 153 resonances in ¹⁹²Pt and 161 resonances in ¹⁹⁴Pt, using the new rescaling formula f²(E_λn) = (1/π)(2m/ℏ)^(1/2) √(E_λn)/(E_λn + |E₀|) with the single-particle state energy E₀ as a free parameter. They then compared the resulting ν_ML values as a function of |E₀| to the values obtained with the standard √(E_λn) rescaling reported in Koehler (2010).

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁹²Pt, the ML estimate of ν using the standard rescaling was ν_ML = 0.57^(+0.16)_(-0.15); for ¹⁹⁴Pt, ν_ML = 0.47^(+0.19)_(-0.18); for ¹⁹⁶Pt, ν_ML = 0.60^(+0.28)_(-0.26).
- The combined ¹⁹²,¹⁹⁴Pt data reject the validity of the Porter-Thomas distribution (ν = 1) with at least 99.997% confidence.
- The mean resonance spacings D₀ are 23 eV for ¹⁹²Pt, 50 eV for ¹⁹⁴Pt, and 153 eV for ¹⁹⁶Pt.

## Context

This Comment directly challenges the claim in Weidenmüller (2010) that a new energy-dependent rescaling of neutron widths could resolve the disagreement between neutron resonance data from Koehler (2010) and the Porter-Thomas distribution from Porter and Thomas (1956). The authors demonstrate that the proposed transformation fails for the specific platinum isotopes studied, leaving the original discrepancy unexplained. The work reinforces the need for alternative theoretical approaches beyond standard R-matrix theory if the single-particle state energy E₀ is not much larger than the mean resonance spacing.
