# Impact of the nuclear symmetry energy on the post-merger phase of a binary neutron star coalescence

> The nuclear symmetry energy plays a key role in determining the equation of state of dense, neutron-rich matter, which governs the properties of both terrestrial nuclear matter as well as astrophysical neutron stars. A recent measurement of the neutron skin thickness from the PREX collaboration has lead to new constraints on the slope of the nuclear symmetry energy, L, which can be directly compared to inferences from gravitational-wave observations of the first binary neutron star merger inspiral, GW170817 In this paper, we explore a new regime for potentially constraining the slope, L, of the nuclear symmetry energy with future gravitational wave events: the post-merger phase a binary neutron star coalescence. In particular, we go beyond the inspiral phase, where imprints of the slope parameter L may be inferred from measurements of the tidal deformability, to consider imprints on the post-merger dynamics, gravitational wave emission, and dynamical mass ejection. To this end, we perform a set of targeted neutron star merger simulations in full general relativity using new finite-temperature equations of state, which systematically vary L. We find that the post-merger dynamics and gravitational wave emission are mostly insensitive to the slope of the nuclear symmetry energy. In contrast, we find that dynamical mass ejection contains a weak imprint of L, with large values of L leading to systematically enhanced ejecta.

# Impact of the nuclear symmetry energy on the post-merger phase of a binary neutron star coalescence

## Key claim
The slope L of the nuclear symmetry energy does not leave clear imprints on post-merger dynamics or gravitational wave emission, but dynamical mass ejection contains a weak imprint of L, with large values of L leading to systematically enhanced ejecta. This conclusion is conditional on fixing macroscopic properties (radius or tidal deformability) to isolate the effect of L alone.

## Method
The authors performed seven numerical relativity simulations of binary neutron star mergers in full general relativity using the Frankfurt-/IllinoisGRMHD code, following the final orbits, merger, and up to ~25 ms post-merger. They constructed a set of new finite-temperature equations of state using a piecewise polytropic framework with a Fermi Liquid Theory thermal extension, systematically varying the slope L of the nuclear symmetry energy (40, 100, and 120 MeV) while holding fixed either the radius R1.4 or tidal deformability Λ1.4 of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star, and keeping the low-density EoS (SFHo) and finite-temperature parameters identical across all models.

## Key numbers
- Slope L values explored: 40, 100, and 120 MeV, chosen to span constraints from astrophysics and the PREX-II measurement
- For the R1.4 = 12 km subset: L = 40 MeV gives Λ1.4 = 425; L = 100 MeV gives Λ1.4 = 311; L = 120 MeV gives Λ1.4 = 287
- For the soft EoS subset (R1.4 ≈ 11 km): L = 40 MeV gives R1.4 = 10.8 km and Λ1.4 = 193; L = 100 MeV gives R1.4 = 11.2 km and Λ1.4 = 193
- Maximum temperatures reached in the remnant: >120 MeV for R1.4 = 11 km models; <100 MeV for R1.4 = 13 km models
- The L = 40 MeV, R1.4 = 10.8 km remnant collapses after 15 ms post-merger; all other remnants survive to ~25 ms
- Nuclear saturation density n_sat = 0.16 fm^-3; symmetry energy at saturation S fixed to 32 MeV

## Context
This work extends previous studies that used GW170817 inspiral measurements to constrain L (e.g., Raithel (2019) found L between 9 and 65 MeV from tidal deformability), and addresses the tension between astrophysical constraints (L = 52+20-18 MeV from Essick (2021)) and the PREX-II measurement (L = 106 ± 37 MeV from Reed (2021)). By constructing EoSs that fix R1.4 or Λ1.4 while varying L, the authors disentangle the role of macroscopic properties from the symmetry energy slope. The results suggest that future gravitational wave observations of the post-merger phase may not directly constrain L, but that kilonova observations sensitive to dynamical ejecta mass could provide complementary constraints.
