# Effect of an anisotropic escape mechanism on elliptic flow in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We study the effect of anisotropic escape mechanism on elliptic flow in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. We use Glauber model to generate initial conditions and ignore hydrodynamic expansion in the transverse direction. We employ Beer-Lambert law to allow for the transmittance of produced hadrons in the medium and calculate the anisotropy generated due to the suppression of particles traversing through the medium. In order to separate non-flow contribution due to surface bias effects, we ignore hydrodynamic expansion in the transverse direction and consider purely longitudinal boost-invariant expansion. We calculate the transverse momentum dependence of elliptic flow, generated from anisotropic escape mechanism due to surface bias effects, for various centralities in $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV Au$+$Au collisions at RHIC and $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV Pb$+$Pb collisions at LHC. We find that the surface bias effects have a sizable contribution to the total elliptic flow observed in heavy-ion collisions indicating that the viscosity of the QCD matter extracted from hydrodynamic simulations may be underestimated.

## Key claim

The anisotropic escape mechanism due to surface bias effects has a sizable contribution to the total elliptic flow observed in heavy-ion collisions, but it is not sufficient alone to explain the experimental data; this indicates that the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio extracted from hydrodynamic simulations may be underestimated.

## Method

The authors used the optical Glauber model to generate initial energy density distributions and assumed purely longitudinal boost-invariant (Bjorken) expansion with zero transverse fluid velocity to isolate surface bias effects. They employed a Beer-Lambert law for hadron transmittance through the medium, with an energy-density-dependent attenuation probability, and calculated the transmitted hadron spectra using a modified Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription. The elliptic flow \(v_2(p_T)\) was then computed from the azimuthal anisotropy of the transmitted hadrons for various centralities at both RHIC and LHC energies.

## Key numbers

- For Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200\) GeV (RHIC): initial central energy density \(\epsilon_i(0,0)=30\) GeV/fm\(^3\), initial thermalization time \(\tau_i=0.6\) fm/\(c\), freeze-out temperature \(T_f=130\) MeV.
- For Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV (LHC): initial central energy density \(\epsilon_i(0,0)=85\) GeV/fm\(^3\), initial thermalization time \(\tau_i=0.4\) fm/\(c\), same freeze-out temperature \(T_f=130\) MeV.
- Attenuation length scales \(\Lambda\) were varied: 1, 2.5, 5, and 7.5 fm; even for the smallest value \(\Lambda=1\) fm, the model could not reproduce the magnitude of experimentally observed elliptic flow.
- For very low transverse momentum \(p_T \leq 0.1\) GeV, the anisotropic escape mechanism generated no elliptic flow.

## Context

This work extends the study of escape-mechanism contributions to anisotropic flow, previously investigated within transport theory by He et al. (2015), by implementing the effect within a hydrodynamic framework with Bjorken expansion. It contradicts the claim that escape mechanism alone can generate the majority of elliptic flow, finding instead that hydrodynamic buildup of collective flow is necessary to match experimental data. The model provides a simple method to account for surface bias effects that can be incorporated into the Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription in realistic hydrodynamic calculations, and may be particularly relevant for small-system collisions (e.g., \(p\)+\(p\), \(p\)+Pb) where hydrodynamic medium formation is questionable.
