# Normalizing Flows for Bayesian Posteriors: Reproducibility and Deployment

> We present a computational framework for efficient learning, sampling, and distribution of general Bayesian posterior distributions. The framework leverages a machine learning approach for the construction of normalizing flows for the general probability distributions typically encountered in Bayesian uncertainty quantification studies. This normalizing flow can map a trivial distribution to a more complicated one and can be stored more efficiently than the empirical distribution samples themselves. Once the normalized flow is trained, it further enables parallelized and uncorrelated sampling of the learned distribution. We demonstrate our framework with three test distributions with strong non-linear correlations, multi-modality, and heavy tails, as well as with a realistic posterior distribution obtained from a Bayesian calibration of a nuclear relativistic mean-field model. The performance of the framework, as well as its relatively simple implementation, positions it as one fundamental cornerstone in the development and deployment of continuous calibration pipelines of physical models and as a key component of future reproducible science workflows.

## Key claim

The paper presents a computational framework using normalizing flows (NFs) to efficiently learn, sample, and distribute Bayesian posterior distributions, claiming that once trained, the NF enables parallelized and uncorrelated sampling while requiring less storage than empirical samples. The hedge is that the NF is only an approximation to the true posterior, and the quality depends on the training data covering the parameter region where the posterior has appreciable support.

## Method

The authors use a supervised learning approach where training data consists of Markov-Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) samples from the posterior distribution along with their density values. A 6-layer Real NVP (non-volume preserving) neural network is trained to minimize Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized Kullback-Leibler divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution induced by the normalizing flow. The trained NF maps samples from a standard multivariate Gaussian distribution to the target posterior distribution, enabling direct, uncorrelated sampling.

## Key numbers

- For the Rosenbrock-banana test distribution (c1=1.0, c2=20.0), the auto-correlation length in MCMC was estimated at l ~ [500-1000]; the trained NF achieved Jeffreys divergence of 1.0 x 10^-5
- For the Himmelblau test distribution (c1=11.0, c2=7.0, c3=100.0), the auto-correlation length was l ~ 100; the trained NF achieved Jeffreys divergence of 3.8 x 10^-3
- For the Student-t test distribution (c=3.0), the auto-correlation length was l ~ 100; the trained NF achieved Jeffreys divergence of 6.0 x 10^-5
- For the relativistic mean field model posterior (8 parameters), training used N_t = 5 x 10^4 samples taken from 5 x 10^6 original MCMC samples; Jeffreys divergence reduced from ~10 at initialization (Gaussian fit) to 2 x 10^-3 after training
- Training used batch size N_b = 10^3 and learning rate fixed at 10^-3 with the Adam optimizer

## Context

This work extends the application of normalizing flows to Bayesian uncertainty quantification in nuclear physics, building on prior work by Giuliani (2023) who performed Bayesian calibration of a relativistic mean field model. The framework addresses limitations of storing empirical MCMC samples and of approximating posteriors with multivariate Gaussian distributions as done in covariance analyses for nuclear Density Functional Theory models by Chen (2014), Klupfel (2009), and Erler (2013). The approach enables continuous calibration pipelines where trained NFs from previous calibrations can serve as starting points for updating posterior distributions when new data becomes available.
