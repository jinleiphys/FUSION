# Eigenvector Continuation and Projection-Based Emulators

> Eigenvector continuation is a computational method for parametric eigenvalue problems that uses subspace projection with a basis derived from eigenvector snapshots from different parameter sets. It is part of a broader class of subspace-projection techniques called reduced-basis methods. In this colloquium article, we present the development, theory, and applications of eigenvector continuation and projection-based emulators. We introduce the basic concepts, discuss the underlying theory and convergence properties, and present recent applications for quantum systems and future prospects.

## Key claim

Eigenvector continuation (EC) is a reduced-basis method that uses eigenvector snapshots from selected parameter sets to construct a low-dimensional subspace, enabling accurate emulation of parametric eigenvalue problems; the method can extrapolate beyond the nearest branch point in parameter space, not merely interpolate, because the eigenvectors are analytic functions for real parameters and the variational subspace can capture behavior across avoided level crossings.

## Method

The method constructs a reduced basis from high-fidelity eigenvector snapshots \(\ket{\psi(\params_i)}\) computed at selected training parameter values \(\params_i\). In the offline stage, the Hamiltonian and norm matrices are projected onto this low-dimensional subspace, yielding \(n_b \times n_b\) matrices \(\wt H_{ij}(\params) = \mel{\psi_i}{H(\params)}{\psi_j}\) and \(\wt N_{ij} = \braket{\psi_i|\psi_j}\). In the online stage, a generalized eigenvalue problem \(\wt H(\params)\betavec(\params) = \wt E(\params)\wt N(\params)\betavec(\params)\) is solved for the expansion coefficients \(\beta_i\) and eigenvalue \(\wt E(\params)\), requiring only operations scaling with the small basis size \(n_b\) rather than the original high-fidelity dimension \(N_h\).

## Key numbers

- For the Bose-Hubbard model with four bosons on a \(4\times 4 \times 4\) spatial lattice, EC with subspace dimension 4 using snapshots at \(U/t = -2.0, -1.9, -1.8, -1.7\) extrapolates past a sharp bend in the ground-state energy near \(U/t = -3.8\) caused by an avoided level crossing.
- For a nuclear physics energy density functional, singular values from \(n_b = 50\) snapshots decrease rapidly with principal component number, enabling speed-ups of several thousand compared to the original solver.
- The power series expansion of the eigenvector around \(\theta = 0\) converges only for \(|\theta| < |z|\) where \(z\) is the nearest branch point; EC extends the region of approximation beyond this radius by using multiple expansion points.

## Context

This colloquium article reviews the development of eigenvector continuation, originally introduced by Frame (2017) for quantum many-body problems, and places it within the broader context of reduced-basis methods (RBMs) that have been developed over several decades in applied mathematics and structural engineering, including work by Aktas (1998), Nair (1998), and Machiels (2000). The method extends prior approaches by enabling fast emulation for nuclear structure and reactions, including applications to coupled cluster calculations by Ekström (2019), quantum scattering by Furnstahl (2020), and quantum Monte Carlo simulations by Frame (2019). The trimmed sampling algorithm introduced by Hicks (2022) addresses numerical instabilities from poorly-conditioned norm matrices using physics-informed Bayesian inference.
