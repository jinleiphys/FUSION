# Prospects for a Solid-State Nuclear Clock

> Motivated by recent experimental breakthroughs toward a realization of a solid-state Thorium-229 nuclear clock, we review the technology, basic physics motivation, and limitations of the present generation of atomic clocks. We then discuss prospects for a new generation of clocks based on an anomalous low-energy 8.4 eV nuclear transition in Th-229, with an extremely long lifetime of 641 seconds when doped into CaF crystals. To realize such solid-state nuclear clocks one must confront basic nuclear, AMO, and solid state physics questions. Key challenges are understanding and minimizing the effects of inhomogeneous broadening, associated with strains and electric field gradients due to both the Th dopants and intrinsic crystal defects.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a solid-state nuclear clock based on the 8.4 eV isomeric transition in Thorium-229 holds great promise for surpassing current atomic clocks, but only if the dominant challenge of inhomogeneous broadening, caused by crystal strains and electric field gradients from Th dopants and intrinsic defects, can be understood and reduced by many orders of magnitude.

## Method

The authors review the physics of current atomic clocks and the nuclear structure of Th-229, then analyze three recent experimental breakthroughs: direct frequency measurement of the Th-229 transition linked to a Sr-87 optical clock (Zhang et al., 2024), temperature sensitivity measurements of the transition in CaF2 crystals (Higgins et al., 2025), and long-term frequency reproducibility studies across different samples (Ooi et al., 2025). They derive the quadrupolar coupling Hamiltonian for the nuclear spin states and discuss how electric field gradients from the crystal environment split the ground and isomeric nuclear levels.

## Key numbers

* The Th-229 isomeric transition energy is 8.4 eV, corresponding to a frequency of 2,020,407,384.335(2) MHz, measured relative to the Sr-87 optical clock with a frequency ratio of 4.707072615078(5) (Zhang et al., 2024).
* The excited-state lifetime of Th-229 in a CaF2 host crystal is 641 ± 4 seconds (Zhang et al., 2024), compared to approximately 2000 seconds in free space.
* The electric field gradient at the Th nucleus in CaF2 was measured as V_zz = 109.1(7) V/angstrom^2 (Zhang et al., 2024).
* The ratio of spectroscopic quadrupole moments between the isomeric and ground states is Q_iso/Q_g = 0.57003(1) (Zhang et al., 2024).
* The intrinsic linewidth from the finite lifetime is approximately 10^-3 Hz, while observed spectroscopic line widths are approximately 300 kHz (reduced to 20 kHz in newer samples), representing 8 orders of magnitude of inhomogeneous broadening (Zhang et al., 2024; Ooi et al., 2025).
* A 5 microKelvin temperature control at the zero-sensitivity operating point (approximately 190 K) would correspond to 10^-18 fractional frequency uncertainty (Higgins et al., 2025; Ooi et al., 2025).

## Context

This review extends the decades-long development of optical atomic clocks (Ludlow, 2015; Aeppli, 2024) by proposing a nuclear clock based on the anomalously low-energy Th-229 transition first proposed by Peik (2003). The paper directly analyzes the breakthrough measurements by Zhang et al. (2024), which connected the Th-229 nuclear transition to the Sr-87 clock for the first time, and the subsequent temperature and reproducibility studies by Higgins et al. (2025) and Ooi et al. (2025). The work identifies key open questions about the microscopic origin of inhomogeneous broadening, the role of Sternheimer anti-screening (Sternheimer, 1951, 1966), and the need for first-principles calculations of Th doping sites in CaF2, building on DFT calculations by Hiraki et al. (2025).
