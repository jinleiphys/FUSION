# The falsification of Chiral Nuclear Forces

> Predictive power in theoretical nuclear physics has been a major concern in the study of nuclear structure and reactions. The Effective Field Theory (EFT) based on chiral expansions provides a model independent hierarchy for many body forces at long distances but their predictive power may be undermined by the regularization scheme de- pendence induced by the counterterms and encoding the short distances dynamics which seem to dominate the uncertainties. We analyze several examples including zero energy NN scattering or perturbative counterterm-free peripheral scattering where one would ex- pect these methods to work best and unveil relevant systematic discrepancies when a fair comparison to the Granada-2013 NN-database and partial wave analysis is undertaken.

## Key claim

Chiral nuclear forces, despite 25 years of development and widespread use, may not be more predictive than traditional phenomenological approaches because the regularization-scheme dependence from finite cut-offs dominates the uncertainties; the authors find systematic discrepancies when chiral two-pion-exchange predictions are compared quantitatively to the Granada-2013 NN database and partial wave analysis, even in peripheral scattering where counterterms are absent and the method should work best.

## Method

The authors separate the NN potential into a short-distance part represented by delta-shells on a radial grid (coarse-grained potential) and a long-distance chiral two-pion-exchange (χTPE) tail, then fit the short-range parameters to the self-consistent Granada-2013 NN database of 6713 scattering data below 350 MeV laboratory energy. They compare the resulting phase shifts to those from a partial wave analysis (PWA) and test whether peripheral partial waves predicted by chiral perturbation theory up to N4LO are consistent with the PWA within statistical uncertainties. They also perform zero-energy renormalization by fixing short-range counterterms to reproduce low-energy threshold parameters (scattering lengths and effective ranges) and examine the resulting phase shifts up to 20 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The Granada-2013 database contains 6713 NN scattering data below 350 MeV laboratory energy, with a maximal center-of-mass momentum of 2 fm⁻¹.
- The coarse-grained potential uses a radial grid spacing Δr = 0.6 fm up to a radius r_c = 3 fm, with about 40 delta-shell parameters.
- For fits with r_c = 1.8 fm and maximum T_LAB = 350 MeV, the chiral constants obtained are c₁ = -0.4(11) GeV⁻¹, c₃ = -4.7(6) GeV⁻¹, c₄ = 4.3(2) GeV⁻¹, with χ²/ν = 1.08.
- For r_c = 1.2 fm and maximum T_LAB = 350 MeV, the values become c₁ = -9.8(2) GeV⁻¹, c₃ = 0.3(1) GeV⁻¹, c₄ = 2.84(5) GeV⁻¹, with χ²/ν = 1.26.
- The theoretical uncertainty in binding energies per nucleon is estimated at ΔB_th/A ∼ 0.5 MeV (earlier) and upgraded to ∼ 2 MeV; for ¹⁶O, ΔB_th(¹⁶O)/16 ∼ 4 MeV.
- The Weizsäcker semiempirical mass formula gives ΔB_sem/A ∼ 0.1 MeV.
- Peripheral waves predicted by N4LO chiral perturbation theory show discrepancies exceeding the statistical uncertainty of the PWA, sometimes reaching 3σ.
- Below 40 MeV, the χTPE potential becomes compatible with zero.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous coarse-grained potential analyses (Perez 2013, Navarro Perez 2012) and directly challenges the chiral effective field theory program initiated by Weinberg (1990) and developed by Bedaque (2002), Epelbaum (2008), and Machleidt (2011). The paper argues that the systematic uncertainties from cut-off variation are larger than those of the Weizsäcker mass formula and that chiral potentials using cut-offs as low as r_c = 0.9–1.1 fm (as in Gezerlis 2014 and Piarulli 2014) are not supported by the data. The results question the status of Weinberg's power counting for encoding short-distance ignorance and suggest that efforts should focus on reducing the cut-off dependence as the dominant source of error.
