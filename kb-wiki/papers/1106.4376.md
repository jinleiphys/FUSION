# Low energy ω(\to π^0 γ) meson photoproduction in the nucleus

> The $π^0 γ$ invariant mass distribution spectra in the $ (γ, π^0γ) $ reaction were measured by TAPS/ELSA collaboration to look for the hadron parameters of the $ω$ meson in Nb nucleus. We study the mechanism for this reaction, where we consider that the elementary reaction in Nb nucleus proceeds as $ γN \to ωN; ~ ω\to π^0γ$. The $ω$ meson photoproduction amplitude for this reaction is extracted from the measured four momentum transfer distribution in the $ γp \to ωp $ reaction. The propagation of the $ω$ meson and the distorted wave function for the $π^0$ meson in the final state are described by the eikonal form. The $ω$ and $π^0$ mesons nucleus optical potentials, appearing in the $ω$ meson propagator and $π^0$ meson distorted wave function respectively, are estimated using the "$t\varrho$" approximation. The effects of pair correlation and color transparency are also studied. The calculated results do not show medium modification for the $ω$ meson produced in the nucleus for its momentum greater than 200 MeV. It occurs since the $ω$ meson dominantly decays outside the nucleus. The dependence of the cross section on the final state interaction is also investigated. The broadening of the $ω$ meson mass distribution spectra is shown to occur due to the large resolution width associated with the detector used in the experiment.

# Low energy ω(→ π⁰γ) meson photoproduction in the nucleus

## Key claim
The calculated results do not show medium modification for the ω meson produced in the Nb nucleus for its momentum greater than 200 MeV, because the ω meson dominantly decays outside the nucleus. The broadening of the ω meson mass distribution spectra is shown to occur due to the large resolution width (∼55 MeV) associated with the detector used in the experiment, not due to in-medium effects.

## Method
The reaction mechanism is modeled as γN → ωN followed by ω → π⁰γ in the Nb nucleus. The ω meson photoproduction amplitude is extracted from measured four-momentum transfer distributions in the γp → ωp reaction. The ω meson propagation and the π⁰ meson distorted wave function are described using the eikonal form, with optical potentials estimated via the "tϱ" approximation. The calculated cross sections are folded with a Gaussian detector resolution function (FWHM = 55 MeV) and compared to the CBELSA/TAPS data.

## Key numbers
- The ω meson free space decay width Γ_ω = 8.43 MeV (Particle Data Group value).
- The ω → π⁰γ decay width at the ω meson mass m_ω = 782 MeV is approximately 0.72 MeV.
- The free space ω meson decay length L_ω is about 5.8 fm for k_ω = 200 MeV/c and about 10.66 fm for k_ω = 400 MeV/c.
- The rms radius of ⁹³Nb nucleus is R_rms ∼ 4.3 fm.
- The detector resolution width (FWHM) is 55 MeV, which is about 6.5 times larger than the free space ω meson decay width.
- The incorporation of pair correlation increases the magnitude of the calculated cross section by a factor of ∼1.46 at the peak.
- The nuclear density distribution parameters for ⁹³Nb are c = 4.87 fm and z = 0.573 fm.
- The tagged photon beam energy range is 0.64–2.53 GeV.
- The ωπγ coupling constant f_ωπγ = 0.095.
- The correlation length l_c for pair correlation is 0.3 fm.

## Context
This work extends the theoretical study of the (γ,π⁰γ) reaction on Nb nucleus, following the CBELSA/TAPS collaboration measurement (ELSA, 2008) which initially claimed ω meson mass modification at ∼730 MeV for low ω momentum. However, that claim was later retracted (Metag, 2009) as reanalysis could not reproduce the spectral shape. The calculation uses the eikonal formalism and "tϱ" approximation for optical potentials, similar to approaches by Das (2009) and Pashovkin (2005). The results support the conclusion that no medium modification of the ω meson is observable under these kinematic conditions, and that the observed broadening is purely instrumental. This work enables future experiments to focus on higher momentum transfers or heavier nuclei where the ω meson decay length might become comparable to nuclear dimensions.
