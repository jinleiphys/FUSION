# The landscape of disk outflows from black hole - neutron star mergers

> We investigate mass ejection from accretion disks formed in mergers of black holes (BHs) and neutron stars (NSs). The third observing run of the LIGO/Virgo interferometers provided BH-NS candidate events that yielded no electromagnetic (EM) counterparts. The broad range of disk configurations expected from BH-NS mergers motivates a thorough exploration of parameter space to improve EM signal predictions. Here we conduct 27 high-resolution, axisymmetric, long-term hydrodynamic simulations of the viscous evolution of BH accretion disks that include neutrino emission/absorption effects and post-processing with a nuclear reaction network. In the absence of magnetic fields, these simulations provide a lower-limit to the fraction of the initial disk mass ejected. We find a nearly linear inverse dependence of this fraction on disk compactness (BH mass over initial disk radius). The dependence is related to the fraction of the disk mass accreted before the outflow is launched, which depends on the disk position relative to the innermost stable circular orbit. We also characterize a trend of decreasing ejected fraction and decreasing lanthanide/actinide content with increasing disk mass at fixed BH mass. This trend results from a longer time to reach weak freezout and an increasingly dominant role of neutrino absorption at higher disk masses. We estimate the radioactive luminosity from the disk outflow alone available to power kilonovae over the range of configurations studied, finding a spread of two orders of magnitude. For most of the BH-NS parameter space, the disk outflow contribution is well below the kilonova mass upper limits for GW190814.

## Key claim

The authors find a nearly linear inverse dependence of the ejected mass fraction on disk compactness (black hole mass over initial disk radius), but this result is explicitly a lower limit because the simulations omit magnetic fields, which can enhance the ejected fraction and average velocity by up to a factor of approximately 2 relative to these hydrodynamic models.

## Method

The authors conducted 27 axisymmetric, long-term hydrodynamic simulations of black hole accretion disks using the FLASH code with a pseudo-Newtonian potential, alpha-viscosity parameterization, and a neutrino leakage scheme with absorption. They post-processed outflow trajectories from tracer particles with the SkyNet nuclear reaction network to compute nucleosynthesis yields and radioactive heating, sampling disk masses from 0.01 to 0.30 solar masses and black hole masses from 3 to 15 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- Ejected mass fraction ranges from 4% for the heaviest disk with an 8 solar mass black hole to 21% for the lightest disk around a 3 solar mass black hole
- Disk compactness parameter C_d ranges from 0.60 (for M_bh=3 M_sun, R_d=50 km) to 1.33 (for M_bh=8 M_sun, R_d=60 km)
- Radioactive heating power at 1 day spans two orders of magnitude, from 2.3 x 10^40 erg/s (model b08d01) to 150 x 10^40 erg/s (model b03d30)
- Average outflow velocities are 3.1-3.9 x 10^-2 c for baseline models, increasing to 5.5-5.6 x 10^-2 c for models with higher viscosity parameter alpha=0.1
- For the highest compactness model (b08d03), 85% of the initial disk mass is accreted before weak interactions freeze out
- Median disk masses from the parameter space mapping range from 0.1 M_sun for a 3 M_sun post-merger black hole to 0.02 M_sun for a 15 M_sun black hole

## Context

This work extends the parameter space exploration of BH-NS merger disk outflows beyond previous studies by Fernandez and Metzger (2013), Just et al. (2015), and Fujibayashi et al. (2020), which covered only narrow subsets of possible configurations. The results provide lower limits for kilonova predictions that can be used in parameter estimation models such as those of Barbieri et al. (2019), Hinderer et al. (2019), and Coughlin et al. (2020), and show that for most BH-NS parameter space, the disk outflow contribution is well below the kilonova mass upper limits for GW190814.
