# Results from Shell Model Monte Carlo Studies

> We review results obtained using Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) techniques. These methods reduce the imaginary-time many-body evolution operator to a coherent superposition of one-body evolutions in fluctuating one-body fields; the resultant path integral is evaluated stochastically. After a brief review of the methods, we discuss a variety of nuclear physics applications. These include studies of the ground-state properties of pf-shell nuclei, Gamow-Teller strength distributions, thermal and rotational pairing properties of nuclei near N=Z, $γ$-soft nuclei, and $ββ$-decay in ^{76}Ge. Several other illustrative calculations are also reviewed. Finally, we discuss prospects for further progress in SMMC and related calculations.

## Key claim

The paper reviews Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) techniques as an alternative to direct diagonalization for nuclear structure calculations, claiming that SMMC can treat much larger model spaces than traditional methods, but with the caveat that it provides average nuclear properties rather than detailed spectroscopic information about specific excited states.

## Method

The authors apply a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to convert the imaginary-time many-body evolution operator into a path integral over auxiliary fields, reducing the problem to one-body evolutions in fluctuating fields. They then evaluate the resulting multidimensional integrals using Metropolis Monte Carlo sampling, and overcome the sign problem that arises for realistic nuclear Hamiltonians by extrapolating from a family of Hamiltonians with no sign problem to the physical Hamiltonian. Calculations are performed on parallel MIMD architectures using a FORTRAN code that handles initialization, thermalization, sampling, and extraction of static observables and response functions.

## Key numbers

- Ground state mass excesses for 28 even-even Ti, Cr, Fe, Ni, and Zn isotopes and 4 odd-odd N=Z nuclei in the pf-shell show an average error of +0.45 MeV compared to experimental data
- For the KB3 interaction in the pf-shell, the single-particle basis has N_s=20 for both protons and neutrons
- The computational time scales as N_s^3 N_t, with N_t=32 time slices used at beta=2 MeV^{-1} (Delta beta=0.0625 MeV^{-1}) for benchmark calculations
- On an IBM-SP2 Thin66 processor, the code achieves 179 samples per hour with an average performance of 36 MFLOPS (peak 56 MFLOPS)
- Memory requirements scale as N_s^2 N_t, with a calculation for N_s=32 and N_t=64 fitting in 12 MB of memory
- For the 0f5/2 1p 0g9/2 model space, binding energies for nuclei in the mass range A=64-80 are calculated with a correction formula using parameters alpha=0.234, beta=0.0156, delta=0.0316, and epsilon=1.828 (all in MeV)

## Context

This work extends the nuclear shell model approach beyond the limits of direct diagonalization, which for the pf-shell had been restricted to A=48,49 by Caurier (1994) due to combinatorial growth of basis states. The SMMC method builds on earlier auxiliary field methods applied to condensed matter systems such as the Hubbard model by Linden and Hirsch, and enables calculations throughout the pf-shell and into heavier systems like the 0f5/2 1p 0g9/2 model space. The results for Gamow-Teller strengths are noted as crucial for supernova physics, following the work of Bethe (1990) on electron capture in stellar cores.
