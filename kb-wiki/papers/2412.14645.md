# Correlations between the Neutron Star Mass-Radius Relation and the Equation of State of Dense Matter

> We develop an analytic method of inverting the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) relations to high accuracy. In principle, a specified $\mathcal{E}\mbox{-}P$ relation gives a unique $M\mbox{-}R$ relation, and vice-versa. Our method is developed from the strong correlations that are shown to exist between the neutron star mass-radius curve and the equation of state (EOS) or pressure-energy density relation. Selecting points that have masses equal to fixed fractions of the maximum mass, we find a semi-universal power-law relation between the central energy densities, pressures, sound speeds, chemical potentials and number densities of those stars, with the maximum mass and the radii of one or more fractional maximum mass points. Root-mean-square fitting accuracies, for EOSs without large first-order phase transitions, are typically 0.5% for all quantities at all mass points. The method also works well, although less accurately, in reconstructing the EOS of hybrid stars with first-order phase transitions. These results permit, in effect, an analytic method of inverting an arbitrary mass-radius curve to yield its underlying EOS. We discuss applications of this inversion technique to the inference of the dense matter EOS from measurements of neutron star masses and radii as a possible alternative to traditional Bayesian approaches.

## Key claim
The authors develop an analytic method to invert the neutron star mass-radius (M-R) relation to recover the equation of state (EOS) using power-law fitting formulae, but note that these correlations are only accurate to order 5% due to the non-uniqueness of central energy density-pressure values for a given M-R point: two EOSs predicting the same M-R values generally do not have the same central values.

## Method
The authors selected 316 EOSs from a larger database that satisfy a maximum mass of at least 2 solar masses, using a common SLy4 crustal EOS and a cubic polynomial interpolation between the crust and uniform matter above nuclear saturation density. They identified 11 fractional maximum mass points (f = M/M_max from 1 down to 1/3) on each M-R curve and performed least-squares power-law fits relating central energy densities, pressures, sound speeds, and dimensionless pressures to the maximum mass and the radius at each fractional mass point. The TOV equations were integrated using ln P as the independent variable with a surface boundary pressure of 10^-10 MeV fm^-3.

## Key numbers
- Root-mean-square fitting accuracies for EOSs without large first-order phase transitions are typically 0.5% for all quantities at all mass points
- For the maximum mass point, power-law fits yield accuracies of 1.5% for central energy density, 4.5% for central pressure, and 3.6% for sound speed
- The TPE approach fits for maximum mass quantities gave accuracies of 4.2% for energy density, 7.1% for pressure, and 5.9% for sound speed
- For the maximum mass point, M_max and R_max can be fit from central energy density and pressure with RMS errors of 0.61% and 0.79% respectively
- The TPE relations fit central energy density, pressure and sound speed to M_max and R_max with RMS errors of about 12%, 18% and 4.5% respectively using the Ofengeim (2020) relations
- The fundamental limits to single-point fitting accuracies are in the range of 8-10%

## Context
This work extends prior correlations identified by Ofengeim (2020) and Cai (2023) between the maximum mass point properties and the EOS, using a larger database of over 500 EOS models tabulated by Sun (2024). The method provides an analytic alternative to traditional Bayesian approaches for inferring the dense matter EOS from neutron star mass and radius measurements, which have systematic uncertainties from prior choices of EOS parameterizations. The technique also works for hybrid stars with first-order phase transitions, although with reduced accuracy, and could enable new Bayesian methods starting from parameterized M-R curves instead of parameterized EOS relations.
