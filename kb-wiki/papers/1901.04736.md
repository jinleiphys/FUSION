# Continuum damping effects in nuclear collisions associated with twisted boundary conditions

> The time-dependent Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations have been performed to study $^{24}$Mg +$^{24}$Mg collisions. The twisted boundary conditions, which can avoid finite box-size effects of the employed 3D coordinate space, have been implemented. The prolate deformed $^{24}$Mg has been set to different orientations to study vibrations and rotations of the compound nucleus $^{48}$Cr. Our time evolution results show continuum damping effects associated with the twist-averaged boundary condition play a persistent role after the fusion stage. In particular, a rotational damping in continuum is presented in calculations of both twist-averaged and absorbing boundary conditions, in which damping widths can be clearly extracted. It is unusual that the rotating compound nucleus in continuum evolves towards spherical but still has a considerable angular momentum.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that continuum damping effects, previously studied for small-amplitude giant resonances, also play a persistent role in large-amplitude nuclear collisions and rotations after the fusion stage. The authors show that "molecular vibration states" obtained in time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculations with periodic boundary conditions are questionable because they arise from finite box-size effects rather than physical dynamics.

## Method

The authors performed 3D time-dependent Skyrme Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations using the Sky3D solver for 24Mg+24Mg collisions at various energies and orientations. They implemented twisted averaged boundary conditions (TABC) using a four-point Gauss-Legendre quadrature over twist angles from 0 to π, requiring 64 TDHF calculations per case, and compared results with periodic boundary conditions (PBC) and absorbing boundary conditions (ABC). The time evolution of kinetic energies, quadrupole deformations, and angular momenta were analyzed using Fourier transformation to extract oscillation frequencies and damping widths.

## Key numbers

- The ground state of 24Mg has a prolate deformation of β2=0.49 (axis ratio 1.7:1) from Skyrme Hartree-Fock+BCS calculations
- Fusion barriers range from 22 MeV (head-to-head) to 24 MeV (side-to-side) depending on collision orientation
- For head-to-head collisions at Ec.m.=29, 49, and 69 MeV, the main oscillation peaks from TABC calculations are at 5.8, 5.0, and 6.0 MeV respectively, with damping widths of about 2.2, 1.4, and 2.3 MeV
- For PBC calculations of head-to-head collisions, oscillation frequencies are 7.4, 7.0, and 7.6 MeV respectively
- Final kinetic energies for head-to-head collisions at Ec.m.=29, 49, and 69 MeV are 783.5, 776, and 768 MeV respectively (initial kinetic energies: 809, 829, and 849 MeV)
- Equilibrium quadrupole deformations for head-to-head collisions are 448, 485, and 510 fm2, with axis ratios 3.9:1, 3.8:1, and 3.2:1
- For side-to-side collision at 49 MeV, final kinetic energy is about 824 MeV and final quadrupole deformation is about 220 fm2
- For the fusion-rotation reaction (Ec.m.=40 MeV, impact parameter 2 fm), rotation frequencies are 0.43, 0.46, and 0.49 MeV for TABC, PBC, and ABC respectively
- Damping width Γ for rotation is about 0.16 MeV for TABC and ABC calculations, and about 0.08 MeV for PBC
- The rotation period is about 1300 fm/c
- The averaged angular momentum ⟨Jy⟩ is about 6ℏ at 7000 fm/c for TABC calculations

## Context

This work extends the application of twisted averaged boundary conditions from small-amplitude giant resonance studies (Schuer et al.) to large-amplitude nuclear collisions and rotations. The results contradict the interpretation of persistent small-amplitude oscillations in PBC calculations as physical molecular vibrations, which had been suggested by experiments reporting narrow resonances in 24Mg+24Mg collisions (Wuosmaa et al., Nitto et al., Zurmuhle et al.), though other experiments did not find such resonances (Jachcinski). The rotational damping widths obtained (about 0.16 MeV) are comparable to results from cranking shell model calculations of compound nuclei (rotdamp), and the work enables better understanding of rotating compound nuclei in continuum, with potential applications to nuclear reactions and weakly bound nuclei.
