# Quartet n-d Scattering Lengths

> Quartet n-d scattering lengths are calculated using second-generation nucleon-nucleon potential models. These results are compared to the corresponding quantity recently calculated using chiral perturbation theory.

## Key claim

Second-generation nucleon-nucleon potential models produce quartet n-d scattering lengths that agree with each other within a factor of 2 x 10^{-3}, resolving the larger spread seen in first-generation potentials; the chiral perturbation theory result, while carrying a relatively large theoretical error bar, is consistent with these values, though the authors suggest that error is likely overestimated for this reaction.

## Method

The authors solved exact few-body equations for zero-energy neutron-deuteron elastic scattering in the total angular momentum 3/2 state, using five second-generation nucleon-nucleon potential models: Nijmegen 93, Nijmegen II, Reid Soft Core 93, CD-Bonn, and Argonne V18. The quartet scattering length a_4 was extracted from the s-wave eigenphase shift in the limit of zero relative n-d momentum. For the Argonne V18 potential, a configuration-space calculation was also performed to isolate the effect of the electromagnetic force component.

## Key numbers

- Second-generation potential model results for a_4 (in fm): N93 = 6.346, N II = 6.343, RSC93 = 6.353, CDB = 6.350, AV18 = 6.339
- Chiral perturbation theory result: a_4 = 6.33(10) fm
- Experimental value: a_4 = 6.35(2) fm
- First-generation potential results (for comparison): RSC = 6.304 fm, AV14 = 6.380 fm
- Removing the electromagnetic force from AV18 lowers a_4 by approximately 0.018 fm
- Variations due to partial-wave limitations or three-nucleon forces are of order 10^{-3} a_4 or less

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations of the quartet n-d scattering length using first-generation potentials such as RSC and AV14 (Friar et al., 1989), which showed a spread of roughly 1% between models. The new second-generation results are in close agreement with each other and with the recent chiral perturbation theory calculation (Friar et al., 1999), which motivated this update. The findings demonstrate that improved fits to the nucleon-nucleon data base have eliminated the previous model dependence, and confirm that this observable is insensitive to three-nucleon forces and higher partial waves due to Pauli repulsion constraints.
