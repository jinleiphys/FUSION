# Elastic proton scattering off non-zero spin nuclei

> In recent years, we constructed a microscopic optical potential (OP) for elastic nucleon-nucleus ($NA$) scattering using modern approaches based on chiral theories for the nucleon-nucleon ($NN$) interaction. The OP was derived at first order of the spectator expansion in Watson multiple scattering theory and its final expression was a folding integral between the $NN$ $t$ matrix and the nuclear density of the target. Two- and three-body forces are consistently included both in the target and in the projectile description. The purpose of this work is to apply our microscopic OP to nuclei characterized by a ground state of spin-parity quantum numbers $J^π\ne 0^+$. We extended our formalism to include the spin of the target nucleus. The full amplitudes of the $NN$ reaction matrix are retained in the calculations starting from two- and three-body chiral forces. We show a remarkable agreement with experimental data for the available observables and, simultaneously, provide reliable estimates for the theoretical uncertainties. This work paves the way toward a full microscopic approach to inelastic $NA$ scattering, showing that the derivation of optical potentials between states with $J^π\ne 0^+$ is completely under control.

## Key claim

The authors extend their previously developed microscopic optical potential (OP) for elastic nucleon-nucleus scattering to targets with non-zero ground-state spin, demonstrating that the formalism works for spin-parity quantum numbers Jπ ≠ 0+ and that the agreement with experimental data is comparable to that achieved for spin-zero nuclei, with the caveat that the agreement worsens at larger scattering angles beyond approximately 70° for cross sections and 55° for analyzing power.

## Method

The optical potential is derived at first order of the spectator expansion in Watson multiple scattering theory, expressed as a folding integral between the nucleon-nucleon (NN) t matrix and the nonlocal nuclear density of the target. The formalism is extended to include the target spin by retaining the full amplitudes of the NN reaction matrix from chiral two- and three-body forces, with the nonlocal densities computed using the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) approach with harmonic oscillator basis parameters ħω = 20 MeV and Nmax ranging from 8 to 12 depending on the nucleus. The calculations employ NN chiral potentials at N³LO order with a 500 MeV cutoff, supplemented by chiral three-nucleon forces with density-dependent medium corrections varied between 0.08 fm⁻³ and 0.13 fm⁻³ to produce uncertainty bands.

## Key numbers

- Energy range of applicability: 100 MeV ≤ E ≤ 350 MeV, with the low-energy limit set by the impulse approximation (effects become important below 100 MeV) and the high-energy limit set by the NN interaction cutoff at approximately 500 MeV/c (equivalent to Tlab ≈ 340-350 MeV)
- For ¹³C (Jπ = 1/2⁻) at 200 MeV: reasonable agreement with experimental data up to scattering angles of ~70° for differential cross section and ~55° for analyzing power
- For ⁶Li (Jπ = 1⁺) at 200.4 MeV: differences between N³LO and N⁴LO chiral potential results are small, practically negligible for the cross section
- NCSM parameters: ħω = 20 MeV, λSRG = 2.0 fm⁻¹ cutoff, with Nmax = 8 for ⁹C, ¹³C, and ¹⁰B; Nmax = 10 for ⁷Li; Nmax = 12 for ⁶Li
- Three-body force low-energy constants: cD = 0.7, cE = -0.06 for N³LO calculations; cD = -1.8, cE = -0.31 for N⁴LO calculations

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic OP framework (Vorabbi 2015, 2017, 2018, 2020) from spin-zero nuclei to non-zero spin targets, representing the first application of a microscopic OP derived within Watson multiple scattering theory using NCSM nonlocal densities to nuclei with Jπ ≠ 0⁺. The study builds on prior chiral-inspired optical potentials (Dickhoff 2017, Idini 2017, PhysRevC.95.024315, PhysRevC.100.014601) and NCSM-based approaches (Burrows 2018, 2020), and it enables future work toward a fully microscopic treatment of inelastic nucleon-nucleus scattering, which is necessary for analyzing inverse kinematics experiments at facilities such as FAIR at GSI, SPIRAL2 at GANIL, and the RIBF at RIKEN.
