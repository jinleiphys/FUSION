# New Realization of the Conversion Calculation for Reactor Antineutrino Fluxes

> Validation of the effective conversion method in the reactor antineutrino flux calculation is examined using the \textit{ab initio} calculation of the electron and antineutrino spectra from the state-of-the-art nuclear database. It turns out that neglecting the shape variation of beta decay branches between the allowed and forbidden transitions would induce significant bias in the total inverse-beta-decay yields and energy spectral distributions. We propose a new realization of the conversion method with both the allowed and forbidden virtual branches, and apply it to both the \textit{simulated} data from the nuclear database and \textit{real} data from the fission measurements at ILL by virtue of statistical properties of the allowed and forbidden decays in the database. Two kinds of dominant uncertainty sources are identified and it turns out that the new realization of the conversion calculation can largely reduce the rate and spectral bias and thus present a reliable prediction of the antineutrino fluxes if accurate beta decay information is available in the high endpoint energy range.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that neglecting the shape variation between allowed and forbidden beta decay transitions in the conversion method for reactor antineutrino flux calculations induces significant bias, and proposes a new realization that includes both allowed and forbidden virtual branches to reduce this bias, provided accurate beta decay information is available in the high endpoint energy range.

## Method

The authors used the Evaluated Nuclear Data File (ENDF) B.VIII.0 and the Evaluated Nuclear Structure Data Files (ENSDF) to generate *ab initio* reference models of electron and antineutrino spectra for fission isotopes, treating the electron spectra as mock data for conversion tests. They performed the conversion procedure using 50 virtual beta decay branches with four data points per branch, fitting the electron spectrum from high to low energies with two free parameters per branch (normalization and endpoint energy), and compared the converted antineutrino spectra to the reference models. The new realization incorporates both allowed and first forbidden Gamow-Teller transitions (0⁻, 1⁻, and 2⁻) as virtual branches, using statistical properties of the nuclear database to assign transition types and the effective nuclear charge number as a function of endpoint energy.

## Key numbers

- Around 30% of the beta decay branches in the nuclear database are forbidden transitions, contributing to each fission isotope.
- For the case assuming all branches are allowed GT transitions, the converted antineutrino spectrum accuracy is better than 1% in the energy range from 1.8 to 8 MeV.
- When nonunique forbidden decays are taken as GT 1⁻ transitions, the deviation in the converted antineutrino spectrum can reach 50% at high energies.
- For pure GT 0⁻, 1⁻, and 2⁻ transitions for all forbidden decays, the largest deviations are 1%, 55%, and 5%, respectively.
- The electron and antineutrino spectra were generated in 50 keV bins from 0 to 10 MeV, yielding 200 data points per spectrum.

## Context

This work extends the effective conversion method developed from ILL measurements by VonFeilitzsch (1982), Schreckenbach (1985), and Hahn (1989), and the subsequent calculations by Huber (2011) and Vogel (2007) that assumed all virtual branches were allowed transitions. The paper addresses the reactor antineutrino rate and spectral anomalies reported by Mention (2011) and others, and is consistent with the conclusion of Hayes (2013) regarding forbidden transition effects, but provides a self-consistent analysis using the state-of-the-art nuclear database. The new realization enables more reliable predictions of antineutrino fluxes for future reactor neutrino experiments studying neutrino mass hierarchy.
