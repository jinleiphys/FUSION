# Solution to the hyperon puzzle using dark matter

> In this paper, we studied the ``hyperon puzzle", a problem that nevertheless the large number of studies is still an open problem. The solution of this issue requires one or more mechanisms that could eventually provide the additional repulsion needed to make the EoS stiffer and, therefore, the value of $M_{\rm{max}, T}$ compatible with the current observational limits. In this paper we proposed that including dark matter (DM) admixed with ordinary matter in neutron stars (NSs), change the hydrostatic equilibrium and may explain the observed discrepancies, regardless to hyperon multi-body interactions, which seem to be unavoidable. We have studied how non-self-annihilating, and self-interacting, DM admixed with ordinary matter in NSs changes their inner structure, and discussed the mass-radius relations of such NSs. We considered DM particle masses of 1, 10, and 100 GeV, while taking into account a rich list of the DM interacting strengths, $y$. By analyzing the multidimensional parameter space, including several quantities like: a. the DM interacting strength, b. the DM particle mass as well as the quantity of DM in its interior, and c. the DM fraction, ${\rm f}_{DM}$, we put constraints in the parameter space ${\rm f}_{DM} - p^{\prime}_{\rm DM}/p^{\prime}_{\rm OM}$. Our bounds are sensitive to the recently observed NSs total masses.

## Key claim

Including dark matter (DM) admixed with ordinary matter in neutron stars (NSs) can resolve the "hyperon puzzle" by providing the additional repulsion needed to stiffen the equation of state (EoS) and raise the maximum total mass to values compatible with observational limits, regardless of hyperon multi-body interactions which appear unavoidable.

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for a two-fluid system of ordinary matter (OM) and non-self-annihilating, self-interacting fermionic dark matter coupled only through gravity. The OM EoS was taken from a global fit interpolating between low-energy chiral effective theory and high-density perturbative QCD (Kurkela 2009), while the DM EoS included repulsive vector interactions parameterized by the interaction strength y = m_f/m_I. The dimensionless ratio of DM pressure to OM pressure, p'_DM/p'_OM, was assumed constant throughout the star and scanned over the range 10^-5 to 10^5, with DM particle masses of 1, 10, and 100 GeV and interaction strengths y from 0.1 to 1000.

## Key numbers

- Maximum total masses of DM-admixed NSs fluctuate around 2 M_sun when the DM fraction f_dm is close to zero or p'_DM/p'_OM is below 10^-3
- For DM particle mass 1 GeV with y = 0.1 and 1, NS mass decreases with increasing DM content; for y = 100 and 1000, NS masses may yield > 2 M_sun with increasing DM content for light DM particles
- For DM particle mass 100 GeV, total mass decreases with DM content, with a slower decrease for weakly interacting strength parameters
- The hyperon puzzle arises because including hyperons reduces the theoretically predicted maximum NS mass from 2.28 M_sun to 1.38 M_sun (Bombaci 2016)
- Observed NS masses include PSR J1614-2230 at 1.97 +/- 0.04 M_sun (Demorest 2010) and PSR J0348+0432 at 2.01 +/- 0.04 M_sun (Antoniadis 2013)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of DM-admixed compact objects (Ciarcelluti 2010, Leung 2011, Li 2012, Tolos 2015, Deliyergiyev 2019) by systematically scanning the multidimensional parameter space of DM particle mass, interaction strength, and DM fraction to address the hyperon puzzle. The hyperon puzzle arises because hyperon formation at 2-3 times nuclear saturation density softens the EoS, reducing maximum masses below 2 M_sun (Bombaci 2016, Chatterjee 2015), while three-body forces (Vidana 2010, Logoteta 2019) do not seem to provide a complete solution. The results enable future constraints on DM properties from NS observations and suggest that DM-admixed NSs could explain both the observed 2 M_sun pulsars and the mass-radius relations from gravitational wave events like GW170817 and GW190425.
