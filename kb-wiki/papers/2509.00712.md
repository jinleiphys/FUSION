# Machine learning driven identification of heavy flavor decay leptons in proton-proton collisions at the Large Hadron Collider

> The study of heavy-flavor hadrons is topical in the era of precision measurements, which is useful to test theories based on pQCD. The heavy-flavor hadrons are produced initially during heavy-ion or hadronic collisions and are one of the best probes to understand the initial stages of the collisions as well as the system evolution. In experiments, the heavy-flavor sectors are studied directly via their decay to different hadrons or di-leptons or via their semi-leptonic decay, which is accompanied by additional neutrinos. However, their measurement in experiments is resource-intensive and requires input from different Monte-Carlo event generators. In this study, we provide an independent method based on Machine Learning algorithms to separate such leptons coming from heavy-flavor semi-leptonic decays. We use PYTHIA8 to generate events for this study, which gives a good qualitative and quantitative description of heavy-flavor production in $pp$ collisions. We use the XGBoost model for this study, which is trained with $pp$ collisions at $\sqrt{s}=13.6$~TeV. We use \DCAXY, \DCAZ~and pseudo-rapidity as the input to the machine. The ML model provides an accuracy of 98\% for heavy-flavor decay electrons and almost 100\% for heavy-flavor decay muons.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a machine learning model (XGBoost) can separate leptons from heavy-flavor semi-leptonic decays from inclusive lepton samples using only track-level topological variables (DCAXY, DCAZ, and pseudorapidity), achieving 98% accuracy for electrons and nearly 100% for muons in PYTHIA8-simulated proton-proton collisions at √s = 13.6 TeV, though the model's performance degrades at low transverse momentum and when tested on data generated with different PYTHIA8 tunes.

## Method

The authors generated approximately 10^9 proton-proton collision events at √s = 13.6 TeV using the PYTHIA8 Monte Carlo event generator with tune 4C and color reconnection enabled. They trained an XGBoost gradient boosting classifier on track-level features (DCAXY, DCAZ, and pseudorapidity) to perform binary classification between heavy-flavor decay leptons and light-flavor decay leptons, training separately for electrons in mid-rapidity (|y| < 0.8) and muons in forward rapidity (2.5 < y < 4.0), with pT-binned training and SMOTE oversampling to address class imbalance. The model was then tested on PYTHIA8 data at different center-of-mass energies (900 GeV, 7 TeV, and 13.6 TeV) and validated by comparing predicted transverse momentum spectra, self-normalized yields, and azimuthal correlations against PYTHIA8 truth and ALICE experimental data.

## Key numbers

- XGBoost model accuracy: 98% for heavy-flavor decay electrons and approximately 100% for heavy-flavor decay muons, with light-flavor muon accuracy dropping to 91%
- Training data: approximately 10^9 PYTHIA8 events at √s = 13.6 TeV
- For electrons at low pT, XGBoost predictions deviate from PYTHIA8 by approximately 10%, decreasing to about 5% for higher transverse momentum bins
- When tested on data generated with color reconnection turned off (CR:Off tune), HFE accuracy decreased to 94% and LFE accuracy to 93%
- Azimuthal correlation studied for HFE with pT range 4–12 GeV/c and charged hadrons with pT range 1–2 GeV/c at √s = 5.02 TeV

## Context

This work extends prior machine learning applications in heavy-flavor physics, specifically building on classification algorithms used to distinguish prompt and nonprompt charmed hadrons such as J/ψ (Prasad 2023), D0 mesons (Goswami 2024), and Λc baryons (Mallick 2025). The study offers an alternative to traditional cocktail fitting methods for separating heavy-flavor decay leptons from inclusive lepton spectra, which have been used experimentally by ALICE (2020, 2023) and STAR (2014) for measurements of momentum anisotropy and nuclear modification factors. The approach enables track-level identification of heavy-flavor decay leptons without relying on Monte Carlo input for background subtraction, and the authors suggest it could be extended to future facilities including FCC, NICA, and EIC with incorporation of realistic detector effects.
