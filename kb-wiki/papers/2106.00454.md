# Wave-packet continuum discretisation for nucleon-nucleon scattering predictions

> In this paper we analyse the efficiency, precision, and accuracy of computing elastic nucleon-nucleon (NN) scattering amplitudes with the wave-packet continuum discretisation method (WPCD). This method provides approximate scattering solutions at multiple scattering energies simultaneously. We therefore utilise a graphics processing unit (GPU) to explore the benefits of this inherent parallelism. From a theoretical perspective, the WPCD method promises a speedup compared to a standard matrix-inversion method. We use the chiral NNLO$_{\rm opt}$ interaction to demonstrate that WPCD enables efficient computation of NN scattering amplitudes provided one can tolerate an averaged method error of $~1-5$ mb in the total cross section at scattering energies $0-350$ MeV in the laboratory frame of reference. Considering only scattering energies $\sim40-350$ MeV, we find a smaller method error of $\lesssim 1-2$ mb. By increasing the number of wave-packets we can further reduce the overall method error. However, the parallel leverage of the WPCD method will be offset by the increased size of the resulting discretisation mesh. In practice, a GPU-implementation is mainly advantageous for matrices that fit in the fast on-chip shared memory. We find that WPCD is a promising method for computationally efficient, statistical analyses of nuclear interactions from effective field theory, where we can utilise Bayesian inference methods to incorporate relevant uncertainties.

## Key claim

The wave-packet continuum discretisation (WPCD) method enables efficient computation of nucleon-nucleon scattering amplitudes at multiple energies simultaneously, provided one can tolerate an averaged method error of approximately 1-5 mb in the total cross section across scattering energies 0-350 MeV in the laboratory frame, with smaller errors of less than 1-2 mb when restricting to energies above approximately 40 MeV.

## Method

The authors implement the WPCD method by diagonalising the full Hamiltonian in a finite basis of free wave-packets, then using the resulting discrete eigenstates to approximate all scattering states of interest and evaluate the full resolvent at any on-shell energy. They compare this approach to the standard matrix-inversion (MI) method for solving the Lippmann-Schwinger equation, using the chiral NNLO$_{\rm opt}$ interaction. The WPCD method is implemented on a graphics processing unit (GPU) using CUDA with cuBLAS and cuSOLVER libraries to exploit its inherent parallelism across multiple scattering energies.

## Key numbers

- Averaged method error of approximately 1-5 mb in the total cross section for scattering energies 0-350 MeV in the laboratory frame
- Method error of less than 1-2 mb when considering only scattering energies approximately 40-350 MeV
- Chebyshev distribution parameters used: sparseness degree t=2 and scaling parameter alpha=100 MeV for momentum wave-packets
- The MI method has computational complexity O_MI(T) = 2n_E(4/3(N_Q+1)^3 + 5(N_Q+1)^2 + N_Q+1) for complex T-matrix calculations, where n_E is the number of scattering energies and N_Q is the number of quadrature points
- The MI method using K-matrix representation yields O_MI(K) = n_E(2/3(N_Q+1)^3 + 5(N_Q+1)^2 + N_Q+1), roughly a factor 4 speedup over the T-matrix approach

## Context

This work extends the WPCD method previously developed by Rubtsova et al. (2015) and applies it specifically to nucleon-nucleon scattering with chiral effective field theory potentials. The method complements eigenvector continuation emulation approaches developed by Frame (2017), Konig (2019), Furnstahl (2020), and Melendez (2021) for accelerating NN-scattering calculations, particularly for potentials that do not exhibit linear parameter dependencies. The results enable computationally efficient Bayesian inference for calibrating low-energy constants in chiral effective field theory, where the method error is compared to recent estimates of model discrepancy from PhysRevC.96.024003.
