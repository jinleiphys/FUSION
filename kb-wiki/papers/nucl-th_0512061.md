# Nuclear multifragmentation within the framework of different statistical ensembles

> The sensitivity of the Statistical Multifragmentation Model to the underlying statistical assumptions is investigated. We concentrate on its micro-canonical, canonical, and isobaric formulations. As far as average values are concerned, our results reveal that all the ensembles make very similar predictions, as long as the relevant macroscopic variables (such as temperature, excitation energy and breakup volume) are the same in all statistical ensembles. It also turns out that the multiplicity dependence of the breakup volume in the micro-canonical version of the model mimics a system at (approximately) constant pressure, at least in the plateau region of the caloric curve. However, in contrast to average values, our results suggest that the distributions of physical observables are quite sensitive to the statistical assumptions. This finding may help deciding which hypothesis corresponds to the best picture for the freeze-out stage

## Key claim

The microcanonical, canonical, and isobaric versions of the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) produce very similar average values (caloric curves, pressure, entropy) when the relevant macroscopic variables (temperature, excitation energy, breakup volume) are matched across ensembles, but the distributions of physical observables (isotopic yields, excitation energy distributions, largest fragment mass) are quite sensitive to the statistical assumptions and may therefore distinguish which ensemble best describes the freeze-out stage.

## Method

The authors implemented three statistical ensembles within the SMM framework for a source with A=168 and Z=75: a microcanonical ensemble (fixed excitation energy, mass, charge, and breakup volume), a canonical ensemble (fixed temperature and breakup volume), and an isobaric ensemble (fixed temperature and pressure, with fluctuating breakup volume). They compared average values and distributions of observables such as the caloric curve, pressure, entropy, isotopic yields, excitation energy, and largest fragment mass, using the isobaric ensemble's average breakup volume as input for the canonical and microcanonical calculations to ensure equivalent conditions.

## Key numbers

- The isobaric ensemble used a fixed pressure of p = 0.114 MeV/fm³ for the A=168, Z=75 system.
- The caloric curve plateau onset occurs at T = 6 MeV in the isobaric ensemble.
- The critical temperature for surface tension vanishing is T_c = 18.0 MeV.
- The liquid-drop parameters used: w_0 = 16.0 MeV, beta_0 = 18.0 MeV, C_C = 0.737 MeV, K_asym = 30.0 MeV, Q_asym = 35.0 MeV, epsilon_0 = 16.0 MeV.
- The multiplicity-dependent free volume parameter D = 2.3 r_0, with r_0 the nuclear radius constant.
- For the canonical and standard microcanonical calculations with fixed volume, V/V_0 = 3 was used for all energies.
- The average pressure stays fairly constant for E*/A > 5 MeV when using the isobaric average breakup volume.
- The average breakup volume in the isobaric ensemble shows a linear dependence on excitation energy after the onset of multifragment emission.

## Context

This work extends the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) comparisons by Bondorf (1995) and Gross (1997) by systematically comparing microcanonical, canonical, and isobaric formulations for the same nuclear system. It contradicts earlier claims of negative heat capacity at constant pressure (C_p) in the plateau region found in microcanonical SMM calculations by the same group (SMM2, 2001) and by Chomaz (2000), showing instead that the isobaric ensemble yields large but always positive C_p. The results enable future experimental analyses to use distributions of observables (isotopic yields, excitation energy distributions, largest fragment mass) rather than just averages to determine which statistical ensemble best describes the freeze-out stage in nuclear multifragmentation.
