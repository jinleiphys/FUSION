# Effective interactions and large-scale diagonalization for quantum dots

> The widely used large-scale diagonalization method using harmonic oscillator basis functions (an instance of the Rayleigh-Ritz method, also called a spectral method, configuration-interaction method, or ``exact diagonalization'' method) is systematically analyzed using results for the convergence of Hermite function series. We apply this theory to a Hamiltonian for a one-dimensional model of a quantum dot. The method is shown to converge slowly, and the non-smooth character of the interaction potential is identified as the main problem with the chosen basis, while on the other hand its important advantages are pointed out. An effective interaction obtained by a similarity transformation is proposed for improving the convergence of the diagonalization scheme, and numerical experiments are performed to demonstrate the improvement. Generalizations to more particles and dimensions are discussed.

## Key claim

The widely used configuration-interaction (CI) method with harmonic oscillator basis functions converges only algebraically for quantum dot Hamiltonians containing non-smooth interaction potentials, and this slow convergence persists even for arbitrarily small interaction strengths because the eigenfunctions themselves become non-smooth. The authors propose an effective interaction obtained via a similarity transformation that reproduces exact eigenvalues of the separable part of the Hamiltonian regardless of the energy cut parameter, thereby improving the algebraic convergence rate considerably.

## Method

The authors analyze the convergence of Hermite function series expansions for eigenfunctions of a one-dimensional two-particle quantum dot model with a screened Coulomb interaction U(x) = λ/(|x|+δ). They apply the Suzuki-Okamoto similarity transformation technique to construct an effective two-body interaction that yields exact eigenvalues for the Hamiltonian in centre-of-mass coordinates when the trap perturbation v(x) is zero. Numerical CI calculations are performed for the trap Hamiltonian (with a Gaussian perturbation creating an asymmetric double-well potential) and the interaction Hamiltonian separately, then for the full two-particle problem, comparing convergence rates with and without the effective interaction.

## Key numbers

- The screened Coulomb interaction uses λ = 1 and δ = 0.01 for numerical experiments
- For the interaction Hamiltonian ground state, Hermite coefficients decay as o(n^{-1.57}) around n=600 and o(n^{-1.73}) around n=5000
- The CI energy error for the interaction Hamiltonian decays as o(N_max^{-1.24}) evaluated at n=600, and the wave function error decays as o(N_max^{-1.20})
- For the full two-particle CI calculation, the energy error decays as o(N_max^{-1.02}) at n=70
- For the trap Hamiltonian with smooth Gaussian perturbation (A=4, C=2, μ=0.75), N_max ~ 40 yields ground state energy accurate to ~10^{-10}
- The Hermite functions are uniformly bounded with |φ_n(x)| ≤ 0.816 for all x and n

## Context

This work extends convergence analysis techniques from Boyd (1984) and Hille (1939) for Hermite function series to the configuration-interaction method used extensively in quantum dot calculations (Ezaki 1997, Maksym 1998, Reimann 2000, Rontani 2006). The effective interaction approach via similarity transformation was developed by Suzuki and Okamoto (1982, 1994, 1995) and has been applied successfully in nuclear physics by Navratil (1998, 2000) and others. The method enables systematic convergence studies for many-electron quantum dots and could be combined with other many-body methods like coupled cluster theories, with implications for studies of coherence in quantum dot-based quantum gates (Loss 1998).
