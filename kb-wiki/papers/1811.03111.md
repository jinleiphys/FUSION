# Recent developments for the optical model of nuclei

> A brief overview of various approaches to the optical-model description of nuclei is presented. A survey of some of the formal aspects is given which links the Feshbach formulation for either the hole or particle Green's function to the time-ordered quantity of many-body theory. The link between the reducible self-energy and the elastic nucleon-nucleus scattering amplitude is also presented using the development of Villars. A brief summary of the essential elements of the multiple-scattering approach is also included. Several ingredients contained in the time-ordered Green's function are summarized for the formal framework of the dispersive optical model (DOM). Empirical approaches to the optical potential are reviewed with emphasis on the latest global parametrizations for nucleons and composites. Various calculations that start from an underlying realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction are discussed with emphasis on more recent work. The efficacy of the DOM is illustrated in relating nuclear structure and nuclear reaction information. Its use as an intermediate between experimental data and theoretical calculations is advocated. Applications of the use of optical models are pointed out in the context of the description of nuclear reactions other than elastic nucleon-nucleus scattering.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the dispersive optical model (DOM), which enforces causality through dispersion relations linking real and imaginary potentials, provides a crucial framework for unifying nuclear structure and reaction information, particularly for rare isotopes where experimental data are scarce. The authors hedge that while the DOM is advocated as an intermediate between experiment and theory, the review is necessarily selective and biased by the authors' interests, and the number of works on the optical model is "immense."

## Method

The paper surveys formal derivations of the optical potential, linking the Feshbach formulation for hole and particle Green's functions to the time-ordered Green's function of many-body theory, and presents Villars' derivation connecting the reducible self-energy to the elastic nucleon-nucleus scattering amplitude. It reviews empirical global parametrizations for nucleons and composites, microscopic calculations from realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions (including nuclear-matter approaches, multiple-scattering theory, and Green's-function methods), and applications of the dispersive optical model to relate nuclear structure and reaction data.

## Key numbers

- The average Fermi energy is defined as ε_F = 1/2 [ε_F^+ - ε_F^-] = 1/2 [(E^{A+1}_0 - E^A_0) - (E^A_0 - E^{A-1}_0)], providing a key reference energy linking particle and hole domains.
- The hole spectral density S^{(h)}(r, r'; E) is given by a sum over discrete bound states with delta functions δ(E - E_n^-) plus a sum over continuum channels, with thresholds T^{(-)}_c defined relative to the A-body ground-state energy.
- The particle Green's function has discrete poles at negative energy on the real axis and a cut just below the real-energy axis for E > 0, with thresholds T^{(+)}_c extending to infinity.
- The time-ordered Green's function exhibits a left-hand cut from -∞ to T^-_0 and a right-hand cut from 0 to ∞ on the real axis.

## Context

This review extends the earlier reviews by Feshbach (1958) and Hodgson (1967, 1971, 1984, 2002), updating the field since the last comprehensive review in 2002. It synthesizes formal developments from Capuzzi and Mahaux (2000) linking Feshbach theory to many-body Green's functions, and from Bell (1959) and Villars (1967) connecting the self-energy to scattering amplitudes. The work enables improved optical potentials for radioactive-beam experiments and establishes connections between nuclear reactions and structure needed for understanding rare isotopes near the drip lines.
