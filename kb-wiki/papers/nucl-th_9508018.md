# Direct radiative capture of p-wave neutrons

> The neutron direct radiative capture (DRC) process is investigated, highlighting the role of incident p-wave neutrons. A set of calculations is shown for the 12-C(n,gamma) process at incoming neutron energies up to 500 keV, a crucial region for astrophysics. The cross section for neutron capture leading to loosely bound s, p and d orbits of 13-C is well reproduced by the DRC model demonstrating the feasibility of using this reaction channel to study the properties of nuclear wave functions on and outside the nuclear surface. A sensitivity analysis of the results on the neutron-nucleus interaction is performed for incident s- as well as p-waves. It turned out that the DRC cross section for p-wave neutrons is insensitive to this interaction, contrary to the case of incident s-wave neutrons. PACS number(s): 25.40Lw,21.10Gv,23.40.Hc

## Key claim

The direct radiative capture (DRC) cross section for incident p-wave neutrons is insensitive to the neutron-nucleus interaction, in contrast to the case of incident s-wave neutrons, where the cross section is sensitive to this interaction.

## Method

The authors performed DRC model calculations for the ¹²C(n,γ) process at incoming neutron energies up to 500 keV, reproducing the cross section for neutron capture leading to loosely bound s, p, and d orbits of ¹³C. A sensitivity analysis of the results on the neutron-nucleus interaction was carried out for both incident s-wave and p-wave neutrons.

## Key numbers

- The calculations cover incoming neutron energies up to 500 keV for the ¹²C(n,γ) process.
- The DRC model reproduces the cross section for neutron capture leading to loosely bound s, p, and d orbits of ¹³C.

## Context

This work extends the study of the direct radiative capture process to p-wave neutrons, highlighting their role in a neutron energy region crucial for astrophysics (up to 500 keV). The finding that p-wave DRC cross sections are insensitive to the neutron-nucleus interaction, while s-wave DRC cross sections are sensitive, enables the use of this reaction channel to study nuclear wave function properties on and outside the nuclear surface without the complication of interaction uncertainties that affect s-wave analyses.
