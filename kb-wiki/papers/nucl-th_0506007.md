# Study of hyperfine anomaly in $^9$Be within three-cluster model

> The study of the hyperfine (hfs) anomaly in neutron rich nuclei can give a very specific and unique way to study the neutron distribution and the clustering structure. We study the sensitivity of the hfs anomaly to the clustering effects in the 9Be isotope using two different nuclear wave functions obtained in the three-cluster (alpha+alpha+n) model. The results are compared to those obtained for 9,11Be in a two-body core + neutron model to examine whether the hfs anomaly is sensitive to a halo structure in 11Be.

## Key claim

The hyperfine (hfs) anomaly in \(^9\)Be is more sensitive to the neutron spatial distribution than to the charge distribution of the whole nucleus, but the total hfs anomaly \(\epsilon\) varies within 14% depending on the choice of the three-cluster wave function, with the neutron root-mean-square distance \(r_n\) being a crucial parameter.

## Method

The authors calculated the hyperfine anomaly (Bohr-Weisskopf effect \(\epsilon_{BW}\) plus Breit-Rosenthal-Crawford-Shawlow correction \(\delta\)) for \(^9\)Be using two different three-body (\(\alpha+\alpha+n\)) wave functions obtained via the hyperspherical harmonics method. The wave functions (WF1 and WF2) differ in their \(\alpha-\alpha\) and \(\alpha-n\) interaction potentials and in the weights of partial states, and the results were compared to earlier core+neutron model calculations for \(^{9,11}\)Be. The magnetic dipole and electric quadrupole moments were also computed to test the accuracy of the ground-state wave functions.

## Key numbers

- The \(^9\)Be charge rms radius from WF1 is 2.564 fm (experimental values: 2.519(12) and 2.50(9) fm); from WF2 it is 2.707 fm.
- The magnetic dipole moment from WF1 is \(-1.0531 \mu_N\) (experimental: \(-1.177432(3) \mu_N\)); from WF2 it is \(-1.316 \mu_N\).
- The electric quadrupole moment from WF1 is 53.39 mb (experimental: 52.88(38) mb); from WF2 it is 65.42 mb.
- The total hfs anomaly \(\epsilon\) for WF1 is \(-0.06756\%\); for WF2 it is \(-0.07809\%\); for the core+neutron model it is \(-0.0687\%\).
- The neutron contribution \(\epsilon_{BW}^{(n)}\) is \(-0.02281\%\) for WF1 and \(-0.03059\%\) for WF2; the \(\alpha\)-particle contribution \(\epsilon_{BW}^{(\alpha)}\) is 0.00085% for WF1 and 0.00088% for WF2.
- The BRCS correction \(\delta\) is \(-0.04644\) for WF1 and \(-0.04926\) for WF2.
- The neutron radial distance \(r_n\) is 3.207 fm for WF1 and 3.621 fm for WF2.
- Varying the \(\alpha\)-particle charge radius from 1.676 to 1.636 fm increases \(\epsilon_{BW}\) by 1.6% for WF1 and 0.5% for WF2.
- With WF2 and an \(\alpha\)-particle radius of 1.46 fm (WF2\(^*\)), the \(^9\)Be charge radius is 2.533 fm and \(\epsilon = -0.07666\%\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous two-body core+neutron model calculations for \(^{9,11}\)Be (Parfenova 2005) by introducing a three-cluster (\(\alpha+\alpha+n\)) description of \(^9\)Be to study clustering effects on the hyperfine anomaly. The results are compared to those from the core+neutron model of Fujimura (1999) and show that the hfs anomaly is sensitive to the neutron spatial distribution, with potential application to probing halo structures in neutron-rich nuclei such as \(^{11}\)Be. The study enables future experimental tests of neutron clustering via hyperfine measurements, though no experimental data on the hyperfine anomaly currently exist for these nuclei.
