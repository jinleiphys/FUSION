# Hadron-Quark Combustion as a Nonlinear, Dynamical System

> The hadron-quark combustion front is a system that couples various processes, such as chemical reactions, hydrodynamics, diffusion, and neutrino transport. Previous numerical work has shown that this system is very nonlinear, and can be very sensitive to some of these processes. In these proceedings, we contextualize the hadron-quark combustion as a nonlinear system, subject to dramatic feedback triggered by leptonic weak decays and neutrino transport.

# Hadron-Quark Combustion as a Nonlinear, Dynamical System

## Key claim

The hadron-quark combustion front is a genuinely nonlinear system out of equilibrium, where leptonic weak decays and neutrino transport generate positive feedback loops that can slow the burning speed by orders of magnitude or even halt the front entirely, but only if neutrinos are free-streaming rather than trapped.

## Method

The authors review and contextualize one-dimensional numerical simulations (Niebergal et al. 2010, Ouyed et al. 2017) that solve the coupled reaction-diffusion-advection equations for the conversion of two-flavored quark matter to three-flavored quark matter, incorporating neutrino transport via flux-limited diffusion, electron capture reactions, and hydrodynamic pressure gradients. They analyze the enhancing processes (flavor equilibration, electron capture heating, neutrino pressure, Joule heating from lepton number loss) and quenching processes (electron degeneracy pressure gradients, neutrino cooling) that create feedback loops in the reaction zone.

## Key numbers

- Burning speed from numerical simulations: approximately 0.002c to 0.04c, where c is the speed of light, orders of magnitude faster than some earlier semi-analytic models predicted
- A temperature reduction of only 0.1 MeV in the three-flavored quark ash can reduce thermal pressure dramatically enough to almost halt the burning interface
- Numerical simulations used a grid of 600 cm with 48,000 zones (zone size dx = 0.0125 cm)
- Initial thermodynamic parameters for the simulation showing front halting: temperature T = 20 MeV, initial lepton fraction Y_L = 0.2, initial baryonic density n_B = 0.35 fm^{-3}

## Context

This work extends the numerical approach pioneered by Niebergal et al. (2010) and Ouyed et al. (2017), which showed large differences from earlier semi-analytic methods by Olinto (1987) and Benvenuto (1989) that simplified the reaction-diffusion-advection equations. The key advance is identifying leptonic weak interactions as a source of nonlinear feedback that can change burning speeds by orders of magnitude, whereas earlier work focused primarily on the high-density equation of state. The results suggest that multidimensional simulations are needed to determine whether deleptonization instabilities lead to slow burning over hours or supersonic detonation within milliseconds, analogous to how multidimensional hydrodynamic instabilities proved crucial for understanding core-collapse supernova explosion mechanisms (Muller 2016).
