# Molecular and Exotic Dibaryons and Other Hadrons

> Experimental evidence has been growing for the existence of both molecular and exotic dibaryons. The former are dominated by hadron and the latter by quark-gluon degrees of freedom. Exotic dihadrons are of particular interest because their properties are closely related to the parameters and non-perturbative properties of QCD models. Molecular structures provide information about confinement. Purely exotic models, such as bag and valence quark models, ignore the multi-hadron phase of confinement. Multi-configuration quark models and R-matrix boundary condition models better represent the medium range repulsive effects leading to confinement, predicting higher excitation energies of the exotic resonances. The ^1S_0 exotic predicted at 2.70 GeV mass has been identified in pp spin observables, implying that there are several more even and odd parity exotics in the NN system between 2.6 and 3.0 GeV. The method also predicts exotic strange and doubly strange dibaryons (including a di-lambda unbound by about 0.13 GeV) and exotics in baryon and meson channels. The R-matrix formalism also describes the properties of molecular structures and enables a classification of these into several types. Observed and predicted examples of each type are discussed, including the recently observed d'.

# Molecular and Exotic Dibaryons and Other Hadrons

## Key claim
The R-matrix boundary condition method, which connects short-range perturbative QCD to the long-range hadronic region, predicts that the \(^1S_0\) exotic dibaryon at 2.70 GeV mass has been identified in pp spin observables, implying several more even and odd parity exotic resonances exist in the NN system between 2.6 and 3.0 GeV. The claim is hedged by the fact that the identification relies on the specific R-matrix formalism and that further experiments in finer energy steps are still needed, particularly for the predicted I=0 state.

## Method
The author applies an R-matrix boundary condition method that connects the short-range perturbative QCD region (described by quark and gluon degrees of freedom) to the long-range hadronic region (described by meson-exchange potentials). The method uses a boundary condition at a radius \(r_0\) that relates the logarithmic derivative of the external hadron wave function to an energy-dependent f-matrix, which contains poles from internal quark configuration energies and fractional parentage coefficients. By integrating the Schrodinger equation from \(r_0\) to large distances, the S-matrix is obtained, and the positions of its poles and branch points determine the resonant and bound-state structures of two-hadron systems.

## Key numbers
- The \(^1S_0\) exotic dibaryon is predicted at a mass of 2.70 GeV and has been identified in pp spin observables.
- Several more even and odd parity exotic resonances are implied in the NN system between 2.6 and 3.0 GeV.
- A di-lambda (doubly strange dibaryon) is predicted to be unbound by about 0.13 GeV.
- The recently observed \(d'(2060)\) has a width \(\leq 1\) MeV and could be described as a bound state of a nucleon and an odd-parity nuclear isobar coupled electromagnetically to an \(NN\pi\) state.
- Exotic resonance widths from quark degrees of freedom are predicted to be \(\sim 50\) MeV to \(\sim 100\) MeV.
- The lowest predicted I=0 NN state is at 2.63 GeV (consistent with some recent np data, but requiring finer energy steps).
- Constituent quark calculations predict similar spectra to the R-matrix method, at about 100 MeV less mass.

## Context
This work extends the R-matrix approach previously applied to NN systems (Lomon, 1987) and classifies dibaryon structures into molecular types (dominated by hadron exchange forces) and exotic types (dominated by quark-gluon degrees of freedom). It contrasts with purely exotic models such as bag models and valence quark models, which ignore the multi-hadron phase of confinement, and argues that multi-configuration quark models and the R-matrix method better represent medium-range repulsive effects leading to confinement. The paper enables future experimental searches for predicted exotic states in the NN, \(\Lambda N - \Sigma N\), \(\Lambda\Lambda - \Xi N\), and \(KN\) systems, and provides a framework for distinguishing narrow molecular resonances (such as Dalitz-Tuan resonances) from broader exotic resonances.
