# Spectroscopy of resonance decays in high-energy heavy-ion collisions

> Invariant mass distributions of the hadronic decay products from resonances formed in relativistic heavy ion collision (RHIC) experiments are investigated with a view to disentangle the effects of thermal motion and the phase space of decay products from those of intrinsic changes in the structure of resonances at the freeze-out conditions. Analytic results of peak mass shifts for the cases of both equal and unequal mass decay products are derived. The shift is expressed in terms of the peak mass and width of the vacuum or medium-modified spectral functions and temperature. Examples of expected shifts in meson (e.g., rho, omega, and sigma) and baryon (e.g., Delta) resonances that are helpful to interpret recent RHIC measurements at BNL are provided. Although significant downward mass shifts are caused by widened widths of the $ρ-$meson in medium, a downward shift of at least 50 MeV in its intrinsic mass is required to account for the reported downward shift of 60-70 MeV in the peak of the rho-invariant mass distribution. An observed downward shift from the vacuum peak value of the Delta distinctively signals a significant downward shift in its intrinsic peak mass, since unlike for the rho-meson, phase space functions produce an upward shift for the Delta isobar.

# Spectroscopy of resonance decays in high-energy heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The observed 60-70 MeV downward shift of the rho-meson peak in RHIC data cannot be explained by thermal phase space effects or broadening alone; an intrinsic downward mass shift of at least 50 MeV in the rho-meson's spectral function is required, whereas for the Delta isobar, an observed downward shift from vacuum would distinctively signal a significant intrinsic mass shift because phase space functions alone produce an upward shift for the Delta.

## Method

The authors derive analytic expressions for invariant mass distributions of hadronic resonance decays in a thermalized medium, starting from the thermal phase space distribution of off-shell resonances combined with a Breit-Wigner spectral function. They separate the effects of thermal motion and decay product phase space (evaluated without approximation) from intrinsic medium modifications of resonance mass and width, using the self-energy formalism where Re Pi gives the intrinsic mass shift and Im Pi gives the in-medium width. For the rho-meson, they use a vacuum Breit-Wigner peak mass of 775.9 MeV and width of 147.9 MeV from e+e- collisions, and for the sigma meson they consider two parameter sets (M_R=600 MeV with Gamma_R=400 MeV, and M_R=800 MeV with Gamma_R=800 MeV).

## Key numbers

- The rho-meson vacuum peak mass is taken as 775.9 MeV with vacuum width 147.9 MeV from e+e- collisions
- Phase space alone produces a downward shift of only -10.6 MeV at T=100 MeV and an upward shift of +1.5 MeV at T=175 MeV for the rho-meson
- An intrinsic downward mass shift of at least 50 MeV in the rho-meson is required to account for the reported 60-70 MeV downward shift in the rho-invariant mass distribution
- For the sigma meson with M_R=600 MeV and Gamma_R=400 MeV, the peak shift is -72.8 MeV at T=125 MeV and -141.5 MeV at T=100 MeV
- For the sigma meson with M_R=800 MeV and Gamma_R=800 MeV, the peak shift is -349.5 MeV at T=125 MeV and -409.0 MeV at T=100 MeV
- The temperature at which phase space shifts cancel (Delta M = 0) for the rho-meson is approximately 160 MeV

## Context

This work provides a theoretical framework to interpret resonance spectroscopy measurements from RHIC experiments at BNL, particularly the preliminary rho-meson results from Fachini (2002) showing a 60-70 MeV downward shift, and the K*(892)0 and phi-meson reconstructions from STAR at sqrt(s_NN)=130 GeV. The analysis extends prior work on in-medium spectral functions by Rapp, Wambach, and collaborators (1996-2000) and by Klingl, Kaiser, and Weise (1997), and addresses ongoing debates about medium modifications of spectral functions and partial chiral symmetry restoration. The results enable experimentalists to disentangle thermal phase space effects from genuine medium modifications when interpreting invariant mass distributions from heavy-ion collisions, and provide a method to estimate freeze-out temperatures from measured peak shifts if medium effects are under theoretical control.
