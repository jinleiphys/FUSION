# The Radius of PSR J0740+6620 from NICER and XMM-Newton Data

> PSR J0740$+$6620 has a gravitational mass of $2.08\pm 0.07~M_\odot$, which is the highest reliably determined mass of any neutron star. As a result, a measurement of its radius will provide unique insight into the properties of neutron star core matter at high densities. Here we report a radius measurement based on fits of rotating hot spot patterns to Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER) and X-ray Multi-Mirror (XMM-Newton) X-ray observations. We find that the equatorial circumferential radius of PSR J0740$+$6620 is $13.7^{+2.6}_{-1.5}$ km (68%). We apply our measurement, combined with the previous NICER mass and radius measurement of PSR J0030$+$0451, the masses of two other $\sim 2~M_\odot$ pulsars, and the tidal deformability constraints from two gravitational wave events, to three different frameworks for equation of state modeling, and find consistent results at $\sim 1.5-3$ times nuclear saturation density. For a given framework, when all measurements are included the radius of a $1.4~M_\odot$ neutron star is known to $\pm 4$% (68% credibility) and the radius of a $2.08~M_\odot$ neutron star is known to $\pm 5$%. The full radius range that spans the $\pm 1σ$ credible intervals of all the radius estimates in the three frameworks is $12.45\pm 0.65$ km for a $1.4~M_\odot$ neutron star and $12.35\pm 0.75$ km for a $2.08~M_\odot$ neutron star.

## Key claim

The equatorial circumferential radius of PSR J0740+6620 is measured to be 13.7^{+2.6}_{-1.5} km (68% credibility), but this radius estimate is roughly proportional to the mass, so if the true mass is lower than the adopted 2.08 M_sun (e.g., 1.8 M_sun), the radius posterior would largely overlap the reported value.

## Method

The authors fit rotating hot spot patterns to X-ray data from NICER (providing modulated emission information) and XMM-Newton (providing total flux constraints with lower background) for the 346.53 Hz pulsar PSR J0740+6620. They modeled the emission using two uniform-temperature circular hot spots on a pure hydrogen atmosphere, with partial ionization allowed, and assumed the magnetic field could be neglected. A Gaussian prior on the mass (2.08 ± 0.09 M_sun) and an asymmetric Gaussian prior on the distance (1.136 kpc central value) from radio timing observations were incorporated, along with a prior on the observer inclination flat within 5 degrees of the orbital inclination of 87.5 degrees.

## Key numbers

- PSR J0740+6620 gravitational mass: 2.08 ± 0.07 M_sun (68% credibility), the highest reliably determined neutron star mass
- Equatorial circumferential radius of PSR J0740+6620: 13.7^{+2.6}_{-1.5} km (68% credibility)
- For a 1.4 M_sun neutron star, radius known to ±4% (68% credibility) within a given EOS framework when all measurements are combined
- For a 2.08 M_sun neutron star, radius known to ±5% (68% credibility) within a given EOS framework when all measurements are combined
- Full radius range spanning ±1σ credible intervals across three EOS frameworks: 12.45 ± 0.65 km for a 1.4 M_sun neutron star and 12.35 ± 0.75 km for a 2.08 M_sun neutron star
- NICER net exposure time: 1602.68 ksec, yielding 521004 events in the 0.31-1.18 keV energy range
- PSR J0740+6620 rotational frequency: 346.53 Hz
- Distance: 1.136^{+0.174}_{-0.152} kpc (68% credibility)
- Orbital inclination: 87.5° ± 0.17°
- Surface magnetic field (centered dipole): approximately 3 × 10^8 G

## Context

This work extends the NICER mass and radius measurements previously performed on PSR J0030+0451 (Miller et al. 2019; Riley et al. 2019) to the highest-mass reliably measured neutron star, probing matter at approximately 2.2 times nuclear saturation density rather than the 1.6 times probed by 1.4 M_sun stars. The results are combined with the masses of two other ~2 M_sun pulsars (PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0348+0432) and tidal deformability constraints from gravitational wave events GW170817 and GW190425 to constrain the equation of state across three different modeling frameworks, enabling ±4-5% radius precision for both canonical and high-mass neutron stars.
