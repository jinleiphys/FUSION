# The $ν$ process in the light of an improved understanding of supernova neutrino spectra

> We study the neutrino-induced production of nuclides in explosive supernova nucleosynthesis for progenitor stars with solar metallicity including neutrino nucleus reactions for all nuclei with charge numbers $Z < 76$ with average neutrino energies in agreement with modern Supernova simulations. Considering progenitors with initial main sequence masses between 13~M$_\odot$ and 30~M$_\odot$, we find a significant production of $^{11}$B, $^{138}$La, and $^{180}$Ta by neutrino nucleosynthesis, despite the significantly reduced neutrino energies. The production of $^{19}$F turns out to be more sensitive to the progenitor mass and structure than to the $ν$ process. With our complete set of cross sections we have identified effects of the $ν$~process on several stable nuclei including $^{33}$S, $^{40}$Ar, $^{41}$K, $^{59}$Co, and $^{113}$In at the 10\% level. Neutrino-induced reactions contribute to a similar extent to the production of radioactive ${}^{26}$Al and increase the yield of $^{22}$Na by 50\%. Future $γ$~ray astronomy missions may reach the precision at which the contribution from the $ν$~process becomes relevant. We find that the production of $^{22}$Na by the $ν$~process could explain the Ne-E(L) component of meteoritic graphite grains. The $ν$~process enhances the yield of $^{36}$Cl and we point out that the resulting $^{36}$Cl/$^{35}$Cl ratio is in agreement with the values infrerred for the early solar system. Our extended set of neutrino-nucleus interactions also allows us to exclude any further effects of the $ν$ process on stable nuclei and to quantify the effects on numerous, hitherto unconsidered radioactive nuclei, e.g., $^{36}$Cl, $^{72}$As, $^{84}$Rb, and $^{88}$Y.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that with modern, lower-energy supernova neutrino spectra, the neutrino process still produces significant amounts of \(^{11}\)B, \(^{138}\)La, and \(^{180}\)Ta, but the production of \(^{7}\)Li becomes negligible and the production of \(^{19}\)F depends more on progenitor mass and structure than on the neutrino process itself.

## Method

The authors calculated partial differential cross sections for neutrino-induced charged- and neutral-current reactions for all nuclei with charge numbers Z < 76 using Random Phase Approximation for nuclear excitation and statistical model codes (Modified Smoker and ABLA) for decay probabilities. They coupled these cross sections with a nuclear reaction network of 1988 species and the KEPLER hydrodynamics code to simulate explosive nucleosynthesis in solar-metallicity progenitor stars with initial masses between 13 and 30 solar masses, using neutrino spectra with average energies of 9 MeV for electron neutrinos and 12 MeV for other flavors, consistent with modern supernova simulations.

## Key numbers

* Average neutrino energies used for the "low energies" case: \(\langle E_{\nu_{\mathrm{e}}}\rangle = 9\) MeV, \(\langle E_{\bar{\nu}_{\mathrm{e}},\nu_{\mu,\tau}}\rangle = 12\) MeV, corresponding to temperatures \(T_{\nu_{\mathrm{e}}}=2.8\) MeV and \(T_{\bar{\nu}_{\mathrm{e}},\nu_{\mu,\tau}}=4.0\) MeV.
* Average neutrino energies used for the "high energies" case: \(\langle E_{\nu_{\mathrm{e}}}\rangle = 12\) MeV, \(\langle E_{\bar{\nu}_{\mathrm{e}}}\rangle = 15.8\) MeV, \(\langle E_{\nu_{\mu,\tau}}\rangle = 19\) MeV.
* Production factor for \(^{11}\)B with low energies: 0.31 (compared to 1.57 with high energies and 0.01 without neutrinos), averaged over progenitors weighted by a Salpeter initial mass function.
* Production factor for \(^{138}\)La with low energies: 0.46 (compared to 0.77 with high energies and 0.16 without neutrinos).
* Production factor for \(^{180}\)Ta with low energies: 0.49 (compared to 0.84 with high energies and 0.20 without neutrinos), assuming 35% survives in the long-lived isomeric state.
* Neutrino-induced reactions increase the yield of \(^{22}\)Na by 50% and contribute to the production of radioactive \(^{26}\)Al to a similar extent as other processes.
* Effects of the neutrino process on stable nuclei \(^{33}\)S, \(^{40}\)Ar, \(^{41}\)K, \(^{59}\)Co, and \(^{113}\)In are at the 10% level.
* Average yield of \(^{10}\)Be from the neutrino process: \(3.9 \times 10^{-11}\) solar masses for low energies and \(2.5 \times 10^{-10}\) solar masses for high energies.
* The \(^{11}\)B/\(^{10}\)B ratio from the neutrino process is approximately 100, compared to the solar system value of 4.
* The \(^{7}\)Li/\(^{6}\)Li ratio from the neutrino process is approximately 200, compared to the solar system value of 12.
* Explosion energy used for all models: \(1.2 \times 10^{51}\) erg.
* Total energy emitted as neutrinos: \(3 \times 10^{53}\) erg, distributed equally over six neutrino flavors with an exponential decay timescale \(\tau_{\nu}=3\) s.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutrino nucleosynthesis by Woosley, Hartmann, et al. (1990) and Heger, Kolbe, et al. (2005) by incorporating a complete set of neutrino-nucleus cross sections for all nuclei with Z < 76 and using lower neutrino energies consistent with modern supernova simulations by Huedepohl, Muller, et al. (2010) and Martinez-Pinedo, Fischer, et al. (2012). The improved cross sections and realistic neutrino spectra resolve the previous overproduction of \(^{11}\)B and show that the neutrino process can explain the Ne-E(L) component of meteoritic graphite grains through \(^{22}\)Na production, while also providing a \(^{36}\)Cl/\(^{35}\)Cl ratio consistent with early solar system values. The results enable future gamma-ray astronomy missions to potentially detect neutrino process contributions to radioactive nuclei such as \(^{22}\)Na and \(^{26}\)Al, and provide a benchmark for self-consistent three-dimensional supernova simulations that are currently under development.
