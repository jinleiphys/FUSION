# Applicability of Hydrodynamics in Hadronic Phase of Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The hadronic phase and its dynamics in relativistic heavy-ion collisions are topics of immense discussion. The hadronic phase contains various massive hadrons with an abundance of the lightest hadron, i.e., $π$-mesons (pions). In this paper, we consider that pions are in thermal equilibrium in the hadronic phase and use second-order viscous hydrodynamics for a medium of massive pions to obtain its expansion to the boundary of the kinetic freeze-out. We achieve the kinetic freeze-out boundary with the Knudsen number $Kn>1$ limit. When this condition is met, hydrodynamics expansion breaks down, and the mean free path becomes sufficiently large in comparison with the system size so that the particle yields are preserved. Further, we investigate the effect of the massive fluid on the resonance particle yields, including re-scattering and regeneration, along with the natural decay widths of the resonances. The resonances can play an essential role in determining the characteristics of the hadronic phase as they have sufficiently small lifetimes, which may be comparable to the hadronic phase lifetime. In the current study, we predict the hadronic phase lifetime, which is further used to determine the $K^*(892)^0/K$, $ϕ(1020)/K$, and $ρ(770)^0/π$ yield ratios at the kinetic freeze-out. We calculate these ratios as a function of charged particle multiplicity and transverse momentum and compare the findings with experimental data. Our calculations qualitatively agree with the experimental data, indicating a possible hydrodynamical evolution of the hadronic phase.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the hadronic phase in relativistic heavy-ion collisions can be described by second-order viscous hydrodynamics for a medium of massive pions, with the hydrodynamic description breaking down at the kinetic freeze-out boundary defined by Knudsen number \(Kn > 1\). The authors claim only qualitative agreement with experimental data, indicating a possible but not definitive hydrodynamical evolution of the hadronic phase.

## Method

The authors model the hadronic phase as a medium of massive pions in thermal equilibrium, starting from the chemical freeze-out temperature \(T_c = 156\) MeV. They derive and solve second-order (0+1D) viscous hydrodynamic equations for a massive pionic medium using the Muller-Israel-Stewart formalism, incorporating the temperature-dependent relaxation time obtained from the Boltzmann transport equation. The kinetic freeze-out boundary is determined by the condition \(Kn > 1\), and the evolution of resonance yields (\(\rho(770)^0\), \(K^*(892)^0\), \(\phi(1020)\)) is calculated using a kinetic formation model that includes both re-scattering and regeneration effects, with initial yields determined from the excluded volume hadron resonance gas (EVHRG) model.

## Key numbers

- Chemical freeze-out temperature taken as \(T_c = 156\) MeV, based on Wuppertal–Budapest Collaboration results (Borsanyi 2010, Borsanyi 2014)
- Pion mass assumed as \(m_{\pi} = 0.139\) GeV
- AdS/CFT lower bound for shear viscosity to entropy density ratio \(\eta/s = 1/(4\pi)\) used as input
- Relaxation time for massive pion gas fitted as \(\tau_\phi = A\exp(-\lambda T)\) with fit parameters \(A = 1362.73 \pm 212.198\) fm/c and \(\lambda = 32.7087 \pm 1.5375\) GeV\(^{-1}\)
- Resonance lifetimes: \(K^*(892)^0\) lifetime of 4.16 fm/\(c\); \(\phi(1020)\) lifetime of 46.3 fm/\(c\); \(f_2(1270)\) lifetime of 1.1 fm/\(c\)
- Regeneration cross-section parameterizations given for \(\rho(770)^0\) and \(K^*(892)^0\) as functions of \(T/T_c\)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Scaria 2022) which explored the Knudsen number in the excluded volume hadron resonance gas formalism, by explicitly deriving second-order hydrodynamic equations for a massive system. It contrasts with transport model approaches like UrQMD (Petersen 2008) and AMPT (Lin 2004), which do not adequately describe intrinsic medium properties such as viscosity (Song 2010, Demir 2008). The study enables a hydrodynamic framework for determining the hadronic phase lifetime and resonance yield ratios that can be compared with experimental data from the ALICE collaboration (ALICE 2019).
