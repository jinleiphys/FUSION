# How to Find the QCD Critical Point

> The event-by-event fluctuations in heavy ion collisions carry information about the thermodynamic properties of the hadronic system at the time of freeze-out. By studying these fluctuations as a function of varying control parameters, such as the collision energy, it is possible to learn much about the phase diagram of QCD. As a timely example, we stress the methods by which present experiments at the CERN SPS can locate the second order critical point at which a line of first order phase transitions ends. Those event-by-event signatures which are characteristic of freeze-out in the vicinity of the critical point will exhibit nonmonotonic dependence on control parameters. We focus on observables constructed from the multiplicity and transverse momenta of charged pions. We find good agreement between NA49 data and thermodynamic predictions for the noncritical fluctuations of such observables. We then analyze the effects due to the critical fluctuations of the sigma field. We estimate the size of these nonmonotonic effects which appear near the critical point, including restrictions imposed by finite size and finite time.

## Key claim

The paper argues that event-by-event fluctuations of pion multiplicity and mean transverse momentum in heavy ion collisions can serve as a nonmonotonic signature of the QCD critical point, provided that the freeze-out point can be moved toward and past the critical endpoint by varying control parameters such as collision energy. The authors hedge that they are more confident in their ability to describe how to find the critical point than in predicting where it is located.

## Method

The authors model the hadronic matter at freeze-out as a resonance gas in thermal equilibrium, calculating the variance of event-by-event fluctuations of pion multiplicity and mean transverse momentum. They then analyze how these fluctuations are modified when the system freezes out near the critical endpoint, using the coupling between pions and the sigma field (the order parameter of the chiral transition) and applying finite size and finite time scaling to estimate the magnitude of critical effects in heavy ion collisions.

## Key numbers

- For noncritical multiplicity fluctuations: ⟨(ΔN)²⟩/⟨N⟩ ≈ 1.5 from the resonance gas model, compared to NA49 data giving 2.008 ± 0.009 for central PbPb collisions at 160 AGeV with a 5% centrality cut (impact parameters b < 3.5 fm)
- For noncritical transverse momentum fluctuations: v_inc(p_T)/⟨p_T⟩ = 0.66 from the resonance gas model (before flow corrections), compared to NA49 data giving 0.749 ± 0.001
- The scaled event-by-event ratio √F = ⟨N⟩^{1/2} v_ebe(p_T)/v_inc(p_T) = 1.002 ± 0.002 from NA49 data
- The vacuum σππ coupling constant G ∼ 1900 MeV, estimated using m_σ = 600 MeV
- The chemical freeze-out parameters used in the model: T_ch = 170 MeV, μ_baryon = 200 MeV; thermal freeze-out temperature T_f = 120 MeV, μ_π = 60 MeV

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposal by Stephanov, Rajagopal, and Shuryak (1998) for observing the QCD critical endpoint through event-by-event fluctuations, and builds on the theoretical framework of Berges and Rajagopal (1997) and Halasz and Jackson (1997) who established the existence and universal properties of the critical point. The paper provides a detailed comparison with NA49 data from PbPb collisions at 160 AGeV, finding broad agreement with thermodynamic predictions for noncritical fluctuations, and then estimates that critical effects could produce nonmonotonic changes one to two orders of magnitude larger than current statistical errors. The methods enable experimental searches at the CERN SPS by comparing data at 40 AGeV, 160 AGeV, and intermediate energies, or at RHIC if the critical point lies at lower chemical potential.
