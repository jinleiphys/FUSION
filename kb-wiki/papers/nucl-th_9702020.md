# Emission of Gamma Rays by X-ray Electron-Nuclear Double Transitions

> This is a paper about induced gamma emission. The X-ray electron-nuclear double transitions (XENDT) are processes in which a transition effected by an inner atomic electron takes place simultaneously with a nuclear electromagnetic transition. We give expressions for the cross sections of electric and magnetic XENDTs of various multipole orders. We calculate the rate of deexcitation of isomeric nuclei induced by XENDTs for the case when the holes in the atomic shells are produced by incident ionizing electrons and find that the induced nuclear deexcitation rate becomes comparable to the natural decay rate for ionizing fluxes of the order of $10^{14}$ W cm$^{-2}$. We show that for E1 and M1 nuclear processes for which there is a matching between the electron and the nuclear transition energies, the XENDTs can be used to produce pulses of Mössbauer radiation, with yields of the order of $10^4$ Bq mA$^{-1}$.

# Emission of Gamma Rays by X-ray Electron-Nuclear Double Transitions

## Key claim

The paper claims that X-ray electron-nuclear double transitions (XENDTs) can induce deexcitation of isomeric nuclei at rates comparable to natural decay, but only for ionizing electron fluxes of order 10^14 W cm^-2; for E1 and M1 nuclear processes with matching electron and nuclear transition energies, XENDTs can produce pulses of Mössbauer radiation with yields of order 10^4 Bq mA^-1, though the efficiency of the upconversion process is very low.

## Method

The authors derived expressions for the cross sections of electric and magnetic XENDTs of arbitrary multipole order using a two-state Hamiltonian model with imaginary width terms to represent atomic deexcitation via X-ray and Auger emission. They calculated the total transition probability as proportional to the squared matrix element of the electron-nuclear interaction divided by the squared detuning between the total initial and final energies, then multiplied by the cross section for hole production by incident ionizing electrons (estimated using Gryziński's formula). For isomeric deexcitation, they considered both direct transitions from the isomeric state to a lower state and two-step transitions via an intermediate nuclear state, evaluating the required ionizing electron fluxes for which the induced rate equals the natural decay rate.

## Key numbers

- Induced nuclear deexcitation rate becomes comparable to natural decay rate for ionizing fluxes of order 10^14 W cm^-2 (for two-step deexcitation of 174mLu and 188mRe).
- For direct induced emission, lowest ionizing electron energy fluxes are of order 10^15 W cm^-2 (for 174mLu and 99mTc).
- Mössbauer activity per unit incident electron current is of order 10^4 Bq mA^-1 for E1 and M1 transitions with matching energies.
- The probability P_tot of the XENDT process is in the range 10^-7 to 10^-4 for the listed E1 and M1 transitions.
- The cross section of the XENDT process with respect to incident ionizing electrons is in the range 10^-30 to 10^-28 cm^2.
- The energy of interaction V_12 for E1 and M1 transitions is of order 1 eV.
- The detuning condition for listed nuclides requires |Δ| < 10 keV.
- The function g_i(1.6) = 0.109 is used for hole production cross section at E_el = 1.6 E_nl_J.

## Context

This work extends the concept of nuclear excitation by electron transition (NEET), first described by Morita (1973) and observed in 189Os, 235U, 237Np, and 197Au, to the case of induced deexcitation of isomeric nuclei and generation of Mössbauer radiation. The authors build on prior treatments of electron-nuclear double resonance and optical-frequency electron-nuclear double transitions, and use Weisskopf estimates for nuclear transition rates and atomic level widths from Keski-Rahkonen and Krause (1974). The results suggest that while direct gamma-ray amplification from isomers requires extremely high electron fluxes (10^14-10^15 W cm^-2), the production of pulsed Mössbauer sources via XENDTs is more feasible for specific nuclides like 119Sn, 161Dy, 189Os, and 193Ir, enabling time-resolved Mössbauer experiments.
