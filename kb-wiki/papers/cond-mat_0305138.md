# Theory of the Weakly Interacting Bose Gas

> We review recent advances in the theory of the three-dimensional dilute homogeneous Bose gas at zero and finite temperature. Effective field theory methods are used to formulate a systematic perturbative framework that can be used to calculate the properties of the system at T=0. The perturbative expansion of these properties is essentially an expansion in the gas parameter $\sqrt{na^3}$, where $a$ is the s-wave scattering length and $n$ is the number density. In particular, the leading quantum corrections to the ground state energy density, the condensate depletion, and long-wavelength collective excitations are rederived in and efficient and economical manner. We also discuss nonuniversal effects. These effects are higher-order corrections that depend on properties of the interatomic potential other than the scattering length, such as the effective range. We critically examine various approaches to the dilute Bose gas in equilibrium at finite temperature. These include the Bogoliubov approximation, the Popov approximation, the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approximation, the $Φ$-derivable approach, optimized perturbation theory, and renormalization group techniques. Finally, we review recent calculations of the critical temperature of the dilute Bose gas, which include 1/N-techniques, lattice simulations, self-consistent calculations, and variational perturbation theory.

## Key claim

This review argues that effective field theory methods provide a systematic and economical perturbative framework for calculating properties of the three-dimensional dilute homogeneous Bose gas, with the expansion controlled by the gas parameter $\sqrt{na^3}$. The paper critically examines various finite-temperature approximations and notes that the validity of perturbation theory at high temperature requires the condition $T\sqrt{g^3v^2}/gv^2 \ll 1$, a stricter requirement than the zero-temperature condition $\sqrt{na^3}\ll 1$.

## Method

The author uses effective field theory to formulate a perturbative expansion in the gas parameter $\sqrt{na^3}$ for the zero-temperature dilute Bose gas. At finite temperature, the paper critically examines and compares multiple approaches: the Bogoliubov approximation, Popov approximation, Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approximation, Phi-derivable approach, optimized perturbation theory, and renormalization group techniques. The review also discusses number-conserving approaches introduced by Giradeau and Arnowitt (1959) and later generalized by Morgan, which treat cubic and quartic terms in the Hamiltonian as perturbations.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy density: the Bogoliubov result is given in Eq. (ed) of the paper, and a second-order calculation reproduces the $na^3\log(na^3)$-term of Wu.
- Chemical potential at high temperature: $\mu = gv^2\left[1-{3\over8\pi^2}{T\over gv^2}\sqrt{2g^3v^2}\right]$, valid when $\beta gv^2\ll 1$.
- Zero-temperature expansion parameter: $\sqrt{na^3}\ll 1$, where $a$ is the s-wave scattering length and $n$ is the number density.
- High-temperature expansion parameter: $T\sqrt{g^3v^2}/gv^2\ll 1$, which has an extra factor of $T/gv^2$ compared to the zero-temperature case.
- Phonon spectrum: the Beliaev results for the phonon spectrum (Eq. pre) and damping rate (Eq. imw) are reproduced by number-conserving approaches.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical understanding of the dilute Bose gas beyond the foundational work of Bogoliubov (1947) and Beliaev (1958), and critically examines finite-temperature approximations developed by Popov, Griffin, and others. The paper enables future work by providing a systematic framework for calculating higher-order corrections, including nonuniversal effects that depend on properties of the interatomic potential beyond the scattering length (such as the effective range), and by reviewing recent calculations of the critical temperature using 1/N-techniques, lattice simulations (Arnold and Moore, 2001), self-consistent calculations, and variational perturbation theory.
