# Influence of Dark Matter on Hybrid and Twin Stars

> We investigate the impact of dark matter (DM) on hybrid and twin stars within a two-fluid framework, where DM and normal matter interact only through gravity. A self-interacting fermionic DM model is considered, while for the nuclear and quark matter we employ relativistic mean-field models and the constant sound-speed parametrization, respectively. Solving the two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations over a five-dimensional parameter space spanning the quark-matter transition pressure $p_t$, energy density jump $Δε$, sound speed $C_s^2$, DM particle mass $m_χ$, and DM fraction $f_χ$, we classify the resulting configurations into hybrid stars and four twin star categories and map the onset properties of the quark phase. The effect of DM on the hybrid and twin star population is regime-dependent: the DM particle mass determines whether DM forms an extended halo or a compact core, while the DM fraction sets the magnitude of the effect. In the halo regime, DM enhances quark matter formation and increases the number of hybrid and twin stars; in the core regime, it suppresses them. DM also stabilizes hybrid and twin configurations that are unstable in the pure-baryonic case and systematically pushes the nucleon-to-quark transition to higher densities, with the onset pressure, mass, and radius all rising with the DM fraction, potentially hiding quark matter from current neutron star observations.

## Key claim

Dark matter (DM) influences hybrid and twin star populations through two opposite mechanisms selected by DM particle mass: light DM (0.5 GeV) forms an extended halo that enhances quark matter formation and increases the number of hybrid and twin stars, while heavy DM (1.0 GeV) forms a compact core that suppresses them, with the DM fraction controlling the magnitude of the effect.

## Method

The authors solved the two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations over a five-dimensional parameter space spanning quark-matter transition pressure, energy density jump, sound speed, DM particle mass, and DM fraction. The hadronic sector was modeled with relativistic mean-field equations of state (NITR-1 and DD2), the quark sector with the constant sound-speed parametrization, and the DM sector with a self-interacting fermionic model. The resulting configurations were classified into hybrid stars and four twin star categories based on maximum mass thresholds, and the onset properties of the quark phase were mapped across the parameter space.

## Key numbers

- DM particle masses considered: 0.5 GeV (halo regime) and 1.0 GeV (core regime)
- DM fractions explored: 0.1, 0.2, and 0.3
- Quark sound speeds: 0.6, 0.8, and 1.0
- Parameter grid: 200 points each for transition pressure (10 to 250 MeV/fm³) and energy density jump (100 to 500 MeV/fm³), yielding 40,000 grid points
- Onset masses for quark core formation range from approximately 0.6 solar masses (low transition pressure) to approximately 2.0 solar masses (high transition pressure)
- Onset radii range from approximately 20 km (high transition pressure) to approximately 45-50 km (low transition pressure)
- At C_s² = 1.0 and f_χ = 0.3, the twin star population reaches over 2000 configurations
- Maximum masses for nuclear models: NITR-1 reaches 2.34 solar masses, DD2 reaches 2.42 solar masses

## Context

This work extends previous studies of hybrid and twin stars by Christian (2018) and Montana (2019) by incorporating DM as a gravitationally coupled second fluid, and builds on two-fluid DM treatments by Liu (2024) and Rutherford (2023). It goes beyond earlier DM-quark matter studies by Lenzi (2023) and Biesdorf (2025), which used single-fluid approaches or specific quark models, by systematically mapping how DM reshapes the population of hybrid and twin stars across the quark-matter parameter space. The results enable future observational constraints on DM properties through neutron star observations, particularly showing that DM can push the nucleon-to-quark transition to higher densities, potentially hiding quark matter from current observations.
