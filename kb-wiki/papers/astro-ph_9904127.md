# Impact of internal heating on the thermal evolution of neutron stars

> The impact of various competing heating processes on the thermal evolution of neutron stars is investigated. We show that internal heating leads to significantly enhanced surface temperatures for pulsars of middle and old age. The heating due to thermal creep of pinned vortices and due to outward motion of proton vortices in the interior of the star leads to a better agreement with the observed data in the case of enhanced cooling. The strong pinning models are ruled out by a comparison with the cooling data on the old pulsars. For millisecond pulsars, the heating due to thermal creep of pinned vortices and chemical heating of the core have the largest impact on the surface temperatures. The angular dependence of the heating rates require two dimensional cooling simulations in general. Such a simulation is performed for a selected case in order to check the applicability of one-dimensional codes used in the past.

## Key claim

Internal heating from vortex creep and proton vortex motion significantly enhances surface temperatures of middle-aged and old pulsars, but only for specific pinning models; strong pinning models are ruled out by comparison with cooling data on old pulsars, and the agreement with observations is conditional on the choice of enhanced cooling scenarios.

## Method

The authors performed fully relativistic, one-dimensional thermal evolution simulations of neutron stars, incorporating multiple heating processes including thermal creep of pinned neutron vortices, corotating vortex motion, electron-vortex scattering in the superfluid core, vortex decay at the crust-core boundary, crust cracking, and chemical heating from non-equilibrium composition. They used both relativistic and non-relativistic equations of state for superdense matter and compared two pinning models (Epstein-Baym and Pizzochero-Vinas-Barranco). A two-dimensional cooling simulation was performed for one selected case to verify the reliability of the one-dimensional approximation.

## Key numbers

- The typical pinning energy in the Epstein-Baym (EB) model is approximately 10 MeV at densities above 1.5 × 10^13 g cm^-3, while in the Pizzochero-Vinas-Barranco (PVB) model it is approximately 1 MeV at the same densities.
- The drag coefficient η_0 in the corotating regime is estimated as ~10^5 g cm^-1 s^-1 at density ρ_s = 10^13 g cm^-3, giving tan θ_d ~ 5 × 10^-6.
- The next-to-leading order drag coefficient η_1 is estimated as ~10^10 g cm^-1 s^-1 at density ρ_s ~ 10^13 g cm^-3, giving tan θ_d ~ 1.
- The number of proton vortices per neutron vortex is of order n ≥ 10^12 for a magnetic field B ~ 10^12 G and neutron vortex lattice constant d_n ~ 10^-3 cm.
- The fixed upper limit for the vortex decay heating parameter g is 10^28.
- The central density of a rotating neutron star can increase by up to 20% as the star spins down.
- The critical shear angle θ_c for an ideal Coulomb lattice is of order 10^-1 to 10^-2, but with impurities or defects it is reduced to roughly 10^-4 to 10^-5.

## Context

This work extends earlier cooling simulations by Van Riper (1991), who treated heating in general phenomenological terms, and by individual studies such as Shibazaki (1989), Cheng (1992), Sedrakian (1993), and Reisenegger (1995) that each focused on only a few heating processes. By comparing multiple heating mechanisms within a single, consistent simulation framework, the authors enable future studies to determine which heating processes dominate for different pulsar ages and spin-down rates, and they demonstrate that one-dimensional cooling codes are generally adequate despite the azimuthal asymmetry of heating rates.
