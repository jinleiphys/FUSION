# Electromagnetic Dissociation of Nuclei in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Large discrepancies have been observed between measured Electromagnetic Dissociation(ED) cross sections and the predictions of the semiclassical Weizäcker-Williams-Fermi(WWF) method. In this paper, the validity of the semiclassical approximation is examined. The total cross section for electromagnetic excitation of a nuclear target by a spinless projectile is calculated in first Born approximation, neglecting recoil. The final result is expressed in terms of correlation functions and convoluted densities in configuration space. The result agrees with the WWF approximation to leading order(unretarded electric dipole approximation), but the method allows an analytic evaluation of the cutoff, which is determined by the details of the electric dipole transition charge density. Using the Goldhaber-Teller model of that density, and uniform charge densities for both projectile and target, the cutoff is determined for the total cross section in the nonrelativistic limit, and found to be smaller than values currently used for ED calculations. In addition, cross sections are calculated using a phenomenological momentum space cutoff designed to model final state interactions. For moderate projectile energies, the calculated ED cross section is found to be smaller than the semiclassical result, in qualitative agreement with experiment.

## Key claim

The first-Born-approximation quantum calculation of electromagnetic dissociation (ED) cross sections agrees with the semiclassical Weizäcker-Williams-Fermi (WWF) method only in the asymptotic high-energy limit (unretarded electric dipole approximation); at moderate projectile energies (gamma = 2-3) the quantum E1 cross section is suppressed by 20-25 percent relative to the semiclassical result, resolving observed discrepancies with single-neutron removal data.

## Method

The total cross section for electromagnetic excitation of a nuclear target by a spinless projectile was calculated in first Born approximation, neglecting recoil, and expressed in terms of correlation functions and convoluted densities in configuration space. The Goldhaber-Teller model was used for electric dipole transition densities, with uniform charge densities assumed for both projectile and target, and the cutoff parameter was evaluated analytically in the nonrelativistic limit. A phenomenological momentum-space cutoff (q_max approximately 1/b_min) was introduced to model final-state strong interactions, and the resulting cross sections were compared to the semiclassical WWF results and to experimental single-neutron removal data.

## Key numbers

- The quantum E1 cross section is enhanced by about 10 percent relative to the classical result at high projectile energies (gamma large).
- The E2 cross section remains a factor of two larger than the classical result at gamma = 100.
- For gamma = 2-3, the quantum E1 cross section is suppressed by 20-25 percent relative to the semiclassical result, matching the discrepancy in single-neutron removal data for 238U on 197Au.
- At energies of 5 GeV/nucleon and higher (gamma > 5), the WWF method is a good approximation for the total E1 cross section when the minimum impact parameter is properly chosen.
- For low projectile energies (gamma = 2), discrepancies as large as 20 percent persist in the E1 cross section even after adjusting b_min.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical Weizäcker-Williams-Fermi method (Fermi, 1924; von Weizäcker and Williams, 1934) by providing a quantum-mechanical first-Born-approximation calculation that allows analytic evaluation of the impact-parameter cutoff, which had previously been chosen phenomenologically (e.g., by Benesh, Vary, and others in 1989-1992). The results resolve large discrepancies between measured ED cross sections and WWF predictions observed in target fragmentation experiments (reported by several groups in the late 1980s and early 1990s). The approach enables more reliable calculations of electromagnetic dissociation backgrounds at RHIC and improved analysis of single-neutron removal data, particularly for heavy projectiles.
