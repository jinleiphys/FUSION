# A Bayesian Analysis of Nuclear Deformation Properties with Skyrme Energy Functionals

> In spite of numerous scientific and practical applications, there is still no comprehensive theoretical description of the nuclear fission process based solely on protons, neutrons and their interactions. The most advanced simulations of fission are currently carried out within nuclear density functional theory (DFT). In spite of being fully quantum-mechanical and rooted in the theory of nuclear forces, DFT still depends on a dozen or so parameters characterizing the energy functional. Calibrating these parameters on experimental data results in uncertainties that must be quantified for applications. This task is very challenging because of the high computational cost of DFT calculations for fission. In this paper, we use Gaussian processes to build emulators of DFT models in order to quantify and propagate statistical uncertainties of theoretical predictions for a range of nuclear deformations relevant to describing the fission process.

# A Bayesian Analysis of Nuclear Deformation Properties with Skyrme Energy Functionals

## Key claim

Gaussian process emulators can reliably reproduce potential energy curves of heavy nuclei for fission studies, but their accuracy degrades at points where triaxiality introduces discontinuities in the self-consistent calculations, and they may not be the best tool for emulating scission characteristics. The authors demonstrate this for the specific case of 240Pu using the UNEDF1HFB functional, finding that emulation errors reach up to 0.68 MeV in regions where triaxial deformation appears.

## Method

The authors used the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) theory with a Skyrme energy density functional to compute one-dimensional potential energy curves of 240Pu as a function of the axial quadrupole moment. They reduced the 12-parameter UNEDF1HFB functional to 6 varying coupling constants by fixing bulk nuclear matter parameters, sampled the 6-dimensional hypercube with Latin Hypercube Sampling to generate 70 training runs, and built a Gaussian Stochastic Process (GaSP) emulator using the Matern 5/2 covariance structure. Bayesian inference with a truncated normal likelihood and Delayed Rejection Adaptive Metropolis MCMC sampling was then applied to obtain posterior distributions of the parameters conditioned on experimental fission isomer excitation energy and fission barrier heights.

## Key numbers

- Emulation error |epsilon_Emul| <= 0.68 MeV, with largest errors occurring at local maxima and minima of the potential energy curve
- Training set consisted of N=70 samples of the 6-dimensional parameter space
- The scission point location was reproduced within about 5-10 barns on average (leave-one-out prediction error)
- The number of particles in the heavy fragment at scission was reproduced to within about half a particle on average
- MCMC chain ran for 7.5 million samples, thinned to 100,000 uncorrelated posterior samples
- The weighted average experimental values used for calibration were: E_FI = 2.3500 MeV (fission isomer excitation energy), E_A = 5.8975 MeV (inner barrier), E_B = 5.2825 MeV (outer barrier)

## Context

This work extends earlier uncertainty quantification studies in nuclear DFT that focused on ground-state properties such as masses (Gao 2013, Goriely 2014, Utama 2016, Haverinen 2017), drip-line properties (Neufcourt 2018, 2019), or neutron skin (Kortelainen 2013, Reinhard 2013), and specifically builds on the work of McDonnell (2015) on fission barriers. The paper demonstrates that Gaussian process emulation can be applied to fission pathways and scission configurations, but reveals that discontinuities from triaxiality and the model-dependent nature of fission barrier extraction from cross-section data (Bjornholm and Lynn 1980) introduce significant challenges. This enables future work on higher-dimensional collective spaces for fission fragment distributions, though the authors caution that emulation uncertainties will likely increase with the dimensionality of the collective space.
