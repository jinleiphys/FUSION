# Contrasting N Delta and N N parity-violating asymmetries in nuclei

> Several perspectives that can be opened by studying the electroexcitation of the delta via parity-violating electron scattering from nuclei are examined, working within the context of the relativistic Fermi gas model. A strong enhancement of the left-right asymmetry in the delta sector compared with that in the quasi-elastic regime is found and the potential to find clear signatures for the axial-vector contributions of the nucleon-to-delta transition to the asymmetry identified at specific low momentum transfer kinematics. Possibilities of probing the deformation of the delta are also explored, and using both the proton and nuclei as targets, the abilitiy to study the asymmetry on neutrons is studied.

# Contrasting N → Δ and N → N parity-violating asymmetries in nuclei

## Key claim

Within the relativistic Fermi gas model, the parity-violating left-right asymmetry in the Δ (1232) excitation region is strongly enhanced compared to the quasi-elastic regime at small electron scattering angles, because the Δ sector is purely isovector and thus avoids the isoscalar-isovector cancellation that suppresses the asymmetry in the quasi-elastic peak. The axial-vector contributions of the nucleon-to-Δ transition can be identified at low momentum transfer kinematics, but the authors caution that the study is confined to the relativistic Fermi gas model and does not include NN or NΔ correlations, meson-exchange currents, or non-resonant pionic background contributions.

## Method

The authors computed parity-conserving (electromagnetic) and parity-violating (weak neutral current) response functions for both the N → Δ and N → N (quasi-elastic) channels within the relativistic Fermi gas model using the Δ-hole polarization propagator method. They employed the γNΔ vertices of Devenish et al. (1976) and parametrized the N → Δ form factors using a dipole form with parameters from the constituent quark model and the Adler scaling hypothesis (1968). The asymmetry was evaluated for several momentum transfers (q = 350, 520, and 1000 MeV/c) and electron scattering angles, both with the Δ treated as a stable particle and with a finite width (constant Γ = 120 MeV and energy-dependent width).

## Key numbers

- The asymmetry in the Δ sector at q = 350 MeV/c and θ = 10° shows an increase of about 10% due to the axial contribution, relative to a flat baseline.
- The vector form factor at the origin for the N → Δ magnetic transition is G_M^(v)(0) = 2.97; for the electric transition G_E^(v)(0) = -0.03; for the Coulomb transition G_C^(v)(0) = -0.44.
- The axial electric form factor at the origin is G_E^(a)(0) = 2.22; the axial magnetic and Coulomb form factors at the origin are set to zero.
- The Fermi momentum used in the calculations is k_F = 220 MeV/c, corresponding approximately to the density of ¹²C.
- The longitudinal PC N → Δ Coulomb sum rule saturates at large momentum transfer of about 2.5 GeV/c to a value set by the Fermi momentum.
- The normalization constant A₀ for the asymmetry is approximately 6.5 × 10⁻⁴.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of parity-violating asymmetry on the single proton by Mukhopadhyay et al. (1998) to nuclear targets using the relativistic Fermi gas model. The authors build on their own previous calculation of the vector-sector N → Δ responses (Amore et al., 1999) and on the quasi-elastic parity-violating formalism of Donnelly et al. (1992). The results suggest that measurements at TJNAF (CEBAF) could achieve precision in the Δ region close to or better than that attainable in the quasi-elastic region, and that the asymmetry in the Δ sector is largely insensitive to Fermi motion, remaining essentially equal to the single-particle asymmetry over a wide range of k_F. The work opens the possibility of extracting the elusive axial N → Δ response and probing the deformation of the Δ, but the authors note that future work must assess the role of NN and NΔ correlations, meson-exchange currents, and non-resonant pionic background contributions.
