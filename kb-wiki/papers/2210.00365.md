# Efficient Solutions of Fermionic Systems using Artificial Neural Networks

> We discuss differences and similarities between variational Monte Carlo approaches that use conventional and artificial neural network parameterizations of the ground-state wave function for systems of fermions. We focus on a relatively shallow neural-network architectures, the so called restricted Boltzmann machine, and discuss unsupervised learning algorithms that are suitable to model complicated many-body correlations. We analyze the strengths and weaknesses of conventional and neural-network wave functions by solving various circular quantum-dots systems. Results for up to 90 electrons are presented and particular emphasis is placed on how to efficiently implement these methods on homogeneous and heterogeneous high-performance computing facilities.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for circular quantum-dot systems, a variational Monte Carlo approach using a restricted Boltzmann machine (RBM) combined with a Padé-Jastrow correlation factor achieves ground-state energies within 0.2% of diffusion Monte Carlo reference values for small systems, but the simpler Slater-Jastrow ansatz outperforms the neural-network-based ansatz for larger systems (above approximately 12 electrons), likely due to the increased optimization difficulty of the RBM's larger parameter space.

## Method

The authors performed variational Monte Carlo (VMC) calculations for two-dimensional circular quantum dots with up to 90 electrons, comparing four trial wave function ansätze: a plain Slater determinant (reference), a Slater-Jastrow wave function, an RBM multiplied by a Slater determinant, and an RBM with a Padé-Jastrow factor multiplied by a Slater determinant. The RBM used Gaussian visible nodes with fixed widths of σ=1/√ω and binary hidden nodes, with the marginal distribution derived from summing over hidden node configurations. The wave functions were optimized by minimizing the variational energy using gradient-based methods, with parallel sampling across MPI processes and analytical expressions for gradients and Laplacians of the log-wave function to reduce computational cost.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energy for N=2 electrons at ω=1/6: RBM+PJ gave 0.666665(1) Hartree, compared to the semi-analytical value of 2/3 Hartree
- Ground-state energy for N=90 electrons at ω=0.1: Slater-Jastrow gave 394.621(4) Hartree, compared to the reference value of 401.19(2) Hartree
- Relative errors for RBM+PJ and Slater-Jastrow ansätze with respect to DMC calculations were less than 0.2% for all systems studied
- For N=30 electrons at ω=0.5 with sorted inputs, RBM+PJ achieved 187.311(1) Hartree, lower than the Slater-Jastrow energy of 187.325(3) Hartree
- The RBM ansatz with H=6 hidden nodes produced ground-state energies lower than the reference for low-frequency dots (ω<0.28) but failed for large high-frequency dots (N>6, ω>0.28)
- CPU time per iteration scaled with system size, with RBM+PJ and Slater-Jastrow being the most computationally intensive due to the Padé-Jastrow factor requiring all electron-electron distances

## Context

This work extends the neural-network quantum state approach pioneered by Carleo and Troyer (2017) for fermionic systems, applying it specifically to circular quantum dots that exhibit shell structures and magic numbers similar to atoms and nuclei. The paper compares RBM-based wave functions against traditional Slater-Jastrow ansätze and references diffusion Monte Carlo results from Hogberget (2013) and semi-analytical results from Taut (1994). The findings suggest that while neural-network wave functions can match or slightly exceed traditional methods for small systems, the increased parameter count makes optimization challenging for larger systems, pointing toward the need for more sophisticated optimization algorithms or alternative neural-network architectures for scalable fermionic simulations.
