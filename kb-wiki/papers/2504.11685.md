# Quantum simulations of nuclear resonances with variational methods

> The many-body nature of nuclear physics problems poses significant computational challenges. These challenges become even more pronounced when studying the resonance states of nuclear systems, which are governed by the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian. Quantum computing, particularly for quantum many-body systems, offers a promising alternative, especially within the constraints of current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices. This work aims to simulate nuclear resonances using quantum algorithms by developing a variational framework compatible with non-Hermitian Hamiltonians and implementing it fully on a quantum simulator. We employ the complex scaling technique to extract resonance positions classically and adapt it for quantum simulations using a two-step algorithm. First, we transform the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian into a Hermitian form by using the energy variance as a cost function within a variational framework. Second, we perform theta-trajectory calculations to determine optimal resonance positions in the complex energy plane. To address resource constraints on NISQ devices, we utilize Gray Code (GC) encoding to reduce qubit requirements. We first validate our approach using a schematic potential model that mimics a nuclear potential, successfully reproducing known resonance energies with high fidelity. We then extend the method to a more realistic alpha-alpha nuclear potential and compute the resonance energies with a basis size of 16, using only four qubits. This study demonstrates, for the first time, that the complete theta-trajectory method can be implemented on a quantum computer without relying on any classical input beyond the Hamiltonian. The results establish a scalable and efficient quantum framework for simulating resonance phenomena in nuclear systems. This work represents a significant step toward quantum simulations of open quantum systems.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate for the first time that the complete theta-trajectory method for extracting optimal resonance positions can be implemented on a quantum computer without any classical input beyond the Hamiltonian itself, using a variational framework that handles non-Hermitian Hamiltonians via energy variance minimization.

## Method

The authors employ the complex scaling method (CSM), rotating coordinates by an angle theta to transform divergent resonance wave functions into square-integrable forms, then construct a Hermitian operator from the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian using the energy variance as a cost function. They implement a two-step variational quantum algorithm: first, they minimize the expectation value of the Hermitianized operator to find complex eigenvalues for a fixed theta, and second, they perform theta-trajectory calculations by varying theta in small steps to identify the optimal resonance position where the trajectory forms a loop, kink, or slowdown. The Hamiltonian is mapped to qubits using Gray Code (GC) encoding to reduce qubit requirements, and the wave function is expanded in either Gaussian basis (for the schematic potential) or harmonic oscillator basis (for the alpha-alpha potential).

## Key numbers

- For the schematic potential (J=0+ and 1- states), the method reproduces known resonance energies with high fidelity using a Gaussian basis; specific numerical values for the schematic potential resonances are not given in the truncated text.
- For the alpha-alpha scattering potential, the first 2+ (D-wave) resonance state is located at 2.88 - i0.62 MeV, and the first 4+ (G-wave) resonance state is found at 11.78 - i1.78 MeV.
- The alpha-alpha potential parameters reproduce the 0+ resonance at 92.12 +/- 0.05 keV with a measured width of 6.8 +/- 1.7 eV.
- The calculation uses a basis size of N=16, mapped to only four qubits using Gray Code encoding.
- The alpha-alpha potential produces three redundant bound states: two 0+ states at -72.7 MeV and -25.8 MeV, and one 2+ state at -22.2 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the variational method for non-Hermitian Hamiltonians proposed by Zhao et al. (2023) and the complex scaling method previously applied to atomic and nuclear systems by Bian et al. (2021) and Zhang et al. (2024), where the rotation angle theta was fixed a priori rather than determined dynamically. The authors improve upon the linear combination of unitaries (LCU) approach used in those prior works, which was restricted to very small systems (typically two qubits) due to exponential resource growth, and upon the variational quantum universal eigensolver (VQUE) of Zhao et al. (2023), which is also highly resource-intensive. This work enables scalable quantum simulations of resonance phenomena in nuclear, atomic, and molecular physics on NISQ devices, and lays the foundation for future investigations into open quantum systems.
