# The sensitivity of r-process nucleosynthesis to the properties of neutron-rich nuclei

> About half of the heavy elements in the Solar System were created by rapid neutron capture, or r-process, nucleosynthesis. In the r-process, heavy elements are built up via a sequence of neutron captures and beta decays in which an intense neutron flux pushes material out towards the neutron drip line. The nuclear network simulations used to test potential astrophysical scenarios for the r-process therefore require nuclear physics data (masses, beta decay lifetimes, neutron capture rates, fission probabilities) for thousands of nuclei far from stability. Only a small fraction of this data has been experimentally measured. Here we discuss recent sensitivity studies that aim to determine the nuclei whose properties are most crucial for r-process calculations.

# The sensitivity of r-process nucleosynthesis to the properties of neutron-rich nuclei

## Key claim

Sensitivity studies that vary individual nuclear masses (rather than neutron separation energies) more accurately gauge the potential importance of individual mass measurements for r-process abundance patterns, but the nuclei identified as most important shift depending on the astrophysical conditions (temperature, entropy, and electron fraction) assumed in the baseline simulation.

## Method

The authors performed r-process network simulations using a dedicated code from Surman (2001), starting from a baseline simulation that reproduces the solar abundance pattern for A > 120. They varied individual nuclear masses by ±1 MeV (or beta decay rates by a factor of 10) and repeated the simulation, quantifying the global impact on the final abundance pattern using a sensitivity measure F = 100 × Σ_A |X_baseline(A) − X(A)|. The study used three different mass models (FRDM from Moller (1995), DZ from Duflo (1995), and HFB-21 from Goriely (2010)) and three different sets of astrophysical conditions parameterized as adiabatic winds with varying entropy and electron fraction.

## Key numbers

- Sensitivity measure F defined as F = 100 × Σ_A |X_baseline(A) − X(A)|, where X(A) are mass fractions summing to 1.
- Binding energy variations of ±1 MeV were applied to individual nuclear masses in the sensitivity study.
- Beta decay rate variations of a factor of 10 were applied in the beta decay sensitivity study.
- Astrophysical wind parameters used: ρ(0) = 1.58 × 10⁸ g/cm³, τ_dyn = 80 ms, and Δ = 10 ms.
- Three entropy/electron fraction combinations tested: s/k = 10 with Y_e = 0.150; s/k = 100 with Y_e = 0.250; s/k = 200 with Y_e = 0.300.

## Context

This work extends the neutron separation energy sensitivity study of Brett (2012) by varying individual masses instead of separation energies, revealing that the odd-even sensitivity pattern seen in the earlier study was an artifact of the method. It also builds on prior sensitivity studies for neutron capture rates (Beun 2008; Surman 2009, 2011; Mumpower 2012) and beta decay rates (Cass 2012). The results show that many of the same nuclei at closed shells and in the rare earth region near stability appear important across different astrophysical conditions, and these nuclei are within reach of current radioactive beam experiments such as CARIBU and FRIB.
