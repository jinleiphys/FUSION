# Spin and pseudospin symmetries and the equivalent spectra of relativistic spin-1/2 and spin-0 particles

> We show that the conditions which originate the spin and pseudospin symmetries in the Dirac equation are the same that produce equivalent energy spectra of relativistic spin-1/2 and spin-0 particles in the presence of vector and scalar potentials. The conclusions do not depend on the particular shapes of the potentials and can be important in different fields of physics. When both scalar and vector potentials are spherical, these conditions for isospectrality imply that the spin-orbit and Darwin terms of either the upper component or the lower component of the Dirac spinor vanish, making it equivalent, as far as energy is concerned, to a spin-0 state. In this case, besides energy, a scalar particle will also have the same orbital angular momentum as the (conserved) orbital angular momentum of either the upper or lower component of the corresponding spin-1/2 particle. We point out a few possible applications of this result.

# Spin and pseudospin symmetries and the equivalent spectra of relativistic spin-1/2 and spin-0 particles

## Key claim

The conditions that produce spin and pseudospin symmetries in the Dirac equation (vector and scalar potentials of equal magnitude, up to a constant) are the same conditions that make the energy spectrum of a relativistic spin-1/2 particle identical to that of a spin-0 particle subject to the same potentials, regardless of the specific shapes of those potentials. This equivalence holds for both bound and scattering states, provided the spin-orbit and Darwin terms vanish for either the upper or lower component of the Dirac spinor.

## Method

The authors start from the time-independent Dirac equation with scalar potential V_s and vector potential V_v, decompose the four-spinor into upper and lower two-component spinors, and derive second-order equations for each component. They show that when the gradient of Delta = V_v - V_s (or Sigma = V_v + V_s) vanishes, the second-order equation for the upper (or lower) component reduces exactly to the Klein-Gordon equation for a spin-0 particle with the same potentials. For spherical potentials, they analyze the spin-orbit and Darwin terms explicitly and demonstrate that the vanishing of Delta' or Sigma' eliminates these terms, making the orbital angular momentum of the corresponding component a good quantum number through the generators of the spin or pseudospin SU(2) symmetries.

## Key numbers

- The condition for isospectrality is V_v = V_s + C_Delta or V_v = -V_s + C_Sigma, where C_Delta and C_Sigma are arbitrary constants (no specific numerical values given).
- For spherical potentials, the spin-orbit term in the second-order equation for the upper component is proportional to (2/r) * Delta' * (L dot S), and for the lower component proportional to (2/r) * Sigma' * (L dot S), where Delta' and Sigma' are radial derivatives.
- The Casimir operator L^2 applied to the Dirac spinor yields eigenvalue hbar^2 * l(l+1) when Delta' = 0, confirming that l is a good quantum number.
- The pseudospin symmetry generator yields eigenvalue hbar^2 * tilde_l(tilde_l+1) when Sigma' = 0, making tilde_l a good quantum number.

## Context

This work extends the known spin and pseudospin symmetries of the Dirac equation, first identified by Bell and Ruegg (1975) and later developed by Ginocchio (1997, 2005), by showing that these symmetries imply spectral equivalence with spin-0 particles. The result is relevant for relativistic quark models used in studying quark-hadron duality, where the spin structure of particles can sometimes be neglected (Jeschonnek, 2000). The authors note that in nuclear mean-field theories, scalar and vector potentials have opposite signs but similar magnitudes, while in heavy-light meson systems (D- or B-mesons) they have the same sign and similar strengths, making these conditions physically realizable. The work enables simplified calculations where spin-1/2 particles can be replaced by spin-0 particles in systems where the spin-orbit and Darwin terms vanish, and suggests experimental signatures for detecting Lorentz scalar fields of comparable magnitude to vector fields.
