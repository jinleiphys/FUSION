# Stellar Sources for Heavy r-Process Nuclei

> (Abridged) The stellar sites and the complete mechanism of r-process nucleosynthesis are still unresolved issues. From consideration of the observed abundances in metal-poor stars, it is proposed that the production of the heavy r-process nuclei (with mass numbers A>130) is not related to the production of the Fe group elements or of the elements with lower atomic numbers: Na, Mg, Al, Si, Ca, Sc, and Ti. This requires that the production of the heavy r-nuclei not occur in supernovae with extended shell structure, but be associated with either bare neutron stars or SNe II in the range of 8-10 solar masses. From the observations of stars with [Fe/H]=-3 but with high abundances of r-elements, it is clear that these r-process enrichments cannot represent the composition of the interstellar medium from which the stars were formed, but must represent very local contamination from binary companions. Further evidence for very high enrichments of s-process elements in metal-poor stars also requires binary systems for explanation. We propose that the accretion-induced collapse (AIC) of a white dwarf into a neutron star in a binary system may be associated with the production of the heavy r-nuclei and may provide occasional coupling of high r-process and high s-process enrichments in the envelopes of low-mass stars with low [Fe/H]. If we assume that the bulk of the heavy r-nuclei are produced in AIC events, then these events would have produced about 1.6 billion neutron stars in the Galaxy.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the production of heavy r-process nuclei (mass numbers A > 130) is decoupled from the production of elements from Na to Zn (including Fe and the alpha-elements), and that accretion-induced collapse (AIC) of a white dwarf into a neutron star in a binary system may be the primary stellar source for these nuclei, rather than Type II supernovae with extended shell structure. The authors hedge that this proposal is based on observational requirements from metal-poor star abundances and that the conditions in the neutrino-driven wind from AIC events are assumed to be suitable for producing heavy r-nuclei, pending further theoretical study.

## Method

The authors analyzed elemental abundance data from metal-poor halo stars, particularly those with [Fe/H] ~ -3, comparing the abundances of heavy r-process elements (Ba and above) with those of elements from Na to Zn. They used the observed scatter in Eu and Ba abundances relative to Fe, the solar r-process abundance pattern as a reference, and the Ba/Eu ratio to distinguish r-process from s-process contributions. From these observations, they inferred that the stellar sources for heavy r-nuclei must produce essentially bare neutron stars, leading them to evaluate low-mass Type II supernovae (8-10 solar masses) and AIC events in binary systems as candidate sites.

## Key numbers

- The transition at [Fe/H] ~ -3 corresponds to an increase by a factor of 10 to 20 in the relative production of Ba with respect to Fe from [Fe/H] < -3 to [Fe/H] > -3.
- For the three stars HD 115444, HD 122563, and CS 31082-001 at [Fe/H] ~ -3, the abundances of elements from Na to Zn are constant within ~0.3 dex in log epsilon, while heavy r-elements show a range of ~2 dex in log epsilon.
- The number of events contributing to heavy r-element abundances in these stars corresponds to n ~ 1-50, where n is the number of events per standard reference mass of interstellar medium.
- The star HE 2148-1247 has log epsilon(Eu) = 0.17, corresponding to n ~ 460, with [Fe/H] = -2.3.
- The solar r-process Ba/Eu ratio is (Ba/Eu)_odot,r approximately 9, while the solar main s-component Ba/Eu ratio is (Ba/Eu)_odot,s approximately 650.
- For the mixture in HE 2148-1247, beta_s(Eu) approximately 0.14, corresponding to beta_r(Ba) = 0.077.
- If AIC events are the major source for heavy r-nuclei, they would have produced approximately 1.6 x 10^9 neutron stars in the Galaxy, with a Galactic rate of approximately (3 yr)^{-1}.
- The mass range 8-10 solar masses corresponds to approximately 28% of all stars with M ~ 8-60 solar masses for a Salpeter IMF.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier phenomenological model (Wasserburg and Qian 2000; Qian and Wasserburg 2001b, 2002) which attributed heavy r-nuclei to Type II supernovae, but now reassigns the bulk of heavy r-nuclei production to AIC events and relegates r-process production in SNe II to light r-nuclei with A less than or equal to 130. This new assignment removes the earlier requirement that most SNe II produce no Fe, bringing Fe yields into accord with observed values for most SNe II. The paper also builds on observations of s-process enrichments in metal-poor stars by Aoki (2000), Hill (2000), and others, and on the discovery of HE 2148-1247 by Cohen and Christlieb (2003) showing a mixture of high s-process and high r-process enrichments. The AIC scenario enables predictions about binary disruption, neutron star companions, and proper motions that can be tested by future observations.
