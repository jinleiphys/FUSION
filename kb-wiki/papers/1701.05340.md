# Impact of the ionization of the atomic shell on the lifetime of the $^{229m}$Th isomer

> Contemporary data are analyzed concerning the half-lives of the $^{229m}$Th isomer in neutral atoms and various ions. It is explicitly shown that the isomer lifetime may strongly depend on the plain environmental physical conditions like pressure and temperature. Calculation is performed on the united platform of interplay of traditional and subthreshold resonance conversion. General agreement with experiment is obtained in the cases of Th$^I$ and Th$^{III}$, a prediction is made concerning Th$^{IV}$. Disagreement with the experiment in the case of Th$^{II}$ may be a consequence of a random very sharp resonance with an electronic level. Account of the influence of the environment on the decay rate helps one to loose the resonance condition, which may lead to agreement with experiment. This will mean the first experimental observation of strong dependence of the nuclear decay rate on the environment.

# Impact of the ionization of the atomic shell on the lifetime of the $^{229m}$Th isomer

## Key claim
The half-life of the $^{229m}$Th isomer can depend strongly on environmental physical conditions such as pressure and temperature, because in the subthreshold resonance conversion regime the atomic width enters the nuclear decay width directly as a factor. The authors obtain general agreement with experiment for neutral Th$^I$ and doubly ionized Th$^{III}$, but the disagreement for singly ionized Th$^{II}$ (experimental lifetime at least 100 times shorter than calculated) may be resolved either by a random very sharp resonance with an electronic level or by a higher isomer energy above 12.1 eV.

## Method
The authors performed systematic calculations of the isomer half-life across different charge states (Th$^I$ through Th$^{IV}$) using the Dirac-Fock method with configuration interaction, treating both traditional internal conversion (IC) and subthreshold resonance conversion (also called bound internal conversion, BIC) on a unified platform. For each ion, they computed the discrete conversion factor $R$ from the analogue of the internal conversion coefficient $\alpha_d(M1)$, the atomic widths, and the resonance defect $\Delta$, then obtained the total decay width as $\Gamma = (1+R)\Gamma_\gamma^{(n)}$.

## Key numbers
- In neutral Th$^I$, the internal conversion coefficient is $\alpha(M1) = 1.1\times 10^9$ in the 7s shell, yielding a radiative nuclear half-life of approximately 2 hours.
- For Th$^{II}$, the resonance conversion factor is estimated as $R^{II} \approx 100$ to 300, giving a calculated half-life of about 25 seconds; the experimental value is shorter than about 10 ms, a discrepancy of at least three orders of magnitude.
- For Th$^{III}$, the expected $R$ value is $R^{III} \lesssim 10$, leading to a half-life between 12 minutes and 2 hours, consistent with the observed lower bound of $T_{1/2} > 100$ s.
- For Th$^{IV}$, the predicted half-life is approximately 5 hours, close to that of the bare nucleus.
- The isomer energy is taken as $7.6 \pm 1$ eV following Beck et al., with the ionization potential of neutral Th being $I_a = 6.3067_3$ eV.

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of electronic bridges and bound internal conversion previously applied to the 76-eV $^{235}$U isomer (Karpeshin et al.) and to neutral $^{229}$Th at 3.5 eV (Karpeshin et al. in Physical Review C and Yadernaya Fizika). It directly addresses the recent experimental detection of the $^{229m}$Th isomer decay (Larsen et al., 2016) and the measured half-life of 7 $\mu$s in neutral atoms. The results enable predictions for the lifetime of Th$^{IV}$ and highlight the possibility that the nuclear decay rate may be strongly modified by the environment, which would be the first such observation; this has implications for the design of a nuclear optical clock based on $^{229}$Th.
