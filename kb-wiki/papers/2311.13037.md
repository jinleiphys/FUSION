# Strong Gravity Extruding Peaks in Speed of Sound Profiles of Massive Neutron Stars

> The speed of sound squared (SSS) $s^2$ in massive neutron stars (NSs) characterizes not only the stiffness of supradense neutron-rich matter within but also equivalently properties of the curved geometry due to the strong-field gravity and matter-geometry coupling. A peaked density or radius profile of $s^2$ has been predicted for massive NSs using various NS Equation of State (EOS) models. However, the nature, cause, location and size of the peak in $s^2$ profiles are still very EOS model dependent. In this work, we investigate systematically $s^2$ profiles in massive NSs in a new approach that is independent of the nuclear EOS model and without any presumption about the NS structure and/or composition. In terms of the small quantities (reduced radius, the energy density and pressure scaled by their central values), we perform double-element perturbative expansions in solving perturbatively the scaled Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff (TOV) equations and analyzing $s^2$ profiles from the Newtonian limit to the general relativistic (GR) case. The GR term in the TOV equations plays a twofold role: it compresses NS matter and modifies the pressure/energy density ratio from small values in Newtonian stars showing no $s^2$ peak to large ones for massive NSs possessing a peak in their $s^2$ profiles, and eventually takes away the peak in extremely compact/massive NSs approaching the causality limit. {In particular, the peaked behavior in $s^2$ is expected to emerge near the center of massive NSs like PSR J0740+6620, while a sharp phase transition is unlikely to occur there.} These features revealed from our analyses are universal as they are intrinsic properties of the GR stellar structure equations independent of the still very uncertain EOS of supradense neutron-rich matter in NSs.

## Key claim
The peaked behavior of the speed of sound squared in massive neutron stars is an intrinsic property of the general relativistic stellar structure equations, independent of the nuclear equation of state model, emerging when the pressure-to-energy-density ratio becomes sizable (greater than about 0.1) due to strong gravity, rather than being caused by a sharp phase transition near the center.

## Method
The authors performed double-element perturbative expansions of the scaled Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations in terms of reduced radius, energy density, and pressure scaled by their central values, without using any nuclear equation of state model. They analyzed the speed of sound squared profiles from the Newtonian limit to the general relativistic case by expanding the reduced pressure, energy density, and mass as polynomials in the reduced radial coordinate up to order \(\widehat{r}^4\), deriving conditions for the emergence of a peak near the neutron star center.

## Key numbers
- The reduced central pressure \(\widehat{P}_{\rm{c}}\) for massive neutron stars is in the range \(0.1 \lesssim \widehat{P}_{\rm{c}} \lesssim 0.374\), with the upper limit set by the causality condition \(s_{\rm{c}}^2 \leq 1\).
- For a neutron star with central pressure \(P_{\rm{c}} = 200\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}^3\) and central energy density \(\varepsilon_{\rm{c}} = 800\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}^3\), the predicted maximum mass is \(M_{\rm{TOV}} \approx 2.26\ M_{\odot}\) and radius \(R_{\max} \approx 11.9\ \mathrm{km}\).
- The central speed of sound squared for the maximum-mass configuration satisfies \(\gamma_{\rm{c}} = s_{\rm{c}}^2 / \widehat{P}_{\rm{c}} \geq 4/3 \approx 1.33\), indicating matter in neutron star cores can hardly be conformal.
- The coefficient \(a_2\) (from the radial expansion of reduced energy density) lies in the range \(-1.5 \lesssim a_2 \lesssim -0.5\) for \(0.1 \lesssim \widehat{P}_{\rm{c}} \lesssim 0.374\).
- The contribution of the \(a_4\widehat{r}^4\) term to the reduced energy density at \(\widehat{r} \approx 0.20\) is less than \(0.4\%\) using \(-2 \lesssim a_4 \lesssim 2\).

## Context
This work extends previous studies of peaked speed-of-sound profiles in neutron stars that were performed within specific nuclear equation of state models, such as those by Tews (2018), Baym (2019), and others. The authors demonstrate that the peaked behavior is a universal feature of general relativistic stellar structure rather than being equation-of-state dependent, contradicting interpretations that require sharp phase transitions near the center. The results enable model-independent predictions for the central equation of state of massive neutron stars like PSR J0740+6620, and suggest that while a sharp phase transition near the center is unlikely, continuous crossovers or phase transitions occurring far from the center are not excluded.
