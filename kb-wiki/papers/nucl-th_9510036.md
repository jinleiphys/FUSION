# Modelling distribution functions and fragmentation functions

> We present examples for the calculation of the distribution and fragmentation functions using the representation in terms of non-local matrix elements of quark field operators. As specific examples, we use a simple spectator model to estimate the leading twist quark distribution functions and the fragmentation functions for a quark into a nucleon or a pion.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a simple spectator model, using scalar or axial-vector diquarks for the nucleon and an antiquark spectator for the pion, can provide estimates of leading twist quark distribution functions and fragmentation functions, but only at a fixed scale without QCD evolution, so no direct comparison with experimental data is yet possible.

## Method

The authors compute quark correlation functions as non-local matrix elements of quark field operators, reducing the sum over intermediate states to a single spectator term (a scalar or axial-vector diquark for the nucleon, an antiquark for the pion). They parameterize the quark-spectator-hadron vertices with form factors containing a cutoff parameter Λ and a power α, then extract the leading twist distribution functions f₁(x) and g₁(x) from the correlation function, and obtain the fragmentation functions D₁(z) and G₁(z) via the relations D₁(z) = z/2 f₁(1/z) and G₁(z) = z/2 g₁(1/z). No gluon contributions or QCD corrections are included.

## Key numbers

- Quark effective mass set to m = 0.36 GeV; scalar diquark mass m_s = 0.6 GeV; axial-vector diquark mass m_a = 0.8 GeV; cutoff parameter Λ = 0.7 GeV for the nucleon.
- For the pion, the form factor power is α = 3/2 and the cutoff is Λ = 0.4 GeV.
- The form factor for the nucleon uses α = 2 to ensure correct large-x behavior of the distributions.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of quark correlation functions developed by Mulders and others (1990s) for describing distribution and fragmentation functions in terms of non-local matrix elements. It builds on the spectator model approach used by Thomas (1990s) and follows the calculation of fragmentation functions for baryons by Hoodbhoy (1990s). The results enable future work incorporating QCD evolution to allow comparison with experimental deep inelastic scattering and semi-inclusive data, and provide a framework for modeling both distribution and fragmentation functions within the same theoretical scheme.
