# Inclusive Particle Spectra at RHIC

> A simulation is performed of the recently reported data from PHOBOS at energies of 56 and 130 A GeV using the relativistic heavy ion cascade LUCIFER which had previously given a good description of the NA49 inclusive spectra at E=17.2 A GeV. The results compare well with these early measurements at RHIC.

# Inclusive Particle Spectra at RHIC

## Key claim

The purely hadronic cascade LUCIFER provides a satisfactory explanation of the PHOBOS central rapidity charged meson density measurements at √s = 56 and 130 A GeV, consistent with the previous normalization of the code to NA49 data at √s = 17.2 A GeV, suggesting that no quark-gluon plasma signature is required to describe these early RHIC results.

## Method

The authors used the relativistic heavy ion cascade LUCIFER, a two-stage hadronic simulation: phase I records collision histories at high energy with no soft energy loss, and phase II is a conventional hadronic cascade at reduced energy beginning after a meson formation time τf ~ 0.6-0.8 fm/c. The simulation was calibrated against NA49 negatively charged hadron rapidity spectra at √s = 17.2 A GeV for Pb+Pb collisions, then applied to Au+Au collisions at √s = 56 and 130 A GeV with a centrality cut of b ≤ 4 fm to approximately reproduce the PHOBOS 6% centrality selection.

## Key numbers

- Charged particle multiplicity at √s = 130 A GeV: total mesonic production is approximately 6500 particles, compared to near 2600 at √s = 17.2 A GeV
- The combined multiplicity increase from phases I+II over phase I with decays is about a factor of 2.25 at √s = 130 A GeV
- The calculated ratio of central rapidity densities grows by close to a factor of 1.6 from SPS energy (√s = 17.2 A GeV) to √s = 130 A GeV at RHIC
- The meson formation time τf used is 0.6-0.8 fm/c, the same value employed at SPS energies
- Frame dependence in the cascade for a zero impact parameter Au+Au collision at 200 A GeV, as measured by variation in dN/dy for π- at y=0, was ≤ 10%

## Context

This work extends the hadronic cascade approach previously applied to NA49 data at the CERN SPS (Wienold and NA49 Collaboration, 1996; Appleshauser et al., 1999) to the higher RHIC energies measured by the PHOBOS Collaboration (Back et al., 2000). The authors compare their purely hadronic simulation with the partonic code HIJING (Wang and Gyulassy, 1991) and note that predictions of apparently widely different theoretical approaches are alike. The results suggest that quark-gluon plasma signatures at RHIC will be harder to detect than anticipated and must be sought in rarer events, possibly requiring centrality cuts of ≤ 1% or searches for large multiplicity fluctuations.
