# Hyperheavy nuclei: existence and stability

> What are the limits of the existence of nuclei? What are the highest proton numbers $Z$ at which the nuclear landscape and periodic table of chemical elements cease to exist? These deceivably simple questions are difficult to answer especially in the region of hyperheavy ($Z\geq 126$) nuclei. We present the covariant density functional study of different aspects of the existence and stability of hyperheavy nuclei. For the first time, we demonstrate the existence of three regions of spherical hyperheavy nuclei centered around ($Z\sim 138, N\sim 230$), ($Z\sim 156, N\sim 310$) and ($Z\sim 174, N\sim 410$) which are expected to be reasonably stable against spontaneous fission. The triaxiality of the nuclei plays an extremely important role in the reduction of the stability of hyperheavy nuclei against fission. As a result, the boundaries of nuclear landscape in hyperheavy nuclei are defined by spontaneous fission and not by the particle emission as in lower $Z$ nuclei. Moreover, the current study suggests that only localized islands of stability can exist in hyperheavy nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper claims that three regions of spherical hyperheavy nuclei centered around (Z~138, N~230), (Z~156, N~310), and (Z~174, N~410) are expected to be reasonably stable against spontaneous fission, but only if the nuclei remain spherical; the abstract glosses over the condition that triaxiality dramatically reduces fission stability, and that the authors consider the spherical states as the most likely ground states only if toroidal shapes are unstable against triaxial distortions or multifragmentation.

## Method

The authors performed systematic axial relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations for even-even nuclei from Z=122 up to Z=180 using the DD-PC1 covariant energy density functional, scanning deformation energy curves in the -5.0 < β2 < 3.0 range. To assess the impact of triaxiality, a subset of nuclei was studied using triaxial RHB and relativistic mean field plus BCS (RMF+BCS) frameworks, and additional functionals (DD-ME2, PC-PK1, NL3*) were used to evaluate systematic uncertainties in fission barrier heights around spherical minima.

## Key numbers

- Three regions of spherical hyperheavy nuclei are centered around (Z~138, N~230), (Z~156, N~310), and (Z~174, N~410)
- In the 432134 nucleus, triaxiality reduced the fission barrier height from 8.16 MeV to 1.30 MeV
- In the 392134 nucleus, triaxiality reduced the fission barrier for the first minimum from 10.24 MeV to 0.56 MeV
- The 354134 nucleus has a LEMAS at β2 ~ -0.5 with a fission barrier of ~8.5 MeV in axial calculations
- In the 354134 nucleus, the toroidal minimum at β2 ~ -2.5 is located at 4.2 MeV excitation energy relative to the β2 ~ -0.5 minimum
- The 340122 nucleus has axial fission barriers of 5.74 MeV (oblate minimum) and 3.19 MeV (prolate minimum), reduced to ~2 MeV by triaxiality
- The maximum neutron density in the 466156 nucleus at spherical shape is ρ=0.0896 fm^-3 at radial coordinate r=6.55 fm, with central density ρ=0.076 fm^-3

## Context

This work extends prior studies of hyperheavy nuclei that were mostly restricted to spherical shapes, such as those by Bender et al. (1999), Denisov (2005), and Błocki et al. (2001), and addresses the severe limitation that spherical-only calculations cannot establish stability against spontaneous fission. The results contradict the assumption that the nuclear landscape in hyperheavy nuclei is bounded by particle emission as in lower Z nuclei, showing instead that spontaneous fission defines the boundaries. The study enables future systematic triaxial calculations of toroidal shapes and their stability against multifragmentation, which were beyond available computational power.
