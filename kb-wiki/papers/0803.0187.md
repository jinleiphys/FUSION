# Difference between interaction cross sections and reaction cross sections

> We revisit the commonly accepted notion that the difference between interaction and reaction cross sections is negligible at relativistic energies, and show that, especially in small mass number region, it is large enough to help probe nuclear structure. For analyses of the difference, we construct "pseudo data" for the reaction cross sections using a phenomenological black-sphere model of nuclei since empirical data are very limited at high energies. The comparison with the empirical interaction cross sections suggests a significant difference between the reaction and interaction cross sections for stable projectiles on a carbon target, which is of the order of 0-100 mb.

# Difference between interaction cross sections and reaction cross sections

## Key claim

The commonly accepted notion that the difference between interaction and reaction cross sections is negligible at relativistic energies is not always valid; for stable projectiles on a carbon target, the difference is of the order of 0–100 mb and is especially large in the small mass number region, where it can help probe nuclear structure.

## Method

The authors compare empirical interaction cross section data for stable projectiles on a carbon target at energies above 800 MeV per nucleon with "pseudo data" for reaction cross sections constructed using a phenomenological black-sphere model of nuclei. In this model, the black-sphere radius is determined from proton-nucleus elastic scattering diffraction peaks, and the nucleus-nucleus absorption cross section is calculated as π(a_P + a_T)^2, where a_P and a_T are the black-sphere radii of projectile and target. The difference between the empirical interaction cross sections and the pseudo reaction cross sections is then analyzed as a function of projectile mass number.

## Key numbers

- The difference σ_I − σ_BS (interaction cross section minus black-sphere reaction cross section) averaged over various projectiles is about −60.4 mb.
- For ¹²C+¹²C at 870 MeV per nucleon, the reaction cross section is 939 ± 17 mb and 939 ± 49 mb; the interaction cross section is 856 ± 9 mb at 790 MeV per nucleon and 853 ± 6 mb at 950 MeV per nucleon.
- From the ¹²C+¹²C data, the black-sphere radius a(C) = 2.73 ± 0.03 fm (from reaction cross section) and the interaction radius a_I = 2.53 ± 0.10 fm (at 790 MeV/nucleon) and 2.52 ± 0.09 fm (at 950 MeV/nucleon), giving a difference Δa = a_P − a_I of about 0–0.3 fm.
- For ⁴He+¹²C, the empirical reaction cross sections are 542 ± 16 mb and 527 ± 26 mb, which are significantly overestimated by both σ_BS and σ_scaling.
- The black-sphere radius for carbon target from proton elastic scattering is a(C) = 2.69 ± 0.07 fm.
- The scaling relation for the black-sphere radius is a ≃ 1.2135 A^{1/3} fm for stable nuclei from He to Pb.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous black-sphere model (Kohama, Iida, Oyamatsu 2004, 2005) for proton-nucleus reactions to nucleus-nucleus reactions, and revisits the assumption made in earlier interaction cross section measurements (Tanihata 1985; Ozawa, Suzuki, Tanihata 2001) that σ_I ≅ σ_R at relativistic energies. The finding that the difference is significant for stable projectiles, especially at small mass number, contradicts the earlier theoretical work by Ogawa, Yabana, and Suzuki (1992) and the experimental estimate by Ozawa et al. (2002) for ³⁴Cl, which found negligible σ_inel for loosely-bound projectiles. The results enable more precise determination of nuclear matter radii from interaction cross section measurements and suggest that the difference Δa between black-sphere and interaction radii decreases no slower than A^{−1/3} with increasing projectile mass, which has implications for understanding the energy scale controlling projectile breakup.
