# Unmixing symmetries

> The low-lying spectra of atomic nuclei display diverse behaviors, for example rotational bands, which can be described phenomenologically by simple symmetry groups such as spatial SU(3). This leads to the idea of dynamical symmetry, where the Hamiltonian commutes with the Casimir operator(s) of a group, and is block-diagonal in subspaces defined by the group's irreducible representations or irreps. Detailed microscopic calculations, however, show these symmetries are in fact often strongly mixed and the wave function fragmented across many irreps. More commonly the fragmentation across members of a band are similar, or a quasi-dynamical symmetry. In this Letter I explicitly, albeit numerically, construct unitary transformations from a quasi-dynamical symmetry to a dynamical symmetry, adapting the similarity renormalization group, or SRG. The standard SRG produces unsatisfactory results, forcing the induced dynamical symmetry to be dominated by high-weight irreps irrespective of the original decomposition. Using spectral distribution theory to rederive and diagnose standard SRG, I introduce a new form of SRG. The new SRG transforms a quasi-dynamical symmetry to a dynamical symmetry, that is, unmixes the mixed symmetries, with intuitively more appealing results.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a novel variant of the similarity renormalization group (SRG) can numerically construct unitary transformations that convert a quasi-dynamical symmetry (where symmetry mixing patterns are similar across band members) into a true dynamical symmetry (where the Hamiltonian is block-diagonal in a single irreducible representation), whereas standard SRG forces the induced dynamical symmetry to be dominated by high-weight irreps regardless of the original wave function decomposition.

## Method

The author decomposes configuration-interaction wave functions for 36Ar in the sd shell using the USDB interaction into subspaces defined by eigenvalues of the quadratic SU(3) Casimir operator. Standard SRG and a new SRG variant (derived from spectral distribution theory to maximize the decrease of the commutator between the Hamiltonian and the Casimir generator) are applied directly to the 640-dimensional many-body Hamiltonian matrix, evolving it via fourth-order Runge-Kutta while preserving the energy spectrum exactly.

## Key numbers

- The Hamiltonian matrix dimension is 640, evolved using fourth-order Runge-Kutta with a dimensionless evolution parameter s.
- For the 0_1+ ground state at s=3, the overlap between unevolved and standard SRG wave functions is 0.669, while for the new SRG it is 0.643; for the 2_2+ state, the overlap with standard SRG is 0.336 and with new SRG is 0.695.
- For the 0_2+ state at s=3, the overlap with standard SRG is 0.008 and with new SRG is 0.561; for the 4_2+ state, the overlap with standard SRG is 0.382 and with new SRG is 0.836.
- The overlap between standard and new SRG wave functions at s=3 is 0.999 for the 0_1+ state, 0.992 for 2_1+, 0.991 for 4_1+, but only 0.007 for 0_2+, 0.201 for 2_2+, and 0.170 for 4_2+.

## Context

This work extends the concept of quasi-dynamical symmetries, previously studied by Rochford (1988), Bahri (1995), Escher (1998), and Rowe (2000), by providing a numerical method to transform such mixed symmetries into pure dynamical symmetries. The new SRG variant may enable symmetry-adapted structure calculations (as reviewed by 0954-3899-35-12-123101) to achieve results closer to full-space calculations by reducing wave function fragmentation across irreducible representations.
