# Chiral restoration from pionic atoms?

> We evaluate widths and shifts of pionic atoms using a theoretical microscopical potential in which the pion decay constant $f_π$ is changed by an in--medium density dependent one ($f_π(ρ)$), predicted by different partial Chiral restoration calculations. We show that the results obtained for shifts and widths are worse than if this modification were not implemented. On the other hand, we argue that in microscopic many body approaches for the pion selfenergy, based on effective Lagrangians, the mechanisms responsible for the change of $f_π$ in the medium should be automatically incorporated. Therefore, the replacement of $f_π$ by $f_π(ρ)$ in the many body derivation of the microscopic potential would be inappropriate.

# Chiral restoration from pionic atoms?

## Key claim

Replacing the free pion decay constant $f_\pi$ with its in-medium density-dependent value $f_\pi(\rho)$ in all terms of a microscopic many-body optical potential for pionic atoms produces worse agreement with experimental shifts and widths than using the unmodified potential, contradicting earlier claims that pionic atom data provide evidence for partial chiral restoration in nuclei.

## Method

The authors took the theoretical microscopic optical potential (potential **I**) developed in their earlier work (Garcia-Recio et al., 1993) and modified it in two ways: potential **II** replaced the $s$-wave parameters $b_0$, $b_1$, and Im $B_0$ by $F_\chi \times b_0$, $F_\chi \times b_1$, and $F_\chi^3 \times$ Im $B_0$ respectively, where $F_\chi(\rho) = (f_\pi/f_\pi(\rho))^2 \approx 1/(1-2.3\ \text{fm}^3 \rho)$; potential **III** additionally scaled the $p$-wave $\pi NN$ and $\pi N\Delta$ coupling constants $f$ and $f^*$ by $\sqrt{F_\chi}$ and the in-medium $\Delta$ self-energy by $F_\chi^2$. They computed $\chi^2$ values against 61 experimental pionic atom data points (29 shifts and 32 widths) for each potential, using two different sets of $\pi N$ scattering lengths.

## Key numbers

- For the older $\pi N$ scattering lengths ($b_0 = -0.013\ m_\pi^{-1}$, $b_1 = -0.092\ m_\pi^{-1}$, Im $B_0 = 0.041\ m_\pi^{-4}$): potential **I** gave $\chi^2/N = 5$; potential **II** gave $\chi^2/N = 47$; potential **III** gave $\chi^2/N = 67$.
- For the newer $\pi N$ scattering lengths ($b_0 = -0.0001^{+0.0009}_{-0.0021}\ m_\pi^{-1}$, $b_1 = -0.0885^{+0.0010}_{-0.0021}\ m_\pi^{-1}$, Im $B_0 = 0.0345\ m_\pi^{-4}$): potential **I** gave $\chi^2/N = 84$; potential **II** gave $\chi^2/N = 47$; potential **III** gave $\chi^2/N = 207$.
- The chiral factor $F_\chi(\rho)$ is given by $F_\chi(\rho) \approx 1/(1-2.3\ \text{fm}^3 \rho)$ for $\sigma_N = 50$ MeV.
- Best-fit phenomenological additions to potential **I** (potential **I$^{\rm fit}$**) achieved $\chi^2/N = 1.8$; best-fit additions to potential **III** (potential **III$^{\rm fit}$**) achieved $\chi^2/N = 3.1$.

## Context

This work directly tests the proposal of Wolfram (1995) and the subsequent analysis by Friedman (2001) that replacing $f_\pi$ by $f_\pi(\rho)$ in the $s$-wave pion optical potential could resolve the long-standing puzzle of missing repulsion in pionic atoms. The authors argue that standard many-body approaches using effective Lagrangians (as in their own 1993 work and in the work of Weise and collaborators) already incorporate the mechanisms responsible for the in-medium change of $f_\pi$, so adding an explicit $f_\pi(\rho)$ replacement constitutes double-counting. The paper also notes that recent determinations of $\pi N$ scattering lengths from pionic hydrogen (Schroder et al., 2001) reopen the missing-repulsion problem, and calls for renewed theoretical efforts and resolution of the discrepancy between scattering-length values from different experiments.
