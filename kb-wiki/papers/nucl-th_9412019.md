# Spin-Isospin Modes in Heavy-Ion Collisions I: Nuclear Matter at Finite Temperatures

> With a view towards implementation in microscopic transport simulations of heavy-ion collisions, the properties of spin-isospin modes are studied in nuclear matter consisting of nucleons and Delta isobars that interact by the exchange of pi and rho mesons. For a standard p-wave interaction and an effective g' short-range interaction, the dispersion relations for the spin- isospin modes, and the associated amplitudes, are calculated at various nuclear densities and temperatures, within the random-phase approximation. Quantities of physical interest are then extracted, including the total and partial Delta decay widths and the Delta cross sections in the nuclear medium. The self-consistent inclusion of the Delta width has a strong effect on the Delta cross sections at twice normal nuclear density, as compared with the result of ignoring the width. Generally, the obtained quantities exhibit a strong density dependence, but are fairly insensitive to the temperature, at least up to T=25 MeV. Finally, it is described how these in-medium effects may be consistently included into microscopic transport simulations of nuclear collisions, and the improvements over previous approaches are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper develops a refined model for spin-isospin modes in nuclear matter that self-consistently includes the Delta width, dispersion relations, and cross sections, with the hedge that the calculated quantities exhibit a strong density dependence but are fairly insensitive to temperature up to T=25 MeV, and that the self-consistent inclusion of the Delta width has a strong effect on Delta cross sections at twice normal nuclear density compared to ignoring the width.

## Method

The authors studied infinite nuclear matter composed of nucleons and Delta isobars interacting via pi and rho meson exchange, using a standard p-wave interaction and an effective g' short-range interaction. They calculated the dispersion relations for spin-isospin modes and associated amplitudes at various nuclear densities and temperatures within the random-phase approximation (RPA), using an iterative procedure to simultaneously determine the Delta width, dispersion relations, and cross sections. The formalism was developed using Green's function techniques, with the RPA equations solved to obtain eigenvectors and eigenenergies for the different spin-isospin modes, from which total and partial Delta decay widths and Delta cross sections in the nuclear medium were extracted.

## Key numbers

* The temperature insensitivity was tested up to T=25 MeV, with the obtained quantities showing strong density dependence but being fairly insensitive to temperature in this range.
* The self-consistent inclusion of the Delta width had a strong effect on Delta cross sections at twice normal nuclear density, as compared with the result of ignoring the width.
* The free pion mass m_pi and rho meson mass m_rho are used as input parameters, with the meson energies given by hbar omega_{pi,rho} = [m_{pi,rho}^2 + q^2]^{1/2}.
* The monopole form factor uses cutoff parameters Lambda_pi and Lambda_g, with the form factor defined as F_pi(q) = (Lambda_pi^2 - (m_pi c^2)^2) / (Lambda_pi^2 - (c q)^2).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of in-medium properties of pions and Delta isobars in heavy-ion collisions, which had only considered particular aspects such as the Delta production cross section (Bertsch, 1990s), production of pionic modes (Weise, 1990s), or changed dispersion relations (Giessen, Texas groups, 1990s), rather than treating all aspects consistently within the same model. The authors improved upon earlier approximations by including the Delta width self-consistently (where previous works excluded it), including the nucleon-hole channel (which some treatments omitted), and calculating properties at finite temperatures rather than only in cold nuclear matter. The results are intended for implementation in microscopic transport simulations such as BUU and QMD using a local density approximation, enabling more realistic descriptions of heavy-ion collisions at beam energies up to about one GeV per nucleon.
