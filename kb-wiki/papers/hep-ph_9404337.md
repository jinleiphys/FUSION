# Vector Mesons in Nuclear Medium -- an Effective Lagrangian Approach --

> Effective masses of $ρ$ and $ω$ mesons in nuclear medium are studied in a hadronic effective theory. Both the pole position and the screening mass decrease in nuclear matter due to the polarization of the nucleon Dirac sea. The physical origin of the decrease is a reduction of the wave function renormalization constant induced by the tensor (vector) interaction of the $ρ$ ($ω$) with the nucleon. Relation to the results of the QCD sum rules is also discussed.

## Key claim

The effective masses of both ρ and ω mesons decrease in nuclear matter when the polarization of the nucleon Dirac sea is properly included in a hadronic effective theory, contrary to conventional hadronic approaches that only consider the Fermi sea and find masses that stay constant or increase slightly. The decrease is driven by a reduction of the wave function renormalization constant Z induced by the tensor coupling for ρ and the vector coupling for ω with the nucleon.

## Method

The authors compute the one-loop self-energy of ρ and ω mesons in nuclear matter using an effective Lagrangian with ρNN and ωNN interactions, including both vector and tensor couplings. The nucleon propagator in the medium is split into a Feynman (Dirac sea) part and a density-dependent (Fermi sea) part, with the effective nucleon mass M* taken from the relativistic Hartree approximation. Divergences from the Dirac sea loop are subtracted by imposing renormalization conditions that normalize the propagator to 1/(q²-m²) in vacuum and at finite density, isolating the density-dependent contribution. Three types of masses are defined: the real mass (pole position at zero three-momentum), the invariant mass (pole with Fermi sea polarization neglected), and the screening mass (pole in the spacelike region).

## Context

This work extends earlier hadronic calculations by Chin (1977) and Asakawa (1990) that considered only Fermi sea polarization and found little or no mass decrease, by showing that Dirac sea polarization produces a significant mass reduction. The results are consistent with the QCD sum rule predictions of Hatsuda and Lee (1992) and the scaling hypothesis of Brown and Rho (1991), both of which attribute the mass decrease to chiral symmetry restoration. The distinction between real mass and screening mass is emphasized for interpreting different experiments: real mass is relevant for lepton pair production from vector meson decays in nuclei, while screening mass is relevant for t-channel exchange in processes such as K⁺–¹²C scattering.
