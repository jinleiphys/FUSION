# Exciting Prospects for Detecting Late-Time Neutrinos from Core-Collapse Supernovae

> The importance of detecting neutrinos from a Milky Way core-collapse supernova is well known. An under-studied phase is proto-neutron star cooling. For SN 1987A, this seemingly began at about 2 s, and is thus probed by only 6 of the 19 events (and only the $\barν_e$ flavor) in the Kamiokande-II and IMB detectors. With the higher statistics expected for present and near-future detectors, it should be possible to measure detailed neutrino signals out to very late times. We present the first comprehensive study of neutrino detection during the proto-neutron star cooling phase, considering a variety of outcomes, using all flavors, and employing detailed detector physics. For our nominal model, the event yields (at 10 kpc) after 10 s -- the approximate duration of the SN 1987A signal -- far exceed the entire SN 1987A yield, with $\simeq$250 $\barν_e$ events (to 50 s) in Super-Kamiokande, $\simeq$110 $ν_e$ events (to 40 s) in DUNE, and $\simeq$10 $ν_μ, ν_τ, \barν_μ, \barν_τ$ events (to 20 s) in JUNO. These data would allow unprecedented probes of the proto-neutron star, including the onset of neutrino transparency and hence its transition to a neutron star. If a black hole forms, even at very late times, this can be clearly identified. But will the detectors fulfill their potential for this perhaps once-ever opportunity for an all-flavor, high-statistics detection of a core collapse? Maybe. Further work is urgently needed, especially for DUNE to thoroughly investigate and improve its MeV capabilities.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the proto-neutron star cooling phase, which is under-studied and was only probed by 6 of the 19 SN 1987A events, will yield far larger event numbers in present and near-future detectors, but this potential may not be realized unless further work is done, especially for DUNE to improve its MeV capabilities.

## Method

The authors performed the first comprehensive study of neutrino detection during the proto-neutron star cooling phase by combining a spherically symmetric PNS cooling simulation with detailed detector physics for Super-Kamiokande, DUNE, and JUNO. They calculated event yields for all neutrino flavors at a distance of 10 kpc, considering both neutron-star-forming and black-hole-forming outcomes, and used effective flavor spectra after accounting for neutrino mixing effects.

## Key numbers

- For the nominal model at 10 kpc, Super-Kamiokande yields approximately 250 \(\bar{\nu}_e\) events out to 50 seconds.
- For the nominal model at 10 kpc, DUNE yields approximately 110 \(\nu_e\) events out to 40 seconds.
- For the nominal model at 10 kpc, JUNO yields approximately 10 \(\nu_\mu, \nu_\tau, \bar{\nu}_\mu, \bar{\nu}_\tau\) events out to 20 seconds.
- The total gravitational binding energy released is approximately \(3 \times 10^{53}\) erg, which is about 10% of the rest-mass energy of a 1.4 \(M_\odot\) neutron star.
- The total lepton number carried away is approximately \(8 \times 10^{56}\), corresponding to the number excess of \(\nu_e\) relative to \(\bar{\nu}_e\).
- For SN 1987A, only 6 of the 19 events probed the proto-neutron star cooling phase, beginning at about 2 seconds, and only the \(\bar{\nu}_e\) flavor was detected.
- The neutronization burst carries away only about 1–3% of the total energy release and about 40% of the total lepton-number release.
- For black hole formation via the direct mechanism, the time to black hole formation ranges from about 0.9 seconds for a 60 \(M_\odot\) progenitor to about 8 seconds for a 20 \(M_\odot\) progenitor.
- The duration of the neutrino flux truncation at black hole formation is about 0.5 milliseconds.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of proto-neutron star cooling by Keil (1995), Pons (1999, 2001), Roberts (2012), Nakazato (2012, 2020), and others, by providing a complete conceptual framework and calculating results for all flavors with detailed detection physics. The paper enables future work on optimizing detector capabilities for the next Milky Way core collapse, which is likely 5 times closer than SN 1987A, and highlights the need for DUNE to thoroughly investigate and improve its MeV capabilities.
