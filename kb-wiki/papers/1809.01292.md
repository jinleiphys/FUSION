# Gogny-force derived effective shell-model Hamiltonian

> The density-dependent finite-range Gogny force has been used to derive the effective Hamiltonian for the shell-model calculations of nuclei. The density dependence simulates an equivalent three-body force, while the finite range gives a Gaussian distribution of the interaction in the momentum space and hence leads to an automatic smooth decoupling between low-momentum and high-momentum components of the interaction, which is important for finite-space shell-model calculations. Two-body interaction matrix elements, single-particle energies and the core energy of the shell model can be determined by the unified Gogny force. The analytical form of the Gogny force is advantageous to treat cross-shell cases, while it is difficult to determine the cross-shell matrix elements and single-particle energies using an empirical Hamiltonian by fitting experimental data with a large number of matrix elements. In this paper, we have applied the Gogny-force effective shell-model Hamiltonian to the ${\it p}$- and ${\it sd}$-shell nuclei. The results show good agreements with experimental data and other calculations using empirical Hamiltonians. The experimentally-known neutron drip line of oxygen isotopes and the ground states of typical nuclei $^{10}$B and $^{18}$N can be reproduced, in which the role of three-body force is non-negligible. The Gogny-force derived effective Hamiltonian has also been applied to the cross-shell calculations of the ${\it sd}$-${\it pf}$ shell.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the density-dependent finite-range Gogny force can be used to derive a unified effective shell-model Hamiltonian (including two-body matrix elements, single-particle energies, and core energy) that reproduces experimental data for p- and sd-shell nuclei, with the caveat that the density dependence (simulating a three-body force) is essential for correctly describing the neutron drip line of oxygen isotopes and the ground states of 10B and 18N, which empirical interactions without explicit three-body effects fail to reproduce.

## Method

The authors derived two-body matrix elements (TBMEs) from the Gogny D1S interaction using Moshinsky transformations to convert from relative and center-of-mass coordinates to the laboratory frame, with the density-dependent term evaluated using the ground-state density obtained through iterative self-consistent diagonalization. Single-particle energies were calculated from the valence-particle kinetic energy plus its interaction with all core nucleons using the same Gogny force, and the core energy was computed directly from the Gogny interaction rather than taken from experimental data. The resulting Hamiltonian was then used in standard shell-model diagonalizations for p-shell and sd-shell nuclei, with the harmonic-oscillator parameter hbar-omega determined by the empirical formula 45 A^{-1/3} - 25 A^{-2/3}.

## Key numbers

* The rms deviation between Gogny D1S TBMEs and the empirical USDB interaction in the sd shell is 333 keV, and 322 keV compared to the realistic-force RGSD interaction.
* The neutron drip line of oxygen isotopes is reproduced at 24O; 25O is calculated to be approximately 0.5 MeV unbound with respect to 24O.
* 28F is calculated to be unbound by 0.4 MeV above the threshold of neutron emission.
* Standard effective charges of e_p = 1.5 and e_n = 0.5 were used for B(E2) calculations in the sd shell.
* The Gogny force parameters used (D1S) include two Gaussian ranges: mu_1 = 0.7 fm and mu_2 = 1.2 fm.

## Context

This work extends the approach of using phenomenological interactions for shell-model effective Hamiltonians, previously done with zero-range Skyrme forces by Sagawa (1985) and Gomez (1993), by employing the finite-range Gogny force which provides a natural momentum-space cutoff. The method enables self-consistent determination of cross-shell matrix elements and single-particle energies, which are difficult to obtain empirically for multi-shell model spaces. The results enable future applications to cross-shell calculations in the sd-pf shell and provide a framework for unified treatment of binding energies across isotopic chains without fitting to experimental data for each nucleus.
