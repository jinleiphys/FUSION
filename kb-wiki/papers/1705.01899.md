# A novel multiquark approach to hadron resonances

> A new approach to description of hadron spectroscopy is proposed. By assumption, the form of spectrum is dictated by the trace of energy momentum tensor in QCD. This provides the relativistic and renormalization invariance of hadron masses. The constructed scheme is applied to the light mesons for which two complementary interpretations are worked out. The first one represents an "atomic" structure of resonances above 1 GeV in which the quanta of non-perturbative gluon contributions are quantified via an effective formation of quasiparticles representing likely gluon analogues of positronium. This picture allows to build a "periodic table of the hadronic elements", i.e. to classify hadrons, in some sense, in analogy with Mendeleev table in chemistry. The classification does not require introduction of the orbital angular momentum associated with hadron constituents. The Regge and radial trajectories emerge in a natural way. The second interpretation is based on a "collisional" nature of some (or many) hadrons, specifically of scalar resonances below 1 GeV. Here the role of quasiparticles is played by another hadron. This picture in particular leads to a simple explanation of the puzzling scalar sector below 1 GeV with correct predictions for masses and dominant decay modes.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that hadron masses are determined by the trace of the energy-momentum tensor in QCD, with the squared mass (not linear mass) being the relativistic and renormalization-invariant quantity; this approach is applied specifically to light mesons composed of u and d quarks, with the hedge that the underlying nature of the proposed "underquarkonia" quasiparticles is not essential for the phenomenological scheme.

## Method

The author assumes a mass formula m_h^2 = Λ E_h + m_π^2 derived from the Ward identity 2m_h^2 = ⟨h|Θ^μ_μ|h⟩, where Λ = 1830 MeV is fixed from the Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation. For mesons above 1 GeV, the excitation energy E_h is built from quantized contributions of hypothetical "underquarkonia" quasiparticles A_0 (pseudoscalar, J^PC=0^{-+}) and A_1 (vector, J^PC=1^{--}), whose energies E_0 and E_1 are treated as input parameters. For scalar resonances below 1 GeV, a separate "collisional" interpretation is applied where a pion colliding with a quark in another pion produces a coherent state whose mass follows from the same basic relation.

## Key numbers

- Λ = 1830 MeV, derived from the Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation using ⟨q̄q⟩ = -(250 MeV)^3, m_u+m_d = 11 MeV, and f_π = 92.4 MeV, yielding m_π = 140 MeV
- E_ρ ≈ 310 MeV, the excitation energy for the ρ-meson, giving m_ρ ≈ 766.5 MeV (the hadronic-process mass)
- E_0 ≈ 440-450 MeV, fixed from π(1800) as a ^0π_{A_0^4} state
- E_1 ≈ 570 MeV, fixed from fitting f_1(1285) and f_2(1270) as ^Jρ_{A_1} states
- m_σ ≈ 525 MeV, predicted for the σ-meson (f_0(500)) from the collisional formula m_σ^2 = Λ m_π + m_π^2
- M_{A_0} ≈ 910 MeV, the mass of a "materialized" pseudoscalar underquarkonium (close to η' mass 958 MeV)
- M_{A_1} ≈ 1020 MeV, the mass of a "materialized" vector underquarkonium (coincides with φ-meson mass)
- Regge slope Λ E_1 ≈ 1.04 GeV^2 for the spin trajectory

## Context

This work extends the approach of using the trace anomaly and Ward identities for hadron masses, building on the Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation (Gell-Mann, Oakes, Renner 1968) and QCD sum rules (Shifman, Vainshtein, Zakharov 1979). It offers an alternative to the standard quark model's use of orbital angular momentum L for classifying light mesons, and addresses puzzles such as the scalar mesons below 1 GeV (traditionally treated as tetraquarks, as reviewed by Pelaez 2016) and the π_1 exotic mesons (usually interpreted as hybrids). The scheme reproduces known relations including the Weinberg relation m_{a1}^2 = 2m_ρ^2 (Weinberg 1967) and the linear Regge trajectories typical of Veneziano dual amplitudes (Veneziano 1968) and Nambu–Goto strings, while predicting a richer spectrum of radial and daughter trajectories than the quark model allows.
