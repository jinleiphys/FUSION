# The Neutron-Rich Edge of the Nuclear Landscape. Experiment and Theory

> In this review, we describe the experimental facilities and methods which make it possible to produce and measure the properties of the extreme neutron rich nuclei. We then develop the theoretical framework that will accompany us along the review; the shell-model approach with large scale configuration interaction (mixing) SM-CI, with special emphasis in the competition between the spherical mean field and the nuclear correlations (mainly pairing and quadrupole-quadrupole). The symmetry properties of the latter are treated in detail as they will show to be of great heuristic value. We explore in detail the Islands of Inversion (IoI) at N=20 and N=28. We make a side excursion into the heavier Calcium and Potassium isotopes, to discuss current issues on shell evolution and new magic numbers far from stability. We visit the N=40 Island of Inversion. We discuss the doubly magic nucleus 78Ni, its shape coexistence and the prospect of a new IoI at N=50 below Z=28. We examine the N=70 and N=82 neutron closures approaching the neutron drip line.

## Key claim
This review argues that the shell-model approach with large-scale configuration interaction (SM-CI), emphasizing the competition between the spherical mean field and nuclear correlations (pairing and quadrupole-quadrupole), provides the theoretical framework to understand the Islands of Inversion and shell evolution at the neutron-rich edge, but the experimental access to these extreme nuclei remains limited by production rates and the need for new-generation facilities.

## Method
The authors review experimental facilities and methods for producing and measuring very neutron-rich nuclei, including ISOL (Isotope Separation On-Line) and in-flight fragmentation techniques at facilities such as ISOLDE at CERN, the RIBF at RIKEN, and upcoming facilities like FRIB and FAIR. They develop the theoretical shell-model framework with configuration mixing (SM-CI), focusing on the symmetry properties of pairing and quadrupole-quadrupole correlations, and apply it to discuss specific regions: the Islands of Inversion at N=20, N=28, N=40, and N=50 below Z=28, as well as the calcium and potassium isotopes and the neutron closures at N=70 and N=82.

## Key numbers
- The first 2+ state in 78Ni was measured at 2.6 MeV, with a state at 2.9 MeV tentatively assigned to a second deformed 2+ state, indicating shape coexistence near the doubly magic nucleus 78Ni.
- At ISOLDE, more than 1300 different isotopes from 75 different chemical elements can be produced, with an average proton beam intensity of 2 microamperes (3.3 x 10^13 protons per pulse) at 1.4 GeV.
- At the RIBF, uranium beams have reached an intensity of 72 pnA, and calcium beams are routinely delivered at 500 pnA; the facility discovered the very neutron-rich isotope 60Ca and determined the neutron dripline at 31F and 34Ne.
- The spin alignment measured in two-step projectile fragmentation was 30(5)% for 75Cu produced from an intermediate 76Zn.
- The MINOS device at RIBF used liquid hydrogen targets up to twenty centimeters thick (1.3 x 10^23 cm^-2) for spectroscopy of eighteen new 2+ states in neutron-rich even-even isotopes.

## Context
This review extends the line of work on nuclear shell evolution and Islands of Inversion that began with the discovery of the anomalous ground state of 11Be by Wilkinson and Alburger (1959) and the mass measurements of neutron-rich sodium isotopes by Thibault and the Orsay group (1975). It builds on the shell-model framework developed by Talmi and Unna (1960) and the later understanding of shape coexistence in doubly magic nuclei like 16O by Brown, Gerace, and Zuker (1966-1969). The review enables future experimental programs at new-generation facilities (FRIB, FAIR, HIAF, RAON) to probe the neutron drip line and test theoretical predictions for shell evolution and deformation in regions such as N=70 and N=82.
