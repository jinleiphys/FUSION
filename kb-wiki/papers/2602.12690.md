# Dynamical Origin of Spectroscopic Quenching in Knockout Reactions

> Nucleon-removal reactions are a primary tool for extracting single-particle structure of rare isotopes, yet the ratio $R_s=σ_{\exp}/σ_{\mathrm{th}}$ of measured to theoretical cross sections drops systematically below unity for deeply bound nucleons. I derive the exact effective three-body Hamiltonian for composite-projectile reactions using a sequential double Feshbach projection and show that the standard additive model misses two induced interactions: a non-additive term from virtual target excitations and a polarization potential from excluded projectile configurations. Their omission overestimates the stripping cross section, producing apparent quenching distinct from genuine nuclear-structure correlations. This mechanism offers a dynamical origin for the strong separation-energy dependence of the quenching ratio, a feature unique to knockout analyses. Existing four-body CDCC calculations for $^{6}$Li validate the framework: the proper Feshbach reference reproduces elastic scattering data, while a phenomenological optical potential double counts the breakup absorption and fails.

# Dynamical Origin of Spectroscopic Quenching in Knockout Reactions

## Key claim

The standard additive three-body Hamiltonian used in knockout reaction analyses systematically omits two induced interactions, a non-additive term from virtual target excitations and a polarization potential from excluded projectile configurations, whose omission overestimates the stripping cross section and produces apparent spectroscopic quenching (R_s < 1) that is distinct from genuine nuclear-structure correlations. This mechanism offers a dynamical origin for the strong separation-energy dependence of the quenching ratio, a feature unique to knockout analyses that is not seen in transfer, (p,2p), or (e,e'p) reactions.

## Method

The author derives the exact effective three-body Hamiltonian for composite-projectile reactions using a sequential double Feshbach projection, first eliminating target excitations (Q_A-space) then projecting onto the projectile model space (P_bx). The resulting decomposition separates the Hamiltonian into the conventional additive three-body term H_3^(0), a non-additive effective interaction U^(nonadd) from virtual target excitations coupling the two projectile fragments, and a dynamical polarization potential U^(pol) from excluded projectile configurations. The framework is validated against published four-body CDCC calculations for ^6Li + ^209Bi elastic scattering at 29.9 MeV, comparing three-body CDCC calculations using either a single-folding deuteron potential (U_d^SF) that satisfies the Feshbach condition or a phenomenological deuteron optical potential (U_d^OP) that does not.

## Key numbers

- The ratio R_s = sigma_exp / sigma_th drops systematically from near unity for loosely bound nucleons to approximately 0.25 for deeply bound nucleons, with a pronounced dependence on the separation-energy asymmetry Delta S.
- Transfer reactions yield R_s approximately 0.5–0.7 with essentially no asymmetry dependence; (e,e'p) data exhibit a uniform approximately 0.6–0.7 depletion.
- For ^6Li + ^209Bi elastic scattering at E_in = 29.9 MeV, four-body CDCC serves as the benchmark; three-body CDCC with U_d^SF reproduces the four-body result with no adjustable parameters, while three-body CDCC with U_d^OP strongly underestimates the elastic cross section.
- The ^6Li alpha+d spectroscopic factor S_dalpha is approximately 0.70, leaving approximately 30% of the wave function in non-d+alpha configurations.
- The deuteron binding energy is epsilon_d = 2.22 MeV; the alpha particle binding energy is approximately 28 MeV.

## Context

This work generalizes earlier formal analyses of the deuteron case by Austern (1968), Polyzou (1979), and Kozack and Levin (1986), and the quantitative studies by Timofeyuk (2013), Johnson (2014), and Bailey (2017) that were restricted to A(d,p) reactions and did not establish the connection to spectroscopic quenching. The paper extends these results to arbitrary composite projectiles and directly links the missing induced interactions to the observed spectroscopic quenching puzzle identified by Gade (2008), Tostevin (2014, 2021), and Aumann (2021). The framework enables a self-consistency criterion for distinguishing genuine structure depletion from dynamical artifacts in knockout analyses, and provides a prescription for avoiding double counting when using coupled-channel methods with phenomenological optical potentials.
