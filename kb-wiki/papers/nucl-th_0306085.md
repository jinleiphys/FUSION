# Finite Temperature Study of Nuclear Matter in SU(2) Chiral Sigma Model

> We study here the equation of state of symmetric nuclear matter at finite temperatures using a modified SU(2) Chiral Sigma model. The effect of temperature on effective mass, pressure, entropy and binding energy is discussed. The liquid-gas phase transition is investigated and the critical values of temperature, density and pressure are calculated.

## Key claim

The modified SU(2) Chiral Sigma model predicts a liquid-gas phase transition in symmetric nuclear matter with a critical temperature of approximately 17.2 MeV, which is lower than the Walecka model prediction of 18.3 MeV but higher than the experimental estimate of 13.1 ± 0.6 MeV from heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors used a relativistic mean-field approach with the modified chiral sigma model (MCH) Lagrangian, which includes two extra higher-order scalar field interaction terms beyond the standard chiral sigma model. They solved the equations of motion for the scalar and vector fields self-consistently at finite temperature using Fermi-Dirac distribution functions for nucleons and antinucleons, then computed the energy density, pressure, and entropy density as functions of baryon density and temperature.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature for liquid-gas phase transition: T_c ≈ 17.2 MeV
- Critical density: n_c ≈ 0.045 fm^{-3}
- Critical pressure: p_c ≈ 0.274 MeV/fm^{-3}
- Saturation density used for parameter fitting: 0.153 fm^{-3}
- Binding energy per nucleon at saturation: -16.3 MeV
- Effective Landau mass at saturation: 0.85 M (where M is the free nucleon mass)
- Nuclear incompressibility: approximately 300 MeV
- Coupling parameters: C_ω = 1.999 fm^2, C_σ = 6.816 fm^2, B = -99.985, C = -132.246
- Phase transition at zero density occurs at T ≈ 235 MeV (compared to T ≈ 185 MeV in the Walecka model)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous zero-temperature investigations with the MCH model (Jena and Singh, earlier work) to finite temperature, and it builds on the relativistic mean-field studies of nuclear matter by Walecka (1974) and others. The results are compared with the Walecka model predictions (critical temperature 18.3 MeV, reducing to 14.2 MeV with nonlinear terms) and with experimental data from heavy-ion collisions suggesting T_c ≈ 13.1 ± 0.6 MeV. The study demonstrates that the MCH model produces a softer equation of state than the Walecka model and provides a viable framework for describing nuclear matter at high density, enabling further investigations of hot dense matter relevant to heavy-ion collisions and neutron star physics.
