# Big Bang nucleosynthesis as a probe of new physics

> The Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) model is a cornerstone for the understanding of the evolution of the early universe, making seminal predictions that are in outstanding agreement with the present observation of light element abundances in the universe. Perhaps, the only remaining issue to be solved by theory is the so-called "lithium abundance problem". Dedicated experimental efforts to measure the relevant nuclear cross sections used as input of the model have lead to an increased level of accuracy in the prediction of the light element primordial abundances. The rise of indirect experimental techniques during the preceding few decades has permitted the access of reaction information beyond the limitations of direct measurements. New theoretical developments have also opened a fertile ground for tests of physics beyond the standard model of atomic, nuclear, statistics, and particle physics. We review the latest contributions of our group for possible solutions of the lithium problem.

# Big Bang nucleosynthesis as a probe of new physics

## Key claim

The lithium abundance problem may be resolvable by invoking non-extensive (Tsallis) statistics with a parameter q in the range 1.069 < q < 1.082, which brings the predicted 7Li abundance into agreement with observations while leaving other light element abundances essentially unchanged. However, the authors acknowledge that this agreement could be fortuitous and represents a fine-tuning of the BBN model.

## Method

The authors performed standard BBN calculations using an extended version of the Wagoner code reformulated by Kawano, updated with recent nuclear reaction rates including R-matrix fits and Trojan Horse Method measurements. They introduced modifications to the Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution using Tsallis non-extensive statistics with parameter q, and properly accounted for modifications to reaction Q-values for backward reaction rates. They also modeled the effect of multiple dark parallel universe sectors by adding dark degrees of freedom to the Friedmann equation and computing light element abundances as functions of the dark-to-visible temperature ratio T'/T and number of dark sectors N_DM.

## Key numbers

- BBN-predicted 7Li/H abundance: (4.283 +0.335 -0.292) x 10^-10, compared to observed value (1.58 +0.35 -0.28) x 10^-10 (discrepancy factor ~3)
- BBN-predicted deuterium abundance D/H = 2.692 (+0.177 -0.070) x 10^-5, compared to observed (2.527 ± 0.03) x 10^-5
- BBN-predicted 4He mass fraction Y_p = 0.2485 (+0.001 -0.002), compared to observed 0.2453 ± 0.0034
- BBN-predicted 3He/H = 9.441 (+0.511 -0.466) x 10^-6, compared to observed ≥ (11 ± 2) x 10^-6
- Tsallis parameter range solving lithium puzzle: 1.069 < q < 1.082
- For dark parallel universe model with N_DM = 5, compatibility with D, 3He, and 4He observations requires T'/T ~ 0.2-0.3
- 7Be(n,α) cross section from Trojan Horse Method yields reaction rate smaller by factor ~10 compared to Wagoner (1969), giving 7Li/H = 4.441 x 10^-10
- 6Li/7Li isotopic ratio predicted as (1.5 ± 0.3) x 10^-5
- Neutron half-life τ_n ≃ 877.75 ± 28 s
- Baryon-to-photon ratio from Planck: η = (6.12 ± 0.06) x 10^-10
- Number of neutrino families: N_ν = 2.9840 ± 0.0082

## Context

This review extends the authors' prior work on BBN calculations (Bertulani and Kajino 2016) and their investigations of non-extensive statistics (Bertulani, Hall, and Farias 2013; Hou et al. 2017) and dark parallel universe models (Bertulani et al. 2017). The lithium problem, first identified from the Spite plateau observations (Spite and Spite 1982), has resisted solution through improved nuclear reaction measurements including Trojan Horse Method studies by Lamia et al. (2017) and the LUNA collaboration. The paper enables future work testing non-extensive statistics in other astrophysical contexts and constraining dark sector models through combined BBN and CMB observations.
