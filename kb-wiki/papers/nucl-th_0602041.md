# Correlations in Nuclear Masses

> It was recently suggested that the error with respect to experimental data in nuclear mass calculations is due to the presence of chaotic motion. The theory was tested by analyzing the typical error size. A more sensitive quantity, the correlations of the mass error between neighboring nuclei, is studied here. The results provide further support to this physical interpretation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the error between calculated and measured nuclear masses is not random noise but originates from chaotic motion in the nucleonic mean field, and that the autocorrelation function of these mass errors between neighboring isotopes follows a specific "universal" functional form derived from semiclassical theory, with the caveat that the universal form is an approximation that replaces the discrete spectrum of periodic orbits with a continuous one.

## Method

The authors computed the difference between experimentally measured masses and masses calculated from three global nuclear mass models (a macroscopic-microscopic model, a Hartree-Fock-BCS self-consistent calculation, and a shell-model based calculation) for isotopic chains. They then calculated the normalized autocorrelation function of these mass errors as a function of neutron number difference dN, averaged over all available isotopic chains and over neighboring Z values. The experimental correlation was plotted against a dimensionless parameter ζ derived from the ratio of the variance of the derivative of the error to the variance of the error itself, and compared to the theoretical prediction C(ζ) = (1 − ζ²/4) exp(−ζ²/4) + (ζ⁴/16) Γ(0, ζ²/4).

## Key numbers

- The typical size of the chaotic contribution to nuclear masses is given by σ_ch = 2.8 / A^(1/3) MeV, where A is the mass number.
- A typical accuracy of global mass models for medium-heavy nuclei (binding energy ~1000 MeV) is 5 × 10⁻⁴ relative error.
- The parameter a in the correlation function is estimated as a ~ 37/(2π A^(2/3)), giving a ≈ 0.5 for A ≈ 50 and a ≈ 0.2 for A ≈ 200.
- Setting a=0 in the correlation function introduces an error less than 7 × 10⁻² for any ζ.
- The half-width of the correlation function C(ζ) is of order 1.5, corresponding to neutron differences dN ≈ 2.
- The parameter σ_x (the root-mean-square of the derivative of the error with respect to neutron number) is estimated as ~ 3/A^(1/3) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the theory proposed by Bohigas and Leboeuf (2005) that the error in nuclear mass calculations arises from chaotic motion, by moving beyond the typical error size to study the more sensitive autocorrelation function. The results support the interpretation that chaotic dynamics effects are present in the ground state of atomic nuclei, and the existence of correlations extending over a few nucleons is consistent with the high accuracy with which Garvey-Kelson-type mass relations are fulfilled (Barea et al., 2005). The paper contrasts with the view expressed by Molinari and Weidenmüller (2005) that chaotic motion imposes limits in principle on the accuracy of binding energy calculations, instead arguing that the detailed oscillatory structure is sensitive to the Hamiltonian but does not set an a priori bound.
