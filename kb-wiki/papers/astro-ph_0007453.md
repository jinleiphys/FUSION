# Prediction of Astrophysical Reaction Rates: Methods, Data Needs, and Consequences for Nucleosynthesis Studies

> The majority of nuclear reactions in astrophysics involve unstable nuclei which are not fully accessible by experiments yet. Therefore, there is high demand for reliable predictions of cross sections and reaction rates by theoretical means. The majority of reactions can be treated in the framework of the statistical model (Hauser-Feshbach). The global parametrizations of the nuclear properties needed for predictions far off stability probe our understanding of the strong force and take it to its limit. The sensitivity of astrophysical scenarios to nuclear inputs is illustrated in the framework of a detailed nucleosynthesis study in type II supernovae. Abundances resulting from calculations in the same explosion model with two different sets of reaction rates are compared. Key reactions and required nuclear information are identified.

## Key claim

The majority of nuclear reactions in astrophysics involve unstable nuclei and can be treated in the framework of the statistical model (Hauser-Feshbach), but the global parametrizations of nuclear properties needed for predictions far off stability probe the understanding of the strong force to its limit. The paper demonstrates that when two different theoretical reaction rate sets are used in the same type II supernova explosion model, the final nucleosynthesis yields for most stable isotopes are robust to within about 20%, despite individual reaction rates differing by up to a factor of 10 or more.

## Method

The authors used the NON-SMOKER Hauser-Feshbach code to calculate cross sections and reaction rates for reactions with nucleons, alpha particles, or gamma rays for all targets between the proton and neutron drip lines in the range 9 < Z < 84. They then performed hybrid nucleosynthesis calculations in type II supernovae of 15 and 25 solar masses, using the same stellar evolution code from Woosley and Weaver (1995) but with two different reaction rate sets: the WFHZ set from Woosley et al. (1978) and the TAT set from Thielemann et al. (1987). The yield ratios from the two rate sets were compared to isolate the nuclear physics contribution to abundance differences.

## Key numbers

- Typical differences between the two theoretical rate sets at astrophysically interesting temperatures are less than a factor of two, but individual cases exceed a factor of 10.
- For a 15 solar mass supernova, only about a dozen out of 70 stable isotopes in the mass range 12 to 70 have nucleosynthesis yields that differ by over 20% when using the same rate for 12C(alpha,gamma)16O.
- None of the isotope yields differ by more than a factor of 1.7 between the two rate sets in the 15 solar mass model.
- Both theoretical rate sets agree with experimental cross section data typically to within a factor of two, with almost identical standard deviations.

## Context

This work extends the comparison of nucleosynthesis results from the supernova models of Woosley and Weaver (1995) and Thielemann et al. (1996) by isolating the effects of nuclear reaction rate uncertainties from differences in stellar physics. The paper identifies that the critical reaction rates governing energy generation and major nucleosynthesis are those for nuclei below magnesium, such as 12C(alpha,gamma)16O, while the Hauser-Feshbach rates for heavier nuclei produce only perturbations on these dominant flows. The new NON-SMOKER rate set is presented as preferable to both prior sets and will be adopted by both research groups for future work, enabling more accurate predictions for trace isotopes and for reactions involving self-conjugate nuclei where isospin suppression remains uncertain.
