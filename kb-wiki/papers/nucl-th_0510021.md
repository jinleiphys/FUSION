# Nanoparticles as a possible moderator for an ultracold neutron source

> Ultracold and very cold neutrons (UCN and VCN) interact strongly with nanoparticles due to the similarity of their wavelengths and nanoparticles sizes. We analyze the hypothesis that this interaction can provide efficient cooling of neutrons by ultracold nanoparticles at certain experimental conditions, thus increasing the density of UCN by many orders of magnitude. The present analytical and numerical description of the problem is limited to the model of independent nanoparticles at zero temperature. Constraints of application of this model are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper hypothesizes that ultracold nanoparticles (at ≈1 mK) can efficiently cool neutrons via multiple collisions, potentially increasing ultracold neutron density by many orders of magnitude, but the analysis is limited to a model of independent nanoparticles at zero temperature and neglects inter-particle interactions, rotation, and collective degrees of freedom.

## Method

The authors model neutron-nanoparticle interaction using a complex square-well potential (real part from coherent scattering lengths, imaginary part from absorption cross sections) and compute elastic scattering cross sections, absorption cross sections, and mean relative energy loss per collision within the first Born approximation. They then solve the stationary Boltzmann transport equation for an infinite homogeneous moderator to derive the neutron energy spectrum and a necessary condition for efficient cooling, and extend the analysis to finite moderators by estimating characteristic thermalization length and time.

## Key numbers

- For deuterium nanoparticles, the real potential V₀ = 85 neV and the imaginary potential V₁ = 2.2 × 10⁻⁶ neV.
- The maximum radius for 10% accuracy of the first Born approximation is R_Born = 4.5 nm for D₂, 3.7 nm for D₂O, 5.4 nm for O₂, 4.5 nm for CO₂, 2.6 nm for C, and 2.9 nm for Be.
- The critical momentum for anisotropic scattering is k_c ≈ 1/R, where R is the nanoparticle radius.
- For low energies where kR ≪ 1, the elastic cross section scales as σ_s ∼ R⁶, the absorption cross section scales as σ_a ∼ R³, and the mean relative energy loss scales as ξ ∼ R⁻³.
- The moderator size needed to increase phase-space density by a factor e is nearly constant over the efficiency domain, while the thermalization time has a clear minimum near the critical velocity.
- For small nanoparticles, the thermalization time is smaller but the moderator size is larger; for larger nanoparticles, the opposite holds.

## Context

This work extends the observation of quasi-elastic UCN scattering on surface nanoparticles (Nesvizhevsky et al., prior studies) to propose a new UCN production method based on equilibrium cooling, in contrast to conventional sources that select a narrow fraction of a broad energy spectrum (e.g., the liquid-deuterium source at ILL). The approach aims to overcome the limitation of traditional moderators where neutrons cannot be cooled below the energy corresponding to the inter-atomic distance scale, and it enables the possibility of achieving thermal equilibrium between neutrons and a moderator at ≈1 mK for the first time.
