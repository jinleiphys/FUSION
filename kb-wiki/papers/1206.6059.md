# The coexistence of superconductivity and ferromagnetism in nano-scale metallic grains

> A nano-scale metallic grain in which the single-particle dynamics are chaotic is described by the so-called universal Hamiltonian. This Hamiltonian includes a superconducting pairing term and a ferromagnetic exchange term that compete with each other: pairing correlations favor minimal ground-state spin, while the exchange interaction favors maximal spin polarization. Of particular interest is the fluctuation-dominated regime where the bulk pairing gap is comparable to or smaller than the single-particle mean level spacing and the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer theory of superconductivity breaks down. Superconductivity and ferromagnetism can coexist in this regime. We identify signatures of the competition between superconductivity and ferromagnetism in a number of quantities: ground-state spin, conductance fluctuations when the grain is weakly coupled to external leads and the thermodynamic properties of the grain, such as heat capacity and spin susceptibility.

## Key claim

Superconductivity and ferromagnetism can coexist in nano-scale metallic grains in the fluctuation-dominated regime where the bulk pairing gap is comparable to or smaller than the single-particle mean level spacing, but this coexistence is not a sharp phase transition; instead, the ground-state spin increases stepwise as a function of the exchange coupling constant, and mesoscopic fluctuations can enlarge the parameter regime where coexistence signatures are observable.

## Method

The authors solved the universal Hamiltonian (including a BCS-like pairing term and a ferromagnetic exchange term) using Richardson's exact solution generalized to include exchange interactions, combined with exact diagonalization for transport calculations and a semi-analytical spin-projection method for thermodynamic observables. For transport, they used a rate equation approach in the sequential tunneling regime with a bandwidth of 17 single-particle levels and up to 19 electrons, averaging over approximately 4000 random-matrix realizations. For thermodynamics, they employed a spin-projection identity to express the canonical partition function at fixed total spin in terms of traces at fixed spin projection, combined with a functional integral approach for the pairing part.

## Key numbers

- The dimensionless Thouless conductance must satisfy \(g_\Th = E_\Th/\delta \gg 1\) for the universal Hamiltonian to be valid.
- For an equidistant spectrum with no pairing (\(\Delta=0\)), the transition from spin \(S\) to spin \(S+1\) occurs at \(J_s/\delta = (2S+1)/(2S+2)\).
- For \(\Delta/\delta < 0.6\), the first spin step is \(\Delta S=1\); for \(0.6 < \Delta/\delta < 0.8\), the ground-state spin jumps from \(S=0\) to \(S=2\) in one step for \(0.87 < J_s/\delta < 0.9\).
- At \(\Delta/\delta=1\), the first step jumps from \(S=0\) to \(S=3\).
- At \(\Delta/\delta=1\) and \(J_s/\delta=0.9\), approximately 43% of even grains have spin \(S=0\), 5% have spin \(S=1\), and 9% have spin \(S=3\).
- For strong pairing (\(\Delta \gg \delta, J_s\)), the peak spacing separation is approximately \(\delta\Delta_2 \approx 4\Delta - 3J_s\).
- Platinum has an exchange interaction strength of \(J_s/\delta \sim 0.59 - 0.72\) (Gorokhov 2004).
- The spin susceptibility is measured in units of the Pauli susceptibility \(\chi_P = 2\mu_B^2/\delta\).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of pairing correlations in ultra-small metallic grains (Di Lorenzo 2000, Falci 2000, Schechter 2001) and the mesoscopic Stoner staircase (Kurland 2000) by including both pairing and exchange interactions on equal footing. It builds on the universal Hamiltonian framework (Kurland 2000, Aleiner 2002) and prior ground-state phase diagram calculations for equidistant spectra (Schmidt 2007). The results enable experimental identification of the coexistence regime in materials like platinum (Konig 1999, Gorokhov 2004) through measurements of conductance peak spacing bimodality and suppression of peak height fluctuations, as well as through thermodynamic signatures such as the re-entrant spin susceptibility in odd grains.
