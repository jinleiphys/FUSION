# Direct Boundary Matching: A Bound-State Technique for Nuclear Scattering with Lagrange-Legendre Functions

> I present a direct boundary matching method (DBMM) for solving nuclear scattering problems using Lagrange-Legendre basis functions. This approach belongs to the family of bound-state techniques for the continuum, reformulating scattering problems into a localized, square-integrable ($L^2$) representation. The key feature is the direct incorporation of the outgoing wave boundary condition into the last row of the matrix equation, eliminating the need for Bloch operators and two-step matching procedures required in traditional R-matrix methods. Unlike the complex scaling method that rotates coordinates into the complex plane, DBMM operates entirely in real coordinate space. The formalism is extended to coupled-channel problems, where the wave function decomposition naturally leads to an effective source potential that distinguishes between the entrance channel and other channels. Benchmark calculations for p~+~$^{12}$C scattering demonstrate excellent agreement with the Numerov integration method.

## Key claim

The Direct Boundary Matching Method (DBMM) reformulates nuclear scattering into a localized, square-integrable representation by directly incorporating the outgoing wave boundary condition into the last row of the matrix equation, eliminating the need for Bloch operators and two-step matching procedures required in traditional R-matrix methods. The method operates entirely in real coordinate space, unlike the complex scaling method which rotates coordinates into the complex plane.

## Method

The radial Schrodinger equation for the scattered wave is discretized using Lagrange-Legendre basis functions defined on a finite interval [0, R], with mesh points obtained from zeros of shifted Legendre polynomials. The outgoing wave boundary condition is imposed directly in the last row of the matrix equation using the complex logarithmic derivative gamma_s = k H_ell^+prime / H_ell^+ evaluated at the boundary, rendering the matrix complex but yielding scattering amplitudes in a single solve. For coupled-channel problems, the wave function decomposition leads to an effective source potential that distinguishes between the entrance channel (short-range potential only) and other channels (full coupling potential including Coulomb multipoles), with the block matrix structure accommodating channel coupling without additional formal machinery.

## Key numbers

- Benchmark calculations for p + 12C scattering at E_lab = 30 MeV used N = 80 Lagrange-Legendre mesh points on interval [0, R] with R = 25 fm
- Differences in |S_ell| between DBMM and Numerov method are below 2.5 x 10^-5 across all partial waves
- Phase differences between DBMM and Numerov method are less than 0.01 degrees
- Low partial waves (ell <= 3) exhibit |S_ell| approximately 0.3-0.5, indicating significant absorption
- Phase varies from -104 degrees at ell = 0 through -147 degrees at ell = 1 to +169 degrees at ell = 2
- For ell >= 6, the phase approaches zero, indicating predominantly Coulomb scattering
- The Koning-Delaroche potential parameters at E_lab = 30 MeV: real volume depth V = 45.44 MeV with r_v = 1.127 fm and a_v = 0.676 fm; imaginary volume depth W = 3.08 MeV; imaginary surface depth W_d = 6.37 MeV with r_d = 1.306 fm and a_d = 0.525 fm; Coulomb radius parameter r_c = 1.538 fm
- Numerov reference calculations used step size h = 0.02 fm with matching at the same boundary radius

## Context

This work extends the family of "bound-state techniques for the continuum" that reformulate scattering problems into localized, L^2 representations, including the R-matrix method (Wigner 1947, Descouvemont 2010, Jin 2020), Complex Scaling Method (Kruppa 2007, Lazauskas 2011, Moiseyev 1998, Myo 2014), Lorentz Integral Transform method (Efros 1994, Efros 2007), and Kohn variational principle (Kievsky 1997, Viviani 2020). The novelty lies in directly integrating Lagrange functions and boundary matching to bypass Bloch operator formalism, providing conceptual transparency and implementation simplicity. Future applications include breakup reactions within the continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) framework (Austern 1987) and combination with microscopic structure calculations for unified descriptions of nuclear structure and reactions.
