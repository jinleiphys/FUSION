# Applications of emulation and Bayesian methods in heavy-ion physics

> Heavy-ion collisions provide a window into the properties of many-body systems of deconfined quarks and gluons. Understanding the collective properties of quarks and gluons is possible by comparing models of heavy-ion collisions to measurements of the distribution of particles produced at the end of the collisions. These model-to-data comparisons are extremely challenging, however, because of the complexity of the models, the large amount of experimental data, and their uncertainties. Bayesian inference provides a rigorous statistical framework to constrain the properties of nuclear matter by systematically comparing models and measurements. This review covers model emulation and Bayesian methods as applied to model-to-data comparisons in heavy-ion collisions. Replacing the model outputs (observables) with Gaussian process emulators is key to the Bayesian approach currently used in the field, and both current uses of emulators and related recent developments are reviewed. The general principles of Bayesian inference are then discussed along with other Bayesian methods, followed by a systematic comparison of seven recent Bayesian analyses that studied quark-gluon plasma properties, such as the shear and bulk viscosities. The latter comparison is used to illustrate sources of differences in analyses, and what it can teach us for future studies.

## Key claim

Bayesian inference and Gaussian process emulation provide a rigorous statistical framework for constraining quark-gluon plasma properties from heavy-ion collision data, but the resulting constraints on shear and bulk viscosities differ across seven recent analyses due to differences in model choices, data sets, priors, treatment of correlations, and numerical uncertainties.

## Method

The review systematically compares seven recent Bayesian analyses that constrained the temperature-dependent shear viscosity over entropy density ratio and bulk viscosity of quark-gluon plasma by comparing multi-stage hydrodynamic models to experimental measurements from the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and the Large Hadron Collider. Each analysis replaced computationally expensive model outputs with Gaussian process emulators trained on model evaluations at parameter points selected primarily via Latin hypercube sampling, then used Bayesian inference with Gaussian likelihood functions to obtain posterior distributions for the model parameters.

## Key numbers

- The number of model parameters in systematic model-to-data comparisons typically ranges from 5 to 20 parameters
- Parameter space is sampled 10 to 40 times the number of parameters; with 20 parameters this means 200 to 800 parameter samples
- Emulator uncertainty ranges from a few percent to approximately 20 percent depending on the observable and analysis
- The hydrodynamic initialization time parameter tau_hydro is studied over a range of [0.1, 1.5] fermi
- Center-of-mass energies per nucleon pair at RHIC are at most 0.2 TeV, while LHC data have energies at least 10 times larger (2.76 TeV, 5.02 TeV, 5.44 TeV)

## Context

This review extends the methodology of Bayesian parameter estimation in heavy-ion physics, building on earlier work by Bernhard (2018), Moreland (2019), and the JETSCAPE collaboration (2020) who developed the principal component analysis approach for dimensionality reduction of observables. The systematic comparison of seven analyses reveals that differences in model choices, data sets, and priors lead to different viscosity constraints, enabling future studies to better understand sources of systematic uncertainty in model-to-data comparisons.
