# Bound and resonance states of the dipolar anion of hydrogen cyanide: competition between threshold effects and rotation in an open quantum system

> Bound and resonance states of the dipole-bound anion of hydrogen cyanide HCN$^-$ are studied using a non-adiabatic pseudopotential method and the Berggren expansion technique involving bound states, decaying resonant states, and non-resonant scattering continuum. We devise an algorithm to identify the resonant states in the complex energy plane. To characterize spatial distributions of electronic wave functions, we introduce the body-fixed density and use it to assign families of resonant states into collective rotational bands. We find that the non-adiabatic coupling of electronic motion to molecular rotation results in a transition from the strong-coupling to weak-coupling regime. In the strong coupling limit, the electron moving in a subthreshold, spatially extended halo state follows the rotational motion of the molecule. Above the ionization threshold, electron's motion in a resonance state becomes largely decoupled from molecular rotation. Widths of resonance-band members depend primarily on the electron orbital angular momentum.

## Key claim

The non-adiabatic coupling of electronic motion to molecular rotation in HCN$^-$ causes a transition from a strong-coupling regime for subthreshold bound states (where the electron follows the molecular rotation) to a weak-coupling regime for resonance states above the ionization threshold (where the electron motion becomes largely decoupled from rotation), with resonance widths depending primarily on the electron orbital angular momentum.

## Method

The authors used a non-adiabatic pseudopotential method combined with the Berggren expansion technique (BEM), which diagonalizes the Hamiltonian in a complete basis of single-particle states including bound states, decaying resonant states (Gamow states), and non-resonant scattering continuum states. The coupled-channel equations for the radial wave functions were solved for total angular momenta J^pi = 0^+, 1^-, 2^+, 3^-, 4^+, and 5^- of HCN$^-$, including all partial waves with l_max = 9 and using 165 discretization points on the complex contour. Resonances were identified by their stability under variation of the complex contour parameters, and the body-fixed intrinsic density was introduced to assign states into rotational bands.

## Key numbers

- Experimental ground state energy of HCN$^-$ (J^pi = 0^+): E_exp(0_1^+) = -1.1465789 x 10^{-4} Ry, used to adjust the short-range potential parameter r_c = 3.071622666 a_0
- Experimental 1^- band head energy: E_exp(1_1^-) = -8.8198377 x 10^{-5} Ry; calculated value: E(1_1^-) = -8.96 x 10^{-5} Ry
- Dipole moment of HCN: mu = 1.174 e a_0; moment of inertia: I = 7.42 x 10^4 m_e a_0^2
- Calculated bound state energies: E(0_1^+) = -1.15 x 10^{-4} Ry; E(1_1^-) = -8.96 x 10^{-5} Ry; E(2_1^+) = -3.69 x 10^{-5} Ry
- No bound 3^- state was found; the lowest 3^- state is a resonance at E = 3.89 x 10^{-8} - i 1.06 x 10^{-8} Ry
- In the adiabatic limit (I -> infinity), the maximum energy difference between members of the ground-state band (0_1^+ through 5_1^-) is 1.5 x 10^{-7} Ry, better than 0.1% of the 0^+ state energy (E = -1.2308 x 10^{-4} Ry)
- For J^pi = 2^+ resonances, relative variations of Re(E) with contour changes are always smaller than 1%, while relative variations of Im(E) can reach approximately 15%

## Context

This work extends previous studies of bound states of dipolar molecules by Fossez et al. (2013) to the unbound part of the spectrum, using the Berggren expansion method which offers superior accuracy for weakly-bound states compared to direct integration approaches. The study addresses the competition between continuum effects, collective rotation, and non-adiabatic coupling in dipole-bound anions, building on earlier theoretical work by Garrett (1982, 2010) and Clary (1988, 1989) on rotational bands in such systems. The results enable future investigations of threshold effects and resonance structure in other dipole-bound molecular anions, and provide a framework for interpreting high-resolution electron photodetachment experiments such as those by Ard et al. (2009).
