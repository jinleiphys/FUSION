# Non-Hermitian quantum mechanics approach for extracting and emulating continuum physics based on bound-state-like calculations: Detailed description

> This work applies a reduced basis method to study the continuum physics of a finite quantum system -- either few or many-body. Specifically, I develop reduced-order models, or emulators, for the underlying inhomogeneous Schrödinger equation and train the emulators against the equation's bound-state-like solutions at complex energies. The emulators rapidly and accurately interpolate and extrapolate the matrix elements of the Hamiltonian resolvent operator (Green's function) across a parameter space that includes both complex energy and other real-valued physical inputs in the Schrödinger equation. The spectra, discretized and compressed as the result of emulation, and the associated resolvent matrix elements (or amplitudes), have the defining characteristics of non-Hermitian quantum mechanics calculations, featuring complex eigenenergies with negative imaginary parts and branch cuts moved below the real axis in the complex energy plane. Therefore, one now has a method that extracts continuum physics from bound-state-like calculations and emulates those extractions in the input parameter space. Building on a prior Letter [arXiv:2408.03309], this article provides the full theoretical details, a comprehensive analysis of the method's performance, and a brief discussion of how it can be coupled with existing continuum approaches to perform emulations in their input parameter spaces.

## Key claim

This work develops reduced-order models (emulators) for the inhomogeneous Schrödinger equation that, when trained on bound-state-like solutions at complex energies, can extrapolate to real energies and thereby extract continuum physics without directly solving the scattering problem. The key qualifier is that the method works because the emulator is "biased" toward physical continuum physics rather than reproducing the high-fidelity Hermitian-matrix calculations at real energies, which are known to fail due to discretized continuum poles.

## Method

The author applies the reduced basis method (RBM) to construct a low-dimensional subspace from high-fidelity solutions of the inhomogeneous Schrödinger equation at selected complex energies and parameter values (snapshots). The equation is then projected into this subspace to form a reduced-order model that emulates the Hamiltonian resolvent (Green's function) matrix elements across a parameter space including both complex energy and other real-valued physical inputs. The resulting emulator produces a non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian matrix whose complex eigenenergies have negative imaginary parts, effectively moving branch cuts below the real axis and enabling continuum physics extraction from bound-state-like calculations.

## Key numbers

- Training calculations achieve relative errors on the order of \(10^{-12}\) in the two- and three-body systems studied
- With \(R_{IR} = 10\) fm box size, computed resolvent matrix elements with \(|\Im E| > 10\) MeV already converge to exact results
- The method is demonstrated numerically for two- and three-body systems with short-range interactions

## Context

This work extends the author's prior Letter (Zhang, 2024, arXiv:2408.03309) by providing full theoretical details and comprehensive performance analysis. It connects to existing non-Hermitian quantum mechanics methods such as complex scaling (Reinhardt, 1982; Moiseyev, 2011) and Berggren-basis methods (Berggren, 1968), but differs fundamentally in how the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian matrix is constructed. The approach also relates to complex-energy methods (Schlessinger, 1966; Deltuva, 2012) and Lorentz integral transform methods (Efros, 1985) by providing a new way to analytically continue complex-energy results to the real axis. The emulator technology enables rapid parameter-space exploration for model calibration and uncertainty quantification in continuum physics calculations.
