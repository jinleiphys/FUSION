# Isotropization far from equilibrium

> Isotropization occurs on time scales much shorter than the thermal equilibration time. This is a crucial ingredient for the understanding of collision experiments of heavy nuclei or other nonequilibrium phenomena in complex many body systems. We discuss in detail the limitations of estimates based on standard ``linear'' or relaxation-time approximations, where isotropization and thermal equilibration rates agree. For a weak-coupling $ϕ^4$-model the relaxation-time approximation underestimates the thermal equilibration time by orders of magnitude, in contrast to the isotropization time. The characteristic nonequilibrium isotropization rate can be enhanced as compared to the close-to-equilibrium value. Our results are obtained from the two-particle irreducible effective action, which includes off-shell and memory effects and does not involve a gradient expansion. This allows us to determine the range of validity of a description to lowest-order in gradients, which is typically employed in kinetic equations.

## Key claim

Isotropization occurs on time scales much shorter than thermal equilibration, but the relaxation-time approximation (which predicts they are equal) fails dramatically for thermalization time estimates while remaining valid for isotropization at sufficiently late times; the characteristic nonequilibrium isotropization rate can be enhanced compared to the close-to-equilibrium value for large initial anisotropies.

## Method

The authors solved the nonequilibrium dynamics of a scalar \(g^2 \phi^4\) theory using the two-particle irreducible (2PI) effective action truncated to three-loop order, which includes scattering, off-shell, and memory effects without employing a gradient expansion. They considered anisotropic initial conditions with high occupation numbers in a narrow momentum range around a "beam direction" \(p_3 = \pm p_{\rm ts}\), reminiscent of heavy-ion collision scenarios. The evolution equations for the statistical and spectral functions were solved numerically, and the results were compared to the relaxation-time approximation derived from the lowest-order gradient expansion.

## Key numbers

* For a weak coupling \(g = 1/2\) in units of the renormalized thermal mass \(m_R\), the isotropization time is \(\tau_{\rm iso} \simeq 100/m_R\), while thermal equilibration requires times much longer than this.
* At late times \(t \gtrsim 500/m_R\), the isotropization rate is well described by the thermal equilibrium on-shell value \(\tau_{\rm iso}^{-1} = -\Sigma^{(\rm eq)}_{\varrho}/2\omega^{(\rm eq)}\).
* The relaxation-time approximation underestimates the thermal equilibration time by "orders of magnitude" for the weak-coupling \(\phi^4\) model.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous finding (Berges 2004) that prethermalization of the equation of state occurs on very short time scales, and it addresses the question of isotropization as a separate, faster process. The paper contrasts the relaxation-time approximation (Weldon 1983) with full nonequilibrium calculations, showing that the approximation fails for thermalization but works for isotropization at late times. The results enable a better understanding of the early applicability of hydrodynamics in heavy-ion collisions, as discussed by Heinz (2004) and Arnold (2004), and establish that the lowest-order gradient expansion becomes valid on a time scale similar to the isotropization time.
