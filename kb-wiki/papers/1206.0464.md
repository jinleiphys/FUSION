# Nuclear matrix elements for neutrinoless double-beta decay and double-electron capture

> A new generation of neutrinoless double beta decay experiments with improved sensitivity is currently under design and construction. They will probe inverted hierarchy region of the neutrino mass pattern. There is also a revived interest to the resonant neutrinoless double-electron capture, which has also a potential to probe lepton number conservation and to investigate the neutrino nature and mass scale. The primary concern are the nuclear matrix elements. Clearly, the accuracy of the determination of the effective Majorana neutrino mass from the measured 0νββ-decay half-life is mainly determined by our knowledge of the nuclear matrix elements. We review recent progress achieved in the calculation of 0νββand 0νECEC nuclear matrix elements within the quasiparticle random phase approximation. A considered self-consistent approach allow to derive the pairing, residual interactions and the two-nucleon short-range correlations from the same modern realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials. The effect of nuclear deformation is taken into account. A possibility to evaluate 0νββ-decay matrix elements phenomenologically is discussed.

## Key claim

The accuracy of extracting the effective Majorana neutrino mass from measured neutrinoless double-beta decay half-lives is primarily limited by the uncertainty in nuclear matrix elements, which the authors aim to reduce through self-consistent quasiparticle random phase approximation calculations that derive pairing, residual interactions, and short-range correlations from the same realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials.

## Method

The authors performed calculations of neutrinoless double-beta decay and neutrinoless double-electron capture nuclear matrix elements using the quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA), its renormalized version (RQRPA), and the self-consistent renormalized QRPA (SRQRPA). They derived the pairing interaction, residual interactions, and two-nucleon short-range correlations from the same modern realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials (CD-Bonn and Argonne V18) using the coupled cluster method, and incorporated nuclear deformation effects for deformed nuclei like 150Nd. The calculations used three different single-particle model spaces ranging from 9 to 23 orbits, with the particle-particle interaction strength adjusted to reproduce experimental 2νββ-decay matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- For 76Ge with free nucleon coupling (gAeff = 1.254) and QRPA, the averaged nuclear matrix element ⟨M'0ν⟩ ranges from 4.92 (Jastrow SRC) to 6.89 (CCM CD-Bonn SRC), with corresponding variances in parentheses
- For 136Xe with free nucleon coupling and QRPA, ⟨M'0ν⟩ ranges from 2.16 (Jastrow SRC) to 3.23 (CCM CD-Bonn SRC)
- The predicted half-life range for 76Ge with |mββ| = 50 meV is (3.0-6.3)×10^26 years in QRPA, compared to the experimental lower bound of 1.9×10^25 years from the Heidelberg-Moscow experiment
- For 150Nd, the QRPA calculations including nuclear deformation showed a suppression of M0ν by about 40% compared to previous spherical QRPA results
- The nuclear radius parameter used is r0 = 1.2 fm, with R = r0 A1/3
- The vector and axial-vector form factor masses are MV = 850 MeV and MA = 1086 MeV, with gA = 1.254

## Context

This work extends the QRPA-based calculations of neutrinoless double-beta decay nuclear matrix elements previously performed by Rodin et al. (2005) and Simkovic et al. (2009), incorporating for the first time a fully self-consistent treatment where pairing, residual interactions, and short-range correlations are all derived from the same realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials (CD-Bonn and Argonne V18). The results enable more reliable interpretation of current and next-generation experiments (GERDA, CUORE, EXO, KamLAND-Zen, MAJORANA, SuperNEMO, SNO+) that aim to probe the inverted hierarchy region of neutrino masses with sensitivity to |mββ| of a few tens of meV. The paper also addresses the unresolved claim of evidence for 0νββ-decay in 76Ge by some participants of the Heidelberg-Moscow collaboration with half-life 2.23+0.44-0.31×10^25 years.
