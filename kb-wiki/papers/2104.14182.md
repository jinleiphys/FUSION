# Unveiling the pole structure of S-matrix using deep learning

> Particle scattering is a powerful tool to unveil the nature of various subatomic phenomena. The key quantity is the scattering amplitude whose analytic structure carries the information of the quantum states. In this work, we demonstrate our first step attempt to extract the pole configuration of inelastic scatterings using the deep learning method. Among various problems, motivated by the recent new hadron phenomena, we develop a curriculum learning method of deep neural network to analyze coupled channel scattering problems. We show how effectively the method works to extract the pole configuration associated with resonances in the $πN$ scatterings.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate a first-step attempt to extract the pole configuration of inelastic scatterings using deep learning, but the method can only determine which Riemann sheets contain poles, not the exact pole positions; the approach is limited to identifying the number of poles per sheet within a defined counting region.

## Method

The authors construct a general S-matrix for two-channel scattering (πN and ηN) using analyticity, unitarity, and hermiticity, then generate ~1.8×10⁶ simulated amplitudes with known pole configurations as a teaching dataset. A deep neural network (DNN) with three hidden layers (200 nodes each, ReLU activation) and a 35-node softmax output layer is trained using curriculum learning, where easy at-most-1-pole configurations are introduced first and multi-pole classifications are added gradually. The DNN takes as input 37 randomly selected energy points and the real and imaginary parts of the amplitude (111 input nodes plus bias), and the final inference on experimental data uses 10⁶ amplitudes generated by treating each error bar as one standard deviation of a Gaussian distribution.

## Key numbers

- Training dataset: ~1.8×10⁶ simulated amplitudes; independent test set: ~3.5×10⁴ simulated amplitudes
- After curriculum learning (3,200 epochs): training accuracy 63.5%, testing accuracy 68.3%
- After full training (31,050 epochs): training accuracy 76.5%, testing accuracy 80.4%
- The counting region for poles is defined as T₂−50 ≤ Re E_pole ≤ T₂+200 MeV and −200 ≤ Im E_pole < 0 MeV for [bt] and [bb] sheets, and 0 < Im E_pole ≤ 200 MeV for [tb] sheet
- Maximum number of nearby poles per unphysical sheet is restricted to four, giving 35 possible configurations
- For the GW-SAID πN scattering amplitude, the DNN identified only four pole configurations out of 35 possibilities: 44.6% (1 pole in [bt], 1 in [bb], 2 in [tb]), 34.1% (1,1,1), 16.4% (0,1,3), 4.9% (0,1,2)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous single-channel deep learning analysis (Sombillo 2020) to coupled-channel scattering, motivated by recent hadron phenomena near two-hadron thresholds. The method provides a model-independent classification of pole configurations that complements the pole-counting method of Morgan and Pennington (1991) and the compositeness criterion of Weinberg (1965). The approach can be extended to general resonance analysis in hadron, nuclear, or atomic physics where low-energy scattering reveals universal features, but further dynamical modeling (e.g., Pearce and Gibson, Badalyan 1982) is needed to interpret the origin of the detected poles.
