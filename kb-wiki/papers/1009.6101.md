# Nuclear Excitation by a Zeptosecond Multi--MeV Laser Pulse

> A zeptosecond multi--MeV laser pulse may either excite a "plasma" of strongly interacting nucleons or a collective mode. We derive the conditions on laser energy and photon number such that either of these scenarios is realized. We use the nuclear Giant Dipole Resonance as a representative example, and a random--matrix description of the fine--structure states and perturbation theory as tools.

# Nuclear Excitation by a Zeptosecond Multi–MeV Laser Pulse

## Key claim

A zeptosecond multi–MeV laser pulse can excite either a "plasma" of strongly interacting nucleons or a collective Giant Dipole Resonance mode, depending on whether the photon absorption time is shorter or longer than the nuclear equilibration time. The collective excitation scenario is realistic even for intense laser pulses provided the laser energy is sufficiently far below the GDR peak energy, with the condition modified by the Lorentzian tail of the resonance and the cubic energy dependence of the dipole width.

## Method

The author constructs a schematic nuclear Hamiltonian with a single dipole mode embedded in a sea of 2p-2h states, a double dipole mode embedded in 3p-3h states, and additional states implementing the Brink-Axel hypothesis, using random matrices from the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble to model the complex configurations. Time-dependent perturbation theory is applied to calculate the probabilities P1 and P2 for single and double dipole absorption from a laser pulse described by a Gaussian wave packet, with ensemble averages taken in the limit of infinite matrix dimensions.

## Key numbers

- The GDR peak energy is approximately 80 A^{-1/3} MeV (where A is nuclear mass number), and the spreading width Gamma_down = 5 MeV, giving an equilibration time tau_eq = hbar / Gamma_down.
- The width for gamma decay of the GDR to the ground state is Gamma_dip = 5–10 keV; for a 15 MeV laser energy, Gamma_dip ≈ 10 keV.
- For a laser pulse with energy spread sigma ≈ 10 keV (corresponding to pulse length ≈ 10^{-19} s), the criterion for collective GDR excitation at E_L = 7 MeV with E_dip = 14 MeV, Gamma_dip = 10 keV, Gamma_down = 5 MeV yields N < 5 × 10^3.
- The naive estimate N < Gamma_down / Gamma_dip ≈ 700 is significantly smaller than the corrected bound.
- The probability for single dipole absorption is P1 = (2 pi N Gamma_dip Gamma_down) / [(E_L - E_dip)^2 + (1/4)(Gamma_down)^2].
- The probability for double dipole absorption is P2 = (1/2) P1^2, with the Brink-Axel mechanism dominating over direct double excitation of the dipole mode by a factor of order sigma / Gamma_down.
- For E_L ≈ E_dip, collective excitation gives way to plasma formation already for N ≈ 10.

## Context

This paper provides the first theoretical framework for distinguishing between two competing nuclear excitation mechanisms under ultra-intense laser pulses, extending earlier qualitative considerations of laser-nucleus interactions motivated by facilities like ELI and NIF (if reconfigured as femtosecond pulse systems). The work uses the random-matrix approach of Weidenmueller (2009) and the Brink-Axel hypothesis (Brink 1955, Axel 1962) to model the spreading of collective modes. The results enable experimental design for studying compound nuclei at excitation energies and spin values not previously accessible, and establish the conditions under which collective GDR excitation versus nucleon plasma formation can be separately investigated.
