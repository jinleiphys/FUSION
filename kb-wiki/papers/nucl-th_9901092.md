# Collapse and the Tritium Endpoint Pileup

> The beta-decay of a tritium nucleus produces an entangled quantum system, a beta electron, a helium nucleus, and an antineutrino. For finite collapse times, the post-collapse beta electron energy can originate from a range of pre-collapse energies due to the uncertainty principle. Long collapse times give negligible uncertainty, so the pre-collapse spectrum must approach that of isolated nuclei. We calculate the post-collapse electron spectrum which shows a collapse-dependent pileup near the endpoint. Comparison with observation shows that a collapse time of 1 x 10^-17 s explains the observed pileup. The collapse of the entangled quantum system must be triggered by the environment: most likely an atomic (molecular) electron initially bound to the atomic (molecular) tritium source or perhaps ambient gas molecules. Coincidentally, the 40 eV tritium atom-helium ion energy level shift is unobservably small for times shorter than the system collapse time. We conclude that an atomic (molecular) electron triggers the collapse once the 40 eV shift becomes detectible and the electron detects the helium nucleus. Thus collapse may explain the tritium endpoint pileup.

# Collapse and the Tritium Endpoint Pileup

## Key claim
The observed anomalous pileup of events near the endpoint of tritium beta-decay may be explained by finite collapse times of the entangled quantum system (beta electron, helium nucleus, and antineutrino), with a collapse time of approximately 1 × 10⁻¹⁷ s providing agreement with observations. The collapse is likely triggered by an atomic (molecular) electron bound to the tritium source detecting the 40 eV energy level shift between the tritium atom and helium ion states.

## Method
The author calculates the post-collapse beta electron spectrum by averaging the standard emission probability P(E₁) over an energy interval ΔE determined by the uncertainty principle, where ΔE ≥ ħ/(2T) for collapse time T. The averaged spectrum is compared to the observed spectrum from Stoeffl and Decman (1991), and the energy uncertainty ΔE is adjusted to match the reported 20% excess at 18550 eV. The collapse time is then derived from the uncertainty relation, and the mechanism is explored by comparing this time to the time scale set by the 40 eV energy shift of atomic electrons bound to the decaying tritium.

## Key numbers
- Endpoint energy E₀ ≈ 18.6 keV for tritium beta-decay
- At E = 18550 eV, the observed probability is 1.2 times the standard emission probability P, giving an excess of 20%
- The energy uncertainty required to match the observed excess is ΔE = 30 eV
- The collapse time lower bound is T ≥ ħ/(2ΔE) ≈ 1.1 × 10⁻¹⁷ s
- The electron near the endpoint travels at v = 0.26c, covering approximately 9 atomic diameters (9 × 10⁻¹⁰ m) in the collapse time
- The 40 eV energy shift between tritium atom and helium-3 ground state gives a collapse time T ≥ 0.8 × 10⁻¹⁷ s
- When accounting for only 70% of decays contributing to the excess, the required ΔE becomes 35 eV and T ≈ 0.9 × 10⁻¹⁷ s

## Context
This work addresses the anomalous endpoint pileup in tritium beta-decay reported by Stoeffl and Decman (1991) and noted in the Particle Data Group listing for neutrino mass squared. The standard phase-space spectrum for isolated nuclei (neglecting neutrino mass and Coulomb corrections) predicts a quadratic falloff near the endpoint, but observations show an excess of events. The paper proposes that quantum mechanical collapse of the entangled decay products, rather than neutrino mass or other conventional complications, produces the pileup. This mechanism would imply that the observed spectrum depends on environmental factors such as gas density or laser illumination of the tritium source, enabling experimental tests of the collapse hypothesis.
