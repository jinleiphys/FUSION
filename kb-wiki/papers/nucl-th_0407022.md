# Towards the 3D-Imaging of Sources

## Key claim
Geometric details of a nuclear reaction zone at the time of particle emission can be restored from low relative-velocity particle correlations via imaging, but some source details are erased and cause instabilities that require the method of discretized optimization. So far only 1-dimensional (angle-averaged) emission source images have been produced, and efforts are in progress to restore angular details for full 3D imaging.

## Method
The authors invert the integral relation R(q) = integral dr K(q,r) S(r), where R(q) is the measured correlation function minus unity, K(q,r) is the kernel derived from the two-particle wavefunction, and S(r) is the source function. For angle-averaged (lambda=0) imaging, they discretize the integral and minimize chi-squared to solve for S(r); for higher multipolarities (lambda >= 1), they perform angular decomposition of both the correlation function and source into spherical harmonics, then apply the same inversion methods per multipole. The optimized discretization method varies the source binning prior to inversion to minimize anticipated errors and avoid instabilities from inverting a near-zero kernel.

## Key numbers
- For neutral pions or gammas with pure interference, the kernel becomes K(q,r) = cos(2 q dot r), and the angle-averaged kernel is K_0(q,r) = sin(2qr)/(2qr), representing a Fourier-cosine transform.
- In the discretized imaging approach, the source is binned with an assumed bin size of 2 fm in the naive test case shown.
- The pp correlation functions were measured for the 14N + 197Au reaction at 75 MeV/nucleon, for different total momentum ranges.
- For identical particles, only even lambda contribute to the angular decompositions; for nonidentical particles there are 6 parameters (as functions of q or r) needed to describe correlations or sources, while for identical particles there are 4 parameters (dipole deformations vanish).
- A test with pi- pairs showed successful semiquantitative 3D source reconstruction from an assumed source modified by errors.

## Context
This work extends the imaging methodology previously developed for 1-dimensional (angle-averaged) source reconstruction (Brown 1997) to full 3D imaging by decomposing correlation functions and sources into spherical harmonics. The imaging approach has already changed the interpretation of pp correlation data previously described in terms of Gaussian sources (Verde 2002), revealing non-Gaussian short-range portions and long-range tails. The 3D imaging enables access to emission source structure including phase-space density at freeze-out, reaction geometry, collective motion, resonance decays, and prolonged emission effects, building on prior work using correlation anisotropies to access emission time differences (Ghetti 2003) and emission zone asymmetries (Adler 2001).
