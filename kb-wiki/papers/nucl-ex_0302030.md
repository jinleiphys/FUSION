# Measuring Neutron Separation Energies Far from Stability

> A method is proposed for the experimental measurement of neutron separation energies for nuclei far from stability. The procedure is based on determining cross sections for the production of nuclei, by projectile fragmentation, for which only protons are removed but for which the number of neutrons is left unchanged. A simple Abrasion-Ablation analysis leads to a cross section prediction which is sensitive to the neutron separation energy after a single parameter is adjusted in comparison with data. Examples which illustrate the method are presented.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that cross sections for projectile fragmentation chains where only protons are removed (p-removal chains) can be used to estimate neutron separation energies for neutron-rich nuclei far from stability, after a single parameter (the mean excitation energy per abraded nucleon) is calibrated against data for nuclei with known separation energies. The method is presented as a complementary approach that cannot compete with dedicated mass measurements but may allow wide use for extremely neutron-rich nuclei.

## Method

The authors use an Abrasion-Ablation (A-A) model to calculate cross sections for projectile fragmentation where only protons are removed and no neutrons are abraded. The cross section is factorized into an abrasion term (geometric overlap of projectile and target, multiplied by the probability that all abraded particles are protons assuming uncorrelated nucleon positions) and an ablation term (the probability that the excitation energy of the residue is below the neutron separation energy). The ablation term depends on the neutron separation energy and on the distribution of excitation energy per abraded nucleon, characterized by a mean excitation energy parameter ⟨e*⟩ that is adjusted by fitting to measured cross sections for nuclei with known separation energies.

## Key numbers

- For the fragmentation of ⁸⁶Kr, fitting the p-removal chain (x=2-5) with known separation energies gave a χ² per degree of freedom of 0.99 for the exponential distribution, with fitted ⟨e*⟩ values of 11.7 MeV for the triangle distribution and 16.6 MeV for the exponential distribution.
- For the fragmentation of ⁴⁸Ca with a ⁹Be target, the fit parameters for the first two p-removal members (⁴⁵Cl and ⁴⁴Si) used separation energies of 6.241 MeV and 5.21 MeV respectively.
- For ⁴¹Al produced by 7-proton removal from ⁴⁸Ca, the cross section was 8 pb with a ¹⁸¹Ta target, scaled to 4.4 pb for a ⁹Be target, yielding an estimated separation energy of 3.5 ± 0.5 MeV.
- For the ¹⁹⁷Au fragmentation with a ⁹Be target, the best-fit ⟨e*⟩ was 36.3 MeV (exponential distribution); with a ²⁷Al target, the best-fit ⟨e*⟩ was 32.2 MeV.
- The ratio of fitted ⟨e*⟩ values between the triangle and exponential distributions was approximately 2:3 across all systems studied.

## Context

This work extends the use of projectile fragmentation, a method successfully used to produce neutron-rich rare nuclides (Benlliure 1999, Friedman 1985), to estimate neutron separation energies near the neutron drip-line where few experimental values exist (Audi 2003, GANIL). The method is intended to complement dedicated mass measurements (Val 2000, Val 2001, Val 2002) which achieve uncertainties better than 10⁻⁷, by providing a simpler cross-section-based approach that could measure separation energies to a couple hundred keV for extremely neutron-rich nuclei. The authors note a puzzling disagreement for the ¹⁹⁷Au + ⁹Be reaction where the method failed to produce reasonable separation energies for ¹⁹³Re and ¹⁹²W, indicating the need for more data and understanding of cross-section measurement uncertainties.
