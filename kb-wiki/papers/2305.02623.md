# Probabilistic neural networks for improved analyses with phenomenological models

> Physics models typically contain adjustable parameters to reproduce measured data. While some parameters correspond directly to measured features in the data, others are unobservable. These unobservables can, in some cases, cause ambiguities in the extraction of observables from measured data, or lead to questions on the physical interpretation of fits that require these extra parameters. We propose a method based on deep learning to extract values of observables directly from the data without the need for unobservables. The key to our approach is to label the training data for the deep learning model with only the observables. After training, the deep learning model can determine the values of observables from measured data with no ambiguities arising from unobservables. We demonstrate this method on the phenomenological R-matrix that is widely utilized in nuclear physics to extract resonance parameters from cross section data. Our deep learning model based on Transformers successfully predicts nuclear properties from measurements with no need for the channel radius and background pole parameters required in traditional R-matrix analyses. Details and limitations of this method, which may be useful for studies of a wide range of phenomena, are discussed.

## Key claim

The authors propose a deep learning method based on probabilistic neural networks (deep ensembles) that can extract nuclear resonance parameters directly from cross section data without needing to determine the channel radius or background pole parameters that are required in traditional R-matrix analyses and that can introduce ambiguities. The key hedge is that the method works when the training data is labeled only with the parameters of interest, bypassing extraneous parameters, but the performance may degrade if variations of the eliminated parameters impact the distinctive features in the data.

## Method

The authors generated training data using the phenomenological R-matrix code AZURE2 to calculate cross section spectra for the 12C+p elastic scattering system, sampling resonance parameters (spins, parities, energies, widths) along with random values of the channel radius (3-8 fm) and random configurations of background poles. They trained a Transformer-based deep ensemble model on these spectra, using only the resonance parameters as labels and not the channel radius or background pole values, with separate output branches for classification (J, pi) and regression (log E, log Gamma). The trained model was then applied to experimental data from Meyer (1976) to predict resonance parameters.

## Key numbers

* Spin classification accuracy for the second resonance: 98.7% for J, 100.0% for pi
* Spin classification accuracy for the third resonance: 99.0% for J
* Mean error for the first resonance energy E1: 0.00014 MeV (training distribution Normal(0.425, 0.003) MeV)
* Mean error for the second resonance energy E2: 0.00124 MeV (training distribution Normal(1.565, 0.010) MeV)
* Mean error for the third resonance energy E3: 0.00059 MeV (training distribution Normal(1.610, 0.006) MeV)
* Mean error for the first resonance width Gamma1: 0.20 keV (training distribution Normal(30, 3) keV)
* Mean error for the second resonance width Gamma2: 1.98 keV (training distribution Normal(50, 15) keV)
* Mean error for the third resonance width Gamma3: 1.32 keV (training distribution Normal(60, 8) keV)
* Model performance showed less than ~2% accuracy variation and ~1 keV (35%) error variation when tested with different values of eliminated parameters (channel radius and background poles)
* Predicted values from measurement data: E1 = 0.4244(2) MeV, E2 = 1.5590(12) MeV, E3 = 1.6029(8) MeV; Gamma1 = 34.3(3) keV, Gamma2 = 55.0(8) keV, Gamma3 = 50.3(7) keV

## Context

This work extends the use of deep learning for nuclear physics analyses, specifically addressing the well-known ambiguity problem in R-matrix analyses caused by the arbitrary choice of channel radius and background poles, as discussed by deBoer (2017) and Descouvemont (2010). The method enables rapid extraction of resonance parameters from scattering data without the need for iterative chi-squared minimization or Bayesian inference that traditionally require specifying these extraneous parameters. The approach can be generalized to other phenomenological models and may enable pre-trained models that require only slight adjustment for specific tasks, as well as interpolation or extrapolation of measured data.
