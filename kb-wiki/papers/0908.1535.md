# Electromagnetic Form Factors and Charge Densities From Hadrons to Nuclei

> A simple exact covariant model in which a scalar particle is modeled as a bound state of two different particles is used to elucidate relativistic aspects of electromagnetic form factors. The model form factor is computed using an exact covariant calculation of the lowest-order triangle diagram and shown to be the same as that obtained using light-front techniques. The meaning of transverse density is explained using coordinate space variables, allowing us to identify a true mean-square transverse size directly related to the form factor. We show that the rest-frame charge distribution is generally not observable because of the failure to uphold current conservation. Neutral systems of two charged constituents are shown to obey the lore that the heavier one is generally closer to the transverse origin than the lighter one. It is argued that the negative central charge density of the neutron arises, in pion-cloud models, from pions of high longitudinal momentum. The non-relativistic limit is defined precisely and the ratio of the binding energy to that of the mass of the lightest constituent is shown to govern the influence of relativistic effects. The exact relativistic formula for the form factor reduces to the familiar one of the three-dimensional Fourier transform of a square of a wave function for a very limited range of parameters. For masses that mimic the quark-di-quark model of the nucleon we find substantial relativistic corrections for any value of $Q^2$. A schematic model of the lowest s-states of nuclei is used to find that relativistic effects decrease the form factor for light nuclei but increase the form factor for heavy nuclei. Furthermore, these states are strongly influenced by relativity.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the textbook interpretation of electromagnetic form factors as three-dimensional Fourier transforms of charge densities is generally incorrect due to relativistic motion of constituents, and that the transverse density obtained from two-dimensional Fourier transforms provides the only observable spatial charge distribution. The non-relativistic limit only applies when the ratio of binding energy to the lightest constituent mass is less than about 0.001.

## Method

The author constructs an exactly solvable covariant scalar model where a bound state Ψ of two different scalar particles φ and ξ is described by a φ³-type interaction Lagrangian. The form factor is computed exactly using the lowest-order triangle Feynman diagram, and the result is shown to be identical to that obtained from light-front techniques and infinite-momentum-frame time-ordered perturbation theory as originally derived by Gunion et al. (1973). The transverse density is derived from the form factor via two-dimensional Fourier transform, and the non-relativistic limit is examined by varying the binding energy relative to constituent masses.

## Key numbers

- The non-relativistic limit of the form factor formula only holds for values of B/M (binding energy divided by lightest constituent mass) less than about 0.001
- For masses mimicking the quark-di-quark model of the nucleon, substantial relativistic corrections appear for any value of Q²
- Relativistic effects in a schematic nuclear model decrease the form factor for light nuclei but increase it for heavy nuclei, with corrections of order 15-20%
- The asymptotic behavior of the form factor at high Q² follows F(Q²) ~ (1/2 log² Q²)/Q², modified by logarithms from the expected power-law falloff
- For a neutral system with one heavy (mass M) and one light (mass 0.14M) constituent, the effective squared radius R*² is negative and dominated by a term proportional to 1/m₂²

## Context

This work extends the foundational studies of form factors in the infinite-momentum-frame by Weinberg (1966) and Gunion et al. (1973), and directly addresses the interpretation of recent experimental findings that the proton's G_E/G_M ratio drops rapidly with Q². The author's previous finding (Miller, 2007) that the neutron's central charge density is negative motivates the analysis of neutral systems. The paper enables a proper relativistic understanding of charge densities in hadrons and nuclei, showing that the transverse density ρ(b) rather than the three-dimensional rest-frame density is the physically observable quantity, and provides a framework for interpreting future high-Q² form factor measurements.
