# Viscous hydrodynamics

> We study the role of viscosity in the early stages of relativistic heavy ion collisions. We investigate the role of viscosity on the chemical equilibration of a parton gas. In the presence of viscosity the lifetime of the system is increased. The temperature as well as the parton fugacities evolve more slowly compared to the ideal fluid dynamics.

## Key claim

Viscosity increases the lifetime of the parton gas system formed in relativistic heavy ion collisions, causing both temperature and parton fugacities to evolve more slowly toward chemical equilibrium compared to ideal fluid dynamics, but only when using causal second-order (Israel-Stewart) theory rather than acausal first-order (Navier-Stokes-Fourier) theory.

## Method

The authors coupled second-order viscous hydrodynamics (Israel-Stewart theory) with rate equations for chemical equilibration of a massless quark-gluon plasma, considering only the dominant reactions \(gg\leftrightarrow ggg\) and \(gg\leftrightarrow q\bar{q}\). They solved the coupled evolution equations for temperature, shear pressure, and parton fugacities under longitudinal boost-invariant flow, using initial conditions relevant for RHIC and LHC energies with shear viscosity coefficients dependent on the strong coupling constant \(\alpha_s = 0.4\).

## Key numbers

- RHIC initial conditions: \(\tau_0 = 0.25\) fm/c, \(T_0 = 0.66\) GeV, \(\Phi_0 = p_0/5\), \(\lambda_{g0} = 0.34\), \(\lambda_{q0} = 0.064\)
- LHC initial conditions: \(\tau_0 = 0.25\) fm/c, \(T_0 = 1.0\) GeV, \(\Phi_0 = p_0/5\), \(\lambda_{g0} = 0.43\), \(\lambda_{q0} = 0.082\)
- Shear viscosity coefficients: \(\eta_g = b_g T^3\) with \(b_g = 0.20 (\alpha_s^2 \ln(1/\alpha_s))^{-1}\) and \(\eta_q = b_q T^3\) with \(b_q = 0.82 (\alpha_s^2 \ln(1/\alpha_s))^{-1}\)
- Reaction rates: \(R_2 \simeq 0.24 N_f \alpha_s^2 \lambda_g T \ln(1.65/\alpha_s \lambda_g)\) and \(R_3 \simeq 2.1 \alpha_s^2 T \sqrt{2\lambda_g - \lambda_g^2}\)

## Context

This work extends the earlier viscous hydrodynamics study of Muronga (2004) to include chemical non-equilibrium effects, building on prior ideal-fluid studies of chemical equilibration by Traxler, Strickland, Biro, and Muller (1999) and by Strickland, Mustafa, and Muller (2000). The results show that first-order (Navier-Stokes-Fourier) theory predicts unphysical heating during expansion and overestimates freeze-out temperatures, while second-order (Israel-Stewart) theory avoids these problems; the next step is to include transverse expansion and strange quark mass effects in the chemically non-equilibrated viscous system.
