# Empirical information on nuclear matter fourth-order symmetry energy from an extended nuclear mass formula

> We establish a relation between the equation of state (EOS) of nuclear matter and the fourth-order symmetry energy $a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)$ of finite nuclei in a semi-empirical nuclear mass formula by self-consistently considering the bulk, surface and Coulomb contributions to the nuclear mass. Such a relation allows us to extract information on nuclear matter fourth-order symmetry energy $E_{\rm{sym,4}}(ρ_0)$ at normal nuclear density $ρ_0$ from analyzing nuclear mass data. Based on the recent precise extraction of $a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)$ via the double difference of the "experimental" symmetry energy extracted from nuclear masses, for the first time, we estimate a value of $E_{\rm{sym,4}}(ρ_0) = 20.0\pm4.6$ MeV. Such a value of $E_{\rm{sym,4}}(ρ_0)$ is significantly larger than the predictions from mean-field models and thus suggests the importance of considering the effects of beyond the mean-field approximation in nuclear matter calculations.

# Empirical information on nuclear matter fourth-order symmetry energy from an extended nuclear mass formula

## Key claim

The authors estimate a value of the nuclear matter fourth-order symmetry energy at normal density of \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0) = 20.0 \pm 4.6\) MeV, which is significantly larger than mean-field model predictions (typically less than 2 MeV). This estimate relies on the smallest extracted value of the constant fourth-order symmetry energy coefficient \(c_{\rm{sym,4}} = 3.28 \pm 0.50\) MeV from prior work (Jia 2014), and the result depends on the empirical values chosen for lower-order parameters such as \(L\) and \(K_0\).

## Method

The authors extend the Danielewicz (2003) nuclear mass formula by self-consistently including corrections from the central density variation of finite nuclei and a higher-order fourth-order symmetry energy term \(a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A) I^4\). They derive an explicit relation between the fourth-order symmetry energy of finite nuclei \(a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)\) and the nuclear matter fourth-order symmetry energy \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0)\) at normal density. Using the arithmetic average of \(a_{\rm{sym,4}}(A)\) over 2348 measured nuclei from the AME2012 mass table (with \(20 < A < 270\)), they equate this average to the constant coefficient \(c_{\rm{sym,4}}\) extracted by Jia (2014) and solve for \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0)\) using empirical values of \(L\), \(K_0\), and the symmetry volume-surface ratio.

## Key numbers

- \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0) = 20.0 \pm 4.6\) MeV, estimated for the first time from nuclear mass data, using \(c_{\rm{sym,4}} = 3.28 \pm 0.50\) MeV from Jia (2014), \(L = 45.2 \pm 10.0\) MeV, \(K_0 = 240 \pm 40\) MeV, and \(E_{\rm{sym}}(\rho_0)/\beta = 2.4 \pm 0.4\).
- The constant fourth-order symmetry energy coefficient from simple mass formula fitting is \(c_{\rm{sym,4}} = 3.28 \pm 0.50\) MeV (Jia 2014), with alternative extractions giving \(8.47 \pm 0.49\) MeV (Jia 2014) and \(8.33 \pm 1.21\) MeV (Tian 2016).
- The symmetry volume-surface ratio is \(E_{\rm{sym}}(\rho_0)/\beta = 2.4\) (Danielewicz 2003, 2017; Tian 2016), leading to \(\langle D_2^2(A)\rangle = 0.2\) for the 2348 nuclei considered.
- Mean-field models generally predict \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0) \lesssim 2\) MeV (Chen 2009, Cai 2012, Argonne 2017, Pu 2017), while the quantum molecular dynamics model gives \(3.27\) to \(12.7\) MeV (Nandi 2016).

## Context

This work extends the Danielewicz (2003) mass formula by incorporating central density variations and fourth-order symmetry energy terms, enabling a direct link between finite-nuclei data and nuclear matter EOS parameters. It builds on recent extractions of the constant fourth-order symmetry energy coefficient \(c_{\rm{sym,4}}\) from nuclear mass double-difference analyses by Jia (2014, 2015), Wang (2015), and Tian (2016). The result challenges mean-field models (Chen 2009, Cai 2012, Argonne 2017, Pu 2017) that predict \(E_{\rm{sym,4}}(\rho_0) < 2\) MeV, and suggests that beyond-mean-field effects such as short-range correlations (as explored in the interacting Fermi gas model by Cai 2015) may be necessary. This work enables future studies of neutron star properties, including proton fraction at beta-equilibrium and core-crust transition density, where the fourth-order symmetry energy may have substantial effects.
