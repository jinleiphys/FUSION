# Bose-Einstein Correlations for Expanding Finite Systems or from a Hot Fireball to a Snow-Flurry

> Most boson emitting sources contain a core of finite dimensions surrounded by a large halo, due to long-lived resonances like $ω,η,η',K^{0}$ etc. When the Bose-Einstein correlation (BEC) function of the core can be determined we show that its intercept ($λ$) measures, as a function of momentum, the square of the fraction of core particles produced. A simultaneos measurement of BEC and the single-particle distributions can thus determine the characteristics of the core. If the geometrical sizes of the core are sufficiently large the parameters of the BEC function obey the $m_{t}$-scaling observed in $SPb$ and $PbPb$ reactions at CERN. The model can describe the measurements of the single- and two-particle distributions in the central region of $SPb$ reactions. A fit to experimental data shows that the freeze-out of hadrons occurs at a larger volume and at a much lower temperature than that given by the measurement of the inverse slope of the $m_{t}$-spectrum and standard BEC analysis.

## Key claim

The paper shows that when a boson-emitting source consists of a finite core surrounded by a large, unresolvable halo from long-lived resonance decays, the measured intercept parameter of the Bose-Einstein correlation function equals the square of the fraction of core particles, and this fraction can be momentum-dependent. The model can describe NA44 data for central S+Pb reactions at 200 AGeV, but only if the freeze-out occurs at a larger volume and much lower temperature than inferred from standard analysis of the transverse-mass spectrum and BEC parameters.

## Method

The authors develop a core/halo model using the Wigner-function formalism, where the emission function is split into a central core and a surrounding halo from long-lived resonances (ω, η, η', K⁰). They assume the halo size exceeds the maximum resolvable scale (≈20 fm, corresponding to Q_min ≈ 10 MeV/c), so the measured BECF for Q > 10 MeV/c depends only on the core emission function, with an effective intercept parameter λ_* equal to the square of the core fraction. They then model the core with a three-dimensionally expanding, cylindrically symmetric hydrodynamical emission function including transverse flow, temperature gradients, and finite emission duration, and fit this model simultaneously to NA44 invariant momentum distributions and BEC radius parameters for pions and kaons.

## Key numbers

- Intercept parameter for π⁺: λ = 0.56 ± 0.02 at mean transverse momentum 150 MeV, and 0.55 ± 0.02 at 450 MeV, indicating a constant core fraction
- Halo contains 25 ± 2% of all pions within NA44 acceptance (from 1 - √λ)
- Freeze-out temperature: T₀ = 100 ± 2 MeV
- Mean emission time: τ₀ = 6.9 ± 0.2 fm/c
- Core geometrical radius for pions: R_G(π) = 6.9 ± 0.4 fm
- Core geometrical radius for kaons: R_G(K) = 4.1 ± 0.4 fm
- Longitudinal geometrical size: R_G,L = 21 ± 6 fm
- Emission duration for pions: Δτ(π) = 0.1 ± 0.1 fm/c
- Emission duration for kaons: Δτ(K) = 6 ± 4 fm/c
- Transverse temperature profile parameter: a² = 0.03 ± 0.01
- Transverse flow strength parameter: b² = 0.85 ± 0.01
- Temporal temperature change parameter: d² = 0.9 ± 0.3
- χ²/ndf for full fit: 490/220

## Context

This work extends the core/halo picture previously discussed by several groups (halo1, halo2, halo3, halo4) and provides an analytic framework connecting the momentum-dependent intercept parameter to the core fraction, going beyond earlier recognition that resonance decays reduce the intercept. The model explains the m_t-scaling of BEC radius parameters observed by NA44 in S+Pb and PbPb reactions at CERN, and shows that simultaneous analysis of BECF and single-particle distributions can disentangle geometrical and thermal length-scales. The results enable future experiments to extract core properties by measuring the momentum dependence of λ, and suggest that standard BEC analyses may significantly overestimate freeze-out temperatures and underestimate source volumes.
