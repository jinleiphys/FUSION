# Shape Coexistence and Mixing of Low-Lying $0^+$ States in $^{96}$Sr

> The low energy excited $0_{2,3}^+$ states in $^{96}$Sr are amongst the most prominent examples of shape coexistence across the nuclear landscape. In this work, the neutron $[2s_{1/2}]^2$ content of the $0_{1,2,3}^+$ states in $^{96}$Sr was determined by means of the d($^{95}$Sr,p) transfer reaction at the TRIUMF-ISAC2 facility using the SHARC and TIGRESS arrays. Spectroscopic factors of 0.19(3) and 0.22(3) were extracted for the $^{96}$Sr ground and 1229~keV $0^+$ states, respectively, by fitting the experimental angular distributions to DWBA reaction model calculations. A detailed analysis of the $γ$-decay of the isomeric $0_3^+$ state was used to determine a spectroscopic factor of 0.33(13). The experimental results are compared to shell model calculations, which predict negligible spectroscopic strength for the excited $0^+$ states in $^{96}$Sr. The strengths of the excited $0_{2,3}^+$ states were also analyzed within a two-level mixing model and are consistent with a mixing strength of $a^2$=0.40(14) and a difference in intrinsic deformations of $|Δβ|=0.31(3)$. These results suggest coexistence of three different configurations in $^{96}$Sr and strong shape mixing of the two excited $0^+$ states.

# Shape Coexistence and Mixing of Low-Lying \(0^+\) States in \(^{96}\)Sr

## Key claim

The neutron \([2s_{1/2}]^2\) content of the \(0^+\) states in \(^{96}\)Sr was measured via the d(\(^{95}\)Sr,p) transfer reaction, revealing that the spherical component resides primarily in the excited \(0^+\) states rather than the ground state. The results suggest triple shape coexistence in \(^{96}\)Sr with strong mixing of the two excited \(0^+\) states, contrary to shell model predictions that place most of the \(\nu[2s_{1/2}]^2\) strength in the ground state.

## Method

The d(\(^{95}\)Sr,p) transfer reaction was performed in inverse kinematics at 5.5 \(A\)MeV at the TRIUMF-ISAC2 facility using the SHARC silicon detector array and the TIGRESS \(\gamma\)-ray detector array. Spectroscopic factors for the \(0^+_{1,2}\) states were extracted by fitting experimental angular distributions to DWBA and ADWA reaction model calculations using FRESCO, restricted to forward center-of-mass angles \(\theta_\text{cm}<45^\circ\). For the isomeric \(0^+_3\) state at 1465 keV, the spectroscopic factor was determined from a relative \(\gamma\)-ray intensity analysis of the 650 keV \(0^+_3 \rightarrow 2^+_1\) and 414 keV \(0^+_2 \rightarrow 2^+_1\) transitions, compared to Geant4 simulations of the decay.

## Key numbers

- Spectroscopic factor for the \(^{96}\)Sr ground state (\(0^+_1\)): \(C^2S = 0.19(3)\) (DWBA) or 0.15(3) (ADWA)
- Spectroscopic factor for the 1229 keV \(0^+_2\) state: \(C^2S = 0.22(3)\) (DWBA) or 0.19(3) (ADWA)
- Spectroscopic factor for the 1465 keV \(0^+_3\) state: \(C^2S = 0.33(12)\) (DWBA) or 0.29(13) (ADWA)
- Mixing strength between the two excited \(0^+\) states: \(a^2 = 0.40(14)\)
- Difference in intrinsic deformations: \(|\Delta\beta| = 0.31(3)\)
- Relative population strength of the 1465 keV state compared to the 1229 keV state: 1.50(52)
- Monopole transition strength between \(0^+_2\) and \(0^+_3\) states: \(\rho^2(E0) = 0.185(50)\) (from prior work by Jung, 1980)

## Context

This work extends studies of shape coexistence in the \(A \approx 100\) region, particularly in Sr and Zr isotopes near \(N=60\) where a sudden onset of deformation occurs. Prior experimental work by Clement (2016) and Park (2016) on \(^{98}\)Sr found weak mixing between coexisting shapes, while the present study reveals strong mixing in \(^{96}\)Sr. The results contradict shell model calculations using the glek interaction, which predict negligible spectroscopic strength for the excited \(0^+\) states, and challenge the complex excited VAMPIR calculations by Petrovici (2012) that could not reproduce the strong \(E0\) transition. The findings enable future large-scale shell model and beyond mean field calculations to better describe the single-particle structure at the shape transition.
