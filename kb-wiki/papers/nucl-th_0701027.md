# E0 emission in alpha + ^12C fusion at astrophysical energies

> We show that E0 emission in alpha + ^12C fusion at astrophysically interesting energies is negligible compared to E1 and E2 emission.

# E0 emission in alpha + 12C fusion at astrophysical energies

## Key claim
E0 emission in alpha + 12C fusion at astrophysically interesting energies is negligible compared to E1 and E2 emission, with the best estimate for the E0 contribution to the astrophysical S-factor being 0.02 keV b at 0.3 MeV center-of-mass energy, which is roughly 10,000 times smaller than the combined E1 and E2 S-factor of about 170 keV b.

## Method
The authors derived a general relation for the direct E0/E2 cross section ratio using radial matrix elements and penetrability factors, then performed potential model calculations using a real Woods-Saxon potential with radius parameter 1.25 fm and diffuseness 0.65 fm to bind the 0_1^+ ground state, 0_2^+ state, and 2_1^+ state of 16O at their measured energies. They also evaluated contributions from tails of higher-lying 0^+ resonances using Breit-Wigner extrapolation with s-wave penetrability and known monopole decay strengths.

## Key numbers
- Total S-factor for 12C + alpha capture at E_c.m. = 0.3 MeV: about 170 keV b (required by nucleosynthesis)
- E1 and E2 cross sections at 0.3 MeV: sigma_E1(0.3) approximately equal to sigma_E2(0.3) approximately 1.4 x 10^-17 b
- Direct (nonresonant) E0/E2 cross section ratio at 0.3 MeV: 4.3 x 10^-3 (from penetrability estimate)
- Potential model E0/E2 cross section ratio at 0.3 MeV: 2.6 x 10^-4
- Best estimate for E0 S-factor at 0.3 MeV: S_E0(0.3) = 0.02 keV b
- Potential model E2 S-factor at 0.3 MeV: S_E2(0.3) = 85 keV b (within factor of 2 of measured values)
- Radial matrix element ratio |R_00|^2/|R_02|^2 at 0.3 MeV: 1.1 (potential model), compared to 3.2 from pure Coulomb scattering and 18 from penetrability estimate
- Q-value for the reaction: 7.16 MeV

## Context
This work addresses the 12C + alpha capture reaction, known as the "Holy Grail" of nuclear astrophysics, which determines the 16O to 12C ratio at the end of helium burning in stars. The authors extend the approach of Snover and Hurd (2003) for direct E0 emission in 3He + 4He fusion to the 12C + alpha system, where E0 emission had not been previously examined. The results confirm that the dominant contributions remain E1 and E2 decay to the 16O ground state, with approximately equal intensity as found by Hammer et al. (2005), and that no other electromagnetic processes (E0 emission or two-photon emission) contribute significantly to the astrophysical rate. This enables continued reliance on single-photon gamma-ray measurements for determining the astrophysical S-factor.
