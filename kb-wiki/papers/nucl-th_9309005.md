# Hanbury-Brown--Twiss Analysis in a Solvable Model

> The analysis of meson correlations by Hanbury-Brown--Twiss interferometry is tested with a simple model of meson production by resonance decay. We derive conditions which should be satisfied in order to relate the measured momentum correlation to the classical source size. The Bose correlation effects are apparent in both the ratio of meson pairs to singles and in the ratio of like to unlike pairs. With our parameter values, we find that the single particle distribution is too distorted by the correlation to allow a straightforward analysis using pair correlation normalized by the singles rates. An analysis comparing symmetrized to unsymmetrized pairs is more robust, but nonclassical off-shell effects are important at realistic temperatures.

## Key claim

The Hanbury-Brown–Twiss (HBT) correlation analysis can be tested with a solvable model of meson production by resonance decay, but the standard doubles-to-singles correlation function is too distorted by Bose correlations to allow straightforward extraction of source size; the like-to-unlike correlation function is more robust, though nonclassical off-shell effects remain important at realistic temperatures.

## Method

The authors constructed a one-dimensional quantum mechanical model in which an initial excitation decays into two resonances and N additional spectator particles, with each resonance subsequently emitting a meson. They computed the two-particle decay rates from the symmetrized Feynman amplitudes and evaluated the doubles-to-singles and like-to-unlike correlation functions, comparing finite-N results to the thermal (canonical) limit as N increases. They also derived the classical limit of the one-particle Wigner function by expanding the resonance propagator to first order in momentum difference and imposing on-shell conditions, then used this classical source to compute the correlation function via Pratt's formula.

## Key numbers

- The resonance width used for the Delta resonance was Γ_r = 115 MeV, with masses m_π = 139 MeV, m_f = 939 MeV, and m_r = 1232 MeV.
- The temperature used in the thermal limit calculations was T = 3 m_π, corresponding to total energy E = 1.5 m_π N.
- The width of the correlation function Δq_c was approximately 3/4 m_π, and the typical momentum difference <(q_1−q_2)^2>^{1/2} was approximately 3 m_π, giving a ratio Δq_c / <(q_1−q_2)^2>^{1/2} ≈ 25%.
- Reducing the resonance width to Γ_r = Γ_Δ/10 (i.e., 11.5 MeV) made the singles distortion negligible and restored the expected correlation function shape.
- The effective mean square source size at q_av = 0 from the classical source was <x^2>_cl^I = 2 (k_f^0 / m_r / Γ_r)^2, where k_f^0 = √(2 m_f m_r (1 − ω/m_diff)) evaluated at q=0.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical foundation of HBT interferometry in nuclear physics by testing the statistical assumptions underlying the standard analysis, particularly the factorization of the two-particle Wigner function and the neglect of Bose symmetrization in the singles spectrum. The model is motivated by meson production in nucleon-antinucleon annihilation and e⁺e⁻ annihilation, and the authors note that similar distortions of 10–20% were found in Fritiof Monte Carlo simulations of NA35 data (KS92) and in experimental analyses (Hu93). The results enable more careful interpretation of source size extraction from pion and kaon correlations measured in experiments such as Akesson (1987), Sedykh (1992), and others cited in the paper.
