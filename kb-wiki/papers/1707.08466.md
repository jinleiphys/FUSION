# Constraining the equation of state with identified particle spectra

> We show that in a central nucleus-nucleus collision, the variation of the mean transverse mass with the multiplicity is determined, up to a rescaling, by the variation of the energy over entropy ratio as a function of the entropy density, thus providing a direct link between experimental data and the equation of state. Each colliding energy thus probes the equation of state at an effective entropy density, whose approximate value is $19$ fm$^{-3}$ for Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV and $41$ fm$^{-3}$ for Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV, corresponding to temperatures of $227$ MeV and $279$ MeV if the equation of state is taken from lattice calculations. The relative change of the mean transverse mass as a function of the colliding energy gives a direct measure of the pressure over energy density ratio $P/ε$, at the corresponding effective density. Using RHIC and LHC data, we obtain $P/ε=0.21\pm 0.10$, in agreement with the lattice value $P/ε=0.23$ in the corresponding temperature range. Measurements over a wide range of colliding energies using a single detector with good particle identification would help reducing the error.

# Constraining the equation of state with identified particle spectra

## Key claim

The variation of the mean transverse mass with multiplicity in central nucleus-nucleus collisions is determined, up to rescaling factors, by the variation of the energy over entropy ratio as a function of entropy density, providing a direct link between experimental data and the equation of state. The relative change of mean transverse mass with colliding energy gives a direct measure of the pressure over energy density ratio P/epsilon at the corresponding effective density, though the mapping depends on the unknown initial transverse size R0 and on transport coefficients.

## Method

The authors performed (2+1)-dimensional ideal and viscous hydrodynamic simulations with boost-invariant longitudinal expansion, using a Monte Carlo Glauber model for initial conditions with proper time tau0 = 0.4 fm/c. They varied the equation of state around the lattice QCD result by changing either the high-temperature limit of P/T^4 (number of degrees of freedom) or the transition temperature range, then converted fluid to hadrons via Cooper-Frye freeze-out at Tf = 140 MeV including resonance decays. The mapping between observables (mean transverse mass <m_T> and multiplicity dN/dy) and thermodynamic quantities (epsilon/s and s) was established through dimensionless parameters a and b fitted simultaneously across all equations of state.

## Key numbers

- Effective entropy density for Au+Au at 200 GeV: 19 fm^-3 (PHENIX data) or 19.9 fm^-3 (STAR data)
- Effective entropy density for Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV: 40.7 +/- 2.6 fm^-3
- Effective temperature for Au+Au at 200 GeV: 227 +/- 4 MeV (PHENIX) or 231 +/- 5 MeV (STAR)
- Effective temperature for Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV: 279 +/- 5 MeV
- P/epsilon = 0.21 +/- 0.10 obtained from RHIC and LHC data, compared to lattice value 0.23 in the corresponding temperature range
- Entropy per particle at freeze-out before decays: (S/N)_Tf = 6.5
- Fit parameter b = 0.202, compared to (N/S)_Tf = 0.154
- Uncertainty on parameter a from viscosity: 7%; uncertainty on parameter b from viscosity: 11%
- MCKLN model gives R0 values 5% smaller than Glauber model, implying 15% larger density

## Context

This work extends the original idea of Van Hove (1982) that the transition from hadronic gas to quark-gluon plasma should produce a flattening of mean transverse momentum as a function of multiplicity. It provides a simple analytic mapping between heavy-ion observables and the equation of state, complementing Bayesian approaches such as Pratt (2015) and Sangaline (2015) that constrain the equation of state through simultaneous fits but lack transparent relationships. The result enables direct experimental extraction of P/epsilon from the energy dependence of identified particle spectra, and shows that current RHIC and LHC data are compatible with the lattice QCD equation of state from Borsanyi (2010) and Bazavov (2014), while ruling out equations of state with very high pressure (EOS A and B). Future measurements at 5.02 TeV with a single detector would probe temperatures near 300 MeV and reduce experimental errors.
