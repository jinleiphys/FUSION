# Thermodynamic analogy for quantum phase transitions at zero temperature

> We propose a relationship between thermodynamic phase transitions and ground-state quantum phase transitions in systems with variable Hamiltonian parameters. It is based on a link between zeros of the canonical partition function at complex temperatures and exceptional points of a quantum Hamiltonian in the complex-extended parameter space. This approach is applied in the interacting boson model, where it is shown to properly distinguish the first- and second-order phase transitions.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a thermodynamic analogy for zero-temperature quantum phase transitions by linking zeros of the canonical partition function at complex temperatures to exceptional points of a quantum Hamiltonian in a complex-extended parameter space, but the authors acknowledge that the order of the quantum phase transition deduced from the alternative specific heat \(C_2\) cannot be _a priori_ expected to coincide with the order determined via the standard specific heat \(C_1\).

## Method

The authors relate ground-state quantum phase transitions to thermodynamic phase transitions by connecting zeros of the canonical partition function at complex temperatures to exceptional points (branch points) of the Hamiltonian in the complex-extended parameter space. They define two "specific heat" quantities: \(C_1 = -\lambda d^2E_0/d\lambda^2\) based on the ground-state energy, and \(C_2\) derived from a thermodynamic analogy using the partial discriminant \(D_0(\lambda)\) whose zeros represent exceptional points on the ground-state Riemann sheet. These quantities are computed for the interacting boson model (IBM) with Hamiltonian \(H(\lambda) = (1-\lambda)[-q_\chi\cdot q_\chi/N] + \lambda n_d\) for boson numbers up to \(N=1000\), using the O(5) symmetry for the \(\chi=0\) case to enable calculations for large dimensions.

## Key numbers

* For the IBM second-order quantum phase transition (\(\chi=0\)), the normalized maximal value of \(C_1\) converges to \(A_1/N \to 12.5\) in the \(N\to\infty\) limit.
* For the first-order quantum phase transition (\(\chi=-\sqrt{7}/2\)), the critical parameter is \(\lambda_c(\chi) = (4+2\chi^2/7)/(5+2\chi^2/7)\).
* The dimension of the \(J=0\) subspace grows roughly as \(n \sim N^2/12\) for very large boson numbers, while the total number of IBM states is \(\sim N^5/120\).
* For the second-order QPT, the correct normalization of \(C_2\) is by \(1/N^2\), while for the first-order QPT, \(C_1/N\) diverges at \(\lambda_c\).
* For the \(\chi=0\) case using only the \(v=0\) subspace (dimension \(n\sim N/2\)), the power \(\kappa\) in the scaling \(S\propto 1/N^\kappa\) satisfies \(\kappa < 1.35\) for boson numbers up to 5000.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of quantum phase transitions in the interacting boson model by Dieperink, Scholten, and Iachello (1980) and Feng, Gilmore, and Deans (1981), and builds on the connection between exceptional points and quantum phase transitions established by Heiss (1988, 1994). The method enables indirect measurement of the distribution of exceptional points on the ground-state Riemann sheet near the real parameter axis, which is numerically difficult to calculate directly for large dimensions, and is expected to be applicable to other quantum phase transition systems beyond nuclear physics.
