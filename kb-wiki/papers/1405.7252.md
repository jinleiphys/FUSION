# Transverse-momentum spectra of strange particles produced in Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV in the chemical non-equilibrium model

> We analyze the transverse-momentum spectra of strange hadrons produced in Pb+Pb collisions at the collision energy $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV. Our approach combines the concept of chemical non-equilibrium with the single-freeze-out scenario. The two ideas are realized in the framework of the Cracow model, whose thermodynamic parameters have been established in earlier studies of the ratios of hadron multiplicities. The geometric parameters of the model are obtained from the fit to the spectra of pions and kaons, only. Using these parameters, we obtain an excellent description of the spectra of protons and the $K_S^0$, $K^*(892)^0$, and $ϕ(1020)$ mesons. A satisfactory description is also obtained for the $Λ$, $Ξ$ and $Ω$ hyperons. Further improvement of the hyperon spectra may be achieved if we assume that they are emitted from a smaller, internal part of the system but at the same thermodynamic conditions. Our work not only includes all particle species measured up to now in heavy-ion collisions at the LHC energies but, in addition, discusses the centrality dependence of the particle production.

## Key claim

The chemical non-equilibrium version of the Cracow single-freeze-out model, using thermodynamic parameters fixed from earlier fits to hadron multiplicity ratios and geometric parameters fitted only to pion and kaon transverse-momentum spectra, provides an excellent description of the spectra of protons, \(K_S^0\), \(K^*(892)^0\), and \(\phi(1020)\) mesons, and a satisfactory description of \(\Lambda\), \(\Xi\), and \(\Omega\) hyperons, with the caveat that hyperon spectra can be further improved by assuming emission from a smaller internal volume at the same thermodynamic conditions.

## Method

The authors used the Monte-Carlo implementation of the Cracow model (THERMINATOR) with a Hubble-like flow at freeze-out and the Cooper-Frye formula. Thermodynamic parameters (temperature \(T\), non-equilibrium factors \(\gamma_q\) and \(\gamma_s\)) were taken from earlier fits to hadron multiplicity ratios by Petran et al. (2013). Geometric parameters (maximum transverse radius \(r_{\rm max}\) and freeze-out proper time \(\tau_f\)) were obtained by fitting only the transverse-momentum spectra of pions (\(\pi^++\pi^-\)) and kaons (\(K^++K^-\)) across all centrality classes, using the volume per unit rapidity from Petran et al. (2013) to reduce the fit to a single parameter \(r_{\rm max}/\tau_f\). The same parameters were then used to predict spectra for all other hadron species.

## Key numbers

- Temperature in the non-equilibrium model is about 140 MeV, lower than the transition temperature \(T_c = 150\)–170 MeV from the Wuppertal-Budapest Collaboration.
- The value of \(\gamma_q\) used corresponds to a pion chemical potential \(\mu_\pi = 2T\ln\gamma_q \simeq 134\) MeV, very close to the \(\pi^0\) mass of about 134.98 MeV.
- The \(\chi^2\) for the joint fit of pion and kaon spectra in the non-equilibrium model is three times better than in the equilibrium model for central and semi-central collisions.
- For hyperons, reducing the emission volume by 20% for \(\Lambda\) and \(\Xi\) and by 30% for \(\Omega\) yields remarkable agreement with data.
- The freeze-out radius for \(\Omega\) and \(\Xi\) is \(r = \sqrt{0.7}\, r_{\rm max}\) and for \(\Lambda\) is \(r = \sqrt{0.8}\, r_{\rm max}\), while all other particles are produced up to \(r = r_{\rm max}\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier study (Begun et al., 2013) that analyzed pions, kaons, and protons, to include all strange hadrons measured by the ALICE collaboration at LHC energies. It addresses the "proton puzzle", the failure of equilibrium statistical models to describe proton yields, by using a chemical non-equilibrium approach with \(\gamma_q > 1\) and \(\gamma_s > 1\), following the work of Petran et al. (2013). The simultaneous description of short-lived \(K^*(892)^0\) and long-lived \(\phi(1020)\) supports the single-freeze-out approximation, and the results suggest that a new physics mechanism of particle production may be at work at LHC energies compared to lower energies where equilibrium models were successful (Prorok, 2006).
