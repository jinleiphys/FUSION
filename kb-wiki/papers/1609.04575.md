# Model prediction for temperature dependence of meson pole masses from lattice QCD results on meson screening masses

> We propose a practical effective model by introducing temperature ($T$) dependence to the coupling strengths of four-quark and six-quark Kobayashi-Maskawa-'t Hooft interactions in the 2+1 flavor Polyakov-loop extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model. The $T$ dependence is determined from LQCD data on the renormalized chiral condensate around the pseudocritical temperature $T_c^χ$ of chiral crossover and the screening-mass difference between $π$ and $a_0$ mesons in $T > 1.1T_c^χ$ where only the $U(1)_{\rm A}$-symmetry breaking survives. The model well reproduces LQCD data on screening masses $M_ξ^{\rm scr}(T)$ for both scalar and pseudoscalar mesons, particularly in $T \ge T_c^χ$. Using this effective model, we predict meson pole masses $M_ξ^{\rm pole}(T)$ for scalar and pseudoscalar mesons. For $η'$ meson, the prediction is consistent with the experimental value at finite $T$ measured in heavy-ion collisions. We point out that the relation $M_ξ^{\rm scr}(T)-M_ξ^{\rm pole}(T) \approx M_{ξ'}^{\rm scr}(T)-M_{ξ'}^{\rm pole}(T)$ is pretty good when $ξ$ and $ξ'$ are scalar mesons, and show that the relation $M_ξ^{\rm scr}(T)/M_{ξ'}^{\rm scr}(T) \approx M_ξ^{\rm pole}(T)/M_{ξ'}^{\rm pole}(T)$ is well satisfied within 20% error when $ξ$ and $ξ'$ are pseudoscalar mesons and also when $ξ$ and $ξ'$ are scalar mesons.

## Key claim

The authors propose a practical effective model by introducing temperature-dependent coupling strengths for four-quark and six-quark interactions in the 2+1 flavor Polyakov-loop extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) model, where the temperature dependence is determined from lattice QCD data on the renormalized chiral condensate around the pseudocritical temperature and the screening-mass difference between pi and a0 mesons above 1.1 times the pseudocritical temperature, enabling prediction of meson pole masses that are not directly calculable in lattice QCD.

## Method

The authors introduced temperature-dependent coupling strengths G_S(T) and G_D(T) into the 2+1 flavor PNJL model, where G_S(T) was determined from lattice QCD data on the renormalized chiral condensate Delta_{l,s}(T) and the pseudocritical temperature T_c^chi, while G_D(T) was determined from lattice QCD data on the screening-mass difference between pi and a0 mesons in T > 1.1T_c^chi = 170 MeV. The model parameters were fitted using the Pauli-Villars regularization scheme to maintain Lorentz invariance, and meson pole masses were calculated via the random-phase approximation to the Schwinger-Dyson equation for the mesonic correlation function.

## Key numbers

* Pseudocritical temperature of chiral crossover: T_c^chi = 154 +/- 9 MeV, determined from lattice QCD simulations by Borsanyi (2010) and Bazavov (2011)
* Parameter T_1 = 121 MeV and b_1 = 43.5 MeV for the temperature-dependent KMT coupling G_D(T)
* Parameter T_2 = 131 MeV and b_2 = 83.3 MeV for the temperature-dependent four-quark coupling G_S(T)
* At vacuum, the model reproduces M_pi = 138 MeV, M_K = 495 MeV, M_eta' = 958 MeV, and f_pi = 92.4 MeV using parameter set (A) with m_l = 8 MeV, m_s = 191 MeV, G_S(0)Lambda^2 = 2.72, G_D(0)Lambda^5 = 40.4, and Lambda = 660 MeV
* The relation M_xi^scr(T)/M_xi'^scr(T) is approximately equal to M_xi^pole(T)/M_xi'^pole(T) within 20% error for pseudoscalar mesons and for scalar mesons

## Context

This work extends the approach of Ishii (2015) who determined temperature dependence of the KMT coupling from lattice QCD data on screening-mass differences, and builds on the PNJL model framework developed by Fukushima (2004), Ratti (2006), and others. The model enables prediction of meson pole masses from lattice QCD screening mass data, which is important because pole masses are experimentally measurable but difficult to compute directly in lattice QCD simulations at finite temperature, while screening masses are accessible in lattice QCD but not experimentally measurable. The predicted temperature dependence of the eta' meson pole mass can be compared with experimental measurements from heavy-ion collisions by Csorgo (2010) and the STAR collaboration (2014).
