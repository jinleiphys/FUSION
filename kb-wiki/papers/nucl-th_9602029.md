# Astrophysical Reaction Rates for $^{10}$B(p,$α$)$^{7}$Be and $^{11}$B(p,$α$)$^{8}$Be From a Direct Model

> The reactions $^{10}$B(p,$α$)$^{7}$Be and $^{11}$B(p,$α$)$^{8}$Be are studied at thermonuclear energies using DWBA calculations. For both reactions, transitions to the ground states and first excited states are investigated. In the case of $^{10}$B(p,$α$)$^{7}$Be, a resonance at $E_{Res}=10$ keV can be consistently described in the potential model, thereby allowing the extension of the astrophysical $S$-factor data to very low energies. Strong interference with a resonance at about $E_{Res}=550$ keV require a Breit-Wigner description of that resonance and the introduction of an interference term for the reaction $^{10}$B(p,$α_1$)$^{7}$Be$^*$. Two isospin $T=1$ resonances (at $E_{Res1}=149$ keV and $E_{Res2}=619$ keV) observed in the $^{11}$B+p reactions necessitate Breit-Wigner resonance and interference terms to fit the data of the $^{11}$B(p,$α$)$^{8}$Be reaction. $S$-factors and thermonuclear reaction rates are given for each reaction. The present calculation is the first consistent parametrization for the transition to the ground states and first excited states at low energies.

## Key claim

The authors use a direct reaction potential model (DWBA) to consistently describe a resonance at 10 keV in the $^{10}$B(p,$\alpha$)$^{7}$Be reaction, extending astrophysical S-factor data to very low energies, but note that the calculated energy dependence of the S-factor is only valid for bare nuclei and that electron screening effects cause deviations in experimental data at the lowest measured energies.

## Method

Zero-range DWBA calculations using the code TETRA were performed for the $^{10}$B(p,$\alpha$)$^{7}$Be and $^{11}$B(p,$\alpha$)$^{8}$Be reactions at thermonuclear energies, with optical potentials obtained from a folding potential method using DDM3Y effective NN interactions and density distributions from DeVries (1987). For $^{10}$B(p,$\alpha_0$)$^{7}$Be, the strength factor $\lambda$ in the proton channel was adjusted to produce an s-wave resonance at $E_{Res}=0.01$ MeV with a phase shift of $\pi/2$; for the $^{10}$B(p,$\alpha_1$)$^{7}$Be$^*$ transition, a single-level Breit-Wigner term for a 5/2$^+$ resonance at about 550 keV and an interference term were added to the DWBA contribution. For $^{11}$B(p,$\alpha$)$^{8}$Be, two isospin T=1 resonances at 149 keV and 619 keV were described by Breit-Wigner terms with an interference term between the non-resonant DWBA contribution and the 619 keV resonance.

## Key numbers

- $^{10}$B(p,$\alpha_0$)$^{7}$Be: resonance at $E_{Res}=10$ keV with width about 15 keV, consistent with measurements from Wiescher (1983) and Ajzenberg-Selove (1990)
- $^{10}$B(p,$\alpha_1$)$^{7}$Be$^*$: interfering 5/2$^+$ resonance at about $E_{Res}=550$ keV (with example fit parameters: $\Gamma_{\alpha}=0.1$ keV, $\theta_l=0.49$ or $\Gamma_{\alpha}=500.0$ keV, $\theta_l=0.01$)
- $^{11}$B(p,$\alpha_1$)$^{8}$Be$^*$: resonance at $E_{c.m.}=149$ keV ($J^\pi=2^+$, $l_R=1$) with $\Gamma_\alpha=5.7$ keV and $\theta_p^2=0.570$; resonance at $E_{c.m.}=660$ keV ($J^\pi=2^-$, $l_R=0$) with $\Gamma_\alpha=296.5$ keV and $\theta_p^2=0.604$
- $^{11}$B(p,$\alpha_0$)$^{8}$Be: resonance at $E_{c.m.}=148.5$ keV with $\Gamma_\alpha=5.5$ keV and $\theta_p^2=0.017$
- $^{10}$B(p,$\alpha_1$)$^{7}$Be$^*$ contribution is less than $10^{-3}$ of the rate for the ground state transition
- $^{11}$B(p,$\alpha_0$)$^{8}$Be contribution is about $10^{-2}$ of the rate for the transition to the first excited state

## Context

This work extends previous DWBA analyses of sub-Coulomb transfer reactions by Raimann (1990), Herndl (1991), and Rauscher (1992) to (p,$\alpha$) reactions on boron isotopes, providing the first consistent parametrization for transitions to ground states and first excited states at low energies. The calculated reaction rates revise the earlier compilations by Fowler, Caughlan and Zimmerman (1975) and Caughlan and Fowler (1988), which did not include the 10 keV resonance in $^{10}$B(p,$\alpha$)$^{7}$Be and assumed a fairly constant S-factor at low energies. The results enable improved modeling of $^{10}$B destruction in astrophysical scenarios and better assessment of $^{11}$B as a fusion fuel, where the $^{10}$B(p,$\alpha$)$^{7}$Be reaction produces $^7$Be contaminations.
