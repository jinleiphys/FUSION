# QCD Perturbation Theory at Finite Temperature/Density and its Application

> In order to describe properties of an equilibrated quark-gluon plasma, QCD at finite temperature (and density) has to be considered. Besides lattice calculations, which can be applied only to static quantities at zero density, perturbative QCD has been used. At finite temperature, however, serious problems such as infrared divergent and gauge dependent results have been encountered. These difficulties can be (partially) avoided if one starts from effective Green functions constructed by resumming a certain class of diagrams (hard thermal loops). Within the last few years this improved perturbation theory (Braaten-Pisarski method) turned out to be a powerful tool for computing interesting quantities of the quark-gluon plasma phase. In the present talk a basic introduction to the Braaten-Pisarski method is provided and its applications and limits are reviewed. In particular, damping rates, the energy loss of energetic partons, thermalization times, the viscosity of the quark-gluon plasma, and the production of photons and dileptons are discussed.

# QCD Perturbation Theory at Finite Temperature/Density and its Application

## Key claim

The Braaten-Pisarski hard thermal loop (HTL) resummation method provides a consistent, gauge-independent perturbative framework for computing properties of the quark-gluon plasma, but only under the assumption of weak coupling (α_s ≪ 1); for realistic coupling values (α_s > 0.2) the method fails for quantities involving energies of order T or smaller, while it produces reasonable results for high-energy observables (E ≫ T).

## Method

The author reviews the HTL resummation technique, in which effective Green functions are constructed by resumming hard thermal loop diagrams into effective propagators and vertices. This method is applied to compute damping rates, energy loss, thermalization times, viscosity, and photon/dilepton production rates in a quark-gluon plasma at finite temperature and chemical potential, using resummed propagators for soft momenta (p² ∼ α_s T²) and bare propagators for hard momenta (p² ∼ T²).

## Key numbers

- Effective gluon mass: m_g² = g²T²(1 + N_f/6)/3, where N_f is the number of thermalized quark flavors
- At finite chemical potential: m_g² = (g²T²/3)[1 + (1/6)(N_f + (3/π²)∑_f μ_f²/T²)]
- Plasmon damping rate at zero momentum: γ = 6.636 g²T/(8π) (gauge independent result)
- Damping rate of a hard quark: γ_q = (g²T/(3π)) ln(1/g)
- Collisional energy loss of an energetic quark: dE/dx = (16π/9) α_s² T² ln(9E/(16π α_s T)); for α_s = 0.3–0.5, T = 200–300 MeV, E = 10–50 GeV, dE/dx ≃ 0.5–1.5 GeV/fm
- Inverse thermalization time for quarks: τ_q⁻¹ = 2.5 α_s² T ln(0.21/α_s)
- Inverse thermalization time for gluons: τ_g⁻¹ = 6.6 α_s² T ln(0.19/α_s)
- Shear viscosity coefficient: η = (T³/α_s²)[0.11/ln(0.19/α_s) + 0.37/ln(0.21/α_s)]
- Hard photon production rate: E dR/d³p = (5α α_s/(18π²)) e^{-E/T} (T² + μ²/π²) ln(0.13E/(α_s T))
- Hard dilepton production rate: dR/(d⁴x d⁴p) = (10/(27π³)) α² α_s (T²/M²) e^{-E/T} (ln(T(m_q + k_min)/m_q²) + C), with m_q² = g²T²/6 and C ≃ −0.6

## Context

This review extends the work of Braaten and Pisarski (1990) on HTL resummation, which resolved the gauge-dependent and infrared-divergent results found in naive finite-temperature perturbation theory by Kajantie and Lopez. The method enables calculations of QGP signatures relevant to heavy ion collisions at SPS, RHIC, and LHC, including jet quenching and electromagnetic probes. Open problems remain: the method assumes weak coupling (α_s ≪ 1) and fails for realistic couplings when energies are of order T or smaller, and infrared singularities persist from the absence of static magnetic screening (requiring non-perturbative treatment as noted by Linde) and from collinear divergences at the light cone (requiring improved resummation as proposed by Rebhan).
