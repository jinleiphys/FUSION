# Signatures of alpha clustering in light nuclei from relativistic nuclear collisions

> We argue that relativistic nuclear collisions may provide experimental evidence of alpha clustering in light nuclei. A light alpha-clustered nucleus has a large intrinsic deformation. When collided against a heavy nucleus at very high energies, this deformation transforms into the deformation of the fireball in the transverse plane. The subsequent collective evolution of the fireball leads to harmonic flow reflecting the deformation of the initial shape, which can be measured with standard methods of relativistic heavy-ion collisions. We illustrate the feasibility of the idea by modeling the 12C-208Pb collisions and point out that very significant quantitative and qualitative differences between the alpha-clustered and uniform 12C nucleus occur in such quantities as the triangular flow, its event-by-event fluctuations, or the correlations of the elliptic and triangular flows. The proposal offers a possibility of studying low-energy nuclear phenomena with "snapshots" made with relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

# Signatures of alpha clustering in light nuclei from relativistic nuclear collisions

## Key claim

The authors argue that ultra-relativistic collisions of light alpha-clustered nuclei (such as 12C) against heavy nuclei (such as 208Pb) can reveal the geometric cluster structure through harmonic flow measurements, provided that the fireball is sufficiently large and dense to exhibit collective behavior. The claim is conditional on the existence of collectivity in the subsequent evolution, which is expected for asymmetric light-on-heavy collisions but not necessarily for light-light collisions.

## Method

The authors model 12C-208Pb collisions at SPS energy (sqrt(s_NN)=17 GeV) using a Glauber Monte Carlo approach implemented in GLISSANDO, with three configurations for the 12C nucleus: a Bose-Einstein condensation (BEC) clustered case, a fermionic molecular dynamics (FMD) clustered case, and an unclustered uniform case. The nucleon distributions in the clustered cases are generated by placing three alpha clusters at the vertices of an equilateral triangle with side length optimized to reproduce the elastic charge form factor data, with nucleons distributed within each cluster via Gaussian radial profiles and short-distance repulsion enforced. The resulting fireball eccentricities (ellipticity epsilon_2 and triangularity epsilon_3) are computed event-by-event from the transverse-plane distribution of wounded nucleons and binary collisions, and their averages and fluctuations are analyzed as functions of the number of wounded nucleons.

## Key numbers

- Average triangularity in the cluster plane: 0.59 for the BEC case, 0.55 for the FMD case, and 0 for the unclustered case
- Average ellipticity in the plane perpendicular to the cluster plane: 0.61 for the BEC case, 0.58 for the FMD case, and 0 for the unclustered case
- Cluster separation in 12C: approximately 3 fm between alpha clusters
- Alpha cluster binding energy per nucleon: B_alpha/4 ~ 7 MeV; bonding between alpha particles: V_alpha_alpha/bond ~ 2 MeV
- Charge proton radius squared used in unfolding: r_p^2 = 0.77 fm^2
- Short-distance NN repulsion distance: 0.9 fm
- Total NN inelastic cross section at SPS energy: sigma_NN_inel = 32 mb
- Mixed model parameter for particle production: a = 0.12
- Correlation coefficient between ellipticity and triangularity for the BEC clustered case: rho(epsilon_2, epsilon_3) ~ -0.3

## Context

This work extends the methodology of harmonic flow analysis, well-established in relativistic heavy-ion collisions for studying initial-state anisotropies (Ollitrault 1992, Voloshin 2008), to the domain of nuclear structure, proposing that alpha clustering in light nuclei can be probed via relativistic collisions. It builds on the alpha cluster model dating back to Gamow (1931) and later developments including the Brink (1965) cluster model, the BEC description of 12C by Funaki (2006), and the FMD calculations of Chernykh (2007) that reproduce the elastic charge form factor. The proposal enables a new experimental approach to study low-energy nuclear structure phenomena using high-energy collisions, complementing traditional fragmentation studies (Zarubin) and extending recent work on deformed heavy systems such as U-U and Cu-Au at RHIC (Voloshin 2010) as well as ongoing studies of d-Au and 3He-Au collisions (Sickles 2013).
