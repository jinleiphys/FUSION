# Insights into the Emergence of Mass from Studies of Pion and Kaon Structure

> There are two mass generating mechanisms in the standard model of particle physics (SM). One is related to the Higgs boson and fairly well understood. The other is embedded in quantum chromodynamics (QCD), the SM's strong interaction piece; and although responsible for emergence of the roughly 1 GeV mass scale that characterises the proton and hence all observable matter, the source and impacts of this emergent hadronic mass (EHM) remain puzzling. As bound states seeded by a valence-quark and -antiquark, pseudoscalar mesons present a simpler problem in quantum field theory than that associated with the nucleon. Consequently, there is a large array of robust predictions for pion and kaon properties whose empirical validation will provide a clear window onto many effects of both mass generating mechanisms and the constructive interference between them. This has now become significant because new-era experimental facilities, in operation, construction, or planning, are capable of conducting such tests and thereby contributing greatly to resolving the puzzles of EHM. These aspects of experiment, phenomenology, and theory, along with contemporary successes and challenges, are sketched herein, simultaneously highlighting the potential gains that can accrue from a coherent effort aimed at finally reaching an understanding of the character and structure of Nature's Nambu-Goldstone modes.

## Key claim

The paper argues that pseudoscalar mesons (pions and kaons) provide a simpler quantum field theory problem than the nucleon for studying emergent hadronic mass (EHM), and that new experimental facilities can test robust predictions for their properties to resolve puzzles about EHM, though the abstract's claim of a "large array of robust predictions" is hedged by the paper's focus on sketching "contemporary successes and challenges" rather than claiming definitive resolution.

## Method

The authors review theoretical approaches including continuum Schwinger function methods (Dyson-Schwinger equations and Bethe-Salpeter equations) and lattice-regularized QCD, alongside experimental programs using the Sullivan process for meson structure studies. They synthesize predictions for pion and kaon distribution amplitudes, distribution functions, form factors, and generalized parton distributions, comparing results from different theoretical frameworks and assessing experimental capabilities at facilities like Jefferson Lab (12 GeV), the future Electron-Ion Collider, and CERN.

## Key numbers

- The emergent hadronic mass scale characterizing the proton is roughly 1 GeV, as stated in the abstract
- Pion distribution function calculations are presented at scales of 2 GeV and 5.2 GeV (from section headings in the table of contents)
- The paper references the pion mass of approximately 140 MeV and kaon mass of approximately 494 MeV as known Nambu-Goldstone boson masses (standard values implied by context)
- Jefferson Lab 12 GeV experiments E12-06-101, E12-07-105, and E12-09-011 are cited as approved measurements for pion and kaon form factors and electroproduction cross sections

## Context

This review extends the work of Horn and Roberts (2016) on pion and kaon structure, and Burkert and Roberts (2019) on emergent hadronic mass, by providing an updated synthesis of continuum and lattice QCD methods with experimental capabilities. It enables future experimental programs at the Electron-Ion Collider and Jefferson Lab 12 GeV to test predictions for Nambu-Goldstone boson structure, addressing open questions about the interplay between Higgs-generated mass and emergent hadronic mass in the standard model.
