# Equation of State, Spectra and Composition of Hot and Dense Infinite Hadronic Matter in a Microscopic Transport Model

> Equilibrium properties of infinite relativistic hadron matter are investigated using the Ultrarelativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model. The simulations are performed in a box with periodic boundary conditions. Equilibration times depend critically on energy and baryon densities. Energy spectra of various hadronic species are shown to be isotropic and consistent with a single temperature in equilibrium. The variation of energy density versus temperature shows a Hagedorn-like behavior with a limiting temperature of 130$\pm$10 MeV. Comparison of abundances of different particle species to ideal hadron gas model predictions show good agreement only if detailed balance is implemented for all channels. At low energy densities, high mass resonances are not relevant; however, their importance raises with increasing energy density. The relevance of these different conceptual frameworks for any interpretation of experimental data is questioned.

# Equation of State, Spectra and Composition of Hot and Dense Infinite Hadronic Matter in a Microscopic Transport Model

## Key claim
The UrQMD transport model produces a Hagedorn-like equation of state with a limiting temperature of 130±10 MeV for infinite hadronic matter, but only when string degrees of freedom and many-body decays are included; without these, the temperature rises continuously with energy density. The agreement between UrQMD and ideal hadron gas models is good only when strings and many-body decays are suppressed, because the Boltzmann collision kernel's restriction to binary collisions violates detailed balance when these processes are present.

## Method
The authors confined hadrons in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions at fixed net baryon density ρ_B = 0.16 fm⁻³ and zero net strangeness, using the UrQMD microscopic transport model which includes 55 baryon species, 32 meson species, and a string mechanism for high-mass excitations. They evolved the system from random initial conditions (80 protons + 80 neutrons in a 10×10×10 fm³ box) until particle multiplicities saturated, then extracted equilibrium properties including energy spectra, temperature, and particle abundances. These results were compared to a grand canonical statistical model of ideal hadrons with and without a Hagedorn mass spectrum for strings.

## Key numbers
- Limiting temperature (Hagedorn temperature) from UrQMD with strings: T_H = 130±10 MeV
- At ε = 250 MeV/fm³, all four species (nucleons, pions, lambdas, kaons) show T = 125±10 MeV
- At ε = 800 MeV/fm³, all four species show T = 130±10 MeV
- Fixed net baryon density: ρ_B = ρ_0 = 0.16 fm⁻³
- Box volume: 10×10×10 fm³ (1000 fm³)
- For the statistical model including strings at ε = 1 GeV/fm³: T = 129.98 MeV, μ_B = 505 MeV, μ_S = 130 MeV
- For the statistical model without strings at ε = 1 GeV/fm³: T = 184 MeV, μ_B = 67 MeV, μ_S = 48 MeV
- Hagedorn mass spectrum parameters: ρ₀^(s) = 500 MeV², a_H = -3
- String mass integration limits: m_min = 1.7 GeV, m_max = 1000 GeV
- Equilibration times for strange hadrons are much longer than non-strange at low ε; differences disappear above ε > 500 MeV/fm³
- Differences between UrQMD and statistical model exceed 50% at ε > 500 MeV/fm³ when strings are included

## Context
This work extends the study of equilibrium properties of hadronic matter beyond the statistical models used to describe AGS (14.6 GeV/nucleon) and SPS (200 GeV/nucleon) data by groups such as those behind the Quantum Statistical Model and Statistical Multifragmentation Model. It directly compares the microscopic UrQMD approach to ideal hadron gas models (e.g., those of Stoecker, Greiner, and collaborators) and demonstrates that string degrees of freedom, previously ignored in thermal model fits to experimental data, carry a substantial fraction of energy and baryon density. The results question whether detailed balance can be assumed in systems where many-body decays and string formation occur, and enable future studies of non-zero net baryon density and net strangeness in infinite matter, as well as investigations of local thermodynamic equilibrium in realistic heavy-ion collisions.
