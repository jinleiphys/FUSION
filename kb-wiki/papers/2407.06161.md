# Magnetic dipole $γ$-ray strength functions in the crossover from spherical to deformed neodymium isotopes

> We calculate the magnetic dipole $γ$-ray strength functions in a chain of even-mass neodymium isotopes $^{144-152}$Nd in the framework of the configuration-interaction (CI) shell model. We infer the strength function by applying the maximum entropy method (MEM) to the exact imaginary-time response function calculated with the shell-model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method. The success of the MEM depends on the choice of a good strength function as a prior distribution. We investigate two choices for the prior strength function: the static path approximation (SPA) and the quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA). We find that the QRPA is a better approximation at low temperatures (i.e., near the ground state), while the SPA is a better choice at finite temperatures. We identify a low-energy enhancement (LEE) in the MEM deexcitation $M1$ strength functions of the even-mass neodymium isotopes and compare with recent experimental results for the total deexcitation $γ$-ray strength functions. The LEE is already seen in the SPA strength function but not in the QRPA strength function, indicating the importance of large-amplitude static fluctuations around the mean field in reproducing the LEE. Our method is currently the only one which can reproduce LEE in heavy open-shell nuclei where conventional CI shell model calculations are prohibited. With the onset of deformation as number of neutrons increases along the chain of neodymium isotopes, we observe that some of the LEE strength transfers to a low-energy excitation, which we interpret as a finite-temperature "scissors" mode. We also observe a finite-temperature spin-flip mode.

## Key claim

The magnetic dipole low-energy enhancement (LEE) in the deexcitation gamma-ray strength function of neodymium isotopes is reproduced by the shell-model Monte Carlo method combined with maximum entropy method using a static path approximation prior, but only when large-amplitude static fluctuations around the mean field are included; the quasiparticle random-phase approximation, which lacks these fluctuations, does not produce the LEE.

## Method

The authors calculated the magnetic dipole imaginary-time response function for the chain of even-mass neodymium isotopes 144-152Nd using the shell-model Monte Carlo method within a configuration-interaction shell model space. They applied the maximum entropy method to invert the Laplace transform and extract the strength function, using either the static path approximation or the finite-temperature quasiparticle random-phase approximation as a prior distribution. The SPA and QRPA response functions were compared directly to the exact SMMC response functions to determine which prior is more reliable at different temperatures.

## Key numbers

* For 144Nd at near-ground-state temperature T = 0.05 MeV, the QRPA and SPA response functions both agree well with SMMC, with the QRPA response function being essentially on top of the SMMC result.
* At temperatures corresponding to the neutron separation energy (initial excitation energy Ei ~ 7-8 MeV), the SPA response function is a superior approximation to the QRPA across all isotopes studied (144,148,152Nd).
* For 144Nd, a peak at omega = 0 (the LEE) appears already at T = 0.32 MeV (corresponding to Ei ~ 0.96 MeV) and becomes more pronounced with increasing temperature up to T = 0.73 MeV (neutron separation energy ~ 7.94 MeV).
* The spin-flip mode appears at approximately 6 MeV in the ground-state strength functions of the spherical isotopes 144Nd and 146Nd.
* The scissors mode appears at approximately 2-3 MeV in the ground-state M1 strength functions of the deformed isotopes.

## Context

This work extends the SMMC-based approach previously applied to samarium isotopes by Fanto (2024) using the SPA+RPA prior, now applied to a chain of neodymium isotopes spanning the spherical-to-deformed crossover. The results contradict QRPA-only calculations (Litvinova 2013) which proposed the LEE is of E1 origin, and instead support CI shell-model calculations (Schwengner 2013, Sieja 2017, Karampagia 2017) that identified the LEE as M1 in nature. The method enables calculation of M1 strength functions in heavy open-shell nuclei where conventional CI shell-model calculations are computationally prohibitive, and provides predictions for 152Nd where no experimental measurements exist.
