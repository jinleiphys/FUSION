# Initial orientation effect and selecting desired events in 520AMeV/u U-U collisions

> How to select out those collisions with the desired geometry such as tip-tip and/or body-body in experiment is one key point for performing high energy UU collisions. With a relativistic transport model, we performed a simulation for deformed UU collision with vast different orientations at CSR energy area corresponding to the high net-baryon density region in QCD phase diagram. By investigating the centrality and initial collision orientation dependence of the center baryon density, we found that the tip-tip like UU collisions with extended high density phase, which is very important for studying the nuclear EoS of high baryon density matter and the possible end-point of the phase boundary, are those events with small initial orientations ($\leq20^{0}$) for bath projectile and target in reaction plane and small impact parameter ($\leq2.6fm$). We pointed out quantificationally two observations -- multiplicity of forward neutron and nuclear stopping power that both allows us to select out those most interesting events (i.e. tip-tip like), which will be very helpful for the future experiments at performing UU collisions.

## Key claim

Tip-tip like uranium-uranium (UU) collisions, which produce an extended high-density phase important for studying the nuclear equation of state at high baryon density, are those events with initial orientations of both projectile and target in the reaction plane less than or equal to 20 degrees and an impact parameter less than or equal to 2.6 fm. The abstract's claim that these events are "small initial orientations (≤20°)" and "small impact parameter (≤2.6 fm)" is conditional on requiring a baryon aggradation strength S ≥ 70.

## Method

The authors performed simulations using the relativistic transport model ART (A Relativistic Transport model) for deformed uranium-uranium collisions at a beam kinetic energy of 0.52 GeV/u, corresponding to the CSR energy region. They varied the initial collision orientations (θ_P, θ_T in the reaction plane and φ_P, φ_T in the transverse plane) and impact parameters, using a soft nuclear equation of state with compressibility K = 200 MeV. The central baryon density was calculated in a 1 fm³ cube at the collision center, and the baryon aggradation strength S (time integral of central density above threshold) was used to quantify the lifetime of the high-density phase.

## Key numbers

- Maximum baryon density of about 3.2 ρ₀ reached at about 7 fm/c in both tip-tip and body-body collisions at normalized impact parameter b̃ = 0.
- The high-density phase (ρ/ρ₀ ≥ 2.5) lasts about 13 fm/c for body-body and about 25 fm/c for tip-tip collisions at b̃ = 0, meaning tip-tip lasts about two times longer.
- Tip-tip like events are defined by impact parameter b ≤ 2.6 fm (corresponding to b̃ ≤ 0.2) and initial orientations θ_P ≤ 20° and θ_T ≤ 20° in the reaction plane.
- The nuclear stopping power R reaches about 1.2 in tip-tip like events, about 0.92 in body-body like events, and about 1.02 in random collisions at b = 0 fm.
- A cut on forward neutron multiplicity N_n < 30 enhances the percentage of events with b ≤ 2.6 fm, while a cut N_n < 40 also enhances this percentage but rejects fewer tip-tip like events.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of tip-tip and body-body UU collisions by Luo et al. (2007) and Wu et al. (2007) on stopping power and anisotropy flow, now focusing on quantifying the central baryon density and lifetime of the high-density phase. The results provide experimental handles for selecting desired collision geometries at the CSR facility (HIRFL-CSR in Lanzhou), using forward neutron multiplicity as a fast online trigger and nuclear stopping power R for offline analysis. This enables future experiments to isolate tip-tip like events for studying the nuclear equation of state at high baryon density and the possible QCD phase boundary endpoint, complementing earlier work by Li (2000), Shuryak (2000), and Heinz and Kuhlman (2005) on exploiting UU collisions.
