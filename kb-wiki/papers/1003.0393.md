# Dilaton and gluon condensate in a nucleon medium

> The gluon condensate as a function of temperature and baryon density in a nucleon medium is obtained from an effective dilaton Lagrangian. It is shown that, at a normal nuclear density of nucleons, n_0 = 0.17 fm^{-3} the gluon condensate decreases by about 10%.

## Key claim

The gluon condensate decreases by about 10% at normal nuclear density (n_0 = 0.17 fm^{-3}), but this result is derived under the nonrelativistic approximation and the assumption that the nucleon medium is a perfect Fermi gas, with the linear density dependence holding only when m_N n is much smaller than 16 times the vacuum energy density.

## Method

The author employs an effective dilaton Lagrangian to describe the interaction of the scalar glueball (dilaton) with nucleons, where the dilaton field is related to the trace of the energy-momentum tensor and thus to the gluon condensate. The thermodynamic potential for nucleons interacting with the dilaton is minimized to find the equilibrium dilaton field as a function of temperature and baryon density, using the nonrelativistic approximation for densities near nuclear matter density and temperatures much less than the nucleon mass.

## Key numbers

- At normal nuclear density n_0 = 0.17 fm^{-3}, the gluon condensate decreases by about 10% (specifically, m_N n / 4|ε_v| ≈ 0.1).
- The nonperturbative vacuum energy density used is |ε_v| ≈ 3.375 GeV^4, taken from Shifman (1978).
- The nucleon mass m_N is used as the scale, with the condition T ≪ m_N for the temperature-dependent case and p_F^2 ≪ m_N^2 for the density-dependent case.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of the gluon condensate in medium by Cohen (1991), Agasian (1993, 1997, 1999, 2001, 2003), Sollfrank (1994), Carter (1996, 1998), Schaefer (2001), Sannino (2002), and Drago (2001), which used various physical approaches. The dilaton Lagrangian method enables the inverse effect of condensate change on particle masses to be included, allowing determination of the gluon condensate over the entire range of temperature and density up to the phase transition point. The results enable predictions for how hadron masses and the pion decay constant f_π behave in nuclear matter under extreme conditions such as heavy-ion collisions.
