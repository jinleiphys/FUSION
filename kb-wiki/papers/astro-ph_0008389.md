# Evolution of Proto-Neutron stars with kaon condensates

> We present simulations of the evolution of a proto-neutron star in which kaon-condensed matter might exist, including the effects of finite temperature and trapped neutrinos. The phase transition from pure nucleonic matter to the kaon condensate phase is described using Gibbs' rules for phase equilibrium, which permit the existence of a mixed phase. A general property of neutron stars containing kaon condensates, as well as other forms of strangeness, is that the maximum mass for cold, neutrino-free matter can be less than the maximum mass for matter containing trapped neutrinos or which has a finite entropy. A proto-neutron star formed with a baryon mass exceeding that of the maximum mass of cold, neutrino-free matter is therefore metastable, that is, it will collapse to a black hole at some time during the Kelvin-Helmholtz cooling stage. The effects of kaon condensation on metastable stars are dramatic. In these cases, the neutrino signal from a hypothetical galactic supernova (distance $\sim8.5$ kpc) will stop suddenly, generally at a level above the background in the SuperK and SNO detectors, which have low energy thresholds and backgrounds. This is in contrast to the case of a stable star, for which the signal exponentially decays, eventually disappearing into the background. We find the lifetimes of kaon-condensed metastable stars to be restricted to the range 40--70 s and weakly dependent on the proto-neutron star mass, in sharp contrast to the significantly larger mass dependence and range (1--100 s) of hyperon-rich metastable stars.

## Key claim

Proto-neutron stars with baryon masses exceeding the maximum mass of cold, neutrino-free matter containing kaon condensates are metastable and will collapse to a black hole during the Kelvin-Helmholtz cooling stage, but the lifetimes of such metastable stars are restricted to the narrow range 40–70 s and are only weakly dependent on mass, unlike hyperon-rich metastable stars whose lifetimes span 1–100 s with strong mass dependence.

## Method

The authors performed numerical simulations of proto-neutron star evolution during the Kelvin-Helmholtz epoch using a Henyey-like evolution scheme with flux-limited diffusion modules, incorporating a field-theoretical equation of state for kaon-condensed matter with boson exchange interactions. The phase transition from nucleonic matter to the kaon condensate phase was treated using Gibbs' rules for phase equilibrium, allowing a mixed phase, and the opacities included recent advances in neutrino cross sections for nucleons with modifications for kaon-condensed matter. The simulations used initial entropy and lepton fraction profiles from prior work (Pons et al. 1999) and assumed instantaneous chemical equilibrium for kaon condensation.

## Key numbers

- Lifetimes of kaon-condensed metastable stars are restricted to the range 40–70 s, weakly dependent on proto-neutron star mass.
- For hyperon-rich metastable stars, lifetimes range from 1–100 s with significantly larger mass dependence.
- The mass window for metastability with optical potential U_K = −120 MeV is 1.7 < M_B/M_⊙ < 2.05, and with U_K = −100 MeV is 2.0 < M_B/M_⊙ < 2.2.
- The binary pulsar mass constraint (1.44 M_⊙) limits |U_K| to less than about 126 MeV for the interaction used.
- The kaon condensation formation timescale is less than or about 10^−4 s at temperatures above a few MeV.
- For a hypothetical galactic supernova at distance ∼8.5 kpc, the neutrino signal from a metastable star will stop suddenly at a level above background in SuperK and SNO detectors.
- The mixed phase occupies only a small fraction (∼0.2) of the total volume even in the most massive proto-neutron stars.

## Context

This work extends prior simulations of proto-neutron star evolution with hyperons (Pons et al. 1999, paper I) to the case of kaon condensates, and contrasts with earlier studies by Baumgarte et al. (1996) who used a Maxwell construction and rescaled neutrino mean free paths. The results show that kaon-induced metastability produces a narrower lifetime range than hyperon-induced metastability, and the authors note that if a metastable star collapses in less than about 30 s, it is not likely due to kaon condensation, pointing instead to hyperons or quark matter as the cause.
