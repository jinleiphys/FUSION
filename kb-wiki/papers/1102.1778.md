# Thermal nonlocal Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model in the real time formalism

> The real-time formalism at finite temperature and chemical potential for the nonlocal Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model is developed in the presence of a Gaussian covariant regulator. We construct the most general thermal propagator, by means of the spectral function. As a result, the model involves the propagation of massive quasiparticles. The appearance of complex poles is interpreted as a confinement signal, and in this case we have unstable quasiparticles with a finite decay width. An expression for the propagator along the critical line, where complex poles start to appear, is also obtained. A generalization to other covariant regulators is proposed.

# Thermal nonlocal Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model in the real time formalism

## Key claim

The real-time formalism at finite temperature and chemical potential is developed for the nonlocal Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model with a Gaussian covariant regulator, constructing thermal propagators that describe quasiparticles with temperature- and chemical-potential-dependent masses; the appearance of complex poles in the propagator is interpreted as a confinement signal, but this interpretation depends on the parameter set used, as Set I (confining) and Set II (nonconfining) produce qualitatively different pole structures at zero temperature and chemical potential.

## Method

The authors construct the $S_{11}$ component of the thermal propagator matrix through the spectral density function, generalizing the standard Dolan-Jackiw procedure to handle dressed propagators with nontrivial analytic structure including complex poles. They apply the real-time formalism to the nonlocal NJL model in the mean-field approximation with a Gaussian regulator $r(q_E^2) = \exp(-q_E^2/2\Lambda^2)$, obtaining separate propagators for the deconfined phase (real poles), confined phase (complex poles), and the critical case separating them. The gap equation and thermodynamic potential are then derived by integrating the thermal propagators, with the finite temperature and chemical potential contributions separated from the zero-temperature terms.

## Key numbers

* Set I parameters: $\langle \bar qq\rangle = -(200\ \text{MeV})^3$, $m = 10.5\ \text{MeV}$, $G = 50\ \text{GeV}^{-1}$, $\Lambda = 627\ \text{MeV}$
* Set II parameters: $\langle \bar qq\rangle = -(220\ \text{MeV})^3$, $m = 7.7\ \text{MeV}$, $G = 30\ \text{GeV}^{-1}$, $\Lambda = 760\ \text{MeV}$
* Critical self-energy at zero 4th-momentum: $\Sigma_c = 273\ \text{MeV}$ for Set I, $\Sigma_c = 329\ \text{MeV}$ for Set II
* Zero-temperature self-energy at zero 4th-momentum: $\bar\Sigma = 350\ \text{MeV}$ for Set I, $\bar\Sigma = 300\ \text{MeV}$ for Set II
* The first-order phase transition endpoint occurs at approximately $(T,\mu) \approx (70, 180)\ \text{MeV}$
* Pion mass $m_\pi = 139\ \text{MeV}$ and pion decay constant $f_\pi = 92.4\ \text{MeV}$ are used to fix parameters

## Context

This work extends the nonlocal NJL model studies of GomezDumm (2001) and Bowler (1994) from the Matsubara imaginary-time formalism to the real-time formalism, providing a quasiparticle interpretation that separates thermal effects from zero-temperature contributions. The approach enables calculation of the gap equation and thermodynamic potential in terms of effective masses, and the authors verify their results coincide with those reported in GomezDumm (2001). The generalization to other covariant regulators (e.g., Lorentzian regulators with fractional powers) is proposed as straightforward, and the inclusion of the Polyakov loop is noted as a possible fix for instabilities in the thermodynamic potential arising from the cosine term in the confined phase.
