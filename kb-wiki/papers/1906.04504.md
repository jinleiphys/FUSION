# Some Features of Semiclassical Chiral Transport in Rotating Frames

> Semiclassical chiral kinetic theories in the presence of electromagnetic fields as well as vorticity can be constructed by means of some different relativistic or nonrelativistic approaches. To cover the noninertial features of rotating frames one can start from the modified quantum kinetic equation of Wigner function in Minkowski spacetime. It provides a relativistic chiral transport equation whose nonrelativistic limit yields a consistent three-dimensional kinetic theory which does not depend explicitly on spatial coordinates. Recently a chiral transport equation in curved spacetime has been proposed and its nonrelativistic limit in rotating coordinates was considered in the absence of electromagnetic fields. We show that the modified theory can be extended to curved spacetime. The related particle current density and chiral transport equation for an inertial observer in the rotating frame are derived. A novel three-dimensional chiral kinetic transport equation is established by inspecting the nonrelativistic limit of the curved spacetime approach in the rotating frame for a comoving observer in the presence of electromagnetic fields. It explicitly depends on spatial coordinates. We prove that it is consistent with the chiral anomaly, chiral magnetic and vortical effects.

## Key claim

The authors show that a modified quantum kinetic equation originally formulated in Minkowski spacetime can be extended to curved spacetime to describe chiral transport in rotating frames, and that the resulting three-dimensional chiral kinetic transport equation explicitly depends on spatial coordinates and is consistent with the chiral anomaly, chiral magnetic effect, and chiral vortical effect. The key qualifier is that centrifugal force terms are ignored throughout (terms of order O(u^2) and their spatial derivatives are set to zero), and the analysis is performed under the approximation that rotation velocity is much smaller than the speed of light.

## Method

The authors start from the quantum kinetic equation for the Wigner function of spin-1/2 fermions and modify it with frame-dependent terms to incorporate noninertial effects, then extend this modified formalism to curved spacetime by substituting the derivative operator with one containing Christoffel symbols. They work in rotating coordinates with metric components for a frame rotating with constant angular velocity, consider two different observers (inertial and comoving/rotating), and derive the semiclassical solution for the chiral vector current. The three-dimensional limit is obtained by integrating over the time component of momentum after solving the mass-shell condition, yielding a transport equation whose consistency with the chiral anomaly and anomalous currents is verified.

## Key numbers

* The chiral magnetic effect current density is given by j_B = (Q mu / (4 pi^2 hbar^2)) B, where Q is electric charge, mu is chemical potential, and B is magnetic field
* The chiral vortical effect current density is j_omega = ((mu^2/2) + (T^2 pi^2/6)) (omega / (2 pi^2 hbar^2)), where T is temperature and omega is angular velocity
* The combined chiral magnetic and vortical currents yield j = ((3 mu^2 + T^2 pi^2) / (12 pi^2 hbar^2)) omega + (Q mu / (4 pi^2 hbar^2)) B
* The continuity equation with source term is (partial n / partial t) + (nabla dot j) = (Q^2 E dot B) / ((2 pi hbar)^2) f|_{q=0}

## Context

This work extends the modified quantum kinetic equation approach of Dayi and Kilincarslan (2018) from Minkowski spacetime to curved spacetime, and relates it to the curved spacetime chiral kinetic theory of Liu, Glorioso, and Muzikar (2019) which only considered the case without electromagnetic fields. The resulting three-dimensional chiral kinetic transport equation resembles the one previously derived directly in 3D by Dayi, Kilincarslan, and Yunt (2018) but differs in the explicitly position-dependent terms. The formalism enables future studies of collisional transport in rotating chiral plasmas using the relaxation time method, which could help determine which chiral kinetic theory better matches observable effects in heavy-ion collisions.
