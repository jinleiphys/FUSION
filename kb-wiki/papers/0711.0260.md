# Rotation-driven prolate-to-oblate shape phase transition in 190W: A projected shell model study

> A shape phase transition is demonstrated to occur in 190W by applying the Projected Shell Model, which goes beyond the usual mean-field approximation. Rotation alignment of neutrons in the high-j, i_{13/2} orbital drives the yrast sequence of the system, changing suddenly from prolate to oblate shape at angular momentum 10$\hbar$. We propose observables to test the picture.

## Key claim

A prolate-to-oblate shape phase transition occurs along the yrast cascade of 190W at angular momentum 10ℏ, driven specifically by rotation alignment of neutrons in the high-j i13/2 orbital, but the authors note that this result is restricted to axial symmetry and that a more general case would require minimization in the ε-γ plane with three-dimensional angular momentum projection.

## Method

The authors applied the Projected Shell Model (PSM), which uses angular-momentum-projected multi-quasiparticle states built from a deformed Nilsson basis with BCS pairing, including quadrupole-plus-pairing residual interactions. They calculated projected energy surfaces as a function of deformation ε for states with angular momenta I=0,2,...,20ℏ, comparing calculations with only the quasiparticle vacuum versus those including two-quasiparticle states. Shell model diagonalization was performed at two fixed deformations (ε=±0.15) with about 100 two-quasiparticle states included in each diagonalization.

## Key numbers

- The ground state of 190W has prolate shape at ε≈+0.15, with the oblate minimum at ε≈-0.15 lying about 0.6 MeV higher in energy
- The shape phase transition occurs at angular momentum I=10ℏ, where the yrast sequence jumps from the prolate to the oblate side
- The oblate 0+ shape isomer is predicted at approximately 0.6 MeV excitation energy above the prolate ground state
- Several high-K two-quasiparticle bands with prolate shape have bandhead energies ranging down to 1.2 MeV of excitation
- B(E2) values at the oblate minimum are predicted to be smaller than those at the prolate minimum for spin states I≤6, with a drastic drop at I=8
- g-factors show a much greater drop for oblate states than prolate states, with sign change predicted for oblate g-factors

## Context

This work extends mean-field energy-surface calculations for 190W by Xu and Walker (2006) that suggested a candidate for "giant backbending" of the type originally described by Hilton and Mang (1979). The authors contrast this shape phase transition with the spherical-to-deformed ground-state phase transitions studied systematically by Casten et al. (1999) and the collective vibrational-to-rotational evolution observed by Regan et al. (2003). The paper enables experimental tests through measurement of B(E2) values and g-factors, with new experimental information potentially forthcoming from Regan et al. (2007).
