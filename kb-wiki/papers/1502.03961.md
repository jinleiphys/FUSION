# A revised thermonuclear rate of $^{7}$Be($n$,$Œ±$)$^{4}$He relevant to Big-Bang nucleosynthesis

> In the standard Big-Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) model, the primordial $^7$Li abundance is overestimated by about a factor of 2--3 comparing to the astronomical observations, so called the pending cosmological lithium problem. The $^7$Be($n$,$Œ±$)$^4$He reaction, which may affect the $^7$Li abundance, was regarded as the secondary important reaction in destructing the $^7$Be nucleus in BBN. However, the thermonuclear rate of $^7$Be($n$,$Œ±$)$^4$He has not been well studied so far. This reaction rate was firstly estimated by Wagoner in 1969, which has been generally adopted in the current BBN simulations and the reaction rate library. This simple estimation involved only a direct-capture reaction mechanism, but the resonant contribution should be also considered according to the later experimental results. In this work, we have revised this rate based on the indirect cross-section data available for the $^4$He($Œ±$,$n$)$^7$Be and $^4$He($Œ±$,$p$)$^7$Li reactions, with the charge symmetry and detailed-balance principle. Our new result shows that the previous rate (acting as an upper limit) is overestimated by about a factor of ten. The BBN simulation shows that the present rate leads to a 1.2\% increase in the final $^7$Li abundance compared to the result using the Wagoner rate, and hence the present rate even worsens the $^7$Li problem. By the present estimation, the role of $^7$Be($n$,$Œ±$)$^4$He in destroying $^7$Be is weakened from the secondary importance to the third, and the $^7$Be($d$,$p$)2$^4$He reaction becomes of secondary importance in destructing $^7$Be.

# A revised thermonuclear rate of $^{7}$Be($n$,$\alpha$)$^{4}$He relevant to Big-Bang nucleosynthesis

## Key claim
The previously adopted thermonuclear rate for $^{7}$Be($n$,$\alpha$)$^{4}$He, estimated by Wagoner (1969) and used in standard BBN simulations, is overestimated by about a factor of ten and should be regarded as an upper limit. The revised rate leads to a 1.2% increase in the final $^{7}$Li abundance, thereby worsening rather than alleviating the cosmological lithium problem.

## Method
The authors derived the cross section of $^{7}$Be($n$,$\alpha$)$^{4}$He using indirect experimental data from the $^{4}$He($\alpha$,$p$)$^{7}$Li reaction measured by King et al. (1977) and Sloboda et al. (1982), together with data from $^{7}$Li($p$,$\alpha$)$^{4}$He measured by Cassagnou et al. (1963). They applied charge symmetry and the detailed-balance principle to convert the $^{4}$He($\alpha$,$p$)$^{7}$Li cross sections to $^{4}$He($\alpha$,$n$)$^{7}$Be cross sections, using penetrability factors calculated with a channel radius of $R=4.1$ fm. The thermonuclear rate was then obtained by numerical integration of the derived cross sections using the EXP2RATE code, and a BBN simulation was performed with a network including nuclei up to $^{16}$O.

## Key numbers
- The previous Wagoner rate is overestimated by about a factor of ten in the BBN temperature range (0.1-5 GK), with the ratio of present rate to Wagoner rate ranging from 0.09 to 0.13.
- At $T=0.1$ GK, the present rate is $(9.6\pm8.3)\times10^5$ cm$^3$s$^{-1}$mol$^{-1}$, compared to Wagoner's $7.7\times10^6$ cm$^3$s$^{-1}$mol$^{-1}$.
- At $T=1.0$ GK, the present rate is $(7.2\pm3.1)\times10^6$ cm$^3$s$^{-1}$mol$^{-1}$, compared to Wagoner's $7.7\times10^7$ cm$^3$s$^{-1}$mol$^{-1}$.
- The BBN simulation with the new rate yields a 1.2% increase in the final $^{7}$Li abundance compared to using the Wagoner rate.
- The cross section $\sigma_1$ of $^{7}$Be($n$,$\alpha$)$^{4}$He at thermal neutron energy is about 0.01 mb, one order of magnitude smaller than the previous upper limit of 0.1 mb from Bassi et al. (1963).
- The derived cross sections at center-of-mass energies range from 8.4$\pm$8.5 mb at $E_{\rm c.m.}=0.0113$ MeV to 8.6$\pm$0.5 mb at $E_{\rm c.m.}=5.754$ MeV.

## Context
This work revises the thermonuclear rate originally estimated by Wagoner (1969), which was based solely on a direct-capture mechanism and the thermal neutron measurement of Bassi et al. (1963). The new rate incorporates resonant contributions through indirect data from King et al. (1977) and Sloboda et al. (1982). The result contradicts the TENDL-2014 evaluation based on TALYS calculations, which gives entirely different cross sections. The BBN simulation shows that the $^{7}$Be($n$,$\alpha$)$^{4}$He reaction is demoted from secondary to tertiary importance in destroying $^{7}$Be, with the $^{7}$Be($d$,$p$)2$^{4}$He reaction becoming the secondary destruction channel. The authors conclude that further study of this reaction is unlikely to resolve the cosmological lithium problem, and that resolution may require other mechanisms or new physics beyond the standard model.
