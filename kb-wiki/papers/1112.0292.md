# The Rayleigh Quotient

> The central role of the Rayleigh quotient in many body physics is discussed. Various many body methods can be obtained from either an attempt to evaluate the Rayleigh Quotient directly or through various variational approximations. Rather than dwell on the technical details necessary to obtain the equations of the various many body methods, we concentrate on how they can be obtained from the Rayleigh Quotient, and some of the consequences of the approximations involved in their evaluation.

## Key claim

The Rayleigh quotient provides a unifying framework for deriving many standard many-body approximation methods, but each method introduces specific approximations whose consequences (such as loss of size extensivity, spurious solutions, or center-of-mass contamination) must be understood separately; the paper emphasizes that even size-extensive methods like coupled cluster may still suffer from basis truncation issues that prevent convergence to the true eigenvalues.

## Method

The paper reviews how minimizing the Rayleigh quotient \(\delta(\langle\psi|\hat{H}|\psi\rangle/\langle\psi|\psi\rangle)=0\) yields the many-body Schroedinger equation, then shows how restricting the trial wave function before variation leads to Hartree-Fock (HF), multi-configurational HF, HFB, and BCS approximations. It further discusses how evaluating the Rayleigh quotient via path integrals with overcomplete sets of states (e.g., coherent states or Slater determinants) yields time-dependent HF, and how Monte Carlo techniques (Shell Model Monte Carlo, Quantum Monte Carlo Diagonalization) evaluate the quotient stochastically. The paper also contrasts configuration interaction (CI), many-body perturbation theory (MBPT), and coupled cluster (CC) methods in terms of size extensivity and basis truncation schemes.

## Key numbers

- The paper notes that in the electron gas, Brueckner (1955) found that Rayleigh-Schroedinger perturbation theory produced unphysical terms proportional to \(N^2\) and \(N^3\) (where \(N\) is particle number), which are canceled to all orders by the linked diagram theorem (Goldstone, 1957).
- Coupled cluster calculations for nuclear structure have been applied to doubly-magic nuclei such as \(^{40}\)Ca (Hagen et al., 2007), and recently extended to \(A\pm2\) nuclei where \(A\) represents a doubly magic nucleus (Jansen et al., 2011).
- The paper mentions that for simple one-dimensional potentials with \(V \propto 1/x^n\) for \(n \geq 5/2\), perturbative techniques fail and re-summation methods are required (M. 1994).

## Context

This paper extends the pedagogical tradition of showing how many-body methods derive from a single variational principle, as seen in earlier treatments of the Rayleigh-Ritz method and the Bloch-Horowitz effective operator approach (Bloch and Horowitz, 1958). It contrasts the size-extensive property of coupled cluster (reviewed in Bartlett 1978 and the CC review 2007) with the non-size-extensive behavior of truncated CI, and highlights the basis truncation differences between CI/CC (truncated at single-particle level) and the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM, Navratil 2009) which truncates at the level of energy quanta. The paper enables practitioners to understand the trade-offs between different many-body methods and to recognize that even size-extensive methods may suffer from center-of-mass contamination (Hagen et al., 2009) or spurious solutions (Andrews et al., 2003, 2008, 2009).
