# Alpha decay of thermally excited nuclei

> One of the prominent decay modes of heavy nuclei which are produced in astrophysical environments at temperatures of the order of $10^9$ K is the $α$ ($^4$He) decay. Thermally enhanced $α$ decay rates are evaluated within the standard scheme of a tunneling decay where the $α$ particle tunnels through the potential barrier formed by its interaction with the daughter nucleus. Following the observation that there exist several excited levels with the possibility of an $α$ decay when the daughter nucleus is at a shell closure, we focus in particular on decays producing daughter nuclei with the neutron number, N = 126. Within a statistical approach we find that the half-lives, $t_{1/2}(T)$, for temperatures ranging from $T$ = 0 to 2.4 GK can decrease by 1 - 2 orders of magnitude with the exception of the decay of $^{212}$Po which decays to the doubly magic daughter $^{208}$Pb, where $t_{1/2}(T)$ decreases by 5 orders of magnitude. The effect of these thermally enhanced $α$ decays on the $r$-process nucleosynthesis can be significant in view of the mass build up at the waiting point nuclei with closed neutron shells.

## Key claim

Thermally enhanced alpha decay half-lives for nuclei with N=128 (producing daughters at the N=126 shell closure) decrease by 1-2 orders of magnitude at temperatures up to 2.4 GK, except for 212Po decaying to doubly magic 208Pb where the half-life decreases by 5 orders of magnitude; this is a more modest effect than the 20-60 order-of-magnitude decrease predicted by Perrone and Clayton (1970) who used a continuum-of-states approximation rather than discrete experimental level data.

## Method

The authors evaluated temperature-dependent alpha decay half-lives using two approaches: (i) a statistical model summing over experimentally measured excited levels of parent nuclei with Boltzmann population factors, using empirical universal decay law (UDL) half-lives where experimental level half-lives were unavailable, and (ii) an effective Q-value model where the average excitation energy from a partition function (combining discrete levels and a nuclear level density with parameter a=A/9) was added to the ground-state Q-value, then used in a density-dependent double folding model (DFM) and the UDL. The calculations focused on five nuclei (212Po, 214Rn, 215Fr, 216Ra, 217Ac) whose daughters have N=126, identified as having multiple experimentally observed alpha-decaying excited states.

## Key numbers

- 212Po half-life at T=0: 2.9 × 10⁻⁷ s (experimental); at T=2.4 GK, half-life decreases by 5 orders of magnitude
- 214Rn half-life at T=0: 2.7 × 10⁻⁷ s (experimental)
- 215Fr half-life at T=0: 8.6 × 10⁻⁸ s (experimental)
- 216Ra half-life at T=0: 1.8 × 10⁻⁷ s (experimental)
- 217Ac half-life at T=0: 6.9 × 10⁻⁸ s (experimental)
- Average excitation energy for 212Po: 0.000096 MeV at 0.8 GK, 0.0034 MeV at 1.2 GK, 0.0231 MeV at 1.6 GK, 0.08142 MeV at 2.0 GK, 0.2004 MeV at 2.4 GK
- Average excitation energy for 215Fr: 0.00096 MeV at 0.8 GK, 0.02738 MeV at 1.2 GK, 0.1349 MeV at 1.6 GK, 0.3031 MeV at 2.0 GK, 0.4646 MeV at 2.4 GK
- DFM preformation factors: 0.22 for 212Po, 0.29 for 214Rn, 0.33 for 215Fr, 0.37 for 216Ra, 0.43 for 217Ac
- Q-values: 8.954 MeV (212Po), 9.208 MeV (214Rn), 9.540 MeV (215Fr), 9.526 MeV (216Ra), 9.832 MeV (217Ac)

## Context

This work extends the only prior estimate of temperature-dependent alpha decay rates by Perrone and Clayton (1970), who predicted half-life decreases of 20-60 orders of magnitude at 2 GK using a continuum-of-states approximation; the present authors find that using discrete experimental level data yields much smaller but still significant enhancements. The results are relevant for r-process nucleosynthesis network calculations, where alpha decay rates currently use ground-state laboratory half-lives, and the enhanced decay rates at shell closures (N=126 daughters) could affect mass buildup at waiting-point nuclei. The study enables improved kilonova modeling and abundance calculations by providing temperature-dependent alpha decay rates for inclusion in reaction networks such as those using Reaclib, Starlib, or Bruslib libraries.
