# Continuous Decoupling of Dynamically Expanding Systems

> The question of decoupling and freeze-out is reinvestigated and analysed in terms of transparent semi-classical decoupling formulae, which provide a smooth decoupling in time both, for single and two particle inclusive spectra. They generalise frequently employed instantaneous freeze-out procedures and provide simple relations between the damping width and the duration of the decoupling process. The implications on physical phenomena arising from the expansion and decay dynamics of the highly compressed hadronic matter generated in high energy nuclear collisions are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that decoupling and freeze-out in dynamically expanding systems (such as those created in high-energy nuclear collisions) are inherently continuous processes occurring over a finite time span, and that the frequently employed instantaneous freeze-out procedures (like the Cooper-Frye formula) are inadequate approximations that can be generalized by semi-classical decoupling formulae providing a smooth decoupling in time for both single and two-particle inclusive spectra.

## Method

Starting from an exact microscopic formulation using distorted waves and the Kadanoff-Baym non-equilibrium quantum many-body formalism, the author derives semi-classical decoupling rates by applying a first-order gradient approximation and the small damping-width limit. The resulting expressions describe the local decoupling rate and the detector yield in terms of classical escape paths, where the damping width Γ and the optical depth χ determine the probability that particles created at a space-time point can escape to infinity without further absorption. The formalism is then extended to two-particle final state correlations (HBT effect) and to a thermal source limit, where the gain term Π^gain relates to the damping width through the thermal distribution function.

## Key numbers

- For typical fireball parameters (freeze-out radius R_dec ≈ 6 fm, collective velocity Ṙ_dec = 0.5 c), the damping width at the decoupling peak is Γ_dec = 0.5 c/fm ≈ 100 MeV for inelastic processes (n=2) and 50 MeV for scattering processes (n=1)
- The decoupling duration Δt_dec lies above 5 fm/c, during which the volume increases by approximately an order of magnitude
- The damping width varies between start and stop of decoupling by a factor of about e^(-e) ≈ 1/15, with representative values Γ_i:Γ_dec:Γ_f ≈ 390 MeV:100 MeV:26 MeV
- The decoupling uncertainty relation gives Γ(t_max) × Δt_dec ≈ e (approximately 2.718)
- For the early universe case, the cosmological red shift factor during photon decoupling varies between Z=1300 and 800 with peak at Z≈1050

## Context

This work extends earlier efforts by Grassi (1994), Csernai (1997), Magas (1999), and Molnar (2005) that introduced a finite depth of the decoupling layer but parametrized the finite decoupling rather than inferring it microscopically. The paper provides a microscopic derivation that generalizes the Cooper-Frye formula (Cooper, 1974) to smooth decoupling situations, and connects to the Kadanoff-Baym formalism (Kadanoff and Baym, 1962). The results enable improved treatment of freeze-out in fluid dynamical descriptions of nuclear collisions, with implications for the interpretation of thermal model fits and the observability of structural features of the evolving matter, particularly regarding short-lived resonances and the distinction between chemical and thermal freeze-out.
