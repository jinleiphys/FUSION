# Hydrodynamical description of first-order phase transitions: Analytical treatment and numerical modeling

> Solutions of hydrodynamical equations are presented for an equation of state allowing for a first-order phase transition. The numerical analysis is supplemented by analytical treatment provided the system is close to the critical point. The processes of growth and dissolution of seeds of various sizes and shapes in meta-stable phases (like super-cooled vapor and super-heated liquid) are studied, as well as the dynamics of unstable modes in the spinodal region. We show that initially nonspherical seeds acquire spherical shape with passage of time. Applications to the description of the first-order phase transitions in nuclear systems, such as the nuclear gas-liquid transition occurring in low energy heavy-ion collisions and the hadron-quark transition in the high energy heavy-ion collisions are discussed. In both cases we point out the important role played by effects of viscosity and surface tension. It is shown that fluctuations dissolve and grow as if the fluid were effectively very viscous. Even in the spinodal region seeds may grow slowly due to viscosity and critical slowing down. This prevents the enhancement of fluctuations in the near-critical region, which is frequently considered as a signal of the critical point in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that, contrary to expectations that fluctuations near the critical point of a first-order phase transition in heavy-ion collisions should be enhanced, such fluctuations are actually suppressed because viscosity and critical slowing down cause seeds to grow slowly even in the spinodal region, preventing the enhancement of fluctuations that is frequently considered as a signal of the critical point.

## Method

The authors solve the full system of non-ideal non-relativistic hydrodynamical equations (Navier-Stokes, continuity, and heat transport) numerically in two spatial dimensions and analytically for arbitrary dimensions near the critical point, using a modified Van der Waals equation of state. They reduce the hydrodynamical equations to a single dimensionless equation for the density order parameter and study the growth and dissolution of seeds (droplets, bubbles, rods, slabs) in metastable phases and the spinodal region, comparing analytical predictions with numerical results.

## Key numbers

- The parameter β controlling the effective viscosity of seeds is given by β = (32Tcr)^{-1}[d̃ηr + ζr]^{-2}σ0²m, where d̃ = 2(d-1)/d for spatial dimension d
- The surface tension scales as σ = σ0 |δT|^(3/2) with δT = (T - Tcr)/Tcr
- The correlation length scales as l ∝ |δT|^(-1/2) and the relaxation time scales as t0 ∝ |δT|^(-1) near the critical point
- The critical seed radius in dimensionless units is ξ0^cr = 2(dsol - 1)/(3|ε̃|) for seeds in metastable surroundings
- The maximum value of the dimensionless parameter ε̃ is ε̃^max = 4/(3√3)
- For the modified Van der Waals equation of state, ε^max = √3 ncr |δT|^(3/2)/Tcr^(1/2)

## Context

This work extends earlier analytical treatments by Pshenichnov (1990s) and Voskresensky (1993) and fragmentary two-dimensional numerical solutions by Onuki, providing a more complete hydrodynamical description of first-order phase transitions. The authors apply their results to two nuclear systems: the nuclear gas-liquid transition in low-energy heavy-ion collisions (studied by Bondorf and others) and the hadron-to-sQGP phase transition in high-energy collisions (discussed by Berges, Stephanov, and lattice QCD groups). The paper enables quantitative modeling of how viscosity and surface tension control seed dynamics in these systems, and it challenges the expectation from Stephanov (2004) that the fireball may linger near the critical point due to diverging susceptibilities, instead arguing that critical slowing down prevents fluctuation enhancement.
