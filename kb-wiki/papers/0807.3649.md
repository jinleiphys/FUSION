# Impact of Quantum Phase Transitions on Excited Level Dynamics

> The influence of quantum phase transitions on the evolution of excited levels in the critical parameter region is discussed. The analysis is performed for 1D and 2D systems with first- and second-order ground-state transitions. Examples include the cusp and nuclear collective Hamiltonians.

## Key claim

The paper argues that quantum phase transitions (QPTs) at zero temperature have significant consequences for the dynamics and density of excited levels in the critical parameter region, with the specific signatures depending on the order of the transition and the number of degrees of freedom. The authors show that first-order ground-state QPTs produce a "nabla-shaped" region of phase coexistence in the control-parameter versus energy plane, where level trajectories develop a characteristic zig-zag pattern, while second-order transitions produce smoother, continuous changes.

## Method

The authors analyze one-dimensional (1D) and two-dimensional (2D) quantum Hamiltonians in the semiclassical limit (K to 0, equivalent to infinite system size), where the level density becomes proportional to the classical phase-space volume. For the 1D case, they study the cusp Hamiltonian with potential forms V1 = x^4 - x^2 + lambda x (first-order transition) and V2 = x^4 + lambda x^2 (second-order transition). For the 2D case, they study a nuclear collective Hamiltonian with potential V = r^4 + lambda r^2 + r^3 cos(3 phi), performing numerical diagonalization in truncated oscillator bases to obtain level dynamics and level densities.

## Key numbers

- For the cusp Hamiltonian V1 = x^4 - x^2 + lambda x, the first-order ground-state transition occurs at the critical point lambda_c = 0, with spinodal points at lambda = +/- 4/(3*sqrt(6)) (approximately +/- 0.544)
- For the 2D collective Hamiltonian V = r^4 + lambda r^2 + r^3 cos(3 phi), the critical point is at lambda_c = 1/4, with spinodal points at lambda = 0 and lambda = 9/32 (0.28125)
- The numerical diagonalization for the cusp Hamiltonian used K = 10^(-2)
- The numerical diagonalization for the 2D Hamiltonian used K values in the range 10^(-4) to 5*10^(-3)

## Context

This work extends earlier findings on excited-state QPTs in second-order transitions (Cejnar 2006, Cejnar 2007, Caprio 2008) to systems with first-order ground-state transitions. The analysis builds on the thermodynamic formulation of QPTs developed by Hertz (1976) and Gilmore (1978, 1979), and applies to systems where the infinite-size limit coincides with the classical limit, such as interacting boson models used in nuclear and molecular physics (Iachello 2006, Frank 1994). The results enable classification of excited-state phase transitions in higher-dimensional systems and provide a framework for detecting such transitions in finite samples of nuclear, molecular, and mesoscopic systems.
