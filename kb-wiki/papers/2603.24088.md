# Deep learning approaches to extract nuclear deformation parameters from initial-state information in heavy-ion collisions

> The deformation of heavy nuclei leaves characteristic imprints on the initial conditions of relativistic heavy-ion collisions. However, event-by-event fluctuations make the quantitative extraction of this information challenging. This study examines the identifiability of the quadrupole ($β_2$) and hexadecapole ($β_4$) deformation parameters from nucleon configurations sampled from a deformed Woods-Saxon distribution commonly used in initial-state modeling of heavy-ion collisions. As a baseline, we first establish an upper bound on the "intrinsic identifiability" of deformation information at the most microscopic level by constructing permutation-invariant point-cloud networks under controlled multi-event grouping. We then extend the analysis to the more realistic initial entropy-density profiles generated by the TRENTo model, where both standard regression and simulation-based inference (SBI) with conditional normalizing flows are employed to reconstruct the deformation parameters from ensembles of event images supplemented with global attributes. Multi-event averaging is found to be essential in this setting for suppressing stochastic fluctuations and revealing the underlying deformation information. While standard regression efficiently captures the central trends of deformation through point estimates, SBI provides calibrated posterior distributions, offering a more complete and robust characterization of uncertainty. Collectively, our results demonstrate that deformation information is effectively encoded in the initial state and becomes increasingly identifiable with sufficient ensemble averaging, laying a solid foundation for future extensions toward more complete dynamical modeling and final-state observables.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that nuclear quadrupole ($\beta_2$) and hexadecapole ($\beta_4$) deformation parameters can be extracted from initial-state information in heavy-ion collisions using deep learning, but only when multiple events are averaged together to suppress stochastic fluctuations; single-event predictions are unreliable, and the method provides an upper bound on extractable information since subsequent hydrodynamic evolution will further blur geometric features.

## Method

The authors first established a baseline using a permutation-invariant point-cloud neural network to infer $(\beta_2, \beta_4)$ directly from nucleon positions sampled from a deformed Woods-Saxon distribution for $^{238}$U, with group sizes of 1, 5, 10, and 20 events. They then extended the analysis to initial entropy-density profiles generated by the TRENTo model for $^{238}$U+$^{238}$U collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=193$ GeV, using a ConvNeXt-Small convolutional backbone with a Set Transformer aggregator to process bags of 1, 10, 50, or 100 event images supplemented by global attributes, employing both standard regression with Huber loss and simulation-based inference (SBI) with RealNVP conditional normalizing flows.

## Key numbers

- For nucleon configurations with group size $N=20$, the point-cloud model achieved test $R^2$ scores of 0.99062 for $\beta_2$ and 0.95998 for $\beta_4$.
- For single-event inputs ($N=1$) from nucleon configurations, test $R^2$ scores were 0.89969 for $\beta_2$ and 0.58252 for $\beta_4$.
- For TRENTo entropy profiles at 0-10% centrality with bag size $M=100$, regression achieved test $R^2$ scores of 0.93200 for $\beta_2$ and 0.61965 for $\beta_4$.
- For single-event TRENTo profiles ($M=1$) at 0-10% centrality, regression test $R^2$ scores were only 0.24239 for $\beta_2$ and 0.08706 for $\beta_4$.
- The SBI model at $M=100$ achieved a test log-likelihood gain of 2.40813 relative to a Gaussian baseline, compared to 0.75596 at $M=1$.
- Deformation parameters were sampled on a $21 \times 21$ grid with $\beta_2 \in [-0.5, 0.5]$ and $\beta_4 \in [-0.2, 0.2]$, with 2000 independent events per parameter set.

## Context

This work extends the program outlined by Jia et al. (2024) to use collider observables to constrain nuclear deformation, and builds on Bayesian-imaging approaches by Cheng et al. (2023) that demonstrated single-system observables can constrain Woods-Saxon parameters. The results establish that machine learning can extract deformation information from initial-state entropy profiles, providing a necessary prerequisite for future work that will incorporate full hydrodynamic evolution and final-state observables, where viscous dissipation will further blur geometric features.
