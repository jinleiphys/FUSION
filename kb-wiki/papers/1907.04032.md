# Uncertainty quantification and falsification of Chiral Nuclear Potentials

> Are chiral theories at present describing experimental NN scattering data satisfactorily ?. Will the chiral approach offer a framework where fitting and selecting the existing np and pp data can be done without theoretical bias ?. While predictive power in theoretical nuclear physics has been a major concern in the study of nuclear structure and reactions, the Effective Field Theory (EFT) based on chiral expansions has emerged after Weinberg as a model independent hierarchy for many body forces and much progress has been achieved over the last decades. We review some of the issues involved which point to being close to the solution, but also that work remains still to be done to validate the theory. We analyze several examples including zero energy NN scattering and perturbative counterterm -- free peripheral scattering where one would expect these methods to work best and unveil relevant systematic discrepancies when a fair comparison to the Granada-2013 NN-database and partial wave analysis (PWA) based on coarse graining the interaction is undertaken.

## Key claim

Chiral nuclear potentials, despite their theoretical appeal and systematic power counting, show systematic discrepancies when compared to the Granada-2013 NN database and partial wave analysis, even in peripheral scattering where the chiral expansion should work best, indicating that the chiral approach to nuclear forces has not yet been fully validated against existing scattering data.

## Method

The authors review and analyze the consistency of chiral NN potentials by comparing them to the Granada-2013 NN database and partial wave analysis (PWA), which is based on coarse-graining the interaction. They examine zero-energy NN scattering and perturbative counterterm-free peripheral scattering, where chiral methods are expected to be most reliable, to identify systematic discrepancies. The analysis uses the framework of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation and partial wave expansion to evaluate how well chiral potentials describe the approximately 8000 np+pp scattering data points below 350 MeV laboratory energy.

## Key numbers

- The pion production threshold is at T_LAB = 290 MeV (k = 370 MeV), with the customary upper limit for elastic scattering taken as T_LAB ≤ 350 MeV (k ~ 400 MeV)
- The average internucleon distance in nuclear matter at saturation density ρ = 0.17 fm^{-3} is d = 1.8 fm, giving d m_π ~ 1.3
- The chiral potential becomes singular at short distances as V_NN^χ(r) = a_1/(f_π^2 r^3) + a_2/(f_π^4 r^5) + a_3/(f_π^6 r^7) + ... for r ≪ r_c
- The theoretical uncertainty in binding energy per nucleon for ^16O from chiral interactions is ΔB^th(^16O)/16 ~ 4 MeV, dominated by cut-off variation
- The semi-empirical mass formula gives ΔB_sem/A ~ 0.1 MeV, while chiral estimates range from ΔB^th/A ~ 0.5 MeV (earlier estimate) to ~ 2 MeV (updated estimate)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous uncertainty quantification studies of NN interactions (Perez 2014, NavarroPerez 2012) and critically examines the Weinberg chiral EFT approach to nuclear forces (Weinberg 1990) against the Granada-2013 NN database. The paper highlights that while chiral potentials provide a theoretically appealing hierarchy of many-body forces, their predictive power for nuclear binding energies remains poorer than the ancient five-parameter Weiszacker semi-empirical mass formula. The analysis points to the need for proper validation and falsification of chiral potentials before they can be considered a reliable framework for nuclear structure calculations, and raises questions about what "reasonable" cut-off values should be used in chiral EFT calculations.
