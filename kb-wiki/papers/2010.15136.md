# Non-thermal neutrinos created by shock acceleration in successful and failed core-collapse supernova

> We present a comprehensive study of neutrino shock acceleration in core-collapse supernova (CCSN). The leading players are heavy leptonic neutrinos, $ν_μ$ and $ν_τ$; the former and latter potentially gain the energy up to $\sim 100$ MeV and $\sim 200$ MeV, respectively, through the shock acceleration. Demonstrating the neutrino shock acceleration by Monte Carlo neutrino transport, we make a statement that it commonly occurs in the early post bounce phase ($\lesssim 50$ ms after bounce) for all massive stellar collapse experiencing nuclear bounce and would reoccur in the late phase ($\gtrsim 100$ ms) for failed CCSNe. This opens up a new possibility to detect high energy neutrinos by terrestrial detectors from Galactic CCSNe; hence, we estimate the event counts for Hyper(Super)-Kamiokande, DUNE, and JUNO. We find that the event count with the energy of $\gtrsim 80$ MeV is a few orders of magnitude higher than that of the thermal neutrinos regardless of the detectors, and muon production may also happen in these detectors by $ν_μ$ with the energy of $\gtrsim 100$ MeV. The neutrino signals provide a precious information on deciphering the inner dynamics of CCSN and placing a constraint on the physics of neutrino oscillation; indeed, the detection of the high energy neutrinos through charged current reaction channels will be a smoking gun evidence of neutrino flavor conversion.

## Key claim
The paper claims that neutrino shock acceleration commonly occurs in the early post-bounce phase (less than 50 ms after bounce) for all massive stellar collapse experiencing nuclear bounce, and may reoccur in the late phase (greater than 100 ms) for failed core-collapse supernovae, but the authors note that the efficiency depends on the shock morphology and multi-dimensional fluid instabilities, which are not fully addressed in this work.

## Method
The authors performed Monte Carlo neutrino transport simulations with a dynamical fluid background approximated from spherically symmetric Boltzmann neutrino transport simulations, including nucleon scattering, coherent scattering with nuclei, nucleus inelastic scattering, electron scattering, and quasi-nucleon and quasi-nucleus scattering for muon production. Neutrino particles were injected at 50 km with a thermal spectrum of zero chemical potential at T=6 MeV, and the emergent spectra were recorded at the outer boundary of 700 km for the early post-bounce phase, while a steady-state approximation was used for the failed CCSN case with a shock wave fixed at 80 km.

## Key numbers
- Heavy leptonic neutrinos gain energy up to approximately 100 MeV for nu_mu and approximately 200 MeV for nu_tau through shock acceleration.
- The fluid-velocity difference between upstream and downstream at the shock wave is approximately 0.2c.
- The maximum energy for nu_tau is limited by nucleon recoil to approximately 200 MeV, while nu_mu acceleration stops below 200 MeV due to charged-current reactions above approximately 100 MeV.
- The number of shock crossings needed to accelerate a seed neutrino from 50 MeV to 100 MeV and 200 MeV is roughly 5 and 10, respectively.
- The optical depth for neutrino-nucleus scattering at the shock is approximately 1 times (r_sh/100 km)^{-3.4} times (E/50 MeV)^alpha, where alpha is between 0.5 and 2.
- The acceleration time scale is roughly less than 3 ms for 10 shock crossings, based on a shock radius of 100 km.
- In the Monte Carlo simulation, the magnitudes of the non-thermal tail relative to the thermal flux at 80 MeV are approximately (8, 8, 13, 5, 3) at t=(15, 20, 25, 30, 35) ms post bounce.
- For the failed CCSN case, neutrino shock acceleration occurs when (M_dot/1 M_sun/s) times (80 km/r_sh) is greater than or approximately equal to 1.

## Context
This work extends the earlier suggestion of neutrino shock acceleration in CCSNe by Kazanas (1981) and Giovanoni (1989), who used older models and fewer neutrino interactions, by providing a comprehensive study with modern Monte Carlo transport and realistic neutrino opacities. The paper also builds on CCSN simulations with full Boltzmann neutrino transport by Nagakura (2019) that showed evidence of neutrino acceleration, but addresses the limitations of those simulations regarding nucleon recoil and charged-current reactions for nu_mu. The results enable future detection strategies for high-energy neutrinos (above 80 MeV) from Galactic CCSNe using Hyper-Kamiokande, DUNE, and JUNO, and suggest that flavor-dependent features in the spectrum could serve as a smoking gun for neutrino flavor conversion.
