# KHIFC-user friendly program for studying Heavy ion fusion barrier characteristics

> We have developed an application for studying heavy ion fusion barrier characteristics such as such as fusion barrier heights ($V_B$), positions ($R_B$) and curvature of the inverted parabola ($\hbarω$). We call this application as KHIFC (Kolar Heavy Ion fusion barrier Characteristics). This software application hosted in the domain "https://systematics-of-heavy-ion-fusion.vercel.app/". This KHIFC produces fusion cross-sections with the simple input of projectile, target and center of mass energy. The values produced by the KHIFC is validated with experiments. Efficient tools like KHIFC are essential for researchers in nuclear physics, particularly when dealing with complex systems such as actinide and superheavy nuclei. By providing quick calculations and insights, it can significantly aid in experiment planning and theoretical investigations.

## Key claim

The authors present KHIFC, a web-based program that computes heavy-ion fusion barrier characteristics (barrier height, position, and curvature) and fusion cross-sections for actinide and superheavy nuclei, claiming validation against experimental data with deviations less than 2.5% for barrier heights, though they acknowledge that deviations below the barrier may arise because deformation and angular momentum effects are not included.

## Method

The program implements the Wong formalism to compute fusion cross-sections from barrier characteristics, using separate parameterized formulas for actinide and superheavy nuclei derived from earlier work by Manjunatha (2018). For actinides, the formalism covers 7205 projectile-target combinations with projectile atomic numbers 3 < Z1 < 51 and target atomic numbers 45 < Z2 < 100; for superheavy nuclei (Z = 104–130), it covers 14054 combinations with projectile atomic numbers 6 < Z1 < 58 and target atomic numbers 52 < Z2 < 98. The web application is built with JavaScript, HTML, CSS, and React, hosted at a public domain, and accepts projectile and target atomic/mass numbers plus center-of-mass energy as input.

## Key numbers

- Fusion barrier heights from KHIFC show percentage deviation less than 2.5% compared to experimental values across 17 tested reactions.
- For the reaction $^{16}O+^{208}Pb\to^{224}Th$, KHIFC gives V_B = 75.362 MeV versus experimental 74.55 MeV (Morton, 1999).
- For $^{48}Ca+^{208}Pb\to^{256}No$, KHIFC gives V_B = 176.224 MeV versus experimental 173.40 ± 0.1 MeV (Banerjee, 2019).
- For $^{86}Kr+^{208}Pb\to^{294}Og$, KHIFC gives V_B = 308.386 MeV versus experimental 299.00 MeV (Dutt, 2010) and 303.3 MeV (Ntshangase, 2007).
- The program applies to projectile atomic numbers 3 < Z_P < 51 and mass numbers 6 < A_1 < 127, with target atomic numbers 45 ≤ Z_T ≤ 100 and mass numbers 101 ≤ A_T ≤ 260.
- For superheavy nuclei, the formalism covers atomic numbers 104 < Z < 130.
- The program produces fusion cross-sections over a 100 MeV range of center-of-mass energy, requiring input E_cm greater than V_B − 20 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical formalism for fusion barrier characteristics developed by Manjunatha (2018) for actinides and superheavy nuclei, providing a user-friendly web implementation that contrasts with existing codes such as CASCADE (Puhlhofer, 1977), HIVAP (Reisdorf, 1981), CCFUL (Hagino, 1999), PACE (Gavron, 1980), and POTFUS + GEMINI++ (Mancusi, 2009). The program enables rapid calculation of fusion barriers and cross-sections for experiment planning in heavy and superheavy element synthesis, particularly for elements up to Z = 128, and the authors demonstrate its use for predicting fusion cross-sections for superheavy elements Z = 119 and 120.
