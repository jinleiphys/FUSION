# Non-purely transverse Magnus force in superconducting neutron stars

> The force acting on a proton vortex in extreme type-II superconducting neutron star matter is studied in the limit of vanishing temperature. A detailed analysis is presented on how momentum is transferred from length scales on the order of the London penetration depth to the vortex core. To examine the momentum flux, expressions for proton and electron currents are derived for arbitrary distances from the vortex line. It is shown that, in the regime of a large electron mean free path, the only force acting directly on the vortex core is the Magnus force. Notably, the correction to the proton current near the vortex core generates a component of the Magnus force aligned with the incident current measured far from the vortex. This contribution, responsible for longitudinal force, is usually overlooked in the literature. The results obtained in this work for a relatively simple problem concerning the force on a vortex in cold matter of neutron stars may also be relevant to other superconducting systems.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that, in the regime of a large electron mean free path at vanishing temperature, the Magnus force acting on a proton vortex in extreme type-II superconducting neutron star matter acquires a longitudinal component (aligned with the incident current) due to corrections to the proton current near the vortex core, contradicting the common assumption that the Magnus force is purely transverse.

## Method

The authors model an isolated straight proton vortex in cold npe-matter (neutrons, protons, electrons) with strong superfluidity/superconductivity, treating the vortex core as infinitely thin and assuming extreme type-II superconductivity (coherence length much smaller than London penetration depth). They derive expressions for proton and electron currents at arbitrary distances from the vortex line, solve the Boltzmann-Vlasov equation for electrons without collisions, and compute the momentum flux through cylindrical surfaces surrounding the vortex to determine the force on the core. The force is evaluated using two independent methods based on momentum conservation and the stress tensor.

## Key numbers

- Coherence length: ξ ≈ 28 fm × (n_p / 0.18 n_0)^(1/3) × (m_p / m_p*) × (0.456 MeV / Δ^(p)), where n_0 = 0.16 fm^(-3) is nuclear saturation density
- London penetration depth: λ ≈ 42.4 fm × (0.18 n_0 / n_p)^(1/2)
- Distance between neighboring vortices: d_B = 4.89 × 10^3 fm × sqrt(10^12 / B̅), where B̅ is the averaged magnetic field induction
- Electron mean free path in normal non-magnetized matter: ℓ ∼ 10^7 × T_8^(-1) fm, where T_8 = T / 10^8 K
- Particle wavelengths (divided by 2π): ℏ / p_Fα = 1.05 fm × (0.18 n_0 / n_α)^(1/3) for α = n, p, e

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Gusakov (2019) on forces acting on proton vortices in neutron stars, which calculated the force as momentum flux through a cylindrical surface and found that only electron scattering off the vortex magnetic field contributes significantly at large radii. The present paper resolves a paradox identified in that earlier work: while the transverse (perpendicular) force component is conserved and transferred to the vortex core by superconducting protons, the fate of the longitudinal (parallel) force component was unclear. The results contradict claims by Jones (1991b, 2006, 2009) and Bransgrove, Levin, and Beloborodov (2018) that the transverse force vanishes, while supporting the finite-transverse-force conclusions of Alford and Sedrakian (2010), Glampedakis, Andersson, and Samuelsson (2011), and Graber et al. (2015). The findings may also be relevant to other superconducting systems beyond neutron stars.
