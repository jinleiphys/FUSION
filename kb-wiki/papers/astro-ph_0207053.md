# Nuclear and High-Energy Astrophysics

> There has never been a more exciting time in the overlapping areas of nuclear physics, particle physics and relativistic astrophysics than today. Orbiting observatories such as the Hubble Space Telescope, Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer (RXTE), Chandra X-ray satellite, and the X-ray Multi Mirror Mission (XMM) have extended our vision tremendously, allowing us to see vistas with an unprecedented clarity and angular resolution that previously were only imagined, enabling astrophysicists for the first time ever to perform detailed studies of large samples of galactic and extragalactic objects. On the Earth, radio telescopes (e.g., Arecibo, Green Bank, Parkes, VLA) and instruments using adaptive optics and other revolutionary techniques have exceeded previous expectations of what can be accomplished from the ground. The gravitational wave detectors LIGO, LISA VIRGO, and Geo-600 are opening up a window for the detection of gravitational waves emitted from compact stellar objects such as neutron stars and black holes. Together with new experimental forefront facilities like ISAC, ORLaND and RIA, these detectors provide direct, quantitative physical insight into nucleosynthesis, supernova dynamics, accreting compact objects, cosmic-ray acceleration, and pair-production in high energy sources which reinforce the urgent need for a strong and continuous feedback from nuclear and particle theory and theoretical astrophysics. In my lectures, I shall concentrate on three selected topics, which range from the behavior of superdense stellar matter, to general relativistic stellar models, to strange quark stars and possible signals of quark matter in neutron stars.

## Key claim

This lecture series argues that the combination of new observational facilities (Hubble, RXTE, Chandra, XMM, radio telescopes, gravitational wave detectors) and new experimental nuclear facilities (ISAC, ORLaND, RIA) now makes it possible for the first time to seriously explore the subatomic structure of matter in the high-density low-temperature portion of its phase diagram from observed pulsar data, but only if there is strong and continuous feedback from nuclear and particle theory and theoretical astrophysics.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical models for the equation of state of superdense neutron star matter, covering both non-relativistic variational approaches (using phenomenological nucleon-nucleon interactions with two- and three-nucleon forces) and relativistic field-theoretical models based on Lagrangians with baryon octet and decuplet states mediated by scalar, vector, and isovector mesons. The equations of motion are solved self-consistently using either the relativistic Hartree (mean-field), Hartree-Fock, or ladder (T-matrix) approximations, combined with the constraints of electric charge neutrality and chemical equilibrium to determine the baryon-lepton composition of neutron star matter.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear matter saturation density: $\rho_0 = 0.15$ nucleons/fm$^3$, corresponding to a mass density of $\epsilon = 2.5 \times 10^{14}$ g/cm$^3$
- Normal nuclear matter energy density: $\epsilon_0 = 140$ MeV/fm$^3$
- Core densities of neutron stars can reach more than ten times normal nuclear density, approximately $1.5$ GeV/fm$^3$
- Binding energies per nucleon at saturation for non-relativistic models range from $-11.5$ MeV (WFF(UV$_{14}$+UVII)) to $-16.6$ MeV (WFF(UV$_{14}$+TNI))
- Incompressibility values for non-relativistic models range from $K=202$ MeV (WFF(UV$_{14}$+UVII)) to $K=261$ MeV (WFF(UV$_{14}$+TNI))
- Incompressibility values for relativistic models range from $K=115$ MeV ($\Lambda^{00}_{\rm HEA}+{\rm HFV}$) to $K=376$ MeV (HFV)
- Asymmetry energy values for relativistic models range from $a_{\rm sy}=29$ MeV ($\Lambda^{00}_{\rm HEA}+{\rm HFV}$) to $a_{\rm sy}=36.8$ MeV (HV, G$^\pi_{200}$)
- About 1400 pulsars are known as of the time of writing

## Context

This lecture series extends the long-standing program of connecting nuclear matter theory to compact star observations, building on the foundational work of Pandharipande (1974, 1979) on variational methods, Wiringa (1988) on non-relativistic equations of state, and Serot (1986) on relativistic mean-field theory. The paper reviews specific prior equation-of-state models including the WFF family (Wiringa 1988), FP (Friedman 1981), APR (Akmal 1998), TF96 (Myers and Swiatecki 1995), and the relativistic models of Glendenning (1985-1997) and Huber (1993-1998). The work enables future identification of quark matter signals in neutron stars and strange quark star candidates by linking observable stellar properties to the underlying equation of state.
