# Quantum mechanical aspects of coherent photoproduction: the limits of coherence, and multiple vector mesons

> Quantum mechanics is central to coherent photoproduction in ultra-peripheral collisions (UPCs). This writeup will discuss some surprising aspects of UPCs that stem from these quantum mechanical roots. The Good-Walker (GW) paradigm, which connects coherent photoproduction with the target nucleus remaining in its ground state. This contrasts with a semi-classical picture, where coherence depends on the positions of the individual nucleons and the momentum transfer. Unlike the GW approach, the semiclassical picture is consistent with the observed data on coherent photoproduction with nuclear breakup, and with coherent photoproduction in peripheral collisions. The semiclassical approach allows for a wider variety of coherent UPC reactions, such as coherent photoproduction of charged mesons, including some non $q\overline q$ exotica. Quantum mechanics is also key to the coherent photoproduction of multiple vector mesons by the interactions of a single ion pair. The vector mesons share a common impact parameter, and so can exhibit richer interference patterns than single mesons. At forward rapidities, the cross sections to produce multiple identical vector mesons are enhanced due to superradiance. With enough statistics, multi-meson events may provide an opportunity to observe stimulated decays.}

# Quantum mechanical aspects of coherent photoproduction: the limits of coherence, and multiple vector mesons

## Key claim
The Good-Walker paradigm, which identifies coherent photoproduction with the target nucleus remaining in its ground state, disagrees with experimental observations of coherent photoproduction accompanied by nuclear breakup and in peripheral collisions; a semi-classical approach where coherence depends on the positions of individual nucleons and momentum transfer can explain this data where Good-Walker fails, and also allows for a wider variety of coherent reactions including charged meson production.

## Method
The author contrasts the Good-Walker paradigm, where coherent production is defined by the target remaining in the ground state and amplitudes are averaged before squaring, with a semi-classical approach where the cross section is calculated as the square of the sum of amplitudes from individual nucleons weighted by phase factors exp(i·t·x_i). For multiple vector meson production, the cross section is calculated in impact-parameter space by integrating the product of production probabilities at a given impact parameter, with additional diagrams included for identical mesons that lead to superradiant enhancement.

## Key numbers
- For lead-208, the lowest excited state is at 2.6 MeV, corresponding to a minimum momentum transfer of 71 MeV/c for incoherent production; for gold-197, the lowest excited state is at 77 keV, corresponding to a minimum momentum transfer of 12 MeV/c
- For producing rho mesons with lead beams at the LHC, the single-meson production probability at impact parameter b = 2R_A is approximately 0.03
- The cross section for Z_c^+(4430) production is roughly 1/400 of that of the a_2(1320)^+
- For emission of M identical mesons at forward rapidities with sufficiently low p_T, the production probability is M! times the probability if superradiance is neglected
- The coherence condition for two identical mesons becomes |k_1|, |k_2| < hbar/(2R_A), tighter than the single-meson condition |k| < hbar/R_A

## Context
This work extends the author's previous critique of the Good-Walker paradigm (Klein 2023) and builds on the semi-classical formulation for coherent photoproduction. It contrasts with the standard Good-Walker approach (Good and Walker 1960) and the Miettinen-Pumplin formulation (Miettinen and Pumplin 1978) that links incoherent production to event-by-event fluctuations. The paper enables future experimental searches for charged meson photoproduction (such as a_2^+(1320) at STAR's forward upgrade) and multi-meson events that could exhibit superradiance and potentially stimulated decays, building on prior calculations of multiple vector meson production (Klein 1999, Baltz 2002, Klusek-Gawenda 2013).
