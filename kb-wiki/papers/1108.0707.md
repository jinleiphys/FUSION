# Nuclear masses, deformations and shell effects

> We show that the Liquid Drop Model is best suited to describe the masses of prolate deformed nuclei than of spherical nuclei. To this end three Liquid Drop Mass formulas are employed to describe nuclear masses of eight sets of nuclei with similar quadrupole deformations. It is shown that they are able to fit the measured masses of prolate deformed nuclei with an RMS smaller than 750 keV, while for the spherical nuclei the RMS is, in the three cases, larger than 2000 keV. The RMS of the best fit of the masses of semi-magic nuclei is also larger than 2000 keV. The parameters of the three models are studied, showing that the surface symmetry term is the one which varies the most from one group of nuclei to another. In one model, isospin dependent terms are also found to exhibit strong changes. The inclusion of shell effects allows for better fits, which continue to be better in the prolate deformed nuclei region

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the Liquid Drop Model (LDM) is systematically better at fitting the measured masses of prolate deformed nuclei (RMS below 750 keV) than those of spherical or semi-magic nuclei (RMS above 2000 keV), across three different LDM formulas. This conclusion is conditional on grouping nuclei by their quadrupole deformation before fitting, rather than fitting all nuclei globally.

## Method

The authors grouped 2149 nuclei (with N, Z ≥ 8) from the AME03 mass evaluation into nine sets: one set of all nuclei, seven sets defined by ranges of quadrupole deformation (taken from the FRDM), and one set of semi-magic nuclei. For each of three distinct Liquid Drop Mass formulas (LDM1 from Die09, LDM2 from Wang10, LDM3 from Roger10), they performed separate least-squares fits to the binding energies of each group, minimizing the RMS deviation using the Minuit routine. They then compared the resulting RMS values and the stability of the fitted parameters across groups.

## Key numbers

- For LDM1, the best-fit RMS for the most prolate deformed nuclei (group 7, 364 nuclei) is 746 keV; for the most spherical nuclei (group 3, 332 nuclei) it is 2063 keV.
- For LDM2, the best-fit RMS for group 7 is 656 keV; for group 3 it is 2069 keV.
- For LDM3, the best-fit RMS for group 7 is 629 keV; for group 3 it is 2151 keV.
- The global RMS for all 2149 nuclei is 2387 keV (LDM1), 2374 keV (LDM2), and 2422 keV (LDM3).
- For semi-magic nuclei (185 nuclei), the best-fit RMS is 2113 keV (LDM1), 2056 keV (LDM2), and 1967 keV (LDM3).
- Including shell corrections (linear and quadratic in valence nucleon number) reduces the global RMS for LDM1 from 2387 keV to 1075 keV; adding 2-, 3-, and 4-body terms further reduces it to 888 keV.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing observation that macroscopic liquid-drop models capture bulk nuclear properties, but it directly challenges the common practice of treating deformation as a small correction to a spherical baseline. The authors compare three specific LDM variants: the consistent treatment of Die09, the isospin-dependent formula of Wang10, and the curvature-corrected formula of Roger10. The results suggest that the Duflo-Zuker model (Duf94, Zuk94, Duf95), which incorporates shell effects through valence nucleon counting, may offer a more natural framework for describing spherical nuclei, while the LDM itself is intrinsically better suited to deformed systems. The work enables future studies to focus on why spherical and semi-magic nuclei require more sophisticated microscopic treatments beyond the LDM.
