# ${\it Ab~initio}$ constraints on thermal effects of the nuclear equation of state

> We exploit the many-body self-consistent Green's function method to analyze finite-temperature properties of infinite nuclear matter and to explore the behavior of the thermal index used to simulate thermal effects in equations of state for astrophysical applications. We show how the thermal index is both density and temperature dependent, unlike often considered, and we provide an error estimate based on our ${\it ab~initio}$ calculations. The inclusion of many-body forces is found to be critical for the density dependence of the thermal index. We also compare our results to a parametrization in terms of the density dependence of the nucleon effective mass. Our study questions the validity of predictions made for the gravitational-wave signal from neutron-star merger simulations with a constant thermal index.

## Key claim

The thermal index \(\Gamma_{\rm th}\) used to model thermal effects in neutron-star merger simulations is both density and temperature dependent, contrary to the constant value often assumed; the inclusion of three-nucleon forces is critical for this density dependence, and a constant \(\Gamma_{\rm th}\) provides only a crude approximation that may lead to shortcomings in predictions for gravitational-wave signals.

## Method

The authors employed the finite-temperature self-consistent Green's function (SCGF) method in the ladder approximation to compute the energy, free energy, pressure, and entropy of symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) and pure neutron matter (PNM) at finite temperature. They used several chiral two-nucleon (2N) interactions up to N3LO and three-nucleon (3N) interactions at N2LO, including evolved Hamiltonians (1.8/2.0, 2.0/2.0, 2.8/2.0 EM), the N2LOsat interaction, and a 2N N3LO + 3N N2LO fit. The thermal index was extracted from the ratio of thermal pressure to thermal energy density, and also characterized via the density-dependent nucleon effective mass from the same SCGF calculations.

## Key numbers

- Thermal index \(\Gamma_{\rm th}\) varies between about 1 and 1.7 across the studied density and temperature range, including nuclear physics uncertainties.
- At low densities, \(\Gamma_{\rm th}\) approaches the nonrelativistic ideal gas value of 5/3; a maximum of \(\approx 1.7\) is observed near saturation density for SNM at low temperatures.
- At high densities, \(\Gamma_{\rm th}\) reaches lower values down to \(\approx 1\) or around \(1.3-1.4\) at higher temperatures.
- Calculations were performed for temperatures \(T = 20, 30, 40,\) and \(50\) MeV, and densities up to twice saturation density (\(n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\)).
- The thermal energy decreases with increasing density for all studied temperatures and both SNM and PNM; at low densities up to \(n = 0.05\) fm\(^{-3}\) the decrease is steeper.
- For SNM, the thermal pressure reaches a maximum around \(0.20\) fm\(^{-3}\) for all temperatures; for PNM, the maximum occurs around saturation density.
- The effective mass at \(T = 30\) MeV first decreases and then increases with density for most interactions; for PNM it reaches values above 1 as density increases.

## Context

This work extends ab initio many-body calculations of nuclear matter to finite temperature using the SCGF method, building on prior zero-temperature studies with chiral interactions (Hebeler 2011, Drischler 2016) and earlier finite-temperature work using many-body perturbation theory (Tolos 2008, Wellenhofer 2014). The results contradict the common practice in neutron-star merger simulations of using a constant thermal index (Bauswein 2010, Rezzolla 2016), and instead support recent findings from Fermi liquid theory and mean-field approaches that the thermal index is non-constant (Constantinou 2015, Lattimer 2016). The study enables improved modeling of thermal effects in astrophysical simulations by providing ab initio constraints on the density and temperature dependence of \(\Gamma_{\rm th}\), and by showing that the thermal index can be characterized through the density-dependent nucleon effective mass.
