# Dynamics of Complex Quantum Systems: Dissipation and Kinetic Equations

> We present a microscopic approach to quantum dissipation and sketch the derivation of the kinetic equation describing the evolution of a simple quantum system in interaction with a complex quantum system. A typical quantum complex system is modeled by means of parametric banded random matrices coupled to the subsystem of interest. We do not assume the weak coupling limit and allow for an independent dynamics of the ``reservoir''. We discuss the reasons for having a new theoretical approach and the new elements introduced by us. The present approach incorporates known limits and previous results, but at the same time includes new cases, previously never derived on a microscopic level. We briefly discuss the kinetic equation and its solution for a particle in the absence of an external field.

## Key claim

The paper presents a microscopic approach to quantum dissipation that does not assume weak coupling between a simple subsystem and a complex "reservoir," and allows the reservoir to have its own independent dynamics; the approach can reproduce known limits (such as the Kramers equation) but also yields new cases, including fractional kinetics with Lévy flights and turbulent-like diffusion, depending on the shape of the correlation function \(G(x)\).

## Method

The authors model the "rest of the Universe" (the complex quantum system) using a parametric banded random matrix Hamiltonian \(H(Q)\), whose matrix elements have specified second cumulants characterized by a spreading width \(\Gamma^\downarrow\), a band width \(\kappa_0\), and a correlation length \(X_0\). They write a double path-integral for the density matrix of the entire system, integrate out the reservoir degrees of freedom following the Feynman-Vernon strategy, and derive an evolution equation for the reduced density matrix of the subsystem \((Q,P)\). In the wide-band limit (\(\kappa_0 \rho(\varepsilon) \gg 1\)) and high-temperature regime, this reduces to a simple kinetic equation (Eq.~(\ref{eq:evol})), which can be solved in quadratures for arbitrary coupling forms.

## Key numbers

* The spreading width \(\Gamma^\downarrow\) controls the "intensity" of coupling between the subsystem and the reservoir; the correlation length \(X_0\) defines the typical momentum exchanged in one interaction act.
* In the classical limit (\(\hbar \rightarrow 0\)) with a Gaussian correlator \(G(x) \approx 1 - x^2/2\), the friction coefficient is \(\gamma = \frac{\beta \Gamma^\downarrow \hbar}{2 M X_0^2}\) and the diffusion coefficient is \(D_{QQ} = \frac{2 X_0^2}{\beta^2 \Gamma^\downarrow \hbar}\) (Eqs.~(\ref{eq:g},\ref{eq:D})).
* For a Gaussian correlator \(G(x) = \exp(-x^2/2)\), the equilibrium even momentum cumulants are \(\langle\langle P^{2n} \rangle\rangle = (-1)^{n-1} \frac{(2n-1)!!}{n} \frac{M X_0^2}{\hbar^2 \beta} \left( \frac{\hbar}{X_0} \right)^{2n}\) for \(n \ge 2\); the second cumulant gives \(\langle\langle P^2 \rangle\rangle = 2 M T\).
* For a correlator \(G(x) = 1 - |x|^\alpha + \ldots\) with \(0 < \alpha \le 2\), the approach yields fractional kinetics with Lévy flights; for another choice, turbulent-like diffusion \(\langle\langle Q^2 \rangle\rangle \propto t^3\) emerges.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic theory of quantum dissipation beyond the Caldeira-Leggett model (1981), which couples a heavy particle to a reservoir of harmonic oscillators and assumes a specific form of the coupling that breaks translation invariance. The present approach uses parametric banded random matrices to model the reservoir, allowing translation-invariant coupling and the derivation of kinetic equations without assuming weak coupling. It enables the microscopic description of fractional kinetics and Lévy flights (previously studied phenomenologically) and turbulent-like diffusion, and it provides a framework for studying dissipative dynamics in systems such as atomic nuclei where no classical limit exists.
