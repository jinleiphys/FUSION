# A simple formula for local burnup based on constant relative reaction rate per nuclei

> A simple and analytical formula is suggested to solve the problems of the local burnup and the isotope distributions. The present method considers two extreme conditions of neutrons penetrating the fuel rod. Based on these considerations, the formula is obtained to calculate the reaction rates of $^{235}$U, $^{238}$U, and $^{239}$Pu and straightforward the local burnup and the isotope distributions. Starting from an initial burnup level, the parameters of the formula are fitted to the reaction rates given by a Monte Carlo (MC) calculation. Then the present formula independently gives very similar results as the MC calculation from the starting to high burnup level, but takes just a few minutes. The relative reaction rates are found to be almost independent on the radius (except $(n,γ)$ of $^{238}$U) and the burnup, providing a solid background for the present formula. A more realistic examination is also performed when the fuel rods locate in an assembly. A combination of the present formula and the MC calculation is expected to have a nice balance on the accuracy and the cost on time.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the relative reaction rates per nucleus for (n,f) of 235U, 238U, and 239Pu and (n,γ) of 235U are approximately constant across the fuel rod radius and with increasing burnup, with only the (n,γ) reaction of 238U showing strong radial dependence. This constancy allows a simple analytical formula, fitted to a single Monte Carlo calculation at an initial burnup level, to reproduce subsequent local burnup and isotope distributions with average errors under 1% for burnup and under 8% for isotope concentrations up to 43.30 MWd/kgU.

## Method

The authors derived an analytical formula for reaction rates by considering two extreme neutron penetration conditions: strong absorption (mean free path much smaller than rod radius) giving exponential radial decay, and weak absorption (mean free path much larger than rod radius) giving constant radial flux. They fitted the formula's coefficients to reaction rates from a single Monte Carlo (TRIPOLI-4) calculation at an initial burnup of 3.4 MWd/kgU for a single UO2 fuel rod with 3.3% enrichment, then used the formula independently to evolve concentrations of 235U, 238U, and 239Pu through burnup steps of 2.85 MWd/kgU up to 43.30 MWd/kgU. The method was also tested against four selected fuel rods in a 24-rod assembly geometry.

## Key numbers

- Initial burnup level bu1 = 3.4 MWd/kgU used as the fitting point for the formula parameters
- Fuel rod radius r0 = 0.4127 cm, with cladding between 0.4127 and 0.4744 cm
- Burnup step Δbu = 2.85 MWd/kgU, with total calculation to bu15 = 43.30 MWd/kgU
- Average error in local burnup between analytical formula and Monte Carlo: 0.32% at bu2, 0.55% at bu8, 0.84% at bu14
- Average error in 239Pu concentration: 1.06% at bu2, 7.03% at bu8, 7.89% at bu14
- Relative reaction rate R'9,f decreased from 2.49 at bu2 to 2.25 at bu9
- Initial 235U concentration at rod center (r/r0 = 1/4): 6.65 × 10^20 cm^-3; 238U: 2.17 × 10^22 cm^-3; 239Pu: 3.69 × 10^19 cm^-3

## Context

This work extends the TRANSURANUS model approach (Lassmann 1994, Schubert 2008), which assumes constant neutron flux and uses a radial form factor only for 238U and 240Pu, by instead assuming constant relative reaction rates per nucleus for most reactions. The formula provides a computationally cheap alternative to full Monte Carlo simulations, requiring only minutes versus the much longer MC runtime, and the authors suggest it can be combined with periodic MC recalculations for improved accuracy. The method is limited to 235U, 238U, and 239Pu in this demonstration, but the authors expect expansion to other nuclides including minor actinides and poisons.
