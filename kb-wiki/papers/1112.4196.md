# Numerical search of discontinuities in self-consistent potential energy surfaces

> Potential energy surfaces calculated with self-consistent mean-field methods are a very powerful tool, since their solutions are, in theory, global minima of the non-constrained subspace. However, this minimization leads to an incertitude concerning the saddle points, that can sometimes be no more saddle points in bigger constrained subspaces (fake saddle points), or can be missing on a trajectory (it missing saddle points). These phenomena are the consequences of discontinuities of the self-consistent potential energy surfaces (SPES). These discontinuities may have important consequences, since they can for example hide the real height of an energy barrier, and avoid any use of a SPES for further dynamical calculations, barrier penetrability estimations, or trajectory predictions. Discontinuities are not related to the quality of the production of a SPES, since even a perfectly converged SPES with an ideally fine mesh can be discontinuous. In this paper we explain what are the discontinuities, their consequences, and their origins. We then propose a numerical method to detect and identify discontinuities on a given SPES, and finally we discuss what are the best ways to transform a discontinuous SPES into a continuous one.

## Key claim

Self-consistent potential energy surfaces (SPES) can be discontinuous even when perfectly converged and finely meshed, and these discontinuities produce "fake" or "missing" saddle points that invalidate dynamical calculations, barrier penetrability estimates, and trajectory predictions. The paper proposes a numerical method to detect such discontinuities and discusses ways to obtain a continuous SPES.

## Method

The authors use a two-center basis Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) model with the Gogny D1S effective interaction, solving constrained HFB equations for multipole moments Q20, Q30, Q40, and Q60. They define a "density distance" Dρρ' as the integral of the absolute difference between two local spatial densities, and compute the maximum density distance Dmax between each point and its Delaunay-defined nearest neighbors on the SPES mesh. A spike in Dmax indicates a discontinuity, whose "signature" is the lowest-order multipole moment whose mean value jumps across the discontinuity.

## Key numbers

- For 256Fm symmetric fission, a discontinuity is found between q20 = 260 b and q20 = 261 b; the density distance spike occurs at these points while the HFB energy curve remains smooth and monotonic.
- For 240Pu symmetric first barrier, a discontinuity is found between q20 = 48 b and q20 = 50 b; the density distance spike occurs at these points while the HFB energy curve is smooth.
- The average Dρρ' for continuous points is around 0.3 for 256Fm and around 1.0 for 240Pu.
- A SPES is considered continuous if Dmax < 2 (dimensionless, measured in nucleons).
- The density distance of any A-nucleon state with the void state is A (e.g., for 240Pu, Dρρ' with void would be 240).

## Context

This work addresses a known problem in self-consistent mean-field calculations: Moller (2009) argued that saddle-point shapes and energies from HFB calculations with multiple constraints "frequently cannot be determined accurately and are subject to errors of fairly random magnitude." The paper extends the understanding of discontinuities as "tipping points" from Catastrophe Theory (Gilmore, 1981) and provides a practical detection method. It enables reliable production of continuous SPES for subsequent dynamical calculations such as the Time-Dependent Generator Coordinate Method (Goutte, 2005; Dubray, 2008), and contrasts with the NSPES problem (non-self-consistent methods) for which no analogous numerical fix exists.
