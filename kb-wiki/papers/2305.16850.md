# Pulsar as a Weber detector of gravitational waves and a probe to its internal phase transitions

> It is believed that cores of neutron stars provide a natural laboratory where exotic high baryon density QCD phases may exist.The theoretically well established {\it neutron superfluid phase} is also believed to be found only inside neutron stars. Focus on neutron stars has intensified in recent years with the direct detection of gravitational waves (GWs) from binary neutron star (BNS) merger, which has allowed the possibility of directly probing the properties of the interior of a neutron star. A remarkable phenomenon manifested by rapidly rotating neutron stars is in their {\it avatar} as {\it Pulsars}. The accuracy of pulsar timing allowed the first indirect detection of GWs from a BNS system and opened up a few exciting possibilities. Any pulsar deformation, even if incredibly tiny, can leave imprints on the pulses by introducing tiny perturbations of the moment of inertia (MI) tensor components. While the diagonal MI components of the perturbed MI tensor affect the pulse timings, the off-diagonal components lead to the pulsar's wobbling and affecting the pulse profile. This opens up an opportunity to explore various phase transitions inside a pulsar core by induced density fluctuations through the observable effects on the pulse timing and profile. Such perturbations also naturally induce a rapidly changing quadrupole moment of the star, thereby providing a new source of GW emission. Another remarkable possibility arises when we consider the effect of an external GW on a neutron star. With the possibility of detecting any minute changes in its configuration through pulse observations, the neutron star has the potential to perform as a Weber detector of GWs. This brief review focuses on these specific aspects of a pulsar, specifically on the type of physics that can be probed by utilizing the effect of changes in the MI tensor on pulse properties.

## Key claim

This review argues that tiny density fluctuations inside a pulsar, whether from internal phase transitions or from an external gravitational wave, perturb the entire moment-of-inertia tensor, producing observable changes in both pulse timing and pulse profile; the pulse profile modification can be relatively large even when timing changes are tiny, because the profile effect scales as \(\epsilon/\eta^2\) where \(\eta\) (the NS deformation parameter) is typically \(10^{-8}\)–\(10^{-4}\).

## Method

The authors review the QCD phase diagram at high baryon density, then discuss how phase transitions (e.g., nuclear matter to quark-gluon plasma) in a pulsar core generate density fluctuations that modify all components of the moment-of-inertia tensor. They examine the effect of random MI-tensor perturbations on pulse timing (diagonal components) and pulse profile (off-diagonal components inducing wobble), and extend the analysis to the case of an external gravitational wave deforming the neutron star, treating the pulsar as a remotely stationed Weber detector whose ringing can be monitored via pulse observations.

## Key numbers

- Pulsar timing accuracy can reach one part in \(10^{15}\), comparable to atomic clocks.
- The neutron star deformation parameter \(\eta\) is typically in the range \(10^{-8}\)–\(10^{-4}\).
- The quark-hadron transition temperature from lattice calculations is \(T_c = 156 \pm 0.6\) MeV.
- The critical point is estimated at \(T \lesssim 132\) MeV, \(\mu_B \gtrsim 485\) MeV.
- For a stellar-mass black hole, the Schwarzschild radius is about one third of a typical neutron star radius, implying baryon densities in collapsing matter may become about 20 times larger than in a neutron star.
- The quarkyonic phase may produce sound speed exceeding \(1/\sqrt{3}\) at densities 3–4 times nuclear density.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on phase-transition-induced density fluctuations in neutron stars (Bagchi 2015, Srivastava 2017, Bagchi 2021) and on the concept of a neutron star as a Weber detector for gravitational waves (Das 2018, Biswal 2019). It contrasts with prior studies that focused only on diagonal MI changes affecting spin rate (Glendenning 1997, Heiselberg 1998). The review enables future searches for past gravitational-wave events, such as the supernova SN185 observed in AD 185, by predicting when perturbed pulses from specific pulsars (e.g., J0900-3144 and J1858-2216) will reach Earth (2016–2049).
