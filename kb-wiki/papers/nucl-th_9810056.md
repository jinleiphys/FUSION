# Strange Messages: Chemical and Thermal Freeze-out in Nuclear Collisions

> Thermal models are commonly used to interpret heavy-ion data on particle yields and spectra and to extract the conditions of chemical and thermal freeze-out in heavy-ion collisions. I discuss the usefulness and limitations of such thermal model analyses and review the experimental and theoretical evidence for thermalization in nuclear collisions. The crucial role of correlating strangeness production data with single particle spectra and two-particle correlation measurements is pointed out. A consistent dynamical picture for the heavy-ion data from the CERN SPS involves an initial prehadronic stage with deconfined color and with an appreciable isotropic pressure component. This requires an early onset of thermalization.

## Key claim

Thermal model analyses of heavy-ion data can provide only a rough qualitative picture of bulk phenomena; they must necessarily fail beyond a certain level of detail due to the small size of collision systems and the strong dynamical evolution on time scales comparable to the microscopic thermalization time, but this failure is expected and should not be taken as evidence against the creation of hot QCD matter.

## Method

The author reviews experimental and theoretical evidence for thermalization in nuclear collisions by comparing thermal model fits to hadron yields (chemical freeze-out) and momentum spectra (thermal freeze-out) across a range of collision systems from SIS to SPS energies. The analysis distinguishes between statistical phase-space occupation (maximum entropy principle without rescattering) and genuine kinetic equilibration (with rescattering and collective flow), and emphasizes the importance of combining single-particle spectra with two-particle HBT correlation measurements to separate thermal motion from collective flow.

## Key numbers

- Chemical freeze-out temperature in e+e- and pp collisions: T_chem = T_had ≈ 170 MeV, with universal strangeness fraction λ_s ≈ 0.2-0.25
- Global strangeness fraction in AA collisions: λ_s ≈ 0.4-0.45, about a factor 2 larger than in e+e- and pp collisions
- Enhancement factor for Ω+Ω̄ at midrapidity in Pb+Pb relative to p+Pb: approximately 17
- Strangeness saturation coefficient γ_s ≈ 0.7 in nuclear collisions, corresponding to λ_s = 0.45
- Thermal freeze-out temperature in 158 A GeV/c Pb+Pb collisions: T_therm ≈ 120 MeV, with average transverse flow velocity ⟨v_⊥⟩ ≈ 0.5 c
- Thermal freeze-out in S+S collisions: T_therm ≈ 140-150 MeV, ⟨v_⊥⟩ ≈ 0.25-0.35 c
- Canonical suppression factor for Ω+Ω̄ in pp collisions at √s = 27 GeV: factor 12 relative to grand canonical treatment, for V = 17.6 fm³, T_had = 170 MeV, γ_s = 0.5

## Context

This paper extends the thermal model analyses of Becattini (1997) and Braun-Munzinger et al. (1997) for chemical freeze-out, and the flow analyses of Sollfrank et al. (1993) and NA49 HBT measurements for thermal freeze-out. The work enables a consistent dynamical picture where strangeness enhancement in AA collisions originates from a prehadronic deconfined stage rather than hadronic reinteractions, and points to the need for future systematic studies of the range 1 < N_part < 100 to investigate the onset of thermal and collective behavior.
