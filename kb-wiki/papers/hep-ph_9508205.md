# Meson Cloud of the Nucleon in Polarized Semi-Inclusive Deep-Inelastic Scattering

> We investigate the possibility of identifying an explicit pionic component of the nucleon through measurements of polarized $Δ^{++}$ baryon fragments produced in deep-inelastic leptoproduction off polarized protons, which may help to identify the physical mechanism responsible for the breaking of the Gottfried sum rule. The pion-exchange model predicts highly correlated polarizations of the $Δ^{++}$ and target proton, in marked contrast with the competing diquark fragmentation process. Measurement of asymmetries in polarized $Λ$ production may also reveal the presence of a kaon cloud in the nucleon.

# Meson Cloud of the Nucleon in Polarized Semi-Inclusive Deep-Inelastic Scattering

## Key claim

The pion-exchange model predicts highly correlated polarizations of the target proton and produced Δ⁺⁺ baryon in semi-inclusive deep-inelastic leptoproduction, in marked contrast with the competing diquark fragmentation process, which could provide direct experimental evidence for a pionic component of the nucleon. However, the measurement of polarization asymmetries tests only the presence of a pionic component independent of the details of the πNΔ form factor, provided the form factor is relatively soft (Λ ≲ 700 MeV).

## Method

The authors calculated the differential cross section for polarized Δ⁺⁺ production from a polarized proton target using the pion-exchange model, where the virtual photon strikes a pion emitted by the nucleon, leaving a Δ baryon. They compared these predictions with a parton model calculation of spectator diquark fragmentation, using SU(6) spin-flavor wave functions to relate polarized fragmentation functions and parametrizing the unpolarized Δ⁺⁺ fragmentation function from EMC data. The calculations were performed for kinematics typical of CEBAF (E = 8 GeV, x = 0.14, Q² = 2 GeV²) and HERMES (E = 30 GeV, x = 0.075, Q² = 4 GeV²).

## Key numbers

- The pion-exchange model predicts that for a proton with spin S = +1/2, the yield of Δ⁺⁺ with spin s = +1/2 is enhanced relative to s = -1/2 by a factor (1 + cosα) vs (1 - cosα), where α is the angle between the proton and Δ momentum vectors.
- The πNΔ coupling constant f_πNΔ is the physical coupling defined at the pion pole (t = m_π²).
- The pion number <n>_πΔ is approximately 0.01, 0.02, and 0.04 for cut-offs Λ = 600, 800, and 1000 MeV respectively in the s_πΔ-dependent form factor.
- For a t-dependent dipole form factor, a cut-off of ~700 MeV corresponds to <n>_πΔ ≈ 0.02.
- The unpolarized Δ⁺⁺ fragmentation function is parametrized as D_uu(z → 1) = α(1 - z)^β with β ≈ 0.3 and α ≈ 0.68, based on EMC data.
- In the quark-parton model, the ratio of polarized Δ states is s = +3/2 : +1/2 : -1/2 : -3/2 ≈ 3 : 2 : 1 : 0.
- At CEBAF energies (E ≈ 8-10 GeV), values of x ≈ 0.13-0.14 can be reached with Q² ≈ 2 GeV² and W² ~ 15 GeV².
- At HERMES (30 GeV beam), the x ≈ 0.05-0.1 region can be probed up to Q² ~ 5 GeV² and W² ≲ 50 GeV².
- The slowest possible Δ produced at ζ = 1 has momentum |p_min| ≈ 340 MeV; for Λ the value is ≈ 193 MeV.
- The peak in the differential cross section for pion exchange occurs at |p| ~ 600 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the pion cloud model previously applied to inclusive DIS by Thomas (1983) and others (Henley and Miller, Signal and Thomas, etc.) to explain the Gottfried sum rule violation observed by the NMC collaboration and the flavor asymmetry (d̄ > ū) confirmed by the NA51 Drell-Yan experiment. The paper proposes semi-inclusive measurements of polarized Δ⁺⁺ and Λ production as a way to obtain direct experimental evidence for mesonic degrees of freedom, which inclusive measurements cannot provide because the pionic signal at small x (x ~ 0.1) is submerged beneath the perturbative sea background. The authors also note that similar measurements of polarized Λ production could reveal a kaon cloud in the nucleon, relevant to understanding the violation of the Ellis-Jaffe sum rule and the small first moment of the proton's spin-dependent structure function g₁ measured by EMC, SMC, and SLAC.
