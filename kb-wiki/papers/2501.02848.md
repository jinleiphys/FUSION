# Direct observation of $β$ and $γ$ decay from a high-spin long-lived isomer in $^{187}$Ta

> $^{187}$Ta ($Z=73$, $N=114$) is located in the neutron-rich $A \approx 190$ region where a prolate-to-oblate shape transition via triaxial softness is predicted to take place. A preceding work on the $K^π = (25/2^-)$ isomer and a rotational band to which the isomer decays carried out by the same collaboration revealed that axial symmetry is slightly violated in this nucleus. This paper focuses on a higher-lying isomer, which was previously identified at 2933(14) keV by mass measurements with the Experimental Storage Ring at GSI. The isomer of interest has been populated by a multi-nucleon transfer reaction with a $^{136}$Xe primary beam incident on a natural tungsten target, using the KEK Isotope Separation System at RIKEN. New experimental findings obtained in the present paper include the internal and external $β$-decay branches from the high-spin isomer and a revised half-life of 136(24) s. The evaluated hindrances for $K$-forbidden transitions put constraints on the spin-parity assignment, which can be interpreted as being ascribed to a prolate shape with a five-quasiparticle configuration by model calculations.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first direct observation of both internal gamma decay and external beta decay from the high-spin isomer in 187Ta (previously identified only by mass measurements), and revises its neutral-atom half-life to 136(24) s, which is much shorter than the lower limit of 5 minutes obtained for fully stripped ions at the ESR storage ring.

## Method

The high-spin isomer in 187Ta was populated via multi-nucleon transfer reactions using a 136Xe primary beam at 7.2 MeV per nucleon incident on a natural tungsten target at the KEK Isotope Separation System (KISS) at RIKEN. The reaction products were stopped in a gas cell, selectively laser-ionized for tantalum, mass-separated, and implanted into a decay station equipped with a multi-segmented proportional gas counter (MSPGC) for electron detection and four Clover-type HPGe detectors for gamma-ray spectroscopy. Decay measurements were performed with beam-on/beam-off cycles of 30/30, 300/300, and 1800/1800 seconds to accommodate the different half-lives of the ground state and isomers.

## Key numbers

- Half-life of the 187Tam2 isomer: 136(24) s (weighted average from internal and external decay branches)
- Excitation energy of the higher-lying isomer (187Tam2): 2933(14) keV (from prior ESR mass measurements)
- Excitation energy of the lower isomer (187Tam1): 1778(1) keV (revised from prior work)
- Half-life of 187Tam1: 7.3(9) s (from prior work)
- Ground-state half-life of 187Ta: 283(10) s (from prior work)
- Gamma-ray energies observed from 187Tam2 decay: 569 keV and 327 keV (in mutual coincidence)
- Energy gap between 187Tam2 and 187Tam1: 259 keV smaller than the sum of the 569 and 327 keV transitions
- Half-life of the (11/2+) isomeric state in 187W populated by beta decay: 1.54(13) microseconds
- Average beam intensity: 1.5 ions/s; total collected: 1.36 x 10^5 187Ta nuclides (1800/1800 s dataset)
- Full-energy peak efficiency of HPGe detectors: 7.8% at 1 MeV, 15% at 150 keV
- MSPGC detection efficiency: 58(5)% for M=2 mode, 43(8)% for M=1 mode

## Context

This work extends the collaboration's previous studies on 187Ta, which identified the K-pi = (25/2-) isomer (187Tam1) with a half-life of 7.3(9) s and revealed slight axial symmetry violation from signature splitting in the rotational band (Chen et al., 2020; Watanabe et al., 2021). The higher-lying isomer was previously identified only by mass measurements at the ESR at GSI (Litvinov et al., 2010; Reed et al., 2012). The observed hindrances for K-forbidden transitions constrain the spin-parity assignment and support a prolate shape with a five-quasiparticle configuration, consistent with the predicted prolate-to-oblate shape transition in the A approximately 190 region. The results enable future experiments to identify the intermediate states populated by beta decay from 187Tam2 and to measure the bare-ion half-life at the ESR for comparison with the neutral-atom value.
