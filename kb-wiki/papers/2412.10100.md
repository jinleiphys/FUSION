# Photon-induced neutron, proton and alpha evaporation from heavy nucleus

> In ultraperipheral heavy-ion collisions (UPCs) at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) Pb nuclei are excited through interactions induced by strong electromagnetic fields. The expected excitation energy could reach hundreds MeV, which leads to the subsequent emission of various particles, including neutrons, protons, and alpha particles. To accurately describe deexcitation of nuclei, we have developed two novel approaches. The first method utilizes the Heavy Ion Phase Space Exploration (HIPSE) model to simulate pre-equilibrium emissions and to estimate the excitation energy of the remaining nucleus. Our second approach introduces a new technique for modeling the excitation energy of the nucleus. This method consists of a two-component function to represent the excitation energy distribution more precisely, accounting for the energy loss due to the interaction between photons and quasi-deuteron. Both of these modeling techniques are integrated with the results produced by the GEMINI++ generator, which implements the Hauser-Feshbach formalism to simulate the statistical decay of excited nuclei. The alternative calculations are done with EMPIRE platform. Using these approaches, we obtained the cross sections for the emission of neutrons, protons, and alpha particles resulting from UPC at the LHC. Our results were compared with the experimental data of the ALICE group on neutron and proton multiplicities.

## Key claim

The paper claims that pre-equilibrium emission significantly reduces the excitation energy available for statistical decay in photon-induced reactions on heavy nuclei above about 30–50 MeV, and that models incorporating this effect (HIPSE+GEMINI++ and EMPIRE) reproduce ALICE neutron and proton multiplicity data more accurately than the simple assumption that full photon energy becomes excitation energy (pure GEMINI++).

## Method

The authors compute cross sections for neutron, proton, deuteron, and alpha emission from ultraperipheral Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC by combining the Equivalent Photon Approximation for the photon flux with three deexcitation frameworks: pure GEMINI++ (which assumes full photon energy becomes excitation energy), HIPSE+GEMINI++ (where HIPSE simulates pre-equilibrium emission before statistical decay in GEMINI++), and EMPIRE (a modular nuclear reaction code that includes pre-equilibrium physics). They also introduce a two-component model (TCM) that corrects the GEMINI++ excitation energy distribution with a phenomenological parametrization. Up to four photon exchanges are included in the cross section calculation.

## Key numbers

* Total neutron production cross section in UPC \(^{208}\)Pb+\(^{208}\)Pb at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\) = 5.02 TeV: HIPSE+GEMINI++ gives 151.6 b, EMPIRE gives 152.2 b, pure GEMINI++ gives 122.6 b, ALICE data is 151.5 ± 4.7 b.
* 1n cross section: HIPSE+GEMINI++ = 124.1 b, EMPIRE = 100.8 b, pure GEMINI++ = 90.4 b, ALICE data = 108.4 ± 3.9 b.
* 3n cross section: HIPSE+GEMINI++ = 4.9 b, EMPIRE = 5.9 b, pure GEMINI++ = 3.3 b, ALICE data = 7.95 ± 0.25 b.
* 1p cross section: HIPSE+GEMINI++ = 28.5 b, EMPIRE = 7.1 b, pure GEMINI++ = 19.6 b, ALICE data = 40.4 ± 1.7 b.
* 1α cross section: HIPSE+GEMINI++ = 42.4 b, EMPIRE = 64.0 b, pure GEMINI++ = 30.7 b (no ALICE data available).
* Fission cross section: HIPSE+GEMINI++ = 18.3 b, pure GEMINI++ = 10.8 b.
* Pre-equilibrium emission onset: for photon energies above 30 MeV (EMPIRE) or above about 50 MeV (HIPSE), the photon energy is partially dissipated before thermal equilibration.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical description of photon-induced deexcitation in ultraperipheral collisions, building on the Equivalent Photon Approximation framework of Klusek-Gawenda (2013) and the ALICE experimental measurements of neutron multiplicities (ALICE, 2022) and proton multiplicities (ALICE, 2023). The authors compare three deexcitation models (GEMINI++, HIPSE+GEMINI++, EMPIRE) and show that pre-equilibrium emission is essential for reproducing the measured cross sections, particularly for high neutron multiplicities and charged particle emission. The results enable predictions for future measurements of alpha particles and deuterons in UPC experiments, and highlight the need for further development of the two-component model (TCM) to improve agreement with ALICE proton data.
