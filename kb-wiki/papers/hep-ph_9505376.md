# Self-Organized Criticality in Quark-Hadron Phase Transition

> The problem of clusters growth in quark-hadron phase transition in heavy-ion collision is investigated by cellular automata. The system is found to exhibit self-organized criticality with the distribution of cluster sizes having universal scaling behavior.

# Self-Organized Criticality in Quark-Hadron Phase Transition

## Key claim
The cluster-size distribution of hadrons produced at the end of the quark-hadron phase transition in heavy-ion collisions exhibits a universal power-law scaling characteristic of self-organized criticality, provided that an extended mixed-phase region persists for a long time. The authors note that the proposed signature might be suppressed by factors not yet considered, but if it survives, there are no competing processes that can cause ambiguity in interpretation.

## Method
The authors model the mixed-phase region as an annular ring in a 2D section at midrapidity of an expanding cylinder after a head-on heavy-ion collision, mapped onto a square lattice with periodic boundary conditions. A cellular automaton simulates nucleation (with probability p per time step for an unoccupied quark site to become an occupied hadron site), random walks with average drift, cluster coalescence upon contact, and removal of clusters reaching the right boundary. An improved automaton also allows cluster deformation (parametrized by shape factor α) and breakup (with probability B depending on fragment sizes and parameter β).

## Key numbers
- The cluster-size distribution follows P(S) ∝ S^(-γ) with γ ≈ 1.9, averaged over p ranging from 0.05 to 0.5.
- For p from 0.05 to 0.5, γ varies only within 10% of the average value.
- Simulations were performed for lattice sizes L = 16 and L = 32, and nucleation sizes S0 = 1 and S0 = 2.
- For p > 0.05, the distribution is insensitive to the shape parameter α for 1.0 < α < 1.4.
- The breakup probability is given by B(S, S1, S2) = β (√(S1/S) + √(S2/S))^(-1), where β is a parameter; the scaling law shows virtually no dependence on β when p is not too small.

## Context
This work extends the authors' own earlier cellular automaton studies in 1D (Hwa 1994) and 2D (Hwa 1995) by introducing improved rules for cluster deformation and breakup. It connects heavy-ion collision physics to the concept of self-organized criticality (SOC) as developed by Bak, Tang, and Wiesenfeld (1988) for systems like sandpiles, forest fires, and earthquakes. The finding suggests that event-by-event analysis of particle density in η-φ space, partitioned by pT bins, could be used to search experimentally for the predicted clustering of hadrons, which would serve as a signature of quark-gluon plasma formation.
