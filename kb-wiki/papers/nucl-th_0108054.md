# Randomly interacting bosons, mean-fields and L=0 ground states

> Random interactions are used to investigate to what extent the low-lying behavior of even-even nuclei depend on particular nucleon-nucleon interactions. The surprising results that were obtained for the interacting boson model, i.e. the dominance of ground states with L=0 and the occurrence of both vibrational and rotational structure, are interpreted and explained in terms of a mean-field analysis.

# Randomly interacting bosons, mean-fields and L=0 ground states

## Key claim
The dominance of L=0 ground states and the occurrence of both vibrational and rotational band structures in the interacting boson model with random interactions can be explained by a mean-field analysis that identifies three distinct geometric equilibrium shapes, rather than requiring specific forms of the nucleon-nucleon interaction.

## Method
The authors generated the most general one- and two-body IBM Hamiltonian with matrix elements chosen independently from a Gaussian distribution of random numbers with zero mean and width σ, ensuring invariance under orthogonal basis transformations. For each set of random matrix elements, they calculated the entire energy spectrum exactly and also performed a mean-field analysis using coherent states to construct potential energy surfaces, identifying equilibrium configurations as spherical, deformed, or d-boson condensate shapes.

## Key numbers
- L=0 ground states occur in approximately 60-75% of cases across all boson numbers N (exact calculation)
- The spherical equilibrium shape contributes a constant 39.4% to the L=0 ground state percentage
- The deformed equilibrium shape contributes a constant 23.4% to the L=0 ground state percentage
- In the mean-field analysis, the ground state has L=0 or L=2 in approximately 77% of cases
- The probability distribution P(R) of the energy ratio R = [E(4₁)-E(0₁)]/[E(2₁)-E(0₁)] shows two pronounced peaks at the vibrational value R=2 and the rotational value R=10/3 for N=16

## Context
This work extends earlier shell model studies by Johnson, Bertsch, and Dean (1998) that first showed a statistical preference for L=0 ground states with random two-body interactions, and prior IBM studies by the same authors (Bijker and Frank, 2000). The mean-field analysis provides a geometric explanation for the regular features observed numerically, bypassing the need for large-scale diagonalization. The approach was previously applied to the simpler vibron model by Bijker and Frank (2000), and here it is generalized to the full IBM, enabling a direct connection between random Hamiltonian parameters and the three geometric shapes (spherical, deformed, d-boson condensate) that produce the observed vibrational and rotational band structures.
