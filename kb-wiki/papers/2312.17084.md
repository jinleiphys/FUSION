# Production of super-heavy nuclei in hot-fusion reactions

> A model for hot-fusion reactions leading to the synthesis of super-heavy nuclei is discussed. The values of the hot-fusion cross-sections obtained in the model agree with the available experimental data. The hot-fusion cross-sections are found for two different models of the fission barrier heights of super-heavy nuclei. The calculations of the cross-sections for the various hot-fusion reactions leading to the 119 and 120 elements are presented. Simple expressions useful for qualitative analysis of the cross-section for forming super-heavy nuclei are obtained. It is shown that the super-heavy nuclei production cross section is proportional to the transmission coefficient of the capture barrier, realization probability of the $xn$-evaporation channel, and exponentially depends on the quasi-elastic barrier, fusion reaction Q-value, compound nucleus formation barrier, neutron separation energies, and fission barrier heights.

## Key claim

The model reproduces available experimental data for hot-fusion super-heavy nuclei production cross-sections, but the results depend strongly on which fission barrier model is used; the difference between the FRDM and MMWS fission barrier heights can reach approximately 4 MeV for super-heavy nuclei, leading to production cross-sections that differ by up to two orders of magnitude for elements with Z = 120.

## Method

The cross-section for super-heavy nucleus synthesis via hot-fusion reactions is calculated as the product of the transmission coefficient through the capture barrier (averaged over orientations of the deformed target nucleus using the Ahmed formula), the probability of compound nucleus formation (derived from the ratio of the compound-nucleus formation width to the quasi-elastic decay width), and the survival probability after evaporation of x neutrons in competition with fission. The compound nucleus formation probability is evaluated using the back-shifted Fermi gas model for level densities, with the compound nucleus formation barrier height set equal to the fission barrier height plus a small correction that decreases with increasing proton number. The fission width is calculated using an energy-dependent fission barrier that includes liquid-drop and shell-correction contributions with a damping factor.

## Key numbers

- The difference between neutron separation energies obtained from the FRDM and MMWS models for most super-heavy nuclei with 110 ≤ Z ≤ 122 is close to or smaller than 0.8 MeV
- The difference between fission barrier heights from the FRDM and MMWS models can reach approximately 4 MeV for super-heavy nuclei formed in hot-fusion reactions
- The difference in production cross-sections for super-heavy nuclei with Z = 120 obtained using the two different fission barrier models reaches two orders of magnitude
- The number of neutrons evaporated from the compound nucleus in experimentally measured hot-fusion reactions is 3 or 4, and rarely 2 or 5
- The asymptotic level density parameter is given by a⁰_A = 0.0722396 A + 0.195267 A^(2/3) MeV⁻¹
- The damping parameter for the shell correction is γ = 0.410289/A^(1/3) MeV⁻¹
- The back shift energy is Δ = 12n/A^(1/2) + 0.173015 MeV, where n = -1, 0, and 1 for odd-odd, odd-A, and even-even nuclei respectively

## Context

This work extends the models previously developed for cold-fusion reactions (Denisov, 2021) and compound nucleus formation in heavy-ion reactions (Denisov, 2023) to the case of hot-fusion reactions, where spherical projectiles collide with deformed actinide targets. The calculations use two different fission barrier models: the macroscopic-microscopic finite-range liquid-drop model (FRDM) from Moller et al. and the microscopic-macroscopic method with deformed Woods-Saxon single-particle potential (MMWS) from Jachimowicz et al. The results enable predictions of production cross-sections for elements 119 and 120, which are important for planning future experiments, and provide simple analytical expressions for qualitative analysis of the cross-section dependence on model parameters.
