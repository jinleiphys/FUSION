# Kilonovae and Long-duration Gamma-ray Bursts

> Recent detections of kilonova-like emission following long-duration gamma-ray bursts GRB211211A and GRB230307A have been interpreted as originating from the merger of two neutron stars. In this work, we demonstrate that these observations are also consistent with nucleosynthesis originating from a collapsar scenario. Our model accurately predicts the observed optical and infrared light curves using a single weak $r$-process component. The absence of lanthanide-rich material in our model, consistent with the data, challenges the prevailing interpretation that a red evolution in such transients necessarily indicates the presence of heavy $r$-process elements.

# Kilonovae and Long-duration Gamma-ray Bursts

## Key claim

The kilonova-like emission observed following long-duration gamma-ray bursts GRB211211A and GRB230307A, previously interpreted as evidence of neutron star mergers, is also consistent with nucleosynthesis occurring in a collapsar scenario. The authors demonstrate that a single weak r-process component can reproduce the observed optical and infrared light curves, and that the absence of lanthanide-rich material in their model challenges the assumption that red evolution in such transients necessarily indicates the presence of heavy r-process elements.

## Method

The authors performed nucleosynthesis calculations using version 1.6.0 of the Portable Routines for Integrated nucleoSynthesis Modeling (PRISM) reaction network, considering fiducial models for nucleosynthesis from a collapsar jet head scenario. They generated simulated kilonova spectra using the SuperNu Monte Carlo radiation transport code with a single-component, spherically symmetric, homologously expanding ejecta model parameterized by ejecta mass and velocity. A grid of 59 simulations spanning ejecta masses from 0.001 to 0.5 solar masses and velocities from 0.01c to 0.3c was used to train Gaussian process surrogate models, and Bayesian parameter inference was performed using the RIFT algorithm against observations of GRB211211A and GRB230307A.

## Key numbers

- GRB211211A and GRB230307A both had prompt gamma-ray durations of approximately 40 seconds
- The fiducial model with ejecta mass 0.1 solar masses and velocity 0.2c consisting of a weak r-process composition reproduced the light curves of both GRBs prior to any data fitting
- GRB211211A best-fit parameters: ejecta mass 0.13 solar masses, velocity 0.221c
- GRB230307A best-fit parameters: ejecta mass 0.27 solar masses, velocity 0.03c
- The simulation grid spanned ejecta masses from 10^{-3} to 0.5 solar masses and velocities from 0.01c to 0.3c
- The weak r-process produces material up to mass number A approximately 130
- The magnetic sieve simulation showed electron fraction Ye approximately 0.3 for radial distances 0.8 to 1.2 times the jet radius in the strong magnetic field case (B greater than or equal to 10^{12} Gauss)

## Context

This work extends the collapsar nucleosynthesis model proposed by Mum25, which suggested that photo-hadronic interactions at the jet head could produce neutrons for r-process nucleosynthesis in the cocoon. It directly challenges the interpretation of Rastinejad (2022) and Yang (2024) that GRB211211A and GRB230307A originated from binary neutron star mergers, and is consistent with the analysis of Neg25 and Zhu25 showing these GRBs align with the collapsar phase space. The results contradict the prevailing view that red kilonova emission is a "smoking gun" of lanthanide production, supporting instead the findings of Tak23 and 2024ApJ...961....9F that late-time infrared emission can occur without heavy r-process elements. The large ejecta masses (0.13 and 0.27 solar masses) rule out double neutron star mergers as the source and are at the edge of what is possible for black hole-neutron star mergers, while the high velocities disfavor disk wind scenarios which are typically slower-peaked as shown by Miller (2020).
