# Chiral condensates and screening masses of neutral pseudoscalar mesons from lattice QCD at physical quark masses

> We investigate the effects of temperature $T$ and external magnetic fields $eB$ on the chiral condensates and screening masses of neutral pseudoscalar mesons, including $π^0$, $K^0$, and $η_{s\bar{s}}^0$, in (2+1)-flavor lattice QCD with physical quark masses. The chiral condensates are intrinsically connected to the screening masses via Ward-Takahashi identities, with the latter characterizing the inverse of the spatial correlation length in the pseudoscalar channel. Using highly improved staggered quarks, we perform simulations on lattices with temporal extents $N_τ= 8, 12, 16$ and an aspect ratio of 4, covering five temperatures from 145 MeV to 166 MeV. For each temperature, eight magnetic field strengths are simulated, reaching up to $eB \sim 0.8$ GeV$^2$. These simulations allow us to provide continuum estimates for the chiral condensates and screening masses. We observe intricate behavior in the light ($ud$), strange-light ($ds$) and strange ($s$) quark condensates as functions of the magnetic field and temperature, reflecting the competition between magnetic catalysis and inverse magnetic catalysis effects. This complex behavior is also mirrored in the screening masses of the neutral pseudoscalar mesons. Notably, the screening masses of $π^0$ and $K^0$ exhibit a non-monotonic dependence on $eB$, closely following the variations in their corresponding chiral condensates. Meanwhile, the screening mass of $η_{s\bar{s}}^0$ decreases monotonically with increasing $eB$. These findings provide valuable insights for understanding the behavior of QCD in a thermomagnetic medium and can serve as benchmarks for low-energy QCD models and effective theories.

## Key claim

The paper provides continuum-extrapolated lattice QCD results for chiral condensates and screening masses of neutral pseudoscalar mesons at physical quark masses, but the results are limited to five temperatures between 145 MeV and 166 MeV and magnetic fields up to eB ~ 0.8 GeV², with the continuum estimate obtained using only three lattice spacings (Nτ = 8, 12, 16) and a linear or quadratic ansatz in 1/Nτ².

## Method

The authors performed (2+1)-flavor lattice QCD simulations using highly improved staggered quarks (HISQ) with physical quark masses (Mπ ≈ 135 MeV) on lattices with temporal extents Nτ = 8, 12, and 16 and aspect ratio Nσ/Nτ = 4. For each of five temperatures (145-166 MeV), eight magnetic field strengths were simulated up to eB ~ 0.8 GeV², and the chiral condensates and meson spatial correlation functions were computed to extract screening masses via exponential fits with multiple states, using the corrected Akaike information criterion for model selection.

## Key numbers

- Five temperatures simulated: 145, 151, 157, 162, and 166 MeV
- Eight magnetic field strengths per temperature, reaching up to eB ~ 0.8 GeV²
- Three lattice spacings used: Nτ = 8, 12, and 16
- Physical pion mass Mπ = 135 MeV and kaon mass MK = 498 MeV used for normalization
- Normalization constants: fπ = 86 MeV, fK/fπ = 1.2
- Fictitious η⁰_s\bar{s} mass set to M_η⁰_s\bar{s} = √(2MK² - Mπ²) ≈ 684 MeV
- Light quark mass ratio: ml = ms/27
- Peak position of ΔΣud shifts from ~0.46 GeV² at lowest temperature to ~0.1 GeV² at highest temperature
- Magnetic field quantization: eB = 6πNb/(Nσ²a²) with Nb ranging from 0 to 24

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study Ding (2022) which used a single lattice spacing with larger-than-physical pion mass (~220 MeV) and a fixed-scale approach. The results provide continuum-extrapolated benchmarks for low-energy QCD models and effective theories studying magnetic catalysis and inverse magnetic catalysis, complementing earlier lattice studies by Bali (2012) which used Nτ = 6, 8, 10 with a linear continuum extrapolation. The findings enable quantitative comparisons with effective model calculations such as those by Sheng (2021) and can guide future studies of QCD in thermomagnetic media relevant to heavy-ion collisions and early universe physics.
