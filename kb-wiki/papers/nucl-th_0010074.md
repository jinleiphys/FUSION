# Barrier penetration and rotational damping of thermally excited superdeformed nuclei

> We construct a microscopic model of thermally excited superdeformed states that describes both the barrier penetration mechanism, leading to the decay-out transitions to normal deformed states, and the rotational damping causing fragmentation of rotational E2 transitions. We describe the barrier penetration by means of a tunneling path in the two-dimensional deformation energy surface, which is calculated with the cranked Nilsson-Strutinsky model. The individual excited superdeformed states and associated E2 transition strengths are calculated by the shell model diagonalization of the many-particle many-hole excitations interacting with the delta-type residual two-body force. The effect of the decay-out on the excited superdeformed states are discussed in detail for $^{152}$Dy, $^{143}$Eu and $^{192}$Hg.

## Key claim

The paper constructs a microscopic model of thermally excited superdeformed states that simultaneously describes barrier penetration (leading to decay-out transitions to normal deformed states) and rotational damping (causing fragmentation of rotational E2 transitions), but the model's quantitative predictions depend on a specific choice of pairing force strength (set to give a barrier height similar to previous calculations) and on whether the tunneling width is evaluated using the yrast formula or the statistically averaged formula for excited states.

## Method

The authors combine a cranked Nilsson-Strutinsky mean-field calculation of the two-dimensional deformation energy surface with a shell model diagonalization of many-particle many-hole excitations using a delta-type residual two-body force to obtain excited superdeformed states and their E2 transition strengths. They then couple these states to normally deformed compound states using the Vigezzi tunneling model, where the tunneling width is calculated from the classical action integral along a minimal-action path in the deformation space, using a collective mass tensor based on the pair-hopping model. The average decay-out branching ratio is evaluated using a random matrix (GOE) description of the normally deformed states, and the E2 transition probabilities are renormalized by the factor (1 - average decay-out probability).

## Key numbers

- For $^{152}$Dy, $^{143}$Eu and $^{192}$Hg, the potential energy surfaces are calculated with a grid of deformation parameters: $-0.18 \le q_1 \le 0.60$, $-0.12 \le q_2 \le 0.42$ and $-0.10 \le \epsilon_4 \le 0.14$ with an interval of 0.06.
- The maximum rotational frequency is extended to give calculated spin values up to 60 $\hbar$.
- The pairing force strength is determined by the smoothed gap method with $\tilde{\Delta} = 10/\sqrt{A}$ MeV, which is smaller by about 6% for nuclei in both the Dy and Hg region compared to the standard value of $12/\sqrt{A}$ MeV.
- The cut-off parameter for calculating the effective RPA pairing gap is taken as $\omega_{\rm cut} = 400$ keV/$\hbar$, and the imaginary part of frequency in the response function is $\delta = 200$ keV/$\hbar$.
- The model-space energy cut-off for the Nilsson potential is $1.2\hbar\omega_0$ below and above the Fermi surface, where $\hbar\omega_0 = 41.0/A^{1/3}$ MeV.
- The empirical constants for the mass tensor are $G_0 = 25/A$ MeV and $\Delta_0 = 12/\sqrt{A}$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic rotational damping model previously applied to normal deformed nuclei in the rare-earth region (Matsuo, 1997; Bracco, 1998) and to superdeformed nuclei (Yoshida, 1998; Yoshida, 1999; Leoni, 1999) by incorporating the barrier penetration model of Vigezzi (1990) and Shimizu (1993, 1994). It goes beyond phenomenological Monte-Carlo statistical simulation models (Schiffer, 1994; Khoo, 1996) by providing a fully microscopic description of both rotational damping and decay-out. The model enables quantitative predictions for quasi-continuum gamma-ray spectra from thermally excited superdeformed nuclei, allowing tests of the potential energy surface far from the minimum points and studies of large-amplitude shape dynamics under thermal and rotational motion.
