# Light particle and quark chemical potentials from negatively to positively charged particle yield ratios corrected by removing strong and weak decays

> The yield ratios of negatively to positively charged pions ($π^-/π^+$), negatively to positively charged kaons ($K^-/K^+$), and anti-protons to protons ($\bar p/p$) produced in mid-rapidity interval in central gold-gold (Au-Au) collisions, central lead-lead (Pb-Pb) collisions, and inelastic (INEL) or non-single-diffractive (NSD) proton-proton ($pp$) collisions, as well as in forward rapidity region in INEL $pp$ collisions are analyzed in the present work. Over an energy range from a few GeV to above 10 TeV, the chemical potentials of light flavor particles (pion, kaon, and proton) and quarks (up, down, and strange quarks) are extracted from the mentioned yield ratios in which the contributions of strong decay from high-mass resonance and weak decay from heavy flavor hadrons are removed. Most energy dependent chemical potentials show the maximum at about 4 GeV, while the energy dependent yield ratios do not show such an extremum.

# Light particle and quark chemical potentials from negatively to positively charged particle yield ratios corrected by removing strong and weak decays

## Key claim
The chemical potentials of light flavor particles (pion, kaon, proton) and light quarks (up, down, strange) extracted from yield ratios of negatively to positively charged particles show a maximum at about 4 GeV for proton, up, down, and strange chemical potentials, but only after the contributions of strong decay from high-mass resonances and weak decay from heavy flavor hadrons are removed from the measured yield ratios.

## Method
The authors analyzed published yield ratios of pi-/pi+, K-/K+, and antiproton/proton from central Au-Au collisions at AGS and RHIC, central Pb-Pb collisions at SPS and RHIC, and inelastic or non-single-diffractive pp collisions at SPS and LHC, over a center-of-mass energy per nucleon pair range from a few GeV to above 10 TeV. They applied corrections from a recent statistical thermal model study (2019) to remove the contributions of strong decays from high-mass resonances and weak decays from heavy flavor hadrons from the measured yield ratios. Using the Boltzmann approximation in a grand-canonical ensemble with a single chemical freeze-out temperature parameterization, they extracted particle chemical potentials from the corrected yield ratios via the relation mu_j = -(1/2)T_ch ln(k_j), and quark chemical potentials from combinations of the yield ratios.

## Key numbers
- The limiting temperature T_lim used in the chemical freeze-out temperature parameterization is approximately 0.16 GeV.
- The fitted empirical function for k_pi in central Au-Au (Pb-Pb) collisions without decay corrections is: k_pi = (4.212 +/- 0.682) * (sqrt(s_NN))^(-1.799 +/- 0.152) + (1.012 +/- 0.019), with chi^2/dof = 0.162.
- The fitted empirical function for k_pi in central Au-Au (Pb-Pb) collisions with decay corrections is: k_pi = (3.712 +/- 0.611) * (sqrt(s_NN))^(-1.519 +/- 0.148) + (1.012 +/- 0.019), with chi^2/dof = 0.392.
- The fitted empirical function for k_pi in INEL or NSD pp collisions is: k_pi = -(2.453 +/- 0.292) * (sqrt(s_NN))^(-0.943 +/- 0.057) + (0.984 +/- 0.009), with chi^2/dof = 1.559.
- The fitted empirical function for k_p in central Au-Au (Pb-Pb) collisions without decay corrections is: k_p = exp[-(34.803 +/- 3.685) * (sqrt(s_NN))^(-0.896 +/- 0.041) - (0.008 +/- 0.004)], with chi^2/dof = 7.715.
- The fitted empirical function for k_p in central Au-Au (Pb-Pb) collisions with decay corrections is: k_p = exp[-(37.403 +/- 3.776) * (sqrt(s_NN))^(-0.884 +/- 0.036) - (0.007 +/- 0.003)], with chi^2/dof = 5.323.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous analysis (Lao, Gao, and Liu, 2019) by removing the contributions of strong and weak decays from the yield ratios before extracting chemical potentials, following the decay correction framework of a recent statistical thermal model study (2019). The extracted chemical potentials show a maximum at about 4 GeV for proton, up, down, and strange chemical potentials, which the authors interpret as possibly the initial energy of hadronic limiting fragmentation for heavy nuclei like Au and Pb, and possibly the onset energy of a phase transition from a liquid-like state of nucleons and mesons to a gas-like state. The method provides a simple alternative to the standard approach using conserved charges (baryon number, strangeness, isospin, charm) and enables simultaneous determination of both particle and quark chemical potentials from experimentally measured yield ratios.
