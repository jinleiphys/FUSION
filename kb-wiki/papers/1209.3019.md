# Calculating the Charge of a Jet

> Jet charge has played an important role in experimentally testing the Parton Model and the Standard Model, and has many potential LHC applications. The energy-weighted charge of a jet is not an infrared-safe quantity, so hadronization must be taken into account. Here we develop the formalism to calculate it, cleanly separating the nonperturbative from the perturbative physics, which we compute at one-loop order. We first study the average and width of the jet charge distribution, for which the nonperturbative input is related to (dihadron) fragmentation functions. In an alternative and novel approach, we consider the full nonperturbative jet charge distribution and calculate its evolution and jet algorithm corrections, which has a natural Monte Carlo-style implementation. Our numerical results are compared to Pythia and agree well in almost all cases. This calculation can directly be extended to similar track-based observables, such as the total track momentum generated by an energetic parton.

## Key claim

Jet charge can be calculated analytically by cleanly separating nonperturbative from perturbative physics, with the nonperturbative input related to (dihadron) fragmentation functions, and the perturbative part computed at one-loop order; the formalism agrees well with Pythia in almost all cases, but the comparison is not particularly constraining because uncertainties on charge-separated fragmentation functions are fairly large.

## Method

The author develops a factorization formalism in Soft-Collinear Effective Theory (SCET) for the energy-weighted jet charge, first computing the average and width of the jet charge distribution using fragmenting jet functions matched onto fragmentation functions. An alternative approach treats the full nonperturbative jet charge distribution, calculating its renormalization group evolution and jet algorithm corrections at one-loop order, which admits a natural Monte Carlo-style implementation. Numerical results are compared to the Pythia event generator for e+e- collisions using kT-type jet algorithms.

## Key numbers

- For a d-quark jet with E = 100 GeV using the e+e- anti-kT algorithm with R=0.5, the jet charge distribution is shown at hadronic and partonic levels (Figure 1).
- The average jet charge for a quark jet is given by a perturbative coefficient multiplied by a nonperturbative number D_q^Q(kappa, mu) related to fragmentation functions, with values at mu = 1 GeV given in the appendix for several FF sets.
- The difference between kT, Cambridge-Aachen, and anti-kT algorithms for average and width of jet charge is only a few percent and grows for small R (Figure 2).
- The boost invariance of the average and width of jet charge, keeping 2E tan(R/2) = 100 GeV fixed, holds at the percent level (Figure 3).
- A Monte Carlo implementation (JetFrag) with parameters delta=0.05 and d ln mu=0.05 yields average jet charge 0.128 and width 0.339, compared to analytic values 0.126 and 0.331 (Table I).

## Context

This work extends the framework for fragmentation in jets developed by Procura (2009), Jain (2011), and others in SCET, providing the first analytic calculation of jet charge that had previously relied solely on Monte Carlo programs such as Pythia (Sjostrand 2006, 2007). The formalism enables systematic uncertainty estimation and is improvable through higher-order perturbative calculations or power corrections; it can be directly extended to other track-based observables such as total track momentum from an energetic parton, and has potential applications at the LHC for separating quark from antiquark jets and distinguishing quark flavors (Krohn 2012).
