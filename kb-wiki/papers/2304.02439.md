# Unstable Anisotropic Neutron Stars: Probing the Limits of Gravitational Collapse

> Neutron stars (NSs) are incredibly versatile for studying various important aspects of high-energy and compact-object physics. These celestial objects contain extreme matter at incredibly high densities in their interiors, leading to the risk of instabilities that may cause them to collapse into a black hole (BH). This paper focuses on exploring the stability and gravitational collapse of NSs. For a more realistic approach we have considered the pressure to be locally anisotropic. We utilize the BL-Model to describe the anisotropy inside the NS. The presence of quarks in the core of an NS can heavily affect its stability. Hence, along with pure hadronic EOSs, we have also considered Hadron-Quark phase transition (HQPT) EOSs for this paper's analysis. We subject the anisotropic NSs to radial perturbations to study their stability against radial oscillations. NSs exhibiting imaginary eigen-frequencies are identified as unstable, and their inevitable destiny is gravitational collapse, resulting in the formation of a BH. We consider the interior of these unstable anisotropic NSs to be a non-ideal fluid in a non-adiabatic background in order to study its dynamical evolution during the collapse. We examine the temporal evolution of key properties of NSs, such as mass, density, heat flux, and anisotropy during the process of gravitational collapse. We present an innovative and viable approach to detect such high-energy gravitational collapse events, providing valuable insights into the properties of the static NS before its collapse.

## Key claim
The paper claims that for anisotropic neutron stars described by the Bowers-Liang model, the stability limit determined by zero eigenfrequency of the fundamental radial oscillation mode is attained earlier (at lower central density) than the maximum-mass configuration for positive anisotropy, except for soft hadron-quark phase transition equations of state where the f-mode frequency tends to remain constant during the phase transition, delaying the onset of instability.

## Method
The authors solved the modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for spherically symmetric neutron stars with locally anisotropic pressure described by the Bowers-Liang model, using two hadronic equations of state (BSk21 and SLY4) and two hadron-quark phase transition equations of state (soft and stiff variants). They computed mass-radius relations, tidal deformabilities, and moments of inertia for varying anisotropy parameters, then performed linear radial perturbation analysis to determine oscillation eigenfrequencies and identify unstable configurations. For unstable stars, they modeled the dynamical gravitational collapse as a non-adiabatic process with bulk viscosity, radial heat flow, and outward radiation flux.

## Key numbers
- Maximum mass increases with anisotropy parameter β_BL from 0 to 0.5 for all equations of state; for BSk21, values of 0.4 < β_BL < 0.5 satisfy the GW190814 mass constraint of 2.50–2.67 M⊙
- Tidal deformability at 1.4 M⊙ (Λ_1.4) satisfies the GW170817 limit of 190_{-120}^{+390} for almost all β_BL values, while 0 < β_BL < 0.1 satisfies the GW190814 limit of 616_{-158}^{+273}
- The range 0.15 < β_BL < 0.35 is consistent with the latest observational data from NICER and X-ray observations
- For soft HQPT equation of state, the zero eigenfrequency is attained later than the maximum-mass configuration, unlike for hadronic equations of state where zero eigenfrequency occurs before maximum mass

## Context
This work extends the stability analysis of Pretel (2020) to anisotropic hybrid neutron stars with hadron-quark phase transitions, which had not been previously studied. The authors use the Bowers-Liang model because, as shown by Pretel (2020), it is one of the anisotropic models where the stability limit from radial oscillations does not coincide with the maximum-mass criterion, unlike the Horvat et al. (2011) and Raposo et al. (2019) models. The paper enables future detection strategies for gravitational collapse events from unstable anisotropic neutron stars and provides constraints on the degree of anisotropy from observational data including GW170817, GW190814, and NICER results from Miller (2019) and Riley (2019).
