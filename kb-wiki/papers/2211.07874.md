# On the sound velocity bound in neutron stars

> It has been suggested in the literature that the sound velocity of the nuclear matter $v_s$ violates the so-called sound velocity bound $v_s \le c/\sqrt{3}$ at high density, where $c$ is the speed of light. In this paper, we revisit this issue and confront the current measurements of mass, radius, and tidal deformability of neutron stars with $10^5$ different equations of state which are parametrized at low density and saturates the sound velocity bound beyond twice the saturation density where the equation of state has not been constrained yet, by which we can conservatively obtain the maximum mass of the neutron stars compatible both with the observed properties of neutron stars and the sound velocity bound. We find that majority of the models are eliminated by the incompatibility with the observations and, especially, the recently detected massive pulsar ($2.35\pm 0.17 M_\odot$) is hardly realized by our simulations. Our study strongly supports the violation of the sound velocity bound.

## Key claim

The authors find that the maximum mass of a neutron star compatible with the sound velocity bound \(v_s \le c/\sqrt{3}\) is at most \(2.178\,M_\odot\), which is very close to the lower end of the mass range of the recently detected massive pulsar (\(2.35 \pm 0.17\,M_\odot\)). However, they note that the sound velocity bound is not robustly ruled out because their sample is limited and a few equations of state in their set do achieve \(M_{\rm max} \gtrsim 2.1\,M_\odot\) after observational constraints are applied.

## Method

The authors generate \(10^5\) different equations of state using a Skyrme-based parametrization (Hebeler 2013) with five parameters randomly sampled from normal distributions whose means and variances are set by experimental values of saturation density, binding energy, incompressibility, symmetry energy, and its slope. Below twice the saturation density (\(2n_0\)), the EoS is given by the nuclear model; above \(2n_0\), the EoS is forced to saturate the sound velocity bound \(v_s = c/\sqrt{3}\) by imposing a linear relation between pressure and energy density. For each EoS, they solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain the mass-radius relation and compute the tidal deformability, then apply observational constraints from NICER mass-radius measurements of PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620 and from the tidal deformability limit \(\Lambda < 1400\) for a \(1.4\,M_\odot\) star from GW170817.

## Key numbers

* The histogram of maximum masses for the \(10^5\) EoS (without observational constraints) centers at a mean value of \(1.892\,M_\odot\).
* After applying observational constraints, only \(7.05\%\) of the models remain.
* The highest maximum mass found among the constrained models is \(2.178\,M_\odot\).
* The recently detected massive pulsar has mass \(2.35 \pm 0.17\,M_\odot\).
* The tidal deformability constraint imposed is \(\Lambda < 1400\) for a neutron star of mass \(1.4\,M_\odot\).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Bedaque (2014), who used a similar EoS that saturates the sound velocity bound above a critical density to derive a robust maximum mass. The authors compare their histogram to that of Bedaque (2014) and note a discrepancy whose origin remains unsettled. The study supports the conclusion of Ecker (2022) and others that the sound velocity bound is violated in nature, but leaves the question open due to limited sample size; the authors argue that more realistic EoS satisfying the bound would shift the histogram to lower masses, making the bound even more incompatible with observations.
