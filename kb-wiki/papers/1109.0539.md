# Three-dimensional hydrodynamic simulations of the combustion of a neutron star into a quark star

> We present three-dimensional numerical simulations of turbulent combustion converting a neutron star into a quark star. Hadronic matter, described by a micro-physical finite-temperature equation of state, is converted into strange quark matter. We assume this phase, represented by a bag-model equation of state, to be absolutely stable. Following the example of thermonuclear burning in white dwarfs leading to Type Ia supernovae, we treat the conversion process as a potentially turbulent deflagration. Solving the non-relativistic Euler equations using established numerical methods we conduct large eddy simulations including an elaborate subgrid scale model, while the propagation of the conversion front is modeled with a level-set method. Our results show that for large parts of the parameter space the conversion becomes turbulent and therefore significantly faster than in the laminar case. Despite assuming absolutely stable strange quark matter, in our hydrodynamic approximation an outer layer remains in the hadronic phase, because the conversion front stops when it reaches conditions under which the combustion is no longer exothermic.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for large parts of the parameter space, the conversion of hadronic matter into strange quark matter proceeds as a turbulent deflagration, significantly faster than laminar burning, but that an outer hadronic layer always remains because the combustion stops when it is no longer exothermic, even under the assumption that strange quark matter is absolutely stable.

## Method

The authors performed three-dimensional, non-relativistic hydrodynamic simulations using a piecewise parabolic method (PPM) Godunov scheme, treating the conversion front as a discontinuity tracked with a level-set method. They modeled the hadronic phase with the Lattimer-Swesty equation of state (incompressibility modulus K=180 MeV) and the strange quark matter with a bag-model equation of state, and included a subgrid-scale turbulence model to account for unresolved turbulent motions. The simulations were initialized with a cold, non-rotating neutron star of 1.4 solar masses and an initial central energy density of 1.0 × 10¹⁵ g/cm³, with the conversion ignited from a small central seed.

## Key numbers

* The laminar burning velocity adopted from Niebergal (2010) is approximately 10⁸ cm/s at central densities of ~10¹⁵ g/cm³.
* The Gibson scale, below which turbulence cannot distort the flame front, is estimated as l_G ≈ 10² cm, while the Kolmogorov scale is l_K ≈ 10⁻⁸ cm.
* For a bag constant of B^(1/4) = 150 MeV, the remaining unburnt hadronic mass at t = 3.0 ms is 0.66 M_⊙ (model B150_128) and 0.67 M_⊙ (model B150_192).
* The minimum length scale for turbulent burning λ_min ranges from 3.6 × 10⁴ cm (B^(1/4) = 145 MeV) to 4.5 × 10⁵ cm (B^(1/4) = 157 MeV) at t = 0.1 ms.
* The gravitational mass of the remaining hadronic outer layer varies from 0.48 M_⊙ (B^(1/4) = 147 MeV) to 0.99 M_⊙ (B^(1/4) = 155 MeV) at t = 3.0 ms.

## Context

This work extends earlier one-dimensional studies of laminar conversion by Olinto (1987) and Heiselberg (1991) and the turbulent combustion suggestion by Horvath (1988) by providing the first three-dimensional, multi-dimensional simulations of the neutron-star-to-quark-star conversion. It follows the methodological approach used for thermonuclear burning in Type Ia supernovae (e.g., Reinecke 2002, Roepke 2005) and adapts it to the neutron star context with an effective relativistic gravitational potential. The results enable future studies of the observational signatures of quark novae, such as the extremely luminous SN 2006gy proposed by Leahy (2008) and Ouyed (2010), and highlight the need for general relativistic simulations and more realistic equations of state.
