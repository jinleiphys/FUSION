# Clustering in hadrons and light nuclei from Lorentz boosted form factors

> The determination of nuclear charge radii is crucial for understanding the internal structure of nuclei and their fundamental interactions. A persistent discrepancy, not only in the measured proton charge radius but also in the light nucleus charge radius, between electron scattering and muonic spectroscopy has fueled ongoing debates in nuclear and particle physics. Using this discrepancy, we revisit the role of one of the proposed solutions, namely the use of Lorentz-boosted nuclear form factors to find a subtle connection between the boost and the cluster structure of nuclei. By applying two distinct relativistic formalisms, namely the Licht-Pagnamenta and Mitra-Kumari approaches, we systematically analyze corrections to the moments of the density distributions in hadrons and nuclei. Our results demonstrate that boosting the form factors from the Breit to the rest frame of the nucleus not only assists in reconciling the spectroscopic and scattering measurements but also provides a method to infer on the quark and nucleon cluster configurations within nuclei.

## Key claim
By applying Lorentz boosts to nuclear form factors to relate scattering and spectroscopic measurements, the discrepancy in charge radii can be reduced, and the number of constituent clusters (n) required for the correction reveals the internal cluster structure of hadrons and light nuclei. The authors explicitly state that the work is not an attempt to comment on clustering based on fundamental theories of the strong interaction, but rather to use a subtle connection between radii measurements that depends on the number of constituent clusters.

## Method
The authors applied two relativistic formalisms, the Licht-Pagnamenta (LP) and the Mitra-Kumari (MK) approaches, to Lorentz-boost form factors from the Breit frame to the rest frame of the nucleus. They derived relations between the charge radius in the moving frame (from scattering) and the rest frame (from spectroscopy) that depend on the number of constituent clusters (n) and the nuclear mass. Using published experimental radii for the proton, deuteron, helium-3, and helium-4, they solved for the value of n that best reconciles the scattering and spectroscopic values, thereby inferring the dominant cluster configuration (e.g., nucleons vs. quarks).

## Key numbers
*   **Proton charge radius (LP, n=3):** Corrected value of 0.84157 fm, compared to the spectroscopic value of 0.84184(67) fm.
*   **Proton magnetic radius (MK, n=3):** Corrected value of 0.768 fm, compared to the spectroscopic value of 0.778(29) fm.
*   **Deuteron charge radius (MK, n=6):** Corrected value of 2.12192 fm, compared to the spectroscopic value of 2.12562(13) fm.
*   **Helium-3 charge radius (MK, n=3):** Corrected value of 1.96927 fm, compared to the spectroscopic value of 1.97007(94) fm.
*   **Helium-4 charge radius (LP, n=4):** Corrected value of 1.67916 fm, compared to the spectroscopic value of 1.67824(13) fm.

## Context
This work extends the proposal by Miller (2019) that a bound proton is larger than a free one and revisits the Lorentz-boost solution to the proton radius puzzle first proposed by Licht and Pagnamenta (1970s) and later generalized by Mitra and Kumari. The authors use the persistent discrepancy between electron scattering and muonic spectroscopy radii (e.g., the CODATA 2010 proton radius values) as a tool rather than a problem to be solved. The method enables a novel way to probe precluster or subcluster structures in light nuclei, such as the 3-alpha structure of the Hoyle state in carbon-12 or the alpha-particle condensation in oxygen-16, but the authors note its limitation for heavy nuclei where the mass-dependent correction becomes too small relative to experimental precision.
