# Shear viscosity expression for a graphene system in relaxation time approximation

> We have gone through the detailed microscopic calculation of the shear viscosity of a 2-dimensional graphene system in the relaxation time approximation-based kinetic theory framework. After getting its final expressions, we compared it with the shear viscosity expressions of other possible 2-dimensional as well as 3-dimensional nonrelativistic and ultra-relativistic fluid systems. The aim of the comparison is to reveal how their different one-body dispersion relations affect their many-body fluid properties like shear viscosity and the viscosity to entropy density ratio. It is also aimed to reveal the 3-dimension to the 2-dimension transformation of their mathematical structures. We have numerically explored the differences in their order of magnitude and dependence on thermodynamical parameters-temperature and chemical potential. Marking two thermodynamical domains-Dirac fluid and Fermi liquid-for a 2-dimensional graphene system, we have noticed that shear viscosity, entropy density as well as their ratios decrease toward saturated values when one goes from Fermi liquid to Dirac fluid domain. When one shifts from mili-electron volt scales of temperature and chemical potential in condensed matter physics location to their mega-electron volt scales in high energy physics location, then the same results may be expected for hot quark matter case, where the transition from the neutron star to early universe domains may be considered as Fermi liquid to Dirac fluid transition.

## Key claim

The paper derives analytic expressions for shear viscosity and entropy density of a two-dimensional graphene system using the relaxation time approximation, and shows that these quantities decrease toward saturated values when the system transitions from the Fermi liquid domain (high chemical potential relative to temperature) to the Dirac fluid domain (low chemical potential relative to temperature). The authors emphasize that their comparison is between hypothetical 2D and 3D nonrelativistic and ultra-relativistic fluids on equal footing, not directly between graphene and real-world fluids like water.

## Method

The authors start from the energy-momentum tensor and use kinetic theory with the relaxation time approximation to solve the Boltzmann transport equation for electrons in graphene, which obey the linear dispersion relation \(E = p v_g\) where \(v_g \approx 10^6\) m/s is the graphene Fermi velocity. They derive closed-form expressions for shear viscosity \(\eta\), entropy density \(s\), and their ratio \(\eta/s\) in terms of Fermi integral functions of the fugacity \(A = e^{\beta\mu}\), temperature \(T\), and chemical potential \(\mu\). The same formalism is then applied to obtain analogous expressions for 2D and 3D nonrelativistic fluids (\(E = p^2/(2m)\)) and ultra-relativistic fluids (\(E = p\)), allowing a systematic comparison of how different dispersion relations affect these transport coefficients.

## Key numbers

* Graphene Fermi velocity: \(v_g \approx 10^6\) m/s, corresponding to \(v_g = 0.003\) to \(0.01\) in natural units (with \(c=1\)); the paper uses a constant intermediate value \(v_g = 0.006\) for numerical plots.
* Condensed matter physics domain: temperature \(T \approx 1\) to \(23\) meV and chemical potential \(\mu \approx 0\) to \(10\) eV.
* High energy physics domain: temperature \(T = 1\) to \(400\) MeV and chemical potential \(\mu = 0\) to \(1000\) MeV.
* For 2D graphene, the shear viscosity expression is \(\eta_g^{2D} = \frac{3 N_s}{8 \pi v_g^2} \tau_c f_3(A) T^3\), where \(N_s = 2\) is the spin degeneracy and \(\tau_c\) is the relaxation time.
* For 2D nonrelativistic fluid, the shear viscosity is \(\eta_{NR}^{2D} = \frac{N_s m}{8\pi} \tau_c f_2(A) T^2\).
* The entropy density ratio \(s/s_{SB}\) for 3D graphene is approximately \(1/v_g^3 \approx 5 \times 10^6\) times larger than the ultra-relativistic case at the same temperature and chemical potential.

## Context

This work extends the kinetic theory approach previously applied to relativistic quark-gluon plasma (QGP) in heavy ion collision physics (e.g., Refs. QGP1-QGP10) to the electron hydrodynamic regime in graphene, following the theoretical framework of Ref. [24PhysRevLett.103.025301] which first addressed graphene shear viscosity microscopically. The paper systematically compares the mathematical structure of shear viscosity expressions across 2D and 3D systems with three different dispersion relations (graphene, nonrelativistic, and ultra-relativistic), filling a gap in the literature where such a side-by-side comparison was missing. The results enable future experimental tests of electron hydrodynamics in graphene, where Poiseuille flow patterns have already been observed (Refs. 1Ku:2019lgj, 9sulpizio2019visualizing), and suggest that analogous behavior may occur in hot quark matter when transitioning from neutron star to early universe conditions.
