# Effects of hydrodynamic and initial longitudinal fluctuations on rapidity decorrelation of collective flow

> We investigate the interplay between hydrodynamic fluctuations and initial longitudinal fluctuations for their effects on the rapidity decorrelation of collective flow in high-energy nuclear collisions. We use a (3+1)-dimensional integrated dynamical model in which we combine initial conditions with longitudinal fluctuations, fluctuating hydrodynamics and hadronic cascades. We analyse the factorisation ratio in the longitudinal direction to study the effect of these fluctuations on the rapidity decorrelation. We find an essential difference between the effects of the hydrodynamic fluctuations and the initial longitudinal fluctuations in the centrality dependence of the factorisation ratios. A combination of the hydrodynamic fluctuations and the initial longitudinal fluctuations leads to reproduction of the centrality dependence of the second-order factorisation ratio, $r_2(η_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{a},η_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{b})$, measured by the CMS Collaboration. Our model also qualitatively describes the centrality dependence of the third-order factorisation ratio, $r_3(η_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{a},η_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{b})$. These results demonstrate the importance of the hydrodynamic fluctuations, as well as the initial longitudinal fluctuations, in understanding the longitudinal dynamics of high-energy nuclear collision reactions.

## Key claim

The paper claims that both initial longitudinal fluctuations and hydrodynamic fluctuations must be included simultaneously in a dynamical model to reproduce the centrality dependence of the longitudinal factorisation ratio \(r_2(\eta_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{a},\eta_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{b})\) measured by the CMS Collaboration; neither type of fluctuation alone can reproduce the observed centrality dependence across all centrality classes.

## Method

The authors used a (3+1)-dimensional integrated dynamical model combining Monte-Carlo Glauber initial conditions with longitudinal fluctuations implemented via Pythia hadron generation and rejection sampling, the causal fluctuating hydrodynamics code `rfh` with shear viscosity \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\), and the hadronic cascade model JAM. They performed simulations of Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}} = 2.76\ \text{TeV}\) for both fluctuating hydrodynamics and viscous hydrodynamics (with fluctuations turned off), generating 400,000 events per model, and computed the longitudinal factorisation ratios \(r_2\) and \(r_3\) as functions of pseudorapidity and centrality.

## Key numbers

- Specific shear viscosity set to \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\), relaxation time \(\tau_\pi = 3/4\pi T\), initial proper time \(\tau_0 = 0.6\ \text{fm}\), switching temperature \(T_\mathrm{sw} = 155\ \text{MeV}\).
- Cutoff parameters for hydrodynamic fluctuations: \(\lambda_\perp = 2.0\ \text{fm}\) and \(\lambda_\eta = 2.0\) (with \(\lambda_\perp/\text{fm} = \lambda_\eta\)).
- Hadron profile widths: \(\sigma_\perp = 0.3\ \text{fm}\) and \(\sigma_\eta = 0.3\).
- Normalisation parameter \(K = 4.8\) for both viscous and fluctuating hydrodynamics; \(p_\mathrm{T0} = 1.75\ \text{GeV}\) (viscous) and \(1.80\ \text{GeV}\) (fluctuating); \(\Delta p_\mathrm{T} = 1.0\ \text{GeV}\) for both.
- 4,000 minimum-bias hydrodynamic events per model, each with 100 independent particlisation and cascade runs, yielding 400,000 events total per model.
- Factorisation ratio \(r_2(\eta_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{a},\eta_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{b})\) computed with reference hadrons in \(3.0 < \eta_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{b} < 4.0\) and \(\eta_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{a}\) in \(0 < \eta_\mathrm{p}^\mathrm{a} < 2.5\), following CMS Collaboration setup.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Sakai et al. (2020) that showed the importance of hydrodynamic fluctuations for longitudinal decorrelation but lacked initial longitudinal fluctuations, and by other groups (Bozek 2015, Pang 2014, 2015, 2018, Wu 2018) who studied initial longitudinal fluctuations without hydrodynamic fluctuations. The paper demonstrates that neither effect alone can reproduce the centrality dependence of the CMS Collaboration measurements (Khachatryan 2015), and that both must be included simultaneously. This enables future models to incorporate both fluctuation sources for quantitative understanding of longitudinal dynamics in heavy-ion collisions.
