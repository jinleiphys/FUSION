# Combining phase-space and time-dependent reduced density matrix approach to describe the dynamics of interacting fermions

> The possibility to apply phase-space methods to many-body interacting systems might provide accurate descriptions of correlations with a reduced numerical cost. For instance, the so--called stochastic mean-field phase-space approach, where the complex dynamics of interacting fermions is replaced by a statistical average of mean-field like trajectories is able to grasp some correlations beyond the mean-field. We explore the possibility to use alternative equations of motion in the phase-space approach. Guided by the BBGKY hierarchy, equations of motion that already incorporate part of the correlations beyond mean-field are employed along each trajectory. The method is called Hybrid Phase-Space (HPS) because it mixes phase-space techniques and the time-dependent reduced density matrix approach. The novel approach is applied to the one-dimensional Fermi-Hubbard model. We show that the predictive power is improved compared to the original stochastic mean-field method. In particular, in the weak-coupling regime, the results of the HPS theory can hardly be distinguished from the exact solution even for long time.

## Key claim

The Hybrid Phase-Space (HPS) method, which replaces the mean-field-like trajectory equations of the stochastic mean-field approach with equations of motion derived from the BBGKY hierarchy that incorporate part of the correlations beyond mean-field, improves predictive power compared to the original stochastic mean-field method; in the weak-coupling regime, the HPS results can hardly be distinguished from the exact solution even for long times, whereas the original SMF approach showed deviations at long times.

## Method

The authors start from the stochastic mean-field (SMF) phase-space approach, where an ensemble of one-body density matrices is initialized with Gaussian fluctuations matching the quantum initial state and each trajectory evolves via the time-dependent Hartree-Fock equation. They replace this mean-field evolution with a coupled system: the one-body density follows an equation containing a two-body source term (Eq. 9), and a fluctuating two-body density matrix is propagated using an equation closed by approximating the three-body density as a product of the two-body and one-body densities (Eq. 13). The initial two-body density is taken as non-fluctuating and equal to the exact initial two-body density, while the one-body density retains the same Gaussian initial fluctuations as in SMF. The method is applied to the one-dimensional Fermi-Hubbard model with sharp boundary conditions, for systems of 4 and 8 particles on 4 and 8 sites respectively, with initial particles all located on the left half of the lattice.

## Key numbers

* For the 4-site, 4-particle Fermi-Hubbard model at coupling strength U/J = 0.1, the HPS method reproduces the exact occupation probability of the leftmost site n_1(t) up to at least t = 100 J^{-1}, whereas the original SMF approach shows visible deviation from the exact solution after approximately t = 40 J^{-1}.
* For the 8-site, 8-particle system at U/J = 0.1, the HPS method again closely follows the exact solution for n_1(t) over the full time range shown (up to t = 100 J^{-1}), while the SMF result deviates from the exact solution at long times.
* The HPS results in both the 4-site and 8-site cases are obtained by averaging over 10000 trajectories.

## Context

This work extends the stochastic mean-field (SMF) phase-space approach of Ayik (2008) and the fermion truncated-Wigner approximation of Davidson and Polkovnikov (2017) by incorporating guidance from the BBGKY hierarchy. The authors had previously explored improving SMF by relaxing the Gaussian approximation for initial fluctuations (Yilmaz 2014, Ulgen 2019), but found only marginal improvements for the Hubbard model. The HPS method enables more accurate long-time descriptions of interacting fermion dynamics in the weak-coupling regime, and the authors note that the approach could potentially be extended to treat systems with initial residual two-body correlations, though not systems with initial three-body correlations.
