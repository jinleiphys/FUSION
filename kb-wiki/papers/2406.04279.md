# Genetic Programming for the Nuclear Many-Body Problem: a Guide

> Genetic Programming is an evolutionary algorithm that generates computer programs, or mathematical expressions, to solve complex problems. In this Guide, we demonstrate how to use Genetic Programming to develop surrogate models to mitigate the computational costs of modeling atomic nuclei with ever increasing complexity. The computational burden escalates when uncertainty quantification is pursued, or when observables must be globally computed for thousands of nuclei. By studying three models in which the mean field depends on the total particle density self-consistently, we show that by constructing reduced order models supported by Genetic Programming one can speed up many-body computations by several orders of magnitude with a negligible loss in accuracy

# Genetic Programming for the Nuclear Many-Body Problem: a Guide

## Key claim

Genetic Programming can construct reduced-order model equations for non-affine and non-linear many-body quantum systems where traditional Galerkin projection methods fail, achieving speedups of several orders of magnitude with negligible loss in accuracy. The authors demonstrate this specifically for cases where the density-dependent interaction contains fractional powers, which are problematic for standard reduced basis method emulation.

## Method

The authors apply the reduced basis method to obtain reduced coordinates (coefficients) from high-fidelity solutions, then use tree-based Genetic Programming to evolve symbolic expressions that map control parameters to these reduced coordinates, bypassing the need for Galerkin projection. They test this on two self-consistent mean-field problems: a modified Gross-Pitaevskii equation with a nonlinear density-dependent term $\rho(x)^\sigma$, and a Skyrme Density Functional Theory calculation for $^{48}$Ca varying the symmetry energy $J_\text{sym}^\text{NM}$ and its slope $L_\text{sym}^\text{NM}$. For both systems, three reduced basis components are used, and the Genetic Programming results are compared against regularized polynomial linear regression models.

## Key numbers

- For the modified Gross-Pitaevskii equation: 492 training evaluations and 212 test evaluations of densities were used; all supervised machine learning methods were more than 4 orders of magnitude faster than the high-fidelity solver (Python implementation).
- For the DFT case: 25 training evaluations and 16 test evaluations of proton and neutron densities for $^{48}$Ca were used; all methods were faster by almost 4 orders of magnitude compared to the FORTRAN DFT solver.
- The control parameter ranges explored were: $\kappa \in [0.5, 3.0]$, $q \in [-2.0, 2.0]$, $\sigma \in [0.5, 3.0]$ for Gross-Pitaevskii; $J_\text{sym}^\text{NM} \in [20, 40]$ MeV and $L_\text{sym}^\text{NM} \in [25, 65]$ MeV for DFT.
- Genetic Programming used a population size of 300, ran for 5 minutes per run, with 16 independent runs; crossover probability was 0.9 and mutation probability was 0.1.

## Context

This work extends the reduced basis method approach previously applied to nuclear physics by Bonilla (2022) and others, which used Galerkin projection for constructing reduced equations but struggled with non-affine and non-linear operators. The authors specifically note that terms in realistic Energy Density Functionals (from the UNEDF series of parameterizations) contain fractional powers of densities, motivating this alternative data-driven approach. The Genetic Programming method enables emulation of nuclear Density Functional Theory calculations without explicitly tracking underlying wave functions, and the authors suggest it could be applied to direct model discovery from experimental data.
