# Reply to comment on "Equation of state of dense and magnetized fermion system"

> A Comment [1] to our recent article [2], expressed criticism on our estimate of the maximum magnetic field that can exist inside a neutron star and on the pressure anisotropy we found for a magnetized gas of fermions. These two points are relevant for the physics of neutron stars. With our reply we attempt not only to address the Comment's criticisms, but for the sake of the reader's understanding, we also try to clarify the connection between our and some literature's results mentioned in the comment.

## Key claim
The maximum magnetic field inside a hybrid neutron star with a quark-matter core can be as large as \(10^{20}\) G, not the widely cited \(10^{18}\) G, because the estimate depends critically on the baryon density at the core; the authors' ad-hoc model with nonuniform mass and field distributions was only illustrative and not used for the paper's main results.

## Method
The authors defend two independent estimates from their original paper: a macroscopic estimate based on equipartition of gravitational and magnetic energies with nonuniform distributions (ad-hoc and not used for main results), and a microscopic, model-independent estimate equating the magnetic energy density to the baryon energy density. They also argue that the pressure anisotropy in a magnetized fermion gas arises from the breaking of spatial rotational symmetry by the magnetic field, as captured by the energy-momentum tensor's covariant structure, and that the Comment's proposed cancellation via magnetization currents does not apply because \(\nabla \times M = 0\) for a static system with uniform magnetic field.

## Key numbers
- Maximum magnetic field at the core of a hybrid star: \(10^{20}\) G, based on the condition that magnetic energy density is at most as large as the baryon energy density, with quark-matter chemical potential \(\mu \gtrsim 400\) MeV.
- The widely cited estimate of \(10^{18}\) G (Lai and Shapiro, 1991) applies to nuclear matter, not quark matter, and assumes uniform mass and field distributions.
- The proton critical field is \(\sim 10^{20}\) G, below which the equation of state is substantially softened by the magnetic field.

## Context
This reply defends the authors' earlier work (Ferrer and de la Incera, 2010) against a Comment that criticized their maximum field estimate and pressure anisotropy. The authors distinguish their result from the virial-theorem-based estimate of Lai and Shapiro (1991) and from numerical simulations by Bocquet et al. (1995) and Kiuchi et al. (2009), which they argue omitted Landau quantization effects or did not include the magnetic field in the equation of state. The discussion highlights the unresolved question of whether pressure anisotropy can be compensated by magnetohydrodynamic effects in strongly magnetized stars, requiring future self-consistent treatment using axisymmetric TOV equations.
