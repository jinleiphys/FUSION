# Two center shell model with Woods-Saxon potentials: adiabatic and diabatic states in fusion

> A realistic two-center shell model for fusion is proposed, which is based on two spherical Woods-Saxon potentials and the potential separable expansion method. This model describes the single-particle motion in a fusing system. A technique for calculating stationary diabatic states is suggested which makes use of the formal definition of those states, i.e., they minimize the radial nonadiabatic coupling between the adiabatic states. As an example the system $^{16}$O + $^{40}$Ca $\to$ $^{56}$Ni is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a realistic two-center shell model for fusion based on two spherical Woods-Saxon potentials and the potential separable expansion method, extending previous work by conserving the system volume during amalgamation. The authors claim that their technique for calculating stationary diabatic states minimizes the radial nonadiabatic coupling between adiabatic states, but this minimization is performed only for couplings whose strength exceeds a threshold value \(\gamma_{thr}\), and only between neighbouring levels.

## Method

The two-center problem is solved in momentum representation using the potential separable expansion method, where each fragment's Woods-Saxon potential (including spin-orbit and Coulomb terms for protons) is expanded in a truncated harmonic oscillator basis. The potential parameters are interpolated between separated-nuclei and compound-nucleus values using a function \(y(R)\) derived from the condition that the volume enclosed by the Fermi-level equipotential surface is conserved for all internuclear distances \(R\). Stationary diabatic states are obtained by diagonalizing the radial nonadiabatic coupling matrix (after removing the spurious displacement term) within a truncated set of adiabatic states with the same quantum number \(\Omega\), including only couplings between neighbouring levels that exceed a threshold \(\gamma_{thr}\).

## Key numbers

- For the system \(^{16}\text{O} + ^{40}\text{Ca} \to ^{56}\text{Ni}\), the contact configuration occurs at \(R \approx 7.3\) fm.
- The threshold value for the strength of radial nonadiabatic couplings used to calculate neutron diabatic states was \(\gamma_{thr} = 1.4\).
- The threshold value for the strength of radial nonadiabatic couplings used to calculate proton diabatic states was \(\gamma_{thr} = 1.7\).
- The neutron potential depth for \(^{16}\text{O}\) is 74.90 MeV, for \(^{40}\text{Ca}\) is 51.40 MeV, and for \(^{56}\text{Ni}\) is 46.20 MeV.
- The proton potential depth for \(^{16}\text{O}\) is 79.10 MeV, for \(^{40}\text{Ca}\) is 70.80 MeV, and for \(^{56}\text{Ni}\) is 53.70 MeV.
- The harmonic oscillator basis used \(n_{max} = 4\) and \(l_{max} = 3\) for all nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the two-center shell model beyond the traditional double-oscillator potential approach introduced by the Frankfurt school (Holzer, Maruhn) and later used in fission and fusion studies (Park, Mirea, Radu). It builds on the potential separable expansion method previously applied to peripheral collisions by Gareev et al. and Milek and Reif, but now conserves the system volume during amalgamation, enabling description of very asymmetric reactions such as those used to form superheavy elements (e.g., \(^{40}\text{Ca} + ^{208}\text{Pb}\)). The technique for calculating stationary diabatic states by minimizing radial nonadiabatic coupling follows the formal definition of Delos and complements earlier approximate methods (maximum symmetry method, maximum overlap method) used by Lukasiak et al. and by Diaz-Torres and Scheid in prior work.
