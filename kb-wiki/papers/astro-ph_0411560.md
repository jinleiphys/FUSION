# A Hot Water Bottle for Aging Neutron Stars

> The gapless color-flavor locked (gCFL) phase is the second-densest phase of matter in the QCD phase diagram, making it a plausible constituent of the core of neutron stars. We show that even a relatively small region of gCFL matter in a star will dominate both the heat capacity C_V and the heat loss by neutrino emission L_ν. The gCFL phase is characterized by an unusual quasiparticle dispersion relation that makes both its specific heat c_V and its neutrino emissivity epsilon_νparametrically larger than in any other phase of nuclear or quark matter. During the epoch in which the cooling of the star is dominated by direct Urca neutrino emission, the presence of a gCFL region does not strongly alter the cooling history because the enhancements of C_V and L_νcancel against each other. At late times, however, the cooling is dominated by photon emission from the surface, so L_νis irrelevant, and the anomalously large heat capacity of the gCFL region keeps the star warm. The temperature drops with time as T\sim t^{-1.4} rather than the canonical T\sim t^{-5}. This provides a unique and potentially observable signature of gCFL quark matter.

## Key claim

The presence of even a small region of gapless color-flavor locked (gCFL) quark matter in a neutron star core will dominate both the heat capacity and neutrino emission, but the key observable signature occurs at late times (stars older than about a million years): the temperature drops as T ~ t^{-1.4} rather than the canonical T ~ t^{-5}, keeping old neutron stars anomalously warm by an order of magnitude or more.

## Method

The authors calculated the specific heat c_V and neutrino emissivity ε_ν of the gCFL phase using the quasiparticle dispersion relations derived from model calculations at μ = 500 MeV, imposing color and electric neutrality constraints. They then applied these results to a simplified neutron star cooling model, comparing the cooling evolution when gCFL matter is present versus when it is absent, considering both the neutrino-dominated and photon-dominated cooling epochs.

## Key numbers

- The gCFL specific heat is dominated by a quasiparticle with quadratic dispersion relation, giving c_V ≈ 0.146 (p_{1,2}^{bu})^2 Δ_2^{1/2} T^{1/2}, where Δ_2 is the us-pairing gap parameter and p_{1,2}^{bu} is the gapless momentum
- For the linear-dispersion gapless modes (bd-gs pairing), the specific heat contribution is c_V = (1/3) p_F^2 T / v, where v_bd is the Fermi velocity given by v_bd = sqrt(1 - Δ_1^2 / [½(μ_gs - μ_bd)]^2)
- The gCFL phase exists for M_s^2/μ between approximately 2Δ_0 and 5Δ_0, where Δ_0 is the CFL gap at M_s = 0 (estimated between 10 and 100 MeV)
- For Δ_0 = 25 MeV and M_s = 250 MeV, the gCFL window corresponds to μ between 320 MeV and 800 MeV
- At late times, the temperature scales as T ~ t^{-1.4} instead of the canonical T ~ t^{-5}
- The electron chemical potential μ_e in the example calculation (μ = 500 MeV, M_s^2/μ = 100 MeV, Δ_0 = 25 MeV) is 25.9 MeV
- The gapless bd-gs modes occur at momenta p_1^{bd} = 461 MeV and p_2^{bd} = 512 MeV in the example calculation
- The bu-rs gapless point is at p_{1,2}^{bu} = 469 MeV, with the two gapless momenta separated by only 0.026 MeV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of color-superconducting quark matter phases, specifically the gCFL phase discovered by Alford (2003) and Alford (2004), to observable astrophysical consequences. It contrasts with prior proposals for detecting quark matter in neutron stars such as mass-radius measurements, double pulsar timing, gravitational wave signatures, and r-mode instabilities, none of which provided unambiguous signatures. The paper enables a potential observational test: measuring temperatures of old neutron stars (significantly older than a million years) to look for anomalously warm objects that would indicate gCFL quark matter in their cores.
