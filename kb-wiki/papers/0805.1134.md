# Role of the tetraquark in the chiral phase transition

> We investigate the implications of a tetraquark field on chiral symmetry restoration at nonzero temperature. In order for the chiral phase transition to be cross-over, as shown by lattice QCD studies, a strong mixing between scalar quarkonium and tetraquark fields is required. This leads to a light ($\sim0.4$ GeV), predominantly tetraquark state, and a heavy ($\sim1.2$ GeV), predominantly quarkonium state in the vacuum, in accordance with recently advocated interpretations of spectroscopy data. The mixing even increases with temperature and leads to an interchange of the roles of the originally heavy, predominantly quarkonium state and the originally light, predominantly tetraquark state. Then, as expected, the scalar quarkonium is a light state when becoming degenerate in mass with the pion as chiral symmetry is restored at nonzero temperature.

## Key claim

The chiral phase transition in QCD can be a crossover (as required by lattice QCD) even when the scalar chiral partner of the pion is heavy (~1.2 GeV), provided there is sufficiently strong mixing between the scalar quarkonium and a tetraquark field; this mixing causes the two states to interchange their roles at a temperature \(T_s \leq T_c\), so that the predominantly quarkonium state becomes light and degenerate with the pion at the critical temperature.

## Method

The authors construct a chirally symmetric \(SU(2)_r \times SU(2)_l\) linear sigma model containing a scalar quarkonium field \(\varphi\), a pion triplet \(\vec{\pi}\), and a scalar tetraquark field \(\chi\) that is a singlet under chiral transformations. They compute the temperature-dependent masses, condensates, and mixing angle using the Cornwall-Jackiw-Tomboulis (CJT) formalism in the Hartree-Fock approximation, and map the order of the chiral phase transition as a function of the coupling strength \(g\) and the vacuum masses \(M_H\) and \(M_S\).

## Key numbers

- The vacuum masses are chosen as \(M_H = 0.4\) GeV (predominantly tetraquark, identified with \(f_0(600)\)) and \(M_S = 1.2\) GeV (predominantly quarkonium, identified with \(f_0(1370)\)).
- The pion decay constant is set to \(f_\pi = 92.4\) MeV and the pion mass to \(M_\pi = 0.139\) GeV.
- For the crossover scenario with \(M_H = 0.4\) GeV and \(M_S = 1.2\) GeV, the coupling strength is set to \(g = 3.4\) GeV, yielding \(M_\chi = 0.82\) GeV, \(M_\varphi = 0.96\) GeV, and \(\lambda = 52.85\).
- The temperature at which the two scalar states interchange roles is \(T_s \simeq 160\) MeV, which is below the chiral critical temperature \(T_c\).
- Along the second-order transition line, \(T_c\) decreases from \(\simeq 241\) MeV at \(g = 0\) to \(\simeq 186\) MeV at \(g = 3\) GeV and \(\simeq 173\) MeV at \(g = 4\) GeV; the minimum \(T_c \simeq 145\) MeV occurs at \(g \simeq 2\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the linear sigma model approach to chiral symmetry restoration by introducing a tetraquark field, motivated by the longstanding debate over the nature of light scalar mesons below 1 GeV (Jaffe, 1977; Maiani, 2004) and the interpretation of \(f_0(600)\) as a tetraquark state. It addresses an inconsistency noted in earlier studies (Stefan, 2007) where a heavy quarkonium chiral partner of the pion (above 1 GeV) in a standard sigma model without tetraquarks forces a first-order phase transition, contradicting lattice QCD results (Karsch, 2006; Fodor, 2004) that indicate a crossover. The results enable a unified description of vacuum spectroscopy (where \(f_0(1370)\) is the predominantly quarkonium state) and finite-temperature behavior, and motivate extending the model to include the full scalar nonet, the glueball, and (axial-)vector mesons for a more realistic phenomenology.
