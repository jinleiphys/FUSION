# $R$-mode Stability of GW190814's Secondary Component as a Supermassive and Superfast Pulsar

> The nature of GW190814's secondary component $m_2$ of mass $(2.50-2.67)\,\text{M}_{\odot}$ in the mass gap between the currently known maximum mass of neutron stars and the minimum mass of black holes is currently under hot debate. Among the many possibilities proposed in the literature, the $m_2$ was suggested as a superfast pulsar while its r-mode stability against the run-away gravitational radiation through the Chandrasekhar-Friedman-Schutz mechanism is still unknown. Using those fulfilling all currently known astrophysical and nuclear physics constraints among a sample of 33 unified equation of states (EOSs) constructed previously by Fortin {\it et al.} (2016) using the same nuclear interactions from the crust to the core consistently, we compare the minimum frequency required for the $m_2$ to rotationally sustain a mass higher than $2.50\,\text{M}_{\odot}$ with the critical frequency above which the r-mode instability occurs. We use two extreme damping models assuming the crust is either perfectly rigid or elastic. Using the stability of 19 observed low-mass x-ray binaries as an indication that the rigid crust damping of the r-mode dominates within the models studied, we find that the $m_2$ is r-mode stable while rotating with a frequency higher than 870.2 Hz (0.744 times its Kepler frequency of 1169.6 Hz) as long as its temperate is lower than about $3.9\times 10^7 K$, further supporting the proposal that GW190814's secondary component is a supermassive and superfast pulsar.

## Key claim

The secondary component of GW190814 (mass 2.50–2.67 M☉) can be r-mode stable as a superfast pulsar, but only if its internal temperature is below about 3.9 × 10⁷ K; the stability holds for rotation frequencies above 870.2 Hz (0.744 times its Kepler frequency of 1169.6 Hz) under the assumption that a rigid crust provides the dominant damping mechanism.

## Method

The authors selected 6 unified equations of state (EOSs) from the 33 constructed by Fortin et al. (2016) that satisfy constraints on the symmetry energy slope L and curvature Ksym from nuclear experiments and astrophysical observations, the maximum mass of PSR J0740+6620 (2.14 M☉), and NICER mass-radius measurements of PSR J0030+0451. They computed the minimum rotation frequency needed for a 2.50 M☉ star (the lower mass bound of GW190814’s secondary) using the RNS code, and compared it with the critical frequency for r-mode instability under two extreme crust damping models (perfectly rigid or perfectly elastic). The stability of 19 observed low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs) was used to infer that the rigid crust damping is the physically relevant case.

## Key numbers

- The secondary component is r-mode stable while rotating at frequencies higher than 870.2 Hz (0.744 times its Kepler frequency of 1169.6 Hz) for the DDME2 EOS, provided its temperature is below 3.9 × 10⁷ K.
- For the 5 EOSs that can support a 2.50 M☉ star, the minimum rotation frequency ranges from 0.744 νK (DDME2, νK = 1169.6 Hz) to 0.855 νK (SLy9, νK = 1515.4 Hz).
- The corresponding maximum temperature for r-mode stability ranges from 7.5 × 10⁶ K (SLy9) to 3.9 × 10⁷ K (DDME2).
- For a typical LMXB temperature of 10⁸ K, the critical frequency νcrit is lifted from about 0.16 νK (elastic crust) to about 0.79 νK (rigid crust) for a 1.4 M☉ neutron star.
- The Kepler frequency of the DDME2 EOS is 1169.6 Hz; the minimum frequency to support 2.50 M☉ with that EOS is 870.2 Hz.

## Context

This work extends the debate on the nature of GW190814’s secondary component, previously proposed as a superfast pulsar by several groups (e.g., Most et al. 2020; Zhang & Li 2020; Biswas et al. 2020), by testing its r-mode stability using the Chandrasekhar-Friedman-Schutz mechanism. It uses the unified EOSs of Fortin et al. (2016) and the r-mode formalism of Lindblom et al. (1998, 2000), and finds that the rigid crust damping favored by LMXB observations (e.g., Haskell et al. 2011, 2012) allows the secondary to be stable at the required high spin rates. The results enable future studies of the formation and merger dynamics of GW190814, and suggest that the temperature limit (about 3.9 × 10⁷ K) is an order of magnitude higher than that of known old neutron stars (e.g., Wang et al. 2019), so the r-mode instability is not a barrier to the supermassive, superfast pulsar interpretation.
