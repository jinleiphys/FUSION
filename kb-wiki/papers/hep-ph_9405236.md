# QCD and Intrinsic Heavy Quark Predictions for Leading Charm and Beauty Hadroproduction

> Recent experiments at Fermilab and CERN have observed a strong asymmetry between the hadroproduction cross sections of leading $D$ mesons, containing projectile valence quarks, and nonleading charmed mesons, without projectile valence quarks. The observed correlations of the $π^{\pm} N \to D^\pm X$ cross section with the projectile charge violates the usual assumption that heavy quark jet fragmentation factorizes. We examine the asymmetry between leading and nonleading charm production as a function of $x_f$ and $p_T^2$ assuming a two-component model combining leading-twist fusion subprocesses and charm production from intrinsic heavy quark Fock states. We predict a sizable asymmetry at low $p_T^2$ and high $x_f$ from coalescence of the charm quarks with the comoving spectator quarks of the projectile. An intrinsic $c \overline c$ production cross section of 0.5 $μ$b is sufficient to explain both the magnitude and kinematic dependence of the asymmetry. In contrast, the charm jet hadronization mechanisms contained in PYTHIA predict a sizeable leading charm asymmetry even at low $x_F.$ The two-component model is extended to predict the asymmetry in $B$ meson production in proton-proton and pion-proton interactions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the observed asymmetry between leading and nonleading charmed mesons in hadroproduction can be explained by a two-component model combining leading-twist parton fusion with intrinsic heavy quark Fock states, where an intrinsic charm production cross section of 0.5 μb is sufficient to explain both the magnitude and kinematic dependence of the asymmetry, but only if the asymmetry is localized at low pT² and high xf.

## Method

The authors calculate charm hadroproduction cross sections using a two-component model: leading-twist parton fusion (gluon-gluon and quark-antiquark fusion) with either delta-function or Peterson fragmentation, combined with intrinsic charm production from four-particle Fock states (e.g., |ū d c c̄⟩ for π⁻ projectiles). The intrinsic charm component includes both fragmentation and valence quark coalescence mechanisms, where the coalescence contribution is controlled by a parameter ξ (set to 1/2 for most calculations). The model is extended to beauty hadroproduction using the same formalism with appropriate mass scaling.

## Key numbers

- Intrinsic charm pair production cross section: 0.5 μb, sufficient to explain the observed asymmetry magnitude and kinematic dependence
- Intrinsic charm cross section for a single charmed hadron with xf > 0: approximately 1 μb
- Intrinsic charm probability Pic = 0.31%, based on Hoffmann and Moore calculations compared to EMC muoproduction data
- Measured asymmetry A(xf) increases from approximately 0 for xf near zero to approximately 0.5 around xf = 0.65 (WA82 and E769 data)
- Measured asymmetry A(pT²) integrated over all xf is consistent with zero in the range 0 < pT² < 10 GeV² (E769)
- Intrinsic beauty cross section predicted to be on the order of 2-3 nb, similar to the fusion cross section at the energies investigated
- Charmed quark mass mc = 1.5 GeV; effective transverse mass for charm quarks m̂c = 1.8 GeV; effective transverse mass for light quarks m̂q = 0.45 GeV; effective transverse mass for beauty quarks m̂b = 4.6 GeV
- Hadron scale parameter μ² ≈ 0.2 GeV², fixed from the NA3 ratio of 0.18 at 200 GeV

## Context

This work extends the intrinsic charm model developed by Brodsky, Hoyer, Mueller, and Tang (1990) and the authors' previous work (Vogt and Brodsky, 1993) to explain leading charm asymmetries observed by the WA82 and E769 collaborations. The model contrasts with PYTHIA (based on the Lund string fragmentation model), which predicts a sizeable leading charm asymmetry even at low xf and a nonzero A(pT²) ≈ 0.3, overestimating the observed effects. The two-component model enables predictions for beauty hadroproduction asymmetries that could be tested by future fixed-target experiments, and suggests that the asymmetry will decrease at higher energies as the fusion cross section increases relative to the intrinsic charm contribution.
