# Relativistic Boltzmann transport approach with Bose-Einstein statistics and the onset of gluon condensation

> We study the evolution of a gluon system under conditions of density and temperature similar to those explored in the early stage of ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. We first describe the implementation of Relativistic Boltzmann-Nordheim (RBN) transport approach that includes in the collision integral the quantum effects of Bose-Einstein Statistics. Then, we describe the evolution of a spatially uniform gluon system in a box under elastic collisions solving the RBN for various initial conditions. We discuss the critical phase-space density that leads to the onset of a Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC) and the time scale for this process to occur. In particular, thanks to the fact that RBN allows to relax the small angle approximation, we study the effect at both small and large screening mass $ m_{D} $. For small $ m_{D}\ll T $ we see that our solution of RBN is in agreement with the recent extensive studies within a Fokker-Planck scheme in small angle approximation. For the same total cross section but with large $ m_{D}\simeq 2\, T $ (large angle scatterings), we see a significant time speed-up of the onset of BEC respect to small $m_{D}\ll T$. This further strengthen the possibility that at least a transient BEC is formed in the early stage of ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

# Relativistic Boltzmann transport approach with Bose-Einstein statistics and the onset of gluon condensation

## Key claim

The authors show that when the screening mass is large (m_D approximately 2 T), corresponding to large-angle scatterings relevant for ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, the onset of Bose-Einstein condensation occurs significantly faster than predicted by Fokker-Planck approaches that assume small-angle scattering, strengthening the possibility that at least a transient gluon condensate forms in the early stage of such collisions.

## Method

The authors implement a Relativistic Boltzmann-Nordheim (RBN) transport approach that includes Bose-Einstein statistics via (1+f) enhancement factors in the collision integral, solved numerically using a stochastic test-particle method in a static cubic box of volume 27 fm^3. They initialize the gluon system with a color-glass condensate-inspired step-function momentum distribution f(p) = f_0 for p below a saturation scale Q_s = 1 GeV, with an exponential tail for p > Q_s, and evolve the system under elastic 2-to-2 collisions using a differential cross section dσ/dt proportional to 9π α_s^2 / (t - m_D^2)^2 for two values of the screening mass: m_D = 0.1 GeV (forward-peaked, small-angle regime) and m_D = 1 GeV (large-angle regime).

## Key numbers

- Critical value of the initial occupation number for onset of Bose-Einstein condensation: f_0^{cr} = 0.154 for a pure step function, and f_0^{cr} = 0.1675 for the smoothed initial distribution used in simulations.
- For the underpopulated case (f_0 = 0.1) with m_D = 0.1 GeV, thermalization time τ_therm ≈ 15 fm/c; with m_D = 1 GeV and the same total cross section, τ_therm ≈ 3 fm/c.
- Equilibrium temperature for the underpopulated case (f_0 = 0.1): T_eq = 0.354 GeV, with equilibrium chemical potential |μ*| ≈ 0.11 GeV.
- For the overpopulated case (f_0 = 0.2) with m_D = 1 GeV, the effective temperature reaches T* ≈ 0.280 GeV at equilibrium.
- The collision rate per particle at initial time for f_0 = 0.15 is approximately Γ_BE = Γ_Boltz (1+f_0)^2, with Γ_Boltz = ρ^2 σ/2.

## Context

This work extends the Fokker-Planck studies of Blaizot (2013) on gluon condensation in static media by solving the full Boltzmann equation without the small-angle approximation. The authors find that for large screening masses (m_D ≈ 2 T), relevant for the temperatures typical of ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, the condensation onset is significantly faster than predicted by the Fokker-Planck approach, which is strictly valid only for m_D ≪ T. This result supports the possibility of transient Bose-Einstein condensation in the early stage of heavy-ion collisions as suggested by Color Glass Condensate theory (McLerran, 1993; Gelis, 2010) and enables more realistic modeling of the pre-thermalization dynamics in such systems.
