# The Fate of the Compact Remnant in Neutron Star Mergers

> Neutron star (binary neutron star and neutron star - black hole) mergers are believed to produce short-duration gamma-ray bursts. They are also believed to be the dominant source of gravitational waves to be detected by the advanced LIGO and the dominant source of the heavy r-process elements in the universe. Whether or not these mergers produce short-duration GRBs depends sensitively on the fate of the core of the remnant (whether, and how quickly, it forms a black hole). In this paper, we combine the results of merger calculations and equation of state studies to determine the fate of the cores of neutron star mergers. Using population studies, we can determine the distribution of these fates to compare to observations. We find that black hole cores form quickly only for equations of state that predict maximum non-rotating neutron star masses below 2.3-2.4 solar masses. If quick black hole formation is essential in producing gamma-ray bursts, LIGO observed rates compared to GRB rates could be used to constrain the equation of state for dense nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The paper finds that the fate of the merged core in neutron star mergers depends sensitively on the nuclear equation of state: black hole cores form quickly only for equations of state that predict maximum non-rotating neutron star masses below 2.3-2.4 solar masses, with the caveat that this conclusion assumes quick black hole formation is essential for producing gamma-ray bursts.

## Method

The authors combined smooth particle hydrodynamics simulations of neutron star mergers from Korobkin (2012) with nuclear equation of state models (NL3, FSU2.1, and a parameterized model from Steiner 2010-2015) to determine the maximum stable mass of merged cores as a function of angular momentum and thermal energy. They then used the StarTrack population synthesis code to generate populations of binary neutron star systems, applying different initial orbital conditions, metallicities, and neutron star mass prescriptions, and mapped the merger outcomes onto four possible fates: direct collapse to black hole, collapse during disk accretion, collapse after spin-down, or stable neutron star.

## Key numbers

* For the FSU2.1 equation of state, the maximum neutron star mass increases nearly linearly with rotational support: M_max,NS = (2.1 + J/(10^50 g cm^2 s^-1)) M_sun
* For a 2.0 M_sun non-rotating maximum mass, a maximally-rotating neutron star lies between 2.35-2.5 M_sun depending on the equation of state
* For M_0 = 2.0, 2.5, 2.7 M_sun, the fitted parameters for rotating maximum mass are M_1 = 0.0219, 0.0354, 0.0574 and beta = 1.75, 1.5, 1.6 respectively
* The fraction of systems that collapse to a black hole is above 90% for any equation of state with maximum non-rotating mass below approximately 2.3-2.4 M_sun
* Generated NS-NS merger rates from population synthesis are approximately 10-40 Myr^-1, in agreement with empirically estimated Galactic rates of 10-100 Myr^-1 (Kim 2015)
* Only 0.3% of population synthesis systems have a neutron star component with mass above 1.8 M_sun

## Context

This work extends previous merger simulations by Korobkin (2012) and equation of state studies by Steiner (2010, 2013, 2015) to determine the distribution of merger fates across astrophysically realistic populations. The results enable using observed rates of short-duration gamma-ray bursts compared to gravitational wave detections from advanced LIGO to constrain the nuclear equation of state, building on the framework that neutron star mergers are the leading progenitors for short GRBs (Fryer 1999, Popham 1999, Lee 2007, Rosswog 2015). The paper also addresses the baryon contamination problem raised by Dessart (2009) and Perego (2014), where neutrino-driven winds from long-lived neutron star remnants could choke GRB jets.
