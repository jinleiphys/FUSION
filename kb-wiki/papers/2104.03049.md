# Single-nucleon removal cross sections on nucleon and nuclear targets

> The eikonal direct-reaction model, as used in spectroscopic studies of intermediate-energy nucleon-removal reactions on light target nuclei, is considered in the case of a proton target and applied to neutron removal from $^{29}$Ne at 240 MeV/nucleon. The computed cross sections and their sensitivities are compared using an earlier detailed analysis of carbon target data. The nuclear structure input, for the $^{29}$Ne ground-state and $^{28}$Ne final states, is that deduced from the carbon target analysis. The comparisons quantify the sensitivity of the two reactions to the angular momenta and binding energies of the active valence orbitals - showing the carbon target to be relatively more efficient for removals from weakly-bound, low-$\ell$, halo-like orbitals. Probing this sensitivity experimentally would provide useful tests of these predictions and of the model's description of the reaction mechanism.

# Single-nucleon removal cross sections on nucleon and nuclear targets

## Key claim
The eikonal direct-reaction model predicts that proton-target single-nucleon removal reactions are significantly less effective than carbon-target reactions at removing neutrons from weakly-bound, low-ℓ, halo-like orbitals; the carbon target is relatively more efficient for such removals, with the ratio of proton-target to carbon-target cross sections for p-wave removal being 0.36 compared to 0.65 for d- and f-wave orbitals.

## Method
The author performed eikonal-model calculations for neutron removal from ²⁹Ne at 240 MeV/nucleon on a proton target, using the elastic breakup (diffraction dissociation) mechanism as the dominant contribution. The nucleon-nucleon S-matrix was parameterized using total cross sections from the Charagi-Gupta parameterization (σ_np = 37.14 mb, σ_pp = 22.02 mb) and real-to-imaginary ratios α from the Ray tabulation (α_np = 0.533, α_pp = 0.991), with range parameters fixed by requiring equality of total and elastic cross sections. The residue-proton optical potential was computed in the tρ folding approximation using Skyrme Hartree-Fock (SkX interaction) densities, and bound-state overlaps were generated with Woods-Saxon potentials constrained by the same HF calculations. Spectroscopic factors from the sdpf-m shell-model interaction were used, matching those from the earlier carbon-target analysis of Kobayashi et al.

## Key numbers
- Inclusive neutron-removal cross section on proton target: 32.3 mb; on carbon target: 69.0 mb (ratio 0.47)
- Ground-state to ground-state cross section on proton target: 10.3 mb; on carbon target: 31.6 mb (ratio 0.33)
- NN total cross sections at 240 MeV: σ_pp = 22.02 mb, σ_np = 37.14 mb
- α_np = 0.533, α_pp = 0.991 from Ray tabulation
- ²⁹Ne neutron separation energy: 963 keV (with evaluated value 971(196) keV)
- Ground-state cross section with S_n = 971 keV: 10.30^{+0.29}_{-0.25} mb; excited-states inclusive: 22.0^{+0.19}_{-0.18} mb
- Percentage contributions to inclusive cross section: proton target 51% s+p : 49% d+f; carbon target 65% s+p : 35% d+f
- Ground-state to excited-states ratio: proton target 32:68%; carbon target 46:54% (experimental carbon ratio 49(9):51%)
- Single-particle cross sections for 2p_3/2 ground-state removal: 21.19 mb (α_np=0.533) vs 20.67 mb (α_np=0.0), showing weak parameter sensitivity

## Context
This work extends the eikonal direct-reaction model, previously applied to light nuclear targets like carbon and beryllium (Tostevin 2001, Hansen and Tostevin 2003), to the case of a proton target for intermediate-energy nucleon-removal reactions. It builds directly on the carbon-target analysis of ²⁹Ne by Kobayashi et al., which established the 3/2⁻ ground-state assignment and p-wave halo character of ²⁹Ne. The calculations enable quantitative comparison of the sensitivity of proton and carbon targets to the angular momentum and binding energy of valence orbitals, and suggest that measurements of cross-section ratios between the two targets could help identify halo-like states. The work also notes that alternative approaches such as continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) calculations by Kondo et al. and distorted-waves impulse approximation (DWIA) models have been applied to similar reactions, but the eikonal model offers computational efficiency for systematic comparisons.
