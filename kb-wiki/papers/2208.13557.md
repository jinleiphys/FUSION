# Controlled Gate Networks: Theory and Application to Eigenvalue Estimation

> We introduce a new scheme for quantum circuit design called controlled gate networks. Rather than trying to reduce the complexity of individual unitary operations, the new strategy is to toggle between all of the unitary operations needed with the fewest number of gates. We present the general theory of controlled gate networks and show that, under quite general conditions, it can significantly reduce the number of two-qubit gates needed to produce linear combinations of unitary operators. The first example we consider is a variational subspace calculation for a two-qubit system. The second example is estimating the eigenvalues of a two-qubit Hamiltonian via the rodeo algorithm using operators that we call controlled reversal gates. We use the Quantinuum H1-2 and IBM Perth devices to realize the quantum circuits. The third example is the application of controlled gate networks to the controlled time evolution of a free nucleon on a three-dimensional lattice. For all of the examples, we show very substantial reductions in the number of two-qubit gates required. Our work demonstrates that controlled gate networks are a useful tool for reducing gate complexity in quantum algorithms for quantum many-body problems such as those relevant to nuclear physics.

## Key claim
Controlled gate networks reduce the number of two-qubit (CNOT) gates needed to produce linear combinations of unitary operators by exploiting structural similarities between the operators, rather than minimizing the complexity of each individual unitary. The reduction is at least one CNOT gate per decomposition step when the controlled transformation gate is no more complex than the controlled target operator, and the total reduction scales with the number of ancilla qubits.

## Method
The authors decompose two unitary operators U and V into products of K simpler operators U_k and V_k, then find transformation gates A_k and B_k such that A_k U_k B_k = V_k. By controlling only the transformation gates with an ancilla qubit (rather than controlling U_k and V_k independently), the circuit produces arbitrary linear combinations of U and V. The method generalizes to N = 2^n unitary operators using n ancilla qubits, where the number of removed controlling operators is N-1. The authors demonstrate the technique on three examples: a variational subspace calculation for a two-qubit Heisenberg model, eigenvalue estimation via the rodeo algorithm on Quantinuum H1-2 and IBM Perth devices, and controlled time evolution of a free nucleon on a three-dimensional lattice.

## Key numbers
- For the two-qubit variational subspace calculation using the controlled gate network approach: 13 CNOT gates and 21 single-qubit gates required after transpilation to IBM native gates.
- For the standard Hadamard test approach to the same variational subspace calculation: 64 CNOT gates and 82 single-qubit gates required after transpilation.
- The controlled gate network reduces the gate count by a factor of approximately 5 for CNOT gates and approximately 4 for single-qubit gates compared to the standard approach in this example.
- For a system with N = 2^n unitary operators and n ancilla qubits, the reduction in CNOT gates at each step k is at least 2^n - 1 = N - 1 when the analogous conditions hold.

## Context
This work introduces a new circuit design strategy that complements existing local optimization methods such as gate fusion, gate cancellation, and gate commutation used in transpiling software (Chen 2021, Iten 2022). The controlled gate network approach is applicable to quantum algorithms that use controlled operator structures, including linear combinations of unitary operators for Hamiltonian simulation (Berry 2014, Low 2019), phase estimation (Kitaev 1995), imaginary time evolution (Motta 2020), and variational subspace methods (Parrish 2019, Mejuto-Zaera 2023). The variational subspace example builds on the QAOA framework (Farhi 2014) and symmetry restoration techniques widely used in nuclear physics (Satula 2009, Yao 2019, Sheikh 2019).
