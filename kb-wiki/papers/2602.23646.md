# Realistic Equations of State Informing Neutron Star Post-Merger Gravitational-Wave Frequencies

> Binary neutron star mergers are thought to produce hot, rapidly rotating neutron stars with masses that can far exceed their Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff mass. The gravitational-wave emission from such remnants provides a unique opportunity to measure the nuclear equation of state at densities and temperatures not available to terrestrial experiments. Current detector design is informed by gravitational-wave signals from general relativistic hydrodynamics simulations of neutron star mergers, typically with hybrid thermal treatments for the equation of state, where a cold equation of state is modified by adding a thermal component. We use realistic equations of state based on the relativistic mean field model with consistent treatment of thermal effects to compute the distribution of expected peak gravitational-wave frequencies. Marginalising over equation of state and progenitor neutron star masses, we show the peak frequency of emission ranges from $\sim2.5$ to 4 kHz. The width of this distribution suggests the need for broadband observatories with kHz sensitivity, and calls into question some of the so-called post-merger optimised configurations. We show the proposed KAGRA high-frequency design is well-suited to measuring post-merger remnants when compared to the KAGRA broadband design.

## Key claim
Using realistic finite-temperature equations of state based on the relativistic mean field model, the peak gravitational-wave frequency from binary neutron star post-merger remnants ranges from approximately 2.5 to 4 kHz when marginalising over equation of state and progenitor masses, with the inclusion of thermal effects systematically lowering the median frequency by 200–600 Hz compared to cold treatments.

## Method
The authors constructed approximately 3300 nucleonic equations of state using a non-linear relativistic mean field model, constrained by chiral effective field theory at low densities and astrophysical observations at high densities. They computed the fundamental quadrupolar f-mode frequency for cold (T=0), warm (S/A=1), and hot (S/A=2) thermodynamic configurations by solving the TOV equations for non-rotating models, then applying the Doneva (2013) relations to approximate Keplerian rotation and converting from the co-rotating to inertial frame. They injected exponentially-damped sinusoid signals into KAGRA broadband and high-frequency detector noise curves to compute normalised signal-to-noise ratios.

## Key numbers
- Peak frequency distribution median and 90% credible intervals: cold (T=0) = 3627 +581/-389 Hz; warm (S/A=1) = 3476 +591/-397 Hz; hot (S/A=2) = 3039 +633/-424 Hz.
- The 3 kHz configuration of KAGRA HF offers on average a signal-to-noise ratio increase of approximately 2.5 times the broadband configuration across all equation of state sets.
- The 2 kHz configuration of KAGRA HF offers on average a signal-to-noise ratio improvement of 1.8–2 times the broadband configuration.
- The peak frequency of emission ranges from approximately 2.5 to 4 kHz when marginalising over equation of state and progenitor neutron star masses.

## Context
This work extends prior studies that used hybrid thermal treatments (cold equation of state plus an ideal gas gamma-law component) for post-merger gravitational-wave predictions, such as those by Bauswein and Baumgarte (2013) and Takami (2015). It builds on the relativistic mean field framework of Chen and Piekarewicz (2014) and the Bayesian constraints from Ghosh and Chatterjee (2022), and directly uses the finite-temperature formalism of Barman (2025) and Barman and Chatterjee (2025). The results enable quantitative comparison of proposed detector configurations, specifically the KAGRA high-frequency design versus broadband designs, and call into question some post-merger optimised narrowband configurations due to the width of the predicted frequency distribution.
