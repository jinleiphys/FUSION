# The Neutron Mean Life and Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

> We explore the effect of neutron lifetime and its uncertainty on standard big-bang nucleosynthesis (BBN). BBN describes the cosmic production of the light nuclides $^1{\rm H}$, ${\rm D}$, $^3{\rm H}$+$^3{\rm He}$, $^4{\rm He}$, and $^7{\rm Li}$+$^7{\rm Be}$ in the first minutes of cosmic time. The neutron mean life $τ_n$ has two roles in modern BBN calculations: (1) it normalizes the matrix element for weak $n \leftrightarrow p$ interconversions, and (2) it sets the rate of free neutron decay after the weak interactions freeze out. We review the history of the interplay between $τ_n$ measurements and BBN, and present a study of the sensitivity of the light element abundances to the modern neutron lifetime measurements. We find that $τ_n$ uncertainties dominate the predicted $^4{\rm He}$ error budget, but these theory errors remain smaller than the uncertainties in $^4{\rm He}$ observations, even with the dispersion in recent neutron lifetime measurements. For the other light-element predictions, $τ_n$ contributes negligibly to their error budget. Turning the problem around, we combine present BBN and cosmic microwave background (CMB) determinations of the cosmic baryon density to $\textit{predict}$ a "cosmologically preferred" mean life of $τ_{n}({\rm BBN+CMB}) = 870 \pm 16 \ \rm sec$, which is consistent with experimental mean life determinations. We go on to show that if future astronomical and cosmological helium observations can reach an uncertainty of $σ_{\rm obs}(Y_p) = 0.001$ in the $^4{\rm He}$ mass fraction $Y_p$, this could begin to discriminate between the mean life determinations.

## Key claim

The neutron lifetime uncertainty dominates the predicted helium-4 error budget in standard BBN, but these theory errors remain smaller than the uncertainties in helium-4 observations, even when accounting for the dispersion between recent neutron lifetime measurements (bottle vs. trap methods) that differ by about 5 seconds.

## Method

The authors performed standard BBN calculations assuming Lambda-CDM cosmology with three neutrino flavors, using the baryon density from Planck CMB data and the current PDG neutron mean life of 878.4 ± 0.5 s. They constructed likelihood functions combining CMB data, BBN predictions, and neutron lifetime measurements, then compared these with observational abundance data for helium-4, deuterium, helium-3, and lithium-7. As a test of systematic effects, they also sampled neutron lifetimes from the PDG ideogram distribution rather than a simple Gaussian.

## Key numbers

- Current PDG neutron mean life: τ_n = 878.4 ± 0.5 s (based on eight measurements, scale factor 1.8)
- BBN+CMB predicted helium-4 mass fraction: Y_p = 0.2467 ± 0.0002 (Gaussian τ_n) or 0.2469 ± 0.0004 (ideogram τ_n)
- BBN+CMB predicted deuterium abundance: D/H = (2.506 ± 0.083) × 10^{-5}
- BBN+CMB predicted lithium-7 abundance: Li/H = (4.96 ± 0.70) × 10^{-10}
- Observed primordial helium-4 mass fraction: Y_{p,obs} = 0.2448 ± 0.0033
- Observed primordial deuterium abundance: (D/H)_{obs} = (2.55 ± 0.03) × 10^{-5}
- Cosmologically preferred neutron mean life from BBN+CMB: τ_n(BBN+CMB) = 870 ± 16 s (with current Y_p uncertainty)
- With improved Y_p uncertainty of σ_obs(Y_p) = 0.001, the BBN+CMB prediction tightens to τ_n = 869.8 ± 4.8 s
- Helium-4 sensitivity relation: ΔY_p/Y_p ≈ 0.730 Δτ_n/τ_n
- A 5 s systematic shift in τ_n gives ΔY_p = 0.0010; a 10 s shift gives ΔY_p = 0.0021

## Context

This work extends the long history of interplay between neutron lifetime measurements and BBN calculations, updating earlier analyses by Fields et al. (2020) and Yeh et al. (2021) with the latest PDG average and the current discrepancy between bottle and trap measurements (about 5 s) and the in-beam measurement by Yue et al. (2013) (about 10 s higher). The paper shows that while τ_n uncertainty dominated BBN predictions in the 1980s (when τ_n ranged from 877 to 937 s), current precision makes τ_n a minor contributor to error budgets for all elements except helium-4. The authors demonstrate that future improvements in astronomical helium observations to σ(Y_p) = 0.001 could begin to discriminate between competing neutron lifetime determinations, and that BBN+CMB can provide an independent cross-check on τ_n measurements.
