# Color-flavor locked strange matter and strangelets at finite temperature

> It is possible that a system composed of up, down and strange quarks consists the true ground state of nuclear matter at high densities and low temperatures. This exotic plasma, called strange quark matter (SQM), seems to be even more favorable energetically if quarks are in a superconducting state, the so-called color-flavor locked state. Here are presented calculations made on the basis of the MIT bag model considering the influence of finite temperature on the allowed parameters characterizing the system for stability of bulk SQM (the so-called stability windows) and also for strangelets, small lumps of SQM, both in the color-flavor locking scenario. We compare these results with the unpaired SQM and also briefly discuss some astrophysical implications of them. Also, the issue of strangelet's electric charge is discussed. The effects of dynamical screening, though important for non-paired SQM strangelets, are not relevant when considering pairing among all three flavor and colors of quarks.

# Color-flavor locked strange matter and strangelets at finite temperature

## Key claim

Color-flavor locked (CFL) strange quark matter at finite temperature remains more stable than unpaired strange quark matter even when the temperature approaches the critical temperature for quark pairing, but finite temperature always destabilizes the system relative to the zero-temperature case, with the temperature-dependent gap parameter making the system slightly less stable than the constant-gap approximation.

## Method

The authors use the MIT bag model with zero strong coupling constant to compute thermodynamic potentials for bulk CFL strange quark matter and for spherical strangelets (finite-size lumps), incorporating surface and curvature terms via the multiple reflection expansion. They calculate stability windows in the plane of strange quark mass versus bag constant for temperatures up to 30 MeV, comparing cases with a constant pairing gap Δ₀ and with a temperature-dependent gap Δ(T) that vanishes at a critical temperature T_c = 0.57 × 2^(1/3) Δ₀. The mechanical equilibrium condition B = −∂Ω/∂V is imposed for strangelets, and the electric charge is determined by finite-size effects only since Debye screening is absent in the CFL phase.

## Key numbers

- Minimum bag constant for stability: B^(1/4) = 145 MeV (below this value, spontaneous decay to non-strange matter occurs)
- Critical temperature for pairing: T_c = 0.57 × 2^(1/3) Δ₀ (e.g., for Δ₀ = 100 MeV, T_c ≈ 72 MeV)
- Surface energy fit for Δ₀ = 100 MeV, B^(1/4) = 145 MeV, m_s = 150 MeV: σ_CFL(T,A) = (81.09 + 0.013 T − 0.026 T²) × (0.96 + 0.17 e^(−A/22.5) + 0.053 e^(−A/384.2)) in MeV
- Curvature energy fit for same parameters: C_CFL(T,A) = (163.85 + 0.003 T − 0.093 T²) × (0.98 + 0.082 e^(−A/23.2) + 0.026 e^(−A/393.9)) in MeV
- For Δ = 100 MeV, the chemical potential for the strange quark in CFL strangelets is very close to the common chemical potential for unpaired strangelets at the same temperature, B, and m_s, but the light quark chemical potential is much lower
- Surface energy shows a maximum at m_s ≈ 150 MeV and goes to zero for massless quarks
- For Δ ≲ 20 MeV, the critical temperature falls below 15 MeV; for Δ ≲ 40 MeV, it falls below 30 MeV

## Context

This work extends the zero-temperature CFL strangelet calculations of Madsen (2001) and Peng (2000) to finite temperature, and complements the finite-temperature unpaired strangelet analysis of He et al. (2000) and Madsen (1998). The results show that CFL strangelets remain stable at temperatures up to ~30 MeV for Δ = 100 MeV, which suggests that the conversion from neutron stars to strange stars could occur immediately after formation, potentially skipping the neutron star stage entirely. The finding that curvature energy is always lower for CFL strangelets than for unpaired strange matter may influence fragmentation processes of bulk CFL matter, relevant for cosmic ray strangelet searches and heavy ion collision experiments.
