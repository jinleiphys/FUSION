# Impact of The Newly Revised Gravitational Redshift of X-ray Burster GS 1826-24 on The Equation of State of Supradense Neutron-Rich Matter

> Thanks to the recent advancement in producing rare isotopes and measuring their masses with unprecedented precision, the updated nuclear masses around the waiting-point nucleus $^{64}$Ge in the rapid-proton capture process have led to a significant revision of the surface gravitational redshift of the neutron star (NS) in GS 1826-24 by re-fitting its X-ray burst light curve ({\it X. Zhou et al., Nature Physics {\bf 19}, 1091 (2023)}) using Modules for Experiments in Stellar Astrophysics (MESA). The resulting NS compactness $ξ$ is between 0.244 and 0.342 at 95\% confidence level and its upper boundary is significantly smaller than the maximum $ξ$ previously known. Incorporating this new data within a comprehensive Bayesian statistical framework, we investigate its impact on the Equation of State (EOS) of supradense neutron-rich matter and the required spin frequency for GW190814's minor $m_2$ with mass $2.59\pm 0.05$M$_{\odot}$ to be a rotationally stable pulsar. We found that the EOS of high-density symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) has to be softened significantly while the symmetry energy at supersaturation densities stiffened compared to our prior knowledge from earlier analyses using data from both astrophysical observations and terrestrial nuclear experiments. In particular, the skewness $J_0$ characterizing the stiffness of high-density SNM decreases significantly, while the slope $L$, curvature $K_{\rm{sym}}$, and skewness $J_{\rm{sym}}$ of nuclear symmetry energy all increase appreciably compared to their fiducial values. We also found that the most probable spin rate for the $m_2$ to be a stable pulsar is very close to its mass-shedding limit once the revised redshift data from GS 1826-24 is considered, making the $m_2$ unlikely the most massive NS observed so far.

## Key claim

Incorporating the newly revised gravitational redshift of the neutron star in GS 1826-24 (which yields a compactness between 0.183 and 0.259 at 95% confidence, with an upper boundary significantly smaller than previously known) forces the equation of state of symmetric nuclear matter to soften and the symmetry energy at supersaturation densities to stiffen compared to prior knowledge from earlier analyses of astrophysical observations and terrestrial nuclear experiments.

## Method

The authors performed a Bayesian statistical inference using a meta-model nuclear equation of state parameterized by six parameters (incompressibility K0 and skewness J0 for symmetric nuclear matter; magnitude Esym, slope L, curvature Ksym, and skewness Jsym of the symmetry energy) plus a spin parameter. They constructed neutron star mass-radius sequences by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations, then computed likelihood functions from six radius observations (from GW170817, quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries, PSR J0030+0451, and PSR J0740+6620), two redshift distributions extracted from the GS 1826-24 light-curve reanalysis by Zhou et al. (2023), and the mass of GW190814's minor component m2.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star compactness ξ from GS 1826-24: between 0.183 and 0.259 at 95% confidence level (upper boundary significantly smaller than previously known maximum)
- Gravitational redshift z of GS 1826-24: 0.29 ± 0.04 (68% confidence) from the 95% contour; also a narrower 68% contour at z = 0.25 ± 0.0065 and a broader one at z = 0.29 ± 0.034
- Mass of GW190814's minor component m2: 2.59 ± 0.05 M⊙ (68% confidence)
- Most probable maximum mass of static neutron stars (MTOV) from earlier Bayesian analysis: 2.14 M⊙ with corresponding radius Rmax obtained using L = 58.7 MeV, Ksym = -100 MeV, Jsym = 800 MeV, and J0 = -190 MeV
- Prior ranges for EOS parameters (in MeV): K0 = 220-260, J0 = -800 to 400, Ksym = -400 to 100, Jsym = -200 to 800, L = 30-90, Esym(ρ0) = 28.5-34.9
- Radius constraints used: 1.4 M⊙ NS radius 11.9+1.4-1.4 km (90% CFL, GW170817), 10.8+2.1-1.6 km (90% CFL, GW170817), 11.7+1.1-1.1 km (90% CFL, quiescent LMXBs); PSR J0030+0451 radius 12.71+1.14-1.19 km (68% CFL); PSR J0740+6620 radius 13.7+2.6-1.5 km (68% CFL)

## Context

This work extends the reanalysis of the GS 1826-24 X-ray burst light curve by Zhou et al. (2023, Nature Physics 19, 1091), who used precision mass measurements of nuclei around the waiting-point 64Ge from the Lanzhou rare isotope facility to revise the neutron star's gravitational redshift. The authors compare their results to earlier Bayesian analyses by Xie et al. (2020) and Li et al. (2021) that used astrophysical data including NICER observations of PSR J0740+6620 (Miller et al. 2021; Riley et al. 2021) and PSR J0030+0451 (Riley et al. 2019; Fonseca et al. 2021). The findings enable refined constraints on the equation of state of supradense neutron-rich matter and inform the debate on whether GW190814's minor component m2 (Abbott et al. 2020) is the most massive neutron star or the lightest black hole, using the universal relation between static and rotating maximum masses from Breu and Rezzolla (2016) and Musolino et al. (2024).
