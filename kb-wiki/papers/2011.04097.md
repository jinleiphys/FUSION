# Lipkin model on a quantum computer

> Atomic nuclei are important laboratories for exploring and testing new insights into the universe, such as experiments to directly detect dark matter or explore properties of neutrinos. The targets of interest are often heavy, complex nuclei that challenge our ability to reliably model them (as well as quantify the uncertainty of those models) with classical computers. Hence there is great interest in applying quantum computation to nuclear structure for these applications. As an early step in this direction, especially with regards to the uncertainties in the relevant quantum calculations, we develop circuits to implement variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) algorithms for the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model, which is often used in the nuclear physics community as a testbed for many-body methods. We present quantum circuits for VQE for two and three particles and discuss the construction of circuits for more particles. Implementing the VQE for a two-particle system on the IBM Quantum Experience, we identify initialization and two-qubit gates as the largest sources of error. We find that error mitigation procedures reduce the errors in the results significantly, but additional quantum hardware improvements are needed for quantum calculations to be sufficiently accurate to be competitive with the best current classical methods.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) algorithms can be implemented for the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model on current quantum hardware, but error mitigation procedures, while significantly reducing errors, are insufficient to make quantum calculations competitive with classical methods without additional hardware improvements.

## Method

The authors constructed quantum circuits to implement VQE algorithms for the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model with two, three, and four particles, using a single variational parameter trial state that exploits the parity symmetry of the model. They implemented the two-particle VQE on the IBM Quantum Experience, measuring expectation values of Pauli operators to compute the Hamiltonian, and applied error mitigation techniques from prior work to reduce computational errors.

## Key numbers

- For the two-particle system, the exact ground state energy is E_gnd = -sqrt(1+V^2), where V is the dimensionless coupling strength
- The two-particle ground state wavefunction has the form |Psi> = [V|up up> - (1+sqrt(1+V^2))|down down>] / sqrt(2V^2+2+2sqrt(1+V^2))
- For the three-particle system, the ground state energy is -1/2 - sqrt(1+3V^2)
- For the four-particle system, the ground state energy is -2 sqrt(1+3V^2)
- The two-qubit Hamiltonian matrix has eigenvalues 0 (multiplicity 2) and +/- sqrt(1+V^2)

## Context

This work extends the application of quantum computing to nuclear structure problems, specifically using the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model as a testbed that has been widely used for validating many-body methods including time-dependent Hartree-Fock (Krieger 1977), coupled-clusters (PhysRevC 1980), and density functional theory (PhysRevC 2009). The results identify initialization and two-qubit gates as the largest error sources, enabling future work to focus on hardware improvements needed for quantum calculations to become competitive with classical configuration-interaction and coupled-cluster methods for heavy nuclei relevant to dark matter detection experiments.
