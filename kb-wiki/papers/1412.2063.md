# Exact solutions of the (0+1)-dimensional kinetic equation in the relaxation time approximation

> We present exact solutions of the (0+1)-dimensional kinetic equation for a massive gas in the relaxation time approximation. At first, we analyse the case of classical statistics and argue that the traditional second-order hydrodynamics misses the shear-bulk coupling. In the next step, we include Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac statistics in the calculations and show that they are important for description of the effects connected with bulk viscosity.

## Key claim

The paper presents exact solutions of the (0+1)-dimensional kinetic equation for a massive gas in the relaxation time approximation, showing that traditional second-order hydrodynamics misses the shear-bulk coupling and that quantum statistics (Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac) are important for describing bulk viscosity effects, though the abstract does not specify that these conclusions are drawn specifically for a boost-invariant longitudinal expansion with a fixed relaxation time of 0.25 fm/c.

## Method

The author solves the boost-invariant Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation for a massive gas, using the Romatsche-Strickland form as the initial condition and an iterative method to solve the integral equation for the effective temperature T(tau). The shear and bulk viscosities are computed both from analytic formulas (using the equilibrium distribution function) and from effective expressions derived from the exact kinetic solution, comparing results for classical (Boltzmann), Bose-Einstein, and Fermi-Dirac statistics.

## Key numbers

- Relaxation time tau_eq = 0.25 fm/c used throughout the calculations.
- The effective shear viscosity is computed as eta_eff(tau) = (1/2) tau [P_T(tau) - P_L(tau)].
- The effective bulk viscosity is computed as zeta_eff(tau) = -(1/3) tau [P_L(tau) + 2 P_T(tau) - 3 P_eq(tau)].
- For the shear viscosity, only small differences are found between Bose-Einstein, Boltzmann, and Fermi-Dirac statistics; for the bulk viscosity, important differences are found, especially for initially isotropic systems.

## Context

This work extends earlier exact solutions for massless particles (Florkowski 2013) to the massive case and generalizes results for classical statistics (Florkowski 2014) to include quantum statistics. The findings enable improved second-order hydrodynamic models by identifying the need for shear-bulk coupling terms, as discussed by Denicol (2014) and Jaiswal (2014), and are relevant for describing the very early stages of relativistic heavy-ion collisions.
