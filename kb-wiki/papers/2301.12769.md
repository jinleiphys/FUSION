# Insights into the physics of neutron star interiors from pulsar glitches

> The presence of superfluid phases in the interior of a neutron star affects its dynamics, as neutrons can flow relative to the non-superfluid (normal) components of the star with little or no viscosity. A probe of superfluidity comes from pulsar glitches, sudden jumps in the observed rotational period of radio pulsars. Most models of glitches build on the idea that a superfluid component of the star is decoupled from the spin-down of the normal component, and its sudden recoupling leads to a glitch. This transition in the strength of the hydrodynamic coupling is explained in terms of quantum vortices (long-lived vortices that are naturally present in the neutron superfluid at the microscopic scale). After introducing some basic ideas, we derive (as a pedagogical exercise) the formal scheme shared by many glitch studies. Then, we apply these notions to present some recent advances and discuss how observations can help us to indirectly probe the internal physics of neutron stars.

## Key claim

Pulsar glitches provide the clearest observational evidence for extended (kilometer-sized) superfluid regions in neutron star interiors, but most theoretical studies remain qualitative and based on Newtonian phenomenological models, so the field is far from settled.

## Method

The authors review the standard two-component model where a superfluid component (loose) and a normal component (observable) are treated as rigid bodies coupled by a linear internal torque, deriving the formal scheme shared by many glitch studies. They then apply this framework to discuss recent advances in constraining dense matter properties from glitch observations across the pulsar population, including analyses of vortex pinning in the inner crust and outer core, collective vortex dynamics, and the starquake model.

## Key numbers

- Interior neutron star temperatures are in the range 10^7-10^9 K, compared to typical Fermi energies of about 10-100 MeV (equivalent to 10^11-10^12 K)
- Relative glitch jumps bigger than Delta-nu/nu ~ 10^-7 produce clear signatures in timing residuals
- A vortex avalanche during a glitch involves up to ~10^12 out of ~10^18 vortices expected in a fast radio pulsar
- The weighted mean of ~20 measured healing parameter Q values for Crab glitches yields Q ~ 0.7 +/- 0.05
- The mean healing parameter Q for Vela glitches yields Q ~ 0.12 +/- 0.07
- The implied Vela mass from the starquake model would be unreasonably small, about ~0.5 solar masses
- Vela glitch amplitudes require Delta-nu/nu ~ 10^-6, with a frequency of roughly one glitch every 2-3 years

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Baym (1969) and Anderson (1975) on superfluid vortex pinning and glitch mechanisms, and complements recent reviews by Zhou (2022) and Antonopoulou (2022). The authors discuss how glitch observations can constrain neutron star interior physics, but note that more high-cadence pulsar timing observations are necessary before theoretical dynamical models can be falsified. The work also addresses the starquake model of Ruderman (1969) and its limitations for explaining large Vela glitches, suggesting that crustal failure may require internal loading forces from vortex pinning or magnetic fields.
