# The Strange Prospects for Astrophysics

> The implications of the formation of strange quark matter in neutron stars and in core-collapse supernovae is discussed with special emphasis on the possibility of having a strong first order QCD phase transition at high baryon densities. If strange quark matter is formed in core-collapse supernovae shortly after the bounce, it causes the launch of a second outgoing shock which is energetic enough to lead to a explosion. A signal for the formation of strange quark matter can be read off from the neutrino spectrum, as a second peak in antineutrinos is released when the second shock runs over the neutrinosphere.

## Key claim

If a strong first-order QCD phase transition to strange quark matter occurs in core-collapse supernovae shortly after bounce (within 200–500 ms), it can launch a second outgoing shock energetic enough to produce an explosion, even in one-dimensional simulations that normally fail to explode; the signal of this transition appears as a second peak in the antineutrino spectrum.

## Method

The authors model the hadronic phase using the Shen et al. (1998) equation of state and the quark phase using the MIT bag model with a bag constant of \(B^{1/4}=165\) MeV and a strange quark mass of 100 MeV, applying a Maxwell construction for the phase transition. They perform one-dimensional core-collapse supernova simulations with this hybrid equation of state, varying the bag constant and progenitor mass, and track the time evolution of the neutrino luminosity and mean energy. They also compute the critical density and baryochemical potential for the phase transition under both supernova conditions (weak equilibrium for strangeness) and heavy-ion conditions (no weak equilibrium, net strangeness zero).

## Key numbers

- The critical density for the phase transition in supernova matter at bounce (\(T=10-20\) MeV, \(Y_p=0.2-0.3\)) is near \(n \approx n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\), making immediate quark matter production possible.
- For the adopted equation of state with \(B^{1/4}=165\) MeV, the maximum mass of a cold hybrid star is \(1.5 M_\odot\); for \(B^{1/4}=162\) MeV it is \(1.56 M_\odot\), both above the Hulse-Taylor pulsar mass of \(1.4414 \pm 0.0002 M_\odot\).
- The quark core appears at \(t_{\rm pb} = 200\) ms to 500 ms post bounce in the simulations.
- For heavy-ion conditions with isospin-symmetric matter (\(Y_p=0.5\)), the phase transition occurs at baryon densities above five times normal nuclear matter density for low temperatures, precluding quark matter formation in low-energy heavy-ion collisions.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of quark matter in proto-neutron stars (Pons et al., 2001) which used larger bag constants (\(B>180\) MeV) and found quark matter appearing only 5–20 s after bounce, well after the supernova explosion. The authors show that with a smaller bag constant (\(B^{1/4}=165\) MeV) the phase transition can occur at bounce densities, enabling a second shock mechanism that may resolve the long-standing difficulty of achieving explosions in one-dimensional supernova simulations (Buras et al., 2003; Marek et al., 2007). The predicted second antineutrino peak provides a potential observational signature for next-generation neutrino detectors.
