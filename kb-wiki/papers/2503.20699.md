# Quantum decoherence in the Caldeira-Leggett model by the real-time path integral on a computer

> We propose first-principle calculations of an open system based on the real-time path integral formalism treating the environment as well as the system of our interest together on a computer. The sign problem that occurs in applying Monte Carlo methods can be overcome in general by using the so-called Lefschetz thimble method, which has been developed over the past decade. Here we focus on the Caldeira-Leggett model, which is well known, in particular, as a model of quantum decoherence. In this case, the calculation simplifies drastically since the path integral becomes Gaussian for typical initial conditions. The relevant saddle point, which is unique and complex, can be determined by solving a linear equation with a huge but sparse coefficient matrix, and the integration over the Lefschetz thimble can be performed analytically. Thus we obtain, without assumptions or approximations, the reduced density matrix after a long-time evolution, tracing out a large number of harmonic oscillators in the environment. In particular, we confirm the dependence of the decoherence time on the coupling constant and the temperature that has been predicted from the master equation in a certain parameter regime.

## Key claim

The authors confirm the dependence of the decoherence time on the coupling constant and temperature predicted from the master equation in a certain parameter regime (high temperature and weak coupling), but stress that their method is applicable to general parameter regions where the master equation is not valid.

## Method

The authors perform first-principle calculations of the Caldeira-Leggett model using the real-time path integral formalism, treating both the system and environment together on a computer. They overcome the sign problem via the Lefschetz thimble method; for typical initial conditions the path integral becomes Gaussian, so the unique complex saddle point is found by solving a linear equation with a huge but sparse coefficient matrix, and the integration over the Lefschetz thimble is performed analytically. The reduced density matrix is obtained after long-time evolution by tracing out a large number of harmonic oscillators in the environment.

## Key numbers

* The number of harmonic oscillators in the environment is taken up to \(N_\mathcal{E}=64\) with reasonable computer resources such as PCs.
* The Ohmic spectral density is defined as \(\rho(\omega) C^2(\omega) = 8mM\gamma\,\omega^2/(2\pi)\) for \(\omega \le \omega_\mathrm{cut}\) and zero for \(\omega > \omega_\mathrm{cut}\), introducing the coupling parameter \(\gamma\) and cutoff frequency \(\omega_\mathrm{cut}\).
* The decoherence time scaling with coupling constant and temperature is confirmed in the high temperature regime where \(1 \ll \omega_\mathrm{cut} \lesssim \beta^{-1}\).

## Context

This work extends the real-time path integral approach to open quantum systems, building on the Caldeira-Leggett model (Caldeira 1982) which was originally proposed for quantum Brownian motion and later shown to exhibit quantum decoherence (Unruh 1989, Paz 1992, Zurek 1992). The method enables first-principle calculations without the assumptions (such as high temperature, Born and Markov approximations) required by master equation approaches (Lindblad 1975, Gorini 1975). This paves the way for investigating the time evolution of open quantum systems numerically in parameter regimes where master equations are not valid.
