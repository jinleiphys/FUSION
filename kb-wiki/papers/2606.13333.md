# Hadron polarization and equation of state at FAIR/RHIC-BES energies

> The $Λ$ global polarization indicates that hot and dense matter created in non-central heavy-ion collisions carries large orbital angular momentum. However, the relation between hadronic polarization and the medium's collective rotation remains to be validated. Using the UrQMD transport model, we calculate the thermal vorticity-induced polarization of $Λ$s in Ag+Ag and Au+Au collisions from $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.24$--$7.7$~GeV and a range of centralities. Two different equations of state used in the UrQMD simulation are compared: one resembles a hadron resonance gas, while the other is based on the chiral mean field (CMF) model, providing a more realistic description of dense nuclear matter including a chiral transition that is consistent with lattice QCD expectations. The polarization is sensitive to the equation of state and a softer EoS leads to smaller values. In addition, we show that the $Λ$ polarization in the experimental acceptance and centrality selection does not decrease for even lower beam energies. Our results indicate that the process leading to the large vorticity is a result of the large shear in the baryon current created by its stopping.

## Key claim
The relation between hadronic polarization and the medium's collective rotation remains to be validated; the authors use the UrQMD transport model to show that the polarization is sensitive to the equation of state, with a softer EoS leading to smaller values, and that the polarization does not decrease for even lower beam energies, indicating that the large vorticity arises from shear in the baryon current created by baryon stopping.

## Method
The authors simulate Au+Au and Ag+Ag collisions using the UrQMD transport model with two different equations of state: one resembling a hadron resonance gas (HRG/CASCADE mode) and the other based on the chiral mean field (CMF) model. They perform a coarse-graining procedure on a four-dimensional lattice (spatial spacing 1 fm, temporal spacing 1 fm/c up to 50 fm/c) to extract local flow velocity and temperature from event-averaged energy density, net-baryon density, and momentum density. The thermal vorticity tensor is then calculated at the space-time point of each Lambda hyperon's last scattering, and the mean spin vector is obtained to first order in gradients, with the global polarization defined as twice the average spin projection along the global angular momentum direction.

## Key numbers
- Beam energy range studied: sqrt(s_NN) = 2.24 to 7.7 GeV for Au+Au and Ag+Ag collisions.
- Average freeze-out temperature of Lambda hyperons increases from 50 MeV to 90 MeV across the energy range 2.24 to 7.7 GeV.
- Lambda freeze-out vorticity peaks at approximately sqrt(s_NN) = 2.5 GeV and remains large down to sqrt(s_NN) = 2.24 GeV.
- For Au+Au at sqrt(s_NN) = 3 GeV, the model uses rapidity range y in [-1, 1] and p_T in [0.4, 3] GeV/c for STAR comparison; for energies below 3 GeV, HADES cuts are y in [-0.5, 0.3] and p_T in [0.2, 1.5] GeV/c.
- For Ag+Ag at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.55 GeV, the model over-predicts HADES data, especially at forward rapidities and large momenta.
- Predictions are made for Au+Au at E_lab = 0.8 A GeV (sqrt(s_NN) = 2.24 GeV) with 2x10^6 events per centrality bin.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of Lambda polarization using transport models (e.g., Li (2017), Deng (2021), Yi (2026)) and hydrodynamic approaches (e.g., Karpenko (2016), Fu (2020)) by systematically comparing two equations of state in the UrQMD framework across a wide beam energy range down to SIS18 energies. It directly addresses the claim by Yi (2026) that only a pure hadron gas EoS can describe measured Lambda polarization, showing instead that both the CMF EoS and HRG/CASCADE EoS are consistent with data within errors. The results enable future experimental tests at FAIR and RHIC-BES, particularly for Au+Au at the lowest beam energies, and motivate further study of the relation between polarization and the tilt of the emission source observed in heavy-ion reactions (STAR (2026)).
