# Further investigation of the relativistic symmetry by similarity renormalization group

> Following a recent rapid communications[Phys.Rev.C85,021302(R) (2012)], we present more details on the investigation of the relativistic symmetry by use of the similarity renormalization group. By comparing the contributions of the different components in the diagonal Dirac Hamiltonian to the pseudospin splitting, we have found that two components of the dynamical term make similar influence on the pseudospin symmetry. The same case also appears in the spin-orbit interactions. Further, we have checked the influences of every term on the pseudospin splitting and their correlations with the potential parameters for all the available pseudospin partners. The result shows that the spin-orbit interactions always play a role in favor of the pseudospin symmetry, and whether the pseudospin symmetry is improved or destroyed by the dynamical term relating the shape of the potential as well as the quantum numbers of the state. The cause why the pseudospin symmetry becomes better for the levels closer to the continuum is disclosed.

## Key claim

The pseudospin symmetry (PSS) in nuclei is not simply a static property but arises from the competition between the dynamical effect and the spin-orbit interactions, with the spin-orbit interactions always improving PSS while the dynamical term can either improve or destroy PSS depending on the shape of the potential and the quantum numbers of the state. The improvement of PSS for levels closer to the continuum is explained by the dynamical term switching from a symmetry-breaking role for deeply bound states to a symmetry-improving role for loosely bound states.

## Method

The authors used the similarity renormalization group (SRG) to transform the Dirac Hamiltonian into a block-diagonal form, yielding a Schrödinger-like operator \(H_P\) expanded to order \(1/M^3\). They decomposed \(H_P\) into eight Hermitian components (nonrelativistic limit, two dynamical terms, two spin-orbit terms, and three higher-order terms) and calculated the contribution of each component to the pseudospin energy splitting by evaluating expectation values \(\langle k|O_i|k\rangle\) for eigenstates of a Woods-Saxon potential with parameters fitted to neutron spectra of \(^{208}\)Pb.

## Key numbers

- The Woods-Saxon potential parameters used: \(\Sigma_0 = -66\) MeV, \(\Delta_0 = 650\) MeV, diffuseness \(a = 0.6\) fm, radius \(R = 7\) fm.
- The \(1/M^3\) approximation to \(H_P\) reproduces exact Dirac energies to within about 0.2–0.7 MeV for deeply bound states (e.g., \(1s_{1/2}\): \(-59.408\) MeV vs. exact \(-59.226\) MeV; \(1p_{3/2}\): \(-53.088\) MeV vs. exact \(-52.782\) MeV).
- For the loosely bound state \(2g_{9/2}\), the \(1/M^3\) approximation gives \(-1.636\) MeV vs. exact \(-1.304\) MeV.
- The spin-orbit interactions always contribute negative values to the pseudospin splitting (improving PSS) for all pseudospin partners studied, with the improvement increasing with orbital angular momentum for states of the same radial quantum number.
- For the doublet \((3p_{3/2},2f_{5/2})\), the dynamical term reduces the pseudospin splitting (improving PSS), while for \((3s_{1/2},2d_{3/2})\) it adds to the splitting (destroying PSS).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous rapid communication (Guo 2012) by providing a detailed decomposition of the SRG-derived Hamiltonian to study the dependence of PSS on potential shape and quantum numbers. It builds on the dynamical character of PSS identified by Alberto et al. (2001, 2002) and Lisboa et al. (2010), and on the nonperturbative nature noted by Liang et al. (2011) and Gonoc et al. (2011). The results enable a quantitative understanding of why PSS improves for states near the continuum, and clarify the separate roles of the dynamical term and spin-orbit interactions in a way that could guide future studies of pseudospin symmetry in exotic nuclei and resonant states.
