# Wave-packet continuum discretization for quantum scattering

> A general approach to a solution of few- and many-body scattering problems based on a continuum-discretization procedure is described in detail. The complete discretization of continuous spectrum is realized using stationary wave packets which are the normalized states constructed from exact non-normalized continuum states. Projecting the wave functions and all scattering operators like $t$-matrix, resolvent, etc. on such a wave-packet basis results in a formulation of quantum scattering problem entirely in terms of discrete elements and linear equations with regular matrices. It is demonstrated that there is a close relation between the above stationary wave packets and pseudostates which are employed often to approximate the scattering states with a finite $L_2$ basis. Such a fully discrete treatment of complicated few- and many-body scattering problems leads to significant simplification of their practical solution. Also we get finite-dimensional approximations for complicated operators like effective interactions between composite particles constructed via the Feshbach-type projection formalism. As illustrations to this general approach we consider several important particular problems including multichannel scattering and scattering in the three-nucleon system within the Faddeev framework.

## Key claim

The paper claims that stationary wave packets (eigendifferentials) provide a fully discrete formulation of quantum scattering problems that reduces complicated integral equations with singular kernels to simple matrix equations with regular matrices, but this equivalence relies on the approximation that pseudostates from finite \(L_2\) basis diagonalization can be treated as approximations for exact eigendifferentials, which is valid only when the basis is large enough and the discretization intervals are sufficiently small.

## Method

The authors construct stationary wave packets (WPs) as normalized integrals of exact continuum wave functions over non-overlapping energy or momentum bins, forming an orthonormal discrete basis. They project the Hamiltonian, resolvent, and \(t\)-matrix operators onto this WP basis, replacing continuous spectral expansions with finite-dimensional matrix representations. The method is illustrated for multichannel scattering and for the three-nucleon system within the Faddeev framework, where the WP basis is built as direct products of step-like functions in momentum space, effectively creating a momentum lattice.

## Key numbers

* Free WP functions in coordinate space for \(s\)-wave with \(f(q)=1\) take the form \(x_i(r) = \sqrt{\frac{2d_i}{\pi}} \sin(q_i^* r) \frac{\sin(d_i r/2)}{d_i r/2}\), where \(d_i = q_i - q_{i-1}\) is the momentum bin width and \(q_i^* = \frac{1}{2}(q_{i-1}+q_i)\) is the bin midpoint.
* For the free particle in a spherical box of radius \(R\) with \(S\)-wave, the equivalent quadrature weights are \(\omega_n = \pi / R\), which exactly equal the discrete momentum differences \(d_n = q_{n+1}^* - q_n^*\).
* The free Hamiltonian matrix eigenvalues for momentum WPs are \(\mathcal{E}_i^* = \frac{1}{6\mu}(q_{i-1}^2 + q_{i-1}q_i + q_i^2)\), while for energy WPs they are the bin midpoints \(\mathcal{E}_i^* = \frac{1}{2}(\mathcal{E}_{i-1} + \mathcal{E}_i)\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous development of wave-packet continuum discretization (Rubtsova et al., prior papers cited as KC, K2, K3, K4, Moro, KPR1, KPR2, KPRF, KPR_br) and provides a unified framework connecting eigendifferentials (originally introduced by Weyl, Wigner, and Bethe) to pseudostates used in methods like the continuum-discretized coupled-channel method (CDCC) of Piyadasa, Kamimura, and Rawitscher. The approach enables solving few-body scattering problems on a personal computer rather than requiring supercomputers as in conventional three-nucleon scattering calculations with realistic interactions (Gloeckle, 2012). It also relates to the equivalent quadrature technique (Langhoff, Reinhardt) and the Lorentz integral transform method (LIT).
