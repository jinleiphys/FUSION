# Nuclear Spectra from Quantum Lanczos Algorithm with Real-Time Evolution and Multiple Reference States

> Models of quantum systems scale exponentially with the addition of single-particle states, which can present computationally intractable problems. Alternatively, quantum computers can store a many-body basis of $2^n$ dimensions on $n$ qubits. This motivated the quantum eigensolver algorithms developed in recent years, such as the quantum Lanczos algorithm based on the classical, iterative Lanczos algorithm. I performed numerical simulations to find the low-lying eigenstates of $^{20}$Ne, $^{22}$Na, and $^{29}$Na to compare imaginary- and real-time evolution. Though imaginary-time evolution leads to faster convergence, real-time evolution still converges within tens of iterations and satisfies the requirement for unitary operators on quantum computers. Additionally, using multiple reference states leads to faster convergences or higher accuracy for a fixed number of real-time iterations. I performed quantum circuit prototype numerical simulations on a classical computer of the QLanczos algorithm with real-time evolution and multiple reference states to find the low-lying eigenstates of $^{8}$Be. These simulations were run in both the spherical basis and Hartree-Fock basis, demonstrating that an M-scheme spherical basis leads to lower depth circuits than the Hartree-Fock basis. Finally, I present the quantum circuits for the QLanczos algorithm with real-time evolution and multiple references.

# Nuclear Spectra from Quantum Lanczos Algorithm with Real-Time Evolution and Multiple Reference States

## Key claim

Real-time evolution in the quantum Lanczos algorithm, while slower to converge than imaginary-time evolution, still converges to ground-state energies within tens of iterations and satisfies the unitary requirement for quantum computers; using multiple reference states further improves convergence speed or accuracy for a fixed number of real-time iterations, though the paper's numerical demonstrations are limited to classical simulations of small nuclei in the eigenbasis rather than actual quantum hardware.

## Method

The author performed numerical simulations of the quantum Lanczos (QLanczos) algorithm on a classical computer using exact time evolution in the eigenbasis for three nuclei (²⁰Ne, ²²Na, ²⁹Na) with an inert ¹⁶O core and valence particles restricted to the sd-shell, using the USDB interaction. The algorithm was also tested with multiple reference states generated by evolving random vectors with imaginary-time evolution to near the Hartree-Fock energy. Additionally, quantum circuit prototype simulations were performed for ⁸Be in both the spherical M-scheme basis and the Hartree-Fock basis, using Trotterized real-time evolution in the computational basis with Jordan-Wigner mapping.

## Key numbers

- ²⁰Ne: M-scheme dimension of 640; ground state energy converged within 5% of correlation energy using real-time evolution with Δt = 0.1 MeV⁻¹
- ²²Na: M-scheme dimension of 6,116; ground state energy converged within 5% of correlation energy using real-time evolution with Δt = 0.1 MeV⁻¹
- ²⁹Na: M-scheme dimension of 1,935; ground state energy converged within 5% of correlation energy using real-time evolution with Δt = 0.1 MeV⁻¹
- Imaginary-time evolution used Δτ = 0.1 MeV⁻¹ and converged in fewer iterations than real-time evolution for all three nuclei
- Real-time evolution converged to the ground state within ten iterations for all three nuclei tested

## Context

This work extends the quantum Lanczos algorithm introduced by Motta (2019) and the real-time evolution approach to quantum computing first proposed by Parrish and McMahon (2019), building on the multireference selected quantum Krylov (MRSQK) algorithm of Stair (2020). The study compares the spherical M-scheme basis against the Hartree-Fock basis for quantum circuit depth, finding that the spherical basis leads to lower-depth circuits. The results enable future implementation of the QLanczos algorithm on actual quantum hardware, though the author notes that systems using only a few logical qubits still require extensive noise mitigation and the quantum circuits were only sketched, not executed.
