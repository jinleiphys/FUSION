# Effect of tensor correlations on the depletion of nuclear Fermi sea within the extended BHF approach

> We have investigated the effect of tensor correlations on the depletion of nuclear Fermi sea in symmetric nuclear matter within the framework of the extended Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach by adopting the AV18 two-body interaction and a microscopic three-body force. The contributions from various partial wave channels including the isospin-singlet T=0 channel, the isospin-triplet T=1 channel and the T=0 tensor 3SD1 channel have been calculated. The T=0 neutron-proton correlations play a dominated role in causing the depletion of nuclear Fermi sea. The T=0 correlation-induced depletion turns out to stem almost completely from the $^3SD_1$ tensor channel. The isospin-singlet T=0 3SD1 tensor correlations is shown to response for most of the depletion, which amounts to more than 70 percent out of the total depletion in the density region considered. The TBF turns out to leads to an enhancement of the depletion at high densities well above the empirical saturation density and its effect increases as a function of density.

## Key claim

The depletion of the nuclear Fermi sea in symmetric nuclear matter is dominated by isospin-singlet T=0 neutron-proton correlations, and this depletion stems almost completely from the \(^3SD_1\) tensor channel; the three-body force (TBF) enhances the depletion only at high densities well above empirical saturation density, with its effect increasing monotonically with density.

## Method

The authors used the extended Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (EBHF) approach with the AV18 two-body interaction and a microscopic three-body force (TBF) constructed via the meson-exchange current approach. They calculated the nucleon momentum distribution by expanding the mass operator in a perturbation series up to third order, including the Pauli rearrangement term (\(M_2\)) and the renormalization term (\(M_3\)), and decomposed the contributions from partial wave channels: isospin-singlet T=0, isospin-triplet T=1, and the T=0 tensor \(^3SD_1\) channel.

## Key numbers

- At \(\rho = 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\) without TBF, total depletion of the lowest hole state [\(1-n(k=0)\)] is 0.142 (14.2%); the T=0 channel contributes 0.115 (11.5%), of which the \(^3SD_1\) tensor channel contributes 0.113 (11.3%).
- At \(\rho = 0.34\) fm\(^{-3}\) without TBF, total depletion is 0.148 (14.8%); T=0 contributes 0.108 (10.8%), \(^3SD_1\) contributes 0.105 (10.5%).
- At \(\rho = 0.51\) fm\(^{-3}\) without TBF, total depletion is 0.153 (15.3%); T=0 contributes 0.102 (10.2%), \(^3SD_1\) contributes 0.099 (9.9%).
- Including TBF at \(\rho = 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\): total depletion 0.147 (14.7%); T=0 contributes 0.119 (11.9%), \(^3SD_1\) contributes 0.118 (11.8%).
- Including TBF at \(\rho = 0.34\) fm\(^{-3}\): total depletion 0.181 (18.1%); T=0 contributes 0.135 (13.5%), \(^3SD_1\) contributes 0.133 (13.3%).
- Including TBF at \(\rho = 0.51\) fm\(^{-3}\): total depletion 0.202 (20.2%); T=0 contributes 0.142 (14.2%), \(^3SD_1\) contributes 0.140 (14.0%).
- With TBF, the T=0 channel contributes 81%, 75%, and 70% of the total depletion at \(\rho = 0.17\), 0.34, and 0.51 fm\(^{-3}\), respectively.
- With TBF, about 90.6%, 87.6%, and 85.0% of the total depletion of the lowest hole state stems from np correlations at \(\rho = 0.17\), 0.34, and 0.51 fm\(^{-3}\), respectively; only about 4.8%, 6.3%, and 7.5% is induced by nn or pp correlations.

## Context

This work extends previous BHF calculations of nucleon momentum distribution (Baldo 1991) and the authors' own earlier study of TBF effects (Yin 2013) by isolating the role of tensor correlations in specific partial wave channels. The finding that np tensor correlations dominate the depletion provides microscopic support for the experimental observation at JLab (Subedi 2008) that about 90% of short-range correlated pairs in \(^{12}\)C are np pairs. The results enable more precise modeling of short-range correlations in finite nuclei and neutron star matter, where tensor correlations affect cooling, pairing, and transport phenomena.
