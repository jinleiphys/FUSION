# Impact of the symmetry energy on nuclear pasta phases and crust-core transition in neutron stars

> We study the impact of the symmetry energy on properties of nuclear pasta phases and crust-core transition in neutron stars. We perform a self-consistent Thomas--Fermi calculation employing the relativistic mean-field model. The properties of pasta phases presented in the inner crust of neutron stars are investigated and the crust-core transition is examined. It is found that the slope of the symmetry energy plays an important role in determining the pasta phase structure and the crust-core transition. The correlation between the symmetry energy slope and the crust-core transition density obtained in the Thomas--Fermi approximation is consistent with that predicted by the liquid-drop model.

## Key claim
The slope of the symmetry energy \(L\) at saturation density strongly determines both the complexity of the pasta phase structure and the crust-core transition density in neutron stars, but the correlation is method-dependent: the self-consistent Thomas-Fermi approximation yields slightly higher transition densities than the coexisting phases method, and the bubble configuration appears only in the Thomas-Fermi calculation for small \(L\).

## Method
A self-consistent Thomas-Fermi calculation was performed using the relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with TM1 and IUFSU parametrizations. Two sets of generated models were constructed by adjusting \(g_{\rho}\) and \(\Lambda_{\rm{v}}\) to achieve different symmetry energy slopes \(L\) while keeping \(E_{\rm{sym}}\) fixed at 0.11 fm\(^{-3}\). The Wigner-Seitz cell approximation was used to describe the inner crust, and the total energy density was minimized for each pasta configuration (droplet, rod, slab, tube, bubble) to determine the most stable shape at a given average baryon density.

## Key numbers
- The original IUFSU model (\(L=47.2\) MeV) predicts the droplet-to-rod transition at \(n_b \sim 0.049\) fm\(^{-3}\) and the crust-core transition at \(n_b \sim 0.092\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The original TM1 model (\(L=110.8\) MeV) predicts only the droplet configuration, with the crust-core transition at \(n_b \sim 0.062\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- For \(L \geq 80\) MeV, only the droplet configuration occurs before the crust-core transition; for smaller \(L\) (e.g., \(L=50\) MeV), the full sequence of pasta phases appears.
- The transition from slab to tube in the original IUFSU model occurs at \(n_b \sim 0.079\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The crust-core transition density \(n_{b,t}\) decreases monotonically with increasing \(L\) across both model sets.
- The proton fraction at the transition point \(Y_{p,t}\) decreases significantly with increasing \(L\).

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous liquid-drop model study (Bao 2014) by using a self-consistent Thomas-Fermi approximation that treats surface effects and nucleon distributions without assuming a sharp interface. The results are consistent with earlier Thomas-Fermi calculations (Grill 2012) and liquid-drop model predictions (Oyamatsu 2007) regarding the correlation between the symmetry energy slope and crust-core transition density. The findings enable more precise constraints on the symmetry energy slope \(L\) by comparing predicted pasta phase structures and transition densities with neutron star observations.
