# Opening the Crystalline Color Superconductivity Window

> Cold dense quark matter is in a crystalline color superconducting phase wherever pairing occurs between species of quarks with chemical potentials whose difference δμlies within an appropriate window. If the interaction between quarks is modeled as point-like, this window is rather narrow. We show that when the interaction between quarks is modeled as single-gluon exchange, the window widens by about a factor of ten at accessible densities and by much larger factors at higher density. This striking enhancement reflects the increasingly (1+1)-dimensional nature of the physics at weaker and weaker coupling. Our results indicate that crystalline color superconductivity is a generic feature of the phase diagram of cold dense quark matter, occurring wherever one finds quark matter which is not in the color-flavor locked phase. If it occurs within the cores of compact stars, a crystalline color superconducting region may provide a new locus for glitch phenomena.

## Key claim

When quark interactions are modeled by single-gluon exchange rather than a point-like four-fermion interaction, the window of chemical potential differences $\delta\mu$ where crystalline color superconductivity occurs widens by about a factor of ten at densities accessible in compact stars, and by much larger factors at higher densities, making this phase a generic feature of cold dense quark matter.

## Method

The authors derived and solved the Schwinger-Dyson gap equation for crystalline color superconductivity in the limit where the gap $\Delta$ approaches zero, using a gluon propagator with hard dense loop (HDL) screening functions $F(p)$ and $G(p)$. They numerically evaluated the resulting zero-gap equation to map out curves relating the pair momentum $|{\bf q}|$ and the chemical potential difference $\delta\mu$, extracting $\delta\mu_2$ as the largest $\delta\mu$ on each curve for various values of the QCD coupling $g$ and the average chemical potential $\bar\mu$.

## Key numbers

- At $\bar\mu = 400$ MeV ($g=3.4328$), $\delta\mu_2/\Delta_0 = 1.24$, giving a window width $(\delta\mu_2 - \delta\mu_1) \approx (1.24 - 1/\sqrt{2})\Delta_0$, much larger than the point-like result $(0.754 - 1/\sqrt{2})\Delta_0$.
- At $\bar\mu = 10^4$ MeV ($g=1.4450$), $\delta\mu_2/\Delta_0 = 20.3$, with $\delta\mu_2/\bar\mu = 0.0241$.
- At $\bar\mu = 10^8$ MeV ($g=0.7890$), $\delta\mu_2/\Delta_0 = 1254$, with $\delta\mu_2/\bar\mu = 1.63 \times 10^{-4}$.
- For a weak point-like interaction, $\delta\mu_2 = 0.754 \Delta_0$ and $|{\bf q}| = 0.906 \Delta_0$ (from Larkin and Ovchinnikov (1964) and Fulde and Ferrell (1964)).
- The BCS gap at $\bar\mu = 400$ MeV is $\Delta_0 = 107$ MeV; at $\bar\mu = 10^4$ MeV, $\Delta_0 = 11.9$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of crystalline color superconductivity by Bowers, Rajagopal, and collaborators (2001) from point-like interactions to the more realistic single-gluon exchange interaction, which is quantitatively valid at asymptotically high densities. The results show that the LOFF window widens dramatically as coupling weakens, reflecting the increasingly $(1+1)$-dimensional nature of the physics, and that this enhancement is already significant at densities relevant for compact stars. This opens the possibility that crystalline color superconducting regions in neutron star cores could provide a new locus for glitch phenomena, as previously estimated by Bowers, Rajagopal, and collaborators (2001).
