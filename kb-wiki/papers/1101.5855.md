# Nuclear modification factor in an anisotropic {\em Quark-Gluon-Plasma}

> We calculate the nuclear modification factor ($R_{AA}$) of light hadrons by taking into account the initial state momentum anisotropy of the quark gluon plasma (QGP) expected to be formed in relativistic heavy ion collisions. Such an anisotropy can result from the initial rapid longitudinal expansion of the matter. A phenomenological model for the space time evolution of the anisotropic QGP is used to obtain the time dependence of the anisotropy parameter $ξ$ and the hard momentum scale, $p_{\rm hard}$. The result is then compared with the PHENIX experimental data to constrain the isotropization time scale, $τ_{\rm iso}$ for fixed initial conditions (FIC). It is shown that the extracted value of $τ_{\rm iso}$ lies in the range $0.5 \leq τ_{\rm iso} \leq 1.5$. However, using fixed final multiplicity (FFM) condition does not lead to any firm conclusion about the extraction of the isotropization time. The present calculation is also extended to contrast with the recent measurement of nuclear modification factor by ALICE collaboration at $\sqrt{s}=2.76$ TeV. It is argued that in the present approach, the extraction of $τ_{\rm iso}$ at this energy is uncertain and, therfore, refinement of the model is necessary. The sensitivity of the results on the initial conditions has been discussed. We also present the nuclear modification factor at LHC energies with $\sqrt{s} = 5.5$ TeV.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the nuclear modification factor (R_AA) of light hadrons can be used to constrain the isotropization time scale τ_iso of the quark-gluon plasma, but only under fixed initial conditions (FIC); using fixed final multiplicity (FFM) conditions does not lead to any firm conclusion about the extraction of the isotropization time, and at LHC energies the extraction of τ_iso is uncertain, requiring model refinement.

## Method

The authors calculate radiative energy loss of light quarks in an anisotropic quark-gluon plasma using first-order opacity expansion with static scattering centers, incorporating a direction-dependent two-body potential derived from the hard thermal loop gluon propagator in anisotropic media. A phenomenological model for the space-time evolution of the anisotropy parameter ξ and the hard momentum scale p_hard is used, interpolating between pre-equilibrium free-streaming (δ=2) and hydrodynamic behavior via a smeared step function at τ_iso. The nuclear modification factor R_AA is computed by modifying the fragmentation function to account for energy loss, averaging over jet production positions in the transverse plane, and comparing with PHENIX and ALICE experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The extracted isotropization time τ_iso lies in the range 0.5 ≤ τ_iso ≤ 1.5 (units: fm/c, implied from context) for RHIC energies with fixed initial conditions.
- Initial temperature T_i = 440 MeV with initial time τ_i = 0.147 fm/c for RHIC Au+Au collisions at 0-10% centrality.
- For LHC at √s = 2.76 TeV, initial temperature T_i ≈ 650 MeV with τ_i = 1 fm/c, based on dN_ch/dy = 1600 for most central collisions.
- For LHC at √s = 5.5 TeV, initial temperature T_i = 830 MeV with τ_i = 0.08 fm/c.
- The fractional energy loss in anisotropic media increases by a factor of 1.5-2 depending on the anisotropy parameter ξ, with a maximum ratio of approximately 2 for ξ = 5.
- The average path length ⟨L⟩ ≈ 5.8 fm for RHIC and 6.2 fm for LHC.
- The collisional energy loss in anisotropic media shows deviations from isotropic results of order 10% for ξ = 1 and 20% for ξ = 10, with variation up to 50% depending on propagation angle.

## Context

This work extends prior calculations of radiative energy loss in anisotropic QGP by Prabhee et al. and applies them to compute the nuclear modification factor, building on the phenomenological space-time evolution model of Mauricio et al. The study contrasts with earlier isotropic QGP calculations of jet quenching by Bjorken and others, and addresses the open question of thermalization and isotropization times in heavy ion collisions, where hydrodynamic fits (e.g., PRC75_ref1) suggest rapid thermalization while perturbative estimates (PRC75_ref2) indicate slower equilibration. The results enable future refinement of energy loss models to include plasma instabilities and transverse expansion effects, as noted by the authors.
