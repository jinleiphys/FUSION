# The Massive Pulsar PSR J1614-2230: Linking Quantum Chromodynamics, Gamma-ray Bursts, and Gravitational Wave Astronomy

> The recent measurement of the Shapiro delay in the radio pulsar PSR J1614-2230 yielded a mass of 1.97 +/- 0.04 M_sun, making it the most massive pulsar known to date. Its mass is high enough that, even without an accompanying measurement of the stellar radius, it has a strong impact on our understanding of nuclear matter, gamma-ray bursts, and the generation of gravitational waves from coalescing neutron stars. This single high mass value indicates that a transition to quark matter in neutron-star cores can occur at densities comparable to the nuclear saturation density only if the quarks are strongly interacting and are color superconducting. We further show that a high maximum neutron-star mass is required if short duration gamma-ray bursts are powered by coalescing neutron stars and, therefore, this mechanism becomes viable in the light of the recent measurement. Finally, we argue that the low-frequency (<= 500 Hz) gravitational waves emitted during the final stages of neutron-star coalescence encode the properties of the equation of state because neutron stars consistent with this measurement cannot be centrally condensed. This will facilitate the measurement of the neutron star equation of state with Advanced LIGO/Virgo.

# The Massive Pulsar PSR J1614-2230: Linking Quantum Chromodynamics, Gamma-ray Bursts, and Gravitational Wave Astronomy

## Key claim

The measurement of PSR J1614-2230 at 1.97 ± 0.04 M⊙ provides a lower limit on the maximum neutron star mass that, even without a radius measurement, constrains quark matter to be strongly interacting and color superconducting if a transition to quark matter occurs at densities near nuclear saturation density; this mass also makes the coalescing neutron star scenario for short gamma-ray bursts viable only if the maximum non-spinning neutron star mass exceeds about 2 M⊙.

## Method

The authors used the Shapiro delay measurement from Green Bank Telescope timing observations of the 3.1 ms radio pulsar PSR J1614-2230 in an 8.7 day orbit with a white dwarf companion at 89.17° inclination to obtain the pulsar mass. They then applied the causality bound (requiring sound speed less than light speed) to derive a lower limit on the neutron star radius, and used the Alford et al. (2005) phenomenological quark matter equation of state with parameters a₄ (quartic coefficient for quark interactions) and a₂ (quadratic coefficient for strange quark mass and color superconductivity) to calculate maximum neutron star masses for a transition density of 1.5 times nuclear saturation density. They also analyzed the parameter space of coalescing neutron star masses to determine which mergers can produce short gamma-ray bursts via delayed collapse or prompt collapse with a massive torus.

## Key numbers

- Pulsar mass: 1.97 ± 0.04 M⊙, from Shapiro delay measurement of PSR J1614-2230
- Causality lower limit on radius: R ≥ 8.3 km for a 1.97 M⊙ neutron star, derived from R ≥ 2.83 GM/c²
- Nuclear saturation density: ρ_sat = 2.7 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³, with transition density set at 1.5 n_sat where n_sat = 0.16 fm⁻³
- Quark matter parameter constraint: a₄ ≤ 0.63 and a₂ ≤ 10⁴ MeV² required to support a 1.97 M⊙ neutron star when quark matter appears at 1.5 n_sat
- Maximum mass thresholds for coalescence: solid-body rotation support at 1.2 M_max and differential rotation support at 1.4 M_max, where M_max is the maximum non-spinning neutron star mass
- Mass ratio threshold for torus formation: M₂/M₁ ≥ 4/3 for prompt collapse channel
- Gravitational wave frequency range: ≤ 600 Hz for low-frequency waves encoding equation of state information

## Context

This paper extends the work of Lattimer and Prakash (2001) on equation of state constraints from maximum neutron star mass, and the Alford et al. (2005) quark matter model. It directly uses the mass measurement from Demorest et al. (2010) to rule out free quark matter (a₄ = 1) at densities relevant to neutron star interiors. The results contradict earlier assumptions that maximum neutron star masses below 1.8 M⊙ were sufficient, and make the coalescing neutron star scenario for short gamma-ray bursts (reviewed by Nakar 2007 and Lee and Ramirez-Ruiz 2007) viable. The work enables future measurements of the neutron star equation of state with Advanced LIGO/Virgo by showing that neutron stars consistent with this mass are not centrally condensed, making tidal deformation detectable in low-frequency gravitational waves.
