# Constraining Superfluidity in Dense Matter from the Cooling of Isolated Neutron Stars

> We present a quantitative analysis of superfluidity and superconductivity in dense matter from observations of isolated neutron stars in the context of the minimal cooling model. Our new approach produces the best fit neutron triplet superfluid critical temperature, the best fit proton singlet superconducting critical temperature, and their associated statistical uncertainties. We find that the neutron triplet critical temperature is likely $2.09^{+4.37}_{-1.41} \times 10^{8}$ K and that the proton singlet critical temperature is $7.59^{+2.48}_{-5.81} \times 10^{9}$ K. However, we also show that this result only holds if the Vela neutron star is not included in the data set. If Vela is included, the gaps increase significantly to attempt to reproduce Vela's lower temperature given its young age. Further including neutron stars believed to have carbon atmospheres increases the neutron critical temperature and decreases the proton critical temperature. Our method demonstrates that continued observations of isolated neutron stars can quantitatively constrain the nature of superfluidity in dense matter.

# Constraining Superfluidity in Dense Matter from the Cooling of Isolated Neutron Stars

## Key claim
The neutron triplet superfluid critical temperature is likely 2.09^{+4.37}_{-1.41} × 10^8 K and the proton singlet superconducting critical temperature is 7.59^{+2.48}_{-5.81} × 10^9 K, but these results only hold if the Vela neutron star is excluded from the data set; including Vela increases the gaps significantly to reproduce its lower temperature given its young age.

## Method
The authors applied Bayesian inference to fit the minimal cooling model (assuming no direct Urca process, no exotic matter, and the Akmal-Pandharipande-Ravenhall equation of state) to observed surface temperatures and luminosities of 18 isolated neutron stars. The neutron triplet and proton singlet critical temperatures were parameterized as Gaussian functions of Fermi momentum, and envelope light-element content was varied for stars younger than 10^5 years. Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling was used to obtain posterior distributions for the six superfluid/superconducting gap parameters and the envelope composition parameters.

## Key numbers
- Neutron triplet critical temperature (excluding Vela and carbon atmosphere stars): 2.09^{+4.37}_{-1.41} × 10^8 K (log10 = 8.48 ± 0.57)
- Proton singlet critical temperature (excluding Vela and carbon atmosphere stars): 7.59^{+2.48}_{-5.81} × 10^9 K (log10 = 8.57 ± 0.60)
- Neutron triplet critical temperature (including Vela, excluding carbon stars): log10 = 9.11 ± 0.19 (approximately 1.29 × 10^9 K)
- Proton singlet critical temperature (including Vela, excluding carbon stars): log10 = 8.81 ± 0.81 (approximately 6.46 × 10^8 K)
- Neutron triplet critical temperature (all stars including carbon atmosphere stars): log10 = 9.61 ± 0.22 (approximately 4.07 × 10^9 K)
- Proton singlet critical temperature (all stars including carbon atmosphere stars): log10 = 8.72 ± 0.45 (approximately 5.25 × 10^8 K)
- Neutron Fermi momentum peak (excluding Vela and carbon): 1.70 ± 0.12 fm^{-1}
- Proton Fermi momentum peak (excluding Vela and carbon): 0.67 ± 0.10 fm^{-1}
- Vela's kinetic age: (5-16) × 10^3 years (from Tsuruta 2009)

## Context
This work extends the minimal cooling model approach of Page (2004) by providing the first quantitative Bayesian fit of superfluid and superconducting critical temperatures to neutron star cooling data, including statistical uncertainties. The results contradict earlier qualitative inferences that required strong proton superconductivity, showing instead that the constraints are highly sensitive to whether Vela is included. The method enables future observations of isolated neutron stars to further constrain the density dependence of nucleon pairing gaps, and the authors note that including carbon atmosphere stars (Cassiopeia A and XMMU J1732) increases the neutron critical temperature while decreasing the proton critical temperature.
