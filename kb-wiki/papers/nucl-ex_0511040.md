# New $^{32}$Cl(p,$γ$)$^{33}$Ar reaction rate for astrophysical rp-process calculations

> The $^{32}$Cl(p,$γ$)$^{33}$Ar reaction rate is of potential importance in the rp-process powering type I X-ray bursts. Recently Clement et al. \cite{CBB04} presented new data on excitation energies for low lying proton unbound states in $^{33}$Ar obtained with a new method developed at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory. We use their data, together with a direct capture model and a USD shell model calculation to derive a new reaction rate for use in astrophysical model calculations. In particular, we take into account capture on the first excited state in $^{32}$Cl, and also present a realistic estimate of the remaining uncertainties. We find that the $^{32}$Cl(p,$γ$)$^{33}$Ar reaction rate is dominated entirely by capture on the first excited state in $^{32}$Cl over the whole temperature range relevant in X-ray bursts. In the temperature range from 0.2 to 1 GK the rate is up to a factor of 70 larger than the previously recommended rate based on shell model calculations only. The uncertainty is now reduced from up to a factor of 1000 to a factor of 3 at 0.3-0.7 GK and a factor of 6 at 1.5 GK.

# New $^{32}$Cl(p,$\gamma$)$^{33}$Ar reaction rate for astrophysical rp-process calculations

## Key claim
The $^{32}$Cl(p,$\gamma$)$^{33}$Ar reaction rate is dominated entirely by proton capture on the first excited state of $^{32}$Cl (at 89.9 keV) over the entire X-ray burst temperature range (0.2-2 GK), not by ground-state capture as previously assumed. The rate is up to a factor of 70 larger than the previously recommended shell-model-based rate, but only when this thermally populated excited state is included.

## Method
The authors used experimental excitation energies of low-lying proton unbound states in $^{33}$Ar from Clement et al. (2004), measured at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory, combined with USD shell model calculations for spectroscopic factors and a Woods-Saxon potential model for direct capture. Resonant reaction rates were calculated using the narrow-resonance formalism, summing contributions from capture on both the $^{32}$Cl ground state (1$^+$) and first excited state (2$^+$), weighted by their thermal population factors from the Saha equation. Direct capture contributions were computed with a potential model using Woods-Saxon nuclear and Coulomb potentials.

## Key numbers
- The $^{32}$Cl(p,$\gamma$)$^{33}$Ar reaction rate at 0.3-0.7 GK is a factor of 3 uncertain (reduced from up to a factor of 1000 in previous shell-model-only rates).
- At 1.5 GK the uncertainty is a factor of 6.
- The rate is up to a factor of 70 larger than the previously recommended rate (Herndl et al., 1995) in the temperature range 0.2-1 GK.
- The first excited state in $^{32}$Cl has an excitation energy of 89.9 keV; its population at 0.3 GK is only 5%, yet it dominates the total rate.
- The stellar enhancement factor (SEF) reaches a factor of 5 at temperatures around 0.2-0.4 GK.
- The reaction Q-value is 3.343 $\pm$ 0.007 MeV (from Audi and Wapstra, 2003).
- The dominant resonance is the 5/2$^+$ state at 3.819 MeV in $^{33}$Ar, with a resonance energy of 0.386 $\pm$ 0.008 MeV for capture on the excited state.
- The recommended rate at 0.3 GK is 3.44 $\times$ 10$^{-5}$ cm$^3$ s$^{-1}$ mole$^{-1}$ (lower: 1.94 $\times$ 10$^{-5}$, upper: 6.04 $\times$ 10$^{-5}$).
- The recommended rate at 1.5 GK is 7.94 cm$^3$ s$^{-1}$ mole$^{-1}$ (lower: 3.40, upper: 2.03 $\times$ 10$^1$).

## Context
This work extends the experimental excitation energy measurements of Clement et al. (2004) by incorporating capture on thermally excited states of the target nucleus, which was not considered in that earlier work. It contradicts the previous shell-model-based rate of Herndl et al. (1995), which only considered ground-state capture and had uncertainties up to three orders of magnitude. The new rate is critical for modeling the rp-process in type I X-ray bursts, particularly the reaction flow through the $^{30}$S-$^{34}$Ar region, which is an important bottleneck possibly related to the observed double-peaked structure of some X-ray burst profiles (Fisker et al., 2004). The work demonstrates that statistical Hauser-Feshbach models (e.g., NON-SMOKER) fail to predict the stellar enhancement factor when rates are dominated by a single resonance, and that indirect methods are essential since direct measurements cannot capture reactions on thermally excited target states.
