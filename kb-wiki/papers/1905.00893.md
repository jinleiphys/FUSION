# Dark matter and bubble nucleation in old neutron stars

> We study the probability for nucleation of quark matter droplets in the dense cold cores of old neutron stars induced by the presence of a self-annihilating dark matter component, $χ$. Using a parameterized form of the equation of state for hadronic and quark phases of ordinary matter, we explore the thermodynamic conditions under which droplet formation is facilitated by the energy injection from $χ$ self-annihilations. We obtain the droplet nucleation time as a function of the dark matter candidate mass, $m_χ$. We discuss further observational consequences.

# Dark matter and bubble nucleation in old neutron stars

## Key claim

The presence of self-annihilating dark matter in old neutron stars can facilitate the nucleation of quark matter droplets by injecting energy into the hadronic medium, but this mechanism only becomes effective for dark matter masses above a threshold that depends sensitively on the bag constant, surface tension, and strong coupling constant of the quark phase, for some parameter choices, nucleation times remain longer than the age of the Universe even with dark matter assistance.

## Method

The authors model the neutron star core using a relativistic mean-field approach (Walecka model) for hadronic matter and the MIT Bag model with perturbative QCD corrections for deconfined quark matter. They compute the dark matter capture rate and self-annihilation rate in the neutron star core, obtaining the asymptotic dark matter population from the steady-state solution of the population evolution equation. Using the relativistic Lifshitz-Kagan theory with a WKB approximation, they calculate the bubble nucleation time as a function of dark matter mass, considering the energy injection from dark matter self-annihilation into quark pairs that produce photon showers.

## Key numbers

- Central baryonic mass density in the neutron star core: approximately three times nuclear saturation density, $\rho_B \sim 7 \times 10^{14}$ g/cm³
- Internal temperature of old neutron stars: $T \sim 10^5$ K
- Dark matter capture rate: $C_\chi \simeq 1.8 \times 10^{23} \, (100 \, \text{GeV}/m_\chi) \, (\rho_\chi/\rho_{\chi,\text{local}}) \, f_{\chi,N}$ s⁻¹, where $\rho_{\chi,\text{local}} = (0.39 \pm 0.03)$ GeV/cm³
- Dark matter self-annihilation rate: $C_a = 1.1 \times 10^{-30} \, (\langle \sigma_a v \rangle / (3 \times 10^{-26} \, \text{cm}^3 \text{s}^{-1})) \, (10^5 \, \text{K} / T)^{3/2} \, (m_\chi / 100 \, \text{GeV})^{3/2}$ s⁻¹
- Transition pressures for $\alpha_S = 0.4$: $P_0 = 588, 483, 401$ MeV fm⁻³ for $B = 150, 100, 70$ MeV fm⁻³ respectively, with corresponding baryonic densities $n_b = 0.92, 0.87, 0.78$ fm⁻³
- For $\alpha_S = 0.5$ and $B = 100$ MeV fm⁻³: $P_0 = 600$ MeV fm⁻³, $n_{\text{Had}} = 0.95$ fm⁻³, $n_{\text{Quark}} = 1.80$ fm⁻³
- For $B = 120$ MeV fm⁻³, $\alpha_S = 0.4$: $P_0 = 525$ MeV fm⁻³, $n_{\text{Had}} = 0.88$ fm⁻³, $n_{\text{Quark}} = 1.58$ fm⁻³
- Asymptotic dark matter population ranges from $N_\chi \sim 10^{30}$ for $m_\chi = 1$ GeV to $N_\chi \sim 10^{23}$ for $m_\chi = 10^6$ GeV
- For the $B = 70$ MeV fm⁻³ parameter set, nucleation requires $m_\chi \gtrsim 4 \times 10^5$ GeV
- Old neutron star ages: $\tau_{\text{NS}} \sim 10^{9.5} - 10^{13.5}$ s, with some reaching up to 4.9 Gyr (PSR J0437–4715)
- Universe lifetime: $\tau_U \sim 10^{17.6}$ s

## Context

This work extends the study of quark deconfinement in neutron stars, previously explored by Bodmer (1971) and Witten (1984), by introducing a novel dark-matter-mediated nucleation mechanism. Prior approaches relied on increasing central pressure through accretion or slowed rotation (Berezhiani, 2003) or on thermal effects (several fluctuation studies from the 2000s), with a limiting conversion temperature concept introduced by the authors' earlier work (2013). The results show that for most parameter choices within the currently allowed dark matter mass range (10–1000 GeV from SUSY models), nucleation times remain longer than typical neutron star ages, but the mechanism becomes viable for higher dark matter masses or specific combinations of the bag constant, surface tension, and strong coupling constant. This enables future observational constraints on dark matter properties through neutron star population studies.
