# Mapping the QCD phase diagram with statistics friendly distributions

> We demonstrate that the multiplicity distribution of a system located in the vicinity of a first-order phase transition can be successfully measured in terms of its factorial cumulants with a surprisingly small number of events. This finding has direct implications for the experimental search of a QCD phase transition conjectured to be located in the high baryon density region of the QCD phase diagram.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a two-component multiplicity distribution, as expected near a first-order phase transition, allows factorial cumulants of high order to be reliably extracted from a surprisingly small number of events (approximately 144,393), even when the second component is tiny and difficult to see directly in the multiplicity distribution.

## Method

The authors numerically sampled proton multiplicity distributions 144,393 times (matching the STAR Collaboration's statistics for central Au+Au collisions at √s = 7.7 GeV) and calculated factorial cumulants C_n for n = 2 to 9. They repeated this sampling many times to obtain the variance of each cumulant, and also applied the delta method to analytically compute expected statistical errors. The key test case was a two-component distribution consisting of a binomial distribution (B = 350, p ≈ 0.1144) with a small admixture (α ≈ 0.0033) of a Poisson distribution (⟨N_(b)⟩ = 25.3525), which had been shown in prior work by Bzdak (2018) to reproduce the preliminary STAR data for proton cumulants up to fourth order.

## Key numbers

- The two-component distribution used 144,393 events, matching the STAR statistics for most central Au+Au collisions at √s = 7.7 GeV
- The two-component model parameters: α ≈ 0.0033, binomial with B = 350 and p ≈ 0.1144, Poisson with ⟨N_(b)⟩ = 25.3525
- Relative errors for factorial cumulants from the two-component distribution (no efficiency correction): ΔC₂/C₂ = 0.036, ΔC₃/C₃ = 0.16, ΔC₄/C₄ = 0.13, ΔC₅/C₅ = 0.14, ΔC₆/C₆ = 0.18, ΔC₇/C₇ = 0.26, ΔC₈/C₈ = 0.42, ΔC₉/C₉ = 0.91
- Relative errors with detection efficiency ε = 0.65: ΔC₂/C₂ = 0.056, ΔC₃/C₃ = 0.29, ΔC₄/C₄ = 0.27, ΔC₅/C₅ = 0.31, ΔC₆/C₆ = 0.41, ΔC₇/C₇ = 0.61, ΔC₈/C₈ = 1.06, ΔC₉/C₉ = 2.55
- Predicted factorial cumulants for efficiency uncorrected data (assuming C₄ = 170): C₅ = -307 (1 ± 0.31), C₆ = 3085 (1 ± 0.41), C₇ = -30155 (1 ± 0.61), C₈ = 271492 (1 ± 1.06)
- Predicted factorial cumulants for efficiency corrected data (⟨N⟩ = 40): C₅ = -2645 (1 ± 0.14), C₆ = 40900 (1 ± 0.18), C₇ = -615135 (1 ± 0.26), C₈ = 8520220 (1 ± 0.42)
- The ratio C_{n+1}/C_n for the two-component model is approximately -15, with specific values: C₄/C₃ = -17, C₅/C₄ = -15.56, C₆/C₅ = -15.46, C₇/C₆ = -15.04, C₈/C₇ = -13.85, C₉/C₈ = -10.66, and C₁₀/C₉ = 0.72

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Bzdak (2018), which showed that preliminary STAR data for proton cumulants at √s = 7.7 GeV are consistent with a two-component multiplicity distribution. The paper contradicts the common belief that high-order factorial cumulants require very large statistics, showing instead that two-component distributions relevant for a first-order phase transition are "statistics friendly." This enables experimental tests using existing STAR data from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan, where the statistics are expected to increase by a factor of about 25 in the next phase, reducing errors by about a factor of 5. The approach is general and not restricted to QCD phase transitions, and the authors suggest measuring factorial cumulants at higher energies like √s = 200 GeV where a first-order transition is not anticipated, to provide a baseline comparison.
