# A pathway to unveiling neutrinoless $ββ$ decay nuclear matrix elements via $γγ$ decay

> We investigate the experimental feasibility of detecting second-order double-magnetic dipole ($γγ$-$M1M1$) decays from double isobaric analog states (DIAS), which have recently been found to be strongly correlated with the nuclear matrix elements of neutrinoless $ββ$ decay. Using the nuclear shell model, we compute theoretical branching ratios for $γγ$-$M1M1$ decays and compare them with other competing processes, such as single-$γ$ decay and proton emission, which represent the dominant decay channels. We also estimate the potential competition from internal conversion and internal pair creation, which can influence the decay dynamics. Additionally, we propose an experimental strategy based on using LaBr$_3$ scintillators to identify $γγ$-$M1M1$ transitions from the DIAS amidst the background of the competing processes. Our approach emphasizes the challenges of isolating the rare $γγ$-$M1M1$ decay and suggests ways to enhance the experimental detection sensitivity. Our simulations suggest that it may be possible to access experimentally $γγ$-$M1M1$ decays from DIAS, shedding light on the neutrinoless $ββ$ decay nuclear matrix elements.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether second-order double-magnetic dipole (γγ-M1M1) decays from double isobaric analog states (DIAS) can be measured experimentally, which would provide indirect access to neutrinoless ββ decay nuclear matrix elements. The authors hedge that while their simulations suggest experimental access may be possible, the γγ-M1M1 decay widths are extremely narrow (10⁻⁶–10⁻⁷ eV) and face severe competition from single-γ decay and proton emission.

## Method

The authors used the nuclear shell model with isospin-symmetric interactions (KB3G, GXPF1B, GCN2850, JUN45, JJ4BB, GCN5082) in different configuration spaces to compute theoretical branching ratios for γγ-M1M1 decays from DIAS in five 0νββ daughter nuclei: ⁴⁸Ti, ⁷⁶Se, ⁸²Kr, ¹³⁰Xe, and ¹³⁶Ba. They calculated competing decay channels including single-γ decay, proton emission, internal conversion, and internal pair creation, using systematics and the WKB method with spectroscopic factors from semi-empirical, Coulomb, and chiral interactions. They also proposed an experimental strategy using LaBr₃ scintillators to identify the rare γγ-M1M1 transitions.

## Key numbers

- γγ-M1M1 decay widths range from 0.55 × 10⁻⁷ eV (⁴⁸Ti with GXPF1B) to 32 × 10⁻⁷ eV (⁷⁶Se with JJ44B)
- DIAS energies range from 16.1 MeV (⁴⁸Ti with KB3G) to 29.1 MeV (¹³⁶Ba with GCN5082)
- Internal pair creation widths range from 0.05 eV (⁴⁸Ti at Q_E0 = 17.4 MeV) to 3 eV (¹³⁶Ba at Q_E0 = 29.1 MeV)
- Internal conversion widths range from 2 × 10⁻⁶ eV (⁴⁸Ti) to 0.002 eV (¹³⁶Ba)
- Single-γ M1 decay widths for dominant transitions range from 0.42 eV (¹³⁰Xe with GCN5082) to 13 eV (⁴⁸Ti with GXPF1B)
- For ⁴⁸Ti, the experimental DIAS energy is 17.4 MeV, in good agreement with the GXPF1B calculation of 17.3 MeV
- The semi-empirical spectroscopic factor for proton emission from DIAS in ³²S is S^emp = 3.7 × 10⁻⁵, based on a measured width of 38 eV and a calculated single-particle width of 1.05 MeV
- Total proton emission width from the DIAS in ⁴⁸Ti to all energetically allowed states in ⁴⁷Sc ranges from 130 eV to 280 eV depending on the method

## Context

This work extends the correlation between γγ-M1M1 and 0νββ nuclear matrix elements first proposed by Romeo (2022) using the nuclear shell model, which was later confirmed with the quasiparticle random-phase approximation method by Jokiniemi and Menendez (2023). The approach offers advantages over other proposed constraints on 0νββ matrix elements, such as muon capture (Jokiniemi, 2019-2024), heavy ion charge-exchange reactions (NUMEN collaboration, 2022), and double Gamow-Teller transitions (Shimizu, 2017; Wang, 2024), because electromagnetic reactions are easier to model than those mediated by the strong force and may avoid the quenching problem seen in two-neutrino ββ decay calculations. The paper enables future experimental searches for γγ-M1M1 decays from DIAS in medium-mass nuclei, which have not been measured yet, and provides the theoretical framework for designing such experiments.
