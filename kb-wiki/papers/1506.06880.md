# Shape evolution and collective dynamics of quasifission in TDHF

> Background: At energies near the Coulomb barrier, capture reactions in heavy-ion collisions result either in fusion or in quasifission. The former produces a compound nucleus in statistical equilibrium, while the second leads to a reseparation of the fragments after partial mass equilibration without formation of a compound nucleus. Extracting the compound nucleus formation probability is crucial to predict superheavy-element formation cross-sections. It requires a good knowledge of the fragment angular distribution which itself depends on quantities such as moments of inertia and excitation energies which have so far been somewhat arbitrary for the quasifission contribution. Methods: We investigate the evolution of the nuclear density in time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations leading to quasifission. Our main goal is to extract ingredients of the formula used in the analysis of experimental angular distributions. These include the moment-of-inertia and temperature. We study the dependence of these quantities on various initial conditions of the reaction process. Results: The evolution of the moment of inertia is clearly non-trivial and depends strongly on the characteristics of the collision. The temperature rises quickly when the kinetic energy is transformed into internal excitation. Then, it rises slowly during mass transfer. Conclusions: Fully microscopic theories are useful to predict the complex evolution of quantities required in macroscopic models of quasifission.

## Key claim

Fully microscopic TDHF calculations can provide realistic, time-dependent values for the moment of inertia and temperature needed in statistical models of fragment angular distributions, replacing the somewhat arbitrary constant values (such as $\Im_0/\Im_{eff}=1.5$) that have been used previously for the quasifission component.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations on a 3D Cartesian grid without symmetry restrictions for collisions of calcium isotopes ($^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca) with actinide targets ($^{238}$U, $^{249}$Bk) at energies near the Coulomb barrier. They extracted the time evolution of the moment-of-inertia tensor by diagonalizing the density-weighted quadrupole matrix, and computed the dynamical excitation energy using the density-constraint TDHF (DC-TDHF) method to obtain the nuclear temperature along the quasifission path.

## Key numbers

- For $^{48}$Ca+$^{249}$Bk at $E_{c.m.}=218$ MeV with impact parameters $b=0.5$ to $3$ fm, the ratio $\Im_0/\Im_{eff}$ at the final touching configuration ranges from $1.4$ to $1.8$, showing a strong impact parameter dependence.
- For $^{40}$Ca+$^{238}$U central collisions at $E_{c.m.}=208$ and $211$ MeV, the final ratio $\Im_0/\Im_{eff}\simeq 1.2$ is approximately the same for both energies, significantly smaller than the commonly assumed value of $1.5$.
- For $^{48}$Ca+$^{238}$U at $E_{c.m.}=203$ MeV with zero impact parameter, the ratio $\Im_0/\Im_{eff}$ at scission is $\sim 1.1-1.2$ for both tip and side orientations of $^{238}$U.
- The temperature for $^{40}$Ca+$^{238}$U at $E_{c.m.}=211$ MeV rises rapidly to about $1.0$ MeV during the initial overlap phase, then more slowly to $1.25-1.5$ MeV along the scission path.
- Using $\Im_0/\Im_{eff}=1.1$ instead of $1.5$ in the angular distribution formula (with $J_{CN}=46$, $T=1.2$ MeV) changes the ratio $W(\theta)/W(90^\circ)$ by up to $20\%$ at forward and backward angles; fitting with the wrong value would yield $J_{CN}=37$ instead of $46$.

## Context

This work extends the application of TDHF to quasifission dynamics, building on earlier studies by the same group (Umar, Oberacker, Simenel, 2014-2015) and others (Wakhle, 2014; Hammerton, 2015) that demonstrated TDHF's ability to describe quasifission in heavy-ion collisions. The results challenge the standard practice in experimental analyses (Back, 1985; Yanez, 2013; Loveland, 2015) of using a fixed ratio $\Im_0/\Im_{eff}=1.5$ for the quasifission component, and provide a microscopic foundation for extracting the fusion probability $P_{CN}$ from fragment angular distributions. The approach enables more reliable determination of $P_{CN}$ for superheavy element formation, where uncertainties can be as large as 1-2 orders of magnitude.
