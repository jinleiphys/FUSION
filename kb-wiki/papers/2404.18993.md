# Alpha radioactivity deep-underground as a probe of axion dark matter

> We propose to investigate the time modulation of radioisotope decays deep underground as a method to explore axion dark matter. In this work, we focus on the $α$-decay of heavy isotopes and develop a theoretical description for the $θ$-dependence of $α$-decay half-lives, which enables us to predict the time variation of $α$-radioactivity in response to an oscillating axion dark matter background. To probe this scenario, we have recently constructed and installed a setup deep underground at the Gran Sasso Laboratory, based on the $α$-decay of Americium-241. This prototype experiment, named RadioAxion-$α$, will allow us to explore a broad range of oscillation's periods, from a micro-second up to one year, thus providing competitive limits on the axion decay constant across 13 orders of magnitude in the axion mass, ranging from $10^{-9}$ eV to $10^{-20}$ eV after one month of data collection, and down to $10^{-22}$ eV after three years.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that time modulation of alpha-decay rates, measured deep underground, can probe axion dark matter via the axion's coupling to gluons; the key hedge is that the predicted modulation amplitude is extremely small (order 10^-6) and requires both the unique low-background environment of an underground laboratory and a theoretical framework linking the QCD theta-angle to alpha-decay half-lives.

## Method

The authors developed a theoretical description of the theta-dependence of alpha-decay half-lives using a WKB tunneling framework with a microscopic alpha-daughter-nucleus potential, where the dominant effect comes from the theta-dependence of the Q-value via changes in nuclear binding energy mediated by the pion mass. They constructed and installed the RadioAxion-alpha prototype experiment at the Gran Sasso Laboratory, using a NaI crystal to detect the 59.5 keV gamma ray from Americium-241 decay, with data acquisition in list mode at 160 ns time resolution and a Rb atomic clock for timing calibration.

## Key numbers

* Predicted modulation amplitude I(t) = -4.3 x 10^-6 cos(2 m_a t) for the alpha-decay of Americium-241, assuming standard dark matter density rho_DM = 0.45 GeV/cm^3 and scaling as (10^-16 eV / m_a)^2 (10^8 GeV / f_a)^2
* Americium-241 half-life: 432.2 years; dominant alpha-decay Q-value: 5.486 MeV
* Experimental rate with Am-241 source: approximately 4 kHz; background without source: 0.2 Hz
* Projected 2-sigma sensitivity on I_exp: 2 x 10^-5 after one month of data collection (Phase 1), and 4 x 10^-6 after three years (Phase 2)
* Time resolution: 160 ns; oscillation periods probed: from 1 microsecond up to one year
* Axion mass range covered: 10^-9 eV to 10^-20 eV after one month, down to 10^-22 eV after three years (13 orders of magnitude)
* Analytical approximation for the theta-sensitivity factor: mathring{T}_{1/2}(0)/T_{1/2}(0) approximately 125 for Am-241, with general formula 8.45 (Z-2) [4^(2/3) - 8/(3A^(1/3))] (MeV/Q_alpha)^(3/2)

## Context

This work extends the proposal of Zhang (2023) to search for axion-induced time variation of beta-decay rates, but focuses on alpha-decay of heavy isotopes and uses a setup designed to probe a much broader range of oscillation periods (microsecond to one year) rather than the narrower range accessible to Tritium decay experiments. The authors' previous gamma-spectroscopy experiments at Gran Sasso (Bellotti 2012, 2015, 2018) had already excluded decay constant modulations above a few parts per 10^5 for various isotopes, and the current work enables competitive constraints on the axion-gluon coupling across a wide mass range, complementing laboratory limits from EDM searches (Abel 2017, Roussy 2020, Schulthess 2022), radio-frequency atomic transitions (Zhang 2022), and astrophysical bounds from SN 1987A (Graham 2013, Lucente 2022).
