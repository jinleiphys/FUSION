# Resonances in 28Si+28Si. II

> Resonances observed in the 28Si+28Si collision are studied by the molecular model. In the preceding paper, it is clarified that at high spins in 28Si+28Si (oblate-oblate system), the stable dinuclear configuration of the system is equator-equator touching one, and that the axially asymmetric shape of the stable configuration of 28Si+28Si gives rise to a wobbling motion ($K$-mixing). There, the normal modes around the equilibrium have been solved and various excited states have been obtained. Those states are expected to be the origin of a large number of resonances observed. Hence their physical quantities are analyzed theoretically. The results are compared with the recent experiment performed in Strasbourg and turn out to be in good agreement with the data. Disalignments between the orbital angular momentum and the spins of the constituent 28Si nuclei in the resonance state are clarified. Moreover the analyses of the angular correlations indicate characteristic features for each normal-mode excitation. Thus it is possible to identify the modes, and a systematic experimental study of angular correlation measurements is desired.

## Key claim

The molecular model, extended to treat the triaxial (axially asymmetric) equilibrium configuration of the oblate-oblate \(^{28}\mathrm{Si}+{}^{28}\mathrm{Si}\) system, produces resonance states whose calculated angular distributions and angular correlations agree with the Strasbourg data at \(E_{\mathrm{cm}}=55.8\ \mathrm{MeV}\), but only when the \(K\)-mixing (wobbling motion) arising from the triaxiality is included; the model does not claim to reproduce every observed resonance peak without further experimental identification of the normal modes.

## Method

The authors apply an \(R\)-matrix formalism to the molecular wave functions obtained in the preceding paper (Paper I), which describe the dinuclear system as an asymmetric rotator with seven internal degrees of freedom (including the relative orientation angles of the two oblate \(^{28}\mathrm{Si}\) nuclei). They calculate partial widths and collision matrices for the elastic, single \(2^+\), and mutual \(2^+\) channels, and then compute differential cross sections and fragment-fragment-\(\gamma\) angular correlations for the resonance at \(E_{\mathrm{cm}}=55.8\ \mathrm{MeV}\). The theoretical angular correlations are averaged over the finite angular acceptance of the particle detectors (\(\theta_{\mathrm{cm}} = 90^\circ \pm 7^\circ\) in the reaction plane) and compared with the experimental \(4\pi\) \(\gamma\)-ray data from the Strasbourg experiment.

## Key numbers

- The resonance analyzed is at \(E_{\mathrm{cm}} = 55.8\ \mathrm{MeV}\) in the \(^{28}\mathrm{Si}+{}^{28}\mathrm{Si}\) system.
- The total width of the sharp resonances is taken as \(\Gamma^J_\lambda = 150\ \mathrm{keV}\), as suggested by the experiments of Betts et al.
- The spin assignment for the resonance is \(J = 38\), with \(L = 38\) dominance (disalignment of fragment spins and orbital angular momentum).
- The angular range of the particle detectors is \(\theta_{\mathrm{cm}} = 90^\circ \pm 7^\circ\) in the reaction plane, with vertical acceptance \(\pm 4^\circ\).
- The connecting distances for the radial wave function tails are in the range \(R_c = 8.3 - 8.8\ \mathrm{fm}\).
- The half-life of the first \(2^+\) state of \(^{28}\mathrm{Si}\) is \(700\ \mathrm{fs}\).

## Context

This work extends the molecular model previously applied to the prolate-prolate \(^{24}\mathrm{Mg}+{}^{24}\mathrm{Mg}\) system (Uegaki and Abe, 1990s) to the oblate-oblate \(^{28}\mathrm{Si}+{}^{28}\mathrm{Si}\) system, where the stable equator-equator configuration is triaxial and induces a wobbling motion (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975). The results are compared with the Strasbourg experiment (Nouicer et al., 1999; Beck et al., 2000), which measured angular distributions and \(\gamma\)-ray angular correlations at \(E_{\mathrm{cm}}=55.8\ \mathrm{MeV}\). The disalignments observed here contrast with the spin alignments seen in lighter systems such as \(^{12}\mathrm{C}+{}^{12}\mathrm{C}\) and \(^{16}\mathrm{O}+{}^{16}\mathrm{O}\), and even with the \(^{24}\mathrm{Mg}+{}^{24}\mathrm{Mg}\) system (Trombik, 1984; Konnerth, 1985; Mattis, 1987; Wuosmaa, 1987). The work enables future systematic experimental studies of angular correlations to identify specific normal-mode excitations in the resonance spectrum.
