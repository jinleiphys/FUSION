# Gauging of equations method. II. Electromagnetic currents of three identical particles

> The gauging of equations method, introduced in the preceding paper, is applied to the four-dimensional integral equations describing the strong interactions of three identical relativistic particles. In this way we obtain gauge invariant expressions for all possible electromagnetic transition currents of the identical three-particle system. In the three-nucleon system with no isospin violation, for example, our expressions describe the electromagnetic form factors of ^3H, pd->pd gamma, gamma ^3He-> pd, gamma ^3He-> ppn, etc. A feature of our approach is that gauge invariance is achieved through the coupling of the photon to all possible places in the (nonperturbative) strong interaction model. Moreover, once the proper identical particle symmetry is incorporated into the integral eqations describing the strong interactions, the gauging procedure automatically provides electromagnetic transition currents with the proper symmetry. In this way the gauging of equations method results in a unified description of strong and electromagnetic interaction of strongly interacting systems.

## Key claim

The gauging of equations method, when applied to the four-dimensional integral equations for three identical relativistic particles, yields gauge-invariant electromagnetic transition currents that automatically incorporate the correct identical-particle symmetry, provided the strong-interaction equations themselves are constructed consistently with quantum field theory rather than by antisymmetrizing distinguishable-particle results.

## Method

The authors start from the quantum-field-theoretic Green function for three identical fermions and its seven-point extension with an external electromagnetic current. They gauge the strong-interaction integral equation \(G = G_0^P + G_0 V G\) by replacing each quantity with its gauged counterpart, obtaining the vertex function \(\Gamma^\mu = \frac{1}{6}(G_0^{-1}G_0^\mu G_0^{-1} + V^\mu)\). For practical calculations, they introduce an Alt-Grassberger-Sandhas (AGS) formulation for identical particles, defining a Green function \(\tZ\) that obeys \(\tZ = G_0 - G_0 P_{12} T_1 \tZ\) (with no permutation sums in the kernel), and then gauge this equation to obtain \(\tZ^\mu\). All electromagnetic transition currents (bound-state, elastic scattering, breakup) are expressed in terms of \(\tZ^\mu\), the two-body \(t\)-matrix, and the gauged two-body \(t\)-matrix.

## Key numbers

* The three-body bound-state current is given by \(j^\mu = \bar{\Psi}_K \Gamma^\mu \Psi_P\) where the vertex function is \(\Gamma^\mu = \frac{1}{6}\sum_{i=1}^3 (\ggama_i^\mu D_{0i}^{-1} + \frac{1}{2} v_i^\mu d_i^{-1} - \frac{1}{2} v_i \ggama_i^\mu)\) (Eq. howdy).
* The wave-function normalisation for three identical particles is \(i \bar{\Psi}_P \frac{\partial}{\partial P^2}(G_0^{-1} - V) \Psi_P = 6\) (Eq. wave_norm_id), compared to unity for distinguishable particles.
* The two-body bound-state normalisation is \(i \bar{\psi}_P \frac{\partial}{\partial P^2}(D_0^{-1} - \frac{1}{2} v) \psi_P = 2\).
* The AGS-like Green function \(\tZ\) satisfies \(\tZ = G_0 - G_0 P_{12} T_1 \tZ\) (Eq. tZ), and its gauged version is \(\tZ^\mu = \tZ d^{-1} d^\mu + \tZ ( D_0^{-1} D_0^\mu D_0^{-1} d^{-1} - d^{-1} t^\mu P_{12}) \tZ\) (Eq. Zmu).

## Context

This paper extends the gauging of equations method from three distinguishable particles (Paper I, Kvinikhidze and Blankleider 1999) to three identical particles, providing a field-theoretic alternative to the standard quantum-mechanical procedure of antisymmetrizing distinguishable-particle results. The work enables gauge-invariant calculations of electromagnetic processes in three-nucleon systems (e.g., form factors of \(^3\)H, \(pd \rightarrow pd\gamma\), \(\gamma ^3\)He \(\rightarrow pd\)) without the need for explicit symmetrization. The AGS formulation used here differs from the conventional AGS equations of Alt, Grassberger, and Sandhas (1967) and from the form used in three-nucleon calculations by Glockle (1983), because it is derived from quantum field theory rather than from antisymmetrization of the distinguishable-particle case.
