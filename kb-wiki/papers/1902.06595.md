# States of the $^{12}$C Nucleus in the Toroidal Configuration

> The $^{12}$C nucleus with $N$=6 and $Z$=6 is a doubly closed-shell nucleus in a toroidal potential. In the description of the ground state and the Hoyle state of $^{12}$C in the resonating group method or the generator coordinate method, a superposition of the orientations of Wheeler's triangular cluster on the cluster plane would naturally generate an intrinsic toroidal density. A toroidal state also has a probability amplitude to overlap with a 3-alpha cluster, which is the dominant decay mode for the Hoyle state. For these reasons, we study a toroidal description of the states of $^{12}$C in the toroidal configuration both phenomenologically and microscopically. A toroidal $^{12}$C nucleus distinguishes itself by toroidal particle-hole multiplet excitations between one toroidal single-particle shell to another. From such a signature and experimental data, we find phenomenologically that the Hoyle state and many of its higher excited states may be tentatively attributed to those of the $^{12}$C nucleus in a toroidal configuration. We then study the $^{12}$C system from a microscopic mean-field approximation using variational wave functions. We find that the equidensity surfaces of the $^{12}$C ground state exhibit a dense toroidal core immersed in lower-density oblate spheroids in the surface region. Furthermore, there are prominent toroidal features of the equidensity surfaces for the state at the Hoyle excitation energy, at which previous cluster model calculations indicate the presence of a 3-alpha cluster state. A toroidal coexistence model therefore may emerge to suggest the possibility that the physical Hoyle state may have probability amplitudes to be in the toroidal configuration and the 3-alpha cluster configuration.

## Key claim

The Hoyle state at 7.654 MeV and many of its higher excited states in $^{12}$C may be tentatively attributed to states of the $^{12}$C nucleus in a toroidal configuration, based on the signature of toroidal particle-hole multiplet excitations; the authors propose a toroidal coexistence model where the physical Hoyle state may have probability amplitudes to be in both the toroidal configuration and the 3-alpha cluster configuration.

## Method

The authors studied $^{12}$C in a toroidal configuration using two complementary approaches: a phenomenological analysis of toroidal particle-hole multiplet excitations between single-particle $\Lambda$-shells, and a microscopic mean-field approximation with variational wave functions using the Skyrme SkM* energy density functional. The phenomenological method identified the signature of toroidal states through the bunching of single-particle states into $\Lambda$-shells and the resulting pattern of (npnh) multiplet excitations, with the energy scale set by $\epsilon_0 = \hbar^2/(2mR^2)$ where $R$ is the effective toroidal major radius. The microscopic calculations examined the energy landscape across shapes from spherical to toroidal density distributions and determined equidensity surfaces for the ground state and the state at the Hoyle excitation energy.

## Key numbers

- The Hoyle state is at 7.654 MeV excitation energy in $^{12}$C (the 0$^+$ state identified by Cook et al. 1957)
- The 2$^{+}$ state at 4.43 MeV and the 0$^{+}$ ground state of $^{12}$C belong to a collective rotational band with quadrupole deformation parameter $\beta_2 = -0.6$
- The (1p1h)$_{12}^-$ multiplet excitation energy is $3\epsilon_0$, where $\epsilon_0 = 1.25$ MeV is determined by matching the theoretical spectrum to experimental data, placing these states at 11.41 MeV
- The (1p1h)$_{02}^+$ multiplet lies at $4\epsilon_0$ (5.0 MeV above the toroidal ground state)
- The (2p2h)$_{12}^+$ multiplet lies at $6\epsilon_0$ (7.5 MeV above the toroidal ground state)
- The (1p1h)$_{13}^+$ multiplet lies at $8\epsilon_0$ (10.0 MeV above the toroidal ground state)
- The (1p1h)$_{03}^-$ multiplet lies at $9\epsilon_0$ (11.25 MeV above the toroidal ground state)

## Context

This work extends Wheeler's concept of toroidal nuclei (Wheeler 1950, 1998) to the $^{12}$C nucleus, which had previously been described primarily through cluster models such as Wheeler's triangular 3-alpha model (Wheeler 1937), the Brink cluster model (Brink 1966), and the generator coordinate method. The study also connects to the extensive body of work on the Hoyle state and $^{12}$C structure reviewed by Freer (2017) and others. The toroidal description provides a complementary framework to the 3-alpha cluster description: while the 3-alpha cluster description is simpler for decay processes, the toroidal description may be more efficient for particle-hole excitations, as the 3-alpha cluster description requires large mixing of many determinants to bring unperturbed levels down to the observed few-MeV excitation energies. This work enables future projected shell model calculations using toroidal states as basis states, and suggests experimental searches for toroidal states via stripping reactions such as ($^3$He,d) on $^{11}$B.
