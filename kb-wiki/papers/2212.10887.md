# Neutron Star versus Neutral Star: On the 90th anniversary of Landau's publication in astrophysics

> In the late age of developing quantum mechanics, Lev Landau, one of the distinguished players, made great efforts to understand the nature of matter, even stellar matter, by applying the quantum theory. Ninety years ago, he published his idea of "neutron" star, which burst upon him during his visit over Europe in the previous year. The key point that motivated Landau to write the paper is to make a state with lower energy for "gigantic nucleus", avoiding extremely high kinematic energy of electron gas due to the new Fermi-Dirac statistics focused hotly on at that time. Landau had no alternative but to neutronize/neutralize by "combining a proton and an electron", as electron and proton were supposed to be elementary before the discovery of neutron. However, our understanding of the Nature has fundamentally improved today, and another way (i.e., strangeonization) could also embody neutralization and thus a low-energy state that Landau had in mind, which could further make unprecedented opportunities in this multi-messenger era of astronomy. Strangeon matter in "old" physics may impact dramatically on today's physics, from compact stars initiated by Landau, to cosmic rays and dark matter. In this essay, we are making briefly the origin and development of neutron star concept to reform radically, to remember Landau's substantial contribution in astrophysics and to recall those peculiar memories.

# Neutron Star versus Neutral Star: On the 90th anniversary of Landau's publication in astrophysics

## Key claim

Landau's 1932 paper proposed a "neutral star" (not necessarily a "neutron star") as a way to achieve a low-energy state for a "gigantic nucleus" by eliminating the high kinetic energy of degenerate electron gas, but the neutronization he invoked was historically contingent on pre-neutron-discovery particle physics; today, an alternative neutralization via strangeonization (3-flavored quark matter) could also satisfy Landau's original goal, though the critical baryon number \(A_c\) between normal and gigantic nuclei remains unknown.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of Landau's idea from his 1932 publication through the lens of modern quantum chromodynamics, contrasting the conventional neutron star model (2-flavored nucleon matter) with the strangeon star model (3-flavored quark clusters). It presents a phenomenological comparison of flavor symmetry, mass spectra, and equation of state between neutron matter and strangeon matter, and discusses starquake models for glitch amplitudes in both scenarios using calculations from Lu & Peng (2022).

## Key numbers

- Landau's mass limit for white dwarfs: \(M_{\rm max} = 1.5 M_\odot\), expressed in terms of fundamental constants with Planck mass \(M_{\rm p} = \sqrt{\hbar c/G} = 1.2 \times 10^{19}\) GeV.
- Characteristic magnitudes for compact stars: \(N_{\rm max} \sim 10^{57}\) and \(M_{\rm max} \sim M_\odot\) for unit mass \(m_0 \sim 1\) GeV.
- Typical kinematic energy of dense electron gas: \(\hbar c/\Delta x \sim 300\) MeV if electrons remain present.
- Estimated critical baryon number \(A_c\) between normal and gigantic nuclei: between \(10^3\) and \(10^{10}\), with a weak-interaction estimate giving \(A_c \sim 10^9\) from the electron Compton wavelength \(\hbar/(m_e c) \sim 10^3\) fm.
- Minimum mass of conventional neutron star: \(\sim 0.1 M_\odot\); stable strangeon matter could have baryon number as low as \(10^{10}\) (strangeon nugget).
- Glitch magnitude range from starquake models: \(10^{-10}\) to \(10^{-5}\) for strangeon stars, with observed glitch amplitudes falling within this range.

## Context

This essay extends the historical and conceptual re-examination of Landau (1932) previously presented by Xu (2011), and contrasts the conventional neutron star paradigm (developed after the discovery of pulsars and elaborated in Shapiro & Teukolsky 1983) with the strangeon star model proposed by Xu (2003) and Lai & Xu (2017). The work connects to ongoing debates about the hyperon puzzle in neutron star physics and proposes that strangeon matter could provide a unified explanation for compact stars, cosmic rays, and dark matter without introducing particles beyond the standard model, as also explored by Wu (2022) for strangeon nuggets formed in the early universe.
