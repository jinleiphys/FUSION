# A novel way of recasting the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer gap equations

> The gap equations lie at the core of the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory, a standard tool in the description of superfluidity. As a set of non-linear integral equations, the gap equations' inherent difficulties oftentimes hinder even the crudest descriptions of superfluid states. Hard-core potentials, high-density superfluids, and coupled-channel pairing are all reasons that have historically required one to provide special treatment to the gap equations to get a solution. In this paper we present a new method for solving the gap equations that holds the promise of being an efficient universal solver that requires the minimum amount of \textit{a priori} knowledge of the targeted solutions. With theoretical evidence of exotic nuclear superfluidity posing new questions to our understanding of this fundamental property of nuclear systems, the presented method can be a valuable tool when exploring new pairing states, finite-temperature properties, or the development of sophisticated descriptions of nuclear superfludity.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new collocation-type method for solving the BCS gap equations that recasts the problem in terms of the kernel \(F(k)=\Delta(k)/E(k)\) rather than the gap function itself, claiming it can serve as an efficient universal solver requiring minimal _a priori_ knowledge of the targeted solutions, though the method's equivalence to the standard gap equations depends on three formal conditions (boundedness of the potential integral, continuity of the potential, and continuity of the nonlinear function \(g(k,v)\)) that are satisfied for most realistic nuclear systems.

## Method

The authors reformulate the s-wave BCS gap equation into an equivalent equation for \(F(k)=\Delta(k)/E(k)\), then apply a collocation method that expands \(F(k)\) on a basis of cardinal polynomials anchored on a Legendre grid. The resulting system of nonlinear algebraic equations (Eq. 18 in the text) is solved using a standard multidimensional Newton's method, with the integrals in the recast equation evaluated once via Gauss-Legendre quadrature on a composite grid that includes a mapping to handle the high-momentum tail.

## Key numbers

* For the Pöschl-Teller potential and realistic N2LO and N3LO (450 MeV) neutron-neutron interactions at chemical potential \(\mu=5~\mathrm{MeV}\) (Fermi momentum \(k_\mathrm{F}\approx 0.5~\mathrm{fm}^{-1}\)), the new method converges within 30 Newton steps regardless of the interaction chosen.
* The convergence separates into three regimes: a qualitative regime (steps 0–5), a quantitative regime (steps 5–10), and a final regime (steps 10–30) where changes in \(F(k)\) are of order \(10^{-6}\).
* The calculations used a grid of 1500 points made of three Legendre grids spanning the intervals \((0,1)\), \((1,10)\), and \((10,51)\), with the interval \((50,51)\) mapped onto \((50,400)\) using a joint transformation.

## Context

This work extends the collocation approach previously applied to integral equations by Kumar (1987) to the specific case of the BCS gap equations, which are nonlinear Hammerstein-type integral equations. The method addresses limitations of standard iterative approaches (direct iteration, modified Broyden's method, and the method of Khodel (2001)) that scale poorly with precision and require fine-tuning of learning rates or special treatment for potentials that vanish near the Fermi surface. The recast method enables efficient exploration of exotic nuclear superfluidity, finite-temperature properties, and coupled-channel pairing without system-specific adjustments.
