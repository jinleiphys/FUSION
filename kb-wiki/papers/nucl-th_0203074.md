# Large-scale QRPA calculation of E1-strength and its impact on the neutron capture cross section

> Large-scale QRPA calculations of the E1-strength are performed as a first attempt to microscopically derive the radiative neutron capture cross sections for the whole nuclear chart. A folding procedure is applied to the QRPA strength distribution to take the damping of the collective motion into account. It is shown that the resulting E1-strength function based on the SLy4 Skyrme force is in close agreement with photoabsorption data as well as the available experimental E1-strength at low energies. The increase of the E1-strength at low energies for neutron-rich nuclei is qualitatively analyzed and shown to affect the corresponding radiative neutron capture cross section significantly. A complete set of E1-strength function is made available for practical applications in a table format for all 7<Z<111 nuclei lying between the proton and the neutron drip lines.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first large-scale QRPA calculation of E1-strength for over 6000 nuclei, claiming that the resulting strength function based on the SLy4 Skyrme force agrees closely with photoabsorption data and available experimental E1-strength at low energies; however, the authors qualify that the BCS approximation used may not properly describe pairing correlations for many drip-line nuclei and that the phenomenological folding procedure adopted for broadening cannot be confirmed at this stage.

## Method

The authors performed QRPA calculations on top of HF+BCS ground states using the SLy4 Skyrme effective interaction, solving the QRPA equations in configuration space for Jπ=1- states in a spherical approximation. The discrete QRPA strength distribution was then folded with a normalized Lorentzian function using an energy-independent width taken from experimental GDR data or empirical systematics, with deformation effects introduced phenomenologically by splitting the resonance strength into two peaks according to the ratio of nuclear diameters. The resulting E1-strength functions were used as input to Hauser-Feshbach statistical model calculations with the MOST code to compute neutron capture cross sections and Maxwellian-averaged rates.

## Key numbers

- Root mean square deviation between calculated centroid and measured GDR energies for 48 spherical nuclei: 457 keV for SLy4, 573 keV for SGII, 564 keV for MSk7, and 2267 keV for SIII
- Pairing gap set to Δ = 12 A^{-1/2} MeV throughout the calculation
- QRPA calculations performed for all 8 ≤ Z ≤ 110 nuclei between proton and neutron drip lines, approximately 6000 nuclei
- For the Sn isotopic chain, the QRPA E1-strength gives an increase of the neutron capture cross section at 10 MeV by a factor up to 20 close to the neutron drip line compared to the Hybrid Lorentzian formula
- Maxwellian-averaged rates at 1.5×10^9 K show deviations up to a factor of 5 for Sn isotopes relative to the Hybrid formula
- For the full set of approximately 4000 nuclei with 8 ≤ Z ≤ 84, increases up to a factor of 30 in the cross section at 10 MeV and a factor of 8 in the rate at 1.5×10^9 K are obtained for the most exotic neutron-rich nuclei
- PR energy observed experimentally: around 8 MeV in fp-shell region, 6.5 MeV around N=50/Z=50, 5.5 MeV for Pb; PR strength B(E1)=20-60 (10^{-3} e^2fm^2) for fp-shell, 70-90 in N=50 region, 80-180 in Z=50 region, 250 for 208Pb

## Context

This work extends earlier RPA calculations by Catara et al. (1997) and relativistic mean field approaches by Vretenar et al. (2001) by performing the first large-scale QRPA calculation covering the entire nuclear chart, and provides a microscopic alternative to the phenomenological Lorentzian-type formulas (such as the Hybrid formula of Goriely (1998) and the generalized Lorentzian model of Kopecky and Uhl (1990)) that were previously the only practical option for global predictions. The resulting E1-strength functions enable systematic evaluation of neutron capture rates for r-process nucleosynthesis calculations and accelerator-driven systems, though the authors note that improvements from quasiparticle-phonon coupling models (Colo et al., 2001) could shift the predicted pygmy resonance energies to better match experimental observations.
