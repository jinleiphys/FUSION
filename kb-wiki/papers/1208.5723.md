# Solar Neutrinos: Status and Prospects

> We describe the current status of solar neutrino measurements and of the theory -- both neutrino physics and solar astrophysics -- employed in interpreting measurements. Important recent developments include Super-Kamiokande's determination of the neutrino-electron elastic scattering rate for 8B neutrinos to 3%; the latest SNO global analysis in which the inclusion of low-energy data from SNO I and II significantly narrowed the range of allowed values for the neutrino mixing angle theta12; Borexino results for both the 7Be and pep neutrino fluxes, the first direct measurements constraining the rate of ppI and ppII burning in the Sun; global reanalyses of solar neutrino data that take into account new reactor results on theta13; a new decadal evaluation of the nuclear physics of the pp chain and CNO cycle defining best values and uncertainties in the nuclear microphysics input to solar models; recognition of an emerging discrepancy between two tests of solar metallicity, helioseismological mappings of the sound speed in the solar interior, and analyses of the metal photoabsorption lines based on our best current description of the Sun's photosphere; a new round of standard solar model calculations optimized to agree either with helioseismology or with the new photospheric analysis; and, motivated by the solar abundance problem, the development of nonstandard, accreting solar models, in order to investigate possible consequences of the metal segregation that occurred in the proto-solar disk. We review this progress and describe how new experiments such as SNO+ could help us further exploit neutrinos as a unique probe of stellar interiors.

## Key claim

Solar neutrino measurements have now reached sufficient precision that, with weak interaction uncertainties largely removed, the remaining discrepancies between standard solar model predictions and observations, particularly the "solar abundance problem" where helioseismological sound-speed mappings disagree with photospheric metal abundance analyses, can be used to probe limitations in solar models, rather than primarily testing neutrino physics.

## Method

The authors review the current status of solar neutrino experiments (Super-Kamiokande, SNO, Borexino) and their measurements of neutrino fluxes from the pp chain (pp/pep, 7Be, 8B) and CNO cycle (13N, 15O, 17F). They compare these measurements to two standard solar models (GS98-SFII and AGSS09-SFII) that differ in their assumed photospheric metallicity, and discuss how helioseismological constraints on sound speed and convective zone radius test these models. The review also summarizes the Solar Fusion II evaluation of nuclear reaction cross sections and the impact of reactor measurements of the mixing angle θ13 on global analyses of solar neutrino data.

## Key numbers

- Super-Kamiokande's determination of the ν-e elastic scattering rate for 8B neutrinos to 3% precision
- SNO's combined analysis of all three phases narrowed the allowed range for the neutrino mixing angle θ12
- Borexino achieved a 5% measurement of the 7Be neutrino flux and provided the first direct measurement of the pep neutrino flux
- The sound speed throughout most of the Sun's interior has been extracted from helioseismology to an accuracy of approximately 0.1%
- The photospheric metal abundance from 3D atmospheric models (AGSS09) is approximately 30% below the 1D value (GS98)
- The 8B neutrino flux from the GS98-SFII SSM is 5.58(1 ± 0.14) × 10^6/cm^2s, while from AGSS09-SFII it is 4.59(1 ± 0.14) × 10^6/cm^2s
- The 7Be neutrino flux from GS98-SFII is 5.00(1 ± 0.07) × 10^9/cm^2s, and from AGSS09-SFII is 4.56(1 ± 0.07) × 10^9/cm^2s
- The pp neutrino flux from GS98-SFII is 5.98(1 ± 0.006) × 10^10/cm^2s
- The final Homestake chlorine experiment result was 2.56 ± 0.16 ± 0.16 SNU, about a factor of three below the GS98-SFII SSM prediction of 8.00 ± 0.97 SNU
- The S-factor for 7Be(p,γ)8B from Solar Fusion II is S17(0) = 20.8 ± 0.7 (expt) ± 1.4 (theor) keV-barn

## Context

This review extends the long history of solar neutrino physics from the early Homestake experiment (Davis, 1968) through the discoveries of neutrino oscillations by Super-Kamiokande (1998) and SNO (2001-2002), and updates the status since the previous Annual Reviews article by Haxton (1995). It addresses the emerging "solar abundance problem" where standard solar models using the new low-metallicity photospheric abundances from Asplund et al. (2009) disagree with helioseismological constraints, while models using the older high-metallicity abundances from Grevesse and Sauval (1998) agree better. The review enables future experiments such as SNO+ to further exploit neutrinos as probes of stellar interiors and to constrain the PMNS neutrino mass matrix.
