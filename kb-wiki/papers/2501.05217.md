# Probing Nuclear Excitation by Electron Capture in an Electron Beam Ion Trap with Non-destructive Isomer Detection via Precision Mass Spectrometry

> Nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) is an important nuclear excitation mechanism which still lacks conclusive experimental verification. This is primarily attributed to strong background x-/$γ$-ray noise and competing nuclear excitation processes which would overshadow the signals in various environments that NEEC takes place. Here, we propose an experimental approach to observe the NEEC process within a background-free environment. Through collisions with a highly-compressed mono-energetic electron beam in an electron beam ion trap, nuclei may get excited to a long-lived isomeric state via the NEEC process. Subsequently, ions can be extracted and Penning-trap mass spectrometry employed to unambiguously detect the isomer. Our study focuses on the promising candidate $^{189}\mathrm{Os}$, demonstrating measurable detection rates of the NEEC process and discussing the feasibility of the proposed approach. This new approach for observing the NEEC process may be realized in the near future.

# Probing Nuclear Excitation by Electron Capture in an Electron Beam Ion Trap with Non-destructive Isomer Detection via Precision Mass Spectrometry

## Key claim

The authors propose that nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) can be conclusively observed by combining an electron beam ion trap (EBIT) for isomer production with Penning-trap mass spectrometry (PTMS) for isomer detection, provided that the isomer generation rate of approximately 1.35e-7 per second per ion can be accumulated over sufficiently long breeding times (10 hours or more) to yield detectable isomer populations.

## Method

The authors calculate NEEC cross sections for the candidate nucleus 189Os using the method developed by Palffy (2006) and Gunst (2015), focusing on the channel where 189Os75+ captures a free electron with kinetic energy 132.70 keV to excite the nucleus to the 7/2- state at 216.67 keV, which subsequently decays to the long-lived 9/2- isomeric state at 30.82 keV with a branching ratio of 25.9%. They model the charge-state evolution and ion escape rates in the EBIT using the theoretical framework of Penetrante (1991), incorporating atomic processes (collisional ionization, radiative recombination, charge exchange) and nuclear processes (NEEC, internal conversion, spontaneous decay) in a set of multi-charge-state rate equations. The isomer detection relies on cyclotron frequency measurements in a Penning trap, where the mass difference between ground-state and isomeric 189Os74+ produces a frequency shift of approximately 7 Hz at 7 Tesla.

## Key numbers

- The long-lived isomer 189mOs (9/2-) lies at 30.82 keV above the nuclear ground state, with a half-life of approximately 5.8 hours for neutral atoms and more than 206.6 years for highly charged ions such as 189mOs74+.
- The NEEC resonance strength for excitation to the 7/2- state at 216.67 keV is 1.05e-3 barn-eV, yielding an isomer generation rate of 1.35e-7 per second per 189Os75+ ion under EBIT conditions of 300 mA beam current, 40 micrometer beam radius, and 30 eV energy spread.
- The branching ratio for decay from the 7/2- state at 216.67 keV to the 9/2- isomeric state is 25.9%.
- At steady state with 6e4 highly charged Os ions and a Ne cooling gas density of 2.5e5 per cubic centimeter, the average ion escape rate is 1.5e-5 per second.
- Approximately 18 isomers (primarily 189mOs74+) can be generated after a breeding duration of 10 hours in the EBIT.
- The cyclotron frequency of 189Os74+ at 7 Tesla is about 42 MHz, with the isomeric state frequency approximately 7 Hz lower.
- The expected isomer probability over a 10-hour period is 0.07%, and Monte Carlo simulations predict approximately 28 detected NEEC events over 60 days with a statistical uncertainty of about 20%.

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing challenge of conclusively observing NEEC, which was first predicted by Goldanskii (1976) but has remained experimentally unverified due to background x-ray and gamma-ray noise and competing processes such as photoexcitation and inelastic scattering. The only claimed observation of NEEC, reported by Chiara (2018) in the isomer depletion of 93mMo, showed excitation probabilities significantly exceeding theoretical expectations and has been questioned by subsequent measurements under low-background conditions by Guo (2022). The proposed EBIT-PTMS combination avoids the background issues of photon or internal conversion electron detection by using mass spectrometry to identify isomers, and the authors suggest this approach could be extended to study other nuclear excitation mechanisms including nuclear excitation by electronic bridge, nuclear excitation by electron transition, and nuclear excitation by two-photon electron transition.
