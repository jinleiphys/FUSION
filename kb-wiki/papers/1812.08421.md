# A formally exact one-frequency-only Bethe-Salpeter-like equation. Similarities and differences between GW +BSE and self-consistent RPA

> A formally exact Bethe-Salpeter-like equation for the linear-response function is introduced with a kernel which depends only on the one frequency of the applied field. This is in contrast with the standard Bethe-Salpeter equation (BSE) which involves multiple-frequency integrals over the kernel and response functions. From the one-frequency kernel, known approximations are straightforwardly recovered. However, the present formalism lends itself to more powerful approximations. This is demonstrated with the exact analytical solution of the Hubbard molecule. Similarities and differences of the $GW$+BSE approach with the self-consistent random-phase approximation (RPA) is also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper derives a formally exact Bethe-Salpeter-like equation for the linear-response function whose kernel depends only on the single frequency of the applied external field, in contrast to the standard Bethe-Salpeter equation which involves multiple-frequency integrals. The authors demonstrate that this one-frequency formalism can recover known approximations and lends itself to more powerful approximations, as shown by the exact analytical solution of the Hubbard molecule, though the practical advantage for realistic systems remains to be established.

## Method

The authors use the equation-of-motion (EOM) technique applied to a four-point one-frequency linear-response function, analogous to the standard derivation of the Dyson equation for the one-body Green function. They derive a kernel that splits into a purely static (instantaneous) part \(K^0\) and a dynamic (time-dependent) part \(K^\mathrm{dyn}\), where the dynamic part corresponds to a four-body Green function of the 2p-2h and 2h-2p type. The resulting Bethe-Salpeter-like equation is then transformed into an eigenvalue problem of the typical RPA form, with the \(A\) and \(B\) matrices related to the one-frequency kernel.

## Key numbers

- The norm kernel is given by \(N_{k_1k_2} = n_{k_2} - n_{k_1}\), where \(n_k\) are the occupation numbers, with the sign factor \(N^0_{k_1k_2} = 1\) for \(k_1 > k_2\) and \(-1\) for \(k_1 < k_2\).
- The static part of the kernel to first order in the interaction reduces to \(K_{php'h'} \rightarrow \bar v_{ph'hp'}\) and \(K_{phh'p'} \rightarrow \bar v_{pp'hh'}\), recovering the standard RPA (with exchange) or TDHF.
- The second-order contribution to the \(B\) matrix from the particle-hole fluctuation terms is given by \(K^{0,4}_{p_1h_2h_3p_4} \simeq \sum_{ll'}\bar v_{p_1lh_3l'}\frac{\bar n^0_{p_4}\bar n^0_{l'}n^0_{h_2}n^0_l}{\tilde \epsilon_{p_4} +\tilde \epsilon_{l'}-\tilde \epsilon_{h_2}-\tilde \epsilon_{l}}\bar v_{l'p_4lh_2}\), with analogous expressions for the other terms.

## Context

This work extends the equation-of-motion formalism developed by Rowe (1970) and further elaborated in nuclear physics (NPA512, Hubbard, 3-Lipkin, Tohy1) to provide a formally exact one-frequency Bethe-Salpeter-like equation for the linear-response function. It contrasts with the standard multi-frequency Bethe-Salpeter equation derived from the Hedin equations (Hedin, 1965) and the GW+BSE approach which typically uses a static \(W(r,r',\omega=0)\) kernel. The formalism enables systematic improvements beyond the static BSE approximation and connects to the self-consistent random-phase approximation (SCRPA), which maintains sum rules and conservation laws. The approach can be applied to study dynamical effects in the Bethe-Salpeter equation, including double excitations and electron-hole screened interactions in metals.
