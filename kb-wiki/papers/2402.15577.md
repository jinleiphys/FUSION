# Shell-model study of $^{58}$Ni using quantum computing algorithm

> This study presents a simulated quantum computing approach for the investigation into the shell-model energy levels of $^{58}$Ni through the application of the variational eigensolver (VQE) method in combination with a problem-specific ansatz. The primary objective is to achieve a fully accurate low-lying energy spectrum of $^{58}$Ni. The chosen isotope, $^{58}$Ni is particularly interesting in nuclear physics through its role in astrophysical reactions while also being a simple but not-trivial nucleus for shell-model study, it being two particles outside a closed shell. Our ansatz, along with the VQE method are shown to be able to reproduce exact energy values for the ground state and first and second excited states. We compare a classical shell model code, the values obtained by diagonalization of the Hamiltonian after qubit mapping, and a noiseless simulated ansatz+VQE simulation. The exact agreement between classical and qubit-mapped diagonalisation shows the correctness of our method, and the high accuracy of the simulation means that the ansatz is suitable to allow a full reconstruction of the full nuclear wave function.

# Shell-model study of $^{58}$Ni using quantum computing algorithm

## Key claim

The variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) method with a problem-specific ansatz can reproduce exact shell-model energy values for the ground state and first two excited states of $^{58}$Ni in noiseless simulation, but the accuracy depends on the choice of classical optimizer, with COBYLA achieving the best convergence while SPSA and gradient descent require substantially more iterations and yield less accurate results for the first excited state.

## Method

The authors map the $fp$-shell valence space of $^{58}$Ni (two neutrons above a $^{56}$Ni core) onto 12 qubits using the Jordan-Wigner transformation, with single-particle states in the $1p_{3/2}$, $0f_{5/2}$, and $1p_{1/2}$ orbitals. They construct problem-specific parameterized ansatz circuits using Givens rotation gates for single and double excitations that conserve particle number and total angular momentum projection, then apply the VQE algorithm with four classical optimizers (COBYLA, SLSQP, SPSA, and gradient descent) to minimize the energy expectation value of the JUN45 Hamiltonian transformed from $J$-scheme to $M$-scheme two-body matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- Ground state ($0^+$) energy: -20.990 MeV from both classical shell model and exact diagonalization after qubit mapping; COBYLA, SLSQP, and gradient descent all reach -20.990 MeV, while SPSA reaches -20.988 MeV
- First excited state ($2^+$) energy: -20.024 MeV from classical shell model and exact diagonalization; COBYLA reaches -20.022 MeV, while SLSQP reaches -19.919 MeV, SPSA reaches -19.912 MeV, and gradient descent reaches -19.918 MeV
- Second excited state ($4^+$) energy: -18.908 MeV from all methods including all four optimizers
- COBYLA converges for the ground state within 80 iterations with $10^{-3}$ MeV accuracy; for the first excited state it converges in 105 iterations with $10^{-2}$ MeV accuracy
- The ground state wavefunction is dominated by the $(1p_{3/2})^2$ configuration with over 80% probability; the first excited state has $(1p_{3/2})^2$ at 63.49% and $(1p_{3/2}1p_{1/2})$ at 12.54%
- Circuit properties: ground state uses 5 parameters, 70 two-qubit gates, and depth 96; first excited state uses 7 parameters, 82 two-qubit gates, and depth 108; second excited state uses 1 parameter, 4 two-qubit gates, and depth 7

## Context

This work extends simulated quantum computing shell-model calculations beyond the lightest nuclei previously explored by Visnak (2015, 2017), Dumitrescu (2018), and others, reaching the $fp$-shell nucleus $^{58}$Ni which is relevant to astrophysical $s$-process reactions in AGB stars studied by Massimi (2022) and the n_TOF collaboration (2014). The problem-specific ansatz approach keeps gate counts low compared with more general ansatzes used by Perez-Obiol (2023), and the method can be extended to other $fp$-shell nuclei with more complicated circuits or to heavier nuclei with linearly increasing qubit requirements.
