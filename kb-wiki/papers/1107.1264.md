# Dynamical effects in proton breakup from exotic nuclei

> We study dynamical effects in proton breakup from a weakly bound state in an exotic nucleus on a heavy target. The Coulomb interactions between the proton and the core and the proton and the target are treated to all orders, including also the full multipole expansion of the Coulomb potential. The dynamics of proton nuclear and Coulomb breakup is compared to that of an equiva- lent neutron of larger binding energy in order to elucidate the differences with the well understood neutron breakup mechanism. A number of experimentally measurable observables such as parallel momentum distributions, proton angular distributions and total breakup cross sections are calculated. With respect to nuclear breakup it is found that a proton behaves exactly as a neutron of larger binding energy. The extra 'effective energy' is due to the combined core-target Coulomb barrier. In Coulomb breakup we distinguish the effect of the core-target Coulomb potential (called recoil effect), with respect to which the proton behaves again as a more bound neutron, from the direct proton-target Coulomb potential. The latter gives cross sections about an order of magnitude larger than the recoil term. The two effects give rise to complicated interferences in the parallel momentum distributions. They are instead easily separable in the proton angular distributions which are therefore suggested as a very useful observable for future experimental studies.

## Key claim
Proton breakup from a weakly bound state can be understood in terms of an equivalent neutron with a larger effective binding energy, but the effective energy depends on the combined core-target Coulomb barrier and is smaller than earlier model predictions (e.g., 0.6 MeV instead of 2.4 MeV for $^{8}$B). The direct proton-target Coulomb potential gives cross sections about an order of magnitude larger than the recoil term from the core-target Coulomb potential, and these two effects interfere in complicated ways in parallel momentum distributions but are separable in angular distributions.

## Method
The authors used an all-order eikonal formalism with a regularized first-order Coulomb term, treating the Coulomb interactions between the proton and core and between the proton and target to all orders including the full multipole expansion. They calculated parallel momentum distributions, angular distributions, and total breakup cross sections for $^{8}$B and $^{17}$F projectiles on a $^{208}$Pb target at 72 A.MeV beam energy, comparing proton results to calculations for hypothetical neutrons with various binding energies. The nuclear breakup amplitude included a core-target elastic scattering probability, while the Coulomb amplitude was separated into a recoil term (core-target Coulomb) and a direct term (proton-target Coulomb).

## Key numbers
- For $^{8}$B on $^{208}$Pb: the proton ground state separation energy is 0.137 MeV (1p$_{3/2}$ state); the effective neutron separation energy that best reproduces proton breakup is 0.6 MeV, a factor four smaller than the 2.4 MeV predicted in an earlier model (Bonaccorso 2004).
- For $^{17}$F on $^{208}$Pb: the proton ground state separation energy is 0.6 MeV (1d$_{5/2}$ state); the best effective neutron separation energy is 1.7 MeV, which agrees with the new formula given in Eq. (1abis) but is smaller than the earlier model prediction.
- The direct proton-target Coulomb term gives cross sections about an order of magnitude larger than the recoil (core-target Coulomb) term in the angular distributions.
- The effective energy shift $\Delta$ for $^{8}$B is -0.4 MeV, giving an effective binding energy $\tilde{\varepsilon}_i = -0.54$ MeV for the 1p$_{3/2}$ state; for $^{17}$F the shift is -1.2 MeV, giving $\tilde{\varepsilon}_i = -1.8$ MeV for the 1d$_{5/2}$ state.
- The Coulomb breakup probabilities from the authors' method are about 50% larger than those of Esbensen (2002) due to the straight-line trajectory approximation.

## Context
This work extends the neutron breakup formalism developed by the authors in earlier papers (Marqués 2002, 2003) to proton-rich nuclei, and directly addresses the experimental findings of Liang et al. (2011) who studied $^{17}$F Coulomb breakup and found that an effective binding energy increase of 1.2 MeV reproduced their data, contrary to the earlier model prediction of 3.2 MeV from Bonaccorso (2004). The paper provides a corrected formula for the effective energy that reconciles theory with experiment, and enables future experimental studies to use proton angular distributions as a clean observable to separate direct and recoil Coulomb effects in breakup reactions.
