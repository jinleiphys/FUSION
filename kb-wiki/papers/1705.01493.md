# Extracting $\hat{q}$ in event-by-event hydrodynamics and the centrality/energy puzzle

> In our analysis, we combine event-by-event hydrodynamics, within the EKRT formulation, with jet quenching -ASW Quenching Weights- to obtain high-$p_T$ $R_{\rm AA}$ for charged particles at RHIC and LHC energies for different centralities. By defining a $K$-factor that quantifies the departure of $\hat{q}$ from an ideal estimate, $K = \hat{q}/(2ε^{3/4})$, we fit the single-inclusive experimental data for charged particles. This $K$-factor is larger at RHIC than at the LHC but, surprisingly, it is almost independent of the centrality of the collision.

## Key claim

The extracted jet quenching parameter K = q̂/(2ε³/⁴) is larger at RHIC than at the LHC by a factor of approximately 2–3, and surprisingly shows almost no dependence on collision centrality, suggesting that K depends on global collision energy rather than local medium properties like temperature.

## Method

The authors combine event-by-event (EbyE) relativistic hydrodynamics using the EKRT initial-state model (with constant shear viscosity η/s = 0.2 and starting time τ₀ = 0.197 fm) with the ASW Quenching Weights formalism in the multiple soft scattering approximation. They compute the nuclear modification factor R_AA for charged particles at high p_T at NLO using CTEQ6.6M proton PDFs, DSS vacuum fragmentation functions, and EPS09 nuclear PDFs, with energy loss implemented via medium-modified fragmentation functions. A K-factor is defined as K = q̂/(2ε³/⁴) and fitted to experimental R_AA data via χ² minimization for each energy and centrality class.

## Key numbers

- The K-factor at RHIC is approximately 2–3 times larger than at the LHC.
- The LHC fitted K-factor is close to unity, while the RHIC value confirms large corrections to the ideal case.
- The starting time for viscous hydrodynamics is τ₀ = 0.197 fm.
- The constant shear viscosity used is η/s = 0.2.
- The uncertainty band for each K-factor fit is determined by Δχ² = 1.

## Context

This work extends previous smooth-averaged hydrodynamic analyses by Andres et al. (2016) to event-by-event hydrodynamics, finding compatible results. The centrality/energy puzzle, where K depends on collision energy but not centrality despite overlapping temperatures, contradicts the expectation that q̂ should scale with local medium properties. Other groups such as Burke et al. (2013) found a factor of approximately 1.25 between RHIC and LHC. The results enable future studies to investigate whether global collision variables beyond local temperature govern jet quenching, and motivate improvements to the quenching weights formalism including broken color coherence, finite energy effects, and collisional energy loss.
