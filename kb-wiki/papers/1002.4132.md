# Isospin Constraints on the Parametric Coupling Model for Nuclear Matter

> We make use of isospin constraints to study the parametric coupling model and the properties of asymmetric nuclear matter. Besides the usual constraints for nuclear matter - effective nucleon mass and the incompressibility at saturation density - and the neutron star constraints - maximum mass and radius - we have studied the properties related with the symmetry energy. These properties have constrained to a small range the parameters of the model. We have applied our results to study the thermodynamic instabilities in the liquid-gas phase transition as well as the neutron star configurations.

## Key claim

The parametric coupling model (PCM) can be constrained to a small range of its free parameters by simultaneously satisfying isospin constraints from heavy-ion collisions and giant monopole resonances, as well as neutron star observations, but the model systematically predicts direct Urca process onset densities that are too low to match cooling observations, indicating a weak point of the model.

## Method

The authors studied four versions of the PCM (S, SI, SV, SVI) by varying the scalar, vector, and isovector coupling parameters \(\alpha\), \(\beta\), and \(\gamma\) while fixing the binding energy (-15.75 MeV), saturation density (0.16 fm\(^{-3}\)), and symmetry energy (32.5 MeV) at saturation. They calculated the symmetry energy slope \(L\), the symmetry energy incompressibility \(K_{sym}\), and the isospin incompressibility coefficient \(K_\tau\) for each parametrization, then compared these with experimental constraints from isospin diffusion, giant monopole resonance, and neutron skin measurements. They also integrated the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation to obtain neutron star mass-radius relations and determined the crust-core transition density from the crossing of the \(\beta\)-equilibrium equation of state with the thermodynamic spinodal.

## Key numbers

*   Symmetry energy slope \(L\) ranges from approximately 111 MeV (for \(\alpha=0\)) down to about 61 MeV (for SVI, \(\alpha=1\)), with experimental constraints giving \(L = 88 \pm 25\) MeV from isospin diffusion and \(L \sim 65\) MeV from isoscaling.
*   Isospin incompressibility coefficient \(K_\tau\) ranges from -970 MeV (for \(\alpha=0\)) to -231 MeV (for SVI, \(\alpha=1\)), with experimental constraints giving \(K_\tau = -500 \pm 50\) MeV from isospin diffusion and \(K_\tau = -550 \pm 100\) MeV from giant monopole resonance.
*   Maximum neutron star masses for the S and SI models with \(\alpha < 0.1\) are greater than 1.8 \(M_\odot\), while for SV and SVI models with \(\alpha < 0.3\) they also exceed 1.8 \(M_\odot\); for \(\alpha=1\), maximum masses drop to about 1.5 \(M_\odot\) (S, SI) or below 1 \(M_\odot\) (SV, SVI).
*   Crust-core transition density \(\rho_t\) varies from 0.1025 fm\(^{-3}\) (for \(\alpha=0\)) down to 0.0699 fm\(^{-3}\) (for SV, \(\alpha=1\)).
*   Direct Urca process onset density \(\rho_{DU}\) ranges from 0.194 fm\(^{-3}\) to 0.293 fm\(^{-3}\), corresponding to neutron star masses \(M_{DU}\) between 0.57 \(M_\odot\) and 0.77 \(M_\odot\), all below the observational threshold of \(M > 1.35 M_\odot\) for cooling.

## Context

This work extends the parametric coupling model (PCM) introduced by Taurines et al. (2003) by applying isospin constraints from recent experimental data, including isospin diffusion measurements by Chen et al. (2005) and Li et al. (2008), giant monopole resonance studies by Garg et al. (2007) and Li et al. (2007), and neutron skin analyses by Centelles et al. (2009). The results are compared with the non-linear Walecka model parametrization NL3 and the density-dependent relativistic hadronic model TW, showing that PCM can reproduce a range of behaviors from NL3-like to TW-like. The findings enable further refinement of the PCM parameters for studies of the liquid-gas phase transition and neutron star configurations, though the systematic failure to reproduce the direct Urca threshold suggests the need for alternative approaches to the isovector channel.
