# Fission of relativistic nuclei with fragment excitation and reorientation

> Experimental studies of fission induced in relativistic nuclear collisions show a systematic enhancement of the excitation energy of the primary fragments by a factor of ~ 2, before their decay by fission and other secondary fragments. Although it is widely accepted that by doubling the energies of the single-particle states may yield a better agreement with fission data, it does not prove fully successful, since it is not able to explain yields for light and intermediate mass fragments. State-of-the-art calculations are successful to describe the overall shape of the mass distribution of fragments, but fail within a factor of 2-10 for a large number of individual yields. Here, we present a novel approach that provides an account of the additional excitation of primary fragments due to final state interaction with the target. Our method is applied to the 238U + 208Pb reaction at 1 GeV/nucleon (and is applicable to other energies), an archetype case of fission studies with relativistic heavy ions, where we find that the large probability of energy absorption through final state excitation of giant resonances in the fragments can substantially modify the isotopic distribution of final fragments in a better agreement with data. Finally, we demonstrate that large angular momentum transfers to the projectile and to the primary fragments via the same mechanism imply the need of more elaborate theoretical methods than the presently existing ones.

# Fission of relativistic nuclei with fragment excitation and reorientation

## Key claim
The authors propose that the systematic factor-of-two enhancement of excitation energy in primary fission fragments, previously addressed by artificially doubling single-particle state energies, can instead be explained by final-state electromagnetic excitation of giant resonances in the fragments as they exit the target. This mechanism is shown to substantially modify isotopic distributions toward better agreement with data, but the authors acknowledge that it alone is unlikely to provide an accurate description of all experimental yields, with remaining discrepancies of a factor of 2-10 for individual yields.

## Method
The authors combine a Glauber-model abrasion stage (calculating primary fragment production via binary nucleon-nucleon collisions) with an ablation stage using the Ewing-Weisskopf statistical decay model (ABLA07 code). They add coupled-channels calculations of electromagnetic and nuclear excitation of giant resonances (IVGDR, ISGQR, IVGQR, DGDR) and rotational states in both the projectile and the outgoing primary fragments, then include the resulting additional excitation energy in the ablation stage. The method is applied to the 238U + 208Pb reaction at 1 GeV/nucleon.

## Key numbers
- Total abrasion cross section for 238U + 208Pb at 1 GeV/nucleon: 8.06 b (with deformation beta=0.29); 7.89 b (with beta=0); 7.65 b (using total densities from electron scattering)
- Cross sections for giant resonance excitation: ISGQR = 408.2 mb, IVGQR = 531.0 mb, IVGDR = 4183 mb, DGDR = 227.3 mb
- Nuclear excitation contribution to ISGQR: 18.72 mb (much smaller than EM excitation)
- Total cross section for primary fragments from all processes combined: 13.41 b
- Additional electromagnetic excitation energy for uranium fragments: nearly constant 11.1 - 12.5 MeV
- Cross section for rotational state excitation in 238U: I=2 state = 3.21 b, I=4 state = 41.7 mb
- Cross section for rotational states built on IVGDR: |IVGDR> ⊗ |I=2>_rot = 1.24 b, |IVGDR> ⊗ |I=4>_rot = 35.5 mb
- About 23% of primary fragments yield fission products after ablation; fission products from EM excitation of 238U correspond to about 18% of EM excitation at large impact parameters
- Angular momentum transfer to fragments from abrasion: J ~ 3-5 hbar for large mass fragments

## Context
This work extends the abrasion-ablation model originally developed by Hufner (1975), Gaimard and Schmidt (1991), and Carlson (1995), which was previously found to require an artificial factor of 2-3 multiplication of excitation energies to match experimental data. The authors address the long-standing problem of fragment yield discrepancies observed in relativistic fragmentation experiments including those by Benlliure (1998), Schmidt (1999), and Enqvist (1999). The proposed post-excitation mechanism could enable new techniques to study fission times of excited nuclei, with typical electromagnetic collision times of ~10^-21 s comparable to transient and saddle-to-scission times.
