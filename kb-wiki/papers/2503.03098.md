# Quantum Magic in Quantum Electrodynamics

> In quantum computing, non-stabilizerness -- the magic -- refers to the computational advantage of certain quantum states over classical computers and is an essential ingredient for universal quantum computation. Employing the second order stabilizer R√©nyi entropy to quantify magic, we study the production of magic states in Quantum Electrodynamics (QED) via 2-to-2 scattering processes involving electrons and muons. Considering all 60 stabilizer initial states, which have zero magic, the angular dependence of magic produced in the final states is governed by only a few patterns, both in the non-relativistic and the ultra-relativistic limits. Some processes, such as the low-energy $e^-őľ^-\to e^-őľ^-$ and Bhabha scattering $e^-e^+\to e^-e^+$, do not generate magic at all. In most cases the largest magic generated is significantly less than the maximal possible value of $\log (16/7) \approx 0.827$. The only instance where QED is able to generate maximal magic is the low-energy $őľ^-őľ^+\to e^-e^+$, in the limit $m_e/m_őľ\to 0$, which is well approximated in nature. Our results suggest QED, although capable of producing maximally entangled states easily, may not be an efficient mechanism for generating quantum advantages.

## Context

This work extends the study of quantum information in particle scattering beyond entanglement (Cervera-Lierta 2017, Low 2021) to the resource of non-stabilizerness (magic), using the stabilizer R√©nyi entropy measure introduced by Leone (2021). It builds on the authors' own recent derivation of the tighter bound M‚āā ‚Č§ log(16/7) for two-qubit states (Liu, Low, Yin 2025) and applies it to QED as a testbed for whether fundamental interactions can efficiently generate quantum computational advantage. The results enable future studies of magic production in other gauge theories (weak and strong interactions) and suggest that while QED readily produces entanglement, it is inefficient at generating the non-stabilizerness required for universal quantum computation.
