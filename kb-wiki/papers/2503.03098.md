# Quantum Magic in Quantum Electrodynamics

> In quantum computing, non-stabilizerness -- the magic -- refers to the computational advantage of certain quantum states over classical computers and is an essential ingredient for universal quantum computation. Employing the second order stabilizer Rényi entropy to quantify magic, we study the production of magic states in Quantum Electrodynamics (QED) via 2-to-2 scattering processes involving electrons and muons. Considering all 60 stabilizer initial states, which have zero magic, the angular dependence of magic produced in the final states is governed by only a few patterns, both in the non-relativistic and the ultra-relativistic limits. Some processes, such as the low-energy $e^-μ^-\to e^-μ^-$ and Bhabha scattering $e^-e^+\to e^-e^+$, do not generate magic at all. In most cases the largest magic generated is significantly less than the maximal possible value of $\log (16/7) \approx 0.827$. The only instance where QED is able to generate maximal magic is the low-energy $μ^-μ^+\to e^-e^+$, in the limit $m_e/m_μ\to 0$, which is well approximated in nature. Our results suggest QED, although capable of producing maximally entangled states easily, may not be an efficient mechanism for generating quantum advantages.

## Key claim

QED is capable of producing maximally entangled states easily, but it may not be an efficient mechanism for generating quantum advantages, as the largest magic generated in most scattering processes is significantly less than the maximal possible value of log(16/7) ≈ 0.827, with the only exception being low-energy μ⁻μ⁺ → e⁻e⁺ in the limit mₑ/m_μ → 0.

## Method

The authors computed tree-level 2-to-2 scattering amplitudes for QED processes involving electrons and muons (e⁻e⁺ → μ⁻μ⁺, Møller scattering e⁻e⁻ → e⁻e⁻, Bhabha scattering e⁻e⁺ → e⁻e⁺, e⁻μ⁻ → e⁻μ⁻, and μ⁻μ⁺ → e⁻e⁺) in both the non-relativistic and ultra-relativistic limits. For each of the 60 two-qubit stabilizer initial states (which have zero magic), they calculated the second-order stabilizer Rényi entropy M₂ of the normalized final spin state as a function of the scattering angle θ in the center-of-mass frame, using spin projections along the incoming beam axis.

## Key numbers

- The maximal possible magic for any two-qubit state is M₂ = log(16/7) ≈ 0.827, a bound derived by the authors in prior work (Liu, Low, Yin 2025).
- For low-energy e⁻e⁺ → μ⁻μ⁺ with the real-world mass ratio λ = mₑ/m_μ ≈ 0.005, the generated magic is minuscule: M₂ ≈ 9 × 10⁻⁵ for one group of initial states and M₂ ≈ 5 × 10⁻⁵ for another.
- The largest magic achievable in low-energy Møller scattering e⁻e⁻ → e⁻e⁻ is M₂ = log(9/5) ≈ 0.588, attained at θ = arctan(2√2) or π − arctan(2√2).
- Low-energy μ⁻μ⁺ → e⁻e⁺ with initial states |ψ_s⟩₁₃ through |ψ_s⟩₂₈ can reach M₂ = log(16/7) ≈ 0.827 at θ = π/4 or 3π/4 in the limit λ → 0, which is well approximated in nature (λ ≈ 0.005).
- Low-energy e⁻μ⁻ → e⁻μ⁻ and Bhabha scattering e⁻e⁺ → e⁻e⁺ generate zero magic for all 60 stabilizer initial states.

## Context

This work extends the study of quantum information in particle scattering beyond entanglement (Cervera-Lierta 2017, Low 2021) to the resource of non-stabilizerness (magic), using the stabilizer Rényi entropy measure introduced by Leone (2021). It builds on the authors' own recent derivation of the tighter bound M₂ ≤ log(16/7) for two-qubit states (Liu, Low, Yin 2025) and applies it to QED as a testbed for whether fundamental interactions can efficiently generate quantum computational advantage. The results enable future studies of magic production in other gauge theories (weak and strong interactions) and suggest that while QED readily produces entanglement, it is inefficient at generating the non-stabilizerness required for universal quantum computation.
