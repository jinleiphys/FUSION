# Production of Light Nuclei in Heavy Ion Collisions via Hagedorn Resonances

> The physical processes behind the production of light nuclei in heavy ion collisions are unclear. The nice theoretical description of experimental yields by thermal models conflicts with the very small binding energies of the observed states, being fragile in such a hot and dense environment. Other available ideas are delayed production via coalescence, or a cooling of the system after the chemical freeze-out according a Saha equation, or a `quench' instead of a thermal freeze-out. A recently derived prescription of an (interacting) Hagedorn gas is applied to consolidate the above pictures. The tabulation of decay rates of Hagedorn states into light nuclei allows to calculate yields usually unaccessable due to very poor Monte Carlo statistics. Decay yields of stable hadrons and light nuclei are calculated. While the scale-free decays of Hagedorn states alone are not compatible with the experimental data, a thermalized hadron and Hagedorn state gas is able to describe the experimental data. Applying a cooling of the system according a Saha-equation with conservation of nucleons and anti-nucleons in number leads to (nearly) temperature independent yields, thus a production of the light nuclei at temperatures much lower than the chemical freeze-out temperature is possible.

## Key claim

While a thermalized gas of hadrons and Hagedorn states can describe experimental yields of light nuclei, the scale-free decays of Hagedorn states alone (without a thermal distribution) overpredict heavy mass states and are not compatible with ALICE data; the production of light nuclei can occur at temperatures much lower than the chemical freeze-out temperature when a Saha-equation cooling with conserved nucleon number is applied.

## Method

The authors extend a previously developed microcanonical bootstrap of Hagedorn states (with conserved baryon number, strangeness, and isospin) by including light nuclei (deuteron, triton, helium-3, helium-4, and hypertriton) as stable particles in the input spectrum. They tabulate the decay branching ratios of Hagedorn states into these light nuclei using an iterative equation that sums over all possible two-body decay chains, circumventing the need for Monte Carlo simulations that would require unfeasible statistics due to branching ratios as low as 10^{-9}. They then compute final yields from a thermalized gas of hadrons and Hagedorn states by integrating the mass spectrum weighted by Boltzmann factors, and also apply a cooling scenario where chemical potentials for baryon number are adjusted to keep nucleon and antinucleon yields fixed according to a Saha equation.

## Key numbers

* The Hagedorn temperature is T_H = 167 MeV for a Hagedorn state radius R = 1.0 fm, and T_H = 152 MeV for R = 1.2 fm.
* The total particle multiplicity from Hagedorn state decays scales as ⟨N_tot⟩ ≈ 0.27 + 1.44 GeV^{-1} m, where m is the Hagedorn state mass.
* For a thermalized Hagedorn gas with T_H = 167 MeV, a fit to ALICE data yields a temperature T = 149 MeV; for T_H = 152 MeV, the fit yields T = 144 MeV.
* The interaction rate of particles in the Hagedorn gas at temperatures T = 150-160 MeV is Γ ≈ 0.2 GeV, corresponding to a lifetime τ ≈ 1 fm/c.
* The binding energies of the light nuclei considered range from 2.2 MeV to 28.3 MeV.
* For a Hagedorn state of mass m = 10 GeV, the relative branching ratios into light nuclei are compared to ALICE data; the scale-free decay scenario overestimates the yields of heavier nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the Hagedorn bootstrap framework developed by Beitel et al. (2014, 2016) and Gallmeister et al. (2017) to include light nuclei production, and tests it against the thermal model descriptions of Andronic et al. (2010, 2017) and Lorenz et al. (2017) that successfully describe experimental yields despite the fragility of light nuclei at chemical freeze-out temperatures around 150 MeV. The authors also compare with the coalescence approach of Scheibl et al. (1998), Mrowczynski et al. (2016), Zhao et al. (2018), and Bellini et al. (2020), and with the Saha-equation cooling approach of Vovchenko et al. (2019). The results enable future studies of light nuclei production in low-energy collisions (e.g., HADES at GSI) using the full dynamical transport code, and suggest that the criticism of thermal models based on low binding energies versus high temperatures may not be legitimate if production occurs via Hagedorn state decays at lower effective temperatures.
