# Thermal quark production in ultra-relativistic nuclear collisions

> We calculate thermal production of u, d, s, c and b quarks in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. The following processes are taken into account: thermal gluon decay (g to ibar i), gluon fusion (g g to ibar i), and quark-antiquark annihilation (jbar j to ibar i), where i and j represent quark species. We use the thermal quark masses, $m_i^2(T)\simeq m_i^2 + (2g^2/9)T^2$, in all the rates. At small mass ($m_i(T)<2T$), the production is largely dominated by the thermal gluon decay channel. We obtain numerical and analytic solutions of one-dimensional hydrodynamic expansion of an initially pure glue plasma. Our results show that even in a quite optimistic scenario, all quarks are far from chemical equilibrium throughout the expansion. Thermal production of light quarks (u, d and s) is nearly independent of species. Heavy quark (c and b) production is quite independent of the transition temperature and could serve as a very good probe of the initial temperature. Thermal quark production measurements could also be used to determine the gluon damping rate, or equivalently the magnetic mass.

## Key claim

Even under an optimistic scenario with very high initial temperature (950 MeV) and short equilibration time (0.063 fm/c), all quark species remain far from chemical equilibrium throughout the hydrodynamic expansion, contrary to assumptions often made in calculations of other signals such as thermal photon and dilepton emission.

## Method

The authors calculate thermal quark production rates for u, d, s, c, and b quarks from three processes: thermal gluon decay (g → i i-bar), gluon fusion (gg → i i-bar), and quark-antiquark annihilation (j j-bar → i i-bar), using thermal quark masses m_i²(T) ≈ m_i² + (2g²/9)T². They then perform boost-invariant one-dimensional hydrodynamic expansion of an initially pure gluon plasma, solving coupled evolution equations for energy density and quark number densities while recalculating temperature and chemical potentials at each time step.

## Key numbers

- Initial conditions for optimistic scenario: τ₀ = 0.063 fm/c, T₀ = 950 MeV; for standard scenario: τ₀ = 0.2 fm/c, T₀ = 650 MeV
- Thermal gluon decay dominates quark production for m_i(T) < 2T and is about ten times larger than gluon fusion or quark-antiquark annihilation at m ≈ 0.5 T
- Gluon fusion dominates for m_i > 3.5 T
- Thermal gluon decay accounts for 70% of charm production and 50% of bottom production at T₀ = 950 MeV; at T₀ = 650 MeV it accounts for 60% of charm and 40% of bottom
- Light quark production scales as dN_q/dy ∝ τ₀² T₀⁶ T_c⁻², while heavy quark production is independent of transition temperature T_c when 2M/T_c - 2M/T₀ ≫ 1
- Results vary less than 30% for renormalization scale Λ = 300-400 MeV

## Context

This work extends the approach of Biró, Müller, Thoma and Wang (1993) by including thermal gluon decay and thermal quark masses for all species, which were not considered in previous calculations. The results contradict the parton cascade model of Geiger (1992), which showed large heavy quark production rates that the authors attribute to high-energy parton collisions rather than thermal plasma processes. The finding that heavy quark production is independent of transition temperature enables using charm and bottom yields as probes of initial plasma temperature, while the dominance of gluon decay opens the possibility of measuring the gluon damping rate (magnetic mass) at future heavy-ion colliders.
