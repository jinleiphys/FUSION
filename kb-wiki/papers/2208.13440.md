# Dynamics of Hot QCD Matter -- Current Status and Developments

> The discovery and characterization of hot and dense QCD matter, known as Quark Gluon Plasma (QGP), remains the most international collaborative effort and synergy between theorists and experimentalists in modern nuclear physics to date. The experimentalists around the world not only collect an unprecedented amount of data in heavy-ion collisions, at Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC), at Brookhaven National Laboratory (BNL) in New York, USA, and the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), at CERN in Geneva, Switzerland but also analyze these data to unravel the mystery of this new phase of matter that filled a few microseconds old universe, just after the Big Bang. In the meantime, advancements in theoretical works and computing capability extend our wisdom about the hot-dense QCD matter and its dynamics through mathematical equations. The exchange of ideas between experimentalists and theoreticians is crucial for the progress of our knowledge. The motivation of this first conference named "HOT QCD Matter 2022" is to bring the community together to have a discourse on this topic. In this article, there are 36 sections discussing various topics in the field of relativistic heavy-ion collisions and related phenomena that cover a snapshot of the current experimental observations and theoretical progress. This article begins with the theoretical overview of relativistic spin-hydrodynamics in the presence of the external magnetic field, followed by the Lattice QCD results on heavy quarks in QGP, and finally, it ends with an overview of experiment results.

## Key claim
The paper derives second-order relativistic magnetohydrodynamics evolution equations for dissipative stresses from kinetic theory using the relaxation time approximation, finding new transport coefficients not present in earlier 14-moment method studies by Denicol et al. (2018, 2019). The authors claim that the two newly derived leading-order transport coefficients δ_VB and δ_πB are sensitive to temperature and hadronic mass, particularly at low mass and low temperature regions.

## Method
The authors start from the relativistic Boltzmann equation with a force term from electromagnetic fields, using the relaxation time approximation for the collision kernel. They perform a Chapman-Enskog-like gradient expansion of the single-particle distribution function, truncating at second order in the Knudsen number, magnetic field parameter χ = qBτ_c/T, and electric field parameter ξ = qEτ_c/T. The dissipative parts of the energy-momentum tensor (shear, bulk viscosity, and diffusion) are evaluated using the off-equilibrium distribution functions for both particles and antiparticles, from which the magnetohydrodynamics evolution equations for the dissipative stresses are derived for both non-resistive and resistive cases.

## Key numbers
- Magnetic field strengths in heavy-ion collisions: approximately 10^14-10^15 T at RHIC and LHC
- Earth's surface magnetic field: 10^-4 T (given as comparison)
- For massless quark matter with lowest possible relaxation time τ_c = 5/(4πT) (from η/s = 1/(4π) quantum lower bound), the normalized electrical conductivity σ/T = 0.0135
- The numerical band of σ/T from various microscopic calculations spans approximately (0.25-15) × 0.0135 for strongly coupled QGP, and beyond 20 × 0.0135 for weakly coupled QGP
- Lattice QCD calculations by Amato et al. provide σ/T values from 0.003 to 0.015

## Context
This work extends the second-order causal magnetohydrodynamics framework previously developed by Denicol et al. (2018, 2019) using the 14-moment method, by instead employing a Chapman-Enskog-like gradient expansion within the relaxation time approximation and including both particle and antiparticle contributions. The derived anisotropic transport coefficients (κ_∥, κ_⊥, κ_× for diffusion; η_0-η_4 for shear; σ_E^∥, σ_E^⊥, σ_E^× for conductivity) enable more complete modeling of QGP evolution in the strong magnetic fields present in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. The electrical conductivity results provide a reference point (σ/T = 0.0135) from the quantum lower bound of η/s, which can be used to determine the decay profile of the magnetic field produced in heavy-ion collision experiments.
