# Short-range expansion for the quantum many-body problem

> In this work we derive a systematic short-range expansion of the many-body wave function. At leading order, the wave function is factorized to a zero-energy $s$-wave correlated pair and spectator particles, while terms that include energy derivatives and larger orbital angular momentum two-body functions appear at subleading orders. The validity of the expansion is tested for the two-body case, as well as the many-body case, where infinite neutron matter is considered. An accurate and consistent description of both coordinate-space two-body densities and the one-body momentum distribution is obtained. These results show the possibility to utilize such an expansion for describing different observables in strongly-interacting many-body systems, including nuclear, atomic and condensed-matter systems. This work also enables a systematic description of large momentum transfer reactions in nuclear systems sensitive to short-range correlations, provides a link between such experiments and low-energy nuclear physics, and motivates measurement of new observables in these experiments.

## Key claim

The paper derives a systematic short-range expansion of the many-body wave function where, at leading order, the wave function factorizes into a zero-energy s-wave correlated pair and spectator particles, with energy-derivative terms and higher orbital angular momentum two-body functions appearing at subleading orders; the expansion is tested specifically for infinite neutron matter using the AV4' potential and central UIX_c three-body force, not for all strongly-interacting systems in general.

## Method

The authors start from the two-body Schrödinger equation and perform a Taylor expansion of the two-body eigenstate around zero energy, identifying the zero-energy wave function as the leading term with energy-derivative corrections appearing at subleading orders. They then generalize this expansion to the many-body case by expanding the full antisymmetric wave function in a complete set of two-body eigenstates and applying the same energy Taylor expansion, organizing terms by both the number of energy derivatives and the orbital angular momentum quantum number. The expansion is tested against auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) calculations of infinite neutron matter at density 0.16 fm^{-3}, comparing the short-range expansion for both the two-body density and the one-body momentum distribution.

## Key numbers

- Infinite neutron matter at density 0.16 fm^{-3} is used as the test system for the many-body expansion.
- The leading-order s-wave contribution describes the two-body density for r less than about 0.5 fm; including NLO corrections (C_s^{01} and C_p^{00}) extends agreement to r less than about 1.3 fm; N^2LO corrections (C_s^{11} and C_p^{01}) extend agreement to r less than about 2 fm.
- Using the expansion with contact parameters fixed by matching to the Fermi-gas model, the per-particle potential energy obtained is -29.3 MeV (with the AV4' potential) and the kinetic energy is 43.2 MeV, compared to AFDMC values of -30.1 MeV for potential energy and 43.3 MeV for kinetic energy.
- The deuteron bound-state wave function using the AV4' potential shows the N^2LO expansion agrees with the exact wave function up to r approximately 7 fm.

## Context

This work extends the Generalized Contact Formalism (GCF), which was developed as an extension of Tan's theory for the zero-range model (Tan, 2008) and has been used to describe nuclear short-range correlations and their impact on nuclear structure and reactions (Weiss, 2014; Weiss, 2015; Weiss, 2016). The expansion provides next-order corrections to the leading-order short-range factorization that underlies GCF spectral function calculations for large momentum transfer electron scattering experiments (Weiss, 2018; Schmidt, 2020; Pybus, 2020; Duer, 2018; CLAS, 2020), and enables a systematic connection between such experiments and low-energy nuclear physics. The approach is also relevant for atomic systems such as Helium atoms (Bazak, 2020) and ultra-cold atomic gases (Stewart, 2010).
