# The Early History of the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> We present the historical antecedents to the field of relativistic heavy ion physics, beginning with early attempts to model the strong interaction and ending with the endorsement of a relativistic heavy ion collider in the 1983 U.S. Long-Range Plan for Nuclear Science. Particular attention is paid to two major themes: 1) A program to study high density states of nuclear matter emerging from the 1974 Bear Mountain conference and 2) Efforts to understand the predictions of QCD for matter at high densities and/or temperatures.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the field of relativistic heavy ion physics emerged from two independent but converging threads: a long, evolutionary experimental program studying multiparticle production (tracing back to cosmic ray studies in the 1930s) and a revolutionary theoretical development from 1973 to 1983 that shifted focus from exotic hadronic states to the quark-gluon plasma, with the convergence culminating in the 1983 U.S. Long-Range Plan for Nuclear Science.

## Method

The authors provide a historical review organized around two themes: first, the experimental observations of particle production via the strong interaction from the 1930s to the early 1980s, including cosmic ray studies, fixed-target experiments at Fermilab and the CERN SPS, and collider studies at the CERN ISR; second, the theoretical progress from 1973 to 1983 covering the development of QCD, lattice gauge theory calculations, and phenomenological models of high-density nuclear matter. ## Key numbers

- The Hagedorn statistical bootstrap model described transverse momentum distributions as \( \frac{dn}{p_T dp_T} \sim \sqrt{m_T} \ e^{-m_T / T_0} \) with \( T_0 =158 \pm 3\) MeV, interpreted as the highest temperature allowed in nature prior to QCD.
- Heavy ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC routinely create matter at temperatures larger than \(10^{12}\) K (\(\sim 100\) MeV) and pressures exceeding \(10^{30}\) atmospheres (0.6 GeV/cm\(^3\)).
- The Bevalac provided beams of heavy nuclei with 1-2 GeV/nucleon from 1974 to 1993.
- Chapline and Nauenberg (1977) found that a phase transition to a deconfined state would require densities as high as 10-20 times normal nuclear density \(\rho_0\).
- The CERN ISR operated with p+p collisions at \(\sqrt{s} =53\) GeV beginning in 1971.
- The predicted energy density for the planned RHIC collider was determined from nuclear stopping power measurements in the late 1970s/early 1980s.

## Context

This review extends the historical narrative of relativistic heavy ion physics from Fermi's 1950 statistical model and Landau's 1953 hydrodynamic approach through the 1974 Bear Mountain conference, which focused on pion condensation, nuclear shock waves, and strange nuclei without mentioning quarks or gluons. It contrasts the evolutionary experimental path (cosmic ray studies, Glauber model development by Bialas, Bleszynski, and Czyz, Gottfried's Energy Flux Cascade Model) with the revolutionary theoretical period (1973-1983) that produced QCD, lattice calculations by Creutz (1980), and the first numerical studies of the deconfinement phase transition by McLerran and Svetitsky, Kuti et al., and Engels et al. The paper enables future work by documenting how the convergence of these threads established the science case for RHIC and the heavy ion programs at Brookhaven and CERN.
