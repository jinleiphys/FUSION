# Identifying the nature of the QCD transition in heavy-ion collisions with deep learning

> In this proceeding, we review our recent work using deep convolutional neural network (CNN) to identify the nature of the QCD transition in a hybrid modeling of heavy-ion collisions. Within this hybrid model, a viscous hydrodynamic model is coupled with a hadronic cascade "after-burner". As a binary classification setup, we employ two different types of equations of state (EoS) of the hot medium in the hydrodynamic evolution. The resulting final-state pion spectra in the transverse momentum and azimuthal angle plane are fed to the neural network as the input data in order to distinguish different EoS. To probe the effects of the fluctuations in the event-by-event spectra, we explore different scenarios for the input data and make a comparison in a systematic way. We observe a clear hierarchy in the predictive power when the network is fed with the event-by-event, cascade-coarse-grained and event-fine-averaged spectra. The carefully-trained neural network can extract high-level features from pion spectra to identify the nature of the QCD transition in a realistic simulation scenario.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a deep convolutional neural network can distinguish between a crossover and a first-order QCD equation of state from final-state pion spectra even after including hadronization, hadronic cascade, and resonance decays in a hybrid model, but the predictive accuracy depends strongly on how much event-by-event fluctuation is removed from the input spectra.

## Method

The authors used the iEBE-VISHNU hybrid model, which couples viscous hydrodynamics with the UrQMD hadronic cascade afterburner, to simulate heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC energies. They generated pion spectra at mid-rapidity as 24x24 histograms in transverse momentum and azimuthal angle, and trained a deep convolutional neural network as a binary classifier to distinguish between a crossover equation of state from lattice QCD and a first-order equation of state with a Maxwell construction at T_c = 165 MeV. To test robustness, they varied the switching temperature T_sw between hydrodynamics and cascade (137 MeV and above 150 MeV) and compared three input scenarios: event-by-event spectra, cascade-coarse-grained spectra averaged over 30 cascade runs, and event-fine-averaged spectra averaged over 30 events in a 1% centrality bin.

## Key numbers

* The CNN achieved approximately 80% validation accuracy when using event-by-event pion spectra in the late-transition scenario (T_sw = 137 MeV).
* The CNN achieved approximately 90% validation accuracy when using cascade-coarse-grained spectra (averaged over 30 cascade runs) in the late-transition scenario.
* The CNN achieved approximately 99% validation accuracy when using 30-events-fine-averaged spectra in the late-transition scenario, matching the pure hydrodynamic result from Pang et al. (2018).
* The first-order equation of state used a transition temperature T_c = 165 MeV.
* The input histograms used 24 p_T-bins and 24 Phi-bins for pions at mid-rapidity |y| <= 1.

## Context

This work extends the pure hydrodynamic study of Pang et al. (2018), which achieved ~95% accuracy in identifying the QCD transition using deep learning, by adding a more realistic hadronic cascade afterburner via the iEBE-VISHNU hybrid model of Shen et al. (2016). The results show that the imprint of the equation of state survives stochastic hadronic dynamics, but fluctuations from finite particle number and resonance decays degrade classification accuracy unless mitigated by averaging. This enables future attempts to apply similar deep learning methods to experimental data from RHIC and LHC, though the authors note generalization to other simulation models and experimental data remains an open question.
