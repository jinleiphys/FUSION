# Sensitivity of the C and O production on the 3-alpha rate

> We investigate the dependence of the carbon and oxygen production in stars on the 3-alpha rate by varying the energy of the 02+-state of 12C and determine the resulting yields for a selection of low-mass, intermediate-mass, and massive stars. The yields are obtained using modern stellar evolution codes that follow the entire evolution of massive stars, including the supernova explosion, and consider in detail the 3rd dredge-up process during the thermally pulsating asymptotic giant branch of low-mass and intermediate-mass stars. Our results show that the C and O production in massive stars depends strongly on the initial mass, and that it is crucial to follow the entire evolution. A rather strong C production during the He-shell flashes compared to quiescent He burning leads to a lower sensitivity of the C and O production in low-mass and intermediate-mass stars on the 3-alpha-rate than predicted in our previous work. In particular, the C production of intermediate-mass stars seems to have a maximum close to the actual value of the 02+ energy level of 12C.

## Key claim
The sensitivity of carbon and oxygen production in low-mass and intermediate-mass stars to the 3-alpha rate is lower than previously predicted (Schlattl et al. 2000), because strong carbon production during He-shell flashes partially compensates for changes in the quiescent helium-burning rate. The carbon production of intermediate-mass stars appears to have a maximum close to the actual value of the 0+2 energy level of 12C.

## Method
The authors used two modern stellar evolution codes: the KEPLER code for 15 and 25 solar mass massive stars, following evolution through core collapse and supernova explosion, and the Garching stellar evolution code for 1.3 and 5 solar mass low- and intermediate-mass stars through the thermally pulsating asymptotic giant branch. They varied the 0+2 resonance energy of 12C by ±100 keV for massive stars and by five different values for intermediate-mass stars, then computed the resulting carbon and oxygen yields in the stellar ejecta.

## Key numbers
- For the 15 solar mass star with ΔE_R = -100 keV: carbon yield after supernova = 0.36 solar masses; oxygen yield = 0.06 solar masses
- For the 15 solar mass star with ΔE_R = +100 keV: carbon yield after supernova = 0.02 solar masses; oxygen yield = 0.33 solar masses
- For the 25 solar mass star with ΔE_R = -100 keV: carbon yield after supernova = 2.57 solar masses; oxygen yield = 0.81 solar masses
- For the 25 solar mass star with ΔE_R = +100 keV: carbon yield after supernova = 0.02 solar masses; oxygen yield = 1.64 solar masses
- Central carbon mass fraction after core helium depletion in the 15 solar mass standard case: 21.47%; with ΔE_R = -100 keV: 93.03%; with ΔE_R = +100 keV: less than 0.01%
- Central oxygen mass fraction after core helium depletion in the 15 solar mass standard case: 76.16%; with ΔE_R = -100 keV: 4.68%; with ΔE_R = +100 keV: 49.76%
- The modern experimental value of the 0+2 resonance energy is 379.47 ± 0.15 keV above the ground state of three alpha particles

## Context
This work extends the earlier study by Schlattl et al. (2000, Paper I) by following the full stellar evolution through supernova explosion and including detailed third dredge-up during the thermally pulsating AGB phase, which were neglected previously. The results weaken the fine-tuning arguments for carbon and oxygen production presented in that earlier work, showing that the suppression of C and O is smaller and depends on initial mass. The findings are relevant to discussions of varying fundamental constants, such as the fine-structure constant variations reported by Murphy et al. (2001), and provide constraints for galacto-chemical evolution models on allowable variations in the 3-alpha resonance energy.
