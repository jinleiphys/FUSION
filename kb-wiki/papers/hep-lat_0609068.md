# The QCD transition temperature: results with physical masses in the continuum limit

> The transition temperature ($T_c$) of QCD is determined by Symanzik improved gauge and stout-link improved staggered fermionic lattice simulations. We use physical masses both for the light quarks ($m_{ud}$) and for the strange quark ($m_s$). Four sets of lattice spacings ($N_t$=4,6,8 and 10) were used to carry out a continuum extrapolation. It turned out that only $N_t$=6,8 and 10 can be used for a controlled extrapolation, $N_t$=4 is out of the scaling region. Since the QCD transition is a non-singular cross-over there is no unique $T_c$. Thus, different observables lead to different numerical $T_c$ values even in the continuum and thermodynamic limit. The peak of the renormalized chiral susceptibility predicts $T_c$=151(3)(3) MeV, wheres $T_c$-s based on the strange quark number susceptibility and Polyakov loops result in 24(4) MeV and 25(4) MeV larger values, respectively. Another consequence of the cross-over is the non-vanishing width of the peaks even in the thermodynamic limit, which we also determine. These numbers are attempted to be the full result for the $T$$\neq$0 transition, though other lattice fermion formulations (e.g. Wilson) are needed to cross-check them.

## Key claim

The QCD transition is a non-singular cross-over, so there is no unique transition temperature; different observables yield different numerical \(T_c\) values even in the continuum and thermodynamic limit, and the paper reports these values with the caveat that other lattice fermion formulations (e.g. Wilson) are needed to cross-check them.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD simulations using a tree-level Symanzik improved gauge action and stout-link improved staggered fermions, with physical light and strange quark masses tuned to reproduce experimental \(m_K/f_K\) and \(m_K/m_\pi\) ratios to better than 2%. They used four temporal lattice spacings (\(N_t = 4, 6, 8, 10\)) but excluded \(N_t = 4\) from the continuum extrapolation because it was outside the \(a^2\) scaling region, performing the continuum extrapolation with \(N_t = 6, 8, 10\). The transition temperature was determined from the peak of the renormalized chiral susceptibility and from the inflection points of the strange quark number susceptibility and the renormalized Polyakov loop.

## Key numbers

* Chiral susceptibility peak: \(T_c = 151(3)(3)\ \text{MeV}\) (first error from \(T\neq0\) analysis, second from \(T=0\) scale setting).
* Strange quark number susceptibility inflection point: \(T_c = 175(2)(4)\ \text{MeV}\), which is \(24(4)\ \text{MeV}\) larger than the chiral susceptibility value.
* Renormalized Polyakov loop inflection point: \(T_c = 176(3)(4)\ \text{MeV}\), which is \(25(4)\ \text{MeV}\) larger than the chiral susceptibility value.
* Width of the chiral susceptibility peak: \(\Delta T_c = 28(5)(1)\ \text{MeV}\).
* Width of the strange quark number susceptibility peak: \(\Delta T_c = 42(4)(1)\ \text{MeV}\).
* Width of the Polyakov loop peak: \(\Delta T_c = 38(5)(1)\ \text{MeV}\).

## Context

This work extends earlier staggered and Wilson fermion studies (e.g. Karsch 2000, Bernard 1997, Ali Khan 2000, Bernard 2004, Cheng 2006) by using physical quark masses, an exact simulation algorithm, and a controlled continuum extrapolation from lattice spacings small enough to lie in the \(a^2\) scaling region, thereby eliminating four serious limitations of prior calculations: unphysical spectra, uncontrolled continuum extrapolation, ambiguous scale setting, and the assumption of a singular transition. The results enable precise comparisons with other lattice fermion formulations (e.g. Wilson) and provide the absolute scale for the QCD cross-over at zero chemical potential, which is directly relevant for early-universe cosmology and heavy-ion collision phenomenology at RHIC, LHC, and FAIR.
