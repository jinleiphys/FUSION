# Quantum Statistical Model of Nuclear Multifragmentation in the Canonical Ensemble Method

> A quantum statistical model of nuclear multifragmentation is proposed. The recurrence equation method used within the canonical ensemble makes the model solvable and transparent to physical assumptions and allows to get results without involving the Monte Carlo technique. The model exhibits the first order phase transition. Quantum statistics effects are clearly seen on the microscopic level of occupation numbers but are almost washed out for global thermodynamic variables and the averaged observables studied. In the latter case, the recurrence relations for multiplicity distributions of both intermediate-mass and all fragments are derived and the specific changes in the shape of multiplicity distributions in the narrow region of the transition temperature is stressed. The temperature domain favorable to search for the HBT effect is noted.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a quantum statistical model (QSM) of nuclear multifragmentation that uses recurrence equations within the canonical ensemble to exactly conserve baryon number, avoiding Monte Carlo techniques. The authors claim that quantum statistics effects are clearly visible at the microscopic level of occupation numbers but are almost completely washed out for global thermodynamic variables and averaged observables.

## Method

The authors develop a canonical ensemble formalism for an ideal gas mixture of Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac fragments (nucleons and clusters) using recursive relations for the partition function and occupation number moments, derived from the multinomial sum representation of the quantum gas. The model treats fragments as non-interacting quantum particles in a finite volume, with the total nucleon number exactly conserved, and the partition function for each fragment species is computed recursively via the recurrence relation Q(N_i) = (1/N_i) * sum_{l=1}^{N_i} f_{il} Q(N_i-l), where f_{il} contains the quantum statistics sign and the single-particle level sum. The approach is then applied to nuclear multifragmentation by summing over all possible fragment partitions, following the physical approximations of earlier statistical fragmentation models such as Bondorf (1995).

## Key numbers

- The excitation energy range of interest for the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition is about 5-10 AMeV, as stated in the introduction.
- The single-particle momentum grid step is Delta p^{(a)} = 2 pi hbar / L, where L = V^{1/3}, defining the discrete momentum states.
- The recurrence relation for the partition function is Q_N = (1/N) * sum_{j=1}^{N} f_j Q_{N-j}, with Q_0 = 1, valid for both Bose and Fermi statistics.
- For the Boltzmann gas limit, the partition function reduces to Q_N^B = omega^N / N!, where omega = f_1 = sum_p exp(-beta epsilon_p).
- The correlation coefficient eta for the Fermi gas is eta = -(1/N) * sum_p <nu_p>_N^2, and at T=0, eta = -1 for all statistics (Bose, Fermi, Boltzmann).

## Context

This work extends the statistical treatment of nuclear multifragmentation from the grand canonical ensemble approaches used by earlier authors such as Sneppen (1981), Hufner and Snell (1988), and Gulminelli (1997) to a canonical ensemble that exactly conserves baryon number. It also generalizes the classical Boltzmann fragment treatment of Bondorf (1995) and Chase (1949, 1952) to include quantum statistics for both bosons and fermions. The model enables analytical calculation of thermodynamic quantities and multiplicity distributions without Monte Carlo methods, and identifies the temperature domain favorable for searching for the Hanbury-Brown-Twiss (HBT) effect in fragmenting nuclear systems.
