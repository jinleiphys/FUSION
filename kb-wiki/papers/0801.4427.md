# On the physics behind the form factor ratio $μ_p G_E^p (Q^2) / G_M^p (Q^2)$

> We point out that there exist two natural definitions of the nucleon magnetization densities : the density $ρ_M^K (r)$ introduced in Kelly's phenomenological analysis and theoretically more standard one $ρ_M (r)$. We can derive an explicit analytical relation between them, although Kelly's density is more useful to disentangle the physical origin of the different $Q^2$ dependence of the Sachs electric and magnetic form factors of the nucleon. We evaluate both of $ρ_M (r)$ and $ρ_M^K (r)$ as well as the charge density $ρ_{ch}(r)$ of the proton within the framework of the chiral quark soliton model, to find a noticeable qualitative difference between $ρ_{ch}(r)$ and $ρ_M^K (r)$, which is just consistent with Kelly's result obtained from the empirical information on the Sachs electric and magnetic form factors of the proton.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the observed faster falloff of the proton's electric form factor compared to its magnetic form factor at high momentum transfer originates from a qualitative difference between the intrinsic charge density and the intrinsic magnetization density (as defined by Kelly) in the nucleon rest frame, with the qualification that the model predictions are parameter-free for the intrinsic densities but rely on an approximate treatment of Lorentz boost effects for the final form factors.

## Method

The authors use the chiral quark soliton model (CQSM) with a single parameter (dynamical quark mass M = 375 MeV) to compute the intrinsic charge density ρ_ch(r) and the standard magnetization density ρ_m(r) in the nucleon rest frame. They then derive and apply an analytical relation to convert ρ_m(r) into Kelly's magnetization density ρ_m^K(r), enabling a direct comparison of the two intrinsic densities without Lorentz boost complications. Finally, they compute the Sachs form factors G_E(Q^2) and G_M(Q^2) by applying a simple Lorentz contraction prescription (with λ_E = λ_M = 0 and a fitted boost mass M_B ≈ 1.2 GeV) to the intrinsic densities.

## Key numbers

- The dynamical quark mass used in the CQSM is M = 375 MeV.
- The boost mass parameter fitted to reproduce the form factor ratio trend is M_B ≈ 1.2 GeV.
- The first zero of the electric form factor G_E(Q^2) appears around Q^2 ≈ 7 GeV^2, while G_M(Q^2) remains positive in the investigated Q^2 range.
- The dipole mass that reproduces the average Q^2-dependence of the theoretical form factors is M_D^2 ≈ 0.62 GeV^2, compared to the standard empirical value of M_D^2 = 0.71 GeV^2.
- The peak of the r^2-weighted Kelly magnetization density r^2 ρ_m^K(r) is located at smaller r than the charge density r^2 ρ_ch(r), with the crossover between the two densities occurring in the range 0.5 fm ≲ r ≲ 1.0 fm.

## Context

This work extends Kelly's phenomenological analysis (Kelly, 2002), which extracted intrinsic charge and magnetization densities from empirical form factor data, by providing a theoretical calculation of those same densities within the CQSM framework. It contrasts with Holzwarth's approach (Holzwarth, 1996; 2002-2005), which used a generalized Skyrme model with many adjustable parameters to precisely reproduce form factors, whereas the present study uses a nearly parameter-free model to understand the qualitative origin of the form factor ratio behavior. The results support the interpretation that the faster falloff of G_E relative to G_M is driven by the broader spatial extent of the charge density compared to the magnetization density, and they enable future work to connect this coordinate-space picture to the breakdown of the perturbative QCD counting rule for the form factor ratio.
