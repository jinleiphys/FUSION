# Bayesian Inference of High-density Nuclear Symmetry Energy from Radii of Canonical Neutron Stars

> The radius $R_{1.4}$ of neutron stars (NSs) with a mass of 1.4 M$_{\odot}$ has been extracted consistently in many recent studies in the literature. Using representative $R_{1.4}$ data, we infer high-density nuclear symmetry energy $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$ and the associated nucleon specific energy $E_0(ρ)$ in symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) within a Bayesian statistical approach using an explicitly isospin-dependent parametric Equation of State (EOS) for nucleonic matter. We found that: (1) The available astrophysical data can already improve significantly our current knowledge about the EOS in the density range of $ρ_0-2.5ρ_0$. In particular, the symmetry energy at twice the saturation density $ρ_0$ of nuclear matter is determined to be $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(2ρ_0)$ =39.2$_{-8.2}^{+12.1}$ MeV at 68\% confidence level. (2) A precise measurement of the $R_{1.4}$ alone with a 4\% 1$σ$ statistical error but no systematic error will not improve much the constraints on the EOS of dense neutron-rich nucleonic matter compared to what we extracted from using the available radius data. (3) The $R_{1.4}$ radius data and other general conditions, such as the observed NS maximum mass and causality condition introduce strong correlations for the high-order EOS parameters. Consequently, the high-density behavior of $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$ inferred depends strongly on how the high-density SNM EOS $E_0(ρ)$ is parameterized, and vice versa. (4) The value of the observed maximum NS mass and whether it is used as a sharp cut-off for the minimum maximum mass or through a Gaussian distribution affect significantly the lower boundaries of both the $E_0(ρ)$ and $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$ only at densities higher than about $2.5ρ_0$.

## Key claim

Using three representative radius measurements of canonical (1.4 M☉) neutron stars, the authors infer that the nuclear symmetry energy at twice saturation density is constrained to 39.2 MeV with asymmetric error bars at 68% confidence, but caution that the high-density behavior of both the symmetric nuclear matter energy and the symmetry energy depends strongly on how the symmetric nuclear matter equation of state is parameterized, and vice versa.

## Method

The authors performed a Bayesian inference using a Markov-Chain Monte Carlo approach with an explicitly isospin-dependent parametric equation of state for nucleonic matter (neutrons, protons, electrons, and muons). They sampled six equation-of-state parameters (K0, J0, Ksym, Jsym, L, and Esym(ρ0)) within prior ranges informed by terrestrial nuclear experiments, then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations to generate mass-radius sequences and computed the likelihood of each parameter set against three observed R1.4 values from GW170817 and quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries.

## Key numbers

* The symmetry energy at twice saturation density is determined to be Esym(2ρ0) = 39.2 (+12.1, -8.2) MeV at 68% confidence level.
* The most probable value of the slope parameter L is 39 (+19, -0) MeV at 68% confidence boundaries.
* The most probable value of the curvature parameter Ksym is -230 (+90, -50) MeV at 68% confidence boundaries.
* The most probable value of the skewness parameter Jsym is 800 (+0, -360) MeV at 68% confidence boundaries.
* The most probable value of the symmetric nuclear matter incompressibility K0 is 222 (+26, -0) MeV at 68% confidence boundaries.
* The three real radius data points have 6-13% 1σ statistical errors with mean values varying by about 1 km, representing approximately a 10% systematic error.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Zhang et al. (2018, 2019) that fixed low-order equation-of-state parameters and inverted observables in a three-dimensional high-density parameter space, by now treating all six equation-of-state parameters as free parameters in a full Bayesian framework. The results provide posterior probability distribution functions that can be used to screen predictions of nuclear many-body theories and establish a reference for measuring progress from upcoming observations with NICER, Athena, eXTP, and other facilities.
