# Systematic Uncertainties in the Analysis of the Reactor Neutrino Anomaly

> We examine uncertainties in the analysis of the reactor neutrino anomaly, wherein it is suggested that only about 94% of the emitted antineutrino flux was detected in short baseline experiments. We find that the form of the corrections that lead to the anomaly are very uncertain for the 30% of the flux that arises from forbidden decays. This uncertainty was estimated in four ways, is as large as the size of the anomaly, and is unlikely to be reduced without accurate direct measurements of the antineutrino flux. Given the present lack of detailed knowledge of the structure of the forbidden transitions, it is not possible to convert the measured aggregate fission beta spectra to antineutrino spectra to the accuracy needed to infer an anomaly. Neutrino physics conclusions based on the original anomaly need to be revisited, as do oscillation analyses that assumed that the antineutrino flux is known to better than approximately 4%.

## Key claim

The reactor neutrino anomaly, a claimed ~6% deficit in detected antineutrinos relative to predictions, cannot be reliably inferred because the corrections for the 30% of the flux arising from forbidden decays are uncertain by an amount as large as the anomaly itself, and this uncertainty cannot be reduced without direct antineutrino flux measurements.

## Method

The authors derived first-principles analytic expressions for finite-size (FS) and weak-magnetism (WM) corrections to allowed Gamow-Teller (GT) transitions, and for shape factors and WM corrections to first-forbidden GT transitions. They applied these corrections to the measured aggregate fission beta spectrum of ²³⁵U using four different prescriptions for treating forbidden transitions: assuming all transitions are allowed; allowing a mix of allowed and forbidden; selecting 30% forbidden at equal energy intervals; and selecting 30% forbidden biased toward higher energies. They also examined the effect of treating forbidden transitions with different operators ([Σ,r]⁰⁻, [Σ,r]¹⁻, [Σ,r]²⁻) in a summation over the ENDF/B-VII.1 decay library, and evaluated the bi-variant function k(E_e, E_ν) and the ratio of partial derivatives T to quantify the uncertainty.

## Key numbers

- The forbidden transitions contribute approximately 30% of the total fission antineutrino flux.
- Different treatments of forbidden transitions lead to changes in the antineutrino spectrum relative to the Schreckenbach spectrum ranging from 0% to 4%.
- For the assumption of all allowed transitions, the number of detectable antineutrinos increases by 2.2% relative to Schreckenbach; including 25% forbidden transitions yields a 0.06% increase.
- The change in detectable antineutrinos relative to Schreckenbach for different forbidden operators in the summed actinide spectrum is -0.75% (0⁻ operator), 5.8% (1⁻ operator), and 1.85% (2⁻ operator).
- Taking fission fragment Z values directly from ENDF/B-VII.1 instead of the ILL parameterization changes the antineutrino spectrum by less than 1% for E_ν ≤ 7 MeV and less than 1.5% for E_ν > 7 MeV.
- No path in the (E_e, E_ν) plane left the function k(E_e, E_ν) unchanged by as little as 5% when assumptions for forbidden transitions were varied.

## Context

This work extends the analyses of Mueller et al. (2011) and Huber (2011), which reexamined the reactor antineutrino flux and identified the reactor neutrino anomaly, and the earlier ILL measurements by Schreckenbach et al. (1985). It contradicts the assumption in those works that the forbidden transitions could be treated with a single prescription (e.g., unique first-forbidden shape factors for all forbidden decays, as in Mueller et al. and Fallot et al.). The paper enables a reassessment of sterile neutrino oscillation analyses that assumed the antineutrino flux is known to better than approximately 4%, and it identifies the need for direct antineutrino flux measurements or improved nuclear structure data for the dominant forbidden transitions.
