# Entanglement study in the island of inversion region using \textit{ab initio} approach

> Quantum entanglement provides a unique perspective for probing nuclear structure. In this work, we employ quantum entanglement measures, including proton-neutron entanglement entropy, mutual information, and quantum relative entropy, to investigate the evolution of entanglement patterns as we approach neutron-rich nuclei. The study is carried out in the vicinity of the $N=20$ island of inversion region consisting of even-$A$ Ne, Mg, and Si isotopes, and also for isotones corresponding to $N=20$. The state-of-the-art \textit{ab initio} valence space in-medium similarity renormalization group method has been used for this purpose. We have highlighted the role of proton-neutron entanglement entropy in the formation of the island of inversion region. Mutual information provides insight into the strength of correlations between proton-proton, neutron-neutron, and proton-neutron single-particle states. While these correlations are relatively weak between protons and neutrons in the ground states, they become comparable to like-particle correlations in excited states. The quantum relative entropy is also studied between $0^+$ and $2^+$ states of the Ne, Mg, and Si isotopes, as well as $N=20$ isotones, using the Kullback-Leibler divergence and Jensen-Shannon divergence. We have performed these calculations by expressing the nuclear wavefunctions in a Slater-determinant basis and analyzing them through complementary partitions, including proton-neutron and mode-resolved factorizations.

## Key claim

The paper claims that proton-neutron entanglement entropy can serve as a probe for the emergence of the island of inversion region around N=20, but this enhancement of entropy is conditional on the inclusion of cross-shell excitations into the pf shell; within a single major shell, entropy would instead decrease toward neutron-rich nuclei as previously reported by Carl (2023).

## Method

The authors performed ab initio valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) calculations using the chiral EM1.8/2.0 interaction (N3LO NN evolved to λ=1.8 fm⁻¹ and N2LO NNN with cutoff Λ=2.0 fm⁻¹) in a harmonic oscillator basis at ħω=16 MeV with emax=12 and E3max=24. They diagonalized the effective Hamiltonians in the sdpf model space (sd shell for protons, sdpf shell for neutrons) above a ¹⁶O core using the BIGSTICK code, then computed proton-neutron entanglement entropy, mutual information between single-particle orbitals, and quantum relative entropy (Kullback-Leibler and Jensen-Shannon divergences) from the wavefunction coefficients expressed in a Slater-determinant basis.

## Key numbers

- Proton-neutron entanglement entropy S_pn was computed for isotopic chains ²²⁻³⁴Ne, ²⁴⁻³⁶Mg, and ²⁶⁻³⁸Si for the first 0⁺, 2⁺, and 4⁺ states, and for N=20 isotones from ²⁹F to ³⁵P
- Mutual information was computed for isotopic chains ²⁴⁻³⁴Ne, ²⁶⁻³⁶Mg, and ³⁰⁻³⁸Si for the first 0⁺ and 2⁺ states
- The valence space consisted of 12 proton single-particle states and 24 neutron single-particle states in the M-scheme basis
- For ground states, proton-neutron mutual information was approximately an order of magnitude smaller than like-particle correlations
- For 2⁺ states, the proton-neutron sector colorbar scale was restricted to 0.3, comparable to the like-particle scale of 0.5
- IMSRG calculations used ħω=16 MeV with e_max=12 and E3max=24

## Context

This work extends previous shell-model entanglement studies by Carl (2023) and others from single-major-shell calculations to the full sdpf model space using ab initio VS-IMSRG interactions, enabling a microscopic description of the island of inversion without empirical adjustments. The results demonstrate that proton-neutron entanglement entropy can identify the onset of intruder configurations at N=20, and the mutual information analysis reveals how cross-shell excitations shift correlations from sd-shell orbitals to pf-shell orbitals. This enables future studies using quantum information measures to probe structural evolution in exotic nuclei and to guide truncation schemes for large-scale nuclear structure calculations.
