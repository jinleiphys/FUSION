# Phase Transitions and Chaos in Long-Range Models of Coupled Oscillators

> We study the chaotic behavior of the synchronization phase transition in the Kuramoto model. We discuss the relationship with analogous features found in the Hamiltonian Mean Field (HMF) model. Our numerical results support the connection between the two models, which can be considered as limiting cases (dissipative and conservative, respectively) of a more general dynamical system of damped-driven coupled pendula. We also show that, in the Kuramoto model, the shape of the phase transition and the largest Lyapunov exponent behavior are strongly dependent on the distribution of the natural frequencies.

# Phase Transitions and Chaos in Long-Range Models of Coupled Oscillators

## Key claim

The Kuramoto model and the Hamiltonian Mean Field (HMF) model, despite being respectively dissipative and conservative, share a common microscopic chaotic signature near their phase transitions: the largest Lyapunov exponent peaks around the critical point. However, the shape of the phase transition and the Lyapunov exponent behavior in the Kuramoto model depend strongly on the distribution of natural frequencies, ranging from a continuous 2nd-order-like transition (for Gaussian distributions) to an abrupt 1st-order-like transition (for uniform distributions).

## Method

The authors performed large-scale numerical simulations of the Kuramoto model for systems of N=20000 to N=50000 oscillators, using both Gaussian and uniform distributions of natural frequencies, and compared the results with equilibrium microcanonical simulations of the HMF model for N=1000 to N=10000. They computed the asymptotic order parameter r and the largest Lyapunov exponent (LLE) as functions of the coupling strength K, following the standard Benettin algorithm for Lyapunov exponents. For the Kuramoto model, they also varied the standard deviation sigma of bounded Gaussian distributions (omega in [-3,3]) to continuously interpolate between Gaussian-like and uniform-like frequency distributions.

## Key numbers

- For the HMF model, the critical temperature is T_C = 1/2, corresponding to critical energy density U = 4/3.
- For the Kuramoto model with a Gaussian distribution (mean 0, variance 1), the critical coupling is K_C = 2/(pi * g(0)) = 1.59617...
- For the Kuramoto model with a uniform distribution in omega in [-2,2], the critical coupling is K_C = 8/pi.
- For the Kuramoto model with a uniform distribution in omega in [-3,3], the theoretical critical coupling is K_C = 12/pi.
- System sizes simulated for the Kuramoto model: N = 20000, 30000, 40000, and 50000.
- System sizes simulated for the HMF model: N = 1000, 5000, and 10000.
- Each data point in the figures represents an average over 10 realizations.
- In the HMF model, the LLE goes to zero above the transition as N^{-1/3}.

## Context

This work extends the connection between the Kuramoto model and the HMF model previously explored by the same authors (Miritello, Pluchino, Rapisarda, 2008) and by Tanaka and colleagues (Tanaka, 2004), who studied a generalized Kuramoto model with similarities to HMF behavior. The results confirm earlier findings by Pazo (2005) regarding the dependence of the transition order on the frequency distribution, and extend the Lyapunov exponent analysis of Maistrenko and colleagues (Maistrenko, 2004), who computed the full Lyapunov spectrum only for small systems (N up to 200) and for a single frequency distribution. The paper enables a unified dynamical understanding of phase transitions in long-range interacting systems, showing that the Kuramoto model and HMF model can be seen as limiting cases (overdamped and conservative, respectively) of a more general system of damped-driven coupled pendula. The phase diagram of K versus sigma for the Kuramoto model is reported for the first time, analogous to the U versus M_0 phase diagram shown for the HMF model by Antoniazzi and colleagues (Antoniazzi, 2007).
