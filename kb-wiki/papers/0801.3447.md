# Scalar Nature of the Nuclear Density Functional

# Scalar Nature of the Nuclear Density Functional

## Key claim

Because the nuclear Hamiltonian is rotationally invariant, the density functional for nuclei can be expressed using only two scalar densities (proton and neutron monopole densities), reducing practical calculations to one-dimensional radial problems. This holds formally for exact energies, but at the cost of ignoring density multipoles other than monopoles, and requires a harmonic trap to link the internal density (measured from the center of mass) to the laboratory-frame density.

## Method

The paper derives a density functional theory (DFT) for nuclei by applying a density-constrained search, restricting the many-body density operator to be a scalar under rotations. A harmonic trap is added to the Hamiltonian to localize the center of mass, allowing a convolution relation between the laboratory-frame density and the internal density defined via Jacobi coordinates. The functional is then defined in the laboratory radial frame using scalar proton and neutron densities, with the ground-state energy obtained by minimizing over densities subject to proton and neutron number constraints.

## Key numbers

- The center-of-mass Gaussian width is given by \( b = [\hbar/(A m \omega)]^{1/2} \), where \( \omega = \sqrt{K A} \) and \( K \) is an arbitrary trap constant.
- The convolution linking laboratory density \( \rho(r) \) to internal density \( \sigma(s) \) is: \( r \rho(r) = \frac{2 \pi^{-1/2} A^3}{(A-1)^3 b} \int_0^\infty ds \, \exp\left[-\frac{r^2+s^2}{b^2}\right] \sinh\left[\frac{2rs}{b^2}\right] s \, \sigma\left[\frac{A}{A-1} s\right] \).
- The ground-state energy relation includes a correction: \( E_{ZN} + \frac{1}{2} \hbar \sqrt{K A} = \text{Inf}_\rho \, \mathcal{F}[\rho] \), with density normalizations \( \int_0^\infty r^2 dr \, \rho_p(r) = Z \) and \( \int_0^\infty r^2 dr \, \rho_n(r) = N \).

## Context

This work extends the Hohenberg-Kohn (1964) theorem and the constrained-search formulation of Levy (1979) and Lieb (1983) to the nuclear many-body problem, incorporating rotational invariance to reduce the density functional to scalar densities. It builds on prior work by Dobaczewski and by Giraud (2005) on center-of-mass corrections and density convolutions, and references Gorling (1992) for symmetrized DFT approaches. The dimensional reduction is intended to simplify the design of nuclear density functionals, particularly for exotic nuclei near the neutron drip line where halos are difficult to describe, and may also apply to non-local and quasi-local versions of DFT derived from Skyrme force models.
