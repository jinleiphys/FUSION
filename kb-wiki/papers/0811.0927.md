# Emergence of quantum mechanics from classical statistics

> The conceptual setting of quantum mechanics is subject to an ongoing debate from its beginnings until now. The consequences of the apparent differences between quantum statistics and classical statistics range from the philosophical interpretations to practical issues as quantum computing. In this note we demonstrate how quantum mechanics can emerge from classical statistical systems. We discuss conditions and circumstances for this to happen. Quantum systems describe isolated subsystems of classical statistical systems with infinitely many states. While infinitely many classical observables "measure" properties of the subsystem and its environment, the state of the subsystem can be characterized by the expectation values of only a few probabilistic observables. They define a density matrix, and all the usual laws of quantum mechanics follow. No concepts beyond classical statistics are needed for quantum physics - the differences are only apparent and result from the particularities of those classical statistical systems which admit a quantum mechanical description. In particular, we show how the non-commuting properties of quantum operators are associated to the use of conditional probabilities within the classical system, and how a unitary time evolution reflects the isolation of the subsystem.

## Key claim

Quantum mechanics can emerge from classical statistical systems, but only for isolated subsystems of classical statistical ensembles with infinitely many states; the apparent differences between quantum and classical statistics result from the particularities of those classical statistical systems that admit a quantum mechanical description, and the non-commuting properties of quantum operators are associated with the use of conditional probabilities within the classical system.

## Method

The author constructs a classical statistical ensemble with infinitely many states labeled by τ, where classical deterministic observables A^(k) take fixed values ±1 in each state τ. By "integrating out" environment degrees of freedom, these classical observables are mapped to probabilistic observables characterized by a spectrum of possible measurement values and associated probabilities. The expectation values ρ_k = ⟨A^(k)⟩ define a density matrix ρ = (1/M)(1 + ρ_k L_k) using SU(M) generators L_k, and the author shows that for ensembles obeying a purity constraint P ≤ M-1, all standard quantum mechanical rules follow, including unitary time evolution and the algebra of quantum observables.

## Context

This work extends the author's prior explicit constructions of classical statistical ensembles equivalent to four-state and two-state quantum mechanics (Wetterich, earlier papers CW1 and CW2). It addresses the Kochen-Specker theorem (Kochen and Specker, 1967) by noting that the non-invertible map from classical to probabilistic observables avoids conflicts, and it addresses Bell's theorem (Bell, 1964) by proposing that quantum correlations rather than classical correlations should be used for describing two measurements. The approach enables a classical statistical interpretation of quantum phenomena including entanglement, teleportation, and quantum cryptography, and suggests that unitary time evolution reflects the isolation of the subsystem as a partial fixed point of a more general classical time evolution that can also describe decoherence and syncoherence.
