# Enhanced E1 transition between weakly-bound excited states in the nucleus 27Ne

> Inspired by the recently-reported strong electric-dipole (E1) transition between the weakly-bound first and second excited states, 3/2- at 765 keV and 1/2+ at 885 keV, in the nucleus 27Ne, the E1 transition is estimated in a model by properly taking into account the effect of both deformation and weakly-bound neutrons. In addition to both the spin-parities, 1/2+ and 3/2-, and observed nearly degenerate energies of the two excited states, the observed order of magnitude of the E1 transition strength between the two states is very naturally explained in the case that these two excited states are prolately deformed, in terms of the transitions between the halo components of the wave functions of the weakly-bound odd-neutrons, s1/2 -> p3/2 and s1/2 -> p1/2, in addition to the large probability of the p3/2 component in the weakly-bound neutron [330 1/2] orbit. The large probability is the result of the shell-structure unique in weakly-bound or resonant neutrons.

## Key claim

The observed order of magnitude of the enhanced E1 transition strength between the 1/2⁺ and 3/2⁻ excited states in ²⁷Ne can be naturally explained if both states are prolately deformed (β ≈ 0.4), with the transition arising primarily from halo components of weakly-bound odd-neutron wave functions (s₁/₂ → p₃/₂ and s₁/₂ → p₁/₂), provided the large probability of the p₃/₂ component in the [330 1/2] orbit is taken into account.

## Method

The author solves coupled differential equations for radial wave functions in coordinate space using an axially-symmetric quadrupole-deformed Woods-Saxon potential, with correct asymptotic behavior for weakly-bound neutrons. The one-particle wave functions for the [200 1/2] and [330 1/2] Nilsson orbits are calculated at the deformation where these levels cross (β = 0.44), and the radial matrix elements of the E1 operator (rY₁₀ and rY₁₁) are evaluated. The calculation includes multiple coupled channels: s₁/₂, d₃/₂, d₅/₂, g₇/₂, and g₉/₂ for the [200 1/2] orbit; p₁/₂, p₃/₂, f₅/₂, f₇/₂, h₉/₂, and h₁₁/₂ for the [330 1/2] orbit.

## Key numbers

- The neutron separation energy Sₙ of the ²⁷Ne ground state is 1.515 MeV; the Sₙ values of the 1/2⁺ and 3/2⁻ excited states are 0.63 MeV and 0.75 MeV, respectively
- The two excited states have excitation energies of 765 keV (3/2⁻) and 885 keV (1/2⁺)
- The calculated radial matrix elements for weakly-bound neutrons at β = 0.44 are: ⟨[330 1/2]|rY₁₀|[200 1/2]⟩ = 0.458 fm and ⟨[330 1/2]|rY₁₁|[200 1/2]⟩ = −0.013 fm
- Using eⁿ_eff(E1) = (Z/A)e, the resulting B(E1; 1/2⁺ → 3/2⁻) = 0.020 e² fm²
- For well-bound neutrons (binding ≈ 8 MeV) at the same deformation β = 0.44, the matrix element is ⟨[330 1/2]|rY₁₀|[200 1/2]⟩ = 0.035 fm, yielding B(E1) = 0.00011 e² fm², more than two orders of magnitude smaller
- The probability of the s₁/₂ component in the weakly-bound [200 1/2] orbit is 0.518; the p₃/₂ component probability in the weakly-bound [330 1/2] orbit is 0.512
- The deformation parameter at level crossing is β = 0.44 for V_WS = −41.0 MeV, and β = 0.42 for V_WS = −43.0 MeV
- The lower limit of the experimental B(E1; 1/2⁺ → 3/2⁻) is 0.030 e² fm² (from Ref. CL18)

## Context

This work explains the unusually strong E1 transition reported by Chen and Liang (2018) between the weakly-bound excited states of ²⁷Ne, which is at least 30 times larger than the E1 transition from the 3/2⁻ state to the 3/2⁺ ground state. The model extends the deformed Woods-Saxon approach previously applied to ¹¹Be by Hamamoto, Sagawa, and Zhang (2007) and to stable deformed nuclei like ²⁵Mg by Bohr and Mottelson (1975). The key insight is that the near-degeneracy of 2p₃/₂ and 1f₇/₂ levels for weakly-bound neutrons, a shell-structure feature unique to nuclei far from stability, produces a large p₃/₂ component in the [330 1/2] orbit, which together with halo effects from the small binding energies (0.63–0.75 MeV) enables the enhanced E1 strength. The work enables future experimental studies of the shapes of these excited states and suggests that similar enhanced E1 transitions may be found in other neutron-rich nuclei with weakly-bound deformed states.
