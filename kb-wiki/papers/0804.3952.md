# Early dissipation and viscosity

> We consider dissipative phenomena due to the relaxation of an initial anisotropic local pressure in the fireball created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, both for the Bjorken boost-invariant case and for the azimuthally symmetric radial expansion with boost-invariance. The resulting increase of the entropy can be counterbalanced by a suitable retuning of the initial temperature. An increase of the transverse collective flow is observed. The influence of the shear viscosity on the longitudinal expansion is also studied. Viscosity reduces the cooling rate from the longitudinal work and counteracts the pressure gradients that accelerate the longitudinal flow.

# Early dissipation and viscosity

## Key claim
Dissipative effects from the relaxation of an initial anisotropic local pressure in the fireball, separate from shear viscosity itself, produce significant entropy increases (up to 30%) and harder transverse momentum spectra, but these effects can be counterbalanced by retuning the initial temperature; the exact impact depends on the relaxation time τ_π, which may differ from the relaxation time near equilibrium used in second-order viscous hydrodynamics.

## Method
The study uses second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamics for both a Bjorken boost-invariant expansion and an azimuthally symmetric radial expansion with boost-invariance. The stress correction Π is modeled with a dynamic equation containing the shear viscosity coefficient η and relaxation time τ_π, where the initial pressure asymmetry is taken as Π(τ₀)=p(τ₀) and allowed to relax exponentially as Π(τ)=p(τ₀)e^{-(τ-τ₀)/τ_π}. The evolution equations are solved for 1+1-dimensional longitudinally expanding systems with finite gradients in space-time rapidity, using initial energy density profiles tuned to reproduce BRAHMS meson rapidity distributions.

## Key numbers
- Entropy increase from initial dissipation can reach up to 30% (Fig. 1, for τ₀=0.2, 0.5, and 1 fm/c with η/s=0.1)
- Initial proper times considered: τ₀=0.2, 0.5, and 1 fm/c
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio η/s=0.2 used for longitudinal dynamics calculations
- For η/s=0.2, the initial energy density at central rapidity must be reduced by a factor of 3 to accommodate dissipative effects
- Relaxation time condition: τ_π < τ₀

## Context
This work extends studies of viscous hydrodynamics in relativistic heavy-ion collisions (Kolb 2003, Huovinen 2003) and second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamics (Israel and Stewart, Muronga 2003) by focusing specifically on early-stage dissipation from initial pressure anisotropy rather than shear viscosity alone. It builds on the observation that initial velocity gradients responsible for shear viscosity corrections are largest at the beginning of expansion (Muronga 2003, Teaney 2003). The results show that early dissipation produces effects similar to shear viscosity, harder transverse momentum spectra, reduced cooling rate, and reduced longitudinal flow acceleration, but requires separate treatment of the relaxation time. This enables more realistic modeling of the full hydrodynamic evolution where both initial dissipation and later-stage shear viscosity (Romatschke 2007, Song 2007) must be included simultaneously.
