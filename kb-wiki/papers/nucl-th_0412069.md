# Shape-phase transitions in nuclei and random interactions

> In these lecture notes I present a short review of nuclear shapes, shape coexistence and shape-phase transitions in the interacting boson model. In a study with random interactions it is shown that the appearance of regular spectral features is a far more common phenomenon than was previously thought. The origin of these features are explained by studying the relation with the underlying geometric shapes.

## Key claim

Regular spectral features such as vibrational and rotational band structures, previously thought to require very specific forms of interactions, appear far more commonly than expected when the interacting boson model is studied with random interactions; the origin of these features is explained by the relation with underlying geometric shapes and critical points.

## Method

The author uses the interacting boson model (IBM) with the most general one- and two-body Hamiltonian, whose nine parameters are taken as independent random numbers on a Gaussian distribution with zero mean. Exact numerical diagonalization is performed for each random Hamiltonian to study energy spectra and electromagnetic transition rates, and the results are analyzed in terms of energy ratios and B(E2) values. The connection to geometric shapes is made via coherent-state potential-energy surfaces and catastrophe theory.

## Key numbers

- The probability distribution of the energy ratio \(R_{4/2} = E(4^+_1)-E(0^+_1) / E(2^+_1)-E(0^+_1)\) shows two pronounced peaks: one at approximately 1.95 (harmonic vibrator value) and a narrower one at approximately 3.35 (rotor value), for boson number \(N = 16\).
- The ratio of B(E2) values for the \(4^+_1 \rightarrow 2^+_1\) and \(2^+_1 \rightarrow 0^+_1\) transitions is 2 for the harmonic oscillator (U(5) limit) and 10/7 for the deformed oscillator and rotor (SO(6) and SU(3) limits) in the large \(N\) limit.
- Ground states with angular momentum \(L=0\) occur in approximately 60-75% of cases across boson numbers, with an enhancement for \(N = 3k\) (multiples of 3).
- The U(5)-SO(6) transition exhibits a second-order phase transition at the critical point \(\eta_c = 1/4\).
- The U(5)-SU(3) transition exhibits a first-order phase transition at the critical point \(\eta_c = 2/9\), with spinodal point \(\eta_s = 0.219\) and antispinodal point \(\eta_a = 1/4\).

## Context

These lecture notes review and extend the work on shape-phase transitions in the IBM originally laid out by Dieperink, Scholten and Iachello (1980) and later revisited by Lopez-Moreno and Castanos (1996) using catastrophe theory. The random interaction studies extend the shell-model work of Johnson, Bertsch and Dean (1998) to the IBM framework, showing that regular spectral features are robust even with random interactions. The results enable further investigation of shape-phase diagrams for the neutron-proton IBM (IBM-2) and provide a geometric interpretation for the prevalence of vibrational and rotational structures in random-interaction studies.
