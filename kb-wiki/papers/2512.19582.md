# Trigonometric continuous-variable gates and hybrid quantum simulations of the sine-Gordon model

> Hybrid qubit-qumode quantum computing platforms provide a natural setting for simulating interacting bosonic quantum field theories. However, existing continuous-variable gate constructions rely predominantly on polynomial functions of canonical quadratures. In this work, we introduce a complementary universality paradigm based on trigonometric continuous-variable gates, which enable a Fourier-like representation of bosonic operators and are particularly well suited for periodic and non-perturbative interactions. We present a deterministic ancilla-based method for implementing unitary and non-unitary trigonometric gates whose arguments are arbitrary Hermitian functions of qumode quadratures. As a concrete application, we develop a hybrid qubit-qumode quantum simulation of the lattice sine-Gordon model. Using these gates, we prepare ground states via quantum imaginary-time evolution, simulate real-time dynamics, compute time-dependent vertex two-point correlation functions, and extract quantum kink profiles under topological boundary conditions. Our results demonstrate that trigonometric continuous-variable gates provide a physically natural framework for simulating interacting field theories on near-term hybrid quantum hardware, while establishing a parallel route to universality beyond polynomial gate constructions. We expect that the trigonometric gates introduced here to find broader applications, including quantum simulations of condensed matter systems, quantum chemistry, and biological models.

## Key claim

Trigonometric continuous-variable gates (exponentials of cosine and sine of Hermitian operators) provide a complementary universality paradigm to polynomial-based CV gates, enabling Fourier-like representations of bosonic operators that are particularly efficient for periodic and non-perturbative interactions, but the construction relies on Trotter approximations and is not exact for finite time steps.

## Method

The authors introduce a deterministic ancilla-based method for implementing unitary trigonometric gates of the form \(e^{-it\cos\hat A}\) and \(e^{-it\sin\hat A}\) by embedding the non-Hermitian operator \(e^{i\hat A}\) into a larger Hilbert space via entanglement with an ancillary qubit, constructing Hermitian and unitary hybrid operators \(\Sigma\) and \(\overline{\Sigma}\) that can be exponentiated using standard Pauli-string exponentiation circuits. Non-unitary trigonometric gates (for imaginary-time evolution) are obtained through an additional ancilla qubit with post-selection. As a concrete application, the lattice sine-Gordon Hamiltonian is mapped onto a hybrid qubit-qumode architecture with one bosonic mode per lattice site, using the Bloch-Messiah decomposition to diagonalize the quadratic part and trigonometric gates for the cosine potential.

## Key numbers

* The sine-Gordon coupling parameter \(\beta\) has special points at \(\beta^2 = 4\pi\) (free massive fermion theory) and \(\beta^2 = 8\pi\) (marginal cosine potential); for \(0 < \beta^2 < 4\pi\) the spectrum contains solitons, antisolitons, and breathers, while for \(4\pi < \beta^2 < 8\pi\) only kinks and antikinks remain.
* The Trotter error for the trigonometric gate construction is \(\mathcal{O}(t^2)\) when using the first-order decomposition \(e^{i\frac{t}{2}\Sigma}\cdot e^{i\frac{t}{2}\overline{\Sigma}} = e^{it\cos\hat A\otimes Z_a} + \mathcal{O}(t^2)\), requiring \(t \ll 1\) for accuracy.
* The lattice sine-Gordon Hamiltonian uses lattice spacing \(a\) (set to unity in the work) with periodic boundary conditions and \(L\) lattice sites, where the gradient term eigenvalues are \(\omega^2_s = 2-2\cos(2\pi s/L)\) for \(s = 0,\dots,L-1\).

## Context

This work extends the polynomial-based continuous-variable universality paradigm established by Lloyd (1999) and Liu (2024) by introducing trigonometric gates that provide a Fourier-type operator basis complementary to Taylor-like expansions. The sine-Gordon simulation builds on prior qubit-based and analog quantum simulation protocols by Roy (2020), Horvath (2021), Wybo (2022, 2023), and Kormos (2022), while the hybrid qubit-qumode approach leverages experimental platforms in trapped ions and superconducting circuits where qubit-qumode interactions have been demonstrated. The trigonometric gates enable simulations of non-perturbative phenomena such as quantum kink profiles under topological boundary conditions and time-dependent vertex two-point correlation functions, with potential applications extending to lattice gauge theories (Jha 2023, Ale 2024, 2025, Crane 2024), condensed matter systems, quantum chemistry, and biological models.
