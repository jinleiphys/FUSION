# Using Neutron Star Observations to Determine Crust Thicknesses, Moments of Inertia, and Tidal Deformabilities

> We perform a systematic assessment of models for the equation of state (EOS) of dense matter in the context of recent neutron star mass and radius measurements to obtain a broad picture of the structure of neutron stars. We demonstrate that currently available neutron star mass and radius measurements provide strong constraints on moments of inertia, tidal deformabilities, and crust thicknesses. A measurement of the moment of inertia of PSR J0737-3039A with 10% error, without any other information from observations, will constrain the EOS over a range of densities to within 50%$-$60%. We find tidal deformabilities between 0.6 and $6\times 10^{36}$ g cm$^{2}$ s$^{2}$ (to 95% confidence) for $M=1.4~\mathrm{M}_{\odot}$, and any measurement which constrains this range will provide an important constraint on dense matter. The crustal fraction of the moment of inertia can be as large as 10% for $M=1.4~\mathrm{M}_{\odot}$ permitting crusts to have a large enough moment of inertia reservoir to explain glitches in the Vela pulsar even with a large amount of superfluid entrainment. Finally, due to the uncertainty in the equation of state, there is at least a 40% variation in the thickness of the crust for a fixed mass and radius, which implies that future simulations of the cooling of a neutron star crust which has been heated by accretion will need to take this variation into account.

# Using Neutron Star Observations to Determine Crust Thicknesses, Moments of Inertia, and Tidal Deformabilities

## Key claim
Current neutron star mass and radius measurements provide strong constraints on moments of inertia, tidal deformabilities, and crust thicknesses, but the results depend on which equation of state (EOS) model is assumed and whether systematic uncertainties in the observations are considered. A measurement of the moment of inertia of PSR J0737-3039A with 10% error, without any other information from observations, will constrain the EOS over a range of densities to within 50%-60%.

## Method
The authors performed Markov chain Monte Carlo simulations using two different EOS models near nuclear saturation density (the Gandolfi-Carlson-Reddy quantum Monte Carlo model and the Hebeler-Lattimer-Pethick-Schwenk chiral effective theory model) combined with two different high-density parametrizations (piecewise polytropes or line segments in the energy density-pressure plane). They used three data sets: current mass and radius observations from photospheric radius expansion bursts and quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries; those observations plus a hypothetical 10% moment of inertia measurement; and only a hypothetical moment of inertia measurement. Moments of inertia were computed using the slow rotation (Hartle-Thorne) approximation, and tidal deformabilities were computed using the correlation from Yagi and Yunes (2013a, 2013b).

## Key numbers
- Tidal deformabilities for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: between 0.6 and 6 × 10^36 g cm^2 s^2 (to 95% confidence) across all models and data sets
- Crustal fraction of the moment of inertia can be as large as 10% for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star
- At least 40% variation in crust thickness for a fixed neutron star mass and radius due to EOS uncertainty
- Moment of inertia for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star from mass-radius data: 60.62 to 77.06 solar mass km^2 (95% confidence, GCR model with Model A)
- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star from mass-radius data: 10.79 to 12.44 km (95% confidence, GCR model with Model A)
- Crust thickness (at transition density 0.08 fm^-3, M=1.4 solar mass): 0.7455 to 1.206 km (95% confidence, GCR model with Model A)
- Pressure at energy density 450 MeV/fm^3 constrained to within 53% by mass-radius data, and to within 55% by a 10% moment of inertia measurement alone

## Context
This work extends previous theoretical predictions of neutron star moments of inertia, crust thicknesses, and tidal polarizabilities that used smaller samples of representative EOSs (Kalogera 1999, Lattimer 2001, Morrison 2004, Bejger 2005, Steiner 2005, Lattimer 2005, Lattimer 2007, Fattoyev 2010, Fattoyev 2013, Lattimer 2013). The results enable future tests: a moment of inertia measurement of PSR J0737-3039A can constrain the EOS independently of mass-radius observations; gravitational wave observations from neutron star mergers can measure tidal deformabilities; and crust cooling simulations of accreting neutron stars must account for the 40% variation in crust thickness. The finding that crustal moment of inertia fractions can reach 10% for 1.4 solar mass neutron stars can resolve the Vela pulsar glitch quandary identified by Link (1999), Andersson (2012), and Chamel (2013), even with large superfluid entrainment.
