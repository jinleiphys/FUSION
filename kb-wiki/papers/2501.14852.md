# Signature of the $α$-clustering structure of Light Nuclei in Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

> The "imaging-by-smashing" technique has been developed recently in relativistic nuclear collisions. By smashing heavy nuclei at RHIC and the LHC and analyzing the anisotropic expansion (flow) of the final state produced particles, unique information on the structure of the collided nuclei has been obtained. Existing efforts primarily focus on the colliding mode of heavy nuclei collisions. In contrast, nuclear structure studies with collisions of light nuclei and the fixed target mode, despite their significant impact and broad interest, have not been thoroughly explored. In this Letter, we investigate the $α$-clustering signature of $^{20}$Ne and $^{16}$O in the fixed-target $^{208}$Pb--$^{20}$Ne and $^{208}$Pb--$^{16}$O collisions at $\sqrt{s_{_\mathrm{NN}}}$ = 68.5 GeV, using the parton transport model AMPT. The results of two- and four-particle cumulants of anisotropic flow demonstrate a robust $α$-clustering signature that persists regardless of the complex dynamic evolution of the created systems. This study highlights the significant impact of the LHCb SMOG (SMOG2) project in discovering the $α$-clustering signature of light nuclei at relativistic energies.

# Signature of the α-clustering structure of Light Nuclei in Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

## Key claim
The ratio of elliptic flow between Pb–Ne and Pb–O collisions at fixed-target LHCb SMOG energies provides a robust signature of α-clustering in ²⁰Ne and ¹⁶O that persists regardless of whether the created system undergoes dense hydrodynamic evolution or only dilute partonic interactions, as demonstrated using the AMPT transport model.

## Method
The authors performed AMPT (A Multi-Phase Transport) model simulations with string melting for fixed-target ²⁰⁸Pb–²⁰Ne and ²⁰⁸Pb–¹⁶O collisions at √sNN = 68.5 GeV. Nucleon distributions for ²⁰Ne and ¹⁶O were configured either from Nuclear Lattice Effective Field Theory (NLEFT) calculations (which predict α-clustering structures) or from Woods-Saxon (3pF) profiles as baselines. Two- and four-particle cumulants of anisotropic flow (v₂{2} and v₂{4}) were computed within the LHCb acceptance (0.2 < pT < 5.0 GeV, 2.0 < η < 5.0), with non-flow suppression via a |Δη| > 1 gap for v₂{2}. To test sensitivity to dynamic evolution, simulations were repeated with the parton scattering cross-section effectively turned off by increasing the screening mass to μ = 226.5 fm⁻¹.

## Key numbers
- Collision energy: √sNN = 68.5 GeV for both Pb–Ne and Pb–O systems
- For Pb–Ne collisions with NLEFT (α-cluster) configuration, the ratio v₂{2}(NLEFT)/v₂{2}(Woods-Saxon) is less than 0.8 in the most central (0–10%) collisions
- For Pb–O collisions, the ratio v₂{2}(NLEFT)/v₂{2}(Woods-Saxon) stays around 0.95 in the 0–70% centrality range
- The ratio v₂{2}(Pb–Ne)/v₂{2}(Pb–O) with NLEFT configurations starts at about 1.2 in most central collisions and decreases to unity at around 50% centrality
- With Woods-Saxon configurations, the ratio v₂{2}(Pb–Ne)/v₂{2}(Pb–O) starts at about 0.96 in most central collisions and stays near unity for centrality > 30%
- When ZPC parton interactions are turned off (μ = 226.5 fm⁻¹), v₂{2} decreases to about 75% of the default value in most central collisions, rising to about 85% at 70% centrality
- The ratio v₂{2}(Pb–Ne)/v₂{2}(Pb–O) with ZPC off agrees with the default μ = 2.265 fm⁻¹ results

## Context
This work extends the "imaging-by-smashing" technique, previously applied to heavy nuclei such as ⁹⁶Zr, ⁹⁶Ru, ²³⁸U, and ¹²⁹Xe at RHIC and the LHC, to light nuclei in fixed-target mode. It complements a hydrodynamic study by Giacalone (2024) that also investigated α-clustering in Ne and O using NLEFT initial conditions, but addresses the open question of whether the signature survives in transport models where partonic interactions are limited rather than sufficient for hydrodynamics. The results demonstrate that the LHCb SMOG and SMOG2 projects can discover α-clustering in light nuclei, and the sign of c₂{4} (negative in AMPT vs. positive in hydrodynamics) provides a tool to distinguish between dilute and dense partonic evolution in small systems.
