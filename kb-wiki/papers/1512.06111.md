# Polynomial Similarity Transformation Theory: A smooth interpolation between coupled cluster doubles and projected BCS applied to the reduced BCS Hamiltonian

> We present a similarity transformation theory based on a polynomial form of a particle-hole pair excitation operator. In the weakly correlated limit, this polynomial becomes an exponential, leading to coupled cluster doubles. In the opposite strongly correlated limit, the polynomial becomes an extended Bessel expansion and yields the projected BCS wavefunction. In between, we interpolate using a single parameter. The effective Hamiltonian is non-hermitian and this Polynomial Similarity Transformation Theory follows the philosophy of traditional coupled cluster, left projecting the transformed Hamiltonian onto subspaces of the Hilbert space in which the wave function variance is forced to be zero. Similarly, the interpolation parameter is obtained through minimizing the next residual in the projective hierarchy. We rationalize and demonstrate how and why coupled cluster doubles is ill suited to the strongly correlated limit whereas the Bessel expansion remains well behaved. The model provides accurate wave functions with energy errors that in its best variant are smaller than 1\% across all interaction stengths. The numerical cost is polynomial in system size and the theory can be straightforwardly applied to any realistic Hamiltonian.

## Key claim

The paper presents a Polynomial Similarity Transformation (PoST) theory that smoothly interpolates between coupled cluster doubles (pCCD) in the weakly correlated limit and projected BCS (PBCS) in the strongly correlated limit, using a single parameter α that controls the polynomial form of the wave operator. The method achieves energy errors smaller than 1% across all interaction strengths for the reduced BCS Hamiltonian, but the theory is not size-extensive except in the α=1 (coupled cluster) limit.

## Method

The authors define a wave operator F(T) = Σ (1/(n!)^α) T^n where T is a pair excitation operator, and construct a similarity-transformed Hamiltonian H̄ = F⁻¹(T) H F(T). The energy and T amplitudes are determined projectively by requiring ⟨0|H̄|0⟩ = E and ⟨2|H̄|0⟩ = 0, while the interpolation parameter α is determined by minimizing the quadruple-excitation residual R₄ = Σ|⟨4|H̄|0⟩|². The method is applied to the reduced BCS (pairing) Hamiltonian with equally spaced single-particle levels at half filling.

## Key numbers

- Energy errors smaller than 1% across all interaction strengths in the best variant of the theory
- For the 16-site half-filled pairing Hamiltonian, the PoST method recovers nearly 100% of the correlation energy across all values of G/Gc, compared to pCCD which fails for G > Gc and PBCS which is inaccurate for small G
- The computational scaling is O(o²v²) where o is the number of occupied orbitals and v is the number of virtual orbitals, compared to O(ov²) for pCCD
- The parameter α varies smoothly from α=1 at small G (corresponding to coupled cluster doubles) to α=2 at large G (corresponding to projected BCS)
- For large G, all singular values of the T₂ amplitude matrix except one tend to zero, indicating the T₂ amplitudes factorize into the PBCS form

## Context

This work extends the pair coupled cluster doubles (pCCD) method of Dukelsky (2003) and Henderson (2014), which fails for attractive interactions beyond the mean-field instability point Gc, and the projected BCS approach, which is inaccurate for weak correlations. The polynomial similarity transformation reconciles these two seemingly incompatible theories by abandoning both the variational principle of PBCS and the exponential form of pCCD. The method enables accurate treatment of strongly correlated pairing Hamiltonians with polynomial cost and can be straightforwardly applied to any realistic Hamiltonian, though the non-exponential form introduces unlinked terms that break size extensivity except in the α=1 limit.
