# Freeze out in narrow and wide layers

> The freeze out of particles from a layer of finite thickness is discussed in a phenomenological kinetic model. The proposed model, based on the Modified Boltzman Transport Equation, is Lorentz invariant and can be applied equally well for the freeze out layers with space-like and time-like normal vectors. It leads to non-equilibrated post freeze out distributions. The dependence of the resulting distribution on the thickness of the layer is presented and discussed for a space-like freeze out scenario.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a Lorentz-invariant kinetic freeze-out model for a layer of finite thickness \(L\), showing that the final post-freeze-out particle distributions are non-thermal and that the layer thickness strongly affects the evolution of the interacting component; however, for \(L > 5\lambda\) (where \(\lambda\) is the mean free path) the final distribution closely matches the infinitely thick layer result, while for very thin layers (\(L = 2\lambda\)) the distribution remains nearly thermal.

## Method

The authors solve a 1D phenomenological kinetic model based on the Modified Boltzmann Transport Equation, using an invariant escape probability \(P_{esc}\) for particles within a freeze-out layer of finite thickness \(L\). The distribution function is split into an interacting component \(f_i\) and a frozen-out component \(f_f\), with the interacting component evolving via a relaxation-time approximation toward an equilibrated Jüttner distribution \(f_{eq}(x)\) over a relaxation length \(\lambda_0\). The system is solved semi-analytically in the immediate rethermalization limit for a massless ideal gas without conserved charges, focusing on a space-like freeze-out scenario with normal vector \(d\sigma^\mu = (0,1,0,0)\).

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature \(T(0) = 170\ \text{MeV}\) and flow velocity \(v(0) = 0.5\) used as initial conditions for all calculations.
- For layer thickness \(L > 5\lambda\), the final post-freeze-out distribution closely matches the infinitely long freeze-out result from the earlier model (old_SL_FO).
- For a very thin layer \(L = 2\lambda\), the final post-freeze-out distribution is practically a thermal distribution.
- An effective thermal distribution with \(T = 140\ \text{MeV}\) and \(v = 0.33\) is shown for comparison with the \(L \to \infty\) curve.

## Context

This work extends the earlier kinetic freeze-out models of old_SL_FO and old_TL_FO, which required \(L \to \infty\) for complete freeze-out, by introducing a finite layer thickness \(L\) that bridges the gap between zero-thickness hypersurface freeze-out and volume emission models. The results justify the use of freeze-out hypersurfaces in hydrodynamical models of heavy ion collisions, provided that proper non-thermal post-freeze-out distributions (such as the Canceling Jüttner distribution of karolis) are employed, and suggest that curved low-\(p_t\) spectra indicate a thick freeze-out layer (\(L \ge 5\lambda\)), while narrow layers can be approximated by a freeze-out hypersurface.
