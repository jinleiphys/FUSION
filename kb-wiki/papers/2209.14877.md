# The Nuclear Physics of Neutron Stars

> Neutron stars -- compact objects with masses similar to that of our Sun but radii comparable to the size of a city -- contain the densest form of matter in the universe that can be probed in terrestrial laboratories as well as in earth- and space-based observatories. The historical detection of gravitational waves from a binary neutron star merger has opened the brand new era of multimessenger astronomy and has propelled neutron stars to the center of a variety of disciplines, such as astrophysics, general relativity, nuclear physics, and particle physics. The main input required to study the structure of neutron stars is the pressure support generated by its constituents against gravitational collapse. These include neutrons, protons, electrons, and perhaps even more exotic constituents. As such, nuclear physics plays a prominent role in elucidating the fascinating structure, dynamics, and composition of neutron stars.

## Key claim

Neutron star structure is determined by the equation of state (EOS) of dense matter, which must be inferred from the interplay between nuclear physics experiments, theoretical models, and multimessenger observations because the relevant densities and neutron-proton asymmetries are unattainable in terrestrial laboratories.

## Method

The paper reviews the structure and composition of neutron stars from the outer crust to the inner core, describing how each region is governed by different physics: nuclear masses and the symmetry energy in the crust, the nucleon-nucleon interaction and equation of state in the outer core, and possible exotic phases (hyperons, meson condensates, deconfined quark matter) in the inner core. It connects these nuclear-physics inputs to the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations of general relativity, which relate the pressure and energy density to the star's mass and radius.

## Key numbers

- The original Oppenheimer-Volkoff prediction gave a maximum neutron star mass of M_max = 0.7 solar masses, assuming non-interacting neutrons; current observations show masses near 2 solar masses, with one report of M_max = 2.35 solar masses (Romani 2022).
- Nuclear matter saturation density is n_0 = 0.15 fm^{-3}, corresponding to a mass density of rho_0 approximately 10^14 g/cm^3.
- The outer crust spans densities from 10^4 g/cm^3 to about 10^11 g/cm^3 (neutron-drip density).
- The crust-to-core transition density lies between one-third and one-half of nuclear matter saturation density.
- A simple geometric estimate suggests a transition to deconfined quark matter at a critical density n_c approximately 2 n_0, based on close packing of spheres with the proton charge radius r_0 approximately 0.84 fm.
- The 33 ms Crab pulsar spins down by about 13 microseconds per year.

## Context

This review extends the line of work connecting nuclear physics to neutron star structure that began with Baade and Zwicky (1934) and the first TOV calculations by Oppenheimer and Volkoff (1939). It builds on the multimessenger era opened by the gravitational-wave detection GW170817 (LIGO-Virgo 2017) and on recent measurements of neutron star masses near two solar masses (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013, Cromartie 2019, Fonseca 2021). The paper enables future work by identifying key open questions: the density dependence of the symmetry energy, the nature of the crust-core transition, the hyperon puzzle, and the possible existence of deconfined quark matter in the inner core.
