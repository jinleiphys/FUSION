# Higher-Harmonic Collective Modes in a Trapped Gas from Second-Order Hydrodynamics

> Utilizing a second-order hydrodynamics formalism, the dispersion relations for the frequencies and damping rates of collective oscillations as well as spatial structure of these modes up to the decapole oscillation in both two- and three- dimensional gas geometries are calculated. In addition to higher-order modes, the formalism also gives rise to purely damped "non-hydrodynamic" modes. We calculate the amplitude of the various modes for both symmetric and asymmetric trap quenches, finding excellent agreement with an exact quantum mechanical calculation. We find that higher-order hydrodynamic modes are more sensitive to the value of shear viscosity, which may be of interest for the precision extraction of transport coefficients in Fermi gas systems.

## Key claim

Second-order hydrodynamics predicts that higher-harmonic collective modes (hexapole, octupole, decapole) in trapped Fermi gases have damping rates proportional to shear viscosity times a mode-order-dependent prefactor, making them more sensitive probes for extracting transport coefficients than the commonly used quadrupole mode, though the analysis is limited to linear response and assumes constant η/P and ideal equation of state.

## Method

The authors linearize second-order hydrodynamic equations (including a relaxation equation for the viscous stress tensor with relaxation time τ_π) around a harmonic trap equilibrium, then expand perturbations in tensor Hermite polynomials orthogonal with respect to the Gaussian equilibrium density. They solve the resulting matrix eigenvalue problem for complex frequencies ω - iΓ of collective modes up to decapole order in both d=2 and d=3, assuming vanishing bulk viscosity and thermal conductivity, ideal equation of state, and constant η/P. Mode amplitudes for trap quench excitations are computed by projecting initial conditions onto the eigenmodes.

## Key numbers

- In d=2, hydrodynamic mode frequencies (η/P, τ_π ≪ 1): breathing (monopole) ω=2, Γ=0; sloshing (dipole) ω=1, Γ=0; quadrupole ω=√2, Γ=η/P; hexapole ω=√3, Γ=2η/P; octupole ω=2, Γ=3η/P; decapole ω=√5, Γ=4η/P
- In d=3, hydrodynamic mode frequencies (η/P, τ_π ≪ 1): breathing ω=√(10/3), Γ=η/(3P); sloshing ω=1, Γ=0; quadrupole ω=√2, Γ=η/P; hexapole ω=√3, Γ=2η/P; octupole ω=2, Γ=3η/P; decapole ω=√5, Γ=4η/P
- Non-hydrodynamic mode damping rates in d=2: quadrupole Γ=1/τ_π - 2η/P; hexapole Γ=1/τ_π - 4η/P; octupole Γ=1/τ_π - 6η/P; decapole Γ=1/τ_π - 8η/P
- Non-hydrodynamic mode damping rates in d=3: breathing Γ=1/τ_π - 2η/(3P); quadrupole Γ=1/τ_π - 2η/P; hexapole Γ=1/τ_π - 4η/P; octupole Γ=1/τ_π - 6η/P; decapole Γ=1/τ_π - 8η/P
- For isotropic trap quench in d=2, breathing mode amplitude agrees exactly with quantum mechanical scaling solution by Moroz (2012) for all quench strengths γ
- Non-hydrodynamic to hydrodynamic quadrupole mode amplitude ratio in d=2 reaches up to approximately 20% for large η/P

## Context

This work extends the second-order hydrodynamic framework (previously applied to relativistic systems and to anisotropic hydrodynamics by Bluhm (2015)) to collective oscillations of trapped Fermi gases above the superfluid transition temperature T_c. It complements the study by Rosi (2015) which focused on polytropic equations of state with zero shear viscosity, whereas this work emphasizes effects of non-vanishing shear viscosity. The results suggest that measuring damping rates of higher-order modes (particularly the hexapole mode, which avoids nonlinear mode mixing issues) could provide stronger constraints on shear viscosity in unitary Fermi gases, analogous to multi-mode analyses used in relativistic heavy-ion collisions by Schenke (2012). The non-hydrodynamic mode predictions connect to early-time dynamics studies by Brewer (2015) and Bantilan (2016).
