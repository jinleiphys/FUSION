# Why is lead so kinky?

> We revisit the problem of the kink in the charge radius shift of neutron-rich even lead isotopes. We show that the ability of a Skyrme force to reproduce the isotope shift is determined by the occupation of the neutron 1i11/2 orbital beyond N=126 and the corresponding change it causes to deeply-bound protons orbitals with a principal quantum number of 1. Given the observed position of the single-particle energies, one must either ensure occupation is allowed through correlations, or not demand that the single-particle energies agree with experimental values at the mean-field level.

## Key claim

The kink in the charge radius isotope shift of neutron-rich even lead isotopes across the N=126 shell gap is determined by the occupation of the neutron 1i11/2 orbital beyond N=126, not by the traditionally invoked 2g9/2 orbital; the mechanism requires either that correlations allow occupation of the 1i11/2 orbital or that one does not demand mean-field single-particle energies to agree with experimental values.

## Method

The authors performed spherical Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations with BCS pairing using a delta volume pairing force for even lead and polonium isotopes. They compared five Skyrme parameterizations (SKRA, NRAPRii, SLy4, SLy4mod, and SkI4), modifying the spin-orbit coupling parameters in some cases (NRAPRii with doubled W0; SLy4mod with RMF-type spin-orbit) to test the effect on the isotope shift. They computed radial overlaps between neutron and proton orbitals in 208Pb to quantify the mechanism.

## Key numbers

- Experimental slopes of the charge radius isotope shift for lead: m1 = 0.0598(7) fm^2 for A < 208, m2 = 0.1203(9) fm^2 for A > 208, i.e. the slope doubles at A=208.
- The spin-orbit parameter for NRAPR, W0 = 2b4 = 2b4' = 41.958 MeV fm^5, is very low compared to typical values of W0 ≈ 120 MeV fm^5 for other forces.
- For SLy4mod, the original parameters (b4 = b4' = 61.5 MeV fm^5) were changed to b4 = 75.0 MeV fm^5 and b4' = 0.001 MeV fm^5, keeping the spin-orbit field magnitude in 208Pb the same as SLy4.
- BCS occupation threshold used: states above N=126 with occupation above 3% were considered substantially occupied.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing problem of the kink in lead isotope shifts, previously studied by Tajima (1990s), Reinhard and Flocard (1995), and Bender (2006), who attributed the effect to the spin-orbit force and the 2g9/2 neutron orbital. The authors contradict that traditional explanation by showing that the 1i11/2 orbital (with principal quantum number n=1) is the key driver, due to its stronger radial overlap with deeply-bound proton orbitals. This mechanism also explains the similar kink observed in polonium isotopes by Cocolios (2011) and is consistent with older radon and radium data from Otten (1989). The work enables future studies to either include correlations that allow 1i11/2 occupation or adjust single-particle levels via tensor forces without compromising nuclear matter properties.
