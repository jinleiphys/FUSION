# Lectures on non-equilibrium effective field theories and fluctuating hydrodynamics

> We review recent progress in developing effective field theories (EFTs) for non-equilibrium processes at finite temperature, including a new formulation of fluctuating hydrodynamics, and a new proof of the second law of thermodynamics. There are a number of new elements in formulating EFTs for such systems. Firstly, the nature of IR variables is very different from those of a system in equilibrium or near the vacuum. Secondly, while all static properties of an equilibrium system can in principle be extracted from the partition function, there appears no such quantity which can capture all non-equilibrium properties. Thirdly, non-equilibrium processes often involve dissipation, which is notoriously difficult to deal with using an action principle. The purpose of the review is to explain how to address these issues in a pedagogic manner, with fluctuating hydrodynamics as a main example.

## Key claim

The paper reviews recent progress in formulating effective field theories (EFTs) for non-equilibrium processes at finite temperature, arguing that while such EFTs share features with the Martin-Siggia-Rose-De Dominicis-Janssen functional integral approach at the Gaussian level, the formalism presented is derived from first principles based on symmetries and action principle rather than from phenomenological equations, and can treat noise systematically at full nonlinear level.

## Method

The authors develop a non-equilibrium EFT framework by identifying the relevant infrared variables as those associated with conserved quantities (hydrodynamic variables), since at finite temperature non-conserved quantities relax on microscopic timescales and can be integrated out. They formulate the theory using a closed time path (Schwinger-Keldysh) path integral, imposing a Z2 dynamical KMS symmetry that encodes local equilibrium and generalizes Onsager relations to nonlinear level, with fluctuating hydrodynamics as the main example.

## Key numbers

* The relaxation time tau and relaxation length ell are described as microscopic, with tau typically of order 1/beta where beta is the inverse temperature, for strongly interacting systems.
* For a long wavelength perturbation with wavelength lambda much greater than ell, the relaxation time for conserved quantities scales as t_lambda much greater than tau, and as lambda approaches infinity, t_lambda approaches infinity.
* The EFT is valid at length scales larger than some UV cutoff scale epsilon, with the derivative expansion parameter epsilon partial_mu ~ epsilon/L much less than 1 for processes with typical variation scale L much greater than epsilon.

## Context

This work extends the Landau-Ginzburg-Wilson paradigm, which has been tremendously successful for equilibrium systems, to non-equilibrium processes at finite temperature. It provides a first-principles formulation of fluctuating hydrodynamics that goes beyond traditional phenomenological approaches (such as those reviewed in Appendix A of the paper) and the MSR functional integral methods of Martin, Siggia, Rose (1973), De Dominicis (1976), and Janssen (1976). The framework enables systematic treatment of hydrodynamic fluctuations in contexts including non-equilibrium steady states, scale dependence of transport coefficients (long time tails, Arnold 1997, Kovtun 2003), the QCD critical point search at Brookhaven, quantum gravitational fluctuations via holographic duality (Caron-Huot 2009), and turbulent flows.
