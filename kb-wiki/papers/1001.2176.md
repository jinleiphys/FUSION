# Shedding Light on the Symmetries of Dark Matter

> I consider symmetries which could explain observed properties of dark matter, namely, its stability on Gyr time scales or its relic density and discuss how such symmetries can be discovered through the study of the propagation and polarization of light in its transit through dark matter.

# Shedding Light on the Symmetries of Dark Matter

## Key claim
Dark matter's stability on Gyr time scales or its relic density could be explained by hidden symmetries (such as a U(1) gauge symmetry giving millicharge, or an asymmetry in particle-antiparticle content), and these symmetries can be discovered through the study of light propagation and polarization in transit through dark matter, provided the appropriate frequency-dependent signatures are isolated from source effects.

## Method
The author analyzes dispersive effects in light propagation by expanding the forward Compton amplitude for photon-dark-matter scattering in a power series for photon energies much smaller than the threshold energy for particle production, using analyticity and unitarity to derive a frequency-dependent index of refraction. For millicharged dark matter, the author fits time delays between radio and higher-frequency observations of 53 Gamma-Ray Bursts (GRBs) with measured redshifts, using a model that separates propagation effects from intrinsic GRB emission time lags. For magnetic moment detection, the author describes the gyromagnetic Faraday effect, where a magnetic field induces circular birefringence in a medium with free magnetic moments, producing a rotation angle that depends on the magnetization and magnetic moment of dark matter.

## Key numbers
- The combined WMAP five-year data yields $H_0 = 70.5 \pm 1.3 \,\hbox{km\, s}^{-1}\hbox{Mpc}^{-1}$, $\Omega_M = 0.274 \pm 0.015$, and $\Omega_\Lambda = 1 - \Omega_M$ for the $\Lambda$CDM cosmological model.
- The local dark matter density is $\rho_0 \simeq 1.19 \times 10^{-6}$ GeV/cm${}^3$.
- For GRB 980703A at $z=0.967 \pm 0.001$, a time lag of $2.700 \pm 0.006$ day at $\nu=1.43$ GHz gives $K(z)/\nu^2 = 1170 \pm 10$ Mpc GHz$^{-2}$, fixing $|\varepsilon|/M \simeq 9 \times 10^{-6}$ eV$^{-1}$.
- From the full fit of 53 GRBs with frequencies 4.0–75 GHz in the GRB rest frame, $\tilde A_0 = 0.0010 \pm 0.0019$ day GHz$^2$ Mpc$^{-1}$, yielding $|\varepsilon|/M < 1 \times 10^{-5}$ eV$^{-1}$ at 95% CL.
- The limit is operative if $\omega_{\rm th}/2\pi > 4$ GHz, or crudely if $M > 8 \times 10^{-6}$ eV.
- The strongest laboratory bound on millicharge is $|\varepsilon| < 3-4 \times 10^{-7}$ for $M \le 0.05$ eV (Ahlers, 2007), and the strongest stellar evolution bound is $|\varepsilon| < 2 \times 10^{-14}$ for $M < 5$ keV (Davidson, 1991).

## Context
This work extends the program of using GRB light curves to probe fundamental physics, previously applied to Lorentz violation (Amelino-Camelia, 1998; Ellis, 2000), by applying it to dark matter interactions. The dispersive effect analysis builds on the effective theory approach of Gardner and Latimer (2009) and uses the GRB Hubble diagram study of Schaefer (2007) for cosmological consistency. The gyromagnetic Faraday effect proposal extends the work of Gardner (2009) on dark matter magnetic moments and provides a complementary probe to spin-dependent scattering experiments, with the unique ability to detect particle-antiparticle asymmetry in the dark sector. The millicharge limits are compared to laboratory constraints from Ahlers (2007), stellar evolution bounds from Davidson (1991), and galactic structure constraints from Gradwohl (1992) and Feng (2009).
