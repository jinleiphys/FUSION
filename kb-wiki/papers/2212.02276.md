# Complex eigenenergy of GDR for $^{16}$O by the Jost function within RPA framework

> The Jost function method is extended within the framework of the RPA theory to find poles on the complex energy plane that exhibit complex RPA eigenenergies. Poles corresponding to the RPA excited states such as the giant resonance of $^{16}$O electric dipole excitations were successfully found on the complex energy plane. Although the giant resonance has been known as a single resonance with large strength and width, it is found that, at least within the RPA framework, the $^{16}$O electric dipole giant resonance is formed by multiple poles, each of which is an independent pole with different widths, origins, response properties to residual interactions, and components structures of the density fluctuation.

## Key claim

The electric dipole giant resonance of $^{16}$O, traditionally understood as a single broad resonance, is actually formed by multiple independent poles on the complex energy plane within the RPA framework, each with different widths, origins, response properties to residual interactions, and density fluctuation component structures.

## Method

The authors extended the Jost function method to the random-phase-approximation (RPA) framework by reformulating the RPA equation as a system of coupled second-order differential equations, then defining regular and irregular solution matrices and a Jost function matrix whose determinant vanishes at complex eigenenergies. They applied this Jost-RPA method to the electric dipole excitation of $^{16}$O using a Woods-Saxon mean-field potential and a simple density-dependent contact residual interaction, solving the equations numerically in coordinate space up to 20 fm with 0.2 fm spacing.

## Key numbers

- The bound single-particle energies for neutrons range from -36.17 MeV (s1/2) to -4.90 MeV (s1/2), and for protons from -31.16 MeV (s1/2) to -1.43 MeV (s1/2), as obtained from the Woods-Saxon potential.
- The residual interaction parameters used are t0 = -1100 MeV fm^3, t3 = 16000 MeV fm^6, x0 = 0.5, and x3 = 1.0.
- The Woods-Saxon potential parameters are V0 = -60(1 ∓ 0.67(N-Z)/A) MeV for neutrons (minus sign) and protons (plus sign), with spin-orbit strength V1 = 15(1 ∓ 0.67(N-Z)/A) MeV, radius parameter r0 = 1.2 fm, and diffuseness a = 0.65 fm.
- For the E1 dipole operator, the external field uses f_n(r) = e(Z/A)r and f_p(r) = -e(N/A)r, with N = Z = 8 for ^16O.

## Context

This work extends the Jost function method, previously applied to Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov theory by Mizuyama et al. (Refs. jost-hfb, jost-fano, jost-class), into the RPA framework to directly compute complex eigenenergies and widths of resonances. It contrasts with the continuum RPA (cRPA) method of Shlomo, which can calculate strength functions as smooth functions of energy but cannot extract widths directly, and with discrete-basis RPA methods that require phenomenological Lorentzian fits to estimate widths. The approach enables future microscopic calculations of resonance widths for giant resonances in heavier nuclei and opens the possibility of analyzing the component structure of density fluctuations, which existing cRPA methods cannot decompose by transition component.
