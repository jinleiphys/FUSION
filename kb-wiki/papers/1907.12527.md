# Theory of strongly paired fermions with arbitrary short-range interactions

> We develop an effective field theory to describe the superfluid pairing in strongly interacting fermions with arbitrary short-range attractions, by extending Kaplan's idea of coupling fermions to a fictitious boson state in Nucl. Phys. B \textbf{494}, 471 (1997). This boson field is assigned with unconventional kinetic term to recover the exact scattering phase shift obtained either from scattering data or model calculations. The theory works even if the explicit form of the interaction potential has not been constructed from scattering data. The contact boson-fermion coupling allows us to go beyond mean-field to include Gaussian pair fluctuations, yielding reliable predictions on equations of state. As an application, we use our theory to address the non-univerisal ground-state energy of strongly paired fermions, due to the non-trivial momentum dependence of the phase shift characterized, for example, by effective range. We find a good agreement between our predictions and recent quantum Monte Carlo simulations on the effective-range dependence in both three and two spatial dimensions. We propose that in cold-atom experiments, the non-universal dependence in thermodynamics can be probed using dark-state optical control of Feshbach resonances.

## Key claim

The authors develop an effective field theory that reproduces the *full* two-body scattering phase shift for arbitrary short-range interactions, not just the effective-range expansion, by extending Kaplan's fictitious boson method (Kaplan, 1997). The theory works even when the explicit form of the interaction potential has not been constructed from scattering data, and the contact boson-fermion coupling allows going beyond mean-field to include Gaussian pair fluctuations.

## Method

The authors introduce a boson field with an unconventional kinetic term designed to recover the exact scattering phase shift obtained from scattering data or model calculations. They integrate out fermion fields in the path integral formalism and treat the boson field at the saddle-point level for mean-field theory, then include Gaussian pair fluctuations around the saddle point to compute the grand potential and equation of state. The method is applied to both three-dimensional and two-dimensional Fermi gases, with the effective-range dependence of the ground-state energy calculated near s-wave resonances.

## Key numbers

* In 3D at resonance, the Bertsch parameter from GPF theory is ξ = 0.401, compared to the mean-field value ξ = 0.591; the GPF result agrees with experimental and QMC results in the range 0.36-0.42
* The coefficient ζ for the linear effective-range correction E/E_FG = ξ + ζ k_F r_e is ζ = 0.105 in GPF theory, compared to ζ = 0.273 in mean-field theory; the GPF result agrees with diffusion QMC values ζ = 0.087(1) (Conduit, 2013) and ζ = 0.127(4) (Forbes, 2012)
* For zero-range interaction in 3D, the dimer-dimer scattering length from GPF theory is a_dd(0) ≈ 0.57a, close to the exact four-body result a_dd(0) = 0.6a (Petrov, 2004)
* For 40K at 202 G resonance, the effective range parameter without optical control is k_F r_0 = -2.7×10^{-2}; for 6Li at 832 G, it is k_F r_0 = -4.5×10^{-4}

## Context

This work extends Kaplan's fictitious boson approach (Kaplan, 1997) from effective-range expansion to arbitrary short-range interactions, going beyond earlier treatments that truncated at the effective-range level (Tajima, 2015; Hu, 2018). The theory enables reliable predictions for non-universal thermodynamics in strongly paired fermions, relevant to neutron superfluids in neutron star crusts (Schwenk, 2005; Gezerlis, 2010) and to cold-atom experiments using dark-state optical control of Feshbach resonances (Thomas, 2012; Thomas, 2016; Thomas, 2018). The results agree with quantum Monte Carlo simulations (Conduit, 2013; Forbes, 2012) and can be generalized to include higher partial waves and bosonic systems.
