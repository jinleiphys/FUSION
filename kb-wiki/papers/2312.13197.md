# Photons from neutrinos: the gamma ray echo of a supernova neutrino burst

> When a star undergoes core collapse, a vast amount of energy is released in a ~10 s long burst of neutrinos of all species. Inverse beta decay in the star's hydrogen envelope causes an electromagnetic cascade which ultimately results in a flare of gamma rays - an "echo" of the neutrino burst - at the characteristic energy of 0.511 MeV. We study the phenomenology and detectability of this flare. Its luminosity curve is characterized by a fast, seconds-long, rise and an equally fast decline, with a minute- or hour-long plateau in between. For a near-Earth star (distance D<1 kpc) the echo will be observable at near future gamma ray telescopes with an effective area of 10^3 cm^2 or larger. Its observation will inform us on the envelope size and composition. In conjunction with the direct detection of the neutrino burst, it will also give information on the neutrino emission away from the line of sight and will enable tests of neutrino propagation effects between the stellar surface and Earth.

## Key claim

Inverse beta decay of supernova neutrinos in the hydrogen envelope of a collapsing star produces a gamma-ray echo at 0.511 MeV that is detectable by near-future telescopes only for stars within about 1 kpc, provided the telescope effective area is at least 10^3 cm^2; the signal must also exceed the diffuse galactic background, which limits robust detection to stars with favorable sky positions.

## Method

The authors model the time-dependent gamma-ray flux by convolving the antineutrino luminosity with a box function that accounts for the light travel time across the stellar surface, using the inverse beta decay cross section averaged over a neutrino spectrum with shape parameter alpha = 2.3 and mean energy 15 MeV. They compute positron production in the outermost Compton-thin layer of the hydrogen envelope, then calculate the resulting 0.511 MeV gamma-ray emission from positron annihilation, accounting for thermalization and absorption. The detectability is evaluated against the diffuse galactic 511 keV background for specific nearby supernova candidates using published telescope specifications.

## Key numbers

- Total number of positrons produced: N_+ approximately 1.25 x 10^41, with about 50% uncertainty
- Total time-integrated photon flux at Earth: Phi_gamma,tot approximately 10^-3 cm^-2 (D/kpc)^-2
- For a star at D = 1 kpc with radius R = 10^13.5 cm, the echo duration is R/c approximately 10^3 s (about 17 minutes) and maximum flux is approximately 10^-6 cm^-2 s^-1
- The fraction of positrons that annihilate after thermalization is P approximately 0.87, giving eta_gamma = 1.74 gamma rays per positron
- The galactic background flux at 511 keV away from the galactic center is dPhi_gal/dOmega approximately 3 x 10^-4 cm^-2 s^-1 sr^-1, corresponding to Phi_gal approximately 3 x 10^-6 cm^-2 s^-1 for AMEGO's angular resolution of about 3 degrees
- For the candidate star Rigel (D = 0.264 kpc, R = 4.94 x 10^12 cm), the predicted signal-to-background ratio N_s/N_B is 2.90 x 10^2
- For Betelgeuse (D = 0.222 kpc, R = 5.32 x 10^13 cm), N_s/N_B is 38.1
- For Antares (D = 0.169 kpc, R = 4.73 x 10^13 cm), N_s/N_B is 0.968 due to higher background near the Galactic Center

## Context

This work revives and modernizes an idea first studied in the 1970s by Bisnovatyi-Kogan (1975) and Ruffini (1999), and later by Lu (2007), who predicted the gamma-ray echo but considered it too faint for observation. The authors show that next-generation gamma-ray telescopes such as COSI (funded, A > 20 cm^2 at 511 keV), AMEGO (A approximately 3 x 10^3 cm^2), and GECCO (A approximately 800 cm^2) could detect the echo from nearby supernova candidates within 1 kpc. The observation would provide information on the stellar envelope size and composition, and when combined with direct neutrino detection, would enable tests of neutrino emission away from the line of sight and searches for exotic neutrino propagation effects such as decay or sterile neutrino conversion.
