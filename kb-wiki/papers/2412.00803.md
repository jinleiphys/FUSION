# Quantum simulation of the phase transition of the massive Thirring model

> Recent advancements in quantum computing technology have enabled the study of fermionic systems at finite temperature via quantum simulations. This presents a novel approach to investigating the chiral phase transition in such systems. Among these, the quantum minimally entangled typical thermal states~(QMETTS) algorithm has recently attracted considerable interest. The massive Thirring model, which exhibits a variety of phenomena at low temperatures, includes both a chiral phase transition and a topologically non-trivial ground state. It therefore raises the intriguing question of whether its phase transition can be studied using a quantum simulation approach. In this study, the chiral phase transition of the massive Thirring model and its dual topological phase transition are studied using the QMETTS algorithm. Numerical results are obtained on a classical computer simulating circuit-based quantum computations. The results show that QMETTS is able to accurately reproduce the phase transition and thermodynamic properties of the massive Thirring model.

## Key claim

The QMETTS algorithm can accurately reproduce the chiral phase transition and thermodynamic properties of the massive Thirring model, including the topological phase transition dual to the sine-Gordon model, when simulated on a classical computer with noise neglected; however, the results are obtained for a small system (N=4 sites) and the algorithm's ability to cross topological sector barriers in finite-volume systems remains an open question.

## Method

The authors mapped the massive Thirring model in 1+1 dimensions to a spin Hamiltonian using staggered fermions and the Jordan-Wigner transformation, then applied the Quantum Minimally Entangled Typical Thermal States (QMETTS) algorithm to compute thermal expectation values of the chiral condensate and fermion number (topological charge). The QMETTS algorithm approximates the imaginary time evolution operator e^{-βH} by a sequence of unitary operators e^{-i a^{-1} Δβ A}, where the operator A is determined by solving a linear system constructed from measurements on the current state. The simulations were performed on a classical computer using the qiskit framework, with exact diagonalization results used as a benchmark for N=4 sites.

## Key numbers

- Mass fixed at m = 0.5 a^{-1}, corresponding to m = 50 MeV when a^{-1} = 100 MeV
- Temperature range considered: 0.01 a^{-1} ≤ T ≤ a^{-1} (1 MeV ≤ T ≤ 100 MeV when a^{-1} = 100 MeV)
- Coupling constant range: 0 ≤ g² ≤ 3 for Euclidean Hamiltonian H_E, 0 ≤ g² ≤ 5 for Minkowski Hamiltonian H_M
- QMETTS parameters: a^{-1}Δβ = 0.25, K = 20 steps, Trotter steps t = 10, with coefficients |c_j| > 0.001 retained
- Measurements repeated r = 1024 times for each observable O_i
- For finite volume analysis at a^{-1}β = 10 and g² = 1, the chiral condensate per site changed from ⟨ψ̄ψ⟩/N ≈ -0.07 at N=4 to ⟨ψ̄ψ⟩/N ≈ -0.13 at N=12

## Context

This work extends the application of the QMETTS algorithm, previously used by Czajka et al. (2021) to study chiral phase transitions, to the massive Thirring model which is exactly soluble in the massless case (Hagen, 1967) and soluble by Bethe ansatz in the massive case (Korepin, Bogoliubov, and Izergin, 1993). The Thirring model is dual to the sine-Gordon theory (Coleman, 1974), enabling the study of topological soliton dynamics, and can also be related to the Gross-Neveu model with finite chemical potential. The results demonstrate that QMETTS can reproduce known phase transition behavior at low temperatures (T = 10 MeV) despite the presence of topologically non-trivial ground states, though the study is limited to small system sizes and neglects noise effects, with noise resilience deferred to future research.
