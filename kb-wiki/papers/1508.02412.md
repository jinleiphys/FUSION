# Di-photon "Ridge" in p+p and p+A collisions at RHIC and the LHC

> We obtain prompt di-photon cross-section in proton-nucleus collisions in Hamiltonian light-cone approach within a hybrid approximation, treating the projectile proton in the parton model and the target nucleus in the Color-Glass-Condensate approach. We study in details the di-photon correlations in quark-nucleus and proton-nucleus collisions at RHIC and the LHC. We show that the single fragmentation di-photon produces the away side correlations peak, and the double fragmentation component of prompt di-photon is responsible for the near-side peak, and the long-range in rapidity near-side azimuthal collimation, the so-called "ridge" structure. We study the transverse momentum, density and energy dependence of the di-photon ridge and show that it strongly depends on the kinematics and saturation dynamics. We show that while di-photon ridge exists at the LHC in quark-nucleus collisions, the effect disappears in proton-nucleus collisions at the LHC. At RHIC the ridge-type structure persists at low transverse momenta of di-photon even in proton-nucleus collisions. We argue that di-photon correlation measurments in p+A collisions can help to discriminate among models and understand the true origin of the observed di-hadron ridge in p+A collisions. We also show that in addition to the ridge structure, prompt di-photon correlation also exhibits some distinct novel features, including the emergence of away side double-peak structure at intermediate transverse momenta.

## Key claim

The paper claims that prompt di-photon production in high-energy collisions exhibits a ridge-type structure (long-range in rapidity near-side azimuthal collimation) in quark-nucleus collisions, but this effect disappears in proton-nucleus collisions at the LHC, while persisting at low transverse momenta at RHIC; the authors hedge that the di-photon ridge strongly depends on kinematics and saturation dynamics, and that the observed correlations decrease for moderate rapidity separation between the two photons (approximately 3 units).

## Method

The authors calculate the prompt di-photon cross-section in proton-nucleus collisions using a Hamiltonian light-cone approach within a hybrid approximation, treating the projectile proton in the parton model and the target nucleus in the Color-Glass-Condensate (CGC) framework. They derive the full leading-order expression for the cross-section without resorting to the soft approximation, then numerically evaluate the azimuthal correlation function \(C_2\) using the running-coupling Balitsky-Kovchegov (rcBK) evolution equation with initial conditions fitted to HERA data and minimum-bias p+p and p+A data.

## Key numbers

- Initial saturation scale of the proton: \(Q_{0s}^2 \simeq 0.168\ \text{GeV}^2\) with \(\gamma \simeq 1.119\), extracted from a global fit to proton structure functions in DIS and single inclusive hadron data in p+p collisions at RHIC and the LHC.
- For the nucleus case, the initial saturation scale is taken as \(Q_{0A}^2 \approx 5 \div 7 Q_{0s}^2\), extracted from minimum-bias data in deuteron-gold at RHIC and proton-lead collisions at the LHC.
- The di-photon correlations are computed at \(\sqrt{s} = 5.02\ \text{TeV}\) with photon rapidities \(\eta_1 = 1\) and \(\eta_2 = 2\) and transverse momenta \(k_{1T} = k_{2T} = 2\ \text{GeV}\).
- The onset of small-x evolution is assumed at \(x_0 = 0.01\), and the infrared scale is taken as \(\Lambda = 0.241\ \text{GeV}\).

## Context

This work extends the CGC-based analysis of di-hadron ridge phenomena (previously studied by the same authors and others, e.g., ridge0, ridge1, ridge2) to prompt di-photon production, which is free from final-state hadronization effects and depends only on the dipole amplitude rather than higher-order Wilson line correlators. The results enable future experimental discrimination between initial-state (CGC) and final-state (hydrodynamic) explanations of the ridge in p+p and p+A collisions, as di-photon correlations can be directly compared to di-hadron correlation measurements at RHIC and the LHC.
