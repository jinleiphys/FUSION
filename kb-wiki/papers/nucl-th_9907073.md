> The implications of an effective field theory (EFT) interpretation of nuclear mean-field phenomenology are reviewed.

# Effective Field Theory and Nuclear Mean-Field Models

## Key claim
The paper argues that effective field theory (EFT) concepts and naive dimensional analysis (NDA) can explain the successes and limitations of both relativistic (QHD) and nonrelativistic (Skyrme) mean-field models of nuclear structure, provided the expansion parameter ρ/fπ²Λ is between 1/7 and 1/4 at equilibrium nuclear density and coefficients are natural (order unity).

## Method
The authors analyze the coefficients and energy contributions of several QHD meson models and point-coupling models, comparing them to NDA estimates with natural coefficients spanning a range from 1/2 to 2. They perform χ²-type optimizations to bulk properties of magic nuclei and examine how truncation order affects fit quality, while also comparing generalized Skyrme models to relativistic point-coupling models.

## Key numbers
- The pion-decay constant fπ ≈ 93 MeV sets the scale for the NDA expansion parameter ρ/fπ²Λ
- The expansion parameter at equilibrium nuclear density lies between 1/7 and 1/4
- A scale of Λ = 600 MeV is consistent with trends in all relativistic and nonrelativistic models examined
- Models must go to the fourth power of fields to obtain excellent fits to nuclear data, but no further
- Point-coupling models can truncate at third order and still find a good fit

## Context
This work extends the EFT interpretation of nuclear mean-field phenomenology developed by Friar (1996), Furnstahl (1996), Serot (1997), and Rusnak (1997). It directly contradicts claims that QHD models fail to incorporate nucleon compositeness or QCD vacuum effects, showing that quark-meson coupling (QMC) models are actually a subset of general QHD models. The results enable a systematic framework for understanding truncation errors in mean-field models and suggest that combining EFT with density functional theory could allow systematic inclusion of correlation effects and nonanalytic terms.
