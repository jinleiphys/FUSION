# Low Mass Neutron Stars and the Equation of State of Dense Matter

> Neutron-star radii provide useful information on the equation of state of neutron rich matter. Particularly interesting is the density dependence of the equation of state (EOS). For example, the softening of the EOS at high density, where the pressure rises slower than anticipated, could signal a transition to an exotic phase. However, extracting the density dependence of the EOS requires measuring the radii of neutron stars for a broad range of masses. A ``normal'' 1.4 solar mass neutron star has a central density of a few times nuclear-matter saturation density. In contrast, low mass (of the order of 0.5 solar masses) neutron stars have central densities near nuclear-matter saturation density so its radius provides information on the EOS at low density. Unfortunately, low-mass stars are rare because they may be hard to form. Instead, a precision measurement of nuclear radii on atomic nuclei may contain similar information. Indeed, we find a strong correlation between the neutron radius of 208Pb and the radius of a 0.5 solar-mass neutron star. Thus, the radius of such a neutron star can be inferred from a measurement of the the neutron radius of 208Pb. Comparing this value to the measured radius of a 1.4 solar-mass neutron star should provide the strongest constraint to date on the density dependence of the equation of state.

# Low Mass Neutron Stars and the Equation of State of Dense Matter

## Key claim
The radius of a low-mass (approximately 0.5 solar mass) neutron star can be inferred from a precision measurement of the neutron radius of 208Pb, because both probe the equation of state at densities near nuclear-matter saturation density. Comparing this inferred radius to the measured radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star should provide the strongest constraint to date on the density dependence of the equation of state, but this relies on the assumption that low-mass neutron stars exist and that the correlation holds across model parameter sets.

## Method
The authors used a relativistic effective field theory formalism in a mean-field approximation, supplemented with new couplings between isoscalar and isovector mesons, to correlate nuclear observables with neutron star properties. They considered three parameter sets (NL3, S271, Z271) and varied the nonlinear couplings Lambda_s and Lambda_v to change the density dependence of the symmetry energy while keeping other nuclear properties fixed. A random-phase-approximation stability analysis determined the transition density between the nonuniform inner crust and the uniform liquid interior, and a polytropic interpolation formula was used for the crust equation of state, with the outer crust following the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland equation of state.

## Key numbers
- The neutron skin of 208Pb (R_n minus R_p) ranges from approximately 0.13 fm to 0.28 fm across the parameter sets studied.
- For a 0.5 solar mass neutron star, if R_n minus R_p in 208Pb is approximately 0.25 fm, then the neutron star radius is approximately 16 km; if R_n minus R_p is approximately 0.15 fm, then the radius is less than 13 km.
- The symmetry energy at saturation density is predicted to be 37.3 MeV for NL3, 36.6 MeV for S271, and 36.3 MeV for Z271 (with Lambda_s = Lambda_v = 0).
- Nuclear matter saturation occurs at a Fermi momentum of k_F = 1.30 fm^{-1} with binding energy per nucleon of -16.24 MeV and incompressibility K = 271 MeV for all parameter sets.
- The transition density between inner crust and liquid interior ranges from approximately 0.0519 fm^{-3} to 0.1007 fm^{-3} across the parameter sets.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies of correlations between the neutron radius of 208Pb and neutron-star crust properties (Carriere, Horowitz, Piekarewicz 2002), radii of 1.4 solar mass neutron stars (Horowitz and Piekarewicz 2001), and direct URCA cooling (Horowitz and Piekarewicz 2002). The paper builds on the suggestion by Brown (2000) that the neutron radius of a heavy nucleus is related to the pressure of neutron-rich matter. The results enable a method to determine the density dependence of the equation of state even if only a single mass-radius measurement for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is available, by using the atomic nucleus as a proxy for low-mass neutron star properties. The parity radius experiment (PREX) at Jefferson Laboratory aims to measure the neutron radius of 208Pb to 1% accuracy (within approximately plus or minus 0.05 fm), which would directly enable this inference.
