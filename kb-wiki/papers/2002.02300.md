# Nuclear effects in proton transport and dose calculations

> Interactions of protons with nuclei are modeled in a form that is suitable for Monte Carlo simulation of proton transport. The differential cross section (DCS) for elastic collisions of protons with neutral atoms is expressed as the product of the Rutherford DCS, which describes scattering by a bare point nucleus, and two correction factors that account for the screening of the nuclear charge by the atomic electrons and for the effect of the structure of the nucleus. The screening correction is obtained by considering the scattering of the projectile by an atom with a point nucleus and the atomic electron cloud described by a parameterization of the Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater self-consistent electron density. The DCS for scattering by this point nucleus atom is calculated by means of the eikonal approximation. The nuclear correction to the DCS for elastic collisions is calculated by conventional partial-wave analysis with a global optical-model potential that describes the interaction with the bare nucleus. Inelastic interactions of the projectile with target nuclei are described by using information from data files in ENDF-6 format, which provide cross sections, multiplicities, and angle-energy distributions of all reaction products: light ejectiles (neutrons, protons, . . . ), gammas, as well as recoiling heavy residuals. These interaction data have been used in the Monte Carlo transport code PENH, an extension of the electron-gamma code PENELOPE, which originally accounted for electromagnetic interactions only. The combined code system PENH/PENELOPE performs simulations of coupled electron-photon-proton transport. A few examples of simulation results are presented to reveal the influence of nuclear interactions on proton transport processes and on the calculation of dose distributions from proton beams.

## Key claim

The paper presents a practical model for including nuclear effects in Monte Carlo proton transport simulations, but the authors acknowledge that a detailed microscopic description of the proton-nucleus interaction is not practicable, and that the central-potential approximation used for the nuclear optical-model becomes inconsistent at high energies due to Lorentz-FitzGerald contraction destroying spherical symmetry.

## Method

The differential cross section (DCS) for elastic proton-atom collisions was computed by combining two separate calculations: an eikonal approximation for scattering by the screened Coulomb potential of the atomic electron cloud (using Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater electron densities), and a conventional partial-wave analysis with a global optical-model potential for scattering by the bare finite nucleus. Inelastic nuclear reactions were incorporated using data from ENDF-6 formatted files, which provide cross sections, multiplicities, and angle-energy distributions of all reaction products. These models were implemented in the Monte Carlo code PENH, an extension of the PENELOPE code, which uses class II (mixed) simulation schemes for charged particle transport.

## Key numbers

- The DCS database covers elements with atomic number Z from 1 to 99 and proton kinetic energies between 100 keV and 1 GeV.
- The nuclear radius is approximately 1.2 times A^(1/3) fm, where A is the mass number.
- The atomic screening radius is approximately Z^(-1/3) times the Bohr radius a0 = 5.29 x 10^4 fm.
- The de Broglie wavelength of protons is 4.5 fm at 1 MeV and 0.44 fm at 100 MeV.
- The eikonal approximation is considered valid for scattering angles up to about 200/(k*R_at) radians or 0.1*pi radians, whichever is smaller.
- The partial-wave series requires calculation of more than 100,000 phase-shifts for convergence.

## Context

This work extends the PENELOPE/PENH code system, which originally only considered electromagnetic interactions with nuclei as point charges (Salvat 2013), to include nuclear effects in proton transport. The approach uses global optical-model potentials from the nuclear physics literature (Becchetti and Greenlees 1969; Koning and Delaroche 2003) and standard ENDF-6 nuclear data files, enabling realistic simulations of coupled electron-photon-proton transport for applications such as proton therapy dose calculations. The paper enables future studies of how nuclear interactions affect dose distributions from proton beams, particularly the influence of nuclear reactions as a sink for primary protons and a source of secondary particles.
