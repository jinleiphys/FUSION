# Density Matrix Renormalization

> The Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) has become a powerful numerical method that can be applied to low-dimensional strongly correlated fermionic and bosonic systems. It allows for a very precise calculation of static, dynamic and thermodynamic properties. Its field of applicability has now extended beyond Condensed Matter, and is successfully used in Statistical Mechanics and High Energy Physics as well. In this article, we briefly review the main aspects of the method. We also comment on some of the most relevant applications so as to give an overview on the scope and possibilities of DMRG and mention the most important extensions of the method such as the calculation of dynamical properties, the application to classical systems, inclusion of temperature, phonons and disorder and a recent modification for the {\it ab initio} calculation of electronic states in molecules.

# Density Matrix Renormalization

## Key claim
The Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) allows for systematic truncation of Hilbert space by keeping the most probable states describing a wave function (e.g., the ground state) rather than the lowest energy states kept in previous real-space renormalization techniques, achieving unprecedented accuracy for low-dimensional strongly correlated systems.

## Method
The method starts from a small system and gradually increases its size by adding sites. At each iteration, the Hamiltonian is constructed for the full "universe" (superblock) and its ground state is obtained. The density matrix is constructed on one part of the system, treating the other part as a statistical bath, and the eigenstates of the density matrix with the highest eigenvalues are kept as the most relevant states. These states are used to change basis and reduce all operators, and the process continues until the desired system size is reached, with a finite system algorithm available to improve precision at each fixed length.

## Key numbers
- Spin-1 Heisenberg chain: for hundreds of sites, a precision of 10^{-10} for the ground state energy can be achieved
- Spin gap in S=1 Heisenberg chain: Delta = 0.41050 J
- Spin correlation length in S=1 Heisenberg chain: 6.03 lattice spacings
- S=2 antiferromagnetic Heisenberg open chain: spin gap Delta = 0.085 J, spin correlation length of 49 lattice spacings (up to 350 sites)
- S=1/2 isotropic Heisenberg model: for N=100 sites, error of 10^{-5} achieved keeping m=150 states per block
- Water molecule test: offset of 0.00024 Hartrees with respect to exact ground state energy (keeping up to 25 orbitals and m approximately 200 states per block)
- Anisotropic S=1/2 Heisenberg model: relative errors for spin susceptibility less than 10^{-3} down to temperatures of order 0.01 J keeping m=80 states

## Context
This paper reviews the DMRG method developed by White (1992) that overcame the poor performance of standard real-space renormalization group procedures on interacting systems, such as the 1D Hubbard model where keeping m approximately 1000 states still gave 5-10% error for 16 sites (Bray and Chui). The method has been extended to classical systems via the transfer matrix (Nishino), finite temperature calculations using Trotter-Suzuki methods (Bursill, Xiang and Wang), phonon problems (Jeckelmann and White), and ab initio molecular calculations (White). These developments enable precise calculations of static, dynamic, and thermodynamic properties in low-dimensional systems, with applications now extending beyond condensed matter into statistical mechanics and high energy physics.
