# The crust of neutron stars

> The structure of the crust of a neutron star is completely determined by the experimentally measured nuclear masses up to a density of the order of 10^11 g.cm^{-3}. At higher densities, the composition of the crust still remains uncertain, mainly due to the presence of ``free'' superfluid neutrons which affect the properties of the nuclear ``clusters''. After briefly reviewing calculations of the equilibrium structure of the crust, we point out that the current approach based on the Wigner-Seitz approximation does not properly describe the unbound neutrons. We have recently abandoned this approximation by applying the band theory of solids. We have shown that the dynamical properties of the free neutrons are strongly affected by the clusters by performing 3D calculations with Bloch boundary conditions.

## Key claim

The structure of the outer crust of a neutron star is completely determined by experimentally measured nuclear masses only up to a density of about 10^11 g.cm^{-3}; at higher densities the composition remains uncertain, mainly because the current Wigner-Seitz approximation does not properly describe the unbound superfluid neutrons, and the author has recently applied band theory of solids to show that the dynamical properties of these free neutrons are strongly affected by the nuclear clusters.

## Method

The author reviews the equilibrium structure of the neutron star crust under the cold catalyzed matter hypothesis, minimizing total energy per nucleon with beta equilibrium and charge neutrality. For the inner crust, the author abandons the Wigner-Seitz approximation and instead applies the band theory of solids, performing 3D calculations with Bloch boundary conditions to study the topology of the Fermi surface and the effective mass of unbound neutrons, while initially ignoring pairing effects.

## Key numbers

- The outer crust structure is determined by experimental nuclear masses up to a density of about 6 x 10^10 g.cm^{-3} (Ruster et al., 2006).
- The neutron drip density is approximately 4 x 10^11 g.cm^{-3}, estimated from E_0 = -16 MeV and S_0 = 32 MeV.
- In the inner crust, the effective neutron mass can reach up to about 15 m_n at a baryon density n_b = 0.03 fm^{-3} (Chamel, 2005).
- At a baryon density of about 1.9 x 10^13 g.cm^{-3}, including neutron pairing increases the cluster charge Z by about 8 (from Z ~ 52) and the Wigner-Seitz cell radius by about 3.5 fm (Baldo et al., 2005).
- The pasta phases cover a small range of densities near the crust-core interface, with energy differences between shapes typically less than about 1 keV.fm^{-3}.

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent quantum calculations of the inner crust pioneered by Negele and Vautherin (1973), which used the Wigner-Seitz approximation, and addresses the limitations of that approximation for describing unbound neutrons. The band theory approach, analogous to solid-state physics, enables more accurate calculations of crustal properties such as neutron superfluidity and effective mass, which are essential for interpreting observations of neutron star cooling, glitches, and gravitational wave emission. The results are a first step toward realistic calculations that could replace the Wigner-Seitz approximation in future studies of the inner crust.
