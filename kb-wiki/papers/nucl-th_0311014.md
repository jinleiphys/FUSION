# Is bimodal fission an indirect experimental evidence for pionic radioactivity?

> In this paper new predictions for the spontaneous pion emission accompanied by fission are presented for all nuclei with Z between 100 and 108. The bimodal fission as an indirect experimental evidence for the pionic radioactivity is demonstrated. The experimental tests of this important connection are suggested.

# Is bimodal fission an indirect experimental evidence for pionic radioactivity?

## Key claim

The authors propose that bimodal fission observed in heavy nuclides (Z between 100 and 108) may be indirect experimental evidence for nuclear pionic radioactivity (NPIR), where spontaneous pion emission accompanies fission. The connection is conditional: it holds only if the symmetric fission mode proceeds via a two-step process in which a pion on mass shell is created in an intermediate step, and the predicted pionic yields exceed unity for certain nuclei.

## Method

The authors apply a fission-like model from prior work to calculate the ratio of pionic decay width to spontaneous fission width (Γπ/ΓSF) for all nuclei with atomic numbers 100 ≤ Z ≤ 108. The model uses a parameter δα(A,Z) derived from the liquid-drop model with shell corrections, where the fissility parameter θ = Z²/A − 37.5 and the pion mass mπ enter via Δθα. The authors classify nuclei as SF-nuclides (Γπ/ΓSF < 1), πF-nuclides (Γπ/ΓSF > 1), or T-transition nuclides (Γπ/ΓSF = 1), and compare these predictions to known bimodal fission data for isotopes of Fm, Md, No, Lr, Rf, Sg, and Hs.

## Key numbers

- For ²⁵⁸Fm, the predicted pionic yield is 0.95, with the expectation that if the bimodal fission interpretation via unitarity saturation is correct, the yield should be higher than 1.
- For ²⁵⁷Fm, the predicted pionic yield is 1.4 × 10⁻¹⁴, supporting its role as a transition nuclide.
- For ²⁵⁹Fm and ²⁶⁰Fm, predicted pionic yields are 5.1 × 10⁻² and 5.6 × 10⁻², respectively, both potentially being πF-nuclides.
- For ²⁵⁰No, the predicted pionic yield is 6.67, the highest among the listed nuclides.
- For ²⁵⁸No and ²⁶²No, predicted pionic yields are 7.1 × 10⁻² and 1.2 × 10⁻², respectively.
- For ²⁴²Fm, the predicted pionic yield is 3.3 × 10⁻², suggesting it may be a πF-nuclide if the model underestimates yields.
- For seaborgium isotopes: ²⁵⁸Sg (1.3 × 10⁷), ²⁵⁹Sg (6.1 × 10⁶), ²⁶⁰Sg (1.2 × 10⁸), ²⁶¹Sg (1.3 × 10¹¹), ²⁶³Sg (5.2 × 10²⁷), and for ²⁶⁴Hs (1.2 × 10²), all predicted to be πF-nuclides.
- The high-energy mode of bimodal fission in ²⁵⁸Fm has total kinetic energy near 233 MeV, while the low-energy mode lies near 200 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the concept of nuclear pionic radioactivity introduced by Ion, Ivascu, and Ion-Mihai (1985) and applies it to explain the bimodal fission phenomenon first observed in Fm isotopes by Hulet et al. (1980s). The authors build on the observation by other theorists (Refs. [11] in the paper) that new fission valleys can explain bimodal fission, and on the suggestion by the authors of Ref. [12] that symmetric fission in these nuclei may relate to a new phase of nuclear matter. The predictions enable experimental tests using π-fission detection methods to search for spontaneous pion emission in transfermium and superheavy element (SHE) regions, and suggest that the usual SHE island of stability around ²⁹⁸[114] may be destroyed by dominant pionic radioactivity.
