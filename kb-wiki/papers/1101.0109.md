# QCD at finite density

> Developments in QCD at finite density are reviewed. I begin by discussing some new algorithms which have been applied to other theories with sign problems. Then I discuss the method of analytic continuation in QCD using a series expansion and review some of the results obtained using this method. By now there are several different simulations using the method, and together they give estimates of the systematic lattice effects, which turn out to be controlled. Finally I discuss a direct comparison of some of these lattice predictions with new experimental data which results in a very pleasant agreement.

# QCD at finite density

## Key claim
The paper reviews evidence that the sign problem in QCD at finite baryon density can be fruitfully attacked using analytic continuation via a Madhava-Maclaurin series expansion in the chemical potential, yielding lattice predictions that show "very pleasant agreement" with experimental heavy-ion collision data, though the direct application of new algorithms (complex Langevin, worm algorithm) to QCD is not yet at a mature stage.

## Method
The review examines three approaches: (1) reweighting methods (Budapest group, Bielefeld-Swansea algorithm) and two newer algorithms tested on the 3-d XY model, the worm algorithm (which transforms fields into a flux representation to eliminate the sign problem) and the complex Langevin method (which complexifies fields while keeping noise real); (2) the Madhava-Maclaurin series expansion method, where the pressure is expanded in powers of μ/T around μ=0 and the non-linear susceptibilities (NLS) are computed from μ=0 lattice simulations; (3) comparison of lattice predictions for cumulant ratios of conserved quantities (electric charge, baryon number, strangeness) with experimental data from the STAR collaboration at RHIC.

## Key numbers
- Signal-to-noise ratios for fermion traces entering NLS evaluation were measured on a 4×24³ lattice at T/T_c=0.75 with N_f=2 and m_π=230 MeV using staggered quarks, and at T/T_c=0.84 with N_f=2+1 using P4 quarks; in both cases N_v=400 noise vectors were used.
- Near T_c, autocorrelations between successive configurations are of order 200–250 trajectories; at 2T_c, autocorrelation time drops to about 4 trajectories.
- The ratio of CPU time for measurement to that for generating a decorrelated configuration is 0.24 near T_c (with N_v=500) and 4.5 at 2T_c (with N_v=100).
- At T=0.94T_c with two flavours of staggered quarks (m_π=230 MeV, lattice spacing 1/(6T)), the radius of convergence analysis shows finite-volume effects: when LT increases from 2 to 4, n_*(L) increases significantly.
- Extrapolation to physical m_π shows a 15% drop in the value of μ^E (the critical chemical potential) relative to the m_π=230 MeV value.
- The Padé analysis indicates a width of the critical region of about Δμ/T^E ≃ 0.25.
- The freezeout curve is parametrized using T_c=175 MeV.

## Context
This review extends the line of work on lattice QCD at finite density that began with the sign problem analysis by Forcrand (2008) and the random matrix theory estimates of sign fluctuations by the random matrix theory community. The series expansion method was first described by the author and collaborators (2003) and demonstrated in quenched QCD; subsequent work by the same group (2005, 2008) and by the RBC-Bielefeld collaboration (2009, 2010) produced estimates of the QCD critical point. The paper enables direct comparison of lattice predictions with heavy-ion collision data from the STAR collaboration (2010), following the proposal of cumulant ratios by the CPOD collaboration (2009). The freezeout parametrization follows Cleymans (2006). The paper also notes that the Columbia plot shows the physical point corresponds to a thermal crossover, and that the line of critical points is far from the physical point (the pion needs to be about 10 times lighter and the strange quark about 3 times lighter).
