# Review of Multi-messenger observations of neutron rich matter

> At very high densities, electrons react with protons to form neutron rich matter. This material is central to many fundamental questions in nuclear physics and astrophysics. Moreover, neutron rich matter is being studied with an extraordinary variety of new tools such as the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) and the Laser Interferometer Gravitational Wave Observatory (LIGO). We describe the Lead Radius Experiment (PREX) that uses parity violating electron scattering to measure the neutron radius of 208Pb. This has important implications for neutron stars and their crusts. We discuss X-ray observations of neutron star radii. These also have important implications for neutron rich matter. Gravitational waves (GW) open a new window on neutron rich matter. They come from sources such as neutron star mergers, rotating neutron star mountains, and collective r-mode oscillations. Using large scale molecular dynamics simulations, we find neutron star crust to be very strong. It can support mountains on rotating neutron stars large enough to generate detectable gravitational waves. Finally, neutrinos from core collapse supernovae (SN) provide another, qualitatively different probe of neutron rich matter. Neutrinos escape from the surface of last scattering known as the neutrino-sphere. This is a low density warm gas of neutron rich matter. Neutrino-sphere conditions can be simulated in the laboratory with heavy ion collisions. Observations of neutrinos can probe nucleosyntheses in SN. We believe that combing astronomical observations using photons, GW, and neutrinos, with laboratory experiments on nuclei, heavy ion collisions, and radioactive beams will fundamentally advance our knowledge of compact objects in the heavens, the dense phases of QCD, the origin of the elements, and of neutron rich matter.

## Key claim

Multi-messenger astronomy combining electromagnetic, gravitational wave, and neutrino observations with laboratory experiments can fundamentally advance knowledge of neutron rich matter, but the interpretation of neutron star radii from X-ray data remains controversial because different models for X-ray bursts yield significantly different results (10 km versus 12 km), which either implies or rules out a phase transition to exotic QCD phases.

## Method

The paper reviews four complementary probes of neutron rich matter: (1) the PREX experiment at Jefferson Laboratory, which measures parity-violating electron scattering asymmetry from 208Pb at 1.05 GeV and five degrees to extract the neutron radius; (2) X-ray observations of neutron star radii using luminosity and temperature measurements with atmospheric models; (3) gravitational wave observations from neutron star mergers and continuous waves from rotating neutron star mountains; (4) neutrino observations from core collapse supernovae emitted from the neutrino-sphere. Large-scale molecular dynamics simulations are used to study neutron star crust strength and crystallization.

## Key numbers

- PREX first measurement: parity-violating asymmetry A_pv = 0.656 ± 0.060 (statistical) ± 0.014 (systematic) ppm for 1.05 GeV electrons on 208Pb at five degrees
- Neutron skin thickness from PREX: R_n - R_p = 0.33 +0.16/-0.18 fm for 208Pb
- Chiral perturbation theory prediction: R_n - R_p = 0.17 ± 0.03 fm for 208Pb (Hebeler et al.)
- Steiner et al. prediction from X-ray observations: R_n - R_p = 0.15 ± 0.02 fm for 208Pb, with 1.4 solar mass neutron star radius near 12 km
- Ozel et al. result: neutron star radii near 10 km or below from X-ray bursts
- Neutron star crust transition density correlated with R_n - R_p (C.J. Horowitz and J. Piekarewicz, Physical Review Letters)
- Neutron star radii are of order 10 km, 18 orders of magnitude larger than 208Pb nucleus (approximately 6 fm)

## Context

This review extends the program of connecting laboratory nuclear physics measurements to astrophysical observations, building on the correlation between the neutron radius of 208Pb and neutron star properties established by Horowitz and Piekarewicz (Physical Review Letters) and the equation of state calculations from chiral perturbation theory by Hebeler et al. The paper contrasts the conflicting neutron star radius results from Ozel et al. (small radii near 10 km suggesting a phase transition) and Steiner et al. (radii near 12 km with no phase transition). It enables future joint constraints from PREX-II, Advanced LIGO gravitational wave detections, and neutrino observations from core collapse supernovae to determine the equation of state of neutron rich matter across 18 orders of magnitude in size.
