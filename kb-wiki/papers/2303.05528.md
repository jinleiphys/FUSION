# Towards Resolving the Gallium Anomaly

> A series of experiments studying neutrinos from intense radioactive sources have reported a deficit in the measured event rate which, in combination, has reached a statistical significance of $\sim 5σ$. In this paper, we explore avenues for explaining this anomaly, both within the Standard Model and beyond. First, we discuss possible biases in the predicted cross section for the detection reaction $ν_e + ^{71}\text{Ga} \to e^- + ^{71}\text{Ge}$, which could arise from mismeasurement of the inverse process, $^{71}\text{Ge}$ decay, or from the presence of as yet unknown low-lying excited states of $^{71}\text{Ga}$. The latter would imply that not all $^{71}\text{Ge}$ decays go to the ground state of $^{71}\text{Ga}$, so the extraction of the ground state-to-ground state matrix element relevant for neutrino capture on gallium would be incorrect. Second, we scrutinize the measurement of the source intensity in gallium experiments, and we point out that a $\sim 2\%$ error in the branching ratios for $^{51}\text{Cr}$ decay would be enough to explain the anomaly. Third, we investigate the calibration of the radiochemical germanium extraction efficiency as a possible origin of anomaly. Finally, we outline several new explanations beyond the Standard Model, including scenarios with sterile neutrinos coupled to fuzzy dark matter or to dark energy, as well as a model with decaying sterile neutrinos. We critically assess the viability of these scenarios, and others that have been proposed, in a summary table.

## Key claim

The gallium anomaly, a ~5σ deficit in measured neutrino event rates from radioactive source experiments, could potentially be explained by several Standard Model effects (mismeasurement of the germanium-71 half-life, unknown excited states in gallium-71, errors in chromium-51 branching ratios, or calibration issues in germanium extraction efficiency) before invoking beyond-Standard-Model physics, though each Standard Model explanation requires a systematic error far larger than current uncertainties suggest.

## Method

The authors performed a statistical analysis of data from six gallium experiment datasets (GALLEX-1, GALLEX-2, SAGE-Ar, SAGE-Cr, BEST-inner, BEST-outer) using a chi-squared test statistic with a nuisance parameter accounting for cross-section uncertainty. They rescaled the measured-to-predicted event rate ratios to test hypotheses about the germanium-71 half-life, the fraction of decays to the gallium-71 ground state, the chromium-51 branching ratio to excited states, and the germanium extraction efficiency. For beyond-Standard-Model scenarios, they explored resonant active-to-sterile neutrino conversion via couplings to fuzzy dark matter or dark energy, and decaying sterile neutrino models.

## Key numbers

* The combined gallium anomaly has a statistical significance of ~5σ, expressed as the ratio of measured to predicted events R = 0.803 ± 0.035.
* The adopted germanium-71 half-life is T1/2 = 11.43 ± 0.03 days; explaining the anomaly would require this value to be larger by at least 2 days (67σ), and reducing significance below 3σ would require an increase of about 1 day (33σ).
* A ~2% error in the branching ratio for chromium-51 decay to the excited state of vanadium-51 (currently ~10%) would be enough to explain the anomaly; increasing the branching ratio to 12-13% would lower significance below 1σ.
* The lowest-lying known excited state of gallium-71 is at 389.94 ± 0.03 keV, above the Q-value of germanium-71 decay (232.49 ± 0.22 keV); if ~20% of germanium-71 decays went to an undiscovered low-lying state, the anomaly could be resolved.
* The chromium-51 half-life is 27.704 ± 0.004 days; the de-excitation gamma ray from the first excited state of vanadium-51 is at 320.0835 ± 0.0004 keV.
* The nominal germanium extraction efficiency from calibration measurements is εcal = 0.95 ± 0.016; resolving the anomaly would require the true efficiency to be ~0.75, a ~20% discrepancy.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the gallium anomaly previously reported by GALLEX (Anselmann 1994, Hampel 1997), SAGE (Abdurashitov 1996, 1998, 2005), and BEST (Barinov 2021, 2022), which together reached ~5σ significance. The paper critically examines Standard Model explanations that were partially considered by earlier authors (Bahcall 1997, Haxton 1998, Giunti 2022) and introduces new beyond-Standard-Model scenarios involving sterile neutrinos coupled to fuzzy dark matter or dark energy. The results enable future experimental efforts to focus on remeasuring the germanium-71 half-life, searching for low-lying excited states in gallium-71, and improving chromium-51 branching ratio measurements, while also providing a framework for evaluating sterile neutrino models against the growing tension with reactor neutrino data (Giunti 2021, Berryman 2021, STEREO 2022).
