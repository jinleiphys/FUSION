# New generation low-energy probes for ultralight axion and scalar dark matter

> We present a brief overview of a new generation of high-precision laboratory and astrophysical measurements to search for ultralight (sub-eV) axion, axion-like pseudoscalar and scalar dark matter, which form either a coherent condensate or topological defects (solitons). In these new detection methods, the sought effects are linear in the interaction constant between dark matter and ordinary matter, which is in stark contrast to traditional searches for dark matter, where the sought effects are quadratic or higher order in the underlying interaction constants (which are extremely small).

# New generation low-energy probes for ultralight axion and scalar dark matter

## Key claim

This review argues that a new generation of laboratory and astrophysical searches for ultralight (sub-eV) axion, axion-like pseudoscalar, and scalar dark matter can achieve sensitivity to effects that are linear in the interaction constant between dark matter and ordinary matter, in contrast to traditional searches which are quadratic or higher order in the underlying interaction constants. The authors present these methods as promising despite the fact that no strong positive detection has yet been made by any direct dark matter search.

## Method

The paper reviews proposed detection schemes for ultralight bosonic dark matter that forms either a coherently oscillating condensate or topological defects (solitons). For axion dark matter, the methods exploit the "axion wind" effect from derivative coupling to fermion axial-vector currents, oscillating P,T-odd electromagnetic moments (neutron electric dipole moment, nuclear Schiff moments, magnetic quadrupole moments), and oscillating electric currents along magnetic field lines. For scalar dark matter, the methods search for oscillating variations of fundamental constants (fine-structure constant and particle masses) via linear and quadratic couplings, "slow" drifts of constants from the time-averaged quadratic coupling, and transient-in-time variations from topological defects passing through detectors.

## Key numbers

- Local cold dark matter energy density: ρ_CDM^local ≃ 0.4 GeV/cm^3
- Mean dark matter energy density from WMAP: ρ_bar_DM = 1.3 × 10^{-6} GeV/cm^3
- QCD axion mass relation: m_a ≃ 6 × 10^{-5} eV × (10^{11} GeV / f_a)
- Axion condensate amplitude: φ_0 ≃ √(2 ρ_scalar) / m_φ
- Oscillation frequency of condensate: ω_φ ≃ m_φ (for non-relativistic cold dark matter)
- Lower mass limit for bosonic dark matter from de Broglie wavelength: m_φ ≳ 10^{-22} eV
- Upper mass limit for elementary bosonic dark matter: m_φ < 10^{28} eV
- Oscillating neutron electric dipole moment: d_n = 1.2 × 10^{-16} × (a_0 cos(m_a t) / f_a) e·cm
- Sidereal day duration: T_sidereal = 23.93 hours
- Axion-induced oscillating electric dipole moment of paramagnetic atom (non-relativistic): d_a = -(3 C_e a_0 m_a^2 α_s) / (2 f_a α) × e cos(m_a t)
- Relativistic corrections to paramagnetic EDM: ∼10% for moderately heavy atoms (Z ∼ 50)
- Scalar DM mass range for first clock-based search: 10^{-24} eV ≲ m_φ ≲ 10^{-16} eV
- Scalar DM mass range for BBN constraints from quadratic couplings: m_φ ≲ 10^{-4} eV
- Scalar DM mass range for linear coupling BBN constraints: m_φ ≲ 10^{-16} eV

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework for ultralight bosonic dark matter detection beyond traditional WIMP scattering searches (SuperCDMS 2014, CoGeNT 2011, CRESST 2014, DAMA 2008, LUX 2013, XENON 2013) and beyond traditional haloscope (ADMX 2010) and helioscope (CAST 2014) experiments. The linear-in-coupling methods build on earlier proposals for macroscopic force searches (Wilczek 1983), atomic magnetometry (Lamoreaux 1996, Walsworth 2008, Romalis 2009), and torsion pendulum experiments (Heckel 2011). The first laboratory clock-based search for ultralight scalar DM using dysprosium spectroscopy has recently been completed (Budker 2015), and the paper enables future searches using atomic clocks, laser interferometers, global networks of atomic magnetometers (GNOME 2013), and pulsar timing arrays (PTA 2010).
