# Partial correlation analysis in ultra-relativistic nuclear collisions

> We show that the method of partial covariance is a very efficient way to introduce constraints (such as the centrality selection) in data analysis in ultra-relativistic nuclear collisions. The technique eliminates spurious event-by-event fluctuations of physical quantities due to fluctuations of control variables. Moreover, in the commonly used superposition approach to particle production the method can be used to impose constraints on the initial sources rather than on the finally produced particles, thus separating out the trivial fluctuations from statistical hadronization or emission from sources and focusing strictly on the initial-state physics. As illustration, we use simulated data from hydrodynamics started on the wounded-quark event-by-event initial conditions, followed with statistical hadronization, to show the practicality of the approach in analyzing the forward-backward multiplicity fluctuations. We mention generalizations to the case with several constraints and other observables, such as the transverse momentum or eccentricity correlations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that partial covariance analysis provides a simpler and more general alternative to narrow-bin centrality selection for removing spurious correlations from control variables in ultra-relativistic nuclear collisions, with the caveat that the equality between partial and conditional covariance holds exactly only when the expectation value of the physical variable at fixed control variable is an affine function of the control variable.

## Method

The authors define partial covariance as cov(X_i, X_j • Z) = cov(X_i, X_j) - cov(X_i, Z_a)[cov(Z)]^{-1}_{ab} cov(Z_b, X_j), which projects out components spuriously correlated to control variables. They test the method using simulated events from 3+1D viscous hydrodynamics with wounded-quark initial conditions and statistical hadronization via THERMINATOR, applying the technique to forward-backward multiplicity correlations. The method is combined with a superposition approach to impose constraints at the level of initial sources rather than final particles.

## Key numbers

- The forward-backward partial correlation for source multiplicities C(S_F, S_B • S_C) is given by C(N_F, N_B) - [C(N_F, N_C) C(N_B, N_C)] / [var(N_C) - ω(m)⟨N_C⟩], where ω(m) = 1 for Poisson-distributed particle emission from sources
- In the Bzdak-Teaney model, C(S_F, S_B • S_C) = [var(Q_A - Q_B) / ⟨Q_A + Q_B⟩²] × (y₁ y₂ / y_b²), where Q_A and Q_B are numbers of wounded quarks in each nucleus and y_b is the beam rapidity
- The partial variance of a single physical variable X with one constraint Z equals var(X) - cov(X, Y)² / var(Y)

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to eliminate centrality fluctuations in nuclear collision data, referencing prior methods by Gazdzicki (1992), Gorenstein (2011), Bhalerao (2014), Broniowski (2017), and Rogly (2018). The partial covariance approach is widely used in other scientific fields (Cramer, 1946; Krzanowski, 2000) and is here adapted to nuclear collisions with the novel capability of imposing constraints at the level of initial sources rather than final particles. The method enables future application to real experimental data for extracting information on correlations between multiplicities of initial sources, and can be generalized to other observables such as transverse momentum or harmonic flow coefficients.
