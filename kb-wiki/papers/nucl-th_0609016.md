# Antibound States and Halo Formation in the Gamow Shell Model

> The open quantum system formulation of the nuclear shell model, the so-called Gamow Shell Model (GSM), is a multi-configurational SM that employs a single-particle basis given by the Berggren ensemble consisting of Gamow states and the non-resonant continuum of scattering states. The GSM is of particular importance for weakly bound/unbound nuclear states where both many-body correlations and the coupling to decay channels are essential. In this context, we investigate the role of l=0 antibound (virtual) neutron single-particle states in the shell model description of loosely bound wave functions, such as the ground state wave function of a halo nucleus 11Li.

# Antibound States and Halo Formation in the Gamow Shell Model

## Key claim
Including an $l=0$ antibound (virtual) single-particle state in the Berggren basis for Gamow Shell Model calculations of bound halo nuclei such as $^{11}$Li is not beneficial: it requires two-to-four times more discretized scattering states to reach the same precision as a basis without the antibound state, because the exponentially growing wave function of the virtual state interferes destructively with the non-resonant continuum.

## Method
The authors performed one-body Berggren completeness tests by expanding a loosely bound $1s_{1/2}$ wave function from a Woods-Saxon potential in bases generated by different Woods-Saxon potentials, with and without including the antibound $1s_{1/2}$ pole. They then carried out two-body Gamow Shell Model calculations for the ground state of $^{11}$Li using a schematic model with two valence neutrons coupled to a $^{9}$Li core, comparing convergence of the ground-state energy and wave function amplitudes when the $s_{1/2}$ Berggren basis either included or excluded the antibound $1s_{1/2}$ state.

## Key numbers
- The $1s_{1/2}$ antibound state energy used in the Woods-Saxon potential $WS_1$ was $-0.002955$ MeV; the loosely bound $1s_{1/2}$ state in $WS_2$ was at $-0.0329$ MeV; the well-bound $1s_{1/2}$ state in $WS_3$ was at $-1.0372$ MeV.
- Without an antibound state in the basis, 40 to 50 non-resonant scattering states achieved $10^{-6}$ precision for the $s_{1/2}$ wave function; with the antibound state included, 150 non-resonant scattering states were necessary for the same precision.
- For the $^{11}$Li ground state, the energy converged to 0.1 keV precision with 20 $s_{1/2}$ shells when no antibound pole was present, but required 50 shells when the antibound pole was included.
- The $^{11}$Li ground-state binding energy relative to $^{9}$Li was set to $E_B = -0.295$ MeV; the $0p_{1/2}$ resonance had energy $E=0.24$ MeV and width $\Gamma=118$ keV.
- In the basis with the antibound state, the $(1s_{1/2})^2$ configuration contributed only about 10% of the squared amplitude, while the mixed $(1s_{1/2}$ $s_{1/2})$ configurations contributed $-0.5887$ (real part), cancelling about half of the $(s_{1/2})^2$ contribution of $1.0034$ (real part).

## Context
This work extends earlier Gamow Shell Model studies by Betan et al. (2004, 2005) that first argued for the importance of the antibound $1s_{1/2}$ state in $^{11}$Li halo formation, but also noted destructive interference with the continuum. The present paper systematically quantifies the numerical cost of including the antibound state and concludes it is disadvantageous for bound-state calculations. The results contradict the notion that explicit inclusion of the virtual state is necessary; instead, a Berggren basis with only non-resonant $s_{1/2}$ continuum states close to the real-$k$ axis is more efficient. This enables more practical GSM calculations of halo nuclei and suggests that the density matrix renormalization group approach by Rotureau et al. (2006) can handle the required increased density of discretized states around $k=0$ without including the antibound pole.
