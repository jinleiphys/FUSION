# Schematic baryon models, their tight binding description and their microwave realization

> A schematic model for baryon excitations is presented in terms of a symmetric Dirac gyroscope, a relativistic model solvable in closed form, that reduces to a rotor in the non-relativistic limit. The model is then mapped on a nearest neighbour tight binding model. In its simplest one-dimensional form this model yields a finite equidistant spectrum. This is experimentally implemented as a chain of dielectric resonators under conditions where their coupling is evanescent and good agreement with the prediction is achieved.

# Schematic baryon models, their tight binding description and their microwave realization

## Key claim

The authors present a schematic model for baryon excitations using a symmetric Dirac gyroscope that can be mapped onto a nearest-neighbour tight-binding model and realized experimentally as a chain of dielectric resonators, but the model is explicitly limited to a simplified one-dimensional form yielding a finite equidistant spectrum, and the comparison with actual baryon mass spectra is only qualitative, using crude identification of quantum numbers and adjustable parameters.

## Method

The authors first construct a relativistic quantum rotor (Dirac gyroscope) based on three postulates for rigidity, then simplify it to a prolate (cigar-shaped) ultra-relativistic limit where the Hamiltonian reduces to a term proportional to the angular momentum component L_x, producing a finite equidistant spectrum. This operator is mapped onto a nearest-neighbour tight-binding Hamiltonian with coupling strengths Δ_m = ε √[(l-m)(l+m+1)], where ε = c ħ √(M/I_xx) sets the level spacing. The experimental realization uses dielectric disks (radius 4 mm, height 5 mm, refractive index ≈6) sandwiched between two metallic plates, coupled evanescently, with distances between disks adjusted according to the required coupling strengths derived from the angular momentum matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- Disk radius: r_D = 4 mm; disk height: h_D = 5 mm; refractive index n_r ≈ 6
- Optimal plate distance: h = 13 mm; optimal inter-disk distance range: d = 9-14 mm
- Next-nearest neighbour coupling less than 7.5% of nearest neighbour coupling in the optimal parameter range
- Coupling fit parameters: Δ_0 = 3.94 GHz, γ = 0.20 mm⁻¹, C = 0.006 (exponential approximation); Δ′_0 = 1.53 GHz, γ′ = 0.148 mm⁻¹, C′ = 0.0039 GHz (Bessel function fit)
- Level spacing for gyroscope realizations: ε = 25 MHz
- Experimental chains realized with N = 7, 9, and 11 disks
- For nucleon fit: M = 1 GeV, I_xx/I = √(13/10), I M c²/ħ² = 1/10
- For lambda baryon fit: I_xx/I = √(145/100)
- Estimated nucleon size from model: λ ∼ 10⁻¹⁵ m, compared to proton charge radius R_p = 0.8 × 10⁻¹⁵ m and neutron mean square charge radius √⟨R_n²⟩ = 0.4 × 10⁻¹⁵ m

## Context

This work extends the Dirac gyroscope model previously introduced by Sadurní (2009) for relativistic quantum rotors, and connects it to the broader program of emulating relativistic systems using classical wave experiments, particularly microwave realizations of graphene-like systems (Kuhl et al. 2010, Bellec et al. 2013, Bittner et al. 2010, 2012). The schematic baryon model relates to earlier multi-quark approaches such as the Isgur-Karl model (Isgur and Karl 1978, 1979), the Capstick-Isgur model (Capstick and Isgur 1986), and spectrum-generating algebra approaches (Bijker et al. 1994, 2000), but abandons the multiparticle approach to focus on structural parameters like size, internal spin, and moments of inertia. The experimental technique enables future emulation of more complex relativistic and non-relativistic systems, potentially including quantum graphs for improved suppression of higher-order neighbour couplings.
