# Meson Spectral Functions at Finite Temperature and Isospin Density with Functional Renormalization Group

> The pion superfluid and the corresponding Goldstone and soft modes are investigated in two-flavor quark-meson model with functional renormalization group. By solving the flow equations for the effective potential and the meson two-point functions at finite temperature and isospin density, the critical temperature for the superfluid increases sizeably in comparison with solving the flow equation for the potential only. The spectral function for the soft mode shows clearly a transition from meson gas to quark gas with increasing temperature and a crossover from BEC to BCS pairing of quarks with increasing isospin density.

# Meson Spectral Functions at Finite Temperature and Isospin Density with Functional Renormalization Group

## Key claim

The critical temperature for pion superfluidity increases sizeably when the flow equations for both the effective potential and the meson two-point functions are solved together, compared to solving the flow equation for the potential only. The spectral function of the soft mode reveals a transition from meson gas to quark gas with increasing temperature and a crossover from BEC to BCS pairing of quarks with increasing isospin density, but these conclusions are drawn from calculations performed only in the normal phase (above the critical temperature) to avoid the complexity of off-diagonal propagator elements in the superfluid phase.

## Method

The authors employ the functional renormalization group (FRG) approach to the two-flavor quark-meson model, solving flow equations for the effective potential and for the meson two-point functions at finite temperature and isospin chemical potential. They use a three-dimensional optimized regulator function, perform the Matsubara sums analytically, and then carry out an analytic continuation from Euclidean to Minkowski space to obtain the retarded propagators and spectral functions. The flow equations for the two-point functions are truncated by treating the three-point and four-point vertices as scale-dependent but momentum-independent couplings extracted from the global minimum of the effective potential.

## Key numbers

- The critical isospin chemical potential at zero temperature for pion superfluidity is $\mu_I^c = m_\pi(0,0)/2 = 67$ MeV, where $m_\pi = 134$ MeV is the vacuum pion mass.
- The quark mass in vacuum is set to $m_q = 300$ MeV, and the pion decay constant is $f_\pi = 94$ MeV.
- The ultraviolet cutoff is $\Lambda = 900$ MeV; the initial parameters for the potential are $m_\Lambda^2/\Lambda^2 = 0.618$ (case A, potential only) or $0.645$ (case B, including two-point functions), $\lambda_\Lambda = 1$, and $c/\Lambda^3 = 0.0023$ (case A) or $0.0045$ (case B).
- The sigma meson mass on the phase boundary is always above $300$ MeV; the quark mass drops below $100$ MeV for isospin chemical potential $\mu_I > 130$ MeV, opening the quark decay channel.
- In case A (potential only), the pion spectral function peak is located at $94$ MeV in vacuum, which is far from the known pion mass of $134$ MeV; case B (including two-point functions) reproduces the correct pion mass.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the QCD phase diagram at finite isospin density using effective models such as the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model (He, 2005), the quark-meson model (Kamikado, 2012), and the linear sigma model (Svanes, 2010; Phat, 2011). It goes beyond the potential-level FRG calculations of those earlier works by including the flow equations for meson two-point functions, enabling the extraction of spectral functions and pole masses. The BEC-BCS crossover in pion superfluid, previously studied by Sun (2007) and He (2013), is here characterized via the spectral function of the soft mode slightly above the phase transition line. The method of analytic continuation for spectral functions follows the approach of Pawlowski (2015), Strodthoff (2016), and Tripolt (2013, 2014). The results enable future calculations of transport coefficients and a more detailed understanding of the nature of collective excitations in pion superfluid.
