# Neutrino Fast Flavor Instability in three dimensions for a Neutron Star Merger

> The flavor evolution of neutrinos in core collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers is a critically important unsolved problem in astrophysics. Following the electron flavor evolution of the neutrino system is essential for calculating the thermodynamics of compact objects as well as the chemical elements they produce. Accurately accounting for flavor transformation in these environments is challenging for a number of reasons, including the large number of neutrinos involved, the small spatial scale of the oscillation, and the nonlinearity of the system. We take a step in addressing these issues by presenting a method which describes the neutrino fields in terms of angular moments. We apply our moment method to neutron star merger conditions and show it simulates fast flavor neutrino transformation in a region where this phenomenon is expected to occur. By comparing with particle-in-cell calculations we show that the moment method is able to capture the three phases of growth, saturation, and decoherence, and correctly predicts the lengthscale of the fastest growing fluctuations in the neutrino field.

## Key claim

The authors show that a two-moment method with a maximum entropy closure can qualitatively capture fast flavor neutrino oscillations in neutron star merger conditions, but the moment method predicts more flavor conversion than the more accurate particle-in-cell method, with the final electron neutrino number density asymptoting to 64% of its original value in the moment method versus 74% in the particle-in-cell calculation.

## Method

The authors extract neutrino angular moments from a general-relativistic two-moment radiation hydrodynamics simulation of a neutron star merger (Foucart 2016) and use them as initial conditions for a quantum kinetic equation solver implemented in the FLASH code. They evolve the first two angular moments (energy density and flux) of the neutrino density matrix in a 3D Cartesian domain with periodic boundary conditions, using the maximum entropy closure to relate the pressure tensor to the evolved moments. They compare these results to particle-in-cell calculations using the EMU code, which evolves neutrino quantum states along discrete directions rather than angular moments.

## Key numbers

- Initial electron neutrino number density: $1.422 \times 10^{33} \, \mathrm{cm}^{-3}$
- Initial electron antineutrino number density: $1.915 \times 10^{33} \, \mathrm{cm}^{-3}$
- Initial heavy lepton neutrino plus antineutrino number density: $1.965 \times 10^{33} \, \mathrm{cm}^{-3}$
- Simulation domain: cube with side length $L = 7.87 \, \mathrm{cm}$, divided into 128 cells per side
- Growth rate for FLASH moment method: $8.09 \times 10^{10} \, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$; for EMU 2-flavor PIC: $5.58 \times 10^{10} \, \mathrm{s}^{-1}$
- Fastest growing mode wavenumber: $6.39 \, \mathrm{cm}^{-1}$ for FLASH; $4.79 \, \mathrm{cm}^{-1}$ for EMU (2-flavor), corresponding to wavelength $\lambda \sim 1.0 \, \mathrm{cm}$
- Final electron neutrino number density in moment method: 64% of original value; in PIC calculation: 74% of original value
- At saturation peak, off-diagonal $|N_{ex}|$ in moment method: 16% of original electron neutrino number density; in PIC: 18%
- Ratio of vacuum to self-interaction potential: $10^{-6}$ at the conditions studied
- Computational time for FLASH moment method: factor of 30 less than EMU PIC calculation

## Context

This work extends previous studies of fast flavor oscillations in neutron star mergers that used parameterized outcomes (Wu 2017, Li 2021, Just 2022) or multi-angle methods in idealized domains (Duan 2021, Tamborra 2021, Richers 2022). The moment method approach offers a computationally cheaper alternative to particle-in-cell or multi-angle Boltzmann calculations, with the authors noting that FLASH and comparable codes already take millions of CPU hours for 3D classical moment-based neutrino transport. The method enables future coupling of flavor transformation with hydrodynamic simulations, though the authors identify several open issues including the need for quantum closures rather than classical maximum entropy closures, inclusion of collision terms, and validation across a wider range of angular distributions.
