# Jet modification in the next decade: a pedestrian outlook

> In this review, intended for non-specialists and beginners, we recount the current status of the theory of jet modification in dense matter. We commence with an outline of the "traditional" observables which may be calculated without recourse to event generators. These include single and double hadron suppression, nuclear modification factor versus reaction plane etc. All of these measurements are used to justify both the required underlying physical picture of jet modification as well as the final obtained values of jet transport coefficients. This is followed by a review of the more modern observables which have arisen with the ability to reconstruct full jets, and the challenges faced therein. This is followed by a preview of upcoming theoretical developments in the field and an outlook on how the interface between these developments, phenomenological improvements, and upcoming data will allow us to quantitatively determine properties of the medium which effect the modification of hard jets.

## Key claim

This review argues that the successful description of jet modification data at both RHIC and LHC energies requires a specific set of physical ingredients in perturbative QCD-based calculations, but notes that the extracted jet transport coefficient $\hat{q}_0$ differs by up to a factor of 5 between different theoretical approaches when constrained to the same hydrodynamic background.

## Method

The author reviews the theoretical status of jet modification in dense matter by comparing multiple perturbative QCD approaches (ASW, AMY, GLV, and Higher-Twist schemes) that were constrained to run on identical fluid-dynamical backgrounds. The review outlines five essential features that any successful calculation must incorporate: near-eikonal trajectories of hard jets, inclusion of vacuum radiation and vacuum-medium interference, running of the strong coupling constant with the hard scale, prevalence of ordered virtuality evolution over unordered rare emissions, and incorporation of realistic hydrodynamic simulations of the medium.

## Key numbers

- The jet transport coefficient $\hat{q}_0$ (maximum value at the center of most central collisions at $\tau = 0.6$ fm/c) differed by a factor of 5 between the ASW approach and other approaches when fit to RHIC data at $\sqrt{s} = 200$ GeV per nucleon pair
- RHIC data established three regions in the particle spectrum: soft bulk with $p_T \lesssim 2$ GeV, hard sector with $p_T \gtrsim 8$ GeV, and intermediate region with $2$ GeV $\lesssim p_T \lesssim 8$ GeV
- LHC heavy-ion runs occurred at $\sqrt{s} = 2.75$ TeV per nucleon pair, while RHIC runs were at $\sqrt{s} = 200$ GeV per nucleon pair
- Calculations based on strong coupling approaches requiring $\hat{q}_0 > 5$ GeV$^2$/fm at RHIC energies were disfavored by LHC data
- The single parameter $\hat{q}_0$ was set using data points around $p_T \simeq 10$ GeV for 0-5% most central collisions at RHIC

## Context

This review extends the comparison work of Bass (2008), where three different jet modification schemes (ASW, AMY, and HT) were first constrained to run on the same hydrodynamic background from Nonaka (2006). The paper builds on earlier perturbative QCD approaches by Gyulassy, Wang, and Wiedemann, as well as strong coupling approaches based on AdS/CFT by Casalderrey-Solana and Gubser. The review enables future quantitative determination of medium properties affecting hard jet modification through the interface of upcoming theoretical developments, phenomenological improvements, and new data from both RHIC and LHC.
