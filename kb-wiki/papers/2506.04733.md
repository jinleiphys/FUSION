# The quark-gluon plasma: diagnosis with thermal hadron production from the early history until detailed characterization at high energy colliders

> In nuclear collisions at relativistic energies, matter is created which resembles closely the matter that filled all space until about 15 microseconds after the big bang. Here we summarize selected aspects of the research that led to the establishment of this new sub-field of physics and briefly describe its current `state of the art' with emphasis on matter creation through thermal particle production. In particular, we will focus on particle production at low transverse momentum and explain how its analysis sheds light on one of the key open questions, i.e. what is the mechanism of hadronization of colored objects such as quarks and gluons.

## Key claim

The paper argues that thermal hadron production in high-energy nuclear collisions provides a direct diagnostic of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP), with the key hedge being that the chemical freeze-out temperature extracted from hadron yields coincides with the lattice QCD pseudo-critical temperature for the chiral crossover only for collision energies where the baryon chemical potential is within the range accessible by lattice QCD expansion techniques (μ_B/T ≤ 3), while the phase structure at higher baryon density remains uncertain.

## Method

The authors review the statistical hadronization model (SHM) and hadron resonance gas (HRG) approach, where the partition function is built from all known hadrons and resonances with their masses, quantum numbers, and decay branching ratios, neglecting interactions among hadrons except through resonance formation. They compare SHM predictions for integrated hadron yields (particle multiplicities) to experimental data from AGS, SPS, RHIC, and LHC energies, extracting chemical freeze-out temperatures and baryon chemical potentials by fitting the measured yields of light (u,d,s) hadrons. The S-matrix formalism is introduced to incorporate two-body scattering phase shifts for a more accurate treatment of interactions beyond the simple resonance gas approximation.

## Key numbers

- Pseudo-critical temperature for chiral crossover from lattice QCD: T_pc = 156.5 ± 1.5 MeV (HotQCD Collaboration, 2018) and 158.0 ± 0.6 MeV (Borsanyi et al., 2020)
- Critical temperature for massless u,d quarks at vanishing chemical potential: T_c ≈ 132^{+3}_{-6} MeV
- Initial energy density at LHC (√s_NN = 2.76 TeV): ε_BJ ≈ 14.5 GeV/fm³, more than a factor of 30 above the critical energy density
- Initial temperature at LHC: T ≈ 307 MeV (estimated from quark-gluon gas with two quark flavors)
- Energy density at chemical freeze-out along the phase boundary: ε_crit ≈ 0.46 GeV/fm³
- Rapidity shift saturates at approximately two units from √s_NN ≈ 17.3 GeV upwards, implying fractional energy loss of about 86%
- Hagedorn temperature range extracted from fits: 135 < T_H < 190 MeV
- Lattice QCD expansion techniques cover the regime of μ_B/T ≤ 3

## Context

This review extends the line of work initiated by Hagedorn (1965) on statistical bootstrap and thermal particle production, and by Shuryak (1977) who coined the term "quark-gluon plasma." It synthesizes results from lattice QCD calculations (HotQCD Collaboration, 2018; Borsanyi et al., 2020) with experimental data from ALICE at LHC, STAR at RHIC, and earlier SPS experiments (WA97, NA57). The close agreement between chemical freeze-out temperatures and the chiral crossover temperature from lattice QCD enables the use of hadron yields as a probe of the QCD equation of state, while open questions remain about the existence and location of a critical end point (CEP) at high baryon density, which future experiments at facilities like FAIR and NICA aim to address.
