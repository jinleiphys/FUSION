# Neutron star tidal deformability and equation of state constraints

> Despite their long history and astrophysical importance, some of the key properties of neutron stars are still uncertain. The extreme conditions encountered in their interiors, involving matter of uncertain composition at extreme density and isospin asymmetry, uniquely determine the stars' macroscopic properties within General Relativity. Astrophysical constraints on those macroscopic properties, such as neutron star masses and radii, have long been used to understand the microscopic properties of the matter that forms them. In this article we discuss another astrophysically observable macroscopic property of neutron stars that can be used to study their interiors: their tidal deformation. Neutron stars, much like any other extended object with structure, are tidally deformed when under the influence of an external tidal field. In the context of coalescences of neutron stars observed through their gravitational wave emission, this deformation, quantified through a parameter termed the \emph{tidal deformability}, can be measured. We discuss the role of the tidal deformability in observations of coalescing neutron stars with gravitational waves and how it can be used to probe the internal structure of Nature's most compact matter objects. Perhaps inevitably, a large portion of the discussion will be dictated by GW170817, the most informative confirmed detection of a binary neutron star coalescence with gravitational waves as of the time of writing.

## Key claim

The tidal deformability of neutron stars, measurable through gravitational wave signals from binary coalescences, provides a complementary probe of neutron star interior structure that is sensitive to the equation of state, but the most informative constraints as of 2020 come from a single event (GW170817) and remain limited by the weakness of subsequent detections such as GW190425.

## Method

The author reviews the theoretical formalism for computing the tidal deformability parameter lambda from the Einstein equations by matching interior and exterior metric solutions at the neutron star surface, following the approach of Hinderer (2007). The review then describes how tidal effects enter the gravitational wave phase as a 5PN-order correction proportional to the binary tidal parameter tilde-Lambda, and summarizes how this parameter was extracted from the GW170817 signal using Bayesian parameter estimation with waveform models calibrated against numerical relativity simulations.

## Key numbers

- GW170817 binary component masses around 1.35 solar masses, merging approximately 40 Mpc from Earth
- GW170817 signal accumulated approximately 4200 phase cycles between 23 Hz and merger
- The dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda varies from order 10^4 for 1 solar mass stars to order 10 for the most massive neutron stars possible
- For a 1.4 solar mass neutron star, typical Lambda values are order 100 for realistic equations of state
- For tilde-Lambda = 400 (consistent with GW170817), the tidal term prefactor is 7800 times larger than the leading-order Newtonian term
- The upper limit on Lambda at 1.4 solar masses from GW170817 is Lambda_1.4 less than 800
- Fully relativistic computation of the Love number k_2 differs by 10-20 percent compared to Newtonian results
- GW190425 component masses estimated at 1.4-1.9 solar masses (assuming small spins) or 1.1-2.5 solar masses (assuming arbitrary spins)

## Context

This review extends the long-standing effort to constrain neutron star properties through macroscopic observables, building on prior work using pulsar mass measurements (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013, Cromartie 2019), electromagnetic radius constraints (Miller 2019, Riley 2019), and terrestrial nuclear experiments (Danielewicz 2002, Abrahamyan 2012). The tidal deformability formalism was developed by Hinderer (2007), Damour and Nagar (2009), and Binnington and Poisson (2009), with the first gravitational-wave measurement enabled by the GW170817 detection (LIGO/Virgo 2017). Future detections of post-merger signals could provide complementary information about the equation of state and potential phase transitions.
