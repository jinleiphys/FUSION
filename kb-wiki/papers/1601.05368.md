# Rotating neutron stars with exotic cores: masses, radii, stability

> A set of theoretical mass-radius relations for rigidly rotating neutron stars with exotic cores, obtained in various theories of dense matter, is reviewed. Two basic observational constraints are used: the largest measured rotation frequency (716 Hz) and the maximum measured mass ($2\;M_\odot$). Present status of measuring the radii of neutron stars is described. The theory of rigidly rotating stars in general relativity is reviewed and limitations of the slow rotation approximation are pointed out. Mass-radius relations for rotating neutron stars with hyperon and quark cores are illustrated using several models. Problems related to the non-uniqueness of the crust-core matching are mentioned. Limits on rigid rotation resulting from the mass-shedding instability and the instability with respect to the axisymmetric perturbations are summarized. The problem of instabilities and of the back-bending phenomenon are discussed in detail. Metastability and instability of a neutron star core in the case of a first-order phase transition, both between pure phases, and into a mixed-phase state, are reviewed. The case of two disjoint families (branches) of rotating neutron stars is discussed and generic features of neutron-star families and of core-quakes triggered by the instabilities are considered.

## Key claim

The paper reviews theoretical mass-radius relations for rigidly rotating neutron stars with exotic cores (hyperon, quark, or boson-condensate matter), but emphasizes that current observational uncertainties in radius measurements prevent a definitive constraint on the equation of state, and that the existence of a 2 solar mass neutron star and a 716 Hz pulsar provide the only firm observational bounds.

## Method

The authors survey multiple theoretical models of dense matter equations of state (EOS) for neutron star cores containing hyperons, quarks, or boson condensates, and compute axisymmetric hydrostatic equilibria in general relativity using both the slow-rotation approximation (Hartle-Thorne formalism to second order in spin frequency) and exact numerical solutions of Einstein's equations. They compare these rotating configurations with standard nucleonic neutron star models, applying observational constraints from the maximum measured mass (2.01 solar masses) and the fastest measured rotation frequency (716 Hz). The analysis includes stability criteria for mass-shedding (Keplerian limit), axisymmetric perturbations, and first-order phase transitions between normal and exotic phases.

## Key numbers

* Maximum observed neutron star mass: 2.01 ± 0.04 solar masses (Antoniadis 2013), used as a strong constraint on the equation of state
* Fastest measured rotation frequency: 716 Hz (PSR J1748-2446a, Hessels 2006)
* Normal nuclear density: ρ₀ = 2.7 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³ (corresponding to baryon number density n₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³)
* Central density of a 2 solar mass neutron star may reach approximately 7ρ₀
* Theoretical maximum rotation frequency for stably rotating neutron stars: 1500 Hz to 2000 Hz, depending on the equation of state
* Critical ratio of kinetic to potential energy for dynamical bar-mode instability in rigidly rotating stars: β ≈ 0.24
* Critical β for secular bar-mode instability: β ≈ 0.14
* Minimum mass for a non-rotating neutron star: approximately 0.1 solar masses

## Context

This review extends the work of Lattimer (2012) on neutron star masses and the compilation of Haensel, Potekhin, and Yakovlev (2007) on neutron star structure, while addressing the specific challenges posed by exotic core compositions. The paper builds on the discovery of two 2 solar mass pulsars (Demorest 2010; Antoniadis 2013) and the fastest millisecond pulsar at 716 Hz (Hessels 2006) to constrain dense matter theories. It enables future work by identifying the observational precision needed from upcoming X-ray missions such as NICER, Athena, and LOFT to distinguish between nucleonic and exotic core equations of state, and by clarifying the conditions under which back-bending phenomena and disjoint neutron star families can arise from first-order phase transitions.
