# Boson Interferometry in High Energy Physics

> Intensity interferometry and in particular that due to Bose Einstein correlations (BEC) constitutes at present the only direct experimental method for the determination of sizes and lifetimes of sources in particle and nuclear physics. The measurement of these is essential for an understanding of the dynamics of strong interactions which are responsible for the existence and properties of atomic nuclei. Moreover a new state of matter, quark matter, in which the ultimate constituents of matter move freely, is within the reach of present accelerators or those under construction. The confirmation of the existence of this new state is intimately linked with the determination of its space-time properties. Furthermore BEC provides information about quantum coherence which lies at the basis of the phenomenon of Bose-Einstein condensation seen in many chapters of physics. Coherence and the associated classical fields are essential ingredients in modern theories of particle physics including the standard model. Last but not least besides this "applicative" aspect of BEC, this effect has implications for the foundations of quantum mechanics including the understanding of the concept of ``identical particles". Recent theoretical developments in BEC are reviewed and their application in high energy particle and heavy-ion reactions is analyzed. The treated topics include: a) a comparison between the wave-function approach and the space-time approach based on classical currents, which predicts "surprising" particle anti-particle BEC b) the study of final state interactions c) the use of hydrodynamics d) the relation between correlations and multiplicity distributions.

## Key claim

Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) constitute the only direct experimental method for determining sizes and lifetimes of sources in particle and nuclear physics, but the interpretation of correlation functions is complicated by the fact that the empirical $\lambda$ parameter does not correctly describe partial coherence; a proper treatment requires quantum statistical methods that distinguish coherent and chaotic field components.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical developments in BEC after 1989-1990, comparing the wave-function approach with the quantum field theoretical (second quantization) approach based on classical currents. It analyzes the use of the Glauber-Sudarshan ${\cal P}$ representation for density matrices, the application of coherent and squeezed coherent states, and the generalization of correlation functions to include partial coherence through superposition of coherent and chaotic fields. The review also covers final state interactions, hydrodynamic models, and the relation between correlations and multiplicity distributions.

## Key numbers

- The intercept of the second order correlation function $C_2(k,k)$ in $\bar{p}-p$ annihilation reactions consistently exceeds the canonical value of 2, reaching values up to 4.
- For a Lorentzian spectrum, the second order correlation function for partially coherent fields takes the form $C_2(x,x')=1+2p(1-p)e^{-|x-x'|/\xi}+p^{2}e^{-2|x-x'|/\xi}$, where $p$ is the chaoticity varying between 0 (purely coherent) and 1 (totally chaotic).
- The analysis by Kulka and Lörstad used BEC data from $pp$ and $\bar{p}p$ reactions at $\sqrt{s}=53$ GeV to compare correlation function forms.
- For squeezed states, the transformation parameters are $\mu = \cosh r$ and $\nu = \sinh r$ with $r = \frac{1}{2} \log (E_a/E_b)$.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of BEC beyond the original GGLP effect discovered by Goldhaber, Goldhaber, Lee and Pais (1959-1960) and the Hanbury-Brown and Twiss method from optics (mid-1950s). It builds on the space-time generalization of the classical current formalism developed around 1993 (Andreev, Plümer, Weiner) and addresses limitations of the wave-function approach identified by Boal, Gelbke, and Jennings. The work enables improved searches for quark-gluon plasma through space-time measurements of fireballs in heavy-ion reactions and provides a framework for detecting quantum coherence in strong interactions, which is essential for understanding Bose-Einstein condensation phenomena.
