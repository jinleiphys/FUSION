# Potential Constraints to Neutrino-Nucleus Interactions Based on Electron Scattering Data

> A thorough understanding of neutrino-nucleus interactions physics is crucial to achieving precision goals in broader neutrino physics programs. The complexity of nuclei comprising the detectors and limited understanding of their weak response constitutes one of the biggest systematic uncertainties in neutrino experiments - both at intermediate energies affecting the short- and long-baseline neutrino programs as well as at lower energies affecting coherent scattering neutrino programs. While electron and neutrino interactions are different at the primary vertex, many underlying relevant physical processes in the nucleus are the same in both cases, and electron scattering data collected with precisely controlled kinematics, large statistics and high precision allows one to constrain nuclear properties and specific interaction processes. To this end, electron-nucleus scattering experiments provide vital complementary information to test, assess and validate different nuclear models and event generators intended to be used in neutrino experiments. In fact, for many decades, the study of electron scattering off a nucleus has been used as a tool to probe the properties of that nucleus and its electromagnetic response. While previously existing electron scattering data provide important information, new and proposed measurements are tied closely to what is required for the neutrino program in terms of expanding kinematic reach, the addition of relevant nuclei and information on the final states hadronic system.

# Potential Constraints to Neutrino-Nucleus Interactions Based on Electron Scattering Data

## Key claim

Electron scattering data can provide vital complementary constraints on nuclear models and event generators for neutrino experiments, but the author emphasizes that while existing data are useful, new dedicated measurements with kinematics, targets, and final-state information specifically matched to neutrino program needs are essential for achieving precision goals in both accelerator-based and low-energy neutrino experiments.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical and experimental connections between electron-nucleus and neutrino-nucleus scattering, showing that both processes share the same initial nuclear state description and final-state interaction effects, with the vector current conserved between electromagnetic and weak interactions via the conserved vector current (CVC) hypothesis. It surveys current and planned electron scattering experiments (e4nu/CLAS at JLab, LDMX at SLAC, A1 at MAMI) and parity-violating electron scattering (PVES) experiments (PREX, CREX, Qweak, MREX) that can constrain weak form factors and nuclear responses relevant to neutrino interactions across energy scales from tens of MeV to several GeV.

## Key numbers

- PREX on $^{208}$Pb: measured parity-violating asymmetry $A_{pv} = 0.550 \pm 0.018$ ppm at $q^2 = 0.00616$ GeV$^2$, constraining neutron skin thickness to $\pm 1.3\%$ in $\delta R_n$
- CREX on $^{48}$Ca: planned measurement at $q^2 = 0.0297$ GeV$^2$ with projected $\pm 0.7\%$ precision on $\delta R_n$
- Qweak on $^{27}$Al: measured $A_{pv} = 2.16 \pm 0.19$ ppm at $q^2 = 0.0236$ GeV$^2$, constraining $\delta R_n$ to $\pm 4\%$
- MREX on $^{208}$Pb: planned measurement at $q^2 = 0.0073$ GeV$^2$ with projected $\pm 0.52\%$ precision on $\delta R_n$
- e4nu/CLAS at JLab: beam energies of 1, 2, 4, 6 GeV with scattering angles $\theta_e > 5^\circ$ on targets including H, D, He, C, Ar, $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca, Fe, Sn
- LDMX at SLAC: planned beam energies of 4.0 and 8.0 GeV with $\theta_e < 40^\circ$ on W, Ti, Al targets
- A1 at MAMI: beam energies from 50 MeV to 1.5 GeV with scattering angles from $7^\circ$ to $160^\circ$ on targets including H, D, He, C, O, Al, Ca, Ar, Xe
- E12-14-012 at JLab: beam energy 2.222 GeV with electron scattering angles $15.5^\circ \leq \theta_e \leq 21.5^\circ$ on Ar, Ti, Al, C targets

## Context

This conference paper from NuFACT 2022 extends the long tradition of using electron scattering to probe nuclear structure, as reviewed in the Snowmass white paper by Ankowski (2022), and specifically addresses how electron data can constrain neutrino-nucleus interaction uncertainties that dominate systematic errors in experiments like T2K, NOvA, DUNE, and HyperK. The work connects to the COHERENT collaboration's detection of coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering (CEvNS) and the need for percent-level weak form factor knowledge to disentangle beyond-Standard-Model signals, as discussed by Tomalak (2020) and Van Dessel (2020). The paper enables a roadmap for future electron scattering measurements that directly target the kinematic phase space of DUNE's near detector (as estimated using GENIE 3.0.6) and the tens-of-MeV regime needed for supernova neutrino detection.
