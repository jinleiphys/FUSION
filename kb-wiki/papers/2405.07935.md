# Study of the direct $^{16}{\rm O}(p, γ)^{17}{\rm F}$ astrophysical capture reaction within a potential model approach

> A potential model is applied for the analysis of the astrophysical direct nuclear capture process $^{16}$O(p,$γ)^{17}$F. The phase-equivalent potentials of the Woods-Saxon form for the p$-^{16}$O interaction are examined which reproduce the binding energies and the empirical values of ANC for the $^{17}$F(5/2$^+$) ground and $^{17}$F(1/2$^+$) ($E^*$=0.495 MeV) excited bound states from different sources. The best description of the experimental data for the astrophysical $S$ factor is obtained within the potential model which yields the ANC values of 1.043 fm$^{-1/2}$ and 75.484 fm$^{-1/2}$ for the $^{17}$F($5/2^{+}$) ground and $^{17}$F($1/2^{+}$) excited bound states, respectively. The zero-energy astrophysical factor $S(0)=9.321$ KeV b is obtained by using the asymptotic expansion method of D. Baye. The calculated reaction rates within the region up to 10$^{10}$ K are in good agreement with those from the R-matrix approach and the Bayesian model in both absolute values and temperature dependence.

## Key claim

The potential model approach, using Woods-Saxon phase-equivalent potentials fitted to reproduce both binding energies and empirical asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) from different sources, can describe the experimental astrophysical S-factor data for the 16O(p,γ)17F reaction; the best description is obtained with the VM2 potential, which yields ANC values of 1.043 fm^{-1/2} for the ground state and 75.484 fm^{-1/2} for the first excited state, and the resulting zero-energy S-factor S(0)=9.321 keV b is consistent with the ANC method of Artemov (2009) and the NACRE compilation.

## Method

The authors solved the two-body Schrodinger equation for the p-16O system using Woods-Saxon central and spin-orbit potentials with a spherical Coulomb term. Four potential models (VM1 through VM4) were fitted to reproduce the experimental binding energies of the 17F(5/2+) ground state (-0.6005 MeV) and 17F(1/2+) excited state (-0.1052 MeV), along with empirical ANC values from four different sources (Feruz (2022), Artemov (2009), Gagliardi (1999), and Iliadis (2022)). The potentials were also adjusted to reproduce experimental phase shifts for p-16O elastic scattering in the 2S1/2 and 2D5/2 partial waves, and the astrophysical S-factor was calculated from the electric dipole (E1), electric quadrupole (E2), and magnetic dipole (M1) transition matrix elements between the initial scattering and final bound states.

## Key numbers

- ANC for 17F(5/2+) ground state: 1.043 fm^{-1/2} (from the best-fit VM2 model, reproducing the empirical value from Artemov (2009))
- ANC for 17F(1/2+) excited state (E* = 0.495 MeV): 75.484 fm^{-1/2} (from the best-fit VM2 model, reproducing the empirical value from Artemov (2009))
- Zero-energy astrophysical S-factor total: S(0) = 9.321 keV b (from the VM2 model using the asymptotic expansion method of Baye)
- Zero-energy S-factor for ground state only: 0.406 keV b (VM2 model)
- Zero-energy S-factor for excited state only: 8.915 keV b (VM2 model)
- Reaction rate at T9 = 0.001: 4.66 x 10^{-63} cm^3 mol^{-1} s^{-1} (VM2 model)
- Reaction rate at T9 = 10: 6.25 x 10^3 cm^3 mol^{-1} s^{-1} (VM2 model)
- Forbidden state energy in the 2S1/2 partial wave for VM2: -30.32 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous potential model studies of the 16O(p,γ)17F reaction by systematically comparing four phase-equivalent Woods-Saxon potentials fitted to different empirical ANC values from proton transfer reactions, including those from Feruz (2022), Artemov (2009), Gagliardi (1999), and Iliadis (2022). The calculated reaction rates agree well with the phenomenological R-matrix approach of Iliadis (2008) up to T9=10 and the Bayesian model of Iliadis (2022) up to T9=1, validating the potential model as a simpler alternative for astrophysical rate calculations. The results enable more reliable reaction rate inputs for stellar nucleosynthesis models of the CNO cycle in hot-bottom burning AGB stars, particularly for predicting the 17O/16O abundance ratio.
