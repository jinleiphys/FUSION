# Quantum Chromodynamics Resolution of the ATOMKI Anomaly in ${\rm {^4He}}$ Nuclear Transitions

> Observations of anomalous angular correlations in electron-positron pairs produced from excited states of $^{4}$He, $^{8}$Be and $^{12}$C nuclei have been suggested as due to the creation and subsequent decay of a new light particle of mass $\sim$17 MeV. In this work, we investigate the possibility that the source of the observed signals is a set of new excitation channels created by the 12-quark hidden-color Fock state within the ${\rm {^4He}}$ nuclear wavefunction dubbed the ``hexadiquark.'' We calculate the invariant $e^+e^-$ mass spectrum for the electromagnetic transition from a new excitation of $^{4}$He, estimating its differential and total decay width. We find that we can fit the shape of the anomalous signal with the QCD Fock state at excitation energy $\rm E^{*} = 17.9 \pm 1$ MeV and a Gaussian form factor for the electromagnetic decay. We address the physical issues with the fit parameters using properties of the hexadiquark state, in particular the three weakly repulsive $\rm 6_C$ interactions of $\rm SU(3)_C$ between diquark pairs. Experimental tests of our model are described in detail. In light of this work, we emphasize the need for independent experimental confirmation or refutation of the ATOMKI results as well as dedicated experiments to search for the proposed new excitations of ${\rm ^4He}$ and other $Î±$-cluster nuclei.

# Quantum Chromodynamics Resolution of the ATOMKI Anomaly in ${\rm {^4He}}$ Nuclear Transitions

## Key claim
The anomalous electron-positron angular correlations observed by the ATOMKI collaboration in $^4$He nuclear transitions can be explained by the excitation and decay of a 12-quark hidden-color Fock state (the "hexadiquark") within the $^4$He nuclear wavefunction, rather than by a new 17 MeV particle (X17). The fit requires an excitation energy of $17.9 \pm 1$ MeV and a Gaussian form factor with parameter $\lambda = 6.2 \pm 0.5$ MeV, which the authors acknowledge is an order of magnitude smaller than expected for a typical nuclear transition.

## Method
The authors calculated the invariant $e^+e^-$ mass spectrum for the electromagnetic transition ${\rm ^4He^*}(E^*) \to {\rm ^4He} + e^+e^-$ using a three-body decay formalism with a matrix element depending on $q^2 = m_{e^+e^-}^2$ and the electron angle. They extracted the ATOMKI signal template from published data, fitted it with a Gaussian (mean 16.8 MeV, width 0.7 MeV) and a polynomial background, then compared theoretical curves with form factors $F(q^2) \propto \exp(q^2/\lambda^2)$ to the template. The excitation energy $E^*$ was allowed to float from the known 20.21 MeV value to a new value corresponding to hexadiquark excitations.

## Key numbers
- Excitation energy of the proposed new $^4$He state: $E^* = 17.9 \pm 1$ MeV
- Form factor parameter from the successful fit: $\lambda = 6.2 \pm 0.5$ MeV
- ATOMKI reported invariant mass of the lepton pair: $m_X = 16.94 \pm 0.12(\text{stat.}) \pm 0.21(\text{syst.})$ MeV
- Gaussian fit to the signal template: peak at $16.8 \pm 0.23$ MeV, width $\sigma = 0.70 \pm 0.17$ MeV
- Mass difference between first known excited state and ground state of $^4$He: 20.21 MeV
- Known excited state widths: $^4$He$^*(20.21)$ has $\Gamma = 0.50$ MeV; $^4$He$^*(21.01)$ has $\Gamma = 0.84$ MeV
- Predicted additional excitations in $^4$He at approximately 36 MeV and 54 MeV (multiples of 17.9 MeV)

## Context
This work extends the hexadiquark hidden-color Fock state proposal by West (2020) to explain the ATOMKI anomalies observed by Krasznahorkay and collaborators (2015, 2019, 2021, 2022) in $^4$He, $^8$Be, and $^{12}$C nuclear transitions. It contradicts the new particle (X17) interpretation that has motivated searches by NA64, MEG II, and PADME collaborations. The model predicts that the signal should appear in all $A \geq 4$ $\alpha$-cluster nuclei but not in $A=3$ nuclei, deuterium, or nucleons, and enables experimental tests via missing mass measurements in $e + ^4$He scattering and searches for coincident gamma rays at 2.31 MeV and 3.11 MeV from two-stage decays.
