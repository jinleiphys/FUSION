# Effective Field Theory for Nuclear Physics

> I review the current status of the application of effective field theory to nuclear physics, and its present implications for nuclear astrophysics.

# Effective Field Theory for Nuclear Physics

## Key claim

Effective field theory (EFT) provides a systematic framework for connecting low-energy nuclear observables to the underlying theory of QCD, but the formal power-counting schemes that define how operators are ordered remain unsettled: Weinberg counting works phenomenologically despite formal problems in certain scattering channels, KSW counting fails in the coupled $^3S_1$-$^3D_1$ channels at next-to-next-to-leading order, and the newer BBSvK counting attempts to combine the advantages of both.

## Method

The paper reviews the construction of two distinct EFTs for nuclear systems: the pionless EFT ($\nopi$) for momenta much less than the pion mass $m_\pi$, where only nucleon and photon fields appear and interactions are described by contact operators; and the pionful EFT for momenta above $m_\pi$, where pions are explicit degrees of freedom and the chiral expansion of the nucleon-nucleon potential is inserted into the Schroedinger equation. The author compares three power-counting schemes (Weinberg, KSW, and BBSvK) and surveys applications to two-nucleon, three-nucleon, and halo-nuclear systems, as well as the quark-mass dependence of scattering lengths.

## Context

This review extends the program of applying chiral EFT to nuclear physics that began with Weinberg's pioneering work in the early 1990s, and it directly compares three competing power-counting schemes: Weinberg counting (used extensively by Epelbaum et al. and by Entem and Machleidt), KSW counting (which Fleming, Mehen, and Stewart showed fails in the $^3S_1$-$^3D_1$ coupled channels), and the newer BBSvK counting. The paper enables future connections between lattice QCD calculations (which will be performed at unphysically large quark masses for the foreseeable future) and physical nuclear observables through EFT matching, and it identifies the need for higher-order calculations and more precise single-nucleon parameters to reduce uncertainties in multi-nucleon predictions.
