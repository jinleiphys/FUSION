# Competition of the shell closure and deformations across the doubly magic $^{78}$Ni

> The properties of the neutron-rich isotope $^{78}$Ni, long postulated to be doubly magic, have been extensively explored through recent experimental and theoretical studies. Confirmations of robust shell closures at $Z=28$ and $N=50$ as well as hints of competing deformations in neighboring isotopes have been obtained. Innovations of a thick liquid hydrogen target system with vertex reconstructions and the in-beam $γ$-ray spectroscopy technique have facilitated detailed investigations into the nuclear structure of these extreme systems. Proton knockout reactions conducted at relativistic energies have provided the first experimental evidence of shape coexistence at the cornerstone nucleus $^{78}$Ni and its vicinity. As the nuclear structure around $^{78}$Ni influences the description of very neutron-rich systems and r-process nucleosynthesis, these findings underscore the importance of further investigations. This review encapsulates the recent results concerning the nuclear structure at the vicinity of $^{78}$Ni on both experimental and theoretical aspects. It outlines prospective research directions that could further illuminate this complex and intriguing area of the nuclear chart.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that while $^{78}$Ni exhibits robust doubly-magic shell closures at $Z=28$ and $N=50$, experimental evidence from proton knockout reactions reveals shape coexistence, a competing deformed configuration, at the cornerstone nucleus itself, challenging the traditional picture of a rigid spherical ground state.

## Method

The review synthesizes results from in-beam $\gamma$-ray spectroscopy experiments conducted at the RIBF facility using a thick liquid hydrogen target system (MINOS) with vertex reconstruction via a coaxial time-projection chamber, coupled with the DALI2 NaI(Tl) scintillator array. Proton knockout reactions at relativistic energies from a $^{238}$U primary beam were used to populate excited states of $^{79}$Cu and $^{78}$Ni, with de-excitation $\gamma$ rays measured in inverse kinematics. The experimental data are compared with large-scale shell-model calculations (LNPS, PFSDG-U, A3DA-m), Monte Carlo Shell Model (MCSM) calculations, first-principles Coupled-Cluster (CCSD(T)) and In-Medium Similarity Renormalization Group (IM-SRG) approaches, and beyond-mean-field models.

## Key numbers

- The first excited state ($2^+_1$) of $^{78}$Ni was measured at 2.6 MeV, indicating doubly-magic nature.
- A second $2^+$ state at 2.9 MeV was identified in the two-proton knockout channel, suggesting shape coexistence.
- The inclusive cross sections of one- and two-proton knockout reactions to produce $^{78}$Ni were almost five times smaller than those of other isotopes in the region.
- The neutron separation energy of $^{78}$Ni is $S_n = 5.60(57)$ MeV from the atomic mass evaluation AME2020.
- The CCSD(T) calculation reproduced the first $2^+$ energy of $^{78}$Ni at 2.45 MeV.
- The IM-SRG(2) approximation predicted the $2^+_1$ energy at 3.25 MeV.
- In $^{80}$Zn, a direct feeding to the ground state from a 2627(39)-keV state was observed.
- The long-lived isomer in $^{79}$Zn was confirmed at 943 keV.

## Context

This review extends the experimental and theoretical work on the $N=40$ Island of Inversion, which Santamaria (2015) showed extends towards $N=50$ in cobalt and iron isotopes, while nickel isotopes seemingly sustain the shell closure. The results confirm the shell evolution picture where the ordering of $\pi f_{5/2}$ and $\pi p_{3/2}$ states swaps at $^{75}$Cu ($N=46$), as first observed by Flanagan (2009). The findings enable future investigations into how the $N=40$ Island of Inversion extends beyond $N=50$, the driving forces of shell evolution, and the limits of existence of neutron-rich isotopes relevant to r-process nucleosynthesis.
