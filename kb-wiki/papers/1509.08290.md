# Clustering in nuclei from ab initio nuclear lattice simulations

> Nuclear Lattice Effective Field Theory is a new many-body approach that is firmly rooted in the symmetries of QCD. In particular, it allows for truly ab initio calculations of nuclear structure and reactions. In this talk, I focus on the emergence of alpha-clustering in nuclei based on this approach. I also discuss various recent achievements, the deficiencies of the chiral forces used at present and the prospects to improve upon these and the calculations of nuclear properties and dynamics.

## Key claim

Nuclear Lattice Effective Field Theory (NLEFT) can produce ab initio calculations of nuclear structure and reactions, but the current chiral forces at next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO) exhibit systematic overbinding for nuclei with atomic number A ≥ 16, which is attributed to deficiencies in the soft interactions rather than the computational method.

## Method

The authors discretize space-time on a lattice with spacing a = 1.97 fm and perform a Wick rotation to Euclidean time, using the time evolution operator exp(-Ht) to filter out ground states. Nucleons are placed on lattice sites with interactions derived from chiral Effective Field Theory at NNLO, including one- and two-pion exchanges, multi-nucleon operators, Coulomb forces, and isospin-breaking effects. The simulations use projection Monte Carlo with approximate SU(4) symmetry to suppress sign oscillations, and a "triangulation" method with SU(4) symmetric filters to improve extrapolation to large Euclidean time.

## Context

This work extends the NLEFT approach previously applied to 12C (Epelbaum et al., 2012) and the Hoyle state (Epelbaum et al., 2011) to heavier alpha-cluster nuclei up to 28Si, and to 16O structure including electromagnetic transitions. The results confirm the tetrahedral and square alpha-cluster configurations in 16O predicted by Wheeler (1937) and later studied by Tohsaki (2001) and Freer (2005). The overbinding problem at NNLO is shared by other ab initio methods using soft potentials (Hagen et al., 2012; Jurgenson et al., 2013; Roth et al., 2011). The symmetry-sign extrapolation method enables future calculations of halo nuclei and exploration of the limits of nuclear stability, while the adiabatic projection method (Rupak et al., 2013) opens the door to ab initio calculations of scattering and inelastic reactions.
