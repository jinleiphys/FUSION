# Collective vibrational states with fast iterative QRPA method

> An iterative method we previously proposed to compute nuclear strength functions is developed to allow it to accurately calculate properties of individual nuclear states. The approach is based on the quasi-particle-random-phase approximation (QRPA) and uses an iterative non-hermitian Arnoldi diagonalization method where the QRPA matrix does not have to be explicitly calculated and stored. The method gives substantial advantages over conventional QRPA calculations with regards to the computational cost. The method is used to calculate excitation energies and decay rates of the lowest lying 2+ and 3- states in Pb, Sn, Ni and Ca isotopes using three different Skyrme interactions and a separable gaussian pairing force.

# Collective vibrational states with fast iterative QRPA method

## Key claim

The iterative Arnoldi method previously developed for computing nuclear strength functions is generalized to accurately calculate properties of individual nuclear states, not just smoothed strength distributions, by using the Implicitly Restarted Arnoldi (IRA) method with an electromagnetic pivot vector as the initial guess to accelerate convergence.

## Method

The authors implement the QRPA using an iterative non-hermitian Arnoldi diagonalization method (via the ARPACK library) where the QRPA matrix is never explicitly constructed or stored; instead, the action of the matrix on a vector is computed by generating transition densities in coordinate space, calculating the linearized HFB fields from those densities, and then multiplying by the U and V matrices. The method is applied within the spherical HOSPHE code using a harmonic oscillator basis, and results are benchmarked against a conventional QRPA diagonalization using the HFBTHO code for the test nucleus 18O with the SLy4 interaction.

## Key numbers

- For the benchmark nucleus 18O with SLy4 and a delta pairing force (V0 = -200 MeV fm^3, Nmax=5), the HFB ground-state energy is -131.6770225 MeV; the 2+ excitation energy is 8.69120 MeV and the B(E2) value is 0.3352 e^2 fm^4, with both codes agreeing to roughly 10^-4 precision.
- For 214Pb with SLy4 and a separable Gaussian pairing force, using 17 oscillator shells (Nmax=16) gives a QRPA matrix dimension of 8016, compared to 59296 for 33 shells; the truncation error relative to the 33-shell calculation is 0.01 MeV for the energy and 0.002 (e b)^2 for the reduced transition probability.
- With 17 shells and a tolerance of 0.001, the average time to calculate the lowest state for a lead isotope is 6.5 minutes on an Intel Core i7-2600K at 3.4 GHz, requiring an average of 2663 iterations.
- For 192Pb, extracting 10, 20, and 30 positive-energy eigenstates requires 1974, 2648, and 3427 iterations respectively.
- The separable Gaussian pairing strengths (range a = 0.660 fm) are: for SLy4, Gn = 655 and Gp = 600 MeV fm^3; for SkM*, Gn = 610 and Gp = 550 MeV fm^3; for SkX, Gn = 560 and Gp = 530 MeV fm^3.
- For Ni40 with SkX, the neutron average pairing gap is Delta_n = 1.38 MeV, and the 2+ state is obtained with both correct energy and transition strength, built from approximately 23% proton and 77% neutron excitations.

## Context

This work extends the iterative Arnoldi method for strength functions previously proposed by Toivanen et al. (2010) to enable extraction of individual eigenstates, complementing the Finite Amplitude Method (FAM) developed by Nakatsukasa and Avogadro which provides smoothed strength functions but cannot easily extract exact QRPA eigenamplitudes. The method enables systematic QRPA calculations for heavy and deformed nuclei that would otherwise be prohibited by the memory and time requirements of constructing and diagonalizing the full QRPA matrix, and it allows dynamical properties to be considered when fine-tuning density-functional parameters. The results for 2+ and 3- states in Pb, Sn, Ni, and Ca isotopes using three Skyrme interactions (SkM*, SLy4, SkX) provide benchmarks for future comparisons with experiments planned around 208Pb and with other theoretical approaches such as the generator-coordinate method (GCM) as studied by Toivanen et al. for 2+ states.
