# Correction to the Chiral Magnetic Effect from axial-vector interaction

> The recent lattice calculation at finite axial chemical potential suggests that the induced current density of the chiral magnetic effect (CME) is somehow suppressed comparing with the standard analytical formula. We show in a NJL-type model of QCD that such a suppression is a natural result when considering the influence of the attractive axial-vector interaction. We point out that the lattice result doesn't need to be quantitatively consistent with the analytical formula due to the chirality density-density correlation. We also investigate the nonperturbative effect of instanton molecules on the CME. Since an unconventional repulsive axial-vector interaction is induced, the CME will be enhanced significantly by the instanton-anti-instanton pairings. Such a prediction needs to be tested by more improved lattice simulations. We further demonstrate that the axial-vector interaction plays an important role on the $T-μ_A$ phase diagram.

## Key claim

The suppression of the chiral magnetic effect (CME) current observed in lattice QCD at finite axial chemical potential is a natural consequence of the attractive axial-vector interaction in a NJL-type model, and the lattice result does not need to be quantitatively consistent with the standard analytical formula due to the chirality density-density correlation.

## Method

The authors employ a two-flavor Polyakov-loop-enhanced NJL-type model that includes effective four-quark interactions in the vector and axial-vector channels, treating the axial-vector coupling \(G_A\) as a free parameter. They compute the induced electric current density \(\mathbf{j}_{em}\) from the mean-field thermodynamic potential using the derivative with respect to the gauge field, obtaining a modified expression \(\mathbf{j}_{em} = (5 \tilde{\mu}_A e^2 \mathbf{B})/(6\pi^2)\) where \(\tilde{\mu}_A = \mu_A - 2G_A n_A\) is the effective axial chemical potential and \(n_A\) is the chirality density determined self-consistently. The ratio \(R = \mathbf{j}_{em}/\mathbf{j}_{em}(G_A=0) = \tilde{\mu}_A/\mu_A\) is then studied as a function of \(G_A\), \(\mu_A\), temperature \(T\), and magnetic field \(B\).

## Key numbers

* For \(G_A/G_S = -0.5\) (repulsive) at \(\mu_A = 15\ \text{MeV}\), \(eB = 4\times10^{18}\ \text{Gauss}\), and \(T = 230\ \text{MeV}\), the electric current density increases by \(\sim 62\%\) relative to the \(G_A=0\) case; for \(G_A/G_S = 0.5\) (attractive), it decreases by \(\sim 27\%\).
* For \(G_A/G_S = -0.75\) at the same conditions, the current increases by \(\sim 140\%\); for \(G_A/G_S = 0.75\), it decreases by \(\sim 34\%\).
* The lattice coefficient \(C\) from Yamamoto (2011) is around \(0.013\), far less than the analytical value \(1/(2\pi^2) \simeq 0.05\); quenched lattice results give \(C\) in the range \(0.02-0.03\), corresponding to a ratio \(C/2\pi^2\) of \(0.4-0.6\) at \(T \sim 1.1 T_c\) (\(T_c \sim 270\ \text{MeV}\)).
* The model with \(G_A/G_S\) between \(0.5\) and \(1.0\) yields a ratio \(R\) in the range \(0.5-0.65\) at \(T \sim 1.1 T_c\) (\(T_c \sim 230\ \text{MeV}\)), consistent with the lattice range.
* For \(G_A/G_S = 0.5\), the ratio \(R\) decreases by \(\sim 0.07\) per \(0.1 T_c\) as temperature increases, compared to the lattice decline rate of \(\sim 0.04\) per \(0.1 T_c\) from \(317\ \text{MeV}\) to \(475\ \text{MeV}\).

## Context

This work extends the analytical CME formula of Fukushima (2008) by incorporating axial-vector interactions, which were omitted in the original derivation. It addresses the suppression observed in lattice QCD simulations by Yamamoto (2011) and Yamamoto (2011, quenched), where the induced current was significantly smaller than the standard result \(\mathbf{j}_{em} = (N_c N_f e^2 \mu_A \mathbf{B})/(2\pi^2)\). The study contrasts the predictions of the instanton-anti-instanton molecule model (IIMM), which gives a repulsive axial-vector interaction and enhanced CME, with the one-gluon exchange approximation (OGEA) or global color model (GCM), which give an attractive interaction and suppressed CME. The results enable future lattice simulations to test whether the axial-vector interaction is attractive or repulsive by measuring the temperature and chemical potential dependence of the CME coefficient.
