# Least-squares fitting applied to nuclear mass formulas. Resolution by the Gauss-Seidel method

> A numerical method optimizing the coefficients of the semi empirical mass formula or those of similar mass formulas is presented. The optimization is based on the least-squares adjustments method and leads to the resolution of a linear system which is solved by iterations according to the Gauss-Seidel scheme. The steps of the algorithm are given in detail. In practice the method is very simple to implement and is able to treat large data in a very fast way. In fact, although this method has been illustrated here by specific examples, it can be applied without difficulty to any experimental or statistical data of the same type, i.e. those leading to linear system characterized by symmetric and positive-definite matrices.

## Key claim

The paper presents a numerical method for optimizing coefficients of nuclear mass formulas using least-squares fitting solved via the Gauss-Seidel iterative scheme, claiming the method is simple to implement and can treat large datasets quickly, but the actual demonstration shows that convergence to 8-digit stability requires up to 20 million iterations for a 14-parameter model.

## Method

The authors construct a linear system from the least-squares minimization of the sum of squared deviations between experimental and theoretical binding energies, where the theoretical expression is a linear combination of independent functions with coefficients to be determined. The resulting symmetric positive-definite matrix system is solved iteratively using the Gauss-Seidel method, where each coefficient is updated sequentially using the most recent values of the other coefficients. The algorithm is applied to two cases: the standard Bethe-Weizsacker formula with four varied coefficients and a more elaborate liquid drop mass model with 14 varied coefficients, using experimental binding energies from the AME 2020 atomic mass evaluation.

## Key numbers

- For the Bethe-Weizsacker formula fit to 2452 nuclei (N>=8, Z>=8): optimized coefficients are a_V = 15.5461, a_S = 16.9598, a_C = 0.7036, a_a = 23.0254, yielding a root mean square deviation of 3.06 MeV
- For the liquid drop mass model with 14 varied coefficients: optimized coefficients include a_V = 16.3985, a_S = -24.8569, a_C = -0.7207, yielding a root mean square deviation of 1.81 MeV
- With shell and pairing corrections added to the liquid drop model, the root mean square deviation is reduced to 0.864 MeV
- Execution time for 80 million iterations was approximately 21 seconds for the 4-parameter Bethe-Weizsacker case and approximately 91 seconds for the 14-parameter liquid drop case, on a desktop PC with Intel i3 4160 processor and 16 GB DDR3 memory
- Stability (8 digits after decimal point unchanged) was reached after 100,000 iterations for the 4-parameter case and after 20,000,000 iterations for the 14-parameter case

## Context

This work extends the application of the Gauss-Seidel iterative method to the optimization of nuclear mass formula coefficients, building on the least-squares approach historically attributed to Gauss (1795). The method is demonstrated on the Bethe-Weizsacker formula and a liquid drop model similar to those used in the FRDM and FRLDM models of Moller and collaborators (1995), the Pomorski-Dudek model, and the Royer model. The approach enables rapid fitting of mass formulas to large datasets such as the AME 2020 atomic mass evaluation, and the authors note that the algorithm can be applied to any experimental or statistical data leading to linear systems with symmetric positive-definite matrices.
