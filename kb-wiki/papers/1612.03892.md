# Spatial heterogeneity of W transmutation in a fusion device

> Accurately quantifying the transmutation rate of tungsten (W) under neutron irradiation is a necessary requirement in the assessment of its performance as an armour material in a fusion power plant. The usual approach of calculating average responses, assuming large, homogenised material volumes, is insufficient to capture the full complexity of the transmutation picture in the context of a realistic fusion power plant design, particularly for rhenium (Re) production from W. Combined neutron transport and inventory simulations for representative {\it spatially heterogeneous} models of a fusion power plant show that the production rate of Re is strongly influenced by the local spatial environment. Localised variation in neutron moderation (slowing down) due to structural steel and coolant, particularly water, can dramatically increase Re production because of the huge cross sections of giant resolved resonances in the neutron-capture reaction of \(^{186}\)W at low neutron energies. Calculations using cross section data corrected for temperature (Doppler) effects suggest that temperature may have a relatively lesser influence on transmutation rates.

## Key claim

The production rate of rhenium from tungsten under fusion neutron irradiation is more strongly influenced by local spatial heterogeneity in the neutron environment (due to structural steel and water coolant) than by the well-known self-shielding correction factors for giant neutron-capture resonances, and temperature effects are relatively minor in comparison.

## Method

Combined neutron transport simulations (MCNP6) and inventory simulations (FISPACT-II) were performed for a simplified spherical model of a fusion reactor, consisting of a 2 cm thick tungsten shell with a steel-water moderator behind it. The tungsten shell was divided into 0.1 mm spatial layers, and volume-averaged neutron flux spectra were tallied in each layer using 660 energy bins. These spectra were then used in separate FISPACT-II inventory simulations with self-shielding factors (SSFs) from probability tables to compute transmutation rates after 2 full-power years of irradiation.

## Key numbers

- Total neutron flux in the tungsten shell dropped from \(3.2\times10^{14}\) n cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\) in the first 0.1 mm layer to \(2.5\times10^{14}\) n cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\) at the back layer.
- The difference between maximum and minimum rhenium concentration after 2 full-power years was more than 4600 atomic parts per million (0.46 atm.%).
- The volume-averaged rhenium concentration was around 3500 appm, with a standard deviation of more than 400 appm.
- Effective self-shielding factors for the \(^{186}\)W(n,\(\gamma\))\(^{187}\)W reaction ranged from 0.56 to 0.68 (56 to 68% reduction), and for \(^{184}\)W(n,\(\gamma\))\(^{185}\)W ranged from 0.43 to 0.58.
- The reaction rate for \(^{186}\)W(n,\(\gamma\)) increased by a factor of 7 between the front and back of the tungsten shell.
- The reaction rate for \(^{186}\)W(n,2n) fell by almost 50% between the front and back layers.
- In scenario 2 (water cooling channel behind tungsten), rhenium production increased by more than 30% at the back of the tungsten and around 15% at 50 mm away from the water.
- In an experimental validation, a FISPACT-II simulation using a representative reactor spectrum predicted 1.4 atm.% rhenium after 208 days, in agreement with energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy measurements of 1.2-1.4 atm.%.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Gilbert and Sublet (2011) and others (Sublet and Sawan, 1999; Cottrell et al., 2006; Sawan, 2014) that used volume-averaged neutron fluxes for homogenised first wall armour tiles, showing that such averaging misses significant spatial variation on sub-millimeter scales. The findings were experimentally confirmed by Klimenkov et al. (2016) and Abernethy and Armstrong (2017) using tungsten samples irradiated in the high-flux reactor at NRG, Petten. The results enable more accurate predictions for experimental testing, theoretical materials models, and engineering design decisions for fusion power plant components.
