# The entanglement spectrum and Rényi entropies of non-relativistic conformal fermions

> We characterize non-perturbatively the Rényi entropies of degree n=2,3,4, and 5 of three-dimensional, strongly coupled many-fermion systems in the scale-invariant regime of short interaction range and large scattering length, i.e. in the unitary limit. We carry out our calculations using lattice methods devised recently by us. Our results show the effect of strong pairing correlations on the entanglement entropy, which modify the sub-leading behavior for large subsystem sizes (as characterized by the dimensionless parameter x=kF L_A, where kF is the Fermi momentum and L_A the linear subsystem size), but leave the leading order unchanged relative to the non-interacting case. Moreover, we find that the onset of the sub-leading asymptotic regime is at surprisingly small x=2-4. We provide further insight into the entanglement properties of this system by analyzing the spectrum of the entanglement Hamiltonian of the two-body problem from weak to strong coupling. The low-lying entanglement spectrum displays clear features as the strength of the coupling is varied, such as eigenvalue crossing, a sharp change in the Schmidt gap, and scale invariance at unitarity. Beyond the low-lying component, the spectrum appears as a quasi-continuum distribution, for which we present a statistical characterization; we find, in particular, that the mean shifts to infinity as the coupling is turned off, which indicates that that part of the spectrum represents non-perturbative contributions to the entanglement Hamiltonian. In contrast, the low-lying entanglement spectrum evolves to finite values in the noninteracting limit. The scale invariance of the unitary regime guarantees that our results are universal features intrinsic to 3D quantum mechanics and represent a well-defined prediction for ultracold atom experiments, which were recently shown to have direct access to the entanglement entropy.

## Key claim

The Rényi entropies of unitary fermions follow the same leading-order logarithmic scaling with subsystem size as non-interacting fermions, with strong pairing correlations only modifying the sub-leading behavior; surprisingly, the asymptotic regime sets in at very small subsystem sizes (x = k_F L_A ≈ 2–4), which the authors note was not guaranteed and made the lattice calculations feasible.

## Method

The authors used lattice methods with a Hubbard-Stratonovich auxiliary field transformation to compute the Rényi entanglement entropies of degrees n = 2, 3, 4, and 5 for three-dimensional many-fermion systems at unitarity. For the two-body problem, they implemented a direct (non-stochastic) projection method on the lattice to obtain the ground-state wavefunction and entanglement spectrum. For the many-body problem, they used a Monte Carlo approach previously developed by the authors (Drut and Porter, 2015) that overcomes the signal-to-noise problem of naive Monte Carlo methods, based on the work of Grover (2013).

## Key numbers

* The onset of the sub-leading asymptotic regime for the Rényi entropies occurs at x = k_F L_A ≈ 2–4, where k_F is the Fermi momentum and L_A is the linear subsystem size.
* Rényi entropies of degree n = 2, 3, 4, and 5 were computed for the three-dimensional unitary Fermi gas.
* The two-body problem was analyzed across the BCS-BEC crossover, with the unitary limit defined by the condition 0 ← r_0 ≪ n^{-1} ≪ a → ∞, where r_0 is the interaction range and a is the s-wave scattering length.

## Context

This work extends the study of entanglement properties from low-dimensional systems and non-interacting fermions (Wolf, 2006; Gioev and Klich, 2006; Swingle, 2010) to the strongly coupled unitary Fermi gas, which serves as a model for dilute neutron matter and ultracold atomic gases. The results provide universal predictions for ultracold atom experiments that have recently gained direct access to entanglement entropy (Greiner group, 2015–2016), and the scale invariance of the unitary regime guarantees these findings are intrinsic to three-dimensional quantum mechanics. The method builds on the authors' previous lattice approach for one-dimensional systems (Drut and Porter, 2015) and the replica-field technique of Grover (2013).
