# The Physics of ELFE

> This paper presents an overview of the physics program of the 15-30~GeV continuous beam electron facility proposed by the European community of nuclear physicists to study the quark and gluon structure of hadrons. The goal of this new facility is to explore the quark structure of matter by exclusive and semi-inclusive electron scattering from nuclear targets.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a dedicated 15-30 GeV continuous beam electron facility (ELFE) is necessary to study exclusive and semi-inclusive reactions at large momentum transfers, because existing high-energy accelerators designed for electroweak physics cannot provide the high intensity and duty cycle needed to access the hadron distribution amplitudes that encode confinement dynamics.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework of hard exclusive reactions in QCD, where scattering matrix elements factorize into a long-distance hadronic distribution amplitude and a short-distance perturbatively calculable hard scattering amplitude. It then surveys the proposed experimental program for ELFE, covering exclusive reactions (baryon form factors, Compton scattering, meson electroproduction), color transparency measurements using nuclear targets, diffractive vector meson production, and semi-inclusive reactions for studying quark distributions and hadronization. The accelerator requirements are discussed in terms of energy range (15-30 GeV), high duty cycle, and luminosity.

## Key numbers

- The proposed electron beam energy range is 15-30 GeV.
- The proton form factor shows scaling established above 10 GeV^2.
- The transparency ratio for diffractive electroproduction of rho mesons shows an interesting increase at Q^2 approximately 7 GeV^2, measured at Fermilab with initial lepton energy around 200 GeV.
- A typical experiment at 15 GeV requires beam energy resolution of about 5 MeV.
- High luminosity of 10^38 nucleons/cm^2/s is targeted for magnetic focusing spectrometers.
- The momentum resolution goal for high-resolution spectrometers is 5 x 10^-4.

## Context

This paper presents the physics case for the ELFE facility as recommended by the Nuclear Physics European Collaboration Committee (NuPECC) of the European Science Foundation in 1995. It extends the theoretical framework of hard exclusive reactions developed by the author and others (referenced as EXCLTHEORY) and the color transparency phenomenon (referenced as jpr, PR91). The work contrasts with the limited experimental capabilities at SLAC and HERA (HERMES program), which due to low duty cycle can only perform inclusive experiments and a very limited set of exclusive measurements. The paper enables future experimental programs to extract hadron distribution amplitudes from exclusive reactions and to study color neutralization processes using nuclear targets as femtoscale detectors.
