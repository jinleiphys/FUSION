# Topological Dimensions from Disorder and Quantum Mechanics?

> We have recently shown that critical Anderson electron in $D=3$ dimensions effectively occupies a spatial region of infrared (IR) scaling dimension $d_\text{IR} \approx 8/3$. Here we inquire about the dimensional substructure involved. We partition space into regions of equal quantum occurrence probability, such that points comprising a region are of similar relevance, and calculate the IR scaling dimension $d$ of each. This allows us to infer the probability density $p(d)$ for dimension $d$ to be accessed by electron. We find that $p(d)$ has a strong peak at $d$ very close to 2. In fact, our data suggests that $p(d)$ is non-zero on the interval $[d_\text{min}, d_\text{max}] \approx [4/3,8/3]$ and may develop a discrete part ($δ$-function) at $d=2$ in infinite-volume limit. The latter invokes the possibility that combination of quantum mechanics and pure disorder can lead to emergence of topological dimensions. Although $d_\text{IR}$ is based on effective counting of which $p(d)$ has no a priori knowledge, $d_\text{IR} \ge d_\text{max}$ is an exact feature of the ensuing formalism. Possible connection of our results to recent findings of $d_\text{IR} \approx 2$ in Dirac near-zero modes of thermal quantum chromodynamics is emphasized.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the spatial geometry of critical Anderson electron states in three dimensions contains a discrete dimension at exactly \(d=2\), which may be topological in origin, emerging purely from the combination of quantum mechanics and disorder. The claim is hedged by noting that the data "suggests" and "may develop" a delta-function component at \(d=2\) in the infinite-volume limit, and that the mathematical meaning of "topological" in this context requires clarification.

## Method

The authors partition three-dimensional space into regions of equal quantum occurrence probability based on the critical wave functions of the Anderson model in the orthogonal class at the critical point \((E_c,W_c) = (0, 16.543(2))\). They compute the infrared scaling dimension \(d\) of each region using the cumulative count function \(\nu(q,\psi)\) and its scaling with system size \(L\), then infer the probability density \(p(d)\) for the electron to access dimension \(d\). For 34 system sizes in the range \(16 \le L \le 144\), two near-zero eigenmodes were computed at 40,000 to 100,000 disorder samples using the JADAMILU package, with \(\epsilon = 10^{-3}\) splitting the cumulative probability interval into 1000 bins.

## Key numbers

- The infrared scaling dimension of the critical Anderson electron in \(D=3\) is \(d_\text{IR} \approx 8/3\) (specifically \(2.665(2)\) from prior work).
- The probability density \(p(d)\) is non-zero on the interval \([d_\text{min}, d_\text{max}] \approx [4/3, 8/3]\).
- The peak of \(p(d)\) occurs at \(d_\text{m} \approx 2\), with accuracy estimated at a couple per thousand.
- The probability weight of the discrete component at \(d_\text{m}\) is \(\mathcal{P} \gtrsim 1/2\).
- The thresholds \(d_\text{min}\) and \(d_\text{max}\) are estimated with accuracy at a couple percent.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior finding (Horvath 2021) that the Anderson transition in three dimensions is a two-step dimension reduction from \(d_\text{IR}=3\) to \(d_\text{IR}\approx 8/3\) to \(d_\text{IR}=0\), using effective number theory (ENT) developed by Horvath (2018). The new dimension content analysis reveals substructure within the critical effective support, showing that the electron mostly resides in regions of dimension near 2, with a continuum of dimensions from 4/3 to 8/3 also present. The results connect to recent findings (Alexandru 2021) of \(d_\text{IR}\approx 2\) in Dirac near-zero modes of thermal quantum chromodynamics, suggesting a possible relation between Anderson localization and QCD Dirac spectrum phenomena. The approach differs from traditional multifractal analysis (Castellani 1986, Janssen 1994) by focusing on measure-based Minkowski dimensions rather than the multifractal spectrum \(f(\alpha)\).
