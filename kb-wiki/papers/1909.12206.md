# $\mathbfβ$-delayed proton emission from $\mathbf{^{11}}$Be in effective field theory

> We calculate the rate of the rare decay $^{11}\text{Be}$ into $^{10}\text{Be} + p +e^- + \barν_e$ using Halo effective field theory, thereby describing the process of beta-delayed proton emission. We assume a shallow $1/2^+$ resonance in the $^{10}\text{Be}-p$ system with an energy consistent with a recent experiment by Ayyad et al. and obtain $b_p = 4.9_{-2.9}^{+5.6}\text{(exp.)}_{-0.8}^{+4.0}\text{(theo.)} \times 10^{-6}$ for the branching ratio of this decay, predicting a resonance width of $Γ_R = (9.0^{+4.8}_{-3.3}\text{(exp.)}^{+5.3}_{-2.2}\text{(theo.)})~\text{keV}$. Our calculation shows that the experimental branching ratio and resonance parameters of Ayyad et al. are consistent with each other. Moreover, we analyze the general impact of a resonance on the branching ratio and demonstrate that a wide range of combinations of resonance energies and widths can reproduce branching ratios of the correct order. Thus, no exotic mechanism (such as beyond the standard model physics) is needed to explain the experimental decay rate.

## Key claim

The branching ratio for beta-delayed proton emission from $^{11}$Be can be explained by a low-lying $1/2^+$ resonance in the $^{10}$Be-proton system within Halo effective field theory, without requiring exotic mechanisms such as beyond the Standard Model physics. The authors show that a wide range of resonance energy and width combinations can reproduce branching ratios of the correct order, and that the experimental branching ratio and resonance parameters of Ayyad et al. (2019) are consistent with each other.

## Method

The authors used Halo effective field theory (Halo EFT) to calculate the weak decay of the valence neutron in $^{11}$Be into the continuum, producing $^{10}$Be, a proton, an electron, and an antineutrino. They included both Gamow-Teller and Fermi transitions, considered resonant final-state interactions from a low-lying $1/2^+$ resonance in $^{11}$B, and enforced the beta-strength sum rule at each order in the EFT expansion. Calculations were performed at leading order (LO) and next-to-leading order (NLO), with the NLO calculation including effective range corrections in both the incoming and outgoing channels.

## Key numbers

* Branching ratio with resonant final state interactions: $b_p = 4.9_{-2.9}^{+5.6}\text{(exp.)}_{-0.8}^{+4.0}\text{(theo.)} \times 10^{-6}$, using resonance energy $E_R = 0.196(20)$ MeV from Ayyad et al. (2019)
* Predicted resonance width: $\Gamma_R = (9.0^{+4.8}_{-3.3}\text{(exp.)}^{+5.3}_{-2.2}\text{(theo.)})$ keV, consistent with the experimental value of $12(5)$ keV from Ayyad et al. (2019)
* Branching ratio without resonant final state interactions: $b_p = (1.31 \pm 0.51) \times 10^{-8}$, corresponding to decay rate $\Gamma = (6.6 \pm 2.6) \times 10^{-10}$ s$^{-1}$
* One-neutron separation energy of $^{11}$Be: $S_n = 0.5016$ MeV
* Effective range in the incoming channel at NLO: $r_0 = 2.7$ fm, with Coulomb-modified effective range in the outgoing channel $r_0^C = 1.5$ fm
* Comparative half-life for $E_R = 0.196(20)$ MeV: log$(ft) = 3.0$ for combined Gamow-Teller and Fermi transitions, and log$(ft) = 3.1$ for pure Gamow-Teller transition

## Context

This work extends the application of Halo EFT to weak decays of halo nuclei, following earlier applications to electromagnetic capture reactions and photodissociation processes by Hammer et al. (2011), Rupak and Higa (2011), and others. The calculation addresses the unexpectedly large branching ratio measured by Riisager et al. (2014) and later remeasured by Ayyad et al. (2019), which was more than two orders of magnitude larger than the cluster model prediction by Baye and Tursunov (2010). The results confirm the suggestion by Riisager et al. (2014) that a low-lying resonance is responsible for the large decay rate, and show that the recent upper limit from Riisager et al. (2020) can be accommodated for resonance energies $E_R \geq 0.214$ MeV. The work enables future tests of whether this resonance affects Halo EFT predictions for deuteron-induced neutron transfer reactions off $^{11}$Be.
