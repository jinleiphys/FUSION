# Extended Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model for quark and nuclear matters

> In this work, we extend the two-flavor Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model to one capable of exploring quark and nuclear matter consistently. With an extra term standing for quark-nucleon interactions, nucleons could automatically emerge as color-singlet three-quark entities by following a process similar to mesons. Besides the quark part in mean field approximation, both mesons and nucleons could contribute to the thermodynamic potential thus possibly give rise to quarkyonic matter beyond mean field. In the study, two kinds of "confining" couplings are adopted for the new interaction term and two different quark masses are considered for comparison. It turns out that only confined nuclear matter or deconfined quark matter is possible for all the cases at zero temperature, thus quarkyonic matter is not favored at all. Even more strictly, only the case with stronger confinement effect and a smaller quark mass admits a physical first-order phase transition from nuclear matter to quark matter around twice saturation density.

## Key claim

The paper claims that, contrary to expectations from large-\(N_c\) arguments, quarkyonic matter (a coexistent state of quarks and nucleons) is not favored at zero temperature in the extended Nambu–Jona-Lasinio model; only confined nuclear matter or deconfined quark matter is possible, and a physical first-order phase transition from nuclear matter to quark matter occurs only for the case with stronger confinement effect and a smaller quark mass, around twice saturation density.

## Method

The author extends the two-flavor Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model by adding a chiral-symmetric six-quark interaction term in the nucleonic channel, which allows nucleons to emerge as color-singlet three-quark collective excitations via a random phase approximation (RPA) similar to that used for mesons. Two forms of the six-quark coupling are adopted, with the coupling constant \(\tilde{G}_3 = G_3 / |\tilde{P}^2|^d\) for \(d = 1\) and \(d = 3/2\), and two different sets of vacuum parameters (small quark vacuum mass and large quark vacuum mass) are considered. The thermodynamic potential is evaluated beyond mean field by including contributions from quarks, pions, and nucleons in pole approximation, and the ground state is determined by comparing pressures of the chiral symmetry breaking and chiral symmetry restored phases at zero temperature.

## Key numbers

* The vacuum dynamical quark mass is \(m_q = 0.3135\ \text{GeV}\) for the small quark vacuum mass (SQVM) set, and \(m_q = 0.320\ \text{GeV}\) for the large quark vacuum mass (LQVM) set.
* The six-quark coupling constant is fixed as \(G_3 m_N^{2-2d} = 2284\ \text{GeV}^{-4}\) for SQVM and \(2375\ \text{GeV}^{-4}\) for LQVM, by fitting to the nucleon vacuum mass \(m_N = 0.939\ \text{GeV}\).
* For the SQVM case with \(d = 3/2\) in the consistent beyond mean field (CBMF) approximation, the first-order phase transition from nuclear matter to quark matter occurs at \(\mu_B = 1.02\ \text{GeV}\), with a total baryon density around \(n_B = 2 n_0\) (where \(n_0\) is nuclear saturation density).
* For the LQVM case with \(d = 3/2\), the first-order phase transition occurs at \(\mu_B = 1.065\ \text{GeV}\), but the critical baryon density in nuclear matter is larger than that in quark matter at the transition point.

## Context

This work extends the two-flavor NJL model, which is widely used to study chiral symmetry breaking and QCD phase transitions (Klevansky, 1992; Hatsuda, 1994), by introducing a six-quark interaction in the nucleonic channel. It contrasts with earlier approaches that used diquark channels and Faddeev equations to describe nucleons (Buck, 1992; Ishii, 1995), and with the quarkyonic matter concept proposed by McLerran (2007) based on large-\(N_c\) arguments. The model enables a consistent study of transitions among nuclear, quark, and quarkyonic matters, but the numerical results at zero temperature rule out quarkyonic matter and identify only one parameter set (stronger confinement with \(d = 3/2\) and smaller quark mass) that yields a physical first-order transition around twice saturation density. Future work must address nucleonic pairing feedback and nuclear matter saturation properties.
