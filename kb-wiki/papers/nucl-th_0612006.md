# Shapes and Sizes from Non-Identical-Particle Correlations

> I review the prospects for measuring source characteristics from correlations other than those involving identical pions. Correlations generated from Coulomb and strong interactions are shown to provide remarkable resolving power for determining three-dimensional information, in some cases accessing more detail than can be represented by Gaussian fits.

## Key claim

Non-identical-particle correlations, driven by Coulomb and strong interactions, can provide resolving power for three-dimensional source shapes that in some cases exceeds what can be represented by Gaussian fits, but the ability to extract non-Gaussian features is constrained by the strength and form of scattering phase shifts and by statistical and systematic errors.

## Method

The paper derives the kernel \( \mathcal{K}(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{r}) = |\phi(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{r})|^2 - 1 \) that relates the correlation function \( \mathcal{R}(\mathbf{q}) \) to the source function \( \mathcal{S}(\mathbf{r}) \) via \( \mathcal{R}(\mathbf{q}) = \int d^3r \, \mathcal{S}(\mathbf{r}) \mathcal{K}(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{r}) \). The resolving power is analyzed by projecting both the correlation and source functions onto spherical harmonics or Cartesian harmonics, which reduces the inversion to a one-dimensional integral over \( r \) for each harmonic order \( \ell \). The kernels for strong interactions are evaluated using scattering phase shifts and classical shadowing approximations, while Coulomb kernels are treated with classical trajectory calculations and small-\( x \) expansions where \( x = 2\mu e^2 / (q^2 r) \).

## Key numbers

- For \( pK^+ \) correlations, the Bohr radius is 390 fm for \( \pi\pi \) but only 58 fm for \( pp \); the Coulomb interaction provides reasonable resolving power for \( pK \) or heavier pairs, whereas pairs involving pions offer weak resolving power.
- For a Gaussian source with dimensions \( R_x = R_y = 4 \) fm, \( R_z = 8 \) fm, angular sensitivity at \( q = 55 \) MeV/\( c \) is significant for all particle pairs; pairs involving two baryons tend to be especially strong.
- For \( pK \) or \( p\pi \) correlations: \( \ell=1 \) terms are easy; \( \ell=2 \) requires 1% or better accuracy; \( \ell=3 \) is probably prohibitively small.
- For baryon-baryon correlations: \( \ell=1,2 \) are easy; \( \ell=3 \) requires 1% or better accuracy.
- The intercept of the Coulomb correlation function at \( q=0 \) gives \( C(q \rightarrow 0) = (1 - f_{\text{l.l.}})^2 \), where \( f_{\text{l.l.}} \) is the fraction of particles from long-lived decays.
- For a \( pp \) correlation function with parameters \( \lambda=0.6 \), \( R_G=5 \) fm, \( X=10 \) fm, \( X_{\text{frac}}=0.5 \), extracting three parameters is usually robust but determining all four parameters is not possible with expected RHIC errors.

## Context

This work extends the well-known identical-particle correlation technique (Lisa, 2005) to non-identical-particle correlations, which are less appreciated by the community despite offering similar resolving power (Pratt, 2003). The paper builds on imaging methods developed by Danielewicz and Brown (2000, 1999, 1997) for extracting non-Gaussian source features, and on earlier work on source offsets by Lednicky (1995), Gelderloos, and Voloshin (1997). The analysis enables alternative measurements of \( R_{\text{out}}/R_{\text{side}}/R_{\text{long}} \) and access to odd harmonics that probe new features of emission geometry, providing complementary constraints to identical-pion correlations which have already excluded strong first-order equations of state and hard pion-gas-like equations of state at RHIC.
