# Study of Coulomb interactions at VECC energies

> we study the effect of Coulomb interactions on balance energy for various colliding nuclei in terms of mass asymmetry. This study shows that the balance energy is affected by the Coulomb interactions as well as different nuclear equations of state. The preliminary results calculated theoretically will be of great use for scientists at VECC. This study is further in progress.

## Key claim

The balance energy (energy of vanishing flow) in heavy-ion collisions is affected by both Coulomb interactions and the nuclear equation of state, but the shift due to Coulomb interactions is larger than the shift due to changing the equation of state from soft to hard, at least for the fixed total mass A_TOT = 152 and the mass asymmetries studied.

## Method

The authors used the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model to simulate collisions of nuclei with fixed total mass A_TOT = 152 but varying mass asymmetry η (from 0.2 to 0.7). They computed the balance energy for each system under three conditions: Coulomb interactions switched off with a soft nuclear equation of state (NEOS), Coulomb off with a hard NEOS, and Coulomb on with a soft NEOS, then fitted the results to a power law E_bal = C(η)^τ.

## Key numbers

- For Coulomb-off + soft NEOS, the power-law exponent τ = 0.375.
- For Coulomb-off + hard NEOS, the power-law exponent τ = 0.282.
- For Coulomb-on + soft NEOS, the power-law exponent τ = 0.06.
- The total mass of the combined system was fixed at A_TOT = 152, with projectile masses ranging from 16 to 56.
- Mass asymmetry values studied: η = 0.2 (Fe-56 + Ru-96), η = 0.3 (Cr-50 + Ru-102), η = 0.4 (Ca-40 + Sn-112), η = 0.5 (S-32 + Sn-120), η = 0.6 (Si-28 + Xe-124), η = 0.7 (O-16 + Xe-136).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of balance energy (Sood, year not given) by focusing on mass asymmetry rather than total mass, and by isolating the role of the Coulomb potential within the IQMD model (Hartnack, year not given). The preliminary results are intended to guide experiments at the Variable Energy Cyclotron Center (VECC), and the authors note the study is ongoing.
