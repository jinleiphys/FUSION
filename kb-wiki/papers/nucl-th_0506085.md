# Bosonization of the Pairing Hamiltonian

> We address the problem of the bosonization of finite fermionic systems with two different approaches. First we work in the path integral formalism, showing how a truly bosonic effective action can be derived from a generic fermionic one with a quartic interaction. We then apply our scheme to the pairing hamiltonian in the degenerate case proving that, in this instance, several of the features characterizing the spontaneous breaking of the global gauge symmetry U(1) occurring in the infinite system persist in the finite system as well. Accordingly we interpret the excitations associated with the addition and removal of pairs of fermions as a quasi-Goldstone boson and the excitations corresponding to the breaking of a pair (seniority one states in the language of the pairing hamiltonian) as Higgs modes. Second, we face the more involved problem of a non-degenerate single particle spectrum, where one more kind of excitations arises, corresponding to the promotion of pairs to higher levels. This we do by solving directly the Richardson equations. From this analysis the existence emerges of critical values of the coupling constant, which signal the transition between two regimes, one dominated by the mean field physics, the other by the pairing interaction.

## Key claim

The paper shows that in a finite fermionic system described by the pairing Hamiltonian, the excitations associated with adding or removing pairs of fermions can be interpreted as quasi-Goldstone bosons and the excitations corresponding to breaking a pair (seniority one states) as Higgs modes, even though spontaneous symmetry breaking cannot rigorously occur in a finite system due to tunnelling between degenerate states.

## Method

The authors first use a path integral formalism with Grassmann variables to derive a purely bosonic effective action from the fermionic pairing Hamiltonian, applying it to the degenerate single-particle level case. They then perform a saddle point expansion around the minimum of the effective action and introduce a Hubbard-Stratonovich linearization with a polar representation of auxiliary fields to identify the Goldstone nature of pair addition/removal excitations. For the non-degenerate case, they solve the Richardson equations directly to study one and two pairs in many non-degenerate single-particle levels.

## Key numbers

- The pairing Hamiltonian energy spectrum for the degenerate case: \(E(n,s) = 2 e n - G [n(\Omega - n + 1) - s(\Omega - s + 1)]\), where \(n\) is the number of pairs, \(s\) is the number of broken pairs, \(\Omega\) is the total degeneracy, and \(e\) is the single-particle energy
- The minimum of the energy occurs at \(n_0 = [\nu_0]\) with \(\nu_0 = \frac{1}{2}(\Omega + 1) - \frac{e}{G}\)
- The gap parameter from the saddle point expansion: \(\overline\rho_0 = \frac{1}{(2G)^2}[(G\Omega)^2 - 4e^2] = \frac{\Delta^2}{G^2}\), where \(\Delta\) is the BCS gap
- The effective bosonic Hamiltonian parameters: \(h_1 = 2e - G\Omega\), \(h_2 = G\)
- The degeneracy of each seniority state: \(\delta_s = \binom{\Omega}{s} - \binom{\Omega}{s-1}\)

## Context

This work extends the understanding of bosonization in finite fermionic systems, building on the BCS theory of superconductivity (Bardeen, 1957) and the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) by Arima and Iachello. The paper connects the pairing Hamiltonian's exact solution via Richardson equations (Cambiaggio, 1997) to the path integral formalism, showing that features of spontaneous U(1) gauge symmetry breaking in infinite systems persist in finite systems. This enables a microscopic foundation for bosonic models of nuclear structure and clarifies the relationship between Goldstone and Higgs excitations in finite fermionic systems.
