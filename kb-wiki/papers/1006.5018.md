# A systematic description of evaporation spectra for light and heavy compound nuclei

> To systematically describe evaporation spectra for light and heavy compound nuclei over a large range of excitation energies, it was necessary to consider three ingredients in the statistical model. Firstly, transmission coefficients or barrier penetration factors for charged-particle emission are typically taken from global fits to elastic-scattering data. However, such transmission coefficients do not reproduce the barrier region of evaporation spectra and reproduction of the data requires a distributions of Coulomb barriers. This is possibly associated with large fluctuations in the compound-nucleus shape or density profile. Secondly for heavy nuclei, an excitation-energy dependent level-density parameter is required to describe the slope of the exponential tails of these spectra. The level-density parameter was reduced at larger temperatures, consistent with the expected fadeout of long-range correlation, but the strong $A$ dependence of this effect is unexpected. Lastly to describe the angular-momentum dependence of the level density in light nuclei at large spins, the macroscopic rotational energy of the nucleus has to be reduced from the values predicted with the Finite-Range Liquid-Drop model.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a systematic description of evaporation spectra across a wide range of compound-nucleus masses and excitation energies requires three modifications to the standard statistical model: a distribution of Coulomb barriers (not a single barrier) for charged-particle transmission coefficients, an excitation-energy-dependent level-density parameter for heavy nuclei, and a reduction of the macroscopic rotational energy from Finite-Range Liquid-Drop model predictions for light nuclei at high spins.

## Method

The author performed statistical-model calculations using the Hauser-Feshbach formalism implemented in the code GEMINI++, comparing predicted kinetic-energy spectra for protons, alpha particles, and neutrons to experimental data from multiple studies covering compound nuclei from A=59 to A=224. For heavy nuclei (A>150), only residue-gated spectra were used; for lighter systems, inclusive spectra at backward or forward angles were employed. The calculations systematically varied three ingredients: transmission coefficients (using an average of three IWBC calculations with different nuclear radii to simulate a barrier distribution), the level-density parameter (allowing an excitation-energy dependence), and the yrast energy (using Sierk's macroscopic predictions with modifications for light nuclei).

## Context

This work extends the systematic statistical-model studies of compound-nucleus decay by addressing the long-standing problem that no single prescription gives accurate predictions over the entire table of isotopes. It builds on prior observations by Alexander et al. (1990) regarding the inappropriateness of optical-model transmission coefficients for evaporation, and by Nicolis et al. (1990), Gonin et al. (1990), Kildir et al. (1992), and others who found that standard transmission coefficients fail to reproduce the sub-barrier region of alpha-particle spectra. The barrier distribution approach is consistent with thermal shape fluctuations proposed by Huizenga (1989) and Charity et al. (2000, 2001), though the required fluctuation magnitude (25% of nuclear radius) is larger than the ~7% predicted from ellipsoidal shape fluctuations alone. The excitation-energy-dependent level-density parameter shows a stronger A dependence than expected from the fadeout of long-range correlations, enabling improved predictions for spallation reaction modelling and other applications requiring statistical-model parameters at high excitation energies.
