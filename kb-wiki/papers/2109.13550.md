# Multinucleon excitations in neutrino-nucleus scattering: connecting different microscopic models for the correlations

> The problem of nucleon-nucleon correlations and meson exchange currents has been vividly debated in connection with the neutrino-nucleus cross sections. In this work we focus on nucleon-nucleon correlations by discussing a formal correspondence between the approaches based on independent particles and the ab initio approaches involving correlated wave functions. We use a general technique based on unitary transformation mapping the Fermion operators relative to bare nucleons into quasi-particle operators relative to dressed nucleons. We derive formulas for spectral functions, response functions, momentum distribution, separation energy, general enough to be applied with any kind of effective nucleon-nucleon interaction. We establish the relation between the non-energy-weighted sum rule and the Fermi sea depopulation. With our tools we evaluate whether approaches based on effective interactions are compatible with the expected amount of correlations coming from ab initio calculations. For this purpose we use as a test the Fermi sea depopulation and the value of the kinetic energy per nucleon.

## Key claim

The paper establishes a formal correspondence between independent-particle models (which treat nucleons as moving in a mean field) and ab initio approaches (which use correlated wave functions) for describing nucleon-nucleon correlations in neutrino-nucleus scattering, showing that the two descriptions are connected via a unitary transformation that maps bare nucleon operators into quasi-particle operators for dressed nucleons.

## Method

The authors use a unitary transformation technique that maps fermion operators for bare nucleons into quasi-particle operators for dressed nucleons, following a method proposed by Desplanques for nuclear matter. They derive explicit formulas for spectral functions, response functions, momentum distributions, and separation energies that can be applied with any effective nucleon-nucleon interaction. The formalism is then used to evaluate whether approaches based on effective interactions (such as those used by the Lyon and Valencia groups) are compatible with the expected amount of correlations from ab initio calculations, using the Fermi sea depopulation and kinetic energy per nucleon as test quantities.

## Key numbers

* For nuclei from 12C to 56Fe, the separation energy from correlated wave function calculations is approximately -50 MeV, giving a correct fit to the EMC effect.
* For 56Fe with a SIII Skyrme interaction, the binding energy per nucleon is 8.5 MeV, the kinetic energy per nucleon is 18 MeV, and the rearrangement term delta V3 is 6 MeV, yielding a separation energy of -23 MeV.
* In the independent particle picture with bare nucleons, the kinetic energy per nucleon from correlated wave function calculations is approximately 35 MeV for nuclei from 12C to 56Fe.
* The Koltun sum rule gives the relation -<epsilon> = <t> + 2B, where <epsilon> is the separation energy, <t> is the kinetic energy, and B is the binding energy per nucleon.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing debate about nucleon-nucleon correlations and meson exchange currents in neutrino-nucleus cross sections, which began with the MiniBooNE measurement in 2009-2010 showing disagreement with Fermi gas model predictions. The paper connects the independent particle model approaches of the Lyon group (Martini et al., 2009) and Valencia group (Nieves et al., 2011) with ab initio approaches such as Green's function Monte Carlo (Lovato et al., 2014-2020) and spectral function methods (Benhar et al., 2005-2016). The formalism also relates to the earlier EMC effect debate from the 1980s, where similar questions about correlations and the distinction between bare and dressed nucleons arose in the work of Ericson (1987) and Ciofi degli Atti et al. The technique enables future evaluation of whether effective interaction approaches are compatible with ab initio correlation calculations, which is important for interpreting neutrino oscillation experiments at T2K, NOvA, Hyper-K, and DUNE.
