# Constituent quark model for nuclear stopping in high energy nuclear collisions

> We study the nuclear stopping in high energy nuclear collisions using the constituent quark model. It is assumed that wounded nucleons with different number of interacted quarks hadronize in different ways. The probabilities of having such wounded nucleons are evaluated for proton-proton, proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions. After examining our model in proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions and fixing the hadronization functions, it is extended to nucleus-nucleus collisions. It is used to calculate the rapidity distribution and the rapidity shift of final state protons in nucleus-nucleus collisions. The computed results are in good agreement with the experimental data on $^{32}\mbox{S} +\ ^{32}\mbox{S}$ at $E_{lab} = 200$ AGeV and $^{208}\mbox{Pb} +\ ^{208}\mbox{Pb}$ at $E_{lab} = 160$ AGeV. Theoretical predictions are also given for proton rapidity distribution in $^{197}\mbox{Au} +\ ^{197}\mbox{Au}$ at $\sqrt{s} = 200$ AGeV (BNL-RHIC). We predict that the nearly baryon free region will appear in the midrapidity region and the rapidity shift is $\langle Δy \rangle = 2.22$.

# Constituent quark model for nuclear stopping in high energy nuclear collisions

## Key claim

The constituent quark model, which distinguishes three types of wounded nucleons (with one, two, or three interacted quarks that hadronize differently), reproduces experimental proton rapidity distributions in nucleus-nucleus collisions at SPS energies and predicts a nearly baryon-free midrapidity region in Au+Au collisions at RHIC, contingent on the specific hadronization functions extracted from proton-proton and proton-nucleus data.

## Method

The authors formulate a constituent quark model where nucleons consist of three spatially separated quarks; in a collision, quarks interact independently with an inelastic quark-quark cross section, and the number of quarks that interact per nucleon (1, 2, or 3) determines the type of wounded nucleon. Probabilities for each wounded-nucleon type are calculated from the nuclear density and the quark-quark cross section using Glauber-like multiple-scattering expansions for proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions. Phenomenological fragmentation functions for each wounded-nucleon type are fixed by fitting proton fractional-momentum distributions in pp and pA data, then applied to compute rapidity distributions in AA collisions via the relation between Feynman x and rapidity.

## Key numbers

- Inelastic quark-quark cross section: σ_{inel}^{qq} = 4.32 mb (to reproduce σ_{inel}^{pp} = 30 mb for √s between 3 and 100 GeV) and 6.64 mb (to give σ_{inel}^{pp} = 41 mb at √s = 200 GeV)
- Proton root-mean-square radius: r_{rms}^{p} = 0.862 fm (from electron scattering data)
- Woods-Saxon parameters for heavy nuclei: R = 1.12 A^{1/3} - 0.86 A^{-1/3} fm, a = 0.54 fm
- Gaussian radius for ⁹Be: r_{rms}^{Be} = 2.3 fm
- Average rapidity shift predicted for ¹⁹⁷Au + ¹⁹⁷Au at √s = 200 AGeV: ⟨Δy⟩ = 2.22
- Average fractional momenta for wounded nucleons: ⟨x⟩₁ = 0.52, ⟨x⟩₂ = 0.28, ⟨x⟩₃ = 0.15
- Flavor factors: λ₁ = 0.65, λ₂ = 0.61, λ₃ = 0.50
- Impact parameter cutoffs for central S+S triggers: b_max = 1.18 fm (Veto, 2% of σ_{inel}) and b_max = 2.78 fm (E_T, 11% of σ_{inel})
- Impact parameter cutoff for central Pb+Pb and Au+Au (15% of σ_{inel}): b_max = 6.05 fm (Pb+Pb) and b_max = 5.86 fm (Au+Au)
- More than 94% of incident nucleons are wounded in central collisions of both Pb+Pb and Au+Au

## Context

This work extends the constituent quark model approach of Anisovich et al. (1978), Nikolaev (1979), Dar and Vary (1980), Bialas et al. (1979), and Takagi (1981, 1983) from hadron-nucleus collisions to nucleus-nucleus collisions, providing a systematic treatment of three types of wounded nucleons rather than the additive quark approximation. The model reproduces S+S data from the NA35 Collaboration (1994) and Pb+Pb data from the NA44 Collaboration (1995) at CERN SPS, and offers predictions for RHIC experiments. The results contrast with models such as RQMD (Sorge et al., 1989), VENUS (Werner, 1993), and HIJING (Wang et al., 1991), which differ significantly in their predictions for baryon stopping at midrapidity. The formalism enables calculation of various spectra in AA collisions using the probabilities for different quark interaction configurations.
