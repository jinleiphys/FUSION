# Puzzles in hadronic transitions of heavy quarkonium with two pion emission

> We study the anomalously large rates of some hadronic transitions observed in heavy quarkonia using a constituent quark model which has been successful in describing meson and baryon phenomenology. QCD multipole expansion (QCDME) is used to described the hadronic transitions. The hybrid intermediate states needed in the QCDME method are calculated in a natural, parameter-free extension of our constituent quark model based on the Quark Confining String (QCS) scheme. Some of the anomalies are explained due to the presence of a hybrid state with a mass near the one of the decaying resonance whereas others are justified by the presence of molecular components in the wave function. Certain unexpected results are pointed out.

## Key claim

The anomalously large rates of some hadronic transitions in heavy quarkonia can be partially explained by the presence of a hybrid state with a mass near the decaying resonance, while other anomalies are justified by molecular components in the wave function; however, certain unexpected results remain, notably the failure to explain the anomalous widths of the $\Upsilon(10860)$ via the hybrid mechanism.

## Method

The authors used a constituent quark model with a screened linear confinement potential and one-gluon exchange to describe conventional $c\bar{c}$ and $b\bar{b}$ states. Hadronic transitions with two-pion emission were treated using the QCD multipole expansion (QCDME) approach, where the intermediate hybrid states were calculated in a parameter-free extension of the quark model based on the Quark Confining String (QCS) scheme. The two unknown constants $C_1$ and $C_2$ in the gluon-to-pion conversion amplitude were fixed by fitting the well-established $\psi(2S)\to J/\psi\pi^{+}\pi^{-}$ and $\psi(3770)\to J/\psi\pi^{+}\pi^{-}$ transitions.

## Key numbers

- Mass of the ground-state $c\bar{c}g$ hybrid with $J^{PC}=1^{--}$: $4351$ MeV (with $\alpha_n=\sqrt{1.5}$)
- Mass of the ground-state $b\bar{b}g$ hybrid with $J^{PC}=1^{--}$: $10785$ MeV
- Fitted parameters: $|C_1|^2 = (9.396\pm0.503)\times10^{-5}$, $|C_2|^2 = (3.051\pm0.430)\times10^{-4}$; ratio $C_2/C_1 \sim 1.8$
- For $X(4360)$ decaying to $\psi(2S)\pi^{+}\pi^{-}$: $R_{\psi(2S)} = {\cal B}_{\pi^{+}\pi^{-}\psi(2S)} \times \Gamma_{e^{+}e^{-}} = 5.05$ eV (theory) vs $7.4\pm 0.9$ eV (experiment)
- For $X(4660)$ decaying to $\psi(2S)\pi^{+}\pi^{-}$: $R_{\psi(2S)} = 1.08$ eV (theory) vs $1.04\pm 0.5$ eV (experiment)
- For $\Upsilon(10860)$ decaying to $\Upsilon(1S)\pi^{+}\pi^{-}$: $R_{\Upsilon(1S)} = 4.1\times10^{-2}$ eV (theory) vs $1.64 \pm 0.40$ eV (experiment), a discrepancy of about two orders of magnitude
- $\psi(3770)$ mixing angle determined dynamically: $\vartheta \approx 1^\circ$

## Context

This work extends the QCD multipole expansion approach of Kuang (2006) and the constituent quark model of Vijande et al. (2004) to address anomalous hadronic transition rates observed in charmonium and bottomonium. The results explain why no signals of $\psi(4040)$, $\psi(4160)$, and $\psi(4415)$ appear in the $\psi(2S)\pi^{+}\pi^{-}$ channel, consistent with the experimental data of Lees et al. (2012). The failure to explain the $\Upsilon(10860)$ anomaly via hybrid states suggests that tetraquark components, as proposed by Ali et al. (2009), may be needed, opening the question of whether molecular or four-quark configurations are required to fully describe heavy quarkonium transitions above open-flavor threshold.
