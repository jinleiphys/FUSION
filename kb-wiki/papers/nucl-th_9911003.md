# Few-Body States in Fermi-Systems and Condensation Phenomena

> Residual interactions in many particle systems lead to strong correlations. A multitude of spectacular phenomenae in many particle systems are connected to correlation effects in such systems, e.g. pairing, superconductivity, superfluidity, Bose-Einstein condensation etc. Here we focus on few-body bound states in a many-body surrounding.

## Key claim

The paper argues that few-body bound states (deuterons, tritons, alpha particles) and their condensation phenomena in nuclear matter can be described by in-medium n-body Schrödinger-type equations derived from the Dyson equation approach to many-body Green's functions, with the caveat that these equations are only modified by RPA phase space factors on the two-body matrix elements to lowest order.

## Method

The authors derive in-medium few-body equations from the Dyson equation approach to many-body Green's functions at finite temperature and density, using the cluster mean field approximation. For two-body correlations, they solve the Bethe-Goldstone or Feynman-Galitski equations in ladder approximation; for three- and four-body bound states, they extend the formalism using suitably modified Alt-Grassberger-Sandhas (AGS) equations. The in-medium bound state equations are solved for deuterons, tritons, and alpha particles using separable potentials and variational ansätze, with medium effects entering through Pauli blocking factors and self-energy corrections.

- Cluster lifetime in second order Born approximation: Γₙ↓ = a (E - nε_F)² + bT², where n is the nucleon number in the cluster

## Context

This work extends the in-medium few-body formalism developed by the Japanese school (Marumori and collaborators) and earlier work by Schuck (1973) on three-body equations for quasiparticles. It builds on the Beth-Uhlenbeck approach (Schuck, 1990) for treating correlated densities and the AGS formalism (Alt, Grassberger, Sandhas) for scattering phenomena. The paper enables further study of cluster formation in heavy ion collisions, neutron star crusts (Pines, 1992), and the transition from quark-gluon plasma to hadronic matter, as well as applications to exciton and trion formation in semiconductors.
