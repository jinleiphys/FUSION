# Natural orbitals for the no-core configuration interaction approach

> Ab initio calculations face the challenge of describing a complex multiscale quantum many-body system. The nuclear wave function has both strong short-range correlations and long-range contributions. Natural orbitals provide a means of adapting the single-particle basis for ab initio no-core configuration interaction (NCCI) calculations to better match the many-body wave function. Natural orbitals are obtained by diagonalizing the one-body density matrix from a calculation using an initial single-particle reference basis, such as the traditional harmonic oscillator basis. The natural orbital basis builds in contributions from high-lying oscillator shells, thus accelerating convergence of wave functions, energies, and other observables. The convergence of the ground and excited state energies, radii, and electromagnetic observables of He, Li, and Be isotopes calculated using natural orbitals in ab initio NCCI calculations is discussed. It is found that electromagnetic observables involving the M1 operator fully converge, while the calculated energies, radii, and observables involving the E2 operator converge significantly faster with the natural orbital basis than with the harmonic oscillator basis. The use of infrared (IR) extrapolation schemes with the natural orbital calculations is also explored.

## Key claim

Natural orbitals, obtained by diagonalizing the one-body density matrix from an initial no-core configuration interaction (NCCI) calculation in the harmonic oscillator basis, accelerate convergence of energies, radii, and electromagnetic observables in truncated model spaces, but the improvement is not uniform: electromagnetic observables involving the M1 operator fully converge, while energies, radii, and E2 observables converge significantly faster than with the harmonic oscillator basis but may still require extrapolation.

## Method

The author performed NCCI calculations for He, Li, and Be isotopes using the JISP16 nucleon-nucleon interaction, first in the traditional harmonic oscillator single-particle basis with an Nmax truncation scheme. Natural orbitals were then constructed by diagonalizing the one-body density matrix obtained from that initial calculation, and the NCCI calculations were repeated in this adapted single-particle basis. Convergence of ground and excited state energies, radii, and electromagnetic observables (M1 and E2 operators) was compared between the two bases, and infrared (IR) extrapolation schemes were applied to the natural orbital results.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy of 6He calculated with JISP16 interaction, Nmax up to 16, and hw from 10-40 MeV: results still depend on both hw and Nmax at Nmax=16 (full convergence not reached).
- Proton radius of 6He in the 0+ ground state: convergence in terms of Nmax is so slow that "no reliable quantitative estimate" can be made from the harmonic oscillator basis results shown.
- Many-body basis dimensions for nuclei with A ≤ 24 reach approximately 10^10 at the largest Nmax truncations accessible with current computational power.
- The oscillator length parameter is defined as b = (hbar c) / sqrt((m_N c^2)(hbar omega)), with m_N c^2 = 938.92 MeV (average of proton and neutron mass).

## Context

This work extends the NCCI approach (Navratil 2000, Barrett 2013) by introducing natural orbitals (Lowdin 1955) to overcome slow convergence of observables that persists with the harmonic oscillator basis. Prior attempts to improve convergence include the Laguerre basis (Caprio 2012), which carries correct exponential asymptotics but was also found to converge slowly for energies and radii (Caprio 2012, 2014). The natural orbital approach builds on infrared extrapolation methods (Furnstahl 2012, More 2013, Wendt 2015) and aims to reduce the need for high-lying oscillator shells, enabling future applications to halo nuclei such as 6,8He and mirror nuclei 7Li and 7Be.
