# Simplified dynamical eikonal approximation

> Background: Breakup reactions are often used to probe the nuclear structure of halo nuclei. The eikonal model diverges for Coulomb breakup since it relies on the adiabatic approximation. To correct this weakness, a Coulomb-corrected eikonal method (CCE) using the Coulomb first-order-perturbation approximation was developed. Purpose: Since the CCE mixes two reaction models and treats the Coulomb and nuclear interactions on different footings, we study here an alternative approach. We develop a simplification to the dynamical eikonal approximation (S-DEA) which has a similar numerical cost as the usual eikonal model, and study its efficiency for both nuclear- and Coulomb-dominated breakup reactions. Methods: We compare the energy and parallel-momentum cross sections obtained with the dynamical eikonal approximation, the usual eikonal approximation, the CCE and the S-DEA. Results: The S-DEA leads to precise energy distributions for both breakup reactions. The corresponding parallel-momentum distributions obtained with the S-DEA are improved compared to the ones computed with the eikonal model. It is more efficient for nuclear-dominated breakup than the CCE since it reproduces better the shape and magnitude of the distribution. However, for the Coulomb breakup, the distribution lacks asymmetry. Conclusions: The simplification of the DEA developed in this work improves significantly the eikonal descriptions of breakup energy distribution for both Coulomb- and nuclear-dominated reactions. The asymmetry of the parallel-momentum distribution is enhanced for nuclear-dominated breakup. This study confirms that the asymmetry is due to dynamical effects. A direct prospect of this work would be to extend this model to two-neutron halo-nucleus projectiles.

## Key claim

The Simplified Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (S-DEA) improves the eikonal description of breakup energy distributions for both Coulomb- and nuclear-dominated reactions, but for Coulomb breakup the parallel-momentum distribution lacks asymmetry due to destructive interferences between partial waves that reduce the asymmetry from the dominant E1 transition.

## Method

The authors develop the S-DEA by approximating the solution of the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) equation using the first term of its Magnus expansion, then replacing the internal Hamiltonian by its eigenvalues within the breakup matrix element. This yields an eikonal-like model where the eikonal phases are replaced by first-order-perturbation approximations, applied only to the real parts of the potentials while the imaginary parts are treated with the usual eikonal model. The S-DEA is tested on the breakup of 11Be on 12C at 67A MeV (nuclear-dominated) and on 208Pb at 69A MeV (Coulomb-dominated), comparing energy and parallel-momentum distributions against the DEA, the usual eikonal approximation, and the Coulomb-corrected eikonal method (CCE).

## Key numbers

- 11Be neutron separation energy: 0.504 MeV
- Beam energies: 67A MeV for 12C target, 69A MeV for 208Pb target
- For the 12C target, the S-DEA energy distribution is accurate over the whole considered energy range (up to 45 MeV, corresponding to kmax = 1.4 fm^-1)
- For the 208Pb target, the S-DEA energy distribution is accurate up to approximately 12 MeV, where an unphysical increase begins (E_max = 11.26 MeV, kmax = 0.7 fm^-1)
- For the nuclear-dominated breakup on 12C, the S-DEA improves both shape and magnitude of the parallel-momentum distribution compared to the eikonal model, and reproduces the asymmetry better than the CCE
- For the Coulomb-dominated breakup on 208Pb, the S-DEA parallel-momentum distribution is too symmetrical compared to the DEA

## Context

This work extends the dynamical eikonal approximation (DEA) developed by Baye, Capel, and collaborators (2005) and the Coulomb-corrected eikonal method (CCE) developed by Margueron, Bonaccorso, and Brink (2003) and Capel, Baye, and collaborators (2008). The CCE mixes two reaction models and treats Coulomb and nuclear interactions on different footings, whereas the S-DEA provides a unified framework. The S-DEA enables extension to two-neutron halo-nucleus projectiles and could be improved by expanding the wave function onto a basis of eigenstates and pseudostates for the core-neutron continuum, as in the continuum-discretized coupled-channels method (CDCC) developed by Kamimura (1986) and Yahiro, Ogata, and Minomo (2012).
