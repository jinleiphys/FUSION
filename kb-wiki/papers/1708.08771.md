# A Minimal Nuclear Energy Density Functional

> We present a minimal nuclear energy density functional (NEDF) called "SeaLL1" that has the smallest number of possible phenomenological parameters to date. SeaLL1 is defined by 7 significant phenomenological parameters, each related to a specific nuclear property. It describes the nuclear masses of even-even nuclei with a mean energy error of 0.97 MeV and a standard deviation 1.46 MeV, two-neutron and two-proton separation energies with r.m.s. errors of 0.69 MeV and 0.59 MeV respectively, and the charge radii of 345 even-even nuclei with a mean error $ε_r=$0.022 fm and a standard deviation $σ_r=$0.025 fm. SeaLL1 incorporates constraints on the EoS of pure neutron matter from quantum Monte Carlo calculations with chiral effective field theory two-body (NN) interactions at N3LO level and three-body (NNN) interactions at the N2LO level. Two of the seven parameters are related to the saturation density and the energy per particle of the homogeneous symmetric nuclear matter, one is related to the nuclear surface tension, two are related to the symmetry energy and its density dependence, one is related to the strength of the spin-orbit interaction, and one is the coupling constant of the pairing interaction. We identify additional phenomenological parameters that have little effect on ground-state properties, but can be used to fine-tune features such as the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule, the excitation energy of the giant dipole and Gamow-Teller resonances, the static dipole electric polarizability, and the neutron skin thickness.

## Key claim

The paper presents a minimal nuclear energy density functional (NEDF) called "SeaLL1" with only 7 significant phenomenological parameters that achieves global mass fits better than the four-parameter Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula while also providing accurate charge radii, though the authors note that additional parameters beyond these seven have little effect on ground-state properties but can fine-tune collective excitations and neutron skin thickness.

## Method

The authors constructed SeaLL1 by starting with an orbital-free formulation of neutron and proton densities in the spirit of the original Hohenberg-Kohn density functional theory, then minimally extending it with three additional parameters to describe shell effects, pairing correlations, and the density dependence of the symmetry energy. The functional incorporates constraints on the equation of state of pure neutron matter from quantum Monte Carlo calculations using chiral effective field theory two-body interactions at N3LO level and three-body interactions at N2LO level. The seven parameters are each related to specific nuclear properties: saturation density, energy per particle of symmetric nuclear matter, surface tension, symmetry energy and its density dependence, spin-orbit interaction strength, and pairing interaction coupling constant.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear masses of even-even nuclei fitted with mean energy error of 0.97 MeV and standard deviation 1.46 MeV
- Two-neutron separation energies fitted with r.m.s. error of 0.69 MeV
- Two-proton separation energies fitted with r.m.s. error of 0.59 MeV
- Charge radii of 345 even-even nuclei fitted with mean error epsilon_r = 0.022 fm and standard deviation sigma_r = 0.025 fm
- The four-parameter Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula fits 2375 measured nuclear masses with r.m.s. error chi_E = 3.30 MeV per nucleus
- The six-parameter mass formula with surface corrections fits the same 2375 nuclei with r.m.s. error chi_E = 2.64 MeV

## Context

This work extends the tradition of nuclear mass formulas dating back to Weizsäcker (1935) and Bethe (1936), and the macroscopic-microscopic method developed by Strutinsky (1966, 1967, 1968) and later refined by Myers, Swiatecki, and Moller. The authors argue that most modern NEDFs (such as Skyrme and Gogny functionals, and relativistic mean-field models reviewed by Bender in 2003) have seen only incremental improvements despite significant research investment from collaborations such as UNEDF and NUCLEI. SeaLL1 enables future systematic improvement by cleanly separating phenomenological parameters into hierarchies, and the authors identify that additional parameters can fine-tune the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule, giant dipole and Gamow-Teller resonance excitation energies, static dipole electric polarizability, and neutron skin thickness.
