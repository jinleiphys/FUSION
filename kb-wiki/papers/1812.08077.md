# Lepton-jet Correlations in Deep Inelastic Scattering at the Electron-Ion Collider

> We propose the lepton-jet correlation in deep inelastic scattering as a unique tool for nucleon/nucleus tomography at the electron-ion collider. The azimuthal angular correlation between the final state lepton and jet depends on the transverse momentum dependent quark distributions. We take the example of single transverse spin asymmetries to show the sensitivity to the quark Sivers function. When the correlation is studied in lepton-nucleus collisions, transverse momentum broadening effects can be used to explore cold nuclear matter effects. These features make lepton-jet correlations an important new hard probe at the EIC.

# Lepton-jet Correlations in Deep Inelastic Scattering at the Electron-Ion Collider

## Key claim
Lepton-jet azimuthal angular correlations in deep inelastic scattering at the future Electron-Ion Collider can serve as a unique probe for nucleon and nucleus tomography, with the advantage that they depend only on transverse momentum dependent (TMD) quark distributions and not on TMD fragmentation functions, unlike semi-inclusive hadron production. The authors demonstrate this using single transverse spin asymmetries sensitive to the quark Sivers function and transverse momentum broadening in lepton-nucleus collisions, but note that the numerical results for the Sivers asymmetry rely on the assumption that the twist-three correlation of the quark Sivers function has a similar scale dependence as the integrated quark distribution, which introduces a small theoretical uncertainty at low transverse momentum.

## Method
The authors derive TMD factorization for the process ℓ + A → ℓ' + Jet + X in the center-of-mass frame of the incoming lepton and nucleon, where the final-state lepton and jet are nearly back-to-back in the transverse plane. They perform a one-loop calculation of the soft factor associated with the jet, the hard factor, and the TMD quark distribution, including resummation of large logarithms via evolution equations and incorporating non-global logarithms at leading color. For the Sivers asymmetry, they use the parametrization of the quark Sivers function from Sun (2013) and compute the spin-dependent cross section; for nuclear broadening, they modify the TMD factor by an exponential term exp(−q̂L b⊥²/4) representing multiple interactions with cold nuclear matter.

## Key numbers
- The azimuthal angular correlation is computed at √S_ep = 80 GeV, with lepton transverse momentum k_ℓ⊥ = 15 GeV, jet transverse momentum integrated over 10–20 GeV, and jet radius R = 0.5, at equal rapidities y_ℓ = y_J = 1.
- The non-global logarithm contribution yields a suppression of about 5% near Δφ = 0 for the unpolarized correlation.
- The non-perturbative parametrization uses S_NP^q = 0.106 b⊥² + 0.42 ln(Q/Q₀) ln(b⊥/b_*), with Q₀² = 2.4 GeV² and b_max = 1.5 GeV⁻¹.
- The Sivers function parametrization uses g_s = 0.062 GeV² for the Gaussian factor exp(g_s b⊥²).
- For the Sivers asymmetry, results are shown for k_ℓ⊥ = 7, 10, and 15 GeV; at k_ℓ⊥ = 7 GeV the asymmetry is very small because the probed x ≈ 0.03 and the quark Sivers function is very small.
- Nuclear P_T-broadening effects are shown for two typical values of q̂L, in the range of a theoretical estimate for cold nuclear matter from Baier (1996).

## Context
This work extends the study of jet correlations from hadron colliders (dijet measurements at RHIC and LHC, e.g., Adamczyk (2013, 2017)) to deep inelastic scattering at the future EIC, and is complementary to semi-inclusive hadron production in DIS (SIDIS) as developed by Mulders (1995), Boer (1997), and Bacchetta (2006). Unlike SIDIS, the lepton-jet correlation involves only TMD quark distributions without TMD fragmentation functions, enabling direct access to the quark Sivers function and cold nuclear matter effects. The results enable future comparisons with dijet spin asymmetry measurements in pp collisions (Boer 2003, Vogelsang 2005, Bomhof 2007) and with dijet correlation measurements in heavy-ion collisions (Chen 2016, 2018, Adamczyk 2017, Aad 2010, Chatrchyan 2011) to distinguish hot and cold dense nuclear matter effects.
