# Postbounce evolution of core-collapse supernovae: Long-term effects of equation of state

> We study the evolution of supernova core from the beginning of gravitational collapse of a 15Msolar star up to 1 second after core bounce. We present results of spherically symmetric simulations of core-collapse supernovae by solving general relativistic neutrino-radiation-hydrodynamics in the implicit time-differencing. We aim to explore the evolution of shock wave in a long term and investigate the formation of protoneutron star together with supernova neutrino signatures. These studies are done to examine the influence of equation of state (EOS) on the postbounce evolution of shock wave in the late phase and the resulting thermal evolution of protoneutron star. We make a comparison of two sets of EOS, that is, by Lattimer and Swesty (LS-EOS) and by Shen et al.(SH-EOS). We found that, for both EOSs, the core does not explode and the shock wave stalls similarly in the first 100 milliseconds after bounce. The revival of shock wave does not occur even after a long period in either cases. However, the recession of shock wave appears different beyond 200 milliseconds after bounce, having different thermal evolution of central core. A more compact protoneutron star is found for LS-EOS than SH-EOS with a difference in the central density by a factor of ~2 and a difference of ~10 MeV in the peak temperature. Resulting spectra of supernova neutrinos are different to the extent that may be detectable by terrestrial neutrino detectors.

## Key claim

The paper finds that for both the Lattimer-Swesty equation of state (LS-EOS) and the Shen equation of state (SH-EOS), the core does not explode and the shock wave stalls similarly in the first 100 milliseconds after bounce, with no revival even after 1 second; however, the recession of the shock wave differs beyond 200 milliseconds, with a more compact protoneutron star for LS-EOS than SH-EOS.

## Method

The authors performed spherically symmetric simulations of core-collapse supernovae by solving general relativistic neutrino-radiation-hydrodynamics with an implicit time-differencing scheme, following the evolution of a 15 solar mass progenitor from the beginning of gravitational collapse up to 1 second after core bounce. They compared two sets of equations of state: the Lattimer-Swesty EOS (LS-EOS) based on a compressible liquid-drop model, and the Shen EOS (SH-EOS) based on relativistic mean field theory, using standard weak reaction rates from Bruenn (1986) with additional plasmon and nucleon-nucleon bremsstrahlung processes.

## Key numbers

- Central density at bounce: 3.4 x 10^14 g/cm^3 for SH-EOS and 4.4 x 10^14 g/cm^3 for LS-EOS
- Central electron fraction at bounce: Y_e = 0.31 for SH-EOS and Y_e = 0.29 for LS-EOS
- Inner core mass at bounce: 0.61 solar masses for SH-EOS and 0.55 solar masses for LS-EOS
- Central lepton fraction at bounce: Y_L = 0.36 for SH-EOS and Y_L = 0.35 for LS-EOS
- Baryon mass of protoneutron star at 1 second after bounce: 1.60 solar masses for both cases
- Accretion rate: about 0.2 solar masses per second on average, decreasing from 0.25 to 0.15 solar masses per second
- Difference in central density between LS-EOS and SH-EOS: a factor of approximately 2
- Difference in peak temperature between LS-EOS and SH-EOS: approximately 10 MeV
- Incompressibility of nuclear matter: 281 MeV for SH-EOS (TM1 parameter set) and 180 MeV for LS-EOS
- Symmetry energy: 36.9 MeV for SH-EOS and 29.3 MeV for LS-EOS
- Maximum mass of cold neutron star: 2.2 solar masses for SH-EOS and 1.8 solar masses for LS-EOS

## Context

This work extends previous spherically symmetric simulations that had typically followed postbounce evolution only up to about 300 milliseconds, such as those by Liebendorfer (2004) and Janka (2004), by using an implicit method to reach 1 second after bounce. The study enables comparison of the long-term effects of two different nuclear equations of state on protoneutron star formation and supernova neutrino signatures, and the results suggest that the differences in neutrino spectra may be detectable by terrestrial neutrino detectors, providing constraints on the nuclear equation of state at high densities.
