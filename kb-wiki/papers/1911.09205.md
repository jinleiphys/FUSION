# Experimental validation of the theoretical prediction for the optical $S$ matrix

> Scattering of waves is omnipresent in nature in systems with sizes varying from $10^{-15}$ to $10^{25}$ m. Within this 40 orders of magnitude, in a great number of systems, the scattering can be separated in an averaged response that crosses rapidly the scattering region and a fluctuating delayed response. This fact is the basis of the optical model; the averaged response, represented by the optical matrix $\langle S\rangle$, is composed with the fluctuating part that can be taken as a random matrix. Although the optical model was developed more than 60 years ago, a theoretical prediction for the optical matrix was obtained until very recently. The validity of such prediction is experimentally demonstrated here. This is done studying the scattering of torsional waves in a quasi-1D elastic system in which a locally periodic system is built; the distribution of the scattering matrix is calculated completely free of parameters. In contradistinction to all previous works, in microwaves and in elasticity, in which the value of $\langle S\rangle$ is obtained from the experiment, here the theoretical prediction is used to compare with the experiment. Numerical simulations show that the theoretical value is still valid when strong disorder is present. Several applications of the theoretical expression for the optical matrix in other areas of physics are proposed. Possible extensions of this work are also discussed.

# Experimental validation of the theoretical prediction for the optical S matrix

## Key claim

The theoretical prediction for the optical matrix ⟨S⟩, recently derived for a one-dimensional chain of delta potentials, is experimentally validated using torsional waves in a quasi-1D elastic system with a locally periodic structure; the validation is parameter-free, with the only effective parameter being a geometric correction for the three-dimensional nature of the machined beam.

## Method

Torsional waves were excited and detected by electromagnetic acoustic transducers (EMATs) on an aluminum beam of square cross-section (2.54 cm width) containing 100 machined notches forming a locally periodic structure, with a passive vibration isolation system at one end to simulate a semi-infinite beam. The scattering matrix S was measured as a function of frequency using a vector network analyzer, and the phase distribution of a single resonance was compared to Poisson's kernel using the theoretical ⟨S⟩ from Eq. (4) of the paper, which depends only on the reflection and transmission amplitudes of a single unit cell. Numerical simulations with random disorder in the groove depth (uniform distribution of width δ from 0% to 90%) were also performed to test robustness.

## Key numbers

- Beam dimensions: width D = 2.54 cm, total length 3.6 m, with 100 notches of length d = 1.5875 cm and unit cell length D = 2.54 cm; notch height h = 0.9525 cm, with an effective value of 0.746h used for the theoretical comparison due to three-dimensional punching effects.
- The experimental resonance analyzed was centered at f = 2570 Hz, spanning the interval from 2530.0 to 2638.8 Hz.
- Additional resonances were analyzed in intervals: [3244.0, 3399.0] Hz, [4859.8, 5019.8] Hz, [5792.0, 5998.0] Hz, and [9560.0, 9760.0] Hz.
- Numerical disorder simulations used δ values of 0%, 10%, 25%, 50%, 75%, and 90% for the uniform distribution of the groove depth variation.

## Context

This work provides the first experimental validation of the analytical expression for the optical matrix ⟨S⟩, which had been missing for over 60 years since the optical model was introduced by Feshbach, Porter, and Weisskopf (1950s). Previous experiments in microwave cavities and graphs, and in elastic systems (Baez et al., 2014; Martinez-Arguello et al., 2015), extracted ⟨S⟩ from measurements rather than using a theoretical prediction. The validated formula, which depends only on the reflection and transmission amplitudes of a single scatterer, can now be applied to photonic crystals, superlattices, layered media in geology, tight-binding chains, microwave billiards and graphs, and potentially to nuclear scattering systems including neutron scattering from periodic nuclear structures.
