# Hydrodynamic models

> Recent developments based on relativistic hydrodynamic models in high energy heavy ion collisions are discussed. I focus especially on how hydrodynamics works at RHIC energies and how one can use the most of it in analyses of jet quenching and thermal electromagnetic radiations. I also comment on improvement of initial conditions and viscosity in hydrodynamic models.

# Hydrodynamic models

## Key claim

Relativistic hydrodynamics works well at RHIC energies for describing soft physics in limited kinematic regions, but only under the condition that thermalisation is achieved; the paper explicitly notes that hydrodynamics overpredicts data in forward/backward rapidity regions and in peripheral collisions where multiplicity is small, indicating that thermalisation is only partially achieved in those regimes.

## Method

The author reviews relativistic hydrodynamic model results at RHIC, comparing hydrodynamic predictions with experimental data on elliptic flow, jet quenching, and thermal photon emission. The analysis includes (2+1)-dimensional and full 3D hydrodynamic simulations with parametrised initial conditions, as well as event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations using initial conditions from event generators (NeXus) and from colour glass condensate (CGC) calculations. Viscous corrections to momentum distribution functions are also examined using the sound attenuation length formalism.

## Key numbers

- Initial time for hydrodynamic evolution: tau ~ 0.6 fm/c in Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 130 and 200 GeV
- Scaled elliptic flow v2/epsilon becomes almost constant around 0.2 from hydrodynamic simulations
- Hydrodynamics works well for charged hadrons in the region: impact parameter |b| <~ 5 fm, p_T <~ 1.5 GeV, and pseudorapidity |eta| <~ 2
- Deviation of hydrodynamic results from experimental data starts at N_ch/N_max ~ 0.5, corresponding to impact parameter b ~ 5 fm
- Initial time in hydrodynamic simulations is typically chosen as tau_0 ~ 0.6-1.0 fm/c
- Typical lifetime of the QGP phase from hydrodynamic simulations is around 3 fm/c in central collisions at RHIC

## Context

This review extends the work of Kolb, Heinz, Huovinen, and others (KHHH) who first showed that large elliptic flow at RHIC is consistent with hydrodynamic predictions, in contrast to lower energies at AGS and SPS where hydrodynamics overpredicts data and hadronic transport models (e.g., Bass and Srivastava, Ref. BS) describe data well. The paper enables systematic studies combining hydrodynamics with jet quenching analyses (following Gyulassy, Vitev, Wang, and Horowitz, Ref. GVWH) and thermal photon calculations, and points to open questions about early thermalisation times and the HBT puzzle.
