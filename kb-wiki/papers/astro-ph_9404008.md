# Geminga: A Cooling Superfluid Neutron Star

> We show that Geminga's surface temperature can be understood by both types of neutrino cooling scenarios, i.e, slow neutrino cooling by the modified Urca process or fast neutrino cooling by the direct Urca process or by some exotic matter, and thus does not allow us to discriminate between these two competing schemes. However, for both types of scenarios, agreement with the observed temperature can only be obtained if BARYON PAIRING IS PRESENT IN MOST, IF NOT ALL, OF THE CORE OF THE STAR. We also comment on the recent temperature estimates for PSR 0656+14 and PSR 1055-52, which pertain to the same photon cooling era. We argue though that observational evidence for the slow neutrino cooling model (the ``standard'' model) is in fact very dim and that the interpretation of the surface temperature of all neutron stars could be done with a reasonable theoretical a priori within the fast neutrino cooling scenarios only. In this case, Geminga, PSR 0656+14, and PSR 1055-52 all show evidence of baryon pairing down to their very centers. xxx----To be published in Ap.J. (June 10 isue).---xxx

## Key claim

Geminga's observed surface temperature can be explained by either slow neutrino cooling (modified Urca process) or fast neutrino cooling (direct Urca process or exotic matter), but only if baryon pairing is present in most, if not all, of the star's core. The paper argues that observational evidence for the slow neutrino cooling model is very dim, and that all neutron star surface temperatures could be interpreted within fast neutrino cooling scenarios alone.

## Method

The author compared Geminga's estimated age (from spin-down) and surface temperature (from ROSAT observations) with theoretical neutron star cooling models. Cooling curves were calculated using a Heyney-type code solving general relativistic heat transport and energy balance equations, incorporating neutrino emissivities from various processes (modified Urca, direct Urca, kaon/pion condensate), nucleon pairing effects via BCS theory, and five different equations of state (MPA, FP, WFF(av14), PAL32, PAL33) for the slow cooling scenario.

## Key numbers

- Geminga's spin-down age: τ = 3.4 × 10^5 yr (from P = 0.2370974 s, Ṗ = 1.099 × 10^{-14} s/s)
- Geminga's surface temperature: T = 5.2 ± 1.0 × 10^5 K (from Halpern & Ruderman 1993 blackbody fit)
- Age range considered: 2.3 × 10^5 yr ≤ t ≤ 6.8 × 10^5 yr (for braking indices n = 2 to 4)
- Effective temperature range for comparison: 4 × 10^5 K ≤ T_e ≤ 6 × 10^5 K
- Ratio of hot to cold emitting region areas: about 3 × 10^{-5}
- For a 1.4 M⊙ star with direct Urca but no pairing, temperature at Geminga's age: 1.5 × 10^5 K (too low for ROSAT detection)
- Fast cooling with pion condensate and maximum heating: at most 3 × 10^5 K at Geminga's age
- In slow cooling with full core neutron pairing, specific heat reduced by factor of 20 (only electron and muon contributions remain)

## Context

This work extends previous neutron star cooling studies by Page & Baron (1990), Page & Applegate (1992), and Umeda et al. (1992) to include the newly identified Geminga pulsar. It contradicts the assumption that the "standard" slow neutrino cooling model can explain observed neutron star temperatures without extensive pairing. The paper enables future work to focus on determining which specific fast neutrino cooling process operates in neutron stars, and to use pairing critical temperatures as a diagnostic of dense matter properties. The author also comments on similar temperature estimates for PSR 0656+14 and PSR 1055-52 from Finley, Ogelman & Kiziloglu (1992) and Ogelman & Finley (1993).
