# INFN What Next: Ultra-relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> This document was prepared by the community that is active in Italy, within INFN (Istituto Nazionale di Fisica Nucleare), in the field of ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. The experimental study of the phase diagram of strongly-interacting matter and of the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) deconfined state will proceed, in the next 10-15 years, along two directions: the high-energy regime at RHIC and at the LHC, and the low-energy regime at FAIR, NICA, SPS and RHIC. The Italian community is strongly involved in the present and future programme of the ALICE experiment, the upgrade of which will open, in the 2020s, a new phase of high-precision characterisation of the QGP properties at the LHC. As a complement of this main activity, there is a growing interest in a possible future experiment at the SPS, which would target the search for the onset of deconfinement using dimuon measurements. On a longer timescale, the community looks with interest at the ongoing studies and discussions on a possible fixed-target programme using the LHC ion beams and on the Future Circular Collider.

## Key claim

The Italian heavy-ion community outlines a 10-15 year programme where the ALICE detector upgrade will enable high-precision characterisation of the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) at LHC energies, while the community also expresses growing interest in a possible future SPS experiment targeting the onset of deconfinement via dimuon measurements, contingent on the successful completion of the ALICE upgrade programme.

## Method

The paper reviews the experimental programme for ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, focusing on three main physics topics: low-mass dileptons, jets, and (hyper)nuclei production. For each topic, the authors summarise existing measurements from RHIC (PHENIX, STAR) and LHC (ALICE, CMS, ATLAS) experiments, then present projected performance studies for the upgraded ALICE detector in LHC Run-3, based on simulations of expected yields and statistical sensitivities for an integrated luminosity of 3-10 nb$^{-1}$ in Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.5$ TeV.

## Key numbers

- Dielectron spectrum measured in minimum-bias Au-Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV by STAR and PHENIX at RHIC, consistent with calculations showing strong broadening of the $\rho$ spectral function
- ALICE upgrade projected to collect $L_{\rm int} = 3$ nb$^{-1}$ in one month of running with 0.2 T magnetic field, enabling electron detection down to $p_T \approx 50$ MeV/$c$
- Temperature of thermal dilepton source measurable with statistical precision of about 10% and systematic uncertainty of about 20% in the invariant-mass region $1.1 < M_{ee} < 2.5$ GeV/$c^2$
- Jet $R_{\rm CP}$ ratios for $R = 0.4$ and $0.5$ at $p_T < 100$ GeV/$c$ differ from unity, indicating jet broadening; for $R \leq 0.4$ at $p_T > 100$ GeV/$c$, ratio consistent with jet production in vacuum
- Expected yields for central Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.5$ TeV with $L_{\rm int} = 10$ nb$^{-1}$: $3.1 \times 10^8$ deuterons, $2.2 \times 10^6$ $^3$He nuclei, $1.5 \times 10^3$ $^4$He nuclei, $4.4 \times 10^3$ $^3_{\Lambda}$H hypernuclei, $1.9 \times 10^5$ $\Lambda\Lambda$ dibaryons
- Signal-to-background ratio for $^3_{\Lambda}$H at $p_T > 2$ GeV/$c$ expected to be about 0.1 with significance of about 60
- ALICE Run-1 limits on mass-to-charge ratio differences: $2.4 \times 10^{-4}$ for deuteron/anti-deuteron and $2.1 \times 10^{-3}$ for $^3$He/$^3\overline{\rm He}$ at 90% confidence level
- Projected precision for mass-to-charge ratio measurements with LHC Run-3 and Run-4 data-taking potentially down to $10^{-6}$

## Context

This white paper extends the community roadmap from earlier INFN planning documents and builds on measurements from PHENIX and STAR at RHIC (Adare 2015, Adamczyk 2013, 2015) and ALICE at LHC (Adam 2015). The low-mass dilepton programme follows the theoretical framework of Rapp (2000) that successfully described SPS data from NA60, while the jet programme extends measurements from ALICE (Adam 2015), CMS (Chatrchyan 2011, 2012, 2013), and ATLAS (Aad 2012). The (hyper)nuclei programme enables CPT tests extending the work of ALICE (Adam 2015) and searches for exotic states following predictions from the statistical hadronisation model (Andronic 2010) and lattice QCD studies (HAL QCD Collaboration, Inoue 2011).
