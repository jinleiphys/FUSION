# Influence of shear viscosity on the correlation between the triangular flow and initial spatial triangularity

> In a hydrodynamic model, with fluctuating initial conditions, the correlation between triangular flow and initial spatial triangularity is studied. The triangular flow, even in ideal fluid, is only weakly correlated with the initial triangularity. The correlation is largely reduced in viscous fluid. Elliptic flow on the other hand appears to be strongly correlated with initial eccentricity. Weak correlation between triangular flow and initial triangularity indicate that a part of triangular flow is unrelated to initial triangularity. Triangularity acquired during the fluid evolution also contributes to the triangular flow.

# Influence of shear viscosity on the correlation between the triangular flow and initial spatial triangularity

## Key claim

Triangular flow ($v_3$) is only weakly correlated with initial spatial triangularity ($\epsilon_3$) even in ideal fluid, and the correlation is further reduced in viscous fluid; this indicates that a substantial part of triangular flow arises from triangularity acquired during fluid evolution rather than from the initial state, unlike elliptic flow which remains strongly correlated with initial eccentricity across all viscosities studied.

## Method

The author used a hydrodynamic model with fluctuating initial conditions generated by a hot-spot model, where each participating nucleon pair deposits energy in a Gaussian-distributed hot spot with width $\sigma=1$ fm, superimposed according to a Glauber model for Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s}_{NN}=2.76$ TeV. The space-time evolution of the fluid was obtained by solving Israel-Stewart's second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamics (code AZHYDRO-KOLKATA) with a lattice-based equation of state, for impact parameter $b=8.9$ fm (30-40% centrality) and four values of shear viscosity to entropy density ratio ($\eta/s=0$, 0.08, 0.12, 0.16). For each viscosity, 500 events were simulated, and the correlation between flow coefficients and initial spatial asymmetry was quantified using a correlation measure $C_{measure}$ that compares dispersion of simulated flow coefficients around a best-fit line to that of random flow coefficients.

## Key numbers

- For elliptic flow in ideal fluid ($\eta/s=0$), the correlation measure $C_{measure}(v_2)=0.46/8.77\approx 0.05$, indicating strong correlation (only ~5% of flow unrelated to initial eccentricity).
- For triangular flow in ideal fluid ($\eta/s=0$), $C_{measure}(v_3)=2.46/8.77\approx 0.28$, indicating weak correlation (~28% of flow unrelated to initial triangularity).
- For the most viscous fluid studied ($\eta/s=0.16$), $C_{measure}(v_2)=1.77/8.77\approx 0.20$ (elliptic flow) and $C_{measure}(v_3)=4.50/8.77\approx 0.51$ (triangular flow).
- The initial time for hydrodynamic evolution was set to $\tau_i=0.6$ fm, and freeze-out temperature was $T_F=130$ MeV.
- The pseudo-critical temperature for the QCD transition was taken as $T_c\approx 170$ MeV from Wuppertal-Budapest lattice simulations.

## Context

This work extends the study of event-by-event fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions, following the conjecture by Alver and Roland (2010) and Teaney and Yan (2010) that triangular flow arises from initial triangularity of the medium. The author previously used the same hot-spot model to study viscous effects on elliptic and triangular flow (Chaudhuri, arXiv:1108.5552), and Bhalerao et al. (2011) used a similar model for anisotropy studies. The finding that triangular flow is only weakly correlated with initial triangularity challenges the standard interpretation and suggests that final-state effects, such as those from jet quenching or Cerenkov radiation, which the author notes can produce triangularity in density distributions at late times (Chaudhuri, 2006), contribute significantly to the observed $v_3$. This result implies that extracting initial-state properties from triangular flow measurements requires accounting for dynamical triangularity generation during the fluid evolution.
