# Field-theoretical description of the deuteron breakup in the clothed particle representation

> We present a field-theoretical description of the deuteron electrodisintegration reaction d(e,e'p)n induced by unpolarized and polarized electrons. The approach combines the Lehmann-Symanzik-Zimmermann in(out) formalism with the clothed particle representation in the instant form of relativistic dynamics, providing a fully relativistic and gauge-independent framework based on the Fock-Weyl criterion. Within the method of unitary clothing transformations, one and the same transformation that generates the relativistic nucleon-nucleon interaction (the Kharkiv potential) also induces a fresh family of electromagnetic current operators. As a result, one-body and two-body (meson-exchange) currents emerge on a common footing. We compute differential cross sections and polarization observables with the inclusion of final-state interaction effects and meson-exchange current contributions, and compare the results with Saclay and Jefferson Lab data as well as with earlier theoretical predictions. The role of relativistic ingredients (one- and two-body currents, Fermi-motion effects, etc.) and the interplay between them are analyzed in several kinematic regimes of the experiments at Saclay and Jefferson Lab.

## Key claim

The paper presents a fully relativistic and gauge-independent field-theoretical description of deuteron electrodisintegration that, by using the same unitary clothing transformation to generate both the nucleon-nucleon interaction (Kharkiv potential) and the electromagnetic current operators, ensures that one-body and two-body (meson-exchange) currents emerge on a common footing, with the caveat that the approach is applied within the one-photon-exchange approximation.

## Method

The authors combine the Lehmann-Symanzik-Zimmermann in(out) formalism with the clothed particle representation in the instant form of relativistic dynamics, constructing the deuteron and moving neutron-proton pair states as eigenvectors of the energy-momentum operator. They employ unitary clothing transformations to derive the relativistic nucleon-nucleon interaction (Kharkiv potential) and a consistent family of electromagnetic current operators from a meson-nucleon field model including pi, rho, delta, eta, omega, and sigma mesons. The reaction amplitude is computed within the one-photon-exchange approximation, with final-state interaction effects included via Moller operators and meson-exchange currents incorporated through both isoscalar and isovector two-nucleon currents.

## Key numbers

* Differential cross sections and polarization observables are computed for the deuteron electrodisintegration reaction d(e,e'p)n and compared with Saclay and Jefferson Lab data, with the kinematic regime reaching missing neutron momenta up to approximately 1.0 GeV/c at Jefferson Lab.
* The structure functions W_C, W_T, W_S, and W_I (Coulomb, transverse, spin, and interference) are defined in terms of the hadronic tensor components, with the cross section expressed as sigma_0 = sigma_M [xi^2 W_C + (1/2 xi + eta) W_T + (xi + eta) W_S + xi sqrt(xi+eta) W_I], where xi = Q^2/|q|^2 and eta = tan^2(theta_e/2).
* The induced proton polarization P (orthogonal to the reaction plane) and the transferred polarization P' (with components P'_X and P'_Z in the reaction plane) are computed, where P'_Y = 0 and the transferred polarization does not vanish when final-state interaction is neglected.

## Context

This work extends the non-relativistic treatment of deuteron electrodisintegration by Arenhövel and colleagues (1979) and the earlier calculations by Korchin, Mel'nik, and Shebeko (1988, 1990, 1993, 1995), which included final-state interaction and meson-exchange current effects but lacked full relativistic invariance. It also builds upon the relativistic descriptions by Laget (2005), Jeschonnek and Van Orden (2008), and Sargsian and Boeglin (2001, 2010, 2024), while providing a gauge-independent framework that satisfies the Poincare algebra. The approach enables consistent treatment of relativistic one-body and two-body currents for future applications to three-nucleon systems and higher momentum transfer regimes.
