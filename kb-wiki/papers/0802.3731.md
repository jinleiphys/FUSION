# Non-Spherical Shapes of the Proton: Existence, Measurement and Computation

> The nature of the proton wave function can be elucidated by studying the matrix elements of spin-dependent quark density operators SDDs, defined as matrix elements of density operators in proton states of definite spin-polarization. These have an infinite variety of non-spherical shapes. The matrix elements of the SDDs are closely related to specific transverse momentum dependent TMD parton distributions accessible in the angular dependence of certain semi-inclusive processes. New measurements would allow the direct exhibition of the non-spherical nature of the proton. The TMDs can be computed using lattice QCD so that the non-spherical shapes could eventually be measured experimentally and computed using fundamental theory.

# Non-Spherical Shapes of the Proton: Existence, Measurement and Computation

## Key claim

The proton can exhibit non-spherical shapes when probed via spin-dependent density operators (SDDs), even though the Wigner-Eckart theorem forbids a static quadrupole moment for a spin-1/2 particle. The non-sphericity is revealed specifically in the rest-frame matrix elements of these SDDs, not in elastic scattering form factors, and requires controlling three independent vectors: the quark spin direction, the proton polarization direction, and the quark momentum direction.

## Method

The author defines spin-dependent density operators (SDDs) as rest-frame matrix elements of density operators in proton states of definite spin-polarization, including both non-relativistic and relativistic (gamma-matrix) formulations. These SDDs are then related to transverse-momentum-dependent (TMD) parton distributions by integrating over the longitudinal momentum component, showing that the TMD function h_{1T}^\perp (the pretzelosity distribution) directly encodes the non-spherical shape. The shapes are computed using a relativistic constituent quark model (Frank et al., 1995) and a spectator model (Jakob et al., 1997), and a lattice QCD observable is proposed via the angular integral of a two-point correlation function weighted by a spherical harmonic Y_{20}.

## Key numbers

- The quark and anti-quark spin contribution to the proton total angular momentum is only about 30%, as discovered in the "spin crisis" (oldspin).
- The integrated SDD difference (quark spin parallel minus anti-parallel to proton spin) yields a value of 0.68 from experimental inputs (Delta q = 0.3, g_A = 1.26), while the model gives 0.74.
- The hydrogen atom ground state SDD shows a non-spherical term proportional to alpha^2/4 cos^2 theta, where alpha is the fine structure constant, giving a deviation from sphericity of order 10^{-5}.
- The model proton shapes are displayed for quark momenta K ranging from 0 to 4 GeV/c, showing evolution from spherical to peanut-shaped (spin parallel) or bagel/torus-shaped (spin anti-parallel).
- Transverse shapes are shown for K_T from 0 to 2.0 GeV in steps of 0.25 GeV, with increasing deformation.

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier demonstration (Miller, 2003) that relativistic constituent quark models predict non-spherical proton shapes via SDDs, and connects those shapes to experimentally accessible TMD parton distributions. The paper builds on the discovery that the ratio G_E/G_M falls rapidly with Q^2 (Jones, 1999; Gayou, 2001), which the author's 1995 model (Frank et al., 1995; Miller, 2002) explained through relativistic effects and quark orbital angular momentum. The key TMD h_{1T}^\perp (pretzelosity) produces a cos(3 phi_h^l) angular dependence in semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering (Boer, 1997; Bacchetta, 2000) and can be measured in planned Jefferson Laboratory experiments (Avakian). The paper also proposes a lattice QCD observable using spherical harmonic projections of two-point correlation functions, with preliminary one-link results (Negele) but insufficient statistics.
