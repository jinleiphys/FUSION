# Coulomb excitation at intermediate energies

> Straight line trajectories are commonly used in semi-classical calculations of the first-order Coulomb excitation cross section at intermediate energies, and simple corrections are often made for the distortion of the trajectories that is caused by the Coulomb field. These approximations are tested by comparing to numerical calculations that use exact Coulomb trajectories. In this paper a model is devised for including relativistic effects in the calculations. It converges at high energies towards the relativistic straight-line trajectory approximation and approaches the non-relativistic Coulomb trajectory calculation at low energies. The model is tested against a number of measurements and analyses that have been performed at beam energies between 30 and 70 MeV/nucleon, primarily of quadrupole excitations. Remarkably good agreement is achieved with the previous analyses, and good agreement is also achieved in the few cases, where the B(E$λ$) value is known from other methods. The magnitudes of the relativistic and Coulomb distortion effects are discussed.

# Coulomb excitation at intermediate energies

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a simple interpolating model, which combines relativistic straight-line trajectory expressions with non-relativistic Coulomb trajectory calculations, can reproduce measured Coulomb excitation cross sections at intermediate beam energies (30-70 MeV/nucleon) to within a few percent, despite earlier claims that corrections could be as large as 20-30%.

## Method

The author develops an interpolating model by replacing the adiabaticity parameter in the non-relativistic Coulomb trajectory orbital integrals with a relativistically corrected form, $\xi_a = \omega a/(\gamma v)$, and incorporating relativistic factors $\bar{G}_{\lambda\mu}$ from the straight-line trajectory formalism of Winther and Alder. The model is tested by comparing calculated cross sections to experimental measurements for dipole and quadrupole excitations, primarily using numerical integration of the orbital integrals for Coulomb trajectories in coordinate system A, then transforming to coordinate system H.

## Key numbers

- The interpolating model reproduces measured cross sections with an average ratio $\sigma_{exp}/\sigma_{Coul}$ about 2% less than 1, based on comparisons to experiments at beam energies between 30 and 70 MeV/nucleon.
- The relativistic straight-line trajectory approximation (without Coulomb corrections) gives cross sections typically 5-10% higher than measured values.
- Coulomb distortion effects on the cross section amount to about 3-9% for the experiments considered (runs no. 4-8 in Table I).
- For the test cases of $^{24}$Mg and $^{26}$Mg, where B(E2) values are known from other methods, the experimental uncertainties are about 5% on cross sections and 3% on B(E2) values.
- Comparison with the more elaborate theory of Bertulani et al. shows agreement at the level of $1.005 \pm 0.002$ (average ratio) after correcting for a missing factor of $e^2$ in the earlier work.
- The asymptotic high-energy expression for quadrupole excitation cross section is $\sigma_{\lambda=2} = \frac{1}{3} \left(\frac{4\pi Z_1 e^2}{5\hbar v b_0}\right)^2 B(E2)$, which is insensitive to excitation energy.
- The dipole cross section in the high-energy limit shows a logarithmic dependence on $\gamma$: $\sigma_{\lambda=1} = \left(\frac{4\pi Z_1 e^2}{3\hbar v}\right)^2 B(E1) \left[2\ln\left(\frac{1.123 \gamma v}{\omega b_0}\right) - \left(\frac{v}{c}\right)^2\right]$.

## Context

This work extends the semi-classical Coulomb excitation formalism of Alder and Winther (1956, 1975), which provided non-relativistic Coulomb trajectory calculations and relativistic straight-line trajectory approximations separately, by bridging the gap at intermediate energies (20-200 MeV/nucleon) where both relativistic effects and Coulomb trajectory distortions matter. The model enables reliable analysis of Coulomb excitation experiments at radioactive beam facilities, including Coulomb dissociation experiments of $^8$B, $^{11}$Be, and $^{11}$Li, and inverse-kinematics measurements of $2^+$ states in light and medium-heavy nuclei. The paper resolves a debate in the literature (Bertulani et al., 2007; Scheit, 2007) about the validity of previous analyses by showing that the commonly used "Coulomb corrected" straight-line trajectory method, originally proposed by Winther and Alder (1975), is sufficiently accurate when properly applied, with uncertainties reduced to only a few percent rather than the 20-30% previously claimed.
