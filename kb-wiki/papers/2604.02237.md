# Formal definition of intrinsic collectivity in the continuum via Takagi factorization of the Jost-RPA S-matrix residue

> A formal and systematic framework is proposed to quantify the intrinsic collectivity of resonance states in the continuum, independent of their extrinsic manifestation in the strength function. By integrating Takagi factorization into the Jost-RPA framework, we utilize the rank-1 property of the S-matrix residue at a resonance pole to uniquely decompose it into microscopic transition amplitudes for each configuration. To evaluate the nature of these modes, we introduce the Intrinsic Coherence Index ($C^{(n)}$) and the Collective Phase ($Θ^{(n)}$), which characterize the dynamical phase synchronization and the line-shape orientation, respectively. Furthermore, a unified Total Collectivity Index ($R^{(n)}$) is defined by combining the coherence index with the Normalized Participation Ratio ($η^{(n)}$). Applying this framework to the isoscalar $2^+$, isovector $2^+$, and $E1$ excitations in $^{16}$O, we demonstrate that the intrinsic collectivity is decoupled from the observable line shape. Our analysis identifies "hidden" collective modes -- states with high internal synchronization that do not appear as prominent peaks -- and clarifies that distorted structures or dips can either be highly collective or non-collective depending on their microscopic phase alignment. This approach provides a well-defined structural basis for investigating many-body excitations in open quantum systems and nuclei near the drip lines.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a resonance state's intrinsic collectivity, the degree of dynamical phase synchronization among its microscopic configurations, is formally decoupled from its extrinsic manifestation as a peak, dip, or asymmetric line shape in the strength function, and can be quantified independently via Takagi factorization of the S-matrix residue within the Jost-RPA framework.

## Method

The authors integrate Takagi factorization into the Jost-RPA framework, exploiting the rank-1 property of the complex-symmetric S-matrix residue at an isolated resonance pole to uniquely decompose it into microscopic transition amplitudes \(F_i^{(n)}\) for each particle-hole configuration. Using these amplitudes, they define the Intrinsic Coherence Index \(C^{(n)}\) (measuring phase alignment of configurations), the Collective Phase \(\Theta^{(n)}\) (determining line-shape orientation), the Normalized Participation Ratio \(\eta^{(n)}\) (measuring how many configurations effectively contribute), and the Total Collectivity Index \(R^{(n)}\) (the \(L_2\) norm of \(C^{(n)}\) and \(\eta^{(n)}\)). The framework is applied to isoscalar \(2^+\), isovector \(2^+\), and \(E1\) excitations in \(^{16}\)O using Woods-Saxon potential and residual interaction parameters identical to those in prior Jost-RPA studies by Mizuyama (2025).

## Key numbers

- Isoscalar \(2^+\) pole (1) at \(E_n = 16.76 - i0.11\) MeV: \(C^{(1)} = 0.95\), \(R^{(1)} = 0.76\), \(\Theta^{(1)} = 0.99\pi\), \(\text{Re } r_F^{(1)} = 2.64 \times 10^3\) fm\(^4\), asymmetry ratio \(-0.05\) (prototype of ideal collective motion, the ISGQR).
- Isoscalar \(2^+\) poles (4) at \(27.02 - i1.50\) MeV and (5) at \(28.48 - i0.57\) MeV: \(C \approx 6.4 \times 10^{-2}\) to \(7.5 \times 10^{-2}\), \(R \approx 0.20\) to \(0.25\), negative \(\text{Re } r_F^{(n)}\) (non-collective dip structures).
- Isoscalar \(2^+\) poles (8) at \(34.75 - i0.59\) MeV and (9) at \(36.61 - i0.42\) MeV: \(C \approx 0.11\) to \(0.17\), \(R \approx 0.24\) to \(0.27\), but appear as symmetric peaks due to \(\Theta\) near \(0\) or \(\pm\pi\) (deceptive line shapes).
- Isovector \(2^+\) pole (9) at \(36.61 - i0.42\) MeV: highest coherence in channel with \(C^{(9)} = 0.59\), \(R^{(9)} = 0.48\), \(\Theta^{(9)} = -0.22\pi\) (most synchronized mode despite asymmetric line shape).
- Isovector \(2^+\) pole (6) at \(30.05 - i0.66\) MeV: extreme asymmetry ratio \(-16.58\) (highly distorted structure).
- \(E1\) pole (4) at \(19.34 - i0.53\) MeV: largest real residue \(\text{Re } r_F = 9.36 \times 10^{-2} e^2\)fm\(^2\) but lowest collectivity among selected \(E1\) poles with \(R^{(4)} = 0.49\).
- \(E1\) pole (6) at \(20.76 - i0.34\) MeV: "hidden" collective mode with \(R^{(6)} = 0.61\), \(C^{(6)} = 0.36\), \(\eta^{(6)} = 0.78\), despite appearing as the smallest peak.
- \(E1\) pole (7) at \(21.71 - i0.63\) MeV: highest intrinsic collectivity in \(E1\) channel with \(R^{(7)} = 0.71\), \(C^{(7)} = 0.54\), \(\eta^{(7)} = 0.85\), despite highly asymmetric shape (\(\Theta^{(7)} = -0.22\pi\)).

## Context

This work extends the Jost-RPA framework developed by Mizuyama (2025) and earlier Jost-RPA studies (JostRPA, JostRPA2), which provided the complex-symmetric S-matrix and response function decomposition but lacked a formal method to extract configuration-specific amplitudes at resonance poles. It contrasts with traditional collectivity measures based on strength function peak prominence, Weisskopf single-particle units, or energy-weighted sum rule exhaustion (Weisskopf, 1951), which the authors argue provide only extrinsic measures. The approach also addresses limitations of conventional continuum RPA methods (Shlomo, 1975) and Berggren-basis approaches (Berggren, 1968) that could not uniquely decompose resonance residues. The framework enables systematic classification of resonant states near the drip lines and in high-energy regions, particularly identifying "hidden" collective modes that would be overlooked by strength-function analysis alone.
