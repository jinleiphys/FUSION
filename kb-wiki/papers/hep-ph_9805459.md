# Mapping of composite hadrons into elementary hadrons and effective hadronic Hamiltonians

> A mapping technique is used to derive in the context of constituent quark models effective Hamiltonians that involve explicit hadron degrees of freedom. The technique is based on the ideas of mapping between physical and ideal Fock spaces and shares similarities with the quasiparticle method of Weinberg. Starting with the Fock-space representation of single-hadron states, a change of representation is implemented by a unitary transformation such that composites are redescribed by elementary Bose and Fermi field operators in an extended Fock space. When the unitary transformation is applied to the microscopic quark Hamiltonian, effective, hermitian Hamiltonians with a clear physical interpretation are obtained. Applications and comparisons with other composite-particle formalisms of the recent literature are made using the nonrelativistic quark model.

## Key claim

The Fock-Tani mapping technique provides a systematic, unitary method to derive effective hadronic Hamiltonians from microscopic constituent quark models, redescribing composite hadrons as elementary Bose and Fermi fields in an extended Fock space, but the resulting transformed operators are obtained as an infinite series expansion in powers of the bound-state wave functions, and practical calculations require truncation to a finite order (typically up to third order in the wave functions).

## Method

The authors start with the Fock-space representation of single-hadron states (mesons as quark-antiquark pairs, baryons as three-quark states) and introduce an enlarged "ideal state space" containing both the original quark operators and new ideal hadron operators that satisfy canonical (anti)commutation relations. A unitary transformation generated by an operator \(F = m^\dagger_\alpha O_\alpha - O^\dagger_\alpha m_\alpha\) is constructed to map composite hadron states into ideal elementary hadron states; the transformed quark operators are obtained by solving equations of motion iteratively up to third order in the bound-state amplitudes. The unitary transformation is then applied to a microscopic quark Hamiltonian to produce effective Hamiltonians that describe interactions among hadrons and between hadrons and quarks, with the binding effects absorbed into the non-interacting part.

## Key numbers

- The meson creation operator is defined as \(M^\dagger_\alpha = \Phi_\alpha^{\mu\nu} q_\mu^\dagger \bar{q}_\nu^\dagger\), where \(\Phi_\alpha^{\mu\nu}\) is the Fock-space amplitude satisfying the normalization condition \(\Phi_\alpha^{*\mu\nu} \Phi_\beta^{\mu\nu} = \delta_{\alpha\beta}\).
- The meson operators satisfy noncanonical commutation relations \([M_\alpha, M^\dagger_\beta] = \delta_{\alpha\beta} - \Delta_{\alpha\beta}\), where \(\Delta_{\alpha\beta} = \Phi_\alpha^{*\mu\nu} \Phi_\beta^{\mu\sigma} \bar{q}^\dagger_\sigma \bar{q}_\nu + \Phi_\alpha^{*\mu\nu} \Phi_\beta^{\rho\nu} q^\dagger_\rho q_\mu\).
- The transformed quark operators are obtained by setting \(t = -\pi/2\) in the solutions of the equations of motion, with the generator \(F = m^\dagger_\alpha O_\alpha - O^\dagger_\alpha m_\alpha\) where \(O_\alpha = M_\alpha + \frac{1}{2}\Delta_{\alpha\beta} M_\beta - \frac{1}{2} M^\dagger_\beta [\Delta_{\beta\gamma}, M_\alpha] M_\gamma + \mathcal{O}(\Phi^3)\).

## Context

This work extends the Fock-Tani representation, originally developed by Girardeau (1971) and Vorob'ev and Khomkin (1976) in atomic physics, to hadronic physics within constituent quark models. It shares similarities with the quasiparticle method of Weinberg (1963) and with the Composite Particle Representation (CPR) of Wu and Feng (1980s) used by Zhu et al. (1990s) and Pittel et al. (1990s) for baryon spectra and hadron-hadron interactions. The method enables systematic derivation of effective hadronic Hamiltonians suitable for applications such as meson-meson scattering, nucleon-nucleon potentials (compared with the Quark-Born diagram method of Barnes et al., 1990s), and charmonium dissociation in matter, while avoiding the complications of noncanonical commutation relations by shifting the composite nature into the effective interactions.
