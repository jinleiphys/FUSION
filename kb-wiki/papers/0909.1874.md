# Beyond mean-field description of break-up, transfer and fusion

> Microscopic theories beyond mean-field are developed to include pairing, in-medium nucleon-nucleon collisions as well as effects of initial fluctuations of one-body observables on nuclear dynamics. These theories are applied to nuclear reactions. The role of pairing on nuclear break-up is discussed. By including the effect of zero point motion of collective variables through a stochastic mean-field theory, not only average evolution of one-body observables are properly described but also fluctuations. Diffusion coefficients in fusion as well as mass distributions in transfer reactions are estimated.

# Beyond mean-field description of break-up, transfer and fusion

## Key claim

Microscopic theories beyond the standard Time-Dependent Energy Density Functional (TD-EDF) approach can incorporate pairing correlations and fluctuations of one-body observables, but the practical implementations (TDDM$^{\rm P}$ and Stochastic Mean-Field theory) rely on specific approximations, such as restricting correlations to time-reversed pairs or using ensembles of independent Slater determinants, that limit the scope of correlations included.

## Method

The authors develop two complementary extensions of mean-field theory. The first, TDDM$^{\rm P}$, reduces the Time-Dependent Density-Matrix formalism by assuming that the effective interaction and two-body correlation matrix elements are non-zero only between pairs of time-reversed states, thereby isolating pairing correlations while discarding other channels. The second, Stochastic Mean-Field (SMF) theory, constructs an ensemble of initial Slater determinants with Gaussian-distributed density-matrix fluctuations whose variance is chosen to reproduce the quantal zero-point motion of one-body observables; each determinant then evolves independently under its own self-consistent mean-field Hamiltonian. Macroscopic reductions of the SMF evolution yield Langevin-type equations for collective variables such as the relative distance between colliding nuclei or the fragment mass, from which diffusion coefficients are extracted.

## Key numbers

- For head-on $^{40}$Ca+$^{40}$Ca collisions at center-of-mass energy $E_{\rm c.m.} = 100$ MeV, reduced friction $\beta(R)$ and momentum diffusion coefficient $D_{PP}(R)$ are estimated as functions of relative distance (shown in Figure 2 of the paper).
- In $^{40}$Ca+$^{40}$Ca transfer reactions below the Coulomb barrier, the asymptotic mass dispersion $\sigma^2_{AA}$ from SMF is 0.73, 1.72, and 3.79 for the three energies studied, compared to mean-field values of 0.004, 0.008, and 0.008 respectively.
- The number of exchanged nucleons $N_{\rm ex}$ in those same reactions is 0.43, 1.44, and 3.63 from low to high energy, closely matching the SMF mass dispersions.

## Context

This work extends the standard Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) framework, which describes only mean values of one-body observables and fails to account for fluctuations or pairing correlations in nuclear reactions. The TDDM$^{\rm P}$ approach builds on earlier Time-Dependent Density-Matrix methods (Wang and Cassing, 1985) by reducing numerical complexity through the time-reversed-pair approximation. The SMF theory, introduced by Ayik (2008), provides a practical route toward multi-reference TD-EDF and is shown here to reproduce the quantal dissipation-fluctuation relation derived from the Balian-Venéroni variational principle (Balian and Venéroni, 1984). The results enable microscopic estimation of transport coefficients for fusion and transfer reactions, and suggest that coincidence measurements of nucleon emission angles in break-up reactions can probe correlation properties inside nuclei.
