# Toward the discovery of matter creation with neutrinoless double-beta decay

> The discovery of neutrinoless double-beta decay could soon be within reach. This hypothetical ultra-rare nuclear decay offers a privileged portal to physics beyond the Standard Model of particle physics. Its observation would constitute the discovery of a matter-creating process, corroborating leading theories of why the universe contains more matter than antimatter, and how forces unify at high energy scales. It would also prove that neutrinos and anti-neutrinos are not two distinct particles, but can transform into each other, with their mass described by a unique mechanism conceived by Majorana. The recognition that neutrinos are not massless necessitates an explanation and has boosted interest in neutrinoless double-beta decay. The field stands now at a turning point. A new round of experiments is currently being prepared for the next decade to cover an important region of parameter space. In parallel, advances in nuclear theory are laying the groundwork to connect the nuclear decay with the underlying new physics. Meanwhile, the particle theory landscape continues to find new motivations for neutrinos to be their own antiparticle. This review brings together the experimental, nuclear theory, and particle theory aspects connected to neutrinoless double-beta decay, to explore the path toward - and beyond - its discovery.

## Key claim

The discovery of neutrinoless double-beta decay could soon be within reach, but the paper emphasizes that there is no definitive prediction of its half-life because multiple lepton-number-violating mechanisms could mediate the decay, and the observation would require experiments to extend half-life sensitivity by two orders of magnitude beyond current limits.

## Method

This review synthesizes three interconnected aspects of neutrinoless double-beta decay research: particle theory (including global symmetries, Majorana neutrino mass mechanisms, and effective field theory operators), nuclear theory (including nuclear matrix element calculations using many-body approaches and the impact of short-range operators), and experimental techniques (including detection principles, background sources, and statistical methods for signal extraction). The authors compare recent and future experiments across multiple isotopes and detection techniques, evaluating their projected sensitivities and discovery potential.

## Key numbers

* Current experimental limits on neutrinoless double-beta decay half-lives exceed 10^26 years, meaning a nucleus would take on average more than a million billion times the age of the universe before undergoing the decay
* The first limit on neutrinoless double-beta decay was 3 x 10^15 years, set with ^124Sn in Geiger counters
* The first observation of double-beta decay (the two-neutrino mode) in ^130Te yielded a half-life of 1.4 x 10^21 years
* Next-generation experiments aim to extend half-life sensitivity in multiple nuclei by two orders of magnitude beyond current limits, reaching 10^28 years and beyond
* Goeppert-Mayer predicted that two-neutrino double-beta decay would have half-life values exceeding 10^17 years

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes prior review works on neutrinoless double-beta decay, including those focused on experimental aspects (Avignone 2008, Elliott 2012, Giuliani 2012), nuclear physics (Vergados 2012, Vogel 2012, Engel 2017), and neutrino mass connections (Petcov 2013, Bilenky 2014, Dell'Oro 2016). It brings together particle theory, nuclear theory, and experimental perspectives to address the path toward discovery, building on the historical development from Majorana (1937) and Furry (1939) through modern experiments like GERDA, KamLAND-Zen, CUORE, and EXO-200. The work enables a comprehensive framework for interpreting future observations and connecting decay rates to underlying new physics mechanisms.
