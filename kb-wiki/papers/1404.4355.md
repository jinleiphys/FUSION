# Influence of multiphonon excitations and transfer on the fusion of Ca+Zr

> Fusion data for $^{48}$Ca+$^{90,96}$Zr are analyzed by coupled-channels calculations that are based on the M3Y+repulsion, double-folding potential. By applying a previously determined nuclear density of $^{48}$Ca, the neutron densities of the zirconium isotopes are adjusted to optimize the fit to the fusion data, whereas the proton densities are determined by electron scattering experiments. It is shown that the fusion data can be explained fairly well by including couplings to one- and two-phonon excitations of the reacting nuclei and to one- and two-nucleon transfer reactions but there is also some sensitivity to multiphonon excitations. The neutron skin thicknesses extracted for the two zirconium isotopes are consistent with anti-proton measurements. The densities of the zirconium isotopes are used together with the previously determined nuclear density of $^{40}$Ca to calculate the M3Y+repulsion potentials and predict the fusion cross sections of $^{40}$Ca+$^{90,96}$Zr. The predicted cross sections for $^{40}$Ca+$^{90}$Zr are in reasonable agreement with the data when the influence of multiphonon excitations and a modest transfer is considered. The prediction of the $^{40}$Ca+$^{96}$Zr fusion cross section, on the other hand, is poor and under-predicts the data by 30 to 40%. Although couplings to transfer channels with positive $Q$ values were expected to play an important role, they are not able to explain the data, primarily because the predicted Coulomb barrier is about 1.5 MeV too high. Possible reasons for this failure are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper claims that fusion data for the Ca+Zr systems can be explained fairly well by coupled-channels calculations including one- and two-phonon excitations and one- and two-nucleon transfer, but the prediction for 40Ca+96Zr fails, under-predicting data by 30-40% because the predicted Coulomb barrier is about 1.5 MeV too high, and couplings to transfer channels with positive Q values cannot resolve this discrepancy.

## Method

The authors performed coupled-channels calculations using the M3Y+repulsion double-folding potential, including couplings to one- and two-phonon excitations of low-lying surface modes, mutual excitations, and up to three-phonon excitations, as well as one- and two-nucleon transfer reactions modeled with a monopole form factor. The neutron densities of the zirconium isotopes were adjusted to optimize fits to the 48Ca+90,96Zr fusion data, while proton densities were fixed from electron scattering experiments. The extracted densities were then used to predict fusion cross sections for 40Ca+90,96Zr.

## Key numbers

- For 48Ca+90Zr, the best Ch-45 calculation (including one- and two-proton transfer) achieved a chi-squared per degree of freedom of 0.69 for the M3Y+rep potential, compared to 1.06 for the standard Woods-Saxon potential.
- The adjusted pair-transfer strength for 48Ca+90Zr was sigma_2p = 0.15 fm, producing a pair-transfer cross section of about 80 mb at 110 MeV.
- The effective Q value for one-proton transfer in 48Ca+90Zr was set to -0.92 MeV; the effective Q value for two-proton transfer was adopted as +1 MeV (the optimum fit value, while the calculated value was +2.22 MeV).
- The neutron skin thickness extracted for 90Zr decreased from delta_r_np = 0.14 fm in the Ch-15 calculation to delta_r_np = 0.08 fm in the Ch-45 calculation, the latter agreeing with the anti-proton experiment value of 0.09 +/- 0.025 fm.
- For 40Ca+96Zr, the predicted fusion cross section under-predicts the data by 30 to 40%, and the predicted Coulomb barrier is about 1.5 MeV too high.
- The lowest measured cross section for 40Ca+96Zr was 2.4 +/- 1.7 microbarns.

## Context

This work extends the coupled-channels analysis of fusion reactions for calcium isotopes (Esbensen 2011, Montagnoli 2011) and for 48Ca+96Zr (Esbensen 2012) to the full set of Ca+Zr systems, using the M3Y+repulsion potential that previously resolved discrepancies at very low and very high energies. The results contradict the expectation that couplings to transfer channels with positive Q values would explain the strong subbarrier enhancement in 40Ca+96Zr, as had been suggested by Beckerman (1980) for Ni+Ni and by Jiang (2006) for Ca+Ca systems. The failure for 40Ca+96Zr raises open questions about missing reaction mechanisms or inadequacies in the reaction model, and the extracted neutron skin thicknesses provide a consistency check against anti-proton measurements (Trzcinska 2001).
