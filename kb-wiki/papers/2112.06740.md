# Single-Nucleon Energies Changing with Nucleon Number

> The broad range of accumulated experimental data on the binding energies for single-particle states in nuclei is examined as a function of the constituent number of neutrons and protons and an unexpectedly simple pattern emerges. The dependence of the energies of neutron states on the number of constituent protons, or of proton states on the number of neutrons, are very similar to each other and the sign reflects the well-known strong attraction. For the same kind of nucleons changing as in the state -- energies for neutron states with neutron number changing or proton states with protons -- the dependence is at least a factor of four weaker in magnitude and slightly repulsive, except when the changing nucleons are only within the same orbit as the state. The systematics of the accumulated data are presented with a minimum of use made of model assumptions.

# Single-Nucleon Energies Changing with Nucleon Number

## Key claim

The binding energies of single-particle states in nuclei, when plotted against the number of constituent nucleons on a logarithmic scale, show a simple and universal pattern: the dependence on the opposite kind of nucleon (neutron states vs. proton number, or proton states vs. neutron number) is strongly attractive and similar across all nuclei, while the dependence on the same kind of nucleon is at least a factor of four weaker in magnitude and slightly repulsive, except when the changing nucleons occupy only the same orbit as the state being tracked.

## Method

The authors compiled approximately 200 experimental data points from published transfer reaction measurements spanning the past 60 years, representing effective single-particle energies (ESPEs) for 33 segments across nuclei from A=16 to A=208. They plotted these binding energies as a function of the changing nucleon number on a logarithmic scale, separately for cases where the changing nucleons are of the opposite type (np interaction) and the same type (T=1 interaction). For fragmented single-particle strength, spectroscopic-factor weighted centroid energies were used, and for same-species cases, the Baranger interpolation between particle-adding and particle-removing strengths was applied.

## Key numbers

* Average logarithmic slope for np interaction (changing nucleons of opposite type): -21.3 MeV, with rms variation 3.7 MeV
* Average logarithmic slope for T=1 interaction when only the same orbit is changing occupancy: -4.1 MeV, with rms variation 1.3 MeV
* Average logarithmic slope for T=1 interaction when several orbits are changing occupancy: +4.6 MeV, with rms variation 1.5 MeV
* Separation between the two T=1 subgroups: 9.1 MeV, compared to within-group scatter of 1.4 and 1.7 MeV
* From Woods-Saxon potential analysis: V_np approximately -80 MeV and V_T=1 approximately -20 MeV, implying V_T=0 approximately -140 MeV

## Context

This work extends the early systematic studies of Cohen (1963) and the Bansal and French (1964, 1965) approach, but presents the data in a more model-independent way than previous parameterizations of the nuclear mean field. The observed pattern is consistent with the empirical symmetry term in the semi-empirical mass formula of Weizsacker (1935) and Bethe and Bacher (1936), and the authors show that a Woods-Saxon potential with a symmetry term and radius scaling as A^{1/3} reproduces the qualitative features. The results connect to the monopole moments of two-body matrix elements from Schiffer and True (1976), confirming that the patterns are not artifacts of the Baranger interpolation. This survey provides a systematic reference for interpreting single-particle energies in exotic nuclei near the limits of binding.
