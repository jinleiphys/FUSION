# Layers of Cold Dipolar Molecules in the Harmonic Approximation

> We consider the N-body problem in a layered geometry containing cold polar molecules with dipole moments that are polarized perpendicular to the layers. A harmonic approximation is used to simplify the hamiltonian and bound state properties of the two-body inter-layer dipolar potential are used to adjust this effective interaction. To model the intra-layer repulsion of the polar molecules, we introduce a repulsive inter-molecule potential that can be parametrically varied. Single chains containing one molecule in each layer, as well as multi-chain structures in many layers are discussed and their energies and radii determined. We extract the normal modes of the various systems as measures of their volatility and eventually of instability, and compare our findings to the excitations in crystals. We find modes that can be classified as either chains vibrating in phase or as layers vibrating against each other. The former correspond to acoustic and the latter to optical phonons. Instabilities can occur for large intra-layer repulsion and produce diverging amplitudes of molecules in the outer layers. Lastly, we consider experimentally relevant regimes to observe the structures.

## Key claim

The harmonic approximation predicts that single-molecule-per-layer chains are always bound for any interaction strength, whereas configurations with two molecules per layer (two chains) will likely not be bound under current experimental conditions; however, because experiments include external confinement, the unstable modes of multi-chain complexes should still appear as resonances in the dynamics.

## Method

The authors replace the true dipole-dipole interlayer potential with a shifted harmonic oscillator potential whose strength is adjusted to reproduce the exact two-body binding energy of the dipolar interaction. For intralayer repulsion, they introduce an inverted harmonic oscillator potential whose parameters are set by matching the number of bound states excluded by the repulsion. The resulting N-body Hamiltonian is solved exactly by separating center-of-mass and relative coordinates, yielding analytical expressions for energies, radii, and normal mode frequencies.

## Key numbers

* For a chain of N molecules with only nearest-neighbor interactions, the ratio B_N/[(N-1)B_2] lies between 1 and 1.12 for all dipole strengths studied, with a monotonous decrease as interaction strength increases.
* When all interlayer interactions are included, the ratio B_N/[(N-1)B_2] lies between 1 and 1.6, with large-N asymptotics above 1.202 (the value from a 1/n^3 scaling estimate).
* The nearest-neighbor contribution to the total energy ranges from 70% to 90% depending on chain length and dipole strength.
* For a chain of 30 molecules, the root-mean-square radii of individual molecules range from approximately 0.3d to 0.9d, depending on dipole strength and position in the chain.
* The critical repulsive frequencies for multi-chain instability are in the range md^2ω_r/ℏ ∼ 1–10.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of layered dipolar systems by Wang (2006) and Santos (2010) on single-chain bound states, and by Lu (2008) on classical bilayer crystals. It goes beyond these by using the two-body binding energy to fix the interlayer harmonic interaction (rather than a simple expansion around the potential minimum) and by parametrically including intralayer repulsion, which was neglected in earlier quantum treatments. The results enable predictions of the stability and breakup mechanisms of multi-chain structures, and the normal mode analysis provides a connection to acoustic and optical phonon excitations in crystals. The method is analogous to effective interaction approaches used in dripline nuclear physics (Jensen, 2004) and cold atom physics where scattering length characterizes two-body interactions.
