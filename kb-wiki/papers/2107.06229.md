# Constraining Neutron-Star Matter with Microscopic and Macroscopic Collisions

> Interpreting high-energy, astrophysical phenomena, such as supernova explosions or neutron-star collisions, requires a robust understanding of matter at supranuclear densities. However, our knowledge about dense matter explored in the cores of neutron stars remains limited. Fortunately, dense matter is not only probed in astrophysical observations, but also in terrestrial heavy-ion collision experiments. In this work, we use Bayesian inference to combine data from astrophysical multi-messenger observations of neutron stars and from heavy-ion collisions of gold nuclei at relativistic energies with microscopic nuclear theory calculations to improve our understanding of dense matter. We find that the inclusion of heavy-ion collision data indicates an increase in the pressure in dense matter relative to previous analyses, shifting neutron-star radii towards larger values, consistent with recent NICER observations. Our findings show that constraints from heavy-ion collision experiments show a remarkable consistency with multi-messenger observations and provide complementary information on nuclear matter at intermediate densities. This work combines nuclear theory, nuclear experiment, and astrophysical observations, and shows how joint analyses can shed light on the properties of neutron-rich supranuclear matter over the density range probed in neutron stars.

## Key claim
Including heavy-ion collision data from gold-gold collisions at relativistic energies in a Bayesian multi-messenger analysis increases the inferred pressure in dense matter relative to analyses using only astrophysical observations, shifting neutron-star radii toward larger values that are consistent with recent NICER observations.

## Method
The authors constructed a prior set of 15,000 equations of state constrained by chiral effective field theory calculations up to 1.5 times nuclear saturation density, then extended each EOS to higher densities using a speed-of-sound extrapolation. They applied Bayesian inference to combine constraints from astrophysical multi-messenger observations (gravitational waves from GW170817 and GW190425, NICER X-ray pulse-profile data, kilonova AT2017gfo, and pulsar mass measurements) with experimental data from the FOPI and ASY-EOS heavy-ion collision campaigns at GSI, where gold nuclei were collided at beam energies of 0.4 to 1.5 GeV per nucleon.

## Key numbers
- Pressure at 1.5 times nuclear saturation density from the combined analysis: \(6.25^{+1.90}_{-2.26}\) MeV/fm³ (median with 95% credible interval)
- Radius of a 1.4 solar-mass neutron star from the combined analysis: \(12.01^{+0.78}_{-0.77}\) km (95% credible interval)
- Radius of a 1.4 solar-mass neutron star from astrophysical data alone: \(11.93^{+0.80}_{-0.75}\) km (95% credible interval)
- Bayes factor in favor of a strong first-order phase transition in neutron-star cores: \(0.419 \pm 0.012\), slightly disfavoring its presence

## Context
This work extends the Bayesian multi-messenger astrophysics framework of Dietrich (2020) by incorporating heavy-ion collision data from the FOPI and ASY-EOS experiments, which probe the nuclear equation of state in the density range of 1-2 times nuclear saturation density. The results show consistency between terrestrial heavy-ion collision constraints and astrophysical multi-messenger observations, and demonstrate that joint analyses can reduce uncertainties on neutron-star properties. The findings enable future combined studies with upcoming experiments at the Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research, the Rare Isotope Beam Facility at RIKEN, and the Nuclotron-based Ion Collider Facility.
