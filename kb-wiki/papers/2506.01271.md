# Transport Theory and Correlation Measurements: Coming to Terms on Emission Sources

> Two-particle correlations play a pivotal role in understanding the space-time characteristics of particle emission in Heavy-ion collisions. These characteristics are typically represented by a relative emission source and can be obtained using transport model simulations such as the Boltzmann- Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport model. In this paper, we utilize the BUU transport model to simulate the p-p source. Subsequently, we integrate this source and the p-p kernel within the KP formula to calculate the correlations. By comparing the correlations obtained from the BUU simulation with those obtained using imaging methods, such as the deblurring method, we aim to gain a deeper understanding of the impact of fast and slow emissions on the measured correlations. Specifically, this comparison is used as a tool to determine a function (tail) that represents the relative distribution of the particle pair from secondary decay emissions. Thus, we correct the BUU source function by incorporating a tail to account for the contribution of secondary decay emissions, which cannot be accurately captured by BUU simulations. Resulting source function reproduces the features in the measured correlations. To illustrate our approach, we examine p-p correlations measured in Ar + Sc reactions at E/A = 80 MeV, considering both momentum-independent and momentum-dependent nuclear equations of state (EOS).

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport model alone cannot reproduce the full measured proton-proton correlation function because it misses secondary decay emissions; by adding an exponential tail to the BUU source function to account for these long-lived emissions, the corrected source reproduces the experimental correlation features, but only for the specific case of Ar + Sc reactions at E/A = 80 MeV.

## Method

The authors used the pBUU transport model to simulate the two-proton source function for central collisions of 36Ar + 45Sc at 80 MeV per nucleon, then folded this source with the proton-proton kernel in the Koonin-Pratt (KP) formula to calculate correlation functions. They compared these BUU-derived correlations with experimental data and with source functions extracted from the data using the Richardson-Lucy deblurring imaging method, which inverts the KP equation without assuming a Gaussian source shape. The discrepancy between BUU and data was corrected by adding an exponential decay parameterization (a "tail") to the BUU source to represent secondary decay emissions, with the tail parameters determined by matching the theoretical source to the deblurred experimental source.

## Key numbers

- Reaction system: 36Ar + 45Sc at E/A = 80 MeV, impact parameter b = 1.9 fm, total pair momentum cut 200-400 MeV/c
- Incompressibility values used: K = 214 MeV for softer equation of state (EoS), K = 280 MeV for stiffer EoS
- Effective mass when momentum-dependent potential is on: m* = 0.7 m
- Saturation density: rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3
- Symmetry energy density dependence exponent: gamma = 0.5 for soft symmetry energy, gamma = 1.6 for stiff symmetry energy
- Source function discretized from r = 0.5 to 22 fm into M = 11 bins with bin size Delta r = 2 fm; correlation function has N = 16 data points
- BUU source functions lack significant contributions past approximately 10 fm regardless of EoS
- The attractive nuclear interaction resonance appears at q = 20 MeV/c in the correlation function
- Coulomb anticorrelation observed at q < 10 MeV/c

## Context

This work extends the comparison between BUU transport model sources and experimentally imaged sources first reported by Verde et al. (2002), who found that two-proton correlations are sensitive to the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross-section. The paper introduces the deblurring method from Nzabahimana et al. (2023) and Danielewicz (2022) as a more flexible imaging approach that does not assume a Gaussian source shape, unlike earlier Gaussian parameterizations used by Goldhaber (1960), Pratt (1984), and Boal (1990). The results enable future work to use the corrected source function to constrain the nuclear equation of state and transport properties, particularly the density dependence of the symmetry energy, which previous work by the Isospin collaboration showed affects nucleon emission times and source sizes.
