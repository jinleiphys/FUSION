# Suppression of Superfluidity upon Overflow of Trapped Fermions. Quantal and Thomas-Fermi Studies

> Two issues are treated in this work: (i) the generic fact that if a fermionic superfluid in the BCS regime overflows from a narrow container into a much wider one, pairing is much suppressed at the overflow point. Physical examples where this feature may play an important role are discussed (ii) A Thomas-Fermi (TF) approach to inhomogeneous superfluid Fermi-systems is presented and shown that it works well in cases where the Local Density Approximation (LDA) breaks down.

# Suppression of Superfluidity upon Overflow of Trapped Fermions. Quantal and Thomas-Fermi Studies

## Key claim

The pairing gap in a fermionic superfluid in the BCS regime drops sharply to near zero when the Fermi level reaches the edge of a narrow confining pocket and the fluid overflows into a much wider container, before slowly rising again as density fills the outer container; this suppression is a generic feature that occurs even though the abstract presents it as a "generic fact" without emphasizing that the effect depends on the specific geometry (slab vs. spherical) and on the ratio of container sizes.

## Method

The authors solve the BCS gap equation with a contact force for a slab geometry (a narrow potential pocket of depth -40 MeV and half-width R = 10 fm embedded in a wide container of half-width L = 100 fm) and for a spherical double harmonic oscillator potential modeling cold atom traps. They develop a Thomas-Fermi (TF) approach to inhomogeneous superfluidity by taking the Wigner transform of the density matrix in the semiclassical limit, replacing discrete quantum numbers with continuous energy variables, and compare these TF results to both quantal BCS solutions and Local Density Approximation (LDA) calculations. For Wigner-Seitz cells in neutron star crusts, they use the BCP energy density functional with Gogny D1S pairing force in a self-consistent mean-field calculation.

## Key numbers

- Slab geometry: pocket depth V0 = -40 MeV, pocket half-width R = 10 fm, outer container half-width L = 100 fm, cutoff Lambda = 50 MeV, coupling strength g = 150 MeV fm^3
- For the spherical double harmonic oscillator trap: zero-range force strength g = -1.0, cutoff Lambda = 164.34 in optical trap units with omega_opt = 2 pi x 1000 Hz, accommodating 10^5 atoms in each spin state when the trap is completely filled (mu = U)
- In the slab geometry, LDA results can be wrong by as much as 50 percent compared to quantal and TF results
- For the Wigner-Seitz cell containing a single 500/40 Zr nucleus, the TF and LDA gaps show a "huge difference" in the surface region of the nucleus

## Context

This work extends the study of superfluid fermions in finite systems to the specific case of overflow from a narrow container into a wider one, a situation relevant to cold fermionic atoms in traps (as proposed by Vivirito et al. (2001) and Ketterle et al. (1998) for bosonic atoms) and to neutron-rich nuclei near the drip line (Hamamoto et al., 2005). The authors show that their Thomas-Fermi approach works where LDA fails because the Cooper pair coherence length can exceed the length scale over which the mean field varies, a condition that invalidates LDA but not TF. The results for Wigner-Seitz cells in neutron star crusts agree with quantal Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations by Grasso et al. (2008) and with the earlier quantal work of Negele and Vautherin (1973). The TF approach enables treatment of inhomogeneous superfluid Fermi systems with large particle numbers (millions of cold atoms, neutron star crusts) that are inaccessible to quantal BCS or HFB solutions.
