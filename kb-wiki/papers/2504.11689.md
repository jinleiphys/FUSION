# Advancing quantum simulations of nuclear shell model with noise-resilient protocols

> Some of the computational limitations in solving the nuclear many-body problem could be overcome by utilizing quantum computers. The nuclear shell-model calculations providing deeper insights into the properties of atomic nuclei, is one such case with high demand for resources as the size of the Hilbert space grows exponentially with the number of particles involved. Quantum algorithms are being developed to overcome these challenges and advance such calculations. To develop quantum circuits for the nuclear shell-model, leveraging the capabilities of noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices. We aim to minimize resource requirements (specifically in terms of qubits and gates) and strive to reduce the impact of noise by employing relevant mitigation techniques. We achieve noise resilience by designing an optimized ansatz for the variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) based on Givens rotations and incorporating qubit-ADAPT-VQE in combination with variational quantum deflation (VQD) to compute ground and excited states incorporating the zero-noise extrapolation mitigation technique. Furthermore, the qubit requirements are significantly reduced by mapping the basis states to qubits using Gray code encoding and generalizing transformations of fermionic operators to efficiently represent manybody states. By employing the noise-resilient protocols, we achieve the ground and excited state energy levels of 38Ar and 6Li with better accuracy. These energy levels are presented for noiseless simulations, noisy conditions, and after applying noise mitigation techniques. Results are compared for Jordan Wigner and Gray code encoding using VQE, qubit-ADAPT-VQE, and VQD. Our work highlights the potential of noise-resilient protocols to leverage the full potential of NISQ devices in scaling the nuclear shell model calculations.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that combining Gray code encoding with a qubit-ADAPT-VQE algorithm and zero-noise extrapolation can achieve accurate ground and excited state energies for nuclear shell-model calculations on noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices, but only for the specific light nuclei 38Ar and 6Li under noiseless, noisy, and noise-mitigated conditions.

## Method

The authors constructed nuclear shell-model Hamiltonians for 38Ar (modeled as a 28Si core with two holes in the sd-shell using USDB interaction) and 6Li (modeled as a 4He core with one valence neutron and proton). They implemented variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) with a particle-conserving ansatz based on Givens rotations, qubit-ADAPT-VQE with a minimal operator pool, and variational quantum deflation (VQD) for excited states, using both Jordan-Wigner and Gray code encoding schemes. Noise resilience was achieved through zero-noise extrapolation mitigation, and the Gray code encoding reduced qubit requirements to ceil(log2(N_b)) where N_b is the number of basis states.

## Key numbers

* For 38Ar: the sd-shell model space required N = 6 qubits for six proton orbitals in the m-scheme mapping (Table 1 in the paper).
* For the qubit-ADAPT-VQE gradient threshold: set to 10^-5 relative to the ground state energy.
* The double excitation circuit was built with 6 single-qubit and 11 CNOT gates, compared to 14 single-qubit and 14 CNOT gates in prior work (Arrazola 2022).
* The Gray code encoding requires ceil(log2(Q)) qubits where Q is the number of particle-conserving states contributing to the Hamiltonian, compared to N qubits for one-hot encoding.

## Context

This work extends prior quantum computing approaches to nuclear shell-model calculations, specifically building on the Gray code encoding introduced by Siwach (2021) and Dimatteo (2021) for one-body fermionic systems, and generalizing it to many-body fermionic systems with proper antisymmetrization. The optimized ansatz with reduced CNOT count improves upon the Givens rotation circuits of Arrazola (2022). The results enable scaling nuclear shell-model calculations on NISQ devices by demonstrating noise-resilient protocols that reduce qubit and gate requirements, though the method is currently demonstrated only for light nuclei with small valence spaces.
