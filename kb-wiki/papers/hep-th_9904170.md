# A second order differential equation for the relativistic description of electrons and photons

> A new relativistic description of quantum electrodynamics is presented. Guideline of the theory is the Klein-Gordon equation, which is reformulated to consider spin effects. This is achieved by a representation of relativistic vectors with a space-time algebra made up of Pauli matrices and hyperbolic numbers. The algebra is used to construct the differential operator of the electron as well as the photon wave equation. The properties of free electron and photon states related to this wave equation are investigated. Interactions are introduced as usual with the minimal substitution of the momentum operators. It can be shown that the new wave equation is equivalent to the quadratic form of the Dirac equation. Furthermore, the Maxwell equations can be derived from the corresponding wave equation for photons.

## Key claim

The paper presents a reformulation of quantum electrodynamics based on a second-order differential equation derived from the Klein-Gordon equation, claiming equivalence to the quadratic form of the Dirac equation and the ability to derive Maxwell equations from the same operator, but this equivalence is conditional on the specific algebraic representation using Pauli matrices and hyperbolic numbers rather than the standard Dirac algebra.

## Method

The author constructs a space-time algebra using Pauli matrices multiplied by the hyperbolic unit \(j\) (where \(j^2=1\)) as basis vectors for relativistic vectors, then defines a differential operator \(P\bar{P}\) where \(P=i\nabla\) and \(\bar{P}\) is the dual operator. This operator is applied to spinor wave functions of the form \(\psi(x)=\varphi(x)+j\chi(x)\) to form the quantum wave equation \(P\bar{P}\psi(x)=m^2\psi(x)\) for electrons, and a similar construction is used for photons. Interactions are introduced via minimal substitution of momentum operators.

## Key numbers

- The metric tensor is diagonal with elements \(g_{\mu\nu}=\text{diag}(1,-1,-1,-1)\)
- The spinor for a particle with momentum \(\mathbf{p}\) and polarization \(\lambda\) is \(u(\mathbf{p},\lambda)=\sqrt{\frac{p^0+m}{2m}}\left(1+\frac{j\mathbf{p}}{p^0+m}\right)\chi_{\lambda}\)
- Particle spinors normalize to \(+\delta_{\lambda\lambda^\prime}\) and antiparticle spinors to \(-\delta_{\lambda\lambda^\prime}\)
- The momentum delta function is \(\delta(P-P^\prime)=(2\pi)^3\,2p^0\,\delta^3(\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{p}^{\prime})\) with integration measure \(dP = d^3\mathbf{p}/((2\pi)^3\,2p^0)\)
- The eight complex coordinates of the multivector \(Z=X+ijY\) are the maximum number that can be placed in a \(2\times 2\) matrix, halving the sixteen dimensions of the Dirac \(4\times 4\) matrices

## Context

This work extends the geometric algebra approach of Hestenes (1966) by introducing a modified quaternion formalism using hyperbolic numbers, which have been rarely used in physical applications despite a long history (Dur 1935, Cap 1941). The paper aims to unify the description of electrons and photons under a single second-order differential operator, enabling a reformulation of quantum electrodynamics that the author claims can produce the same physical results as conventional Dirac-based QED while using a smaller (2x2 matrix) algebraic structure.
