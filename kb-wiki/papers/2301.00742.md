# Multicomponent Van der Waals model of a nuclear fireball in the freeze-out stage

> A two-component van der Waals gas model is proposed to describe the hadronic stages of the evolution of a nuclear fireball in the cooling stage. At the first stage of hadronization, when mesons dominate, a two-component meson model ($π^0$ and $π^+$ -mesons) with an effective two-particle interaction potential of a rectangular well is proposed. At the late-stage hadronization, when almost all mesons have decayed, a two-component nucleon model of protons and neutrons is proposed with the corresponding effective rectangular nucleon potential. The saddle point method has been applied for analytical calculations of the partition function. This made it possible to uniformly obtain analytical expressions for both the pressure and density, taking into account the finite dimensions of the system, and the analytical expressions for chemical potentials. It is assumed that the proposed models and derived formulas can be used to analyze experimental data connected to the quantitative characteristics of the particle yields of different types in the final state from the hadronic stages of the evolution of a nuclear fireball, as well as to determine the critical parameters of the system in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a two-component van der Waals gas model for describing the hadronic stages of a nuclear fireball during freeze-out, but the model's applicability is explicitly hedged by the assumption that the number of particles in the fireball (approximately 3-5 thousand at early stages) is large enough to justify the thermodynamic limit, while at later stages when nucleon numbers drop to about 200, the approximation becomes "somewhat dubious."

## Method

The authors apply the saddle point method to analytically compute the grand canonical partition function for a two-component van der Waals gas, first for a meson system (π⁰ and π⁺ mesons) at temperatures above 135 MeV, then for a nucleon system (protons and neutrons) at temperatures below 135 MeV. The effective two-particle interaction is modeled as a rectangular well potential with hard-core repulsion and finite-range attraction, with parameters a_ij (attraction) and b_ij (repulsion) derived from effective radii R_i⁰ (repulsion) and R_i (attraction). The formalism yields analytical expressions for pressure, density, and chemical potentials that include finite-size corrections from the fireball volume.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature threshold for hadronization/chemical freeze-out: T > 135 MeV (in units where k_B = 1)
- Hagedorn temperature for hadron "melting": T > 150 MeV
- Nuclear fireball dimensions: <r> ~ 7-10 fm
- Meson fireball volume range: <V_f> ~ 600-1000 fm³, with a representative value of <V_f> ~ 600 fm³ used in calculations
- Temperature used in meson model calculations: T = 147 MeV
- Hard-core radius of π⁺-meson: R⁰_+ = 0.46 fm
- Hard-core radius of π⁰-meson: R⁰_0 = 0.01 fm
- Density ratio parameter for mesons: k = 0.5 (n_0 = k n_+)
- Effective potential well depth for meson interactions: u₀^(+,0) ~ 80-100 MeV
- Number of nucleons in fireball at late stages: N_pn ~ 200-300
- Number of mesons at early stages: N_π,ρ,ω ~ 3000-5000
- Fireball existence duration: t > 10⁻²² s, compared to relaxation time τ ~ 10⁻²¹-10⁻²² s

## Context

This work extends the van der Waals hadron gas models previously developed by Gorenstein and collaborators, which introduced hard-core radii parameters R_ii and R_ij to describe excluded volume effects in particle yields. The author's earlier work (Krivenko-Emetov, 2021) had already applied a two-component nucleon model to the kinetic freeze-out stage. The present paper adds a two-component meson model for the chemical freeze-out stage (T > 135 MeV) and incorporates finite-size corrections to pressure and density that become significant at low densities in the final freeze-out stages. The proposed models are intended for analyzing experimental data from LHC, RHIC, AGS, and SPS on particle yields in relativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions, and for determining critical parameters of the system.
