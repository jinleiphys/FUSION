# Theories of Relativistic Dissipative Fluid Dynamics

> Relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics finds widespread applications in high-energy nuclear physics and astrophysics. However, formulating a causal and stable theory of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics is far from trivial; efforts to accomplish this reach back more than 50 years. In this review, we give an overview of the field and attempt a comparative assessment of (at least most of) the theories for relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics proposed until today and used in applications.

## Key claim

This review provides a comparative assessment of (at least most of) the theories for relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics proposed until today, but the authors acknowledge that the presentation is not entirely balanced, with some formulations receiving more attention due to the particular expertise of the authors, and that several relevant topics (such as quantum anomalies and hydrodynamic attractors) are not discussed.

## Method

The authors survey and compare the major formulations of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics, organizing them by their foundational approach: space-time gradient expansions (Navier-Stokes, Burnett, BDNK, BRSSS), derivations from the second law of thermodynamics (first-order Navier-Stokes, second-order Israel-Stewart, divergence-type theories), and derivations from kinetic theory via the Boltzmann equation (Chapman-Enskog, 14-moments approximation, DNMR theory, IReD theory). For each formulation, they present a brief derivation of the equations of motion for the dissipative currents and discuss how they differ from other formulations, using a general classification scheme introduced in the preliminaries.

## Key numbers

* The conservation laws constitute five equations for 17 independent variables: the Lorentz scalars n, epsilon, p; the Lorentz 4-vectors u^mu, n^mu, and h^mu (each having three independent variables); and the rank-2 Lorentz tensor pi^{mu nu} (five independent variables).
* For an ideal fluid, the twelve constraints that the dissipative currents vanish reduce the number of independent variables from 17 to five: epsilon_0, n_0, and the three independent components of u^mu.
* The Knudsen number is defined as Kn = lambda/L, where lambda is the microscopic scale and L is the macroscopic scale; fluid dynamics is applicable when Kn << 1.

## Context

This review extends and systematizes the work on relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics that began with the recognition that the naive relativistic generalization of Navier-Stokes theory (Eckart 1940, Landau) is acausal (Pichon, Hiscock and Lindblom 1985, 1987) and unstable. It builds upon the foundational second-order theory of Israel and Stewart (1979) and the subsequent development of causal and stable formulations motivated by heavy-ion collision phenomenology (reviewed by Shen 2020, Heinz 2013, Gale 2013) and astrophysical applications (reviewed by Rezzolla). The review enables practitioners to compare different formulations (such as DNMR theory from Denicol 2012, IReD theory, BDNK theory, and BRSSS theory) and select appropriate approaches for numerical simulations in nuclear physics and astrophysics.
