# All orders proton breakup from exotic nuclei

> We present a semiclassical method to treat the proton breakup from a weakly bound state in an exotic nucleus. The Coulomb interactions between the proton, core and target are treated to all orders and including the full multipole expansion of the Coulomb potential. The nuclear proton-target interaction is also treated to all orders. The core-target interaction is included as an absorption. The method is semi-analytical thus allowing for a detailed understanding of the short range and long range effects of the interactions in the reaction dynamics. It explains also the origin of possible asymmetries in the core parallel momentum distributions when the full multipole expansion of the Coulomb potential is used. Calculations are compared to results of other, fully numerical, methods and to experimental data in order to establish the accuracy and reliability of the method.

## Key claim

The paper presents a semiclassical, semi-analytical method for treating proton breakup from weakly bound exotic nuclei that includes Coulomb interactions to all orders and all multipoles, but the method is a high-energy approximation and may require corrections (such as barrier penetration factors) when applied at lower beam energies, as demonstrated by discrepancies of about an order of magnitude for $^{17}$F at 65 A.MeV.

## Method

The authors extend a previous all-orders semiclassical formalism for neutron breakup (Bonaccorso and Brink, 2004) to proton breakup by writing the Coulomb phase as a sum of two terms: one describing core recoil and the other the direct proton-target Coulomb interaction. The differential cross section is computed from a probability amplitude that sums recoil, direct Coulomb, and nuclear contributions, with the core-target interaction treated as an absorption via an eikonal S-matrix, and the nuclear proton-target interaction included to all orders via an eikonal phase. Calculations for $^8$B and $^{17}$F use Woods-Saxon potentials for bound states, Glauber optical-limit S-matrices for nuclear breakup, and include both diffractive and stripping contributions.

## Key numbers

- For $^8$B breakup on Pb at 936 A.MeV, the calculated inclusive cross section is 652.4 mb, consistent with the experimental result of $662 \pm 60$ mb.
- For $^8$B breakup on Pb at 936 A.MeV, the exclusive cross section for the excited state is 57.8 mb, in agreement with the experimental measurement of $56 \pm 5$ mb.
- For $^8$B breakup on C at 936 A.MeV, the calculated inclusive cross section is 92.6 mb, consistent with the experimental result of $94 \pm 9$ mb.
- The theoretical exclusive/inclusive ratio for the Pb target is $R_{theo}=0.089$, compared to the experimental $R_{exp}=0.085 \pm 0.021$.
- For $^8$B on Pb at 936 A.MeV, the single-particle cross section from Coulomb plus diffractive nuclear breakup is 558 mb for the ground state and 442 mb for the excited state.
- The barrier penetration factor $w_1$ for $^8$B at a proton-core energy of 1.5 MeV is 0.81, indicating a 10-20% possible error in cross sections.
- The barrier penetration factor $w_2$ for $^{17}$F at a proton-core energy of 1.5 MeV is 0.39, indicating a 40-60% possible error in cross sections.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical all-orders method for neutron breakup developed by Bonaccorso and Brink (2004) to the proton breakup case, where the long-range Coulomb interaction adds significant complexity. The method is tested against experimental data for $^8$B from Lola et al. (2003) and compared to fully numerical approaches such as the continuum discretised coupled channel method (CDCC), with the authors noting that relativistic corrections in CDCC can increase proton breakup cross sections by as much as 15% at 250 A.MeV. The formalism analytically demonstrates that asymmetries in core parallel momentum distributions arise from higher multipole terms in the Coulomb potential, in agreement with earlier numerical work by Davids et al. (1998, 2001, 2003) and Esbensen et al. (1996, 2000, 1995), and it enables future studies of proton halo nuclei such as $^{17}$F, which is relevant for astrophysical radiative capture reactions and explosive nucleosynthesis.
