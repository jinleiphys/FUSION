# Origin of the Relaxation Time in Dissipative Fluid Dynamics

> We show how the linearized equations of motion of any dissipative current are determined by the analytical structure of the associated retarded Green's function. If the singularity of the Green's function, which is nearest to the origin in the complex-frequency plane, is a simple pole on the imaginary frequency axis, the linearized equations of motion can be reduced to relaxation-type equations for the dissipative currents. The value of the relaxation time is given by the inverse of this pole. We prove that, if the relaxation time is sent to zero, or equivalently, the pole to infinity, the dissipative currents approach the values given by the standard gradient expansion.

## Key claim

The linearized equation of motion for any dissipative current reduces to a relaxation-type equation if and only if the singularity of the associated retarded Green's function nearest to the origin in the complex-frequency plane is a simple pole on the imaginary frequency axis; the relaxation time equals the inverse of this pole, and the gradient expansion is recovered only in the limit where the relaxation time is sent to zero (the pole to infinity).

## Method

The authors start from the general linear relation between a dissipative current J and a thermodynamic force F via a retarded Green's function in Fourier space. They show that the gradient expansion corresponds to a Taylor expansion of the Green's function around zero frequency, which requires all singularities to be pushed to infinity. When the Green's function has a simple pole on the imaginary axis, they perform a Laurent expansion around that pole and inverse-Fourier-transform to obtain a first-order differential equation for J, identifying the relaxation time as the inverse pole position. They extend the analysis to two poles, deriving a second-order equation, and discuss the conditions under which a single relaxation time can be defined.

## Key numbers

- The relaxation time is given by τ_R = 1/ζ, where ω_0 = -iζ is the simple pole on the imaginary frequency axis and ζ is positive and real.
- For the case of shear viscosity only, the ratio of shear relaxation time τ_π to shear viscosity η must obey τ_π / [η/(ε+P)] ≥ 4/[3(1-c_s²)], where ε is energy density, P is pressure, and c_s is the speed of sound.
- The gradient expansion coefficients are related to derivatives of the Green's function at ω=0: D̄_0 = G̃_R(0), D̄_1 = i ∂_ω G̃_R(ω)|_{ω=0}, D̄_2 = -(1/2) ∂_ω² G̃_R(ω)|_{ω=0}.
- For a single-pole Green's function, the coefficients in the relaxation equation are D_0 = G̃_R(0) and D_1 = i ∂_ω G̃_R(ω)|_{ω=0} + D_0 τ_R.

## Context

This work provides a general, model-independent derivation of relaxation-type equations for dissipative currents in relativistic fluid dynamics, extending the Israel-Stewart theory (IS) (Israel and Stewart, 1979) which was based on Grad's moment method (Grad, 1949). The authors show that the relaxation time is determined by the slowest microscopic time scale (the pole nearest the origin), not by the fastest fluid-dynamical scale, contradicting attempts to derive it from the long-wavelength limit of the Green's function. The result enables a systematic understanding of causality and stability constraints, such as those derived earlier by the same authors (Denicol, Noronha, Niemi, Rischke, 2011) for bulk and shear viscosity, and connects to the oscillatory behavior of dissipative currents noted by Kovtun (2005).
