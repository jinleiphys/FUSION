# Beyond the Unified Model

> The key elements of the Unified Model are reviewed and checked against modern experimental data. For medium-mass or heavy nuclei it is found that separation between collective and intrinsic degrees freedom becomes invalid for after exciting five to ten collective quanta along the yrast line and two quanta above it. The microscopic derivation of the Bohr Hamiltonian by means of adiabatic time-dependent mean field theory is presented and results compared with experiment. The description of the strong coupling between the rotational and intrinsic degrees of freedom in framework of the rotating mean field is described from a conceptual point of view. The classification of rotational bands as configurations of rotating quasiparticles is introduced. Using the concept of spontaneous symmetry breaking the microscopic underpinning of the rotational degrees is refined. Resulting phenomena, as tilted-axis rotation, transverse wobbling, chirality, magnetic rotation, and band termination are discussed.

# Beyond the Unified Model

## Key claim

The adiabatic separation between collective and intrinsic degrees of freedom, which is the foundation of the Unified Model, becomes invalid for medium-mass and heavy nuclei after exciting five to ten collective quanta along the yrast line and two quanta above it, as determined by checking the model against modern experimental data.

## Method

The paper reviews the key elements of the Unified Model and presents the microscopic derivation of the Bohr Hamiltonian using adiabatic time-dependent mean field theory. It then introduces the rotating mean field approach to describe strong coupling between rotational and intrinsic degrees of freedom, classifying rotational bands as configurations of rotating quasiparticles. The concept of spontaneous symmetry breaking is used to refine the microscopic underpinning of rotational degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of intrinsic to rotational frequencies for normal deformed nuclei is approximately 60/R at best, where R is the angular momentum in units of hbar, based on an average single-particle level splitting of about 0.3 x 7.4 MeV and a moment of inertia of about 30/MeV
- The nuclear radius parameter is R0 = 1.2 A^(1/3) fm
- The pairing gap Delta is approximately given by the even-odd mass difference Delta_eo (three-point even-odd mass difference)
- The energy scale for the modified oscillator potential is hbar omega_0 = 41 A^(-1/3) MeV = 7.5 MeV
- For well-deformed nuclei at low spin, rotational transition energies are about a factor of 10 lower than intrinsic excitation energies; for vibrational excitations the ratio is only about 3 at best

## Context

This paper extends the work of Bohr and Mottelson (1975) on the Unified Model as presented in their monograph "Nuclear Structure Vol. II: Nuclear Deformations," and reviews developments since 1970 that were stimulated by new experimental data from heavy ion accelerators with gamma-detector arrays. The author draws on his own previous review (Frauendorf, 2000) of the cranking model's development, and the paper serves as an introduction to rotating nuclei at the graduate student level, discussing phenomena beyond the Unified Model such as tilted-axis rotation, transverse wobbling, chirality, magnetic rotation, and band termination.
