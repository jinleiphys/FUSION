# Non-degenerate shell-model effective interactions from the Okamoto-Suzuki and Krenciglowa-Kuo iteration methods

> We present calculations of shell-model effective interactions for both degenerate and non-degenerate model spaces using the Krenciglowa-Kuo (KK) and the extended Krenciglowa-Kuo iteration method recently developed by Okamoto, Suzuki {\it et al.} (EKKO). The starting point is the low-momentum nucleon-nucleon interaction $V_{low-k}$ obtained from the N$^3$LO chiral two-nucleon interaction. The model spaces spanned by the $sd$ and $sdpf$ shells are both considered. With a solvable model, we show that both the KK and EKKO methods are convenient for deriving the effective interactions for non-degenerate model spaces. The EKKO method is especially desirable in this situation since the vertex function $\hat Z$-box employed therein is well behaved while the corresponding vertex function $\hat Q$-box employed in the Lee-Suzuki (LS) and KK methods may have singularities. The converged shell-model effective interactions given by the EKKO and KK methods are equivalent, although the former method is considerably more efficient. The degenerate $sd$-shell effective interactions given by the LS method are practically identical to those from the EKKO and KK methods. Results of the $sd$ one-shell and $sdpf$ two-shell calculations for $^{18}$O, $^{18}$F, $^{19}$O and $^{19}$F using the EKKO effective interactions are compared, and the importance of the shell-model three-nucleon forces is discussed.

## Key claim

The extended Krenciglowa-Kuo (EKKO) iteration method is especially desirable for deriving shell-model effective interactions in non-degenerate model spaces because its vertex function $\hat Z$-box remains well-behaved and continuous, while the $\hat Q$-box employed in the Lee-Suzuki (LS) and Krenciglowa-Kuo (KK) methods may have singularities that cause convergence difficulties.

## Method

The authors start from the low-momentum nucleon-nucleon interaction $V_{low-k}$ derived from the N$^3$LO chiral two-nucleon potential at a decimation scale of $\Lambda =$ 2.1 fm$^{-1}$. They compute the $\hat Q$-box diagrams (first- and second-order) for the $sd$ one-shell and $sdpf$ two-shell model spaces, using hole orbits summed over the $0s0p$ shells and particle orbits over the $0d1s1p0f$ shells. The effective interactions are then obtained via iterative solution of the KK and EKKO methods, with the $\hat Z$-box defined from the $\hat Q$-box and its first derivative to avoid singularities.

## Key numbers

- Decimation scale for $V_{low-k}$: $\Lambda =$ 2.1 fm$^{-1}$
- Harmonic oscillator constant: $\hbar \omega =$ 14 MeV
- Experimental single-particle energies for $sd$ shell orbits: (0.0, 5.08, 0.87) MeV for ($0d_{5/2}, 0d_{3/2}, 1s_{1/2}$) respectively
- For the $sdpf$ two-shell calculations, the four $pf$ orbits are placed at a separation of one $\hbar \omega$ (14 MeV) above the $d_{5/2}$ level
- In the solvable model with $x=0.60$ and non-degenerate $PH_0P=(0,6)$, the KK and EKKO methods both converge to eigenvalues $E_n = -3.51$ and 14.53 with $P$-space probabilities of 0.70 and 0.87 respectively
- In the solvable model with $x=0.30$ and degenerate $PH_0P=(0,0)$, the EKKO method converges to eigenvalues -1.45 and 0.91 with $P$-space probabilities 0.87 and 0.46, while the KK method converges to -1.13 and 5.52

## Context

This work extends the Krenciglowa-Kuo (KK) iteration method (Krenciglowa and Kuo, 1975) and the recently developed extended KK method of Okamoto, Suzuki, Kumagai and Fujii (2010) to shell-model effective interaction calculations for non-degenerate model spaces. It contrasts these approaches with the commonly used Lee-Suzuki (LS) iteration scheme (Lee and Suzuki, 1980; Suzuki and Lee, 1980), which is less convenient for non-degenerate model spaces and may require high-order iterations for accuracy. The results enable future microscopic calculations of shell-model effective interactions for larger non-degenerate model spaces needed to describe exotic nuclei with large neutron excess, and the authors note that the importance of three-nucleon forces will be addressed in a separate publication.
