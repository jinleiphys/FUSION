# Relativistic Mean-Field Hadronic Models under Nuclear Matter Constraints

> Relativistic mean-field (RMF) models have been widely used in the study of many hadronic frameworks because of several important aspects not always present in nonrelativistic models, such as intrinsic Lorentz covariance, automatic inclusion of spin, appropriate saturation mechanism for nuclear matter, causality and, therefore, no problems related to superluminal speed of sound. With the aim of identifying the models which best satisfy well known properties of nuclear matter, we have analyzed $263$ parameterizations of seven different types of RMF models under three different sets of constraints related to symmetric nuclear matter, pure neutron matter, symmetry energy, and its derivatives. One of these (SET1) is formed of the same constraints used in a recent work [M. Dutra et al., Phys. Rev. C 85, 035201 (2012)] in which we analyzed $240$ Skyrme parameterizations. The results pointed to $2$ models consistent with all constraints. By using another set of constraints, namely, SET2a, formed by the updated versions of the previous one, we found $4$ models approved simultaneously. Finally, in the third set, named SET2b, in which the values of the constraints are more restrictive, we found $3$ consistent models. Another interesting feature of our analysis is that the results change dramatically if we do not consider the constraint regarding the volume part of the isospin incompressibility ($K_{τ,\rm v}$). In this case, we have $35$ approved models in SET2a and $30$ in SET2b.

## Key claim

Out of 263 relativistic mean-field (RMF) parameterizations tested against three sets of nuclear matter constraints, only 2 to 4 models simultaneously satisfy all constraints in each set, but this number jumps to 30–35 if the constraint on the volume part of the isospin incompressibility ($K_{\tau,\rm v}$) is omitted, revealing that this single constraint is the most restrictive.

## Method

The authors computed the equation of state and derived nuclear-matter properties (incompressibility, symmetry energy, its slope and curvature, skewness, and the volume part of the isospin incompressibility) for 263 RMF parameterizations belonging to seven model types (linear Walecka, nonlinear Walecka with $\sigma$ self-interactions, with $\sigma$ and $\omega$ self-interactions, with cross terms, density-dependent couplings, point-coupling, and $\delta$-meson models). They then compared each parameterization against three sets of constraints: SET1 (identical to the 11 constraints used in a prior Skyrme study, Dutra et al. (2012)), SET2a (updated constraints with broader ranges), and SET2b (more restrictive updated constraints), all applied at zero temperature for nucleonic matter up to about three times saturation density.

## Key numbers

*   **263** RMF parameterizations were analyzed, belonging to seven different model types.
*   Under SET1, only **2** models satisfied all constraints simultaneously.
*   Under SET2a (updated, less restrictive), **4** models were approved.
*   Under SET2b (updated, more restrictive), **3** models were approved.
*   When the constraint on the volume part of the isospin incompressibility ($K_{\tau,\rm v}$) was removed, the number of approved models rose to **35** for SET2a and **30** for SET2b.
*   The incompressibility constraint used was $K_0 = 230 \pm 40$ MeV, following Khan et al.
*   The constraint on the volume part of the isospin incompressibility was $K_\tau = -550 \pm 150$ MeV, chosen to encompass values from isospin diffusion, neutron skin thickness, and GMR measurements.

## Context

This work extends the systematic constraint-based assessment previously applied to 240 non-relativistic Skyrme parameterizations by Dutra et al. (2012) to the relativistic mean-field framework. By identifying the very few RMF models that satisfy all modern nuclear-matter constraints, the study provides a vetted set of parameterizations for future applications in neutron-star structure, core-collapse supernovae, and finite-nuclei calculations, and it highlights the need to include the $K_{\tau,\rm v}$ constraint to avoid a proliferation of models that otherwise appear acceptable.
