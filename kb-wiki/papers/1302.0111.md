# Canonical and kinetic decompositions of the proton spin

> We propose a short summary of the present situation concerning the proton spin decomposition. We briefly discuss some of the main controversies about the issues of gauge invariance, uniqueness and measurability. As a conclusion, we argue that part of the controversies is actually undecidable.

# Canonical and kinetic decompositions of the proton spin

## Key claim
The paper argues that part of the controversies surrounding the decomposition of the proton spin into quark and gluon contributions is actually undecidable, because there exist infinitely many inequivalent gauge-invariant definitions of canonical orbital angular momentum (OAM) operators, and no experimental or theoretical principle uniquely selects one.

## Method
The author reviews the Chen et al. decomposition of the gauge field into pure-gauge and physical parts, and identifies a Stueckelberg symmetry that reflects the freedom in defining this separation. The kinetic OAM (based on the full covariant derivative) and the canonical OAM (based on the pure-gauge covariant derivative) are compared, and their relation to Wigner operators with different Wilson line paths is examined.

## Key numbers
- The kinetic OAM operator is defined with the full covariant derivative $D^\mu(x)=\partial^\mu-igA^\mu(x)$, while the canonical OAM uses $D^\mu_\text{pure}(x)=\partial^\mu-igA^\mu_\text{pure}(x)$.
- The difference between kinetic and canonical OAM is a potential term $\mathsf M^{\mu\nu\rho}_\text{pot}(x)=-g\,\bar\psi(x) \gamma^\mu x^{[\nu}A^{\rho]}_\text{phys}(x)\psi(x)$, which is usually non-vanishing.
- Using a straight gauge link in the Wigner operator yields the kinetic OAM $L_z$, while a staple-like gauge link yields the canonical OAM $\ell_z$.

## Context
This work extends the discussion initiated by Chen et al. (2008, 2011) and Wakamatsu (2010) on separating gauge degrees of freedom, and contrasts with the earlier kinetic OAM definition by Ji (1996). The paper clarifies that the Jaffe-Manohar OAM (1989) is recovered as a special gauge choice of the canonical OAM. The analysis enables a clearer understanding of how experimental conditions in high-energy scattering (such as Semi-Inclusive DIS and Drell-Yan) select specific Wilson line paths, making the light-front gauge-invariant extension more natural for interpreting measurements.
