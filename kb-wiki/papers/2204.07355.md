# Visualization of nuclear many-body correlations with the most probable configuration of nucleons

> A method to visualize many-body correlations using the information of the full wave function is presented. The set of nucleon coordinates which maximizes the square of the wave function, that is, the most probable spatial configuration of nucleons, is visualized. The method is applied to Hartree-Fock (HF) and HF+BCS wave functions of $p$- and $sd$-shell $N=Z$ even-even nuclei to analyze the many-body correlations in those systems. It is found that there are $α$-cluster-like four-body correlations already at the HF level in some of the nuclei. The effects of pairing on the most probable configuration are also investigated. The method is useful to analyze the nuclear many-body correlations, and it suggests a new viewpoint to microscopic nuclear wave functions.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a method to visualize nuclear many-body correlations by finding and plotting the set of nucleon coordinates that maximizes the square of the many-body wave function, but the authors caution that this maximum may not always be representative and that global behavior of the probability distribution should also be studied for a complete picture.

## Method

The authors search for the global maximum of the N-body probability density \(|\Psi|^2\) for a given nuclear wave function using the conjugate gradient method, starting from random initial spatial configurations. They apply this "\(|\Psi|^2\)-maximization method" to Hartree-Fock (HF) and HF+BCS wave functions of p- and sd-shell N=Z even-even nuclei, using the SLy4 Skyrme effective interaction for the HF part and a constant-gap approximation for the BCS part, with axial, reflection, and time-reversal symmetries imposed.

## Key numbers

* For \(^{20}\)Ne, the maximum value of \(\rho^{(N)}_{d_s=0}\) converges to the largest value among \(d_s\) values from 0 to 4, with other \(d_s\) values converging to values smaller by orders of magnitude.
* For \(^{12}\)C, without pairing the probability is nearly constant when rotating spin-up neutrons relative to spin-down neutrons, whereas with pairing the probability changes by approximately 60\%.
* The potential-energy curve of \(^{16}\)O is shown as a function of quadrupole deformation \(Q_2\), with abrupt transitions in neutron arrangement occurring around \(Q_2 \approx -25\) fm\(^2\), 25 fm\(^2\), and 100 fm\(^2\).
* For \(^{20}\)Ne and \(^{24}\)Mg, the probability variation as a function of relative rotation angle between spin-up and spin-down neutrons increases with pairing strength, using pairing gaps of \(\Delta = 6/A^{1/2}\) MeV and \(\Delta = 12/A^{1/2}\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the "dynamic Voronoi metropolis sampling" method from quantum chemistry (Liu 2016) to nuclear physics, providing a way to visualize many-body correlations without relying on molecular orbitals or cluster assumptions. The method is applied to mean-field wave functions and reveals \(\alpha\)-cluster-like four-body correlations already at the HF level in some deformed nuclei, consistent with the localization function approach of Reinhard et al. (2011). The technique enables future analyses of more correlated wave functions such as shell model or ab initio states, and could be extended to study reaction dynamics, molecular-bond structures in nuclei, and collective correlations beyond the mean field.
