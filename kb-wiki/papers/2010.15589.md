# How do I introduce Schrödinger equation during the quantum mechanics course?

> In this paper I explain how I usually introduce the Schrödinger equation during the quantum mechanics course. My preferred method is the chronological one. Since the Schrödinger equation belongs to a special case of wave equations I start the course with introducing the wave equation. The Schrödinger equation is derived with the help of the two quantum concepts introduced by Max Planck, Einstein, and de Broglie, i.e., the energy of a photon $E=\hbarω$ and the wavelength of the de Broglie wave $λ=h/p$. Finally, the difference between the classical wave equation and the quantum Schrödinger one is explained in order to help the students to grasp the meaning of quantum wavefunction $Ψ({\bf r},t)$. A comparison of the present method to the approaches given by the authors of quantum mechanics textbooks as well as that of the original Nuffield A level is presented. It is found that the present approach is different from those given by these authors, except by Weinberg or Dicke and Wittke. However, the approach is in line with the original Nuffield A level one.

## Key claim

The paper argues that introducing the Schrödinger equation through a chronological, historical approach, starting with the classical wave equation, then incorporating Planck's photon energy and de Broglie's matter wave, provides a smoother conceptual transition from classical to quantum mechanics for students, though the author notes this is a pedagogical preference and not a quantitatively tested method.

## Method

The author presents a step-by-step pedagogical derivation: first introducing the classical wave equation using sine waves and the complex exponential form, then incorporating the quantum concepts of photon energy (E = ħω) and de Broglie wavelength (λ = h/p) to rewrite the plane wave function, and finally taking derivatives to obtain the free-particle Schrödinger equation, which is then generalized to include a potential. The approach is compared qualitatively to the methods used in textbooks by Gasiorowicz, Griffiths, Dicke and Wittke, Weinberg, and others, as well as the original Nuffield A level physics curriculum.

## Key numbers

- The classical wave equation is derived from the sine wave A(x,t) = A₀ sin(kx − ωt), with phase velocity v = ω/k.
- Planck's quantum energy is given as E = hν = ħω, where ħ = h/2π.
- The de Broglie wavelength is λ = h/p, leading to the wave number k = p/ħ.
- The free-particle Schrödinger equation is iħ ∂Ψ/∂t = −(ħ²/2m) ∂²Ψ/∂x².
- The general Schrödinger equation is iħ ∂Ψ(r,t)/∂t = −(ħ²/2m) ∇² Ψ(r,t) + V(r) Ψ(r,t).

## Context

This pedagogical paper extends the approach of Dicke and Wittke (1960) and Weinberg (2015), who also introduce the Schrödinger equation chronologically, and contrasts with the methods of Gasiorowicz (1974) and Griffiths (2005), who introduce the equation abruptly without historical derivation. The work is in line with the original Nuffield A level physics curriculum, which sequences the discussion from photons and wave-particle duality to waves in boxes and the Schrödinger equation. The author proposes that future quantitative classroom studies comparing this chronological method to other approaches could provide more objective evidence of its effectiveness.
