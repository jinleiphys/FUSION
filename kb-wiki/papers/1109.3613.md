# Formation Of The Rare Earth Peak: Gaining Insight Into Late-Time r-Process Dynamics

> We study the formation and final structure of the rare earth peak ($A\sim160$) of the $r$-process nucleosynthesis. The rare earth peak forms at late times in the $r$-process after neutron exhaustion (neutron-to-seed ratio unity or R=1) as matter decays back to stability. Since rare earth peak formation does not occur during \nggn \ equilibrium it is sensitive to the strong interplay between late time thermodynamic evolution and nuclear physics input. Depending on the conditions the peak forms either because of the pattern of the neutron capture rates or because of the pattern of the separation energies. We analyze three mass models under different thermodynamic conditions. We find that the subtleties of each mass model, including separation energies and neutron capture rates, influence not only the final shape of the peak but also when it forms. We identify the range of nuclei which are influential in rare earth peak formation.

## Key claim

The rare earth peak forms via two distinct mechanisms depending on the thermodynamic evolution: in hot environments it forms through a "funneling" mechanism driven by a kink in separation energies, while in cold environments it forms through a "trapping" mechanism driven by local structure in neutron capture rates; the specific mechanism and its success depend on which mass model is used and whether the relevant nuclear structure features align with the r-process path during freeze-out.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis calculations using a fully implicit nuclear reaction network that includes beta-decay, neutron capture, and photo-dissociation channels, starting at T9=2 with densities of approximately 9e8 g/cm3 for hot evolutions (Ye=0.30) and 5e8 g/cm3 for cold evolutions (Ye=0.40). They compared three mass models (FRDM, ETFSI-Q, and HFB-17) under two parameterized thermodynamic evolutions: a hot evolution with density decaying as t^-2 and a cold evolution with density decaying as t^-6. The analysis tracked abundance-weighted lifetimes and the progression of the r-process path via the average number of neutrons from stability to identify when and how peak formation occurs.

## Key numbers

- Hot evolutions start at T9=2 with density approximately 9e8 g/cm3 and Ye=0.30, giving neutron-to-seed ratio R approximately 45
- Cold evolutions start at T9=2 with density approximately 5e8 g/cm3 and Ye=0.40, giving neutron-to-seed ratio R approximately 35
- For FRDM mass model, peak formation occurs when the r-process path is between 15 and 20 neutrons from stability; the structure in neutron capture rates occurs between approximately 10 and 15 neutrons from stability
- For ETFSI mass model, solar-like peaks form far from stability at approximately 20 or more neutrons from stability, but dissolve as the path approaches 15 neutrons from stability
- For HFB-17 mass model, the root mean square error of mass predictions is less than 0.6 MeV relative to over 2000 measured masses
- The influential nuclei for peak formation lie between 10 and 20 neutrons from stability, with the most influential being 10 to 15 neutrons from stability

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of rare earth peak formation by Surman et al. (1997), who investigated peak formation in hot r-process environments under n-gamma-gamma-n equilibrium and attributed peak formation to the co-action of nuclear deformation and beta-decay during freeze-out. It also builds on the sensitivity study by Arcones et al. (2011), who showed that the rare earth peak is sensitive to non-equilibrium effects at late times even when free neutron abundances become very low. The paper introduces a new "trapping" mechanism for cold evolutions where photo-dissociation plays no role after neutron exhaustion, contrasting with the "funneling" mechanism previously described for hot evolutions, and demonstrates that the choice of mass model (FRDM, ETFSI-Q, or HFB-17) dramatically affects whether and when the peak forms. The identification of nuclei 10 to 15 neutrons from stability as most influential provides targets for future measurements at radioactive ion beam facilities such as FRIB and FAIR.
