# Shell Corrections of Superheavy Nuclei in Self-Consistent Calculations

> Shell corrections to the nuclear binding energy as a measure of shell effects in superheavy nuclei are studied within the self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock and Relativistic Mean-Field theories. Due to the presence of low-lying proton continuum resulting in a free particle gas, special attention is paid to the treatment of single-particle level density. To cure the pathological behavior of shell correction around the particle threshold, the method based on the Green's function approach has been adopted. It is demonstrated that for the vast majority of Skyrme interactions commonly employed in nuclear structure calculations, the strongest shell stabilization appears for Z=124, and 126, and for N=184. On the other hand, in the relativistic approaches the strongest spherical shell effect appears systematically for Z=120 and N=172. This difference has probably its roots in the spin-orbit potential. We have also shown that, in contrast to shell corrections which are fairly independent on the force, macroscopic energies extracted from self-consistent calculations strongly depend on the actual force parametrisation used. That is, the A and Z dependence of mass surface when extrapolating to unknown superheavy nuclei is prone to significant theoretical uncertainties.

# Shell Corrections of Superheavy Nuclei in Self-Consistent Calculations

## Key claim

Shell corrections extracted from self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) and Relativistic Mean-Field (RMF) calculations predict different magic numbers depending on the theoretical framework: SHF models favor strongest spherical shell stabilization at Z=124-126 and N=184, while RMF models systematically predict Z=120 and N=172, with the difference attributed to the spin-orbit potential. However, the macroscopic energies extracted from these self-consistent calculations depend strongly on the force parametrization, introducing significant theoretical uncertainties when extrapolating to unknown superheavy nuclei.

## Method

Spherical self-consistent SHF and RMF calculations were performed for superheavy nuclei using a coordinate-space code with a 21 fm box and 0.3 fm mesh spacing, with pairing correlations practically neglected (constant pairing gaps below 100 keV). The single-particle level density was computed using a Green's function approach to avoid the pathological behavior of shell corrections around the particle threshold caused by low-lying proton continuum states. Shell corrections were extracted from the self-consistent single-particle energies using the Strutinsky procedure with a generalized plateau condition, employing smoothing parameters of gamma_p=1.54 and gamma_n=1.66 for SHF and gamma_p=2.0 and gamma_n=2.2 for RMF, with curvature correction order p=10.

## Key numbers

- For SHF models, the strongest spherical shell stabilization appears at Z=124 and 126, and at N=184
- For RMF models, the strongest spherical shell effect appears systematically at Z=120 and N=172
- The smoothing parameter gamma for SHF calculations was gamma_p=1.54 for protons and gamma_n=1.66 for neutrons (in units of oscillator frequency hbar*omega_0=41/A^(1/3))
- The smoothing parameter gamma for RMF calculations was gamma_p=2.0 for protons and gamma_n=2.2 for neutrons
- The curvature correction order p=10 was used for both SHF and RMF calculations
- The box size for both SHF and RMF calculations was 21 fm with mesh spacing 0.3 fm
- The effective nucleon mass for Skyrme forces ranges from approximately 0.65 (SLy4, SkI1, SkI3, SkI4) to 1.0 (SkT6, SkP)
- The effective mass for RMF forces is approximately m*/m ≈ 0.6, with values about 10% larger at the Fermi energy
- The compressibility of RMF forces ranges from approximately 170 MeV (NL-Z) to 355 MeV (NL-SH)
- The symmetry energy coefficient of RMF forces ranges from 36.1 MeV (NL-SH) to 43.5 MeV (NL1)
- The saturation density for RMF forces is around 0.15 nucleons/fm^3
- The energy per nucleon for RMF forces is around -16.3 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous self-consistent studies of superheavy nuclei by Cwiok (1996) and Lalazissis (1996), which used less satisfactory treatments of continuum states, by implementing a Green's function method developed by Kruppa (1998) for proper handling of the particle continuum. The results confirm the N=184 magic number found in earlier SHF calculations by Cwiok (1996) but reveal systematic differences between non-relativistic and relativistic approaches regarding proton magic numbers, with implications for predicting the stability of superheavy elements beyond Z=118. The findings enable more reliable extrapolations to unknown superheavy nuclei while highlighting that the spin-orbit interaction, particularly its treatment in RMF versus SHF models, remains a key source of theoretical uncertainty that future work must address.
