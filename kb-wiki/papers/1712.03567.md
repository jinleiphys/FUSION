# Why does the thermal model for hadron production in heavy ion collisions work?

> The yields for hadrons and even light nuclei measured at midrapidity in relativistic heavy ion collisions are found to be dictated exclusively by their thermal Boltzmann factor for a common temperature of approximately 155 MeV. The reason for the validity of the thermal model description is widely discussed. Here, we offer a new type of argument in its favor.

## Key claim

The thermal model's success in describing hadron and light-nucleus yields in heavy ion collisions follows from unitary time evolution and quantum entanglement: if a small part of the highly entangled fireball state is projected out, its probability distribution looks thermal, with the common temperature being the QCD phase transition temperature rather than a lower freeze-out temperature.

## Method

The authors argue that because QCD is time-reversal invariant and the collision system is isolated, the probability for forming any asymptotic hadron state can be evaluated at the break-up time of the quark-gluon plasma using a thermal parton distribution, provided entanglement among the partons forming the hadron can be neglected. They expand the hadron eigenstate in a parton basis, assume the fireball state is quasi-thermal at break-up, and show that the Boltzmann factor for the hadron mass emerges naturally from the thermal weights of the coalescing partons, independent of the number of partons.

## Key numbers

- The common temperature for all produced hadrons and light nuclei is approximately 155 MeV, identified as the QCD phase transition temperature.
- The thermal Boltzmann factor for a hadron or nucleus N is \(e^{-E_N/kT}\), where \(E_N\) is the hadron's energy, and the temperature T is the same for all species.
- The ALICE experiment showed that even weakly bound light nuclei like the hypertriton \(^3_{\Lambda}H\) are produced with the same thermal temperature (approximately 155 MeV) as tightly bound baryons such as the proton.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier argument (Müller and Schäfer, 2011) that decoherence of entanglement entropy explains a sizable fraction of the apparent entropy in heavy ion collisions, and builds on the Eigenstate Thermalization Hypothesis (ETH) as applied to heavy ion collisions by Becattini and Fries (2009). It also draws an analogy to the black hole information paradox, where few-photon observables appear thermal despite unitary evolution. The argument enables a theoretical justification for why the thermal model works for all hadrons and light nuclei, and suggests that two-particle correlations (e.g., HBT interferometry) are dominated by freeze-out conditions, while single-particle yields reflect the phase transition temperature.
