# Inverse magnetic catalysis -- how much do we know about?

> Some of the advances made in the literature to understand the phase transitions of quark matter in the presence of strong magnetic field and finite temperature (zero quark chemical potential) are reviewed. We start by discussing the physics behind the Magnetic catalysis (MC) at zero/finite temperature and then focus on the lattice predictions for inverse magnetic catalysis (IMC) at high temperature and strong magnetic fields. Possible explanations for the IMC are covered as well. Finally, we discuss recent efforts to modify QCD (quantum chromodynamics) effective models in order to reproduce the IMC observed on the lattice simulations. We emphasize the fact that applying thermo-magnetic effects on the coupling constant of the NJL model significantly improve the effectiveness of the NJL model to obtain a reasonable physical description of hot and magnetized quark matter being in agreement with lattice results.

## Key claim
This review argues that inverse magnetic catalysis (IMC) at high temperature and strong magnetic fields can be reproduced in the local Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model by introducing a temperature- and magnetic-field-dependent coupling constant, but the authors caution that a rigorous microscopic understanding of IMC is still lacking.

## Method
The paper reviews lattice QCD simulations for Nf=2+1 with physical pion mass (mπ=140 MeV) that show a decreasing pseudocritical temperature with increasing magnetic field, and separates valence and sea contributions to the chiral condensate. It then describes how the NJL model’s coupling constant G is made a function of eB and T, with parameters fitted to lattice data for the average quark condensate, to capture IMC effects that standard constant-coupling NJL models miss.

## Key numbers
- Magnetic field in heavy-ion collisions reaches up to 10^18–10^19 Gauss, exceeding the Schwinger critical field.
- Lattice QCD with Nf=2+1 and physical pion mass mπ=140 MeV shows a decreasing pseudocritical temperature Tpc with increasing magnetic field, in contrast to earlier Nf=2 simulations with unphysical pion masses (200–480 MeV) that showed increasing Tpc.
- At T=160 MeV (near Tpc), the sea contribution suppresses the condensate, dominating the valence enhancement and producing IMC.
- NJL coupling constant at zero field: G0 = 5.022 GeV^{-2}.
- Mott dissociation temperature for π0 meson at eB=0.1 GeV^2: TMott = 195.0 MeV with fixed G(0,0); TMott = 166.84 MeV with G(eB,T).
- Mott dissociation temperature for π0 meson at eB=0.2 GeV^2: TMott = 200.2 MeV with fixed G(0,0); TMott = 164.90 MeV with G(eB,T).

## Context
This review extends the line of work on magnetic catalysis (MC) established by Gusynin, Miransky, and Shovkovy (1994–1995), who showed that strong magnetic fields reduce fermion dynamics by two spatial dimensions, enhancing chiral symmetry breaking. It contrasts with the lattice QCD results of Bali et al. (2011, 2012) that discovered IMC for light quarks near Tpc, and with earlier lattice work by D’Elia et al. (2010) using heavier quarks that only saw MC. The review enables future studies to apply the thermo-magnetic coupling approach to other observables such as axion properties, magnetization, and neutral pion masses in hot magnetized matter.
