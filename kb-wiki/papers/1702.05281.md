# Azimuthal flow in hadron collisions from quark-gluon string repulsion

> Color flux tubes (quark-gluon strings), formed at early stages of hadron-hadron collisions, may overlap in case of sufficiently high densities and interact, producing long-range azimuthal correlations. In the hypothesis of repulsive interaction, each string may acquire, before the hadronization, the additional transverse boost, which is an efficient sum of all accounted string-string interactions. This modifies transverse momenta to the particles formed in string decay, leading to modification of event-wise observables, like azimuthal asymmetry of two-particle correlations, over a wide range of rapidity. In this article we discuss results of Monte Carlo model with string repulsion, where efficient string-string interaction radius is introduced. We show that the effect of string repulsion can be the main dynamic origin of the elliptic flow and of the higher harmonics, which are reflected in the complicated structures observed in two-particle long-range correlation topology in nucleus-nucleus collisions at RHIC and at LHC.

# Azimuthal flow in hadron collisions from quark-gluon string repulsion

## Key claim

String-string repulsion, implemented via an effective interaction radius in a Monte Carlo model, can serve as the primary dynamical origin of elliptic flow and higher harmonic flow coefficients in nucleus-nucleus collisions, without requiring hydrodynamic assumptions. The authors show that this mechanism qualitatively reproduces the observed two-particle correlation structures at RHIC and LHC, but note that the required effective interaction radius is quite large (about 2 fm) and the exact type of string-string interaction remains unknown.

## Method

The authors developed a Monte Carlo model that simulates Pb-Pb collisions by generating nucleon positions via a Woods-Saxon distribution, distributing partons within each nucleon, and forming quark-gluon strings when partons from colliding nuclei are within 0.4 fm. Soft strings (97% of all strings) are assigned a rapidity range of y ∈ (-4, 4) and interact via a repulsive mechanism with an effective interaction radius R_int as a free parameter; each string acquires a transverse boost from the summed repulsion of neighboring strings, with the mean boost adjusted to approximately 0.65 in central collisions. Strings then fragment into quark-antiquark pairs with exponentially distributed transverse momentum, which combine into mesons (pions, kaons, protons) that receive the collective string boost in the laboratory frame.

## Key numbers

- Woods-Saxon radius for Pb nuclei: 6.62 fm, diffusion parameter: 0.546 fm
- Parton distribution width inside nucleons: σ_xy = 0.4 fm (2D Gaussian)
- Parton interaction distance for string formation: d = 0.4 fm
- Mean transverse boost of strings in central Pb-Pb collisions: β ≈ 0.65
- Effective string-string interaction radius required: R_int = 2 fm
- Soft strings occupy rapidity range y ∈ (-4, 4)
- 3% probability for hard scattering (jet-like) strings; 97% are soft strings
- Particles analyzed with p_T > 0.15 GeV/c for two-particle correlation functions
- v_2(p_T) calculated for pions, kaons, and protons in centrality class 10-20% of Pb-Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV
- Pseudorapidity gap for v_2 analysis: |Δη| > 1.0, with particles of interest in |η| < 1 and reference particles in -3 < η < -1 and 1 < η < 3

## Context

This work extends the string-based approach to collective flow originally proposed by Abramovsky (1980) and builds on the authors' prior Monte Carlo studies (Altsybeev 2014, Altsybeev and Feofilov 2015). It contrasts with hydrodynamic interpretations of flow in heavy-ion collisions and with the color glass condensate (CGC) glasma flux tube model of Dumitru (2008). The string repulsion mechanism offers an alternative to the string fusion and percolation approach of Braun (2015) and the particle free-streaming model. The results enable future tests of whether the same mechanism can describe flow in smaller systems (p-Pb and pp collisions), which would establish a uniform origin of collective effects across all hadronic collision systems.
