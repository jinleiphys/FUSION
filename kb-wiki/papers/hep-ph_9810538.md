# Lorentz Covariance and Internal Space-time Symmetry of Relativistic Extended Particles

> The difference between Lorentz invariance and Lorentz covariance is discussed in detail. A covariant formalism is developed for the internal space-time symmetry of extended particles, especially in connection with the insightful observations Feynman made during the period 1969-72. A Lorentz-group formalism is presented for the harmonic oscillator model of Feynman, Kislinger and Ravndal, which was originally based on hadronic mass spectra. This covariant version allows us to construct a parton distribution function by Lorentz-boosting the oscillator wave function of a hadron at rest. The role of the time-separation variable is discussed in detail. It is shown that, due to our inability to make measurements on this variable, it belongs to Feynman's rest of the universe. Our failure to observe the rest of the universe leads to an increase in entropy.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the quark model (valid for slow hadrons) and Feynman's parton model (valid for ultra-fast hadrons) are two different manifestations of the same covariant entity, unified through a Lorentz-group formalism for harmonic oscillator wave functions; the key hedge is that this unification works only if one accepts that the time-separation variable between quarks, which becomes mixed with longitudinal spatial separation under Lorentz boosts, belongs to Feynman's "rest of the universe" and is unmeasurable.

## Method

The author constructs a representation of Wigner's O(3)-like little group for massive particles using normalizable harmonic oscillator wave functions in a covariant formalism, solving the Lorentz-invariant oscillator equation of Feynman, Kislinger and Ravndal (1971). The internal space-time separation between two quarks is described by the four-vector \(x = (x_a - x_b)/2\sqrt{2}\), and the wave function for a hadron at rest is written in terms of Hermite polynomials and Gaussian factors in the spatial and time coordinates. Lorentz boosts along the \(z\) direction are applied using light-cone variables \(u = (z+t)/\sqrt{2}\) and \(v = (z-t)/\sqrt{2}\), which transform as \(u' = e^\eta u\) and \(v' = e^{-\eta}v\), producing a squeeze transformation that expands one coordinate while contracting the other.

## Key numbers

- The Lorentz boost parameter \(\eta\) is defined as \(\tanh^{-1}(v/c)\), where \(v\) is the hadron speed.
- The ground-state wave function for the hadron at rest is \(\psi_0(z,t) = (1/\pi)^{1/2} \exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2}(u^2 + v^2)\right\}\), distributed within a circular region in the \(uv\) plane.
- The boosted ground-state wave function is \(\psi_\eta(z,t) = (1/\pi)^{1/2} \exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2}(e^{-2\eta}u^2 + e^{2\eta}v^2)\right\}\), distributed in an elliptic region with the same area as the circle.
- The one-dimensional oscillator wave function for the \(n\)-th excited state is \(\phi_n(z) = (1/\pi n! 2^n)^{1/2} H_n(z) \exp(-z^2/2)\).

## Context

This work extends the harmonic oscillator model of Feynman, Kislinger and Ravndal (1971), which was originally based on hadronic mass spectra, by providing a fully covariant formalism that allows Lorentz-boosting the wave functions. It builds on Wigner's (1939) classification of little groups of the Poincare group and the group contraction of O(3) to E(2) by Inonu and Wigner (1953), as well as the observation by Ferrara and Savoy (1982) that the E(2)-like little group for massless particles can be obtained as a limit of the O(3)-like little group under Lorentz boost. The formalism enables a unified description of the quark model and Feynman's parton picture (1969), and it introduces the time-separation variable as part of Feynman's "rest of the universe" (1972), whose unobservability leads to an increase in entropy.
