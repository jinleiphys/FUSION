# Machine learning transforms the inference of the nuclear equation of state

> Our knowledge of the properties of dense nuclear matter is usually obtained indirectly via nuclear experiments, astrophysical observations, and nuclear theory calculations. Advancing our understanding of the nuclear equation of state (EOS, which is one of the most important properties and of central interest in nuclear physics) has relied on various data produced from experiments and calculations. We review how machine learning is revolutionizing the way we extract EOS from these data, and summarize the challenges and opportunities that come with the use of machine learning.

## Key claim

Machine learning (ML) is transforming the inference of the nuclear equation of state (EOS) by enabling data-driven frameworks that can extract physical information from event-by-event heavy-ion collision data, but the field is still in its earliest stages and faces open questions regarding interpretability, generalizability, and data requirements.

## Method

The authors review how ML algorithms, particularly supervised learning and Bayesian inference, are applied to infer the nuclear EOS from experimental data and theoretical model simulations. They describe a conventional framework where observables are constructed from human knowledge and compared to transport model simulations, versus an ML-based framework where algorithms can automatically construct features from event-by-event data and identify the most important observables via feature attribution methods. The review covers specific applications including Bayesian inference combining heavy-ion collision data with astrophysical observations, and supervised ML for extracting the symmetry energy from transport model simulations.

## Key numbers

- Several terabytes per second: the size of data acquired from detectors in heavy-ion collision experiments
- Several fm/c: the typical lifetime of nuclear matter created in heavy-ion collisions
- The binding energy per nucleon E(ρ,δ) is expressed as E(ρ,δ=0) + E_sym(ρ)δ² + O(δ⁴), where E_sym(ρ) is the density-dependent nuclear symmetry energy

## Context

This review extends the long-standing effort to constrain the nuclear EOS from experiments and theory, referencing prior work by Drischler (2021), Li (2008), Oertel (2017), Sorensen (2023), and Lattimer (2021). It highlights the Nature work by Huth et al. (2022) that used Bayesian inference to combine heavy-ion collision data with astrophysical observations and microscopic nuclear theory, showing consistent EOS constraints across disciplines. The review enables future work by identifying four improvement areas: improving synthetic training data quality, introducing physical information into ML algorithms, adopting successful ML techniques from condensed matter and particle physics, and incorporating latest ML developments. Open questions include whether ML outputs are interpretable, whether algorithms can generalize across different data sources, and whether knowledge can be learned from small datasets.
