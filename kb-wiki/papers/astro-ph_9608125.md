# Neutron-induced nucleosynthesis

> Neutron--induced nucleosynthesis plays an important role in astrophysical scenarios like in primordial nucleosynthesis in the early universe, in the s--process occurring in Red Giants, and in the $α$--rich freeze--out and r--process taking place in supernovae of type II. A review of the three important aspects of neutron--induced nucleosynthesis is given: astrophysical background, experimental methods and theoretical models for determining reaction cross sections and reaction rates at thermonuclear energies. Three specific examples of neutron capture at thermal and thermonuclear energies are discussed in some detail.

## Key claim

This review paper surveys neutron-induced nucleosynthesis across primordial, s-process, and r-process astrophysical sites, but the authors note that many open questions remain, particularly regarding the explosion mechanism of type II supernovae and the identification of the r-process site, which "has not been clearly identified yet" despite strong clues from recent studies and observations.

## Method

The paper reviews three aspects of neutron-induced nucleosynthesis: astrophysical background (primordial nucleosynthesis, s-process in Red Giants, and r-process/alpha-rich freeze-out in type II supernovae), experimental methods (direct detection via prompt gamma-ray detection, total absorption detection using BaF2 crystal balls, and total energy detection), and theoretical models (compound-nucleus formation using statistical Hauser-Feshbach calculations and direct reaction mechanisms using potential models). Three specific examples of neutron capture at thermal and thermonuclear energies are discussed in detail: 36S(n,gamma)37S where the direct reaction mechanism dominates, 208Pb(n,gamma)209Pb where resonant compound-nucleus and non-resonant direct contributions are comparable, and neutron capture of Gd-isotopes where only compound-nucleus contributions are relevant.

## Key numbers

- Thermal neutron energy defined as kBT = 25.3 meV; thermonuclear energies range from kBT ≈ 1 keV to 1 MeV
- The dominant peak of the Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution is at E = 0.0862 T9, with E in MeV and T9 in 10^9 K; maximum temperatures in nucleosynthesis processes are T9 = 7–10
- The 13C(alpha,n)16O neutron source operates at radiative burning temperatures T ≈ 0.9 × 10^8 K and convective burning at T ≈ 1.5 × 10^8 K; the 22Ne(alpha,n)25Mg source requires T ≥ 3 × 10^8 K
- The Karlsruhe BaF2 detector consists of 42 crystals forming a spherical shell of 15 cm thickness, with less than 5% loss in 4π solid angle and total efficiency close to 95%; accuracy claimed in capture measurements is as good as 1%
- For the r-process, neutron number densities n_n > 10^20 cm^-3 and temperatures around 10^9 K are required; an average of 80 neutrons per seed nucleus is needed, with the constraint 1 < Y_n/Y_seed < 180
- Only a mass of 10^-6 to 10^-4 M_sun of processed matter needs to be ejected per supernova event to provide the quantities derived from galactic properties
- In primordial nucleosynthesis, the photon-to-baryon ratio is eta^-1 = n_gamma/n_b ≈ 10^10; the characteristic length of density inhomogeneity regions has expanded to a current value of 10^14 m

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957) on neutron-capture nucleosynthesis and the classical s-process models of Seeger, Fowler, and Clayton (1965) and Ulrich (1973). It summarizes the state of the field in 1996, covering the three-component s-process (weak, main, strong) described by Kaeppeler et al. (1989) and Beer et al. (1991), and the r-process parameter studies of Thielemann et al. (1993, 1994) and Kratz et al. (1988, 1993). The paper enables future work by identifying open questions, particularly the need for improved nuclear theory for neutron-rich isotopes far from stability, better understanding of the supernova explosion mechanism, and the unresolved identification of the r-process site, while providing a benchmark for neutron-capture calculations using nuclear-structure models.
