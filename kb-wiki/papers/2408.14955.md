# De-excitations of highly excited $^{11}$B$^*$ and $^{15}$N$^*$ based on the GEMINI++ code

> Nuclear de-excitations associated with neutrino-nucleus interactions and nucleon decays are playing an increasingly significant role in neutrino experiments. We explore the GEMINI++ code and estimate its ability to account for the de-excitation processes of highly excited $^{11}$B$^*$ and $^{15}$N$^*$, which can be created in the liquid scintillator and water Cherenkov detectors respectively. It is found that GEMINI++ can not describe the nuclear experimental data of $^{11}$B$^*$ and $^{15}$N$^*$ well. To improve its performance for de-excitations of light nuclei, we modify GEMINI++ and then develop a code of GEMINI++4$ν$, which can give the best predictions compared with experimental measurements among some widely used statistical model codes.

# De-excitations of highly excited $^{11}$B$^*$ and $^{15}$N$^*$ based on the GEMINI++ code

## Key claim

The authors find that the standard GEMINI++ code cannot describe experimental de-excitation data for $^{11}$B$^*$ and $^{15}$N$^*$ well, but after modifying three aspects (back-shifted level density term, inclusion of discrete levels, and suppression factors) they produce a new code called GEMINI++4$\nu$ that gives the best predictions among widely used statistical model codes, though the agreement is not coincidental and some discrepancies remain, particularly for alpha and triton emissions at high excitation energies.

## Method

The authors used the GEMINI++ Monte Carlo code, which implements statistical sequential binary decays via the Weisskopf-Ewing formalism, to simulate de-excitations of $^{11}$B$^*$ and $^{15}$N$^*$ produced by quasifree proton knockout reactions. They compared predictions to experimental branching ratios from Yosoi et al. (2003) for charged particles and neutrons, and from Panin et al. (2016) for two-body decay channels. Three modifications were made to create GEMINI++4$\nu$: removal of the back-shifted term $E_1$ in the Fermi-gas level density formula, inclusion of discrete nuclear levels from the NNDC database for daughter nuclei below 6 MeV excitation, and adjustment of suppression factors $F_s$ for charged particle emission widths.

## Key numbers

- For $^{11}$B$^*$ de-excitations with $16 \text{ MeV} \leq E_x \leq 35 \text{ MeV}$, GEMINI++4$\nu$ with $F_s=0.5$ gives total branching ratio differences between measurements and predictions of less than 1% for proton and deuteron emissions.
- The critical excitation energy for $^{11}$B$^* \rightarrow p + ^{10}\text{Be}$ in GEMINI++ was 17.1 MeV versus the theoretical separation energy of 11.2 MeV; after modification in GEMINI++4$\nu$ it became 12.2 MeV.
- For $^{11}$B$^* \rightarrow t + ^{8}\text{Be}$, the critical energy in GEMINI++ was 20.5 MeV versus the theoretical 11.2 MeV; after modification it became 12.1 MeV.
- The measured neutron branching ratio from $^{15}$N$^*$ de-excitations with $20 \text{ MeV} \leq E_x \leq 40 \text{ MeV}$ is about 50% of total emissions.
- Detection thresholds in the Yosoi et al. experiments were 3.1 MeV for protons, 4.0 MeV for deuterons, 4.6 MeV for tritons, and 4.5 MeV for alpha particles; the neutron detection lower limit was 3.2 MeV.
- Including discrete levels enhanced predicted branching ratios by less than 20% for both two-body and three-body decay categories.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to model de-excitations of light nuclei relevant to neutrino detectors, where $^{11}$B$^*$ and $^{15}$N$^*$ are produced in liquid scintillator and water Cherenkov detectors respectively. Previous statistical model codes including CASCADE (used by Yosoi et al., 2003), TALYS (used by Hu et al., 2021 and Abe, 2023), ABLA (used by Abe, 2024), and SMOKER all failed to adequately describe the experimental data, especially for triton and alpha emissions. The new GEMINI++4$\nu$ code provides event-by-event output suitable for integration into neutrino generators such as GENIE and NuWro, enabling improved simulations of de-excitation products in experiments like JUNO, Hyper-Kamiokande, and DUNE. The authors note that residual discrepancies in alpha and triton channels may be due to cluster structure effects in light nuclei, such as the alpha cluster in $^{10}$Be.
