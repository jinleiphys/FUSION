# Vortex pinning and dynamics in the neutron star crust

> The nature of the interaction between superfluid vortices and the neutron star crust, conjectured by Anderson and Itoh in 1975 to be at the heart vortex creep and the cause of glitches, has been a long-standing question in astrophysics. Using a qualitatively new approach, we follow the dynamics as superfluid vortices move in response to the presence of "nuclei" (nuclear defects in the crust). The resulting motion is perpendicular to the force, similar to the motion of a spinning top when pushed. We show that nuclei repel vortices in the neutron star crust, and characterize the force as a function of the vortex-nucleus separation.

## Key claim

The authors show that nuclei repel superfluid vortices in the neutron star crust, using dynamical simulations that allow all degrees of freedom to evolve, in contrast to previous static energy-difference calculations that could not determine the sign of the force. The repulsive nature is confirmed by observing that the vortex moves perpendicular to the applied force in a direction consistent with repulsion, and that initially "pinned" configurations rapidly unpin within less than 1000 fm/c.

## Method

The authors perform time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) simulations on a 3D lattice of size 75 fm x 75 fm x 60 fm with momentum cutoff 400 MeV/c, using the FaNDF^0 energy density functional for the normal part and a density-dependent pairing functional. They drag a nucleus (Z=50) toward a vortex at constant velocity v_0 = 0.001c by applying an external force only to protons, and extract the vortex-nucleus force by balancing it against the known external force. The force per unit length is extracted by fitting a Pade approximant to the total force data, assuming each vortex line element contributes according to dF = f(r) sin(alpha) u_r dl.

## Key numbers

- Background neutron densities studied: n = 0.014 fm^{-3} and n = 0.031 fm^{-3}, corresponding to zones 3 and 4 of the neutron star crust according to Negele and Vautherin (1973)
- Vortex tension upper bound: T less than or equal to 1.4 MeV/fm at n = 0.014 fm^{-3} and 7.3 MeV/fm at n = 0.031 fm^{-3}
- Vortex length increase at t_max approximately 14,000 fm/c: Delta L = 3.5 fm at low density and 1.5 fm at high density
- Excitation energy at t_max: E* = 5 MeV at low density and 11 MeV at high density
- Effective range of the force: about 10 fm at lower density, increasing to about 15 fm at higher density
- Critical velocity for pinning to store enough angular momentum for giant glitches: v_c approximately (1-5) x 10^{-4} c
- Dragging velocity used in simulations: v_0 = 0.001c
- Simulation box dimensions: 75 fm x 75 fm x 60 fm
- Confining tube radius: 30 fm
- Proton number: Z = 50
- Maximum pairing gap: 2 MeV
- Unpinning timescale: shorter than 1,000 fm/c

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing question of vortex-nucleus interaction in neutron star crusts, originally conjectured by Anderson and Itoh (1975) to be responsible for vortex creep and pulsar glitches. Previous static calculations by Pizzochero (1998), Donati and Pizzochero (2003, 2004, 2006), Seveso (2012), and Avogadro and Pizzochero (2013, 2014) could only compute pinning energies by subtracting large energies (approximately 10^4 MeV) to obtain differences of order 1 MeV, and could not determine the sign of the force. The dynamical TDSLDA approach, following the suggestion by Bulgac (2007), unambiguously shows repulsion and provides the force per unit length needed for effective hydrodynamic models such as that developed by Link (2014). The results enable construction of realistic glitch models by providing microscopic input for vortex dynamics in the neutron star crust.
