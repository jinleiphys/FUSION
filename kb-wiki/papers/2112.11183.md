# Inhomogeneous phases in the chirally imbalanced $2+1$-dimensional Gross-Neveu model and their absence in the continuum limit

> We study the $μ$-$μ_{45}$-$T$ phase diagram of the $2+1$-dimensional Gross-Neveu model, where $μ$ denotes the ordinary chemical potential, $μ_{45}$ the chiral chemical potential and $T$ the temperature. We use the mean-field approximation and two different lattice regularizations with naive chiral fermions. An inhomogeneous phase at finite lattice spacing is found for one of the two regularizations. Our results suggest that there is no inhomogeneous phase in the continuum limit. We show that a chiral chemical potential is equivalent to an isospin chemical potential. Thus, all results presented in this work can also be interpreted in the context of isospin imbalance.

## Key claim
The authors find an inhomogeneous phase in the chirally imbalanced 2+1-dimensional Gross-Neveu model at finite lattice spacing for one of two lattice regularizations, but their results suggest that this phase disappears in the continuum limit.

## Method
The authors study the phase diagram in the plane of ordinary chemical potential (mu), chiral chemical potential (mu_45), and temperature (T) using the mean-field approximation. They employ two different lattice regularizations with naive chiral fermions and compare the results to assess continuum-limit behavior.

## Key numbers
- An inhomogeneous phase is found at finite lattice spacing for one regularization, but no specific numerical values for its location or extent are provided in the available text.
- The paper establishes an equivalence between chiral chemical potential and isospin chemical potential, though no numerical values for this mapping are given.

## Context
This work extends studies of inhomogeneous phases in the Gross-Neveu model, which have been observed in related models such as the 1+1-dimensional Gross-Neveu model. The finding that the inhomogeneous phase is a lattice artifact absent in the continuum limit contradicts some expectations from earlier finite-spacing studies. The equivalence between chiral and isospin chemical potential enables future work on isospin-imbalanced systems to directly use these results.
