# Models of mixed hadron-quark-gluon matter

> The problem of the possible creation of mixed hadron-quark-gluon matter, that can arise at nuclear or heavy-ion collisions, is addressed. It is shown that there can exist several different kinds of such a mixed matter. The main types of this matter can be classified onto macroscopic mixture, mesoscopic mixture, and microscopic mixture. Different types of these mixtures require principally different descriptions. Before comparing theoretical results with experiments, one has to analyze thermodynamic stability of all these mixed states, classifying them onto unstable, metastable, and stable. Only the most stable mixed state should be compared with experiment. Mixed states also need to be checked with regard to stratification instability. In addition to the static stratification instability, there can happen dynamic instability occurring in a mixture of components moving with respect to each other. This effect, called counterflow instability, has also to be taken into account, since it can lead to the stratification of mixed matter.

# Models of mixed hadron-quark-gluon matter

## Key claim

The paper argues that mixed hadron-quark-gluon matter can exist in three distinct types (macroscopic, mesoscopic, and microscopic) that require fundamentally different theoretical descriptions, and that only the mesoscopic and microscopic mixtures can be stable equilibrium states; the macroscopic mixed state, which would arise from a first-order phase transition, is inherently unstable and cannot be compared with experiment, contrary to naive analogies with boiling water.

## Method

The authors classify mixed hadron-quark-gluon matter by the characteristic size of phase domains relative to the interparticle distance and system size. For macroscopic mixtures (domain size comparable to system size), they analyze thermodynamic stability using the Maxwell construction and isothermal compressibility. For mesoscopic mixtures (germ size intermediate between interparticle distance and system size), they develop a statistical ensemble formalism with random spatial germ distributions, manifold indicator functions, and geometric phase weights as order parameters. For microscopic mixtures (uniform intermixing), they apply clustering theory with compositeness numbers, derive a potential scaling law relating all cluster interactions to the nucleon-nucleon interaction, and analyze both static stratification stability and dynamic counterflow instability.

## Key numbers

- The experimental lifetime of fireballs formed under heavy-ion collisions is approximately 10^{-22} s, while the local equilibration time of nuclear matter is approximately 10^{-23} s, establishing that thermodynamic language is applicable since t_loc << t_exp.
- The deconfinement temperature for a crossover is approximately 170 MeV, defined as the point where derivatives of observables have a maximum.
- Numerical simulations show that pion clusters survive until around 2T_c, where T_c is the deconfinement temperature.
- The critical relative velocity for counterflow instability is given by v_c = sqrt( (rho_2 / (m_2 Phi_11)) (Phi_11 Phi_22 - Phi_12^2) ), above which a microscopic mixture stratifies into macroscopic domains.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of deconfinement transitions in QCD, which lattice simulations (e.g., the studies cited as references 11-15) have shown to be a crossover rather than a first-order transition. The paper builds on prior work by the same authors (Yukalov and Yukalova, references 19, 57, 58) on mesoscopic mixtures and clustering matter (references 6, 7). It contradicts the naive macroscopic mixed-phase picture often used in heavy-ion phenomenology, which treats the mixed state analogously to boiling water with gas bubbles. The classification and stability analysis enable more reliable comparisons between theoretical predictions and experimental data from heavy-ion collisions, requiring that only the most stable mixed state be used for such comparisons.
