# Probing additional dimensions in the universe with neutron experiments

> We carry out a simple analysis of (n+3)-dimensional gravity in the context of recent work on 'large' supplementary dimensions and deduce a formula for the expected compactification radius for the n additional dimensions in the universe, as a function of the Planck and the electro-weak scales. We argue that the correspondingly modified gravitational force gives rise to effects that might be within the detection range of dedicated neutron experiments. A scattering analysis of the corresponding modified gravitational forces suggests that slow neutron scattering off atomic nuclei with null spin may provide an experimental test for these ideas.

## Key claim

A simple classical analysis of (n+3)-dimensional gravity, combined with the ADD conjecture that the N-dimensional gravitational force at the electro-weak length scale is as strong as the 3-dimensional force at the Planck length, yields compactification radii that may be detectable through neutron scattering experiments, but only for specific numbers of extra dimensions (notably n=2) and only if the tiny gravitational phase shifts can be isolated from the much larger nuclear scattering background.

## Method

The authors derive a formula for the compactification radius R_c of n additional spatial dimensions by equating the N-dimensional gravitational force at the electro-weak length scale R_e to the 3-dimensional gravitational force at the Planck length R_P, using a sudden-transition approximation between N-D and 3-D gravity at R_c. They then compute the gravitational potential V_n(r) for distances below R_c and estimate phase shifts for neutron scattering off a heavy spin-zero nucleus (²⁰⁸Pb) using the eikonal approximation, considering only angular momenta L > 7 to avoid contamination from nuclear bound states. The optimal neutron energy and phase shift are calculated by setting the impact parameter equal to the distance R_= where nuclear and gravitational potentials are equal.

## Key numbers

- For n=2, R_c = 2.8 × 10¹³ fm (≈ 2.8 cm) from Eq. (conj), or 2.5 × 10¹² fm (≈ 2.5 mm) from Eq. (conj3)
- For n=2, the gravitational potential at r=30 fm is V_n(r=30) = 2.4 × 10⁻¹² (units not specified but consistent with MeV)
- For n=2, the distance where nuclear and gravitational potentials are equal is R_= = 25 fm
- For n=2, the optimal neutron energy for scattering is E_opt = 2.3 MeV
- For n=2, the optimal phase shift is φ_opt = 1.2 × 10⁻¹² rad
- For n=2, the gravitational scattering amplitude for neutrons on ²⁰⁸Pb is A_g = 0.298 × 10⁻⁸ fm, compared to typical nuclear amplitudes A_n ≈ 7 fm
- The interference term 2|A_n(E,0)|A_g is about 0.4 nb/sr, while the pure nuclear cross section |A_n(E,0)|² is about 5 b/sr

## Context

This work extends the large extra dimensions (LED) framework proposed by Arkani-Hamed, Dimopoulos, and Dvali (1998) by applying a simplified classical gravity analysis to derive compactification radii, finding values close to those from more sophisticated general relativity and string theory calculations by Blumenhagen et al. (2000). The paper proposes neutron scattering experiments as an alternative to submillimeter torsion-balance experiments (Hoyle et al., 2001) and high-energy collider searches for missing energy (Culbertson et al., 1999), noting that for n=2 the predicted compactification radius is in the mm-to-cm range where torsion-balance experiments may already have ruled out deviations, but arguing that uncertainties in the estimate leave room for neutron-based tests. The approach is enabled by recent advances in slow-neutron physics, including the observation of neutron quantum states in Earth's gravitational field by Nesvizhevsky et al. (2002), and could potentially test deviations from Newton's law at micrometer scales using neutron interference patterns.
