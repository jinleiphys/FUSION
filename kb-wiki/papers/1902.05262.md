# Experimental nuclear astrophysics in Italy

> Nuclear astrophysics, the union of nuclear physics and astronomy, went through an impressive expansion during the last twenty years. This could be achieved thanks to milestone improvements in astronomical observations, cross section measurements, powerful computer simulations and much refined stellar models. Italian groups are giving quite important contributions to every domain of nuclear astrophysics, sometimes being the leaders of worldwide unique experiments. In this paper we will discuss the astrophysical scenarios where nuclear astrophysics plays a key role and we will provide detailed descriptions of the present and future of the experiments on nuclear astrophysics which belong to the scientific programme of INFN (the National Institute for Nuclear Physics in Italy).

# Experimental Nuclear Astrophysics in Italy

## Key claim
Italian nuclear astrophysics groups have provided fundamental contributions across multiple domains, from Big Bang nucleosynthesis to neutron star equations of state, but the paper presents this as a status report and future roadmap rather than claiming a single breakthrough; many results remain preliminary or await future experimental runs (e.g., LHC Run-3 and Run-4, the 3.5 MV underground accelerator at Gran Sasso) to achieve definitive conclusions.

## Method
The paper reviews experimental programs funded by INFN (the Italian National Institute for Nuclear Physics) across several facilities: underground measurements at LUNA (Gran Sasso) for low-energy cross sections; indirect measurements via the Trojan Horse Method (THM) at AsFiN; neutron-induced reaction studies at n_TOF (CERN); heavy-ion collision experiments at GSI (ASY-EOS, FOPI, LAND); hypernuclear spectroscopy at JLab and J-PARC (ULYSSES); kaon-nucleon interaction studies at DAFNE (SIDDHARTA-2, AMADEUS); and hyperon-nucleon femtoscopy at ALICE (LHC). The review covers Big Bang nucleosynthesis, explosive nucleosynthesis in novae and supernovae, the r-process in neutron star mergers, and the equation of state of dense matter.

## Key numbers
- Slope parameter of the symmetry energy from ASY-EOS: L = 72 ± 13 MeV, measured in Au+Au collisions at 400 AMeV, improving accuracy by a factor of 2 over the previous result of L = 83 ± 26 MeV.
- Density region explored in ASY-EOS measurements: between 0.8 and 1.6 times nuclear saturation density (ρ₀).
- Maximum temperature in nova bursts: between 200 and 400 MK, depending on white dwarf mass.
- Peak temperature in X-ray burster He burning: T > 500 MK.
- Central density for C ignition in type Ia supernovae: approximately 10⁶ g/cm⁻³.
- Core density at nuclear density in core-collapse supernovae: approximately 10¹⁴ g/cm³.
- Gravitational energy converted into neutrinos in core collapse: about 10⁵³ erg.
- Neutron star core density threshold for exotic matter: about 6 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³.
- Two neutron stars observed with masses as large as 2 solar masses, used to constrain the equation of state.
- ALICE Pb-Pb data sample for Run-3 and Run-4 will be larger by a factor of 100 compared to Run-2.
- Neutron density required for r-process: > 10²⁰ neutrons per cm³.
- Kilonova ejecta mass: a few 10⁻³ M☉ of heavy radioactive nuclei.

## Context
This review extends the work of multiple prior experimental programs: LUNA underground measurements (e.g., Broggini et al., various years) for low-energy cross sections; the Trojan Horse Method developed by the AsFiN group; n_TOF measurements at CERN; the FOPI/LAND collaboration results on symmetry energy (Russo et al., 2011, 2016); and the ALICE collaboration's femtoscopy measurements (Acharya et al., 2018). It also references the theoretical framework of the hyperon puzzle (Bombaci, 2017) and the need for three-body forces to reconcile neutron star observations. The paper enables future work by identifying specific open questions: the cosmological lithium problem remains unsolved by nuclear solutions; the hyperon puzzle requires precise hypernuclear spectroscopy from JLab and J-PARC; and the density dependence of the symmetry energy above 2.5 ρ₀ remains largely unconstrained, motivating higher-energy heavy-ion experiments.
