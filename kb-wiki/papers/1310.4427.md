# Neutron-skin effects in isobaric yield ratio for mirror nuclei in statistical abrasion-ablation model

> \item[Purpose] We suppose that the IYR(m) depends on the neutron-skin thickness ($δ_{np}$) of the projectile, and check the idea of whether the neutron-skin thickness effects can fully explain the systematic dependence of the IYR(m). \item[Methods] A modified statistical abrasion-ablation model is used to calculate the reactions induced by projectiles of three series. \item[Results] The IYR(m) shows a distribution of a linear part in the small-$x$ fragments, and a nonlinear part in the large-$x$ fragments. The linear part of IYR(m) is fitted. (1) In the calcium isotopic reactions, the IYR(m) depends on the isospin or the volume of the projectile, but $δ_{np}$ greatly influences the nonlinear part of the IYR(m). The IYR(m) does not depend on the colliding source in reactions of small $b_{\mbox{max}}$ for the nonneutron-rich projectiles, and does not depend on the collision sources in reactions by the neutron-rich projectiles; (2) In reactions of the $A = 45$ isobars, though IYR(m) depends on the isospin of projectile, IYR(m) shows small dependence on isospin if $δ_{np} > 0$; (3) In the reactions of projectiles having similar $δ_{np}$, the IYR(m) in the small mass fragments show no dependence on the volume and the isospin of the projectile when the mass of the projectile is relatively large. Specially, the dependence of IYR(m) on the mass of the isospin of the projectile vanishes when $δ_{np}\sim0.02$fm. \item[Conclusions] The linear and nonlinear parts of the IYR(m) are governed by the core and the surface (skin) of the projectile, respectively. The neutron-skin effects can well explain the systematic dependence of the IYR(m).

# Neutron-skin effects in isobaric yield ratio for mirror nuclei in statistical abrasion-ablation model

## Key claim

The systematic dependence of the isobaric yield ratio for mirror nuclei [IYR(m)] on the reaction system, previously attributed to volume or isospin effects, can be fully explained by the neutron-skin thickness (δₙₚ) of the projectile. The linear part of IYR(m) is governed by the core of the projectile, while the nonlinear part is governed by the surface (skin), and the dependence of IYR(m) on projectile mass and isospin vanishes when δₙₚ is approximately 0.02 fm.

## Method

A modified statistical abrasion-ablation (SAA) model was used to calculate fragment yields in 140A MeV reactions with a ⁹Be target, using Fermi-type density distributions for neutrons and protons with adjustable diffuseness parameters. Three series of projectiles were studied: (1) calcium isotopes from ³⁶Ca to ⁵⁶Ca with varying maximum impact parameter (bₘₐₓ) limitations; (2) A = 45 isobars with Z from 14 to 24; and (3) projectiles having similar δₙₚ values (approximately 0.02, 0.06, 0.09, 0.13, and 0.18 fm). The linear part of IYR(m) as a function of x = 2(Z-1)/A¹/³ was fitted to extract a_c/T and Δμ/T.

## Key numbers

- δₙₚ values for calcium projectiles ³⁶Ca, ⁴⁰Ca, ⁴⁴Ca, ⁴⁸Ca, and ⁵⁶Ca are -0.117, -0.05, 0.005, 0.053, and 0.129 fm, respectively
- δₙₚ values for A = 45 isobars with Z = 14, 16, 18, 20, 22, and 24 are 0.179, 0.127, 0.073, 0.018, -0.038, and -0.096 fm, respectively
- The dependence of IYR(m) on projectile mass and isospin vanishes when δₙₚ is approximately 0.02 fm
- For the ⁶⁵₂₉ nucleus, δₙₚ = 0.02 fm when fₙ = 1 and 0.85 fm when fₙ = 2
- bₘₐₓ was varied from 1 fm to 11 fm in steps of 2 fm for the calcium isotopic reactions
- Projectile masses in the similar-δₙₚ study range from 25 to 86, with I' from 0.1 to 0.4

## Context

This work extends the study of IYR(m) methods previously applied to symmetry energy investigations by Huang (2010) and Ma (2011, 2012, 2013), and addresses contradictions between the standard grand-canonical and canonical statistical ensembles (SGC/CSE) theories of Souza (2012) and earlier SAA model results of Ma (2013). The paper resolves the disagreement by showing that neutron-skin effects, rather than pure volume or isospin effects, govern the systematic behavior of IYR(m). This enables more reliable extraction of a_c/T and Δμ/T from experimental data by accounting for the projectile's density distribution, and suggests that finite effects proposed in SGC/CSE results are inadequate to explain experimental observations.
