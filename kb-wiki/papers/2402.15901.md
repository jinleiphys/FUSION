# Ab initio description of monopole resonances in light- and medium-mass nuclei: II. Ab initio PGCM calculations in $^{46}$Ti, $^{28}$Si and $^{24}$Mg

> Giant resonances (GRs) are a striking manifestation of collective motions in atomic nuclei. The present paper is the second in a series of four dedicated to the use of the projected generator coordinate method (PGCM) for the ab initio determination of the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (GMR) in closed- and open-shell mid-mass nuclei. While the first paper was dedicated to quantifying various uncertainty sources, the present paper focuses on the first applications to three doubly-open shell nuclei, namely $^{46}$Ti, $^{28}$Si and $^{24}$Mg. In particular, the goal is to investigate from an ab initio standpoint (i) the coupling of the GMR with the giant quadrupole resonance (GQR) in intrinsically-deformed nuclei, (ii) the possible impact of shape coexistence and shape mixing on the GMR, (iii) the GMR based on shape isomers and (iv) the impact of anharmonic effects on the monopole response. The latter is studied by comparing PGCM results to those obtained via the quasi-particle random phase approximation (QRPA), the traditional many-body approach to giant resonances, performed in a consistent setting. Eventually, PGCM results for sd-shell nuclei are in excellent agreement with experimental data, which is attributed to the capacity of the PGCM to capture the important fragmentation of the monopole response in light, intrinsically-deformed systems. Still, the comparison to data in $^{28}$Si and $^{24}$Mg illustrates the challenge (and the potential benefit) of extracting unambiguous experimental information.

## Key claim

The projected generator coordinate method (PGCM) can capture the fragmentation of the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (GMR) in light, intrinsically-deformed nuclei, including the coupling between the GMR and the giant quadrupole resonance (GQR), but the comparison to experimental data in ²⁸Si and ²⁴Mg illustrates the challenge of extracting unambiguous experimental information.

## Method

Two-dimensional PGCM calculations were performed mixing constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) states with axial symmetry, using the root-mean-square radius and the axial mass quadrupole deformation parameter β₂ as generator coordinates. A chiral effective field theory Hamiltonian at N³LO with two- and three-nucleon interactions was evolved via similarity renormalization group transformation to λ=1.88 fm⁻¹, with three-body forces approximated via rank reduction. The QRPA was performed at the HFB minimum using the quasi-particle finite amplitude method for comparison.

## Key numbers

- ¹⁶O: PGCM GMR centroid at 21.13 MeV, compared to experimental centroid at 22.26 MeV (Lui 2001)
- ⁴⁶Ti: PGCM centroid at 19.40 MeV, compared to experimental centroid at 18.66 MeV (Tokimoto 2006)
- ⁴⁶Ti: HFB minimum at β₂=0.27 (prolate), oblate shape isomer at β₂=-0.15 located 6.1 MeV above the minimum
- ⁴⁶Ti: GMR peak (b) at 20.4 MeV; low-energy GMR component (a) at 16.5 MeV; GQR peak (d) at 17.1 MeV
- ⁴⁶Ti: B(E2) transition between 0⁺ state (a) and 2⁺ state (d) of 294 fm⁴MeV⁻¹
- ²⁸Si: oblate HFB minimum at β₂=-0.45, prolate isomer at β₂=0.60, separated by a barrier at 16.5 MeV above the oblate minimum
- ²⁸Si: GMR built on prolate-shape isomer has low-energy component at 12.2 MeV and high-energy component at higher energy; B(E2) between 0⁺(a) and GQR of 1408 fm⁴MeV⁻¹

## Context

This work extends the PGCM framework from Paper I (Porro 2024), which quantified uncertainty sources, to first applications in doubly-open shell nuclei ⁴⁶Ti, ²⁸Si, and ²⁴Mg. The study investigates GMR-GQR coupling in intrinsically-deformed systems, building on fluid-dynamical models (Nishizaki 1985) and prior work on deformation effects (Peru 2008, Gambacurta 2020). The comparison between PGCM and QRPA results enables evaluation of anharmonic effects, while the treatment of shape coexistence in ²⁸Si extends theoretical studies (Darai 2012) and experimental observations (Kelly 1998, Jenkins 2012). The results enable future systematic ab initio studies of giant resonances in open-shell nuclei across the nuclear chart.
