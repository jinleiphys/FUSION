# Cluster Formation and The Virial Equation of State of Low-Density Nuclear Matter

> We present the virial equation of state of low-density nuclear matter composed of neutrons, protons and alpha particles. The virial equation of state is model-independent, and therefore sets a benchmark for all nuclear equations of state at low densities. We calculate the second virial coefficients for nucleon-nucleon, nucleon-alpha and alpha-alpha interactions directly from the relevant binding energies and scattering phase shifts. The virial approach systematically takes into account contributions from bound nuclei and the resonant continuum, and consequently provides a framework to include strong-interaction corrections to nuclear statistical equilibrium models. The virial coefficients are used to make model-independent predictions for a variety of properties of nuclear matter over a range of densities, temperatures and compositions. Our results provide constraints on the physics of the neutrinosphere in supernovae. The resulting alpha particle concentration differs from all equations of state currently used in supernova simulations. Finally, the virial equation of state greatly improves our conceptual understanding of low-density nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The virial expansion provides a model-independent benchmark for the equation of state of low-density nuclear matter, but the approach is limited to densities where the fugacity is small and the system remains in a gas phase with no phase transition; the alpha particle concentration predicted by this method differs from all equations of state currently used in supernova simulations.

## Method

The authors calculate the second virial coefficients for nucleon-nucleon, nucleon-alpha, and alpha-alpha interactions directly from experimental binding energies and elastic scattering phase shifts, using the Nijmegen partial wave analysis for NN phase shifts, Arndt-Roper fits and Amos-Karataglidis optical model predictions for N-alpha phase shifts, and Afzal et al. plus Bacher et al. data for alpha-alpha phase shifts. They then construct the grand-canonical partition function expanded to second order in the fugacities of neutrons, protons, and alpha particles, with the alpha fugacity related to the nucleon fugacities through chemical equilibrium. The pressure, densities, entropy, and energy are derived from this partition function, and the equation of state is generated in tabular form for a range of neutron fugacities at fixed proton fraction and temperature.

## Key numbers

- Neutron matter virial coefficient b_n at T=4 MeV: 0.307 (full calculation), compared to 0.434 from S-wave scattering lengths only
- pn virial coefficient b_pn at T=4 MeV: 3.19 (full), compared to 3.40 from S-wave scattering lengths and deuteron binding energy only
- N-alpha virial coefficient b_alpha n at T=4 MeV: 2.73 (full), dominated by the P_3/2 resonance with scattering length a_P_3/2 = -62.95 fm^3
- Alpha-alpha virial coefficient b_alpha at T=4 MeV: 7.44 (full), compared to 6.02 from S- and D-waves only
- Deuteron binding energy E_d = 2.22 MeV, alpha particle binding energy E_alpha = 28.3 MeV
- Nuclear saturation density n_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}; neutrinosphere density n ~ 10^11 g/cm^3 ~ 1/1000 n_0 at T ~ 4 MeV

## Context

This work extends the virial expansion approach previously applied to pure neutron matter by Horowitz and Schwenk (2005) and to Fermi gases near Feshbach resonances by Ho and Mueller (2004), providing a systematic framework to include strong-interaction corrections to nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) models. The virial equation of state sets a benchmark that can be compared with microscopic calculations such as variational (Friedman and Pandharipande, 1981; Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall, 1998) and Brueckner approaches, as well as with phenomenological equations of state used in supernova simulations like the Lattimer-Swesty (1991) and Shen-Toki-Oyamatsu-Sumiyoshi (1998) models. The results enable model-independent predictions for the neutrinosphere region in core-collapse supernovae and provide a check for lattice effective field theory simulations of neutron matter.
