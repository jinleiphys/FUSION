# Exploring Shell Evolution and N = 40 Magicity in Light-Mass Nuclei with Relativistc Mean Field Approach

> We employ the relativistic mean-field (RMF) approach with NL3 parameters to study shell and sub-shell closures in the isotopic chains of Cl, Ar, K, Ca, Sc, Ti, V, and Cr nuclei. By analyzing nuclear bulk properties, binding energy, charge radii, two-neutron separation energies, deformation parameters ($β_2$), and single-particle levels we trace the evolution of magic numbers. Our results highlight the single-particle energy levels to examine nuclear shell closure and the occupancy of individual nucleon orbitals. A comprehensive picture of N = 34 shell closure is particularly prominent in the isotopic chains of Cl, Ar, and Ti nuclei, where the magicity associated with this number remains evident across several isotopes. In contrast, the N = 40 exhibits a more robust and widespread manifestation across all the examined nuclei, indicating that its shell closure is less sensitive to the specific isotopic environment and more universally applicable across the given nuclear systems. To further validate these closures, we apply the coherent density fluctuation model (CDFM) to assess isospin-dependent observables, such as the symmetry energy and its surface and volume components. A systematic analysis of the symmetry energy, computed using the relativistic mean-field (RMF) approach, maps the evolution of the shell structure at $N = 20$ and 28, supports sub-magicity at $N = 34$, and strongly suggests a shell closure at $N = 40$, reflected consistently in both bulk and surface properties. The interplay between shell structure and nuclear deformation remains a central topic of research, and future theoretical and experimental studies will continue to shed light on the complex behaviour of these fascinating systems.

## Key claim

The paper claims that N = 40 exhibits a robust and widespread shell closure across all examined isotopic chains (Cl through Cr), while N = 34 shows sub-shell closure that is prominent only in Cl, Ar, and Ti isotopes; these conclusions are drawn from relativistic mean-field calculations with the NL3 parameter set, not from experimental measurements, and the authors note that the N = 34 magicity is not supported by data from Ti and V isotopes in prior work.

## Method

The authors employed the axially deformed relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with the NL3 parameter set and constant-gap BCS pairing to calculate ground-state properties (binding energy, charge radii, two-neutron separation energies, quadrupole deformation parameters, and single-particle energy levels) for isotopic chains of Cl, Ar, K, Ca, Sc, Ti, V, and Cr nuclei. They then applied the coherent density fluctuation model (CDFM) to compute the nuclear symmetry energy and its volume and surface components from the RMF-derived density distributions, using these isospin-dependent observables as complementary probes of shell structure.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per nucleon peaks near mass numbers A = 40 and A = 48 for Ca and K isotopes, corresponding to magic numbers N = 20 and N = 28.
- Two-neutron separation energy curves show discontinuities or kinks at neutron numbers N = 20, N = 28, N = 34, and N = 40 across the studied isotopic chains.
- A distinct shell gap at N = 34 is observed in single-particle energy levels, with the gap becoming more pronounced in heavier isotopes such as Ti (Z = 22), V (Z = 23), and Cr (Z = 24) compared to lighter nuclei like Cl (Z = 17) and Ar (Z = 18).
- A substantial energy gap at N = 40 is highlighted in single-particle energy levels, particularly in Ca (Z = 20) and Sc (Z = 21) isotopes.
- The charge radius shows a flattening or reduced rate of increase around N = 34 for Cl, Ti, V, and Cr isotopes, and around N = 40 for several nuclei.
- The pairing gaps used in the constant-gap BCS approach are given by the formulas Δ_p = 5.72 B_s e^{0.118I - 8.12I^2} / Z^{1/3} and Δ_n = 5.72 B_s e^{-0.118I - 8.12I^2} / A^{1/3}, where I = (N-Z)/(N+Z) and B_s = 1.

## Context

This work extends prior theoretical studies of shell evolution in neutron-rich nuclei, particularly addressing the controversies surrounding N = 34 and N = 40 magicity. The authors note that shell model calculations by Honma et al. (2005) suggested stronger magicity at N = 34 due to tensor forces, but data from Fornal et al. (2004) on Ti did not support N = 34 magicity, and Iimura et al. (2023) found no confirmation of N = 34 shell closure in Ti and V isotopes from two-neutron shell gaps. For N = 40, the study builds on work showing magicity in Ni (Brodsky et al., 1995) but also notes contradictory predictions for Ca (Nakamura et al., 2008; Nakamura et al., 2010; Terasaki et al., 2006) and evidence that Cr isotopes exhibit an island of inversion at N = 40 (Mouginot et al., 2018). The use of CDFM to extract symmetry energy components as shell-closure indicators follows the methodology of Gaidarov et al. (2011, 2012), Bhuyan et al. (2018), and Yadav et al. (2022). The results enable future experimental studies to test the predicted N = 40 shell closure across a wider range of isotopic chains and to investigate the interplay between shell structure and deformation in neutron-rich light-mass nuclei.
