# Equilibration of quantum many-body fast neutrino flavor oscillations

> Neutrino gases are expected to form in high density astrophysical environments, and accurately modeling their flavor evolution is critical to understanding such environments. In this work we study a simplified model of such a dense neutrino gas in the regime for which neutrino-neutrino coherent forward scattering is the dominant mechanism contributing to the flavor evolution. We show evidence that the generic potential induced by this effect is non-integrable and that the statistics of its energy level spaces are in good agreement with the Wigner surmise. We also find that individual neutrinos rapidly entangle with all of the others present which results in an equilibration of the flavor content of individual neutrinos. We show that the average neutrino flavor content can be predicted utilizing a thermodynamic partition function. A random phase approximation to the evolution gives a simple picture of this equilibration. In the case of neutrinos and antineutrinos, processes like $ν_e {\barν}_e \leftrightarrows ν_μ{\barν_μ} $ yield a rapid equilibrium satisfying $n( ν_e) n({\bar ν}_e) = n( ν_μ) n({\bar ν}_μ) = n( ν_τ) n({\bar ν}_τ)$ in addition to the standard lepton number conservation in regimes where off-diagonal vacuum oscillations are small compared to $ν-ν$ interactions.

## Key claim

The paper provides evidence that the generic neutrino-neutrino coherent forward scattering Hamiltonian is non-integrable and that individual neutrinos rapidly entangle with all others, leading to equilibration of flavor content that can be predicted using a thermodynamic partition function, but only in regimes where off-diagonal vacuum oscillations are small compared to neutrino-neutrino interactions.

## Method

The authors construct a quantum many-body Hamiltonian for N neutrinos with all-to-all Heisenberg-like interactions from coherent forward scattering, using randomly drawn velocity vectors to break simplifying symmetries. They diagonalize the Hamiltonian in angular momentum subspaces for up to 16 neutrinos, compute level spacing statistics, and compare the distribution of ratios of consecutive level spacings to the Wigner surmise (GOE) and Poisson distributions. They then evolve product state initial conditions by solving the many-body Schrödinger equation up to t = 10^3 μ^{-1} and compare time-averaged expectation values of the flavor operator σ₃,i to predictions from an energy mixed state distribution and a grand-canonical partition function fitted to conserved quantities.

## Key numbers

- Neutrino density estimate: n_ν ≈ 6.6 × 10⁻⁷ fm⁻³ for a core-collapse supernova with luminosity L = 10⁵³ erg/sec, radius R = 50 km, and average energy Ē = 10 MeV.
- Interaction scale: μ = √2 G_F n_ν ≈ 1 cm⁻¹ for a typical supernova flux at ~50 km radius.
- For N = 16 neutrinos in the j = 2, m = 2 subspace, the average ratio of consecutive level spacings for the generic Hamiltonian H_νν is r̄ = 1.7512, compared to r̄ = 3.8224 for the axially symmetric integrable case.
- Average ratios for the generic Hamiltonian across j = 0 to 4 subspaces range from r̄ = 1.6651 (j = 0) to r̄ = 1.8508 (j = 4), while for the axially symmetric case they range from r̄ = 3.6091 (j = 3) to r̄ = 3.8764 (j = 4).
- The equilibration time T_S (when one-body von Neumann entropy exceeds 0.95) and the turning point time T_{P₃} are studied for system sizes N = 10 to 16.

## Context

This work extends the study of fast neutrino flavor oscillations in dense astrophysical environments, building on the quantum many-body formalism used by Bell (2003), Sawyer (2004), Friedland (2006), and Pehlivan (2011). It contrasts with prior work that imposed simplifying symmetries such as uniform coupling or axial symmetry, which yield integrable Hamiltonians diagonalizable by algebraic Bethe ansatz as shown by Patwardhan (2019) and in Phys. Rev. D 107 (2023). The demonstration of non-integrability and thermalization enables the use of statistical mechanics to predict flavor content in supernovae and neutron star mergers, potentially impacting models of r-process nucleosynthesis.
