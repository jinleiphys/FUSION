# Dynamical Coupled-Channels Analysis at EBAC

> The status of dynamical coupled-channel analysis at Excited Baryon Analysis Center (EBAC) of Jefferson Laboratory is reported.

## Key claim

The dynamical coupled-channel analysis at EBAC aims to extract nucleon excited state (N*) parameters from meson production data by solving coupled integral equations that account for both unitarity and short-range reaction mechanisms, enabling a more direct interpretation of N* parameters in terms of quark-gluon substructure compared to on-shell K-matrix models that avoid explicit treatment of the short-range region.

## Method

The analysis solves coupled integral equations (Eqs. 1-2) for meson-baryon transition amplitudes, derived from a Hamiltonian with non-resonant meson-exchange interactions (v22, v23, v33) and resonant terms from bare N* states. The non-resonant amplitudes are computed by solving Eq. 5 with a driving term (Eq. 9) that includes one-particle-exchange and three-body unitarity cut contributions, using a Spline-function method to handle singular matrix elements. The resonant amplitudes are generated from dressed N* vertices and propagators (Eqs. 11-13), with parameters first determined by fitting πN elastic scattering data from SAID up to 2 GeV, then refined by comparing predicted differential cross sections and polarization asymmetries with original data.

## Key numbers

- The analysis fits πN elastic scattering amplitudes from SAID up to 2 GeV (center-of-mass energy)
- One or two bare N* states are included in each of S, P, D, and F partial waves
- The πN invariant mass distribution of πN → ππN is shown at W = 1880 MeV
- The γp → π+n fit extends up to W = 2 GeV
- Matrix elements of the one-particle-exchange term Z(E)πΔ,πΔ are computed at k' = 300 MeV/c and E = 1.88 GeV for L = L' = 1, J = 5/2, T = 1/2

## Context

This work extends the dynamical coupled-channel model developed in Ref. msl (Matsuyama, Sato, Lee, 2007) by applying it at the Excited Baryon Analysis Center (EBAC), established in January 2006. The approach contrasts with on-shell K-matrix models (such as those by Said and collaborators) that avoid explicit treatment of short-range reaction mechanisms, and instead aims to enable direct interpretation of N* parameters in terms of hadron structure calculations like the constituent quark model (Capstick, 1992) and Dyson-Schwinger Equation models (Roberts, 2007), with future connections to Lattice QCD calculations.
