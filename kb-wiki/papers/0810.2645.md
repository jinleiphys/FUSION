# Coulomb corrected eikonal description of the breakup of halo nuclei

> The eikonal description of breakup reactions diverges because of the Coulomb interaction between the projectile and the target. This divergence is due to the adiabatic, or sudden, approximation usually made, which is incompatible with the infinite range of the Coulomb interaction. A correction for this divergence is analysed by comparison with the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation, which is derived without the adiabatic approximation. The correction consists in replacing the first-order term of the eikonal Coulomb phase by the first-order of the perturbation theory. This allows taking into account both nuclear and Coulomb interactions on the same footing within the computationally efficient eikonal model. Excellent results are found for the dissociation of 11Be on lead at 69 MeV/nucleon. This Coulomb Corrected Eikonal approximation provides a competitive alternative to more elaborate reaction models for investigating breakup of three-body projectiles at intermediate and high energies.

## Key claim

The Coulomb Corrected Eikonal (CCE) approximation, which replaces the first-order term of the eikonal Coulomb phase with the first-order of perturbation theory, resolves the divergence in the eikonal description of breakup reactions caused by the adiabatic approximation's incompatibility with the infinite-range Coulomb interaction, and provides results in excellent agreement with the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) for the dissociation of 11Be on lead at 69 MeV/nucleon.

## Method

The authors model the breakup of a two-body projectile (core plus fragment) impinging on a target using a three-body Schrodinger equation. The CCE correction replaces the first-order term of the eikonal Coulomb phase (which diverges due to the adiabatic approximation) with the first-order term of perturbation theory, which does not diverge because it avoids the adiabatic approximation. The corrected eikonal phase is then used to compute breakup amplitudes and cross sections, which are compared to results from the DEA (which solves the eikonal equation without the adiabatic approximation) for the breakup of 11Be on lead and carbon targets at around 70 MeV/nucleon.

## Key numbers

- Breakup of 11Be on lead at 69 MeV/nucleon: the CCE energy distribution is nearly superimposed on the DEA reference calculation, with only a slight overestimation at low energy attributed to the far-field approximation used in the perturbative correction.
- For the usual eikonal model on lead, a cutoff at b_max = 71 fm (from the condition b_max = hbar v / (2|E_0|)) is required to avoid divergence; this yields an energy distribution that is too small at low energy and too large at high energy compared to the DEA.
- For first-order perturbation theory on lead, a lower cutoff at b_min = 15 fm is chosen to fit the DEA energy distribution at the maximum; this yields a cross section that decreases too quickly with energy.
- Convergence of the CCE multipole expansion for the parallel-momentum distribution on lead requires lambda_max = 8 to achieve differences of about 0.5% from lambda_max = 12; lambda_max = 4 leaves a 4% difference at the maximum.
- For the breakup of 11Be on carbon at 69 MeV/nucleon, the CCE results are also in good agreement with the DEA (as described in the text, though specific numerical values for carbon are not given in the truncated text).

## Context

This work extends the eikonal model by correcting its divergence for Coulomb-dominated breakup reactions, a problem previously identified by Margueron, Bonaccorso, and Brink (2003) and developed by Abu-Ibrahim and Suzuki (2004). The CCE is compared to the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) of Capel, Baye, and Goldstein (2005, 2006), which solves the eikonal equation without the adiabatic approximation and agrees well with RIKEN experiments by Fukuda et al. (2004). The CCE provides a computationally efficient alternative (a factor of 100 faster than the DEA) for simulating breakup of three-body (Borromean) projectiles at intermediate and high energies, where more elaborate models like CDCC or four-body CDCC are very time-consuming.
