# Dense Stellar Matter and Structure of Neutron Stars

> After giving an overview of the history and idea of neutron stars, I shall introduce, in part one of my lectures, a representative collection of models for the equation of state of superdense neutron star matter, which account for various physical possibilities concerning the unknown behavior of such matter. Part two of my lectures deal with the construction of models of static as well as rapidly rotating neutron stars, and an investigation of the cooling behavior of such objects. Both is performed in the framework of Einstein's theory of general relativity. For this purpose the broad collection of models for the equation of state, derived in part one, will be used as an input. Various computed neutron star properties (e.g., masses, radii, moments of inertia, red and blueshifts, limiting rotational periods, cooling behavior) will be compared with the body of observed pulsar data. Finally, part three deals with an investigation of the properties of stars made up of absolutely stable strange quark matter. Their properties are compared with those of their nonstrange counterparts, i.e., neutron stars.

## Key claim

This lecture series presents a broad collection of models for the equation of state of superdense neutron star matter, but the authors emphasize that the physical behavior of matter at extreme densities in stellar cores is "rather uncertain" and the associated equation of state is "only poorly known," with models differing considerably depending on the many-body technique, nucleon-nucleon interaction, and composition assumptions.

## Method

The authors derive equations of state using both non-relativistic variational methods (with phenomenological two- and three-nucleon potentials fit to scattering data) and relativistic nuclear field theory based on meson-exchange models solved via the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach and the T-matrix (ladder) approximation. These equations of state are then used as input to construct models of static and rapidly rotating neutron stars within Einstein's general relativity, solving the field equations subject to conditions of electric charge neutrality and beta equilibrium among baryons (including hyperons and delta resonances) and leptons.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear matter density: ρ₀ = 0.15 nucleons/fm³, corresponding to a mass density of 2.5 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³
- Fastest observed pulsar rotational period: P = 1.6 ms (about 620 rotations per second)
- Number of baryons forming a neutron star: A ≈ 10⁵⁷
- Transition to quark matter in the G^DCM1_B180 equation of state sets in at density ε = 2.3 ε₀ (where ε₀ = 140 MeV/fm³)
- The mixed phase of baryons and quarks ends at ε ≈ 15 ε₀ for the G^DCM1_B180 equation of state
- Bag constant used for quark matter transition: B¹/⁴ = 180 MeV, placing the energy per baryon of strange matter at 1100 MeV (above the 930 MeV per nucleon in ⁵⁶Fe)
- Compression moduli of relativistic equations of state range from K = 186 MeV (Λ⁰⁰_Bonn+HV) to K = 376 MeV (HFV)
- Binding energies of relativistic equations of state at saturation range from -11.9 MeV (Λ⁰⁰_Bonn+HV) to -16.3 MeV (G_300 and G^π_300)

## Context

This work extends the program of relating nuclear equation of state models to observed neutron star properties, building on prior approaches by Bethe (1974), Wiringa (1988), and Friedman and Pandharipande (1981) for non-relativistic models, and by Glendenning (1985, 1989, 1991) and Weber (1989, 1990, 1991) for relativistic field-theoretical treatments. The authors specifically note that the proper treatment of the transition from confined hadronic matter to quark matter, accounting for two chemical potentials under baryon and charge conservation, was only performed by Glendenning (1991), correcting earlier inconsistent treatments. The work enables comparison of computed neutron star properties (masses, radii, moments of inertia, redshifts, limiting rotational periods, cooling behavior) with observed pulsar data to constrain the behavior of matter at supernuclear densities.
