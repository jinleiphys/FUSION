# Initial, effective, and kinetic freeze-out temperatures from transverse momentum spectra in high energy proton(deuteron)-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions

> The transverse momentum spectra of charged particles produced in proton(deuteron)-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions at high energies are analyzed by the Hagedorn thermal model and the standard distribution in terms of multi-component. The experimental data measured in central and peripheral gold-gold (Au-Au) and deuteron-gold ($d$-Au) collisions by the PHENIX Collaboration at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC), as well as in central and peripheral lead-lead (Pb-Pb) and proton-lead ($p$-Pb) collisions by the ALICE Collaboration at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) are fitted by the two models. The initial, effective, and kinetic freeze-out temperatures are then extracted from the fitting to the transverse momentum spectra. It is shown that the initial temperature is larger than the effective temperature, and the effective temperature is larger than the kinetic freeze-out temperature. The three types of temperatures in central collisions are comparable with those in peripheral collisions, and those at the LHC are comparable with those at the RHIC.

## Key claim

The initial, effective, and kinetic freeze-out temperatures extracted from transverse momentum spectra using the Hagedorn thermal model and the standard distribution show that the initial temperature is larger than the effective temperature, and the effective temperature is larger than the kinetic freeze-out temperature; however, the authors note that these temperatures are model dependent due to the use of different functions or distributions to fit the spectra, and the absolute values differ depending on the method used.

## Method

The authors fitted transverse momentum spectra of charged pions, kaons, and (anti)protons from Au-Au and d-Au collisions at RHIC (sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV) and Pb-Pb and p-Pb collisions at LHC (sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV respectively) using a multi-component Hagedorn thermal model and a multi-component standard (Boltzmann) distribution. The effective temperature was extracted from the fits, and the kinetic freeze-out temperature was obtained as the intercept of the linear relationship between effective temperature and particle mass. The initial temperature was derived from the effective temperature by removing the contribution of transverse flow.

## Key numbers

- For central (0-5%) Au-Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, the effective temperature T_1 for pi+ was 0.267 +/- 0.009 GeV (first component) from the Hagedorn model fit.
- For peripheral (60-92%) Au-Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, the effective temperature T_1 for pi+ was 0.238 +/- 0.008 GeV (first component) from the Hagedorn model fit.
- For central (0-5%) Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV, the effective temperature T_1 for pi++pi- was 0.236 +/- 0.012 GeV (first component) from the Hagedorn model fit.
- For peripheral (70-80%) Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV, the effective temperature T_1 for pi++pi- was 0.272 +/- 0.013 GeV (first component) from the Hagedorn model fit.
- The kinetic freeze-out temperature T_01 (intercept from Hagedorn model) for central Au-Au collisions was 0.294 +/- 0.004 GeV for positive particles and 0.385 +/- 0.003 GeV for negative particles.
- The kinetic freeze-out temperature T_01 for peripheral Au-Au collisions was 0.230 +/- 0.005 GeV for positive particles and 0.232 +/- 0.004 GeV for negative particles.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of freeze-out temperatures by Waqas and Liu (2019) using an alternative method to extract kinetic freeze-out temperature from the intercept of effective temperature versus particle mass, building on the blast-wave model approaches of Schnedermann et al. (1993) and the Tsallis statistics methods of Cleymans et al. (2013). The results confirm that kinetic freeze-out temperatures in central collisions are slightly larger than or equal to those in peripheral collisions, and that temperatures at LHC and RHIC energies are comparable, which enables systematic studies of temperature dependence on collision centrality, system size, and energy for mapping the phase diagram of strongly interacting matter.
