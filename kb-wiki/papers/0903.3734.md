# The Fate of the Initial State Perturbations in Heavy Ion Collisions

> Heavy ion collisions at RHIC are well described by the (nearly ideal) hydrodynamics. In the present paper we study propagation of perturbations induced by moving charges (jets) on top of the expanding fireball, using hydrodynamics and (dual) magnetohydrodynamics. Two experimentally observed structures, called a "cone" and a "hard ridge", have been discovered in dihadron correlation function with large-$p_t$ trigger, while "soft ridge" is a similar structure seen without hard trigger. All three can be viewed as traces left by a moving charge in matter, on top of overall expansion. A puzzle is why those perturbations are apparently rather well preserved at the time of the fireball freezeout. We study two possible solutions to it: (i) a "wave-splitting" acoustic option and (ii) a "metastable electric flux tubes". In the first case we show that rapidly variable speed of sound under certain conditions leads to secondary sound waves, which are at freezeout time closer to the original location and have larger intensities than the first wave. In the latter case we rely on (dual) magnetohydrodynamics, which also predicts two cones or cylinders of the waves. We also briefly discuss metastable electric flux tubes in the near-$T_c$ phase and their relation to clustering data.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the puzzling persistence of small-scale correlation structures (cone, hard ridge, soft ridge) in heavy ion collisions at RHIC through the freezeout time can be explained either by secondary sound waves generated by a rapidly varying speed of sound near the phase transition, or by metastable electric flux tubes described by dual magnetohydrodynamics, but notes that neither solution is definitively established.

## Method

The author studies the propagation of small perturbations on top of an expanding Hubble-like fireball background using two theoretical frameworks: (i) ideal hydrodynamics with a time-dependent speed of sound, solving for acoustic wave propagation in a flat Freedman-Robertson-Walker metric; and (ii) dual magnetohydrodynamics (DMHD) for the near-Tc region where electric fields remain unscreened while magnetic fields are screened, analyzing Alfven wave propagation in a magnetized medium. The analysis focuses on the "wave-splitting" phenomenon where secondary sound waves are generated when the speed of sound changes rapidly, and on the formation of metastable electric flux tubes in the magnetic-dominated (M) phase near Tc.

## Key numbers

- The Hubble expansion rate for the fireball is approximately H ≈ 0.08 fm^{-1}, approximately space and time independent
- The sound horizon at freezeout proper time τ_f ∼ 10-15 fm/c is approximately 6 fm, with speed of sound varying between c_s = 1/√3 ≈ 0.58 in QGP and about 0.3 at its minimum near T_c
- The magnetic screening mass at T → T_c is M_M ≈ 3 T_c, while the electric screening mass M_E → 0 near T_c
- The observed cone angle is in the range θ_M = 1.2-1.4 radians, giving a wave velocity ⟨v_wave⟩ = cos(θ_M) ≈ 0.2
- The transverse particle density threshold for ridge disappearance is ρ_c = (3/2)dN_ch/dy/S ≈ 3
- The radius of a spot at freezeout is restricted to R(τ_freezeout) < 3 fm from azimuthal width measurements
- The initial stage proper time is τ ∼ 1/Q_s ∼ 0.2 fm/c with Q_s ∼ 1 GeV at RHIC
- The freezeout temperature ranges from T_c ≈ 170 MeV in peripheral collisions down to T_f ≈ 90 MeV for central collisions

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamical description of heavy ion collisions at RHIC, which was shown to agree well with data by Hirano (2004), Nonaka (2006), and others, to the propagation of small perturbations. It addresses a puzzle identified in experimental observations of dihadron correlations by STAR and PHENIX collaborations, where the "cone" (discovered by STAR and PHENIX), "hard ridge" (reported by Putschke), and "soft ridge" (found by STAR collaboration in 2005) appear better preserved at freezeout than naive sound horizon estimates predict. The paper builds on prior work by Casalderrey-Solana and Shuryak (2005) on conical flow and wave splitting, and on the "magnetic scenario" for near-Tc QCD developed by Liao and Shuryak (2007, 2008) and Chernodub (2006). The analysis enables future experimental tests at lower RHIC energies and LHC to distinguish between the acoustic and dual magnetohydrodynamic explanations.
