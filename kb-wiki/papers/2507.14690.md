# Toward scalable quantum computations of atomic nuclei

> We solve the nuclear two-body and three-body bound states via quantum simulations of pionless effective field theory on a lattice in position space. While the employed lattice remains small, the usage of local Hamiltonians including two- and three-body forces ensures that the number of Pauli terms scales linearly with increasing numbers of lattice sites. We use an adaptive ansatz grown from unitary coupled cluster theory to parametrize the ground states of the deuteron and $^3$He, compute their corresponding energies, and analyze the scaling of the required computational resources. Our quantum simulations reproduce exact benchmarks for $^2$H and $^3$He within 100 keV, requiring at most 30 layers in the ansatz and thus resulting in modest circuit depths. Additionally, we find the number of shots required to reach a given precision scales linearly in the lattice size and more mildly in the system size. Based on the agreement with exact benchmarks and mild scaling, we conclude that this can be an efficient, scalable approach for quantum computations of nuclear ground states, particularly to prepare initial states for quantum phase estimation or other filtering algorithms.

# Toward scalable quantum computations of atomic nuclei

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that quantum simulations of nuclear ground states using pionless effective field theory on a position-space lattice can reproduce exact benchmarks for the deuteron and $^3$He within 100 keV, but only on a small lattice ($L=2$) where finite-size corrections are substantial and the binding energies are about 12.9 and 29.5 MeV respectively, not the physical binding energies.

## Method

The authors construct a Hamiltonian from pionless effective field theory at leading order on a three-dimensional cubic lattice with nearest-neighbor kinetic terms and on-site two-body and three-body contact interactions. They use the adaptive derivative-assembled pseudo-Trotter variational quantum eigensolver (ADAPT-VQE) with an operator pool that includes correlated two-body hopping operators to parametrize ground states, and optimize the variational parameters via gradient descent. The quantum computations are simulated classically using PennyLane, with both exact simulations and noisy simulations using finite numbers of measurement shots.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy of the deuteron: exact diagonalization gives $-12.874$ MeV on a $L=2$ lattice with $a=2.0$ fm, $v=-9.0$, $w=10.8$
- Ground state energy of $^3$He: approximately 29.5 MeV binding energy on the same lattice (finite-size corrected)
- ADAPT-VQE reproduces exact benchmarks for $^2$H and $^3$He within 100 keV
- Maximum of 30 layers in the ansatz required, resulting in modest circuit depths
- Initial state for the deuteron has energy 62.206 MeV and fidelity 0.013 with the exact ground state
- For lattice sizes $L=2$–4, the number of shots required to reach $\varepsilon = 1$ MeV precision is on the order of $10^4$–$10^6$
- The deuteron computation on $L=2$ requires $n_q=16$ qubits; $^3$He requires $n_q=24$ qubits
- The Hamiltonian contains approximately $10n_q$ Pauli terms due to the short-range nature of the interactions

## Context

This work extends the lattice-based quantum computing approaches of Roggero (2018, 2020), Baroni (2022), and Watson (2023), who used two-dimensional lattices for two-nucleon systems or studied resource requirements, by presenting quantum computations of $A=2,3$ dynamical nucleon systems on three-dimensional lattices. The use of local Hamiltonians with short-range interactions ensures that the number of Pauli terms scales linearly with lattice sites, in contrast to Gray-code encoding approaches used by Di Matteo (2021), Siwach (2023), and Singh (2025) where Hamiltonian operators scale exponentially, or shell-model approaches used by Kiss (2022) where Hamiltonians in larger valence spaces can have hundreds of thousands of Pauli terms. This work enables scalable quantum computations of nuclear ground states, particularly for preparing initial states for quantum phase estimation or other filtering algorithms on future fault-tolerant quantum computers.
