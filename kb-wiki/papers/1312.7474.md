# Heavy Quark Diffusion in Strongly Coupled Anisotropic Plasmas

> We study the Langevin diffusion of a relativistic heavy quark in anisotropic strongly coupled theories in the local limit. Firstly, we use the axion space-dependent deformed anisotropic N=4 sYM, where the geometry anisotropy is always prolate, while the pressure anisotropy may be prolate or oblate. For motion along the anisotropic direction we find that the effective temperature for the quark can be larger than the heat bath temperature, in contrast to what happens in the isotropic theory. The longitudinal and transverse Langevin diffusion coefficients depend strongly on the anisotropy, the direction of motion and the transverse direction considered. We analyze the anisotropy effects to the coefficients and compare them to each other and to them of the isotropic theory. To examine the dependence of the coefficients on the type of the geometry, we consider another bottom-up anisotropic model. Changing the geometry from prolate to oblate, certain diffusion coefficients interchange their behaviors. In both anisotropic backgrounds we find cases that the transverse diffusion coefficient is larger than the longitudinal, but we find no negative excess noise.

## Key claim

The authors find that in strongly coupled anisotropic plasmas, the effective temperature experienced by a heavy quark moving along the anisotropic direction can exceed the heat bath temperature, reversing the inequality observed in isotropic theories; however, this inversion occurs only for sufficiently large anisotropy parameters and high quark velocities, and no negative excess noise is found in either anisotropic model studied.

## Method

The authors use holographic gauge/gravity duality to model a heavy quark as the endpoint of a trailing fundamental string moving with constant velocity through anisotropic strongly coupled plasmas. They compute the worldsheet Hawking temperature from the induced metric on the string worldsheet, then obtain the longitudinal and transverse Langevin diffusion coefficients via the membrane paradigm applied to fluctuations around the classical string solution, evaluating all quantities at the worldsheet horizon. Two anisotropic backgrounds are studied: a top-down axion-deformed N=4 super Yang-Mills theory with a prolate geometry, and a bottom-up anisotropic model that can exhibit both prolate and oblate geometries depending on its parameters.

## Key numbers

* For motion along the anisotropic direction at large anisotropy a/T ~ 10, the worldsheet temperature T_ws can exceed the heat bath temperature T for very large velocities, while T_ws for motion in the transverse plane remains always below T.
* The longitudinal Langevin coefficient for motion along the anisotropic direction, k_L^parallel, is always enhanced relative to the isotropic result; at small anisotropy a/T = 0.5, the ratio k_L^parallel / k_L^iso exceeds unity for all velocities.
* The ratio k_L^perp / k_T^(perp,parallel) violates the inequality k_L > k_T for small velocities at a/T = 0.55, and the range of violating velocities increases with larger anisotropy.
* The worldsheet temperatures for small anisotropy a/T are given analytically as T_ws^perp = T/sqrt(gamma) [1 - (a^2/T^2) * (1-gamma)(4+gamma-gamma^2+gamma^2(gamma+1)log(1/gamma+1))/(48 pi^2 gamma^2)] and similarly for T_ws^parallel, with both always less than T for v > 0.

## Context

This work extends the holographic study of heavy quark Langevin diffusion from isotropic plasmas (Herzog 2006, Gubser 2006, Casalderrey-Solana 2006) to anisotropic plasmas, building on the generic formalism developed by Giataganas (2013) which showed that the universal inequality k_L >= k_T can be violated in anisotropic theories. The authors use the top-down anisotropic N=4 sYM background of Mateos and Trancanelli (2011) and the bottom-up anisotropic model of Janiszewski and Witaszczyk (2012). The results enable future studies of how anisotropy affects heavy quark energy loss and momentum broadening in the quark-gluon plasma created in heavy ion collisions, particularly during the early anisotropic phase before isotropization.
