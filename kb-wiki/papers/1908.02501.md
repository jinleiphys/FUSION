# Formation and evaporation of strangelets during the merger of two compact stars

> We study the partial fragmentation of a strange quark star into strangelets during the process of merger of two strange quark stars. We discuss the fate of the fragments considering their possible evaporation into nucleons. We show that only a rather small amount of large size strangelets, ejected from the spiral arms in the post-merger, survives a total evaporation into nucleons. In this way we demonstrate that: 1) the density of strangelets in the galaxy is too low to trigger the conversion of all neutron stars into strange quark stars and it allows the co-existence of both types of compact objects; 2) the probability of direct detection of a strangelet is negligible and therefore its nondetection is compatible with the strange quark matter hypothesis; 3) most of the matter ejected during and after the merger of two strange quark stars evaporates into nucleons and therefore it can generate a kilonova-like signal.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that strangelets produced during mergers of two strange quark stars are unlikely to be detected or to convert neutron stars into quark stars, but only under the condition that fragmentation and subsequent evaporation are properly modeled rather than assuming all strangelets have the same mass; the authors show that approximately 93% of ejected quark matter evaporates into nucleons, leaving only large fragments that are too rare to pose a threat to the coexistence of both types of compact objects.

## Method

The authors analyzed trajectories of fluid elements from smoothed particle hydrodynamics simulations of strange quark star mergers (Bauswein 2009), focusing on matter ejected from spiral arms. They modeled turbulent fragmentation using Kolmogorov and Hinze scales with quark matter shear viscosity and surface tension, then performed Monte Carlo simulations of collisional fragmentation based on the Weber number. Finally, they solved coupled equations for strangelet evaporation and neutrino reheating to determine which fragment sizes survive.

## Key numbers

* Minimum droplet size from surface tension limit: baryon number \(A_H \approx 10^{26}\), corresponding to Hinze scale \(l_H \sim 5\times 10^{-5}\) cm
* Kolmogorov scale for turbulent fragmentation: \(l_K \sim 3\times 10^{-6}\) cm (using \(\alpha_s=0.1\), \(\rho=5\times 10^{14}\) g cm\(^{-3}\), \(T=10^{11}\) K)
* After evaporation, only about 7% of ejected baryonic mass survives as strangelets: \(\int A\,Q(A)\,dA \simeq 0.07 A_\mathrm{tot}\)
* Total number of strangelets in the Galaxy from QS-QS mergers: \(N_\mathrm{tot}^\mathrm{gal} = 3\times 10^{31}\)
* Flux of strangelets onto a proto-neutron star during its first months: \(F\,\tau_\mathrm{melt} \sim 5\times 10^{-4}\, v_{250}^{-1} \ll 1\)
* Total strangelet flux in the galaxy: \(dj_s/d\Omega \sim 8.4\times 10^{-30}\) cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\) sr\(^{-1}\)
* Evaporation ceases for ambient temperatures below approximately 5.6 MeV

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing objection by Madsen (1989) that if strange quark stars exist, strangelets from mergers would convert all neutron stars into quark stars, and the objection that the non-detection of strangelets contradicts the strange quark matter hypothesis of Witten (1984). The authors extend the evaporation formalism of Alcock (1985) and Madsen (1986) from cosmological contexts to the merger scenario, using merger simulations by Bauswein (2009). The results enable the coexistence of neutron stars and strange quark stars, and show that kilonova signals like AT2017gfo can be produced even in mergers involving strange quark stars, since most ejected strange matter evaporates into nucleons that can synthesize heavy nuclei.
