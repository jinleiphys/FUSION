# Statistical framework for nuclear parameter uncertainties in nucleosynthesis modeling of r- and i-process

> Propagating nuclear uncertainties to nucleosynthesis simulations is key to understand the impact of theoretical uncertainties on the predictions, especially for processes far from the stability region, where nuclear properties are scarcely known. While systematic (model) uncertainties have been thoroughly studied, the statistical (parameter) ones have been more rarely explored, as constraining them is more challenging. We present here a methodology to determine coherently parameter uncertainties by anchoring the theoretical uncertainties to the experimentally known nuclear properties through the use of the Backward Forward Monte Carlo method. We use this methodology for two nucleosynthesis processes: the intermediate neutron capture process (i-process) and the rapid neutron capture process (r-process). We determine coherently for the i-process the uncertainties from the (n,$γ$) rates while we explore the impact of nuclear mass uncertainties for the r-process. The effect of parameter uncertainties on the final nucleosynthesis is in the same order as model uncertainties, suggesting the crucial need for more experimental constraints on key nuclei of interest. We show how key nuclear properties, such as relevant (n,$γ$) rates impacting the i-process tracers, could enhance tremendously the prediction of stellar evolution models by experimentally constraining them.

## Key claim
The paper presents a methodology to determine nuclear parameter uncertainties coherently by anchoring theoretical uncertainties to experimentally known nuclear properties via the Backward Forward Monte Carlo method, and finds that the effect of parameter uncertainties on final nucleosynthesis is in the same order as model uncertainties, suggesting the crucial need for more experimental constraints on key nuclei.

## Method
The authors apply the Backward Forward Monte Carlo (BFMC) method, which consists of a backward step where model parameters are sampled and evaluated against experimental data using a chi-squared estimator, followed by a forward step where the constrained parameter sets are used to simulate quantities for conditions where experimental data are sparse. For the i-process, they assess parameter uncertainties for two nuclear models with different combinations of nuclear level density and photon strength function models, generating 50 sets of rates with randomly selected minimum or maximum values for 868 unknown (n,gamma) reaction rates. For the r-process, they evaluate parameter uncertainties in HFB-24 masses using BFMC, selecting 10,931 parameter combinations constrained by experimental masses, then propagate these to neutron separation energies and beta-decay Q-values.

## Key numbers
- Approximately 240 neutron capture rates are experimentally known, while the i-process nuclear network contains more than 1000 nuclei
- For the i-process, uncertainties on neutron capture rates can reach up to a factor of 100 for neutron-rich nuclei
- For the r-process, 10,931 parameter combinations were selected from the backward MC step, constrained by experimental masses with sigma_M,exp less than or equal to 0.8 MeV
- The HFB-24 model has an rms deviation of 0.55 MeV relative to experimental masses
- Mass uncertainties for unknown neutron-rich nuclei near the neutron drip line reach up to sigma_M approximately 3 MeV
- For the i-process, odd-Z elements are affected by approximately 1 dex uncertainty on average, and even-Z elements by approximately 0.5 dex uncertainty
- The total nuclear uncertainty on actinides (U and Th) from the i-process is around 2 dex

## Context
This work extends previous efforts to quantify nuclear uncertainties in nucleosynthesis, which historically used arbitrary scaling factors applied to nuclear rates or masses as in Mumpower (2016), Surman (2016), Nikas (2020), and Jiang (2021). The BFMC method was introduced in nuclear data evaluations by Chadwick (2007) and Bauge (2011), and earlier parameter uncertainty work on HFB masses was done by Goriely (2014). The i-process modeling builds on the work of Cowan (1977) and subsequent studies by Choplin (2021, 2022), while the r-process modeling in neutron star mergers follows the simulations of Just (2015, 2023). The paper enables future experimental campaigns to target specific key reactions, such as the 217Bi(n,gamma)218Bi reaction identified as a gate to actinide production in the i-process, and highlights the need for more experimental mass constraints to reduce r-process abundance uncertainties.
