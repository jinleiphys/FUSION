# Comparative study of the requantization of the time-dependent mean field for the dynamics of nuclear pairing

> To describe quantal collective phenomena, it is useful to requantize the time-dependent mean-field dynamics. We study the time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) theory for the two-level pairing Hamiltonian, and compare results of different quantization methods. The one constructing microscopic wave functions, using the TDHFB trajectories fulfilling the Einstein-Brillouin-Keller quantization condition, turns out to be the most accurate. The method is based on the stationary-phase approximation to the path integral. We also examine the performance of the collective model which assumes that the pairing gap parameter is the collective coordinate. The applicability of the collective model is limited for the nuclear pairing with a small number of single-particle levels, because the pairing gap parameter represents only a half of the pairing collective space.

## Key claim

The paper finds that constructing microscopic wave functions via the stationary-phase approximation to the path integral, using TDHFB trajectories that satisfy the Einstein-Brillouin-Keller (EBK) quantization condition, is the most accurate requantization method for the two-level pairing Hamiltonian; however, this accuracy is demonstrated specifically for a simplified model, and the method's superiority becomes more evident for smaller values of the degeneracy parameter \(\Omega\), which corresponds to more realistic nuclear systems.

## Method

The authors study the time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) theory for a two-level pairing Hamiltonian with equal degeneracy \(\Omega\), using a coherent-state representation of the SU(2) quasi-spin operators. They compare three requantization approaches: (1) constructing microscopic wave functions via the stationary-phase approximation to the path integral with the EBK quantization condition, (2) canonical quantization of the TDHFB Hamiltonian with symmetrized ordering, and (3) extracting matrix elements as Fourier components of time-dependent mean values of pair-creation operators. The TDHFB trajectories are analyzed in a two-dimensional phase space of relative gauge angle \(\phi\) and its conjugate momentum \(j\), with the total particle number \(N\) and energy \(E\) as constants of motion.

## Key numbers

* For the two-level pairing model with \(\Omega_1 = \Omega_2 = 8\) and \(N = 16\), the phase-space trajectories are shown for different values of the pairing strength \(g\), with a dimensionless parameter \(x = 2g\Omega / \Delta\epsilon\) marking the transition from normal (\(x < 1\)) to superfluid (\(x > 1\)) phase.
* The moment of inertia for pair rotation in the single-\(j\) model is \(\mathcal{J} = 2/g \times \Omega/(\Omega-1) \approx 2/g\) at \(\Omega \rightarrow \infty\).
* The EBK quantization condition for open TDHFB trajectories yields \(\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} j d\phi = 2k\pi\) for \(N = 4n\) and \(\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} j d\phi = (2k+1)\pi\) for \(N = 4n+2\), where \(k\) and \(n\) are integers.
* In Ref. [CDS84], two-particle-transfer matrix elements evaluated as Fourier components showed nice agreement with exact results at large \(\Omega = 40\), but the present study shows that deviation is substantial at realistic values of \(\Omega\).

## Context

This work extends the requantization approach of the time-dependent mean field, previously applied to the TDHFB theory for nuclear pairing dynamics, and directly compares it to the collective model of pairing motion introduced by Bes, Broglia, and collaborators (1970) which assumes the pairing gap parameter \(\Delta\) as the collective coordinate. The study is motivated by the need to understand low-lying excited \(0^+\) states in even-even nuclei, where the interplay between pairing and quadrupole correlations is crucial but poorly described by the quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA). The results enable future application of the EBK-based requantization method to more realistic nuclear systems with multiple single-particle levels, particularly for calculating two-particle-transfer matrix elements and describing large-amplitude pairing dynamics.
