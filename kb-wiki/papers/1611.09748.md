# Nucleon-Nucleon Correlations, Short-lived Excitations, and the Quarks Within

> This article reviews our current understanding of how the internal quark structure of a nucleon bound in nuclei differs from that of a free nucleon. We focus on the interpretation of measurements of the EMC effect for valence quarks, a reduction in the Deep Inelastic Scattering (DIS) cross-section ratios for nuclei relative to deuterium, and its possible connection to nucleon-nucleon Short-Range Correlations (SRC) in nuclei. Our review and new analysis (involving the amplitudes of non-nucleonic configurations in the nucleus) of the available experimental and theoretical evidence shows that there is a phenomenological relation between the EMC effect and the effects of SRC that is not an accident. The influence of strongly correlated neutron-proton pairs involving highly virtual nucleons is responsible for both effects. These correlated pairs are temporary high-density fluctuations in the nucleus in which the internal structure of the nucleons is briefly modified. This conclusion needs to be solidified by the future experiments and improved theoretical analyses that are discussed herein.

# Nucleon-Nucleon Correlations, Short-lived Excitations, and the Quarks Within

## Key claim

The phenomenological relation between the EMC effect (reduction in deep inelastic scattering cross-section ratios for nuclei relative to deuterium) and the effects of short-range correlated nucleon pairs is not accidental; strongly correlated neutron-proton pairs involving highly virtual nucleons are responsible for both effects, but this conclusion needs to be solidified by future experiments and improved theoretical analyses.

## Method

The authors review experimental and theoretical evidence connecting the EMC effect to nucleon-nucleon short-range correlations (SRC), and present a new analysis involving the amplitudes of non-nucleonic configurations in the nucleus. They derive the asymptotic $1/k^4$ behavior of the deuteron momentum-space density from iterated one-pion exchange (OPE) contributions to the tensor potential, showing that the effective S-state potential is approximately independent of momentum arguments, which is the hallmark of short-ranged interactions. They also demonstrate that nucleon-only models of the EMC effect violate baryon and momentum sum rules, and that pion-only models are ruled out by Drell-Yan experiments.

## Key numbers

- The deuteron momentum-space density exhibits an approximate $k^{-4}$ behavior for large values of momentum $k$, with the overall strength varying by a factor of 9 depending on the potential model used (Nijmegen, Reid93, Argonne V18).
- The S-state radial wave function at the origin, $\lim_{r\to 0} u(r)/r$, takes values of 0.0267, 0.0584, and 0.0792 fm$^{-3/2}$ for the Nijmegen, Reid93, and Argonne V18 potentials respectively.
- The D-state probability $P_D$ computed from the approximate formula yields 2%, 10%, and 18% for the three potentials, compared to the actual value of about 6% for all of these potentials.
- The EMC effect slope $dR_{EMC}/dx$ ranges from $-0.070\pm0.029$ for $^3$He to $-0.409\pm0.039$ for $^{197}$Au, with corresponding SRC scaling factors $a_2(A)$ ranging from $1.7\pm0.3$ to $5.2\pm0.9$ depending on the measurement.
- The EMC-SRC correlation slope is $0.079\pm0.006$ (from egiyan06 data) and $0.084\pm0.004$ (from fomin12 data), with $\chi^2/ndf$ values of 0.7688/3 and 4.895/5 respectively.
- The average separation energy for nuclear matter can be as large as 70 MeV, corresponding to $\epsilon \approx 0.04$ in the zero-width approximation.
- The average nucleon in a nucleus has $p^+ = M_N - 8$ MeV, leading to a momentum sum rule violation of $1-\epsilon$ in nucleon-only models.

## Context

This review extends the work of Frankfurt and Strikman (1985) on binding energy effects in the EMC effect, and the work of Weinstein et al. (2011) and Hen et al. (2012) establishing the correlation between EMC effect magnitude and SRC pair probability. It contradicts the nucleon-only explanation of the EMC effect (e.g., Jung 1988, Ciofi degli Atti 1989, Benhar 2012) by showing such models violate sum rules, and contradicts the pion-excess model (Llewellyn Smith 1983, Ericson 1983) which was ruled out by Drell-Yan experiments (Alde 1990). The work enables future experiments and theoretical analyses to solidify the connection between short-range correlations and quark modification in nuclei, and builds on the independent pair approximation formalism of Bethe (1971) and the factorization approach of Frankfurt (1993).
