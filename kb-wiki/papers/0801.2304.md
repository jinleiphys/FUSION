# Recent Advances in the Description of Electromagnetic Two-Nucleon Knockout Reactions

> Recent advances in the description of electromagnetic two-nucleon knockout reactions are reviewed. The sensitivity to different types of correlations and to their treatment in the nuclear wave functions, the effects of final-state interactions and the role of center-of-mass effects in connection with the problem of the lack of orthogonality between initial bound states and final scattering states obtained by the use of an energy-dependent optical-model potential are discussed. Results are presented for proton-proton and proton-neutron knockout off 16O also in comparison with the available data.

# Recent Advances in the Description of Electromagnetic Two-Nucleon Knockout Reactions

## Key claim
The paper reviews how electromagnetic two-nucleon knockout reactions can probe nuclear correlations, but emphasizes that extracting unambiguous information on correlations requires a reliable theoretical model where all ingredients, including final-state interactions, center-of-mass effects, and the lack of orthogonality between initial and final states, are well controlled. The authors show that the super-parallel kinematics, while highly sensitive to correlations, is also sensitive to all other model ingredients, making it difficult to isolate correlation effects without experiments in a wider range of kinematics.

## Method
The authors present a theoretical model for exclusive (e,e'pp) and (e,e'pn) reactions based on transition matrix elements of the charge-current density operator between initial and final nuclear states. The model includes one-body (OB) and two-body (TB) nuclear currents (with meson-exchange and Delta-isobar contributions), final-state interactions described by optical potentials and mutual nucleon-nucleon interactions, and two-nucleon overlap functions (TOFs) that incorporate short-range, tensor, and long-range correlations. The authors enforce orthogonality between initial bound and final scattering states via a Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization procedure, which preserves center-of-mass effects that were previously subtracted as spurious.

## Key numbers
- Coupling constants for the Delta-current in pp-knockout: f²_πNN/4π = 0.078, f²_πNΔ/4π = 0.224, f²_ρNN/4π = 7.10, f²_ρNΔ/4π = 20.45
- Cutoff parameters for hadronic form factors in pp-knockout: Λ_πNN = 1300 MeV, Λ_πNΔ = 1200 MeV, Λ_ρNN = 1400 MeV, Λ_ρNΔ = 1000 MeV
- For pn-knockout, simpler parameters: f²_πNΔ/4π = 0.35, Λ_πNN = Λ_πNΔ = 700 MeV
- For the 16O(e,e'pp)14C reaction to the 0+ ground state in super-parallel kinematics, the orthogonalized approach gives an enhancement factor of about 5 in the maximum region of the OB current cross section compared to the previous prescription
- For the 16O(e,e'pn)14N reaction to the 1₂+ state, the orthogonalized approach gives an enhancement larger than one order of magnitude at low recoil momenta for the OB current cross section
- The Bonn parametrization for TB currents in pn-knockout reduces the cross section by at most 30-40% compared to the unregularized parametrization
- With the simpler SM-SRC approach for pn-knockout, the OB current contribution is up to about three orders of magnitude lower than with the SF-B spectral function approach

## Context
This review extends the authors' previous work on electromagnetic two-nucleon knockout reactions, including earlier calculations by Giusti, Pacati, and collaborators (1997-1999) and the spectral function calculations of Barbieri (2004). The paper addresses the conceptual problem of non-orthogonality between initial bound and final scattering states arising from the use of energy-dependent optical-model potentials, which was previously handled by subtracting the uncorrelated OB current contribution. The authors show that this earlier prescription also removed legitimate center-of-mass effects, and they demonstrate that the new orthogonalization procedure significantly changes cross sections, especially in super-parallel kinematics used in MAMI experiments by Rosner (2000) and Duncan. The work enables more reliable extraction of correlation information from experimental data and highlights the need for measurements across a wider kinematic range to disentangle correlation effects from other reaction mechanism contributions.
