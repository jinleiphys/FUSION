# Mechanism of the quasi-elastic scattering based on the dinuclear system concept

> A unified description of full reaction channels in low-energy heavy-ion collisions is a great challenge. Although the theoretical models based on the dinuclear system (DNS) concept have been successfully employed in multinucleon transfer (MNT) reactions, the underestimation of the quasi-elastic (QE) channel results in unreliable description of few nucleon transfer, especially for the light reaction systems. In this work, the DNS-sysu model is improved by introducing the impact-parameter-dependent transition probabilities for a unified description of few nucleon and many nucleon transfer in MNT reactions. Extensive experimental data -- including reactions such as 40Ca, 58Ni, 64Ni, 136Xe, and 208Pb + 208Pb -- were compared with the model predictions. The calculated isotopic distributions, mass distributions, and charge distributions show good agreement with experimental measurements. The improved DNS-sysu model enables reasonable characterization and description of the QE/grazing collisions, notably resolving long-standing underestimation in the QE channel.

## Key claim
The improved DNS-sysu model, by introducing impact-parameter-dependent transition probabilities with a relaxation behavior for the contact distance between nuclei, resolves the long-standing underestimation of the quasi-elastic (QE) channel in multinucleon transfer (MNT) reactions, particularly for few-nucleon transfer in light systems, but only when the interaction time is sufficiently short that the nuclei remain at relatively large separations.

## Method
The authors extended the DNS-sysu model by incorporating a contact distance \(R_{\text{cont}}\) that relaxes from the distance of closest approach \(R_{\mathrm{closest}}\) toward the bottom of the potential pocket \(R_{\mathrm{bottom}}\) with a characteristic time \(\tau_{\mathrm{C}} = 2 \times 10^{-22}\,\mathrm{s}\), using a smoothing function \(f(t)=\exp(-t/\tau_{\mathrm{C}})\). A three-dimensional master equation in proton number \(Z_1\), neutron number \(N_1\), and dynamical deformation \(\beta_2\) was solved with transition rates that include a semiclassical nucleon transfer probability \(P_{\mathrm{tr}}(R_{\mathrm{cont}})\) depending on the internuclear separation. The reaction time at each angular momentum was obtained via the deflection function method, and the de-excitation of primary fragments was treated with the code GEMINI++.

## Key numbers
- Characteristic relaxation time \(\tau_{\mathrm{C}} = 2 \times 10^{-22}\,\mathrm{s}\) used in the smoothing function for the contact distance.
- Nucleon transfer rate \(\lambda_0 \approx 10^{22}\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}\), with the specific form \(5 \times A_{\mathrm{tot}}^2 \times (T/\mathrm{MeV}) \times 10^{16}\) adopted from Saiko (2022).
- Level density parameter \(\alpha = A_{\mathrm{tot}}/12\,\mathrm{MeV^{-1}}\) used to compute local nuclear temperature \(T = \sqrt{E^*/\alpha}\).
- For the semiclassical transfer probability, the threshold distance is \(R_{\mathrm{tr}} = R_{\mathrm{pro}} + R_{\mathrm{tar}} + 2.5\,\mathrm{fm}\); for systems without a potential pocket, \(R_{\mathrm{bottom}}\) is taken at a surface separation of approximately \(0.7\,\mathrm{fm}\).
- Reactions compared: \(^{40}\mathrm{Ca} + ^{208}\mathrm{Pb}\) at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 197\) and \(209\,\mathrm{MeV}\); \(^{58}\mathrm{Ni} + ^{208}\mathrm{Pb}\) at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 256\) and \(270\,\mathrm{MeV}\); \(^{64}\mathrm{Ni} + ^{208}\mathrm{Pb}\) at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 268\,\mathrm{MeV}\); \(^{136}\mathrm{Xe} + ^{208}\mathrm{Pb}\) at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 526\) and \(617\,\mathrm{MeV}\); \(^{208}\mathrm{Pb} + ^{208}\mathrm{Pb}\) at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 786\,\mathrm{MeV}\).

## Context
This work extends the DNS-sysu model of Zhu et al. (2021) by addressing its known failure in the QE/grazing regime, where the assumption of a frozen internuclear distance and a well-formed dinuclear configuration breaks down (Wen, 2017; PhysRevC.102.054613). The improved model now unifies the description of quasi-elastic scattering, deep-inelastic collisions, and quasi-fission within a single framework, enabling systematic surveys of MNT reactions to identify optimal systems and beam energies for producing exotic nuclei. Future development could integrate microscopic inputs from constrained density functional theory to provide more accurate potential energy surfaces and transition rates.
