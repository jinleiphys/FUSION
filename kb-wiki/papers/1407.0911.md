# Information and statistics: a new paradigm in theoretical nuclear physics

> Theoretical predictions of physical observables often involve extrapolations to regions that are poorly constrained by laboratory experiments and astrophysical observations. Without properly quantified theoretical errors, such model predictions are of very limited utility. In this contribution we use maximum-likelihood estimation to compute theoretical errors and assess correlations between physical observables. We illustrate the power and elegance of these methods using examples of both pedagogical and realistic interest. In particular, we implement a gaussian approximation to the likelihood function to develop a new relativistic effective interaction constrained by ground-state properties of finite nuclei, their monopole response, and masses of neutron stars.

## Key claim

Maximum-likelihood estimation, implemented via a Gaussian approximation to the likelihood function, can provide quantified theoretical uncertainties and reveal correlations between observables in nuclear density functional theory, even when the model is extrapolated to poorly constrained regions such as neutron-star matter.

## Method

The authors define a chi-squared objective function from the sum of squared deviations between theoretical predictions and experimental data, weighted by adopted errors that include both experimental and theoretical components. They then construct the likelihood function as exp(-chi-squared/2) and, in the Gaussian approximation, expand the chi-squared to second order around its minimum to obtain the covariance matrix of model parameters. Using this covariance matrix, they compute theoretical uncertainties (variances) for any observable and Pearson correlation coefficients between pairs of observables, and illustrate the approach with a liquid-drop-model calibration and a relativistic energy density functional (EDF) fit.

## Key numbers

- The liquid-drop model was calibrated against binding energies of all 576 even-even nuclei with N >= 8 and Z >= 8 from the AME-2003 atomic mass evaluation (Audi, Wapstra, and Thibault).
- For a Gaussian-distributed observable, the 1-sigma confidence interval contains 68.3% of the probability, the 2-sigma interval contains 95.5%, and the 3-sigma interval contains 99.7%.
- For a two-dimensional confidence ellipse, the R=1 ellipse contains 39.35% probability, the R=2.4478 ellipse contains 95.00%, and the R=3.0349 ellipse contains 99.00%.
- Laplace estimated the mass of Saturn to be 3,512 times smaller than the mass of the Sun, with a better than 99% probability that the actual mass lies within 1% of that value; the modern value is (1/3,499) solar masses, a deviation of 0.3%.

## Context

This work extends the statistical methodology advocated by the UNEDF collaboration (Kortelainen et al., 2010) and by Reinhard (2010) for quantifying theoretical uncertainties in nuclear density functional theory. It builds on prior studies by Fattoyev et al. (2011, 2012) and Piekarewicz et al. (2012) that identified soft directions in parameter space for the isovector sector, particularly the slope of the symmetry energy L. The approach enables the identification of experimentally accessible observables, such as the neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb, that are strongly correlated with poorly constrained nuclear-matter properties, guiding future measurements to improve the EDF.
