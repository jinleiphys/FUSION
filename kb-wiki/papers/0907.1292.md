# Photon HBT interferometry for non-central heavy-ion collisions

> Currently, the only known way to obtain experimental information about the space-time structure of a heavy-ion collision is through 2-particle momentum correlations. Azimuthally sensitive HBT interferometry (Hanbury Brown-Twiss intensity interferometry) can complement elliptic flow measurements by constraining the spatial deformation of the source and its time evolution. Performing these measurements on photons allows us to access the fireball evolution at earlier times than with hadrons. Using ideal hydrodynamics to model the space-time evolution of the collision fireball, we explore theoretically various aspects of 2-photon intensity interferometry with transverse momenta up to 2 GeV, in particular the azimuthal angle dependence of the HBT radii in non-central collisions. We highlight the dual nature of thermal photon emission, in both central and non-central collisions, resulting from the superposition of QGP and hadron resonance gas photon production. This signature is present in both the thermal photon source function and the HBT radii extracted from Gaussian fits of the 2-photon correlation function.

## Key claim

Using ideal hydrodynamics to model the fireball evolution, the authors show that thermal photon HBT radii in non-central heavy-ion collisions exhibit a dual structure from QGP and hadron resonance gas emission, but this signature cannot be cleanly separated in a single measurement and requires systematic exploration of the pair-momentum dependence.

## Method

The authors used the (2+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamic code AZHYDRO with boost invariance to simulate the space-time evolution of the fireball, folding thermal photon emission rates with the hydrodynamic temperature and flow fields to compute the photon emission function. They then computed the two-photon correlation function numerically from this emission function and extracted HBT radii by performing 3-dimensional Gaussian fits in the outward-side-longitudinal coordinate system, including the cross-term \(R_{os}^2\), using a generalized version of the fitting algorithm from Frodermann (2006) and Lisa (2007). For comparison, they also computed pion HBT radii from the same hydrodynamic model and compared Gaussian-fit results with RMS variance calculations.

## Key numbers

* For 200 \(A\) GeV Au+Au collisions at impact parameter \(b = 7\) fm, the pion HBT radii \(R_o\), \(R_s\), and \(R_{os}^2\) were computed at \(K_\perp = 0\) and 1 GeV/\(c\), showing that the azimuthal oscillation amplitudes from Gaussian fits agree well with those from RMS radii, while the \(\Phi\)-averaged values differ.
* The pion correlation function contours at \(K_\perp \to 0\) rotate clockwise as the pair emission angle \(\Phi\) rotates counter-clockwise, with successive contours separated by 0.05 starting from \(C(0)-1 = 1\) at the center.
* The photon HBT analysis was performed for transverse momenta up to 2 GeV, with the on-shell approximation breaking down for soft photon pairs when \(|\bm{K}| \lesssim 1/R_\mathrm{source}\).
* The Gaussian fit for pions used a restricted \(q_l\) fit range with \(q_l < q_\mathrm{max} = 0.02\) GeV to avoid distortions from non-Gaussian tails in the longitudinal direction.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of photon elliptic flow by Chatterjee (2005), Heinz (2006), and Turbide (2007) to the spatial domain via two-photon HBT interferometry, and complements prior pion HBT calculations by Heinz (2002) that used RMS variances as proxies for HBT radii. It also builds on the Gaussian fitting methodology developed by Frodermann (2006) and Lisa (2007) for non-central collisions. The results enable future experimental constraints on the early-time spatial deformation and time evolution of the fireball in heavy-ion collisions, potentially helping to validate hydrodynamic models and the QGP equation of state, though the authors note that their study lacks quantitative features such as viscous effects, pre-equilibrium photon emission, and a chemically non-equilibrium equation of state.
