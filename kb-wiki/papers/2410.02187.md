# On the extraction of fission mode properties from fragment mass distributions

> Background: Fission modes are typically characterised by fragment mass and total kinetic energy centroids, around which a distribution of these variables is observed. These distributions are usually fitted with Gaussian functions. Purpose: To investigate how the properties of these ``Gaussian fission modes'' compare with underlying ``theoretical fission modes'' defined from the potential energy surface of the fissioning nuclei. Methods: A simple approach, inspired by the scission point model, is introduced to investigate the impact of anharmonicity of the potential along the scission line on Gaussian mode properties. This approach is also used to evaluated an ``effective potential'' from the yields. Results: Several Gaussian functions are usually required to fit yields from non-harmonic potentials associated with a unique theoretical mode. Similarly, ``effective fission modes'', defined from the wells of the effective potentials, are sometime very different to the Gaussian modes. For instance, the S1 and S2 Brosa modes contribute to the same effective potential well at scission. Conclusions: Gaussian fits are useful to identify the role of shell effects in fission. However, comparisons with theoretical potential energy surfaces are better carried with effective potentials extracted from the yields, assuming that a broad range of excitation energies is available.

# On the extraction of fission mode properties from fragment mass distributions

## Key claim

Gaussian fits of fission fragment mass distributions are useful for identifying shell effects, but the resulting "Gaussian fission modes" (such as S1 and S2) often correspond to the same underlying potential well at scission and therefore do not map directly onto "theoretical fission modes" defined from potential energy surfaces. The authors propose that effective potentials extracted from yields provide a better bridge between experimental data and theoretical predictions, provided a broad range of excitation energies is available.

## Method

The authors introduce a simple scission-point-model-inspired approach where the yield Y(x) is expressed as exp(-V(x)/T), with V(x) being the potential energy along the scission line and T the collective temperature (T = sqrt(E*/a) with a = 10 MeV^-1). They investigate how non-harmonic potentials (quartic single-well and double-well) produce yields that require multiple Gaussian functions for adequate fitting, then extract "effective potentials" from Gaussian fits of experimental data for 180Hg, 238U, and 232Th to compare Gaussian modes with effective modes defined from minima in the extracted potential.

## Key numbers

- The temperature parameter T is related to excitation energy E* by T = sqrt(E*/a) with a = 10 MeV^-1
- For 180Hg, excitation energies up to E* = 65.5 MeV are considered, spanning beta-delayed fission (Andreyev 2010) and fusion-fission reactions (Nishio 2015)
- For 238U and 232Th, neutron-induced fission data from Simutkin (2014) are analyzed at various neutron energy ranges
- The coordinate x is defined as x = 4(A1 - AT/2)/AT, ranging from -2 to 2 with the region of interest generally within [-1,1]
- The two-well potential example uses V(x) = 1/4 - x^2 + x^4 with theoretical mode at x0 = 1/sqrt(2)

## Context

This work extends the foundational classification of fission modes by Brosa (1990), who originally noted that Gaussian representations of fission channels are only approximate, and challenges the common practice of interpreting Gaussian fit components as distinct physical fission modes. The authors connect to the scission point model of Wilkins (1976) and to theoretical potential energy surface calculations from Goutte (2005) and Schunck (2016), noting that theoretical PES typically do not exhibit separate valleys corresponding to S1 and S2 Gaussian modes. The proposed effective potential method enables direct comparison between experimental yields and theoretical predictions, though the authors acknowledge limitations from neglecting pre-scission dynamics and complex PES topography that require more advanced approaches like TDGCM.
