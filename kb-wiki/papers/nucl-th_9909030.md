# Quasi-nuclear and quark model baryonium: historical survey

## Key claim
The paper argues that while most early baryonium candidates were not confirmed by careful scans at LEAR, some intriguing signals remain (such as the AX/f2(1565) state, P-wave enhancements in total cross sections, and an anomalously large protonium shift) that prevent a definitive conclusion that baryonium is "completely dead." The author notes that these residual signals could alternatively be explained by glueballs or hybrids, but it remains useful to recall and update the theoretical speculations from the 1970s and early 1980s.

## Method
The paper is a historical survey that reviews two theoretical approaches to baryonium: the quasi-nuclear approach, which treats nucleon-antinucleon (NNbar) bound states using meson-exchange potentials transformed by the G-parity rule from the NN potential; and the multiquark approach, which describes baryonium as q2-qbar2 configurations in the quark model, including Jaffe's bag model estimates and Chan and collaborators' "colour chemistry" with diquark clustering. The author compares the predictions and limitations of both frameworks, including the effects of annihilation on the predicted spectra.

## Key numbers
- The state called "AX" (now f2(1565)) is identified as a possible baryonium candidate below the NNbar threshold.
- The scalar channel (3P0) protonium shift is "slightly larger than expected," as reported by Gotta (1999).
- The hadron spectrum around 2 GeV is cited as the energy region of interest for baryonium candidates.
- In the multiquark section, "mock-baryonium" states with colour-6 diquarks are predicted to be very narrow, with widths of "a few MeV, perhaps."

## Context
This paper extends the earlier work on NNbar bound states by Fermi and Yang (1959) and the bootstrap ideas of the 1960s, and specifically reviews the quasi-nuclear approach pioneered by Shapiro (1978) and Dover (1975), as well as the multiquark approach of Jaffe (1977) and Chan and collaborators (1978). It contrasts these with the later focus on glueballs and hybrids that became fashionable after the LEAR experiments failed to confirm most baryonium candidates. The survey enables future work by reminding the community of the theoretical frameworks that could still be relevant for interpreting residual signals in the NNbar system, and by noting that the debate over whether scalar and tensor mesons are qqbar or q2-qbar2 states remains open.
