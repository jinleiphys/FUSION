# Glassy quantum nuclear pasta in neutron star crusts

> We investigate the nuclear pasta phases in neutron star crusts by conducting a large number of three-dimensional Hartree-Fock+BCS calculations at densities leading to the crust-core transition. We survey the shape parameter space of pasta at constant pressure. Spaghetti, waffles, lasagna, bi-continuous phases and cylindrical holes occupy local minima in the resulting Gibbs energy surfaces. The bi-continuous phase, in which both the neutron gas and nuclear matter extend continuously in all dimensions and therefore protons are delocalized, appears over a large range of depths. Our results support the idea that nuclear pasta is a glassy system. Multiple pasta configurations coexist in a given layer of the crust. At a characteristic temperature, of order $10^8$-$10^9$K, different phases become frozen into domains whose sizes we estimate to be 1-50 times the lattice spacing and over which the local density and electron fraction can vary. Above this temperature, there is very little long-range order and matter is an amorphous solid. Electron scattering off domain boundaries may contribute to the disorder resistivity of the pasta phases. Annealing of the domains may occur during cooling; repopulating of local minima during crustal heating might lead to temperature dependent transport properties in the deep layers of the crust. We identify 4 distinct regions: (1) nuclear pasta first appears as a local minima, but spherical nuclei are the ground state; (2) nuclear pasta become the absolute minimum, but spherical nuclei are still a local minimum (3) only nuclear pasta appears in local minima, and protons are still localized in at least one dimension (4) only pasta appears, and protons are delocalized. The whole pasta region can occupy up to 70% of the crust by mass and 40% by thickness, and the layer in which protons are delocalized could occupy 45% of the crust mass and 25% of its thickness.

## Key claim

The paper argues that nuclear pasta in neutron star crusts behaves as a glassy system, where multiple pasta configurations coexist at a given depth and become frozen into microscopic domains below a characteristic temperature of order 10^8-10^9 K, rather than forming a single ordered ground state as previous simpler models assumed.

## Method

The authors performed approximately 500,000 CPU hours of three-dimensional Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS calculations at zero temperature, using a quadrupole constraint to systematically survey the shape parameter space of nuclear pasta configurations at constant pressure. They imposed parity conservation in all three dimensions to restrict computations to one octant of the unit cell, enabling a wide survey of triaxially symmetric pasta shapes across multiple densities and proton fractions near beta-equilibrium.

## Key numbers

- The characteristic temperature below which pasta phases become frozen into domains is of order 10^8-10^9 K
- Domain sizes are estimated to be 1-50 times the lattice spacing
- The whole pasta region can occupy up to 70% of the crust by mass and 40% by thickness
- The layer in which protons are delocalized (bi-continuous phase) could occupy 45% of the crust mass and 25% of its thickness
- Calculations were performed at baryon number density n_b = 0.06 fm^-3 as a case study, with cell containing A_cell = 614 nucleons and proton fractions ranging from 0.023 to 0.029
- The NRAPR Skyrme parameterization used gives a symmetry energy slope of L = 60 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous three-dimensional quantum mean-field simulations of nuclear pasta by Magierski (2001), Newton (2009), and Schuetrumpf (2013-2020), as well as classical and quantum molecular dynamics simulations by Horowitz (2004-2008), Schneider (2013-2018), and Caplan (2015-2020). The results support the glassy nature of pasta suggested by earlier work and provide quantitative estimates of domain sizes and freezing temperatures. The findings enable predictions for how disordered pasta may affect neutron star cooling curves and magnetic field evolution, as discussed by Horowitz (2015) and Pons (2013), and suggest that temperature-dependent transport properties in deep crustal layers could arise from annealing and repopulation of local minima during crustal heating.
