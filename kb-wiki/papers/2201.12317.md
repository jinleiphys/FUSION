# Conservative finite volume scheme for first-order viscous relativistic hydrodynamics

> We present the first conservative finite volume numerical scheme for the causal, stable relativistic Navier-Stokes equations developed by Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha, and Kovtun (BDNK). BDNK theory has arisen very recently as a promising means of incorporating entropy-generating effects (viscosity, heat conduction) into relativistic fluid models, appearing as a possible alternative to the so-called Müller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) theory successfully used to model quark-gluon plasma. The major difference between the two lies in the structure of the system of PDEs: BDNK theory only has a set of conservation laws, whereas MIS also includes a set of evolution equations for its dissipative degrees of freedom. The simpler structure of the BDNK PDEs in this respect allows for rigorous proofs of stability, causality, and hyperbolicity in full generality which have as yet been impossible for MIS. To capitalize on these advantages, we present the first fully conservative multi-dimensional fluid solver for the BDNK equations suitable for physical applications. The scheme includes a flux-conservative discretization, non-oscillatory reconstruction, and a central-upwind numerical flux, and is designed to smoothly transition to a high-resolution shock-capturing perfect fluid solver in the inviscid limit. We assess the robustness of our new method in a series of flat-spacetime tests for a conformal fluid, and provide a detailed comparison with previous approaches of Pandya & Pretorius (2021).

## Key claim

The authors present the first conservative finite volume numerical scheme for the causal, stable BDNK first-order relativistic Navier-Stokes equations, demonstrating that the method can stably evolve high-Lorentz-factor flows, strong shockwaves, and near-vacuum states while smoothly reducing to a perfect fluid solver in the inviscid limit.

## Method

The scheme uses a flux-conservative finite volume discretization with non-oscillatory WENO reconstruction of primitive variables and a central-upwind Kurganov-Tadmor numerical flux function, applied to the BDNK equations for a conformal fluid in 4D Minkowski spacetime. A novel adaptive primitive variable recovery algorithm handles the numerically difficult inviscid limit by switching to the perfect fluid solver in cells where viscous terms are unresolved. The method is assessed in one- and two-dimensional flat-spacetime tests for a conformal fluid with shear viscosity and two relaxation times, using the hydrodynamic frame where characteristic speeds are exactly unity.

## Key numbers

- The transport coefficients are parameterized as $\eta = \eta_0 \epsilon^{3/4}$, $\tau_{\epsilon} = \frac{3}{4\epsilon} \chi_0 \epsilon^{3/4}$, and $\tau_{Q} = \frac{3}{4\epsilon} \lambda_0 \epsilon^{3/4}$, with the chosen frame $(\chi_0, \lambda_0) = \left( \frac{25}{4} \eta_0, \frac{25}{7} \eta_0 \right)$ fixing characteristic speeds to exactly unity.
- The inviscid limit corresponds to $\eta_0 \to 0$, where all dissipative corrections vanish and the BDNK conserved currents reduce exactly to those of the perfect fluid.
- The maximum local wave speed is set to $a = 1$, which is the exact local characteristic speed for the BDNK equations in the chosen hydrodynamic frame.

## Context

This work extends the exploratory numerical study of Pandya and Pretorius (2021) for BDNK theory, providing enhanced stability for flows with high Lorentz factors, strong shockwaves, and near-vacuum states that arise in astrophysical and nuclear-physics applications. BDNK theory was developed by Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha, and Kovtun as an alternative to Muller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) theory, which has been the standard for modeling quark-gluon plasma but lacks rigorous proofs of stability and causality in full generality. The scheme enables future applications of BDNK theory to physical systems such as heavy-ion collisions and neutron star mergers, though the consistent inclusion of magnetic fields remains an open problem currently only solved for MIS-type theories.
