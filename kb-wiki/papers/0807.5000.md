# Signals for strange quark contributions to the neutrino (antineutrino) scattering in quasi-elastic region

> Strange quark contributions to the neutrino (antineutrino) scattering are investigated on the elastic neutrino-nucleon scattering and the neutrino-nucleus scattering for 12C target in the quasi-elastic region on the incident energy of 500 MeV, within the framework of a relativistic single particle model. For the neutrino-nucleus scattering, the effects of final state interaction for the knocked-out nucleon are included by a relativistic optical potential. In the cross sections we found some cancellations of the strange quark contributions between the knocked-out protons and neutrons. Consequently, the asymmetries between the incident neutrino and antineutrino which is the ratio of neutral current to charged current, and the difference between the asymmetries are shown to be able to yield more feasible quantities for the strangeness effects. In order to explicitly display importance of the cancellations, results of the exclusive reaction 16O(ν, ν' p) are additionally presented for detecting the strangeness effects.

## Key claim

Strange quark contributions to neutrino-nucleus scattering in the quasi-elastic region are largely cancelled out in inclusive cross sections on carbon-12 because the enhancement from knocked-out protons is nearly balanced by the reduction from knocked-out neutrons, making the net effect only a few percent; however, the asymmetry between neutrino and antineutrino cross sections relative to charged-current reactions, and especially the difference of those asymmetries, can provide a more feasible signal for the axial strangeness \(g_A^s\).

## Method

The authors calculated elastic neutrino-nucleon and neutrino-nucleus cross sections for a carbon-12 target at an incident energy of 500 MeV using a relativistic single-particle model. For the nucleus, final-state interactions of the knocked-out nucleon were included via a relativistic optical potential, and the cross sections were separated into contributions from final protons and neutrons. They also computed asymmetries defined as the ratio of neutral-current to charged-current cross sections, the difference and sum of those asymmetries, and exclusive oxygen-16(\(\nu, \nu' p\)) cross sections at 2.4 GeV to isolate the strangeness effect without nucleon cancellation.

## Key numbers

- For neutrino scattering on carbon-12 at 500 MeV, the \(g_A^s\) effect (with \(g_A^s = -0.19\)) increases the cross section from protons by about 30% and decreases it from neutrons by about 30%, but the net effect on the total cross section is below 3%.
- For antineutrino scattering on carbon-12 at 500 MeV, the \(g_A^s\) effect enhances the proton contribution by about 20% and reduces the neutron contribution by about 20%, with a net effect below 2%.
- The asymmetry \(A_{NC/CC}^p\) for the proton is given as \(0.12 - 0.12 (g_A^s / g_A) - 0.13 (G_M^s / G_M^3)\), and \(A_{NC/CC}^n\) for the neutron is \(0.16 + 0.16 (g_A^s / g_A) + 0.13 (G_M^s / G_M^3)\).
- The difference of asymmetries \(DA_{NC/CC} = A_{NC/CC}^p - A_{NC/CC}^n\) is approximately \(-0.04 - 0.28 (g_A^s / g_A + G_M^s / G_M^3)\).
- The sum of asymmetries \(SA_{NC/CC} = A_{NC/CC}^p + A_{NC/CC}^n\) is \(0.28 + 0.04 (g_A^s / g_A)\), which is nearly constant at about 0.28.
- The axial coupling constant \(g_A^s\) is allowed to vary in the range \(0 < g_A^s < -0.19\) based on prior extractions.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of strange quark contributions to neutrino scattering from the nucleon level, as studied by Garvey (1993) and Alberico (2002), to nuclear targets where cancellations between protons and neutrons occur. The authors build on their own prior work (Cheoun 2008) and suggest that the difference of asymmetries between neutrino and antineutrino neutral-current reactions relative to charged-current reactions is a more robust observable than cross sections or simple ratios. The exclusive reaction on oxygen-16 is presented as a way to avoid the cancellation effect, enabling future experimental tests at facilities such as JLAB.
