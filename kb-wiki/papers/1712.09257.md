# New Insights on Low Energy $πN$ Scattering Amplitudes

> The $S$- and $P$- wave phase shifts of low-energy pion-nucleon scatterings are analysed using Peking University representation, in which they are decomposed into various terms contributing either from poles or branch cuts. We estimate the left-hand cut contributions with the help of tree-level perturbative amplitudes derived in relativistic baryon chiral perturbation theory up to $\mathcal{O}(p^2)$. It is found that in $S_{11}$ and $P_{11}$ channels, contributions from known resonances and cuts are far from enough to saturate experimental phase shift data -- strongly indicating contributions from low lying poles undiscovered before, and we fully explore possible physics behind. On the other side, no serious disagreements are observed in the other channels.

## Key claim

The Peking University (PKU) representation analysis of low-energy pion-nucleon scattering reveals that in the \(S_{11}\) and \(P_{11}\) channels, the combined contributions from known resonances and branch cuts are far from sufficient to reproduce experimental phase shift data, strongly indicating the existence of previously undiscovered low-lying poles; the authors explore possible physics behind this discrepancy, while noting that no serious disagreements are observed in the other four channels.

## Method

The authors applied the PKU representation, a model-independent method based on axiomatic S-matrix theory, to decompose the S- and P-wave phase shifts of elastic pion-nucleon scattering into contributions from poles (bound states, virtual states, resonances) and branch cuts (left-hand cuts and right-hand inelastic cuts). The left-hand cut contributions were estimated using tree-level perturbative amplitudes derived in relativistic baryon chiral perturbation theory up to \(\mathcal{O}(p^2)\), with the coupling constants \(c_1, c_2, c_3, c_4\) determined by a K-matrix fit to GWU data. Known poles (e.g., nucleon bound state, \(\Delta(1232)\), \(N^*(1535)\), \(N^*(1440)\)) were included as inputs, and the remaining discrepancies in \(S_{11}\) and \(P_{11}\) were analyzed by fitting additional hidden poles.

## Key numbers

- The K-matrix fit to five channels (\(S_{11}\), \(S_{31}\), \(P_{11}\), \(P_{31}\), \(P_{13}\)) using 20 data points per channel (center-of-mass energy \(W \in [1.0776, 1.1600]\) GeV) yielded coupling constants: \(c_1 = -0.841\ \text{GeV}^{-1}\), \(c_2 = 1.170\ \text{GeV}^{-1}\), \(c_3 = -2.618\ \text{GeV}^{-1}\), \(c_4 = 1.677\ \text{GeV}^{-1}\), with fit quality \(\chi^2/\text{d.o.f} = 1.850\).
- In the \(P_{11}\) channel, the right-hand inelastic cut contributed about 8 degrees to the phase shift at \(\sqrt{s} = 1.16\) GeV, far from the approximately 32-degree discrepancy; a fit requiring a virtual state near threshold located that pole at 983 MeV (with \(\chi^2_{P_{11}}/\text{d.o.f} = 0.240\)).
- In the \(S_{11}\) channel, a fit with an extra resonance pole below threshold gave \(\sqrt{s}^{\text{II}} = 0.808 - 0.055i\) GeV (with \(\chi^2_{S_{11}}/\text{d.o.f} = 0.109\)); varying the cut-off parameter \(s_c\) to \(-\infty\) gave \(\sqrt{s}^{\text{II}} = 0.914 - 0.205i\) GeV (with \(\chi^2_{S_{11}}/\text{d.o.f} = 0.018\)), leading to an estimated pole location \(\sqrt{s}^{\text{II}} = (0.861 \pm 0.053) - (0.130 \pm 0.075)i\) GeV.
- The perturbative S-matrix in the \(P_{11}\) channel predicted a zero at approximately 976 MeV, consistent with the fitted virtual state at 983 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the PKU representation method, previously applied successfully to \(\pi\pi\) and \(\pi K\) scatterings (Xiao 2000, Zheng 2003), to the pion-nucleon system for the first time. The analysis contradicts the adequacy of known resonances and chiral perturbation theory alone to describe the \(S_{11}\) and \(P_{11}\) channels, and it suggests the need for hidden poles, such as a virtual state near threshold in \(P_{11}\) (related to a kinematic companion of the nucleon bound state) and a subthreshold resonance in \(S_{11}\). This enables future studies to refine the pole structure of low-energy \(\pi N\) scattering and to investigate the origin of these hidden states, potentially clarifying the nature of resonances like \(N^*(1440)\) and \(N^*(1535)\).
