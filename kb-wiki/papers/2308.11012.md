# On scattering problem off the potential, decreasing as inverse square of distance

> A solution of the scattering problem is obtained for the Schrödinger equation with the potential of induced dipole interaction, which decreases as the inverse square of the distance. Such a potential arises in the collision of an incident charged particle with a complex of charged particles (for example, in the collision of electrons with atoms). For the wave function, an integral equation is constructed for an arbitrary value of the orbital momentum of relative motion. By solving this equation, an exact integral representation for the $K$-matrix of the problem is obtained in terms of the wave function. This representation is used to analyze the behavior of the $K$-matrix at low energies and to obtain comprehensive information on its threshold behavior for various values of the dipole momentum. The resulting solution is applied to study the behavior of the scattering cross sections in the electron, positron and antiproton system.

# On scattering problem off the potential, decreasing as inverse square of distance

## Key claim
The authors construct an exact integral representation for the K-matrix of the Schrödinger equation with an attractive dipole potential (−α²/r²) using a potential splitting method and Lippmann-Schwinger integral equations, but the solution is limited to potentials that become exactly −α²/r² beyond a finite distance R, with an arbitrary short-range part V(r) inside that radius.

## Method
The potential is split into a finite-range part V_R (nonzero only for r < R) and a long-range part V^R that equals −α²/r² for r ≥ R. The homogeneous equation with V^R is solved analytically using Bessel functions of non-integer order λ, where λ(λ+1) = ℓ(ℓ+1) − α². A Green's function is constructed from regular and irregular solutions, converting the scattering problem into a Fredholm integral equation on the finite interval [0,R], which is further reduced to a Volterra equation guaranteeing existence and uniqueness of the solution.

## Key numbers
- The dipole potential parameter α² appears in the relation λ = −1/2 + √(1/4 + ℓ(ℓ+1) − α²); for the Ps−p̅ system with ℓ = 1, the effective channel yields ℓ(ℓ+1) − α² = −22.9947.
- The diagonalized effective potential matrix for the Ps−p̅ (n=2) system gives eigenvalues 24.9947/r² and −22.9947/r², corresponding to λ₁ = 4.52441 and λ₂ = −0.5 + i 4.76914.
- The matching radius R is set to 6.0 a₀ for the Ps−p̅ model calculation.
- For p = 0.02 a₀⁻¹: 𝒦_{λℓ} = 3.12×10⁵ + i 1.58×10⁶, K_ℓ = −0.822; for p = 0.006 a₀⁻¹: 𝒦_{λℓ} = −1.25×10⁶ + i 1.01×10⁶, K_ℓ = −2.82.
- The wave function reaches its physical asymptote only beyond r > 2000 a₀ for p = 0.02 a₀⁻¹ and r > 5000 a₀ for p = 0.006 a₀⁻¹.

## Context
This work extends the theoretical predictions of Gailitis and Damburg (1963) on threshold oscillations in electron-hydrogen scattering, providing a rigorous integral-equation foundation for the K-matrix behavior. It addresses the computational difficulty noted by Hu (2001) and Gradusov et al. (2015–2023) that three-particle wave functions approach their asymptotes extremely slowly due to the dipole interaction. The formalism enables replacing the standard asymptotic condition with a representation valid from r = R, reducing the required integration domain by approximately three orders of magnitude for realistic three-body calculations of antiproton-positronium scattering relevant to antimatter experiments (Amoretti et al., 2002; Gabrielse et al., 2002).
