# Electromagnetic Radiation in Hot QCD Matter: Rates, Electric Conductivity, Flavor Susceptibility and Diffusion

> We discuss the general features of the electromagnetic radiation from a thermal hadronic gas as constrained by chiral symmetry. The medium effects on the electromagnetic spectral functions and the partial restoration of chiral symmetry are quantified in terms of the pion densities. The results are compared with the electromagnetic radiation from a strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma in terms of the leading gluon condensate operators. We use the spectral functions as constrained by the emission rates to estimate the electric conductivity, the light flavor susceptibility and diffusion constant across the transition from the correlated hadronic gas to a strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim

The paper argues that electromagnetic radiation from hot QCD matter can be systematically described across the hadronic-to-QGP transition using chiral reduction for the hadronic phase and gluon condensate operators for the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma (sQGP), but the electric conductivity estimates from the hadronic gas are about an order of magnitude smaller than reported lattice results, and the sQGP results with constant condensates are also much smaller than lattice estimates unless temperature-dependent condensates are used.

## Method

The authors compute dilepton and photon emission rates from a thermal hadronic gas using a spectral function approach organized as a virial-like expansion in pion density, with vector and axial correlators constrained by chiral symmetry and fixed by experimental electroproduction data. For the sQGP, they use a re-ordered operator product expansion (OPE) at high temperature incorporating non-perturbative thermal gluon condensates (electric and magnetic) to correct the leading Born term, and compare with hard thermal loop (HTL) results. The spectral functions are then used to extract the electric conductivity via Kubo-like formulas, and the flavor susceptibility and diffusion constant are derived from density expansions in both phases.

## Key numbers

* Pion density parameter κ reaches approximately 1/8 at T ≈ 190 MeV with μ_π = 100 MeV, giving maximal vector-axial mixing with equal contributions from Π_V and Π_A.
* The ρ-meson peak around 0.78 GeV is reduced in the dilepton rate due to partial chiral symmetry restoration, while low invariant mass dilepton production is enhanced.
* Electric conductivity from the hadronic gas is about an order of magnitude smaller than lattice results, which lie in the range 0.3 < σ_E / (ẽ^2 T) < 0.8 for 3 flavors (Ding 2011, Kaczmarek 2013).
* The sQGP electric conductivity with constant condensates is much smaller than lattice estimates; using T-dependent condensates ⟨α_s E^2⟩ ≈ ⟨α_s B^2⟩ ≈ 48 T^4 gives σ_E/(ẽ^2 T) ≈ 0.5, matching the mean lattice value.
* The flavor diffusion constant for non-singlet quarks is estimated as T D_f^{NS} ≈ 1/2 using the central lattice conductivity, and the drag coefficient η_f/T ≈ 2/π across the transition temperature.
* The vacuum gluon condensate is taken as ⟨α_s G^2⟩_0 = 0.068 GeV^4 (Narison 2009).
* The leading QGP flavor susceptibility is χ_f = (N_c/3) T^2, and lattice results show quark susceptibilities drop by 15–25% compared to the Stefan-Boltzmann limit near T_c (Bazavov 2013).

## Context

This work extends the spectral function approach for hadronic electromagnetic emissivities developed by Steele (1996, 1997) and Dusling (2007–2010) to include finite pion chemical potential, and compares with the sQGP description using gluon condensates originally formulated by Hansson and Zahed (1987) and applied to thermal dileptons by Lee (1999). The results are compared to lattice simulations of electromagnetic spectral functions by Ding (2011) and Kaczmarek (2013), and to the lower bound on electric conductivity by Burnier and Laine (2012). The paper enables future work on connecting electromagnetic observables from heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC to the transport properties (conductivity, diffusion, drag) of the QCD medium across the phase transition.
