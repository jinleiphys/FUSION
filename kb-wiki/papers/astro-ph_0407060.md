# Helioseismological Implications of Recent Solar Abundance Determinations

> We show that standard solar models are in good agreement with the helioseismologically determined sound speed and density as a function of solar radius, the depth of the convective zone, and the surface helium abundance, as long as those models do not incorporate the most recent heavy element abundance determinations. However, sophisticated new analyses of the solar atmosphere infer lower abundances of the lighter metals (like C, N, O, Ne, and Ar) than the previously widely used surface abundances. We show that solar models that include the lower heavy element abundances disagree with the solar profiles of sound speed and density as well as the depth of the convective zone and the helium abundance. The disagreements for models with the new abundances range from factors of several to many times the quoted uncertainties in the helioseismological measurements. The disagreements are at temperatures below what is required for solar interior fusion reactions and therefore do not significantly affect solar neutrino emission. If errors in thecalculated OPAL opacities are solely responsible for the disagreements, then the corrections in the opacity must extend from 2 times 10^6 K (R = 0.7R_Sun)to 5 times 10^6 K (R = 0.4 R_Sun), with opacity increases of order 10%.

## Key claim

Standard solar models that incorporate the older Grevesse & Sauval (1998) heavy element abundances agree well with helioseismological data, but models using the newer, lower abundances for lighter metals (C, N, O, Ne, Ar) disagree with measured sound speed, density, convective zone depth, and surface helium abundance by factors of several to many times the quoted uncertainties; however, these disagreements occur at temperatures too low to significantly affect solar neutrino emission.

## Method

The authors constructed six solar models using the Garching stellar evolution code with OPAL radiative opacities and OPAL equations of state, varying the heavy element abundances (older Grevesse & Sauval 1998 composition with Z/X=0.0229 versus newer determinations with Z/X=0.0176) and artificially increasing the radiative opacity in some models. They compared model predictions to helioseismological inversions of sound speed and density profiles from Michelson Doppler Imager (MDI) data on the SOHO spacecraft, using the Subtractive Optimally Localized Averages (SOLA) inversion technique.

## Key numbers

- Convective zone depth measured by helioseismology: R_CZ = 0.713 ± 0.001 R_sun; models with old abundances (BP00, BP04) give R_CZ = 0.7141 and 0.7146 R_sun respectively, while the model with new abundances (BP04+) gives R_CZ = 0.7259 R_sun
- Surface helium abundance from helioseismology: Y = 0.2485 ± 0.0034; models with old abundances give Y_surf = 0.243, while the model with new abundances gives Y_surf = 0.238
- RMS fractional sound-speed difference for BP00 model: 0.0010 (0.1%); for BP04+ model with new abundances: 0.0046 (more than a factor of three worse)
- RMS fractional density difference for BP00 model: 0.005; for BP04+ model: 0.037
- A model with new abundances and a constant 11% opacity increase from 2 × 10^6 K (R = 0.7 R_sun) to 5 × 10^6 K (R = 0.4 R_sun) yields RMS sound-speed difference of 0.0014 and convective zone depth of 0.7162 R_sun

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing program of precision solar model comparisons with helioseismology (Bahcall & Pinsonneault 1995; Bahcall, Pinsonneault & Basu 2001) by testing the impact of new lower heavy-element abundance determinations from Asplund et al. (2000, 2004) and Allende Prieto et al. (2001, 2002). It identifies a "convective zone problem" where models with the new abundances disagree strongly with helioseismic data, and demonstrates that opacity increases of order 10% over a broad temperature range could resolve the discrepancy. This work enables future investigations into whether errors in OPAL opacities or systematic uncertainties in the new abundance determinations are responsible, and provides a benchmark for opacity calculations from the Opacity Project (Seaton & Badnell 2004).
