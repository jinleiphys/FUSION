# Hadron Physics and QCD: Just the Basic Facts

> With discovery of the Higgs boson, the Standard Model of Particle Physics became complete. Its formulation is a remarkable story; and the process of verification is continuing, with the most important chapter being the least well understood. Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is that part of the Standard Model which is supposed to describe all of nuclear physics and yet, almost fifty years after the discovery of quarks, we are only just beginning to understand how QCD moulds the basic bricks for nuclei: pious, neutrons, protons. QCD is characterized by two emergent phenomena: confinement and dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB), whose implications are extraordinary. This contribution describes how DCSB, not the Higgs boson, generates more than 98% of the visible mass in the Universe, explains why confinement guarantees that condensates, those quantities that were commonly viewed as constant mass-scales that fill all spacetime, are instead wholly contained within hadrons, and elucidates a range of observable consequences of confinement and DCSB whose measurement is the focus of a vast international experimental programme.

## Key claim

Dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB), not the Higgs boson, generates more than 98% of the visible mass in the Universe, and confinement guarantees that condensates are wholly contained within hadrons rather than being constant mass-scales that fill all spacetime.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework of Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) using Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) and lattice-regularised QCD to analyze dressed-quark and dressed-gluon propagators, Bethe-Salpeter amplitudes for pions, and parton distribution functions. It compares top-down calculations from gauge-sector gap equations with bottom-up fits to hadron observables, demonstrating unification of these approaches via a renormalisation-group-invariant running interaction.

## Key numbers

- DCSB generates approximately 98% of the proton's mass, with the proton's mass being two orders-of-magnitude larger than the sum of the current-masses of its three valence quarks.
- The dressed-quark mass function yields an effective spectrum mass M_D ~ 350 MeV for a constituent-like quark at low energies.
- The gluon mass function at zero momentum is m_g(0) ≈ 0.5 GeV, with a corresponding length-scale ς ≈ 0.5 fm characterizing the emergence of a maximum wavelength for gluons and quarks.
- The QCD vacuum contribution to the cosmological constant exceeds the observed value by a factor of 10^46, which is reduced by putting condensates back into hadrons.
- The running interaction strength at zero momentum is α_s(0) ≈ 0.9 π, with all curves identical on the perturbative domain k^2 > 2.5 GeV^2.

## Context

This paper extends the understanding of confinement and DCSB as emergent phenomena in QCD, building on foundational work by Brodsky (1973, 1974), Farrar (1979), and Lepage (1979, 1980) on parton-model scaling, and on lattice-QCD simulations by Bowman (2005) and others. It enables future experimental programmes at Jefferson Lab (JLab 12), Fermilab, and a possible electron ion collider (EIC) to test predictions for hadron elastic and transition form factors, parton distribution functions on the valence domain, and the infrared running of QCD's coupling and dressed-masses.
