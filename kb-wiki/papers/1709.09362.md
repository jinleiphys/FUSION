# Bose-Einstein condensation in heavy ion collisions: importance of and uncertainties in the finite volume corrections

## Key claim
The density of the Bose-Einstein condensate for non-interacting pions in a cubic box is much larger than predicted from thermodynamics and depends significantly on the choice of boundary conditions, even for volumes as large as 6.4 × 10⁴ fm³. The thermodynamic approximation grossly underestimates the condensate amount at given temperature and overall pion density, and the finite volume corrections cannot be calculated reliably without specifying the boundary conditions.

## Method
The author calculates the condensate fraction for non-interacting pions (mass 140 MeV) in a cubic box of volume L³ at fixed temperature and total pion density, using three different boundary condition models: periodic boundary conditions, wave functions vanishing at the boundaries, and free boundary conditions (continuous momentum spectrum). The total pion density is set to 20/19 of the thermodynamic maximum non-condensate density at μ = E₀, corresponding to a 5% condensate fraction in the thermodynamic limit. The chemical potential is then determined self-consistently from the relation between total density, condensate density, and excited-state density, and the resulting condensate fractions are compared across models and with the thermodynamic approximation.

## Key numbers
- Pion mass assumed: m = 140 MeV
- Temperature used for main results: T = 100 MeV (also checked at T = 150 MeV)
- Edge length range considered: 0 ≤ L ≤ 40 fm
- Maximum volume studied: L³ = 6.4 × 10⁴ fm³
- At L = 40 fm and T = 100 MeV, ratio of exact condensate fraction to thermodynamic approximation: 2.3 for periodic boundary conditions, 4.2 for wave functions vanishing at boundaries, 1.7 for free boundary conditions
- At L = 40 fm and T = 150 MeV, corresponding ratios: 1.8 for periodic, 3.4 for vanishing boundaries, 1.4 for free boundary conditions

## Context
This work extends the finite-volume corrections to Bose-Einstein condensate density previously estimated by Begun and Gorenstein for a free pion fluid, and by Ayala, Mercado and Villavicencio for a pion fluid in a uniform magnetic field. The author shows that the choice of boundary conditions introduces large uncertainties that persist up to volumes of 6.4 × 10⁴ fm³, which is relevant for interpreting the observed surplus of low-transverse-momentum pions at LHC energies as reported by Begun and Florkowski (2014). The results indicate that reliable predictions for condensate fractions in heavy ion collisions require both a realistic treatment of boundary conditions and inclusion of finite volume effects beyond the thermodynamic limit.
