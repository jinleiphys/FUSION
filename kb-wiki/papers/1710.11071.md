# Envelope and phase distribution of a resonance transmission through a complex environment

> A transmission amplitude is considered for quantum or wave transport mediated by a single resonance coupled to the background of many chaotic states. Such a model provides a useful approach to quantify fluctuations in an established signal induced by a complex environment. Applying random matrix theory to the problem, we derive an exact result for the joint distribution of the transmission intensity (envelope) and the transmission phase at arbitrary coupling to the background with finite absorption. The intensity and phase are distributed within a certain region, revealing essential correlations even at strong absorption. In the latter limit, we obtain a simple asymptotic expression that provides a uniformly good approximation of the exact distribution within its whole support, thus going beyond the Rician distribution often used for such purposes. Exact results are also derived for the marginal distribution of the phase, including its limiting forms at weak and strong absorption.

## Key claim

The paper derives an exact joint distribution for the transmission intensity (envelope) and phase of a single resonance coupled to a chaotic background, valid at arbitrary coupling strength and finite absorption; the intensity and phase remain correlated even at strong absorption, contrary to what simpler models like the Rician distribution would suggest.

## Method

The author models the chaotic background using a random matrix from the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) and represents the resonant transmission amplitude as a function of the background's local Green's function. The joint probability density function of the transmission intensity and phase is obtained by a change of variables from the known joint distribution of the real and imaginary parts of that Green's function, yielding an exact expression in terms of the function \(P_0(x)\) which encodes the absorption rate. The marginal phase distribution is then derived by integrating over the intensity.

## Key numbers

* The mean transmission amplitude at any absorption is \(\langle t \rangle = (1+\eta)^{-1}\), where \(\eta = \Gamma_\downarrow / \Gamma_0\) is the ratio of the spreading width to the escape width.
* At zero absorption, the joint distribution is singular: \(\mathcal{P}_{\eta,\gamma=0}(T,\theta) = \delta(T - \cos^2\theta) \mathcal{P}_0(\theta)\), with the phase distribution \(\mathcal{P}_0(\theta) = 1/[\pi (\eta \cos^2\theta + \eta^{-1} \sin^2\theta)]\).
* In the strong-absorption limit (\(\gamma \gg 1\)), the asymptotic joint distribution is given by Eq. (22), with the variances of \(\sqrt{T}\) and \(\theta\) being \(\sigma_T^2 = 4\eta^2 / [\gamma (1+\eta)^4]\) and \(\sigma_\theta^2 = 4\eta^2 / [\gamma (1+\eta)^2]\).
* For weak absorption (\(\gamma \ll 1\)), the phase distribution is approximately \(\mathcal{P}_{\gamma\ll1}(\theta) \approx \mathcal{P}_0(\theta) [\mathrm{erfc}(\sqrt{\mu}) + 2\sqrt{\mu/\pi} e^{-\mu}]\) with \(\mu = (\gamma/4\eta)(\sec^2\theta - 1 + \eta)\).

## Context

This work extends the author's previous exact results for the intensity distribution (Savin 2017) and the joint distribution of reflection and transmission (Savin 2017a) to include the transmission phase. The model is relevant to atomic and nuclear physics (Sokolov 1997, Harney 1986, Zelevinsky 2016), open mesoscopic systems (Aberg 2008, Guhr 2009, Sokolov 2010), and wireless communications (Simon, Yeh 2012). The exact results enable quantitative tests in microwave cavity experiments (Kuhl 2005, Dietz 2010, Gradoni 2014) where the full scattering matrix, including phases, is now accessible.
