# Photoproduction of the Etas near Threshold

> We extract a parameter $ξ$, a characteristic product of the strong and electromagnetic amplitudes for the N^*(1535), to be (2.20+-0.15) in unit of 10^(-4)MeV^(-1) from the new data on photoproduction of etas off proton.

## Key claim

The parameter ξ, a characteristic product of the strong and electromagnetic amplitudes for the N*(1535) resonance, can be extracted from new photoproduction data in a model-independent manner, yielding a stable value of (2.20 ± 0.15) in units of 10⁻⁴ MeV⁻¹, even though the extraction of the helicity amplitude A₁/₂ itself remains model-dependent.

## Method

The authors performed eight different fits to new differential and total cross section data for the reaction γp → pη from threshold to 790 MeV lab photon energy, comparing two approaches: a simple Breit-Wigner form assuming the E₀₊ amplitude comes entirely from the N*(1535), and an effective Lagrangian approach that includes background contributions from nucleon Born terms, vector meson exchange, and the N*(1520) [D13] resonance. The fits varied resonance parameters such as W_R, Γ_R, and the branching ratio b_η, and the stability of the extracted ξ parameter was evaluated across all fits.

## Key numbers

- ξ = (2.20 ± 0.15) in units of 10⁻⁴ MeV⁻¹, extracted from the new photoproduction data set of Krusche et al. (1995).
- Two extreme Breit-Wigner fits used parameters: W_R = 1549 MeV, Γ_R = 202 MeV, b_η = 0.55 (row a/d); and W_R = 1539 MeV, Γ_R = 208 MeV, b_η = 0.35 (row b).
- The data cover the reaction γp → pη from threshold to 790 MeV lab photon energy.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Krusche et al. (1995) by showing that their simple Breit-Wigner extraction of the helicity amplitude A₁/₂ is model-dependent, while the parameter ξ introduced in the authors' earlier effective Lagrangian study (Mukhopadhyay, Zhang, and Benmerrouche, 1995) remains stable across different fitting approaches. The inclusion of the N*(1520) [D13] resonance along with nucleon and vector meson exchange terms improves fits to differential cross sections, but further progress requires polarization data that were not yet available at the time.
