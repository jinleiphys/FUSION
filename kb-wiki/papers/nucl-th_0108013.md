# A Realistic Solvable Model for the Coulomb Dissociation of Neutron Halo Nuclei

> As a model of a neutron halo nucleus we consider a neutron bound to an inert core by a zero range force. We study the breakup of this simple nucleus in the Coulomb field of a target nucleus. In the post-form DWBA (or, in our simple model CWBA (``Coulomb Wave Born Approximation'')) an analytic solution for the T-matrix is known. We study limiting cases of this T-matrix. As it should be, we recover the Born approximation for weak Coulomb fields (i.e., for the relevant Coulomb parameters much smaller than 1). For strong Coulomb fields, high beam energies, and scattering to the forward region we find a result which is very similar to the Born result. It is only modified by a relative phase (close to 0) between the two terms and a prefactor (close to 1). A similar situation exists for bremsstrahlung emission. This formula can be related to the first order semiclassical treatment of the electromagnetic dissociation. Since our CWBA model contains the electromagnetic interaction between the core and the target nucleus to all orders, this means that higher order effects (including postacceleration effects) are small in the case of high beam energies and forward scattering. Our model also predicts a scaling behavior of the differential cross section, that is, different systems (with different binding energies, beam energies and scattering angles) show the same dependence on two variables x and y.

## Key claim

For high beam energies and forward scattering, the fully quantal Coulomb Wave Born Approximation (CWBA) T-matrix for neutron halo breakup reduces to a form nearly identical to the first-order Born result, even when the Coulomb parameters \(\eta_a\) and \(\eta_c\) are large; the only modifications are a small relative phase (close to 0) and a prefactor (close to 1), implying that higher-order electromagnetic effects and postacceleration are small in this regime.

## Method

The authors model a neutron halo nucleus as a neutron bound to an inert core by a zero-range force, and study its breakup in the Coulomb field of a target nucleus. They evaluate the T-matrix analytically in the post-form Distorted Wave Born Approximation (termed CWBA for this model) using the Nordsieck formula, which expresses the result in terms of a hypergeometric function and its derivative. By applying a linear transformation to the hypergeometric function's argument (making it close to 0) and keeping only the lowest-order term, they derive a simplified analytic expression for the T-matrix valid for high beam energies and forward scattering.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy \(E_{\mathrm{bind}}\) is related to \(\kappa\) by \(E_{\mathrm{bind}} = \hbar^2 \kappa^2 / (2\mu)\), where \(\mu = m_n m_c/(m_n+m_c)\).
- The zero-range constant \(D_0\) is given by \(D_0 = (\hbar^2/(2\mu)) \sqrt{8\pi \kappa}\).
- For deuteron breakup on \(^{197}\)Au at \(E_d = 12\) MeV, the measured proton energy maximum is \(\sim 7.5\) MeV and the neutron energy maximum is \(\sim 2.5\) MeV, demonstrating postacceleration.
- The condition for the nonadiabatic (sudden) regime is \(q_\perp \gg 2 \eta_a q_\|\), where \(q_\| = \omega/v\) with \(\hbar\omega = E_{\mathrm{bind}} + E_{\mathrm{rel}}\).
- The scaling formula for the breakup probability in lowest-order semiclassical theory is \(dP_{\mathrm{LO}}/dq = (16 y^2)/(3\pi\kappa) \cdot x^4/(1+x^2)^4\), where \(x = q/\kappa\) and \(y = 2 Z Z_c m_n e^2/(\hbar v_a m_a b \kappa)\).

## Context

This work extends the analytic treatment of Coulomb breakup from deuteron reactions (Baur 1972) to neutron halo nuclei such as \(^{11}\)Be and \(^{19}\)C, which were studied experimentally by Nakamura (1999, 1994). The model provides a "theoretical laboratory" to compare the fully quantal CWBA with the semiclassical approach used by Typel and Baur (2001) and Baur, Hencken, and Trautmann (2001). It enables a rigorous test of whether higher-order electromagnetic effects and postacceleration are negligible for high-energy, forward-angle Coulomb dissociation, which is relevant for extracting photoneutron cross-sections from dissociation measurements (Leistenschneider 2001, Aumann 2001) and for using Coulomb dissociation to study radiative capture reactions of astrophysical interest.
