# The Concept of Potential in Quantum Field Theory

> In quantum field theory the concept of a Lagrangian interaction density, expressed in terms of fields, is primary. Forces between two particles are regarded as arising primarily from the exchange of quanta of the bosonic fields. Thus, in contrast to nonrelativistic quantum mechanics, the concept of a two-body poten tial is secondary. Potentials are not given a priori but must be defined. Part of the purpose of this talk is to review and discuss the issues involved when such definitions are made. In this context I describe a gauge-independent approach to some aspects of the problem of determining the energy levels of bound states, developed in collaboration with the late G. Feinberg, and report some recent results on the long-range potential associated with two-photon exchange between charged particles.

## Key claim

In quantum field theory, two-body potentials are not fundamental but must be defined from the underlying Lagrangian interaction density; the paper argues that a gauge-independent definition of such potentials can be obtained from on-shell scattering amplitudes via a spectral representation, avoiding the gauge-fixing and truncation issues that plague the Bethe-Salpeter approach.

## Method

The authors define an effective potential operator V as the Fourier transform of an on-shell Feynman amplitude, modified by subtractions to remove double counting from iteration of lower-order potentials. For spin-0 particles, a local potential U is extracted from the spectral function ρ(s,t) via the Laplace transform relation U(r;p²) = (16π² m_A m_B)⁻¹ ∫ dt ρ(s,t) exp(-√t r), and the potential is constrained to reproduce the center-of-mass scattering amplitude when used in a relativistic Schrödinger equation with kinetic operator h₀ = √(m_A² + p²) + √(m_B² + p²). The spectral function is computed using Cutkosky's generalized unitarity rules.

## Key numbers

* The Yukawa potential for massive scalar exchange is U_Y = -(g_A g_B / 4π r) exp(-μ r), where μ is the meson mass and g_i = G_i/2m_i are dimensionless coupling constants.
* For two-pion exchange at p² = 0, the net fourth-order potential is U_net^(4) = (m/μ)² (g_A g_B / 4π r)² exp(-2μ r) [1 - η (μ r)^(-1/2) + O(r⁻¹)], with η = 4m/μ√π; for μ/M ≈ 1/7 (pion-nucleon mass ratio), η ≈ 15, so the r^(-5/2) term dominates the r⁻² term at distances up to about 10 fermi.
* The long-range potential from massless quanta exchange has the asymptotic expansion U^LR(r; p²) = c₂(p²) r⁻² + c₃(p²) r⁻³ + c₄(p²) r⁻⁴ + ..., where c_n(p²) = [(n-2)!/8π² m_A m_B] a_n(s) and the a_n(s) are coefficients from the Laurent expansion of the spectral function ρ(s,t) = a₂(s) t^(-1/2) + a₃(s) + a₄(s) t^(1/2) + ... near t = 0.

## Context

This work extends the program of deriving effective potentials from quantum field theory using S-matrix methods, building on earlier studies of long-range forces between composite systems by Feinberg and Sucher (1970s-1980s). It contrasts with the Bethe-Salpeter equation approach, which requires a messy reduction to equal times and destroys gauge invariance when the kernel is truncated, and with Tamm-Dancoff type calculations. The method enables direct computation of potentials for use in three-dimensional wave equations while maintaining Lorentz and gauge invariance at every stage, and is applied here to both one- and two-quantum exchange between spinless particles, with extension to photon exchange between charged particles.
