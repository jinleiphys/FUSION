# Implications of Theoretical Ideas Regarding Cold Fusion

> A lot of theoretical ideas have been floated to explain the so called cold fusion phenomenon. I look at a large subset of these and study further physical implications of the concepts involved. I suggest that these can be tested by other independent physical means. Because of the significance of these the experimentalists are urged to look for these signatures. The results in turn will be important for a better understanding and hence control of the cold fusion phenomenon.

# Implications of Theoretical Ideas Regarding Cold Fusion

## Key claim

Theoretical models that explain cold fusion by positing a reduced deuteron separation in the D₂ molecule (by a factor of five from the equilibrium 0.74 Å) or a reduced electron-nucleus distance should produce measurable changes in the quadrupole interaction constant of the D₂ molecule under cold fusion conditions, which can be detected through hyperfine spectroscopy.

## Method

The author examines a large subset of theoretical models proposed for cold fusion and categorizes them into three types: those requiring decreased internuclear distance in D₂, those requiring decreased electron-nucleus distance, and those mixing both effects. For each category, the author derives predictions for the quadrupole interaction energy of the D₂ molecule using standard molecular spectroscopy formalism, specifically the dependence of the molecular field gradient q_J on internuclear distance R and electron charge distribution. The author then proposes that experimental measurement of the quadrupole interaction constant under cold fusion conditions can test which theoretical category applies, with rotational spectroscopy suggested as a way to isolate the internuclear distance effect.

## Key numbers

- The equilibrium internuclear distance of the D₂ molecule in free space is 0.74 Å, and some cold fusion models require this distance to decrease by a factor of five under experimental conditions
- The free-space electron-nucleus distance in D₂ is 0.5 Å, which some models require to decrease under cold fusion conditions

## Context

This paper addresses the theoretical confusion surrounding the cold fusion phenomenon following the initial claims and counterclaims of 1989, as documented in experimental work by Fleishman and Pons (1993), Karabut et al. (1992), and others. The author builds on the formalism of Townes and Schawlow (1975) for microwave spectroscopy and the historical determination of the deuteron quadrupole moment by Kellog et al. (1939). The proposed hyperfine spectroscopy tests could help distinguish between competing theoretical explanations and enable better understanding and control of the cold fusion phenomenon, which remains experimentally sporadic with unknown optimum conditions.
