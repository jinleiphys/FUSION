# Nambu-Goldstone modes in the random phase approximation

> I show that the kernel of the random phase approximation (RPA) matrix based on a stable Hartree, Hartree-Fock, Hartree-Bogolyubov or Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov mean field solution is decomposed into a subspace with a basis whose vectors are associated, in the equivalent formalism of a classical Hamiltonian homogeneous of second degree in canonical coordinates, with conjugate momenta of cyclic coordinates (Nambu-Goldstone modes) and a subspace with a basis whose vectors are associated with pairs of a coordinate and its conjugate momentum neither of which enters the Hamiltonian at all. In a subspace complementary to the one spanned by all these coordinates including the conjugate coordinates of the Nambu-Goldstone momenta, the RPA matrix behaves as in the case of a zerodimensional kernel. This result was derived very recently by Nakada as a corollary to a general analysis of RPA matrices based on both stable and unstable mean field solutions. The present proof does not rest on Nakada's general results.

# Nambu-Goldstone modes in the random phase approximation

## Key claim

The paper proves that when the RPA stability matrix is positive semidefinite (as occurs when a mean-field solution violates a continuous symmetry), the vector space decomposes into three subspaces: one corresponding to coordinates and momenta that do not appear in the Hamiltonian at all, one corresponding to Nambu-Goldstone mode pairs (cyclic coordinates and their conjugate momenta), and a residual space where the RPA matrix behaves as if the stability matrix were positive definite. This result was derived very recently by Nakada as a corollary to a general analysis, but the present proof does not rest on Nakada's general results.

## Method

The author starts from the standard RPA matrix M and stability matrix S, then applies a unitary transformation to obtain a real symmetric positive semidefinite diagonal matrix Δ and an imaginary antisymmetric matrix N satisfying N² = 1. For the case of a one-dimensional kernel of Δ, the author constructs basis vectors x⁽¹⁾ and x⁽²⁾ that correspond to a cyclic coordinate and its conjugate momentum, then shows the residual space is invariant under iM and that M restricted to this space is similar to the original form with positive definite Δ. The proof is then generalized to a multidimensional kernel of arbitrary dimension m, where the kernel is further decomposed into vectors associated with coordinates entirely absent from the Hamiltonian and those associated with Nambu-Goldstone modes.

## Key numbers

- The kernel of the stability matrix Δ can have dimension m greater than one; within this kernel, a subspace of dimension m' (which is even) corresponds to coordinates and momenta entirely absent from the Hamiltonian.
- For the one-dimensional kernel case, the residual space has dimension 2n - 2, where 2n is the dimension of the total space.
- For the multidimensional kernel case with m' > 0, the residual space has dimension 2n - (2m - m').

## Context

This work extends the standard analysis of the RPA matrix when the stability matrix is positive definite, as described in textbooks such as Ring and Schuck (1980) and earlier work by Thouless (1960-1961). It provides a rigorous proof of the interpretation of zero-energy RPA modes as Nambu-Goldstone modes, which had been assumed but not formally proven until very recently. The result was derived independently and nearly simultaneously by Nakada (2016) as a corollary to a more general analysis of RPA matrices with no definiteness assumptions on the stability matrix; the present proof avoids reliance on that general formalism. This enables a cleaner understanding of symmetry-breaking in mean-field approaches to nuclear structure and other many-body systems.
