# Regularity and chaos in the nuclear masses

> Shell effects in atomic nuclei are a quantum mechanical manifestation of the single--particle motion of the nucleons. They are directly related to the structure and fluctuations of the single--particle spectrum. Our understanding of these fluctuations and of their connections with the regular or chaotic nature of the nucleonic motion has greatly increased in the last decades. In the first part of these lectures these advances, based on random matrix theories and semiclassical methods, are briefly reviewed. Their consequences on the thermodynamic properties of Fermi gases and, in particular, on the masses of atomic nuclei are then presented. The structure and importance of shell effects in the nuclear masses with regular and chaotic nucleonic motion are analyzed theoretically, and the results are compared to experimental data. We clearly display experimental evidence of both types of motion

# Regularity and chaos in the nuclear masses

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the fluctuating (dynamical) component of nuclear masses can be separated into contributions from regular and chaotic single-particle motion, and that experimental mass data show evidence of both types of motion, with a dominant regular component described by a spheroidal potential plus additional chaotic layers that explain discrepancies between experimental data and previous theoretical calculations.

## Method
The author reviews random matrix theory and semiclassical trace formula methods for describing single-particle level fluctuations, then applies these to compute shell corrections to nuclear masses for regular (integrable) and chaotic dynamics. The theoretical predictions for mass fluctuations are compared to experimental nuclear mass data, using a spheroidal cavity model with finite quasiparticle lifetime to describe the regular component, while chaotic contributions are computed from the hypothesis of chaotic layers in the nuclear ground state.

## Key numbers
- The mean level spacing δ is related to the Heisenberg time τ_H by δ = h/τ_H (Eq. rhob0)
- For a 3D cavity, the smooth counting function is given by N̄ = (V/6π²)(2m/ħ²)^(3/2) E^(3/2) - (S/16π)(2m/ħ²)E + (L/8π)(2m/ħ²)^(1/2) E^(1/2) + O(1) (Eq. nb)
- The characteristic energy scale for shell effects is E_c = h/τ_min, where τ_min is the shortest periodic orbit time (Eq. ec)
- For the spherical cavity, the smooth counting function is N̄_sph(ε) = (2/9π)ε^(3/2) - (1/4)ε + (2/3π)ε^(1/2) (Eq. nssa)
- The form factor for random matrix theory (β=2) is K_rmt(τ) = τ Θ(τ_H - τ) + τ_H Θ(τ - τ_H) (Eq. krmt)
- The form factor for uncorrelated (Poisson) levels is K_unc(τ) = τ_H (Eq. kint)

## Context
This lecture paper extends the Bohigas-Giannoni-Schmit conjecture (BGS, 1984) connecting random matrix theory to classically chaotic systems, and applies it to nuclear masses following the tradition of shell correction methods (Bohr and Mottelson; Strutinsky). The work builds on prior semiclassical treatments of shell effects by Balian and Bloch (1970s) and Brack (1990s), and on the author's own collaborations with Bohigas and Monastra. The analysis enables a quantitative separation of regular and chaotic contributions to nuclear binding energies, potentially improving mass predictions for nuclei far from stability and connecting nuclear structure to the broader field of quantum chaos in mesoscopic systems.
