# Higher and missing resonances in omega photoproduction

> We study the role of the nucleon resonances ($N^*$) in $ω$ photoproduction by using the quark model resonance parameters predicted by Capstick and Roberts. The employed $γN \to N^*$ and $N^* \to ωN$ amplitudes include the configuration mixing effects due to the residual quark-quark interactions. The contributions from the nucleon resonances are found to be important in the differential cross sections at large scattering angles and various spin observables. In particular, the parity asymmetry and beam-target double asymmetry at forward scattering angles are suggested for a crucial test of our predictions. The dominant contributions are found to be from $N\frac32^+ (1910)$, a missing resonance, and $N\frac32^- (1960)$ which is identified as the $D_{13}(2080)$ of the Particle Data Group.

## Key claim

The nucleon resonances $N\frac32^+ (1910)$ (a missing resonance) and $N\frac32^- (1960)$ (identified as the two-star resonance $D_{13}(2080)$ of the Particle Data Group) dominate the resonance contributions to $\omega$ photoproduction at all energies up to $E_\gamma = 3$ GeV, and the parity asymmetry and beam-target double asymmetry at forward angles provide a crucial test of this prediction.

## Method

The authors constructed a model for $\omega$ photoproduction including non-resonant amplitudes from Pomeron exchange (Donnachie-Landshoff model), pseudoscalar meson ($\pi$, $\eta$) exchange, and direct and crossed nucleon pole terms, then added $s$-channel nucleon resonance ($N^*$) contributions using quark model parameters from Capstick and Roberts (1994) that include configuration mixing effects from residual quark-quark interactions. They considered 12 positive parity and 10 negative parity nucleon resonances up to spin-9/2, calculating the $\gamma N \to N^*$ photoexcitation and $N^* \to \omega N$ decay amplitudes using the ${}^3P_0$ model.

## Key numbers

- Differential cross sections were calculated at photon energies $E_\gamma = 1.23$, 1.45, 1.68, 1.92, 2.8, and 4.7 GeV, covering the range $E_\gamma \leq 5$ GeV
- Pseudoscalar meson exchange coupling constants used: $g_{\pi NN}^2/4\pi = 14$, $g^2_{\eta NN}/4\pi = 0.99$, $g_{\omega\gamma\pi} = 1.823$, $g_{\omega\gamma\eta} = 0.416$
- Form factor cutoffs: $\Lambda_\pi = 0.6$ GeV, $\Lambda_{\omega\gamma\pi} = 0.7$ GeV, $\Lambda_\eta = 1.0$ GeV, $\Lambda_{\omega\gamma\eta} = 0.9$ GeV, $\Lambda_N = 0.5$ GeV
- Nucleon anomalous magnetic moments: $\kappa_p = 1.79$, $\kappa_n = -1.91$; $\omega NN$ coupling $g_{\omega NN} = 10.35$, $\kappa_\omega = 0$
- The two dominant resonances, $N\frac32^+ (1910)$ and $N\frac32^- (1960)$, give the largest contributions at all energies up to $E_\gamma = 3$ GeV

## Context

This work extends the study of the "missing resonance problem" (where constituent quark models predict more nucleon resonances than observed in $\pi N$ scattering) by examining $\omega$ photoproduction, whose isosinglet nature isolates isospin-1/2 resonances. It contrasts with the SU(6) $\times$ O(3) quark model calculations of Zhao et al. (1998, 2001), finding significantly different resonance contributions due to the inclusion of configuration mixing effects. The results enable experimental tests at facilities such as TJNAF, ELSA-SAPHIR, GRAAL, and LEPS at SPring-8, and suggest that measuring parity asymmetry and beam-target double asymmetry at forward angles could help resolve the missing resonance problem.
