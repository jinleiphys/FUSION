# Medium Characterization with Hard Probes: From Cherenkov Light in QED to Jet Drift in QCD

> This dissertation presents a unified framework for medium characterization with hard probes spanning from Cherenkov light in quantum electrodynamics (QED) to jet drift in quantum chromodynamics (QCD). We first develop a dispersive fit to the refractive index $n(λ)$ of liquid argon (LAr) by incorporating anomalous dispersion at the 106.6 nm resonance for the first time. We show that the angular distribution of Cherenkov radiation is highly sensitive to the peak of the refractive index and contributes a significant excess over isotropic scintillation in certain angular bins. This work is important for precision Particle Identification (PID) for experiments like DUNE and CCM. Transitioning to high-energy nuclear collisions, we utilize ``jet drift'' -- the flow-induced deflection of partons -- as a tomographic probe of the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP). Using the Anisotropic Parton Evolution (APE) Monte Carlo simulation across various collision systems (PbPb, AuAu, and UU), we disentangle how the jet modification depends on medium size, temperature, and geometry. We show that jet drift exhibits distinct systematics in observables like the elliptic flow ($v_2$) and dihadron acoplanarity ($Δϕ$), which helps disentangle it from conventional energy loss. Together, these studies demonstrate how the angular and kinematic signatures of hard probes revolutionize our ability to resolve the fundamental properties of matter.

## Key claim
This dissertation presents a unified framework for medium characterization using hard probes, showing that the angular distribution of Cherenkov radiation in liquid argon is highly sensitive to the peak of the refractive index near the 106.6 nm resonance, and that jet drift in quark-gluon plasma exhibits distinct systematics in observables like elliptic flow and dihadron acoplanarity that help disentangle it from conventional energy loss.

## Method
The author first developed a dispersive fit to the refractive index of liquid argon by incorporating anomalous dispersion at the 106.6 nm resonance, using prior experimental measurements from Sinnock and Smith (1969) for wavelengths between 361.2 and 643.9 nm and from Babicz et al. (2020) at the scintillation wavelength of 128 nm. For the QCD portion, the author used the Anisotropic Parton Evolution (APE) Monte Carlo simulation, which takes initial hard scattering events from PYTHIA and embeds them into the Duke QCD medium model, to calculate standard energy loss and jet drift across PbPb, AuAu, and UU collision systems.

## Key numbers
- The refractive index of liquid argon at the scintillation wavelength of 128 nm was measured by Babicz et al. (2020) as n = 1.357 ± 0.001
- The liquid argon resonance occurs at a wavelength of 106.6 nm
- The QGP transition temperature is approximately 155 MeV (about 1.8 × 10^12 K)
- Neutrino energies for coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering are around 50 MeV
- Resonance production processes dominate at energy scales between 0.5 GeV and 3.0 GeV

## Context
This work extends the program of jet tomography based on energy loss and nuclear modification factor measurements, which has been developed over two decades by authors including Vitev (2002), the JET Collaboration (2013), and Cao et al. (2020). The jet drift mechanism was introduced by Sadofyev et al. (2021) as a sub-eikonal correction that goes beyond traditional energy loss and isotropic momentum broadening. The Cherenkov analysis builds on the refractive index measurements of Sinnock and Smith (1969) and Babicz et al. (2020) to enable precision particle identification for experiments like DUNE and CCM.
