# Observational signatures of neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries climbing a stability peak

> In the recent papers by Gusakov, Chugunov, and Kantor (2014) a new scenario describing evolution of rapidly rotating neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries was proposed. The scenario accounts for a resonant interaction of normal r modes with superfluid inertial modes at some specific internal stellar temperatures ("resonance temperatures"). This interaction results in an enhanced damping of r mode and appearance of the "stability peaks" in the temperature -- spin frequency plane, which split the r-mode instability window in the vicinity of the resonance temperatures. The scenario suggests that the hot and rapidly rotating NSs spend most of their life climbing up these peaks and, in particular, are observed there at the moment. We analyze in detail possible observational signatures of this suggestion. In particular, we show that these objects may exhibit `anti-glitches' -- sudden frequency jumps on a time scale of hours-months.

## Key claim

Hot and rapidly rotating neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries may spend most of their lives "climbing" stability peaks in the temperature-spin frequency plane, and this scenario predicts that these objects can exhibit anti-glitches (sudden frequency jumps on timescales of hours to months), provided that the condition gamma(A0) > 0 is satisfied, which makes alpha-oscillations inevitable.

## Method

The authors analyze the evolution equations for neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries, consisting of thermal balance, spin frequency evolution, and r-mode amplitude evolution equations (Equations 1-3). They perform a linear stability analysis around the equilibrium solution at the left edge of the stability peak, deriving a criterion for the excitation of oscillations in the r-mode amplitude (alpha-oscillations) based on the sign of the function gamma(A0). They then estimate oscillation parameters such as the maximum r-mode amplitude reached during fully developed oscillations, using energy conservation arguments and assuming saturation at either a thermal floor (alpha_th = 10^-12) or a nonlinear saturation amplitude (alpha_sat = 10^-4).

## Key numbers

* The equilibrium r-mode amplitude alpha_0 is determined by the thermal balance condition F1(T0,Omega0) * Omega0^2 * A0 ≈ F2(T0), where A0 = alpha_0^2.
* For the fiducial thermal floor alpha_th = 10^-12, the coefficient k relating maximum amplitude to equilibrium amplitude varies in the range k ≈ 5-6, where alpha_max ≈ k * alpha_0.
* The ratio of rotational energy to thermal energy in the relevant range of Omega0 and T0 is approximately 10^5, making the spin frequency perturbations delta-Omega negligible compared to temperature perturbations delta-T.
* The cooling timescale t_cool ≈ T0/F2(T0) is less than or approximately 10^6 years, much shorter than the spin-up timescale of approximately 10^8 years.
* For the source 4U 1608-522, the time interval shown in the evolution figure is approximately 3 years, with direct Urca processes forbidden.

## Context

This work extends the scenario proposed by Gusakov, Chugunov, and Kantor (2014), which introduced the concept of stability peaks arising from resonant interaction of r-modes with superfluid inertial modes at specific resonance temperatures. The paper addresses the apparent contradiction noted by Levin (1999) and Heyl (2002) that standard evolution models predict a negligibly small probability of finding neutron stars in the r-mode instability window, despite observations of many neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries falling in that region (as compiled by Haskell et al. 2011 and Ho et al. 2012). The analysis enables predictions for observational signatures including anti-glitches and potentially detectable gravitational radiation, and suggests that existing timing data for sources like 4U 1608-522 may already contain evidence for the predicted alpha-oscillations.
