# Particle production from off-shell nucleons

> Particle production in equilibrium and nonequilibrium quantum systems is calculated. The effects of the off-shell propagation of nucleons in medium on the particle production are discussed. Comparision to the semiclassical production rate is given.

## Key claim

Particle production rates from off-shell nucleons in medium are calculated using quantum Kadanoff-Baym equations, and the results show that quantum effects become important for high-momentum or high-mass mesons, but the differences between quantum and semiclassical rates are smaller in equilibrium than in nonequilibrium systems.

## Method

The author solves the Kadanoff-Baym equations for a spatially homogeneous system of interacting nucleons with an initial momentum distribution corresponding to two Fermi spheres, then calculates the meson production rate using the one-loop meson self-energy. For equilibrium calculations, the real-time relations between fermion Green's functions and the spectral density are iterated, with the spectral density given by the Born self-energy. The quantum production rate is compared to semiclassical rates obtained by cutting the one-loop self-energy diagram and using retarded fermion Green's functions with either in-medium or vacuum matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- Nonequilibrium meson production rate calculated at t = 20 fm/c for a collision energy of 180 MeV
- Equilibrium calculations performed at temperature T = 20 MeV and chemical potential μ = 30 MeV
- Meson production rates compared for meson masses of 140 MeV, 500 MeV, and 20 MeV at equilibrium
- Production rates are scaled by the number of nucleon collisions and divided by λ² (the squared interaction strength)

## Context

This work extends the author's previous calculation of meson production rates in nonequilibrium systems (Bozek, 1997, referred to as m1 in the text) by performing more precise equilibrium calculations and comparing quantum and semiclassical approaches. The results show that medium effects, particularly the real and imaginary parts of the fermion self-energy, substantially influence production rates for soft particles, regularizing the vacuum divergence noted by Klevansky (referred to as kv in the text). The equilibrium spectral function approach offers a numerically efficient alternative to full Kadanoff-Baym solutions, potentially enabling more realistic descriptions of nuclear collision dynamics using local spectral functions.
