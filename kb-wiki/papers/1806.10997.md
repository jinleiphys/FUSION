# Transport peak in thermal spectral function of ${\cal N}=4$ supersymmetric Yang-Mills plasma at intermediate coupling

> We study the structure of thermal spectral function of the stress-energy tensor in ${\cal N}=4$ supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory at intermediate 't Hooft coupling and infinite number of colors. In gauge-string duality, this analysis reduces to the study of classical bulk supergravity with higher-derivative corrections, which correspond to (inverse) coupling corrections on the gauge theory side. We extrapolate the analysis of perturbative leading-order corrections to intermediate coupling by non-perturbatively solving the equations of motion of metric fluctuations dual to the stress-energy tensor at zero spatial momentum. We observe the emergence of a separation of scales in the analytic structure of the thermal correlator associated with two types of characteristic relaxation modes. As a consequence of this separation, the associated spectral function exhibits a narrow structure in the small frequency region which controls the dynamics of transport in the theory and may be described as a transport peak typically found in perturbative, weakly interacting thermal field theories. We compare our results with generic expectations drawn from perturbation theory, where such a structure emerges as a consequence of the existence of quasiparticles.

## Key claim

The paper shows that at intermediate 't Hooft coupling, the thermal spectral function of the stress-energy tensor in N=4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills plasma develops a narrow transport peak at low frequencies, a feature previously associated only with weakly coupled plasmas; this result is obtained by non-perturbatively solving the equations of motion for metric fluctuations with leading-order higher-derivative corrections, effectively resumming a set of gamma-dependent terms.

## Method

The authors study the retarded stress-energy tensor correlator at zero spatial momentum using gauge-gravity duality, where the finite-coupling correction is encoded in a four-derivative Weyl tensor term proportional to gamma ~ lambda^{-3/2} in the five-dimensional bulk action. They solve the second-order ordinary differential equation for the metric fluctuation h_xy, including the gamma-dependent source terms, by treating gamma as a finite parameter rather than a perturbative expansion parameter. The spectral function is then extracted from the near-boundary asymptotics of the numerical solution, and the pole structure of the correlator is analyzed via the quasinormal mode spectrum of the black brane geometry.

## Key numbers

- The shear viscosity eta is related to the spectral function via rho_{xy,xy}(omega,0) = 2 eta omega + O(omega^3), with the low-frequency spectral function approximated as rho^{Hol}_L approx (4 pi T eta Gamma_T^2) / (omega^2 + Gamma_T^2)
- The approximate analytic expression for the low-frequency spectral function is rho^{Hol}_L approx (2 pi^2 N_c^2 T^4 (1 + 135 gamma)) / ((373 gamma - ln 2)^2 omega^2 + 4)
- The ratio Gamma_T / mu_T and Gamma_P / mu_T are rapidly decreasing functions of gamma, with Gamma_T/mu_T << 1 and Gamma_P/mu_T << 1 already for gamma ~ 10^{-2}
- The temperature is related to the non-extremality parameter via T = r_0 (1 + 15 gamma)/pi

## Context

This work extends previous holographic studies of finite-coupling corrections to N=4 SYM plasma, including the analysis of quasinormal mode spectra in higher-derivative gravity by Grozdanov et al. (2016) and the treatment of leading-order corrections by Waeber et al. (2015). The results are compared with perturbative expectations for weakly coupled plasmas, where a transport peak arises from quasiparticle scattering processes with thermal width Gamma_T ~ lambda^2 and thermal mass mu_T ~ lambda, as studied by Moore (2008) and others. The holographic extrapolation enables a qualitative bridge between the strong-coupling limit (where no transport peak exists) and the weak-coupling regime, suggesting that the resummation captures essential low-frequency dynamics even though the high-frequency structures may be more sensitive to omitted higher-order corrections.
