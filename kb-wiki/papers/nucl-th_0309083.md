# P and T odd nuclear moments

> We discuss the nuclear anapole moment, a new P odd, T even electromagnetic multipole. Its discovery in the cesium experiment gives a first-rate information on parity-nonconserving nuclear forces. New prospects in the field are considered. Upper limits on the electric dipole moments, which are P odd, T odd multipoles, of elementary particles and atoms are presented, and their physical implications discussed. The atomic experiments are demonstrated to be as informative in this respect as the neutron ones. Tremendous progress in the field can be expected from experiments at ion storage rings and linear electrostatic traps.

## Key claim

The nuclear anapole moment, a P-odd, T-even electromagnetic multipole, was discovered in cesium and provides first-rate information on parity-nonconserving nuclear forces; the experimental result for the cesium anapole constant indicates that the "best values" of P-odd nuclear forces from Desplanques, Donoghue, and Holstein (1980) somewhat underestimate the magnitude of these forces, though the thallium anapole measurement does not comply with this conclusion.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework for nuclear anapole moments and electric dipole moments (EDMs), combining analytical estimates in the single-particle approximation with more refined many-body calculations using the Woods-Saxon potential, random-phase approximation (RPA), and large-basis shell-model calculations. For the anapole moment of cesium-133, the experimental value is extracted from atomic parity-nonconservation measurements by subtracting the neutral-current nuclear-spin-dependent contribution and the combined action of the weak nuclear charge and hyperfine interaction. For EDMs, the paper derives the Schiff moment formalism to relate nuclear EDMs to observable atomic EDMs, and discusses how limits on CP-odd nuclear forces are obtained from the mercury-199 atomic EDM experiment.

## Key numbers

- Anapole constant for cesium-133: experimental total effective constant \(\kappa_{tot} = 0.44(6)\), with the anapole contribution alone \(\kappa = 0.37(6)\)
- Theoretical predictions for anapole constants at "best values" of P-odd constants: \(\kappa(^{133}{\rm Cs}) = 0.15-0.21\), \(\kappa(^{203,205}{\rm Tl}) = 0.24\), \(\kappa(^{209}{\rm Bi}) = 0.15\)
- Energy separation between opposite-parity levels in beryllium-11: \(|\Delta E| = 0.32\) MeV; binding energy of the odd neutron: \(|\Delta E_0| = 0.50\) MeV
- Neutron EDM upper limit: \(d_n < (6-10) \times 10^{-26}\;e\,{\rm cm}\)
- Electron EDM from thallium experiment: \(d_e = (0.69 \pm 0.74) \times 10^{-27}\;e\,{\rm cm}\)
- Mercury-199 atomic EDM upper limit: \(d(^{199}{\rm Hg}) < 2.1 \times 10^{-28}\;e\,{\rm cm}\)
- Upper limit on CP-odd pion-nucleon coupling constant from mercury: \(g_1 < 0.5 \times 10^{-11}\)
- Standard Model prediction for \(g_1\): \(g_1 \sim 10^{-17}\)
- Lifetime of the ytterbium \(^3D_1\) state: 380(30) ns
- E2 amplitude of the ytterbium \(^1S_0 \to ^3D_2\) transition: \(0.65(3)\;e a_0^2\)
- M1 amplitude of the ytterbium \(^1S_0 \to ^3D_1\) transition: \(1.33(26) \times 10^{-4}\,\mu_{\rm B}\)

## Context

This review extends the theoretical and experimental work on parity-nonconserving nuclear moments, building on the prediction of the anapole moment by Zel'dovich (1957) and its experimental discovery in cesium by Wood et al. (1997). The paper discusses how the cesium anapole result provides information on P-odd nuclear forces that is more reliable than earlier nuclear experiments reviewed by Adelberger and Haxton (1985), and how the mercury-199 EDM limit constrains CP-odd nuclear forces more strongly than the thallium fluoride molecular experiment. The work enables future searches for EDMs in storage rings and linear electrostatic traps, with a proposed muon EDM experiment aiming for sensitivity of \(10^{-24}\;e\,{\rm cm}\), and identifies ytterbium as a promising system for measuring anapole moments in isotopes with valence neutrons.
