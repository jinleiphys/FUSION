# Dynamics of the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal Effect in Au+Au Collisions at 200 AGeV

> We study the role played by the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Midgal (LPM) effect in relativistic collisions of hadrons and heavy nuclei, within a parton cascade model. We find that the LPM effect strongly affects the gluon multiplication due to radiation and considerably alters the space-time evolution of the dynamics of the collision. It ensures a multiplicity distribution of hadrons in aggreement with the experimental proton-proton data. We study the production of single photons in relativistic heavy ion collisions and find that the inclusion of LPM suppression leads to a reduction in the single photon yield at small and intermediate transverse momenta. The parton cascade calculation of the single photon yield including the LPM effect is shown to be in good agreement with the recent PHENIX data taken at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider.

# Dynamics of the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal Effect in Au+Au Collisions at 200 AGeV

## Key claim

The Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal (LPM) effect, implemented via a formation-time-based suppression of radiated partons and photons in a parton cascade model (PCM), simultaneously reproduces the multiplicity distribution in proton-proton collisions and the single photon yield in Au+Au collisions at 200 AGeV, but only after removing zero-multiplicity events from the PCM sample to match non-single-diffractive data. The agreement is described as "fair" for the photon data, with thermal contributions from a fireball model or hydrodynamics needed to improve the low-pT region.

## Method

The authors used a semi-classical parton cascade model (PCM) based on a relativistic Boltzmann equation for quarks and gluons, including all lowest-order QCD 2-to-2 scattering processes and photon emission channels (Compton, annihilation, bremsstrahlung). Final-state radiation was treated via leading-logarithmic parton showers with angular ordering; the LPM effect was implemented by assigning a formation time tau = omega/k_perp^2 to each radiated particle, during which the shower particles do not interact, and if the emitter rescatters within that formation time the entire shower is rejected and removed from the evolution. The GRV-HO parton distribution functions were sampled at an initialization scale Q0^2, and a low momentum-transfer cutoff pT_min was used to regularize infrared divergences.

## Key numbers

- The apparent temperature T* of the gluon transverse momentum spectrum (fit to exp[-pT/T*] in the range 1 GeV < pT < 4 GeV) is 0.44 GeV without LPM suppression and 0.64 GeV with LPM suppression.
- Including LPM suppression leads to a factor of approximately 2.5 reduction in the gluon yield below 0.7 GeV (the pT_min cutoff).
- The collision rate at maximum overlap (t=0) is almost an order of magnitude lower when LPM suppression is included compared to the standard scenario without it.
- Reducing the virtuality cutoff mu0 from 1 GeV to 0.5 GeV leads to less than 30% change in parton production when LPM suppression is included.
- Without rescattering processes, the photon yield in heavy-ion collisions is reduced by a factor of about 3 to 4 compared to the full PCM calculation.
- The LPM-suppressed PCM calculation overpredicts the PHENIX photon data in the region 2 < pT < 3.5 GeV when LPM is not included, but gives a fair description when LPM is included; thermal contributions improve agreement at low pT.

## Context

This work extends the parton cascade model (PCM) of Geiger and Muller (1992) by incorporating the LPM effect, which had previously been studied only in limiting cases (infinitely many or very few rescatterings, static or boost-invariant expanding media) by Gyulassy and Wang (1994), Baier et al. (1997), Zakharov (1996), and Urs (2001). The authors contrast their approach with other hard-scattering models like PYTHIA, the Dual Parton Model, and HIJING, which lack space-time dynamics and cannot address the LPM effect. The results enable a unified description of the regime between soft fluid dynamics and hard pQCD binary-collision scaling, and the authors note that a newer stochastic implementation of the PCM by Greiner and Xu (2004) requires a different modeling of the LPM effect via a lower momentum cutoff on gluon emission rates.
