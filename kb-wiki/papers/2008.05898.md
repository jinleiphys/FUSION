# Simulating core excitation in breakup reactions of halo nuclei using an effective three-body force

> We extend our previous calculation of the breakup of 11Be using Halo Effective Field Theory and the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation to include an effective 10Be-n-target force. The force is constructed to account for the virtual excitation of 10Be to its low-lying 2+ excited state. In the case of breakup on a 12C target this improves the description of the neutron-energy and angular spectra, especially in the vicinity of the 11Be 5/2+ state. By fine-tuning the range parameters of the three-body force, a reasonable description of data in the region of the 3/2+ 11Be state can also be obtained. This sensitivity to the three-body force's range results from the structure of the overlap integral that governs the 11Be s-to-d-state transitions induced by the three-body force.

# Simulating core excitation in breakup reactions of halo nuclei using an effective three-body force

## Key claim

Adding an effective three-body force between the target, core, and halo neutron can reproduce the missing breakup strength in the $^{11}$Be $5/2^+$ resonance region on a $^{12}$C target, but the description of the $3/2^+$ resonance region requires fine-tuning of the three-body force range parameters and may reach the limits of validity for an effective description without explicit $2^+$ core excitation.

## Method

The authors coupled a Halo Effective Field Theory description of $^{11}$Be (with the valence neutron bound to a $^{10}$Be core) to the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation for the reaction dynamics. They added a three-body potential $V_{3b}$ of quadrupole form, constructed to simulate virtual excitation of the $^{10}$Be core to its $2^+$ state, and varied its strength and range parameters in the core-target and core-neutron coordinates to fit breakup data on $^{12}$C at 67 MeV/nucleon.

## Key numbers

- The $^{11}$Be $5/2^+$ resonance lies at 1.27 MeV above the one-neutron threshold; the $3/2^+$ resonance lies at approximately 3 MeV above threshold.
- The $^{10}$Be $2^+$ excitation energy is 3.368 MeV, setting the energy below which the effective three-body force description is best justified.
- The experimental energy resolution used for folding theoretical curves is a Gaussian with $\sigma = 0.191~\mathrm{MeV}^{1/2} \sqrt{E}$; the angular resolution is a Gaussian with $\sigma = 0.48^\circ$.
- With the natural choice $R_0^{cT} = 3.5$ fm and $R_0^{c\rm n} = 1.7$ fm, a three-body force strength $V_0^{3b} = -100$ MeV reproduces the $5/2^+$ peak in both energy and angular distributions.
- The Love and Satchler prescription overestimates the three-body force strength by roughly a factor of five in the $5/2^+$ resonance region.
- With $R_0^{c\rm n} = 1$ fm and $V_0^{3b} = +1000$ MeV, both the $5/2^+$ and $3/2^+$ resonances are excited with breakup strengths in qualitative agreement with data.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Halo EFT + DEA calculation (Capel et al., 2018) which reproduced $^{11}$Be breakup on a Pb target but missed strength at the $5/2^+$ and $3/2^+$ resonances on a $^{12}$C target. The effective three-body force approach is analogous to the core-polarization method of Love and Satchler (1967, 1971) for proton scattering from deformed nuclei, and complements the explicit $2^+$ core-excitation treatment of Moro and Lay (2012). The results enable systematic inclusion of virtual core excitation in Halo EFT without numerically expensive explicit state expansions, and suggest that the $3/2^+$ resonance near the $^{10}$Be($2^+$)-n threshold may require explicit treatment of the $2^+$ degree of freedom.
