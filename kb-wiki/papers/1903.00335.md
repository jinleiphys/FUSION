# Revisiting fission-probability data using $\mathcal{R}$-matrix Monte-Carlo simulations : application to Pu fissile isotopes over the 4 to 8 MeV excitation energy range

> This article describes an original approach to analyze simultaneously cross sections and surrogate data measurements using efficient Monte Carlo extended $\mathcal{R}$-matrix theory algorithm based on unique set of nuclear structure parameters. The alternative analytical path based on the manifold Hauser-Feshbach equation was intensively used in this work to gauge the errors carried by the surrogate-reaction method commonly taken to predict neutron-induced cross sections from observed partial decay probabilities. Present paper emphasizes in particular a dedicated way to treat direct reaction entrance and prior decay excited nucleus outgoing channels widths correlations. Present smart theoretical foundation brought the opportunity to apply successfully our method to both fission-probability data and directly measured neutron cross sections according to Pu fissile isotopes; namely the $^{237, 238, 240, 242~and~244}$Pu$^*$ nuclei. This new capability opens genuine perspectives in matter of 'evaluation process' from foreseen fission- and $γ$-decay probabilities simultaneously measured as derived data will become available.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that surrogate-reaction data can be reliably used to extract neutron-induced fission cross sections for Pu fissile isotopes when analyzed with a Monte Carlo extended R-matrix approach that properly accounts for spin-parity population differences and channel width fluctuation corrections, contrary to the historical surrogate-reaction method which relies on the Weisskopf-Ewing approximation and neglects these effects.

## Method

The authors employed their in-house AVXSF-LNG program to perform Monte Carlo extended R-matrix simulations of fission probabilities and neutron-induced fission cross sections for the Pu isotopes 237, 238, 240, 242, and 244 over the 4 to 8 MeV excitation energy range. They calculated the three components of the Hauser-Feshbach equation for surrogate reactions, spin-parity population fractions, channel width fluctuation correction factors, and decay probabilities, using direct-reaction entrance distributions from early DWBA calculations by Andersen et al. (1970) and Back et al. (1974), and compared these against neutron-induced reaction distributions to quantify the errors introduced by the surrogate-reaction method.

## Key numbers

- The channel width fluctuation correction factor W_{n,f} for fertile heavy isotopes is approximately 35% at 1 keV neutron-incident energy, compared to 20% for fissile isotopes, as cited from prior work by Bouland (2014).
- For the 243Pu+n system, capture and fission cross section uncertainties integrated over a LWR reactor neutron spectrum were estimated at 275% and 118% respectively using the EAF2007/UN data library, as cited from Cabellos (2010).
- The neutron strength function values assumed for the Pu isotope family calculations are S_l = 1.044 × 10^{-4} for even-l waves and S_l = 1.48 × 10^{-4} for odd-l waves, with uncertainties of ±0.1 × 10^{-4} and ±0.4 × 10^{-4} respectively for s- and p-waves.
- Fluctuation calculations were carried up to a maximum excitation energy of 2.1 MeV above the neutron binding energy for conservative statistical treatment.

## Context

This work extends the earlier R-matrix Monte Carlo analysis by Bouland (2013) that predicted fission cross sections for short-lived Pu isotopes but did not document the surrogate data aspect. It directly addresses the limitations of the historical surrogate-reaction method identified by Cramer and Britt (1970) and Britt and Wilhelmy (1979), and responds to the recommendation by Romain (2012) against using the Weisskopf-Ewing approximation for surrogate experiments. The approach enables future evaluation of neutron-induced cross sections for higher transuranic nuclei such as Am, Np, and Cm, and is designed to incorporate simultaneously measured fission- and gamma-decay probabilities from upcoming experiments such as those by Ducasse (2016) and Marini (2019).
