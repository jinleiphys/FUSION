# Revisiting the Hybrid Quantum Monte Carlo Method for Hubbard and Electron-Phonon Models

> A unique feature of the hybrid quantum Monte Carlo (HQMC) method is the potential to simulate negative sign free lattice fermion models with subcubic scaling in system size. Here we will revisit the algorithm for various models. We will show that for the Hubbard model the HQMC suffers from ergodicity issues and unbounded forces in the effective action. Solutions to these issues can be found in terms of a complexification of the auxiliary fields. This implementation of the HQMC that does not attempt to regularize the fermionic matrix so as to circumvent the aforementioned singularities does not outperform single spin flip determinantal methods with cubic scaling. On the other hand we will argue that there is a set of models for which the HQMC is very efficient. This class is characterized by effective actions free of singularities. Using the Majorana representation, we show that models such as the Su-Schrieffer-Heeger Hamiltonian at half filling and on a bipartite lattice belong to this class. For this specific model sub-cubic scaling is achieved.

## Key claim

The Hybrid Quantum Monte Carlo (HQMC) method, while promising subcubic scaling for sign-problem-free lattice fermion models, suffers from ergodicity issues and unbounded forces in the effective action for the Hubbard model; these issues can be circumvented by complexifying the auxiliary fields, but this implementation does not outperform standard single spin-flip determinantal methods with cubic scaling. For a specific class of models characterized by effective actions free of singularities, such as the Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) Hamiltonian at half filling on a bipartite lattice, the HQMC achieves subcubic scaling and is efficient.

## Method

The authors implement the HQMC algorithm by introducing continuous auxiliary fields via a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation, integrating out fermions to obtain a determinant, and then replacing the determinant with pseudofermion fields to avoid explicit cubic-scaling matrix inversions. They use a symplectic Leapfrog integrator to evolve the auxiliary fields according to Hamilton's equations of motion, with forces evaluated via the conjugate gradient method preconditioned by an incomplete Cholesky decomposition. For the Hubbard model, they complexify the auxiliary fields by interpolating between charge and spin decoupling channels to circumvent ergodicity barriers; for the SSH model, they use a Majorana representation to ensure a positive-semidefinite fermion determinant.

## Key numbers

- For the Hubbard model on a \(4 \times 4\) lattice at \(U=4\) and \(\triangle_{\tau}=0.1\), the real-field HQMC produces different Green's function results depending on the starting sector, demonstrating ergodicity failure.
- The conjugate gradient termination criterion is set to \(\varepsilon_n = \sqrt{(\phi - O X_n)^2 / \phi^2} \leq 10^{-7}\), following Scalettar (1987).
- The preconditioner uses a shift parameter \(\alpha = 1.05\) to prevent ill-conditioning and pivot breakdown in the conjugate gradient method.
- The Trotter discretization uses \(\triangle_\tau\) slices such that \(N_\tau \triangle_\tau = \beta\), with the discretization error shared with the BSS-QMC method.

## Context

This work revisits the HQMC method originally formulated by Scalettar (1987) for condensed matter systems, contrasting it with the standard Blankenbecler-Scalapino-Sugar (BSS) determinantal QMC technique (Blankenbecler, 1981; White, 1989) which scales cubically with volume. The authors identify ergodicity barriers in the Hubbard model that prevent the real-field HQMC from sampling configuration space, a problem not present in the BSS approach due to its discrete Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation (Hirsch, 1983). The paper extends recent progress in classifying sign-problem-free fermion models (Wu, 2004; Yao, 2014; Li, 2016; Wei, 2016) to identify the SSH model as a candidate for efficient HQMC simulation, and benchmarks results against the continuous-time interaction expansion (CT-INT) algorithm (Rubtsov, 2004; Assaad, 2007).
