# Spin polarization induced by the hydrodynamic gradients

> We systematically analyze the effects of the derivatives of the hydrodynamic fields on axial Wigner function that describes the spin polarization vector in phase space. We have included all possible first-order derivative contributions that are allowed by symmetry and compute the associated transport functions at one-loop using the linear response theory. In addition to reproducing known effects due to the temperature gradient and vorticity, we have identified a number of potentially significant contributions that are overlooked previously. In particular, we find that the shear strength, the symmetric and traceless part of the flow gradient, will induce a quadrupole for spin polarization in the phase space. We refer to this novel effect as the shear-induced polarization (SIP). Our results, together with hydrodynamic gradients obtained from hydrodynamic simulations, can be employed as a basis for the interpretation of the $Λ$ (anti-$Λ$) spin polarization measurement in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper systematically analyzes all possible first-order derivative contributions of hydrodynamic fields to the axial Wigner function that describes spin polarization in phase space, and identifies that shear strength (the symmetric and traceless part of the flow gradient) induces a quadrupole pattern in spin polarization, an effect overlooked in previous studies that assumed such patterns arise solely from thermal vorticity.

## Method

The authors performed a derivative expansion of the axial Wigner function in phase space, including all first-order gradient terms allowed by symmetry, and computed the associated transport functions at one-loop order using linear response theory (Kubo relations). They extracted the transport coefficients by comparing the behavior of retarded correlation functions in the slow limit (where flow is fully developed) and the fast limit (where flow has not yet developed), evaluating the relevant correlation functions for Dirac fermions with arbitrary mass at finite temperature and chemical potential.

## Key numbers

* The shear-induced polarization (SIP) effect arises from the term proportional to the transport function \(g_{\sigma,\pm}\) in the expansion of the transverse axial Wigner function: \(\mathcal{A}^{\mu}_{\perp,\pm} = \ldots + g_{\sigma,\pm}\,\epsilon^{\mu\nu\lambda\alpha}u_{\nu}Q_{\lambda}^{\ \rho}\,\sigma_{\rho\alpha}\), where \(Q^{\mu\nu} = -p^{\mu}_{\perp}p^{\nu}_{\perp}/p^{2}_{\perp} + (1/3)\Delta^{\mu\nu}\) is the generalized quadrupole moment
* The quadrupole moment in the fluid rest frame is given by \(Q^{ij} = \hat{p}^{i}\hat{p}^{j} - (1/3)\delta^{ij}\), with the identity \(Q_{ij}Q^{ij} = 2/3\) used in the Kubo relation derivation
* The one-loop calculation yields the retarded correlation function \(G^{i,0j}_{\pm}(q_0,\mathbf{q},\mathbf{p}) = -\frac{i}{2}\epsilon^{ijm}q_m\,[(\mp\Delta\varepsilon D_{\pm})-b_{\pm}] - \frac{i}{2}\epsilon^{ijl}v_l\Delta\varepsilon\,q_0D_{\pm} - \frac{i}{2}\epsilon^{iml}q_m v_l v^j\,[(\mp\Delta\varepsilon D_{\pm})+b_{\pm}]\), where \(D_{\pm} = a_{\pm}/(q_0\mp\Delta\varepsilon+i0^{+})\), \(a_{\pm} = -\partial n_{\pm}(\varepsilon_{\mathbf{p}})/\partial\varepsilon_{\mathbf{p}}\), and \(b_{\pm} = n_{\pm}(\varepsilon_{\mathbf{p}})/\varepsilon_{\mathbf{p}}\)

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions beyond the standard treatment based solely on thermal vorticity, as studied by Liang (2004), Becattini (2013, 2020), and others. The observed quadrupole pattern in \(\Lambda\) hyperon polarization measurements by the STAR collaboration (2017, 2018, 2019) was in tension with theories assuming such patterns arise only from thermal vorticity (Becattini 2017, Xia 2018). The shear-induced polarization (SIP) effect identified here provides a new mechanism that can be tested against hydrodynamic simulations and may resolve this tension. The results enable a more complete interpretation of spin polarization measurements in heavy-ion collisions by incorporating all first-order gradient contributions.
