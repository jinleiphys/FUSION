# Relativistic effects in quasielastic electron scattering

> 6 pages, 5 figures, to appear in the Proceedings of "The Nuclear Many Body Problem 2001", NATO Science Series II - Mathematics, Physics and Chemistry, Kluwer Academic Publishers

## Key claim

Pionic correlations and meson-exchange currents produce modest but non-negligible relativistic effects in quasielastic electron scattering responses; these effects do not vanish at high momentum transfer and break scaling of the second kind, even though scaling of the first kind is attained above about 1 GeV/c.

## Method

The author computes the longitudinal and transverse electromagnetic response functions for a relativistic Fermi gas model of ⁴⁰Ca, including one-pion-exchange meson-exchange currents (MEC) and correlation currents (diagrams with an intermediate virtual nucleon) to first order in perturbation theory. The currents are constructed to be fully relativistic in the energy and momentum transfer variables, and gauge invariance is enforced by using the same isovector form factor in all currents. Results are compared with the free Fermi gas response and with non-relativistic calculations at selected kinematics.

## Key numbers

- At q = 500 MeV/c, the relativistic MEC contribution to the transverse response is about 30% smaller than the non-relativistic calculation of Amaro (1994).
- The MEC contribution to the transverse response is typically 5–10% of the free response.
- At q = 1 GeV/c and k_F = 300 MeV/c, the relative MEC contribution to the transverse response reaches about 20%.
- The ratio MEC/free in the transverse channel saturates at q ≈ 1 GeV/c, stabilizing around 10%.
- The free response decreases as k_F⁻¹, while the MEC contribution grows roughly as k_F².

## Context

This work extends the relativistic treatment of quasielastic electron scattering developed by Amaro (1998, 1999) and Alvarez-Ruso (2001), who derived current operators exact in ω and q. It contrasts with non-relativistic calculations (e.g., Amaro 1994) that use expansions treating q and ω as small compared to the nucleon mass. The results enable analysis of scaling of first and second kinds in nuclei (as defined in the y-scaling literature), showing that pionic effects break scaling of the second kind through a density dependence roughly proportional to k_F².
