# Scalar field with a time-independent classical source, not trivial after all: from vacuum decay to scattering

> Historically it has been believed that a time-independent classical source has no effect on the scattering of relativistic uncharged field, in contrast with single particle quantum mechanics. In this work we show that the dynamics is not trivial. We solve exactly for the scattering amplitudes and find that a key ingredient is the production of particles from the unstable vacuum, conceptually similar to the Schwinger mechanism. We compute exactly the probabilities for the vacuum to decay in $n$ particles. The time dependence of such probabilities displays interesting properties such as the quantum Zeno effect and in particular has no regime where the exponential decay law is a good approximation. We show that the trivial scattering found in the past is the byproduct of the adiabatic switching of the interaction. In fact, it is not possible to switch off the interaction (adiabatically or otherwise) at distant times and recover the exact results. Finally, this non trivial vacuum behavior is a source of particle production. We argue that such non-perturbative calculations can be phenomenologically relevant for the production processes that are suppressed at the lower orders in perturbation theory, for instance dilaton production in a medium.

## Key claim

The authors show that a time-independent classical source coupled to a relativistic scalar field does produce nontrivial scattering and particle production, contrary to the long-held textbook claim that such dynamics are trivial; the key qualifier is that the trivial result previously reported arises from the adiabatic switching of the interaction, which is not physically realizable.

## Method

The authors solve exactly for the time evolution of a free scalar field coupled to a static classical source using the Heisenberg picture, obtaining the exact form of the field operator with time-dependent ladder operators. They compute scattering amplitudes by relating the time-dependent particle number eigenstates to coherent states, using the interaction picture propagator to connect states at different times without solving the Dyson series. The vacuum decay probabilities are derived from the normalization condition of the initial vacuum state.

## Key numbers

- The expectation value of the particle number operator for the vacuum at time t is given by \( N_0(t) = \int \frac{d^3 p}{(2\pi)^3} |f(t,\mathbf{p})|^2 = 2\int \frac{d^3p}{(2\pi)^3} \left(1 - \cos(E_{\mathbf{p}}t)\right) |h(\mathbf{p})|^2 \), where \( h(\mathbf{p}) = -g / \sqrt{2E_{\mathbf{p}}^3} \int d^3y \, e^{-i\mathbf{p}\cdot\mathbf{y}} \rho(\mathbf{y}) \).
- The modulus of the vacuum overlap is \( |{}_t\langle 0|0\rangle| = e^{-\frac{1}{2}N_0(t)} \).
- The probability for the vacuum to decay into n particles follows a Poissonian distribution with expectation value \( N_0(t) \), as shown by the sum \( \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{|{}_t\langle 0|0\rangle|^2}{n!} \left( \int \frac{d^3 p}{(2\pi)^3} |f(t,\mathbf{p})|^2 \right)^n = |{}_t\langle 0|0\rangle|^2 e^{N_0(t)} = 1 \).

## Context

This work contradicts the textbook claim (Peskin 1995, Coleman 2018) that a time-independent classical source produces trivial scattering for an uncharged scalar field. The authors show that the vacuum decay is conceptually similar to the Schwinger mechanism (Schwinger 1951) but differs in that single particles and arbitrary numbers of particles can be emitted, and the decay law never approximates exponential decay, enabling the quantum Zeno effect. The results enable non-perturbative calculations for production processes suppressed at lower orders in perturbation theory, such as dilaton production in a medium.
