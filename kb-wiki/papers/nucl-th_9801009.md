# Multiparticle Bose-Einstein Correlations

> Multiparticle symmetrization effects are contributions to the spectra of Bose-symmetrized states which are not the product of pairwise correlations. Usually they are neglected in particle interferometric calculations which aim at determining the geometry of the boson emitting source from the measured momentum distributions. Based on a method introduced by Zajc and Pratt, we give a calculation of all multiparticle symmetrization effects to the one- and two-particle momentum spectra for a Gaussian phase space distribution of emission points. Our starting point is an ensemble of N-particle Bose-symmetrized wavefunctions with specified phase space localization. In scenarios typical for relativistic heavy ion collisions, multiparticle effects steepen the slope of the one-particle spectrum for realistic particle phase space densities by up to 20 MeV, and they broaden the relative momentum dependence of the two-particle correlations. We discuss these modifications and their consequences in quantitative detail. Also, we explain how multiparticle effects modify the normalization of the two-particle correlator. The resulting normalization conserves event probabilities, which is not the case for the commonly used pair approximation. Finally, we propose a new method of calculating Bose-Einstein weights from the output of event generators, taking multiparticle correlations into account.

## Key claim

Multiparticle Bose-Einstein symmetrization effects, which are usually neglected in particle interferometric calculations, modify the one- and two-particle momentum spectra in ways that depend quantitatively on the phase space density of the boson-emitting source; for scenarios typical of relativistic heavy ion collisions, these effects steepen the one-particle spectrum slope by up to 20 MeV and broaden the relative momentum dependence of two-particle correlations.

## Method

The author uses the Zajc-Pratt algorithm to calculate all multiparticle symmetrization contributions to one- and two-particle momentum spectra for an ensemble of N-particle Bose-symmetrized wavefunctions with Gaussian phase space distribution of emission points. The single-particle states are taken as Gaussian wavepackets centered at specified phase space points, and the building blocks of the spectra (the Pratt terms C_m and G_m) are computed analytically for a source with Gaussian distributions in both position and momentum. The weights of different multiparticle contributions are then determined from the normalization function ω(N), which is expressed in terms of Stirling numbers of the first kind.

## Key numbers

- Multiparticle effects steepen the slope of the one-particle spectrum by up to 20 MeV for realistic particle phase space densities in relativistic heavy ion collisions.
- The first-order weight v_1 for the one-particle spectrum is approximately v_1 ≈ 1/(1 + ρ_vol), where ρ_vol = N ε, with ε defined in Eq. (4.10b) as a function of the source size R, momentum spread Δ, and wavepacket width σ.
- The m-th order weight v_m for the one-particle spectrum is approximately v_m ≈ ρ_vol^(m-1) / (1 + ρ_vol)^m.
- In the Zajc model, the limiting behavior gives g_K^(m) → m and g_Q^(m) → 1/m for large phase space volume V → ∞.
- For the Gaussian wavepacket model, the limits are lim_(a→0, b→1) g_Q^(m) = 1/m and lim_(a→1, b→0) g_K^(m) = m, but both limits cannot be realized simultaneously.

## Context

This work extends the earlier approaches of Zajc (1987) and Pratt (1993), who developed the formalism and Monte Carlo techniques for multiparticle Bose-Einstein correlations, and provides the first detailed quantitative study of the momentum dependence of these effects for a Gaussian source model. The results enable more accurate extraction of source geometry from experimental two-particle correlation measurements at CERN SPS and the future RHIC facility, and the author proposes a new method for calculating Bose-Einstein weights from event generator output that properly accounts for multiparticle correlations, unlike the commonly used pair approximation which does not conserve event probabilities.
