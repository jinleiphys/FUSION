# Unmasking short-range correlations via initial-state fluctuations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Although relativistic heavy-ion collisions have emerged as a powerful probe for studying nuclear structure, the potential influence of nucleon-nucleon short-range correlations (NN-SRCs) on the initial state has remained an open question. By incorporating NN-SRCs into the initial conditions, we demonstrate that higher-order fluctuations of the initial transverse size, $n$-particle $c_{E/S}\{n\}$, which can be directly mapped to final-state mean transverse momentum fluctuations, exhibit remarkable sensitivity to NN-SRCs. Quantitatively, $c_{E/S}\{3\}$ and $c_{E/S}\{4\}$ differ by more than 10\% between systems with and without NN correlations. Moreover, we report a universal scaling of these quantities with $A^{-1/3}$ and the average nuclear density, mirroring the connection between the SRC effect and the EMC effect in electron scatterings. This work establishes relativistic heavy-ion collisions as a new tool for probing nuclear structure and constraining two-body or many-body NN interactions across different energy scales and system sizes.

## Key claim
By incorporating nucleon-nucleon short-range correlations (NN-SRCs) into the initial conditions of relativistic heavy-ion collisions, the authors demonstrate that higher-order fluctuations of the initial transverse size, specifically the n-particle cumulants \(c_{E/S}\{n\}\), exhibit remarkable sensitivity to NN-SRCs, with the caveat that this sensitivity is most pronounced in ultra-central collisions and for quantities of order \(n \ge 3\).

## Method
The authors incorporate NN-SRCs into the initial-state nuclear density using a two-point correlation function \(F(\Delta r)\) parameterized from the Alvioli-AV8' interaction, which modifies the two-body density relative to the product of one-body densities. Analytical calculations are performed for a tetrahedral \(4\alpha\)-cluster structure of \(^{16}\)O, and Monte Carlo sampling using an improved VEGAS algorithm is employed within the T\(_{\text{R}}\)ENTo model to simulate initial states for \(^{16}\)O, \(^{40}\)Ca, and \(^{208}\)Pb with Woods-Saxon distributions. The sensitivity of various initial-state cumulants, including eccentricity cumulants \(c_n\{m\}\) and inverse transverse size cumulants \(c_{E/S}\{n\}\), to the presence of NN correlations is then quantified by comparing results with and without the correlation function.

## Key numbers
- The relative deviation \(\Delta R(c_{E/S}\{3\})\) and \(\Delta R(c_{E/S}\{4\})\) between systems with and without NN correlations exceeds 10% in ultra-central \(^{16}\)O+\(^{16}\)O collisions.
- For the T\(_{\text{R}}\)ENTo model with a Woods-Saxon distribution, the 3- and 4-point correlators \(c_{E/S}\{n\}\) show a relative deviation \(\Delta R(c_{E/S}\{n\}) \approx 20\%\) under attractive (\(\gamma=0.5\)) and repulsive (\(\gamma=-0.7\)) leading-order correlations.
- The root-mean-square (RMS) radius of \(^{16}\)O used in the analytical tetrahedral cluster calculation is \(\sqrt{\langle r^2\rangle}=2.7\) fm, consistent with the experimental charge RMS radius of 2.73 fm.
- The linear scaling coefficient \(k\) of the EMC-SRC relationship extracted from the dependence of \(\Delta R(c_{E/S}\{n\})\) on the EMC effect slope \(|\mathrm{d}R_{EMC}/\mathrm{d}x_B|\) is approximately 0.08.

## Context
This work extends the use of relativistic heavy-ion collisions as a probe of nuclear structure, moving beyond the single-nucleon density distribution \(\rho^{(1)}(r)\) used in prior studies (e.g., Zhang (2021), STAR (2024)) to include the lowest-order two-body correlations \(\rho^{(2)}(\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2)\). It establishes a linear scaling of the NN-SRC effect with \(A^{-1/3}\) and average nuclear density, mirroring the phenomenological connection between the SRC effect and the EMC effect observed in low-energy electron scattering experiments (e.g., Weinstein (2010), Hen (2012)). The findings enable future experimental studies at the LHC and RHIC to quantitatively isolate NN correlation effects and constrain two-body or many-body NN interactions across different energy scales and system sizes.
