# Photons from relativistic nuclear collisions

> Collisions of atomic nuclei at relativistic velocities allow to recreate the conditions encountered in neutron stars or in the early universe micro-seconds after the Big Bang. These reactions are performed in today's largest accelerator facilities, e.g. at CERN in Geneva, at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider at Brookhaven, NY or in the planned FAIR facility in Darmstadt Germany. During such a collision the matter is heated up to hundreds of MeV (billions of degrees) and compressed to densities of $3-10$ times the density inside ordinary atomic nuclei (i.e. $10^{17}-10^{18}$ kg/m$^3$). Usually these collisions are studied via the measurement of a multitude of strongly interacting particles, called hadrons, that are emitted at the end of the collision. However, also some photons are created. These photons are of special interest as they allow to look into the early stage of the collisions, because they are only very weakly (namely only electro-magnetically) interacting with the hadrons of the created fireball. This paper elucidates the physics of the photons and what can be learned from them.

## Key claim

Photons emitted from the hot and dense fireball created in relativistic nuclear collisions allow direct observation of the early, high-temperature stage of the system, because their mean free path is larger than the fireball itself; however, the authors note that the assumption of instant local thermalization used in their hydrodynamic model is crude and becomes questionable at beam energies above \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\approx 5-10\) GeV.

## Method

The authors performed (3+1)-dimensional ideal relativistic hydrodynamic simulations of gold-gold (Au+Au) collisions, using the SHASTA algorithm to solve the conservation equations for energy, momentum, and baryon current. The equation of state was a hadron resonance gas, and photon emission rates from the dominant hadronic channel \(\pi\rho \rightarrow \pi\gamma\) were calculated using the parametrization of Turbide, Rapp, and Gale (2003). The photon yield was integrated over each hydrodynamic cell's volume and time step to produce invariant photon spectra.

## Key numbers

* Peak temperatures in the central region of Au+Au collisions: \(T_{max}=90\) MeV at \(E_{lab}=1.23 A\) GeV, \(T_{max}=130\) MeV at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=3\) GeV, and \(T_{max}=225\) MeV at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=7.7\) GeV.
* Extracted 4-volume (volume \(\times\) lifetime) and average temperature from fits to photon spectra: at \(E_{lab}=1.23 A\) GeV, \(V_4=2014\) fm\(^4\) and \(T=89\) MeV; at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=3\) GeV, \(V_4=2197\) fm\(^4\) and \(T=125\) MeV; at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=7.7\) GeV, \(V_4=2652\) fm\(^4\) and \(T=200\) MeV.
* The critical temperature for the QCD phase transition at vanishing baryon density is \(T_c\approx 150-160\) MeV.
* The hadronic vertex form factor uses a cutoff parameter \(\Lambda=1\) GeV.
* The \(\pi\rho \rightarrow \pi\gamma\) rate parametrization is given in units of fm\(^{-4}\) GeV\(^{-2}\).

## Context

This pedagogical study extends the hydrodynamic modeling approach of Kolb and Heinz (2003) and the photon rate calculations of Turbide, Rapp, and Gale (2003) to lower beam energies in the GSI and RHIC-BES energy region. The work demonstrates that photon spectra can extract both the space-time volume and average temperature of the fireball, and that the extracted average temperatures cross the QCD deconfinement temperature in the \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=3-7.7\) GeV range. The authors compare their Pb+Pb calculation at \(E_{lab}=158 A\) GeV to data from the CERN WA98 experiment (2000), finding good agreement within errors despite using only the dominant hadronic channel. This enables future studies to include baryon-density-dependent rates from Heffernan (2014) and more sophisticated equations of state to probe the QCD phase diagram and connect to neutron star physics.
