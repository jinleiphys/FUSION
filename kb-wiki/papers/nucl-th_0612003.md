# Empirical formula for the excitation energies of the first $2^+$ and $3^-$ states in even-even nuclei

> We report empirical findings that a simple formula in terms of the mass number $A$, the valence proton number $N_p$, and the valence neutron number $N_n$ can describe the essential trends of excitation energies $E_x$ of the first $2^+$ and $3^-$ states in even-even nuclei throughout the periodic table. The formula reads as $E_x = αA^{-β} + \exp (- λN_p) + \exp (- λN_n)$. The parameter $β$ in the first term is determined by the mass number $A$ dependence of the bottom contour line of the excitation energy systematics. The other two parameters $α$ and $λ$ are fitted by minimizing the $χ^2$ value between the measured and calculated excitation energies. Our results suggest that the single large-$j$ shell simulation can be applied to the excitation energies of the first $2^+$ and $3^-$ states in even-even nuclei.

# Empirical formula for the excitation energies of the first 2⁺ and 3⁻ states in even-even nuclei

## Key claim
A three-term empirical formula depending on mass number A, valence proton number Nₚ, and valence neutron number Nₙ can reproduce the essential trends of the first 2⁺ and 3⁻ excitation energies across the entire periodic table, but the authors acknowledge that the formula has shortcomings in explaining either the detailed shape or actual height of measured isotopic or isotonic chains, particularly for nuclei between A ≈ 100 and 200.

## Method
The authors fitted a four-parameter formula Eₓ = αA⁻ᵞ + β[exp(-λNₚ) + exp(-λNₙ)] to compiled excitation energy data for even-even nuclei. The parameter γ was determined first by fitting only the mass-dependent term αA⁻ᵞ to the bottom contour line of the excitation energy systematics, then α, β, and λ were obtained by minimizing the χ² value between logarithms of measured and calculated excitation energies for all data points. The same procedure was applied separately to the first 2⁺ states (using data from Raman et al., 2001) and the first 3⁻ states (using data from Kibédi and Spear, 2002).

## Key numbers
- For the first 2⁺ states: α = 34.9 MeV, β = 1.00 MeV, γ = 1.19, λ = 0.36
- For the first 3⁻ states: α = 62.8 MeV, β = 0.90 MeV, γ = 0.79, λ = 0.24
- The formula reproduces the V-shaped pattern within major shells where the minimum excitation energy occurs at mid-shell, with magic numbers N = 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, and 126 forming the boundaries between adjacent major shells
- The best quantitative agreement for 2⁺ states occurs for nuclei roughly between A = 100 and A = 200

## Context
This work extends the single large-j shell simulation concept previously applied to B(E2) values by Raman et al. (1988, 1991) and to collective variables parameterized by the product NₚNₙ by Casten (1985) and Casten and Zamfir (1996). Unlike prior studies that applied valence nucleon parametrization only within single major shells, the authors demonstrate that a single formula works across the entire periodic table. The results suggest the same approach may apply to first 4⁺ and second 2⁺ excitation energies (Kim, private communication), and the estimated excitation energies may assist various nuclear structure studies (Lee and Lee, 2006; Zamick, Mekjian, and Lee, 2005).
