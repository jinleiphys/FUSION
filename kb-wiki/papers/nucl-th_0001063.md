# Soft Modes, Quantum Transport and Kinetic Entropy

> The effects of the propagation of particles which have a finite life-time and an according width in their mass spectrum are discussed in the context of transport descriptions. In the first part the coupling of soft photon modes to a source of charged particles is studied in a classical model which can be solved completely in analytical terms. The solution corresponds to a re-summation of certain field theory diagrams. The general properties of broad resonances in dense finite temperature systems are discussed at the example of the $ρ$-meson in hadronic matter. The second part addresses the problem of transport descriptions which also account for the damping width of the particles. The Kadanoff--Baym equation after gradient approximation together with the $Φ$-derivable method of Baym provides a self-consistent and conserving scheme. Memory effects appearing in collision term diagrams of higher order are discussed. We derive a generalized expression for the nonequilibrium kinetic entropy flow, which includes corrections from fluctuations and mass-width effects. In special cases an $H$-theorem is proved. Memory effects in collision terms provide contributions to the kinetic entropy flow that in the Fermi-liquid case recover the famous bosonic type $T^3 \ln T$ correction to the specific heat of liquid Helium-3.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a consistent transport description of particles with broad damping widths (e.g., resonances like the rho-meson or collisional broadened states) requires going beyond the quasi-particle approximation and must include the full spectral function and medium-dependent total width, with the caveat that the classical soft-photon problem can be solved exactly and shows that the soft part of the spectrum is non-perturbative and suppressed with increasing collision rate, contrary to perturbative expectations.

## Method

The authors first solve a classical model of a charged Brownian particle coupled to the Maxwell field via a Fokker-Planck equation, obtaining the current-current correlation function in closed analytical form. They then discuss the rho-meson in dense hadronic matter at finite temperature, using a two-channel model (pi+ pi- and pi N -> rho N) to compute the spectral function and di-lepton production rate. Finally, they analyze the Kadanoff-Baym equation in the first gradient approximation combined with the Phi-derivable method of Baym to derive a generalized expression for nonequilibrium kinetic entropy flow including mass-width and fluctuation corrections.

## Key numbers

* The rho-meson vacuum decay width into pi+ pi- is taken as Gamma_{rho -> pi pi}(m_rho) = 150 MeV.
* The width for the pi N -> rho N channel at the rho mass is taken as Gamma_{rho -> pi N N^{-1}}(m_rho) = 70 MeV.
* The thermal di-lepton rate calculations are performed at a temperature T = 110 MeV.
* The classical current-current correlation decays exponentially as ~ exp(-Gamma_x tau) at zero photon momentum, where Gamma_x is the friction coefficient.
* The soft photon suppression factor is C_0(omega) = omega^2 / (omega^2 + Gamma_x^2).
* Memory effects in collision terms provide contributions that in the Fermi-liquid case recover the bosonic type T^3 ln T correction to the specific heat of liquid Helium-3.

## Context

This work extends the real-time nonequilibrium field theory framework of Schwinger, Kadanoff and Baym (1962), and Keldysh (1964) to systems with broad damping widths, addressing problems that earlier transport treatments (e.g., Danielewicz, and various ad hoc resonance recipes criticized in refs. DB and Weinhold) handled incorrectly by violating detailed balance or unitarity. The classical bremsstrahlung solution relates to the Landau-Pommeranchuk-Migdal effect. The results enable a consistent implementation of broad resonances in transport simulation codes for heavy-ion collisions and provide a foundation for deriving kinetic entropy with fluctuation corrections, with the specific heat result connecting to Fermi-liquid theory for Helium-3.
