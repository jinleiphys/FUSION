# Large-Scale Self-Consistent Nuclear Mass Calculations

> The program of systematic large-scale self-consistent nuclear mass calculations that is based on the nuclear density functional theory represents a rich scientific agenda that is closely aligned with the main research directions in modern nuclear structure and astrophysics, especially the radioactive nuclear beam physics. The quest for the microscopic understanding of the phenomenon of nuclear binding represents, in fact, a number of fundamental and crucial questions of the quantum many-body problem, including the proper treatment of correlations and dynamics in the presence of symmetry breaking. Recent advances and open problems in the field of nuclear mass calculations are presented and discussed.

## Key claim
The paper argues that systematic large-scale nuclear mass calculations based on nuclear density functional theory (DFT) represent a rich scientific agenda aligned with modern nuclear structure and astrophysics, but acknowledges that the quest for microscopic understanding of nuclear binding involves fundamental open questions about correlations and dynamics in the presence of symmetry breaking that have not yet been fully resolved.

## Method
The authors review the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) method based on Skyrme energy density functionals, which are parametrized by about 14 coupling constants adjusted to nuclear matter properties and selected data on magic nuclei. They describe computational tools including the HFBRAD code for spherical HFB calculations (taking no more than 10 CPU minutes per nucleus on an Intel Xeon 2.8 GHz processor) and the HFBTHO code for axially deformed HFB calculations (averaging about 1 CPU hour per nucleus), with a load-balancing routine scaling to 200 processors for parallel computation. The paper discusses specific technical developments including the transformed harmonic oscillator (THO) basis for drip-line nuclei, regularization of contact pairing interactions, and particle-number projection techniques.

## Key numbers
- Standard Skyrme functionals reproduce total binding energies with an rms error of 2 to 4 MeV
- The HFBRAD code takes no more than 10 CPU minutes per nucleus on an Intel Xeon 2.8 GHz processor
- The HFBTHO code averages about 1 CPU hour per nucleus for axially deformed HFB calculations
- The load-balancing routine scales the problem to 200 processors, allowing calculations in a single 24 wall-clock hour run on a 4 Tflop machine at ORNL
- The surface energy coefficient differs by as much as 0.7 MeV between SLy4 and SLy6 functionals due to different treatment of center-of-mass correction
- The pairing functional uses a density-dependent delta interaction with ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ and γ = 1, with V₁ = 0, 1, or 1/2 for volume-, surface-, or mixed-type pairing

## Context
This paper reviews and extends the program of large-scale microscopic nuclear mass calculations previously carried out by groups such as those behind the SkP functional (Dobaczewski 1984) and more recent mass tables (Goriely 2003, 2005; Samyn 2004; Stoitsov 2003). It discusses open problems including the need for a universal energy density functional with proper treatment of time-odd fields, dynamical corrections from symmetry restoration, and systematic error analysis, which would enable reliable predictions for nuclei far from stability and superheavy elements.
