# Quantum-classical framework for many-fermion response and structure

> Response functions are key observables for probing the structure and dynamics of many-body systems. We introduce and demonstrate a quantum-classical framework for computing response functions of general many-fermion systems that also provides the full bound-state spectrum. The framework employs the Lorentz integral transform and a new Hamiltonian input scheme that enables practical and scalable circuit constructions for general many-fermion Hamiltonians. Within this framework, we develop a hybrid strategy to evaluate the Lorentz integral and propose three protocols to extract response functions and bound-state structural information. As a demonstration, we apply the method to \({}^{19}\mathrm{O}\) with realistic internucleon interactions, computing both the bound-state spectrum and the response function. We envision that our approach will open new avenues for exploring the structure and dynamics of a broad class of many-body systems across diverse fields.

# Quantum-classical framework for many-fermion response and structure

## Key claim

The paper introduces a hybrid quantum-classical framework for computing response functions and full bound-state spectra of many-fermion systems, demonstrated on ¹⁹O with realistic internucleon interactions, but the demonstration uses a test (unitless) system-probe interaction rather than a physically realistic probe, and the calculations are performed in a limited model space excluding the 0d₃/₂ orbitals.

## Method

The framework combines the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) with Chebyshev polynomial expansion, where the Lorentz integral is expressed as a linear combination of Chebyshev moments that are evaluated on quantum computers using a Hamiltonian input scheme based on Boolean masking and discrete-time quantum walks. The Chebyshev moments are then processed on classical computers to reconstruct the Lorentz integral, from which three protocols extract bound-state energies, angular momenta, and the response function via integral inversion.

## Key numbers

- The threshold energy for neutron separation in ¹⁹O is computed as e_th = 3.919 MeV, compared to the experimental value of 3.956 MeV (NNDC 2022)
- Ground state energies: E₀(¹⁸O) = -11.155 MeV and E₀(¹⁹O) = -15.074 MeV
- The response function R(e) results were tested to be independent of σ_I over the range σ_I ∈ [5, 14] MeV based on L_C(σ) results with σ_R ∈ [e_th, 40] MeV
- The gate complexity for encoding Hamiltonians with up to η-body operators scales as Õ(N_sp^(2η+1)), with the number of monomials scaling as Õ(N_sp^(2η))
- The number of qubits required to encode a many-fermion Hamiltonian in a basis of N_sp single-particle states is N_sp + ⌈log₂ D⌉ + 4

## Context

This work extends the Lorentz integral transform method (Efros 1994, Efros 2007), which has been widely applied to nuclear photoabsorption and electroweak reactions on classical computers, by introducing quantum computation to handle the computationally demanding many-body calculations. The framework builds on a Hamiltonian input scheme (Du 2025) that avoids expensive compilation overhead of Jordan-Wigner (Jordan and Wigner 1928) or Bravyi-Kitaev (Bravyi 2002, Seeley 2012) encodings. The approach enables access to complete bound-state spectra, surpassing quantum eigensolvers such as VQE (Tilly 2022) that target only a limited set of eigenenergies, and opens avenues for computing response functions of realistic many-body systems in quantum chemistry, nuclear physics, and field theories.
