# Mass and radius formulas for low-mass neutron stars

> Neutron stars, produced at the death of massive stars, are often regarded as giant neutron-rich nuclei. This picture is especially relevant for low-mass (below about solar mass) neutron stars, where non-nucleonic components are not expected to occur. Due to the saturation property of nucleonic matter, leading to the celebrated liquid-drop picture of atomic nuclei, empirical nuclear masses and radii can be approximately expressed as function of atomic mass number. It is, however, not straightforward to express masses and radii of neutron stars even in the low-mass range where the structure is determined by a balance between the pressure of neutron-rich nucleonic matter and the gravity. Such expressions would be of great use given possible simultaneous mass and radius measurements. Here we successfully construct theoretical formulas for the masses and radii of low-mass neutron stars from various models that are consistent with empirical masses and radii of stable nuclei. In this process, we discover a new equation-of-state parameter that characterizes the structure of low-mass neutron stars. This parameter, which plays a key role in connecting the mass-radius relation of the laboratory nuclei to that of the celestial objects, could be constrained from future observations of low-mass neutron stars.

## Key claim

The authors construct theoretical formulas for the masses and radii of low-mass neutron stars (below about one solar mass) that depend on a newly introduced equation-of-state parameter eta, defined as (K0 L^2)^(1/3), and the central density; these formulas are valid only for central densities between approximately 0.9 and 2.0 times the nuclear saturation density, where non-nucleonic components are not expected.

## Method

The authors constructed nonrotating neutron star models by integrating the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations using various unified equations of state that simultaneously describe crust and core matter and are consistent with empirical masses and radii of stable nuclei. They used three groups of equations of state: the OI-EOSs based on a phenomenological extended Thomas-Fermi theory, relativistic mean field and Hartree-Fock models (Shen and Miyatsu), and Skyrme-type effective interactions (FPS, SLy4, BSk19, BSk20, BSk21). For each model, they computed the mass-radius relation for central densities up to 2.0 times the nuclear saturation density and then fitted the resulting masses and gravitational redshifts as linear functions of the new parameter eta.

## Key numbers

* The nuclear saturation density rho_0 is set to 2.68 x 10^14 g cm^-3.
* The mass formula for low-mass neutron stars is M/M_sun = 0.371 - 0.820 u_c + 0.279 u_c^2 - (0.593 - 1.25 u_c + 0.235 u_c^2)(eta/100 MeV), where u_c = rho_c/rho_0, valid for u_c between approximately 0.9 and 2.0.
* The gravitational redshift formula is z = 0.00859 - 0.0619 u_c + 0.0255 u_c^2 - (0.0429 - 0.108 u_c + 0.0120 u_c^2)(eta/100 MeV).
* For sufficiently large eta, the radius converges to R = 10.32 + 2.57(eta/100 MeV) km.
* The radiation radius of the X-ray source CXOU 132619.7-472910.8 in omega Cen is R_infinity = 14.3 +/- 2.1 km.
* The eta values for the adopted equations of state range from 55.7 MeV (OI-EOS with K0=180 MeV, L=31.0 MeV) to 197 MeV (OI-EOS with K0=360 MeV, L=146 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the liquid-drop model approach used for atomic nuclei to low-mass neutron stars, providing a systematic connection between nuclear matter parameters (the incompressibility K0 and symmetry energy slope L) and stellar observables. The formulas enable constraints on the new parameter eta from simultaneous mass and radius measurements, which could come from observations of thermonuclear X-ray bursts and quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries as discussed by Steiner et al. (2013) and Lattimer and Steiner (2013). The authors note that the candidate neutron star in 4U 1538-52 and the X-ray burster 4U 1724-307 in Terzan 2 could provide tests of the eta value, potentially suggesting values of order or larger than 130 MeV.
