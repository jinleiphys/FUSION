# Neutron Stars and the Nuclear Equation of State

> We review the current status and recent progress of microscopic many-body approaches and phenomenological models, which are employed to construct the equation of state of neutron stars. The equation of state is relevant for the description of their structure and dynamical properties, and it rules also the dynamics of core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers. We describe neutron star matter assuming that the main degrees of freedom are nucleons and hyperons, disregarding the appearance of quark matter. We compare the theoretical predictions of the different equation-of-state models with the currently available data coming from both terrestrial laboratory experiments and recent astrophysical observations. We also analyse the importance of the nuclear strong interaction and equation of state for the cooling properties of neutron stars. We discuss the main open challenges in the description of the equation of state, mainly focusing on the limits of the different many-body techniques, the so-called "hyperon puzzle," and the dependence of the direct URCA processes on the equation of state.

## Key claim

This review paper surveys the current theoretical and phenomenological approaches to constructing the neutron star equation of state (EoS), but explicitly restricts its scope to models where the main degrees of freedom are nucleons and hyperons, disregarding the possible appearance of quark matter. The authors acknowledge that the theoretical predictions remain subject to significant uncertainties from the limits of many-body techniques, the unresolved "hyperon puzzle," and the dependence of direct URCA processes on the chosen EoS.

## Method

The authors review both microscopic many-body approaches (including Brueckner-Hartree-Fock, chiral effective field theory, and lattice QCD-based interactions) and phenomenological models (meson-exchange potentials, potential models like Argonne and Urbana, and relativistic mean-field models) used to construct the EoS of neutron star matter. They compare theoretical predictions from these different EoS models against available constraints from terrestrial laboratory experiments (heavy-ion reactions, nuclear scattering data) and recent astrophysical observations (NICER, gravitational-wave detections from AdvLIGO and Virgo). The review also analyzes how the EoS determines the cooling properties of neutron stars, with particular attention to the roles of superfluidity and nuclear pairing gaps.

## Key numbers

- The outer crust extends from the neutron drip density \(\rho_\text{drip}\approx 4\times10^{11}\ \text{g/cm}^3\) to about \(10^{4}\ \text{g/cm}^3\) at the surface.
- At densities larger than \(1.5\times10^{14}\ \text{g/cm}^3\), nuclear matter forms a homogeneous liquid composed of neutrons plus a fraction of protons, electrons, and muons in beta-equilibrium.
- The safe maximum density for chiral perturbation expansion applications is around the nuclear saturation density \(n_0\).
- The weak pion decay constant used in chiral perturbation theory is \(F_\pi = 92.4\ \text{MeV}\).
- The cut-off parameter \(\lambda\) in chiral effective field theory is usually taken in the range \(450-700\ \text{MeV}\).
- The cut-off \(\lambda\) for \(V_\text{low k}\) interactions is taken above \(300\ \text{MeV}\) in the laboratory, corresponding to relative momentum \(q \approx 2.1\ \text{fm}^{-1}\).
- The fss2 resonating-group method model reproduces the triton binding energy within \(300\ \text{keV}\).

## Context

This review extends and systematizes the extensive body of work on neutron star EoS construction, building on foundational studies of neutron star structure (Haensel 2007), crust properties (Baym 1971, Lattimer 1996), and nuclear pasta phases (Ravenhall 1983, Oyamatsu 1993). It directly addresses the "hyperon puzzle" identified in earlier work and discusses how the EoS determines cooling behavior through direct URCA processes, connecting to prior studies of proto-neutron star evolution (Prakash 1997, Pons 1999) and neutron star mergers (Shibata 2011, Rosswog 2015, Baiotti 2017). The review enables future work by cataloging which EoS models satisfy current constraints from NICER (Gendreau 2017) and gravitational-wave observations (Aasi 2015, Abbott 2017, Acernese 2014), while identifying open challenges in many-body techniques and the need for improved hyperon-nucleon interactions.
