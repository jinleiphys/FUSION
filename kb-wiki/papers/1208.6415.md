# Halos and related structures

> The halo structure originated in nuclear physics but is now encountered more widely. It appears in loosely bound, clustered systems where the spatial extension of the system is significantly larger than that of the binding potentials. A review is given on our current understanding of these structures, with an emphasis on how the structures evolve as more cluster components are added, and on the experimental situation concerning halo states in light nuclei.

## Key claim

Halos are defined by a large spatial extension caused by quantum tunneling of neutrons (or other particles) from a core, but this is not sufficient: the tunneling must be a prominent feature of the system, and the system must be divisible into a core and one or more halo particles, making cluster or few-body models the natural description. The paper emphasizes that the only truly pathological system is the two-body s-wave where the probability of being outside the binding potential can approach 1, and that this does not happen in any other case.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical classification of halo systems using few-body models, hyperspherical coordinates, and scaling variables, with particular attention to the effective angular momentum arising from N-body kinematics and the distinction between Borromean, Brunnian, and Efimov states. It surveys experimental probes including mass measurements, optical measurements, beta decay, electromagnetic transitions, and nuclear reactions, and compares results across nuclear and molecular halo systems to extract generic traits.

## Key numbers

- For two-body halo nuclei, the equivalent square-well radius is related to mean-square radii by \(3/5 R^2 = \langle r^2 \rangle_1 + \langle r^2 \rangle_2 + 3.3\) fm\(^2\), where the last term reflects the finite range of the nuclear force.
- The effective angular momentum for an N-body system in relative s-waves is \(\ell^* = 3(N-2)/2\), giving values of 0 for N=2, 3/2 for N=3, 3 for N=4, and 9/2 for N=5.
- For s-wave neutron halo states, the binding energy must satisfy \(B < 270\,\mathrm{keV}\,(A/Z)^2 \exp(-4\sqrt{aE^*})\) with \(D_0 = 7\) MeV and \(a = A/7.5\) MeV\(^{-1}\); for p-waves the restriction is \(Z < 0.44 A^{4/3}\exp(-2\sqrt{aE^*})\).
- The magnetic moment of \(^{11}\)Be is \(\mu = -1.6816(8) \mu_N\).
- The ratio of the electric quadrupole moment of \(^{11}\)Li to that of \(^{9}\)Li is \(|Q/Q_{core}| = 1.088(15)\).
- The \(^4\)He dimer size is 5.2(4) nm and the trimer size is 1.1(5) nm.
- The hypertriton \(^3_{\Lambda}\)H is bound by about 0.14 MeV with respect to break-up into d+\(\Lambda\).

## Context

This review extends the understanding of halo structures from the foundational work of Tanihata et al. (1985) and Hansen and Jonson (1987), and builds on the comprehensive review by Jensen et al. (2004). It connects nuclear halos to the Efimov effect predicted by Efimov (1970) and to molecular halos observed in cold atom systems, and identifies open questions including the location of nuclear halo states in heavier nuclei, the role of pairing, and the need for mass measurements of heavier halo candidates such as \(^{19}\)C.
