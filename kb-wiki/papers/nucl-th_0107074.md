# The low-energy nuclear density of states and the saddle point approximation

> The nuclear density of states plays an important role in nuclear reactions. At high energies, above a few MeV, the nuclear density of states is well described by a formula that depends on the smooth single particle density of states at the Fermi surface, the nuclear shell correction and the pairing energy. In this paper we present an analysis of the low energy behaviour of the nuclear density of states using the saddle point approximation and extensions to it. Furthermore, we prescribe a simple parabolic form for excitation energy, in the low energy limit, which may facilitate an easy computation of level densities.

# The low-energy nuclear density of states and the saddle point approximation

## Key claim
The standard saddle point approximation for the nuclear level density diverges at low excitation energies, but a modification proposed by Grossjean and Feldmeier (1985) that explicitly handles the ground state delta function removes this divergence and gives a level density that goes smoothly to zero at the ground state. The authors further claim that a simple parabolic form for excitation energy as a function of temperature, with parameters depending only on level spacing and degeneracy near the Fermi surface (not on shell corrections), provides a good approximation for computing level densities at low energies.

## Method
The authors use the grand canonical partition function and invert the Laplace transform via the saddle point approximation to obtain the nuclear density of states. They rewrite the determinant of second derivatives in the saddle point expression as a product of three independent thermodynamic derivatives, simplifying the calculation. They then apply the Grossjean-Feldmeier modification, which subtracts the ground state contribution explicitly, and test both the standard and modified saddle point approximations using single-particle energies from the Nilsson shell model for the nuclei 32S, 88Sr, and 208Pb. They also fit the excitation energy as a function of temperature to a parabolic form E - E0 = c (tau - tau0)^2 and compare the resulting level densities to the full modified saddle point calculation.

## Key numbers
- For 32S, the parabolic fit parameters are E0 = 5.62 and tau0 = 0.11 (units not specified in text, presumably MeV for energy and MeV for temperature).
- For 88Sr, the parabolic fit parameters are E0 = 15.71 and tau0 = 0.14.
- For 208Pb, the parabolic fit parameters are E0 = 40.0 and tau0 = 0.20.
- The standard saddle point approximation diverges at low energies for all three nuclei, while the modified version goes to zero smoothly; the differences are more pronounced for lighter nuclei like 32S compared to 208Pb.

## Context
This work extends the standard treatment of nuclear level densities dating back to Rosenzweig (1957) and Gilbert and Cameron (1965), which uses the back-shifted Fermi gas model and works well at energies above a few MeV where shell and pairing effects are washed out. The authors address the problematic low-energy region where the saddle point approximation breaks down, building on the correction method of Grossjean and Feldmeier (1985). The results are relevant for Hauser-Feshbach calculations of nuclear reaction rates for astrophysics, where uncertainties in the nuclear density of states are a leading source of error as noted by Rauscher, Thielemann, and Kratz (1997). The parabolic fit prescription may enable easier computation of level densities for direct application to astrophysical reactions, though the authors note more work is needed.
