# Managing Information for Sparsely Distributed Articles and Readers: The Virtual Journals of the Joint Institute for Nuclear Astrophysics (JINA)

> The research area of nuclear astrophysics is characterized by a need for information published in tens of journals in several fields and an extremely dilute distribution of researchers. For these reasons it is difficult for researchers, especially students, to be adequately informed of the relevant published research. For example, the commonly employed journal club is inefficient for a group consisting of a professor and his two students. In an attempt to address this problem, we have developed a virtual journal (VJ), a process for collecting and distributing a weekly compendium of articles of interest to researchers in nuclear astrophysics. Subscribers are notified of each VJ issue using an email-list server or an RSS feed. The VJ data base is searchable by topics assigned by the editors, or by keywords. There are two related VJs: the Virtual Journal of Nuclear Astrophysics (JINA VJ), and the SEGUE Virtual Journal (SEGUE VJ). The JINA VJ also serves as a source of new experimental and theoretical information for the JINA REACLIB reaction rate database. References to review articles and popular level articles provide an introduction to the literature for students. The VJs and support information are available at http://groups.nscl.msu.edu/jina/journals

## Key claim

The authors describe a practical, semi-automated system for producing weekly virtual journals that collect relevant articles from over forty journals, addressing the problem that nuclear astrophysics researchers are sparsely distributed and cannot rely on traditional journal clubs. The system is not fully automatic due to frequent structural changes in journal tables of contents, requiring about 30 minutes per week for maintenance and 60 minutes per editor per week for article selection.

## Method

The authors developed a process that downloads tables of contents from over forty journals using regular expressions, which are updated as journal websites change. Two editors (a nuclear physicist and an astronomer) independently scan the lists via a web-based PHP interface and assign category tags to selected articles. The chosen titles, links, and categories are entered into a MySQL database, and subscribers receive weekly notifications via email list server or RSS feed.

## Key numbers

- Over forty journals are scanned for appropriate articles, as listed in Table 1 of the paper.
- The first weekly issue of the JINA VJ was published on July 18, 2003.
- A typical issue contains 20-60 articles; 1500 to 2000 articles are included per year.
- A total of 10381 articles were included through the December 2008 issues.
- The subscriber list is approximately 270 individuals.
- From April 2007 to May 2009, Google Analytics recorded 11863 visits with approximately 3 pages viewed per visit.
- Of the 11863 visitors, 6640 were from the Americas, 4390 from Europe, 736 from Asia, 84 from Oceania, and 8 from Africa.
- The top ten countries by visitor count are: United States, Germany, Italy, Brazil, United Kingdom, France, Canada, Hungary, Israel, and Spain.
- In 2008, the largest number of articles came from arXiv.org - Astrophysics (436 articles), Physical Review C (126 articles), and The Astrophysical Journal (113 articles).

## Context

This work extends the concept of curated literature aggregation to the interdisciplinary field of nuclear astrophysics, where researchers are often the only specialist at their institution. The JINA VJ was founded in 2003 and later complemented by the SEGUE VJ in 2006 for astronomical observers. The system also feeds into the JINA REACLIB reaction rate database, providing a pipeline from published literature to recommended nuclear reaction rates used by the community of modelers of stellar and cosmic processes.
