# Mounting Evidence for the Violation of Lepton Flavor Universality

> The Standard Model (SM) of particle physics was finalized in its current form in the mid-1970s, and has been extensively tested and confirmed ever since, with the discovery of the Higgs boson in 2012 being the last missing piece. While no new particles have been directly discovered at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN so far, precision observables sensitive to quantum effects of new particles have accumulated intriguing hints for physics beyond the SM. All these anomalies can be interpreted from the point of view of lepton flavor universality, i.e., as hints that electrons, muons, and tau leptons differ much more than predicted by the SM. These tensions can be explained by postulating the existence of new exotic particles. Future measurements will be able to conclusively test this hypothesis, potentially providing long-awaited evidence how the SM needs to be extended at high energies.

## Key claim

The paper argues that multiple precision observables show deviations from Standard Model predictions that collectively point toward the violation of lepton flavor universality, but notes that none of these anomalies individually constitute a definitive discovery and that future measurements are required for conclusive confirmation.

## Method

The authors review and combine results from several precision experiments: the muon g-2 measurement at Fermilab and Brookhaven; LHCb measurements of rare B meson decays involving b→sμ⁺μ⁻ transitions and the ratios R(K^(*)); measurements of b→cℓν transitions including R(D^(*)) from HFLAV; nuclear beta decay data for the Cabibbo Angle anomaly; and CMS measurements of qq̅→e⁺e⁻ production. They interpret these anomalies collectively as evidence for new particles such as leptoquarks that would violate lepton flavor universality.

## Key numbers

- Muon g-2 combined result shows a 4.2σ tension with the Standard Model prediction from the Muon g-2 Theory Initiative
- LHCb measurement of R(K^(*)) alone quotes a significance of 3.1σ
- Global fits of b→sμ⁺μ⁻ anomalies find a preference over the Standard Model hypothesis of >5σ
- R(D^(*)) deviations from Standard Model predictions have a combined significance of about 3σ (HFLAV)
- Cabibbo Angle anomaly displays a significance around 3σ (Particle Data Group)

## Context

This paper extends the ongoing search for physics beyond the Standard Model that began with the 2006 Brookhaven muon g-2 measurement and continued with LHCb results on rare B decays. The anomalies reviewed here build on earlier work by Capdevila (2017), Aebischer (2019), and others who performed global fits of b→sμ⁺μ⁻ data. The paper enables future experimental tests at the LHC, proposed FCC-ee collider, and other facilities that could confirm or refute the existence of leptoquarks and other new particles at scales accessible to direct detection.
