# Core-Collapse Astrophysics with a Five-Megaton Neutrino Detector

> The legacy of solar neutrinos suggests that large neutrino detectors should be sited underground. However, to instead go underwater bypasses the need to move mountains, allowing much larger water Cherenkov detectors. We show that reaching a detector mass scale of ~5 Megatons, the size of the proposed Deep-TITAND, would permit observations of neutrino "mini-bursts" from supernovae in nearby galaxies on a roughly yearly basis, and we develop the immediate qualitative and quantitative consequences. Importantly, these mini-bursts would be detected over backgrounds without the need for optical evidence of the supernova, guaranteeing the beginning of time-domain MeV neutrino astronomy. The ability to identify, to the second, every core collapse in the local Universe would allow a continuous "death watch" of all stars within ~5 Mpc, making practical many previously-impossible tasks in probing rare outcomes and refining coordination of multi-wavelength/multi-particle observations and analysis. These include the abilities to promptly detect otherwise-invisible prompt black hole formation, provide advance warning for supernova shock-breakout searches, define tight time windows for gravitational-wave searches, and identify "supernova impostors" by the non-detection of neutrinos. Observations of many supernovae, even with low numbers of detected neutrinos, will help answer questions about supernovae that cannot be resolved with a single high-statistics event in the Milky Way.

## Key claim

A 5-Megaton water Cherenkov detector, such as the proposed Deep-TITAND, would enable routine detection of neutrino "mini-bursts" (3 or more events within 10 seconds) from core-collapse supernovae in nearby galaxies on a roughly yearly basis, without requiring optical confirmation; this claim depends on the assumption that the nearby supernova rate is at least 1 per year within approximately 6 Mpc, which the authors argue is supported by observations but remains uncertain.

## Method

The authors calculate neutrino event yields for a 5-Megaton water Cherenkov detector using inverse beta decay of electron antineutrinos on free protons, assuming a Fermi-Dirac spectrum with average energy 15 MeV and total energy 5 × 10^52 erg for the received electron antineutrino spectrum after neutrino mixing. They estimate background rates by scaling from Super-Kamiokande measurements, using a threshold of 18 MeV positron energy to suppress low-energy backgrounds, and require at least 3 detected events within a 10-second window to claim a detection. The nearby supernova rate is estimated from the Karachentsev (2004) galaxy catalog with B-band luminosity conversion, and from direct counts of supernovae observed between 1999-2008 within 10 Mpc.

## Key numbers

- A 5-Megaton detector would yield approximately 5 neutrino events (E_e+ > 18 MeV) from a supernova at 3.9 Mpc distance, scaling as (3.9 Mpc / D)^2
- The accidental coincidence rate for 3 events in a 10-second window is approximately once every five years; for 4 or more events, approximately once per 3000 years
- The background rate in the 18-60 MeV range for a 5-Megaton detector is approximately 5 × 10^-4 events per second, scaled from the Super-Kamiokande rate of 0.2 events per day
- The core-collapse supernova rate within approximately 6 Mpc is estimated to be at least 1 per year; within 10 Mpc, at least 2 per year
- The Milky Way supernova rate is estimated at 2 ± 1 per century
- SN 2008S in NGC 6946 (5.6 Mpc) would yield 2.4 neutrino events; the NGC 300 transient (2.15 Mpc) would yield 16.0 events
- A 5-Megaton detector would collect approximately 100 times the exposure of the Super-Kamiokande DSNB search after four years

## Context

This paper extends earlier work by Ando (2005) on neutrino detection from nearby supernovae, which considered detectors at the 0.5-Megaton scale where typically one neutrino at a time is detected and optical coincidence is required. The authors show that scaling to 5 Megatons qualitatively changes the science possibilities, enabling neutrino-only detection of core collapses and a continuous "death watch" of all stars within approximately 5 Mpc. This enables prompt detection of otherwise-invisible prompt black hole formation (following the "unnova" concept of Kochanek, 2008), provides advance warning for supernova shock-breakout searches, defines tight time windows for gravitational-wave searches, and can identify "supernova impostors" such as LBV outbursts (e.g., SN 2002kg) by neutrino non-detection. The proposed Deep-TITAND detector concept was developed by Suzuki (2001).
