# A meson-exchange model of the associated photoproduction of vector mesons and N*(1440) resonances

> We discuss the photoproduction of omega- and rho0-mesons off protons in the particular channel where the target proton is excited to a Roper resonance N*(1440). We propose a simple meson-exchange model for these processes in order to evaluate their cross sections near threshold and at low momentum transfers. It is suggested in particular that the differential cross section for the associated photoproduction of a rho0-meson and a Roper resonance in these kinematic conditions could provide direct information on the strength of the scalar-isoscalar excitation of the N*(1440) and hence on the magnitude of an effective sigma N N*(1440) coupling. The latter quantity is poorly known and of much interest for the nuclear many-body problem.

## Key claim

The differential cross section for the associated photoproduction of a rho0-meson and a Roper resonance N*(1440) near threshold and at low momentum transfers could provide direct information on the strength of the scalar-isoscalar excitation of the N*(1440) and hence on the magnitude of an effective sigma NN*(1440) coupling, which is poorly known and of much interest for the nuclear many-body problem. The author cautions that the simple meson-exchange model is only expected to be valid for momentum transfers up to about q^2 = -0.5 GeV^2, set by the cutoff in the pi NN* form factor.

## Method

The author constructs a one-meson exchange model for the reactions gamma p -> omega N*+(1440) and gamma p -> rho0 N*+(1440) near threshold, using pi-exchange and sigma-exchange diagrams in the t-channel within the Vector Dominance Model. The pi NN*(1440) coupling constant is extracted from the partial decay width of the Roper resonance into the pi N channel, while the sigma NN*(1440) coupling constant is extracted from the decay into the N(pi pi)_S-wave channel via an intermediate sigma-meson model. The differential cross sections are calculated both in the zero-width approximation for the Roper resonance and including its finite width, with the cross sections evaluated for the subsequent decay channels omega pi0 p and rho0 pi+ n respectively.

## Key numbers

- The pi NN*(1440) coupling constant extracted from the partial decay width: g^2_piNN*/4pi = 3.4 +/- 1.2, corresponding to f^2_piNN*/4pi = 0.011 +/- 0.004
- The sigma NN*(1440) coupling constant extracted from the partial decay width: g^2_sigmaNN*/4pi = 0.34 +/- 0.21, assuming sigma-meson mass m^0_sigma = 500 MeV and width Gamma_sigma->pipi = 250 MeV
- The sigma NN*(1440) coupling constant varies strongly with sigma-meson mass: g^2_sigmaNN*/4pi = 0.23 +/- 0.14 for m^0_sigma = 450 MeV, and g^2_sigmaNN*/4pi = 0.56 +/- 0.35 for m^0_sigma = 550 MeV
- The threshold photon energy for gamma p -> omega N*+(1440) at the peak of the Roper resonance: E_gamma^threshold = 2.16 GeV
- At q^2 = -0.36 GeV^2 and E_gamma = 2.5 GeV, the differential cross section for gamma p -> omega N*+(1440) -> omega pi0 p is about 20 times smaller than the zero-width approximation value
- At q^2 = -0.36 GeV^2 and E_gamma = 2.5 GeV, the differential cross section for gamma p -> rho0 N*+(1440) -> rho0 pi+ n is about 10 times smaller than the zero-width approximation value

## Context

This work extends the meson-exchange model of Friman (1995) for vector meson photoproduction off protons to the case where the target proton is excited to a Roper resonance N*(1440). The sigma NN*(1440) coupling is poorly constrained, with values ranging from g^2_sigmaNN*/4pi = 0.05 used in the transport models of Huber (1995) and Mao (1996) to g^2_sigmaNN*/4pi = 1.33 extracted from alpha-particle scattering data by Hirenzaki (1996). The proposed photoproduction reactions could resolve this uncertainty, which is important for understanding three-nucleon interactions as studied by Coon (1992) and for describing subthreshold antiproton production in heavy ion collisions as studied by Li (1995). The author suggests that measurements at ELSA with the Crystal Barrel detector or with linearly polarized photons could separate the pi- and sigma-exchange contributions.
