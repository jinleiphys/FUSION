# Impact of level densities and $γ$-strength functions on $r$-process simulations

> Studies attempting to quantify the sensitivity of the $r$-process abundances to nuclear input have to cope with the fact that the theoretical models they rely on, rarely come with confidence intervals. This problem has been dealt with by either estimating these intervals and propagating them statistically to the final abundances using reaction networks within simplified astrophysical models, or by running more realistic astrophysical simulations using different nuclear-physics models consistently for all the involved nuclei. Both of these approaches have their strengths and weaknesses. In this work, we run $r$-process calculations for five trajectories using 49 different neutron-capture rate models. Our results shed light on the importance of taking into account shell effects and pairing correlations in the network calculations.

# Impact of level densities and γ-strength functions on r-process simulations

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that using a model-consistent approach (varying neutron-capture rate models coherently across all nuclei) produces smaller abundance uncertainties than the statistical approach (varying rates individually), suggesting that shell effects and pairing correlations reduce prediction uncertainties in r-process abundances when properly accounted for.

## Method

The authors ran one-zone r-process simulations using the SkyNet reaction network for five different trajectories (hot and cold r-process conditions) with 49 different neutron-capture rate models. The rates were calculated using TALYS 1.95 with combinations of six nuclear level density models and eight γ-ray strength function models, applied consistently across all nuclei from Fe (Z=26) to Sg (Z=106), using both the FRDM-2012 and HFB-17 mass models.

## Key numbers

- 49 different neutron-capture rate models were used: 48 combinations of 6 NLD models and 8 GSF models, plus the JINA REACLIB default rates
- Five trajectories were simulated: hot r-process with S=30 kB, Ye=0.20, τ=70 ms; hot with S=100 kB, Ye=0.25, τ=80 ms; cold NDW with Ye=0.31; neutron-rich NSM; hot with S=200 kB, Ye=0.30, τ=80 ms
- The spread in predicted neutron-capture rates at T=1.0 GK can reach several orders of magnitude, with the largest differences near neutron magic numbers N=82 and N=126 and close to the neutron drip line
- The A≈186 region shows the largest relative uncertainty in final abundances, originating from the most neutron-rich region near the N=126 isotone

## Context

This work extends the sensitivity study approach of Mumpower et al. (2016) by using a model-consistent methodology rather than the statistical approach of varying rates individually. It contrasts with prior work by Nikas et al. (2020) and Surman et al. (2009, 2014) that used multiplicative factors or probability distributions for rate variations. The results enable comparison between the two methodological approaches and highlight that the statistical approach may overestimate uncertainties by washing out shell effects and pairing correlations. The study uses the same trajectories as Mumpower et al. (2016) and builds on the nuclear reaction code TALYS framework for calculating neutron-capture rates.
