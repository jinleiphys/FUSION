# Traversing the QCD Phase Transition: Quenching Out of Equilibrium vs. Slowing Out of Equilibrium vs. Bubbling Out of Equilibrium

> I review arguments for the existence of a critical point E in the QCD phase diagram as a function of temperature T and baryon chemical potential μ. I describe how heavy ion collision experiments at the SPS and RHIC can discover the tell-tale signatures of such a critical point, thus mapping this region of the QCD phase diagram. I contrast the different ways in which the matter produced in a heavy ion collision can be driven out of equilibrium: quenching out of equilibrium (possible, but not guaranteed, if the transition region is traversed at μ<< μ_E) vs. slowing out of equilibrium (guaranteed for μ\sim μ_E) vs. bubbling out of equilibrium (possible, but not guaranteed, for μ>> μ_E). Quenching or bubbling create and amplify distinct, detectable, non-gaussian fluctuations. In contrast, slowing out of equilibrium reduces the magnitude of the specific, detectable, gaussian fluctuations which signal the presence of the critical point.

# Traversing the QCD Phase Transition: Quenching Out of Equilibrium vs. Slowing Out of Equilibrium vs. Bubbling Out of Equilibrium

## Key claim
The QCD critical point E can be discovered experimentally via nonmonotonic signatures as collision energy is varied, but critical slowing down limits the correlation length to about 2/T_E ~ 3 fm even for trajectories passing near E, meaning that signatures will have approximately the same magnitude over a broad range of chemical potential rather than being narrowly peaked.

## Method
The author reviews theoretical arguments for the existence of a critical point E in the QCD phase diagram and describes how heavy ion collisions at SPS and RHIC can detect it through event-by-event fluctuations of mean transverse momentum and multiplicity. The analysis uses the theory of dynamical critical phenomena applied to the three-dimensional Ising model to describe how the correlation length ξ evolves as the system cools through the critical point, incorporating critical slowing down and the cooling rate estimated at 4 MeV/fm.

## Key numbers
- The critical point correlation length at freezeout is estimated as ξ_freezeout ~ 2/T_E ~ 3 fm for trajectories passing near E
- The maximum ξ scales with cooling rate as a^0.215, where a is proportional to |dT/dt|^{-1}; a factor of 25 smaller cooling rate would yield ξ ~ 4/T_E instead of 2/T_E
- For ξ_freezeout ~ 3 fm, the ratio √F (width of event-by-event mean p_T distribution relative to mixed events) increases by ~3-5%, which is ten to twenty times the statistical error in the present NA49 measurement of √F = 1.002 ± 0.002
- NA49 data at √s = 17 GeV (158 AGeV) show freezeout near μ ~ 200 MeV; AGS at √s = 5 GeV (11 AGeV) shows freezeout near μ ~ 500-600 MeV
- Crude models suggest μ_E could be ~600-800 MeV in the absence of the strange quark; with strange quark effects, Δμ/μ_E ~ 15% is a conservative estimate for the range over which ξ_eq > 2 fm
- T_E is taken to be ~140 MeV; the cooling rate |dT/dt| is estimated at 4 MeV/fm

## Context
This paper extends the theoretical framework of Stephanov, Rajagopal, and Shuryak (1998, 1999) who first proposed experimental signatures of the QCD critical point, by incorporating the dynamical effects of critical slowing down analyzed in detail by Berdnikov and Rajagopal (2000). The work contrasts three regimes of out-of-equilibrium behavior: quenching (possible for μ << μ_E, producing DCC oscillations as studied by Rajagopal and Wilczek (1993) and seen in simulations by others), bubbling (possible for μ >> μ_E, producing non-gaussian fluctuations), and slowing out of equilibrium (guaranteed for μ ~ μ_E). The analysis enables experimental searches by showing that the critical point can be detected without needing finely spaced energy scans, and that observables focusing on low-momentum pions (such as √F_soft and excess soft pion multiplicity from post-freezeout sigma decay) will be most sensitive. The paper also notes that NA49 data show no signs of critical fluctuations at 158 AGeV, suggesting either that SPS probes the crossover region left of E (in which case lower SPS energies could find E) or that RHIC will find E with μ_E < 200 MeV.
