# Towards Closing the Window on Strongly Interacting Dark Matter: Far-Reaching Constraints from Earth's Heat Flow

> We point out a new and largely model-independent constraint on the dark matter scattering cross section with nucleons, applying when this quantity is larger than for typical weakly interacting dark matter candidates. When the dark matter capture rate in Earth is efficient, the rate of energy deposition by dark matter self-annihilation products would grossly exceed the measured heat flow of Earth. This improves the spin-independent cross section constraints by many orders of magnitude, and closes the window between astrophysical constraints (at very large cross sections) and underground detector constraints (at small cross sections). In the applicable mass range, from about 1 to about 10^{10} GeV, the scattering cross section of dark matter with nucleons is then bounded from above by the latter constraints, and hence must be truly weak, as usually assumed.

# Towards Closing the Window on Strongly Interacting Dark Matter: Far-Reaching Constraints from Earth's Heat Flow

## Key claim

The dark matter scattering cross section with nucleons can be constrained from above by requiring that energy deposition from dark matter self-annihilation in Earth's core does not exceed Earth's measured internal heat flow of 44.2 TW, but this constraint only applies when the capture rate is efficient enough that dark matter particles are gravitationally captured and then drift to the core within Earth's lifetime, and it assumes dark matter is its own antiparticle with Standard Model annihilation products.

## Method

The authors calculated the minimum dark matter-nucleon scattering cross section required for efficient gravitational capture of dark matter in Earth by determining the number of elastic scatterings needed to reduce a dark matter particle's velocity below Earth's escape speed (11.2 km/s), using oxygen as the dominant target nucleus in the crust with a path length of 0.2 Earth radii. They then compared the maximal heating rate from dark matter annihilation (assuming all captured dark matter annihilates and deposits energy in Earth's core) to Earth's measured internal heat flow, deriving an excluded region in the spin-independent cross section versus dark matter mass plane bounded below by the requirement of efficient capture and above by the requirement that captured dark matter drifts to the core within Earth's age.

## Key numbers

- Earth's measured internal heat flow: 44.2 ± 1 TW, from averaging over 20,000 borehole measurements giving a surface heat flux of 0.087 ± 0.002 W/m²
- Maximal dark matter capture rate for an opaque Earth: 2 × 10²⁵ (GeV/m_χ) s⁻¹
- Maximal heating rate from dark matter annihilation (opaque Earth): 3330 TW
- Heating rate from dark matter annihilation under the authors' assumptions: 3260 TW
- Residual heat flow used as the target limit for exclusion: 20 TW
- Dark matter mass range considered: approximately 1 to 10¹⁰ GeV
- Dark matter density near the solar system: 0.3 GeV/cm³
- Average dark matter velocity: 270 km/s
- Earth's escape speed: 11.2 km/s
- Solar heating rate (received and returned): 170,000 TW
- Kinetic heating from dark matter: approximately 3000 × 10⁻⁶ TW

## Context

This work extends prior constraints on strongly interacting dark matter from Starkman, Gould, Esmailzadeh, and Dimopoulos (1990) and Natarajan et al. (2002) by providing a new, largely model-independent constraint based on Earth's heat flow, closing the window between astrophysical constraints at very large cross sections and underground detector constraints at small cross sections. Previous studies of dark matter heating of planets focused on Jovian planets (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus) as in work by Kawasaki, Fukugita, and others, but the authors argue Earth provides more robust results due to detailed knowledge of its properties and the much more favorable ratio of potential dark matter heating to measured internal heat flow. The constraint enables the conclusion that dark matter-nucleon scattering must be truly weak (as usually assumed) across the mass range from about 1 to 10¹⁰ GeV, though the authors call for new analyses of the exact region excluded by CDMS and other underground detectors to be certain.
