# An optical-lensing inspired data thinning method for nuclear cross section data

> In the study of nuclear cross sections, the computational demands of data assimilation methods can become prohibitive when dealing with large data sets. We have developed a novel variant of the data thinning algorithm, inspired by the principles of optical lensing, which effectively reduces data volume while preserving critical information. We show how it improves fitting through a toy problem and for several examples of total cross sections for neutron-induced reactions on rare-earth isotopes. We demonstrate how this method can be applied as an efficient pre-processing step prior to smoothing, significantly improving computational efficiency without compromising the quality of uncertainty quantification.

## Key claim
The authors claim that their "optical-lensing inspired" data thinning method reduces data volume while preserving critical information for nuclear cross section analysis, but the hedge is that it works best as a pre-processing step prior to smoothing (FBET) and that the method tends to exclude points within pronounced resonance peaks, favoring smoother samples more representative of the average cross section rather than preserving all structural features.

## Method
The method bins experimental cross section data by energy, then within each bin selects the measurement that maximizes a likelihood function based on percentiles (minimum, median, maximum) of cross section values in the adjacent bin. A kernel-density-estimate (KDE) variant uses the full distribution of values in the adjacent bin instead of just three percentiles, and a third variant incorporates heteroscedastic measurement uncertainties as the kernel bandwidth. The selected points are removed and the procedure repeats until a desired number of measurements per bin remains.

## Key numbers
- For the linear model simulation (y = 2x + 2, N_s = 1000), the percentile-based lensing method recovered slope = 0.98 ± 0.31 and intercept = 2.62 ± 0.16, with Chow's F = 0.12 and P = 0.9965
- Computation time for percentile-based lensing on n+124Sn: 0.0312 ± 0.0014 seconds; for KDE-based: 0.237 ± 0.018 seconds; for classical thinning: 1500 ± 390 seconds
- Computation time for percentile-based lensing on n+144Sm: 0.0121 ± 0.0023 seconds; for KDE-based: 0.546 ± 0.013 seconds; for classical thinning: 1.98 × 10^5 seconds (omitted due to long runtimes)
- Bootstrap simulations used N_B = 10000 resamples for the linear model test and 100 bootstrap samples for nuclear data timing estimates

## Context
This work extends data thinning methods previously developed for spatial datasets by Ochotta (2005) and applies them to nuclear cross section data, which are typically processed using the Full Bayesian Evaluation Technique (FBET) developed by Leeb (2008, 2011). The method is demonstrated on total cross section data for neutron-induced reactions on rare-earth isotopes (124Sn, 144Sm, 143Nd, 150Nd) from the EXFOR database, using the CoH3 Hauser-Feshbach code with the Koning-Delaroche optical potential (Koning, 2003). The approach enables more efficient data assimilation for nuclear data evaluation projects like ENDF/B (Chadwick, 2011) and could be applied to other data-intensive fields such as r-process nucleosynthesis modeling (Kajino, 2019; Mumpower, 2016) and supernova modeling (Langanke, 2003).
