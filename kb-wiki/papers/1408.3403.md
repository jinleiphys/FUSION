# Mass depletion: a new parameter for quantitative jet modification

> We propose an extension to classify jet modification in heavy-ion collisions by including the jet mass along with its energy. The mass of a jet, as measured by jet reconstruction algorithms, is constrained by the jet's virtuality, which in turn has a considerable effect on such observables as the fragmentation function and jet shape observables. The leading parton, propagating through a dense medium, experiences substantial virtuality (or mass) depletion along with energy loss. Meaningful comparisons between surviving jets and jets produced in $p$-$p$ collisions require mass depletion to be taken into account. Using a vacuum event generator, we show the close relationship between the actual jet mass and that after applying a jet reconstruction algorithm. Using an in-medium event generator, we demonstrate the clear difference between the mass of a surviving parton exiting a dense medium and a parton with a similar energy formed in a hard scattering event. Effects of this difference on jet observables are discussed.

## Key claim

The authors propose that jets escaping a dense medium are fundamentally different from jets of the same energy produced in vacuum, because the leading parton experiences substantial "mass depletion" (reduced virtuality) in addition to energy loss; this mass depletion must be accounted for when comparing jets from heavy-ion collisions to those from proton-proton collisions, as it can explain the observed rise in the fragmentation function at high momentum fraction.

## Method

The authors used the MATTER event generator, which implements a medium-modified DGLAP shower based on Higher-Twist expressions for single gluon emission, to simulate 100 GeV quark jets propagating through a static QGP medium. They tracked the virtuality (mass) and energy of the leading parton as a function of propagation length, comparing results in vacuum and in media with different transport coefficients (hat-q = 1 and 2 GeV^2/fm) and medium lengths (2 fm and 4 fm). They also used PYTHIA and JETSET vacuum event generators to establish the relationship between the true jet mass and the reconstructed jet mass from FASTJET (with R=0.7), and to demonstrate the sensitivity of the fragmentation function to the reconstructed jet mass.

## Key numbers

* For jets with energies between 90-110 GeV reconstructed with FASTJET (R=0.7), the reconstructed jet mass is closely correlated with an underestimate of the parent parton mass (Fig. 1).
* The fragmentation function for jets with p_T^Jet = 70-80 GeV/c (R=0.7) shows strong sensitivity to reconstructed mass bins: 8-12 GeV, 12-16 GeV, and 16-20 GeV, with larger masses suppressing the high-z portion and enhancing the low-z portion (Fig. 2).
* For a 100 GeV jet in vacuum, the mean mass of the leading parton drops below 2 GeV by a distance of 4 fm, while the leading parton still carries approximately 90% of the jet energy on average.
* For a 100 GeV jet in a static medium with hat-q = 1 GeV^2/fm, the mass of the leading parton at L=2 fm and L=4 fm is higher than the vacuum case at the same distances, indicating that scattering in the medium reduces the rate of mass depletion (Fig. 4).
* For a medium with double the density (hat-q = 2 GeV^2/fm) and L=2 fm, the in-medium mass of the leading parton separates more swiftly from the vacuum case up to 2 fm.

## Context

This work extends the phenomenology of jet modification in heavy-ion collisions from single-particle observables (such as R_AA at RHIC, as studied by STAR and PHENIX) to the four-vector level by introducing jet mass as a new parameter. It builds on the MATTER event generator (Majumder, 2013) and the Higher-Twist formalism for medium-induced gluon emission (Majumder, 2009). The results suggest that the observed suppression at intermediate-z and rise at high-z in the fragmentation function measured by ATLAS and CMS in Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC can be partially explained by the reduced mass of escaping jets. This work enables future experimental measurements of jet mass to provide non-trivial tests for models of in-medium shower evolution.
