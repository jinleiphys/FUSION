# Quark phases in neutron stars and a "third family" of compact stars as a signature for phase transitions

> The appearance of quark phases in the dense interior of neutron stars provides one possibility to soften the equation of state (EOS) of neutron star matter at high densities. This softening leads to more compact equilibrium configurations of neutron stars compared to pure hadronic stars of the same mass. We investigate the question to which amount the compactness of a neutron star can be attributed to the presence of a quark phase. For this purpose we employ several hadronic EOS in the framework of the relativistic mean-field (RMF) model and an extended MIT bag model to describe the quark phase. We find that - almost independent of the model parameters - the radius of a pure hadronic neutron star gets typically reduced by 20-30% if a pure quark phase in the center of the star does exist. For some EOS we furthermore find the possibility of a "third family" of compact stars which may exist besides the two known families of white dwarfs and neutron stars. We show how an experimental proof of the existence of a third family by mass and radius measurements may provide a unique signature for a phase transition inside neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the radius of a pure hadronic neutron star is typically reduced by 20-30% if a pure quark phase exists in the star's center, and that this reduction is "almost independent of the model parameters." However, the actual hedge is that this result depends on the specific choice of hadronic equation of state and quark phase parameters (bag constant and coupling constant), and for some equations of state a "third family" of compact stars may exist only under certain parameter combinations.

## Method

The authors employed several hadronic equations of state (TM1, TM2, GL85, GPS) calculated in the relativistic mean-field model including hyperons, matched at nuclear density to the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland EOS for the crust. The quark phase was described using an extended MIT bag model with density-dependent effective quark masses from the hard dense loop approximation, treating the bag constant B and strong coupling constant g as free parameters. A first-order phase transition from hadronic to quark matter was constructed using the Gibbs condition for a two-component system (with neutron and electron chemical potentials as independent variables), allowing for a mixed phase region where global charge neutrality is maintained by a positively charged hadronic component and a negatively charged quark component. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations were then solved to obtain mass-radius relations for non-rotating neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- The radius of a pure hadronic neutron star is typically reduced by 20-30% if a pure quark phase exists in the star's center.
- The bag constant B was varied from B^(1/4) = 165 MeV (B ≈ 96 MeV/fm^3) to B^(1/4) ≈ 200 MeV (B ≈ 208 MeV/fm^3).
- The strong coupling constant g was varied from g=0 (no medium effects) to g ≈ 2-3 (α_s ≈ 0.31-0.72).
- Normal nuclear matter density is ε_0 = 140 MeV/fm^3 ≈ 2.5 × 10^14 g/cm^3.
- The canonical neutron star mass used is M = 1.4 M_⊙ (solar masses).
- For the GPS hadronic EOS with g=0, a 1.4 M_⊙ star possesses a pure quark phase core for bag constants below B^(1/4) ≈ 180 MeV, a mixed phase core up to B^(1/4) ≈ 195 MeV, and is purely hadronic above that.
- The radius separation between stars possessing a quark core and pure hadronic stars of the same mass is about 2-4 km.
- The energy per baryon of the quark phase at the lower bag constant bound (B^(1/4)=165 MeV) is E/A ≈ 990 MeV, about 60 MeV unbound with respect to ^56Fe (930 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the earlier finding by Glendenning (1992) on the possibility of mixed phases in neutron stars and the concept of a "third family" of compact stars first proposed by Gerlach (1968). The paper systematically investigates how the compactness of neutron stars can be attributed to quark phases, building on prior work by Schertler et al. (1998, 1999) on the effective mass bag model. The results enable future mass and radius measurements of compact stars to potentially provide a unique signature for a phase transition inside neutron stars, distinguishing between purely hadronic stars, hybrid stars with quark cores, and a possible third family of compact stars that would exist alongside white dwarfs and neutron stars.
