# Mixed-Symmetry Shell-Model Calculations

> The one-dimensional harmonic oscillator in a box problem is used to introduce the concept of an oblique-basis shell-model theory. The method is applied to nuclei by combining traditional spherical shell-model states with SU(3) collective configurations. An application to $^{24}$Mg, using the realistic two-body interaction of Wildenthal, is used to explore the validity of this oblique-basis, mixed-symmetry shell-model concept. The applicability of the theory to the lower pf-shell nuclei $^{44-48}$Ti and $^{48}$Cr using the Kuo-Brown-3 interaction is also discussed. While these nuclei show strong SU(3) symmetry breaking due mainly to the single-particle spin-orbit splitting, they continue to yield enhanced B(E2) values not unlike those expected if the symmetry were not broken. Other alternative basis sets are considered for future oblique-basis shell-model calculations. The results suggest that an oblique-basis, mixed-symmetry shell-model theory may prove to be useful in situations where competing degrees of freedom dominate the dynamics.

# Mixed-Symmetry Shell-Model Calculations

## Key claim
The oblique-basis mixed-symmetry shell-model approach, which combines traditional spherical shell-model states with SU(3) collective configurations, can reproduce low-energy nuclear properties within 2% of full-space results while using less than 10% of the full model space; however, the method is most useful in systems where competing single-particle and collective degrees of freedom dominate the dynamics, and its success depends on the system being far from any exactly solvable limit of the Hamiltonian.

## Method
The authors first introduce the concept using a one-dimensional harmonic oscillator in a box toy model, demonstrating that a mixed basis of harmonic oscillator states and particle-in-a-box states (an oblique, nonorthogonal, overcomplete set) can describe the spectrum near the crossover energy. They then apply the method to real nuclei by combining traditional spherical shell-model states (which diagonalize the single-particle interaction) with SU(3) collective configurations (which diagonalize the quadrupole-quadrupole interaction), using the realistic Wildenthal interaction for ²⁴Mg and the Kuo-Brown-3 (KB3) interaction for lower pf-shell nuclei ⁴⁴–⁴⁸Ti and ⁴⁸Cr.

## Key numbers
- For ²⁴Mg: the oblique-basis calculation uses a model space spanning less than 10% of the full sd-shell space and reproduces the correct binding energy, low-energy spectrum, and states with greater than 90% overlap with full-space results, within 2% of the full-space result.
- For ²⁴Mg: adding the SU(3) configurations (8,4) and (9,2) to the SM(2) spherical basis (a 0.5% increase in dimensionality) yields a dramatic 3.3 MeV increase in binding energy; enlarging from SM(2) to SM(4) (a 54% increase in dimensionality) adds 4.2 MeV.
- For the 1D harmonic oscillator in a box toy model: the numerical study includes up to 50 harmonic oscillator states below the crossover energy Ec = ω²L²/2, with parameters ω = 16, L = π/2, ħ = m = 1.
- For lower pf-shell nuclei ⁴⁴–⁴⁸Ti and ⁴⁸Cr: the KB3 Hamiltonian shows strong SU(3) symmetry breaking due to single-particle spin-orbit splitting, yet the yrast band B(E2) values remain enhanced, similar to expectations if the symmetry were not broken.

## Context
This work extends the spherical shell model of Whitehead and the Elliott SU(3) model by introducing a mixed-symmetry oblique-basis approach that handles systems where single-particle and collective degrees of freedom compete. The results for pf-shell nuclei support the quasi-SU(3) symmetry concept previously discussed in the literature (Gueorguiev, 2002; Adiabatic mixing). The method enables future multi-mode extensions, potentially incorporating pairing eigenvectors within the Sp(4) algebraic approach (Sviratcheva), Hamiltonian-driven J-pair states mimicking the Interacting Boson Model (Iachello, 1987) and broken-pair theory (Heyde), and three-mode systems corresponding to the three exact limits of the IBM (Moshinsky).
