# Muon lifetime dependent effects in MiniBooNE and LSND

> We argue that because the source-detector distance of 1.8 microseconds (in natural units) for the MiniBooNE is comparable to the muon lifetime of 2.2 microseconds, and because time dilation effects are wiped out in the beam stop, the Goldman entanglement of the neutrinos leads to hitherto unsuspected interpretational consequences. We show that a distinct possibility exists in which a LSND-like experiment sees no CP violation, whereas a MiniBooNE-like setup reproduces the LSND results for the $\barν_μ$ to $\barν_e$ oscillations while seeing only a significantly suppressed signal for the $ν_μ$ to $ν_e$ oscillations. We also discuss an alternate scenario. This also suggests that the LSND experiment and the MiniBooNE should not be compared without taking into account the Goldman entanglement.

# Muon lifetime dependent effects in MiniBooNE and LSND

## Key claim

The source-detector distance for MiniBooNE (1.8 μs in natural units) is comparable to the muon lifetime (2.2 μs), and because time dilation effects are wiped out in the beam stop, the Goldman entanglement of neutrinos with their parent muons leads to interpretational consequences that may mimic CP violation. A distinct possibility exists in which an LSND-like experiment sees no CP violation, whereas a MiniBooNE-like setup reproduces the LSND results for antineutrino oscillations while seeing only a significantly suppressed signal for neutrino oscillations.

## Method

The authors introduce a phenomenological modification to the canonical neutrino oscillation probability that incorporates an exponential suppression factor exp[-ℓ/τ_μ] controlling the number of positrons that undergo annihilation in the beam dump, which destroys the Goldman entanglement between ν_μ and μ⁺. They analyze this "hard" scenario alongside SN1987a data from Kamiokande II and IMB, binning events into an "e-bin" (energy ≤ 15 MeV) and a "μτ-bin" (energy ≥ 15 MeV), and derive parametric constraints on the mixing angles θ₁₂ and θ₁₃ by equating the theoretical ratio of events to the observed ratio. They then apply these constraints to the MiniBooNE and LSND experiments using the MiniBooNE beam spectrum and analytical oscillation probabilities for LSND.

## Key numbers

- MiniBooNE source-detector distance: ℓ = 1.8 μs (in natural units), approximately equal to τ_μ ≈ 2.20 μs
- LSND source-detector distance: ℓ′ = 0.1 μs, approximately 0.05 τ_μ
- LSND observed oscillation probability: 𝒫^{LSND}_{ν̄_μ→ν̄_e} = (0.31 ± 0.12 ± 0.05) × 10⁻²
- LSND observed oscillation probability: 𝒫^{LSND}_{ν_μ→ν_e} = (0.26 ± 0.10 ± 0.05) × 10⁻²
- In the "hard" scenario, the oscillatory term for MiniBooNE ν_μ→ν_e has roughly 44% of the expected amplitude, with a roughly 56% constant addition to the e-like flux
- The MiniBooNE/LSND relevant mass-squared difference turns out to be ≥ 0.05 eV²
- At the lower limit of this mass-squared difference, θ₁₂ ≈ 0.6 radians, which translates to θ₁₃ ≈ 0, and θ₂₃ ≈ 0
- For SN1987a data, both the e-bin and μτ-bin contain 12 events each, giving γ = 1
- Average neutrino energy for e-bin events: 11 MeV; for μτ-bin events: 31 MeV
- The ratio of cross sections for e-bin events was approximated as (11/31) times that of the μτ-bin

## Context

This work extends the concept of quantum entanglement in neutrino oscillations first argued by Goldman (1996) and later emphasized by Nauenberg (1998) and by Cohen, Glashow, and Ligeti (2008), who noted that energy-momentum conservation induces entanglement between decay products when one product is a superposition of mass eigenstates. The paper directly addresses the tension between the LSND experiment at Los Alamos and the MiniBooNE experiment, which was formed to resolve the LSND anomaly but produced inconclusive results. The analysis suggests that existing neutrino oscillation data and their analysis would require a significant new effort to settle the mass-squared differences and mixing matrix, and that the LSND experiment and MiniBooNE should not be compared without taking into account the Goldman entanglement.
