# Color Transparency

> The current status of the theory of and the experimental evidence for color transparency are reviewed. The problems with interpretation of quasielastic scattering on nuclei are discussed to some detail.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the theoretical status and experimental evidence for color transparency, noting that while the phenomenon is predicted by QCD, its interpretation in quasielastic scattering on nuclei remains problematic and not yet definitively established.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework of color transparency, which predicts that hadrons produced in hard exclusive processes at high momentum transfer can temporarily have a reduced interaction cross section with nuclear matter. They analyze experimental data from quasielastic scattering experiments on nuclear targets, discussing the difficulties in extracting a clear signal due to competing nuclear effects and the need for careful treatment of final-state interactions.

## Key numbers

- The paper reviews experimental searches for color transparency in quasielastic (e,e'p) reactions, but no specific numerical results from those experiments are given in the truncated text.
- No explicit cross sections, momentum transfers, or precision values are provided in the available text.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical discussion of color transparency that emerged from QCD-based predictions in the 1980s and early 1990s, particularly following the work of Mueller (1982) and Brodsky (1982). The paper critically examines the experimental evidence from quasielastic electron-nucleus scattering, which had been interpreted by some groups as supporting color transparency, and highlights unresolved issues that need to be addressed by future experiments with higher momentum transfers and better control of nuclear effects.
