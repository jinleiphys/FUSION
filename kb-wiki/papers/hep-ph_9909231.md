# Neutrinos From the Next Galactic Supernova

> The next core-collapse supernova in our Galaxy will be a spectacular event, with some $10^4$ neutrino detections in total expected among several detectors. This data will allow unprecedented tests of neutrino properties and new opportunities in astrophysics. In this paper, I focus on two main topics: (1) Measurement of the $ν_μ$ and $ν_τ$ masses by time-of-flight, with an emphasis on introducing as little supernova model dependence as possible, and (2) Methods for locating a supernova by its neutrinos in advance of the light, which may allow improved astronomical observations. In the latter, I also discuss the recent result that the positrons from $\barν_e + p \to e^+ + n$ are not isotropically emitted, as commonly thought.

# Neutrinos From the Next Galactic Supernova

## Key claim

The next Galactic core-collapse supernova will allow a direct measurement of the ν_μ and ν_τ masses down to about 30 eV using time-of-flight differences between neutral-current and charged-current events, but this sensitivity depends on the assumption that the luminosities of different neutrino flavors have approximately equal shapes as a function of time, and the mass limit scales with the fourth root of the number of events.

## Method

The author proposes measuring the ν_τ mass by comparing the average arrival time ⟨t⟩ of neutral-current events (dominated by ν_μ, ν_τ, and their antiparticles) against the average arrival time of charged-current events (ν_e and ν̄_e) in a supernova neutrino signal. The analysis uses Monte Carlo simulations to generate statistical instances of event rates for Super-Kamiokande (SK) and the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO), testing whether the difference ⟨t⟩_S - ⟨t⟩_R is significantly greater than zero. The method avoids relying on detailed supernova luminosity shapes by using the data itself to calibrate the reference signal, and the characteristic energy E_c is determined from the peak of the product f(E)σ(E) for the relevant detection reactions.

## Key numbers

- SNO is sensitive to a ν_τ mass down to about 30 eV if the SK Reference R(t) is used, and down to about 35 eV if the SNO Reference R(t) is used
- The characteristic delay formula is ⟨t⟩_S - ⟨t⟩_R ≃ frac(m > 0) × 0.515 (m/E_c)² D, where D is in 10 kpc, m in eV, and E_c in MeV
- For SNO, the characteristic energy E_c ≃ 32 MeV
- The ν_μ and ν_τ neutrinos have a temperature of about 8 MeV, the ν̄_e about 5 MeV, and the ν_e about 3.5 MeV; average energies are about 25 MeV, 16 MeV, and 11 MeV respectively
- Expected events in SK (32 kton H₂O) at 10 kpc: 8300 from ν̄_e + p → e⁺ + n; 355 each from ν_μ + ¹⁶O and ν_τ + ¹⁶O neutral-current reactions; 200 from ν_e + e⁻ scattering; 60 each from ν_μ + e⁻ and ν_τ + e⁻ scattering
- Expected events in SNO (1 kton D₂O) at 10 kpc: 485 neutral-current events on deuterons; 160 from ν_e + d and ν̄_e + d charged-current reactions; 365 ν̄_e + p → e⁺ + n events in the 1.4 kton H₂O portion
- For supernova location via neutrino-electron scattering in SK, the cone center could be defined to within about 1.5° (N_S ≃ 320 events), but background corrections make the centroiding error larger by a factor of about 4
- For ν̄_e + p → e⁺ + n in SK (10⁴ events), the forward-backward asymmetry gives δ(cosθ) ≃ 0.2, providing reasonable pointing; for SNO (400 events), δ(cosθ) ≃ 1.0, too large to be useful
- The mass limit formula is m_limit ∼ T^{3/4} τ^{1/2} ε_n^{-1/4}, independent of distance D for any Galactic supernova

## Context

This work extends earlier time-of-flight mass measurement concepts (Totani, 1998) and previous supernova neutrino studies (SKpaper, SNOpaper) by introducing a model-independent ⟨t⟩ analysis that avoids relying on detailed supernova luminosity shapes. The paper also addresses supernova location techniques, building on the work of the international supernova early alert network (Habig, 1998; nu98 workshop) and the SNpointing study. The key advance is showing that the ν_τ mass sensitivity depends only on the pulse duration and not on the detailed luminosity shape, and that the mass limit scales with the fourth root of the number of events. The results enable future detectors (LAND, OMNIS) to potentially reach sensitivities of order 10 eV if the supernova pulse duration is as short as τ ≃ 0.5 s.
