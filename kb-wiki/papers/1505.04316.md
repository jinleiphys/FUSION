# Heavy flavor suppression predictions at 5.1 TeV Pb+Pb collisions at LHC

> High momentum hadron suppression is considered to be an excellent probe of jet-medium interactions in QCD matter created in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. We previously showed that our dynamical energy loss formalism can accurately explain suppression measurements at 200 GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC and 2.76 TeV Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC. With the upcoming LHC measurements at notably higher collision energies, there is a question of what differences, with respect to the current (2.76 TeV) measurements, can be expected. In this paper we concentrate on heavy flavor suppression at the upcoming 5.1 TeV Pb+Pb collisions energy at the LHC. Naively, one would expect a notably ($\sim 30\%$) larger suppression at 5.1 TeV collision energy, due to estimated (significant) energy loss increase when transitioning from 2.76 to 5.1 TeV. Surprisingly, more detailed calculations predict nearly the same suppression results at these two energies. We show that this unexpected result is due to an interplay of the following two effects, which essentially cancel each other: i) flattening of the initial distributions with increasing collision energies, and ii) significantly slower than naively expected increase in the energy loss. Therefore, the obtained nearly the same suppression provides a clear (qualitative and quantitative) test of our energy loss formalism.

## Key claim

Contrary to the naive expectation of a ~30% larger suppression at 5.1 TeV due to increased energy loss, detailed calculations predict nearly the same heavy flavor suppression at 5.1 TeV and 2.76 TeV Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC, because the flattening of initial parton distributions and a slower-than-expected increase in energy loss essentially cancel each other.

## Method

The authors used their dynamical energy loss formalism, which includes both radiative and collisional energy losses in a finite-size QCD medium with dynamical scattering centers, finite magnetic mass effects, and running coupling. They computed quenched heavy flavor spectra via a pQCD convolution of initial heavy quark spectra (computed at next-to-leading order), an energy loss probability (including multi-gluon and path-length fluctuations), fragmentation functions (BCFY for D mesons, KLP for B mesons), and decays to experimentally measured final states. The medium was modeled with a constant average temperature, determined from gluon rapidity density scaling, with 313 MeV for 2.76 TeV and 335 MeV for 5.1 TeV 0-10% central collisions.

## Key numbers

- Average QGP temperature for 0-10% central collisions: 313 MeV at 2.76 TeV, 335 MeV at 5.1 TeV (a ~7% increase)
- Gluon rapidity density expected to be 25% higher at 5.1 TeV than at 2.76 TeV
- Debye mass: ~0.9 GeV for 2.76 TeV, ~0.97 GeV for 5.1 TeV
- Charm quark mass: 1.2 GeV; bottom quark mass: 4.75 GeV
- Relative energy loss increase between 2.76 and 5.1 TeV: 5-10% for radiative energy loss (far from the common T^3 expectation), ~8.5% constant with momentum for collisional energy loss
- Effect on suppression from distribution flattening: ~5%; effect from energy loss increase: 5-10% (charm) and ~10% (bottom)
- Power factor decrease in initial distributions between energies: δ ≈ 0.4

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous successful application of their dynamical energy loss formalism to 200 GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC and 2.76 TeV Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC (Djordjevic 2014). It provides predictions for the upcoming 5.1 TeV LHC measurements, enabling a direct test of the formalism. The result contradicts simple estimates based on the common T^3 scaling assumption for radiative energy loss (Betz and Gyulassy 2014) and demonstrates that the temperature dependence of energy loss in a finite-size dynamical medium is much weaker, close to linear rather than cubic.
