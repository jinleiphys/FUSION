# The effect upon neutrinos of core-collapse supernova accretion phase turbulence

> During the accretion phase of a core-collapse supernovae, large amplitude turbulence is generated by the combination of the standing accretion shock instability and convection driven by neutrino heating. The turbulence directly affects the dynamics of the explosion, but there is also the possibility of an additional, indirect, feedback mechanism due to the effect turbulence can have upon neutrino flavor evolution and thus the neutrino heating. In this paper we consider the effect of turbulence during the accretion phase upon neutrino evolution, both numerically and analytically. Adopting representative supernova profiles taken from the accretion phase of a supernova simulation, we find the numerical calculations exhibit no effect from turbulence. We explain this absence using two analytic descriptions: the Stimulated Transition model and the Distorted Phase Effect model. In the Stimulated Transition model turbulence effects depend upon six different lengthscales, and three criteria must be satisfied between them if one is to observe a change in the flavor evolution due to Stimulated Transition. We further demonstrate that the Distorted Phase Effect depends upon the presence of multiple semi-adiabatic MSW resonances or discontinuities that also can be expressed as a relationship between three of the same lengthscales. When we examine the supernova profiles used in the numerical calculations we find the three Stimulated Transition criteria cannot be satisfied, independent of the form of the turbulence power spectrum, and that the same supernova profiles lack the multiple semi-adiabatic MSW resonances or discontinuities necessary to produce a Distorted Phase Effect. Thus we conclude that even though large amplitude turbulence is present in supernova during the accretion phase, it has no effect upon neutrino flavor evolution.

## Key claim

The authors find that, despite the presence of large-amplitude turbulence during the accretion phase of core-collapse supernovae, the turbulence has no effect on neutrino flavor evolution for the representative supernova profiles they examine; this null result is explained by the failure of three specific criteria required for the Stimulated Transition model and the absence of multiple semi-adiabatic MSW resonances or discontinuities needed for the Distorted Phase Effect.

## Method

The authors adopted one-dimensional mass density and electron fraction profiles from the Basel group simulation of a 10.8 solar mass progenitor at post-bounce times of 0.3 s, 0.5 s, and 0.7 s, and inserted turbulence as a Gaussian random field with a Kolmogorov power spectrum (spectral index alpha = 5/3) and amplitude C_star = 0.1, using an envelope function derived from the anisotropic velocity in three-dimensional Couch and O'Connor simulations. They numerically integrated the Schrodinger equation for a 10 MeV neutrino with standard mixing parameters to compute transition probabilities in the instantaneous eigenstate basis, and then applied two analytic frameworks, the Stimulated Transition model and the Distorted Phase Effect model, to explain why no significant flavor conversion occurred.

## Key numbers

- The MSW resonance densities for a 10 MeV neutrino are rho_MSW,12 ≈ 38 g/cm³ and rho_MSW,23 ≈ 3000 g/cm³, both well below the densities behind the shock during the accretion phase.
- The oscillation wavelengths near the neutrinosphere can be as small as 10 micrometers, many orders of magnitude smaller than the resolution of modern supernova simulations.
- The turbulence cutoff wavelength lambda_cut is on the order of 500 km to 5000 km, while the dissipation scale lambda_diss is approximately 10⁻⁵ cm, giving a dynamic range ratio of order 10¹³.
- The anisotropic velocity envelope parameters approach asymptotes of Lambda_NS = 16 km and Lambda_S = 7 km for post-bounce times beyond 250 ms.

## Context

This work extends the purely numerical study by Reid, Adams, and Seunarine (2011), which found no effect of accretion-phase turbulence on neutrino flavor evolution but could not fully explore the multi-dimensional parameter space. The authors apply analytic tools developed by Kneller and Kabadi (2015) and Patton, Kneller, and McLaughlin (2013, 2014) to explain the null result and to determine whether any combination of parameters could produce flavor conversion; they conclude that the three Stimulated Transition criteria cannot be satisfied for these profiles independent of the turbulence power spectrum, and that the profiles lack the multiple semi-adiabatic MSW resonances or discontinuities required for the Distorted Phase Effect.
