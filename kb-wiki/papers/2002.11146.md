# Quantum Algorithms for Simulating the Lattice Schwinger Model

> The Schwinger model (quantum electrodynamics in 1+1 dimensions) is a testbed for the study of quantum gauge field theories. We give scalable, explicit digital quantum algorithms to simulate the lattice Schwinger model in both NISQ and fault-tolerant settings. In particular, we perform a tight analysis of low-order Trotter formula simulations of the Schwinger model, using recently derived commutator bounds, and give upper bounds on the resources needed for simulations in both scenarios. In lattice units, we find a Schwinger model on $N/2$ physical sites with coupling constant $x^{-1/2}$ and electric field cutoff $x^{-1/2}Λ$ can be simulated on a quantum computer for time $2xT$ using a number of $T$-gates or CNOTs in $\widetilde{O}( N^{3/2} T^{3/2} \sqrt{x} Λ)$ for fixed operator error. This scaling with the truncation $Λ$ is better than that expected from algorithms such as qubitization or QDRIFT. Furthermore, we give scalable measurement schemes and algorithms to estimate observables which we cost in both the NISQ and fault-tolerant settings by assuming a simple target observable---the mean pair density. Finally, we bound the root-mean-square error in estimating this observable via simulation as a function of the diamond distance between the ideal and actual CNOT channels. This work provides a rigorous analysis of simulating the Schwinger model, while also providing benchmarks against which subsequent simulation algorithms can be tested.

## Key claim

The paper provides explicit digital quantum algorithms for simulating the lattice Schwinger model with rigorous resource bounds, claiming that the T-gate or CNOT count scales as \(\widetilde{O}(N^{3/2} T^{3/2} \sqrt{x} \Lambda)\) for fixed operator error, which is better than the scaling expected from qubitization or QDRIFT; however, this result holds under the condition that the simulated states reside far from the electric field cutoff and that the errors are evenly distributed among circuit synthesis and Trotter approximation.

## Method

The authors decompose the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian for the Schwinger model in 1+1 dimensions into a sum of simulatable terms: kinetic hopping terms \(T_r\) and diagonal terms \(D_r^{(1)}\) (mass term) and \(D_r^{(2)}\) (electric field squared). They implement the time evolution using a second-order Trotter-Suzuki formula (Strang splitting), with each kinetic term further split into four non-commuting parts implemented via circuits involving incrementers, CNOTs, and single-qubit rotations. The diagonal terms are implemented using arithmetic circuits for squaring the electric field (for \(D_r^{(2)}\)) and single-qubit Z rotations (for \(D_r^{(1)}\)), with error bounds derived from commutator calculations and circuit synthesis precision.

## Key numbers

* For a Schwinger model on \(N/2\) physical sites with coupling constant \(x^{-1/2}\) and electric field cutoff \(x^{-1/2}\Lambda\), simulated for time \(2xT\), the T-gate or CNOT count scales as \(\widetilde{O}(N^{3/2} T^{3/2} \sqrt{x} \Lambda)\) for fixed operator error.
* The Trotter error bound for a single time step \(t\) is \(\delta \leq N t^3 \bigl( (24|\mu| + 14) K^2 + (8|\mu|+40)K + 24\mu^2 + 66|\mu| + 57 \bigr)\), where \(K\) is the gauge field cutoff, \(\mu\) is the fermion mass, and \(N\) is the number of lattice sites.
* Implementing a kinetic term \(T_r\) to spectral-norm error \(\delta\) requires on average at most \(8(\lceil\log_2 K_r\rceil - 1) + 9.2 \log_2(16/\delta)\) T-gates, where \(K_r\) is the cutoff at site \(r\).
* Implementing the electric field squared term \(D_r^{(2)}\) for all \(N\) sites to error \(\delta\) requires on average at most \(N[2(\eta-1)(15\eta - 3\lfloor\log(\eta+1)\rfloor - 12) + 2]\) Toffoli gates and \(3.45 N \eta \log(N\eta/\delta)\) T-gates, where \(\eta = \lceil \log_2(2K) \rceil\).
* The number of time steps \(s\) required to achieve spectral-norm error \(\delta_T\) in the Trotter approximation satisfies \(s \propto T^{3/2} / \sqrt{\delta_T}\) from the second-order formula.

## Context

This work extends the line of quantum simulation algorithms for lattice gauge theories, specifically building on the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian formulation (Kogut and Susskind, 1975) and the Jordan-Wigner transformation for fermions. It provides rigorous resource estimates that improve upon naive scaling from generic methods like qubitization or QDRIFT, and it offers explicit circuit constructions for both NISQ and fault-tolerant architectures. The results enable benchmarking of future simulation algorithms for the Schwinger model and provide a template for scaling analysis of more complex gauge theories such as lattice QCD.
