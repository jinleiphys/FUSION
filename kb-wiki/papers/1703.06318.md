# Can one determine the neutrino mass by electron capture?

> There are three different methods used to search the neutrino mass: - The electron antineutrino mass can probably best be determined by the Triton decay. - The neutrinoless Double Beta Decay yields information, if the neutrino is a Dirac or a Majorana particle. It can also determine the Majorana neutrino mass. - Electron capture of an atomic bound electron by a proton in a nucleus bound electron plus proton to neutron plus electron-neutrino can give the mass of the electron neutrino. This contribution summarizes our theoretical work on the possibility to determine the electron neutrino mass by electron capture. One expects the largest influence of the neutrino mass on this decay for a small Q = 2.8 keV for electron capture in Holmium. The energy of the Q value is distributed to the emitted neutrino and the excitation of the Dy atom. Thus the energy difference between the Q value and the upper end of the deexcitation spectrum is the electron neutrino mass. The excitation spectrum of Dy is calculate by one-, two- and three-electron hole excitations, and by the shake-off process. The electron wave functions are calculated selfconsistently by the Dirac-Hartree-Fock approach for the bound and the continuum states. To extract the neutrino mass from the spectrum one must adjust simultaneously the neutrino mass, the Q value, the position, the relative strength and the width of the highest resonance. This fit is only possible, if the background is reduced relative to the present situation. In case of a drastically reduced background a fit of the Q-value and the neutrino mass only seems also to be possible. The analysis presented here shows, that the determination of the electron neutrino mass by electron capture is difficult, but seems not to be impossible.

## Key claim

The determination of the electron neutrino mass by electron capture in Holmium-163 is difficult but not impossible, provided the experimental background is drastically reduced relative to the present situation; the analysis requires simultaneously fitting the neutrino mass, the Q value, and the position, relative strength, and width of the highest resonance.

## Method

The excitation spectrum of Dysprosium-163 after electron capture from Holmium-163 was calculated using the sudden approximation with one-, two-, and three-electron hole excitations (shake-up) and shake-off into the continuum. Electron wave functions for both bound and continuum states were computed self-consistently using the relativistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock approach, with continuum wave functions orthogonalized to all bound orbitals. The theoretical bolometer spectrum was constructed by summing Lorentzian-shaped resonances for one-hole, two-hole, and shake-off contributions, normalized to the experimental N1 (4s1/2) peak from the ECHo collaboration.

## Key numbers

- Q value for electron capture in Holmium-163: Q = 2.833 (30_stat) (15_sys) keV, from Penning trap measurement by Eliseev et al.
- Binding energy of the 3s1/2 (M1) electron in Holmium-163: 2.128 keV; only electrons with binding energy less than Q = 2.8 keV can be captured.
- Dirac-Hartree-Fock overlap between Ho and Dy for the 3s orbital: <3s, Ho|3s, Dy> = 0.99940; for the 4s orbital: <4s, Ho|4s, Dy> = 0.99909.
- Upper limit on shake-off probability relative to one-hole states from Vatai approximation: 0.4% (using DHF overlaps); in contrast, perturbation theory with 10% overlap error yields an overestimate of 34%.
- Two-hole binding energy for 3s1/2, 4s1/2 in Dysprosium-163: 2.4742 keV with width 0.0264 keV; for 4s1/2, 4s1/2: 0.8414 keV with width 0.0108 keV.
- Shake-off integration for capture from 3s1/2 with second hole in 4s1/2 restricted to continuum electron energies from zero to 0.236 keV (Q minus two-hole binding energy).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical treatment of electron capture in Holmium-163 by the Tuebingen group (Faessler et al., 2014-2017), improving upon earlier calculations by Inteman and Polok (non-relativistic) and DeRujula and Lusignoli (2013) who used first-order perturbation theory with non-relativistic screened Coulomb wave functions. The key improvement is the use of self-consistent relativistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock wave functions, which yield overlaps between Ho and Dy close to 0.999 and avoid the factor-of-85 overestimate of shake-off probability that arises from perturbation theory. The work enables a more reliable extraction of the electron neutrino mass from the ECHo collaboration bolometer data, provided background can be sufficiently reduced.
