# Path-integral approaches to strongly-coupled quantum many-body systems

> The core of this thesis is the path-integral formulation of quantum field theory and its ability to describe strongly-coupled quantum many-body systems of finite size. Collective behaviors can be efficiently described in such systems through the implementation of spontaneous symmetry breaking (SSB) in mean-field approaches. However, as the thermodynamic limit does not make sense in finite-size systems, the latter can not exhibit any SSB and the symmetries which are broken down at the mean-field level must therefore be restored. The efficiency of theoretical approaches in the treatment of finite-size quantum systems can therefore be studied via their ability to restore spontaneously broken symmetries. In this thesis, a zero-dimensional $O(N)$ model is taken as a theoretical laboratory to perform such an investigation with many state-of-the-art path-integral techniques: perturbation theory combined with various resummation methods (Padé-Borel, Borel-hypergeometric, conformal mapping), enhanced versions of perturbation theory (transseries derived via Lefschetz thimbles, optimized perturbation theory), self-consistent perturbation theory based on effective actions (auxiliary field loop expansion (LOAF), Cornwall-Jackiw-Tomboulis (CJT) formalism, 4PPI effective action, ...), functional renormalization group (FRG) techniques (FRG based on the Wetterich equation, DFT-FRG, 2PI-FRG). Connections between these different techniques are also emphasized. In addition, the path-integral formalism provides us with the possibility to introduce collective degrees of freedom in an exact fashion via Hubbard-Stratonovich transformations: the effect of such transformations on the aforementioned methods is also examined in detail.

## Key claim

This thesis uses a zero-dimensional O(N) model as a theoretical laboratory to systematically compare the ability of many state-of-the-art path-integral techniques to restore spontaneously broken symmetries in finite-size quantum systems, where the thermodynamic limit does not apply and spontaneous symmetry breaking is therefore forbidden.

## Method

The author performs a comparative study of path-integral approaches applied to a (0+0)-D O(N)-symmetric φ⁴ theory, which serves as a toy model for strongly-coupled finite-size quantum many-body systems. The methods examined include perturbation theory with various resummation schemes (Padé-Borel, Borel-hypergeometric, conformal mapping), transseries derived via Lefschetz thimbles, optimized perturbation theory, self-consistent perturbation theories based on effective actions (auxiliary field loop expansion, Cornwall-Jackiw-Tomboulis formalism, 4PPI effective action), and functional renormalization group techniques (Wetterich equation, DFT-FRG, 2PI-FRG). The effect of Hubbard-Stratonovich transformations on these methods is also examined.

## Key numbers

- Bulk correlations captured at the single-reference EDF level constitute at least 98% of the binding energy for ²⁴⁰Pu (the black curve at |q|=0).
- Spontaneous rotational symmetry breaking in the reference state gains around 14 MeV of correlation energy, representing approximately 2% of the system's binding energy for ²⁴⁰Pu.
- A full projected generator coordinate method calculation yields a binding energy about 0.5 MeV below the global minimum of the projected HFB curve for ²⁴⁰Pu.
- Bulk correlation energy scales as approximately 8 A MeV, varying with mass number A.
- Collective static correlations contribute up to 25 MeV, varying with the number of valence nucleons and valence shell degeneracy.
- Collective dynamical correlations contribute up to 5 MeV, varying with the number of valence nucleons and valence shell degeneracy.

## Context

This work extends the program of Furnstahl and collaborators (2000-2020) to formulate nuclear energy density functionals within the path-integral language as effective field theories, and connects to the earlier demonstration by Valiev and Fernando (1997) that the 2-particle-point-irreducible effective action is suited to formulate a Kohn-Sham density functional theory. The comparative study of path-integral techniques on the O(N) model is motivated by the need to construct systematically improvable nuclear EDFs that overcome the limitations of traditional phenomenological parametrizations such as Skyrme (Vautherin, 1972), Gogny (1975), and covariant functionals (Serot, 1984; Gambhir, 1990), and to provide a rigorous foundation for the multi-reference EDF methods currently used in nuclear structure calculations.
