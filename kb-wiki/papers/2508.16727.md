# A Journey of Seeking Pressure and Forces in the Nucleon

> Momentum current density (MCD) $T^{ij}$ is a general physics concept describing the momentum conservation through momentum flow generated from both the kinetic motion of particles and the interacting forces among them. It has been suggested by M. Polyakov et al. that the MCD in the nucleon, characterized by the form factor $C/D$ of the QCD energy-momentum tensor, can be interpreted as the pressure and shear forces between adjacent parts of the system because the nucleon interior approximates a continuous medium. While intuitively appealing, we find that the interpretation is hard to justify from a detailed examination of the physical mechanisms for the momentum flow in QCD. After reviewing through a broad range of classical and quantum systems, we find that while thermal and/or quantum average of isotropic motion contributes to kinetic MCD a pressure term proportional to $δ^{ij}$, when there is an anisotropic motion, the pressure cannot simply be identified from the MCD tensor. Furthermore, kinetic pressure cannot be considered as the surface force between adjacent parts of a system. More importantly, at the scale of the nucleon dimension, the color forces among quarks and gluons is by no means short-ranged as in a continuous medium, and the resulting interaction MCD cannot be interpreted as normal or shear ``stress'' force, although an isotropic term from the QCD trace anomaly may be interpreted as a ``vacuum pressure.'' Following our previous study of force densities through divergences of kinetic MCDs, we affirm that the vacuum pressure term provides a confining potential on the quarks through color Lorentz forces.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the widely-adopted interpretation of the momentum current density (MCD) in the nucleon, characterized by the form factor \(C/D\) of the QCD energy-momentum tensor, as mechanical pressure and shear forces between adjacent parts of the system is hard to justify, because the color forces among quarks and gluons at the nucleon scale are not short-ranged as in a continuous medium, and because anisotropic kinetic motion prevents a simple identification of pressure from the MCD tensor.

## Method

The authors review the physics of momentum current densities across a broad range of classical and quantum systems, including ideal gases, liquids, solids, electromagnetic fields, single-particle quantum systems, and many-body quantum systems. They then analyze the separate contributions to the symmetric and gauge-invariant QCD MCD in the nucleon, quark kinetic MCD, gluon MCD (including radiative and static interaction parts), and the QCD trace anomaly, and examine whether any part can be interpreted as mechanical pressure or surface shear forces. They also reconsider force densities obtained from divergences of kinetic MCDs, following their previous work.

## Key numbers

* The von-Laue condition for any isolated static system is \(\int T^{ii}(r) d^3 \vec{r} = 0\), meaning the 3-trace of the total MCD must be equally positive and negative throughout space.
* For an ideal gas in local equilibrium, the statistically averaged kinetic MCD is \(\langle T^{ij}_{K} \rangle (\vec{r}) = \rho(\vec{r}) v^i(\vec{r}) v^j(\vec{r}) + p_K(\vec{r})\delta^{ij}\), where the kinetic pressure is \(p_K(\vec{r}) = \frac{2}{3} n(\vec{r}) \varepsilon_K (\vec{r})\).
* The interaction MCD for a gas with two-body potential \(V\) satisfies \(\nabla_{i}T_{I}^{ij} = -\mathcal{F}^{j}(\vec{r}) = \sum_{a}[\sum_{b\neq a}\nabla_{a}^{j}V(|\vec{r}_{a}-\vec{r}_{b}|)]\delta^{(3)}(\vec{r}-\vec{r}_{a})\).

## Context

This work critically examines the "mechanical interpretation" of the nucleon's gravitational form factors proposed by Polyakov (2002) and Polyakov (2002), which has been widely applied in hadron mass decompositions and stability conditions. The authors extend their previous study of force densities through divergences of kinetic MCDs (Ji 2025) and contrast their findings with the continuous-medium analogy used in the literature. The paper enables a re-evaluation of whether the \(D\)-term form factor can be interpreted as a mechanical stability condition, especially given recent reports of positive \(D\)-terms for the hydrogen atom (Ji 2022, Czarnecki 2023) and spin-\(3/2\) particles such as the \(\Delta\) resonance and \(\Omega^-\) (Fu 2022, Fu 2023, Wang 2023).
