# Robustness of anomaly-related magnetoresistance in doped Weyl semimetals

> Weyl semimetal with Weyl fermions at Fermi energy is one of the topological materials, and is a condensed-matter realization of the relativistic fermions. However, there are several crucial differences such as the shift of Fermi energy, which can hinder the expected interesting physics. Chiral anomaly is a representative nontrivial phenomenon associated with Weyl fermions, which dictates the transfer of fermions between the Weyl fermions with opposite chirality; it is manifested as the negative magnetoresistance. Here we demonstrate that the magnetoresistance is robust against the deviation from the ideal Weyl Hamiltonian such as the shifted Fermi energy and nonlinear dispersions. We study a model with the energy dispersion containing two Weyl nodes, and find that the magnetoresistance persists even when the Fermi level is far away from the node, even above the saddle point that separates the two nodes. Surprisingly, the magnetoresistance remains even after the pair annihilation of the nodes.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the negative longitudinal magnetoresistance (LMR) associated with the chiral anomaly in Weyl semimetals persists even when the Fermi level is far above the Weyl nodes, above the saddle point that separates the two nodes, and even after the Weyl nodes have annihilated via pair annihilation, contrary to the expectation that only the ideal Weyl Hamiltonian near the nodes would produce this effect.

## Method

The authors reformulate semiclassical Boltzmann transport theory with Berry phase corrections to derive a general formula for the anomaly-related current at order O(B²E) in the weak-field limit (ω_cτ ≪ 1). They apply this formula to a two-node model Hamiltonian with dispersion σ₁k¹ + σ₂k² + σ₃((k³)² - m²) - μσ₀, analyzing the chemical potential dependence of the magnetoresistance both below and above the saddle point. For the strong-field limit (ω_cτ ≫ 1), they study the Landau level structure of the same model using Boltzmann theory for Landau levels, and also examine a gapped variant of the model where the two Weyl nodes have annihilated.

## Key numbers

- In the μ → 0 limit (near the Weyl nodes), the anomaly-related current diverges as ∝ 1/μ², with the coefficient for the (B·E)B term being 14πm/(15μ²) for the in-plane components
- In the μ ≫ m² limit (far above the saddle point), the current decays as ∝ μ^{-3/2} for in-plane components and ∝ μ^{-1/2} for the z-component, slower than the μ^{-2} decay near the nodes
- At the critical point m² = 0 (pair annihilation point), the current diverges as ∝ μ^{-3/2} when μ → 0, a different power law from the Weyl case (μ^{-2})
- For the gapped model with m² > 0, the current near the band edge (δ ≡ μ - m² ≥ 0) increases as δ^{3/2} for in-plane currents and as δ^{5/2} for the z-axis current
- In the strong-field limit, when μ² < 2qB_z, only the chiral mode contributes, giving j_z = (q³/(2π²))τ E_z B_z

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical transport theory of Son (2013) for chiral anomaly in Weyl semimetals to realistic situations where the Fermi energy is shifted away from the Weyl nodes, addressing a fundamental controversy in experiments on materials like Cd₃As₂ where the Fermi level lies above the saddle point (Li 2016, Nishihaya 2018). The results show that the negative LMR observed in such materials can indeed be attributed to the chiral anomaly rather than other mechanisms, and that the anomaly-related current remains a robust signature even when the system is far from the ideal Weyl Hamiltonian limit. The work enables interpretation of magnetotransport experiments in doped Weyl semimetals and provides a theoretical foundation for detecting proximity to a Weyl semimetal phase even when the Fermi level is far from the nodes.
