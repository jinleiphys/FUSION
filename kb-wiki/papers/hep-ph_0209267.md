# What Can Be Learned with a Lead-Based Supernova-Neutrino Detector?

> We examine the prospects for using lead as a supernova-neutrino detector by considering the spectrum of electrons produced, and the number of one- and two-neutron events. We show that the electron energy spectrum from charged-current reactions can be used to extract information about the high-temperature component of the neutrino spectrum. Some degree of electron neutrino oscillation is expected in the supernova envelope. We examine the prospects for untangling the signatures of various oscillation scenarios, including, e.g. normal or inverted hierarchies, and different values for the small mixing angle, theta_13.

## Key claim

The electron energy spectrum from charged-current reactions on lead can be used to extract information about the high-temperature component of the supernova neutrino spectrum, but the method is primarily sensitive to temperature rather than chemical potential, and the ability to distinguish oscillation scenarios depends on combining charged-current and neutral-current event rates.

## Method

The authors calculated neutrino-nucleus cross sections for charged-current reactions (ν_e + 208Pb → 207Bi + n + e^- and ν_e + 208Pb → 206Bi + 2n + e^-) and neutral-current reactions (ν_x + 208Pb → 207Pb + n and ν_x + 208Pb → 206Pb + 2n) using the coordinate-space Skyrme-Hartree-Fock method followed by random phase approximation (RPA) with two different Skyrme forces (SIII and SkO+). They folded these cross sections with Fermi-Dirac neutrino flux distributions parameterized by temperature T and effective chemical potential η, assuming equipartition of energy among the six neutrino and antineutrino species, to predict event rates and electron energy spectra for a lead perchlorate detector.

## Key numbers

* For a supernova at 10 kpc with luminosity 3 × 10^53 ergs, per kilotonne of lead perchlorate: about 378 electrons produced with one neutron and about 234 with two neutrons in the charged-current channel, assuming oscillations induced only by the large solar mixing angle and high-energy neutrinos with T = 8.0 MeV, η = 0.
* In the neutral-current channel for the same scenario: about 105 one-neutron events and about 72 two-neutron events.
* For complete transformation at the δm²₁₃ resonance: about 460 one-neutron events with average electron energy 22.9 MeV, and about 310 two-neutron events with average electron energy 26.2 MeV.
* For oscillations due to the δm²₁₂ resonance (sin² 2θ_solar ≈ 0.75): about 370 one-neutron events with average energy 22.1 MeV, and two-neutron events reduced by almost exactly 25% with little spectral shape change.
* Without oscillations (T = 3 MeV, η_eff = 3): about 92 one-neutron events and about 5 two-neutron events.
* The two different RPA calculations (SIII and SkO+ forces) differ by only 8% for one-neutron cross sections and 4% for two-neutron cross sections at T = 8, η = 0.
* With ±10% uncertainty in average electron energy, the neutrino temperature can be determined to within about 1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous proposals for lead-based supernova neutrino detectors such as OMNIS (Omnis, year not specified) and LAND (Land, year not specified), and the specific lead perchlorate detector concept from Elliott (year not specified). The calculations build on earlier cross-section work by FHM (Fhm, year not specified), KL (Kl, year not specified), and Volpe (year not specified), with the authors noting their results are close to those of KL and Volpe but considerably smaller than those of FHM. The paper enables future supernova neutrino detectors to potentially determine the temperature of the high-energy neutrino component and distinguish between normal and inverted mass hierarchies, as well as different values of θ₁₃, by combining charged-current electron energy measurements with comparisons of charged-current and neutral-current event rates.
