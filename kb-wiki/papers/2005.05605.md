# Systematic study on the role of various higher-order processes in the breakup of weakly-bound projectiles

> The virtual photon theory (VPT), which is based on first-order Coulomb dissociation restricted to the electric dipole ($E1$), has been successfully used to explain the breakup data for several cases. Our aim is to study the role of various higher-order processes that are ignored in the VPT, such as the nuclear breakup, interference between nuclear and Coulomb amplitudes, and multistep breakup processes mainly due to strong continuum-continuum couplings in the breakup of two-body projectiles on a heavy target at both intermediate and higher incident energies. For the purpose of numerical calculations, we employed eikonal version of three-body continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) reaction model. Our results for the breakup of $^{11}$Be and $^{17}$F on $^{208}$Pb target at 100, 250, and 520 MeV/A, show the importance of nuclear breakup contribution, and its significant role in the multistep processes. The multistep effect on Coulomb breakup for core-neutron projectile was found to be negligible, whereas it was important for core-proton projectile. Coulomb-nuclear interference (CNI) effect was also found to be non-negligible. Quantitatively, the multistep effects due to the nuclear breakup was found to depend on the incident energy through the energy dependence of the core-target and nucleon-target nuclear potentials. The nuclear breakup component, the CNI effect, and the multistep breakup processes are all found to be non-negligible; hence, the assumptions adopted in the VPT for the accurate description of breakup cross sections are not valid.

## Key claim
The virtual photon theory (VPT), which assumes first-order Coulomb dissociation restricted to electric dipole (E1) transitions, fails to accurately describe breakup cross sections even when it appears to agree with full calculations near the peak region, because nuclear breakup, Coulomb-nuclear interference, and multistep breakup processes are all non-negligible at intermediate and higher incident energies.

## Method
The authors employed the eikonal version of the three-body continuum-discretized coupled-channels (E-CDCC) reaction model to calculate breakup cross sections for 11Be (core-neutron) and 17F (core-proton) projectiles on a 208Pb target. They compared full CDCC calculations with calculations mimicking VPT (first-order Coulomb E1 only), one-step CDCC (first-order perturbative without multistep processes), pure nuclear breakup, and incoherent sums of Coulomb and nuclear breakup to isolate the contributions of nuclear breakup, Coulomb-nuclear interference, and multistep effects.

## Key numbers
- 11Be on 208Pb at 100 MeV/A: full CDCC integrated breakup cross section = 1442.23 mb; VPT = 1209.58 mb (-16% deviation); 1step CDCC = 2011.09 mb (+39% deviation)
- 11Be on 208Pb at 250 MeV/A: full CDCC = 783.97 mb; VPT = 716.43 mb (-9% deviation); 1step CDCC = 851.87 mb (+9% deviation)
- 11Be on 208Pb at 520 MeV/A: full CDCC = 604.69 mb; VPT = 498.39 mb (-18% deviation); 1step CDCC = 752.32 mb (+24% deviation)
- Multistep effect on nuclear breakup for 11Be: 39% at 100 MeV/A, 9% at 250 MeV/A, 24% at 520 MeV/A
- 17F on 208Pb: multistep effect differences of 37% at 100 MeV/A, 14% at 250 MeV/A, and 12% at 520 MeV/A
- Expectation value of core-valence nucleon relative distance: 6.97 fm for 11Be, 3.59 fm for 17F
- 17F bound excited state energy: 0.105 MeV

## Context
This work extends previous studies of higher-order effects in breakup reactions (Hussein 2006, Thompson 2009, Chatterjee 2002-2007, Tarutina 2004, Mukeru 2015, Dasso 1998, Nunes 1998-1999) that primarily focused on lower incident energies (below 100 MeV/A). The study systematically investigates the role of nuclear breakup, Coulomb-nuclear interference, and multistep processes at intermediate and higher energies (100, 250, and 520 MeV/A) where the VPT has been commonly applied. The findings enable more accurate modeling of breakup reactions for weakly-bound and halo nuclei, and caution experimentalists against relying on angular cuts to suppress nuclear contributions without careful system-dependent investigation.
