# Comparison of equation of state models with different cluster dissolution mechanisms

> The appearance of nuclear clusters in stellar matter at densities below nuclear saturation is an important feature in the modeling of the equation of state for astrophysical applications. There are different theoretical concepts to describe the dissolution of nuclei with increasing density and temperature. In this contribution, the predictions of two approaches are compared: the medium dependent change of the nuclear masses in a generalized relativistic density functional approach and the excluded-volume mechanism in a statistical model. Both approaches use the same description for the interaction between the nucleons. The composition of neutron star matter, in particular the occurrence of light and heavy nuclei, and its thermodynamic properties are studied.

## Key claim

The two models (statistical model with excluded-volume mechanism and generalized relativistic density functional with cluster mass shifts) predict significantly different compositions of neutron star matter at subsaturation densities, even when using the same nucleon-nucleon interaction (DD2 parametrization), because the cluster dissolution mechanisms produce different density and temperature dependencies for the suppression of light and heavy nuclei.

## Method

The authors compared two theoretical approaches for describing nuclear cluster formation and dissolution in stellar matter: the HS statistical model using an excluded-volume mechanism (where finite particle volumes reduce available space for other particles) and the gRDF approach using medium-dependent mass shifts (where cluster binding energies decrease with density due to Pauli blocking). Both models employed the same DD2 relativistic mean-field parametrization for the nucleon-nucleon interaction and included nucleons, light nuclei (deuterons, tritons, helions, alpha particles), heavy nuclei up to the driplines, electrons, muons, and photons, with calculations performed for neutron star matter in beta-equilibrium.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: \(n_{\rm sat} \approx 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Temperature range of interest for cluster formation: \(T \lesssim 15\) MeV
- Temperature above which light nuclei are most relevant: \(T > 2\) MeV, with densities up to approximately \(0.001 n_{\rm sat}\)
- Radius parameter used for nuclei: \(R_0 = 1.25\) fm for Coulomb shift calculations and \(R_i = 1.4 \cdot A^{1/3}\) fm for level density calculations
- Flashing temperature of the DD2-RMF model: \(T_{\rm fl} = 11.26430\) MeV, defined by the condition that pressure attains a minimum of \(p=0\) as a function of baryon density in symmetric nuclear matter
- For heavy nuclei, the dissolution density approaches one third of the saturation density (\(n_{\rm sat}/3\)) with increasing mass number \(A\)

## Context

This work extends the comparison of cluster dissolution mechanisms in nuclear astrophysics, building on the HS statistical model (Hempel, 2009) with excluded-volume effects and the gRDF approach (Typel, 2009) with medium-dependent mass shifts. The study enables improved modeling of the equation of state for core-collapse supernova simulations and neutron star crust properties, where the composition of matter affects neutrino opacities, shock wave development, proto-neutron star cooling, and pulsar glitch interpretations.
