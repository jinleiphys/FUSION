# The impact of individual nuclear properties on $r$-process nucleosynthesis

> The astrophysical rapid neutron capture process or `$r$ process' of nucleosynthesis is believed to be responsible for the production of approximately half the heavy element abundances found in nature. This multifaceted problem remains one of the greatest open challenges in all of physics. Knowledge of nuclear physics properties such as masses, $β$-decay and neutron capture rates, as well as $β$-delayed neutron emission probabilities are critical inputs that go into calculations of $r$-process nucleosynthesis. While properties of nuclei near stability have been established, much still remains unknown regarding neutron-rich nuclei far from stability that may participate in the $r$ process. Sensitivity studies gauge the astrophysical response of a change in nuclear physics input(s) which allows for the isolation of the most important nuclear properties that shape the final abundances observed in nature. This review summarizes the extent of recent sensitivity studies and highlights how these studies play a key role in facilitating new insight into the $r$ process. The development of these tools promotes a focused effort for state-of-the-art measurements, motivates construction of new facilities and will ultimately move the community towards addressing the grand challenge of `How were the elements from iron to uranium made?'.

## Key claim

Sensitivity studies can identify which individual nuclear properties (masses, beta-decay rates, neutron capture rates, and beta-delayed neutron emission probabilities) most strongly influence the final heavy-element abundances produced in r-process nucleosynthesis, but the importance of each nucleus and property depends critically on the astrophysical scenario (e.g., low-entropy hot wind, high-entropy hot wind, cold wind, or neutron star merger) and on the threshold criterion used (here, \(F_{max} \geq 0.1\)).

## Method

The authors compiled and reviewed results from multiple sensitivity studies that varied nuclear physics inputs in r-process network calculations and computed the sensitivity metric \(F_{max}\) for each nucleus and property. They tabulated \(F_{max}\) values for beta-decay rates, neutron capture rates, and nuclear masses across four astrophysical conditions: (a) low entropy hot wind, (b) high entropy hot wind, (c) cold wind, and (d) neutron star merger. Nuclei with \(F_{max} \geq 0.1\) for at least one condition were considered important and listed in a comprehensive table.

## Key numbers

*   Nucleus \(^{126}\)Ru (Z=44, N=82) shows the highest beta-decay sensitivity in the low entropy hot wind scenario, with \(F_{max} = 73.94\).
*   Nucleus \(^{130}\)Pd (Z=46, N=84) shows the highest beta-decay sensitivity in the low entropy hot wind scenario, with \(F_{max} = 96.99\).
*   Nucleus \(^{132}\)Cd (Z=48, N=84) shows the highest beta-decay sensitivity in the high entropy hot wind scenario, with \(F_{max} = 54.52\).
*   Nucleus \(^{128}\)Ru (Z=44, N=84) shows the highest beta-decay sensitivity in the cold wind scenario, with \(F_{max} = 44.89\).
*   Nucleus \(^{126}\)Ru (Z=44, N=82) shows the highest neutron capture sensitivity in the cold wind scenario, with \(F_{max} = 1.25\).
*   Nucleus \(^{134}\)Cd (Z=48, N=86) shows the highest neutron capture sensitivity in the cold wind scenario, with \(F_{max} = 18.99\).
*   Nucleus \(^{130}\)Pd (Z=46, N=84) shows the highest mass sensitivity in the cold wind scenario, with \(F_{max} = 43.73\).
*   Nucleus \(^{132}\)Cd (Z=48, N=84) shows the highest mass sensitivity in the high entropy hot wind scenario, with \(F_{max} = 101.39\).
*   Nucleus \(^{136}\)Sn (Z=50, N=86) shows the highest mass sensitivity in the high entropy hot wind scenario, with \(F_{max} = 83.68\).

## Context

This review synthesizes the results of numerous prior sensitivity studies (e.g., those by Mumpower and collaborators, and other groups cited in the paper) that have mapped the nuclear physics uncertainties affecting r-process abundance predictions. The work extends earlier efforts by systematically comparing the most influential nuclei across four distinct astrophysical environments (hot winds, cold winds, and neutron star mergers). The tabulated nuclei and their \(F_{max}\) values provide a prioritized list for experimental measurements at rare-isotope facilities and motivate the construction of new facilities, ultimately aiming to address the open question of how the heavy elements from iron to uranium are made.
