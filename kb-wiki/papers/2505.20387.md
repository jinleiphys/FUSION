# Observation of hadron scattering in a lattice gauge theory on a quantum computer

> Scattering experiments are at the heart of high-energy physics (HEP), breaking matter down to its fundamental constituents, probing its formation, and providing deep insight into the inner workings of nature. In the current huge drive to forge quantum computers into complementary venues that are ideally suited to capture snapshots of far-from-equilibrium HEP dynamics, a major goal is to utilize these devices for scattering experiments. A major obstacle in this endeavor has been the hardware overhead required to access the late-time post-collision dynamics while implementing the underlying gauge symmetry. Here, we report on the first quantum simulation of scattering in a lattice gauge theory (LGT), performed on \texttt{IBM}'s \texttt{ibm\_marrakesh} quantum computer. Specifically, we quantum-simulate the collision dynamics of electrons and positrons as well as mesons in a $\mathrm{U}(1)$ LGT representing $1+1$D quantum electrodynamics (QED), uncovering rich post-collision dynamics that we can precisely tune with a topological $Θ$-term and the fermionic mass. By monitoring the time evolution of the scattering processes, we are able to distinguish between two main regimes in the wake of the collision. The first is characterized by the delocalization of particles when the topological $Θ$-term is weak, while the second regime shows localized particles with a clear signature when the $Θ$-term is nontrivial. Furthermore, we show that by quenching to a small mass at the collision point, inelastic scattering occurs with a large production of matter reminiscent of quantum many-body scarring. Our work provides a major step forward in the utility of quantum computers for investigating the real-time quantum dynamics of HEP collisions.

## Key claim

The authors report the first quantum simulation of scattering in a lattice gauge theory (LGT), performed on IBM's ibm_marrakesh quantum computer, but note that a parallel submission by Davoudi, Hsieh, and Kadam reports a similar experiment in a Z2 LGT on an IonQ quantum computer. The claim is qualified by the use of a first-order Trotterization with a relatively large time step of Delta t = 1 kappa^{-1}, which may lead to deviations from exact dynamics, though the desired effects are still observed qualitatively.

## Method

The authors simulate electron-positron and meson-meson scattering in a 1+1D U(1) lattice gauge theory representing quantum electrodynamics, using a spin-1/2 quantum link model formulation. They implement gauge-invariant dynamics via first-order Trotter-Suzuki decomposition of the time-evolution operator, with the gauge fields integrated out using Gauss's law to obtain a spin model. The quantum circuits are executed on IBM's ibm_marrakesh processor for systems of up to 45 qubits and up to 35 Trotter steps (two-qubit gate depth of 280), with error mitigation performed using marginal Distribution Error Mitigation (mDEM).

## Key numbers

- System size: up to 45 qubits for electron-positron scattering, 43 qubits for meson-meson scattering
- Trotter step size: Delta t = 1 kappa^{-1} for all simulations
- Maximum Trotter steps: 30 for electron-positron scattering, 35 for meson-meson scattering (two-qubit gate depth of 280)
- Number of measurements per experiment: 50,000
- Mass values used: initial mass m_i = 1.5 kappa; quench final masses m_f = 0.0 kappa, m_f = m_c = 0.3275 kappa, and m_f = 0.8 kappa
- Confining potential values: chi = 0.0 kappa (no confinement), chi = 0.15 kappa (confinement), chi = 0.01 kappa (meson-meson scattering)
- Quantum critical point: m_c = 0.3275 kappa (Coleman's phase transition)

## Context

This work extends prior quantum simulations of LGT dynamics (Martinez 2016, Klco 2018, Zhang 2023, Zhu 2024) and wave-packet preparation in LGTs (Farrell 2024, Davoudi 2024) to achieve the first full scattering simulation in an LGT on quantum hardware. While quantum simulations of scattering in non-LGT Hamiltonians have been performed (Chai 2023, Zemlevskiy 2024, Farrell 2025, Ingoldby 2025), this work provides a necessary LGT implementation for faithfully probing high-energy physics collisions like those at the LHC and RHIC. The results enable future exploration of scattering phenomena in more complex gauge theories, including higher spatial dimensions and non-Abelian gauge groups, and the protocol is claimed to be scalable toward achieving quantum advantage in high-energy physics simulations.
