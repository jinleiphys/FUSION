# Adiabatic projection method with Euclidean time subspace projection

> Euclidean time projection is a powerful tool that uses exponential decay to extract the low-energy information of quantum systems. The adiabatic projection method, which is based on Euclidean time projection, is a procedure for studying scattering and reactions on the lattice. The method constructs the adiabatic Hamiltonian that gives the low-lying energies and wave functions of two-cluster systems. In this paper we seek the answer to the question whether an adiabatic Hamiltonian constructed in a smaller subspace of the two-cluster state space can still provide information on the low-lying spectrum and the corresponding wave functions. We present the results from our investigations on constructing the adiabatic Hamiltonian using Euclidean time projection and extracting details of the low-energy spectrum and wave functions by diagonalizing it. In our analyses we consider systems of fermion-fermion and fermion-dimer interacting via a zero-range attractive potential in one dimension, and fermion-fermion interacting via an attractive Gaussian potential in three dimensions. The results presented here provide a guide for improving the adiabatic projection method and for reducing the computational costs of large-scale calculations of \emph{ab initio} nuclear scattering and reactions using Monte Carlo methods.

## Key claim

The adiabatic Hamiltonian constructed using a smaller subspace of the two-cluster state space (fewer initial cluster states than the complete set) can still provide accurate low-energy spectrum and wave functions, provided Euclidean time projection is used to systematically remove contaminations from high-energy states.

## Method

The author constructs radial adiabatic Hamiltonians by projecting initial two-cluster states (parameterized by relative separation R) onto subspaces using a modulo constraint (m = 2 or 3) that selects every other or every third separation state. These states are evolved in Euclidean time using the microscopic Hamiltonian, then used to compute projected Hamiltonian and norm matrices, from which the adiabatic Hamiltonian is formed via orthogonalization. Scattering phase shifts are extracted using the spherical wall method applied to the adiabatic Hamiltonian at various Euclidean times.

## Key numbers

- For two-particle scattering in one dimension: lattice spacing a = 0.01 MeV^{-1}, interaction strength C_0 = -0.103, forming a dimer with binding energy 2.5 (units not specified but in lattice units)
- For two-particle scattering in three dimensions: lattice spacing a = 0.01 MeV^{-1}, interaction strength C_g = -8.0 MeV, Gaussian potential range R_g = 0.02 MeV^{-1}
- For fermion-dimer scattering in one dimension: lattice spacing a = 0.01 MeV^{-1}, interaction strength C_0 = -0.103
- Euclidean times used for two-particle phase shifts in one dimension: tau = 0.025, 0.050, 0.075, 0.150 MeV^{-1}
- Euclidean times used for two-particle phase shifts in three dimensions: tau = 0.033, 0.047, 0.073 MeV^{-1}
- The number of initial cluster states is reduced by a factor of 1/m, where m = 2 or 3

## Context

This work extends the adiabatic projection method previously applied to neutron-deuteron scattering (Pine 2013, Elhatisari 2014), proton-proton fusion (Rupak 2014), and alpha-alpha scattering (Elhatisari 2015, 2016). The key advance is demonstrating that using fewer initial cluster states (by a factor of 1/m) still yields accurate low-energy scattering information, which reduces computational cost that scales quadratically with the number of initial states. This enables larger-scale ab initio calculations of nuclear scattering and reactions using Monte Carlo methods, where the computational cost scales as 2 L^D A (L_t + A) N_R^2 floating-point operations.
