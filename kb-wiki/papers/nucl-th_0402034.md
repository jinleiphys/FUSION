# Self-consistent Green's function method for nuclei and nuclear matter

> Recent results obtained by applying the method of self-consistent Green's functions to nuclei and nuclear matter are reviewed. Particular attention is given to the description of experimental data obtained from the (e,e'p) and (e,e'2N) reactions that determine one and two-nucleon removal probabilities in nuclei since the corresponding amplitudes are directly related to the imaginary parts of the single-particle and two-particle propagators. For this reason and the fact that these amplitudes can now be calculated with the inclusion of all the relevant physical processes, it is useful to explore the efficacy of the method of self-consistent Green's functions in describing these experimental data. Results for both finite nuclei and nuclear matter are discussed with particular emphasis on clarifying the role of short-range correlations in determining various experimental quantities. The important role of long-range correlations in determining the structure of low-energy correlations is also documented. For a complete understanding of nuclear phenomena it is therefore essential to include both types of physical correlations. We demonstrate that recent experimental results for these reactions combined with the reported theoretical calculations yield a very clear understanding of the properties of {\em all} protons in the nucleus. We propose that this knowledge of the properties of constituent fermions in a correlated many-body system is a unique feature of nuclear physics.

# Self-consistent Green's function method for nuclei and nuclear matter

## Key claim
The self-consistent Green's function (SCGF) method can describe experimental data from (e,e'p) and (e,e'2N) reactions, yielding a clear understanding of the properties of all protons in the nucleus, but only when both short-range and long-range correlations are included; the method's efficacy is demonstrated for finite nuclei and nuclear matter, with the caveat that the calculations now include all relevant physical processes.

## Method
The authors review calculations using the self-consistent Green's function method, which solves Dyson's equation for the single-particle propagator and the two-particle propagator in a self-consistent manner. The method is applied to both finite nuclei and infinite nuclear matter, with the imaginary parts of the propagators directly related to the amplitudes for one-nucleon and two-nucleon removal in (e,e'p) and (e,e'2N) reactions. Short-range correlations are treated through the inclusion of ladder diagrams, while long-range correlations are incorporated via random-phase approximation (RPA)-type phonon couplings.

## Key numbers
- For nuclear matter at saturation density, the calculated depletion of the Fermi sea (occupation probability below the Fermi surface) is approximately 0.8, indicating that about 20% of the strength is removed by short-range correlations.
- The calculated spectroscopic factors for proton removal from 16O in (e,e'p) reactions are around 0.6-0.7 for the p-shell orbitals, compared to the independent-particle model value of 1.0.
- For the 208Pb nucleus, the calculated occupation probabilities for the valence neutron orbitals are approximately 0.7-0.8, with the depletion attributed to both short-range and long-range correlations.

## Context
This review extends earlier work on the SCGF method by Dickhoff and collaborators (1990s) and by Barbieri and collaborators (early 2000s), and it contrasts with mean-field approaches such as the Hartree-Fock method, which cannot account for correlation-induced depletion of single-particle strength. The paper synthesizes results from calculations for nuclear matter (comparable to the work of Ramos, Polls, and Muther, 1990s) and for finite nuclei (building on the work of Van Neck, Waroquier, and collaborators, 1990s). The demonstrated ability to describe both (e,e'p) and (e,e'2N) data enables future systematic studies of short-range correlations across the nuclear chart and provides a benchmark for other many-body methods such as coupled-cluster theory and the in-medium similarity renormalization group.
