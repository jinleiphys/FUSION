# Embedded Representations and Quasi-Dynamical Symmetry

> This presentation explains why models with a dynamical symmetry often work extraordinarily well even in the presence of large symmetry breaking interactions. A model may be a caricature of a more realistic system with a "quasi-dynamical" symmetry. The existence of quasi-dynamical symmetry in physical systems and its significance for understanding collective dynamics in complex nuclei is explained in terms of the precise mathematical concept of an "embedded representation". Examples are given which exhibit quasi-dynamical symmetry to a remarkably high degree. Understanding this unusual symmetry and why it occurs, is important for recognizing why dynamical symmetries appear to be much moreprevalent than they would otherwise have any right to be and for interpreting the implications of a model's successes. We indicate when quasi-dynamical symmetry is expected to apply and present a challenge as to how best to make use of this potentially powerful algebraic structure.

# Embedded Representations and Quasi-Dynamical Symmetry

## Key claim

Models with a dynamical symmetry can work extraordinarily well even in the presence of large symmetry-breaking interactions because the system's states span an "embedded representation" of the Lie algebra, meaning the symmetry is preserved as a quasi-dynamical symmetry rather than an exact one. The paper argues that this phenomenon explains why dynamical symmetries appear much more prevalent than they would otherwise have any right to be, particularly when rotational dynamics is adiabatic relative to intrinsic vibrational dynamics.

## Method

The paper defines quasi-dynamical symmetry mathematically via the concept of an embedded representation: if a Hilbert space carries a reducible representation of a Lie algebra, and a subspace of that Hilbert space has matrix elements equal to those of an irreducible representation, that irreducible representation is embedded. The author demonstrates this structure analytically for the rigid-rotor algebra rot(3) as a quasi-dynamical symmetry of the soft-rotor model, and numerically for SU(3) and symplectic model algebras by computing wave function expansion coefficients in mixed representations under spin-orbit, pairing, and quadrupole-quadrupole interactions.

## Key numbers

- For the Hamiltonian H(alpha) = H_0 + (1-alpha) V_su(2) + alpha V_su(3) with pairing and quadrupole interactions, a phase transition occurs at a critical value of alpha approximately 0.6, which becomes increasingly sharp as the number of particles is increased.
- In the SU(3) mixing calculation with spin-orbit interaction, two irreps were mixed: a (100,0) irrep with spin S=0 and a (101,1) irrep coupled to states of spin S=1, producing ground and beta-like vibrational bands that were "essentially 50-50" mixed but experimentally indistinguishable from pure SU(3) bands.
- The symplectic model wave functions for 166Er were expanded in a multi-shell SU(3) basis covering 12 major harmonic oscillator shells, showing expansion coefficients independent of angular momentum over a large range.

## Context

This work extends the concept of quasi-dynamical symmetry developed by Rowe, Rochford, and collaborators (Rowe, Rochford, and Repka, 1988) and by Carvalho (1984), who predicted that adiabatic separation of rotational and intrinsic degrees of freedom would preserve algebraic structures as quasi-dynamical symmetries. The paper directly addresses the long-standing puzzle of why rotational bands persist in nuclei despite strong symmetry-breaking interactions like spin-orbit coupling and pairing, which would naively destroy the SU(3) and symplectic model symmetries. The results enable a microscopic theory of nuclear rotational states built on the adiabatic principle, with the symplectic algebra sp(3,R) identified as an excellent candidate for a realistic quasi-dynamical symmetry that can incorporate both long-range rotational correlations and major shell mixing.
