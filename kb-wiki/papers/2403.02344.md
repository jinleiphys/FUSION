# Biquaternion representation of the spin one half and its application on the relativistic one electron atom

> In this work we represent the $1/2$ Spin particles with complex quaternions using a transformation to 2x2 matrices in order to obtain the Pauli matrices. With this representation we determine the states, rotation operators and the total angular momentum function in the complex quaternion space. Using this representation we work the solution for the relativistic hydrogen atom.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a complete complex quaternion (biquaternion) representation of spin-1/2 particles can be constructed and used to solve the relativistic hydrogen atom, yielding the same results as the standard matrix formalism, but the representation is limited to problems where the quaternionic multiplication rules can be applied and the inverse quaternion is lost due to zero divisors in the complex quaternion space.

## Method

The author defines a transformation from complex quaternions to 2x2 matrices (Equation labeled "Transfomracion de Alex") that reproduces the Pauli matrices, then maps spin operators, spin states, rotation operators, and spinor functions into the complex quaternion space. This representation is applied to the radial Dirac equation for the hydrogen atom by substituting the Pauli matrices in the Dirac Hamiltonian with their quaternion equivalents, separating the resulting quaternionic radial equation into a system of two differential equations, and obtaining the wave function as a quaternion-valued expression.

## Key numbers

- The energy eigenvalues for the relativistic hydrogen atom are given by: E = mc^2 (1 + (Z a_0)/(n - |k| + sqrt(k^2 - Z^2 a_0^2)))^(-1/2)
- The norm of the spin states is verified: |q_+|^2 = 1/2((1)^2 + (-1)^2) = 1 and |q_-|^2 = 1/2((-1)^2 + (-1)^2) = 1
- The probability to measure the down state in the spinor function y_2^(2 +/- 1/2) is calculated as |(1/2)(2/sqrt(5)) Y_2^2|^2 = (1/5)|Y_2^2|^2

## Context

This work extends the quaternion formulation of spin-1/2 previously developed by Kravchenko and Shapiro (1996) and the biquaternion formulation of the Dirac equation by Silvis (2010), applying these mathematical tools specifically to the relativistic hydrogen atom problem. The representation enables a linear (non-matrix) approach to spin-1/2 problems and could be extended to quantum computing, particle physics, the two-electron (helium) atom problem, and general relativity applications of the Dirac equation.
