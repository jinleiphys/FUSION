# Nuclear pasta in hot and dense matter and its influence on the equation of state for astrophysical simulations

> We explore the properties of nuclear pasta appearing in supernova matter, i.e., matter at finite temperature with a fixed proton fraction. The pasta phases with a series of geometric shapes are studied using the compressible liquid-drop (CLD) model, where nuclear matter separates into a dense liquid phase of nucleons and a dilute gas phase of nucleons and $α$ particles. The equilibrium conditions for two coexisting phases are derived by minimization of the total free energy including the surface and Coulomb contributions, which are clearly different from the Gibbs conditions for phase equilibrium due to the finite-size effects. Compared to the results considering only spherical nuclei, the inclusion of pasta phases can delay the transition to uniform matter and enlarge the region of nonuniform matter in the phase diagram. The thermodynamic quantities obtained in the present calculation with the CLD model are consistent with those in the realistic equation of state table for astrophysical simulations using the Thomas--Fermi approximation. It is found that the density ranges of various pasta shapes depend on both the temperature $T$ and the proton fraction $Y_p$. Furthermore, the nuclear symmetry energy and its density dependence may play crucial roles in determining the properties of pasta phases. Our results suggest that the pasta phase diagram is most sensitively dependent on the symmetry energy slope $L$ especially in the low-$Y_p$ and high-$T$ region.

## Key claim

The inclusion of nonspherical pasta phases (rod, slab, tube, bubble) in the compressible liquid-drop model delays the transition to uniform matter and enlarges the region of nonuniform matter in the phase diagram, but this effect is most sensitively dependent on the symmetry energy slope \(L\) especially in the low-proton-fraction and high-temperature region, where the TM1e model (\(L=40\) MeV) predicts pasta phases down to \(Y_p \approx 0.05\) at \(T=10\) MeV while the TM1 model (\(L=110.8\) MeV) shows no pasta for \(Y_p < 0.2\) at the same temperature.

## Method

The authors used the compressible liquid-drop (CLD) model with a sharp interface between a dense liquid phase of nucleons and a dilute gas phase of nucleons and \(\alpha\) particles, employing the Wigner-Seitz approximation for five geometric shapes (droplet, rod, slab, tube, bubble). Equilibrium conditions were derived by minimizing the total free energy including surface and Coulomb contributions, which differ from the Gibbs conditions due to finite-size effects, and the calculations were performed with two relativistic mean-field parametrizations (TM1e with \(L=40\) MeV and TM1 with \(L=110.8\) MeV) that share identical isoscalar properties but differ in symmetry energy behavior.

## Key numbers

- TM1e model: symmetry energy \(E_{\text{sym}} = 31.38\) MeV and slope \(L = 40\) MeV at saturation density; TM1 model: \(E_{\text{sym}} = 36.89\) MeV and \(L = 110.8\) MeV.
- TM1e model predicts a neutron-star radius for a \(1.4 M_\odot\) star of \(R_{1.4} = 13.1\) km; TM1 model predicts \(R_{1.4} = 14.2\) km.
- Maximum neutron-star masses: \(2.18 M_\odot\) for TM1 and \(2.12 M_\odot\) for TM1e.
- At \(T=10\) MeV and \(Y_p=0.3\) with the TM1e model, the transition from droplet to rod occurs at a liquid volume fraction \(u \approx 0.21\), close to the Bohr-Wheeler estimate of \(u > 1/8\).
- At \(T=10\) MeV, nuclear pasta exists down to \(Y_p \approx 0.05\) in the TM1e model, whereas in the TM1 model no pasta forms for \(Y_p < 0.2\).
- The critical temperature \(T_c\) for \(Y_p=0.1\) is much smaller in the TM1 model than in the TM1e model.

## Context

This work extends the compressible liquid-drop model studies of pasta phases by Pais et al. (2015) and Bao et al. (2014b) to supernova matter at finite temperature with fixed proton fraction, using the TM1e parametrization that is compatible with recent neutron-star observations including GW170817 and NICER measurements. The results are compared to the Shen equation of state tables (Shen 2011, 2020) based on the Thomas-Fermi approximation, showing consistency in thermodynamic quantities, and the findings enable more accurate modeling of the equation of state for core-collapse supernova simulations and neutron-star merger calculations where pasta phases may affect neutrino scattering and gravitational wave signals.
