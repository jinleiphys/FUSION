# A nonlinear classical model for the decay widths of Isoscalar Giant Monopole Resonances

> The decay of the Isoscalar Giant Monopole Resonance (ISGMR) in nuclei is studied by means of a nonlinear classical model consisting of several noninteracting nucleons (particles) moving in a potential well with an oscillating nuclear surface (wall). The motion of the nuclear surface is described by means of a collective variable which appears explicitly in the Hamiltonian as an additional degree of freedom. The total energy of the system is therefore conserved. Although the particles do not directly interact with each other, their motions are indirectly coupled by means of their interaction with the moving nuclear surface. We consider as free parameters in this model the degree of collectivity and the fraction of nucleons that participate to the decay of the collective excitation. Specifically, we have calculated the decay width of the ISGMR in the spherical nuclei $^{208}\rm{Pb}$, $^{144}\rm{Sm}$, $^{116}\rm{Sn}$ and $^{90}\rm{Zr}$. Despite its simplicity and its purely classical nature, the model reproduces the trend of the experimental data which show that with increasing mass number the decay width decreases. Moreover the experimental results (with the exception of $^{90}\rm{Zr}$) can be well fitted using appropriate values for the free parameters mentioned above. It is also found that these values allow for a good description of the experimentally measured $^{112}\rm{Sn}$ and $^{124}\rm{Sn}$ decay widths. In addition, we give a prediction for the decay width of the exotic isotope $^{132}Sn$ for which there is experimental interest. The agreement of our results with the corresponding experimental data for medium-heavy nuclei is dictated by the underlying classical mechanics i.e. the behaviour of the maximum Lyapunov exponent as a function of the system size.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a purely classical, nonlinear model of noninteracting nucleons moving in an oscillating potential well can reproduce the experimentally observed trend of decreasing ISGMR decay width with increasing mass number for medium-heavy nuclei, provided that the degree of collectivity and the fraction of participating nucleons are treated as free parameters. The hedge is that the model fails for the lightest nucleus studied (90Zr) and that the quantitative agreement relies on tuning these two free parameters per nucleus or assuming they are universal across nuclei.

## Method

The authors model the ISGMR as a classical system of N noninteracting nucleons moving in a Woods-Saxon potential well whose surface (wall) oscillates radially as a harmonic oscillator with angular frequency ω corresponding to the experimental centroid energy. The collective variable R (nuclear radius) appears explicitly in the Hamiltonian as an additional degree of freedom, ensuring total energy conservation. For each nucleus, a microcanonical ensemble of 5000 initial conditions is generated, and the equations of motion are solved numerically; the decay width Γ is extracted from the Fourier transform of the mean collective variable ⟨R(t)⟩ by taking the full width at half maximum of the power spectrum around ω.

## Key numbers

* For 208Pb: experimental centroid energy Ex = 13.96 ± 0.20 MeV, experimental decay width Γexp = 2.88 ± 0.20 MeV.
* For 144Sm: Ex = 15.40 ± 0.30 MeV, Γexp = 3.40 ± 0.20 MeV.
* For 116Sn: Ex = 15.85 ± 0.20 MeV, Γexp = 5.27 ± 0.25 MeV.
* For 90Zr: Ex = 17.81 (+0.20, -0.32) MeV, Γexp = 7.86 (+0.89, -1.41) MeV.
* Optimal global fit (Ξ ≈ 69%, Λ ≈ 62%) yields calculated widths: 208Pb = 3.08 MeV, 144Sm = 3.94 MeV, 116Sn = 4.64 MeV, 90Zr = 5.05 MeV.
* For the same optimal parameters, calculated widths for Sn isotopes: 112Sn = 4.68 MeV (experiment 5.18 MeV), 124Sn = 4.05 MeV (experiment 5.00 MeV), and a prediction for 132Sn = 3.87 MeV (using Ex = 15.29 MeV from theory).
* The fraction of the width due to nucleon escape (μesc) in the optimal parameter region is between 0.20 and 0.28 for all nuclei studied.

## Context

This work extends the classical vibrating potential model of Baldo et al. (1995) and Burgio et al. (1998) by considering diabatic motion for the nuclear surface and a realistic Woods-Saxon geometry, and by treating the number of participating nucleons and the collective mass as free parameters. The model reproduces the experimental trend of decreasing width with mass number (You et al., 2004; Itoh et al., 2003) for medium-heavy nuclei, achieving agreement comparable to more sophisticated quantum approaches such as self-consistent Hartree-Fock plus RPA with Skyrme or Gogny interactions, which generally yield smaller widths. The results suggest that the underlying classical chaotic dynamics, quantified by the maximum Lyapunov exponent, governs the mass-number dependence of the decay width, and the model enables predictions for exotic isotopes such as 132Sn that are of experimental interest.
