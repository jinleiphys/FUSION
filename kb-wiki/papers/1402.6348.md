# Thermal properties of supernova matter: The bulk homogeneous phase

> We investigate the thermal properties of the potential model equation of state of Akmal, Pandharipande and Ravenhall. This equation of state approximates the microscopic model calculations of Akmal and Pandharipande, which feature a neutral pion condensate. We treat the bulk homogeneous phase for isospin asymmetries ranging from symmetric nuclear matter to pure neutron matter and for temperatures and densities relevant for simulations of core-collapse supernovae, proto-neutron stars, and neutron star mergers. Numerical results of the state variables are compared with those of a typical Skyrme energy density functional with similar properties at nuclear densities, but which differs substantially at supra-nuclear densities. Analytical formulas, which are applicable to non-relativistic potential models such as the equations of state we are considering, are derived for all state variables and their thermodynamic derivatives. A highlight of our work is its focus on thermal response functions in the degenerate and non-degenerate situations, which allow checks of the numerical calculations for arbitrary degeneracy. These functions are sensitive to the density dependent effective masses of neutrons and protons, which determine the thermal properties in all regimes of degeneracy. We develop the "thermal asymmetry free energy" and establish its relation to the more commonly used nuclear symmetry energy. We also explore the role of the pion condensate at supra-nuclear densities and temperatures. Tables of matter properties as functions of baryon density, composition (i.e., proton fraction) and temperature are being produced which are suitable for use in astrophysical simulations of supernovae and neutron stars.

## Key claim

The thermal properties of the Akmal-Pandharipande-Ravenhall (APR) equation of state are investigated for the bulk homogeneous phase, with the finding that the density-dependent effective masses of neutrons and protons govern the thermal response functions in all regimes of degeneracy, but the results are limited to the uniform phase and do not include the inhomogeneous phase containing nuclei, hyperons, or deconfined quark matter.

## Method

The authors performed a detailed analysis of the APR potential model equation of state, which approximates the microscopic calculations of Akmal and Pandharipande featuring a neutral pion condensate, and compared it with the SKa Skyrme energy density functional. They derived analytical formulas for all state variables and thermodynamic derivatives applicable to non-relativistic potential models, computed numerical results for baryon densities from 10^-7 fm^-3 to 1 fm^-3, temperatures up to 50 MeV, and proton fractions from 0 to 0.5, and employed a Maxwell construction for the transition between the low-density phase and the high-density pion condensate phase.

## Key numbers

- The transition density from low-density phase to high-density phase is n_t = 0.32 fm^-3 for symmetric nuclear matter (x=1/2) and n_t = 0.195 fm^-3 for pure neutron matter (x=0)
- For the APR model at zero temperature: K_0 = 266 MeV (incompressibility), S_2 = 32.6 MeV (symmetry energy), L = 58.5 MeV (symmetry energy stiffness), and m*_0/m = 0.7 (effective mass at saturation)
- The APR model supports a maximum neutron star mass of 2.19 M_sun, and the radius of a 1.4 M_sun star is about 12 km
- The SKa model parameters include t_0 = -1602.78 MeV fm^6, t_1 = 570.88 fm^3, t_2 = -67.7 fm^3, t_3 = 8000.0 MeV fm^7, and epsilon = 1/3

## Context

This work extends the zero-temperature APR equation of state of Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall (1998) to finite temperatures for the first time, providing a three-dimensional tabular equation of state suitable for astrophysical simulations of core-collapse supernovae, proto-neutron stars, and neutron star mergers. The study contrasts the APR model with the SKa Skyrme force of Kohler (1976), showing that while both models yield similar results for equilibrium density, binding energy, symmetry energy, and compression modulus of symmetric matter, they differ significantly in Landau effective masses and high-density behavior. The analytical expressions developed here can be used to update Lattimer-Swesty type liquid droplet equation of state models (1991) to include realistic effective masses, and the results enable future work extending calculations to the inhomogeneous phase containing finite nuclei.
