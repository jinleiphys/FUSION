# Relativity Damps OPEP in Nuclear Matter

> Using a relativistic Dirac-Brueckner analysis the OPEP contribution to the ground state energy of nuclear matter is studied. In the study the pion is derivative-coupled. We find that the role of the tensor force in the saturation mechanism is substantially reduced compared to its dominant role in a usual nonrelativistic treatment. We show that the damping of derivative-coupled OPEP is actually due to the decrease of $M^*/M$ with increasing density. We point out that if derivative-coupled OPEP is the preferred form of nuclear effective lagrangian nonrelativistic treatment of nuclear matter is in trouble. Lacking the notion of $M^*$ it cannot replicate the damping. We suggest an examination of the feasibility of using pseudoscalar coupled $π$N interaction before reaching a final conclusion about nonrelativistic treatment of nuclear matter.

## Key claim

Using a relativistic Dirac-Brueckner analysis, the contribution of derivative-coupled one-pion exchange potential (OPEP) to the ground state energy of nuclear matter is substantially reduced compared to its dominant role in nonrelativistic treatments; this damping is due to the decrease of the effective nucleon mass ratio \(M^*/M\) with increasing density, and if derivative-coupled OPEP is the correct form of the nuclear effective Lagrangian, nonrelativistic treatments of nuclear matter are in trouble.

## Method

The author performed a Dirac-Brueckner (D-B) analysis of nuclear matter using a derivative-coupled pion-nucleon interaction, computing the OPEP contribution to the ground state energy via the Hellmann-Feynman theorem. The two-nucleon propagator in the G-matrix equation was expressed in terms of dimensionless momenta scaled by \(M^*\), revealing an explicit \((M^*/M)^2\) damping factor in the derivative-coupled OPEP matrix element relative to scalar-exchange potentials. To isolate the effect of \(M^*\), the scalar self-energy \(S\) from the self-consistent D-B calculation was artificially scaled by a factor \(\alpha\) (with \(\alpha=1\) being self-consistent) and the resulting OPEP contribution was recalculated.

## Key numbers

- The nonrelativistic OPEP contribution to nuclear matter energy is \(\langle NM | V_\pi | NM \rangle_{\text{nonrelativistic}} \sim -34\, (\rho/\rho_0)^{0.45}\) MeV.
- The relativistic (Dirac-Brueckner) OPEP contribution is \(\langle NM | V_\pi | NM \rangle_{\text{Relativistic}} \sim -20\, (\rho/\rho_0)^{0.16}\) MeV.
- The deuteron tensor force matrix element in nonrelativistic treatment is \(2\langle ^3D_1 | V_{\text{tensor}} | ^3S_1\rangle \sim -22\) MeV, and the deuteron kinetic energy is \(\langle \text{Deuteron} | p^2/(2M) | \text{Deuteron} \rangle \sim +20\) MeV.
- The relativistic deuteron expectation of \(V_\pi\) is \(\langle \text{Deuteron} | V_\pi | \text{Deuteron} \rangle_{\text{relativistic}} = -22\) MeV.
- The scalar and vector fields from the self-consistent D-B calculation are \(S_{D-B} = -306\, (\rho/\rho_0)^{0.81}\) MeV and \(V_{D-B} = 233\, (\rho/\rho_0)^{0.97}\) MeV; the corresponding mean-field theory (MFT) values are \(S_{MFT} = -358\, (\rho/\rho_0)^{0.92}\) MeV and \(V_{MFT} = 295\, (\rho/\rho_0)\) MeV.
- The derivative-coupled OPEP matrix element carries an explicit damping factor of \((M^*/M)^2\) relative to the sigma-exchange potential.

## Context

This work extends the Dirac-Brueckner studies of nuclear matter by Amorim and Tjon (1990s) and the Bonn C potential by Machleidt (1989), showing that the tensor force from derivative-coupled OPEP plays a minor role in saturation, in sharp contrast to nonrelativistic Bethe-Brueckner calculations (e.g., by Friman and others). The results challenge the validity of nonrelativistic treatments if derivative-coupled OPEP is the correct effective Lagrangian, and suggest that a pseudoscalar-coupled pion-nucleon interaction should be examined before concluding on nonrelativistic approaches. The damping mechanism is generic to any relativistic treatment using derivative-coupled pions and is not specific to the particular quasipotential prescription used.
