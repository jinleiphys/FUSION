# Spin Clustering of Accreting X-ray Neutron Stars as Possible Evidence of Quark Matter

> A neutron star in binary orbit with a low-mass non-degenerate companion becomes a source of x-rays with millisecond variability when mass accretion spins it up. Centrifugally driven changes in density profile may initiate a phase transition in a growing region of the core parallel to what may take place in an isolated millisecond pulsar, but in reverse. Such a star will spend a longer time in the spin frequency range over which the transition occurs than elsewhere because the change of phase, paced by the spinup rate, is accompanied by a growth in the moment of inertia. The population of accreters will exhibit a clustering in the critical frequency range. A phase change triggered by changing spin and the accompanying adjustment of moment of inertia has its analogue in rotating nuclei.

# Spin Clustering of Accreting X-ray Neutron Stars as Possible Evidence of Quark Matter

## Key claim
The observed clustering of spin frequencies in accreting X-ray neutron stars around 300 Hz may be caused by a temporary stalling of spinup due to a phase transition from quark matter to confined hadronic matter in the stellar core, paced by the changing centrifugal force during accretion. The authors hedge that this phase transition can also occur without any remarkable signal, and that the discrepancy between the calculated peak position (around 200 Hz) and the observed clustering (around 300 Hz) is not surprising given uncertainties in the equation of state above nuclear saturation density.

## Method
The authors model the spin evolution of an accreting neutron star using a torque equation that includes accretion torque and magnetic braking, combined with general relativistic calculations of the moment of inertia for rapidly rotating stars. They assume a neutron star with initial mass 1.42 solar masses that accretes up to 0.4 solar masses, using the MIT bag model for quark matter (bag constant B^{1/4}=180 MeV, three light flavor quarks with m_s=150 MeV) and a magnetic field decaying from 10^12 G to 10^8 G with a decay time of 10^6 years. The frequency distribution of X-ray stars is computed by assuming neutron stars begin accretion at an average rate of one per million years, with donor masses uniformly distributed between 0.1 and 0.4 solar masses.

## Key numbers
- The calculated spin plateau where spinup stalls occurs around 200 Hz, signaling the ongoing process of quark confinement in the stellar centers
- The anomalous spinup era for isolated millisecond pulsars was found to last for approximately 2×10^7 years, or about 1/50 of the spindown time, occurring in a small frequency band around 220 Hz
- Three average accretion rates are assumed: M_dot = 1, 10, and 100 in units of 10^{-10} solar masses per year
- The initial neutron star mass is 1.42 solar masses, close to the rotating star mass limit of 1.66 solar masses
- The magnetic field decays from B(0)=10^12 G to B(∞)=10^8 G with a decay time t_d=10^6 years
- The minimum accreted mass required for the effect is approximately 0.1 solar masses
- Objects above about 400 Hz are stated to be unstable to collapse to very high-spin black holes
- The observed clustering in X-ray neutron stars is at approximately 300 Hz, about 100 Hz higher than the calculated value

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies of phase transitions in isolated millisecond pulsars (Glendenning 1997, 2000) to accreting X-ray neutron stars in low-mass binaries. It offers an alternative to other proposed mechanisms for the observed frequency clustering, such as gravitational wave torque from thermally induced density asymmetries (Bildsten 1998) or r-mode instabilities from Rossby waves (Andersson 2000). The authors draw an analogy to the "backbending" phenomenon in rotating nuclei discovered in the 1970s (Johnson 1972, Stephens 1972), where a phase transition between spin-aligned and BCS paired states causes a similar anomaly in the moment of inertia. The paper enables future observational tests: if the same spin anomaly near the same frequency were discovered in an isolated millisecond pulsar spontaneously spinning up while radiating angular momentum, it would support the quark matter hypothesis over competing explanations.
