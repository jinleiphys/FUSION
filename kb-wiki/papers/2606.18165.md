# Observation of a dominant $\boldsymbol{0f_{7/2}}$ neutron configuration in the $\boldsymbol{^{32}}$Si $\boldsymbol{J^π=5^-}$ isomeric state

> An yrast, $J^π=5^-$, spin-trap isomer has been previously identified in $^{32}$Si. The isomeric state decays predominantly via a hindered $E3$ transition [B($E3$) = 0.0841(10)~W.u.], bypassing a nearby $E2$ decay path to the first excited $3^-$ level. The single-neutron aspects of these negative parity levels were investigated via the $^{31}$Si$(d$,$p)^{32}$Si reaction at 9.6~MeV/$u$ using HELIOS and the ATLAS in-flight facility. The $5^-$ state appears as a dominant $\ell=3$ transfer with a relatively large spectroscopic factor, confirming its single-particle $\nu0f_{7/2}$ character. The yrast $3^-$ level had a reduced $\ell=3$ spectroscopic factor of $\approx$ 0.44 compared to that of the $5^-_1$ level. This is similar to the situation observed in nearby $^{34}$S which by contrast has a measured B($E2, 5^-\rightarrow 3^-$) transition strength closer to 1~W.u.. It has been concluded that the hinderance of the $5^-_1\rightarrow 3^-_1$ transition in $^{32}$Si is not primarily due to the differing overlaps in the neutron structure. Instead, the lack of participation by both the protons and the neutrons in the transition is proposed as the transition-strength reduction mechanism.

# Observation of a dominant $0f_{7/2}$ neutron configuration in the $^{32}$Si $J^\pi=5^-$ isomeric state

## Key claim

The $5^-$ isomeric state in $^{32}$Si is confirmed to have a dominant $(0d_{3/2})^1(0f_{7/2})^1$ single-neutron configuration via $\ell=3$ transfer with a large spectroscopic factor, but the hindered $E2$ decay to the $3^-_1$ level is not primarily due to differences in neutron wave function overlaps; instead, the hindrance is proposed to arise from a lack of participation by both protons and neutrons in the transition.

## Method

The single-neutron transfer reaction $^{31}$Si$(d,p)^{32}$Si was performed at 9.6 MeV/u using the HELIOS spectrometer at the ATLAS in-flight facility at Argonne National Laboratory. Protons were measured in coincidence with $^{32}$Si recoils within a 2.5 T solenoidal field, and excitation energies from $E_x \approx 3.5$ to 8.5 MeV were reconstructed. A Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) analysis using the finite-range code PTOLEMY was applied to extract angular distributions and spectroscopic factors ($C^2S$) normalized to the $5^-$ state.

## Key numbers

- The $5^-$ isomeric state has a mean lifetime $\tau_{\text{mean}} = 46.9(5)$ ns and decays predominantly via an $E3$ transition of 3562.84(14) keV with $B(E3) = 0.0841(10)$ W.u.
- The $3^-_1$ level has a relative $\ell=3$ spectroscopic factor $C^2S = 0.44(11)$ compared to the $5^-_1$ level (normalized to 1.00).
- The $5^-_1$ state shows an $\ell=1$ contribution limit of $<10\%$ of the $\ell=3$ strength.
- The $B(E2, 5^-_1 \rightarrow 3^-_1)$ transition strength in $^{32}$Si is $<0.053$ W.u., compared to $\approx 0.5-1$ W.u. in $^{34}$S and $^{36}$Ar.
- The $3^-_1$ to $5^-_1$ $C^2S$ ratio in $^{34}$S is $\approx 0.25(4)$, compared to $0.44(11)$ in $^{32}$Si.
- The $B(E2, 2^+_1 \rightarrow 0^+_1)$ transition strength in $^{32}$Si is reduced relative to $^{34}$S and $^{36}$Ar by at most 60\%.
- The $Q$-value resolution achieved was $\sim 300$ keV, with a systematic uncertainty of 10\% applied to relative $C^2S$ values.

## Context

This work extends single-neutron transfer studies of $N=18$ isotones, comparing $^{32}$Si to $^{34}$S where prior ($d$,$p$) measurements by Crozier (1972) and Van Der Baan (1971) established the $3^-_1$ to $5^-_1$ spectroscopic factor ratio. The results contradict the hypothesis from Williams (2023, 2025) that differing neutron configurations between the $5^-$ and $3^-$ states primarily cause the hindered $E2$ transition; instead, the authors propose that both proton and neutron participation are suppressed due to the robust $Z=14$ sub-shell gap. The findings enable predictions for the $N=20$ isotone $^{34}$Si, where a similarly hindered $5^- \rightarrow 3^-$ transition is expected but has not yet been measured.
