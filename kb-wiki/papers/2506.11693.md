# Gapless neutron superfluidity in the crust of the accreting neutron stars KS 1731-260 and MXB 1659-29

> The interpretation of the thermal evolution of the transiently accreting neutron stars MXB 1659-29 and KS 1731-260 after an outburst is challenging, both within the traditional deep-crustal heating paradigm and the thermodynamically consistent approach of Gusakov and Chugunov that accounts for neutron diffusion throughout the crust. All these studies assume that the neutron superfluid in the crust is at rest. However, we have recently shown that a finite superflow could exist and could lead to a new gapless superfluid phase if quantized vortices are pinned. We have revisited the cooling of MXB 1659-29 and KS 1731-260 and we have found that gapless superfluidity could naturally explain their late time cooling. We pursue here our investigation by performing new simulations of the thermal relaxation of the crust of MXB 1659-29 and KS 1731-260 within a Markov Chain Monte Carlo method accounting for neutron diffusion and allowing for gapless superfluidity. We have varied the global structure of the neutron star, the composition of the heat-blanketing envelope, and the mass accretion rate. In all cases, observations are best fitted by models with gapless superfluidity. Finally, we make predictions that could be tested by future observations.

## Key claim
Gapless neutron superfluidity, arising from a finite superflow due to pinned quantized vortices, can naturally explain the late-time cooling of the accreting neutron stars KS 1731-260 and MXB 1659-29, but only if the superfluid velocity lies within the range between Landau's velocity and the critical velocity; shallow heating is still required to explain the early cooling.

## Method
The authors performed new simulations of the thermal relaxation of the crusts of KS 1731-260 and MXB 1659-29 using a Markov Chain Monte Carlo method that accounts for neutron diffusion and allows for gapless superfluidity. They varied the global structure of the neutron star, the composition of the heat-blanketing envelope, and the mass accretion rate, fitting the resulting cooling curves to observational data from Chandra, XMM-Newton, and Swift.

## Key numbers
- Landau's velocity for neutron superfluidity is approximately \( \VLn \approx 1.2 \times 10^8 \, \text{cm} \, \text{s}^{-1} \left( \frac{\Delta_n^{(0)}}{1 \, \text{MeV}} \right) \left( \frac{10^{14} \, \text{g} \, \text{cm}^{-3}}{\rho Y_{\rm nf}} \right)^{1/3} \).
- The critical velocity for destruction of superfluidity is \( \Vcn \approx 1.359 \, \VLn \) in the weak-coupling approximation.
- At the onset of gapless superfluidity (\( \Vn = \VLn \)), the critical temperature is reduced to \( T_{cn}(\Vn=\VLn) \approx 0.7321 \, \Tcn \).
- The reduction factor of neutron specific heat at Landau's velocity is \( R^{\rm Gapless}_{00}(T\ll \Tcn,\Vn=\VLn) \approx 0.9328 \sqrt{T/\Tcn} \).
- The thermal relaxation time in the gapless regime is estimated as \( \tau \approx 3 \times 10^4 \, \text{days} \times R_{00}^{\rm Gapless}(T,\Vn/\VLn) \) times factors involving crust composition and thickness.
- The pinning force per unit length is estimated as \( f_p(\rho) \approx 2.5 \times 10^{19} \, \text{dyn} \, \text{cm}^{-1} \left( \frac{\Delta_n^{(0)}}{1 \, \text{MeV}} \right) \left( \frac{\rho Y_{\rm nf}}{10^{14} \, \text{g} \, \text{cm}^{-3}} \right)^{2/3} \).
- The critical lag from glitch statistics is \( V_{\rm cr} \sim 10^5 \, \text{cm} \, \text{s}^{-1} \) (from Melatos 2023), but entrainment effects may increase it to \( V_{\rm cr} \sim 10^8 \, \text{cm} \, \text{s}^{-1} \).

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study (Allard and Chamel 2023) showing that gapless superfluidity explains late-time cooling of these two neutron stars, and contrasts with the standard deep-crustal heating paradigm and the thermodynamically consistent approach of Gusakov and Chugunov (2020, 2021) that assumed the neutron superfluid is at rest. Prior attempts to explain the late-time cooling included low thermal conductivity in dense crust layers (Horowitz et al. 2015), fine-tuning of neutron pairing gaps (Turlione et al. 2015), and extrapolation of pairing gaps from quantum Monte Carlo calculations (Deibel et al. 2017), but these were not supported by later calculations. The results enable predictions for future observations of these and other transiently accreting neutron stars.
