# Microscopic calculation of neutrino mean free path inside hot neutron matter

> We calculate the neutrino mean free path and the Equation of State of pure neutron matter at finite temperature within a selfconsistent scheme based on the Brueckner--Hartree--Fock approximation. We employ the nucleon-nucleon part of the recent realistic baryon-baryon interaction (model NSC97e) constructed by the Nijmegen group. The temperatures considered range from 10 to 80 MeV. We report on the calculation of the mean field, the residual interaction and the neutrino mean free path including short and long range correlations given by the Brueckner--Hartree--Fock plus Random Phase Approximation (BHF+RPA) framework. This is the first fully consistent calculation in hot neutron matter dedicated to neutrino mean free path. We compare systematically our results to those obtain with the D1P Gogny effective interaction, which is independent of the temperature. The main differences between the present calculation and those with nuclear effective interactions come from the RPA corrections to BHF (a factor of about 8) while the temperature lack of consistency accounts for a factor of about 2.

# Microscopic calculation of neutrino mean free path inside hot neutron matter

## Key claim

The first fully consistent calculation of the neutrino mean free path in hot neutron matter using the Brueckner–Hartree–Fock plus Random Phase Approximation (BHF+RPA) framework shows that the temperature consistency between the equation of state and the dynamical response function matters: the lack of such consistency leads to an underestimation of the mean free path by about 30–50% at T = 80 MeV, while the dominant correction relative to effective-interaction calculations comes from RPA correlations (a factor of about 8) rather than from temperature effects (a factor of about 2).

## Method

The authors solve the Bethe–Goldstone equation for pure neutron matter at finite temperature using the nucleon–nucleon part of the Nijmegen NSC97e realistic baryon–baryon interaction, within the Brueckner–Hartree–Fock (BHF) approximation extended to finite temperature via the Bloch–De Dominicis expansion. The single-particle potential and the chemical potential are determined self-consistently at each temperature, and the Landau parameters (F0, F1, G0, G1) are extracted from the resulting mean field and effective mass. The dynamical structure factors for the density (S=0) and spin-density (S=1) channels are computed both at the BHF level and with long-range correlations included via the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) using a particle–hole interaction consistently derived from the same G-matrix. The neutrino mean free path is then obtained from the structure factors using the standard electroweak neutral-current formula, assuming an average neutrino energy Eν = 3T.

## Key numbers

- Temperatures considered: 10 to 80 MeV.
- Neutrino mean free path computed at densities up to ρ = 0.25 fm⁻³ (kF = 1.95 fm⁻¹).
- The ratio R = λ^(BHF+RPA l=0,1) / λ^(BHF) is about 8 at high density for the Nijmegen potential, compared to about 1 for the Gogny D1P interaction.
- The lack of temperature consistency (using T=10 MeV Landau parameters at T=80 MeV) leads to an underestimation of the neutrino mean free path of about 30–50% for T=80 MeV.
- The effective mass is nearly independent of temperature up to T=80 MeV.
- The neutrino mean free path approximately follows a T⁻² scaling law.

## Context

This work extends the zero-temperature BHF calculations of the neutrino mean free path (e.g., Lombardo et al., 2002) to finite temperature in a fully consistent manner, and it is the first such calculation in hot neutron matter. It contrasts with earlier approaches that used the D1P Gogny effective interaction (Farine et al., 1999), which is temperature-independent and predicts a ferromagnetic instability at high density that artificially suppresses the mean free path. The results enable more reliable predictions for neutrino transport in supernova cores and hot neutron stars, and they quantify the importance of both the realistic interaction and the self-consistent treatment of temperature when modeling the equation of state and response functions together.
