# Dynamics of few-body states in a medium

> Strongly interacting matter such as nuclear or quark matter leads to few-body bound states and correlations of the constituents. As a consequence quantum chromodynamics has a rich phase structure with spontaneous symmetry breaking, superconductivity, condensates of different kinds. All this appears in many astrophysical scenarios. Among them is the formation of hadrns during the early stage of the Universe, the structure of a neutron star, the formation of nuclei during a supernova explosion. Some of these extreme conditions can be simulated in heavy ion colliders. To treat such a hot and dense system we use the Green function formalism of many-body theory. It turns out that a systematic Dyson expansion of the Green functions leads to modified few-body equations that are capable to describe phase transitions, condensates, cluster formation and more. These equations include self energy corrections and Pauli blocking. We apply this method to nonrelativistic and relativistic matter. The latter one is treated on the light front. Because of the medium and the inevitable truncation of space, the few-body dynamics and states depend on the thermodynamic parameters of the medium.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a systematic Dyson expansion of many-body Green functions leads to modified few-body equations that can describe phase transitions, condensates, and cluster formation in hot and dense nuclear or quark matter, but only under the condition that the few-body dynamics and states depend on the thermodynamic parameters of the medium due to medium effects and the inevitable truncation of space.

## Method

The authors used the Green function formalism of many-body theory, organizing Green functions into a Dyson expansion that yields a hierarchy of linked cluster equations. They applied this formalism to both nonrelativistic nuclear matter (for heavy ion collisions at 30-100 MeV per nucleon) and relativistic quark matter treated on the light front using the Nambu Jona-Lasinio model. The resulting cluster mean-field equations incorporate self-energy corrections and Pauli blocking, and were solved to obtain cluster fractions as a function of collision time and to map dissociation lines in the QCD phase diagram.

## Key numbers

* At T=10 MeV in symmetric nuclear matter, the fraction of alpha particles peaks at around t=100-120 fm/c (corresponding to 3-4 times the initial volume), which is considered the freeze-out time.
* The nonrelativistic treatment is applied to heavy ion collisions at energies of about 30-100 MeV per nucleon, specifically referencing the Xe on Sn experiment at 50 MeV per nucleon.
* For quark matter, nucleon dissociation lines were calculated for different cut-off parameters: Lambda=4m, 6m, and 8m, where m is the medium-dependent quark mass.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous applications of the Green function approach to finite temperatures for multi-nucleon clusters (Beyer 1996, 1999, 2000) and generalizes it to light-front dynamics (Beyer 2001, Mattiello 2002). It enables further studies of three-nucleon correlations on superconductivity, four-nucleon condensation, and larger clusters than alpha particles, as well as the application of real light-front QCD as developed by Brodsky, Pauli and Pinsky (1998) to quantum statistics.
