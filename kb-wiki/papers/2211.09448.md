# Fermi operator expansion method for nuclei and inhomogeneous matter with nuclear energy density functional

> The nuclear energy density functional method at finite temperature is a useful tool for studies of nuclear structure at high excitation, and also for researches of nuclear matter involved in explosive stellar phenomena and neutron stars. However, its unrestricted calculation requires large computational costs for the three-dimensional coordinate-space solvers, especially for the Hamiltonian matrix diagonalization and (or) the Gram-Schmidt orthonormalization of the single-particle wave functions. We test numerical performance of the Fermi operator expansion method, that requires neither the diagonalization nor the Gram-Schmidt orthonormalization, for finite nuclei and inhomogeneous nuclear matter. The method is applied to isolated finite N=Z nuclei and to non-uniform symmetric nuclear matter at finite temperature, which turns out be very effective with the three-dimensional coordinate-space representation, especially at high temperature. The Fermi operator expansion method is a useful tool for studies of various nuclear phases at finite temperature with the energy density functional calculations. The method is suitable for massively parallel computing with distributed memory. Furthermore, when the space size is large, the calculation may benefit from its order-N scaling property.

## Key claim

The Fermi operator expansion (FOE) method, which avoids Hamiltonian diagonalization and Gram-Schmidt orthonormalization, is numerically effective for finite-temperature nuclear energy density functional calculations in three-dimensional coordinate-space representation, especially at high temperature where the Fermi-Dirac function becomes smoother and the required polynomial degree scales inversely with temperature.

## Method

The author implements the FOE method using Chebyshev polynomial expansion of the Fermi-Dirac distribution function to construct the one-body density matrix without diagonalizing the mean-field Hamiltonian. The method is applied to the BKN energy density functional (a simplified Skyrme-type functional without spin-orbit or pairing) using a three-dimensional Cartesian grid representation with 1.0 fm spacing, periodic boundary conditions, and the modified Broyden's mixing method for self-consistent iteration. The entropy is computed via a separate Chebyshev expansion of the entropy function using the same recursively generated vectors, avoiding additional eigenvalue calculations.

## Key numbers

- For the Woods-Saxon test potential (V_ws = -50 MeV, R_ws = 3 fm, a_ws = 0.5 fm) in a (13 fm)^3 box at T = 1 MeV with M = 1.5 × e_r β: nucleon number A = 9.8125, kinetic energy E_kin = 159.5157 MeV, Woods-Saxon energy E_ws = -183.8843 MeV, entropy S/k_B = 8.3121
- At T = 10 MeV with same potential and M = 1.5 × e_r β: A = 27.3152, E_kin = 532.9055 MeV, E_ws = -248.7954 MeV, S/k_B = 78.6634
- For 16O at T = 1 MeV in a (13 fm)^3 box: total energy E = -132.6 MeV, free energy F = -132.9 MeV
- The polynomial degree M is set as M = 1.5 × e_r β, corresponding to accuracy of 10^{-5.5}; the maximum deviation of the Chebyshev approximation from the exact Fermi-Dirac function is on the order of 10^{-6}
- The nearsightedness range is estimated as r_N ~ sqrt(ℏ²/(3m) × (D-1)β), where D is the desired decimal accuracy

## Context

This work extends the FOE method, previously used in condensed matter physics (Goedecker and Teter, 1995; Gibson and Haydock, 1994), to nuclear physics applications at finite temperature. It provides an alternative to the shifted Krylov method proposed for Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov theory (Jin et al., 2017; Kha and Nakatsukasa, 2020), and avoids the Wigner-Seitz approximation commonly used for inhomogeneous nuclear matter (Negele and Vautherin, 1973). The method enables fully quantum mean-field calculations without spatial symmetry assumptions for hot nuclei and non-uniform nuclear matter, and is suitable for massively parallel computing with order-N scaling for large systems.
