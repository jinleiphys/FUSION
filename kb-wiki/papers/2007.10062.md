# Misconceptions on Effective Field Theories and spontaneous symmetry breaking: Response to Ellis' article

> In an earlier paper~\cite{Luu:2019jmb} we discussed emergence from the context of effective field theories, particularly as related to the fields of particle and nuclear physics. We argued on the side of reductionism and weak emergence. George Ellis has critiqued our exposition in~\cite{Ellis:2020vij}, and here we provide our response to his critiques. Many of his critiques are based on incorrect assumptions related to the formalism of effective field theories and we attempt to correct these issues here. We also comment on other statements made in his paper. Important to note is that our response is to his critiques made in archive versions arXiv:2004.13591v1-5 [physics.hist-ph]. That is, versions 1-5 of this archive post. Version 6 has similar content as versions 1-5, but versions 7-9 are seemingly a different paper altogether (even with a different title).

## Key claim

The authors argue that George Ellis's critiques of their earlier work on emergence and effective field theories (EFTs) are based on incorrect assumptions about the EFT formalism, and that EFTs can indeed capture spontaneous symmetry breaking (SSB) and topological effects, contrary to Ellis's claims that these phenomena constitute strong emergence.

## Method

The paper responds point-by-point to Ellis's criticisms, correcting what the authors identify as misconceptions about EFTs. They explain how EFTs are derived from fundamental theories (e.g., NRQED from QED), how SSB is captured in EFTs via Goldstone's theorem (e.g., pions in chiral perturbation theory, phonons in crystals), and how topological effects are incorporated (e.g., the Wess-Zumino-Witten action for anomalies, Chern-Simons terms for the quantum Hall effect). They also address Ellis's distinction between micro and macro SSB and his challenge to derive high-temperature superconductivity bottom-up.

## Key numbers

* The breakdown scale of the Fermi theory of weak interactions is about 80 GeV, given by the mass of the heavy vector bosons.
* The QCD vacuum theta parameter is smaller than 10^{-11}, deduced from the experimental upper limit on the neutron electric dipole moment (Abel 2020).
* The scale of supersymmetry is about 1 TeV, while the chiral Lagrangian scale is well below 1 GeV, bridging about 3 orders of magnitude via a succession of EFTs.
* The Hoyle state in 12C was calculated ab initio in 2011 (Epelbaum 2011), a problem previously considered intractable.

## Context

This paper is a direct response to Ellis (2020), who critiqued the authors' earlier work (Luu and Meissner 2019) on emergence and reductionism in EFTs. The authors defend the position of weak emergence and reductionism against Ellis's arguments for strong emergence, correcting what they see as misunderstandings of the EFT formalism. The work enables further philosophical and scientific debate on whether emergent phenomena in condensed matter, nuclear, and particle physics can be derived from fundamental theories, and clarifies the role of EFTs in describing SSB and topological effects across multiple fields.
