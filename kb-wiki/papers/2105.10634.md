# Nuclear masses in extended kernel ridge regression with odd-even effects

> The kernel ridge regression (KRR) approach is extended to include the odd-even effects in nuclear mass predictions by remodulating the kernel function without introducing new weight parameters and inputs in the training network. By taking the WS4 mass model as an example, the mass for each nucleus in the nuclear chart is predicted with the extended KRR network, which is trained with the mass model residuals, i.e., deviations between experimental and calculated masses, of other nuclei with known masses. The resultant root-mean-square mass deviation from the available experimental data for the 2353 nuclei with $Z\ge8$ and $N\ge8$ can be reduced to 128 keV, which provides the most precise mass model from machine learning approaches so far. Moreover, the extended KRR approach can avoid the risk of worsening the mass predictions for nuclei at large extrapolation distances, and meanwhile, it provides a smooth extrapolation behavior with respect to the odd and even extrapolation distances.

# Nuclear masses in extended kernel ridge regression with odd-even effects

## Key claim
By remodulating the kernel function rather than adding new weight parameters or inputs, the extended kernel ridge regression (KRR) approach reduces the root-mean-square mass deviation from experimental data to 128 keV for 2353 nuclei with Z≥8 and N≥8, which is claimed as the most precise mass model from machine learning approaches so far; however, the improvement is demonstrated only for the WS4 mass model as the base theoretical model, and the extrapolation advantage is specifically that the method avoids worsening predictions at large extrapolation distances while providing smooth behavior with respect to odd and even extrapolation distances.

## Method
The authors extend the standard KRR approach by adding a second Gaussian kernel term K_oe that is nonzero only when two nuclei share the same parity of proton and neutron numbers, thereby encoding odd-even effects directly into the similarity measure. The network is trained on mass residuals (deviations between experimental masses from AME2012 and theoretical masses from the WS4 mass model) for 2353 nuclei with Z≥8 and N≥8, using leave-one-out cross-validation to optimize the four hyperparameters (σ=1.25, λ=0.05, σ_oe=2.65, λ_oe=0.15). The predicted mass for any nucleus is the WS4 mass plus the extended KRR correction S(N,Z).

## Key numbers
- Root-mean-square mass deviation for 2353 nuclei (Z≥8, N≥8) reduced from 298 keV (WS4 alone) to 199 keV (standard KRR) to 128 keV (extended KRR with odd-even effects).
- For heavy nuclei with Z > 26, the rms deviation of the extended KRR model is 100 keV.
- For the extended KRR model, mass deviations are smaller than 100 keV for more than 70% of nuclei, and smaller than 200 keV for more than 90% of nuclei; only 200 nuclei have deviations larger than 200 keV.
- Optimized hyperparameters: σ=1.25, λ=0.05, σ_oe=2.65, λ_oe=0.15.

## Context
This work extends the kernel ridge regression approach previously applied to nuclear masses by Wu et al. (2020) to explicitly incorporate odd-even effects, which had been included in the radial basis function (RBF) approach by Niu et al. (2016) through building and training additional networks, and in the Bayesian neural network (BNN) approach by Niu et al. (2018) through additional inputs. The key advance is that the extended KRR achieves higher precision (128 keV versus approximately 200 keV for prior machine learning approaches) without increasing the number of weight parameters, and it avoids the problematic extrapolation behavior seen in RBF and BNN approaches, where predictions for nuclei far from the known region can worsen relative to the base mass model. The method enables more reliable mass predictions for neutron-rich nuclei relevant to r-process simulations, with the rms deviation for heavy nuclei reaching the chaos-related unpredictability limit discussed by Barea et al. (2005) and Niu et al. (2018).
