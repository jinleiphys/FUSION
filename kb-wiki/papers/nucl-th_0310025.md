# Strangeness production via electromagnetic probes: 40 years later

> A brief review of the associated strangeness electromagnetic production is presented. Very recent K+ Lambda photoproduction data on the proton from threshold up to E=2.6 GeV are interpreted within a chiral constituent quark formalism, which embodies all known nucleonic and hyperonic resonances. The preliminary results of this work are reported here.

## Key claim

A chiral constituent quark model, based on broken SU(6)⊗O(3) symmetry, can reproduce the very recent K+Λ photoproduction data from threshold up to Eγlab = 2.6 GeV, but the discrepancies between the CLAS and SAPHIR data sets prevent strong conclusions on the underlying reaction mechanism.

## Method

The authors used a chiral constituent quark formalism derived from the low-energy QCD Lagrangian, incorporating all known nucleonic and hyperonic resonances. The model includes background terms (seagull, nucleon and hyperon Born terms, and excited u-channel hyperon resonances), high-mass resonances (above 2 GeV treated in compact form), and individual nucleon resonances from Table II. The adjustable parameters are the KYN coupling constants and one strength parameter C_N* per resonance, which measure the breaking of SU(6)⊗O(3) symmetry; configuration mixing between S11 and D13 states is expressed via mixing angles.

## Key numbers

- Data range: K+Λ photoproduction on the proton from threshold up to Eγlab = 2.6 GeV.
- Three excitation functions are shown at θKCM = 31.79°, 56.63°, and 123.37° as a function of total center-of-mass energy W.
- The S11(1650) resonance has mass 1.650 GeV and width 0.150 GeV; the D13(1520) has mass 1.520 GeV and width 0.130 GeV.
- The P13(1900) resonance has mass 1.900 GeV and width 0.500 GeV; the F15(2000) has mass 2.000 GeV and width 0.490 GeV.
- The F15(1680) resonance has mass 1.680 GeV and width 0.130 GeV.
- The P11(1440) Roper resonance has mass 1.440 GeV and width 0.150 GeV; the P11(1710) has mass 1.710 GeV and width 0.100 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the isobaric models of the 1980s and 1990s (e.g., Thom (year not given), Adelseck and Saghai (year not given), and others) that used Feynman diagrammatic techniques with s-, u-, and t-channel contributions but suffered from too many free parameters. The chiral constituent quark approach reduces the number of free parameters by using SU(6)⊗O(3) symmetry predictions, enabling a meaningful study of resonance content. The results enable future coupled-channel treatments and the extraction of elementary operators for electroproduction on both protons and nuclei, as well as providing input for studies of strangeness production on nuclei.
