# Uses of Effective Field Theory in Lattice QCD

> Several physical problems in particle physics, nuclear physics, and astrophysics require information from non-perturbative QCD to gain a full understanding. In some cases the most reliable technique for quantitative results is to carry out large-scale numerical calculations in lattice gauge theory. As in any numerical technique, there are several sources of uncertainty. This chapter explains how effective field theories are used to keep them under control and, then, obtain a sensible error bar. After a short survey of the numerical technique, we explain why effective field theories are necessary and useful. Then four important cases are reviewed: Symanzik's effective field theory of lattice spacing effects; heavy-quark effective theory as a tool for controlling discretization effects of heavy quarks; chiral perturbation theory as a tool for reaching the chiral limit; and a general field theory of hadrons for deriving finite volume corrections.

## Key claim

Effective field theories (EFTs) are necessary to control systematic uncertainties in lattice QCD calculations, but the abstract's claim that they provide "a sensible error bar" glosses over the fact that the low-energy constants of the quenched approximation cannot be rigorously related to those of QCD, and that common practice in quenched calculations circumvents unphysical chiral logs by assuming little error from quenching at strange-quark mass scales and extrapolating linearly.

## Method

The paper reviews four EFT frameworks applied to lattice QCD: Symanzik's effective field theory for lattice-spacing effects; heavy-quark effective theory (HQET) and non-relativistic QCD (NRQCD) for controlling discretization effects of heavy quarks; chiral perturbation theory (χPT) for extrapolating light quark masses to the physical chiral limit; and a general field theory of hadrons for deriving finite-volume corrections. For the chiral case specifically, the author examines how quenched χPT (with no sea quarks, n_f=0) and partially quenched χPT (with valence and sea quarks at different masses) modify the chiral-logarithm structure of observables such as the B-meson decay constant, and how the reduced chiral symmetry of Wilson and staggered fermion formulations requires modified short-distance coefficients in the chiral Lagrangian.

## Key numbers

- Light quark masses in lattice calculations are typically run in the range 0.2 m_s ≲ m_q ≲ m_s, where m_s is the strange quark mass.
- The concrete scale arising in the χPT expansion is 4π f_π = 1.65 GeV.
- The pion mass used for determining the average light quark mass is m_π = 140 MeV from experiment.
- For heavy quarks, even when Λa ~ 0.1–0.3, m_b a ~ 1–2 and m_c a is about one-third of that.
- In free field theory for Wilson fermions, the ratio m_1/m_2 = 1 − (2/3) m_1² a² + (1/2) m_1³ a³ + …; for m a = 0.8 the two shown terms contribute −0.46 and +0.26.
- The separation scale μ in χPT is usually taken to be around 1 GeV.

## Context

This chapter extends the program of using EFTs to control lattice QCD uncertainties, building on the foundational work of Symanzik for lattice-spacing effects, Gasser and Leutwyler (1984) for chiral perturbation theory, and Caswell and Lepage (1986) and Eichten and collaborators (1987, 1990) for heavy-quark effective theories. The paper enables future unquenched QCD calculations by providing the theoretical framework for partially quenched χPT, where low-energy constants can be related to QCD (Sharpe, 2000), and by clarifying how the Fermilab method (El-Khadra et al., 1997) and the non-relativistic interpretation of Wilson fermions (Kronfeld, 2000) allow heavy quarks to be treated on the lattice even when m a is not small.
