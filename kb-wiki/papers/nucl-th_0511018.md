# Unique features of structure in an odd-proton $N \approx Z$ nucleus $^{69}$As

> We apply a large-scale shell model to the study of proton-rich odd-mass nuclei with $N \approx Z$. Calculations predict unexpected structure in the $^{69}$As nucleus for which a detailed experiment was recently performed. In this odd-proton nucleus, one neutron competes with one proton for occupying the high-$j$ intruder orbit $g_{9/2}$ in the $9/2_1^+$ state and almost solely occupies the $g_{9/2}$ orbit in other low-lying positive-parity states. The T=0, J=9 one-proton-one-neutron alignment takes place in the negative-parity states with medium-high spins. A unique coexistence of two lowest bands with positive and negative signs of spectroscopic quadrupole moments is predicted.

# Unique features of structure in an odd-proton $N \approx Z$ nucleus $^{69}$As

## Key claim

Large-scale shell model calculations predict that in the odd-proton $N \approx Z$ nucleus $^{69}$As, one neutron competes with one proton for occupying the high-$j$ intruder orbit $g_{9/2}$ in the $9/2_1^+$ state, while one neutron almost solely occupies the $g_{9/2}$ orbit in other low-lying positive-parity states; the paper hedges that the calculated ground state $3/2_1^-$ is inconsistent with the experimental ground state $5/2^-$, suggesting an effect missing in the model.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale shell model calculations in the $(p_{3/2}, f_{5/2}, p_{1/2}, g_{9/2})$ model space using an extended $P+QQ$ Hamiltonian with single-particle energies, monopole corrections, $J=0$ pairing, quadrupole-quadrupole, and octupole-octupole forces. For low-spin states with dimensions exceeding $2 \times 10^8$, the Hamiltonian matrix was truncated by limiting the configuration space, and an energy variance extrapolation method was applied to obtain exact energies within 1 keV accuracy. The calculations used effective charges $e_{\rm eff}^\pi = 1.5e$ and $e_{\rm eff}^\nu = 0.5e$ for $B(E2)$ and spectroscopic quadrupole moments.

## Key numbers

- Single-particle energies: $\varepsilon_{p3/2} = 0.0$, $\varepsilon_{f5/2} = 0.77$, $\varepsilon_{p1/2} = 1.11$, and $\varepsilon_{g9/2} = 2.50$ MeV
- Force strengths: $g_0 = 0.27(64/A)$, $\chi_2 = 0.25(64/A)^{5/3}$, and $\chi_3 = 0.05(64/A)^2$ in MeV
- Monopole corrections: $\Delta k^{T=1}_{\rm mc}(p_{3/2},f_{5/2}) = \Delta k^{T=1}_{\rm mc}(p_{3/2},p_{1/2}) = -0.3$ MeV and $\Delta k^{T=1}_{\rm mc}(f_{5/2},p_{1/2}) = -0.4$ MeV; $\Delta k^{T=0}_{\rm mc}(a,g_{9/2}) = -0.18$ MeV for $a = p_{3/2}, f_{5/2}, p_{1/2}$
- $B(E2:25/2_3^+ \rightarrow 21/2_2^+) = 12.7$ W.u. compared to $B(E2:25/2_3^+ \rightarrow 21/2_1^+) = 0.15$ W.u.
- $B(M1:15/2_1^+ \rightarrow 13/2_1^+) = 2.96$ W.u., $B(E2:15/2_1^+ \rightarrow 13/2_1^+) = 12.6$ W.u.
- $B(E2:11/2_1^+ \rightarrow 9/2_1^+) = 7.3$ W.u. and $B(M1:11/2_1^+ \rightarrow 9/2_1^+) = 0.22$ W.u.
- $B(E3:9/2_1^+ \rightarrow 3/2^-,5/2^-,7/2^-) < 0.08$ W.u. and $B(M2:9/2_1^+ \rightarrow 5/2^-,7/2^-) < 0.38$ W.u.
- Spectroscopic quadrupole moments for band 6A: $Q_{\rm sp}(3/2_1^-) = 22$ $e$ fm$^2$, $Q_{\rm sp}(7/2_2^-) = 18$ $e$ fm$^2$, $Q_{\rm sp}(11/2_2^-) = 41$ $e$ fm$^2$, $Q_{\rm sp}(15/2_2^-) = 13$ $e$ fm$^2$
- Spectroscopic quadrupole moments for band 8A: $Q_{\rm sp}(5/2_1^-) = -30$ $e$ fm$^2$, $Q_{\rm sp}(9/2_1^-) = -19$ $e$ fm$^2$, $Q_{\rm sp}(13/2_1^-) = -17$ $e$ fm$^2$

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous shell model studies of $N \approx Z$ nuclei in the $A=64-70$ region, including even-even nuclei like $^{64}$Ge to $^{68}$Se (Hasegawa 2001, Kaneko 2002, Hasegawa 2003) and the odd-odd nucleus $^{66}$As (Hasegawa 2004). The calculations directly address the recent experiment by Stefanescu (2005) on $^{69}$As, which provided detailed band structures up to high spins. The paper demonstrates that $T=0$, $J=9$ one-proton-one-neutron alignment in the $g_{9/2}$ orbit, previously seen in even-even and odd-odd nuclei, also occurs in odd-mass nuclei, and predicts a unique coexistence of two lowest bands with opposite signs of spectroscopic quadrupole moments distinct from the oblate-prolate shape coexistence seen in $^{68}$Se. This enables future studies of neighboring odd-mass $N \approx Z$ nuclei such as $^{72}$Kr, $^{72}$Br, and $^{73}$Br.
