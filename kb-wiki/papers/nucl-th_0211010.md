# Scaling behavior at high p_T and the p/pi ratio

> We first show that the pions produced at high $p_T$ in heavy-ion collisions over a wide range of high energies exhibit a scaling behavior when the distributions are plotted in terms of a scaling variable. We then use the recombination model to calculate the scaling quark distribution just before hadronization. From the quark distribution it is then possible to calculate the proton distribution at high $p_T$, also in the framework of the recombination model. The resultant $p/π$ ratio exceeds one in the intermediate $p_T$ region where data exist, but the scaling result for the proton distribution is not reliable unless $p_T$ is high enough to be insensitive to the scale-breaking mass effects.

# Scaling Behavior at High \(p_T\) and the \(p/\pi\) Ratio

## Key claim

The authors show that pions produced at high transverse momentum in heavy-ion collisions exhibit a scaling behavior when plotted in terms of a scaling variable \(z = p_T/K\), and then use the recombination model to calculate the proton distribution, finding a \(p/\pi\) ratio exceeding one in the intermediate \(p_T\) region (2–3 GeV/c); however, the scaling result for the proton distribution is not reliable unless \(p_T\) is high enough to be insensitive to scale-breaking mass effects, and the calculation is only trustworthy for \(p_T > 3\) GeV/c.

## Method

The authors first analyze experimental \(\pi^0\) data from PHENIX (AuAu at \(\sqrt{s}=130\) and 200 GeV) and WA98 (PbPb at \(\sqrt{s}=17\) GeV) to demonstrate that the scaled distribution \(\Phi(z) = K^2 f(p_T,s)\) forms a universal curve when plotted against \(z = p_T/K\), with \(K\) adjusted for each energy. They then apply the recombination model, assuming factorizable quark and antiquark distributions \(F_q(z)\) and \(F_{\bar{q}}(z)\) with identical \(z\)-dependence but different normalization based on the \(\bar{p}/p\) ratio, to extract the quark distribution from the pion data. Using this quark distribution and the valon model for the proton structure, they compute the proton distribution and the resulting \(p/\pi\) ratio.

## Key numbers

* The scaling variable \(K\) takes values \(K = 1\) GeV at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV, \(K = 0.9\) GeV at \(\sqrt{s}=130\) GeV, and \(K = 0.717\) GeV at \(\sqrt{s}=17\) GeV.
* The universal scaling function is parametrized as \(\Phi(z) = 1500 (z^2 + 2)^{-4.9}\).
* The best linear fit for \(K(s)\) is \(K(s) = 0.69 + 1.55 \times 10^{-3} \sqrt{s}\), with \(\sqrt{s}\) in GeV.
* The extracted quark distribution is \(F_q(z) = 90 (z^2 + z + 1)^{-4.65}\).
* The \(\bar{p}/p\) ratio for central collisions is taken as 0.7, giving \(F_{\bar{q}}(z)/F_q(z) = 0.7^{1/3}\).
* The proton valon distribution parameters are \(\alpha = 1.755\) and \(\beta = 1.05\), with normalization constant \(R_p^0 = 0.057\).

## Context

This work extends the recombination model, previously applied only to the fragmentation region (Hwa and Yang, 1985; Hwa, 1980), to the central region at high \(p_T\) in heavy-ion collisions. The scaling behavior discovered is analogous to KNO scaling of multiplicity distributions in \(pp\) collisions (Koba, Nielsen, and Olesen, 1972). The finding that the \(p/\pi\) ratio exceeds one in the intermediate \(p_T\) region addresses the "proton anomaly" observed by PHENIX, which contradicted expectations from jet fragmentation where proton mass suppresses production; the authors contrast their approach with the gluonic baryon junction mechanism proposed by others. The results enable predictions for proton production at higher \(p_T\) that can be tested at RHIC and future LHC experiments.
