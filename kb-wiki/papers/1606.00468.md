# Multi-Nucleon Short-Range Correlation Model for Nuclear Spectral Functions: I. Theoretical Framework

> We develop a theoretical approach for nuclear spectral functions at high missing momenta and removal energies based on the multi-nucleon short-range correlation~(SRC) model. The approach is based on the effective Feynman diagrammatic method which allows to account for the relativistic effects important in the SRC domain. In addition to two-nucleon SRC with center of mass motion we derive also the contribution of three-nucleon SRCs to the nuclear spectral functions. The latter is modeled based on the assumption that 3N SRCs are a product of two sequential short range NN interactions. This approach allowed us to express the 3N SRC part of the nuclear spectral function as a convolution of two NN SRCs. Thus the knowledge of 2N SRCs allows us to model both two- and three-nucleon SRC contributions to the spectral function. The derivations of the spectral functions are based on the two theoretical frameworks in evaluating covariant Feynman diagrams: In the first, referred as virtual nucleon approximation, we reduce Feynman diagrams to the time ordered noncovariant diagrams by evaluating nucleon spectators in the SRC at their positive energy poles, neglecting explicitly the contribution from vacuum diagrams. In the second approach, referred as light-front approximation, we formulate the boost invariant nuclear spectral function in the light-front reference frame in which case the vacuum diagrams are generally suppressed and the bound nucleon is described by its light-cone variables such as momentum fraction, transverse momentum and invariant mass.

## Key claim

The paper develops a theoretical framework for nuclear spectral functions at high missing momenta and removal energies based on multi-nucleon short-range correlations, with the hedge that the three-nucleon SRC contribution is modeled under the assumption that 3N SRCs arise from two sequential short-range NN interactions, allowing expression as a convolution of two 2N SRCs rather than from irreducible three-nucleon forces.

## Method

The authors use an effective Feynman diagrammatic method to calculate covariant forward scattering amplitudes for semi-inclusive high-energy reactions, then extract spectral functions from the imaginary parts of these amplitudes. They evaluate the diagrams within two distinct relativistic frameworks: the virtual nucleon approximation, where spectator nucleons are taken at their positive energy poles and the bound nucleon is treated as virtual, and the light-front approximation, where the spectral function is formulated in a boost-invariant light-front reference frame that suppresses vacuum diagram contributions. The spectral function is decomposed into mean-field, two-nucleon SRC, and three-nucleon SRC contributions, with the 3N SRC modeled as two sequential NN short-range interactions.

## Key numbers

- The Fermi momentum threshold for the SRC domain is given as \(k_F \sim 250\) MeV/c, below which mean-field contributions dominate
- The 2N SRC momentum range is specified as \(k_F < p \le 600-700\) MeV/c
- The \(pn\) SRC dominance over \(pp\) and \(nn\) correlations is observed for internal momentum range \(\sim 250-650\) MeV/c, with a factor of 20 dominance
- The threshold energy for \(\Delta\) excitations from irreducible three-nucleon forces is given as \(\sim 300\) MeV/c (missing energy scale)
- The average nucleon separation corresponding to tensor force dominance is \(\sim 1.1\) fm

## Context

This work extends the short-range correlation approach to nuclear wave functions previously developed by FS81, Ciofi (1991), Ciofi and Simula, and others, providing a relativistic framework that accounts for effects important in the SRC domain where non-relativistic approximations become invalid. The model incorporates recent experimental findings on 2N SRC properties from inclusive electron-nucleus scattering experiments (Kim1, Kim2, Fomin 2011) and semi-inclusive experiments (Eip3, Eip4) that established the strong \(pn\) dominance in SRCs. The framework enables practical calculations of nuclear spectral functions for interpreting data from neutrino-nucleus experiments, electron-ion colliders, and studies of nuclear medium modification effects such as the EMC effect, with numerical estimates and parameterizations promised in a follow-up paper.
