# Baseline predictions of elliptic flow and fluctuations at the RHIC Beam Energy Scan using response coefficients

> Currently the RHIC Beam Energy Scan is exploring a new region of the Quantum Chromodynamic phase diagram at large baryon densities that approaches nuclear astrophysics regimes. This provides an opportunity to study relativistic hydrodynamics in a regime where the net conserved charges of baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge play a role, which will significantly change the theoretical approach to simulating the baryon-dense Quark-Gluon Plasma. Here we detail many of the important changes needed to adapt both initial conditions and the medium to baryon-rich matter. Then, we make baseline predictions for the elliptical flow and fluctuations based on extrapolating the physics at LHC and top RHIC energies to support future analyses of where and how the new baryon-dense physics causes these extrapolations to break down. First we compare eccentricities across beam energies, exploring their underlying assumptions; we find the the extrapolated initial state is predicted to be nearly identical to that at AuAu $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV. Then the final flow harmonic predictions are based on linear+cubic response. We discuss preliminary STAR results in order to determine the implications that they have for linear+cubic response coefficients at the lowest beam energy of AuAu $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=7$ GeV.

# Baseline predictions of elliptic flow and fluctuations at the RHIC Beam Energy Scan using response coefficients

## Key claim

The authors extrapolate the high-energy (LHC and top RHIC) paradigm of linear+cubic hydrodynamic response from initial-state eccentricities to final-state elliptic flow down to lower beam energies, predicting that the initial-state eccentricities are nearly identical across beam energies and that any significant deviations in the measured flow at low energies would signal the onset of new baryon-dense physics. The baseline predictions are explicitly intended as a reference to identify where and how these extrapolations break down, rather than as definitive low-energy predictions.

## Method

The authors use the Trento model for initial conditions (with parameter p varied to mimic IP-Glasma-like, MC-KLN-like, and Glauber-like scenarios) combined with the 2+1D event-by-event viscous relativistic hydrodynamics code v-USPhydro, using parameters τ0=0.6 fm, η/s≈0.05, ζ/s=0, TFO=150 MeV, and the PDG16+/EOS21 lattice QCD equation of state. They extract linear+cubic response coefficients κ1,n and κ2,n from full hydrodynamic simulations at top beam energies, then extrapolate these coefficients along with the eccentricities and residuals down to lower beam energies to predict v2{2} and v2{4}/v2{2} for AuAu collisions at 54 GeV, 27 GeV, and 7 GeV.

## Key numbers

- The inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross sections used in Trento are: 42.3 mb at √sNN=200 GeV, 35 mb at 54 GeV, 33.2 mb at 27 GeV, and 31.2 mb at 7.7 GeV (Table 1).
- For MC-KLN+v-USPhydro, η/s must change significantly with beam energy: η/s=0.05 for PbPb 5.02 TeV, η/s=0.11 for PbPb 2.76 TeV, and η/s=0.08 for AuAu 200 GeV.
- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio extracted at top beam energies is η/s≈0.05-0.2.
- The initialization time used is τ0=0.6 fm and freeze-out temperature TFO=150 MeV (Trento+v-USPhydro) or TFO=120 MeV (MC-KLN+v-USPhydro).
- The inelastic cross section decreases only ~25% over two orders of magnitude in √sNN (from 42.3 mb at 200 GeV to 31.2 mb at 7.7 GeV).

## Context

This work extends the linear+cubic response framework for elliptic flow developed in Noronha-Hostler (2015) and the Trento initial condition model of Moreland (2014) and Bernhard (2016) to the RHIC Beam Energy Scan regime. It provides baseline predictions against which future data from Beam Energy Scan II can be compared to identify the onset of baryon-dense physics, including baryon stopping, finite chemical potentials, and possible critical point effects. The approach enables disentangling whether deviations from the baseline arise from changes in the initial state or final state (medium properties), and complements ongoing work by Du (2019), Denicol (2018), and Batyuk (2017) to incorporate finite-density effects into hydrodynamic models.
