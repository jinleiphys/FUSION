# Resonance Structure and Polarizability of the nucleon

> The main features of the resonance structure of the nucleon are discussed, particular with regard to the helicity dependence of real and virtual photoabsorption. The dependence of the partial cross sections on both the resonance helicity amplitudes and the electromagnetic multipoles is outlined. The general structure of the Compton tensor is reviewed and applied to the special cases of real to real, virtual to real, and virtual to virtual Compton scattering. Recent theoretical developments in dispersion relations are presented, together with a short overview regarding static, dynamical, and generalized polarizabilities of the nucleon as well as the status of the Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule and related integrals.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the resonance structure and polarizabilities of the nucleon, arguing that the helicity dependence of photoabsorption and Compton scattering amplitudes can be systematically described by dispersion relations, with the caveat that unsubtracted dispersion relations at constant t require modeling of higher-energy contributions via t-channel poles (such as the sigma meson), while subtracted or hyperbolic dispersion relations improve convergence but introduce limitations at low energies.

## Method

The paper analyzes the helicity structure of real and virtual photoabsorption cross sections on the nucleon, relating them to resonance helicity amplitudes (A_{1/2}, A_{3/2}, S_{1/2}) and electromagnetic multipoles (E, M, S). It then develops the Compton scattering tensor for real Compton scattering (RCS), virtual Compton scattering (VCS), and doubly virtual Compton scattering (VVCS) using Lorentz and gauge invariance, crossing symmetry, parity, and time reversal invariance. Dispersion relations are applied to construct the forward scattering amplitudes from integrals over photoabsorption cross sections, with extensions to fixed-angle (hyperbolic) dispersion relations for non-forward kinematics.

## Key numbers

- Total photoabsorption cross section levels off at about 120 μb above the third resonance region
- At total c.m. energy W ≈ 200 GeV, the cross section increases slowly in accordance with soft pomeron exchange
- The Δ(1232) σ_{3/2}-dominated cross section remains nearly constant up to Q² ≈ 0.5 GeV² but drops strongly thereafter
- The helicity difference σ_{3/2} − σ_{1/2} in the second and third resonance regions becomes negative already at Q² ≈ 0.5 GeV²
- Experimental value (α − β)_{exp} = [10.7 ± 0.6 (stat) ± 0.8 (syst)] × 10⁻⁴ fm³ from TAPS at MAMI
- Hyperbolic dispersion relation result: (α − β)_{hyp} = 10.9 × 10⁻⁴ fm³, from s-channel contribution −5.6 and t-channel contribution 16.5 (same units)
- Unsubtracted DR at t=const predict α − β ≈ −6 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ for πN intermediate states up to E_γ = 1.5 GeV
- Forward spin polarizability γ₀(0) ≈ [−1.01 ± 0.08 (stat) ± 0.10 (syst)] × 10⁻⁴ fm⁴ from the GDH experiment at MAMI
- Longitudinal-transverse polarizability δ_{LT}(0) ≈ 1.4 × 10⁻⁴ fm⁴ from dispersion relations
- First unpolarized VCS data obtained at Q² = 0.33 GeV² (MAMI) and at 1 GeV² < Q² < 2 GeV² (JLab)

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of Drechsel (2001) on virtual photon absorption and the Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule (Gerasimov 1965, Drell and Hearn 1965) by incorporating recent developments in dispersion relations for Compton scattering. The work builds on the low-energy theorem of Low (1954) and Gell-Mann and Goldberger (1954), Baldin's sum rule (Baldin 1960), and the forward spin polarizability sum rule (Gell-Mann, Goldberger, and Thirring 1954). The hyperbolic dispersion relation approach of Pasquini (2002) resolves the long-standing puzzle of the large α − β difference seen in backward Compton scattering, which unsubtracted fixed-t dispersion relations could only reproduce by invoking a sigma meson pole. The generalized polarizabilities extracted from VCS at MAMI (Roche 2000) and JLab (Fonvieille 2002) enable spatial mapping of the nucleon's electromagnetic response, while the generalized GDH integral measured at JLab (de Jager 2002) shows a transition between resonance-dominated and deep-inelastic regimes.
