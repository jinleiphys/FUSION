# Stochastic Methods for Zero Energy Quantum Scattering

> We investigate the use of stochastic methods for zero energy quantum scattering based on a path integral approach. With the application to the scattering of a projectile from a nuclear many body target in mind, we use the potential scattering of a particle as a test for the accuracy and efficiency of several methods. To be able to deal with complex potentials, we introduce a path sampling action and a modified scattering observable. The approaches considered are the random walk, where the points of a path are sequentially generated, and the Langevin algorithm, which updates an entire path. Several improvements are investigated. A cluster algorithm for dealing with scattering problems is finally proposed, which shows the best accuracy and stability.

## Key claim

Stochastic path-integral methods can be used to compute zero-energy quantum scattering amplitudes, including for complex potentials, by introducing a real sampling action and a modified observable; however, the accuracy and stability of the simplest random-walk and Langevin approaches are insufficient for many-body targets, and only the newly proposed cluster algorithm achieves both accuracy and stability.

## Method

The authors evaluate the zero-energy scattering length \(f_0\) for a particle scattering from a Gaussian potential \(V(\vec{r}) = (U_0 + iW_0) \exp(-\vec{r}^2/(2b^2))\) using a path-integral representation of the Euclidean-time projector \(\hat{\rho}(\beta) = \exp(-\beta\hat{H})/\mathcal{N}_\beta\). They test several stochastic sampling methods: a free-action random walk, a random walk with path replication (sources and sinks), guided random walks using an approximate wavefunction, Langevin and hybrid algorithms that update entire paths, and a newly developed cluster algorithm. For complex potentials, a real reference action \(\tilde{\mathcal{S}}\) is used to generate paths, and the ratio of the full to reference scattering amplitude is computed via a complex observable \(\mathcal{O}[\vec{R}] = \exp(-\Delta\mathcal{S})\).

## Context

This work extends earlier path-integral studies of potential scattering by Lenz (1995) and addresses the need for efficient stochastic methods to compute exact scattering amplitudes for nuclear many-body targets, where the frozen-nucleus picture revealed projectile trapping by clusters (StoLe, StoLe2). The paper systematically compares random-walk, Langevin, hybrid, and cluster algorithms for both real and complex potentials, with the cluster algorithm emerging as the most promising candidate for future applications to antiproton-nucleus scattering (Koh86, Bat89) and other low-energy nuclear scattering problems where target dynamics must be included.
