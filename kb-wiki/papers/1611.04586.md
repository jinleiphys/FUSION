# Quantifying Chiral Magnetic Effect from Anomalous-Viscous Fluid Dynamics

> The Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) is a macroscopic manifestation of fundamental chiral anomaly in a many-body system of chiral fermions, and emerges as anomalous transport current in the fluid dynamics framework. Experimental observation of CME is of great interest and has been reported in Dirac and Weyl semimetals. Significant efforts have also been made to look for CME in heavy ion collisions. Critically needed for such search, is the theoretical prediction for CME signal. In this paper we report a first quantitative modeling framework, the Anomalous Viscous Fluid Dynamics (AVFD), which computes the evolution of fermion currents on top of realistic bulk evolution in heavy ion collisions and simultaneously accounts for both anomalous and normal viscous transport effects. The AVFD allows a quantitative understanding of the generation and evolution of CME-induced charge separation during hydrodynamic stage as well as its dependence on theoretical ingredients. With reasonable estimates of key parameters, the AVFD simulations provide the first phenomenologically successful explanation of the measured signal in 200AGeV AuAu collisions.

# Quantifying Chiral Magnetic Effect from Anomalous-Viscous Fluid Dynamics

## Key claim

The Anomalous Viscous Fluid Dynamics (AVFD) framework provides the first phenomenologically successful explanation of the measured CME-induced charge separation signal in 200AGeV AuAu collisions, but only under the plausible assumption that the magnetic field lifetime is short but comparable to the initial hydrodynamic time (tau_B approximately equal to tau_0 = 0.6 fm/c) and with a saturation scale Q_s^2 in the range of 1 to 1.5 GeV^2.

## Method

The authors developed the AVFD framework, which solves fluid dynamical equations for chiral fermion currents (right-handed and left-handed currents for u and d quark flavors) as perturbations on top of a 2+1D second-order viscous hydrodynamic bulk evolution (VISHNU) with Monte-Carlo Glauber initial conditions. The framework simultaneously accounts for both anomalous transport (the B-field-driven CME current term) and normal viscous transport (diffusion with second-order relaxation), using lattice-computed quark number susceptibilities and treating the magnetic field with a parametrized time dependence B(tau) = B_0 / (1 + (tau/tau_B)^2). The initial axial charge density is set proportional to entropy density via a ratio lambda_5 derived from glasma flux tube estimates, and final hadron observables are computed via the Cooper-Frye procedure on the hydrodynamic freeze-out surface.

## Key numbers

- The initial hydrodynamic evolution time tau_0 = 0.60 fm/c is used as the starting point for AVFD simulations.
- The magnetic field lifetime tau_B is assumed to be comparable to tau_0 (tau_B approximately equal to 0.6 fm/c), as a plausible assumption.
- The saturation scale Q_s^2 is varied in the range of 1 to 1.5 GeV^2 for RHIC 200AGeV collisions, controlling the initial axial charge estimate.
- For a "canonical choice" of diffusion coefficient sigma/T = 0.3 and relaxation time tau_r T = 0.5, the computed charge separation signal a^{ch}_1 shows a +/- 5% deviation band around this choice.
- The computed azimuthal correlation observable (H_SS - H_OS) for various centrality bins at 200AGeV AuAu collisions shows quantitative agreement with STAR data for Q_s^2 values near 1.5 GeV^2.

## Context

This work extends earlier attempts at anomalous transport modeling in the ideal hydrodynamic limit (Yin 2015, Yee 2013, Hirono 2014, Hongo 2013) by incorporating viscous effects for the first time, allowing quantification of theoretical uncertainty from normal transport parameters. The AVFD framework enables comparison with the STAR Collaboration's extracted flow-independent H_SS/OS correlation (STAR 2015), which is considered the best available estimate for potential CME signal after background subtraction. The tool enables future studies of the Chiral Magnetic Wave, finite-mass effects on kaon separation, and event-by-event simulations, with definitive conclusions requiring further narrowing of uncertainties in magnetic field lifetime and initial axial charge.
