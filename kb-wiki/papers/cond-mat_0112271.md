# Identifying Complexity by Means of Matrices

> Complexity is an interdisciplinary concept which, first of all, addresses the question of how order emerges out of randomness. For many reasons matrices provide a very practical and powerful tool in approaching and quantifying the related characteristics. Based on several natural complex dynamical systems, like the strongly interacting quantum many-body systems, the human brain and the financial markets, by relating empirical observations to the random matrix theory and quantifying deviations in term of a reduced dimensionality, we present arguments in favour of the statement that complexity is a pheomenon at the edge between collectivity and chaos.

# Identifying Complexity by Means of Matrices

## Key claim
Complexity emerges at the interface between chaos and collectivity, and can be quantified by analyzing deviations from random matrix theory (RMT) predictions in the eigenvalue spectra of correlation matrices, where the deviations are characterized by a reduced effective dimensionality of the matrix.

## Method
The authors construct correlation matrices W = XY^T from empirical data (n observations, each an m-dimensional vector) and compare their eigenvalue distributions to the predictions of random matrix theory, specifically the Wishart ensemble for symmetric matrices and the Ginibre ensemble for asymmetric matrices. Deviations from RMT are quantified by identifying a small number k of large eigenvalues separated from the bulk, corresponding to coherent patterns, and by fitting the distribution of large off-diagonal matrix elements to a modified Bessel function form P(w) that depends on an effective dimensionality parameter M.

## Key numbers
- For the Wishart ensemble, the theoretical eigenvalue bounds are λ_min/max = 1 + 1/Q ± 2√(1/Q) where Q = m/n ≥ 1.
- In nuclear shell-model calculations with two-body interactions drawn from a Gaussian ensemble, the distribution of off-diagonal Hamiltonian matrix elements for J > 0 is reproduced by the formula with M ≈ 2, indicating a strong reduction of effective dimensionality.
- The ground state energy gaps for J > 0 are larger than for J = 0 when measured relative to the average level spacing, and J > 0 ground states are more orderly as quantified by information entropy.

## Context
This work extends the application of random matrix theory, originally developed in nuclear physics by Wigner (1950s) and Mehta (1960s), to three distinct complex systems: strongly interacting quantum many-body systems (atomic nuclei), human brain activity measured via electro- or magnetoencephalography, and financial markets (stock market data). The paper builds on prior work by Drozdz (2000) on nuclear Hamiltonian matrices, Kwapien (2000) on asymmetric correlation matrices for brain signals, and Laloux (1999) and Plerou (1999) on financial correlation matrices. The approach enables identification of coherent patterns in noisy systems by quantifying the gap between the largest eigenvalues and the RMT-predicted bulk, and suggests that complexity requires coexistence of coherence, chaos, and a gap between them.
