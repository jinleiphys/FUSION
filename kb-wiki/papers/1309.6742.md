# Quark contribution for center domain in heavy ion collisions

> The center domain structure is revisited with introduction of quark contribution to understand phenomenology in high-energy heavy ion collisions. We show the quark contribution may allow metastable states that would lead to a natural and consistent explanation for the temperature dependences of color opacity and viscosity. We also argue the possibility of indirect observations of the center domain structure in experiments due to a topological critical temperature.

## Key claim

The quark contribution to the center domain structure may allow metastable states that provide a natural explanation for the temperature dependences of color opacity and viscosity in the quark-gluon plasma, and there may exist a topological critical temperature that separates the strongly-coupled and perturbative QCD pictures.

## Method

The authors compute the perturbative one-loop effective potential for gluons and massless quarks in a background field for the time-like component of the vector potential, using the fourth Bernoulli polynomial. They analyze the flavor-dependence of the effective potential normalized by \(T^4\) to identify metastable minima and their stability, and then relate the domain structure to the mean free path of partons and the temperature dependence of shear viscosity.

## Key numbers

- The reached initial temperature at RHIC is roughly estimated as 300-600 MeV.
- The shear viscosity-to-entropy ratio changes from \((\eta/s)_\text{RHIC} \simeq 0.12\) to \((\eta/s)_\text{LHC} \simeq 0.2\).
- The typical size of a center domain is \(R_d \sim 0.5\) fm.
- The radius of a proton is \(\sim 0.8\) fm.
- Metastable minima become unphysical above \(N_f \sim 3\) when they reach the \(F>0\) region.
- The effective number of flavors is already \(N_f \sim 2\)-3 for the energy scales of recent heavy ion experiments.

## Context

This work extends the center domain structure proposed by Asakawa (2012) by introducing quark contributions, which explicitly break the \({\mathbb Z}_3\) symmetry and allow metastable states. The picture provides a microscopic explanation for the transition from a strongly-coupled QGP with short mean free path to a weakly-coupled QGP with long mean free path, and suggests that a topological critical temperature could be observed as a sudden change in jet quenching and elliptic flow in future heavy ion experiments at higher energies.
