# Novel analysis of chiral loop effects in the generalized Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule

> We study the chiral loop corrections to the generalized Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule of the nucleon for finite photon virtuality in the framework of a Lorentz-invariant formulation of baryon chiral perturbation theory. We perform a complete one-loop calculation and obtain significant differences to previously found results based on the heavy baryon approach for the proton and neutron spin-dependent forward Compton amplitudes.

## Key claim

The chiral loop corrections to the generalized Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule for finite photon virtuality, calculated in a Lorentz-invariant formulation of baryon chiral perturbation theory, yield significant differences from earlier heavy baryon results for the proton and neutron spin-dependent forward Compton amplitudes, particularly because the 1/m expansion underlying the heavy baryon approach converges slowly in the spin sector.

## Method

The authors performed a complete one-loop (fourth order) calculation of the subtracted forward spin-dependent Compton amplitudes \(\bar{S}_1(0,Q^2)\) and \(\bar{S}_2(0,Q^2)\) for the proton and neutron using infrared regularized relativistic baryon chiral perturbation theory, which automatically fulfills analyticity constraints and resums recoil corrections to all orders. The calculation included tree-level graphs from the effective chiral Lagrangian up to fourth order and loop graphs with at most one insertion from the dimension-two Lagrangian, with the low-energy constants \(c_6\) and \(c_7\) fixed from the anomalous magnetic moments of the proton and neutron. The results were compared to the earlier heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory calculations of Ji et al. (2000) and Burkert and Ioffe (1994).

## Key numbers

* Slope of the proton structure function \(\bar{S}_1^p\) at \(Q^2=0\): \(d\bar{S}_1^p/dQ^2 = 0.8\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\) (sum of third-order contribution \(-0.4\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\) and fourth-order contribution \(+1.2\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\)), compared to the heavy baryon result of \(2.9\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\).
* Slope of the neutron structure function \(\bar{S}_1^n\) at \(Q^2=0\): \(d\bar{S}_1^n/dQ^2 = 0.2\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\) (third-order \(-1.7\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\) plus fourth-order \(+1.9\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\)), compared to the heavy baryon result of \(2.4\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\).
* Slope of the first moment difference \(\Gamma_1^{p-n}\) at \(Q^2=0\): \(d\Gamma_1^{p-n}/dQ^2 = (\kappa_n^2 - \kappa_p^2)/(8m^2) = 0.063\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-2}\).
* Slope of the combination \(\bar{S}_{12}^p\) for the proton at \(Q^2=0\): \(d\bar{S}_{12}^p/dQ^2 = 5.0\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\) (third-order \(3.2\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\) plus fourth-order \(1.8\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\)), compared to the heavy baryon result of \(-6.0\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\).
* Slope of the combination \(\bar{S}_{12}^n\) for the neutron at \(Q^2=0\): \(d\bar{S}_{12}^n/dQ^2 = -2.1\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\) (third-order \(-0.3\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\) plus fourth-order \(-1.8\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\)), compared to the heavy baryon result of \(-4.3\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-4}\).
* The proton first moment \(\Gamma_1^p(Q^2)\) crosses zero at \(Q^2 \simeq 0.33\ \mathrm{GeV}^2\), while \(\Gamma_1^n(Q^2)\) remains negative for all \(Q^2\) considered.

## Context

This work extends the chiral perturbation theory analysis of the generalized Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule previously performed by Ji et al. (2000) and Burkert and Ioffe (1994) using the heavy baryon formalism, and by Bernard, Kaiser, and Meissner (1996) for the slope at \(Q^2=0\). The Lorentz-invariant infrared regularization approach, proposed by Becher and Leutwyler (1999), resums recoil corrections that are only slowly convergent in the heavy baryon expansion, particularly in the nucleon spin sector. The main result, the first moment difference \(\Gamma_1^{p-n}(Q^2)\), is expected to be largely free of resonance contributions (especially from the \(\Delta(1232)\)) and can be compared directly with Jefferson Laboratory data; the authors note that preliminary CLAS data starting at \(Q^2 = 0.2\ \mathrm{GeV}^2\) appear consistent with both their result and the heavy baryon result, while a new experimental point at \(Q^2 = 0.1\ \mathrm{GeV}^2\) from the Jefferson Lab E94010 collaboration agrees well with their prediction.
