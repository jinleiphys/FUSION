# Effect of shell structure on the fission of sub-lead nuclei

> Fission of atomic nuclei often produces mass asymmetric fragments. However, the origin of this asymmetry was believed to be different in actinides and in the sub-lead region [A. Andreyev {\it et al.}, Phys. Rev. Lett. {\bf 105}, 252502 (2010)]. It has recently been argued that quantum shell effects stabilising pear shapes of the fission fragments could explain the observed asymmetries in fission of actinides[G. Scamps and C. Simenel, Nature {\bf 564}, 382 (2018)]. This interpretation is tested in the sub-lead region using microscopic mean-field calculations of fission based on the Hartree-Fock approach with BCS pairing correlations. The evolution of the number of protons and neutrons in asymmetric fragments of mercury isotope fissions is interpreted in terms of deformed shell gaps in the fragments. A new method is proposed to investigate the dominant shell effects in the pre-fragments at scission. We conclude that the mechanisms responsible for asymmetric fissions in the sub-lead region are the same as in the actinide region, which is a strong indication of their universality.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the same mechanism based on octupole (pear shape) deformed shell effects in fission fragments that explains asymmetric fission in actinides also drives asymmetric fission in the sub-lead region, suggesting universality of the mechanism. The claim is qualified by noting that shell gaps associated with large quadrupole deformations at 34 and 42-46 protons also appear to contribute.

## Method

Constrained Hartree-Fock calculations with BCS pairing correlations were performed using the SLy4d parametrisation of the Skyrme energy density functional. The authors studied static fission valleys by applying quadrupole and octupole deformation constraints to mercury isotopes from A=176 to A=198 and A≈178 isobars. A new method was introduced where an asymmetric fission is substituted by a symmetric one (e.g., 156Er → 78Se + 78Se) to disentangle the shell structure of the two fragments by studying single-particle energy gaps in the entire fissioning nucleus near scission.

## Key numbers

- For 180Hg asymmetric fission, the predicted light fragment is centered around 80Kr (Z=36, N=44) and heavy fragment around 100Ru (Z=44, N=56), in agreement with experimental masses from Andreyev (2010)
- Quadrupole deformation parameters at scission for 180Hg fragments: β2L ≈ 0.75 for 80Kr (elongated light fragment) and β2H ≈ 0.25 for 100Ru (compact heavy fragment)
- Octupole deformation parameters at scission for 180Hg fragments: β3L ≈ β3H ≈ 0.25 for both fragments
- Constrained calculations used Q20 = 47.3 b with octupole constraints from Q30 = 0 to 22 b3/2
- Spatial grid dimensions: Lx × Ly × 2Lz = 40 × 19.2 × 19.2 fm3 with mesh spacing of 0.8 fm
- Pairing interaction strengths: V0nn = 1256 MeV·fm3 and Vpp = 1462 MeV·fm3

## Context

This work extends the octupole-driven fission mechanism proposed by Scamps and Simenel (2018) for actinides to the sub-lead region, where Andreyev (2010) had discovered a "new type of asymmetric fission" in 180Hg that could not be explained by spherical shell effects. The results contradict earlier beliefs that different mechanisms were responsible for asymmetry in actinides versus sub-lead nuclei. The findings enable future investigations of octupole correlations in fission of superheavy elements and quasifission processes.
