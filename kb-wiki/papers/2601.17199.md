# Entanglement Enabled Tomography of Flux Tubes in (2+1)D Yang-Mills Theory

> We investigate the entangling properties of the color flux tube between a static quark-antiquark pair in pure gauge Yang-Mills theory. In earlier works, we defined a gauge-invariant flux tube entanglement entropy (FTE$^2$), the excess entanglement entropy of a region of gluon fields that can be attributed to the color flux tube, and demonstrated that it is finite in the continuum limit. FTE$^2$ was shown to have two contributions, one from the vibrations of the QCD string, and the other from its internal (color) degrees of freedom. In this work, we further explore the internal color component in (2+1)D Yang-Mills theory for $SU(N_c)$ gauge groups, varying $2\le N_c\le5$. We identify a novel physical scale in the theory, the entanglement radius $ξ_0$. This radius characterizes the transverse extent of the flux tube that must be completely severed by an entangling region to capture the entanglement entropy of color degrees of freedom. The key feature underlying this phenomenon is its topological nature. This is revealed through systematic studies of multi-slab entangling regions in which FTE$^2$ changes sharply when boundaries of the slabs completely cross-cut the flux tube. We find that $ξ_0$ increases approximately linearly with $N_c$ and is independent of both Rényi replica number and the inter-quark separation length. We also study FTE$^2$ as a function of the entangling region's transverse displacement from the static quark pair and observe behavior consistent with a previously identified intrinsic width $λ$ of the flux tube, with an extracted value in agreement with the inverse mass of the lightest glueball for the gauge groups studied.

## Key claim

The paper identifies a novel physical scale called the entanglement radius ξ₀, which characterizes the transverse extent of the color flux tube that must be completely severed by an entangling region to capture the entanglement entropy of color degrees of freedom; this effect is topological in nature, with the flux tube entanglement entropy (FTE²) changing sharply only when boundaries completely cross-cut the flux tube, contrary to what a simple one-dimensional string model would predict.

## Method

The authors performed lattice Monte Carlo simulations of (2+1)D pure gauge Yang-Mills theory for SU(N_c) gauge groups with N_c ranging from 2 to 5, using the standard Wilson plaquette action. They computed the flux tube entanglement entropy (FTE²) by measuring ratios of Polyakov loop correlators on replicated lattices with modified temporal boundary conditions, studying both single-slab and multi-slab entangling region geometries at temperatures T_c/2 and T_c/4. The entanglement radius ξ₀ was extracted by analyzing how FTE² changes as entangling regions with varying widths and gaps are positioned relative to the flux tube between static quark sources.

## Key numbers

- The entanglement radius ξ₀ increases approximately linearly with N_c across the range 2 ≤ N_c ≤ 5
- ξ₀ is independent of both the Rényi replica number q and the inter-quark separation length L
- The extracted intrinsic width λ of the flux tube agrees with the inverse mass of the lightest glueball for all gauge groups studied
- Lattice studies were performed at T_c/2 for single-slab runs and T_c/4 for multi-slab runs, with slab widths near w√σ₀ ∼ 0.25
- Quark separations studied were L√σ₀ ∼ 1

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Amorosso 2024) where FTE² was first defined and shown to have two contributions: one from vibrations of the QCD string and another from internal color degrees of freedom. The paper complements studies of flux tube internal structure using field strength correlators (Cea 2012, Verzichelli 2025) and the Clem formula adapted from type-II superconductors, as well as effective string theory predictions (Luscher 1980) and QED₃ soliton solutions (Aharony 2024). The results enable future work distinguishing between different confinement mechanisms and effective models of the flux tube using entanglement-based probes.
