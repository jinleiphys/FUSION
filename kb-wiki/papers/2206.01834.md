# A holographic bottom-up description of light nuclide spectroscopy and stability

> This work explores a holographic proposal to describe light nuclide spectroscopy by considering extensions to the well-known bottom-up AdS/QCD proposals, the hardwall and softwall models. We also propose an alternative description inspired by the Woods-Saxon potential. We find the static dilaton associated with this potential in this Wood-Saxon-like model. We compute the nuclide spectra finding that, despite their pure AdS/QCD origin, hardwall and softwall, as monoparametric models, have good accuracy and precision since the RMS error is near 11 $\%$ and 4 $\%$ respectively. In the case of the Wood-Saxon model, the RMS was around 1 $\%$. We also discuss configurational entropy as a tool to categorize which model is suitable to describe nuclides in terms of stability. We found that configurational entropy resembles a stability line, independent from nuclear spin, for symmetric light nuclides when considering softwall and Wood-Saxon-like models. For the hardwall case, configurational entropy, despite increasing with the constituent number, depends on the nuclear spin. Thus, the Woods-Saxon-like model emerges as the best choice to describe light nuclide spectroscopy in the bottom-up scenario.

## Key claim

The paper claims that bottom-up holographic models (hardwall, softwall, and a proposed Woods-Saxon-like model) can describe light nuclide spectroscopy, with the Woods-Saxon-like model achieving the best accuracy (RMS error near 1%) despite being a three-parameter model, while the monoparametric hardwall and softwall models achieve RMS errors near 11% and 4% respectively; however, the claim is qualified by the restriction to symmetric light nuclides (A=2Z) and ground states only.

## Method

The authors model light nuclides as p-form bulk fields in a five-dimensional AdS space, where the conformal dimension of the dual operator is set by the mass number A (twist from nucleon constituents). They compute mass spectra by solving the holographic Schrodinger-like equation derived from the bulk action, using three different confinement mechanisms: a hard cutoff (hardwall model), a quadratic static dilaton (softwall model), and a dilaton reconstructed from a Woods-Saxon-like potential. The RMS error is evaluated for 29 symmetric light nuclides from Z=2 (He) to Z=30 (Ca) using experimental masses from Huang (2021) as reference.

## Key numbers

- Hardwall model: RMS error of 11.6% for 29 light nuclide masses fitted with one parameter (Lambda_N = 0.7794 u)
- Softwall model: RMS error of 4.4% for 29 light nuclide masses fitted with one parameter (kappa_0 = 1.0006 u)
- Woods-Saxon-like model: RMS error near 1% with three parameters (A1 = A2 = 1.863 u GeV, B = 2.5 u)
- For the hardwall model, the mass of He-4 (Z=2) is used to fix the cutoff: Lambda_N = M_He4 / alpha_{2,1} = 4.00260 u / alpha_{2,1}
- For the softwall model, the mass spectrum is given by M_0^2(Delta) = Delta * kappa_0^2 * (Delta - p), where Delta = A = 2Z for symmetric nuclides
- The hardwall mass spectrum is given by M_0(Delta) = Lambda_N * alpha_{Delta-2,1}, where alpha_{n,m} are Bessel function zeros

## Context

This work extends bottom-up AdS/QCD models (hardwall from Boschi-Filho (2002) and Erlich (2005); softwall from Karch (2006)) from hadronic spectroscopy to nuclear spectroscopy, treating nucleons as constituent objects with twist one rather than quarks. The Woods-Saxon-like model is inspired by the Woods-Saxon nucleon-nucleon potential from Woods (1954) used in nuclear shell models. The configurational entropy analysis, following the approach of Gleiser (2011) and applied holographically by Braga (2017), provides a stability criterion independent of nuclear spin for the softwall and Woods-Saxon-like models, while the hardwall model shows spin-dependent configurational entropy. This enables future holographic studies of nuclear stability and spectroscopy beyond the symmetric light nuclide approximation.
