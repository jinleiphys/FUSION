# Reaction rates and transport in neutron stars

> Understanding signals from neutron stars requires knowledge about the transport inside the star. We review the transport properties and the underlying reaction rates of dense hadronic and quark matter in the crust and the core of neutron stars and point out open problems and future directions.

## Key claim

This review synthesizes the current theoretical understanding of transport properties and reaction rates in neutron stars across all density regimes (crust, nuclear matter core, and quark matter core), while explicitly noting that large uncertainties remain due to the strong interaction's role in many processes and that approximations such as weak-coupling techniques or one-pion exchange for nucleon-nucleon collisions are still being used.

## Method

The authors provide a pedagogical review structured by moving from the outer layers of the star inward, covering the crust (where a lattice or strongly coupled liquid of ions coexists with an electron gas and, in the inner crust, a neutron superfluid), the nuclear matter core (including possible hyperons and meson condensates), and the deconfined quark matter core. They derive transport coefficients from kinetic theory and the Boltzmann equation, focusing on how particle collisions (scattering processes and flavor-changing electroweak processes) determine shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, thermal conductivity, electrical conductivity, and neutrino emissivity, and they discuss open problems and future directions for each region.

## Key numbers

* Electron degeneracy temperature: \(T_{Fe} = 5.9 \times 10^9 \, \text{K} \left(\sqrt{1+x_r^2}-1\right)\), where \(x_r = p_{Fe}/(m_e c)\) is the electron relativistic parameter.
* Ion coupling parameter: \(\Gamma \approx 153 \, x_r \left(Z/50\right)^{5/3} \left(T/10^8 \, \text{K}\right)^{-1}\), with crystallization occurring at \(\Gamma_m \approx 175\).
* Effective electron-ion collision frequency: \(\nu_{ei} \approx 8.8 \times 10^{17} \, (Z/50) \sqrt{1+x_r^2} \, \Lambda_{ei} \, \text{s}^{-1}\), where \(\Lambda_{ei}\) is the Coulomb logarithm.
* In the solid regime, electron-phonon collision frequencies scale as \(\nu_{ei}^{\kappa,\sigma} = \alpha_f u_{-2} \beta_r^{-1} (k_B T/\hbar) (2-\beta_r^2) F_{\kappa,\sigma}(T/T_{pi})\), with \(u_{-2} = 13.0\) for a bcc lattice and \(T_{pi}\) the ion plasma temperature.
* Quantum corrections in the solid regime become important at \(T \lesssim 0.15 \, T_{pi}\), where \(F_{\sigma,\kappa,\eta}(t) \propto t\) suppresses electron-ion collisions.
* Freezing of Umklapp processes becomes important only at \(T \lesssim 10^{-2} T_{pi}\).

## Context

This review updates and extends earlier reviews on neutron star transport, including those by Yakovlev (2001) on neutrino emissivity, Chamel and Haensel (2008) on the crust, Page and Reddy (2012) on inner crust transport, and Potekhin (2015) on cooling of isolated neutron stars. It uniquely brings together transport properties from the crust through nuclear matter to deconfined quark matter, enabling improved interpretation of astrophysical observations such as pulsar glitches, neutron star cooling, and neutron star merger simulations, while also connecting to fundamental questions about transport in strongly interacting relativistic theories.
