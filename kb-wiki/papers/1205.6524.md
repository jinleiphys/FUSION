# Determining Reactor Flux from Xenon-136 and Cesium-135 in Spent Fuel

> The ability to infer the reactor flux from spent fuel or seized fissile material would enhance the tools of nuclear forensics and nuclear nonproliferation significantly. We show that reactor flux can be inferred from the ratios of xenon-136 to xenon-134 and cesium-135 to cesium-137. If the average flux of a reactor is known, the flux inferred from measurements of spent fuel could help determine whether that spent fuel was loaded as a blanket or close to the mid-plane of the reactor. The cesium ratio also provides information on reactor shutdowns during the irradiation of fuel, which could prove valuable for identifying the reactor in question through comparisons with satellite reactor heat monitoring data. We derive analytic expressions for these correlations and compare them to experimental data and to detailed reactor burn simulations. The enrichment of the original uranium fuel affects the correlations by up to 3 percent, but only at high flux.

# Determining Reactor Flux from Xenon-136 and Cesium-135 in Spent Fuel

## Key claim

The ratios of xenon-136 to xenon-134 and cesium-135 to cesium-137 in spent reactor fuel can be used to infer the thermal neutron flux to which the fuel was exposed, but the cesium ratio is significantly affected by reactor shutdowns at high flux (above approximately 5 × 10¹³ n/cm²/sec), and the xenon ratio determines the *average* flux over the burn history even when power fluctuates.

## Method

Analytic expressions were derived for the xenon-136/xenon-134 and cesium-135/cesium-137 ratios as functions of thermal neutron flux, based on the competition between beta decay of xenon-135 (9.14 hour half-life) and its neutron capture to xenon-136 (capture cross section 2.6 × 10⁶ barns). These analytic results were compared to experimental data from Maeck et al., who irradiated enriched uranium targets in the Advanced Test Reactor (ATR) and Engineering Test Reactor (ETR) at Idaho National Laboratory, and to full reactor burn simulations that included production/depletion chains for major actinides and fission fragments for three uranium-235 enrichments (3%, 20%, and 99.9%).

## Key numbers

- Thermal neutron capture cross section on xenon-135: 2.6 × 10⁶ barns
- Cumulative fission yield for xenon-136: approximately 7% per fission for uranium and plutonium isotopes
- Flux range studied: 6 × 10¹² to 1.5 × 10¹⁴ n/cm²/sec (experimental data); up to 5 × 10¹⁴ n/cm²/sec (simulations)
- The xenon-136/xenon-134 ratio varies by a factor of approximately 2 over the flux range 10¹² to 5 × 10¹⁴ n/cm²/sec
- The cesium-137/cesium-135 ratio varies by a factor of 50 over the thermal flux range 10¹² to 5 × 10¹⁴ n/cm²/sec
- Fuel enrichment affects the correlations by up to 3% at high flux (5 × 10¹⁴ n/cm²/sec for xenon ratio; 2% for cesium ratio at same flux)
- The xenon ratio retains reactor information for dilutions up to about 10⁹ m³ of air per kilogram of heavy metal reprocessed
- The cesium ratio can be determined to 1% accuracy in very low-level samples using resonance ionization mass spectrometry (Pibida et al.)
- Xenon isotope ratios can be determined to better than 0.5% in samples of 10⁻¹² cm³ STP of xenon (Gilmour et al.)

## Context

This work extends the experimental observations of Maeck et al. by identifying the underlying nuclear physics governing the flux dependence of the xenon and cesium ratios, and by providing analytic expressions that explain those observations. Previous studies by Nakhleh, Charlton, Feary, and Burr omitted xenon-136 from analyses of fission gases released during fuel reprocessing due to concerns about unknown power history; the present work shows that the xenon-136/xenon-134 ratio actually determines the average flux even with power fluctuations. The cesium ratio has been used as a forensic tool by Taylor for identifying sources of radioactive contamination in soil samples. The results enable nuclear forensics applications where spent fuel or seized fissile material can be analyzed to determine reactor flux and, by combining the cesium ratio with the plutonium-240/plutonium-239 ratio, to infer the number of reactor shutdowns during irradiation, which could be compared with satellite reactor heat monitoring data for reactor identification.
