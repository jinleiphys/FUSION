# Nuclear excitation cross section of $^{229}$Th via inelastic electron scattering

> Nuclear excitation cross section of $^{229}$Th from the ground state to the low-lying isomeric state via inelastic electron scattering is calculated, on the level of Dirac distorted wave Born approximation. With electron energies below 100 eV, inelastic scattering is very efficient in the isomeric excitation, yielding excitation cross sections on the order of 10$^{-27}$ to 10$^{-26}$ cm$^2$. Systematic analyses are presented on elements affecting the excitation cross section, including the ion-core potential, the relativistic effect, the knowledge of the reduced nuclear transition probabilities, etc.

# Nuclear excitation cross section of $^{229}$Th via inelastic electron scattering

## Key claim

Inelastic electron scattering with electron energies below 100 eV can excite the $^{229}$Th nucleus from its ground state to the low-lying isomeric state with cross sections on the order of $10^{-27}$ to $10^{-26}$ cm$^2$, but only when using Dirac distorted wave calculations that include the ion-core potential; plane-wave calculations without this potential yield cross sections 5 to 6 orders of magnitude smaller, and the authors identify that Tkalya (2020) results are larger by an overall factor of two and contain a confusion of the nuclear transition direction in the reduced transition probabilities.

## Method

The authors calculate the nuclear excitation cross section using Dirac distorted wave Born approximation, where the scattering electron wave functions are obtained by solving the Dirac equation with a potential that includes both the nuclear charge distribution (modeled as a Fermi distribution) and the electron cloud potential from a Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater calculation. The radial wave functions are computed using the RADIAL code, and the cross section is expressed in terms of reduced nuclear transition probabilities $B(E2)$ and $B(M1)$ for the electric quadrupole and magnetic dipole channels, with comparisons made to Schr\"odinger distorted waves and Dirac plane waves to isolate relativistic and potential effects.

## Key numbers

* Excitation cross sections for $^{229}$Th using Dirac distorted waves are on the order of $10^{-27}$ to $10^{-26}$ cm$^2$ for electron energies below 100 eV.
* Dirac plane-wave cross sections are on the order of $10^{-33}$ to $10^{-32}$ cm$^2$ for the same energy range, meaning distorted waves yield cross sections 5 to 6 orders of magnitude higher.
* Dirac distorted waves give cross sections about one order of magnitude higher than Schr\"odinger distorted waves for the same system.
* The isomeric state energy is 8.28 eV above the nuclear ground state, with an uncertainty of $\pm 0.17$ eV from Seiferle (2019).
* Four sets of reduced nuclear transition probabilities are compared: Set 1 has $B(E2, e\rightarrow g) = 27$ W.u. and $B(M1, e\rightarrow g) = 0.0076$ W.u.; Set 2 has $B(E2) = 42.9$ W.u. and $B(M1) = 0.008$ W.u.; Set 3 has $B(E2) = 33.8$ W.u. and $B(M1) = 0.005$ W.u.; Set 4 has $B(E2) = 17.55$ W.u. and $B(M1) = 0.048$ W.u.
* The ratio of reduced transition probabilities for excitation versus de-excitation is $B(g \rightarrow e) / B(e \rightarrow g) = 2/3$, following from the angular momentum factors $2I_e+1 = 2$ and $2I_g+1 = 3$.

## Context

This work extends the proposal by Tkalya (2020) that low-energy inelastic electron scattering can efficiently excite the $^{229}$Th isomer, providing an independent derivation and correcting an overall factor of two and a confusion in the nuclear transition direction. The paper enables quantitative evaluation of experimental schemes for controlled isomeric excitation, including external electron beam irradiation, laser-driven electron recollision as proposed by Wang (2021, 2022), and laser-cluster interactions as proposed by Feng (2022), all of which rely on the cross sections calculated here. The results also highlight the critical importance of relativistic distorted-wave treatment for heavy nuclei like $^{229}$Th (Z=90), where the wave function near the nucleus differs dramatically from non-relativistic or plane-wave approximations.
