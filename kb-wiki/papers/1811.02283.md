# Microscopic treatment of energy partition in fission

> The transformation of an atomic nucleus into two excited fission fragments is modeled as a strongly damped evolution of the nuclear shape, until scission occurs at a small critical neck radius, at which point the mass, charge, and shape of each fragment are extracted. The available excitation energy then is divided statistically on the basis of the microscopic level densities. This approach takes account of the important (and energy-dependent) finite-size effects. After the fragments have been fully accelerated and their shapes have relaxed to their equilibrium form, they undergo sequential neutron evaporation. The dependence of the resulting mean neutron multiplicity on the fragment mass, $\barν$(A), including the dependence on the initial excitation energy of the fissioning compound nucleus, is in good agreement with the observed behavior, as demonstrated here for $^{235}$U(n,f).

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that using shape-dependent microscopic level densities (rather than the simplified Fermi-gas formula) to partition excitation energy between fission fragments at scission yields a remarkably good reproduction of experimental mean neutron multiplicity data for \(^{235}\)U(n,f), including the sawtooth shape of \(\bar{\nu}(A)\) and its evolution with incident neutron energy.

## Method

The authors model fission as a strongly damped Brownian shape evolution using Metropolis walks on a five-dimensional potential-energy surface (quadrupole moment, neck radius, two spheroidal deformations, and mass asymmetry) for \(^{236}\)U\(^*\) at specified initial excitation energies. At scission (neck radius \(c_0 = 1.5\) fm), the available excitation energy is partitioned statistically between the two nascent fragments using microscopic level densities calculated via a combinatorial model that includes pairing and rotational bands, rather than the standard Fermi-gas formula. The fragments then undergo sequential neutron evaporation using the same microscopic level densities, and the resulting mean neutron multiplicities \(\bar{\nu}(A)\) are compared to experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The critical neck radius for scission is set to \(c_0 = 1.5\) fm.
- For the division \((A_H:A_L) = (130:106)\) at \(E_{\rm sc}^* = 10\) MeV, the heavy fragment (\(^{130}\)Sn, shell correction \(-10.2\) MeV) receives only \(\approx 2\) MeV while the light fragment (\(^{106}\)Mo, shell correction \(+0.35\) MeV) receives \(\approx 8\) MeV, versus the Fermi-gas prediction of \(\approx 5.5\) and \(4.5\) MeV respectively.
- For the division \((A_H:A_L) = (152:84)\), the heavy fragment (\(^{152}\)Nd) has a shell correction of \(+6.1\) MeV at scission deformation, while the light fragment (\(^{84}\)Ge, \(N=52\) near magic) has low level density.
- Mean distortion energies increase from 2–3 MeV for light fragments to 6–7 MeV for heavy fragments.
- The calculation uses ensembles of \(10^6\) scission configurations for \(^{236}\)U\(^*\).
- The Fermi-gas level density used for comparison has parameter \(a = A/(8\ {\rm MeV})\).

## Context

This work extends the level-density guided Metropolis shape evolution treatment of Ward et al. (2017) by adding shape-dependent microscopic level densities for the nascent fission fragments, replacing the simplified Fermi-gas (Bethe) formula used in earlier treatments by Madland and Nix (1981), Lemaire et al. (1972), and Kornilov et al. (2004). The approach builds on the Brownian shape evolution method of Randrup et al. (2011) and the macroscopic-microscopic model of Möller et al. (1992) for potential-energy surfaces. The results enable predictive calculations of neutron multiplicities for fission cases where no experimental data exist, and clarify the role of shell structure in the sawtooth shape of \(\bar{\nu}(A)\).
