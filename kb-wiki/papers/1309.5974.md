# Detection of small low emission sources - case studies

> The article is devoted to a further study of the Compton camera method of passive detection of small amounts of special nuclear materials, developed by the authors in their previous work. Various cargo scenarios, detector errors, and other issues are addressed.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Compton camera method for passive detection of small amounts of special nuclear materials can succeed under realistic cargo scenarios, detector angular errors, and non-uniform backgrounds, but only when the total number of detected particles is sufficiently large; the theoretical detection threshold depends on the signal-to-noise ratio, pixel size, angular error, and desired confidence and sensitivity levels, and the method may indicate a source's presence even when its exact location cannot be determined.

## Method

The authors simulate two-dimensional photon transport through cargo containers using the linear Boltzmann equation with multigroup energy discretization, solved via an S_n angular quadrature and bilinear discontinuous finite element spatial discretization. They model a small (p=0.01) HEU source emitting gamma rays from the 1.001 MeV line of U-238, background radiation from a concrete slab underneath the container, and various cargo materials (plastic, wood, cotton, iron, fertilizer, air). Detector arrays record particle directions with a Gaussian angular error of standard deviation alpha degrees; backprojection of detected particles onto the domain creates an image, and a source is declared detected if any pixel exceeds a threshold of mu + k_t sigma, where k_t is chosen to achieve a specified confidence level (e.g., 99% true negative rate).

## Key numbers

* Signal-to-noise ratios considered range from 0.01% to 100%, with the primary regime of interest being SNR on the order of 0.1% (0.001).
* The linear size ratio p (source radius to cargo radius) is set to 0.01, corresponding to a cargo container several meters long versus a source several centimeters long.
* For a 99% true negative rate with M=10^4 pixels, the threshold k_t must exceed 4.753; for 90% true negative rate with M=10^3 pixels, k_t > 3.706.
* The four main gamma lines for Uranium-235 have energies 143.8, 166.3, 185.7, and 205.3 keV; the 185.7 keV line has a mean-free-path in high-Z materials of about 0.69 mean-free-paths.
* The U-238 1.001 MeV line has a mean-free-path in high-Z materials of 13.3 mean-free-paths.
* Background gamma lines considered include: 1.461 MeV from Potassium-40, 1.12 MeV and 1.76 MeV from Bismuth-214, and 2.61 MeV from Thallium-208.
* The concrete slab thickness is 40 cm with a 60 cm gap between concrete and container bottom.
* Detector arrays consist of 48 detectors per side with 180 angular bins each.
* Angular error standard deviation values tested are 0 degrees and 2 degrees; neutron Compton cameras can have angular error up to 12 degrees standard deviation.
* The theoretical required total particle count N is proportional to (8/SNR)^2 * p(1-p) for the ideal case, and to [K/(C(p,alpha)*s)]^2 * p(1-p) when angular errors are present.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous development of Compton camera detection algorithms (Olson et al., 2013) by testing them under more realistic conditions: non-uniform backgrounds due to cargo materials, spatially varying absorption and scattering cross sections, and detector angular errors. The earlier work (Olson et al., 2013) assumed uniform random background, no cargo influence, and perfect directional information. The present study uses forward radiation transport modeling to simulate realistic cargo scenarios (plastic, wood, cotton, iron, fertilizer) and incorporates angular errors typical of neutron Compton cameras (up to 12 degrees). The results enable assessment of practical observation times needed for border security applications and show that detection may be possible even when the source location cannot be precisely determined.
