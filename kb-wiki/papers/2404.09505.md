# Historical introduction to ultra peripheral collisions

> This is a brief history of photons, both soft and hard, real and virtual. About 150-100 years ago, Maxwell and Einstein discovered intriguing properties of electromagnetic fields and how to understand them both macroscopically and microscopically. Decades later, physicists developed the theory of renormalized quantum electrodynamics (QED), an incredibly accurate theory describing interactions of photons and other particles. Photons are used everywhere in academia and technological devices, from supermarket lasers and doors to academic studies in atomic, nuclear, and particle physics. In this article, I attempt to convey how the field of relativistic heavy ions rediscovered ultra-peripheral collisions (UPC) as a source of intense, almost real photons, and how it permits the study of a plethora of phenomena in the aforementioned academic fields. These phenomena are not always accessible by other means.

## Key claim

Ultra-peripheral collisions (UPCs) provide a source of intense, almost real photons that enable the study of phenomena in atomic, nuclear, and particle physics that are not always accessible by other means, but the equivalent photon method underlying UPC calculations is valid only to first order in perturbation theory and requires careful separation of electromagnetic and strong-interaction contributions.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of the equivalent photon method (EPN) for UPCs, where the electromagnetic fields of a relativistic charged particle induce excitation processes in a nucleus with matrix elements equivalent to those induced by real photons. The cross section is expressed as a sum over electric and magnetic multipoles weighted by equivalent photon numbers that depend on beam energy and photon energy. The author traces applications from the 1980s onward, including Coulomb dissociation for nuclear astrophysics, giant resonance excitation, lepton pair production, light-by-light scattering, vector meson production, and the production of exotic atoms and antimatter.

## Key numbers

- Cross section for electron-positron pair production in UPCs: σ = (28/27π) (Z₁Z₂ α r_e)² ln³(γ/2), reaching approximately 200 kbarn for PbPb at LHC
- Cross section for electron capture into K-shell orbit: σ_K = (33/20) Z₁⁵ Z₂⁵ α⁵ r_e² ln(γ/2)
- Light-by-light scattering cross section for PbPb at LHC: σ_D ∼ 50 barn for low-energy photons, but ∼ 30 nb for E_γ > m_μ
- Lorentz boost factor γ at LHC: approximately 7000 for p-p, 3000 for Pb-Pb collisions
- Single pair production probability at impact parameter b: P₀(b) = (14/9π²b²) (Z₁Z₂ α r_e)² ln²(γ/2 m_e b), valid for γ/m_e > b > 1/m_e
- Higgs production cross section in UPCs estimated at 1 nb (1988 calculation)

## Context

This historical review extends the foundational work of Fermi (1924) on the equivalent photon method and traces the development of UPC physics from the author's PhD thesis (1988) through contemporary LHC measurements. The paper describes how early proposals by Bertulani and Baur (1985-1988) that were initially met with skepticism have since been validated by experiments at GSI, RHIC, and LHC, including the first production of anti-hydrogen at CERN (1996) and the observation of light-by-light scattering by the ATLAS collaboration (2017). The work enables future studies of nuclear gluon shadowing at low x, exotic atoms such as dimuonium and ditauonium, and searches for physics beyond the Standard Model.
