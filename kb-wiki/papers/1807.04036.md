# Consolidating the concept of low-energy magnetic dipole decay radiation

> We have made a thorough study of the low-energy behaviour of the $γ$-ray strength function within the framework of the shell model. We have performed large-scale calculations spanning isotopic and isotonic chains over several mass regions, with the purpose of studying the systematic behavior of the low-energy enhancement (LEE) for $M1$ transitions. There are clear trends in the calculations: From being all but absent in the lowest mass region, the LEE becomes steeper and more pronounced as the mass number increases, and for a given mass region it further increases towards shell closures. Moreover, the LEE is found to be steeper in regions near doubly-magic nuclei where proton particles couple to neutron holes. These trends enable us to consolidate several previous works on the LEE into a single, consistent concept. We compare the inferred trends to the available experimental data from the Oslo method, and find suppport for the systematic behaviour. Lastly we have compared the calculations to strength functions compiled from discrete, experimental lifetimes, and find excellent agreement; the discrete data are consistent with a LEE, and indicate that the slope varies as function of mass number.

## Key claim

The low-energy enhancement (LEE) in M1 gamma-ray strength functions is a systematic feature present across many mass regions, but its slope varies significantly: it becomes steeper and more pronounced as mass number increases, and for a given mass region it further increases towards shell closures, particularly near doubly-magic nuclei where proton particles couple to neutron holes.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale shell-model calculations using the KSHELL code with the jun45 interaction (f5/2pg9/2 orbitals atop a 56Ni core) for isotopic chains of Ni, Cu, Zn, Ga, Ge, As, and some Se isotopes, totaling 283 nuclei. They also performed calculations in the sd shell using the USDA interaction (16O core) and for Ni isotopes using a 48Ca core with the ca48mh1g interaction. For each nucleus, they calculated 100 levels of each parity and spin between J=0 (J=1/2) and J=14 (J=29/2) for even (odd) A, computed B(M1) transition strengths for all allowed transitions, and compiled the gamma-ray strength function using a bin size of 0.2 MeV. They compared these calculations to experimental data from the Oslo method and to strength functions compiled from discrete experimental lifetimes in the RIPL library.

## Key numbers

- 283 nuclei were calculated in total across isotopic and isotonic chains
- For the jun45 calculations, the valence space allows up to 22 protons and neutrons
- The bin size used for strength function compilation was Delta E = 0.2 MeV
- For the exponential fit to the strength function f(E_gamma) = B exp(E_gamma / T): in the sd shell, B = 1.30 x 10^-8 MeV^-3 and T = 5.09 MeV; in the f5/2pg9/2 shell, B = 0.77 x 10^-8 MeV^-3 and T = 1.73 MeV; for 56Fe separately, B = 0.94 x 10^-8 MeV^-3 and T = 2.07 MeV
- For the discrete experimental data from RIPL, the 56Fe strength function was based on 90 transitions
- The lower limit on E_gamma for Oslo method extraction is typically about 1.5 MeV, with an exception for 151,153Sm where Compton suppression allowed extraction down to 700 keV
- For the sd shell calculations, effective g_s values of g_s,eff = 0.9 g_s,free were used; for jun45 calculations, g_s,eff = 0.7 g_s,free was used

## Context

This work consolidates several previous studies on the low-energy enhancement (LEE) into a single consistent concept, extending the shell-model calculations of Schwengner et al. (2013) on Zr and Mo isotopes, Brown and Larsen (2014) on 56,57Fe, and Schwengner et al. (2017) on Fe isotopes. It also relates to the shears-band mechanism proposed by Frauendorf (2001) and the toy-model study by Karampagia et al. (2017). The findings enable improved modeling of (n,gamma) capture cross sections relevant to the r-process nucleosynthesis, particularly for neutron-rich nuclei where the LEE can impact cross sections by orders of magnitude as shown by Larsen and Goriely. The authors propose that an M1 correction to the strength function at the tail of the E1 Giant Dipole Resonance is probably required for all nuclei, and that the LEE is especially strong for very neutron-rich nuclei.
