# Effect of double-polarization data in fits to single-pion photoproduction

> We investigate the influence of new Jefferson Lab polarization-transfer data, Cx and Cz, and recoil polarization, Py, measurements in multipole fits to the single-pion photoproduction database. Results are compared to those found with the addition of a double-polarization quantity associated with the Gerasimov- Drell-Hearn sum rule.

## Key claim

The new Jefferson Lab double-polarization measurements ($C_{x'}$, $C_{z'}$, and $P_y$) can be incorporated into multipole fits without substantial degradation of fits to other observables, but the region above 1 GeV remains underconstrained and further multipole shifts should be expected as more data become available.

## Method

The authors performed multipole fits to the single-pion photoproduction database using the SAID parametrization, adding new Jefferson Lab polarization-transfer ($C_{x'}$, $C_{z'}$) and recoil polarization ($P_y$) measurements. They compared three solutions: SM02 (prior to the new data), SG02 (including the new data with standard weighting), and SC02 (a forced fit weighting the new data by a factor of 4 to magnify changes in multipole amplitudes and identify data conflicts).

## Key numbers

- The new double-polarization measurements from Jefferson Lab Hall A cover an energy range from 0.83 GeV to 4.03 GeV
- An early forced fit to $\Sigma$ data resulted in an E2/M1 ratio of -2.9%, when the predominant value was -1.5%
- The single-pion photoproduction database contains approximately twenty thousand angular and total cross section measurements over the resonance region
- Deviations between SAID/MAID predictions and the new data become much larger at 1620 MeV and 1900 MeV

## Context

This work extends the SAID multipole analysis program (Arndt et al., ongoing) by incorporating new double-polarization data from the Jefferson Lab Hall A Collaboration (Gilman et al., 2002). The results are compared to the MAID model (Drechsel et al., ongoing) and to earlier GDH sum-rule measurements that provided the beam-target polarization quantity $E$. The analysis shows that the new data improve fits to the target-polarization asymmetry $T$ and reveals a sharp angular structure at 1900 MeV previously seen in the analysis of Barbour, Crawford, and Parsons (1970s), while demonstrating that the region above 1 GeV remains underconstrained and will benefit from the proposed polarized-target/beam program for Hall B at Jefferson Lab.
