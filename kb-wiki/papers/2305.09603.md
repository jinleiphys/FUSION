# Ultrastable optical, XUV and soft-x-ray clock transitions in open-shell highly charged ions

> Highly charged ions (HCIs) are insensitive to external perturbations and are attractive for the development of ultrastable clocks. However, only a few HCI candidates are known to provide optical clock transitions. In this Letter, we show that, as a result of strong relativistic effects, there are more than 100 suitable optical HCI clock candidates in more than 70 elements. Their transitions are embedded in the fine-structure splitting of the $nd^4$, $nd^5$ and $nd^6$ ground-state configurations with $n=3,4,5$ being the principal quantum numbers. The corresponding high multipolarity transitions in these ions have lifetimes and quality factors many orders of magnitude longer and larger, respectively, than those in state-of-the-art clocks. Their polarizabilities are also orders of magnitude smaller, rendering them more stable against external electromagnetic fields. Furthermore, within the same electronic configurations, the clock transitions in heavy ions scale up to the XUV and soft-x-ray region, thus enable the development of clocks based on shorter wavelengths. The existence of multiple clock transitions in different charge states of a single element, as well as in a whole isoelectronic sequence, would significantly enrich the detection of fine-structure constant variations, the search for new physics and the test of nuclear theories via high-precision spectroscopy.

## Key claim

The authors claim that more than 100 highly charged ions (HCIs) across more than 70 elements provide suitable optical clock transitions, arising from fine-structure splittings in open-shell $nd^4$, $nd^5$, and $nd^6$ ground-state configurations, but this claim is conditional on strong relativistic effects causing a reordering of atomic terms that enables high-multipolarity $E2/M3$ forbidden transitions rather than the usual fast $M1$ decays.

## Method

The authors performed fully relativistic multiconfiguration Dirac-Hartree-Fock (MCDHF) and configuration interaction (RCI) calculations using the GRASP2018 codes to predict transition energies, lifetimes, polarizabilities, and sensitivity to fine-structure constant variations for ions with $nd^4$, $nd^5$, and $nd^6$ configurations for $n=3,4,5$. For specific ions requiring high accuracy, large-scale calculations with millions of configuration state functions were performed by allowing single and double excitations from the $ns$, $np$, and $nd$ orbitals up to the $8k$ orbitals.

## Key numbers

- More than 100 suitable optical HCI clock candidates identified in more than 70 elements
- For Mo-like Ce$^{16+}$: $^5D_4$ to $^3D_2$ transition energy 1.70(1) eV, lifetime 1156 s, quality factor $Q=1.6\times10^{18}$
- For Mo-like U$^{50+}$: $^5D_4$ to $^3D_2$ transition energy 3.99(1) eV, lifetime 125 s, quality factor $Q=3.8\times10^{17}$
- For Cr-like Cd$^{24+}$: transition energy 3.78 eV, lifetime 437 s, $Q$ factor around $10^{18}$
- For Cr-like U$^{68+}$: transition energy 11.4 eV, lifetime 18.3 s, $Q$ factor around $10^{18}$
- For Nb-like U$^{51+}$: clock state at 57.2 eV above ground state, lifetime 8.3 days, $Q=6.3\times10^{22}$
- For V-like U$^{69+}$: transition energy 197 eV, lifetime 12 hours, $Q=1.3\times10^{22}$
- For Nb-like Pb$^{41+}$: measured energy 31.68(13) eV, calculated lifetime 26 days, $Q=1.1\times10^{23}$
- Differential polarizabilities $\Delta\alpha_{eg}$ for selected [Kr]$4d^6$ ions range from $-0.017$ to $-0.45$ in units of $10^{-40}$ J m$^2$/V$^2$
- For U$^{50+}$, polarizabilities are estimated to be less than $10^{-41}$ J m$^2$/V$^2$

## Context

This work extends the search for HCI clock candidates beyond the few previously known systems such as Ar$^{13+}$ (demonstrated via quantum logic spectroscopy with $Q=4\times10^{13}$ by the group in 2022) and the level-crossing candidates in $4f-5s$, $4f-5p$, and $5f-6p$ configurations studied by various groups (2010-2021). The paper enables the development of ultrastable clocks with quality factors exceeding $10^{23}$ and polarizabilities orders of magnitude smaller than current optical clocks based on neutral atoms or singly charged ions, and provides multiple clock transitions in single ions with distinct sensitivities to fine-structure constant variations for tests of fundamental physics.
