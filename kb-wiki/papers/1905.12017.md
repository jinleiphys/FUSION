# New physics via pion capture and simple nuclear reactions

> Light, beyond-the-standard-model particles $X$ in the 1-100 MeV mass range can be produced in nuclear and hadronic reactions but would have to decay electromagnetically. We show that simple and well-understood low-energy hadronic processes can be used as a tool to study $X$ production and decay. In particular, the pion capture process $π^- p \to X n \to e^+ e^- n$ can be used in a new experimental setup to search for anomalies in the angular distribution of the electron-positron pair, which could signal the appearance of dark photons, axion-like particles and other exotic states. This process can be used to decisively test the hypothesis of a new particle produced in the $^7{\rm Li}+p$ reaction. We also discuss a variety of other theoretically clean hadronic processes, such as $p+{\rm D(T)}$ fusion, as a promising source of $X$ particles.

## Key claim

The authors argue that simple, well-understood low-energy hadronic processes involving few nucleons can be used to search for light beyond-the-standard-model particles X in the 1-100 MeV mass range, avoiding the nuclear physics complications that complicate interpretation of anomalies in more complex nuclei like 8Be. The pion capture process π⁻p → Xn → e⁺e⁻n can decisively test the hypothesis of a new particle produced in the ⁷Li+p reaction that was invoked to explain the "beryllium anomaly."

## Method

The authors calculate production rates for exotic bosons (dark photons and protophobic vectors) in π⁻ capture on protons using a chiral effective Lagrangian with nucleons and pions, normalizing to the well-measured radiative pion capture rate. They simulate signal and background distributions for e⁺e⁻ invariant mass and opening angle, assuming 50% energy resolution and 1° angular resolution, and estimate the minimum coupling values that can be probed with 10¹⁰ pion captures. They also relate the emission of vector bosons in the D+p → ³He+γ reaction to the standard photon emission rate under the assumption of E1 amplitude dominance.

## Key numbers

- Radiative pion capture cross section: (σv)π⁻p→γn = 5.1×10⁻²⁸ cm² × c (theoretical) and approximately 5.7×10⁻²⁸ cm² × c (measured, with ~3% experimental error)
- Panofsky ratio: P = 1.546 ± 0.009, giving radiative branching Brπ⁻p→γn = 0.3928 ± 0.014
- Full probability of emitting an e⁺e⁻ pair relative to emitting a photon in π⁻ capture: approximately 7.3×10⁻³
- For a protophobic vector with m_V = 17 MeV (suggested by the ⁸Be anomaly), the minimum opening angle for e⁺e⁻ pairs from on-shell X decay is θ_min = 2m_X/mπ ≈ 14 degrees
- Estimated sensitivity: ε ≳ 0.02 × (δ_ee/m_X / 30%)^(1/4) × (10⁴/N_cap)^(1/4) for 3σ significance
- For D+p → ³He+X reaction with proton beams of 20-40 MeV and up to 1 mA currents, production rates of V-bosons can reach 10⁴ s⁻¹ for ε² = 10⁻⁴

## Context

This work extends the search for light weakly coupled particles (dark photons, axion-like particles, protophobic vectors) into the 10-30 MeV mass range where higher-energy probes like B-factories become less efficient. The paper specifically addresses the "beryllium anomaly" reported by Krasznahorkay (2015), where an unexpected angular distribution of e⁺e⁻ pairs in ⁸Be decay was interpreted as a possible 17 MeV particle. The authors propose that π⁻ capture on protons provides a cleaner test than the 8-nucleon system, and note that previous constraints from the SINDRUM collaboration (MeijerDrees, 1992) did not probe invariant masses below 20 MeV. The D+p → ³He+e⁺e⁻ process, previously studied experimentally by Johansson (1998), offers an alternative avenue with potentially higher statistics using proton beams.
