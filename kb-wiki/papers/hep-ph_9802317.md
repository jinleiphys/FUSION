# Parity Violating Energy Shifts and Berry Phases in Atoms, I

> We present a study of parity (P) violating contributions to the eigenenergies of stationary systems containing atoms in spatially inhomogeneous external electric fields. In this context the subtle interplay of P-violation and time reversal (T) invariance plays an important role. If the entire field configuration is chosen to exhibit chirality the energies are in general shifted by pseudoscalar contributions which change sign under a planar reflection of the field. To calculate the effects we use the standard model of elementary particle physics where the P-odd interaction arises through the exchange of Z-bosons between the quarks in the nucleus and the atomic electrons. We consider in detail hydrogen-like systems in unstable levels of principal quantum number n=2. The nominal order of P-violating effects is 10^-5...10^-9 Hz which is determined by the mixing of the 2S_1/2 and 2P_1/2 states. However we point out that it is possible to enhance the P-violating energy shifts dramatically! Instead of energy shifts linear in the P-violation parameters we get then shifts proportional to the square root of these parameters. Numerically we find such energy shifts to be of order 10^-5...1 Hz. Application of our technique to hydrogen-like atoms with a nucleus of spin I=1/2 yields P-violating energy shifts which are very sensitive to the nuclear spin dependent P-odd force, which receives a rather large contribution from the polarized strange quark density in polarized nuclei. Thus, a measurement of these energy shifts could provide an important tool to elucidate nuclear properties connected to the so called ``spin crisis''. We also present a method for treating degenerate perturbation theory which combines advantages of both, Kato's and Bloch's methods.

## Key claim

The paper shows that parity-violating energy shifts in hydrogen-like atoms placed in chiral, spatially inhomogeneous external electric fields can be dramatically enhanced from the nominal range of 10⁻⁵–10⁻⁹ Hz to 10⁻⁵–1 Hz, but this enhancement occurs only when the field configuration satisfies specific resonance conditions that make the energy shift proportional to the square root of the parity-violation parameter rather than linear in it.

## Method

The authors use the standard model's Z-boson exchange to model the parity-odd interaction between quarks in the nucleus and atomic electrons, then apply degenerate perturbation theory to hydrogen-like atoms in unstable n=2 levels. They develop a modified version of Bloch's method for degenerate perturbation theory that preserves hermiticity, and they analyze the interplay of parity violation with time-reversal invariance in chiral electric field configurations. The calculation treats both nuclear spin I=0 and I=1/2 cases, with the latter incorporating nuclear spin-dependent parity-odd forces.

## Key numbers

- Nominal parity-violating energy shifts for hydrogen-like n=2 systems: 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁹ Hz (determined by 2S₁/₂ and 2P₁/₂ state mixing)
- Enhanced parity-violating energy shifts under resonance conditions: 10⁻⁵ to 1 Hz
- For a specific hydrogen-like atom with unperturbed configuration parameters, resonance field configurations are found at electric field strength ℰ' = 0.3 V/cm, with specific dimensionless field component values: ηᵉ_res,+ = (1, 0, 117.00), ηᶻ_res,+ = (0, 0, -702.30), ηᵈ_res,+ = (0, 1, 117.21)
- The functional determinant for the existence proof evaluates to ∓2.63×10⁻⁵ (MHz h V⁻¹ cm)²

## Context

This work extends the study of parity violation in atomic systems, building on the authors' prior framework (BBN95) for treating n=2 hydrogen-like states in external fields. The paper enables experimental searches for nuclear spin-dependent parity-odd forces, particularly those sensitive to polarized strange quark densities in nuclei, which are connected to the nucleon "spin crisis" discussed by previous authors. The modified perturbation theory method presented combines advantages of both Kato's and Bloch's approaches for treating degenerate systems.
