# Constraining the parameterized neutron star equation of state with astronomical observations

> We utilise the phenomenologically parameterized piecewise polytropic equations of state to study various neutron star properties. We investigate the compliance of these equations of state with several astronomical observations. We also demonstrate that the theoretical estimates of the fractional moment of inertia cannot explain all the pulsar glitches observed. We model the crust as a solid spheroidal shell to calculate the fractional moment of inertia of fast-spinning neutron stars. We also show that the braking index obtained in a simple magnetic dipole radiation model with a varying moment of inertia deviates significantly from the observed data. Future developments in both theory and observations may allow us to use the fractional moment of inertia and braking index as observational constraints for neutron star equation of state.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that theoretical estimates of the fractional moment of inertia (FMI) from piecewise polytropic equations of state cannot explain all observed pulsar glitches, and that the braking index from a simple magnetic dipole radiation model with a varying moment of inertia deviates significantly from observed data, so neither quantity currently provides strong constraints on the neutron star equation of state.

## Method

The authors constructed piecewise polytropic equations of state (ALF4, AP3, BBB2, GNH3, WFF3, SLy) following the approach of Read (2009) and Lackey (thesis), with the low-density SLy equation of state for the crust and various polytropes for the core. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations for non-rotating neutron stars and used the Hartle-Thorne approximation for slow rotation, while for fast-spinning neutron stars they computed equilibrium configurations in axially symmetric spacetime using the LORENE libraries. The fractional moment of inertia was calculated as the ratio of crustal moment of inertia to total moment of inertia, and the braking index was derived from a frequency-dependent moment of inertia in a magnetic dipole radiation model.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass constraints: PSR J0348+0432 at 2.01 ± 0.04 solar masses (constraint C0), PSR J1614-2230 at 1.908 ± 0.016 solar masses (C1), PSR J0740+6620 at 2.14 +0.20/-0.18 solar masses (C2), PSR J1810+1744 at 2.13 ± 0.04 solar masses (C3)
- Mass-radius constraints: PSR J0030+0451 at 1.44 +0.15/-0.14 solar masses and 13.02 +1.24/-1.06 km (C4), and 1.34 +0.15/-0.16 solar masses and 12.71 +1.14/-1.19 km (C5); PSR J0740+6620 at 2.072 +0.067/-0.066 solar masses and 12.39 +1.30/-0.98 km (C6), and 2.08 ± 0.07 solar masses and 13.71 +2.67/-1.50 km (C7)
- Tidal deformability constraints for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: 70-580 (C8), 240-730 (C9), 133-686 (C10)
- Fraction of glitches (from 384 glitches in the Jodrell Bank catalogue) that cannot be explained: AP3 14.84%, GNH3 21.88%, SLy 18.23%
- Crust-core transition densities range from 6.659 × 10^13 to 2.014 × 10^14 g/cc
- Fastest rotation considered: 700 Hz, close to the 716 Hz observed for the fastest spinning radio pulsar
- Observed braking indices: Crab (B0531+21) at 2.342, Vela (B0833-45) at 1.4, B1509-58 at 2.839, J1846-0258 at 2.65, J1119-6127 at 2.684

## Context

This work extends the piecewise polytropic equation of state approach of Read (2009) and Lackey (thesis) by testing compliance with multiple astronomical constraints including mass measurements from Antoniadis (2013), Demorest (2010), Cromartie (2020), and Romani (2021); mass-radius measurements from Miller (2019) and Riley (2019, 2021); and tidal deformability constraints from Abbott (2018), Jiang (2020), and Li (2021). The authors show that the fractional moment of inertia cannot explain all glitches, supporting the need for core superfluidity as suggested by Anderson and Basu (2018), and that the magnetic dipole radiation model with varying moment of inertia fails to match observed braking indices, pointing to additional factors such as magnetic field evolution as discussed by other authors. The work enables future use of fractional moment of inertia and braking index as constraints once theoretical and observational capabilities improve.
