# Morphology of High-Multiplicity Events in Heavy Ion Collisions

> We discuss opportunities that may arise from subjecting high-multiplicity events in relativistic heavy ion collisions to an analysis similar to the one used in cosmology for the study of fluctuations of the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB). To this end, we discuss examples of how pertinent features of heavy ion collisions including global characteristics, signatures of collective flow and event-wise fluctuations are visually represented in a Mollweide projection commonly used in CMB analysis, and how they are statistically analyzed in an expansion over spherical harmonic functions. If applied to the characterization of purely azimuthal dependent phenomena such as collective flow, the expansion coefficients of spherical harmonics are seen to contain redundancies compared to the set of harmonic flow coefficients commonly used in heavy ion collisions. Our exploratory study indicates, however, that these redundancies may offer novel opportunities for a detailed characterization of those event-wise fluctuations that remain after subtraction of the dominant collective flow signatures. By construction, the proposed approach allows also for the characterization of more complex collective phenomena like higher-order flow and other sources of fluctuations, and it may be extended to the characterization of phenomena of non-collective origin such as jets.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that applying spherical harmonic analysis (commonly used in Cosmic Microwave Background studies) to high-multiplicity heavy ion collision events can provide novel opportunities for characterizing event-wise fluctuations after subtraction of dominant collective flow signatures, though the spherical harmonic expansion coefficients contain redundancies compared to the standard harmonic flow coefficients used in heavy ion physics.

## Method

The authors simulated particle distributions using the HIJING event generator for Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies, then superimposed elliptic flow a posteriori by modulating the azimuthal distribution with prescribed flow amplitudes. They applied a spherical harmonic decomposition (using the GLESP pixelization method) to the two-dimensional particle density maps in polar angle and azimuthal angle, and derived relations between the spherical harmonic coefficients and the standard flow coefficients for both even and odd harmonic modes.

## Key numbers

- The angular resolution used for the spherical harmonic decomposition corresponds to \(l_{max} = 100\), giving an angular resolution of approximately \(1.8^\circ\)
- A single semi-central Pb-Pb collision at \(\sqrt{s_{nn}} = 2.76\) TeV produced 12316 particles over more than 16 units of pseudorapidity
- For a baseline HIJING event without flow, the odd multipole power spectrum values \(C^S(l)\) were in the range \([10^{-5}; 10^{-4}]\) while even modes were up to a factor 1000 larger
- The model used an average elliptic flow amplitude \(v_2 = 0.10\) for the modulated events shown in Fig. 2, and \(v_2 = 0.07\) for the power spectrum analysis in Fig. 5

## Context

This work extends the analogy between Big Bang cosmology and heavy ion collisions ("Little Bangs") previously discussed in the literature, drawing on the fluid dynamic paradigm established by Heinz (2009), Romatschke (2009), and Teaney (2009). The approach builds on the discovery of non-vanishing odd harmonic flow coefficients by Alver and Roland (2010) and others, which demonstrated that initial-state fluctuations are propagated fluid dynamically. The method enables a new way to characterize event-wise fluctuations and could be extended to study phenomena of non-collective origin such as jets, complementing existing techniques like the Fourier decomposition of azimuthal dependence used by Ollitrault (1992) and the VPS review (2010).
