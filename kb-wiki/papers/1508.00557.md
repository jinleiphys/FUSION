# Woods-Saxon equivalent to a double folding potential

> A Woods-Saxon equivalent to a double folding potential in the surface region is obtained for the heavy-ion scattering potential. The Woods-Saxon potential has fixed geometry and was applied as a bare potential in the analysis of experimental data of several systems. A new analytical formula for the position and height of the Coulomb barrier is presented, which reproduces the results obtained using double folding potentials. This simple formula has been applied to estimate the fusion cross section above the Coulomb barrier. A comparison with experimental data is presented.

## Key claim

A Woods-Saxon potential with fixed geometry (radius parameter r0 = 1.3 fm, diffuseness a = 0.65 fm) can reproduce the tail of the double folding Sao Paulo potential in the surface region (r > R) for heavy-ion scattering, but only for strengths V0 between 10 and 20 MeV; the authors note a continuum ambiguity between V0 and r0 such that any combination leaving V0 exp(R/a) unchanged gives the same surface potential.

## Method

The authors developed a computer program to automatically search the three Woods-Saxon parameters (V0, r0, a) to fit the real part of the double folding Sao Paulo potential in the region r > R for several heavy-ion systems. They then fixed the geometry at r0 = 1.3 fm and a = 0.65 fm, allowed V0 and the imaginary depth W0 to vary freely, and performed optical model fits to elastic scattering angular distributions for six systems using the program SFRESCO. Based on the real potential with V0 = 15 MeV, they derived analytical formulas for the Coulomb barrier position RB and height VB that depend only on masses and charges.

## Key numbers

- Woods-Saxon diffuseness a = 0.65 fm and radius parameter r0 = 1.3 fm for all systems analysed.
- Real strength V0 ranges between 10 and 20 MeV for the fitted Woods-Saxon potentials.
- For the 6He+9Be system: RB = 7.62 fm (WS formula) vs 8.00 fm (PSP); VB = 1.22 MeV (WS) vs 1.32 MeV (PSP).
- For the 16O+208Pb system: RB = 11.68 fm (WS) vs 11.65 fm (PSP); VB = 77.07 MeV (WS) vs 75.90 MeV (PSP).
- For the 9Be+27Al system: Coulomb barrier in lab frame VB(lab) = 10.65 MeV from the analytical formula.
- For the 16O+58Ni system: Coulomb barrier in lab frame VB(lab) = 40.80 MeV.
- For the 9Be+208Pb system: Coulomb barrier in lab frame VB(lab) = 40.40 MeV.
- For the 9Be+144Sm system: Coulomb barrier in lab frame VB(lab) = 33.24 MeV.
- For the 9Be+64Zn system: Coulomb barrier in lab frame VB(lab) = 19.4 MeV.
- For the 9Be+89Y system: Coulomb barrier in lab frame VB(lab) = 23.25 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the Sao Paulo potential (PSP) approach of Chamon and collaborators, which uses double folding potentials with no free parameters, by providing a simple Woods-Saxon parameterization that reproduces the double folding tail. The analytical formulas for the Coulomb barrier (RB and VB) enable quick estimation of fusion cross sections using the Wong formula without numerical double folding calculations, and the fixed-geometry Woods-Saxon potential can be used directly in standard optical model codes that accept Woods-Saxon forms. The paper compares its fusion cross section predictions with experimental data from Gomes (2005), Moraes (2000), and Dasgupta (2007), among others, and notes that below-barrier fusion is underestimated because coupled-channels effects are not included.
