# Simulating the scalar glueball on the lattice

## Key claim
The scalar glueball mass in pure SU(3) Yang-Mills theory can be computed with high precision on anisotropic lattices using Symanzik-improved actions, but the lattice results show a strong dependence on the lattice spacing (the "scalar dip") that must be removed by including adjoint-representation terms in the action or by careful continuum extrapolation; the scalar glueball has not been unambiguously identified in experiment, though an extra state between 1.5 and 1.8 GeV is broadly expected.

## Method
The authors use Monte Carlo simulations of SU(3) Yang-Mills theory on anisotropic lattices (with temporal spacing smaller than spatial spacing) to compute glueball masses from two-point correlation functions of closed Wilson loop operators. The gauge action is Symanzik-improved by including larger loops (e.g., 2x1 rectangles) to remove O(a^2) discretisation errors, and for some simulations an adjoint-representation term is added to suppress the "scalar dip" artefact. Masses are extracted from effective mass plots and extrapolated to the continuum limit using data at multiple lattice spacings.

## Key numbers
- The scalar glueball mass in units of the physical scale r_0 (derived from the static potential) shows a strong dependence on lattice spacing, falling to a minimum then rising again, this is the "scalar dip" effect.
- On a highly anisotropic lattice with a_s/a_t = 6, the ground-state scalar and first excited scalar energies are measured to 1% statistical precision.
- Simulations using the new discretisation with adjoint-representation terms show very weak lattice spacing dependence out to coarse spatial lattices with a ≈ 0.25 fm.
- The lightest six states in the SU(3) Yang-Mills spectrum are presented, with the ground-state and first-excited scalar glueball masses determined after removing scalar dip artefacts and performing continuum extrapolation; these masses are consistent within errors with earlier determinations by Michael (1989), Bali (1993), and Morningstar (1999).

## Context
This work extends the lattice glueball spectrum calculations of Michael (1989), Bali (1993), and Morningstar (1999) by addressing the "scalar dip" artefact, a bulk first-order phase transition line in the fundamental-adjoint coupling plane, first noted by Heller (1995), that artificially lowers the scalar mass near a critical point. The improved anisotropic lattice method enables reliable continuum extrapolation and enables future calculations of glueball properties in full QCD with dynamical quarks, including mixing with quark-model mesons and decay widths, as well as calculations of chromoelectric and magnetic matrix elements and structure form factors.
