# Bose-Einstein correlations from "within"

> We describe an attempt to model numerically Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) from "within", i.e., by using them as the most fundamental ingredient of some Monte Carlo event generator (MC) rather than considering them as a kind of (more or less important, depending on the actual situation) "afterburner", which inevitably changes original physical content of the MC code used to model multiparticle production process.

## Key claim
The authors propose a Monte Carlo event generator that incorporates Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) as a fundamental built-in ingredient rather than as an afterburner added to existing codes, but they note that their approach is still exploratory and requires further development to fully account for multiparticle symmetrization effects.

## Method
The algorithm divides available energy among pions by forming elementary emitting cells (EECs), each containing particles of the same charge. Within each EEC, particles are added sequentially with probability \(P = P_0 \cdot e^{-E/T}\) until a failure occurs, producing a geometrical (Bose-Einstein) multiplicity distribution. To extend the model to three dimensions, the authors assign space-time positions from a static source distribution \(\rho(r)\) and correlate momenta with positions via the symmetrization condition \(1 + \cos(\delta p \cdot \delta r)\), accepting only configurations where \(\cos(\delta p \cdot \delta r) \le 2 \cdot \text{Rand} - 1\).

## Key numbers
- Source radius \(R = 1\) fm used for the static spherical source \(\rho(r)\) in the 3-dimensional version.
- The probability to add a particle to an EEC is \(P = P_0 \cdot e^{-E/T}\), where \(P_0 \in (0,1)\) is a constant, \(E\) is the energy of the first particle, and \(T\) is a temperature-like parameter.
- Most EECs contain only 1, 2, or 3 particles, justifying the approximation of symmetrizing only with the seed particle.

## Context
This work extends the earlier approach of OMT (2004), which used a grand-canonical-like distribution with temperature and chemical potential to describe rapidity and multiplicity distributions in \(e^+e^-\) annihilation. It also builds on the concept of elementary emitting cells proposed by BSWW (2000) and on the authors' own simple cascade model (CAS) and afterburner method (UWW, 2004). The new 3-dimensional algorithm enables calculation of \(C_2(Q_{x,y,z})\) in addition to \(C_2(Q_{\text{inv}})\), and future work could include Coulomb and other final-state interactions, resonances, finite source lifetime, and full multiparticle symmetrization.
