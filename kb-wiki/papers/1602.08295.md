# Global correlations between electromagnetic and spectroscopic properties of collective $2^+_1$ and $2^+_2$ states

> By using the general triaxial rotor model (TRM) and the phonon-configuration mixing scheme within an anharmonic-vibrator(AHV) framework, a series of global correlations between electromagnetic properties of nuclear $2^+_1$ and $2^+_2$ states are analytically established. The correlations from both models can roughly describe the experimental data involving quadrupole collectivity with few exceptions. Furthermore, there seems to be a robust orthogonal transformation between the AHV and TRM bases for realistic nuclear systems, suggesting that the two models may in fact be describing the collective features of nuclear low-lying states in similar model spaces.

# Global correlations between electromagnetic and spectroscopic properties of collective \(2^+_1\) and \(2^+_2\) states

## Key claim

Both the triaxial rotor model (TRM) and the anharmonic-vibrator (AHV) model with phonon-configuration mixing can describe global correlations between electromagnetic properties of the lowest two \(2^+\) states, including the \(Q(2^+_1) = -Q(2^+_2)\) correlation, across a wide range of nuclei, but the abstract's claim that "both models can roughly describe the experimental data" is qualified by the finding that the TRM fails for vibrational Ru, Pd, and Cd isotopes with \(R = 2.1 \sim 2.4\) when spectral information is used, and that the AHV gives closer agreement with experiment for the E2 sum-rule coefficients.

## Method

The authors analytically derived correlations among excitation energies, E2 transition rates, quadrupole moments, and magnetic moments for the \(2^+_1\) and \(2^+_2\) states using the general triaxial rotor model (TRM) with three independent inertia parameters and the anharmonic-vibrator (AHV) model with one-phonon and two-phonon configuration mixing. They then compared these model predictions against experimental data from the ENSDF database for 203 TRM-solvable nuclei and 177 AHV-applicable nuclei, performing a multiple linear fit of the generalized Kumar-Cline sum rule to 78 nuclei with complete E2 data. Finally, they computed the orthogonal transformation angle \(\theta\) between the TRM and AHV Hamiltonian matrices for the 203 nuclei that are describable by both models, and constructed an \(R\)-normalized distribution \(P(|\theta|)\) to test whether the transformation is globally robust.

## Key numbers

- 203 nuclei in the ENSDF are TRM-solvable; their \(R = E(4^+_1)/E(2^+_1)\) distribution shows peaks at \(R \simeq 5/2\) (gamma-instability) and \(R \simeq 10/3\) (axially-symmetric rotor).
- 177 nuclei with \(R = 2.05 \sim 3.15\) are identified as AHV-applicable; most fall beyond the \(E(2^+_2) = 2E(2^+_1)\) line.
- Multiple linear fit of Eq. (fit-be2-q) to 78 nuclei yields coefficients \(c_1 = 0.479\) and \(c_2 = 0.188\) with correlation coefficient \(R = 0.980\).
- The \(R\)-normalized \(P(|\theta|)\) distribution for 203 TRM-and-AHV-describable nuclei shows a dominant peak at \(|\theta| = 34.9(2)^\circ\).
- In the IBM-1 with two bosons, the SU(3) transformation angle is numerically found to be \(\theta = 28.1^\circ\).
- Experimental magnetic moment ratios \(\mu(2^+_2)/\mu(2^+_1)\) for 20 nuclei are listed; most are close to 1 within error, except \(^{92}\)Zr (ratio \(-4(3)\)) and \(^{184,186}\)W (ratios \(0.4(1)\) and \(0.6(1)\)).

## Context

This work extends the experimental survey of Allmond (2014) which demonstrated a global \(Q(2^+_1) = -Q(2^+_2)\) correlation, and the shell-model random-interaction study by Lei (2015) which observed the same correlation. The authors show that both the TRM (previously applied by the same group to Os, Pt, Er, and Ge isotopes) and the AHV (previously used by Casten et al. (1990) for yrast-band systematics) can reproduce this correlation, and that the two models are connected by a nearly universal orthogonal transformation. This enables future predictions of electromagnetic properties for nuclei where experimental data are missing, and raises the question of whether collective rotational features have a deeper origin than simple rotor behavior.
