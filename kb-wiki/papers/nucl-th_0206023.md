# Hadrons in the Nuclear Medium-Role of Light Front Nuclear Theory

> The problem of understanding the nuclear effects observed in lepton-nucleus deep-inelastic-scattering (the EMC effect) is still with us. Standard nuclear models (those using only hadronic degrees of freedom) are not able to account for the EMC effect. Thus it is necessary to understand how the nuclear medium modifies quark wave functions in the nucleus. Possibilities for such modifications, represented by the quark meson coupling model, and the suppression of point-like-configurations are discussed, and methods to experimentally choose between these are reviewed.

# Hadrons in the Nuclear Medium – Role of Light Front Nuclear Theory

## Key claim

Standard nuclear models using only hadronic degrees of freedom cannot account for the EMC effect; therefore quark-gluon modifications of nucleon structure in the medium must be invoked, but no consensus has been reached on which modification mechanism is correct, and the goal of explaining the EMC effect and predicting new experimental consequences is not yet attained but is within reach.

## Method

The paper uses light-front quantization of the Walecka model in the mean-field approximation to derive nuclear wave functions expressed in terms of plus-momenta, then computes the nucleon light-cone momentum distribution \(f_N(y)\) for infinite nuclear matter. This distribution is used in a convolution formula for the nuclear structure function \(F_{2A}\), and the result is expanded about \(y=1\) to isolate binding and Fermi-motion contributions. The analysis is extended beyond mean-field to Hamiltonians with only nucleon degrees of freedom and to models including explicit meson degrees of freedom. Two non-standard mechanisms are then examined using a light-front quark model of the proton: the quark meson coupling model (QMC), where the quark mass is reduced by a scalar field, and the suppression of point-like configurations (PLC), where the nuclear mean field depends on the quark separation \(r^2\).

## Key numbers

- The binding energy per nucleon is approximately \(1 - \overline{M}/M_N \approx 16/940\), giving \(\epsilon \approx 0.017\).
- The scalar field in the QMC model is taken as \(\sigma \approx 40\ \text{MeV}\), reducing the quark mass by that amount.
- For the meson-included model, reproducing the EMC effect requires \(\epsilon \equiv P_m^+/M_A \approx 0.07\), which corresponds to a large enhancement of the nuclear sea.
- The nucleon Fermi momentum \(k_F\) appears in the width of the distribution \(f_N(y)\), which is peaked at \(y=1\) with support between \(1 \pm k_F/\overline{M}\).
- In the tagged structure function reaction \(e'D \to e+N+X\), different models that give the same deep-inelastic scattering produce differences in the ratio \(G(x_1,x_2)\) of more than \(50\%\).

## Context

This paper reviews the long-standing failure of conventional nuclear theory to explain the EMC effect, first observed in 1982, and extends the analysis using the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem (1958) to show that binding and Fermi motion alone cannot account for the data. It contrasts the mean-field results of the Walecka model with calculations including nucleon-nucleon correlations and explicit meson degrees of freedom, noting that the meson-included approach conflicts with Drell-Yan data from E772 at Fermilab (Alde et al.). The paper then examines two non-standard mechanisms: the quark meson coupling model of Guichon (1987) and Saito and Thomas, and the suppression of point-like configurations of Frankfurt and Strikman, validated by Frank, Jennings, and Miller (1995). It proposes tagged structure functions in deuterium as a way to discriminate between models, following Melnitchouk et al. (1996).
