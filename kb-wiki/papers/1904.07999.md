# Improved photomeson model for interactions of cosmic ray nuclei

> Photon-hadronic interactions are important for the sources and the transport of Ultra-High Energy Cosmic Rays (UHECRs). Current state-of-the-art cosmic ray transport simulations handle nuclear disintegration at energies of the Giant Dipole Resonance at a more sophisticated level, as well as the photohadronic interactions of nucleons in the high-energy regime above the pion production threshold. However, the interactions of nuclei above the pion production threshold are commonly modeled by treating the nucleus as a superposition of free nucleons -- ignoring the effect of the nuclear medium. We construct an improved, inclusive model for the photomeson regime for nuclei with $A \leq 56$ by employing more accurate, data-driven parametrizations of the interaction cross section, the fragmentation of the primary nucleus and the inclusive pion production cross section that directly affects the production of astrophysical neutrinos. We apply our results to two multi-messenger scenarios (Tidal Disruption Events and Gamma-Ray Bursts) in which photonuclear interactions in the photomeson regime are the dominant cooling process for the highest energy cosmic rays. While we find moderate changes to the mass composition of UHECRs, the astrophysical neutrino fluxes exhibit a significant (factor of a few) reduction compared to the naïve superposition of free nucleons for sources of UHECR nuclei with a populated cascade. The numerical code implementing the model has been made publicly available, which facilitates the integration of our results in similar frameworks.

## Key claim

The paper constructs an improved, data-driven model for photomeson interactions of cosmic ray nuclei (A ≤ 56) that replaces the common "superposition of free nucleons" approach, finding that while the mass composition of ultra-high energy cosmic rays changes only moderately, astrophysical neutrino fluxes are reduced by a factor of a few compared to the naive superposition model for sources where photomeson production is the dominant cooling process.

## Method

The authors developed an Empirical Model (EM) that improves upon the Single Particle Model (SPM) in three aspects: (1) a universal function for the total photonuclear cross section per nucleon near the Δ-resonance with an energy-dependent mass scaling exponent α(ε_r) above 1 GeV to account for nuclear shadowing; (2) a data-driven inclusive pion production cross section with a separate mass scaling exponent α_π(ε_r) that transitions from A^(2/3) scaling near threshold to A scaling above 50 GeV; and (3) empirical parametrizations for nuclear fragmentation that go beyond the simple A-1 remnant assumption. The model was applied to two astrophysical multi-messenger scenarios: Tidal Disruption Events and Gamma-Ray Bursts.

## Key numbers

- The Δ-resonance peak in the nuclear cross section per nucleon is 20% wider at half height and 30% lower at the peak compared to the free nucleon cross section
- For ε_r < 1 GeV, the universal curve uses α = 1 (A scaling); for ε_r ≥ 1 GeV, the mass scaling exponent α(ε_r) transitions from near 1 toward the theoretical limit α_lim = 2/3 at high energies
- The inclusive pion production cross section uses α_π = 2/3 scaling near threshold (ε_r < 1 GeV), transitioning to α_π = 1 (A scaling) for ε_r > 50 GeV
- The SPM with A scaling predicts on average more than twice the number of pions compared to data for ⁴⁰Ca, while A^(2/3) scaling leads to less than half
- The multiplicity ratio data show a sharp increase of the scaling exponent in the interval from 1-3 GeV
- The model applies to nuclei with A ≤ 56

## Context

This work extends the photomeson modeling beyond the Single Particle Model (SPM) used in prior cosmic ray transport simulations by Kampert (2012) and Batista (2016), which treated nuclei as superpositions of free nucleons with a global mass scaling assumption. The improved model enables more accurate predictions for multi-messenger scenarios where photomeson production dominates UHECR cooling, particularly for neutrino flux calculations from sources like Gamma-Ray Bursts and Tidal Disruption Events. The numerical code has been made publicly available for integration into other simulation frameworks.
