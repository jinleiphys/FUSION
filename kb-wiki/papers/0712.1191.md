# Nuclear alpha-particle condensates: Definitions, occurrence conditions, and consequences

> There has been a recent flurry of interest in the possibility of condensates of $α$-particles in nuclei. In this letter we discuss occurrence conditions for such states. Using the quantality condition of Mottelson we show that condensates are only marginally expected in $α$-particle states. We proceed to demonstrate that few-body nuclear condensates are ill-defined, and emphasize the conflict between $α$-localization and $α$-condensate formation. We also explore the connection between Ikeda diagrams, linear chains, and Tonks-Girardeau gases. Our findings show that no new information is contained in the approximations of nuclear states as $α$-cluster condensates. Furthermore, condensates of more than three $α$-particles are very unlikely to exist due to couplings to other degrees of freedom.

# Nuclear alpha-particle condensates: Definitions, occurrence conditions, and consequences

## Key claim

Alpha-particle condensates in nuclei are only marginally expected based on the Mottelson quantality condition, and the concept is of little use because such states are merely approximations to well-known alpha-cluster states with no observable differences. Condensates of more than three alpha-particles are very unlikely to exist due to couplings to other degrees of freedom, and few-body nuclear condensates are ill-defined due to conflicts between localization and condensate formation.

## Method

The authors apply the Mottelson quantality condition to the alpha-alpha potential parametrized as a sum of attractive and repulsive gaussians, evaluating whether alpha-particles fall in the mean-field or localization regime. They analyze the one-body density matrix for translationally invariant wave functions of N identical gaussians and compute condensate fractions for both product and localized wave functions. They estimate the spreading width of alpha-particle states using Fermi-liquid transport theory and compare it to the average level spacing at excitation energies near the alpha-breakup threshold to assess the survival probability of condensate structures.

## Key numbers

- The Mottelson quantality parameter for the alpha-alpha system is Lambda_Mot ≈ 0.1–0.2, placing alpha-particles in the mean-field range but with strong tendency to localize
- The alpha-alpha potential minimum V_min ≈ 5–8 MeV (including Coulomb energy of ≈ 2 MeV) at distance c_min ≈ 2.5–3.0 fm
- The condensate fraction for the translationally invariant gaussian product wave function decreases from 1 for N=2 toward about 0.57 for large N
- The Hoyle state in 12C has about 90% overlap with alpha-particles in relative s-waves, corresponding to an eigenvalue of about 0.7
- The spreading width of an alpha-particle state is estimated as Gamma_alpha ≈ 6 MeV, corresponding to an imaginary potential strength W_alpha ≈ 3 MeV
- The level spacing at excitation energy E* = 2Delta gives conservative estimates for preservation of condensate identity: A < sqrt(20) < 5 or A < 20, depending on the criterion used
- The excitation energy for alpha-condensates increases by about 7 MeV per additional alpha-particle: E* ≈ 7(A/4 – 2) MeV

## Context

This work extends the critical assessment of alpha-condensate claims following the proposal by Tohsaki et al. (2001) of a condensate-like wave function for the Hoyle state in 12C, and subsequent extensions by Funaki et al. (2002, 2003) and Yamada et al. (2004). The authors use the quantality condition from Mottelson (1999) and compare with the Brink-type alpha-cluster model (1966) and the microscopic cluster calculations of Uegaki et al. (1977). The paper contradicts the interpretation of these states as genuine Bose-Einstein condensates analogous to atomic condensates, and instead argues they are well-known cluster states. The connection to Ikeda diagrams (1968) and Tonks-Girardeau gases is explored but found to yield no new observable consequences. The work enables future studies to focus on the actual cluster structure of these states rather than pursuing the condensate interpretation.
