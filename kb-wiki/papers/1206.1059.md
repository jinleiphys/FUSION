# Thermodynamics of nuclear condensates and phase transitions in white dwarfs

> We study the thermodynamics of helium at densities relevant for white dwarf physics. We find evidence that, as the temperature is increased, there is first a first order transition between two superconducting phases followed by a second order transition to the normal state. These transitions occur, for realistic densities, at temperatures below the crystallization temperature and the crystalline state is likely to remain as the true ground state of the system. The calculations are performed with a screening but non-dynamical electron background and we comment on the impact of this and other approximations to our result.

## Key claim

The paper finds that a nuclear Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC) in helium white dwarfs would undergo a first-order phase transition between two superconducting phases followed by a second-order transition to the normal state as temperature increases, but these transitions occur below the crystallization temperature, so the crystalline state likely remains the true ground state; the authors can only set an upper bound for the first-order transition temperature and conjecture an additional second-order transition at the free Bose gas critical temperature.

## Method

The authors compute the one-loop effective potential for a system of nonrelativistic charged spin-0 bosons (helium nuclei) interacting via a screened Coulomb (Yukawa) potential, with the electrons treated as a screening but non-dynamical background. They solve the coupled equations for the condensate value and chemical potential at fixed ion density, using phonon and plasmon approximations in different momentum regimes, and compare free energies of the condensed and uncondensed phases to determine global stability.

## Key numbers

- The free Bose gas critical temperature is given by T_c^(0) = 2π (4π ζ(3/2)/3)^(-2/3) / (M l^2) ≈ 1.27 / (M l^2), where M is the helium nucleus mass and l is the interparticle spacing.
- The crystallization temperature is estimated as T_Coulomb ∼ (Ze)^2 / (180 l), corresponding to T_Coulomb ∼ (a_0 / l) × 7000 K, where a_0 is the Bohr radius.
- The numerical solution is shown for a density parameter l/a_0 = 35, corresponding to a mass density of ρ = 4.6 × 10^5 g/cm^3.
- The phonon approximation gives n = v^2 + (M m_s / (12 m_A)) T^2, where m_A^2 = 4π α Z^2 v^2 / M and m_s is the screening mass.
- The plasmon approximation gives n = v^2 + (Γ(5/4) / (2^(1/4) π^2)) M^(3/2) m_A^(5/4) T^(1/4) e^(-m_A/T).
- The free energy of the v=0 state at T_c^(0) is F_(v=0) = -ζ(5/2) T_c^(0) (M T_c^(0) / (2π))^(3/2).
- The free energy of the condensed state at T_c^(0) is smaller in magnitude than the v=0 free energy by a factor proportional to (M/m)^(3/2), where m is the electron mass.

## Context

This work extends earlier suggestions by Gabadadze et al. (2008, 2009) and Ashcroft that a nuclear BEC might form in helium white dwarfs at temperatures above the crystallization temperature, and builds on the authors' own prior work on the unusual phonon excitations (Bedaque et al., 2011) and enhanced neutrino emission (Bedaque et al., 2012) in such a condensate. The paper contradicts the expectation of a simple first-order transition by finding that the condensed phase is globally disfavored wherever the uncondensed phase exists, leading to a conjectured more complex phase diagram with both first-order and second-order transitions. This enables future work to determine whether the nuclear condensate phase can exist at all in realistic white dwarf conditions, which would have implications for white dwarf cooling via the predicted T^11 neutrino emission scaling.
