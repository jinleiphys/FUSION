# Delta-N Electromagnetic Transition

## Key claim

The nonrelativistic approach gives a good account of data for a free Delta electromagnetic transition, but is less important for an intrinsically relativistic nuclear many-body problem. The calculated E2/M1 ratio from the helicity amplitude method yields a value of -0.001, which differs from the experimental range of -0.013 ± 0.005 to -0.015 ± 0.002.

## Method

The authors calculate the E2/M1 electromagnetic transition ratio for the free Delta isobar using a nonrelativistic reduction of the current matrix element, starting from a Lorentz-invariant tensor form with form factors a1, a2, and a3. They relate these form factors to the magnetic dipole (G_M1) and electric quadrupole (G_E2) form factors, then derive helicity amplitudes A_{3/2} and A_{1/2} for the gamma N -> Delta transition. The ratio of these helicity amplitudes is used to extract the E2/M1 ratio.

## Key numbers

- Experimental E2/M1 ratio from data analysis: -0.013 ± 0.005 and -0.015 ± 0.002
- Calculated E2/M1 ratio from the nonrelativistic helicity amplitude method: -0.001
- Helicity amplitudes for proton excitation (in unspecified units): A_{3/2} = -179, A_{1/2} = -103
- Center-of-mass excitation energy of the Delta resonance: roughly 300 MeV
- Delta resonance quantum numbers: (J^p, T) = (3/2^+, 3/2)

## Context

This work extends the constituent quark model treatment of Becchi and Morpuro (1965), where the E2 transition is forbidden and the E2/M1 ratio vanishes, by incorporating the effect of quark-quark tensor and spin-spin forces as studied by subsequent authors. The nonrelativistic approach provides a good description for the free Delta decay but the authors note that a relativistic approach to nucleons and Delta in nuclear matter is needed to address the intrinsically relativistic nuclear many-body problem.
