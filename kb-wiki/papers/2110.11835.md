# Quantifying modeling uncertainties when combining multiple gravitational-wave detections from binary neutron star sources

> With the increasing sensitivity of gravitational-wave detectors, we expect to observe multiple binary neutron-star systems through gravitational waves in the near future. The combined analysis of these gravitational-wave signals offers the possibility to constrain the neutron-star radius and the equation of state of dense nuclear matter with unprecedented accuracy. However, it is crucial to ensure that uncertainties inherent in the gravitational-wave models will not lead to systematic biases when information from multiple detections are combined. To quantify waveform systematics, we perform an extensive simulation campaign of binary neutron-star sources and analyse them with a set of four different waveform models. Based on our analysis with about 38 simulations, we find that statistical uncertainties in the neutron-star radius decrease to $\pm 250\rm m$ ($2\%$ at $90\%$ credible interval) but that systematic differences between currently employed waveform models can be twice as large. Hence, it will be essential to ensure that systematic biases will not become dominant in inferences of the neutron-star equation of state when capitalizing on future developments.

## Key claim

Statistical uncertainties in the neutron-star radius decrease to ±250 m (2% at 90% credible interval) when combining 38 simulated binary neutron-star detections, but systematic differences between currently employed waveform models can be twice as large (up to ~440 m), meaning that systematic biases will become dominant over statistical ones when multiple detections are combined.

## Method

The authors performed 152 Bayesian parameter estimation simulations using 38 injected binary neutron-star signals (generated with the IMRPhenomD_NRTidalv2 waveform model and the most-likely equation of state from Dietrich (2020)) and recovered them with four different waveform models: TaylorF2, IMRPhenomD_NRTidal, IMRPhenomD_NRTidalv2, and SEOBNRv4_ROM_NRTidalv2. They combined the posterior distributions from multiple events using equation-of-state-based sampling that directly relates masses and tidal deformabilities through a set of 5000 chiral effective field theory constrained equations of state, and corrected for selection bias using a neural-network classifier trained on binary neutron-star parameter distributions.

## Key numbers

- Statistical uncertainty in the neutron-star radius (for a 1.4 solar mass star) decreases to ±250 m (2% at 90% credible interval) when combining all 38 simulated detections with the injection model IMRPhenomD_NRTidalv2
- Systematic shift for IMRPhenomD_NRTidal: radius recovered as 11.22 km (with +0.26/-0.30 km uncertainty), a shift of -329 m from the injected value of 11.55 km
- Systematic shift for SEOBNRv4_ROM_NRTidalv2: radius recovered as 11.12 km (with +0.28/-0.25 km uncertainty), a shift of -437 m from the injected value
- Systematic shift for TaylorF2: radius recovered as 11.71 km (with +0.24/-0.26 km uncertainty), a shift of +158 m from the injected value
- Injected neutron-star radius for a 1.4 solar mass star: 11.55 km, corresponding to a dimensionless tidal deformability of 292.5
- The prior on the equation of state gives a radius of 12.24 km (with +1.21/-1.44 km at 90% credibility) before gravitational-wave data are included
- Signal-to-noise ratio threshold for detection: 7
- Component masses sampled uniformly from 1 to 2 solar masses
- Spin magnitudes restricted to the range -0.05 to 0.05

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Dudi (2018), Samajdar (2018), and Gamba (2020) that investigated waveform systematics for individual binary neutron-star detections, but is the first to quantify how these systematics propagate when combining multiple detections (stacking). The results show that while statistical uncertainties follow the expected 1/sqrt(N) reduction predicted by Del Pozzo (2013), systematic biases from waveform models can become dominant after combining 20-30 events. This enables future observing runs (O4 and beyond) to incorporate these systematic uncertainties when combining multiple detections to constrain the neutron-star equation of state, particularly given projected detection rates of 10 (+52/-10) per year from KAGRA (2020) or 9-88 per year from Petrov (2021).
