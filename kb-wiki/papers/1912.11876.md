# Dense Matter and Neutron Stars: Some Basic Notions

> A number of properties of dense matter can be understood semiquantitatively in terms of simple physical arguments. We begin with the outer parts of neutron stars, and consider the density at which pressure ionization occurs, the density at which electrons become relativistic, the density at which neutrons drip out of nuclei, and the size of the equilibrium nucleus in dense matter. Subsequently, we treat the so-called "pasta" phases expected to occur at densities just below the density at which the transition from the crust to the liquid core of a neutron star occurs. We then consider aspects of superfluidity in dense matter. Estimates of pairing gaps in homogeneous nuclear matter are given, and the effect of the dense medium on the interaction between nucleons is described. Finally, we turn to superfluidity in the crust of neutron stars and especially the neutron superfluid density, an important quantity in the theory of sudden speedups of the rotation rate of some pulsars.

## Key claim

Many properties of dense matter in neutron stars can be understood semiquantitatively using simple physical arguments, but the paper explicitly restricts its scope to subnuclear densities because, while such matter makes up only a small fraction of the total mass of a neutron star, many observational effects depend crucially on the outer layers.

## Method

The paper uses analytical estimates based on the liquid drop model for nuclear masses, Thomas-Fermi theory for electron distributions, and BCS (Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer) theory for neutron superfluidity, supplemented by phase shift analysis of the neutron-neutron interaction. For the pasta phases, the author applies scaling arguments for surface and Coulomb energies as functions of nuclear dimensionality (spheres, rods, plates) and compares with results from quantum molecular dynamics simulations and relativistic mean field theory calculations.

## Key numbers

- The density at which electrons become relativistic is of order 10^6 gm/cm^3
- The bulk binding energy per nucleon in symmetric nuclear matter is approximately 16 MeV; the symmetry energy is approximately 32 MeV
- The saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter is n_s ≈ 0.16 fm^{-3}
- The neutron drip density is more than two orders of magnitude less than the saturation density of nuclear matter
- The proton fraction at neutron drip is x_drip ≈ 0.29
- The atomic number of nuclei in the outer parts of neutron stars ranges from 26 (iron) at the surface to around 40 in the inner crust
- The surface energy coefficient for a single nucleus is C_surf ≈ 18 MeV; the Coulomb energy coefficient is C_Coul ≈ 0.7 MeV
- The neutron-neutron scattering length is -18.5 fm; the effective range is approximately 2.7 fm
- The S-wave pairing gap in the BCS approximation has a maximum of around 3 MeV at a density of about one eighth of nuclear density, and vanishes for densities greater than 0.6 n_s
- When medium effects (spin fluctuation exchange) are included, the maximum neutron superfluid gap is reduced to the range 1-1.5 MeV
- The parameter L (derivative of the symmetry energy) from chiral effective field theory lies in the range 58.3-68.5 MeV; pasta phases are never the most stable state for L ≳ 80 MeV

## Context

This paper extends the line of work on dense matter physics for neutron stars reviewed in Baym (year not given in text) and Pethick and Ravenhall (year not given in text). It provides simple physical estimates that complement more detailed numerical calculations, such as those by Lorenz, Ravenhall, and Pethick (year not given in text) for the FPS interaction, and by Bao and Shen (year not given in text) for relativistic mean field models. The treatment of pasta phases and neutron superfluidity enables further work on observable phenomena including pulsar glitches (sudden speedups of rotation) and continuous gravitational waves from rotating neutron stars.
