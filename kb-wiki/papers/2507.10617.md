# Oscillations of hypothetical strange stars as an efficient source ultra-high-energy particles

> We investigate the dynamical behavior of strange quark matter (SQM) objects, such as stars and planets, when subjected to radial oscillations induced by tidal interactions in stellar systems. Our study demonstrates that SQM objects can efficiently convert mechanical energy into hadronic energy due to the critical mass density at their surfaces of 4.7*10^{14} g/cm^3, below which SQM becomes unstable and decays into photons, hadrons, and leptons. We show that even small-amplitude radial oscillations, with a radius change of as little as 0.1%, can result in significant excitation energies near the surface of SQM stars. This excitation energy is rapidly converted into electromagnetic energy over short timescales approximately 1 ms, potentially leading to observable astrophysical phenomena. Higher amplitude oscillations may cause fragmentation or dissolution of SQM stars, which has important implications for the evolution of binary systems containing SQM objects and the emission of gravitational waves.

# Oscillations of hypothetical strange stars as an efficient source ultra-high-energy particles

## Key claim

Strange quark matter (SQM) objects undergoing radial oscillations as small as 0.1% radius change can convert mechanical energy into hadronic and then electromagnetic energy on millisecond timescales, but only because the surface density falls below the critical threshold of 4.7×10¹⁴ g/cm³ where SQM becomes unstable and decays. The paper demonstrates this conversion efficiency for both planetary-mass and stellar-mass SQM objects, with the caveat that the mechanism depends on the specific MIT bag model parameters chosen (B=60 MeV/fm³, m_s=150 MeV).

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations with an MIT bag model equation of state for SQM, then perturbed equilibrium configurations by imposing monopole (breathing-mode) radial oscillations with fractional amplitude x = ΔR/R. They computed the excitation energy per baryon from density fluctuations around the saturation density n₀, integrated this over the volume where density falls below n₀, and estimated the electromagnetic energy release rate assuming rapid decay of unstable SQM into photons, hadrons, and leptons.

## Key numbers

- Critical surface density for SQM stability: ρ₀ = 4.665 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³ (also given as 4.7 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³)
- Saturation baryon density: n_s = 0.296136 fm⁻³, with corresponding energy per baryon ε_sat = 883.623 MeV
- Convexity parameter at saturation: β_s = 0.307124
- For an Earth-mass SQM planet (M = 1.0 M⊕, R = 145.1 m) with amplitude x = 0.001: total radiated energy E_rad = 7.315 × 10⁴² erg, average excitation per baryon = 1.205 keV
- For a 1.4 M⊙ SQM star (R = 10.31 km) with amplitude x = 0.001: E_rad = 6.630 × 10⁴⁵ erg, average excitation per baryon = 321.3 eV
- For the same 1.4 M⊙ star with amplitude x = 0.1: E_rad = 6.160 × 10⁵¹ erg, average excitation per baryon = 3.321 MeV
- Energy release timescale: approximately 1 ms
- Scaling law for planetary SQM: E_rad = 7.416 × 10⁴⁸ erg × (M/M⊕) × x²
- Scaling law for stellar SQM: E_rad ≈ 6.634 × 10⁵⁴ × x³ erg (for 1.4 M⊙, R = 10.31 km)

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of SQM stars and their gravitational-wave properties by Gondek-Rosinska et al. (2015) and builds on the foundational hypothesis by Witten (1984) that SQM may be the true ground state of baryonic matter. The specific mechanism of radial oscillation energy conversion was previously explored by Kutschera (2020). The paper addresses the less-investigated role of small radial oscillations in SQM objects, with implications for interpreting gravitational wave signals from binary systems containing SQM stars and for explaining unusually dense planetary objects such as those in the PSR B1257+12 system studied by Kuerban (2020). The results enable future modeling of electromagnetic transients coincident with SQM star oscillations or mergers, and refinement of gravitational-wave templates to distinguish SQM objects from neutron stars.
