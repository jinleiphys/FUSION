# Improved Maximum Entropy Method with an Extended Search Space

> We report on an improvement to the implementation of the Maximum Entropy Method (MEM). It amounts to departing from the search space obtained through a singular value decomposition (SVD) of the Kernel. Based on the shape of the SVD basis functions we argue that the MEM spectrum for given $N_τ$ data-points $D(τ)$ and prior information $m(ω)$ does not in general lie in this $N_τ$ dimensional singular subspace. Systematically extending the search basis will eventually recover the full search space and the correct extremum. We illustrate this idea through a mock data analysis inspired by actual lattice spectra, to show where our improvement becomes essential for the success of the MEM. To remedy the shortcomings of Bryan's SVD prescription we propose to use the real Fourier basis, which consists of trigonometric functions. Not only does our approach lead to more stable numerical behavior, as the SVD is not required for the determination of the basis functions, but also the resolution of the MEM becomes independent from the position of the reconstructed peaks.

# Improved Maximum Entropy Method with an Extended Search Space

## Key claim
The global extremum of the Maximum Entropy Method (MEM) functional does not in general lie in the Nτ-dimensional singular subspace obtained from Bryan's singular value decomposition (SVD) prescription; systematically extending the search basis beyond this subspace is required to recover the correct spectrum, and the Fourier basis provides a parametrization that makes reconstruction success independent of the arbitrary choice of ω_min.

## Method
The author demonstrates that Bryan's SVD basis functions for the exponential kernel K(τ,ω)=e^{-ωτ} possess an oscillating part that damps to zero beyond a certain frequency, meaning peaks at higher frequencies cannot be reconstructed. The proposed improvement involves parametrizing the spectral function using an extended search space with Next > Nτ parameters, systematically adding basis vectors until the MEM probability stops increasing. As an alternative, the author introduces a real Fourier basis (trigonometric functions) that requires no SVD computation and provides analytic basis functions.

## Key numbers
- Nτ = 12 data points were used in mock data analyses, with Nω = 1500 frequency points discretizing the interval ω ∈ [-10, 20]
- The SVD basis functions oscillate only up to ω_osz ≃ 0 for ω_min = -10, leaving positive frequencies above ω = 5 without structure
- With Bryan's SVD basis (Next = 12), the log-probability was Q = log[P_MEM] ≃ -10^4; extending to Next = 50 improved this to Q = -5.5
- Mock data were perturbed with Gaussian noise σ_j = 0.01 × j × D_ideal_j for the multi-peak test, and σ_i = 0.0001 × D_ideal_i for the single delta peak test
- The Fourier basis yields position-independent reconstruction width, while the SVD basis shows deteriorating resolution for peaks at ω > 5

## Context
This work extends the standard MEM implementation of Bryan (1990) and the Bayesian inference framework established by Jarrell (1996), Nakahara (1999), and Asakawa (2000). The proof of uniqueness of the MEM solution in the full Nω-dimensional space was given by Asakawa (2000). The mock data spectra are inspired by lattice QCD correlators studied in Rothkopf (2011). The proposed Fourier basis addresses the arbitrary ω_min dependence that limits Bryan's SVD prescription, enabling more reliable reconstruction of spectral functions from Euclidean lattice data, particularly for heavy quarkonium studies where peak positions vary.
