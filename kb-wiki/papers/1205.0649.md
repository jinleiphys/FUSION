# Theory of neutrinoless double beta decay

> Neutrinoless double beta decay, which is a very old and yet elusive process, is reviewed. Its observation will signal that lepton number is not conserved and the neutrinos are Majorana particles. More importantly it is our best hope for determining the absolute neutrino mass scale at the level of a few tens of meV. To achieve the last goal certain hurdles have to be overcome involving particle, nuclear and experimental physics. Nuclear physics is important for extracting the useful information from the data. One must accurately evaluate the relevant nuclear matrix elements, a formidable task. To this end, we review the sophisticated nuclear structure approaches recently been developed, which give confidence that the needed nuclear matrix elements can be reliably calculated. From an experimental point of view it is challenging, since the life times are long and one has to fight against formidable backgrounds. If a signal is found, it will be a tremendous accomplishment. Then, of course, the real task is going to be the extraction of the neutrino mass from the observations. This is not trivial, since current particle models predict the presence of many mechanisms other than the neutrino mass, which may contribute or even dominate this process. We will, in particular, consider the following processes: (i)The neutrino induced, but neutrino mass independent contribution. (ii)Heavy left and/or right handed neutrino mass contributions. (iii)Intermediate scalars (doubly charged etc). (iv)Supersymmetric (SUSY) contributions. We will show that it is possible to disentangle the various mechanisms and unambiguously extract the important neutrino mass scale, if all the signatures of the reaction are searched in a sufficient number of nuclear isotopes.

## Key claim

The observation of neutrinoless double beta decay would establish that neutrinos are Majorana particles and provide the best hope for determining the absolute neutrino mass scale at the level of a few tens of meV, but extracting the neutrino mass from observations is not trivial because many mechanisms other than the neutrino mass may contribute or even dominate the process.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework of neutrinoless double beta decay, covering the light and heavy neutrino exchange mechanisms, left-right current interference, intermediate scalar exchange, and supersymmetric contributions. They examine the nuclear matrix element calculations using four main approaches: Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximations, the interacting boson model, the energy density functional method, and the large basis Interacting Shell Model. The paper also discusses the short-range behavior of transition operators, including the role of pion exchange between nucleons and six-quark clusters in nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The strongest experimental lower limit on the half-life of $^{76}$Ge is $T_{1/2}^{0\nu} \geq 1.9 \times 10^{25}$ years (Heidelberg-Moscow experiment)
- The strongest experimental lower limit on the half-life of $^{100}$Mo is $T_{1/2}^{0\nu} \geq 1.0 \times 10^{24}$ years (NEMO3 experiment)
- The strongest experimental lower limit on the half-life of $^{130}$Te is $T_{1/2}^{0\nu} \geq 3.0 \times 10^{24}$ years (CUORICINO experiment)
- The strongest experimental lower limit on the half-life of $^{136}$Xe is $T_{1/2}^{0\nu} \geq 5.7 \times 10^{24}$ years (KamLAND-Zen experiment)
- There is a claimed observation of $0\nu\beta\beta$-decay of $^{76}$Ge with half-life $T^{0\nu}_{1/2} = 2.23^{+0.44}_{-0.31}\times 10^{25}$ years
- The first geochemical observation of $\beta\beta$-decay gave an estimated half-life $T_{1/2} (^{130}Te) = 1.4 \times 10^{21}$ years (Ingram and Reynolds, 1950)
- The $2\nu\beta\beta$-decay has been recorded for eleven nuclei: $^{48}$Ca, $^{76}$Ge, $^{82}$Se, $^{96}$Zr, $^{100}$Mo, $^{116}$Cd, $^{128}$Te, $^{130}$Te, $^{150}$Nd, $^{136}$Xe, $^{238}$U

## Context

This review extends the theoretical understanding of neutrinoless double beta decay following the discovery of neutrino oscillations by SuperKamiokande (1998), SNO, and KamLAND, which established that neutrinos have mass. The paper builds on earlier work by Kotani and collaborators that revived interest in the process, and on the left-right symmetric models of Grand Unification developed by Salam, Pati, Mohapatra, and Senjanovic. The review enables future experimental programs to disentangle the various possible mechanisms contributing to $0\nu\beta\beta$-decay by searching for all signatures of the reaction across a sufficient number of nuclear isotopes, and provides the nuclear matrix element calculations needed to extract the neutrino mass scale from future observations.
