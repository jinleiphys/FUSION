# Role of electronic shell in the double beta decay

> We demonstrate that the limiting energy available for ejected electrons in double beta decay is diminished by about 400 eV due to inelastic processes in the electronic shell.

# Role of electronic shell in the double beta decay

## Key claim
The limiting energy available for ejected electrons in double beta decay is diminished by about 400 eV due to inelastic processes in the atomic electronic shell, but this shift does not alter the earlier conclusion that the neutrinoless double beta decay mode has not been observed yet.

## Method
The authors employed the shake-off (SO) approximation, neglecting final state interactions between beta electrons and the atomic electronic shell, to compute the shift in available energy. They used nonrelativistic Hartree-Fock computer codes to calculate the ground state energies of the initial atom and the final doubly charged ion, then applied closure to sum over excited states. Corrections from final state interactions (FSI) were estimated separately using the Sommerfeld parameter.

## Key numbers
- For germanium (Z=32) double beta decay: δ₁ = 8797.1 eV, δ₂ = -8446.1 eV, giving δ₁+δ₂ = 351 eV; FSI contribution = 0.6 eV; total δQ = 352 eV.
- For xenon (Z=54) double beta decay: δ₁ = 17705.1 eV, δ₂ = -17292.4 eV, giving δ₁+δ₂ = 413 eV; FSI contribution = 1 eV; total δQ = 414 eV.
- The ground state energy of the Ge atom (Z=32) is ε(Z) = -56449.2 eV; the ground state energy of the Se⁺⁺ ion (Z=34) is ε⁰ = -65246.3 eV.
- The ground state energy of the Xe atom (Z=54) is ε(Z) = -196714.2 eV; the ground state energy of the Ba⁺⁺ ion (Z=56) is ε⁰ = -214419.3 eV.
- Relativistic corrections to δ₁ are about 2% for germanium and about 1 keV for xenon, giving an uncertainty of order 10% for the nonrelativistic result.

## Context
This work extends earlier claims from preprints (referenced as [5]-[7]) that the atomic electronic shell inserts an uncertainty of order 3 keV into double beta decay analysis. The authors show the actual shift is smaller, about 400 eV, and that their result does not support the claim (from [7]) that neutrinoless double beta decay had been detected in experiments [8] and [9]. The calculation enables more precise determination of the energy endpoint for both two-neutrino and neutrinoless double beta decay experiments, particularly for germanium and xenon isotopes.
