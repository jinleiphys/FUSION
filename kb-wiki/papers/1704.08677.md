# Fluctuations in an established transmission in the presence of a complex environment

> In various situations where wave transport is preeminent, like in wireless communication, a strong established transmission is present in a complex scattering environment. We develop a novel approach to describe emerging fluctuations, which combines a transmitting channel and a chaotic background in a unified effective Hamiltonian. Modeling such a background by random matrix theory, we derive exact non-perturbative results for both transmission and reflection distributions at arbitrary absorption that is typically present in real systems. Remarkably, in such a complex scattering situation, the transport is governed by only two parameters: an absorption rate and the ratio of the so-called spreading width to the natural width of the transmission line. In particular, we find that the established transmission disappears sharply when this ratio exceeds unity. The approach exemplifies the role of the chaotic background in dephasing the deterministic scattering.

# Fluctuations in an established transmission in the presence of a complex environment

## Key claim

The paper develops an exact non-perturbative theory for transmission and reflection fluctuations when a single deterministic transmission channel coexists with a chaotic background, showing that the transport is governed by only two parameters: an absorption rate and the ratio of the spreading width to the natural width of the transmission line. The established transmission disappears sharply when this ratio exceeds unity, but this threshold behavior is modified by finite absorption in the background.

## Method

The authors model the system using an effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian that combines a single resonance (doorway state) coupled to two scattering channels with a chaotic background represented by a random matrix from the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE). The scattering matrix is derived via the strength function formalism, and the joint distribution of the real and imaginary parts of the background Green's function is used to obtain exact expressions for the transmission and reflection distributions at arbitrary absorption. The analytical results are validated against Monte Carlo simulations with random matrices of size 200x200.

## Key numbers

- The transmission distribution at zero absorption is given by $\mathcal{P}_0(T) = \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{T(1-T)}} \frac{\eta}{1+(\eta^2-1)T}$ for $0 \leq T \leq 1$, where $\eta = \Gamma_\downarrow / \Gamma_0$ is the ratio of spreading width to escape width.
- The mean transmission at zero absorption is $\langle T \rangle = (1+\eta)^{-1}$, and the variance is $\langle T^2 \rangle - \langle T \rangle^2 = \frac{\eta}{2(1+\eta)^2}$.
- The transmission distribution becomes symmetric at $\eta = 1$, where the variance attains its maximum value.
- For weak absorption ($\gamma \ll 1$), the leading-order correction to the transmission distribution is $\mathcal{P}_{\gamma \ll 1}(T) \approx \mathcal{P}_0(T) \exp\left[-\frac{\gamma(1+(\eta-1)\sqrt{T})^2}{8\eta\sqrt{T}(1-\sqrt{T})}\right]$.
- For strong absorption ($\gamma \gg 1$), the transmission distribution approximates $\mathcal{P}_{\gamma \gg 1}(T) \approx \frac{\sqrt{\gamma\eta}(1+\sqrt{T})}{4\sqrt{\pi}(1-T)T^{3/4}\sqrt{1+(\eta^2-1)T}} \exp\left[-\frac{\gamma(1-(\eta+1)\sqrt{T})^2}{8\eta\sqrt{T}(1-\sqrt{T})}\right]$.
- Numerical simulations used $5 \times 10^4$ realizations of random $200 \times 200$ GOE matrices.

## Context

This work extends the strength function formalism from nuclear physics (Bohr) and the random matrix theory approach to scattering (Verbaarschot 1985, Fyodorov 1997, Mitchell 2010, Fyodorov 2011) to the case where a deterministic transmission channel coexists with a chaotic background. It provides the first complete characterization of transmission fluctuations for such a model, which was not reported in the literature despite the formalism's long history (Harn 1986, Sokolov 1997, Guhr 1999, Savin 2003, Sokolov 2010, Zelevinsky 2016). The results are relevant for wireless communication devices and chaotic reverberation chambers, and the method can be generalized to other symmetry classes and multichannel transmission.
