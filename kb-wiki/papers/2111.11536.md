# Decision Theory for the Mass Measurements at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams

> Nuclear physics facilities, like the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB), can potentially perform many nuclear mass measurements of exotic isotopes. Each measurement comes with a particular cost, both in time and money, and thus it is important to establish which mass measurements are the most informative. In this article, we show that one can use the Kullback-Leibler divergence to determine the information gained by a mass measurement. We model the information gain obtained by nuclear mass measurements from two perspectives: first from the perspective of theoretical nuclear mass models, and the second from the perspective of r-process nucleosynthesis. While this work specifically analyzes the abilities of FRIB, other facilities worldwide could benefit from a similar use of information gain in order to decide which experiments are optimal.

# Decision Theory for the Mass Measurements at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams

## Key claim

The Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence can be used to rank which nuclear mass measurements at FRIB provide the most information, but the ranking depends strongly on whether the goal is to constrain theoretical mass models or to improve r-process nucleosynthesis models, and the authors assume that measurement cost is dominated by beam time.

## Method

The authors model the information gain from FRIB mass measurements using the KL divergence between prior and posterior Gaussian distributions, where the prior represents either the spread across 14 theoretical mass models or the sensitivity of four r-process scenarios (low-entropy hot wind, high-entropy hot wind, cold wind, and neutron star mergers) to mass changes. They estimate experimental uncertainties for Penning trap mass spectrometry (PTMS) and time-of-flight mass spectrometry (TOFMS) using projected FRIB beam rates, assuming a 24-hour PTMS measurement and a 100-hour TOFMS measurement, and select the smaller uncertainty for each nucleus.

## Key numbers

- For PTMS, the relative statistical uncertainty is approximately $\delta m/m \approx R^{-1} n^{-1/2}$, with assumed resolving power $R = 10^5$ and observation time $t_{\rm obs} = 100$ ms; systematic uncertainty is assumed to be $\delta m/m|_{\rm syst} = 10^{-8}$.
- For TOFMS, the relative statistical uncertainty is approximately $\delta m/m \approx \sigma_{\rm TOF}/({\rm TOF} \sqrt{n})$, with assumed $\sigma_{\rm TOF}/{\rm TOF} = 10^{-4}$; systematic uncertainty is $\delta m/m|_{\rm syst} = 5 \times 10^{-6} (1 + (N - N_{\rm ref}))$.
- The ten nuclei with largest KL divergence relative to theoretical mass models are, in order: $^{42}{\rm Si}$ ($D_{\rm KL} = 8.36$), $^{41}{\rm Si}$ ($D_{\rm KL} = 8.34$), $^{43}{\rm P}$ ($D_{\rm KL} = 8.19$), $^{67}{\rm Mn}$, $^{59}{\rm Ti}$, $^{44}{\rm P}$, $^{77}{\rm Ni}$, $^{45}{\rm S}$, $^{68}{\rm Mn}$, and $^{47}{\rm Cl}$.
- For the low-entropy hot wind r-process scenario, the nucleus with largest KL divergence is $^{133}{\rm In}$ ($D_{\rm LEHW} = 8.41$); for the high-entropy hot wind, $^{136}{\rm Sn}$ ($D_{\rm HEHW} = 10.6$); for the cold wind, $^{133}{\rm In}$ ($D_{\rm CW} = 7.60$); for neutron star mergers, $^{133}{\rm In}$ ($D_{\rm NSM} = 7.97$).
- The $F$ values from Mumpower (2016) used for r-process theoretical uncertainty range over four orders of magnitude (0.01 to 101.39), while theoretical mass model uncertainties span only a factor of about four.

## Context

This work extends the application of decision theory and information gain to nuclear mass measurements, building on prior sensitivity studies such as Mumpower (2016) for r-process abundances. The authors note that the $^{133}{\rm In}$ mass was measured by Izzo (2021) while this work was being completed, and they identify the ($Z=50$, $N=82$) region as a source of many informative measurements. The approach enables facilities like FRIB to prioritize measurements based on quantitative information gain, and the authors suggest that after each measurement the calculation should be repeated with updated experimental data to maintain optimal efficiency.
