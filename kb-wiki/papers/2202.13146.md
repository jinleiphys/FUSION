# Effective field theory of pairing rotations

> Pairing rotations are the low-energy excitations of finite superfluid systems, connecting systems that differ in their number of Cooper pairs. This paper presents a model-independent derivation of pairing rotations within an effective theory that exploits the emergent breaking of U(1) phase symmetries. The symmetries are realized nonlinearly and the Nambu-Goldstone modes depend only on time because the system is finite. Semi-magic nuclei exhibit pairing rotational bands while the pairing spectrum becomes an elliptical paraboloid for open-shell nuclei. Model-independent relations between double charge-exchange reactions and alpha particle capture or knockout in open-shell nuclei are in analogy to the pair transfer reactions in a single superfluid. Odd semi-magic nuclei are described by coupling a fermion to the superfluid. The leading-order theories reproduce data for pairing rotational bands within uncertainty estimates.

# Effective field theory of pairing rotations

## Key claim

Pairing rotations in finite superfluid systems can be derived model-independently from an effective field theory based on the emergent breaking of U(1) phase symmetry, where the Nambu-Goldstone mode depends only on time because the system is finite; the leading-order theory reproduces data for pairing rotational bands within uncertainty estimates, but the actual predictive power is limited to systems where the number of pairs deviates from the central value by less than the breakdown scale set by shell closures.

## Method

The author constructs a Lagrangian for the Nambu-Goldstone mode of broken U(1) symmetry, which reduces to quantum mechanics because the phase field depends only on time in finite systems. For a single superfluid (semi-magic nuclei), the leading-order Hamiltonian is quadratic in the pair-number operator, yielding a parabolic spectrum. For two coupled superfluids (open-shell nuclei), the most general Lagrangian with two phase velocities leads to an elliptical paraboloid spectrum. Low-energy constants are adjusted to binding energies of neighboring nuclei using three-point and four-point differences, and subleading corrections are estimated from the breakdown scale set by the number of pairs in a major shell.

## Key numbers

- Pairing rotational constant $(2a)^{-1}$ for tin isotopes: approximately 0.38 MeV (average)
- Pairing rotational constant $(2a)^{-1}$ for lead isotopes: approximately 0.26 MeV (average)
- Pairing rotational constant $(2a)^{-1}$ for $N=82$ isotones: approximately 0.97 MeV (average)
- Subleading correction magnitude $|\langle g\rangle|$ for tin: 0.016 MeV
- Subleading correction magnitude $|\langle g\rangle|$ for lead: 0.0057 MeV
- Subleading correction magnitude $|\langle g\rangle|$ for $N=82$ isotones: 0.038 MeV
- Maximum number of pairs in major shell $n_b$: 16 for tin and $N=82$ isotones, 22 for lead
- Theoretical estimate for $|g|$ from power counting: 0.027 MeV for tin, 0.0097 MeV for lead, 0.068 MeV for $N=82$ isotones

## Context

This work extends the effective field theory approach to pairing rotations, differing from the earlier EFT for dilute Fermi systems by Furnstahl (2007) by focusing on the dynamics of Nambu-Goldstone modes in finite systems. The results recover the pairing rotational spectra previously studied in pairing models by Bes (1970), Beck (1972), Broglia (1973), and Matsuo (1986), and in Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov computations by Hinohara (2015, 2018). The model-independent relations between double charge-exchange reactions and alpha particle capture or knockout in open-shell nuclei provide testable predictions that can be examined experimentally.
