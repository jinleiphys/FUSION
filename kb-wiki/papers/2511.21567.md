# Enhanced antineutrino emission from $β$ decay in core-collapse supernovae with self-consistent weak decay rates

> Nuclear weak-interaction rates are known to exert a prominent effect in the late-stages of stellar collapse. Despite their importance, most studies to date on core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe) have focused primarily on the effects of electron captures, neglecting $β$~decay contributions. In this work, we present the first CCSNe simulation incorporating global $β$~decay rates from a microscopic theory. These are enabled by a large-scale evaluation of both electron capture and $β$~decay rates, obtained self-consistently utilizing the relativistic energy density functional theory and finite-temperature quasiparticle random-phase approximation. Including $β$ decay leads to a dramatic enhancement of the pre-bounce antineutrino signal as the antineutrino emissivity increases by more than two orders of magnitude and the luminosity by a factor of 50 relative to thermal emission alone, while the average antineutrino energy increases by over 1 MeV. It is expected that these new rates could help us constrain the model uncertainties related to weak-interaction processes, improving the prediction of antineutrino signal during the final stages of stellar death.

## Key claim

The first core-collapse supernova simulation to include global beta-decay rates from a microscopic theory (relativistic energy density functional plus finite-temperature quasiparticle random-phase approximation) shows that antineutrino emissivity increases by more than two orders of magnitude and luminosity by a factor of 50 relative to thermal emission alone, but this enhancement does not alter the collapse dynamics itself and the pre-bounce signal remains undetectable at 10 kpc.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale calculations of beta-decay rates for 902 nuclei from calcium (Z=20) to zirconium (Z=40) using the relativistic energy density functional theory with the D3C* interaction and finite-temperature quasiparticle random-phase approximation (FT-QRPA), including both allowed Gamow-Teller and first-forbidden transitions. These rates were implemented in the open-source code GR1D for spherically symmetric general relativistic hydrodynamics simulations of 15 and 20 solar mass progenitors, using the SFHo equation of state and the NuLib neutrino library, with electron capture rates from the same theoretical framework (Ravlic 2025). The antineutrino emissivity was compared against a thermal baseline (positron capture, electron-positron annihilation, nucleon-nucleon bremsstrahlung) and against shell-model beta-decay rates from Langanke (2001) and Zha (2019).

## Key numbers

* Antineutrino emissivity increases by more than two orders of magnitude relative to thermal emission alone at the onset of collapse for a 20 solar mass progenitor.
* Antineutrino luminosity increases by a factor of 50 compared to the thermal baseline for both 15 and 20 solar mass progenitors.
* Average antineutrino energy increases by 1.1 MeV for the 15 solar mass progenitor and 1.3 MeV for the 20 solar mass progenitor.
* The enhanced antineutrino spectrum extends up to approximately 11 MeV in antineutrino energy.
* At 10 kpc distance, the predicted pre-bounce inverse beta-decay event counts in the final 300 ms are vanishingly small: for the 20 solar mass model with beta decays, 2.40 x 10^-3 events in a Hyper-K-like detector and 2.54 x 10^-3 events in a JUNO-like detector.
* The FT-QRPA beta-decay rates are typically higher than shell-model values by up to 3 orders of magnitude for pf-shell nuclei along the core-collapse trajectory.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Ravlic (2025) for electron capture rates to beta-decay rates using the same self-consistent EDF+FT-QRPA framework, addressing the limitation that previous shell-model calculations (Langanke 2001, Zha 2019) were restricted to nuclei with A less than 65, primarily in the pf-shell region. The new rate table covers 902 nuclei from the proton to neutron drip line, enabling the first inclusion of global microscopic beta-decay rates in core-collapse supernova simulations. The results show that while beta decays do not affect collapse dynamics, they dramatically enhance the antineutrino signal, and the authors note that future studies starting earlier in stellar evolution (presupernova stage) could potentially show enhanced detectability, building on the work of Patton (2017a, 2017b) and Kato (2017) on presupernova antineutrino emission.
