# Thermal vorticity production in relativistic dissipative fluids

> We have computed the circulation integrations of thermal vorticity with and without charged currents in dissipative fluids. We find that the relativistic Kelvin circulation theorem will be modified by the dissipative effects, therefore, the circulation integrations of thermal vorticity may not be conserved during the fluid evolution.

# Thermal vorticity production in relativistic dissipative fluids

## Key claim

The relativistic Kelvin circulation theorem, which states that the circulation integral of thermal vorticity is conserved in ideal fluids, is modified by dissipative effects such as shear viscosity and heat conductivity; however, the authors cannot conclusively state that dissipative effects always violate the theorem, as the entropy density is also not conserved in such systems, and in certain symmetric flows (e.g., (1+1)-dimensional Bjorken flow) the circulation integral may still vanish.

## Method

The authors derived the dynamical equations for the circulation integration of thermal vorticity in relativistic dissipative fluids by starting from the energy-momentum conservation equation with a viscous tensor term, both in the local rest frame and the laboratory frame. They then extended the analysis to fluids with charged particles and electromagnetic fields, incorporating conserved currents and the enthalpy vorticity, following the approach of Gao (2014) for ideal fluids.

## Key numbers

- The extremely strong magnetic field produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions is of order \(10^{18} - 10^{19} \, \text{G}\), as cited from Kharzeev (2007) and others.
- The metric tensor convention used is \(g_{\mu\nu} = g^{\mu\nu} = \text{diag}\{+, -, -, -\}\) with the Levi-Civita tensor \(\epsilon^{0123} = -\epsilon_{0123} = 1\).

## Context

This work extends the relativistic Kelvin circulation theorem, originally proven for ideal fluids by PhysRevLett.105.095005 and Gao (2014), to dissipative fluids by including shear viscosity and heat conductivity. The results provide a theoretical foundation for studying thermal vorticity generation in relativistic heavy ion collisions, where chiral transport phenomena such as the chiral magnetic and vortical effects (Kharzeev 2007, Fukushima 2008) and local polarization effects (Gao 2012) are relevant. Future work will involve kinetic theory and hydrodynamic simulations to further understand vorticity production.
