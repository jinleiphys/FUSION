# Collision broadening of rho meson in a dropping mass scenario

> Vector mesons containing light quarks are thought to have their masses reduced in dense nuclear matter, sacrificing some of their energy to the scalar field which becomes appreciable at finite baryon density. Model calculations find masses which fall by a couple tens of percents in normal nuclear matter, and by several hundred MeV in dense matter. We estimate the collision rate for rho mesons in such a scenario and at finite temperature. Compared to its free-mass value, the collision rate changes by nearly a factor of two both above and below, depending on the density. This collision broadening effect could be important for estimates of low-mass dilepton production in heavy-ion collisions.

# Collision Broadening of Rho Meson in a Dropping Mass Scenario

## Key claim
The collision rate of rho mesons in hot and dense hadronic matter changes by nearly a factor of two both above and below its free-mass value depending on density, but this broadening is only significant for intermediate rho masses and at higher temperatures; for the lightest rho masses near the two-pion threshold, collision broadening is not important.

## Method
The collision rate for rho mesons was estimated using kinetic theory within a fireball model, assuming thermal equilibrium distributions for pions, kaons, rho mesons, and nucleons. Cross sections for rho-pion scattering through the a1(1260) resonance and rho-kaon scattering through the K1(1270) resonance were parametrized using Breit-Wigner functions with energy-dependent widths, with the effective masses of all hadrons determined by a Walecka-type mean-field model where masses drop with increasing density via coupling to the scalar field.

## Key numbers
- At T = 150 MeV and zero chemical potential, the free-mass collision rate is approximately 40 MeV, consistent with earlier calculations by Haglin (1995) to within 5%.
- As the rho mass drops from 770 MeV, the collision rate rises to a maximum of 75 MeV at a mass around 500 MeV, then drops to 40 MeV at 400 MeV mass and to 20 MeV at 300 MeV mass.
- At T = 200 MeV and zero chemical potential, the free-mass collision rate is approximately 140 MeV, about 15% higher than the result of Haglin (1995).
- At T = 200 MeV with a pion chemical potential of 130 MeV, collision rates reach 100 to 250 MeV.

## Context
This work extends earlier calculations of meson collision rates by Haglin (1995) to include the effects of dropping masses in the Brown-Rho scaling scenario (Brown and Rho, 1991). The dropping mass picture is motivated by QCD sum-rule approaches (Dey, Eletsky, and Ioffe, 1990; Hatsuda and Lee, 1992), effective field theories (Shiomi and Hatsuda, 1994), quark models (Saito and Thomas, 1994), and Walecka-type mean-field models (Serot and Walecka, 1986). The results are relevant for interpreting low-mass dilepton enhancements observed in 200 GeV/u S+Au collisions at the Super Proton Synchrotron (Agakichiev et al., 1995), where dropping rho masses have been proposed as one explanation (Li, Ko, and Brown, 1995). The findings enable more quantitative model calculations of dilepton production in heavy-ion collisions by accounting for collision broadening effects that add in quadrature with experimental mass resolution.
