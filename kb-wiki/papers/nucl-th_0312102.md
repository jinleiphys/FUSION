# Inelastic interactions between nuclei at high energies

> A theory of nucleus-nucleus collisions has been developed for kinetic energies substantially in excess of the binding energy. The very high pressure produced in the compound system as a result of the fusion of the two colliding nuclei is the reason for the subsequent hydrodynamic expansion of the nuclear medium. The energy and angular distributions of the reaction products are investigated. The charge distribution is also determined in the case where the nucleon and ion components of the reaction products are predominant. A solution is found for the expansion into vacuum of a sphere in which the initially uniformly distributed material is initially at rest and at an ultrarelativistic temperature.

# Inelastic interactions between nuclei at high energies

## Key claim

The paper develops a hydrodynamic theory for nucleus-nucleus collisions at kinetic energies substantially exceeding the nucleon binding energy, where the compound system formed by fusion undergoes expansion into vacuum. The theory is explicitly limited to head-on collisions between identical nuclei and only analyzes the expansion stage, not the fusion stage itself, with the caveat that the validity of the ultrarelativistic treatment is constrained by the narrow energy range where nucleon and pion rest masses are not too different.

## Method

The authors treat the collision as a two-stage process: first, fusion of the two nuclei into a compound system with high pressure, followed by hydrodynamic expansion into vacuum modeled using adiabatic (isentropic) flow equations. For nonrelativistic collisions (E₀ ≪ mₙc²), they use the Poisson adiabatic curve with an ideal gas equation of state, solving one-dimensional hydrodynamic equations for a disk-shaped initial configuration. For ultrarelativistic collisions (E₀ ≫ mₙc²), they employ relativistic hydrodynamics with the equation of state p = e/3 and solve the spherically symmetric flow equations in the limit of large Lorentz factors, obtaining a solution for the entropy density and Lorentz factor as functions of time and radial coordinate.

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamic approach originally proposed by Fermi (1950) for collisions between relativistic strongly-interacting elementary particles and further developed by Landau (1953) for hadron collisions. The authors adapt these ideas to nucleus-nucleus collisions, noting that unlike elementary particle collisions, the number of nucleons is macroscopically large from the beginning, enhancing the credibility of thermodynamic and hydrodynamic treatment. The paper builds on the authors' previous work (Nosov and Kamchatnov, 1975-1976) on macroscopic treatment of nuclear phenomena where the condition k_fR ≫ 1 was always encountered. The theory enables predictions of energy and angular distributions of reaction products (nucleons and pions) that can be tested against experimental data from heavy-ion accelerators, and the charge distribution analysis based on isotopic invariance provides additional testable predictions for systems with neutron excess.
