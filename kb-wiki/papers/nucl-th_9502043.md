# 1/N expansion formalism for high-spin states

## Key claim

The 1/N expansion solutions for the interacting boson model (IBM) are extended to higher orders (up to the third layer, corresponding to terms of order 1/N^6) using computer algebra, and these analytic results are shown to be very accurate when compared with exact diagonalization, provided that variation after projection (VAP) is used rather than variation before projection (VBP).

## Method

The authors use Mathematica to compute analytic 1/N expansions for matrix elements of one-body and two-body boson operators in the ground band of the IBM, including s, d, and g bosons. They evaluate the normalization integral F(N,L) and the expectation values of number operators and multipole operators to third layer (terms up to order 1/N^6 and (L/N)^6), then perform variation after projection to determine the boson mean fields x_l for each angular momentum L. The results are compared to exact numerical diagonalization for N=10 bosons in two parameter sets, and then applied to superdeformed bands in Hg isotopes.

## Key numbers

- Boson numbers for superdeformed nuclei are around N=30-40, based on microscopic studies by Honma (1992, 1993)
- The 1/N expansion is extended to order 1/N^6 (third layer), where terms actually scale as (L/N)^6
- For the test case with N=10 bosons, the third layer calculation agrees with exact results up to maximum spin L=4N (L=40)
- Second layer results start deviating from exact ones around L=2N (L=20)
- The quadrupole parameters q_{22}, q_{24}, q_{44} are scaled from their SU(3) values with a single factor q; fits to Hg isotope data indicate SU(3) symmetry breaking of around 30%
- The E2 transition matrix elements are given to first layer accuracy, which is found sufficient for high-spin states

## Context

This work extends the 1/N expansion formalism of Kuyucak (1988), which was previously restricted to order 1/N^2 and sufficient only for low-spin (L<N) phenomenology. It addresses the need for sdg-IBM calculations in deformed nuclei where boson numbers N=12-16 (normal) or N=30-40 (superdeformed) make numerical diagonalization impractical even on supercomputers. The results enable analytic description of high-spin states in both normal and superdeformed nuclei, including the super IBM proposed by Otsuka and Honma (1992, 1993), and can be used to study the identical band phenomenon suggested by Iachello (1991) and Janssens (1992).
