# Quark matter under strong magnetic fields

> We revisit three of the mathematical formalisms used to describe magnetized quark matter in compact objects within the MIT and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio models and then compare their results. The tree formalisms are based on 1) isotropic equations of state, 2) anisotropic equations of state with different parallel and perpendicular pressures and 3) the assumption of a chaotic field approximation that results in a truly isotropic equation of state. We have seen that the magnetization obtained with both models is very different: while the MIT model produces well-behaved curves that are always positive for large magnetic fields, the NJL model yields a magnetization with lots of spikes and negative values. This fact has strong consequences on the results based on the existence of anisotropic equations of state. We have also seen that, while the isotropic formalism results in maximum stellar masses that increase considerably when the magnetic fields increase, maximum masses obtained with the chaotic field approximation never vary more than 5.5$\%$. The effect of the magnetic field on the radii is opposed in the MIT and NJL models: with both formalisms, isotropic and chaotic field approximation, for a fixed mass, the radii increase with the increase of the magnetic field in the MIT bag model and decrease in the NJL, the radii of quark stars described by the NJL model being smaller than the ones described by the MIT model.

## Key claim

The paper compares three formalisms for describing magnetized quark matter in compact objects using the MIT bag model and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, finding that the choice of formalism strongly affects predicted stellar properties; the isotropic formalism yields maximum stellar masses that increase considerably with magnetic field, while the chaotic field approximation limits maximum mass variation to at most 5.5%.

## Method

The authors computed equations of state for magnetized quark matter using the MIT bag model and the NJL model under three formalisms: (1) isotropic equations of state with a single pressure, (2) anisotropic equations of state with separate parallel and perpendicular pressures derived from the magnetization, and (3) a chaotic field approximation that yields a truly isotropic equation of state by averaging the stress tensor components. They imposed charge neutrality and beta-equilibrium conditions, used an energy-density-dependent magnetic field prescription, and solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to obtain stellar mass-radius relations.

## Key numbers

- Maximum stellar mass obtained with the chaotic field approximation never varies more than 5.5% as magnetic field increases.
- For the MIT bag model with isotropic EOS at B = 10^19 G, maximum mass is 2.16 solar masses, radius is 10.68 km, and central energy density is 6.33 fm^-4.
- For the MIT bag model with anisotropic EOS at B = 10^19 G, maximum mass is 1.99 solar masses, radius is 9.31 km, and central energy density is 8.39 fm^-4.
- For the MIT bag model with chaotic field at B = 10^19 G, maximum mass is 1.99 solar masses, radius is 10.97 km, and central energy density is 5.87 fm^-4.
- For the MIT bag model with isotropic EOS at B = 10^17 G, maximum mass is 1.81 solar masses, radius is 9.99 km, and central energy density is 6.77 fm^-4.
- For the MIT bag model with isotropic EOS at B = 3 x 10^18 G, maximum mass is 1.89 solar masses, radius is 9.78 km, and central energy density is 7.52 fm^-4.
- For the MIT bag model describing a 1.4 solar mass star with isotropic EOS at B = 10^19 G, radius is 11.10 km and central energy density is 2.07 fm^-4.
- For the MIT bag model describing a 1.4 solar mass star with anisotropic EOS at B = 10^19 G, radius is 9.85 km and central energy density is 2.97 fm^-4.
- The MIT bag model uses quark masses m_u = m_d = 5 MeV, m_s = 120 MeV, and bag constant B = 148 MeV^1/4.
- The NJL model uses the HK parametrization with cutoff Lambda = 631.4 MeV, m_u = m_d = 5.5 MeV, m_s = 135.7 MeV, G Lambda^2 = 1.835, and K Lambda^5 = 9.29.
- The anisotropic pressures start to deviate from each other around 3.1 x 10^18 G.
- The maximum allowed magnetic field for a quark star is estimated to be of order 10^20 G.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of magnetized quark matter in compact objects, comparing the MIT bag model (used in earlier works by Chakrabarty (1996) and Lattimer (2000)) with the NJL model (used in works by Menezes and collaborators). The paper revisits three formalisms previously employed in the literature: isotropic equations of state from Blandford (1997), anisotropic treatments from Efrain (2005) and Sedrakian (2007), and the chaotic field approximation from a 2014 work. The results enable future studies to assess which formalism is most appropriate for describing magnetars and quark stars, and highlight the need to incorporate anomalous magnetic moments of quarks in future calculations.
