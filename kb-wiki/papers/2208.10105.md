# Models for Pairing Phenomena

> Pairing effects manifests themselves in many aspects in nuclear systems ranging from finite nuclei to nuclear matter and compact stars. Although with some specific features for nuclear systems, the mechanism of pairing between nucleons in these systems resembles that of electrons in superconductors. The Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory, the first successful and microscopic theory for superconductivity, and the Bogoliubov transformation, the generalization of the BCS theory, have been widely used to describe pairing correlations in nuclear systems. To deal with the problem of particle number non-conservation in the BCS method and generalized Bogoliubov transformation, particle number projection techniques as well as several approaches which keep the particle number conserved, have been proposed. In the study of exotic nuclei, which are quantum open systems, the continuum contributions have to be taken into account. In this chapter, a thorough but brief discussion of pairing effects in nuclear systems will be introduced. Then nuclear models dealing with pairing correlations in nuclear structure properties will be presented to different extent of details. Although formulas are given, the emphasis is mainly put on the basic ideas concerning these models.

## Key claim

This chapter provides a thorough but brief discussion of pairing effects in nuclear systems, emphasizing the basic ideas behind various nuclear models rather than detailed formulas, while noting that the microscopic understanding of pairing correlations starting from underlying forces remains an open question.

## Method

The authors review the seniority model for single-j-shell configurations, the BCS theory and Bogoliubov transformation for describing pairing correlations in nuclear systems, and various methods to address particle number non-conservation including the Lipkin-Nogami approximation and particle number projection techniques. They also discuss the treatment of continuum contributions for exotic nuclei near drip lines, where conventional BCS methods are not justified because they cannot properly include continuum states.

## Key numbers

- Neutron gaps for even-even nuclei calculated from AME2020 masses are almost systematically larger than 1 MeV
- For the δ-interaction in a single j-shell, the J=0 state energy is E₀ = -(2j+1)/2 × V₀ × I(j), while E₂ ~ (1/4)E₀, E₄ ~ (9/64)E₀, E₆ ~ (25/256)E₀
- In the seniority model, pairing gaps are G(2Ω+1)/2 for even particle number and GΩ for odd particle number
- The excitation energy of the first excited state in the seniority model is GΩ, independent of particle number N
- For ²¹⁰Po, the 2⁺ state excitation energy is 1.18 MeV, explained by the seniority scheme with two protons in the 1h₉/₂ orbital giving about 1 MeV from the empirical formula (13.43 ± 1.38)A^(-0.48±0.03) MeV
- In the BCS model for a single j-shell, the pairing energy is E_BCS^(N) = -(1/2)GNΩ(1 - N/(2Ω) + N/(2Ω²))

## Context

This chapter extends the foundational work on nuclear pairing from Bohr, Mottelson, and Pines (1958) who suggested applying BCS theory to nuclei, and Belyaev (1959) who implemented it. It covers developments from the seniority model of Racah (1942) and Mayer (1950) through modern density functional theories using various pairing forces including the Gogny force from Decharge (1980) and separable pairing forces. The review enables further work on exotic nuclei near drip lines, neutron star thermal evolution, and two-nucleon decay studies, while noting that open questions remain about understanding pairing from bare NN interactions and the influence of medium polarization and three-nucleon forces.
