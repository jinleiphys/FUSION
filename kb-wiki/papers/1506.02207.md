# Constraints on the symmetry energy from observational probes of the neutron star crust

> A number of observed phenomena associated with individual neutron star systems or neutron star populations find explanations in models in which the neutron star crust plays an important role. We review recent work examining the sensitivity to the slope of the symmetry energy $L$ of such models, and constraints extracted on $L$ from confronting them with observations. We focus on six sets of observations and proposed explanations: (i) The cooling rate of the neutron star in Cassiopeia A, confronting cooling models which include enhanced cooling in the nuclear pasta regions of the inner crust, (ii) the upper limit of the observed periods of young X-ray pulsars, confronting models of magnetic field decay in the crust caused by the high resistivity of the nuclear pasta layer, (iii) glitches from the Vela pulsar, confronting the paradigm that they arise due to a sudden re-coupling of the crustal neutron superfluid to the crustal lattice after a period during which they were decoupled due to vortex pinning, (iv) The frequencies of quasi-periodic oscillations in the X-ray tail of light curves from giant flares from soft gamma-ray repeaters, confronting models of torsional crust oscillations, (v) the upper limit on the frequency to which millisecond pulsars can be spun-up due to accretion from a binary companion, confronting models of the r-mode instability arising above a threshold frequency determined in part by the viscous dissipation timescale at the crust-core boundary, and (vi) the observations of precursor electromagnetic flares a few seconds before short gamma-ray bursts, confronting a model of crust shattering caused by resonant excitation of a crustal oscillation mode by the tidal gravitational field of a companion neutron star just before merger.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that six distinct observational probes of neutron star crusts can each place constraints on the slope of the symmetry energy \(L\), but the constraints come with significant caveats regarding model assumptions, interpretation of data, and the possibility that alternative physical mechanisms could explain the observations without invoking a particular value of \(L\).

## Method

The authors review six sets of astrophysical observations and confront them with theoretical models of neutron star crusts that incorporate the slope of the symmetry energy \(L\) as a key parameter. They use Skyrme and Relativistic Mean-Field nuclear matter models to construct families of neutron star equations of state parameterized by \(L\), varying \(L\) between 25 MeV and 115 MeV while ensuring consistency with ab initio calculations of pure neutron matter at sub-saturation densities. For each observational probe, they examine how the predicted crust properties (thickness, composition, pasta phase extent, transport properties) depend on \(L\) and compare with the observations to extract allowed ranges.

## Key numbers

- The community average for the slope of the symmetry energy is \(L \approx 60\) MeV, with a loose range \(30 \lesssim L \lesssim 80\) MeV from terrestrial experiments and astrophysical observations.
- For the Cassiopeia A neutron star cooling, with bubble cooling processes inactive, the constraint is \(L \lesssim 70\) MeV for masses \(1.25 M_{\odot} < M < 1.8 M_{\odot}\), and \(35 \lesssim L \lesssim 55\) MeV for \(M = 1.6 M_{\odot}\).
- For the Cassiopeia A neutron star cooling, with bubble cooling processes active, the constraint is \(L \lesssim 45\) MeV for masses \(1.25 M_{\odot} < M < 1.8 M_{\odot}\), and \(35 \lesssim L \lesssim 45\) MeV for \(M = 1.6 M_{\odot}\).
- The observed upper limit of periods for young X-ray pulsars is of order 10 seconds, which is consistent with a highly resistive pasta layer requiring \(Q_{\rm imp} \approx 20-100\) at densities above \(6\times10^{13}\) g cm\(^{-3}\), implying \(L \lesssim 80\) MeV.
- The Cassiopeia A neutron star surface temperature is \(\langle T_{\rm eff} \rangle \approx 2.1\times10^6\) K, with a temperature decline of approximately 4% (or a more conservative 2-5.5%) over the period 2000-2009.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on constraining the symmetry energy from nuclear experiments (summarized by Li (2008), Tsang et al. (2012), and Lattimer (2013)) and from ab initio calculations of pure neutron matter (Schwenk (2005), Hebeler (2010), Gandolfi et al. (2009, 2010), Gezerlis et al. (2013)). It specifically examines six astrophysical probes that were previously studied individually, including the Cassiopeia A cooling analyzed by Shternin (2011) and Page (2011), the magnetic field decay model of Pons (2013), and the glitch model for the Vela pulsar. The review enables future work by identifying which observational constraints are most robust and which model assumptions most urgently need improvement, particularly the treatment of nuclear pasta phases and their transport properties.
