# v4 from ideal and viscous hydrodynamic simulations of nuclear collisions at the BNL Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) and the CERN Large Hadron Collider (LHC)

> We compute v4/(v2)^2 in ideal and viscous hydrodynamics. We investigate its sensitivity to details of the hydrodynamic model and compare the results to experimental data from RHIC. Whereas v2 has a significant sensitivity only to initial eccentricity and viscosity while being insensitive to freeze out temperature, we find that v4/(v2)^2 is quite insensitive to initial eccentricity. On the other hand, it can still be sensitive to shear viscosity in addition to freeze out temperature, although viscous effects do not universally increase v4/(v2)^2 as originally predicted. Consistent with data, we find no dependence on particle species. We also make a prediction for v4/(v2)^2 in heavy ion collisions at the LHC.

# v4 from ideal and viscous hydrodynamic simulations of nuclear collisions at RHIC and LHC

## Key claim

While the abstract states that viscous effects do not universally increase v4/(v2)^2 as originally predicted, the paper shows that at a realistic freeze-out temperature of 140 MeV, viscosity actually tends to *decrease* the ratio compared to ideal hydrodynamics, making agreement with experimental data worse rather than better, contrary to earlier expectations from Bhalerao (2005).

## Method

The authors solve 2+1 dimensional conformal viscous hydrodynamic equations (with zero bulk viscosity) using both Glauber-type and CGC-type initial conditions, with a Cooper-Frye freeze-out routine followed by resonance feed-down. They compute v4/(v2)^2 for pions, kaons, and protons at RHIC energies and make predictions for LHC Pb-Pb collisions at 5.5 TeV, varying the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio η/s, freeze-out temperature Tf, and impact parameter. All calculations use parameters that gave the best fit to RHIC data from Luzum (2008), except where specified.

## Key numbers

- For ideal hydrodynamics with Tf = 100 MeV and CGC initial conditions at b = 11 fm and pt = 3.5 GeV, v2 reaches 70% and v4/(v2)^2 approaches almost 0.62 instead of 0.50 at large pt.
- At Tf = 140 MeV, v4/(v2)^2 becomes very flat as a function of pt, reminiscent of experimental data; this Tf = 140 MeV is the same value that gives the best fit of charged hadron multiplicity, average transverse momentum, and v2 in viscous hydrodynamics.
- For LHC predictions, the initial temperature Ti is set to 420 MeV for Glauber and 390 MeV for CGC initial conditions, adjusted to reproduce the expected multiplicity at top-energy Pb-Pb collisions.
- For a centrality bin of 20–60% at the LHC, the measured v4/(v2)^2 is expected to be larger by a factor of approximately 1.34 due to eccentricity fluctuations.

## Context

This work extends earlier ideal hydrodynamic calculations of v4 by Kolb (2003, 2004) and the analytic prediction v4 = 0.5 (v2)^2 from Borghini (2005), which assumed a boosted thermal freeze-out distribution in the low-temperature, large-pt limit. The paper addresses the discrepancy between that prediction and experimental data from STAR and PHENIX, where v4/(v2)^2 is close to 1 rather than 0.5, a difference largely attributable to event-by-event eccentricity fluctuations as shown by Gombeaud (2009). The results enable future use of v4/(v2)^2 as a probe of freeze-out dynamics and the form of the viscous correction to the distribution function at freeze-out, as discussed in Luzum (2010).
