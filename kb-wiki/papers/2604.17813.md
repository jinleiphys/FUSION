# Compositeness of near-threshold eigenstates with Coulomb plus short-range interactions

> We investigate the internal structure of near-threshold $s$-wave eigenstates in a two-body system with Coulomb plus short-range interactions. Using a nonrelativistic effective field theory, we derive the expression for the compositeness in terms of the energy derivative of the self-energy, which is applicable to the present system with the non-separable Coulomb interaction. For near-threshold states, the compositeness can be written solely in terms of the Coulomb scattering length, the Coulomb effective range, and the Bohr radius, providing the weak-binding relation in the presence of the Coulomb interaction. We numerically study the pole trajectories and the compositeness and find that the Coulomb interaction qualitatively modifies the threshold behavior of the poles and the internal structure of the eigenstates. We show that when the Coulomb interaction is relatively strong, the enhancement of the compositeness near the threshold is absent, in contrast to purely short-range interactions. On the other hand, for a weak Coulomb interaction, a remnant of short-range universality survives, and near-threshold bound states tend to be composite dominant. Furthermore, even resonances are dominated by the composite component in the presence of the Coulomb interaction, owing to their continuous connection to the bound-state regime. We apply the formalism to realistic systems with near-threshold eigenstates, including exotic hadrons and nuclei.

## Key claim
The paper derives a weak-binding relation for the compositeness of near-threshold eigenstates in systems with both Coulomb and short-range interactions, showing that the compositeness can be expressed solely in terms of the Coulomb scattering length, Coulomb effective range, and Bohr radius. However, the abstract's claim of applicability is qualified: the enhancement of compositeness near threshold is absent when the Coulomb interaction is relatively strong, in contrast to purely short-range systems, and only for weak Coulomb interactions does a remnant of short-range universality survive.

## Method
The authors employ a nonrelativistic effective field theory (EFT) with a Hamiltonian coupling two scattering particles to a discrete state via short-range interactions, then incorporate the Coulomb interaction through the Coulomb Green function. They derive the s-wave scattering amplitude by separating the pure-Coulomb contribution from the Coulomb-distorted short-range contribution, obtaining the pole condition expressed in terms of the Coulomb scattering length \(a_s^C\), Coulomb effective range \(r_e^C\), and Bohr radius \(a_B\). The compositeness is derived from the energy derivative of the self-energy, and the pole trajectories are numerically studied by solving the pole condition for both attractive and repulsive Coulomb interactions.

## Key numbers
- The Bohr radius is defined as \(a_B = 1/(\mu \alpha |Z_1 Z_2|)\), where \(\mu\) is the reduced mass and \(\alpha \sim 1/137\) is the fine-structure constant.
- The pure-Coulomb scattering amplitude has poles at \(k_n = -i k_C / n\) for \(n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\), where \(k_C = \mu \alpha Z_1 Z_2\).
- The ratio \(|r_e^C|/a_B\) serves as a measure of Coulomb interaction strength: \(|r_e^C|/a_B \gg 1\) indicates strong Coulomb interaction, while \(|r_e^C|/a_B \ll 1\) indicates weak Coulomb interaction.
- The Coulomb effective range \(r_e^C\) is given by \(r_e^C = -\sigma (2\pi)/(\mu^2 g_0^2)\), where \(\sigma = \pm 1\) determines the sign of the effective range.

## Context
This work extends the compositeness framework developed by Weinberg (1965) and later applied to hadronic molecules by Hyodo (2013) and Kinugawa (2024), which previously treated only short-range interactions. It addresses the need to understand near-threshold states in systems like \({}^{8}\mathrm{Be}\) (an \(\alpha\alpha\) resonance that would be bound without Coulomb repulsion, as noted by Braaten (2004) and Higa (2008)) and exotic hadrons such as \(X(3872)\) and \(T_{cc}\) with binding energies comparable to the Coulomb scale. The results enable systematic classification of near-threshold eigenstates in nuclear and hadronic systems where Coulomb effects are non-negligible, and provide a theoretical foundation for the empirical threshold rule in nuclear physics that was previously justified only for short-range interactions.
