# Solution of the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock equations in the Cartesian deformed harmonic oscillator basis. (II) The program HFODD

> We describe the code HFODD which solves the nuclear Skyrme-Hartree-Fock problem by using the deformed Cartesian harmonic oscillator basis. The user has a possibility of choosing among various symmetries of the nuclear HF problem for rotating or non-rotating nuclei; they vary from the non-axial parity-invariant nuclear shapes, through those also breaking the intrinsic parity, towards the least-restrictive case corresponding to only one symmetry plane. The code provides a solution for a complete superdeformed rotational band in an A~150 nucleus within one CPU hour of the CRAY C-90 supercomputer or within two-three CPU hours of a fast workstation.

## Key claim

The paper presents the public-domain code HFODD for solving the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock equations in a Cartesian deformed harmonic oscillator basis, claiming that for sufficiently large basis sizes (N0 up to 20 for spherical cases and 26 for deformed cases) the basis parameters do not need to be optimized; instead, "physical" basis parameters derived from known nuclear deformations yield results of comparable or better accuracy than optimized parameters, contrary to earlier assumptions that optimization was essential.

## Method

The code expands single-particle wave functions of neutrons and protons in a Cartesian harmonic oscillator basis and solves the self-consistent Hartree-Fock equations by iterative diagonalization of the mean-field Hamiltonians or Routhians, which depend nonlinearly on local neutron and proton densities. The user can select among various nuclear symmetries for rotating or non-rotating nuclei, ranging from non-axial parity-invariant shapes through parity-breaking shapes down to cases with only one symmetry plane. Pairing correlations are included in the BCS limit with conserved time-reversal symmetry.

## Key numbers

- For the spherical nucleus 208Pb, the exact total energy is −1635.956 MeV; with N0=10 the error is about 6 MeV, and for N0≥16 the error becomes smaller than 1 MeV.
- For 208Pb, the exact matter rms radius is 5.5546 fm; for any N0≥10 the physical basis gives radii precise to ±0.003 fm (±0.05%).
- For the superdeformed state in 152Dy, the exact total energy from spatial-coordinate calculations is −1229.365 MeV; with M=1200 basis states the code yields −1229.383 MeV.
- For 152Dy, the proton quadrupole moment from spatial-coordinate calculations is 18.376 b; the code with M=1200 gives Qp=18.393 b, with a conservative error of 0.05 b (0.3%).
- One Hartree-Fock iteration for the superdeformed rotating parity-conserving state of 152Dy takes about nine seconds on the CRAY C-90 computer.
- Starting from Woods-Saxon wave functions, about fifty iterations are required to converge the energy within about 0.1 keV.
- A complete superdeformed rotational band in an A∼150 nucleus can be obtained within one CPU hour on the CRAY C-90, or within two to three CPU hours on a fast workstation.
- For rotating nuclei at ħω=0.5 MeV, the angular momentum I is precise to within 0.1 ħ.
- The dynamic moment of inertia J(2) is precise to 0.1 ħ²/MeV.
- Proton separation energies in superdeformed nuclei are precise to about 50 keV, two orders of magnitude better than total energies.
- Quadrupole polarizations (differences of quadrupole moments between Tb and Dy) are precise to 0.01 b.
- Relative alignments between Tb and Dy at ħω=0.5 MeV are precise to 0.03 ħ.

## Context

This work extends the method described in the authors' previous publication (referred to as Paper I) and makes the code publicly available for the first time, replacing earlier beta versions. It builds on the spherical Skyrme-HF code published by Reinhard (1991) and on earlier triaxial HF codes by various groups (Girod and Grammaticos 1983; Bonche et al. 1985; Umar et al. 1991; Chinn et al. 1995). The paper demonstrates that for large basis sizes, the basis optimization procedure advocated by Flocard et al. (1973) is unnecessary, and that physical basis parameters based on liquid-drop-model estimates give sufficient precision for practical nuclear-structure calculations. This enables systematic studies of superdeformed rotational bands, identical bands, and quadrupole polarizations in heavy nuclei.
