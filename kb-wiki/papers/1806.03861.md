# Crustal heating in accreting neutron stars from the nuclear energy-density functional theory. I. Proton shell effects and neutron-matter constraint

> Observations of soft X-ray transients in quiescence suggest the existence of heat sources in the crust of accreting neutron stars. Heat is thought to be released by electroweak and nuclear processes triggered by the burying of ashes of X-ray bursts. The heating is studied using a fully quantum approach taking consistently into account nuclear shell effects. We have followed the evolution of ashes made of $^{56}$Fe employing the nuclear energy-density functional theory. Both the outer and inner crusts are described using the same functional, thus ensuring a unified and thermodynamically consistent treatment. To assess the role of the neutron-matter constraint, we have employed the set of accurately calibrated Brussels-Montreal functionals BSk19, BSk20, and BSk21 and for comparison the SLy4 functional. Due to nuclear shell effects, the fully accreted crust is found to be much less stratified than in previous studies. In particular, large regions of the inner crust contain clusters with the magic number $Z=14$. The heat deposited in the outer crust is tightly constrained by experimental atomic mass data. The shallow heating we obtain does not exceed $0.2$~MeV and is therefore not enough to explain the cooling of some soft X-ray transients. The total heat released in the crust is very sensitive to details of the nuclear structure and is predicted to lie in the range from $1.5$~MeV to $1.7$~MeV. The evolution of an accreted matter element and therefore the location of heat sources are governed to a large extent by the existence of nuclear shell closures. Ignoring these effects in the inner crust, the total heat falls to $\sim 0.6$~MeV. The neutron-matter constraint is also found to play a key role. The large amount of heat obtained by Steiner et al. (2012) could thus be traced back to unrealistic neutron-matter equations of state.

## Key claim
The total heat released in the crust of accreting neutron stars is very sensitive to nuclear shell effects and the neutron-matter equation of state, with the fully accreted crust found to be much less stratified than in previous studies; the shallow heating does not exceed 0.2 MeV and is therefore insufficient to explain the cooling of some soft X-ray transients, while the total heat lies in the range from 1.5 MeV to 1.7 MeV per accreted nucleon.

## Method
The authors followed the evolution of ashes made of ⁵⁶Fe using the nuclear energy-density functional (EDF) theory, employing the Brussels-Montreal functionals BSk19, BSk20, and BSk21, and for comparison the SLy4 functional. Both the outer and inner crusts were described using the same functional, ensuring a unified and thermodynamically consistent treatment. The outer crust was modeled using the standard model of Baym, Pethick, and Sutherland (1971) with experimental nuclear masses from the 2016 Atomic Mass Evaluation and theoretical masses from Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations, while the inner crust was treated using the 4th order Extended Thomas-Fermi method with proton shell corrections added perturbatively via the Strutinsky integral theorem. The evolution of matter elements was followed through electron captures, neutron emissions, and pycnonuclear fusion, assuming ground-state transitions and neglecting neutrino losses.

## Key numbers
- Shallow heating does not exceed 0.2 MeV per accreted nucleon
- Total heat released in the crust predicted to lie in the range from 1.5 MeV to 1.7 MeV per accreted nucleon
- Ignoring shell effects in the inner crust, the total heat falls to approximately 0.6 MeV per accreted nucleon
- The Brussels-Montreal functionals BSk19, BSk20, and BSk21 were fitted to 2149 measured masses from the 2003 Atomic Mass Evaluation with a root-mean-square deviation of 0.58 MeV
- The SLy4 functional yields a root-mean-square deviation of 3.961 MeV from the same mass data
- The symmetry coefficient was set at J = 30 MeV for the three Brussels-Montreal functionals
- The incompressibility K_v of symmetric nuclear matter at saturation was required to fall in the experimental range 240 ± 10 MeV
- The time needed to replace the outer crust is t_oc = 10^5.4 yr / ⟨Ṁ⟩_{-10}, where ⟨Ṁ⟩_{-10} = ⟨Ṁ⟩ / 10^{-10} M⊙ yr^{-1}
- The time needed to replace the inner crust layer where deep crustal heating occurs is t_DCH = 10^6.4 yr / ⟨Ṁ⟩_{-10}
- The time needed for formation of a fully accreted crust is t_FA ≈ 40 t_DCH = 10^8 yr / ⟨Ṁ⟩_{-10}

## Context
This work extends the earlier compressible liquid-drop model calculations of Haensel and Zdunik (1990a, 2003, 2008) by incorporating fully quantum nuclear shell effects through the energy-density functional theory, which were previously neglected or treated only empirically. The results contradict the significantly higher total heat of about 2 MeV per nucleon found by Steiner et al. (2012), which the authors attribute to unrealistic neutron-matter equations of state used in that work. The study enables more reliable predictions of crustal heating for interpreting the observed surface temperatures of soft X-ray transients in quiescence, and demonstrates that the large amount of heat obtained by Steiner et al. (2012) can be traced back to unrealistic neutron-matter equations of state.
