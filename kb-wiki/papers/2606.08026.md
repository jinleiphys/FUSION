# The ABC classification of exotic nuclei: a proposal

> The large number of existing nuclear species and the long list of their possible different exotic properties, such as presence of a halo (A), Borromean structure (B), clusterisation (C) and others, calls for a classification scheme that is universal, concise, categorical, informative, accessible and easily extensible. We provide here a first reasoned attempt to fill this gap with an abridged naming scheme, called $ABC$, based on definitions and properties that characterise modern nuclear physics. We limit our chart to light isotopes with $Z\le 10$ where most of these features appear presently.

# The ABC classification of exotic nuclei: a proposal

## Key claim
The authors propose a universal, concise, and extensible alphabetical classification scheme (called ABC) for labeling exotic properties of unstable nuclei, but explicitly limit the current chart to light isotopes with Z ≤ 10, where most of these features are presently observed. The scheme is presented as a first reasoned attempt rather than a definitive system, and the authors acknowledge that some properties may not yet be experimentally verified, in which case the corresponding letter should be wrapped in parentheses.

## Method
The authors define a set of letter labels corresponding to specific nuclear-structure features: A (halo, with subscript for number of halo particles), B (Borromean structure), C (cluster structure, defined by the lowest energy threshold not being single-nucleon emission), D (drip-line nucleus, with zero separation energy), U (unbound or resonant), and W (weakly-bound, with separation energy ≤ 2.5 MeV). They apply this scheme to known light isotopes up to Z = 10, compiling classifications from a large number of measurements and datasets, and present the results in a modified Segrè chart.

## Key numbers
- The weakly-bound criterion (W) is defined as separation energies of the valence nucleon/cluster not exceeding 2.5 MeV; by this definition, the deuteron (binding energy ≈ 2.224 MeV), ⁶He, and ⁷Li are weakly-bound.
- ⁸He is classified as A₄BDW, indicating a four-neutron halo (A₄), Borromean structure, drip-line status, and weak binding.
- ¹¹Li is classified as A₂BDW, indicating a two-neutron halo, Borromean structure, drip-line status, and weak binding.
- ⁵He is classified as U (unbound), ⁶He as BW, ⁶Li as CSW, ⁷Li as CSW, ⁸Be as CU, and ³¹F as A₂(B)DW (with Borromean property in parentheses as unconfirmed).
- More than 3000 nuclear isotopes are known, of which only about 250-260 are stable.

## Context
This work extends the tradition of taxonomic classification in physics, analogous to the Linnaean system for biology, the crystal classification by point-group symmetries, and astronomical classification schemes for galaxies (Morgan, Gray) and stars. The authors explicitly compare their effort to Mendeleev's periodic table, noting that classification gaps will be filled as tools improve. The scheme is intended to complement existing nuclear databases (NNDC, TUNL, JENDL) and to highlight patterns on the nuclear chart for further exploration, particularly for light isotopes where the drip-line has been confirmed only up to Z = 10.
