# Does the effective Lagrangian for low-energy QCD scale?

> QCD is not an approximately scale invariant theory. Hence a dilaton field is not expected to provide a good description of the low-energy dynamics associated with the gluon condensate. Even if such a field is introduced, it remains almost unchanged in hadronic matter at normal densities. This is because the large glueball mass together with the size of the phenomenological gluon condensate ensure that changes to that condensate are very small at such densities. Any changes in hadronic masses and decay constants in matter generated by that condensate will be much smaller that those produced directly by changes in the quark condensate. Hence masses and decay constants are not expected to display a universal scaling.

## Key claim

QCD is not an approximately scale invariant theory, so a dilaton field does not provide a good description of low-energy dynamics associated with the gluon condensate; even if such a field is introduced, it remains almost unchanged in hadronic matter at normal densities because the large glueball mass (around 1.5 GeV) together with the size of the phenomenological gluon condensate ensure that changes to that condensate are very small.

## Method

The author uses the trace anomaly of QCD and the Feynman-Hellmann theorem, as applied by Cohen, Furnstahl and Griegel (1992), to relate the change in the gluon condensate to the energy density of hadronic matter. The change in the gluon condensate at finite density is calculated from the trace of the stress-energy tensor, assuming the change in the nonstrange quark condensate follows the leading model-independent result from Drukarev and Levin (1990) and neglecting the strange quark content of the proton. The resulting expression depends on the energy per nucleon, the pion-nucleon sigma commutator, and the baryon density.

## Key numbers

- Vacuum gluon condensate: ⟨(α_s/π)G^a_{μν}G^{a μν}⟩ ≃ (360 ± 20 MeV)^4, corresponding to about 2200 MeV fm^{-3}.
- Normal nuclear matter density: ρ ≃ 0.17 fm^{-3}.
- Change in gluon condensate at normal nuclear density: about 150 MeV fm^{-3}, which is at most a 15% effect relative to the vacuum value.
- Fourth root of the condensate (corresponding to change in dilaton field or change of scale): altered by no more than 4% at normal nuclear density.
- For pionic droplets studied by Mishustin and Greiner (1993): energy density about 20 MeV fm^{-3}, so the dilaton field is barely changed.
- Lightest scalar glueball mass: estimated at around 1.5 GeV.

## Context

This paper directly challenges the universal scaling hypothesis of Brown and Rho (1991), which proposed that all hadron masses and decay constants scale in dense matter. The author argues that changes in hadronic masses and decay constants in matter are driven by changes in the quark condensate, not the gluon condensate, and that different condensates behave very differently at finite density. The work extends the analysis of Cohen, Furnstahl and Griegel (1992) on the stiffness of the gluon condensate and contrasts with dilaton-based models by Gomm, Jain, Johnson and Schechter (1986) and others. The result enables a clearer separation of quark and gluon condensate effects in dense matter and suggests that scaling could only be recovered in models where the lightest scalar meson has a much larger mass than the dilaton, as noted by Kusaka and Weise (1992).
