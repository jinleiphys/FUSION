# Directly probing existence of $α$-cluster structure in $^{20}$Ne by relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Can relativistic heavy-ion collisions only probe the global shape of colliding nuclei, or their detailed internal structure as well? Taking $^{20}$Ne as an example, we attempt to directly probe its internal $α$-cluster structure, by comparing experimentally measured observables in collisions at relativistic energies from density distributions of $^{20}$Ne with and without $α$-cluster structure. Since the two density distributions give the same nucleus size and deformation, they lead to similar mid-rapidity observables. However, the $α$-cluster structure may considerably reduce the free spectator nucleon yield and enhance the spectator light nuclei yield, as a result of more compact initial phase-space distribution of nucleons inside $α$ clusters. We propose to measure the scaled yield ratio of free spectator neutrons to charged particles with mass-to-charge ratio $A/Z = 3$, 3/2, and 2 in ultra-central $^{20}$Ne+$^{20}$Ne collisions, which is found to be reduced by about $25\%$ at $\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}} = 7$ TeV and about $20\%$ at $\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}} = 200$ GeV with $α$-cluster structure in $^{20}$Ne. This scaled yield ratio thus serves as a robust and direct probe of the existence of $α$-cluster structure in $^{20}$Ne free from the uncertainty of mid-rapidity dynamics.

## Key claim
Relativistic heavy-ion collisions can directly probe the internal α-cluster structure of ²⁰Ne, not just its global shape, by measuring spectator particle yields in ultra-central collisions. The claim is conditional: the two density distributions (with and without α-cluster structure) give the same nucleus size and deformation, so mid-rapidity observables cannot distinguish them, but the scaled yield ratio of free spectator neutrons to charged particles with specific mass-to-charge ratios is reduced by about 25% at √sNN = 7 TeV and about 20% at √sNN = 200 GeV when α-cluster structure is present.

## Method
The authors used a hybrid simulation framework combining the AMPT (a multiphase transport) model with the specCOAL+TestP+GEMINI spectator particle generator. They compared two density distributions of ²⁰Ne: a realistic one from a microscopic cluster model with five α clusters in a bowling-pin configuration, and a fitted axially symmetric deformed Woods-Saxon distribution matched to the same size and deformation. Event-by-event simulations of ultra-central ²⁰Ne+²⁰Ne collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV and 7 TeV were performed, with spectator nucleons clustered into heavy and light fragments using a minimum spanning tree algorithm and Wigner-function coalescence, followed by deexcitation via the GEMINI model.

## Key numbers
- The scaled yield ratio of free spectator neutrons to charged particles with A/Z = 2 is reduced by about 20% at √sNN = 200 GeV in ultra-central ²⁰Ne+²⁰Ne collisions with α-cluster structure.
- The scaled yield ratio of free spectator neutrons to charged particles with A/Z = 2 is reduced by about 25% at √sNN = 7 TeV in ultra-central ²⁰Ne+²⁰Ne collisions with α-cluster structure.
- The α-cluster effect reduces the yield ratios for A/Z = 3 by about 19% and for A/Z = 3/2 by about 21% at √sNN = 200 GeV.
- The largest difference in ⟨v²₂⟩ between the two density distributions is only about 6% at 200 GeV and about 4% at 7 TeV in ultra-central collisions.
- The α-cluster structure suppresses free spectator nucleon yield by about 5% and enhances light nuclei yield by more than 15% in ultra-central collisions at 200 GeV.

## Context
This work extends previous studies by some of the same authors (Wang 2024) which showed that mid-rapidity observables cannot distinguish α-cluster from deformed Woods-Saxon density distributions in ¹⁶O and ²⁰Ne. It also builds on earlier Glauber-model studies (Liu 2023) that demonstrated different α-cluster structures in ¹²C and ¹⁶O affect spectator yields. The proposed spectator yield ratios provide a direct probe of α-cluster structure free from mid-rapidity dynamics, enabling experimental tests at RHIC and LHC with light-ion collisions.
