# Origin of the Covariant Wigner Operator as a Quantum Amplitude in QCD

> The Wigner function plays a central role in QCD as a phase space object encoding correlations among quarks, antiquarks, and gluons, yet its interpretation remains subtle due to its quasiprobabilistic nature and possible negativity. Recent work based on the Koopman-von Neumann-Sudarshan (KvNS) Hilbert space formulation of classical mechanics suggests the Wigner function arises as a quantum probability amplitude projected onto classical phase space, rather than a quasiprobability density (Bondar et al., 2013; McCaul et al., 2023). In the classical limit, this amplitude reduces to the classical Koopman wavefunction. In this work, we extend this perspective to relativistic QCD by constructing a Koopman description of the quark Wigner operator. We show that the Wigner operator is naturally isomorphic to a phase space spinor via an idempotent projection, providing a unified framework in which both classical and quantum dynamics are expressed. Within this formulation, the Wigner function retains its interpretation as an amplitude even in the relativistic regime. This viewpoint clarifies the origin of negativity and other nonclassical features, and provides a more transparent foundation for parton distribution functions in QCD. Remarkably, the relativistic Koopman framework reproduces the classical limit of QCD.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the covariant Wigner operator in QCD is not a quasiprobability density but a quantum probability amplitude projected onto classical phase space, and that this interpretation holds even in the relativistic regime through a Koopman-von Neumann-Sudarshan (KvNS) Hilbert space formulation, provided one introduces a Koopman spinor on classical phase space to which the Wigner operator is isomorphic via an idempotent projection.

## Method

The authors extend the non-relativistic Operational Dynamic Modeling (ODM) framework, which unifies classical and quantum dynamics through a single commutator algebra with an interpolating parameter, to relativistic QCD by constructing a Koopman description of the quark Wigner operator. They develop both a four-operator first-quantized approach (introducing relativistic operators \(X^\mu, P^\mu, \Lambda^\mu, \Theta^\mu\) with Koopman commutation relations \([X^\mu,\Lambda_\nu] = -i\delta^\mu_\nu\) and \([P_\mu,\Theta^\nu] = -i\delta^\nu_\mu\)) and a field-theoretic formulation where space and time are demoted to parameters, using infinitesimal generators of motion in place of Ehrenfest theorems to derive the relativistic Wigner operator.

## Key numbers

* The unified quantum-classical commutator is written as \([\boldsymbol{\hat{x}},\boldsymbol{\hat{p}}]=i\hbar\kappa\), where \(\kappa \to 0\) recovers classical mechanics and \(\kappa \to 1\) recovers quantum mechanics.
* The relativistic Wigner operator is defined as \(\hat{W}(X^\mu,P^\nu) = \int d^4\Theta~\Psi(X^\mu+\Theta^\mu/2)\bar{\Psi}(X^\mu-\Theta^\mu/2)e^{-iP^\nu\Theta_\nu}\), with the metric signature \(\eta^{\mu\nu}=\text{diag}[1,-1,-1,-1]\).
* The classical (pre-quantized) Poincaré commutation relations include \([J^j,J^k]=-i\epsilon^{jkl}J^l\), \([K^j,K^k]=-i\epsilon^{jkl}J^l\), and \([P^\mu,P^\nu]=0\), among others.
* The quantum case arises from the additional commutator \([X^\mu,P_\nu] = -i\hbar\delta^\mu_\nu\), while the classical case uses \([X^\mu,P_\nu]=0\) with the relativistic Koopman algebra \([X^\mu,\Lambda_\nu]= -i\delta^\mu_\nu\) and \([P_\mu,\Theta^\nu] = -i\delta^\nu_\mu\).

## Context

This work extends the KvNS-based interpretation of the Wigner function as a probability amplitude (Bondar et al., 2013; McCaul et al., 2023) from non-relativistic quantum mechanics to relativistic QCD, building on the Operational Dynamic Modeling framework developed by the same group. It provides a foundation for understanding Parton Distribution Functions (PDFs), Generalized Parton Distributions (GPDs), and Transverse Momentum Dependent Parton Distributions (TMDs) as derived from the Wigner operator rather than postulated a priori, and clarifies the origin of negativity in the Wigner function as a natural property of a wavefunction amplitude rather than a quasiprobability.
