# Electromagnetic Heavy Lepton Pair Production in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We calculate the cross sections of electromagnetic productions of muon and tauon pair productions from the ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. Since the Compton wavelengths of muon and tauon are comparable to the radius of the colliding ions, nuclear form factors play important roles for calculating the cross sections. Recent measurement [1] indicates that the neutrons are differently distributed from the protons therefore this affects the cross section of the heavy lepton pair production. In order to see the effects of the neutron distributions in the nucleus, we used analytical expression of the Fourier transforms of the Wood-Saxon distribution. Cross section calculations show that Wood-Saxon distribution function is more sensitive to the parameter R compare to the parameter a.

## Key claim

The cross sections for electromagnetic muon and tauon pair production in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions are significantly reduced when realistic nuclear form factors (Wood-Saxon distribution) are used instead of point-like nuclei, and the reduction depends sensitively on the neutron distribution parameters, particularly the nuclear radius parameter R rather than the diffuseness parameter a.

## Method

The authors calculated the two-photon Feynman diagrams for lepton pair production in peripheral heavy-ion collisions, using the external field model with classical ion trajectories. They incorporated an analytical Fourier transform of the Wood-Saxon (two-parameter Fermi) nuclear charge distribution to account for the finite size of the nuclei and the difference between proton and neutron distributions, and included the proton dipole form factor. Cross sections were computed for symmetric Au+Au collisions at RHIC energies (100 GeV/nucleon) and Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies (3400 GeV/nucleon) for electron, muon, and tauon pairs.

## Key numbers

- Muon pair production cross section at RHIC (Au+Au): 7.3e-1 barn without form factor, reduced to 2.03e-1 barn with Wood-Saxon form factor
- Muon pair production cross section at LHC (Pb+Pb): 5.2 barn without form factor, reduced to 2.7 barn with Wood-Saxon form factor
- Tauon pair production cross section at RHIC (Au+Au): 4.7e-4 barn without form factor, reduced to 3.9e-6 barn with Wood-Saxon form factor (reduction by factor of about 100)
- Tauon pair production cross section at LHC (Pb+Pb): 7.7e-3 barn without form factor, reduced to 1.6e-3 barn with Wood-Saxon form factor (reduction by factor of about 5)
- Electron pair production cross section at RHIC: 3.63e4 barn without form factor, 3.62e4 barn with form factor (negligible difference)
- Electron pair production cross section at LHC: 2.44e5 barn without form factor, 2.43e5 barn with form factor (negligible difference)
- For muon pairs at RHIC, varying R by 0.9 fm (from 6.1 fm to 7.0 fm) with fixed a=0.55 fm gives a cross section change of 11%; varying a by 0.2 fm (from 0.4 fm to 0.6 fm) with fixed R gives a change of 4%
- For tauon pairs at RHIC, varying R by 0.9 fm gives a cross section change of 35%; varying a by 0.2 fm gives a change of 14%
- Neutron skin thickness for lead nucleus: R_skin = 0.33^{+0.16}_{-0.18} fm, from the JLab parity-violating electron scattering experiment (Abrahamyan et al.)

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of electromagnetic lepton pair production by the same group (Guclu et al., earlier works) and others (Bottcher, Baltz, Baur) who studied electron-positron production in the 1990s and 2000s motivated by RHIC. The key advance is using an analytical Fourier transform of the Wood-Saxon distribution rather than the monopole approximation, allowing the authors to incorporate the neutron skin effect measured by Abrahamyan et al. (2012) at JLab. The results enable more accurate background estimates for dilepton signals from hadronic interactions in heavy-ion collisions, and show that the nuclear radius parameter R affects cross sections more strongly than the diffuseness parameter a, which is relevant for future experiments probing neutron distributions.
