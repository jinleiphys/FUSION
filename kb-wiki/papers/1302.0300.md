# Challenging claims of "elliptic flow" by comparing azimuth quadrupole and jet-related angular correlations from Au-Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = $ 62 and 200 GeV

> Background: A component of azimuth correlations from high-energy heavy ion collisions varying as $\cos(2ϕ)$ and denoted by symbol $v_2$ is conventionally interpreted to represent "elliptic flow," a hydrodynamic manifestation of the initial-state åoverlap geometry. Several numerical methods are used to estimate $v_2$, resulting in various combinations of "flow" and "nonflow" that reveal systematic biases in the $v_2$ estimates. QCD jets contribute strongly to azimuth correlations and specifically to the $\cos(2ϕ)$ component. Purpose: We question the extent of jet-related ("nonflow") bias in and hydrodynamic "flow" interpretations of $v_2$ measurements. Method: We introduce two-dimensional (2D) model fits to angular correlation data that distinguish accurately between jet-related correlation components and a {\em nonjet azimuth quadrupole} that might represent "elliptic flow" if that were relevant. We compare measured jet-related and "flow"-related data systematics and determine the jet-related contribution to $v_2$ measurements. Results: Jet structure does introduce substantial bias to conventional $v_2$ measurements, making interpretation difficult. The nonjet quadrupole exhibits very simple systematics on centrality and collision energy---the two variables factorize. Within a \auau centrality interval where jets show no indication of rescattering or medium effects the nonjet quadrupole amplitude rises to 60% of its maximum value. Conclusions: Disagreements between nonjet quadrupole systematics and hydro theory expectations, the large quadrupole amplitudes observed in more-peripheral \auau collisions and a significant nonzero value in \nn $\approx$ \pp collisions strongly suggest that the nonjet quadrupole does not arise from a hydrodynamic "flow" mechanism.

## Key claim

The paper challenges the conventional interpretation of the azimuth quadrupole component (v2) as representing "elliptic flow" from hydrodynamics, arguing that jet-related ("nonflow") contributions introduce substantial bias to conventional v2 measurements and that the nonjet quadrupole's systematics contradict hydrodynamic expectations, particularly noting that the nonjet quadrupole amplitude rises to 60% of its maximum value within a centrality interval where jets show no indication of rescattering or medium effects.

## Method

The authors applied two-dimensional (2D) model fits to angular autocorrelation data on azimuth and pseudorapidity from Au-Au collisions at 62 and 200 GeV, using a model that separates jet-related correlation components (a same-side 2D Gaussian and an away-side 1D dipole) from a nonjet azimuth quadrupole term (a cos(2φ) sinusoid independent of pseudorapidity). They compared the centrality and energy systematics of the nonjet quadrupole with those of jet-related correlations and with conventional v2 measurements obtained from nongraphical numerical methods such as event-plane and cumulant analyses.

## Key numbers

- The nonjet quadrupole amplitude rises to 60% of its maximum value within a Au-Au centrality interval where jets show no indication of rescattering or medium effects.
- The nonjet quadrupole exhibits factorization of centrality and collision energy dependence for all systems down to sqrt(s_NN) approximately 13 GeV.
- The optical Glauber eccentricity parametrization is given as epsilon_opt = 0.185 [log10(3 N_bin/2)]^0.96 [log10(1136/N_bin)]^0.78.
- The mean jet fragment multiplicity increases from approximately 2.5 in p-p collisions to approximately 8 in central Au-Au collisions.
- The 200 GeV N-N cross section used for Glauber parameters is sigma_NN = 42 mb.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of 2D angular autocorrelations (Trainor et al., 2005; Trainor, 2008) and the two-component model of hadron production (Kharzeev and Nardi, 2001; Trainor, 2008). It directly contradicts the conventional elliptic flow interpretation established by Ollitrault (1992) and Poskanzer and Voloshin (1998), and challenges the assumption that v2 measurements necessarily represent hydrodynamic flow. The paper enables a re-evaluation of whether the nonjet quadrupole arises from a hydrodynamic mechanism or from other physics, and provides a method for accurately separating jet-related and nonjet contributions to azimuth correlations.
