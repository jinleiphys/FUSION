# Microscopic cluster study of the 10Be and 11Be nuclei

> We use a microscopic multicluster model to investigate the structure of $^{10}$Be and of $^{11}$Be. These nuclei are described by $α+α+n+n$ and $α+α+n+n+n$ configurations, respectively, within the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM). The 4- and 5-body models raise the problem of a large number of generator coordinates (6 for $^{10}$Be and 9 for $^{11}$Be), which requires specific treatment. We address this issue by using the Stochastic Variational Method (SVM), which is based on an optimal choice of the basis functions, generated randomly. The model provides good energy spectra for low-lying states of both nuclei. We also compute rms radii and densities, as well as electromagnetic transition probabilities. We analyze the structure of $^{10}$Be and of $^{11}$Be by considering energy curves, where one of the generator coordinates is fixed during the minimization procedure.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a microscopic multicluster model using the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) combined with the Stochastic Variational Method (SVM) provides good energy spectra for low-lying states of 10Be and 11Be, but the abstract glosses over that the admixture parameter \(u\) of the Minnesota interaction must be adjusted separately for positive and negative parity states in 11Be to reproduce the parity inversion, and that the model underestimates several experimental observables.

## Method

The authors describe 10Be and 11Be as \(\alpha+\alpha+n+n\) and \(\alpha+\alpha+n+n+n\) configurations, respectively, within the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM). To handle the large number of generator coordinates (6 for 10Be and 9 for 11Be), they employ the Stochastic Variational Method (SVM) to randomly select and optimize the basis functions. The Minnesota interaction with a short-range spin-orbit term is used as the nucleon-nucleon force, with the admixture parameter \(u\) adjusted to reproduce experimental binding energies; wave functions are projected on angular momentum and parity, and the final states are obtained by diagonalizing the Hamiltonian and overlap kernels.

## Key numbers

- For 10Be, the Minnesota parameter \(u = 0.973\) reproduces the experimental binding energy with respect to the \(\alpha+\alpha+n+n\) threshold of \(-8.64\) MeV.
- For 10Be, the GCM gives a proton rms radius \(\sqrt{\langle r^2\rangle_p} = 2.27\) fm (experiment: \(2.357 \pm 0.018\) fm) and a matter rms radius \(\sqrt{\langle r^2\rangle} = 2.52\) fm (experiment: \(2.30 \pm 0.02\) fm).
- For 10Be, the GCM gives \(B(E2,0^+\rightarrow 2^+) = 28.3\) \(e^2\)fm\(^4\) (experiment: \(52 \pm 6\) \(e^2\)fm\(^4\)).
- For 11Be, with \(u = 1.066\) for positive parity and \(u = 0.893\) for negative parity, the model reproduces the \(1/2^+\) state at \(-0.50\) MeV and the \(1/2^-\) state at \(-0.18\) MeV with respect to the 10Be+\(n\) threshold.
- For 11Be, the GCM gives a proton rms radius \(\sqrt{\langle r^2\rangle_p} = 1.94\) fm (experiment: \(2.463 \pm 0.015\) fm) and a matter rms radius \(\sqrt{\langle r^2\rangle} = 2.36\) fm (experiment: \(2.73 \pm 0.05\) fm).
- For 11Be, the GCM gives \(B(E1,1/2^+\rightarrow 1/2^-) = 6.3 \times 10^{-3}\) W.u. (experiment: \(0.360 \pm 0.033\) W.u.).

## Context

This work extends a previous microscopic study of 9Be by Descouvemont and Itagaki (2018) using the same \(\alpha+\alpha+n\) model to the more complex 10Be and 11Be systems. The results contradict the experimental \(B(E1)\) value in 11Be, consistent with earlier multicluster calculations by Descouvemont (2002) and by Kanada-En'yo and collaborators, and with the No Core Shell Model by Calci and collaborators (2016), all of which also underestimate this transition; the authors suggest that explicit coupling to 10Be+\(n\) configurations is needed to improve agreement. The work enables future determination of folding potentials from the computed densities and highlights the need for effective charges or additional configurations to match electromagnetic transition data.
