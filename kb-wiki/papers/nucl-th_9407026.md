# Dynamics of Fragment Formation in the Nuclear Spinodal Region

> The Vlasov-Nordheim equation is solved numerically on a lattice for nuclear matter in two dimensions. We discuss the reliability of the model at normal density and then study the response of the system to small perturbations. We find deterministic chaos inside the spinodal zone where fragment formation occurs. We discuss in detail the dynamical features of this phenomenon in order to clarify the mechanisms leading to nuclear disassembly in heavy--ion collisions.

## Key claim

The authors find that deterministic chaos, characterized by positive Lyapunov exponents, governs fragment formation inside the nuclear spinodal zone, but this chaotic behavior is a transient phenomenon that persists only until primary fragments form, after which the dynamics changes.

## Method

The Vlasov-Nordheim equation was solved numerically on a two-dimensional lattice with periodic boundary conditions, using a local Skyrme interaction and a Fermi gas initial momentum distribution at fixed temperature. The authors introduced small sinusoidal density perturbations and tracked the time evolution of density profiles, calculating the largest Lyapunov exponent from the divergence of nearby trajectories in phase space using a metric based on spatial density differences.

## Context

This work extends earlier investigations by the same authors (Baldo, Burgio, and Rapisarda, 1994) on chaotic dynamics in nuclear matter, building on the BUU simulation framework used by Bauer (1992) and collaborators to study spinodal instabilities in heavy-ion collisions. The results challenge the validity of linear response theory for multifragmentation, showing that nonlinear mode coupling dominates after approximately $25\ \mathrm{fm}/c$ and that the Lyapunov exponent differs from the linear growth rate by almost a factor of two. This enables a new dynamical understanding of multifragmentation that connects the observed event-by-event fluctuations in charge and mass distributions (Bauer, 1986) to deterministic chaos rather than purely statistical phase-space filling.
