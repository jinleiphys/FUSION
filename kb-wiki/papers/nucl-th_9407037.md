# Effects of nuclear structure on average angular momentum in subbarrier fusion

> We investigate the effects of nuclear quadrupole and hexadecapole couplings on the average angular momentum in sub-barrier fusion reactions. This quantity could provide a probe for nuclear shapes, distinguishing between prolate vs. oblate quadrupole and positive vs. negative hexadecapole couplings. We describe the data in the O + Sm system and discuss heavier systems where shape effects become more pronounced.

## Key claim

The average angular momentum transferred to the compound nucleus in subbarrier fusion reactions can serve as a probe of nuclear shapes, distinguishing prolate from oblate quadrupole deformations and positive from negative hexadecapole couplings, but the effect is only clearly observable when the projectile is heavy enough (e.g., \(^{40}\)Ca rather than \(^{16}\)O) to magnify the coupling effects.

## Method

The authors calculated fusion cross sections and average angular momenta using a partial-wave expansion with penetration probabilities evaluated via a uniform WKB approximation. The barriers were determined from eigenchannels of a coupling operator in the interacting boson model (IBM), which describes quadrupole and hexadecapole couplings through boson operators representing correlated pairs of valence nucleons. The target wave functions were projected from intrinsic states with mean fields \(x_0\) and \(x_2\) determined from IBM Hamiltonians fitted to low-lying spectroscopic data.

## Key numbers

- For a fictitious prolate target (A=170, Z=70) with \(^{16}\)O projectile, positive (negative) hexadecapole moments add (subtract) about one unit of angular momentum near the barrier energy of about 66 MeV.
- Including hexadecapole coupling changes \(\langle\ell\rangle\) near the barrier by about 25% of the increase due to quadrupole coupling alone.
- The global Woods-Saxon potential parameters used were: \(R_2 = 2.62\) fm, \(R_1 = 1.04 A_1^{1/3}\) fm, \(V_0 = 67.5 (A_1/144)^{1/3}\) MeV, \(a = 1.22\) fm for \(^{16}\)O projectiles; for \(^{40}\)Ca projectiles, \(V_0 = 123.9 (A_1/144)^{1/3}\) MeV.
- Coulomb excitation measurements give \(\beta_2(^{152}\mathrm{Sm}) = 0.29\) and \(\beta_2(^{148}\mathrm{Sm}) = 0.14\), but subbarrier fusion data require these deformations to change much more slowly across the phase transition.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of subbarrier fusion enhancement (Beckerman 1988, Vandenbosch 1992) and barrier distributions (Rowley 1991) by focusing on average angular momentum as a complementary observable. The authors note that other authors (Rowley 1992) had previously noted differences in \(\ell\)-distributions between prolate and oblate nuclei, but this paper emphasizes the characteristic "bump" in \(\langle\ell\rangle\) for prolate systems and its absence for oblate ones. The results enable future experiments on \(^{16}\)O + \(^{192}\)Os and \(^{16}\)O + \(^{194}\)Pt (currently being carried out at the Australian National University and the University of Washington) to test whether the bump can distinguish prolate from oblate targets, especially with heavier projectiles like \(^{40}\)Ca.
