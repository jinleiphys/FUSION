# Single-particle motion at large distances in 2N+core cluster systems near the drip line: a challenge for nuclear theory and experiment

> There exists a class of nuclei that are obtained by adding one nucleon to a loosely-bound nucleon-core system, for example $^{12}$Be, $^9$C, $^{18}$Ne. For such nuclei, one-nucleon overlap integrals that represent single-particle motion can strongly differ from the standard ones due to the correlations between the two nucleons above the core. The possible non-standard overlap behaviour should be included in the interpretation of the experimental data derived from one nucleon removal reactions such as knockout, transfer and breakup, as well as the predictions of low-energy nucleon capture that leads to these nuclei. We investigate the non-standard behaviour within a three-body model and discuss the challenges associated with this problem.

## Key claim

One-nucleon overlap integrals for nuclei formed by adding a nucleon to a loosely-bound nucleon-core system (e.g., \(^{12}\)Be, \(^9\)C, \(^{18}\)Ne) can converge to their asymptotic exponential form much more slowly than standard potential models predict, due to correlations between the two valence nucleons; this non-standard behaviour is strongest for final core+N systems in relative \(l=0\) states and when the Coulomb interaction is absent.

## Method

The authors performed exact dynamical three-body (core+2N) calculations for \(^{12}\)Be (\(^{10}\)Be+n+n), \(^9\)C (\(^7\)Be+p+p), and \(^{18}\)Ne (\(^{16}\)O+p+p), solving the Schrodinger equation via expansion onto a hyperspherical harmonics basis. Overlap integrals \(\langle A-1|A\rangle\) were obtained by overlapping the three-body and two-body wave functions, using Woods-Saxon N-core potentials fitted to chosen separation energies and the GPT potential for the NN interaction, with Pauli projection for antisymmetrization. For pseudo \(^{12}\)Be, convergence up to 30 fm was achieved with \(K_{\text{max}} = 120\); for realistic \(^{12}\)Be, \(K_{\text{max}} = 34\) provided stability at 5–10 fm.

## Key numbers

- For pseudo \(^{12}\)Be with \(S_{1N}(^{11}\text{Be}) = 0.102\) MeV, the overlap r.m.s. radius was 7.650 fm, compared to 6.983 fm from a standard Woods-Saxon potential.
- For pseudo \(^{12}\)Be with \(S_{1N}(^{11}\text{Be}) = 0.496\) MeV, the overlap r.m.s. radius was 5.807 fm, compared to 5.423 fm standard.
- For realistic \(^{12}\)Be populating \(^{11}\text{Be}(1/2^+)\), the overlap r.m.s. radius was 4.412 fm (standard: 4.298 fm); for \(^{11}\text{Be}(1/2^-)\), 3.588 fm (standard: 3.473 fm); for \(^{11}\text{Be}(5/2^+)\), 3.738 fm (standard: 3.244 fm).
- For pseudo \(^9\)C, the overlap r.m.s. radius was 3.175 fm (standard: 3.016 fm).
- For \(^{18}\text{Ne}(0^+_1)\) to \(^{17}\text{F}(5/2^+)\), r.m.s. radius was 3.602 fm (standard: 3.337 fm); to \(^{17}\text{F}(1/2^+)\), 4.068 fm (standard: 3.602 fm).
- The effective local potential for pseudo \(^{12}\)Be (with \(S_{1N}=0.496\) MeV) required a second Woods-Saxon term with diffuseness \(a_1 = 3.1\) fm.
- The Feynman diagram analysis gives the non-standard term as \(\exp(-\kappa_1 r)/r^{7/2}\) with \(\kappa_1\) larger than \(\kappa\) by at most 40%; for \(^{12}\)Be, the condition \(r \gg |\kappa_1 - \kappa|^{-1}\) corresponds to \(r \gg 2.5\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the Feynman diagram analysis of Timofeyuk (2003), which first suggested abnormal asymptotic behaviour for one-nucleon overlaps in core+2N systems, by performing exact three-body dynamical calculations that confirm the effect. The results challenge the standard use of Woods-Saxon potentials in analyses of knockout, transfer, and breakup reactions for near-drip-line nuclei, and show that neutron capture rates on weakly-bound s-wave nuclei (e.g., \(^{11}\)Be(n,\(\gamma\))\(^{12}\)Be) are sensitive to the overlap shape. The paper identifies key open questions: the need for convergence-accelerating methods in three-body models with core deformation, the influence of antisymmetrization, and the difficulty of observing the effect experimentally due to reaction mechanism uncertainties.
