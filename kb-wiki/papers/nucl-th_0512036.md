# Microscopic analysis of shape-phase transitions in even-even N~90 rotating nuclei

> We study in cranked Nilsson plus random phase approximation shape transitions in fast rotating nuclei undergoing backbending, more specifically 156Dy and 162Yb. We found that a backbending in 156Dy is correlated with the disappearance of the collective, positive signature gamma-vibrational mode in the rotating frame, and, a shape transition (from axial to nonaxial) is accompanied with a large acquiring of the gamma-deformation. We show that such a shape transition can be considered as a phase transition of the first order. In 162Yb the quasiparticle alignment dominates in the backbending and the shape transition (from axial to nonaxial) is accompanied with a smooth transition from zero to nonzero values of the gamma-deformation. We extend the classical Landau theory for rotating nuclei and show that the backbending in 162Yb is identified with the second order phase transition. A description of spectral and decay properties of the yrast states and low-lying excitations demonstrates a good agreement between our results and experimental data.

## Key claim

The backbending phenomenon in even-even N~90 rotating nuclei can be interpreted as a quantum shape-phase transition, but the order of the transition differs between the two studied nuclei: in 156Dy it is a first-order phase transition characterized by a discontinuous change in gamma deformation, while in 162Yb it is a second-order phase transition with a smooth onset of triaxiality, with the specific type depending on the interplay between single-particle and collective degrees of freedom.

## Method

The authors employed a cranked Nilsson plus random phase approximation (CRPA) approach, solving Hartree-Bogoliubov equations for the rotating mean field with monopole pairing and separable quadrupole-quadrupole, monopole-monopole, and spin-spin residual interactions. They enforced self-consistency via stability conditions on doubly-stretched quadrupole moments and included a term restoring local Galilean invariance broken in the Nilsson potential. The potential energy surfaces were analyzed as functions of the gamma deformation parameter at fixed beta deformation near the transition points, and the Landau theory of phase transitions was extended to rotating nuclei by fitting polynomial expansions of the energy functional.

## Key numbers

- Critical rotational frequencies for the first band crossing: hbar Omega_c = 0.301 MeV for 156Dy and hbar Omega_c = 0.250 MeV for 162Yb
- For 156Dy at the transition point: beta_t approximately 0.31; the polynomial fit coefficients at hbar Omega_c = 0.301 MeV are F_2 = 0.12239, F_3 = 0.009199, F_4 = 1.7 x 10^-4 (gamma in degrees, energy in MeV)
- For 162Yb at the transition point: beta_t approximately 0.20; the energy difference between collective and non-collective minima near the transition is about 1 MeV
- For 156Dy: the energy difference between the two minima near the transition point is about 15 keV; the generic model predicts gamma approximately 27 degrees at the second minimum, compared to the actual value gamma approximately 20 degrees
- Pairing gaps at zero rotational frequency: for 156Dy, Delta_N(0) = 0.940 MeV, Delta_P(0) = 0.985 MeV; for 162Yb, Delta_N(0) = 0.967 MeV, Delta_P(0) = 0.975 MeV

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposal by Kvasil and Nazmitdinov (2004) that backbending in N~90 nuclei results from a transition from axial to nonaxial shape due to disappearance of a collective gamma-vibrational mode in the rotating frame. It contrasts with standard Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations (such as those by Frauendorf, 1990) that treat backbending solely as quasiparticle band crossing, and with interacting boson model studies of shape-phase transitions (such as those by Jolie, 2002 and Cejnar, 2003) that do not capture the single-particle-collective interplay crucial at high spins. The analysis enables future application of Landau theory to classify shape-phase transitions in other rotating nuclei and provides a framework for understanding low-lying excitations such as gamma-vibrational and wobbling modes at high spin.
