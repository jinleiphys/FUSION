# Low temperature limit of lattice QCD

> We study the low temperature limit of lattice QCD by using a reduction formula for a fermion determinant. The reduction formula, which is useful in finite density lattice QCD simulations, contains a reduced matrix defined as the product of $N_t$ block-matrices. It is shown that eigenvalues of the reduced matrix follows a scaling law with regard to the temporal lattice size $N_t$. The $N_t$ scaling law leads to two types of expressions of the fermion determinant in the low temperature limit; one is for small quark chemical potentials, and the other is for larger quark chemical potentials.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the fermion determinant in lattice QCD becomes independent of the quark chemical potential for values up to \(\mu = m_\pi/2\) in the low temperature limit, and that a separate expression valid for large chemical potentials yields the maximum possible quark number density on a finite lattice. These results rely on an assumed \(N_t\) scaling law for eigenvalues of the reduced matrix, which is supported by data for \(N_t=4\) and \(N_t=8\) but has not been confirmed for larger \(N_t\).

## Method

The authors use a reduction formula for the clover-improved Wilson fermion determinant, which expresses the determinant in terms of a reduced matrix \(Q\) defined as the product of \(N_t\) block-matrices describing quark hops between time slices. They compute the eigenvalues of \(Q\) on gauge configurations for \(8^3\times 4\) and \(8^3\times 8\) lattices at \(\beta=1.86\) (corresponding to \(T_c\) at \(\mu=0\) for \(N_t=4\)) with a fixed light-cone parameter \(m_{\rm PS}/m_{\rm V}=0.8\). By comparing eigenvalue distributions for \(N_t=4\) and \(N_t=8\), they infer a scaling law \(|\lambda_n| \sim l_n^{N_t}\) and use it to take the formal limit \(N_t \to \infty\) while keeping the lattice spacing and spatial volume fixed.

## Key numbers

* Eigenvalues of the reduced matrix have magnitude \(|\lambda| = 10^2 \sim 10^3\) for \(N_t=4\) and \(|\lambda| = 10^4 \sim 10^5\) for \(N_t=8\) on an \(8^3\) spatial lattice at \(\beta=1.86\).
* The eigenvalue density becomes independent of \(N_t\) when plotted as a function of \((\ln |\lambda|)/N_t\), supporting the scaling law \(|\lambda_n| \sim l_n^{N_t}\).
* The condition for the small-\(\mu\) limit is \(\exp(\mu a) \ll \min(l_n)\), which translates to \(\mu < m_\pi/2\) via the relation \(a m_\pi = -\frac{1}{N_t} \max_{|\lambda_k|<1} \ln |\lambda_k|^2\) (Gibbs 1986) or the ensemble-averaged expression of Fodor, Szabo and Toth (2007).
* In the large-\(\mu\) limit, the quark number density saturates at \(\langle n \rangle = 2 N_f N_c\) in lattice units, which is the maximum number of quarks on a finite lattice.

## Context

This work extends the reduction formula approach of Gibbs (1986), Borici (2004), Adams (2003), and the authors' own prior studies (Nagata et al. 2010, 2011, 2012) to the low temperature regime of lattice QCD. The result that the fermion determinant is independent of \(\mu\) for \(\mu < m_\pi/2\) is consistent with the analytic arguments of Cohen (2003) and Adams (2004), and provides a lattice-based explanation for this phenomenon. The large-\(\mu\) limit expression is real and could potentially be used for Monte Carlo sampling at nonzero chemical potential, though the authors note that the saturation of the quark number density is a finite-volume artifact that would shift to larger \(\mu\) as the spatial volume increases. The work also shows that the sign problem becomes more severe with increasing spatial volume because the fermion determinant scales as \(\exp(V_s)\).
