# Forming a Single, Large, DCC Domain in a Heavy-Ion Collision

> We demonstrate the possibility of forming a single, large domain of disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) in a heavy-ion collision. In our scenario, rapid initial heating of the parton system provides a driving force for the chiral field, moving it away from the true vacuum and forcing it to go to the opposite point on the vacuum manifold. This converts the entire hot region into a single DCC domain. Subsequent rolling down of the chiral field to its true vacuum will then lead to emission of a large number of (approximately) coherent pions. The requirement of suppression of thermal fluctuations to maintain the (approximate) coherence of such a large DCC domain, favors three dimensional expansion of the plasma over the longitudinal expansion even at very early stages of evolution. This also constrains the maximum temperature of the system to lie within a window. We roughly estimate this window to be about 200 - 400 MeV. These results lead us to predict that extremely high energy collisions of {\it very small nuclei} (possibly hadrons) are better suited for observing signatures of a large DCC. Another possibility is to focus on {\it peripheral} collisions of heavy nuclei.

# Forming a Single, Large, DCC Domain in a Heavy-Ion Collision

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate the possibility of forming a single, large domain of disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) in a heavy-ion collision, but only if the maximum temperature of the quark-gluon plasma lies within a window of roughly 200–400 MeV and the plasma undergoes three-dimensional (spherical) expansion rather than longitudinal expansion, even at very early stages. The scenario requires that rapid initial heating drives the chiral field to overshoot the central barrier and reach the opposite point on the vacuum manifold, converting the entire hot region into a single DCC domain.

## Method

The authors work within the framework of the linear sigma model with a finite-temperature effective potential at one-loop order, using Langevin equations of motion to incorporate thermal fluctuations. They model the temperature evolution as an initial heating stage (with various time dependencies, including linear and instantaneous heating) followed by cooling due to plasma expansion, using either the Bjorken longitudinal expansion model (η = 1) or spherical expansion (η = 3). The chiral field dynamics are evolved for soft modes over a volume that expands with the plasma, and an ensemble of trajectories is generated to study the variation of the chiral field due to thermal fluctuations, with the friction coefficient η′ taken as 0.6 fm⁻¹ for the detailed results.

## Key numbers

- The critical temperature for chiral symmetry restoration in the chiral limit is T_c = √2 f_π ≈ 134 MeV (with f_π = 94.5 MeV and λ ≈ 55, corresponding to m_σ = 1 GeV).
- The allowed maximum temperature window for forming a large DCC domain is roughly 200–400 MeV; the lower bound arises because the chiral field does not overshoot at small T_0, and the upper bound arises because thermal fluctuations become too large.
- For the instantaneous heating case with τ_0 = 0.4 fm and T_0 = 350 MeV, the σ field overshoots the central barrier and becomes maximally disoriented with chiral angle close to π; for τ_0 = 0.2 fm with the same T_0, the field does not overshoot.
- For the spherical expansion case with τ_0 = 5 fm, overshooting occurs with T_0 as low as 114 MeV (in the absence of thermal fluctuations).
- The thermally generated pionic components at the formation stage of the DCC (temperature about 115 MeV) remain less than about 13 MeV, and the patch area in pion-component space is less than 3% of the total surface area of the vacuum manifold at that stage.
- For η′ ≈ 4 fm⁻¹, the probability of forming a large DCC is estimated to be about 1/1000 per event compared to the case with η′ ≈ 1 fm⁻¹.
- The DCC domain can have a diameter as large as 14 fm for a gold nucleus.

## Context

This work extends the conventional picture of DCC formation, where small domains of order m_π⁻¹ arise from a chirally symmetric state cooling through the phase transition, as discussed in Rajagopal and Wilczek (1993) and others. The authors propose a novel mechanism where the entire plasma region is converted into a single DCC domain via rapid initial heating, in contrast to the multi-domain structure predicted by standard scenarios such as the annealing scenario of Bjorken, Kowalski, and Taylor (1993) and the parametric resonance studies of Kaiser (1994). The results suggest that extremely high energy collisions of very small nuclei (possibly hadrons) or peripheral collisions of heavy nuclei are better suited for observing large DCC signatures, and the authors note that the Tevatron may be a good place to search for DCC, consistent with expectations from Centauro events in cosmic ray experiments.
