# Global properties of nuclei at finite-temperature within the covariant energy density functional theory

> In stellar environments nuclei appear at finite temperatures, becoming extremely hot in core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers. However, due to theoretical and computational complexity, most model calculations of nuclear properties are performed at zero temperature, while those existing at finite temperatures are limited only to selected regions of the nuclide chart. In this study we perform the global calculation of nuclear properties for even-even $8 \leq Z \leq 104$ nuclei at temperatures in range $0\le T \le 2$ MeV. Calculations are based on the finite temperature relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov model supplemented by the Bonche-Levit-Vautherin vapor subtraction procedure. We find that near the neutron-drip line the continuum states have significant contribution already at moderate temperature $T\approx 1$ MeV, thus emphasising the necessity of the vapor subtraction procedure. Results include neutron emission lifetimes, quadrupole deformations, neutron skin thickness, proton and neutron pairing gaps, entropy and excitation energy. Up to the temperature $T\approx 1$ MeV nuclear landscape is influenced only moderately by the finite-temperature effects, mainly by reducing the pairing correlations. As the temperature increases further, the effects on nuclear structures become pronounced, reducing both the deformations and the shell effects.

## Key claim

The abstract states that finite-temperature effects become pronounced above T ≈ 1 MeV, but the paper demonstrates that for nuclei near the neutron-drip line, continuum states contribute significantly already at T = 1 MeV, requiring the Bonche-Levit-Vautherin vapor subtraction procedure to achieve convergence of binding energies.

## Method

The authors performed global finite-temperature relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (FT-RHB) calculations for even-even nuclei with proton numbers 8 ≤ Z ≤ 104 at temperatures from 0 to 2 MeV, assuming axial symmetry. They employed three relativistic energy density functionals (DD-ME2, DD-PC1, and DD-PCX) with a separable pairing interaction, and implemented the Bonche-Levit-Vautherin vapor subtraction procedure to properly treat the particle continuum in weakly-bound nuclei near the neutron drip line.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range studied: 0 ≤ T ≤ 2 MeV for even-even nuclei with 8 ≤ Z ≤ 104
- At T = 1 MeV, the number of neutron continuum states in ²¹⁰Gd increased from approximately 5.4 for N_osc = 20 harmonic oscillator shells to approximately 7.1 for N_osc = 28 shells
- At T = 2 MeV, adding four oscillator shells shifted the minimum of the potential energy curve for ²¹⁰Gd by more than 6 MeV without vapor subtraction
- With the BLV subtraction procedure, the change in free energy when increasing the basis from 20 to 28 harmonic oscillator shells was around 100 keV
- Two-neutron drip line nuclei at T = 1 MeV have neutron emission lifetimes of order 10⁻²¹ to 10⁻²² seconds
- At T = 2 MeV, no nuclei are stable with respect to neutron emission

## Context

This work extends the finite-temperature nuclear structure calculations beyond previous studies that were limited to selected regions of the nuclide chart, such as those by Yuksel (2014) on neutron skin thickness and by PhysRevC.88.034308 on pairing gaps. It builds upon the recent mapping of finite-temperature drip lines by Ravlic (2023) within the relativistic EDF framework. The global calculation enables systematic studies of nuclear properties relevant for core-collapse supernova and neutron star merger environments, where temperatures exceed 2 MeV, and provides a benchmark for future inclusion of thermal fluctuations.
