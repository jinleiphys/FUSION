# Weak Decay of Lambda Hypernuclei

> In this review we discuss the present status of strange nuclear physics, with special attention to the weak decay of Lambda hypernuclei. The models proposed for the evaluation of the Lambda decay widths are summarized and their results are compared with the data. Despite the recent intensive investigations, the main open problem remains a sound theoretical interpretation of the large experimental values of the ratio G_n/G_p. Although recent works offer a step forward in the solution of the puzzle, further efforts must be invested in order to understand the detailed dynamics of the non-mesonic decay. Even if, by means of single nucleon spectra measurements, the error bars on G_n/G_p have been considerably reduced very recently at KEK, a clean extraction of G_n/G_p is needed. What is missing at present, but planned for the next future, are measurements of 1) nucleon energy spectra in double coincidence and 2) nucleon angular correlations: such observations allow to disentangle the nucleons produced in one- and two-body induced decays and lead to a direct determination of G_n/G_p. For the asymmetric non-mesonic decay of polarized hypernuclei the situation is even more puzzling. Indeed, strong inconsistencies appear already among data. A recent experiment obtained a positive intrinsic Lambda asymmetry parameter, a_{Lambda}, for 5_{Lambda}He. This is in complete disagreement with a previous measurement, which obtained a large and negative a_{Lambda} for p-shell hypernuclei, and with theory, which predicts a negative value moderately dependent on nuclear structure effects. Also in this case, improved experiments establishing with certainty the sign and magnitude of a_{Lambda} for s- and p-shell hypernuclei will provide a guidance for a deeper understanding of hypernuclear dynamics and decay mechanisms.

## Key claim

This review paper summarizes the current status of weak decay of Lambda hypernuclei, but the central unresolved problem is that theoretical models cannot satisfactorily explain the large experimental values of the neutron-to-proton decay width ratio Gamma_n/Gamma_p, and the experimental uncertainties are still too large to reach definitive conclusions about this puzzle or the related Delta I=1/2 isospin rule.

## Method

The authors review and compare various theoretical models proposed for evaluating the mesonic and non-mesonic decay widths of Lambda hypernuclei, summarizing the calculated results alongside available experimental data. They analyze the status of single-nucleon and double-coincidence measurements, the extraction of the Gamma_n/Gamma_p ratio, and the asymmetric decay of polarized hypernuclei, including measurements of the intrinsic Lambda asymmetry parameter a_Lambda.

## Key numbers

- The free Lambda decay releases Q = 40 MeV, while the free neutron decay releases Q = 0.78 MeV
- The Lambda binding energy in hypertriton (^3_Lambda H) is approximately 130 KeV
- The charge symmetry breaking in the A=3 doublet is given by (B_Lambda(^4_Lambda He) - B_Lambda(^4_Lambda H)) / B_Lambda(^4_Lambda He) = 0.35 MeV / 2.39 MeV ≈ 0.15
- The M1 transition energy for the ^7_Lambda Li ground state doublet is 691.7 ± 1.2 KeV
- The Lambda-nucleus mean field depth is about 30 MeV for hypernuclei beyond the s-shell, compared to 50-55 MeV for nucleons
- The Lambda binding energy in ^5_Lambda He is approximately 3.12 MeV
- The Sigma-nucleus potential depth is in the range 8 to 15 MeV for hypernuclei beyond the s-shell
- The ^4_Sigma He bound state has binding energy 2.8 to 4.4 MeV and width 7.0 to 12.1 MeV
- The Xi-nucleus potential depth from old emulsion data is 20 to 28 MeV, while more recent estimates favor 12 to 16 MeV for ^12_Xi- C and ^12_Xi- Be
- The conversion width for Xi- p → Lambda Lambda is estimated at about 5 MeV or less, with more recent calculations giving 1.6 MeV for s-states and 0.9 MeV for p-states
- For ^5_Xi- He the calculated width is 0.76 MeV, with a Xi- binding energy of only 1.7 MeV

## Context

This review extends the extensive body of work on hypernuclear physics that began with the first hypernucleus observation by Danysz and Pniewski (1953) and covers developments through the late 1990s and early 2000s. The paper discusses the Gamma_n/Gamma_p puzzle in the context of recent KEK experiments that reduced error bars on single nucleon spectra measurements, and it highlights the need for future double-coincidence measurements and nucleon angular correlation studies to directly determine Gamma_n/Gamma_p. The review also addresses the contradictory experimental results for the intrinsic Lambda asymmetry parameter a_Lambda, where a recent experiment found a positive value for ^5_Lambda He in complete disagreement with an earlier measurement that obtained a large negative value for p-shell hypernuclei and with theoretical predictions.
