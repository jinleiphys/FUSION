# Quantum Monte Carlo Methods for Nuclei at Finite Temperature

> We discuss finite temperature quantum Monte Carlo methods in the framework of the interacting nuclear shell model. The methods are based on a representation of the imaginary-time many-body propagator as a superposition of one-body propagators describing non-interacting fermions moving in fluctuating auxiliary fields. Fermionic Monte Carlo calculations have been limited by a ``sign'' problem. A practical solution in the nuclear case enables realistic calculations in much larger configuration spaces than can be solved by conventional methods. Good-sign interactions can be constructed for realistic estimates of certain nuclear properties. We present various applications of the methods for calculating collective properties and level densities.

# Quantum Monte Carlo Methods for Nuclei at Finite Temperature

## Key claim

The paper presents a practical solution to the fermionic Monte Carlo "sign" problem that enables realistic shell model calculations in much larger configuration spaces than conventional diagonalization methods, but only for interactions that can be decomposed into a "good-sign" part (with all negative eigenvalues) plus a small "bad" part that can be extrapolated away, or for specially constructed good-sign Hamiltonians that capture the dominant collective components of realistic effective interactions.

## Method

The method uses the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to represent the imaginary-time many-body propagator as a path integral over one-body propagators describing non-interacting fermions moving in fluctuating auxiliary fields. The multi-dimensional integral over auxiliary fields is evaluated by Monte Carlo sampling, with the integrand calculated using matrix algebra in the single-particle space via determinants and matrix inversions. For realistic interactions that produce a sign problem, the Hamiltonian is decomposed into good-sign and bad-sign parts, Monte Carlo calculations are performed for a family of Hamiltonians with a continuous coupling parameter on the bad part, and results are extrapolated to the physical value. Alternatively, good-sign Hamiltonians are constructed that include only attractive pairing and multipole-multipole interactions.

## Key numbers

- Conventional diagonalization of the nuclear Hamiltonian in a full shell is limited to nuclei up to A ~ 50
- The total Gamow-Teller strength is renormalized by (1/1.26)^2 ~ 63% to account for the normalization of the axial coupling constant from g_A = 1.26 to g_A = 1 in the nuclear medium
- The good-sign Hamiltonian uses renormalization factors of k_2=2, k_3=1.5 and k_4=1 for quadrupole, octupole and hexadecupole terms
- The pairing strength g_0 is approximately 0.2 MeV, determined from experimental odd-even mass differences
- The model space including the pf+g_{9/2} shell is good for excitation energies up to E_x ~ 20 MeV
- In the Hartree-Fock approximation for 56Fe, a discontinuity in the heat capacity occurs around beta ~ 1.3 MeV^{-1} due to a shape transition from deformed to spherical nucleus

## Context

This work extends the shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) methods developed by Lang, Johnson, Koonin (1993) and Alhassid, Dean, Koonin (1994) for finite-temperature nuclear calculations, building on the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation (1957). The method enables calculations in configuration spaces that cannot be solved by conventional diagonalization, which is limited to A ~ 50, and goes beyond thermal mean-field approximations such as Hartree-Fock and random phase approximation (RPA) by including large-amplitude fluctuations. The applications demonstrate that proton-neutron correlations account for the observed quenching of Gamow-Teller strength in pf-shell nuclei, provide the first microscopic evidence of gamma-softness in the 100-140 mass region, and yield level densities for iron-region nuclei that agree with experimental data without adjustable parameters, enabling predictions for nuclei where experimental data are unavailable.
