# Transformation between statistical ensembles in the modelling of nuclear fragmentation

> We explore the conditions under which the particle number conservation constraint deforms the predictions of fragmentation observables as calculated in the grand canonical ensemble. We derive an analytical formula allowing to extract canonical results from a grand canonical calculation and vice versa. This formula shows that exact canonical results can be recovered for observables varying linearly or quadratically with the number of particles, independent of the grand canonical particle number fluctuations. We explore the validity of such grand canonical extrapolation for different fragmentation observables in the framework of the analytical Grand Canonical or Canonical Thermodynamical Model [(G)CTM] of nuclear multifragmentation. It is found that corrections to the grand canonical expectations can be evaluated with high precision, provided the system does not experience a first order phase transition. In particular, because of the Coulomb quenching of the liquid-gas phase transition of nuclear matter, we find that mass conservation corrections to the grand canonical ensemble can be safely computed for typical observables of interest in experimental measurements of nuclear fragmentation, even if deviations exist for highly exclusive observables.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that canonical and grand-canonical ensemble predictions for nuclear fragmentation observables can be transformed into each other with high precision using a simple analytical formula, provided the system does not experience a first-order phase transition; in the specific case of nuclear multifragmentation, the Coulomb interaction quenches the liquid-gas phase transition, making the transformation safe for typical inclusive observables even though deviations persist for highly exclusive observables like the distribution of the largest cluster.

## Method

The authors derived an analytical transformation formula (Equation 8) that expresses canonical results as a function of grand-canonical results using a second-order Taylor expansion of the observable around the grand-canonical average particle number, truncated at the variance term. They tested this formula by comparing exact canonical and grand-canonical calculations within the analytically solvable (Grand) Canonical Thermodynamical Model [(G)CTM] of nuclear multifragmentation, using a Wigner-Seitz approximation for the Coulomb interaction and a recursion relation for the canonical partition sum. The test was performed for systems with and without Coulomb interactions, at various temperatures and particle numbers, to identify the conditions under which the transformation holds.

## Key numbers

* For a system of N=200 nucleons at T=5 MeV and volume V=6V0(200), the exact canonical total average multiplicity is 18.034, while the grand-canonical approximation via Equation 8 gives 18.028 (0.03% difference); the exact canonical average size of the largest cluster is 39.896, while the approximation gives 39.920 (0.06% difference).
* At T=3 MeV for the same system, the exact canonical total multiplicity is 3.344, but the grand-canonical approximation gives 3.242 (3.0% difference); the exact canonical largest cluster size is 83.672, while the approximation gives 86.116 (2.9% difference).
* For a system of N=400 at T=4 MeV, the exact canonical total multiplicity is 14.932, and the grand-canonical approximation gives 14.931 (0.007% difference); the exact canonical largest cluster size is 73.589, while the approximation gives 73.631 (0.06% difference).
* With Coulomb interactions switched off at T=5 MeV for N=200, the exact canonical total multiplicity is 3.748, but the grand-canonical approximation gives 0.543 (85.5% error), demonstrating catastrophic failure in the presence of a phase transition.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of ensemble inequivalence in finite nuclear systems by Gargi (2007) and Gargi (2012), which showed that inclusive fragmentation variables converge when temperature is not too low and multiplicity is sufficiently high. The analytical transformation formula was previously introduced for one-dimensional metallic alloys by Maras and for free ideal gases by Kosov. The paper enables practical use of the computationally simpler grand-canonical ensemble to approximate canonical results for experimental multifragmentation data, and suggests future extension to implement energy conservation constraints.
