# Numerical Study of Stellar Core Collapse and Neutrino Emission Using the Nuclear Equation of State Obtained by the Variational Method

> Core-collapse simulations of massive stars are performed using the equation of state (EOS) based on the microscopic variational calculation with realistic nuclear forces. The progenitor models with the initial masses of $15M_\odot$, $9.6M_\odot$, and $30M_\odot$ are adopted as examples of the ordinary core-collapse supernova with a shock stall, the low-mass supernova with a successful explosion, and the black hole formation, respectively. Moreover, the neutrinos emitted from the stellar collapse are assessed. Then, the variational EOS is confirmed to work well in all cases. The EOS dependences of the dynamics, thermal structure, and neutrino emission of the stellar collapse are also investigated.

## Key claim

The Togashi equation of state (EOS), based on microscopic variational calculations with realistic nuclear forces, works well for core-collapse simulations across diverse progenitor masses, but the success or failure of explosion depends sensitively on the initial entropy profile and resulting mass accretion rate, which differ between EOS models due to differences in the dissociation temperature of heavy nuclei at low densities.

## Method

The authors performed spherically symmetric, general relativistic neutrino-radiation hydrodynamics simulations using a code that solves the Boltzmann equation for neutrino distributions by the discrete ordinate method. They adopted three progenitor models (9.6, 15, and 30 solar masses) and compared results using the Togashi variational EOS against the Shen EOS and, for the black hole formation case, the LS220 EOS. The simulations followed collapse, bounce, shock propagation, and neutrino emission, with the 9.6 and 15 solar mass models tracked for 200-500 ms after bounce and the 30 solar mass model until black hole formation.

## Key numbers

- For the 15 solar mass model, the Togashi EOS model had a smaller lepton fraction than the Shen EOS model at core bounce, leading to a smaller inner core size because the homologous core mass is proportional to the square of the lepton fraction.
- For the 9.6 solar mass model, the Togashi EOS model exhibited a successful explosion with the shock reaching 500 km at 137 ms after bounce, while the Shen EOS model did not explode; the bounce time was 252 ms for Togashi versus 228 ms for Shen.
- For the 30 solar mass black hole formation model, the time from bounce to black hole formation was 533 ms for the Togashi EOS, compared to 342 ms for LS220 EOS and 842 ms for Shen EOS.
- The critical baryonic mass triggering recollapse to a black hole was 2.49 solar masses for Togashi EOS, 2.32 solar masses for LS220 EOS, and 2.71 solar masses for Shen EOS.
- The Togashi EOS has saturation density 0.16 fm^-3, saturation energy -16.09 MeV, incompressibility 245 MeV, symmetry energy 29.1 MeV, and symmetry energy slope parameter 38.7 MeV.
- The maximum neutron star mass for the Togashi EOS is 2.21 solar masses, and the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is 11.6 km.

## Context

This work extends previous core-collapse simulations using the Togashi EOS for proto-neutron star cooling by Nakazato (2018) and for binary neutron star mergers by Ma (2019), applying it to the full collapse phase including bounce and shock propagation. The results confirm that the Togashi EOS produces different initial entropy profiles than the Shen EOS due to differences in heavy nucleus dissociation temperature, as previously noted by Togashi (2017), and that this difference can determine whether a low-mass progenitor explodes, consistent with the findings of Suwa (2016) on the importance of initial entropy for explosion success. The black hole formation results extend the work of Nakazato (2013) and Nadezhin (2015) by providing a new EOS comparison, and the longer time to black hole formation for Togashi versus LS220 EOS is consistent with the incompressibility hierarchy.
