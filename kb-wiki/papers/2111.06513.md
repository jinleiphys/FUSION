# Smoothing of one- and two-dimensional discontinuities in potential energy surfaces

> Background: The generation of potential energy surfaces is a critical step in theoretical models aiming to understand and predict nuclear fission. Discontinuities frequently arise in these surfaces in unconstrained collective coordinates, leading to missing or incorrect results. Purpose: This work aims to produce efficient and physically-motivated computational algorithms to refine potential energy surfaces by removing discontinuities. Method: Procedures based on tree-search algorithms are developed which are capable of smoothing discontinuities in one and two-dimensional potential energy surfaces while minimising their overall energy. Results: Each of the new methods is applied to smooth candidate discontinuities in ${}^{252}\mathrm{Cf}$, ${}^{222}\mathrm{Th}$ and ${}^{218}\mathrm{Ra}$. The effectiveness of each case is analysed both qualitatively and quantitatively. The one-dimensional method is also compared to the adiabatic and linear interpolation approaches which are commonly used to remove discontinuities. Conclusions: The smoothing methods presented in this work are resource-efficient and successful for one- and two-dimensional discontinuities; they will improve the fidelity of potential energy surfaces as well as their subsequent uses in beyond mean-field applications. Complex discontinuities occurring in higher dimensions may require alternative approaches which better utilise prior knowledge of the potential energy surface to narrow their searches.

## Key claim

The paper presents tree-search-based algorithms (Dynamic Programming Method and Frontier DPM) that can smooth discontinuities in one- and two-dimensional potential energy surfaces while minimizing total energy, but the authors note that complex discontinuities in higher dimensions may require alternative approaches that better exploit prior knowledge of the potential energy surface.

## Method

The authors adapted the Dynamic Programming Method (DPM), originally developed for fission lifetime calculations, to find minimum-energy continuous paths across one-dimensional discontinuities in two-dimensional PES grids, imposing maximum gradient and minimum overlap conditions to ensure continuity. For two-dimensional discontinuities in three-dimensional PES grids, they developed a "Frontier DPM" algorithm that iteratively builds a surface from boundary conditions by generating successors for each frontier point and retaining only the lowest-energy surface among those sharing identical frontiers. Both methods were applied to discontinuities in the PESs of Cf-252, Th-222, and Ra-218, with the one-dimensional method compared against adiabatic and linear interpolation approaches.

## Key numbers

* The mesh sizes for all PES calculations were 2 b^(l/2) in Q20, Q30, and Q40 coordinates.
* A continuous PES is suggested to have a maximum density distance between closest neighbours of D_max < 2, based on empirical grounds from prior work (Dubray, 2012).
* The DPM algorithm reduces the search space from M^N possible paths to exploring only MN paths, where M is the number of y-values and N is the number of steps.
* The Frontier DPM algorithm reduces the search from L^(MN) possible surfaces to considering on the order of NML^(M+1) surfaces, where L is the number of z-values, M and N are grid dimensions.

## Context

This work extends the conceptual "connecting points" method proposed by Dubray (2012) for smoothing discontinuities by implementing concrete tree-search algorithms, and contrasts with the adiabatic and linear interpolation approaches commonly used to remove discontinuities. The methods are designed to improve the fidelity of potential energy surfaces for use in beyond mean-field applications such as the Time-Dependent Generator Coordinate Method (TDGCM), where discontinuities cause overlaps to go to zero and prohibit time evolution. The work builds on prior studies of discontinuities in PESs by Dubray (2012), Regnier (2016, 2019), and Zdeb (2021), and the authors suggest that future work could extend these methods to handle discontinuities near scission or incorporate non-adiabatic effects.
