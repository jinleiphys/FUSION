# Hyperon-Nucleon Spectrometer

> Chirality lies at the heart of low-energy QCD, governing the symmetry structure that shapes hadron masses and strong interaction dynamics. Among the most compelling open questions tied to chiral dynamics and spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking is the longstanding $Λ$ polarization puzzle, in which $Λ$ hyperons produced in unpolarized hadronic collisions exhibit a surprisingly large transverse polarization that remains theoretically unexplained. This whitepaper presents the proposal for the Hyperon-Nucleon Spectrometer (H-NS) at the High-Intensity heavy-ion Accelerator Facility (HIAF). Leveraging the high energy and high intensity of HIAF's proton and heavy-ion beams, the H-NS experiment will perform systematic studies of hyperon polarization phenomena and their underlying mechanisms in proton-proton ($pp$), proton-nucleus ($pA$), and nucleus-nucleus ($AA$) collisions in the fixed target mode. A wide-range beam energy scan, including proton beams from 3 GeV up to 9.3 GeV (HIAF) and up to 32 GeV (upgraded HIAF), will be conducted to examine the dependence of polarization on collision energy. The spectrometer is designed with specialized detectors capable of high-precision reconstruction of final-state baryon polarizations. Among its many interesting and important measurements, H-NS will simultaneously measure hyperon and proton spin observables to explore the polarization mechanism in hadronic interactions and the spin structure of baryons. Furthermore, the use of $pA$ and $AA$ collisions will enable detailed investigations of cold and hot nuclear matter effects on spin polarization. Its physics program and detector development will significantly benefit the future Electron-ion Collider in China.

# Hyperon-Nucleon Spectrometer

## Key claim
The Hyperon-Nucleon Spectrometer (H-NS) at HIAF will perform systematic studies of hyperon polarization phenomena across proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions, with the specific aim of addressing the longstanding Lambda polarization puzzle; however, the paper presents only the detector design and physics requirements, not any polarization measurements themselves, and the proposed beam energy scan from 3 GeV to 9.3 GeV (HIAF) and up to 32 GeV (upgraded HIAF) is contingent on future beam energy upgrades.

## Method
The H-NS spectrometer integrates a five-layer Monolithic Active Pixel Sensor (MAPS) vertex tracker with 30-micrometer pixel size and 0.5% radiation length per layer, a Low Gain Avalanche Diode (LGAD) based time-of-flight system with 30 picosecond timing resolution, a PbWO4/lead-glass electromagnetic calorimeter, and a superconducting solenoid providing 1.5 Tesla magnetic field. A thin carbon foil between tracking layers serves as a baryon polarimeter for direct proton polarization measurement via elastic scattering. The detector operates in fixed-target mode with unpolarized targets (liquid hydrogen, carbon, calcium, titanium) in the first stage, with future polarized helium-3 and solid polarized proton/deuteron targets planned.

## Key numbers
- Momentum resolution: approximately 2% at 1 GeV/c
- Vertex resolution: less than 500 micrometers
- Material budget per tracker layer: less than 0.5% X/X0
- LGAD timing resolution: 30 picoseconds per track
- Calorimeter energy resolution: 3%/sqrt(E) (GeV)
- Calorimeter spatial resolution: approximately 3 mm
- Polar angle acceptance: 5 degrees to 100 degrees
- Event rate capability: up to 1 MHz
- Solenoid field strength: 1.5 Tesla
- MAPS pixel size: 30 x 27 square micrometers
- Total MAPS area: 5.2 square meters (2.9 square meters barrel, 2.3 square meters forward)
- Beam energy range: 3 GeV to 9.3 GeV (HIAF), up to 32 GeV (upgraded HIAF)
- PbWO4 crystal array: 35 x 35 modules (1225 total), each 2.05 x 2.05 x 20 cubic centimeters

## Context
This whitepaper extends the line of experimental investigations into the Lambda polarization puzzle, a phenomenon where Lambda hyperons produced in unpolarized hadronic collisions exhibit large transverse polarization that remains theoretically unexplained despite decades of study. The H-NS design builds on MAPS technology previously deployed in the STAR Heavy Flavor Tracker and ALICE ITS2, and on LGAD technology developed by the RD50 collaboration for HL-LHC timing detectors. The spectrometer will enable simultaneous measurement of hyperon and proton spin observables across a wide energy range, and its physics program and detector development will significantly benefit the future Electron-Ion Collider in China.
