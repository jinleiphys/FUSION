# Chiral phase transition in a rotating sphere

> We study the chiral phase transition of the two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model in a rotating sphere, which includes both rotation and finite size effects. We find that rotation leads to a suppression of the chiral condensate at a finite temperature, while its effects are smaller than the finite size effects. Our work can be helpful to study the effects relevant to rotation in heavy-ion collisions in a more realistic way.

# Chiral phase transition in a rotating sphere

## Key claim

The authors find that rotation suppresses the chiral condensate at finite temperature in the two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model confined to a sphere, but this rotational suppression is smaller than the finite-size suppression; rotation has no effect at zero temperature.

## Method

The authors solve the Dirac equation in a rotating frame with a spherical boundary, imposing the MIT boundary condition to confine the system. They then incorporate the resulting discrete momentum spectrum into the NJL gap equation at finite temperature, treating the rotational term Omega m_j as an effective chemical potential. The proper time regularization scheme is used to handle ultraviolet divergences, and the chiral condensate is computed in the mean-field approximation assuming spatial homogeneity.

## Key numbers

- The effective mass decreases with decreasing sphere radius; results are shown for radii R = 0.985 fm, 1.576 fm, 2.955 fm, and 5.91 fm.
- The model parameters are: current quark mass m_0 = 5 MeV, proper time cutoff tau_UV = 1/1080 MeV^{-2}, and coupling G = 3.26 x 10^{-6} MeV^{-2}.
- Rotation has considerable effects only when the product Omega R is close to unity, which corresponds to the causality limit Omega R < 1.
- The typical system size in heavy-ion collisions is estimated to be about 2-10 fm (Palhares 2011).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of rotating fermionic matter in unbounded geometries (Yin and Jiang) and in cylindrical geometries (bound1, cylinder) by adding a spherical boundary with the MIT boundary condition. It builds on the authors' prior comparison of chiral phase transitions in a sphere versus a box (Zhang et al.), where the MIT boundary condition was found to induce stronger finite-size effects than antiperiodic boundary conditions. The results enable more realistic modeling of the hot, dense QCD matter created in noncentral heavy-ion collisions, where both rotation and finite size are relevant.
