# Random-Matrix Approach to RPA equations. I

> We study the RPA equations in their most general form by taking the matrix elements appearing in the RPA equations as random. This yields either a unitarily or an orthogonally invariant random-matrix model which is not of the Cartan type. The average spectrum of the model is studied with the help of a generalized Pastur equation. Two independent parameters govern the behaviour of the system: The strength $α^2$ of the coupling between positive- and negative-energy states and the distance between the origin and the centers of the two semicircles that describe the average spectrum for $α^2 = 0$, the latter measured in units of the equal radii of the two semicircles. With increasing $α^2$, positive- and negative-energy states become mixed and ever more of the spectral strength of the positive-energy states is transferred to those at negative energy, and vice versa. The two semicircles are deformed and pulled toward each other. As they begin to overlap, the RPA equations yield non--real eigenvalues: The system becomes unstable. We determine analytically the critical value of the strength for the instability to occur. Several features of the model are illustrated numerically.

# Random-Matrix Approach to RPA Equations. I

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a random-matrix model for the RPA equations, where the residual interaction matrix elements are treated as random variables, yields a non-Hermitian ensemble not belonging to the Altland-Zirnbauer classification; the average spectrum is governed by two parameters (the coupling strength α² between positive- and negative-energy states and the distance r of the semicircle centers from the origin), and the system becomes unstable when the two spectral branches begin to overlap, producing non-real eigenvalues.

## Method

The authors construct a Gaussian random-matrix model for the RPA matrix ℋ⁰, where the Hermitian (or real symmetric) matrix A representing particle-hole interactions is drawn from the GUE (or GOE) with variance λ²/N, and the complex (or real) symmetric matrix C representing pairing-type couplings is drawn independently with variance γ²/N. They derive a generalized Pastur equation for the average Green's function in the limit N → ∞, obtaining two coupled quadratic equations for the functions σ₁(E) and σ₂(E) that describe the spectral density projected onto the two N-dimensional subspaces. The average spectrum is analyzed analytically via these equations and illustrated with numerical diagonalizations for finite matrix dimension (2N = 40).

## Key numbers

- The average level density for A alone (γ = 0) consists of two semicircles each with radius 2λ, measured in units of that radius.
- The coupling strength parameter is defined as α² = γ²/λ², where γ² and λ² are the variances of C and A matrix elements respectively.
- For the N = 1 case, the two eigenvalues are Ω = ±√(a² − |c|²), and coalescence at E = 0 occurs when |a| = |c|.
- For the N = 2 case with eigenvalues E₁, E₂ (0 < E₁ < E₂), the condition for two eigenvalues to coalesce at E = 0 is D_C = T_AC, where D_C = |det C|² and T_AC = E₁²|c₂|² + E₂²|c₁|² + 2E₁E₂|c₁₂|².
- Numerical simulations for r = 4 and 2N = 40 show eigenvalue distributions at α² = 1, 30, 50, and 150, with quartets forming in the complex plane before all eigenvalues reach the imaginary axis at α² = 150.

## Context

This work bridges the gap between purely statistical random-matrix descriptions and highly specific dynamical descriptions of collective states in many-body systems, particularly giant resonances in atomic nuclei. It extends the schematic model of Brown (1960s) and the RPA framework of Rowe and Bohr by introducing a random-matrix version of the RPA equations, in contrast to prior work that treated collective features through separable interactions. The model differs from the non-Hermitian random-matrix model studied by Lueck et al. (2006) by allowing eigenvalues to become imaginary, and it does not belong to the ten canonical Altland-Zirnbauer ensembles classified by Heinzner et al. (2005). The results enable future incorporation of collective features into the random-matrix RPA framework (planned in a sequel paper by Barillier-Pertuisel et al., in preparation) and provide a case study in non-Hermitian random-matrix theory.
