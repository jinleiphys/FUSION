# Entropy and Size in HIC

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates an inverse correlation between residual size and entropy in simulated Heavy Ion Collisions, which explains the experimentally observed inverse correlation between limit temperature and residual size reported by Natowitz et al. (2002). This correlation holds for both classical gas entropy and information entropy definitions, but the result depends on using the participant region temperature defined via Kinetic Gas Theory and fragment identification via an Early Cluster Recognition Algorithm.

## Method

Heavy Ion Collisions were simulated using the LATINO semiclassical model with a Pandharipande potential for binary internucleonic interactions. Fragments were identified using an Early Cluster Recognition Algorithm that optimizes partitions in energy space, and the system temperature was defined by applying Kinetic Gas Theory to nucleons in the participant region. Entropy was computed using two distinct definitions: information entropy and classical gas entropy, while fragment formation time was determined using the Microscopic Persistence Coefficient.

## Key numbers

- Energy conservation in the Verlet integration was maintained within 0.05% during the simulations.
- The limit temperature estimation from D'Agostino et al. (2002) for collisions of 200 particles against Au as target was close to 5.5 MeV.
- Raduta et al. (2000) obtained a limit temperature in the range of 6 to 7 MeV using improved microcanonical caloric capacity computations.

## Context

This work extends the experimental findings of Natowitz et al. (2002), who reported an inverse correlation between limit temperature and residual size, by providing a theoretical explanation through entropy-size correlations in simulated collisions. The study builds on prior work by Barrañón et al. (2003) on phase transitions in finite and transient systems using the LATINO model, and connects to broader discussions about finite-size effects in caloric curves from Pochodzalla et al. (1995), Serfling et al. (1998), and Hauger et al. (1996). The results motivate further studies on how entropy influences the caloric curve limit temperature in Heavy Ion Collisions.
