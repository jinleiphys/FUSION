# The Validity of the Weizsacker-Williams Approximation and the Analysis of Beam Dump Experiments: Production of a New Scalar Boson

> Beam dump experiments have been used to search for new particles with null results interpreted in terms of limits on masses $m_ϕ$ and coupling constants $ε$. However these limits have been obtained by using approximations [including the Weizsäcker-Williams (WW) approximation] or Monte-Carlo simulations. We display methods, using a new scalar boson as an example, to obtain the cross section and the resulting particle production numbers without using approximations or Monte-Carlo simulations. We show that the approximations cannot be used to obtain accurate values of cross sections. The corresponding exclusion plots differ by substantial amounts when seen on a linear scale. In the event of a discovery, we generate pseudodata (assuming given values of $m_ϕ$ and $ε$) in the currently allowed regions of parameter space. The use of approximations to analyze the pseudodata for the future experiments is shown to lead to considerable errors in determining the parameters. Furthermore, a new region of parameter space can be explored without using one of the common approximations, $m_ϕ\gg m_e$. Our method can be used as a consistency check for Monte-Carlo simulations.

# The Validity of the Weizsacker-Williams Approximation and the Analysis of Beam Dump Experiments: Production of a New Scalar Boson

## Key claim
The Weizsacker-Williams (WW) and improved WW (IWW) approximations, commonly used to set exclusion limits in beam dump experiments, produce cross sections with order-of-magnitude accuracy but can have relative errors of 100% for scalar masses below 1 MeV (WW) or above 100 MeV (IWW), and using these approximations to analyze a hypothetical discovery signal leads to fitted mass and coupling values that lie outside the 2-sigma range of the true parameters.

## Method
The authors performed a complete analytic calculation of the 2-to-3 differential cross section for scalar boson production via electron bremsstrahlung on a nuclear target, without using the WW approximation or Monte Carlo simulations. They compared this exact result to the WW and IWW approximations for the E137 beam dump experiment (20 GeV electron beam on an aluminum target), computing cross sections, particle production numbers, exclusion plots, and pseudoexperiment fits for scalar masses from sub-MeV to several hundred MeV.

## Key numbers
- The WW approximation can differ from the complete calculation by 100% when the scalar mass m_phi is less than about 1 MeV
- The IWW approximation starts to fail when m_phi is greater than about 100 MeV
- For E137: beam energy E_0 = 20 GeV, energy cutoff E_cut = 2 GeV, number of incident electrons N_e = 1.87 x 10^20, shield length L_sh = 179 m, decay region length L_dec = 204 m
- The maximum angular acceptance for E137 is theta_max approximately 4.4 x 10^-3 radians
- The WW approximation causes less than 20% error along the lower exclusion boundary, though the cross section error can be 100%
- Pseudoexperiments assumed scalar masses of 110 MeV, 200 MeV, and 0.3 MeV with couplings of 10^-7, 1.3 x 10^-7, and 8 x 10^-6 respectively, with luminosities increased by factors of 36, 36, and 137

## Context
This work extends the analysis of beam dump experiments previously performed by Bjorken et al. (2009) and Tsai (1986), who used the WW and IWW approximations to set limits on new particle production. The authors use a new scalar boson motivated by the proton radius puzzle (Liu et al., 2016) as an example, but expect similar qualitative results for pseudoscalar and vector particles. The complete calculation enables exploration of parameter space where m_phi is not much greater than m_e, a region inaccessible to the common approximation m_phi >> m_e, and can serve as a consistency check for Monte Carlo simulations such as MadGraph/MadEvent used by Essig et al. (2010).
