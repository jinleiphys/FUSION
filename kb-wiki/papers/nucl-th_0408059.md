# Electromagnetic (${\vec e},e'{\vec p}$) Observables & Relativistic Nucleon Dynamics

> At present there exists a great interest in the search for evidence of possible modification of the nucleon form factors inside the nuclear medium. Recent theoretical work predict changes in the form factors within the experimental limits. Importantly, the longitudinal to sideways transferred polarization ratio has been identified as being ideally suited for such studies, as these polarization observables are believed to be the least sensitive to most standard nuclear structure uncertainties while their ratio shows a high sensitivity to the ratio of the electric to magnetic form factors. The kinematic regime where the measurements have been undertaken is at relatively high energy and it is clear that relativistic effects in wave functions and operators are essential. In this work we focus on the analysis of polarized $^{16}O(\vec{e},e'\vec{p})^{15}N$ observables. Our aim is to explore a selected set of model dependences that could contaminate any attempt to infer medium modifications, mainly related to the description of FSI and to the role played by relativity.

## Key claim

The longitudinal to sideways transferred polarization ratio in \(^{16}O(\vec{e},e'\vec{p})^{15}N\) is identified as ideally suited for studying possible modifications of nucleon form factors inside the nuclear medium, but only at low missing momenta (p \(\leq\) 200 MeV/c) where final-state interaction (FSI) effects and relativistic dynamical effects are minimal; at higher missing momenta, model dependences from optical potential choice and current operator choice contaminate any attempt to infer medium modifications.

## Method

The authors performed relativistic distorted wave impulse approximation (RDWIA) calculations for proton knockout from the \(1p_{1/2}\), \(1p_{3/2}\), and \(1s_{1/2}\) shells in \(^{16}O\), using Dirac equation solutions with scalar-vector (S-V) optical potentials for the outgoing nucleon and a relativistic independent particle shell model for the bound state. They computed the longitudinal (\(P'_l\)) and sideways (\(P'_s\)) transferred polarization asymmetries, comparing fully relativistic results with positive-energy projected wave functions and the effective momentum approximation (EMA-noSV) of Kelly (1997), and tested three different relativistic optical potential parameterizations (EDAIO, EDAD1, EDAD2) and two current operator choices (CC1 and CC2).

## Key numbers

* Kinematics: \(q = 1\) GeV/c, \(\omega = 439\) MeV, yielding \(|Q^2| = 0.8\) (GeV/c)\(^2\), corresponding to JLab experiments E89-003 and E89-033.
* For low missing momentum \(p \leq 200\) MeV/c, FSI effects do not substantially modify the polarization asymmetries compared to plane wave impulse approximation (PWIA) results.
* For \(p \geq 200\) MeV/c, FSI strongly modify the polarization behavior, with oscillations in RPWIA results also present in RDWIA but with maxima and minima at different \(p\)-values.
* At low missing momenta up to \(p = 150\) MeV/c, transferred polarization asymmetries are relatively insensitive to the choice of optical potential.
* For \(p \geq 150\) MeV/c, \(P'_l\) exhibits strong dependence on the optical potential, with differences of the same order as those introduced by the current operator choice.

## Context

This work extends the RDWIA framework of Udías et al. (1993) for cross sections and response functions to the analysis of polarization observables in \(^{16}O(\vec{e},e'\vec{p})^{15}N\), building on the identification by Lu et al. (1998) that the longitudinal-to-sideways transferred polarization ratio is sensitive to the electric-to-magnetic form factor ratio. The calculations improve agreement with JLab experimental data from Malov et al. (2000) compared to previous semi-relativistic analyses of Farkas et al. (2003), and the low-missing-momentum region where model dependences are minimal is identified as suitable for searching for medium modifications of nucleon form factors, though caution is noted that meson exchange currents, \(\Delta\)-isobar contributions, and short-range correlations may also play a role at higher momenta.
