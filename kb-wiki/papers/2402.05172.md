# Can quantum statistics help distinguish Dirac from Majorana neutrinos?

> Finding out if neutrinos are Dirac or Majorana particles is known to be extremely difficult due to the smallness of neutrino mass and the fact that in the limit $m_ν=0$ both Dirac and Majorana neutrinos become Weyl particles, i.e. are indistinguishable. There have been suggestions in the literature that in the case of processes with production of a neutrino-antineutrino pair (if neutrinos are Dirac particles) or two neutrinos (if they are of Majorana nature) quantum statistics may be of help. This is because for Majorana neutrinos quantum indistinguishability of identical particles requires the amplitude of the process to be antisymmetrized with respect to the interchange of the final-state neutrinos, whereas no such antisymmetrization must be done for Dirac neutrinos. It has been claimed that the resulting differences between the cross sections for Dirac and Majorana neutrinos persist even for arbitrarily small but not exactly vanishing neutrino mass. We demonstrate that, at least in the framework of the Standard Model, this is not the case. We also give a general proof that within the Standard Model quantum statistics does not help tell Dirac and Majorana neutrinos apart in the limit of negligibly small $m_ν/E$.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that, contrary to earlier claims in the literature, quantum statistics does not help distinguish Dirac from Majorana neutrinos in any Standard Model process, because any observable differences from antisymmetrization of identical Majorana neutrinos are either suppressed by powers of \(m_\nu/E\) or disappear when the neutrinos are detected or their spins are summed over.

## Method

The authors analyze pair-production processes of neutrinos (both neutral-current \(e^+e^- \to Z^* \to \nu\bar{\nu}/\nu\nu\) and charged-current \(B^0 \to \mu^+\mu^-\nu_\mu\bar{\nu}_\mu/\nu_\mu\nu_\mu\)) and show that the claimed non-vanishing differences between Dirac and Majorana cross sections in the limit \(m_\nu \to 0\) arise from either incorrect integration variables or from terms that vanish upon spin summation or detection of the neutrinos. They provide a general proof that within the Standard Model, chirality conservation (approximate for massive neutrinos) plays the role of lepton number conservation, ensuring that all observable effects of antisymmetrization are suppressed by \(m_\nu/E\).

## Key numbers

* For relativistic neutrinos, the difference between chirality and helicity is of order \(m_\nu/(2E)\), leading to suppression factors of \((m_\nu/(2E))^2 \lesssim 10^{-14}\) for "wrong-helicity" processes in the Majorana case.
* In the neutral-current process \(e^+e^- \to Z^* \to \nu\nu\), the differential cross section for Majorana neutrinos contains a term proportional to \(n_x n_x' - n_y n_y'\) (transverse spin components) that is not suppressed by \(m_\nu/E\), but this term vanishes upon summation over even one of the neutrino spins.
* For the charged-current decays \(B^0 \to \mu^+\mu^-\nu_\mu\bar{\nu}_\mu\) and \(B^0 \to \mu^+\mu^-\nu_\mu\nu_\mu\), the authors show that after correcting the angular integration variable from \(\mathrm{d}\!\sin\theta\) to \(\mathrm{d}\!\cos\theta\), the differential decay rates for Dirac and Majorana neutrinos become identical in the limit of negligibly small \(m_\nu\).

## Context

This work extends the Practical Dirac-Majorana Confusion Theorem (Kayser 1981, 1982) by addressing claimed exceptions based on quantum statistics. It corrects erroneous conclusions in Ma (1989), Kogo (1991), Hofer (1996), and Kim (2021, 2023), who argued that differences between Dirac and Majorana neutrino cross sections in pair-production processes survive for arbitrarily small but non-zero neutrino mass. The authors show that these claims are either technically incorrect or that the problematic terms vanish when observable quantities are properly computed. This result enables future searches for the Dirac/Majorana nature of neutrinos to focus on processes that genuinely violate lepton number (such as neutrinoless double beta decay) rather than on quantum statistics effects.
