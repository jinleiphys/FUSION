# Few-body quantum method in a $d$-dimensional space

> In this work we investigate the continuous confinement of quantum systems from three to two dimensions. Two different methods will be used and related. In the first one the confinement is achieved by putting the system under the effect of an external field. This method is conceptually simple, although, due to the presence of the external field, its numerical implementation can become rather cumbersome, especially when the system is highly confined. In the second method the external field is not used, and it simply considers the spatial dimension $d$ as a parameter that changes continuously between the ordinary integer values. In this way the numerical effort is absorbed in a modified strength of the centrifugal barrier. Then the technique required to obtain the wave function of the confined system is precisely the same as needed in ordinary three dimensional calculations without any confinement potential. The case of a two-body system squeezed from three to two dimensions is considered, and used to provide a translation between all the quantities in the two methods. Finally we point out perspectives for applications on more particles, different spatial dimensions, and other confinement potentials.

# Few-body quantum method in a $d$-dimensional space

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that continuous confinement of a two-body quantum system from three to two dimensions can be equivalently described either by an external harmonic oscillator squeezing field or by treating the spatial dimension $d$ as a continuous parameter between 2 and 3, with a universal, model-independent translation between the two methods. This equivalence holds for relative $s$-waves and large scattering lengths, where the short-range potential details become unimportant.

## Method

The authors solve the two-body Schrödinger equation using two approaches: (1) an external harmonic oscillator potential in the $z$-direction with oscillator length $b_{ext}$, requiring a partial-wave expansion with many coupled channels for strong confinement; and (2) a $d$-dimensional formulation where the centrifugal barrier term $(d-3)(d-1)/(4r^2)$ replaces the external field, allowing a single differential equation in the radial coordinate $r$ for any non-integer $d$ between 2 and 3. They compute energies and wave functions for Gaussian and Morse potentials with three different scattering lengths, then establish the mapping between $b_{ext}$ and $d$ by requiring identical energies, and validate the wave function equivalence through overlap integrals and a scaling transformation of the radial coordinate.

## Key numbers

- Three Gaussian potentials with 3D scattering lengths $a_{3D} = 2.033$, 18.122, and 40.608 (in units of potential range $b$), with corresponding 3D binding energies $E_{3D} = -0.269$, $-1.651\times10^{-3}$, and $-3.144\times10^{-4}$ (in units of $\hbar^2/\mu b^2$)
- Three Morse potentials with 3D scattering lengths $a_{3D} = 2.033$, 18.122, and 40.608, with 3D binding energies $E_{3D} = -0.189$, $-1.875\times10^{-3}$, and $-3.325\times10^{-4}$ (in units of $\hbar^2/\mu b^2$)
- 2D scattering length at infinite 3D scattering length: $a_{2D}^\infty = 1.99$ for Gaussian and 4.15 for Morse (in their respective length units)
- Fitting constants for the universal $b_{ext}$-to-$d$ relation: $(c_1, c_2, c_3) = (-0.207, 0.535, 0.592)$
- Fitting constants for the scaling parameter $s$: $(c_1, c_2, c_3) = (2.75, 2.92, 0.64)$
- Overlap between $d$-method and external-field wave functions exceeds 0.977 for all tested potentials, even at extreme squeezing $b_{ext}^b = 0.1$

## Context

This work extends the $d$-dimensional formulation developed by Nielsen et al. (2001) for few-body systems, which treated non-integer dimensions as physically meaningful for describing confinement, in contrast to earlier $1/d$-expansion methods (Doren and Herschbach, 1986; Herschbach, 1992; Dunn, 1993) that used integer dimensions only as expansion points. The paper provides the first explicit translation between the efficient $d$-method and experimentally realizable external-field squeezing, which is relevant to cold atom experiments (Klaers et al., 2017; Naidon and Endo, 2017; Levinsen et al., 2014) where strong external fields effectively confine systems to lower dimensions. The results enable future applications to three-body systems, where the $d$-method is already mathematically available but the external-field brute-force approach becomes computationally prohibitive.
