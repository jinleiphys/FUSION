# Neutron-Antineutron Oscillations: Theoretical Status and Experimental Prospects

> This paper summarizes the relevant theoretical developments, outlines some ideas to improve experimental searches for free neutron-antineutron oscillations, and suggests avenues for future improvement in the experimental sensitivity.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a next-generation free neutron-antineutron oscillation search, using modern slow neutron sources and optics technology developed for materials research, could improve existing limits on the free oscillation probability by at least three orders of magnitude, provided a dedicated beamline optimized for slow neutron production and delivery is constructed.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework for neutron-antineutron oscillations, which require baryon number violation by two units, and survey the experimental approaches: free neutron beam experiments (where neutrons propagate through a magnetically-shielded vacuum chamber to an annihilation target) and intranuclear searches (where neutrons bound in nuclei oscillate and annihilate, detected in large underground detectors). They analyze the figure of merit for free neutron searches as N_n t^2, where N_n is the total number of free neutrons observed and t is the observation time, and discuss how modern cold neutron sources, supermirror optics, and magnetic shielding can maximize this quantity.

## Key numbers

- The present baryon-to-photon ratio of the universe is measured as eta = (6.19 +/- 0.14) x 10^{-10}, determined independently from Big Bang Nucleosynthesis and cosmic microwave background measurements.
- The baryon asymmetry parameter eta lies in the range 5.7 x 10^{-10} <= eta <= 6.7 x 10^{-10} at 95% confidence level.
- Typical lower limits for proton lifetimes across various decay modes exceed 10^{32} to 10^{34} years.
- The electroweak phase transition temperature is expected at T approximately 170 GeV.
- Slow neutrons used in such experiments have kinetic energies of a few meV.

## Context

This paper extends the theoretical and experimental discussion of neutron-antineutron oscillations as a probe of baryon number violation, building on earlier work by Stueckelberg (1938) on baryon number conservation and by Sakharov (1967) on the conditions for baryogenesis. It connects to searches for proton decay in grand unified theories (Georgi, 1974) and to the seesaw mechanism for neutrino masses, noting that discovery of neutrinoless double beta decay would strongly suggest neutron-antineutron oscillations must also exist. The review enables future experimental programs by identifying that no new technology is required, only detailed research and development on optimizing cold neutron sources, supermirror optics, vacuum systems, magnetic shielding, and annihilation detectors.
