# The ${}^9$Be photodisintegration cross section within Cluster Effective Field Theory

> A low-energy calculation of ${}^9$Be photodisintegration cross section is presented within an $ααn$ cluster approach. The $αn$ and $αα$ contact interactions are derived from cluster effective field theory. The two-body potentials defined in momentum space are regularized by a Gaussian cutoff. The associated low-energy constants are found by comparing the calculated low-energy T-matrix with its effective range expansion. A three-body state-dependent potential is also introduced in the model. First, the ${}^9$Be three-body binding energy is studied within the non-symmetrized hyperspherical harmonics method. Then, the low-energy cross section is calculated via the Lorentz integral transform method, focussing on the dominant electric dipole transitions. A twofold evaluation of the nuclear current matrix element is presented, employing both the electric dipole transition operator (Siegert theorem) and the one-body convection current operator. This approach is adopted to allow for a discussion of the effect of the many-body currents.

## Key claim

The paper presents a low-energy calculation of the ${}^9$Be photodisintegration cross section using an $\alpha\alpha n$ cluster approach with interactions derived from Cluster Effective Field Theory, but the results depend on the introduction of a three-body force to achieve cutoff independence, and the calculation focuses only on the dominant electric dipole transitions rather than the full cross section.

## Method

The authors construct two-body $\alpha n$ and $\alpha\alpha$ potentials in momentum space from Cluster EFT contact interactions, regularized by Gaussian cutoffs, with low-energy constants fixed by matching to effective range expansion parameters from experimental scattering data. The three-body bound state of ${}^9$Be is solved using the non-symmetrized hyperspherical harmonics method in momentum space, and the photodisintegration cross section is calculated via the Lorentz integral transform method, which avoids explicit continuum wave functions by solving a bound-state-like inhomogeneous equation. The nuclear current matrix element is evaluated in two ways: using the electric dipole transition operator (Siegert theorem) and using the one-body convection current operator, allowing comparison of the effects of many-body currents.

## Key numbers

* The three-body binding energy of ${}^9$Be relative to the $\alpha\alpha n$ threshold is $B_3 = 1.57$ MeV
* The $\alpha n$ $P_{3/2}$ scattering volume is $a^{\text{exp}}_1 = -62.951$ fm$^3$ and effective range is $r^{\text{exp}}_1 = -0.8819$ fm$^{-1}$
* The $\alpha n$ $S_{1/2}$ scattering length is $a^{\text{exp}}_0 = 2.4641$ fm and effective range is $r^{\text{exp}}_0 = 1.385$ fm
* The $\alpha\alpha$ $S_0$ scattering length is $a^{\text{exp}}_0 = -1920$ fm and effective range is $r^{\text{exp}}_0 = 1.099$ fm
* The low-momentum scale for the $\alpha n$ system is $M_{lo} \approx 50$ MeV and the high-momentum scale is $M_{hi} \approx 170$ MeV, giving an expansion parameter $M_{lo,\alpha n} / M_{hi,\alpha n} \approx 0.3$
* The low-momentum scale for the $\alpha\alpha$ system is $M_{lo} \approx 20$ MeV and the high-momentum scale is $M_{hi} \approx 170$ MeV, giving an expansion parameter $M_{lo,\alpha\alpha} / M_{hi,\alpha\alpha} \approx 0.1$
* The breakdown scale of the $\alpha\alpha n$ system is $M_{hi} = 170$ MeV
* Wigner bound constraints on the momentum cutoff: $\Lambda^{S}_{\alpha n} < 843$ MeV, $\Lambda^{P}_{\alpha n} < 340$ MeV, and $\Lambda^{S}_{\alpha\alpha} < 230$ MeV

## Context

This work extends previous cluster model calculations of ${}^9$Be by Efros et al., Casal et al., and Japanese groups, which used phenomenological potentials, by instead employing interactions derived from Halo/Cluster EFT, providing a more theoretically grounded framework. The inverse reaction, $\alpha\alpha n \rightarrow {}^9$Be + $\gamma$, is of astrophysical relevance for bridging the mass gaps at $A=5$ and $A=8$ in nucleosynthesis scenarios from neutron star mergers or supernova explosions, as discussed by Oberhummer (2001), Sumiyoshi (2002), and Sasaqui (2005). The calculation enables comparison with experimental data from Utsunomiya et al., Arnold et al., Burda et al., and Goryachev et al., which show discrepancies in the low-energy resonance peaks, and provides a framework for quantifying two-body current effects through comparison of Siegert theorem and one-body convection current results.
