# Neural network study on nuclear ground-state spin distribution within random interaction ensemble

> The distribution of nuclear ground-state spin in the two-body random ensemble (TBRE) is studied by using a general classification neural network (NN) model with the two-body interaction matrix elements as input features and corresponding ground-state spins as labels or output predictions. It seems that quantum many-body system problem exceeds the capability of our optimized neural networks when it comes to accurately predicting the ground-state spin of each sample within the TBRE. However, our neural network model effectively captures the statistical properties of the ground-state spin. This may be attributed to the fact that the neural network (NN) model has learned the empirical regularity of the ground-state spin distribution in TBRE, as discovered by human physicists.

## Key claim

The neural network model cannot accurately predict the ground-state spin of individual samples within the two-body random ensemble (TBRE) for complex quantum many-body systems, but it successfully captures the statistical distribution of ground-state spins across the ensemble, having learned the empirical regularity previously discovered by human physicists.

## Method

The authors performed approximately 100,000 TBRE shell-model calculations in six model spaces: (f7/2)^4, (h11/2)^4, 18Ne, 20Ne, 22Ne, and 46Ca. They trained a fully connected classification neural network using the two-body interaction matrix elements as input features and the corresponding ground-state spins from shell-model calculations as labels, with a single hidden layer of 32 ReLU nodes for the four multi-shell spaces and no hidden layer for the (f7/2)^4 space. The network was optimized using the Adam algorithm with a cross-entropy loss function and a Softmax output layer to produce probabilities for each possible ground-state spin.

## Key numbers

- (f7/2)^4 space: prediction accuracy of 98% with no hidden layer, dimension 8
- (h11/2)^4 space: prediction accuracy of 97% with one hidden layer of 64 nodes, dimension 23
- 18Ne space: prediction accuracy of 86% with one hidden layer of 32 ReLU nodes, dimension 14
- 20Ne space: prediction accuracy of 68% with one hidden layer of 32 ReLU nodes, dimension 81
- 22Ne space: prediction accuracy of 80% with one hidden layer of 32 ReLU nodes, dimension 142
- 46Ca space: prediction accuracy of 56% with one hidden layer of 32 ReLU nodes, dimension 3952
- Pearson correlation coefficient of -0.753 between prediction accuracy and the logarithm of model space dimension
- For the (f7/2)^4 space, setting G^0 = -1 and all other G^J = 0 yields ground-state spin 0 from both shell model and neural network; setting G^2 = -1 yields spin 4 from both

## Context

This work extends the empirical approach of Zhao et al. (2002) which proposed that ground-state spin probabilities in TBRE correlate with specific two-body interaction matrix elements, by using a neural network to learn these correlations directly from data. The study builds on the discovery by Johnson, Bertsch, et al. of the "predominance of spin-zero ground state" in even-even nuclei within TBRE, and on earlier attempts to understand this phenomenon through methods such as random polynomial theory (Kusnezov et al.), mean-field methods (Bijker et al.), and geometric chaos analysis. The results show that while the neural network cannot match the shell model's accuracy for individual predictions in high-dimensional spaces, it reproduces the statistical spin distribution, suggesting that machine learning can serve as a surrogate for understanding robust statistical properties of quantum many-body systems where analytical approaches are intractable.
