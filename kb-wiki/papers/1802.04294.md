# Discriminating WIMP-nucleus response functions in present and future XENON-like direct detection experiments

> The standard interpretation of direct-detection limits on dark matter involves particular assumptions of the underlying WIMP-nucleus interaction, such as, in the simplest case, the choice of a Helm form factor that phenomenologically describes an isoscalar spin-independent interaction. In general, the interaction of dark matter with the target nuclei may well proceed via different mechanisms, which would lead to a different shape of the corresponding nuclear structure factors as a function of the momentum transfer $q$. We study to what extent different WIMP-nucleus responses can be differentiated based on the $q$-dependence of their structure factors (or "form factors"). We assume an overall strength of the interaction consistent with present spin-independent limits and consider an exposure corresponding to XENON1T-like, XENONnT-like, and DARWIN-like direct detection experiments. We find that, as long as the interaction strength does not lie too much below current limits, the DARWIN settings allow a conclusive discrimination of many different response functions based on their $q$-dependence, with immediate consequences for elucidating the nature of dark matter.

## Key claim

The paper finds that, provided the WIMP-nucleon cross section is not too far below current experimental limits, a DARWIN-like experiment can conclusively discriminate many non-standard WIMP-nucleus response functions from the standard isoscalar spin-independent Helm form factor based on their different momentum-transfer dependence, but this discrimination is not possible for a WIMP mass of 10 GeV due to the narrow range of accessible momentum transfers.

## Method

The authors used nuclear structure factors for xenon isotopes derived from large-scale shell-model calculations within chiral effective field theory, considering eight different response functions including isoscalar and isovector M responses, pion-mediated two-body currents, and Phi'' responses. They performed toy-Monte Carlo simulations based on XENON100 detector parameters, scaling the active mass and background rates to match XENON1T-like, XENONnT-like, and DARWIN-like experiments, and applied an unbinned likelihood-ratio test to quantify the discrimination power between each non-standard structure factor and the standard Helm form factor.

## Key numbers

- For XENON1T-like settings after 10 ton years exposure with m_chi = 100 GeV and sigma_0 = 10^-46 cm^2: discrimination power ranges from 17% for |F_-^M|^2 to 100% for q^4/m_N^4 |F_+^M|^2, q^4/m_N^4 |F_-^M|^2, q^4/4m_N^4 |F_+^{Phi''}|^2, and q^4/4m_N^4 |F_-^{Phi''}|^2
- For XENONnT-like settings after 30 ton years exposure with m_chi = 100 GeV and sigma_0 = 10^-47 cm^2: discrimination power ranges from 13% for |F_-^M|^2 to 93% for q^4/4m_N^4 |F_+^{Phi''}|^2
- For DARWIN-like settings after 200 ton years exposure with m_chi = 100 GeV and sigma_0 = 10^-47 cm^2: discrimination power ranges from 26% for |F_-^M|^2 to 100% for q^2/4m_chi^2 |F_+^M|^2, q^4/m_N^4 |F_+^M|^2, q^4/m_N^4 |F_-^M|^2, q^4/4m_N^4 |F_+^{Phi''}|^2, and q^4/4m_N^4 |F_-^{Phi''}|^2
- For DARWIN-like settings with sigma_0 = 10^-48 cm^2 and m_chi = 100 GeV: discrimination power reaches at most 58% for q^4/4m_N^4 |F_+^{Phi''}|^2
- No discriminating power was obtained for any structure factor at m_chi = 10 GeV, even for DARWIN-like settings
- The background rate for XENON1T was taken as 0.036 times the XENON100 rate, for XENONnT as 0.0036 times, and for DARWIN as 0.0011 times

## Context

This work extends the chiral effective field theory framework for WIMP-nucleus responses developed by Hoferichter et al. (2016) by quantifying the experimental discrimination power between different response functions, building on earlier studies of NREFT operator discrimination by Rogers et al. (2016). The results enable future dark matter direct detection experiments to plan strategies for identifying the nature of a potential WIMP signal, showing that DARWIN can distinguish most non-standard responses from the standard Helm form factor if the cross section is within reach, while also demonstrating that isoscalar and isovector M responses are difficult to separate without multiple target nuclei.
