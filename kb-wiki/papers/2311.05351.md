# The $GW$ Approximation: A Quantum Chemistry Perspective

> We provide an in-depth examination of the $GW$ approximation of Green's function many-body perturbation theory by detailing both its theoretical and practical aspects in the realm of quantum chemistry. First, the quasiparticle context is introduced before delving into the derivation of Hedin's equations. From these, we explain how to derive the well-known $GW$ approximation of the self-energy. In a second time, we meticulously explain each step involved in a $GW$ calculation and what type of physical quantities can be computed. To illustrate its versatility, we consider two contrasting systems: the water molecule, a weakly correlated system, and the carbon dimer, a strongly correlated system. Each stage of the process is thoroughly detailed and explained alongside numerical results and illustrative plots. We hope that the contribution will facilitate the dissemination and democratization of Green's function-based formalisms within the computational and theoretical quantum chemistry community.

## Key claim

The paper provides a pedagogical derivation and practical guide to the $GW$ approximation of Green's function many-body perturbation theory specifically for quantum chemistry applications, emphasizing that while the method is well-established in condensed matter physics, its implementation in molecular systems requires careful attention to basis sets, self-consistency levels, and the treatment of both weakly and strongly correlated systems.

## Method

The authors derive Hedin's equations starting from the equation of motion of the one-body Green's function, then obtain the $GW$ approximation by neglecting vertex corrections (setting the irreducible vertex function $\tilde{\Gamma}$ to its zeroth-order term $\delta(12')\delta(1'2)$). They compute the irreducible polarizability at the random-phase approximation (RPA) level by solving Casida-like eigenvalue equations, construct the dynamically screened Coulomb interaction $W$ from RPA eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and evaluate the correlation part of the $GW$ self-energy via convolution of the Green's function with the screened interaction. The quasiparticle equation is solved at various self-consistency levels: one-shot $G_0W_0$, linearized $G_0W_0$, eigenvalue self-consistent ev$GW$, and quasiparticle self-consistent qs$GW$, with all calculations initiated from Hartree-Fock quantities using the aug-cc-pVTZ basis set.

## Key numbers

- The RPA eigenvalue problem is of size $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ where $N$ is the number of orbitals, making the diagonalization step cost $\mathcal{O}(N^6)$ and the computational bottleneck of a $GW$ calculation
- A broadening parameter of 1 eV was applied to the Gaussian convolution for the absorption spectrum plots
- The renormalization factor $Z_p$ is strictly restricted between 0 and 1, with its inverse given by $Z_p^{-1} = 1 - \partial\Sigma^c_{pp}(\omega)/\partial\omega$ evaluated at $\omega = \epsilon_p^{\text{HF}}$
- The Galitskii-Migdal correlation energy expression is given as $E_c^{\text{GM}} = -\sum_{iam} M_{ia,m}^2 / (\epsilon_a - \epsilon_i + \Omega_m)$

## Context

This work extends the pedagogical tradition of $GW$ reviews such as Golze (2019) and the book of Martin, Reining, and Ceperley, but provides a quantum-chemistry-specific perspective with explicit molecular examples (water and carbon dimer) rather than solid-state systems. The paper enables practitioners to implement $GW$ methods within existing quantum chemistry software by detailing each computational step, including the RPA eigenvalue problem, screened integral construction, and self-consistency procedures, while noting that the contour deformation approach and other techniques (van Setten 2013, Neuhauser 2013, Govoni 2015) have been designed to overcome the $\mathcal{O}(N^6)$ computational bottleneck.
