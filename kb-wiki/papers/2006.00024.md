# Isotopically resolved neutron total cross sections at intermediate energies

> The neutron total cross sections $σ_{tot}$ of $^{16,18}$O, $^{58,64}$Ni, $^{103}$Rh, and $^{112,124}$Sn have been measured at the Los Alamos Neutron Science Center (LANSCE) from low to intermediate energies (3 $\leq E_{lab} \leq$ 450 MeV) by leveraging waveform-digitizer technology. The $σ_{tot}$ relative differences between isotopes are presented, revealing additional information about the isovector components needed for an accurate optical-model description away from stability. Digitizer-enabled $σ_{tot}$-measurement techniques are discussed and a series of uncertainty-quantified dispersive optical model (DOM) analyses using these new data is presented, validating the use of the DOM for modeling light systems ($^{16,18}$O) and systems with open neutron shells ($^{58,64}$Ni and $^{112,124}$Sn). The valence-nucleon spectroscopic factors extracted for each isotope reaffirm the usefulness of high-energy proton reaction cross sections for characterizing depletion from the mean-field expectation.

## Key claim

The paper presents new isotopically resolved neutron total cross section measurements for 16,18O, 58,64Ni, 103Rh, and 112,124Sn from 3 to 450 MeV, but the abstract's claim of "validating" the dispersive optical model (DOM) for light systems and open-shell nuclei is qualified by the fact that the DOM analysis yields uncertainty-quantified parameters with substantial ranges, and the validation applies specifically to the ability of the DOM to reproduce these new data when combined with existing scattering and bound-state data.

## Method

Neutron total cross sections were measured at the Los Alamos Neutron Science Center (LANSCE) Weapons Neutron Research facility using waveform-digitizer technology with a time-of-flight method over a 25-meter flight path, employing both DPP (peak-sensing) and waveform digitizer modes to reduce per-event deadtime to 150-230 ns. Samples were cycled into the beam using a programmable six-position sample changer with 150-second intervals per sample, and cross sections were extracted from the flux attenuation through each sample after correcting for deadtime using a logistic fit to the time-difference spectrum between consecutive events. For oxygen isotopes, water samples (H2O, D2O, H218O) were used and the hydrogen contribution was subtracted using literature neutron-proton cross sections from Clement et al. (1972) and Abfalterer et al. (2001).

## Key numbers

- Neutron total cross sections measured over energy range 3 ≤ Elab ≤ 450 MeV
- Sample areal densities: 58Ni 0.1197(3) mol/cm2, 64Ni 0.1192(6) mol/cm2, 112Sn 0.08332(5) mol/cm2, 124Sn 0.08399(5) mol/cm2
- 103Rh sample areal density: 0.02426(4) mol/cm2 (only 2.8359 g of material)
- Oxygen samples (as water): areal density 0.1107(3) mol/cm2
- Final corrected TOF resolution (FWHM of gamma-ray peak): 0.60-0.90 ns
- For a 100-MeV neutron at 25 meters: energy resolution approximately 900 keV
- For a 5-MeV neutron: energy uncertainty 13 keV
- Mean deadtime: 228.1 ns for Sn and O configurations, 159.7 ns for Ni and Rh configurations
- Flight path distances: 2709 ±1 cm (Ni and Rh configuration), 2554 ±1 cm (Sn and O configuration)
- Sample masses: 58Ni 3.6438 g, 64Ni 3.9942 g, 112Sn 4.9720 g, 124Sn 5.5492 g, 103Rh 2.8359 g

## Context

This work significantly expands on the authors' earlier digitizer-enabled total cross section measurement on 40,48Ca (Shane, 2010) by providing data for additional closed-shell isotopes (16,18O, 58,64Ni, 112,124Sn) and demonstrating feasibility for small samples with the 103Rh measurement. The data address the longstanding dearth of isotopically resolved neutron total cross sections identified by Dietrich et al. (2003), who found that standard isovector terms in optical potentials worsened agreement with experimental relative differences for tungsten isotopes. The results enable improved dispersive optical model analyses to extract neutron skin thicknesses and spectroscopic factors, with companion DOM results presented in Pruitt (2020) in Physical Review Letters.
