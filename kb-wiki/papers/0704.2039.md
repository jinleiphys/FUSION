# Relativistic hydrodynamics - causality and stability

> Causality and stability in relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics are important conceptual issues. We argue that causality is not restricted to hyperbolic set of differential equations. E.g. heat conduction equation can be causal considering the physical validity of the theory. Furthermore we propose a new concept of relativistic internal energy that clearly separates the dissipative and non-dissipative effects. We prove that with this choice we remove all known instabilities of the linear response approximation of viscous and heat conducting relativistic fluids. In this paper the Eckart choice of the velocity field is applied.

# Relativistic hydrodynamics - causality and stability

## Key claim

The authors argue that first-order relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics (such as the Eckart theory) cannot be excluded by causality arguments alone, because parabolic equations can have finite signal propagation speeds when physical validity limits (mean free path, observability thresholds) are considered. They further claim that a redefinition of relativistic internal energy as the absolute value of the energy flux four-vector removes all known instabilities of the linear response approximation for viscous and heat-conducting relativistic fluids, without requiring the additional parameters of second-order theories.

## Method

The authors first demonstrate, using the Fourier heat conduction equation as an example, that the mean free path of the medium imposes a finite upper bound on signal propagation speed even for parabolic equations, and that observability thresholds similarly restrict propagation. They then propose a new definition of relativistic internal energy as \(\|E\| = \sqrt{e^2 - q_\alpha q^\alpha}\) (the absolute value of the energy flux four-vector), which separates dissipative and non-dissipative effects. Using this definition, they derive modified constitutive relations (Fourier law and Newtonian viscous stress) from the entropy production inequality via linear irreversible thermodynamics, and perform a linear stability analysis of the homogeneous equilibrium for a viscous, heat-conducting relativistic fluid in the Eckart frame.

## Key numbers

- For heat conduction in water at room temperature, the maximum signal propagation speed is estimated as \(v_{max} \sim \frac{\Lambda}{c_v \rho \xi} \simeq 14\) m/s, where \(\Lambda\) is the Fourier heat conduction coefficient, \(\rho\) is density, \(c_v\) is specific heat, and \(\xi\) is the mean free path.
- The linearized perturbation equations yield a 14x14 matrix with block-diagonal form; the dispersion relations from the submatrices **R** and **N** are analyzed using the Routh-Hurwitz criteria for a fourth-order polynomial.
- The Routh-Hurwitz conditions are satisfied given the thermodynamic stability conditions \(\partial_e T > 0\), \(\partial_n (\mu/T) > 0\), and \(\partial_e T \, \partial_n (\mu/T) - \partial_n T \, \partial_e (\mu/T) \geq 0\), without requiring additional stability conditions beyond standard thermodynamic inequalities.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Hiscock and Lindblom (1985, 1987), who demonstrated that first-order Eckart fluids are unstable in linear response, and that Israel-Stewart second-order theories require additional stability conditions. The authors also reference the observation by Geroch and Lindblom (1995, 1996) that physical fluid states in second-order theories relax to solutions of the underlying first-order theory, motivating the search for a viable first-order formulation. The proposed internal energy definition builds on a suggestion by García-Colín and Sandoval-Villalbazo (2006) for separating internal energy from total energy, but adds a stability analysis. The work enables a first-order relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics that is linearly stable without the extra parameters of second-order theories, potentially applicable to heavy-ion collision modeling.
