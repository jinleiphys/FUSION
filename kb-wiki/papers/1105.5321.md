# A Comparison between a Minijet Model and a Glasma Flux Tube Model for Central Au-Au Collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$=200 GeV

> In is paper we compare two models with central Au-Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$=200 GeV. The first model is a minijet model which assumes that around $\sim$50 minijets are produced in back-to-back pairs and have an altered fragmentation functions. It is also assumed that the fragments are transparent and escape the collision zone and are detected. The second model is a glasma flux tube model which leads to flux tubes on the surface of a radial expanding fireball driven by interacting flux tubes near the center of the fireball through plasma instabilities. This internal fireball becomes an opaque hydro fluid which pushes the surface flux tubes outward. Around $\sim$12 surface flux tubes remain and fragment with $\sim$1/2 the produced particles escaping the collision zone and are detected. Both models can reproduce two particle angular correlations in the different $p_{t1}$ $p_{t2}$ bins. We also compare the two models for three additional effects: meson baryon ratios; the long range nearside correlation called the ridge; and the so-called mach cone effect when applied to three particle angular correlations.

## Key claim

Both a minijet model (with ~50 back-to-back minijets) and a glasma flux tube model (GFTM, with ~12 surface flux tubes) can reproduce the two-particle angular correlations observed in central Au-Au collisions at √sNN=200 GeV, but they differ in their predictions for meson-baryon ratios, the ridge effect, and the mach cone effect in three-particle correlations.

## Method

The minijet model assumes ~50 minijets produced in back-to-back pairs with altered fragmentation functions, where fragments are transparent and escape the collision zone. The GFTM assumes flux tubes on the surface of a radial expanding fireball driven by interacting flux tubes near the center through plasma instabilities, with ~12 surface flux tubes that fragment and emit ~1/2 of produced particles escaping the collision zone. Both models were used to generate two-particle charge-independent (CI) correlation functions C(Δφ, Δη) in seven pt bins ranging from 0.2 to 4.0 GeV/c, and were compared for meson-baryon ratios, the ridge correlation with a jet trigger, and three-particle angular correlations.

## Key numbers

- ~50 minijets per central Au-Au collision, each showering into 13 charged particles on average, with 837 soft uncorrelated particles (57% of charged particles)
- ~12 final state tubes on the fireball surface per central Au-Au collision, each showering into 49 charged particles on average, with 873 soft uncorrelated particles (60% of charged particles)
- In the GFTM, approximately one half of particles emitted from tubes escape; 20% of charged particles come from tube emission, adding 294 particles to the soft component (total 1167)
- For the highest pt bin (4.0-1.1 GeV/c), 100% of tube particles survive; for the lowest pt bin (0.3-0.2 GeV/c), only 25% survive
- In the minijet model, at yt=2 (pt=0.5 GeV/c) for central Au-Au (0-12%), there are 5 times as many pions from minijet fragmentation as in N-N collisions
- At yt=5 (pt=10 GeV/c), rAA=0.2 for both models, consistent with jet quenching measurements
- The GFTM ridge yield is 35% larger than estimated in the STAR experiment measurement (Putschke, 2006)
- For the PHOBOS comparison, the GFTM generated correlations with trigger pt>2.5 GeV/c and associated particle pt>7 MeV/c over |η|<3.0

## Context

This work extends the minijet model of Trainor (2008) and the glasma flux tube model of Dumitru (2008) to compare their predictions for central Au-Au collisions at RHIC energies. The GFTM builds on the color glass condensate framework (CGC, 2001) and the Parton Bubble Model (PBM, 2009), which was shown to be a good approximation to GFTM surface flux tube formation. The paper enables future work to distinguish between these two competing explanations for the ridge effect, baryon enhancement, and conical emission patterns, and suggests that the mach cone effect and the ridge may be the same phenomenon originating from flux tube dynamics rather than from energy loss in a medium.
