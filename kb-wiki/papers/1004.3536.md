# Shell-correction and orbital-free density-functional methods for finite systems

> Orbital-free (OF) methods promise significant speed-up of computations based on density functional theory (DFT). In this field, the development of accurate kinetic-energy density functionals remains an open question. In this chapter we review the shell-correction method (SCM, commonly known as Strutinsky's averaging method) applied originally in nuclear physics and its more recent formulation in the context of DFT [Yannouleas and Landman, Phys. Rev. B 48, 8376 (1993)]. We demonstrate the DFT-SCM method through its earlier applications to condensed-matter finite systems, including metal clusters, fullerenes, and metal nanowires. The DFT-SCM incorporates quantum mechanical interference effects and thus offers an improvement compared to the use of Thomas-Fermi-type kinetic energy density functionals in OF-DFT.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the shell-correction method (SCM), originally developed in nuclear physics by Strutinsky, can be reformulated within density functional theory (DFT) to provide an orbital-free approach that incorporates quantum mechanical interference effects, offering an improvement over Thomas-Fermi-type kinetic energy density functionals. The authors show that the DFT-SCM yields total energies and ionization potentials for metal clusters that agree with full Kohn-Sham calculations to within 1-2%, but the method relies on an optimized input density from an extended-Thomas-Fermi functional, and the accuracy depends on this optimization.

## Method

The authors derive a microscopic DFT-SCM by identifying the total energy of a finite system with the Harris functional, using an input density obtained from minimization of an extended-Thomas-Fermi (ETF) kinetic energy functional expanded to fourth-order in density gradients. The shell correction is expressed as the difference between the kinetic energy from the single-particle eigenvalues of the ETF-derived potential and the ETF kinetic energy itself, avoiding the traditional Strutinsky averaging procedure. For jellium calculations, a parametrized trial density profile with variational parameters is used in the ETF-DFT minimization, and the resulting smooth density and energy are combined with the Harris functional to produce the shell-corrected total energy.

## Context

This work extends the shell-correction method of Strutinsky (1960s) from nuclear physics to condensed-matter finite systems, building on earlier ETF calculations for metal clusters by Brack (1984), Serra (1989), and others that did not include shell corrections. The DFT-SCM approach was first formulated by Yannouleas and Landman (1993) and is demonstrated here for metal clusters, fullerenes, and metal nanowires. The method enables efficient calculation of electronic properties for systems where Kohn-Sham DFT may face convergence difficulties, such as multiply charged clusters, and provides a framework for studying size-evolutionary patterns in finite systems including stability borders analogous to nuclear drip lines.
