# Hydrodynamic flow in heavy-ion collisions with large hadronic viscosity

> Using the (2+1)-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic code VISH2+1 with a temperature dependent specific shear viscosity (eta/s)(T), we present a detailed study of the influence of a large hadronic shear viscosity and its corresponding relaxation time tau_pi on the transverse momentum spectra and elliptic flow of hadrons produced in 200 A GeV Au+Au collisions. Although theory, in principle, predicts a well-defined relation T*tau_pi = kappa(T)*(eta/s)(T), the precise form of kappa(T) for the matter created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is not known. For the popular choice kappa=3 the hadron spectra are found to be insensitive to a significant rise of eta/s in the hadronic stage, whereas their differential elliptic flow v_2(p_T) is strongly suppressed by large hadronic viscosity. The large viscous effects on v_2 are strongly reduced if (as theoretically expected) kappa(T) is allowed to grow with decreasing temperature in the hadronic stage. This implies that, until reliable calculations of kappa(T) become available, an extraction of the hadronic shear viscosity from a comparison between VISH2+1 and a microscopic hadron cascade or experimental data requires a simultaneous fit of (eta/s)(T) and kappa(T).

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the strong suppression of elliptic flow \(v_2(p_T)\) by large hadronic shear viscosity is critically dependent on the choice of the proportionality constant \(\kappa(T)\) in the relation \(\tau_\pi T = \kappa(T) (\eta/s)(T)\); if \(\kappa(T)\) is allowed to grow with decreasing temperature in the hadronic stage (as theoretically expected), the large viscous effects on \(v_2\) are strongly reduced, meaning that extraction of hadronic shear viscosity requires a simultaneous fit of both \((\eta/s)(T)\) and \(\kappa(T)\).

## Method

The authors used the (2+1)-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic code VISH2+1 with a temperature-dependent specific shear viscosity \((\eta/s)(T)\) that is constant at 0.16 in the QGP phase but increases to values between 0.16 and 0.48 in the hadron gas phase, using a hyperbolic tangent parametrization centered at \(T_c = 170\) MeV. They simulated 200 \(A\) GeV Au+Au collisions with initial conditions from the optical fKLN model, using the s95p-PCE equation of state with chemical decoupling at 165 MeV, and computed transverse momentum spectra and differential elliptic flow \(v_2(p_T)\) for hadrons via the Cooper-Frye procedure with viscous \(\delta f\) corrections at a kinetic freeze-out temperature of 120 MeV.

## Key numbers

*   \((\eta/s)_{\mathrm{QGP}} = 0.16\) was held constant in the QGP phase, while \((\eta/s)_{\mathrm{HG}}\) was varied from 0.16 to 0.48 in the hadron gas phase.
*   The critical temperature for the parametrization was \(T_c = 170\) MeV, and the chemical decoupling temperature was \(T_{\mathrm{chem}} = 165\) MeV.
*   For the constant choice \(\kappa = 3\), increasing \((\eta/s)_{\mathrm{HG}}\) from 0.16 to 0.48 caused the flow momentum anisotropy \(\epsilon_p\) to decrease by about 25%, while the total momentum anisotropy \(\epsilon'_p\) decreased by almost twice as much.
*   For \((\eta/s)_{\mathrm{HG}} = 0.48\) and \(\kappa = 3\), the proton elliptic flow \(v_2(p_T)\) turned negative at low \(p_T\) (around 0.5 GeV/\(c\)), an effect caused entirely by the \(\delta f\) correction.
*   The \(\delta f\) correction to the spectra for constant \(\eta/s = 0.16\) remained below 25% up to \(p_T = 2\) GeV/\(c\) for all spectra shown.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Song and Heinz (2010) using the VISHNU hybrid code, which coupled VISH2+1 to the UrQMD cascade and indicated that microscopic hadronic dynamics cannot be faithfully simulated with viscous hydrodynamics using the constant \(\kappa = 3\) relation. It differs from an earlier study by Bozek (2009) by focusing on shear viscosity in situations where the hadron gas viscosity is larger than that of the QGP, as expected on theoretical grounds by Csernai (2006). The results imply that reliable extraction of hadronic shear viscosity from comparisons between VISH2+1 and microscopic cascades or experimental data requires simultaneous determination of both \((\eta/s)(T)\) and \(\kappa(T)\), motivating further theoretical work on the temperature dependence of the relaxation time coefficient.
