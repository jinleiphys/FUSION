# Impact of the $^7$Be($α, γ$)$^{11}$C reaction on the primordial abundance of $^7$Li

> We calculate the radiative capture cross section for $^7$Be($α, γ$)$^{11}$C and its reaction rate of relevance for the big bang nucleosynthesis. The impact of this reaction on the primordial $^7$Li abundance is revised including narrow and broad resonances in the pertinent energy region. Our calculations show that it is unlikely that very low energy resonances in $^{11}$C of relevance for the big bang nucleosynthesis would emerge within a two-body potential model. Based on our results and a comparison with previous theoretical and experimental analyses, we conclude that the impact of this reaction on the so-called "cosmological lithium puzzle" is completely irrelevant.

## Key claim

The authors conclude that the $^7$Be($\alpha, \gamma$)$^{11}$C reaction has no appreciable effect on the primordial $^7$Li abundance, and that even multiplying the reaction rate by a factor of $10^4$ does not yield any significant change, making the reaction "completely irrelevant" to the cosmological lithium puzzle.

## Method

The authors performed two-body potential model calculations using the RADCAP code with Woods-Saxon potentials to compute the radiative capture cross section and astrophysical S-factor for the $^7$Be($\alpha, \gamma$)$^{11}$C reaction in the energy range 0-3 MeV. They included contributions from non-resonant direct capture, a sub-threshold resonance at $E_x = 7.50$ MeV, four higher-energy resonances (5/2$^+$, 3/2$^-$, 5/2$^-$, 7/2$^-$), and two narrow resonances at $E_x = 8.105$ and 8.421 MeV. Spectroscopic factors for the sub-threshold state were extracted from the mirror nucleus $^{11}$B. The resulting reaction rates were then input into a BBN network code based on the Wagoner code to study the effect on $^7$Li abundance.

## Key numbers

- The predicted BBN $^7$Li abundance from WMAP is $5.12^{+0.71}_{-0.62}\times10^{-10}$ (Cyburt et al.) and from Planck is $4.89^{+0.41}_{-0.39}\times10^{-10}$ (Coc et al.), while the observed value in metal-poor halo stars is $1.58^{+0.35}_{-0.28}\times10^{-10}$ (Sbordone et al.)
- Two narrow resonances in $^{11}$C are known at excitation energies $E_x = 8.105$ MeV (c.m. energy = 0.884 MeV) and $E_x = 8.421$ MeV (c.m. energy = 1.376 MeV) (Hardie et al.)
- The sub-threshold resonance at $E_x = 7.50$ MeV has $E_r = -0.045$ MeV; its calculated $\Gamma_\alpha$ ranges from $5.87\times10^{-72}$ eV to $5.87\times10^{-71}$ eV depending on spectroscopic factor, with $\Gamma_\gamma = 20.85$ eV
- The Gamow peak for this reaction lies in the energy range 0.14-0.7 MeV for temperatures $T_9 = 0.1-1$
- Multiplying the $^7$Be($\alpha, \gamma$)$^{11}$C reaction rate by a factor of $10^4$ yields no significant change in the $^7$Li abundance

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical calculations of the $^7$Be($\alpha, \gamma$)$^{11}$C reaction by Buchmann et al., the NACRE-II compilation, and Pierre et al. (1995) using the three-cluster Generator Coordinate Method. It directly addresses the speculation by Broggini et al. (2012) and Civitarese et al. that an undetected low-energy resonance in $^{11}$C could significantly reduce the primordial $^7$Li abundance. The results rule out this reaction as a solution to the cosmological lithium puzzle, consistent with the experimental findings of Hammache et al. suggesting no resonance exists in the low-energy region.
