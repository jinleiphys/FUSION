# The Sound of the Little Bangs

> Measurements of particles emitted from collisions of heavy ions at relativistic energies show evidence for temperature-fluctuations on the freeze-out surface of the expanding fireball. These can be understood as remnants of the density inhomogeneities in the initial collision overlap region. In this paper we present an analysis of heavy-ion collisions analogous to the analysis of the cosmic microwave background radiation. We use data on transverse momentum correlations to extract a power-spectrum from heavy-ion collisions and use a Glauber model for eccentricity to extract the transfer function needed to produce the observed correlations. We also discuss the acoustic signal, the sound that an observer inside the matter would hear.

## Key claim

The paper presents a power-spectrum analysis of heavy-ion collisions analogous to cosmic microwave background (CMB) analysis, but the extracted power-spectrum and transfer-function are derived from transverse momentum correlations that contain contributions from all sources (including jets and resonances), not solely from initial density fluctuations; the interpretation in terms of hotspots is supported by ancillary evidence rather than proven uniquely.

## Method

The authors used published STAR collaboration data on two-particle transverse momentum correlations as a function of relative azimuthal angle, Fourier-transformed these correlations to extract the power-spectrum coefficients \(a_n\) for each harmonic \(n\). They then calculated the participant eccentricity \(\langle\varepsilon_{n,part}^2\rangle\) for all harmonics using a Monte Carlo Glauber model, and derived the transfer-function as the ratio of the measured power-spectrum to the initial eccentricity spectrum. The acoustic horizon was computed from lattice QCD data on the speed of sound combined with a hydrodynamic model for the energy density evolution.

## Key numbers

- Hotspot size in Au+Au collisions: \(l_{spot} \approx 1.5\) fm, from simulations of Au nuclei (12 fm across) using an event generator.
- Acoustic horizon at freeze-out: \(H \approx 5\) fm at \(\tau \approx 10\) fm/c, where the phase transition from QGP to hadron-gas causes a flattening in the slope.
- Crude estimate of mean-free-path: \(l_{mfp} \approx 3.5\) fm, derived from the condition that conversion becomes inefficient for harmonic \(n=5\) with average participant radius \(\langle R \rangle = 3\) fm.
- The transfer-function shows efficiency drops off quickly as \(n\) increases, consistent with the condition that conversion is efficient only when \(l_{mfp} < 2\pi\langle R \rangle/n\).

## Context

This work extends the analogy between heavy-ion collisions and Big Bang cosmology first pointed out by Mishra et al. (2008), who proposed measuring RMS values of \(v_n\) as a power-spectrum but did not connect to existing two-particle correlation measurements. The analysis builds on prior work by Alver and Roland (2010) using the RQMD model to show lumpy initial conditions produce \(\sqrt{\langle v_3^2\rangle}\), and by Petersen et al. (2010) using event-by-event hydrodynamics. The approach enables a new method for constraining the mean-free-path and viscosity of the quark-gluon plasma by comparing the transfer-function to complete hydrodynamic models, and facilitates a more direct comparison between relativistic heavy-ion collisions and the early universe.
