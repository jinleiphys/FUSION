# Experimental neutrino physics in a nuclear landscape

> There are profound connections between neutrino physics and nuclear experiments. Exceptionally precise measurements of single and double beta-decay spectra illuminate the scale and nature of neutrino mass and may finally answer the question of whether neutrinos are their own antimatter counterparts. Neutrino-nucleus scattering underpins oscillation experiments and probes nuclear structure, neutrinos offer a rare vantage point into collapsing stars and nuclear fission reactors, and techniques pioneered in neutrino nuclear-physics experiments are advancing quantum-sensing technologies. In this article, we review current and planned efforts at the intersection of neutrino and nuclear experiments.

## Key claim

This review paper surveys the intersection of neutrino and nuclear experiments, arguing that exceptionally precise measurements of single and double beta-decay spectra can illuminate the scale and nature of neutrino mass and potentially determine whether neutrinos are Majorana particles, but the interpretation of neutrinoless double-beta decay limits in terms of an effective Majorana mass is only valid under the standard assumption of light Majorana neutrino exchange.

## Method

The authors review current and planned experimental efforts at the intersection of neutrino and nuclear physics, covering kinematic neutrino-mass measurements from tritium beta decay (KATRIN, Project 8) and holmium-163 electron-capture decay (ECHo, HOLMES), as well as neutrinoless double-beta decay searches across multiple isotopes including germanium-76, xenon-136, tellurium-130, molybdenum-100, selenium-82, and calcium-48. The review examines experimental parameters such as background index, detector energy resolution, isotopic mass, and half-life sensitivity for recently completed, currently running, and proposed next-generation experiments.

## Key numbers

- KATRIN experiment: best current kinematic limit of mβ < 0.8 eV/c² (90% C.L.) from first two measurement campaigns, with design sensitivity goal of mβ < 300 meV
- Project 8 experiment: Bayesian upper limit of mβ < 155 eV/c² (90% C.L.) using tritium in a small trap, with ultimate goal of mβ < 40 meV/c² (90% C.L.) using atomic tritium
- ECHo experiment: limit of mβ < 150 eV/c² (95% C.L.) from holmium-163 measurements; ECHo-1k phase will reach sensitivity below 20 eV/c²; ECHo-100k phase projected to reach sensitivity below 2 eV/c²
- Holmium-163 Q-value: 2.833 keV, half-life of 4570 years
- GERDA completed experiment: lower half-life limit T½⁰ν = 1.8 × 10²⁶ years, corresponding mββ limit of 79-180 meV
- KamLAND-Zen currently taking data: lower half-life limit T½⁰ν = 2.3 × 10²⁶ years, corresponding mββ limit of 36-156 meV
- LEGEND-1000 proposed: projected half-life sensitivity T½⁰ν = 8.5 × 10²⁸ years, corresponding mββ limit of 9-21 meV
- nEXO proposed: projected half-life sensitivity T½⁰ν = 1.3 × 10²⁸ years, corresponding mββ limit of 6.1-27 meV
- Next-generation experiments aim to probe half-lives in range of 10²⁶-10²⁸ years, setting limit of mββ < (18.4 ± 1.3) meV

## Context

This review extends and contextualizes the experimental programs described in earlier reviews by Dolinski (2019), Agostini (2022), and Dell'Oro (2016), covering both kinematic neutrino-mass measurements and neutrinoless double-beta decay searches. The paper connects the KATRIN experiment's approach, which builds on decades of tritium beta-decay measurements dating back to the 1980s, with newer techniques such as Cyclotron Radiation Emission Spectroscopy (CRES) from Project 8 and microcalorimetry for holmium-163 from the ECHo and HOLMES collaborations. The review enables comparison of different experimental strategies across multiple isotopes, which is essential for confirming any future discovery of neutrinoless double-beta decay and identifying the underlying decay mechanism.
