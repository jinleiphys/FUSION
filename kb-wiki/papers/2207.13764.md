# Clustering in nuclei at finite temperature

> We investigate the localization and clustering features in $^{20}$Ne ($N=Z$) and neutron-rich $^{32}$Ne nuclei at zero and finite temperatures. The finite temperature Hartree-Bogoliubov theory is used with the relativistic density-dependent meson-nucleon coupling functional DD-ME2. It is shown that clustering features gradually weaken with increasing temperature and disappear when the shape phase transition occurs. Considering thermal fluctuations in the density profiles, the clustering features vanish at lower temperatures, compared to the case without thermal fluctuations. The effect of the pairing correlations on the nucleon localization and the formation of cluster structures are also studied at finite temperatures. Due to the inclusion of pairing in the calculations, cluster structures are preserved until the critical temperatures for the shape phase transition are reached. Above the critical temperature of the shape phase transition, the clustering features suddenly disappear, which differs from the results without pairing.

## Key claim

Clustering features in both \(^{20}\)Ne and neutron-rich \(^{32}\)Ne gradually weaken with increasing temperature and disappear when the shape phase transition occurs; when thermal fluctuations in the density profiles are considered, clustering vanishes at lower temperatures than in the case without such fluctuations, and including pairing correlations preserves cluster structures until the critical temperature for the shape phase transition is reached.

## Method

The authors solved the finite-temperature Hartree-Bogoliubov (FT-RHB) equations with a constraint on the axial mass quadrupole moment, using the relativistic density-dependent meson-nucleon coupling functional DD-ME2. They computed free-energy surfaces over a deformation range of \(\beta_2 = -0.6\) to \(1.0\) in steps of \(0.05\), and then calculated thermal averages of observables (including densities and localization functions) by ensemble averaging over quadrupole shapes using the probability factor \(\exp(-\Delta F/T)\). The nucleon localization function (NLF) was used to quantify clustering, and the role of pairing was studied by increasing the pairing strength in \(^{20}\)Ne and by examining the naturally paired neutron-rich nucleus \(^{32}\)Ne.

## Key numbers

* \(^{20}\)Ne at zero temperature: ground-state deformation \(\beta_2 = 0.55\) and total binding energy \(157.64\) MeV (FT-RHB minimum); experimental values are \(\beta_2 = 0.720(20)\) and binding energy \(160.64\) MeV.
* \(^{20}\)Ne shape phase transition from prolate to spherical occurs just above \(T = 1.5\) MeV in the FT-RHB minimum; with thermal fluctuations, deformation remains low (\(\beta_2 \simeq 0.1\)) even at \(T = 3.0\) MeV.
* \(^{20}\)Ne with increased pairing strength (\(G_{p(n)} = 910\) MeV fm\(^3\)): zero-temperature deformation \(\beta = 0.45\); pairing persists beyond \(T > 1\) MeV and vanishes completely at \(T = 1.6\) MeV; cluster structures disappear suddenly after the shape phase transition at \(T = 1.1\) MeV.
* \(^{32}\)Ne at zero temperature: ground-state deformation \(\beta = 0.35\) and total binding energy \(214.84\) MeV (experimental: \(213.47\) MeV); shape phase transition occurs just after \(T = 0.6\) MeV.
* Thermal average densities show cluster states disappear completely at \(T = 1\) MeV for \(^{20}\)Ne (without pairing) and at \(T = 0.3\) MeV for \(^{32}\)Ne.

## Context

This work extends the study of clustering in finite nuclei, previously investigated at zero temperature using relativistic and non-relativistic nuclear energy density functionals (Ebran 2012, PhysRevC.87.044307, PhysRevC.89.031303), into the finite-temperature regime. While temperature effects on clustering had been studied only in nuclear matter (PhysRevLett.80.3177, PhysRevC.77.055804), this paper provides the first systematic analysis of how temperature, thermal shape fluctuations, and pairing correlations affect localization and cluster structures in finite nuclei. The results enable future studies of clustering in astrophysical contexts, such as the nuclear equation of state at finite temperature, and motivate inclusion of quantum fluctuations for light nuclei at low temperatures.
