# Light Front Nuclear Physics: Toy Models, Static Sources and Tilted Light Front Coordinates

> The principles behind the detailed results of a light-front mean field theory of finite nuclei are elucidated by deriving the nucleon mode equation using a simple general argument, based on the idea that a static source in equal time coordinates corresponds to a moving source in light front coordinates. This idea also allows us to solve several simple toy model examples: scalar field in a box, 1+1 dimensional bag model, three-dimensional harmonic oscillator and the Hulthén potential. The latter provide simplified versions of momentum distributions and form factors of relevance to experiments. In particular, the relativistic correction to the mean square radius of a nucleus is shown to be very small. Solving these simple examples suggests another more general approach-- the use of tilted light front coordinates. The simple examples are made even simpler.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that static sources in equal-time coordinates correspond to moving sources in light-front coordinates, but this dependence can be removed via a simple transformation, yielding light-front equations that reproduce equal-time results while enabling computation of light-front momentum distributions relevant to high-energy experiments. The authors hedge that their simplified toy models do not enforce the exact support condition (no components with \(k^+<0\)), but for nucleons the errors introduced are "very small indeed."

## Method

The authors derive the nucleon mode equation for light-front mean field theory by starting from the Dirac equation with static potentials in equal-time coordinates, applying a transformation to remove the \(x^+\) dependence that arises when rewriting in light-front coordinates. They then solve four toy models (scalar field in a box, 1+1 dimensional bag model, three-dimensional harmonic oscillator, and Hulthén potential) by transforming the light-front equations into forms analogous to equal-time problems, and compute momentum distributions and form factors. Finally, they introduce tilted light-front coordinates \((\tau = x^0 + x^3, \zeta = -x^3)\) in which static sources remain static, and re-solve the same problems to demonstrate equivalence.

## Key numbers

- Scalar field in a box: quantized energies satisfy \(k_n^- = 2E_n\) with \(E_n^2 = m^2 + (n\pi/L)^2\), where \(k_n^-\) is the light-front energy and \(n = 1,2,3,\ldots\)
- 1+1 dimensional bag model (massless case): \(p_n^- = (n + 1/2)(2\pi/L)\) for \(n = 0,1,\ldots\), which equals \(2E_n\)
- Three-dimensional harmonic oscillator: ground state mean square radius \(R^2_{NR} = (3/2)(1/\sqrt{\kappa})\) in the non-relativistic limit, with relativistic correction \(\delta \approx \sqrt{\pi}/(12 A^{2/3})\) yielding effects of about 0.004 for \(A \sim 200\)
- Three-dimensional harmonic oscillator: for massless quarks, the quark distribution function \(q(x) \propto e^{-27(x-1/3)^2}\), with support errors of about 5% at \(x=0\)
- Three-dimensional harmonic oscillator: for constituent quark models with \(m^2 = \sqrt{\kappa}\), the distribution at \(x=0\) gives a factor \(e^{-4}\); for nuclear physics with \(R_A = 4\) Fm, the factor is \(e^{-400}\) at \(x=0\)
- Hulthén potential: momentum distribution \(n^H_0(p_\perp) = \tilde{N}_0^H [1/(a^2 + p_\perp^2 + (p_n^-/2)^2(\alpha-1)^2)^2 - 1/(b^2 + p_\perp^2 + (p_n^-/2)^2(\alpha-1)^2)^2]\), with parameters \(m = 0.94\) GeV, \(E = 0.932\) GeV, and \(b = 5a\)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous mean field theory for finite nuclei (Blunden, Burkardt, and Miller, 1999) by providing simpler derivations and toy model illustrations of the principles behind that formalism. The paper enables computation of light-front momentum distributions and form factors relevant to deep inelastic lepton-nucleus scattering and high-momentum transfer nuclear reactions such as \((e,e'), (e,e'p), (p,2p)\), and addresses the need for relativistic formulations using \(k^+\) variables closely related to experimental observables. The tilted light-front coordinates introduced here provide a shortcut for constructing light-front Hamiltonians in problems with external fields, with potential applications to heavy-light systems in QCD (e.g., B-mesons) and quantum field theory treatments of large nuclei.
