# Deconfinement transition dynamics and early thermalization in QGP

> We perform SU(3) Lattice Gauge Theory simulations of the deconfinement transition attempting to mimic conditions encountered in heavy ion collisions. Specifically, we perform a sudden temperature quench across the deconfinement temperature, and follow the response of the system in successive simulation sweeps under spatial lattice expansion and temperature fall-off. In measurements of the Polyakov loop and structure functions a robust strong signal of global instability response is observed through the exponential growth of low momentum modes. Development of these long range modes isotropizes the system which reaches thermalization shortly afterwards, and enters a stage of quasi-equilibrium expansion and cooling till its return to the confinement phase. The time scale characterizing full growth of the long range modes is largely unaffected by the conditions of spatial expansion and temperature variation in the system, and is much shorter than the scale set by the interval to return to the confinement phase. The wide separation of these two scales is such that it naturally results in isotropization times well inside 1 fm/c.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a sudden quench across the deconfinement temperature in SU(3) lattice gauge theory produces a robust signal of exponential growth of low-momentum modes, leading to isotropization and thermalization on time scales well inside 1 fm/c, and that this rapid growth is largely unaffected by the presence of spatial expansion and temperature variation over a wide range of parameters.

## Method

The authors performed SU(3) lattice gauge theory simulations using a field-based heat bath update on anisotropic lattices, starting from an equilibrated confined phase and applying a sudden temperature quench across the deconfinement temperature. They then followed the system's evolution under conditions of uniform spatial expansion (Hubble-like) and temperature fall-off (T ~ tau^{-1/3}), measuring the Polyakov loop and structure functions (connected Polyakov 2-point functions) over successive simulation sweeps. The simulations were done on 16^3 x 4 and 32^3 x 4 and 32^3 x 8 lattices, with quenches from beta=5.5 to beta=5.92 (T_final=1.57 T_c) and from beta=5.90 to beta=6.85 (T_final=2.95 T_c).

## Key numbers

* Quench from beta=5.5 to beta=5.92 on a 16^3 x 4 lattice corresponds to T_final = 1.57 T_c, where the phase transition at xi=1 is at beta=5.6902(2).
* Quench from beta=5.90 to beta=6.85 on a 32^3 x 8 lattice corresponds to T_final = 2.95 T_c, with beta_c = 6.0625 at T_c.
* The structure function peak location occurs at approximately 300 simulation sweeps after the quench, largely unaffected by expansion or temperature variation.
* Fits to the structure function growth give exponent alpha = 1.22 for the expanding system (tau_0=500) and alpha = 1.18 for the non-expanding system, with exponential growth (linear tau dependence in exponent) providing the best fit per parameter degree of freedom.
* The expanding system shows enhancement of the structure function signal by a factor of approximately 7 compared to the non-expanding system.
* The return to the confinement phase occurs after about 700 sweeps for the 16^3 x 4 lattice with combined expansion and temperature fall-off.
* For the 32^3 x 8 lattice with parameters x ~ 9 and y ~ 3.25 (derived from hydrodynamical phenomenology), the system shows isotropization times well inside 1 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends previous lattice gauge theory studies of phase transition dynamics by Bazavov, Berg, and Velytsky (2004) and Berg, Velytsky (2003-2004), and builds on the pioneering lattice simulation approach of Miller (2000-2001). The results contradict perturbative estimates of thermalization times (such as the parton-cascade approach and the bottom-up scenario by Mueller, Bjoraker, and others) which yield much longer times than the ~0.5 fm/c needed for hydrodynamical simulations of RHIC data. The paper enables a non-perturbative, ab initio understanding of early thermalization in heavy-ion collisions, showing that spinodal decomposition-like dynamics naturally produce the rapid isotropization required by hydrodynamical models of Heinz and Kolb (2004).
