# Applications of Chiral Perturbation Theory in Reactions with Heavy Particles

> Effective field theory techniques are used to describe the interaction of heavy hadrons in a model independent way. Predictability is obtained by exploiting the symmetries of QCD. Heavy hadron chiral perturbation theory is reviewed and used to describe $D^*$ decays. The phenomenologically important $D^*Dπ$ coupling is extracted from data working to first order in the chiral and heavy quark symmetry breaking parameters. A method is described for determining $|V_{ub}|$ from exclusive semileptonic $B$ and $D$ decays with 10% uncertainty. An effective field theory for two-nucleon systems is then discussed. The large S-wave scattering lengths necessitate expanding around a non-trivial fixed point. A detailed discussion of the interplay between renormalization and the power counting is given. In power counting pion interactions with nucleons it is useful to consider three classes of pion: potential, radiation, and soft. A power counting for massive radiation is developed. Finally, it is shown that the leading terms in the effective theory for nucleon-nucleon interactions are invariant under Wigner's SU(4) spin-isospin symmetry in the infinite scattering length limit.

# Applications of Chiral Perturbation Theory in Reactions with Heavy Particles

## Key claim

Effective field theory techniques can describe interactions of heavy hadrons in a model-independent way by exploiting QCD symmetries, but the predictive power depends on expanding around symmetry limits and organizing terms by power counting in small parameters; the claimed 10% uncertainty for determining $|V_{ub}|$ from exclusive semileptonic decays relies on chiral perturbation theory corrections to form factor ratios being small, and the method requires combining experimental data from multiple decay channels.

## Method

Heavy hadron chiral perturbation theory was constructed by combining heavy quark effective theory (HQET) with SU(3) chiral perturbation theory, using velocity-dependent fields for heavy mesons and an exponential representation for pseudo-Goldstone bosons. For two-nucleon systems, an effective field theory was developed by expanding around a non-trivial ultraviolet fixed point to handle large S-wave scattering lengths, with power counting implemented through the Power Divergence Subtraction (PDS) scheme and an off-shell momentum subtraction (OS) scheme. Pion interactions with nucleons were classified into three types (potential, radiation, and soft) with a dedicated power counting developed for massive radiation pions.

## Key numbers

- $f_\pi = 131$ MeV: the pion decay constant used in the chiral Lagrangian
- $\Lambda_{\rm QCD} \sim 250$ MeV: the dynamically generated scale of QCD
- $\Lambda_\chi \sim 4\pi f \sim 1.2$ GeV: the chiral symmetry breaking scale
- $m_D = 1.867$ GeV, $m_{D^*} = 2.008$ GeV: masses of the charm meson doublet
- $m_B = 5.279$ GeV, $m_{B^*} = 5.325$ GeV: masses of the bottom meson doublet
- $m_\rho = 770$ MeV: mass of the lightest vector meson
- $\Delta \mu = \mu_0 - \mu_8 < 200$ MeV: the octet-singlet vector meson mass difference, treated as order $m_q$
- $\tan \theta \simeq \pm 0.76$: the SU(3)$_V$ prediction for the $\phi$-$\omega$ mixing angle

## Context

This work extends the chiral perturbation theory framework developed by Weinberg (1979) and the heavy quark effective theory of Georgi (1990) and others to processes involving heavy hadrons and two-nucleon systems. It builds on the heavy hadron chiral perturbation theory formalism of Burdman (1992), Yan (1992), and Jenkins and Manohar (1992), and on the nucleon-nucleon effective field theory of Weinberg (1990, 1991), van Kolck (1994), Lepage (1997), and Kaplan, Savage, and Wise (1998). The method for determining $|V_{ub}|$ with 10% uncertainty enables model-independent extraction of this CKM matrix element from exclusive semileptonic $B$ and $D$ decays, while the two-nucleon effective theory provides a framework for systematically including pion effects and understanding the role of Wigner's SU(4) spin-isospin symmetry in the infinite scattering length limit.
