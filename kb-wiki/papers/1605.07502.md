# Nucleosynthesis of $^{92}$Nb and the relevance of the low-lying isomer at 135.5 keV

> Background: Because of its half-life of about 35 million years, 92Nb is considered as a chronometer for nucleosynthesis events prior to the birth of our sun. The abundance of 92Nb in the early solar system can be derived from meteoritic data. It has to be compared to theoretical estimates for the production of 92Nb to determine the time between the last nucleosynthesis event before the formation of the early solar system. Purpose: The influence of a low-lying short-lived isomer on the nucleosynthesis of 92Nb is analyzed. The thermal coupling between the ground state and the isomer via so-called intermediate states affects the production and survival of 92Nb. Method: The properties of the lowest intermediate state in 92Nb are known from experiment. From the lifetime of the intermediate state and from its decay branchings, the transition rate from the ground state to the isomer and the effective half-life of 92Nb are calculated as a function of the temperature. Results: The coupling between the ground state and the isomer is strong. This leads to thermalization of ground state and isomer in the nucleosynthesis of 92Nb in any explosive production scenario and almost 100% survival of 92Nb in its ground state. However, the strong coupling leads to a temperature-dependent effective half-life of 92Nb which makes the 92Nb survival very sensitive to temperatures as low as about 8 keV, thus turning 92Nb at least partly into a thermometer. Conclusions: The low-lying isomer in 92Nb does not affect the production of 92Nb in explosive scenarios. In retrospect this validates all previous studies where the isomer was not taken into account. However, the dramatic reduction of the effective half-life at temperatures below 10 keV may affect the survival of 92Nb after its synthesis in supernovae which are the most likely astrophysical site for the nucleosynthesis of 92Nb.

# Nucleosynthesis of $^{92}$Nb and the relevance of the low-lying isomer at 135.5 keV

## Key claim

The low-lying $2^+$ isomer at 135.5 keV in $^{92}$Nb does not affect production in explosive nucleosynthesis scenarios because thermal coupling via the $4^+$ intermediate state at 480.3 keV is so strong that thermal equilibrium between ground state and isomer is reached almost instantaneously; however, the same strong coupling produces a temperature-dependent effective half-life that drops dramatically at temperatures as low as about 8 keV, potentially turning $^{92}$Nb from a chronometer into a thermometer for the thermal history after nucleosynthesis.

## Method

The properties of the lowest intermediate state (IMS) in $^{92}$Nb, the $4^+$ state at 480.3 keV, were taken from experimental data (ENSDF database and an unpublished laboratory report USIP-74-17). From the lifetime and decay branchings of this IMS, the stellar transition rate from the $7^+$ ground state to the $2^+$ isomer was calculated using the integrated cross section formalism, and the effective half-life of $^{92}$Nb was derived as a function of temperature, accounting for thermal photon density via the Planck distribution.

## Key numbers

- Half-life of $^{92}$Nb ground state: $t_{1/2} = 3.47 \times 10^7$ y (electron capture to $^{92}$Zr)
- Half-life of $^{92}$Nb $2^+$ isomer at 135.5 keV: $t_{1/2} = 10.15$ d (9 orders of magnitude shorter than ground state)
- IMS is the $4^+$ state at $E^\ast = 480.3$ keV; its half-life is 0.62 ps (from USIP-74-17), with decay branches: $b_\gamma = 23.7 \pm 3.2\%$ to the $5^+$ state at 357.4 keV and $b_\gamma = 76.3 \pm 3.2\%$ to the $3^+$ state at 285.7 keV
- Integrated cross section for the $7^+ \rightarrow 2^+$ transition via the IMS: $I_\sigma^\ast = 133$ eV fm$^2$ with about 20% uncertainty
- At $kT = 50$ keV, the transition rate exceeds $10^6$/s, ensuring thermalization within less than 1 microsecond
- Freeze-out temperature for thermal equilibrium in a typical supernova explosion (timescale ~1 s): $kT \approx 19$ keV, where Boltzmann ratio $n(2^+)/n(7^+) \lesssim 10^{-4}$
- At $kT = 8.3$ keV, the effective half-life is reduced by one order of magnitude; at $kT = 10.4$ keV, reduction reaches a factor of 1000
- $\beta$-decay constant of ground state: $\lambda_\beta(7^+) = 6.3 \times 10^{-16}$/s; of isomer: $\lambda_\beta(2^+) = 7.9 \times 10^{-7}$/s
- $K$-shell binding energy in $^{92}$Nb: 19.0 keV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of isomer-mediated thermal coupling in odd-odd nuclei, previously studied for $^{176}$Lu (Mohr 2009), $^{180}$Ta (Belic et al. 1999, 2002), and $^{108}$Ag (Set 2016), where the integrated cross sections of the lowest IMS vary by many orders of magnitude. The strong coupling found in $^{92}$Nb validates all previous nucleosynthesis studies (e.g., Meyer 2003, Pignatari 2016) that neglected the isomer in production calculations. The dramatic reduction of the effective half-life below 10 keV opens the possibility of using $^{92}$Nb as a thermometer for the thermal evolution of supernova ejecta (Vink 2012, Badenes 2010) after the last nucleosynthesis event before solar system formation, rather than solely as a chronometer.
