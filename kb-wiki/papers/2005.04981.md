# Laser-nucleus interactions in the sudden regime

> The interaction between medium-weight nuclei and a strong zeptosecond laser pulse of MeV photons is investigated theoretically. Multiple absorption of photons competes with nuclear equilibration. We investigate the sudden regime. Here the rate of photon absorption is so strong that there is no time for the nucleus to fully equilibrate after each photon absorption process. We follow the temporal evolution of the system in terms of a set of rate equations. These account for dipole absorption and induced dipole emission, equilibration (modeled in terms of particle-hole states coupled by the residual nuclear interaction), and neutron decay (populating a chain of proton-rich nuclei). Our results are compared with earlier work addressing the adiabatic regime where equilibration is instantaneous. We predict the degree of excitation and the range of nuclei reached by neutron evaporation. These findings are relevant for planning future experiments.

## Key claim

The paper predicts that in the sudden regime, where the effective dipole absorption width \(\widetilde{\Gamma}_{\rm dip}\) (1--20 MeV) far exceeds the nuclear spreading width \(\Gamma^\downarrow\) (approximately 5 MeV), multiple photon absorption produces compound nuclei at several hundred MeV excitation energy and low angular momentum, leading to a chain of proton-rich nuclei via sequential neutron decay; however, the authors note that this picture is "qualitatively similar to but quantitatively somewhat different from" the quasiadiabatic regime results of Palffy et al. (2014, 2015).

## Method

The authors model the interaction of a zeptosecond laser pulse of 5 MeV photons with a medium-weight nucleus using a set of rate equations for the average occupation probabilities of classes of particle-hole states labeled by mass number, excitation energy (in multiples of the photon energy), and particle-hole number \(m\). The rate equations account for three competing processes: (i) equilibration between neighboring \(m\)p-\(m\)h classes at fixed energy via the residual nuclear interaction, modeled using the optical model with \(W(\varepsilon) = c \varepsilon^2\) and \(c = 0.003\) MeV\(^{-1}\); (ii) dipole absorption and stimulated emission by the laser pulse, with the effective dipole width \(\widetilde{\Gamma}_{\rm dip}\) as an input parameter in the range 1--20 MeV; and (iii) neutron evaporation described by the Weisskopf estimate, populating a chain of daughter nuclei with reduced mass number. The level densities for \(m\)p-\(m\)h states at high excitation energy are computed using the approximation of Palffy et al. (2013a, 2013b).

## Key numbers

* The spreading width \(\Gamma^\downarrow\) of the nuclear giant dipole resonance is approximately 5 MeV for low-lying modes with excitation energies up to 10 or 20 MeV.
* The effective dipole width \(\widetilde{\Gamma}_{\rm dip}\) is taken in the range 1--20 MeV for the sudden regime calculations.
* The photon energy \(\hbar \omega_0\) is 5 MeV, and the pulse duration is \(\tau \approx 10^{-20}\) s (corresponding to an energy spread \(\sigma\) of several 10 keV).
* Up to \(N_0 \approx 140\) photons may be absorbed in the course of the reaction.
* The equilibration time scale is \(\tau_{\rm eq} \simeq 10^{-22}\) s, derived from \(\tau_{\rm eq} = \hbar / \Gamma^\downarrow\).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Palffy et al. (2014, 2015), who studied the quasiadiabatic regime where equilibration is instantaneous after each photon absorption, to the sudden regime where the photon absorption rate is so high that the nucleus does not fully equilibrate between absorptions. The results are relevant for planning future experiments at facilities such as the Nuclear Pillar of the Extreme Light Infrastructure in Romania and the proposed Gamma Factories at the Large Hadron Collider at CERN. The paper enables predictions of the degree of excitation and the range of nuclei reached by neutron evaporation in the sudden regime, which can guide experimental design for laser-nucleus interactions with zeptosecond MeV photon pulses.
