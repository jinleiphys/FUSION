# Constraints on the symmetry energy and its associated parameters from nuclei to neutron stars

> The symmetry energy obtained with the effective Skyrme energy density functional is related to the values of isoscalar effective mass and isovector effective mass, which is also indirectly related to the incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter. In this work, we analyze the values of symmetry energy and its related nuclear matter parameters in five-dimensional parameter space by describing the heavy ion collision data, such as isospin diffusion data at 35 MeV/u and 50 MeV/u, neutron skin of $^{208}$Pb, and tidal deformability and maximum mass of neutron star. We obtain the parameter sets which can describe the isospin diffusion, neutron skin, tidal deformability and maximum mass of neutron star, and give the incompressibility $K_0$=250.23$\pm$20.16 MeV, symmetry energy coefficient $S_0$=31.35$\pm$2.08 MeV, the slope of symmetry energy $L$=59.57$\pm$10.06 MeV, isoscalar effective mass $m_s^*/m$=0.75$\pm$0.05 and quantity related to effective mass splitting $f_I$=0.005$\pm$0.170. At two times normal density, the symmetry energy we obtained is in 35-55 MeV. To reduce the large uncertainties of $f_I$, more critical works in heavy ion collisions at different beam energies are needed.

## Key claim

The symmetry energy and its associated nuclear matter parameters are constrained simultaneously in a five-dimensional parameter space (incompressibility, symmetry energy coefficient, slope of symmetry energy, isoscalar effective mass, and effective mass splitting) by requiring consistency with heavy ion collision data, neutron skin measurements, and neutron star observables; the abstract's quoted uncertainties for each parameter are conditional on the specific ranges sampled and the chosen set of observables, and the effective mass splitting \(f_I\) remains very poorly determined.

## Method

The authors used the ImQMD-Sky transport model to simulate isospin diffusion in \(^{112,124}\)Sn+\(^{112,124}\)Sn collisions at 35 MeV/u and 50 MeV/u, the restricted density variational method to compute the neutron skin of \(^{208}\)Pb, and the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equation with a polytropic high-density extension to compute neutron star tidal deformability and maximum mass. They sampled 120 parameter sets in a five-dimensional space of nuclear matter parameters (\(K_0\), \(S_0\), \(L\), \(m_s^*/m\), \(f_I\)) using Latin Hypercube Sampling, then selected the 22 sets that simultaneously reproduced the isospin diffusion data, the neutron skin of \(^{208}\)Pb, and the neutron star constraints.

## Key numbers

* Incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter: \(K_0 = 250.23 \pm 20.16\) MeV, obtained from the 22 selected parameter sets that describe all observables.
* Symmetry energy coefficient at saturation: \(S_0 = 31.35 \pm 2.08\) MeV, from the same 22-parameter-set average.
* Slope of symmetry energy: \(L = 59.57 \pm 10.06\) MeV, from the same average.
* Isoscalar effective mass: \(m_s^*/m = 0.75 \pm 0.05\), from the same average.
* Quantity related to effective mass splitting: \(f_I = 0.005 \pm 0.170\), from the same average; the uncertainty is large and the sign is not robust.
* Symmetry energy at twice normal density: 35–55 MeV, from the selected parameter sets.
* Neutron skin of \(^{208}\)Pb predicted by the 22 sets: \(\Delta r_{np} = 0.179 \pm 0.040\) fm.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to constrain the density dependence of the symmetry energy by combining multiple observables, following the approach of Margueron et al. (2018) who used a meta-EOS model, and Dutra et al. (2012) who compiled Skyrme parameter sets. It simultaneously fits heavy ion collision data (isospin diffusion from Tsang et al. (2004) and Sun et al. (2010)), neutron skin measurements (e.g., PREX from Abrahamyan et al. (2012)), and neutron star constraints (tidal deformability from LIGO/Virgo, Abbott et al. (2017, 2018)). The large remaining uncertainty in \(f_I\) indicates that new heavy ion collision experiments at different beam energies are needed to pin down the effective mass splitting.
