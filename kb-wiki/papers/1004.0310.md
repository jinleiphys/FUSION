# Low energy neutrino scattering measurements at future Spallation Source facilities

> In the future several Spallation Source facilities will be available worldwide. Spallation Sources produce large amount of neutrinos from decay-at-rest muons and thus can be well adapted to accommodate state-of-the-art neutrino experiments. In this paper low energy neutrino scattering experiments that can be performed at such facilities are reviewed. Estimation of expected event rates are given for several nuclei, electrons and protons at a detector located close to the source. A neutrino program at Spallation Sources comprises neutrino-nucleus cross section measurements relevant for neutrino and core-collapse supernova physics, electroweak tests and lepton-flavor violation searches.

# Low energy neutrino scattering measurements at future Spallation Source facilities

## Key claim
Future Spallation Source facilities can host low-energy neutrino scattering experiments that are complementary to higher-energy neutrino programs, but the expected event rates depend strongly on detector distance from the source and target nucleus choice, with heavy nuclei giving the highest rates. The paper presents estimates assuming a neutrino flux of 10^15 ν_e/s and a fully efficient 1-ton detector, noting that these numbers can be scaled for any actual production rate and running time.

## Method
The authors calculate expected event rates for neutrino-nucleus charged-current scattering on 12C, 16O, 40Ar, 56Fe, 100Mo, and 208Pb, as well as neutrino-electron and neutrino-proton scattering, using flux-averaged cross sections from the literature and their own QRPA calculations with the Skyrme interaction. They assume a detector located at 10, 20, and 50 meters from the source, with a 1-ton cubic detector operating for one year (3×10^7 seconds), and account for the finite detector size effect on the flux. The multipole decomposition of the cross sections is presented to identify the nuclear structure response.

## Key numbers
- For 208Pb at 10 meters: 34,500 events/year for total charged-current, 1,630 events for 1-neutron emission, and 9,420 events for 2-neutron emission, assuming 10^15 ν_e/s and a 1-ton detector.
- For 100Mo at 10 meters: 17,300 events/year; for 56Fe at 10 meters: 9,100 events/year; for 40Ar at 10 meters: 8,860 events/year.
- For 12C (in C16H18 scintillator) at 10 meters: 1,470 events/year; for 16O (in water) at 10 meters: 998 events/year.
- Flux-averaged DAR cross sections: 208Pb total = 49.6×10^-40 cm^2; 100Mo = 11.95×10^-40 cm^2; 56Fe = 3.53×10^-40 cm^2; 40Ar = 2.56×10^-40 cm^2; 16O = 0.131×10^-40 cm^2; 12C ≈ 0.14×10^-40 cm^2.
- For neutrino-electron scattering in water at 10 meters: ν_e-e = 230 events/year; ν_μ-e = 35 events/year; anti-ν_μ-e = 38 events/year.
- For electron anti-neutrino-proton scattering in water at 10 meters: 11,300 events/year, with flux-averaged cross section 0.738×10^-40 cm^2.
- The sensitivity S for Weinberg angle determination using the ratio O = N(ν_μ-e)/[N(anti-ν_μ-e)+N(ν_e-e)] is S=1.68; using the jump height in the electron energy distribution at 29.7 MeV gives S=2.
- The current best value for ν_e-e scattering cross section is σ = (10.1 ± 1.1 stat ± 1.0 sys) × E_ν_e × 10^-45 cm^2 (LSND, Auerbach 2001).
- The current best limit for the lepton-flavor-violating decay μ^+ → e^+ anti-ν_e ν_μ is branching ratio 0.9×10^-3 (KARMEN, Armbruster 2003).

## Context
This paper extends the earlier work of LSND (Athanassopoulos 1996, 1997) and KARMEN (Armbruster 2002) at existing spallation sources (LANSCE and ISIS) by providing new predictions for the upcoming European Spallation Source (ESS) and other planned facilities (SNS, JSNS, SPL). The results are complementary to low-energy beta-beam studies (Volpe 2003, Serreau 2004) and to higher-energy neutrino experiments such as MiniBOONE (AguilarArevalo 2008). The measurements would enable precise neutrino-nucleus cross section determinations needed for supernova neutrino detection, tests of the Standard Model (Weinberg angle measurement), and searches for lepton-flavor violation, as well as help resolve discrepancies among theoretical models (QRPA, CRPA, Shell Model) that differ by factors of 3-4 for nuclei like 56Fe (Samana 2008, Paar 2007).
