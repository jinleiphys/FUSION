# The Chiral Restoration Transition of QCD and Low Mass Dileptons

> Recent developments in the evaluation of vector-meson spectral functions in hot and dense matter are discussed with emphasis on connections to the chiral phase transition in QCD. Model independent approaches including chiral low-density expansions, lattice QCD, chiral and QCD sum rules are put into context with model predictions for in-medium vector-spectral function utilizing effective Lagrangians. Hadronic many-body calculations predict a strong broadening (and little mass shift) of the $ρ$ spectral function which rapidly increases close to the expected phase boundary of hadronic and quark-gluon matter. Pertinent dilepton rates appear to degenerate with perturbative quark-antiquark annihilation in the Quark-Gluon Plasma, suggestive for chiral symmetry restoration. Applications to low-mass dilepton spectra in heavy-ion collisions result in quantitative agreement with recent high-quality data at the CERN-SPS. Thermal radiation from temperatures around $T_c$ consistently reproduces the experimental dilepton excess observed at masses above 1 GeV as well. The interpretation of dilepton sources at high transverse momentum appears to be more involved.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the observed strong broadening (rather than mass shift) of the ρ-meson spectral function in hot and dense matter, when extrapolated to the critical temperature T_c ≈ 175 MeV, leads to a complete "melting" of the resonance structure, and that the resulting dilepton rates become degenerate with perturbative quark-antiquark annihilation in the Quark-Gluon Plasma, suggesting chiral symmetry restoration; however, the connection to chiral restoration is less direct than in dropping-mass scenarios and relies on the reduction of the "duality threshold" from M_dual = 1.5 GeV in vacuum to essentially zero around T_c.

## Method

The authors review hadronic many-body calculations of the ρ-meson spectral function based on effective Lagrangians, where the ρ meson undergoes rescattering on constituents of a hadronic medium (pions, nucleons, etc.). They compare these model predictions with model-independent constraints from chiral low-density expansions, lattice QCD, and chiral/QCD sum rules, and then apply the resulting in-medium spectral functions to compute thermal dilepton rates and compare them with experimental dilepton spectra from heavy-ion collisions at the CERN-SPS (CERES/NA45 and NA60).

## Key numbers

- The vacuum quark condensate is ⟨0|q̄q|0⟩ ≈ (-250 MeV)^3 per light-quark flavor, with about 50% of that for strange quarks.
- The (pseudo-)critical temperature for chiral symmetry restoration from lattice QCD is T_c ≈ 160-190 MeV, corresponding to an energy density of ε_c ≈ 1 GeV/fm^3.
- The nucleon mass is m_N ≈ 940 MeV/c^2, while the bare masses of u and d quarks are only about 5-10 MeV/c^2.
- The pion mass is m_π ≈ 140 MeV, and the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner relation gives m_π^2 f_π^2 = -2 m_q ⟨q̄q⟩.
- The ρ(770) and a_1(1260) resonances exhibit a mass splitting of ΔM ≈ 500 MeV, signaling chiral symmetry breaking.
- The vacuum duality threshold for parton-hadron duality in e⁺e⁻ annihilation is M_dual = 1.5 GeV.
- The chemical freeze-out temperature in heavy-ion collisions is T_fo^ch ≈ 160 MeV, while thermal freeze-out occurs at T_fo^th ≈ 100 MeV.
- The hadronic fireball lifetime is τ_FB ≈ 10 fm/c, and thermalization at RHIC occurs on a time scale τ ≤ 1-2 fm/c.
- Nuclear matter saturation density is ρ_0 = 0.16 fm⁻³ (ε_0 = 0.15 GeV/fm³).

## Context

This review extends earlier work on in-medium vector-meson spectral functions by Rapp, Wambach, and collaborators (1997-2004) and contrasts with the dropping-mass scenario proposed by Brown and Rho (1991). It connects hadronic many-body calculations to lattice QCD results for the critical temperature (Cheng 2006, Aoki 2006) and to chiral sum rules originally developed by Weinberg (1967). The paper enables a consistent interpretation of dilepton data from the CERN-SPS (CERES/NA45 and NA60) and provides a framework for future dilepton measurements at RHIC and the LHC, as well as for vector-meson spectroscopy in cold nuclei using photon (Trnka 2005) and proton (Naruki 2005) beams.
