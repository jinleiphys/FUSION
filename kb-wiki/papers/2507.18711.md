# Color-glass condensate beyond the Gaussian approximation

> In the high-energy limit, perturbative calculations in QCD are conveniently done using the dipole picture which factorizes the scattering amplitude into a perturbative part and the nonperturbative scattering off the nuclear target, described using correlators of Wilson lines. These correlators can be computed in the color-glass condensate effective field theory by using a Gaussian model for the color density of the target. In this work, we generalize the Gaussian model to a generic function that is local in the transverse coordinates and the light-cone time, and show how to compute physical Wilson-line correlators in this model. We also consider a simple model for the color density based on stable probability distributions and show that the small-dipole behavior of the dipole amplitude is modified from quadratic to a power law, where the power is given by the stability parameter of the distribution. This generalization of the Gaussian model is suitable for numerical applications in the high-energy limit and can be used in future phenomenological studies of the nuclear structure.

## Key claim

The paper generalizes the Gaussian model of the color-glass condensate (CGC) to a generic weight function that is local in transverse coordinates and light-cone time, showing that the small-dipole behavior of the dipole amplitude changes from quadratic to a power law, where the power is given by the stability parameter of the distribution. This generalization relaxes the Gaussian assumption while maintaining the locality assumption that weights at different points are independent.

## Method

The author starts from the CGC effective field theory where observables are averaged over color-field configurations with a weight functional W[ρ]. The Gaussian McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model is generalized to a weight function that is a product of local probability distributions p_z(ρ²_z) depending only on the squared color density, expressed via characteristic functions φ_z(σ²) = exp(-Δ³z w_z(σ²)). Wilson-line correlators are computed by expanding the characteristic function to leading order in the longitudinal lattice spacing Δz⁺, then exponentiating the result to obtain the dipole amplitude in any representation. Higher-order correlators are handled by forming and solving a matrix differential equation in the longitudinal coordinate L⁺.

## Key numbers

* The dipole amplitude N(x,y) = 1 - exp(-∫ d³z F^R_z(x,y)) where F^R_z contains the model-dependent function w_z and the representation-dependent function ξ_R(σ) defined via a Bessel integral with dimension D = N_c² - 1
* For stable distributions, the small-dipole behavior of the dipole amplitude is modified from quadratic (as in the Gaussian MV model) to a power law, where the power is given by the stability parameter α of the distribution
* The adjoint dipole amplitude is verified to satisfy the relation ⟨S^(2)(x,y)S^(2)(y,x)⟩ = 1/N_c² + (N_c²-1)/N_c² exp(-∫ d³z F^A_z(x,y)), consistent with the Fierz identity for N_c colors

## Context

This work extends the McLerran-Venugopalan model (McLerran 1993, 1994) and its Gaussian-based generalizations, including non-local Gaussian forms (Blaizot 2004, Dominguez 2008, Lappi 2015) and subleading correlations (Jeon 2004, Dumitru 2011). The key innovation is relaxing the Gaussian approximation while keeping the locality assumption, motivated by the observation that the number of color sources in a nucleus fluctuates, leading to heavy-tailed distributions p(ρ) ~ 1/|ρ|^(D+2ν) for large ρ². The framework enables future phenomenological studies of nuclear structure in deep inelastic scattering and proton-nucleus collisions, where experimental data can constrain the color-field structure beyond the Gaussian approximation.
