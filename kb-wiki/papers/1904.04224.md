# Investigating the 10Li continuum through 9Li(d,p)10Li reactions

> The continuum structure of the unbound system $^{10}$Li, inferred from the $^{9}$Li$(d,p)^{10}$Li transfer reaction, is reexamined. Experimental data for this reaction, measured at two different energies, are analyzed with the same reaction framework and structure models. It is shown that the seemingly different features observed in the measured excitation energy spectra can be understood as due to the different incident energy and angular range covered by the two experiments. The present results support the persistence of the $N=7$ parity inversion beyond the neutron dripline as well as the splitting of the well-known low-lying $p$-wave resonance. Furthermore, they provide indirect evidence that most of the $\ell=2$ single-particle strength, including possible $d_{5/2}$ resonances, lies at relatively high excitations energies.

## Key claim

The seemingly different features observed in the measured excitation energy spectra of the two experiments can be understood as due to the different incident energy and angular range covered, rather than reflecting contradictory structure of the 10Li continuum; the results support the persistence of the N=7 parity inversion beyond the neutron dripline and the splitting of the low-lying p-wave resonance, but provide only indirect evidence that most of the ℓ=2 single-particle strength lies at relatively high excitation energies.

## Method

The authors reanalyzed experimental data from the 9Li(d,p)10Li transfer reaction measured at two different incident energies (2.36 MeV/u at REX-ISOLDE and 11.1 MeV/u at TRIUMF) using the Transfer to the Continuum (TC) formalism, which expands the n+9Li continuum in energy bins via the continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) method. They employed two structure models for 10Li: a simple model (P3) ignoring the 9Li spin, and a more realistic model (P1I) including spin-dependent interactions that split the s-wave and p-wave resonances into doublets. Calculations were performed with the code FRESCO, and the results were folded with experimental energy resolutions and acceptance functions.

## Key numbers

* The REX-ISOLDE experiment was measured at E = 2.36 MeV/u and the TRIUMF experiment at E = 11.1 MeV/u.
* The experimental energy resolution was FWHM = 250 keV for the REX-ISOLDE data and FWHM = 200 keV for the TRIUMF data.
* The P1I model parameters include central potential depths v_c^0 = -5.4 MeV and v_c^1 = 260.75 MeV, with a fixed radius R = 2.55 fm for the Gaussian radial functions.
* The p-wave resonance centroid was adjusted to around 0.5 MeV in the P1I model.
* The P3 model uses Woods-Saxon parameters R = 2.642 fm and a = 0.67 fm, with central depths v_c^0 = -50.5 MeV and v_c^1 = -39.0 MeV.
* For the TRIUMF data, the calculated angular distributions show that the s-wave contribution is negligible for θ_c.m. < 60°, while the p-wave dominates in the measured angular range.
* The d-wave resonance at E_x = 1.5 MeV (suggested by previous work) was found to produce a pronounced resonant peak incompatible with the TRIUMF data.
* A d-wave model with a broad resonance-like structure peaking at 6 MeV for the 4^- component gave a reasonable reproduction of the high-energy tail of the TRIUMF data.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the 10Li continuum from transfer reactions, reconciling the seemingly conflicting results of Jepsen (2006) at REX-ISOLDE and Cavallaro (2017) at TRIUMF by showing they are consistent when accounting for different angular ranges and incident energies. The P1I structure model was previously developed to describe 11Li(p,d)10Li (Cas17) and 11Li(p,pn)10Li (Gom17) reactions. The results contradict the suggestion of a low-lying d-wave resonance at 1.5 MeV from fragmentation experiments (Simon 2007) and the theoretical analysis of Blanchon (2007), and instead support predictions by Katayama (1999) that d-wave strength lies at higher excitation energies. The work enables a consistent description of multiple reaction channels populating the 10Li continuum and clarifies the role of the s-wave virtual state and p-wave resonance splitting.
