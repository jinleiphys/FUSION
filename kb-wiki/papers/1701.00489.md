# Uncertainties in s-process nucleosynthesis in massive stars determined by Monte Carlo variations

> The $s$-process in massive stars produces the weak component of the $s$-process (nuclei up to $A \sim 90$), in amounts that match solar abundances. For heavier isotopes, such as barium, production through neutron capture is significantly enhanced in very metal-poor stars with fast rotation. However, detailed theoretical predictions for the resulting final $s$-process abundances have important uncertainties caused both by the underlying uncertainties in the nuclear physics (principally neutron capture reaction and $β$-decay rates) as well as by the stellar evolution modeling. In this work, we investigated the impact of nuclear-physics uncertainties relevant to the $s$-process in massive stars. Using a Monte-Carlo based approach, we performed extensive nuclear reaction network calculations that include newly evaluated upper and lower limits for the individual temperature dependent reaction rates. We found that most of the uncertainty in the final abundances is caused by uncertainties in the neutron capture rates, while $β$-decay rate uncertainties affect only a few nuclei near $s$-process branchings. The $s$-process in rotating metal-poor stars shows quantitatively different uncertainties and key reactions, although the qualitative characteristics are similar. We confirmed that our results do not significantly change at different metallicities for fast rotating massive stars in the very low metallicity regime. We highlight which of the identified key reactions are realistic candidates for improved measurement by future experiments.

## Key claim
The dominant source of uncertainty in final s-process abundances for massive stars is neutron capture rates, while β-decay rate uncertainties affect only a few nuclei near s-process branchings; the qualitative characteristics of uncertainty are similar between rotating and non-rotating models, but rotating metal-poor stars show quantitatively different key reactions.

## Method
The authors used a Monte Carlo framework (PizBuin) coupled with a nuclear reaction network of 943 isotopes, performing 10,000 iterations where neutron capture and β-decay rates for nuclei beyond iron (Z>26) were varied simultaneously within temperature-dependent uncertainty factors. They adopted a single-zone thermodynamic trajectory extracted from a 25 solar mass stellar model to mimic the average conditions during helium-core and carbon-shell burning, and considered five metallicity values from solar (Z_m = 1.4 × 10⁻²) to very metal-poor (Z_m = 1.0 × 10⁻⁵) with five rotation strengths characterized by primary ¹⁴N mass fractions from 0 to 0.01.

## Key numbers
- Solar metallicity model (z0r0) used Z_m = 1.4 × 10⁻² as the standard case for the weak s-process
- Very metal-poor model (z4) used Z_m = 1.0 × 10⁻⁵
- Maximum rotation case (r4) used primary ¹⁴N mass fraction X(¹⁴N) = 0.01
- 10,000 Monte Carlo iterations were performed for each variation case
- 900 reactions were varied in total (510 neutron capture and 390 weak reactions)
- Temperature-dependent uncertainty for β-decay used u_g.s. = 1.3 and u_e.s. = 10 for experimentally unknown rates
- For unmeasured neutron capture rates and reactions on thermally excited states, uncertainty factor u = 2 was adopted
- Correlation threshold for identifying key reactions was set at |r_cor| > 0.65

## Context
This work extends the Monte Carlo uncertainty framework previously applied to the γ-process in massive stars by Rauscher et al. (2016) to the s-process, specifically the weak component (nuclei up to A ~ 90) and the enhanced s-process in rotating metal-poor stars. The study builds on prior work by Frischknecht et al. (2012, 2016) showing that rotation-induced mixing in very metal-poor stars enhances production of heavier nuclei like barium, and by Pignatari et al. (2010) on the weak s-process in massive stars. The results identify specific neutron capture reactions that are realistic candidates for improved measurement by future experiments, enabling more precise predictions for galactic chemical evolution models.
