# Structural effects of $^{34}$Na in the $^{33}$Na(n,$γ)^{34}$Na radiative capture reaction

> The path towards the production of \textit{r}-process seed nuclei follows a course where the neutron-rich light and medium mass nuclei play a crucial role. The neutron capture rates for these exotic nuclei could dominate over their $α$-capture rates, thereby enhancing their abundances at or near the drip line. We calculate the radiative neutron capture cross-section for the $^{33}$Na(n,$γ)^{34}$Na reaction via the Coulomb dissociation of $^{34}$Na as it undergoes elastic breakup on $^{208}$Pb when directed at a beam energy of 100 MeV/u using the entirely quantum mechanical theory of finite range distorted wave Born approximation upgraded to incorporate deformation effects. The non-resonant one neutron radiative capture cross-section for $^{33}$Na(n,$γ)^{34}$Na is calculated and is found to increase with increasing deformation of $^{34}$Na. An analytic scrutiny of the capture cross-section with neutron separation energy as a parameter is also done at different energy ranges. The calculated reaction rate is compared with the rate of the $^{33}$Na($α$,n)$^{36}$Al reaction (deduced from the Hauser-Feshbach theory), and is found to be significantly higher below a temperature of $T_9 = 2$. Further, at the equilibrium temperature of $T_9 = 0.62$, the rate for the neutron capture had a small but non-negligible dependence on the structural parameters of $^{34}$Na. In addition, this neutron capture rate exceeded that of the $α$-capture reaction by orders of magnitude, indicating that the $α$-process should not break the (n,$γ$) \textit{r-}process path at the $^{33}$Na isotope, thus, effectively pushing the abundance of sodium isotopes towards the neutron drip line.

## Key claim

The radiative neutron capture cross-section for \(^{33}\text{Na}(n,\gamma)^{34}\text{Na}\) increases with increasing quadrupole deformation of \(^{34}\text{Na}\), and the reaction rate exceeds that of the competing \(^{33}\text{Na}(\alpha,n)^{36}\text{Al}\) reaction by orders of magnitude at the equilibrium temperature \(T_9 = 0.62\), but this conclusion depends on the assumed ground-state spin-parity and one-neutron separation energy of \(^{34}\text{Na}\), which are not fully established.

## Method

The authors used the finite-range distorted wave Born approximation (FRDWBA) upgraded to include deformation effects, applied to the Coulomb dissociation of \(^{34}\text{Na}\) on a \(^{208}\text{Pb}\) target at a beam energy of 100 MeV/u. From the calculated relative energy spectrum of the \(^{33}\text{Na} + n\) breakup, they extracted the photodisintegration cross-section assuming E1 multipole dominance, then used the principle of detailed balance to obtain the non-resonant radiative neutron capture cross-section for the reverse reaction. The reaction rate was computed by integrating the capture cross-section over a Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution and compared with the \(\alpha\)-capture rate from the Hauser-Feshbach theory using the NON-SMOKER code.

## Key numbers

- The E1 contribution to the total Coulomb dissociation cross-section of \(^{34}\text{Na}\) on \(^{208}\text{Pb}\) at 100 MeV/u is 1.743 barns, while the E2 contribution is only 0.167 millibarns.
- The one-neutron separation energy of \(^{34}\text{Na}\) is fixed at 0.17 MeV when not treated as a parameter, based on Gaudefroy (year not given in text).
- The \(^{33}\text{Na}(n,\gamma)^{34}\text{Na}\) reaction rate is significantly higher than the \(^{33}\text{Na}(\alpha,n)^{36}\text{Al}\) rate below a temperature of \(T_9 = 2\).
- At the equilibrium temperature \(T_9 = 0.62\), the neutron capture rate exceeds the \(\alpha\)-capture rate by orders of magnitude.
- The capture cross-section increases with deformation; for a spherical \(^{34}\text{Na}\) (\(\beta_2 = 0.0\)) and \(S_n = 0.17\) MeV, the cross-section shows a trend reversal at a center-of-mass energy of approximately 0.75 MeV, where lower binding energies give higher cross-sections below that energy.

## Context

This work extends the study of neutron-rich sodium isotopes in the "island of inversion" (\(N = 20-30\)), where deformation effects are significant, following earlier Coulomb dissociation studies on halo nuclei such as \(^{9}\text{Li}\) and \(^{16}\text{N}\) using FRDWBA. The results contradict the possibility that \(\alpha\)-capture could break the \((n,\gamma)\) r-process path at \(^{33}\text{Na}\), as suggested by the Hauser-Feshbach calculations of the \(^{33}\text{Na}(\alpha,n)^{36}\text{Al}\) reaction. The findings enable more reliable r-process network calculations for sodium isotopes near the neutron drip line, particularly for the production of \(^{35}\text{Na}\), the most abundant sodium isotope at the drip line according to Terasawa (year not given in text).
