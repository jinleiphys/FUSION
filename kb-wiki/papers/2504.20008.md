# The shape of differential radial flow $v_0(p_T)$, not its zero-crossing, carries physical information

> Radial flow, a key collective phenomenon in heavy-ion collisions, manifests itself through event-by-event fluctuations of transverse-momentum ($p_{\mathrm{T}}$) spectra. The $p_{\mathrm{T}}$-differential radial flow observable, $v_0(p_{\mathrm{T}})$, was introduced to quantify local spectral-shape fluctuations, but it is unavoidably influenced by global multiplicity fluctuations. Using the HIJING model, we show that different event-activity definitions for centrality classification and different spectral normalization schemes generate a constant vertical offset in $v_0(p_{\mathrm{T}})$ without altering its shape. This offset reflects the impact of residual volume/centrality fluctuations rather than genuine dynamical radial flow fluctuations. Accordingly, only the shape of $v_0(p_{\mathrm{T}})$, or equivalently its derivative $dv_0(p_{\mathrm{T}})/dp_{\mathrm{T}}$, carries physical information about radial-flow dynamics; its zero crossing does not. Practical implications include the need to vertically align measurements from different experiments before comparison, thereby removing normalization ambiguities when constraining QGP properties.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the differential radial flow observable \(v_0(p_T)\) is meaningful only up to an arbitrary constant offset arising from residual volume or centrality fluctuations; consequently, only the shape of \(v_0(p_T)\) (or its derivative \(dv_0(p_T)/dp_T\)) carries physical information about radial-flow dynamics, while the zero-crossing point does not.

## Method

The authors used the HIJING model to generate Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.02\) TeV, treating heavy-ion collisions as a superposition of independent \(pp\) collisions with no genuine collective radial flow. They systematically varied three analysis ingredients: the event-activity definition for centrality classification (using charged-particle multiplicity in different pseudorapidity ranges or number of participating nucleons from the Glauber model), the \(p_T\) range for spectral normalization, and the reference \(p_T\) range for calculating the average transverse momentum \([p_T]\). Two-particle observables were evaluated using the two-subevent method with pseudorapidity gaps of 0 and 3 to assess short-range non-flow correlations.

## Key numbers

- The zero-crossing point of \(v_0(p_T)\) occurs near \(\langle [p_T] \rangle \approx 1\) GeV when using normalized spectra, but shifts systematically with the choice of normalization range.
- The offset \(\Delta v_0\) between \(v_0'(p_T)\) (using unnormalized spectra) and \(v_0(p_T)\) (using normalized spectra) is constant across the entire \(p_T\) range from 0 to 10 GeV.
- For \(\sqrt{N_a}\Delta v_0\), the product remains approximately constant across centrality, consistent with independent source scaling \(\propto 1/\sqrt{N_s}\).
- The correlation coefficient \(\rho(N_a, [p_T]_b)\) is close to zero or slightly positive when event-activity particles are separated from analysis particles, but becomes negative when they overlap (e.g., \(N_{\mathrm{ch}}\) in \(|\eta|<2.5\)).

## Context

This work extends recent experimental measurements of differential radial flow fluctuations by ATLAS (2025) and ALICE (2025), which confirmed the collective and long-range nature of radial flow through factorization relations. The paper resolves a fundamental ambiguity in separating global multiplicity fluctuations from spectral shape fluctuations, a decomposition previously used in principal component analysis approaches by Mazeliauskas (2015) and Gardim (2020). The findings enable meaningful comparisons of \(v_0(p_T)\) measurements across experiments with different \(p_T\) acceptances (ALICE: \(p_T>0.1\) GeV, CMS: \(p_T>0.3\) GeV, ATLAS: \(p_T>0.5\) GeV) by requiring vertical alignment to a common normalization, and they motivate the use of the derivative observable \(dv_0(p_T)/dp_T\) as an unambiguous alternative for constraining QGP transport properties such as bulk viscosity, as identified by Parida (2024) and ATLAS (2025).
