# Microscopic study of the Shell Structure evolution in isotopes of light to middle mass range Nuclides

> The shell structure of even-even isotopes in Si, S, Ar and Ca has been analysed. The theoretical calculations of shell closure parameter $D_{n} (N)$ and the differential variation of the two-neutron separation energy $dS_{2n} (Z, N )$ are carried out within the framework of Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov theory. Calculations are carried out for different skyrme forces and their sensitivity has been tested. Same nuclides are studied by employing the relativistic mean field aproach based on meson exchange and point coupling models. Theoretically calculated estimates are in good agreement with the recently available experimental data which fortifies signature of shell closure at N = 14 and 20 in case of Si, N = 14, 20 and 28 in S and N=20 and 28 in Ar and Ca isotopes.

## Key claim

The paper claims that theoretical calculations using Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) and Relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) models reproduce experimental data well enough to confirm shell closures at N = 14 and 20 in Si, N = 14, 20, and 28 in S, and N = 20 and 28 in Ar and Ca isotopes, but the abstract glosses over the fact that the N = 28 shell closure in Si is found to be collapsed (disappeared) in the isotope \(^{42}\)Si, and that the N = 28 peak in S is described as a "small peak" indicating only a weak shell closure.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent mean-field calculations using the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) theory with Skyrme effective interactions (parameterizations SLY5, UNEDF0, and SKM*) and the Relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) model with density-dependent meson exchange (DD-ME2) and point coupling (DD-PC1 and DD-PCX) effective interactions, employing a separable pairing interaction. They computed binding energies, the shell closure parameter \(D_n(N)\), and the differential variation of the two-neutron separation energy \(dS_{2n}(Z,N)\) for even-even isotopes of Si, S, Ar, and Ca, and compared these with experimental data from Wang (2017).

## Key numbers

- The absolute value of the difference between experimental and theoretical binding energy per nucleon (\(\Delta E\)) for RHB theory ranges from 0.01 to 0.26 MeV for Si isotopes, 0.01 to 0.25 MeV for S isotopes, 0.01 to 0.17 MeV for Ar isotopes, and 0.01 to 0.11 MeV for Ca isotopes.
- The absolute value of \(\Delta E\) for HFB theory ranges from 0.01 to 0.22 MeV for Si isotopes, 0.01 to 0.23 MeV for S isotopes, 0.01 to 0.21 MeV for Ar isotopes, and 0.01 to 0.15 MeV for Ca isotopes.
- The \(^{34}\)Si nucleus exhibits a relatively high \(E(2^+_1) = 3.33\) MeV, indicating a subshell gap at Z = 14 with N = 20.
- The \(E(2^+_1)\) energies decrease from 1.4 MeV to 0.74 MeV in \(^{36}\)Si to \(^{42}\)Si, suggesting the collapse of the N = 28 shell closure in \(^{42}\)Si.
- In the S isotopic chain, the \(E(4^+_1)\) energy is 5.17 MeV at N = 14 in \(^{30}\)S, decreasing to 4.46 MeV at N = 16 in \(^{32}\)S, substantiating the N = 14 shell closure.

## Context

This work extends the study of shell structure evolution in neutron-rich exotic nuclei, building on experimental investigations of shell closures and their collapse in light to middle-mass nuclides, such as the weakening of the N = 28 shell closure reported by Bastin (2007) for Si isotopes and the identification of N = 32 as a new magic number in Ca isotopes by Huck (1985). The calculations enable further testing of nuclear density functionals (Skyrme and relativistic) against experimental data for isotopes near the dripline, and they support the interpretation of new magic numbers (e.g., N = 14) due to spin-orbit partner filling, as seen in \(^{28}\)Si, \(^{30}\)S, \(^{32}\)Ar, and \(^{34}\)Ca.
