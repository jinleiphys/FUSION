# Computing Quark and Gluon Distribution Functions for Very Large Nuclei

> We argue that the distribution functions for quarks and gluons are computable at small x for sufficiently large nuclei, perhaps larger than can be physically realized. For such nuclei, we argue that weak coupling methods may be used. We show that the computation of the distribution functions can be recast as a many body problem with a modified propagator, a coupling constant which depends on the multiplicity of particles per unit rapidity per unit area, and for non-abelian gauge theories, some extra media dependent vertices. We explicitly compute the distribution function for gluons to lowest order, and argue how they may be computed in higher order.

## Key claim

The authors argue that for sufficiently large nuclei (perhaps larger than physically realizable), quark and gluon distribution functions at small Bjorken x become computable using weak coupling methods, because the parton density per unit area per unit rapidity becomes large enough that the strong coupling constant is small. The hedge is that this condition may not hold for realistic nuclei, making the computation a theoretical laboratory rather than a directly applicable result.

## Method

The authors set up the problem in light cone gauge using light cone Hamiltonian methods, treating valence quarks as recoilless static sources of color charge moving along the light cone. For a large nucleus with uniform transverse density, they integrate out the fluctuating color charges of the valence quarks using a Gaussian approximation for the density of states, generating an effective field theory with a modified gluon propagator and media-dependent vertices. They then compute the gluon distribution function to lowest order in weak coupling.

## Key numbers

- The gluon distribution function per unit transverse area is given by: (1/πR²) dN/d³k = α_s μ² (N_c² - 1) / (π² k⁺ k_t²), where μ² is the average color charge squared per unit area.
- The parameter μ² is estimated as μ² = 1.1 A^{1/3} fm⁻², based on the quark density n_q = 3 (A/πR²) ~ 0.8 A^{1/3} fm⁻² and average quark charge squared ⟨Q²⟩ = (4/3) g².
- The kinematic region of validity for the lowest order computation is Λ²_QCD << α_s ρ << k_t² << ρ, where ρ = (1/πR²) dN/dy is the parton density per unit area per unit rapidity.
- The transverse momentum resolution limit for treating color charges classically is q_t² << (200 MeV)² A^{1/3}, corresponding to q_t ~ 1-2 GeV for realistic large nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the Weiszacker-Williams approximation from QED to QCD for the case of large nuclei, showing that the gluon distribution function takes the same 1/(x k_t²) form to lowest order. The authors build on light cone Hamiltonian methods developed by Soper (1977) and Brodsky (1973), and on Mueller's (1990) work on parton densities at small x. The paper enables a systematic weak coupling expansion for computing parton distribution functions in a dense nuclear environment, though the authors note that higher orders may generate non-trivial x-dependence and that infrared screening may require non-perturbative treatment, analogous to problems in finite temperature QCD.
