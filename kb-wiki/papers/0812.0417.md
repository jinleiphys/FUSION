# Fermionic Molecular Dynamics for nuclear dynamics and thermodynamics

> A new Fermionic Molecular Dynamics (FMD) model based on a Skyrme functional is proposed in this paper. After introducing the basic formalism, some first applications to nuclear structure and nuclear thermodynamics are presented

## Key claim
The paper proposes a new Fermionic Molecular Dynamics (FMD) model based on a Skyrme functional, but the results are presented as first applications with limited scope: ground-state calculations are restricted to light even-even nuclei, and the thermodynamic method is demonstrated only for a single very small system (⁴He) due to extremely long equilibration times.

## Method
The FMD model represents the nuclear system as a single Slater determinant of Gaussian wave packets, with the widths of the single-particle states included as dynamical variational parameters. The equations of motion are derived from a time-dependent variational principle using a Skyrme energy functional (SLy4, SLy5, or SGII parametrisations). For ground states, the static limit is solved by gradient minimization; for thermodynamics, the nucleus is coupled to a harmonic-oscillator thermometer via a contact interaction, and the temperature is extracted from time averages of the thermometer energy over trajectories of approximately 50000 fm/c.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state energy of ⁴He: -26.156 MeV (FMD with SLy4), compared to -26.7 MeV (HF) and -28.296 MeV (experimental)
- Ground-state energy of ¹⁶O: -127.9 MeV (FMD with SLy4), compared to -128.485 MeV (HF) and -127.62 MeV (experimental)
- Ground-state energy of ⁴⁰Ca: -344.98 MeV (FMD with SLy4), compared to -344.22 MeV (HF) and -342.056 MeV (experimental)
- Giant Monopole Resonance frequency for ¹²C with SLy5: ω_FMD = 24.017 MeV, compared to experimental value ω_Exp = 21.9 ± 0.3 MeV (Youngblood et al.)
- Equilibration time for thermodynamic calculations: approximately 50000 fm/c

## Context
This work extends the Fermionic Molecular Dynamics approach originally developed by Drozdz, Caurier, and Feldmeier (1990s) by incorporating modern Skyrme functionals (SLy and SGII parametrisations) instead of the earlier effective interactions. The model enables simultaneous study of nuclear structure and thermodynamics within a unified framework, though the thermodynamic method is currently limited to very light systems due to computational cost. The results for ground-state energies are comparable to standard Hartree-Fock calculations, while the thermodynamic approach opens the possibility of extracting caloric curves for finite nuclei, with calculations for heavier systems and different multipolarities stated as work in progress.
