# Observability of dynamical tides in merging eccentric neutron star binaries

> While dynamical tides only become relevant during the last couple of orbits for circular inspirals, orbital eccentricity can increase their impact during earlier phases of the inspiral by exciting tidal oscillations at each close encounter. We investigate the effect of dynamical tides on the orbital evolution of eccentric neutron star binaries using post-Newtonian numerical simulations and construct an analytic stochastic model that reproduces the numerical results. Our study reveals a strong dependence of dynamical tides on the pericenter distance, with the fractional energy transferred to dynamical tides over that dissipated in gravitational waves (GWs) exceeding $\sim1\%$ at separations $r_\mathrm{p}\lesssim50$ km for large eccentricities. We demonstrate that the effect of dynamical tides on orbital evolution can manifest as a phase shift in the GW signal. We show that the signal-to-noise ratio of the GW phase shift can reach the detectability threshold of $8$ with a single aLIGO detector at design densitivity for eccentric neutron star binaries at a distance of $40$ Mpc. This requires a pericenter distance of $r_\mathrm{p0}\lesssim68$ km ($r_\mathrm{p0}\lesssim76$ km) at binary formation with eccentricity close to $1$ for a reasonable tidal deformability and f-mode frequency of $500$ and $1.73$ kHz ($700$ and $1.61$ kHz), respectively. The observation of the phase shift will enable measuring the f-mode frequency of neutron stars independently from their tidal deformability, providing significant insights into neutron star seismology and the properties of the equation of state. We also explore the potential of distinguishing between equal-radius and twin-star binaries, which could provide an opportunity to reveal strong first-order phase transitions in the nuclear equation of state.

## Key claim

Dynamical tides in eccentric neutron star binaries can produce a gravitational-wave phase shift with a signal-to-noise ratio reaching the detectability threshold of 8 for a single aLIGO detector at design sensitivity, but only for binaries formed with pericenter distances below approximately 68–76 km and eccentricity close to 1, depending on the specific tidal deformability and f-mode frequency values.

## Method

The authors performed post-Newtonian numerical simulations of eccentric neutron star binary evolution including dynamical tides, using the 2.5PN leading-order gravitational radiation reaction and conservative 1PN and 2PN corrections. They also constructed an analytic stochastic model based on the Lagrangian formalism of Flanagan and Racine (2007) to calculate the energy transferred to f-mode oscillations and the corresponding orbital phase shift, expanding the tidal field in Hansen coefficients to handle arbitrary eccentricities.

## Key numbers

- Fractional energy transferred to dynamical tides relative to gravitational-wave energy dissipation exceeds approximately 1% at pericenter separations r_p less than about 50 km for large eccentricities.
- Signal-to-noise ratio of the gravitational-wave phase shift reaches the detectability threshold of 8 with a single aLIGO detector at design sensitivity for eccentric neutron star binaries at a distance of 40 Mpc.
- Detectability requires a pericenter distance at binary formation r_p0 less than about 68 km for a tidal deformability of 500 and f-mode frequency of 1.73 kHz, or r_p0 less than about 76 km for a tidal deformability of 700 and f-mode frequency of 1.61 kHz, with eccentricity close to 1.
- The f-mode frequency of 1.4 solar mass neutron stars is expected to fall between 1.5 kHz and 2.5 kHz based on prior work by Pradhan et al. (2022).
- The tidal deformability of 1.4 solar mass neutron stars has been constrained to Lambda(1.4 Msun) less than 720 by the observation of GW170817 (LIGO Scientific Collaboration, 2018).

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Yang et al. (2018, 2019) who derived energy transfer for dynamical tides in the small eccentricity limit, and by Vick and Lai (2019) who solved the orbital equations numerically for high-eccentricity orbits and found phase shifts an order of magnitude larger than the small-eccentricity case. The paper provides the first investigation of the stochastic evolution of dynamical tides from orbit-to-orbit and the corresponding phase shift, and demonstrates that measuring dynamical tides could enable independent measurement of the f-mode frequency and tidal deformability, potentially distinguishing between equal-radius and twin-star binaries to reveal strong first-order phase transitions in the nuclear equation of state.
