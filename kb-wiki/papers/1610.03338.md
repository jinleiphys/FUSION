# Across the deconfinement

> The deconfinement transition at vanishing chemical potential can be reliably studied by lattice simulations and its general features are by now well known. On the contrary, what happens at finite density is still largely unknown and we will review the results obtained in the last year regarding the dependence, for small density, of the (pseudo)critical temperature on the baryonic chemical potential.

## Key claim

The curvature of the QCD (pseudo)critical line at small baryon chemical potential is insensitive to the value of the strange quark chemical potential within numerical accuracy, and no evidence is found for a critical endpoint close to the real chemical potential axis.

## Method

The authors review lattice QCD results for the curvature of the critical line, obtained using both the Taylor expansion method (evaluating derivatives at zero chemical potential) and the analytic continuation method (simulating at imaginary chemical potential and fitting the results). They compare determinations from multiple groups, focusing on observables related to chiral symmetry restoration (chiral condensate and chiral susceptibility), and examine the dependence on the strange quark chemical potential setup and on the isospin breaking constraint relevant for heavy-ion collisions.

## Key numbers

- The curvature κ of the critical line is defined by T_c(μ_B)/T_c = 1 − κ (μ_B/T_c)^2 + O(μ_B^4/T_c^4), where T_c is the (pseudo)critical temperature at μ_B=0.
- For heavy-ion collisions, the electric charge is related to the baryon number by ⟨Q⟩ = r ⟨B⟩ with r ≃ 0.4.
- Strangeness neutrality near the transition implies μ_s ≈ μ_u/4.
- The Roberge-Weiss transition takes place at μ_u = μ_d = μ_s = iπT/3.

## Context

This work reviews and compares recent lattice QCD determinations of the curvature of the QCD critical line, extending the seminal studies by Kaczmarek (2011) and Endrodi (2011). The results enable a quantitative comparison with the freeze-out curve extracted from heavy-ion collisions by Cleymans (2005) and Becattini, and provide constraints on the possible location of a critical endpoint in the QCD phase diagram.
