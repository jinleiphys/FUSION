# Neutron Star Mass-Radius Constraints of the Quiescent Low-mass X-ray Binaries X7 and X5 in the Globular Cluster 47 Tuc

> We present Chandra ACIS-S sub-array observations of the quiescent neutron star low-mass X-ray binaries X7 and X5 in the globular cluster 47 Tuc. The large reduction in photon pile-up compared to previous deep exposures enables a substantial improvement in the spectroscopic determination of the neutron star radius and mass of these neutron stars. Modeling the thermal emission from the neutron star surface with a non-magnetized hydrogen atmosphere and accounting for numerous sources of uncertainties, we obtain for the neutron star in X7 a radius of $R=11.1^{+0.8}_{-0.7}$ km for an assumed stellar mass of $M=1.4$ M$_{\odot}$ (68% C.L.). We argue, based on astrophysical grounds, that the presence of a He atmosphere is unlikely for this source. Due to eclipses and variable absorption, the quiescent low-mass X-ray binary X5 provides less stringent constraints, leading to a radius of $R=9.6^{+0.9}_{-1.1}$ km, assuming a hydrogen atmosphere and a mass of $M=1.4$ M$_{\odot}$. When combined with all other existing spectroscopic radius measurements, these measurements strongly favor radii in the 9.9-11.2 km range for a ~1.5 M$_{\odot}$ neutron star and point to a dense matter equation of state that is somewhat softer than the nucleonic ones that are consistent with laboratory experiments at low densities.

## Key claim
The neutron star in X7 has a radius of \(R=11.1^{+0.8}_{-0.7}\) km (68% C.L.) assuming a mass of \(M=1.4\) M\(_{\odot}\) and a hydrogen atmosphere, but this result depends on the assumption that a helium atmosphere is unlikely on astrophysical grounds; the X5 measurement yields a less stringent radius of \(R=9.6^{+0.9}_{-1.1}\) km under the same assumptions, with the caveat that eclipses and variable absorption reduce reliability.

## Method
The authors analyzed new Chandra ACIS-S sub-array observations of the quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries X7 and X5 in the globular cluster 47 Tuc, totaling 181 ks acquired between 2014 September and 2015 February with a 1/8 sub-array (0.4 s frame time) to reduce photon pile-up to about 1%. They modeled the thermal emission using non-magnetized hydrogen atmosphere models (nsatmos) in XSPEC, accounting for interstellar absorption with the tbabs model (Wilms 2000 abundances), and included a pile-up correction model; for X7, they also tested helium atmosphere models (nsx). They incorporated a 3% systematic uncertainty for instrument calibration and used a distance of \(d = 4.53^{+0.08}_{-0.04}\) kpc derived from a weighted mean of 22 distance measurements to 47 Tuc.

## Key numbers
- X7 neutron star radius: \(R = 11.1^{+0.8}_{-0.7}\) km (68% C.L.) for an assumed mass of \(M = 1.4\) M\(_{\odot}\) with a hydrogen atmosphere.
- X5 neutron star radius: \(R = 9.6^{+0.9}_{-1.1}\) km (68% C.L.) for an assumed mass of \(M = 1.4\) M\(_{\odot}\) with a hydrogen atmosphere.
- Combined with other spectroscopic radius measurements, the results favor radii in the 9.9–11.2 km range for a \(\sim 1.5\) M\(_{\odot}\) neutron star.
- Distance to 47 Tuc: \(d = 4.53^{+0.08}_{-0.04}\) kpc, based on a distance modulus of \((m-M)_0 = 13.26\)–\(13.32\).
- Hydrogen column density toward 47 Tuc: \(N_{\rm H} = 1.3 \times 10^{20}\) cm\(^{-2}\).
- No temperature variation greater than 0.9% (90% confidence) was observed for X7 between 2000/2002 and 2014/2015 epochs.
- Upper limit of 4% to any change in normalization for X7 at 90% confidence.

## Context
This work extends prior radius measurements of X7 and X5 from Heinke (2006) and Heinke (2003), which were affected by strong photon pile-up (15% in the 2002 full-frame data) and yielded a radius of \(14.5^{+1.8}_{-1.6}\) km for X7 (90% C.L.). The new results are in excellent agreement with the combined qLMXB and thermonuclear burster analysis of Ozel (2015), which found a radius range of 10.1–11.1 km (95% C.L.) but excluded X5 and X7 due to data quality concerns. The measurements point to a dense matter equation of state that is somewhat softer than nucleonic ones consistent with laboratory experiments at low densities, and they enable tighter combined constraints on the neutron star mass-radius relation when merged with other spectroscopic radius measurements.
