# The virial expansion of attractively interacting Fermi gases in 1D, 2D, and 3D, up to fifth order

> The virial expansion characterizes the high-temperature approach to the quantum-classical crossover in any quantum many-body system. Here, we calculate the virial coefficients up to the fifth-order of Fermi gases in 1D, 2D, and 3D, with attractive contact interactions, as relevant for a variety of applications in atomic and nuclear physics. To that end, we discretize the imaginary-time direction and calculate the relevant canonical partition functions. In coarse discretizations, we obtain analytic results featuring relationships between the interaction-induced changes $Δb_3$, $Δb_4$, and $Δb_5$ as functions of $Δb_2$, the latter being exactly known in many cases by virtue of the Beth-Uhlenbeck formula. Using automated-algebra methods, we push our calculations to progressively finer discretizations and extrapolate to the continuous-time limit. We find excellent agreement for $Δb_3$ with previous calculations in all dimensions and we formulate predictions for $Δb_4$ and $Δb_5$ in 1D and 2D. We also provide, for a range of couplings,the subspace contributions $Δb_{31}$, $Δb_{22}$, $Δb_{41}$, and $Δb_{32}$, which determine the equation of state and static response of polarized systems at high temperature. As a performance check, we compare the density equation of state and Tan contact with quantum Monte Carlo calculations, diagrammatic approaches, and experimental data where available. Finally, we apply Padé and Padé-Borel resummation methods to extend the usefulness of the virial coefficients to approach and in some cases go beyond the unit-fugacity point.

# The virial expansion of attractively interacting Fermi gases in 1D, 2D, and 3D, up to fifth order

## Key claim

The authors calculate virial coefficients up to fifth order for attractively interacting Fermi gases in 1D, 2D, and 3D using a Trotter-Suzuki factorization of imaginary time, extrapolating from coarse discretizations to the continuous-time limit; the results for Delta b_3 agree with previous calculations in all dimensions, while predictions for Delta b_4 and Delta b_5 in 1D and 2D are formulated, but the method's accuracy degrades at strong coupling where the virial expansion's radius of convergence becomes very small.

## Method

The authors discretize the imaginary-time direction using a Trotter-Suzuki factorization of the Boltzmann weight, calculating canonical partition functions for up to five particles in 1D, 2D, and 3D with attractive contact interactions. For coarse discretizations (k=1,2), they obtain analytic results expressing Delta b_3, Delta b_4, and Delta b_5 as functions of Delta b_2, which is exactly known from the Beth-Uhlenbeck formula; they then push calculations to progressively finer discretizations (k up to 21 for Delta b_3, up to 12 for Delta b_4, and up to 9 for Delta b_5) using automated-algebra methods and extrapolate to the continuous-time limit.

## Key numbers

- Delta b_3 calculated up to k=21, Delta b_4 up to k=12, and Delta b_5 up to k=9 before extrapolation to k -> infinity
- In 1D, the two-body binding energy is epsilon_B = 1/a_0^2, where a_0 is the 1D scattering length; the dimensionless coupling is lambda_1 = 2*sqrt(beta)/a_0
- In 2D, the dimensionless coupling is lambda_2^2 = beta*epsilon_B, where epsilon_B is the two-body binding energy
- The noninteracting virial coefficients for nonrelativistic fermions in d spatial dimensions are b^(0)_n = (-1)^(n+1) n^(-(d+2)/2)
- At k=1, Delta b_3 = -2^(1-d/2) * Delta b_2 and Delta b_4 = 2(3^(-d/2) + 2^(-d-1)) * Delta b_2 + 2^(1-d/2)(2^(-d/2-1) - 1) * (Delta b_2)^2
- At k=2, Delta b_2 = tilde_C + 2^(d/2-1) * tilde_C^2, where tilde_C = (e^(beta*g_d/2) - 1)*ell^d/lambda_T^d
- The 1D Beth-Uhlenbeck result: Delta b_2^(1D,exact) = -1/(2*sqrt(2)) + e^(lambda_1^2/4)/(2*sqrt(2)) * [1 + erf(lambda_1/2)]
- The 2D Beth-Uhlenbeck result: Delta b_2^(2D,exact) = e^(lambda_2^2) - integral_0^infinity (dy/y) * 2*e^(-lambda_2^2*y^2)/(pi^2 + 4*ln^2(y))

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical lattice approximation of Shill and Drut (2018), which calculated Delta b_3 and Delta b_4 at leading order, and Hou et al. (2019), which extended to Delta b_7 at coarse discretization. The authors compare their results with quantum Monte Carlo calculations from Shill and Drut (2018) for 1D and 2D, with diagrammatic approaches for 2D from the virial2D literature, and with complex-Langevin results from Loheac, Drut, and Braun (2018) for polarized 1D systems. The calculated virial coefficients enable determination of the high-temperature equation of state, Tan contact, and isothermal compressibility for unpolarized and polarized systems, and the authors apply Pade and Pade-Borel resummation methods to extend the virial expansion beyond the unit-fugacity point.
