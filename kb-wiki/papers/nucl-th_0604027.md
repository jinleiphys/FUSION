# New features in the stability and fission decay of superheavy Thorium isotopes

> Superheavy isotopes are highly neutron rich nuclei in the vicinity of neutron drip-line, stabilized by shell effect against the instability due to repulsive component of nuclear force, analogous to superheavy elements similarly stabilized against Coulomb instability. Here we discuss the stability and fission decay properties of such nuclei in the $^{254}$Th region and show that they are stable against $α$ and fission decay and have $β$-decay life time of several tens of seconds. In particular, the $^{254}$Th nucleus has a low fission barrier and unusally large barrier width. This makes it an ideal thermally fissile nucleus, if formed by means of a thermal neutron, like other known nuclei such as $^{233}$U, $^{235}$U, $^{239}$Pu in this actinide region. It shows a new mode of fast fission decay, which may be termed as multifragmentation fission, in which in addition to two heavy fragments large number of scission neutrons are simultaneously produced. Its likely synthesis during the r-process nucleosynthesis will have important bearing on steller evolution, and here in the laboratory, it has great potential in energy production.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the superheavy neutron-rich isotope ²⁵⁴Th (Z=90, N=164) is stable against alpha decay and spontaneous fission, has a beta-decay half-life of several tens of seconds, and exhibits a new fission mode called multifragmentation fission. The key qualifier is that this stability and the unusual fission properties depend on the nucleus being a doubly magic shell-stabilized system near the neutron drip-line, with the fission barrier being unusually wide despite its low height.

## Method

The authors used three theoretical approaches to predict nuclear masses and decay properties: the infinite nuclear matter (INM) model, relativistic mean field (RMF) theory, and the Howard-Moller (HM) mass model. Alpha-decay half-lives were calculated using the Viola-Seaborg empirical formula with Q-values from the mass models, while beta-decay half-lives were estimated using the Seeger-Fowler-Clayton formula. Fission barrier widths were extracted from systematics of experimental fission half-lives and barrier heights for known actinide nuclei, then extrapolated to ²⁵⁴Th using a linear relationship between barrier height and width.

## Key numbers

- ²⁵⁴Th has a fission barrier height of 3.57 MeV, with an unusually large barrier width (hbar-omega close to zero, approximately 0.05 MeV)
- The N/Z ratio of ²⁵⁴Th is 1.82, compared to 1.54 for the doubly closed shell superheavy element ²⁹⁸X₁₁₄
- Alpha-decay half-lives for Th isotopes in the N=160-170 region exceed 10²⁰ years for all three mass models
- Beta-decay half-life of ²⁵⁴Th is several tens of seconds
- The neutron separation energy S_n exceeds the fission barrier B_f for Th isotopes in the neutron range N=162-170, making them thermally fissile
- For comparison, ²³²Th has a fission barrier of 7.40 MeV and hbar-omega of 0.415 MeV
- The multifragmentation fission mode produces 2 to 3 additional scission neutrons per fission, on top of the normal multiplicity of 2.3 to 2.5 neutrons from fragment emission

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier prediction (Satpathy, Patra, and Choudhury, 2004) of new islands of stability around magic numbers N=164, Z=90; N=150, Z=78; and N=100, Z=62, which were identified using INM, RMF, and Strutinsky shell correction calculations. The paper contrasts these superheavy isotopes with superheavy elements along the beta-stability line (such as those around Z=114-126 studied by other groups), noting that the isotopes are stabilized against repulsive nuclear force instability rather than Coulomb instability. The finding that ²⁵⁴Th is thermally fissile places it alongside known nuclei like ²³³U, ²³⁵U, and ²³⁹Pu, and the predicted multifragmentation fission mode has implications for r-process nucleosynthesis in stellar evolution and potential applications in energy production.
