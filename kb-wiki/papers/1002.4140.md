# The information content of a new observable: the case of the nuclear neutron skin

> We address two questions pertaining to the uniqueness and usefulness of a new observable: (i) Considering the current theoretical knowledge, what novel information does new measurement bring in? (ii) How can new data reduce uncertainties of current theoretical models? We illustrate these points by studying the radius of the neutron distribution of a heavy nucleus, a quantity related to the equation of state for neutron matter that determines properties of nuclei and neutron stars. By systematically varying parameters of two theoretical models and studying the resulting confidence ellipsoid, we quantify the relationships between the neutron skin and various properties of finite nuclei and infinite nuclear matter. Using the covariance analysis, we identify observables and pseudo-observables that correlate, and do not correlate, with the neutron skin. By adding the information on the neutron radius to the pool of observables determining the energy functional, we show how precise experimental determination of the neutron radius in $^{208}$Pb would reduce theoretical uncertainties on the neutron matter equation of state.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a covariance analysis within a single theoretical model (nuclear Density Functional Theory) can quantify the information content of a new observable, specifically the neutron radius in 208Pb, but the authors caution that even the best statistical analysis cannot eliminate systematic errors due to incorrect theoretical assumptions, as shown by the model dependence between the Skyrme functional SV-min and the relativistic mean-field functional RMF-delta-t.

## Method

The authors performed a least-squares regression analysis using two nuclear energy density functionals: the Skyrme functional SV-min with 14 coupling constants and the relativistic mean-field functional RMF-delta-t. They computed the covariance matrix and Pearson correlation coefficients between the neutron form factor in 208Pb and various observables including nuclear matter properties, neutron skins, dipole polarizability, giant resonance energies, and low-energy dipole strength. To assess the impact of a precise neutron radius measurement, they generated a new functional SV-min-Rn by adding the neutron radius in 208Pb with adopted errors of 0.02 fm and 0.05 fm to the fit observables.

## Key numbers

- The Pearson correlation coefficient between neutron skin and dipole polarizability in 208Pb is c_AB = 0.98 (strong correlation)
- The Pearson correlation coefficient between neutron skin in 208Pb and effective nucleon mass m*/m is c_AB = 0.11 (weak correlation)
- The experimental value of dipole polarizability alpha_D for 208Pb is 13.3 +/- 1.4 fm^2/MeV, with a Lorentz fit value of 13.6 fm^2/MeV
- SV-min predicts neutron skin in 208Pb of r_skin = 0.170(0.037) fm; a new fit with increased weight on neutron-rich nuclei gives r_skin = 0.191(0.024) fm
- Adding the neutron radius in 208Pb with 0.4% uncertainty (0.02 fm) reduces uncertainties on isovector indicators by about a factor of two
- SV-min nuclear matter properties: incompressibility K = 222 MeV, symmetry energy a_sym = 30.7 MeV, effective nucleon mass m*/m = 0.95
- RMF-delta-t nuclear matter properties: K = 197 MeV, a_sym = 38 MeV, m*/m = 0.59

## Context

This work extends prior correlation studies of neutron skin with nuclear matter properties by authors such as Tondeur (1984), Furnstahl (2002), and Chen (2005), but uses a more systematic covariance analysis rather than explicit variation of selected properties. The paper directly addresses the upcoming PREX experiment at Jefferson Laboratory, which aims to determine the neutron radius of 208Pb with 1% precision. The methodology enables future work to assess the information content of any new observable within parameterized theoretical frameworks, including fits of nucleon-nucleon forces and shell-model matrix elements.
