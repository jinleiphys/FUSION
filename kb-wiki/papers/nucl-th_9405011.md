# Generator Coordinate Calculations for the Breathing-Mode Giant Monopole Resonance in Relativistic Mean Field Theory

> The breathing-mode giant monopole resonance (GMR) is studied within the framework of the relativistic mean-field theory using the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM). The constrained incompressibility and the excitation energy of isoscalar giant monopole states are obtained for finite nuclei with various sets of Lagrangian parameters. A comparison is made with the results of nonrelativistic constrained Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations and with those from Skyrme RPA calculations. In the RMF theory the GCM calculations give a transition density for the breathing mode, which resembles much that obtained from the Skyrme HF+RPA approach and also that from the scaling mode of the GMR. From the systematic study of the breathing-mode as a function of the incompressibility in GCM, it is shown that the GCM succeeds in describing the GMR energies in nuclei and that the empirical breathing-mode energies of heavy nuclei can be reproduced by forces with an incompressibility close to $K = 300$ MeV in the RMF theory.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) applied within relativistic mean-field (RMF) theory can reproduce empirical breathing-mode giant monopole resonance (GMR) energies in heavy nuclei, but only when using forces with a nuclear matter incompressibility close to \(K = 300\) MeV; this conclusion is hedged by the fact that the relationship between GMR energies and incompressibility in RMF theory differs from the Skyrme approach, and that the surface incompressibility in RMF is not a simple function of the bulk incompressibility.

## Method

The authors performed constrained RMF calculations for the closed-shell nuclei \(^{16}\)O, \(^{40}\)Ca, \(^{90}\)Zr, and \(^{208}\)Pb, using the quadrupole operator \(r^2\) as a constraint to generate Slater determinants \(\Phi(q)\) at different values of the Lagrange multiplier \(q\). These determinants were used as generator functions in the relativistic GCM, where the Hill-Wheeler integral equation was solved numerically to obtain the ground state and excited GMR states. The constrained incompressibility \(K_C(A)\) was extracted from the curvature of the energy surface around equilibrium, and the GCM excitation energies were compared with nonrelativistic constrained Skyrme Hartree-Fock results and with experimental data.

## Key numbers

* The nuclear matter incompressibility values for the Lagrangian parameter sets used are: NL1 with \(K_{NM} = 211.7\) MeV, NL-SH with \(K_{NM} = 355.0\) MeV, NL2 with \(K_{NM} = 399.2\) MeV, HS with \(K_{NM} = 545\) MeV, and L1 with \(K_{NM} = 626.3\) MeV; a schematic force NL-S1 was constructed with \(K_{NM} = 296\) MeV.
* The mass dependence coefficient \(c\) in \(\Delta E_1 = c A^{-1/3}\) for \(^{208}\)Pb is 69.1 MeV for NL1, 79.6 MeV for NL-S1, 93.6 MeV for NL-SH, 104.9 MeV for NL2, 97.0 MeV for HS, and 126.2 MeV for L1.
* The GCM ground-state \(rms\) radius for \(^{16}\)O differs from the RMF value by about 0.0025 fm, the largest difference among the nuclei studied.
* The \(rms\) radius in the first excited GMR state is larger than the ground-state value by about 0.15 fm in \(^{16}\)O and by only 0.015 fm in \(^{208}\)Pb.
* The transition density for \(^{208}\)Pb with force NL-SH shows a node at 6 fm for protons and at about 6.4 fm for neutrons.

## Context

This work extends earlier constrained RMF calculations for the GMR (Stoitsov, Ring, and Sharma, 1994) by incorporating the GCM to go beyond the mean-field approximation and include correlations. It contrasts with the nonrelativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus RPA approach, where forces like SkM\(^*\) reproduce the GMR energy of \(^{208}\)Pb but overestimate energies for medium-heavy nuclei such as \(^{90}\)Zr by 1-2 MeV (Blaizot, 1980; Treiner, 1981). The results enable future systematic studies of the GMR across the periodic table within RMF theory and provide a framework for extracting the nuclear matter incompressibility from finite-nucleus data, though the authors note that empirical GMR data for light nuclei remain uncertain.
