# Comprehensive Table of Calculated Huff Factors

> We present a systematic calculation of the Huff factor for nuclei with atomic numbers ($ Z $) in the range of $ 6 \leq Z \leq 94 $. The Huff factor quantifies the increase in the partial lifetime of the decay-in-orbit (DIO) of the muonic atom and serves as an essential correction factor for extracting the nuclear muon capture rate from the measured lifetimes of the muonic atom. However, previous calculations typically provided only the atomic number dependence and neglected isotope dependence -- an assumption whose reliability had not been examined, despite its importance for a comprehensive understanding of the nuclear muon capture rate. In this work, we calculate the Huff factor using nuclear charge distributions obtained from a fully self-consistent microscopic nuclear structure model that incorporates pairing and deformation effects. The resulting Huff factors exhibit a monotonic decrease with increasing $ Z $, while the isotope dependence is found to be small. Our results also show good agreement with previous calculations, supporting the reliability of the present framework. The comprehensive set of Huff factors presented here constitutes the first unified values currently available and will serve as a basis for future evaluations of muon nuclear data.

## Key claim

The paper provides the first systematic calculation of Huff factors across a wide range of nuclei (Z = 6 to 94) using a fully self-consistent microscopic nuclear structure model, and finds that while the Huff factor decreases monotonically with increasing atomic number, the isotope dependence is small enough (within approximately 0.1% even for heavy nuclei) that the previous assumption of a constant Huff factor for a given element is sufficiently accurate for stable nuclei.

## Method

The authors calculated Huff factors by first obtaining proton density distributions from a self-consistent Hartree-Fock plus Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (HF+BCS) calculation in three-dimensional Cartesian coordinates, incorporating pairing and deformation effects using the SkM* effective interaction. They then derived nuclear charge distributions by convolving the spherically averaged proton densities with the proton electric form factor, and solved the Dirac equation to obtain muon and electron wave functions in the resulting Coulomb potential. Finally, they computed the electron energy spectrum from decay-in-orbit (DIO) using quantum electrodynamics and integrated it to obtain the Huff factor, truncating the partial wave expansion at a cutoff chosen to keep truncation error below 0.002%.

## Key numbers

* The positive muon lifetime used is 2.1969811(22) microseconds, from the Particle Data Group (2024).
* The target numerical precision for calculated Huff factors is 0.1%, matching the typical 1% experimental uncertainty in total muonic atom lifetimes.
* For carbon-12, contributions up to |kappa| ≤ 46 are required to achieve truncation error below 0.1%; for lead-208, contributions up to |kappa| ≤ 5 suffice.
* The electron-mass correction function f(m_e/m_mu) evaluates to approximately 0.99981.
* For aluminum-27, the present calculation gives a Huff factor of 0.9935, in excellent agreement with the value of 0.9934 reported by Czarnecki et al. (2011).
* The Fermi coupling constant used is G_F = 1.166 × 10^{-5} GeV^{-2}.

## Context

This work extends the earlier Huff factor calculations by Suzuki et al. (1987), which provided values only as a function of atomic number Z and neglected isotope dependence, and by Watanabe et al. (1993), which used a two-parameter Fermi function for the nuclear charge density. The present results enable a unified evaluation of nuclear muon capture rates from measured muonic atom lifetimes, and will be incorporated into an updated nuclear data evaluation by Iwamoto et al. (2025) as part of establishing a comprehensive muon nuclear data library.
