# Pion abundance and entropy in the hydrodynamical description of relativistic nuclear collisions

> We show that a hydrodynamical model with continuous particle emission instead of sudden freeze out can explain both the strange particle abundances and pion abundance from NA35 without extra assumption (e.g., sequential freeze out, modified equation of state, sudden plasma hadronization,...). In this scenario, the observation of a larger pion abundance is natural and does not imply a higher initial entropy and early plasma phase.

## Key claim

A hydrodynamical model with continuous particle emission (rather than sudden freeze-out) can simultaneously reproduce both the strange particle abundances and the pion abundance measured by NA35 without requiring extra assumptions such as sequential freeze-out, modified equation of state, or sudden plasma hadronization; the larger observed pion abundance does not necessarily imply higher initial entropy or an early quark-gluon plasma phase.

## Method

The authors implement a hydrodynamical model with longitudinal boost invariance and no transverse expansion, using a two-component distribution function (free plus interacting particles) where the invariant momentum distribution is computed via a covariant divergence integral over the whole fluid history rather than the Cooper-Frye freeze-out surface integral. The escape probability for continuous emission is calculated using a Glauber formula with a cutoff at probability 0.5, and the fluid evolution is solved for a hadronic gas with strangeness neutrality imposed. The allowed initial conditions (temperature T0 and baryochemical potential μb0) are determined by fitting NA35 midrapidity data for Λ, anti-Λ, KS0, negative hadrons h-, and p-pbar.

## Key numbers

- Allowed initial conditions for the hadronic gas: T0 ≈ 185 MeV, μb0 ≈ 100 MeV, with strangeness saturation factor γs = 1.3
- Using a more realistic equation of state, T0 decreases by 10-15% to 155-165 MeV, compatible with QCD lattice phase transition temperatures
- Continuous emission predictions vs. freeze-out predictions for NA35 data: Λ = 1.05 (continuous) vs. 0.98 (freeze-out), experimental 1.26 ± 0.22 (p⊥ > 0.5 GeV)
- Anti-Λ = 0.28 (continuous) vs. 0.46 (freeze-out), experimental 0.44 ± 0.16 (p⊥ > 0.5 GeV)
- p-pbar = 3.6 (continuous) vs. 1.52 (freeze-out), experimental 3.2 ± 0.4
- h- = 27.6 (continuous) vs. 16 (freeze-out), experimental 27 ± 1
- KS0 = 1.27 (continuous) vs. 1.08 (freeze-out), experimental 1.30 ± 0.22 (p⊥ > 0.62 GeV)
- For a massless pion gas, the relationship s = [1 + (3β - α)/(4(1+α))] × 3.6 nπ holds, with s ≤ 3.6 nπ for all radii, times, and initial temperatures

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing "pion underprediction" problem in standard hydrodynamical models with sudden freeze-out, first noted by Davidson et al. (1992) in NA35 data and emphasized by Letessier et al. (1993, 1995) in analyses of WA85 strange particle ratios and EMU05 net charge data. Previous proposed solutions included sequential freeze-out (Cleymans et al., 1993), excluded volume corrections (Ritchie et al., 1997; Yen et al., 1997, 1998), non-zero pion chemical potential (Davidson et al., 1992; Yen et al., 1997), and equilibrated plasma undergoing sudden hadronization (Letessier et al., 1993, 1995, 1998). The continuous emission approach, developed by Grassi, Hama and Kodama (1995, 1996), provides a simpler explanation without extra assumptions and demonstrates that large pion yields do not necessarily indicate large entropy or QGP formation, contrary to the interpretation of Letessier et al. (1993, 1995). This enables more careful assessment of QGP signatures in relativistic heavy ion collisions and motivates further scrutiny of particle emission mechanisms in hydrodynamical descriptions.
