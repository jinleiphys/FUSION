# Scattering processes and resonances from lattice QCD

> The vast majority of hadrons observed in nature are not stable under the strong interaction, rather they are resonances whose existence is deduced from enhancements in the energy dependence of scattering amplitudes. The study of hadron resonances offers a window into the workings of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) in the low-energy non-perturbative region, and in addition, many probes of the limits of the electroweak sector of the Standard Model consider processes which feature hadron resonances. From a theoretical standpoint, this is a challenging field: the same dynamics that binds quarks and gluons into hadron resonances also controls their decay into lighter hadrons, so a complete approach to QCD is required. Presently, lattice QCD is the only available tool that provides the required non-perturbative evaluation of hadron observables. In this article, we review progress in the study of few-hadron reactions in which resonances and bound-states appear using lattice QCD techniques. We describe the leading approach which takes advantage of the periodic finite spatial volume used in lattice QCD calculations to extract scattering amplitudes from the discrete spectrum of QCD eigenstates in a box. We explain how from explicit lattice QCD calculations, one can rigorously garner information about a variety of resonance properties, including their masses, widths, decay couplings, and form factors. The challenges which currently limit the field are discussed along with the steps being taken to resolve them.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD, combined with the L眉scher finite-volume formalism, can extract resonance properties (masses, widths, decay couplings, and form factors) from the discrete energy spectrum of QCD eigenstates in a periodic box, but the approach is currently limited to systems below three-body thresholds and requires multiple volumes or moving frames to constrain the energy dependence of scattering amplitudes.

## Method

The authors review the L眉scher quantization condition, which relates the discrete finite-volume spectrum of QCD eigenstates to infinite-volume scattering amplitudes via a determinant equation involving the scattering matrix and a kinematic function encoding finite-volume effects. They describe how lattice QCD calculations compute correlation functions using operator bases that include single-hadron and hadron-hadron constructions, then extract energy levels from these correlators across multiple volumes and moving frames to map out the energy dependence of phase shifts and resonance pole positions.

## Key numbers

* For the sigma meson at heavier quark mass (m_pi ~ 391 MeV), a bound-state pole was found on the real energy axis at E* = 758(4) MeV, lying below the pipi threshold at 2 m_pi = 782 MeV
* The NPLQCD Collaboration determined elastic I=2 pipi scattering phase shifts at m_pi ~ 390 MeV using low-lying rest and boosted lattice spectra across multiple volumes
* The Hadron Spectrum Collaboration computed elastic I=1 pipi scattering (rho resonance) at m_pi ~ 391 MeV using three volumes: 16^3, 20^3, 24^3 (L ~ 2.0, 2.4, 2.9 fm) in all relevant irreps up to momentum P=[200]
* A calculation at m_pi ~ 236 MeV used a single 32^3 volume with multiple moving frames to determine the rho resonance pole position with tight constraints
* Chiral perturbation theory was found to describe the pipi scattering length up to surprisingly large pion masses, m_pi ~ 400 MeV
* The coupled-channel a0(980) study at m_pi ~ 391 MeV used 47 energy levels below the pi eta' threshold to constrain the two-channel pi eta, K anti-K scattering matrix

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of L眉scher (1986, 1990) on finite-volume quantization conditions and summarizes progress from multiple lattice QCD collaborations including NPLQCD, Hadron Spectrum Collaboration, and others. The techniques described enable the first serious lattice QCD determination of elastic I=0 pipi scattering (Briceno 2016) and coupled-channel systems such as pi K, eta K scattering (Dudek 2014, Wilson 2014) and pi eta, K anti-K scattering (Dudek 2016). The field is now moving toward the major challenge of three-particle systems, for which a complete finite-volume formalism is not yet in place, and toward applications to resonances with nonzero intrinsic spin and elastic form factors of unstable particles.
