# Density Functional Theory (DFT) for atomic nuclei: a simple introduction

> The present contribution does not aim at replacing the huge and often excellent literature on DFT for atomic nuclei, but tries to provide an updated introduction to this topic. The goal would be, ideally, to help a fresh M.Sc. or Ph.D. student (or a researcher from other fields) to become acquainted with some basic concepts, and then move to the specialized textbooks or papers with some ability for orienteering. We first introduce the basics of DFT, and show the difference with the "naive" mean-field theory, that is doomed to fail as a model even in the simple case of uniform nuclear matter. We introduce the Energy Density Functionals (EDFs) that are used in nuclear structure, with few examples of their applications. The concepts of symmetry breaking and restoration are briefly discussed. We also include an introduction to the time-dependent extension of DFT that, so far, has been implemented essentially only in the adiabatic approximation and has been applied mainly to the study of nuclear vibrations. With this material, we hope that any reader is able to deal with the texts that go deeper into each of the topics, having understood that DFT is probably the best compromise in nuclear structure theory between simplicity, accuracy, and broad range of applicability.

# Density Functional Theory (DFT) for atomic nuclei: a simple introduction

## Key claim

This pedagogical review argues that Density Functional Theory, rather than naive Hartree-Fock with density-independent forces, is the viable theoretical framework for nuclear structure because no effective interaction has been found that simultaneously reproduces bulk nuclear properties (saturation, masses, radii) and spectroscopic properties (effective mass, level density) without introducing an explicit density dependence in the Hamiltonian, which is only consistent if treated as a generator of an energy functional.

## Method

The paper provides a nonrelativistic introduction to DFT for atomic nuclei, first reviewing the Hohenberg-Kohn theorem and Kohn-Sham scheme from electronic DFT, then contrasting the nuclear case with the electron gas. It derives Hartree-Fock equations for uniform nuclear matter using plane-wave single-particle states, calculates the energy per particle for Gaussian and Yukawa interactions with exchange operators, and demonstrates that density-independent forces cannot reproduce empirical saturation properties or the empirical effective mass range of 0.7-1. The paper then introduces the Gogny finite-range force and Skyrme zero-range functionals as examples of nuclear Energy Density Functionals (EDFs), and briefly discusses symmetry breaking, restoration, and the time-dependent extension in the adiabatic approximation for nuclear vibrations.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of ordinary nuclei: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³
- Nuclear surface thickness: approximately 0.6 fm
- Interparticle distance 2r₀: approximately 2.4 fm
- Fermi momentum at saturation density: k_F ≈ 1.33 fm⁻¹
- Volume term a_V from Bethe-Weizsäcker formula: approximately -16 MeV
- Energy per particle of symmetric nuclear matter at equilibrium: e₀ = -16 MeV
- Effective mass m*/m empirical range: approximately 0.7-1
- Effective mass from simple Hartree-Fock: between 0.2 and 0.4
- Range parameter μ for Brink-Boeker forces allowing saturation: approximately 0.5 fm < μ < 1.5 fm
- Number of new nuclei discovered per year in the last decade: approximately 20-30

## Context

This review extends the pedagogical tradition of nuclear DFT introductions such as Bender, Heenen, and Reinhard (2003) and the lecture notes in Lecture Notes in Physics volumes 641 and 879, while contrasting with ab initio methods based on chiral Effective Field Theory that remain too demanding for heavy nuclei or highly excited states (as noted by Hagen et al., 2010). The paper enables students and researchers from other fields to access specialized textbooks and current literature on nuclear EDFs, including the Gogny force introduced by Dechargé and Gogny (1980) and Skyrme functionals, and provides the conceptual foundation for understanding time-dependent DFT applications to nuclear vibrations.
