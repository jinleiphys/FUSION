# Proton superconductivity in pasta phases in neutron star crusts

> In the so-called pasta phases predicted to occur in neutron star crusts, protons are able to move easily over large distances because the nuclear matter regions are extended in space. Consequently, electrical currents can be carried by protons, an effect not possible in conventional crystalline matter with isolated nuclei. With emphasis on the so-called lasagna phase, which has sheet-like nuclei, we describe the magnetic properties of the pasta phases allowing for proton superconductivity. We predict that these phases will be Type II superconductors and we calculate the energy per unit length of a flux line, which is shown to be strongly anisotropic. If, as seems likely, the pasta structure is imperfect, flux lines will be pinned and matter will behave as a good electrical conductor and flux decay times will be long. We describe some possible astrophysical manifestations of our results.

# Proton superconductivity in pasta phases in neutron star crusts

## Key claim

The pasta phases in neutron star crusts, particularly the lasagna phase with sheet-like nuclei, are predicted to be Type II proton superconductors, but this conclusion depends on the assumption that the pasta structure is imperfect (disordered), which would cause flux lines to be pinned and result in long flux decay times. The authors hedge that the stability of pasta phases themselves depends on the nucleon-nucleon interaction and is not universally accepted.

## Method

The authors develop a hydrodynamic description of superfluid proton currents in the lasagna phase, using coarse-grained averages of the condensate phase to avoid modeling local currents on the scale of individual nuclear sheets. They solve the Maxwell equation coupled to the anisotropic superfluid current density tensor to calculate the magnetic field distribution and energy per unit length of quantized flux lines, treating the flux line both perpendicular to and at arbitrary angles relative to the lasagna sheets. The calculation uses the London approximation, valid because the penetration depth (~81 fm) is much larger than both the sheet spacing (~44 fm) and the proton coherence length (~8 fm).

## Key numbers

- Sheet thickness of lasagna: approximately 27 fm for surface tension 0.1 MeV/fm², proton density in nuclear matter n_pi = n_s/20, and filling fraction u = 0.5
- Lattice spacing of periodic structure: approximately 44 fm for u = 0.5, σ = 0.1 MeV fm⁻², and n_pi = 0.05 n_s
- Proton superconducting coherence length in bulk matter: approximately 8.3 fm × (1 MeV / Δ_p) × (n_pi / (n_s/20))^(1/3)
- London penetration depth: approximately 81 fm × (n_pi / (n_s/20))^(-1/2)
- Lower critical field H_c1 for θ = 0: approximately 1.27 × 10^14 Oe × (n_pp^(s⊥) / (n_s/40)) × ln(λ_⊥ / ξ_p), with λ_⊥/ξ_p ~ 10 giving H_c1 ~ 3 × 10^14 Oe
- Upper critical field H_c2: of order 3 × 10^16 Oe, similar to bulk matter in the liquid interior

## Context

This work extends the study of pasta phases first proposed by Ravenhall, Pethick, and Wilson (1983) and later studied for neutron star conditions by Lorenz, Ravenhall, and Pethick (1993) and Oyamatsu (1993). It builds on earlier work on collective modes in pasta phases by Martin and Urban (2014), Kobyakov and Pethick (2014, 2019), and Durel and Urban (2018), and on the possibility of flux lines mentioned by Kobyakov (2018). The paper contradicts the argument by Pons, Viganò, and Rea (2013) that pasta phases are poor electrical conductors, instead arguing that proton superconductivity makes them good conductors with long flux decay times. The results enable future calculations of magnetic field evolution in neutron star crusts and could help explain the observed magnetic field decay in isolated neutron stars.
