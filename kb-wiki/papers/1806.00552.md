# Bayesian approach to model-based extrapolation of nuclear observables

> The mass, or binding energy, is the basis property of the atomic nucleus. It determines its stability, and reaction and decay rates. Quantifying the nuclear binding is important for understanding the origin of elements in the universe. The astrophysical processes responsible for the nucleosynthesis in stars often take place far from the valley of stability, where experimental masses are not known. In such cases, missing nuclear information must be provided by theoretical predictions using extreme extrapolations. Bayesian machine learning techniques can be applied to improve predictions by taking full advantage of the information contained in the deviations between experimental and calculated masses. We consider 10 global models based on nuclear Density Functional Theory as well as two more phenomenological mass models. The emulators of S2n residuals and credibility intervals defining theoretical error bars are constructed using Bayesian Gaussian processes and Bayesian neural networks. We consider a large training dataset pertaining to nuclei whose masses were measured before 2003. For the testing datasets, we considered those exotic nuclei whose masses have been determined after 2003. We then carried out extrapolations towards the 2n dripline. While both Gaussian processes and Bayesian neural networks reduce the rms deviation from experiment significantly, GP offers a better and much more stable performance. The increase in the predictive power is quite astonishing: the resulting rms deviations from experiment on the testing dataset are similar to those of more phenomenological models. The empirical coverage probability curves we obtain match very well the reference values which is highly desirable to ensure honesty of uncertainty quantification, and the estimated credibility intervals on predictions make it possible to evaluate predictive power of individual models.

## Key claim

Bayesian Gaussian process (GP) and Bayesian neural network (BNN) emulators of two-neutron separation energy residuals can significantly reduce the rms deviation from experiment for global nuclear mass models, but GP offers better and much more stable performance than BNN, and the resulting rms deviations on the testing dataset are similar to those of more phenomenological models.

## Method

The authors constructed emulators of S2n residuals and credibility intervals using Bayesian Gaussian processes and Bayesian neural networks, applied to 10 global nuclear Density Functional Theory models and two phenomenological mass models. They trained the statistical models on 537 experimental S2n values from the AME2003 mass evaluation for even-even nuclei, then tested predictions on 55 new data points from AME2016 and 4 points from JYFLTRAP. Extrapolations were carried out toward the 2n dripline, and empirical coverage probability curves were computed to assess the honesty of uncertainty quantification.

## Key numbers

- Training dataset: 537 S2n values from AME2003 for even-even nuclei
- Testing dataset: 55 new data points from AME2016 evaluation plus 4 points from JYFLTRAP
- Rms mass deviation for HFB-31 model: 0.56 MeV (best overall agreement with experimental masses)
- Rms mass deviation range for Skyrme-DFT models: from 1.5 MeV (UNEDF0) to approximately 5 MeV (SLy4)
- Rms mass deviation range for covariant-DFT models: between 2 and 3 MeV
- Rms mass deviation for FRDM-2012 and HFB-24: around 0.6 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to assess predictive power of mass models (Sobiczewski 2014) and to use Bayesian methods for nuclear mass predictions (Utama 2016, 2017, 2018; Bertsch 2017; Zhang 2017; Niu 2018). The study differs from earlier work by testing extrapolations to nuclei outside the training domain rather than testing on interior points randomly removed from the training sample. The results enable quantified predictions for astrophysical r-process simulations where experimental masses are unavailable.
