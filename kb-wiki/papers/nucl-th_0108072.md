# A Quasi-Classical Model of Intermediate Velocity Particle Production in Asymmetric Heavy Ion Reactions

> The particle emission at intermediate velocities in mass asymmetric reactions is studied within the framework of classical molecular dynamics. Two reactions in the Fermi energy domain were modelized, $^{58}$Ni+C and $^{58}$Ni+Au at 34.5 MeV/nucleon. The availability of microscopic correlations at all times allowed a detailed study of the fragment formation process. Special attention was paid to the physical origin of fragments and emission timescales, which allowed us to disentangle the different processes involved in the mid-rapidity particle production. Consequently, a clear distinction between a prompt pre- equilibrium emission and a delayed aligned asymmetric breakup of the heavier partner of the reaction was achieved.

# A Quasi-Classical Model of Intermediate Velocity Particle Production in Asymmetric Heavy Ion Reactions

## Key claim
Classical molecular dynamics simulations of $^{58}$Ni+C and $^{58}$Ni+Au at 34.5 MeV/nucleon can distinguish two distinct mechanisms for mid-rapidity particle production: a prompt pre-equilibrium emission of light charged particles and a delayed aligned asymmetric breakup of the heavier reaction partner, though the model overestimates intermediate mass fragment production from the quasi-projectile due to its inability to accurately handle slow nuclear deexcitation processes.

## Method
The authors used a classical molecular dynamics (CMD) model with a spherical two-body interaction potential including Coulomb interactions, employing the M-set parameters that give a compressibility around 250 MeV and equilibrium density of 0.16 fm$^{-3}$. They simulated 8400 events for $^{58}$Ni+$^{12}$C at 34.5 MeV/nucleon with impact parameters $b \le 4.5$ fm, and 3400 events for $^{58}$Ni+$^{197}$Au at 34.5 MeV/nucleon with $5$ fm $\le b \le 8$ fm, integrating equations of motion up to 2000 fm/c. Fragment recognition used the minimum spanning tree in energy space (MSTE) clusterization algorithm, and emission times were extracted by tracing back the dynamical evolution of each asymptotic cluster.

## Key numbers
- Beam energy: 34.5 MeV/nucleon for both $^{58}$Ni+C and $^{58}$Ni+Au reactions
- Compressibility of the nuclear equation of state: approximately 250 MeV
- Equilibrium density: $\rho_0 = 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$
- Binding energy at equilibrium: $E(\rho_0) = -16$ MeV/A
- Cutoff radius for nuclear interaction: $r_c = 5.4$ fm
- Integration time step: $t_{int} = 0.02$ fm/c, achieving energy conservation of 0.01%
- Final integration time: $t = 2000$ fm/c
- Number of events analyzed: 8400 for $^{58}$Ni+$^{12}$C ($b \le 4.5$ fm), 3400 for $^{58}$Ni+$^{197}$Au ($5$ fm $\le b \le 8$ fm)
- Three emission time scales identified: $t_e < 150$ fm/c (prompt), $150$ fm/c $\le t_e < 600$ fm/c (delayed), $t_e \ge 600$ fm/c (statistical evaporation)
- Experimental total detected charge selection: at least 24 units for $^{58}$Ni+Au, at least 34 units for $^{58}$Ni+C

## Context
This work extends classical molecular dynamics approaches originally introduced by Lenk et al. (Pandharipande, 1992) for testing the Vlasov-Nordheim approximation, and applies them to the problem of intermediate mass fragment production at mid-rapidity in the Fermi energy domain (20-100 MeV/nucleon). The study addresses controversies between statistical approaches (which attribute mid-rapidity emission to Coulomb interactions) and dynamical descriptions using Landau-Vlasov transport models (Lukasik, 1997; Eudes, 2000) or quantum molecular dynamics (Colonna, 1995; Nebauer, 1999). The findings support recent experimental observations of alignment and proximity effects in neck breakup of mass asymmetric collision partners (Gingras, 2001), and suggest that dynamical effects cannot be disregarded even for light systems, contrary to purely statistical interpretations.
