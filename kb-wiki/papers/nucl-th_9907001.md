# Theoretical predictions of residues cross sections of superheavy elements

> Dynamical reaction theories are reviewed for synthesis of superheavy elements. Characteristic features of formation and surviving are discussed with reference to possible incident channels. Theoretical predictions are presented on favorable incident channels and on optimum energies for synthesis of Z=114.

# Theoretical predictions of residues cross sections of superheavy elements

## Key claim
The paper predicts that for synthesizing Z=114 superheavy elements, the optimum balance between formation probability and survival probability depends critically on the neutron richness of the compound nucleus, with neutron-rich isotopes having smaller neutron separation energies that enable faster cooling and thus higher survival probabilities even at higher excitation energies.

## Method
The authors calculated formation probabilities using a multi-dimensional Langevin equation in three-dimensional space (elongation, mass-asymmetry, and fragment deformation), with sub-barrier penetration treated via WKB approximation using Coulomb and nuclear proximity potentials. Survival probabilities were computed using statistical theory for particle evaporation combined with a one-dimensional Smoluchowski equation for fission dynamics under strong friction. Excitation functions of evaporation residue cross sections were then obtained by combining these two probabilities for various incident channels leading to Z=114 compound nuclei.

## Key numbers
- For Z=114 compound nuclei, formation probabilities increase by several orders of magnitude as mass asymmetry increases, with the most asymmetric channels having the largest formation probabilities.
- The incident channels $^{250}$Cm + $^{44}$Ar and $^{244}$Pu + $^{48}$Ca are predicted to have evaporation residue cross sections more than 1 pb, which is stated as the limit for measurements.
- Survival probabilities for Z=114 decrease very quickly as excitation energy E* increases, with a strong mass-number dependence: larger mass numbers (more neutron-rich) are favorable for surviving due to smaller neutron separation energies Bn.
- The paper references that the Dubna group recently observed an event possibly related to synthesis of Z=114 using the $^{244}$Pu + $^{48}$Ca channel (Oganessian 1999).

## Context
This work extends the dynamical reaction theory approach previously developed by Abe et al. (1997) and Aritomo et al. (1997, 1999) for superheavy element synthesis, applying it specifically to Z=114. It builds on the cold fusion reactions that succeeded in synthesizing elements up to Z=112 at GSI (Hofmann 1996), and contrasts with hot/warm fusion approaches. The paper enables quantitative predictions for selecting optimal projectile-target combinations and incident energies for future superheavy element experiments, particularly encouraging exploration of more neutron-rich targets and projectiles.
