# Chiral Symmetry in Nuclei

> The impact of chiral symmetry on nuclear physics is discussed in the context of recent advances in the few-nucleon systems and of dimensional power counting. The tractability of few-nucleon calculations, illustrated by very recent solutions for $A = 2-6$, is shown to follow from power counting based on chiral Lagrangians. The latter predicts the suppression of $N$-body forces, as originally shown by Weinberg. Isospin violation in the nuclear force is similarly analyzed using the results of van Kolck, and this is shown to be consistent with results from the Nijmegen phase-shift analysis. Conventional $ρ- ω$ and $π- η$ mixing models with on-shell mixing strength are not inconsistent with naive power counting. Meson-exchange currents calculated in chiral perturbation theory are in good agreement with experiment.

## Key claim

Chiral symmetry and dimensional power counting, as originally formulated by Weinberg (1990), predict that many-body nuclear forces are progressively suppressed, making few-nucleon calculations tractable; the paper argues that this suppression is the reason nuclear physics is calculable, rather than a separate empirical fact.

## Method

The paper reviews recent numerical solutions of the Schrödinger equation for nuclei with A = 2–6 using realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials (specifically the Argonne V18 potential) plus a weak three-nucleon force adjusted to fit the triton binding energy. It then interprets the resulting binding energies and the hierarchy of many-body forces in terms of Weinberg's chiral power-counting rules, which assign a dimension ν to each nuclear operator based on the number of nucleons, loops, and chiral Lagrangian vertices. The same power-counting framework is applied to isospin-violating forces using effective Lagrangians constructed by van Kolck (1995) from quark-mass differences and electromagnetic effects.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energies for A = 2–6 calculated with Argonne V18 plus a three-nucleon force: deuteron −2.22 MeV (fitted), triton −8.47(2) MeV (fitted), ⁴He −28.3(1) MeV, ⁵He(3/2⁻) −26.5(2) MeV, ⁵He(1/2⁻) −25.7(2) MeV, ⁶Li(1⁺) −32.4(9) MeV; experimental values are −2.22, −8.48, −28.3, −27.2, −25.8, and −32.0 MeV respectively.
- Average potential energy per pair from the NN force: ∼20 MeV/pair; per triplet from the three-nucleon force: ∼1 MeV/triplet; upper limit on four-nucleon force: <0.1 MeV/quartet.
- Pion masses extracted from Nijmegen phase-shift analysis: m(π±) = 139.4(10) MeV, m(π⁰) = 135.6(13) MeV.
- Pion decay constant: fπ = 92.4 MeV; large-mass scale Λ ∼ 1 GeV.
- Average nuclear momentum scale: p̄c ∼ 100–150 MeV, comparable to the pion mass; Fermi momentum in nuclear matter: ħkFc = 260 MeV.
- ³He–³H mass difference: 764 keV, of which 648(4) keV from Coulomb interaction and 45(5) keV from other electromagnetic effects.
- T=1 s-wave scattering lengths: np −23.7 fm, pp −17.3(4) fm, nn −18.8(3) fm.

## Context

This talk extends the chiral perturbation theory framework for nuclear forces introduced by Weinberg (1990) and the power-counting analysis of isospin violation by van Kolck (1995). It interprets the recent numerical few-body calculations of the Argonne group (1994) as quantitative confirmation of the predicted hierarchy of many-body forces. The work enables systematic inclusion of chiral constraints in nuclear potentials and meson-exchange currents, and opens the door to calculations for A = 7 and 8 systems with improved computers.
