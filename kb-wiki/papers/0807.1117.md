# The Schwinger mechanism revisited

> The vacuum persistence probability, $P_{vac}(t)$, for a system of charged fermions in a fixed, external, and spatially homogeneous electric field, was derived long ago by Schwinger; $w = -log[P_{vac}(t)]/ (V t)$ has often been identified as the rate at which fermion-antifermion pairs are produced per unit volume due to the electric field. In this paper, we separately compute exact expressions for both $w$ and for the rate of fermion-antifermion pair production per unit volume, $Γ$, and show that they differ. While $w$ is given by the standard Schwinger mechanism result $w$, an infinite series, the pair production rate, $Γ$, is just the first term of that series. Our calculation is done for a system with periodic boundary conditions in the $A_0=0$ gauge but the result should hold for any consistent set of boundary conditions. We discuss, the physical reason why the rates $w$ and $Γ$ differ.

## Key claim
The vacuum decay rate per unit volume, \(w\), and the actual fermion-antifermion pair production rate per unit volume, \(\Gamma\), are not equal; \(\Gamma\) is given by only the first term of the infinite series that defines \(w\), a distinction that holds for any consistent set of boundary conditions despite the common assumption that \(w\) itself gives the pair production rate.

## Method
The authors compute exact expressions for both the vacuum persistence probability and the pair production rate for charged fermions in a fixed, external, spatially homogeneous electric field, using periodic boundary conditions in the \(A_0=0\) gauge. They first analyze the (1+1)-dimensional case, where the Dirac equation reduces to a two-level system whose transition probability is given by the Landau-Zener formula, and then generalize to (3+1) dimensions by integrating over transverse momenta. The electric field is switched on at \(t=0\) and off at \(t=T\), with the large-\(T\) limit taken to isolate the steady-state Schwinger mechanism.

## Key numbers
- In (1+1) dimensions, the pair production rate per unit length per unit time is \(\Gamma^{1+1} = \frac{q E}{2 \pi} \exp\left(-\frac{\pi m^2}{q E}\right)\), while the vacuum decay rate per unit length is \(w^{1+1} = \frac{q E}{2 \pi} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n} \exp\left(-\frac{n \pi m^2}{q E}\right)\).
- In (3+1) dimensions, the pair production rate per unit volume is \(\Gamma = \frac{(q E)^2}{4 \pi^3} \exp\left(-\frac{\pi m^2}{q E}\right)\), while the vacuum decay rate per unit volume is \(w = \frac{(q E)^2}{4 \pi^3} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} \exp\left(-\frac{n \pi m^2}{q E}\right)\).
- In the strong-field limit, the ratio \(w/\Gamma\) approaches \(\zeta(2) = \pi^2/6 \approx 1.64\) in (3+1) dimensions, and more generally in \(d\) space-time dimensions the ratio approaches \(\zeta(d/2)\).
- In the massless limit in (1+1) dimensions, \(\Gamma^{1+1}_{m \to 0} = \frac{q E}{2 \pi}\) is finite, while \(w^{1+1}\) diverges.

## Context
This work revisits and clarifies the original Schwinger mechanism (Schwinger, 1951), which had long been interpreted as giving the pair production rate via the vacuum decay rate \(w\). The authors explicitly rederive and confirm the earlier result of Nikishov (date not given in text) that the actual pair production rate \(\Gamma\) is only the first term of the Schwinger series. The paper provides a physically transparent derivation using periodic boundary conditions and the Landau-Zener formalism, and it corrects a common misinterpretation found in textbooks such as Itzykson and Zuber (date not given). This distinction is exponentially small for weak fields but becomes significant for strong fields, and the results enable more accurate calculations of pair production in contexts such as QCD string breaking and black hole physics.
