# Nuclear aspects of the solar neutrino problem

> The present status of the nuclear reaction rates determining the solar neutrino flux is discussed. This includes the reaction rates for the two branching ratios of the three pp-chains involving the reactions 3He(3He,2p)4He and 3He(4He,gamma)7Be for the first branching, and 7Be(e-,nu_e)7Li and 7Be(p,gamma)7B for the second branching. Mainly we will concentrate on the basic nuclear reaction p + p -> D + e+ + nu_e of the pp-chains. We use a relativistic field theory model of the deuteron to calculate the low-energy cross section for this reaction. The theoretical prediction of the cross section obtained is about 2.9 times largerthan given in the conventional potential approach. The consequences of this new reaction rate for the solar neutrino problem will be presented and discussed.

# Nuclear aspects of the solar neutrino problem

## Key claim

Using a relativistic field theory model of the deuteron, the cross section for the fundamental p + p → D + e⁺ + νₑ reaction is predicted to be about 2.9 times larger than the value obtained from the conventional potential approach, with an estimated theoretical uncertainty of ±30%. This enhancement, if correct, would require a lower solar core temperature (T_c = 13.8⁻⁰·⁴⁺⁰·⁵ × 10⁶ K) to maintain solar luminosity, which in turn suppresses the high-energy neutrino fluxes but still leaves discrepancies with the chlorine and KAMIOKANDE experiments.

## Method

The authors compute the low-energy cross section for two-proton fusion using a relativistic field theory model of the deuteron constructed in analogy with the σ-model and the extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model. The amplitude is evaluated via one-nucleon loop diagrams, where the dominant contribution comes from the anomalous part of the AAV (axial-vector–axial-vector–vector) one-nucleon loop diagram, analogous to the Adler-Bell-Jackiw-Bardeen anomaly. The resulting reaction rate is then used in an alternative solar model (ASM) that adjusts the solar core temperature to satisfy the luminosity constraint, and the predicted neutrino fluxes for the gallium, chlorine, and KAMIOKANDE detectors are compared with experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The cross section for p + p → D + e⁺ + νₑ is enhanced by a factor of **2.9** relative to the conventional potential approach.
- The theoretical uncertainty of the relativistic field theory model is estimated at **±30%**.
- The predicted reaction rate at solar core temperature T_c = 15.5 × 10⁶ K is **3.44 × 10⁻⁴³ cm³ s⁻¹**.
- To reconcile the enhanced reaction rate with solar luminosity, the required solar core temperature is **T_c = 13.8⁻⁰·⁴⁺⁰·⁵ × 10⁶ K**.
- The experimental ¹⁵S₀ pp scattering length used is **a_S = (−17.1 ± 0.2) fm**.
- For the gallium detectors, the ASM predicts a total signal of **91.9⁻²·⁹⁺⁶·² SNU** compared to the experimental value of **77.1 ± 13.4 SNU** and the SSM value of **136.6 SNU**.
- For the chlorine detector, the ASM predicts **1.10⁻⁰·³³⁺⁰·⁸⁸ SNU** compared to the experimental **2.55 ± 0.35 SNU** and the SSM value of **9.30 SNU**.
- For KAMIOKANDE, the ASM predicts **0.37⁻⁰·¹⁹⁺⁰·⁶¹ × 10⁶ cm⁻² s⁻¹** compared to the experimental **2.73 ± 0.51 × 10⁶ cm⁻² s⁻¹** and the SSM value of **6.62 × 10⁶ cm⁻² s⁻¹**.
- The deviation of the ASM from experimental data is +3σ for gallium, −4σ for chlorine, and −5σ for KAMIOKANDE, compared to SSM deviations of +8σ, +19σ, and +8σ respectively.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing solar neutrino problem, where observed neutrino fluxes from the Homesteak chlorine detector, KAMIOKANDE, GALLEX, and SAGE are substantially lower than predictions from standard solar models (SSMs) such as those by Bahcall (1995) and Castellani (1997). The authors challenge the conventional assumption that the p + p reaction rate is known to within 3% from potential-model calculations (Bethe 1939, Bahcall 1992, Kamionkowski 1994) by proposing a relativistic field theory approach that yields a significantly larger cross section. The resulting alternative solar model reduces the discrepancy with gallium experiments but produces too few neutrinos for the chlorine and KAMIOKANDE detectors, and the authors note that the required 9% reduction in solar core temperature may conflict with helioseismological measurements of sound speed (Bahcall 1997, Christensen-Daalgaard 1996). This work enables further investigation into whether nuclear physics modifications alone can resolve the solar neutrino problem or whether new particle physics (e.g., neutrino oscillations) is required.
