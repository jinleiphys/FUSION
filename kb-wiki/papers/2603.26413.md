# Neural network enhanced Bayesian global analysis of relativistic heavy ion collisions

> We introduce a novel deep convolutional neural network (NN) -enhanced Bayesian global analysis of bulk observables in highest-energy heavy-ion collisions, using relativistic 2+1 D second-order viscous hydrodynamics with a dynamical freeze-out, and with perturbative QCD and saturation -based initial conditions from the event-by-event EKRT-model. Our analysis has 13+2 free parameters for the QCD-matter properties + initial state, which are constrained by the experimental data from $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC and $2.76$ TeV Pb+Pb, $5.02$ TeV Pb+Pb, and $5.44$ TeV Xe+Xe collisions at the LHC. We replace the computationally demanding hydrodynamical simulations by NNs, which predict bulk observables directly from the initial energy density profiles, event-by-event, and account for the QCD-matter properties. With the NN output, we train the Gaussian process emulators for obtaining centrality-class averaged observables and their uncertainties. The NNs reduce the computing time significantly, enabling us to include also statistics-hungry flow observables like $v_4$ and the normalized symmetric cumulant $NSC(4,2)$ in the analysis. In this paper, we demonstrate the feasibility of the NN based Bayesian global analysis. We find the data favoring a specific shear viscosity $η/s$ with a minimum-value plateau at temperatures $150\lesssim T \lesssim 230$ MeV, with $0.12 \lesssim (η/s)_{\mathrm{min}} \lesssim 0.18$. The bulk viscous coefficient $ζ/s$ is non-zero at $200\lesssim T \lesssim 300$ MeV. The Knudsen number at the freeze-out is $0.8-2.3$, while the ratio of the mean free path to the system size at freeze-out is in the range $0.3-1.2$, implying that the freeze-out indeed happens at the expected limit of the applicability of hydrodynamics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates the feasibility of using deep convolutional neural networks to replace computationally expensive hydrodynamic simulations in a Bayesian global analysis, enabling the inclusion of statistics-hungry observables like $v_4$ and $NSC(4,2)$. The data favor a specific shear viscosity $\eta/s$ with a minimum-value plateau at temperatures $150\lesssim T \lesssim 230$ MeV, with $0.12 \lesssim (\eta/s)_{\mathrm{min}} \lesssim 0.18$, and a non-zero bulk viscous coefficient $\zeta/s$ at $200\lesssim T \lesssim 300$ MeV.

## Method

The authors used 2+1 D second-order viscous hydrodynamics with dynamical freeze-out and event-by-event EKRT-model initial conditions based on perturbative QCD and saturation. They trained deep convolutional neural networks (DenseNet architecture) on hydrodynamic simulations to predict bulk observables directly from initial energy density profiles and QCD matter parameters, then used the NN-generated events to train Gaussian process emulators for centrality-class averaged observables. The Bayesian analysis constrained 15 free parameters (10 for $\eta/s(T)$ and $\zeta/s(T)$, 1 for chemical decoupling temperature, 2 for dynamical freeze-out conditions, and 2 for initial state) against experimental data from $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC and $2.76$ TeV Pb+Pb, $5.02$ TeV Pb+Pb, and $5.44$ TeV Xe+Xe collisions at the LHC.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity minimum plateau: $0.12 \lesssim (\eta/s)_{\mathrm{min}} \lesssim 0.18$ at temperatures $150\lesssim T \lesssim 230$ MeV
- Bulk viscosity non-zero in temperature range $200\lesssim T \lesssim 300$ MeV
- Knudsen number at freeze-out: $0.8-2.3$
- Ratio of mean free path to system size at freeze-out: $0.3-1.2$
- Neural network performance: approximately 2 full hydrodynamic simulations per CPU-hour versus approximately 100 events per second using a GPU (NVIDIA V100)
- Training used 950 initial parameter points plus 100 additional points from posterior distribution, with 40 events per training point per collision system
- Neural network validation showed average deviation of approximately 1% of observable value for event-averaged observables
- For $NSC(4,2)$ validation, neural network accuracy was of order 1-10% in the 10-70% centrality range

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian global analysis approach previously applied by Novak (2013), Bernhard (2019), Auvinen (2020), and the JETSCAPE collaboration (2020-2024) for determining QCD matter properties from heavy-ion collision data. The novel neural network framework builds on the authors' prior work (Hirvonen 2023, 2024) and similar efforts by Sun (2024), OmanaKuttan (2025), and Stewart (2025) to use generative neural networks as fast emulators of event-by-event hydrodynamics. The method enables inclusion of statistics-hungry observables like $v_4$ and normalized symmetric cumulants that were previously computationally prohibitive, and can be applied to future analyses with additional observables or different initial state models.
