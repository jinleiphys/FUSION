# Are Occupation Numbers Observable?

> The question of whether occupation numbers and momentum distributions of nucleons in nuclei are observables is considered from an effective field theory perspective. Field redefinitions lead to variations that imply the answer is negative, as illustrated in the interacting Fermi gas at low density. Implications for the interpretation of (e,e'p) experiments with nuclei are discussed.

## Key claim
Occupation numbers and momentum distributions of nucleons in nuclei are not observables because they depend on the particular representation of the effective Lagrangian, which can be changed by field redefinitions that leave all true observables invariant. The ambiguity is fundamental and cannot be resolved by experiment, even though standard analyses of (e,e'p) experiments assume a unique extraction is possible.

## Method
The authors use effective field theory (EFT) for a nonrelativistic interacting Fermi gas at low density, with interactions parameterized by the s-wave scattering length a and effective range r_e. They perform a local field redefinition of the fermion field parameterized by a dimensionless constant alpha, generating an equivalent class of Lagrangians, and compute the momentum distribution n(k) from the one-particle Green's function to order C_0^2 and order alpha C_0. They compare the results for the original Lagrangian (alpha=0) and the transformed Lagrangian (alpha nonzero), and also examine how an external source J(x) couples to the system to mimic (e,e'p) knockout reactions.

## Key numbers
- The s-wave scattering length a and effective range r_e appear in the EFT coefficients: C_0 = 4πa/M and C_2 = C_0 a r_e/2.
- The field redefinition parameter alpha is dimensionless, with a factor 1/Λ^3 introduced where Λ is the EFT breakdown scale.
- The noninteracting momentum distribution is n(k) = g θ(k_F - k), where g is the spin degeneracy factor.
- At order alpha C_0, the change in n(k) from the naive operator insertion includes a term Δn(k)_(i) = -2(g-1)ρ (4πα/Λ^3) θ(k_F - k), where ρ is the density.

## Context
This work extends the EFT analysis of Furnstahl and Hammer (2001) on thermodynamic observables to the question of momentum distributions, showing that unlike total particle number (which is protected by U(1) symmetry), occupation numbers lack a symmetry principle to define a unique operator. The paper contradicts the common assumption in nuclear physics that momentum distributions can be unambiguously extracted from (e,e'p) experiments (Boffi 1996, Pandharipande 1997), and it parallels the known scheme and scale dependence of parton distributions in deep inelastic scattering (Chen 2001). The results enable a clearer understanding of why different many-body calculations (e.g., nonrelativistic vs. relativistic Brueckner approaches, Jaminon 1990) give starkly different occupation numbers, and they raise analogous questions about the condensate fraction in Bose-Einstein condensates (Leggett 2001).
