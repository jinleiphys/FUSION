# Fluctuation and flow probes of early-time correlations in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> Fluctuation and correlation observables are often measured using multi-particle correlation methods and therefore mutually probe the origins of genuine correlations present in multi-particle distribution functions. We investigate the common influence of correlations arising from the spatially inhomogeneous initial state on multiplicity and momentum fluctuations as well as flow fluctuations. Although these observables reflect different aspects of the initial state, taken together, they can constrain a correlation scale set at the earliest moments of the collision. We calculate both the correlation scale in an initial stage Glasma flux tube picture and the modification to these correlations from later stage hydrodynamic flow and find quantitative agreement with experimental measurements over a range of collision systems and energies.

# Fluctuation and flow probes of early-time correlations in relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim
Multiplicity fluctuations, transverse momentum fluctuations, flow fluctuations, and the ridge observable are all linked by a common correlation scale R (the "robust variance"), and this scale originates from spatially inhomogeneous initial-state correlations in the Glasma flux tube picture. The authors find quantitative agreement with experimental measurements over a range of collision systems and energies, but this agreement holds best in central collisions where gluon saturation is most valid, while partial thermalization is needed to describe peripheral collisions.

## Method
The authors express multiplicity fluctuations, transverse momentum fluctuations, flow fluctuations, and the ridge observable in terms of the genuine two-particle correlation function r(p1,p2) from a cumulant expansion of the pair distribution. They model the initial-state spatial correlations using a Color Glass Condensate-Glasma flux tube picture, where correlated partons originate from the same flux tube, and then propagate these correlations through a blast-wave description of the Boltzmann phase-space density on a Cooper-Frye freeze-out surface to compute final-state momentum-space correlations.

## Key numbers
- The correlation strength R = (⟨N(N-1)⟩ - ⟨N⟩²)/⟨N⟩² is equivalent to the inverse of the negative binomial distribution width parameter, R = k⁻¹_NBD, as measured by PHENIX (Adare 2008).
- The Glasma correlation scale is R dN/dy = κ α_s⁻¹(Q_s²), where Q_s is the saturation scale and κ is a dimensionless coefficient fixed by ridge measurements at 200 GeV Au+Au collisions.
- The number of gluons in a rapidity interval Δy is ⟨N⟩ = (dN/dy)Δy ∼ α_s⁻¹(Q_s)⟨K⟩, where ⟨K⟩ is the average number of flux tubes per event.
- Flow fluctuations are defined as σ_n² = (v_n{2}² - v_n{4}²)/2, and the difference between v_2{2} and v_2{4} from STAR (Pruthi 2011) is attributed to flow fluctuations, with the largest fluctuations in central collisions where ⟨v_2⟩ approaches zero.
- The coefficient of variation for elliptic flow, σ_{v_n}/⟨v_n⟩ = √[(v_n{2}² - v_n{4}²)/(v_n{2}² + v_n{4}²)], is compared to STAR data at RHIC energies and a prediction is given for Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV.

## Context
This work extends the approach started in Gavin (2008) and Moschelli (2009) in which long-range correlations result from the fragmentation of Glasma flux tubes, and builds on the cumulant expansion methods of Borghini (2000, 2001). The authors argue that the same local correlations that give rise to multiplicity and p_t fluctuations also drive fluctuations of harmonic flow coefficients, contradicting the assumption that flow fluctuations arise only from changes in the shape of the collision area (which would predict ⟨δp_t1δp_t2⟩ = 0). The framework enables constraining theories of the initial state of heavy ion collisions through measurements of the correlation scale R, and suggests that saturation-based descriptions are consistent with mid-central to central collisions, while partial thermalization (as in Gavin 2003, 2004) is needed to describe peripheral collisions and pp collisions in the same model.
