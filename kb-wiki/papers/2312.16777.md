# Multiphoton fusion of light nuclei in intense laser fields

> We investigate the fusion cross sections of light nuclei in the presence of linearly polarized intense laser fields. By combining the Coulomb-Volkov solutions with the complex spherical square-well nuclear potential, we derive an explicit formulation of the multiphoton cross section in a self-consistent manner. Our analysis is specifically focused on deuteron-triton (DT) and proton-boron (p$^{11}{\rm B}$) fusion reactions, both of which have garnered widespread attention. The theoretical results reveal that, under conditions of longer laser wavelengths and lower incident particle kinetic energies, a few thousands of photons can participate in the fusion reactions, resulting in a substantial enhancement of fusion cross sections by almost ten orders of magnitude. We elucidate the multiphoton mechanism underlying these findings and discuss their implications.

# Multiphoton fusion of light nuclei in intense laser fields

## Key claim
The authors derive a self-consistent multiphoton fusion cross-section formula by combining Coulomb-Volkov solutions with a complex spherical square-well nuclear potential, and show that under conditions of longer laser wavelengths (e.g., 800 nm) and lower incident kinetic energies (e.g., 1 keV), fusion cross sections for DT and p$^{11}$B reactions can be enhanced by up to ten orders of magnitude, but only at laser intensities approaching $10^{22}$–$10^{23}$ W/cm$^2$, and the enhancement is negligible at shorter wavelengths (e.g., 100 nm) or higher energies.

## Method
The authors solve the time-dependent Schrodinger equation for the relative motion of two nuclei in a linearly polarized laser field (dipole approximation) by expanding the wave function on spherical Coulomb-Volkov states. They match the wave function and its derivative at the nuclear well radius $r_N$ using a complex spherical square-well optical potential (with real part $V_r$ and imaginary part $V_i$ calibrated to field-free experimental data), and derive the total fusion cross section as a sum over photon number $n$ of the product $\sigma_l(E_n) P_n(\theta)$, where $P_n$ is the generalized Bessel function probability for absorbing or emitting $n$ photons. They then compute angle-averaged effective cross sections $\sigma_L$ for DT and p$^{11}$B fusion at low energies ($E < 1$ MeV), considering only $S$-wave ($l=0$) contributions.

## Key numbers
- For DT fusion at 800 nm wavelength, $10^{20}$ W/cm$^2$ intensity, and 5 keV kinetic energy, the total cross section is approximately $6.5 \times 10^{-4}$ b when the incident direction is parallel to the laser polarization ($\theta=0$), compared to $2.3 \times 10^{-4}$ b in the field-free case.
- For DT fusion at 800 nm and 1 keV kinetic energy, the angle-averaged effective cross section $\sigma_L$ increases by approximately ten orders of magnitude relative to the laser-free cross section at intensities near $10^{22}$ W/cm$^2$.
- For DT fusion at 100 nm wavelength and 10 keV kinetic energy, $\sigma_L$ shows only a one-fold increase over the field-free value.
- For p$^{11}$B fusion at 800 nm wavelength, $10^{23}$ W/cm$^2$ intensity, and 20 keV kinetic energy, $\sigma_L$ exhibits a nearly four-order-of-magnitude increase over the laser-free case.
- For p$^{11}$B fusion at 100 nm wavelength and 100 keV kinetic energy, the increase is only 0.2-fold.
- At 400 nm wavelength and 5 keV DT kinetic energy, increasing intensity from $10^{19}$ W/cm$^2$ to $10^{21}$ W/cm$^2$ increases the number of involved photons from about 300 to more than 2000.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study (Wu et al., 2022) that used Kramers's approximation for high-frequency laser fields, now addressing the low-frequency regime where multiphoton processes dominate. It contrasts with prior approaches (Queisser 2019, Lv 2019, Wang 2020, Liu 2021, Kohlfu 2021, Lv 2022, Qi 2022, Bekx 2022, Liu 2022) that used the Gamow form for fusion cross sections and focused only on tunneling enhancement through the Coulomb barrier, lacking a self-consistent treatment of the nuclear potential. The results suggest that intense low-frequency lasers could relax the temperature requirements of the Lawson criterion for controlled fusion, and open the door to studying gamma-ray-assisted multiphoton fusion and more realistic optical potentials such as Woods-Saxon forms.
