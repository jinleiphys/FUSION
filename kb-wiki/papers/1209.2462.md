# Spinodal amplification of density fluctuations in fluid-dynamical simulations of relativistic nuclear collisions

> Extending a previously developed two-phase equation of state, we simulate head-on relativistic lead-lead collisions with fluid dynamics, augmented with a finite-range term, and study the effects of the phase structure on the evolution of the baryon density. For collision energies that bring the bulk of the system into the mechanically unstable spinodal region of the phase diagram, the density irregularities are being amplified significantly. The resulting density clumping may be exploited as a signal of the phase transition, possibly through an enhanced production of composite particles.

# Spinodal amplification of density fluctuations in fluid-dynamical simulations of relativistic nuclear collisions

## Key claim

The first-order phase transition in compressed baryonic matter can cause significant amplification of initial density irregularities in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, but only for beam energies that bring the bulk of the system into the mechanically unstable spinodal region of the phase diagram for a sufficient time; the effect is demonstrated using a specific two-phase equation of state and the optimal energy range is found to be approximately 2–4 A GeV for lead-lead collisions.

## Method

The authors extended a previously developed two-phase equation of state to super-critical temperatures and augmented an ideal fluid-dynamical code (SHASTA) with a gradient term in the local pressure, governed by a length parameter a, to incorporate finite-range effects necessary for describing spinodal decomposition. Head-on collisions of lead nuclei on a stationary lead target were simulated at various beam energies, with initial conditions generated from 200 separate UrQMD cascade evolutions per energy to include event-by-event fluctuations; the fluid-dynamical evolution was then compared against a one-phase partner equation of state that uses the Maxwell construction to remove the phase transition.

## Key numbers

- The gradient term strength parameter a = 0.033 fm was chosen to give a zero-temperature interface tension of approximately 10 MeV/fm².
- The nuclear saturation density ρ_s = 0.153/fm³ and the associated energy density ε_s ≈ m_N ρ_s are used as reference scales.
- For the two-phase equation of state at E_lab = 3 A GeV, the normalized density moment for N=7 shows enhancement of well over an order of magnitude compared to the one-phase partner.
- Some individual evolutions experience amplifications larger than the ensemble average by up to a factor of five or so.
- The optimal beam energy range for the effect is E_lab ≈ 2–4 A GeV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Randrup (2009) and Randrup (2010) by incorporating a finite-range gradient term into fluid-dynamical simulations of relativistic nuclear collisions, enabling the first transport model for high-energy nuclear collisions that simultaneously has an explicitly known two-phase equation of state and treats spinodal instabilities in a numerically reliable manner. The phenomenon of spinodal amplification was previously exploited to obtain experimental evidence of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition (Borderie 1986). The results suggest that the resulting density clumping may be observable through enhanced production of composite particles such as deuterons and tritons, and the authors call for further studies with different equations of state and inclusion of dissipative effects and thermal noise.
