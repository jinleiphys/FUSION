# QCD collinear factorization, its extensions and the partonic distributions

> I review the basics of the collinear factorization theorem applied primarily to deep inelastic scattering (DIS) involving forward parton distributions (PDFs) and the extensions of this theorem for exclusive processes probing non-forward parton distributions (GPDs), the generalized distribution amplitudes (GDAs) and the transition distribution amplitudes (TDAs). These QCD factorization theorem is an important tool in the description of hard processes in QCD. Whenever valid, it permits to represent the cross section or the scattering amplitude for such a process as a convolution in partonic momenta of a perturbatively calculable part (the coefficient function, CF) which involves the hard scale of the process with non-perturbative (soft) distributions of active partons inside the hadrons involved in a process. The reliability of the perturbatively determined hard part together with high precision experimental data on relevant observables gives a hope for the possibility to uncover fine details of interpartonic interactions and spatial distributions of partons inside hadrons. I conclude with some remarks about QCD factorization with transverse momentum dependent PDFs

## Key claim

The collinear QCD factorization theorem, proven for inclusive deep inelastic scattering (DIS), can be extended to a wide class of exclusive hard processes, but the validity of the factorization for each process depends on specific kinematic conditions and polarization states; for example, factorization holds for longitudinally polarized meson production but is violated for transversely polarized meson production due to endpoint singularities.

## Method

The paper reviews the all-order diagrammatic proof of collinear factorization, which proceeds by identifying reduced graphs via power counting (Libby and Sterman, 1978), separating leading regions using a Grammer-Yennie inspired method, and absorbing collinear gluon exchanges into Wilson lines that define gauge-invariant non-perturbative matrix elements. For exclusive processes, the same methodology is applied to double deeply virtual Compton scattering (DDVCS), deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS), generalized distribution amplitudes (GDAs) in \(\gamma^*\gamma\) scattering, meson production, and backward meson production via nucleon-meson transition distribution amplitudes (TDAs). The renormalization group evolution of the partonic distributions is governed by the DGLAP equation.

## Key numbers

- The factorization formula for DIS involves a convolution over longitudinal momentum fractions \(x\) of partons, with the cross section \(\sigma \sim \int_{-1}^1 \sigma_j(x,\mu) f_{j/h}(x,\mu)\).
- For DDVCS and DVCS, the scattering amplitude depends on the skewness parameter \(\xi = (p^+_A - p'^+_A)/(p^+_A + p'^+_A)\), which is non-zero due to momentum transfer in the \(t\)-channel.
- For \(\rho_L\)-meson production, the factorized amplitude involves a convolution over \(x\) and \(z\): \({\cal A}(\xi,t) \sim \int_{-1}^{1} dx \int_0^1 dz\; H(x,z,\mu)\,\Phi^q(z,\mu)\,f_{q/h}(x,\xi,t,\mu)\), where \(\Phi^q(z,\mu)\) is the twist-2 distribution amplitude.
- For \(\rho_T\)-meson production, the collinear factorization is violated because the convolution integrals diverge at the edge of the kinematic domain, leading to endpoint singularities; this is related to the higher twist-3 distribution amplitude.

## Context

This review extends the classic collinear factorization theorem for DIS, as proven by Collins, Soper, and Sterman, to exclusive processes involving generalized parton distributions (GPDs) introduced by Mueller (1994), Radyushkin (1996), and Ji (1996). It also covers generalized distribution amplitudes (GDAs) for \(\gamma^*\gamma\) scattering studied by BABAR and BELLE, and transition distribution amplitudes (TDAs) for backward meson production, which are planned for study at JLab and Panda (GSI). The paper enables future work on processes where collinear factorization fails, such as transversely polarized meson production, motivating approaches that include transverse momentum dependent PDFs (TMDs) or higher Fock states, as discussed by Anselmino, Gamberg, Besse, and Kroll at the same conference.
