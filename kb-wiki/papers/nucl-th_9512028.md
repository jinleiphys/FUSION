# Lattice gas model for fragmentation: From Argon on Scandium to Gold on Gold

> The recent fragmentation data for central collisions of Gold on Gold are even qualitatively different from those for central collisions of Argon on Scandium. The latter can be fitted with a lattice gas model calculation. Effort is made to understand why the model fails for Gold on Gold. The calculation suggests that the large Coulomb interaction which is operative for the larger system is responsible for this discrepancy. This is demonstrated by mapping the lattice gas model to a molecular dynamics calculation for disassembly. This mapping is quite faithful for Argon on Scandium but deviates strongly for Gold on Gold. The molecular dynamics calculation for disassembly reproduces the characteristics of the fragmentation data for both Gold on Gold and Argon on Scandium.

## Key claim

The lattice gas model, which successfully fits fragmentation data for medium-mass systems like Argon on Scandium, fails for heavy systems like Gold on Gold because the large Coulomb interaction in the latter case destroys the simple predictions; the authors demonstrate this by mapping the lattice gas model to a molecular dynamics calculation, showing that the mapping is faithful for Argon on Scandium but deviates strongly for Gold on Gold when Coulomb is included.

## Method

The authors performed lattice gas model calculations for central collisions of Argon on Scandium and Gold on Gold, using a cubic lattice with nearest-neighbor attractive interactions of strength -ε and a freeze-out density of 0.39ρ₀. They then mapped the lattice gas model to a molecular dynamics disassembly calculation using a Stillinger-type potential that reproduces the lattice gas interaction, first without Coulomb and then with explicit Coulomb interaction added. For molecular dynamics, nucleons were initialized at lattice sites with Maxwell-Boltzmann momenta at a given temperature, then evolved classically until the system expanded to 64 times its initial volume; clusters were defined as nucleons within 1.3r₀ of each other after asymptotic time.

## Key numbers

- For Argon on Scandium, the lattice gas model used ε = 3.7 MeV without explicit Coulomb and ε = 4.7 MeV with explicit Coulomb; the critical temperature T_c = 1.1275ε.
- For Gold on Gold at a beam energy of 35 MeV/nucleon (estimated to correspond to 0.3 T_c), the calculated τ value was 1.4, compared to the experimental value of 1.25.
- The molecular dynamics calculation used a time step of 0.1 fm/c and evolved for up to 1000 fm/c for low temperatures; energy conservation was accurate to within 1 percent.
- For Argon on Scandium, the freeze-out density that gave the best fit with data was ρ = 0.39ρ₀.

## Context

This work extends the lattice gas model previously developed by the same authors (Gupta and Pan, 1995) for medium-mass collisions, which had successfully reproduced power-law mass distributions and the minimum in the exponent τ at the critical temperature seen in experiments at Michigan State University (Li et al., 1993) and Chalk River (Lucas et al., 1995). The paper directly addresses the puzzle that recent data for Gold on Gold central collisions (MSU Miniball Group, 1995) showed no minimum in τ and a value of τ as low as 1.25 at 35 MeV/nucleon, which contradicted expectations from percolation models (Bauer, 1988; Campi, 1986) and lattice gas predictions. The results enable future work to incorporate Coulomb effects into simple theoretical models of nuclear fragmentation, and suggest that larger colliding masses, while potentially better for studying phase transitions, require careful treatment of the Coulomb field before signals for phase transitions can be understood.
