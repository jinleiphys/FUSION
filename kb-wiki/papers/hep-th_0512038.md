# Possible evidence for "dark radiation" from Big Bang Nucleosynthesis Data

> We address the emerging discrepancy between the Big Bang Nucleosynthesis data and standard cosmology, which asks for a bit longer evolution time. If this effect is real, one possible implication (in a framework of brane cosmology model) is that there is a ``dark radiation'' component which is negative and makes few percents of ordinary matter density. If so, all scales of this model can be fixed, provided brane-to-bulk leakage problem is solved.

# Possible evidence for "dark radiation" from Big Bang Nucleosynthesis Data

## Key claim

The discrepancy between Big Bang Nucleosynthesis data and standard cosmology, which suggests a slightly longer evolution time, could be explained by a negative "dark radiation" component making up a few percent of ordinary matter density within a brane cosmology model, but the effect is only about two standard deviations and may not be real.

## Method

The authors analyze the existing BBN data discrepancy, quantified as an effective number of extra neutrino species ΔNν = -0.65 ± 0.35, and translate this into a ratio of dark radiation to ordinary matter density χ/ρ using the relation χ/ρ = (7/43)(ΔNν/Nν). They then interpret this within the framework of a simple single-brane cosmology model with one extra dimension, where the dark radiation term χ appears as a leading correction to standard cosmology in the effective Friedmann equation on the brane.

## Key numbers

- ΔNν = -0.65 ± 0.35, the effective number of extra neutrino species from BBN data analysis (Barger et al., 2003)
- χ/ρ ≈ -0.11 ± 0.06, the inferred ratio of dark radiation to ordinary matter density at BBN time
- δQd/Qd = -0.019 ± 0.005, the fractional variation in deuteron binding energy that provides a better fit to BBN data and brings baryon-to-photon ratio into agreement with CMB data
- Δ(ms/ΛQCD)/(ms/ΛQCD) ≈ 10⁻³, the corresponding variation in strange quark to QCD scale ratio

## Context

This work extends the analysis of BBN discrepancies previously discussed by Barger et al. (2003), who found a persistent negative effective neutrino number, and builds on the authors' own earlier work (Flambaum and Shuryak) identifying deuteron binding as a sensitive probe of fundamental constant variation. The paper proposes brane cosmology as an alternative to variable physical constants or quintessence models for explaining the anomaly, and suggests that if confirmed, the dark radiation signal could fix the remaining scale ambiguity in brane models and enable tests by comparing BBN and CMB data for the same χ/ρ ratio.
