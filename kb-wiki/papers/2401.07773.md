# Towards Uncovering Dark Matter Effects on Neutron Star Properties: A Machine Learning Approach

> In recent years, researchers have become increasingly interested in understanding how dark matter affects neutron stars, helping them to better understand complex astrophysical phenomena. In this paper, we delve deeper into this problem by using advanced machine learning techniques to find potential connections between dark matter and various neutron star characteristics. We employ Random Forest classifiers to analyze neutron star (NS) properties and investigate whether these stars exhibit characteristics indicative of dark matter admixture. Our dataset includes 32,000 sequences of simulated NS properties, each described by mass, radius, and tidal deformability, inferred using recent observations and theoretical models. We explore a two-fluid model for the NS, incorporating separate equations of state for nucleonic and dark matter, with the latter considering a fermionic dark matter scenario. Our classifiers are trained and validated in a variety of feature sets, including the tidal deformability for various masses. Based on confusion matrices, these classifiers can identify NS with admixed dark matter with approximately 17% probability of misclassification. In particular, we find that additional tidal deformability data do not significantly improve the precision of our predictions. This article also delves into the potential of specific NS properties as indicators of the presence of dark matter. Radius measurements, especially at extreme mass values, emerge as particularly promising features. The insights gained from our study will guide future observational strategies and enhance dark matter detection capabilities. According to this study, neutron stars at 1.4 and 2.07 solar masses have radii that strongly suggest dark matter in neutron stars more likely than just hadronic composition, based on NICER data from pulsars PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620.

## Key claim
Random Forest classifiers trained on simulated neutron star properties can identify stars containing admixed fermionic dark matter with a misclassification probability of approximately 17%, but the addition of tidal deformability data at multiple masses does not significantly improve classification precision. The study suggests that radius measurements at extreme mass values, particularly for 1.4 and 2.07 solar mass neutron stars, are the most promising discriminators between purely hadronic composition and dark matter admixture, based on NICER data from pulsars PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620.

## Method
The authors constructed a two-fluid model of neutron stars with separate equations of state for nucleonic matter and fermionic dark matter, generating 32,000 simulated sequences of neutron star properties (mass, radius, and tidal deformability). They trained Random Forest classifiers on these simulated datasets, using confusion matrices to evaluate classification performance across various feature sets that included tidal deformability for different masses.

## Key numbers
- Dataset of 32,000 simulated sequences of neutron star properties was used for training and validation
- Classifiers achieved approximately 17% probability of misclassification when identifying neutron stars with admixed dark matter
- Analysis focused on neutron stars at 1.4 and 2.07 solar masses, using NICER data from pulsars PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620

## Context
This work extends the line of research using neutron star observations to constrain dark matter properties, building on the two-fluid model approach previously applied to study dark matter effects on compact objects. The study directly compares its findings against NICER observations of PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620, and demonstrates that machine learning techniques can complement traditional equation-of-state inference methods. The results enable future observational strategies by identifying which neutron star properties (particularly radius at extreme masses) are most sensitive to dark matter admixture, guiding next-generation X-ray timing missions and gravitational-wave detectors.
