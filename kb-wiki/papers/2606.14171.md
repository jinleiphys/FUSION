# Teleportation in Proton Systems Revisited

> We discuss the peculiarities of scattering in a three-proton system induced by the presence of an entangled proton-proton Bell-like state in the initial configuration. We formulate the problem using the standard spin formalism of nuclear physics in order to follow in detail the reaction pathways leading to the teleportation of a quantum-mechanical state within this system. By performing numerical simulations of the system and calculations based on a realistic nucleon-nucleon potential, we evaluate the relevant spin observables and search for a simple, experimentally feasible signal that could provide clear evidence of quantum teleportation. For the case in which proton 2, belonging to the entangled proton pair 23, interacts with hydrogen target 1, thereby triggering the teleportation process, we find clear evidence of teleportation through measurements of the final polarization of proton 3', even for small values of the polarization of target proton 1. The dominant component of this polarization is transferred almost entirely from target proton 1 to proton 3 in the angular region characterized by strong entanglement. For an unpolarized target proton 1, quantum teleportation is no longer possible. The only unambiguous signature of the residual quantum correlations would be provided by measurements of the spin correlations within the strongly entangled final-state proton pair 1'2'. Indirect evidence may also be obtained from measurements of the polarization of proton 2' in the final state, although the corresponding effectis relatively weak.

# Teleportation in Proton Systems Revisited

## Key claim

Quantum teleportation of a spin state from a polarized target proton to an entangled proton in a three-proton system is clearly observable through measurements of the final polarization of the third proton, even for target polarizations as small as 1%, but only when the target proton is polarized; for an unpolarized target, teleportation does not occur and only residual spin correlations remain measurable.

## Method

The author formulated the three-proton scattering problem using the standard spin formalism of nuclear physics, expressing the pp transition operator in the Bell-state basis. Numerical simulations were performed by solving the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for pp scattering using the high-precision AV18 nucleon-nucleon potential with explicit Coulomb interaction, at incident laboratory energies of 5, 10, 15, and 20 MeV. The final spin density matrix was computed to evaluate polarizations and spin correlations of all outgoing protons as functions of the center-of-mass scattering angles.

## Key numbers

- At Elab ≈ 10 MeV and center-of-mass angles θc.m. ∈ (55°,125°), the pp transition matrix is well approximated by M ≈ |ψ⁻⟩⟨ψ⁻| and the final spin density matrix by ρf ≈ |ψ⁻⟩⟨ψ⁻|
- At Elab ≈ 150 MeV, the transition matrix is approximated by M ≈ |ψ⁺⟩⟨Φ⁻| and ρf ≈ |ψ⁺⟩⟨ψ⁺|, but the angular region for strong entanglement is restricted to θc.m. ∈ (85°,95°)
- The polarization-transfer coefficient for proton 3' reaches its maximal value Ky^(y')(1 → 3') = 1 in the angular region of strong entanglement
- The teleportation effect remains observable for target polarizations as small as Py^1 = 1% (0.01)
- A realistic polarized hydrogen target corresponds to Py^1 = 0.034
- The spin correlation of the final-state pair 1'2' approaches -1 in the angular region θc.m. ∈ (55°,125°)
- For incident energies of 10, 15, and 20 MeV, the final polarization of proton 2' after the second scattering shows approximately a threefold difference compared to the polarization without the target present

## Context

This work extends the author's previous proposal (Witała, 2025) for producing entangled Bell-like proton-proton pairs in unpolarized pp and pd scattering, and the teleportation scheme originally proposed in earlier work (Witała, 2025). The paper demonstrates that while a polarized hydrogen target enables clear teleportation signatures, the practical difficulty of realizing such targets means experiments must currently rely on unpolarized targets, where only residual spin correlations in the final-state proton pair 1'2' can be measured. The analysis also shows that at low energies around 10 MeV, successive scatterings can generate networks of entangled proton pairs through entanglement transfer, which is not possible at 150 MeV because the scattered proton energies fall outside the regime where a single Bell term dominates the M-matrix.
