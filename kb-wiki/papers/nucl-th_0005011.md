# Screened Coulomb potentials for astrophysical nuclear fusion reactions

> The electron-screening acceleration of laboratory fusion reactions at astrophysical energies is an unsolved problem of great importance to astrophysics. That effect is modeled here by considering the fusion of hydrogen-like atoms whose electron probability density is used in Poisson's equation in order to derive the corresponding screened Coulomb potential energy. That way atomic excitations and deformations of the fusing atoms can be taken into account. Those potentials are then treated semiclassically in order to obtain the screening (accelerating) factor of the reaction. By means of the proposed model the effect of a superstrong magnetic field on laboratory Hydrogen fusion reactions is investigated here for the first time showing that, despite the considerable increase in the cross section of the $% dd$ reaction, the $pp$ reaction is still too slow to justify experimentation. The proposed model is finally applied on the $H^{2}(d,p) H^{3}$ fusion reaction describing satisfactorily the experimental data although some ambiguity remains regarding the molecular nature of the deuteron target. Notably, the present method gives a sufficiently high screening energy for Hydrogen fusion reactions so that the take-away energy of the spectator nucleus can also be taken into account.

# Screened Coulomb potentials for astrophysical nuclear fusion reactions

## Key claim

The paper proposes a mean-field model for electron screening in laboratory fusion reactions at astrophysical energies that can account for atomic excitations and deformations, but acknowledges that some ambiguity remains regarding the molecular nature of the deuteron target when applied to the H²(d,p)H³ reaction. The model describes experimental data satisfactorily but does not fully resolve the long-standing discrepancy between theoretical and experimental screening energies.

## Method

The author models hydrogen-like atoms by using the electron probability density from quantum wavefunctions (1s and 2s states) in Poisson's equation to derive screened Coulomb potentials, then treats the tunneling probability semiclassically via the WKB approximation to obtain the screening enhancement factor. For magnetically catalyzed screening, the electron cloud is treated as deformed under superstrong magnetic fields using a variational approach. The model is applied to the H²(d,p)H³ reaction by fitting the screening energy and astrophysical S-factor parameters to experimental data from Greife (1995), using three different analysis methods.

## Key numbers

- For the H²(d,p)H³ reaction, the asymptotic fit to high-energy data from Brown and Jarmie (1990) yields S_b(E) = 55.49(0.46) + 0.094(0.0054)E keV-b, with a fitted screening energy U_e^as = 0.019(0.003) keV and chi-squared = 0.028
- Simultaneous four-parameter fit to all uncorrected data gives U_e^all = 0.017(0.003) keV and S_b(E) = 54.54(1.39) + 0.608(0.265)E - 0.026(0.026)E² with chi-squared = 0.011
- The theoretical screening energy for a molecular deuteron target using the ground-state hydrogen-like model is U_e = 0.010 keV (from Eq. myshift), and using the simplified parabolic model gives U_e_tilde = 0.016 keV (from Eq. uesimple)
- For the pp reaction in a superstrong magnetic field B_12 = 4.7 (4.7 × 10¹² G), the zero-energy astrophysical factor S_pp(0) ≈ 4 × 10⁻²² keV-b is increased by roughly one order of magnitude compared to the unmagnetized case
- The intense magnetic field regime threshold is B_IMF = 4.7 × 10⁹ G
- For the simplified constant-density model, the screening radius for hydrogen isotopes is a = 0.842 a₀ (from Eq. radius2)

## Context

This work extends earlier mean-field screening models by Bencze (1989), which assumed constant electron charge density, and the few-body treatment by Bracci et al. (1990) that established sudden and adiabatic limits for screening energy. The paper also builds on the atomic screening calculations of Shoppa et al. (1990) and the molecular fusion studies of Shoppa et al. (1996). The model enables improved extraction of the bare-nucleus astrophysical S-factor from low-energy laboratory data, and provides a framework for estimating screening enhancement under superstrong magnetic fields relevant to neutron star surface reactions. The author notes that despite the model's success, the discrepancy between experimental and theoretical screening energies noted by Rolfs (2000) and Junker et al. (1998) remains partially unresolved.
