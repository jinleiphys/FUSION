# Delineating Effects of Nuclear Symmetry Energy on the Radii and Tidal Polarizabilities of Neutron Stars

> What can we learn about the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$ from precise measurements of the radius ($R_{\rm{1.4}}$) and/or tidal polarizability ($Λ_{1.4}$) of canonical neutron stars (NSs) with a mass of 1.4 M$_\odot$? With the $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$ parameterized using three parameters $L$, $K_{\rm{sym}}$, and $J_{\rm{sym}}$ which have the asymptotic meaning of being respectively the slope, curvature, and skewness of symmetry energy at saturation density, we found that, while both the $R_{\rm{1.4}}$ and $Λ_{1.4}$ depend strongly on the slope $L$, the $K_{\rm{sym}}$ and $J_{\rm{sym}}$ parameters characterizing the high-density behavior of $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$ also play appreciable roles. Thus, there is not a simple relation between the $Λ_{\rm{1.4}}$/$R_{\rm{1.4}}$ and $L$ alone. Precise measurements of just the $Λ_{\rm{1.4}}$ and $R_{\rm{1.4}}$ can not completely determine the $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$ but limit combinations of its parameters. In particular, stringent constraints approximately independent of the $J_{\rm{sym}}$ on the $L$-$K_{\rm{sym}}$ correlations can be obtained. However, infinite combinations of the larger (smaller) $L$ and smaller (larger) $K_{\rm{sym}}$ can lead to the same $Λ_{\rm{1.4}}$ and $R_{\rm{1.4}}$. Additional observables including those from terrestrial nuclear experiments are thus necessary to break this degeneracy in order to completely determine the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$.

## Key claim

While both the radius and tidal polarizability of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star depend strongly on the slope parameter L of the nuclear symmetry energy, the curvature parameter Ksym and skewness parameter Jsym also play appreciable roles, meaning there is no simple relation between these observables and L alone; precise measurements of just the radius and tidal polarizability cannot completely determine the density dependence of the symmetry energy but can only limit combinations of its parameters.

## Method

The authors parameterized the nuclear symmetry energy Esym(ρ) using a Taylor expansion around saturation density ρ0 with three parameters: the slope L, curvature Ksym, and skewness Jsym. They fixed the symmetric nuclear matter parameters K0 and J0 at 230 MeV and -180 MeV respectively, and varied L, Ksym, and Jsym within their known uncertain ranges (L=40-80 MeV, Ksym=-400 to 100 MeV, Jsym=-200 to 800 MeV). They then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equation with an explicitly isospin-dependent parametric equation of state, connecting the core EOS to the NV EOS for the inner crust and the BPS EOS for the outer crust, to compute the mass-radius relations and tidal polarizabilities of neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- For a 1.4 solar mass neutron star, varying L from 40 to 80 MeV changes the radius R1.4 from 12.2 km to 13.1 km (a 7% variation) with Ksym=0 and Jsym=200 MeV.
- Varying Ksym by 150% (with L fixed at 60 MeV) leads to about a 5% change in R1.4.
- Varying Jsym by 200% (with L fixed at 60 MeV) leads to about a 3% change in R1.4.
- At 1.4 solar masses, the un-normalized relative change of the tidal polarizability Λ1.4 is about 36% for the 50% variation in L, 47% for the 150% variation in Ksym, and 32% for the 200% variation in Jsym.
- As L varies from 40 to 60 to 80 MeV, R1.4 changes from 12.2 to 12.6 to 13.1 km while Λ1.4 changes from 439 to 511 to 595.
- The most probable values used for Esym(ρ0) and L are 31.7 ± 3.2 MeV and 58.7 ± 28.1 MeV, respectively.
- The known ranges for Ksym and Jsym are approximately -400 ≤ Ksym ≤ 100 MeV and -200 ≤ Jsym ≤ 800 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing effort to constrain the equation of state of dense neutron-rich matter following the first detection of gravitational waves from the binary neutron star merger GW170817 by the LIGO+Virgo Collaborations (2018). It addresses cautions raised by earlier studies, such as Ang-quark (2017), who concluded there is no evidence for a simple relation between the symmetry energy slope L and the tidal deformability. The results enable future combined analyses where additional observables from terrestrial nuclear experiments, such as neutron skin measurements, are necessary to break the degeneracy between L and Ksym that prevents a complete determination of the symmetry energy density dependence from neutron star observations alone.
