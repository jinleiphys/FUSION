# Pion Excess, Nuclear Correlations, and the Interpretation of ($\vec p, \vec n$) Spin Transfer Experiments

> Conventional theories of nuclear interactions predict a net increase in the distribution of virtual pions in nuclei relative to free nucleons. Analysis of data from several nuclear experiments has led to claims of evidence against such a pion excess. These conclusions are usually based on a collective theory (RPA) of the pions, which may be inadequate. The issue is the energy dependence of the nuclear response, which differs for theories with strong NN correlations from the RPA predictions. In the present paper, information about the energy dependence is extracted from sum rules, which are calculated for such a correlated, noncollective nuclear theory. The results lead to much reduced sensitivity of nuclear reactions to the correlations that are responsible for the pion excess. The primary example is $(\vec p,\vec n)$ spin transfer, for which the expected effects are found to be smaller than the experimental uncertainties. The analysis has consequences for Deep Inelastic Scattering (DIS) experiments as well.

# Pion Excess, Nuclear Correlations, and the Interpretation of ($\vec p, \vec n$) Spin Transfer Experiments

## Key claim

The paper argues that the lack of observed enhancement in the $R_L/R_T$ ratio from ($\vec p, \vec n$) spin transfer experiments does not contradict conventional nuclear structure theories with strong NN correlations, provided the pion excess arises from two-nucleon correlations rather than from collective RPA pionic modes. The expected effects of correlations on the response functions are smaller than the experimental uncertainties, not because the pion excess is absent, but because the high-energy tail of the correlated response is kinematically suppressed in the measured energy range.

## Method

The author uses sum rules for the longitudinal ($R_L$) and transverse ($R_T$) response functions, calculated for fully correlated nuclear ground states from variational Monte Carlo calculations by the Argonne and Urbana groups, to construct model response functions. The response functions are separated into a quasifree (shell-model) part and a correlation contribution, with the latter constrained by both the energy-unweighted sum rule $S_\alpha(q)$ and the energy-weighted sum rule $W_\alpha(q)$. Simple functional forms (constant and exponential) for the correlation contribution $\Delta R_\alpha(q,\omega)$ are adopted, with parameters adjusted to reproduce the sum-rule values for $^{16}$O. These model response functions are then used to estimate the size of correlation effects in the ($\vec p, \vec n$) data and in the Sullivan-process calculation of pion contributions to deep inelastic scattering.

## Key numbers

- For $^{16}$O, the integrated pion excess per nucleon is $\delta n_A/A \simeq 0.03$ (static approximation, nucleons-only theory); theories with explicit $\Delta$ components give $\delta n_A/A \simeq 0.08 - 0.1$.
- For the typical momentum transfer range $200 \leq q \leq 400$ MeV/$c$, the longitudinal sum-rule difference is $\Delta S_L = 0.15$ and the energy-weighted difference is $\Delta W_L = 44$ MeV, giving a mean excitation energy $\omega_L = 293$ MeV for the correlation contribution.
- The peak experimental response functions from ($\vec p, \vec n$) data for $240 \leq q \leq 380$ MeV/$c$ are $\max R_\alpha \simeq 1.2 - 1.7 \times 10^{-2}$ MeV$^{-1}$, while the estimated correlation contributions are $\Delta R_L \simeq 2.6 \times 10^{-4}$ MeV$^{-1}$ (constant model) to $3.8 \times 10^{-4}$ MeV$^{-1}$ (exponential model), and $\Delta R_T \simeq -4.5 \times 10^{-4}$ MeV$^{-1}$, all at the few percent level of the peak.
- The ratio $R_L/R_T$ is estimated to show effects of order $10\%$ in the quasifree peak region, comparable to experimental uncertainties (counting $\leq 10\%$, systematic $6-8\%$, model extraction $20\%, 10\%$).
- For $q = 400$ MeV/$c$, the sum function $S_L(q) = 1.11$; the kinematic cutoff in DIS reduces the fraction of $\Delta S_L$ included in the pion distribution integral to values below 1.0 for $y \simeq 0.3$, effectively eliminating the excess pion contribution.

## Context

This work extends the correlated nuclear structure calculations of the Argonne and Urbana groups (Benhar and Pandharipande, 1993) to the interpretation of ($\vec p, \vec n$) spin transfer experiments at LAMPF (Chen et al., 1993; Prout et al., 1995). It directly contradicts the conclusions of Bertsch, Frankfurt, and Strikman (1993) and Miller (1993), who argued that the experimental data rule out the pion excess predicted by meson theory. The paper also addresses the EMC effect analysis of Ericson and Thomas (1983) and the dimuon production experiments of Alde et al. (1990), showing that kinematic constraints in the Sullivan process reduce the expected pion contribution. The analysis enables a reinterpretation of these experiments as consistent with conventional correlated nuclear theory, and suggests that the Landau parameter $g'$ should be $\geq 0.9$ rather than the collective RPA value of $0.6-0.7$.
