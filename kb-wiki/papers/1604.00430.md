# The influence of quantum vacuum friction on pulsars

> We firstly revisit the energy loss mechanism known as quantum vacuum friction (QVF), clarifying some of its subtleties. Then we investigate the observables that could easily differentiate QVF from the classical magnetic dipole radiation for pulsars with braking indices (n) measured accurately. We show this is specially the case for the time evolution of a pulsar's magnetic dipole direction ($\dotϕ$) and surface magnetic field ($\dot{B}_0$). As it is well known in the context of the classic magnetic dipole radiation, $n<3$ would only be possible for positive $(\dot{B}_0/B_0 + \dotϕ/\tanϕ)$, which, for instance, leads to $\dot{B}_0>0$ ($\dotϕ>0$) when $ϕ$ ($B_0$) is constant. On the other hand, we show that QVF can result in very contrasting predictions with respect to the above ones. Finally, even in the case $\dot{B}_0$ in both aforesaid models for a pulsar has the same sign, for a given $ϕ$, we show that they give rise to different associated timescales, which could be another way to falsify QVF.

## Key claim

Quantum vacuum friction (QVF) can produce braking indices \(n < 3\) naturally when included alongside classical magnetic dipole radiation, but the model's consistency with observed pulsars requires either non-constant surface magnetic fields or non-constant inclination angles, and the predicted timescales for these changes can differ dramatically from classical predictions, providing a way to falsify QVF.

## Method

The authors derived the energy loss rate due to QVF by treating the nonlinear QED vacuum as an effective magnetized medium that exerts a backreaction torque on a rotating magnetic dipole, using the Euler-Heisenberg Lagrangian in the weak-field limit. They then combined this QVF power with the classical magnetic dipole radiation power to obtain the total spin-down evolution of a pulsar's period, and derived expressions for the braking index \(n\) under assumptions of constant or time-varying magnetic field \(B_0\) and inclination angle \(\phi\). For nine pulsars with accurately measured braking indices, they solved for the inclination angle \(\phi\) (where possible) and for the time derivative \(\dot{\phi}\) assuming a representative magnetic field decay rate \(\dot{B}_0 = -0.05\) G/s.

## Key numbers

* For the Crab pulsar (PSR B0531+21), QVF predicts \(\dot{\phi} \approx 2.3 \times 10^{-12}\) rad/s for \(\phi = \pi/4\) and \(\dot{B}_0 = -0.05\) G/s, matching the observationally inferred value of \(\dot{\phi} \simeq 3 \times 10^{-12}\) rad/s in sign and order of magnitude.
* For PSR J1734-3333, the braking index is \(n = 0.9 \pm 0.2\); adopting \(n = 1.01\) yields \(\phi \sim 28.6^\circ\) and \(\dot{\phi} \approx 0.02 \times 10^{-12}\) rad/s.
* For PSR J1846-0258, PSR J1119-6127, and PSR B1509-58, no \(\phi\) could be found from Eq. (sin) using fiducial parameters; requiring \(M = 1.4 M_\odot\) would force radii larger than 29 km, 38 km, and 29 km respectively, which are unrealistic for neutron stars.
* The QVF torque magnitude is \(\langle\vec{N}\rangle = -\frac{\alpha}{75}\frac{B_0^4 R^4 \sin^2\phi}{B_c^2 \pi c} \vec{\omega}\), where \(B_c = m_e^2 c^3/(e\hbar) \approx 4.4 \times 10^{13}\) G.

## Context

This work extends the QVF mechanism proposed by Dupays et al. (2008, 2012) by applying it to pulsars with measured braking indices and comparing its predictions to the classical magnetic dipole model (Pacini 1968, Gold 1968, Gunn & Ostriker 1969). The authors show that QVF can explain braking indices \(n < 3\) without requiring magnetic field growth, unlike the classical model where \(n < 3\) forces \(\dot{B}_0 > 0\) or \(\dot{\phi} > 0\). The analysis enables future observational tests: measurements of \(\dot{\phi}\) for pulsars where QVF predicts negative values (e.g., PSR J1846-0258, PSR J1119-6127, PSR B1509-58) could falsify either model, and the predicted magnetic field decay timescales of \(10^3\)–\(10^4\) years for some pulsars contrast with the classical model's prediction of field growth on timescales of \(10^2\)–\(10^3\) years (Musslimov et al. 1996).
