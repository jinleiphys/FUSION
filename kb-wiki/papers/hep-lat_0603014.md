# QCD Critical Point and Complex Chemical Potential Singularities

> The thermodynamic singularities of QCD in the plane of complex baryo-chemical potential mu are studied. Predictions are made using scaling and universality arguments in the vicinity of the massless quark limit. The results are illustrated by a calculation of complex mu singularities in a random matrix model at finite temperature. Implications for lattice QCD simulations aimed at locating the QCD critical point are discussed.

## Key claim

The convergence radius of the Taylor expansion of QCD thermodynamic quantities around zero chemical potential is determined by the location of complex singularities (branch cuts) in the complex baryo-chemical potential plane; for sufficiently small quark masses, the minimum convergence radius scales as \(m^{0.54}\) and vanishes in the chiral limit, but this scaling is derived using universality and scaling arguments near the massless quark limit, not from first-principles QCD.

## Method

The paper applies scaling and universality arguments from statistical mechanics to the QCD phase diagram, identifying the thermal variable \(t \sim \mu^2 - \mu_c^2(T)\) and the symmetry-breaking variable \(h \sim m\) (quark mass) near the O(4) chiral transition. The location of branch cuts (Stokes boundaries) and branching points in the complex \(\mu\) plane is determined from the analytic structure of the singular part of the free energy, using the electrostatic analogy for partition function zeros. These universal predictions are then illustrated by explicit calculation of the complex \(\mu\) singularities in a random matrix model (introduced by Halasz et al.) at finite temperature, solved in the thermodynamic limit via the replica trick and at finite matrix size \(N=120\) by numerical root-finding.

## Key numbers

* The minimum convergence radius scales as \(\min_T \mu_R(T) \sim m^{1/(2\beta\delta)}\), where \(1/(\beta\delta) \approx 0.54\) using O(4) critical exponents in 3 dimensions.
* The angle of the branch cuts relative to the negative real axis at zero quark mass is \(\varphi \approx 77^\circ\), estimated from \(\alpha \approx -0.25\) and \(A_+/A_- \approx 1.6\).
* The angle of the shift of the branching point at nonzero quark mass is \(\psi = \pi/(2\beta\delta) \approx 48^\circ\).
* At the tricritical point (mean field), \(\varphi = 60^\circ\) and \(\psi = 72^\circ\); in the random matrix model near the second-order line, \(\varphi = 45^\circ\) and \(\psi = 60^\circ\).
* The random matrix model parameters are dimensionless: \(T\) in units of \(T_c = 160\) MeV, \(\mu\) in units of 2.27 GeV, and \(m\) in units of 100 MeV; the model is solved for \(N=120\).
* The temperature at which the minimum radius occurs scales as \(T - T_c \sim m^{1/(\beta\delta)} \sim m^{0.54}\).
* At the critical endpoint, the nonanalytic contribution to \(\mu_R^2(T)\) is of order \((T_E - T)^{\beta\delta}\) with \(\beta\delta \approx 1.56\) for the 3d Ising model (mean field value 3/2 in the random matrix model).

## Context

This work extends the Lee-Yang (1952) and Fisher (1965) analysis of partition function zeros in the complex plane to QCD at finite temperature and baryo-chemical potential, building on earlier universality studies by Itzykson, Pearson, and Zuber (1983). It provides a theoretical foundation for interpreting lattice QCD Taylor expansion methods (Allton et al., Gavai et al., Lombardo) aimed at locating the QCD critical point, and predicts that the convergence radius shrinks with decreasing quark mass, vanishing in the chiral limit. The results enable improved extrapolation techniques (e.g., Pade approximants) that incorporate the universal singular behavior, and offer a cross-check for lattice Monte Carlo simulations by predicting the expected pattern of partition function zeros.
