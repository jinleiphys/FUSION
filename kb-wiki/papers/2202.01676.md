# Neutrino Astronomy with IMB, Kamiokande and Super Kamiokande

> Some of the earliest work on neutrino astronomy was accomplished by a class of underground detectors primarily designed for particle physics goals . These detectors used inexpensive water to obtain the large masses needed to observe the very low interaction rates expected from neutrinos. They exploited the relatively large light attenuation length and the index of refraction of the water to get a very inexpensive cost per thousand tons of detector. The results obtained from these pioneering neutrino detectors have included real time observation of solar neutrinos, supernova neutrinos, and atmospheric neutrinos. Searches for neutrino point sources, dark matter and primordial magnetic monopoles were also made using them.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the earliest neutrino astronomy was accomplished by underground water Cherenkov detectors originally designed for proton decay searches, and that these detectors, despite being built for particle physics goals, produced the first real-time observations of solar, supernova, and atmospheric neutrinos, while also enabling searches for dark matter and magnetic monopoles. The hedge is that the detectors were not originally optimized for astrophysics; their astrophysical results emerged as secondary or serendipitous outcomes of proton decay and neutrino oscillation studies.

## Method

The paper reviews the design and operation of three imaging water Cherenkov detectors (IMB, Kamiokande, Super-Kamiokande) and one segmented liquid scintillation detector (MACRO). These detectors used large masses of water (or, for MACRO, liquid scintillator) viewed by arrays of photomultiplier tubes to detect Cherenkov light from charged particles produced by neutrino interactions via charged-current and neutral-current weak interactions. The detectors reconstructed event direction, energy, and particle type from the Cherenkov ring patterns, and used time-of-flight and spatial coincidence methods (e.g., for inverse beta decay) to identify neutrino signals and reject backgrounds.

## Key numbers

* IMB detector: 8000 tons of water viewed by 2048 photomultiplier tubes; operated from 1979 to 1992.
* Kamiokande detector: 3000 tons of water viewed by 1000 20-inch photomultiplier tubes; constructed in 1982.
* Super-Kamiokande detector: 50,000 tons of water in a cylindrical tank 40 meters high and 40 meters in diameter, viewed by 13,000 20-inch photomultiplier tubes.
* MACRO detector: 76.6 m long, 12 m wide, 9.3 m high; total mass of 476 scintillators about 600 tons; installed at a depth of about 3700 hg/cm².
* Neutrino-nucleon cross section at 1 GeV: about σ = 10⁻³⁸ cm² GeV⁻¹ per neutrino per nucleon; scattering length for a 1 GeV neutrino in water is about 1.7 × 10¹⁴ cm.
* Neutrino-electron cross section at 1 MeV: about 10⁻⁴⁵ cm² for ν_μ or ν_τ, and about 10⁻⁴⁴ cm² for ν_e.
* Glashow resonance threshold: 6.3 PeV; cross section expected to increase by about a factor of 200 near this resonance.
* Super-Kamiokande lower bound on proton lifetime: τ/Br(p → e⁺ π⁰) > 1.6 × 10³⁴ years (no candidate events); τ/Br(p → μ⁺ π⁰) > 7.7 × 10³³ years (two candidates, 0.87 background expected).
* Atmospheric neutrino flux peaks near 1 GeV.
* Neutrino oscillation parameter range probed by atmospheric neutrinos: Δm²_Atm from about 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻² eV².
* Expected ν_e/ν_μ flux ratio from atmospheric neutrinos: 0.64; Kamiokande measured 0.36 ± 0.08; Nusex measured 0.28 ± 0.11; IMB observed 26% muon decay fraction, corresponding to ν_e/ν_μ ≈ 1.3 under certain assumptions.

## Context

This review extends the history of neutrino astronomy from the DUMAND concept (1973) and the Sulak (1978) proposal for massive water Cherenkov detectors, through the IMB, Kamiokande, and Super-Kamiokande experiments. It documents how proton decay searches led to the first atmospheric neutrino anomaly (IMB, 1980s) and the eventual discovery of neutrino oscillations by Super-Kamiokande (1998). The paper enables future work by summarizing the detector technologies and oscillation parameters that must be accounted for in interpreting astrophysical neutrino signals, and notes that only the sun has yielded a positive astrophysical signal so far from these detectors.
