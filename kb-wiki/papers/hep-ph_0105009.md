# The Minimal CFL-Nuclear Interface

> At nuclear matter density, electrically neutral strongly interacting matter in weak equilibrium is made of neutrons, protons and electrons. At sufficiently high density, such matter is made of up, down and strange quarks in the color-flavor locked phase, with no electrons. As a function of increasing density (or, perhaps, increasing depth in a compact star) other phases may intervene between these two phases which are guaranteed to be present. The simplest possibility, however, is a single first order phase transition between CFL and nuclear matter. Such a transition, in space, could take place either through a mixed phase region or at a single sharp interface with electron-free CFL and electron-rich nuclear matter in stable contact. Here we construct a model for such an interface. It is characterized by a region of separated charge, similar to an inversion layer at a metal-insulator boundary. On the CFL side, the charged boundary layer is dominated by a condensate of negative kaons. We then consider the energetics of the mixed phase alternative. We find that the mixed phase will occur only if the nuclear-CFL surface tension is significantly smaller than dimensional analysis would indicate.

## Key claim

The paper constructs a model for a sharp interface between electron-free CFL quark matter and electron-rich nuclear matter, finding that such an interface is characterized by charged boundary layers tens of fermi wide, and that a mixed phase region between the two phases will occur only if the nuclear-CFL surface tension is significantly smaller than the value suggested by naive dimensional analysis (approximately 300 MeV/fm²).

## Method

The authors used a Thomas-Fermi description for fermions (electrons and protons) and a Landau-Ginzburg description for kaons on the CFL side, solving a self-consistent Poisson equation for the electrostatic potential across the interface. The CFL phase was described using a free energy expression including pairing contributions and a bag constant, while the nuclear matter side was modeled using a Walecka-type relativistic mean-field theory with omega, rho, and sigma mesons, with coupling constants chosen to reproduce five empirical properties of nuclear matter at saturation density.

## Key numbers

- The electron Debye length setting the scale for the charged boundary layer thickness is approximately 10 fm
- The boundary layer surface tension calculated for the single interface is about 420 MeV/fm²
- The mixed phase is more stable than the single interface only if the QCD-scale surface tension σ_QCD is less than approximately 40 MeV/fm²
- Naive dimensional analysis suggests σ_QCD ~ 300 MeV/fm²
- For the particular parameter set analyzed, nuclear matter at the interface has baryon density 2.1 n_0 (where n_0 = 0.16 fm⁻³ is nuclear saturation density) and energy density 343 MeV/fm³, while CFL matter has baryon and energy density both about twice as large
- The CFL phase is stable relative to unpaired quark matter when the pairing gap Δ > m_s²/(4μ)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Glendenning (1992) on mixed phases in neutron star interiors, and is reminiscent of earlier calculations by Alcock, Farhi, and Olinto analyzing electric fields at the interface between vacuum and unpaired quark matter. The paper enables future investigation of how a density discontinuity at the nuclear-CFL interface affects the properties of static compact stars and the dynamics of binary inspiral, while also establishing criteria for whether a mixed phase region or a single sharp interface is more likely to occur in neutron star cores.
