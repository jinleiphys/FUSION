# Symmetry-guided large-scale shell-model theory

> In this review, we present a symmetry-guided strategy that utilizes exact as well as partial symmetries for enabling a deeper understanding of and advancing ab initio studies for determining the microscopic structure of atomic nuclei. These symmetries expose physically relevant degrees of freedom that, for large-scale calculations with QCD-inspired interactions, allow the model space size to be reduced through a very structured selection of the basis states to physically relevant subspaces. This can guide explorations of simple patterns in nuclei and how they emerge from first principles, as well as extensions of the theory beyond current limitations toward heavier nuclei and larger model spaces. This is illustrated for the ab initio symmetry-adapted no-core shell model (SA-NCSM) and two significant underlying symmetries, the symplectic Sp(3,R) group and its deformation-related SU(3) subgroup. We review the broad scope of nuclei, where these symmetries have been found to play a key role: from light to intermediate-mass nuclei, based on first-principle explorations; through the Hoyle state in C-12, within a no-core shell-model perspective; up to strongly deformed species of the rare-earth and actinide regions, as investigated in earlier studies. A complementary picture, driven by symmetries dual to Sp(3,R), is also discussed. We briefly review symmetry-guided techniques that prove useful in various nuclear-theory models, such as Elliott model, ab initio SA-NCSM, symplectic model, pseudo-SU(3) and pseudo-symplectic models, ab initio hyperspherical harmonics method, ab initio lattice effective field theory, exact pairing-plus-shell model approaches, and cluster models. Important implications of these approaches that have deepened our understanding of emergent phenomena in nuclei, such as enhanced collectivity, giant resonances, pairing, halo, and clustering, are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that exact and partial symmetries, particularly the symplectic Sp(3,R) group and its SU(3) subgroup, can guide the reduction of model space size in ab initio nuclear structure calculations by selecting physically relevant basis states, but this strategy is conditional on the degree to which the chosen symmetry is preserved by the nuclear interaction, if the symmetry is largely broken, convergence requires many basis states and the approach loses its advantage.

## Method

The authors review the symmetry-adapted no-core shell model (SA-NCSM), which constructs many-nucleon basis states using SU(3)×SU(2)_S configurations labeled by (λ μ) quantum numbers and intrinsic spin S, truncated by a cutoff N_max on the maximum number of harmonic oscillator quanta above the minimum for a given nucleus. The basis states are built by first enumerating SU(3)×SU(2)_S configurations for each major harmonic oscillator shell via the U(Ω_η)⊃SU(3) reduction, then coupling these inter-shell configurations, and finally coupling proton and neutron configurations to good total quantum numbers (λ μ)κ L, intrinsic spins S_p, S_n, and S, and total angular momentum J. Hamiltonian matrix elements are computed using an SU(3)-type Wigner-Eckhart theorem and group-theoretical methods implemented in the LSU3shell code.

## Key numbers

- The ground-state quadrupole moment of ^6Li is Q(1^+_gs) = -0.0818(17) e fm^2 (Tilley 2002), which the SA-NCSM closely reproduces while showing that L=0 configurations contribute approximately 87% to the ground state.
- For ^12C and ^16O, typically one or two symplectic irreducible representations (vertical cones) suffice to represent in excess of about 80% of the ab initio wave functions.
- In the Elliott model with an SU(3)-preserving interaction plus pairing for ^22Ne, comparable results to full sd-shell calculations are obtained in a truncated model space that includes only about 10 most deformed configurations.
- For 4 protons (or neutrons) in the pf shell (η=3 with Ω_η=10), the SU(3)×SU(2)_S configurations include irreps such as (8 2), (7 1), (4 4), (5 2), (0 6), (6 0), (3 3), (1 4), (4 1), (2 2), (1 1) for S=0; (9 0), (6 3), (7 1), (4 4), (2 5), (5 2), (3 3), (1 4), (4 1), (2 2), (0 3), (3 0), (1 1) for S=1; and (5 2), (0 6), (3 3), (2 2), (3 0) for S=2.

## Context

This review extends the line of work from the Elliott model (Elliott 1958) and the symplectic model (Rosensteel and Rowe 1977, Rowe 1985) by incorporating ab initio interactions into a symmetry-guided framework, and it contrasts with conventional no-core shell model (NCSM) approaches (Navratil 2000, Barrett 2013) that suffer from combinatorial growth of basis states. The SA-NCSM enables first-principles studies of emergent phenomena such as enhanced collectivity, giant resonances, pairing, halo, and clustering in light to intermediate-mass nuclei, and it provides a path toward extending ab initio calculations to heavier nuclei and larger model spaces that are currently inaccessible to conventional methods.
