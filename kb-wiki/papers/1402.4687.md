# First calculation of cosmic-ray muon spallation backgrounds for MeV astrophysical neutrino signals in Super-Kamiokande

> When muons travel through matter, their energy losses lead to nuclear breakup ("spallation") processes. The delayed decays of unstable daughter nuclei produced by cosmic-ray muons are important backgrounds for low-energy astrophysical neutrino experiments, e.g., those seeking to detect solar neutrino or Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB) signals. Even though Super-Kamiokande has strong general cuts to reduce these spallation-induced backgrounds, the remaining rate before additional cuts for specific signals is much larger than the signal rates for kinetic energies of about 6 -- 18 MeV. Surprisingly, there is no published calculation of the production and properties of these backgrounds in water, though there are such studies for scintillator. Using the simulation code FLUKA and theoretical insights, we detail how muons lose energy in water, produce secondary particles, how and where these secondaries produce isotopes, and the properties of the backgrounds from their decays. We reproduce Super-Kamiokande measurements of the total background to within a factor of 2, which is good given that the isotope yields vary by orders of magnitude and that some details of the experiment are unknown to us at this level. Our results break aggregate data into component isotopes, reveal their separate production mechanisms, and preserve correlations between them. We outline how to implement more effective background rejection techniques using this information. Reducing backgrounds in solar and DSNB studies by even a factor of a few could help lead to important new discoveries.

## Key claim

The authors present the first detailed calculation of cosmic-ray muon spallation backgrounds in water, using the FLUKA simulation code, and reproduce Super-Kamiokande measurements of the total background to within a factor of 2, noting that this agreement is good given that isotope yields vary by orders of magnitude and that some experimental details are unknown to them.

## Method

The authors used the Monte Carlo code FLUKA (version 2011.2b.3) to simulate muon energy loss and secondary particle production in a water target matching the Super-Kamiokande detector geometry, including a 22.5 kton fiducial volume and 2 m of surrounding rock. They simulated vertically down-going muons with the energy spectrum at Super-K (average energy 271 GeV) and calculated isotope yields per muon path length, breaking aggregate data into component isotopes with their separate production mechanisms and preserving correlations between them.

## Key numbers

- Muon flux at Super-K is reduced from 6.0 × 10^5 m^-2 hr^-1 at sea level to 9.6 m^-2 hr^-1, corresponding to a muon rate of about 2 Hz in the detector
- Average muon energy at Super-K is 271 GeV, with most muons in the range 30–700 GeV
- Muon critical energy in water is about 1000 GeV, above which radiative energy losses dominate
- The sum of unstable isotope yields is 58 × 10^-7 μ^-1 g^-1 cm^2, corresponding to about 0.02 unstable isotopes per muon (multiplying by the vertical distance of 3220 cm)
- The sum of stable or invisible isotope yields is about 2950 × 10^-7 μ^-1 g^-1 cm^2, or about 0.9 isotopes per muon
- Neutron yield is 2030 × 10^-7 μ^-1 g^-1 cm^2
- The dominant background isotope 16N has a yield of 18 × 10^-7 μ^-1 g^-1 cm^2 (total) and 18 × 10^-7 μ^-1 g^-1 cm^2 (above 3.5 MeV threshold)
- The 16N yield from the dominant channel 16O(n,p)16N is 14 × 10^-7 μ^-1 g^-1 cm^2, compared to 23 × 10^-7 μ^-1 g^-1 cm^2 found by Galbiati (2005)
- Average neutron distance from muon track in water is 74 cm, compared to 81.5 cm in scintillator (Bellini 2013)
- 98% of neutrons are contained within 3 m of the muon track
- The remaining spallation background rate after Super-K cuts (∼90% rejection efficiency) is still ∼10 times greater than the solar neutrino signal rate above several MeV

## Context

This work extends the approach of spallation studies done for scintillator detectors such as KamLAND (Abe 2010) and Borexino (Bellini 2013) to water-based detectors, specifically Super-Kamiokande, for which no such calculation had been published. The results enable more effective background rejection techniques for solar neutrino and Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB) searches by breaking aggregate data into component isotopes, revealing their separate production mechanisms, and preserving correlations between them. The authors note that reducing backgrounds by even a factor of a few could help improve the significance of the 2.7-sigma hint of the day-night effect from neutrino mixing in Earth and could help lead to the first detection of the hep neutrino flux.
