# Bayesian analysis of neutron-star properties with parameterized equations of state: the role of the likelihood functions

> We have investigated the systematic differences introduced when performing a Bayesian-inference analysis of the equation of state of neutron stars employing either variable- or constant-likelihood functions. The former have the advantage that it retains the full information on the distributions of the measurements, making an exhaustive usage of the data. The latter, on the other hand, have the advantage of a much simpler implementation and reduced computational costs. In both approaches, the EOSs have identical priors and have been built using the sound-speed parameterization method so as to satisfy the constraints from X-ray and gravitational-waves observations, as well as those from Chiral Effective Theory and perturbative QCD. In all cases, the two approaches lead to very similar results and the $90\%$-confidence levels are essentially overlapping. Some differences do appear, but in regions where the probability density is extremely small and are mostly due to the sharp cutoff set on the binary tidal deformability $\tilde Λ\leq 720$ employed in the constant-likelihood analysis. Our analysis has also produced two additional results. First, a clear inverse correlation between the normalized central number density of a maximally massive star, $n_{\rm c, TOV}/n_s$, and the radius of a maximally massive star, $R_{\rm TOV}$. Second, and most importantly, it has confirmed the relation between the chirp mass $\mathcal{M}_{\rm chirp}$ and the binary tidal deformability $\tildeΛ$. The importance of this result is that it relates a quantity that is measured very accurately, $\mathcal{M}_{\rm chirp}$, with a quantity that contains important information on the micro-physics, $\tildeΛ$. Hence, once $\mathcal{M}_{\rm chirp}$ is measured in future detections, our relation has the potential of setting tight constraints on $\tildeΛ$.

## Key claim

The paper systematically compares Bayesian-inference analyses of neutron-star equations of state using variable-likelihood versus constant-likelihood (sharp-cutoff) functions, finding that the two approaches yield very similar results with essentially overlapping 90% confidence levels, with differences appearing only in regions of extremely small probability density mostly due to the sharp cutoff on binary tidal deformability.

## Method

The authors constructed equation-of-state models using the sound-speed parameterization method with 11 piecewise-linear segments for the sound speed as a function of chemical potential, imposing constraints from Chiral Effective Theory at low densities and perturbative QCD at high densities. They performed Bayesian inference with over 1.5 x 10^5 posterior EOSs using either variable likelihood functions (Gaussian distributions for mass measurements, kernel density estimation for NICER mass-radius data, and interpolated likelihood for GW170817) or constant likelihood functions (Heaviside step functions with sharp cutoffs at M_TOV >= 2.01 M_sun, R > 10.8 km at 1.1 M_sun, R > 10.75 km at 2.0 M_sun, and binary tidal deformability <= 720).

## Key numbers

- For the variable likelihood (VL) method: radius of 1.4 M_sun neutron star R_1.4 = 12.2_{-0.9}^{+0.9} km (90% confidence)
- For the constant likelihood (CL) method: R_1.4 = 12.4_{-1.0}^{+0.5} km (90% confidence)
- VL method: radius of maximum-mass star R_TOV = 11.8_{-1.0}^{+1.3} km
- CL method: R_TOV = 12.1_{-1.3}^{+1.1} km
- VL method: tidal deformability at 1.4 M_sun Lambda_1.4 = 450_{-190}^{+230}
- CL method: Lambda_1.4 = 490_{-220}^{+130}
- VL method: central sound speed squared at 1.4 M_sun (c^2_s,c)_1.4 = 0.60_{-0.22}^{+0.23}
- CL method: (c^2_s,c)_1.4 = 0.63_{-0.25}^{+0.23}
- VL method: central number density of maximum-mass star n_c,TOV = 5.5_{-1.2}^{+1.2} n_s
- CL method: n_c,TOV = 5.2_{-1.1}^{+1.5} n_s
- Chirp mass of GW170817: M_chirp = 1.186^{+0.001}_{-0.001} M_sun
- Binary tidal deformability upper bound: Lambda_tilde <= 720

## Context

This work extends the constant-likelihood approach of Altiparmak (2022) and Ecker (2022) by performing a full Bayesian analysis with variable likelihoods using the same sound-speed parameterization method, enabling a direct comparison of the two inference techniques. The paper confirms the inverse correlation between normalized central number density and radius of maximally massive stars, and establishes a relation between chirp mass and binary tidal deformability that could allow future gravitational-wave detections to set tight constraints on the equation of state. The results enable more informed choices between computationally expensive variable-likelihood analyses and simpler constant-likelihood methods for future neutron-star EOS inference studies.
