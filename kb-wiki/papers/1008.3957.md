# Manifestations of a spatial variation of fundamental constants on atomic clocks, Oklo, meteorites, and cosmological phenomena

> The remarkable detection of a spatial variation in the fine-structure constant, alpha, from quasar absorption systems must be independently confirmed by complementary searches. In this letter, we discuss how terrestrial measurements of time-variation of the fundamental constants in the laboratory, meteorite data, and analysis of the Oklo nuclear reactor can be used to corroborate the spatial variation seen by astronomers. Furthermore, we show that spatial variation of the fundamental constants may be observable as spatial anisotropy in the cosmic microwave background, the accelerated expansion (dark energy), and large-scale structure of the Universe.

# Manifestations of a spatial variation of fundamental constants on atomic clocks, Oklo, meteorites, and cosmological phenomena

## Key claim

The spatial variation in the fine-structure constant α detected from quasar absorption systems (the "Australian dipole") can be independently corroborated by terrestrial measurements, but current laboratory limits on time-variation of α must improve by two orders of magnitude to compete with the astronomical data, and none of the existing terrestrial limits contradict the reported spatial gradient.

## Method

The authors model the spatial variation of α as a dipole in the cosmic microwave background rest-frame, described by δα/α₀ = (1.10±0.25)×10⁻⁶ r cosψ, where r is light-travel distance in gigalightyears and ψ is the angle from the dipole axis. They then compute the expected signals in three terrestrial systems: atomic clock comparisons (using the Earth's motion through the CMB rest-frame), the Oklo natural nuclear reactor (extrapolating galactic motion over 1.8 billion years), and meteorite Re-Os decay rates (over 4.6 billion years), relating each to the dipole model via coupling coefficients between α and other fundamental constants.

## Key numbers

- Australian dipole amplitude: δα/α₀ = (1.10±0.25)×10⁻⁶ r cosψ, with dipole axis at (17.4±0.6 h, −58±6°) in equatorial coordinates
- Expected yearly variation in laboratory α measurements: 1.35×10⁻¹⁸ cosψ yr⁻¹, with best-fit cosψ ≈ 0.07 (uncertainty ∼0.1)
- Annual modulation from Earth's orbit: δα/α = 1.4×10⁻²⁰ cos ωt
- Current best laboratory limit on α time-variation: (−1.6±2.3)×10⁻¹⁷ yr⁻¹ from Hg⁺ and Al⁺ clocks (Rosenband 2008)
- Expected α-variation at Oklo time: δα/α ∼ 10⁻⁹, corresponding to resonance energy shift δE_r|_Oklo ≈ 1 meV − R_q^α × 10 meV
- Current best 2σ Oklo limits on resonance energy shift: −12 < δE_r < 26 meV (Gould 2006) and −73 < δE_r < 62 meV (Petrov 2006)
- Expected relative difference between meteorite Re decay rate and laboratory rate: (λ̄ − λ₀)/λ₀ = −2.2×10⁻⁵
- Current meteorite decay rate: λ̄ = 1.666(9)×10⁻¹¹ yr⁻¹ (Smoliar 1996); laboratory rate: λ₀ = 1.68(5)×10⁻¹¹ yr⁻¹ (Galeazzi 2000), giving current accuracy ∼10⁻²

## Context

This paper extends the detection of a spatial gradient in α from quasar absorption systems (Webb 2010) by providing a framework to test the dipole model using terrestrial experiments. It builds on earlier work showing hints of time-varying α from Keck data (Webb 1999, Murphy 2001-2004) and subsequent null constraints from VLT data (Srianand 2004) that were later revised with larger error bars (Murphy 2007-2008). The authors show how atomic clock comparisons (Rosenband 2008), Oklo reactor analysis (Gould 2006, Petrov 2006), and meteorite Re-Os dating (Smoliar 1996, Dyson 1967, Olive 2002) can be reinterpreted as spatial rather than purely temporal variation tests, enabling independent confirmation of the astronomical dipole. The work also suggests that spatial variation may be observable in cosmic microwave background anisotropy, supernova redshift-luminosity relationships, and large-scale structure.
