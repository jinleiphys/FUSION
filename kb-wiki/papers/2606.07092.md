# Impact of Shape Coexistence on Nuclear Stability

> Nuclear decay properties are conventionally predicted assuming nuclei decay from their ground-state configurations. However, this often neglects a fundamental structural complexity which is the phenomenon of shape coexistence, where nuclei possess multiple competing configurations at nearly degenerate energies. When both parent and daughter nuclei can exist in different energy minima, multiple decay pathways become possible. We systematically investigate how shape coexistence influences nuclear decay for approximately 1500 even-even nuclei ($8 \leq Z \leq 118$, $8 \leq N \leq 184$) using the Nilsson-Strutinsky method and relativistic mean-field calculations with NL3$^*$, DD-ME2, and DD-PC1 functionals. We identify around 400 nuclei exhibiting competing energy minima separated by less than 1 MeV. For these shape-coexisting nuclei, we calculate $α$, $β^+$ and $β^-$ decay half-lives considering all possible transition pathways between the competing minima. Our results demonstrate that shape coexistence substantially impacts decay predictions, with half-lives showing variations up to nearly one logarithmic unit depending on which configurations participate in the transition. Comparison with experimental data from NUBASE2020 shows that pathways involving the second minimum sometimes reproduce measured lifetimes more closely than conventional ground-state to ground-state assumptions. Branching ratios exhibit even stronger sensitivity, with certain nuclei displaying complete inversions of the dominant decay mode depending on configuration choice. These pathway-dependent variations are not due to model uncertainties but reflect inherent structural effects. The correlation between the shape dynamics and nuclear stability establishes the shape coexistence as an essential component in predictive nuclear structure and astrophysics studies.

# Impact of Shape Coexistence on Nuclear Stability

## Key claim

Shape coexistence, where nuclei possess multiple competing configurations at nearly degenerate energies, substantially alters predicted alpha and beta decay half-lives and branching ratios, with variations up to nearly one logarithmic unit depending on which energy minima are assumed for parent and daughter nuclei; these pathway-dependent variations are not due to model uncertainties but reflect inherent structural effects, and pathways involving the second minimum sometimes reproduce measured lifetimes from NUBASE2020 more closely than conventional ground-state to ground-state assumptions.

## Method

The authors mapped potential energy surfaces as functions of quadrupole deformation for approximately 1500 even-even nuclei (Z=8-118, N=8-184) using the Nilsson-Strutinsky method and three relativistic mean-field functionals (NL3*, DD-ME2, DD-PC1). They identified approximately 400 nuclei exhibiting competing energy minima separated by less than 1 MeV, then refined to 80 nuclei where all four models agreed on near-degeneracy within this threshold. For these shape-coexisting nuclei, they calculated alpha, beta-plus, and beta-minus decay half-lives using deformation-dependent semi-empirical formulas across four possible transition pathways between competing minima in parent and daughter nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Approximately 1500 even-even nuclei analyzed across the range 8 ≤ Z ≤ 118 and 8 ≤ N ≤ 184
- Around 400 nuclei identified with competing energy minima separated by less than 1 MeV
- Refined set of 80 nuclei exhibiting shape coexistence within ΔE ≤ 1.0 MeV across all four theoretical models
- 24 nuclei from the refined set had experimental half-lives and decay modes available in NUBASE2020
- For 100Sr, experimental log10(T1/2) ≈ -0.70 s; predictions across four pathways ranged from approximately -0.85 s to -0.40 s depending on model
- For 100Zr, experimental log10(T1/2) ≈ 0.88 s; predictions ranged from approximately 0.7 s to 1.75 s depending on pathway
- Half-life variations of 0.3 to 0.9 logarithmic units depending on which energy minima the parent and daughter nuclei occupy
- For 178Hg, pathway selection determined complete dominance reversal: 100% alpha through G-G' and S-G' pathways versus 100% beta-plus/EC through G-S' and S-S' pathways (NSM results)
- For 186Pb, NSM predicted 88% alpha branching through G-G' and 100% through S-G', but G-S' inverted to 79% beta-plus/EC
- Parametric uncertainty from deformation-dependent decay formulas amounted to approximately 0.04-0.05 s in log10(T1/2)
- For 194W, NL3* predictions spanned approximately 0.25 s to 2.06 s in log10(T1/2) across pathways

## Context

This work extends the extensive investigation of shape coexistence reviewed by Heyde and Wood (2011) and Garrett and collaborators (2024), who documented experimental evidence in regions including Pb-Hg isotopes around Z≈82 (Andreyev 2000), the A≈100 region at N=60 (Clement 2016, Wu 2004), and the Cd-Sn region (Garrett 2019). While phenomenological formulas widely used for half-life predictions (Saxena 2021, Cheng 2022, Royer 2020) are parameterized in terms of mass, charge, and Q-value but do not account for deformation or distinguish between configurations, and microscopic approaches based on density functional theory (Goriely 1996) or QRPA (Marketin 2016) can capture deformation effects but are computationally restricted, this work provides a deformation-dependent, efficient framework addressing shape coexistence. The results have direct implications for astrophysical nucleosynthesis studies, where half-life uncertainties in key waiting-point nuclei propagate into uncertainties in abundance patterns, freeze-out timescales, and heavy element synthesis (Mumpower 2016, Surman 2014).
