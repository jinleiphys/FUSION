# On the unimportance of memory for the time non-local components of the Kadanoff-Baym equations

> The generalized Kadanoff-Baym ansatz (GKBA) is an approximation to the Kadanoff-Baym equations (KBE), that neglects certain memory effects that contribute to the Green's function at non-equal times. Here we present arguments and numerical results to demonstrate the practical insignificance of the quantities neglected when deriving the GKBA at conditions at which KBE and GKBA are appropriate. We provide a mathematical proof that places a scaling bound on the neglected terms, further reinforcing that these terms are typically small in comparison to terms that are kept in the GKBA. We perform calculations in a range of models, including different system sizes and filling fractions, as well as experimentally relevant non-equilibrium excitations. We find that both the GKBA and KBE capture the dynamics of interacting systems with moderate and even strong interactions well. We explicitly compute terms neglected in the GKBA approximation and show, in the scenarios tested here, that they are orders of magnitude smaller than the terms that are accounted for, i.e., they offer only a small correction when included in the full Kadanoff-Baym equations.

## Key claim

The memory integrals neglected in the Hartree-Fock generalized Kadanoff-Baym ansatz (HF-GKBA) are typically orders of magnitude smaller than the terms that are kept, and in regimes where the full Kadanoff-Baym equations (KBE) are reliable, the HF-GKBA yields dynamics of comparable quality; however, this conclusion is conditional on the system having moderate to strong interactions where many-body perturbation theory remains valid, and the authors note that in strongly interacting regimes where both methods deviate from exact results, improved self energies are more important than retaining the neglected memory terms.

## Method

The authors compared the time-dependent density from the full KBE and the HF-GKBA for a one-dimensional lattice model with Coulomb interactions (scaled by gamma = 0.5), using the second-Born self energy approximation. They prepared correlated initial states via adiabatic switching for the HF-GKBA and via Matsubara equation solution for the KBE, then applied two types of non-equilibrium excitations (dipole and X-ray) with varying interaction strengths (U = 0.1J to 1.5J), system sizes, and filling fractions, benchmarking against exact diagonalization results. They also explicitly computed the memory integrals in the exact expression for the off-diagonal lesser Green's function and compared their magnitude to the non-integral terms retained in the GKBA.

## Key numbers

- For the second-Born self energy, the neglected memory integrals scale as O(w^2 c^3) where w is the interaction strength and c is the uniform bound on the Green's function (0 < c <= 1), making them typically much smaller than the non-integral terms.
- Time steps used were 0.02 J^{-1} for HF-GKBA and 0.025 J^{-1} for KBE simulations.
- Inverse temperature beta = 40 was used for zero-temperature KBE simulations.
- The adiabatic switching used a Fermi-Dirac function with t_0 = 25 and tau = 3.
- The perturbation parameters were sigma = 0.5 J^{-1} and t_0 = 5 J^{-1}, with field amplitudes E = 0.5J and 1.0J.
- Interaction strengths tested: U = 0.1J, 0.2J, 0.5J, 1.0J, and 1.5J.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the HF-GKBA approximation originally introduced by Lipavsky (1986) and later developed by Hermanns (2012), and directly addresses the question of when the neglected memory integrals are justified. The authors build on studies by Stahl (2022) showing that full memory is not always needed, and by Kwong (1998) hinting at the unimportance of neglected effects. The results enable practitioners to confidently use the computationally cheaper HF-GKBA (especially its O(N_t) formulation by Joost (2020)) for extended systems and more advanced self energies like GW, while identifying that improved self energy formulations (e.g., Mejuto 2022) are more critical than retaining memory effects in strongly correlated regimes.
