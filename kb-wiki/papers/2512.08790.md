# The Radius of PSR J0437-4715 from NICER Data

> Neutron star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER) data have been used to estimate the masses and radii of the rotation-powered millisecond pulsars PSR J0030$+$0451, PSR J0740$+$6620, PSR J0437$-$4715, PSR J1231$-$1411, and PSR J0614$-$3329, sometimes in joint analyses with X-ray Multi-Mirror (XMM-Newton) data. These measurements provide invaluable information about the properties of cold, catalyzed matter beyond nuclear saturation density. Here we present the results of our modeling of NICER data on PSR J0437$-$4715 using several different models of hot thermal X-ray emitting spots on the stellar surface. For this pulsar, previous Nuclear Spectroscopic Telescope Array (NuSTAR) observations established that there is also a modulated nonthermal component to the emission, but the previously published analysis of NICER data did not model this component. We find that the Bayesian evidence is significantly higher when the modulated nonthermal component is included, and that omission of this component leads to poor fits to the bolometric NICER data and thus risks bias in the resulting radius estimates. Our models, which we pursue to inferential convergence, therefore have modulated nonthermal emission, and our headline model has in addition three uniform-temperature thermally-emitting circular spots. Using this model, the symmetric 68% credible range in the radius is 11.8 km to 15.1 km, which at the independently-measured mass of $M=1.418\pm 0.044~M_\odot$ is consistent with previous reports of the radius of the $\sim 1.4~M_\odot$ pulsar PSR J0030$+$0451. We discuss the implications of this measurement for the equation of state of dense matter.

## Key claim

The radius of PSR J0437-4715 inferred from NICER data is 11.8 km to 15.1 km (symmetric 68% credible range), but this result depends critically on including a modulated nonthermal power-law component in the model; omission of this component leads to poor bolometric fits and risks bias in the radius estimate.

## Method

The authors modeled NICER X-ray data (0.3-3.0 keV) on the 173.7 Hz millisecond pulsar PSR J0437-4715 using the oblate Schwarzschild approximation for ray-tracing, with fully ionized nonmagnetic hydrogen atmosphere models (NSX code) for thermal emission from hot spots on the stellar surface. They included a modulated nonthermal component modeled as a Gaussian profile in rotational phase with a power-law spectrum, based on prior NuSTAR observations, and used Bayesian inference with sequential Monte Carlo (pocomc) followed by Markov chain Monte Carlo (emcee) sampling to estimate parameters and Bayesian evidence. The headline model used three uniform-temperature circular spots plus the modulated nonthermal component, with the pulsar mass fixed to the independently measured value of 1.418 ± 0.044 solar masses from radio timing.

## Key numbers

- Radius symmetric 68% credible range: 11.8 km to 15.1 km
- Pulsar mass: M = 1.418 ± 0.044 solar masses (from radio timing by Reardon et al. 2024)
- Pulsar distance: D = 156.96 ± 0.11 pc (from Shklovskii effect on orbital period derivative)
- Orbital inclination: i = 137.506 ± 0.016 degrees
- Rotation period: P = 0.005757 seconds
- Intrinsic period derivative: P_dot_intrinsic = 1.369 × 10^-20
- Surface magnetic field estimate: B ≈ 4 × 10^8 G (for inclination π/2)
- NICER exposure after filtering: 1.310 Ms (from initial 2.736 Ms)
- AGN average intensity (0.3-3.0 keV): 6.26 counts/s, scaled by factor 1/42 for offset pointing

## Context

This work extends the NICER program of neutron star radius measurements, following prior analyses of PSR J0030+0451 (Miller et al. 2019, Riley et al. 2019), PSR J0740+6620 (Miller et al. 2021, Riley et al. 2021), and an earlier analysis of PSR J0437-4715 (Chirenti et al. 2024) that did not include the modulated nonthermal component. The result is consistent with previous radius reports for the ~1.4 solar mass pulsar PSR J0030+0451, and together with other measurements (GW170817 tidal deformability constraints, high-mass neutron star discoveries) provides constraints on the equation of state of cold, catalyzed matter beyond nuclear saturation density. The measurement enables tighter constraints on dense matter models, particularly when combined with the independent mass measurement from radio timing.
