# SQM2022: Theory Overview in Heavy-Ion Physics

## Key claim
The paper presents an overview of open questions in heavy-ion physics rather than advancing a single new result; it argues that many fundamental issues, such as the existence of a QCD critical point, the correct treatment of out-of-equilibrium dynamics, and the connection between heavy-ion collisions and neutron-star mergers, remain unresolved and require coordinated theory and simulation efforts.

## Method
The author surveys theoretical challenges across multiple subfields of heavy-ion physics, including the QCD equation of state, relativistic hydrodynamics, transport theory, strangeness and charm production, fluctuations, correlations, critical-point dynamics, and neutron-star mergers. The paper lists publicly available simulation codes (e.g., MUSIC, iEBE-VISHNU, UrQMD, AMPT) and identifies specific open problems that speakers at the SQM2022 conference addressed.

## Key numbers
- The critical region may require being within \(10^{-2}\) or \(10^{-3}\) of \(T_c\) to observe true critical exponents, beyond which mean-field exponents are measured.
- Supernovae may reach temperatures of 50 MeV; neutron stars may reach energy densities greater than 1 GeV/fm\(^3\) with correspondingly high baryon densities.
- The introduction of strange degrees of freedom (quasi-free quarks or hyperons) softens the equation of state, making it challenging to produce neutron stars with two or more solar masses.

## Context
This overview extends the tradition of theory summaries at the Strangeness in Quark Matter (SQM) conference series, which originated from interest in strange-quark production. It complements prior work by lattice practitioners (e.g., on the sign problem) and by hydrodynamic simulation groups (e.g., MUSIC, iEBE-VISHNU). The paper enables future work by identifying specific open questions, such as the beam-energy dependence of initial conditions, the transition from transport to hydrodynamics, and the role of spin hydrodynamics, that experimental and theoretical efforts at RHIC, LHC, and in neutron-star astrophysics can address.
