# r-Java 2.0: the astrophysics

> [Context:] This article is the second in a two part series introducing r-Java 2.0, a nucleosynthesis code for open use that performs r-process calculations and provides a suite of other analysis tools. [Aims:] The first paper discussed the nuclear physics inherent to r-Java 2.0 and in this article the astrophysics incorporated into the software will be detailed. [Methods:] R-Java 2.0 allows the user to specify the density and temperature evolution for an r-process simulation. Defining how the physical parameters (temperature and density) evolve can effectively simulate the astrophysical conditions for the r-process. Within r-Java 2.0 the user has the option to select astrophysical environments which have unique sets of input parameters available for the user to adjust. In this work we study three proposed r-process sites; neutrino-driven winds around a proto-neutron star, ejecta from a neutron star merger and ejecta from a quark nova. The underlying physics that define the temperature and density evolution for each site is described in this work. [Results:] In this paper a survey of the available parameters for each astrophysical site is undertaken and the effect on final r-process abundance is compared. The resulting abundances for each site are also compared to solar observations both independently and in concert. R-Java 2.0 is available for download from the website of the Quark-Nova Project: http://quarknova.ucalgary.ca/

## Key claim

The paper presents r-Java 2.0 as a flexible, open-source nucleosynthesis code that allows users to simulate r-process nucleosynthesis across three astrophysical sites (neutrino-driven winds, neutron star mergers, and quark novae), but the authors acknowledge that the high-entropy wind scenario faces challenges from modern hydrodynamic simulations that cannot produce the required extreme entropy values without artificial adjustments.

## Method

The authors implemented three distinct astrophysical modules within r-Java 2.0, each with analytic prescriptions for temperature and density evolution. For high-entropy winds, temperature follows adiabatic expansion with radiation-dominated entropy; for neutron star mergers, density evolution uses a polytropic equation of state with Newtonian dynamics including self-gravity, pressure-driven expansion, and tidal stretching; for quark novae, the code models ejection of neutron-rich crust material heated by a photon-electron-positron fireball. The code uses the HFB21 mass model with temperature-dependent neutron capture cross-sections from TALYS, beta-decay rates from Moller et al. (1995), and includes spontaneous, neutron-induced, and beta-delayed fission.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range for r-Java 2.0 simulations with default cross-sections: 0.001 < T9 < 10, where T9 is in units of 10^9 K
- For high-entropy wind simulations: initial temperature of 3×10^9 K, initial electron fraction of 0.35, with expansion velocity and entropy pairs of (3750 km s^-1:270), (1.5×10^4 km s^-1:175), and (3.0×10^4 km s^-1:140)
- For neutron star merger simulations: initial density values of 2×10^11, 3×10^11, and 4×10^11 g cm^-3 were compared, with expansion velocities of 10^2, 10^3, and 10^4 km s^-1
- For neutron star merger ejecta mass: approximately 10^-3 solar masses can be ejected per event (citing Goriely 2011), with expected Galactic rate of 10^-5 yr^-1
- For quark novae: up to 10^-2 solar masses of r-process material can be produced (citing Vogt et al. 2004), with dynamical timescales of 0.1-1 ms
- For high-entropy wind simulations with fixed neutron-to-seed ratio of 50: the lowest entropy case (S=140) produced the largest abundance of heavy nuclei
- For high-entropy wind with varying neutron-to-seed ratios: Yn/Yseed = 25 (S=175), 75 (S=250), and 100 (S=270) were compared

## Context

This paper is the second in a two-part series introducing r-Java 2.0, following the first paper (Kostka et al.) that covered the nuclear physics aspects. The work extends prior studies of r-process sites including neutrino-driven winds (Woosley et al. 1992, Qian and Woosley 1996, Thompson et al. 2001, Farouqi et al. 2010), neutron star mergers (Freiburghaus et al. 1999, Goriely et al. 2011), and quark novae (Jaikumar et al. 2007, Ouyed et al. 2009). The code enables systematic parameter surveys across multiple proposed r-process sites and comparison to solar abundance observations, addressing open questions about which astrophysical environments dominate r-process nucleosynthesis and whether multiple sites are required to explain the full solar r-process pattern.
