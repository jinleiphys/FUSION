# On the Relativistic Description of the Nucleus

> We discuss a relativistic theory of the atomic nuclei in the framework of the hamiltonian formalism and of the mesonic model of the nucleus. Attention is paid to the translational invariance of the theory. Our approach is centered on the concept of spectral amplitude, a function in the Dirac spinor space. We derive a Lorentz covariant equation for the latter, which requires as an input the baryon self-energy. For this we either postulate the most general Lorentz-Poincaré invariant expression or perform a calculation via a Bethe-Salpeter equation starting from a nucleon-nucleus interaction. We discuss the features of the nuclear spectrum obtained in the first instance. Finally the general constraints the self-energy should satisfy because of analyticity and Poincaré covariance are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper outlines a relativistic, translationally invariant Hamiltonian framework for finite nuclei based on the spectral amplitude concept, but acknowledges that the approach is not a full solution to the problem of confined states in quantum field theory; instead it pragmatically searches for relations between physical observables and the spectral amplitude, using ad hoc vacuum subtractions to handle infinities.

## Method

The authors construct a Hamiltonian model of nucleons interacting via scalar meson exchange, quantize the fermion field using free-particle expansions without normal ordering, and introduce compensating vacuum subtractions to avoid infinities. They define a spectral amplitude in Dirac spinor space that generalizes the quasiparticle concept to relativistic regimes, derive a Lorentz-covariant Dyson equation for the fermion propagator, and obtain an eigenvalue equation for the spectral amplitude that requires the baryon self-energy as input. The self-energy is either postulated in its most general Lorentz-Poincaré invariant form or calculated via a Bethe-Salpeter equation starting from a phenomenological nucleon-nucleus interaction.

## Key numbers

- The normalization condition for the spectral amplitude yields: \(\sum_{l}\int\frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3}|\phi_l({\bf k},{\bf p})|^2 = A + 4\Omega\int\frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\), where the term proportional to the normalization volume \(\Omega\) arises from the Dirac sea.
- For a free Fermi gas at zero temperature, the momentum distribution is \(n_s^F({\bf k}) = \Omega\,\theta(k_F - k)\).
- The effective mass from the canonical transformation is \(\tilde m = m - \frac{g^2}{m_\sigma^2}\langle\overline{\psi}\psi\rangle\), always lower than the bare mass regardless of the sign of the coupling constant.
- The energy of a purely nucleonic Fock state satisfies \(p_0^F > mA\), showing that such a state cannot correspond to a bound nucleus.

## Context

This work extends and deepens a previous paper by Molinari and Vagradov (1989), and contrasts with Quantum Hadrodynamics (QHD) of Walecka as reviewed by Serot and Walecka (1986) and Horowitz and Serot (1987). While QHD has been successful in the mean field approximation for both nuclear matter and finite nuclei, the authors note that beyond mean field the loop expansion converges poorly, with the two-loop contribution to binding energy being very large at nuclear matter density as shown by Perry and Walecka (1988). The present approach instead centers on the spectral amplitude as a relativistic generalization of Landau-Migdal quasiparticles, aiming to provide a framework for computing nuclear binding energy, nucleon momentum distributions, and cross sections for reactions such as exclusive \((e,e'p)\) in a Poincaré-invariant form.
