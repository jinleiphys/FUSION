# Remarks on meson loop effects on quark models

> We investigate the effect of meson loops on the spectrum of quark states. We demonstrate that in general quark states do not tend to get very broad if their coupling to the continuum increases, but instead they decouple from the latter in the large coupling limit. We ascribe this effect to the essentially nonperturbative unitarization procedure involved. In the meantime, some quark resonances behave very differently and demonstrate collectivity in the sense that their pole trajectories span a wide, as compared to the level spacing, region therefore acquiring contributions from multiple bare poles rather than from the closest neighbors. While the actual calculations are done within particular, very simplified models, it is argued that the findings might well be general.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that, contrary to naive expectations, quark states coupled to a continuum channel do not become arbitrarily broad as the coupling increases; instead, most states decouple from the continuum in the large-coupling limit, while at least one state in the spectrum exhibits a "collective" behavior with a pole trajectory spanning several level spacings. The authors emphasize that these findings come from "particular, very simplified models" but argue they "might well be general."

## Method

The authors study a system of scalar quark-model resonances \(R_n\) coupled to a single elastic continuum channel \(\varphi\bar{\varphi}\) via a Dyson equation, using a scalar loop selfenergy \(\Pi(s)\) taken to leading order in the center-of-mass momentum \(k = \frac12\sqrt{s-4m^2}\). They employ a linear confinement potential to generate the bare mass spectrum \(M_n = 2M_Q + \sqrt{4\pi\sigma (n-1)}\) and vary the overall coupling strength \(g\) from 0 to 25 GeV, tracking pole trajectories in the complex \(s\)-plane for systems with \(N=10\) and \(N=20\) states. The real part of the loop is controlled by a single subtraction constant \(\Delta\), which is varied to simulate different renormalization conditions or different quark models.

## Key numbers

* For the single-resonance case with \(M=2.5\) GeV and \(m=1\) GeV, the maximal imaginary part of the pole in the complex \(s\)-plane is \((M^2-4m^2)/4 = (6.25-4)/4 = 0.5625\) GeV\(^2\), so the state never gets arbitrarily broad.
* For the two-resonance case with \(M_1=1\) GeV, \(M_2=3\) GeV, and \(m=1\) GeV, in the limit \(g\rightarrow\infty\) with \(\Pi=i\) (purely imaginary), one pole goes to \(s_p^{(1)} = \frac12(1+9) + 2ig^2 = 5 + 2ig^2\) while the other approaches \(s_p^{(2)} = 5 - \frac{i}{2g^2}(9-1)^2 = 5 - 32i/g^2\).
* For the multi-resonance model, the string tension is \(\sigma = 0.16\) GeV\(^2\), the heavy-quark mass is \(M_Q = 1.7\) GeV, and the continuum meson mass is \(m = 2\) GeV, giving a threshold at \(2m = 4\) GeV; the bare masses are \(M_1 = 3.4\) GeV and \(M_2 = 4.8\) GeV.
* The coupling \(g\) is varied from 0 to 25 GeV in the multi-resonance calculations.

## Context

This work extends the line of research on "unquenching" quark models by incorporating hadronic loops and unitarization, building on earlier studies of hadronic shifts and loop theorems by Barnes (2007) and on coupled-channel approaches by van Beveren (1979, 2003, 2006, 2008), Tornqvist (1995), Boglione (1997), Rupp (2012, 2015), and Wolkanowski (2015). The authors contrast their findings with the naive expectation that high-lying states acquire large widths due to large phase space, and they connect their study to the large-\(N_c\) limit of QCD as formulated by 't Hooft (1973) and Witten (1980). The results enable future work on more realistic models that include \(t\)-channel exchanges and vertex dressing, which are left for subsequent publications.
