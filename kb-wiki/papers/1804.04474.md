# Is bottomonium suppression in proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions at LHC energies due to the same effects?

> We show that we can reproduce all the features of bottomonium suppression in both proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions at LHC energies in a comover-interaction picture. For each collisions system, we use the measured {\it relative} suppression of the excited $Υ$(2S) and $Υ$(3S) states to $Υ$(1S) by ATLAS and CMS to parametrise the scattering cross sections of all $S$- and $P$-wave bottomonia with the comoving particles created during the collisions. In addition to a single nonpertubative parameter, these cross sections depend on the momentum distribution of these comovers which we found to be the same for proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions as well as for partonic and hadronic comovers. Moreover, we can also reproduce the {\it absolute} suppresion rates measured by ALICE, ATLAS, CMS and LHCb when the nuclear modifications of the parton densities are taken into account.

## Key claim

The authors show that the relative suppression of excited bottomonium states (Υ(2S) and Υ(3S) relative to Υ(1S)) observed in both proton-lead (pPb) and lead-lead (PbPb) collisions at LHC energies can be explained entirely by interactions with comoving particles, using a single set of parameters for both collision systems, provided that nuclear modifications of parton densities are included for absolute suppression rates.

## Method

The authors apply the comover interaction model (CIM), where bottomonia are suppressed by collisions with particles of similar rapidity (comovers). They parametrize the comover-bottomonium cross sections using a generic formula that interpolates from zero at threshold to the geometrical cross section (πr², where r is the quarkonium Bohr radius from a Schrödinger equation) at high relative momentum, with the energy dependence governed by an exponent n. The momentum distribution of comovers in the transverse plane is taken as a Bose-Einstein distribution with an effective temperature T_eff. The two parameters (n and T_eff) are fitted to the relative suppression data from CMS and ATLAS for pPb and PbPb collisions, and the resulting cross sections are then used to compute absolute suppression when combined with nPDF shadowing from nCTEQ15.

## Key numbers

* For n=1 and T_eff = 250 ± 50 MeV, the comover-bottomonium cross sections range from σ^{co-Υ(1S)} = 0.02^{+0.020}_{-0.010} mb for the most tightly bound state to σ^{co-χ_b(3P)} = 12.55^{+1.53}_{-1.88} mb for the loosely bound χ_b(3P) state in the hadronic case.
* The fitted T_eff lies in the range 200 to 300 MeV for n values from 0.5 to 2.
* χ² per degree of freedom ranges from 1.0 to 1.4 for pPb data and from 1.4 to 2.0 for PbPb data.
* For pPb collisions at 5.02 TeV, the CIM gives Υ(2S)/Υ(1S) = 0.91 ± 0.03 and Υ(3S)/Υ(1S) = 0.72 ± 0.02 (CMS rapidity range -1.93 < y < 1.93), compared to CMS data of 0.83 ± 0.05 (stat.) ± 0.05 (syst.) and 0.71 ± 0.08 (stat.) ± 0.09 (syst.) respectively.
* For pPb collisions at 5.02 TeV in the ATLAS rapidity range (-2.0 < y < 1.5), the CIM gives Υ(2S)/Υ(1S) = 0.90 ± 0.03 and Υ(3S)/Υ(1S) = 0.71 ± 0.02, compared to ATLAS data of 0.76 ± 0.07 (stat.) ± 0.05 (syst.) and 0.64 ± 0.14 (stat.) ± 0.06 (syst.) respectively.

## Context

This work extends the comover interaction model (CIM), previously applied successfully to charmonia by Capella et al. (2007) and Ferreiro et al. (2014), to the bottomonium family for the first time. It addresses the unexpected observation by CMS (2013) and ATLAS (2017) of relative suppression of excited Υ states in pPb collisions, which could not be explained by cold nuclear matter effects such as nPDF modifications (Kovarik 2015, Eskola 2009) or coherent energy loss (Arleo 2014). The finding that the same comover momentum distribution describes both pPb and PbPb collisions enables a unified description of bottomonium suppression across collision systems and suggests that QGP effects in PbPb may be half of what was previously attributed to them, as noted by Lansberg (2015).
