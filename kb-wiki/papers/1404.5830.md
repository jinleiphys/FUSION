# Primary Isotope Yields and Characteristic Properties of the Fragmenting Source in Heavy-ion Reactions near the Fermi Energies

> For central collisions of $^{40}$Ca $+ ^{40}$Ca at 35 MeV/nucleon, the density and temperature of a fragmenting source have been evaluated in a self-consistent manner using the ratio of the symmetry energy coefficient relative to the temperature, $a_{sym}/T$, extracted from the yields of primary isotopes produced in antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) simulations. The $a_{sym}/T$ values are extracted from all isotope yields using an improved method based on the Modified Fisher Model (MFM). The values of $a_{sym}/T$ obtained, using different interactions with different density dependencies of the symmetry energy term, are correlated with the values of the symmetry energies at the density of fragment formation. Using this correlation, the fragment formation density is found to be $ρ/ρ_0 = 0.67 \pm 0.02$. Using the input symmetry energy value for each interaction temperature values are extracted as a function of isotope mass $A$. The extracted temperature values are compared with those evaluated from the fluctuation thermometer with a radial flow correction.

## Key claim

For central collisions of ⁴⁰Ca + ⁴⁰Ca at 35 MeV/nucleon, the density and temperature of the fragmenting source are determined self-consistently from the ratio of the symmetry energy coefficient to the temperature, extracted from primary isotope yields in AMD simulations, yielding a fragment formation density of ρ/ρ₀ = 0.67 ± 0.02 and a source temperature of T₀ = 5.5 ± 0.2 MeV, but the extracted temperature shows a mass dependence that is attributed to system size effects rather than impact parameter variations.

## Method

The authors used antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) simulations with three Gogny interactions (g0, g0AS, g0ASS) having different density dependencies of the symmetry energy to generate primary fragment yields for central collisions (b = 0 fm) of ⁴⁰Ca + ⁴⁰Ca at 35 MeV/nucleon. An improved Modified Fisher Model (MFM) was applied to extract a_sym/T₀ values from all available isotope yields, incorporating a mass-dependent apparent temperature T(A) = T₀(1 − kA) to account for system size effects. The density of the fragmenting source was determined by correlating the ratios of a_sym/T₀ between different interactions with the density-dependent symmetry energy curves of those interactions, and the temperature was then calculated as T₀ = a_sym / (a_sym/T₀).

## Key numbers

- Fragment formation density: ρ/ρ₀ = 0.67 ± 0.02, extracted from the overlap of density values derived from ratios of a_sym/T₀ between the g0/g0AS and g0/g0ASS interactions.
- Source temperature: T₀ = 5.5 ± 0.2 MeV, determined after iterative correction for mass dependence with final parameter k = 0.007.
- Symmetry energy coefficient at the extracted density: a_sym = 26.6 ± 0.3 MeV for the g0 interaction, 23.7 ± 1.3 MeV for g0AS, and 18.7 ± 0.7 MeV for g0ASS (for k = 0.0); values for k = 0.007 are 26.8 ± 0.2 MeV (g0), 24.0 ± 1.3 MeV (g0AS), and 18.7 ± 0.7 MeV (g0ASS).
- Ratio of a_sym/T₀ values: g0/g0AS = 1.14 ± 0.02 and g0/g0ASS = 1.41 ± 0.03 (for k = 0.0); similar values for k = 0.007.
- Extracted temperature from the first round (k = 0) as a function of mass: T₀ = 5.5(1 − 0.012A), with temperatures decreasing from approximately 5 MeV at Z = 4 to approximately 3 MeV at Z = 18.
- Fluctuation thermometer temperature from AMD events with g0 interaction shows a broad peak of T_Qxy ∼ 8 MeV around A ∼ 12, then decreases monotonically with increasing A.
- Radial flow parameter from Monte-Carlo simulation: f_r = 0.022 c/fm (with Coulomb) and f_r = 0.016 c/fm (without Coulomb), used to reproduce fluctuation thermometer results.

## Context

This work extends the isotopic yield ratio method previously applied to experimentally reconstructed primary fragment yields by Lin (2014) and Huang (2010), using AMD as an event generator to eliminate secondary decay effects and simplify initial conditions. The self-consistent determination of density, temperature, and symmetry energy from a_sym/T₀ ratios across different interactions builds on the framework of Ono (2003), who showed that differences in extracted a_sym/T₀ reflect differences in the symmetry energy at the density of fragment formation. The extracted temperature values are compared with those from the fluctuation thermometer of Wuenschel (2010), and a Monte-Carlo percolation model with radial flow correction reproduces both the fluctuation thermometer results and the symmetry-energy-derived temperatures, enabling future extraction of these source properties from experimental data.
