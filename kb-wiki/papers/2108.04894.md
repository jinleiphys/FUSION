# Nuclear Dynamics and Reactions in the Ab Initio Symmetry-Adapted Framework

> We review the ab initio symmetry-adapted (SA) framework for determining the structure of stable and unstable nuclei, along with related electroweak, decay and reaction processes. This framework utilizes the dominant symmetry of nuclear dynamics, the shape-related symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry, which has been shown to emerge from first principles and to expose dominant degrees of freedom that are collective in nature, even in the lightest species or seemingly spherical states. This feature is illustrated for a broad scope of nuclei ranging from helium to titanium isotopes, enabled by recent developments of the ab initio symmetry-adapted no-core shell model expanded to the continuum through the use of the SA basis and that of the resonating group method. The review focuses on energies, electromagnetic transitions, quadrupole and magnetic moments, radii, form factors, and response function moments, for ground-state rotational bands and giant resonances. The method also determines the structure of reaction fragments that is used to calculate decay widths and alpha-capture reactions for simulated x-ray burst abundance patterns, as well as nucleon-nucleus interactions for cross sections and other reaction observables.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the ab initio symmetry-adapted (SA) framework and claims that nuclear dynamics is dominated by a few collective shapes described by the symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry, which emerges naturally from first principles; however, this dominance is approximate and holds at the level of 70-80% or more for low-lying states, with additional symplectic irreps contributing at least an order of magnitude less.

## Method

The authors employ the ab initio symmetry-adapted no-core shell model (SA-NCSM), which solves the many-body Schrodinger equation using a basis organized by SU(3) and Sp(3,R) symmetry labels that track nuclear shapes and their vibrations. For reactions, the framework is extended to the continuum via the symmetry-adapted resonating group method (SA-RGM) for low-energy nucleon-nucleus scattering and via the spectator expansion of multiple scattering theory for intermediate-energy proton scattering, using ab initio one-body densities from the SA-NCSM as input.

## Key numbers

- For $^{20}$Ne, the ground state and its rotational band ($0^+$, $2^+$, $4^+$, $6^+$, $8^+$) are dominated by a single symplectic irrep at a level typically in excess of 70-80%
- Excitation energies calculated within a few symplectic irreps converge with typical errors of approximately 100 keV for excitation energies and approximately 4% for $B(E2)$ transition strengths
- The corresponding NCSM dimension for $J^\pi=0^+,2^+,4^+$ in $^{20}$Ne in 11 harmonic oscillator shells is $3.8 \times 10^{10}$, while the SA-NCSM uses significantly reduced selected model spaces
- For $^4$He, ground-state energy and point-proton rms radius are benchmarked against hyperspherical harmonics calculations using JISP16 and N3LO-EM interactions (specific numerical values not given in the truncated text)
- The SA-NCSM calculations for $^{20}$Ne use NNLO$_{\rm opt}$ interaction in a model space of 11 harmonic oscillator shells with $\hbar\Omega=15$ MeV inter-shell distance

## Context

This review extends the decades-long line of work on symplectic symmetry in nuclei initiated by Elliott (1958), Hecht (1969), and developed further by Draayer, Rowe, and Rosensteel through the 1970s-1990s, who used group theory to understand nuclear collectivity. The SA framework enables ab initio calculations for nuclei from helium to titanium isotopes that were previously inaccessible, and it enables reaction calculations for alpha capture on $^{16}$O relevant to x-ray burst nucleosynthesis, as well as nucleon-nucleus scattering via the SA-RGM and multiple scattering theory, providing a path to first-principles optical potentials for unstable nuclei.
