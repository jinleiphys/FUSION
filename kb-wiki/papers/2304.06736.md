# Significance of classically forbidden regions for short baseline neutrino experiments

> Classically forbidden regions ($\mathtt{CFRs}$) are common to both non-relativistic quantum mechanics, and to relativistic quantum field theory. It is known since 2001 that $\mathtt{CFR}$ contributes roughly sixteen percent of energy to the ground state of a simple harmonic oscillator (Adunas G. Z. et al., Gen. Relativ. Gravit., 33 (2001) 183). Similarly, quantum field theoretic arguments yield a non-zero amplitude for a massive particle to cross the light cone (that is, into the $\mathtt{CFR}$). The signs of these amplitudes are opposite for fermions and antifermions. This has given rise to an erroneous conclusion that amplitude to cross the lightcone is identically zero. This is true as long as a measurement does not reveal the considered object to be a particle or antiparticle. However, neutrino oscillation experiments do measure a neutrino $ν$, or an antineutrino $\barν$. Here we show that in the context of neutrino oscillations these observations have the potential to resolve various short baseline anomalies for a sufficiently light lowest mass eigenstate. In addition, we make a concrete prediction for the upcoming results to be announced later this year by JSNS$^2$.

## Key claim

The paper argues that short baseline neutrino anomalies (LSND, KARMEN, reactor, and gallium anomalies) may be resolved by a first-principles quantum field theoretic mechanism where a sufficiently light lowest mass eigenstate (of order neV) has a non-zero amplitude to cross the light cone into classically forbidden regions, rather than by invoking sterile neutrinos within the Pontecorvo framework.

## Method

The author derives the quantum field theoretic amplitude for a massive fermionic particle to propagate across a spacelike separation, using the modified Bessel function of the second kind, and integrates this amplitude over all spacelike separations (the entire classically forbidden region) to obtain the total probability. For accelerator-based experiments, the ratio of expected events between LSND and KARMEN, and between far and near detectors for JSNS^2, is computed as a function of the lowest mass eigenstate mass, with detector/beam parameters and flux reduction factors included.

## Key numbers

- For a proton mass, the scale ℏ/m = 2 × 10⁻¹⁴ cm; for a neV mass, ℏ/m ∼ 100 meters, covering source-detector distances for all mentioned short baseline experiments.
- The classically forbidden region contributes roughly 16% of energy to the ground state of a simple harmonic oscillator, from Adunas et al. (2001).
- For LSND versus KARMEN comparison, the ratio of expected events uses r = 1 (in units of 6.53 neV)⁻¹ for LSND and r = 17.6/30 for KARMEN, with α defined by: (20000 Coulombs / 2897 Coulombs) × (167 tons / 56 tons) × (17.6 meters / 30.0 meters)².
- For JSNS^2, the first phase source-detector distance is 24 meters, and the second phase far detector is at 48 meters; the ratio of events per unit volume of far versus near detector is given by equation (12) in the paper.

## Context

This work extends the observation by Adunas et al. (2001) that classically forbidden regions contribute significantly to quantum systems, and applies it to the long-standing LSND-KARMEN anomaly (LSND collaboration, 1996; Yellin, 1998) and other short baseline anomalies including the gallium anomaly (BEST, 2021). It contradicts the conclusion that the amplitude to cross the light cone is identically zero, noting that this holds only when a measurement does not reveal the object as particle or antiparticle. The framework enables a concrete prediction for the upcoming JSNS^2 experiment and suggests re-interpretation of Neutrino-4 reactor data, potentially providing a mass for the lowest mass eigenstate without invoking sterile neutrinos.
