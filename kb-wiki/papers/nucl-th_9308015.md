# Bose-condensation through resonance decay

> We show that a system described by an equation of state which contains a high number of degrees of freedom (resonances) can create a considerable amount of superfluid (condensed) pions through the decay of short-lived resonances, if baryon number and entropy are large and the dense matter decouples from chemical equilibrium earlier than from thermal equilibrium. The system cools down faster in the presence of a condensate, an effect that may partially compensate the enhancement of the lifetime expected in the case of quark-gluon-plasma formation.

# Bose-condensation through resonance decay

## Key claim

The authors show that a hadronic resonance gas can produce a pionic Bose condensate through the decay of short-lived resonances, provided that the system has high baryon number and entropy density and that chemical freeze-out occurs earlier than thermal freeze-out at temperatures of approximately 180 MeV or higher. The condensate formation is conditional on the pion chemical potential reaching the pion mass, which depends sensitively on the resonance content of the equation of state and on the baryonic density at chemical decoupling.

## Method

The authors model a hadronic equation of state containing all known resonances up to masses of 2 GeV, with baryons treated using Fermi statistics and compression effects included. They solve a coupled system of equations enforcing conservation of energy-momentum, baryon number, and strangeness at the chemical freeze-out hypersurface, determining the chemical potentials (including a pion chemical potential) and the condensate density. The subsequent hydrodynamic expansion between chemical and thermal freeze-out is described using a one-dimensional scaling expansion with conserved entropy, baryon number, strangeness, and pion number.

## Key numbers

- The pion chemical potential reaches the pion mass (approximately 140 MeV) in the temperature region 170–200 MeV, with the exact temperature depending on baryonic density.
- The strange chemical potential induced by finite baryon number never exceeds 150 MeV across the investigated parameter range.
- The decay time of short-lived resonances is on the order of 1 fm/c, and the transition to a non-zero pion chemical potential occurs in less than 1 fm/c for initial conditions of τ₀ = 1 fm/c and T₀ = 200 MeV.
- For a pion gas in the temperature range between 200 MeV and 150 MeV, the fraction of thermal pions varies by less than 10% during the expansion.
- For an equation of state joining lattice QCD data to a Hagedorn-type resonance gas, about 40% of pions would be in the condensed phase at freeze-out temperatures of approximately 140 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the scenario proposed by Leutwyler (1993), who argued that resonances may decouple from chemical equilibrium while remaining in thermal equilibrium, leading to a non-zero pion chemical potential. The authors go beyond that work by explicitly determining whether the pion chemical potential can reach the pion mass and form a condensate, and by studying the condensate's evolution during the subsequent expansion. The results are relevant to the soft-pion puzzle in heavy-ion collisions and to distinguishing conventional hadronic effects from quark-gluon plasma formation; the authors note that a condensate would reduce the fireball lifetime, partially compensating the lifetime enhancement expected from QGP formation as discussed by Bertsch (1984). The implications for pion interferometry measurements are deferred to a separate paper.
