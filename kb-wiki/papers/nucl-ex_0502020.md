# Is There a Significant Difference Between the Results of the Coulomb Dissociation of 8B and the Direct Capture 7Be(p,g)8B Reaction?

> Recent claims of the Seattle group of evidence of "slope difference between CD [Coulomb Dissociation] and direct [capture] results" are based on wrong and selective data. When the RIKEN2 data are included correctly, and previously published Direct Capture (DC) data are also included, we observe only a 1.9 sigma difference in the extracted so called "scale independent slope (b)", considerably smaller than claimed by the Seattle group. The very parameterization used by the Seattle group to extract the so called b-slope parameter has no physical foundation. Considering the physical slope (S' = dS/dE), we observe a 1.0 sigma agreement between slopes (S') measured in CD and DC, refuting the need for new theoretical investigation. The claim that S17(0) values extracted from CD data are approximately 10% lower than DC results, is based on misunderstanding of the CD method. Considering all of the published CD S17(0) results, with adding back an unconfirmed E2 correction of the MSU data, yields very consistent S17(0) results that agree with recent DC measurements of the Seattle and Weizmann groups. The recent correction of the b-slope parameter (0.25 1/MeV) suggested by Esbensen, Bertsch and Snover was applied to the wrong b-slope parameter calculated by the Seattle group. When considering the correct slope of the RIKEN2 data, this correction in fact leads to a very small b-slope parameter (0.14 1/MeV), less than half the central value observed for DC data, refuting the need to correct the RIKEN2 data. In particular it confirms that the E2 contribution in the RIKEN2 data is negligible. The dispersion of measured S17(0) is mostly due to disagreement among individual DC experiments and not due to either experimental or theoretical aspects of CD.

## Key claim

The claimed "slope difference" between Coulomb Dissociation (CD) and Direct Capture (DC) results for the ⁷Be(p,γ)⁸B reaction is not significant; when the RIKEN2 data are plotted correctly and omitted DC data are included, the difference in the extracted b-slope parameter is only 1.9 sigma, and the difference in the physically meaningful slope S' = dS/dE is only 1.0 sigma, refuting the need for new theoretical investigation.

## Method

The author reanalyzed published CD data (RIKEN2, GSI1, GSI2, MSU) and DC data (Seattle, Weizmann, Orsay, Bochum, and older data from Filippone, Vaughn, Parker, and Kavanagh) by extracting two slope parameters: the "scale independent slope (b)" from a linear fit S = a(1+bE) over 300–1400 keV, and the physical slope S' = dS/dE over the same energy range. The RIKEN2 b-slope was corrected from the value plotted by the Seattle group, and three previously omitted DC data sets were added. Weighted averages and chi-squared values were computed for both CD and DC data sets.

## Key numbers

- The corrected b-slope parameter for RIKEN2 data is b = 0.39 ± 0.08 MeV⁻¹, not the value plotted by the Seattle group.
- The 1/σ weighted average b-slope for all published CD data (RIKEN2, GSI1, GSI2, MSU) is b = 0.51 ± 0.06 MeV⁻¹ with χ²/ν = 1.1.
- The 1/σ weighted average b-slope for all published DC data (including older data) is b = 0.38 ± 0.03 MeV⁻¹ with χ²/ν = 1.8, giving a 1.9 sigma difference from the CD average.
- The 1/σ weighted average physical slope S' for CD data is S' = 7.9 ± 1.0 eV-b/MeV with χ²/ν = 0.5.
- The 1/σ weighted average physical slope S' for DC data is S' = 6.7 ± 0.6 eV-b/MeV with χ²/ν = 1.5, giving a 1.0 sigma agreement with the CD average.
- The correction suggested by Esbensen, Bertsch and Snover (0.25 MeV⁻¹) applied to the correct RIKEN2 b-slope yields b = 0.14 ± 0.08 MeV⁻¹, less than half the DC central value of 0.38 MeV⁻¹.
- The 1/σ weighted average S₁₇(0) from CD data (with the unconfirmed E2 correction of the MSU data added back) is S₁₇(0) = 20.0 ± 0.7 eV-b with χ²/ν = 0.5.

## Context

This work directly contradicts the claims of the Seattle group (Jung et al., 2003) and the subsequent Physical Review Letter by Esbensen, Bertsch and Snover (2004) that CD and DC results show a significant slope difference requiring new theoretical investigation. The author argues that the Seattle group misplotted the RIKEN2 b-slope and selectively omitted three older DC data sets (Vaughn, Parker, Kavanagh) while including Filippone (1983). The analysis extends the long-standing debate over the ⁷Be(p,γ)⁸B reaction, which is critical for understanding solar neutrino production, and concludes that the dispersion in measured S₁₇(0) arises primarily from disagreements among individual DC experiments rather than from CD methodology. The paper calls for an accurate measurement of the slope S' and the d-wave contribution to enable reliable extrapolation to zero energy.
