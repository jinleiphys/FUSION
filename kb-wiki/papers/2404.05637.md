# First-order phase transitions in the cores of neutron stars

> I explore various scenarios for the phase transition within neutron-star matter. I do so by generating large model-agnostic ensemble using Gaussian processes, both with and without explicit inclusion of first-order phase transitions (FOPTs). The ensemble is conditioned with state-of-the-art astrophysical and theoretical inputs in a fully Bayesian approach. I study how the current data affect the posterior probability of the location and the strength of FOPT. I find that the previously observed peak structure of the sound speed remains stable against inclusion of FOPTs. While the current data cannot differentiate between a smooth crossover and a first-order phase transition, 91% of the total evidence consists of equations of state with some form of phase changes, such as FOPT occurring within or terminating the stable branch of neutron stars, or an indication of a crossover to quark matter.

# First-order phase transitions in the cores of neutron stars

## Key claim

While the current data cannot differentiate between a smooth crossover and a first-order phase transition, 91% of the total evidence consists of equations of state with some form of phase changes, such as a first-order phase transition occurring within or terminating the stable branch of neutron stars, or an indication of a crossover to quark matter. The previously observed peak structure of the sound speed remains stable against inclusion of first-order phase transitions.

## Method

The author generates a large model-agnostic ensemble of equations of state using Gaussian process regression, both with and without explicit inclusion of first-order phase transitions. The ensemble is conditioned with state-of-the-art astrophysical inputs (tidal deformability from GW170817, radio mass measurements of the heaviest pulsar, 12 X-ray mass-radius measurements) and theoretical inputs (chiral effective field theory up to 2 nuclear saturation density, perturbative QCD calculations) in a fully Bayesian approach. Each equation of state is categorized into mutually exclusive sets: no first-order phase transition, first-order phase transition inside the neutron star, destabilizing first-order phase transition, and twin stars.

## Key numbers

- 91% of the total evidence consists of equations of state with some form of phase changes (first-order phase transition within or terminating the stable branch, or crossover to quark matter), leaving 9% for the absence of any form of phase change
- Bayes factor for twin stars relative to no first-order phase transition is 0.001, indicating twin stars are not supported by current data
- Bayes factor for destabilizing first-order phase transition relative to no first-order phase transition is 1.5 (with marginalized QCD input and X-rays)
- Bayes factor for first-order phase transition inside neutron star relative to no first-order phase transition is 0.7 (with marginalized QCD input and X-rays)
- Posterior probability of crossover to quark matter (defined as d_c < 0.2) in the no first-order phase transition set is 64% for the most massive neutron stars, decreasing to 50% for first-order phase transition inside neutron stars and 30% for destabilizing first-order phase transition
- Any first-order phase transition with strength Delta n greater than approximately 1.2 nuclear saturation density results in destabilization of the neutron star
- The prior for first-order phase transition location is uniform between 1.1 and 10 nuclear saturation density, and strength between 0 and 8 nuclear saturation density

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian inference approach of Gorda (2022) and Annala (2023) by explicitly including first-order phase transitions in the prior, which previous studies either did not model explicitly (Brandes 2023, Essick 2023) or lacked Bayesian interpretation (Gorda 2022). The study uses the marginalized QCD likelihood function introduced by Komoltsev (2023) and the chiral effective field theory likelihood function from Koehn (2024). The results enable future work to determine which mass-radius measurements could help distinguish between phase transition scenarios, and suggest that precise mapping of the equation of state near the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit will be needed to resolve whether the stable branch terminates with a crossover or first-order phase transition.
