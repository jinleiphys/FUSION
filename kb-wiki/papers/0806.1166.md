# Cooling of the crust in the neutron star low-mass X-ray binary MXB 1659-29

> In quasi-persistent neutron star transients, long outbursts cause the neutron star crust to be heated out of thermal equilibrium with the rest of the star. During quiescence, the crust then cools back down. Such crustal cooling has been observed in two quasi-persistent sources: KS 1731-260 and MXB 1659-29. Here we present an additional Chandra observation of MXB 1659-29 in quiescence, which extends the baseline of monitoring to 6.6 yr after the end of the outburst. This new observation strongly suggests that the crust has thermally relaxed, with the temperature remaining consistent over 1000 days. Fitting the temperature cooling curve with an exponential plus constant model we determine an e-folding timescale of 465 +/- 25 days, with the crust cooling to a constant surface temperature of kT = 54 +/- 2 eV (assuming D=10 kpc). From this, we infer a core temperature in the range 3.5E7-8.3E7 K (assuming D=10 kpc), with the uncertainty due to the surface composition. Importantly, we tested two neutron star atmosphere models as well as a blackbody model, and found that the thermal relaxation time of the crust is independent of the chosen model and the assumed distance.

# Cooling of the crust in the neutron star low-mass X-ray binary MXB 1659-29

## Key claim

The neutron star crust in MXB 1659-29 has thermally relaxed to equilibrium with the core, with the temperature remaining consistent over 1000 days; however, this conclusion depends on the exponential-plus-constant cooling model fitting the data well, and the inferred core temperature range (3.5E7-8.3E7 K) carries significant uncertainty due to the unknown surface composition and depth of the light-element layer.

## Method

The authors analyzed a new 28 ksec Chandra ACIS-S observation of MXB 1659-29 taken on 2008 April 27, combined with five previous Chandra and one XMM-Newton observation spanning 6.6 years after the end of the 2.5-year outburst. They fitted the spectra simultaneously using absorbed neutron star atmosphere models (nsa and nsatmos) and a blackbody model (bbodyrad), fixing mass at 1.4 M_sun and radius at 10 km, and tested distances of 5, 10, and 13 kpc. The temperature cooling curve was fitted with an exponential decay plus constant model of the form y(t) = a exp[-(t-t0)/b] + c, with t0 set to the last day the source was observed active (MJD 52159.5).

## Key numbers

- e-folding timescale: 465 +/- 25 days (from nsa model, D=10 kpc)
- Constant surface temperature (asymptotic): kT_eff^infinity = 54 +/- 2 eV (nsa model, D=10 kpc)
- Core temperature range: (3.5-8.3) x 10^7 K (assuming D=10 kpc), depending on light-element layer depth
- Net count rate in 0.5-10 keV band: (1.0 +/- 0.2) x 10^-3 counts/s (corrected exposure 26.7 ks)
- e-folding timescale for KS 1731-260: 305 +/- 47 days (updated from Cackett 2006)
- Power-law index for middle section of MXB 1659-29 cooling curve: -0.33 +/- 0.02 (nsa, D=10 kpc)
- Time-averaged mass accretion rate estimate: 7 x 10^-11 M_sun/yr
- Time-averaged crust nuclear heating estimate: ~6 x 10^33 erg/s (D=10 kpc)

## Context

This work extends the quiescent monitoring of MXB 1659-29 from approximately 4 years (Cackett 2006) to 6.6 years, confirming that the crust has thermally relaxed. The results are consistent with the cooling observed in the other quasi-persistent transient KS 1731-260 (Wijnands 2001, 2003, 2004; Cackett 2006), and the e-folding timescale is robust across different spectral models and distance assumptions. The inferred high thermal conductivity supports the picture from molecular dynamics simulations (Horowitz 2007, 2008) that the crust freezes into an ordered, low-impurity lattice, as Shternin (2007) found for KS 1731-260. The core temperature range allows testing of neutrino cooling mechanisms: standard modified Urca cooling (Yakovlev 2004) is insufficient by a factor of ~30 to balance time-averaged heating, but the "minimal cooling model" (Page 2004) with pair breaking and formation neutrino emissivity from neutrons in the 3P2 state can balance heating if the core temperature is in the upper half of the inferred range. Future observations of KS 1731-260 are needed to determine whether its crust has also fully relaxed.
