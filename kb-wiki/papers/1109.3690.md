# Exciting Baryons: now and in the future

> This is the final talk of NSTAR2011 conference. It is not a summary talk, but rather a looking forward to what still needs to be done in excited baryon physics. In particular, we need to hone our tools connecting experimental inputs with QCD. At present we rely on models that often have doubtful connections with the underlying theory, and this needs to be dramatically improved, if we are to reach definitive conclusions about the relevant degrees of freedom of excited baryons. Conclusions that we want to have by NSTAR2021.

## Key claim

The field of excited baryon physics must dramatically improve its tools for connecting experimental inputs with QCD, moving beyond models with doubtful connections to the underlying theory, in order to reach definitive conclusions about the relevant degrees of freedom of excited baryons by the NSTAR2021 conference.

## Method

The paper reviews the current state of baryon spectroscopy, comparing the quark model predictions of Capstick and Roberts with the experimentally observed spectrum, and discusses the Excited Baryon Analysis Center (EBAC) program at Jefferson Lab, which uses an effective Lagrangian approach to fit data on pi N and gamma N production channels across multiple final states to determine resonance masses, widths, and couplings. The author also examines Schwinger-Dyson/Bethe-Salpeter equation approaches to QCD and compares lattice QCD results with quark model and diquark-quark model predictions.

## Key numbers

- The EBAC team is analyzing approximately 10^5 data points to determine N* and Delta resonances below 1.6 GeV
- The P11 sector shows poles in the complex energy plane around 1400 and 1800 MeV, corresponding to the N*(1440) and N*(1710)
- When explicit pi Delta and eta N channels are switched off in the EBAC model, the two P11 poles merge into a single entity on the real axis around 1800 MeV
- Many baryon states above 1.6 GeV are described as "missing" from experimental observation
- The lattice QCD pion mass is still 3 or 4 times too heavy for physical decay patterns

## Context

This conference talk extends the long-standing effort to understand the baryon spectrum that has been pursued for the past 50 years, directly comparing the quark model predictions of Capstick and Roberts with the PDG judgement of identified states. The paper contrasts the three-quark model with the diquark-quark model proposed by Lichtenberg and Tassie over 40 years ago, and notes that lattice QCD calculations by Robert Edwards reveal a pattern like SU(6) x O(3) of the quark model rather than a pointlike diquark-quark system. The work enables future calculations that may test whether the evolution from bare to dressed states in Schwinger-Dyson/Bethe-Salpeter equation systems accords with experimental results, and highlights the need for coupled-channel treatments analogous to those that explain the X(3872) in charmonium and the light scalar mesons like the f0(980) and sigma.
