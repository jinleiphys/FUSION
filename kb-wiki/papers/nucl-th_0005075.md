# Soft Modes, Resonances and Quantum Transport

> Effects of the propagation of particles, which have a finite life-time and an according width in their mass spectrum, are discussed in the context of transport description. First, the importance of coherence effects (Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal effect) on production and absorption of field quanta in non-equilibrium dense matter is considered. It is shown that classical diffusion and Langevin results correspond to re-summation of certain field-theory diagrams formulated in terms of full non-equilibrium Green's functions. Then the general properties of broad resonances in dense and hot systems are discussed in the framework of a self-consistent and conserving Phi-derivable method of Baym at the examples of the rho-meson in hadronic matter and the pion in dilute nuclear matter. Further we address the problem of a transport description that properly accounts for the damping width of the particles. The Phi-derivable method generalized to the real-time contour provides a self-consistent and conserving kinetic scheme. We derive a generalized expression for the non-equilibrium kinetic entropy flow, which includes corrections from fluctuations and mass-width effects. In special cases an H-theorem is proved. Memory effects in collision terms give contributions to the kinetic entropy flow that in the Fermi-liquid case recover the famous bosonic type T^3 ln T correction to the specific heat of liquid Helium-3. At the example of the pion-condensate phase transition in dense nuclear matter we demonstrate important part played by the width effects within the quantum transport.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a consistent transport description of particles with broad damping widths (such as resonances in hadronic matter) requires going beyond the quasiparticle approximation, and that the Phi-derivable method of Baym generalized to the real-time contour provides a self-consistent and conserving kinetic scheme that properly accounts for finite-width effects, memory effects, and entropy production.

## Method

The authors first analyze the classical diffusion and Langevin description of bremsstrahlung from a stochastic source, showing that the resulting current-current correlation function corresponds to a re-summation of certain field-theory diagrams in the real-time non-equilibrium Green's function formalism. They then apply the Phi-derivable method of Baym to equilibrium systems, studying the rho-meson in dense hadronic matter and the pion in dilute nuclear matter as examples of broad resonances. Finally, they generalize the Phi-derivable approach to the real-time contour to derive a kinetic scheme with a generalized entropy flow, including memory effects in collision terms, and demonstrate the role of width effects in the pion-condensate phase transition in dense nuclear matter.

## Key numbers

* The damping widths of delta and rho-meson resonances in vacuum are above 100 MeV, with lifetimes less than 2 fm/c.
* Typical mean temperatures in heavy-ion collisions range between 50 to 150 MeV depending on beam energy.
* For the classical bremsstrahlung problem, the soft part of the photon spectrum behaves as omega/Gamma_x, showing a suppression factor C_0(omega) = omega^2/(omega^2 + Gamma_x^2) by which incoherent quasi-free results must be corrected.
* The classical current-current correlation function decays exponentially as ~ exp(-Gamma_x tau) at photon momentum q=0, with the width decreasing for increasing momentum q.
* The mass-dependent electromagnetic decay rate of the rho-meson into di-leptons is proportional to 1/m^3.

## Context

This work extends the quasiparticle concepts of Landau, Migdal, and Galitsky for Fermi liquids to systems with broad resonances and finite damping widths, addressing the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal effect in dense matter. It builds on the non-equilibrium field theory methods of Schwinger, Kadanoff and Baym, and Keldysh from the early 1960s, and on Baym's Phi-derivable method for conserving approximations. The paper enables a consistent transport description of unstable particles in heavy-ion collisions, going beyond ad-hoc recipes that violate detailed balance and conservation laws, and provides a framework for treating soft-mode problems and phase transitions such as pion condensation in dense nuclear matter.
