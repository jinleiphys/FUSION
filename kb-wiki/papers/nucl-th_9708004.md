# The Spectral Function of the Rho Meson in Nuclear Matter

> We calculate the modification of a rho meson in nuclear matter through its coupling to resonance-hole states. Starting from a recently proposed model, we include all four star resonances up to 1.9 GeV. In contrast to previous works, we include not only resonances that couple to the rho in a relative p-wave, but also those that couple to an s-wave state. In addition, we solve the equation for the rho spectral function self-consistently. We find that s-wave resonances affect the in medium spectral function of the rho strongly. In the transverse channel the rho meson is, especially at non zero momentum, completely washed out and can in the presence of nuclear matter no longer be viewed as a resonant excitation of the vacuum. Instead, our model shows a continuum of possible excitations with the quantum numbers of a transversely polarized rho. In the longitudinal channel, however, the rho retains its resonant character in our calculation. As a consequence of the self-consistent treatment we also find a strong enhancement of the widths of the included nucleon resonances in medium.

# The Spectral Function of the Rho Meson in Nuclear Matter

## Key claim
The resonant nature of a transversely polarized rho meson vanishes completely in nuclear matter at normal nuclear density, becoming a continuum of excitations, while a longitudinally polarized rho retains its resonant character for momenta above 0.5 GeV; this distinction between polarizations means that a simple scaling law for the rho mass and width cannot account for hadronic in-medium effects.

## Method
The authors calculate the rho meson self-energy in nuclear matter from resonance-hole loops, including all four-star nucleon resonances up to 1.9 GeV (excluding the N(1440) due to poorly known rho decay width). They extend prior work by including both p-wave and s-wave couplings of resonances to the rho, and solve the equation for the rho spectral function self-consistently by iterating the spectral function through the resonance decay widths. The coupling constants are fixed by reproducing partial decay widths from the Particle Data Group, and the calculation is performed at normal nuclear density with integration over the Fermi sphere of nucleons.

## Key numbers
- All four-star nucleon resonances up to 1.905 GeV are included; the N(1440) is excluded because its partial decay width into rho + nucleon is compatible with zero.
- The monopole formfactor cutoff is Lambda = 1.5 GeV.
- For the Delta(1232), a Landau-Migdal parameter g' = 0.5 is used for short-range correlations.
- The N(1520) has an observed partial decay width of about 25 MeV into a rho and a nucleon.
- At normal nuclear density, the rho decay widths of resonances (except the Delta(1232)) increase dramatically; the Delta(1232) rho-width stays below 1 MeV.
- For the longitudinal spectral function at momenta above 0.5 GeV, the rho peak is reduced by a factor of two relative to the vacuum.
- At twice normal nuclear density (2 rho_0), the rho branch is still visible but much weaker than at normal density.

## Context
This work extends the resonance-hole model of Friman and Pirner (1997), who included only two p-wave resonances and found the vacuum propagator recovered at zero three-momentum. By including s-wave resonances (especially the N(1520)), the authors find strong modifications even at zero momentum, contradicting the earlier result. The calculation also addresses the CERES dilepton data discussion, where models using vacuum rho properties (Cassing 1997, Ko 1997) underestimated the dilepton yield for invariant masses 300-600 MeV. The finding that transverse and longitudinal spectral functions differ qualitatively agrees with QCD sum rules at finite momentum (Lee 1997) and with the analysis of Eletsky (1997). The significant broadening of nucleon resonances with large rho-decay widths contributes to explaining the observed disappearance of higher-lying nucleon resonances beyond the Delta in total photoabsorption cross sections on nuclei (Bianchi 1996).
