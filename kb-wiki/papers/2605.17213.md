# Identifying observable MeV lines from the decays of weak and main $r$-process isotopes in mergers

> We consider predictions for the MeV gamma-ray spectrum emitted by the $β$ decays of freshly synthesized isotopes from a neutron star merger at timescales of relevance for post-merger (days) and remnant (years) emission. We develop a search algorithm to identify observable spectral peaks and then determine if a specific isotope has a dominant emission line producing the spectral feature. We predict emission spectra using nucleosynthesis calculations which consider nuclear models with distinct masses, $β$-decays, and fission properties as well as variations on main ($A>130$) and weak ($A<130$) $r$-process astrophysical conditions. We tabulate all lines from decaying isotopes that our procedure identifies and provide the predicted range in time over which each line could be visible. We find that Rh-106 presents a unique opportunity to distinguish between main and weak $r$-process emission, as our calculated spectrum above $\sim 1$ MeV for an event dominated by the weak $r$ process is identical to the Rh-106 emission spectrum from $\sim$ 0.2 to $\sim$17 years. We further find emission from species such as Hf-181, Ta-182, Ta-184, and Re-188 offers the potential to be able to distinguish between nuclear models. We investigate whether the 2.6 MeV strong gamma-ray line from Tl-208 is predicted to be robustly observable across calculation variations on both timescale of days and years. We find Tl-208 to consistently shine through on the order of years, though it can face competition from Ga-72 and La-140 at early times ($\sim$ days). We additionally highlight numerous isotopes of interest for observation and nuclear experiment.

## Key claim

The paper identifies specific MeV gamma-ray lines from beta-decaying isotopes produced in neutron star mergers that could be observed by near-future telescopes, but the observability of these lines depends strongly on the assumed nuclear mass model (FRDM2012, HFB27, or Thomas-Fermi), beta-decay treatment (Moller et al. or Marketin et al.), and astrophysical conditions (main versus weak r-process). The abstract's claim that Rh-106 can distinguish main from weak r-process emission is qualified by the finding that this holds only for the Thomas-Fermi nuclear model and only for the weak r-process case.

## Method

The authors used the PRISM reaction network to calculate nucleosynthesis for mass-weighted total ejecta from a 1.2-1.4 solar mass neutron star merger simulation (Radice 2018), considering three nuclear mass models (FRDM2012, HFB27, Thomas-Fermi) and two beta-decay treatments (Moller et al., Marketin et al.). They extracted gamma-ray emission spectra from the ENDF/B-VIII.1 database between 0.1 and 20 MeV, applied a peak-finding algorithm based on scipy.signal.find_peaks to identify spectral features across timescales from 1 hour to 1 Gyr, and then determined which isotope contributed at least 50% of the flux in each identified peak bin. Radiation transfer calculations using a Monte Carlo code with homologous expansion (assuming 0.1c or 0.3c ejecta velocities) were performed to test which spectral lines survive photon propagation through the ejecta.

## Key numbers

- Tl-208 2.615 MeV line: predicted to be observable from 0.384 days to 18 days post-merger (early window) and from 139 years to 19,000 years (remnant window) across all nuclear model variations, with flux above 1e-7 counts s^-1 cm^-2 for post-merger and 1e-10 counts s^-1 cm^-2 for remnant searches
- Rh-106 2.366 MeV line: for the weak r-process case with Thomas-Fermi model, the calculated spectrum above approximately 1 MeV is identical to the Rh-106 emission spectrum from approximately 0.2 to 17 years
- Co-60 lines at 1173 keV and 1332 keV: predicted to be visible from 444,000 years to 4.82 million years across all nuclear model variations
- Sb-126 lines at 666, 695, 697 keV: predicted to be visible from 54.2 years to 2.26 million years across all nuclear model variations
- Ga-72 line at 2202 keV: visible from 0.853 days to 12.3 days, found across all nuclear model variations
- La-140 line at 1596 keV: visible from 2.3 days to 142 days across all nuclear model variations
- Eu-156 line at 2098 keV: visible from 10.2 days to 135 days across all nuclear model variations
- Tl-209 line at 1567 keV: visible from 101 years to 64,700 years across all nuclear model variations
- Detectability threshold for post-merger signals: 1e-7 counts s^-1 cm^-2 (assuming 1 day observation); for remnant signals: 1e-10 counts s^-1 cm^-2

## Context

This work extends previous studies of gamma-ray emission from r-process nucleosynthesis in mergers (Hotokezaka 2016, Li 2019, Korobkin 2020, Wang 2020, Chen 2021, Terada 2022, Vassh 2023, Liu 2025, Gross 2025) by systematically searching for dominant emitters across time from days to thousands of years while explicitly considering nuclear physics uncertainties from three mass models and two beta-decay treatments. The paper builds on the identification of the Tl-208 2.6 MeV line (Vassh 2023) and prompt fission gamma dominance above 3.5 MeV (Wang 2020), and provides a comprehensive tabulation of which isotopes produce observable lines under which nuclear model assumptions. The results enable future MeV telescopes such as COSI, AMEGO/AMEGO-X, e-ASTROGAM, MeVGRO, and GRAMS to target specific isotopes for observation, and identify which nuclear physics uncertainties could be constrained by detecting particular lines.
