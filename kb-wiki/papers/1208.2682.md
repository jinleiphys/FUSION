# Systematics of quadrupole moments and energies

> We define the "quadrupole ratio" r_{Q}=\dfrac{Q_{0}(S)}{Q_{0}(B)} where Q_{0}(S) is the intrinsic quadrupole moment obtained from the static quadrupole moment of the 2_{1}^{+} state of an even-even nucleus and Q_{0}(B) the intrinsic quadrupole moment obtained from B(E2)_{0\rightarrow2} . In both cases we assume a simple rotational formula connecting the rotating frame to the laboratory frame. The quantity r_{Q} would be one if the rotational model were perfect and the energy ratio E(4)/E(2) would be 10/3. In the simple vibrational model, r_{Q} would be zero and E(4)/E(2) would be two. There are some regions where the rotational limit is almost met and fewer where the vibrational limit is also almost met. For most cases, however, it is between these two limits, i.e. 0<|r_{Q}|<1 . There are a few cases where r_{Q} is bigger than one, especially for light nuclei. In most cases the quadrupole ratio is positive but there are two regions with negative ratios. The first case is that of light nuclei and the second has certain nuclei close to ^{208} Pb.

# Systematics of quadrupole moments and energies

## Key claim

The paper defines a "quadrupole ratio" r_Q = Q_0(S)/Q_0(B) to quantify how closely even-even nuclei follow the rotational model (r_Q = 1) versus the vibrational model (r_Q = 0), finding that most nuclei fall between these limits but that a few light nuclei exceed r_Q = 1 and two regions (light nuclei and nuclei near 208Pb) show negative ratios.

## Method

The authors extracted experimental static quadrupole moments Q(2+) from Stone's compilation and B(E2) values from Raman's tables, selecting the latest measurement for each nucleus when multiple values existed. They then computed the intrinsic quadrupole moments Q_0(S) and Q_0(B) using the rotational-model formulas connecting laboratory-frame and intrinsic-frame quantities, and plotted the resulting ratio r_Q alongside the energy ratio E(4)/E(2) for all even-even nuclei with available data up to 206Pb.

## Key numbers

- For a perfect rotor, r_Q = 1 and E(4)/E(2) = 10/3 ≈ 3.33; for a simple vibrator, r_Q = 0 and E(4)/E(2) = 2.
- In the midrange region (mass numbers roughly 70 to 110), r_Q is around 0.5 and E(4)/E(2) lies between 2 and 3.
- Nuclei near the vibrational limit include 112Sn, 118Sn, 120Sn, 122Sn, 124Sn, and 128Te, where Q(2+) is almost zero and E(4)/E(2) is close to 2.
- The rotational limit is realized in a band from roughly mass 152 to 186, including 146Nd, 148Nd, 150Nd, 152Sm, 154Sm, Gd isotopes, 160Dy, 164Dy, Er isotopes, Yb isotopes, 182W, 184W, 186W, most Os isotopes, and 202Hg.
- For 42Ca, a single-particle f7/2 configuration yields r_Q = 0.78, compared to the experimental value of 1.023.
- Nuclei with negative quadrupole ratios include 12C, 28Si, 34S, 36Ar, 40Ar, 50Ti, 60Ni, 62Ni, 64Ni, and 70Ge.
- Nuclei with positive Q(2+) near 208Pb include 192Pt, 194Pt, 196Pt, 198Pt, 198Hg, 200Hg, 202Hg, 204Hg, 204Pb, and 206Pb.

## Context

This work expands the 2006 study by Robinson et al. that considered only light nuclei, now surveying all even-even nuclei with measured Q(2+) values. The paper connects to theoretical calculations using the Gogny interaction by Bertsch et al. (2007), Skyrme Hartree-Fock by Jaqaman et al. (1984), and phase-transition studies in platinum isotopes by Morales et al. (2008). The results provide a systematic benchmark for nuclear structure models and highlight regions where the simple rotational or vibrational pictures break down, particularly for light nuclei and those near doubly magic 208Pb.
