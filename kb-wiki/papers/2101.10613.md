# Determining the temperature in heavy-ion collisions with multiplicity distribution

> By relating the charge multiplicity distribution and the temperature of a de-exciting nucleus through a deep neural network, we propose that the charge multiplicity distribution can be used as a thermometer of heavy-ion collisions. Based on an isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics model, we study the caloric curve of reaction $^{103}$Pd + $^9$Be with the apparent temperature determined through the charge multiplicity distribution. The caloric curve shows a characteristic signature of nuclear liquid-gas phase transition around the apparent temperature $T_{\rm ap}$ $=$ $6.4~\rm MeV$, which is consistent with that through a traditional heavy-ion collision thermometer, and indicates the viability of determining the temperature in heavy-ion collisions with multiplicity distribution.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the final-state charge multiplicity distribution, when processed through a deep neural network, can serve as a thermometer for heavy-ion collisions at intermediate-to-low energies, but the method's viability is demonstrated only in simulations using the IQMD model, not with experimental data.

## Method

The authors used the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model to simulate the de-excitation of a finite-temperature nuclear source (¹¹²Sn) at temperatures from 0 to 20 MeV, generating charge multiplicity distributions for each temperature. They trained a feed-forward deep neural network with four hidden layers (32 neurons each) to map the charge-weighted charge multiplicity distribution to the source temperature. They then applied the trained network to determine the apparent temperature of simulated ¹⁰³Pd + ⁹Be fragmentation reactions at incident energies from 20A MeV to 400A MeV, and compared the results with a momentum fluctuation thermometer.

## Key numbers

- The standard error of the DNN prediction on the testing set is about 0.62 MeV.
- The apparent temperature at which the specific heat capacity reaches its maximum (limiting temperature T_lim) is 6.4 MeV for the ¹⁰³Pd + ⁹Be reaction.
- The nuclear source ¹¹²Sn was simulated at temperatures ranging from 0 to 20 MeV with 1 MeV intervals, using 50 runs of 2,000 events per temperature.
- The incident energies for the ¹⁰³Pd + ⁹Be reaction ranged from 20A MeV to 400A MeV.
- The Gaussian wave packet width L in the IQMD model was set to 2.18 fm².
- The initial central density of the nuclear source was 0.156 fm⁻³.

## Context

This work extends the application of machine-learning techniques to nuclear thermometry, following earlier work by Wang et al. (2020) that used machine learning to study nuclear liquid-gas phase transitions from charge multiplicity distributions. The method provides an alternative to traditional thermometers such as the momentum fluctuation thermometer (Wuester et al., 2009) and isotope ratio thermometers (Tsang et al., 1998; Serfling et al., 2000), and the resulting limiting temperature of 6.4 MeV follows the general trend of the system-size dependence established by Natowitz et al. (2002). The approach enables future application to experimental data once accurate theoretical descriptions of fragment multiplicities are achieved, for instance by combining transport models with statistical multifragmentation models.
