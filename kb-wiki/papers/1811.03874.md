# A first sketch: Construction of model defect priors inspired by dynamic time warping

> Model defects are known to cause biased nuclear data evaluations if they are not taken into account in the evaluation procedure. We suggest a method to construct prior distributions for model defects for reaction models using neighboring isotopes of $^{56}$Fe as an example. A model defect is usually a function of energy and describes the difference between the model prediction and the truth. Of course, neither the truth nor the model defect are accessible. A Gaussian process (GP) enables to define a probability distribution on possible shapes of a model defect by referring to intuitively understandable concepts such as smoothness and the expected magnitude of the defect. Standard specifications of GPs impose a typical length-scale and amplitude valid for the whole energy range, which is often not justified, e.g., when the model covers both the resonance and statistical range. In this contribution, we show how a GP with energy-dependent length-scales and amplitudes can be constructed from available experimental data. The proposed construction is inspired by a technique called dynamic time warping used, e.g., for speech recognition. We demonstrate the feasibility of the data-driven determination of model defects by inferring a model defect of the nuclear models code TALYS for (n,p) reactions of isotopes with charge number between 20 and 30. The newly introduced GP parametrization besides its potential to improve evaluations for reactor relevant isotopes, such as $^{56}$Fe, may also help to better understand the performance of nuclear models in the future.

## Key claim

The authors propose a method to construct Gaussian process (GP) priors for model defects with energy-dependent amplitude and length-scale, inferred from experimental data of neighboring isotopes, rather than using standard GPs with constant hyperparameters that are often unjustified when the model covers both resonance and statistical energy ranges.

## Method

The authors replace the standard squared exponential covariance function with one having an energy-dependent amplitude function δ(E) and a metric function m(E), both parametrized as continuous piecewise linear functions. They determine these functions by maximizing the marginal likelihood (evidence) using experimental (n,p) cross section data from the EXFOR database for isotopes with charge numbers between 20 and 30, combined with the nuclear model code TALYS and a first-order Taylor approximation of the model. The optimization employs the L-BFGS-B algorithm with box constraints to regularize the shape of the amplitude and metric functions, using analytic gradients of the marginal likelihood.

## Key numbers

- The energy grid for constructing δ(E) and m(E) uses 100 points covering 0.1 to 30 MeV, corresponding to roughly 0.3 MeV energy resolution.
- The optimization involves 200 hyperparameters (the u_i and v_i values) that must be optimized.
- The lower limit for change in the metric function between consecutive grid points is 0.03, and the upper limit is 0.15, translating to effective length-scales between roughly 1 MeV and 10 MeV.
- The amplitude function may not change more than about three percent between consecutive grid points, corresponding to roughly 10% per MeV.
- The optimization took about 630 iterations to converge, requiring tens of minutes on a personal computer with eight cores.
- Experimental data were retrieved from 207 entries in the EXFOR database, with an assigned uncorrelated uncertainty of 10% to all data points.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Pigni and Leeb (2003) for introducing model defect information as prior knowledge in nuclear data evaluation, and builds on earlier work by Schnabel (2015) that recognized these developments as essentially designing covariance functions for Gaussian processes. The method is inspired by dynamic time warping from speech recognition (Myers, 1980) and differs from prior approaches by Leeb (2008) in that the model defect is determined by maximizing a score function (the marginal likelihood) rather than by other means. The resulting energy-dependent amplitude and metric functions can be used to improve evaluations for reactor-relevant isotopes such as ⁵⁶Fe, and may enable quantitative insight into nuclear model performance on a per-energy basis.
