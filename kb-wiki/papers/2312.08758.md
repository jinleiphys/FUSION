# Effects of phase transition in hybrid stars from quark-meson coupling hadronic matter to deconfined quark matter

> Different types of phase transition from hadron to quark at high density near zero temperature may occur in the inner core of hybrid stars. We investigate the impacts of phase transition types and quark models on properties of hybrid star and quark cores. The quark-meson coupling (QMC) model is used to describe hadronic matter, and the MIT bag model as well as the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model are employed to describe quark matter for comparison. From the mass-radius curves obtained by using equations of state (EOS), we find that EOSs of hadron matter have a decisive influence on the maximum mass of a hybrid star in the first-order phase transition case, while quark matter EOSs have more impacts on results in the crossover transition case. It is also found in the present work that the thermodynamic correction arising from an interpolation scheme considerably stiffens the EOSs. Therefore the crossover type phase transition generally leads to hybrid stars with higher masses. In particular, by using the QMC model and the NJL model to construct crossover EOSs with thermodynamic correction, we discover that the maximum masses of hybrid stars can meet the recent observational constraint on mass from PSR J0952-0607, i.e., $2.35\pm0.17M_\odot$.

## Key claim

The paper shows that crossover-type phase transitions from hadronic to quark matter, constructed using an interpolation scheme with a thermodynamic correction term, generally produce hybrid stars with higher maximum masses than first-order transitions. Specifically, using the QMC model for hadronic matter and the NJL model for quark matter with a crossover EOS and thermodynamic correction, the maximum masses can satisfy the observational constraint from PSR J0952-0607 (2.35 ± 0.17 solar masses), but only for certain choices of vector coupling strength and interpolation parameters.

## Method

The authors used the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model to describe hadronic matter including hyperons, and both the MIT bag model and the SU(3) Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model to describe deconfined quark matter. For first-order phase transitions, they employed the Gibbs construction with global charge neutrality and baryon number conservation. For crossover transitions, they used a phenomenological interpolation scheme with a hyperbolic tangent function between the hadronic and quark equations of state, including a thermodynamic correction term derived from the interpolation to maintain consistency.

## Key numbers

- The QMC model parameters were fitted to nuclear saturation density rho_0 = 0.15 fm^{-3} and binding energy per baryon E(rho = rho_0) = -15.7 MeV at saturation.
- The asymmetry energy coefficient was fixed to a_sym = 32.5 MeV at saturation density.
- The bag constant for the MIT bag model was varied with values B^{1/4} = 210.854 MeV, 200 MeV, and 190 MeV.
- The NJL model used the RKH parameter set: m_u^0 = m_d^0 = 5.5 MeV, m_s^0 = 140.7 MeV, Lambda = 602.3 MeV, G_S Lambda^2 = 1.835, and K Lambda^5 = 12.36.
- Vector coupling constants in the NJL model were varied from 0 G_S to 1 G_S.
- The crossover interpolation parameters were taken as (bar_rho, Gamma) = (3 rho_0, rho_0) for the QMC-NJL case.
- The maximum masses of hybrid stars from crossover EOSs with thermodynamic correction can meet the observational constraint from PSR J0952-0607: 2.35 +/- 0.17 solar masses.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of hybrid stars using the QMC model (Guichon 1988, Panda 2004) and the NJL model (Buballa 2005) by systematically comparing first-order (Gibbs) and crossover phase transitions. It builds on the interpolation scheme for crossover transitions developed by Masuda (2013) and addresses the role of the thermodynamic correction term. The results are relevant to interpreting observations of massive pulsars such as PSR J0952-0607 (Romani 2022) and PSR J0348+0432 (Antoniadis 2013), and suggest that the type of phase transition and the choice of quark model significantly affect whether hybrid star models can satisfy current mass constraints.
