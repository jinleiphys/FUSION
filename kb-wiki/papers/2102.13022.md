# Transverse charge density and the radius of the proton

> A puzzling discrepancy exists between the values of the proton charge radius obtained using different experimental techniques: elastic electron-proton scattering and spectroscopy of electronic and muonic hydrogen. The proton radius is defined through the slope of the electric form factor, $G_E(Q^2)$, at zero four-momentum transfer, which is inaccessible in scattering experiments. We propose a novel method for extracting the proton radius from scattering data over a broad $Q^2$ range rather than attempting to directly determine the slope of $G_E$ at $Q^2 = 0$. This method relates the radius of the proton to its transverse charge density, which is the two-dimensional Fourier transform of the Dirac form factor, $F_1(Q^2)$. We apply our method to reanalyze the extensive data obtained by the A1 Collaboration [J. C. Bernauer et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 105, 242001 (2010)] and extract a radius value, $r_E = 0.889(5)_{\text{stat}}(5)_{\text{syst}}(4)_{\text{model}}~\text{fm}$, that is consistent with the original result. We also provide new parametrizations for the Dirac and Pauli form factors and the transverse charge and magnetization densities of the proton. Our reanalysis shows that the proton radius discrepancy cannot be explained by issues with fitting and extrapolating the A1 data to $Q^2 = 0$.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a novel method for extracting the proton charge radius from elastic electron-proton scattering data that avoids directly determining the slope of the electric form factor at zero four-momentum transfer, which is experimentally inaccessible. The method instead relates the radius to the second moment of the transverse charge density, and the authors' reanalysis of the A1 Collaboration data yields a radius consistent with the original result, showing that the proton radius discrepancy cannot be explained by issues with fitting and extrapolating scattering data to \(Q^2 = 0\).

## Method

The authors relate the proton charge radius \(r_E\) to the mean-square transverse charge radius \(\langle b_1^2 \rangle\) via \(r_E = \sqrt{\frac{3}{2} (\langle b_1^2 \rangle + \kappa / M^2)}\), where \(\kappa\) is the anomalous magnetic moment and \(M\) the proton mass. They parametrize the Dirac form factor \(F_1(Q^2)\) and Pauli form factor \(F_2(Q^2)\) using orthogonal polynomial expansions that allow analytic Fourier transforms to transverse charge and magnetization densities, then fit these parametrizations to the 1422 cross sections from the A1 Collaboration using cross-validation and Tikhonov regularization to avoid overfitting.

## Key numbers

* Proton charge radius extracted from the full A1 data: \(r_E = 0.889(5)_{\text{stat}}(5)_{\text{syst}}(4)_{\text{model}}~\text{fm}\).
* Mean-square transverse charge radius from the best fit (\(N=5\), \(\lambda=0.02\)): \(\langle b_1^2 \rangle = 11.49~\text{GeV}^{-2}\).
* Scale parameter from the best fit: \(\Lambda = 1.156 \pm 0.029~\text{GeV}\).
* The A1 data span \(Q^2\) values from 0.004 to \(1~\text{GeV}^2\) using six beam energies (180, 315, 450, 585, 720, and 855 MeV) and three magnetic spectrometers, yielding 18 experimental data groups.
* The original A1 Collaboration radius was \(r_E = 0.879(5)_{\text{stat}}(4)_{\text{syst}}(2)_{\text{model}}(4)_{\text{group}}~\text{fm}\); the new result is larger by 0.01 fm but consistent within uncertainties.

## Context

This work addresses the "proton radius puzzle," the discrepancy between the proton charge radius from muonic hydrogen spectroscopy (0.84087 fm, Antognini 2013) and values from electronic hydrogen spectroscopy and electron-proton scattering (2014 CODATA value 0.8751 fm). The method extends prior approaches by using the transverse charge density, which has a proper relativistic interpretation, rather than relying on extrapolating the electric form factor slope to \(Q^2 = 0\). The reanalysis confirms that the A1 data (Bernauer 2010) imply a larger radius than muonic hydrogen results, ruling out fitting and extrapolation issues as the cause of the discrepancy, and the approach can be extended to study the magnetic radius and higher-order density moments.
