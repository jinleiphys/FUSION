# Generator coordinate method with a conjugate momentum: application to the particle number projection

> We discuss an extension of the generator coordinate method (GCM) by taking simultaneously a collective coordinate and its conjugate momentum as generator coordinates. To this end, we follow the idea of the dynamical GCM (DGCM) proposed by Goeke and Reinhard. We first show that the DGCM method can be regarded as an extension of the double projection method for the center of mass motion. As an application of DGCM, we then investigate the particle number projection, for which we not only carry out an integral over the gauge angle as in the usual particle number projection but also take a linear superposition of BCS states which have different mean particle numbers. We show that the ground state energy is significantly lowered by such effect, especially for magic nuclei for which the pairing gap is zero in the BCS approximation. This suggests that the present method makes a good alternative to the variation after projection (VAP) method, as the method is much simpler than the VAP.

# Generator coordinate method with a conjugate momentum: application to the particle number projection

## Key claim

The dynamical generator coordinate method (DGCM), which treats both a collective coordinate and its conjugate momentum as generator coordinates, can be applied to particle number projection by superposing BCS states with different mean particle numbers after performing the usual gauge-angle projection. This yields a significant lowering of the ground-state energy compared to the standard variation-before-projection (VBP) method, especially for magic nuclei where the BCS pairing gap is zero, and the authors suggest this method is a simpler alternative to the variation-after-projection (VAP) method.

## Method

The authors construct a set of BCS states |N⟩ with different average neutron numbers N (not necessarily integers) using the Skyrme–Hartree–Fock–BCS method with the SIII energy functional and a density-dependent pairing interaction. They then apply the particle number projection operator to each basis state and solve the Hill–Wheeler equation for the linear superposition of these projected states, using the mixed density prescription to compute Hamiltonian and overlap kernels. To avoid overcompleteness, eigenstates of the overlap kernel with eigenvalues below a cutoff of 10⁻⁵ are removed, and the discretized Hill–Wheeler equation is diagonalized with the LAPACK DSYEV routine.

## Key numbers

- For ⁵⁶Ni, the DGCM ground-state energy with 21 basis states (N_DGCM=21) is −485.04 MeV, compared to −482.74 MeV for VBP (N_DGCM=1), giving an energy gain of approximately 2.30 MeV.
- For ¹⁶O, the DGCM(21) energy is −129.29 MeV versus −128.01 MeV for VBP, a gain of about 1.28 MeV.
- For ⁴⁰Ca, the DGCM(21) energy is −342.79 MeV versus −341.30 MeV for VBP, a gain of about 1.49 MeV.
- For ⁵⁸Ni, the DGCM(21) energy is −507.79 MeV versus −506.31 MeV for VBP, a gain of about 1.48 MeV.
- For ⁶⁴Ni, the DGCM(21) energy is −559.84 MeV versus −559.44 MeV for VBP, a gain of about 0.40 MeV.
- The pairing interaction strengths V_n used range from −700.00 MeV fm⁻³ (⁴⁰Ca) to −901.98 MeV fm⁻³ (¹⁸O).
- The gauge angle φ is discretized with Δφ = 2π/80 for the particle number projection integral.
- The basis states are taken in the range N₀−2 ≤ N ≤ N₀+2 with ΔN = 0.2.

## Context

This work extends the dynamical GCM formalism originally proposed by Goeke and Reinhard (1978–1980), which had not previously been applied to concrete numerical calculations. The method is related to the double projection method of Peierls and Thouless (1962) for center-of-mass motion and to recent GCM calculations for rotational bands using cranked states by Borrajo et al. (2015) and Shimada et al. (2015, 2016). The authors note that similar GCM treatments of pairing fluctuation as a generator coordinate have been carried out by Vaquero et al. (2013) and Giuliani (2014), but argue that the DGCM approach provides a more systematic framework. The method is presented as a simpler alternative to the variation-after-projection method and the Lipkin–Nogami method, which does not work well for nuclei near shell closures according to Zuo et al. (1992) and Dobaczewski et al. (1993).
