# The Role of the Hadron-Quark Phase Transition in Core-Collapse Supernovae

> The hadron-quark phase transition in quantum chromodyanmics has been suggested as an alternative explosion mechanism for core-collapse supernovae. We study the impact of three different hadron-quark equations of state (EoS) with first-order (DD2F\_SF, STOS-B145) and second-order (CMF) phase transitions on supernova dynamics by performing 97 simulations for solar- and zero-metallicity progenitors in the range of $14\texttt{-}100\,\text{M}_\odot$. We find explosions only for two low-compactness models ($14 \text{M}_\odot$ and $16\,\text{M}_\odot$) with the DD2F\_SF EoS, both with low explosion energies of $\mathord{\sim}10^{50}\,\mathrm{erg}$. These weak explosions are characterised by a neutrino signal with several mini-bursts in the explosion phase due to complex reverse shock dynamics, in addition to the typical second neutrino burst for phase-transition driven explosions. The nucleosynthesis shows significant overproduction of nuclei such as $^{90}\mathrm{Zr}$ for the $14\,\text{M}_\odot$ zero-metallicity model and $^{94}\mathrm{Zr}$ for the $16\,\text{M}_\odot$ solar-metallicity model, but the overproduction factors are not large enough to place constraints on the occurrence of such explosions. Several other low-compactness models using the DD2F\_SF EoS and two high-compactness models using the STOS EoS end up as failed explosions and emit a second neutrino burst. For the CMF EoS, the phase transition never leads to a second bounce and explosion. For all three EoS, inverted convection occurs deep in the core of the proto-compact star due to anomalous behaviour of thermodynamic derivatives in the mixed phase, which heats the core to entropies up to $4k_\text{B}/\text{baryon}$ and may have a distinctive gravitational wave signature, also for a second-order phase transition.

## Key claim

The hadron-quark phase transition can drive weak supernova explosions only for low-compactness progenitors (ξ₂.₅ < 0.15) using the DD2F_SF equation of state, but these explosions have low energies of ~10⁵⁰ erg, much weaker than the hyperenergetic explosions previously suggested for very massive stars by Fischer (2017).

## Method

The authors performed 97 general-relativistic hydrodynamic simulations in spherical symmetry with neutrino transport using the CoCoNuT-FMT code, scanning 40 progenitor models in the mass range 14–100 M⊙ at solar and zero metallicity. They tested three hybrid equations of state: DD2F_SF (first-order transition), STOS-B145 (first-order transition), and CMF (second-order/crossover transition), and post-processed the two successful explosions for nucleosynthesis using the Kepler reaction network.

## Key numbers

- Only 2 explosions out of 97 simulations: zero-metallicity 14 M⊙ and solar-metallicity 16 M⊙ models, both using the DD2F_SF EoS
- Explosion energies: 3.64 × 10⁴⁹ erg for z14 and 1.25 × 10⁵⁰ erg for s16 at the end of the simulations
- Both exploding models have compactness parameter ξ₂.₅ < 0.15
- Inverted convection heats the core to entropies up to 4 k_B/baryon for all three equations of state
- The 50 M⊙ progenitor z50 has a He core mass of 17.78 M⊙, CO core mass of 11.34 M⊙, and Fe core mass of 1.86 M⊙, compared to 21.8 M⊙, 19.3 M⊙, and 2.21 M⊙ respectively in the Umeda (2008) models used by Fischer (2017)

## Context

This work systematically extends prior studies of phase-transition-driven supernovae by Fischer (2017, 2020, 2021) and Zha (2021) to a much larger progenitor set (40 models versus the few previously studied). The results contradict the expectation of hyperenergetic explosions from very massive progenitors and instead find only weak explosions in low-mass, low-compactness stars. The study also provides the first systematic comparison of first-order (DD2F_SF, STOS-B145) and second-order/crossover (CMF) phase transitions in supernova dynamics, finding that the CMF EoS never produces a second bounce or explosion. The nucleosynthesis analysis shows overproduction of ⁹⁰Zr and ⁹⁴Zr but at levels insufficient to constrain the occurrence of such explosions.
