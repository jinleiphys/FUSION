# Topics in Chiral Perturbation Theory

> I consider some selected topics in chiral perturbation theory (CHPT). For the meson sector, emphasis is put on processes involving pions in the isospin zero S-wave which require multi-loop calculations. The advantages and shortcomings of heavy baryon CHPT are discussed. Some recent results on the structure of the baryons are also presented.

## Key claim

Chiral perturbation theory (CHPT) provides a rigorous and systematic calculational scheme for low-energy QCD, but its applicability is limited to energies below the scale Λ ≃ Mρ = 770 MeV, and multi-loop calculations become increasingly cumbersome yet are sometimes unavoidable, particularly for processes involving pions in the isospin zero S-wave where strong final-state interactions dominate.

## Method

The paper reviews the effective Lagrangian approach to CHPT, expanding the QCD Lagrangian in terms of increasing dimension (Eq. 4) with Goldstone boson fields and external sources. For the meson sector, multi-loop calculations are performed for the scalar form factor of the pion and for γγ → π⁰π⁰, using unitarity to extend the range of CHPT via dispersion-theoretical representations. For the baryon sector, the heavy baryon formalism is employed, expanding the nucleon four-momentum as pμ = m vμ + lμ (Eq. 15) to eliminate the large baryon mass term and establish a consistent power counting.

## Key numbers

- Weinberg's leading-order result for the S-wave, isospin zero ππ scattering length: a₀⁰ = 7 Mπ² / (32 π Fπ²) (Eq. 6)
- Gasser-Leutwyler one-loop prediction: a₀⁰ = 0.20 ± 0.01 (Eq. 7), compatible with data a₀⁰ = 0.23 ± 0.08
- Phase of the CP-violation parameter ε': Φ(ε') = (45 ± 6)° (Eq. 9), compared to tree-level value 37° and experimental value (43 ± 8)°
- Leading-order CHPT prediction for nucleon electromagnetic polarizabilities: ᾱp = ᾱn = 12.2 × 10⁻⁴ fm³, β̄p = β̄n = 1.2 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ (Eq. 20)
- Empirical proton polarizabilities: ᾱp = (10.4 ± 0.6) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, β̄p = (3.8 ∓ 0.6) × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Empirical neutron polarizabilities: ᾱn = (12.3 ± 1.3) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, β̄n = (3.5 ∓ 1.3) × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Complete O(E⁴) calculation results: ᾱp = 10.5 ± 2.0, β̄p = 3.5 ± 3.6, ᾱn = 13.4 ± 1.5, β̄n = 7.8 ± 3.6 (all in 10⁻⁴ fm³) (Eq. 22)
- Ratio of experimental to one-loop cross section for γγ → π⁰π⁰ at 400 MeV: (σ_exp / σ_1-loop)^(1/2) = 1.3 (Eq. 13)
- Hyperon polarizabilities at leading order: ᾱΣ⁻ = 6, ᾱΣ⁺ = 9 (in canonical units) (Eq. 27)
- Baryon mass decomposition for nucleon: mN = (0.965 - 0.018 - 0.264 + 0.248) GeV = 0.936 GeV (Eq. 32)
- Baryon mass decomposition for Λ: mΛ = (0.965 - 0.006 - 0.588 + 0.743) GeV = 1.141 GeV (Eq. 32)
- KN σ-terms at O(E³): σ^(1)_KN(0) ≃ 200 ± 50 MeV, σ^(2)_KN(0) ≃ 140 ± 40 MeV (Eq. 34)

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on CHPT by Weinberg (1979), Gasser and Leutwyler (1984, 1985), and the heavy baryon formalism developed by Jenkins and Manohar (1991). It addresses the long-standing discrepancy between one-loop CHPT predictions and Crystal Ball data for γγ → π⁰π⁰, resolved by the two-loop calculation of Bellucci, Gasser and Sainio (1994). The paper also discusses the alternative framework of Stern et al. (1993) for the case of a small quark condensate B ∼ Fπ, and highlights the need for novel high-precision low-energy experiments at facilities like DAΦNE to distinguish between conventional and generalized CHPT scenarios. Open questions include the extension to three-flavor baryon CHPT with decuplet fields, the proper treatment of analytic structures in the heavy mass approach, and the need for O(E⁵) calculations for magnetic polarizabilities.
