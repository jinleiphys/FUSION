# The quest for novel modes of excitation in exotic nuclei

> This article provides an insight into several open problems in the quest for novel modes of excitation in nuclei with isospin asymmetry, deformation and finite temperature characteristic in stellar environment. Major unsolved problems include the nature of pygmy dipole resonances, the quest for various multipole and spin-isospin excitations both in neutron-rich and proton drip-line nuclei mainly driven by loosely bound nucleons, excitations in unstable deformed nuclei and evolution of their properties with the shape phase transition. Exotic modes of excitation in nuclei at finite temperatures characteristic for supernova evolution present open problems with possible impact in modeling astrophysically relevant weak interaction rates. All these issues challenge self-consistent many body theory frameworks at the frontiers of on-going research, including nuclear energy density functionals, both phenomenological and constrained by the strong interaction physics of QCD, models based on low-momentum two-nucleon interaction V_{low-k} and correlated realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction V_{UCOM}, supplemented by three-body force, as well as two-nucleon and three-nucleon interactions derived from the chiral effective field theory. Joined theoretical and experimental efforts, including research with radioactive isotope beams, are needed to provide insight into dynamical properties of nuclei away from the valley of stability, involving the interplay of isospin asymmetry, deformation and finite temperature.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) has been the focus of most research on exotic excitations, there remain fundamental open questions about its collectivity, location relative to the neutron separation energy, and connection to other modes; these issues challenge current self-consistent many-body frameworks and require both theoretical advances and experimental efforts with radioactive isotope beams.

## Method

The paper reviews the current status of theoretical and experimental research on exotic nuclear excitation modes, surveying results from self-consistent random phase approximation (RPA) and quasiparticle RPA (QRPA) frameworks based on Skyrme, Gogny, and relativistic energy density functionals, as well as from models using low-momentum two-nucleon interactions (V_low-k and V_UCOM) and chiral effective field theory. It identifies specific unsolved problems across multiple domains: multipole excitations in neutron-rich and proton-rich nuclei, charge-exchange modes relevant for weak interaction rates, excitations in deformed nuclei, and excitations at finite temperatures characteristic of stellar environments.

## Key numbers

* The phase transition from superfluid to normal state in open-shell nuclei occurs at temperatures T approximately 0.5-1 MeV, while for temperatures above T approximately 4 MeV contributions from continuum states become large.
* In neutron-rich 154Sm, a magnetic dipole state with unique structure properties appears at low excitation energy of 2.5 MeV, corresponding to a novel exotic resonance with isoscalar character.
* Missing low-energy dipole transition strength in photon scattering experiments can vary from a few percent to a factor of three, depending on the nucleus under consideration.
* For lower-mass isotopes the PDR is located below the neutron threshold, while in more neutron-rich isotopes the PDR energy is higher than the neutron separation energy.
* The general trend of increasing pygmy dipole strength with neutron-proton asymmetry alpha = (N-Z)/A has been established, though uncertainties remain in the systematic dependence on alpha.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on nuclear structure away from stability, particularly following studies of the pygmy dipole resonance in neutron-rich nuclei (Paar et al. 2007) and experimental investigations using photon scattering (Savran et al. 2008, 2009) and Coulomb dissociation of radioactive beams (Adrich et al. 2005). The paper identifies open problems that challenge current self-consistent frameworks, including energy density functionals (Bender et al. 2003, Dobaczewski et al. 2007), low-momentum interactions (Bogner et al. 2003), and chiral effective field theory (Epelbaum et al. 2009), and calls for future developments that can simultaneously describe ground-state properties and excitation spectra, as well as enable systematic studies of weak interaction rates for astrophysical applications such as supernova core collapse and r-process nucleosynthesis.
