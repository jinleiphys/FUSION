# Superfluid quantum criticality and the thermal evolution of neutron stars

> The neutron star starts to cool down shortly after its birth by emitting neutrinos. As it becomes cold enough, the Cooper pairs of neutrons are formed, triggering a superfluid transition. Previous studies on neutron superfluidity focused on finite-temperature transitions, with little attention paid to the potentially important quantum critical phenomena associated with superfluidity. Here, we provide the first theoretical analysis of superfluid quantum criticality, concentrating on its impact on neutron star cooling. Extensive calculations found that superfluidity occurs within a finite range of neutron star density $ρ$. The density serves as a nonthermal parameter for a superfluid quantum phase transition. In a broad quantum critical region, gapless neutrons are strongly coupled to the quantum critical fluctuations of the superfluid order parameter. We handle this coupling using both perturbation theory and renormalization group methods and find that it leads to non-Fermi liquid behavior, which yields a logarithmic $T\ln(1/T)$ correction to the neutron specific heat $c_{\mathrm{n}}\propto T$ and also dramatically alters the neutrino emissivity. Quantum critical phenomena emerge much earlier than the onset of superfluidity and persist throughout almost the entire lifetime of a neutron star. At low temperatures, these phenomena coexist with superfluidity in the neutron star interior but occupy different layers. We incorporate superfluid quantum criticality into the theoretical description of neutron star cooling and show that it substantially prolongs the thermal relaxation time. By varying the strength of superfluid fluctuations and other quantities, we obtain an excellent fit to the observed cooling data of a number of neutron stars. Our results indicate an intriguing correlation between superfluid quantum criticality and the thermal evolution of neutron stars.

## Key claim

This paper provides the first theoretical analysis of superfluid quantum criticality in neutron stars, showing that quantum critical fluctuations of the superfluid order parameter produce non-Fermi liquid behavior that substantially prolongs the thermal relaxation time of neutron stars, but the authors hedge that the agreement with observations depends on varying the strength of superfluid fluctuations and other quantities to obtain fits.

## Method

The authors constructed an effective low-energy field theory describing the Yukawa-type coupling between gapless neutrons on the Fermi surface and quantum fluctuations of the superfluid order parameter near the quantum critical points at densities where the neutron superfluid gap vanishes. They computed the neutron self-energy using both perturbation theory based on a 1/N expansion scheme (with N=2) and renormalization group methods, then incorporated the resulting non-Fermi liquid corrections to specific heat and neutrino emissivity into numerical simulations of neutron star cooling curves.

## Key numbers

- The surface temperature of the Cassiopeia A neutron star was observed to decrease from 2.12 × 10^6 K to 2.04 × 10^6 K during 2000-2009, with a revised cooling rate of 2% over a decade (down from an earlier 4% estimate)
- The Cas A neutron star is approximately 340 years old
- The neutron specific heat acquires a logarithmic T ln(1/T) correction to the original linear c_n ∝ T behavior
- The maximum superfluid transition temperature T_cn^max is approximately 10^9 K for weak superfluidity (model "a") and approximately 10^10 K for strong superfluidity (model "c")
- The density ρ_0 ≈ 2.0 × 10^14 g/cm^3 marks the boundary between crust and core
- Two rotation-powered pulsars (PSR J0205+6449 and PSR B2334+61) younger than 10^4 yr have surface temperatures approximately 5 × 10^5 K
- Some X-ray emitting isolated neutron stars with ages roughly 10^6 yr are estimated to be as warm as approximately 10^6 K

## Context

This work extends the minimal cooling paradigm of Page (2011) and Shternin (2011), which relied on pairing breaking and formation (PBF) processes to explain the Cas A neutron star cooling, but which Potekhin (2018) and Leinson (2022) indicated was not efficient enough. The paper introduces concepts from condensed-matter quantum criticality studied by Sachdev (2000), Vojta (2003), and others into neutron star physics, enabling a new cooling paradigm that can simultaneously explain the cooling data of multiple neutron star classes including thermally emitting isolated neutron stars, rotation-powered pulsars, and X-ray emitting isolated neutron stars.
