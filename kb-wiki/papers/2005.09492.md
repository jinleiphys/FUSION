# A two-neutron halo is unveiled in $^{29}$F

> We report the measurement of reaction cross sections ($σ_R^{\rm ex}$) of $^{27,29}$F with a carbon target at RIKEN. The unexpectedly large $σ_R^{\rm ex}$ and derived matter radius identify $^{29}$F as the heaviest two-neutron Borromean halo to date. The halo is attributed to neutrons occupying the $2p_{3/2}$ orbital, thereby vanishing the shell closure associated with the neutron number $N = 20$. The results are explained by state-of-the-art shell model calculations. Coupled-cluster computations based on effective field theories of the strong nuclear force describe the matter radius of $^{27}$F but are challenged for $^{29}$F.

## Key claim

The measurement of reaction cross sections for $^{27,29}$F with a carbon target at RIKEN reveals $^{29}$F as the heaviest two-neutron Borromean halo known, attributed to neutrons occupying the $2p_{3/2}$ orbital and thereby vanishing the shell closure at neutron number $N = 20$, though coupled-cluster computations based on chiral effective field theory describe the matter radius of $^{27}$F but are challenged for $^{29}$F.

## Method

The experiment was performed at the RIKEN Radioactive Isotope Beam Factory using the BigRIPS fragment separator and ZeroDegree spectrometer. $^{27,29}$F isotopes were produced from fragmentation of a $^{48}$Ca beam at 345$A$ MeV on a 10 mm thick rotating Be target, and reaction cross sections were measured via the transmission technique with a $2.01 \pm 0.01$ g/cm$^2$ thick carbon target at F8, with unreacted nuclei counted at the F11 focal plane. The experimental matter radii were extracted by comparing measured reaction cross sections to Glauber model calculations using harmonic oscillator densities, and the neutron orbital composition was assessed by modeling $^{29}$F as $^{27}$F+n+n with Woods-Saxon wavefunctions.

## Key numbers

- Reaction cross section $\sigma_R^{\rm ex}$ for $^{27}$F: 1243(14) mb on a carbon target at approximately 240$A$ MeV
- Reaction cross section $\sigma_R^{\rm ex}$ for $^{29}$F: 1396(28) mb on a carbon target at approximately 240$A$ MeV, showing a steep increase of about 12(2)% relative to $^{27}$F
- Experimental point matter radius $R_m^{\rm ex}$ for $^{27}$F: 3.15 $\pm$ 0.04 fm
- Experimental point matter radius $R_m^{\rm ex}$ for $^{29}$F: 3.50 $\pm$ 0.07 fm, an increase of about 11(3)% compared to $^{27}$F
- Derived root-mean-square halo radius for $^{29}$F: 6.6 fm, assuming similar proton distributions in $^{27}$F and $^{29}$F
- Two-neutron separation energy $S_{2n}$ for $^{29}$F: 1.4(6) MeV from prior work (Gaudefroy 2012)
- Difference between $R_m^{\rm ex}$ of $^{29}$F and its core $^{27}$F: 0.35 $\pm$ 0.08 fm

## Context

This work extends the known set of Borromean two-neutron halos (previously $^6$He, $^{11}$Li, $^{14}$Be, $^{17}$B, and $^{22}$C) to the heaviest such system and the first in the proton $sd$-shell, demonstrating that the $N = 20$ shell closure vanishes at the neutron drip line in fluorine. The finding challenges coupled-cluster ab initio computations using chiral interactions such as NNLO$_{\rm sat}$ and $\Delta$-NNLO$_{\rm GO}$ (Ekstrom 2015, Jiang 2019), which describe $^{27}$F but underpredict the $^{29}$F radius, while shell-model calculations with the EEdf1 interaction (Tsunoda 2017) reproduce the observed halo. This discovery enables future experiments to characterize the predicted halo in $^{31}$F (Michel 2020) and investigate pairing antihalo effects (Masui 2020), with implications for neutron capture rates in the rapid neutron capture process near $N = 50$, 82, and 126.
