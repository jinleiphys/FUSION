# Anti-cluster Decay and Anti-alpha Decay of Antimatter nuclei

> A broad extension of periodic system into the sector of antimatter could be possible sometimes in a remote future. We expect that anti-alpha spontaneous emission from an antimatter nucleus will have the same Q-value and half-life as alpha emission from the corresponding mirror nucleus. This is the consequence of the invariance of binding energy as well as of the surface and Coulomb energy when passing from matter to antimatter nuclei with the the same mass number and the same atomic number. The Q-values and half-lives of all measured up to now 27 cluster radioactivities are given together with Q-values and half-lives of the most important competitor --- $α$ decay. The lightest anti-alpha emitter, $^8\bar{Be}$, will have a very short half-life of about $81.9\cdot 10^{-18}$ s.

## Key claim

The authors expect that anti-alpha spontaneous emission from an antimatter nucleus will have the same Q-value and half-life as alpha emission from the corresponding mirror nucleus, provided the mass number and atomic number are the same. This prediction depends on the invariance of binding energy, surface energy, and Coulomb energy when passing from matter to antimatter nuclei.

## Method

The authors applied the macroscopic-microscopic method, adding shell and pairing corrections to deformation energies calculated from the liquid drop model (LDM) and the Yukawa-plus-Exponential model (Y+EM). They computed potential barriers for binary decays of antinuclei using a two-center shell model parametrization with two intersected spheres, and calculated half-lives using the analytical superasymmetric fission (ASAF) model. The Q-values were updated using the 2012 atomic mass evaluation of Wang et al. (2012).

## Key numbers

- The lightest anti-alpha emitter, \(^8\bar{Be}\), will have a half-life of about \(81.9 \cdot 10^{-18}\) s (81.9 attoseconds), the same as \(^8Be \rightarrow \alpha + \alpha\).
- For \(^{242}Cm\) emitting \(^{34}Si\), the Q-value is 96.509 MeV and the experimental half-life is \(\log_{10} T_c^{exp}(s) = 23.15\).
- For \(^{221}Fr\) emitting \(^{14}C\), the Q-value is 31.291 MeV and the experimental half-life is \(\log_{10} T_c^{exp}(s) = 14.52\).
- For \(^{232}U\) emitting \(^{24}Ne\), the Q-value is 62.309 MeV and the experimental half-life is \(\log_{10} T_c^{exp}(s) = 20.42\).
- The radius constant used in the LDM is \(r_0 = 1.2249\) fm; in the Y+EM it is \(r_0 = 1.16\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the concept of cluster radioactivity, previously studied for heavy nuclei by Poenaru and collaborators (1995, 2010, 2011), into the domain of antimatter nuclei. The authors rely on the established equality of mass and binding energy between antinuclei and their mirror nuclei, as confirmed by experiments summarized by Cloet et al. (2009). The results suggest that if antimatter nuclei can be produced in sufficient quantity, their decay modes could be predicted from known nuclear data, with the lightest candidate \(^8\bar{Be}\) offering the most accessible test case.
