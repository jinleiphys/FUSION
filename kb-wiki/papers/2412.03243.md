# Impact of nuclear masses on r-process nucleosynthesis: bulk properties versus shell effects

> We investigate the impact of the model estimating the masses of exotic nuclei on r-process nucleosynthesis, assessing the dependence of the abundance distribution on the specific properties of nuclear masses. By decomposing theoretical nuclear mass predictions into a liquid-drop parametrization and local shell effects, we show that r-process abundances are virtually insensitive to large variations of the masses which originate from nuclear bulk properties of the model, such as the symmetry energy. In contrast, the mass component associated with local shell effects is the main driver of r-process abundance variations, despite its relatively minor contribution to the absolute value of neutron separation energies. Our work suggests that experimental and theoretical studies of masses devoted to r-process applications, such as the nucleosynthesis in the ejecta of neutron star mergers, should focus on the physical origin and determination of local changes in mass trends.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that r-process abundance distributions are virtually insensitive to large variations in nuclear masses originating from bulk properties (such as symmetry energy), while local shell effects are the main driver of abundance variations, despite their relatively minor contribution to the absolute value of neutron separation energies.

## Method

The authors decomposed theoretical nuclear mass predictions from the Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM) and the Duflo-Zuker mass formula (DZ31) into a liquid-drop parametrization (smooth bulk part) and local shell effects (residuals). They then generated new mass tables by mixing the smooth energy part and shell corrections of the original models, and performed nuclear network calculations using the Hauser-Feshbach statistical theory with TALYS 1.95 for 2015 trajectories simulating dynamical ejecta of neutron star mergers.

## Key numbers

- The root-mean-square (rms) error between DZ31 and FRDM for nuclear masses is 3.30 MeV, for two-neutron separation energies is 0.98 MeV, and for two-neutron shell gaps is 1.09 MeV
- Changing shell effects by 0.57 MeV (rms in masses) translates to an average change in final mass abundance distribution of approximately 35%
- For mixed mass models, increasing DZ31 shell effects by 25% produces increases of 0.57 MeV, 0.24 MeV, and 0.27 MeV in the rms error of binding energies, two-neutron separation energies, and two-neutron shell gaps, respectively
- The rms error between FRDM and AME2020 experimental masses is 1.07 MeV, while between DZ31 and AME2020 it is 0.43 MeV
- For the mixed model with 100% DZ31 shell effects, the average deviation of elemental abundances from FRDM is 105.0% and for mass abundances is 134.41%

## Context

This work extends previous studies comparing different mass models for r-process nucleosynthesis (Arcones 2011, Arcones 2012, Mendoza-Temis 2015, Mumpower 2015, Martin 2016a, Sprouse 2020, Vassh 2021, Zhu 2021, Kullmann 2023a) by explicitly decomposing mass predictions to determine which features of the mass surface are important. The findings suggest that experimental campaigns at radioactive ion-beam facilities should focus on determining mass trends across extended regions rather than measuring isolated individual nuclei, and that ab-initio calculations capturing local trends may provide valuable information even without accurate absolute mass values.
