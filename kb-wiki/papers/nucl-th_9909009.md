# "Pion laser" phenomenon and other possible signatures of the DCC at RHIC and LHC energies

> The decay of fireballs containing the disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) in A+A collisions has been analyzed. We found that the high phase-space density and probably the large final fireball volume are the favorable factors to extract a DCC signal from the thermal background. Both of these factors are expected to take place at RHIC and LHC energies. A coherent pion component then can be observed in pion spectra, in Bose-Einstein correlations and in distribution of the ratio of neutral to total pions.

# Pion laser phenomenon and other possible signatures of the DCC at RHIC and LHC energies

## Key claim

The decay of a disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions can produce a coherent pion component observable above the thermal background, provided the phase-space density is sufficiently high and the fireball volume is large, conditions expected at RHIC and LHC. The "pion laser" effect, a strengthening of a weak coherent signal in a dense bosonic thermal environment, requires that the system be effectively finite and that a symmetry-breaking term (e.g., a classical source from DCC decay) be present in the Hamiltonian, unlike the model of independent factorized sources which yields only chaotic radiation.

## Method

The authors model DCC decay using the linear sigma model in the Hartree (mean-field) approximation, treating the DCC as a classical field localized in a hot, dense region and interacting with thermal quasipions. The system evolves through a quasiequilibrium thermal stage (described by a statistical operator with chemical potentials for pions) followed by a freeze-out stage where the DCC relaxes to the normal vacuum; the evolution is solved in the Heisenberg representation. Observable signatures, one-particle inclusive spectra, two-particle Bose-Einstein correlation functions, and the distribution of the neutral-to-total pion ratio, are computed by averaging over random orientations of the DCC field in isospin space.

## Key numbers

- The "pion laser" regime requires phase-space density \( n_{ps} \gg 1 \); the signal-to-noise ratio \( D({\bf p}) \propto \kappa \sqrt{n_{ps}} \), where \( \kappa \) characterizes the initial classical field strength.
- For the "pion laser" regime, the soft-pion energy region where effects are observable is \( (p^0 - m_\pi) < R^{-1} \ln n_{ps} \); the inverse slope of the transverse energy spectrum at small energies is \( 1/(4 m_\pi R_c^2) \), compared to \( T_f \) at high energies.
- For the rare-gas regime (no overpopulation), the soft-pion region is narrower: \( (p^0 - m_\pi) < (4 m_\pi R^2)^{-1} \); the inverse slope is \( (4 m_\pi R^2)^{-1} \), considerably less than \( T_f \).
- For fully coherent emission (\( D({\bf p}) \to \infty \)), the intercepts of the correlation functions become \( C^{++}({\bf p},{\bf 0}) = C^{+-}({\bf p},{\bf 0}) \to 1.2 \).
- The effective interferometry radius squared in the condensation regime is \( R_{\rm eff}^2({\bf p}) \simeq R_c^2 / [1 + 2 \ln(N_{\pi^+}({\bf p})/N_{\rm gas}({\bf p}))] \).
- The \( f \)-distribution (ratio of neutral to total pions) is nonzero in the interval \( 1/[3(1+D)] < f < (1/3 + D)/(1+D) \); its width is \( D/(1+D) \), which must exceed the thermal fluctuation width \( \sqrt{2/(9 N_{\pi_{\rm tot}})} \) for detectability.
- For the rare-gas case, the integrated coherent fraction \( D \simeq D({\bf 0}) / [8 (R \sqrt{T_f m(t_f)})^3] \), which can be much smaller than \( D({\bf 0}) \).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of DCC signals by Bjorken (1997) and others, and addresses the problem that previous experimental searches found no clear DCC evidence due to thermal background contamination. The "pion laser" concept was introduced by Pratt (1993) but in the model of independent factorized sources (Pratt, Lednicky, Csorgo, Zajc) leads only to chaotic radiation; the present paper shows that a classical source from DCC decay can produce coherence. The analysis builds on the linear sigma model treatment of Randrup (1997) and the finite-lifetime DCC decay of Steele and Koch (1998). The results enable experimental searches at RHIC and LHC using combined analysis of soft-pion spectra, HBT correlations, and the neutral-to-total pion ratio, with the possibility of detecting weak DCC signals even at constant phase-space densities if the fireball volume is large.
