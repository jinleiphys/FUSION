# Comment on "Negative heat and first order phase transitions in nuclei" by Moretto et al

> The recent paper nucl-th/0208024 by Moretto et al. is commented: Their picture of nuclear phase transition in terms of macroscopic control parameters, temperature and pressure, is irrelevant. Their criticism of order-disorder phase-transitions on a periodic lattice uses the wrong scenario. This transition has nothing to do with the liquid-gas transition of a single spherical droplet.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Moretto et al.'s criticism of negative heat capacities in lattice calculations (Ising or Potts model) with periodic boundaries is based on an irrelevant macroscopic picture; the transition from ordered to disordered spin distributions at constant volume has nothing to do with the liquid-gas transition of a single spherical droplet at constant pressure.

## Method

The author critiques Moretto et al.'s use of macroscopic control parameters (temperature and pressure) for describing phase transitions in small nuclear systems, contrasting it with microcanonical ensemble calculations on a q=10 Potts model lattice at constant volume. The comment emphasizes that in a completely filled lattice, phase separation involves multiple regions of ordered and disordered spins (bubbles and droplets), not a single spherical droplet, and that surface entropy from fluctuations in shape and number of interfaces diminishes surface tension toward the critical point.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out volume in nuclear fragmentation calculations: approximately 4–6 times normal nuclear volume (no further precision given).
- The q=10 Potts model is cited as the system used in prior lattice simulations of the caloric curve (Gross, 2001).

## Context

This comment extends a long-standing dispute between Gross and Moretto (Gross, 1997; Gross, 2001) about whether macroscopic intensive variables like temperature and pressure are appropriate control parameters for phase transitions in small nuclear systems. The author argues that nuclear multifragmentation experiments have constant energy but no heat bath or vapor pressure, and that Moretto et al.'s figure 3 fails to account for surface entropy. The work enables further debate on the correct ensemble (microcanonical vs. canonical) for describing nuclear phase transitions.
