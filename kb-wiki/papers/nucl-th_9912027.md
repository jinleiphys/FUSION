# Determination of the Mean-Field Momentum-Dependence using Elliptic Flow

> Midrapidity nucleon elliptic flow is studied within the Boltzmann-equation simulations of symmetric heavy-ion collisions. The simulations follow a lattice Hamiltonian extended to relativistic transport. It is demonstrated that in the peripheral heavy-ion collisions the high-momentum elliptic flow is strongly sensitive to the momentum dependence of mean field at supranormal densities. The high transverse-momentum particles are directly and exclusively emitted from the high-density zone in the collisions, while remaining particles primarily continue along the beam axis. The elliptic flow was measured by the KaoS Collaboration as a function of the transverse momentum at a number of impact parameters in Bi + Bi collisions at 400, 700, and 1000 MeV/nucleon. The observed elliptic anisotropies in peripheral collisions, which quickly rise with momentum, can only be explained in simulations when assuming a strong momentum dependence of nucleonic mean field. This momentum dependence must strengthen with the rise of density above normal. The mean-field parametrizations, which describe the data in simulations with various success, are confronted with mean fields from microscopic nuclear-matter calculations. Two of the microscopic potentials in the comparisons have unacceptably weak momentum-dependencies at supranormal densities. The optical potentials from the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations, on the other hand, together with the UV14 + TNI potential from variational calculations, agree rather well within the region of sensitivity with the parametrized potentials that best describe the data.

## Key claim

The high-momentum elliptic flow in peripheral heavy-ion collisions is strongly sensitive to the momentum dependence of the mean field at supranormal densities, and the observed elliptic anisotropies can only be explained in simulations when assuming a strong momentum dependence that must strengthen with the rise of density above normal.

## Method

The study used Boltzmann-equation simulations of symmetric heavy-ion collisions, following a lattice Hamiltonian extended to relativistic transport. The simulations employed parametrizations of the energy functional that allowed flexibility in the mean field and equation of state, with the phase-space density represented by test-particles. The elliptic flow was calculated as the second moment of the azimuthal angle relative to the reaction plane, \(v_2 = \langle \cos(2\phi) \rangle\), at midrapidity as a function of transverse momentum and impact parameter.

## Key numbers

- Bi + Bi collisions at 400, 700, and 1000 MeV/nucleon were used for comparison with KaoS Collaboration data.
- Normal nuclear matter density is taken as \(\rho_0 = 0.160\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- For momentum-independent mean fields, the gradient coefficient is \(a_1 = 21.4\) MeV fm\(^2\); for momentum-dependent fields, \(a_1 = 18.2\) MeV fm\(^2\).
- The isospin interaction coefficient is \(a_T = 97\) MeV.
- Two incompressibility values were used: \(K = 210\) MeV and \(K = 380\) MeV, with corresponding parameter sets for the mean-field potential.
- The energy per nucleon at normal density was set to \(e/\rho - m_N \approx -16.0\) MeV for \(K=210\) MeV and \(-17.0\) MeV for \(K=380\) MeV.
- The optical potential fit by Feldmeier and Lindner has an attributed error of the order of 5 MeV.

## Context

This work extends prior investigations of momentum dependence in the nucleonic optical potential at normal and subnormal densities using nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering data (Menet 1971, Schwandt 1982, Hama 1990, Klein 1994) and microscopic predictions for supranormal densities (Wiringa 1988, Baldo 1989, Li 1993, Inns 1994, Lee 1997). Previous circumstantial evidence for momentum dependence was seen in flow measurements (Pan 1993, Zhang 1994, Pak 1996, Huns 1996), but no quantitative assessment was possible. The results enable quantitative determination of the momentum dependence at supranormal densities and allow comparison with microscopic nuclear-matter calculations, specifically finding that Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock optical potentials and the UV14 + TNI variational potential agree well with the parametrizations that best describe the data, while two other microscopic potentials have unacceptably weak momentum dependencies at supranormal densities.
