# Neural network-based prediction of particle-induced fission cross sections for r-process nucleosynthesis trained with dynamical reaction models

> Large-scale computations of fission properties play a crucial role in nuclear reaction network calculations simulating rapid neutron-capture process (r-process) nucleosynthesis. Due to the large number of fissioning nuclei contributing to the r-process, a description of particle-induced fission reactions is computationally challenging. In this work, we use theoretical calculations based on the INCL+ABLA models to train neural networks (NN). The results for the prediction of proton-induced spallation reactions, in particular fission, utilizing a large variety of NN models across the hyper-parameter space are presented, which are relevant for r-process calculations.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a neural network trained on theoretical INCL+ABLA model calculations can reproduce proton-induced fission and total reaction cross sections for stable nuclei with 1-2% error and can extrapolate predictions to exotic, neutron-rich nuclei beyond the known range, though the current work only demonstrates this for proton-induced spallation reactions, not neutron-induced fission directly relevant to r-process conditions.

## Method

The authors used the INCL++ intranuclear cascade model coupled with the ABLA++ de-excitation code to generate simulated cross section data for known nuclei, then trained the SoKAI neural network framework on this synthetic dataset. The network architecture consisted of four layers with 184 trainable parameters, using LeakyReLU and Sigmoid activation functions, with input features including nuclear charge (Z), mass number (A), and the radii of proton (Rp) and neutron (Rn) density profiles. The model was evaluated using absolute loss function and applied to predict both total reaction and fission cross sections for proton-induced spallation reactions.

## Key numbers

- Neural network reproduced fission and total cross sections with an estimated error of 1-2% for stable nuclei
- Network architecture contained four layers with a total of 184 trainable parameters
- Input layer received information on nuclear charge (Z), mass number (A), and radii of proton (Rp) and neutron (Rn) density profiles

## Context

This work extends the use of machine learning for nuclear data by training on the INCL+ABLA dynamical reaction models (Rodríguez-Sánchez 2022, 2024) rather than on experimental data alone, addressing the computational challenge of describing particle-induced fission for the thousands of nuclei involved in r-process nucleosynthesis calculations. The approach enables predictions for exotic nuclei beyond the neutron drip line where no experimental data or theoretical models currently exist, and the authors plan to extend the framework to predict fission yields and prompt neutron multiplicities using recent inverse-kinematics fission data from GANIL and GSI/FAIR facilities (Ramos 2020, Gabriel 2023, Graña-González 2023, Chatillon 2022).
