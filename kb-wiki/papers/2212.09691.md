# Monopole and instanton effects on connected and disconnected correlations for scalar density

> This study investigates the effects on the connected and disconnected correlations for the scalar density that are induced by created monopoles and instantons in the QCD vacuum. To reveal the effects, we add a monopole and anti-monopole pair in the gauge field configurations in \textit{SU}(3) by applying the monopole creation operator to the vacuum. We vary the magnetic charges of the monopole and anti-monopole and increase the number of monopoles and anti-monopoles in the configurations. The Dirac operator of overlap fermions preserves the exact chiral symmetry in lattice gauge theory and exact zero-modes exist in its spectrum. The eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the overlap Dirac operator have been calculated using these configurations, and the numbers of instantons and anti-instantons which are created by these additional monopoles and anti-monopoles have been estimated from the numbers of topological charges in our previous studies. In this study, we demonstrate the preliminary results that instantons and monopoles influence the masses that are evaluated from the connected and disconnected correlation functions for the scalar density using low-lying eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the overlap Dirac operator.

## Key claim
The paper presents preliminary results that instantons and monopoles influence the masses evaluated from connected and disconnected correlation functions for the scalar density, with the hedge that the mass estimated from the disconnected correlation may not correspond to a stable state because the effective mass does not achieve a plateau even at large temporal separations.

## Method
The author adds monopole and anti-monopole pairs to quenched SU(3) gauge field configurations using the monopole creation operator, varying the magnetic charges from 2 to 6. The eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the overlap Dirac operator (which preserves exact chiral symmetry) are computed, and the number of instantons and anti-instantons is estimated from the topological charge. Connected and disconnected correlation functions for the scalar density are calculated using low-lying eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and masses are extracted by fitting exponential and hyperbolic cosine functions to the correlation functions.

## Key numbers
- Lattice volume: V = 18^3 × 32, lattice spacing a = 8.527×10^{-2} fm (β = 6.0522)
- Input quark mass: am_q = 1.729×10^{-2} (approximately 40 MeV in physical units)
- Number of instantons and anti-instantons N_I: Normal conf = 9.7(5), m_c=2 → 13.6(7), m_c=4 → 15.7(8), m_c=6 → 17.7(9)
- Square root of instanton density ρ_I^{1/2}: Normal conf = 3.85(9)×10^{-2} GeV^2, m_c=6 → 5.22(13)×10^{-2} GeV^2
- Connected correlation mass m_s from curve fit: Normal conf = 5.78(5)×10^2 MeV, m_c=6 → 6.27(5)×10^2 MeV
- Disconnected correlation mass m_{dis-s} from curve fit: Normal conf = 73(3) MeV, m_c=6 → 1.15(2)×10^2 MeV
- Scalar-prime meson mass m_{s-prime}: Normal conf = 6.5(2)×10^2 MeV, m_c=6 → 9.3(2)×10^2 MeV
- Mass ratio m_{dis-s}/m_s from curve fit: Normal conf = 0.125(5), m_c=6 → 0.183(4)
- Mass ratio m_{s-prime}/m_s: Normal conf = 1.13(4), m_c=6 → 1.49(4)

## Context
This work extends the author's previous studies (Hasegawa 2022) on how added monopoles and anti-monopoles create instantons and anti-instantons in the QCD vacuum, and how these affect observables such as the chiral condensate, light quark masses, and pion/kaon properties. The connected and disconnected correlations for the scalar density were previously estimated by Isgur (1989), and the instanton model predictions for these correlations were given by Schafer and Shuryak (1998). The method for estimating the scalar-prime meson mass follows the approach used for the eta-prime meson mass in lattice gauge theory by DeGrand (2004). The results enable quantitative study of how monopole and instanton densities separately affect scalar meson spectroscopy, particularly the disconnected contributions which are more sensitive to topological effects.
