# Geometry of Borromean Halo Nuclei

> We discuss the geometry of the highly quantal nuclear three-body systems composed of a core plus two loosely bound particles. These Borromean nuclei have no single bound two-body subsystem. Correlation plays a prominent role. From consideration of the $B(E1)$ value extracted from electromagnetic dissociation, in conjunction with HBT-type analysis of the two valence-halo particles correlation, we show that an estimate of the over-all geometry can be deduced. In particular we find that the opening angle between the two neutrons in $^{6}$He and $^{11}$Li are, respectively, $θ_{nn} = {83^{\circ}}^{+20}_{-10}$ and ${66^{\circ}}^{+22}_{-18}$. These angles are reduced by about 12% to $θ_{nn} = {78^{\circ}}^{+13}_{-18}$ and ${58^{\circ}}^{+10}_{-14}$ if the laser spectroscopy values of the rms charge radii are used to obtain the rms distance between the cores and the center of mass of the two neutrons. The opening angle in the case of $^{11}$Li is more than 20% larger than recently reported by Nakamura \cite{Nak06}. The analysis is extended to $^{14}$Be and the two-proton Borromean nucleus $^{17}% $Ne where complete data is still not available. Using available experimental data and recent theoretical calculations we find, $θ_{nn} = {64^{0}}^{+9}_{-10}$ and $θ_{pp} = 110^{0}$, respectively.

# Geometry of Borromean Halo Nuclei

## Key claim

By combining B(E1) values from electromagnetic dissociation with Hanbury-Brown-Twiss (HBT) two-neutron correlation measurements, the opening angle between the two valence nucleons in Borromean nuclei can be estimated with reduced model dependence compared to using B(E1) alone. However, the HBT analysis probes the continuum states rather than the ground state, so the extracted r_nn values likely overestimate the ground-state neutron-neutron distance, and the opening angles derived from them should be considered estimates rather than precise determinations.

## Method

The authors combine two independent experimental observables: the B(E1) value (which gives the rms distance r_c-2N between the core and the center of mass of the two valence nucleons) and the HBT two-particle correlation function (which gives the rms distance r_NN between the two valence nucleons). Using the geometric relation cos(θ_NN/2) = r_c-2N / sqrt(r_c-2N² + r_NN²/4), they extract the average opening angle θ_NN between the two halo nucleons. For ⁶He and ¹¹Li, they also use laser spectroscopy measurements of charge radii to obtain more accurate values of r_c-2N. The analysis is extended to ¹⁴Be and ¹⁷Ne using available experimental data and theoretical calculations.

## Key numbers

- Opening angle between the two neutrons in ⁶He: θ_nn = 83° (+20, -10) using B(E1)-derived r_c-2N; reduced to 78° (+13, -18) using laser spectroscopy charge radii
- Opening angle between the two neutrons in ¹¹Li: θ_nn = 66° (+22, -18) using B(E1)-derived r_c-2N; reduced to 58° (+10, -14) using laser spectroscopy charge radii
- Opening angle for ¹¹Li is more than 20% larger than the value of 48° (+14, -18) reported by Nakamura (2006)
- Opening angle between the two neutrons in ¹⁴Be: θ_nn = 64° (+9, -10)
- Opening angle between the two protons in ¹⁷Ne: θ_pp = 110°
- HBT-derived average distances between valence nucleons: r_nn = 6.6 fm (¹¹Li), 5.9 fm (⁶He), 5.6 fm (¹⁴Be); r_pp = 4.45 fm (¹⁷Ne, from theoretical calculation by Grigorenko et al., 2006)
- rms matter radii calculated from the model: 2.67 fm (⁶He), 3.17 fm (¹¹Li), 3.10 fm (¹⁴Be), 2.70 fm (¹⁷Ne)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Nakamura (2006), who extracted the opening angle in ¹¹Li using only B(E1) measurements with a dipole sum rule value, by incorporating HBT correlation data from Marques et al. (2000, 2001) to reduce model dependence. The results are consistent with the three-body pairing calculations of Hagino and Sagawa (2005). The opening angles for two-neutron Borromean nuclei show a decreasing trend with increasing mass (from ⁶He to ¹⁴Be), while the two-proton Borromean nucleus ¹⁷Ne exhibits a much larger opening angle (110°) due to Coulomb repulsion, which the authors note is remarkably close to the 104.45° bond angle in the water molecule. The analysis enables future combined B(E1) and HBT measurements for ¹⁷Ne, currently planned at GSI, and provides a framework for extracting three-body geometry with multiple experimental inputs.
