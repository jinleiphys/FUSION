# Nuclear mean field and double-folding model of the nucleus-nucleus optical potential

> Realistic density dependent CDM3Yn versions of the M3Y interaction have been used in an extended Hartree-Fock (HF) calculation of nuclear matter (NM), with the nucleon single-particle potential determined from the total NM energy based on the Hugenholtz-van Hove theorem that gives rise naturally to a rearrangement term (RT). Using the RT of the single-nucleon potential obtained exactly at different NM densities, the density- and energy dependence of the CDM3Yn interactions was modified to account properly for both the RT and observed energy dependence of the nucleon optical potential. Based on a local density approximation, the double-folding model of the nucleus-nucleus optical potential has been extended to take into account consistently the rearrangement effect and energy dependence of the nuclear mean-field potential, using the modified CDM3Yn interactions. The extended double-folding model was applied to study the elastic $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C and $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C scattering at the refractive energies, where the Airy structure of the nuclear rainbow has been well established. The RT was found to affect significantly the real nucleus-nucleus optical potential at small internuclear distances, giving the potential strength close to that implied by the realistic optical model description of the Airy oscillation.

## Key claim

The paper claims that including the rearrangement term (RT) of the single-nucleon potential, derived from the Hugenholtz-van Hove theorem, significantly modifies the real nucleus-nucleus optical potential at small internuclear distances, bringing its strength close to that required by optical model descriptions of Airy oscillations in refractive scattering. This effect had not been previously studied in double-folding model calculations.

## Method

The authors performed an extended Hartree-Fock calculation of symmetric nuclear matter using density-dependent CDM3Y3 and CDM3Y6 versions of the M3Y interaction, where the single-particle potential was determined from the total nuclear matter energy via the Hugenholtz-van Hove theorem to obtain the rearrangement term exactly at each nuclear matter density. They then modified the density- and energy dependence of the CDM3Yn interactions to account for both the rearrangement term and the observed energy dependence of the nucleon optical potential, introducing a momentum-dependent scaling factor g(k) adjusted to empirical nucleon optical potential data. Using a local density approximation, they extended the double-folding model of the nucleus-nucleus optical potential to consistently include the rearrangement effect and energy dependence of the nuclear mean-field potential, and applied this extended model to study elastic 12C+12C and 16O+12C scattering at refractive energies where nuclear rainbow patterns are established.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear matter saturation point: E/A ≈ -15.9 MeV at ρ₀ ≈ 0.17 fm⁻³
- Nuclear incompressibility K ≈ 218 MeV for CDM3Y3 interaction and K ≈ 252 MeV for CDM3Y6 interaction
- The momentum-dependent scaling factor g(k) was parameterized as a cubic polynomial: g(k) = 1.015 - 0.109k + 0.17k² - 0.07k³, where k is the nucleon momentum in fm⁻¹
- The rearrangement term correction ΔF₀(ρ) is negative over the whole range of nuclear matter densities, making the contribution of the rearrangement term always repulsive
- The energy window for observing nuclear rainbow patterns in 12C and 16O scattering is about 10 to 40 MeV/nucleon (around the Fermi energy)

## Context

This work extends the double-folding model approach developed by Khoa et al. (1997, 2000, 2007) for nucleus-nucleus optical potentials by incorporating the rearrangement term that arises naturally from the Hugenholtz-van Hove theorem, which was previously missing in standard Hartree-Fock calculations using density-dependent interactions. The method builds on a recent study by Loan et al. (2015) that accounted for the rearrangement term in nuclear matter, and applies it for the first time to finite nucleus-nucleus systems. This enables more accurate optical model analyses of refractive heavy-ion scattering, particularly for light systems like 12C+12C and 16O+12C where nuclear rainbow patterns allow determination of the real optical potential at small internuclear distances.
