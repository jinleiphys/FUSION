# Simulated Annealing Clusterization Algorithm for Studying the Multifragmentation

> We present the details of the numerical realization of the recently advanced algorithm developed to identify the fragmentation in heavy ion reactions. This new algorithm is based on the Simulated Annealing method and is dubbed as Simulated Annealing Clusterization Algorithm [SACA]. We discuss the different parameters used in the Simulated Annealing method and present an economical set of the parameters which is based on the extensive analysis carried out for the central and peripheral collisions of Au-Au, Nb-Nb and Pb-Pb. These parameters are crucial for the success of the algorithm. Our set of optimized parameters gives the same results as the most conservative choice, but is very fast. We also discuss the nucleon and fragment exchange processes which are very important for the energy minimization and finally present the analysis of the reaction dynamics using the new algorithm. This algorithm is can be applied whenever one wants to identify which of a given number of constituents form bound objects.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new algorithm (SACA) for identifying fragments in heavy-ion reaction simulations that can find the most bound configuration of nucleons even during the high-density phase, unlike previous spatial-correlation methods. The authors claim that their optimized parameter set gives the same results as the most conservative choice but is much faster, though they note the algorithm only guarantees reaching the global minimum in the limit of infinite steps.

## Method

The authors use the Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) model to generate phase-space coordinates of nucleons at multiple time steps during simulated heavy-ion collisions. They then apply a two-stage simulated annealing algorithm: first exchanging individual nucleons between fragments, then exchanging entire pre-fragments between super-fragments, with a binding energy threshold of -4.0 MeV per nucleon for fragments with mass >= 3. The algorithm minimizes the total binding energy of the configuration, accepting both energy-lowering and (with decreasing probability) energy-raising moves to escape local minima.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy threshold L_be = -4.0 MeV for fragments with N^f >= 3, and L_be = 0 otherwise
- Initial control parameter (temperature) theta_i = 4 MeV used in the conservative parameter set
- Markov chain length = 70 * eta, where eta is the number of entities at the start of minimization
- Temperature decrement factor sigma = 0.95, with theta_{i+1} = sigma * theta_i
- Algorithm terminates if no change in binding energy for 60 * eta iterations
- Gaussian width L fixed to 1.08 fm^3 in the QMD model
- QMD model applicable between 30 A*MeV to 1 A*GeV
- In central Au-Au collisions at 600 MeV/nucl., 99% of nucleons have experienced a collision by 40 fm/c
- The largest fragment detected by MST method at high density consisted of 394 nucleons
- Critical time for proper fragment identification with MST method is about 300 fm/c
- Variation of initial temperature between 1 MeV and 500 MeV showed no effect on fragment distribution at zero fm/c

## Context

This work extends earlier attempts at fragment identification in heavy-ion reactions, particularly the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) method of Aichelin (1991) and the binding-energy-filtered MST* approach. The authors note that previous work by Dorso (1993) attempted energy minimization for small systems but failed for heavy systems due to local minima problems. The SACA algorithm enables studying the time scale of fragment formation in heavy systems like Au-Au, Nb-Nb, and Pb-Pb, addressing whether fragments are created after thermalization or can be recognized early in the reaction, which has implications for testing momentum-dependent equations of state.
