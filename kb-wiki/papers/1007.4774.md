# Microscopic study of the $^{132,124}$Sn+$^{96}$Zr reactions: dynamic excitation energy, energy-dependent heavy-ion potential, and capture cross section

> We study reactions between neutron-rich $^{132}$Sn nucleus and $^{96}$Zr within a dynamic microscopic theory at energies in the vicinity of the ion-ion potential barrier peak, and we compare the properties to those of the stable system $^{124}$Sn+$^{96}$Zr. The calculations are carried out on a three-dimensional lattice using the density-constrained Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock method. In particular, we calculate the dynamic excitation energy $E^*(t)$ and the quadrupole moment of the dinuclear system, $Q_{20}(t)$, during the initial stages of the heavy-ion collision. Capture cross sections for the two reactions are analyzed in terms of dynamic effects and a comparison with recently measured data is given.

## Key claim

The density-constrained Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) method predicts that for the very heavy neutron-rich system \(^{132}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr, the heavy-ion potential barrier height and width increase dramatically with increasing center-of-mass energy, and only potentials calculated at energies \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} \geq 230\) MeV lead to capture (a true composite system with overlapping cores), while potentials at lower energies correspond to deep-inelastic collisions where the nuclei maintain separate cores.

## Method

The authors performed unrestricted TDHF calculations on a three-dimensional Cartesian lattice using the full Skyrme interaction (SLy4) including all time-odd terms, then applied the density-constrained (DC) method to extract ion-ion interaction potentials \(V(R)\) from the TDHF time-evolution. Dynamic excitation energies \(E^*(t)\) were computed by subtracting the collective energy (kinetic plus DC energy) from the conserved TDHF total energy. Capture cross sections were obtained by numerical integration of the Schrödinger equation with the Incoming Wave Boundary Condition method using the energy-dependent DC-TDHF potentials, and were compared to unrestricted TDHF runs at non-zero impact parameters using a sharp cut-off model.

## Key numbers

- Interaction barrier height for \(^{132}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr: \(V_I = 195\) MeV at distance \(R_I = 14.77\) fm (from unrestricted TDHF at zero impact parameter)
- DC-TDHF potential barrier height for \(^{132}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr: \(V_B = 211.4\) MeV at \(R_B = 13.03\) fm (at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 230\) MeV) and \(V_B = 215.0\) MeV at \(R_B = 12.56\) fm (at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 300\) MeV)
- Interaction barrier height for \(^{124}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr: \(V_I = 204\) MeV at \(R_I = 14.05\) fm
- DC-TDHF potential barrier height for \(^{124}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr: \(V_B = 210.6\) MeV at \(R_B = 13.06\) fm (at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 230\) MeV) and \(V_B = 213.7\) MeV at \(R_B = 12.59\) fm (at \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 300\) MeV)
- Q-values: \(Q_{gg} = -188.7\) MeV for \(^{132}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr and \(Q_{gg} = -188.4\) MeV for \(^{124}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr
- At \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 250\) MeV for \(^{132}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr: maximum impact parameter for capture \(b_{max} = 3.375 \pm 0.125\) fm, corresponding to \(L_{max} = 87\)
- At \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 230\) MeV for \(^{132}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr: \(b_{max} = 2.05 \pm 0.15\) fm, \(L_{max} = 51\)
- At \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 250\) MeV for \(^{124}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr: \(b_{max} = 3.0\) fm, \(L_{max} = 77\)

## Context

This work extends the DC-TDHF method, previously applied to systems such as \(^{132}\)Sn+\(^{64}\)Ni (Oberacker et al., 2006, 2007), \(^{64}\)Ni+\(^{64}\)Ni (Oberacker et al., 2008), and \(^{16}\)O+\(^{208}\)Pb (Oberacker et al., 2009), to the very heavy neutron-rich system \(^{132}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr and its stable counterpart \(^{124}\)Sn+\(^{96}\)Zr. The results demonstrate a strong energy dependence of the ion-ion potential for these massive systems, contrasting with the weak energy dependence seen in lighter systems. The calculated capture cross sections provide an upper limit for experimental capture-fission data from HRIBF (Vandebrouck et al., 2008), and the authors suggest that low-energy experimental data may include deep-inelastic events misidentified as capture-fission, motivating improved fission fragment detectors.
