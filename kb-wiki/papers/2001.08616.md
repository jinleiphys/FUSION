# Efficient method for estimation of fission fragment yields of r-process nuclei

> $\textbf{Background}$ More than half of all the elements heavier than iron are made by the rapid neutron capture process (or r process). For very neutron-rich astrophysical conditions, such at those found in the tidal ejecta of neutron stars, nuclear fission determines the r-process endpoint, and the fission fragment yields shape the final abundances of $110\le A \le 170$ nuclei. The knowledge of fission fragment yields of hundreds of nuclei inhabiting very neutron-rich regions of the nuclear landscape is thus crucial for the modeling of heavy-element nucleosynthesis. $\textbf{Purpose}$ In this study, we propose a model for the fast calculation of fission fragment yields based on the concept of shell-stabilized prefragments defined with help of the nucleonic localization functions. $\textbf{Methods}$ To generate realistic potential energy surfaces and nucleonic localizations, we apply Skyrme Density Functional Theory. The distribution of the neck nucleons among the two prefragments is obtained by means of a statistical model. $\textbf{Results}$ We benchmark the method by studying the fission yields of $^{178}$Pt, $^{240}$Pu, $^{254}$Cf, and $^{254,256,258}$Fm and show that it satisfactorily explains the experimental data. We then make predictions for $^{254}$Pu and $^{290}$Fm as two representative cases of fissioning nuclei that are expected to significantly contribute during the r-process nucleosynthesis occurring in neutron star mergers. $\textbf{Conclusions}$ The proposed framework provides an efficient alternative to microscopic approaches based on the evolution of the system in a space of collective coordinates all the way to scission. It can be used to carry out global calculations of fission fragment distributions across the r-process region.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that fission fragment yields can be predicted using only the prefragment configurations determined at the outer turning point of the fission path, combined with a statistical redistribution of neck nucleons, without needing to calculate the full potential energy surface all the way to scission. The method is benchmarked against experimental data for several nuclei and is claimed to be robust with respect to the choice of energy density functional.

## Method

The authors use Skyrme Density Functional Theory (with SkM* and UNEDF1$_\text{HFB}$ functionals) to generate potential energy surfaces and nucleonic localization functions (NLFs) for fissioning nuclei. Prefragments are identified from NLF patterns at a compact configuration on the most probable fission path, corresponding to the outer turning point. The neck nucleons between the two prefragments are then distributed among them using a statistical model based on a microcanonical probability distribution, with fragment binding energies calculated from the isospin-dependent liquid drop model.

## Key numbers

- For $^{178}$Pt, the configuration $\mathcal{C}$ was chosen at $Q_{20}=220$ b (UNEDF1$_\text{HFB}$) and 190 b (SkM*)
- The Gaussian smoothing function used to fold probabilities has width 3 for mass number $A$ and 2 for charge number $Z$
- The level density parameter is taken as $a_i = A_i/10$ MeV$^{-1}$
- For $^{290}$Fm, the method predicts symmetric fission centered around $^{145}$Sn
- The two-particle uncertainty in prefragment particle numbers is taken as $\pm$1 around the average integer value

## Context

This work extends the approach of Sadhukhan (2016, 2017) and Matheson (2019), who used microscopic Langevin calculations to show that prefragments form early along the fission path and that neck nucleons redistribute rapidly at scission. The method provides a computationally inexpensive alternative to full Langevin calculations (Sadhukhan 2016, Sierk 2017, Usang 2019) and time-dependent DFT approaches (Simenel 2018, Bulgac 2018, 2019, Regnier 2019, Zhao 2019). It enables global calculations of fission fragment distributions across the r-process region, which could be combined with systematic fission rate calculations (Goriely 2009, Erler 2012, Giuliani 2018, 2019) to study the impact of fission on r-process nucleosynthesis.
