# Strangeness in Neutron Star Cooling

> We study the thermal evolution of neutron stars in the presence of hyperons or kaons in the core. Our results indicate that the nucleon and hyperon direct Urca processes play crucial roles for the cooling of neutron stars. The presence of hyperons drives fast cooling mechanisms in two ways: 1) it allows the hyperon direct Urca prior to the nucleon direct Urca, 2) and it makes the nucleon direct Urca more feasible by reducing the neutron Fermi momentum. We found that the neutron star equation of state (EOS) with hyperons can be consistent with both mass and temperature observations. We also found that the neutron star EOS with kaon condensation can be consistent with observations, even though the cooling behavior is seldom useful to identify or isolate the effect of kaon condensation.

# Strangeness in Neutron Star Cooling

## Key claim

The authors show that neutron star equations of state (EOS) including hyperons can be consistent with both mass and temperature observations, but only for neutron stars with masses below approximately 1.6 solar masses; above this threshold, hyperon-driven cooling makes stars too cold to match observed data. For kaon condensation, the cooling behavior is seldom useful to identify or isolate the effect, except in very massive stars (above about 1.96 solar masses) for EOS models that do not already allow the nucleon direct Urca process.

## Method

The authors combine non-relativistic Skyrme nucleon-nucleon interactions (SLy4, SkI4, SGI) with non-relativistic hyperon potential models for N-Lambda and Lambda-Lambda interactions, and with an SU(3) non-linear chiral model for kaon interactions. They compute thermal evolution curves including direct Urca processes for both nucleons and hyperons, plus neutrino emission from condensed kaons, while neglecting nucleon and Lambda superfluidity effects. Cooling curves are compared against temperature estimates from 19 observed neutron stars across a range of ages.

## Key numbers

- Maximum masses without strangeness: SLy4 = 2.07 solar masses (no nucleon direct Urca), SkI4 = 2.19 solar masses (nucleon direct Urca threshold at 1.63 solar masses), SGI = 2.25 solar masses (nucleon direct Urca threshold at 1.72 solar masses)
- With hyperons, neutron star mass ranges: SLy4 = 1.15 to 1.85 solar masses, SkI4 = 1.47 to 1.97 solar masses, SGI = 1.44 to 2.04 solar masses
- Threshold masses for hyperon direct Urca (nucleon direct Urca) with hyperons present: SLy4 = 1.16 (1.85) solar masses, SkI4 = 1.49 (1.63) solar masses, SGI = 1.45 (1.64) solar masses
- Hyperon direct Urca emissivity: Q_Lambda = 4.0 x 10^27 erg cm^-3 s^-1 times (m_Lambda* m_p* / m_Lambda m_p) (n_e / n_0)^(1/3) R T_9^6, where R = 0.0394 and T_9 = T / 10^9 K
- For SLy4+HP-Lambda2+S-Lambda-Lambda3, critical density for hyperon appearance is n = 0.453 fm^-3 and threshold for Lambda direct Urca is n = 0.454 fm^-3
- Kaon condensation mass ranges for a_3 m_s = -134 MeV: SLy4 = 1.94 to 1.99 solar masses, SkI4 = 2.00 to 2.06 solar masses, SGI = 2.12 to 2.19 solar masses
- Kaon condensation reduces maximum masses by 3 to 15 percent depending on model; a_3 m_s = -222 MeV is inconsistent with 2 solar mass observations
- Kaon neutrino emissivity: Q_K = 2.5 x 10^26 erg cm^-3 s^-1 times (m_n*^2 / m_n^2) (n_e / n_0)^(1/3) T_9^6 theta_K^2 tan^2 theta_C

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of neutron star cooling without strangeness (Lim et al., 2015) by incorporating hyperons and kaons as additional degrees of freedom in the core. It builds on hyperon potential models from Gul (2012) (HP-Lambda2), Yamamoto, Bando, and Zong (1988) (YBZ6), and Lambda-Lambda interactions from Lanskoy (1998) (S-Lambda-Lambda3) and Minato (2011) (S-Lambda-Lambda3-prime), as well as the kaon condensation framework of Kaplan and Nelson (1986). The results enable future work to distinguish between hyperonic and kaonic EOS models by comparing cooling predictions against a wider range of neutron star temperature observations, particularly for stars in the 1.5 to 1.6 solar mass range where hyperon effects are most distinguishable.
