# Strangeness in Compact Stars

> We discuss the impact of strange hadrons, in particular hyperons, on the gross features of compact stars and on core-collapse supernovae. Hyperons are likely to be the first exotic species which appears around twice normal nuclear matter density in the core of neutron stars. Their presence largely influences the mass-radius relation of compact stars, the maximum mass, the cooling of neutron stars, the stability with regard to the emission of gravitational waves from rotation-powered neutron stars and the possible early onset of the QCD phase transition in core-collapse supernovae. We outline also the constraints from subthreshold kaon production in heavy-ion collisions for the maximum possible mass of neutron stars.

## Key claim

Hyperons are likely the first exotic species to appear in neutron star cores at around twice normal nuclear matter density, but their presence and the resulting maximum neutron star mass depend critically on the sign and strength of hyperon-nucleon and hyperon-hyperon interactions, particularly the poorly-constrained three-body forces involving hyperons.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical models of neutron star composition and structure, including relativistic mean-field models, Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations, and chiral effective Lagrangians, all constrained by hypernuclear data from experiments. It also analyzes subthreshold kaon production data from the KaoS collaboration in heavy-ion collisions (Au+Au and C+C at 0.8 and 1.0 AGeV) to constrain the nuclear equation of state, and discusses cooling simulations and r-mode instability calculations that incorporate weak nonmesonic reactions involving hyperons.

## Key numbers

- Hyperons appear at around \(n \approx 2n_0\) (twice normal nuclear matter density, where \(n_0 = 3 \times 10^{14}\) g/cm\(^3\)) in models adjusted to hypernuclear data
- For a free gas of hadrons, the \(\Sigma^-\) appears at \(4n_0\) and the \(\Lambda\) at \(8n_0\)
- The Hulse-Taylor pulsar has mass \(M = (1.4414 \pm 0.0002) M_\odot\)
- PSR J1903+0327 has mass \(M = (1.67 \pm 0.01) M_\odot\) (not yet finalized)
- The spin rate of PSR B1937+21 (641 Hz) gives a radius constraint of \(R < 15.5\) km for \(M = 1.4 M_\odot\)
- Maximum mass from relativistic mean-field models with hyperons is about \(M \approx 1.7 M_\odot\); without hyperon interactions it drops to \(M < 1.4 M_\odot\)
- Modern many-body approaches give maximum masses: \(M_{\rm max} = 1.47 M_\odot\) (Vidana et al.), \(M_{\rm max} = 1.34 M_\odot\) with hyperon-hyperon potentials, \(M_{\rm max} = 1.26 M_\odot\) (Baldo et al. with three-body nucleon interactions)
- The causality limit from heavy-ion data gives a new upper mass limit of about \(2.7 M_\odot\) using a fiducial density of \(\epsilon_f \approx 2\epsilon_0\)
- For supernova matter at \(T = 20\) MeV and proton fraction \(Y_p = 0.4\), hyperon population reaches about 0.1%

## Context

This review extends the discussion of hyperons in neutron stars from earlier work by Glendenning (1991) and Schaffner-Bielich (2008), and addresses the tension between modern microscopic approaches (Vidana 2000, Baldo 1999, Schulze 2006, Djapo 2008) that predict maximum masses below \(1.4 M_\odot\) and the observed pulsar masses. The paper connects hypernuclear physics to constraints from heavy-ion experiments (KaoS collaboration, Sturm 2000, Forster 2007) and outlines how hyperons catalyze the QCD phase transition in supernovae (Mintz 2009, Sagert 2009), which could produce a detectable second peak in antineutrino emission from a galactic supernova.
