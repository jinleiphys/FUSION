# Color transparency and suppression of high-pT hadrons in nuclear collisions

> The production length l_p of a leading (large z_h) hadron produced in hadronization of a highly virtual high-p_T parton is short because of the very intensive vacuum gluon radiation and dissipation of energy at the early stage of process. Therefore, the main part of nuclear suppression of high-p_T hadrons produced in heavy ion collisions is related to the survival probability of a colorless dipole propagating through a dense medium. This is subject to color transparency, which leads to a steep rise with p_T of the nuclear ratio R_{AA}(p_T), in good agreement with the recent data from the ALICE experiment at LHC, CERN. No adjustment, except the medium density is made, and the transport coefficient is found to be \hat q_0=0.8 GeV^2/fm. This is close to the value extracted from the analysis of RHIC data for J/Psi suppression, but is an order of magnitude smaller than the value found from jet quenching data within the energy loss scenario. Although the present calculations have a status of a postdiction, the mechanism and all formulas have been published, and are applied here with no modification, except the kinematics. At the same time, p_T-dependence of R_{AA} at the energy of RHIC is rather flat due to the suppression factor steeply falling with rising x_T, related to the energy conservation constraints. This factor is irrelevant to the LHC data, since x_T is much smaller.

## Key claim

The observed steep rise with $p_T$ of the nuclear suppression factor $R_{AA}(p_T)$ in LHC lead-lead collisions can be explained by color transparency of small colorless dipoles propagating through a moderately opaque medium, provided the production length of the pre-hadron is short (less than about 1 fm). The transport coefficient extracted from this mechanism is $\hat q_0 = 0.8$ GeV²/fm, an order of magnitude smaller than values obtained from energy-loss-based analyses of jet quenching data.

## Method

The authors calculate the nuclear suppression factor $R_{AA}(p_T)$ for central lead-lead collisions by modeling the survival probability of a colorless dipole (pre-hadron) that expands according to $r^2(t) = 4t/p_T$ as it propagates through the medium. The dipole attenuation rate is controlled by the transport coefficient $\hat q(l)$, which scales with the participant density and dilutes as $1/l$ due to longitudinal expansion. The production length $l_p$ of the pre-hadron is taken from a previously published model (Kopeliovich et al., 2004) that accounts for vacuum gluon radiation and Sudakov suppression, and is short ($\langle l_p \rangle \lesssim 1$ fm) for gluon jets at LHC energies. The transport coefficient $\hat q_0$ at time $t_0 = 1$ fm is the only adjusted parameter.

## Key numbers

- Transport coefficient extracted from the fit to ALICE data: $\hat q_0 = 0.8$ GeV²/fm, at time $t_0 = 1$ fm after collision.
- For comparison, the transport coefficient from $J/\Psi$ suppression at RHIC is $\hat q_0 \sim 0.2$ GeV²/fm, while energy-loss analyses of RHIC jet quenching give values an order of magnitude larger.
- The mean production length for gluon jets at high energies is $\langle l_p \rangle < 1$ fm.
- The calculation agrees with ALICE data for $p_T > 7$ GeV at $\sqrt{s} = 2.76$ TeV for central (0-5%) lead-lead collisions.
- The time scale of medium creation is taken as $t_0 = l_0 \sim 1$ fm.

## Context

This work extends the same dipole-attenuation formalism previously applied to $J/\Psi$ suppression in heavy ion collisions (Kopeliovich et al., 2008) to high-$p_T$ hadron production. It contradicts the standard energy-loss scenario (Baier et al., 2001; Gyulassy et al., 2003) which assumes long hadronization times and yields transport coefficients an order of magnitude larger. The short production length model was previously validated against HERMES data on semi-inclusive DIS on nuclei (Kopeliovich et al., 2004). The results enable a unified description of both heavy quarkonium and light hadron suppression in heavy ion collisions, and suggest that the medium created at LHC is moderately opaque rather than extremely dense.
