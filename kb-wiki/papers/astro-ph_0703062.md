# Phase separation in the crust of accreting neutron stars

> Nucleosynthesis, on the surface of accreting neutron stars, produces a range of chemical elements. We perform molecular dynamics simulations of crystallization to see how this complex composition forms new neutron star crust. We find chemical separation, with the liquid ocean phase greatly enriched in low atomic number elements compared to the solid crust. This phase separation should change many crust properties such as the thermal conductivity and shear modulus. The concentration of carbon, if present, is enriched in the ocean. This may allow unstable thermonuclear burning of the carbon and help explain the ignition of the very energetic explosions known as superbursts.

# Phase separation in the crust of accreting neutron stars

## Key claim
Molecular dynamics simulations show that when the complex mixture of elements produced by nucleosynthesis on accreting neutron stars crystallizes, the liquid ocean becomes strongly enriched in low-atomic-number elements while the solid crust is enriched in high-Z elements. The authors caution that the system may not have reached full thermodynamic equilibrium by the end of the simulation, but the observed composition differences are large and increasing with time, so the qualitative conclusion of chemical separation is robust.

## Method
The authors performed classical molecular dynamics simulations of a 27,648-ion system with a composition based on rp-process ash abundances from Schatz et al. and Gupta et al., using screened Coulomb (Yukawa) interactions between ions. The simulation volume was initialized with a solid phase in the upper half and a liquid phase in the lower half, both with identical initial composition, and evolved for 151 million fm/c (over six million time steps) at constant energy after an initial temperature equilibration period. Composition was measured in regions away from the solid-liquid interfaces.

## Key numbers
- The average ion charge in the liquid phase is ⟨Z⟩ = 28.04, compared to ⟨Z⟩ = 30.48 in the solid phase, versus ⟨Z⟩ = 29.30 in the initial mixture.
- The impurity parameter Q = (ΔZ)² is reduced from 38.9 in the original mixture to 22.3 in the solid phase, while increasing to 52.7 in the liquid phase.
- The Coulomb coupling parameter Γ for the mixture is 247 overall, with Γ = 233 for the liquid phase and Γ = 261 for the solid phase, compared to Γ ≈ 175 for a pure one-component plasma.
- The simulation was run at an ion density n = 7.18 × 10⁻⁵ fm⁻³, corresponding to a mass density of 1.04 × 10¹³ g/cm³, with temperature T = 0.3436 MeV.
- For a pure test system, the melting point was found at Γ = 176.1 ± 0.7, consistent with the known OCP value of Γ = 175.
- The latent heat per ion for the pure system was 0.758 ± 0.002 T_M, where T_M is the melting temperature.
- Oxygen (Z=8) abundance in the liquid is 0.0529 versus 0.0087 in the solid; the dominant species Z=34 has abundance 0.3408 in the liquid versus 0.4297 in the solid.
- The crystallization density for typical crust temperatures is estimated as ρ = 2.1 × 10¹⁰ g cm⁻³ (T/5×10⁸ K)³ (Γ/233)³.

## Context
This work extends the concept of phase separation during crystallization, previously studied for white dwarf stars, to the context of accreting neutron stars where rp-process nucleosynthesis produces a complex multi-element composition. The authors note that Jones (1988) considered phase separation in non-accreting neutron stars but based on early two-component Coulomb plasma work, and that Jones later suggested the system would form an amorphous solid. The results directly demonstrate that crystallization of the complex mixture produces chemical separation, which should affect crust properties including thermal conductivity (previously studied by Itoh and Kohyama) and shear modulus, and may help explain the ignition conditions for superbursts (Cumming and Bildsten). The work enables future self-consistent calculations of composition evolution including both electron capture and phase separation, and suggests that the reduced impurity parameter Q in the solid could significantly change thermal conductivity calculations.
