# Description of $^{93}$Nb stellar electron-capture rates by the Projected Shell Model

> Capture of electrons by nuclei is an important process in stellar environments where excited nuclear states are thermally populated. However, accurate treatment for excited configurations in electron capture (EC) rates has been an unsolved problem for medium-heavy and heavy nuclei. In this work, we take the $^{93}$Nb $\rightarrow$ $^{93}$Zr EC rates as the example to introduce the Projected-Shell-Model (PSM) in which excited configurations are explicitly included as multi-quasiparticle states. Applying the prevalent assumption that the parent nucleus always stays in its ground state in stellar conditions, we critically compare the obtained PSM results with the recently-measured Gamow-Teller transition data, and with the previous calculations by the conventional shell model and the quasiparticle random-phase approximation. We discuss important ingredients that are required in theoretical models used for stellar EC calculations, and demonstrate effects of the explicit inclusion of excited nuclear states in EC rate calculations, especially when both electron density and environment temperature are high.

## Key claim

The Projected Shell Model (PSM), which explicitly includes excited nuclear configurations as multi-quasiparticle states, can describe stellar electron-capture rates for medium-heavy odd-mass nuclei like $^{93}$Nb, but the accuracy of the GT strength distribution depends on correctly reproducing the level structure of the daughter nucleus $^{93}$Zr, which the PSM describes only at a qualitative level.

## Method

The authors calculated Gamow-Teller transition strengths and stellar electron-capture rates for $^{93}$Nb $\rightarrow$ $^{93}$Zr using the Projected Shell Model with a large model space of four major harmonic-oscillator shells and a configuration space including up to 7-quasiparticle states. They compared their results with recent charge-exchange reaction data from MSU and with calculations from the large-scale shell model (LSSM) and quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA), considering both the zero-temperature approximation (parent nucleus always in ground state) and the full thermal population of excited parent states at stellar temperatures from 1 to 15 GK and densities $\rho Y_e = 10^7, 10^9$, and $10^{11}$ g/cm$^3$.

## Key numbers

- Quenching factor $f_{\text{quench}} = 0.9$ adopted for the effective axial-vector coupling constant ratio $(g_A/g_V)_{\text{eff}}$
- Constant $K = 6146 \pm 6$ s used for the electron-capture rate formula
- Quadrupole deformation $\varepsilon_2 = 0.1$ adopted for both $^{93}$Nb and $^{93}$Zr
- The LSSM calculation required a phenomenological hindrance factor $h = 5.43$ to renormalize GT strengths due to truncations
- The QRPA used a quenching factor $f_{\text{quench}} \approx 0.8$
- The PSM calculation included about 1000 daughter states for each of the $\{7/2^+, 9/2^+, 11/2^+\}$ states in $^{93}$Zr, almost double the number in the LSSM calculation
- The first excited $1/2^-$ state in $^{93}$Nb lies at 30.77 keV experimentally and can be populated at stellar temperature $T \sim 1$ GK (corresponding to about 90 keV)
- An energy gap in $^{93}$Nb above the $1/2^-$ state leads to sudden enhancement of excited-state contributions at $T \approx 6$ GK (corresponding roughly to 550 keV)

## Context

This work extends the Projected Shell Model approach to stellar electron-capture rates for medium-heavy odd-mass nuclei, taking $^{93}$Nb as the first example. It builds on the pioneering framework of Fuller, Fowler, and Newman (FFN) for stellar EC rates and addresses limitations of the large-scale shell model (which becomes physically inappropriate at high temperatures due to missing cross-shell configurations) and the QRPA (which produces resonance-like GT peaks with insufficient fragmentation). The PSM results are compared with recent experimental data from charge-exchange reactions at MSU (Gao et al., 2021) and with LSSM and QRPA calculations from the same experimental paper. The work enables more reliable stellar EC rate calculations for medium-heavy and heavy nuclei needed for core-collapse supernova simulations and neutron star crust cooling, particularly at high temperatures and densities where excited-state contributions become important.
