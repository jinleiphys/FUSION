# A NICER View of PSR J1231$-$1411: A Complex Case

> Recent constraints on neutron star mass and radius have advanced our understanding of the equation of state (EOS) of cold dense matter. Some of them have been obtained by modeling the pulses of three millisecond X-ray pulsars observed by the Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER). Here, we present a Bayesian parameter inference for a fourth pulsar, PSR J1231$-$1411, using the same technique with NICER and XMM-Newton data. When applying a broad mass-inclination prior from radio timing measurements and the emission region geometry model that can best explain the data, we find likely converged results only when using a limited radius prior. If limiting the radius to be consistent with the previous observational constraints and EOS analyses, we infer the radius to be $12.6 \pm 0.3$ km and the mass to be $1.04_{-0.03}^{+0.05}$ $M_\odot$, each reported as the posterior credible interval bounded by the $16\,\%$ and $84\,\%$ quantiles. If using an uninformative prior but limited between $10$ and $14$ km, we find otherwise similar results, but $R_{\mathrm{eq}} = 13.5_{-0.5}^{+0.3}$ km for the radius. In both cases, we find a nonantipodal hot region geometry where one emitting spot is at the equator or slightly above, surrounded by a large colder region, and where a noncircular hot region lies close to southern rotational pole. If using a wider radius prior, we only find solutions that fit the data significantly worse. We discuss the challenges in finding the better fitting solutions, possibly related to the weak interpulse feature in the pulse profile.

## Key claim

The paper reports that for PSR J1231$-$1411, Bayesian parameter inference using NICER and XMM-Newton data yields converged mass and radius results only when a limited radius prior is applied; with a wider radius prior, only significantly worse-fitting solutions are found, and the inferred geometry requires a nonantipodal hot region configuration with one spot near the equator and another near the southern rotational pole.

## Method

The authors performed Bayesian parameter inference using the X-PSI code to model X-ray pulse profiles from NICER and XMM-Newton observations of PSR J1231$-$1411, a millisecond pulsar spinning at 271 Hz. They applied two hot region geometry models (Single Temperature-Unshared and Protruding Dual Temperature-Unshared) with fully ionized hydrogen atmosphere models, used a joint NICER and XMM-Newton likelihood, and incorporated a mass-inclination prior from radio timing Shapiro delay measurements. Four different radius priors were tested: the standard X-PSI prior, a prior with lower limit 8 km, a prior restricted to 10-14 km, and a prior (R21) consistent with previous observational and equation of state constraints from Raaijmakers (2021).

## Key numbers

* Radius inferred with the R21 prior: 12.6 +/- 0.3 km (68% credible interval), with mass 1.04_{-0.03}^{+0.05} solar masses
* Radius inferred with the 10-14 km uniform prior: 13.5_{-0.5}^{+0.3} km (68% credible interval), with mass 1.06_{-0.04}^{+0.06} solar masses
* Spin period of PSR J1231$-$1411: 3.6839 ms (fixed in the analysis)
* NICER exposure after filtering: 2,651,393 seconds
* XMM-Newton exposures: 28.8 ks (EPIC-MOS) and 17.7 ks (EPIC-pn)
* Distance inferred with R21 prior: 0.65_{-0.05}^{+0.03} kpc (68% credible interval)
* Interstellar hydrogen column density inferred with R21 prior: 1.62_{-0.48}^{+0.47} x 10^20 cm^{-2} (68% credible interval)

## Context

This work extends the NICER pulse profile modeling program previously applied to three other millisecond pulsars: PSR J0030+0451 (Riley 2019, Miller 2019), PSR J0740+6620 (Miller 2021, Riley 2021), and PSR J0437-4715 (Choudhury 2024). The results for PSR J1231$-$1411 are more challenging to obtain than for those earlier targets, with convergence only achieved under restricted radius priors, and the inferred low mass (approximately 1.04 solar masses) and complex nonantipodal hot region geometry differ from the simpler configurations seen in some previous NICER analyses. The findings enable future comparisons with dense matter equation of state models across a broader range of neutron star masses, though the authors note that the weak interpulse feature in the pulse profile may contribute to the fitting difficulties.
