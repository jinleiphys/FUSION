# Thermonuclear $^{19}$F($p$,$α_0$)$^{16}$O reaction rate

> The thermonuclear $^{19}$F($p$,$α_0$)$^{16}$O reaction rate in a temperature region of 0.007--10 GK has been derived by re-evaluating the available experimental data, together with the low-energy theoretical $R$-matrix extrapolations. Our new rate deviates up to about 30\% compared to the previous ones, although all rates are consistent within the uncertainties. At very low temperature (e.g. 0.01 GK) our reaction rate is about 20\% smaller than the most recently published rate, because of a difference in the low energy extrapolated $S$-factor and a more accurate estimate of the reduced mass entering in the calculation of the reaction rate. At temperatures above $\sim$1 GK, our rate is smaller, for instance, by about 20\% around 1.75 GK, because we have re-evaluated in a meticulous way the previous data (Isoya et al., Nucl. Phys. 7, 116 (1958)). The present interpretation is supported by the direct experimental data. The uncertainties of the present evaluated rate are estimated to be about 20\% in the temperature region below 0.2 GK, which are mainly caused by the lack of low-energy experimental data and the large uncertainties of the existing data. The asymptotic giant branch (AGB) star evolves at temperatures below 0.2 GK, where the $^{19}$F($p$,$α$)$^{16}$O reaction may play a very important role. However, the current accuracy of the reaction rate is insufficient to help to describe, in a careful way, for the fluorine overabundances phenomenon observed in AGB stars. Precise cross section (or $S$ factor) data in the low energy region are therefore mandatory for astrophysical nucleosynthesis studies.

## Key claim

The newly evaluated thermonuclear \(^{19}\text{F}(p,\alpha_0)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction rate deviates up to about 30% from previous rates, but all rates are consistent within uncertainties; at very low temperature (0.01 GK) the new rate is about 20% smaller than the most recently published rate due to a difference in the low-energy extrapolated \(S\)-factor and a more accurate estimate of the reduced mass, while at temperatures above \(\sim\)1 GK the rate is smaller (e.g., about 20% around 1.75 GK) because of a meticulous re-evaluation of the data of Isoya et al. (1958).

## Method

The authors re-evaluated all available experimental cross-section data for the \(^{19}\text{F}(p,\alpha_0)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction in the center-of-mass energy region up to 10 MeV, digitizing published figures with the GetData Graph Digitizer program and consulting the EXFOR library. They corrected the integrated cross sections from Isoya et al. (1958) by properly accounting for the energy dependence of the \(\lambdabar^2\) factor in the Legendre polynomial expansion, and renormalized the data of Clarke et al. (1957) and Morinaga et al. (1966) to match the corrected Isoya data. For the low-energy region below 0.2 MeV, they adopted the theoretical \(R\)-matrix results of Lombardo et al. (2015) with a scaling factor of 1.16 on the NACRE non-resonant contribution, and calculated the reaction rate by numerical integration of the evaluated \(S\)-factors over 0.007–10 GK.

## Key numbers

- At 0.01 GK, the new reaction rate is about 20% smaller than the most recently published rate.
- At around 1.75 GK, the new rate is about 20% smaller than previous rates.
- The peak cross section of the 1.3 MeV resonance is evaluated to be \((26.0 \pm 2.6)\) mb, compared to the 42 mb value adopted by NACRE.
- The evaluated \(S\)-factor at the 1.290 MeV resonance (Isoya et al.) is 72.1 MeV\(\cdot\)b, and at 1.292 MeV (Clarke et al.) is 72.4 MeV\(\cdot\)b.
- The uncertainties of the present evaluated rate are estimated to be about 20% in the temperature region below 0.2 GK.
- The fitting error of the parameterized rate (Eq. 11) is less than 1.5% over the entire temperature region of 0.007–10 GK.

## Context

This work extends the NACRE compilation (Angulo et al., 1999) by re-evaluating the same experimental datasets with corrected normalization and energy dependence, and by incorporating newer direct measurements from Lombardo et al. (2013, 2015) and indirect Trojan Horse method results from La Cognata et al. (2011, 2015) and Indelicato et al. (2017). The new rate is intended for use in asymptotic giant branch (AGB) star models, where the \(^{19}\text{F}(p,\alpha)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction is a key destruction channel for fluorine; however, the current accuracy (about 20% uncertainty below 0.2 GK) is still insufficient to describe the fluorine overabundance phenomenon observed in AGB stars, motivating future precise low-energy cross-section measurements.
