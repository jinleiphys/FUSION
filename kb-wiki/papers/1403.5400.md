# Decomposition of scattering phase shifts and reaction cross sections using the complex scaling method

> We apply the complex scaling method to the calculation of scattering phase shifts and extract the contributions of resonances in a phase shift and a cross section. The decomposition of the phase shift is shown to be useful to understand the roles of resonant and non-resonant continuum states. As examples, we apply this method to several two-body systems: (i) a schematic model with the Gyarmati potential which produces many resonances, (ii) the alpha-alpha system which has a Coulomb barrier potential in addition to an attractive nuclear interaction, and (iii) the alpha-n system which has no barrier potential. Using different kinds of potentials, we discuss the reliability of this method to investigate the resonance structure in the phase shifts and cross sections.

## Key claim

The complex scaling method (CSM) can decompose scattering phase shifts into separate contributions from resonant poles and non-resonant continuum states, but the decomposition depends on the scaling angle \(\theta\) because the number of identified resonances \(N_r^\theta\) changes with \(\theta\); only the total phase shift (sum of resonance and continuum terms plus \(N_b\pi\)) is \(\theta\)-independent.

## Method

The authors solve the complex-scaled Schrödinger equation for two-body systems using \(L^2\) Gaussian basis functions, obtaining discretized complex eigenvalues for bound states, resonant states, and continuum states. They then compute the continuum level density (CLD) from these eigenvalues via the extended completeness relation, and integrate the CLD to obtain the scattering phase shift as a sum of arctangent terms from each eigenvalue. The phase shift is decomposed into a resonance part \(\delta_R^\theta\) (sum over identified resonant poles) and a continuum part \(\delta_C^\theta\) (sum over discretized continuum states minus free-particle continuum states).

## Context

This work extends the use of the complex scaling method from bound and resonant state calculations (Ho 1997, Myo 1998, Aoyama 2006) to a systematic decomposition of scattering phase shifts and cross sections into resonance and background contributions. The approach builds on the continuum level density formalism of Suzuki (2005) and provides a framework analogous to Fano (1961) resonance–background interference analysis, but using complex-scaled eigenvalues directly. The method is demonstrated on the Gyarmati schematic potential, the \(\alpha-\alpha\) system (compared with experimental data from He 2010 and Tilley 2004), and the \(\alpha-n\) system (compared with experimental data from Hoop 1966 and Auslander 1962), enabling future studies of resonance structures in unstable nuclei where most excited states are resonances above decay thresholds.
