# Quantum simulating multi-particle processes in high energy nuclear physics: dijet production and color (de)coherence

> Hard scattering events in high-energy collisions produce highly virtual partons that subsequently fragment into collimated hadronic cascades. When such partonic showers evolve in a QCD medium, as in deep-inelastic scattering or heavy-ion collisions, the resulting multi-particle distributions encode information about the surrounding matter. Decades of theoretical developments have led to a consistent and order-by-order improvable perturbative description of the shower. This description needs, however, the non-perturbative input that encodes the structure of the hadronic matter. The determination of such input remains challenging within conventional computational approaches, thereby limiting the applicability of the approach. In this work, we develop a framework that employs quantum simulation techniques to compute multi-particle processes in such environments by mapping partonic cross-sections to quantum circuits. As benchmarks, we analyze dipole formation and the QCD antenna radiation pattern at leading order in the strong coupling constant, comparing the results with analytic estimates in simplified limits. The quantum circuit formulation here introduced naturally extends to higher perturbative orders and enables amplitude-level computations in complex matter backgrounds. This provides a systematic foundation for applying quantum information science methods to study multi-particle dynamics in QCD media.

## Key claim

The paper develops a framework that maps non-perturbative multi-parton correlation functions in QCD media to quantum circuits, enabling amplitude-level computation of multi-particle processes; the approach is benchmarked only at leading order in the strong coupling constant and in simplified limits, with the claim that it naturally extends to higher perturbative orders.

## Method

The authors map the cross-section for dijet production (dipole formation from a virtual photon) and QCD antenna color (de)coherence onto quantum circuits by expressing the in-medium evolution operators for quark-antiquark pairs as unitary operations on color and momentum states. The non-perturbative medium correlators C4 and C2 are rewritten as matrix elements of evolution operators between initial and final momentum states, which are then evaluated by preparing color-singlet initial states, evolving them unitarily under a classical background field with Gaussian statistics, and performing projective measurements. The framework treats the background field as a stochastic classical Yang-Mills field with local correlations in light-cone time and color, and the authors compare their quantum simulation results with analytic estimates in the eikonal limit.

## Key numbers

- The vacuum cross-section for dipole formation is given by (2π) dσ^vac / (dz d^2p) = N_c (α_e/π) P_qγ(z) / p^2 times an integral over the initial photon amplitude, where P_qγ(z) = z^2 + (1-z)^2 is the Altarelli-Parisi splitting function.
- The medium modification factor F_med(p^2,z) involves integrals over light-cone times x^+ in (0,L) and over transverse momenta, with the medium length L typically of order a few fm.
- The field correlator is ⟨A_a^-(x) A_b^-(y)⟩ = δ_ab n(x^+,x) δ(x^+-y^+) γ(x-y), where γ(x-y) is directly related to the in-medium elastic scattering rate and n(x^+,x) = n(x^+) Θ(x^+<L) for a homogeneous medium.

## Context

This work extends the light-front Hamiltonian approach to jet evolution in QCD media developed by Li et al. (2020-2025) and the quantum simulation framework of Qian et al. (2024), providing a systematic mapping of partonic cross-sections to quantum circuits that avoids perturbative expansions in the medium interaction. It contrasts with traditional methods that rely on simplifying assumptions for non-perturbative correlators, such as those by Apolinario et al. (2014), Feal et al. (2018), and Isaksen et al. (2023), and enables future application of quantum information science to compute multi-particle dynamics in complex matter backgrounds, including at higher perturbative orders and for the Electron Ion Collider program.
