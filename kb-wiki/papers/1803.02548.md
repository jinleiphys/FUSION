# Dynamics of first-order quantum phase transitions in extended Bose-Hubbard model: From density wave to superfluid and vice-versa

> In this paper, we study the nonequilibrium dynamics of the Bose-Hubbard model with the nearest-neighbor repulsion by using time-dependent Gutzwiller (GW) methods. In particular, we vary the hopping parameters in the Hamiltonian as a function of time, and investigate the dynamics of the system from the density wave (DW) to the superfluid (SF) crossing a first-order phase transition and vice-versa. From the DW to SF, we find scaling laws for the correlation length and vortex density with respect to the quench time. This is a reminiscence of the Kibble-Zurek scaling for continuous phase transitions and contradicts the common expectation. We give a possible explanation for this observation. On the other hand from the SF to DW, the system evolution depends on the initial SF state. When the initial state is the ground-state obtained by the static GW methods, a coexisting state of the SF and DW domains forms after passing through the critical point. Coherence of the SF order parameter is lost as the system evolves. This is a phenomenon similar to the glass transition in classical systems. When the state starts from the SF with small local phase fluctuations, the system obtains a large-size DW-domain structure with thin domain walls.

## Key claim

The authors find that scaling laws for the correlation length and vortex density with respect to quench time emerge during a first-order quantum phase transition from density wave to superfluid, contradicting the common expectation that such scaling should not exist for first-order transitions; the scaling holds only in the vicinity of a triple point in the phase diagram where the density wave, superfluid, and supersolid phases meet.

## Method

The authors study the two-dimensional extended Bose-Hubbard model with nearest-neighbor repulsion near half-filling using time-dependent Gutzwiller methods, where the hopping amplitude is varied linearly in time as J(t)/J_c = 1 + t/tau_Q. They solve the time-dependent Schrodinger equation for the Gutzwiller wave function using fourth-order Runge-Kutta integration on 64x64 or 100x100 square lattices with a maximum of 6 bosons per site, averaging over 10 initial samples with small density fluctuations from the perfect density wave order.

## Key numbers

- Critical point for the first-order phase transition: J_c/U approximately 0.022 for V/U = 0.05 at chemical potential mu/U = 0.1
- For the DW to SF quench at t = hat{t}: correlation length exponent b = 0.25, vortex density exponent d = 0.26, and hat{t} proportional to tau_Q^{0.45}
- For the DW to SF quench at t = t_eq: scaling laws for correlation length, vortex density, and t_eq appear to exist for quench times tau_Q greater than 20
- After the quench, the SF order parameter amplitude |Psi| is approximately 0.5 for t greater than t_eq, compared to approximately 0.8-0.9 in the Mott to SF transition
- The linear quench protocol is terminated at J(t_f) = 0.044 (greater than J_c) in the numerical study

## Context

This work extends the study of Kibble-Zurek scaling from continuous phase transitions to first-order quantum phase transitions, where such scaling was not expected. The authors build on prior work by Kibble (1976) and Zurek (1985) on topological defect formation, and on studies of second-order quantum phase transitions in the Bose-Hubbard model by various groups including Chen (2011), Braun (2015), Anquez (2016), and Clark (2016). The results enable further investigation of first-order quantum phase transition dynamics and suggest that the Gutzwiller methods may capture universal scaling behavior near triple points, though the authors note that critical exponent estimation by Gutzwiller methods is not reliable even for very large systems.
