# Neural-network quantum states for the nuclear many-body problem

> A long-standing goal of nuclear theory is to explain how the structure and dynamics of atomic nuclei and neutron-star matter emerge from the underlying interactions among protons and neutrons. Achieving this goal requires solving the nuclear quantum many-body problem with high accuracy across a wide range of length scales and density regimes. In this review, we discuss how artificial neural network representations of the nuclear many-body wave function have significantly extended the capabilities of continuum quantum Monte Carlo methods. In particular, neural network quantum states enable calculations of larger systems than were previously accessible and provide a flexible framework for capturing phenomena that challenge conventional approaches, including the emergence of nuclear clusters and superfluid phases in dense matter. We highlight recent applications to finite nuclei, infinite nuclear and neutron matter, and dynamical processes relevant to lepton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus scattering. We also discuss conceptual and methodological connections with condensed matter physics, emphasizing developments in neural network quantum states that bridge strongly correlated systems across disciplines. Together, these developments demonstrate how neural-network methods open new avenues toward unified and accurate descriptions of nuclear structure, matter, and reactions.

# Neural-Network Quantum States for the Nuclear Many-Body Problem

## Key claim

Neural-network quantum states (NQS) significantly extend the capabilities of continuum quantum Monte Carlo methods for nuclear systems, enabling calculations of larger nuclei than previously accessible and providing a flexible framework for capturing phenomena such as nuclear clusters and superfluid phases, though the accuracy depends on the specific architecture and the systematic uncertainties from post-training oscillations can dominate over out-of-sample statistical uncertainties.

## Method

The authors review variational Monte Carlo (VMC) calculations using neural-network representations of nuclear wave functions, including the simple feed-forward architecture for the deuteron, the Slater-Jastrow (SJ) ansatz with Deep Sets architectures for nuclei up to A=6, and the hidden-nucleon (HN) and FeynmanNet architectures that extend to 16O. These methods are benchmarked against exact diagonalization, hyperspherical harmonics (HH) calculations, and Green's function Monte Carlo (GFMC) or auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) results, using Hamiltonians from pionless effective field theory at leading order.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron binding energy converged to within 1% of the exact benchmark using a network with N_hid=10 hidden nodes and softplus activation, with residual oscillations of 3-4 eV above the exact minimum after 150,000 iterations
- For the deuteron with N_hid=2, post-evolution uncertainty in energy was approximately 2.5 keV; at N_hid=100, this fell below 1 keV
- For 3H with the LO pionless-EFT Hamiltonian at Lambda=4 fm^-1, the SJ ansatz gave -8.26(1) MeV compared to GFMC at -8.38(2) MeV, an improvement of about 0.5 MeV over conventional VMC
- For 4He at Lambda=4 fm^-1, the SJ ansatz gave -23.30(2) MeV compared to GFMC at -23.62(3) MeV, improving conventional VMC by about 0.8 MeV
- For 16O, the HN ansatz with increasing hidden nucleons A_h reproduced the AFDMC ground-state energy within uncertainties, whereas earlier NQS applications were limited to A<=6
- The simple one-layer MLP for the deuteron achieved 100% acceptance rate for nearly all N_hid values, while more complex architectures showed acceptance rates ranging from 1% to 90%

## Context

This review extends the pioneering NQS work of Carleo and Troyer (2017) and the first nuclear application by Keeble et al. (2020) on the deuteron, to finite nuclei up to 16O and infinite nuclear matter. The SJ ansatz builds on conventional VMC with Jastrow correlations (Contessi et al., 2017) while the HN and FeynmanNet architectures (Lovato et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023) overcome limitations of the SJ ansatz in capturing nodal structure. The relativistic extension by Yang et al. (2022) demonstrates that relativistic dynamics alone can remove the Thomas collapse in pionless EFT without a leading-order three-nucleon force. These developments enable unified descriptions of nuclear structure, matter, and reactions, with connections to condensed matter physics through shared NQS methodology.
