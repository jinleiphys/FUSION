# Secondary Lund jet plane as a gluon enriched sample

> We propose a new strategy to obtain a high-purity sample of gluon-initiated jets at the LHC. Our approach, inspired by the Lund jet plane picture, is to perform a dijet selection where the two jets are collinear to each other and their momentum fraction share is highly asymmetric, and to measure the primary Lund plane density of emissions of the subleading jet. The subleading jet in this topology is practically equivalent to a secondary Lund jet plane. We demonstrate by means of fixed-order calculations that such a simple setup yields gluon jet fractions of around 90% for the subleading jet for both quark- and gluon-initiated jets. This observation is confirmed using hadron-level Monte Carlo generated events. We also show that the extracted gluon purities are highly resilient to the overall colour structure of the event, to the flavour of the hard-scattering process, and to the parton distribution functions. This strategy is well-suited for constraining the radiation pattern of gluon-initiated jets using a set of fiducial cuts that can readily be tested at the LHC, without relying on taggers or statistical demixing.

## Key claim

The authors propose that selecting a dijet pair where the two jets are collinear and have a highly asymmetric momentum fraction yields a subleading jet that is initiated by a gluon approximately 90% of the time, based on fixed-order calculations and hadron-level Monte Carlo simulations, with the caveat that this purity holds for both quark- and gluon-initiated hard scatterings and is resilient to the overall colour structure of the event.

## Method

The authors construct the secondary Lund jet plane density by first reclustering jets with the Cambridge/Aachen algorithm, then iteratively declustering along the hardest branch to obtain primary emissions, and finally applying the same declustering procedure to the softest branch of each primary emission to obtain secondary emissions. For the dijet selection, they run anti-kt clustering with R=0.4, select jet pairs where the leading jet has pt > 700 GeV and the subleading jet has 150 < pt < 200 GeV, impose an angular separation 1.0 < Delta R < 1.2 between the two jets, and then compute the primary Lund plane density of the subleading jet. The gluon purity is evaluated using fixed-order calculations at O(alpha_s) with NLOJet++ and analytic DGLAP-based estimates, and confirmed with hadron-level Monte Carlo simulations using Pythia 8 and Herwig 7.

## Key numbers

*   Gluon jet fractions of approximately 90% for the subleading jet in the dijet selection, for both quark- and gluon-initiated jets, as shown by fixed-order calculations.
*   The dijet selection uses a leading jet with pt > 700 GeV and a subleading jet with 150 < pt < 200 GeV, with angular separation 1.0 < Delta R < 1.2.
*   The grooming setup uses large-radius jets with R = 1.2, pt > 1 TeV, and SoftDrop grooming with zcut = 0.1 and beta = 0, achieving gluon fractions ranging from 87% to 93%.
*   Changes in the hard-scattering process (qq to qq versus gg to gg) translate into less than 10% differences in the secondary Lund plane density at both large and small angles.
*   The secondary Lund plane density agrees within 10% deviations with the primary Lund plane density of gluon-initiated jets from gg to gg scatterings across the full kt range.

## Context

This work extends the Lund jet plane picture introduced by Andersson (1988) and Dreyer (2018) to create a gluon-enriched sample without relying on jet-by-jet taggers or statistical demixing methods such as topic modeling used by Larkoski (2014) and Metodiev (2018). The strategy contrasts with previous LHC approaches that used inclusive dijet and boson-tagged jet samples to constrain quark and gluon jet substructure simultaneously, which convolve final-state radiation effects with other effects like PDF choice and hard-scattering order. The proposed method enables constraining the radiation pattern of gluon-initiated jets in an isolated way, which could reduce the leading systematic uncertainty in the jet energy scale at the LHC arising from the gluon-jet response in Monte Carlo generators, and facilitate the extraction of the strong coupling alpha_s from jet substructure observables by breaking the degeneracy between alpha_s and the quark versus gluon fraction.
