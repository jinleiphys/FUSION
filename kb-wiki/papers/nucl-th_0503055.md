# An Approach To Potential Scattering

> Recently developed time-independent bound-state perturbation theory is extended to treat the scattering domain. The changes in the partial wave phase shifts are derived explicitly and the results are compared with those of other methods.

# An Approach To Potential Scattering

## Key claim
The authors extend their previously developed time-independent bound-state perturbation theory to the scattering domain, deriving explicit expressions for partial wave phase shift corrections. The method is non-perturbative, self-consistent, and systematically improvable, but the explicit derivation is carried out only for s-wave scattering from a potential that vanishes beyond a finite radius R.

## Method
The formalism expresses the radial wave function as a product of the known unperturbed eigenfunction and a moderating function, then works with logarithmic derivatives (superpotentials) of both. For scattering states, the authors expand the phase shift in a perturbation series and derive first-order and second-order corrections by solving differential equations for the perturbed superpotential, using the square of the unperturbed wave function as an integrating factor. The method is applied to s-wave scattering with the assumption that the potential vanishes beyond a finite radius R, and the results are compared with the first Born approximation, the two-potential formula, and the variable phase equation approach.

## Key numbers
- First-order phase shift correction: δ₁ = −k ∫₀^∞ χ²(r) ΔV₁(r) dr, where χ is the unperturbed s-wave function and ΔV₁ is the first-order perturbing potential.
- For vanishing unperturbed potential (δ₀ = 0), the first-order correction reduces to δ₁ = −(1/k) ∫₀^∞ sin²(kr) ΔV₁(r) dr, which is the first Born approximation for the phase shift.
- For the angular momentum barrier as unperturbed potential V₀ = ℓ(ℓ+1)/r² with perturbation ΔV₁ = λ/r², the first-order phase shift correction is δ₁ = −π/[2(2ℓ+1)].
- First-order scattering length correction: a₁ = lim_{r→∞} [∫₀^r (z − a₀)² ΔV₁(z) dz], where a₀ is the unperturbed scattering length.
- Second-order phase shift correction for ΔV = ΔV₁ (i.e., ΔV₂ = 0): δ₂(r) = δ₁² cot(kR + δ₀) − 2δ₁² cot(kr + δ₀), where R is the range beyond which the potential vanishes.

## Context
This work extends the authors' time-independent perturbation theory from bound states (Gönül and Koçak, previous papers 2003-2005) to continuum scattering states. The first-order result reproduces the first Born approximation (Thaler, textbook) and the two-potential formula for s-wave scattering amplitude. The second-order result agrees with the variable phase equation approach (Calogero, 1967) and with the work of Milward and Wilkin (2003). The formalism enables systematic calculation of phase shift corrections to arbitrary order and can be generalized to higher partial waves by replacing the s-wave phase shift with δ_ℓ − ℓπ/2 in the asymptotic wave function.
