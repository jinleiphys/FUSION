# Shrunk halo and quenched shell gap at $N=16$ in $^{22}$C: Inversion of $sd$ states and deformation effects

> We investigate the ground state properties of $^{22}$C by using a deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov model in continuum and explore the interplays among the formation of a halo, deformation effects, the inversion of $sd$ states, the shell evolution, and changes of nuclear magicities. It is revealed that there is an inversion between the two spherical orbitals $2s_{1/2}$ and $1d_{5/2}$ in $^{22}$C compared with the conventional single particle shell structure in stable nuclei. This inversion, together with deformation effects, results in a shrunk halo and a quenched shell gap at $N=16$. It is predicted that the core of $^{22}$C is oblate but the halo is prolate. Therefore several exotic nuclear phenomena, including the halo, the shape decoupling effects, the inversion of $sd$ states, and the evolution of shell structure which results in (dis)appearance of magic numbers, coexist in one single nucleus $^{22}\mathrm{C}$.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the inversion of the $2s_{1/2}$ and $1d_{5/2}$ spherical orbitals in $^{22}$C, combined with deformation effects, results in a shrunk halo and a quenched shell gap at $N=16$, resolving the apparent puzzle between a very small two-neutron separation energy and a relatively small matter radius. The key qualifier is that this inversion hinders halo formation in the spherical limit, but strong quadrupole correlations mix the $sd$ orbitals, producing only about 25% $2s_{1/2}$ amplitude in the valence neutron orbitals, leading to a less extended halo than if the valence neutrons were purely in the $2s_{1/2}$ state.

## Method

The authors used a deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov model in continuum (DRHBc model) with the covariant density functional PK1 and a zero-range pairing force. The RHB equation was solved in a Woods-Saxon basis to describe the large spatial extension of halo nuclei, and the augmented Lagrangian method was implemented for deformation constraint calculations. The Borromean feature of $^{22}$C was used to fix the pairing parameters, with the pairing strength $V_0 = 355$ MeV$\cdot$fm$^3$ and a cut-off energy $E^\mathrm{q.p.}_\mathrm{cut} = 60$ MeV in the quasiparticle space.

## Key numbers

* The ground state of $^{22}$C is predicted to be well deformed with a quadrupole deformation parameter $\beta_2 = -0.27$ (oblate shape).
* The calculated matter radius is $r_m = 3.25$ fm, which is close to the recent experimental values of $3.44\pm 0.08$ fm (Togano 2016) and $3.38\pm 0.10$ fm (Nagahisa 2018), but much smaller than the earlier value of $5.4 \pm 0.9$ fm (Tanaka 2010).
* The total amplitude of the $2s_{1/2}$ component in the two $1/2^+$ valence orbitals ($1/2^+_3$ and $1/2^+_4$) is about 25%, meaning about half of the valence neutrons are of $2s_{1/2}$ nature.
* The sum of occupation probabilities of the weakly bound or continuum orbitals ($1/2^+_3$ and those above it) that form the halo is 1.03.
* The pairing parameters result in a one-neutron separation energy $S_n = -28$ keV for $^{21}$C and a two-neutron separation energy $S_{2n} = 0.43$ MeV for $^{22}$C.

## Context

This work extends the study of deformed halo nuclei with shape decoupling effects, previously predicted for $^{42,44}$Mg by Zhou (2010) and Li (2012) using the same DRHBc model, where the core and halo had opposite shapes. The paper addresses the puzzles in $^{22}$C concerning the matter radius and halo configuration that arose from conflicting experimental results from Tanaka (2010) and Togano (2016), and from the assumption in prior work (e.g., Horiuchi 2006, Abu-Ibrahim 2008, Fortune 2012) that the two valence neutrons occupy mostly the $2s_{1/2}$ orbital. The inversion of $(2s_{1/2}, 1d_{5/2})$ had been predicted in $A/Z \sim 3$ nuclei by Ozawa (2000) and is related to the appearance of $N=14$ and $N=16$ shell closures studied by Otsuka (2001) and Sorlin (2008). The work enables further investigations of shape decoupling in other neutron-rich nuclei and suggests similar studies with point-coupling interactions such as PC-PK1.
