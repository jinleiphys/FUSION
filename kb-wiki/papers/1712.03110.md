# Meson effect in the proto neutron star PSR J0348+0432

> The effect of mesons $f_{0}(975)$ (named as $f$), $ϕ(1020)$ (named as $ϕ$) and $δ$ on the moment of inertia of the PNS PSR J0348+0432 is examined in the framework of the relativistic mean field theory considering the baryon octet. It is found that the energy density $\varepsilon$ and pressure $p$ will increase considering the mesons $δ$ whereas will decrease as the mesons $f$ and $ϕ$ being considered. When the mesons $f, ϕ$ and $δ$ are considered, the energy density and pressure will all decrease. It is also found that the contribution of mesons $f$, $ϕ$ and $δ$ to the central energy density is only the central energy density's 0.06$\sim$0.6\% whereas the contribution of mesons $f$, $ϕ$ and $δ$ to the central pressure is the central pressure's 4$\sim$7\%. For the radius, it will decrease when the contributions of mesons $f$, $ϕ$ and $δ$ are considered. The moment of inertia $I$ will increase considering the mesons $δ$ whereas will decrease as the mesons $f$ and $ϕ$ being considered. When the mesons $f$, $ϕ$ and $δ$ are all considered, the moment of inertia will decrease. It is found that the contribution of mesons $f$ and $ϕ$ to moment of inertia is 4$\sim$9 times larger than that of mesons $δ$. Our results show that the mesons $f$, $ ϕ$ and $δ$ contribute to the moment of inertia's 2$\sim$5\%.

## Key claim

The mesons \(f_0(975)\), \(\phi(1020)\), and \(\delta\) collectively contribute only 2–5% to the moment of inertia of the proto neutron star PSR J0348+0432, with the contribution of \(f\) and \(\phi\) being 4–9 times larger than that of \(\delta\); this small effect is conditional on the specific parameter set (GM1 nucleon coupling, hyperon well depths, and temperature \(T = 20\) MeV) used in the relativistic mean field model.

## Method

The study uses the relativistic mean field (RMF) theory with the baryon octet and the \(\sigma\omega\rho f\phi\delta\) model, incorporating mesons \(f_0(975)\), \(\phi(1020)\), and \(\delta\) to describe hyperon interactions and isospin asymmetry. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations are solved for a slowly rotating proto neutron star at temperature \(T = 20\) MeV, using the GM1 parameter set and hyperon coupling constants derived from hyperon well depths (\(U_\Lambda^{(N)} = -30\) MeV, \(U_\Sigma^{(N)} = +20\) MeV, \(U_\Xi^{(N)} = -14\) MeV) and SU(6) symmetry. The moment of inertia is computed via the Hartle formalism for a star with mass \(M = 1.97\)–\(2.05\) \(M_\odot\), matching the observed mass of PSR J0348+0432.

## Key numbers

- Central energy density contribution of mesons \(f\), \(\phi\), and \(\delta\) is only 0.06–0.6% of the total central energy density.
- Central pressure contribution of mesons \(f\), \(\phi\), and \(\delta\) is 4–7% of the total central pressure.
- Contribution of mesons \(f\) and \(\phi\) to the moment of inertia is 4–9 times larger than that of mesons \(\delta\).
- Mesons \(f\), \(\phi\), and \(\delta\) collectively contribute 2–5% to the total moment of inertia.
- For the PNS PSR J0348+0432 (\(M = 1.97\)–\(2.05\) \(M_\odot\)), the radius decreases from \(R = 14.708\)–\(14.246\) km (without \(f\), \(\phi\), \(\delta\)) to \(R = 14.671\)–\(14.108\) km (with all three mesons).
- The moment of inertia \(I\) ranges from \(2.286 \times 10^{45}\) g cm\(^2\) (without \(f\), \(\phi\), \(\delta\)) to \(2.233 \times 10^{45}\) g cm\(^2\) (with all three mesons), for the mass range \(1.97\)–\(2.05\) \(M_\odot\).

## Context

This work extends the RMF treatment of neutron star structure by including the scalar meson \(\delta\) alongside the hyperon-sector mesons \(f_0(975)\) and \(\phi(1020)\), which were introduced by Schaffner (1994) to describe hyperon interactions. The study focuses on the massive proto neutron star PSR J0348+0432, discovered by Antoniadis (2013), and uses the GM1 parameter set from Glendenning (1991). The results show that while these mesons affect the equation of state and moment of inertia, their net contribution is small (2–5%), enabling future work to assess whether such corrections are detectable in gravitational wave or pulsar timing observations.
