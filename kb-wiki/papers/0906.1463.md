# Toward ab initio density functional theory for nuclei

> We survey approaches to nonrelativistic density functional theory (DFT) for nuclei using progress toward ab initio DFT for Coulomb systems as a guide. Ab initio DFT starts with a microscopic Hamiltonian and is naturally formulated using orbital-based functionals, which generalize the conventional local-density-plus-gradients form. The orbitals satisfy single-particle equations with multiplicative (local) potentials. The DFT functionals can be developed starting from internucleon forces using wave-function based methods or by Legendre transform via effective actions. We describe known and unresolved issues for applying these formulations to the nuclear many-body problem and discuss how ab initio approaches can help improve empirical energy density functionals.

## Key claim

The paper argues that *ab initio* density functional theory for nuclei, starting from a microscopic Hamiltonian with internucleon forces, is feasible and can systematically improve empirical energy density functionals, but only if the nuclear Hamiltonian is first softened (e.g., via renormalization group transformations) to make the many-body problem more perturbative and thus amenable to DFT-based methods.

## Method

The authors survey two principal routes to constructing *ab initio* nuclear DFT functionals: (1) wave-function-based methods (e.g., coupled cluster or no-core shell model) that start from internucleon forces and yield orbital-based functionals, and (2) Legendre transform methods via effective actions, which connect DFT to Green's function approaches. Both routes lead to Kohn-Sham equations with multiplicative (local) potentials, where the density is determined entirely by occupied orbitals, and the functional derivative of the energy functional defines the local Kohn-Sham potential. The paper also discusses how the density matrix expansion can reduce non-local orbital-based functionals to semi-local forms (e.g., Skyrme-like functionals with gradients).

## Key numbers

- The paper states that for typical realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions, "correlations are much greater than the Hartree-Fock contribution," motivating the need for softened interactions.
- It notes that Hartree-Fock plus second-order many-body perturbation theory "may be well converged for the bulk energy of the uniform system" after softening, citing Bogner (2005) and Bogner (2009).
- The paper mentions that the uniform electron gas calculations used in Coulomb DFT are "precision numerical calculations (e.g., using GFMC) combined with well-controlled analytic limits," contrasting with the less controlled status of symmetric nuclear matter calculations.
- It reports that present-day nuclear EDFs "have constants fit to properties such as binding energies and radii of a set of nuclei," with the most stringent constraints on saturation properties coming from the uniform limit of fitted EDFs.

## Context

This review extends the methodology of *ab initio* DFT developed for Coulomb systems (e.g., the work of Dreizler (1990), Parr (1994), and Fiolhais (2003)) to the nuclear many-body problem, addressing the unique challenges of self-bound nuclei with strong interactions. It contrasts with purely empirical nuclear EDF approaches (e.g., Skyrme, Gogny, or relativistic mean-field functionals reviewed by Bender (2003)) that fit functionals without relying on an explicit underlying Hamiltonian. The paper enables future work by outlining how renormalization group methods (e.g., Bogner (2003, 2006)) can soften nuclear interactions to make DFT feasible, and by identifying unresolved issues such as handling pairing and non-local contributions from low-lying excitations in finite nuclei.
