# Chiral dynamics in a magnetic field from the functional renormalization group

> We investigate the quark-meson model in a magnetic field using the exact functional renormalization group equation beyond the local-potential approximation. Our truncation of the effective action involves anisotropic wave function renormalization for mesons, which allows us to investigate how the magnetic field distorts the propagation of neutral mesons. Solving the flow equation numerically, we find that the transverse velocity of mesons decreases with the magnetic field at all temperatures, which is most prominent at zero temperature. The meson screening masses and the pion decay constants are also computed. The constituent quark mass is found to increase with magnetic field at all temperatures, resulting in the crossover temperature that increases monotonically with the magnetic field. This tendency is consistent with most model calculations but not with the lattice simulation performed at the physical point. Our work suggests that the strong anisotropy of meson propagation may not be the fundamental origin of the inverse magnetic catalysis.

## Key claim

The authors find that the pseudo-critical temperature of the chiral phase transition increases monotonically with magnetic field strength in their functional renormalization group calculation, contrary to the expectation that anisotropic meson fluctuations might explain the inverse magnetic catalysis seen in lattice simulations; they suggest that gluonic degrees of freedom, which are absent in chiral quark-meson models, may be essential for the inverse magnetic catalysis phenomenon.

## Method

The authors apply the functional renormalization group (FRG) to a one-flavor quark-meson model with a magnetic field, going beyond the local-potential approximation (LPA) by including anisotropic wave function renormalization factors \(Z_k^\perp\) and \(Z_k^\parallel\) for mesons in directions perpendicular and parallel to the magnetic field. They derive and numerically solve coupled flow equations for the effective potential (expanded as a polynomial around its minimum) and the wave function renormalizations, using anisotropic regulators that break rotational symmetry to handle the fermion propagator in a magnetic field. The flow is integrated from an ultraviolet scale \(\Lambda = 600\) MeV down to \(k=0\) to obtain the full quantum effective action, from which physical observables are extracted.

## Key numbers

* Constituent quark mass \(M_q = 257\) MeV at zero temperature and zero magnetic field (full FRG result).
* Pion decay constant \(f_\pi = 93.4\) MeV at zero temperature and zero magnetic field (full FRG result).
* Pion mass \(m_\pi = 138\) MeV and sigma mass \(m_\sigma = 411\) MeV at zero temperature and zero magnetic field (full FRG result).
* Pseudo-critical temperature \(T_{\rm pc} = 178\) MeV at zero magnetic field (full FRG result), compared to 194 MeV in LPA.
* The transverse velocity of mesons \(v_\perp \equiv \sqrt{Z_{k=0}^\perp / Z_{k=0}^\parallel}\) decreases with magnetic field at all temperatures, most prominently at zero temperature.

## Context

This work extends previous FRG studies of chiral models in a magnetic field (Skokov 2011, Scherer 2012, Fukushima 2012, Andersen 2012, 2013) which were limited to the local-potential approximation (LPA) and neglected wave function renormalization. It directly tests the mechanism proposed by Fukushima and Hidaka (2012) that dimensional reduction of neutral pions could cause inverse magnetic catalysis. The authors find that even with anisotropic meson fluctuations included, the chiral restoration temperature still increases with magnetic field, contradicting lattice simulations at physical quark masses (Bali 2011, 2012) which show decreasing pseudo-critical temperature. This suggests that the missing gluonic degrees of freedom in chiral models may be crucial for understanding inverse magnetic catalysis, and the authors' predictions for anisotropic screening masses and transverse meson velocities can be tested in future lattice simulations.
