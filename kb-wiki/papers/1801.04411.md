# Nuclear mass predictions based on Bayesian neural network approach with pairing and shell effects

> Bayesian neural network (BNN) approach is employed to improve the nuclear mass predictions of various models. It is found that the noise error in the likelihood function plays an important role in the predictive performance of the BNN approach. By including a distribution for the noise error, an appropriate value can be found automatically in the sampling process, which optimizes the nuclear mass predictions. Furthermore, two quantities related to nuclear pairing and shell effects are added to the input layer in addition to the proton and mass numbers. As a result, the theoretical accuracies are significantly improved not only for nuclear masses but also for single-nucleon separation energies. Due to the inclusion of the shell effect, in the unknown region, the BNN approach predicts a similar shell-correction structure to that in the known region, e.g., the predictions of underestimation of nuclear mass around the magic numbers in the relativistic mean-field model. This manifests that better predictive performance can be achieved if more physical features are included in the BNN approach.

# Nuclear mass predictions based on Bayesian neural network approach with pairing and shell effects

## Key claim

The Bayesian neural network (BNN) approach can significantly improve nuclear mass predictions of various models, but only if a gamma distribution for the noise error is used in the likelihood function (rather than a fixed noise precision) and if two additional input quantities representing nuclear pairing and shell effects are included alongside proton and mass numbers. The authors claim that including these physical features yields better predictive performance in both known and unknown regions, though extrapolation too far from the training region may miss new physics effects hidden in the known data.

## Method

The authors employed a Bayesian neural network (BNN) to reconstruct mass residuals between experimental masses from AME2016 and theoretical predictions from six mass models (RMF, HFB-31, WS4, FRDM12, BW, and BW2). The input layer included either two variables (proton number Z and mass number A, denoted BNN-I2) or four variables (Z, A, plus a pairing indicator delta and a shell-effect quantity P, denoted BNN-I4). The noise error in the likelihood function was treated with a gamma distribution rather than a fixed value, allowing automatic optimization during Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling. The learning set contained 1800 randomly selected nuclei and the validation set contained the remaining 472 nuclei from the 2272 nuclei with Z,N >= 8 and experimental errors <= 100 keV.

## Key numbers

* For the RMF model, the rms deviation in the learning set was reduced from 2.269 MeV (original) to 0.443 MeV with BNN-I2 and to 0.367 MeV with BNN-I4; in the validation set, from 2.242 MeV to 0.480 MeV (BNN-I2) and 0.415 MeV (BNN-I4).
* For the BW (liquid-drop) model, the rms deviation in the learning set was reduced from 3.236 MeV to 0.850 MeV with BNN-I2 and to 0.266 MeV with BNN-I4; in the validation set, from 3.048 MeV to 0.924 MeV (BNN-I2) and 0.310 MeV (BNN-I4).
* Using a gamma distribution for noise precision instead of a fixed value reduced the rms deviation by 39.5% for the RMF model and by 37.0% for the BW2 model in the learning set.
* For single-neutron separation energies (S_n) in the learning set with the BW model, the rms deviation improved from 1.345 MeV to 0.291 MeV with BNN-I4; for single-proton separation energies (S_p), from 1.439 MeV to 0.290 MeV.
* The BNN-I4 approach reduced the rms deviation of the BW model by 91.8% in the learning set and by 89.8% in the validation set.

## Context

This work extends the BNN approach previously applied to nuclear masses by Utama (2016) and to nuclear charge radii by Utama (2016), which used fixed noise errors and only proton and mass numbers as inputs. It also builds on the radial basis function (RBF) approach developed by Wang (2011), Niu (2013), Zheng (2014), and Niu (2016), which improved mass predictions but could deteriorate single-nucleon separation energies unless applied separately. The present BNN-I4 approach simultaneously improves both masses and separation energies, and its inclusion of pairing and shell inputs enables better extrapolation to unknown regions, such as predicting mass overestimation for nuclei near (Z,N) = (28,82) and (50,126) in the RMF model. This enables more reliable predictions for astrophysical nucleosynthesis studies and tests of the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa matrix unitarity.
