# Is GRO J1744-28 a Strange Star?

> The unusal hard x-ray burster GRO J1744-28 recently discovered by the Compton Gamma-ray Observatory (GRO) can be modeled as a strange star with a dipolar magnetic field $\le 10^{11} $Gauss. When the accreted mass of the star exceeds some critical mass, its crust may break, resulting in conversion of the accreted matter into strange matter and release of energy. Subsequently, a fireball may form and expand relativistically outward. The expanding fireball may interact with the surrounding interstellar medium, causing its kinetic energy to be radiated in shock waves, producing a burst of x-ray radiation. The burst energy, duration, interval and spectrum derived from such a model are consistent with the observations of GRO J1744-28.

# Is GRO J1744-28 a Strange Star?

## Key claim
The hard X-ray burster GRO J1744-28 can be modeled as a strange star (rather than a neutron star) with a dipolar magnetic field ≤ 10¹¹ Gauss, provided that the accreted mass builds up enough pressure to break the star's thin crust, triggering conversion of accreted matter into strange matter and a subsequent fireball that produces the observed bursts.

## Method
The authors model GRO J1744-28 as a strange star accreting from a low-mass companion in a binary system. They calculate the critical column density required to break the strange star's crust (using shear modulus and shear angle estimates from neutron star literature), then derive the expected burst interval, fireball energy, burst duration, and synchrotron photon energy from the resulting relativistic fireball interacting with the interstellar medium. These predictions are compared with observational data from BATSE, OSSE, and RXTE.

## Key numbers
- Burst fluences (25-60 keV): range 1.7×10⁻⁷ to 6.8×10⁻⁷ erg cm⁻²; average fluence (2.7±0.9)×10⁻⁷ erg cm⁻²
- Average peak luminosity at distance ~7.5 kpc: ~2×10³⁸ erg s⁻¹
- Pulsation period: 0.467 s; binary orbital period: 11.8 days; companion mass: 0.22–1.0 M⊙
- Inclination angle: ~18°; X-ray mass function: ~1.31×10⁻⁴ M⊙
- Surface dipolar magnetic field: ≤10¹¹ G; local surface magnetic field: several ×10¹² G
- Burst durations: ~10 s; initial burst interval: ~200 s
- Column density for crust breaking: σ ≈ 1.0×10¹⁰ θ₋₃ g cm⁻² (where θ₋₃ = θ/10⁻³)
- Predicted burst interval: τ₁ ≈ 2.2×10³ θ₋₃ L*⁻⁵/⁷ B*⁻⁴/⁷ s
- Total released energy per burst: Etot ~ 5.5×10⁴⁰ θ₋₃ L*²/⁷ B*⁻⁴/⁷ erg
- Fireball energy: Eγ ~ 2.8×10⁴⁰ ξ₁/₂ θ₋₃ L*²/⁷ B*⁻⁴/⁷ erg
- Predicted burst duration: τ₂ ~ 9.0 ξ₁/₂¹¹/⁹ θ₋₃¹¹/⁹ L*²/²¹ B*⁻⁴/²¹ n₀⁻¹/³ s
- Predicted synchrotron photon energy: εp ~ 1 ξ₁/₂⁻⁴/³ θ₋₃⁻⁴/³ n₀¹/² keV
- Predicted recovery time: τ₃ ~ 2.4×10³ ξ₁/₂ θ₋₃ L*⁻¹ s
- Observed ratio of interval times (30 Jan 1996 vs 5 Dec 1995): ~1.38; model predicts ~1.4
- Observed photon index from OSSE: α = -(2.0±0.6); from RXTE: α ~ -1.3; model predicts α between -1.5 and -2.0

## Context
This work extends the strange star hypothesis proposed by Cheng and Dai (1996) for low-mass X-ray binaries to the specific case of GRO J1744-28, which was discovered by BATSE on the Compton Gamma-Ray Observatory in December 1995. The model contrasts with thermonuclear flash models for type-I X-ray bursts and with the accretion instability model of Cannizzo (1996) for the Rapid Burster. The authors note that a similar strange star model was recently proposed for soft gamma-ray repeaters by Cheng and Dai (1998), but with key differences in the crust-cracking mechanism (spin-down versus accretion), energy release scale, magnetic field strength, and time scales. The model enables future tests against the observed correlation between bursting flux and persistent flux, and against the pulsation amplitude during bursts.
