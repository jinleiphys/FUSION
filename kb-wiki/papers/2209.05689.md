# Hadronization effects on transverse momentum dependent jet fragmentation function in small systems

> The transverse momentum $j_{\rm T}$-dependent jet fragmentation functions have been investigated in proton+proton (p $+$ p) and proton+lead (p $+$ Pb) collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02~{\rm TeV}$ with a multiphase transport model containing both a simple quark coalescence mechanism and a new hybrid hadronization mechanism with coalescence and fragmentation processes. Hadronized by the new hadronization mechanism, the AMPT model achieves a quantitative description of the $j_{\rm T}$-dependent jet fragmentation functions measured by ALICE. Besides, no obvious jet-medium interaction and cold nuclear matter effects on the $j_{\rm T}$-dependent jet fragmentation functions in p $+$ Pb collisions were observed. We found the $j_{\rm T}$-dependent jet fragmentation functions are dominated by the quark coalescence contribution for the new hadronization mechanism, which can be decomposed into narrow and wide parts. The root mean square value of the wide part depends on the jet radius $R$ and jet transverse momentum $p_{\rm{T, jet}}$, which is sensitive to different hadronization mechanisms and their components. Therefore, the $j_{\rm T}$-dependent jet fragmentation functions are proposed as a sensitive probe to study the non-perturbative hadronization effect of jets in small colliding systems.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the transverse momentum dependent jet fragmentation functions are dominated by the quark coalescence contribution in the new hybrid hadronization mechanism, and that the root mean square value of the wide part of these functions is sensitive to different hadronization mechanisms, making it a probe for non-perturbative hadronization effects in small colliding systems. The hedge is that this sensitivity is demonstrated only within the AMPT model framework, and the new hadronization mechanism achieves a quantitative description of ALICE data while the old mechanism fails for the wide component.

## Method

The authors used the string-melting version of the AMPT (A Multi-Phase Transport) model with two different hadronization mechanisms: an old simple quark coalescence model and a new hybrid hadronization model combining dynamical quark coalescence with string fragmentation via PYTHIA 8. They simulated proton+proton and proton+lead collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV, reconstructed jets using the anti-k_T algorithm with resolution parameters R = 0.3, 0.4, and 0.5, and calculated the j_T-dependent jet fragmentation functions of charged particles for four jet transverse momentum intervals between 40 and 150 GeV/c.

## Key numbers

- Collision energy: sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV for both p+p and p+Pb collisions
- Jet transverse momentum intervals: 40-60, 60-80, 80-100, and 100-150 GeV/c
- Parton cross section settings: 3 mb for jet-medium interactions and 0 mb for reference without interactions
- Debye screening mass: 2.265 fm^-1 for 3 mb cross section, 10^5 fm^-1 for 0 mb cross section
- Strong coupling constant: alpha_s = 0.33
- Particle transverse momentum cut: p_T > 0.15 GeV/c for charged particles in jet analysis
- Jet pseudorapidity cut: |eta_jet| < 0.25
- The string fragmentation contribution to the total j_T-dependent JFF reaches up to 25% at j_T approximately 3 GeV/c
- Jet cone radii studied: R = 0.3, 0.4, and 0.5

## Context

This work extends previous studies of jet fragmentation functions using the AMPT model by Ma (2013, 2014) and builds on the hybrid hadronization approach developed by Zhao (2020, 2022) that successfully described nuclear modification factors and elliptic flow in Pb+Pb collisions. The paper directly compares with ALICE measurements of j_T-dependent jet fragmentation functions from 2021, and finds that the new hybrid hadronization mechanism reproduces these data while the old simple coalescence model overestimates the wide component. The results enable future studies of hadronization mechanisms by varying jet cone size to enhance contributions from either coalescence or fragmentation processes.
