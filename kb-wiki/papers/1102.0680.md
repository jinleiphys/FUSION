# Quark-Gluon Plasma at RHIC and the LHC: Perfect Fluid too Perfect?

> Relativistic heavy ion collisions have reached energies that enable the creation of a novel state of matter termed the quark-gluon plasma. Many observables point to a picture of the medium as rapidly equilibrating and expanding as a nearly inviscid fluid. In this article, we explore the evolution of experimental flow observables as a function of collision energy and attempt to reconcile the observed similarities across a broad energy regime in terms of the initial conditions and viscous hydrodynamics. If the initial spatial anisotropies are very similar for all collision energies from 39 GeV to 2.76 TeV, we find that viscous hydrodynamics might be consistent with the level of agreement for v2 of unidentified hadrons as a function of pT . However, we predict a strong collision energy dependence for the proton v2(pT). The results presented in this paper highlight the need for more systematic studies and a re-evaluation of previously stated sensitivities to the early time dynamics and properties of the medium.

# Quark-Gluon Plasma at RHIC and the LHC: Perfect Fluid too Perfect?

## Key claim

If the initial spatial anisotropies are very similar for all collision energies from 39 GeV to 2.76 TeV, viscous hydrodynamics might be consistent with the observed agreement for v2 of unidentified hadrons as a function of pT; however, the authors predict a strong collision energy dependence for the proton v2(pT), and argue that the near-identity of v2(pT) across energies may indicate a lack of sensitivity to the earliest time dynamics and the value of eta/s rather than confirming near-perfect fluid behavior.

## Method

The authors performed viscous hydrodynamic calculations using the publicly available Romatschke-Luzum model with Monte Carlo Glauber initial conditions for Au-Au collisions at impact parameter b = 7 fm, comparing two initial temperatures (Ti = 340 MeV for RHIC and Ti = 420 MeV for LHC) with identical spatial distributions. They varied the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio eta/s from approximately 0 to 2/4pi, used both an ideal gas equation of state and a lattice QCD inspired equation of state, and implemented step-function temperature-dependent eta/s profiles to test sensitivity to early-time viscosity. Additionally, they calculated initial spatial eccentricities using Monte Carlo Glauber and Color Glass Condensate (rc-BK) models across collision energies from 17 GeV to 200 TeV.

## Key numbers

- The agreement between v2(pT) for unidentified charged hadrons at 2.76 TeV (ALICE) and 200 GeV (STAR) is at the level of a few percent from pT = 0.5 to 2.5 GeV, with agreement persisting at the 5% level down to 39 GeV for the 20-30% centrality bin.
- For the 20-30% centrality selection, fitting a fourth-order polynomial to the ALICE data (statistical uncertainties only) yields chi2/d.o.f. = 3.4/13, corresponding to a p-value of 0.996.
- A +/- 40% change in eta/s results in only a 10% variation in v2 for pT from 0 to 5 GeV.
- Even a factor of two change in eta/s at early times (T > 340 MeV) produces no measurable difference in v2(pT) over the pT range shown.
- Monte Carlo Glauber calculations show approximately 5% larger eccentricity for Au-Au at 200 GeV and 10% larger at 39 GeV compared with Pb-Pb at 2.76 TeV.
- The nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross sections used are 34 +/- 3 mb at RHIC 39 GeV, 42 +/- 2 mb at RHIC 200 GeV, and 64 +/- 5 mb at LHC 2.76 TeV.
- CGC (rc-BK) eccentricities agree within less than 2% for much of the centrality range (10-70%) between RHIC and LHC energies.
- The relative increase in eccentricity from CGC saturation effects is approximately 10% from 200 GeV to 2.76 TeV.

## Context

This paper extends the comparison of elliptic flow measurements from RHIC (STAR Collaboration, 2008; PHENIX Collaboration, 2010) and the LHC (ALICE Collaboration, 2010) to question whether the apparent perfect fluid behavior is actually due to compensating effects and limited sensitivity. The work builds on viscous hydrodynamic frameworks developed by Romatschke and Luzum (2008) and addresses the sensitivity of v2 to eta/s raised in studies by Heinz (2009), Teaney (2009), and Romatschke (2009). The results challenge the previously stated sensitivity to early-time dynamics and transport coefficients, and highlight the need for more systematic studies of temperature-dependent eta/s as pursued by Niemi (2011) and Shen (2011), as well as event-by-event hydrodynamic calculations on fluctuating initial conditions as advocated by Schenke (2010).
