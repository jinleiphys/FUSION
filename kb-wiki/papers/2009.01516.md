# Resummed Gluon Propagator and Debye Screening Effect in a Holonomous Plasma

> Based on the Dyson-Schwinger equation, we compute the resummed gluon propagator in a holonomous plasma that is described by introducing a constant background field for the vector potential $A_{0}$. Due to the transversality of the holonomous Hard-Thermal-Loop in gluon self-energy, the resummed propagator has a similar Lorentz structure as that in the perturbative Quark-Gluon Plasma where the holonomy vanishes. As for the color structures, since diagonal gluons are mixed in the over-complete double line basis, only the propagators for off-diagonal gluons can be obtained unambiguously. On the other hand, multiplied by a projection operator, the propagators for diagonal gluons, which exhibit a highly non-trivial dependence on the background field, are uniquely determined after summing over the color indices. As an application of these results, we consider the Debye screening effect on the in-medium binding of quarkonium states by analyzing the static limit of the resummed gluon propagator. In general, introducing non-zero holonomy merely amounts to modifications on the perturbative screening mass $m_D$ and the resulting heavy-quark potential, which remains the standard Debye screened form, is always deeper than the screened potential in the perturbative Quark-Gluon Plasma. Therefore, a weaker screening, thus a more tightly bounded quarkonium state can be expected in a holonomous plasma. In addition, both the diagonal and off-diagonal gluons become distinguishable by their modified screening masses ${\cal M}_D$ and the temperature dependence of the ratio ${\cal M}_D/T$ shows a very similar behavior as that found in lattice simulations.

## Key claim

The paper computes the resummed gluon propagator in a holonomous plasma (semi-QGP) using the Dyson-Schwinger equation, finding that while the Lorentz structure remains similar to the perturbative Quark-Gluon Plasma, the color structure introduces ambiguities for diagonal gluons that are resolved only after summing over color indices; the resulting Debye-screened heavy-quark potential is always deeper than in perturbative QGP, implying weaker screening and more tightly bound quarkonium states.

## Method

The authors introduce a constant background field \(A_0^{\rm cl}\) for the vector potential to describe non-zero holonomy and work in the double line basis for \(SU(N)\) color generators. They compute the resummed gluon propagator by inverting the Dyson-Schwinger equation using the holonomous Hard-Thermal-Loop (HTL) gluon self-energy from an effective theory that includes contributions from two-dimensional ghosts to ensure transversality and remove anomalous terms. The propagators for off-diagonal gluons are obtained unambiguously, while diagonal gluon propagators are determined only after applying a projection operator and summing over color indices.

## Key numbers

* The perturbative Debye mass squared in vanishing holonomy is \(m_D^2 = N g^2 T^2 / 3\).
* The coefficient matrix for diagonal gluon propagators has determinant \(1/N\) for general \(SU(N)\).
* The anomalous term in the gluon self-energy involves the third Bernoulli polynomial \(B_3(q)\) and scales as \(\sim T^3\).
* The effective theory introduces a cutoff scale \(C\) with dimensions of mass squared, corresponding to the upper limit of transverse momentum \(k_\perp^2\) of embedded two-dimensional ghost fields, leading to a non-zero holonomy \(q \sim C/T^2\) at high temperature.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of gluon self-energies in holonomous plasmas by Hidaka (2009) and Korthals-Altes (2020), which identified non-transverse pieces and anomalous contributions requiring constrained terms. The authors build on the effective theory of Hidaka (2020) that introduces two-dimensional ghosts to generate non-zero holonomy dynamically. The results enable more accurate modeling of quarkonium binding in the semi-QGP regime relevant to temperatures probed at RHIC and LHC, and the temperature dependence of the screening mass ratio \(\mathcal{M}_D/T\) shows behavior similar to lattice simulations.
