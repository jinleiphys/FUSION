# Appearance of Light Clusters in Post-bounce Evolution of Core-Collapse Supernovae

> We explore the abundance of light clusters in core-collapse supernovae at post-bounce stage in a quantum statistical approach. Adopting the profile of a supernova core from detailed numerical simulations, we study the distribution of light bound clusters up to alpha particles (A=2-4) as well as heavy nuclei (A > 4) in dense matter at finite temperature. Within the frame of a cluster-mean field approach, the abundances of light clusters are evaluated accounting for self-energy, Pauli blocking and effects of continuum correlations. We find that deuterons and tritons, in addition to 3He and 4He, appear abundantly in a wide region from the surface of the proto-neutron star to the position of the shock wave. The appearance of light clusters may modify the neutrino emission in the cooling region and the neutrino absorption in the heating region, and, thereby, influence the supernova mechanism.

## Key claim

Deuterons, tritons, and helium-3 appear abundantly alongside helium-4 in a wide region from the proto-neutron star surface to the shock wave position in post-bounce core-collapse supernovae, which may modify neutrino emission and absorption and thereby influence the supernova mechanism, but the authors adopt a single spherically symmetric snapshot at 150 ms after bounce as an exemplary case and note that other time sequences and multi-dimensional aspects require further study.

## Method

The authors adopt a profile of a supernova core at 150 ms after bounce from a detailed numerical simulation of a 15 solar mass pre-supernova star using the Shen equation of state. They solve a generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck formula within a cluster-mean field approach that accounts for self-energy shifts, Pauli blocking, and continuum correlations, evaluating the abundances of light clusters (A=2-4) and heavier nuclei (A>4) as a function of radius for given temperature, density, and electron fraction profiles.

## Key numbers

- The shock wave is stalled at approximately 130 km at 150 ms after core bounce.
- The neutrino sphere is located at 20-80 km depending on energies and species.
- The electron fraction is approximately 0.5 in the iron nuclei region at radii greater than or equal to 100 km.
- The fraction of helium-4 rises to about 90% at approximately 150 km radius before decreasing when heavier clusters with A greater than or equal to 5 are formed.
- The abundance of nucleons is reduced by about 10% due to the appearance of light clusters.

## Context

This work extends the quantum statistical approach to nuclear matter developed by Roepke and collaborators (2005, 2006) and the generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck approach (Roepke 1982, Schmidt 1990) to realistic supernova profiles, going beyond previous treatments that considered only helium-4 and one representative heavy nucleus as in the Shen equation of state (Shen 1998) and the Lattimer-Swesty equation of state (Lattimer 1991). The findings motivate inclusion of light clusters in neutrino transport simulations for core-collapse supernovae and proto-neutron star cooling, and enable construction of improved supernova equation of state tables that merge low-density virial expansions with quasiparticle approaches at higher densities.
