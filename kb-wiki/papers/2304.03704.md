# Prospects for GPDs extraction with Double DVCS

> Double deeply virtual Compton scattering (DDVCS) is the process where an electron scatters off a nucleon and produces a lepton pair. The main advantage of this process in contrast with deeply virtual and timelike Compton scatterings (DVCS and TCS) is the possibility of directly measuring GPDs for $x\neq\pmξ$ at leading order in $α_s$ (LO). We present a new calculation of the DDVCS amplitude based on the methods developed by R. Kleiss and W. J. Stirling in the 1980s. These techniques produce expressions for amplitudes that are perfectly suited for implementation in numerical simulations. Via the PARTONS software, the correctness of this new formulation has been tested by comparing the DVCS and TCS limits of DDVCS with independent calculations of DVCS and TCS.

## Key claim

Double deeply virtual Compton scattering (DDVCS) allows direct measurement of generalized parton distributions (GPDs) for \(x \neq \pm \xi\) at leading order in \(\alpha_s\), unlike deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS) and timelike Compton scattering (TCS) which restrict leading-order studies to the line \(x = \xi\). This advantage holds as long as both virtualities \(Q^2\) and \(Q^{\prime 2}\) are non-zero.

## Method

The authors computed the DDVCS amplitude using Kleiss-Stirling spinor techniques from the 1980s, which produce expressions directly suited for numerical simulation. They implemented the amplitude in the PARTONS software framework and validated the formulation by numerically comparing the DVCS limit (\(Q^{\prime 2} \to 0\)) and the TCS limit (\(Q^2 \to 0\)) of DDVCS against independent calculations of DVCS and TCS already present in PARTONS. The tests used the Goloskokov-Kroll GPD model at Born order, with renormalization and factorization scales set to \(\mu_R^2 = \mu_F^2 = Q^2 + Q^{\prime 2}\).

## Key numbers

- Imaginary part of the Compton form factor \(\mathcal{H}\) was computed as a function of skewness \(\xi\) for \(t = -0.15\) GeV\(^2\) and \(Q^2 = 1.5\) GeV\(^2\), showing smooth approach to the DVCS limit as \(Q^{\prime 2}\) decreases.
- DVCS limit test: pure DVCS cross-section compared with DDVCS at \(x_B = 0.2\), \(t = -0.25\) GeV\(^2\), \(Q^2 = 40\) GeV\(^2\), and incoming electron beam energy \(E = 160\) GeV.
- TCS limit test: pure TCS cross-section compared with DDVCS at \(x_B = 10^{-4}\), \(t = -0.25\) GeV\(^2\), \(Q^{\prime 2} = 33\) GeV\(^2\), \(\theta_L = 1.04\pi/4\) rad, and \(E = 160\) GeV.
- Bethe-Heitler contribution to DVCS limit tested at \(x_B = 0.04\), \(t = -0.1\) GeV\(^2\), \(Q^2 = 10\) GeV\(^2\), and \(E = 160\) GeV.
- Bethe-Heitler contribution to TCS limit tested at \(x_B = 10^{-4}\), \(t = -0.1\) GeV\(^2\), \(Q^{\prime 2} = 3\) GeV\(^2\), \(\theta_L = 1.04\pi/4\) rad, and \(E = 160\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier detailed phenomenological studies of DDVCS by Belitsky et al. (2003) and provides a new amplitude formulation suitable for modern experimental facilities. The authors note that near-future experiments at fixed-target facilities (JLab12, JLab20+) and electron-ion colliders (EIC) motivate this revisit. The new calculation enables predictions for cross-sections and asymmetries to assess the measurability of DDVCS, building on prior implementations of DVCS and TCS in PARTONS from Belitsky and Mueller (2014) and Berger et al. (2001).
