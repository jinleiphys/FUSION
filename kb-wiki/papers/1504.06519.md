# CMS physics highlights in the LHC Run 1

> The main physics results obtained by the CMS experiment during the first three years of operation of the CERN Large Hadron Collider (2010--2013, aka. Run 1) are summarized. The advances in our understanding of the fundamental particles and their interactions are succinctly reviewed under the following physics topics: (i) Quantum Chromodynamics, (ii) Quark Gluon Plasma, (iii) Electroweak interaction, (iv) Top quark, (v) Higgs boson, (vi) Flavour, (vii) Supersymmetry, (viii) Dark Matter, and (ix) other searches of physics beyond the Standard Model.

# CMS physics highlights in the LHC Run 1

## Key claim
The CMS experiment's Run 1 data (2010–2013) show no significant deviations from Standard Model predictions across nine major physics topics, despite the expectation that the LHC would address open fundamental problems such as mass generation, dark matter, and naturalness. The Higgs boson discovery and property measurements confirm the Standard Model, but the underlying puzzles that motivated the LHC remain unsolved.

## Method
The CMS Collaboration collected and analyzed data from proton-proton collisions at center-of-mass energies of 7 and 8 TeV (integrated luminosity 25 fb⁻¹), proton-lead collisions at 5.02 TeV per nucleon pair (34 nb⁻¹), and lead-lead collisions at 2.76 TeV per nucleon pair (170 μb⁻¹). The results are organized into nine physics categories: Quantum Chromodynamics, Quark Gluon Plasma, Electroweak interaction, Top quark, Higgs boson, Flavour, Supersymmetry, Dark Matter, and other beyond-Standard-Model searches.

## Key numbers
- Strong coupling αs measured up to energy scales Q ≈ 2 TeV, with current uncertainty around 0.6%
- In central lead-lead collisions, strongly-interacting particles are suppressed by up to a factor of seven in the nuclear modification factor RAA
- Top quark mass combined measurement: mt = 172.38 ± 0.67 GeV (0.4% uncertainty)
- Most precise single top mass measurement: mt = 172.04 ± 0.19 (stat+JSF) ± 0.75 (syst) GeV from lepton+jets channel
- Higgs boson mass: mH = 125.09 ± 0.21 ± 0.11 GeV (below 0.2% uncertainty)
- Higgs boson width constrained to ΓH < 22 MeV (95% CL), less than 5.4 times the Standard Model prediction
- B⁰s → μ⁺μ⁻ decay established with 6.2σ statistical significance, branching ratio compatible with Standard Model prediction of ≈ 10⁻⁸·⁵
- Gluino and squark masses excluded above ≈ 1 TeV; stop and sbottom masses above ≈ 0.5 TeV; electroweak gauginos and sleptons above ≈ 0.3 TeV
- Dark matter-nucleon cross section limits: σ(χ-N) ≲ 10⁻⁴⁰ cm² (spin-independent) and 10⁻⁴¹ cm² (spin-dependent) at 90% CL
- Quark compositeness scale excluded above Λ ≳ 15 TeV; ADD graviton scale above Λ ≳ 5–7 TeV; heavy gauge bosons (Z', W') excluded below masses 1.5–3.5 TeV; Randall-Sundrum extra-dimension scale above Λ ≈ 2.5 TeV; leptoquarks and fourth-generation quarks excluded below ≈ 0.6 TeV

## Context
This paper summarizes the complete CMS Run 1 physics program, extending precision measurements of the Standard Model and searches for new physics beyond what was achieved at previous colliders such as the Tevatron. The results confirm the Higgs boson discovery reported by CMS and ATLAS in 2012 (Chatrchyan et al., 2012) and provide the most stringent limits on supersymmetric particles, dark matter candidates, and other beyond-Standard-Model scenarios to date. The work enables future LHC Run 2 analyses at 14 TeV to focus on the remaining open questions: the hierarchy problem, the nature of dark matter, the matter-antimatter asymmetry, and color confinement.
