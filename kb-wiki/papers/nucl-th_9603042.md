# Composition and Structure of Protoneutron Stars

> We investigate the structure of neutron stars shortly after they are born, when the entropy per baryon is of order 1 or 2 and neutrinos are trapped on dynamical timescales. In all cases, the thermal effects for an entropy per baryon of order 2 or less are small when considering the maximum neutron star mass. Neutrino trapping, however, significantly changes the maximum mass due to the abundance of electrons. When matter is allowed to contain only nucleons and leptons, trapping decreases the maximum mass by an amount comparable to, but somewhat larger than, the increase due to finite entropy. When matter is allowed to contain strongly interacting negatively charged particles, in the form of strange baryons, a kaon condensate, or quarks, trapping instead results in an increase in the maximum mass of order $0.2M_\odot$, which adds to the effects of finite entropy. The presence of negatively-charged particles has two major implications. First, the value of the maximum mass will decrease during the early evolution of a neutron star as it loses trapped neutrinos, so that if a black hole forms, it either does so immediately after the bounce or it is delayed for a neutrino diffusion timescale of $\sim 10$ s. The latter case is most likely if the maximum mass of the hot star with trapped neutrinos is near $1.5M_\odot$. In the absence of negatively-charged hadrons, black hole formation would be due to accretion and therefore is likely to occur only immediately after bounce. Second, the appearance of hadronic negative charges results in a general softening of the equation of state that may be observable in the neutrino luminosities and average energies. Further, these additional negative charges decrease the electron fraction and may be observed in the relative excess of electron neutrinos compared to other neutrinos.

## Key claim

The paper argues that neutrino trapping in protoneutron stars can either decrease or increase the maximum mass depending on the composition of matter, with the effect being opposite in sign when negatively charged strongly interacting particles (hyperons, kaon condensates, or quarks) are present compared to when only nucleons and leptons are allowed.

## Method

The authors constructed equations of state for hot, dense matter using non-relativistic potential models (BPAL and Skyrme-like) and relativistic field theoretical models, incorporating thermal effects at entropy per baryon of 1-2 and neutrino trapping with lepton number conservation (Y_Le ~ 0.4). They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff hydrostatic equilibrium equations for protoneutron stars with compositions including nucleons only, hyperonic matter, kaon condensates, and quark-hadron mixed phases, comparing maximum masses between neutrino-trapped and neutrino-free configurations.

## Key numbers

- Neutrino trapping in matter with negatively charged strongly interacting particles increases the maximum mass by approximately 0.2 solar masses (0.2 M_sun) compared to the neutrino-free case
- The maximum mass of a hot star with trapped neutrinos near 1.5 M_sun is identified as the threshold for delayed black hole formation on a neutrino diffusion timescale of ~10 seconds
- The measured mass of PSR 1913+16 is 1.442 +/- 0.001 M_sun, used as a reference for the largest measured neutron star masses
- The coefficient lambda in the relation M_max(S) = M_max(0)[1 + lambda S^2] is approximately 10^-2 for the models studied
- The unshocked core immediately after bounce contains 0.5-0.7 M_sun in which neutrinos are trapped
- Neutrino diffusion deleptonization timescale is about 10-15 seconds
- The star becomes essentially transparent to neutrinos after approximately 50 seconds
- The crust surface temperature remains relatively warm at about 3 x 10^6 K for up to 100 years

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of cold neutron stars (Wiringa et al., 1988; Prakash, Ainsworth and Lattimer, 1988) to the hot, neutrino-trapped protoneutron star phase, building on dynamical calculations by Burrows and Lattimer (1986) that used schematic equations of state. The paper systematically investigates how the presence of strangeness-rich hyperons, kaon condensates (Kaplan and Nelson, 1986), and quark matter alters the maximum mass evolution, enabling predictions for whether black hole formation occurs immediately after bounce or is delayed by ~10 seconds, which has direct implications for interpreting neutrino signals from supernovae such as SN1987A.
