# High energy cosmic-ray interactions with particles from the Sun

> Cosmic-ray protons with energies above $10^{16}$ eV passing near the Sun may interact with photons emitted by the Sun and be excited to a $Δ^+$ resonance. When the $Δ^+$ decays, it produces pions which further decay to muons and photons which may be detected with terrestrial detectors. A flux of muons, photon pairs (from $π^0$ decay), or individual high-energy photons coming from near the Sun would be a rather striking signature, and the flux of these particles is a fairly direct measure of the flux of cosmic-ray nucleons, independent of the cosmic-ray composition. In a solid angle within $15^\circ$ around the Sun the flux of photon pairs is about $\SI{1.3e-3}{}$ particles/(km$^2\cdot$yr), while the flux of muons is about $\SI{0.33e-3}{}$ particles/(km$^2\cdot$yr). This is beyond the reach of current detectors like the Telescope Array, Auger, KASCADE-Grande or IceCube. However, the muon flux might be detectable by next-generation air shower arrays or neutrino detectors such as ARIANNA or ARA. We discuss the experimental prospects in some detail. Other cosmic-ray interactions occuring close to the Sun are also briefly discussed.

# High energy cosmic-ray interactions with particles from the Sun

## Key claim

Cosmic-ray protons above approximately 10^16 eV passing near the Sun can excite to a Δ^+ resonance via interaction with solar photons, producing pions that decay to muons and photons detectable at Earth; however, the predicted fluxes of about 1.3×10^−3 photon pairs/(km^2·yr) and 0.33×10^−3 muons/(km^2·yr) within 15° of the Sun are beyond the reach of current detectors like the Telescope Array, Auger, KASCADE-Grande, or IceCube, and may only be detectable by next-generation arrays such as ARIANNA or ARA.

## Method

The authors calculate the interaction probability for a cosmic-ray proton passing the Sun at a given impact parameter by integrating over the solar blackbody photon spectrum and measured photopion cross sections, including single-pion channels (pγ → pπ^0 and pγ → nπ^+) from the SAID Partial Wave Analysis and multi-pion channels from published data. The flux at Earth is obtained by integrating over cosmic-ray energies (assuming a power-law spectrum with normalization 3.5×10^24 eV^2/(m^2·s·sr) × E^−3 between the knee at 4×10^15 eV and the ankle near 10^19 eV) and over impact parameters out to a 15° angular separation, with muon survival probability included via exponential decay in flight. Additional calculations cover cosmic-ray interactions with the solar corona (using the Baumbach-Allen density profile and pp cross sections) and Bethe-Heitler e^+e^− pair production in the field of the cosmic ray.

## Key numbers

- Photon pair flux within 15° of the Sun: 1.3×10^−3 particles/(km^2·yr)
- Muon flux within 15° of the Sun: 0.33×10^−3 particles/(km^2·yr)
- Threshold energy for Δ^+(1232) production in head-on collision with a 1 eV optical photon: 1.6×10^17 eV
- Cosmic-ray knee energy assumed: 4×10^15 eV
- Cosmic-ray spectrum normalization for E > Eknee: 3.5×10^24 eV^2/(m^2·s·sr) × E^−3
- Solar temperature assumed: 5778 K
- Muon lifetime: 2.2 μs
- Pion lifetimes: 26 ns (π^±) and 84×10^−9 ns (π^0)
- Predicted multi-core shower observation rate for IceTop: 7×10^−6 yr^−1
- Predicted multi-core shower observation rate for KASCADE-Grande: 9×10^−7 yr^−1
- Pair production flux within 15° of the Sun: approximately 100 e^+ and e^− per km^2 per year, with energies almost all below 1 TeV

## Context

This work extends the study of cosmic-ray interactions with solar photons beyond the photodissociation of nuclei investigated by Lafebere et al. (2008), focusing instead on Δ^+ resonance production as a probe of the cosmic-ray nucleon flux independent of composition. The predicted fluxes are too low for current detectors such as the Telescope Array, Auger, KASCADE-Grande, and IceCube, but the muon signal might be detectable by next-generation neutrino detectors like ARIANNA and ARA, which have active volumes about 100 times larger than IceCube. The paper also briefly considers inelastic cosmic-ray interactions with the solar atmosphere (following earlier work by Seckel et al. (1991) for lower energies) and Bethe-Heitler pair production, finding these channels less promising for detection.
