# A machine learning study to identify spinodal clumping in high energy nuclear collisions

> The coordinate and momentum space configurations of the net baryon number in heavy ion collisions that undergo spinodal decomposition, due to a first-order phase transition, are investigated using state-of-the-art machine-learning methods. Coordinate space clumping, which appears in the spinodal decomposition, leaves strong characteristic imprints on the spatial net density distribution in nearly every event which can be detected by modern machine learning techniques. On the other hand, the corresponding features in the momentum distributions cannot clearly be detected, by the same machine learning methods, in individual events. Only a small subset of events can be systematically differentiated if only the momentum space information is available. This is due to the strong similarity of the two event classes, with and without spinodal decomposition. In such scenarios, conventional event-averaged observables like the baryon number cumulants signal a spinodal non-equilibrium phase transition. Indeed the third-order cumulant, the skewness, does exhibit a peak at the beam energy ($\mathrm{E}_{\mathrm{lab}}= 3-4$ A GeV), where the transient hot and dense system created in the heavy ion collision reaches the first-order phase transition.

## Key claim

Machine learning methods can identify spinodal clumping in coordinate-space net baryon density distributions in nearly every heavy-ion collision event, but the same methods fail to reliably detect corresponding features in momentum-space distributions on an event-by-event basis, with only a small subset of events showing distinguishable momentum-space signatures.

## Method

The authors simulated Pb+Pb collisions at a beam energy of 3.5 A GeV using relativistic ideal fluid dynamics augmented with a gradient term to model spinodal decomposition, comparing two equations of state: a mechanically unstable spinodal EoS and a Maxwell-constructed stable EoS. The initial state was generated with the non-equilibrium transport model UrQMD, and final-state particles were produced via the Cooper-Frye freeze-out procedure with either one test particle (TP=1, global flavor conservation) or twenty test particles (TP=20, near-local flavor conservation). Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) were trained on event-wise images of transverse net baryon density distributions in coordinate space and on momentum-space histograms, while point cloud networks (PCNs) were trained on lists of particle four-momenta and masses; additionally, a fully connected neural network was trained on event-by-event distributions of baryon-pair momentum differences, and principal component analysis (PCA) was applied to these distributions.

## Key numbers

* CNN classification accuracy on coordinate-space density images reached approximately 95% for events at beam energy E_lab = 3.5 A GeV, indicating spinodal features appear in nearly every event.
* CNN validation accuracy on momentum-space histograms was only about 52% for TP=20 and near 50% for TP=1, essentially no better than random guessing.
* PCN validation accuracy on particle momentum lists reached 54% for TP=20 and 52% for TP=1, only marginally above random.
* Fully connected neural network trained on baryon-pair momentum difference distributions achieved 55% accuracy for TP=20 and 52% for TP=1.
* The spinodal clusters formed with a characteristic size of 1-2 fm, governed by the gradient term strength parameter a = 0.033 fm.
* The third-order cumulant (skewness) of the net baryon number distribution exhibits a peak at beam energies E_lab = 3-4 A GeV, where the system reaches the first-order phase transition region.

## Context

This work extends prior fluid-dynamical studies of spinodal decomposition in heavy-ion collisions by Steinheimer et al. (2012, 2013, 2017) and applies machine-learning classification methods previously developed by Pang et al. (2016) for identifying equation-of-state effects. The finding that coordinate-space clumping appears in nearly every event confirms earlier suggestions from Steinheimer et al. (2017) and Paech et al. (2005), while the poor momentum-space classification demonstrates that conventional event-averaged observables like baryon number cumulants remain necessary for detecting spinodal signatures in experimental data. The results enable future work to focus on optimizing event-averaged fluctuation observables rather than relying on single-event momentum-space identification.
