# How Accurately Do We Know the Formation of Solar 8B?

> The large value of \S17 = 22.1 \xpm 0.6 eV-b, reported by the Seattle group, suggests a larger total \b8 solar neutrino flux. Together with the two high precision values quoted for \s34 it is either 20% or 9% larger than measured by SNO. While the accuracy of the Standard Solar Model has recently been revisited, precise nuclear inputs are still relevant, but a detailed examination of current data on \xs17 (as opposed to an examination of \S17 only) excludes quoting \S17 with sufficiently small uncertainty. In contrast to suggestions that \S17 is now known with the (impressive) accuracy of \xpm 3%, the exact value of \S17 is dependent on the choice of the data and the choice of theory used for extrapolation. In addition recent high precision results (including the Seattle data) on \xs17 which are in good agreement, still differ on the measured slopes, as does the theory, precluding an accurate extrapolation to zero energy of the consistent data. Using a common extrapolation of only the consistent high precision data, suggests a value of \S17 = 21.2 \xpm 0.5 eV-b, but a value equal to or smaller than 19.0 eV-b can not be excluded due to the uncertainty in the extrapolation, leading to an additional error of $^{+0.0}_{-3.0}$ eV-b. A proposal to remedy this situation is discussed.

# How Accurately Do We Know the Formation of Solar 8B?

## Key claim
The astrophysical S-factor S17(0) cannot be quoted with the claimed ±3% accuracy because its value depends on which data sets and which theoretical extrapolation model are chosen; using only the consistent high-precision data yields S17(0) = 21.2 ± 0.5 eV-b, but a value as low as 19.0 eV-b cannot be excluded due to extrapolation uncertainty, adding an additional error of +0.0/-3.0 eV-b.

## Method
The paper examines recent direct capture measurements of the 7Be(p,γ)8B reaction using 7Be targets (Seattle, Weizmann, Orsay, Bochum groups) and Coulomb dissociation measurements of 8B (GSI1, GSI2, MSU groups). The author compares the measured slopes of the astrophysical S-factor over the energy range 200-1300 keV and extrapolates to zero energy using two different theoretical models: the calculation of Descouvemont and Baye and the potential model of Typel. Only the high-precision (±3-5%) consistent data from Seattle, Weizmann, GSI1, and GSI2 are used for the central value, while discrepant lower-precision data are excluded.

## Key numbers
- S17(0) = 22.1 ± 0.6 eV-b reported by the Seattle group
- S17(0) = 21.2 ± 0.5 eV-b from the average of consistent high-precision data (Seattle, Weizmann, GSI1, GSI2) using the Descouvemont and Baye extrapolation
- Additional extrapolation uncertainty of +0.0/-3.0 eV-b, allowing values as low as 19.0 eV-b
- Measured slopes: Seattle 5.1 ± 0.6 eV-b/MeV, Weizmann 6.7 ± 1.2 eV-b/MeV, GSI 9.5 ± 2.5 eV-b/MeV
- SNO neutral current flux: φ_NC = 5.21 ± 0.27 (stat) ± 0.38 (syst) × 10^6 cm^-2 sec^-1
- Using the Seattle S17(0) value with the larger S34(0) = 572 ± 26 eV-b gives a predicted total 8B flux 20% larger than SNO; using the smaller S34(0) = 507 ± 16 eV-b reduces the discrepancy to 9%
- The SSM 8B flux prediction uncertainty was re-evaluated from ±6% to ±23% due to unknown solar chemical composition

## Context
This work extends the review by Adelberger et al. (1998) and responds to claims that S17(0) is known with ±3% accuracy. The paper shows that the situation is analogous to the earlier case of the 2H(2H,γ)4He reaction, where a neglected non-d-wave contribution altered the extrapolated value by a factor of 32. The author proposes a new measurement of the slope using the ratio of the 7Be(p,γ)8B cross section to elastic Coulomb scattering, which could be performed with radioactive 7Be beams at CERN-ISOLDE. This would enable quoting S17(0) with ±5% or better uncertainty.
