# Resonances in $^{12}$C and $^{24}$Mg: what do we learn from a microscopic cluster theory?

> We discuss resonance properties in three-body systems, with examples on $^{12}{\rm C}$ and $^{24}{\rm Mg}$. We use a microscopic cluster model, where the generator coordinate is defined in the hyperspherical formalism. The $^{12}{\rm C}$ nucleus is described by an $öÝ+öÝ+öÝ$ structure, whereas $^{24}{\rm Mg}$ is considered as an $^{16}{\rm O}+öÝ+öÝ$ system. We essentially pay attention to resonances. We review various techniques which may extend variational methods to resonances. We consider $0^+$ and $2^+$ states in $^{12}{\rm C}$ and $^{24}{\rm Mg}$. We show that the r.m.s. radius of a resonance is strongly sensitive to the variational basis. This has consequences for the Hoyle state ($0^+_2$ state in $^{12}{\rm C}$) whose radius has been calculated or measured in several works. In $^{24}{\rm Mg}$, we identify two $0^+$ resonances slightly below the three-body threshold.

## Key claim

The bound-state approximation commonly used for narrow resonances can give stable energies but produces r.m.s. radii that are strongly sensitive to the variational basis and diverge as the basis size increases, explaining the wide spread of reported radii for the Hoyle state in carbon-12.

## Method

The author uses a microscopic three-cluster model within the Resonating Group Method and Generator Coordinate Method, with wave functions expanded in hyperspherical coordinates. Carbon-12 is described as an alpha+alpha+alpha system and magnesium-24 as an oxygen-16+alpha+alpha system. The Minnesota nucleon-nucleon interaction (with mixing parameter u=0.9487) is used for carbon-12, and the Volkov V2 force (with Majorana exchange parameter M=0.624) for magnesium-24. The bound-state approximation is applied to compute energies and r.m.s. radii for increasing maximum generator coordinate values up to 24 fm for carbon-12 and 19 fm for magnesium-24.

## Context

This work extends microscopic three-cluster calculations previously applied to carbon-12 by Uegaki (1977), Kamimura (1981), and Descouvemont (1987), and to magnesium-24 by Descouvemont (1989) and Itagaki, Ikeda, and Kato (2011). The paper demonstrates that r.m.s. radii of resonances computed with the bound-state approximation are unreliable, explaining discrepancies in the literature on the Hoyle state radius reviewed by Freer and Fynbo (2014). The stability of the radius against basis extension is proposed as a practical criterion to distinguish physical resonances from continuum pseudostates in three-body cluster models.
