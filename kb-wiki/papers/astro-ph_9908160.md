# Primordial Black Holes and Primordial Nucleosynthesis I: Effects of Hadron Injection from Low Mass Holes

> We investigate the influence of hadron injection from evaporating primordial black holes (PBHs) in the early stage of the primordial nucleosynthesis era (t = 10^{-3} - 10^4 sec). The emitted quark-antiquark pairs or gluons immediately fragment into a lot of hadrons and scatter off the thermal plasma which is constituted by photons, electrons and nucleons. For the relatively low mass holes we point out that the dominant effect is the inter-conversion between ambient proton and neutron through the strong interaction induced by the emitted hadrons. Even after the freeze-out time of the week interactions between neutron and proton, more neutrons are produced and the synthesized light element abundances could be drastically changed. Comparing the theoretical predictions with the observational data, we constrain the PBH's density and their lifetime. We obtain the upper bound for PBH's initial mass fraction, β< 10^{-20} for 10^8 g < M < 10^{10} g, and β< 10^{-22} for 10^{10} g < M < 3 * 10^{10} g.

# Primordial Black Holes and Primordial Nucleosynthesis I: Effects of Hadron Injection from Low Mass Holes

## Key claim

The dominant effect of evaporating low-mass primordial black holes (PBHs) on Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) is the inter-conversion between ambient protons and neutrons via strong interactions induced by hadrons from quark-gluon jets, which can increase the neutron-to-proton ratio even after weak-interaction freeze-out; this effect is conditional on PBH masses below approximately 10^10 g, where the black hole temperature exceeds the QCD scale and hadron injection rather than photodissociation dominates.

## Method

The authors model PBH evaporation using Hawking radiation with a temperature T_BH = 1.06 M_10^{-1} TeV, where quarks and gluons are emitted as asymptotically free particles that fragment into hadron jets, with charged-particle multiplicities extrapolated from e+e- collider data up to sqrt(s) ~ 172 GeV. They compute the time evolution of neutron and proton number densities by adding a term to the standard BBN equations that accounts for hadron-induced nucleon conversion, using thermally averaged strong-interaction cross sections for pions, kaons, and nucleon-antinucleon pairs from Reno and Seckel (1990), and compare the resulting light element abundances (D, 4He, 7Li) with observational data to constrain the PBH initial mass fraction beta.

## Key numbers

- PBH lifetime: tau_BH = 435 M_10^3 sec, where M_10 = M / 10^10 g; equivalently M = 1.32 x 10^9 (tau_BH / 1 sec)^{1/3} g
- PBH temperature: T_BH = 1.06 M_10^{-1} TeV
- Upper bound on PBH initial mass fraction: beta < 10^{-20} for 10^8 g < M < 10^10 g
- Upper bound on PBH initial mass fraction: beta < 10^{-22} for 10^10 g < M < 3 x 10^10 g
- Average charged-particle multiplicity fit: <N_ch> = 1.73 + 0.268 exp(1.42 sqrt(ln(s / Lambda^2))) with Lambda = 1 GeV, chi^2 = 90.3 for 93 data points
- Number of hadrons per two jets at sqrt(s) = 91.2 GeV: n^{pi+} = 14.1, n^{pi-} = 14.1, n^{K+} = 1.67, n^{K-} = 1.67, n^{KL} = 1.19, n^{p} = n^{bar{p}} = 0.772, n^{n} = n^{bar{n}} = 0.772
- Strong interaction cross sections: <sigma v>^{pi+}_{n -> p} = 1.7 mb; <sigma v>^{K-}_{p -> n} = 31 C^K(T) mb; <sigma v>^{KL}_{n -> p} = 7 mb; <sigma v>^{p bar{p}}_{n -> p} = 28 mb

## Context

This work revises earlier PBH-BBN constraints from Vainer and Nasel'skii (1978), Miyama and Sato (1978), Zel'dovich et al. (1977), Vainer, Dryzhakova, and Nasel'skii (1978), and Lindley (1980) by incorporating the modern QCD picture of hadron jet fragmentation from PBH evaporation rather than direct nucleon or meson emission. The authors follow the treatment of hadron injection from long-lived decaying particles by Reno and Seckel (1990) and apply it to PBHs for the first time. This paper (Part I) addresses only low-mass PBHs where hadron-induced neutron-proton conversion dominates; photodissociation of 4He and other effects from higher-mass PBHs are deferred to a subsequent publication (Kohri and Yokoyama, 1999, Part II).
