# Splitting Sensitivity of the Ground and 7.6 eV Isomeric States of 229Th

> The lowest-known excited state in nuclei is the 7.6 eV isomer of 229Th. This energy is within the range of laser-based investigations that could allow accurate measurements of possible temporal variation of this energy splitting. This in turn could probe temporal variation of the fine-structure constant or other parameters in the nuclear Hamiltonian. We investigate the sensitivity of this transition energy to these quantities. We find that the two states are predicted to have identical deformations and thus the same Coulomb energies within the accuracy of the model (viz., within roughly 30 keV). We therefore find no enhanced sensitivity to variation of the fine-structure constant. In the case of the strong interaction the energy splitting is found to have a complicated dependence on several parameters of the interaction, which makes an accurate prediction of sensitivity to temporal changes of fundamental constants problematical. Neither the strong- nor Coulomb-interaction contributions to the energy splitting of this doublet can be constrained within an accuracy better than a few tens of keV, so that only upper limits can be set on the possible sensitivity to temporal variations of the fundamental constants.

## Key claim

The ground and 7.6 eV isomeric states of 229Th are predicted to have identical deformations and therefore the same Coulomb energies within the model accuracy (roughly 30 keV), so there is no enhanced sensitivity to variation of the fine-structure constant; for the strong interaction, the energy splitting depends on several parameters in a complicated way, so only upper limits can be set on sensitivity to temporal variations of fundamental constants.

## Method

The authors used the finite-range microscopic-macroscopic model (FRDM) to calculate the ground-state deformation and quasi-particle energy of 229Th by minimizing the potential energy on a fine deformation grid, varying all four shape parameters (quadrupole, octupole, hexadecapole, and hexacontatetrapole) in steps of 0.001. They then examined the sensitivity of the predicted doublet splitting to four global strong-interaction parameters (depth and diffuseness of the single-particle potential, pairing strength, and neutron spin-orbit strength) by varying each by plus or minus 10 percent.

## Key numbers

- The three lowest neutron quasi-particle states in 229Th are predicted to have deformations: epsilon_2 = 0.170, epsilon_3 = 0.000, epsilon_4 = -0.084, and epsilon_6 = -0.002.
- The ground-state (5/2^+) and isomer (3/2^+) are predicted to lie at 13.6 keV and 21.6 keV above a 5/2^- state, respectively.
- The predicted energy separation of the 3/2^+ and 5/2^+ doublet is 8.3 keV, compared to the actual isomer excitation energy of 7.6 eV.
- Allowing epsilon_2 to vary by the mesh-parameter step size (0.001) produces a variation in the Coulomb energy difference of approximately 30 keV, setting the upper limit for variations in the transition frequency relative to changes in the fine-structure constant.
- For the 235U doublet (observed splitting 76 eV), the predicted energy splitting was 29 keV, with deformations epsilon_2=0.205, epsilon_3=0, epsilon_4=-0.07, and epsilon_6=0.025.
- Varying the depth of the single-particle potential (V_0) by plus or minus 10 percent changed the predicted 229Th doublet splitting from 8.8 keV to -4.4 keV (inverted) or 31.6 keV.
- Varying the diffuseness (a) by plus or minus 10 percent gave splittings of 25.4 keV and 2.0 keV.
- Varying the neutron spin-orbit strength (lambda_n) by plus or minus 10 percent gave splittings of -44.2 keV and 29 keV.
- Varying the pairing strength (G) by plus or minus 10 percent gave splittings of 8.7 keV and 9.2 keV.

## Context

This work extends earlier suggestions by Flambaum (2006) and others that the 229Th isomer could serve as a sensitive probe of temporal variation of the fine-structure constant or quark masses, and directly addresses the nuclear-structure assumptions in those proposals. The authors find that, contrary to some earlier expectations, the near degeneracy is accidental and the Coulomb energy difference is zero within model uncertainties, contradicting the enhanced sensitivity claimed in some prior work (Flambaum, 2006; variation1). The results imply that only upper limits can be set on temporal variation of fundamental constants from this system, and that any experimental program must account for the keV-scale theoretical uncertainties in the nuclear structure.
