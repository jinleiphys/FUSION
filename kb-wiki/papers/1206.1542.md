# Nuclear Level Densities in the Constant-Spacing Model

> A new method to calculate level densities for non-interacting Fermions within the constant-spacing model with a finite number of states is developed. We show that asymptotically (for large numbers of particles or holes) the densities have Gaussian form. We improve on the Gaussian distribution by using analytical expressions for moments higher than the second. Comparison with numerical results shows that the resulting sixth-moment approximation is excellent except near the boundaries of the spectra and works globally for all particle/hole numbers and all excitation energies.

## Key claim

The authors show that level densities for non-interacting Fermions in the constant-spacing model become asymptotically Gaussian for large particle or hole numbers, but the sixth-moment approximation they develop is excellent only except near the boundaries of the spectra, where numerical evaluation remains necessary.

## Method

The authors calculate level densities for spinless non-interacting Fermions distributed over a finite number of equally spaced single-particle states, using an occupation-number representation to derive analytical expressions for the low even moments (second, fourth, and sixth) of the density distribution. They then construct an approximate density function using these moments in an exponential form with polynomial arguments up to sixth order, and compare this sixth-moment approximation to exact numerical results obtained by direct combinatorial counting.

## Key numbers

- For b = 51 equally spaced single-particle states and p ≈ 25 particles, the maximum value of the dimensionless level density ω_b(p, ε) reaches approximately 10^12 at the centroid energy.
- The sixth-moment approximation deviates by 10% or more from exact values only near the spectral boundaries, as shown by white dashed lines in the contour plot for b = 51.
- At the lower spectrum boundary ε_min = (1/2)p(p+1) for b = 51, the sixth-moment approximation overestimates the exact value (which should be approximately 1) by one to two orders of magnitude.
- For the Bethe formula comparison with u = 51 and A = 41, the exact calculation shows that the Bethe formula underestimates the level density below the crossing point and overestimates it above.
- For u = 250 and A = 200 (mimicking a heavy nucleus), the crossing point between the Bethe formula and the sixth-moment approximation occurs at excitation energies around 200 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the Bethe (1936) approach to nuclear level densities, which used the Darwin-Fowler method but neglected finite particle number and finite single-particle state limitations. It also improves upon the work of Ericson (1960), Bohning (1970), Williams (1971), and others who calculated particle-hole state densities using contour integrals that become increasingly involved at high excitation energies. The method enables calculation of level densities for medium-weight and heavy nuclei at excitation energies up to several hundred MeV, which is needed for modeling laser-induced nuclear reactions at facilities like the Extreme Light Infrastructure (ELI). The authors note that the constant-spacing model becomes unrealistic at high excitation energies because the actual single-particle level density increases with energy due to shell structure, and they propose extending their approach by dividing the energy interval into sections with different constant spacings.
