# Atomic Nuclei with Tetrahedral and Octahedral Symmetries

> We present possible manifestations of octahedral and tetrahedral symmetries in nuclei. These symmetries are associated with the OhD and TdD double point groups. Both of them have very characteristic finger-prints in terms of the nucleonic level properties - unique in the Fermionic universe. The tetrahedral symmetry leads to the four-fold degeneracies in the nucleonic spectra; it does not preserve the parity. The octahedral symmetry leads to the four-fold degeneracies in the nucleonic spectra as well but it does preserve the parity. Microscopic predictions have been obtained using mean-field theory based on the relativistic equations and confirmed by using 'traditional' Schroedinger equation formalism. Calculations are performed in multidimensional deformation spaces using newly designed algorithms. We discuss some experimental fingerprints of the hypothetical new symmetries and possibilities of their verification through experiments.

## Key claim

The paper argues that tetrahedral (\(T_d^D\)) and octahedral (\(O_h^D\)) double point-group symmetries, which produce four-fold degeneracies in single-nucleon levels, may exist in deformed nuclei and generate shell gaps comparable in size to those of spherical magic nuclei; however, the authors hedge that the existence of these symmetries is currently based exclusively on theoretical grounds and that experimental verification remains an open question.

## Method

The authors used mean-field theory based on relativistic (Dirac) equations and confirmed the results with the traditional Schroedinger-equation formalism, employing the Strutinsky Woods-Saxon method for total potential energy calculations. They modeled nuclear surfaces with spherical-harmonic expansions constrained by the group-theoretical conditions for octahedral and tetrahedral symmetries, and performed calculations in multidimensional deformation spaces using newly designed algorithms that minimize potential energies in arbitrary subspaces of the multipole expansion.

## Key numbers

- Tetrahedral shell closures predicted at proton numbers \(Z_t = 16, 20, 32, 40, 56, 70, 90, 100, 112, 126\) and neutron numbers \(N_t = 16, 20, 32, 40, 56, 70, 90, 100, 112, 136\)
- Octahedral single-particle spectra show gaps exceeding 3 MeV at Z=70 and N=114, described as "among the strongest known in nuclear structure physics"
- Tetrahedral gaps in the Actinide mass range (around \(^{226}\)Th) are of the order of 2 MeV for first-order tetrahedral deformation
- Excitation energies of octahedral minima vary from about 3 MeV to about 12 MeV depending on neutron number
- Excitation energies of tetrahedral minima vary between (2.5-3) MeV and about 10 MeV

## Context

This work extends the earlier tetrahedral-shape hypothesis of LiD96 and the re-analysis by JDA02, and contrasts with the \(\mathcal{C}_4\)-symmetry hypothesis of IHB96 for which no consistent experimental evidence exists. The paper introduces octahedral symmetry as a new possibility alongside tetrahedral symmetry, and enables future experimental searches for these exotic nuclear shapes, particularly through the identification of parity-doublet rotational bands and characteristic decay patterns. The authors note that self-consistent Hartree-Fock calculations (e.g., DDu01) can converge to the predicted highly symmetric solutions, reproducing the group-theoretical multipole proportions to computer accuracy.
