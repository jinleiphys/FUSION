# Positrons at JLab - Advancing Nuclear Science in Hall B

> In this talk I address two high impact physics programs that require the use of polarized and unpolarized positron beams in addition to using electron beams of the same energy. First, I address what will be gained from using positron beams in addition to electron beams in the extraction of the Compton Form Factors (CFFs) and generalized parton distributions (GPDs) from Deeply Virtual Compton Scattering (DVCS) on a proton target. As a second high impact science program I discuss an experimental scenario using unpolarized positrons to measure elastic scattering on protons in an effort to determine definitively the 2-photon exchange contributions in order to resolve a longstanding discrepancy in the determination of the proton's electric and magnetic form factors.

## Key claim
Using both polarized electron and polarized positron beams at JLab's 11 GeV energy would allow separation of the real and imaginary parts of the DVCS Compton amplitude across the full kinematic range, avoiding the limited kinematic coverage imposed by the Rosenbluth technique when only electron beams are available. For the elastic scattering program, a dedicated CLAS12 configuration with reversed detection capabilities could measure the 2-photon exchange contribution at higher $Q^2$ (2–10 GeV$^2$) and lower $\epsilon$ ($<0.6$) than previous experiments, potentially resolving the proton form factor discrepancy.

## Method
The author proposes two experimental programs using the CLAS12 detector in Hall B with both electron and positron beams at 11 GeV. For DVCS, the differential cross section structure for polarized leptons on unpolarized and polarized proton targets is analyzed, showing that the charge-dependent interference term $\sigma_{INT}$ (related to the real part of the Compton amplitude) can only be extracted using the combination of electron and positron beams. For elastic scattering, a modified CLAS12 configuration is described where the central detector detects electrons/positrons at polar angles 40–125° and the forward detector detects recoil protons at 7–35°, enabling coverage of $\epsilon < 0.6$ and $Q^2$ up to 10 GeV$^2$.

## Key numbers
- For DVCS: projected 1000-hour run with positron beam current 8 nA and luminosity $L = 2 \times 10^{34}$ cm$^{-2}$sec$^{-1}$ at beam polarization $P=0.6$; electron luminosity $L = 10 \times 10^{34}$ cm$^{-2}$sec$^{-1}$ at $P=0.8$
- For elastic scattering: estimated event rates per hour at beam energies 2.2, 4.4, and 6.6 GeV, with statistical precision $\sigma_N / N < 1\%$ achievable within 10 hours at lowest energy and within 1000 hours at highest energy
- For elastic scattering: assumed positron beam current (unpolarized) $I_{e^+} \approx 60$ nA, with 5 cm liquid H$_2$ target and luminosity $0.8 \times 10^{35}$ cm$^{-2}$sec$^{-1}$
- Previous 2-photon exchange experiments (VEPP-3, CLAS, Olympus) covered $Q^2 < 2$ GeV$^2$ and $\epsilon > 0.5$; the combined analysis rejected the $\delta_{\gamma\gamma} = 0$ hypothesis at 99.5% confidence

## Context
This talk extends the DVCS program at JLab that has already demonstrated feasibility with 4–6 GeV electron beams (Jo 2015, Pisano 2015, Seder 2014) and the GPD extraction techniques of Guidal (2013) and Kumericki (2012). The elastic scattering program directly addresses the discrepancy between Rosenbluth separation and polarization transfer measurements of $G_E/G_M$ (reviewed by Afanasev 2017), following three recent 2-photon exchange experiments (VEPP-3 at Novosibirsk, CLAS at JLab, Olympus at DESY) that were limited to $Q^2 < 2$ GeV$^2$. The proposed measurements would enable definitive determination of 2-photon exchange contributions at higher $Q^2$ and lower $\epsilon$ than previously accessible.
