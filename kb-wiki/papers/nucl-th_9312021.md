# Meson phase space density from interferometry

> The interferometric analysis of meson correlations provides a measure of the average phase space density of the mesons in the final state. This quantity is a useful indicator of the statistical properties of the system, and it can be extracted with a minimum of model assumptions. Values obtained from recent measurements are consistent with the thermal value, but do not rule out superradiance effects.

## Key claim

The average phase space density of pions extracted from two-particle interferometry is consistent with a local statistical equilibrium at chemical freezeout, but the possibility of superradiance effects cannot be ruled out, and the analysis includes pions from long-lived resonance decays that if subtracted would raise the density.

## Method

The author derives an expression for the average phase space density of mesons directly from Pratt's interferometric formula, integrating the two-particle correlation function over momentum difference and normalizing to the particle number. The formula is then evaluated using published Gaussian parameterizations of correlation data from the NA44 experiment for S+Pb collisions at midrapidity, with source sizes and transverse slope parameters taken from the experimental fits.

## Key numbers

- For massless bosons in thermal equilibrium, the average phase space density is \(\langle f \rangle_T = \zeta(2)/\zeta(3)-1 \approx 0.37\).
- For pions at a chemical freezeout temperature of 200 MeV, the thermal average phase space density is \(\langle f \rangle_{m,T} \approx 0.15\).
- From NA44 data for S+Pb \(\rightarrow \pi^+ +\)X at midrapidity: \(\lambda \approx 0.4\), \(R_t \approx 6.0\) fm, \(R_L \approx 6.0\) fm, \(dn/dy \approx 40\), and transverse slope \(T_t \approx 187\) MeV/c.
- The extracted average phase space density from these parameters is \(\langle f \rangle_{dy} \approx 0.07\)–\(0.16\), with the range coming from experimental errors combined quadratically.

## Context

This work extends the interferometric analysis of Pratt (1984) to directly extract the phase space density, a quantity that tests whether the system reached local statistical equilibrium. The extracted values are consistent with thermal expectations for freezeout temperatures of 100–200 MeV, but the author notes that NA35 (Baechler, 1992) found somewhat smaller source sizes, indicating experimental disagreement. The result makes it unlikely that abundant long-lived quark-gluon plasma droplets are produced, consistent with lattice QCD expectations of no strong first-order phase transition (Brown et al., 1990).
