# New sum rule for the nuclear magnetic polarizability

> I extend the well-known photonuclear sum rule that relates the strength of the photoexcitation of the giant dipole resonance in a nucleus to the number of elementary scatterers-nucleons to the case of virtual photons. The new sum rule relates the size of the magnetic polarizability of a nucleus to the slope of the transverse virtual photoabsorption cross section integrated over the energy in the nuclear range. I check this sum rule for the deuteron where necessary data is available, discuss possible applications and connection with other sum rules postulated in the literature.

## Key claim

The paper derives a new sum rule that relates the nuclear magnetic polarizability to the slope of the transverse virtual photoabsorption cross section integrated over the nuclear energy range, extending the well-known Levinger-Bethe sum rule for real photons to the case of virtual photons. The sum rule is approximate and relies on a clear separation between nuclear and hadronic energy scales, with neglected contributions from nucleon polarizabilities, nucleon charge radii, and recoil effects estimated to be small for light nuclei.

## Method

The author starts from the once-subtracted dispersion relation for the forward Compton scattering amplitude T1 and separates the nuclear and hadronic contributions by evaluating the dispersion relation at an intermediate energy scale (nu_infinity approximately 70 MeV above breakup threshold) where nuclear excitations are exhausted but nucleon structure is not yet resolved. By equating the low-energy expansion of the nuclear amplitude with the sum of free nucleon amplitudes at that scale, and taking the first derivative with respect to Q^2 at Q^2=0, the sum rule for the nuclear magnetic polarizability beta_M^nucl is obtained. The sum rule is tested on the deuteron using a parametrization of quasielastic D(e,e')pn data from Carlson (2013) that covers Q^2 from 0.005 to 3 GeV^2.

## Key numbers

* For the deuteron, the sum rule yields beta_M^d = 0.096(15) fm^3, compared to the model-based expectation of beta_M^d = 0.072(5) fm^3 from effective field theory and potential model calculations.
* The Levinger-Bethe sum rule for the deuteron gives a right-hand side value of 1.007, in excellent agreement with the expected value NZ = 1.
* For lead, the nuclear charge radius contribution to the sum rule is approximately -0.5 fm^3, while the nucleonic contributions are about 0.08 fm^3; the sum alpha_E + beta_M for lead is approximately 14.5 fm^3.
* The two-photon exchange contribution to the 2S Lamb shift in muonic deuterium is recalculated as Delta E_2S^Tot = -1.945(740)(40) meV, compared to the previous value of -1.750(740) meV from Carlson (2013).
* The extracted deuteron charge radius would shift by delta R_d = 0.007 fm if the new sum-rule-based subtraction function is used.

## Context

This work extends the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn-Levinger-Bethe sum rule (Levinger, 1957; Gell-Mann, Goldberger, and Thirring, 1954) from real photons to virtual photons, providing a model-independent relation for the nuclear magnetic polarizability that complements the Baldin sum rule (Baldin, 1960) which fixes the sum of electric and magnetic polarizabilities. The sum rule is applied to calculate the subtraction function contribution to the Lamb shift in muonic deuterium, offering an alternative to the minimalist assumption of Carlson (2013) that the Q^2-dependence of the deuteron magnetic polarizability resembles that of the charge form factor. The method can be extended to other light muonic atoms and to calculations of two-photon exchange in dark matter detection, and it contradicts an earlier sum rule for the electric polarizability proposed by Bernabeu and Jarlskog (1973) which the author argues is invalid due to the use of an unsubtracted dispersion relation.
