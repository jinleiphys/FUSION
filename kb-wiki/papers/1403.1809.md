# Elastic hadron scattering and optical theorem

> All contemporary phenomenological models of elastic hadronic scattering have been based in principle on the assumption of optical theorem validity that has been overtaken from optics. It will be shown that the given theorem which has not been actually proved in particle physics cannot be applied to short-ranged strong interactions. The analysis of corresponding collision experiments is to be done under new basic assumptions. The actual progress in description of hadronic collision processes might then exist only if the initial states are specified on the basis of impact parameter values of colliding particles and probability dependence on this parameter is established, without limiting corresponding conclusion by optical theorem validity.

## Key claim

The optical theorem, which has been assumed valid in all contemporary phenomenological models of elastic hadronic scattering, cannot be applied to short-ranged strong interactions because all attempts to prove it in particle physics have been based on assumptions that do not hold for hadronic collisions; the theorem has never been actually proved for this domain.

## Method

The authors analyze two standard derivations of the optical theorem, one based on S-matrix unitarity and the other on wave-optical scattering theory, and identify the specific assumptions that fail for short-ranged strong interactions. They then propose an alternative description in which initial and final states belong to mutually orthogonal Hilbert subspaces, with collision probabilities expressed as functions of impact parameter b, and apply this model to experimental elastic proton-proton differential cross-section data at 52.8 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Elastic proton-proton differential cross-section data at center-of-mass energy 52.8 GeV were used to test the proposed impact-parameter-based model (preliminary form).
- The analysis demonstrates that the optical theorem leads to arbitrary values of total cross section for short-ranged strong interactions, with the arbitrariness increasing at higher energies where inelastic processes exist.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier critique of the optical theorem (Lokajicek, 2013) and their impact-parameter description of elastic collisions (Kundrat, 2002; Kaspar, 2011). It contradicts the standard approach found in textbooks such as Barone (2002), which assumes optical theorem validity for hadronic scattering. The paper enables a new ontological description of hadronic collisions based on impact parameter distributions, without the constraint of the optical theorem, and opens the possibility of extracting new characteristics of proton structure from elastic scattering data.
