# Reaction Theory

> Background: Nuclear reactions are complex, with a large number of possible channels. Understanding how different channels contribute to a given reaction is investigated by perturbing the continuous spectrum. Purpose: To develop tools to investigate reaction mechanisms by identifying the contributions from each reaction channel. Method: Cluster decomposition methods, along with the spectral theory of proper subsystem problems, is used to identify the part of the nuclear Hamiltonian responsible for scattering into each channel. Results: The result is an expression of the nuclear Hamiltonian as a sum over all scattering channels of channel Hamiltonians. Each channel Hamiltonian is constructed from solutions of proper subsystem problems. Retaining any subset of channel Hamiltonians results in a truncated Hamiltonian where the scattering wave functions for the retained channels differ from the wave functions of the full Hamiltonian by $N$-body correlations. The scattering operator for the truncated Hamiltonian satisfies an optical theorem in the retained channels. Because different channel Hamiltonians do not commute, how they interact determines their contribution to the full dynamics.

## Key claim

The paper develops a formal method to decompose the full nuclear Hamiltonian into a sum of non-commuting "channel Hamiltonians," each constructed solely from proper subsystem solutions, such that retaining any subset of channel Hamiltonians yields a truncated Hamiltonian whose scattering wave functions agree with the exact ones up to (but not including) N-body correlations, and whose scattering operator satisfies an optical theorem in the retained channels.

## Method

The authors use cluster decomposition methods and the spectral theory of proper subsystem problems to identify the part of the nuclear Hamiltonian responsible for scattering into each channel. They express the full N-body Hamiltonian as a linear combination of non-commuting self-adjoint operators (channel Hamiltonians), each built from solutions of proper subsystem bound-state and scattering problems. Time-dependent scattering theory is then used to show that each channel Hamiltonian has scattering solutions in that channel, and that sums of subsets of channel Hamiltonians produce truncated Hamiltonians satisfying an optical theorem in the retained channels.

## Key numbers

- The combinatoric factor relating the operator decomposition to the cluster expansion is given by \( \mathcal{C}_a = (-)^{n_a} (n_a-1)! \), with the property \( \sum_{a \in \mathcal{P}_N'} \mathcal{C}_a = 1 \), where \( n_a \) is the number of clusters in partition \( a \) and \( \mathcal{P}_N' \) is the set of all partitions of N particles excluding the 1-cluster partition.
- The Möbius function on the partition lattice is given by \( \Delta^{-1}_{a \supseteq b} = (-)^{n_a} \prod_{i=1}^{n_a} (-)^{n_{b_i}} (n_{b_i}-1)! \) when \( a \supseteq b \), and zero otherwise, where \( n_{b_i} \) is the number of clusters of \( b \) in the \( i^{th} \) cluster of \( a \).

## Context

This work extends the formal solution presented in Polyzou (1978), which focused on constructing few-body models of reactions dominated by a small number of few-body channels but was largely academic due to computational limitations at the time. The present paper provides an alternate treatment of that method, including an alternate proof of the optical theorem and a demonstration using time-dependent scattering theory that approximate scattering wave functions in retained channels agree with exact ones up to N-body correlations. The method is applicable to any nuclear Hamiltonian, including those with many-body interactions generated by effective field theory (Epelbaum, 2002; Epelbaum, 1999) or scattering equivalences (Ekstein, 1960; Glockle, 1990; Bogner, 2006; Polyzou), and it enables systematic investigation of how individual reaction channels contribute to nuclear binding, decays, and cross sections.
