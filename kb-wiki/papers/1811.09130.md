# Advanced statistical methods to fit nuclear models

> We discuss advanced statistical methods to improve parameter estimation of nuclear models. In particular, using the Liquid Drop Model for nuclear binding energies, we show that the area around the global $χ^2$ minimum can be efficiently identified using Gaussian Process Emulation. We also demonstrate how Markov-chain Monte-Carlo sampling is a valuable tool for visualising and analysing the associated multidimensional likelihood surface.

## Key claim

Gaussian Process Emulation combined with the Expected Improvement criterion can efficiently identify the global χ² minimum of a nuclear model, but the method is demonstrated only for a unimodal likelihood surface using the Liquid Drop Model, and the authors note that Markov-chain Monte-Carlo may not be efficient for multimodal surfaces.

## Method

The authors used the Liquid Drop Model (LDM) with five adjustable parameters (a_v, a_s, a_c, a_a, a_p) to fit nuclear binding energies from the AME2012 database, excluding nuclei with A<16 and those with experimental errors larger than 100 keV. They applied Gaussian Process Emulation with a squared exponential covariance kernel to emulate the χ² surface, combined with the Expected Improvement criterion to iteratively select new points in parameter space, and used No-U-Turn Sampler Markov-chain Monte-Carlo to visualise the multidimensional likelihood surface and extract the covariance matrix.

## Key numbers

- The LDM fit excluded all nuclei with A<16 and those with experimental binding energy errors larger than 100 keV.
- The initial GPE training set used 60 points for each of the two varied parameters (a_v and a_s), with a uniform prior restricting each to the interval [0, 30] MeV.
- After 51 iterations of the Expected Improvement criterion, the mean values of the a_v and a_s distributions fell within the error bars provided by the complete MCMC sampling.
- The correlation matrix from MCMC showed a_v and a_s correlated at 0.993, a_v and a_c at 0.984, a_v and a_a at 0.917, while a_p correlated with a_v at only 0.038.

## Context

This work extends the use of Gaussian Process Emulation previously applied to nuclear physics by McDermott (2015), Pastore (2017), and Neufcourt (2018), and contrasts with the standard Hessian method for covariance estimation described by Roca-Maza (2015) which assumes a parabolic shape of the likelihood surface around the optimum. The combination of GPE with Expected Improvement and MCMC enables future application to more complex models such as Skyrme functionals, where the likelihood surface may be multimodal and the Hessian approximation may fail.
