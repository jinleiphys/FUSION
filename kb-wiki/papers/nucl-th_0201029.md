# Properties of Valence Nucleon Distributions for Halo Nuclei

> With the binding energies and configurations determined experimentally, the root-mean-square radii are calculated for a number of single-particle states by numerically solving the Sch{ö}rdinger equations. By studying the relations between the radii and separation energies, the new scaling laws and necessary conditions for neutron halos and proton halos are established, respectively. Especially the existence region of true proton halos is pointed out. It is found that the effects of short-distance behaviours of valence nucleons at the edges of interaction potentials can not be disregarded. Moreover, by means of the radii of interaction potentials, the contributions of outer parts are estimated as the criterions of halos.

# Properties of Valence Nucleon Distributions for Halo Nuclei

## Key claim
Using Woods-Saxon potentials instead of the square-well potentials used in prior scaling laws (Hansen 1999, Fedorov 1994), the necessary conditions for neutron and proton halo formation are significantly looser than previously claimed; in particular, l=2 states can form halos under sufficiently stringent conditions, and true proton halos may exist only in resonant states with energies between -0.551/R_cn^2 MeV and the Coulomb barrier height, rather than in the bound states previously identified as proton halos.

## Method
The authors solved the Schrodinger equation numerically for two-body (core + valence nucleon) systems using a Woods-Saxon nuclear potential with standard parameters (r0 = 1.25 fm, a = 0.65 fm), including a Coulomb potential for protons (uniformly charged sphere, r0C = 1.30 fm) and a Thomas-form spin-orbit coupling (lambda = 25). For each single-particle state, the potential depth was adjusted to reproduce the experimentally determined binding energy, angular momentum, and spin; the resulting radial wavefunctions were then used to compute root-mean-square radii and the fraction of the wavefunction outside the interaction potential radius.

## Key numbers
- For neutron halos with l=0, the necessary condition is S_n * R_cn^2 < 17.6 MeV fm^2; for l=1, S_n * R_cn^2 < 5.79 MeV fm^2; for l=2, S_n * R_cn^2 < 0.153 MeV fm^2.
- The scaling law for l=0 neutron states is <r_n^2>/R_cn^2 = 13.5 / (S_n * R_cn^2)^0.667; for l=1, 4.38 / (S_n * R_cn^2)^0.446; for l=2, 1.43 / (S_n * R_cn^2)^0.180.
- For proton halos (l=0,1), the scaling law is <r_p^2>/R_cn^2 = 1.73 / (S_p * R_cn^2)^0.246, with necessary condition S_p * R_cn^2 < 0.551 MeV.
- For ^8B, the authors calculated <r^2>^1/2 = 4.5 fm and D_1 = 0.40, D_2 = 0.80 with R_cn = 3.6 fm, classifying it as a thick-skin nucleus rather than a true proton halo.

## Context
This work extends the scaling-law approach of Fedorov (1994) and Hansen (1999), who used square-well potentials and concluded that l=2 states cannot form halos and that proton halos do not exist due to the Coulomb barrier. By adopting Woods-Saxon potentials, the authors show that the short-distance behavior of valence nucleons at the edge of the interaction potential significantly affects the scaling laws, making halo formation possible under broader conditions. The results enable more realistic searches for unknown neutron halo states and identify the existence region for true proton halos, which may occur in resonant states above the particle-emission threshold but below the Coulomb barrier, rather than in the bound states previously claimed as proton halos (e.g., ^8B, ^17F).
