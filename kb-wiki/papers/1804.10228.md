# Finite-temperature relativistic nuclear field theory: an application to the dipole response

> Nuclear response theory beyond the one-loop approximation is formulated for the case of finite temperature. For this purpose, the time blocking approximation to the time-dependent part of the in-medium nucleon-nucleon interaction amplitude is adopted for the thermal (imaginary-time) Green's function formalism. We found that introducing a soft blocking, instead of a sharp blocking at zero temperature, brings the Bethe-Salpeter equation to a single frequency variable equation also at finite temperatures. The method is implemented self-consistently in the framework of Quantum Hadrodynamics and designed to connect the high-energy scale of heavy mesons and the low-energy domain of nuclear medium polarization effects in a parameter-free way. In this framework, we investigate the temperature dependence of dipole spectra in the even-even nuclei $^{48}$Ca, $^{120}$Sn and $^{132}$Sn with a special focus on the giant dipole resonance's width problem and on the low-energy dipole strength distribution.

## Key claim

The paper presents a finite-temperature generalization of relativistic nuclear field theory that, by introducing a "soft blocking" (via Fermi-Dirac occupation factors) instead of the sharp time projection operator used at zero temperature, reduces the Bethe-Salpeter equation to a single frequency variable equation at finite temperatures; the approach is parameter-free and self-consistently connects high-energy meson scales with low-energy nuclear polarization effects.

## Method

The authors formulate the nuclear response at finite temperature by adopting the time blocking approximation within the Matsubara (imaginary-time) Green's function formalism, generalizing the zero-temperature relativistic time blocking approximation. They solve the self-consistent relativistic mean field problem with NL3 forces to obtain the Dirac-Hartree basis, then compute the finite-temperature relativistic random phase approximation (FT-RRPA) phonon modes, and finally construct the particle-vibration coupling amplitude to obtain the response function via the Bethe-Salpeter equation in the finite-temperature relativistic time blocking approximation (FT-RTBA). The method is applied to compute the electric dipole strength distribution in even-even nuclei \(^{48}\)Ca, \(^{100}\)Sn, \(^{120}\)Sn, and \(^{132}\)Sn at various temperatures.

## Key numbers

- In \(^{48}\)Ca and \(^{100}\)Sn, the imaginary part of the energy variable \(\Delta\) was set to 500 keV and 200 keV, respectively.
- For \(^{120,132}\)Sn, \(\Delta = 200\) keV was adopted.
- The phonon space was truncated at cutoff frequencies \(\Omega_c = 15\) MeV for \(^{120,132}\)Sn and \(\Omega_c = 20\) MeV for \(^{48}\)Ca and \(^{100}\)Sn.
- The critical temperature for superfluidity in \(^{120}\)Sn is \(T_c \approx 0.66\) MeV.
- At temperatures \(T \approx 5\text{--}6\) MeV, the low-energy part of the dipole strength begins to dominate for all multipoles.
- The GDR width in \(^{120}\)Sn decreases in the interval \(0 \leq T \leq T_c\) and increases at \(T > T_c\); widths in \(^{100,132}\)Sn grow over the entire temperature range.
- For \(^{120}\)Sn, the theoretical GDR width shows good agreement with experimental data at \(T = 0\), \(T = 1\) MeV, and reasonable agreement at \(T = 3\) MeV.
- The energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR) in \(^{48}\)Ca and \(^{132}\)Sn shows a slow decrease with temperature; FT-RRPA and FT-RTBA values practically meet at \(T = 6\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the zero-temperature relativistic nuclear field theory (RNFT) developed by Litvinova, Ring, and Tselyaev (2006-2008) to finite temperature, going beyond the one-loop approximations such as thermal random phase approximation (TRPA) and thermal second RPA (SRPA) used by Ring et al. (1984), Niu et al. (2009), and Adachi and Schuck (1989). The approach addresses the long-standing GDR width problem, where earlier models gave contradictory results: Bortignon (1986) found the GDR nearly unchanged with temperature while Lacroix, Chomaz, and Ayik (1998) predicted a rapid width increase. The results enable systematic microscopic studies of compound nucleus deexcitation and r-process nucleosynthesis, with future work needed to include ground state correlations induced by particle-vibration coupling and effects of non-zero angular momenta.
