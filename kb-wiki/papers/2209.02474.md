# Effects of nuclear matter and composition in core-collapse supernovae and long-term proto-neutron star cooling

> We study the influence of hot and dense matter in core-collapse supernovae by adopting up-to-date nuclear equation of state (EOS) based on the microscopic nuclear many-body frameworks. We explore effects of EOS based on the Dirac Brueckner Hartree-Fock theory through comparisons with those based on the variational method. We also examine effects of the differences in the composition of nuclei and nucleons by using the same EOS by the variational method but employing two different treatments in computations of nuclear abundances. We perform numerical simulations of core-collapse supernovae adopting the three EOSs. We also perform numerical simulations of the long-term evolution over 70 s of the proto-neutron star cooling. We show that impacts by different modeling of composition are remarkable as in those by different treatments of uniform matter in the gravitational collapse, bounce, and shock propagation. The cooling of proto-neutron star and the resulting neutrino emission are also affected by the compositional difference even if the same treatment in computing uniform matter of EOS.

## Key claim

The study demonstrates that differences in the modeling of nuclear composition (the mixture of nuclei and nucleons in non-uniform matter) have impacts on core-collapse supernova dynamics and proto-neutron star cooling that are as remarkable as those from different treatments of uniform nuclear matter, even when the same underlying equation of state for uniform matter is used.

## Method

The authors performed spherically symmetric, general relativistic neutrino-radiation hydrodynamics simulations of core-collapse supernovae for 11.2 and 15 solar mass progenitor stars, using three equation of state tables: two based on the variational method (VM EOS with nuclear statistical equilibrium and VM-S EOS with single nucleus approximation) and one based on the Dirac Brueckner Hartree-Fock theory (DBHF EOS). They also performed long-term simulations of proto-neutron star cooling over 70 seconds using a separate code with flux-limited diffusion neutrino transport, adopting a common initial profile of electron fraction and entropy per baryon for all three EOSs.

## Key numbers

- The saturation density for VM EOS is 0.160 fm^-3 and for DBHF EOS is 0.179 fm^-3; the symmetry energy is 29.1 MeV for VM and 34.5 MeV for DBHF, with slope parameters L of 38.7 MeV and 66.8 MeV respectively.
- The incompressibility K is 245 MeV for VM EOS and 232 MeV for DBHF EOS.
- The maximum neutron star masses for both EOSs exceed 2.0 solar masses, fulfilling observational constraints.
- At core bounce for the 11.2 solar mass star, the shock wave position in mass coordinate is at 0.575 solar masses for DBHF and 0.535 solar masses for VM.
- The mass number of nuclei in VM-S EOS becomes approximately 400 at temperatures below 5 MeV at density 5x10^13 g cm^-3 and electron fraction 0.3, while for DBHF and VM EOS it remains below 100 down to about 1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends comparisons of microscopic nuclear many-body frameworks for supernova simulations, building on prior EOS tables from the variational method (Togashi 2017, Furusawa 2017) and the Dirac Brueckner Hartree-Fock theory (Furusawa 2020). It contrasts with earlier studies using effective frameworks such as relativistic mean field theory and Skyrme Hartree-Fock theory (Sumiyoshi 2004, 2005; Hempel 2012; Steiner 2013), and directly examines the separate effects of uniform matter treatment versus composition modeling. The results enable future multi-dimensional simulations to assess whether composition differences can trigger convective motion, as suggested by Harada (2020), and inform interpretation of supernova neutrino signals at detectors.
