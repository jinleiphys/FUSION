# Microscopic derivation of the Boltzmann equation for transport coefficients of resonating fermions at high temperature

> Motivated by the recently observed failure of the kinetic theory for the bulk viscosity, we in turn revisit the shear viscosity and the thermal conductivity of two-component fermions with a zero-range interaction both in two and three dimensions. In particular, we show that their Kubo formula evaluated exactly in the high-temperature limit to the lowest order in fugacity is reduced to the linearized Boltzmann equation. Previously, such a microscopic derivation of the latter was achieved only incompletely corresponding to the relaxation-time approximation. Here, we complete it by resuming all contributions that are naively higher orders in fugacity but become comparable in the zero-frequency limit due to the pinch singularity, leading to a self-consistent equation for a vertex function identical to the linearized Boltzmann equation. We then compute the shear viscosity and the thermal conductivity in the high-temperature limit for an arbitrary scattering length and find that the Prandtl number exhibits a nonmonotonic behavior slightly below the constant value in the relaxation-time approximation.

## Key claim

The authors show that the Kubo formula for shear viscosity and thermal conductivity of two-component fermions with zero-range interaction, evaluated exactly in the high-temperature limit to lowest order in fugacity, reduces to the linearized Boltzmann equation; this completes the microscopic derivation beyond the relaxation-time approximation, but the result does not apply to bulk viscosity because its corresponding operator is essentially a two-body operator without the one-body part.

## Method

The authors evaluate the Kubo formula for shear viscosity and thermal conductivity using the Matsubara formalism and quantum virial expansion with fugacity as the small parameter. They identify that the product of retarded and advanced fermion propagators in the zero-frequency limit produces a "pinch singularity" that promotes contributions naively of order \(z\) to order \(z^0\), requiring resummation of an infinite sequence of diagrams. This resummation leads to a self-consistent integral equation for the vertex function that is analytically continued to become identical to the linearized Boltzmann equation, which is then solved numerically by expanding the vertex function in generalized Laguerre polynomials up to \(N=10\) terms.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity in two dimensions reaches minimum \(\lambda_T^d\eta = 3.257\) at \(\lambda_T/a = 4.019\)
- Thermal conductivity in two dimensions reaches minimum \(\lambda_T^d m\kappa = 13.03\) at \(\lambda_T/a = 4.017\)
- Shear viscosity in three dimensions at infinite scattering length (\(\lambda_T/a = 0\)): \(\lambda_T^d\eta = 4.231\)
- Thermal conductivity in three dimensions at infinite scattering length: \(\lambda_T^d m\kappa = 16.01\)
- Relaxation-time approximation values for three dimensions: \(\lambda_T^d\eta = 15\pi/(8\sqrt2) \approx 4.165\) and \(\lambda_T^d m\kappa = 225\pi/(32\sqrt2) \approx 15.62\)
- Prandtl number in two dimensions reaches minima of \(\Pr = 0.4986\) at \(\lambda_T/a = 0.6948\) and \(\Pr = 0.4985\) at \(\lambda_T/a = 20.45\), with maximum \(\Pr = 0.5000\) at \(\lambda_T/a = 3.871\)
- Prandtl number in three dimensions reaches minimum \(\Pr = 0.6606\) at \(\lambda_T/a = 0\) and maximum \(\Pr = 0.6666\) at \(\lambda_T/a = \pm 4.262\)

## Context

This work extends the microscopic derivation of transport coefficients for resonating fermions, motivated by the previously observed failure of kinetic theory for bulk viscosity (Nishida 2019, Enss 2019, Hofmann 2020). Prior attempts using quantum virial expansion with approximate resummation based on the memory function formalism achieved correspondence with the Boltzmann equation only in the relaxation-time approximation (Enss 2011, Nishida 2019, Hofmann 2020). The complete correspondence established here enables exact computation of shear viscosity and thermal conductivity in the high-temperature limit for arbitrary scattering length, and the authors suggest that developing quantum virial expansion in the Keldysh formalism may provide a framework for studying nonequilibrium phenomena in ultracold-atom physics.
