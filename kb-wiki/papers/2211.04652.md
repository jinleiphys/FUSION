# Resolving the spurious-state problem in the Dirac equation with finite difference method

> To solve the Dirac equation with the finite difference method, one has to face up to the spurious-state problem due to the fermion doubling problem when using the conventional central difference formula to calculate the first-order derivative on the equal interval lattices. This problem is resolved by replacing the central difference formula with the asymmetric difference formula, i.e., the backward or forward difference formula. To guarantee the hermitian of the Hamiltonian matrix, the backward and forward difference formula should be used alternatively according to the parity of the wavefunction. This provides a simple and efficient numerical prescription to solve various relativistic problems in the microscopic world.

## Key claim

The spurious-state problem in solving the Dirac equation with the finite difference method (FDM) is resolved by replacing the central difference formula (CDF) with the asymmetric (backward or forward) difference formula (ADF), applied alternately to the large and small wavefunction components according to their parity to maintain Hermiticity of the Hamiltonian matrix, without adding any artificial terms or evolution processes.

## Method

The authors discretize the radial Dirac equation for a nucleon in a spherical Woods-Saxon potential on an equal-interval lattice (box size 20 fm, 500 lattice points) and replace the first-order derivative operator with either the 3-point or 5-point ADF (backward or forward difference) instead of the conventional CDF. For states with κ = −1, the backward difference is applied to the small component (even parity) and the forward difference to the large component (odd parity); the opposite assignment is used for κ = 1. The resulting Hamiltonian matrix is diagonalized to obtain eigen energies and wavefunctions, and results are compared with the shooting method.

## Key numbers

- Neutron energy levels in ¹³²Sn for κ = −1 and κ = 1 states are computed; the 3-point CDF yields degenerate spurious states (e.g., −55.006 MeV for both κ = −1 and κ = 1), while the 3-point ADF eliminates all spurious states and gives energies matching the shooting method (e.g., −55.005 MeV for κ = −1, −46.162 MeV for κ = 1).
- With the 5-point ADF, the largest energy difference from the shooting method occurs for the weakly bound 3p₁/₂ state (E ≈ −0.3 MeV); enlarging the box to R_box = 40 fm yields identical results (−0.292 MeV) for both methods.
- The FDM with 5-point ADF and box size 20 fm gives more accurate eigen energies for weakly bound states than the shooting method with the same box size.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Stacey (1982), who used ADF for massless fermions in one-dimensional lattice QCD, and Szafran (2019), who applied ADF in a mesh-sweeping method for graphene. It resolves the fermion-doubling problem in the Dirac equation for massive fermions without the Wilson term (Wilson, 1977) or higher-order CDF (Salomonson and Öster, 1989; Fang et al., 2020), which only reduce but do not eliminate spurious states. The prescription enables a simple, efficient FDM for relativistic problems in atomic, nuclear, and particle physics.
