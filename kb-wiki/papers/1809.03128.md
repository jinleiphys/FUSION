# Chiral phase transition within the linear sigma model in the Tsallis nonextensive statistics based on density operator

> We studied the chiral phase transition for small $|1-q|$ within the Tsallis nonextensive statistics of the entropic parameter $q$, where the quantity $|1-q|$ is the measure of the deviation from the Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics. We adopted the normalized $q$-expectation value in this study. We applied the free particle approximation and the massless approximation in the calculations of the expectation values. We estimated the critical physical temperature, and obtained the chiral condensate, the sigma mass, and the pion mass, as functions of the physical temperature $T_{\mathrm{ph}}$ for various $q$. We found the following facts. The $q$-dependence of the critical physical temperature is $1/\sqrt{q}$. The chiral condensate at $q$ is smaller than that at $q'$ for $q>q'$. The $q$-dependence of the pion mass and that of the sigma mass reflect the $q$-dependence of the condensate. The pion mass at $q$ is heavier than that at $q'$ for $q>q'$. The sigma mass at $q$ is heavier than that at $q'$ for $q>q'$ at high physical temperature, while the sigma mass at $q$ is lighter than that at $q'$ for $q>q'$ at low physical temperature. The quantities which are functions of the physical temperature $T_{\mathrm{ph}}$ and the entropic parameter $q$ are described by only the effective physical temperature defined as $\sqrt{q} T_{\mathrm{ph}}$ under the approximations.

## Key claim

The chiral phase transition within the Tsallis nonextensive statistics, for small deviations from Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics (small \(|1-q|\)), shows that all quantities depending on physical temperature \(T_{\mathrm{ph}}\) and entropic parameter \(q\) can be described solely by an effective physical temperature \(\sqrt{q} T_{\mathrm{ph}}\) under the free particle and massless approximations, with the critical physical temperature scaling as \(1/\sqrt{q}\).

## Method

The author applied the linear sigma model with \(N=4\) fields (one sigma field and three pion fields) within the Tsallis nonextensive statistics using the normalized \(q\)-expectation value. The normalized \(q\)-expectation values were expanded to first order in \(\varepsilon = 1-q\) using the density operator formalism, and the free particle approximation and massless approximation were applied to evaluate momentum integrals. The effective potential was derived, from which the chiral condensate, sigma mass, pion mass, and critical physical temperature were obtained as functions of \(T_{\mathrm{ph}}\) for various \(q\) values (0.9, 1.0, and 1.1).

## Key numbers

- Critical physical temperature scales as \(1/\sqrt{q}\): \(T_{\mathrm{ph},q}^{\mathrm{cr}} = (1/\sqrt{q}) T_{\mathrm{ph},q=1}^{\mathrm{cr}}\), where \(T_{\mathrm{ph},q=1}^{\mathrm{cr}} = \sqrt{\frac{12}{(N+2)} \left[ v^2 - \frac{3}{4} \left( \frac{4G}{\lambda} \right)^{2/3} \right] }\).
- Model parameters: \(\lambda = 20\), \(v = 87.4\ \mathrm{MeV}\), \(G^{1/3} = 119\ \mathrm{MeV}\), producing sigma mass \(M_0 = 600\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and pion mass \(M_j = 135\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (\(j \neq 0\)) at zero physical temperature.
- Under massless approximation, the normalized \(q\)-expectation value of the square of a scalar field is \(\frac{1}{12 (\beta_{\mathrm{ph}})^2} \left[ 1 - \varepsilon + O(\varepsilon^2) \right] = \frac{q}{12 (\beta_{\mathrm{ph}})^2} + O(\varepsilon^2)\), where \(\beta_{\mathrm{ph}} = 1/T_{\mathrm{ph}}\).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of chiral phase transitions under power-like distributions using the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model (Rozynek 2009, Rozynek 2016) and the linear sigma model (Ishihara 2015, Ishihara 2016, Shen 2017) by employing the Tsallis nonextensive statistics with the density operator formalism and normalized \(q\)-expectation value. The results are qualitatively consistent with Shen (2017), who studied the linear sigma model with quarks using the path integral method. The findings enable future improvements by including daisy diagrams and solving gap equations for effective masses, as well as calculating quantities without the free particle and massless approximations.
