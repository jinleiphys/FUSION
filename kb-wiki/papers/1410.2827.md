# Medium effects and the shear viscosity of the dilute Fermi gas away from the conformal limit

> We study the shear viscosity of a dilute Fermi gas as a function of the scattering length in the vicinity of the unitarity limit. The calculation is based on kinetic theory, which provides a systematic approach to transport properties in the limit in which the fugacity $z=nλ^3/2$ is small. Here, $n$ is the density of the gas and $λ$ is the thermal wave length of the fermions. At leading order in the fugacity expansion the shear viscosity is independent of density, and the minimum shear viscosity is achieved at unitarity. At the next order medium effects modify the scattering amplitude as well as the quasi-particle energy and velocity. We show that these effects shift the minimum of the shear viscosity to the Bose-Einstein condensation (BEC) side of the resonance, in agreement with the result of recent experiments.

## Key claim

The minimum of the shear viscosity in a dilute Fermi gas shifts from unitarity to the Bose-Einstein condensation (BEC) side of the Feshbach resonance when medium effects are included at next-to-leading order in the fugacity expansion, in qualitative agreement with recent experiments by Elliott et al. (2014).

## Method

The authors used kinetic theory based on a systematic expansion in the fugacity \(z=n\lambda^3/2\) of the gas, where \(n\) is the density and \(\lambda\) is the thermal de Broglie wavelength. They computed the shear viscosity at leading order \(\mathcal{O}((\lambda/a)^2)\) and at next-to-leading order \(\mathcal{O}(z(\lambda/a))\), incorporating medium modifications to the scattering amplitude (via Pauli blocking in the polarization function), the quasi-particle energy, and the quasi-particle velocity. The linearized Boltzmann equation was solved using a Chapman-Enskog expansion with a trial function \(\chi(p)=1\), which is accurate to better than 2% at unitarity.

## Key numbers

* The leading-order coefficient for the \((\lambda/a)^2\) term in the expansion of \((\eta-\eta_\infty)/\eta_\infty\) is \(c_0 = 1/(4\pi) \simeq 0.07958\).
* The next-to-leading-order coefficient for the \(z(\lambda/a)\) term is \(c_1 \simeq -0.03325\), obtained from a numerical calculation.
* The shear viscosity at unitarity to leading order in \(z\) is \(\eta_\infty = \frac{15\pi}{8\sqrt{2}}\frac{1}{\lambda^3}\) times a correction involving an integral over \(\tilde{P}\) and \(\tilde{q}\).
* The minimum of the trap-averaged kinematic shear viscosity \(\langle\eta/n\rangle\) was observed by Elliott et al. (2014) at \(1/(k_Fa)\simeq 0.18\) for a cloud energy \(\tilde{E}/E_F=1\).

## Context

This work extends the leading-order kinetic theory result of Massignan et al. (2005), which predicted the shear viscosity minimum exactly at unitarity, by including medium effects that shift the minimum to the BEC side. The calculation is consistent with the experimental findings of Elliott et al. (2014) and builds on earlier studies of medium effects by Bruun et al. (2005, 2006). The results enable more detailed comparisons between theory and experiment for the shear viscosity of trapped Fermi gases, though improved understanding of trap averaging is needed. The approach is limited to temperatures significantly above the phase transition; near \(T_c\), diagrammatic frameworks such as those of Enss et al. (2010) or Levin et al. (2010, 2014) are likely more reliable.
