# Beta-decay formulas revisited (I): Gamow--Teller and spin-dipole contributions to allowed and first-forbidden transitions

> We propose formulas of the nuclear beta-decay rate that are useful in a practical calculation. The decay rate is determined by the product of the lepton and hadron current densities. A widely used formula relies upon the fact that the low-energy lepton wave functions in a nucleus can be well approximated by a constant and linear to the radius for the $s$-wave and $p$-wave wave functions, respectively. We find, however, the deviation from such a simple approximation is evident for heavy nuclei with large $Z$ by numerically solving the Dirac equation. In our proposed formulas, the neutrino wave function is treated exactly as a plane wave, while the electron wave function is obtained by iteratively solving the integral equation, thus we can control the uncertainty of the approximate wave function. The leading-order approximation gives a formula equivalent to the conventional one and overestimates the decay rate. We demonstrate that the next-to-leading-order formula reproduces well the exact result for a schematic transition density as well as a microscopic one obtained by a nuclear energy-density functional method.

## Key claim

The widely used leading-order (LO) approximation for beta-decay rates, which treats the electron wave function as constant for s-waves and linear in radius for p-waves, overestimates the decay rate for heavy nuclei with large Z; the authors propose a next-to-leading-order (NLO) formula that treats the neutrino wave function exactly as a plane wave and obtains the electron wave function by iteratively solving the integral equation, thereby controlling the uncertainty of the approximate wave function.

## Method

The authors start from the formulation of Koshigiri et al. (1979) and use iterative solutions of the integral equation for the electron Coulomb wave function following Rose (1951) and Behrens and Bühring (1971). They derive explicit leading-order (LO) and next-to-leading-order (NLO) expressions for the electron wave functions, while treating the neutrino wave function exactly as a plane wave without long-wavelength expansion. The formulas are tested against exact numerical solutions of the Dirac equation using a schematic two-Gaussian transition density and then applied to microscopic transition densities from a nuclear energy-density functional (EDF) method for neutron-rich Ni and Sn isotopes.

## Key numbers

- For Z=82, A=208 at E_e=10 MeV, the LO approximation overestimates the s-wave electron wave function amplitude at the nuclear surface; the NLO correction notably reduces this error, with the dimensionless parameters being R_A E_e ~ 0.30 and R_A ξ ~ 0.36 (where ξ = αZ/(2R_A))
- For Z=28, A=80 at E_e=10 MeV, the NLO correction is smaller than for Z=82, with R_A E_e ~ 0.26 and R_A ξ ~ 0.10
- The relative deviation of the LO wave function G_{-1} at the nuclear surface grows with both R_A E_e and Z, as shown in a contour plot covering Z values and R_A E_e values up to approximately 0.5
- For the schematic transition density with a=-0.8, Z=82±1, A=208, and E_0=10 MeV, the NLO formula significantly improves the ratio Γ(NLO)/Γ(exact) compared to Γ(LO)/Γ(exact) for both Gamow-Teller (ΔJ^π=1^+) and spin-dipole (ΔJ^π=0^-) transitions

## Context

This work revisits the formulation of beta-decay rates originally developed by Behrens and Bühring (1971) and Schopper (1969), which has been widely used in r-process nucleosynthesis calculations by Engel (1999), Möller (2003), and others. The authors extend the approach of Koshigiri et al. (1979) to provide a simple NLO correction that improves accuracy for heavy nuclei with large Z and high Q_β values, which are relevant for neutron-rich nuclei near the drip line involved in the r-process. This enables more reliable beta-decay rate calculations for exotic nuclei and supports precision tests of the standard model, including CKM matrix unitarity checks and searches for scalar and tensor interactions.
