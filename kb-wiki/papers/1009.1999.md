# On limits of ab initio calculations of pairing gap in nuclei

> A brief review of recent microscopic calculations of nuclear pairing gap is given. A semi-microscopic model is suggested in which the ab-initio effective pairing interaction is supplemented with a small phenomenological addendum. It involves a parameter which is universal for all medium and heavy nuclei. Calculations for several isotopic and isotonic chains of semi-magic nuclei confirm the relevance of the model.

## Key claim

The paper argues that fully *ab initio* calculations of the nuclear pairing gap are limited because the result depends sensitively on the behavior of the effective mass \(m^*(k)\) over a wide momentum range, a quantity not sufficiently well known from first principles. The authors propose a semi-microscopic model that supplements the *ab initio* effective pairing interaction with a small phenomenological addendum containing a single universal parameter \(\gamma\), which approximately accounts for all corrections to the pure BCS scheme.

## Method

The authors use a two-step renormalization method to solve the gap equation, splitting the Hilbert space into a model subspace (up to \(E_0 = 40\) MeV) and a subsidiary subspace (up to \(E_{\text{max}} = 1000\) MeV). The effective pairing interaction in the model space is computed using the Local Potential Approximation (LPA) with the Argonne v\(_{18}\) potential and free nucleon Green functions (\(m^* = m\)) in the subsidiary space. The gap equation is then solved in the model space using a self-consistent basis from the Generalized Energy Density Functional (GEDF) method with the DF3 functional, which also assumes \(m^* = m\). The phenomenological term is added as a density-dependent contact interaction with a single parameter \(\gamma\) that is universal for all medium and heavy nuclei.

## Key numbers

- For the benchmark nucleus \(^{120}\)Sn, the *ab initio* calculation (\(\gamma = 0\)) gives a neutron gap of 1.80 MeV, while the experimental value is 1.32 MeV; with \(\gamma = 0.08\) the gap becomes 1.31 MeV.
- The optimal value of the phenomenological parameter is \(\gamma = 0.07\), which reproduces experimental gaps for most nuclei within an accuracy of about 0.1–0.2 MeV.
- The overall root-mean-square deviation between theoretical and experimental gap values for 34 semi-magic nuclei is \(\sqrt{\overline{(\delta \Delta)^2}} \simeq 0.13\) MeV.
- For the proton gap in the \(N=82\) isotone chain, the Coulomb interaction reduces the gap by about 0.5 MeV (e.g., for \(^{136}\)Xe, the gap without Coulomb is 1.19 MeV and with Coulomb is 0.87 MeV at \(\gamma = 0.06\)).
- The numerical accuracy of the gap calculation is 0.01 MeV, checked by increasing parameters up to \(E_0 = 60\) MeV, \(E_{\text{max}} = 1200\) MeV, and box radius \(R = 24\) fm.

## Context

This work extends earlier microscopic calculations of nuclear pairing by the Milan group (2005, 2007) and by Duguet et al. (2001, 2003), which gave contradictory results for the gap in \(^{120}\)Sn (1.0 MeV vs. 1.6 MeV). The authors previously analyzed these contradictions (Pankratov et al., 2008; Baldo et al., 2008) and showed they arise from different treatments of the effective mass. The proposed semi-microscopic model enables systematic calculations of pairing gaps across many semi-magic nuclei while avoiding the uncertainties in the high-momentum behavior of the effective mass that plague fully *ab initio* approaches. The model could be improved in the future by explicitly including low-lying phonon exchange and tadpole diagrams, which would change the value of the phenomenological parameter \(\gamma\).
