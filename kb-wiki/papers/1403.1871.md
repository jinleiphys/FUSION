# Chaotic behavior of the Compound Nucleus, open Quantum Dots and other nanostructures

> It is well established that physical systems exhibit both ordered and chaotic behavior. The chaotic behavior of nanostructure such as open quantum dots has been confirmed experimentally and discussed exhaustively theoretically. This is manifested through random fluctuations in the electronic conductance. What useful information can be extracted from this noise in the conductance? In this contribution we shall address this question. In particular, we will show that the average maxima density in the conductance is directly related to the correlation function whose characteristic width is a measure of energy- or applied magnetic field- correlation length. The idea behind the above has been originally discovered in the context of the atomic nucleus, a mesoscopic system. Our findings are directly applicable to graphene.

## Key claim
The average density of maxima in the fluctuating conductance of open quantum dots is directly related to the correlation function, whose characteristic width provides a measure of energy or magnetic field correlation length; this relationship, originally discovered in compound-nucleus physics, provides a new universal observable for ballistic conductance that can be extracted from a single conductance curve without requiring large statistical sampling.

## Method
The authors derive analytical expressions for the average density of maxima in the fluctuating part of the electron conductance using a Gaussian joint probability distribution for the fluctuating observable at three energy points, then integrate under the constraint that the central point is a maximum. They also derive equivalent expressions using a first- and second-derivative condition for maxima, and generalize the results to partially open quantum dots with tunneling probability less than unity using the stub model and random matrix theory. The analytical results are verified against numerical random matrix simulations using a random Hamiltonian S-matrix model with 600 resonances and 60 open channels.

## Key numbers
- For fully open quantum dots (tunneling probability Gamma = 1) with energy variation: average density of maxima <rho_E> = sqrt(3)/(pi * gamma) = 0.55/gamma, where gamma is the correlation width.
- For fully open quantum dots with external parameter variation (e.g., magnetic field): average density of maxima <rho_X> = 3/(pi * sqrt(2) * X_c) = 0.68/X_c, where X_c is the characteristic correlation length.
- For partially open quantum dots in the limit Gamma = 0 (isolated resonances) with energy variation: <rho_E> = sqrt(5)/(pi * gamma).
- For partially open quantum dots in the limit Gamma = 0 with external parameter variation: <rho_X> = 3/(pi * sqrt(2) * X_c), the same as the fully open case.
- The correlation length gamma is related to the density of states through the sum rule: gamma = (D/(2*pi)) * N, where D is the average resonance spacing and N is the number of open channels.
- For partially open quantum dots, the energy correlation function exhibits anti-correlation (negative values) in the limit Gamma -> 0: C(epsilon) = <T^fl T^fl> * (1 - (epsilon/gamma)^2) / [1 + (epsilon/gamma)^2]^2.

## Context
This work extends the Ericson fluctuation analysis from compound-nucleus physics (Ericson 1963) to open quantum dots, building on the formal analogy between nuclear reactions and mesoscopic systems established by Alhassid and others. The authors apply the number-of-maxima method originally developed by Brink and Stephen (1963) and Bizzeti and Maurenzig (1967) for nuclear cross sections to the electronic conductance of quantum dots, using random matrix theory predictions for the correlation function from Beenakker (1997) and Weidenmuller and collaborators. The results enable a new method for extracting correlation widths from single conductance curves in quantum dots, and the authors note that applications to graphene flakes are underway.
