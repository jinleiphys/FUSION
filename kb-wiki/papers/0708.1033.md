# Double Beta Decay, Majorana Neutrinos, and Neutrino Mass

> The theoretical and experimental issues relevant to neutrinoless double-beta decay are reviewed. The impact that a direct observation of this exotic process would have on elementary particle physics, nuclear physics, astrophysics and cosmology is profound. Now that neutrinos are known to have mass and experiments are becoming more sensitive, even the non-observation of neutrinoless double-beta decay will be useful. If the process is actually observed, we will immediately learn much about the neutrino. The status and discovery potential of proposed experiments are reviewed in this context, with significant emphasis on proposals favored by recent panel reviews. The importance of and challenges in the calculation of nuclear matrix elements that govern the decay are considered in detail. The increasing sensitivity of experiments and improvements in nuclear theory make the future exciting for this field at the interface of nuclear and particle physics.

## Key claim

Neutrinoless double-beta decay can only occur if neutrinos are Majorana particles (their own antiparticles), and its observation would establish lepton-number violation while providing a practical way to determine absolute neutrino masses; however, even non-observation will be useful now that neutrinos are known to have mass, and the extraction of the effective Majorana mass from a measured half-life depends critically on the accuracy of calculated nuclear matrix elements, which remain a significant challenge.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework for neutrinoless double-beta decay, deriving the decay rate from the amplitude for light Majorana neutrino exchange using the standard model weak interaction Hamiltonian with left-handed currents, the impulse approximation for hadronic currents, and the closure approximation to sum over intermediate nuclear states. It surveys experimental approaches including germanium and tellurium detectors, presents the sensitivity formula relating half-life limits to experimental parameters (mass, background, energy resolution, running time), and discusses the calculation of nuclear matrix elements via the quasiparticle random-phase approximation and other nuclear structure methods.

## Key numbers

- The most sensitive experimental limits at the time: Heidelberg-Moscow experiment \(T^{0\nu}_{1/2}(^{76}\text{Ge}) \geq 1.9 \times 10^{25}\) y; IGEX experiment \(T^{0\nu}_{1/2}(^{76}\text{Ge}) \geq 1.6 \times 10^{25}\) y; CUORICINO experiment \(T^{0\nu}_{1/2}(^{130}\text{Te}) \geq 3.0 \times 10^{24}\) y
- To reach sensitivity to an effective Majorana mass \(\langle m_{\beta\beta}\rangle \approx 0.1\) eV, an experiment must observe a half-life of \(10^{26} - 10^{27}\) y, based on published nuclear structure factors \(F_N\) between \(10^{-13}\) and \(10^{-14}\) y\(^{-1}\)
- For a 200-kg \(^{76}\text{Ge}\) experiment with background 0.01 counts/(keV kg y), energy resolution 3.5 keV, running 5 years, the 4\(\sigma\) half-life sensitivity is \(1.9 \times 10^{26}\) y
- The claimed observation by a subset of the Heidelberg-Moscow collaboration reported \(28.75 \pm 6.86\) counts under a peak at 2039 keV, with a most probable half-life of \(2.23 \times 10^{25}\) y; the experiment had parameters \(Mt = 71.7\) kg y, background \(b = 0.11\) counts/(keV kg y), energy resolution \(\Delta(E) = 3.27\) keV
- The first geochemical observation of double-beta decay reported \(T^{\beta\beta}_{1/2}(^{130}\text{Te}) = 1.4 \times 10^{21}\) y (1950); the first laboratory observation of two-neutrino double-beta decay was in 1987

## Context

This review synthesizes the theoretical and experimental status of neutrinoless double-beta decay following the discovery of neutrino oscillations, which established that neutrinos have mass and mix significantly. It extends earlier reviews by Haxton and Stephenson (1984), Moe and Vogel (1994), and Elliott and Vogel (2002), and provides the framework for interpreting next-generation experiments such as GERDA and the Majorana project. The paper emphasizes that accurate nuclear matrix elements are essential for extracting neutrino masses from decay rates, and that the field is at an interface where improvements in both experiment and nuclear theory are needed to resolve the claimed observation by Klapdor-Kleingrothaus et al. (2001) and to probe the inverted mass hierarchy region.
