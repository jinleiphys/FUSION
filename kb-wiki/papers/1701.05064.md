# Parametric estimate of the relative photon yields from the Glasma and the Quark-Gluon Plasma in heavy-ion collisions

> Recent classical-statistical numerical simulations have established the "bottom-up" thermalization scenario of Baier et al. as the correct weak coupling effective theory for thermalization in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. We perform a parametric study of photon production in the various stages of this bottom-up framework to ascertain the relative contribution of the off-equilibrium "Glasma" relative to that of a thermalized Quark-Gluon Plasma. Taking into account the constraints imposed by the measured charged hadron multiplicities at RHIC and the LHC, we find that Glasma contributions are important especially for large values of the saturation scale at both energies. These non-equilibrium effects should therefore be taken into account in studies where weak coupling methods are employed to compute photon yields.

## Key claim

The authors perform a parametric estimate of photon production from the Glasma and the thermalized Quark-Gluon Plasma within the "bottom-up" thermalization framework of Baier et al., finding that Glasma contributions are important especially for large values of the saturation scale at both RHIC and LHC energies, but note that the results are sensitive to the saturation scale \(Q_s\) and coefficients relating this scale to the initial temperature and thermalization time, which are subject to significant systematic uncertainties.

## Method

The authors compute photon production rates using the kinetic expression for two-to-two scattering, employing the small-angle approximation for the pre-equilibrium Glasma stages and a thermal rate formula with a \(K\) factor to account for higher-order contributions. They divide the Glasma evolution into three temporal stages based on the BMSS scenario, estimate the parametric dependence of the photon yield on the QCD coupling \(\alpha_s\) and the saturation scale \(Q_s\) for each stage, and compare these to the thermal QGP yield using a 1+1 dimensional hydrodynamic expansion model with temperature scaling as \(T(\tau) = T_\text{th} (\tau_\text{th}/\tau)^{1/3}\). The constants \(c_\text{eq}\) and \(c_T\) in the BMSS expressions for thermalization time and initial temperature are constrained using charged hadron multiplicity data from RHIC and the LHC.

## Key numbers

* Thermal photon yield per unit rapidity per unit transverse area: \(\frac{1}{Q_s^2 S_\perp} \frac{dN^\text{th}}{dy_p} \simeq \frac{5}{3} c_\text{eq}^6 c_T^4 C \frac{\alpha}{2\pi^2} \alpha_s^{-13/5} \left[ c_\text{eq}^2 c_T^2 \alpha_s^{4/5} \left(\frac{Q_s}{T_c}\right)^2 - 1\right]\), where \(T_c = 154\) MeV is the QCD crossover temperature.
* Glasma stage (i) photon yield: \(\frac{1}{Q_s^2 S_\perp} \frac{dN^\text{glasma-i}}{dy_p} = \frac{40}{9\pi} \frac{\alpha}{2\pi^2} \mathcal{L} \kappa_g \kappa_q \log \alpha_s^{-3/2}\), with \(\kappa_g = \kappa_q = c/(2N_c)\) and gluon liberation coefficient \(c = 1.1\).
* Glasma stage (ii) photon yield: \(\frac{1}{Q_s^2 S_\perp} \frac{dN^\text{glasma-ii}}{dy_p} \simeq \frac{80}{9\pi} \frac{\alpha}{2\pi^2} \mathcal{L} \kappa_q \kappa_q \left( \alpha_s^{-1/2} - 1 \right)\), assuming \(c_1 = c_2\).
* BMSS thermalization time: \(\tau_\text{th} \simeq c_\text{eq} \alpha_s^{-13/5} Q_s^{-1}\) and initial temperature: \(T_\text{th} \simeq c_T c_\text{eq} \alpha_s^{2/5} Q_s\), with \(c_T \simeq 0.18\) from the BMSS estimate.

## Context

This work extends the "bottom-up" thermalization scenario of Baier et al. (2000) (BMSS), which was recently confirmed by classical-statistical numerical simulations as the correct weak coupling effective theory for thermalization in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. The parametric estimates of pre-equilibrium photon production from the Glasma complement previous phenomenological studies by McLerran et al. (2014, 2015) that suggested significant Glasma contributions to photon yields and elliptic flow. The results motivate more detailed computations of pre-equilibrium photon production using classical-statistical simulations with dynamical quarks, as initiated by Tanji et al. (2015) for fixed box geometry, and coupled Boltzmann equations for expanding systems, to be presented in a companion paper by Tanji et al. (2017).
