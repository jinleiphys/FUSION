# Electron Structure: Shape, Size and GPDs in QED

> The shape of the electron is studied using lowest-order perturbation theory. Quantities used to probe the structure of the proton: form factors, generalized parton distributions, transverse densities, Wigner distributions and the angular momentum content are computed for the electron-photon component of the electron wave function. The influence of longitudinally polarized photons, demanded by the need for infrared regularization via a non-zero photon mass, is included. The appropriate value of the photon mass depends on experimental conditions, and consequently the size of the electron (as defined by the slope of its Dirac form factor) bound in a hydrogen atom is found to be about four times larger than when the electron is free. The shape of the electron, as determined from the transverse density and generalized parton distributions is shown to not be round, and the free electron is shown to be far less round than the bound electron. An electron distribution function (analogous to the quark distribution function) is defined, and that of the bound electron is shown to be suppressed compared to that of the free electron. If the relative transverse momentum of the virtual electron and photon is large compared with the electron mass, the virtual electron and photon each carry nearly the total angular momentum of the physical electron (1/2), with the orbital angular momentum being nearly (-1/2). Including the non-zero photon mass leads to the suppression of end-point contributions to form factors. Implications for proton structure and color transparency are discussed.

## Key claim

The electron's shape is not round, and its size depends strongly on whether it is free or bound: the bound electron (in hydrogen) is about four times larger than the free electron, as measured by the slope of its Dirac form factor, with the exact size determined by the infrared-regulating photon mass whose value depends on experimental or atomic conditions.

## Method

The electron-photon component of the electron wave function was computed using lowest-order perturbation theory in QED, employing the light-front (infinite momentum frame) formalism to handle Fock-space expansions. The virtual photon was given a non-zero mass to regulate infrared divergences, with the mass value set to μ = m (electron mass) for a free electron and μ ≈ 18α²m for an electron bound in hydrogen. The wave functions were then used to compute form factors, generalized parton distributions, transverse densities, Wigner distributions, and angular momentum content, including the previously omitted longitudinal polarization states of the massive photon.

## Key numbers

- The bound electron size (root-mean-square transverse radius) is about 0.14/m, while the free electron size is about 0.038/m, a factor of approximately four variation.
- The photon mass for a bound electron is μ ≈ 18α²m, where α²m is the Rydberg constant; for a free electron, μ = m (0.51 MeV) is taken as a typical value.
- For any μ > 0.002 m, there is very little variation with μ, so taking μ = m for the free electron is a reasonable simplification.
- The anomalous magnetic moment F₂(0) shows very strong μ² dependence: using μ² = 4m² (analogous to a di-quark mass) gives about a factor of five suppression relative to the μ = 0 case.
- The transverse charge density ρ₀(b) for small b behaves roughly as 1/b², originating from the small-argument behavior of the modified Bessel function K₁.

## Context

This work extends the light-front treatment of the electron-photon Fock state (Brodsky 1980, Brodsky 2000) by including longitudinally polarized virtual photons required for consistent infrared regularization, and by systematically computing the full suite of nucleon-structure observables (form factors, GPDs, transverse densities, Wigner distributions) for the electron. The electron serves as a solvable QED analog for the quark-vector-diquark component of the nucleon, as previously modeled by Brodsky (2003) and Cloet (2012). The finding that bound and free electrons have substantially different sizes is directly analogous to the EMC effect for nucleons bound in nuclei (Aubert 1983, Hen 2013), and the results enable quantitative modeling of how infrared regularization affects nucleon structure observables, particularly the suppression of end-point contributions to form factors.
