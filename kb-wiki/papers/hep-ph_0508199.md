# On the Existence of Heavy Pentaquarks: The large Nc and Heavy Quark Limits and Beyond

> We present a very general argument that the analogue of a heavy pentaquark (a state with the quantum numbers of a baryon combined with an additional light quark and a heavy antiquark) must exist as a particle stable under strong interactions in the combined heavy quark and large Nc limits of QCD. Moreover, in the combined limit these heavy pentaquark states fill multiplets of SU(4)xO(8)xSU(2). We explore the question of whether corrections in the combined 1/Nc and 1/mQ expansions are sufficiently small to maintain this qualitative result. Since no model-independent way is known to answer this question, we use a class of ``realistic'' hadronic models in which a pentaquark can be formed via nucleon-heavy meson binding through a pion-exchange potential. These models have the virtue that they necessarily yield the correct behavior in the combined limit, and the long-distance parts of the interactions are model independent. If the long-distance attraction in these models were to predict bound states in a robust way (i.e., largely insensitive to the details of the short-range interaction), then one could safely conclude that heavy pentaquarks do exist. However, in practice the binding does depend very strongly on the details of the short-distance physics, suggesting that the real world is not sufficiently near the combined large Nc, mQ limit to use it as a reliable guide. Whether stable heavy pentaquarks exist remains an open question.

## Key claim

Heavy pentaquarks (states with baryon quantum numbers plus an additional light quark and a heavy antiquark) must exist as stable particles under strong interactions in the combined large \(N_c\) and heavy quark limits of QCD, but the authors find that \(1/N_c\) and \(1/m_Q\) corrections are large enough that whether such states exist in the real world remains an open question.

## Method

The authors first prove rigorously that heavy pentaquarks exist as bound states in the combined large \(N_c\) and large \(m_Q\) limits by showing that the nucleon-heavy meson potential is local, scales as \(\lambda^0\) (where \(\lambda \sim 1/N_c, \Lambda_{\rm QCD}/m_Q\)), and has attractive regions from one-pion exchange, while the reduced mass \(\mu \sim \lambda^{-1}\) diverges, guaranteeing an infinite number of bound states. They then test whether this result survives for physical \(N_c=3\) and finite \(m_Q\) by solving the nonrelativistic Schrödinger equation with a one-pion exchange potential (OPEP) at long distances and either constant or quadratic phenomenological potentials at short distances (cut off at \(\sim 1\) fm), using coupling constants extracted from \(D^* \to D\pi\) decay.

## Key numbers

- The heavy-meson coupling constant \(g_H\) is extracted from \(D^{*\pm} \rightarrow D^{0}\pi^{\pm}\) decay as \(\pm 0.59 \pm 0.07\).
- For the \(B\) meson with a constant short-distance potential constrained to match the deuteron binding energy (2.2 MeV), bound states appear only in negative-parity, \(I=0\) channels with binding energy \(\sim 1.3\) MeV; for a quadratic potential, binding energy is \(\sim 3.9\) MeV.
- No channel supports a bound state with a \(D\) meson when the short-distance potential is constrained by the deuteron binding energy.
- When the constant short-distance potential is deepened from \(-62.79\) MeV to \(-276\) MeV (about 4 times deeper), the \(D\) meson can form a bound state.
- Increasing the OPEP cutoff from 1.0 fm to 1.5 fm (with potential depth \(-62.79\) MeV) produces stronger binding and allows the \(D\) meson to bind.
- Without any short-distance potential, the OPEP alone does not bind a pentaquark for any channel unless \(g_H\) is raised to near 1 (approximately double the extracted value), and in some cases larger than 2.

## Context

This work extends the large \(N_c\) and heavy quark limit arguments previously applied to nonexotic baryons (Cohen, 2004) and to light pentaquarks (Cohen and Lebed, 2004) to the case of heavy pentaquarks. It contradicts the optimistic predictions of models such as those by Wessling (2001) and Lipkin (1987) that heavy pentaquarks should be bound, by showing that the combined limit predictions are unreliable due to large corrections. The results enable future lattice QCD studies to focus on whether the short-range nucleon-heavy meson interaction is sufficiently attractive to produce bound states, and highlight the need for model-independent approaches to the pentaquark question.
