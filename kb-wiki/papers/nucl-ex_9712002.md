# Progress In Nuclear Astrophysics Using Secondary Radioactive Beams

> We review progress in studying two central problems in Nuclear Astrophysics: The \be7pg reaction is one of the major source of uncertainties in estimating the \b8 solar neutrino flux and is critical for the Solar Neutrino Problem. We discuss a newly emerging method to extract this cross section from the Coulomb dissociation of the radioactive beam of \b8. The Coulomb dissociation appears to provide a viable alternative method for measuring the \be7pg reaction rate. Several attempts to constrain the p-wave S-factor of the \c12ag reaction at Helium burning temperatures (200 MK) using the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of \n16 have been made, and it is claimed that this S-factor is known, as quoted by the TRIUMF collaboration. In contrast reanalyses (by G.M. hale) of all thus far available data (including the \n16 data) does not rule out a small S-factor solution. Furthermore, we improved our previous Yale-UConn study of the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of \n16. Our newly measured spectrum of the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of \n16 is not consistent with the TRIUMF('94) data, but is consistent with the Seattle('95) data, as well as the earlier (unaltered !) data of Mainz('71). The implication of this discrepancies for the extracted astrophysical p-wave s-factor is briefly discussed.

# Progress In Nuclear Astrophysics Using Secondary Radioactive Beams

## Key claim

The Coulomb dissociation of 8B provides a viable alternative method for measuring the 7Be(p,γ)8B reaction rate, with preliminary results consistent with the lower cross section values measured by Filippone et al. and Vaughn et al., but not with those of Kavanagh or Parker. For the 12C(α,γ)16O reaction, the p-wave astrophysical S-factor remains uncertain despite claims from the TRIUMF collaboration, because reanalyses of all available data (including the 16N data) do not rule out a small S-factor solution.

## Method

For the 7Be(p,γ)8B reaction, the cross section was extracted from the Coulomb dissociation of a radioactive 8B beam on a 208Pb target at the RIKEN-RIPS facility, using the inverse process of virtual photon absorption to infer the radiative capture cross section. For the 12C(α,γ)16O reaction, the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission spectrum of 16N was measured at the Yale ESTU tandem van de Graaff accelerator using a 70 MeV 15N beam on a deuterium gas target, with 9 silicon surface barrier detectors in coincidence with 12 plastic scintillator detectors for beta timing, improving statistics by more than a factor of five and energy resolution by 20% compared to the previous Yale-UConn experiment.

## Key numbers

- The 7Be(p,γ)8B S-factor at zero energy (S17(0)) is extracted to be smaller than 20 eV-barn, and considerably smaller (30%) than assumed in the Standard Solar Model.
- The TRIUMF collaboration quoted SE1 = 81 ± 21 keV-b for the p-wave S-factor of the 12C(α,γ)16O reaction, based on their R-matrix analysis of 16N beta-delayed alpha-particle data.
- The new Yale-UConn experiment achieved an energy resolution of 200 keV at 2.36 MeV and collected 292,000 events, a factor of five increase over the previous Yale-UConn experiment.
- The chi-squared per data point for the Yale-UConn data compared to the Seattle theory curve was 1.4, while compared to the TRIUMF theory curve it was 7.2.
- The original Mainz (1971) data, containing approximately 32 million events, agreed with the Seattle data (chi-squared per data point of 2.5) but disagreed with the TRIUMF data (chi-squared per data point of 123).
- The TRIUMF data disagreed with other data sets by up to a factor of 3.

## Context

This work extends the study of two central problems in nuclear astrophysics: the 7Be(p,γ)8B reaction, which is a major source of uncertainty in estimating the 8B solar neutrino flux and critical for the Solar Neutrino Problem, and the 12C(α,γ)16O reaction, which is important for helium burning in massive stars prior to supernova collapse. The Coulomb dissociation method, proposed by Baur (1986) as a Primakoff process, provides an alternative to direct measurements with 7Be beams. For the 12C(α,γ)16O reaction, the work contradicts the TRIUMF collaboration's claim (Azuma et al., 1994) that the p-wave S-factor is known with high accuracy, and instead supports the Seattle data (Zhao et al., 1995) and the original Mainz data (Waffler, 1971). The reanalysis by Hale (1996) of all existing data demonstrates that a small S-factor solution cannot be ruled out, and the sign of the interference of the two 1- states in 12C(α,γ)16O cannot be directly determined from beta-decay of 16N, requiring additional low-energy data on the capture reaction itself.
