# The effect of the neutron and proton numbers ratio in colliding nuclei at formation of the evaporation residues in the $^{34}$S+$^{208}$Pb and $^{36}$S+$^{206}$Pb reactions

> The difference between observed cross sections of the evaporation residues (ER) of the $^{34}$S+$^{208}$Pb and $^{36}$S+$^{206}$Pb reactions formed in the 2n and 3n channels has been explained by two reasons related with the entrance channel characteristics of these reactions. The first reason is that the capture cross section of the latter reaction is larger than the one of the $^{34}$S+$^{208}$Pb reaction since the nucleus-nucleus potential is more attractive in the $^{36}$S+$^{206}$Pb reaction due to two more neutrons in isotope $^{36}$S. The second reason is the difference in the heights of the intrinsic fusion barrier $B^*_{\rm fus}$ appearing on the fusion trajectory by nucleon transfer between nuclei of the DNS formed after the capture. The value of $B^*_{\rm fus}$ calculated for the $^{34}$S+$^{208}$Pb reaction is higher than the one obtained for the $^{36}$S+$^{206}$Pb reaction. This fact has been caused by the difference between the $N/Z$-ratios in the light fragments of the DNS formed during the capture in these reactions. The $N/Z$-ratio has been found by solution of the transport master equations for the proton and neutron distributions between fragments of the DNS formed at capture with the different initial neutron numbers $N=18$ and $N=20$ for the reactions with the $^{34}$S and $^{36}$S, respectively.

## Key claim

The observed difference in evaporation residue cross sections between the two reactions is explained by two entrance-channel effects: the capture cross section is larger for the $^{36}$S+$^{206}$Pb reaction because its nucleus-nucleus potential is more attractive, and the intrinsic fusion barrier $B^*_{\rm fus}$ is lower for that reaction due to the different $N/Z$ ratios in the light fragments of the dinuclear system formed after capture.

## Method

The authors calculated capture cross sections by solving classical equations of motion for the radial and orbital motions, including friction coefficients and a nucleus-nucleus potential that accounts for Coulomb, nuclear, and rotational terms. Surface vibrations of the spherical colliding nuclei were treated as frozen during capture, with averaging over deformation parameters of the first excited $2^+$ and $3^-$ states. The evolution of the dinuclear system (DNS) toward complete fusion or quasifission was modeled by solving transport master equations for proton and neutron distributions between the DNS fragments, with transition coefficients calculated microscopically from single-particle states.

## Key numbers

- The measured cross section for the 2n channel of $^{36}$S+$^{206}$Pb was approximately 25 times larger than that of $^{34}$S+$^{208}$Pb near the maxima of the 2n cross sections.
- For the 3n channel, approximately ten times larger cross section was measured for the $^{36}$S+$^{206}$Pb reaction compared to the $^{34}$S+$^{208}$Pb reaction.
- The capture probability for the $^{36}$S+$^{206}$Pb reaction at $E_{\rm c.m.}=139.24$ MeV and $\ell=10$ is about 4 times larger than for the $^{34}$S+$^{208}$Pb reaction.
- The initial neutron numbers are $N=18$ for $^{34}$S and $N=20$ for $^{36}$S.
- The mean lifetimes of the first excited $2^+$ states are $0.023 \times 10^{-12}$ s for $^{34}$S and $0.110 \times 10^{-12}$ s for $^{36}$S; the $3^-$ state lifetime for $^{34}$S is $0.130 \times 10^{-12}$ s.

## Context

This work extends the experimental findings of Knyazheva et al. (2012) and Knyazheva et al. (2015), who measured evaporation residue and fission cross sections for the $^{34}$S+$^{208}$Pb and $^{36}$S+$^{206}$Pb reactions and noted a strong hindrance to fusion in the former reaction that could not be explained by coupled-channels or statistical model calculations alone. The present theoretical analysis provides a microscopic explanation for this hindrance based on the $N/Z$ ratio difference in the DNS fragments, following the approach of Simenel et al. (2012) who found similar $N/Z$ matching effects in Ca+Pb reactions. The results enable improved predictions for fusion-evaporation reactions used to synthesize heavy and superheavy elements.
