# Perturbative HFB model for many-body pairing correlations

> We develop a perturbative model to treat the off-diagonal components in the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) transformation matrix, which are neglected in the BCS approximation. Applying the perturbative model to a weakly bound nucleus $^{84}$Ni, it is shown that the perturbative approach reproduces well the solutions of the HFB method both for the quasi-particle energies and the radial dependence of quasi-particle wave functions. We find that the non-resonant part of the continuum single-particle state can acquire an appreciable occupation probability when there exists a weakly bound state close to the Fermi surface. This result originates from the strong coupling between the continuum particle state and the weakly bound state, and is absent in the BCS approximation. The limitation of the BCS approximation is pointed out in comparison with the HFB and the present perturbative model.

# Perturbative HFB model for many-body pairing correlations

## Key claim

The perturbative model (p-HFB) reproduces the full HFB quasi-particle energies and wave functions for weakly bound nuclei, provided the BCS approximation is used as a starting point; however, the BCS approximation fails to capture the coupling between hole and non-resonant continuum states that arises when a weakly bound state lies near the Fermi surface, a mechanism that the p-HFB model does capture.

## Method

The authors start from the full HFB equations in coordinate space, expand the quasi-particle wave functions on a Hartree-Fock (HF) basis, and treat the off-diagonal components of the pairing matrix (neglected in BCS) as a perturbation to the BCS Hamiltonian. The perturbative correction to the quasi-particle wave function and energy is computed to leading order, yielding the "p-HFB" model. The method is applied to neutron states in the weakly bound nucleus ⁸⁴Ni using a Woods-Saxon mean-field and a local pairing potential with an average pairing gap of 1.0 MeV and a Fermi energy of −0.5 MeV.

## Key numbers

- For the four lowest neutron s₁/₂ quasi-particle states in ⁸⁴Ni, the HFB quasi-particle energies are 0.421, 1.045, 2.327, and 4.259 MeV; the BCS energies are 0.462, 1.010, 2.305, and 4.244 MeV; the p-HFB energies are 0.400, 1.039, 2.318, and 4.250 MeV.
- The occupation probability v² for the lowest s₁/₂ state is 0.553 (HFB), 0.645 (BCS), and 0.606 (p-HFB); for the second s₁/₂ state it is 0.039 (HFB), 0.007 (BCS), and 0.033 (p-HFB).
- The BCS quasi-particle energy deviates from the HFB energy by at most 0.04 MeV for the s₁/₂ states; the p-HFB reduces this deviation further.
- The off-diagonal pairing matrix elements for weakly bound states (e.g., the 3s₁/₂ state at −0.634 MeV) are of the same order as the diagonal components and persist up to about 30 MeV in single-particle energy.
- The 3g₉/₂ state at HF energy 3.16 MeV acquires an appreciable occupation probability in HFB and p-HFB, while the BCS approximation gives essentially zero occupation for this non-resonant continuum state.

## Context

This work extends the comparison between BCS and HFB methods for weakly bound nuclei, following the observation by Dobaczewski et al. (1996) that the smaller component of BCS quasi-particle wave functions differs significantly from HFB results. It also builds on the resonance BCS approach of Sandulescu et al. (2000, 2001, 2003), who argued that non-resonant continuum states can be neglected in BCS for binding energies and radii. The p-HFB model provides a computationally simpler alternative to full HFB that retains the intuitive single-particle picture, and it enables improved quasi-particle wave functions for applications such as quasi-particle random phase approximation (QRPA) calculations in weakly bound nuclei, where the BCS approximation is inadequate.
