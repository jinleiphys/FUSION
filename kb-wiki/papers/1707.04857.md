# The "Folk Theorem" on Effective Field Theory: How Does It Fare in Nuclear Physics?

> This is a brief history of what I consider as very important, some of which truly seminal, contributions made by young Korean nuclear theorists, mostly graduate students working on PhD thesis in 1990's and early 2000's, to nuclear effective field theory, nowadays heralded as the first-principle approach to nuclear physics. The theoretical framework employed is an effective field theory anchored on a scale-invariant hidden local symmetric Lagrangian constructed along the line of Weinberg's "Folk Theorem" on effective field theory. The problems addressed are the high-precision calculations on the thermal $np$ capture, the solar $pp$ fusion process, the solar $hep$ process -- John Bahcall's challenge to nuclear theorists -- and the quenching of $g_A$ in giant Gamow-Teller resonances and the whopping enhancement of first-forbidden beta transitions in astrophysical processes. Extending adventurously the strategy to a wild uncharted domain in which a systematic implementation of the "theorem" is far from obvious, the same effective Lagrangian is applied to the structure of compact stars. A surprising result on the properties of massive stars, totally different from what has been obtained up to day in the literature, is predicted, such as the precocious onset of conformal sound velocity together with the possible emergence of hidden symmetries such as scale symmetry and hidden local symmetry.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Weinberg's "Folk Theorem" on effective field theory is successfully validated in nuclear physics through high-precision calculations of electroweak response functions, but the author acknowledges that extending the same framework to dense baryonic matter in compact stars involves assumptions and simplifications "with full of holes in the reasoning far fetched even from the non-axiomatic FT."

## Method

The author reviews a series of calculations performed using a scale-invariant hidden local symmetric Lagrangian (bsHLS) that incorporates pions, nucleons, vector mesons (ρ, ω), and a dilaton scalar (σ), with parameters matched to QCD via operator product expansion at a matching scale. For light nuclear systems, the calculations employ chiral perturbation theory to next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N³LO) for electroweak currents combined with accurate nuclear wave functions from realistic potentials, a hybrid approach the author calls "MEEFT" or "EFT*." For dense matter, the same Lagrangian is applied using a Landau Fermi-liquid theory anchored on Wilsonian renormalization group, with intrinsic density dependence inherited from QCD condensates and a topology change from skyrmion matter to half-skyrmion matter at around twice nuclear saturation density.

## Key numbers

- Thermal np capture cross section: σ_th = (334 ± 2) mb (theory) vs. σ_exp = (334.2 ± 0.5) mb (experiment)
- Lattice QCD result for same process: σ_latticeQCD = 334.9 ± 5.3 mb
- Solar pp fusion S-factor: S_pp(0) = 4.05 × 10⁻²⁵ (1 ± 0.01) MeV·b (initial result), later improved to S_pp(0) = 3.91 × 10⁻²⁵ (1 ± 0.008) MeV·b, with accepted value S_pp(0) = 4.01 × 10⁻²⁵ (1 ± 0.009) MeV·b
- Solar hep process S-factor from chiral EFT: S_hep(0) = (8.6 ± 1.3) × 10⁻²⁰ keV·b
- Solar hep process S-factor from standard nuclear physics approach: S_hep^SNPA(0) = (10.1 ± 0.6) × 10⁻²⁰ keV·b
- Effective Gamow-Teller coupling in sd-shell and fp-shell nuclei: g_A* ≈ 0.8 g_A (with g_A ≈ 1.27 in free space), and g_A* = 1 suggested as "universally" applicable in nuclear medium

## Context

This paper reviews and extends the work of Korean nuclear theorists, particularly Tae-Sun Park, who performed the first chiral EFT calculations of the np capture (Park, 1990s) and the solar pp and hep processes (Park et al., 1998-2001), the latter directly responding to John Bahcall's 2000 challenge to nuclear theorists. The author contrasts the chiral EFT approach with the standard nuclear physics approach (SNPA) dating back to Chemtob and Rho (1970s) and Riska and Brown (1980s), arguing that while both yield similar numerical results, only chiral EFT provides a systematic expansion with controlled uncertainties. The dense matter predictions extend earlier work on hidden local symmetry (HLS) by Bando et al. (1980s) and scale symmetry implementation by Li, Ma, and Rho (2010s), and predict a precocious onset of conformal sound velocity in massive stars, contradicting results from other approaches in the literature.
