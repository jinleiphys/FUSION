# Non-rotating and rotating neutron stars in the extended field theoretical model

> We study the properties of non-rotating and rotating neutron stars for a new set of equations of state (EOSs) with different high density behaviour obtained using the extended field theoretical model. The high density behaviour for these EOSs are varied by varying the $ω-$meson self-coupling and hyperon-meson couplings in such a way that the quality of fit to the bulk nuclear observables, nuclear matter incompressibility coefficient and hyperon-nucleon potential depths remain practically unaffected. We find that the largest value for maximum mass for the non-rotating neutron star is $2.1M_\odot$. The radius for the neutron star with canonical mass is $12.8 - 14.1$ km provided only those EOSs are considered for which maximum mass is larger than $1.6M_\odot$ as it is the lower bound on the maximum mass measured so far. Our results for the very recently discovered fastest rotating neutron star indicate that this star is supra massive with mass $1.7 - 2.7M_\odot$ and circumferential equatorial radius $12 - 19$ km.

# Non-rotating and rotating neutron stars in the extended field theoretical model

## Key claim
The maximum mass for non-rotating neutron stars can reach up to 2.1 solar masses, but this value depends on the specific choices of omega-meson self-coupling and hyperon-meson couplings that leave the fit to bulk nuclear observables, nuclear matter incompressibility, and hyperon-nucleon potential depths practically unaffected. The radius for a canonical-mass neutron star is 12.8–14.1 km, but only when considering equations of state that yield a maximum mass larger than 1.6 solar masses (the lower bound on maximum mass measured so far).

## Method
The authors generated 21 parameter sets of the extended field theoretical relativistic mean-field (FTRMF) model by varying the omega-meson self-coupling (zeta = 0.0, 0.03, 0.06), the neutron-skin thickness for Pb-208 (0.16–0.28 fm), and the hyperon-meson couplings (X_omegaY from 0.5–0.8). They calibrated the remaining parameters by fitting to experimental total binding energies and charge rms radii for closed-shell nuclei, while keeping the nuclear matter incompressibility coefficient near 230 MeV. They then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for non-rotating stars and computed Keplerian sequences for rotating stars, using equations of state for matter composed of nucleons, hyperons, and leptons in beta-equilibrium.

## Key numbers
- Largest maximum mass for non-rotating neutron stars: 2.1 solar masses
- Radius for canonical-mass (1.4 solar masses) neutron star: 12.8–14.1 km, considering only equations of state with maximum mass above 1.6 solar masses
- Maximum mass range with hyperons included: 1.4–2.1 solar masses
- Maximum mass range without hyperons: 1.7–2.4 solar masses
- Radius for canonical-mass neutron star (R_1.4) with hyperons: 11.3–14.1 km
- Radius for canonical-mass neutron star without hyperons: 12.5–14.1 km
- Radiation radius (R_infinity) for canonical-mass neutron star: 14.2–16.8 km
- Redshift for canonical-mass neutron star: 0.22 plus or minus 0.03
- For the fastest rotating neutron star (1122 Hz, XTE J1739-285): mass 1.7–2.7 solar masses and circumferential equatorial radius 12–19 km
- Nuclear matter incompressibility coefficient: 230.24 plus or minus 9.80 MeV
- Saturation density: 0.148 plus or minus 0.003 fm^-3
- Binding energy per nucleon: 16.11 plus or minus 0.04 MeV
- Effective nucleon mass ratio (M*_N/M_N): 0.605 plus or minus 0.004
- Symmetry energy linear density dependence L: 80 plus or minus 20 MeV for neutron-skin thickness 0.16–0.28 fm

## Context
This work extends the FTRMF model studies by Muller (1996) and others, but with the crucial improvement that all parameter sets are calibrated to reproduce bulk nuclear observables, nuclear matter incompressibility near 230 MeV (consistent with experimental data from isoscalar giant monopole resonances), and hyperon-nucleon potential depths. Previous FTRMF models often used parameterizations that did not fit finite nuclei properties satisfactorily, leading to wider variations in neutron star properties (maximum mass 1.2–3.0 solar masses and R_1.4 of 10–16 km). The present work reduces these variations by about half. The results enable tighter constraints on the high-density equation of state, particularly through comparison with the recently discovered fastest rotating neutron star XTE J1739-285 (Kaaret 2006) and with future moment-of-inertia measurements from the binary pulsar system PSR J0737-3039A,B (Burgay 2003, Lyne 2004).
