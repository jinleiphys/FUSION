# A stringent upper limit on the direct 3$α$ decay of the Hoyle State in $^{12}$C

> We investigate an implication of the most recent observation of a second $J^π= 2^+$ state in $^{12}$C, which was measured using the $^{12}$C($γ$,$α$)$^8$Be$_{\textrm{(g.s.)}}$ reaction. In addition to the dissociation of $^{12}$C to an $α$-particle and $^8$Be in its ground state, a small fraction of events (2%) were identified as direct decays and decays to excited states in $^8$Be. This allowed a limit on the direct 3$α$ partial decay width to be determined as $Γ_{3α} < 32(4)$ keV. Since this 2$^+$ state is predicted by all theoretical models to be a collective excitation of the Hoyle state, the 3$α$ partial width of the Hoyle state is calculable from the ratio of 3$α$ decay penetrabilities of the Hoyle and 2$^+$ states. This was calculated using the semi-classical WKB approach and we deduce a stringent upper limit for the direct decay branching ratio of the Hoyle state of ${Γ_{3α} \over Γ} < 5.7 \times 10^{-6}$, over an order of magnitude lower than previously reported. This result places the direct measurement of this rare decay mode beyond current experimental capabilities.

## Key claim

By reanalyzing the decay of the second \(J^\pi = 2^+\) state in \(^{12}\)C at approximately 10 MeV, the authors infer an upper limit on the direct \(3\alpha\) decay branching ratio of the Hoyle state that is over an order of magnitude lower than previously reported, placing direct measurement beyond current experimental capabilities. This result is conditional on the assumption that the reduced \(3\alpha\) channel widths of the Hoyle state and the \(2^+\) state are equal, based on their shared underlying structure.

## Method

The authors reanalyzed the excitation curve for the second \(2^+\) state in \(^{12}\)C, measured using the \(^{12}\)C(\(\gamma\),\(\alpha\))\(^8\)Be\(_{\text{(g.s.)}}\) reaction at the HI\(\gamma\)S facility with an optical readout Time Projection Chamber (O-TPC). From a single-level \(R\)-matrix fit, they extracted the total width and branching ratio for non-\(\alpha_0\) events (2.0(2)%), yielding an upper limit on the direct \(3\alpha\) partial width of the \(2^+\) state. Using the semi-classical WKB approach implemented in the PeTA code, they calculated the ratio of \(3\alpha\) decay penetrabilities for the Hoyle and \(2^+\) states in hyperspherical coordinates, and applied this ratio to deduce the Hoyle state's direct \(3\alpha\) width.

## Key numbers

- The total width of the second \(2^+\) state is \(\Gamma = 1.60(13)\) MeV, from a single-level \(R\)-matrix fit (Freer, 2020).
- The fraction of non-\(\alpha_0\) events (direct decays and decays to excited \(^8\)Be) is 2.0(2)% of total on-resonance events at \(E_\gamma = 10.05\) MeV.
- The upper limit on the direct \(3\alpha\) partial width of the \(2^+\) state is \(\Gamma_{3\alpha} < 32(4)\) keV.
- The ratio of \(3\alpha\) penetrabilities \(P_{3\alpha}(\text{Hoyle})/P_{3\alpha}(2^+)\) is \(8(2) \times 10^{-10}\).
- The deduced upper limit on the \(3\alpha\) partial width of the Hoyle state is \(2.6(7) \times 10^{-5}\) eV.
- The resulting upper limit on the direct decay branching ratio of the Hoyle state is \(\Gamma_{3\alpha}/\Gamma < 5.7 \times 10^{-6}\).
- The total width of the Hoyle state is \(\Gamma = 8.5(1)\) eV.

## Context

This work extends the study of the Hoyle state's structure by providing a stringent upper limit on its direct \(3\alpha\) decay, which is two orders of magnitude below the most recent experimental limit of 0.019% (Rana, 2013). The result is inconsistent with predictions for an \(\alpha\)-condensate structure (Smith, 2017; Kokalova, 2006), which gave branching ratios around 0.06%, and with a recent calculation of 0.0036% (Zheng, 2018). It instead supports a \(^8\)Be + \(\alpha\) cluster configuration as predicted by molecular dynamics calculations (AMD, FMD). Future measurements of the direct decay at a level above this limit would challenge the assumption that the Hoyle and \(2^+\) states share the same reduced \(3\alpha\) width, prompting theoretical re-evaluation.
