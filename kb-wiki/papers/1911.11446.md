# The eikonal model of reactions involving exotic nuclei; Roy Glauber's legacy in today's nuclear physics

> In this contribution, the eikonal approximation developed by Roy Glauber to describe high-energy quantum collisions is presented. This approximation has been-and still is-extensively used to analyse reaction measurements performed to study the structure of nuclei far from stability. This presentation focuses more particularly on the application of the eikonal approximation to the study of halo nuclei in modern nuclear physics. To emphasise Roy Glauber's legacy in today's nuclear physics, recent extensions of this model are reviewed.

## Key claim

The eikonal approximation, originally developed by Roy Glauber for high-energy quantum collisions, remains a central tool for analyzing reactions involving exotic halo nuclei, but its validity is limited to beam energies above approximately 50 MeV/nucleon; recent extensions (including a Coulomb-correction and a semiclassical deflection correction) can extend its range down to about 20 MeV/nucleon for heavy targets, though no such correction works for light targets at low energy.

## Method

The paper reviews the eikonal approximation applied to reactions with one-neutron and two-neutron halo nuclei, modeled as few-body systems (core plus one or two loosely bound nucleons). The standard eikonal method factorizes the incoming plane wave from the scattering wave function and uses an adiabatic (sudden) approximation to neglect the projectile excitation energy relative to the beam energy. For breakup reactions, where the Coulomb interaction causes a divergence in the adiabatic eikonal phase, the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) solves the eikonal equation numerically without the adiabatic approximation, while the Coulomb-Corrected Eikonal (CCE) model replaces the Coulomb eikonal phase with its first-order perturbative estimate. For low-energy reactions on heavy targets, a semiclassical correction replaces the transverse impact parameter with the classical distance of closest approach.

## Key numbers

- $^{11}$Be: one-neutron halo nucleus with a neutron bound to a $^{10}$Be core by 500 keV.
- $^{11}$Li: two-neutron halo nucleus with two halo neutrons bound to a $^{9}$Li core by 370 keV.
- $^8$B: one-proton halo nucleus with a valence proton bound by 137 keV.
- $^{11}$Be half-life: 13 s; $^{11}$Li half-life: less than 10 ms.
- Knockout reaction: parallel-momentum distribution of $^{10}$Be from $^{11}$Be measured at 60 MeV/nucleon on a Be target (Aum00).
- Breakup of $^{11}$Be on C at 67 MeV/nucleon: cross section peak at about $E = 1.3$ MeV relative energy between neutron and core, corresponding to a known $\frac{5}{2}^+$ resonant state.
- Breakup of $^{15}$C on Pb at 68 MeV/nucleon: DEA calculation in perfect agreement with experiment (Nak09).
- Breakup of $^{11}$Be on Pb at 520 MeV/nucleon: relativistic CCE model agrees with experimental data (Pal03).
- Breakup of $^{15}$C on Pb at 605 MeV/nucleon: relativistic CCE calculations in excellent agreement with data (Pra03); calculations without relativistic correction confirm the significance of relativistic effects.
- Breakup of $^{11}$Li on Pb at 70 MeV/nucleon: CCE calculations agree with experimental energy and angular distributions (Nak06).
- Low-energy test: DEA fails to reproduce CDCC results for $^{15}$C on Pb at 20 MeV/nucleon; a semiclassical Coulomb-deflection correction (replacing $b$ by $b' = \eta/K + \sqrt{\eta^2/K^2 + b^2}$) restores agreement.
- No correction works for breakup of one-neutron halo nuclei on light targets down to 20 MeV/nucleon (HC18).

## Context

This paper reviews the legacy of Glauber’s eikonal approximation in modern nuclear physics, focusing on its application to halo nuclei (first described by Hansen and Jonson (1987)). It extends earlier work by showing how the eikonal model can be adapted for breakup reactions via the DEA (Capel, Goldstein, and Baye, 2005) and the CCE (Margueron, Bonaccorso, and Brink, 2003). The comparison with the fully quantal Continuum Discretised Coupled Channel (CDCC) model (Austern et al., 1987) validates the eikonal approach at intermediate energies. The recent extensions to relativistic energies (Moro, Crespo, and collaborators, 2019) and to three-body projectiles like $^{11}$Li (Baye, Capel, and Descouvemont, 2009; Pires, Descouvemont, and Baye, 2012) enable the analysis of data from GSI and RIKEN. The low-energy correction for Coulomb-dominated reactions (Fernandez-Garcia, Oulebsir, and Capel, 2014) opens the possibility of analyzing data from HIE-ISOLDE and FRIB, while the failure for light targets (Howell and Capel, 2018) marks the limit of the eikonal approximation.
