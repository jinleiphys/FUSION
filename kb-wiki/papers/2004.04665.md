# Magneto-Seebeck coefficient and Nernst coefficient of hot and dense hadron gas

> We discuss the thermoelectric effect of hot and dense hadron gas within the framework of the hadron resonance gas model. Using the relativistic Boltzmann equation within the relaxation time approximation we estimate the Seebeck coefficient of the hot and dense hadronic medium with a gradient in temperature and baryon chemical potential. The hadronic medium in this calculation is modeled by the hadron resonance gas (HRG) model with hadrons and their resonances up to a mass cutoff $Λ\sim 2.6$ GeV. We also extend the formalism of the thermoelectric effect for a nonvanishing magnetic field. The presence of magnetic field also leads to a Hall type thermoelectric coefficient (Nernst coefficient) for the hot and dense hadronic matter apart from a magneto-Seebeck coefficient. We find that generically in the presence of a magnetic field Seebeck coefficient decreases while the Nernst coefficient increases with the magnetic field. At higher temperature and/or baryon chemical potential these coefficients approach to their values at vanishing magnetic field.

## Key claim

The Seebeck coefficient of hot and dense hadron gas decreases in the presence of a magnetic field, while the Nernst coefficient increases, but only under the assumption that the magnetic field is weak enough to be treated as a perturbation to the strong interaction (i.e., the cyclotron frequency is small compared to the inverse relaxation time) and that Landau quantization of single-particle energies can be ignored.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation in the relaxation time approximation for a multi-component hadron gas described by the hadron resonance gas (HRG) model, including hadrons and resonances up to a mass cutoff of approximately 2.6 GeV. They derived the deviation from equilibrium distribution function in the presence of both an electric field and a magnetic field, then computed the electric current and heat current to extract the magneto-Seebeck coefficient (from the open-circuit condition) and the Nernst coefficient (the Hall-type thermoelectric coefficient). The heat current was defined with respect to the conserved baryon current, using the Gibbs-Duhem relation to relate gradients of temperature and baryon chemical potential.

## Key numbers

*   The hadron resonance gas model includes hadrons and resonances up to a mass cutoff of approximately 2.6 GeV.
*   The magnetic field strength considered is of the order of a few \(m_\pi^2 \sim 0.02\) GeV\(^2\) at RHIC energies, with smaller values expected at lower collision energies.
*   The Seebeck coefficient is defined as \(S = \mathcal{I}_{31} / (T^2 \sigma_{el})\), where \(\sigma_{el}\) is the electrical conductivity and \(\mathcal{I}_{31}\) is an integral over the hadron distribution functions weighted by charge, velocity, and the factor \((\epsilon_a - b_a \omega / n_B)\).
*   The Nernst coefficient is a Hall-type thermoelectric coefficient that is nonvanishing only for a finite magnetic field, arising from the term proportional to \((\omega_{c_a} \tau_a)\) in the electric current expression.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier study of the Seebeck coefficient in hadron gas (Das et al., 2019) by including a gradient in baryon chemical potential (via the Gibbs-Duhem relation) and by incorporating a magnetic field to compute both the magneto-Seebeck and Nernst coefficients. It builds on the formalism of thermoelectric effects in condensed matter systems (Callen, 1948; Sofo & Mahan, 1994) and adapts it to relativistic systems where heat current is defined with respect to the conserved baryon current, following the approach of Gavin (1985). The results are relevant for low-energy heavy-ion collision experiments at FAIR and NICA, where a baryon-rich medium with finite temperature gradients is expected, and they enable future magnetohydrodynamic simulations that include thermoelectric transport.
