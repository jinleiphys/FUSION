# Spontaneous symmetry breaking in single and molecular quantum dots

> Classes of spontaneous symmetry breaking at zero and low magnetic fields in single quantum dots (QD's) and quantum dot molecules (QDM's) are discussed in relation to the ratio R_W between the interelectron Coulomb repulsion and the harmonic confinement, using spin-and-Space unrestricted Hartree-Fock calculations. These include: Wigner crystallization for R_W > 1, and formation of non-crystallized electron puddles localized on the individual dots in QDM's, as well as spin-density waves in single QD's, for R_W < 1. Erratum: Subsequent to the the publication of our Letter, we have performed further systematic spin-and-space unrestricted Hartree-Fock calculations. While the behavior and magnitudes of the addition energies shown in our Letter are maintained, as well as our finding pertaining to the prevalent violation of Hund's first rule, our improved calculations yield in certain instances different spin polarizations.

# Spontaneous symmetry breaking in single and molecular quantum dots

## Key claim

Using spin-and-space unrestricted Hartree-Fock (sS-UHF) calculations, the authors identify three classes of spontaneous symmetry breaking in quantum dots at zero and low magnetic fields, governed by the ratio R_W between Coulomb repulsion and harmonic confinement. However, an erratum notes that while the addition energy magnitudes and the prevalent violation of Hund's first rule are maintained, improved calculations yield different spin polarizations in certain instances (e.g., N=4, 8, 9, 14, 15, 16, 18, and 22 for single quantum dots).

## Method

The authors solve spin-and-space unrestricted Hartree-Fock (sS-UHF) equations using the Pople-Nesbet-Roothaan formalism for two-dimensional single quantum dots and lateral quantum dot molecules (QDM) described by a two-center-oscillator confinement with a smooth neck potential. The calculations use a basis of eigenstates of the single-particle Hamiltonian, with parameters for GaAs (m* = 0.067 m_e, hbar omega_0 = 5 meV) and dielectric constants kappa = 12.9 (R_W = 1.48) and kappa = 20 (R_W = 0.95). The method employs different orbitals for different spin directions and relaxes symmetry constraints of the external confining potential.

## Key numbers

- R_W = 1.48 (kappa = 12.9) yields Wigner crystallization for both single QDs and QDMs; R_W = 0.95 (kappa = 20) yields no Wigner crystallization
- For a 12-electron QDM with d = 70 nm, V_b = 10 meV, and R_W = 1.48 at B = 0 T: mean distance between neighboring density maxima inside each coupled dot is approximately 20 nm, roughly twice the effective Bohr radius a*_B = 10.188 nm
- For a 6-electron single QD with kappa = 6.0 (R_W = 3.18): the fully spin-polarized (P = 6) isomer is lower in energy by 1.72 meV than the P = 0 isomer
- For a 6-electron QDM with R_W = 1.48: the Wigner-crystallized singlet ground state has an excited state with P = 2 only 0.09 meV higher in energy
- For a single QD with kappa = 12.9: addition energies display maxima at closed shells (N = 6, 12, 20) and mid-shell closures (N = 4, 9, 16), but spin polarizations violate Hund's rule for N = 4, 8, 9, 14, 15, 16, 18, and 22
- For N = 17 in a single QD: the energy difference between the Hund (P = 3) and non-Hund (P = 1) isomers is 0.05 meV

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of Wigner crystallization in quantum dots under high magnetic fields by Muller and Koonin (1996) and Maksym (1993) to the zero and low magnetic field regime, using a self-consistent quantum-mechanical treatment that simultaneously allows spin and spatial unrestricted Hartree-Fock. The authors contrast their sS-UHF approach with local-spin-density functional (LSD) calculations by Koskinen et al. (1997) and Barnett et al. (1999), noting that LSD cannot generally yield crystallized solutions. The findings enable mapping of phase boundaries between Wigner-crystallized, electron puddle, and central mean-field regimes through variations of material parameters (dielectric constant), gate voltages, interdot distances, barrier heights, and magnetic fields, as well as spectroscopic probing of Goldstone modes in the symmetry-broken states.
