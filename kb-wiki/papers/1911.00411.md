# Neutron rich matter in the laboratory and in the heavens after GW170817

> The historic observations of the neutron star merger GW170817 advanced our understanding of r-process nucleosynthesis and the equation of state (EOS) of neutron rich matter. Simple neutrino physics suggests that supernovae are not the site of the main r-process. Instead, the very red color of the kilonova associated with GW170817 shows that neutron star (NS) mergers are an important r-process site. We now need to measure the masses and beta decay half-lives of very neutron rich heavy nuclei so that we can more accurately predict the abundances of heavy elements that are produced. This can be done with new radioactive beam accelerators such as the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB). GW170817 provided information on the deformability of NS and the equation of state of dense matter. The PREX II experiment will measure the neutron skin of ${}^{208}$Pb and help constrain the low density EOS. As the sensitivity of gravitational wave detectors improve, we expect to observe many more events. We look forward to exciting advances and surprises!

# Neutron rich matter in the laboratory and in the heavens after GW170817

## Key claim
The gravitational wave event GW170817 and its associated kilonova provide strong evidence that neutron star mergers are an important site for the main r-process, while simple neutrino physics suggests that supernovae are not the site of the main r-process. However, the conclusion that supernovae are not the r-process site depends on modern simulations finding that electron neutrinos have only slightly lower energies than antineutrinos, making the neutrino-driven wind not neutron rich enough for heavy r-process production, and this conclusion could be altered by unexpected neutrino oscillations.

## Method
The paper reviews multi-messenger constraints on neutron rich matter from the GW170817 neutron star merger, combining gravitational wave measurements of neutron star deformability with laboratory measurements of neutron skin thickness in 208Pb from the PREX experiment. The author also analyzes neutrino detection capabilities from future galactic supernovae using Super-Kamiokande, Hyper-Kamiokande, and DUNE, and examines heat capacity measurements of neutron star cores from transiently accreting neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries to constrain the composition of dense matter.

## Key numbers
- The PREX experiment measured the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb as R_skin^208 = 0.33 (+0.16/-0.18) fm, with PREX-II aiming for 0.06 fm sensitivity
- GW170817 was nearly a thousand times further away than SN1987A, corresponding to a neutrino count rate lower by a factor of one million
- Core collapse supernovae radiate approximately 10^58 neutrinos, carrying almost 0.2 solar masses times c^2 of gravitational binding energy
- About 20 neutrino events were detected from SN1987A, while several thousand events are expected from the next galactic supernova in Super-Kamiokande and approximately 100,000 events in Hyper-Kamiokande
- Normal nuclear density is rho_0 = 3 x 10^14 g/cm^3
- The heat capacity of a neutron star modeled with the APR equation of state with all nucleons unpaired is greater than 10^38 erg/K, while with all nucleons paired only electrons and muons contribute

## Context
This review extends the discussion of r-process nucleosynthesis and the equation of state following the GW170817 event, building on earlier work by Horowitz (2001) on neutrino physics in supernovae and the PREX collaboration results from 2012-2013. The paper connects laboratory measurements of neutron skins to astrophysical observations of neutron star radii and deformabilities, as previously explored by PhysRevLett.120.172702 (2018). The heat capacity measurements of accreting neutron stars from PhysRevC.95.025806 (2017) are used to rule out extreme color superconducting quark matter in neutron stars. The paper identifies open questions about the amounts, velocities, and compositions of different ejecta components in neutron star mergers, and emphasizes the need for measurements of masses and beta decay half-lives of very neutron rich heavy nuclei at FRIB to improve r-process abundance predictions.
