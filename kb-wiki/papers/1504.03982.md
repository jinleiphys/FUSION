# Thermal properties of hot and dense matter with finite range interactions

> We explore the thermal properties of hot and dense matter using a model that reproduces the empirical properties of isospin symmetric and asymmetric bulk nuclear matter, optical model fits to nucleon-nucleus scattering data, heavy-ion flow data in the energy range 0.5-2 GeV/A, and the largest well-measured neutron star mass of 2 $\rm{M}_\odot$. Results of this model which incorporates finite range interactions through Yukawa type forces are contrasted with those of a zero-range Skyrme model that yields nearly identical zero-temperature properties at all densities for symmetric and asymmetric nucleonic matter and the maximum neutron star mass, but fails to account for heavy-ion flow data due to the lack of an appropriate momentum dependence in its mean field. Similarities and differences in the thermal state variables and the specific heats between the two models are highlighted. Checks of our exact numerical calculations are performed from formulas derived in the strongly degenerate and non-degenerate limits. Our studies of the thermal and adiabatic indices, and the speed of sound in hot and dense matter for conditions of relevance to core-collapse supernovae, the thermal evolution of neutron stars from their birth and mergers of compact binary stars reveal that substantial variations begin to occur at sub-saturation densities before asymptotic values are reached at supra-nuclear densities.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a finite-range Yukawa-type interaction model (MDI(A)) which reproduces heavy-ion flow data and supports a 2 solar mass neutron star yields thermal properties that differ substantially from those of a zero-range Skyrme model (SkO') with nearly identical zero-temperature properties, with the differences traced primarily to the density dependence of the nucleon effective masses.

## Method

The authors employed the momentum-dependent interaction (MDI) model with Yukawa-type finite-range forces, solved self-consistently at finite temperature through iterative determination of the single-particle potentials, chemical potentials, and phase-space distributions. They contrasted these results with those from the zero-range Skyrme SkO' parametrization that was tuned to match the same zero-temperature properties (energy per particle, pressure, maximum neutron star mass) but lacks the appropriate momentum dependence in its mean field. Exact numerical calculations were checked against analytical formulas derived in the strongly degenerate and non-degenerate limits.

## Key numbers

- The MDI(A) model supports a maximum neutron star mass of 2 solar masses, consistent with the well-measured neutron star masses of 1.97 ± 0.04 M⊙ (Demorest) and 2.01 ± 0.04 M⊙ (Antoniadis)
- Heavy-ion flow data in the energy range 0.5-2 GeV/A are reproduced by the finite-range model but not by the zero-range Skyrme model
- The equilibrium density of isospin symmetric matter is n0 ≈ 0.16 fm^-3
- The symmetric matter compression modulus from giant monopole resonance data is K0 ≈ 240 ± 20 MeV
- For the LS220 EOS at densities ρs/3 to ρs/2, thermal pressure enhancement reaches a factor of 5 times the cold value; for the Hshen EOS it reaches a factor of 3
- At constant temperature T = 50 MeV, the maximum mass of a hot Hshen star increased by 15%, while that of a hot LS220 star increased by approximately 5%
- For thermal profiles similar to Sekiguchi (2011) simulations, mass increases were 3.5% (Hshen) and 1.8% (LS220)
- Neutron star radius is estimated at R = 12 ± 0.5 km from concordance of nuclear physics data, theoretical neutron matter studies, and astrophysical observations

## Context

This work extends the MDI model of Das et al. (2003) and Welke et al. (1988) to finite temperature, contrasting it with the zero-range Skyrme SkO' parametrization of Reinhard and Flocard (1995). The study addresses the fact that commonly used EOS tables (Lattimer and Swesty 1991, Shen et al. 1998, Hempel and Schaffner-Bielich 2012) fail to reproduce heavy-ion flow data at zero temperature due to incorrect momentum dependence in their mean fields. The results enable more realistic simulations of core-collapse supernovae, proto-neutron star evolution, and binary neutron star mergers, where thermal effects become significant at sub-saturation densities before asymptotic values are reached at supra-nuclear densities.
