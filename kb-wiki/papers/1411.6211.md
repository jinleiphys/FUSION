# Elastic Enhancement Factor: from Mesoscopic Systems to Macroscopic Analogous Devices

> Excess of probabilities of elastic processes over inelastic ones is a characteristic feature of the chaotic resonance scattering predicted by the random matrix theory (RMT). Quantitatively, this phenomenon is characterized by the elastic enhancement factor $F^{(β)}$ that is, essentially, a typical ratio of elastic and inelastic cross sections. Being measured experimentally, this quantity can provide important information on the character of dynamics of the complicated intermediate open system formed on the intermediate stage of various resonance scattering processes. We discuss properties of the enhancement factor in a wide scope from mesoscopoic systems as, for example, heavy nuclei to macroscopic electromagnetic analogous devices imitating two-dimensional quantum billiards. We demonstrate a substantial qualitative distinction between the elastic enhancement factor's peculiarities in these two cases. A complete analytical solution is found for the case of systems without time-reversal symmetry and only a few equivalent open scattering channels.

# Elastic Enhancement Factor: from Mesoscopic Systems to Macroscopic Analogous Devices

## Key claim
The elastic enhancement factor, which quantifies the excess of elastic over inelastic scattering probabilities in chaotic resonance scattering, behaves qualitatively differently in mesoscopic systems (such as heavy nuclei with many weak channels) compared to macroscopic electromagnetic analogues (such as microwave cavities with few channels). A complete analytical solution exists only for systems without time-reversal symmetry and with a few equivalent open channels; for systems with time-reversal symmetry, only numerical results are available.

## Method
The authors derive analytical expressions for the elastic enhancement factor \(F^{(\beta)}\) using random matrix theory, considering two regimes: the Verbaarschot regime (many weak channels, \(M \gg 1\), \(T \ll 1\)) where the factor depends only on the parameter \(\eta = MT\), and the few-channel regime (\(M = 2, 3, ...\)) where it depends separately on channel number \(M\) and transmission coefficient \(T\). For systems without time-reversal symmetry (\(\beta = 2\)), explicit analytical formulas are obtained for arbitrary \(M\) and for the two-channel case including absorption effects; for systems with time-reversal symmetry (\(\beta = 1\)), numerical calculations are performed.

## Key numbers
- For the Verbaarschot regime with broken time-reversal symmetry: \(F^{(2)}(\eta) = 1 + (1 - e^{-\eta})/\eta\), with limits \(F^{(2)}(0) = 2\) and \(F^{(2)}(\infty) = 1\).
- For the Verbaarschot regime with preserved time-reversal symmetry: \(F^{(1)}(\eta) = F^{(2)}(\eta) + 1 - \left(\frac{1+\eta/2}{\eta}e^{-\eta} - \frac{1-\eta/2}{\eta}\right)e^{\eta/2} Ei(-\eta/2)\), with limits \(F^{(1)}(0) = 3\) and \(F^{(1)}(\infty) = 2\).
- The slopes at the origin are identical for both symmetry classes: \(dF^{(\beta)}(\eta)/d\eta|_{\eta=0} = -1/2\).
- For two equivalent channels without time-reversal symmetry: \(F_2^{(2)}(T) = (4 - T)/(2 + T)\).
- For three equivalent channels without time-reversal symmetry: \(F_3^{(2)}(T) = 2(6 - 3T + T^2)/(6 + 3T - T^2)\).
- For two channels with absorption at \(T = 0\): \(F(T=0,\gamma) = 1 + 2/[\gamma(1 + \coth(\gamma/2))]\).
- In the Sirko (2014) experiments with 2D electromagnetic resonators, the absorption strength was in the interval \(5.2 < \gamma < 7.4\).

## Context
This work extends the random matrix theory framework for chaotic resonance scattering established by Weidenmüller (1984) and Fyodorov (2005), and builds on the Verbaarschot (1985) regime for mesoscopic systems with many weak channels. The authors provide the first complete analytical solution for systems without time-reversal symmetry with arbitrary fixed numbers of equivalent channels, and they compare their results with experimental measurements from microwave cavity analogues by Sirko (2014). The findings enable quantitative interpretation of elastic enhancement measurements in both nuclear reactions and laboratory electromagnetic analogues, while clarifying the distinct parameter regimes governing each type of system.
