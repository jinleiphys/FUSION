# Protoneutron stars with kaon condensate

> A new formulation is presented to treat fluctuations around the kaon condensate. Equation of state (EOS) is given for isothermal and isentropic cases in the heavy-baryon-limit (HBL). The coexistent phase appears in the latter case. The mass-radius relation is given for protoneutron stars and the possibility of the delayed collapse is discussed.

# Protoneutron stars with kaon condensate

## Key claim
The authors present a new formalism for treating thermal fluctuations around the kaon condensate in protoneutron stars, finding that the equation of state exhibits a first-order phase transition and that thermal effects are insufficient to support higher masses, in fact, the maximum mass is slightly reduced at nonzero temperature, contrary to the expectation that thermal pressure might delay collapse.

## Method
The authors start from the nonlinear sigma model and introduce a local coordinate on the chiral manifold to parametrize fluctuations around the kaon condensate. They derive the thermodynamic potential via path integral methods, obtaining the inverse thermal Green function for kaons and solving for the dispersion relation, which yields a Goldstone mode with Bogoliubov spectrum. The equation of state is then computed in the heavy-baryon-limit for both isothermal and isentropic cases, using Maxwell construction for the first-order phase transition.

## Key numbers
- Maximum mass of protoneutron stars is around 1.6 solar masses (1.6 M⊙)
- Temperature at the center of protoneutron-star matter reaches several tens of MeV for entropies s = 1, 2
- The Goldstone mode dispersion relation is E₋² ∼ (c₃/2C²)p² + p⁴/(4C²) + ..., where C is an effective mass for kaons and c₃ is the product of charge density and KK scattering length

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of kaon condensation in neutron stars at low temperature, particularly the low-mass black hole scenario proposed by Brown and Bethe (1994) based on softening of the equation of state due to kaon condensation. The authors build on their own previous formalism (Tatsumi and Yasuhira, 1998) for treating thermal fluctuations, and note that prior numerical simulations of delayed collapse (Baumgarte et al., 1996; Pons et al., 1999) used zero-temperature equations of state despite the high temperatures involved. The results are relevant for understanding the delayed collapse during the initial cooling era when neutrinos are no longer trapped, and the authors suggest that dynamical simulations incorporating these effects could illuminate the collapsing process and neutrino luminosity profile.
