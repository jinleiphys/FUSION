# Saturation of product's exoticity in compound nuclear reactions and its role in the production of new n-deficient nuclei with radioactive projectiles

> Representation in terms of a new parameter, exoticity, a measure of n-deficiency or p-richness, clearly brings out the saturation tendency of the product's maximum exoticity in a compound nuclear reaction as the compound nucleus is made more and more exotic using radioactive projectile (RIBs). The effect of this saturation on the production of new proton-rich species with RIBs over a wide Z-range has been discussed.

# Saturation of product's exoticity in compound nuclear reactions and its role in the production of new n-deficient nuclei with radioactive projectiles

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in compound nuclear reactions, the maximum exoticity (n-deficiency or p-richness) of evaporation products saturates as the compound nucleus is made more exotic, contradicting the naive expectation that "more exotic the compound nucleus, more exotic is the product." This saturation occurs because beyond a certain point, the effective separation energy of a proton (binding energy plus Coulomb barrier) becomes lower than that of a neutron, making proton evaporation more likely and limiting how far toward the proton drip line the products can reach.

## Method

The authors defined a Z-independent parameter called "exoticity" (zeta) ranging from 0 on the beta-stability line to 1 at the proton drip line, using mass numbers from the Janecke and Masson compilation. They computed production cross-sections using the statistical model code ALICE for compound nuclei with Z between 50 and 82, considering both stable projectile-target combinations and p-rich radioactive ion beams (RIBs). For compound nuclei beyond the proton drip line, ground state proton decay lifetimes were calculated using WKB approximation, and only those with lifetimes greater than 10^-14 seconds were considered. The maximum exoticity of products with cross-sections greater than 1 mb (and 0.01 mb in some cases) was plotted against compound nucleus exoticity for various Z values.

## Key numbers

- For Cerium (Ce), the compound nucleus exoticity at which the neutron separation energy equals the effective proton separation energy (B_n = B_p*) is zeta_CN = 0.54, corresponding to A = 127.
- The lightest Ce compound nucleus reachable with stable target-stable projectile combinations is ^122Ce, with zeta_CN = 0.75.
- For odd-Z products, the proton drip line can be reached with cross-sections >= 1 mb for all nuclei with Z >= 51 using RIBs that are 4 neutrons deficient from the lightest beta-stable isotopes.
- With a relaxed cross-section limit of 0.01 mb, the drip line can be reached for odd-Z nuclei with Z >= 45 using the same 4-neutron deficient projectiles.
- For even-Z products with 1 mb cross-section limit, the drip line can be reached only for Z >= 80 using 4-neutron deficient projectiles; with 0.01 mb limit, even-Z drip line can be reached for Z >= 66.
- The production cross-section of ^150Yb (a new and very exotic nucleus) using a ^60Zn RIB (zeta_CN = 0.93) is about 300 times larger than with the stable projectile ^64Zn (zeta_CN = 0.76).
- With the stable projectile, the production cross-section of ^150Yb is almost four orders of magnitude less than the most favoured channel, whereas with the RIB (zeta_CN = 0.93), ^150Yb is produced with the maximum cross-section.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of compound nuclear reactions for producing neutron-deficient nuclei, which had been studied extensively over the previous two decades using stable heavy-ion fusion. The paper introduces the concept of exoticity as a Z-independent measure, building on the proton drip-line compilation of Janecke and Masson (1988). The findings show that while saturation limits the utility of very p-rich projectiles for producing even-Z drip-line nuclei (especially for Z <= 65), it does not seriously affect production of odd-Z nuclei near the drip line. The results enable experimental planning for future low-energy radioactive ion beam facilities by quantifying the beam intensity and cross-section requirements needed to overcome this saturation effect through improved detection efficiency and background rejection.
