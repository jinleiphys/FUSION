# Reliable estimation of the radius of convergence in finite density QCD

> We study different estimators of the radius of convergence of the Taylor series of the pressure in finite density QCD. We adopt the approach in which the radius of convergence is estimated first in a finite volume, and the infinite-volume limit is taken later. This requires an estimator for the radius of convergence that is reliable in a finite volume. Based on general arguments about the analytic structure of the partition function in a finite volume, we demonstrate that the ratio estimator cannot work in this approach, and propose three new estimators, capable of extracting reliably the radius of convergence, which coincides with the distance from the origin of the closest Lee-Yang zero. We also provide an estimator for the phase of the closest Lee-Yang zero, necessary to assess whether the leading singularity is a true critical point. We demonstrate the usage of these estimators on a toy model, namely 4 flavors of unimproved staggered fermions on a small $6^3 \times 4$ lattice, where both the radius of convergence and the Taylor coefficients to any order can be obtained by a direct determination of the Lee-Yang zeros. Interestingly, while the relative statistical error of the Taylor expansion coefficients steadily grows with order, that of our estimators stabilizes, allowing for an accurate estimate of the radius of convergence. In particular, we show that despite the more than 100\% error bars on high-order Taylor coefficients, the given ensemble contains enough information about the radius of convergence.

## Key claim
The ratio estimator for the radius of convergence of the Taylor series of the pressure in finite density QCD fails in a finite volume unless the leading Lee-Yang zero lies exactly on the real axis or at specific angles (0, ±π/2, π), which excludes generic crossover transitions; the authors propose three new estimators that reliably extract the radius of convergence from high-order Taylor coefficients even when those coefficients have statistical errors exceeding 100%.

## Method
The authors analyze the analytic structure of the QCD partition function in a finite volume, showing that the Taylor expansion coefficients are dominated by complex-conjugate pairs of Lee-Yang zeros. They derive three new estimators, the modified Mercer-Roberts estimator, the doubled index estimator, and the doubled index ratio estimator, that exactly recover the distance to the closest Lee-Yang zero in the single-zero approximation, and also provide a phase estimator. They test these estimators on a toy model of N_f=4 unimproved staggered fermions on a 6^3 × 4 lattice at β=4.940 with bare fermion mass ma=0.01, using 16000 configurations, where the Lee-Yang zeros can be computed directly from the eigenvalues of the reduced matrix P.

## Key numbers
- The toy model uses a 6^3 × 4 lattice (V = 6^3 spatial volume, N_t = 4 time slices) with N_f = 4 unimproved staggered fermions at bare fermion mass ma = 0.01 and gauge coupling β = 4.940.
- The ensemble consists of 16000 configurations, each separated by 10 HMC trajectories.
- The partition function has 2kV = 2 × 3 × 216 = 1296 Lee-Yang zeros in the complex fugacity plane (k = 3 for this model).
- The relative statistical error of the Taylor expansion coefficients grows as O(k) with order k, while the statistical errors of the proposed estimators stabilize to a finite value (the error of the distance to the closest zero) at asymptotically large k.

## Context
This work extends the study of Lee-Yang zeros (Lee 1952) and their relation to the phase diagram of QCD, specifically addressing the failure of the ratio estimator previously used in finite-density lattice QCD studies (D'Elia 2016, Bazavov 2017, Fodor 2018). The proposed estimators enable reliable extraction of the radius of convergence from high-order Taylor coefficients even when the sign problem makes individual coefficients unreliable, and the phase estimator allows assessment of whether the leading singularity corresponds to a true critical point. This approach is complementary to direct calculations of Taylor coefficients at μ=0 (Allton 2002, Borsanyi 2011, Bazavov 2017) and simulations at imaginary chemical potential (de Forcrand 2002, D'Elia 2002, Bonati 2014).
