# Proton fraction in the inner neutron-star crust

> Monte Carlo simulations of neutron-rich matter of relevance to the inner neutron-star crust are performed for a system of A=5,000 nucleons. To determine the proton fraction in the inner crust, numerical simulations are carried out for a variety of densities and proton fractions. We conclude---as others have before us using different techniques---that the proton fraction in the inner stellar crust is very small. Given that the purported "nuclear pasta" phase in stellar crusts develops as a consequence of the long-range Coulomb interaction among protons, we question whether pasta formation is possible in such proton-poor environments. To answer this question, we search for physical observables sensitive to the transition between spherical nuclei and exotic pasta structures. Of particular relevance is the static structure factor S(k)---an observable sensitive to density fluctuations. However, no dramatic behavior was observed in S(k). We regard the identification of physical observables sensitive to the existence---or lack-thereof---of a pasta phase in proton-poor environments as an open problem of critical importance.

## Key claim

The authors conclude that the proton fraction in the inner neutron-star crust is very small (a few percent or less) across the density range relevant to pasta formation, and they question whether nuclear pasta phases can form in such proton-poor environments. The hedge is that this conclusion depends on the assumption that the dilute neutron vapor contributes little to the energy while electrons contribute a lot, which the authors describe as "robust physics that should be fairly model independent."

## Method

Monte Carlo simulations were performed for a system of A=5,000 nucleons using a semi-classical model with short-range nuclear attraction and long-range Coulomb repulsion, with the Coulomb interaction evaluated exactly via an Ewald summation. The simulations were carried out at a fixed temperature of T=1 MeV for baryon densities from 0.01 to 0.08 fm^-3 and proton fractions from Y_p=0 to Y_p=0.25, using a Metropolis algorithm with 3,000 Monte Carlo sweeps (2,500 for thermalization, 500 for observables). The optimal proton fraction was determined by minimizing the total energy per nucleon (including kinetic energies of neutrons, protons, and electrons) with respect to proton fraction at fixed density, imposing beta-equilibrium.

## Key numbers

- Proton fraction at density ρ=0.05 fm^-3: Y_p ≈ 0.006 (assuming no neutron vapor contribution) or Y_p ≈ 0.025 (assuming all neutrons contribute as a free Fermi gas)
- System size: A=5,000 nucleons in all simulations
- Temperature: T=1 MeV used to simulate quantum zero-point motion
- Density range explored: ρ=0.01 to 0.08 fm^-3
- Proton fraction range simulated: Y_p=0 to Y_p=0.25
- Smearing parameter for Ewald summation: s_0=0.12 (dimensionless)
- Quadratic fit coefficients for potential energy per nucleon at ρ=0.05 fm^-3: v_0=1.2924 MeV, v_1=-39.234 MeV, v_2=11.648 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous Monte Carlo simulations by Horowitz et al. (2004, 2005) that used a fixed proton fraction of Y_p=0.2 and a screened Coulomb interaction, by instead evaluating the Coulomb interaction exactly via Ewald summation and exploring a range of proton fractions to determine the beta-equilibrium value. The results confirm predictions from mean-field models such as those by Maruyama et al. (2005), Avancini et al. (2008), and Shen et al. (2011) that the proton fraction in the inner crust is only a few percent. The authors identify the identification of physical observables sensitive to pasta formation in proton-poor environments as an open problem, noting that the static structure factor S(k) showed no dramatic behavior indicative of a pasta phase transition.
