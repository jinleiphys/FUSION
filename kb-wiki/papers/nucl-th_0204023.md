# Chemical Freezeout in Heavy Ion Collisions

> We construct a hadronic equation of state consistent with chemical freezeout and discuss how such an equation of state modifies the radial and elliptic flow in a hydrodynamic + hadronic cascade model of relativistic heavy ion collisions at the SPS. Incorporating chemical freezeout does not change the relation between pressure and energy density. However, it does change the relation between temperature and energy density. Consequently, when the hydrodynamic solution and freezeout are expressed in terms of energy density, chemical freezeout does not modify the hydrodynamic radial and elliptic flow velocities studied previously. Finally, we examine chemical freezeout within the hadronic cascade (RQMD). Once chemical freezeout is incorporated into the hydrodynamics, the final spectra and fireball lifetimes are insensitive to the temperature at which the switch from hydrodynamics to cascade is made. Closer inspection indicates that the pion spectrum in chemically frozen hydrodynamics is significantly cooler than in the hydro+cascade model. This difference is reflected in $v_{2}(p_{T})$. We extract the freezeout hadron density in RQMD and interpret it in thermal terms; the freezeout hadron density corresponds to a freezeout temperature of $T_{f}\approx100 $ MeV and $μ_π \approx 80 $ MeV.

## Key claim

Incorporating chemical freezeout into the hadronic equation of state does not change the relation between pressure and energy density, so when the hydrodynamic solution and freezeout are expressed in terms of energy density, chemical freezeout does not modify the radial and elliptic flow velocities studied previously. However, the temperature on a freezeout surface of constant energy density depends dramatically on chemical freezeout, with the pion spectrum in chemically frozen hydrodynamics being significantly cooler than in the hydro+cascade model.

## Method

The author constructed a hadronic equation of state that includes 16 conserved currents (baryon number, strangeness, isospin, and 13 hadron species such as pions, kaons, and hyperons) to enforce chemical freezeout below the critical temperature. This equation of state was implemented in a (2+1) ideal fluid dynamics model with Bjorken scaling, using initial conditions matched to SPS PbPb collisions at sqrt(s)=17 GeV A with impact parameter b=6 fm and entropy per baryon ratio s/n_B=42. The hydrodynamic evolution was switched to the hadronic cascade RQMD at a variable switching temperature T_switch, and the resulting spectra, elliptic flow, and freezeout densities were compared between cases with and without chemical freezeout.

## Key numbers

- The freezeout hadron density in RQMD corresponds to a freezeout temperature of T_f approximately 100 MeV and mu_pi approximately 80 MeV.
- At the SPS, the initial conditions are characterized by an entropy per baryon ratio s/n_B = 42 and an average initial energy density <epsilon>|_tau0 = 5.4 GeV/fm^3.
- The equation of state has a latent heat of 0.8 GeV/fm^3.
- For T_switch between 115 MeV and 160 MeV, the model spectra are insensitive to variations when chemical freezeout is incorporated.
- Without chemical freezeout, the yields of K and anti-p are reduced by factors of e^(-M_K/T - M_K/T_c) and e^(-M_N/T - M_N/T_c), or 3 and 7 for temperatures of approximately 120 MeV.
- The elliptic flow increases by 25 percent as the switching temperature is lowered from 160 MeV to 117 MeV.
- At an energy density of 0.091 GeV/fm^3, the temperature with mu_pi>0 is 100 MeV, while without chemical freezeout it is 129 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the hydro+cascade model H2H (Teaney, Lauret, and Shuryak, 2001) by consistently incorporating chemical freezeout into the hydrodynamic evolution, addressing the problem that previous hydrodynamic calculations assumed both thermal and chemical equilibrium when chemical equilibration is certainly impossible during the approximately 10 fm/c hadronic stage. The approach builds on prior work on chemical freezeout in hadron gases (Bebie, Gerber, Goity, and Leutwyler, 1992; Shuryak, 1988; Gerber, Leutwyler, and Goity, 1990) and similar recent efforts (Arbex, Grassi, Hama, and Socolowski, 2001; Hirano and Tsuda, 2002). The results enable more consistent comparisons between hydrodynamic models and experimental data at both SPS and RHIC, and provide a framework for understanding the universal freezeout density observed in hadronic cascades.
