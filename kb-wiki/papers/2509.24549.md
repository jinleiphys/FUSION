# Anomaly in first-forbidden transitions of $^{176}$Lu

> $^{176}$Lu is the key nucleus for understanding the evolution of planetary bodies in the solar system, and the temperature and nucleosynthesis of the $s$ process, due to its very long terrestrial half-life $T_{1/2} \approx 3.7\times 10^{10}$ years. The very long half-life is caused by two anomalous first-forbidden transitions from the $7^-$ state with extremely large comparative half-life log$ft \approx 19$ which have never been appeared in other cases of the existing nuclear databases. We analyze the underlying mechanism and reason for the anomaly in first-forbidden transitions of $^{176}$Lu for the first time, which is based on the projected shell model. It is found that the possible $K$-forbidden nature is indispensable for describing the two extremely weak first-forbidden transitions, and the transition strengths are very sensitive to the detailed configuration mixing and $K$ mixing in the nuclear wave functions. The half-life of the $7^-$ state is calculated to be $T_{1/2} \approx 1.95 \times 10^{10}$ years.

## Key claim

The two anomalously weak first-forbidden beta-decay transitions from the \(7^-\) ground state of \(^{176}\)Lu, which have log\(ft \approx 19\) and are unique in nuclear databases, are explained by a \(K\)-forbidden nature; the transition strengths are extremely sensitive to the detailed configuration mixing and \(K\) mixing in the nuclear wave functions, and the half-life is calculated to be \(T_{1/2} \approx 1.95 \times 10^{10}\) years without using any phenomenological quenching factors.

## Method

The authors used the projected shell model (PSM) with large model and configuration spaces, including up to 4-quasiparticle configurations, to calculate the nine nuclear matrix elements for first-forbidden transitions. Nuclear many-body wave functions with good spin and parity were obtained in the laboratory frame via exact angular-momentum projection. The partial decay rates and log\(ft\) values were computed from the shape factor \(C(W)\), and the role of \(K\) forbiddenness was analyzed by comparing calculations with no mixing, a small configuration space (up to 2-quasiparticle), and a large configuration space (including 4-quasiparticle configurations).

## Key numbers

- The terrestrial half-life of \(^{176}\)Lu is \(T_{1/2} \approx 3.7 \times 10^{10}\) years, with a \(Q_{\beta} \approx 1.2\) MeV.
- The two anomalous first-forbidden transitions from the \(7^-\) state have log\(ft \approx 19\), which is far larger than the typical log\(ft \approx 6\) for transitions from the \(1^-\) isomer.
- The PSM calculation with the large configuration space yields a half-life of the \(7^-\) state of \(T_{1/2} \approx 1.95 \times 10^{10}\) years, compared to the experimental range of \((1.90-7.50) \times 10^{10}\) years.
- For the \(7^- \rightarrow 6^+\) transition, the vector contribution to the coefficient \(k\) is \(9.895 \times 10^{-17}\) and the tensor contribution is \(3.965 \times 10^{-16}\); for the \(7^- \rightarrow 8^+\) transition, the vector contribution is \(1.810 \times 10^{-14}\) and the tensor contribution is \(2.857 \times 10^{-16}\).
- In the no-mixing case, the log\(ft\) values for the \(7^- \rightarrow 6^+\) and \(7^- \rightarrow 8^+\) transitions are 19.95 and 21.69, respectively; with the small configuration space they become 17.82 and 17.29; with the large configuration space they become 19.42 and 17.49.

## Context

This work provides the first theoretical explanation for the anomalous first-forbidden transitions in \(^{176}\)Lu, a key nucleus for the Lu-Hf cosmochronometer (Vervoort, 1996) and an \(s\)-process thermometer and branching point (Laeter, 2006). The study extends the authors' recent development of the PSM for forbidden transitions (Wang, 2014; Wang, 2018; Wang, 2024) and contrasts with earlier phenomenological approaches that introduced artificial quenching factors (Suzuki, 2012; Zhi, 2013; Marketin, 2016). The results enable more reliable calculations of stellar beta-decay rates for \(^{176}\)Lu and other heavy deformed nuclei, and demonstrate that high-\(K\) isomers and \(K\)-forbidden beta-decay transitions can serve as stringent tests for nuclear models in the rare-earth region.
