# Quantization of Atomic and Nuclear Rest Masses

> We were able to quantize phenomenologically the first time the atomic and nuclear rest masses. Note that this quantization rule is justified for atoms and nuclei with different A, N and Z and the nuclei and atoms represent a coherent synchronized systems -- a complex of coupled oscillators (resonators). The cooperative resonance synchronization mechanisms are responsible for explanation of how the electron volt world can influence the nuclear mega electron volt world. It means that we created new possibilities for inducing and controlling nuclear reactions by atomic processes.

## Key claim

The authors claim that atomic and nuclear rest masses, and their differences, can be described by a phenomenological quantization rule with a single mass quantum, supporting the hypothesis that nuclei and atoms form coherent, synchronized systems of coupled oscillators where the electron-volt world can influence the nuclear mega-electron-volt world.

## Method

The authors analyzed experimental atomic and nuclear rest mass differences from the database of Moller et al. for processes including beta-decay (single and double), electron capture (single and double), and alpha-decay. They fitted these mass differences to a rational fraction formula involving a fundamental mass quantum and integer ratios, and tested the formula against 3177 atomic and nuclear masses to seven to ten significant figures.

## Key numbers

- The fundamental mass quantum is 0.0076293945 MeV/c² (or 0.0076293945312 MeV/c² with higher precision), used in the formula ΔM = 0.0076293945 · n₁/n₂, where n₁ = 1,2,3,... and n₂ = 1,2,4,8.
- The quantization rule was tested against 3177 atomic and nuclear masses with up to ten significant figures.
- The de Broglie electron wavelength in a hydrogen atom in the ground state is given as λ_e = 0.3324918 nm, used in a homologous formula for interatomic distances: d = (n₁/n₂) λ_e.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous development of the universal cooperative resonance synchronization principle (Gareev et al., earlier papers) and builds on the proposal by Schwartz (1953) to consider nuclear and atomic transitions as a unified process, as well as the resonance approach of Magarshak. The paper aims to provide a phenomenological foundation for understanding low-energy nuclear reactions (LENR) and to enable new methods for inducing and controlling nuclear reactions through atomic processes, with potential applications including room-temperature superconductivity.
