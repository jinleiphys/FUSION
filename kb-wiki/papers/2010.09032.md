# Pulsar glitches from quantum vortex networks

> Neutron stars or pulsars are very rapidly rotating compact stars with extremely high density. One of the unsolved long-standing problems of these enigmatic celestial bodies is the origin of pulsars' glitches, i.e., the sudden rapid deceleration in the rotation speed of neutron stars. Although many glitch events have been reported, there is no consensus on the microscopic mechanism responsible for them. One of the important characterizations of the glitches is the scaling law $P(E) \sim E^{-α}$ of the probability distribution for a glitch with energy $E$. Here, we reanalyse the accumulated up-to-date observation data to obtain the exponent $α\approx 0.88$ for the scaling law, and propose a simple microscopic model that naturally deduces this scaling law without any free parameters. Our model explains the appearance of these glitches in terms of the presence of quantum vortex networks arising at the interface of two different kinds of superfluids in the core of neutron stars; a $p$-wave neutron superfluid in the inner core which interfaces with the $s$-wave neutron superfluid in the outer core, where each integer vortex in the $s$-wave superfluid connects to two half-quantized vortices in the $p$-wave superfluid through structures called "boojums."

# Pulsar glitches from quantum vortex networks

## Key claim

The observed power-law scaling of pulsar glitch energies with exponent α ≈ 0.88 can be explained by a microscopic model of quantum vortex networks at the interface between s-wave and p-wave neutron superfluids in the stellar core, without any free parameters, but the model's predicted cumulative energy exponent of −0.8 ± 0.2 only approximately matches the observed value of −0.88 ± 0.03, and the agreement is best in the central region of the energy distribution, away from extremely small or extremely large glitches.

## Method

The authors reanalyzed 533 glitch events from the pulsar glitch database and pulsar catalogue to extract the cumulative energy distribution P(E) ∼ E^{−α}. They then constructed a statistical model of a triangular vortex lattice in the p-wave inner core, where half-quantized vortices (HQVs) pair with integer-quantized vortices (IQVs) in the s-wave outer core through boojum junctions at the top and bottom interfaces; random pairings at the two interfaces generate clusters of connected vortices whose size distribution was simulated numerically and then translated into an energy distribution.

## Key numbers

- Observed cumulative energy distribution exponent: α = 0.88 ± 0.03, from analysis of 533 glitch events
- Theoretical cluster size probability distribution: p_t(s) ∼ s^{−2.6 ± 0.3}
- Theoretical cumulative energy distribution exponent: P_t(E) ∼ E^{−0.8 ± 0.2}
- Total number of vortices in a neutron star core: of order 10^{19}
- Mean intervortex distance: of order 10^{−6} m
- Radius of the whole core: typically 10 km; p-wave inner core size: a few km

## Context

This work extends the long-standing problem of pulsar glitch mechanisms, previously addressed by the starquake model (Baym 1969; Pines 1972) and the catastrophic vortex unpinning hypothesis (Anderson 1975; Alpar 1977). The authors note that recent microscopic calculations (PhysRevLett.117.232701) showed vortex pinning to impurities is energetically disfavored in nuclear superfluids, motivating their alternative mechanism. The model enables future work connecting glitch statistics to network science and the internal structure of neutron stars, and provides a parameter-free prediction that can be tested against further glitch observations.
