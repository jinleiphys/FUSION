# Probing the symmetry energy at high baryon density with heavy ion collisions

> The nuclear symmetry energy at densities above saturation density ($ρ_0\sim 0.16 fm^{-3}$) is poorly constrained theoretically and very few relevant experimental data exist. Its study is possible through Heavy Ion Collisions (HIC) at energies $E/A> 200$ MeV, particularly with beams of neutron-rich radioactive nuclei. The energy range implies that the momentum dependence of the isospin fields, i.e. the difference of the effective masses on protons and neutrons, also has to be investigated before a safe constraint on $\esy(ρ)$ is possible. We discuss the several observables which have been suggested, like $n/p$ emission and their collective flows and the ratio of meson yields with different isospin projection, $π^-/π^+$ and $K^0/K^+$. We point out several physical mechanisms that should be included in the theoretical models to allow a direct comparison to the more precise experiments which will be able to distinguish the isospin projection of the detected particles: CSR/Lanzhou, FAIR/GSI, RIBF/RIKEN, FRIB/MSU.

# Probing the symmetry energy at high baryon density with heavy ion collisions

## Key claim
The nuclear symmetry energy above saturation density (ρ₀ ∼ 0.16 fm⁻³) remains poorly constrained, and its determination through heavy ion collisions at E/A > 200 MeV requires disentangling the density dependence of the symmetry energy from the momentum dependence of the isospin fields (the difference in effective masses of protons and neutrons), which itself is poorly known and must be constrained simultaneously.

## Method
The paper reviews theoretical approaches, relativistic mean field theory (RMFT), density-dependent relativistic Hartree (DDRH), and Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF), and their implementation in transport codes (relativistic Boltzmann-Ühling-Uhlenbeck, RBUU; Boltzmann-Nordheim-Vlasov, BNV; and isospin-dependent IBUU04) to simulate heavy ion collisions. The authors analyze observables including n/p and ³H/³He emission ratios as functions of transverse momentum, collective flows, and the isospin ratios of meson yields (π⁻/π⁺ and K⁰/K⁺) in Au+Au and Ru+Ru collisions at beam energies from 0.8 to 1.8 AGeV, comparing different symmetry energy parametrizations and isospin-dependent momentum dependencies.

## Key numbers
- Saturation density: ρ₀ ∼ 0.16 fm⁻³
- Beam energy threshold for probing high density: E/A ≥ 200 MeV (to reach ρ > 1.5 ρ₀)
- Maximum density reached in central collisions at 400 AMeV: about 1.8 ρ₀ for 15–20 fm/c
- At 1 AGeV central Au+Au collisions, kaon yield saturates at ∼15 fm/c (compression stage), while pion yield freezes out at ∼50 fm/c (final stage)
- n/p ratio at transverse momentum pₜ ≥ p_proj = 2 fm⁻¹ shows strong dependence on the sign of the neutron-proton effective mass splitting, nearly independent of symmetry energy stiffness
- π⁻/π⁺ and K⁰/K⁺ ratios decrease with increasing beam energy; kaon ratios show greater sensitivity to symmetry energy than pion ratios
- Isovector scalar and vector self-energies at ρ ∼ 2ρ₀ are of order ∼100 MeV in standard NL-RMFT

## Context
This review extends the long-standing effort to determine the nuclear equation of state from heavy ion collisions, previously successful for symmetric matter through collective flows and kaon production (Danielewicz 2002, Fuchs 2005). It addresses the poorly constrained symmetry energy above saturation, relevant for neutron star properties (maximum masses, mass-radius relation, cooling) and hybrid star structure (Klahn 2006, Di Toro 2006). The paper critically evaluates the claim by Xiao (2009) that a very soft symmetry energy is required to reproduce FOPI π⁻/π⁺ data, arguing that multiple competing effects, threshold effects from isospin-dependent self-energies, momentum dependence of isospin fields, isospin-dependent inelastic cross sections, and pion spectral function modifications (Xu 2009), must be treated consistently before reliable constraints can be extracted. The work identifies the need for simultaneous measurement of multiple observables as functions of momentum in upcoming experiments at CSR/Lanzhou, FAIR/GSI, RIBF/RIKEN, and FRIB/MSU.
