# Introduction to statistical models and non-extensive statistics

> Quark matter is being expected to be found in heavy ion collisions on the basis of calculations in the framework of traditional, extensive thermodynamics. Recently a non-extensive generalization of the thermodynamics is emerging in the theoretical research. We review here some basic concepts in statistics, kinetic theory and thermodynamics, in particular those encountered in non-extensive thermodynamics. This offers an introduction into the theoretical basis of considering non-extensive parton kinetics for describing the hadronization of quark matter.

## Key claim

The paper argues that non-extensive thermodynamics, particularly the Tsallis distribution, provides a unified statistical framework for describing particle spectra from heavy-ion collisions that exhibit both exponential and power-law regions, but this interpretation competes with pQCD-based explanations for the hard part of the spectrum and the choice between them remains an open question.

## Method

The paper reviews the mathematical foundations of non-extensive statistics by first examining the central limit theorem and its breakdown for distributions like the Lorentzian (Levy distribution). It then derives generalized Fokker-Planck equations from Langevin dynamics with energy-dependent damping and diffusion coefficients, showing how these lead to stationary Tsallis distributions. Two generalizations of the Boltzmann equation are presented: the nonlinear Boltzmann equation (NLBE), which modifies the product rule for two-particle probabilities, and the non-extensive Boltzmann equation (NEBE), which generalizes the energy addition rule to associative composition laws; both yield non-exponential equilibrium distributions.

## Key numbers

- The Tsallis distribution is given by \( f(p) = \frac{1}{Z} \left(1+(q-1)\frac{E}{T} \right)^{\frac{q}{q-1}} \), with the Tsallis temperature \(T\) defined as the fixed point of the sliding inverse slope: \( \mathcal{T}(T)=T \).
- For the case of independent additive and multiplicative noises, the Tsallis index is \( q = 1 + 2C/G \) and the Tsallis temperature is \( T = D/mG \), with the relation \( v = 1 + E_c/T \) connecting the tail power \(v\), energy scale \(E_c\), and temperature parameter \(T\).
- The Tsallis composition rule is \( h(x,y) = x + y + a x y \), with the mapping \( X_s(S) = \frac{1}{aT} \ln(1 + aT S) \), leading to the Tsallis entropy density \( s(f) = \frac{f^q - f}{q-1} \) where \( q = 1 - aT \).
- The multiplicative composition rule \( h(x,y) = a x y \) gives a pure power-law equilibrium \( Z f_{eq} = (aE)^{-1/aT} \) and the Renyi entropy \( S_R = \frac{1}{1-q} \ln \int f^q \).

## Context

This review extends the traditional extensive thermodynamics framework used in earlier statistical models of heavy-ion collisions, such as Hagedorn's statistical model of meson resonances (Hagedorn, 1960s), by incorporating non-extensive generalizations that can describe power-law tails in particle spectra. The work connects to contemporary developments in superstatistics (Beck and Cohen, 2003), where intensive parameters like temperature are treated as having a probability distribution rather than a fixed value, and enables further theoretical and experimental tests of whether observed non-exponential spectra arise from statistical non-extensivity or from pQCD contributions.
