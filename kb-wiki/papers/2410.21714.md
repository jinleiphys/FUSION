# Microscopic optical potentials from a Greens function approach

> Optical potentials are a standard tool in the study of nuclear reactions, as they describe the interaction between a target nucleus and a projectile. The use of phenomenological optical potentials built using experimental data on stable isotopes is widespread. Although successful in their dedicated domain, it is unclear whether these phenomenological potentials can provide reliable predictions for unstable isotopes. To address this problem, optical potentials based on microscopic nuclear structure input calculations prove to be crucial and are an important current line of research. In this work we present an explicit implementation of the Feshbach formalism for the systematic derivation of optical potentials using input from nuclear structure models. Numerical tools for the derivation of Green's functions associated with nonlocal potentials are presented. The new optical potential, based on the valence shell model, is applied to the calculations of n+24Mg elastic scattering and yields a close agreement with the experimental data.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new implementation of the Feshbach formalism for deriving microscopic optical potentials from nuclear structure input, but the approach relies on an approximation where the Q-space propagator is taken as diagonal and independent of the excited states, and the self-consistency between the Green's function and the optical potential is enforced iteratively rather than exactly.

## Method

The authors implement the Feshbach formalism to derive a non-local, complex, and energy-dependent optical potential from the valence shell model, using the Green's function of the Q-space (excited states of the target) to construct the dynamic polarization potential. The Green's function is calculated by solving the radial integro-differential wave equations for non-local potentials using a Lagrange-mesh method, and the optical potential and Green's function are updated iteratively until convergence of the volume integral. The method is benchmarked by reproducing earlier proton elastic scattering results on 40Ca using a collective model with 10 vibrational states, then applied to neutron elastic scattering on 24Mg using a valence shell model of 25Mg with approximately 600 states up to 15 MeV excitation energy.

## Key numbers

* For the n+24Mg calculation, the static Woods-Saxon potential U00 has depth U0 = 48.5 MeV, radius R0 = 3.69 fm, and diffuseness a = 0.65 fm, chosen to reproduce the experimental neutron separation energy of 25Mg (7.33 MeV) by binding a 1d5/2 single-particle state.
* The n+24Mg optical potential includes contributions from approximately 600 states of 25Mg up to about 15 MeV excitation energy, calculated using the PSDPF interaction with valence nucleons occupying the p, sd, and pf shells.
* For the benchmark p+40Ca calculation at 30.3 MeV, the static potential uses U0 = 50 MeV, W0 = 2 MeV, R0 = 1.12 A^(1/3) fm, and a = 0.75 fm, with 10 vibrational states of 40Ca having excitation energies from 3.73 MeV (3- state) to 20.0 MeV (4+ state) and deformation parameters beta_J from 0.087 to 0.653.
* The numerical calculations use a Lagrange radial box of a = 30 fm with N = 100 Lagrange points.

## Context

This work extends the Feshbach-inspired approach of Rao, Reeves, and Satchler (1973), who used a collective vibrational model for 40Ca, by incorporating a more complete valence shell model description of the target-plus-projectile system (25Mg) with approximately 600 states. The approach enables predictive calculations of elastic scattering for unstable isotopes where phenomenological optical potentials fitted to stable isotopes may be unreliable, and it addresses the challenge of achieving sufficient absorption in microscopic optical potentials noted in prior ab initio calculations such as those by Rotureau et al. (2017).
