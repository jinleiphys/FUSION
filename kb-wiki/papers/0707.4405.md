# Bulk Viscosity driven clusterization of quark-gluon plasma and early freeze-out in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We introduce a new scenario for heavy ion collisions that could solve the lingering problems associated with the so-called HBT puzzle. We postulate that the system starts expansion as the perfect quark-gluon fluid but close to freeze-out it splits into clusters, due to a sharp rise of bulk viscosity in the vicinity of the hadronization transition. We then argue that the characteristic cluster size is determined by the viscosity coefficient and the expansion rate. Typically it is much smaller and independent of the total system volume. These clusters maintain the pre-existing outward-going flow, as a spray of droplets, but develop no flow of their own, and hadronize by evaporation. We provide an ansatz for converting the hydrodynamic output into clusters.

# Bulk Viscosity Driven Clusterization of Quark-Gluon Plasma and Early Freeze-Out in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The authors propose that a sharp rise in bulk viscosity near the QCD phase transition temperature causes the expanding quark-gluon plasma to break into small clusters, providing a potential resolution to the HBT puzzle without requiring a first-order phase transition. This scenario is presented as a speculative alternative that depends on the bulk viscosity diverging or becoming very large close to T_c, with the authors noting that quantitative estimates "should only be taken to understand that clustering is not outright excluded."

## Method

The authors develop analytic estimates based on Bjorken boost-invariant hydrodynamics, assuming a conformal equation of state until the system approaches T_c. They derive conditions for clustering by comparing viscous forces to advective forces (parameter alpha_1) and by checking that the viscosity rise is sudden enough relative to the relaxation time (parameter alpha_2). Cluster size is estimated both from the intrinsic QCD scale Lambda_QCD and from a dynamical balance between dissipated energy and kinetic expansion energy.

## Key numbers

- Cluster mass estimate from intrinsic scale: M ~ 2 GeV, based on T_c = 165 MeV, Lambda_QCD = 250 MeV, and nu_QGP ~ 30 degrees of freedom
- Lattice estimate for bulk viscosity: 0.5 < zeta/s (T = 1.02 T_c) < 2, where s is entropy density
- Cluster decays into approximately 10 pions or a few heavier hadrons
- Relaxation time estimate from supersymmetric Yang-Mills: tau_Pi = (1 - log 2)/(6 pi T)
- Width of bulk viscosity peak estimated as Delta T ~ 0.1 T_c from prior lattice work
- Dynamical cluster size scales as L^2 = 24 zeta_c tau'_c / epsilon_c, where tau'_c is the inverse expansion rate at T_c

## Context

This work addresses the HBT puzzle at RHIC, where hydrodynamic simulations successfully describe flow observables but predict R_o/R_s ratios that disagree with data. The authors contrast their clustering mechanism with spinodal decomposition scenarios (Mishustin 1999, Randrup 2003, Kapusta 2004) that require a first-order phase transition, noting that lattice QCD indicates a smooth crossover at RHIC energies. The scenario extends prior work on bulk viscosity near T_c (Kharzeev 2007, Pratt 2006) and provides a framework for reconciling hydrodynamics with interferometry data. The authors suggest this mechanism could explain resonance over-abundances, p_T fluctuation scaling, and the mass dependence of inverse slopes observed at SPS and RHIC.
