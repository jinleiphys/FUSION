# Study of baryon octet electromagnetic form factors in perturbative chiral quark model

> The electromagnetic properties of baryon octet are studied in the perturbative chiral quark model (PCQM). The relativistic quark wave function is extracted by fitting the theoretical results of the proton charge form factor to experimental data and the predetermined quark wave function is applied to study the electromagnetic form factors of other octet baryons as well as magnetic moments, charge and magnetic radii. The PCQM results are found, based on the predetermined quark wave function, in good agreement with experimental data.

# Study of baryon octet electromagnetic form factors in perturbative chiral quark model

## Key claim

The perturbative chiral quark model (PCQM), when using a quark wave function extracted by fitting the proton charge form factor to experimental data (rather than assuming a Gaussian ansatz), yields electromagnetic form factors for the baryon octet that agree with experimental data up to momentum transfer Q² = 1.0 GeV², but the approach fails to reproduce the neutron charge radius and form factor because the quark propagator is restricted to the ground state only.

## Method

The authors extract the relativistic quark wave function by expanding the upper and lower radial components g(r) and f(r) in a basis of five Sturmian functions (with length parameter b = 0.5 GeV) and fitting the PCQM theoretical result for the proton charge form factor to experimental data. They then apply this predetermined wave function, without any additional free parameters, to compute the electromagnetic form factors, magnetic moments, charge radii, and magnetic radii of all octet baryons in the PCQM framework, including contributions from five Feynman diagrams (three-quark core, counterterm, meson-cloud, vertex correction, and meson-in-flight) at one-loop order with SU(3) flavor symmetry including π, K, and η meson clouds.

- Proton magnetic moment: μ_p = 2.735 ± 0.121 μ_N (experimental: 2.793 μ_N from PDG 2012)
- Neutron magnetic moment: μ_n = -1.956 ± 0.103 μ_N (experimental: -1.913 μ_N from PDG 2012)
- Σ⁺ magnetic moment: μ_Σ⁺ = 2.537 ± 0.201 μ_N (experimental: 2.458 ± 0.010 μ_N from PDG 2012)
- Σ⁻ magnetic moment: μ_Σ⁻ = -0.861 ± 0.040 μ_N (experimental: -1.160 ± 0.025 μ_N from PDG 2012)
- Λ magnetic moment: μ_Λ = -0.867 ± 0.074 μ_N (experimental: -0.613 ± 0.004 μ_N from PDG 2012)
- Ξ⁰ magnetic moment: μ_Ξ⁰ = -1.690 ± 0.142 μ_N (experimental: -1.250 ± 0.014 μ_N from PDG 2012)
- Ξ⁻ magnetic moment: μ_Ξ⁻ = -0.840 ± 0.087 μ_N (experimental: -0.651 ± 0.080 μ_N from PDG 2012)
- Σ⁰Λ transition magnetic moment: μ_Σ⁰Λ = -1.599 ± 0.068 μ_N (experimental: -1.610 ± 0.080 μ_N from PDG 2012)
- Proton magnetic radius: ⟨r_M²⟩^p = 0.909 ± 0.084 fm² (experimental: 0.74 ± 0.10 fm² from PDG 2012)
- Neutron magnetic radius: ⟨r_M²⟩^n = 0.922 ± 0.079 fm² (experimental: 0.76 ± 0.02 fm² from PDG 2012)
- The 3q-core contributes over 90% to charge radii of charged baryons (p, Σ⁺, Σ⁻, Ξ⁻)
- Meson cloud contributes around 20% to nucleon magnetic moments and radii

- Sturmian function length parameter: b = 0.5 GeV
- Pion decay constant in chiral limit: F = 88 MeV
- Baryon chiral mass used for magnetic moments: m_B = 1.039 GeV

## Context

This work extends previous PCQM calculations by Lyubovitskij (2001), Cheedket (2004), Khosonthongkee (2004), and Faessler (2008), which used a variational Gaussian ansatz for the quark wave function and agreed with experiment only at very low momentum transfer. By extracting the wave function from experimental proton form factor data rather than assuming a Gaussian form, the authors achieve agreement up to Q² = 1.0 GeV². The results are compared with chiral perturbation theory by Kubis (2001) and Schindler (2001), various relativistic quark models by Faessler (2006), Ramalho (2011, 2013), and Gutche (2012), and lattice QCD calculations by Wang (2009) and Lin (2009). The work enables future application of the determined quark wave function to axial form factors of the baryon octet, while the failure for neutral baryons (particularly the neutron) indicates the need to include excited-state quark propagators in the calculation.
