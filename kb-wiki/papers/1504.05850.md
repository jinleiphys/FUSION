# Entropic and enthalpic phase transitions in high energy density nuclear matter

> Features of Gas-Liquid (GL) and Quark-Hadron (QH) phase transitions (PT) in dense nuclear matter are under discussion in comparison with their terrestrial counterparts, e.g. so-called "plasma" PT in shock-compressed hydrogen, nitrogen etc. Both, GLPT and QHPT, when being represented in widely accepted temperature - baryonic chemical potential plane, are often considered as similar, i.e. amenable to one-to-one mapping by simple scaling. It is argued that this impression is illusive and that GLPT and QHPT belong to different classes: GLPT is typical enthalpic PT (Van-der-Waals-like) while QHPT ("deconfinement-driven") is typical entropic PT. Subdivision of 1st-order fluid-fluid phase transitions into enthalpy- and entropy-driven subclasses was proposed previously [arXiv:1403.8053]. Properties of enthalpic and entropic PTs differ significantly. Entropic PTs are always internal parts of more general and extended thermodynamic anomalies - domains with abnormal (negative) sign for the set of (usually positive) second derivatives of thermodynamic potential. Three of them are of primary importance: Gruneizen and thermal expansion and thermal pressure coefficients. Negative sign of these derivatives lead to violation of standard behavior and relative order in P-V plane for many iso-lines, e.g. isotherms, isentropes, shock adiabats etc. Entropic PTs have more complicated topology of stable and metastable areas within its two-phase region in comparison with conventional enthalpic (VdW-like) PTs. In particular, new additional metastable region, bounded by new additional spinodal, appears in the case of entropic PT. All the features of entropic PTs and accompanying abnormal thermodynamics region have transparent geometrical interpretation - multi-layered structure of thermodynamic surfaces for temperature, entropy and internal energy as a pressure-volume functions, e.g. T(P,V), S(P,V) and U(P,V).

## Key claim

The paper argues that the apparent similarity between gas-liquid (GLPT) and quark-hadron (QHPT) phase transitions in nuclear matter, when plotted in the temperature-baryonic chemical potential plane, is illusory; GLPT is an enthalpic (van der Waals-like) transition while QHPT is an entropic (deconfinement-driven) transition, and these two classes have fundamentally different thermodynamic and topological properties.

## Method

The author compares the phase boundaries, thermodynamic derivatives, and isotherm structures of gas-liquid and quark-hadron phase transitions in symmetric nuclear matter, using published numerical calculations from the FSUGold model (for GLPT) and SU(3) model (for QHPT). The analysis classifies first-order fluid-fluid phase transitions into enthalpic and entropic subclasses based on the sign of enthalpy and entropy changes across the transition, and examines the resulting abnormal thermodynamics including negative Gruneizen parameter, thermal expansion coefficient, and thermal pressure coefficient. The study also compares these nuclear transitions to analogous ionization-driven and dissociation-driven phase transitions in dense hydrogen and deuterium plasmas.

## Key numbers

- GLPT in nuclear matter occurs at temperatures T ≤ 20 MeV; QHPT occurs at T ≤ 200 MeV
- Critical temperature for dissociation-driven entropic PT in deuterium: Tc ≈ 13500 K (SAHA-D code calculation)
- Critical temperature for ionization-driven plasma PT in xenon: Tc ≈ 12600 K (from DDR-80)
- Density regime for high energy density nuclear matter: ρ ∼ 10^14 g/cc

## Context

This work extends the author's prior classification of phase transitions into enthalpic and entropic subclasses (Iosilevskiy, arXiv:1403.8053) and applies it to nuclear matter, contradicting the common assumption that GLPT and QHPT are similar when viewed in the T-μB plane (as shown in figures from HDSI-13). The analysis draws parallels between QHPT and ionization-driven plasma phase transitions in hydrogen (PPT-Jap-98) and dissociation-driven transitions in deuterium (GrIo-09), suggesting that all "delocalization-driven" transitions share entropic character. The work enables future investigation of abnormal hydrodynamic phenomena such as shock cooling, anomalous isentropic expansion, and spinodal decomposition in nuclear matter and dense plasmas.
