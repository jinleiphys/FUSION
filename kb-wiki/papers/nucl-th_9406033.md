# Classical Hadrodynamics: A New Approach to Ultrarelativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We discuss a new approach to ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions based on classical hadrodynamics for extended nucleons, corresponding to nucleons of finite size interacting with massive meson fields. This new theory provides a natural covariant microscopic approach that includes automatically spacetime nonlocality and retardation, nonequilibrium phenomena, interactions among all nucleons and particle production. In the current version of our theory, we consider $N$ extended unexcited nucleons interacting with massive neutral scalar ($σ$) and neutral vector ($ω$) meson fields. The resulting classical relativistic many-body equations of motion are solved numerically without further approximation for soft nucleon-nucleon collisions at $p_{\rm lab}$ = 14.6, 30, 60, 100 and 200 GeV/$c$ to yield the transverse momentum imparted to the nucleons. For the future development of the theory, the isovector pseudoscalar ($π^+$, $π^-$, $π^0$), isovector scalar ($δ^+$, $δ^-$, $δ^0$), isovector vector ($ρ^+$, $ρ^-$, $ρ^0$) and neutral pseudoscalar ($η$) meson fields that are known to be important from nucleon-nucleon scattering experiments should be incorporated. In addition, the effects of quantum uncertainty on the equations of motion should be included by use of techniques analogous to those used by Moniz and Sharp for nonrelativistic quantum electrodynamics.

## Key claim

Classical hadrodynamics for extended nucleons provides a covariant microscopic approach to ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions that automatically includes spacetime nonlocality, retardation, nonequilibrium phenomena, and particle production, but the current version is limited to neutral scalar (σ) and neutral vector (ω) meson fields and has not yet incorporated the isovector pseudoscalar (π), isovector scalar (δ), isovector vector (ρ), and neutral pseudoscalar (η) meson fields known to be important from nucleon-nucleon scattering experiments.

## Method

The authors derived classical relativistic equations of motion from an action describing N extended unexcited nucleons interacting with massive neutral scalar (σ) and neutral vector (ω) meson fields. They solved these second-order, nonlinear, integrodifferential equations numerically using a fourth-order Adams-Moulton predictor-corrector algorithm with adaptive step sizes for soft nucleon-nucleon collisions at five laboratory momenta. The integrations over proper time were performed with a special error-minimizing application of Lagrange's four-point (cubic) interpolation formulas.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon mass M = 938.91897 MeV/c²
- Scalar (σ) meson mass m_s = 550 MeV/c²
- Vector (ω) meson mass m_v = 781.95 MeV/c²
- Scalar interaction strength g_s² = 7.29
- Vector interaction strength g_v² = 10.81
- Nucleon r.m.s. radius R_rms = 0.862 fm
- Calculations performed for soft nucleon-nucleon collisions at p_lab = 14.6, 30, 60, 100, and 200 GeV/c
- For calculating mean transverse momentum, a total cross section cutoff of σ_tot = 40 mb was used
- A comparison value of g_v² = 17.26 from Bryan and Scott (1969) was used for an alternative estimate

## Context

This work extends the traditional hadronic description of nuclear processes by providing a classical, covariant many-body approach that avoids mean-field approximations, perturbative expansions, or superpositions of two-body collisions. The authors note that their calculated mean transverse momentum as a function of total center-of-mass energy is both lower in magnitude and increases more rapidly with √s than experimental values from Alner et al. (1987) and Breakstone et al. (1988), though the comparison is complicated by differences in rapidity coverage and the inclusion of hard collisions in experimental data. The theory enables future systematic comparisons with experimental data on differential cross sections, transverse momentum distributions, particle multiplicity distributions, rapidity distributions, and particle-particle correlations once additional meson fields and quantum uncertainty effects (using techniques analogous to Moniz and Sharp for nonrelativistic quantum electrodynamics) are incorporated.
