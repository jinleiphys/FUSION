# Probing the gateway to superheavy nuclei in cranked relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory

> The cranked relativistic Hartree+Bogoliubov theory has been applied for a systematic study of the nuclei around 254No, the heaviest nuclei for which detailed spectroscopic data are available. The deformation, rotational response, pairing correlations, quasi-particle and other properties of these nuclei have been studied with different relativistic mean field (RMF) parametrizations. For the first time, the quasi-particle spectra of odd deformed nuclei have been calculated in a fully self-consistent way within the framework of the RMF theory. The energies of the spherical subshells, from which active deformed states of these nuclei emerge, are described with an accuracy better than 0.5 MeV for most of the subshells with the NL1 and NL3 parametrizations. However, for a few subshells the discrepancy reach 0.7-1.0 MeV. The implications of these results for the study of superheavy nuclei are discussed.

## Key claim
The cranked relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (CRHB) theory describes the energies of spherical subshells active in the deformed A~250 mass region with an accuracy better than 0.5 MeV for most subshells using the NL1 and NL3 parametrizations, but for a few subshells the discrepancies reach 0.7-1.0 MeV; these empirical shifts, when applied to superheavy nuclei, still support Z=120 and N=172 or N=184 as magic numbers, but the prediction of Z=114 as a magic proton number from other RMF sets should be treated with caution.

## Method
The authors applied the cranked relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (CRHB) theory with Lipkin-Nogami (LN) pairing to systematically study nuclei around 254No, including deformations, rotational response, pairing correlations, quasiparticle spectra, shell structure, and two-nucleon separation energies. For the first time, quasiparticle spectra of odd deformed nuclei were calculated in a fully self-consistent way within the RMF framework, using the NL1, NL3, NL-Z, NLSH, and NL-RA1 parametrizations of the RMF Lagrangian and the D1S Gogny force in the particle-particle channel. The calculated quasiparticle energies were compared directly with experimental data for 249Bk and 249,251Cf to extract empirical shifts for the energies of spherical subshells.

## Key numbers
- The energies of spherical subshells from which active deformed states emerge are described with an accuracy better than 0.5 MeV for most subshells with the NL1 and NL3 parametrizations.
- For a few subshells, the discrepancies between calculated and experimental energies reach 0.7-1.0 MeV (specifically for the π1h9/2, ν1i11/2, ν1j15/2, and ν2g9/2 subshells).
- The effective mass (Lorentz mass) of nucleons at the Fermi surface in RMF theory is approximately m*(kF)/m ≈ 0.66, which leads to a stretching of the energy scale in calculated quasiparticle spectra.
- The uncertainty in estimating spherical subshell energies from deformed states is estimated to be below 300 keV, assuming the effective mass just stretches the energy scale.
- For the NL1 parametrization, the energies of all proton states in the 292172120 nucleus were increased by 0.78 MeV to align the 1i13/2 state with NL3; for neutrons, all NL1 energies were increased by 0.76 MeV to align the 2g9/2 state.

## Context
This work extends the application of self-consistent relativistic mean field (RMF) theory to the heaviest deformed nuclei with detailed spectroscopic data (around 254No), addressing a gap in knowledge about the accuracy of single-particle energies in self-consistent approaches compared to the macroscopic-microscopic (MM) method (Nilsson et al. 1969, Möller and Nix 1994). The study enables a more reliable assessment of which RMF parametrizations are best suited for predicting magic numbers in superheavy nuclei, and it provides empirical corrections that can be applied to self-consistent calculations. The results contradict predictions from the NLSH and NL-RA1 parametrizations (Lalazissis et al. 1996, NL-RA1) that indicate Z=114 as a magic proton number, instead supporting Z=120 as the magic proton number and N=172 or N=184 as candidate magic neutron numbers.
