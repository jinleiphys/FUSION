# Nonthermal Fixed Points in Quantum Field Theory Beyond the Weak-Coupling Limit

> Quantum systems in extreme conditions can exhibit universal behavior far from equilibrium associated to nonthermal fixed points with a wide range of topical applications from early-universe inflaton dynamics and heavy-ion collisions to strong quenches in ultracold quantum gases. So far, most studies have relied on a mapping of the quantum dynamics onto a classical-statistical theory that can be simulated on a computer. However, the mapping is based on a weak-coupling limit, while phenomenological applications often require moderate interaction strengths. We report on the observation of nonthermal fixed points directly in quantum field theory beyond the weak-coupling limit. For the example of a relativistic scalar O(N)-symmetric quantum field theory, we numerically solve the nonequilibrium dynamics employing a 1/N expansion to next-to-leading order, which does not rely on a small coupling parameter. Starting from two different sets of overoccupied and of strong-field initial conditions, we find that nonthermal fixed points are not restricted to parameter ranges suitable for classical-statistical simulations, but extend also to couplings of order one. While the infrared behavior is found to be insensitive to the differences in the initial conditions, we demonstrate that transport phenomena to higher momenta depend on the presence or absence of a symmetry-breaking field expectation value.

## Key claim

Nonthermal fixed points, previously observed only in classical-statistical simulations valid in the weak-coupling limit, persist in quantum field theory for couplings of order one, though the direct energy cascade at higher momenta deviates from scaling when characteristic occupancies drop below one.

## Method

The authors numerically solved the nonequilibrium dynamics of a relativistic scalar O(N)-symmetric quantum field theory in 3+1 dimensions using a 1/N expansion to next-to-leading order (NLO) based on the two-particle irreducible (2PI) effective action, which does not require a small coupling parameter. They considered two types of initial conditions: overoccupied (fluctuation) initial conditions with large occupancy parameter n0 = 100 and zero macroscopic field, and strong-field initial conditions with a nonzero field expectation value and vacuum fluctuations. The evolution equations were solved on a momentum-space grid with up to 540 spatial points, employing adaptive temporal discretization to reach times up to Q t = 1200 or σ0 t = 1200.

## Key numbers

- Coupling range studied: 10^{-2} ≤ λ ≤ 10 for N = 4.
- For fluctuation initial conditions, the inverse particle cascade at low momenta exhibits a power-law exponent κ_N = 5, and the direct energy cascade at higher momenta exhibits exponent κ_E = 5/3.
- The effective mass gap extracted from the dispersion relation for fluctuation initial conditions is m ≈ 0.6 Q, remaining approximately constant across the coupling range λ = 0.01 to 10.
- For fluctuation initial conditions with λ = 10, infrared occupancies grow by more than an order of magnitude for Q t ≲ 600, compared to three orders of magnitude growth for smaller couplings over the same period.
- For λ = 10 at late times (Q t ≳ 600), the distribution approaches a Bose-Einstein distribution with temperature T ≈ 1.6 Q and chemical potential μ = 0.

## Context

This work extends classical-statistical simulations of nonthermal fixed points (Berges 2008, Nowak 2011, Orioli 2015) beyond the weak-coupling limit where those simulations are valid, and addresses the question of whether such universal scaling persists at moderate couplings relevant to heavy-ion collisions and early-universe dynamics. The results appear to conflict with extrapolated kinetic theory expectations (Kurkela 2015, Epelbaum 2015) that suggested deviations from scaling at stronger couplings, but align with the known robustness of universal scaling phenomena in thermal equilibrium. The observation of the dual cascade (inverse particle cascade and direct energy cascade) from a fully quantum field theoretical calculation is reported for the first time, enabled by algorithmic advances that allow preservation of O(N) symmetry at all times, which classical-statistical simulations cannot achieve.
