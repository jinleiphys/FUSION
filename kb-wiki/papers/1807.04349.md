# Models for nuclear reactions with weakly bound systems

> In this contribution, I present a short overview of the theory of direct nuclear reactions, with special emphasis on the case of reactions induced by weakly-bound nuclei. After introducing some general results of quantum scattering theory, I present specific applications to elastic, inelastic, transfer and breakup reactions. For each of them, I first introduce the most standard framework, followed by some alternative models or extensions suitable for the case of weakly bound nuclei. A short discussion on semiclassical theory of Coulomb excitation and its application to breakup of halo nuclei is also provided.

## Key claim

This paper provides a pedagogical overview of direct nuclear reaction theory for weakly-bound systems, arguing that standard reaction formalisms must be extended to incorporate strong coupling to breakup channels when the projectile is loosely bound, but the author presents this as a review of existing frameworks rather than a new theoretical result.

## Method

The author reviews quantum scattering theory for direct reactions, starting from the time-independent Schrodinger equation and the definition of cross sections, then derives the T-matrix formalism and the Gell-Mann-Goldberger two-potential formula. Specific applications are presented for elastic scattering, inelastic scattering, transfer reactions, and breakup reactions, with each case first treated using the standard framework (such as the optical model and coupled-channels formalism) followed by extensions suitable for weakly-bound nuclei, including a discussion of semiclassical Coulomb excitation theory for halo nuclei breakup.

## Key numbers

- Interaction cross sections at 800 MeV/nucleon for halo nuclei (e.g., He-6, Li-11, Be-14) were found to be much higher than for neighboring isotopes, indicating abnormally large radii (Tanihata et al., 1985)
- Direct reaction timescales are approximately 10^-21 seconds, while compound nucleus reactions occur over 10^-18 to 10^-16 seconds
- Momentum distributions of Li-9 following fragmentation of Li-11 on C-12 were abnormally narrow (Kobayashi et al., 1988)

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework developed by Feshbach (1958, 1962) for nuclear reactions and by Austern (1970) for direct reaction theory, applying it specifically to weakly-bound systems such as halo nuclei discovered by Tanihata et al. (1985). The work enables future calculations of elastic, inelastic, transfer, and breakup observables for exotic nuclei near the driplines, and provides the theoretical basis for interpreting experiments with radioactive beams that probe halo structures and Borromean systems.
