# Physics Opportunities with a Fixed-Target Program at the Electron-Ion Collider

> A fixed-target program at the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) would broaden the facility's scientific reach by providing key measurements for studies of cold nuclear matter (CNM), the QCD phase diagram, and nuclear reactions relevant for space radiation. Constraining CNM effects is essential for interpreting observables in proton-nucleus ($p+A$) and nucleus-nucleus ($A+A$) collisions, yet these effects are poorly understood at low center-of-mass energies. In particular, the range $\sqrt{s}\approx10$--20 GeV, where several CNM effects may compete at similar scales, has not been explored with high statistical precision. Mapping the QCD phase diagram similarly requires high-statistics $A+A$ data with broad rapidity and transverse-momentum coverage to probe the onset of deconfinement and the possible location of the QCD critical point (CP). Currently, such data are limited at 4.5 GeV $< \sqrt{s_{NN}} < 7.7$ GeV A fixed-target program at the EIC would fill these gaps, providing CNM baselines and complementary data for QCD CP studies. By delivering high luminosity and systematic measurements across a broad range of nuclear targets, the program would link $p+A$ and $A+A$ systems at the same center-of-mass energies, enabling a unified, quantitative description of cold QCD matter and clarifying the interpretation of QGP signatures in $A+A$ collisions at low energies where comparable $p+A$ data are lacking. Finally, the program would offer a unique opportunity to measure nuclear cross sections critical for improving cosmic-ray models, including studies of space-radiation protection for both autonomous spacecraft and long-duration human spaceflight.

## Key claim

A fixed-target program at the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) would significantly broaden the facility's scientific reach by providing key measurements for cold nuclear matter (CNM), the QCD phase diagram, and space-radiation studies, but only if the program can be realized with minimal impact on the primary collider operations and if the detector acceptance in the forward region proves sufficient for the boosted fixed-target kinematics.

## Method

The authors propose inserting a thin foil target along the hadron-beam direction of the ePIC detector at the EIC, enabling proton-nucleus (p+A) and nucleus-nucleus (A+A) collisions in fixed-target mode. They estimate achievable luminosities based on a 1 mm thick gold foil target and effective beam currents at the percent level of the nominal circulating currents, drawing on operational experience from the STAR fixed-target program at RHIC and the LHCb SMOG system. The analysis evaluates the kinematic coverage, detector acceptance in the forward (hadron-going) direction, and the physics reach for CNM baselines, QCD phase diagram mapping, and nuclear cross-section measurements relevant for cosmic-ray modeling.

## Key numbers

- The accessible p+A center-of-mass energy range is sqrt(s_NN) approximately 7 to 23 GeV, and the A+A range is sqrt(s_NN) approximately 3 to 14 GeV, depending on projectile species and beam energy.
- Estimated instantaneous luminosities for p+Au and Au+Au fixed-target collisions are greater than 10^36 cm^-2 s^-1 and 10^38 cm^-2 s^-1, respectively, assuming a 1 mm thick gold foil and an effective beam current of 10 mA.
- The energy range sqrt(s) approximately 10 to 20 GeV for p+A collisions has not been explored with high statistical precision for CNM effects.
- Current A+A data are limited at 4.5 GeV less than sqrt(s_NN) less than 7.7 GeV for QCD critical point studies.
- The EIC hadron beam energy can be varied from about 25 to 275 GeV for protons and from about 3.85 to 110 GeV per nucleon for gold nuclei in the baseline configuration.
- Polarization of approximately 70 percent is expected for proton, deuteron, and light-ion beams in the baseline collider program.

## Context

This paper extends the physics case for a fixed-target program at the EIC, building on the CNM review by Arleo (2025) that emphasized the need for coherent experimental measurements across multiple hard probes at sqrt(s_NN) less than 20 GeV. The proposed program would complement and bridge existing measurements from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan (STAR Collaboration, 2017-2026), the NA60 experiment at CERN (2010), the SeaQuest/E906 experiment at Fermilab (2017), and future programs such as NA60+ at the CERN SPS (2022) and CBM at FAIR (2023). The program would enable a unified, quantitative description of cold QCD matter by linking p+A and A+A systems at the same center-of-mass energies within a single detector, and would provide nuclear cross-section data in the 0.1 to 50 GeV per nucleon range critical for improving galactic cosmic-ray models and space-radiation transport calculations.
