# The role of the likelihood for elastic scattering uncertainty quantification

> Background: Analyses of elastic scattering with the optical model (OMP) are widely used in nuclear reactions. Purpose: Previous work compared a traditional frequentist approach and a Bayesian approach to quantify uncertainties in the OMP. In this study, we revisit this comparison and consider the role of the likelihood used in the analysis. Method: We compare the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm for $χ^{2}$ minimization with Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling to obtain parameter posteriors. Following previous work, we consider how results are affected when $χ^{2}$/N is used for the likelihood function, N being the number of data points, to account for possible correlations in the model and underestimation of the error in the data. Results: We analyze a simple linear model and then move to OMP analysis of elastic angular distributions using a) a 5-parameter model and b) a 6-parameter model. In the linear model, the frequentist and Bayesian approaches yield consistent optima and uncertainty estimates. The same is qualitatively true for the 5-parameter OMP analysis. For the 6-parameter OMP analysis, the parameter posterior is no longer well-approximated by a Gaussian and a covariance-based frequentist prediction becomes unreliable. In all cases, when the Bayesian approach uses $χ^{2}$/N in the likelihood, uncertainties increase by $\sqrt{N}$. Conclusions: When the parameter posterior is near-Gaussian and the same likelihood is used, the frequentist and Bayesian approaches recover consistent parameter uncertainty estimates. If the parameter posterior has significant higher moments, the covariance-only frequentist approach becomes unreliable and the Bayesian approach should be used. Empirical coverage can serve as an important internal check for uncertainty estimation, providing red flags for uncertainty analyses.

# The role of the likelihood for elastic scattering uncertainty quantification

## Key claim

When the same likelihood function is used, frequentist (Levenberg-Marquardt) and Bayesian (Markov Chain Monte Carlo) approaches recover consistent parameter uncertainty estimates for optical model potentials, provided the parameter posterior is near-Gaussian; the larger uncertainties previously attributed to Bayesian methods in King (2019) actually arise from using a different likelihood function (chi-squared/N) rather than from the Bayesian framework itself.

## Method

The authors compare four calibration approaches applied to a linear toy model and to Becchetti-Greenlees optical model analyses of elastic scattering angular distributions for 90Zr(n,n)90Zr at 10 MeV using real data from Wang (1990). They use Levenberg-Marquardt minimization with a standard chi-squared likelihood, Levenberg-Marquardt with post-fit uncertainty rescaling by chi-squared per degree of freedom, Markov Chain Monte Carlo with the standard chi-squared likelihood, and Markov Chain Monte Carlo with a chi-squared/N likelihood. The optical model calculations use the TOMFOOL library, and the authors examine both a 5-parameter model (real volume depth, radius, diffuseness; imaginary surface depth and radius) and a 6-parameter model that additionally varies the imaginary surface diffuseness.

## Key numbers

* For the linear toy model with N=25 data points, the MCMC-chi-squared/N approach yields uncertainty estimates that are sqrt(N) ≈ 4.5 times larger than the other approaches.
* The 90Zr(n,n)90Zr angular distribution data set contains N=36 points at 10 MeV incident energy.
* For the 5-parameter optical model, chi-squared per degree of freedom is approximately 1 at the optimum, indicating the model reproduces the data well.
* In the 6-parameter optical model, the posterior distribution for the imaginary surface depth and diffuseness parameters shows a curved, non-Gaussian "banana shape" that the Hessian-based frequentist approach approximates poorly.

## Context

This work revisits the comparison between frequentist and Bayesian uncertainty quantification for the optical model originally performed by King (2019), which found that Bayesian uncertainties were larger than frequentist ones. The authors show that the discrepancy was primarily due to King (2019) using chi-squared/N in the Bayesian likelihood while using standard chi-squared in the frequentist approach, combined with fixing different numbers of parameters between the two methods. The study extends previous work by Lovell (2017) on covariance-based uncertainty propagation and by King (2018, 2019) on Bayesian methods for optical potentials. The results enable practitioners to choose between frequentist and Bayesian approaches based on whether the parameter posterior is well-approximated by a Gaussian, and establish empirical coverage as a diagnostic tool for uncertainty quantification in nuclear reaction analyses.
