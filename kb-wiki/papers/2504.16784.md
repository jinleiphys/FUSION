# Particles in finite volumes and a toy model of decaying neutrons

> It is well-known that the momentum spectra of particles confined to finite spatial volumes deviate from the continuous spectra used for unconfined particles. In this article, we consider real scalar particles confined to finite volumes with periodic boundary conditions, such that the particles' spectra are discrete. We directly compute the density matrices describing the decay processes $ϕ\to φ^2$ and $ϕ\to φχν$, and subsequently derive expressions for the decay probabilities both for confined and unconfined particles. The latter decay process is used as a rough toy model for a neutron decaying into a proton, an electron, and an anti-electron neutrino. We propose that finite volume effects can have an impact on the outcomes of experiments measuring the neutron lifetime. In addition, our findings at the toy model level suggest that taking into account possible initial correlations between neutrons and their daughter particles might be relevant as well.

## Key claim

Finite volume effects from the confining boundaries in neutron lifetime experiments can produce noticeable differences in measured decay rates, potentially contributing to the neutron lifetime discrepancy without requiring new physics; the author explicitly hedges that this is a toy-model-level suggestion and that a more realistic model is needed for confirmation.

## Method

The author computes density matrix elements for scalar field decays \(\phi \to \varphi^2\) and \(\phi \to \varphi\chi\nu\) using the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism and thermo field dynamics, both in infinite volumes and in finite volumes with periodic boundary conditions. For the neutron decay toy model, the author treats the neutron as a scalar \(\phi\) decaying via a contact interaction into three other scalars representing proton, electron, and anti-electron neutrino, and compares decay probabilities between infinite and finite volume cases using the volume of the \(\tau\)SPECT magnetic trap.

## Key numbers

- Neutron mass \(M = 939.5654205\) MeV, proton mass \(m_\varphi = 938.2720881\) MeV, electron mass \(m_\chi = 0.5109980\) MeV, neutrino mass \(m_\nu \approx 0.7\) eV
- Fermi coupling constant \(G_F = 1.16637 \times 10^{-5}\) GeV\(^{-2}\), CKM matrix element \(v_{ud} = 0.97367\)
- Beam method average neutron lifetime: \(\tau = 888.1 \pm 2.0\) s; UCN storage method average: \(\tau = 878.36 \pm 0.45\) s; discrepancy of about 10 s
- Magnetic trap volume for \(\tau\)SPECT experiment: \(V = (50 \,\text{mm}/2)^2 \cdot \pi \cdot 1 \,\text{m}\)
- Ratio of infinite to finite volume decay probabilities for \(\phi \to \varphi^2\): \(P^\infty_{0;2} / P^V_{0;2} \approx 4 M^3 V / \aleph_n\)

## Context

This work extends the finite volume formalism developed by Luscher (1985, 1986, 1990, 1991) and Lellouch and Luscher (2000) to the context of neutron decay, and builds on methods from Burrage (2018, 2019) and Kading (2022) using the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism and thermo field dynamics. The proposal is related to recent suggestions of a measurable neutron Purcell effect by He (2021) and a Casimir effect resolution to the neutron lifetime problem by Cea (2021), but differs by deriving the decay probability directly from the density matrix and accounting for discrete momentum spectra. The work enables future development of a more realistic model that could confirm whether finite volume effects explain the neutron lifetime discrepancy between beam and UCN storage experiments.
