# Influence of measurement on the life-time and the line-width of unstable systems

> We investigate the quantum Zeno effect in the case of electron tunneling out of a quantum dot in the presence of continuous monitoring by a detector. It is shown that the Schrödinger equation for the whole system can be reduced to Bloch-type rate equations describing the combined time-development of the detector and the measured system. Using these equations we find that continuous measurement of the unstable system does not affect its exponential decay to a reservoir with a constant density of states. The width of the energy distribution of the tunneling electron, however, is not equal to the inverse life-time -- it increases due to the decoherence generated by the detector. We extend the analysis to the case of a reservoir described by an energy dependent density of states, and we show that continuous measurement of such quantum systems affects both the exponential decay rate and the energy distribution. The decay does not always slow down, but might be accelerated. The energy distribution of the tunneling electron may reveal the lines invisible before the measurement.

## Key claim

Continuous measurement of an unstable system does not affect its exponential decay rate when the reservoir has a constant density of states, but the energy distribution of the decay products broadens due to detector-induced decoherence; however, when the reservoir has an energy-dependent density of states, continuous measurement can either slow down or accelerate the decay.

## Method

The authors model electron tunneling from a quantum dot into a reservoir, with a point-contact detector placed nearby to monitor the dot occupation. They derive Bloch-type rate equations from the many-body Schrödinger equation for the combined system of dot, reservoir, and detector, without invoking the projection postulate. The analysis is performed for two cases: a reservoir with constant density of states, and a reservoir with a Lorentzian-modulated density of states (e.g., a resonant cavity).

## Key numbers

- For constant density of states, the decay probability of the dot state is $\sigma_{00}(t) = e^{-\Gamma_0 t}$, where $\Gamma_0 = 2\pi\Omega_\alpha^2\rho$ is the constant decay width.
- The energy distribution of the tunneling electron in the constant-density case is a Lorentzian with width $\Gamma_0 + \Gamma_d$, where $\Gamma_d = (\sqrt{D} - \sqrt{D'})^2$ is the decoherence rate from the detector.
- For a Lorentzian density of states with width $\Gamma_1$ and background width $\bar\Gamma$, the decay time is $T = \tau / (1 + \tau\bar\Gamma)$, where $\tau = 1/\Gamma_1 + [4\epsilon_{10}^2 + (\bar\Gamma + \Gamma_1)^2] / [4\Omega_\alpha^2(\bar\Gamma + \Gamma_1)]$.
- In the limit $\Gamma_1 \gg \Omega_\alpha, \epsilon_{01}$ with $\bar\Gamma = 0$, the decay becomes exponential with $\sigma_{00}(t) \simeq \exp(-4\Omega_\alpha^2 t / \Gamma_1)$ and decay time $T \simeq \Gamma_1 / 4\Omega_\alpha^2$.

## Context

This work extends the quantum Zeno effect discussion from oscillatory two-level systems (studied by Stodolsky (1990) and Gurvitz (1997)) to exponential decay in mesoscopic systems. It provides a microscopic quantum-mechanical description of the measurement device (a point-contact detector) without invoking the projection postulate, in contrast to earlier treatments by Fonda (1978) and Panov (1999). The results enable experimental investigation of measurement effects on decay rates and energy distributions using existing mesoscopic systems, and show that the energy distribution of tunneling electrons may reveal spectral lines invisible before measurement.
