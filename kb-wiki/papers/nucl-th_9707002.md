# Modern nucleon-nucleon potentials and symmetry energy in infinite matter

> We study the symmetry energy in infinite nuclear matter employing a non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach and using various new nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials, which fit np and pp scattering data very accurately. The potential models we employ are the recent versions of the Nijmegen group, Nijm-I, Nijm-II and Reid93, the Argonne $V_{18}$ potential and the CD-Bonn potential. All these potentials yield a symmetry energy which increases with density, resolving a discrepancy that existed for older NN potentials. The origin of remaining differences is discussed.

## Key claim

All five modern, phase-shift-equivalent nucleon-nucleon potentials (CD-Bonn, Nijm-I, Nijm-II, Reid93, and Argonne V18) yield a symmetry energy that increases monotonically with density, resolving a discrepancy where older local potentials (Reid, Argonne V14) predicted a saturation or even a decrease at high densities; however, the remaining differences among the modern potentials are traced to the inclusion or neglect of non-localities in the short-range central force and in the one-pion-exchange tensor component.

## Method

The authors employed a non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) approach with a continuous single-particle spectrum to compute the energy per nucleon for symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter. The Bethe-Goldstone equation was solved for each of the five modern NN potentials, and the symmetry energy was obtained as the difference between the energy per particle in pure neutron matter and that in symmetric nuclear matter. The contributions of individual partial waves (e.g., 1S0, 3S1-3D1, P-waves) to the potential energy were isolated to identify the origin of differences among the potentials.

## Key numbers

* At a density of 0.6 fm^-3, the contribution to the potential energy from the 1S0 partial wave in symmetric nuclear matter is -33.4 MeV (CD-Bonn), -33.2 MeV (Nijm-I), -29.2 MeV (Nijm-II), -29.1 MeV (Reid93), and -29.1 MeV (Argonne V18).
* At a density of 0.6 fm^-3, the contribution from the 3S1 partial wave in symmetric nuclear matter is -33 MeV (CD-Bonn), -24 MeV (Nijm-I), -20.4 MeV (Nijm-II), -21.3 MeV (Reid93), and -20.1 MeV (Argonne V18), a difference of more than 50% between CD-Bonn and the local potentials.
* At a density of 0.6 fm^-3, the potential energy per nucleon in pure neutron matter is -29.0 MeV (CD-Bonn), -30.2 MeV (Nijm-I), -28.7 MeV (Nijm-II), -28.1 MeV (Reid93), and -24.0 MeV (Argonne V18).
* The direct URCA process in beta-stable matter starts at a density of 0.88 fm^-3 for CD-Bonn, 1.05 fm^-3 for Argonne V18, 1.25 fm^-3 for Nijm-I, 1.36 fm^-3 for Reid93, and above 1.5 fm^-3 for Nijm-II.
* The CD-Bonn potential yields a deuteron D-state probability of 4.83%, while the other potentials give approximately 5.6%.

## Context

This work extends earlier BHF and variational calculations (Wiringa et al., 1988) that used older potentials (Reid, Argonne V14, Paris) and found conflicting density dependences of the symmetry energy at high densities. By using five modern potentials that fit the same NN scattering data with a chi-squared per datum near 1.0, the authors show that the earlier discrepancies were due to the potentials themselves, not the many-body method. The results enable more reliable predictions for neutron-star properties such as the proton fraction in beta-stable matter and the threshold density for direct URCA cooling, and they motivate the inclusion of three-body forces and relativistic effects for a more realistic equation of state.
