# On deformability of atoms---comparative study between atoms and atomic nuclei

> Atomic nuclei can be spontaneously deformed into non-spherical shapes as many-nucleon systems. We discuss to what extent a similar deformation takes place in many-electron systems. To this end, we employ several many-body methods, such as the unrestricted Hartree-Fock method, post-Hartree-Fock methods, and the density functional theory, to compute the electron density distribution in atoms. We show that the electron density distribution of open-shell atoms is deformed due solely to the single-particle valence orbitals, while the core part remains spherical. This is in contrast to atomic nuclei, which can be deformed collectively. We qualitatively discuss the origin for this apparent difference between atoms and nuclei by estimating the energy change due to deformation. We find that nature of the interaction plays an essential role for the collective deformation.

## Key claim

The electron density distribution of open-shell atoms is deformed, but this deformation originates solely from the single-particle valence orbitals while the core remains spherical, in contrast to atomic nuclei which can deform collectively; the authors argue that the purely repulsive nature of the electron-electron interaction prevents collective deformation, whereas the net attractive interaction in nuclei allows it.

## Method

The authors computed electron density distributions for a wide range of atoms (from Li to Xe) using multiple many-body methods: unrestricted Hartree-Fock (UHF), post-Hartree-Fock methods (CCD, CCSD, CID, CISD, MP2, MP3, MP4), and density functional theory (LDA). They calculated deformation parameters (beta), quadrupole moments (Q), and root-mean-square radii for each atom, and performed natural orbital analysis to determine valence electron configurations. They also computed single-particle quadrupole moments for individual orbitals in selected atoms (Al, Sc, Ti) to identify which orbitals contribute to the total deformation.

## Key numbers

*   For Al atom (Z=13) at UHF/6-31+G level: total quadrupole moment Q_z = +11.1387 (Hartree atomic units), deformation parameter beta_z = +0.2643, with the deformation originating entirely from the 3p_z valence orbital (single-particle q_z = +11.1416) while all core orbitals have q_z = 0.0000.
*   For Sc atom (Z=21) at UHF/6-31+G level: total Q_z = -0.4189, beta_z = -0.0062, with the 3d_{xy} orbital contributing q_z = -2.1642 and the 4s orbital contributing q_z = -0.2152, while core orbitals remain spherical.
*   For Ti atom (Z=22) at UHF/6-31+G level: total Q_z = +0.3250, beta_z = +0.0051.
*   The degeneracy lifting of valence electrons due to deformation is 0.05 Hartree or much less.
*   Noble-gas atoms (Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe), half-shell-closed atoms (N, P, As, Sb, V, Cr, Co, Cu), and s-block atoms (Li, Be, Na, Mg, K, Ca, Rb, Sr) all have beta = 0.0000 (spherical) at UHF/6-31+G level.

## Context

This work extends the concept of nuclear deformation, well-established in nuclear physics through rotational spectroscopy of deformed nuclei, to atomic physics by asking whether isolated atoms can exhibit similar collective deformation. The authors contrast their findings with the collective nuclear deformation observed in gamma-ray spectroscopy of rotational spectra, noting that atomic deformations are neither significant nor collective and should not show rotational spectra. The study enables future experimental tests using techniques such as photo-ionization microscopy (Stodolna 2013, Cohen 2016) or tomographic imaging (Itatani 2004) to directly probe electron density distributions.
