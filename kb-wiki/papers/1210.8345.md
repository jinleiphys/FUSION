# Hydro overview

> We review recent progress in applying relativistic hydrodynamics to the modeling of heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, with emphasis on anisotropic flow and flow fluctuations.

# Hydro overview

## Key claim

Relativistic hydrodynamics can explain the detailed structure of two-particle azimuthal correlations measured in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, but only below transverse momenta of approximately 5 GeV/c; above this threshold, jet-induced correlations break the independent-particle assumption underlying the hydrodynamic picture.

## Method

The authors simulate a single central (b=0) Au-Au collision using the NeXus event generator to obtain an initial energy density profile, then evolve it through ideal hydrodynamics (NeXSPheRIO code). They transform the fluid into hadrons using a Monte-Carlo generator repeated thousands of times per hydro event, and analyze the resulting azimuthal distributions via Fourier decomposition to extract anisotropic flow coefficients v_n and event-plane angles Ψ_n. They compare the predicted two-particle correlation matrix V_{nΔ}(p_t^a, p_t^b) against ALICE data for 0-10% central Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV.

- Azimuthal anisotropies are at the percent level at low p_t and become stronger as p_t increases, as shown in Fig. 1 (right) for a single central Pb-Pb collision at 2.76 TeV.
- In an actual experiment, the relative statistical error is typically 50% for v_2 (in a mid-central Pb-Pb collision analyzed by CMS or ATLAS) and 100% for v_3.
- For n=3 (triangular flow), diagonal elements of the correlation matrix become negative above 5 GeV/c, violating the first inequality and providing evidence for breakdown of the independent-particle hypothesis.
- The p_t bin boundaries used in the ALICE analysis are: 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 15 (in GeV/c).

## Context

This review extends the "flow hypothesis" established by Alver (2010) and Luzum (2010), who showed that long-range azimuthal correlations are compatible with hydrodynamics. It directly tests the predictions of event-by-event hydrodynamics against ALICE data (Aamodt, 2011) for two-particle correlations, demonstrating that deviations from factorization provide evidence for event-by-event flow fluctuations rather than nonflow effects. The work enables future comparisons where event-by-event hydrodynamics should address the full structure of two-particle correlations, and opens the door to studying higher-order correlations and mixed event-plane correlations as measured at the LHC by Jia (2012) and Bilandzic (2012).
