# The Mass and Radius of the Neutron Star in the Bulge Low-Mass X-ray Binary KS 1731-260

> Measurements of neutron star masses and radii are instrumental for determining the equation of state of their interiors, understanding the dividing line between neutron stars and black holes, and for obtaining accurate statistics of source populations in the Galaxy. We report here on the measurement of the mass and radius of the neutron star in the low-mass X-ray binary KS 1731-260. The analysis of the spectroscopic data on multiple thermonuclear bursts yields well-constrained values for the apparent angular area and the Eddington flux of the source, both of which depend in a distinct way on the mass and radius of the neutron star. The binary KS 1731-260 is in the direction of the Galactic bulge, allowing a distance estimate based on the density of stars in that direction. Making use of the Han & Gould model, we determine the probability distribution over the distance to the source, which is peaked at 8 kpc. Combining these measurements, we place a strong upper bound on the radius of the neutron star, R <= 12 km, while confining its mass to M <= 1.8 M_sun.

## Key claim

The neutron star in KS 1731-260 has a radius of R <= 12 km and a mass of M <= 1.8 M_sun, but these are upper bounds rather than precise measurements, and the result depends on a distance estimate based on the stellar density toward the Galactic bulge rather than a direct distance measurement.

## Method

The authors analyzed time-resolved spectra from 24 Type-I thermonuclear X-ray bursts observed with the Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer (RXTE), measuring the apparent angular area from the cooling tails of bursts and the Eddington flux from two photospheric radius expansion bursts at the touchdown moment. They combined these measurements with a distance probability distribution derived from the Han and Gould (2003) model of stellar density along the line of sight toward the Galactic bulge, then performed a Bayesian analysis to obtain confidence contours on mass and radius, marginalizing over distance and hydrogen mass fraction.

## Key numbers

- Apparent angular area: A = 88.4 ± 5.1 (km/10 kpc)^2, corresponding to a radius of 9.4 ± 0.3 (km/10 kpc) during the cooling tails of bursts
- Average touchdown flux: (4.45 ± 0.12) × 10^(-8) erg cm^(-2) s^(-1), with a conservative total uncertainty of 0.22 × 10^(-8) erg cm^(-2) s^(-1)
- Distance probability distribution peaked at 8 kpc, with the source most probably lying between 5 kpc and 11 kpc
- Upper bound on neutron star radius: R <= 12 km
- Upper bound on neutron star mass: M <= 1.8 M_sun

## Context

This work extends the spectroscopic method for measuring neutron star masses and radii previously applied by Ozel et al. (2009) and Guver et al. (2010a, b) to thermonuclear bursters, and is consistent with earlier measurements from both bursting neutron stars and quiescent neutron stars in globular clusters (Webb and Barret 2007; Guillot et al. 2010). The result challenges equations of state that predict neutron star radii larger than approximately 12 km, and the authors demonstrate that their Bayesian marginalization over distance and hydrogen mass fraction does not bias the mass-radius constraints.
