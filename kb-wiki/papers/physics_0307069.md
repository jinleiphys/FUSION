# The unreasonable accuracy of the Jastrow approach in many-body physics

> We investigate in a simple model whether a Jastrow-based approach for a many-body system containing two-body interactions can be exact. By comparison with recent assertions to the contrary, we find that in general the exact wave function is \emph{not} purely two-body correlated. Nonetheless, we show that the energy estimate obtained from the calculations is as accurate as it can possibly be, suggesting why Jastrow wave functions are such a good choice.

# The unreasonable accuracy of the Jastrow approach in many-body physics

## Key claim

The Jastrow wave function is not in general exact for many-body systems interacting via two-body forces, contrary to recent claims by Nooijen and by Piecuch and collaborators; however, the energy estimate obtained from Jastrow-based calculations is surprisingly accurate, almost as accurate as the optimal variational estimate, even when the wave function itself is not exact.

## Method

The authors analyze the Lipkin model, a two-level fermion system with SU(2) dynamical symmetry, using perturbation theory in the coupling parameter λ. They compare three approaches: solving Nooijen's equations order by order, solving the variational problem, and requiring exact equality of the Jastrow-correlated wave function to the exact wave function order by order. All calculations are performed for particle numbers corresponding to J = 1 through J = 8 using computer algebra.

## Key numbers

- For J = 1, 2, 3 the Jastrow wave function is exact at all orders in λ.
- For J > 3, the wave function is correct only up to 7th order in λ.
- The variational energy estimate using the Jastrow wave function is accurate to 14th order in λ for J > 3.
- The solution to Nooijen's equations terminates at order λ^13 for J > 3, yielding energy accuracy up to order λ^12.
- The variational energy deviates from the exact result at order λ^16 for J > 3.

## Context

This work directly addresses claims by Nooijen and by Piecuch and collaborators that a Jastrow-type exponential two-body operator can give the exact ground state for any system with two-body interactions. The authors use the Lipkin model as a test bed to disprove this claim, while explaining why the method appears exact in practical calculations: the energy accuracy is far higher than the wave function accuracy, and for nearly perturbative problems the deviations may be undetectable. The results clarify the limits of Jastrow-based methods in quantum chemistry and nuclear physics, and suggest that variational energy estimates should be preferred over the similarity-transformed energy expression when using Nooijen's equations.
