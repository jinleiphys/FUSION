# Spinodal phase separation in relativistic nuclear collisions

> The spinodal amplification of density fluctuations is treated perturbatively within dissipative fluid dynamics for the purpose of elucidating the prospects for this mechanism to cause a phase separation to occur during a relativistic nuclear collision. The present study includes not only viscosity but also heat conduction (whose effect on the growth rates is of comparable magnitude but opposite), as well as a gradient term in the local pressure, and the corresponding dispersion relation for collective modes in bulk matter is derived from relativistic fluid dynamics. A suitable two-phase equation of state is obtained by interpolation between a hadronic gas and a quark-gluon plasma, while the transport coefficients are approximated by simple parametrizations that are suitable at any degree of net baryon density. We calculate the degree of spinodal amplification occurring along specific dynamical phase trajectories characteristic of nuclear collision at various energies. The results bring out the important fact that the prospects for spinodal phase separation to occur can be greatly enhanced by careful tuning of the collision energy to ensure that the thermodynamic conditions associated with the maximum compression lie inside the region of spinodal instability.

## Key claim

Spinodal phase separation between confined and deconfined phases in relativistic nuclear collisions may be triggered within a certain (likely relatively narrow) optimal energy range, provided the collision energy is carefully tuned so that the thermodynamic conditions at maximum compression lie inside the region of spinodal instability; this expectation is rather insensitive to the still poorly known strength of the transport coefficients.

## Method

The study treats spinodal amplification of density fluctuations perturbatively within relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics, deriving the cubic dispersion relation for collective modes in bulk matter that includes shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, heat conduction, and a gradient term in the local pressure. A two-phase equation of state is constructed by interpolation between a hadronic gas and a quark-gluon plasma, while transport coefficients are parametrized in terms of enthalpy density and interparticle spacing. The degree of spinodal amplification is calculated by integrating growth rates along dynamical phase trajectories characteristic of nuclear collisions at various energies.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature for phase coexistence: \(T_c \approx 142.5\) MeV
- At \(\rho = 6.5\,\rho_s\) and \(T = 70\) MeV with ideal finite-range fluid dynamics: fastest growth time \(t_0 \approx 1.38\) fm/c, optimal wave number \(k_0 \approx 2.0\) fm\(^{-1}\), corresponding wavelength \(\ell_0 = 2\pi/k_0 \approx 3.14\) fm
- The conversion constant \(c_0\) for transport coefficient parametrizations: \(c_0 \approx 0.12779\)
- The effective viscosity length scale: \(\lambda_{\rm visc} = (4/3)\,\eta_0\,c_0\,d(\rho,T)\), where \(d\) is the interparticle spacing
- The heat conduction length scale: \(\lambda_{\rm heat} = \kappa_0\,c_0\,d(\rho,T)\)

## Context

This work extends an earlier preliminary study by Randrup (2009) that employed a schematic equation of state based on a generalized classical gas, now using a more realistic interpolated equation of state and including both viscosity and heat conductivity. The study builds on the fluid-dynamical framework for first-order phase transitions discussed by Skokov and collaborators (2009), and the dispersion relation follows the form given by Heiselberg and collaborators (1993). The results enable estimates of the typical drop size in an assembly of plasma drops embedded in hadron gas that would result from spinodal phase separation, and suggest that beam-energy scan experiments at RHIC, the CBM experiment at FAIR, and the proposed NICA facility may find observable signals by tuning collision energy to the optimal range.
