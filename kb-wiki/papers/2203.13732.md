# Collective and dissipative effects in a common microscopic dynamical description

> Depending on the energy regime, the dynamics of heavy-ion collisions reveals a variety of different mechanisms which are attributed to the combination of collective and dissipative effects. Semi-classical approaches have been successful in describing chaotic regimes at Fermi-energies but they gradually lose precision when extending to collective behaviour and in general when low-energy features become more determinant in the dynamics. To improve on this description, we propose a theoretical approach starting from the TDHF scheme. A quantum representation with a moving basis function has been worked out with a double aim. Firstly, achieving a simplified solution to handle the evolution in time. Secondly, introducing beyond-mean-field extensions and stochastic contributions. Applications to nuclear collisions at incident energies around low to Fermi energy are presented.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a theoretical framework that starts from the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) scheme and decomposes nucleonic wave functions into a set of moving Gaussian basis functions, with the aim of achieving a simplified time evolution while introducing beyond-mean-field extensions and stochastic contributions. The approach is presented as an improvement over semi-classical methods that lose precision when extending to collective behavior at low energies, but the results shown are limited to illustrative test cases rather than a validated quantitative model.

## Method

The authors decompose each nucleonic wave function into a superposition of moving Gaussian basis functions with varying widths in momentum and configuration space, where the coefficients can be both positive and negative to preserve the non-local character of the Hartree-Fock wave function. The mean-field evolution is derived from the Euler-Lagrange equations applied to this Gaussian representation, while a Boltzmann-Langevin One-Body (BLOB)-like collision term is added on top to introduce large-density fluctuations through a stochastic seed. The Pauli principle is checked using the scalar product of the wave functions rather than phase-space occupancy, and nucleon-nucleon collisions are implemented by collapsing the involved wave functions to representative Gaussians and adapting the final scattered wave packets.

## Key numbers

- The low-energy regime is defined as up to around 15 MeV per nucleon, where two-body nucleon-nucleon collisions are suppressed due to Pauli blocking
- The peripheral test collision uses $^{40}$Ca + $^{48}$Ca at 35 AMeV with impact parameter b = 9 fm, with snapshots shown at times 0, 40, 80, 110, 150, 200, and 250 fm/c
- The dissipative test collision uses $^{36}$Ar + $^{58}$Ni at 50 AMeV with impact parameter b = 0 fm, with snapshots shown at times 0, 10, 20, 30, 40, and 50 fm/c

## Context

This work extends stochastic mean-field approaches such as the Boltzmann-Langevin equation (Chomaz, 2004; Napolitani, 2013; Colonna, 2017) and the BLOB model (Napolitani, 2017) by starting from the many-body Schroedinger equation rather than semi-classical approximations. The authors build on previous stochastic TDHF formulations (Jouault, 1998; De La Mota, 2020) and aim to provide a unified description covering the transition from Fermi to low energies, which would enable studying the evolution of fragments and clusters as a function of time and density. The approach is intended to address the limitation that models at Fermi energies lack mean-field phenomenology (isospin drifts, collective modes) while models approaching low energy miss large-amplitude fluctuations and mechanisms where splits occur in the system.
