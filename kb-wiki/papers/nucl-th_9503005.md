# Precise solution of few-body problems with the stochastic variational method

> A precise variational solution to $N$=2--6-body problems is reported. The trial wave functions are chosen to be combinations of correlated Gaussians, which facilitate a fully analytical calculation of the matrix elements. The nonlinear parameters of the trial function are selected by a stochastic method. Fermionic and bosonic few-body systems are investigated for interactions of different type. A comparison of the results with those available in the literature shows that the method is both accurate and efficient.

# Precise solution of few-body problems with the stochastic variational method

## Key claim
The stochastic variational method (SVM) provides an accurate and efficient variational solution to few-body problems with N=2–6 particles, but the method's limitations are those implied by the basis size, which may become excessive as the number of particles and/or spin and isospin configurations become large.

## Method
The trial wave functions are constructed as combinations of correlated Gaussians, with nonlinear parameters selected randomly rather than optimized. The basis is built step by step: a random parameter set is generated, and if the energy gained by including it in the basis exceeds a preset threshold, it is admitted; otherwise it is discarded. Matrix elements are evaluated fully analytically through a three-step procedure involving a single-particle generator-coordinate representation, transformation to Jacobi generator coordinates, and integral transformation to the correlated Gaussian parameters.

## Key numbers
- For the Malfliet–Tjon potential, the SVM ground-state energy for the triton (N=3) agrees with Faddeev and ATMS results; for the alpha particle (N=4), agreement is similarly achieved with basis dimensions much smaller than in the CRCGBV method.
- For the Minnesota potential with Coulomb interaction, the triton energy converges to a value close to experimental data, and the alpha particle energy and size converge to values close to experiment with "rather small bases."
- For the negative positronium ion (e⁺, e⁻, e⁻), the SVM reproduces the first six digits of the extremely accurate variational calculation of Ref. [epe]; the dipositronium molecule (2e⁺, 2e⁻) energy is slightly better than the variational result of Ref. [epep] using the same basis dimension.
- For the (3e⁺, 3e⁻) system, the energy converges to 0.765989 a.u. (sum of dipositronium molecule energy 0.515989 a.u. and positronium energy 0.25 a.u.), with the rms radius tending to very large values, indicating no bound state.
- Average computational time is 10 minutes for a four-body calculation and 2 hours for a six-body calculation on the VPP500 computer of RIKEN.

## Context
This work extends the stochastic variational method previously applied to light exotic nuclei such as ⁶He, ⁸He, ⁹Li, and ⁹C (Varga, Suzuki, and Lovas, 1994; Varga, Suzuki, and Ohbayasi, 1994) to a broader range of few-body systems. It provides an alternative to Faddeev-Yakubovsky methods (Gloeckle, 1983; Faddeev, 1965), Quantum Monte Carlo methods (Carlson, 1987; Pandharipande, 1981), and the Coupled Rearrangement Channel Gaussian Basis Variational method (Kamimura, 1988). The SVM enables systematic calculations without assuming any cluster structure or restricting the model space, and the authors report that calculations with realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials are under way.
