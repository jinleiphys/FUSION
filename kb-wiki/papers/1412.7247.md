# Anisotropic pressure and hyperons in neutron stars

> We study the effects of anisotropic pressure on properties of the neutron stars with hyperons inside its core within the framework of extended relativistic mean field. It is found that the main effects of anisotropic pressure on neutron star matter is to increase the stiffness of the equation of state, which compensates for the softening of the EOS due to the hyperons. The maximum mass and redshift predictions of anisotropic neutron star with hyperonic core are quite compatible with the result of recent observational constraints if we use the parameter of anisotropic pressure model $h \le 0.8$[1] and $Λ\le -1.15$ [2]. The radius of the corresponding neutron star at $M$=1.4 $M_\odot$ is more than 13 km, while the effect of anisotropic pressure on the minimum mass of neutron star is insignificant. Furthermore, due to the anisotropic pressure in the neutron star, the maximum mass limit of higher than 2.1 $M_\odot$ cannot rule out the presence of hyperons in the neutron star core.

## Key claim

The paper argues that introducing anisotropic pressure (where radial and tangential pressures differ) in neutron star matter can stiffen the equation of state enough to compensate for the softening caused by hyperons, making it possible for neutron stars with hyperonic cores to reach maximum masses above 2.1 solar masses, provided the anisotropic pressure parameters are within the ranges \(h \le 0.8\) and \(\Lambda \le -1.15\).

## Method

The author uses an extended relativistic mean field (ERMF) model with the BSP parameter set for nucleons and SU(6) symmetry to determine hyperon-meson coupling constants, including \(\Lambda\), \(\Sigma\), and \(\Xi\) hyperons. The crust equation of state is taken from Miyatsu et al. (2013). Two phenomenological models of local pressure anisotropy are adopted: the HB model, where tangential pressure depends on radial pressure and its gradient, and the DY model, where tangential pressure depends on radial pressure and local compactness \(2GM/r\). The anisotropic Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations are then solved to compute neutron star mass, radius, and gravitational redshift.

## Key numbers

- The maximum mass of anisotropic neutron stars with hyperonic cores can exceed 2.1 \(M_\odot\) when using anisotropic parameters \(h \le 0.8\) (HB model) or \(\Lambda \le -1.15\) (DY model).
- The radius of the corresponding neutron star at \(M = 1.4\ M_\odot\) is more than 13 km.
- The lower bound on the maximum neutron star mass from observations is taken as \(M_G \sim 2.1\ M_\odot\), based on pulsar J1614-2230 (mass \(1.97 \pm 0.04\ M_\odot\)) and pulsar J0348+0432 (mass \(2.01 \pm 0.04\ M_\odot\)).
- For comparison, the IUFSU parameter set yields \(M_{\rm max} = 1.94\ M_\odot\) and the BSP parameter set yields \(M_{\rm max} = 2.02\ M_\odot\) for nucleonic stars without hyperons.
- The Brueckner-Hartree-Fock model with hyperons yields \(M_{\rm max} \sim 1.3\)–\(1.4\ M_\odot\).

## Context

This work addresses the "hyperonization puzzle," where including hyperons in neutron star cores typically softens the equation of state and reduces the predicted maximum mass below the observational lower bound of about 2.1 \(M_\odot\) (Demorest 2010; Antoniadis 2013). Previous attempts to resolve this within relativistic mean field models required adjusting hyperon-sector parameters or introducing hypothetical bosons (e.g., Bednarek et al. 2012; Sulaksono and Agus 2012). The author shows that anisotropic pressure, previously studied with polytropic equations of state by Herrera and Barreto (2013) and Doneva and Yazadjiev (2012), can reconcile hyperonic neutron stars with the observed high masses, enabling future work on more realistic microscopic models of anisotropy.
