# Elliptic flow of charm quarks produced in the early stage of pA collisions

> We investigate the build-up of elliptic flow of charm quarks produced in the early pre-equilibrium stage of high-energy proton--nucleus collisions. The initial stage is modeled within the Color Glass Condensate framework as an evolving glasma, initialized through the McLerran--Venugopalan model. Subnucleonic fluctuations have been implemented as constituent-quark hotspots for both the proton and the nuclear participants. Charm quarks are propagated in the evolving non-Abelian background by solving the relativistic Wong equations for their coordinates, momenta, and color charges. First, we compute the nuclear modification factor of charm quarks, finding a slight migration towards higher $p_T$ states in agreement with previous results in the literature. Then, we focus on the azimuthal anisotropies acquired through the interaction with glasma fields. We find that glasma-induced momentum anisotropies are efficiently transmitted to heavy quarks within $τ\sim 0.4~\mathrm{fm/c}$, leading to a sizeable charm-quark $v_2$, with a magnitude that increases with the strength of the initial fields and with the number of nuclear participants. Remarkably, we show that the early-stage contribution alone can account for a significant fraction of the experimentally observed $J/ψ$ elliptic flow in p-Pb collisions, indicating that pre-hydrodynamic dynamics can play a non-negligible role in the final-state heavy-flavor collectivity, especially in small systems.

## Key claim
The authors claim that glasma-induced momentum anisotropies are efficiently transmitted to charm quarks within τ ∼ 0.4 fm/c, and that this early-stage contribution alone can account for a significant fraction of the experimentally observed J/ψ elliptic flow in p-Pb collisions. The hedge is that this indicates pre-hydrodynamic dynamics can play a "non-negligible" (not dominant) role in final-state heavy-flavor collectivity, especially in small systems.

## Method
The initial stage of proton-nucleus collisions is modeled within the Color Glass Condensate framework as an evolving glasma, initialized via the McLerran-Venugopalan model with subnucleonic fluctuations implemented as constituent-quark hotspots for both the proton and the nucleus. Charm quarks are propagated in the evolving non-Abelian background by solving the relativistic Wong equations for their coordinates, momenta, and color charges, with the glasma fields evolved on a real-time lattice using classical Yang-Mills equations and a leapfrog algorithm.

## Key numbers
- Charm-quark formation time fixed at τ_form = 1/(2M) = 0.077 fm/c for charm quark mass M
- Glasma-induced momentum anisotropies transmitted to heavy quarks within τ ∼ 0.4 fm/c
- Nuclear modification factor R_pA shows a crossing point around k_T = 2.5 GeV where the charm quark spectrum is not modified by glasma fields
- For k_T ≲ 2.5 GeV, R_pA is smaller than 1; for k_T ≳ 2.5 GeV, R_pA is greater than 1
- Simulation parameters: transverse size L_⊥ = 3 fm, N_⊥ = 64 lattice points, N_s = 50 color sheets, coupling g = 2 (α_s = 0.3)
- Infrared regulator m = 0.2 GeV = 1 fm^{-1} used as screening mass
- For pA collisions at LHC energy, relevant x values in range [10^{-4}, 10^{-3}]; xg = 3.94 used for x = 10^{-4}
- Centralities reproduced as p-N_part collisions with N_part = 16 (0-5% centrality) and N_part = 8 (40-60% centrality)

## Context
This work extends previous studies of heavy quark dynamics in glasma fields (Oliva 2024, Parisi 2025, Bazak 2023, Carrington 2020-2021, Avramescu 2023-2024) by introducing nucleonic fluctuations on both the proton and nucleus side, whereas prior work used homogeneous color charge distributions. Previous investigations of elliptic flow from inhomogeneous color charge distributions (Schenke 2015, Carrington 2024) focused on bulk gluon anisotropies in AA and pA collisions, while this work examines how those anisotropies are transmitted to charm quarks. The results suggest that pre-hydrodynamic dynamics can contribute to heavy-flavor collectivity in small systems, motivating further studies of early-stage contributions to final-state observables.
