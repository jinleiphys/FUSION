# Condition for the burning of hadronic stars into quark stars

> We will review the approach used for studying the conversion of a hadronic star into a quark star based on the assumption of a infinitely thin combustion zone and we will discuss why, in this scheme, the combustion stops before the whole hadronic star is converted.

## Key claim

The paper argues that, under the standard assumption of an infinitely thin combustion zone, the conversion of a hadronic star into a quark star stops before the entire star is converted, leaving a residual hadronic core of about 0.1 solar masses that converts only on a much longer timescale of tens of seconds.

## Method

The authors review the relativistic hydrodynamic treatment of combustion as a surface of discontinuity, applying the conservation laws for baryon flux, momentum flux, and energy flux across the burning front. They derive the Coll condition for exothermic combustion, which determines a critical baryon density below which the shock adiabat of the hadronic fuel lies above the detonation adiabat of the quark ash, preventing shock-driven combustion. Using a polytropic equation of state for quark matter, they show analytically that the Coll condition is necessary for detonations and defines the density threshold where combustion halts.

## Key numbers

- The critical baryon density at which combustion stops, denoted n1*, lies in the range 0.2–0.3 fm^{-3} for many equations of state, as found in prior studies by Lugones (1994) and Herzog (2011).
- After the fast conversion front reaches n1*, a residual mass of about 0.1 solar masses remains unburnt and converts on a timescale of tens of seconds, as reported in a companion paper by the same authors.
- The fast conversion phase occurs on timescales of a few milliseconds, consistent with numerical simulations by Herzog (2011) and Pagliara (2013).

## Context

This work extends the seminal study by Olinto (1986) on neutron star to quark star conversion and the subsequent numerical hydrodynamics simulations by Lugones (1994), Drago (2005), Niebergal (2010), Herzog (2011), and Pagliara (2013). It clarifies a limitation of the thin-flame approximation: while fast conversion (ms) converts most of the star, a small hadronic core remains and converts slowly (tens of seconds). The authors note that the conversion process has only been studied for cold, non-rotating hadronic stars, and they identify unexplored scenarios, supernovae, protoneutron stars, and neutron star mergers, where the dynamics could differ qualitatively, enabling future work on the coexistence of hadronic and quark star families as proposed by Drago (2013, 2014).
