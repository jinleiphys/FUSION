# Core Transitions in the Breakup of Exotic Nuclei

> An interesting physical process has been unveiled: dynamical core excitation during a breakup reaction of loosely bound $core+N$ systems. These reactions are typically used to extract spectroscopic information and/or astrophysical information. A new method, the eXtended Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (XCDCC) method, was developed to incorporate, in a consistent way and to all orders, core excitation in the bound and scattering states of the projectile, as well as dynamical excitation of the core as it interacts with the target. The model predicts cross sections to specific states of the core. It is applied to the breakup of $^{11}$Be on $^9$Be at 60 MeV/u, and the calculated cross sections are in improved agreement with the data. The distribution of the cross section amongst the various core states is shown to depend on the reaction model used, and not simply on the ground state spectroscopic factors.

# Core Transitions in the Breakup of Exotic Nuclei

## Key claim
Dynamical core excitation during breakup reactions of loosely bound core+neutron systems significantly modifies the distribution of cross sections among core states, and this effect depends on the reaction model used rather than simply on ground state spectroscopic factors. The eXtended Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (XCDCC) method, which incorporates core excitation consistently and to all orders, produces improved agreement with experimental data for the breakup of ¹¹Be on ⁹Be at 60 MeV/u, but the method is currently limited to core states that can be modeled as collective excitations.

## Method
The authors developed the XCDCC method, which extends standard Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) theory by including core degrees of freedom in the projectile Hamiltonian and in the core-target interaction. Projectile states (both bound and scattering) are obtained as coupled-channel eigenstates of the projectile Hamiltonian expanded in terms of core eigenstates, and continuum-continuum couplings are handled by transforming scattering states into square-integrable coupled-channel bins. The three-body Schrödinger equation is then solved with coupling potentials containing both Coulomb and nuclear interactions between projectile fragments and the target, using a new generation of the code FRESCO optimized and parallelized for these calculations.

## Key numbers
- The ground state of ¹¹Be in the coupled-channel model contains 88.3% s-wave neutron coupled to ¹⁰Be(0⁺) and 11.7% d-wave neutron coupled to ¹⁰Be(2⁺).
- Using a single-particle incoherent sum (SPIS) model, the predicted breakup cross sections are 109 mb to the 0⁺ ground state of ¹⁰Be and 1 mb to the 2⁺ excited state.
- With XCDCC including core excitation consistently, the cross section to the 0⁺ ground state remains 109 mb, but the cross section to the 2⁺ excited state increases to 8 mb (a factor of 8 increase over SPIS).
- The eikonal model prediction (using occupation probabilities consistent with the coupled-channel model) gives 105 mb for the 0⁺ state and 3.4 mb for the 2⁺ state.
- When combining XCDCC breakup with eikonal stripping, the total theoretical cross sections are 200 mb for the 0⁺ state (experimental: 203 ± 31 mb) and 14 mb for the 2⁺ state (experimental: 16 ± 4 mb).
- The eikonal model alone predicted 165 mb for the 0⁺ state and 9 mb for the 2⁺ state, compared to experimental values of 203(31) mb and 16(4) mb respectively.

## Context
This work extends earlier CDCC methods (Nunes 1999) and the spectator core model (Tostevin 1999) by incorporating core excitation consistently throughout the reaction, rather than adding it a posteriori and incoherently. It addresses lingering disagreements in the analysis of ¹¹Be breakup data from Aumann (2000), where eikonal model predictions were too low and corrections from Coulomb and inelastic core excitation had been added incoherently. The XCDCC method enables future studies of reactions such as ¹¹Be(p,p')¹¹Be*, ¹²C(¹¹Be,¹⁰Be+n), and various modes of ⁸B breakup, and suggests that previous spectroscopic analyses and extractions of astrophysical S-factors need to be revisited.
