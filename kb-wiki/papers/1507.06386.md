# Entropy production in the early-cosmology pionic phase

> We point out that in the early universe, for temperatures in the approximate interval 175-80 MeV (after the quark-gluon plasma), pions carried a large share of the entropy and supported the largest inhomogeneities. Thus, we examine the production of entropy in a pion gas, particularizing to inhomogeneities of the temperature, for which we benefit from the known thermal conductivity. We finally put that entropy produced in relaxing such thermal inhomogeneities in the broad context of this relatively unexplored phase of early-universe cosmology.

## Key claim

The authors argue that in the early universe's pionic phase (temperatures roughly 175–80 MeV), pions carried a large share of the entropy and could support the largest inhomogeneities, but they hedge that this analysis assumes thermal inhomogeneities only (ignoring chemical or momentum-distribution departures from equilibrium) and that the existence of such inhomogeneities is hypothetical, the universe may have been perfectly homogeneous.

## Method

The authors solved the Friedmann equation and energy-balance equation numerically for a spatially flat universe containing photons, neutrinos, electrons, muons, and pions, using known particle-physics cross sections and transport coefficients. They then introduced a spherical Gaussian temperature inhomogeneity into the pion gas and solved the heat equation numerically using the thermal conductivity from a variational Chapman-Enskog solution of the Boltzmann equation (Torres-Rincon 2012). Finally, they integrated the entropy-production density σ_s = (κ/T²)|∇δT|² over space and time to obtain the total entropy produced during relaxation of the inhomogeneity.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range of the pionic phase: approximately 175–80 MeV (after the quark-gluon plasma and before pions decay significantly).
- For a single inhomogeneity of size R = 2.5×10⁹ fm with δT₀ = 40 MeV at T_back = 100 MeV, the ratio ΔS_V/S_back = 162.3×10⁻³.
- For the same size and δT₀ at T_back = 130 MeV, ΔS_V/S_back = 56.6×10⁻³.
- For a smaller inhomogeneity of R = 2.5×10² fm with δT₀ = 5 MeV at T_back = 100 MeV, ΔS_V/S_back = 1.9×10⁻³.
- The Hubble horizon during the pion gas was estimated at 10⁻³–10⁻² peV⁻¹, corresponding to a maximum causally connected size of about 10¹⁶–10¹⁷ fm.
- The authors restrict to inhomogeneities no larger than R ≈ 10⁹ fm to ensure they relax while pions are still abundant (T_back ≈ 175–80 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the study of the hadron-lepton epoch of early-universe cosmology (175 MeV to 1 MeV), which has received only moderate attention (Rafelski 2013) because no direct relic messengers survive from that era except the undetectable cosmic neutrino background. The authors apply transport coefficients developed for relativistic heavy-ion collisions (Torres-Rincon 2012, Davesne 1995, Prakash 1993, Mitra 2014) to cosmology, specifically the thermal conductivity of a pion gas. The results provide reference values for entropy production from thermal inhomogeneities that could be used in future precision cosmology studies, though the authors note that the standard assumption of adiabatic expansion and perfect equilibrium is likely valid given that Standard Model reaction rates exceed the Hubble expansion factor.
