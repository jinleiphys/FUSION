# Phases of hot nuclear matter at subnuclear densities

> Structure of hot dense matter at subnuclear densities is investigated by quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations. We analyze nucleon distributions and nuclear shapes using two-point correlation functions and Minkowski functionals to determine the phase-separation line and to classify the phase of nuclear matter in terms of the nuclear structure. Obtained phase diagrams show that the density of the phase boundaries between the different nuclear structures decreases with increasing temperature due to the thermal expansion of nuclear matter region. The critical temperature for the phase separation is $\agt 6$ MeV for the proton fraction $x=0.5$ and $\agt 5$ MeV for $x=0.3$. Our result suggests the existence of "spongelike" phases with negative Euler characteristic in addition to the simple "pasta" phases in supernova cores until $T \alt 3$ MeV.

## Key claim

Using quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations, the authors find that the critical temperature for phase separation in hot subnuclear matter is at least 6 MeV for symmetric nuclear matter (proton fraction x=0.5) and at least 5 MeV for x=0.3, with the caveat that these are lower bounds rather than precise values. The results suggest the existence of "spongelike" phases with negative Euler characteristic in addition to simple "pasta" phases in supernova cores up to temperatures of about 3 MeV.

## Method

The authors performed isothermal QMD simulations of nuclear matter with 2048 nucleons (and up to 16384 nucleons for some cases) in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions, using an effective Hamiltonian developed by Maruyama et al. that includes Skyrme, symmetry, Pauli, momentum-dependent, and Coulomb interactions. They analyzed nucleon distributions and nuclear shapes using two-point correlation functions and Minkowski functionals (integral mean curvature and Euler characteristic) to determine phase-separation lines and classify nuclear structures. Simulations were conducted for proton fractions x=0.5 and x=0.3 across density ranges up to 0.7ρ₀ and temperatures up to 8 MeV, with the system cooled from uniform hot gas at ~20 MeV and then relaxed at each target temperature using a Nosé-Hoover thermostat.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature for phase separation is ≥ 6 MeV for proton fraction x=0.5
- Critical temperature for phase separation is ≥ 5 MeV for proton fraction x=0.3
- "Spongelike" phases exist until T ≤ 3 MeV in supernova cores
- Simulations performed at densities up to 0.7ρ₀ (where ρ₀ = 0.165 fm⁻³) for x=0.5 and up to 0.6ρ₀ for x=0.3
- Temperature intervals between investigated points were 0.5 MeV or 1 MeV; density intervals were 0.025ρ₀ or 0.05ρ₀
- System sizes: 2048 nucleons (with some cases using 16384 nucleons) in cubic boxes with box sizes ranging from 31.42 fm to 82.788 fm depending on density and particle number

## Context

This work extends previous zero-temperature studies of nuclear "pasta" phases (rodlike and slablike nuclei, bubbles) by Ravenhall et al. (1983) and Hashimoto et al. (1984) to finite temperatures relevant to supernova inner cores, where prior work by Lassaut et al. using the Thomas-Fermi approximation had provided some estimates. The QMD approach avoids the limitations of mean-field approximations (Thomas-Fermi, Hartree-Fock) in incorporating thermal fluctuations, and the results are expected to inform understanding of neutrino transport and hydrodynamics in supernova cores, where the periodic spatial structure of nuclear matter can affect neutrino scattering rates and the adiabatic index of the equation of state.
