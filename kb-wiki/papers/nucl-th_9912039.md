# The hot neutron star

> In this paper the equation of state of hot neutron matter is calculated. Involving the Oppenheimer-Volkov-Tolman equation global parameters of a neutron star at the finite temperature are obtained. The objective of our work was to study the influence of the temperature on the main parameters of a neutron star.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the temperature of a neutron star (from 0 to 50 MeV) influences its mass and radius, but the authors explicitly note that the stars compared at different temperatures have different baryon numbers, meaning the results do not represent the thermal evolution of a single star but rather separate static configurations.

## Method

The authors calculate the equation of state for hot neutron matter composed of neutrons, protons, electrons, and muons in beta-equilibrium, using a relativistic mean field model in the Hartree approximation with the L2 parameter set (m_s = 520 MeV, g_s = 10.47). They compute thermodynamic quantities (pressure, energy density, fermion number density) via quantum statistical averages using low-temperature and high-temperature expansions of integrals, then solve the Oppenheimer-Volkoff-Tolman (OVT) equations of hydrostatic equilibrium to obtain mass and radius for central energy densities at fixed temperatures.

## Key numbers

- For T = 50 MeV with interaction (δ ≠ 1): M = 1.3 M⊙, R = 8.00 km
- For T = 50 MeV without interaction (δ = 1): M = 0.62 M⊙, R = 8.08 km
- For T = 0 with interaction (δ ≠ 1): M = 1.24 M⊙, R = 7.98 km
- For T = 0 without interaction (δ = 1): M = 0.7 M⊙, R = 10.54 km
- Temperature range considered: 0 to 50 MeV
- The Higgs field vacuum expectation value used: V = 240 GeV

## Context

This work extends the relativistic mean field approach of Walecka (1974) and Weber (1999) to finite temperatures, and addresses the same problems of hot neutron stars and protoneutron star evolution discussed by Prakash et al. (1997). The paper provides a simplified baseline model for hot neutron star structure that can be extended by including boson fields, hyperons, mesons, or quarks, which the authors state they plan to address in future work.
