# Flow Equations without Mean Field Ambiguity

> We compare different methods used for non-perturbative calculations in strongly interacting fermionic systems. Mean field theory often shows a basic ambiguity related to the possibility to perform Fierz transformations. The results may then depend strongly on an unphysical parameter which reflects the choice of the mean field, thus limiting the reliability. This ambiguity is absent for Schwinger-Dyson equations or fermionic renormalization group equations. Also renormalization group equations in a partially bosonized setting can overcome the Fierz ambiguity if the truncation is chosen appropriately. This is reassuring since the partially bosonized renormalization group approach constitutes a very promising basis for the explicit treatment of condensates and spontaneous symmetry breaking even for situations where the bosonic correlation length is large.

# Flow Equations without Mean Field Ambiguity

## Key claim

The Fierz ambiguity in mean field theory, where results depend on an unphysical parameter γ reflecting the choice of how a four-fermion interaction is distributed among different channels, can be overcome by using fermionic renormalization group equations or by including bosonic fluctuations in a partially bosonized renormalization group approach, provided the truncation is chosen appropriately. The paper demonstrates that the residual γ-dependence in the partially bosonized flow equations is reduced compared to mean field theory and can be eliminated entirely by using k-dependent field redefinitions that make the flow one-loop exact.

## Method

The authors compare mean field theory, perturbation theory, fermionic renormalization group flow equations, and partially bosonized renormalization group flow equations for a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio-type model with a chirally invariant pointlike four-fermion interaction, regularized by a sharp momentum cutoff q² < Λ². They compute the critical coupling for the onset of spontaneous symmetry breaking (a nonzero scalar condensate) as a function of the redundant parameter γ that parametrizes the Fierz transformation, using each method. For the bosonized formulation, they include bosonic fluctuations and apply k-dependent field redefinitions to absorb generated quartic fermion interactions, achieving equivalence to the fermionic flow equation.

## Key numbers

- The mean field theory critical coupling depends linearly on γ: λ̄_σ^crit = 1/(8v₄Λ²) − 2γ λ̄_V, where v₄ = 1/(32π²) and Λ is the ultraviolet cutoff.
- The fermionic renormalization group flow equations yield critical couplings that are independent of γ, with the flow equations given by ∂_t λ̄_σ,k = −8v₄ l₁^(F),4(s) k² (λ̄_σ,k² + 4λ̄_σ,k λ̄_V,k + 3λ̄_V,k²) and ∂_t λ̄_V,k = −4v₄ l₁^(F),4(s) k² (λ̄_σ,k + λ̄_V,k)², where the threshold function l₁^(F),4 = 1/2 for k < Λ.
- The one-loop perturbative corrections to the couplings are Δλ̄_σ = 4v₄Λ² (λ̄_σ² + 4λ̄_σ λ̄_V + 3λ̄_V²) and Δλ̄_V = 2v₄Λ² (λ̄_σ + λ̄_V)², which are independent of γ but cannot produce spontaneous symmetry breaking.
- The Fierz ambiguity in mean field theory is so large for the range 0 ≤ γ ≤ 1 that "no quantitative statement is possible" unless γ can be restricted to a much smaller range, for the parameter values used in Table 2 (not shown in the truncated text).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the Fierz ambiguity in mean field theory previously noted for the Hubbard model by Baier (2000) and for color superconductivity studies by Alford (1997), Bailin, Berges (1998), Schafer (1998), and Oertel (2002). The paper shows that fermionic renormalization group equations (based on the exact renormalization group for the effective average action developed by Wetterich (1993)) are free of the Fierz ambiguity, while partially bosonized approaches (using the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation) require careful treatment of bosonic fluctuations and field redefinitions (as developed by Gies (2002)) to achieve γ-independence. This enables reliable non-perturbative calculations of spontaneous symmetry breaking in strongly interacting fermionic systems, including the computation of condensates and mass gaps beyond mean field theory.
