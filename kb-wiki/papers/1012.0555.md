# Viscous QCD matter in a hybrid hydrodynamic+Boltzmann approach

> A hybrid transport approach for the bulk evolution of viscous QCD matter produced in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions is presented. The expansion of the dense deconfined phase of the reaction is modeled with viscous hydrodynamics while the dilute late hadron gas stage is described microscopically by the Boltzmann equation. The advantages of such a hybrid approach lie in the improved capability of handling large dissipative corrections in the late dilute phase of the reaction, including a realistic treatment of the non-equilibrium hadronic chemistry and kinetic freeze-out. By varying the switching temperature at which the hydrodynamic output is converted to particles for further propagation with the Boltzmann cascade we test the ability of the macroscopic hydrodynamic approach to emulate the microscopic evolution during the hadronic stage and extract the temperature dependence of the effective shear viscosity of the hadron resonance gas produced in the collision. We find that the extracted values depend on the prior hydrodynamic history and hence do not represent fundamental transport properties of the hadron resonance gas. We conclude that viscous fluid dynamics does not provide a faithful description of hadron resonance gas dynamics with predictive power, and that both components of the hybrid approach are needed for a quantitative description of the fireball expansion and its freeze-out.

## Key claim

The hybrid approach (viscous hydrodynamics + Boltzmann cascade) is necessary for quantitative description of fireball expansion and freeze-out because viscous fluid dynamics alone cannot faithfully describe hadron resonance gas dynamics with predictive power; the effective shear viscosity extracted for the hadron gas depends on the prior hydrodynamic history and therefore does not represent a fundamental transport property.

## Method

The authors coupled the (2+1)-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic code VISH2+1 (using Israel-Stewart theory for shear viscosity) with the UrQMD hadronic Boltzmann cascade via a Monte Carlo converter (H2O) that samples the Cooper-Frye phase-space distributions on an isothermal switching surface, including viscous corrections δf. They varied the switching temperature Tsw between the hydrodynamic and cascade stages, using both a partial chemical equilibrium equation of state (s95p-PCE) and a chemical equilibrium equation of state (SM-EOS Q), and compared the resulting hadron spectra and elliptic flow from the hybrid code VISHNU with purely hydrodynamic simulations at various decoupling temperatures and shear viscosity values.

## Key numbers

- For central (b=0) Au+Au collisions, the peak initial energy density was normalized to e0 = 30 GeV/fm³ at τ0 = 0.6 fm/c
- With η/s = 0.08 in the QGP stage and s95p-PCE equation of state, the hybrid model v2 at pT = 2 GeV was suppressed by approximately 10% relative to pure viscous hydrodynamics (compared to ~15% suppression when comparing ideal hydrodynamics to the hybrid model with ideal fluid input)
- Pure viscous hydrodynamics with η/s = 0.08 showed approximately 20% viscous v2 suppression at pT = 2 GeV relative to ideal hydrodynamics
- The chemical freeze-out temperature used in s95p-PCE was Tchem = 165 MeV; the switching temperature for SM-EOS Q was Tsw = 160 MeV
- For pure hydrodynamic runs, the decoupling temperature was Tdec = 100 MeV with s95p-PCE and Tdec = 130 MeV with SM-EOS Q

## Context

This work extends the development of hybrid macro+micro transport approaches that began about ten years earlier with ideal fluid dynamics coupled to hadronic cascades (Bass 2000, Teaney 2000, Hirano 2005, Nonaka 2006). It is the first hybrid model to interface viscous relativistic hydrodynamics with a hadronic Boltzmann cascade, specifically building on the VISH2+1 code (Song 2007, 2008) and the UrQMD model (Bass 1998). The study enables more reliable extraction of QGP transport coefficients by removing uncertainties from non-equilibrium dynamics during the late hadronic stage, and demonstrates that purely hydrodynamic simulations cannot adequately reproduce the microscopic hadronic dynamics even with a partial chemical equilibrium equation of state.
