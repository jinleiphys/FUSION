# Effect of secondary decay on isoscaling: Results from the canonical thermodynamical model

> The projectile fragmentation reactions using $^{58}Ni$ $\&$ $^{64}Ni$ beams at 140 MeV/n on targets$^{9}Be$ $\&$ $^{181}Ta$ are studied using the canonical thermodynamical model coupled with an evaporation code. The isoscaling property of the fragments produced is studied using both the primary and the secondary fragments and it is observed that the secondary fragments also respect isoscaling though the isoscaling parameters $α$ and $β$ changes. The temperature needed to reproduce experimental data with the secondary fragments is less than that needed with the primary ones. The canonical model coupled with the evaporation code successfully explains the experimental data for isoscaling for the projectile fragmentation reactions.

# Effect of secondary decay on isoscaling: Results from the canonical thermodynamical model

## Key claim
Secondary fragments produced after sequential decay from the canonical thermodynamical model still respect isoscaling, but the isoscaling parameters α and β are reduced in magnitude compared to primary fragments, and the temperature needed to reproduce experimental data with secondary fragments (5.8 MeV for Be target) is lower than that needed with primary fragments (about 8 MeV).

## Method
The canonical thermodynamical model (CTM) was used to calculate primary fragment yields from projectile fragmentation of 58Ni and 64Ni beams at 140 MeV/nucleon on 9Be and 181Ta targets, assuming a freeze-out volume of 6V₀. A Monte Carlo evaporation code based on Weisskopf theory was then coupled to CTM to simulate sequential decay of excited primary fragments via emission of nucleons, d, t, ³He, α, γ-rays, and fission, producing secondary fragment distributions for comparison with experimental data from the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory at Michigan State University.

## Key numbers
- Temperature for secondary fragments with 9Be target: 5.8 MeV; temperature for primary fragments: about 8 MeV
- For 9Be target, primary α = 0.713, secondary α = 0.580, experimental α = 0.566
- For 9Be target, primary β = -0.849, secondary β = -0.634, experimental β = -0.621
- For 181Ta target, primary α = 0.619, secondary α = 0.459, experimental α = 0.432
- For 181Ta target, primary β = -0.682, secondary β = -0.489, experimental β = -0.487
- Temperature for 181Ta target reaction: 6.2 MeV
- Dissociating system for 58Ni+9Be: N₀=35, Z₀=32; for 64Ni+9Be: N₀=41, Z₀=32
- Level density parameter used: a = A/16 MeV⁻¹

## Context
This work extends earlier CTM calculations by Chaudhuri and collaborators (2008) by adding a sequential decay code to model secondary fragments, addressing the fact that experimental detection occurs on timescales (~10⁻⁹ s) much longer than nuclear reaction times (~10⁻²⁰ s). The reduction of isoscaling parameters due to secondary decay was previously observed in dynamical models by Ono et al. (2004), though the reduction in the present statistical model is less pronounced. The results enable direct comparison between statistical multifragmentation models and experimental projectile fragmentation data, and confirm that the canonical thermodynamical model can successfully explain isoscaling properties of lighter fragments after accounting for sequential decay.
