# Exotic nuclei far from the stability line

> The recent availability of radioactive beams has opened up a new era in nuclear physics. The interactions and structure of exotic nuclei close to the drip lines have been studied extensively world wide, and it has been revealed that unstable nuclei, having weakly bound nucleons, exhibit characteristic features such as a halo structure and a soft dipole excitation. We here review the developments of the physics of unstable nuclei in the past few decades. The topics discussed in this Chapter include the halo and skin structures, the Coulomb breakup, the dineutron correlation, the pair transfer reactions, the two-nucleon radioactivity, the appearance of new magic numbers, and the pygmy dipole resonances.

## Key claim

This review chapter summarizes the developments in the physics of unstable nuclei over the past few decades, focusing on how weakly bound nucleons in exotic nuclei near the drip lines produce characteristic features such as halo structures and soft dipole excitations, without presenting a single new experimental result or theoretical calculation.

## Method

The authors provide a comprehensive literature review covering halo and skin structures, Coulomb breakup, dineutron correlation, pair transfer reactions, two-nucleon radioactivity, new magic numbers, and pygmy dipole resonances in neutron-rich nuclei. They discuss theoretical frameworks including two-body core-plus-valence-neutron models, three-body models for Borromean nuclei, and the virtual photon theory for Coulomb dissociation, while also summarizing experimental results from radioactive beam facilities worldwide.

## Key numbers

- The two-neutron separation energy of $^{11}$Li was known to be 190$\pm$110 keV in 1985, updated to 378$\pm$5 keV
- The one-neutron separation energy of $^{11}$Be is 504$\pm$6 keV, compared to $S_n$=4.95 MeV for $^{13}$C
- The rms radii for 2s, 1p, and 1d states in $^{11}$Be at $\epsilon=-0.5$ MeV are 7.17 fm, 5.17 fm, and 4.15 fm respectively
- The charge radii of $^6$He and $^8$He are 2.054$\pm$0.014 fm and 1.93$\pm$0.03 fm respectively
- The matter radius of $^6$He from experiment is 2.43$\pm$0.03 fm, with proton radius 1.912$\pm$0.018 fm

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on neutron-rich nuclei that began with the discovery of the anomalously large matter radius of $^{11}$Li in 1985 by Tanihata et al., and the subsequent development of halo physics. It covers developments through approximately 2012, including the operation of new-generation RI beam facilities (RIBF at RIKEN, FAIR at GSI, SPIRAL2 at GANIL, FRIB at MSU) and enables future studies of nuclear structure at the extremes of isospin, particularly regarding the evolution of shell structure, pairing correlations in weakly bound systems, and the nature of the neutron drip line.
