# Fast & rigorous predictions for $A=6$ nuclei with Bayesian posterior sampling

> We make ab initio predictions for the A = 6 nuclear level scheme based on two- and three-nucleon interactions up to next-to-next-to-leading order in chiral effective field theory ($χ$EFT). We utilize eigenvector continuation and Bayesian methods to quantify uncertainties stemming from the many-body method, the $χ$EFT truncation, and the low-energy constants of the nuclear interaction. The construction and validation of emulators is made possible via the development of JupiterNCSM -- a new M-scheme no-core shell model code that uses on-the-fly Hamiltonian matrix construction for efficient, single-node computations up to $N_\mathrm{max} = 10$ for ${}^{6}\mathrm{Li}$. We find a slight underbinding of ${}^{6}\mathrm{He}$ and ${}^{6}\mathrm{Li}$, although consistent with experimental data given our theoretical error bars. As a result of incorporating a correlated $χ$EFT-truncation errors we find more precise predictions (smaller error bars) for separation energies: $S_d({}^{6}\mathrm{Li}) = 0.89 \pm 0.44$ MeV, $S_{2n}({}^{6}\mathrm{He}) = 0.20 \pm 0.60$ MeV, and for the beta decay Q-value: $Q_{β^-}({}^{6}\mathrm{He}) = 3.71 \pm 0.65$ MeV. We conclude that our error bars can potentially be reduced further by extending the model space used by JupiterNCSM.

## Key claim

The authors find a slight underbinding of ${}^{6}\mathrm{He}$ and ${}^{6}\mathrm{Li}$ that is consistent with experimental data given their theoretical error bars, and they achieve more precise predictions for separation energies and beta decay Q-values by incorporating correlated chiral effective field theory truncation errors.

## Method

The authors performed ab initio no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations for A=6 nuclei using two- and three-nucleon interactions up to next-to-next-to-leading order in chiral effective field theory, employing a newly developed M-scheme NCSM code called JupiterNCSM that uses on-the-fly Hamiltonian matrix construction for efficient single-node computations up to Nmax=10 for ${}^{6}\mathrm{Li}$. They constructed eigenvector continuation emulators trained on 16 parameter points to enable rapid evaluation of the many-body problem for approximately 2.5 million samples from the posterior distribution of the three-nucleon force low-energy constants cD and cE, and applied Bayesian statistical models to quantify uncertainties from the many-body method truncation, the chiral effective field theory truncation, and the low-energy constants.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron separation energy for ${}^{6}\mathrm{Li}$: $S_d({}^{6}\mathrm{Li}) = 0.89 \pm 0.44$ MeV
- Two-neutron separation energy for ${}^{6}\mathrm{He}$: $S_{2n}({}^{6}\mathrm{He}) = 0.20 \pm 0.60$ MeV
- Beta decay Q-value for ${}^{6}\mathrm{He}$: $Q_{\beta^-}({}^{6}\mathrm{He}) = 3.71 \pm 0.65$ MeV
- Ground-state energy of ${}^{4}\mathrm{He}$ at NNLO with maximum a posteriori parameters: $-28.16$ MeV (NCSM result) and $-28.16$ MeV (with estimated mean method error correction); experimental value is $-28.296$ MeV from Tilley (1992)
- Ground-state energy of ${}^{6}\mathrm{He}$ at NNLO with maximum a posteriori parameters: $-25.40$ MeV (NCSM result) and $-28.16$ MeV (with estimated mean method error correction); experimental value is $-29.271$ MeV from Wang (2012)
- Ground-state energy of ${}^{6}\mathrm{Li}$ at NNLO with maximum a posteriori parameters: $-29.52$ MeV (NCSM result) and $-31.13$ MeV (with estimated mean method error correction); experimental value is $-31.994$ MeV from Wang (2012)
- The eigenvector continuation emulators were trained on 16 training points and validated against 40 additional points, with the largest model space reaching Nmax=10 for ${}^{4}\mathrm{He}$ and ${}^{6}\mathrm{Li}$, and Nmax=8 for ${}^{6}\mathrm{He}$

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian inference analysis of three-nucleon force low-energy constants performed by Wesolowski (2021) from the few-nucleon sector to A=6 nuclei, and builds upon eigenvector continuation methods developed by Frame (2017) and Konig (2019) for emulating nuclear many-body calculations. The results enable rigorous uncertainty quantification for ab initio predictions of light nuclear systems, with the authors noting that error bars can potentially be reduced further by extending the model space used by JupiterNCSM.
