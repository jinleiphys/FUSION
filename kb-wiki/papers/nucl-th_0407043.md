# Parity-Dependence in the Nuclear Level Density

> Astrophysical reaction rates are sensitive to the parity distribution at low excitation energies. We combine a formula for the energy-dependent parity distribution with a microscopic-macroscopic nuclear level density. This approach describes well the transition from low excitation energies, where a single parity dominates, to high excitations where the two densities are equal.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the assumption of equally distributed state parities at all excitation energies is not valid even at energies of 10–15 MeV, and that a parity-dependent nuclear level density formula is needed for astrophysical reaction rates, especially for nuclei far from stability where no experimental data exist.

## Method

The authors combine a Poisson-distribution-based formula for the energy-dependent parity ratio with a macroscopic-microscopic nuclear level density. Single-particle levels from an axially symmetric deformed Saxon-Woods potential are divided by parity, and BCS occupation numbers are used to compute the average occupancy of the parity group with the smaller occupation number. Canonical thermodynamic relations then yield parity-projected level densities at given excitation energies.

## Key numbers

- For nuclei in the Fe-region, the parity ratio is not yet equilibrated even at excitation energies of 10–15 MeV.
- For ⁷⁰Zn, a pronounced peak in the odd-to-even parity ratio appears around 6 MeV excitation energy.
- Varying the level-density parameter a in the range 8 ≤ a ≤ 12 MeV⁻¹ (a ±20% variation of the standard parameter) changes the total level density by up to a factor of 5 at 6 MeV excitation energy.

## Context

This work extends the parity-distribution approach of Alhassid et al. (1991) by combining it with the microscopic-macroscopic level density of Rauscher et al. (1997). It contradicts the common assumption in global astrophysical rate calculations that parities are equally distributed at all energies. The results enable improved reaction rate predictions for nuclei far from stability, and the authors note that further work is needed to understand peak structures at shell closures and to extend calculations to a large number of proton-rich and neutron-rich nuclei.
