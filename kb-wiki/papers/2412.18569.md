# Non-radial oscillations of hadronic neutron stars, quark stars, and hybrid stars : Calculation of $f$, $p$, and $g$ mode frequencies

> The composition and equation of state (EoS) of dense matter relevant to compact stars are quite inconclusive. However, certain observational constraints on the structural properties of compact stars help us constrain the EoS to a fair extent. Moreover, gravitational asteroseismology gives a notion of the composition and EoS of compact stars. The next generation gravitational wave (GW) detectors are likely to detect several oscillation mode frequencies of the GWs. In this work we compute the fundamental ($f$) and the first pressure ($p_1$) mode frequencies ($f_f$ and $f_{p1}$, respectively) with different compositions viz., hadronic, quark, and hybrid star (HS) matter. For HSs, we also study the gravity ($g$) mode frequency ($f_g$). For each phase we also study the correlation between the oscillation frequencies of 1.4 $M_{\odot}$ and 2.01 $M_{\odot}$ compact stars with other different properties. We find that various possible composition of compact stars substantially affects the oscillation frequencies. However, the mass-scaled angular $f$ mode frequency ($ω_f M$) varies universally with compactness ($C$) for all hadronic, quark and hybrid stars. The $f$ mode frequency ($f_{f_{1.4}}$) of the canonical 1.4 $M_{\odot}$ compact star, obtained with different composition, is quite correlated with the canonical radius ($R_{1.4}$) and tidal deformability ($Λ_{1.4}$) while $f_{p_{1.4}}$ is well correlated with slope parameter of the symmetry energy. We also show that $f_{g_{1.4}}$ of the HSs varies almost linearly with $Λ_{1.4}$. Should $g$ modes be detected, they could not only support the existence of HSs, but $f_g$ could be useful to understand the strength of quark repulsion in HSs.

## Key claim

The authors compute non-radial oscillation frequencies (f, p, and g modes) for hadronic neutron stars, quark stars, and hybrid stars using a variety of equations of state, finding that while the composition substantially affects oscillation frequencies, the mass-scaled angular f mode frequency varies universally with compactness across all star types; however, the g mode is only present for hybrid stars with a density discontinuity, and its detection could support the existence of hybrid stars.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain stellar structure for each equation of state, then compute non-radial oscillation frequencies using the Cowling approximation (neglecting metric perturbations). For hadronic stars they use the KIDS functional (four variants A-D) and two relativistic mean field models (GM1 and DD2); for quark stars they use the vector Bag model, Nambu-Jona-Lasino model, and color-flavor-locked model; for hybrid stars they employ Maxwell construction with KIDS hadronic models and the vector Bag quark model, applying junction conditions at the hadron-quark interface to compute g mode frequencies.

## Key numbers

- KIDS model symmetry energy slope parameter L ranges from 47 MeV (KIDS-D) to 66 MeV (KIDS-A); GM1 has L = 94 MeV, DD2 has L = 55 MeV
- For the vector Bag model, bag constant B^(1/4) ranges from 134-148 MeV for G_V = 0.5 fm^2 and 138-148 MeV for G_V = 0.3 fm^2
- For the CFL model, gap parameter Delta = 50 MeV with B = 75 MeV fm^{-3} (CFL 1) and Delta = 100 MeV with B = 60 MeV fm^{-3} (CFL 2)
- The f mode frequency of a 1.4 solar mass canonical star (f_{f_{1.4}}) correlates with canonical radius (R_{1.4}) and tidal deformability (Lambda_{1.4})
- The p_1 mode frequency of a 1.4 solar mass star (f_{p_{1.4}}) correlates with the slope parameter of the symmetry energy
- The g mode frequency of a 1.4 solar mass hybrid star (f_{g_{1.4}}) varies almost linearly with Lambda_{1.4}

## Context

This work extends prior studies of non-radial oscillations by Andersson (1997), Sotani (2010), and Flores (2013) by systematically comparing hadronic, quark, and hybrid star compositions using a broader set of modern equations of state, including the KIDS functional not previously applied to oscillation studies. The universal relation between mass-scaled f mode frequency and compactness confirms findings by Zhao (2022) and others, while the correlation between g mode frequency and tidal deformability for hybrid stars provides a potential observational signature for next-generation gravitational wave detectors such as the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer.
