# How the Pauli exclusion principle affects fusion of atomic nuclei

> The Pauli exclusion principle induces a repulsion between composite systems of identical fermions such as colliding atomic nuclei. Our goal is to study how heavy-ion fusion is impacted by this "Pauli repulsion". We propose a new microscopic approach, the density-constrained frozen Hartree-Fock method, to compute the bare potential including the Pauli exclusion principle exactly. Pauli repulsion is shown to be important inside the barrier radius and increases with the charge product of the nuclei. Its main effect is to reduce tunnelling probability. Pauli repulsion is part of the solution to the long-standing deep sub-barrier fusion hindrance problem.

## Key claim

The Pauli exclusion principle induces a repulsion between overlapping nuclei that is important inside the barrier radius and increases with the charge product of the nuclei; this "Pauli repulsion" provides a natural but only partial explanation for the experimentally observed deep sub-barrier fusion hindrance, contrary to the common assumption that nuclei do not probe the inner part of the fusion barrier enough for Pauli effects to matter.

## Method

The authors introduce the density-constrained frozen Hartree-Fock (DCFHF) method, which computes the bare nucleus-nucleus potential by performing a Hartree-Fock minimization of the combined system subject to the constraint that the local proton and neutron densities remain frozen to their ground-state values. This approach accounts exactly for the Pauli exclusion principle between nucleons of different nuclei while keeping the densities fixed, unlike the standard frozen Hartree-Fock (FHF) method which neglects Pauli repulsion. The resulting bare potentials are then used in coupled-channels calculations of fusion cross-sections for the doubly-magic systems 40Ca+40Ca, 48Ca+48Ca, and 16O+208Pb, using the Skyrme SLy4d energy density functional.

## Key numbers

- For 40Ca+40Ca, 48Ca+48Ca, and 16O+208Pb, the DCFHF potential shows a repulsive effect inside the barrier radius that increases with charge product Z1Z2, producing a potential pocket at short distance that becomes shallower with increasing Z1Z2.
- The DCFHF potential leads to fusion cross-sections that agree with experimental data over eight orders of magnitude, while FHF calculations systematically overestimate the data.
- The Skyrme SLy4d functional used has a compression modulus of symmetric nuclear matter K∞ ≈ 230 MeV.
- The box size for all calculations was 60 × 30 × 30 fm³ with a mesh spacing of 1.0 fm in all directions.
- Couplings to low-lying collective 2⁺ and/or 3⁻ states were included, with the barrier energy VB lowered by less than 5% to account for couplings in logarithmic slope calculations.

## Context

This work extends the density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock approach of Umar et al. (2006) to static bare potentials, and addresses the long-standing deep sub-barrier fusion hindrance problem first identified by Jiang et al. (2002) and Dasgupta et al. (2007). The DCFHF method provides a microscopic alternative to phenomenological approaches such as the M3Y+rep potential of Misicu and Esbensen (2006), which the authors argue simulates Pauli repulsion rather than incompressibility effects. The results enable future inclusion of dissipative effects and the transition to the adiabatic potential (as discussed by Ichikawa et al., 2009) to fully explain deep sub-barrier fusion data.
