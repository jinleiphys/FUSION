# Nuclear masses: evidence of order-chaos coexistence

> Shell corrections are important in the determination of nuclear ground-state masses and shapes. Although general arguments favor a regular single-particle dynamics, symmetry-breaking and the presence of chaotic layers cannot be excluded. The latter provide a natural framework that explains the observed differences between experimental and computed masses.

# Nuclear masses: evidence of order-chaos coexistence

## Key claim

The observed differences between experimental nuclear masses and computed masses (including shell corrections) can be explained by the presence of chaotic layers in the single-particle dynamics, which produce additional shell corrections that are not captured by standard mean-field calculations. The authors argue that the shell correction energy can be decomposed into a regular component (reproduced by current models) and a chaotic component (responsible for the residual discrepancies), with the chaotic contribution being independent of the fraction of phase space occupied by chaotic motion.

## Method

The authors use periodic orbit theory (semiclassical trace formula) to compute the variance of shell corrections to nuclear ground-state masses, splitting the sum over periodic orbits into contributions from regular and chaotic components. They evaluate the energy variance using the diagonal approximation and the known form factors for regular dynamics (constant form factor) and chaotic dynamics (linear form factor), with the shortest periodic orbit period as the only physical parameter. The resulting expressions are compared to the root-mean-square deviations between experimental masses and computed masses from the macroscopic-microscopic calculations of Moller, Nix, Myers, and Swiatecki (1995).

## Key numbers

* RMS of regular shell correction fluctuations: 2.84 MeV, independent of mass number A, in agreement with the typical size of the difference between experimental masses and the bulk (macroscopic) contribution.
* RMS of chaotic shell correction fluctuations: 2.78 / A^(1/3) MeV, giving approximately 0.5 MeV for medium-heavy nuclei, in agreement with the typical size of the difference between experimental and computed masses including shell effects.
* Energy associated with the shortest periodic orbit: E_c = 77.5 / A^(1/3) MeV, derived assuming the shortest orbit length is two nuclear diameters in a Woods-Saxon potential.
* Number of single-particle states on the scale E_c: g = pi A^(2/3).

## Context

This work extends the Strutinsky (1967) method for incorporating shell corrections into nuclear mass calculations, and the macroscopic-microscopic mass calculations of Moller et al. (1995) and Aboussir et al. (1995). It provides a dynamical explanation for the model-independent residuals between experimental and computed nuclear masses, attributing them to chaotic components of the single-particle motion that coexist with the regular dynamics that dominate shell effects. The theory predicts autocorrelations in total energies and effects on the total level density that deserve further investigation, and suggests that residual interactions may induce chaotic motion in the many-body phase space.
