# Quark-Parton Phase Transitions and the Entropy Problem in Quantum Mechanics

> Since Feynman proposed his parton model in 1969, one of the most pressing problems in high-energy physics has been whether partons are quarks. It is shown that the quark model and the parton model are two different manifestations of one covariant entity. The nature of transition from the confined quarks to plasma-like partons is studied in terms of the entropy and temperature coming from the time-separation variable. According to Einstein, the time-separation variable exists wherever there is a spatial separation, but it is not observed in the present form of quantum mechanics. It belongs to Feynman's rest of the universe.

# Quark-Parton Phase Transitions and the Entropy Problem in Quantum Mechanics

## Key claim
The quark model and the parton model are two different manifestations of one covariant entity, with the transition between confined quarks and plasma-like partons governed by entropy and temperature arising from the unobserved time-separation variable between quarks. The failure to measure this time-separation variable, which belongs to Feynman's "rest of the universe," causes an increase in entropy that defines the phase transition.

## Method
The author constructs a covariant harmonic oscillator wave function for a two-quark hadron that can be Lorentz-boosted, fixing mathematical deficiencies in the earlier Feynman-Kislinger-Ravndal (1971) oscillator model. The ground-state wave function in light-cone coordinates is subjected to Lorentz boosts, producing elliptic (squeezed) deformations in both space-time and momentum-energy representations. The time-separation variable is then treated as an unobserved degree of freedom; a density matrix is constructed by integrating over this variable, and the resulting entropy is calculated and compared to the entropy of thermally excited harmonic oscillators.

## Key numbers
- The entropy from the unobserved time-separation variable is given by: S = 2 {(cosh²η) ln(cosh η) - (sinh²η) ln(sinh η)}, where η is the boost parameter (tanh⁻¹(v/c)).
- The relation between boost and temperature is: tanh²(η) = exp(-ħω/kT), establishing an equivalence between the boost parameter and thermal excitation.
- The entropy takes the form: S = (ħω/kT) / [exp(ħω/kT) - 1] - ln[1 - exp(-ħω/kT)].

## Context
This work combines three strands of Feynman's research: the parton model (Feynman, 1969), the covariant oscillator model for hadrons (Feynman, Kislinger, and Ravndal, 1971), and Feynman's concept of the "rest of the universe" from statistical mechanics (Feynman, 1972). It extends the covariant harmonic oscillator formalism developed within Wigner's little group framework (Wigner, 1939; Kim and Noz, 1986) and builds on earlier entropy calculations for two coupled oscillators (Han, Kim, and Noz, 1999). The work enables a thermodynamic interpretation of the transition between the confined quark phase and the parton plasma phase, with Hussar's (1981) calculation showing that the Lorentz-boosted oscillator wave function produces a reasonably accurate parton distribution compared to experimental data.
