# Relativistic meson spectroscopy

> In this paper a relativistic quantum theory introduced by de Groot and \mbox{Ruijgrok} in 1975, is used as a quark model in momentum space. The complete spectrum, with the exception of the selfconjugate light unflavoured mesons, is calculated. The potential used consists of an one-gluon-exchange (OGE) part and a confining part. For the confining part a relativistic generalization of the linear plus constant potential was used, which is well-defined in momentum space without introducing any singularities. For the OGE part several potentials were investigated. Retardations were included at all places. Using a fitting procedure involving 52 well-established mesons, best results were obtained for a potential consisting of a purely vector Richardson potential and a purely scalar confining potential. In this way a vector confining is entirely induced by the Richardson potential. Reasonable results were also obtained for a modified Richardson potential. Most meson masses, with the exception of the $π$, the $K$ and the $K_0^*$, were found to be reasonably well described by the model.

## Key claim

Using a relativistic quantum theory originally introduced by de Groot and Ruijgrok (1975) as a quark model in momentum space, the author finds that a purely vector Richardson potential combined with a purely scalar confining potential gives the best fit to 52 well-established meson masses, though the pion, kaon, and K0* are exceptions that are not well described by the model.

## Method

The author solves a relativistic wave equation in momentum space that conserves three-velocity rather than four-momentum, with all particles remaining on their mass shell and negative energy states excluded. The potential includes a one-gluon-exchange (OGE) part, for which both the Richardson potential and a modified Richardson potential are tested, and a confining part consisting of a relativistic generalization of a linear plus constant potential that is well-defined in momentum space without singularities. Retardation effects are included everywhere via the replacement of the nonrelativistic momentum transfer with a covariant expression. The eigenvalue equation is solved numerically by expanding the wave function into cubic Hermite splines, and a nonlinear regression method based on the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm is used to fit the model parameters to experimental meson masses.

## Key numbers

- Best fit (case Ia) achieved with Richardson potential parameter α0 = 1.75 (dimensionless), giving χ² ≈ 260 for 52 mesons.
- A fit with α0 varied and both ε and string tension λ set to zero (case Ib) gave χ² = 250.
- The Regge slope β for linear trajectories in the ultrarelativistic limit is related to the vector string tension λV by β ≈ (8√2)λV, with experimental β ≈ 1.2 (GeV)².
- To obtain reasonable Regge slopes, the string tension in the vector direction λV should be around 0.1 GeV².
- The grid size S used as minimum weight for meson masses was 20 MeV for most mesons, and 10 MeV for bottonium (b overline b).
- For the pion, kaon, and K0*, the weights were set to σπ = 0.4 GeV, σK = 0.2 GeV, and σK0* = 0.2 GeV to give them insignificant weight in the fit.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic quark model approach of de Groot and Ruijgrok (1975) and earlier work by the same author (Hersbach, 1992) to the full meson spectrum, excluding selfconjugate light unflavoured mesons. It contrasts with nonrelativistic quark models that work well for heavy charmonia and bottonia but require relativistic corrections for lighter mesons, and with Bethe-Salpeter approaches where different three-dimensional quasi-potential reductions lead to different retardations. The results enable further study of meson spectroscopy within a manifestly Lorentz covariant framework in momentum space, particularly for understanding linear Regge trajectories and the role of retardation effects.
