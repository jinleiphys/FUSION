# Electroweak decay of quark matter within dense astrophysical combustion flames

> We study the weak interaction processes taking place within a combustion flame that converts dense hadronic matter into quark matter in a compact star. Using the Boltzmann equation we follow the evolution of a small element of just deconfined quark matter all along the flame interior until it reaches chemical equilibrium at the back boundary of the flame. We obtain the reaction rates and neutrino emissivities of all the relevant weak interaction processes without making any assumption about the neutrino degeneracy. We analyse systematically the role the initial conditions of unburnt hadronic matter, such as density, temperature, neutrino trapping and composition, focusing on typical astrophysical scenarios such as cold neutron stars, protoneutron stars, and post merger compact objects. We find that the temperature within the flame rises significantly in a timescale of 1 nanosecond. The increase in $T$ strongly depends on the initial strangeness of hadronic matter and tends to be more drastic at larger densities. Typical final values range between $20$ and $60 \, \mathrm{MeV}$. The nonleptonic process $u + d \rightarrow u + s$ is always dominant in cold stars, but in hot objects the process $u + e^{-} \leftrightarrow d + {ν_e}$ becomes relevant, and in some cases dominant, near chemical equilibrium. The rates for the other processes are orders of magnitude smaller. We find that the neutrino emissivity per baryon is very large, leading to a total energy release per baryon of $2-60 \, \mathrm{MeV}$ in the form of neutrinos along the flame. We discuss some astrophysical consequences of the results.

## Key claim

The temperature within a quark-matter combustion flame rises significantly on a timescale of about 1 nanosecond, with final values between 20 and 60 MeV, but this increase strongly depends on the initial strangeness of the hadronic matter and tends to be more drastic at larger densities; the nonleptonic process u + d → u + s is always dominant in cold stars, while in hot objects the process u + e⁻ ↔ d + νₑ becomes relevant and can dominate near chemical equilibrium.

## Method

The authors used the Boltzmann equation to follow the time evolution of a small fluid element of just-deconfined quark matter through the interior of a combustion flame, from the deconfinement zone until it reaches chemical equilibrium at the back boundary. They calculated reaction rates and neutrino emissivities for all relevant weak interaction processes (d → u + e⁻ + ν̅ₑ, s → u + e⁻ + ν̅ₑ, u + e⁻ ↔ d + νₑ, u + e⁻ ↔ s + νₑ, and u + d ↔ u + s) without assuming neutrino degeneracy, using the MIT bag model with QCD corrections for the quark matter equation of state. They systematically varied initial conditions including baryon density (0.32 and 0.96 fm⁻³), strangeness parameter η (0 and 0.4), and neutrino trapping parameter κ (0 and 0.01) to cover scenarios of cold neutron stars, protoneutron stars, and post-merger compact objects.

## Key numbers

* Temperature within the flame rises to final values between 20 and 60 MeV, with the increase occurring on a timescale of 1 nanosecond.
* The total energy release per baryon in the form of neutrinos along the flame is 2–60 MeV.
* Baryon number densities studied: 0.32 fm⁻³ and 0.96 fm⁻³.
* The nonleptonic process u + d → u + s is always dominant in cold stars; the process u + e⁻ ↔ d + νₑ becomes relevant and in some cases dominant near chemical equilibrium in hot objects.
* The rates for other weak processes are orders of magnitude smaller than the dominant ones.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of quark matter conversion in compact stars, such as those by Dai (1995) and Anand (1997), which calculated reaction rates and neutrino emissivities only for the limiting cases of cold deleptonized matter or hot neutrino-rich matter with degenerate neutrinos. The present paper generalizes these results by computing rates without assuming neutrino degeneracy, enabling more accurate modeling of the full range of conditions within a combustion flame. The results enable future studies of the flame structure and neutrino signals from phase transitions in cold neutron stars, protoneutron stars, and binary neutron star merger remnants, and can be used to assess observable astrophysical signatures of quark matter formation.
