# The nucleon thermal width due to pion-baryon loops and its contribution in Shear viscosity

> In the real-time thermal field theory, the standard expression of shear viscosity for the nucleonic constituents is derived from the two point function of nucleonic viscous stress tensors at finite temperature and density. The finite thermal width or Landau damping is traditionally included in the nucleon propagators. This thermal width is calculated from the in-medium self-energy of nucleon for different possible pion-baryon loops. The dynamical part of nucleon-pion-baryon interactions are taken care by the effective Lagrangian densities of standard hadronic model. The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio of nucleonic component decreases with the temperature and increases with the nucleon chemical potential. However, adding the contribution of pionic component, total viscosity to entropy density ratio also reduces with the nucleon chemical potential when the mixing effect between pion and nucleon components in the mixed gas is considered. Within the hadronic domain, viscosity to entropy density ratio of the nuclear matter is gradually reducing as temperature and nucleon chemical potential are growing up and therefore the nuclear matter is approaching toward the (nearly) perfect fluid nature.

# The nucleon thermal width due to pion-baryon loops and its contribution in Shear viscosity

## Key claim

The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio of nuclear matter decreases with both increasing temperature and increasing nucleon chemical potential when the mixing effect between pion and nucleon components in the mixed gas is considered, causing the nuclear matter to approach a nearly perfect fluid; however, the nucleonic component alone shows an increase in this ratio with nucleon chemical potential, and the total behavior depends on including the pionic contribution and the mixing correction.

## Method

The nucleon thermal width (Landau damping) was calculated from the imaginary part of the in-medium nucleon self-energy at finite temperature and density, considering all possible pion-baryon loops for 4-star baryon resonances with spin 1/2 and 3/2. The effective Lagrangian densities for BNπ interactions were used to determine the coupling constants, which were fixed from experimental vacuum decay widths in the Nπ channel. The shear viscosity for nucleonic constituents was then derived from the Kubo relation in real-time thermal field theory, using the one-loop expression where the finite thermal width is included in the nucleon propagators to remove divergences and incorporate interactions.

## Key numbers

- The total decay width of Δ(1232) is 0.117 GeV, with 100% branching ratio to Nπ and coupling constant f/mπ = 15.7
- The total decay width of N*(1440) is 0.300 GeV, with 65% branching ratio to Nπ (0.195 GeV) and coupling constant f/mπ = 2.5
- The total decay width of N*(1520) is 0.115 GeV, with 60% branching ratio to Nπ (0.069 GeV) and coupling constant f/mπ = 11.6
- The nucleon thermal width ΓN produces ηN values in the range 0.0001–0.01 GeV³ for strongly interacting matter
- The relevant temperature region for baryon-free nuclear matter (μN = 0) is T > 0.120 GeV up to Tc ≈ 0.175 GeV, where the nucleon mean free path λN is smaller than the typical medium dimension of 10–40 fm
- For finite baryon chemical potential (μN = 0.7 GeV), the relevant temperature region shifts slightly toward lower temperatures

## Context

This work extends the microscopic calculations of shear viscosity in hadronic matter to finite baryon chemical potential, motivated by the sensitivity of elliptic flow v2 to temperature-dependent η/s observed by Niemi et al. and the need to understand behavior at future FAIR experiments. It addresses the conflicting results between Itakura et al. and Denicol et al. (who found η/s reduced at finite μN) versus Gorenstein et al. (who observed increasing η/s with μN). The calculation uses the same Kubo approach as Hosoya, Nicola, and Weise but incorporates the mixing effect between pion and nucleon components following the method of Itakura et al., enabling a more complete description of nuclear matter viscosity at finite temperature and density.
