# Role of nonlocal probes of thermalization for a strongly interacting non-Abelian plasma

> We use a holographic method to investigate thermalization of a boost-invariant strongly interacting non-Abelian plasma. Boundary sourcing, a distorsion of the boundary metric, is employed to drive the system far from equilibrium. Thermalization is analyzed through nonlocal probes: the equal-time two-point correlation function of large conformal dimension operators in the boundary theory, and Wilson loops of different shapes. We study the dependence of the thermalization time on the size of the probes, and compare the results to the ones obtained using local observables: the onset of thermalization is first observed at short distances.

## Key claim

The thermalization time of a strongly interacting non-Abelian plasma, as measured by nonlocal probes (equal-time two-point correlation functions and Wilson loops), depends on the size of the probe, with the onset of thermalization first observed at short distances; this dependence is compared to the results obtained using local observables (energy density and pressures), which show a single thermalization time for each quench model.

## Method

The authors solve the 5-dimensional Einstein equations with a negative cosmological constant in Eddington-Finkelstein coordinates, imposing boundary sourcing (a distortion of the boundary metric) to drive the system out of equilibrium. Two quench models are considered: model A(2) with two short pulses and model B with a slow deformation plus a short pulse. The metric functions are computed numerically, and nonlocal probes are evaluated using the geodesic approximation for two-point correlation functions of large conformal dimension operators and the Nambu-Goto action for circular and rectangular Wilson loops, with the results compared to the hydrodynamic metric that reproduces the viscous hydrodynamics behavior.

## Key numbers

* Model B: thermalization time from local probes is tau_p^B = 6.74, with a delay tau_p^B - tau_f^B = 1.74 after the quench ends at tau_f^B = 5; in physical units (T_eff = 500 MeV), this delay corresponds to 0.42 fm/c.
* Model A(2): thermalization time from local probes is tau_p^A = 6, with a delay tau_p^A - tau_f^A = 2.75 after the quench ends at tau_f^A = 3.25; in physical units (T_eff = 500 MeV), this delay corresponds to 1.03 fm/c.
* The parameter Lambda for the hydrodynamic expansion is Lambda^B = 1.12 for model B and Lambda^A = 1.73 for model A(2).
* The quench in model A(2) uses parameters c_1 = 1, Delta_1 = 1, tau_{0,1} = 5/4, c_2 = 2, Delta_2 = 1, tau_{0,2} = 9/4, with the quench ending at tau_f^A = 3.25.
* The quench in model B uses parameters w = 2/5, eta = 1.2, tau_0 = 0.25, c_1 = 1, Delta_1 = 1, tau_{0,1} = 4, with the pulse ending at tau_f^B = 5.
* Both quenches are switched on at tau_i = 0.25.

## Context

This work extends the boundary sourcing method of Chesler and Yaffe (2008, 2009) and the local probe analysis of Bellantuono et al. (2015) to nonlocal observables, providing a more complete picture of thermalization in strongly coupled plasmas. The study complements earlier holographic investigations of nonlocal probes in Vaidya geometries by Balasubramanian et al. (2010, 2011) and in Reissner-Nordstrom-AdS black holes by Galante et al. (2012), but focuses on the onset of hydrodynamic behavior rather than black hole formation. The results enable comparison between thermalization times extracted from different observables, which is relevant for understanding the early-time dynamics of quark-gluon plasma produced in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC.
