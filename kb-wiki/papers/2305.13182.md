# Inconsistencies in, and short pathlength correction to, $R_{AA}(p_T)$ in $\mathrm{A}+\mathrm{A}$ and $\mathrm{p} + \mathrm{A}$ collisions

> We present the first leading hadron suppression predictions in $\mathrm{Pb}+\mathrm{Pb}$ and $\mathrm{p}+\mathrm{Pb}$ collisions from a convolved radiative and collisional energy loss model in which partons propagate through a realistic background and in which the inelastic energy loss receives a short pathlength correction. We find that the short pathlength correction is small for $D$ and $B$ meson $R_{AA}(p_T)$ in both $\mathrm{Pb}+\mathrm{Pb}$ and $\mathrm{p}+\mathrm{Pb}$ collisions. However the short pathlength correction leads to a surprisingly large reduction in suppression for $π$ mesons in $\mathrm{p}+\mathrm{Pb}$ and even $\mathrm{Pb}+\mathrm{Pb}$ collisions. We systematically check the consistency of the assumptions used in the radiative energy loss derivation - such as collinearity, softness, and large formation time - with the final numerical model. While collinearity and softness are self-consistently satisfied in the final numerics, we find that the large formation time approximation breaks down at modest to high momenta $p_T \gtrsim 30$ GeV. We find that both the size of the small pathlength correction to $R_{AA}(p_T)$ and the $p_T$ at which the large formation time assumption breaks down are acutely sensitive to the chosen distribution of scattering centers in the plasma.

## Key claim

The short pathlength correction to radiative energy loss reduces suppression for pions in both Pb+Pb and p+Pb collisions, but the large formation time approximation used in the derivation breaks down for parton energies above approximately 30 GeV, meaning the model's predictions at higher momenta may be unreliable.

## Method

The authors implemented a convolved radiative and collisional energy loss model (WHDG framework) with the short pathlength correction to the DGLV opacity expansion radiative energy loss derived by Kolbe and Horowitz (2015). They computed the nuclear modification factor R_AA(p_T) for pions, D mesons, and B mesons in Pb+Pb and p+Pb collisions using an exponential distribution of scattering centers, and systematically checked the consistency of the collinearity, softness, and large formation time assumptions used in the radiative energy loss derivation against the final numerical results.

## Key numbers

- The large formation time approximation breaks down at p_T greater than or approximately equal to 30 GeV for large collision systems
- The large formation time approximation breaks down at p_T greater than or approximately equal to 10 GeV for small collision systems
- The relative size of the short pathlength correction to DGLV radiative energy loss scales linearly with energy E, is 9/4 times larger for gluons than for quarks (from the Casimir ratio C_A/C_F), and is approximately 15 times larger for a system with Debye mass mu = 0.5 GeV and length L = 1 fm compared to L = 5 fm
- The asymptotic fractional energy loss for the short pathlength corrected DGLV is given by (C_R alpha_s)/(2 pi) (L/lambda_g) (-2 C_R/C_A) ln(2 E L/(2+mu L))/(2+mu L)
- The asymptotic fractional energy loss for uncorrected DGLV is given by (C_R alpha_s)/4 (L^2 mu^2/lambda_g) (1/E) log(E/mu)
- Charm quark mass set to 1.2 GeV/c^2, bottom quark mass set to 4.75 GeV/c^2
- Strong coupling constant alpha_s = 0.3, Debye mass mu = 0.5 GeV, gluon mean free path lambda_g = 1 fm

## Context

This work extends the WHDG convolved radiative and collisional energy loss model (Wicks, Horowitz, Djordjevic, Gyulassy, 2005) by incorporating the short pathlength correction to DGLV radiative energy loss derived by Kolbe and Horowitz (2015). The results show that the short pathlength correction is small for heavy flavor mesons but surprisingly large for pions, and that the large formation time assumption breaks down at modest to high momenta, which implies the need for a new derivation of radiative energy loss in the thin plasma limit with the large formation time assumption relaxed. The authors also note that the usual WHDG treatment of elastic energy loss using the fluctuation-dissipation theorem is not appropriate for small collision systems where the number of scatterings is not large.
