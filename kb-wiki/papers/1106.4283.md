# A direct microscopic approach to transition strengths in pre-equilibrium reactions

> We present a microscopic formalism that extends the traditional formulation of Williams, Ericson and Bloch and permits to obtain the transition strengths (TS) of pre-equilibrium nuclear reactions directly from their quantum microscopic description. We calculate the TS without resorting to the Laplace transform approach and the use of the saddle point approximation. We also analyze some problems that may appear in connection with these mathematical tools and the Darwin-Fowler approach in this case. We show that, analogously to the nuclear densities, the strengths for transitions that change the exciton number by two or leave it unchanged can be estimated microscopically as convolutions of the functions of simpler states. When using the HO basis for the Model Space we obtained important departure from the results of the exciton model (EXM), which can partially invalidate our previous analysis on the attainment of equilibrium during the PE stage. On the other hand, by using constant grid of energies for the sp-basis we were able to reproduce the results of EXM quite well in a large range of excitation energies. A new model code, TRANSNU, was developed that can be ported to traditional semi-classical codes like TNG for nuclear data evaluation.

## Key claim

The authors present a microscopic formalism for pre-equilibrium nuclear reaction transition strengths that avoids the Laplace transform and saddle-point approximation used in the traditional Williams-Ericson-Bloch approach, but they show that the Darwin-Fowler saddle-point method is only approximately valid for fermionic systems and can become "totally incorrect" when the auxiliary variable x has argument equal to π.

## Method

The authors derive transition strengths for exciton-number-changing (Δn = ±2) and exciton-number-conserving (Δn = 0) transitions directly from the quantum microscopic description of the nuclear shell model, expressing them as convolutions of single-particle state densities with nuclear degeneracies. They test two basis choices: a harmonic oscillator (HO) basis for the model space and a constant energy grid for the single-particle basis, implementing the formalism in a new code called TRANSNU that can be coupled with semi-classical codes like TNG.

## Key numbers

- For the simplified generating function with 20 single-particle states and arg(x)=0, the maxima along the real axis are less than 10 times greater than other points along the corresponding circumferences for |y| ≤ 0.7
- For arg(x)=π with 20 single-particle states, the maxima along the real axis "have completely disappeared and they have become minima instead"
- The single-particle state energies used as examples range from 4.094 MeV to 26.316 MeV
- Nuclear configuration energies for 3-exciton states are shown ranging from 16.38 MeV to 22.81 MeV

## Context

This work extends the traditional exciton model formalism of Williams, Ericson, and Bloch by providing a direct microscopic derivation of transition strengths without the mathematical approximations (Laplace transform, saddle-point method) that those earlier approaches relied upon. The authors show that when using a harmonic oscillator basis, their results depart significantly from the standard exciton model (EXM), potentially invalidating previous analyses of equilibrium attainment during the pre-equilibrium stage, while a constant energy grid reproduces EXM results well. The new TRANSNU code enables microscopic input for traditional semi-classical evaluation codes like TNG.
