# Gravitational wave asteroseismology with protoneutron stars

> We examine the time evolution of the frequencies of the gravitational wave after the bounce within the framework of relativistic linear perturbation theory using the results of one dimensional numerical simulations of core-collapse supernovae. Protoneutron star models are constructed in such a way that the mass and radius of protoneutron star become equivalent to the results obtained from the numerical simulations. Then, we find that the frequencies of gravitational waves radiating from protoneutron stars strongly depend on the mass and radius of protoneutron stars, but almost independently of the profiles of electron fraction and entropy per baryon inside the star. Additionally, we find that the frequencies of gravitational waves can be characterized by the square root of the average density of protoneutron star irrespectively the progenitor models, which are completely different from the empirical formula for cold neutron stars. The dependence of the spectra on the mass and radius is different from that of the $g$-mode: the oscillations around the surface of protoneutron stars due to the convection and the standing accretion-shock instability. Careful observations of the these modes of gravitational waves can determine the evolution of the mass and radius of protoneutron stars after core-bounce. Furthermore, the expected frequencies of gravitational waves are around a few hundred hertz in the early stage after bounce, which must be a good candidate for the ground-based gravitational wave detectors.

## Key claim
The frequencies of gravitational waves from protoneutron stars (PNSs) after core-bounce depend strongly on the PNS mass and radius but are almost independent of the internal profiles of electron fraction and entropy per baryon, and can be characterized by the square root of the average density of the PNS, which is a different scaling from the empirical formula for cold neutron stars.

## Method
The authors performed one-dimensional (1D) numerical simulations of core-collapse supernovae using a neutrino radiation hydrodynamics code with the LS220 and Shen equations of state, tracking the evolution of PNS mass and radius after bounce. They constructed hydrostatic PNS models at each time step by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation with fitted mass and radius from the simulations, using simplified distributions of electron fraction and entropy per baryon. The oscillation frequencies of the PNS were then computed via relativistic linear perturbation theory in the Cowling approximation (fixed metric) by solving eigenvalue problems for polar-type oscillations.

## Key numbers
- For the LS220 EOS with a 15 solar mass progenitor, the initial PNS radius \(R_{\rm i}\) is 52.1 km and the final radius \(R_{\rm f}\) is 20.1 km, with a characteristic evolution timescale \(\tau = 366\) msec.
- The fitting parameters for the mass evolution of the same model are \(c_0 = -22.86\) msec, \(c_1 = 1.920 \times 10^{-4}\) msec\(^{-1}\), and \(c_2 = 1.517\).
- The \(f\)-mode frequencies of the PNS are around a few hundred hertz in the early stage after bounce.
- The \(f\)-mode frequencies for PNSs deviate from the cold neutron star empirical formula \(f_f^{\rm (NS)} ({\rm kHz}) \approx 0.78 + 1.635 (M/1.4M_\odot)^{1/2} (R/10\ {\rm km})^{-3/2}\), especially at low average density.

## Context
This work extends the asteroseismological approach from cold neutron stars (e.g., Andersson and Kokkotas 1998) to protoneutron stars, which have different internal structure due to non-beta-equilibrium conditions with finite entropy and electron fraction. The results contradict the expectation that PNS oscillation frequencies follow the same scaling as cold neutron stars, and instead show a universal scaling with the square root of average density independent of progenitor mass. This enables the possibility of determining the time evolution of PNS mass and radius from future gravitational wave observations by ground-based detectors such as LIGO, VIRGO, and KAGRA, complementing direct numerical simulations of core-collapse supernovae (e.g., Mueller, Janka, and Marek 2013; Yakunin et al. 2016).
