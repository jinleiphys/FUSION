# Indirect methods in nuclear astrophysics with relativistic radioactive beams

> Reactions with radioactive nuclear beams at relativistic energies have opened new doors to clarify the mechanisms of stellar evolution and cataclysmic events involving stars and during the big bang epoch. Numerous nuclear reactions of astrophysical interest cannot be assessed directly in laboratory experiments. Ironically, some of the information needed to describe such reactions, at extremely low energies (e.g., keVs), can only be studied on Earth by using relativistic collisions between heavy ions at GeV energies. In this contribution, we make a short review of experiments with relativistic radioactive beams and of the theoretical methods needed to understand the physics of stars, adding to the knowledge inferred from astronomical observations. We continue by introducing a more detailed description of how the use of relativistic radioactive beams can help to solve astrophysical puzzles and several successful experimental methods. State-of-the-art theories are discussed at some length with the purpose of helping us understand the experimental results reported. The review is not complete and we have focused most of it to traditional methods aiming at the determination of the equation of state of symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter and the role of the symmetry energy. Whenever possible, under the limitations of our present understanding of experimental data and theory, we try to pinpoint the information still missing to further understand how stars evolve, explode, and how their internal structure might be.

## Key claim

Relativistic radioactive beams at GeV energies can be used to indirectly determine nuclear reaction rates of astrophysical interest that occur at keV energies, but the review is not complete and focuses primarily on traditional methods for determining the equation of state of symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter and the role of the symmetry energy, with the caveat that considerable improvements in experimental accuracy and new dedicated facilities are still needed.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental methods using radioactive beams at relativistic energies (100-2000 MeV/nucleon) at facilities such as RIKEN/Japan and GSI/Germany, combined with inverse kinematics techniques and newly developed detectors. The theoretical framework covers reaction rate calculations from Maxwell-Boltzmann averaged cross sections, weak interaction rates for electron capture and neutrino scattering, and the determination of the nuclear equation of state from experimental observables including total reaction cross sections, Coulomb excitation, pygmy resonances, dipole polarizability, and charge exchange reactions.

## Key numbers

- Beam energies in the range 100-2000 MeV/nucleon are defined as "relativistic" for radioactive beam facilities
- The reaction p + 7Li → α + α was the first nuclear induced reaction carried out in a terrestrial laboratory in 1932
- The reaction D + 3H → α + n liberates about 17.6 MeV of energy
- The lifetime limit for a nuclear beam traveling 100 m near the speed of light is about 0.3 × 10^{-6} s (approximately 1 μs)
- 11Li has a half-life of 1.5 ms and 100Sn has a half-life of 1.6 s
- The triple-alpha capture reaction involves 8Be with an α-decay width of about Γα = 6 eV
- The weak coupling constant G = (3.04545 ± 0.00006) × 10^{-12} in natural units
- The dimensionless effective coupling constants are gV = 1, gA = 1.26, gM = κp - κn = 3.70

## Context

This review extends earlier reviews by Bertulani (2016) and Bertulani (2019) on indirect methods in nuclear astrophysics, covering both experimental techniques and theoretical methods needed to understand stellar evolution, nucleosynthesis, and neutron star properties. The work connects to the determination of the equation of state from gravitational wave observations such as GW170817 by the LIGO-Virgo collaboration (2017), and addresses open questions in Big Bang Nucleosynthesis including the "lithium puzzle" discussed by Bertulani (2016), Hou (2017), and Pitrou (2018). The review enables future work by identifying the need for improved experimental accuracy and new dedicated nuclear facilities to study relativistic reactions with radioactive beams.
