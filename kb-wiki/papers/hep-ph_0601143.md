# Open-charm meson spectroscopy

> We present a theoretical framework that accounts for the new $D_J$ and $D_{sJ}$ mesons measured in the open-charm sector. These resonances are properly described if considered as a mixture of conventional $P-$wave quark-antiquark states and four-quark components. The narrowest states are basically $P-$wave quark-antiquark mesons, while the dominantly four-quark states are shifted above the corresponding two-meson threshold, being broad resonances. We study the electromagnetic decay widths as basic tools to scrutiny their nature. The proposed explanation incorporates in a natural way the most recently discovered mesons in charmonium spectroscopy.

# Open-charm meson spectroscopy

## Key claim

The new narrow $D_J$ and $D_{sJ}$ mesons can be described as a mixture of conventional $P$-wave quark-antiquark states and four-quark components, where the mixing is treated perturbatively with a coupling parameter $\gamma = 240$ MeV. The narrowest states are predominantly $P$-wave $q\bar{q}$ mesons, while the dominantly four-quark states are shifted above the corresponding two-meson threshold and appear as broad resonances.

## Method

The authors use a non-relativistic constituent quark model that includes chiral boson-exchange interactions (pion, kaon, eta, sigma) for light quarks, one-gluon-exchange (OGE) with regularized contact terms, and a screened confining potential. The two-body ($q\bar{q}$) problem is solved exactly via the Numerov algorithm, and the four-body ($qq\bar{q}\bar{q}$) problem is solved variationally using Gaussian trial wave functions with mixed Jacobi coordinates. The mixing between $q\bar{q}$ and $qq\bar{q}\bar{q}$ configurations is introduced through a constant coupling $V_{q\bar{q} \leftrightarrow qq\bar{q}\bar{q}} = \gamma$, treated perturbatively, with $\gamma$ fixed to reproduce the mass of the $D_{sJ}^*(2317)$.

## Key numbers

- $D_{sJ}^*(2317)$: experimental width $\Gamma < 4.6$ MeV; predicted as dominantly $c\bar{s}$ $J^P=0^+$ with approximately 30% four-quark component, shifted below the $DK$ threshold.
- $D_{sJ}(2460)$: experimental width $\Gamma < 5.5$ MeV; predicted as dominantly $c\bar{s}$ $J^P=1^+$ with approximately 30% four-quark component, shifted below the $D^*K$ threshold.
- $D_0^*(2308)$ (Belle): mass $2308 \pm 17 \pm 15 \pm 28$ MeV/$c^2$, width $\Gamma = 276 \pm 21 \pm 18 \pm 60$ MeV; predicted as mixed state at 2241 MeV with 46% four-quark and 53% $c\bar{n}$ components.
- Unmixed $c\bar{n}$ $^3P_0$ mass: 2465 MeV; unmixed $cn\bar{n}\bar{n}$ mass: 2505 MeV.
- Mixing parameter $\gamma = 240$ MeV, giving $|\gamma/(E^{n}_{J^{PC}}-E^{n+1}_{J^{PC}})|^2 \approx 0.25$.
- Predicted ratio $\Gamma[D_{sJ}(2460) \to D_s^+ \gamma] / \Gamma[D_{sJ}(2460) \to D_s^{*+} \gamma] \approx 100$, compared to $\approx 1-2$ in pure $q\bar{q}$ models.
- Four-quark to four-quark $M1$ decay width: $\Gamma[cn\bar{s}\bar{n}(1^+) \to cn\bar{s}\bar{n}(0^+) + \gamma] \approx 4$ eV, contributing $\approx 0.25$ eV to the $D_{sJ}(2460) \to D_{sJ}^*(2317) + \gamma$ decay.
- $D_{sJ}^+(2632)$ (SELEX): mass $2632.5 \pm 1.7$ MeV/$c^2$, width $\Gamma < 17$ MeV; no theoretical partner found in this model (the $2^3S_1$ state lies at 2764 MeV and the $1^3D_1$ at 2873 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the same mixing mechanism previously applied to light-scalar mesons by Vijande et al. (2005) to the open-charm sector, addressing the puzzle that the $D_{sJ}^*(2317)$ and $D_{sJ}(2460)$ masses are significantly lower than predictions from quark potential models by Godfrey and Isgur (1991) (which gave $\approx 2.48$ GeV and $\approx 2.55$ GeV, respectively). The approach contrasts with interpretations as pure $DK$-molecules (Barnes et al., 2003), $D_s\pi$-atoms (Szczepaniak, 2003), or pure four-quark states (Cheng et al., 2003), and with unitarized meson models (van Beveren and Rupp, 2003). The predicted electromagnetic decay ratios provide a diagnostic tool to distinguish between $q\bar{q}$, molecular, and four-quark interpretations, and the absence of a theoretical partner for the $D_{sJ}^+(2632)$ calls for experimental confirmation of that state.
