# Vortex Pinning in Neutron Stars, Slip-stick Dynamics, and the Origin of Spin Glitches

> We study pinning and unpinning of superfluid vortices in the inner crust of a neutron star using 3-dimensional dynamical simulations. Strong pinning occurs for certain lattice orientations of an idealized, body-centered cubic lattice, and occurs generally in an amorphous or impure nuclear lattice. The pinning force per unit length is $\sim 10^{16}$ dyn cm$^{-1}$ for a vortex-nucleus interaction that is repulsive, and $\sim 10^{17}$ dyn cm$^{-1}$ for an attractive interaction. The pinning force is strong enough to account for observed spin jumps (glitches). Vortices forced through the lattice move with a slip-stick character; for a range of superfluid velocities, the vortex can be in either a cold, pinned state or a hot unpinned state, with strong excitation of Kelvin waves on the vortex. This two-state nature of vortex motion sets the stage for large-scale vortex movement that creates an observable spin glitch. We argue that the vortex array is likely to become tangled as a result of repeated unpinnings and repinnings. We conjecture that during a glitch, the Kelvin-wave excitation spreads rapidly along the direction of the mean superfluid vorticity and slower in the direction perpendicular to it, akin to an anisotropic deflagration.

## Key claim

The pinning force per unit length from three-dimensional dynamical simulations is strong enough to account for observed spin glitches, but the actual strength depends critically on the sign of the vortex-nucleus interaction and the lattice structure, with the abstract's quoted values representing angle-averaged results that hide strong orientation dependence for a regular bcc lattice.

## Method

The authors performed three-dimensional dynamical simulations of a single superfluid vortex treated classically as a string with tension, solving the equation of motion that includes bending force, Magnus force, drag force, and the force from interaction with the nuclear lattice using a new spectral method. They modeled the vortex-nucleus interaction parametrically for different lattice structures: an ideal body-centered cubic (bcc) lattice, a repulsive bcc lattice with dilute attractive impurities, and an amorphous nuclear glass, using a random Fourier series to generate the glass potential.

## Key numbers

- Pinning force per unit length for an attractive bcc lattice: $f_p \simeq 3 \times 10^{17}$ dyn cm$^{-1}$ for $|E_p| = 4$ MeV and lattice spacing $b = 30$ fm (angle-averaged)
- Pinning force per unit length for a repulsive bcc lattice: $f_p \simeq 3 \times 10^{16}$ dyn cm$^{-1}$ for $|E_p| = 4$ MeV and $b = 30$ fm (angle-averaged)
- Critical flow velocity for unpinning in an attractive bcc lattice: $v_c \sim 10^{-4}c$
- Critical flow velocity for unpinning in a repulsive bcc lattice: $v_c \sim 10^{-5}c$
- Pinning force per unit length for a nuclear glass: $f_p \sim 10^{17}$ dyn cm$^{-1}$, comparable to the attractive bcc lattice value
- Glitch magnitude estimate: $\Delta\Omega_c/\Omega_c \simeq 2 \times 10^{-5} f^{-1} (R/10 \text{ km})^4 (\Omega_c/10^2 \text{ rad s}^{-1})^{-1} (I/10^{45} \text{ g cm}^2)^{-1} (\Delta R/R / 0.05) (\bar{f}_p/10^{16} \text{ dyn cm}^{-1})$
- The 2016 Vela pulsar glitch magnitude: $\Delta\nu/\nu = 1.4 \times 10^{-6}$, occurring in under 12.6 s
- Vortex tension estimate: $T_v \simeq 0.6 (\rho_s/10^{13} \text{ g cm}^{-3})$ MeV fm$^{-1}$

## Context

This work extends the three-dimensional simulations of vortex pinning by Link (2009) for a nuclear glass, but now uses a vortex-nucleus interaction range smaller than the lattice spacing, as indicated by quantum calculations from Avogadro et al. (2008) and Wlazlowski et al. (2016). The results contradict Jones (1997), who argued that pinning to a regular lattice would be very weak due to mobile kinks, and find pinning forces 300 times larger than Seveso et al. (2016) who assumed infinite vortex tension. The slip-stick character and two-state nature of vortex motion identified here sets the stage for understanding how large-scale vortex unpinning could produce observable spin glitches, and suggests that the vortex array likely becomes tangled through repeated unpinning and repinning events.
