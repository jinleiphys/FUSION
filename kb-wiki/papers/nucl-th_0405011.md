# Exactly solvable Richardson-Gaudin models for many-body quantum systems

> The use of exactly-solvable Richardson-Gaudin (R-G) models to describe the physics of systems with strong pair correlations is reviewed. We begin with a brief discussion of Richardson's early work, which demonstrated the exact solvability of the pure pairing model, and then show how that work has evolved recently into a much richer class of exactly-solvable models. We then show how the Richardson solution leads naturally to an exact analogy between such quantum models and classical electrostatic problems in two dimensions. This is then used to demonstrate formally how BCS theory emerges as the large-N limit of the pure pairing Hamiltonian and is followed by several applications to problems of relevance to condensed matter physics, nuclear physics and the physics of confined systems. Some of the interesting effects that are discussed in the context of these exactly-solvable models include: (1) the crossover from superconductivity to a fluctuation-dominated regime in small metallic grains, (2) the role of the nucleon Pauli principle in suppressing the effects of high spin bosons in interacting boson models of nuclei, and (3) the possibility of fragmentation in confined boson systems. Interesting insight is also provided into the origin of the superconducting phase transition both in two-dimensional electronic systems and in atomic nuclei, based on the electrostatic image of the corresponding exactly-solvable quantum pairing models.

# Exactly solvable Richardson-Gaudin models for many-body quantum systems

## Key claim

The paper reviews how the exact Richardson solution of the pure pairing model, which was largely unused for decades, has been generalized into a richer class of exactly-solvable Richardson-Gaudin models applicable to fermion and boson systems; the authors show that BCS theory emerges as the large-N limit of the pure pairing Hamiltonian, but emphasize that the exact solution is necessary to properly describe the crossover from superconductivity to a fluctuation-dominated regime in small or ultrasmall systems where number fluctuations in BCS (of order sqrt(N)) become significant for systems with N ~ 100 particles.

## Method

The authors review the Richardson solution of the pairing Hamiltonian, which reduces the many-body problem to solving a set of M nonlinear coupled equations (Richardson equations) for pair energies E_alpha, where M is the number of Cooper pairs. They then establish the connection between the Richardson model and Gaudin magnets through the integrability of the pairing model, identifying the complete set of commuting operators (quantum invariants). This connection is generalized to three families of exactly-solvable models (rational, trigonometric, and hyperbolic) for both fermion (SU(2) algebra) and boson (SU(1,1) algebra) systems, with the exact eigenstates written as products of collective pair operators whose amplitudes are determined by the pair energies. The exact solution is mapped onto a two-dimensional classical electrostatic problem where fixed charges ("orbitons") correspond to single-particle levels and free charges ("pairons") correspond to Cooper pairs.

## Key numbers

- For systems with N ~ 100 particles, improvements beyond BCS are required because number fluctuations in BCS are of order sqrt(N)
- Full diagonalization can be carried out for up to L=16 doubly-degenerate levels and M=8 pairs, where the full dimension of the Hamiltonian matrix is 12,870
- The Lanczos method can still obtain lowest eigenvalues for systems with up to L=32 and M=16
- The Richardson method can obtain exact solutions for systems with L=1000 and M=500, where the dimension is 2.7 x 10^299
- The degeneracy of a single-particle level l is Omega_l = 2l+1 for fermions with angular momentum quantum number l
- For scalar bosons confined to a 3D harmonic oscillator, the degeneracy of shell with principal quantum number n is Omega_n = (n+1)(n+2)/2

## Context

This review extends the early work of Richardson (1960s) and Gaudin (1976, 1995) on exactly-solvable pairing models, building on the proof of integrability of the pairing model by Cambiaggio, Rivas, and Saraceno (CRS). The paper connects to applications in ultrasmall superconducting grains (von Delft, 2001), interacting boson models of nuclear structure (Dukelsky and Pittel, 2001), electrons in two-dimensional lattices, and confined Bose systems. The electrostatic mapping, following Richardson (1977) and Gaudin's book, provides a pictorial view of the transition to superconductivity in finite nuclei and enables study of quantum phase transitions for pairing using the Richardson algorithm, though the authors note that temperature-dependent phase transitions would require developing the Thermodynamic Bethe ansatz for the pairing model.
