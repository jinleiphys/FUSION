# Density-constraint Time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method

> Background: The Density-constraint Time-dependent Hartree-Fock method is currently the tool of choice to predict fusion cross-sections. However, it does not include pairing correlations, which have been found recently to play an important role. Purpose: To describe the fusion cross-section with a method that includes the superfluidity and to understand the impact of pairing on both the fusion barrier and cross-section. Method: The density-constraint method is tested first on the following reactions without pairing, $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O and $^{40}$Ca+$^{40}$Ca. A new method is developed, the Density-constraint Time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method. Using the Gogny-TDHFB code, it is applied to the reactions $^{20}$O+$^{20}$O and $^{44}$Ca+$^{44}$Ca. Results: The Gogny approach for systems without pairing reproduces the experimental data well. The DC-TDHFB method is coherent with the TDHFB fusion threshold. The effect of the phase-lock mechanism is shown for those reactions. Conclusions: The DC-TDHFB method is a useful new tool to determine the fusion potential between superfluid systems and to deduce their fusion cross-sections.

# Density-constraint Time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method

## Key claim

The Density-constrained Time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (DC-TDHFB) method provides a coherent description of fusion potentials for superfluid systems, but the effect of the initial gauge angle on the barrier height is partially reduced by dynamical effects above the barrier, and the phase-locking mechanism causes the potential for intermediate angles (e.g., φ=π/4) to shift toward the aligned case (φ=0) rather than remaining midway between extremes.

## Method

The authors first validate the DC-TDHF method (without pairing) using the Gogny D1S interaction for the reactions 16O+16O and 40Ca+40Ca, reproducing experimental fusion cross-sections. They then develop the DC-TDHFB method by adding constraints on both the diagonal one-body density and the norm of the pairing tensor matrix elements κij, solving the HFB equation with Lagrange multipliers adjusted iteratively. The method is applied to the superfluid systems 20O+20O and 44Ca+44Ca using the Gogny-TDHFB code with a hybrid basis of two-dimensional harmonic oscillator eigenfunctions and one-dimensional spatial grid points, with initial gauge angles set by transforming the left fragment's U and V matrices.

## Key numbers

- For 16O+16O: the DC-TDHF potential is converged with Nshell=4-5; the barrier position from TDHF matches the top of the DC-TDHF potential; the potential is unchanged with a 1.5 MeV variation of the center-of-mass energy above the barrier.
- For 40Ca+40Ca: the DC-TDHF results are converged with Nshell=4; the fusion cross-section depends on the TDHF center-of-mass energy, and the method underestimates the cross-section at energies around E_c.m. = 50 MeV.
- For 20O+20O: the barrier heights from the frozen density method were 9.42, 9.6, and 9.79 MeV for φ=0, π/4, and π/2 respectively; the DC-TDHFB method shows the φ=π/4 potential shifting toward the φ=0 curve as R decreases, consistent with phase locking.
- For 44Ca+44Ca: the TDHFB fusion thresholds are 51.63, 51.99, and 52.49 MeV for φ=0, π/4, and π/2 respectively; the DC-TDHFB barrier splitting is 0.5 MeV between highest and lowest, compared to 0.87 MeV for the threshold energy difference.
- The numerical cost of a TDHFB calculation for 44Ca+44Ca with Nshell=6 is 24 hours using 32 CPUs.
- The mesh parameter Δz = 0.91 fm with Nz=23 points for HF initialization and Nz=46 for dynamics; time step Δt = 0.4 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends the Density-constrained Time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) method developed by Cusson (1985), Umar (2006-2015), and others, which previously could not treat pairing correlations. It builds on recent findings by Hashimoto (2016), Magierski (2016), Sekizawa (2017), and Bulgac (2017) that the pairing gauge angle strongly affects fusion in superfluid systems, and on the experimental analysis by Scamps (2018) showing an enhancement of the barrier height of about 1 MeV for superfluid systems. The method enables future study of reactions such as 64Ni+64Ni, 18O+16O, and 40Ca+44Ca, and could be applied to fission and quasi-fission processes.
