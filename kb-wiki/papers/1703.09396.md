# Luneburg-lens-like structural Pauli attractive core of nuclear force at short distances

> The nuclear force has been understood to have a repulsive core at short distances, similar to a molecular force, since Jastrow proposed it in 1951. The existence of the repulsion was experimentally confirmed from the proton-proton scattering 1S_0 phase shift, which becomes negative beyond 230 MeV. This repulsion is essential for preventing the nucleon-nucleon system from collapsing by attraction. The origin of the repulsion has been considered to be due to the Pauli principle, similar to the repulsion originally revealed in alpha-alpha scattering, in many studies including recent lattice QCD calculations. On the other hand, very recently it was shown that an inter-nuclear potential including alpha-alpha interactions has a Luneburg-lens-like attraction at short distances rather than repulsion. We show that the nuclear force with an attractive potential at short distances that reproduces the experimental phase shifts well has a Luneburg-lens-like structural Pauli attractive core (SPAC) at short distances and acts as apparent repulsion. The apparent repulsion is caused by the deeply embedded unobservable Pauli forbidden state similar to nucleus-nucleus potentials.

# Luneburg-lens-like structural Pauli attractive core of nuclear force at short distances

## Key claim

The nuclear force in the $^1S_0$ channel can be described by a deep attractive potential at short distances (a "structural Pauli attractive core" or SPAC) rather than the conventional repulsive core, and this attractive core acts as *apparent* repulsion because it contains a deeply embedded unobservable Pauli forbidden state that forces the scattering wave function to have a node, preventing collapse. The claim is conditional on the interpretation that the repulsive core is an approximate supersymmetric partner of the deeper SPAC potential, analogous to the established case of nucleus-nucleus potentials.

## Method

The author constructed a three-range Gaussian potential for the $^1S_0$ nucleon-nucleon channel by taking the established G3RS potential (Tamagaki 1968) and changing the sign of the innermost (shortest-range) Gaussian term from repulsive (+2000 MeV) to attractive (-1850 MeV), while keeping the intermediate and outer terms unchanged. The resulting SPAC potential was fitted to reproduce the experimental $^1S_0$ proton-proton scattering phase shifts from Arndt (1983) over the energy range $E_{lab}=0$–660 MeV. The wave functions were calculated and compared with those from the repulsive-core G3RS potential, and the short-distance shape was compared analytically and numerically to a Luneburg-lens potential (a truncated harmonic oscillator).

## Key numbers

- The SPAC potential has the form $V(r) = -5 e^{-(r/2.5)^2} -270 e^{-(r/0.942)^2} -1850 e^{-(r/0.447)^2}$ (strengths in MeV, ranges in fm); the innermost term is attractive at -1850 MeV compared to the G3RS repulsive core of +2000 MeV.
- The Luneburg-lens-like fit to the SPAC core gives $V_0 = 2120$ MeV and $R = 0.54$ fm; the third term alone gives $V_0 = 1850$ MeV and $R = 0.48$ fm, close to its Gaussian range 0.447 fm.
- The deeply bound Pauli forbidden $0s$ state lies at about -637 MeV in the SPAC potential.
- The energy-independent node in the scattering wave function occurs at $r \approx 0.5$ fm, corresponding to the Luneburg-lens radius $R$ and the repulsive core radius of the G3RS potential.
- The experimental $^1S_0$ phase shift becomes negative beyond 230 MeV (proton-proton scattering), confirming the existence of repulsion in the conventional interpretation.

## Context

This work extends the recent discovery by Ohkubo (2016) that nucleus-nucleus potentials (e.g., $\alpha$-$\alpha$, $\alpha$-$^{16}$O) have a Luneburg-lens-like structural Pauli attraction at short distances rather than a repulsive core, and applies the same concept to the nucleon-nucleon force. It contradicts the long-standing Jastrow (1951) picture of a repulsive core, which was supported by the negative $^1S_0$ phase shift from Stapp (1959) and incorporated in all major phenomenological potentials (Hamada-Johnston 1962, Reid 1968, Tamagaki's G3RS 1968, Argonne V18, CD-Bonn, Reid93, ESC04). The SPAC interpretation aligns with quark-model approaches by Neudatchin (1977), Kukulin (1984, 1985, 1992), and others who argued that the repulsive core arises from Pauli forbidden states of the [42] orbital symmetry. The work enables re-examination of few-body binding problems (e.g., the triton underbinding and the tetraneutron observed by Kisamori 2016) where the inner oscillations of the SPAC wave function may provide additional binding energy without invoking three-body forces.
