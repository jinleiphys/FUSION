# Chiral Phase Structure of Hot and Dense Nuclear Matter

> The description of nuclear matter bulk properties in the framework of quark-based hadron models in curved unit cells is extended to finite temperature. We discuss the analogy and interplay of density and temperature effects in this formulation and study, as an application, the temperature and density dependence of chiral vacuum properties in the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model.

# Chiral Phase Structure of Hot and Dense Nuclear Matter

## Key claim

The hypersphere unit-cell approach to nuclear matter, previously developed for zero-temperature quark-based hadron models, is extended to finite temperature and applied to the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model; the resulting temperature and density dependence of chiral vacuum properties closely follows that obtained from conventional chemical-potential treatments, despite the fundamentally different geometric description of density.

## Method

The authors place the NJL model on a curved three-dimensional hypersphere \(S^3(L)\) of radius \(L\) as the spatial unit cell, then introduce finite temperature via the standard imaginary-time formalism, compactifying the Euclidean time direction to \(S^1(\beta/2\pi)\) with inverse temperature \(\beta\). The quark propagator in Hartree-Fock approximation is derived with KMS boundary conditions, and the scalar self-energy (gap equation), scalar quark condensate, and pion decay constant are computed analytically in terms of discrete constituent quark spectra on the hypersphere and Matsubara frequency sums.

## Key numbers

- The model parameters are fixed at \(g = 4.08\ \mathrm{GeV}^{-2}\), \(\Lambda = 700\ \mathrm{MeV}\), and \(\epsilon = 2.4\), matching the zero-temperature hypersphere NJL study of Forkel (1994).
- The critical temperature for chiral restoration is stated to be \(T_c \simeq 140-160\ \mathrm{MeV}\) from lattice predictions (Kogut 1991), used as a reference for the expected scale.
- The pion decay constant \(f_\pi\) and the scalar quark condensate both vanish at the same density-dependent critical temperature, confirming pion decoupling at chiral restoration.

## Context

This work extends the hypersphere unit-cell description of nuclear matter, previously applied to the Skyrme model and the NJL model at zero temperature by Forkel (1994) and earlier hypersphere studies, to finite temperature. The results are compared with conventional chemical-potential treatments of the NJL model (NJLfind, 1994) and reproduce them almost quantitatively, despite the fundamentally different geometric encoding of density. The approach enables future studies of hot, dense matter in a framework that treats temperature and density on equal footing as compactifications of space-time directions, though it does not resolve the problem of quark clustering into color singlets.
