# Comment on "Atomic mass compilation 2012" by B. Pfeiffer, K. Venkataramaniah, U. Czok, C. Scheidenberger

> This "Comment" submitted to ADNDT on December 13, 2013 concerns a publication entitled "Atomic Mass Compilation 2012", which appeared in the March 2014 issue of the journal Atomic Data and Nuclear Data Tables (available online on September 6, 2013).

## Key claim

The Atomic Mass Compilation 2012 (AMC12) is not a credible update or continuation of the established Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME) series, because its method of simple averaging of new results with the previous AME2003 values ignores correlations and produces less precise and less accurate masses than the AME2012 evaluation.

## Method

The authors compare the AMC12 compilation method, which combines each new experimental result independently with the previous AME2003 mass values, against the AME approach that uses a least-squares fit of an overdetermined set of linear relations among all measured masses. They present eight illustrative examples of specific nuclides where AMC12 gives incorrect or less precise mass values, omits known isomers, includes theoretically estimated values as experimental, or fails to incorporate important recent measurements that require combination of Q-values.

## Key numbers

- $^{47}$Ar: AMC12 mass is -25910(100) keV compared to AME2012 value of -25210(90) keV, a discrepancy of 700 keV
- $^{73}$Ge: AMC12 gives precision of 1.6 keV, whereas AME2012 gives precision of 0.06 keV (27 times better)
- $^{100}$Sn: AMC12 value is -56780(710) keV instead of AME2012 value of -57280(300) keV, a difference of 500 keV
- $^{286}$Ed (Z=113): mass excess is 168202(896) keV in AMC12 and 169730#(670#) in AME2012, a difference of 1528 keV; the Q_alpha value deduced from AMC12 is 10012 keV, whereas the experimental value is 9770(100) keV

## Context

This comment extends the long-standing Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME) series initiated by A.H. Wapstra in the 1950s, which has provided consistent mass tables endorsed by the IUPAP C2 Commission. The authors contrast the AME2012 evaluation (Audi et al., 2012) and its associated Nubase2012 (Kondev et al., 2012) with the AMC12 compilation (Pfeiffer et al., 2014), warning users not to confuse the two works. The comment enables users of mass tables to distinguish between the rigorous AME methodology and the less reliable AMC12 approach, which could otherwise mislead applications in nuclear energy, astrophysics, and mass model development.
