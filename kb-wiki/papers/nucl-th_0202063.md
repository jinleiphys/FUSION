# From microscopic to macroscopic dynamics in mean-field theory: effect of neutron skin on fusion barrier and dissipation

> In this work, we introduce a new method to reduce the microscopic mean-field theory to a classical macroscopic dynamics during the initial stage of fusion reactions. We show that TDHF (Time-dependent Hartree-Fock) could be a useful tool to gain information on fusion barriers as well as on one-body dissipation effects. We apply the mean-field theory to the case of head-on reaction between $^{16}$O and $^{16,22,24,28}$O in order to quantify the effect of neutron skin on fusion. We show that the determination of fusion barrier requires, in addition to a precise knowledge of the relative distance between the center of mass of the two fusing nuclei, the introduction of an additional collective coordinate that explicitly breaks the neutron-proton symmetry. In this context, we estimate the position, height and diffuseness of the barrier as well as the one-body friction and show that a global enhancement of the fusion cross-section is expected in neutron rich nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a macroscopic classical dynamics (with a potential and friction coefficient) can be extracted from microscopic Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations for fusion reactions, but only if an additional collective coordinate representing the difference between neutron and proton relative distances is introduced for neutron-rich systems; without this coordinate, the extracted potential shows unphysical energy dependence.

## Method

The author performed three-dimensional TDHF calculations using the SLy4d Skyrme force for head-on collisions of ¹⁶O with ¹⁶,²²,²⁴,²⁸O on a (32 fm)³ grid with 0.8 fm spacing and 0.45 fm/c time steps. For each reaction, four initial center-of-mass energies (E/A = 0.60, 0.65, 0.70, 0.75 MeV) were used. The relative distance R(t), momentum P(t), and their time derivatives were extracted from the TDHF densities by splitting the system into two subspaces at the neck position. By inverting the classical equations of motion using pairs of trajectories with slightly different initial energies, the force F(R) and friction coefficient γ(R) were determined; for neutron-rich systems, a third trajectory was needed to also extract the coupling γ_np(R) to the neutron-proton relative distance difference.

## Key numbers

- The reduced mass ratio μ(R)/μ₀(R) is approximately constant at 0.67 for all systems studied, up to formation of a compact shape.
- Barrier heights V_B extracted from the macroscopic reduction: 9.8 MeV for ¹⁶O+¹⁶O, 8.9 MeV for ¹⁶O+²²O, 8.8 MeV for ¹⁶O+²⁴O, 8.9 MeV for ¹⁶O+²⁸O.
- Barrier positions R_B: 8.6 fm for ¹⁶O+¹⁶O, 9.6 fm for ¹⁶O+²²O, 9.8 fm for ¹⁶O+²⁴O, 10.0 fm for ¹⁶O+²⁸O.
- Barrier curvatures ħω_B: 2.39 MeV for ¹⁶O+¹⁶O, 1.21 MeV for ¹⁶O+²²O, 1.36 MeV for ¹⁶O+²⁴O, 1.65 MeV for ¹⁶O+²⁸O.
- The static Hartree-Fock calculation used a (16 fm)³ mesh with 0.8 fm step; the dynamical TDHF used a (32 fm)³ volume with the same step and 0.45 fm/c time step.
- Initial center-of-mass energies used: E/A = 0.60, 0.65, 0.70, and 0.75 MeV for each reaction.

## Context

This work extends earlier attempts to reduce TDHF to macroscopic dynamics by Koonin (1979), Brink and Stancu (1981), and others, but uses a modern three-dimensional code with the full SLy4d Skyrme force including spin-orbit. The finding that an isospin-breaking coordinate is necessary for neutron-rich systems connects to earlier work by Satchler (1991), Dasso and collaborators (1992), and Canto and collaborators (1995), who predicted that neutron excess should increase fusion cross sections. The extracted barriers agree with experimental data for ¹⁶O+¹⁶O from Vaz and collaborators (1981) and Haight and collaborators (1984). The method enables future application to superheavy systems where macroscopic theories are widely used, as noted by Moller and collaborators (1997), Armbruster (1999), and others.
