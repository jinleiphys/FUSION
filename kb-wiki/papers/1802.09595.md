# Elliptic flow from Coulomb interaction and low density elastic scattering

> In high energy heavy ion collisions and interacting cold atom systems, large elliptic flow anisotropies have been observed. For the large opacity ($ρσL\sim 10^{3}$) of the latter hydrodynamics is a natural consequence, but for the small opacity ($ρσL\sim 1$) of the former hydrodynamic description is questionable. To shed light onto the situation, we simulate the expansion of a low density Argon ion (or atom) system, initially trapped in an elliptical region, under the Coulomb interaction (or elastic scattering). Significant elliptic anisotropy is found in both cases, and the anisotropy depends on the initial spatial eccentricity and the density of the system. The results may provide insights into the physics of anisotropic flow in high energy heavy ion collisions and its role in the study of quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim

Significant elliptic flow can develop in low-density systems (opacity ~1) under either Coulomb interaction or short-range elastic scattering, challenging the assumption that large anisotropic flow requires strong coupling or high density; the authors simulate classical Argon ion and atom expansions to demonstrate this universality.

## Method

The authors simulate the expansion of 100–5000 Argon ions (Coulomb interaction) or Argon atoms (isotropic elastic scattering with cross section π nm²) initially trapped in an elliptical region (half-axes a=10 nm, b=24 nm, c=50 nm) at 300 K. They use a time-step approach (10⁻⁴ ns) to compute particle motion under the respective interactions and quantify the resulting momentum-space anisotropy via the elliptic flow parameter v₂ = ⟨cos(2φ)⟩.

## Key numbers

- Initial trap aspect ratio a:b = 1:2.4 (corresponding to Au+Au collisions at impact parameter b=8 fm), with half-axes a=10 nm, b=24 nm, c=50 nm.
- Initial average thermal velocity of particles: 432 m/s at T=300 K.
- Typical Coulomb force for 1000 ions: 10⁻¹⁰ N, giving typical acceleration 10⁶ nm/ns².
- Typical Coulomb potential energy ~1 eV, larger than thermal energy ~0.025 eV.
- Elastic scattering cross section: σ = π nm² (interaction distance 1 nm).
- v₂ increases linearly with initial spatial eccentricity ε₂ at small ε₂, then stronger than linear.
- For elastic scattering, v₂ increases with particle number N (from 100 to 5000) and corresponding opacity ρσL (from ~0.1 to ~5); v₂ is significant even at small opacity.
- For Coulomb interaction, v₂ saturates for most N values and is smaller than for elastic scattering at the same N.

## Context

This work extends the debate on the origin of elliptic flow in heavy-ion collisions, where hydrodynamics (Heinz 2013, Schaefer 2014) assumes high opacity (~10³) but transport models (AMPT and MPC, He 2015) find large flow at low opacity (~1). The authors simulate classical analogs (Coulomb explosion and low-density elastic scattering) to show that large v₂ can arise without strong coupling or hydrodynamics, contradicting the belief that strong interactions are necessary (Ollitrault 1992). The results enable future work distinguishing hydrodynamic (strong radial flow) from low-density (weak radial flow) mechanisms.
