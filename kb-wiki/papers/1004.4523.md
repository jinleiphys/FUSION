# Abrupt changes in alpha decay systematics as a manifestation of collective nuclear modes

> An abrupt change in $α$ decay systematics around the N=126 neutron shell closure is discussed. It is explained as a sudden hindrance of the clustering of the nucleons that eventually form the $α$ particle. This is because the clustering induced by the pairing mode acting upon the four nucleons is inhibited if the configuration space does not allow a proper manifestation of the pairing collectivity.

# Abrupt changes in alpha decay systematics as a manifestation of collective nuclear modes

## Key claim

The universal decay law (UDL) reproduces most alpha decay data within a factor of three, but fails by about one order of magnitude for nuclei with neutron number N=126 or just below, specifically in the decay of 210Po. This failure is explained as a sudden hindrance of alpha-particle clustering because the pairing collectivity that induces clustering of the four nucleons is inhibited when the configuration space is restricted, as occurs for neutron-hole states compared to neutron-particle states.

## Method

The authors extracted experimental alpha formation amplitudes from measured half-lives using the Thomas expression for the decay width, then performed shell-model calculations of the formation amplitudes for 212Po and 210Po using a surface delta interaction with up to nine major harmonic oscillator shells. They compared the two-neutron wave functions for the particle state 210Pb(gs) and the hole state 206Pb(gs) at the nuclear surface, and evaluated the alpha formation amplitudes at the touching radius R=9.0 fm.

## Key numbers

- The formation amplitude in 210Po is 3.305×10⁻³ fm⁻³/², compared to 1.082×10⁻² fm⁻³/² in 212Po, a factor of 3.28 difference in amplitude (factor of 10.73 in formation probability).
- The experimental log₁₀|R F_α(R)|² for 210Po is less than -3 fm⁻¹, while values for 208Po (N=124), 212Rn (N=126), and 194Pb are approximately -2.7 fm⁻¹.
- The UDL predicts experimental values within a factor of three for most cases, but the discrepancy at N=126 is about one order of magnitude.
- The touching radius used in calculations is R=9.0 fm, with R₀=1.2 fm.

## Context

This work extends the universal decay law (UDL) developed by Qi et al. (2008, 2009) and explains a known deviation at the N=126 shell closure that had been noted by Bata (1988), Toth (1999), Wauters (1993), and Van Duppen (2003). The sudden change in alpha decay systematics at N=126 had also been observed in semiclassical approaches by Brown (1992) and Poenaru (1983, 1991, 2006, 2007), where half-lives of N=126 nuclei were significantly underestimated. The paper enables using alpha decay as a probe of shell structure in very unstable and superheavy nuclei, and predicts enhanced alpha clustering in N≈Z regions such as around 100Sn.
