# General quantum algorithms for Hamiltonian simulation with applications to a non-Abelian lattice gauge theory

> With a focus on universal quantum computing for quantum simulation, and through the example of lattice gauge theories, we introduce rather general quantum algorithms that can efficiently simulate certain classes of interactions consisting of correlated changes in multiple (bosonic and fermionic) quantum numbers with non-trivial functional coefficients. In particular, we analyze diagonalization of Hamiltonian terms using a singular-value decomposition technique, and discuss how the achieved diagonal unitaries in the digitized time-evolution operator can be implemented. The lattice gauge theory studied is the SU(2) gauge theory in 1+1 dimensions coupled to one flavor of staggered fermions, for which a complete quantum-resource analysis within different computational models is presented. The algorithms are shown to be applicable to higher-dimensional theories as well as to other Abelian and non-Abelian gauge theories. The example chosen further demonstrates the importance of adopting efficient theoretical formulations: it is shown that an explicitly gauge-invariant formulation using loop, string, and hadron degrees of freedom simplifies the algorithms and lowers the cost compared with the standard formulations based on angular-momentum as well as the Schwinger-boson degrees of freedom. The loop-string-hadron formulation further retains the non-Abelian gauge symmetry despite the inexactness of the digitized simulation, without the need for costly controlled operations. Such theoretical and algorithmic considerations are likely to be essential in quantumly simulating other complex theories of relevance to nature.

## Key claim

The paper introduces general quantum algorithms for Hamiltonian simulation that can efficiently simulate interactions involving correlated changes in multiple quantum numbers with non-trivial functional coefficients, but the efficiency is conditional on the ability to find simple singular-value decompositions of the interaction terms and on the choice of an efficient theoretical formulation of the degrees of freedom.

## Method

The authors develop a method based on singular-value decomposition (SVD) to diagonalize Hamiltonian terms that can be written as A + A† with A² = 0, using an ancillary qubit and projectors onto the nullspace and its complement. They apply this to the SU(2) lattice gauge theory in 1+1 dimensions coupled to one flavor of staggered fermions, comparing three formulations: the standard angular-momentum basis, the Schwinger-boson formulation, and the loop-string-hadron (LSH) formulation. The time-evolution operator is implemented using product formulas, with the diagonal unitaries realized either through Pauli-Z decomposition for near-term applications or through phase-kickback algorithms for fault-tolerant settings.

## Key numbers

* The loop-string-hadron formulation reduces the cost of implementing diagonal functions substantially compared with the standard angular-momentum and Schwinger-boson formulations, though the qubit count and gate complexity of the diagonalization routine are comparable across all three formulations.
* The first-order Lie-Trotter formula approximates e^{-iHt} with error scaling as O(t²/s), where s is the number of Trotter steps.
* The classical pre-processing for Pauli decomposition of diagonal functions scales exponentially with the number of qubits per lattice site but is independent of the system's size, depending only on the cutoff on gauge-field quantum numbers.

## Context

This work extends the shearing transformation approach introduced for the lattice Schwinger model by Stryker (2021) to a general SVD-based diagonalization method applicable to non-Abelian gauge theories. It improves upon the quantum algorithms of Kan (2021) for SU(2) lattice gauge theory by enabling evolution that satisfies both non-Abelian and Abelian Gauss's law constraints through the LSH formulation, without requiring costly controlled operations as in the SU(3) approach of Ciavarella (2021). The results enable future generalizations to higher-dimensional theories and other gauge groups such as quantum chromodynamics.
