# Sensitivity of Heavy-Quark Dipolar Flow to its Initial Spatial Distributions in Cu+Au Collisions

> We investigate charm-quark dynamics in asymmetric Cu+Au collisions at top RHIC energy using a Langevin approach embedded in a realistic hydrodynamic background. The intrinsic asymmetry of the colliding nuclei leads to a spatially lopsided initial energy-density profile, which generates a dipolar flow structure in the transverse plane even at midrapidity. As charm quarks propagate through this medium, they acquire a finite directed flow, $v_1$. We find that the $p_T$-integrated heavy-quark $v_1$ is approximately an order of magnitude larger than that of charged hadrons. In addition, the $p_T$-differential $v_1$ exhibits strong sensitivity to the initial spatial distribution of heavy quarks, emphasizing the importance of pre-equilibrium dynamics in determining final-state anisotropies. Beyond geometric effects, $v_1$ also provides direct sensitivity to medium interactions through the temperature-dependent drag coefficient. Its pronounced dependence on this transport input indicates that precision measurements of heavy-flavor directed flow could place meaningful constraints on heavy-quark transport coefficients, thereby improving Langevin-based descriptions and predictive power for heavy-flavor observables in heavy-ion collisions.

# Sensitivity of Heavy-Quark Dipolar Flow to its Initial Spatial Distributions in Cu+Au Collisions

## Key claim

The directed flow $v_1$ of charm quarks in Cu+Au collisions at top RHIC energy is approximately an order of magnitude larger than that of charged hadrons, and its sign, magnitude, and $p_T$ dependence are strongly sensitive to the initial spatial distribution of heavy quarks relative to the medium energy density, implying that precision measurements could constrain pre-equilibrium dynamics and heavy-quark transport coefficients.

## Method

The authors simulate charm-quark propagation through a quark-gluon plasma created in asymmetric Cu+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV using a Langevin dynamics approach coupled to a (3+1)-dimensional hydrodynamic background from the MUSIC framework. The medium evolution is initialized with Monte Carlo Glauber energy density profiles at $\tau_0 = 0.4$ fm with shear viscosity $\eta/s = 0.08$, and charm quarks are sampled according to three different initial spatial distributions: binary collision ($N_{\rm coll}$) profile, energy density profile, and a uniform box distribution. The drag coefficient $\gamma(T)$ is varied with different temperature dependencies to probe sensitivity to transport properties.

## Key numbers

- The $p_T$-integrated charm-quark $v_1$ is approximately an order of magnitude larger than that of charged hadrons for impact parameters up to $b = 6$ fm.
- The mean position of the $N_{\rm coll}$ distribution is $-1.01$ fm, while the energy density distribution mean is $-0.96$ fm along the impact parameter axis at $b = 6$ fm.
- The uniform box distribution has mean position $0.00$ fm.
- Hydrodynamic evolution starts at $\tau_0 = 0.4$ fm and continues until the local temperature reaches 145 MeV.
- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio is fixed at $\eta/s = 0.08$.
- A total of $5 \times 10^6$ heavy quarks are sampled for statistical precision.
- The drag coefficient is parameterized as $\gamma(T) = \gamma_0 T (T/m)^x$ with $\gamma_0$ taking values 0.6 and 1.2, and $x$ taking values 0 or $1/2$.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of heavy-flavor directed flow in tilted-fireball models by Chatterjee and Bozek (2017), which introduced a phenomenological tilt parameter, by instead using the intrinsic geometric asymmetry of Cu+Au collisions to generate a rapidity-even $v_1$ at midrapidity. The results demonstrate that heavy-quark $v_1$ provides a complementary constraint to $R_{AA}$ and $v_2$ for determining heavy-quark transport coefficients, and suggest that future experimental measurements could distinguish whether the initial heavy-quark distribution follows the binary collision profile or has been modified during the pre-equilibrium stage.
