# A Primer for Electro-Weak Induced Low Energy Nuclear Reactions

> In a series of papers, cited in the main body of the paper below, detailed calculations have been presented which show that electromagnetic and weak interactions can induce low energy nuclear reactions to occur with observable rates for a variety of processes. A common element in all these applications is that the electromagnetic energy stored in many relatively slow moving electrons can -under appropriate circumstances- be collectively transferred into fewer, much faster electrons with energies sufficient for the latter to combine with protons (or deuterons, if present) to produce neutrons via weak interactions. The produced neutrons can then initiate low energy nuclear reactions through further nuclear transmutations. The aim of this paper is to extend and enlarge upon various examples analyzed previously, present simplified order of magnitude estimates for each and to illuminate a common unifying theme amongst all of them.

## Key claim

Electromagnetic energy stored collectively in many slow-moving electrons can be transferred to a smaller number of electrons, accelerating them to MeV-range energies sufficient to combine with protons (or deuterons) via weak interactions to produce neutrons, which then initiate low-energy nuclear reactions; the authors present order-of-magnitude estimates to show feasibility, but emphasize that the Born-Oppenheimer approximation must break down in metallic hydride surfaces and that coronal conductivity must be low enough to sustain electric fields.

## Method

The authors derive scaling relations for two collective acceleration mechanisms: (1) an electric mode using surface plasmon polaritons on metallic hydride surfaces, where coherent proton oscillations produce electric fields that renormalize the electron mass to enable the weak capture reaction e⁻ + p → n + νₑ; and (2) a magnetic mode in current-carrying wires and solar flux tubes, where the inductive chemical potential or Faraday-law acceleration provides MeV-to-GeV kinetic energies for electrons. They apply these mechanisms to three systems, palladium hydride cells, exploding wires, and the solar corona, computing order-of-magnitude rates and comparing with experimental observations of neutron production, nuclear transmutations, and muon fluxes.

## Key numbers

- Neutron production threshold: the neutron-proton mass difference is 1.3 MeV/c², requiring W ≥ 0.78 MeV for the reaction e⁻ + p → n + νₑ to be kinematically allowed.
- Electric field scale for neutron production: cℰ/Ω = (mc²/e) ≈ 0.5 × 10⁶ Volts; for metallic hydride surfaces, ℏΩ/e ≈ 5–6 × 10⁻² Volts, requiring ℰ ≈ 2 × 10¹¹ Volts/meter.
- Alfvén current: I₀ = (mc²/e)(4π/R_vac) ≈ 17 KiloAmperes.
- For metallic hydride cells, the estimated weak neutron production rate per unit area is w̃ ≈ (1.2 × 10¹³ /cm² /second) × (β − β₀)², where β = m̃/m and β₀ ≈ 2.53.
- Neutron absorption cross-section on the surface: σ_T ≈ 2 × 10⁸ barns, giving a mean free path Λ ≈ 50 Angstroms.
- Q-value for the lithium cycle: Q[⁶Li + 2n → 2⁴He + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ] ≈ 26.9 MeV; Q[⁴He + 2n → ⁶Li + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ] ≈ 2.95 MeV.
- For exploding wires with I/I₀ ≈ 200 and v/c ≈ 0.25, the chemical potential μ ≈ 25 MeV; the fraction of electrons undergoing weak interactions is about (15 KeV / 25 MeV) = 6 × 10⁻⁴.
- For steady solar flux tubes: W_mag ≈ 15 GeV for R ≈ 10² km, B ≈ 1 Kilogauss, v/c ≈ 10⁻².
- For solar flares: e V̄ ≈ 300 GeV for R ≈ 10⁴ km, B ≈ 1 Kilogauss, Λ ≈ 3 × 10⁷ km, Δt ≈ 10² seconds.
- Observed muon fluxes from the July 14, 2000 solar flare imply protons with energies in excess of 40 GeV; the BAKSAN measurements for the September 29, 1989 flare imply protons in excess of 500 GeV.

## Context

This paper synthesizes and extends a series of prior works by the same authors (Widom and Larsen 2006; Srivastava, Widom, and Larsen 2007–2008) that proposed electro-weak induced LENR in metallic hydride cells, exploding wires, and the solar corona. It provides order-of-magnitude estimates to unify these cases under a common collective electromagnetic acceleration mechanism, contrasting with the standard solar model assumption that all nuclear reactions occur in the core. The work enables predictions for nuclear transmutation products in condensed-matter systems and for high-energy particle production in solar flares, and it explains experimental anomalies such as short-lived isotopes in magnetically active stars (Fowler 1974; Wurz 2005; Cowley 2003; Goriely 2005; Lodders 2003) and muon observations by the L3+C Collaboration (2000) and BAKSAN (1989).
