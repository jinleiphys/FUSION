# Open Questions in Stellar Helium Burning Addressed With Real Photons

> The outcome of helium burning is the formation of the two elements, carbon and oxygen. The ratio of carbon to oxygen at the end of helium burning is crucial for understanding the final fate of a progenitor star and the nucleosynthesis of heavy elements in Type II supernova, with oxygen rich star predicted to collapse to a black hole, and a carbon rich star to a neutron star. Type Ia supernovae (SNeIa) are used as standard candles for measuring cosmological distances with the use of an empirical light curve-luminosity stretching factor. It is essential to understand helium burning that yields the carbon/oxygen white dwarf and thus the initial stage of SNeIa. Since the triple alpha-particle capture reaction, $^{8}Be(α,γ)^{12}C$, the first burning stage in helium burning, is well understood, one must extract the cross section of the $^{12}C(α,γ)^{16}O$ reaction at the Gamow window (300 keV) with high accuracy of approximately 10% or better. This goal has not been achieved despite repeated strong statements that appeared in the literature. In particular constraint from the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of $^{16}N$ were shown to not sufficiently restrict the p-wave cross section factor; e.g. a low value of $S_{E1}(300)$ can not be ruled out. Measurements at low energies, are thus mandatory for determining the elusive cross section factor for the $^{12}C(α,γ)^{16}O$ reaction. We are constructing a Time Projection Chamber (TPC) for use with high intensity photon beams extracted from the HI$γ$S/TUNL facility at Duke University to study the $^{16}O(γ,α)^{12}C$ reaction, and thus the direct reaction at energies as low as 0.7 MeV. This work is in progress.

# Open Questions in Stellar Helium Burning Addressed With Real Photons

## Key claim
The cross section of the $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ reaction at the Gamow peak (300 keV) must be extracted with high accuracy of approximately 10% or better to understand stellar helium burning, but this goal has not been achieved despite repeated strong statements in the literature; in particular, constraints from beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of $^{16}N$ do not sufficiently restrict the p-wave cross section factor, and a low value of $S_{E1}(300)$ cannot be ruled out.

## Method
The author proposes to measure the inverse photodissociation reaction $^{16}O(\gamma,\alpha)^{12}C$ using high-intensity polarized photon beams from the HI$\gamma$S/TUNL facility at Duke University, with a Time Projection Chamber (TPC) for tracking alpha and carbon fragments. The TPC uses an optical readout with a drift chamber, multi-wire proportional counter, and CCD camera to detect back-to-back alpha-carbon tracks, measure angular distributions, and separate E1 and E2 components via the linear polarization of the photon beam.

## Key numbers
- The Gamow peak for helium burning is at 300 keV, where the $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ cross section must be known to approximately 10% or better.
- The photodissociation cross section of $^{16}O(\gamma,\alpha)^{12}C$ is larger by a factor of approximately 100 than the direct $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ reaction cross section; for the lowest measured direct data point at 0.9 MeV with a cross section of approximately 60 pb, the photodissociation cross section is 6 nb.
- The proposed experiment aims to measure center-of-mass energies as low as 0.7 MeV, where the direct reaction cross section is of the order of 1 pb.
- With a 30 cm fiducial length target of 30% CO2 at 76 torr (100 mbar) and a photon beam of $2 \times 10^9$ /sec, the luminosity is $10^{29}$ sec$^{-1}$cm$^{-2}$, yielding an integrated luminosity of 10 nb$^{-1}$ per day.
- At the HI$\gamma$S facility, for 9 MeV gammas the expected energy resolution is 0.1% and intensity of order $10^9$ /sec; currently achieved intensities are $10^8$ /sec with energy resolution of approximately 0.5%.
- The Q value for $^{16}O(\gamma,\alpha)^{12}C$ is -7.162 MeV, so the experiment uses gammas of energies approximately 8 to 10 MeV.
- The TPC provides energy resolution of approximately 2% and angular resolution better than two degrees.
- The pulsed photon beam has 0.1 ns pulses every 180 nsec with at most 500 gammas per pulse.
- The R-matrix analysis of world data including $^{16}N$ beta decay was found to not rule out a small S-factor solution of 10-20 keV-b.

## Context
This work addresses the long-standing problem of determining the $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ reaction rate, which is critical for predicting whether a progenitor star collapses to a black hole (oxygen-rich) or a neutron star (carbon-rich), and for understanding the initial stage of Type Ia supernovae used as cosmological standard candles. The author notes that prior claims of constraining the p-wave cross section factor from beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of $^{16}N$ (Yale data from Zhao (1993) and improved by Frawley (1996); TRIUMF data from Azuma (1994); Seattle data from Zhao (1995)) were found to be insufficient, and a recent measurement at lower energies by Hammer (2001) suggests a d-wave cross section factor at least twice larger than the accepted value. The proposed photodissociation experiment with a TPC at HI$\gamma$S/TUNL complements a virtual photon experiment at MIT-Bates (proposed by Genya) that extracts only the d-wave component, and is similar to a research program with high-intensity photon beams and a TPC at RCNP in Osaka, Japan (Shima).
