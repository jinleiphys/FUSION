# Features of the IP-Glasma

> We discuss differences between the IP-Glasma model and typical wounded-nucleon model like initial conditions. We point out that the IP-Glasma initial state is more compact in the transverse plane and produces a significant initial flow, both of which contribute to an increased radial flow in the subsequent hydrodynamic evolution. A larger bulk viscosity, compared to calculations that use other initial state models, is required to compensate for these effects and find agreement with experimental data. We further demonstrate the importance of the initial momentum anisotropy of the glasma for anisotropy measures in small collision systems such as p+Pb.

## Key claim
The IP-Glasma initial state produces more radial flow than typical wounded-nucleon models because it is more compact in the transverse plane and generates significant initial flow from the Yang-Mills stage; this requires a larger bulk viscosity to entropy density ratio to match experimental data, and the initial momentum anisotropy of the glasma contributes substantially to final-state anisotropy in small collision systems like p+Pb.

## Method
The authors compare IP-Glasma initial conditions (classical Yang-Mills) with Monte-Carlo Glauber type initial conditions by performing viscous relativistic hydrodynamic simulations with a hadron cascade. They quantify the effects of initial geometry compactness by smearing the IP-Glasma energy density with Gaussians of width 1 fm to increase the rms radius, and separately turn off the initial flow by setting the flow velocity \(u^\mu\) to zero. For small systems, they compute the momentum anisotropy \(\epsilon_p\) from the energy-momentum tensor in both the Yang-Mills and hydrodynamic stages, evaluating the relative contribution of initial-state anisotropy at different switching times (0.2 fm and 0.6 fm).

## Key numbers
- Smearing the IP-Glasma energy density with a Gaussian of width 1 fm increases the rms radius in 30-40% central Pb+Pb collisions by approximately 15%, to a value comparable to MC-Glauber initial conditions with \(\sigma=0.4\) fm.
- The mean transverse momentum \(\langle p_T \rangle\) is reduced by approximately 6.5% when performing this smearing.
- Turning off the initial flow from the IP-Glasma (setting \(u^\mu\) to zero) leads to another \(\sim\)10% reduction of \(\langle p_T \rangle\).
- For p+Pb collisions with \(\langle dN_{\rm ch}/d\eta\rangle \approx 15\) (corresponding to 80-90% central Pb+Pb), the initial state contributes 45% of the final momentum anisotropy when switching at 0.6 fm and 35% when switching at 0.2 fm.
- Without bulk viscosity, the initial state contribution to the final anisotropy increases to approximately 70%.

## Context
This work extends the IP-Glasma + viscous hydrodynamics framework previously applied to Pb+Pb and Au+Au collisions (Schenke 2012, Gale 2012, Schenke 2014) into smaller collision systems such as p+Pb. It contrasts with MC-Glauber type initial conditions (Bozek 2011, Weller 2017, Bernhard 2018) which require less bulk viscosity, and quantifies the initial-state momentum anisotropy contribution analyzed in a Yang-Mills + parton cascade framework by Greif (2017). The results enable more precise extraction of transport coefficients (particularly bulk viscosity) from Bayesian analyses that constrain initial geometry compactness (Bernhard 2016, Bernhard 2018), and motivate inclusion of a more sophisticated intermediate evolution stage (Kurkela 2018) to reduce matching-time uncertainties.
