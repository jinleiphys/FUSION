# Neutron star heating vs. HST observations

> Passively cooling neutron stars (NSs) should reach undetectably low surface temperatures $T_s<10^4$ K in less than $10^7$ yr. However, HST observations have revealed likely thermal UV emission from the Gyr-old millisecond pulsars PSR~J0437$-$4715 and PSR~J2124$-$3358, and from the $\sim10^{7-8}$ yr-old classical pulsars PSR~B0950$+$08 and PSR~J0108$-$1431, implying $T_s\sim10^5$ K and the need for heating mechanisms. We compute the thermal evolution of these NSs including rotochemical heating (RH) in the core with normal or Cooper-paired matter, vortex creep (VC) in the inner crust, and crustal heating through nuclear reactions, and compare the results with observations and with the upper limit for PSR~2144$-$3933. No single mechanism explains all sources. The high temperature of PSR~J0437$-$4715 can be reproduced by RH with a large Cooper pairing gap $Δ_i\sim1.5$ MeV for either neutrons or protons, but this requires an unrealistically short initial period $P_0\lesssim1.8$ ms to activate the same mechanism in PSR~B0950$+$08. Conversely, the latter can be explained by RH with modified Urca reactions in normal matter or by VC with an excess angular momentum $J\sim3\times10^{43}$ erg,s, but these models underpredict PSR~J0437$-$4715. A model combining RH with a large pairing gap and VC matches both pulsars and is consistent with the upper limits for the remaining three. It further predicts that their temperatures should lie close to these limits, suggesting that deeper or broader-wavelength observations would provide a strong test of this scenario.

# Neutron star heating vs. HST observations

## Key claim

No single heating mechanism (rotochemical heating, vortex creep, or crustal nuclear reactions) can explain the observed thermal UV emission from all five old pulsars; however, a combined model including rotochemical heating with a large Cooper pairing gap and vortex creep simultaneously matches the measured temperatures of PSR J0437-4715 and PSR B0950+08 while remaining consistent with the upper limits for the other three pulsars, with the prediction that those three should have temperatures near the current detection thresholds.

## Method

The authors computed the thermal evolution of five neutron stars (PSR J0437-4715, PSR J2124-3358, PSR B0950+08, PSR J0108-1431, and PSR J2144-3933) by solving the energy balance equation for the redshifted core temperature, incorporating rotochemical heating in the core (with both normal matter and Cooper-paired matter assuming isotropic gaps), vortex creep in the inner crust, and crustal heating through nuclear reactions. They compared the resulting surface temperatures against HST observations, using two neutron star models: Model M (1.44 solar masses, allowing only modified Urca reactions) and Model D (1.76 solar masses, allowing direct Urca reactions), with parameter values derived from the equations of state A18+δv+UIX* and BPAL32 respectively.

## Key numbers

- PSR J0437-4715: measured redshifted surface temperature T_s^∞ = (2.36^{+0.15}_{-0.14}) × 10^5 K, from hydrogen atmosphere model fits to HST STIS and ROSAT data
- PSR J2124-3358: upper limit T_s^∞ < 4.9 × 10^5 K (converted from blackbody fit to hydrogen atmosphere)
- PSR B0950+08: measured T_s^∞ = 0.6-1.2 × 10^5 K
- PSR J0108-1431: upper limit T_s^∞ < 0.6 × 10^5 K
- PSR J2144-3933: upper limit T_s^∞ < 0.3 × 10^5 K
- Rotochemical heating with Cooper pairing gap Δ_i ~ 1.5 MeV can explain PSR J0437-4715 but requires initial period P_0 ≲ 1.8 ms to also activate in PSR B0950+08
- Vortex creep model requires excess angular momentum J ~ 3 × 10^43 erg·s to explain PSR B0950+08
- For PSR J0437-4715 in quasi-steady state: inferred chemical imbalance parameter η^∞_qs ≈ 1.5 MeV, requiring initial period P_0 ≲ 1.6 ms (consistent with white dwarf companion age of 6.0 ± 0.5 Gyr)
- PSR J0437-4715 mass: M = 1.418 ± 0.044 M_⊙ from radio timing; coordinate radius R = 11.36^{+0.95}_{-0.63} km from NICER

## Context

This work extends the analysis of González and Reisenegger (2010), who previously considered rotochemical heating and vortex creep for PSR J0437-4715 alone, by now including four additional pulsars observed with HST and incorporating theoretical advances in rotochemical heating with Cooper pairing from Petrovich and Reisenegger (2010) and González-Jiménez et al. (2015). The study contradicts the expectation from standard passive cooling models that neutron stars older than ~10^7 yr should have undetectable surface temperatures below 10^4 K. The results enable future observational tests: deeper or broader-wavelength HST observations of PSR J2124-3358, PSR J0108-1431, and PSR J2144-3933 could confirm or rule out the combined heating scenario by testing whether their temperatures lie close to the current upper limits.
