# Historical perspective and future prospects for nuclear interactions

> The nuclear force is the heart of nuclear physics and, thus, the significance of this force for all of nuclear physics can hardly be overstated. Research on this crucial force has by now spanned eight decades and we are still not done. I will first review the rich history of hope and desperation, which had spin-off far beyond just nuclear physics. Next, I will present the current status in the field which is charcterized by the application of an effective field theory (EFT) that is believed to represent QCD in the low energy regime typical for nuclear physics. During the past two decades, this EFT has become the favorite vehicle to derive nuclear two- and many-body forces. Finally, I will take a look into the future: What developments can we expect from the next decades? Will the 30-year cycles of new and "better" ideas for efficiently describing nuclear forces go on for ever, or is there hope for closure?

## Key claim

The paper argues that chiral effective field theory (chiral EFT) currently provides the most fundamental and systematic framework for deriving nuclear forces from low-energy QCD, but it acknowledges that this approach may represent just another 30-year cycle in a historical pattern, leaving open the question of whether "closure" on a definitive theory of nuclear interactions is achievable.

## Method

The author reviews the four historical phases of nuclear force research: early pion theories (1930–1960), one-boson-exchange (OBE) meson models (1960–1990), chiral effective field theory (1990–2020), and a projected future phase (2020–2050). For the current chiral EFT phase, the method involves constructing the most general Lagrangian consistent with the broken chiral symmetry of low-energy QCD, expanding in powers of \(Q/\Lambda_\chi\) where \(Q \sim m_\pi\) is the soft scale and \(\Lambda_\chi \sim m_\rho\) is the hard scale, and organizing contributions order by order using the heavy-baryon formalism.

## Key numbers

* The pion mass is approximately 138 MeV, identified as the soft scale \(Q \sim m_\pi\) for the chiral expansion.
* The rho meson mass is approximately 770 MeV, setting the hard scale \(\Lambda_\chi \sim m_\rho\) for the chiral expansion.
* The omega meson mass is 783 MeV, and the scalar-isoscalar \(f_0(500)\) or \(\sigma\) boson has a mass around 500 MeV, both used in OBE models.
* The up and down quark masses are \(m_u = 2.3 \pm 0.7\) MeV and \(m_d = 4.8 \pm 0.5\) MeV (in an \(\overline{MS}\) scheme at \(\mu \approx 2\) GeV), which are small compared to the hadronic scale of \(\sim 1\) GeV.
* The pion-nucleon coupling constant is \(g^2_\pi/4\pi \approx 14\), which made perturbation theory in early meson theories useless.
* A lattice QCD study (Org15) investigated the nucleon-nucleon system at a pion mass of about 450 MeV, finding phase shifts in the \(^1S_0\) and \(^3S-D_1\) channels similar to those in nature.

## Context

This paper extends the historical narrative of nuclear force research from Yukawa (1935) through the OBE models of the 1960s–1990s (e.g., Bryan-Scott 1964, Bonn Full Model MHE87, Paris potential Lac80, CD-Bonn Mac01) to the modern chiral EFT framework pioneered by Weinberg (1979, 1990, 1991). It contrasts the meson-exchange phenomenology of Phase II with the more fundamental QCD-based approach of Phase III, and it points to lattice QCD calculations (Org15, Hat12) as a promising but computationally costly future direction that may eventually provide a direct solution from QCD.
