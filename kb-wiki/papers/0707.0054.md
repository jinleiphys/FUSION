# Mach Cones at central LHC Collisions via MACE

> 3 pages, 2 figures. Contributed to the Workshop on Heavy Ion Collisions at the LHC: Last Call for Predictions, Geneva, Switzerland, 14 May - 8 Jun 2007. Added three-particle correlation figure

## Key claim

Mach cones produced by jets in central PbPb collisions at LHC will produce a double-peaked two-particle correlation function, but the peaks will be further apart than the true Mach angle, making the extracted speed of sound appear smaller than its actual value in the medium.

## Method

The authors used the MACE (Mach Cones Evolution) model to simulate sound wave propagation through a medium created by the hydrodynamical Particle-in-Cell (PIC) method, assuming a massless ideal gas equation of state. Sound waves were propagated independently of the medium evolution, using only the velocity field from PIC, and the shape of the region affected by sound waves was evaluated to recognize collective phenomena. Two- and three-particle correlation functions were calculated for central PbPb collisions at LHC energy.

## Key numbers

- Central PbPb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.5\) TeV were simulated.
- For a massless ideal gas equation of state, the speed of sound is \(c_{\rm S} = 1/\sqrt{3}\) and \(\cos^{-1} c_{\rm S} = 0.96\).
- The minimum-bias two-particle correlation function shows peaks at \(\Delta \varphi \approx \pi \pm 1.2\), corresponding to an apparent speed of sound of \(c_{\rm S} \approx 0.36\).
- Only jets originating from the middle of the reaction produce a symmetric correlation function with peaks at the true Mach angle \(\Delta \varphi = \pi \pm 0.96\).

## Context

This work extends earlier findings of Mach-cone-like structures in three-particle correlations at RHIC (Ulery 2007) and builds on the MACE model introduced by Baeuchle (2007). It provides predictions for how Mach cone signals will appear at LHC energies, showing that the apparent speed of sound extracted from two-particle correlations will be systematically lower than the true value unless jets originate from the center of the medium. This enables experimentalists at LHC to interpret correlation measurements correctly and to identify the conditions under which the true speed of sound can be extracted.
