# Beta decays in investigations and searches for rare effects

> Current status of experimental investigations of rare single beta decays (48Ca, 50V, 96Zr, 113Cd, 113mCd, 115In, 123Te, 180mTa, 222Rn) is reviewed. Nuclei which decay through single beta decay very often constitute backgrounds in studies of rare effects like double beta decay, solar neutrinos or dark matter. Summary of correction factors used in description of forbidden beta decays is also briefly given.

# Beta Decays in Investigations and Searches for Rare Effects

## Key claim
The paper reviews the current experimental status of rare single beta decays in nine specific nuclei, emphasizing that many of these decays are poorly studied or unobserved, and that their spectral shapes are often not well measured, which creates significant backgrounds in searches for double beta decay, solar neutrinos, and dark matter.

## Method
The author classifies beta decays by change in spin and parity (allowed, forbidden non-unique, forbidden unique) and summarizes the empirical correction factors used to describe forbidden beta spectra, including polynomial forms in energy and momentum. For each of the nine nuclei (48Ca, 50V, 96Zr, 113Cd, 113mCd, 115In, 123Te, 180mTa, 222Rn), the paper compiles the most recent experimental half-life limits or measurements, the observed or predicted decay transitions, and the status of spectral shape measurements.

## Key numbers
- 48Ca: Best experimental limit for single beta decay is T1/2 > 2.5×10^20 y; theoretical estimate for transition to 131 keV level is T1/2 = (2.6–7.0)×10^20 y.
- 50V: Electron capture to 50Ti measured with T1/2 = (2.3±0.3)×10^17 y; limit for beta decay to 50Cr is T1/2 > 1.7×10^18 y.
- 96Zr: Limit for single beta decay to 44 keV excited level is T1/2 > 3.8×10^19 y.
- 113Cd: Shape of beta spectrum (ΔJΔπ = 4+, 4-fold forbidden non-unique) precisely measured in Bel07, with coefficients c_i in correction factor C(E) = P^6 + c1 P^4 Q^2 + c2 P^2 Q^4 + c3 Q^6.
- 115In → 115Sn*: Lowest known Qβ value of only 155±24 eV (from Mou09); remeasured as 173±12 eV (Urb16) and 148±10 eV (Zhe17).
- 123Te: Only limit set as T1/2 > 5.0×10^19 y (Ale03); earlier claimed value of (2.4±0.9)×10^19 y (Ale96) was found incorrect.
- 180mTa: Best limits from Leh17: T1/2(EC) > 2.0×10^17 y and T1/2(β−) > 5.8×10^16 y.
- 222Rn: Qβ = 24±21 keV; estimated half-life range 6.7×10^4 – 2.4×10^8 y; experimental limit T1/2(β) > 8.0 y (Bel14b).

## Context
This review extends the earlier compilations of experimental correction factors found in Paul (1966), Daniel (1968), Behrens (1976), and Mougeot (2016). It highlights that many rare beta decays (e.g., 50V, 123Te, 180mTa) remain unobserved or poorly measured, and that improved knowledge of their spectral shapes is critical for background modeling in double beta decay experiments (e.g., GERDA, using 39Ar and 42Ar/42K) and dark matter searches (e.g., DarkSide, using 39Ar and 42Ar/42K). The paper also notes a new approach by Haaranen (2016, 2017) and Kostensalo (2017) to extract the weak coupling constants gA and gV from non-unique forbidden beta decays, which could reduce uncertainties in double beta decay half-life predictions.
