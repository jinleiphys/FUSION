# Derivation of transient relativistic fluid dynamics from the Boltzmann equation

> In this work we present a general derivation of relativistic fluid dynamics from the Boltzmann equation using the method of moments. The main difference between our approach and the traditional 14-moment approximation is that we will not close the fluid-dynamical equations of motion by truncating the expansion of the distribution function. Instead, we keep all terms in the moment expansion. The reduction of the degrees of freedom is done by identifying the microscopic time scales of the Boltzmann equation and considering only the slowest ones. In addition, the equations of motion for the dissipative quantities are truncated according to a systematic power-counting scheme in Knudsen and inverse Reynolds number. We conclude that the equations of motion can be closed in terms of only 14 dynamical variables, as long as we only keep terms of second order in Knudsen and/or inverse Reynolds number. We show that, even though the equations of motion are closed in terms of these 14 fields, the transport coefficients carry information about all the moments of the distribution function. In this way, we can show that the particle-diffusion and shear-viscosity coefficients agree with the values given by the Chapman-Enskog expansion.

## Key claim

The authors derive transient relativistic fluid dynamics from the Boltzmann equation using a method-of-moments approach that keeps all terms in the moment expansion, rather than truncating the distribution function expansion as in the traditional 14-moment approximation. The reduction to 14 dynamical variables is achieved by identifying the slowest microscopic time scales and truncating the equations of motion according to a systematic power-counting scheme in Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers, keeping only terms up to second order in these parameters.

## Method

The single-particle distribution function is expanded around local equilibrium using a complete, orthogonal basis of irreducible tensors formed from particle 4-momentum, with the expansion coefficients expressed as irreducible moments of the deviation from equilibrium. Exact equations of motion for these moments are derived from the Boltzmann equation, and the linearized collision operator is expressed in terms of the moments. The infinite set of moment equations is reduced by diagonalizing the linear part to identify the slowest microscopic time scales for each dissipative current (bulk viscous pressure, particle-diffusion current, and shear-stress tensor), then approximating faster modes by their asymptotic long-time solution, yielding closed fluid-dynamical equations for the 14 fields (particle density, energy density, fluid velocity, bulk pressure, diffusion current, and shear stress) with transport coefficients that carry information from all moments.

## Key numbers

- The equations of motion are closed in terms of 14 dynamical variables when keeping terms up to second order in Knudsen number and/or inverse Reynolds number, i.e., O(Kn², R_i⁻¹R_j⁻¹, Kn R_i⁻¹).
- The inverse Reynolds numbers are defined as R_Π⁻¹ ≡ |Π|/P₀, R_n⁻¹ ≡ |n^μ|/n₀, and R_π⁻¹ ≡ |π^μν|/P₀, where P₀ and n₀ are the equilibrium pressure and particle density.
- For a massless classical gas, the calculated heat-conductivity and shear-viscosity coefficients agree with the values from Chapman-Enskog theory (DeGroot, 1980).
- The authors explicitly study extensions of the moment basis to 14 + 9 × n moments for n = 1, 2, and 3.

## Context

This work extends the method of moments beyond the traditional 14-moment approximation of Israel and Stewart (1979), which lacked a systematic power-counting parameter and was shown to be incomplete by neglecting infinitely many terms of first order in Knudsen number. The approach builds on earlier attempts to improve Israel-Stewart theory by Muronga (2006), Denicol, Koide, and Rischke (2010), and Betz, Henkel, and Rischke (2008), but demonstrates that those theories were also incomplete. The derived framework enables systematic improvement of relativistic fluid dynamics for applications in heavy-ion collisions and astrophysics, with transport coefficients that can be successively refined by including more moments.
