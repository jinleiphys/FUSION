# Introduction to Hydrodynamics

> We give a pedagogical review of relativistic hydrodynamics relevant to relativistic heavy ion collisions. Topics discussed include linear response theory derivation of 2nd order viscous hydrodynamics including the Kubo formulas, kinetic theory derivation of 2nd order viscous hydrodynamics, anisotropic hydrodynamics and a brief review of numerical algorithms. Emphasis is given to the theory of hydrodynamics rather than phenomenology.

## Key claim

This pedagogical review argues that relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics can be systematically derived from linear response theory and conservation laws alone, without assuming a specific microscopic model, and that the resulting second-order viscous equations are necessary to describe the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma created in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions.

## Method

The authors derive second-order viscous hydrodynamics using linear response theory of conserved currents, obtaining Kubo formulas that relate transport coefficients to equilibrium correlation functions. They also present a kinetic theory derivation of second-order viscous hydrodynamics through coarse-graining of the Boltzmann equation, and discuss anisotropic hydrodynamics and numerical algorithms for solving the relativistic viscous hydrodynamic equations.

## Key numbers

- QGP properties approach about 80% of the Stefan-Boltzmann limit on lattice around 2T_c (where T_c is the critical temperature)
- The shear viscosity has a non-zero lower bound in the infinite coupling limit, as calculated using string theory techniques by Kovtun, Son, and Starinets (2004)
- The diffusion constant D is defined as D = D_R(0,0) from the small-ω, small-k limit of the retarded response function
- Shear viscosity η = D_T (ε + P), where D_T is the momentum diffusion constant and ε + P is the enthalpy density

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Landau (1953) on relativistic hydrodynamics in high-energy physics and builds on the discovery that QGP at RHIC behaves as the most strongly coupled fluid ever observed, contradicting earlier expectations of weakly coupled plasma behavior. The authors reference the Kovtun, Son, and Starinets (2004) string theory calculation of the shear viscosity lower bound, and subsequent hydrodynamic calculations by Song and Heinz (2007, 2008) and Schenke (2010) that demonstrated the importance of finite shear viscosity in describing RHIC flow data. The review enables further theoretical work on small-system collectivity observed in high-multiplicity proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions, as reported by Aad (2012), the CMS Collaboration (2012), Abelev (2012), and Adare (2013).
