# The nuclear force in the third millennium

> I will review recent progress in our understanding of the nuclear force. In the course of the 1990's, so-called high-precision, charge-dependent nucleon-nucleon potentials have been constructed which are, essentially, phenomenological models. These potentials are now commonly used as input for exact few-body calculations and microscopic nuclear many-body theory. I will critically analyses those models and point out their strenghts and weaknesses. Particular emphasis will be on charge dependence. Other recent research was conducted on a more basic level: understanding the nuclear force in terms of the fundamental theory of strong interactions, QCD. Predictions from this sector are typically qualitative in nature. Thus, the main problem of the current status in the field is that quantitative models for the nuclear force have a poor theoretical background, while theory based models yield poor results. The chief challenge for the new millenium is to overcome this discrepancy. Chiral effective field theory may be a suitable tool to solve the problem.

# The nuclear force in the third millennium

## Key claim

The paper argues that the current status of nuclear force research suffers from a fundamental discrepancy: quantitative phenomenological models have a poor theoretical background, while theory-based models derived from QCD yield only poor quantitative results. The author claims that chiral effective field theory may be a suitable tool to overcome this discrepancy, but this remains a future challenge rather than an accomplished solution.

## Method

The author reviews the construction and performance of high-precision, charge-dependent nucleon-nucleon potentials developed in the 1990s (Nijmegen-I, Nijmegen-II, Reid93, Argonne V18, and CD-Bonn), which fit the pruned Nijmegen 1992 database with about 45 parameters each. The paper then evaluates these potentials against new post-1992 pp data from IUCF, examines their extrapolation to higher energies, and analyzes their treatment of charge dependence and charge symmetry breaking. Finally, the author discusses chiral perturbation theory as a potential framework for deriving nuclear forces from QCD.

## Key numbers

- The Nijmegen phase shift analysis achieved a chi-squared per datum of 0.99 for the combined pp and np database available in 1993 (1787 pp data and 2514 np data below 350 MeV)
- The after-1992 pp database consists of 1145 new data points (below 350 MeV), increasing the total pp database to 2932 data points
- For the after-1992 pp data, the Argonne V18 potential produces a chi-squared per datum of 1.74, while CD-Bonn produces 1.03
- CD-Bonn predicts a deuteron D-state probability of 4.85 percent, while local potentials yield 5.7 plus or minus 0.1 percent
- CD-Bonn predicts 8.00 MeV for the triton binding energy in a nonrelativistic calculation, while local potentials predict 7.62 MeV
- The empirical charge symmetry breaking splitting of the singlet scattering length is 1.6 plus or minus 0.6 fm
- The empirical charge independence breaking splitting of the singlet scattering length is 5.7 plus or minus 0.3 fm
- One-pion-exchange accounts for about 50 percent of the empirical CIB splitting of the singlet scattering length
- Two-boson exchange contributes about 1.3 fm to the CIB splitting of the singlet scattering length
- The CD-Bonn potential predicts a triton-helium-3 binding energy difference of 60 keV

## Context

This review paper extends the line of work on high-precision NN potentials by the Nijmegen group (Stoks et al., 1993), the Argonne group (Wiringa et al., 1995), and the Bonn group (Machleidt et al., 1996). It critically evaluates these potentials against new IUCF data and identifies deficiencies in their treatment of charge dependence. The paper contrasts this phenomenological approach with QCD-inspired models such as Skyrmion models and constituent quark cluster models, which have produced only qualitative predictions. The author points to chiral perturbation theory, following the program initiated by Weinberg (1990, 1992), Ordonez (1992), and van Kolck (1993, 1994, 1999), as a promising direction for deriving nuclear forces that are both theoretically sound and quantitatively accurate. The work of Epelbaum, Gloeckle, and Meissner (1998, 2000) is highlighted as particularly promising for developing chiral NN potentials up to next-to-next-to-leading order.
