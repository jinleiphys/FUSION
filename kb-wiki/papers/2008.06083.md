# Statistical interpretation of sterile neutrino oscillation searches at reactors

> A considerable experimental effort is currently under way to test the persistent hints for oscillations due to an eV-scale sterile neutrino in the data of various reactor neutrino experiments. The assessment of the statistical significance of these hints is usually based on Wilks' theorem, whereby the assumption is made that the log-likelihood is $χ^2$-distributed. However, it is well known that the preconditions for the validity of Wilks' theorem are not fulfilled for neutrino oscillation experiments. In this work we derive a simple asymptotic form of the actual distribution of the log-likelihood based on reinterpreting the problem as fitting white Gaussian noise. From this formalism we show that, even in the absence of a sterile neutrino, the expectation value for the maximum likelihood estimate of the mixing angle remains non-zero with attendant large values of the log-likelihood. Our analytical results are then confirmed by numerical simulations of a toy reactor experiment. Finally, we apply this framework to the data of the Neutrino-4 experiment and show that the null hypothesis of no-oscillation is rejected at the 2.6\,$σ$ level, compared to 3.2\,$σ$ obtained under the assumption that Wilks' theorem applies.

## Key claim

The authors show that, even in the absence of a sterile neutrino, the expectation value for the maximum likelihood estimate of the mixing angle remains non-zero with attendant large values of the log-likelihood, and that the statistical significance of oscillation hints is reduced when the correct distribution of the test statistic is used instead of Wilks' theorem. For the Neutrino-4 experiment, the null hypothesis of no-oscillation is rejected at the 2.6 sigma level, compared to 3.2 sigma obtained under the assumption that Wilks' theorem applies.

## Method

The authors derive an asymptotic form of the log-likelihood distribution by reinterpreting the sterile neutrino search as fitting white Gaussian noise with a cosine function, where the test statistic square-root follows the distribution of the maximum of N standard normal variables. They confirm this analytical result with numerical simulations of a toy reactor experiment using a binned chi-squared analysis with 43 energy bins, 1.5e4 total events, a baseline of 10 m, and a Gaussian energy resolution of 0.03 sqrt(E/MeV). They then apply the framework to the Neutrino-4 experiment data using explicit Neyman-Pearson construction based on the Feldman-Cousins prescription.

## Key numbers

- For the toy reactor experiment with 1.5e4 events, the distribution of the test statistic T agrees with a max. Gauss distribution for N=45 effective bins, rather than a chi-squared distribution with 2 degrees of freedom.
- The expected value of sqrt(T) is approximately 2 to 2.4 for N approximately 30 to 60, leading to an expected best-fit value of sin^2(2theta) approximately 6.2 / sqrt(N_total), where N_total is the total number of observed events.
- For the Neutrino-4 experiment, the null hypothesis of no-oscillation is rejected at the 2.6 sigma level, compared to 3.2 sigma obtained under Wilks' theorem.
- For T = 9.21, the max. Gauss distribution (N=45) gives a p-value of 5.27% (1.94 sigma), while a chi-squared distribution with 2 DOF gives a p-value of 1.00% (2.58 sigma).
- For T = 11.83, the max. Gauss distribution gives a p-value of 1.31% (2.48 sigma), while chi-squared with 2 DOF gives 0.27% (3.00 sigma).

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Agostini (2019), Giunti (2020), and Almazan (2020) who showed via Monte Carlo simulations that corrections to Wilks' theorem reduce the statistical significance of sterile neutrino hints in reactor data. The authors provide analytical arguments for the expected distribution of the test statistic, going beyond purely numerical approaches. The results enable more reliable statistical assessments for current short-baseline reactor experiments such as Neutrino-4, PROSPECT, STEREO, and DANSS, and imply that explicit Monte Carlo simulations are necessary for proper significance evaluation rather than relying on chi-squared distributions.
