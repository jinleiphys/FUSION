# Formation and dynamics of fission fragments

> Although the overall time-scale for nuclear fission is long, suggesting a slow process, rapid shape evolution occurs in its later stages near scission. Theoretical prediction of the fission fragments and their characteristics are often based on the assumption that the internal degrees of freedom are equilibrated along the fission path. However, this adiabatic approximation may break down near scission. This is studied for the symmetric fission of $^{258,264}$Fm. The non-adiabatic evolution is computed using the time-dependent Hartree-Fock method, starting from an adiabatic configuration where the fragments have acquired their identity. It is shown that dynamics has an important effect on the kinetic and excitation energies of the fragments. The vibrational modes of the fragments in the post-scission evolution are also analyzed.

## Key claim

The adiabatic approximation, which assumes internal degrees of freedom are fully equilibrated along the fission path, breaks down near scission; non-adiabatic effects computed with the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method significantly affect the kinetic and excitation energies of fission fragments, and the scission point itself differs when non-adiabatic effects are included.

## Method

The authors used a two-stage approach: first, an adiabatic phase described by static Hartree-Fock with BCS pairing (HF+BCS) using the SLy4d Skyrme energy density functional on a Cartesian grid with 0.8 fm mesh, with an external constraint to induce elongation along the z-axis. Second, a non-adiabatic phase computed with the TDHF method starting from the adiabatic configuration where fragments have established their identity (determined by vanishing pairing energies and appearance of magic shell gaps at Z=50 and N=82), evolving through scission and post-scission dynamics. The transition between adiabatic and non-adiabatic regimes occurs at R0 ≈ 10.5 fm for 264Fm, where all pairing energies vanish.

## Key numbers

- Fission barrier height for 264Fm: VB ≈ 4.3 MeV at RB ≈ 7.3 fm
- Scission occurs after approximately 1.6 zs of non-adiabatic evolution
- Kinetic energy acquired by fragments at neck breaking (t ≈ 1.6 zs): Ekin ≈ 19 MeV
- Total kinetic energy (TKE) for 264Fm: approximately 241 MeV (compared to Viola systematics prediction of approximately 192 MeV)
- Excitation energy acquired during adiabatic evolution: E*_adiab ≈ 12 MeV
- Excitation energy acquired during non-adiabatic phase: E*_TDHF ≈ 22 MeV
- Total excitation energy: E* ≈ 34 MeV (non-adiabatic evolution responsible for approximately 2/3 of final excitation energy)
- For 258Fm: calculated TKE in range 238-241 MeV, in relatively good agreement with experimental high-energy mode (TKE ≈ 232 MeV) from Hulst (1986)

## Context

This work extends previous adiabatic fission models (such as the time-dependent generator coordinate method by Goutte (2005) and random walk approaches by Moller (2001) and Randrup (2011)) by incorporating non-adiabatic dynamics near scission using TDHF, which had previously been limited to qualitative studies (Negele, 1978). The approach enables prediction of fragment kinetic and excitation energies beyond the sudden approximation, and analysis of post-scission vibrational modes of fragments, including low-lying collective vibrations (2+1 and 3-1 states) and giant resonances, which can be compared with experimental data on fission fragment properties and neutron emission.
