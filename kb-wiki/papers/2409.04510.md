# Entropy-driven entanglement forging

> Simulating physical systems with variational quantum algorithms is a well-studied approach, but it is challenging to implement in current devices due to demands in qubit number and circuit depth. We show how limited knowledge of the system, namely the entropy of its subsystems, its entanglement structure or certain symmetries, can be used to reduce the cost of these algorithms with entanglement forging. To do so, we simulate a Fermi-Hubbard one-dimensional chain with a parametrized hopping term, as well as atomic nuclei ${}^{28}$Ne and ${}^{60}$Ti with the nuclear shell model. Using an adaptive variational quantum eigensolver we find significant reductions in both the maximum number of qubits (up to one fourth) and the amount of two-qubit gates (over an order of magnitude) required in the quantum circuits. Our findings indicate that our method, entropy-driven entanglement forging, can be used to adjust quantum simulations to the limitations of noisy intermediate-scale quantum devices.

## Key claim

The paper shows that limited knowledge of a quantum system's entanglement structure, specifically the entropy of its subsystems, can be used to reduce the number of qubits (up to one fourth) and two-qubit gates (over an order of magnitude) required in variational quantum simulations, but the method's efficiency depends on identifying bipartitions with low entanglement and the quality of the approximation is controlled by a cutoff on the number of Schmidt coefficients retained.

## Method

The authors implement Entropy-Driven Entanglement Forging (EDEF) within an Adaptive Derivative-Assembled Pseudo-Trotter ansatz Variational Quantum Eigensolver (ADAPT-VQE) for two systems: a one-dimensional Fermi-Hubbard chain with a tunable central hopping term, and atomic nuclei 28Ne and 60Ti described by the nuclear shell model. For each system, they identify low-entropy bipartitions (left-right halves for the Fermi-Hubbard model; proton-neutron separation for nuclei), compute the Schmidt decomposition of the ground state, and truncate the decomposition to a small number of product states (5 for Fermi-Hubbard, 6 for nuclei) that are then prepared by separate smaller quantum circuits and combined with optimized coefficients.

## Key numbers

- For the Fermi-Hubbard model with 4 sites, U=t, and half-filling, a cutoff of 5 product states achieves infidelity below 10% across all values of the central hopping tm.
- For the Fermi-Hubbard model at tm=t (the regular case), a 1% infidelity is reached with a cutoff of 5 product states.
- For 28Ne, the seventh Schmidt coefficient squared (lambda_7^2) is approximately 10^-4, two orders of magnitude smaller than for 20Ne where lambda_7^2 is approximately 10^-2.
- For 60Ti, the Schmidt coefficients beyond the first six are similarly suppressed compared to 44Ti.
- The maximum number of qubits is reduced by up to one fourth, and the number of two-qubit gates is reduced by over an order of magnitude.

## Context

This work extends the entanglement forging approach introduced by Eddins et al. (2022) by replacing the need for a generative neural network (as used in PhysRevResearch.6.023021) with physically-motivated guidance from subsystem entropy and symmetries. The method builds on prior findings that proton-neutron entanglement is low in atomic nuclei (Johnson et al., 2023; Perez-Obiol et al., 2023) and that the nuclear shell model can be simulated with ADAPT-VQE (Romero et al., 2022; Perez-Marquez et al., 2023). The results enable quantum simulations of larger many-body systems on noisy intermediate-scale quantum devices by reducing circuit requirements.
