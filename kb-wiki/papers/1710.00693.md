# Beth-Uhlenbeck approach for repulsive interactions between baryons in a hadron gas

> The quantum mechanical Beth-Uhlenbeck (BU) approach for repulsive hard-core interactions between baryons is applied to the thermodynamics of a hadron gas. The second virial coefficient $a_2$ -- the "excluded volume" parameter -- calculated within the BU approach is found to be temperature dependent, and it differs dramatically from the classical excluded volume (EV) model result. At temperatures $T =100-200$ MeV, the widely used classical EV model underestimates the EV parameter for nucleons at a given value of the nucleon hard-core radius by large factors of 3-4. Previous studies, which employed the hard-core radii of hadrons as an input into the classical EV model, have to be re-evaluated using the appropriately rescaled EV parameters. The BU approach is used to model the repulsive baryonic interactions in the hadron resonance gas (HRG) model. Lattice data for the second and fourth order net baryon susceptibilities are described fairly well when the temperature dependent BU baryonic excluded volume parameter corresponds to nucleon hard-core radii of $r_c = 0.25-0.3$ fm. Role of the attractive baryonic interactions is also considered. It is argued that HRG model with a constant baryon-baryon EV parameter $v_{NN} \simeq 1$ fm$^3$ provides a simple yet efficient description of baryon-baryon interaction in the crossover temperature region.

## Key claim

The quantum mechanical Beth-Uhlenbeck (BU) approach for repulsive hard-core interactions between baryons yields a temperature-dependent excluded volume parameter that differs dramatically from the classical excluded volume (EV) model; at temperatures T = 100–200 MeV, the classical EV model underestimates the EV parameter for nucleons at a given hard-core radius by factors of 3–4, meaning previous studies using hard-core radii as input into the classical EV model must be re-evaluated with appropriately rescaled parameters.

## Method

The authors applied the Beth-Uhlenbeck formalism to compute the second virial coefficient (excluded volume parameter) for nucleon-nucleon systems with a hard-core interaction potential, using relativistic dispersion relations and summing over all relevant scattering channels (isospin, spin, orbital angular momentum, total angular momentum) with hard-sphere phase shifts. They then incorporated the temperature-dependent excluded volume parameter into the hadron resonance gas (HRG) model, comparing both a second-order virial expansion (BU-HRG) and an exponential excluded volume formulation (EV-HRG) against lattice QCD data for net baryon number susceptibilities.

## Key numbers

- At temperatures T = 100–200 MeV, the classical EV model underestimates the nucleon-nucleon excluded volume parameter by factors of 3–4 compared to the BU approach for the same hard-core radius.
- Lattice data for second and fourth order net baryon susceptibilities are described fairly well when the temperature-dependent BU baryonic excluded volume parameter corresponds to nucleon hard-core radii of r_c = 0.25–0.3 fm.
- The nucleon thermal de Broglie wavelength at T = 150 MeV is approximately 1.3 fm, much larger than typical nucleon hard-core radii of 0.2–0.8 fm.
- A constant baryon-baryon excluded volume parameter v_NN ≈ 1 fm^3 provides a simple yet efficient description of baryon-baryon interaction in the crossover temperature region.
- Decreasing r_c from 0.3 fm to 0.25 fm results in about a 30% decrease of a_2^NN(T) at a given temperature.

## Context

This work extends the Beth-Uhlenbeck approach previously applied to spinless particles (Kostyuk 2000, Typel 2016) to spin-1/2 nucleons with realistic scattering channels, and contrasts with the classical excluded volume model widely used in hadron gas phenomenology (Hagedorn 1980, Gorenstein 1981, Kapusta 1982). The results enable more accurate modeling of repulsive baryonic interactions in the HRG model, reconciling lattice QCD data on baryon number fluctuations with hadronic descriptions, and suggest that the quantum van der Waals model (Vovchenko 2015) and S-matrix calculations (Huovinen 2017) should be compared with the BU approach for consistency.
