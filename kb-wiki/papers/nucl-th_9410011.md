# Pedagogical Studies of Relativistic Hamiltonians

> Relativistic Hamiltonians are defined as the sum of relativistic one-body kinetic energies, two- and many-body interactions and their boost corrections. We review the calculation of the boost correction of the two-body interaction from commutation relations of the Poincare group and show that its important terms can be easily understood from classical relativistic mechanics. The boost corrections for scalar- and vector- meson-exchange interactions, obtained from relativistic field theory, are shown to be in agreement with the results of the classical calculation. These boost corrections are also shown to be necessary to reproduce the known results of relativistic mean-field theories. We conclude with comments on the relativistic boost operator for the wave function of a nucleus. Several of the results presented in this pedagogical article are known. We hope that a better understanding of relativistic Hamiltonians and their relation to relativistic field theory is obtained by putting them together with a few new relations.

## Key claim

Relativistic Hamiltonians, defined as the sum of relativistic one-body kinetic energies plus two- and many-body interactions with their boost corrections, can reproduce the known results of relativistic mean-field theories only when the boost corrections are consistently included; the paper shows that the boost correction to the two-body interaction can be understood from classical relativistic mechanics, but this understanding is limited to the leading order in \(P^2/4m^2\) and does not capture all quantum mechanical terms.

## Method

The authors derive the boost correction \(\delta v(\mathbf{P})\) for two-body interactions from the commutation relations of the Poincaré group, expanding the Hamiltonian and boost generator in powers of \(1/m^2\) and retaining terms up to order \(1/m^3\). They then compare this quantum mechanical result with a classical relativistic calculation that attributes the three leading terms to the relativistic energy-momentum relation, Lorentz contraction, and Thomas precession. The boost corrections for scalar- and vector-meson-exchange interactions are obtained from relativistic field theory using Feynman diagram rules and shown to agree with the classical result. Finally, the relativistic Hamiltonian is applied to infinite nuclear matter in the Hartree (mean-field) approximation and compared to the exact solution of Walecka's relativistic mean-field theory.

## Key numbers

* The contributions of the four components of \(\delta v(\mathbf{P})\) to the triton binding energy are: relativistic energy-momentum term 0.23(2) MeV, Lorentz contraction term 0.10(1) MeV, Thomas precession term 0.016(2) MeV, and quantum mechanical term -0.004(2) MeV (Section III).
* The energy per particle of nuclear matter in the relativistic mean-field theory is expanded in powers of the Fermi momentum \(k_F\); the leading nonrelativistic terms are \(m + 3k_F^2/(10m) + G_V^2\rho/(2\mu_V^2) - G_S^2\rho/(2\mu_S^2)\), with subsequent relativistic corrections given as a series in \(k_F\) up to order \(k_F^{11}\) (Section V, Eq. 5.6).
* The average value of \((k_i - k_j)^2\) and \((k_i + k_j)^2\) in the Hartree approximation for uniform matter is \(6k_F^2/10\) (Section V).

## Context

This pedagogical paper extends the work of Bakamjian and Thomas (1953) and Foldy (1961) on relativistic Hamiltonians, and the formal calculation of boost corrections by Krajcik and Foldy (1974) and Friar (1975). It connects these formal results to classical relativistic mechanics and to relativistic field theory, showing that the boost corrections derived from the Poincaré group are necessary to reproduce the mean-field results of Walecka (1974). The paper enables the use of relativistic Hamiltonians in variational and Green's function Monte Carlo calculations for light nuclei, as previously applied to \(^3\)H and \(^4\)He by Carlson, Pandharipande, and Wiringa (1983), and points toward the need for improved nucleon-nucleon potentials that include the \(p^2/m^2\) term in the one-pion-exchange interaction, which has been neglected in most models except the Bonn potentials.
