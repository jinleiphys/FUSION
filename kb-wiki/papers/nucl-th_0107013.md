# 11Li stability and its single particle structure

> The stability of the 11Li exotic nucleus is discussed focusing on the existence of single particle positive energy components in its ground state structure. The existence of these continuum components is an outcome of many models using the available experimental data of 10Li. We show that if this were the case, the 11Li main decay mode could not be the beta decay.

## Key claim

The authors argue that any theoretical description of the 11Li ground state that includes a continuum (positive energy) single-particle component, such as the 1p1/2 or 2s1/2 state, is incompatible with beta decay being the main decay mode, because such a component would cause neutron emission on a timescale much shorter than the 8.5 ms beta-decay half-life.

## Method

The authors used a BCS treatment of the residual pairing interaction between valence neutrons, including a self-energy term that renormalizes the single-particle energies in 11Li relative to 10Li. They then calculated the widths of the 1p1/2 resonance in 10Li using a spherical Woods-Saxon potential for three different parameter sets (PI, PII, PIII) to examine whether the renormalized continuum component in 11Li could be narrow enough to allow beta decay to compete.

## Key numbers

- The 11Li beta-decay mean life is 8.5 ms; any particle-decay width must be smaller than about 5 x 10^-17 KeV for beta emission to remain the main decay mode.
- For the 1p1/2 resonance in 10Li with energy 508 KeV and width 339 KeV (potential PI), the half-life is about 1 x 10^-21 s.
- For the renormalized 1p1/2 state in 11Li with energy 180 KeV and width 90 KeV (potential PII), the half-life is about 5 x 10^-21 s.
- For a fictitious 1p1/2 state with energy 2 KeV and width 0.15 KeV (potential PIII), the half-life is about 3 x 10^3 x 10^-21 s, still too short for beta decay to compete.
- The two-neutron binding energy in 11Li is experimentally about 294 KeV, but the authors consider a range of 400-1200 KeV for their stability analysis.

## Context

This work extends the BCS-based model of Kyotoku et al. (1996) for 11Li and addresses a contradiction between theoretical ground-state descriptions that include continuum components and the observed beta-decay dominance. It challenges models that rely on positive-energy single-particle states from 10Li data, such as those of Young et al. (1994) and Borge et al. (1997), and is consistent with the findings of Caggiano et al. (2000) who saw no 2s1/2 state. The result constrains future models of halo nuclei to avoid continuum components in the ground state and calls for improved experimental determination of the 1/2+ state in 10Li.
