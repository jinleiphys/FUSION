# Nonconvergence of the Feynman-Dyson diagrammatic perturbation expansion of propagators

> Using a general-order ab initio many-body Green's function method, we numerically illustrate several pathological behaviors of the Feynman-Dyson diagrammatic perturbation expansion of one-particle many-body Green's functions as electron Feynman propagators. (i) The perturbation expansion of the frequency-dependent self-energy is not convergent at the exact self-energy in many frequency domains. (ii) An odd-perturbation-order self-energy has a qualitatively wrong shape and, as a result, many roots of the corresponding Dyson equation are nonphysical in that the poles may be complex or residues can exceed unity or be negative. (iii) A higher even-order self-energy consists of vertical lines at many frequencies, predicting numerous phantom poles with zero residues. (iv) Infinite partial resummations of diagrams by vertex or edge renormalization tend to exacerbate these pathologies. (v) The nonconvergence is caused by the nonanalyticity of the rational-function form of the exact Green's function at many frequencies, where the radius of convergence of its Taylor expansion is zero. This is consistent with the fact that (vi) Padé approximants (power-series expansions of a rational function) can largely restore the correct shape and poles of the Green's function. Nevertheless, not only does the nonconvergence render higher-order Feynman-Dyson diagrammatic perturbation theory useless for many lower-lying ionization or higher-lying electron-attachment states, but it also calls into question the validity of its combined use with the ansätze requiring the knowledge of all poles and residues. Such ansätze include the Galitskii-Migdal identity, the self-consistent Green's function methods, and some models of the algebraic diagrammatic construction.

## Key claim

The Feynman-Dyson diagrammatic perturbation expansion of the one-particle many-body Green's function is nonconvergent at the exact self-energy in many frequency domains, and this nonconvergence is caused by the nonanalyticity of the rational-function form of the exact Green's function, where the radius of convergence of its Taylor expansion is zero.

## Method

Using a general-order ab initio many-body Green's function method, the authors numerically evaluated perturbation corrections to the frequency-dependent self-energy and Green's function at arbitrary orders for the boron hydride (BH) molecule in a minimal basis set. They compared the perturbative results against exact results obtained from full configuration-interaction (FCI) calculations, which serve as the exact finite-basis-set reference. The analysis focused on the diagonal elements of the self-energy and Green's function, examining their functional forms across frequency domains and the resulting roots of the Dyson equation.

## Key numbers

- The exact FCI total energy for BH is -24.809940 Eh, compared to the Hartree-Fock energy of -24.752788 Eh.
- The exact residues from FCI satisfy the particle number sum rule with a precision of 10^-7 and the Galitskii-Migdal identity with a precision of 10^-6 Eh.
- The derivative of the self-energy at poles was approximated using an average of derivatives at omega = omega_q +/- 10^-9 Eh.
- The third-order self-energy has up to second-order poles, while the second-order self-energy and exact self-energy have only first-order poles.

## Context

This work extends the known breakdowns of diagrammatic perturbation theory, following Dyson's (1952) argument about divergence in quantum electrodynamics and Kohn and Luttinger's predictions about finite-temperature perturbation theory. The paper contradicts the assumption that higher-order Feynman-Dyson perturbation theory systematically improves Green's function results, showing instead that odd-order self-energies have qualitatively wrong shapes and even-order self-energies develop phantom poles. The findings call into question methods that require knowledge of all poles and residues, including the Galitskii-Migdal identity, self-consistent Green's function methods, and some models of the algebraic diagrammatic construction (ADC). The authors show that Pade approximants can largely restore the correct shape and poles, suggesting a path forward.
