# Basin of attraction for turbulent thermalization and the range of validity of classical-statistical simulations

> Different thermalization scenarios for systems with large fields have been proposed in the literature based on classical-statistical lattice simulations approximating the underlying quantum dynamics. We investigate the range of validity of these simulations for condensate driven as well as fluctuation dominated initial conditions for the example of a single component scalar field theory. We show that they lead to the same phenomenon of turbulent thermalization for the whole range of (weak) couplings where the classical-statistical approach is valid. In the turbulent regime we establish the existence of a dual cascade characterized by universal scaling exponents and scaling functions. This complements previous investigations where only the direct energy cascade has been studied for the single component theory. A proposed alternative thermalization scenario for stronger couplings is shown to be beyond the range of validity of classical-statistical simulations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that condensate-driven and fluctuation-dominated initial conditions in a single-component scalar field theory both lead to the same universal turbulent thermalization behavior, including a dual cascade with an inverse particle cascade, for the entire range of weak couplings where classical-statistical simulations are valid; a proposed alternative thermalization scenario for stronger couplings is shown to lie outside the range of validity of the classical-statistical approach.

## Method

The authors performed classical-statistical lattice simulations of a massless real scalar field theory with quartic interaction in 3+1 dimensions, using Gaussian initial conditions for both condensate-driven (large field, zero fluctuations) and fluctuation-dominated (zero field, overoccupied distribution) cases. They evolved the classical equations of motion on lattices up to 768^3 sites and extracted occupation numbers from the Fourier-transformed two-point correlation functions, analyzing the time evolution of the field amplitude and distribution functions to identify self-similar scaling behavior and power-law cascades.

## Key numbers

- The instability regime ends at time t ~ Q^{-1} log(lambda^{-1}), with the largest growth rate gamma_0 ~ 0.033 Q for the primary instabilities
- The field amplitude decays as phi_0(t) ~ Q (Qt)^{-delta} with exponent delta = 1/3 during the turbulent regime
- The turbulent regime extends from t ~ Q^{-1} log(lambda^{-1}) to t_quant ~ Q^{-1} lambda^{-5/4}, where typical occupancies become order one
- The universal scaling exponents for hard modes are beta = -1/5 for characteristic momenta and alpha = -4/5 for occupancy
- The power-law exponents for the distribution function are kappa_S = 4 (strong turbulence, ultrasoft momenta), kappa_M = 4/3 (weak wave turbulence, particle cascade), and kappa_H = 3/2 (direct energy cascade)
- The resonance peak initially occurs at p_*/Q ~ 1.1, with p_* = 0.52 sigma_0 where sigma_0 = 2.21 Q for the condensate case
- For lambda = 10^{-4}, the rescaled distribution functions from different initial conditions agree at Qt = 6100
- For lambda <= 0.1, the rescaled distribution functions agree to very good accuracy at Qt = 1200

## Context

This work extends previous investigations by Micha and Tkachev (2004) that established the direct energy cascade for single-component scalar theory but did not study the inverse particle cascade, which had only been established for O(N) symmetric theories with N >= 2 by Berges et al. (2008, 2010). The paper directly addresses conflicting claims by Epelbaum and Gelis (2011) about a different thermalization scenario at stronger couplings, showing that their results lie beyond the range of validity of classical-statistical simulations. The findings enable the classification of nonequilibrium dynamics into universality classes and support the application of classical-statistical methods to heavy-ion collision phenomenology within the Color Glass Condensate framework.
