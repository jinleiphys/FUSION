# Self-Consistent Separable RPA For Density- and Current-Dependent Forces

> Self-consistent factorization of two-body residual interaction is proposed for arbitrary density- and current-dependent energy functionals. Following this procedure, a separable RPA (SRPA) method is constructed. SRPA dramatically simplifies the calculations and demonstrates quick convergence to exact results. The method is tested for SkM* forces.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a self-consistent separable RPA (SRPA) method that factorizes the residual two-body interaction for arbitrary density- and current-dependent energy functionals, dramatically simplifying calculations while maintaining high accuracy; the method is tested only for the SkM* functional and requires at least two separable operators to reproduce isovector E1 transitions accurately.

## Method

The authors start from a general time-dependent energy functional depending on neutron and proton densities and currents, and use a scaling transformation to generate time-dependent Slater determinants with T-even and T-odd generators. They derive analytical expressions for the separable one-body operators and strength constants from the second derivatives of the energy functional, reducing the high-rank RPA matrix to a low-rank (4K with K=3-6) symmetric real matrix whose determinant yields the dispersion equation for eigenvalues. The method is tested by comparing strength functions for isoscalar E2 and isovector E1 transitions in ⁴⁰Ca and ²⁰⁸Pb against exact RPA results using the SkM* functional.

## Key numbers

- For the SRPA test, K=2 generators were used with radial functions R₁(r)=r^λ and R₂(r)=r^(λ+2), where λ is the multipolarity.
- The strength function used a Lorentz averaging parameter Δ=1 MeV.
- The SRPA matrix rank is 4K, with K=3-6, compared to the much larger rank of exact RPA matrices.

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent factorization scheme of L. S. Song (1990) by generalizing it to multiple separable operators, overcoming limitations of earlier approaches that were restricted to analytic estimates (Rowe, 1970; Bertsch and Tsai, 1975) or covered only particular forces (Vorobjev, 1999). The method enables efficient RPA calculations for deformed and heavy spherical nuclei where exact RPA is computationally prohibitive, and can serve as a basis for anharmonic corrections and studies of exotic nuclei from radioactive beams.
