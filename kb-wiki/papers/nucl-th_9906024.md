# Nonequilibrium Weak Processes in Kaon Condensation I --- Reaction rate for the thermal kaon process ---

> We investigate the thermal kaon process,in which kaons are thermally produced via nucleon-nucleon collisions.This process is relevant to nonequilibrium dynamics of kaon condensation inside neutron stars.The reaction rates for these processes are calculated, and their temperature and density dependences are compared with those of other reaction rates.It is shown that the thermal kaon process is dominant over other relevant weak reactions throughout the nonequilibrium process, such as the kaon-induced Urca and the modified Urca reactions, and may control the entire evolution of the kaon condensate. The characteristic role of the soft and hard kaons during the evolution is explained, and implications for astrophysical phenomena are briefly discussed.

## Key claim

The thermal kaon process (KT), in which kaons are thermally produced via nucleon-nucleon collisions, is dominant over other relevant weak reactions (kaon-induced Urca and modified Urca) throughout the nonequilibrium process of kaon condensation in neutron stars, but this dominance holds under the specific condition that the system is far from chemical equilibrium with a negative value of the dimensionless parameter ξ^(KT) ≡ (μ_K + μ_p − μ_n)/T, and the reaction rates are calculated within a low-temperature approximation valid for temperatures less than several tens of MeV.

## Method

The reaction rate for the forward thermal kaon process (n + n → n + p + K⁻) was calculated using a chiral-symmetry-based effective weak Hamiltonian with current-current interaction, treating nucleon-nucleon scattering via the one-pion exchange potential (OPEP) and evaluating matrix elements in the nonrelativistic limit. The phase space integrals were separated into radial and angular parts, with the angular integral approximated under the condition that the kaon momentum plus proton Fermi momentum is less than the neutron Fermi momentum, and the low-temperature approximation was applied to evaluate the four-nucleon energy integrals using a known analytic formula. The resulting reaction rate expression depends on temperature T, the dimensionless parameter ξ^(KT), the neutron and proton Fermi momenta, effective nucleon masses, and the chiral angle θ representing the kaon condensate order parameter.

## Key numbers

- The reaction rate for the forward thermal kaon process is given by Γ^(KT-F) = (4.0 × 10³⁰) × (|p_F(p)|/m_π) × (m_n^*/m_N)³ × (m_p^*/m_N) × cos⁶(θ/2) × T₉⁵ × I^(KT)(ξ^(KT), T) in units of cm⁻³·s⁻¹, where T₉ is temperature in units of 10⁹ K
- The critical baryon number density for kaon condensation is n_B^C = 0.485 fm⁻³ (approximately 3.0 n₀, where n₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ is the standard nuclear matter density)
- The KN sigma term is taken as Σ_KN = 300 MeV
- The effective nucleon mass ratios are taken as m_p^*/m_N = m_n^*/m_N = 0.8
- The proton Fermi momentum in the noncondensed state is estimated as |p_F(p)| = 260–290 MeV for baryon number densities n_B = 0.55–0.70 fm⁻³
- The corresponding neutron Fermi momentum is |p_F(n)| = 470–510 MeV
- The typical momentum of thermal kaons that mainly contribute to the reactions is 120–180 MeV
- The symmetry energy parameter S₀ = 30 MeV at nuclear matter density n₀

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Muto, Tatsumi, Iwamoto 1997) that considered only kaon-induced Urca (KU) and modified Urca (MU) reactions assuming a pre-existing small seed of condensate, by now including the thermal kaon (KT) process which allows kaon creation without a seed. The paper provides the reaction rate formalism needed for the subsequent paper (Muto, Tatsumi, Iwamoto 1998, paper II) that will analyze the time-dependent evolution of the kaon condensate, and it builds on the chiral-symmetry-based equation of state developed by Tatsumi and Yasuhira (1998) for protoneutron stars with kaon condensation. The results enable quantitative modeling of nonequilibrium weak processes during the formation and evolution of kaon condensates in neutron stars, with implications for the dynamical evolution of kaon-condensed stars just after supernova explosions.
