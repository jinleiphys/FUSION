# Initializing BSQ with Open-Source ICCING

> While it is well known that there is a significant amount of conserved charges in the initial state of nuclear collisions, the production of these due to gluon splitting has yet to be thoroughly investigated. The ICCING (Initial Conserved Charges in Nuclear Geometry) algorithm reconstructs these quark distributions, providing conserved strange, baryon, and electric charges, by sampling a given model for the $g \rightarrow q\bar{q}$ splitting function over the initial energy density, which is valid at top collider energies, even when $μ_B=0$. The ICCING algorithm includes fluctuations in the gluon longitudinal momenta, a structure that supports the implementation of dynamical processes, and the c++ version is now open-source. A full analysis of parameter choices on the model has been done to quantify the effect these have on the underlying physics. We find there is a sustained difference across the different charges that indicates sensitivity to hot spot geometry.

# Initializing BSQ with Open-Source ICCING

## Key claim
The ICCING algorithm introduces conserved strange, baryon, and electric charges into the initial state of heavy-ion collisions by sampling gluon-to-quark-antiquark splitting probabilities over the initial energy density, and the resulting charge geometries show a sustained difference across charges that indicates sensitivity to hot spot geometry, but the modification to the bulk energy density is minimal and should not disrupt previous agreement with experimental flow harmonics.

## Method
The ICCING algorithm takes an externally provided initial energy density profile (here from the Trento generator) and, on an event-by-event basis, randomly selects points to act as possible gluon centers. For each candidate gluon, the algorithm samples a distribution to determine the gluon energy, then uses Monte Carlo sampling of quark flavor ratios from a color-glass condensate (CGC) calculation to decide whether the gluon splits into a quark-antiquark pair and what flavor it becomes; the distance between the quarks and their energy sharing are also sampled. The resulting energy, baryon, strange, and electric charge densities are distributed onto output grids, and the process repeats until all input energy is transferred.

## Key numbers
- The strange quark mass threshold is approximately 2 m_s ≈ 200 MeV, which may restrict strange quark production to hot spots with sufficient energy density.
- The analysis uses PbPb collisions at √s_NN = 5.02 TeV with the PDG16+/2+1[WB] equation of state and normalization from Alba (2017).
- For ε₂{2} in the 10–60% centrality range, modification of the event geometry by ICCING is very small; there is a consistently constant increase for ε₃{2} in the same window.
- In the 0–10% centrality bin, the strangeness ε₂ cumulant ratio ε₂{4}/ε₂{2} crosses from being larger than the bulk to smaller than the bulk at around 10% centrality.
- For triangularity ε₃{2}, a clear hierarchy holds up to >80% centrality: ε₃^(S⁺){2} > ε₃^(B⁺, Q⁺){2} > ε₃^(E){2}.

## Context
This work extends the ICCING algorithm introduced in Carzon (2019) and Martinez (2019), which itself builds on the CGC-based calculation of gluon-to-quark splitting probabilities from Martinez (2018). The algorithm is designed to be modular and model-agnostic, taking any initial energy density (here from the Trento generator of Moreland (2014, 2018)) and allowing different choices for splitting functions and charge distributions. The results enable future hydrodynamic simulations that can propagate all three conserved charges (baryon, strangeness, electric) at top collider energies, moving beyond the common mean-field assumption of zero net charge density. The finding that strangeness follows a different geometry than baryon and electric charge, which largely track the energy density, provides a new probe of sub-nucleonic fluctuations in the initial state.
