# Chemical potential and the gap equation

> In general the kernel of QCD's gap equation possesses a domain of analyticity upon which the equation's solution at nonzero chemical potential is simply obtained from the in-vacuum result through analytic continuation. On this domain the single-quark number- and scalar-density distribution functions are mu-independent. This is illustrated via two models for the gap equation's kernel. The models are alike in concentrating support in the infrared. They differ in the form of the vertex but qualitatively the results are largely insensitive to the Ansatz. In vacuum both models realise chiral symmetry in the Nambu-Goldstone mode and in the chiral limit, with increasing chemical potential, exhibit a first-order chiral symmetry restoring transition at mu~M(0), where M(p^2) is the dressed-quark mass function. There is evidence to suggest that any associated deconfinement transition is coincident and also of first-order.

## Key claim
The gap equation's kernel possesses a domain of analyticity in chemical potential such that, below a critical value μ_a, the in-medium dressed-quark propagator is obtained from the vacuum result via analytic continuation and the quark number density and scalar condensate remain μ-independent; however, this domain may be smaller than the domain of the Nambu-Goldstone phase, so that order parameters can evolve continuously before the first-order chiral symmetry restoring transition occurs at μ ≈ M(0).

## Method
The authors solve QCD's gap equation (Dyson-Schwinger equation for the quark propagator) at nonzero chemical potential using two model kernels that concentrate support in the infrared: a rainbow truncation with a bare vertex, and a second model employing a vertex Ansatz that satisfies the vector Ward-Takahashi identity. Both models use a finite-width representation of the effective gluon interaction, with parameters ω = 0.5 GeV and D chosen to reproduce the chiral-limit vacuum quark condensate. The in-medium dressed-quark propagator is analyzed via its Schwinger function in configuration space to determine the domain of analyticity and the nature of the chiral symmetry restoring transition.

## Key numbers
- μ_a^rb = 0.53 GeV: the analyticity threshold for the rainbow truncation model, below which the Schwinger function is obtained by analytic continuation from vacuum.
- μ_c^χ^rb = 0.38 GeV: the critical chemical potential for first-order chiral symmetry restoration in the rainbow truncation model, which is less than μ_a^rb.
- M(0) = 0.40 GeV: the dressed-quark mass function at zero momentum in vacuum for the rainbow truncation model.
- −(⟨q̄q⟩^0)^{1/3} = 0.25 GeV: the chiral-limit vacuum quark condensate for the rainbow truncation model.
- B(0) = (0.16 GeV)^4: the bag constant (pressure difference between Nambu-Goldstone and Wigner phases) at zero chemical potential in the rainbow truncation model.
- ω D = (0.8 GeV)^3: the combination of interaction parameters that keeps fitted vacuum observables approximately constant.

## Context
This work extends earlier Dyson-Schwinger equation studies of nonzero chemical potential by Blaschke (1997), Bender (1997), and Maris (2001), which found coincident deconfinement and chiral symmetry restoration transitions. It complements studies of chiral symmetry restoration by Harada (1998), Liu (2003), and Zong (2005), and of hadron properties by Maris (1997), Liu (2001), and Chang (2005). The paper's analysis of the analyticity domain and the demonstration that the condensate remains μ-independent below μ_a provides a formal foundation for understanding when analytic continuation from vacuum is valid, and enables future work to determine whether the deconfinement transition is coincident with chiral restoration in these models.
