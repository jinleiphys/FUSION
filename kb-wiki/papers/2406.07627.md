# Semi-Analytical Fokker Planck Models for Nuclear Star Clusters

> We study the dynamics of nuclear star clusters, the dense stellar environments surrounding massive black holes in the centers of galaxies. We consider angular momentum diffusion due to two-body scatterings among stellar objects and energy advection due to gravitational wave emission upon interaction with the central massive black hole. Such dynamics is described by a two-dimensional Fokker-Planck equation in energy-angular momentum space. Focusing on the transition between the diffusion-dominated region and the advection-dominated one, we utilize self-similarity to obtain a full solution for the Fokker-Planck equation. This solution provides the density and flux of the stellar objects in nuclear star clusters. This improves the rate estimates for extreme mass-ratio inspirals, and has interesting implications for a new class of galactic center transients called quasi-periodic eruptions.

## Key claim

The paper develops a semi-analytical, self-similar solution to the two-dimensional Fokker-Planck equation describing stellar dynamics in nuclear star clusters, which improves rate estimates for extreme-mass ratio inspirals (EMRIs) and quasi-periodic eruptions (QPEs) by providing explicit numerical coefficients that correct the approximate analytical models previously used in the literature.

## Method

The authors model a nuclear star cluster with a central massive black hole, considering two-body scattering (angular momentum diffusion) and gravitational wave emission (energy advection). They assume a power-law density profile for the cluster and a steady-state solution, reducing the two-dimensional Fokker-Planck equation in energy-angular momentum space to a one-dimensional scale-free ordinary differential equation using self-similarity. This ODE is solved numerically for the function \(g(x)\), where \(x = r_p / r_{\rm gw}\) is the periapsis normalized by the gravitational-wave loss-cone boundary, yielding the density and flux of stellar objects across both the diffusion-dominated and advection-dominated regimes.

## Key numbers

* For a Bahcall-Wolf profile of field stars (\(\gamma_f = 7/4\)), the power-law index \(\beta\) for the subject star density in the gravitational-wave regime is \(\beta = 2\gamma - 3\), where \(\gamma\) is the density slope of the subject stars.
* The gravitational-wave loss-cone boundary is given by \(r_{\rm gw}(r) = R_s \left[ \frac{\xi}{\ln\Lambda} \frac{m}{M_\bullet} \left( \frac{M_\bullet}{m_f} \right)^2 \frac{1}{N_{f0}} \right]^{2/5} \left( \frac{r_h}{r} \right)^{2(3-\gamma_f)/5}\), with numerical factor \(\xi \simeq 1.7\).
* The two-body relaxation timescale is \(T_{\rm 2b}(r) = \frac{3\sqrt{2} \pi^2}{32 C} \frac{T_{\rm Kep}(r)}{\ln\Lambda} \left( \frac{M_\bullet}{m_f} \right)^2 \frac{1}{N_f(r)}\), with \(C \in [0.98, 1.61]\) for \(\gamma_f \in [1.5, 3]\) and \(C=1.35\) used for the Bahcall-Wolf profile.
* The gravitational-wave timescale is \(T_{\rm gw}(r, r_p) = \frac{96 \sqrt{2}}{85} \frac{R_s}{c} \frac{M_\bullet}{m} \left( \frac{r_p}{R_s} \right)^4 \sqrt{\frac{r}{r_p}}\).
* The numerical coefficients \(c_0\) and \(c_1\) are determined numerically for \(\gamma \in [1.505, 6.5]\); for example, for \(\gamma = 7/4\) (Bahcall-Wolf for subject stars), \(c_0 \approx 0.6\) and \(c_1 \approx 0.8\) (values read from Figure 3 in the paper).

## Context

This work extends the analytical model of Sari & Fragione (2019) by providing a full solution that accurately describes the transition between the two-body scattering-dominated and gravitational-wave-dominated regimes, rather than sharply connecting asymptotic solutions at the loss-cone boundary. It enables improved rate calculations for EMRIs (relevant for LISA and TianQin) and for quasi-periodic eruptions observed by X-ray telescopes such as eROSITA and Einstein Probe, by introducing numerical coefficients \(c_0\) and \(c_1\) that correct the density and flux estimates. The approach is valid for subject star density slopes \(\gamma \geq 3/2\) and assumes a power-law density profile for the nuclear star cluster.
