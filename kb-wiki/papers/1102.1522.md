# Low Mass Dark Matter and Invisible Higgs Width In Darkon Models

> The Standard Model (SM) plus a real gauge-singlet scalar field dubbed darkon (SM+D) is the simplest model possessing a weakly interacting massive particle (WIMP) dark-matter candidate. In this model, the parameters are constrained from dark matter relic density and direct searches. The fact that interaction between darkon and SM particles is only mediated by Higgs boson exchange may lead to significant modifications to the Higgs boson properties. If the dark matter mass is smaller than a half of the Higgs boson mass, the Higgs boson can decay into a pair of darkons resulting in a large invisible branching ratio. The Higgs boson will be searched for at the LHC and may well be discovered in the near future. If a Higgs boson with a small invisible decay width will be found, the SM+D model with small dark matter mass will be in trouble. We find that by extending the SM+D to a two-Higgs-doublet model plus a darkon (THDM+D) it is possible to have a Higgs boson with a small invisible branching ratio and at the same time the dark matter can have a low mass. We also comment on other implications of this model.

## Key claim

The Standard Model plus a real gauge-singlet scalar darkon (SM+D) predicts that if the dark matter mass is below half the Higgs boson mass, the Higgs boson will have a very large invisible branching ratio; this tension can be resolved in a two-Higgs-doublet model plus a darkon (THDM+D) by allowing one Higgs boson to be responsible for dark matter physics while the other, lighter Higgs boson can have a small invisible branching ratio.

## Method

The authors computed the dark matter relic density from darkon-pair annihilation through Higgs exchange, the darkon-nucleon elastic scattering cross section for direct detection, and the Higgs boson invisible decay width into darkon pairs, for both the SM+D and THDM+D models. They imposed the 90% C.L. relic density range from WMAP7 and compared the predicted elastic cross sections to experimental upper limits from DAMA, CoGeNT, CDMS, and XENON. In the THDM+D, they explored scenarios where the coupling of the lighter Higgs boson to the darkon is set to zero, so that the heavier Higgs boson controls the dark matter relic density and direct detection, while the lighter Higgs boson can have a small invisible branching ratio.

## Key numbers

- Dark matter relic density constraint: 90% C.L. range from WMAP7 is \(\Omega_D h^2 \subset [0.1065, 0.1181]\).
- Higgs vacuum expectation value: \(v = 246\) GeV.
- Higgs-nucleon coupling in SM+D: \(g_{NNh}^{\rm SM} \simeq 1.71 \times 10^{-3}\).
- For the cancelation mechanism in THDM+D, the ratio \(k_d^{\cal H}/k_u^{\cal H} = -0.405\) is required for exact cancelation; the authors use \(k_d^{\cal H}/k_u^{\cal H} = -0.42\) for numerical illustrations.
- In SM+D, dark matter mass roughly in the range of 10 GeV to half of the Higgs boson mass is ruled out by direct searches; a small range of 2 GeV to 10 GeV remains allowed.
- In THDM+D case a (lighter Higgs \(h\) with \(\lambda_h = 0\)), the heavier Higgs \(H\) masses considered are 250, 300, and 360 GeV, with \(\tan\beta = 1\) and \(\tan\beta = 30\).
- In THDM+D case b (lighter Higgs \(H\) with \(\lambda_H = 0\)), the heavier Higgs \(h\) masses considered are 250, 300, and 360 GeV, with \(\tan\beta = 1\) and \(\tan\beta = 30\).
- The perturbativity bound used is \(|\lambda_H| < 4\pi\) (tree-level unitarity) or \(|\lambda_H| < 2\sqrt{\pi} (m_H / 100\ \text{GeV})^2\).

## Context

This work extends the SM+D darkon model first considered by Silveira and Zee (1985) and later explored by Burgess et al. (2000), among others. The authors show that the SM+D model is severely constrained for low dark matter masses (roughly 10 GeV to half the Higgs mass) by direct detection experiments such as CDMS and XENON, and that the Higgs invisible branching ratio is always large when kinematically allowed. By extending to a two-Higgs-doublet model (THDM+D), they demonstrate that a cancelation mechanism in the darkon-nucleon elastic cross section can evade direct detection limits, and that one Higgs boson can be made nearly decoupled from the darkon to allow a small invisible width for the lighter Higgs boson. This enables future LHC searches for a Higgs boson with small invisible branching ratio to coexist with a low-mass dark matter candidate, and future direct detection experiments such as superCDMS and Xenon100+ can further constrain the parameter space.
