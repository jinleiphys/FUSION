# Cloud Quantum Computing of an Atomic Nucleus

> We report a quantum simulation of the deuteron binding energy on quantum processors accessed via cloud servers. We use a Hamiltonian from pionless effective field theory at leading order. We design a low-depth version of the unitary coupled-cluster ansatz, use the variational quantum eigensolver algorithm, and compute the binding energy to within a few percent. Our work is the first step towards scalable nuclear structure computations on a quantum processor via the cloud, and it sheds light on how to map scientific computing applications onto nascent quantum devices.

## Key claim

The authors report a quantum simulation of the deuteron binding energy using cloud-accessed quantum processors, achieving results within a few percent of the exact value, but note that the computation required careful tailoring of the Hamiltonian, wavefunction preparation, and error mitigation to accommodate the limited connectivity, low circuit depth, and intermittent access of current quantum hardware.

## Method

The authors constructed a deuteron Hamiltonian from pionless effective field theory at leading order, using a discrete variable representation in a harmonic oscillator basis with up to three single-particle states (N=1,2,3). They mapped the fermionic operators onto qubits via the Jordan-Wigner transformation, designed a low-depth unitary coupled-cluster (UCC) ansatz, and employed the variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) algorithm to minimize the Hamiltonian expectation value on IBM QX5 (16 qubits) and Rigetti 19Q (19 qubits) quantum processors accessed via cloud servers. For the N=3 case, they applied zero-noise extrapolation error mitigation by artificially increasing the CNOT gate error rate and extrapolating to the noiseless limit.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron ground-state energy from exact diagonalization: E_2 = -1.749 MeV (N=2 basis), E_3 = -2.046 MeV (N=3 basis); exact infinite-basis value is -2.22 MeV
- Quantum computation on two-qubit system: combined result from IBM QX5 and Rigetti 19Q yields E_2 = -1.74 ± 0.03 MeV, agreeing with exact diagonalization within uncertainties
- Extrapolation to infinite Hilbert space using N=1 and N=2 quantum results: O(kL e^{-4kL}) result of -2.18(3) MeV, deviating less than 2% from the exact deuteron binding energy of -2.22 MeV
- Three-qubit computation on IBM QX5 with error mitigation: E_3 = -2.08 ± 0.03 MeV, agreeing with exact result within uncertainties
- Hamiltonian parameters: V_0 = -5.68658111 MeV, ℏω = 7 MeV, ultraviolet cutoff Λ ≈ 152 MeV, bound-state momentum Q ≈ 46 MeV
- Effective hard-wall radii for N=1,2,3: L = 9.14, 11.45, and 13.38 fm respectively
- Maximum of 8,192 measurements (IBM QX5) or 10,000 measurements (Rigetti 19Q) per expectation value

## Context

This work extends prior quantum computing experiments in quantum chemistry and magnetism (Peruzzo 2014, O'Malley 2016, Kandala 2017) to nuclear physics, specifically the deuteron binding energy problem. The authors used publicly available software (PyQuil, XACC, OpenQASM, OpenFermion) and cloud quantum hardware, demonstrating that remote quantum computation of nuclear structure is feasible. The results open the avenue for quantum computations of heavier nuclei via cloud access, though the authors note that scaling to larger systems will require improved error mitigation and hardware fidelity.
