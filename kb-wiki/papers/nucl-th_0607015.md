# Direct reactions in/for astrophysics

> Precise nuclear reaction rates are needed for a detailed description of the production of elements in primordial nucleosynthesis and during the hydrostatic burning of stars to constrain the astrophysical models. The relevant reactions are extremely difficult to measure directly in the laboratory at the small astrophysical energies. In recent years direct reactions methods have been developed and applied to extract low-energy astrophysical S factors. The application of these methods requires a combination of experimental and theoretical efforts. This contribution focuses on the underlying reaction theories that have to be well understood in order to assess the precision and limitations of the various approaches.

## Key claim
Direct reaction methods (transfer, Coulomb dissociation, charge-exchange, and knockout) can extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors, but the precision and limitations of these approaches depend on a proper treatment of underlying reaction theories, including relativistic effects, higher-order processes, and nuclear structure inputs.

## Method
The paper reviews several direct reaction techniques applied to nuclear astrophysics: transfer reactions (including the Trojan Horse Method and Asymptotic Normalization Coefficients), intermediate-energy Coulomb excitation and the Coulomb dissociation method, charge-exchange reactions (p,n) and heavy-ion analogs for Gamow-Teller strength extraction, and single-nucleon knockout reactions with heavy ions in inverse kinematics. Each method is analyzed for its theoretical foundations, corrections (relativistic, recoil, multistep, nuclear breakup), and experimental applicability to reactions of astrophysical interest.

## Key numbers
- Relativistic corrections increase the scattering angle by up to 6% and the elastic scattering cross section by up to 13% for center-of-mass angles around 0-4 degrees at 100 MeV/nucleon.
- At 10 MeV/nucleon, relativistic corrections are important only at the 1% level; at 500 MeV/nucleon, recoil corrections are relevant at the 1% level.
- At intermediate energies (50-100 MeV/nucleon), relativistic and recoil corrections can add up to 50%.
- The stopping power for proton+hydrogen collisions at very low energies is proportional to v^alpha with alpha=1.35, while Andersen-Ziegler table extrapolations predict a smaller alpha value.
- For protons on helium targets at 4 keV, the stopping power scales as S ~ v_p^3.34 (ref. GS91).
- The S-factor for the reaction 7Be(p,gamma)8B is given by S_17(E) = (22.109 eV.b) * (1+5.30E+1.65E^2+0.857E^3) / (1+E/0.1375), where E is the relative energy in MeV of p+7Be in their center-of-mass.
- 8B has a small proton separation energy of approximately 140 keV.

## Context
This contribution extends the work on direct reaction methods for nuclear astrophysics, building on the Coulomb dissociation method proposed by Baur, Bertulani, and Rebel (1986) and the Trojan Horse Method by Baur, as well as the Asymptotic Normalization Coefficients approach by Mukhamedzhanov and Timofeyuk. It addresses the laboratory electron screening problem first studied by Assenbaum, Rolfs, and collaborators (1987, 1995, 2001), where experimental findings disagree systematically by a factor of two with theory. The paper enables more reliable extraction of astrophysical S-factors from indirect measurements at radioactive beam facilities (GANIL, MSU, RIKEN, GSI) and identifies open questions regarding the validity of Taddeucci's formula for charge-exchange reactions and the proper treatment of relativistic effects in Coulomb excitation at intermediate energies.
