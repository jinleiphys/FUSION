# Atomic effects in tritium beta decay

> The electron neutrino mass has been measured in several tritium beta decay experiments. These experiments are sensitive to a small neutrino mass because the energy release of the decay is small. But the very smallness of the energy release implies that the Coulomb interactions of the slowly moving emitted beta electron are relatively large. Using field theoretic techniques, we derive a systematic and controlled expansion which accounts for the Coulomb effects, including the mutual interaction of the beta ray electron and the electron in the final $^3{\rm He}^+$ ion. In our formulation, an effective potential which describes the long range Coulomb force experienced by the beta ray is introduced to ensure that our expansion is free of infrared divergences. Both the exclusive differential decay rate to a specific final $^3{\rm He}^+$ state and the inclusive differential decay rate are calculated to order $η^2$, where $η$ is the usual Coulomb parameter. We analyze the order $η^2$ correction to the beta ray spectrum and estimate how it may affect the neutrino mass squared parameter and the endpoint energy when this corrected spectrum is used to compare with the experiments. We find that the effect is small.

## Key claim

The authors derive a systematic expansion for Coulomb effects in tritium beta decay that is free of infrared divergences, and find that the atomic correction to the neutrino mass squared parameter is only a few eV², too small to explain the experimentally observed negative value of approximately -60 eV².

## Method

Using quantum field theory reduction techniques, the authors introduce an effective comparison potential that correctly describes the long-range screened Coulomb force experienced by the beta electron, enabling a controlled expansion in the Coulomb parameter η = α/v. They calculate both exclusive differential decay rates to specific final ³He⁺ states and the inclusive differential decay rate to order η², without relying on the closure approximation or uniform phase space factor approximation used in prior work by Durand (1964) and Strikman (1970). The atomic correction function R(Q−E) is computed and then fitted to a linear combination of the sudden approximation spectral functions to extract the shifts in neutrino mass squared and endpoint energy.

## Key numbers

- The Coulomb parameter near the tritium decay endpoint is η ≃ 0.03
- The endpoint energy Q ≃ 18.6 keV
- The atomic energy scale (Rydberg) Ry ≃ 13.6 eV
- The shift in neutrino mass squared from the atomic effect is Δm_ν² ≃ −12.6 η² Ry² ≃ −1.7 eV² for a fitting range of 0−64 Ry (0−870 eV) below the endpoint
- The shift in endpoint energy is ΔQ ≃ −4.6 η² Ry ≃ −0.047 eV
- For fitting ranges from 0−49 Ry (0−670 eV) to 0−79 Ry (0−1080 eV), Δm_ν² varies linearly from −0.80 eV² to −2.8 eV², decreasing by 0.4 eV² per 5 Ry increase in range
- The experimentally reported neutrino mass squared is m_ν² ≈ −60 eV²

## Context

This work extends the theoretical treatment of Coulomb effects in tritium beta decay beyond the sudden approximation used in experimental analyses by Robertson (1991), Holzschuh (1992), Kawakami (1991), Stoeffl (1995), and Backe (1993). It improves upon earlier calculations by Durand (1964) and Strikman (1970) by avoiding the closure approximation and uniform phase space factor approximation, which are invalid near the endpoint region crucial for neutrino mass measurements. The result shows that atomic corrections cannot explain the negative neutrino mass squared found in experiments, and the field-theoretic methods developed here may be applicable to other beta decay processes.
