# Nuclear Physics in the Era of Quantum Computing and Quantum Machine Learning

> In this paper, the application of quantum simulations and quantum machine learning to solve low-energy nuclear physics problems is explored. The use of quantum computing to deal with nuclear physics problems is, in general, in its infancy and, in particular, the use of quantum machine learning in the realm of nuclear physics at low energy is almost nonexistent. We present here three specific examples where the use of quantum computing and quantum machine learning provides, or could provide in the future, a possible computational advantage: i) the determination of the phase/shape in schematic nuclear models, ii) the calculation of the ground state energy of a nuclear shell model-type Hamiltonian and iii) the identification of particles or the determination of trajectories in nuclear physics experiments.

## Key claim

Quantum computing and quantum machine learning can provide a computational advantage for low-energy nuclear physics problems, but the field is in its infancy and the use of quantum machine learning in this realm is "almost nonexistent"; the paper presents three specific examples where such advantage "provides, or could provide in the future" a possible benefit, rather than claiming a demonstrated practical advantage today.

## Method

The authors review the three main nuclear models (shell model, mean-field approaches, and the collective/liquid-drop model) and then present three case studies: (i) using a classical convolutional neural network (CNN) fed with time-evolution data from a digital quantum simulation of the Agassi model to determine nuclear shape/phase; (ii) using a quantum convolutional neural network (QCNN) fed with ground-state energies from a variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) to map the phase diagram of the axial next-nearest-neighbor Ising (ANNNI) model; and (iii) a conceptual discussion of using quantum machine learning for particle identification and trajectory reconstruction in nuclear experiments.

## Key numbers

*   For the Agassi model phase determination via CNN, a lattice of 9261 points in control-parameter space was created, with 10% reserved for testing; global accuracy was 98.7% for exact evolution and 99.2% for a Trotter expansion with n_T=6 steps.
*   The Agassi model simulation was performed for a system with eight sites (N=8, j=2).
*   For the ANNNI model phase diagram predicted by the QCNN, the system size was N=6; the QCNN was trained using only points on the kappa=0 or h=0 axes but correctly reproduced the full phase diagram including the triple point at h=0 and kappa=0.5.

## Context

This perspective review extends the line of work applying quantum simulations to nuclear physics, which has previously focused on toy models or simple scenarios (e.g., the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model and the Agassi model), and connects it to the emerging field of quantum machine learning. The specific examples build on prior analytical mean-field phase-diagram work for the Agassi model by Garcia-Ramos (2018) and on the QCNN approach for the ANNNI model by Mona (2023). The paper enables future work on implementing state-of-the-art nuclear shell model or beyond-mean-field problems on NISQ computers, though it notes this is not yet possible due to qubit count and algorithm development needs.
