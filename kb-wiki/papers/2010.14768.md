# Connection between missing-charge in quasielastic electron scattering and two(one) proton knockout reactions and the island-of-inversion

> Our model, based on Quantum Chromodynamics, emphasizes the role of triton clustering in nuclei. As there is good empirical support of triton clustering in nuclei, we may even treat this as a good phenomenological working hypothesis as well. Here we show how our model finds remarkable success in being able to provide a consistent and interconnected understanding of such diverse empirical realities as the missing electric-charge in quasielastic electron scattering, and simultaneously, also the puzzling data within the studies of exotic nuclei. This gives us confidence, that our model should be taking account of the correct and proper degrees of freedom, to physically specify and explain, the total physical reality as manifested in the nuclear medium.

# Connection between missing-charge in quasielastic electron scattering and two(one) proton knockout reactions and the island-of-inversion

## Key claim

The authors propose that triton clustering in nuclei, derived from a Quantum Chromodynamics-based model with hidden color arguments, can simultaneously explain the missing electric charge observed in quasielastic electron scattering and the suppressed cross sections in two-proton knockout reactions in the island-of-inversion region. The claim is conditional on treating the proton-triton composite structure of the alpha particle as a phenomenological working hypothesis, where the alpha particle in the nuclear medium behaves as a proton-triton composite rather than as two protons and two neutrons in the 1s1/2 shell.

## Method

The authors apply a triton-cluster model where all nuclei of the form 3Z_Z_X_2Z are treated as bound states of Z tritons (3_1_H_2), with tritons treated as "elementary" entities moving in an average binding potential. They extract one-triton separation energies for these pure triton constituent nuclei using experimental binding energies and compare the resulting stability pattern to known magic numbers and exotic nuclei behavior. The model is then applied to interpret the charge quenching in quasielastic electron scattering by decomposing target nuclei into a stable triton-cluster core plus free protons, and to explain suppressed knockout cross sections through reduced wave function overlap between initial and final triton-cluster states.

## Key numbers

- Missing electric charge in the Coulomb Sum Rule ranged from 0% in 3He to 40% in 40Ca and 56Fe, and to about 50% in 208Pb, based on data summarized by Richter (1993)
- The ground state of 4He is dominated by p+t and n+h cluster configurations, with the (pt)+(nh) configuration giving -25.17 MeV compared to the full calculation of -26.50 MeV and experiment of -28.30 MeV
- The model predicts six prominent magic nuclei: 24_8_O_16, 60_20_Ca_40, 105_35_Br_70, 123_41_Nb_82, 189_63_Eu_126, and 276_92_U_184
- The proton size is approximately 0.8 fm, while the triton size is approximately 1.8 fm
- For 40Ca, the model predicts quasielastic scattering will count only ten protons (half of the expected twenty protons)

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier triton-cluster model based on hidden color and Quantum Chromodynamics (Abbas 2001, 2004, 2005, 2011, 2018, 2019, 2020) which has been applied to halo structures, new magic numbers, and nuclear fusion. The model directly addresses the long-standing puzzle of missing charge in quasielastic electron scattering documented by Richter (1993) and Morgenstern and Meziani (2001), and connects it to two-proton knockout reactions studied by Fridmann et al. on 44S and by Fullon et al. on 32Mg. The authors' predictions of magicity for 24O and 60Ca have been confirmed experimentally by Kanungo et al. and Tarasov et al., respectively. The work enables a unified framework for understanding charge quenching in medium-heavy nuclei and the island-of-inversion phenomena, with the expectation that Jefferson Laboratory experiments may provide final resolution of the quasielastic puzzle.
