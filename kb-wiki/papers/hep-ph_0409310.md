# The QCD phase diagram at nonzero baryon, isospin and strangeness chemical potentials: Results from a hadron resonance gas model

> We use a hadron resonance gas model to study the QCD phase diagram at nonzero temperature, baryon, isospin and strangeness chemical potentials. We determine the temperature of the transition from the hadronic phase to the quark gluon plasma phase using two different methods. We find that the critical temperatures derived in both methods are in very good agreement. We find that the critical surface has a small curvature. We also find that the critical temperature's dependence on the baryon chemical potential at zero isospin chemical potential is almost identical to its dependence on the isospin chemical potential at vanishing baryon chemical potential. This result, which holds when the chemical potentials are small, supports recent lattice simulation studies. Finally, we find that at a given baryon chemical potential, the critical temperature is lowered as either the isospin or the strangeness chemical potential are increased. Therefore, in order to lower the critical temperature, it might be useful to use different isotopes in heavy ion collision experiments.

## Key claim

Using a hadron resonance gas model, the critical temperature for the transition from hadronic matter to quark-gluon plasma depends almost identically on baryon chemical potential at zero isospin as it does on isospin chemical potential at zero baryon chemical potential, but this equivalence holds only when the chemical potentials are small.

## Method

The authors modeled the hadronic phase as a free gas of all hadron resonances up to 2 GeV, computing the pressure, energy density, and quark-antiquark condensate from the grand canonical partition function at nonzero temperature, baryon chemical potential, isospin chemical potential, and strangeness chemical potential. They determined the critical temperature using two independent criteria: (1) lines of constant energy density epsilon = 0.5-1.0 GeV/fm^3, as observed on the lattice, and (2) the condition that the light quark-antiquark condensate vanishes at the transition.

## Key numbers

- At zero chemical potentials, the critical temperature from the energy density criterion is T_c = 176 +/- 8 MeV.
- At zero chemical potentials, the critical temperature from the quark-antiquark condensate criterion is T_c = 185 +/- 6 MeV.
- The fitted critical temperature from the energy density method follows T_c/T_0 = 1 - 0.021(2)(mu_B/T_0)^2 - 0.039(1)(mu_I/T_0)^2 - 0.037(2)(mu_S/T_0)^2 - 0.031(3)(mu_B mu_S/T_0^2), with R^2 = 0.994.
- The fitted critical temperature from the condensate method follows T_c/T_0 = 1 - 0.017(1)(mu_B/T_0)^2 - 0.109(4)(mu_I/T_0)^2 - 0.032(2)(mu_S/T_0)^2 - 0.024(2)(mu_B mu_S/T_0^2), with R^2 = 0.991.
- The energy density criterion constrains the critical temperature to a band of approximately 15 MeV; the condensate criterion also constrains it to a band of approximately 15 MeV.
- The critical surface has small curvature, with the critical temperature decreasing slowly as baryon, isospin, or strangeness chemical potentials increase.

## Context

This work extends lattice QCD studies at nonzero baryon chemical potential (Fodor and Katz, 2002; Allton et al., 2002) and nonzero isospin chemical potential (Kogut and Sinclair, 2002) by using a hadron resonance gas model to explore the full three-dimensional phase diagram in baryon, isospin, and strangeness chemical potentials. The results support the lattice finding that the critical temperature depends similarly on small baryon and isospin chemical potentials, and they contradict earlier model studies (random matrix theory, NJL model, ladder QCD) that suggested an arbitrarily small isospin chemical potential could greatly alter the phase diagram. The work enables predictions for heavy ion collision experiments, suggesting that using different isotopes could lower the critical temperature for quark-gluon plasma formation.
