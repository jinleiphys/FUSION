# Collective multipole excitations based on correlated realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions

> We investigate collective multipole excitations for closed shell nuclei from 16O to 208Pb using correlated realistic nucleon -nucleon interactions in the framework of the random phase approximation (RPA). The dominant short-range central and tensor correlations a re treated explicitly within the Unitary Correlation Operator Method (UCOM), which provides a phase-shift equivalent correlated interaction V_UCOM adapted to simple uncorrelated Hilbert spaces. The same unitary transformation that defines the correlated interaction is used to derive correlated transition operators. Using V_UCOM we solve the Hartree-Fock problem and employ the single-particle state s as starting point for the RPA. By construction, the UCOM-RPA is fully self-consistent, i.e. the same correlated nucleon-nucleon interact ion is used in calculations of the HF ground state and in the residual RPA interaction. Consequently, the spurious state associated with t he center-of-mass motion is properly removed and the sum-rules are exhausted within +-3%. The UCOM-RPA scheme results in a collective character of giant monopole, dipole, and quadrupole resonances in closed-shell nuclei across the nuclear chart. For the isoscalar giant monopole resonance, the resonance energies are in agreement with experiment hinting at a reasonable compressibility. However, in the 1- and 2+ channels the resonance energies are overestimated due to missing long-range correlations and three-body contributions.

## Key claim

The UCOM-RPA framework, using a correlated realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction derived from the Argonne V18 potential via the Unitary Correlation Operator Method, reproduces experimental energies for the isoscalar giant monopole resonance across closed-shell nuclei from 16O to 208Pb, but systematically overestimates resonance energies in the 1- and 2+ channels due to missing long-range correlations and three-body contributions.

## Method

The authors first constructed a correlated interaction V_UCOM from the Argonne V18 potential using the Unitary Correlation Operator Method, which treats short-range central and tensor correlations via explicit unitary transformations. They solved the Hartree-Fock equations with this correlated interaction to obtain single-particle states, then used the same interaction to build the residual particle-hole interaction in a fully self-consistent random phase approximation (UCOM-RPA). Correlated transition operators were derived from the same unitary transformation and applied to compute transition strengths for monopole, dipole, and quadrupole excitations.

## Key numbers

- Sum rules were exhausted within ±3% across all studied nuclei, confirming self-consistency of the UCOM-RPA scheme.
- For the isoscalar giant monopole resonance, calculated energies agreed with experimental data, indicating reasonable nuclear compressibility.
- In the 1- and 2+ channels, resonance energies were overestimated relative to experiment, with centroid energies systematically too high by amounts not explicitly quantified in the abstract or truncated text.
- The standard tensor correlator used had a constraint value I_ϑ^(S=1,T=0) = 0.09 fm³, with additional calculations performed at 0.07 and 0.08 fm³ to probe sensitivity.
- The central correlator constraint was I_R+^(S=0,T=0) = 0.1 fm⁴.

## Context

This work extends the UCOM approach, previously applied to ground-state properties in Hartree-Fock and many-body perturbation theory by Roth et al. (2005) and Paar et al. (2005), to collective excitations for the first time. It contrasts with phenomenological approaches such as Skyrme (Vautherin and Brink, 1972) and Gogny (Decharge and Gogny, 1980) interactions, and with the V_low-k potential (Bogner et al., 2003), by using a phase-shift equivalent correlated interaction derived from a realistic potential. The results enable future inclusion of long-range correlations and three-body forces to improve agreement in the dipole and quadrupole channels, and provide a microscopic foundation for describing giant resonances across the nuclear chart.
