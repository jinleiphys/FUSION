# The puzzle of complete fusion suppression in weakly-bound nuclei: a Trojan Horse effect?

> Experimental studies of nuclear collisions involving light weakly-bound nuclei show a systematic suppression of the complete fusion cross section by $\sim$30\% with respect to the expectation for tightly bound nuclei, at energies above the Coulomb barrier. Although it is widely accepted that the phenomenon is related to the weak binding of these nuclei, the origin of this suppression is not fully understood. In here, we present a novel approach that provides the complete fusion for weakly bound nuclei and relates its suppression to the competition between the different mechanisms contributing to the reaction cross section. The method is applied to the $^{6,7}$Li+$^{209}$Bi reactions, where we find that the suppression of complete fusion is mostly caused by the flux associated with non-elastic breakup modes, such as the partial capture of the projectile (incomplete fusion), whereas the elastic breakup mode is found to play a minor role. Finally, we demonstrate that the large $α$ yields observed in these reactions can be naturally explained as a consequence of a {\it Trojan Horse} mechanism.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the systematic ~30% suppression of complete fusion in collisions of weakly-bound nuclei (e.g., ⁶,⁷Li) is primarily caused by non-elastic breakup modes, specifically the partial capture of one projectile fragment (incomplete fusion), rather than by elastic breakup, and that the large alpha yields observed in these reactions arise from a Trojan Horse mechanism rather than a conventional two-step breakup-fusion process.

## Method

The authors decompose the total reaction cross section into contributions from complete fusion, inelastic scattering, elastic breakup (EBU), and non-elastic breakup (NEB) for each projectile fragment. Elastic breakup cross sections are computed using the continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) method, while non-elastic breakup cross sections are evaluated using the Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) inclusive breakup model in its DWBA approximation. Complete fusion is then inferred by subtracting the calculated EBU and NEB cross sections from the reaction cross section, and the method is applied to the ⁶,⁷Li + ²⁰⁹Bi reactions at energies around and above the Coulomb barrier.

## Key numbers

- Complete fusion suppression of ~30% for ⁶Li + ²⁰⁹Bi and ~25% for ⁷Li + ²⁰⁹Bi, relative to single-barrier penetration model predictions, at energies above the Coulomb barrier (Vb ≈ 30 MeV).
- For ⁶Li + ²⁰⁹Bi at 36 MeV (1.2 Vb), varying the projectile separation energy artificially shows that as separation energy increases beyond ~3–4 MeV, the EBU contribution becomes negligible and the calculated CF cross section approaches the BPM limit.
- For ⁷Li + ²⁰⁹Bi at 44 MeV (1.5 Vb), the same trend is observed: at large separation energies the CF cross section tends to the BPM value.
- The Coulomb barrier for the d + ²⁰⁹Bi and t + ²⁰⁹Bi subsystems is ~10–11 MeV; the three-body ²⁰⁹Bi(⁶,⁷Li,αX) cross sections remain large even at energies well below this barrier, illustrating the Trojan Horse mechanism.

## Context

This work extends previous experimental studies by Dasgupta et al. (2002, 2004) that documented the systematic suppression of complete fusion for weakly-bound projectiles, and addresses the failure of classical dynamical models by Diaz-Torres et al. (2007) and Cook et al. (2016, 2018) to reproduce the full suppression magnitude. The method enables a unified quantum-mechanical description of complete fusion, incomplete fusion, and alpha-particle yields, and supports the conjecture by Cook et al. (2018) that clustering and weak binding, rather than breakup in the usual sense, are responsible for the suppression. The Trojan Horse mechanism invoked here was originally described by Baur (1986) and is widely used in nuclear astrophysics as an indirect method for low-energy charged-particle reactions.
