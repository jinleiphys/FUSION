# Matter radii from interaction cross sections using microscopic nuclear densities

> Understanding how nuclear size evolves with the number of protons and neutrons tests our models of strongly interacting matter. The nuclear charge (and proton) radii accessible through electromagnetic probes carry fundamental information on the saturation density and nuclear correlations. The radii of the neutron distribution are more difficult to measure, but they are important for our understanding of the isovector properties of nuclei that depend on the proton-to-neutron asymmetry, and on extended nucleonic matter in neutron stars. Interaction cross sections offer one of the few direct experimental windows into the neutron radii of nuclei far from stability, but translating these measurements into reliable structural information requires an integrated theoretical framework that links structure and reactions with a rigorous treatment of uncertainty. In this work, we compute interaction cross sections by using uncertainty-quantified proton and neutron distributions obtained in the self-consistent nuclear Density Functional Theory (DFT) with the Fayans energy density functional. The resulting densities are used in a modernized Glauber reaction framework, which features the refit of nucleon-nucleon profile functions. Applying this pipeline to the existing data on the calcium isotopic chain, we find no evidence for the dramatic neutron swelling reported earlier. While focusing here on the Ca chain, the methodology proposed in this work is applicable to interaction cross section measurements across the nuclear chart and is well-suited for new experiments currently planned at leading rare isotope facilities.

## Key claim

Using an integrated pipeline that combines uncertainty-quantified densities from the Fayans energy density functional with a modernized Glauber reaction framework featuring recalibrated nucleon-nucleon profile functions, the authors find no evidence for the dramatic neutron swelling in neutron-rich calcium isotopes that was reported in earlier analyses (Tanaka 2020). The tension that remains for the most neutron-rich isotopes is only at the 2σ confidence level.

## Method

The authors compute interaction cross sections for calcium projectiles on a carbon-12 target at 280 MeV/nucleon using the modified optical limit approximation within the Glauber model. Proton and neutron densities are obtained from self-consistent nuclear Density Functional Theory using the Fayans energy density functional Fy(IVP3,0.9), with parametric uncertainties propagated through an ensemble of 500 physically credible samples. For each density sample, the three free parameters of the nucleon-nucleon profile functions are recalibrated against measured interaction cross sections of the stable and long-lived calcium isotopes 42-48Ca, and uncertainties from the profile function calibration and from evaluated NN cross sections are propagated to produce final confidence intervals.

## Key numbers

- Parametric uncertainties from the profile function calibration represent only 3% of the interaction cross sections for all calcium isotopes.
- The approximation of interaction cross sections by reaction cross sections carries an estimated error of about 2%, based on data on magnesium-37 at 240 MeV/nucleon (Takechi 2014).
- The recalibration of profile functions uses measured interaction cross sections for calcium isotopes 42-48Ca at 280 MeV/nucleon from Tanaka (2020).
- None of the theoretical calculations pass the χ²-test at the 1σ confidence level, but all pass at the 2σ and 3σ confidence levels.

## Context

This work extends the Glauber analysis framework by replacing the schematic two-parameter Fermi-type densities used in prior work (Ozawa 2001, Tanaka 2020, Suzuki 1995) with microscopic densities from the Fayans energy density functional, which accurately reproduces charge radii along the calcium chain (Miller 2019, Garcia Ruiz 2016). The methodology resolves discrepancies between theoretical predictions and extracted radii reported in earlier studies (Teixeira 2022, Tanaka 2020, Ponnath 2025) by enforcing consistency between structure and reaction descriptions and by properly quantifying uncertainties. The pipeline is designed for immediate application to upcoming experiments at rare isotope facilities such as FRIB and RIKEN, enabling statistically rigorous extraction of matter and neutron radii across the nuclear chart.
