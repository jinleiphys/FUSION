# The Nuclear Physics of Hyperfine Structure in Hydrogenic Atoms

> The theory of QED corrections to hyperfine structure in light hydrogenic atoms and ions has recently advanced to the point that the uncertainty of these corrections is much smaller than 1 part per million (ppm), while the experiments are even more accurate. The difference of the experimental results and the corresponding QED theory is due to nuclear effects, which are primarily the result of the finite nuclear charge and magnetization distributions. This difference varies from tens to hundreds of ppm. We have calculated the dominant nuclear component of the 1s hyperfine interval for deuterium, tritium and singly ionized helium, using a unified approach with modern second-generation potentials. The calculated nuclear corrections are within 3% of the experimental values for deuterium and tritium, but are roughly 20% discrepant for helium. The nuclear corrections for the trinucleon systems can be qualitatively understood by invoking SU(4) symmetry.

# The Nuclear Physics of Hyperfine Structure in Hydrogenic Atoms

## Key claim

The dominant nuclear component of the 1s hyperfine interval for deuterium, tritium, and singly ionized helium-3 can be calculated using a unified approach with modern second-generation potentials, yielding nuclear corrections within 3% of experimental values for deuterium and tritium but roughly 20% discrepant for helium-3. The authors note that missing isovector meson-exchange currents likely account for the larger discrepancy in the helium case.

## Method

The authors calculated the leading-order nuclear contribution to hyperfine structure by evaluating the Low moment, a two-body integral over nuclear charge and current densities, using the impulse-approximation one-body charge and current operators with finite-size nucleon distributions. They employed the AV18 two-nucleon potential plus a TM-prime three-nucleon force for the trinucleon systems, and used a power-counting scheme borrowed from chiral perturbation theory to drop subleading relativistic corrections and meson-exchange currents. The nuclear matrix elements were decomposed into one-body spin-magnetization terms and two-body Low terms (spin-magnetization and convection current contributions), with the results expressed as fractions of the Fermi hyperfine energy.

## Key numbers

- Proton Zemach moment: 1.086(12) fm, determined from electron-scattering data, leading to a -58.2(6) kHz contribution to hydrogen 1s hyperfine structure, equivalent to -41.0(5) ppm
- Neutron Zemach moment: -0.042 fm (calculated from dipole and Galster form factors)
- Nuclear correction for deuterium: 46.2 kHz total (one-body -41.1 kHz, two-body 87.3 kHz)
- Nuclear correction for tritium: -60.1 kHz total (one-body -50.6 kHz, two-body -9.6 kHz)
- Nuclear correction for helium-3 ion: 1442 kHz total (one-body 13.9 kHz, two-body 1428 kHz)
- Residual discrepancy after including hadronic corrections: 3.2(5) ppm for hydrogen, -3.1 ppm for deuterium, 1.2 ppm for tritium, -46 ppm for helium-3 ion
- Intrinsic size of nuclear corrections given by (-2 m_e alpha R) = -38 ppm times [R/fm], where R is the Low moment in fm

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations of hyperfine structure in light hydrogenic atoms that relied on the Breit approximation and adiabatic nuclear treatments, such as those by Bohr and Low for the deuteron and by various authors for trinucleon systems (referenced as d-th-0, d-th-1, t-th-1, t-th-2, he-th, d-t-he). The authors provide the first unified calculation using modern second-generation nuclear forces (AV18 potential) and a chiral perturbation theory power-counting scheme. The results enable more precise tests of QED in few-nucleon systems and identify missing isovector meson-exchange currents as the likely source of the remaining 20% discrepancy in helium-3, motivating future calculations that include these contributions.
