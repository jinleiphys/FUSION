# Liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear matter including strangeness

> We apply the chiral SU(3) quark mean field model to study the properties of strange hadronic matter at finite temperature. The liquid-gas phase transition is studied as a function of the strangeness fraction. The pressure of the system cannot remain constant during the phase transition, since there are two independent conserved charges (baryon and strangeness number). In a range of temperatures around 15 MeV (precise values depending on the model used) the equation of state exhibits multiple bifurcates. The difference in the strangeness fraction $f_s$ between the liquid and gas phases is small when they coexist. The critical temperature of strange matter turns out to be a non-trivial function of the strangeness fraction.

## Key claim

The liquid-gas phase transition in strange hadronic matter, unlike symmetric nuclear matter, does not occur at constant pressure because baryon number and strangeness are two independent conserved charges; the equation of state exhibits multiple bifurcates in a temperature range around 15 MeV, with the precise values depending on the model used (square-root or linear definition of effective baryon mass).

## Method

The authors applied the chiral SU(3) quark mean field model, which includes quarks confined in baryons by an effective potential and meson self-interactions based on SU(3) chiral symmetry, to study strange hadronic matter at finite temperature. They solved the mean-field equations for the scalar mesons (σ, ζ, χ) and vector mesons (ω, φ) and used the Gibbs conditions (equal chemical potentials and pressure in two phases) to determine liquid-gas phase coexistence as a function of baryon density, temperature, and strangeness fraction f_s. Two treatments of the effective baryon mass were compared: the square-root ansatz and the linear definition.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature of infinite nuclear matter (non-strange) from experiment: T_c = 16.6 ± 0.86 MeV (Natowitz et al.)
- At T = 15 MeV with the square-root ansatz, three critical strangeness fractions exist: f_s ≈ 0.24, 1.06, and 1.66
- For the square-root ansatz at T = 15 MeV, the critical pressure at point A is about 0.244 MeV·fm⁻³ with corresponding f_s = 0.24
- For the square-root ansatz, the critical temperature for strange hadronic matter with f_s = 2 (pure Ξ matter) is about 13.6 MeV
- For the linear definition of effective baryon mass, the highest critical temperature is 17.9 MeV (f_s = 0) and the lowest is 12.0 MeV (f_s = 1.1)
- For the linear definition, there are two critical points in the temperature range 12.0 < T < 15.9 MeV
- For the square-root ansatz, three critical strangeness fractions exist in the temperature range 14.6 < T < 15.3 MeV

## Context

This work extends the study of liquid-gas phase transitions in nuclear matter to include strangeness, following earlier work on asymmetric nuclear matter with two conserved charges (baryon number and isospin) by Muller and Serot, and by Wang et al. using effective chiral models. It directly addresses a problem in the work of Yang et al., who used the extended FST model and found a "critical pressure" above which the liquid-gas phase transition could not exist; the present authors show that for infinite hadronic matter no such critical pressure exists and that the binodal p-μ diagram should terminate at f_s = 2. The results enable future experimental searches for the limiting temperature of hypernuclei and the critical temperature of infinite strange hadronic matter.
