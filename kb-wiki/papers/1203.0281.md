# Shear Viscosities from the Chapman-Enskog and the Relaxation Time Approaches

> The interpretation of the measured elliptic and higher order collective flows in heavy-ion collisions in terms of viscous hydrodynamics depends sensitively on the ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density. Here we perform a quantitative comparison between the results of shear viscosities from the Chapman-Enskog and relaxation time methods for selected test cases with specified elastic differential cross sections: (i) The non-relativistic, relativistic and ultra-relativistic hard sphere gas with angle and energy independent differential cross section (ii) The Maxwell gas, (iii) chiral pions and (iv) massive pions for which the differential elastic cross section is taken from experiments. Our quantitative results reveal that (i) the extent of agreement (or disagreement) depends sensitively on the energy dependence of the differential cross sections employed, and (ii) stress the need to perform quantum molecular dynamical (URQMD) simulations that employ Green-Kubo techniques with similar cross sections to validate the codes employed and to test the accuracy of other methods.

## Key claim

The quantitative agreement between shear viscosities calculated via the Chapman-Enskog and relaxation time methods depends sensitively on the energy dependence of the differential cross sections employed, rather than being universally consistent across all systems.

## Method

The authors performed a quantitative comparison of shear viscosities calculated using the Chapman-Enskog approximation (with first, second, and third order expansions in Laguerre polynomials) and the relaxation time approximation for four test cases: (i) a hard sphere gas with constant differential cross section at non-relativistic, relativistic, and ultra-relativistic limits; (ii) the Maxwell gas; (iii) chiral pions; and (iv) massive pions with experimentally determined elastic differential cross sections. The relativistic transport equation was solved for each case using the specified elastic differential cross sections, with the Chapman-Enskog method employing the full linearized collision integral while the relaxation time method used a simplified collision term with a single relaxation time parameter.

## Key numbers

- For the hard sphere gas in the ultra-relativistic limit, the first Chapman-Enskog approximation gives shear viscosity [η_s]_1 = (k_B T)/(π a^2) × (1/c) × 1.2, where a is the hard sphere radius
- For the hard sphere gas in the ultra-relativistic limit, the third Chapman-Enskog approximation gives shear viscosity [η_s]_3 = (k_B T)/(π a^2) × (1/c) × 1.2642
- For the hard sphere gas with small mass (z = mc^2/kT → 0), the shear viscosity takes the form [η_s]_p = (mc)/(π a^2) × z^(-1) × F_p × (1 + F'_p × z^2 + ...), with F_1 = 1.2 and F'_1 = 0.05 for the first approximation
- In the non-relativistic limit, the Chapman-Enskog shear viscosity reduces to η_s = (5/8) × (k_B T)/Ω_2^(2), where Ω_2^(2) is the non-relativistic omega integral

## Context

This work extends the classic formalism of Chapman and Cowling (1970) and Hirschfelder, Curtiss, and Bird (1954) for non-relativistic systems, and de Groot (1980) for relativistic systems, by providing a systematic quantitative comparison between two commonly used approximation methods for shear viscosity. The results enable validation of quantum molecular dynamical simulations using Green-Kubo techniques, as the authors stress the need for such simulations with similar cross sections to test the accuracy of both methods. The work also builds upon earlier calculations of shear viscosity for chiral pions by Prakash et al. (1993) and for neutrinos by de Groot (1975), and provides test cases that can be used to validate numerical codes in heavy-ion physics applications.
