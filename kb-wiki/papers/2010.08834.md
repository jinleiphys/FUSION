# Future Physics Perspectives on the Equation of State from Heavy Ion Collisions to Neutron Stars

> With the computational power and algorithmic improvements available today, the ongoing STAR/RHIC and HADES/GSI experiments, the future FAIR and NICA facilities becoming operational, and the new precise measurements from NICER and LIGO/VIRGO, the high-energy nuclear physics and astrophysics communities are in the unique position to set very stringent constraints on the equation of state of strongly interacting matter. We review the state-of-the-art of different approaches used in the description of hot and ultradense baryonic matter in and out of equilibrium, and discuss the regions in the phase diagram where heavy-ion collisions and neutron star mergers can overlap. Future perspectives are discussed to help define a comprehensive, multi-disciplinary strategy to map out the phase diagram of strongly interacting matter from heavy ion collisions to neutron stars.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the combination of ongoing and future heavy-ion experiments (STAR/RHIC BESII, HADES/GSI, FAIR, NICA) with precise astrophysical observations (NICER, LIGO/VIRGO) can set stringent constraints on the equation of state of strongly interacting matter, but they emphasize that this requires a coordinated multi-disciplinary strategy because different communities explore different regions of the QCD phase diagram with different conditions on conserved charges (baryon, strangeness, electric charge).

## Method

The paper reviews the state-of-the-art of lattice QCD calculations at zero and finite baryon chemical potential, Taylor expansion methods for the equation of state, hadron resonance gas models, and QCD-inspired phenomenological models for dense matter. It also discusses model-agnostic approaches to extracting the equation of state from gravitational wave data, including parametric methods (piecewise polytropes, spectral representations) and non-parametric methods (Gaussian processes conditioned on nuclear theory models). The review is based on discussions from a 3-day virtual workshop held in August 2020 that brought together lattice QCD theorists, heavy-ion phenomenologists and experimentalists, nuclear astrophysicists, and gravitational wave physicists.

## Key numbers

- Lattice QCD results for the equation of state are available in the continuum limit for physical quark masses with $N_f=2+1$ and $N_f=2+1+1$ quark flavors at zero baryon chemical potential
- Taylor expansion coefficients for the pressure are available up to sixth-order for both $\mu_S=\mu_Q=0$ and strangeness-neutral conditions, with estimates for $c_8$ at finite lattice spacing
- A reliable equation of state from first principles can be reconstructed up to $\mu_B/T \leq 2$ using currently available Taylor expansion coefficients
- Lattice QCD results can only be calculated down to approximately $T \sim 100-130$ MeV
- The STAR Fixed-Target program covers $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 3-7.7$ GeV, HADES at GSI covers $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 1-3$ GeV, FAIR covers $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 4.5-9.3$ GeV, and NICA covers $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 3-5$ GeV
- Protons and neutrons begin to overlap at densities around 3 times saturation density ($\sim 0.5$ baryons per fm$^3$)
- Neutron star cores may reach 4-10 times nuclear saturation density
- A neutron star with mass larger than 2.5 solar masses (e.g., the secondary compact object from GW190814) would imply a sharp rise in $c_s^2$ between 2-3 times nuclear saturation density
- The advanced LIGO A+ upgrade is expected to conclude by 2025

## Context

This review extends the discussion of the QCD phase diagram from earlier works such as the lattice QCD results of Borsanyi (2010, 2013, 2016) and Bazavov (2014, 2017), and the critical point predictions of Stephanov (1998, 2007). It builds on the family of equations of state containing a critical point constructed by Parotto (2018) and the holographic model of Critelli (2017) that successfully predicts baryon susceptibilities later confirmed on the lattice. The paper enables a coordinated strategy between the heavy-ion and neutron-star communities to map out the full four-dimensional phase diagram in temperature, baryon chemical potential, strangeness chemical potential, and electric charge chemical potential, addressing open questions about the existence and location of the QCD critical point and the nature of phase transitions at high density.
