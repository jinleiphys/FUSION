# Quark matter with strong magnetic field and possibility of the third family of compact stars

> We consider the possibility for the existence of the third family of compact objects, considering the effect of strong magnetic fields inside the hybrid stars. As a result, we demonstrate such new sequences of stable equilibrium configurations for some hadronic equations of state. Through the analysis of the adiabatic index inside stars, we find the conditions for appearing the third family of compact objects, i.e., for hadronic stars without quarks, that the maximum mass should be small, the central density for the maximum mass should be also small, and the radius for the the maximum mass should be large. Even for soft hadronic equations of state, the two solar-mass stars might survive as the third family of compact objects, once quark matter with strong magnetic field, such as $\sim {\cal O}(10^{19} {\rm G})$, is taken into account. It might give a hint to solve the so-called hyperon puzzle in nuclear physics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a third family of compact stars (hybrid stars more compact than neutron stars) can exist for some hadronic equations of state that include hyperons, provided quark matter in the core is subject to a strong magnetic field of order 10^19 G such that quarks occupy only the lowest Landau level. The existence of such a third family depends on the hadronic equation of state having a small maximum mass, small central density at maximum mass, and large radius at maximum mass; even soft hadronic equations of state that are normally ruled out by the 2 solar mass observation might be revived through this mechanism.

## Method

The authors construct hybrid star models by connecting hadronic equations of state (the Shen EOS and its modifications including hyperons) to a quark matter equation of state derived from the MIT bag model under the assumption that quarks occupy only the lowest Landau level (the "Landau EOS"), with the connection made at a transition energy density epsilon_L. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain equilibrium sequences and identify the third family as a new stable branch appearing at central densities beyond the maximum central density of the corresponding pure hadronic neutron star sequence.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of pure hadronic neutron stars: 2.21 solar masses (Shen EOS), 1.90 solar masses (Shen with Lambda hyperons), 1.64 solar masses (Shen with Y hyperons), 1.66 solar masses (Shen with Y and pions)
- Corresponding radii at maximum mass: 12.6 km (Shen), 12.5 km (Shen Lambda), 13.5 km (Shen Y), 12.6 km (Shen Y pi)
- Corresponding central densities at maximum mass: 1.82 x 10^15 g/cm^3 (Shen), 1.86 x 10^15 g/cm^3 (Shen Lambda), 1.32 x 10^15 g/cm^3 (Shen Y), 1.62 x 10^15 g/cm^3 (Shen Y pi)
- Critical magnetic field strength for lowest Landau level occupation: approximately 5 x 10^19 G (for transition density 1.4 x 10^15 g/cm^3 with Shen Y EOS)
- Bag constants used: range from 247.1 MeV/fm^3 to 470.2 MeV/fm^3 for the cases shown
- Transition densities examined: 1.0, 1.4, 2.0 x 10^15 g/cm^3 (Shen Y EOS); 1.40, 1.83, 2.40 x 10^15 g/cm^3 (Shen EOS)
- Observational constraint: millisecond pulsar J1614-2230 mass = (1.97 +/- 0.04) solar masses

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Sotani and Tatsumi 2015) which showed that strong magnetic fields stiffen the quark matter equation of state, and applies it to the question of the third family of compact stars originally discussed by Gerlach (1968) and Baym (1977). The paper addresses the "hyperon puzzle" -- the fact that hyperon-containing equations of state typically predict maximum masses below 2 solar masses, contradicting observations of massive neutron stars (Demorest et al. 2010, Antoniadis et al. 2013). The results suggest that soft equations of state with hyperons, which have been ruled out by the 2 solar mass observation, could be revived if quark matter with strong magnetic fields produces a third family of compact objects with masses reaching 2 solar masses. This enables future observational searches for "twin stars" (objects with similar masses but different radii) and studies of cooling histories to distinguish third-family objects from ordinary neutron stars.
