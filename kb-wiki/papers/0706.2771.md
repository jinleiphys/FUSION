# Status of art of reaction models for projectiles far from stability

> This talk will review the status of art of nuclear and Coulomb breakup theories and their relation to optical models of elastic scattering of exotic projectiles. The effect of the final state interactions between the breakup particle and the core and target nuclei will be clarified and some typical numerical calculations for the relevant observables will be presented and compared to experimental data. Finally new results will be shown to demonstrate the feasibility of a novel type of experiment involving heavy projectiles far from stability on heavy targets.

# Status of art of reaction models for projectiles far from stability

## Key claim
Breakup reactions account for up to 10% of the total reaction cross section for halo nuclei due to valence neutron separation energies below 1 MeV, making breakup the most studied reaction for weak beams out of necessity. The paper demonstrates that higher-order multipole terms in the Coulomb interaction, rather than the dipole approximation alone, are responsible for asymmetry in core parallel momentum distributions after proton knockout, with nuclear interactions introducing additional asymmetry through interference.

## Method
The author reviews semiclassical time-dependent models for nuclear and Coulomb breakup, treating the projectile-target relative motion along a classical trajectory with constant velocity and impact parameter. The formalism uses three-body variables (nucleon, core, target coordinates) with complex optical potentials for neutron-target and core-target interactions, and a core survival probability parameterized by a strong absorption radius. For Coulomb breakup, the amplitude is decomposed into recoil, direct, and nuclear contributions, with the Coulomb phase treated via a screening method that separates short-range (sudden approximation) and long-range (first-order perturbation) components.

## Key numbers
- Halo nuclei such as $^{11}$Be, $^{12}$Be, $^{14}$Be, and $^{11}$Li have neutron separation energies S$_n$ less than 1 MeV, compared to the average 8 MeV in nuclear matter
- Up to 10% of the total reaction cross section is due to 1n or 2n breakup channels for these halo nuclei
- The strong absorption radius R$_s$ is parameterized as approximately 1.4(A$_p^{1/3}$+A$_t^{1/3}$) fm, with a diffuseness parameter a=0.6 fm
- For $^{11}$Be, the mass ratio $\beta_1$ of the neutron to the projectile is approximately 0.1
- Calculations for $^{34}$Si beam against Pb at 70 A.MeV show integrated breakup cross-sections as a function of neutron separation energy for s-wave, d-wave, and f-wave initial states
- Momentum distributions of $^7$Be fragments after proton-removal from $^8$B against Pb at 936 MeV/A are compared to data from Lola (2003)
- n-$^{10}$Be relative energy spectra for $^{11}$Be+$^{12}$C at 69 A.MeV are calculated with spectroscopic factor C$^2$S=0.74 for an s initial state with $\varepsilon_i$=-0.50 MeV
- n-$^{12}$Be relative energy spectra for $^{14}$Be+$^{12}$C at 250 A.MeV are calculated with $\varepsilon_i$=-1.85 MeV

## Context
This review extends the transfer-to-the-continuum method introduced by Bonaccorso and Brink (various years) and the eikonal breakup model of Yabana and collaborators (various years), comparing sudden approximation approaches with time-dependent perturbation theory. The work addresses the problem of asymmetry in core parallel momentum distributions after proton knockout, previously studied by Esbensen (1995), Hencken (1996), and Davids (2001), and demonstrates that higher multipole terms beyond the dipole approximation are essential. The formalism enables new experiments with heavy projectiles far from stability on heavy targets, where nuclear breakup can be studied without the asymmetries found in experiments by Enders (2001) and Gade (2005).
