# Nuclear Tetrahedral Symmetry: Possibly Present Throughout the Periodic Table

> More than half a century after the fundamental, spherical shell structure in nuclei has been established, theoretical predictions indicate that the shell-gaps comparable or even stronger than those at spherical shapes may exist. Group-theoretical analysis supported by realistic mean-field calculations indicate that the corresponding nuclei are characterized by the $T_d^D$ ('double-tetrahedral') group of symmetry, exact or approximate. The corresponding strong shell-gap structure is markedly enhanced by the existence of the 4-dimensional irreducible representations of the group in question and consequently it can be seen as a geometrical effect that does not depend on a particular realization of the mean-field. Possibilities of discovering the corresponding symmetry in experiment are discussed.

## Key claim
The paper predicts that tetrahedral ($T_d^D$) symmetry in nuclei, stabilized by strong interactions rather than electromagnetic forces, should produce shell gaps comparable to or larger than those at spherical shapes throughout the periodic table, but the authors hedge that this is a theoretical prediction with no experimental verification existing at the time of writing.

## Method
The authors performed group-theoretical analysis of the $T_d^D$ double-tetrahedral point group and its irreducible representations, supported by realistic mean-field calculations using a deformed Woods-Saxon potential. They employed the Strutinsky method with multidimensional minimization over 10 deformation parameters (quadrupole, octupole, and hexadecapole) to calculate total energy surfaces and identify tetrahedral minima for selected nuclei.

## Key numbers
- Proton tetrahedral shell gaps appear at $Z_t = 16$, 20, 32, 40, $56-58^*$, $70^*$, $90-94^*$, where asterisks denote gaps up to $\sim 3$ MeV or so
- Neutron tetrahedral gaps appear at $N_t = 16$, 20, 32, 40, $56-58^*$, $70^*$, $90-94^*$, 112, and 136/142
- For $^{80}_{40}$Zr$_{40}$, the tetrahedral equilibrium deformation is $\alpha_{32} = 0.13$ with a barrier of approximately 1 MeV
- For $^{108}_{40}$Zr$_{68}$, the tetrahedral equilibrium deformation is $\alpha_{32} = 0.13$
- For $^{160}_{70}$Yb$_{90}$, the tetrahedral equilibrium deformation is $\alpha_{32} = 0.15$ with the oblate minimum at 0.1 MeV above the prolate ground state
- For $^{242}_{100}$Fm$_{142}$, the tetrahedral equilibrium deformation is $\alpha_{32} = 0.11$ with the tetrahedral minimum 7.1 MeV above the ground state
- At $\alpha_{32} = 0.15$, parity expectation values are approximately $\pm 0.5$
- The gap at $Z=40$ is $\Delta E \sim 3$ MeV, and at $Z=32$ is $\Delta E > 2$ MeV
- The $T_d^D$ group contains 48 symmetry elements, compared to 96 for the octahedral $O_h^D$ group

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of non-axially symmetric octupole deformations, including the Strutinsky-method calculations by X. Jing-Dong et al. (1994) suggesting tetrahedral isomers in light Radium nuclei, and the Hartree-Fock calculations by Takami et al. (1998) finding $\alpha_{32}$ instability in light $Z=N$ nuclei. The paper predicts that tetrahedral symmetry should appear in dozens to hundreds of nuclei across the periodic table, and provides experimental signatures including 16-fold degenerate multiplets of particle-hole transitions and the absence of $E1$ inter-band transitions (unlike pear-shaped octupole nuclei), enabling future experimental searches for tetrahedral isomers.
