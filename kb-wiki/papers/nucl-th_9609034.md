# High-momentum proton removal from 16O and the (e,e'p) cross section

> The cross section for the removal of high-momentum protons from 16O is calculated for high missing energies. The admixture of high-momentum nucleons in the 16O ground state is obtained by calculating the single-hole spectral function directly in the finite nucleus with the inclusion of short-range and tensor correlations induced by a realistic meson-exchange interaction. The presence of high-momentum nucleons in the transition to final states in 15N at 60-100 MeV missing energy is converted to the coincidence cross section for the (e,e'p) reaction by including the coupling to the electromagnetic probe and the final state interactions of the outgoing proton in the same way as in the standard analysis of the experimental data. Detectable cross sections for the removal of a single proton at these high missing energies are obtained which are considerably larger at higher missing momentum than the corresponding cross sections for the p-wave quasihole transitions. Cross sections for these quasihole transitions are compared with the most recent experimental data available.

## Key claim

The paper calculates that detectable cross sections for high-momentum proton removal from 16O at missing energies of 60–100 MeV are considerably larger at higher missing momentum than the corresponding cross sections for p-wave quasihole transitions, but this result depends on the inclusion of short-range and tensor correlations from a realistic meson-exchange interaction and on the specific treatment of final state interactions via an optical potential.

## Method

The single-hole spectral function for 16O was calculated directly in the finite nucleus by solving the Dyson equation with a self-energy derived from a G-matrix based on the one-boson-exchange potential B of Machleidt, including 2p1h and 2h1p contributions to account for short-range and tensor correlations. The spectral function was then combined with the Distorted Wave Impulse Approximation (DWIA) formalism, using the CC1 prescription for the half off-shell electron-nucleon scattering amplitude and the Schwandt parametrization of the optical potential for final state interactions, to compute the reduced (e,e'p) cross section for transitions to states in 15N at high missing energies.

## Key numbers

- The experimental quasihole strength for the p1/2 and p3/2 states in 16O corresponds to about 65% (Leuschner et al., 1994), while theoretical results yield about 76% for these p states (Geurts et al., 1996).
- The depletion of single-particle strength due to short-range correlations is typically about 10%, as obtained both in nuclear matter calculations and in calculations for finite nuclei.
- The Hartree-Fock self-energy was calculated using a G-matrix solved for nuclear matter with Fermi momentum k_F = 1.4 fm^{-1} and starting energy -10 MeV.
- The oscillator length α = 1.72 fm^{-1} was chosen for the bound single-particle states in 16O.
- Experimental single-particle energies used for hole states were -47 MeV for s1/2, -21.8 MeV for p3/2, and -15.7 MeV for p1/2 states.
- The data points for the transition to the ground state of 15N were collected at a constant kinetic energy of 90 MeV in the center-of-mass system of the emitted proton and the residual nucleus.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations of high-momentum components in finite nuclei (Muther et al., 1994; Muther et al., 1995; Polls et al., 1995) by converting the spectral function into realistic (e,e'p) cross sections including final state interactions, following the DWIA formalism developed by the Pavia group (Boffi et al., 1981; Boffi et al., 1982; Boffi et al., 1991). The results aim to facilitate experimental verification of theoretical predictions about high-momentum components at high missing energy, which have been suggested by nuclear matter studies (Ciofi degli Atti et al., 1991) and are relevant for understanding the missing single-particle strength observed in experiments on 208Pb (Bobeldijk et al., 1994) and 16O (Blok et al., 1995).
