# Coulomb distortion and medium corrections in nucleon-removal reactions

> One-nucleon removal reactions at or above the Fermi energy are important tools to explore the single-particle structure of exotic nuclei. Experimental data must be compared with calculations to extract structure information, evaluate correlation effects in nuclei or determine reaction rates for nuclear astrophysics. However, there is insufficient knowledge to calculate accurately the cross sections for these reactions. We evaluate the contributions of the final state interaction (FSI) and of the medium modifications of the nucleon-nucleon interactions and obtain the shapes and magnitudes of momentum distributions. Such effects have been often neglected in the literature. Calculations for reactions at energies 35 - 1000 MeV/nucleon are reported and compared to published data. For consistency, the state-of-the-art eikonal method for stripping and diffraction dissociation is used. We find that the two effects are important and their relative contributions vary with the energy and with the atomic and mass number of the projectile involved. These two often neglected effects modify considerably the one-nucleon removal cross sections. As expected, the effect are largest at lower energies, around 50 MeV/nucleon and on heavy targets.

## Key claim

The authors show that final-state interactions (FSI), particularly Coulomb distortion, and medium modifications of nucleon-nucleon cross sections, both often neglected in knockout reaction analyses, modify one-nucleon removal cross sections considerably; the effects are largest at lower energies around 50 MeV/nucleon and on heavy targets, but remain non-negligible even at energies up to 100 MeV/nucleon and for light targets.

## Method

The authors used the eikonal method for stripping and diffraction dissociation, incorporating Coulomb distortion via a Coulomb phase factor in the S-matrix and medium corrections to the nucleon-nucleon cross sections using both a geometrical Pauli-blocking treatment and a Dirac-Brueckner parametrization. They calculated total cross sections and longitudinal and transverse momentum distributions for several knockout reactions on carbon and beryllium targets at beam energies from 35 to 1000 MeV/nucleon, comparing results with and without each correction to published experimental data.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction $^{12}$C($^{23}$Al,$^{22}$Mg)X at 50 MeV/u, the single-particle cross section for the $^{22}$Mg($0^{+}_{gs}$) $\otimes$ $\pi 1d_{5/2}$ configuration is 27.1 mb with full corrections, 33.8 mb without medium corrections, and 23.2 mb without Coulomb corrections.
- For the same reaction, the relative differences between full calculations and calculations without Coulomb corrections range from 15% to 20% across four core configurations; differences without medium corrections range from 13% to 24%.
- For $^{9}$Be($^{15}$O,$^{14}$N)X at 56 MeV/u, the calculated one-proton removal cross section is 78.79 mb with both corrections, 75.20 mb without Coulomb corrections, 93.98 mb without medium corrections, and 90.74 mb with neither correction.
- For the $^{9}$Be($^{15}$O,$^{14}$N)X reaction, the difference between full calculations and calculations without Coulomb corrections is about 5%, and between full calculations and calculations without medium effects is nearly 19%.
- The calculations cover beam energies from 35 to 1000 MeV/nucleon.

## Context

This work extends the study of medium corrections in knockout reactions first reported in Bertulani and Canto (2010) and addresses the Coulomb distortion effects discussed in Bertulani and Hussein (2004), which had been largely neglected in subsequent experimental analyses. The authors compare their calculations to published data for reactions including $^{12}$C($^{23}$Al,$^{22}$Mg)X at 50 MeV/u from Banu et al. (2011), $^{9}$Be($^{15}$O,$^{14}$N)X at 56 MeV/u from Jewell et al. (2004), and $^{12}$C($^{17}$C,$^{16}$B)X at 35 MeV/u from Lecouey et al. (2009). The results enable more accurate extraction of spectroscopic factors from knockout reactions and improve the reliability of using one-nucleon removal as an indirect method for nuclear astrophysics, particularly for reactions on heavy targets and at lower beam energies.
