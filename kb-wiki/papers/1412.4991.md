# Study of clustering structures through breakup reactions

> Models for the description of breakup reactions used to study the structure of exotic cluster structures like halos are reviewed. The sensitivity of these models to the projectile description is presented. Calculations are sensitive to the projectile ground state mostly through its asymptotic normalisation coefficient (ANC). They also probe the continuum of the projectile. This enables studying not only the bound states of the projectile but also its continuum, both resonant and non-resonant. This opens the possibility to study correlations between both halo neutrons in two-neutron halo nuclei.

## Key claim

Breakup reactions are a powerful tool for studying exotic cluster structures like halo nuclei, but extracting reliable structural information requires accurate nonperturbative reaction models because the reaction mechanism is more complicated than initially thought; the sensitivity of these models to the projectile description is mostly confined to the asymptotic tail of the ground-state wave function (the asymptotic normalisation coefficient, ANC) and also probes the projectile continuum, both resonant and non-resonant.

## Method

The paper reviews three nonperturbative reaction models used to analyse breakup data: the Continuum Discretised Coupled Channel method (CDCC), the Time-Dependent approach (TD), and the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA). Each model solves the three-body Schrodinger equation for a projectile described as a two-body system (inert core plus halo nucleon) interacting with a structureless target via optical potentials, with the projectile initially in its ground state. The models are compared for the breakup of 15C on Pb at 68A MeV, and their sensitivity to the projectile description is tested by varying the interior of the bound-state wave function while keeping the tail fixed, and by including or omitting resonant and non-resonant continuum states.

## Key numbers

- For 15C impinging on Pb at 68A MeV, all three models (CDCC, DEA, TD) agree very well with each other in the energy distribution and with data from Nak09, confirming the halo structure of 15C.
- For the breakup of 8B on Ni at 25 MeV, CDCC calculations show that changes in the interior of the projectile wave function do not affect breakup calculations; both curves are superimposed, indicating peripherality.
- For the nuclear breakup of 11Be on C at 67A MeV, a TD calculation including the d5/2 resonance produces a narrow peak in the energy distribution that, once folded with experimental energy resolution, agrees very well with measurements from Fuk04.
- For two-body projectiles, breakup cross sections can be affected by up to 40% depending on the choice of the core-halo potential in the continuum (CN06).
- For the Coulomb breakup of 6He on Pb at 240A MeV, the non-resonant continuum (mostly E1 strength) produces a broad peak when final-state interactions are considered, which disappears when the continuum is described by plane waves (BCD09).
- For the Coulomb breakup of 11Li on Pb at 70A MeV, the CCE model showed excellent agreement with experiment (PDB12), confirming the two-neutron halo in 11Li.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on breakup reaction modelling for halo nuclei, building on earlier developments of CDCC (Kam86, TNT01), TD (KYS94, CBM03c), and DEA (BCG05). It confirms the peripherality of breakup reactions (CN07), showing they are better suited to extract ANCs than spectroscopic factors. The paper also shows that breakup probes not only the ground state but also the continuum, both resonant (CGB04, Fuk04) and non-resonant (CN06, BCD09, KKM10). For three-body projectiles like 6He and 11Li, recent calculations suggest that double-differential cross sections (Dalitz plots) could be used to study correlations between both halo neutrons, opening the path to an extensive study of breakup observables for three-body projectiles.
