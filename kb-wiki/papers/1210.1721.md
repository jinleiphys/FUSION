# Strictly finite-range potential for light and heavy nuclei

> Strictly finite-range (SFR) potentials are exactly zero beyond their finite range. Single-particle energies and densities as well as S-matrix pole trajectories are studied in a few SFR potentials suited for the description of neutrons interacting with light and heavy nuclei. The SFR potentials considered are the standard cut-off Woods--Saxon (CWS) potentials and two potentials approaching zero smoothly: the SV potential introduced by Salamon and Vertse and the SS potential of Sahu and Sahu. The parameters of these latter were set so that the potentials may be similar to the CWS shape. The range of the SV and SS potentials scales with the cube root of the mass number of the core like the nuclear radius itself. For light nuclei a single term of the SV potential (with a single parameter) is enough for a good description of the neutron-nucleus interaction. The trajectories are compared with a bench-mark for which the starting points (belonging to potential depth zero) can be determined independently. Even the CWS potential is found to conform to this bench-mark if the range is identified with the cutoff radius. For the CWS potentials some trajectories show irregular shapes, while for the SV and SS potentials all trajectories behave regularly.

# Strictly finite-range potential for light and heavy nuclei

## Key claim

Strictly finite-range (SFR) potentials, which are exactly zero beyond a finite distance, can reproduce the single-particle properties of conventional cut-off Woods-Saxon (CWS) potentials for both light and heavy nuclei, but only the smooth SFR forms (SV and SS potentials) produce regular S-matrix pole trajectories without the unphysical loops and ripples seen in CWS potentials; the CWS potential conforms to the Newton benchmark for pole starting points only if the range is identified with the cutoff radius.

## Method

The authors constructed SV and SS potentials by fitting their parameters to minimize the squared deviation from standard CWS potential shapes (Perey and Becchetti-Greenlees geometries) using a quadrature over equidistant mesh points, with an additional penalty term for derivative matching controlled by a Lagrange multiplier of 25 fm². They solved the radial Schrödinger equation using Ixaru's numerical integration method and calculated S-matrix pole positions using the ANTI code with extended precision floating-point arithmetic, then compared the pole trajectories for l=0 states against Newton's analytical formulae for the starting points (belonging to potential depth zero) in the k-plane.

## Key numbers

- For the Perey geometry (r₀=1.25 fm, a=0.65 fm), best-fit SV parameters are ρ₀=1.85A_T^(1/3) fm, ρ₀-ρ₁=3.2a, c=-0.051+0.0051A_T-3.9×10⁻⁶A_T²
- For the Becchetti-Greenlees geometry (r₀=1.17 fm, a=0.75 fm), best-fit SV parameters are ρ₀=1.86A_T^(1/3) fm, ρ₀-ρ₁=2.8a, c=-0.055+0.003A_T-7.0×10⁻⁷A_T²
- For ¹⁸F+n with Perey geometry: ρ₀=5.084 fm, ρ₁=3.244 fm, c=0.040; for Becchetti-Greenlees: ρ₀=4.957 fm, ρ₁=2.728 fm, c=0.011
- Single-particle energies for ¹⁸F+n in CWS vs SV (V_so=30 MeV): 0s₁/₂: -38.926 vs -38.119 MeV; 0d₅/₂: -8.985 vs -9.049 MeV; 0d₃/₂: -5.779 vs -5.770 MeV
- For ¹³N+n: 0d₃/₂ single-particle energy is -0.003 MeV in CWS vs -0.548 MeV in SV
- For a CWS potential with R_max=15 fm for ¹⁸F, the fitted range from pole starting points is ℛ=14.67 fm, close to the cutoff radius of 15 fm
- For a SV potential with ρ₀=5.3 fm for ¹⁸F, the fitted range from pole starting points is ℛ=5.17 fm
- For ²⁰⁸Pb+n best-fit SS parameters: ρ₀=10.75 fm, ρ₁=8.94 fm, c=1.528, a_s=1.4 (WS parameters: r₀=1.27 fm, a=0.7 fm)

## Context

This work extends the earlier introduction of the SV potential by Salamon and Vertse (2008) and the SS potential by Sahu and Sahu (2012), which were proposed to avoid the unphysical dependence of resonance pole positions on the cutoff radius in standard CWS potentials. The study systematically fits these SFR forms to the well-known global optical potentials of Perey (1963) and Becchetti and Greenlees, and tests them against Newton's analytical formulae for S-matrix pole starting points from his textbook (1982). The results enable the use of mathematically well-behaved SFR potentials for broad resonance calculations in both light nuclei (relevant for fusion reactions and PET isotope production) and heavy nuclei, while confirming that the SV and SS potentials produce regular pole trajectories without the irregular loops seen in CWS potentials.
