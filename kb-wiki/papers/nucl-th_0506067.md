# Extraction of scattering lengths from final-state interactions

> A recently proposed method based on dispersion theory, that allows to extract the scattering length of a hadronic two-body system from corresponding final-state interactions, is generalized to the situation where the Coulomb interaction is present. The steps required in a concrete practical application are discussed in detail. In addition a thorough examination of the accuracy of the proposed method is presented and a comparison is made with results achieved with other methods like the Jost-function approach based on the effective-range approximation. Deficiencies of the latter method are pointed out. The reliability of the dispersion theory method for extracting also the effective range is investigated.

## Key claim

The dispersion-theory method for extracting scattering lengths from final-state interactions can be generalized to systems with repulsive Coulomb interactions, provided one uses the Coulomb-modified amplitude and extrapolates the effective range function from momenta above the Coulomb scale (around 50–100 MeV/c) to threshold, yielding deviations within the previously estimated theoretical uncertainty of 0.3 fm.

## Method

The authors generalize a dispersion-relation formalism (originally developed for short-range final-state interactions) to include repulsive Coulomb forces by applying the Gell-Mann–Goldberger two-potential formalism. They define a Coulomb-modified production amplitude free of Coulomb singularities, write a dispersion relation for it, and derive an integral expression relating the differential cross section (divided by the Coulomb penetration factor) to the Coulomb-modified nuclear phase shift. For practical extraction, they fit simulated invariant-mass spectra with an exponential parameterization, then compute the effective range function from the dispersion integral and extrapolate it to threshold using a fourth-order polynomial fit in the momentum region 50–100 MeV/c.

## Key numbers

* For the Jülich 01 singlet YN model, the exact scattering length is −1.02 fm; the dispersion integral yields −1.03 fm, the Jost-ERA method gives −1.28 fm, and the Migdal-Watson approach gives −1.67 fm.
* For the Argonne v14 singlet NN potential, the exact scattering length is −23.71 fm; the dispersion integral yields −23.54 fm, Jost-ERA gives −24.56 fm, and Migdal-Watson gives −24.79 fm.
* For the Nijmegen 97a singlet YN model, the exact effective range is 6.01 fm; the dispersion integral yields 4.78 fm, while Jost-ERA gives 2.81 fm.
* For the Coulomb test with model 1 (Coulomb-modified scattering length −3.24 fm), the extracted value from the dispersion integral with extrapolation is −3.10 fm; for model 2 (−1.86 fm), the extracted value is −1.86 fm.
* The theoretical error of the dispersion-integral method is estimated to be 0.3 fm or less.
* The upper limit of the dispersion integral was taken at p_max = 205 MeV/c, corresponding to epsilon_max ≈ 40 MeV for ΛN/ΣN systems and ≈ 45 MeV for the NN system.

## Context

This work extends the dispersion-theory method of Gasparyan (2003) for extracting scattering lengths from final-state interactions to include Coulomb repulsion, enabling analysis of charged systems such as Σ⁺p in reactions like pp → K⁰Σ⁺p. The authors compare their method against the Jost-function approach based on the effective-range approximation (Jost-ERA) and the Migdal-Watson approach, demonstrating that those simpler methods produce systematic offsets (≈0.3 fm for Jost-ERA, ≈0.6 fm for Migdal-Watson) that can lead to misleading results, as exemplified by the analysis of Hinterberger and Sibirtsev (2004) where spin-dependent fits to Λp scattering lengths may be artifacts. The method enables reliable extraction of scattering lengths from large-momentum-transfer production reactions (e.g., pp → K⁺Λp, γd → K⁺nΛ) and could be applied to decays of J/Ψ or B mesons.
