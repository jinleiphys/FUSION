# Four-nucleon system with $Δ$-isobar excitation

> The four-nucleon bound state and scattering below three-body breakup threshold are described based on the realistic coupled-channel potential CD Bonn + $Δ$ which allows the excitation of a single nucleon to a $Δ$ isobar. The Coulomb repulsion between protons is included. In the four-nucleon system the two-baryon coupled-channel potential yields effective two-, three- and four-nucleon forces, mediated by the $Δ$ isobar and consistent with each other and with the underlying two-nucleon force. The effect of the four-nucleon force on the studied observables is much smaller than the effect of the three-nucleon force. The inclusion of the $Δ$ isobar is unable to resolve the existing discrepancies with the experimental data.

## Key claim

The inclusion of the $\Delta$ isobar via the coupled-channel potential CD Bonn + $\Delta$ yields effective two-, three-, and four-nucleon forces that are consistent with each other, but the resulting improvements to four-nucleon observables are insufficient to resolve existing discrepancies with experimental data; the effect of the four-nucleon force is much smaller than that of the three-nucleon force.

## Method

The authors solved the four-nucleon bound state and scattering equations below the three-body breakup threshold using the Faddeev-Yakubovsky formalism for bound states and the symmetrized Alt, Grassberger, and Sandhas (AGS) equations for scattering, with the coupled-channel potential CD Bonn + $\Delta$ that allows excitation of a single nucleon to a $\Delta$ isobar. The Coulomb repulsion between protons was included via a screening and renormalization method. To isolate the separate contributions of the $\Delta$ isobar, a sequence of incomplete calculations was performed that selectively retained only certain components of the two-baryon transition matrix ($T_{NN}$, $T_{\Delta N}$, $T_{N\Delta}$, $T_{\Delta\Delta}$) to distinguish two-nucleon dispersion, Fujita-Miyazawa type three-nucleon force, higher-order three-nucleon force, and four-nucleon force effects.

## Key numbers

- The binding energy of ${}^4\mathrm{He}$ calculated with CD Bonn + $\Delta$ is 27.10 MeV, compared to 26.18 MeV with CD Bonn alone and the experimental value of 28.30 MeV.
- The two-nucleon dispersion contribution to the ${}^4\mathrm{He}$ binding energy is $\Delta E_2 = -2.80$ MeV.
- The Fujita-Miyazawa type three-nucleon force contribution to the ${}^4\mathrm{He}$ binding energy is $\Delta E_{3}^{\mathrm{FM}} = 2.25$ MeV.
- The higher-order three-nucleon force contribution to the ${}^4\mathrm{He}$ binding energy is $\Delta E_3^{\mathrm{h.o.}} = 1.30$ MeV.
- The four-nucleon force contribution to the ${}^4\mathrm{He}$ binding energy is $\Delta E_{4} = 0.17$ MeV.
- For $n\text{-}{}^3\mathrm{H}$ scattering at 3.5 MeV neutron lab energy, the total cross section with CD Bonn + $\Delta$ is 2.130 barns, compared to 2.283 barns with CD Bonn and the experimental value of 2.450 barns.
- The four-nucleon force contribution to all $n\text{-}{}^3\mathrm{H}$ scattering observables at 3.5 MeV is less than 0.001 barns for cross sections and less than 0.001 for the maximum analyzing power.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous fully converged four-nucleon scattering calculations (Deltuva 2007a, 2007b, 2007c) that used purely nucleonic potentials like AV18 (Wiringa 1995), CD Bonn (Machleidt 2001), and INOY04 (Doleschall 2004), by now including $\Delta$-isobar excitation as a source of consistent many-nucleon forces. It follows the approach previously applied to the three-nucleon system (Deltuva 2003c, 2005a, 2005d) and contrasts with alternative methods such as adding a static three-nucleon force like Urbana IX (Pudliner 1997) as used by Lazauskas (2004, 2005) and the Pisa group (Viviani 2001, Fisher 2006), or using chiral effective field theory (Epelbaum 2000). The finding that the four-nucleon force effect is much smaller than the three-nucleon force effect confirms the traditional hierarchy of many-nucleon forces, but the inability of the $\Delta$ isobar to resolve discrepancies with data motivates future work extending the Hamiltonian to pionic reactions or applying chiral effective field theory to four-nucleon observables.
