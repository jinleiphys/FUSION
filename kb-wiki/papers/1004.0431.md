# Relativistic Nucleus-Nucleus Collisions: Zone of Reactions and Space-Time Structure of a Fireball

> A zone of reactions is determined and then exploited as a tool in studying the space-time structure of an interacting system formed in a collision of relativistic nuclei. The time dependence of the reaction rates integrated over spatial coordinates is also considered. Evaluations are made with the help of the microscopic transport model UrQMD. The relation of the boundaries of different zones of reactions and the hypersurfaces of sharp chemical and kinetic freeze-outs is discussed.

# Relativistic Nucleus-Nucleus Collisions: Zone of Reactions and Space-Time Structure of a Fireball

## Key claim
The space-time boundary of a fireball can be identified with the sharp kinetic freeze-out hypersurface by defining reaction zones that contain a specified percentage (99%) of all hadronic reactions, rather than relying on a critical value of energy density, temperature, or particle density. The authors propose that the boundary between the "cold fireball" and the "fireball halo" can be interpreted as the sharp kinetic freeze-out hypersurface, with the caveat that any definition of sharp freeze-out is possible only with chosen accuracy.

## Method
The authors used the microscopic transport model UrQMD v2.3 to calculate the four-density of reactions at every space-time point for central Au+Au collisions at 10.8A GeV (AGS) and Pb+Pb collisions at 158A GeV (SPS). They divided a four-cube of reactions (200 fm edges) into equal four-volume pixels, counted the number of reactions in each pixel averaged over 1000 events, then sorted pixels by decreasing reaction count and summed them to define zones containing specified fractions of all hadronic reactions. The time dependence of reaction rates integrated over spatial coordinates was also evaluated for different reaction types (elastic, inelastic, fusion, decay).

## Key numbers
- For AGS (Au+Au at 10.8A GeV): fireball formation time t_c = 2.9 fm/c; fireball division time t_fd approximately 30 fm/c; cold fireball lives for 30-33 fm/c; hot fireball decays completely within approximately 15 fm/c
- For SPS (Pb+Pb at 158A GeV): fireball formation time t_c = 0.77 fm/c; fireball division time t_fd approximately 36 fm/c; cold fireball lives for 90-100 fm/c; hot fireball decays completely within approximately 35 fm/c
- The space-like hypersurface bounding the cold fireball has the form t(z) = A sqrt(tau_0^2 + z^2) with A = 0.65, tau_0 = 46 fm/c for AGS and A = 0.95, tau_0 = 38 fm/c for SPS
- The time-like hypersurface bounding the cold fireball from below has the form t(z) = t_0 + (1/v)z with v = 0.8 for AGS and v = 0.98 for SPS
- Average pion multiplicity per nucleon: approximately 1.6 for AGS conditions, approximately 6 for SPS conditions
- The frequency of all reactions reaches its first maximum at t approximately 3.9 fm/c for AGS and t approximately 0.84 fm/c for SPS
- For SPS conditions, a second local maximum of the frequency of reactions occurs at t_M approximately 10.6 fm/c

## Context
This work extends the study of fireball space-time structure using reaction rates, building on prior transport model calculations by Bleicher (2002) which showed similar time-dependent behavior from UrQMD for SPS conditions. The authors relate their reaction zone boundaries to the sharp freeze-out hypersurface concept used in hydrodynamic models by Russkikh and Ivanov (2006), Csorgo and Hamma (2003), and Sollfrank et al. (1998), as well as to chemical freeze-out definitions by Heinz (2001). The method enables quantitative comparison of freeze-out hypersurfaces across different collision energies and provides a framework for predicting that time-like hypersurfaces of all reaction zones will merge and coincide with the light cone at RHIC energies.
