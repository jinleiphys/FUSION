# Superfluidity in nuclear systems and neutron stars

> Nuclear matter and finite nuclei exhibit the property of superfluidity by forming Cooper pairs. We review the microscopic theories and methods that are being employed to understand the basic properties of superfluid nuclear systems, with emphasis on the spatially extended matter encountered in neutron stars, supernova envelopes, and nuclear collisions. Our survey of quantum many-body methods includes techniques that employ Green functions, correlated basis functions, and Monte Carlo sampling of quantum states. With respect to empirical realizations of nucleonic and hadronic superfluids, this review is focused on progress that has been made toward quantitative understanding of their properties at the level of microscopic theories of pairing, with emphasis on the condensates that exist under conditions prevailing in neutron-star interiors. These include singlet $S$-wave pairing of neutrons in the inner crust, and, in the quantum fluid interior, singlet-$S$ proton pairing and triplet coupled $P$-$F$-wave neutron pairing. Additionally, calculations of weak-interaction rates in neutron-star superfluids within the Green function formalism are examined in detail. We close with a discussion of quantum vortex states in nuclear systems and their dynamics in neutron-star superfluid interiors.

## Key claim

This review paper surveys microscopic theories of superfluidity in nuclear systems and neutron stars, but acknowledges that a quantitative microscopic understanding of how nucleon correlations affect pairing gap values remains incomplete, despite progress with realistic potentials that fit scattering phase shifts.

## Method

The authors review quantum many-body methods including Green function techniques, correlated basis functions, and Monte Carlo sampling of quantum states applied to pairing in nuclear matter and neutron stars. They examine singlet S-wave pairing of neutrons in the inner crust, singlet S-wave proton pairing and triplet coupled P-F-wave neutron pairing in the quantum fluid interior, and weak-interaction rate calculations in neutron-star superfluids within the Green function formalism. The review also covers quantum vortex states and their dynamics in neutron-star superfluid interiors.

## Key numbers

- Neutron pairing gaps in the inner crust and core are predicted to be about 1 MeV from initial many-body calculations, with the $^1S_0$ state in the inner crust and $^3P_2$ state at higher densities in the core
- The neutron-neutron scattering length is $a_0 \simeq -19$ fm, implying the dilute gas limit $|a_0| p_F \ll 1$ requires $p_F \ll 0.054$ fm$^{-1}$, corresponding to number density $n \ll 10^{-5} n_0$ where $n_0 = 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$ is nuclear saturation density
- The Vela pulsar exhibits glitches with typical spin changes $\Delta\nu/\nu \simeq 10^{-6}$ and spin-derivative changes $\Delta\dot\nu/\dot\nu \simeq 10^{-2}$, while the Crab pulsar shows smaller glitches with $\Delta\nu/\nu \simeq 10^{-8}$
- The zero-temperature BCS gap for contact interaction is $\Delta_0 = \tilde \epsilon \exp(-2/\lambda_c)$ where $\lambda_c = 4p_F|a_0|/\pi$ is the dimensionless contact pairing interaction

## Context

This review extends earlier reviews of pairing theory in nuclear contexts by Lombardo and Schulze (2001), Dean and Hjorth-Jensen (2003), and Page and collaborators (2013), with emphasis on microscopic many-body methods for neutron-star matter. The work connects to observations of pulsar glitches first linked to neutron-star superfluidity by Baym and collaborators (1969), and to neutron-star cooling histories that require superfluid suppression of neutrino emission rates as discussed by Pethick (1992), Yakovlev and Pethick (2004), and Sedrakian (2007). The review enables further quantitative work on pairing gaps using realistic nuclear interactions and provides a framework for interpreting neutron-star observations in terms of superfluid properties.
