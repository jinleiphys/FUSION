# Relation of Strangeness Nuggets to Strangeness Condensation and the Maximum Mass of Neutron Stars

> Combining the recent experimental indications of density dependence in the pion decay constant $f_π^\star$ and the $ω$ meson mass $m_ω^\star$ and the discovery of $S^0 (3115)$ and other "strange nuggets" with the vector manifestation of chiral symmetry in hidden local symmetry proposed by Harada and Yamawaki, we show that the mechanism responsible for dense strangeness nuggets can be related to that responsible for kaon condensation in neutron-star matter. We suggest that this relation assures kaon condensation at a density $\sim$ three times nuclear matter density which supports the Brown-Bethe scenario for the $M_{\rm NS}^{max} \simeq 1.5\msun$. This low $M_{\rm NS}^{\rm max}\sim 1.5\msun$ has major consequences in astrophysics, especially for the merging rate of compact stellar objects.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the mechanism responsible for dense strangeness nuggets (such as the recently discovered S^0(3115)) can be related to that responsible for kaon condensation in neutron-star matter, and that this relation supports kaon condensation setting in at a density approximately three times nuclear matter density, which in turn limits the maximum neutron star mass to about 1.5 solar masses.

## Method

The authors combine a "bottom-up" mean-field approach using an effective chiral Lagrangian with scaling parameters (Brown-Rho scaling) with a "top-down" approach starting from the vector manifestation fixed point of hidden local symmetry proposed by Harada and Yamawaki. They incorporate recent experimental determinations of the in-medium pion decay constant from deeply bound pionic atoms and the in-medium omega meson mass, together with lattice calculations of the kaon-nucleon sigma term, to update the earlier Thorsson et al. calculation of kaon condensation in neutron star matter. The authors then compare the mean-field description of infinite neutron star matter with the finite strangeness nugget system studied by Akaishi et al.

## Key numbers

* The in-medium pion decay constant at nuclear matter density is (F_pi^*(n0)/F_pi)^2 = 0.65 ± 0.05, extracted at tree order from deeply bound pionic atoms.
* The omega meson mass at nuclear matter density is m_omega^*/m_omega ≈ 0.84, as measured by Metag (2004).
* The enhancement factor for vector mean fields due to scaling is (F_pi/F_pi^*)^2 ≈ (1/0.8)^2 ≈ 1.56.
* The lattice calculation by Dong et al. gives the kaon-nucleon sigma term Σ_KN = 362 MeV, corresponding to a_3 m_s = -231 MeV; the authors use the Thorsson et al. central value a_3 m_s = -222 MeV.
* For the Thorsson et al. calculation with nuclear compression modulus K0 = 210 MeV, kaon condensation sets in at n = 3.08 n0 and the maximum neutron star mass is 1.5 solar masses.
* The effective kaon mass at the condensation point without the range term is m_K^* = 333 MeV; with the range term it becomes m_K^* = 347 MeV.
* The vector mean field contribution to the kaon energy at n = 3.1 n0 is approximately -126 MeV before medium enhancement.
* The binding energy of the strangeness nugget S^0(3115) is 194 MeV observed, with Akaishi et al. obtaining 143 MeV in their calculation; the ∼50 MeV difference is attributed to medium effects.

## Context

This work extends the earlier calculation of Thorsson et al. (1994) on kaon condensation in neutron stars and the Brown-Bethe (1994) scenario for a maximum neutron star mass of about 1.5 solar masses. It incorporates recent experimental results on in-medium hadron properties from Suzuki et al. (2004) for deeply bound pionic atoms and Metag (2004) for the omega meson mass, as well as lattice calculations of the kaon-nucleon sigma term by Dong et al. (1996). The paper connects these developments to the experimental discovery of strangeness nuggets by Suzuki et al. (2004, 2005) and their theoretical interpretation by Akaishi et al. (2005), suggesting that the nugget observations provide support for the kaon condensation scenario. The authors argue that a low maximum neutron star mass of about 1.5 solar masses has major consequences for the merging rate of compact stellar objects, which they plan to discuss in a later paper (Brown, Lee, Rho, 2006).
