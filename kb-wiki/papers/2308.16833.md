# Resonance contributions to nucleon spin structure in Holographic QCD

> We study polarized inelastic electron-nucleon scattering at low momentum transfer in the Witten-Sakai-Sugimoto model of holographic QCD, focusing on resonance production contributions to the nucleon spin structure functions. Our analysis includes both spin $3/2$ and spin $1/2$ low-lying nucleon resonances with positive and negative parity. We determine, in turn, the helicity amplitudes for nucleon-resonance transitions and the resonance contributions to the neutron and proton generalized spin polarizabilities. Extrapolating the model parameters to realistic QCD data, our analysis, triggered by recent experimental results from Jefferson Lab, agrees with the observation that the $Δ(1232)$ resonance gives the dominant contribution to the forward spin polarizabilities at low momentum transfer. The contribution is negative and tends to zero as the momentum transfer increases. As expected, the contribution of the $Δ(1232)$ to the longitudinal-transverse polarizabilities is instead negligible. The latter, for both nucleons, turn out the be negative functions with zero asymptote. The holographic results, at least for the proton where enough data are available, are in qualitative agreement with the resonance contributions to the spin polarizabilities extracted from experimental data on the helicity amplitudes.

## Key claim

The holographic Witten-Sakai-Sugimoto model, when extrapolated to realistic QCD data, produces resonance contributions to nucleon spin polarizabilities that are in qualitative agreement with experimental data from Jefferson Lab, but the model leaves the existing puzzles unresolved because non-resonant contributions (not included in the analysis) likely play an important role.

## Method

The authors computed helicity amplitudes for nucleon-to-resonance transitions (including spin 3/2 and spin 1/2 resonances with positive and negative parity) using the Witten-Sakai-Sugimoto holographic QCD model in the planar large-\(N_c\) limit at strong coupling. They then inserted these amplitudes into the standard formulas for sharp resonance contributions to the generalized forward spin polarizability \(\gamma_0(Q^2)\) and the longitudinal-transverse spin polarizability \(\delta_{LT}(Q^2)\), summing over low-lying resonances including the \(\Delta(1232)\), the Roper \(N(1440)\), the \(N(1535)\), the \(\Delta(1600)\), the \(N(1710)\), and the \(N(1650)\).

## Key numbers

* The \(\Delta(1232)\) resonance gives the dominant contribution to the forward spin polarizabilities at low momentum transfer; the contribution is negative and tends to zero as \(Q^2\) increases.
* The contribution of the \(\Delta(1232)\) to the longitudinal-transverse polarizabilities is negligible (zero to leading order in the large \(N_c\) holographic limit).
* For the neutron, spin 1/2 positive parity resonances do not contribute to \(\delta_{LT}(Q^2)\) to leading order in the holographic limit; spin 1/2 negative parity resonances give negative contributions going towards zero as \(Q^2\) increases.
* For the proton, spin 1/2 positive parity resonances give positive and decreasing contributions to \(\delta_{LT}(Q^2)\); spin 1/2 negative parity ones give negative contributions going towards zero as \(Q^2\) increases.
* The Jefferson Lab E97-110 Collaboration presented results on \(\gamma_0(Q^2)\) and \(\delta_{LT}(Q^2)\) for the neutron at \(Q^2\) down to 0.035 \(\mathrm{GeV}^2\) with beam energies between 1.1 and 4.4 \(\mathrm{GeV}\).
* The JLab Hall A \(g_2p\) Collaboration presented the first data for \(\delta_{LT}(Q^2)\) for the proton for \(Q^2\) from 0.02 to 0.13 \(\mathrm{GeV}^2\) with beam energies between 1.2 and 3.4 \(\mathrm{GeV}\).

## Context

This work extends previous holographic studies of resonance contributions to unpolarized proton structure functions in the WSS model by Bayona and collaborators (2011, 2012) to include all spin 1/2 resonances, spin 3/2 resonances, and polarized structure functions for both protons and neutrons. The analysis was triggered by recent Jefferson Lab experimental results from the E97-110 Collaboration (2019, 2021) and the Hall A \(g_2p\) Collaboration (2022), which showed discrepancies between data and chiral effective field theory predictions (Bernard and collaborators, 2002; Alarcon and collaborators, 2020), particularly for the neutron \(\delta_{LT}(Q^2)\) (the "\(\delta_{LT}\)-puzzle"). The holographic results enable a complementary non-perturbative check of resonance contributions, but leave the puzzles unsolved, suggesting that non-resonant contributions must be included in future work.
