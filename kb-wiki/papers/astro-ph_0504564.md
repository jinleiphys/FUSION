# Deconfinement and color superconductivity in cold neutron stars

> We study the deconfinement transition of hadronic matter into quark matter in neutron star conditions in the light of color superconductivity. Deconfinement is considered to be a first order phase transition that conserves color and flavor. It gives a short-lived {($τ\sim τ_{weak}$)} transitory colorless-quark-phase that is {\it not} in $β$-equilibrium. We deduce the equations governing deconfinement when quark pairing is allowed and find the regions of the parameter space (pairing gap $Δ$ versus bag constant $B$) where deconfinement is possible inside cold neutron stars. We show that for a wide region of ($B,Δ$) a pairing pattern is reachable within a strong interaction timescale, and the resulting ``2SC-like'' phase is preferred energetically to the unpaired phase. We also show that although $β$-stable hybrid star configurations are known to be possible for a wide region of the ($B,Δ$)-space, many of these configurations could not form in practice because deconfinement is forbidden, i.e. the here studied non-$β$-stable \emph{intermediate} state cannot be reached.

# Deconfinement and color superconductivity in cold neutron stars

## Key claim

The paper argues that although β-stable hybrid star configurations are possible for a wide region of the parameter space (pairing gap Δ versus bag constant B), many such configurations cannot form in practice because the intermediate non-β-stable quark phase that must be reached first via deconfinement is energetically forbidden. The authors show that for a wide region of (B, Δ) a "2SC-like" pairing pattern is reachable within a strong interaction timescale and is energetically preferred over the unpaired phase, but the existence of this paired intermediate state does not guarantee that the final β-stable configuration can actually be reached.

## Method

The authors model deconfinement as a first-order phase transition that conserves color and flavor, producing a short-lived (τ ∼ τ_weak) colorless quark phase not in β-equilibrium. They derive the equations governing deconfinement when quark pairing is allowed, using a thermodynamic potential that includes a free Fermi gas term, a pairing term (Ω_Q^gap = -A/π² Δ² μ̄²), and the bag constant B. They impose flavor conservation between the hadronic and deconfined phases (Y_f^H = Y_f^Q), color neutrality, and a pairing condition (n_ur = n_dg) to allow 2SC-like pairing, then minimize the Gibbs free energy per baryon to determine whether the paired or unpaired phase is preferred just after deconfinement.

## Key numbers

- For pure neutron matter deconfinement, the minimum of the Gibbs free energy per baryon occurs at x = 1, corresponding to a 2SC-like pattern where quarks u_r-d_g and u_g-d_r pair.
- For the GM1 hadronic equation of state (maximum mass M_max = 1.8 M_⊙), with strange quark mass m_s = 150 MeV, the critical point (B_*, Δ_*) where the parameter space regions meet occurs at B_* = 275 MeV fm⁻³ and Δ_* = 49 MeV.
- The maximum pairing gap Δ_max for which deconfinement is possible with m_s = 150 MeV is 85 MeV.
- For m_s = 0 MeV, B_* = 323 MeV fm⁻³ and Δ_* = 34 MeV, with Δ_max = 78 MeV.
- For m_s = 100 MeV, B_* = 300 MeV fm⁻³ and Δ_* = 37 MeV, with Δ_max = 80 MeV.
- For m_s = 200 MeV, B_* = 241 MeV fm⁻³ and Δ_* = 68 MeV, with Δ_max = 97 MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of deconfinement in neutron stars (Madsen 1994, Iida and Sato 1998, Lugones 1998, Lugones 1999, Bombaci 2004) by incorporating color superconductivity into the analysis of the intermediate non-β-stable quark phase that forms immediately after deconfinement. It contradicts the implicit assumption in many earlier works (Alford 2001, Rajagopal and Wilczek 2000) that β-stable hybrid star configurations can be reached whenever they are energetically favorable, by showing that the intermediate state may be forbidden even when the final β-stable state has lower energy. The results enable future studies to determine which regions of the (B, Δ) parameter space actually allow deconfinement in neutron stars, rather than only which regions allow stable hybrid star configurations.
