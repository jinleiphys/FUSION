# Role of symmetry energy at subnuclear densities in protoneutron star crusts

> The impact of matter properties at subnuclear densities on the evolution of protoneutron stars is investigated. Several models of nuclear equation of state (EOS) are constructed with varying saturation parameters, particularly the symmetry energy $S_0$ and its density slope $L$. Using the Thomas--Fermi approximation, the mass and proton numbers of heavy nuclei at subnuclear densities are systematically evaluated, along with their dependence on the EOS. Cooling simulations of protoneutron stars reveal that EOSs with smaller $L$ values lead to a longer cooling timescale and higher average neutrino energies. This behavior is attributed to the enhanced neutrino scattering caused by larger mass numbers, which increases the thermal insulation. Furthermore, the crystallization temperature, marking the onset of crust formation, is found to be higher for EOSs with smaller values of $L$. This is due to the enhanced Coulomb energy associated with larger proton numbers. As a result, despite slower cooling, crust formation occurs earlier for smaller-$L$ EOSs. These findings indicate that the timing of crust formation is sensitive to the EOS and highlight the importance of late-time neutrino observations as probes of the matter properties at subnuclear densities.

## Key claim
The timing of crust formation in protoneutron stars is sensitive to the symmetry energy slope parameter \(L\) at subnuclear densities: despite slower cooling, EOS models with smaller \(L\) values lead to earlier crust formation because their higher crystallization temperature (driven by larger proton numbers) dominates over the slower thermal evolution.

## Method
The authors constructed finite-temperature nuclear equations of state (EOS) using a Thomas-Fermi approximation extended from the zero-temperature framework of Oyamatsu and Iida (2003, 2007). They adopted four parameter sets from Oyamatsu et al. (2023) with varying symmetry energy slope \(L\) (7.6 to 73.4 MeV) while fixing incompressibility \(K\) at 230 MeV, and extended the EOS to supranuclear densities using a parametric model. Cooling simulations of protoneutron stars were performed with a quasi-hydrostatic evolution code incorporating general relativistic hydrostatic equilibrium and multigroup flux-limited diffusion neutrino transport, using initial conditions from a \(15M_\odot\) progenitor model.

## Key numbers
- EOS models used: B (\(L=73.4\) MeV), E (\(L=42.6\) MeV), Z (\(L=23.7\) MeV), H (\(L=7.6\) MeV), all with saturation density \(n_0=0.160\) fm\(^{-3}\) and incompressibility \(K=230\) MeV.
- Crystallization temperature formula: \(T_{\rm c} \approx 6.4\times10^9\) K \((\Gamma/175)^{-1}(\rho_{\rm B}/10^{14}~{\rm g~cm}^{-3})^{1/3}(Z/26)^{2}(A'/260)^{-1/3}\), with crystallization occurring at Coulomb coupling parameter \(\Gamma > 175\).
- Crust formation time for model Z40 (\(L=23.7\) MeV): \(t=164\) s after onset of protoneutron star cooling.
- Crust formation time for model H30 (\(L=7.6\) MeV): \(t=107\) s.
- Crust formation times for EOS Z models with varying \(S_{00}\): 160 s (Z50), 164 s (Z40), 166 s (Z35), showing insensitivity to supranuclear-density symmetry energy.
- Neutrino luminosity threshold for late-time behavior: \(\lesssim 10^{49}\) erg s\(^{-1}\).

## Context
This work extends the zero-temperature macroscopic nuclear model of Oyamatsu and Iida (2003, 2007) to finite temperatures, using updated interaction parameters from Oyamatsu et al. (2023). It builds on prior findings that smaller \(L\) yields larger mass numbers \(A\) (Oyamatsu and Iida, 2007) and that neutrino-nucleus coherent scattering increases thermal insulation (Nakazato et al., 2018). The results enable predictions for late-time neutrino observations as probes of subnuclear-density matter properties, and suggest that crust formation signatures may be distinguishable through neutrino emission in the low-luminosity phase.
