# Parametrizations of triaxial deformation and E2 transitions of the wobbling band

> By the very definition the triaxial deformation parameter $γ$ is related to the expectation values of the K=0 and K=2 components of the intrinsic quadrupole tensor operator. On the other hand, using the same symbol "$γ$", various different parametrizations of triaxial deformation have been employed, which are suitable for various types of the mean-field potentials. It is pointed out that the values of various "$γ$" are quite different for the same actual triaxial deformation, especially for the large deformation; for example, the difference can be almost a factor two for the case of the triaxial superdeformed bands recently observed in the Hf and Lu nuclei. In our previous work, we have studied the wobbling band in Lu nuclei by using the microscopic framework of the cranked Nilsson mean-field and the random phase approximation, where the most serious problem is that the calculated B(E2) value is about factor two smaller. It is shown that the origin of this underestimation can be mainly attributed to the small triaxial deformation; if is used the same triaxial deformation as in the analysis of the particle-rotor model, the calculated B(E2) increases and gives correct magnitude compared with the experimental data.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the underestimation of calculated B(E2) ratios for wobbling bands in Lu nuclei by a factor of about two in previous microscopic RPA calculations is mainly due to using a triaxiality parameter γ defined for the Nilsson potential (γ≈20°) rather than the density-based γ that corresponds to the same actual nuclear shape; when the density-based γ is used, the calculated B(E2) values agree with experimental data.

## Method

The authors systematically compare three different definitions of the triaxial deformation parameter γ: γ(den) based on intrinsic quadrupole moments of the density distribution, γ(geo) based on the geometric nuclear shape from uniform density, and γ(pot) defined by the deformation parameters of the mean-field potential (Nilsson or Woods-Saxon). They calculate these γ values for the same nuclear shape in 163Lu using both the Nilsson and Woods-Saxon potentials, and then compute the B(E2) ratio of out-of-band to in-band transitions for the one-phonon wobbling band using the particle-rotor model with the density-based γ.

## Key numbers

- The difference between γ(pot:Nils) and γ(den) can be almost a factor of two: γ(pot:Nils)=20° corresponds to γ(den)≈γ(geo)≈11° for the TSD band in 163Lu with ε2=0.43.
- For the Woods-Saxon potential, γ(pot:WS)=20° corresponds to γ(den)≈γ(geo)≈13° for the same nucleus.
- In the small deformation limit for the Nilsson potential, γ(geo) ≈ (1 - 3/2 ε2) γ(pot:Nils), giving a factor of 1.5 reduction when ε2=0.4.
- The experimental B(E2) ratio for the one-phonon wobbling band in 163Lu is reproduced by the particle-rotor model with γ(den)≈20°.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic RPA studies of wobbling motion in Lu nuclei (Shimizu et al., 2004-2006) by resolving a systematic factor-of-two discrepancy in calculated B(E2) values. It clarifies that the triaxial deformation parameter γ≈20° used in particle-rotor model analyses (Hamamoto, 2002; Hamamoto and Hagemann, 2003) corresponds to a density-based γ, while the Nilsson-Strutinsky calculations (Svensson et al., 1995; Bengtsson, 1990) predict γ(pot:Nils)≈20° which gives a much smaller density-based γ≈11°. The paper enables consistent comparison between microscopic mean-field calculations and phenomenological rotor-model analyses of wobbling bands in triaxial superdeformed nuclei.
