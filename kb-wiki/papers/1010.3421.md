# QED confronts the radius of the proton

> Recent results on muonic hydrogen [1] and the ones compiled by CODATA on ordinary hydrogen and $ep$-scattering [2] are $5σ$ away from each other. Two reasons justify a further look at this subject: 1) One of the approximations used in [1] is not valid for muonic hydrogen. This amounts to a shift of the proton's radius by $\sim 3$ of the standard deviations of [1], in the "right" direction of data-reconciliation. In field-theory terms, the error is a mismatch of renormalization scales. Once corrected, the proton radius "runs", much as the QCD coupling "constant" does. 2) The result of [1] requires a choice of the "third Zemach moment". Its published independent determination is based on an analysis with a $p$-value --the probability of obtaining data with equal or lesser agreement with the adopted (fit form-factor) hypothesis-- of $3.92\times 10^{-12}$. In this sense, this quantity is not empirically known. Its value would regulate the level of "tension" between muonic- and ordinary-hydrogen results, currently {\it at most} $\sim 4σ$. There is no tension between the results of [1] and the proton radius determined with help of the analyticity of its form factors.

## Key claim

The 5-sigma tension between the muonic-hydrogen proton radius measurement of Pohl et al. and the CODATA value from ordinary hydrogen and electron-proton scattering is reduced to at most 4-sigma after correcting an invalid approximation in the muonic-hydrogen analysis, and the remaining tension depends on the poorly-known third Zemach moment, whose published determination has a p-value of \(3.92\times 10^{-12}\).

## Method

The author recalculates the finite-size contribution to the Lamb shift in muonic hydrogen without the standard approximation that the lepton wave function is evaluated only at the origin, instead using explicit dipole and single-pole form factors to compute the exact finite-size shift. This correction is reinterpreted as a mismatch of renormalization scales, analogous to the running of the QCD coupling, leading to a "running" proton radius. The author also examines the statistical quality of the electron-proton scattering data fits used to extract the proton radius and the third Zemach moment, reporting the p-values of those fits.

## Key numbers

* The muonic-hydrogen Lamb shift measurement: \(L_{\rm exp} = 206.2949 \pm 0.0032\) meV (Pohl et al.).
* The CODATA proton radius: \(\sqrt{\langle r_p^2\rangle} = 0.8768 \pm 0.0069\) fm.
* The muonic-hydrogen proton radius (using dipole approximation): \(\sqrt{\langle r_p^2\rangle} = 0.84184 \pm 0.00067\) fm (Pohl et al.).
* The correction from the improved wave-function treatment shifts the extracted proton radius by \(2.7\sigma(\mu H)\) (dipole form factor) or \(3\sigma(\mu H)\) (single-pole form factor) in the direction of reconciling the results.
* The p-value of the Sick (2003) fit to electron-proton scattering data (used for the third Zemach moment) is \(3.92\times 10^{-12}\) for \(\chi^2 = 512\) with 310 degrees of freedom.
* The third Zemach moment from electron-proton scattering: \([\langle r_p^3 \rangle_{(2)}]^{1/3} = 1.394 \pm 0.022\) fm (Friar and Sick).
* The Mainz electron-proton scattering experiment yields \(\sqrt{\langle r_p^2\rangle} = 0.879(5)_{\rm stat}(4)_{\rm syst}(2)_{\rm model}(4)_{\rm group}\) fm.

## Context

This paper addresses the "proton radius puzzle" following the muonic-hydrogen measurement by Pohl et al. (2010) that disagreed with the CODATA compilation (2010) and earlier electron-proton scattering analyses by Sick (2003). The author argues that the standard approximation used in the muonic-hydrogen analysis is insufficient, introducing a correction analogous to renormalization-scale matching in quantum field theory. The work also questions the reliability of the third Zemach moment extracted from electron-proton scattering data, citing the extremely poor p-value of the fit by Friar and Sick. The paper calls for new, model-independent analyses of electron-proton scattering data using orthogonal function expansions and Fourier transforms to produce correlated confidence contours for the proton radius and third Zemach moment, which would allow a definitive comparison with the muonic-hydrogen result.
