# Dynamics and neutrino signal of black hole formation in non-rotating failed supernovae. II. progenitor dependence

> We study the progenitor dependence of the black hole formation and its associated neutrino signals from the gravitational collapse of non-rotating massive stars, following the preceding study on the single progenitor model in Sumiyoshi et al. (2007). We aim to clarify whether the dynamical evolution toward the black hole formation occurs in the same manner for different progenitors and to examine whether the characteristic of neutrino bursts is general having the short duration and the rapidly increasing average energies. We perform the numerical simulations by general relativistic neutrino-radiation hydrodynamics to follow the dynamical evolution from the collapse of pre-supernova models of 40Msun and 50Msun toward the black hole formation via contracting proto-neutron stars. For the three progenitor models studied in this paper, we found that the black hole formation occurs in ~0.4-1.5 s after core bounce through the increase of proto-neutron star mass together with the short and energetic neutrino burst. We found that density profile of progenitor is important to determine the accretion rate onto the proto-neutron star and, therefore, the duration of neutrino burst. We compare the neutrino bursts of black hole forming events from different progenitors and discuss whether we can probe clearly the progenitor and/or the dense matter.

# Dynamics and neutrino signal of black hole formation in non-rotating failed supernovae. II. progenitor dependence

## Key claim

The dynamical evolution toward black hole formation and the associated short, energetic neutrino burst occur in a similar manner across different progenitor models of 40–50 solar masses, but the density profile of the progenitor determines the accretion rate onto the proto-neutron star and therefore the duration of the neutrino burst; the influence from the equation of state is more significant than the progenitor dependence.

## Method

The authors performed general relativistic neutrino-radiation hydrodynamics simulations under spherical symmetry, following the collapse of pre-supernova models of 40 and 50 solar mass stars through core bounce, proto-neutron star contraction, and black hole formation. They adopted three progenitor models: a 40 solar mass star from Woosley and Weaver (1995) as the baseline, a 50 solar mass zero-metallicity star from Tominaga et al. (2007), and a 40 solar mass star from Hashimoto (1995). Two equations of state were used: the stiff Shen-EOS and the soft LS-EOS (with incompressibility 180 MeV), and black hole formation was defined by the appearance of an apparent horizon.

## Key numbers

- Black hole formation occurs in ~0.4–1.5 s after core bounce across the three progenitor models studied.
- For the 50 solar mass model (T50) with Shen-EOS, the duration from core bounce to black hole formation is 1.51 s; with LS-EOS, it is 0.51 s.
- For the 40 solar mass model by Hashimoto (H40) with LS-EOS, the time to black hole formation is 0.36 s.
- The critical baryon mass for black hole formation is ~2.7 solar masses (gravitational mass ~2.3 solar masses) for Shen-EOS and ~2.1 solar masses (gravitational mass ~2.0 solar masses) for LS-EOS.
- The iron core sizes are 1.98 solar masses for W40, 1.88 solar masses for T50, and 1.88 solar masses for H40.
- The critical mass can be estimated from the time duration with an error of ~0.3 solar masses within the uncertainty in progenitor density profiles.

## Context

This work extends the single-progenitor study of Sumiyoshi et al. (2007) to examine progenitor dependence, using the same numerical methods and equations of state. The results show that the density profile of the progenitor, rather than the iron core mass alone, controls the accretion rate and thus the neutrino burst duration. The findings enable future work on using neutrino signals to probe the equation of state of dense matter, though the authors note that neutrino oscillations during propagation must be considered for observational applications. The study contrasts with ordinary supernova neutrinos, which last ~20 s with decreasing energies, as described by Burrows (1988) and Suzuki (1994).
