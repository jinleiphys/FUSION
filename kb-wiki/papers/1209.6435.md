# Subbarrier fusion reactions and many-particle quantum tunneling

> Low energy heavy-ion fusion reactions are governed by quantum tunneling through the Coulomb barrier formed by a strong cancellation of the repulsive Coulomb force with the attractive nuclear interaction between the colliding nuclei. Extensive experimental as well as theoretical studies have revealed that fusion reactions are strongly influenced by couplings of the relative motion of the colliding nuclei to several nuclear intrinsic motions. Heavy-ion subbarrier fusion reactions thus provide a good opportunity to address a general problem on quantum tunneling in the presence of couplings, which has been a popular subject in the past decades in many branches of physics and chemistry. Here we review theoretical aspects of heavy-ion subbarrier fusion reactions from the view point of quantum tunneling in systems with many degrees of freedom. Particular emphases are put on the coupled-channels approach to fusion reactions, and the barrier distribution representation for multi-channel penetrability. We also discuss an application of the barrier distribution method to elucidation of the mechanism of dissociative adsorption of H$_2$ melecules in surface science.

## Key claim

This review argues that heavy-ion subbarrier fusion reactions are a prime example of many-particle quantum tunneling, where the relative motion of the colliding nuclei couples to nuclear intrinsic degrees of freedom, and that the coupled-channels approach and barrier distribution representation provide the theoretical framework to understand the substantial enhancement of fusion cross sections below the Coulomb barrier compared to one-dimensional potential model predictions.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical treatment of heavy-ion subbarrier fusion reactions using the coupled-channels formalism, which numerically solves equations that include couplings between the relative motion of the colliding nuclei and nuclear intrinsic motions such as rotational and vibrational excitations. It also details the barrier distribution representation, where fusion cross sections are expressed as a weighted sum over eigen-barriers, and the method to extract barrier distributions from experimental excitation functions via the second derivative \(d^2(E\sigma_{\rm fus})/dE^2\). The iso-centrifugal (rotating frame) approximation is introduced to reduce the complexity of full angular-momentum-coupled calculations.

## Key numbers

* The surface diffuseness parameter \(a\) extracted from heavy-ion elastic scattering is around 0.63 fm, while recent subbarrier fusion data require values ranging from 0.75 to 1.5 fm to fit the data.
* For the \(^{16}\)O + \(^{154}\)Sm system, the Coulomb barrier height is around 59 MeV; the experimental fusion cross section enhancement at subbarrier energies is an order of magnitude compared to the potential model prediction.
* For the \(^{16}\)O + \(^{144}\)Sm system, the enhancement of fusion cross section at subbarrier energies is about a factor of four compared to the potential model prediction.
* The deformation parameters used for \(^{154}\)Sm in the orientation average calculation are \(\beta_2 = 0.306\) and \(\beta_4 = 0.05\).

## Context

This review extends earlier reviews on subbarrier fusion reactions by Balantekin and Takigawa (1998), Dasgupta et al. (1998), and others, and places heavy-ion fusion in the broader context of quantum tunneling in systems with many degrees of freedom, referencing the seminal work of Caldeira and Leggett (1981) on dissipative quantum tunneling. The paper enables future work on deep subbarrier fusion reactions, where cross sections are suppressed compared to standard coupled-channels calculations, potentially related to dissipative quantum tunneling, and also discusses an application of the barrier distribution method to dissociative adsorption of H\(_2\) molecules in surface science.
