# Neutrinos, Rare Isotopes of Exotic Nuclei and Nuclear Astrophysics

> The connection between neutrino physics, nucleosynthesis of elements in astrophysical sites, laboratory measurements with rare exotic nuclei and astronomical observations is discussed. The key role played by neutrinos is emphasized and the close connection between neutrino physics and nucleosynthesis is highlighted.

## Key claim

The paper argues that understanding nucleosynthesis requires integrating neutrino physics, nuclear physics of rare isotopes, and astronomical observations, but it emphasizes that many aspects of this connection remain unexplored and that the adequacy of the mean-field approximation for collective neutrino oscillations is an open question.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical and experimental landscape connecting neutrino physics to nucleosynthesis, focusing on core-collapse supernovae and r-process sites. It discusses the many-body Hamiltonian describing neutrino propagation including neutrino-neutrino interactions, the single-angle and multi-angle approximations for collective oscillations, and the need for charge-exchange reaction experiments and shell-model calculations (such as the SFO Hamiltonian) to determine spin-isospin responses and beta-decay properties of exotic nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Energy released in a core-collapse supernova is about \(10^{53}\) ergs, equivalent to \(10^{59}\) MeV.
- 99% of that energy is carried away by approximately \(10^{58}\) neutrinos and antineutrinos.
- The three most critical stable-beam reactions for nuclear astrophysics are \(^3\)He(\(\alpha, \gamma\))\(^7\)Be, \(^{12}\)C(\(\alpha,\gamma\))\(^{16}\)O, and \(^{14}\)N(p,\(\gamma\))\(^{15}\)O (Adelberger 2010).
- The SFO shell-model Hamiltonian reproduces measured neutrino-\(^{12}\)C cross sections with reduced quenching of the axial-vector coupling strength \(g_A\) compared to previous calculations (Suzuki 2006).

## Context

This review extends the discussion of the neutrino-nucleosynthesis connection detailed in a special issue of Journal of Physics G (Volpe 2014) and builds on prior work on collective neutrino oscillations (Duan 2009, Duan 2010) and the role of neutrino-neutrino interactions in supernovae (Balantekin 2004). It enables future experimental programs at rare-isotope beam facilities to measure beta-decay half-lives and spin-isospin responses of nuclei along the r-process path, and it highlights the need for multi-angle calculations of collective oscillations to accurately determine the electron fraction in r-process sites.
