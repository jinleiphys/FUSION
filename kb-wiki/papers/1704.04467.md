# An Effective Model of QCD Monopoles

> In this work, we carried out quantum many-body studies of magnetic monopole ensembles through numerical simulations of the path integral for one- and two-component Coulomb Bose systems. We found the relation between the critical temperature for the Bose-Einstein condensation phase transition and the Coulomb coupling strength using two methods, the finite-size scaling of the superfluid fraction and statistical analysis of permutation cycles. After finding parameters that match the correlation functions measured in our system with the correlation functions previously measured on the lattice, we arrived at an effective quantum model of color magnetic monopoles in QCD. From this matched model, we were able to extract the monopole contribution to QCD equation of state near $T_\text{c}$.

## Key claim

The authors claim that magnetic monopoles possibly dominate the thermodynamics of QCD just above the critical temperature, but this conclusion is hedged by the fact that the SU(3) and QCD estimates rely on simple scaling arguments (doubling the SU(2) energy density for SU(3) and multiplying by an additional factor of 3 for QCD) that do not account for interactions between monopole species.

## Method

The authors performed Path-Integral Monte Carlo (PIMC) simulations of one- and two-component Coulomb Bose systems, representing each particle as a periodic path in imaginary time and sampling configurations via Metropolis Monte Carlo. They identified the Bose-Einstein condensation critical temperature using two independent methods: finite-size scaling of the superfluid fraction (derived from spatial winding numbers) and statistical analysis of permutation cycles (temporal winding numbers). They then matched the spatial correlation functions from their simulations to those previously measured in pure-gauge SU(2) lattice simulations to determine the effective coupling parameters for the monopole model.

## Key numbers

- For one- and two-component Coulomb Bose gases, as the Coulomb coupling \(\alpha\) is increased, the critical temperature \(T_\text{c}\) rises above the ideal gas critical temperature \(T_0\) before falling back to and below \(T_0\) (exact numerical values of \(T_\text{c}/T_0\) as a function of \(\alpha\) are shown in Fig. 1 but not given as explicit numbers in the text).
- The temperature-dependent coupling for a two-component Coulomb Bose gas with unit density that reproduces lattice results is given by \(\alpha(T/T_\text{c}) \approx 3.4\, \rho_m^{1/3}(T/T_\text{c})\), where \(\rho_m(T)\) is the monopole density in fm\(^{-3}\) from D'Alessandro et al. (2007).
- Matched radial distribution functions are shown for 1.63 \(T_\text{c}\) and 3.80 \(T_\text{c}\) (Fig. 2).
- For SU(3), the monopole contribution to the energy density was estimated by doubling the SU(2) energy density; for QCD with two light flavors, the number of species was increased by an additional factor of 3.

## Context

This work extends the "magnetic scenario" for finite-temperature QCD proposed by Liao and Shuryak (2006), which suggested that interactions of quarks and gluons with magnetic monopoles give rise to the unusual transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma. It directly builds on lattice simulations by D'Alessandro et al. (2007, 2010) and Bonati et al. (2013) that measured monopole spatial correlation functions and densities in pure-gauge theories. The results enable extraction of the monopole contribution to the QCD equation of state near \(T_\text{c}\), which was previously difficult because lattice simulations include many other degrees of freedom (quark and gluon quasiparticles) that obscure the monopole signal.
