# Hybridgen: A Model for the Study of QCD Hybrid States

> We study the mixing of excited states of a Hydrogen atom in a cavity with de-excited states plus a confined photon as a model for the coupling of quark-antiquark and quark-antiquark-gluon hybrid states in QCD. For an interesting range of parameters, the results are analytic. We find a case for which wavefunctions (and hence decay patterns) may be at odds with mass with respect to identification of a state as hybrid or not.

# Hybridgen: A Model for the Study of QCD Hybrid States

## Key claim
The mixing between pure quark-antiquark states and quark-antiquark-gluon hybrid states in QCD can be modeled using a hydrogen atom in a cavity, where the excited atomic state mixes with a de-excited state plus a confined photon. For an interesting range of parameters, the wavefunctions (and hence decay patterns) may be at odds with mass with respect to identification of a state as hybrid or not, meaning a state may lack evidence of hybrid decay patterns even though its mass suggests it does not belong in a pure quark-antiquark representation.

## Method
The authors place a hydrogen atom in a spherical cavity within a magnetic superconducting material, which confines the photon to the same cavity as the atom. They solve the Schrödinger equation for the electron in the Coulomb potential with the boundary condition that the wavefunction vanishes at the cavity surface, then calculate the hyperfine splitting via the Breit-Fermi interaction. The transition matrix element between the spin-up electron state (pure atomic state) and the spin-down electron plus a confined photon (hybrid state) is computed from the Pauli equation using the lowest cavity photon mode with magnetic boundary conditions, and the resulting two-level system is diagonalized analytically in the large-cavity limit.

## Key numbers
- The lowest cavity photon mode has wavenumber $k_0 = 2.7437$, fixed by the boundary condition $k_0 j_1'(k_0) = -j_1(k_0)$.
- The photon normalization constant $c(k_0) = 1.2857$, independent of cavity radius $R$, giving $N_{\gamma}^{-1} = c(k_0)R^{-1}$.
- For cavity sizes $x_0 > 3$ (where $x_0 = \alpha m R$), the eigenvalue difference from the continuum limit is $\leq 6\%$.
- The energy difference between eigenstates in the large-cavity limit is $\delta E/(\alpha m) = \sqrt{\Delta^2 + \frac{2}{3}\left(\frac{2c(k_0)}{k_0}\right)^2 \frac{(k_0/x_0)^4}{[1 + (k_0/(2x_0))^2]^4} \alpha^3}$, where $\Delta = \frac{4}{3}\frac{m}{M}\alpha^3 - \frac{k_0}{x_0}$.
- When photon energy is much larger than the hyperfine splitting, the lower state energy is $E_2/(\alpha m) = \frac{2}{3}\frac{m}{M}\alpha^3 - \frac{1}{6}(k_0/x_0)^3\alpha^3$.

## Context
This work extends the bag model approach (Thorn, 1981; Chodos et al., 1974) and flux tube models (Swanson, 1998) for studying hybrid mesons, using an exactly solvable atomic analog to address ambiguities in identifying hybrid states. The model is motivated by lattice QCD calculations of static potentials (Morningstar, 1999) and the Coulomb gauge Hamiltonian approach (Adam, 1999), which disagree with earlier constituent gluon models (Kalashnikova, 1999). The results enable a framework for interpreting whether a state's mass or its decay patterns should be the primary indicator of hybrid character, particularly relevant for ongoing experimental searches for hybrid mesons.
