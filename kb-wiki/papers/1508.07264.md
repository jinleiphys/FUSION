# Direct and secondary nuclear excitation with x-ray free-electron lasers

> The direct and secondary nuclear excitation produced by an x-ray free electron laser when interacting with a solid-state nuclear target is investigated theoretically. When driven at the resonance energy, the x-ray free electron laser can produce direct photoexcitation. However, the dominant process in that interaction is the photoelectric effect producing a cold and very dense plasma in which also secondary processes such as nuclear excitation by electron capture may occur. We develop a realistic theoretical model to quantify the temporal dynamics of the plasma and the magnitude of the secondary excitation therein. Numerical results show that depending on the nuclear transition energy and the temperature and charge states reached in the plasma, secondary nuclear excitation by electron capture may dominate the direct photoexcitation by several orders of magnitude, as it is the case for the 4.8 keV transition from the isomeric state of $^{93}$Mo, or it can be negligible, as it is the case for the 14.4 keV Mössbauer transition in $^{57}\mathrm{Fe}$. These findings are most relevant for future nuclear quantum optics experiments at x-ray free electron laser facilities.

## Key claim

The paper provides a more reliable theoretical prediction of secondary nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) in XFEL-produced plasmas by including time-dependent plasma dynamics, showing that NEEC can dominate direct photoexcitation by several orders of magnitude for the 4.85 keV transition in $^{93}$Mo but is negligible for the 14.4 keV Mossbauer transition in $^{57}$Fe, with the outcome depending on nuclear transition energy and plasma conditions.

## Method

The authors developed a theoretical model combining a hydrodynamic description of plasma expansion in the quasi-neutral limit with the FLYCHK population kinetics code to track the temporal evolution of plasma parameters (temperature, charge states, density). They calculated direct photoexcitation using a density matrix formalism and NEEC rates by summing over all charge states and capture channels, using the resonant electron flux in the plasma with Fermi-Dirac statistics. The model was applied to two nuclear systems: isomer triggering in $^{93}$Mo via a 4.85 keV transition and ground-state excitation of the 14.4 keV Mossbauer transition in $^{57}$Fe, both assuming a 1 micron thick solid-state target and 10 micron squared focal spot.

## Key numbers

- For $^{93}$Mo isomer triggering (4.85 keV transition), secondary nuclear excitation via NEEC dominates direct photoexcitation by several orders of magnitude
- For $^{57}$Fe (14.4 keV Mossbauer transition), secondary excitation can be safely neglected
- The $^{57}$Fe target with 1 micron thickness corresponds to a resonant thickness parameter of xi = 3.9
- The XFEL pulse duration is approximately 100 fs, while plasma expansion occurs on picosecond timescales
- The focal spot area is 10 micron squared, giving a focal radius of approximately 1.8 microns

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous theoretical study (Gunst et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 112, 082501, 2014) which neglected temporal plasma evolution and assumed NEEC occurred under constant plasma conditions for 100 ps. The improved model shows that while NEEC remains dominant for $^{93}$Mo isomer triggering, the earlier approximation was critical and required further investigation. The results are most relevant for planning nuclear quantum optics experiments at XFEL facilities, particularly for experiments using $^{57}$Fe in thin-film x-ray planar cavities (Rohlsberger, 2010; Rohlsberger, 2012; Heeg, 2013) and for isomer triggering applications (Walker, 1999; Aprahamian, 2005). The paper establishes criteria based on nuclear transition energy, atomic structure, and plasma conditions to predict whether secondary processes will dominate direct photoexcitation.
