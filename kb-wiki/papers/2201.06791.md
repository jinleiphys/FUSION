# Implicit correlations within phenomenological parametric models of the neutron star equation of state

> The rapid increase in the number and precision of astrophysical probes of neutron stars in recent years allows for the inference of their equation of state. Observations target different macroscopic properties of neutron stars which vary from star to star, such as mass and radius, but the equation of state allows for a common description of all neutron stars. To connect these observations and infer the properties of dense matter and neutron stars simultaneously, models for the equation of state are introduced. Parametric models rely on carefully engineered functional forms that reproduce a large array of realistic equations of state. Such models benefit from their simplicity but are limited because any finite-parameter model cannot accurately approximate all possible equations of state. Nonparametric models overcome this by increasing model freedom at the cost of increased complexity. In this study, we compare common parametric and nonparametric models, quantify the limitations of the former, and study the impact of modeling on our current understanding of high-density physics. We show that parametric models impose strongly model-dependent, and sometimes opaque, correlations between density scales. Such interdensity correlations result in tighter constraints that are unsupported by data and can lead to biased inference of the equation of state and of individual neutron star properties.

## Key claim

Parametric models of the neutron star equation of state impose strong, model-dependent correlations between density scales that are not required by causality or thermodynamic stability, resulting in tighter constraints than are supported by data and potentially biasing inference of the equation of state and neutron star properties.

## Method

The authors compared three common parametric models (piecewise-polytrope, spectral, and speed-of-sound parametrizations) against a nonparametric Gaussian process model, using identical astrophysical data from gravitational wave detections, radio pulsar mass measurements, and X-ray observations of neutron star masses and radii. They analyzed the prior and posterior distributions for the equation of state and macroscopic neutron star properties, examining interdensity correlations and the extent to which each model fills the physically allowed parameter space.

## Key numbers

- Nonparametric posterior maximum mass: 2.21^{+0.31}_{-0.21} solar masses
- Nonparametric posterior radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: 12.56^{+1.00}_{-1.07} km
- Nonparametric posterior central density at maximum mass: 1.5^{+0.3}_{-0.4} times 10^15 g/cm^3
- Nonparametric posterior pressure at nuclear saturation density: 4.30^{+3.37}_{-3.80} times 10^33 erg/cm^3
- Nonparametric posterior pressure at twice nuclear saturation: 4.38^{+2.46}_{-2.96} times 10^34 erg/cm^3
- Nonparametric posterior pressure at six times nuclear saturation: 7.41^{+5.87}_{-4.18} times 10^35 erg/cm^3
- Nonparametric posterior maximum compactness: 0.75^{+0.25}_{-0.24}
- Sample sizes: spectral model approximately 1.9 times 10^5 samples, piecewise-polytrope approximately 1.6 times 10^5 samples, speed-of-sound approximately 1.6 times 10^5 samples

## Context

This work extends comparisons between parametric and nonparametric equation of state models (Landry 2018; Essick 2019) by quantifying how parametric models like those of Read (2008), Lindblom (2010), and Greif (2018) impose implicit correlations beyond physical constraints. The findings enable more careful model selection in future neutron star equation of state inference, particularly as statistical uncertainties decrease with additional data from gravitational wave detectors and X-ray observatories.
