# Predicting the moment of inertia of pulsar J0737-3039A from Bayesian modeling of the nuclear equation of state

> We investigate neutron star moments of inertia from Bayesian posterior probability distributions of the nuclear equation of state that incorporate information from microscopic many-body theory and empirical data of finite nuclei. We focus on PSR J0737-3039A and predict that for this 1.338 M_sun neutron star the moment of inertia lies in the range $1.04 \times 10^{45}$ g cm$^{2} < I < 1.51 \times 10^{45}$ g cm$^{2}$ at the 95% credibility level, while the most probable value for the moment of inertia is $\tilde I = 1.36 \times 10^{45}$ g cm$^{2}$. Assuming a measurement of the PSR J0737-3039A moment of inertia to 10% precision, we study the implications for neutron star radii and tidal deformabilities. We also determine the crustal component of the moment of inertia and find that for typical neutron star masses of 1.3 M_sun < M < 1.5 M_sun the crust contributes 1% - 6% of the total moment of inertia, below what is needed to explain large pulsar glitches in the scenario of strong neutron entrainment.

## Key claim

The authors predict that the moment of inertia of the 1.338 solar mass pulsar J0737-3039A lies in the range 1.04 x 10^45 g cm^2 to 1.51 x 10^45 g cm^2 at 95% credibility, with a most probable value of 1.36 x 10^45 g cm^2, but this prediction depends on a minimal model that extrapolates chiral effective field theory constraints without including phase transitions or exotic degrees of freedom, making it most reliable for neutron stars below about 1.4 solar masses.

## Method

The authors constructed a nuclear energy density functional as a fourth-order power series expansion in the Fermi momentum, fitted to ten equations of state from chiral effective field theory many-body perturbation calculations up to twice saturation density. Bayesian posterior probability distributions for the functional parameters were obtained using microscopic chiral EFT predictions as priors and empirical nuclear matter properties from 205 Skyrme force models as likelihood functions. From 300,000 randomly sampled equations of state, the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations and the slow-rotation approximation for the moment of inertia were solved for over 30 million neutron star configurations, using a unified liquid drop model for the crust that is consistent with the same nuclear energy density functional used for the core.

## Key numbers

- For PSR J0737-3039A (1.338 solar masses): moment of inertia lies in the range 1.04 x 10^45 g cm^2 < I < 1.51 x 10^45 g cm^2 at 95% credibility; most probable value is I = 1.36 x 10^45 g cm^2
- For a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: most probable radius is 12.15 km, with 95% credibility range 10.26 km to 12.87 km
- Crustal contribution to the total moment of inertia: for neutron star masses between 1.3 and 1.5 solar masses, the crust contributes 1% to 6% of the total moment of inertia
- Symmetry energy J from the Bayesian posterior: 29.1 MeV to 34.4 MeV; slope parameter L: 34.6 MeV to 72.6 MeV, with correlation coefficient R = 0.93
- Empirical fitting formula for I/(MR^2) as a function of M/R uses parameters a = 27.178 (solar masses/km)^(-3), b = 0.0871 solar masses/km, c = 2.183, with relative error 0.06% for a canonical 1.44 solar mass, 12 km neutron star

## Context

This work extends Bayesian modeling of the nuclear equation of state previously applied to neutron star tidal deformabilities from GW170817 (Lim 2018) to the prediction of neutron star moments of inertia, focusing on the double-pulsar system J0737-3039 discovered by Burgay (2003) and Lyne (2004). The predicted crustal moment of inertia fraction (1-6%) is below the 7-9% range that earlier studies by Andersson (2012) and Chamel (2013) suggested is needed to explain large pulsar glitches in the Vela pulsar under the scenario of strong neutron entrainment. A future measurement of the J0737-3039A moment of inertia to 10% precision, as proposed by Lattimer (2005) and Morrison (2004), would provide constraints on neutron star radii competitive with gravitational wave observations from LIGO/VIRGO and X-ray timing measurements.
