# New Prospects for Detecting High-Energy Neutrinos from Nearby Supernovae

> Neutrinos from supernovae (SNe) are crucial probes of explosive phenomena at the deaths of massive stars and neutrino physics. High-energy neutrinos are produced through hadronic processes by cosmic rays, which are accelerated during interaction between the supernova (SN) ejecta and circumstellar material (CSM). Recent observations of extragalactic SNe have revealed that a dense CSM is commonly expelled by the progenitor star. We provide new quantitative predictions of time-dependent high-energy neutrino emission from diverse types of SNe. We show that IceCube and KM3Net can detect about 1000 events from a SN II-P (and about 300000 events from a SN IIn) at a distance of 10 kpc. The new model also enables us to critically optimize the time window for dedicated searches for nearby SNe. A successful detection will give us a multienergy neutrino view of SN physics and new opportunities to study neutrino properties, as well as clues to the cosmic-ray origin. GeV-TeV neutrinos may also be seen by KM3Net, Hyper-Kamiokande, and PINGU.

# New Prospects for Detecting High-Energy Neutrinos from Nearby Supernovae

## Key claim
High-energy neutrino emission from core-collapse supernovae is detectable by IceCube and KM3Net at levels of ~10^3 events for a Type II-P supernova at 10 kpc, provided that the progenitor star has expelled a dense circumstellar material (CSM) before explosion, a condition that recent optical observations show is common. The detectability depends critically on the CSM density and the time window chosen for the search, which the author's time-dependent model optimizes for the first time.

## Method
The author calculates time-dependent high-energy neutrino emission from hadronuclear (pp) interactions between cosmic rays accelerated at the forward shock and the dense circumstellar material, using self-similar solutions for the shock evolution in a wind-like CSM profile (density ∝ r⁻²). For each supernova type (IIn, II-P, II-L/IIb, Ibc), the shock radius, velocity, and kinetic luminosity are evolved, the maximum proton energy is determined by escape or dynamical time, and neutrino spectra are computed from the pp cross section and secondary particle yields. The number of through-going muon tracks in IceCube is then estimated using the detector's angle-averaged effective area, accounting for neutrino mixing and atmospheric plus astrophysical backgrounds.

## Key numbers
- IceCube can detect ~2.8 × 10² muon events (s = 2.2, E_μ > 1 TeV, integrated over <10⁷ s) from a Type II-P supernova at 10 kpc with enhanced CSM (D_* = 10⁻²); for s = 2.0 the number is ~1.2 × 10³.
- IceCube can detect ~2.7 × 10⁴ muon events (s = 2.2, E_μ > 1 TeV, <10⁷ s) from a Type IIn supernova at 10 kpc; for s = 2.0 the number is ~1.1 × 10⁵.
- For Betelgeuse at 0.197 kpc as a Type II-P without enhanced CSM (D_* = 1.34 × 10⁻⁴), IceCube can detect ~5.5 × 10² muon events (s = 2.2, E_μ > 1 TeV, <10⁷ s); with enhanced CSM the expectation is ~3 × 10⁶ events.
- The optimal time window for Type II-P and II-L/IIb supernovae is ~0.1–1 day after the MeV neutrino detection; for Type IIn supernovae it is ~0.1–1 year.
- The effective pp optical depth for a Type II-P with enhanced CSM is f_pp ≈ 0.82 D_{*,-2}^{6/5} M_{ej,1}^{7/10} ℰ_{ej,51}^{-9/10} t_{5.5}^{-4/5}, reaching unity at ~4 × 10⁵ s.
- The shock kinetic luminosity for a Type II-P is L_s ≈ 1.0 × 10⁴² erg s⁻¹ D_{*,-2}^{7/10} ℰ_{ej,51}^{27/20} M_{ej,1}^{-21/20} t_{5.5}^{-3/10}.

## Context
This work extends earlier predictions for high-energy neutrinos from rare supernova types (Type IIn and Ibc-BL, as in Murase (2010) and Katz (2011)) to include the dominant Type II-P class, motivated by recent optical observations (e.g., SN 2013fs by Yaron (2017)) showing that dense CSM is common around red supergiant progenitors. The time-dependent model enables optimization of search windows for IceCube and KM3Net, which is critical because the signal-to-background ratio evolves with time. A successful detection would provide the first multienergy neutrino view of a supernova (combining thermal MeV neutrinos with nonthermal GeV–PeV neutrinos), test cosmic-ray acceleration in real time, and enable studies of neutrino properties such as decay, sterile neutrino mixing, and flavor oscillations. The predictions are consistent with non-detections from past events like SN 1987A and with gamma-ray constraints on Type IIn supernovae from Fermi-LAT (2015).
