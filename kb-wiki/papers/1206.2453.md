# Microscopic origin of pairing

> A brief review of recent progress in the ab intio theory of nuclear pairing is given. Nowdays several successful solutions of the ab intio BCS theory gap equation were published which show that it is a promising first step in the problem. However, the role of many-body correlations that go beyond the BCS scheme remains uncertain and requires further investigations. As an alternative, the semi-microscopic model is discussed in which the effective pairing interaction calculated from the first principles is supplemented with a small phenomenological addendum containing one phenomenological parameter universal for all medium and heavy atomic nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while *ab initio* BCS theory with bare nucleon mass provides a promising starting point for nuclear pairing, the effects of the effective mass (m* ≠ m) and many-body corrections beyond BCS are both necessary and partially cancel each other, but both contain large uncertainties that make a fully microscopic treatment unreliable at the current level of theory.

## Method

The authors review the *ab initio* BCS gap equation solved with realistic NN potentials (Argonne v14, v18) in a self-consistent basis, comparing results from the Milan group, Duguet et al., and the Moscow-Catania group. They then present a semi-microscopic model where the effective pairing interaction is calculated from first principles using the Local Potential Approximation (LPA) in a two-step renormalization scheme, and supplemented with a small phenomenological term containing one universal parameter γ for all medium and heavy nuclei.

## Key numbers

- For 120Sn, the BCS gap with Argonne v14 potential and bare mass m*=m gave ΔBCS = 2.2 MeV, compared to the experimental value Δexp ≈ 1.3 MeV.
- With the SLy4 effective mass (m* = 0.7 m in nuclear matter), the BCS gap for 120Sn was reduced to 0.7 MeV (Milan group) and 1.04 MeV (Milan group, later calculation).
- Duguet et al. obtained ΔBCS ≈ 1.6 MeV for 120Sn using the low-k force Vlow-k with the same SLy4 basis.
- The phenomenological parameter γ = 0.06 was found sufficient for both neutrons and protons to bring theoretical gaps into agreement with experiment.
- The overall root-mean-square deviation between theoretical and experimental gaps was √(Δth - Δexp)² ≈ 0.13 MeV for the DF3 functional and ≈ 0.14 MeV for the DF3-a functional.
- The separation energy E0 = 40 MeV in the LPA method guarantees accuracy better than 0.01 MeV for the gap Δ.

## Context

This review extends the work of the Milan group (2004-2006) on solving the BCS gap equation with realistic forces, and the work of Duguet and Losinsky (2008) using the low-k force for many nuclei. It also presents the semi-microscopic model developed by the Moscow-Catania group (Pankratov et al., Baldo et al., Saperstein et al.) as an alternative to fully microscopic approaches that suffer from uncertainties in the effective mass and many-body corrections. The model enables systematic calculations of pairing gaps across medium and heavy nuclei, though the authors note that a more consistent scheme should explicitly include low-lying phonon corrections with tadpole diagrams as in the Finite Fermi Systems theory of Khodel and Saperstein.
