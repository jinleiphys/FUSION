# Solar opacity calculations using the super-transition-array method

> A new opacity model based on the Super-Transition-Array (STA) method for the calculation of monochromatic opacities of local thermodynamic equilibrium plasmas, was developed. The atomic code, named STAR (STA-Revised), is described and used to calculate spectral opacities for a solar model implementing the recent AGSS09 composition. Calculations are carried throughout the solar radiative zone. The relative contributions of different chemical elements and atomic processes to the total Rosseland mean opacity are analyzed in detail. Monochromatic opacities and charge state distributions were compared with the widely used Opacity-Project (OP) code, for several elements near the radiation-convection interface. STAR Rosseland opacities for the solar mixture show a very good agreement with OP and the OPAL opacity code, throughout the radiation zone. Finally, an explicit STA calculation of the full AGSS09 photospheric mixture, including all heavy metals was performed. It was shown that due to their extremely low abundance, and despite being very good photon absorbers, the heavy elements do not affect the Rosseland opacity.

## Key claim

The STAR code, based on the Super-Transition-Array (STA) method, produces Rosseland mean opacities for the solar mixture that show "very good agreement" with the OP and OPAL codes throughout the radiation zone, but the paper does not claim exact agreement or that STAR resolves the solar composition problem; rather, it provides a detailed analysis of elemental and process contributions under the specific AGSS09 composition.

## Method

The STAR atomic code calculates monochromatic opacities for local thermodynamic equilibrium plasmas using the STA method, which groups the enormous number of transition lines between configurations into large assemblies called Super-Transition-Arrays, whose moments are computed analytically via partition function algebra and then split recursively until convergence. The code uses a fully relativistic quantum mechanical treatment via the Dirac equation, includes bound-bound, bound-free, free-free, and scattering contributions with corrections for degeneracy, plasma collective effects, and Stark broadening, and calculates effective mass densities for each element in the mixture using a relativistic Hartree-Fock-Slater average atom model.

## Key numbers

- The number of populated electronic configurations near the convection-radiation boundary exceeds \(10^{8}\) for atomic numbers \(Z > 15\), making detailed configuration accounting very costly.
- At the radiation-convection boundary (\(R = 0.726\), \(T = 174.9\) eV, \(\rho = 0.16\) g/cm\(^3\)), monochromatic opacities and charge state distributions were compared with the Opacity-Project code for several elements.
- The Rosseland peak is located at approximately \(3.83 k_B T\).
- The free-free degeneracy correction is shown to be vital at the solar core vicinity, where the plasma is more degenerate.
- The paper shows that heavy elements (beyond the 24 included in the standard mixture) do not affect the Rosseland opacity due to their extremely low abundance, despite being very good photon absorbers.

## Context

This work extends the STA method developed by Bar-Shalom (1989) and applies it to solar opacity calculations using the revised AGSS09 composition from Asplund (2009), addressing the solar composition problem where standard solar models fail to reproduce helioseismic measurements. The results are compared with the widely used Opacity-Project code by Seaton (1994) and Badnell (2005) and the OPAL code by Iglesias and Rogers (1996), and the analysis of elemental contributions agrees with Villante (2010) and Blancard (2012). The paper enables future work on opacity variations needed to resolve the solar composition problem, where changes of 2-5% in central regions and 12-15% at the radiation-convection interface are required.
