# Charge-changing-cross-section measurements of $^{12-16}$C at around $45A$ MeV and development of a Glauber model for incident energies $10A-2100A$ MeV

> We have measured for the first time the charge-changing cross sections ($σ_{\text{CC}}$) of $^{12-16}$C on a $^{12}$C target at energies below $100A$ MeV. To analyze these low-energy data, we have developed a finite-range Glauber model with a global parameter set within the optical-limit approximation which is applicable to reaction cross section ($σ_{\text{R}}$) and $σ_{\text{CC}}$ measurements at incident energies from 10$A$ to $2100A$ MeV. Adopting the proton-density distribution of $^{12}$C known from the electron-scattering data, as well as the bare total nucleon-nucleon cross sections, and the real-to-imaginary-part ratios of the forward proton-proton elastic scattering amplitude available in the literatures, we determine the energy-dependent slope parameter $β_{\rm pn}$ of the proton-neutron elastic differential cross section so as to reproduce the existing $σ_{\text{R}}$ and interaction-cross-section data for $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C over a wide range of incident energies. The Glauber model thus formulated is applied to calculate the $σ_{\text{\tiny R}}$'s of $^{12}$C on a $^9$Be and $^{27}$Al targets at various incident energies. Our calculations show excellent agreement with the experimental data. Applying our model to the $σ_{\text{\tiny R}}$ and $σ_{\text{\tiny CC}}$ for the "neutron-skin" $^{16}$C nucleus, we reconfirm the importance of measurements at incident energies below $100A$ MeV. The proton root-mean-square radii of $^{12-16}$C are extracted using the measured $σ_{\text{CC}}$'s and the existing $σ_{\text{R}}$ data. The results for $^{12-14}$C are consistent with the values from the electron scatterings, demonstrating the feasibility, usefulness of the $σ_{\text{CC}}$ measurement and the present Glauber model.

## Key claim

The authors present the first measurement of charge-changing cross sections for carbon isotopes $^{12-16}$C on a $^{12}$C target at energies around $45A$ MeV, and develop a finite-range Glauber model within the optical-limit approximation that is applicable across incident energies from $10A$ to $2100A$ MeV. The model's success depends on determining the energy-dependent slope parameter $\beta_{\rm pn}$ of the proton-neutron elastic differential cross section, which is the only missing parameter besides density distributions required in the calculation.

## Method

The authors measured charge-changing cross sections ($\sigma_{\text{CC}}$) of $^{12-16}$C on a $^{12}$C target at approximately $45A$ MeV using the transmission method at the RCNP EN course, with secondary beams produced by fragmentation of a $^{22}$Ne primary beam at $80A$ MeV on a $^{9}$Be target. They developed a finite-range Glauber model within the optical-limit approximation, determining the energy-dependent slope parameter $\beta_{\rm pn}(E)$ by fitting to existing reaction cross section ($\sigma_{\text{R}}$) data for $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C from $10A$ to $2100A$ MeV, using the proton-density distribution of $^{12}$C from electron-scattering data and bare total nucleon-nucleon cross sections. The model was then applied to extract proton root-mean-square radii of $^{12-16}$C from the measured $\sigma_{\text{CC}}$ values combined with existing $\sigma_{\text{R}}$ data.

## Context

This work extends the Glauber model approach previously applied at higher energies (above $200A$ MeV) by Tanihata (2012), Terashima (2014), Estrade (2013), and Yamaguchi (2015) to lower incident energies where the standard optical-limit approximation with zero-range approximation had failed, showing discrepancies of almost 20% at a few tens of MeV per nucleon as reported by Takechi (2005). The model enables extraction of proton rms radii for unstable carbon isotopes using charge-changing cross section measurements, providing an alternative to laser spectroscopy isotope-shift methods which are extremely challenging for nuclei with atomic numbers between 4 and 10. The results for $^{12-14}$C are consistent with electron-scattering values from Angeli (1999), demonstrating the feasibility of this method for determining proton radii of short-lived nuclei where electron scattering is not applicable.
