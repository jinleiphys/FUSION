# Geometry of effective Hamiltonians

> We give a complete geometrical description of the effective Hamiltonians common in nuclear shell model calculations. By recasting the theory in a manifestly geometric form, we reinterpret and clarify several points. Some of these results are hitherto unknown or unpublished. In particular, commuting observables and symmetries are discussed in detail. Simple and explicit proofs are given, and numerical algorithms are proposed, that improve and stabilize common methods used today.

## Key claim

The paper provides a complete geometric description of effective Hamiltonians used in nuclear shell model calculations, showing that the Bloch-Brandow (non-Hermitian) and canonical (Hermitian) effective Hamiltonians correspond to different optimization criteria for the effective eigenvectors: the former minimizes the distance between exact and effective eigenvectors globally at the cost of non-orthonormality, while the latter minimizes this distance under the constraint that the effective eigenvectors remain orthonormal.

## Method

The author recasts effective Hamiltonian theory in geometric terms using principal angles and principal vectors between the model space \(\CalP\) and the target eigenspace \(\CalE\) spanned by the exact eigenvectors of interest. The decoupling operator \(\omega\) is expressed via its singular value decomposition in terms of the principal angles \(\theta_k\) and principal vectors, and the unitary rotation \(Z = \exp(G)\) that rotates \(\CalP\) into \(\CalE\) is constructed explicitly. The canonical effective Hamiltonian is then obtained by applying this rotation, and the relationship between the Bloch-Brandow and canonical effective Hamiltonians is shown to be a similarity transformation \(F\) that can be computed via the singular value decomposition of the operator mapping the model space basis to the projected exact eigenvectors.

## Key numbers

- The model space dimension \(m\) is smaller than the Hilbert space dimension \(n\), with \(m \leq n\).
- The canonical angles \(\theta_k\) lie in the interval \([0, \pi/2]\) and satisfy \(\cos\theta_k = \braket{\xi_k|\eta_k}\) for principal vectors \(\ket{\xi_k} \in \CalP\) and \(\ket{\eta_k} \in \CalE\).
- The decoupling operator \(\omega\) has singular values \(\tan\theta_k\) for \(k=1,\ldots,m\).
- The operator \(F = (\omega^\dag\omega + P)^{1/2}\) has singular values \(1/\cos\theta_k\) for \(k=1,\ldots,m\).
- The series for \(\tanh^{-1}(z)\) converges for \(|\theta_k| < \pi/4\) and is analytically continued to arbitrary \(0 \leq \theta_k \leq \pi/2\).

## Context

This work extends the mathematical foundations of effective interaction theory developed by Bloch (1958), Brandow (1967), and Suzuki (1982), and provides a geometric reinterpretation of the Hermitian effective Hamiltonian previously studied by Van Vleck (1929), Kemble (1937), and Shavitt and Redmon (1980). The geometric formulation enables a new numerical algorithm for computing the canonical effective Hamiltonian using only the singular value decomposition, which is conceptually and computationally simpler than the existing approach relying on matrix inversion and square root used in no-core shell model calculations by Navratil (2000) and others. The symmetry analysis shows that model spaces preserving the symmetries of the Hamiltonian (i.e., with \([S,P]=0\) for conserved observables \(S\)) yield effective Hamiltonians that also preserve those symmetries, which has implications for sub-cluster approximations in no-core shell model calculations.
