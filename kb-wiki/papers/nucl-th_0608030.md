# David Brink: A long-standing teacher

## Key claim
This talk presents a short review of David Brink's most important achievements and of my own experience working with him, highlighting his role as a founder of theoretical nuclear physics over the last fifty years through his ideas and intuitions.

## Method
The paper is a conference talk that reviews Brink's career contributions to nuclear structure and reactions, including collective motion built on excited states, the Generator Coordinate Method, microscopic models of the optical potential, and transfer and breakup theories. The author also describes her personal scientific collaboration with Brink from 1978 onward, covering work on the imaginary part of the alpha-40Ca optical potential, transfer to the continuum, and breakup from halo nuclei.

## Key numbers
- Brink's thesis at the University of Oxford was completed in 1955, containing the idea of collective motion built on excited states, confirmed experimentally in the 1970s.
- His 1972 paper on transfer matching conditions remains one of his most cited works.
- Brink has been a Fellow of the Royal Society since 1981.
- He received the Rutherford Medal of the Institute of Physics in 1982.
- He was made Foreign Member of the Royal Society of Sciences of Uppsala in 1992.
- He was awarded the 2006 Lise Meitner Prize of the European Physical Society (shared with Heinz-Jurgen Kluge).
- The author graduated from Oxford on 13 June 1981.

## Context
This talk was presented at the Varenna Conference on Nuclear Reaction Mechanisms in 2006, dedicated to David Brink. It extends the recognition of Brink's foundational work in theoretical nuclear physics, including his development with Vautherin of effective interactions such as the Skyrme force, which led to density functional approaches. The paper enables future work on nuclear structure at the dripline, particularly on halo nuclei and projectile fragmentation, building on prior collaborations with A. Winther, R. Broglia, G. Pollarolo, and others.
