# The inverse Laplace transform as the ultimate tool for transverse mass spectra

> New high statistics data from the second generation of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion experiments open up new possibilities in terms of data analysis. To fully utilize the potential we propose to analyze the $m_\perp$-spectra of hadrons using the inverse Laplace transform. The problems with its inherent ill-definedness can be overcome and several applications in other fields like biology, chemistry or optics have already shown its feasability. Moreover, the method also promises to deliver upper bounds on the total information content of the spectra, which is of big importance for all other means of analysis. Here we compute several Laplace inversions from different thermal scenarios, both analytically and numerically, to test the efficiency of the method. Especially the case of a two component structure, related to a possible first order phase transition to a quark gluon plasma, is closer investigated and it is shown that at least a signal to noise ratio of $10^4$ is necessary to resolve two individual components.

## Key claim

The inverse Laplace transform of transverse mass spectra can be used to resolve multi-component structures in hadron emission, but a signal-to-noise ratio of at least \(10^4\) is required to distinguish two individual components, such as those that might arise from a first-order phase transition to a quark-gluon plasma.

## Method

The paper computes Laplace inversions of transverse mass spectra from several thermal scenarios, both analytically and numerically, to test the method's efficiency. A two-component structure, related to a possible first-order phase transition to a quark-gluon plasma, is investigated to determine the necessary signal-to-noise ratio for resolving the components.

## Key numbers

* A signal-to-noise ratio of at least \(10^4\) is necessary to resolve two individual components in a two-component structure.
* The analysis uses new high-statistics data from the second generation of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion experiments.

## Context

This work extends the use of inverse Laplace transforms, already applied in biology, chemistry, and optics, to the analysis of hadron transverse mass spectra in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. The method is proposed as a tool to fully utilize the potential of new high-statistics data from second-generation experiments, and it also promises to deliver upper bounds on the total information content of the spectra, which is important for other analysis methods. The specific case of a two-component structure is investigated in relation to a possible first-order phase transition to a quark-gluon plasma.
