# Dumbbell shapes in the super-asymmetric fission of heavy nuclei

> We have calculated the fission fragments' mass distributions for several isotopes of heavy and super-heavy nuclei from uranium to flerovium within an improved scission point model. For all considered nuclei, in addition to the standard mass-asymmetric fission mode we have found the mass super-asymmetric mode with the mass of heavy fragments equal 190. For the actinide nuclei, the probability of super-asymmetric fission is by 6 orders of magnitude smaller than for standard asymmetric fission. For the superheavy nuclei this probability is only by 2 orders of magnitude smaller. In all cases, the super-asymmetric scission shapes are dumbbells with the heavy fragment close to a sphere. We have estimated the stability of the light fragment concerning the variation of the neck and found out that sequential ternary fission is not favored energetically. The calculations were carried out with nuclear shape described by generalized Cassinian ovals with 6 deformation parameters, $α, α_1, α_2, α_3, α_4$ and $α_5$. The configuration at the moment of the neck rupture was defined by fixing $α=0.98$. This value corresponds to a neck radius $r_{neck}\approx$ 1.5 fm.

## Key claim
The authors claim that a super-asymmetric fission mode exists for all studied heavy and super-heavy nuclei from uranium to flerovium, with the heavy fragment mass fixed at 190. The probability of this mode is 6 orders of magnitude smaller than standard asymmetric fission for actinides and only 2 orders smaller for superheavy nuclei, but the authors hedge that the super-asymmetric minimum is positive in energy and thus cannot be populated by spontaneous fission, requiring at least 15 MeV of excitation energy.

## Method
The authors used an improved scission point model with nuclear shapes described by generalized Cassini ovals with six deformation parameters (alpha, alpha1 through alpha5). The scission configuration was defined by fixing alpha=0.98, corresponding to a neck radius of approximately 1.5 fm. The potential energy was calculated using a macroscopic-microscopic approach with Woods-Saxon potential, and the fission fragment mass distributions were obtained from the canonical distribution of deformation energies at scission with a collective temperature T_coll=2 MeV.

## Key numbers
- The super-asymmetric mode heavy fragment mass is 190 for all studied nuclei (from U to Fl)
- For actinide nuclei, the probability of super-asymmetric fission is 6 orders of magnitude smaller than for standard asymmetric fission
- For superheavy nuclei, the probability is only 2 orders of magnitude smaller than standard asymmetric fission
- The neck radius at scission is r_neck approximately 1.5 fm (corresponding to alpha=0.98)
- The collective temperature parameter T_coll used in calculations is 2 MeV
- The super-asymmetric minimum requires at least 15 MeV excitation energy to be populated
- For U isotopes, the super-asymmetric peak yield is of order 10^-6; for Fl isotopes, close to 10^-2
- The neck mass A_3 is 2 amu for 236U, 2 for 240Pu, 4 for 246Cm, 3 for 252Cf, 2 for 258Fm, 2 for 256No, 10 for 282Hs, and 6 for 292Fl
- The width of TKE distributions for both main and SAF peaks is approximately 20 MeV

## Context
This work extends the improved scission point model previously applied to standard asymmetric fission of No and Rf isotopes (Ivanyuk and Carjan, 2016) and to bi-modal fission of heavy actinides (Ivanyuk and Carjan, 2020). It contradicts the interpretation of Lohengrin data (Rochman, 2004) which found super-asymmetric fission at constant light fragment mass A_L=70; here the SAF occurs at constant heavy fragment mass A_F=190, with shell stabilization from nuclei near 208Pb rather than from 68Ni. The results enable experimental searches for super-asymmetric fission in 256No and 254No, which could be produced via 48Ca on 208Pb reactions at the SHE-Factory in Dubna, with predicted TKE distributions to help identify the two fission modes.
