# Attractive inter-particle force in Van der Waals model of hadron gas in the grand canonical ensemble

> We generalize derivation of partition functions of the grand canonical ensemble for the multicomponent van der Waals gas of interacting particles by hardcore potentials to the case of the attractive large-distance mean field. The formulas obtained by the saddle point method for the thermodynamic potentials with the transparent non-relativistic limit to the case of conserving large number of particles for different gas components like neutrons and protons of nuclear matter can be used for analysis of experimental data for the particle number ratios in nucleus-nucleus collisions at high excitation energies.

## Key claim

The paper generalizes the derivation of grand canonical partition functions for a multicomponent van der Waals gas from the case of only hard-core repulsion to include a long-range attractive mean field, using the saddle-point method; the resulting formulas are intended for analysis of particle number ratios in nucleus-nucleus collisions, but the derivation is performed in the non-relativistic limit with conserved particle numbers, and the extension to relativistic high-energy collisions is stated as a future step rather than a completed result.

## Method

The author starts from the canonical partition function for a one-component van der Waals gas with hard-core repulsion and then converts the sum over particle numbers in the grand canonical ensemble to an integral, which is evaluated by the saddle-point method. This procedure is extended to a two-component gas, where the interaction potential includes both an infinite repulsive core (hard sphere) and a finite attractive well at intermediate distances, parameterized by radii \(R^0_i\) and \(R_i\) and well depth \(u_0^{(uv)}\). The Mayer function expansion is used to derive the effective second virial coefficients \(B_{uv}(T)\) and the cross-term coefficients \(\tilde{B}_{ij}\), leading to a van der Waals equation of state in the grand canonical ensemble with coupled transcendental equations for the particle densities \(\xi_1\) and \(\xi_2\).

## Key numbers

* For a symmetric two-component gas with \(b_{11}=b_{22}\), the critical density is \(n_c \sim \sqrt{1/(9 \cdot 4 b_{11}^2)}\) (Eq. 38).
* The critical temperature for the symmetric case is \(T_c = 8a/(27 b_{11})\) (Eq. 35).
* The critical pressure for the symmetric case is \(P_c = a/(27 b_{11}^2)\) (Eq. 41).
* The second virial coefficient for a pair of species \(u,v\) is given by \(2B_{uv}(T) = \frac{4}{3}\pi (R^0_u+R^0_v)^3 - (u_0^{(uv)}/T) \frac{4}{3}\pi [(R_u+R_v)^3-(R^0_u+R^0_v)^3]\) (Eq. 18), which is decomposed as \(2(b_{uv} - a_{uv}/T)\) with \(b_{uv} = \frac{2}{3}\pi (R^0_u+R^0_v)^3\) and \(a_{uv} = u_0^{(uv)}(c_{uv}-b_{uv})\) where \(c_{uv} = \frac{2}{3}\pi (R_u+R_v)^3\).

## Context

This work extends the multicomponent van der Waals model of Gorenstein (no year given in text) by adding an attractive mean-field term to the existing hard-core repulsion, and it generalizes the approach of Krivenko (no year given in text) to include attractive interactions. The resulting equation of state and density equations can be used to analyze particle yield ratios in relativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions, and the author suggests future applications to calculating kinetic coefficients such as viscosity, thermal conductivity, and diffusion.
