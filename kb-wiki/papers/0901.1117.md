# Singular Charge Density at the Center of the Pion?

> We relate the three-dimensional infinite momentum frame spatial charge density of the pion to its electromagnetic form factor. Diverse treatments of the measured form factor data including: phenomenological fits, non-relativistic quark models, the application of perturbative QCD, QCD sum rules, holographic QCD, and the Nambu Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model all lead to the result that the charge density at the center of the pion has a logarithmic divergence. Relativistic constituent quark models do not display this singularity. Future measurements planned for larger values of momentum transfer may determine whether or not a singularity actually occurs.

## Key claim

The transverse charge density at the center of the pion, derived from the measured electromagnetic form factor, exhibits a logarithmic divergence under a wide range of theoretical treatments; however, this singularity is not inevitable, as relativistic constituent quark models produce a finite central density, and future higher-\(Q^2\) measurements are needed to determine whether the singularity is real.

## Method

The author relates the three-dimensional infinite-momentum-frame spatial charge density of the pion to its electromagnetic form factor \(F_\pi(Q^2)\) via a two-dimensional Fourier transform, \(\rho(b) = \int d^2q/(2\pi)^2 \, F_\pi(Q^2=\mathbf{q}^2) e^{-i\mathbf{q}\cdot\mathbf{b}}\). The resulting transverse charge density \(\rho(b)\) is then evaluated for a monopole fit to existing form factor data (\(R^2 = 0.431\ \mathrm{fm}^2\)) and compared with predictions from perturbative QCD, the spectral quark model, the Nambu–Jona-Lasinio model, holographic QCD, and a relativistic constituent quark model (Hwang 2001).

## Key numbers

- Monopole fit to pion form factor data: \(F_\pi(Q^2) = 1/(1 + R^2 Q^2/6)\) with \(R^2 = 0.431\ \mathrm{fm}^2\); this yields \(\rho(b) = 3 K_0(\sqrt{6}\,b/R)/(\pi R^2)\), which diverges as \(\log(b)\) for small \(b\).
- Perturbative QCD prediction: \(\lim_{Q^2\to\infty} F_\pi(Q^2) = 16\pi \alpha_s(Q^2) f_\pi^2 / Q^2\) with \(f_\pi = 93\ \mathrm{MeV}\); this leads to a central density singularity \(\sim \log\log(1/b)\).
- NJL model parameters: quark mass \(M = 280\ \mathrm{MeV}\), pion decay constant \(f = 93.3\ \mathrm{MeV}\), regulator \(\Lambda = 870\ \mathrm{MeV}\); the integrated charge density diverges logarithmically upon integration over the longitudinal momentum fraction \(x\).
- Existing form factor data extend up to \(Q^2 = 2.45\ \mathrm{GeV}^2\) (Blok 2008, Huber 2008).

## Context

This work extends the formalism of impact-parameter parton distributions (Soper 1977, Burkardt 2000, Diehl 2002) by directly relating the three-dimensional infinite-momentum-frame charge density to the electromagnetic form factor via a Fourier transform, rather than through the longitudinal-momentum-fraction integral. The analysis shows that a wide class of models, including phenomenological monopole fits, perturbative QCD (Lepage 1979, Efremov 1978), QCD sum rules (Radyushkin 1990), holographic QCD (Brodsky 2007, Kwee 2007, Grigoryan 2007), the spectral quark model (Ruiz Arriola 2003, Broniowski 2003), and the NJL model (Broniowski 2008), all predict a singular central charge density, while relativistic constituent quark models (Hwang 2001) do not. The result motivates new measurements at higher \(Q^2\) (planned at JLab) to discriminate between these scenarios and to test whether the central charge density of the pion is truly singular.
