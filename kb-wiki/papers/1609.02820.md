# Do nuclear collisions create a locally equilibrated quark-gluon plasma?

> Experimental results on azimuthal correlations in high energy nuclear collisions (nucleus-nucleus, proton-nucleus and proton-proton) seem to be well described by viscous hydrodynamics. It is often argued that this agreement implies either local thermal equilibrium or at least local isotropy. In this note, I present arguments why this is not the case. Neither local near-equilibrium nor near-isotropy are required in order for hydrodynamics to offer a successful and accurate description of experimental results. However, I predict the breakdown of hydrodynamics at momenta of order seven times the temperature, corresponding to a smallest possible QCD liquid drop size of 0.15 fm.

## Key claim

Hydrodynamics can provide a quantitatively accurate description of the energy-momentum tensor in high energy nuclear collisions even when the system is far from local thermal equilibrium and far from local isotropy, contradicting the common interpretation that the success of hydrodynamic modeling implies near-equilibrium conditions. The applicability of hydrodynamics is determined solely by the relative importance of non-hydrodynamic modes, not by the degree of equilibration or isotropy.

## Method

The author analyzes the analytic structure of the retarded two-point function of the energy-momentum tensor in both strongly coupled N=4 SYM (via gauge-gravity duality) and weakly coupled kinetic theory in the relaxation time approximation, identifying the critical wave-number k_c at which hydrodynamic poles disappear from the spectrum. The author also examines the divergent nature of the hydrodynamic gradient expansion for Bjorken flow in N=4 SYM, showing that the series can be resummed via Borel transformation to separate hydrodynamic and non-hydrodynamic contributions, with the non-hydrodynamic modes corresponding to quasi-normal modes of the dual black hole.

## Key numbers

- The hydrodynamic gradient expansion for N=4 SYM under Bjorken flow is divergent with coefficients α_n ∝ n! for large n, and the first non-hydrodynamic quasi-normal mode frequency is ω_QNM^(1) = ±3.119452 − 2.746676 i (in units of 2πT)
- In kinetic theory with relaxation time τ_R, hydrodynamic modes vanish from the spectrum at |k|_c τ_R ≃ 4.5, which for QCD with α_s ≃ 0.5 translates to |k|_c/T ≃ 4-7
- The predicted breakdown scale for hydrodynamics in QCD corresponds to a smallest possible QCD liquid drop size of 0.15 fm at T ≃ 200 MeV
- For N=4 SYM at infinite coupling, the pressure anisotropy exceeds 10 percent for all times τ ≲ 10 fm/c in expanding systems
- At phenomenologically relevant times τ ∼ 1 fm/c following a nuclear collision, no theoretical approach finds the longitudinal and transverse pressure to agree to better than a factor of two

## Context

This work challenges the traditional paradigm, established by Heinz (2001), Luzum and Romatschke (2008), and Schenke (2010), that the success of hydrodynamics in describing nuclear collisions implies local thermal equilibrium. It extends the observation by Arnold, Lenaghan, Moore, and Yaffe (2004) that full thermalization was not required for hydrodynamics, showing that even near-isotropy is unnecessary. The analysis builds on the divergent gradient expansion results of Heller, Janik, and Witaszczyk (2013) and the hydrodynamic breakdown scale calculations of Grozdanov (2016) and Romatschke (2015). The work enables a reinterpretation of experimental data from proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions as probing non-equilibrium QCD rather than the equilibrium phase diagram, and opens the possibility of studying non-equilibrium phenomena such as entropy production and non-thermal fixed points in nuclear collisions.
