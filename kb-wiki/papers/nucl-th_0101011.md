# Coulomb versus nuclear break-up of 11Be halo nucleus in a non perturbative framework

> The 11Be break-up is calculated using a non perturbative time-dependent quantum calculation. The evolution of the neutron halo wave function shows an emission of neutron at large angles for grazing impact parameters and at forward angles for large impact parameters. The neutron angular distribution is deduced for the different targets and compared to experimental data. We emphasize the diversity of diffraction mechanisms, in particular we discuss the interplay of the nuclear effects such as the towing mode and the Coulomb break-up. A good agreement is found with experimental data.

# Coulomb versus nuclear break-up of 11Be halo nucleus in a non perturbative framework

## Key claim

The paper claims that a non-perturbative time-dependent quantum calculation simultaneously accounting for both Coulomb and nuclear interactions reproduces experimental neutron angular distributions for 11Be break-up on Au, Ti, and Be targets at 41 MeV per nucleon, but the agreement requires multiplying the calculated cross section by a spectroscopic factor of 0.84 for the 2s state, and the separation between Coulomb and nuclear contributions is empirically validated rather than naturally emerging from the framework.

## Method

The authors solve the full time-dependent Schrödinger equation for the neutron halo wave function on a 3D Cartesian mesh using the split-operator method, with the 11Be ground state obtained from a Wood-Saxon potential with a surface derivative term. The classical Coulomb trajectories of the 10Be core and target are computed via the Runge-Kutta method, and the time-dependent nuclear and Coulomb potentials between the neutron and both the target and the 10Be core are included simultaneously. The emitted wave function is isolated by projecting out all bound states of the neutron in the 10Be potential, and the momentum-space angular distributions are obtained via Fourier transform.

## Key numbers

- The 2s state of the neutron in 11Be is bound by -0.503 MeV in the calculation.
- For the Au target at small angles (below 10 degrees), the experimental cross section is as high as 50 barns per steradian; for the Be target it is around 1 barn per steradian.
- At large angles (around 30 degrees), the cross section is about 100 mbarn per steradian for all studied targets.
- The minimum impact parameters used are: 11 fm for Au, 8 fm for Ti, and 6 fm for Be, following the strong absorption model formula 1.4(AT^(1/3) + ABe^(1/3)).
- The fraction of wave function emitted at large impact parameters follows a fit α b^β with β = -2.4 and α = 0.0088 × Z^2.
- The nuclear contribution scales with AT^(2/3) and follows an exponential fit with γ = 0.40 ± 0.01 fm^(-1).
- The Coulomb break-up cross section scales with Z^(1.65).
- For a non-halo neutron bound by 7 MeV, the differential cross section below 10 degrees is more than 100 times lower than for 11Be; at around 40 degrees it is about 4 times lower.
- The calculation uses a time step of 1.7 fm/c on a mesh of 64×64×64 fm^3 with a step size of 0.5 fm.

## Context

This work extends earlier perturbative calculations by Bonaccorso, Brink, and Margueron (1998) and non-perturbative Coulomb-only calculations by Melezhik and Baye (1999) and Typel and Wolter (1999), as well as the time-dependent approach of Lacroix, Scarpaci, and Chomaz (1999). The paper confirms that forward-peaked neutrons arise from Coulomb dissociation while large-angle emission comes from the nuclear "towing mode" mechanism previously observed in stable nuclei by Scarpaci et al. (1998). The results enable using neutron angular distributions as a spectroscopic tool to extract information on halo wave function properties, particularly the extension of the wave function, and suggest that measurements of both small and large angle neutron emission could distinguish halo from non-halo states.
