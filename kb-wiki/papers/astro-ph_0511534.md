# Primordial Nucleosynthesis: Successes And Challenges

> Primordial nucleosynthesis provides a probe of the Universe during its early evolution. Given the progress exploring the constituents, structure, and recent evolution of the Universe, it is timely to review the status of Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) and to confront its predictions, and the constraints which emerge from them, with those derived from independent observations of the Universe at much later epochs in its evolution. Following an overview of the key physics controlling element synthesis in the early Universe, the predictions of the standard models of cosmology and particle physics are presented, along with those from some non-standard models. The observational data used to infer the primordial abundances are described, with an emphasis on the distinction between precision and accuracy. These relic abundances are compared with predictions, testing the internal consistency of BBN and enabling a comparison of the BBN constraints with those derived from the WMAP Cosmic Background Radiation data. Emerging from these comparisons is a successful standard model along with constraints on (or hints of) physics beyond the standard models of particle physics and of cosmology.

## Key claim

The standard Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (SBBN) model, with its single free parameter (the baryon abundance), successfully predicts the primordial abundances of D, 4He, and 7Li within observational uncertainties, but the 7Li abundance may hint at physics beyond the standard models of particle physics and cosmology, such as a non-standard expansion rate or a neutrino asymmetry.

## Method

The paper reviews the key physics of BBN, including the competition between nuclear reaction rates and the universal expansion rate, the role of the neutron-to-proton ratio, and the deuterium bottleneck. It presents analytic fits to the BBN-predicted primordial abundances of D, 3He, 4He, and 7Li as functions of the baryon abundance parameter η10, the expansion rate factor S (or ΔNν), and the electron neutrino degeneracy parameter ξe, based on numerical calculations from Kneller and Steigman (2004). These fits are compared with observational data to test internal consistency and to derive constraints on non-standard models.

## Key numbers

- The baryon abundance parameter from WMAP CBR data is centered near η10 ≈ 6, with the BBN analysis focused on the range 4 ≤ η10 ≤ 8.
- The SBBN-predicted 4He mass fraction is Yp ≈ 0.25, with an uncertainty of σY ∼ 0.0005 (∼0.2%), dominated by the neutron lifetime τn = 885.7 ± 0.8 s.
- The analytic fit for deuterium abundance is yD ≡ 105(D/H) = 46.5 η10−1.6, accurate to better than 1% over the range 4 ≤ η10 ≤ 8.
- The analytic fit for 4He mass fraction is YpFIT = 0.2384 + 0.0016 η10 + 0.16(S−1) − 0.23 ξe, with an uncertainty of ±0.0006.
- The analytic fit for 7Li abundance is yLi ≡ 1010(Li/H) = η102 / 8.5, accurate to better than 3% over the adopted η10 range.
- For a non-standard expansion rate, ΔYp ≈ 0.16(S−1) ≈ 0.013 ΔNν at fixed η10.
- For a neutrino asymmetry, ΔYp ≈ −0.23 ξe at fixed η10.
- The analytic fits are valid for S in the range 0.85 ≤ S ≤ 1.15 (corresponding to −1.7 ≤ ΔNν ≤ 2.0) and for ξe in the range −0.1 ≤ ξe ≤ 0.1.

## Context

This review extends the work of Burles, Nollett, and Turner (2001) and Kneller and Steigman (2004) by providing simple analytic fits that enable rapid comparison of BBN predictions with observational data and with WMAP CBR constraints. The paper enables future work to test consistency between BBN and CMB data, and to constrain non-standard physics such as extra relativistic degrees of freedom (ΔNν) or a lepton asymmetry, using the complementary sensitivities of D (a baryometer) and 4He (a chronometer/leptometer).
