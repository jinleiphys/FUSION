# Uncertainty quantification due to optical potentials in models for (d,p) reactions

> Recent work has studied the uncertainty in predictions for A(d,p)B reactions using the distorted-wave Born approximation (DWBA), coming from the parameterization of the effective dA interactions [Lovell et al., Phys. Rev. C 95, 024611]. There are different levels of sophistication in theories for one-nucleon transfer reactions, including the adiabatic wave approximation (ADWA) which takes deuteron breakup into account to all orders. In this work, we quantify the uncertainties associated with the ADWA method that come from the parameterization of the NA interactions, and compare ADWA with DWBA. We use nucleon elastic-scattering data on a wide variety of targets to constrain the optical potential input to the ADWA theory. Pulling from the $χ^2$-distribution, we obtain 95% confidence plots for the elastic distributions. From the resulting parameters, we predict 95% confidence bands for the (d,p) transfer cross sections. Results obtained with the uncorrelated $χ^2$ are compared to those using the correlated $χ^2$ of Lovell et al. We also repeat the DWBA calculations for the same reactions for comparison purposes. We find that NA elastic scattering data provides a significant constraint to the interactions, and, when the uncertainties are propagated to the transfer reactions using ADWA, predictions are consistent with the transfer data. The angular distributions for ADWA differ from those predicted by DWBA, particularly at small angles. Confidence bands obtained using the uncorrelated $χ^2$-function are unrealistically narrow and become much wider when the correlated $χ^2$-function is considered. For most cases, the uncertainty bands obtained in ADWA are narrower than DWBA, when using elastic data of similar quality and range. However, given the large uncertainties predicted from the correlated $χ^2$-function, the transfer data cannot discriminate between these two methods.

## Key claim

The paper quantifies uncertainties in (d,p) reaction predictions using the adiabatic wave approximation (ADWA) that arise from optical potential parameterizations, and finds that when using a correlated χ² function, the resulting 95% confidence bands are wide enough that transfer data cannot discriminate between ADWA and the simpler distorted-wave Born approximation (DWBA), despite ADWA including deuteron breakup to all orders.

## Method

The authors used nucleon elastic-scattering data on ⁴⁸Ca, ⁹⁰Zr, and ²⁰⁸Pb targets at relevant incoming and outgoing energies to constrain the optical potential input to ADWA theory. They performed χ² minimizations using both uncorrelated and correlated χ² functions, pulled 800 parameter sets from the resulting distributions, and propagated these to obtain 95% confidence bands for (d,p) transfer cross sections. The same procedure was repeated for DWBA calculations on the same reactions for comparison.

## Key numbers

- For ⁹⁰Zr(d,p) at 22.7 MeV: relative width of 95% confidence band at the peak was 19% (ADWA uncorrelated), 52% (ADWA correlated), 23% (DWBA uncorrelated), and 36% (DWBA correlated)
- For ⁴⁸Ca(d,p) at 19.3 MeV: relative width at peak was 12% (ADWA uncorrelated), 30% (ADWA correlated), 8.3% (DWBA uncorrelated), and 132% (DWBA correlated)
- For ²⁰⁸Pb(d,p) at 32.9 MeV: relative width at peak was 24% (ADWA uncorrelated), 158% (ADWA correlated), 34% (DWBA uncorrelated), and 71% (DWBA correlated)
- The uncorrelated χ² per degree of freedom for ⁹⁰Zr(p,p) at 12.7 MeV was 0.8, compared to 51 for the starting Becchetti-Greenlees global potential
- The remnant term in the transfer calculations contributed less than 3%

## Context

This work extends the uncertainty quantification study of Lovell et al. (2017, Phys. Rev. C 95, 024611), which examined uncertainties in DWBA predictions for (d,p) reactions, by applying the same methodology to the more sophisticated ADWA method that includes deuteron breakup non-perturbatively. The results enable meaningful comparison between reaction models of different sophistication levels and demonstrate that, given the large uncertainties from optical potentials, current transfer data cannot distinguish between DWBA and ADWA, suggesting that future work should focus on reducing optical potential uncertainties rather than solely improving reaction models.
