# Ab Initio No Core Shell Model with Leadership-Class Supercomputers

> Nuclear structure and reaction theory is undergoing a major renaissance with advances in many-body methods, strong interactions with greatly improved links to Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD), the advent of high performance computing, and improved computational algorithms. Predictive power, with well-quantified uncertainty, is emerging from non-perturbative approaches along with the potential for guiding experiments to new discoveries. We present an overview of some of our recent developments and discuss challenges that lie ahead. Our foci include: (1) strong interactions derived from chiral effective field theory; (2) advances in solving the large sparse matrix eigenvalue problem on leadership-class supercomputers; (3) selected observables in light nuclei with the JISP16 interaction; (4) effective electroweak operators consistent with the Hamiltonian; and, (5) discussion of A=48 system as an opportunity for the no-core approach with the reintroduction of the core.

## Key claim

The paper presents an overview of recent developments in the *ab initio* No Core Shell Model (NCSM) using leadership-class supercomputers, but the abstract's claim of "predictive power with well-quantified uncertainty" is qualified by the fact that many results shown use the JISP16 interaction (which overbinds nuclei above A=10) and that consistent three-nucleon interactions from chiral effective field theory were still under development at the time of writing, with expected release in 2018.

## Method

The authors formulate the nuclear quantum many-body problem as a non-relativistic Hamiltonian eigenvalue problem in a harmonic oscillator basis space, treating all nucleons on the same footing. They employ the Lanczos algorithm and the Locally Optimal Block Preconditioned Conjugate Gradient (LOBPCG) algorithm implemented in the MFDn (Many Fermion Dynamics for nuclear structure) software package to solve the resulting large sparse matrix eigenvalue problem on leadership-class supercomputers including Titan, Cori, and Mira. The No-Core Full Configuration (NCFC) method is used, performing calculations in ever-increasing basis spaces and extrapolating eigenvalues and other observables to the infinite matrix limit.

## Key numbers

- TaihuLight supercomputer rated at 93 PetaFlops (nearly 10^17 floating point operations per second) with more than 10 million cores
- Titan supercomputer rated at 17.6 PetaFlops (ranked number 4 at time of writing)
- Cori supercomputer rated at 14 PetaFlops (ranked number 6)
- Mira supercomputer rated at 8.6 PetaFlops (ranked number 9)
- Exascale capability defined as 10^18 floating point operations per second
- For ^6Li ground state energy with JISP16 interaction: NCFC result of -31.53(2) MeV at N_max=18 (experimental value is -31.995 MeV), giving a difference of 470(20) keV
- Extrapolated RMS charge radius for ^6Li with JISP16: 2.28(3) fm (experimental result is 2.38(3) fm)
- Hamiltonian matrix dimensions for ^12C at N_max=10 exceed available memory on Titan or Mira when three-nucleon interactions are included
- Hamiltonian matrix dimensions exceeding 18 billion basis states achieved in Basis Light-Front Quantization (BLFQ) approach for QED

## Context

This work extends the NCSM/NCFC approach developed by Navratil (2000), Barrett (2013), and Maris (2008-2013) to leadership-class supercomputing environments, building on collaborations with the Low Energy Nuclear Physics International Collaboration (LENPIC) for chiral effective field theory Hamiltonians. The results with JISP16 for light nuclei provide a baseline for comparison with newer interactions such as Daejeon16 (Shirokov, 2016) and the upcoming consistent chiral three-nucleon interactions expected in 2018. The paper outlines a path toward calculating A=48 nuclei for evaluating nuclear double-beta decay matrix elements, which would enable comparisons with experimental searches for neutrinoless double-beta decay.
