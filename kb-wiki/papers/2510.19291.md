# Kinetic energy of fission fragments within a dynamical model

> Kinetic energy of individual fission fragment for actinide nuclei is, for example, important for evaluating the prompt-neutron spectrum in the laboratory system. It is experimentally known that kinetic energy for each fragment is constant at about 100 MeV for light fragments and that for heavy fragments decreases linearly with mass number. Most of the theoretical studies carried out so far attempted to calculate the total kinetic energy of both fragments, i.e. sum of the energies of two fragments, but the kinetic energy of each fragment was not analyzed in detail as far as we recognize. We have calculated them in thermal-neutron induced fission of $^{239}\mathrm{Pu}$ with a dynamical model using Langevin equations within a three-dimensional two-center parametrization. Also fission of $^{258}\mathrm{Fm}$ was investigated. It is calculated from the Coulomb energy at the scission point and the pre-scission kinetic energy. It is found that the pre-scission kinetic energy has about 2-4% contribution in the kinetic energy. The calculated results reproduce the trend of the experimental data.

## Key claim

The authors show that the kinetic energy of individual fission fragments can be calculated from the Coulomb energy at the scission point plus a small contribution from pre-scission kinetic energy (PKE), which contributes only 2-4% of the total kinetic energy; the calculated results reproduce the experimental trend of constant kinetic energy for light fragments and linearly decreasing kinetic energy for heavy fragments, though the model overestimates TKE in symmetric fission and shows narrower TKE distributions than measured.

## Method

The authors used three-dimensional Langevin equations with a two-center parametrization (coordinates: distance between centers \(z\), deformation \(\delta\), and mass asymmetry \(\alpha\)) to simulate the time evolution of nuclear shape in thermal-neutron induced fission of \(^{239}\mathrm{Pu}\) (compound nucleus \(^{240}\mathrm{Pu}\) at \(E^* = 6.53\) MeV) and fission of \(^{258}\mathrm{Fm}\) (at \(E^* = 7.0\) MeV). The potential energy combines a finite-range liquid drop model with Strutinsky shell corrections and pairing corrections, using hydrodynamical inertia tensors in the Werner-Wheeler approximation and one-body friction tensors from the wall-and-window formula. The total kinetic energy (TKE) is decomposed into Coulomb energy at scission plus pre-scission kinetic energy, and individual fragment kinetic energies (FKE) are obtained from TKE using momentum conservation.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{240}\mathrm{Pu}\): calculated average TKE = 173.2 MeV, average FKE for light fragments = 100.4 MeV, average FKE for heavy fragments = 72.8 MeV; experimental values from Nishio (1995) are TKE = 178.9 ± 1.2 MeV, light FKE = 103.5 ± 1.0 MeV, heavy FKE = 75.4 ± 0.7 MeV.
- For \(^{240}\mathrm{Pu}\): average total Coulomb energy (TCE) = 165.9 MeV, average pre-scission kinetic energy (PKE) = 7.3 MeV, giving PKE a 4.2% contribution to TKE.
- For \(^{240}\mathrm{Pu}\): average fragment Coulomb energy (FCE) for light fragments = 96.2 MeV, for heavy fragments = 69.7 MeV; average fragment pre-scission kinetic energy (FPKE) for light = 4.3 MeV, for heavy = 3.0 MeV.
- For \(^{258}\mathrm{Fm}\): PKE contributes about 2.2% to TKE (approximately 4 MeV out of ~230 MeV TKE in symmetric fission).
- For \(^{258}\mathrm{Fm}\): the largest TKE yield occurs at \(E_k = 230\) MeV in symmetric fission, with a second asymmetric mode around \((E_k, A_H) = (190\ \mathrm{MeV}, 155\ \mathrm{u})\).
- Excitation energies used: \(E^* = 6.53\) MeV for \(^{240}\mathrm{Pu}\) (thermal-neutron induced fission of \(^{239}\mathrm{Pu}\)), and \(E^* = 7.0\) MeV for \(^{258}\mathrm{Fm}\).

## Context

This work extends dynamical Langevin calculations (Aritomo 2014, Ishizuka 2017) from total kinetic energy to individual fragment kinetic energies, which previous scission-point models (Wilkins 1976, Carjan 2015) could not address because they lack collective motion. The results enable future experimental measurements of individual fragment kinetic energies, which the authors note are not yet available, and provide a framework for evaluating prompt-neutron spectra in the laboratory system. The study also contrasts the behavior of \(^{240}\mathrm{Pu}\) (asymmetric fission with 4.2% PKE contribution) with \(^{258}\mathrm{Fm}\) (symmetric fission with 2.2% PKE contribution), showing that compact scission configurations produce smaller pre-scission kinetic energies.
