# Explosive nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernovae

> The specific mechanism and astrophysical site for the production of half of the elements heavier than iron via rapid neutron capture (r-process) remains to be found. In order to reproduce the abundances of the solar system and of the old halo stars, at least two components are required: the heavy r-process nuclei (A>130) and the weak r-process which correspond to the lighter heavy nuclei (A<130). In this work, we present nucleosynthesis studies based on trajectories of hydrodynamical simulations for core-collapse supernovae and their subsequent neutrino-driven winds. We show that the weak r-process elements can be produced in neutrino-driven winds and we relate their abundances to the neutrino emission from the nascent neutron star. Based on the latest hydrodynamical simulations, heavy r-process elements cannot be synthesized in the neutrino-driven winds. However, by artificially increasing the wind entropy, elements up to A=195 can be made. In this way one can mimic the general behavior of an ejecta where the r-process occurs. We use this to study the impact of the nuclear physics input (nuclear masses, neutron capture cross sections, and beta-delayed neutron emission) and of the long-time dynamical evolution on the final abundances.

## Key claim
Core-collapse supernova neutrino-driven winds can produce the weak r-process elements (A<130), but based on the latest hydrodynamical simulations, heavy r-process elements (A>130) cannot be synthesized in these winds unless the wind entropy is artificially increased by a factor of two to mimic the conditions where the r-process occurs.

## Method
The authors used trajectories (density and temperature evolutions) from hydrodynamical simulations of core-collapse supernovae and their neutrino-driven winds, following the ejecta evolution for several seconds. The composition was calculated using a full reaction network including nuclei from H to Hf with both neutron- and proton-rich isotopes, initially assuming nuclear statistical equilibrium at T=10 GK. For conditions favorable to the r-process, a separate r-process network including photodissociation, neutron capture, beta decay, and fission was used after charged-particle reactions froze out.

## Key numbers
- Weak r-process (LEPP) elements with A<130 can be produced in neutrino-driven winds; heavy r-process elements up to A=195 can be made only when entropy is artificially increased by a factor of two
- Three progenitor masses were studied: 10, 15, and 25 solar masses
- The electron fraction evolution was taken to evolve from Y_e=0.5 at 1s to Y_e=0.65 at 10s for proton-rich wind models
- The reverse shock position was varied, with one trajectory having the reverse shock at T_rs=1 GK and another with no reverse shock
- Four mass models were compared: FRDM, ETFSI-Q, HFB-17, and Duflo-Zuker
- The rare earth peak in abundances is formed by neutron captures when matter moves back to stability, not during freeze-out

## Context
This work extends the line of research on r-process nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernovae following the initial success of Woosley (1994) in reproducing solar r-process abundances. It contradicts earlier expectations that neutrino-driven winds could produce heavy r-process elements, consistent with more recent simulations by Pruet et al. (2006), Arcones et al. (2007), Wanajo et al. (2009), Fischer et al. (2010), and Huedepohl et al. (2010) that find insufficient entropy and high electron fraction. The results enable future observational constraints on neutrino properties from isotopic abundances in ultra metal-poor stars and motivate experiments to measure nuclear masses far from stability.
