# Effects of kinematic cuts on net-electric charge fluctuations

> The effects of kinematic cuts on electric charge fluctuations in a gas of charged particles are discussed. We consider a very transparent example of an ideal pion gas with quantum statistics, which can be viewed as a multi-component gas of Boltzmann particles with different charges and masses. Cumulants of net-electric charge fluctuations $χ_n^Q$ are calculated in a static and expanding medium with flow parameters adjusted to the experimental data. We show that the transverse momentum cut, $p_{t_\text{min}}\leq p_t\leq p_{t_\text{max}}$, weakens the effects of Bose statistics, i.e. contributions of effectively multi-charged states to higher order moments. Consequently, cuts in $p_t$ modify the experimentally measured cumulants and their ratios. We discuss the influence of kinematic cuts on the ratio of mean and variance of electric charge fluctuations in a hadron resonance gas, in the light of recent data of the STAR and PHENIX Collaborations. We find that the different momentum cuts of $p_{t_\text{min}}=0.2$~GeV (STAR) and $p_{t_\text{min}}=0.3$~GeV (PHENIX) are responsible for more than 30\% of the difference between these two data sets. We argue that the $p_t$ cuts imposed on charged particles will influence the normalized kurtosis {$κ_Qσ_Q^2 = χ_4^Q/χ_2^Q$} of the electric charge fluctuations. In particular, the reduction of $κ_Qσ_Q^2$ with increasing $p_{t_\text{min}}$ will lead to differences between PHENIX and STAR data of ${\cal O}(6\%)$ which currently are buried under large statistical and systematic errors. We furthermore introduce the relation between momentum cut-off and finite volume effects, which is of relevance for the comparison between experimental data and lattice QCD calculations.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the different lower transverse momentum cuts used by STAR (0.2 GeV) and PHENIX (0.3 GeV) account for more than 30% of the observed difference in the mean-over-variance ratio of net-electric charge fluctuations between the two experiments, and that the reduction of the normalized kurtosis with increasing lower momentum cut leads to differences of about 6% that are currently buried under large statistical and systematic errors.

## Method

The authors calculated cumulants of net-electric charge fluctuations in an ideal pion gas with Bose statistics, which they treat as a multi-component gas of Boltzmann particles with different charges and masses, using a cluster expansion that makes the influence of quantum statistics transparent. They extended the analysis to a hadron resonance gas model including all known charged hadrons and resonances, and also implemented an expanding source model with longitudinal boost invariance and transverse flow parameters fitted to STAR pion spectra to compare the effects of kinematic cuts imposed at chemical versus thermal freeze-out.

## Key numbers

- The different lower momentum cuts of 0.2 GeV (STAR) and 0.3 GeV (PHENIX) are responsible for more than 30% of the difference between the two data sets for the mean-over-variance ratio.
- The reduction of the normalized kurtosis with increasing lower momentum cut leads to differences between PHENIX and STAR data of about 6% at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV.
- The ratio of PHENIX to STAR mean-over-variance data is 1.35(5) over the entire energy range.
- For a pion gas at T=160 MeV, the normalized kurtosis decreases toward unity as the lower momentum cut increases from 0 to 0.3 GeV.
- The correspondence between finite volume and momentum cut-off effects is given by p_t_min = 2 pi k / L with k=0.707 for pseudo-rapidity acceptance |Delta eta| = 0.35.

## Context

This work extends the study of kinematic cut effects on charge fluctuations in a hadron resonance gas model previously carried out by Garg et al. (2013), and provides a theoretical interpretation based on the suppression of multi-charged states from Bose statistics in a pion gas. The results enable quantitative comparison between experimental data from the STAR and PHENIX Collaborations and lattice QCD calculations by establishing a relation between momentum cut-off and finite volume effects, suggesting that infinite volume extrapolation may not be needed due to the similarity between finite volume and non-zero transverse momentum cut-off effects.
