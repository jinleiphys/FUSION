# Uncertainty band evaluation of optical potentials and differential cross-sections. Application to $^8$Li + $^{58}$Ni elastic scattering

> A statistical method is presented to evaluate the uncertainty bands in the optical nucleus-nucleus potential and in differential cross sections. The starting point is the least square fit of a set of experimental values of elastic differential cross sections, varying the relevant optical potential parameters. This is done using standard $χ^2$ minimization codes, that provide the covariance matrix of the parameters. A maximum likelihood exploration of the $χ^2$ surface in parameter space allows to determine the covariance matrix of the parameters associated to a contour of a given $χ^2$ value. Bayes theorem allows to assign probabilities (p-values) to the regions in parameter space, characterized by $χ^2$ contours. The method allows to obtain uncertainty bands of an arbitrary observables associated to a given p-value using two approaches. The general approach determines the extremes of the observables calculated in the region of parameter space associated to that p-value. This requires an adequate sampling of parameter space, and explicit calculations of the observables on all sampling points. The simplified approach considers uncertainty propagation of the observable in terms of the optical model parameters. This involves the least-square covariance matrix, given by $χ^2$ minimization codes, and analytically calculated enhancement factors for each p-value. The method, in the general and simplified approaches, is applied to recent measurements of the elastic differential cross sections of $^8$Li + $^{58}$Ni. $1σ$ and $2σ$ uncertainty bands are obtained for the optical potentials as a function of the distance, and the differential cross sections as a function of the angle. The general and simplified approaches are very similar in this case. The application of the procedure to determine uncertainty bands of complex scattering calculations is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper presents a statistical method to evaluate uncertainty bands for optical potentials and differential cross sections that bridges the gap between standard least-square fitting (which underestimates uncertainties) and fully Bayesian approaches (which are computationally intensive). The method uses Bayes theorem to assign p-values to regions of parameter space defined by chi-squared contours, enabling uncertainty quantification at specified statistical significance levels (e.g., 1σ, 2σ) without requiring Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling.

## Method

The authors start with a least-square fit of elastic differential cross section data using standard chi-squared minimization codes to obtain the best-fit parameters and their covariance matrix from the Hessian. They then perform a maximum likelihood exploration of the chi-squared surface in parameter space to determine covariance matrices associated with contours of given chi-squared values, and apply Bayes theorem to assign p-values to these contours. Two approaches are used to obtain uncertainty bands for observables: a general approach that samples the parameter space and calculates observables at all sampling points to find extremes, and a simplified approach that uses uncertainty propagation from the least-square covariance matrix with analytically calculated enhancement factors for each p-value.

## Context

This work extends the uncertainty quantification framework pioneered by Nunes and collaborators (2017, 2019), who compared frequentist and Bayesian approaches for nucleon-nucleus systems using Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods. The present paper applies a related but distinct procedure to a heavy-ion system (⁸Li + ⁵⁸Ni) where the Igo ambiguity (Igo, 1959) allows fixing the reduced radii and fitting only four parameters (V, a_V, W, a_W). The method enables systematic uncertainty band evaluation for optical potentials and differential cross sections at specified confidence levels, which can be applied to more complex scattering calculations such as DWBA, coupled channels, or CDCC.
