# Pairing and shell evolution in neutron rich nuclei

> From the experimental data on odd-even staggering of masses, we have shown that variation of pairing as a function of neutron number plays an important role in many distinctive features like occurrence of new shell closures, shell erosion, anomalous reduction of the energy of the first 2+ state and slower increase in the B(E2, 2_{+}^{1} \rightarrow 0_{+}^{1}) in the neutron-rich even-even nuclei in different mass regions. New predictions have been made in a model independent way. Results of theoretical calculations support the phenomenological findings.

# Pairing and shell evolution in neutron rich nuclei

## Key claim
The authors show that variation of pairing as a function of neutron number, specifically, weakening of neutron pairing combined with relative enhancement of proton pairing, plays a crucial role in shell erosion, new shell closures, and anomalous reduction of first 2+ state energies in neutron-rich even-even nuclei, based on empirical odd-even staggering of masses rather than purely theoretical predictions.

## Method
The authors extracted empirical neutron and proton pairing gaps from experimental binding energies using the odd-even staggering formula for nuclei across multiple mass regions (Be, C, O, Ne, Mg, Si, S, Ar, Sn, Te, Xe, Ba, Pb). They correlated these pairing gaps with measured E(2+1) energies and B(E2) values, and supported their phenomenological findings with Shell Model calculations (using Bonn A interaction with reduced pairing matrix elements) and Cranked Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (CHFB) calculations with a pairing-plus-quadrupole Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers
- In Be isotopes at N=8, the single-particle energy difference nu(1p1/2-1d5/2) is approximately 1.8 MeV in Be compared to approximately 3.9 MeV in corresponding C isotopes, providing evidence of shell erosion.
- In Be isotopes at N=10, the neutron pairing gap Delta_n is approximately 0.8 MeV.
- In O and C isotopes at N=14, the single-particle energy difference nu(1d5/2-2s1/2) is approximately 2.7 MeV and 1.3 MeV respectively, with Delta_n approximately 1.1 MeV and 1.0 MeV respectively.
- In O-24, the single-particle energy difference nu(2s1/2-1d3/2) is approximately 3.9 MeV compared to Delta_n approximately 0.6 MeV.
- For Sn-134 at N=84, E(2+1) decreases to 726 keV, correlated with reduction in Delta_n from approximately 1.2 MeV at N=81 to 0.6 MeV at N=83.
- For Pb isotopes above N=126, Delta_n values are approximately 0.68, 0.32, 0.62 MeV at N=123, 125, 127 respectively, with E(2+1) values of 0.899, 0.803, 0.800 MeV for N=122, 124, 128 respectively.
- At N=82, E(2+1) for Te, Xe, Ba is 1.279, 1.313, 1.435 MeV respectively, approximately 2 times Delta_p (0.66, 0.82, 0.87 MeV respectively).
- Beyond N=82, Delta_n at N=84 for Te, Xe, Ba is approximately 0.72, 0.83, 0.84 MeV respectively, with depressed E(2+1) of 0.606, 0.589, 0.602 MeV respectively.
- CHFB calculations for Xe-124 show that the same depressed E(2+1) (approximately 120-150 keV) can result either from reduced pairing (Delta_n approximately 0) with smaller deformation (beta2 approximately 0.26) or from enhanced deformation (beta2 approximately 0.37) with stronger pairing (Delta_n approximately 0.6 MeV).

## Context
This work extends the empirical approach of Bertsch (using odd-even staggering of binding energies) to systematically correlate pairing variations with shell evolution across multiple mass regions, building on observations of N=20 shell erosion in Ne and Mg isotopes (Sorlin) and the anomalous reduction of E(2+1) and B(E2) in Te-136 (Terasaki). The findings support earlier empirical interactions (Epja) that included reduced pairing matrix elements for neutron-rich isotopes above Sn-132 and in the sd-fp shell (Signoracci, Poves). The work enables model-independent predictions of new shell closures and shell erosion in neutron-rich nuclei, and warrants more precise mass measurements away from stability.
