# Optical model potentials for deuteron scattering off $^{24}$Mg, $^{28}$Si, $^{58}$Ni, $^{90}$Zr, $^{116}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb at $\sim$100 MeV/nucleon

> Angular distributions of the elastic and inelastic deuteron-nucleus scattering off $^{24}$Mg, $^{28}$Si, $^{58}$Ni, $^{90}$Zr, $^{116}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb have been measured at a beam energy of 98 MeV/nucleon, with the goal of constraining the deuteron optical potential in this kinematical regime, and to extract the reduced transition probabilities for the ground-state transitions to low-lying excited states of these nuclei. Two potential models were used in the analysis of the measured $(d,d)$ and $(d,d')$ data within the optical model and the distorted-wave Born approximation: the phenomenological optical model potential associated with the collective model of nuclear scattering, and the semi-microscopic double-folding model of the deuteron-nucleus potential based on a realistic density-dependent M3Y interaction. The deuteron optical potential and inelastic $(d,d')$ scattering form factors were calculated using these two potential models, allowing for a direct comparison between the potential models as well as the validation of the deduced $Eλ$ transition rates.

## Key claim
The paper reports that the semi-microscopic double-folding model (DFM) based on a density-dependent M3Y interaction provides a more reliable description of inelastic deuteron scattering for light nuclei than the phenomenological Woods-Saxon optical model potential, which requires a 40% lower deformation parameter to reproduce the data for $^{24}$Mg and $^{28}$Si.

## Method
Elastic and inelastic deuteron scattering angular distributions were measured at 98 MeV/nucleon (196 MeV beam energy) for six targets spanning light to heavy masses. The data were analyzed within the optical model and distorted-wave Born approximation using two approaches: a phenomenological Woods-Saxon optical model potential with collective model form factors, and a semi-microscopic double-folding model potential based on the CDM3Y6 density-dependent interaction with a localized exchange potential and an added Woods-Saxon surface term to account for deuteron breakup effects.

## Key numbers
- Beam energy: 98 MeV/nucleon (196 MeV deuteron beam)
- Angular range measured: $\theta_{\rm lab}\sim 3.5^\circ - 32^\circ$ with experimental angular resolution of $\sim 0.15^\circ$
- For $^{24}$Mg 2$^+_1$ state: $B(E2)_{\rm adopted}=0.0432\ e^2$b$^2$, $B(E2)_{\rm DOP}=0.0284\ e^2$b$^2$ (40% lower), $B(E2)_{\rm DFM}=0.0410\ e^2$b$^2$
- For $^{28}$Si 2$^+_1$ state: $B(E2)_{\rm adopted}=0.0326\ e^2$b$^2$, $B(E2)_{\rm DOP}=0.0196\ e^2$b$^2$, $B(E2)_{\rm DFM}=0.0326\ e^2$b$^2$
- For $^{58}$Ni 2$^+_1$ state: $B(E2)_{\rm adopted}=B(E2)_{\rm DOP}=B(E2)_{\rm DFM}=0.0695\ e^2$b$^2$
- For $^{90}$Zr 2$^+_1$ state: $B(E2)_{\rm adopted}=0.061\ e^2$b$^2$, $B(E2)_{\rm DOP}=0.0641\ e^2$b$^2$
- For $^{90}$Zr 3$^-_1$ state: $B(E3)_{\rm adopted}=0.098\ e^2$b$^3$ (from $(e,e')$), $B(E3)_{\rm DOP}=0.0510\ e^2$b$^3$
- For $^{116}$Sn 2$^+_1$ state: $B(E2)_{\rm adopted}=0.209\ e^2$b$^2$, $B(E2)_{\rm DOP}=0.241\ e^2$b$^2$
- For $^{208}$Pb 3$^-_1$ state: $B(E3)_{\rm adopted}=B(E3)_{\rm DOP}=0.611\ e^2$b$^3$
- Best-fit renormalization coefficients for folded potential: $N_V=0.94-0.98$, $N_W=1.00-1.09$ across all targets

## Context
This work extends the limited deuteron optical potential data above 90 MeV, where the only global potentials were from Daehnick et al. (1980) and Bojowald et al. (1988) covering energies up to 90 MeV. It validates the use of deuteron inelastic scattering for giant resonance studies with radioactive ion beams, following earlier work by Patel et al. (2013) that established agreement between $(d,d')$ and $(\alpha,\alpha')$ results for $^{116}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb. The double-folding model calculation updates the only prior DFM calculation for deuteron-nucleus potentials by Cook (1982) by using a density-dependent interaction. The results enable reliable extraction of transition probabilities from deuteron scattering data for unstable nuclei studied in inverse kinematics with active-target time projection chambers.
