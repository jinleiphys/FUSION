# The Pair-Instability Mass Gap for Black Holes

> Stellar evolution theory predicts a "gap" in the black hole birth function caused by the pair instability. Presupernova stars that have a core mass below some limiting value, Mlo, after all pulsational activity is finished, collapse to black holes, whereas more massive ones, up to some limiting value, Mhi, explode, promptly and completely, as pair-instability supernovae. Previous work has suggested Mlo is approximately 50 solar masses and Mhi is approximately 130 solar masses. These calculations have been challenged by recent LIGO observations that show many black holes merging with individual masses, Mlo is least some 65 solar masses. Here we explore four factors affecting the theoretical estimates for the boundaries of this mass gap: nuclear reaction rates, evolution in detached binaries, rotation, and hyper-Eddington accretion after black hole birth. Current uncertainties in reaction rates by themselves allow Mlo to rise to 64 solar masses and Mhi as large as 161 solar masses. Rapid rotation could further increase Mlo to about 70 solar masses, depending on the treatment of magnetic torques. Evolution in detached binaries and super-Eddington accretion can, with great uncertainty, increase Mlo still further. Dimensionless Kerr parameters close to unity are allowed for the more massive black holes produced in close binaries, though they are generally smaller.

# The Pair-Instability Mass Gap for Black Holes

## Key claim

The boundaries of the pair-instability mass gap for black holes are significantly more uncertain than previously claimed, with the lower boundary Mlo potentially rising from approximately 50 solar masses to as high as 64 solar masses from nuclear reaction rate uncertainties alone, and up to approximately 70 solar masses with rapid rotation, though these higher values depend on currently unfavored combinations of reaction rates that may conflict with nucleosynthetic constraints.

## Method

The authors used the KEPLER stellar evolution code to model non-rotating and rotating helium stars of constant mass, varying the rates for the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction (using S-factor multipliers from 0.75 to 1.4 times the Buchmann 1996 rate) and the triple-alpha reaction (using multipliers up to 1.35 times the Caughlan 1988 rate). They also modeled evolution in detached binaries with mass loss, rotation with centrifugal force added to structure equations, and hyper-Eddington accretion after black hole birth, tracking the final black hole remnant mass and explosion kinetic energy for each combination.

## Key numbers

- Previous work suggested Mlo approximately 50 solar masses and Mhi approximately 130 solar masses; current uncertainties in reaction rates alone allow Mlo to rise to 64 solar masses and Mhi as large as 161 solar masses
- For the standard 12C(alpha,gamma)16O rate multiplier f_Buch = 1.2, the maximum black hole mass Mlo was 46.0 solar masses (for a 58 solar mass helium star)
- For f_Buch = 0.8, Mlo increased to 56.5 solar masses (for a 58 solar mass helium star)
- For the most extreme combination f_Buch = 0.75 and f_3alpha = 1.35, Mlo reached 64.1 solar masses (for a 68 solar mass helium star)
- Rapid rotation could further increase Mlo to approximately 70 solar masses, depending on the treatment of magnetic torques
- The upper boundary Mhi ranged from 136 solar masses (for f_Buch = 1.4) to 161 solar masses (for f_Buch = 0.75 and f_3alpha = 1.35)
- The 12C(alpha,gamma)16O S-factor at 300 keV has a standard value of 146 keV b, with a considered range from 110 to 205 keV b
- The new measurement of the triple-alpha radiative width gives 5.1(6) meV, an upward revision of about 35 percent from the previous standard of 3.78 meV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous calculations (Woosley 2017, 2019) and responds to challenges from LIGO observations (Abbott 2020a, 2020b, 2020c) that found black holes merging with individual masses near 85 solar masses and in the 60 to 70 solar mass range. The paper builds on recent studies by Farmer (2020), Costa (2020), Marchant (2020), and van Son (2020) that independently examined the sensitivity of the mass gap boundaries to reaction rates, rotation, and accretion, but uses a single code (KEPLER) to evaluate which physical effects are dominant. The results enable future gravitational-wave observations to constrain nuclear reaction rates and binary evolution physics by comparing observed black hole masses against the revised theoretical boundaries.
