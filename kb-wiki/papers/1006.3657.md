# Equivalence of volume and temperature fluctuations in power-law ensembles

> Relativistic particle production often requires the use of Tsallis statistics to account for the apparently power-like behavior of transverse momenta observed in the data even at a few GeV/c. In such an approach this behavior is attributed to some specific intrinsic fluctuations of the temperature $T$ in the hadronizing system and is fully accounted by the nonextensivity parameter $q$. On the other hand, it was recently shown that similar power-law spectra can also be obtained by introducing some specific volume fluctuations, apparently without invoking the introduction of Tsallis statistics. We demonstrate that, in fact, when the total energy is kept constant, these volume fluctuations are equivalent to temperature fluctuations and can be derived from them. In addition, we show that fluctuations leading to multiparticle power-law Tsallis distributions introduce specific correlations between the considered particles. We then propose a possible way to distinguish the fluctuations in each event from those occurring from event-to-event. This could have applications in the analysis of high density events at LHC (and especially in ALICE).

## Key claim

Under the condition of constant total energy, fluctuations of volume and fluctuations of temperature in hadronizing systems are mathematically equivalent, meaning that one can derive the Tsallis distribution from volume fluctuations without invoking Tsallis statistics, and conversely derive volume fluctuations from temperature fluctuations. The authors also claim that the specific correlations introduced by multiparticle Tsallis distributions allow a way to distinguish fluctuations occurring within a single event from those occurring from event to event.

## Method

The authors first derive the distribution of inverse temperature \(g(1/T)\) from a Fokker-Planck equation describing a nonhomogeneous heat bath, obtaining a gamma distribution. They then show that, for constant total energy where \(E \sim VT^4\), the KNO scaling form of the multiplicity distribution assumed for volume fluctuations in Vfluct (2010) leads to the same gamma distribution for \(1/T\) as derived from the diffusion model. Conversely, starting from the temperature fluctuation approach, they show that the resulting multiplicity distribution is a negative binomial distribution, which for large multiplicities reduces to the same KNO scaling form assumed for volume fluctuations. They also derive the two-particle correlation function and the distance distribution for Tsallis-distributed variables.

## Key numbers

- The relation between temperature and volume fluctuations is given by \(T = \langle T\rangle (\langle V \rangle / V)^{1/4}\).
- The parameter \(k\) in the negative binomial distribution is related to the nonextensivity parameter by \(k = 1/(q-1)\).
- The covariance of energies for two particles in the Tsallis distribution is \(Cov(E_i, E_j) = \lambda^2 (q-1) / [(3-2q)^2 (4-3q)]\).
- The correlation coefficient for energies is \(\rho = 1/(2-q) - 1\).
- STAR data show \(Var(\bar{p_T}) / \langle \bar{p_T} \rangle^2 \cong 10^{-4}\), leading to \(q-1 \cong 10^{-4}\) for event-to-event fluctuations, while transverse momentum distributions give \(q-1 \cong 10^{-2}\).
- For temperature fluctuations within an event with \(N_P \cong 100\) projectile participants, \(Var(T)/\langle T \rangle^2 \cong 10^{-2}\), comparable to values from transverse momentum distributions.

## Context

This work extends the temperature fluctuation approach of Wilk and Wlodarczyk (2000) and the volume fluctuation approach of Vfluct (2010) by demonstrating their equivalence under constant total energy. It also builds on the connection between the nonextensivity parameter \(q\) and the negative binomial distribution parameter \(k\) first noted in fits to \(p\bar{p}\) data. The proposed method for distinguishing intra-event from event-to-event fluctuations using the distance distribution could be applied to high-multiplicity events at LHC, particularly in the ALICE experiment, and the authors note that existing RHIC data from STAR (2005) and PHENIX (2002) may already provide relevant information.
