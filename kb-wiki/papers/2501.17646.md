# Electric dipole response of sd-shell nuclei within the Configuration-Interaction Shell Model approach

> Reliable theoretical predictions of nuclear dipole excitations are crucial for various nuclear applications, particularly in nuclear astrophysics. Calculations of radiative capture cross sections often rely on theoretical gamma strength functions, with the electric dipole response being the dominant component. We aim at a systematic description of the E1 strength of nuclei with mass numbers between 17 and 40 of interest for various applications and to better understand the nature of the low-energy dipole strength in neutron-rich nuclei, known as pygmy dipole resonances. We use the Configuration Interaction shell-model framework in the p-sd-pf valence space with a previously established empirical Hamiltonian. Systematic results of photoabsorption strength show good agreement with experimental data, provided a renormalization of the dipole operator is applied. Transition densities are computed in 26Ne and confirm the pure isovector character of the Giant Dipole Resonance. The strength at 7-10MeV is shown to have a distinct structure with largely fragmented wave functions and transition densities of isovector character at the edge of the nucleus. The Configuration Interaction shell model is proved to be a valuable tool in the description of the photoresponse of light nuclei, providing more accurate results than the usually employed approaches.

## Key claim

The Configuration-Interaction Shell Model (CI-SM) provides a systematic description of the electric dipole response for nuclei with mass numbers 17 to 40, but only when a renormalization factor of 0.64 is applied to the dipole operator to account for correlations outside the model space; without this renormalization, the calculations systematically overestimate experimental photoabsorption cross sections.

## Method

The authors used the CI-SM framework in a full 1-hbar-omega p-sd-pf valence space with the semi-empirical PSDPF Hamiltonian, performing Lanczos strength function calculations with 300 iterations to obtain E1 strength distributions. They computed transition densities in 26Ne to characterize the nature of different resonances, and extracted effective charges for the dipole operator by comparing theoretical energy-weighted sum rules to experimental photoabsorption data and the Thomas-Reich-Kuhn (TRK) sum rule.

## Key numbers

* The adopted renormalization factor Q^2 = 0.64 was derived from four available datasets for 27Al, corresponding to effective charges of 0.8 N/A for protons and -0.8 Z/A for neutrons
* The rms deviation for 25 centroids of photoabsorption strength distributions is 0.84 MeV, improving to 0.72 MeV when excluding nuclei at the extremes of the valence space
* The rms deviation for the width of strength distributions is 0.56 MeV, improving to 0.17 MeV when excluding O, F, and Ca nuclei
* The enhancement factor K for 27Al is 0.14, consistent with the expectation of small K for nuclei with mass A less than 50
* The total B(E1) strength for 26Mg below 10.1 MeV is 200 x 10^{-4} e^2fm^2 in CI-SM, compared to the experimental value of 79.9(66) x 10^{-4} e^2fm^2
* The reduction of total strength when going from 1-hbar-omega to 3-hbar-omega model spaces ranges from 22% to 1%, decreasing with mass number

## Context

This work extends previous CI-SM studies of E1 response in Ne isotopes (Sieja, 2022) to a systematic survey of sd-shell nuclei, and provides more accurate results than quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) calculations from Goriely (2018), which systematically underestimate centroid positions (rms = 1.3 MeV). The results support the interpretation of pygmy dipole strength as an oscillation of the neutron skin against the proton-neutron core, and the systematic renormalization factor enables reliable predictions for the PANDORA project's measurements of photon strength functions in light nuclei.
