# MuDirac 1.3.0: A Sustainable Software Tool for Calculating Ground State Nuclear Properties Using Muonic X-Ray Measurements

> The nuclear charge radius is one of the most fundamental quantities of the atomic nucleus. It can be deduced from a combination of experimental measurements of muonicX-raytransitionenergieswithmodellingofthoseX-raytransitionenergies. In thisworkwepresentMuDirac (1.3.0), whichisanopen, publiclyavailable, sustainable and computationally efficient software tool that will be at put the disposal of the negative muon community. With MuDirac (1.3.0), the community will be able to accurately and efficiently estimate nuclear properties, such as the nuclear charge radius, by assuming a 2-parameter Fermi distribution of the nuclear charge.

## Key claim

MuDirac 1.3.0 can reverse-engineer the Dirac equation to estimate the 2-parameter Fermi (2pF) model parameters \(c\) and \(t\) from at least two experimentally measured muonic X-ray transition energies, but this method is only valid for atoms with atomic number \(Z \geq 5\); for \(Z < 5\) the exponential term in the mean-square-radius expression cannot be neglected and an integral method is required, which is still under development.

## Method

The software solves the radial Dirac equation for a spherically symmetric nuclear charge distribution described by a 2-parameter Fermi (2pF) model, using experimental muonic X-ray transition energies as input. The optimal 2pF parameters \(c^*\) and \(t^*\) are found by minimising the chi-squared difference between simulated and experimental transition energies via a Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm implemented in the Ceres solver, operating in a polar coordinate system \((R_{\text{rms}}, \theta)\) that parametrises the elliptical relationship between \(c\) and \(t\). From the optimised \(c^*\) and \(t^*\), the root-mean-square nuclear charge radius \(R_{\text{rms}}\) is computed using the analytical expression \(R_{\text{rms}}^2 = \frac{3}{5}(c^2 + a t^2)\) with \(a = 7\pi^2 / [48 \ln(3)^2]\).

## Key numbers

* For \(^{206}\text{Pb}\), the chi-squared minimiser was found at \(R_{\text{rms}} = 5.487\) fm and \(\theta = 0.324\) radians, with \(\chi^2_{\min} = 0.48\), using four muonic X-ray transitions (K1-L2, K1-L3, L2-M4, L3-M5).
* For \(^{197}\text{Au}\), the optimised 2pF parameters were \(c = 6.6744\) fm, \(t = 1.9265\) fm, yielding \(R_{\text{rms}} = 5.4207\) fm with \(\chi^2 = 0.1560\) and a runtime of 59.1980 seconds.
* The best optimisation runtime across 24 muonic atom datasets was on the order of one minute, compared to hours required for high-resolution brute-force scanning.
* The method applies to atoms with \(Z \geq 5\); for \(Z < 5\) the approximation \(R_{\text{rms}}^2 \approx R^2\) fails because \(\exp(-\kappa) \approx 10^{-5}\) is no longer negligible.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Engfer (1974), who fixed \(t = 2.3\) fm and fitted only \(c\), and the brute-force scanning method recently applied by Saito (2025). MuDirac 1.3.0 provides an open-source, computationally efficient alternative that simultaneously optimises both \(c\) and \(t\) using a Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm in polar coordinates, reducing runtime from hours to minutes. The tool enables the negative muon community to extract nuclear charge radii and charge distributions from muonic X-ray measurements for a wide range of stable and unstable nuclei, complementing tabulated databases such as those from Marinova (2013) and recent measurements on unstable nuclei by Suda (2017).
