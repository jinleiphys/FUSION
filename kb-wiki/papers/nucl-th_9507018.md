# Quantum Chaos, Degeneracies and Exceptional Points

> It is argued that, if a regular Hamiltonian is perturbed by a term that produces chaos, the onset of chaos is shifted towards larger values of the perturbation parameter if the unperturbed spectrum is degenerate and the lifting of the degeneracy is of second order in this parameter. The argument is based on the behaviour of the exceptional points of the full problem.

# Quantum Chaos, Degeneracies and Exceptional Points

## Key claim
If a regular Hamiltonian is perturbed by a term that produces chaos, the onset of chaos is shifted towards larger values of the perturbation parameter when the unperturbed spectrum is degenerate and the lifting of the degeneracy is of second order in that parameter; this suppression occurs because exceptional points are trapped at the origin when the leading perturbative term is quadratic rather than linear.

## Method
The authors analyze the spectrum of Hamiltonians of the form \(H^0 + \lambda H^1\) by studying the distribution of exceptional points (branch points in the complex \(\lambda\)-plane where two energy levels coalesce). For each \(m\)-fold degeneracy in \(H^0\), they examine the lowest-order term in the resultant polynomial: linear lifting yields a factor \(\lambda^{m(m-1)}\), while quadratic lifting yields \(\lambda^{2m(m-1)}\), meaning more exceptional points are trapped at \(\lambda=0\) in the quadratic case. They illustrate the effect with a 9-dimensional matrix model and a large (\(N=304\)) random-matrix model, then apply the method to a physical single-particle potential with quadrupole and octupole deformations using \(544 \times 544\) matrices.

## Key numbers
- In the 9-dimensional model with three-fold degeneracies, the number of exceptional points in each half-plane reduces from 36 to 18 when the linear term vanishes; switching on the parameter \(\mu\) adds 9 additional exceptional points emerging from \(\lambda=0\) in each half-plane.
- In the large random-matrix model with \(n_{\text{deg}}=8\) and \(N=304\), 2128 exceptional points emerge from the origin when linear terms are present.
- For the physical potential, the critical value \(\lambda_c\) marks where the potential no longer binds; for \(b=2\) (prolate), the bulk of exceptional points occur at \(\lambda\) values outside the physical range (\(\lambda/\lambda_c \ge 1\)), while for \(b=1/2\) (oblate), the maximum density of exceptional points appears as soon as \(\lambda\) is switched on.
- The intersection point where perturbation breaks down scales as \(\lambda_{\text{intsct}} \propto 1/n_{\text{deg}}\) for the linear case and \(\lambda_{\text{intsct}} \propto 1/\sqrt{n_{\text{deg}}}\) for the quadratic case.

## Context
This work extends the method of using exceptional points to characterize quantum chaos, previously developed by Heiss and collaborators (1994) and applied to the hydrogen atom in a strong magnetic field by Korsch and Heiss (1994). It explains the puzzling difference between prolate and oblate quadrupole-plus-octupole potentials studied by Bauer and Heiss (1992) and Heiss and Nazmitdinov (1994): the prolate case (\(b \ge 2\)) shows suppressed chaos and even new shell structures, while the oblate case (\(b < 1\)) quickly becomes chaotic. The finding enables prediction of level statistics directly from the matrix structure of \(H^0\) and \(H^1\) without solving the full eigenvalue problem, and suggests that finer details such as shell structure might be extractable from exceptional-point distributions.
