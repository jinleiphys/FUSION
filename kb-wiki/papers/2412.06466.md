# Jet momentum reconstruction in the QGP background with machine learning

> We apply a Dense Neural Network (DNN) approach to reconstruct jet momentum within a quark-gluon plasma (QGP) background, using simulated data from PYTHIA and Linear Boltzmann Transport (LBT) Models for comparative analysis. We find that medium response particles from the LBT simulation, scattered out of the QGP background but belonging to medium-modified jets, lead to oversubtraction of the background if the DNN model is trained on vacuum jets from PYTHIA simulation. By training the DNN model on quenched jets generated using LBT or the combination of jet samples from PYTHIA and LBT, we significantly reduce this prediction bias and achieve more accurate background subtraction compared to conventional Area-based and Constituent Subtraction methods widely adopted in experimental measurements. We further study the performance of these machine learning models on evaluating the nuclear modification factor of jets, and find that while the unfolding procedure is necessary for correcting residuals in reconstructed jet momenta, models trained on samples incorporating quenched jets still achieve superior accuracy than those trained on vacuum jets even after unfolding.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a Dense Neural Network (DNN) trained on vacuum jets from PYTHIA systematically underestimates jet transverse momentum in heavy-ion collisions because it misidentifies medium response particles (recoil partons from the QGP) as background, leading to oversubtraction; this bias persists even after unfolding and can only be corrected by training on quenched jets from the Linear Boltzmann Transport (LBT) model that include medium response.

## Method

The authors embed jet partons from PYTHIA (vacuum) or PYTHIA+LBT (quenched) simulations into a thermal toy model background mimicking central Pb+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV, then train Dense Neural Networks with three hidden layers (100, 100, 50 nodes) on jet substructure observables to predict the true jet transverse momentum. They compare the ML predictions to conventional Area-based and Constituent Subtraction methods, and evaluate the nuclear modification factor using iterative Bayesian unfolding with four iterations.

## Key numbers

- Thermal background: total multiplicity of 1538 charged pions with average transverse momentum ⟨pT⟩ = 0.5696 GeV per unit rapidity within |y| < 1, mimicking central (0-10%) Pb+Pb collisions at √sNN = 5.02 TeV
- Jet selection: transverse momentum range 5 GeV < pT < 160 GeV, pseudorapidity |η| < 1 - R, with anti-kT algorithm radius R = 0.4
- ML(PYTHIA) tested on PYTHIA: mean residual -0.5 GeV, σ = 2.0 GeV
- ML(PYTHIA) tested on LBT: mean residual -4.8 GeV, σ = 5.9 GeV
- ML(LBT) tested on LBT: mean residual -0.9 GeV, σ = 4.5 GeV
- ML(PYTHIA+LBT) tested on LBT: mean residual -2.0 GeV, σ = 4.8 GeV
- Area-based method tested on LBT: mean residual -3.9 GeV, σ = 9.0 GeV
- Constituent Subtraction method tested on LBT: mean residual 0.2 GeV, σ = 8.4 GeV
- ML(LBT) tested on PYTHIA: mean residual 2.9 GeV, σ = 3.0 GeV
- ML(PYTHIA+LBT) tested on PYTHIA: mean residual 0.4 GeV, σ = 2.2 GeV

## Context

This work extends the ML-based jet momentum reconstruction approach of Haake (2019), which trained on PYTHIA jets in thermal backgrounds and was applied to ALICE data by Acharya (2024). The authors show that training on vacuum jets alone introduces systematic bias from medium response particles, which cannot be corrected by unfolding. By incorporating the LBT model of Cao (2016) and Luo (2023) that includes recoil and negative partons, the method achieves superior accuracy compared to conventional Area-based (Cacciari, 2011) and Constituent Subtraction (Berta, 2019) methods. This enables more reliable extraction of the nuclear modification factor for jets in heavy-ion collisions and suggests training on multiple jet quenching models for robustness.
