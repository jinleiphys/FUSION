# A Continuous Galactic Line Source of Axions: The Remarkable Case of 23Na

> We argue that $^{23}$Na is a potentially significant source of galactic axions. For temperatures $\gtrsim 7 \times 10^8$K -- characteristic of carbon burning in the massive progenitors of supernovae and ONeMg white dwarfs -- the 440 keV first excited state of $^{23}$Na is thermally populated, with its repeated decays pumping stellar energy into escaping axions. Odd-A nuclear abundances are typically very low in high-temperature stellar environments (or absent entirely due to burn-up). $^{23}$Na is an exception: $\approx 0.1 M_\odot$ of the isotope is synthesized during carbon burning then maintained at $\approx 10^9$K for times ranging up to $6 \times 10^4$y. Using MESA simulations, a galactic model, and sampling over progenitor masses, locations, and evolutionary stages, we find a continuous flux at earth of $\langle ϕ_a \rangle \approx 22$/cm$^2$s for $g^\mathrm{eff}_{aNN} = 10^{-9}$. Some fraction of these axions convert to photons as they propagate through the galactic magnetic field, producing a distinctive 440 keV line $γ$ ray detectable by all-sky detectors like the Compton Spectrometer and Imager (COSI). Assuming a 1$μ$G galactic magnetic field and a sufficiently light axion mass, we find that COSI will be able to probe $| g_{aNN}^\mathrm{eff} g_{a γγ} | \gtrsim1.8 \times 10^{-22}$ GeV$^{-1}$ at $3σ$ after two years of surveying.

# Wiki Page: Continuous Galactic Axion Source from 23Na

## Key claim
The paper argues that 23Na is the strongest continuous source of line axions in the galaxy, producing a detectable 440 keV gamma-ray signal through axion-to-photon conversion in the galactic magnetic field. However, this claim is conditional on the axion being sufficiently light (mass less than approximately 0.1 neV) and on the product of couplings |g_aNN^eff * g_aγγ| being at least 1.8 × 10^{-22} GeV^{-1} for detection by COSI at 3σ after two years.

## Method
The authors used MESA stellar evolution code to model progenitors of 7.5-30 solar masses through carbon burning, computing 23Na synthesis and axion emission rates from the thermally populated 440 keV first excited state. They constructed a galactic model with Monte Carlo sampling over progenitor masses (using a Salpeter initial mass function), positions (using a modified Stecker-Jones distribution), and evolutionary stages, adopting a supernova rate of 2.5 per century and an ONeMg white dwarf birth rate of 1 per 175 years. The axion-to-photon conversion probability in the galactic magnetic field was calculated assuming a constant transverse field of 1 μG and an electron density of 1.1 × 10^{-2} cm^{-3}.

## Key numbers
- 23Na 440 keV first excited state: excitation energy 440.2 keV, B(M1) = 0.225 (nearly 30 times stronger than 57Fe)
- 23Na mass synthesized per star: ranges from 0.044 solar masses (9 solar mass progenitor) to 0.13 solar masses (30 solar mass progenitor)
- Carbon burning duration: 12,500 years for 9 solar mass progenitor, 25 years for 30 solar mass progenitor
- Mean axion flux at Earth: ⟨φ_a⟩ ≈ 21.4 cm^{-2}s^{-1} for g_aNN^eff = 10^{-9}
- Mean gamma-ray flux at Earth: ⟨φ_γ⟩ ≈ 3.05 × 10^{-2} cm^{-2}s^{-1} for light axions (m_a = 10^{-11} eV)
- Mean conversion probability: ⟨P_{a→γ}⟩ ≈ 0.0013
- COSI sensitivity: 4 × 10^{-5} cm^{-2}s^{-1} (3σ) at 440 keV after two years of survey time
- Projected COSI reach: |g_aNN^eff * g_aγγ| ≥ 1.8 × 10^{-22} GeV^{-1} for m_a ≲ 0.1 neV
- Nominal coupling product: |g_aNN^eff * g_aγγ| = 5 × 10^{-21} GeV^{-1} (for KSVZ axion with g_app ≈ 10^{-9})
- Gamma line width: approximately 0.9 keV (Gaussian, from thermal broadening)

## Context
This work extends the axion pump mechanism proposed by Haxton and Lee (1991), who identified 23Na as a potential axion emitter at high temperatures but whose observation drew little attention. The paper contrasts with solar 57Fe axion searches by CAST and others, noting that galactic 23Na axions benefit from a conversion advantage of approximately 10^{17} over the 9.26 m, 9 T CAST magnet due to the much longer path length through galactic magnetic fields. The results enable COSI (launch planned 2027) to probe axion couplings beyond current combined limits from M82 axion-photon conversion, magnetic white dwarf polarization measurements (Ning 2024, Benabou 2025), and isolated neutron star cooling (Buschmann 2021).
