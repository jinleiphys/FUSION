# Particle production at energies available at the CERN Large Hadron Collider within evolutionary model

> The particle yields and particle number ratios in Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV are described within the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) at the two different equations of state (EoS) for the quark-gluon matter and the two corresponding hadronization temperatures, $T=165$ MeV and $T=156$ MeV. The role of particle interactions at the final afterburner stage of the collision in the particle production is investigated by means of comparison of the results of full iHKM simulations with those where the annihilation and other inelastic processes (except for resonance decays) are switched off after hadronization/particlization, similarly as in the thermal models. An analysis supports the picture of continuous chemical freeze-out in the sense that the corrections to the sudden chemical freeze-out results, which arise because of the inelastic reactions at the subsequent evolution times, are noticeable and improve the description of particle and number ratios. An important observation is that although the particle number ratios with switched-off inelastic reactions are quite different at different particlization temperatures which are adopted for different equations of state to reproduce experimental data, the complete iHKM calculations bring very close results in both cases.

## Key claim

The integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) can describe particle yields and ratios in Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies equally well using two different equations of state and corresponding hadronization temperatures (165 MeV and 156 MeV), provided the initial energy density is treated as a free parameter; the key hedge is that the satisfactory description depends on adjusting the initial energy density and initial time to reproduce the measured charged particle multiplicity and pion spectrum slope.

## Method

The authors used the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM), which includes five stages: initial state formation at proper time tau_0 ≈ 0.1 fm/c, pre-thermal evolution using relaxation time approximation up to thermalization time tau_th = 1 fm/c, relativistic viscous hydrodynamics with shear viscosity to entropy density ratio η/s = 1/(4π), sharp particlization at a chosen temperature T_p on an isotherm hypersurface constructed via the Cornelius routine, and a final hadronic cascade stage using the UrQMD model. They performed simulations for central (0-10%) Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV using two different equations of state (Laine-Schröder with T_p = 165 MeV and HotQCD with T_p = 156 MeV) and compared full iHKM results with results where inelastic processes (except resonance decays) were switched off after particlization.

## Key numbers

* Initial energy density at tau_0 = 0.1 fm/c was set to epsilon_0 = 680 GeV/fm^3 for the T_p = 165 MeV case and epsilon_0 = 495 GeV/fm^3 for the T_p = 156 MeV case
* For the T_p = 156 MeV case, the initial time was changed to tau_0 = 0.15 fm/c to ensure correct charged particle multiplicity and pion p_T spectrum slope
* The chi-squared values for full iHKM simulations were 0.7 (T_p = 165 MeV) and 2.2 (T_p = 156 MeV), compared to 37.7 and 14.9 respectively when inelastic processes were switched off
* The thermal model chemical freeze-out temperature estimate is T_ch = 156 ± 1.5 MeV (Stachel 2013)
* The relaxation time at the pre-thermal stage was tau_rel = 0.25 fm/c, with momentum anisotropy parameter Lambda = 100

## Context

This work extends the HKM model (the predecessor of iHKM) which previously described RHIC and LHC observables using the Laine-Schröder equation of state at T_p = 165 MeV (Sinyukov 2010, Shapoval 2014). It directly tests the sudden chemical freeze-out assumption used in thermal models (Braun-Munzinger 2010, Stachel 2013) by comparing full iHKM simulations with truncated calculations where inelastic reactions are switched off. The results support a picture of continuous chemical freeze-out at LHC energies, consistent with earlier findings using K*(892) resonances (Sinyukov 2017) that showed kinetic freeze-out is also continuous, lasting about 5 fm/c after particlization.
