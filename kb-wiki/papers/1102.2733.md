# Energy partition in low energy fission

> The intrinsic excitation energy of fission fragments is dynamically evaluated in terms of the time dependent pairing equations. These equations are corroborated with two conditions. One of them fixes the number of particles and the another separates the pairing active spaces associated to the two fragments in the vicinity of the scission configuration. The fission path is obtained in the frame of the macroscopic-microscopic model. The single particle level schemes are obtained within the two center Woods-Saxon shell model. It is shown that the available intrinsic dissipated energy is not shared proportionally to the masses of the two fission fragments. If the heavy fragment possesses nucleon numbers close to the magic ones, the accumulated intrinsic excitation energy is lower than that of the light fragment.

## Key claim

The intrinsic excitation energy of fission fragments is not shared proportionally to their masses; instead, if the heavy fragment has nucleon numbers close to magic ones, its accumulated intrinsic excitation energy is lower than that of the light fragment, as evaluated dynamically using time-dependent pairing equations with constraints to fix particle number in each fragment after scission.

## Method

The fission path for 234U was obtained using the macroscopic-microscopic model with a five-dimensional shape parametrization (elongation, necking, two eccentricities, mass asymmetry), minimizing the action integral. Single-particle level schemes were computed along this path using a two-center Woods-Saxon shell model. The intrinsic excitation energy (dissipation) was evaluated by solving time-dependent pairing equations (time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov) from the ground state to separated fragments, with a Lagrange multiplier constraint to project the average number of particles onto each fragment after the external saddle point.

## Key numbers

- Total dissipation energy reached about 10.5 MeV for an internuclear velocity of 10^6 m/s (the highest velocity tested), with the larger part formed during penetration of the second barrier and descent to scission.
- The time for descent from saddle to scission was about 4 × 10^(-21) s for a velocity of 10^6 m/s.
- The inertia in the ground state was 1.26 ℏ^2/MeV^2/fm^2 and in the asymptotic region of two separated fragments was 1.208 ℏ^2/MeV^2/fm^2.
- The partition analyzed was 102Zr + 132Te, with 15 neutron pairs assigned to each fragment after applying the particle-number constraint.
- Internuclear velocities tested ranged from 10^4 to 10^6 m/s, corresponding to barrier penetration times in the interval [10^(-18), 10^(-20)] s.

## Context

This work extends dynamical treatments of fission energy partition beyond the statistical energy-sorting mechanism proposed by Schmidt et al. (Ref. sch1), which postulated temperature differences between fragments based on maximum entropy. The results contradict the constant-temperature hypothesis of Bethe (Ref. bet) that assumed proportionality between excitation energy division and mass ratio. The method enables dynamical calculation of fragment excitation energies for specific fission partitions, applicable to cold fission, superheavy element formation (as in Ref. epl9), and cluster- or alpha-decay studies (Refs. m1, m2, m3).
