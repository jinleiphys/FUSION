# Spin-orbit final state interaction in the framework of Glauber theory for (e,e'p) reactions

> We investigate the reactions D(e,e'p)n and D(\vec e,e'p)n at GeV energies and discuss the opportunities to distinguish between different models for the nuclear ground state by measuring the response functions. In calculating the final-state interaction (FSI) we employ Glauber theory, and we also include relativistic effects in the electromagnetic current. We include not only the central FSI, but also the spin-orbit FSI which is usually neglected in (e,e'p) calculations within the Glauber framework and we show that this contribution plays a crucial role for the fifth response function. All of the methods developed here can be applied to any target nucleus.

## Key claim

The authors show that while central final-state interactions (FSI) alone are sufficient for calculating cross sections and large response functions in (e,e'p) reactions at GeV energies, the spin-orbit FSI, usually neglected in Glauber theory calculations for these reactions, plays a crucial role for the fifth response function and significantly affects the transverse-transverse interference response, with the caveat that these effects are demonstrated specifically for the deuteron target.

## Method

The authors employ Glauber theory to calculate final-state interactions in the D(e,e'p)n and D(→e,e'p)n reactions at GeV energies, including both central and spin-orbit contributions to the NN scattering amplitude. They use a parameterization of the central amplitude in terms of the total NN cross section, diffraction slope, and ratio of real to imaginary parts, while the spin-orbit amplitude is parameterized with a longer-range profile function. Relativistic effects in the electromagnetic current are included throughout, and calculations are performed for both Bonn and Paris deuteron wave functions to compare model predictions.

## Key numbers

- The kinetic energy T_p of the outgoing proton is fixed to 1 GeV throughout the calculations.
- For a 1 GeV nucleon, the diffraction slope parameter b_o ≈ 0.5 fm for the central amplitude.
- The spin-orbit amplitude parameter γ ≈ 0.16 fm, with b_s = 0.65 fm and ρ_s = -0.24 for the default parameters.
- The total pn cross section used is σ_tot^{pn} = 41.1 mb, with ρ = -0.48 for the central amplitude.
- The central FSI enhances the cross section by up to an order of magnitude for missing momenta higher than 1.5 fm^{-1} in perpendicular kinematics.
- The spin-orbit FSI increases the fifth response function R_{TL'} by a factor of four and more for small missing momenta, and by more than one order of magnitude for higher missing momenta.
- The S-wave contribution to R_{TT} increases by more than 1.5 orders of magnitude in the region from 2 fm^{-1} to 2.5 fm^{-1} when spin-orbit FSI is included.
- The transverse-transverse response R_{TT} accounts for roughly 1% of the total cross section.

## Context

This work extends previous Glauber theory calculations for (e,e'p) reactions that only included central FSI (Jeschonnek 1997, mydeu; mytensor; fs; kys), which was reasonable for cross sections, large responses, and nuclear transparencies. The authors demonstrate that spin-orbit FSI is necessary for smaller interference responses and polarization observables, contradicting the common neglect of such effects. The methods developed are applicable to any target nucleus, and the results can be compared with a recent Dirac eikonal treatment of spin-orbit FSI (iks) for 16O, where effects on the fifth response were found to be small. The work enables experimental discrimination between different nuclear ground-state models (Bonn vs. Paris wave functions) through measurement of interference responses, though the authors note that meson-exchange current effects and experimental feasibility remain open questions.
