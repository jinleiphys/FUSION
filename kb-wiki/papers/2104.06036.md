# Ambiguities in the hadro-chemical freeze-out of Au+Au collisions at SIS18 energies and how to resolve them

> The thermal fit to preliminary HADES data of Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{_{NN}}}=2.4$ GeV shows two degenerate solutions at $T\approx50$ MeV and $T\approx70$ MeV. The analysis of the same particle yields in a transport simulation of the UrQMD model yields the same features, i.e. two distinct temperatures for the chemical freeze-out. While both solutions yield the same number of hadrons after resonance decays, the feeddown contribution is very different for both cases. This highlights that two systems with different chemical composition can yield the same multiplicities after resonance decays. The nature of these two minima is further investigated by studying the time-dependent particle yields and extracted thermodynamic properties of the UrQMD model. It is confirmed, that the evolution of the high temperature solution resembles cooling and expansion of a hot and dense fireball. The low temperature solution displays an unphysical evolution: heating and compression of matter with a decrease of entropy. These results imply that the thermal model analysis of systems produced in low energy nuclear collisions is ambiguous but can be interpreted by taking also the time evolution and resonance contributions into account.

## Key claim

The thermal fit to preliminary HADES data of Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 2.4 GeV shows two degenerate solutions at T ≈ 50 MeV and T ≈ 70 MeV, but only the high-temperature solution (T ≈ 70 MeV) is physically reasonable when the time evolution of the system is considered; the low-temperature solution displays unphysical heating and compression with decreasing entropy.

## Method

The authors performed thermal model fits using an ideal hadron resonance gas (HRG) model within the grand canonical ensemble to both preliminary HADES data and yields from UrQMD transport model simulations of 10% most central Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 2.4 GeV. They analyzed three scenarios: (a) no light nuclei at freeze-out, (b) stable light nuclei included at freeze-out, and (c) stable plus unstable light nuclei included. The time evolution of particle yields in UrQMD was studied by stopping the transport at various times (1-20 fm/c) and fitting the resulting yields with the thermal model to extract thermodynamic parameters.

## Key numbers

- Two degenerate thermal fit minima found at T ≈ 50 MeV and T ≈ 70 MeV for Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 2.4 GeV
- High-temperature minimum parameters: T = 70.3 ± 2.0 MeV, μB = 872.1 ± 24.3 MeV, R = 6.8 ± 0.9 fm, χ²/Ndf = 1.13/2
- Low-temperature minimum parameters: T = 47.2 ± 2.6 MeV, μB = 780.1 ± 3.8 MeV, R = 18.9 ± 2.2 fm, χ²/Ndf = 1.58/2
- Baryon density at high-temperature minimum: nB ≈ 0.22 fm⁻³; at low-temperature minimum: nB ≈ 0.01 fm⁻³
- Entropy per baryon S/A ≈ 3-5 for high-temperature minimum, versus S/A ≈ 7-11 for low-temperature minimum
- Fit including light nuclei at freeze-out gave χ²/Ndf ≈ 20; including unstable nuclei improved to χ²/Ndf ≈ 12

## Context

This work extends the thermal model analysis of Harabasz et al. (2020) on HADES data, which found only the low-temperature solution (T ≈ 50 MeV). The authors demonstrate that a second, degenerate high-temperature solution exists and use UrQMD transport simulations to show that only the high-temperature solution is physically consistent with fireball cooling and expansion. The results contradict the standard chemical freeze-out paradigm at low collision energies and suggest that measurements of unstable resonances such as φ, ρ⁰, K*, and Δ (as proposed by Reichert et al. (2020) and Motornenko et al. (2019)) could resolve the ambiguity. The work enables future partial chemical equilibrium analyses of HADES data to determine the true freeze-out mechanism.
