# External magnetic field induced paramagnetic squeezing effect in heavy-ion collisions at the LHC

> In non-central heavy-ion collisions, the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) encounters the most intense magnetic field ever produced in nature, with a strength of approximately 10$^{19 - 20}$ Gauss. Recent lattice-QCD calculations reveal that the QGP exhibits paramagnetic properties at high temperatures. When an external strong magnetic field is applied, it generates an anisotropic squeezing force density that competes with pressure gradients resulting from the purely QGP geometric expansion. In this study, we employ (3+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamics simulations to estimate the paramagnetic squeezing effect of this force density on the anisotropic expansion of QGP in non-central Pb+Pb collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). We consider both up-to-date magnetic susceptibility and various magnetic field profiles in this work. We find that the impact of rapidly decaying magnetic fields is insignificant, while enduring magnetic fields produce a strong force density that diminishes the momentum anisotropy of the QGP by up to 10% at the intial stage, leaving a visible imprint on the elliptic flow $v_{2}$ of final charged particles. Our results provide insights into the interplay between magnetic fields and the dynamics of QGP expansion in non-central heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paramagnetic squeezing force density induced by an external magnetic field can reduce the momentum anisotropy of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) by up to 10% at the initial stage in non-central Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC, but this effect is only significant for magnetic fields with long lifetimes; rapidly decaying magnetic fields produce an insignificant impact.

## Method

The authors employed (3+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamics simulations using the CLVisc model to evolve the QGP in non-central Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies, incorporating a paramagnetic squeezing force density term derived from the magnetization M = chi B acting on the fluid. The magnetic field was treated as an external degree of freedom with three different time-decay profiles (exponential, power-law, and inverse-linear), using the spatial distribution from the Lienard-Wiechert potential and the most recent lattice QCD magnetic susceptibility parameterization from Bali et al. (2020). Initial energy density was constructed using the optical Glauber model, and the Cooper-Frye formalism was applied at a switching temperature of 137 MeV to obtain final charged hadron spectra and elliptic flow v2.

## Key numbers

* The initial magnetic field strength in non-central Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC is approximately 10^19-20 Gauss.
* The squeezing force density reaches a maximum of about 5.6 GeV/fm^4 along the x direction, compared to pressure gradients peaking at approximately 48 GeV/fm^4, making the squeezing force about 10% of the pressure gradient at initial time.
* Magnetic field Setup-3 (inverse-linear decay) suppresses the elliptic flow v2 by approximately 3-4%, while Setup-2 (power-law decay) produces a 2% suppression effect on v2.
* The magnetic susceptibility parameterization from lattice QCD-2020 shows diamagnetic behavior below 150 MeV, with a cutoff applied at T = 150 MeV to focus on the paramagnetic squeezing effect.
* For Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 10.6 TeV (HL-LHC), the charged particle multiplicity dN_ch/deta at mid-rapidity is approximately 2408 for 0-5% centrality.

## Context

This work extends earlier estimates by Bali et al. (2013) and Pang et al. (2016) of the paramagnetic squeezing effect in heavy-ion collisions, moving from order-of-magnitude comparisons to full (3+1)-dimensional hydrodynamic simulations with updated lattice QCD magnetic susceptibility from Bali et al. (2020). The study uses three magnetic field decay profiles previously employed by Deng et al. (2012), Pang et al. (2016), and Sun et al. (2020) to systematically investigate how magnetic field lifetime affects the observable elliptic flow v2. The results enable future comparisons with (3+1)-dimensional relativistic magnetohydrodynamics simulations, which are still under development, and motivate experimental searches for magnetic field imprints on v2 at LHC energies.
