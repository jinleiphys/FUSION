# Transport coefficients for bulk viscous evolution in the relaxation time approximation

> We derive the form of the viscous corrections to the phase-space distribution function due to the bulk viscous pressure and shear stress tensor using the iterative Chapman-Enskog method. We then calculate the transport coefficients necessary for the second-order hydrodynamic evolution of the bulk viscous pressure and the shear stress tensor. We demonstrate that the transport coefficients obtained using the Chapman-Enskog method are different than those obtained previously using the 14-moment approximation for a finite particle mass. Specializing to the case of boost-invariant and transversally homogeneous longitudinal expansion, we show that the transport coefficients obtained using the Chapman-Enskog method result in better agreement with the exact solution of the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation compared to results obtained in the 14-moment approximation. Finally, we explicitly confirm that the time evolution of the bulk viscous pressure is significantly affected by its coupling to the shear stress tensor.

# Transport coefficients for bulk viscous evolution in the relaxation time approximation

## Key claim

The Chapman-Enskog method yields transport coefficients for second-order bulk viscous evolution that are different from those obtained via the 14-moment approximation for finite particle mass, and these coefficients produce better agreement with the exact solution of the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation for a boost-invariant expanding system, but only for the bulk viscous pressure evolution, the pressure anisotropy is largely insensitive to the choice of method.

## Method

The authors iteratively solved the relativistic Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation using the Chapman-Enskog method to obtain the non-equilibrium phase-space distribution function up to first order in gradients. They then derived the second-order evolution equations for the bulk viscous pressure and shear stress tensor and expressed all transport coefficients in terms of modified Bessel functions of the second kind. For validation, they specialized to a (0+1)-dimensional boost-invariant and transversally homogeneous longitudinal expansion and compared the resulting pressure anisotropy and bulk pressure evolution against the exact solution of the RTA Boltzmann equation and against results from the 14-moment approximation.

## Key numbers

- For a particle mass of 300 MeV with initial temperature T0 = 600 MeV at proper time tau0 = 0.5 fm/c and relaxation time tau_eq = 0.5 fm/c, the shear-bulk coupling term in the bulk pressure evolution equation is greater than unity in magnitude for the proper-time interval 0.6 less than or similar to tau less than or similar to 3 fm/c, indicating dominance over the first-order expansion scalar term.
- At z = m/T = 1, the values of lambda_piPi, delta_PiPi, and lambda_PiPi differ between the Chapman-Enskog and 14-moment methods by approximately 15%, 20%, and 25%, respectively.
- The ratio zeta/eta = 75*(1/3 - c_s^2)^2 + O(z^5) in the RTA, which is exactly five times larger than the proportionality constant derived by Weinberg.
- For m = 1 GeV with xi0 = 100, the initial shear stress is pi0 = 35.12 GeV/fm^3 and the initial bulk viscous pressure is Pi0 = 3.08 GeV/fm^3.
- For m = 300 MeV with xi0 = 100, the initial shear stress is pi0 = 51.11 GeV/fm^3 and the initial bulk viscous pressure is Pi0 = 0.85 GeV/fm^3.

## Context

This work extends the Chapman-Enskog method previously applied to massless systems (Jaiswal 2013) to massive gases, providing the first complete set of second-order transport coefficients for bulk viscous evolution using this approach. It directly contrasts with the 14-moment approximation results of Denicol (2014) for finite masses, showing that while the first-order coefficients beta_Pi and beta_pi are identical, the second-order coefficients differ. The findings enable more accurate hydrodynamic simulations of relativistic heavy-ion collisions by demonstrating that shear-bulk coupling in the bulk pressure evolution equation is as important as the first-order expansion scalar term, confirming the earlier qualitative observation of Denicol (2014). The exact solution of the RTA Boltzmann equation by Florkowski (2014) serves as the benchmark for validation.
