# Three-Body Barrier Dynamics of Double-Alpha Decay in Heavy Nuclei

> The simultaneous emission of two $α$ particles--double-$α$ decay--represents a long-predicted but unobserved mode of nuclear radioactivity. Here we formulate this process as a genuine three-body problem within the hyperspherical coordinate framework and evaluate decay probabilities by numerically solving the corresponding hyperradial Schrödinger equation, combined with large-scale random sampling of the potential parameters; the latter treatment ensures that the present results are more convincing. Inspired by this, we demonstrate that the penetrability ratio between simultaneous and sequential $α$ emission exhibits a strikingly linear dependence on $ZQ_{αα}^{-1/2}$, extending the barrier penetration dynamics into the correlated few-body regime. The nuclei $^{108}$Xe, $^{218}$Ra, $^{224}$Pu, $^{222}$U, $^{216}$Rn, and $^{220}$Th are suggested as the most promising candidates for the observation of double-$α$ decay, with predicted half-lives potentially accessible within present detection limits. Our results provide a unified framework for multi-$α$ decay and open a pathway to probing nuclear clustering and few-body correlations in heavy nuclei.

## Key claim
The simultaneous emission of two alpha particles (double-alpha decay) is formulated as a genuine three-body problem in hyperspherical coordinates, and the penetrability ratio between simultaneous and sequential alpha emission exhibits a linear dependence on \(Z Q_{\alpha\alpha}^{-1/2}\), but the results depend on large-scale random sampling of Woods-Saxon potential parameters to account for their uncertainties.

## Method
The authors solve the hyperradial Schrodinger equation for the three-body system (core plus two alpha particles) using hyperspherical coordinates, with the total interaction potential comprising nuclear (Woods-Saxon), Coulomb, and centrifugal terms. They perform 5,000 random samples of the Woods-Saxon potential parameters (depth \(V\) between 120 and 220 MeV, diffuseness \(a\) between 0.3 and 0.7 fm) and require each sampled set to consistently describe the sequential single-alpha decay channel. The double-alpha decay width is then computed from the same parameter set, and ensemble averages and uncertainties for the branching ratio and half-life are extracted from the statistical distribution.

## Key numbers
- The branching ratio \(\log_{10}(\Gamma_{\alpha\alpha}/\Gamma_\alpha)\) for the most promising candidates: \(^{108}\)Xe = \(-13.67\), \(^{218}\)Ra = \(-14.28\), \(^{224}\)Pu = \(-15.00\), \(^{222}\)U = \(-15.38\), \(^{216}\)Rn = \(-15.42\), \(^{220}\)Th = \(-15.46\).
- The double-alpha half-lives \(\log_{10}(T_{\alpha\alpha}/\mathrm{s})\) for these candidates: \(^{108}\)Xe = \(9.28\), \(^{218}\)Ra = \(11.88\), \(^{224}\)Pu = \(9.27\), \(^{222}\)U = \(9.85\), \(^{216}\)Rn = \(11.11\), \(^{220}\)Th = \(10.50\).
- The Pearson correlation coefficient for the linear fit of \(\log_{10}(\Gamma_{\alpha\alpha})\) versus \(Z Q_{\alpha\alpha}^{-1/2}\) is \(0.963\); for the branching ratio versus the same variable it is \(0.959\).
- For \(^{212}\)Po (the \(^{208}\)Pb+alpha configuration), the branching ratio is \(\log_{10}(\mathrm{BR}) = -49.07\) and half-life \(\log_{10}(T_{\alpha\alpha}/\mathrm{s}) = 42.11\).
- For experimentally relevant candidates: \(^{220}\)Rn has \(\mathrm{BR} = -21.37\) and \(\log_{10}(T_{\alpha\alpha}/\mathrm{s}) = 23.24\); \(^{224}\)Ra has \(\mathrm{BR} = -24.18\) and \(\log_{10}(T_{\alpha\alpha}/\mathrm{s}) = 29.80\); \(^{228}\)Th has \(\mathrm{BR} = -28.08\) and \(\log_{10}(T_{\alpha\alpha}/\mathrm{s}) = 36.11\).

## Context
This work extends the three-body formalism originally developed for two-proton decay (PhysRevC.68.054005) to the double-alpha decay problem, treating it as a genuine three-body tunneling process rather than using phenomenological cluster models. It contrasts with previous energy-density functional calculations (PRL_2a, Zhao) and phenomenological approaches such as UMADAC (Denisov), MGLDM (Meg), CPPM (San1), and PCM (tbxv-tlbf), which predicted highly suppressed probabilities. The results provide a unified framework for multi-alpha decay and identify specific nuclei whose predicted half-lives are comparable to the shortest observed cluster decays (e.g., \(^{14}\)C emission from \(^{222}\)Ra with \(T_{1/2}=10^{11.01}\) s), enabling experimental searches at facilities such as ISOLDE/CERN, the FRS Ion Catcher, and the GADGET II TPC system.
