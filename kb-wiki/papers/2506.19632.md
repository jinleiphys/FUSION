# Solving the QCD effective kinetic theory with neural networks

> Event-by-event QCD kinetic theory simulations are hindered by the large numerical cost of evaluating the high-dimensional collision integral in the Boltzmann equation. In this work, we show that a neural network can be used to obtain an accurate estimate of the collision integral in a fraction of the time required for the ordinary Monte Carlo evaluation of the integral. We demonstrate that for isotropic and anisotropic distribution functions, the network accurately predicts the time evolution of the distribution function, which we verify by performing traditional evaluations of the collision integral and comparing several moments of the distribution function. This work sets the stage for an event-by-event modeling of the pre-equilibrium initial stages in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a neural network can accurately estimate the QCD effective kinetic theory collision integral for both isotropic and anisotropic distribution functions, achieving a substantial reduction in computational cost compared to traditional Monte Carlo evaluation. However, the network shows difficulties remaining at thermal equilibrium, particularly for anisotropic distributions, indicating that distributions near equilibrium may not be well represented in the training dataset.

## Method

The authors discretize the distribution function using the discrete-momentum method on a fine momentum grid, then coarsen the grid to reduce dimensionality and Monte Carlo noise. They train separate feed-forward neural networks with ReLU activation functions for the elastic (2↔2) and inelastic (1↔2) collision kernels, using training data generated from Monte Carlo evaluations of the collision integral for various classes of distribution functions, including perturbations over thermal equilibrium and time evolutions of far-from-equilibrium initial conditions. The networks are trained in the local rest frame with a specific pressure hierarchy (P_z < P_y < P_x) to exploit symmetries and reduce the required training data.

## Key numbers

- The 't Hooft coupling is set to λ = g²N_c = 10 throughout the paper
- The isotropic training dataset involved approximately 200,000 samples, corresponding to ~100 MB of data
- The anisotropic training dataset consisted of 100,000 data samples, corresponding to ~50 GB of data
- The fine grid for anisotropic data had dimensions 64×63×63, while the coarse grid used for neural network training had dimensions 32×25×25, reducing data size by a full order of magnitude
- For isotropic systems, the momentum grid boundaries were p_min = 0.01T and p_max = 15T; for anisotropic systems, p_min = 0.1T and p_max = 15T
- The best architecture for the 1↔2 kernel was a single layer with 512 nodes and learning rate 0.001312; for the 2↔2 kernel, three layers with 384/128/192 nodes and learning rate 0.000499

## Context

This work extends the QCD effective kinetic theory framework of Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2002) and the numerical methods developed by Abraao York, Kurkela, and others (2014, 2015) for solving the Boltzmann equation in heavy-ion collisions. The approach addresses the prohibitive computational cost of event-by-event simulations that has previously limited EKT applications to highly symmetric systems. By replacing the expensive Monte Carlo evaluation of the collision integral with a trained neural network, this work sets the stage for full 3+3+1D simulations with inhomogeneous backgrounds, which has previously only been achieved using simpler approximations for the collision integrals by Kurkela, Mazeliauskas, and others (2018-2021).
