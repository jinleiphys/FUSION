# Scaling of the Surface Free Energy as a Probe of the QCD Critical Region

> The QCD phase diagram is expected to have a critical point that separates the crossover and first-order transition lines. A realistic model that incorporates phase boundary effects is essential for heavy ion simulations to isolate the experimental signatures of a critical point. We discuss how to construct such an equation of state, and study its critical behavior. The effect of the coefficient of surface energy on the size of the critical region is investigated. We found that with this construction and with the chosen background equation of state, the temperature must be within one percent of its critical value to observe the critical exponents. This makes it doubtful that the critical exponents can be measured in heavy ion collisions, though it may still be feasible to observe signatures of a first-order phase transition. The method presented here is general and can be utilized with any given equation of state to test the viability of observing critical exponents in experiments.

## Key claim

The temperature must be within one percent of its critical value (|(T - T_c)/T_c| < 10^{-2}) for critical scaling of the correlation length and surface free energy to appear, at least for the chosen background equation of state with a critical point at T_c = 120 MeV and mu_c = 670 MeV. This makes it doubtful that critical exponents can be measured in heavy ion collisions, though signatures of a first-order phase transition may still be observable.

## Method

The authors construct an equation of state for QCD matter that extends into the metastable and unstable regions of the phase diagram using an interpolation method based on the 3D Ising model universality class with critical exponent delta = 4.7901. They solve the differential equation for the density profile of a planar interface between hadronic gas and quark-gluon plasma phases, and fit the numerical solution to a tanh profile to extract the correlation length. The surface free energy is then computed from the density gradient integral, and both quantities are studied as functions of reduced temperature tau = (T - T_c)/T_c for three values of the surface energy coefficient K spanning three orders of magnitude.

## Key numbers

- The critical point is placed at T_c = 120 MeV and mu_c = 670 MeV, with the phase boundary determined by condition A from Kapusta (2025).
- Critical scaling of correlation length and surface free energy appears for |tau| less than 10^{-2} (one percent of the critical temperature).
- For K = 10^5 MeV fm^5, the correlation length is on the order of tens of fm at tau = 10^{-2} when critical scaling starts appearing.
- For K = 10^7 MeV fm^5 at T = 0.5 T_c, the surface energy is 26 MeV/fm^2; for K = 10^5 MeV fm^5 at the same temperature, the surface energy is about 2.6 MeV/fm^2.
- The critical exponent for the surface free energy is calculated as bar-mu = 1.2714, using beta = 0.3264, gamma = 1.2371, and nu = gamma/2 = 0.6186.
- The surface thickness is given by t = 4.394 xi, where xi is the correlation length.

## Context

This work extends the interpolation method for constructing QCD equations of state in metastable and unstable regions proposed by Kapusta (2024), and uses the background equation of state from Kapusta (2025). The approach builds on the expectation that the QCD critical point belongs to the same universality class as the 3D Ising model and the liquid-gas phase transition, following the framework of Berges (1998) and Halasz (1998). The results suggest that observing critical scaling in heavy ion collisions will be very difficult due to the required proximity to the critical point, but the effects could be important for binary neutron star mergers, and the formalism can be applied to any proposed equation of state to assess experimental feasibility.
