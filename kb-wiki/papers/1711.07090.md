# Eigenvector continuation with subspace learning

> A common challenge faced in quantum physics is finding the extremal eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a Hamiltonian matrix in a vector space so large that linear algebra operations on general vectors are not possible. There are numerous efficient methods developed for this task, but they generally fail when some control parameter in the Hamiltonian matrix exceeds some threshold value. In this work we present a new technique called eigenvector continuation that can extend the reach of these methods. The key insight is that while an eigenvector resides in a linear space with enormous dimensions, the eigenvector trajectory generated by smooth changes of the Hamiltonian matrix is well approximated by a very low-dimensional manifold. We prove this statement using analytic function theory and propose an algorithm to solve for the extremal eigenvectors. We benchmark the method using several examples from quantum many-body theory.

## Key claim

The authors introduce eigenvector continuation, a variational technique that can extend the reach of existing computational methods (such as quantum Monte Carlo, diagrammatic expansions, and perturbation theory) when a control parameter in the Hamiltonian exceeds a threshold where those methods fail. The key insight is that the eigenvector trajectory generated by smooth changes of the Hamiltonian matrix is well approximated by a very low-dimensional manifold, even though the eigenvector resides in a linear space with enormous dimensions.

## Method

The authors consider a family of Hamiltonian matrices H(c) = H0 + cH1 and sample the ground state eigenvectors at several values of the control parameter c where the computational method is accurate. They compute inner products and matrix elements of the Hamiltonian at the target parameter value, then solve a generalized eigenvalue problem in the low-dimensional subspace spanned by the sampled eigenvectors. The method is benchmarked on the three-dimensional Bose-Hubbard model and on lattice simulations of pure neutron matter using projection Monte Carlo with auxiliary fields.

## Key numbers

- For four bosons in the 3D Bose-Hubbard model on a 4x4x4 lattice with mu = -6t, perturbation theory fails to converge when U/t is less than about -3.8; eigenvector continuation with three sampling points (U/t = -5.0, -1.5, 2.0) reproduces the exact ground state energy accurately across the entire range.
- For six neutrons on a 4x4x4 lattice with spatial spacing 1.97 fm and time spacing 1.32 fm, direct calculation gives E0 = 12(+3/-4) MeV, while eigenvector continuation with three sampling points (gA^2 = 0.25, 0.60, 0.95) gives E0 = 13.7(4) MeV.
- For fourteen neutrons on the same lattice, direct calculation gives E0 = 42(+7/-15) MeV, while eigenvector continuation with three sampling points gives E0 = 48.4(7) MeV.
- The eigenvector continuation results for both six and fourteen neutrons have error bars smaller by an order of magnitude compared to direct calculation.

## Context

This work extends the mathematical literature on eigenvector continuation, subspace tracking (Saad, 2014), and successive constraint methods for subspace acceleration (Sirkovic, 2016) by applying these ideas to quantum many-body problems where the vector space is too large for linear algebra operations on general vectors. The method addresses the breakdown of Monte Carlo simulations due to sign oscillations, the divergence of diagrammatic series expansions, and the limitations of variational methods with truncated basis expansions. It enables extrapolation from regions where computational methods are accurate to regions where they fail, and the authors suggest applications to BCS-BEC crossover, shape phase transitions in atomic nuclei, and quantum phase transitions at finite volume.
