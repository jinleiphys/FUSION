# Supernova neutrinos and nucleosynthesis

> Observations of metal-poor stars indicate that at least two different nucleosynthesis sites contribute to the production of r-process elements. One site is responsible for the production of light r-process elements Z<~50 while the other produces the heavy r-process elements. We have analyzed recent observations of metal-poor stars selecting only stars that are enriched in light r-process elements and poor in heavy r-process elements. We find a strong correlation between the observed abundances of the N=50 elements (Sr, Y and Zr) and Fe. It suggest that neutrino-driven winds from core-collapse supernova are the main site for the production of these elements. We explore this possibility by performing nucleosynthesis calculations based on long term Boltzmann neutrino transport simulations. They are based on an Equation of State that reproduces recent constrains on the nuclear symmetry energy. We predict that the early ejecta is neutron-rich with Ye ~ 0.48, it becomes proton rich around 4 s and reaches Ye = 0.586 at 9 s when our simulation stops. The nucleosynthesis in this model produces elements between Zn and Mo, including 92Mo. The elemental abundances are consistent with the observations of the metal-poor star HD 12263. For the elements between Ge and Mo, we produce mainly the neutron-deficient isotopes. This prediction can be confirmed by observations of isotopic abundances in metal-poor stars. No elements heavier than Mo (Z=42) and no heavy r-process elements are produced in our calculations.

# Supernova neutrinos and nucleosynthesis

## Key claim

Neutrino-driven winds from core-collapse supernovae are the main site for the production of light r-process elements (Sr, Y, Zr) based on a correlation between these N=50 elements and Fe in metal-poor stars, but the calculations produce no elements heavier than Mo (Z=42) and no heavy r-process elements, indicating that neutrino-driven winds alone cannot account for the full r-process abundance pattern.

## Method

The authors performed spherically symmetric radiation hydrodynamics simulations with three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport using the AGILE-Boltztran code, employing the DD2 equation of state that reproduces recent constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy from chiral effective field theory. They enhanced neutrino heating rates in the gain region to trigger explosions for an 11.2 solar mass progenitor and evolved the simulation from core collapse through explosion up to 9 seconds after bounce, then computed nucleosynthesis yields using a full nuclear network including neutrino interactions on nucleons and nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Early ejecta electron fraction: Ye ~ 0.48 (neutron-rich), becoming proton-rich around 4 s and reaching Ye = 0.586 at 9 s when the simulation stops
- Elements produced: between Zn (Z=30) and Mo (Z=42), including 92Mo
- No elements heavier than Mo (Z=42) and no heavy r-process elements produced
- Calculated abundances reproduce observed Zr (Z=40) and other nuclei around A=90 within a factor of 4 compared to the metal-poor star HD 122563
- Neutrinospheres located in density range 10^12 to 10^13 g cm^-3 at around 1 s postbounce, moving to densities around nuclear saturation density only at times around 10 s or later

## Context

This work extends the "LEGO-block" model of Qian and Wasserburg (2007) that proposed two sources for r-process elements: an H-source for heavy r-process elements and an L-source for light r-process elements produced in neutrino-driven winds. The results contradict earlier expectations that neutrino-driven winds could produce heavy r-process elements with Z > 50, consistent with the findings of Arcones and Janka (2011) and Kuroda, Wanajo, and Nomoto (2008) that ruled out neutrino-driven winds as the site for heavy r-process production. The calculations enable future comparisons with isotopic abundance observations in metal-poor stars to confirm the prediction that elements between Ge and Mo are produced mainly as neutron-deficient isotopes.
