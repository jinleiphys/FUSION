# Modified MIT Bag Models -- part II: QCD phase diagram and hot quark stars

> In the present work we use the modified versions of the MIT bag model, on which both a vector field and a self-interacting term are introduced, to obtain hot quark matter and to investigate the QCD phase diagram. We first analyze two-flavored quark matter constrained to both the freeze-out and the liquid-gas phase transition at the hadronic phase. Later, three-flavored quark matter subject to $β$ equilibrium and charge neutrality is used to compute quark star macroscopic properties, which are confronted with recent observational massive and canonical star radius results. Finally, a comparison with QCD phase diagrams obtained from the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model is performed.

# Modified MIT Bag Models, part II: QCD phase diagram and hot quark stars

## Key claim

The modified MIT bag models with a vector field and self-interacting term cannot simultaneously reproduce both the observed chemical freeze-out line at low chemical potential and the existence of massive quark stars (above 2.0 solar masses) when using a constant bag pressure; a temperature-dependent bag parameter that increases with temperature is required to satisfy both constraints, but this parametrization rules out stable strange quark matter (SQM) as proposed by the Bodmer-Witten conjecture.

## Method

The authors extended the MIT bag model by adding a vector field via minimal coupling (analogous to the omega meson in quantum hadrodynamics) and a quartic self-interaction term to mimic the quark Dirac sea contribution, then solved the mean-field equations for two-flavored symmetric quark matter and three-flavored quark matter under beta equilibrium and charge neutrality. They computed the QCD phase diagram from the condition p = 0 (transition between confined hadronic phase and quark-gluon plasma), and used the resulting equations of state as input to the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to obtain mass-radius relations for quark stars, comparing with observational constraints from pulsar MSP J0740+6620 and radius measurements of canonical 1.4 solar mass neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- For B^(1/4) = 148 MeV with no interactions: critical temperature T_c = 121 MeV at zero chemical potential, critical chemical potential μ_c = 938 MeV at zero temperature; maximum quark star mass = 1.85 M_⊙, radius of 1.4 M_⊙ star = 10.36 km
- For B^(1/4) = 165 MeV with G_V = 0.8 fm² and X_V = 1.0 (universal coupling): maximum mass = 2.31 M_⊙, radius of 1.4 M_⊙ star = 10.20 km; critical temperature T_c = 134 MeV, critical chemical potential μ_c = 1249 MeV
- For B^(1/4) = 205 MeV with no interactions: T_c = 168 MeV, μ_c = 1296 MeV; maximum mass = 0.98 M_⊙ (too low for observational constraints)
- For B^(1/4) = 148 MeV with G_V = 0.3 fm², b_4 = 60, X_V = 0.4: maximum mass = 1.96 M_⊙, radius of 1.4 M_⊙ star = 10.67 km; satisfies SQM conjecture (energy per baryon below 930 MeV)
- Temperature-dependent bag model with B_0^(1/4) = 165 MeV, T_0 = 155 MeV: T_c ≈ 168 MeV for two-flavored matter; with G_V = 0.8 fm² and X_V = 1.0 gives maximum mass = 2.31 M_⊙, radius of 1.4 M_⊙ star = 10.20 km
- At T = 40 MeV, critical chemical potential decreases by 7–10% compared to T = 0 for all parameter sets studied

## Context

This work extends the modified MIT bag models introduced by Biesdorf et al. (2020) to finite temperature, building on the original MIT bag model formalism. The authors compare their QCD phase diagrams with the chemical freeze-out line from Cleymans (2006) and lattice QCD results from Aoki et al. (2006) and Bazavov et al. (2014), and with the liquid-gas phase transition region from Finn et al. (1982). The quark star results are confronted with the massive pulsar MSP J0740+6620 from Cromartie et al. (2020) and radius constraints from Capano et al. (2020). The temperature-dependent bag parametrization follows an approach similar to Dey et al. (1998) but with the bag increasing rather than decreasing with temperature. The work enables future studies of hot quark stars and proto-hybrid stars, and highlights the tension between reproducing the QCD phase diagram at low chemical potential and obtaining massive quark stars within a single constant bag pressure model.
