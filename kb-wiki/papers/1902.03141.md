# Les Houches Lectures on Effective Field Theories for Nuclear and (some) Atomic Physics

> These lectures are a pedagogical -- not comprehensive -- introduction to the applications of effective field theory in the context of nuclear and atomic physics. A common feature of these applications is the interplay between nonperturbative physics (needed at leading order to produce nonrelativistic bound states and resonances) and controlled perturbative corrections (crucial for predictive power). The essential ideas are illustrated with the simplest nuclear EFT, Pionless EFT, which contains only contact interactions and, with minor changes, can be adapted to certain atomic systems. This EFT exploits the two-body unitarity limit, where renormalization leads to discrete scale invariance in systems of three and more bodies. Remarkably complex structures then arise from very simple leading-order interactions. Some of the challenges and rewards of including long-range forces -- pion exchange in Chiral EFT for nuclear systems or Van der Waals forces between atoms -- are briefly described.

## Key claim

These lectures argue that effective field theory (EFT) can systematically describe nuclear and atomic systems where nonperturbative leading-order interactions generate bound states and resonances, with controlled perturbative corrections providing predictive power; the pedagogical focus is on Pionless EFT as the simplest example, which exploits the two-body unitarity limit where renormalization leads to discrete scale invariance in three-body and larger systems.

## Method

The author constructs an EFT for nonrelativistic particles interacting via short-range forces, using heavy fields with reparametrization invariance to implement Lorentz invariance in a Q/m expansion. The most general action is written with contact interactions (C0, C2, D0, etc.) and their low-energy constants, then applied to nucleon systems (Pionless EFT) and 4He atomic clusters (Contact EFT), comparing leading-order EFT predictions to experimental data and phenomenological potential calculations for binding energies per particle across cluster sizes from A=2 to infinite matter.

## Key numbers

*   For nucleons, the deuteron binding energy per particle is B2/2 = 0.39 × (B3/3) where B3 = 8.48 MeV (experimental value used for normalization).
*   For 4He atoms, the dimer binding energy per particle is B2/2 = 0.0156 × (B3/3) where B3 = 0.1265 K (from phenomenological LM2M2 potential).
*   The 4He dimer average separation is ⟨r⟩ = 52(4) Å (experimental measurement).
*   The 4He Van der Waals length is lvdW ≃ 5.4 Å.
*   For nucleons, the three-body characteristic momentum is Q3 ∼ 70 MeV, about half of the inverse interaction range R−1 ∼ mπ ≃ 140 MeV.
*   For 4He atoms, Q3 ∼ (12 Å)−1, about half of R−1 ∼ lvdW−1 ≃ (5.4 Å)−1.
*   The pion decay constant is fπ ≃ 92 MeV, and the pion mass is mπ ≃ 140 MeV.
*   The nucleon mass is mN ≃ 940 MeV.
*   The QCD scale is MQCD ∼ 1 GeV.
*   The nuclear matter saturation binding energy per particle is b∞ = 5.7 MeV (experimental extrapolation).
*   The nuclear radius parameter is rnuc ≃ 1.2 fm.
*   The 4He atomic cluster saturation binding energy per particle from the HFDHE2 potential is 180 K (in units of B3 = 0.1265 K, this corresponds to 3B∞/(AB3) = 90 at unitarity and 180 from the potential).

## Context

These lectures extend the EFT approach to nuclear forces originally proposed by Weinberg (1990, 1991) and Rho (1990), addressing the problem that chiral potentials constructed according to Weinberg's suggestion produce scattering amplitudes that do not respect renormalization-group invariance. The work builds on Efimov's (1970) discovery of a geometric tower of three-body bound states in nonrelativistic systems near the two-body unitarity limit, and shows how Pionless EFT captures these regularities (Efimov physics) across both nuclear and atomic systems. The results enable systematic calculations of few-body and many-body properties using controlled EFT expansions, with applications to halo nuclei (Halo EFT, Bertulani 2002, Bedaque 2003) and cold atomic gases.
