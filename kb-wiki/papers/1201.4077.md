# Tensor-force-driven Jahn-Teller effect and shape transitions in exotic Si isotopes

> We show how the shape evolution of the neutron-rich exotic Si and S isotopes can be understood as a Jahn-Teller effect that comes in part from the tensor-driven evolution of single-particle energies. The detailed calculations we present are in excellent agreement with known experimental data, and we point out of new features that should be explored in new experiments. Potential energy surfaces are used to understand the shape evolutions. The sub-shell closed nucleus, $^{42}$Si, is shown to be a perfect example of a strongly oblate shape instead of a sphere through a robust Jahn-Teller mechanism. The distribution of spectroscopic factors measured by $^{48}$Ca(e,e'p) experiment is shown to be well described, providing a unique test on the tensor-driven shell evolution.

## Key claim

The shape evolution of neutron-rich Si and S isotopes is driven by a Jahn-Teller effect that arises from tensor-force-driven changes in single-particle energies, with the sub-shell closed nucleus $^{42}$Si exhibiting a strongly oblate shape rather than a sphere through this mechanism. The authors qualify that the tensor force is a critical but not exclusive driver, as the central force also contributes comparably to the monopole interaction that reduces the $0d_{3/2}$-$1s_{1/2}$ gap in K isotopes.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale shell-model calculations using the SDPF-MU Hamiltonian, which combines the USD interaction for the $sd$ shell and GXPF1B for the $pf$ shell, with cross-shell interactions based on the monopole-based universal interaction ($V_{\rm MU}$) including tensor, central, and two-body spin-orbit forces. They diagonalized the Hamiltonian using the mshell64 code and computed potential energy surfaces via the constrained Hartree-Fock method for Si isotopes with even neutron numbers $N=22$ to 28. The calculations were compared to experimental data on K isotope levels, $^{48}$Ca(e,e'p) spectroscopic factors, and yrast properties of Si and S isotopes.

## Key numbers

- The reduction of the $1/2^+_1$ level relative to $3/2^+_1$ in K isotopes from $N=20$ to 28 is approximately 3 MeV, reproduced by the SDPF-MU Hamiltonian.
- The difference between the proton-neutron monopole interactions $V^{pn}_{0f_{7/2}, 0d_{3/2}}$ and $V^{pn}_{0f_{7/2}, 1s_{1/2}}$ is 0.44 MeV at $A=42$, with the tensor force contributing 0.21 MeV and the central force contributing 0.22 MeV.
- The $0d_{3/2}$-$0d_{5/2}$ gap around $^{40}$Ca is approximately 7 MeV, and the tensor force changes this gap by about 2 MeV from $^{40}$Ca to $^{48}$Ca.
- For $^{40}$Si, the $2^+_2$ level is located at 0.62 MeV above $2^+_1$ with the cross-shell tensor force, and at 1.11 MeV without it.
- Observed gamma-ray energies of 638(8) and 845(6) keV in $^{40}$Si are noted as possibly feeding the $2^+_1$ state.

## Context

This work extends the tensor-force-driven shell evolution proposed by Otsuka et al. (2005) to explain shape transitions in exotic Si and S isotopes, providing a Jahn-Teller interpretation of the deformation. The calculations show better overall agreement with experiment compared to density-functional methods by Lalazissis, Peru, Rodriguez, and Li, and improve upon earlier shell-model calculations by SDPF-U and Kaneko that showed some increase in the $2^+_1$ level at $N=28$. The results enable future experimental searches for triaxial shapes and the predicted robust oblate deformation in $^{42}$Si, and suggest similar phenomena may occur in $^{78}$Ni.
