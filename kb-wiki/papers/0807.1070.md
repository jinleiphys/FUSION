# Random Matrices and Chaos in Nuclear Physics

> The authors review the evidence for the applicability of random--matrix theory to nuclear spectra. In analogy to systems with few degrees of freedom, one speaks of chaos (more accurately: quantum chaos) in nuclei whenever random--matrix predictions are fulfilled. An introduction into the basic concepts of random--matrix theory is followed by a survey over the extant experimental information on spectral fluctuations, including a discussion of the violation of a symmetry or invariance property. Chaos in nuclear models is discussed for the spherical shell model, for the deformed shell model, and for the interacting boson model. Evidence for chaos also comes from random--matrix ensembles patterned after the shell model such as the embedded two--body ensemble, the two--body random ensemble, and the constrained ensembles. All this evidence points to the fact that chaos is a generic property of nuclear spectra, except for the ground--state regions of strongly deformed nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors review evidence that random-matrix theory (RMT) predictions are fulfilled by nuclear spectra, establishing that chaos (quantum chaos) is a generic property of nuclear spectra, except for the ground-state regions of strongly deformed nuclei.

## Method

The paper surveys experimental spectral fluctuation data from neutron and proton resonance measurements and compares them to predictions of the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) of random matrices. It also examines chaos in nuclear structure models including the spherical shell model, the deformed shell model, and the interacting boson model, as well as in random-matrix ensembles patterned after the shell model such as the embedded two-body ensemble, the two-body random ensemble, and the constrained ensembles.

## Key numbers

* The target nucleus 232Th displays narrow resonances with widths less than 1 eV and spacings of about 20 eV, corresponding to excited states of the compound nucleus 233Th at an excitation energy slightly above the neutron separation energy of 4.786 MeV.
* The mean level spacing at the center of the GOE spectrum is d(0) = pi lambda / N, where N is the matrix dimension and lambda is a parameter defining the average level density.
* The Wigner surmise for the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution is P(s) = (pi/2) s exp(-pi s^2 / 4), where s is the ratio of actual level spacing to mean level spacing.

## Context

This review extends the earlier comprehensive review by Brody et al. (1981) and a shorter review by Bohigas and Weidenmuller (1988). It connects the Bohigas-Giannoni-Schmit (1984) conjecture, which links spectral fluctuation properties of quantum systems chaotic in the classical limit with RMT predictions, to nuclear many-body systems. The work enables further investigation of chaos in nuclear reactions, which is treated in a companion second part of the series.
