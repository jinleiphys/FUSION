# Proton structure in the hyperfine splitting of muonic hydrogen

> We present the leading-order prediction of baryon chiral perturbation theory for the proton polarizability contribution to the 2S hyperfine splitting in muonic hydrogen, and compare with the results of dispersive calculations.

## Key claim
The leading-order baryon chiral perturbation theory (BChPT) prediction for the proton polarizability contribution to the 2S hyperfine splitting in muonic hydrogen is considerably smaller than dispersive calculations, but the authors note that the comparison is complicated by differing definitions of the polarizability contribution between the two methods.

## Method
The authors computed the pion-nucleon loop contribution to the 2S hyperfine splitting using forward doubly-virtual Compton scattering amplitudes from BChPT at order O(p^3), evaluated via the standard two-photon exchange formula. They separately calculated the neutral-pion exchange contribution in off-forward kinematics, using the experimental pi0 to e+ e- decay width to constrain the pion-lepton coupling constant and the Goldberger-Treiman relation for the pion-nucleon coupling.

## Key numbers
- Pion-nucleon loop polarizability contribution to the 2S hyperfine splitting in muonic hydrogen: 0.85 ± 0.42 μeV (with 50% error estimate based on cutoff dependence)
- Neutral-pion exchange contribution to the 2S hyperfine splitting in muonic hydrogen: 0.02 ± 0.04 μeV
- Combined pion contribution (pi0 plus pion-nucleon loops): 0.87 ± 0.42 μeV
- Fermi energy for the 2S hyperfine splitting in muonic hydrogen: 22.8054 meV
- Pion-lepton low-energy constant for muons: cA(mu) = -4(1), derived from the experimental value cA(me) = -20(1) for electrons

## Context
This work extends the BChPT calculation of Alarcon et al. (2013) from the Lamb shift to the hyperfine splitting in muonic hydrogen, and compares with dispersive calculations by Carlson et al. (2011, 2008), Faustov et al. (2006, 2001), and Martynenko (2004). The authors find their BChPT prediction is considerably smaller than the dispersive results, unlike the Lamb shift case where BChPT and dispersive calculations agree well. The result enables future investigation of higher-order corrections, such as Delta-excitation contributions, to resolve the discrepancy.
