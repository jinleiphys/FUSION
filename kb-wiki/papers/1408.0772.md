# Symmetry Energy of Nucleonic Matter With Tensor Correlations

> The nuclear symmetry energy (E_sym(\roh)) is a vital ingredient of our understanding of many processes, from heavy-ion collisions to neutron stars structure. While the total nuclear symmetry energy at nuclear saturation density (ρ_0) is relatively well determined, its value at supranuclear densities is not. The latter can be better constrained by separately examining its kinetic and potential terms and their density dependencies. The kinetic term of the symmetry energy, E_sym^kin(ρ_0), equals the difference in the per-nucleon kinetic energy between pure neutron matter (PNM) and symmetric nuclear matter (SNM), often calculated using a simple Fermi gas model. However, experiments show that tensor force induced short-range correlations (SRC) between proton-neutron pairs shift nucleons to high-momentum in SNM, but have almost no effect in PNM. We present an approximate analytical expression for E_sym^kin(ρ) of correlated nucleonic matter. In our model, E_sym^kin(ρ_0) = -10 MeV, which differs significantly from +12.5 MeV for the widely-used free Fermi gas model. This result is consistent with our analysis of recent data on the free proton-to-neutron ratios measured in intermediate energy nucleus-nucleus collisions as well as with microscopic many-body calculations, and previous phenomenological extractions. We then use our calculated E_sym^kin(ρ) in combination with the known total symmetry energy and its density dependence at saturation density to constrain the value and density dependence of the potential part and to extrapolate the total symmetry energy to supranuclear densities.

# Symmetry Energy of Nucleonic Matter With Tensor Correlations

## Key claim

The kinetic symmetry energy at saturation density, when accounting for tensor-force-induced short-range correlations (SRC), is approximately -10 MeV rather than the +12.5 MeV predicted by the free Fermi gas model; this negative value arises because SRC significantly increase the kinetic energy of symmetric nuclear matter while having almost no effect on pure neutron matter.

## Method

The authors constructed a Correlated Fermi Gas (CFG) model for symmetric nuclear matter using a depleted Fermi gas region plus a high-momentum tail proportional to 1/k^4, with parameters extracted from inclusive (e,e') and exclusive (e,e'pN) electron scattering data at Jefferson Lab. They calculated the per-nucleon kinetic energy from this momentum distribution and subtracted the free Fermi gas kinetic energy of pure neutron matter to obtain the kinetic symmetry energy. They validated the model by comparing to transport model (IBUU) analyses of free neutron-to-proton ratios measured in Sn+Sn collisions at MSU and to microscopic many-body calculations (BHF, SCGF, FHNC, VMC).

## Key numbers

- E_sym^kin(rho_0) = -10 MeV (CFG model), compared to +12.5 MeV for the free Fermi gas model
- E_sym^kin(rho_0) found to be between -2.5 and -17.5 MeV within the uncertainty range of the high-momentum cutoff parameter lambda = 2.75 +/- 0.25
- Transport model analysis of Sn+Sn collisions yielded E_sym^kin(rho_0) = -(3.8 +/- 0.7) MeV (upper bound)
- a_2(infinity) = 7 +/- 1 for symmetric nuclear matter, extrapolated from measured cross section ratios
- Probability to find a high-momentum nucleon in SNM is about 28%, in PNM about 1-2%
- Extracted potential symmetry energy density dependence parameter gamma = 0.25 +/- 0.05 (CFG model) assuming total E_sym(rho_0) = 31.0 +/- 1 MeV and L = 50 +/- 5 MeV

## Context

This work extends prior phenomenological and microscopic studies showing that tensor-force-induced SRC reduce the kinetic symmetry energy (Xu and Li, 2011; Vidana, 2011; Lovato, 2011; Carbone, 2012; Rios, 2014). It provides an analytical expression based on electron scattering data from JLab (Egiyan, 2003, 2006; Fomin, 2012; Hen, 2014) and validates against heavy-ion collision data from MSU (Tsang, 2009). The results enable improved extrapolation of the total symmetry energy to supranuclear densities by separately constraining its kinetic and potential parts, which is critical for neutron star structure calculations and transport model simulations of heavy-ion collisions.
