# Nonperturbative contribution to the strange-antistrange asymmetry of the nucleon sea

> We give predictions for the nonperturbative (intrinsic) contribution to the $s(x) - \bar{s}(x)$ asymmetry of the nucleon sea. For this purpose we use different light-front wave functions inspired by the AdS/QCD formalism, together with a model of the nucleon in terms of meson-baryon fluctuations. The holographic wave functions for an arbitrary number of constituents, recently derived by us, give results quite close to known parametrizations that appear in the literature.

## Key claim

The nonperturbative (intrinsic) contribution to the strange-antistrange asymmetry \(s(x) - \bar{s}(x)\) in the nucleon sea is calculated using three different light-front wave functions, and the result is quite sensitive to the choice of wave function; the holographic wave function (variant II) yields results consistent with the MMHT global fit, while the Gaussian and holographic (variant I) wave functions give a zero-crossing at \(x \sim 0.7\) rather than the lower value of \(x \sim 0.35\) seen in variant II.

## Method

The authors use a light-front approach where the proton state includes meson-baryon fluctuations (\(N \rightarrow \Lambda K\) and \(N \rightarrow \Sigma K\)), with a fluctuation probability of 1.27% for \(N \rightarrow \Lambda K\). The \(s(x)\) and \(\bar{s}(x)\) distributions are computed as convolutions of meson-baryon distribution functions (from two-body light-front wave functions for the baryon-meson bound state) with quark distribution functions inside the \(\Lambda\) and \(K\) (from two-body light-front wave functions for the baryon and meson). Three types of light-front wave functions are used: a Gaussian wave function (following Brodsky and Ma (1996)), a holographic wave function (variant I) obtained from matching to light-front QCD at large \(x\), and a holographic wave function (variant II) obtained from matching at all \(x\) and for an arbitrary number of constituents (Gutsche et al. (2013)).

## Key numbers

- Fluctuation probability for \(N \rightarrow \Lambda K\): 1.27%, taken from Cao and Signal (1999).
- Constituent quark masses used: \(m_q = 330\) MeV, \(m_s = 480\) MeV, diquark mass \(m_D = 600\) MeV.
- Hadron masses: \(m_\Lambda = 1115.683\) MeV, \(m_{K^+} = 493.677\) MeV.
- Scale parameter \(\kappa\): 330 MeV for the Gaussian wave function (from Brodsky and Ma (1996)); 350 MeV for both holographic wave functions (from Gutsche et al. (2013)).
- Second moment \(\langle x (s - \bar{s}) \rangle\) for Gaussian LFWF at \(\kappa = 330\) MeV: 0.00134.
- Second moment \(\langle x (s - \bar{s}) \rangle\) for holographic LFWF (variant I) at \(\kappa = 350\) MeV: 0.00157.
- Second moment \(\langle x (s - \bar{s}) \rangle\) for holographic LFWF (variant II) at \(\kappa = 350\) MeV: 0.00091.
- Zero-crossing of \(s(x) - \bar{s}(x)\): at \(x \sim 0.7\) for Gaussian and holographic (variant I) LFWFs; at \(x \sim 0.35\) for holographic LFWF (variant II).
- The authors quote a combined prediction for the second moment: \(\langle x (s - \bar{s}) \rangle = 0.00102 \pm 0.00055\).

## Context

This work extends the light-front approach of Brodsky and Ma (1996), who used Gaussian and power-law wave functions and found \(s(x) < \bar{s}(x)\) at small \(x\) and \(s(x) > \bar{s}(x)\) at large \(x\), a behavior opposite to that of meson-cloud models (Cao and Signal (1999)). The authors introduce more realistic holographic wave functions from the AdS/QCD formalism (Brodsky et al. (2006); Gutsche et al. (2013)) and show that the asymmetry is sensitive to the choice of wave function. The holographic LFWF (variant II) gives results consistent with the MMHT global fit (Harland-Lang et al. (2014)), suggesting that the \(s-\bar{s}\) asymmetry can serve as an independent tool to discriminate between different light-front wave functions. The results for the second moment are consistent with the range \(|\langle x (s - \bar{s}) \rangle| \sim 10^{-4}\) from other nonperturbative models and with the perturbative result of Catani et al. (2004) of approximately \(-5 \times 10^{-4}\) at \(Q^2 = 20\) GeV\(^2\).
