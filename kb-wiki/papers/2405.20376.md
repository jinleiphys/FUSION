# Timescales and necessary conditions for hydrodynamization in one-dimensional Bose gases

> We study the quantum evolution of one-dimensional Bose gases immediately after several variants of high-energy quenches, both theoretically and experimentally. Using the advantages conveyed by the relative simplicity of these nearly integrable many-body systems, we are able to differentiate the behaviors of two distinct but often temporally overlapping processes, hydrodynamization and local prethermalization. We show that the hydrodynamization epoch is itself characterized by two independent timescales, an oscillation period and an observable-dependent damping time. We also show how the existence of a hydrodynamization epoch depends on the exact nature of the high-energy quench. There is a universal character to our findings, which can be applied to the short-time behavior of any interacting many-body quantum system after a sudden high-energy quench. We specifically discuss its potential relevance to heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper shows that hydrodynamization in one-dimensional Bose gases requires not only a high-energy quench but also a multimodal energy distribution of quasiparticles after the quench; when the energy distribution is bimodal (two peaks at similar energies), no hydrodynamization occurs even though the system is far from equilibrium.

## Method

The authors studied theoretically and experimentally the quantum evolution of one-dimensional Bose gases after high-energy quenches, using both Bragg pulse excitations in trapped gases and tailored initial rapidity distributions in translationally invariant systems. They modeled the Tonks-Girardeau gas in the hard-core limit, where the system maps to noninteracting fermions, allowing exact calculation of time-dependent momentum distributions from the conserved rapidity distribution. By independently varying the Bragg wave number \(k\) and the Fermi momentum \(\theta_F\) (which sets the width of rapidity peaks), they disentangled the two timescales within the hydrodynamization epoch: an oscillation period and an observable-dependent damping time.

## Key numbers

- Hydrodynamization coherence period: \(T^c_{\rm hd} = \pi m / (\hbar k^2)\), where \(k = 2\pi/\lambda\) is the Bragg wave number.
- Dephasing timescale: \(T_{\rm dp} = \pi m / (2k\theta_F)\), where \(2\theta_F\) is the width of the rapidity peaks.
- For three-peak states with \(N=33\) (\(\theta_F = 0.825\hbar k_0\)), hydrodynamization oscillations damp within \(t_{\rm ev} \simeq T_{\rm dp}\) for all momentum modes.
- For two-peak states, the dephasing timescale is \(T'_{\rm dp} = \pi m / (4k\theta_F)\), twice as fast as for three-peak states, and no hydrodynamization oscillations occur.
- The prethermalization of the full momentum distribution follows \(\Delta f(t) \propto \exp(-b\, t_{\rm ev}/T_{\rm dp})\) with \(b = 1.7\) for three-peak states and \(b = 3.2\) for two-peak states, with a power-law tail \(\Delta f(t) \propto (t_{\rm ev}/T_{\rm dp})^{-4}\) at late times for three-peak states.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous experimental and theoretical study (Le et al., 2023) which first identified hydrodynamization and local prethermalization as distinct processes in one-dimensional Bose gases after Bragg pulse quenches. The present paper decouples theory from experimental limitations to independently vary the Bragg wave number and peak width, revealing that hydrodynamization requires multimodal energy distributions and that the damping time is observable-dependent. The findings are argued to be universal for any interacting many-body quantum system after a sudden high-energy quench, with specific relevance to relativistic heavy-ion collisions where the estimated shortest time for hydrodynamic description aligns with experimental observations.
