# Symmetry assisted preparation of entangled many-body states on a quantum computer

> Starting from the Quantum-Phase-Estimate (QPE) algorithm, a method is proposed to construct entangled states that describe correlated many-body systems on quantum computers. Using operators for which the discrete set of eigenvalues is known, the QPE approach is followed by measurements that serve as projectors on the entangled states. These states can then be used as inputs for further quantum or hybrid quantum-classical processing. When the operator is associated to a symmetry of the Hamiltonian, the approach can be seen as a quantum--computer formulation of symmetry breaking followed by symmetry restoration. The method proposed in this work, called Discrete Spectra Assisted (DSA), is applied to superfluid systems. By using the blocking technique adapted to qubits, the full spectra of a pairing Hamiltonian is obtained.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that the Quantum-Phase-Estimate (QPE) algorithm, when applied to operators with known discrete spectra, can serve as a quantum-computer implementation of symmetry breaking followed by symmetry restoration to prepare strongly entangled many-body states, but the method is demonstrated only for the specific case of a degenerate pairing Hamiltonian where eigenvalues are exactly representable as binary fractions.

## Method
The Discrete Spectra Assisted (DSA) method starts with an initial state that breaks a symmetry (here, particle-number symmetry via a BCS-like state) and applies the QPE algorithm using a unitary operator \(U_S\) constructed from the symmetry operator \(S\) (here, the particle-number operator \(\hat N\)). The register qubits are measured, projecting the working qubits onto eigenstates of \(S\) with definite particle number. The resulting entangled states are then used as inputs for further processing, such as computing expectation values of the pairing Hamiltonian. The method is implemented using the Jordan-Wigner transformation to map fermionic operators to qubit gates, and the blocking technique (breaking pairs of time-reversed states) is adapted to qubits to access excited states.

## Key numbers
- For a 6-qubit system (\(n_q=6\)) with 3 register qubits (\(n_r=3\)) and initial state prepared with \(\varphi=\pi/4\), \(\pi/2\), and \(3\pi/4\), the qubit counting statistics (QCS) follow the binomial distribution \(P(A) = C^A_{n_q} p^A (1-p)^{n_q-A}\) with \(p=\sin^2(\varphi/2)\).
- For a 12-qubit system (\(n_q=12\), 6 pairs) with 3 register qubits and \(\varphi=\pi/2\), 200 events from the IBM Qiskit emulator recover the ground-state energies of all even particle numbers as given by the analytical formula \(E/g = -\frac{1}{4}(A-\nu)(2n_q - A - \nu - 2)\) with seniority \(\nu=0\).
- For the degenerate pairing Hamiltonian with \(\varepsilon_i = 0\), the full odd-even spectra are obtained by breaking from 1 to \(n_q/2 = 6\) pairs, with seniority values \(\nu = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\) corresponding to different excited states.

## Context
This work extends the symmetry-breaking/symmetry-restoration (SB-SR) strategy, a pillar of nuclear many-body physics (Ring and Schuck 1980, Blaizot and Ripka 1986, Bender et al. 2003, Robledo 2018), to quantum computers. It contrasts with standard QPE applications (Nielsen and Chuang 2002, Overy et al. 2007) by using the algorithm not for eigenvalue estimation but for state preparation via projection onto known eigenstates. The method enables the construction of highly entangled states that can serve as inputs for variational quantum eigenvalue (VQE) algorithms (Peruzzo et al. 2014, McClean et al. 2016) and opens the possibility of performing multiple symmetry projections on quantum computers, which becomes intractable on classical computers for simultaneous restoration of several symmetries (e.g., in nuclear structure).
