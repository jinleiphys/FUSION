# Review on the progress in nuclear fission

> An overview is given on some of the main advances in experimental methods, experimental results and theoretical models and ideas of the last years in the field of nuclear fission. New experimental approaches extended the availability of fissioning systems considerably and provided a full identification of all fission products in A and Z for the first time. In particular, the transition from symmetric to asymmetric fission around 226Th and some unexpected structure in the mass distributions in the fission of systems around Z = 80 to 84 as well as an extended systematics of the odd-even effect in fission fragment Z distributions have been measured [A. N. Andreyev et al., Rep. Progr. Phys. 81 (2018) 016301]. Three classes of theoretical descriptions of fission presently appear to be the most promising or the most successful ones: Self-consistent quantum-mechanical models fully consider the quantum-mechanical features of the fission process. Intense efforts are presently made to develop suitable theoretical tools [N. Schunck, L. M. Robledo, Rep. Prog. Phys. 79 (2016) 116301] for modeling the non-equilibrium, large-amplitude collective motion leading to fission. Stochastic, essentially classical models provide a fully developed technical framework. The main features of the fission-fragment mass distribution are well reproduced from mercury to fermium and beyond [P. Möller, J. Randrup, Phys. Rev. C 91 (2015) 044316]. In an alternative semi-empirical approach [K.-H. Schmidt et al., Nucl. Data Sheets 131 (2016) 107], considerable progress in describing the fission observables has been achieved by combining several general theoretical concepts. This new approach reveals a high degree of regularity and allows calculating high-quality data with a unique parameter set for a large number of systems and an extended excitation-energy range.

## Key claim

This review argues that recent experimental and theoretical advances have transformed the understanding of nuclear fission, but the authors emphasize that the field remains fragmented: no single theoretical model yet captures all observables, and the most successful approaches combine quantum-mechanical, stochastic, and semi-empirical concepts rather than relying on a single unified framework.

## Method

The authors survey experimental innovations (inverse-kinematics techniques at GSI and GANIL, beta-delayed fission at ISOLDE, and electromagnetic-induced fission of relativistic secondary beams) and three classes of theoretical descriptions: self-consistent quantum-mechanical models, stochastic classical models, and a semi-empirical approach (the GEF model) that combines topological properties of continuous functions, separability of shell and macroscopic effects, quantum-oscillator coupling to a heat bath, early freeze-out of collective motion, and statistical mechanics for intrinsic excitations. The review covers fission barriers, fragment yields, total kinetic energies, and prompt-neutron multiplicities, but excludes ternary fission, multi-fragmentation, and quasi-fission.

## Key numbers

* The specific energy release in fission is about 200 MeV per fission, making nuclear fuel about 10^8 times more energy-dense than fossil fuels.
* Electromagnetic excitation of relativistic projectile fragments at GSI provides access to more than 100 mostly neutron-deficient projectile fragments with A ≤ 238 for low-energy fission studies, with induced excitation-energy distributions centered at about 14 MeV above the ground state with a FWHM of about 7 MeV.
* The SOFIA experiment achieved full event-by-event identification of all fission products in Z and A for electromagnetic-induced fission of 238U and its fragmentation residues.
* Beta-delayed fission studies at ISOLDE revealed a pronounced double-humped mass distribution for 180Hg (formed after beta decay of 180Tl) and asymmetric fission for 178Hg, 194Po, 196Po, and 202Rn.
* The transition from asymmetric to symmetric fission occurs around A = 226, with triple-humped distributions observed for fissioning nuclei in the intermediate region; the heavy component of asymmetric fission remains very close to Z = 54 for all actinides investigated.
* The odd-even staggering in Z yields systematically increases with asymmetry and has similar magnitudes for even-Z and odd-Z fissioning nuclei at large asymmetry.
* The fine structure in the fission-product N distribution depends only weakly on excitation energy, in contrast to the odd-even staggering in the Z distribution.

## Context

This review extends the earlier status summarized around the 50th anniversary of fission (1989) and builds on the fission-mode nomenclature of Brosa (1990), the combinatorial model of Nifenecker (1982), and the static scission-point model of Wilkins (1976). It synthesizes recent experimental results from Andreyev (2018), the SOFIA collaboration (Boutoux 2013, Martin 2015, Pellereau 2017), and beta-delayed fission studies (Andreyev 2013, Ghys 2014), alongside theoretical advances from Schunck and Robledo (2016) on self-consistent models, Möller and Randrup (2015) on stochastic models, and the GEF semi-empirical approach (Schmidt 2016). The review enables future work by identifying open questions about the transition from symmetric to asymmetric fission in the lead region, the role of multi-chance fission in interpreting mass distributions, and the need for a complete systematics of low-energy fission across the nuclear chart.
