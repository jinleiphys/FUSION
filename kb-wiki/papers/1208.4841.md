# Coulomb forces in neutron star crusts

> We study the role the proton-electron gas interaction has on the formation of nuclear structures in neutron star crusts. Using a classical molecular dynamics model we study isospin symmetric and asymmetric matter at subsaturation densities and low temperatures varying the Coulomb interaction strength. The effect of such variation is quantified on the fragment size multiplicity, the inter-particle distance, the isospin content of the clusters, the nucleon mobility and cluster persistence, and on the nuclear structure shapes. We find that the proton-electron Coulomb interaction distributes matter more evenly, disrupts the formation of larger objects, reduces the isospin content, modifies the nucleon average displacement, but does not affect the inter-nucleon distance in clusters . The nuclear structures are also found to change shapes by different degrees depending on their isospin content, temperature and density.

## Key claim

The nuclear pasta structures characteristic of neutron star crusts exist even when the Coulomb interaction between protons and the electron gas is completely turned off, because the competition between attractive and repulsive parts of the nuclear forces alone can produce them; the Coulomb force merely emphasizes this competition rather than being its essential cause.

## Method

The authors used a classical molecular dynamics (CMD) model with Pandharipande two-body potentials to simulate systems of 2000–3000 nucleons under periodic boundary conditions at subsaturation densities (0.01–0.072 fm⁻³) and low temperatures (0.1–1.0 MeV). They varied the strength of a screened Coulomb potential (with screening length λ = 10 fm) by a multiplicative factor α from 0 to 1, and analyzed 200 stationary ergodic configurations per set of conditions using a Minimum Spanning Tree cluster recognition algorithm and Minkowski functionals (mean curvature and Euler characteristic) to classify topological shapes.

## Key numbers

- Systems studied: symmetric matter (x = Z/A = 0.5, 1000 protons + 1000 neutrons) and asymmetric matter (x = 0.3, 1000 protons + 2000 neutrons)
- Density range: 0.01 fm⁻³ ≤ ρ ≤ 0.072 fm⁻³; temperature range: 0.1 MeV ≤ T ≤ 1.0 MeV
- Screening length λ = 10 fm, cutoff radius r_c = 5.4 fm for nuclear potentials
- Clusterization radius for MST algorithm: 3.0 fm
- Inter-nucleon distance in clusters: about 1.7 fm at all studied densities (unchanged by Coulomb)
- For asymmetric matter (x=0.3) at ρ=0.015 fm⁻³ and T=1.0 MeV: without Coulomb, fragment sizes grow up to A ≈ 800 and x content enhances up to x ≈ 0.4; with Coulomb, both are reduced
- For symmetric matter (x=0.5) at ρ=0.072 fm⁻³ and T=0.1 MeV: mean curvature changes from −382 (α=1) to 29.4 (α=0); Euler number changes from −15.8 to −1.9
- For symmetric matter (x=0.5) at ρ=0.015 fm⁻³ and T=0.1 MeV: mean curvature changes from 1771 (α=1) to 514 (α=0); Euler number changes from 8.9 to 3.5
- For asymmetric matter (x=0.3) at ρ=0.015 fm⁻³ and T=1.0 MeV: mean curvature changes from 9090 (α=1) to 11011 (α=0); Euler number changes from −612 to −243
- For asymmetric matter (x=0.3) at ρ=0.072 fm⁻³ and T=0.1 MeV: mean curvature changes from −4485 (α=1) to −5914 (α=0); Euler number changes from 798 to 933

## Context

This work extends previous static liquid-drop model studies (2003) and zero-temperature density functional studies (Maruyama 2005) that found Coulomb screening broadens the density range where pasta structures appear, by adding finite-temperature dynamical effects via molecular dynamics. It also complements Monte Carlo simulations by Piekarewicz and Toledo Sanchez (2012) that studied β-equilibrium proton fractions at a single temperature (T=1.0 MeV) using Ewald summation for Coulomb interactions. The finding that pasta structures persist without Coulomb due to nuclear force competition alone is new, and the authors plan to extend the study to investigate the transition from uniform nuclear phase to pasta at very low temperatures and its dependence on isospin content and compressibility using both versions of the Pandharipande potential and other classical potentials.
