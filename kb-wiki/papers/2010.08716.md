# Two-particle correlations at high-energy nuclear collisions, peripheral-tube model revisited

> In this paper, we give an account of the peripheral-tube model, which has been developed to give an intuitive and dynamical description of the so-called ridge effect in two-particle correlations in high-energy nuclear collisions. Starting from a realistic event-by-event fluctuating hydrodynamical model calculation, we first show the emergence of ridge + shoulders in the so-called two-particle long-range correlations, reproducing the data. In contrast to the commonly used geometric picture of the origin of the anisotropic flow, we can explain such a structure dynamically in terms of the presence of high energy-density peripheral tubes in the initial conditions. These tubes violently explode and deflect the near radial flow coming from the interior of the hot matter, which in turn produces a two-ridge structure in single-particle distribution, with approximately two units opening in azimuth. When computing the two-particle correlation, this will result in characteristic three-ridge structure, with a high near-side ridge and two symmetric lower away-side ridges or shoulders. Several anisotropic flows, necessary to producing ridge + shoulder structure, appear naturally in this dynamical description. Using this simple idea, we can understand several related phenomena, such as centrality dependence and trigger-angle dependence.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the ridge and shoulder structures observed in two-particle long-range correlations in high-energy nuclear collisions arise dynamically from the violent explosion of high-energy-density peripheral tubes in the initial conditions, rather than from the commonly used geometric picture of anisotropic flow driven by the global shape of the collision zone. This explanation is presented as complementary to the standard eccentricity-to-flow-harmonics interpretation, not as a replacement.

## Method

The authors used the NeXSPheRIO code, which combines NEXUS event-generator initial conditions with the SPheRIO smoothed particle hydrodynamics code, to simulate Au+Au collisions at 200 A GeV on an event-by-event basis. They computed two-particle correlation functions for charged particles with trigger particles above 2.5 GeV and associated particles above 1.0 GeV, subtracting the average elliptic flow contribution using the ZYAM method. They then introduced a simplified single-peripheral-tube model to isolate the dynamical effect of a single high-energy tube on the final-state correlations.

## Key numbers

* For central Au+Au collisions at 200 A GeV (0-10% centrality), the two-particle correlation after flow subtraction shows a near-side ridge at Delta-phi approximately 0 extending over several units of Delta-eta, and a symmetric double-ridge structure on the away side near Delta-phi approximately plus or minus 2.
* The trigger particles were selected with p_T greater than 2.5 GeV and associated particles with p_T greater than 1.0 GeV, following the STAR experiment analysis.
* The single-particle distribution from a peripheral tube produces a two-ridge structure with approximately two units opening in azimuth.
* The chi-squared per number of degrees of freedom for transverse-momentum spectra improved from 1.6 to 1.3 for the 6%-15% centrality window and from 28 to 1.6 for the 35%-45% centrality window when using fluctuating initial conditions instead of averaged initial conditions.
* The freeze-out temperature was found to increase with impact parameter, from more central to more peripheral collisions.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies using NeXSPheRIO (Hama et al., various years) that reproduced the ridge effect in two-particle correlation data from RHIC experiments (STAR Collaboration, Putschke; PHENIX Collaboration, McCumber). The peripheral-tube model provides a dynamical alternative to the standard geometric interpretation of anisotropic flow based on initial-state eccentricities, which is commonly used in hydrodynamic calculations such as those by Hirano (2004, 2005) and Nonaka (2001). The model enables understanding of centrality dependence and trigger-angle dependence of the ridge and shoulder structures, and the authors suggest that other hydrodynamic models with different initial conditions should produce similar results.
