# Anomaly-induced transport phenomena from the imaginary-time formalism

> A derivation of the anomaly-induced transport phenomena---the chiral magnetic/vortical effect---is revisited based on the imaginary-time formalism of quantum field theory. Considering the simplest anomalous system composed of a single Weyl fermion, we provide two derivations: perturbative (one-loop) evaluation of the anomalous transport coefficient, and the anomaly matching for the local thermodynamic functional.

## Key claim

The paper revisits the derivation of anomaly-induced transport phenomena (chiral magnetic/vortical effect) using the imaginary-time (Matsubara) formalism of quantum field theory, providing a rigorous justification that the hydrostatic partition function method is applicable not only to real hydrostatic situations but also to systems in general local thermal equilibrium.

## Method

The authors consider a system composed of a single right-handed Weyl fermion coupled to an external electromagnetic field and curved spacetime. They provide two derivations: first, a perturbative one-loop evaluation of the anomalous transport coefficients using the Matsubara formalism and linear response theory; second, a nonperturbative derivation based on anomaly matching for the local thermodynamic functional (Massieu-Planck functional) within Zubarev's nonequilibrium statistical operator method.

## Key numbers

* Chiral magnetic conductivity: sigma_B = mu_R / (4 pi^2), where mu_R is the chemical potential for the right-handed Weyl fermion
* Chiral vortical conductivity: sigma_omega = mu_R^2 / (4 pi^2) + T^2 / 12, where T is the temperature
* Gauge anomaly coefficient: C = 1 / (4 pi^2)
* Gravitational anomaly coefficient: lambda = 1 / (768 pi^2)

## Context

This work extends the theoretical development of anomaly-induced transport phenomena that has been pursued through multiple approaches over the past decade, including direct field theoretical evaluation by Fukushima (2008), fluid/gravity correspondence by Banerjee (2008) and Erdmenger (2008), entropy-current analysis by Son (2009), linear response theory by Landsteiner (2011), kinetic theory by Gao (2012) and Son (2012), and the hydrostatic partition function method by Banerjee (2012) and Jensen (2012). The paper provides a clear statistical mechanical foundation for the hydrostatic partition function method by connecting it to Zubarev's nonequilibrium statistical operator formalism, enabling rigorous treatment of anomalous transport in systems away from global equilibrium.
