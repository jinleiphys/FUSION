# Structure of Exotic Nuclei: A Theoretical Review

> The study of exotic nuclei---nuclei with the ratio of neutron number $N$ to proton number $Z$ deviating much from that of those found in nature---is at the forefront of nuclear physics research because it can not only reveal novel nuclear properties and thus enrich our knowledge of atomic nuclei, but also help us to understand the origin of chemical elements in the nucleosynthesis. With the development of radioactive ion beam facilities around the world, more and more unstable nuclei become experimentally accessible. Many exotic nuclear phenomena have been observed or predicted in nuclei far from the $β$-stability line, such as neutron or proton halos, the shell evolution and changes of nuclear magic numbers, the island of inversion, soft-dipole excitations, clustering effects, new radioactivities, giant neutron halos, the shape decoupling between core and valence nucleons in deformed halo nuclei, etc. In this contribution, I will present a review of theoretical study of exotic nuclear structure. I will first introduce characteristic features and new physics connected with exotic nuclear phenomena: the weakly-bound feature, the large-spatial extension in halo nuclei, deformation effects in halo nuclei, the shell evolution, new radioactivities and clustering effects. Then I will highlight some recent progresses corresponding to these features.

## Key claim

This review argues that exotic nuclei far from the beta-stability line exhibit novel phenomena such as halos, shell evolution, and clustering, but emphasizes that nuclear magicity in exotic nuclei may be “local” – manifesting in some properties but not others – as illustrated by the case of calcium isotopes where charge radius measurements cast doubt on the magicity at N=32.

## Method

The author presents a personal review of theoretical nuclear structure for exotic nuclei, organized around six characteristic features: the weakly-bound nature, large spatial extension in halos, deformation effects in halos, shell evolution, new radioactivities, and clustering effects. For each feature, the review summarizes recent theoretical progress using models such as the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) approach, relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) models, complex scaling methods, and density functional theories, citing specific calculations and experimental comparisons.

## Key numbers

- The Weizsacker-Skyrme (WS4) mass model predicts the number of bound nuclides; the number of known unstable nuclei is now close to three thousand.
- In the Ca isotope chain, a prominent shell closure at N=32 was established from measured masses of 53,54Ca (Wienholtz 2013), and a new shell closure at N=34 was indicated by the energy of the first 2+ state for 52,54Ca rising dramatically (Steppenbeck 2013).
- Deformed halo candidates include 31Ne (Nakamura 2014) and 37Mg (Kobayashi 2014).
- Shape decoupling effects were predicted in 42,44Mg: the core is prolate but the halo has an oblate shape (Zhou 2010, Li 2012).
- A recent precise measurement of charge radii in Ca isotopes (Garcia Ruiz 2016) found that 52Ca’s charge radius is not smaller than its neighbors, casting doubt on its doubly magic character.

## Context

This review extends the line of theoretical work on exotic nuclear structure summarized in earlier reviews such as Meng (2006, 2015) and Dobaczewski (2007), and it discusses specific predictions that contradict or challenge the traditional picture of magic numbers. The paper enables future work by identifying open questions, such as whether the magicity at N=32 and 34 is robust across all nuclear properties, and by highlighting the need for unified models that combine ab initio methods with density functional theories.
