# Dark Matter as Dense Color Superconductor

> We discuss a novel cold dark matter candidate which is formed from the ordinary quarks during the QCD phase transition when the axion domain wall undergoes an unchecked collapse due to the tension in the wall. If a large number of quarks is trapped inside the bulk of a closed axion domain wall, the collapse stops due to the internal Fermi pressure. In this case the system in the bulk, may reach the critical density when it undergoes a phase transition to a color superconducting phase with the ground state being the quark condensate, similar to BCS theory. If this happens, the new state of matter representing the diquark condensate with a large baryon number B > 10^{20} becomes a stable soliton-like configuration. Consequently, it may serve as a novel cold dark matter candidate.

# Dark Matter as Dense Color Superconductor

## Key claim
The paper proposes that cold dark matter could consist of stable "QCD-balls", dense color-superconducting quark configurations formed during the QCD phase transition when axion domain walls collapse and trap quarks, but the scenario depends on the existence of axion domain walls as a "squeezer" and on the assumption that quarks cannot escape the shrinking bubble, with the resulting objects being absolutely stable only for baryon numbers above approximately 10^33, while lower values down to 10^20 may produce long-lived metastable states.

## Method
The author estimates the equilibrium radius of a QCD-ball by balancing the Fermi pressure of a relativistic quark gas against the surface tension of the collapsing axion domain wall, using the thermodynamic potential for non-interacting relativistic fermions. The total energy and baryon number density are then derived as functions of baryon charge B, and the condition for absolute stability is obtained by comparing the derivative of the QCD-ball mass with respect to B to one-third of the nucleon mass. Experimental bounds on the flux of neutral soliton-like objects from dark matter searches are used to constrain the minimum viable baryon charge.

## Key numbers
- Baryon number for absolute stability: B_c ~ 10^33, corresponding to stabilization radius R_c ~ 10^12 GeV^{-1} and mass E_c ~ 10^33 GeV, using axion decay constant f_a ~ 10^10 GeV and domain wall tension sigma ~ 1.8 x 10^8 GeV^3.
- Critical density for absolute stability: n_c ~ 9 n_0, where n_0 ~ 0.16 fm^{-3} is the nuclear saturation density.
- Experimental lower limit on QCD-ball mass: M_B^{exp} > 2 x 10^21 GeV, corresponding to baryon charge B^{exp} > 1.6 x 10^20, from flux bounds F < const. x 10^{-16} cm^{-2} sec^{-1} sr^{-1}.
- Energy scaling: E ~ B^{8/9} for large baryon number B.
- Baryon number density scaling: n ~ (6 sigma / c) B^{-1/3}, with c ~ 0.7.

## Context
This work extends the study of color superconducting (CS) phase in QCD, analogous to BCS theory for electrons, from its known astrophysical contexts in neutron star interiors (as studied by many authors in the late 1990s and early 2000s) to a cosmological setting during the QCD phase transition. The QCD-ball concept is formally similar to Q-balls (nontopological solitons associated with conserved global charges), but with the advantage that the underlying theory is QCD with no free parameters. The scenario enables a natural explanation for the observed relation Omega_B ~ Omega_DM within an order of magnitude, since both baryonic and dark matter originate from the same QCD phase transition, and suggests that unexplained events such as Centauro events or Tunguska-like events could be related to dense QCD-balls.
