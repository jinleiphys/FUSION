# Scattering with real-time path integrals

> Sharp-momentum transition matrix elements for scattering from a short-range Gaussian potential are computed using a real-time path integral. The computation is based on a numerical implementation of a new interpretation of the path integral as the expectation of a potential functional with respect to a complex probability distribution on cylinder sets of paths. The method is closely related to a unitary transfer matrix computation.

## Key claim

Sharp-momentum transition matrix elements for scattering from a short-range Gaussian potential can be computed using a real-time path integral, provided the path integral is reinterpreted as the expectation of a potential functional with respect to a complex probability distribution on cylinder sets of paths, and the complex probability is approximately factored into a product of one-step complex probabilities.

## Method

The path integral representation of the unitary time evolution operator is discretized in time and space, with the real line divided into a finite number of disjoint intervals per time slice. The complex probability that a path passes through a given sequence of intervals is approximated as a product of one-step complex probabilities, each of which can be evaluated analytically using Fresnel integrals. This factorization reduces the path integral to the N-th power of a complex matrix applied to a vector, which is then used to compute on-shell transition matrix elements via time-dependent wave packets evolved with free dynamics.

## Key numbers

- The test calculation used a particle of mass μ = 1, a repulsive Gaussian potential with r₀ = 1 and λ = 5, initial momentum pᵢ = 5, and momentum width Δp = 0.25.
- The computation employed 120 time steps and 10000 intervals between ±15.
- At t = -3 the wave packet was outside the range of the potential, allowing the time limit to be replaced by an evaluation at t = 3.
- The sum of one-step complex probabilities over 5000 intervals gave a real part of 1.000000e+00 and an imaginary part on the order of 10⁻¹⁵ to 10⁻¹⁶ for all tested values of y, demonstrating that the imaginary part sums to zero with no visible round-off error.

## Context

This work extends the reinterpretation of the Feynman path integral as a complex probability expectation, developed by Muldowney and by Jørgensen and Nathanson, to the computation of scattering observables. The method is numerically equivalent to a unitary transfer-matrix calculation, and the authors suggest that with refinement it could be applied to the same class of problems treatable by transfer matrix methods. The approach contrasts with standard path integral calculations that are limited to quadratic interactions, perturbation theory, or imaginary-time Monte Carlo integration (Metropolis).
