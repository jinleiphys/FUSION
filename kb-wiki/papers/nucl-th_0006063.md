# Collective Quadrupole Excitations in Transitional Nuclei

> The generalized Bohr Hamiltonian was used to describe the low-lying collective excitations in even-even isotopes of Ru, Pd, Te, Ba and Nd. The Strutinsky collective potential and cranking inertial functions were obtained using the Nilsson potential. The effect of coupling with the pairing vibrations is taken into account approximately when determining the inertial functions. The calculation does not contain any free parameter.

# Collective Quadrupole Excitations in Transitional Nuclei

## Key claim
The generalized Bohr Hamiltonian, when modified to use the most probable pairing gap (rather than the BCS equilibrium gap) in the inertial functions, can reproduce low-lying collective excitation energies and electromagnetic transitions in transitional even-even nuclei without any free parameters, though the agreement is significantly better for neutron-rich Ru and Pd isotopes than for neutron-deficient Te, Ba, and Nd isotopes.

## Method
The authors construct a generalized Bohr Hamiltonian (GBH) for quadrupole collective excitations, with the collective potential obtained via the Strutinsky method and the vibrational and rotational inertial functions computed using the cranking model with the Nilsson single-particle potential. The key modification is that all inertial functions are evaluated at the most probable pairing gap (determined from the ground-state wave function of a separate collective pairing Hamiltonian) rather than at the BCS equilibrium gap, which approximately accounts for the coupling between quadrupole and pairing vibrations. The calculation contains no adjustable parameters.

## Key numbers
- The ratio of the most probable pairing gap (Δ_vib) to the equilibrium BCS gap (Δ_eq) is approximately 0.7.
- In the neutron-rich region, calculations were performed for Ru and Pd isotopes with neutron numbers 64 ≤ N ≤ 74.
- For neutron-deficient Te, Ba, and Nd isotopes, good agreement is obtained for Nd nuclei and for the lightest Te and Ba isotopes only; for isotopes with neutron number N = 78 and 80, the calculated energy levels are too high.

## Context
This work extends the generalized Bohr Hamiltonian approach of earlier studies (e.g., Refs. [2,3] in the paper) which found that microscopic inertial functions were 2 to 3 times too small and required artificial scaling. The authors build on the collective pairing Hamiltonian formalism introduced by Bes and coworkers (Ref. [4]) and later elaborated for realistic cases (Ref. [5]), as well as the axially symmetric treatment of coupling between quadrupole and pairing vibrations (Ref. [6]). The present model enables parameter-free calculations of collective spectra across both neutron-rich and neutron-deficient transitional regions, though it reveals systematic discrepancies for nuclei near the N=78-80 region that point to remaining deficiencies in the treatment of pairing correlations.
