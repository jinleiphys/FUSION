# Impact of chiral hyperonic three-body forces on neutron stars

> We study the effect of the nucleon-nucleon-lambda (NN$Λ$) three-body force on neutron stars. In particular, we consider the NN$Λ$ force recently derived by the Jülich--Bonn--Munich group within the framework of chiral effective field theory at next-to-next-to-leading order. This force, together with realistic nucleon-nucleon, nucleon-nucleon-nucleon and nucleon-hyperon interactions, is used to calculate the equation of state and the structure of neutron stars within the many-body non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach. Our results show that the inclusion of the NN$Λ$ force leads to an equation of state stiff enough such that the resulting neutron star maximum mass is compatible with the largest currently measured ($\sim 2\ M_\odot$) neutron star masses. Using a perturbative many-body approach we calculate also the separation energy of the $Λ$ in some hypernuclei finding that the agreement with the experimental data improves for the heavier ones when the effect of the NN$Λ$ force is taken into account.

## Key claim

The inclusion of the chiral NNΛ three-body force stiffens the equation of state of neutron star matter enough that the predicted maximum neutron star mass becomes compatible with the observed ∼2 M⊙ masses, but only after the force is adjusted to reproduce the empirical Λ single-particle potential at saturation density; the result depends on the specific NΛ two-body interaction model used (NSC97a or NSC97e) and on the chosen value of the rescaling parameter β.

## Method

The authors used the non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) many-body approach to calculate the equation of state of β-stable neutron star matter containing nucleons, Λ hyperons, and leptons. They employed chiral NN and NNN interactions (Piarulli et al. and Epelbaum et al.) together with the Nijmegen NSC97a or NSC97e NΛ meson-exchange potentials, and added the chiral NNΛ three-body force derived by the Jülich-Bonn-Munich group at next-to-next-to-leading order, implemented as an effective density-dependent NΛ interaction. The Λ separation energies in hypernuclei were computed using a perturbative many-body approach to solve the Schrödinger equation for the Λ single-particle states in a finite nucleus.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass with NSC97a+NNΛ₁: 1.96 M⊙; radius: 9.80 km; central baryonic density: 1.30 fm⁻³.
- Maximum neutron star mass with NSC97a+NNΛ₂: 1.97 M⊙; radius: 9.87 km; central baryonic density: 1.28 fm⁻³.
- Maximum neutron star mass with NSC97e+NNΛ₁: 2.01 M⊙; radius: 10.10 km; central baryonic density: 1.20 fm⁻³.
- Maximum neutron star mass with NSC97e+NNΛ₂: 2.02 M⊙; radius: 10.15 km; central baryonic density: 1.19 fm⁻³.
- Without NNΛ force, maximum masses are 1.31 M⊙ (NSC97a) and 1.54 M⊙ (NSC97e).
- Pure nucleonic maximum mass: 2.08 M⊙; radius: 10.26 km; central density: 1.15 fm⁻³.
- Λ separation energy in ⁴¹_ΛCa: NSC97a+NNΛ₁ gives 14.9 MeV, NSC97a+NNΛ₂ gives 13.3 MeV; experimental value for ⁴⁰_ΛCa is 18.7±1.1 MeV.
- Λ separation energy in ²⁰⁹_ΛPb: NSC97a+NNΛ₁ gives 26.8 MeV, NSC97a+NNΛ₂ gives 24.7 MeV; experimental value for ²⁰⁸_ΛPb is 26.9±0.8 MeV.
- The rescaling parameter β ranges from 1.15 (NSC97a+NNΛ₁) to 1.94 (NSC97e+NNΛ₂).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the hyperon puzzle by including a chiral NNΛ three-body force derived within chiral effective field theory, in contrast to earlier phenomenological approaches such as the density-dependent contact terms used by Logoteta et al. (2011) or the multi-Pomeron exchange potential of Yamamoto et al. (2013-2016). The results support the conclusion of Lonardoni et al. (2015) that hyperonic three-body forces can reconcile neutron star masses with observations, but differ from that work by finding that Λ hyperons remain present in the core of a 2 M⊙ star. The calculations enable future studies incorporating additional hyperon species (Σ, Ξ) and their three-body forces, and may inform gravitational-wave signatures of neutron star mergers detectable by next-generation observatories like the Einstein Telescope.
