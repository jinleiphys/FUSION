# Hadron spectra, flow and correlations in PbPb collisions at the LHC: interplay between soft and hard physics

> The started LHC heavy ion program makes it possible to probe new frontiers of the high temperature Quantum Chromodynamics. It is expected that the role of hard and semi-hard particle production processes may be significant at ultra-high energies even for bulk properties of the created matter. In this paper, the LHC data on multiplicity, hadron spectra, elliptic flow and femtoscopic correlations from PbPb collisions at center-of-mass energy 2.76 TeV per nucleon pair are analyzed in the framework of the HYDJET++ model. The influence of the jet production mechanism on these observables is discussed.

# Hadron spectra, flow and correlations in PbPb collisions at the LHC: interplay between soft and hard physics

## Key claim

The HYDJET++ model, combining a thermal soft component with a hard component from in-medium parton fragmentation, can simultaneously reproduce LHC data on multiplicity, hadron spectra, elliptic flow, and femtoscopic correlations in PbPb collisions at 2.76 TeV, but only if the hard (jet) component contributes about 25% of the total multiplicity in central collisions, a significantly larger fraction than the ~15% estimated at RHIC energies.

## Method

The authors used the HYDJET++ event generator, which combines a soft "thermal" hadronic state generated from a parametrized hydrodynamic freeze-out surface (via the FAST MC approach) with a hard component based on the PYQUEN model for partonic energy loss in a quark-gluon plasma. The soft component parameters (chemical freeze-out temperature 165 MeV, thermal freeze-out temperature 105 MeV, maximal transverse flow rapidity 1.265, and freeze-out geometry parameters) were tuned to reproduce hadron yields, spectra, and correlation radii. The hard component used PYTHIA 6.4 with tune Pro-Q20 for initial parton spectra, with nuclear shadowing corrections and medium-induced radiative and collisional energy loss (initial temperature 1 GeV, formation time 0.1 fm/c). The model was compared to published ALICE and CMS data for PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV across multiple observables.

## Key numbers

- Hard component contributes ~25% of total multiplicity at mid-rapidity in most central PbPb collisions (versus ~15% at RHIC), corresponding to minimum transverse momentum transfer p_T^min = 8.2 GeV/c
- Chemical freeze-out temperature T_ch = 165 MeV; thermal freeze-out temperature T_th = 105 MeV; maximal transverse flow rapidity Y_T^max = 1.265
- Maximal longitudinal flow rapidity Y_L^max = 3.5–4.5; freeze-out transverse radius R_f(b=0) = 13.45 fm; proper time tau_f(b=0) = 12.2 fm/c; emission duration Delta tau_f(b=0) = 2 fm/c
- Initial maximal temperature T_0^max = 1 GeV; QGP formation time tau_0 = 0.1 fm/c; number of active flavors N_f = 0 (gluon-dominated plasma)
- Calculated hadron ratios at 0–5% centrality: K^+/pi^+ = 0.153 (experimental 0.155 ± 0.012), p^+/pi^+ = 0.065 (experimental 0.046 ± 0.004)
- Multiplicities of hard and soft components become comparable at p_T ~ 4.5 GeV/c
- Nuclear modification factor R_AA reproduced up to p_T ~ 100 GeV/c in 5% most central collisions
- Elliptic flow v_2 reproduced up to p_T ~ 5 GeV/c and 40% centrality
- Pseudorapidity variation of multiplicity less than 10% in |eta| < 2.5

## Context

This work extends the HYDJET++ model, previously applied to AuAu collisions at RHIC at 200 GeV (Lokhtin 2008), to LHC energies. It demonstrates that the increased contribution of hard processes at LHC energies significantly affects bulk observables including multiplicity, spectra, elliptic flow, and femtoscopic radii. The results are compared with data from ALICE (Aamodt 2010, 2011) and CMS (Chatrchyan 2011, 2012) collaborations. The study complements recent work with the EPOS model (Werner 2012) on jet-soft hadron interactions, and contrasts with full 3D hydrodynamics models (Sinyukov 2010, Bozek 2011) and hydro-kinetic models (Karpenko 2012) that include detailed hydrodynamic evolution and hadronic cascades but are computationally expensive. The HYDJET++ approach enables fast Monte Carlo simulation while still reproducing the key observables, making it suitable for experimental setup studies.
