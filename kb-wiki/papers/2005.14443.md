# Neutron star equation of state and tidal deformability with nuclear energy density functionals

> Neutron star is the ultimate testing place for the physics of dense nuclear matter. Before the detection of gravitational waves from the merger of binary neutron stars, various nuclear equations of state have been used to estimate the macroscopic properties of neutron stars, such as masses and radii, based on the electromagnetic observations. However, recent observations on the tidal deformability of neutron star from the gravitational waves GW170817 opened a new era of multi-messenger astronomy and astrophysics, and many theoretical works have been extended to estimate the tidal deformability of neutron stars. In this article, we review our recent works on the application of nuclear energy density functionals to the properties of neutron stars including tidal deformability. We found that many nuclear energy density functionals, including new KIDS (Korea: IBS-Daegu-Sungkyunkwan) model, satisfy both constraints from current electromagnetic and gravitational wave observations. We discuss future possibilities of constraining nuclear matter equation of state from ground-based experiments and multi-messenger observations.

## Key claim

Many nuclear energy density functionals, including the new KIDS model, satisfy both constraints from current electromagnetic observations (X-ray burst data and the existence of ~2 solar mass neutron stars) and gravitational wave observations (the tidal deformability upper limit from GW170817), but the paper hedges that the validity of these equations of state can be further tested by future observations with less uncertainties.

## Method

The authors adopt the KIDS (Korea: IBS-Daegu-Sungkyunkwan) energy density functional, which expands the energy per particle in homogeneous nuclear matter in powers of the cubic root of density, with parameters fit to symmetric nuclear matter properties and the equation of state of pure neutron matter. They transform the KIDS EDF to a Skyrme-type in-medium nuclear potential and solve the Hartree-Fock equation to obtain nuclear properties for finite nuclei. They then compute neutron star masses, radii, tidal Love numbers, and dimensionless tidal deformabilities by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations and the tidal perturbation equations for several nuclear models including KIDS, GSkI, SLy4, SkI4, SGI, MS1, and MS1b.

## Key numbers

- KIDS model predicts saturation density ρ0 = 0.160 fm⁻³, binding energy E/A = 16.00 MeV, incompressibility K0 = 240.0 MeV, skewness Q0 = -372.7 MeV, symmetry energy J = 32.8 MeV, symmetry energy slope L = 49.1 MeV, and symmetry energy curvature Kτ = -375.1 MeV.
- The most massive neutron star in neutron star-white dwarf binaries has M = 2.14⁺⁰·²⁰₋₀·₁₈ M⊙, setting the lower limit of maximum neutron star mass.
- For the KIDS model, the binding energy per nucleon deviation from experiment is 0.06% for ⁴⁰Ca, 0.02% for ¹³²Sn, and 0.17% for ²⁰⁸Pb; charge radius deviation is 0.01% for ⁴⁰Ca, 0.01% for ¹³²Sn, and 0.23% for ²⁰⁸Pb.
- The largest difference in dimensionless tidal deformability Λ at M = 1.4 M⊙ among models consistent with GW170817 is about a factor of two.
- The upper limit of Λ from GW170817 for M = 1.4 M⊙ is Λ₁.₄ = 190⁺³⁹⁰₋₁₂₀ (revised estimate from LSC18).
- For GW190425, the tidal deformability constraint is Λ̃ ≤ 600 with low-spin prior and Λ̃ ≤ 1100 with high-spin prior.

## Context

This work extends the KIDS energy density functional approach, previously developed for finite nuclei (KIDSprc97, prc99, prc100), to neutron star properties including tidal deformability, and compares it with other Skyrme force models (GSkI, SLy4, SkI4, SGI) and relativistic mean field models (MS1, MS1b). The paper finds that KIDS, GSkI, and SLy4 are consistent with both X-ray observations (Steiner 2010) and the tidal deformability bound from GW170817, while MS1 and MS1b are excluded. The work enables future constraints on the nuclear equation of state from multi-messenger observations including NICER (Riley 2019, Miller 2019) and upcoming rare isotope facilities such as RAON in Korea.
