# Graphene as a Lattice Field Theory

> We introduce effective field theories for the electronic properties of graphene in terms of relativistic fermions propagating in 2+1 dimensions, and outline how strong inter-electron interactions may be modelled by numerical simulation of a lattice field theory. For strong enough coupling an insulating state can form via condensation of particle-hole pairs, and it is demonstrated that this is a theoretical possibility for monolayer graphene. For bilayer graphene the effect of an interlayer bias voltage can be modelled by the introduction of a chemical potential (akin to isopsin chemical potential in QCD) with no accompanying sign problem; simulations reveal the presence of strong interactions among the residual degrees of freedom at the resulting Fermi surface, which is disrupted by an excitonic condensate. We also present preliminary results for the quasiparticle dispersion, which permit direct estimates of both the Fermi momentum and the induced gap.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a quantum critical point (QCP) exists for monolayer graphene at finite coupling, making a Mott insulating phase a theoretical possibility for suspended graphene samples; however, later simulations with a more realistic inter-electron potential suggest the critical coupling may be slightly too strong for the insulating phase to be observed in practice.

## Method
The authors use a lattice field theory approach based on staggered fermions on a cubic lattice, with an instantaneous Coulomb potential modeled by a bosonic field coupled to the fermion density. For monolayer graphene, they simulate the chiral order parameter as a function of coupling strength and fermion flavor number to locate the QCP. For bilayer graphene, they introduce an interlayer bias voltage modeled as an isospin chemical potential, which avoids the sign problem, and measure carrier density, exciton condensate, and quasiparticle dispersion via Monte Carlo simulations on 32^3 and 48^3 lattices.

## Key numbers
- Critical flavor number for the strong-coupling limit: N_fc = 4.8(2)
- Critical coupling for N_f=2 (monolayer value): g_c^{-2} = 0.609(2)
- Critical exponent δ for N_f=2: δ = 2.66(3), compared with δ(N_fc) = 5.5(3)
- Carrier density power law in bilayer model: n_c(j=0) ∝ μ^{3.32(1)}
- Exciton condensate power law: ⟨ΨΨ(j=0)⟩ ∝ μ^{2.39(2)}
- Fermi momentum estimate from dispersion: k_F a ≈ 0.4 at μ a = 0.2
- Gap estimates from dispersion: Δ a(μ a=0.1) ≈ 0.1, Δ a(μ a=0.2) ≈ 0.3
- Directly measured carrier density at μ a=0.4: n_c a^2 ≈ 0.09, compared with free lattice fermion value of 0.06

## Context
This work extends lattice field theory methods from particle physics (previously applied to the Thirring model and QCD) to the electronic properties of graphene. The monolayer results are consistent with earlier braneworld gauge theory simulations by Drut and Lähde, and with honeycomb lattice simulations by Ulybyshev et al. (2013) that found the critical coupling may be too strong for a Mott insulator in suspended samples. The bilayer model introduces a new simulable system with chemical potential, distinct from the NJL model (Hands, 2004) and two-color QCD (Cotter, 2012), where the gap Δ scales with μ rather than being set by an ultraviolet scale. The results enable future direct comparison with diagrammatic approaches predicting Δ/μ ∼ 10^{-7} (Kharitonov).
