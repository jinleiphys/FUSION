# The production of photons in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> In this work it is shown that the use of a hydrodynamical model of heavy ion collisions which incorporates recent developments, together with updated photon emission rates greatly improves agreement with both ALICE and PHENIX measurements of direct photons, supporting the idea that thermal photons are the dominant source of direct photon momentum anisotropy. The event-by-event hydrodynamical model uses IP-Glasma initial states and includes, for the first time, both shear and bulk viscosities, along with second order couplings between the two viscosities. The effect of both shear and bulk viscosities on the photon rates is studied, and those transport coefficients are shown to have measurable consequences on the photon momentum anisotropy.

## Key claim

The paper shows that an up-to-date hydrodynamical model incorporating both shear and bulk viscosities, together with updated photon emission rates, greatly improves agreement with ALICE and PHENIX direct photon measurements, supporting the idea that thermal photons are the dominant source of direct photon momentum anisotropy. The authors qualify that this agreement is achieved specifically when using the IP-Glasma initial state and including second-order couplings between shear and bulk viscosities for the first time.

## Method

The authors performed event-by-event (2+1)D viscous hydrodynamical simulations of Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies and Au-Au collisions at RHIC energies, using IP-Glasma initial conditions with a proper time of 0.4 fm. The hydrodynamic evolution included both shear and bulk viscosities with second-order relaxation-type equations, followed by a Cooper-Frye hadronization stage and UrQMD afterburner for hadronic interactions. Thermal photon emission rates were computed from the QGP phase using leading-order partonic rates and from the hadronic phase using meson gas rates including recent calculations of pi-pi bremsstrahlung and pi-rho-omega reactions, with viscous corrections applied to the photon rates for both shear and bulk sectors.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio used: eta/s = 0.095, obtained from global fits to ALICE and CMS hadron data
- Bulk viscosity to entropy density ratio peaks at zeta/s(T_peak) ~ 0.3, with T_peak = 180 MeV
- Initial hydrodynamical starting time: tau_0 = 0.4 fm
- Average number of binary collisions at RHIC (Au-Au, sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV): 793 for 0-20% centrality, 323 for 20-40% centrality
- Average number of binary collisions at LHC (Pb-Pb, sqrt(s_NN)=2760 GeV): 1231 for 0-20% centrality, 501 for 20-40% centrality
- Perturbative QCD calculations of prompt photons use scale Q = lambda * p_T^gamma, with lambda varied from 1/2 to 8
- Next-to-leading order photon rates are approximately 20% larger than leading-order rates for relevant strong coupling values

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamical modeling approach of Ryu et al. (2015) for hadronic observables to direct photon production, addressing the long-standing tension between earlier hydrodynamical calculations (Dion et al., 2011; Chatterjee et al., 2013; Shen et al., 2013) and experimental measurements of direct photon spectra and momentum anisotropy. The inclusion of both shear and bulk viscosity corrections to photon emission rates, along with updated hadronic photon rates from Heffernan et al. (2014) and Holt et al. (2015), enables a more complete description of thermal photon production. This work enables future efforts to identify and constrain alternative photon production mechanisms beyond thermal emission.
