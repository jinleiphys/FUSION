# Primordial Helium And the Cosmic Background Radiation

> The products of primordial nucleosynthesis and the cosmic microwave background (CMB) photons are relics from the early evolution of the Universe whose observations probe the standard model of cosmology and provide windows on new physics beyond the standard models of cosmology and of particle physics. In the standard, hot big bang cosmology, long before any stars have formed a significant fraction (~25%) of the baryonic mass in the Universe should be in the form of helium-4 nuclei. Since current 4He observations are restricted to low redshift regions where stellar nucleosynthesis has occurred, observations of high redshift, prestellar 4He would constitute a fundamental test of the hot, big bang cosmology. At recombination, long after big bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) has ended, the temperature anisotropy spectrum imprinted on the CMB depends on the 4He abundance through its connection to the electron density and the effect of the electron density on Silk damping. Since the relic abundance of 4He is relatively insensitive to the universal density of baryons, but is sensitive to a non-standard, early Universe expansion rate, the primordial mass fraction of 4He, Yp, offers a test of the consistency of the standard models of BBN and the CMB and, provides constraints on non-standard physics. Here, the WMAP seven year data (supplemented by other CMB experiments), which lead to an indirect determination of Yp at high redshift, are compared to the BBN predictions and to the independent, direct observations of 4He in low redshift, extragalactic HII regions. At present, given the very large uncertainties in the CMB-determined primordial 4He abundance (as well as for the helium abundances inferred from H II region observations), any differences between the BBN predictions and the CMB observations are small, at a level < 1.5 sigma.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the primordial helium-4 mass fraction (Yp) derived from the WMAP seven-year CMB data (supplemented by ACBAR and QUaD) is consistent with both standard BBN predictions and direct observations of helium in low-redshift HII regions, but only within the current large uncertainties; any differences between these determinations are at a level less than 1.5 sigma.

## Method

The author compares three independent determinations of the primordial helium-4 mass fraction: (1) predictions from standard BBN (SBBN) using either the primordial deuterium abundance from QSO absorption line systems or the WMAP baryon density; (2) predictions from non-standard BBN using the WMAP seven-year data combined with large-scale structure data (BAO or LRG) and the Hubble constant to constrain the effective number of neutrino species N_eff; and (3) a direct CMB determination of Yp from the WMAP power spectrum supplemented by small-scale CMB experiments (ACBAR and QUaD), which probes Yp through its effect on Silk damping and the electron density at recombination. These are compared to direct observations of helium in low-metallicity extragalactic HII regions from Izotov et al. (2010) and Aver et al. (2010).

## Key numbers

- SBBN-predicted Yp using the deuterium-derived baryon density: Yp(SBBN,D) = 0.2482 ± 0.0007
- SBBN-predicted Yp using the WMAP baryon density: Yp(SBBN,WMAP) = 0.2488 ± 0.0006
- WMAP+BAO+H0 determination of Delta N_nu (deviation from 3 neutrino species): 1.30 (+0.86, -0.88)
- WMAP+LRG+H0 determination of Delta N_nu: 1.21 (+0.76, -0.80)
- BBN-predicted Yp for WMAP+BAO+H0: 0.2649 (+0.0099, -0.0108)
- BBN-predicted Yp for WMAP+LRG+H0: 0.2639 (+0.0088, -0.0098)
- CMB-determined Yp from WMAP+ACBAR+QUaD: 0.326 ± 0.075
- Direct HII region Yp from Izotov et al. (2010): 0.2565 ± 0.0060 (statistical and systematic errors combined linearly)
- Direct HII region Yp from Aver et al. (2010): 0.2528 ± 0.0028
- Difference between CMB Yp and SBBN+WMAP Yp: 0.077 ± 0.075 (consistent with zero at ~1.0 sigma)
- Difference between CMB Yp and WMAP+BAO+H0 BBN-predicted Yp: 0.061 ± 0.076 (consistent with zero at ~0.8 sigma)

## Context

This work extends the long-standing program of comparing BBN predictions with observations of light element abundances, as reviewed by Steigman (2007) and others. It specifically addresses the tension between the SBBN-predicted Yp (~0.248) and the higher values inferred from HII regions by Izotov et al. (2010) and Aver et al. (2010) (~0.253-0.257), as well as the even higher CMB-based determination from the WMAP team (2010). The paper shows that all these values are currently consistent within their large uncertainties, and anticipates that the Planck experiment will reduce uncertainties in Yp by a factor of 5-7 (to ~0.011-0.014) and in Delta N_nu by a factor of ~3 (to ~0.26), enabling a definitive test of the standard cosmological model.
