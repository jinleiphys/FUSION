# Coulomb Effect: A Possible Probe for the Evolution of Hadronic Matter

> Electromagnetic field produced in high-energy heavy-ion collisions contains much useful information, because the field can be directly related to the motion of the matter in the whole stage of the reaction. One can divide the total electromagnetic field into three parts, i.e., the contributions from the incident nuclei, non-participating nucleons and charged fluid, the latter consisting of strongly interacting hadrons or quarks. Parametrizing the space-time evolution of the charged fluid based on hydrodynamic model, we study the development of the electromagnetic field which accompanies the high-energy heavy-ion collisions. We found that the incident nuclei bring a rather strong electromagnetic field to the interaction region of hadrons or quarks over a few fm after the collision. On the other hand, the observed charged hadrons' spectra are mostly affected (Coulomb effect) by the field of the charged fluid. We compare the result of our model with experimental data and found that the model reproduces them well. The pion yield ratio pi^-/pi+ at a RHIC energy, Au+Au 100+100 GeV/nucleon, is also predicted.

## Key claim

The electromagnetic field produced in high-energy heavy-ion collisions can be divided into contributions from incident nuclei, non-participating (spectator) nucleons, and the charged fluid (hadronic or quark matter); the observed charged hadron spectra are mostly affected by the Coulomb field of the charged fluid, and the model reproduces experimental data well under the assumption of a longitudinally scaling hydrodynamic expansion without transverse expansion.

## Method

The authors used a Glauber model to compute participant and spectator nucleon numbers and their transverse distributions for Pb+Pb collisions at 158 GeV/nucleon. They approximated the space-time evolution of the charged fluid using a longitudinal scaling (Bjorken) solution with no transverse expansion, parametrized the net charge distribution in rapidity using experimental data from NA49, and computed the Liénard-Wiechert potentials and electromagnetic fields from the fluid, incident nuclei, and penetrating spectator systems. They then solved the classical equation of motion for charged pions starting from a freeze-out surface determined by a critical density condition at T=140 MeV.

## Key numbers

- For Pb+Pb at 158 GeV/nucleon with centrality 5% (impact parameter b ≤ 3.5 fm): participant nucleon number N_part = 353.1, penetrating nucleon number N_pen = 30.7 per hemisphere.
- For Pb+Pb at 158 GeV/nucleon with centrality 15% (b ≤ 6.1 fm): N_part = 297.7, N_pen = 58.4 per hemisphere.
- Net negative charge excess in mesons for centrality 5%: N_{h- - h+} ≈ 10.0.
- Net baryon number N_{B-Bbar} = 352 ± 12, net proton number N_{p-pbar} = 148.9, net Lambda number N_{Lambda - Lambdabar} = 27.4 for centrality 5% (from NA49 data).
- Freeze-out temperature T_f = 140 MeV, critical freeze-out density n_f = 0.096 fm^{-3}.
- Maximum transverse electric field E_rho ≈ 7.5 MeV/fm reached at z ≈ 0 fm, rho ≈ R_Pb, t ≈ 1 fm, giving maximum electromagnetic energy density ε_max ≈ 0.3 MeV/fm^3.
- Width of the electric field peak from incident nuclei: Δτ ≈ 3 fm.
- Total pion multiplicity (including π^0) is 1950 for centrality 5% and 1640 for centrality 15%.
- Predicted pion yield ratio π^-/π^+ for Au+Au at 100+100 GeV/nucleon at RHIC.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of Coulomb effects in heavy-ion collisions at Bevalac energies by Benenson (1979), Koonin (1979), Bertsch (1980), and Gyulassy (1981) to higher SPS energies, and builds on hydrodynamic models by Landau and Bjorken (1983). It improves upon the treatment by Barz (1998) by explicitly including the electromagnetic fields from incident nuclei and penetrating spectator systems. The approach enables using the Coulomb distortion of charged pion spectra as a probe of the space-time evolution of the hadronic or quark matter, and provides a prediction for the π^-/π^+ ratio at RHIC energies that can be tested by future experiments.
