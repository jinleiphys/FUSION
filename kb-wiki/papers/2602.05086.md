# Neglecting correlations leads to misestimated model errors in EFT predictions

> Bayesian analyses of the convergence pattern of Effective Field Theories (EFTs) enable estimation of the uncertainty induced by a truncated expansion. When an EFT that has been calibrated to data is used to make a prediction this truncation uncertainty enters the posterior predictive distribution twice: directly from the finite-order calculation of the predicted quantity and indirectly through the posterior probability distributions of the EFT low-energy constants (LECs) determined by the calibration. In this work, we focus on the interplay of these two sources of uncertainty. We do this in the context of a toy EFT that we fit to pseudodata and use to make predictions. Direct EFT truncation uncertainty and LEC uncertainty are correlated in predictions when the predicted quantity is correlated with the observables used to fit the LECs. Here this results in the overall theoretical uncertainty in the EFT prediction being smaller than either the uncertainty induced by the truncation error or that stemming from the LECs alone.

## Key claim

When an EFT prediction includes both parametric uncertainty (from LEC calibration) and truncation uncertainty (from the finite-order expansion), these two sources are anti-correlated if the predicted quantity is correlated with the observables used to fit the LECs; neglecting this anti-correlation and adding the uncertainties in quadrature overestimates the total theoretical uncertainty, which in the toy model studied is actually smaller than either individual uncertainty alone.

## Method

The authors employ a toy EFT where the "true" underlying function is \(g(x) = [1/2 + \tan(\pi x/2)]^2\), approximated by a Taylor expansion truncated at order \(k=3\) with a fourth-order term treated as the truncation error. They generate pseudodata at 10 equally spaced points in \(x\) between 0 and \(1/\pi\) by adding 5% Gaussian noise to \(g(x)\), then perform Bayesian parameter estimation for the polynomial coefficients (LECs) using a Gaussian likelihood and a Gaussian naturalness prior with hyperparameter \(\bar{a}=5\), sampling the posterior via MCMC with the emcee sampler (500 burn-in steps, 10,000 steps, 50 walkers). The posterior predictive distribution for \(g(x)\) is then computed both with and without retaining the correlations between the LECs and the higher-order coefficient \(a_{k+1}\).

## Key numbers

* The toy data set \(D_1\) consists of 10 points with \(x\) values from \(0.05\pi/2\) to \(0.50\pi/2\) and corresponding \(y\) values from 0.31694 to 1.0684, each with a 5% experimental uncertainty (e.g., \(\sigma = 0.0534\) at the largest \(x\)).
* The true coefficients of the underlying function are \(a_0 = 0.25\), \(a_1 = 1.65\), \(a_2 = 2.99\), \(a_3 = 0.32\), and \(a_4 = 0.06\).
* For the \(k=3\), \(k'=4\) fit, the posterior median and 68% interval for \(a_0\) is \(0.25^{+0.02}_{-0.02}\), for \(a_1\) is \(1.65^{+0.45}_{-0.45}\), for \(a_2\) is \(2.99^{+2.3}_{-2.4}\), for \(a_3\) is \(0.32^{+4.4}_{-4.3}\), and for \(a_4\) is \(0.06 \pm 4.9\).
* At \(x \approx 0.25\), the parametric uncertainty is 60% larger than the full uncertainty and the truncation uncertainty is 20% larger than the full uncertainty, demonstrating the anti-correlation.

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian treatment of EFT truncation errors pioneered by Schindler (2008) and Wesolowski (2015), who introduced the toy model and naturalness priors used here. It directly challenges the common practice, seen in Hu (2021) and others, of adding parametric and model uncertainties in quadrature under the assumption of independence. The results show that properly accounting for correlations between LEC uncertainty and truncation uncertainty yields narrower prediction intervals, a finding that parallels similar conclusions about neglected correlations in nuclear symmetry energy studies by Drischler (2020) and p-shell spectra by Maris (2020). This work enables more accurate uncertainty quantification in EFT predictions for nuclear physics, particularly when the predicted observable is kinematically or physically correlated with the calibration data.
