# Density Functional Theory Approach to Nuclear Fission

> The Skyrme nuclear energy density functional theory (DFT) is used to model neutron-induced fission in actinides. This paper focuses on the numerical implementation of the theory. In particular, it reports recent advances in DFT code development on leadership class computers, and presents a detailed analysis of the numerical accuracy of DFT solvers for near-scission calculations.

# Density Functional Theory Approach to Nuclear Fission

## Key claim
The Skyrme nuclear energy density functional theory (DFT) can model neutron-induced fission in actinides, but the numerical accuracy of DFT solvers in the one-center harmonic oscillator basis degrades significantly at the extreme deformations near scission, with truncation errors reaching 2–3 MeV beyond the second fission barrier.

## Method
The nucleus is described in the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) approximation using the SkM* parameterization of the Skyrme interaction for the particle-hole channel and a density-dependent contact interaction for the particle-particle channel. The HFB equations are solved in a one-center harmonic oscillator basis using the DFT solvers HFBTHO (axial and time-reversal symmetry) and HFODD (fully symmetry-unrestricted), with HFBTHO used as a preconditioner for HFODD. The collective space is four-dimensional, spanned by constraints on the axial quadrupole Q20, triaxial Q22, octupole Q30, and hexadecapole Q40 multipole moments.

## Key numbers
- Energy cut-off of E_cut = 60 MeV used to reduce the number of quasiparticles in the density definition.
- Axial quadrupole moments in actinides range from approximately 30 b in the ground state to nearly 600 b at scission for symmetric fission.
- Octupole moments range from 0 to about 70 b^(3/2) for very asymmetric fission.
- Hexadecapole moments range from approximately 3 b^2 near the ground state to more than 350 b^2 for symmetric fission.
- For a 1 b^(λ/2) mesh size, the axial collective space alone contains more than 1.4 million points; adding triaxiality multiplies this by another 2 orders of magnitude.
- Up to 300,000 processors were used in parallel on the Titan supercomputer at Oak Ridge Leadership Computing Facility.
- Restricting N_states to approximately 1000–1200 may lead to 2 to 3 MeV errors beyond the second fission barrier.
- At N_max = 30, the full spherical basis would contain N_states = 5456, implying HFB matrix sizes exceeding 20,000 × 20,000.
- An empirical fit for optimal oscillator frequency is given: ω_0 = 0.1 × Q_20 e^(-0.02 Q_20) + 6.5 MeV for |Q_20| ≤ 30 b, and ω_0 = 8.14 MeV for |Q_20| > 30 b.
- An empirical fit for optimal basis deformation is given: β = 0.05 √(Q_20).

## Context
This work extends the self-consistent nuclear mean-field approach to fission pioneered by Berger (1984) and Berger (1989), which was limited by computing power at the time. The paper addresses numerical accuracy issues that must be resolved before DFT can achieve the predictive power needed for simulating fission of neutron-rich nuclei in the fission-recycling stage of element formation or for next-generation nuclear reactor simulations. The results point to the need for developing bases better adapted to extreme elongations near scission, and enable future work on quantifying uncertainties in fission lifetime calculations, where MeV-level errors can lead to orders-of-magnitude uncertainties.
