# Sudden Hadronization in Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

> We formulate and study a mechanical instability criterion for sudden hadronization of dense matter fireballs formed in 158A GeV Pb--Pb collisions. Considering properties of quark-gluon matter and hadron gas we obtain the phase boundary between these two phases and demonstrate that the required deep QGP supercooling prior to sudden hadronization has occurred.

# Sudden Hadronization in Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

## Key claim

The authors propose that the fireball formed in 158A GeV Pb–Pb collisions undergoes a sudden, explosive hadronization driven by a mechanical instability, which requires deep supercooling of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) prior to breakup; this conclusion depends on the assumption that the hadronizing matter is in chemical nonequilibrium, characterized by an excess of valence quark pair abundances, and the authors note that a chemical equilibrium fit to the same data yields positive pressure and a statistically worse description (chi-squared per degree of freedom greater than 2.5).

## Method

The authors formulate a mechanical instability criterion for sudden fireball breakup by requiring that the momentum flow at the fireball surface vanishes (P → 0), which forces the intrinsic pressure of the expanding matter to become negative. They combine this condition with the Gibbs-Duhem relation to derive a constraint linking the fireball’s energy-to-entropy ratio, temperature, baryochemical potential, and flow velocity, and then compare this constraint to results from a chemical nonequilibrium analysis of experimental hadron abundances from the NA49 and WA97 collaborations. They also compute the equilibrium phase boundary between QGP and hadron gas using a QCD-based equation of state tuned to lattice results, and compare the breakup condition to this boundary to quantify the required supercooling.

## Key numbers

- The equilibrium phase transition temperature at vanishing baryochemical potential is T_pt ≈ 172 MeV; with finite hadron size correction it is T_fp ≈ 166 MeV.
- The supercooled zero-pressure QGP temperature is T_sc = 157 MeV; the deeply supercooled temperature at which mechanical instability occurs is T_dsc ≈ 147 MeV.
- The energy-to-entropy ratio used in the breakup constraint is E/S = 0.184 ± 0.05 GeV.
- The baryon-to-entropy ratio is S/b = 42, and the baryochemical potential is μ_b = 0.2 GeV.
- The shape parameter κ describing the non-spherical flow is in the range 0.1 < κ < 0.6 for Pb–Pb collisions; the analysis uses κ = 0.6.
- The expansion velocity at breakup is v_c = 0.54.
- The latent heat / vacuum pressure is constrained to B ≥ 0.17 GeV/fm^3, obtained from B ≈ 0.2 × 11 × T_h^4 with T_h the hadronization temperature and a ≈ 11 the effective number of degrees of freedom in the QGP energy density.
- The timescale for development of deep supercooling is τ = 2 × 10^{-23} s.

## Context

This work extends the sudden hadronization scenario proposed by Csörgő and Csernai (1994), who required deep supercooling and a first-order phase transition, and contrasts with the droplet-driven reequilibration hadronization picture of Csernai and Kapusta (1992) and Dumitru et al. (1997), in which chemical equilibrium of valence quark pair abundances is maintained and a mixed phase forms. The authors show that their mechanical instability criterion is consistent with the chemical nonequilibrium analysis of Letessier and Rafelski (2000) and that the resulting negative pressure cannot be reproduced by chemical equilibrium fits such as those of Braun-Munzinger, Heppe, and Stachel (1999). The work enables a quantitative test of sudden hadronization via the predicted relation between flow velocity, temperature, and the energy-to-entropy ratio, and it provides a constraint on the vacuum pressure B that can be compared to lattice QCD results.
