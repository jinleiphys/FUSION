# Warm asymmetric quark matter and proto-quark stars within the confined-isospin-density-dependent mass model

> We extend the confined-isospin-density-dependent mass (CIDDM) model to include temperature dependence of the equivalent mass for quarks. Within the CIDDM model, we study the equation of state (EOS) for $β$-equilibrium quark matter, quark symmetry energy, quark symmetry free energy, and the properties of quark stars at finite temperature. We find that including the temperature dependence of the equivalent mass can significantly influence the properties of the strange quark matter (SQM) as well as the quark symmetry energy, the quark symmetry free energy, and the maximum mass of quark stars at finite temperature. The mass-radius relations for different stages of the proto-quark stars (PQSs) along the star evolution are analyzed. Our results indicate that the heating (cooling) process for PQSs will increase (decrease) the maximum mass within CIDDM model by including temperature dependence of the equivalent mass for quarks.

# Warm asymmetric quark matter and proto-quark stars within the confined-isospin-density-dependent mass model

## Key claim

The temperature dependence of the equivalent quark mass, introduced via a temperature-dependent string tension, significantly influences the properties of strange quark matter and the maximum mass of quark stars at finite temperature; however, the heating (cooling) process for proto-quark stars will increase (decrease) the maximum mass only within the CIDDM model when this temperature dependence is included, and the effect depends on the isospin-dependence parameter D_I.

## Method

The authors extended the confined-isospin-density-dependent mass (CIDDM) model by incorporating a temperature-dependent string tension σ(T) into the equivalent quark mass, using the form σ(T)=1−(8T/λT_c)exp(−λT_c/T) with T_c=170 MeV and λ=1.605812. They then solved the thermodynamic potential density for quark matter under beta-equilibrium and charge neutrality conditions, computed the equation of state, and solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equation to obtain mass-radius relations for static quark stars and proto-quark stars at three evolutionary snapshots: (I) S/n_B=1, Y_l=0.4; (II) S/n_B=2, Y_ν_l=0; (III) S/n_B=0, Y_ν_l=0.

## Key numbers

- For parameter set DI-0 (no isospin dependence), the maximum mass of static quark stars increases from 1.65 M_⊙ at zero temperature to 2.417 M_⊙ at T=50 MeV.
- For parameter set DI-85 (isospin-dependent), the maximum mass of static quark stars increases from 2.01 M_⊙ at zero temperature to 2.178 M_⊙ at T=30 MeV; above 30 MeV the EOS violates causality (central baryon density about 1.11 fm^−3).
- For DI-0, the density at the minimum of free energy per baryon for SQM decreases from 0.27 fm^−3 at T=0 to 0.22 fm^−3 at T=30 MeV and then to 0.14 fm^−3 at T=50 MeV.
- For DI-85, the density at the minimum of free energy per baryon for SQM decreases from 0.47 fm^−3 at T=0 to 0.46 fm^−3 at T=30 MeV and then to 0.43 fm^−3 at T=50 MeV.
- For DI-0 along the PQS evolution: maximum mass at stage I is 1.683 M_⊙, at stage II is 1.727 M_⊙, and at stage III (zero temperature) is 1.651 M_⊙.
- For DI-85 along the PQS evolution: maximum mass at stage I is 2.03 M_⊙, at stage II is 2.053 M_⊙, and at stage III is 2.01 M_⊙ (center value of PSR J0348+0432 mass 2.01±0.04 M_⊙).
- The critical temperature for chiral symmetry restoration is T_c=170 MeV, taken from lattice QCD calculations (Fodor, 2002).

## Context

This work extends the CIDDM model previously developed by Chu et al. (2014) for zero-temperature quark matter to finite temperatures, following the approach of Wen et al. (2005) and Zhang et al. (2003) who introduced temperature-dependent string tension into the CDDM model. The results enable studies of proto-quark star evolution along the three-snapshot scheme used by Steiner et al. (2000), Shao et al. (2011), and Menezes et al. (2013), and show that the CIDDM model with DI-85 can describe the massive pulsar PSR J0348+0432 as a quark star while also predicting how its maximum mass changes during heating and cooling stages.
