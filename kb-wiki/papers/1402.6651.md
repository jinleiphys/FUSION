# Numerical Implementation of lepton-nucleus interactions and its effect on neutrino oscillation analysis

> We discuss the implementation of the nuclear model based on realistic nuclear spectral functions in the GENIE neutrino interaction generator. Besides improving on the Fermi gas description of the nuclear ground state, our scheme involves a new prescription for $Q^2$ selection, meant to efficiently enforce energy momentum conservation. The results of our simulations, validated through comparison to electron scattering data, have been obtained for a variety of target nuclei, ranging from carbon to argon, and cover the kinematical region in which quasi elastic scattering is the dominant reaction mechanism. We also analyse the influence of the adopted nuclear model on the determination of neutrino oscillation parameters.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that implementing a nuclear spectral function model (beyond the relativistic Fermi gas model) in the GENIE neutrino interaction generator, together with a new \(Q^2\) selection prescription that enforces energy-momentum conservation, significantly improves agreement with electron scattering data and affects the extraction of neutrino oscillation parameters, though the spectral functions for calcium and argon are less reliable than those for carbon and oxygen.

## Method

The authors implemented nuclear spectral functions from the local-density approximation (LDA) for carbon and oxygen, and from a simpler model (GSF) for calcium and argon, into the GENIE 2.8.0 event generator via custom modules (the \(\nu T\) package). They sampled nucleon momentum and removal energy from the two-dimensional probability distribution \(4\pi p^2 P(p,E)\) and introduced a new \(Q^2\) selection that uses the reduced energy transfer \(\widetilde{\omega}\) (accounting for spectator excitation) and imposes the quasielastic condition \((p+q)^2 = m^2\) to enforce energy-momentum conservation. The simulations were validated by comparing computed double-differential electron-nucleus cross sections to experimental data for carbon, oxygen, calcium, and argon, and then applied to neutrino-nucleus scattering and oscillation parameter extraction.

## Key numbers

* Electron-oxygen cross section at \(E_e = 0.88\) GeV and \(\theta_{e'} = 32^\circ\): the spectral-function calculation reproduces the position and width of the quasielastic bump with no adjustable parameters and no final-state interactions (FSI) modeling.
* Electron-oxygen cross section at \(E_e = 1.20\) GeV and \(\theta_{e'} = 32^\circ\): similarly good agreement with data from Anghinolfi et al. (1996, 1995).
* Electron-carbon cross section at \(E_e = 0.961\) GeV and \(\theta_e = 37.5^\circ\): GENIE 2.8.0+\(\nu T\) (with spectral function) reproduces the data, while GENIE 2.8.0 (with relativistic Fermi gas model) fails to predict both position and width.
* Electron-calcium cross section at \(E_e = 0.841\) GeV and \(\theta_e = 45.5^\circ\): spectral-function results (using GSF) show reasonable agreement with data from Williamson et al. (1997).
* Electron-argon cross section at \(E_e = 0.700\) GeV and \(\theta_e = 32^\circ\): spectral-function results (using GSF) are compared to data from Anghinolfi et al. (1995).
* For neutrino-oxygen scattering at \(E_\nu = 1\) GeV, the differential cross section \(d\sigma/dQ^2\) is computed using the spectral function with Pauli blocking and compared to the relativistic Fermi gas model with and without Pauli blocking.
* The axial mass parameter used in the dipole form of the axial form factor is \(M_A = 1.03\) MeV (from Bernard et al., 2001).

## Context

This work extends the spectral-function approach of Benhar et al. (2005, 2006) and Ankowski et al. (2007) by implementing it in the widely-used GENIE event generator, directly addressing the inadequacy of the relativistic Fermi gas model exposed by experiments such as MiniBooNE and K2K. The improved nuclear model enables more reliable simulations of neutrino-nucleus interactions for current and future oscillation experiments (e.g., T2K, NOvA, DUNE), and the authors show that the choice of nuclear model affects the determination of oscillation parameters. The paper also highlights the need for better spectral functions for calcium and argon, as the LDA procedure cannot currently be applied to those nuclei due to scarce \((e,e'p)\) data.
