# Liquid-Gas phase transition in nuclei

> This review article takes stock of the progress made in understanding the phase transition in hot nuclei and highlights the coherence of observed signatures

## Key claim

This review argues that the liquid-gas phase transition in hot nuclei, originally predicted for infinite nuclear matter, can be studied in finite nuclear systems produced in heavy-ion collisions, but that finite-size effects, surface effects, and Coulomb forces significantly modify the signatures of the transition, reducing the critical temperature by about 7 MeV for systems with A = 50–400.

## Method

The authors review theoretical and experimental work on the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition, covering the equation of state of symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter using Skyrme effective interactions and finite-temperature Hartree-Fock theory, as well as dynamical simulations of heavy-ion collisions. They examine how statistical mechanics must be extended for finite, non-additive systems where ensemble inequivalence can occur, and they survey experimental signatures of the transition including multifragmentation, spinodal instabilities, and isospin distillation.

## Key numbers

- Critical point for infinite symmetric nuclear matter: ρ_c ≈ 0.3–0.4 ρ_0 and T_c ≈ 16–18 MeV
- Normal nuclear saturation density: ρ_0 = 0.155 ± 0.005 nucleons fm⁻³
- Spinodal instability growth times: 30–50 fm/c (30 fm/c = 10⁻²² s) depending on density (ρ_0/2 to ρ_0/8) and temperature (0–9 MeV)
- For finite nuclei (A = 50–400), finite-size effects reduce critical temperature by 2–6 MeV and Coulomb forces by a further 1–3 MeV, giving a total reduction of about 7 MeV and a "critical" temperature of about 10 MeV
- Multifragmentation is the dominant decay mode for excitation energies between about 3A and 10A MeV
- Symmetry energy parameter C ≈ 32 MeV in the parabolic approximation
- Dynamical simulations of central Sn+Sn collisions at 50 MeV per nucleon incident energy used impact parameter b = 2 fm

## Context

This review extends the original suggestion by Bertsch and Siemens (1983) that the nuclear interaction, similar to van der Waals forces, should lead to a liquid-gas phase transition in nuclei, and that multifragmentation may result from spinodal instabilities. It builds on extensive theoretical work on finite-size effects by Gross (2001), Chomaz (2004), and others, and on experimental studies using multidetector arrays. The review enables future work to connect observed signatures of the phase transition across different observables and to constrain the density dependence of the symmetry energy from nuclear structure measurements, heavy-ion collisions, and astronomical observations.
