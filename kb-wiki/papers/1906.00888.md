# Using bottomonium production as a tomographic probe of the quark-gluon plasma

> The suppression of bottomonia in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions is a smoking gun for the production of a strongly interacting final state in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. Furthermore, these final state interactions are consistent with the production of a hot hydrodynamically expanding quark-gluon plasma with initial temperatures on the order of 600-700 MeV at LHC collision energies. Models which incorporate plasma screening and bound-state breakup based on high-temperature quantum chromodynamics are in good agreement with the centrality, transverse-momentum, and rapidity dependence of the suppression seen in the highest energy LHC Pb-Pb collisions. The models of heavy-quark bound state breakup/formation are coupled to the soft dynamics of the quark-gluon plasma using 3+1d relativistic anisotropic hydrodynamics. At later times, excited state feed-down is taken into account using independently constrained excited state feed down fractions. Comparisons with LHC experimental data are consistent with primordial suppression of all bottomonium states, with states having the lowest binding energies being the ones which suffer the most suppression. In this proceedings contribution, I will review recent work to (a) include the effects of regeneration on bottomonium production and (b) to assess the effects of a rapidly changing in-medium potential on bottomonium production in the quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim

Bottomonium suppression in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions is consistent with the production of a hot hydrodynamically expanding quark-gluon plasma with initial temperatures on the order of 600-700 MeV at LHC collision energies, but the adiabatic approximation used in standard models may introduce errors of up to 18% for excited states like the Upsilon(2s) when compared to full real-time evolution.

## Method

The author couples models of heavy-quark bound state breakup and formation to the soft dynamics of the quark-gluon plasma using 3+1d relativistic anisotropic hydrodynamics, which resums an infinite number of terms in the fluid's inverse Reynolds number to describe early-time non-equilibrium dynamics. The in-medium heavy-quark potential is taken as complex-valued, with the real part derived from the internal energy of the heavy quark-antiquark system and the imaginary part from leading-order perturbation theory. At later times, excited state feed-down is included using independently constrained feed-down fractions. For the non-adiabatic treatment, the time-dependent Schrodinger equation is solved numerically using a split-operator method with Fourier sine transforms.

## Key numbers

* Initial QGP temperatures on the order of 600-700 MeV at LHC collision energies (from abstract, consistent with Upsilon(1s) survival to 600-700 MeV)
* Pseudo-critical QCD transition temperature T_pc approximately 155 MeV (from lattice gauge theory, cited as Bazavov (2013) and Borsanyi (2016))
* Upsilon(1s) and J/Psi survive to temperatures of approximately 600-700 MeV and 300-400 MeV respectively (cited from Andronic (2015), Mocsy (2013), Brambilla (2010))
* Rest frame formation times for Upsilon(1s), Upsilon(2s), Upsilon(3s), chi_b1, and chi_b2: 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.4, and 0.6 fm/c respectively
* Bottom quark mass m_b = 4.7 GeV, potential parameters a = 0.385, sigma = 0.223 GeV^2 (from Petreczky (2010))
* For a low initial temperature T_0 = 0.225 GeV, the Upsilon(2s) correction from non-adiabatic evolution can be as large as 18%, with the real-time evolution giving enhanced suppression
* For initial temperature T_0 = 0.6 GeV at tau_0 = 1 GeV^{-1}, the correction to adiabatic evolution for Upsilon(1s) is small (quantitatively on the order of a few percent)

## Context

This work extends the seminal predictions of Matsui and Satz (1986) and Karsch, Matsui, and Satz (1987) that heavy quarkonia would melt in the quark-gluon plasma, using modern effective field theory methods and complex in-medium potentials from Laine (2006), Dumitru (2007), and Brambilla (2008). The KSUa model described here provides good agreement with CMS and ALICE data at LHC energies but under-predicts Upsilon(1s) suppression at RHIC energies compared to preliminary STAR data. The non-adiabatic treatment (KSUd) from Boyd (2019) represents a first step beyond the adiabatic approximation, and the author notes ongoing work to extend the real-time evolution treatment to 3+1d for full phenomenological impact.
