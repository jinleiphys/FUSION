# Deformation effects on the surface neutron densities of stable S and Ni isotopes probed by proton elastic scattering via isotopic analysis

> To extract structure information from proton elastic scattering off S isotopes at 320 MeV and Ni isotopes at $E_p=180$ MeV, this study proposes isotopic analyses combining nuclear structure and reaction calculations. The isotonic analysis was repeated on $^{48}$Ca and $^{50}$Ti. The structure calculations were performed by using the spherical and deformed Relativistic Hartree--Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations, and the spherical nonrelativistic Skyrme Hartree--Fock--Bogoliubov calculations. The $(p,p)$ reactions were calculated using the relativistic impulse approximation (RIA) assuming the theoretical densities of target nuclei. The RIA calculations using the target densities obtained by the RHB calculation, along with the density-dependent point-coupling interactions reasonably reproduced the $(p,p)$ cross sections in the studied mass number region. The nuclear structure and $(p,p)$ reactions were analyzed in detail; especially, the effects of deformation on the isotopic systematics of the surface densities and $(p,p)$ cross sections were clarified. The deformation effects were found to be essential to describe the isotopic systematics of the experimental $(p,p)$ cross sections. Overall, isotopic and isotonic analyses are useful for extracting the nuclear structure information such as nuclear deformations and single-particle features from proton scattering, enabling sensitive probing of surface densities.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that deformation effects are essential for describing the isotopic systematics of experimental proton elastic scattering cross sections for stable S and Ni isotopes, but the conclusion is qualified by the need to combine isotopic cross section ratio data with charge density difference data to distinguish between deformed and spherical structure models.

## Method

The author performed spherical and deformed Relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations with DD-PC1 and DD-ME2 interactions, plus spherical nonrelativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (SHFB) calculations with SKM* and SLy4 interactions, to obtain theoretical densities of target nuclei. Proton elastic scattering cross sections at 318 MeV for S isotopes and 178-295 MeV for Ni isotopes were calculated using the relativistic impulse approximation with density-dependent effective NN interactions (RIA+ddMH model). Isotopic analyses compared calculated cross section ratios and density differences with experimental data to probe deformation effects.

## Key numbers

- Proton elastic scattering off S isotopes calculated at 318 MeV, with experimental cross sections from Kelly (1991)
- Proton elastic scattering off Ni isotopes calculated at 178 MeV (experimental data from Ingemarsson (1981)) and 295 MeV (experimental data from Zenihiro (2010))
- Deformed RHB calculations for Ni isotopes found minimum-energy states at beta = -0.08 for Ni-58, beta = -0.20 for Ni-60, and beta = -0.23 for Ni-62
- The pc1-def2 set with Ni-58 at beta = -0.17, Ni-60 at beta = -0.20, and Ni-62 at beta = -0.23 best reproduced both charge density differences and cross section ratio data
- For Ti-50, the deformed solution at beta = 0.15 was compared with the spherical solution at beta = 0.0
- The N=32 shell gap in SKM* calculations produced a kink in proton radii at N=32, whereas pc1 and me2 results showed no kink due to pairing effects

## Context

This work extends the author's previous isotopic analysis method applied to Pb(p,p) and Sn(p,p) reactions at 295 MeV (Kanada-En'yo 2021) to mid-shell S and Ni isotopes, where deformation effects are significant. The RIA+ddMH model used here was developed by Sakaguchi (1998) and successfully applied to Sn isotopes (Terashima 2008), Pb isotopes (Zenihiro 2010), and Ca isotopes (Zenihiro 2018). The study demonstrates that combining isotopic cross section ratio data with charge density difference data can extract nuclear deformation information, enabling future application to unstable nuclei where deformation properties are less well known.
