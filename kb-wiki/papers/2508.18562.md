# Contribution of Subthreshold States to the Residual Energy Distribution of $^{159}$Dy

> We investigate the residual energy distribution in the electron capture (EC) process of $^{159}$Dy, emphasizing the role of subthreshold atomic states, which have typically been omitted in conventional spectral analyses. By incorporating these energetically forbidden hole states into the spectral function $P(E)$, we demonstrate a significant enhancement - over an order of magnitude - in the EC rate near the zero-momentum neutrino emission region. This enhancement increases further with larger $Q$ values, contrary to standard expectations. Our analysis shows that $P(E)$ can be experimentally determined, resolving ambiguities near the endpoint and enabling new approaches to 'ultra-low $Q$ value' EC reactions for neutrino mass studies.

# Contribution of Subthreshold States to the Residual Energy Distribution of $^{159}$Dy

## Key claim

By including energetically forbidden ("subthreshold") atomic hole states in the spectral function $P(E)$, the electron capture rate near the zero-momentum neutrino emission region is enhanced by over an order of magnitude for $^{159}$Dy, with the enhancement increasing for larger $Q$ values, contrary to standard expectations that only energetically allowed ("continuum") states contribute.

## Method

The authors compute the electron capture (EC) differential decay rate as the product of a nuclear reduced matrix element, the neutrino phase space factor, and the atomic spectral function $P(E_{\rm ex})$, which sums over all possible atomic final states. They model $P(E_{\rm ex})$ using a particle-hole picture with Lorentzian line shapes for $s_{1/2}$ and $p_{1/2}$ hole states from the $K$, $L$, $M$, $N$, $O$, and $P$ orbits, using parameters for binding energies, widths, and electron densities at the origin taken from prior literature. The calculation includes both continuum states (excitation energy below the endpoint) and subthreshold states (excitation energy above the endpoint), with particular focus on the $M$ and $N$ orbits for $^{159}$Dy.

## Key numbers

- The $Q$ value for $^{159}$Dy EC to the $5/2^-$ excited state of $^{159}$Tb is $Q = 364.93(19)$ keV, with nuclear excitation energy $\Delta_{\rm nucl} = 363.5449(14)$ keV, giving $Q - \Delta_{\rm nucl} = 1.18(19)$ keV.
- The binding energies of the $N_1$ and $N_2$ hole states are $0.396$ keV and $0.322$ keV, respectively; the $M_1$ and $M_2$ binding energies are $1.968$ keV and $1.768$ keV, respectively.
- The spectral function $P(E_{\rm ex})$ is enhanced by factors of $1.13 \times 10^1$ at $E_{\rm ex} = 1.18$ keV and $3.02 \times 10^2$ at $E_{\rm ex} = 1.7$ keV when subthreshold $M$ states are included.
- The ratio $r$ (fraction of EC rate in the endpoint region $\delta = 1$ eV) for $^{159}$Dy with subthreshold states included is $3.3 \times 10^{-11}$ for $m_\nu = 1$ eV and $8.4 \times 10^{-12}$ for $m_\nu = 0.1$ eV; excluding subthreshold states gives $2.9 \times 10^{-12}$ and $7.3 \times 10^{-13}$, respectively.
- For comparison, $^3$H beta decay gives $r = 1.5 \times 10^{-12}$ ($m_\nu = 1$ eV) and $3.8 \times 10^{-13}$ ($m_\nu = 0.1$ eV); EC $^{163}$Ho gives $r = 2.6 \times 10^{-12}$ ($m_\nu = 1$ eV) and $6.5 \times 10^{-13}$ ($m_\nu = 0.1$ eV) with subthreshold states included.

## Context

This work extends the standard treatment of EC spectral functions, which previously included only continuum hole states (Bambynek 1977), by demonstrating that subthreshold states, those with excitation energy above the kinematic endpoint, can dominate the spectral function near the neutrino endpoint when the $Q$ value is small. The analysis is motivated by recent proposals for ultra-low $Q$ value EC candidates (Ge 2021, Redshaw 2023) and builds on measurements of the $^{163}$Ho EC spectrum (Velte 2019, PENTATRAP 2024). The results enable new experimental approaches using $^{159}$Dy and potentially $^{111}$In (Ge 2022) for neutrino mass studies, particularly with high-resolution TES detectors (Sakai 2020), and show that $P(E)$ can be determined experimentally by comparing different $Q$ value states, resolving ambiguities near the endpoint.
