# Twenty-Five Years of Progress in the Three-Nucleon Problem

> Twenty-five years ago the International Few-Body Conference was held in Quebec City. It became very clear at that meeting that the theoretical situation concerning the He3 and H3 ground states was confused. A lack of computational power prevented converged brute-force solutions of the Faddeev or Schroedinger equations, both for bound and continuum states of the three-nucleon systems. Pushed by experimental programs at Bates and elsewhere and facilitated by the rapid growth of computational power, converged solutions were finally achieved about a decade later. Twenty-five years ago the first three-nucleon force based on chiral-symmetry considerations was produced. Since then this symmetry has been our guiding principle in constructing three-nucleon forces and, more recently, nucleon-nucleon forces. We are finally nearing an understanding of the common ingredients used in constructing both types of forces. I will discuss these and other issues involving the few-nucleon systems and attempt to define the current state-of-the-art.

## Key claim

Twenty-five years of progress have transformed the three-nucleon problem from a state of confusion where calculations disagreed by 10-20% (1-2 MeV) in the triton binding energy, to a mature field where 1%-accuracy calculations are routine and the remaining discrepancies point specifically to the need for three-nucleon forces (3NFs) beyond the leading two-pion-exchange mechanisms.

## Method

The paper surveys the historical development of three-nucleon calculations, from early non-converged Faddeev and Schrodinger solutions through the first converged bound-state calculations in 1985, then scattering below breakup threshold, and finally above-breakup scattering. It reviews the construction of three-nucleon forces using chiral perturbation theory (χPT) with dimensional power counting, comparing models such as the Fujita-Miyazawa isobar model, the Tucson-Melbourne chiral model, and the Texas χPT model. The author assesses agreement between calculations and experimental data for triton binding, elastic nucleon-deuteron scattering, and spin observables like the asymmetry A_y.

## Key numbers

- The triton binding energy had a 10-20% uncertainty (∼1-2 MeV) in 1974; by the late 1980s converged solutions at the 1% level (∼100 keV) were achieved.
- The one-pion-exchange potential (OPEP) contributes roughly 75% of the total potential energy in few-nucleon systems.
- The estimated off-shell/three-body force scale is ⟨ΔH⟩ ∼ 0.5-1.0 MeV, derived from V²/Mc².
- The pion decay constant f_π ∼ 93 MeV and the QCD cutoff scale Λ ∼ 1 GeV set the chiral perturbation theory scales.
- The two-pion-exchange potential per pair is estimated as ⟨V_π⟩ ∼ Q³/(f_πΛ) ∼ 30 MeV/pair.
- The three-nucleon force per triplet is estimated as ⟨V_3NF⟩ ∼ Q⁶/(f_π²Λ³) ∼ 1 MeV/triplet.
- At 65 MeV proton-deuteron scattering, adding the Tucson-Melbourne 3NF resolves the Sagara discrepancy in the diffraction minimum.
- At 3 MeV neutron-deuteron scattering, calculations with only NN forces underestimate the asymmetry A_y by about 30%; the TM 3NF accounts for only about 1/4 of the discrepancy.

## Context

This review extends the author's earlier work on relativistic corrections in few-nucleon systems and summarizes progress since the 1974 Quebec Few-Body Conference. It contrasts the confused state of 1974 calculations with the converged solutions achieved by the Los Alamos-Iowa group (1985 for ³H, 1987 for ³He including Coulomb), the Pisa group's scattering calculations, and the Nijmegen group's phase-shift analyses. The paper enables the next step of constructing a "standard model" of three-nucleon forces by validating the chiral perturbation theory building blocks against NN data (Martín-Camalich et al.) and identifying the d₁-term as a candidate to resolve the long-standing low-energy A_y puzzle (Kievsky et al.).
