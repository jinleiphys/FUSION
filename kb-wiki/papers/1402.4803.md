# Red or blue? A potential kilonova imprint of the delay until black hole formation following a neutron star merger

> Mergers of binary neutron stars (NSs) usually result in the formation of a hypermassive neutron star (HMNS). Whether- and when this remnant collapses to a black hole (BH) depends primarily on the equation of state and on angular momentum transport processes, both of which are uncertain. Here we show that the lifetime of the merger remnant may be directly imprinted in the radioactively powered kilonova emission following the merger. We employ axisymmetric, time-dependent hydrodynamic simulations of remnant accretion disks orbiting a HMNS of variable lifetime, and characterize the effect of this delay to BH formation on the disk wind ejecta. When BH formation is relatively prompt (~ 100 ms), outflows from the disk are sufficiently neutron rich to form heavy r-process elements, resulting in ~ week-long emission with a spectral peak in the near-infrared (NIR), similar to that produced by the dynamical ejecta. In contrast, delayed BH formation allows neutrinos from the HMNS to raise the electron fraction in the polar direction to values such that potentially Lanthanide-free outflows are generated. The lower opacity would produce a brighter, bluer, and shorter-lived ~ day-long emission (a `blue bump') prior to the late NIR peak from the dynamical ejecta and equatorial wind. This new diagnostic of BH formation should be useful for events with a signal to noise lower than that required for direct detection of gravitational waveform signatures.

# Red or blue? A potential kilonova imprint of the delay until black hole formation following a neutron star merger

## Key claim

The lifetime of a hypermassive neutron star (HMNS) formed after a neutron star merger may be directly imprinted in the kilonova emission, with prompt black hole formation (less than about 100 ms) producing only red near-infrared emission, while delayed black hole formation allows neutrino irradiation from the HMNS to create an additional blue optical component lasting about a day before the later red peak.

## Method

The authors performed axisymmetric, time-dependent hydrodynamic simulations of remnant accretion disks orbiting a HMNS of variable lifetime using the FLASH3.2 code, with a reflecting inner boundary at 30 km to represent the HMNS surface and an absorbing boundary to represent black hole formation. They included a pseudo-Newtonian potential, anomalous shear stress for angular momentum transport, charged-current weak interactions, and a neutrino leakage scheme, evolving models for up to 3000 orbits (approximately 8.7 seconds) while varying the HMNS lifetime from 0 ms to infinity.

## Key numbers

- Prompt black hole formation (0 ms lifetime): equatorial outflow electron fraction 0.18, ejected mass 2.1% of initial torus mass; polar outflow ejected mass 0.9% of initial torus mass
- HMNS lifetime 100 ms: equatorial outflow electron fraction 0.28, ejected mass 9.2% of initial torus mass; polar outflow electron fraction 0.47, ejected mass 4.0% of initial torus mass
- Infinite HMNS lifetime: equatorial outflow electron fraction 0.32, ejected mass 67% of initial torus mass; polar outflow electron fraction 0.43, ejected mass 22% of initial torus mass
- HMNS neutrino luminosity normalization: 2 × 10^52 erg s^-1 at 30 ms, based on Dessart+09 models
- HMNS neutrino temperatures: electron neutrinos 4 MeV, electron antineutrinos 5 MeV, yielding mean energies of approximately 12 and 15 MeV respectively
- Equilibrium electron fraction under high neutrino irradiation: approximately 0.52, derived from Fermi-Dirac spectra with zero chemical potential
- Prompt black hole formation (less than about 100 ms): week-long emission with spectral peak in near-infrared
- Delayed black hole formation: day-long blue emission prior to late near-infrared peak

## Context

This work extends the study of Fernandez and Metzger (2013, FM13), who calculated the viscous evolution of remnant black hole accretion disks but implicitly assumed prompt black hole formation by ignoring the influence of a central HMNS. The results show that the HMNS lifetime produces a monotonic effect on ejected mass and electron fraction, with polar outflows becoming significant only when the HMNS survives for at least about 100 ms. This diagnostic of black hole formation should be useful for events with signal-to-noise lower than that required for direct detection of gravitational waveform signatures, and complements the work of Bauswein+12 and Hotokezaka+13c on HMNS oscillations in gravitational wave data.
