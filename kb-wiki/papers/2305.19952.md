# Optimizing rodeo projection

> The rodeo algorithm has been proposed recently as an efficient method in quantum computing for projection of a given initial state onto a state of fixed energy for systems with discrete spectra. In the initial formulation of the rodeo algorithm these times were chosen randomly via a Gaussian distribution with fixed RMS times. In this paper it is shown that such a random approach for choosing times suffers from exponentially large fluctuations in the suppression of unwanted components: as the number of iterations gets large, the distribution of suppression factors obtained from random selection approaches a log-normal distribution leading to remarkably large fluctuations. We note that by choosing times intentionally rather than randomly such fluctuations can be avoided and strict upper bounds on the suppression can be obtained. Moreover, the average suppression using fixed computational cost can be reduced by many orders of magnitude relative to the random algorithm. A key to doing this is to choose times that vary over exponentially many times scales, starting from a modest maximum scale and going down to time scales exponentially smaller.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the random rodeo algorithm (RRA), which selects evolution times randomly from a Gaussian distribution, suffers from exponentially large fluctuations in the suppression of unwanted energy components as the number of iterations increases, because the distribution of suppression factors approaches a log-normal distribution. By choosing times intentionally rather than randomly, these fluctuations can be avoided and strict upper bounds on suppression can be obtained, with average suppression improved by many orders of magnitude at fixed computational cost.

## Method

The authors analyze the rodeo algorithm for projecting an initial quantum state onto an energy eigenstate, using total time as a proxy for computational cost. They derive the ensemble-averaged suppression factor for the random version of the algorithm, where times per iteration are drawn from a Gaussian distribution, and compute the arithmetic mean, geometric mean, and root-mean-square of the suppression factor as functions of the dimensionless total time ζ_tot = E T_tot / (2πħ). They then introduce a "super iteration" scheme where times are chosen intentionally to vary over exponentially many time scales, starting from a modest maximum scale down to exponentially smaller scales, and compare its performance to the random approach.

## Key numbers

- The minimum excitation energy Δ defines a natural time scale T₀ = 2πħ/Δ.
- For the random rodeo algorithm, the arithmetic mean suppression asymptotes to 2⁻ⁿ for large ζ, while the geometric mean asymptotes to 4⁻ⁿ for large ζ.
- The optimal exponential fit for the separatrix in the ζ_tot–s plane gives β ≈ 2.244 for the arithmetic mean, β ≈ 4.46 for the geometric mean, and β ≈ 1.637 for the root-mean-square.
- The minimal total time required to ensure all excited components are suppressed on average by at least a factor S is T_tot/T₀ ≥ –log(S)/β ≈ 0.4456 log(S) for the arithmetic mean.

## Context

This work extends the original rodeo algorithm proposed by Choi et al. (2020), which used random Gaussian-distributed evolution times, by identifying and quantifying its large fluctuations. The authors show that the random approach leads to a log-normal distribution of suppression factors, making reliable projection computationally expensive. The paper enables a deterministic optimization strategy using "super iterations" with multiple time scales, which can be applied in both digital quantum computers (via Trotterized time evolution) and gate-controlled analog quantum simulators. This approach contrasts with the phase estimation algorithm and complements other state preparation methods such as adiabatic quantum computing.
