# Pre-equilibrium photons from the early stages of heavy-ion collisions

> We use QCD kinetic theory to compute photon production in the chemically equilibrating Quark-Gluon Plasma created in the early stages of high-energy heavy-ion collisions. We do a detailed comparison of pre-equilibrium photon rates to the thermal photon production. We show that the photon spectrum radiated from a hydrodynamic attractor evolution satisfies a simple scaling form in terms of the specific shear viscosity $η/s$ and entropy density $dS/dζ\sim {\scriptstyle \left(Tτ^{1/3}\right)^{3/2}}_\infty$. We confirm the analytical predictions with numerical kinetic theory simulations. We use the extracted scaling function to compute the pre-equilibrium photon contribution in $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\,\text{TeV}$ 0-20\% PbPb collisions. We demonstrate that our matching procedure allows for a smooth switching from pre-equilibrium kinetic to thermal hydrodynamic photon production. Finally, our publicly available implementation can be straightforwardly added to existing heavy ion models.

## Key claim

The photon spectrum radiated from the pre-equilibrium Quark-Gluon Plasma during the early stages of heavy-ion collisions satisfies a simple scaling form in terms of the specific shear viscosity \(\eta/s\) and the asymptotic entropy density per unit rapidity \((s\tau)_\infty\), provided the plasma evolution follows a hydrodynamic attractor. This scaling is confirmed numerically within leading-order QCD kinetic theory, but the authors note that the formula may also be sensible for estimates using different microscopic theories, e.g., by supplying next-to-leading order photon rates.

## Method

The authors used leading-order QCD kinetic theory (AMY effective kinetic theory) to solve the Boltzmann equation for gluons, quarks, and photons in a transversely homogeneous, longitudinally boost-invariant Bjorken expansion. Photon production was computed from elastic processes (Compton scattering and elastic pair annihilation) and inelastic processes (Bremsstrahlung and inelastic pair annihilation), including the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal (LPM) effect via an integral equation for the in-medium splitting rates. The evolution was simulated numerically for different coupling strengths and initial conditions, and the time-integrated photon spectra were extracted and compared to the predicted scaling form.

## Key numbers

* The scaling function \(\mathcal{N}_\gamma\) is expressed in terms of scaled time \(\tilde{w} = \tau T/(4\pi \eta/s)\) and scaled transverse momentum \(\bar{p}_T \propto \sqrt{\eta/s} \, p_T / (s\tau)_\infty^{1/2}\), where \((s\tau)_\infty\) is the entropy density per unit rapidity in the hydrodynamic phase.
* The differential photon spectrum is given by \({dN}/{d^2\xT d^2\pT dy} = (\eta/s)^2 \tilde{C}_\gamma^\text{ideal} \mathcal{N}_\gamma(\tilde{w}, \bar{p}_T)\), where \(\tilde{C}_\gamma^\text{ideal}\) is a numerical constant describing the thermal photon production rate.
* Pre-equilibrium photon contributions were computed for \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 2.76\,\text{TeV}\) 0-20% PbPb collisions, and the authors find that at intermediate momenta \(p_T \sim 3\,\text{GeV}\) the pre-equilibrium contribution can be dominant.
* The elastic photon production rate for coupling \(\lambda = 0.1\) shows perfect agreement with semi-analytical AMY results (Arnold, 2001) for all channels; for \(\lambda = 10\) the elastic channel is greatly suppressed and becomes equal to the inelastic channel for \(p > T\).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of hydrodynamic attractors in QCD kinetic theory (Kurkela, 2018; Du, 2020; Giacalone, 2019) by showing that photon emission from the pre-equilibrium stage also obeys a universal scaling relation. It contrasts with earlier phenomenological treatments that neglected pre-equilibrium photon production (Shen, 2013; Paquet, 2015) or described it only parametrically using the bottom-up thermalization picture (Monnai, 2019; Garcia-Montero, 2019). The results enable a smooth matching of pre-equilibrium photon production to thermal hydrodynamic photon production in heavy-ion collision models, and the publicly available implementation can be added to existing frameworks to address the direct photon puzzle.
