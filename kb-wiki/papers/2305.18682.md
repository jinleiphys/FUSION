# Fate of the topological susceptibility in two-color dense QCD

> We explore the topological susceptibility at finite quark chemical potential and zero temperature in two-color QCD (QC$_2$D) with two flavors. Through the Ward-Takahashi identities of QC$_2$D, we find that the topological susceptibility in the vacuum solely depends on three observables: the pion decay constant, the pion mass, and the $η$ mass in the low-energy regime of QC$_2$D. Based on the identities, we numerically evaluate the topological susceptibility at finite quark chemical potential using the linear sigma model with the approximate Pauli-Gursey $SU(4)$ symmetry. Our findings indicate that, in the absence of $U(1)_A$ anomaly effects represented by the Kobayashi-Maskawa-'t Hooft-type determinant interaction, the topological susceptibility vanishes in both the hadronic and baryon superfluid phases. On the other hand, when the $U(1)_A$ anomaly effects are present, the constant and nonzero topological susceptibility is induced in the hadronic phase, reflecting the mass difference between the pion and $η$ meson. Meanwhile, in the superfluid phase it begins to decrease smoothly. The asymptotic behavior of the decrement is fitted by the continuous reduction of the chiral condensate in dense QC$_2$D, which is similar to the behavior observed in hot three-color QCD matter. In addition, effects from the finite diquark source on the topological susceptibility are discussed. We expect that the present study provides a clue to shed light on the role of the $U(1)_A$ anomaly in cold and dense QCD matter.

## Key claim

The topological susceptibility in two-color QCD at finite quark chemical potential is shown to vanish in both hadronic and baryon superfluid phases when the $U(1)_A$ anomaly is absent, but becomes nonzero and constant in the hadronic phase when the anomaly is present, then decreases smoothly in the superfluid phase; this behavior is contingent on the strength of the Kobayashi-Maskawa-'t Hooft-type determinant interaction and the resulting mass difference between the pion and the $\eta$ meson.

## Method

The authors derive the topological susceptibility from Ward-Takahashi identities of two-color QCD, expressing it in terms of the pion decay constant, pion mass, and $\eta$ meson mass in the low-energy regime. They then numerically evaluate the susceptibility at finite quark chemical potential using a linear sigma model with approximate Pauli-Gursey $SU(4)$ symmetry, incorporating the Kobayashi-Maskawa-'t Hooft determinant interaction to represent $U(1)_A$ anomaly effects, and also consider finite diquark source contributions to facilitate comparison with lattice data.

## Key numbers

* The topological susceptibility in the vacuum is expressed as $\chi_{\rm top} = (f_\pi^2 m_\pi^2 / 2) \, \delta_m$, where $\delta_m = 1 - m_\pi^2 / m_\eta^2$ in the low-energy regime, relating it to three observables: the pion decay constant $f_\pi$, the pion mass $m_\pi$, and the $\eta$ mass $m_\eta$.
* The quantity $\delta_m$ ranges from $\delta_m \to 1$ (significantly large anomaly effects) to $\delta_m \to 0$ (no anomaly effects), with the topological susceptibility vanishing when $\delta_m = 0$.
* In the absence of $U(1)_A$ anomaly effects, the topological susceptibility vanishes in both the hadronic and baryon superfluid phases; when anomaly effects are present, it is constant and nonzero in the hadronic phase and decreases smoothly in the superfluid phase.

## Context

This work extends the study of topological susceptibility from hot three-color QCD matter to cold and dense two-color QCD, where lattice simulations have shown conflicting results: some groups find the susceptibility remains approximately constant with chemical potential (Iida 2019), while others observe suppression at high density (Astrakhantsev 2020). The analysis uses the linear sigma model with Pauli-Gursey $SU(4)$ symmetry developed by Suenaga (2022) and builds on the Ward-Takahashi identity approach previously applied to three-color QCD by Gomez Nicola (2016, 2017) and Kawaguchi (2020). The results enable comparison with lattice data and help clarify the role of the $U(1)_A$ anomaly in dense baryonic matter, with the diquark source effects potentially explaining discrepancies between different lattice observations.
