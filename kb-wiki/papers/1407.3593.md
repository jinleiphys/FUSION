# Description of Collective Motion in Two-Dimensional Nuclei; Tomonaga's Method Revisited

> Four decades ago, Tomonaga proposed the elementary theory of quantum mechanical collective motion of two-dimensional nuclei of N nucleons. The theory is based essentially on the neglect of 1/sqrtN against unity. Very recently we have given exact canonically conjugate momenta to quadrupole-type collective coordinates under some subsidiary conditions and have derived nuclear quadrupole-type collective Hamiltonian. Even in the case of simple two-dimensional nuclei, we have a subsidiary condition to obtain exact canonical variables. Particularly the structure of the collective subspace satisfying the subsidiary condition is studied in detail. This subsidiary condition is important to investigate what is a structure of the collective subspace.

## Key claim

The paper constructs exact canonically conjugate momenta for quadrupole-type collective coordinates in two-dimensional nuclei, but only under a subsidiary condition that restricts the Hilbert space to a collective subspace where the total angular momentum equals the collective angular momentum. The authors show that Tomonaga's original approximate treatment, which neglected terms of order \(1/\sqrt{N}\), can be made exact by imposing this condition.

## Method

The authors start from Tomonaga's collective coordinate approach for a two-dimensional nucleus of \(N\) nucleons, introducing collective coordinates \(\phi_1\) and \(\phi_2\) (quadrupole-type deformations) and approximate conjugate momenta \(\eta_1\) and \(\eta_2\). They then define modified momenta \(\pi_i = \frac{r_0^2}{2}(r^{-2}\eta_i + \eta_i r^{-2})\) and prove that these become exact canonical conjugates to \(\phi_i\) when the Hilbert space is restricted to a collective subspace satisfying the subsidiary condition \((l - \mathbf{L}_{\text{coll}})|\text{coll.subspace}\rangle = 0\), where \(l\) is the total angular momentum and \(\mathbf{L}_{\text{coll}}\) is the collective angular momentum operator. The kinetic part of the Hamiltonian is expanded in powers of \(\pi_i\) and \(\phi_i\) using commutation relations within the symplectic algebra \(sp(2,R)\).

## Key numbers

* The collective coordinates are defined as \(\phi_1 = \frac{1}{N r_0^2} \sum_{n=1}^N \frac{1}{2}(x_n^2 - y_n^2)\) and \(\phi_2 = \frac{1}{N r_0^2} \sum_{n=1}^N x_n y_n\), where \(r_0^2 = R_0^2/2\) and \(R_0\) is the nuclear equilibrium radius.
* The approximate commutation relation is \([\eta_i, \phi_j] \cong -i\hbar \delta_{ij}\), while the exact relation is \([\pi_i, \phi_j] = -i\hbar \delta_{ij}\) on the collective subspace.
* The commutator of the approximate momenta is \([\eta_1, \eta_2] = -2i\hbar l\), while the exact momenta satisfy \([\pi_1, \pi_2] = -2i\hbar (r^2/r_0^2)^{-2}(l - \mathbf{L}_{\text{coll}})\), which vanishes on the collective subspace.
* The kinetic energy expansion yields \(T_{ij}^{(2)} = \frac{r^2}{2\mu N r_0^4} \delta_{ij}\) for the quadratic term in momenta.
* The function \(f(N)\) in the mean value \(\langle \sum_n (x_n p_{x_n} + y_n p_{y_n}) \rangle = f(N)\) is determined as \(f(N) = -N + 1\) to ensure the commutator \([\pi_i, T^{(0)}]\) depends only on \(\phi\).

## Context

This work revisits and extends Tomonaga's (1955) elementary theory of collective motion in two-dimensional nuclei, which was based on neglecting \(1/\sqrt{N}\) against unity. It builds on the earlier work of Miyazima and Tamura (1956) and Tamura (1956), who proposed a collective description of surface oscillations but faced difficulties with non-canonical momenta and non-commuting collective momenta. The paper also connects to the contracted symplectic model of Castanos and Draayer (1989), contrasting their contraction of all generators with the present approach where only the generator \(\sum_n (x_n p_{x_n} + y_n p_{y_n})\) is contracted. This work enables a fully canonical formulation of collective motion in two-dimensional nuclei, providing a foundation for studying the structure of the collective subspace and the nature of large-amplitude collective motions beyond mean-field theories.
