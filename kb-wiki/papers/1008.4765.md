# Primordial Nucleosynthesis: The Predicted and Observed Abundances and Their Consequences

> For a brief time in its early evolution the Universe was a cosmic nuclear reactor. The expansion and cooling of the Universe limited this epoch to the first few minutes, allowing time for the synthesis in astrophysically interesting abundances of only the lightest nuclides (D, 3He, 4He, 7Li). For big bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) in the standard models of cosmology and particle physics (SBBN), the SBBN-predicted abundances depend on only one adjustable parameter, the baryon density parameter (the ratio by number of baryons (nucleons) to photons). The predicted and observed abundances of the relic light elements are reviewed, testing the internal consistency of primordial nucleosynthesis. The consistency of BBN is also explored by comparing the values of the cosmological parameters inferred from primordial nucleosynthesis for the standard model and for models with non-standard early Universe expansion rates with those determined from studies of the cosmic background radiation, which provides a snapshot of the Universe some 400 thousand years after BBN ended.

# Primordial Nucleosynthesis: The Predicted and Observed Abundances and Their Consequences

## Key claim

Within the errors, standard big bang nucleosynthesis (SBBN) predictions for deuterium, helium-3, and helium-4 agree with observationally-inferred primordial abundances and with the cosmic microwave background (CMB) baryon density, but lithium-7 remains discrepant by a factor of 3-4; allowing a non-standard expansion rate (parameterized by Delta N_nu) does not resolve the lithium problem.

## Method

The author reviews the SBBN-predicted abundances of D, He-3, He-4, and Li-7 as functions of the single baryon-to-photon ratio parameter eta_10, then compares these predictions to observationally-inferred primordial abundances from QSO absorption line systems (D), Galactic HII regions (He-3), extragalactic HII regions (He-4), and metal-poor halo stars (Li-7). For non-standard BBN, the expansion rate parameter S (or equivalently Delta N_nu, the effective number of additional neutrino species) is introduced as a second free parameter, and isoabundance contours in the S-eta_10 plane are used to derive joint constraints from D and He-4, which are then compared to CMB results from 7-year WMAP data.

## Key numbers

- Primordial deuterium abundance: log y_DP = 0.45 +/- 0.03, where y_DP = 5 + log(D/H)_P (Pettini et al. 2010)
- SBBN baryon density from deuterium: eta_10(SBBN) = 5.80 +/- 0.27
- SBBN-predicted He-3 abundance: y_3P(SBBN) = 1.08 +/- 0.04, where y_3 = 10^5 (He-3/H)_P
- Observationally-inferred primordial He-4 mass fraction: Y_P = 0.2565 +/- 0.0060 (Izotov and Thuan 2010), combining statistical error +/- 0.0010 and systematic error +/- 0.0050 linearly
- SBBN-predicted He-4 abundance: Y_P(SBBN) = 0.2482 +/- 0.0007
- SBBN-predicted lithium abundance: [Li]_SBBN = 2.65 +/- 0.06, where [Li] = 12 + log(Li/H)
- Observed lithium abundances in metal-poor stars are lower than SBBN prediction by 0.5-0.6 dex (factor of 3-4)
- Non-standard BBN (with Delta N_nu free): eta_10(BBN) = 6.07 +/- 0.33, Delta N_nu = 0.62 +/- 0.46 (consistent with zero at 1.3 sigma)
- CMB baryon density from 7-year WMAP: eta_10 = 6.190 +/- 0.145 (Komatsu et al. 2011)
- CMB-derived Delta N_nu = 1.30 +/- 0.87 (Komatsu et al. 2011)
- Ratio of CMB to SBBN photon number: N_gamma(REC)/N_gamma(SBBN) = 0.94 +/- 0.05
- Ratio of CMB to BBN photon number: N_gamma(REC)/N_gamma(BBN) = 0.98 +/- 0.06
- Gravitational constant ratio: G_REC/G_BBN = 1.07 +/- 0.13, G_BBN/G_0 = 1.10 +/- 0.07, G_REC/G_0 = 1.17 +/- 0.12

## Context

This review extends the author's earlier work (Steigman 2007) on BBN-CMB comparisons and updates the observational data with the Pettini et al. (2010) deuterium sample, the Izotov and Thuan (2010) helium-4 data from 93 spectra of 86 low-metallicity HII regions, and the 7-year WMAP results from Komatsu et al. (2011). The paper tests the internal consistency of SBBN and compares BBN constraints on the baryon density and effective neutrino number with CMB constraints, enabling combined probes of non-standard physics such as varying gravitational constant, entropy production between BBN and recombination, and decaying massive particles. The lithium problem remains unresolved, with possible resolutions lying in stellar astrophysics (depletion in old stars) or cosmology (late-time particle decay), as noted in the discussion.
