# Nuclear Astrophysics in Rare Isotope Facilities

## Key claim
Indirect methods with rare isotopes have been developed and applied to extract low-energy astrophysical cross sections that are difficult to measure directly in the laboratory at the small astrophysical energies. The paper reviews these methods but notes that several techniques (such as the direct relationship between charge-exchange cross sections and Gamow-Teller matrix elements) fail under many circumstances, including for important transitions whose strength is a small fraction of the sum rule.

## Method
The paper reviews five indirect experimental techniques used with rare isotope beams: elastic scattering and (p,p') reactions to probe matter distributions; transfer reactions including the Trojan Horse technique and the Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient (ANC) method; Coulomb excitation and dissociation to extract photo-nuclear and radiative capture cross sections; charge-exchange reactions (p,n), (³He,t), and heavy-ion reactions to obtain Gamow-Teller and Fermi transition strengths; and single-nucleon knockout reactions to study single-particle occupancies and reduce uncertainties in astrophysical reaction rates. Theoretical approaches including the Resonating Group Method, Generator Coordinate Method, and effective field theory are also discussed.

## Key numbers
- ⁸B has a small proton separation energy of approximately 140 keV, making multiple-step or higher-order effects important for such loosely-bound systems.
- For knockout reactions on ³³Ar, excited states were measured with uncertainties of several keV, yielding a 2 orders of magnitude improvement in level energy uncertainty and a 3 orders of magnitude improvement in the uncertainty of the calculated ³²Cl(p,γ)³³Ar rate.
- Beam energies for transfer reactions should be in the range of a few 10–100 MeV per nucleon for effective momentum matching.
- Multistep processes in charge-exchange reactions can still play an important role up to bombarding energies of 100 MeV/nucleon.

## Context
This review extends the body of work on indirect nuclear astrophysics methods developed over the preceding decades, including the Trojan Horse technique (Baur, 1986), the ANC method (Mukhamedzhanov, 1990; Tribble, 2006), Coulomb dissociation (Baur, Bertulani, and Rebel, 1986), and knockout reaction approaches (Hansen, 2003). The paper enables future applications of these techniques at rare isotope facilities such as FRIB and GSI, while identifying open questions about the validity of the direct proportionality between charge-exchange cross sections and beta-decay transition strengths, particularly for heavy-ion reactions and odd-A nuclei.
