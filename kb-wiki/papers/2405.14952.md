# First detection of X-ray pulsations and spectrum of the high Galactic latitude pulsar PSR J0837-2454 and direct Urca cooling implications

> PSR J0837-2454 is a young 629 ms radio pulsar whose uncertain distance has important implications. A large distance would place the pulsar far out of the Galactic plane and suggest it is the result of a runaway star, while a short distance would mean the pulsar is extraordinarily cold. Here we present further radio observations and the first deep X-ray observation of PSR J0837-2454. Data from the Parkes Murriyang telescope show flux variations over short and long timescales and also yield an updated timing model, while the position and proper motion (and, less strongly, parallax) of the pulsar are constrained by a number of low-significance detections with the Very Long Baseline Array. XMM-Newton data enable detection of X-ray pulsations for the first time from this pulsar and yield a spectrum that is thermal and blackbody-like, with a cool blackbody temperature ~70 eV or atmosphere temperature ~50 eV, as well as a small hotspot. The spectrum also indicates the pulsar is at a small distance of <~1 kpc, which is compatible with the marginal VLBA parallax constraint that favours a distance of >~330 pc. The low implied luminosity (~7.6x10^31 erg s^-1 at 0.9 kpc) suggests PSR J0837-2454 has a mass high enough that fast neutrino emission from direct Urca reactions operates in this young star and points to a nuclear equation of state that allows for direct Urca reactions at the highest densities present in neutron star cores.

# First detection of X-ray pulsations and spectrum of the high Galactic latitude pulsar PSR J0837-2454 and direct Urca cooling implications

## Key claim
The low X-ray luminosity of PSR J0837-2454 (~7.6×10^31 erg s^-1 at 0.9 kpc) suggests the neutron star has a mass high enough that fast neutrino emission from direct Urca reactions operates, but this conclusion depends on the pulsar being at a short distance (<~1 kpc) rather than the much larger distance (~6 kpc or more) inferred from radio dispersion measures.

## Method
The authors conducted 18 epochs of Parkes Murriyang radio observations (2021-2023) to obtain an updated timing model and flux monitoring, and 12 VLBA observations (2022-2024) to measure astrometric position, proper motion, and parallax. They performed the first deep X-ray observation of the pulsar using XMM-Newton for ~90 ks, analyzing EPIC pn and MOS data for timing and spectral analysis, fitting thermal blackbody and neutron star atmosphere models to the X-ray spectra.

## Key numbers
- Pulsar spin period: 629.544 ms (from X-ray pulsations detected at 1.58845213 Hz)
- X-ray blackbody temperature: ~70 eV; atmosphere temperature: ~50 eV
- Bolometric luminosity: ~7.6×10^31 erg s^-1 (at assumed distance of 0.9 kpc)
- VLBA parallax: 0.59 ± 0.24 mas (favors distance >~330 pc)
- X-ray pulsed fraction: 39% in 0.5-1 keV band, 17% in 0.2-10 keV band
- Radio spectral index: α = 0.13 ± 0.6 (from Parkes UWL data)
- Rotation measure: RM = 360.72 rad m^-2
- Absorbed 0.3-10 keV flux: 7.2×10^-14 erg s^-1 cm^-2
- Two-temperature blackbody fit: cold component kT = 70 eV, hot component kT = 160 eV with emitting radius ~150 m

## Context
This work extends the study of PSR J0837-2454, previously discovered as a radio pulsar by Burke-Spolaor et al. (2011) with timing parameters measured by Pol et al. (2021). The pulsar's uncertain distance had two competing interpretations: a large distance (>6 kpc) would place it far from the Galactic plane as a runaway star, while a short distance (<1 kpc) would make it extraordinarily cold. The X-ray detection and spectral analysis favor the short distance scenario, placing PSR J0837-2454 among a small group of cool young neutron stars (including PSR J0007+7303, PSR B1727-47, and PSR B2334+61) whose low luminosities require fast direct Urca neutrino cooling, which constrains nuclear equations of state that allow direct Urca reactions at high densities (Lattimer et al. 1991, Page and Applegate 1992). Future VLBA observations could provide a definitive parallax measurement to confirm the distance.
