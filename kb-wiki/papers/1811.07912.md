# Developing the MeV potential of DUNE: Detailed considerations of muon-induced spallation and other backgrounds

> The Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment (DUNE) could be revolutionary for MeV neutrino astrophysics, because of its huge detector volume, unique event reconstruction capabilities, and excellent sensitivity to the $ν_e$ flavor. However, its backgrounds are not yet known. A major background is expected due to muon spallation of argon, which produces unstable isotopes that later beta decay. We present the first comprehensive study of MeV spallation backgrounds in argon, detailing isotope production mechanisms and decay properties, analyzing beta energy and time distributions, and proposing experimental cuts. We show that above a nominal detection threshold of 5-MeV electron energy, the most important backgrounds are --- surprisingly --- due to low-A isotopes, such as Li, Be, and B, even though high-A isotopes near argon are abundantly produced. We show that spallation backgrounds can be powerfully rejected by simple cuts, with clear paths for improvements. We compare these background rates to rates of possible MeV astrophysical neutrino signals in DUNE, including solar neutrinos (detailed in a companion paper [Capozzi et al. arXiv:1808.08232 [hep-ph]]), supernova burst neutrinos, and the diffuse supernova neutrino background. Further, to aid trigger strategies, in the Appendixes we quantify the rates of single and multiple MeV events due to spallation, radiogenic neutron capture, and other backgrounds, including through pileup. Our overall conclusion is that DUNE has high potential for MeV neutrino astrophysics, but reaching this potential requires new experimental initiatives.

## Key claim

The paper claims that DUNE has high potential for MeV neutrino astrophysics, but reaching this potential requires new experimental initiatives; the authors show that above a nominal detection threshold of 5-MeV electron energy, the most important spallation backgrounds are surprisingly due to low-A isotopes (Li, Be, B) rather than high-A isotopes near argon, and that these backgrounds can be powerfully rejected by simple cuts.

## Method

The authors used the FLUKA Monte Carlo code (version 2011.2x-1) to simulate muon propagation in liquid argon, modeling the DUNE far detector as a 10-kton fiducial volume box of liquid argon with dimensions 56 m by 12.5 m by 10 m, surrounded by 2 m of rock. They injected single muons vertically downward at the detector center using a simulated cosmic-ray muon spectrum at the DUNE underground site (4850 ft depth, 4300 m.w.e.) based on the MUSIC/MUSUN simulations of Kudryavtsev et al., with a total muon flux of 5.66 × 10⁻⁹ cm⁻² s⁻¹. The simulation recorded isotope yields via the RESNUCLEi card and tracked decay energy spectra and time/spatial correlations via a modified mgdraw.f subroutine, with all relevant electromagnetic and hadronic processes included.

## Key numbers

* Total muon flux at DUNE site: 5.66 × 10⁻⁹ cm⁻² s⁻¹, with average muon energy 283 GeV and muon rate in four modules approximately 0.2 Hz
* Muon critical energy in liquid argon: 484 GeV; minimum kinetic energy for vertical traversal of fiducial volume: approximately 2 GeV; approximately 2% of muons stop in the detector
* Total isotope production yield: 1.441 per muon, corresponding to 4900 per day per 10-kton module
* Most abundant high-A isotope: ⁴¹Ar with yield 0.474 per muon (1600 per day per module); most abundant low-A isotope: ⁸Li with yield 1.4 × 10⁻³ per muon (4.8 per day per module)
* Sum of yields for isotopes surviving a 250-ms cut and making at least 1 event per year per 10 kton above 5 MeV: 0.046 per muon (160 per day per module)
* Largest Q value among spallation isotopes: 21 MeV (for ¹⁴B and ¹¹Li); ⁴⁰Cl has the largest Q value among high-A isotopes at 7.48 MeV

## Context

This work extends the spallation background methodology developed by Li and Beacom (2014, 2015) for water-based detectors like Super-Kamiokande to the liquid argon detector DUNE, and improves upon prior incomplete studies of spallation in liquid argon: Barker et al. (2012) focused only on high-A isotopes near argon, Gehman et al. considered only isotopes from high-energy neutrons, and Franco et al. (2015) studied spallation for low-energy solar neutrinos (below 1.3 MeV) without providing details for the 5-20 MeV range relevant to DUNE. The results enable quantitative assessment of DUNE's potential for MeV neutrino astrophysics including solar neutrinos (detailed in a companion paper by Capozzi et al., 2018), supernova burst neutrinos, and the diffuse supernova neutrino background, and provide guidance for developing experimental trigger strategies and background rejection cuts.
