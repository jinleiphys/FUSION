# Nuclear quantum transport for barrier problems

> A method is presented which allows one to introduce collective coordinates self-consistently, in distinction to the Caldeira-Leggett model. It is demonstrated how the partition function Z for the total nuclear system can be calculated to deduce information both on its level density as well as on the decay rate of unstable modes. For the evaluation of Z different approximations are discussed. A recently developed variational approach turns out superior to the conventional methods that include quantum effects on the level of local RPA. Dissipation is taken into account by applying energy smearing, simulating in this way the coupling to more complicated states. In principle, such a coupling must depend on temperature. Previous calculations along another microscopic approach show this fact to imply an intriguing variation of the transport coefficients of collective motion with T. The relevance of this feature is demonstrated for the thermal fission rate and for the formation probability of super-heavy elements.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method for introducing collective coordinates self-consistently in nuclear systems, in contrast to the Caldeira-Leggett model which violates essential requirements for nuclear collective motion. The method is demonstrated to be applicable even at very low temperatures where conventional approximations like the Perturbed Static Path Approximation (PSPA) break down.

## Method

The authors start from a nuclear Hamiltonian with a separable two-body interaction and introduce a collective coordinate through a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation in the functional integral for the partition function. The resulting path integral is evaluated using a variational approach that treats quantum fluctuations beyond the local RPA level, with dissipation introduced by smearing the independent-particle spectrum and fitting the response function to a Lorentzian form to extract temperature- and coordinate-dependent transport coefficients for inertia, damping, and stiffness.

## Key numbers

- The PSPA breaks down at a crossover temperature corresponding to $\beta_0 = 1/T_0 = 1.13 \,\text{MeV}^{-1}$, while the variational approach gives good results up to $\beta = 1/T = 5 \,\text{MeV}^{-1}$ for the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model.
- For the barrier of $^{224}\text{Th}$, the microscopic transport coefficients show a transition from under-damped ($\eta < 1$) to over-damped ($\eta > 1$) motion, with the Smoluchowsky limit not fully reached even at $T = 3 \,\text{MeV}$.
- The "onset of dissipation" in thermal fission appears around $T \approx 0.5 \,\text{MeV}$ for microscopic transport coefficients, coinciding with where pairing disappears.
- For super-heavy element formation, the assumed barrier height is $E_b = 10 \,\text{MeV}$, and microscopic transport coefficients yield formation probabilities orders of magnitude larger than macroscopic ones at not too high incident energies.

## Context

This work extends the imaginary-time approach of Puglisi, Bonaccorso, and Brink (1991) and Attias and Alhassid (1997) by developing a variational method superior to the conventional local RPA treatment. It contradicts the Caldeira-Leggett model (Caldeira and Leggett, 1983) which cannot predict transport coefficients self-consistently, and shows that macroscopic models using wall friction, liquid drop stiffness, and irrotational flow inertia fail to reproduce the "onset of dissipation" observed experimentally by Thomsen and Begg (1993). The method enables microscopic calculation of thermal fission rates and formation probabilities for super-heavy elements, with previous calculations by Hofmann and Ivanyuk (1999) and Hofmann, Ivanyuk, Rummel, and Yamaji (2001) providing the basis for comparison.
