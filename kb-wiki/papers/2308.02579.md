# Radiative neutron capture reaction rates for stellar nucleosynthesis

> There is a high demand for nuclear data in multidisciplinary subject like nuclear astrophysics. The two areas of nuclear physics which are most clearly related to one another are stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis. The necessity for nuclear data for astrophysical applications puts experimental methods as well as reliability and predicative ability of current nuclear models to the test. Despite recent, considerable advances, there are still significant issues and mysteries. Only a few characteristics of nuclear astrophysics are covered in the current work which include $^{20}$Ne(n,$Î³$)$^{21}$Ne, $^{52}$Fe(n,$Î³$)$^{53}$Fe, $^{53}$Fe(n,$Î³$)$^{54}$Fe, $^{54}$Fe(n,$Î³$)$^{55}$Fe and $^{55}$Fe(n,$Î³$)$^{56}$Fe reactions which are important in stellar nucleosynthesis. The reaction rates are calculated using nuclear statistical model. These rates are subsequently fitted to polynomials of temperature T$_9$ in order to facilitate calculations for stellar nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

The paper provides theoretical Hauser-Feshbach statistical model calculations of radiative neutron capture reaction rates for five specific reactions (20Ne, 52Fe, 53Fe, 54Fe, 55Fe) that are important in stellar nucleosynthesis, but the authors note that the lack of sufficient experimental data is a major impediment to constraining these rates near mass region fifty-six, and that future full reaction network calculations at evolving neutron densities are needed to study the impact on elemental abundances.

## Method

The authors used the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model implemented in the TALYS computer code to calculate (n,Î³) reaction cross sections for 20Ne(n,Î³)21Ne, 52Fe(n,Î³)53Fe, 53Fe(n,Î³)54Fe, 54Fe(n,Î³)55Fe, and 55Fe(n,Î³)56Fe over an energy range of 10 keV to 1.2 MeV. These cross sections were then convoluted with the Maxwell-Boltzmann energy distribution to obtain thermonuclear reaction rates, which were subsequently fitted to polynomials of temperature T9 (109 K) with nine coefficients each.

## Context

This work extends previous statistical model calculations of stellar reaction rates by Rauscher (1997, 2000, 2001, 2010) and others, using the TALYS code with updated nuclear inputs including temperature-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov level densities using the Gogny force and Brink-Axel Lorentzian gamma-ray strength functions. The results provide polynomial fits of reaction rates for five neutron capture reactions near the 56Fe mass region that can be used in stellar nucleosynthesis network calculations, and the authors suggest that new experimental techniques such as the surrogate method (Kozub, 2012) or the beta-Oslo method (Spyrou, 2014) could help constrain these rates in the future.
