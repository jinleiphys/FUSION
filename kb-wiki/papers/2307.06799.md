# Nature of the $N^*$ and $Δ$ resonances via coupled-channel dynamics

> This work aims at determining the composition of certain $N^*$ and $Δ$ resonances, i.e. whether they are compact states formed directly by quarks and gluons, or hadronic molecules generated from the meson-baryon interaction. The information of the resonance poles is provided by a comprehensive coupled-channel approach, the Jülich-Bonn model. $13$ states that are significant in this approach are studied. Two criteria for each state are adopted in this paper, the comparison thereof roughly indicates the model uncertainties. It is found that the conclusions for $8$ resonances are relatively certain: $N(1535) \frac{1}{2}^-$, $N(1440) \frac{1}{2}^+$, $N(1710) \frac{1}{2}^+$, and $N(1520) \frac{3}{2}^-$ tend to be composite; whereas $N(1650) \frac{1}{2}^-$, $N(1900) \frac{3}{2}^+$, $N(1680) \frac{5}{2}^+$, and $Δ(1600) \frac{3}{2}^+$ tend to be compact.

## Key claim

The paper determines whether 13 selected \(N^*\) and \(\Delta\) resonances are compact quark-gluon states or hadronic molecules, using two complementary criteria applied to poles from the Juelich-Bonn coupled-channel model; the comparison between the two criteria provides an estimate of model uncertainties, and for 8 of the 13 states the conclusions are relatively certain.

## Method

The authors extract resonance pole positions and residues from the Juelich-Bonn dynamical coupled-channel model, which solves scattering equations for channels including \(\pi N\), \(\pi\pi N\) (simulated by \(\sigma N\), \(\rho N\), and \(\pi\Delta\)), \(\eta N\), \(K\Lambda\), and \(K\Sigma\) with angular momentum up to \(J=9/2\). For each resonance, they compute two spectral density functions: one directly from the model's \(s\)-channel bare states, and another constructed locally from the pole position and on-shell residue using a Breit-Wigner form. The ratio of integrals of these two functions over an energy window around the pole mass gives the elementariness estimate, and complex Gamow-state elementariness values are also calculated as a cross-check.

## Key numbers

*   Deuteron binding energy \(B \simeq 2.22\) MeV and scattering length \(a = -5.41\) fm, effective range \(r = +1.75\) fm are cited as the classic example where Weinberg's criterion indicates a composite (molecular) state.
*   For the toy model, the bound state elementariness is given by \(Z = (1 + g^2 \mu^2 / (2\pi \sqrt{2\mu B}))^{-1} + \mathcal{O}(\Lambda^{-1})\), showing the relation between coupling strength and compositeness.
*   In the two-channel toy model, the quasi-bound state pole energy is \(E_{qb} = -B - (g_0^2 \mu_0 \Lambda / 4\pi) Z_B\) and width is \(\Gamma_{qb} = (g_0^2 \mu_0 / \pi) \sqrt{2\mu_0 (\Delta E - B)} Z_B\), where \(Z_B\) is the elementariness of the bound state when the lower channel is switched off.

## Context

This work extends the spectral density function approach of Baru (2003) and the Gamow-state formalism to the Juelich-Bonn model, which was previously used to extract resonance parameters in Ronchen (2022). The study applies these methods to 13 \(N^*\) and \(\Delta\) resonances, many of which are not in \(S\)-wave, not narrow, or not close to thresholds, going beyond the applicability of the pole counting rule of Morgan (1992) and the original Weinberg (1965) criterion for stable bound states. The results enable future studies of the internal structure of baryon resonances using data-driven coupled-channel models, and the authors note that the spectral density function approach works best for narrow resonances and becomes ambiguous for broad states or when multiple resonances overlap strongly.
