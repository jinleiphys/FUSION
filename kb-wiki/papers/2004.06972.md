# Diffractive dijet photoproduction at the EIC

> We present a first, detailed study of diffractive dijet photoproduction at the recently approved electron-ion collider (EIC) at BNL. Apart from establishing the kinematic reaches for various beam types, energies and kinematic cuts, we make precise predictions at next-to-leading order (NLO) of QCD in the most important kinematic variables. We show that the EIC will provide new and more precise information on the diffractive parton density functions (PDFs) in the pomeron than previously obtained at HERA, illuminate the still disputed mechanism of global vs.\ only resolved-photon factorization breaking, and provide access to a completely new quantity, i.e. nuclear diffractive PDFs.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first detailed study of diffractive dijet photoproduction at the EIC, showing that the collider will provide new information on diffractive parton density functions in the pomeron and nuclear diffractive PDFs, but the ability to distinguish between different factorization breaking schemes depends critically on achieving sufficient kinematic range in the photon longitudinal momentum fraction and requires high-precision measurements due to cross sections that fall by two orders of magnitude across the relevant kinematic region.

## Method

The authors performed next-to-leading order (NLO) QCD calculations of diffractive dijet photoproduction cross sections, assuming both QCD and Regge factorization, using the H1 2006 Fit B diffractive PDFs as default and the GRV NLO parametrization for photon PDFs. They computed differential cross sections for various kinematic distributions at the EIC for different beam energies (21 GeV electrons with 100 GeV protons, and 18 GeV electrons with 275 GeV protons) and compared predictions from three different factorization breaking schemes: global suppression by a factor of 0.5, suppression of resolved-photon contributions only by a factor of 0.34, and an interpolated suppression factor depending on parton flavor and x_gamma.

## Key numbers

- For the default EIC setup (21 GeV electrons, 100 GeV protons, CMS energy 92 GeV), the distribution in jet average transverse momentum extends only to 8 GeV, compared to about 15 GeV at HERA with its larger CMS energy of 300-320 GeV
- The NLO K-factors amount to about a factor of two at the low scales accessible at the EIC
- Extending the x_p range from less than 0.03 to less than 0.10 enlargens the reach in average transverse momentum from 8 to 14 GeV
- For 18 GeV electrons with 275 GeV protons (CMS energy 141 GeV), the reach in average transverse momentum extends from 8 to 12 GeV
- The differential cross sections at the higher proton beam energy are larger by one to two orders of magnitude compared to the default setup
- The resolved-photon suppression factor from the two-channel eikonal model is 0.34
- The interpolated scheme uses S^2 = 0.34 for the hadron-like component at small x_gamma, and S^2 = 0.53-0.75 for gluon and quark contributions at large x_gamma, with A_q = 0.37 and A_g = 0.19 for a hard resolution scale of p_T = 5 GeV
- The reggeon contribution increases from less than about 2% at x_p less than or equal to 0.03 to 10-35% at x_p greater than or equal to 0.06-0.10
- For nuclei, the squared CMS energy per nucleon is typically smaller by about a factor of Z/A approximately 0.4 for heavy nuclei

## Context

This work extends the HERA program on diffractive dijet photoproduction, where H1 (Aktas 2007) and ZEUS (Chekanov 2007) data could be described by either suppressing only resolved-photon contributions or by a global suppression factor of 0.42 to 0.71, with the mechanism of factorization breaking remaining disputed. The paper builds on NLO QCD calculations by Klasen (1994) and beyond, and on the two-channel eikonal model by Kaidalov (2003) predicting a suppression factor of 0.34 for resolved-photon processes. The EIC will enable access to a completely new quantity, nuclear diffractive PDFs, which have been previously studied theoretically by Frankfurt (2011) and Guzey (2016) but never measured experimentally.
