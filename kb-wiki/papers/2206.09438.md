# Nuclear pasta structures at high temperatures

> We investigate nuclear pasta structures at high temperatures in the framework of relativistic mean field model with Thomas-Fermi approximation. Typical pasta structures (droplet, rod, slab, tube, and bubble) are obtained, which form various crystalline configurations. The properties of those nuclear pastas are examined in a three-dimensional geometry with reflection symmetry, where the optimum lattice constants are fixed by reproducing the droplet/bubble density that minimizes the free energy adopting spherical or cylindrical approximations for Wigner-Seitz cells. It is found that different crystalline structures can evolve into each other via volume conserving deformations. For fixed densities and temperatures, the differences of the free energies per baryon of nuclear pasta in various shapes and lattice structures are typically on the order of tens of keV, suggesting the possible coexistence of those structures. As temperature increases, the thermodynamic fluctuations are expected to disrupt the long-range ordering in nuclear pasta structures. We then estimate the critical conditions for nuclear pasta to become disordered and behave like liquid, which are found to be sensitive to the densities, temperatures, proton fractions, and nuclear shapes. If we further increase temperature, eventually the nonuniform structures of nuclear pasta become unstable and are converted into uniform nuclear matter. The phase diagrams of nuclear matter are then estimated, which should be useful for understanding the evolutions of neutron stars, supernova dynamics, and binary neutron star mergers.

# Nuclear pasta structures at high temperatures

## Key claim

The paper investigates nuclear pasta structures at high temperatures using a relativistic mean field model with Thomas-Fermi approximation, finding that different crystalline structures can evolve into each other via volume-conserving deformations. The critical conditions for nuclear pasta to become disordered and behave like liquid are sensitive to densities, temperatures, proton fractions, and nuclear shapes, with the nonuniform structures becoming unstable and converting into uniform nuclear matter at sufficiently high temperatures.

## Method

The authors adopt the covariant density functional MTVTC within the relativistic mean field (RMF) framework, solving Klein-Gordon equations for boson fields and using Fermi-Dirac distributions for nucleons and electrons in the Thomas-Fermi approximation. They first fix the optimum Wigner-Seitz cell size using spherical or cylindrical approximations, then consider simple cubic (SC), body-centered cubic (BCC), and face-centered cubic (FCC) lattices for droplets or bubbles, simple and honeycomb configurations for rods or tubes, and slabs in a three-dimensional geometry with reflection symmetry. The density profiles are obtained by minimizing the total free energy at given total particle numbers, temperature, and cell size, with the iteration stopping when the deviation of local chemical potentials from constancy becomes insignificant.

## Key numbers

- The covariant density functional MTVTC predicts symmetry energy S = 32.46 MeV and its slope L = 89.39 MeV at saturation density n0 = 0.153 fm^-3
- Critical end point temperatures for liquid-gas transition: T = 6.45 MeV at Yp = 0.1, T = 13.5 MeV at Yp = 0.3, and T = 16.1 MeV at Yp = 0.5
- Critical end point densities: nb = 0.027 fm^-3 at Yp = 0.1, nb = 0.042 fm^-3 at Yp = 0.3, and nb = 0.047 fm^-3 at Yp = 0.5
- Differences in free energies per baryon of nuclear pasta in various shapes and lattice structures are typically on the order of tens of keV
- Nuclear pasta exists up to densities nb less than about 0.1 fm^-3 and temperatures T less than about 15 MeV, beyond which uniform phases are more stable
- The Bain path parameter for BCC to FCC transition is sigma_F = 2 - 2^(5/6) approximately equal to 0.2182
- For droplet phase at nb = 0.02 fm^-3 and Yp = 0.5, the droplet phase becomes unstable and converts to uniform matter at T = 15 MeV
- For the droplet phase in BCC lattice at nb approximately 0.06 fm^-3, each unit cell contains Np = Ne approximately 22 and Nn approximately 1442

## Context

This work extends previous investigations of nuclear pasta structures using Thomas-Fermi approximation in three-dimensional geometry (Okamoto 2012, Okamoto 2013, Xia 2021) by systematically examining finite temperature effects up to the transition to uniform nuclear matter. The results provide phase diagrams of nuclear matter useful for understanding neutron star evolution, supernova dynamics, and binary neutron star mergers, building on earlier studies of pasta phase transport and elastic properties (Chamel 2008, Caplan 2017) and prior work on crystalline structures (Oyamatsu 1984). The paper enables future studies of neutrino-pasta scattering, thermal conductivity in neutron star crusts, and gravitational wave emission from neutron star mountains.
