# Thermalization, Isotropization and Elliptic Flow from Nonequilibrium Initial Conditions with a Saturation Scale

> In this article we report on our results about the computation of the elliptic flow of the quark-gluon-plasma produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions, simulating the expansion of the fireball by solving the relativistic Boltzmann equation for the parton distribution function tuned at a fixed shear viscosity to entropy density ratio $η/s$. Our main goal is to put emphasis on the role of a saturation scale in the initial gluon spectrum, which makes the initial distribution far from a thermalized one. We find that the presence of the saturation scale reduces the efficiency in building-up the elliptic flow, even if the thermalization process is quite fast $τ_{therm} \approx 0.8 \,\rm fm/c$ and the pressure isotropization even faster $τ_{isotr} \approx 0.3 \,\rm fm/c$. The impact of the non-equilibrium implied by the saturation scale manifests for non-central collisions and can modify the estimate of the viscosity respect to the assumption of full thermalization in $p_T$-space. We find that the estimate of $η/s$ is modified from $η/s \approx 2/4π$ to $η/s \approx 1/4π$ at RHIC and from $η/s \approx 3/4π$ to $η/s \approx 2/4π$ at LHC. We complete our investigation by a study of the thermalization and isotropization times of the fireball for different initial conditions and values of $η/s$ showing how the latter affects both isotropization and thermalization. Lastly, we have seen that the range of values explored by the phase-space distribution function $f$ is such that at $p_T<0.5\, \rm GeV$ the inner part of the fireball stays with occupation number significantly larger than unity despite the fast longitudinal expansion, which might suggest the possibility of the formation of a transient Bose-Einstein Condensate.

## Key claim

The presence of a saturation scale in the initial gluon spectrum, which makes the initial distribution far from thermalized, reduces the efficiency of building up elliptic flow even though thermalization occurs within about 0.8 fm/c and pressure isotropization within about 0.3 fm/c; this non-equilibrium effect modifies the estimate of the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio from about 2/4π to about 1/4π at RHIC and from about 3/4π to about 2/4π at LHC.

## Method

The authors solve the relativistic Boltzmann equation for the parton distribution function, tuning the total cross section via the Chapmann-Enskog relation to achieve a fixed shear viscosity to entropy density ratio η/s. They initialize the fireball using three different conditions: a thermalized Glauber model (Th-Glauber), a thermalized factorized KLN model (Th-fKLN), and a full fKLN model that includes the saturation scale in momentum space, starting at τ₀ = 0.2 fm/c for the fKLN case and τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c for the thermalized cases at RHIC energies.

## Key numbers

- Thermalization time τ_therm ≈ 0.8 fm/c for the fKLN initial condition
- Pressure isotropization time τ_isotr ≈ 0.3 fm/c for the fKLN initial condition
- At RHIC (Au-Au at √s = 200 A GeV), the estimate of η/s is modified from about 2/4π to about 1/4π when using fKLN instead of thermalized initial conditions
- At LHC (Pb-Pb at √s = 2.76 A TeV), the estimate of η/s is modified from about 3/4π to about 2/4π
- For the fKLN initial condition at RHIC, the average saturation scale ⟨Q_s⟩ ≈ 1 GeV at b=0, y=0, with Q₀² = 1 GeV²
- At p_T < 0.5 GeV, the occupation number in the inner part of the fireball remains significantly larger than unity despite fast longitudinal expansion

## Context

This work extends previous viscous hydrodynamic studies (Luzum 2008, Song 2011, Alver 2010, Adare 2011) that used KLN initial conditions but assumed thermalization in momentum space, finding that the uncertainty in initial conditions translates into a factor-of-two uncertainty in η/s. By using kinetic theory rather than hydrodynamics, the authors can incorporate the full non-equilibrium momentum distribution from the fKLN model, which includes the saturation scale Q_s. This enables a more realistic estimate of η/s and suggests that previous hydrodynamic studies may have overestimated the viscosity. The finding of occupation numbers significantly larger than unity at low p_T points toward the possible formation of a transient Bose-Einstein condensate, which could be explored in future work.
