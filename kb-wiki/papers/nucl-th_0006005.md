# Weak proton capture on 3He

> The astrophysical S-factor for the proton weak capture on 3He is calculated with correlated-hyperspherical-harmonics bound and continuum wave functions corresponding to realistic Hamiltonians consisting of the Argonne v14 or Argonne v18 two-nucleon and Urbana-VIII or Urbana-IX three-nucleon interactions. The nuclear weak charge and current operators have vector and axial-vector components, that include one- and many-body terms. All possible multipole transitions connecting any of the p 3He S- and P-wave channels to the 4He bound state are considered. The S-factor at a p 3He center-of-mass energy of 10 keV, close to the Gamow-peak energy, is predicted to be 10.1 10^{-20} keV b with the AV18/UIX Hamiltonian, a factor of about 4.5 larger than the value adopted in the standard solar model. The P-wave transitions are found to be important, contributing about 40 % of the calculated S-factor. The energy dependence is rather weak: the AV18/UIX zero-energy S-factor is 9.64 10^{-20} keV b, only 5 % smaller than the 10 keV result quoted above. The model dependence is also found to be weak: the zero-energy S-factor is calculated to be 10.2 10^{-20} keV b with the older AV14/UVIII model, only 6 % larger than the AV18/UIX result. Our best estimate for the S-factor at 10 keV is therefore (10.1 \pm 0.6) 10^{-20} keV b, when the theoretical uncertainty due to the model dependence is included. This value for the calculated S-factor is not as large as determined in fits to the Super-Kamiokande data in which the hep flux normalization is free. However, the precise calculation of the S-factor and the consequent absolute prediction for the hep neutrino flux will allow much greater discrimination among proposed solar neutrino oscillation solutions.

## Key claim

The astrophysical S-factor for the proton weak capture on 3He is predicted to be (10.1 ± 0.6) × 10^{-20} keV b at 10 keV center-of-mass energy, approximately 4.5 times larger than the value adopted in the standard solar model, but this enhancement is smaller than the factor of 25–30 that would be needed to fully explain the observed excess of high-energy events in Super-Kamiokande data.

## Method

The authors used correlated-hyperspherical-harmonics (CHH) bound and continuum wave functions for the four-nucleon system, solving variationally for both the 4He ground state and p-3He scattering states. The nuclear Hamiltonian consisted of the Argonne v18 or Argonne v14 two-nucleon interactions combined with the Urbana-IX or Urbana-VIII three-nucleon interactions, and the weak charge and current operators included vector and axial-vector components with one-body, two-body, and many-body terms, with the axial N→Δ coupling constant adjusted to reproduce the Gamow-Teller matrix element in tritium β-decay. All multipole transitions connecting the p-3He S-wave channels (1S0, 3S1) and P-wave channels (3P0, 1P1, 3P1, 3P2) to the 4He bound state were included.

## Key numbers

- S-factor at 10 keV center-of-mass energy with AV18/UIX Hamiltonian: 10.1 × 10^{-20} keV b
- Zero-energy S-factor with AV18/UIX Hamiltonian: 9.64 × 10^{-20} keV b, only 5% smaller than the 10 keV result
- Zero-energy S-factor with AV14/UVIII Hamiltonian: 10.2 × 10^{-20} keV b, only 6% larger than the AV18/UIX result
- Best estimate S-factor at 10 keV: (10.1 ± 0.6) × 10^{-20} keV b, including theoretical uncertainty from model dependence
- P-wave transitions contribute approximately 40% of the calculated S-factor
- The SSM value for the S-factor adopted in the literature is 2.3 × 10^{-20} keV b, based on earlier work by Schiavilla et al. (1992)
- The AV18-only Hamiltonian (without three-nucleon interaction) predicts a zero-energy S-factor of 12.1 × 10^{-20} keV b

## Context

This work extends the earlier microscopic calculations of Schiavilla et al. (1992) and Carlson et al. (1991) by using more accurate CHH wave functions corresponding to the latest generation of realistic interactions (AV18/UIX), and by including all S- and P-wave capture channels as well as previously neglected axial charge operators and 1/m^2 relativistic corrections. The predicted S-factor is about 4.5 times larger than the SSM value but smaller than the factor of 25–30 suggested by Bahcall and Krastev (1998) as needed to fit the Super-Kamiokande excess, and the absolute prediction for the hep neutrino flux will allow greater discrimination among proposed solar neutrino oscillation solutions.
