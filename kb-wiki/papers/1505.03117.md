# A General Analysis of Direct Dark Matter Detection: From Microphysics to Observational Signatures

> Beginning with a set of simplified models for spin-0, spin-$\half$, and spin-1 dark matter candidates using completely general Lorentz invariant and renormalizable Lagrangians, we derive the full set of non-relativistic operators and nuclear matrix elements relevant for direct detection of dark matter, and use these to calculate rates and recoil spectra for scattering on various target nuclei. This allows us to explore what high energy physics constraints might be obtainable from direct detection experiments, what degeneracies exist, which operators are ubiquitous and which are unlikely or sub-dominant. We find that there are operators which are common to all spins as well operators which are unique to spin-$\half$ and spin-1 and elucidate two new operators which have not been previously considered. In addition we demonstrate how recoil energy spectra can distinguish fundamental microphysics if multiple target nuclei are used. Our work provides a complete roadmap for taking generic fundamental dark matter theories and calculating rates in direct detection experiments. This provides a useful guide for experimentalists designing experiments and theorists developing new dark matter models.

## Key claim

The paper provides a complete, model-independent framework connecting high-energy microphysics (simplified models for spin-0, spin-1/2, and spin-1 dark matter) to low-energy non-relativistic nuclear responses for direct detection, but the authors hedge that the analysis is restricted to renormalizable, Lorentz-invariant interactions and assumes a single mediator particle, meaning the framework may not capture all possible dark matter models or non-renormalizable interactions.

## Method

The authors construct simplified models for scalar, spinor, and vector dark matter coupled to quarks via scalar, vector, or spin-1/2 mediators, using completely general renormalizable Lagrangians consistent with Lorentz invariance and hermiticity. They integrate out the heavy mediator to obtain relativistic effective interactions, then take the non-relativistic limit and match the resulting operators onto the 15 non-relativistic effective field theory operators from Fitzpatrick et al. (2012). They also identify two new non-relativistic operators (O17 and O18) required for spin-1 dark matter, and compute the full set of nuclear electroweak responses (M, Sigma', Sigma'', Delta, Phi'', and related terms) for each interaction scenario.

## Key numbers

* Two new non-relativistic operators are identified for spin-1 dark matter: O17 = i (q/m_N) · S · v_perp and O18 = i (q/m_N) · S · S_N, where S is the symmetric combination of polarization vectors.
* The typical dark matter velocity in direct detection is O(100 km/s), leading to recoil momenta of O(< 100 keV).
* The analysis covers dark matter masses ranging from the GeV region to several orders of magnitude above, with nucleon masses of roughly GeV scale.
* The paper lists 15 non-relativistic operators (O1 through O15) from Fitzpatrick et al. (2012), plus the two new operators O17 and O18, for a total of 17 operators considered.

## Context

This work extends the non-relativistic effective field theory framework of Fitzpatrick et al. (2012) and Anand et al. (2013) by connecting it explicitly to UV-complete simplified models for dark matter of different spins, building on earlier work by Gresham et al. (2014) that showed using only spin-independent/spin-dependent form factors can lead to incorrect WIMP mass and cross-section inferences. The paper enables experimentalists to design direct detection experiments (e.g., LUX, XENON1T, DARWIN) with optimal target selection for discriminating between fundamental dark matter models, and provides theorists a roadmap for calculating rates from generic dark matter theories.
