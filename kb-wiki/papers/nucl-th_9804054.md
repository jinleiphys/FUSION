# Relativistic y-scaling and the Coulomb sum rule in nuclei

> In this paper dividing factors GL and GT are constructed for the longitudinal and transverse responses of the relativistic Fermi gas in such a way that the reduced responses so obtained scale. These factors parallel another dividing factor studied previously, HL, that yields a (different) reduced response which fulfills the Coulomb sum rule. GL, GT and HL are all found to be only very weakly model-dependent, thus providing essentially universal dividing factors. To explore the residual degree of dependence which remains, the scaling and sum rule properties of several specific models have been considered. It is seen that the relativistic Fermi gas (by construction) and also typical shell-model reduced responses successfully scale and satisfy the Coulomb sum rule, as do experimental results at medium to high momentum transfers. On the other hand, it is observed that the quantum hadrodynamic model does so only if interaction effects become weaker with increasing momentum transfer, as predicted in the most recent versions of that model.

## Key claim

The dividing factors \(G_L\), \(G_T\) (for scaling) and \(H_L\) (for the Coulomb sum rule) are only very weakly model-dependent and thus essentially universal, but the quantum hadrodynamic model satisfies scaling and the Coulomb sum rule only if interaction effects become weaker with increasing momentum transfer, as predicted in the most recent versions of that model.

## Method

The authors constructed dividing factors \(G_L\) and \(G_T\) from the relativistic Fermi gas (RFG) model to produce reduced longitudinal and transverse responses that scale, and compared these to a previously studied factor \(H_L\) that yields a reduced response fulfilling the Coulomb sum rule. They then tested the scaling and sum-rule properties of the RFG, a hybrid shell-model (HM), and the quantum hadrodynamic (QHD) model, using the same dividing factors for all models. The analysis was performed for the longitudinal response at various momentum transfers, examining how well each model's reduced response becomes a function only of the scaling variable \(\psi\) (or \(y_{\text{RFG}}\)) at high \(q\).

## Key numbers

- The RFG scaling variable \(\psi\) is confined to the range \(-1 \leq \psi \leq 1\) by construction.
- For the RFG, the reduced scaling function is \(F_L^{\text{RFG}}(\psi) = \frac{3\xi_F}{2m_N \eta^3_F} (1 - \psi^2)\theta(1 - \psi^2)\left[1+\frac{1}{2} \xi_F(1+\psi^2)\right]\), where \(\xi_F = \epsilon_F - 1\) and \(\eta_F = k_F/m_N\).
- The HM uses a Fermi momentum of 237 MeV/c (versus 230 MeV/c for the RFG) and incorporates a shift \(\lambda_{\text{shift}} = (T_F + E_S)/(2m_N)\).
- The QHD model was tested with effective masses \(m^\ast_N = 0.8\, m_N\) and \(m^\ast_N = 0.68\, m_N\) at momentum transfers \(q = 0.55\) and 1.14 GeV/c.
- The correction term involving \(\xi_F\) in the dividing factors provides only few percent corrections at high momentum transfers, with \(\xi_F\) attaining values as large as 0.04 only in the heaviest nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies on the Coulomb sum rule (Barbaro et al., 1997; Amore et al., 1997) by connecting them to the \(y\)-scaling approach developed by Sick (1980s) and Pace (1980s). It also builds on the RFG-based scaling variable \(\psi\) introduced by Alberico et al. (1988) and tested against medium- and high-energy data by Williamson et al. (1997) and Donnelly et al. (1997). The paper enables future constraints on nuclear models by showing that experimental scaling and Coulomb sum rule success at high momentum transfers can rule out models like QHD unless their interaction effects weaken with increasing \(q\), as seen in comparisons with the Hartree version of QHD (Horowitz, 1980s; Walecka, 1970s).
