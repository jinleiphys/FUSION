# Report by N. Dinh Dang, V. Kim Au, T. Suzuki, and A. Arima on the Comment by V. Yu. Ponomarev

## Key claim

The authors defend the phonon damping model (PDM) against a Comment claiming its physical content is doubtful and its description of the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) is not justified, arguing that the Comment's arguments are either wrong or irrelevant. The defense rests on showing that the Comment misrepresents the PDM's strength function as a Breit-Wigner distribution, misapplies the second moment formula, and uses random single-particle energies that do not correspond to the PDM.

## Method

The authors calculate E1 resonances in neutron-rich oxygen and calcium isotopes within the quasiparticle representation of the phonon damping model (PDM-1 version), including superfluid pairing interaction. The damping gamma_q(omega) is calculated microscopically as the imaginary part of the analytic continuation of the polarization operator into the complex energy plane, and the GDR energy is found as the solution of the pole equation omega_bar - omega_q - P_q(omega) = 0. The model uses two phenomenologically selected parameters (omega_q, f_1) chosen to reproduce the GDR in double closed-shell nuclei, then kept fixed across isotopic chains.

## Key numbers

- The parameter f_1 for 16O is 0.6982 MeV, kept unchanged throughout the chain of oxygen isotopes.
- The EWS of E1 strength below 5 MeV for 40Ca is around 0.25% of the Thomas-Reich-Kuhn (TRK) sum rule.
- The EWS of E1 strength below 5 MeV for 48Ca is around 0.52% of the TRK sum rule.
- Experimental values for comparison: (0.025 +/- 0.004)% of TRK for 40Ca, and (0.29 +/- 0.04)% of TRK for 48Ca, reported by Hartmann et al. (2000).

## Context

This report is a direct rebuttal to a Comment by Ponomarev (submitted to Physical Review C, later rejected) criticizing the phonon damping model calculations of Dang, Au, Suzuki, and Arima (2001) for E1 resonances in neutron-rich nuclei. The work extends the PDM framework previously developed by Dang and Arima (1998) and Dang, Tanabe, and Arima (2000), and enables comparison with experimental data from Hartmann et al. (2000) for average characteristics of E1 resonances such as overall cross-section shape, width, energy, and energy-weighted sum rule strength.
