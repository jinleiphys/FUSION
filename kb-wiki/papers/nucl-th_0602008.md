# alpha-nucleus potentials, alpha-decay half-lives, and shell closures for superheavy nuclei

> Systematic alpha-nucleus folding potentials are used to analyze alpha-decay half-lives of superheavy nuclei. Preformation factors of about several per cent are found for all nuclei under study. The systematic behavior of the preformation factors and the volume integrals of the potentials allows to predict alpha-decay energies and half-lives for unknown nuclei. Shell closures can be determined from measured alpha-decay energies using the discontinuity of the volume integral at shell closures. For the first time a double shell closure is predicted for Z_magic = 132, N_magic = 194, and A_magic = 326 from the systematics of folding potentials. The calculated alpha-decay half-lives remain far below one nanosecond for superheavy nuclei with double shell closure and masses above A > 300 independent of the precise knowledge of the magic proton and neutron numbers.

## Key claim
Systematic alpha-nucleus folding potentials, combined with the Wildermuth condition for the number of nodes in the bound-state wave function, yield preformation factors of about 8% for superheavy even-even nuclei. The paper predicts a double shell closure at Z_magic = 132, N_magic = 194, A_magic = 326 from the discontinuity of the volume integral of the folding potential, but notes that the calculated alpha-decay half-lives for such doubly-magic nuclei remain far below one nanosecond regardless of the exact magic numbers.

## Method
The alpha-nucleus potential was constructed using a double-folding procedure with an effective interaction (Satchler and Love 1979, Kobos et al. 1984), using nuclear densities from Vries (1987) for the alpha particle and two-parameter Fermi distributions for the target nuclei. The total potential is the sum of the scaled folding potential (strength parameter lambda between 1.0 and 1.3) and a Coulomb potential from a homogeneously charged sphere. The alpha-decay width was calculated using the semi-classical formula from Gurvitz and Kalbermann (1987), with the number of nodes determined by the Wildermuth condition (Q = 22 for the superheavy region). Preformation factors were obtained as the ratio of the calculated half-life (assuming P=1) to the experimental half-life.

## Key numbers
- Average preformation factor P ≈ 8% for superheavy even-even nuclei, with most results within a factor of three uncertainty.
- Volume integrals J_R around 300 MeV fm^3 for all nuclei under study, with a lower limit of J_R ≈ 280 MeV fm^3 at shell closures.
- For the decay of 298120 → 294118: predicted decay energy E = 12.87 MeV and predicted half-life T_pre ≈ 8 μs using P = 8%.
- For the doubly-magic nucleus 326132: predicted decay energy E = 18.26 MeV, calculated half-life T_calc = 1.16 × 10^{-12} s (P=1), and predicted half-life T_pre = 1.5 × 10^{-11} s (P=8%).
- For 310126 → 306124 (with Z=126, N=184 shell closures): predicted decay energy E = 18.82 MeV, calculated half-life T_calc = 2.1 × 10^{-14} s (P=1), and predicted half-life T_pre = 2.6 × 10^{-13} s (P=8%).
- For 212Po = 208Pb ⊗ α: quantum-mechanical calculation yields Γ = 3.52 × 10^{-8} eV and T_calc = 13.0 ns; semi-classical approximation yields T_calc = 8.7 ns (about 30% lower).
- Magic numbers derived: Z_magic = 132 ± 4, N_magic = 194 ± 4, A_magic = 326 ± 6.

## Context
This work extends the systematic folding potential approach of Mohr (2000) and Fujiwara et al. (2002) to superheavy nuclei, providing an alternative to the narrow potentials used by Buck et al. (1993) and the smaller quantum numbers used by Xu et al. (2005). The volume integrals J_R ≈ 300 MeV fm^3 are consistent with those derived from elastic alpha scattering by Atzrott et al. (1996), Mohr et al. (1997), and others. The predicted double shell closure at Z=132, N=194 differs from the widely discussed Z=126, N=184 shell closures (e.g., Liran et al. 2002, Baran et al. 2005) and from other predictions (e.g., Silisteanu et al. 2004). The finding that alpha-decay half-lives remain below one nanosecond for all superheavy nuclei above A > 300 sets a strong constraint on the observability of such nuclei.
