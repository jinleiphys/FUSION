# Microcanonical vs. canonical thermodynamics

> The microcanonical ensemble is in important physical situations different from the canonical one even in the thermodynamic limit. In contrast to the canonical ensemble it does not suppress spatially inhomogeneous configurations like phase separations. It is shown how phase transitions of first order can be defined and classified unambiguously for finite systems without the use of the thermodynamic limit. It is further shown that in the case of the 10-states Potts model as well for the liquid-gas transition in Na, K, and Fe the microcanonical transition temperature, latent heat and interphase surface tension are similar to their bulk values for $\sim 200-3000$ particles. For Na and K the number of surface atoms keeps approximately constant over most of the transition energies because the evaporation of monomers is compensated by an increasing number of fragments with $\ge 2$ atoms (multifragmentation).

# Microcanonical vs. canonical thermodynamics

## Key claim

The microcanonical ensemble differs from the canonical ensemble even in the thermodynamic limit for first-order phase transitions, because the canonical ensemble suppresses spatially inhomogeneous configurations such as phase separations. Phase transitions of first order can be defined and classified unambiguously for finite systems of some hundred particles without any reference to the thermodynamic limit.

## Method

The authors performed microcanonical Metropolis Monte Carlo (MMMC) simulations of the 10-state Potts model on a 2D lattice of up to L=200, and of liquid-gas transitions in systems of 200–3000 Na, K, and Fe atoms at normal pressure. They extracted the microcanonical caloric equation of state T(E/N) and identified first-order phase transitions by the characteristic S-bending (backbending) of the inverse temperature β(ε) = ∂s/∂ε, from which the transition temperature, latent heat, and interphase surface tension were determined.

## Key numbers

- For the Potts model with L=200 and q=10, the surface tension for a lattice of L=12 deviates by only 10% from the bulk value, and the transition temperature deviates by less than 2%.
- For Na with N₀=200: T_tr = 816 K, q_lat = 0.791 eV; for N₀=1000: T_tr = 866 K, q_lat = 0.871 eV; bulk T_tr = 1156 K, q_lat = 0.923 eV.
- For K with N₀=200: T_tr = 697 K, q_lat = 0.62 eV; for N₀=1000: T_tr = 767 K, q_lat = 0.7 eV; bulk T_tr = 1033 K, q_lat = 0.80 eV.
- For Fe with N₀=200: T_tr = 2600 K, q_lat = 2.77 eV; for N₀=1000: T_tr = 2910 K, q_lat = 3.18 eV; for N₀=3000: T_tr = 2971 K, q_lat = 3.34 eV; bulk T_tr = 3158 K, q_lat = 3.55 eV.
- For Na and K, the number of surface atoms (N_eff^{2/3}) remains approximately constant over most of the transition energies because monomer evaporation is compensated by an increasing number of fragments with ≥2 atoms (multifragmentation).

## Context

This work extends the microcanonical analysis of phase transitions previously applied to nuclear multifragmentation (Gross 1995, Gross 1994) and contrasts with the canonical ensemble approach, which suppresses coexisting phases by a factor exp(−N^{2/3}σ/T_tr) as shown by Binder (1982) and Janke (1994). The results enable the definition and study of phase transitions in systems that lack a thermodynamic limit, such as self-gravitating systems or charged systems under unscreened Coulomb repulsion like hot nuclei.
