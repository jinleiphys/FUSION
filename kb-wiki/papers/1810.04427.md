# Investigation of the pairing effect in 10B nucleus compared with 10Be and 10C nuclei by using the extended THSR wave function

> In order to study the nucleon-nucleon pairing effects in clustering nuclei, we formulate a superposed Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Roepke (THSR) wave function, which includes both molecular-orbit and pairing configurations explicitly. With this new wave function, we investigate the abnormal deuteron-like pn-pairing effect in 10B with T=0 and S=1 (isoscalar) by comparing with isovector NN pairs (T=1, S=1) in 10Be and 10C. Energies are calculated for the ground states of 10Be, 10B and 10C nuclei, and the 1+ excited state of 10B. These energies are essentially improved comparing with studies using previous version of THSR wave function. Further more, overlaps between the total wave function and the pairing component indicate that the NN pairing effect is more visible in 10B than in 10Be and 10C. By analyzing the energies and the overlaps between wave function components, we observe two different mechanisms enhancing the formation of deuteron-like pairs in 10B. We also discuss the pairing effect by showing average distances between components in each nucleus and density distributions of valance nucleons.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the isoscalar (T=0, S=1) deuteron-like proton-neutron pairing effect in 10B is stronger than the isovector (T=1, S=1) pairing effects in 10Be and 10C, but this conclusion is conditional on the specific extended THSR+pair wave function framework and the particular choice of the Volkov No. 2 and G3RS interactions used in the calculation.

## Method

The authors formulated a superposed Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Roepke (THSR) wave function, called "THSR+pair," which adds a compact NN pairing configuration term to the traditional molecular-orbit THSR wave function for the A=10 nuclei composed of two alpha clusters and two valence nucleons. Variational calculations with angular-momentum and isospin projection were performed to obtain ground-state energies of 10Be, 10B, and 10C, as well as the 1+ excited state of 10B, using the Volkov No. 2 central force and G3RS spin-orbit interaction. Overlaps between the molecular-orbit component, the pairing component, and the total wave function were computed to quantify the pairing strength, and average distances between valence nucleons and density distributions were analyzed.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energy of 10B(3+0) improved by 2.0 MeV with THSR+pair wave function (from -59.8 MeV to -61.8 MeV) compared to traditional THSR wave function; experimental value is -64.8 MeV.
- Ground-state energy of 10Be(0+1) improved by 0.9 MeV (from -58.3 MeV to -59.2 MeV); experimental value is -65.0 MeV.
- Ground-state energy of 10C(0+1) improved by 0.9 MeV (from -54.4 MeV to -55.3 MeV); experimental value is -60.3 MeV.
- Excitation energy of the 1+0 state in 10B improved from 2.8 MeV to 1.0 MeV with THSR+pair wave function; experimental excitation energy is 0.7 MeV.
- Energy of the 1+0 excited state of 10B improved by 3.8 MeV (from -57.0 MeV to -60.8 MeV) with THSR+pair wave function.
- Squared overlap between pairing component and total THSR+pair wave function for 10B(3+0) ground state is 93.3%, compared to 56.6% for 10Be(0+1) and 67.5% for 10C(0+1).
- Squared overlap between molecular-orbit component and pairing component for 10B(3+0) ground state is 75.8%, compared to 45.5% for 10Be and 43.1% for 10C.
- Squared overlap between pairing component and total wave function for 10B(1+0) excited state is 87.5%.

## Context

This work extends the THSR wave function approach, previously successful for describing alpha-cluster states such as the Hoyle state in 12C (Tohsaki 2001), to include explicit NN pairing configurations for the first time. It builds on studies of NN pairing in light nuclei by En'yo (2015) and Morita (2016), and on earlier THSR formulations for valence nucleons by Lyu (2015, 2016, 2017). The results enable future investigations of NN correlations in cluster states and provide theoretical inputs for experimental probes such as (p, pd) nuclear reactions as discussed by Chazono (2017).
