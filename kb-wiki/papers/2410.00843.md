# Ab initio computations of atomic nuclei

> Ab initio computations of atomic nuclei, based on Hamiltonians from effective field theories of quantum chromodynamics, are now routinely used to predict and describe properties of medium heavy nuclei, and even the heavy nucleus $^{208}$Pb has been reached. These lecture notes describe what are the central ideas and concepts behind the Hamiltonians and some of the methods that have enabled this progress.

## Key claim

These lecture notes argue that ab initio nuclear computations, which aim to be systematically improvable while maximizing predictive power, have reached medium-heavy nuclei and even the heavy nucleus 208Pb, but this progress relies on a careful balance between high resolution (starting from quantum chromodynamics) and predictive power, with the latter currently limited for most nuclei when starting from the highest resolution.

## Method

The paper reviews the conceptual framework of ab initio nuclear theory, starting from chiral effective field theory to derive nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon interactions, then solving the nuclear many-body problem using methods such as coupled-cluster theory (with truncations like CCSD or CCSDT), the in-medium similarity renormalization group (IMSRG), and symmetry projection techniques. The coupled-cluster method is used as an illustrative example, where the ground state is written as an exponential of cluster operators acting on a reference state, and the similarity-transformed Hamiltonian is decoupled from particle-hole excitations.

## Key numbers

- The coupled-cluster singles doubles (CCSD) approximation scales as O(A^2 M^4) and the singles doubles triples (CCSDT) approximation scales as O(A^3 M^6), where M is the number of single-particle states (currently up to M ~ 4000).
- The Bogoliubov coupled-cluster method scales as O(M^6) for two-body and O(M^9) for three-body approximations; the IMSRG scales similarly at O(M^6) and O(M^9) when truncated at the two- and three-body cluster level.
- Low-lying excitation energies in heavy nuclei scale as the shell gap, approximately Delta_epsilon ~ (k_F^2 / 2m) A^{-1/3}.
- Rotational band excitation energies scale as O(A^{-5/3}) based on the scaling of the moment of inertia.
- For the nuclei 40,48Ca computed at high order in chiral effective field theory, the ground states were overbound by about 1 MeV per nucleon and the corresponding radii were about 20% too small.

## Context

These lecture notes extend the line of work on ab initio nuclear structure that began with Weinberg's (1990, 1991) application of chiral effective field theory to nuclear forces, and they build on the coupled-cluster method as reviewed by Hagen (2014), the IMSRG as reviewed by Hergert (2016), and Gorkov-Green's function methods as reviewed by Dickhoff (2004) and Soma (2013). The paper discusses the tension between high-resolution approaches starting from lattice quantum chromodynamics (which are currently limited to one or two hadrons, as shown by Durr (2008) and Borsanyi (2014)) and lower-resolution density functional methods (Vretanar 2005, Delaroche 2010, Niksic 2011, Erler 2012) that have predictive power across the nuclear chart. The work enables future systematic improvements by clarifying the scaling of computational costs and the separation of scales between short-range correlations (which yield the bulk of nuclear binding) and long-range correlations from symmetry projection.
