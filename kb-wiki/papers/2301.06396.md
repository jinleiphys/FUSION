# Machine learning in nuclear physics at low and intermediate energies

> Machine learning is becoming a new paradigm for scientific research in various research fields due to its exciting and powerful capability of modeling tools used for big-data processing task. In this mini-review, we first briefly introduce different methodologies of the machine learning algorithms and techniques. As a snapshot of many applications by machine learning, some selected applications are presented especially for low and intermediate energy nuclear physics, which include topics on theoretical applications in nuclear structure, nuclear reactions, properties of nuclear matter as well as experimental applications in event identification/reconstruction, complex system control and firmware performance. Finally, we also give a brief summary and outlook on the possible directions of using machine learning in low-intermediate energy nuclear physics and possible improvements in ML algorithms.

## Key claim
This mini-review surveys selected applications of machine learning (ML) in low- and intermediate-energy nuclear physics, covering both theoretical topics (nuclear structure, reactions, nuclear matter) and experimental topics (event identification/reconstruction, system control, firmware performance), but it does not claim to be exhaustive and explicitly notes that only "part of ML algorithms is mentioned due to the scope of this review paper."

## Method
The authors first introduce common ML methodologies (neural networks, Bayesian neural networks, convolutional neural networks, decision trees, support vector machines, Bayesian inference, and naive Bayesian classifiers) and then present a snapshot of recent applications organized by subfield: nuclear structure observables (masses, charge radii, decay properties), nuclear reactions, nuclear matter properties, and experimental data processing. The review is based on published literature up to 2023, with each application described in terms of the ML algorithm used and the physical problem addressed.

## Key numbers
- The rms deviations between experimental nuclear masses and theoretical mass model predictions are generally around 500 keV, while deviations among various mass models can reach tens of MeV when extrapolated to unknown neutron-rich regions.
- The Bayesian machine learning (BML) mass model crosses the accuracy threshold of 100 keV in the experimentally known region.
- About 1000 known nuclear charge radii have been summarized and evaluated; after refinements with KRR and BNN methods, the rms deviations to known charge radius data are about 0.015 fm.
- The rms deviations of BNN predictions for beta-decay half-lives are compared with those of RHB+QRPA, SHFB+QRPA, FRDM+QRPA, and SHFB+FAM methods (specific numerical values for these comparisons are not given in the truncated text).

## Context
This review extends the growing body of work applying ML to nuclear physics, following earlier reviews such as Bedaque (2021) and Boehnlein (2022). It covers applications that build on prior nuclear mass predictions using ANN (Gazula 1992, Athanassopoulos 2004), SVM (Clark 2006), and more recent methods like BNN (Utama 2016, Niu 2019), LightGBM (Gao 2021), RBF (Wang 2011, Niu 2016), KRR (Wu 2020, Wu 2021), and NBP (Liu 2021). The review enables researchers to identify which ML techniques have been most successful for specific nuclear physics problems and highlights open questions such as improving ML algorithms and extending applications to unexplored nuclear regions.
