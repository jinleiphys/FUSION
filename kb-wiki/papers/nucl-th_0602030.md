# The stability and the shape of the heaviest nuclei

> In this paper, we report a systematic study of the heaviest nuclei within the relativistic mean field (RMF) model. By comparing our results with those of the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method (HFB) and the finite range droplet model (FRDM), the stability and the shape of the heaviest nuclei are discussed. The theoretical predictions as well as the existing experimental data indicate that the experimentally synthesized superheavy nuclei are in between the fission stability line, the line connecting the nucleus with maximum binding energy per nucleon in each isotopic chain, and the $β$-stability line, the line connecting the nucleus with maximum binding energy per nucleon in each isobaric chain. It is shown that both the fission stability line and the $β$-stability line tend to be more proton rich in the superheavy region. Meanwhile, all the three theoretical models predict most synthesized superheavy nuclei to be deformed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that experimentally synthesized superheavy nuclei lie in a "superheavy valley" between the fission stability line and the β-stability line, and that both stability lines become more proton rich in the superheavy region. The authors hedge that this conclusion is drawn from energy considerations only, without including alpha-decay or spontaneous fission lifetime calculations.

## Method

The authors performed systematic relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations with BCS pairing for approximately 600 superheavy nuclei with Z = 101–120 and N = 134–190, using the TMA effective force and a state-dependent BCS method with a zero-range δ-force pairing interaction. Quadrupole constrained calculations were carried out on an axially deformed harmonic-oscillator basis with N_f = N_b = 20 shells to obtain energy surfaces and ground-state deformations. The RMF+BCS results were compared with predictions from the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method (HFB-8) and the finite range droplet model (FRDM).

## Key numbers

- The pairing strength used was V_0 = 300 + 120/A^{1/3} (in MeV·fm^3), with a weak mass number dependence.
- The theoretical fission stability line for N > 126 was approximated by Z = (17.105 ± 0.959) + (0.577 ± 0.006)N (theory, averaged over RMF+BCS, HFB-8, and FRDM).
- The experimental fission stability line from Audi03 was approximated by Z = 16.118 + 0.580 N.
- The phenomenological β-stability line from Bohr-Mottelson was given by N − Z = 6.0 × 10^(−3) A^(5/3).
- The RMF+BCS and HFB-8 calculations predicted very strongly prolate deformations with β_2 ≥ 0.45 for nuclei in the upper-right corner of the studied region, and β_2 ranging from 0.15 to 0.35 for nuclei in the lower-left corner.
- The FRDM model predicted a transition from moderately deformed shapes to spherical shapes from the lower-left to the upper-right corner, with a probable doubly magic nucleus at ²⁹⁸₁₈₄114.

## Context

This work extends previous RMF studies of superheavy nuclei (Geng 2003, Zhang 2004) by performing the first systematic RMF calculation with deformation and pairing properly treated across a large region of the nuclear chart. The results are compared with the HFB-8 mass table (Samyn 2004) and the FRDM mass table (Moller 1995), which achieved root-mean-square deviations of about 0.6 MeV for binding energies of known nuclei. The paper enables future searches for the superheavy island by narrowing the search region to the "superheavy valley" between the fission stability and β-stability lines, and identifies the need for more precise single-particle spectra to resolve model-dependent predictions for the next doubly magic nucleus near N = 184 and Z = 114.
