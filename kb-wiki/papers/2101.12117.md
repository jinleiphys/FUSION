# Nuclear binding energy predictions using neural networks: Application of the multilayer perceptron

> In recent years, artificial neural networks and their applications for large data sets have became a crucial part of scientific research. In this work, we implement the Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), which is a class of feedforward artificial neural network (ANN), to predict ground-state binding energies of atomic nuclei. Two different MLP architectures with three and four hidden layers are used to study their effects on the predictions. To train the MLP architectures, two different inputs are used along with the latest atomic mass table and changes in binding energy predictions are also analyzed in terms of the changes in the input channel. It is seen that using appropriate MLP architectures and putting more physical information in the input channels, MLP can make fast and reliable predictions for binding energies of atomic nuclei, which is also comparable to the microscopic energy density functionals.

## Key claim

The Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) can predict ground-state binding energies with root-mean-square deviations as low as 1.84 MeV for a testing set of 1017 nuclei, which is comparable to microscopic energy density functionals but not as accurate as the best microscopic-macroscopic models (FRDM-2012 at 0.99 MeV and WS3 at 0.55 MeV).

## Method

The authors implemented two MLP architectures (three hidden layers with 32-16-8 units and four hidden layers with 32-32-16-8 units) using the ReLU activation function and the Adam optimizer. They trained on 70% of 3388 nuclei from the AME2016 mass table (A >= 10, N >= 8), using two input sets: (Z, A) alone, and (Z, A, delta, P) where delta is the pairing term and P is the promiscuity factor encoding valence proton-neutron interactions. The models were evaluated on the remaining 30% testing set and compared against the microscopic models UNEDF1 and SkM* and the microscopic-macroscopic models FRDM-2012 and WS3.

## Key numbers

- Best MLP result: root-mean-square deviation of 1.84 MeV for the four-layer architecture (32-32-16-8) with four inputs (Z, A, delta, P) on 1017 testing nuclei
- Three-layer MLP (32-16-8) with four inputs gave 2.16 MeV rms deviation on the same testing set
- For the three-layer MLP with four inputs, rms deviations by region: 3.39 MeV for light nuclei (Z < 20, 93 nuclei), 2.10 MeV for medium-heavy (20 <= Z <= 82, 696 nuclei), 1.61 MeV for super-heavy (Z > 82, 228 nuclei)
- For the four-layer MLP with four inputs, rms deviations by region: 3.03 MeV for light, 1.58 MeV for medium-heavy, 1.94 MeV for super-heavy
- Comparison on 956 common nuclei: MLP three-layer 1.97 MeV, MLP four-layer 1.72 MeV, UNEDF1 2.13 MeV, SkM* 7.81 MeV, FRDM-2012 0.99 MeV, WS3 0.55 MeV
- Adding pairing and promiscuity factors improved the three-layer MLP rms deviation by 45.73% (from 3.98 MeV to 2.16 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the application of neural networks to nuclear mass predictions, following earlier studies using neural networks for mass excess (Gazula 1992), mass defects (Athanassopoulos 2004), and ground-state energies (Bayram 2014). The authors specifically implement the Multilayer Perceptron architecture, which they note had not been previously applied to nuclear binding energy data. The results are comparable to the microscopic energy density functionals UNEDF1 (2.13 MeV) and SkM* (7.81 MeV), though less accurate than the microscopic-macroscopic models FRDM-2012 (0.99 MeV) and WS3 (0.55 MeV). The work enables future extensions including additional physical inputs and using neural networks to train the residues of theoretical models, as done by Niu (2018) and others.
