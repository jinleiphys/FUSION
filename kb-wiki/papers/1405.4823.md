# Spectroscopic properties of nuclear Skyrme energy density functionals

> We address the question of how to improve the agreement between theoretical nuclear single-particle energies (SPEs) and experiment. Empirically, in doubly magic nuclei, the SPEs can be deduced from spectroscopic properties of odd nuclei that have one more, or one less neutron or proton. Theoretically, bare SPEs, before being confronted with experiment, must be corrected for the effects of the particle-vibration-coupling (PVC). In the present work, we determine the PVC corrections in a fully self-consistent way. Then, we adjust the SPEs, with PVC corrections included, to empirical data. In this way, the agreement with experiment, on average, improves; nevertheless, large discrepancies still remain. We conclude that the main source of disagreement is still in the underlying mean fields, and not in including or neglecting the PVC corrections.

## Key claim

After performing a fully self-consistent adjustment of Skyrme energy-density-functional parameters to empirical single-particle energies (SPEs) with particle-vibration-coupling (PVC) corrections included, the agreement with experiment improves only slightly on average, and large discrepancies remain; the authors conclude that the main source of disagreement is still in the underlying mean fields, not in including or neglecting the PVC corrections.

## Method

The authors calculated PVC corrections to single-particle energies in six doubly magic nuclei (16O, 40Ca, 48Ca, 56Ni, 132Sn, 208Pb) using five Skyrme EDF parametrizations (SAMi, SLy5, SIII, SkM*, SkP) with effective masses ranging from 0.675 to 1.0. They performed fully self-consistent mean-field, RPA, and PVC calculations in a spherical harmonic-oscillator basis, then used linear regression to refit the 12 Skyrme coupling constants to empirical SPEs from several data sets, both with and without PVC corrections included.

## Key numbers

- Effective masses of the five Skyrme parametrizations range from m*/m = 0.675 to 1.0.
- PVC corrections were calculated using phonons with multipolarities J = 0 to 15, with significant impact only up to J = 6.
- The single-particle and phonon spaces were restricted to below 15 MeV and 30 MeV, respectively.
- Only phonons contributing more than 5% to the non-energy-weighted sum rule were included.
- Residuals of bare and PVC-corrected SPEs relative to empirical data reach up to about 4 MeV.
- The number of empirical SPEs used in fits was M = 93, 83, 78, 48, and 49 for data sets A, B, C, M, and S, respectively.
- After refitting, the total rms deviation between theory and experiment could not be reduced below about 1 MeV for data sets A, B, C, and S.
- For data set M (where three evaluations agree within 200 keV), the rms deviation went down to about 0.8 MeV.
- For data set S (spectroscopic factors larger than 0.8), the rms deviation stayed at about 1.1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing debate about many-body corrections to nuclear single-particle energies, following studies such as Colo (2010) and other references cited in the paper. The authors show that even after fully self-consistent inclusion of PVC corrections and refitting of EDF parameters, the rms deviation cannot be pushed below about 1 MeV, suggesting that the limitation lies in the form of the Skyrme EDF itself rather than in perturbative corrections. This enables future work to focus on improving the underlying functional forms rather than adding higher-order corrections.
