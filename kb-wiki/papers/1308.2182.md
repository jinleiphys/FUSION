# Event-by-event Hydrodynamic Simulations for Relativistic Heavy-ion Collisions

> In this thesis, I show my Ph.D. work on event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations for relativistic heavy-ion collision. I show that event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations have become an indispensable tool for studying relativistic heavy-ion collisions and how it can be used to explain many phenomena. Different chapters focus on different topics; it mainly includes: Chap 2: comparison between single-shot hydrodynamics event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations. Chap 3: using the elliptic and triangular flow data measured by the ALICE collaboration at the LHC to constrain initial condition models. Chap 4: study on correlations between event-plane angles. Chap 5: how resonance decay calculation can be speed up by a factor of 10. Chap 6: study on fluctuations of event planes angle Ψn(pT) and their theoretical and experimental consequences. Chap 7: sampling particles according to the Cooper-Frye formula.

## Key claim

Event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations are indispensable for quantitative precision in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, but the simplifying assumptions that allow single-shot hydrodynamics to substitute for them (such as Gaussian ellipticity fluctuations and linear hydrodynamic response) hold well only for central to mid-central collisions (up to ~40% centrality) and break down for peripheral collisions.

## Method

The author performed event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations using Monte Carlo Glauber (MC-Glauber) and Monte Carlo fKLN (MC-KLN) models to generate fluctuating initial entropy density profiles for 200 A GeV Au+Au collisions, then evolved them hydrodynamically and compared the resulting harmonic flow coefficients with those obtained from single-shot simulations using smooth averaged initial profiles. The study systematically compared different definitions of eccentricity (participant, reaction plane, and cumulant-based) and tested the validity of Gaussian and Bessel-Gaussian fluctuation assumptions across centrality classes.

## Key numbers

- MC-KLN ellipticities exceed MC-Glauber ones by an approximately constant factor of around 1.2 for non-central collisions
- For centralities greater than 40%, single-shot hydrodynamic simulations with reaction-plane averaged initial conditions should not be trusted quantitatively
- The fitted slope between energy-weighted and entropy-weighted participant ellipticities is 1.00 (from 6000 MC-KLN events across multiple centrality bins)
- 10,000 initial profiles were generated for each impact parameter (30,000 for b = 0, 1, and 2 fm) for the eccentricity comparisons
- The kinetic decoupling temperature used was T_dec = 140 MeV, and the chemical freeze-out temperature was T_chem = 165 MeV
- The hard fraction parameter in the two-component MC-Glauber model was delta = 0.14

## Context

This work extends the comparison between event-by-event and single-shot hydrodynamic simulations initiated in Qiu (2011), supplementing those ideal-fluid calculations with nonzero shear viscosities. It builds on the framework of Alver (2010) for defining higher-order eccentricity coefficients and Voloshin (2007) for Bessel-Gaussian fluctuation analysis. The results enable experimental distinction between MC-Glauber and MC-KLN initial condition models through comparison of viscous suppression of elliptic and triangular flow, and demonstrate that for peripheral collisions there is no substitute for event-by-event hydrodynamics when comparing with experimental elliptic flow data from the ALICE collaboration at the LHC.
