# Machine Learning for the Prediction of Converged Energies from Ab Initio Nuclear Structure Calculations

> The prediction of nuclear observables beyond the finite model spaces that are accessible through modern ab initio methods, such as the no-core shell model, pose a challenging task in nuclear structure theory. It requires reliable tools for the extrapolation of observables to infinite many-body Hilbert spaces along with reliable uncertainty estimates. In this work we present a universal machine learning tool capable of capturing observable-specific convergence patterns independent of nucleus and interaction. We show that, once trained on few-body systems, artificial neural networks can produce accurate predictions for a broad range of light nuclei. In particular, we discuss neural-network predictions of ground-state energies from no-core shell model calculations for 6Li, 12C and 16O based on training data for 2H, 3H and 4He and compare them to classical extrapolations.

## Key claim
The authors claim that a single artificial neural network, trained exclusively on converged no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations for few-body systems (²H, ³H, ⁴He), can predict converged ground-state energies for heavier p-shell nuclei (⁶Li, ¹²C, ¹⁶O) without being retrained for each specific nucleus or interaction. The hedge is that the method requires the evaluation data to use harmonic-oscillator frequencies near the variational minimum for best accuracy, and that the network's predictions respect variational bounds only on average.

## Method
The authors trained fully connected feedforward artificial neural networks on sequences of NCSM ground-state energies for ²H, ³H, and ⁴He, using chiral NN+3N interactions at N²LO, N³LO, and N⁴LO' with three different cutoffs (450, 500, 550 MeV) and four SRG evolution parameters (bare, α=0.02, 0.04, 0.08 fm⁴). Two input modes were tested: "ABS" mode using raw energy eigenvalues for three harmonic-oscillator frequencies and four consecutive Nmax values, and "DIFF" mode using energy differences between consecutive Nmax values. Training data were augmented by random scaling and shifting to cover a broader energy range. The networks output a single converged energy prediction, and 1000 networks with comparable validation performance were used to produce a mean prediction and standard deviation.

## Key numbers
- Training data consisted of 756 converging sequences from NCSM calculations for ²H, ³H, and ⁴He up to Nmax=50, 40, and 24 respectively, for seven HO frequencies from ℏΩ=12 to 32 MeV.
- The ANNs reached an average accuracy of about 60 keV for the ABS mode and 6 keV for the DIFF mode on the validation set.
- For ⁴He at Nmax=12, the DIFF mode predicted -28.532(8) MeV compared to the converged result of -28.524 MeV, while the ABS mode predicted -28.562(81) MeV.
- For ⁶Li at Nmax=12, the DIFF mode predicted -32.126(147) MeV, compared to the classical extrapolation of -32.098(317) MeV.
- For ¹²C at Nmax=10, the DIFF mode predicted -97.64(7) MeV, compared to the classical extrapolation of -97.43(44) MeV.
- For ¹⁶O at Nmax=10, the DIFF mode predicted -147.46(12) MeV, compared to the classical extrapolation of -147.28(1.86) MeV.

## Context
This work extends prior efforts by Negoita et al. (2019) and Jiang and Hagen (2019) who used neural networks to extrapolate NCSM or coupled-cluster results for a single specific nucleus and interaction, requiring many calculations for that target system. The present approach instead trains on few-body systems and generalizes to heavier nuclei, avoiding the need for large model-space data on the target nucleus. It enables robust extrapolation of ground-state energies from limited NCSM data (Nmax ≤ 12) for light p-shell nuclei, with uncertainty estimates that are more consistent than classical exponential extrapolations, particularly for systems like the deuteron where exponential fits break down.
