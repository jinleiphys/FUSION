# Inclusion of virtual nuclear excitations in the formulation of the (e,e'N)

> A wave-function framework for the theory of the (e,e'N) reaction is presented in order to justify the use of coupled channel equations in the usual Feynman matrix element. The overall wave function containing the electron and nucleon coordinates is expanded in a basis set of eigenstates of the nuclear Hamiltonian, which contain both bound states as well as continuum states.. The latter have an ingoing nucleon with a variable momentum Q incident on the daughter nucleus as a target, with as many outgoing channels as desirable. The Dirac Eqs. for the electron part of the wave function acquire inhomogeneous terms, and require the use of distorted electron Green's functions for their solutions. The condition that the asymptotic wave function contain only the appropriate momentum Q_k for the outgoing nucleon, which corresponds to the electron momentum k through energy conservation, is achieved through the use of the steepest descent saddle point method, commonly used in three-body calculations.

## Key claim
The paper provides a wave-function-based framework for the (e,e'N) reaction that justifies the use of coupled-channel equations within the usual Feynman matrix element, but the derivation relies on neglecting the electromagnetic interaction between the electron and nucleon in the continuum and ignoring dispersion corrections, making the treatment schematic rather than fully rigorous.

## Method
The overall wave function containing electron and nucleon coordinates is expanded in a complete basis set of eigenstates of the nuclear Hamiltonian, including both bound states and continuum states with ingoing nucleon momentum Q incident on the daughter nucleus. The coefficients of this expansion, which are functions of the electron coordinates, obey inhomogeneous Dirac equations solved using distorted electron Green's functions. The asymptotic condition that only outgoing nucleon waves survive is enforced via the steepest-descent saddle-point method, commonly used in three-body calculations, yielding a T-matrix expression identical in form to the standard Feynman-diagram result.

## Key numbers
- The continuum nuclear basis states \(\phi_Q^{(-)}(r)\) are orthogonal with normalization \(<\phi_Q^{(-)}|\phi_{Q'}^{(-)}> = \frac{\pi}{2}\delta(Q-Q')\) (Eq. 2.4a).
- The saddle-point condition gives \(Q_0/k_0 = (r_n/r_e)(mc^2/\epsilon_k^{(e)})\) (Eq. 2.14a), which simplifies to \(Q_0 \simeq (r_n/r_e)mc^2/\hbar c\) and \(\hbar c k_0 \simeq E - (r_n/r_e)^2 mc^2/2\) (Eq. 2.14b), where m is the nucleon mass.
- The asymptotic integral over continuum states yields a factor \(\frac{(i)^{3/2}}{2} \frac{r_n^2}{r_e^{5/2}} \sqrt{2\pi mc^2/(\hbar c)}\) multiplying the T-matrix and Hankel function (Eq. 2.15).
- The nuclear continuum energy is \(\epsilon_{Q,i_0}^{(A+1)} = \frac{\hbar^2 Q^2}{2m} + \epsilon_{i_0}^{(A)} + \epsilon_s\) (Eq. 3.4), where \(\epsilon_s\) is the nucleon separation energy.

## Context
This work extends the Feynman-diagram-based (e,e'N) formalism of Gross and Lipperheide (1970) and the subsequent developments by Boffi and collaborators, Walecka and collaborators, and Donnelly and co-workers, by providing a wave-function foundation that clarifies the assumptions underlying coupled-channel treatments of hadron-nucleus channels. The approach enables future inclusion of dispersion corrections to electron-nucleus scattering and provides a foundation for treating (e,e'2N) processes, though the extension to two-nucleon emission is left to a future paper. The method is similar in nature to expansions used in three-body reaction theory and the continuum discretized coupled channel (CDCC) treatment of deuteron breakup.
