# Time-dependent method for many-body problems and its application to nuclear resonant systems

> The decay process of the schematic one-dimensional three-body system is considered. A time-dependent approach is used in combination with a one-dimensional three-body model, which is composed of a heavier core nucleus and two nucleons, with the aim of describing its evolution in two-nucleon emission. The process is calculated from the initial state, in which the three ingredient particles are confined. In this process, two different types of emission can be found: the earlier process includes the emission of spatially correlated two-nucleon pair, like a dinucleon, whereas, at a subsequent time, all the particles are separated from each other. The time-dependent method can be a suitable option to investigate the meta-stable and/or open-quantum systems, where the complicated many-body dynamics should necessarily be taken into account.

## Key claim

The time-dependent method applied to a schematic one-dimensional three-body system reveals that the emission process is a superposition of an earlier dinucleon emission and a later separated three-body emission, and that the overall decay does not follow a simple exponential law; only after decomposing the survival probability by energy (below a threshold of 1.8 MeV) does an exponentially decaying form emerge, with a decay width approximated as 0.41 MeV.

## Method

The authors constructed a one-dimensional three-body model with a heavy core (mass 16 x 939 MeV/c^2) and two nucleons (each 939 MeV/c^2), using a square-well attractive potential for the nucleon-nucleon interaction and a Gaussian potential with a repulsive bump for the core-nucleon channels. They solved the eigenstates of the three-body Hamiltonian by diagonalization within a harmonic oscillator basis (up to 15 quanta, hbar-omega = 0.4 MeV), then prepared an initial confined state using a confining wall potential at 7.5 fm and evolved it in time via the time-dependent Schrodinger equation. The time evolution of the density distribution and the survival probability were analyzed, and the decay probability was decomposed into low-energy (below 1.8 MeV) and high-energy components.

## Key numbers

* The nucleon masses are set to 939 MeV/c^2 and the core mass to 16 x 939 MeV/c^2.
* The nucleon-nucleon square-well potential has depth -2.84 MeV and range 1.2 fm.
* The core-nucleon Gaussian potential parameters: d_r = 5.04 fm, d_a = 3.15 fm, V_r = 24 MeV, V_a = -32 MeV.
* The harmonic oscillator basis uses hbar-omega = 0.4 MeV with truncation a,b <= 15.
* The initial state expectation value of the relative Hamiltonian (energy release) is 0.91 MeV.
* The low-energy component of the survival probability for ct >= 500 fm shows exponential decay with decay width Gamma approximately 0.41 MeV.
* The energy threshold for separating low- and high-energy components is E_T = 1.8 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the time-dependent three-body model approach previously developed by Gurvitz (1987, 2004), Maruyama (2012), and Oishi (2014) to a schematic one-dimensional system, focusing on broad-resonance states where the lifetime is short and multi-particle dynamics are important. The results contrast with Maruyama (2012), where only dinucleon emission was dominant with a pairing force, by showing a superposition of dinucleon and separated emission. The method is intended as a step toward describing realistic three-dimensional nuclear meta-stable systems such as the tetra neutron, whose measured decay width is wide and does not clearly follow exponential decay (Kisamori, 2016).
