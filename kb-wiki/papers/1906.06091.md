# Constraining the Neutron Star Compactness: Extraction of the $^{23}$Al($p,γ$) Reaction Rate for the $rp$-Process

> The $^{23}$Al($p,γ$)$^{24}$Si reaction is among the most important reactions driving the energy generation in Type-I X-ray bursts. However, the present reaction-rate uncertainty limits constraints on neutron star properties that can be achieved with burst model-observation comparisons. Here, we present a novel technique for constraining this important reaction by combining the GRETINA array with the neutron detector LENDA coupled to the S800 spectrograph at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory. The $^{23}$Al($d,n$) reaction was used to populate the astrophysically important states in $^{24}$Si. This enables a measurement in complete kinematics for extracting all relevant inputs necessary to calculate the reaction rate. For the first time, a predicted close-lying doublet of a 2$_2^+$ and (4$_1^+$,0$_2^+$) state in $^{24}$Si was disentangled, finally resolving conflicting results from two previous measurements. Moreover, it was possible to extract spectroscopic factors using GRETINA and LENDA simultaneously. This new technique may be used to constrain other important reaction rates for various astrophysical scenarios.

## Key claim

The paper presents a novel technique using the $^{23}$Al($d,n$) reaction in complete kinematics to constrain the $^{23}$Al($p,\gamma$)$^{24}$Si reaction rate, which is critical for Type-I X-ray burst modeling; however, the extracted resonance energies and spectroscopic factors still carry uncertainties that limit the precision of the final reaction rate, and the spin-parity assignment for the 3471 keV state remains ambiguous between $4_1^+$ and $0_2^+$.

## Method

The experiment used a 48 MeV/u $^{23}$Al beam impinging on a CD$_2$ target at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory, with the GRETINA gamma-ray array, the LENDA neutron detector, and the S800 magnetic spectrograph operating simultaneously. The $^{23}$Al($d,n$)$^{24}$Si transfer reaction populated states in $^{24}$Si, and the complete kinematics measurement allowed extraction of excitation energies from Doppler-corrected gamma rays, partial cross sections, and spectroscopic factors via comparison with ADWA (Adiabatic Distorted Wave Approximation) calculations using the FRESCO code. Background contributions from carbon in the target were subtracted using dedicated runs with a pure carbon target.

## Key numbers

- Excitation energy of the $2_1^+$ state in $^{24}$Si: 1874(3) keV, determined from the gamma-ray transition to ground state
- Excitation energy of the $2_2^+$ state: 3449(5) keV, from the 1575(3) keV gamma-ray transition to the $2_1^+$ state
- Excitation energy of the state at 3471(6) keV (either $4_1^+$ or $0_2^+$), from the 1597(5) keV gamma-ray transition to the $2_1^+$ state
- Experimental spectroscopic factor for the $2_1^+$ state: C$^2$S$_\text{exp}$ = 0.6(2) for $l=0$ transfer
- Experimental spectroscopic factor for the $2_2^+$ state: C$^2$S$_\text{exp}$ = 0.7(4) for $l=0$ transfer
- Resonance strength for the $2_2^+$ state: $\omega\gamma$ = 4.2 $\times$ 10$^{-5}$ eV
- Total ($d,n$) cross section: 563(67) $\mu$b
- Neutron time-of-flight resolution: 744(36) ps (FWHM)
- GRETINA detection efficiency: about 4% at 1800 keV

## Context

This work resolves conflicting results from two previous measurements of the $^{24}$Si level scheme: Schatz et al. (1997) reported a single state at 3441(10) keV, while Yoneda et al. (2006) reported 3410(16) keV; the present measurement disentangles this as a doublet of states at 3449(5) keV and 3471(6) keV. The reduced uncertainty in the $^{23}$Al($p,\gamma$) reaction rate will enable tighter constraints on neutron star compactness from X-ray burst model-observation comparisons, as demonstrated by Meisel et al. (2018) who showed that a factor-30 reduction in this rate significantly affects the inferred surface gravitational redshift. The technique combining gamma-ray, neutron, and heavy-recoil detection in a single experiment can be applied to constrain other astrophysically important reaction rates far from stability.
