# Dynamic microscopic study of pre-equilibrium giant resonance excitation and fusion in the reactions $^{132}$Sn+$^{48}$Ca and $^{124}$Sn+$^{40}$Ca

> We study pre-equilibrium giant dipole resonance excitation and fusion in the neutron-rich system $^{132}$Sn+$^{48}$Ca at energies near the Coulomb barrier, and we compare photon yields and total fusion cross sections to those of the stable system $^{124}$Sn+$^{40}$Ca. The dynamic microscopic calculations are carried out on a three-dimensional lattice using both the Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock method and the Density Constrained TDHF method. We demonstrate that the peak of the GDR excitation spectrum occurs at a substantially lower energy than expected for an equilibrated system, thus reflecting the very large prolate elongation of the dinuclear complex during the early stages of fusion. Our theoretical fusion cross-sections for both systems agree reasonably well with recent data measured at HRIBF.

## Key claim

The peak of the pre-equilibrium giant dipole resonance (GDR) excitation spectrum occurs at a substantially lower energy than expected for an equilibrated system, reflecting the very large prolate elongation of the dinuclear complex during the early stages of fusion; this effect is demonstrated for both the neutron-rich system $^{132}$Sn+$^{48}$Ca and the stable system $^{124}$Sn+$^{40}$Ca, though the paper notes that the system with a larger initial $N/Z$ differential (the stable system) shows stronger initial pre-equilibrium GDR excitation.

## Method

The authors performed fully microscopic Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations on a three-dimensional Cartesian lattice using the Skyrme SLy4 interaction, including all time-odd terms in the mean-field Hamiltonian. For fusion cross sections above the barrier, unrestricted TDHF runs were used with varying impact parameters. For sub-barrier energies, the Density Constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) method was employed to extract energy-dependent heavy-ion interaction potentials $V(R)$ and coordinate-dependent mass parameters $M(R)$, from which barrier penetrabilities were obtained by numerical integration of the Schrodinger equation with the Incoming Wave Boundary Condition (IWBC) method. The dipole radiation power spectrum was computed from the Fourier transform of the dipole acceleration $D''(t)$.

## Key numbers

- For $^{132}$Sn+$^{48}$Ca at $E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=130$ MeV, the maximum impact parameter for fusion is $b_{max}=4.45$ fm, giving a fusion cross section of $622$ mb from the sharp cut-off model.
- The isovector dipole radiation spectrum shows a peak near $10$ MeV for both isotopic combinations at $E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=130$ MeV.
- The pre-compound system ($Z/N = 70/110$) has a dipole polarizability $\alpha_D = 30$ fm$^3$, yielding $0.6-0.8$ MeV excitation energy during the initial contact phase (dipole amplitude $5-6$ fm) and $0.1-0.2$ MeV at later stages (dipole amplitude $2-3$ fm).
- The intrinsic excitation energy from DC-TDHF at this c.m. energy reaches $40$ MeV as the initial composite system is formed.
- For $^{132}$Sn+$^{48}$Ca, the heavy-ion potential barrier height at $E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=114$ MeV is $111.9$ MeV.
- The lattice spacing used is $1.0$ fm, with a time step $\Delta t = 0.4$ fm/c and initial separation of $22$ fm for central collisions.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous DC-TDHF studies of fusion for systems including $^{132}$Sn+$^{64}$Ni (Oberacker and Umar, 2007), $^{64}$Ni+$^{64}$Ni (Oberacker and Umar, 2008), $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb (Oberacker and Umar, 2009), and $^{132,124}$Sn+$^{96}$Zr (Oberacker and Umar, 2010) to the specific case of Sn+Ca isotopes with different neutron excess. The calculations enable comparison with recent experimental data from HRIBF (Li et al., 2011; Kozub and Radford, 2012) and demonstrate that the energy dependence of the heavy-ion potential is dramatically stronger in these heavy systems than in light systems such as $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O (Umar et al., 2009), which showed almost no energy-dependence even when $E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}$ was increased by a factor of four.
