# Precision big bang nucleosynthesis with improved Helium-4 predictions

> Primordial nucleosynthesis is one of the three historical evidences for the big bang model, together with the expansion of the universe and the cosmic microwave background. Now that the number of neutrino families and the baryonic densities have been fixed by laboratory measurements or CMB observations, the model has no free parameter and its predictions are rigid. Departure from its predictions could provide hints or constraints on new physics or astrophysics in the early universe. Precision on primordial abundances deduced from observations have recently been drastically improved and reach the percent level for both deuterium and helium-4. Accordingly, the BBN predictions should reach the same level of precision. For most isotopes, the dominant sources of uncertainty come from those on the laboratory thermonuclear reactions. This article focuses on helium-4 whose predicted primordial abundance depends essentially on weak interactions which control the neutron-proton ratio. The rates of the various weak interaction processes depend on the experimentally measured neutron lifetime, but also includes numerous corrections that we thoroughly investigate here. They are the radiative, zero-temperature, corrections, finite nucleon mass corrections, finite temperature radiative corrections, weak-magnetism, and QED plasma effects, which are for the first time all included and calculated in a self consistent way, allowing to take into account the correlations between them, and verifying that all satisfy detailed balance. The helium-4 predicted mass fraction is $0.24709\pm0.00017$. In addition, we provide a Mathematica code (PRIMAT) that incorporates, not only these corrections but also a full network of reactions, using the best available thermonuclear reaction rates, allowing the predictions of primordial abundances up to the CNO region.

## Key claim

The authors claim a $10^{-4}$ accuracy on the predicted helium-4 mass fraction of $0.24709\pm0.00017$, achieved by including for the first time all weak-interaction corrections (radiative, finite nucleon mass, finite temperature radiative, weak-magnetism, and QED plasma effects) in a self-consistent way that respects detailed balance, with the uncertainty dominated by the experimental neutron lifetime.

## Method

The authors implemented a new Mathematica code (PRIMAT) that solves the BBN network equations, incorporating a comprehensive set of weak-interaction rate corrections for the neutron-proton interconversion reactions. They used a Fokker-Planck expansion in energy transfer to compute finite nucleon mass corrections via one-dimensional integrals, and included finite-temperature radiative corrections alongside bremsstrahlung corrections to satisfy detailed balance, with incomplete neutrino decoupling treated via a fit from the Parthenope collaboration.

## Key numbers

- Helium-4 mass fraction: $0.24709 \pm 0.00017$ (including neutron lifetime uncertainty)
- Sensitivity relation: $\delta \YP / \YP \simeq -0.73 \, \delta \Gamma / \Gamma$ (weak rate variation to helium-4 variation)
- Observed helium-4 mass fraction from Aver et al. (2015): $0.2449 \pm 0.0040$
- Observed deuterium abundance from Cooke et al. (2018): D/H = $(2.527 \pm 0.030) \times 10^{-5}$
- Observed lithium-7 abundance from Sbordone et al. (2010): $^7$Li/H = $(1.58 \pm 0.3) \times 10^{-10}$
- Neutron freeze-out temperature: $T_F \simeq 0.8$ MeV
- Temperature when nucleosynthesis starts: $T_{\rm Nuc} = 0.078$ MeV
- Neutron abundance at nucleosynthesis start: $X^{\rm Nuc}_n \simeq 0.125$

## Context

This work extends the precision BBN calculations of Coc et al. (2015) and earlier works by Dicus (1982) and Lopez and Turner (1998), resolving long-standing controversies about finite-temperature radiative corrections by demonstrating that they nearly cancel with bremsstrahlung corrections. The improved helium-4 prediction, combined with percent-level deuterium observations from Cooke et al. (2018), enables tighter constraints on standard model physics and potential new physics in the early universe, while the lithium problem (factor of approximately 3 discrepancy with observations from Sbordone et al., 2010) remains an open question.
