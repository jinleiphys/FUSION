# Necessary conditions for accurate computations of three-body partial decay widths

> The partial width for decay of a resonance into three fragments is largely determined at distances where the energy is smaller than the effective potential producing the corresponding wave function. At short distances the many-body properties are accounted for by preformation or spectroscopic factors. We use the adiabatic expansion method combined with the WKB approximation to obtain the indispensable cluster model wave functions at intermediate and larger distances. We test the concept by deriving conditions for the minimal basis expressed in terms of partial waves and radial nodes. We compare results for different effective interactions and methods. Agreement is found with experimental values for a sufficiently large basis. We illustrate the ideas with realistic examples from $α$-emission of $^{12}$C and two-proton emission of $^{17}$Ne. Basis requirements for accurate momentum distributions are briefly discussed.

# Necessary conditions for accurate computations of three-body partial decay widths

## Key claim

The partial width for three-body decay is exponentially sensitive to the potential barrier between classical turning points, and accurate computation requires a sufficiently large basis (hypermomentum quantum number $K_{max}$ and partial waves $\ell_x$, $\ell_y$) to describe the barrier up to the outer turning point; an insufficient basis can produce widths overestimated or underestimated by 3–5 orders of magnitude, but a too-small $K_{max}$ and too-few partial waves can partially compensate each other, potentially giving a fortuitously correct result.

## Method

The authors use the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method, solving the Schrödinger or Faddeev equations for each value of the hyperradius $\rho$ to obtain effective adiabatic potentials $V_{\text{eff}}(\rho) = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\lambda_n(\rho)+15/4}{\rho^2} + V_{3b}(\rho)$. They then apply the WKB approximation to compute tunneling probabilities through the one-dimensional potential barriers, fixing the real part of the resonance energy to experimental values by adjusting a short-range three-body square-well potential of radius 4 fm. The method is tested on the Hoyle state in $^{12}$C (three $\alpha$-particles) and two-proton emission of $^{17}$Ne.

## Key numbers

- For the Hoyle state (first $0^+$ resonance in $^{12}$C): experimental width is $8.5 \cdot 10^{-6}$ MeV (8.5 eV) at an energy of 0.38 MeV above the three-body threshold.
- Estimated outer turning point for direct decay of the Hoyle state: $\rho_t \approx 90$ fm; for sequential decay via $^{8}$Be ($E_{12} = 0.1$ MeV): $\rho_t \approx 67$ fm.
- Estimated maximum partial waves for the Hoyle state: $\ell_x \approx \ell_y \approx 7$ (direct) or $\ell_y \approx 8$ (sequential).
- Required $K_{max}$ for converged potential up to the outer turning point: at least 80 (from Eq. (e130) estimate), with the converged outer turning point found at 60 fm.
- WKB width for $K_{max}=20$: $3.6 \cdot 10^{-11}$ MeV; for $K_{max}=80$: $2.7 \cdot 10^{-8}$ MeV; for the full basis ($K_{max}=150$, $\ell_x=\ell_y$ up to 12, $K_{max}$ up to 500 for some components): $5.5 \cdot 10^{-6}$ MeV.
- WKB width with $K_{max}=150$ and $\ell_x=\ell_y=0$: $3.1 \cdot 10^{-3}$ MeV; with $\ell_x=\ell_y \leq 8$: $6.3 \cdot 10^{-6}$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the Gamow theory of two-body $\alpha$-emission (Gamow 1928) to three-body decays, building on the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method developed by Nielsen et al. (2001) and earlier work on three-body decay widths by Fedorov et al. (2003) and Garrido et al. (2004). The paper provides necessary conditions for basis convergence that were previously lacking, distinguishing effects of degrees of freedom, interactions, theoretical method, and numerical convergence. The results enable reliable computation of partial three-body decay widths for systems such as $\alpha$-emission from $^{12}$C and two-proton emission from $^{17}$Ne, and establish benchmark criteria for testing other methods including the Gamow shell-model approach (Michel et al. 2002, Betan et al. 2002) and the semiclassical perturbative method (Grigorenko et al. 2000, 2003, 2007).
