# Different Hagedorn temperatures for mesons and baryons from experimental mass spectra, compound hadrons, and combinatorial saturation

> We analyze the light-flavor particle mass spectra and show that in the region up to ~1.8GeV the Hagedorn temperature for baryons is about 30% smaller than for mesons, reflecting the fact that the number of baryon states grows more rapidly with the mass. We also show that the spectra are well reproduced in a model where hadrons are compound objects of quanta, whose available number increases with mass. The rapid growth of number of hadronic states is a combinatorial effect. We also point out that an upper limit on the excitation energy of these quanta results in a maximum number of hadron states that can be formed. According to this combinatorial saturation, no more light-flavor hadron resonances exist above a certain mass.

# Different Hagedorn Temperatures for Mesons and Baryons from Experimental Mass Spectra, Compound Hadrons, and Combinatorial Saturation

## Key claim

The Hagedorn temperature for baryons is about 30% smaller than for mesons when fitting experimental mass spectra up to approximately 1.8 GeV, but this difference is an effective description in that mass range rather than a universal asymptotic parameter; the authors propose that the rapid growth of baryon states reflects a combinatorial effect from compound hadron structure, and that an upper limit on excitation energy leads to a maximum number of light-flavor hadron resonances beyond which no more states exist.

## Method

The authors analyzed light-flavor hadron mass spectra from the 1998 Particle Data Group tables, comparing cumulative number of states N(m) (summed over spin and isospin degeneracies) for mesons and baryons separately, rather than differential spectra. They performed least-square fits of the Hagedorn-Ranft function ρ(m) = c/(m² + m₀²)^(5/4) exp(m/T_H) with m₀ = 500 MeV to the cumulants up to 1.8 GeV, and also fitted a Compound Hadron Model based on partition combinatorics with a square-root exponent, using the formula ρ(m) = c Θ(m - m₀) exp(2π √(D/12 · (m - m₀)/ΔE)) / ((m - m₀)² + (0.5 GeV)²)^(5/8).

## Key numbers

- Hagedorn temperature for mesons: T_mes = 197 MeV (fit with m₀ = 500 MeV, range up to 1.8 GeV)
- Hagedorn temperature for baryons: T_bar = 141 MeV (fit with m₀ = 500 MeV, range up to 1.8 GeV)
- With m₀ = 1 GeV, fitted temperatures become 228 MeV for mesons and 152 MeV for baryons
- Compound Hadron Model fit gives ΔE_mes/D = 50 MeV for mesons and ΔE_bar/D = 53 MeV for baryons (with D = 1)
- At m = 1.8 GeV, the partition number n is approximately 35 for mesons and 17 for baryons
- To make meson and baryon cumulant slopes equal would require approximately 500 additional meson states up to 1.8 GeV, compared to the present 407 states at that mass
- Toy model with k_max = 12 predicts combinatorial saturation at n = 78, corresponding to maximum masses of approximately 4 GeV for light-quark mesons and approximately 5 GeV for light-quark baryons

## Context

This work extends the Hagedorn hypothesis (Hagedorn, 1965) and the Statistical Bootstrap Model (Frautschi, 1971) by showing that the universal Hagedorn temperature of approximately 160 MeV does not hold separately for mesons and baryons in the experimentally accessible mass range up to 1.8 GeV. The authors contrast their results with the Dual String Model and Veneziano model (Huan and Weinberg, 1972), which they find works for mesons but fails for baryons in its present form. The Compound Hadron Model is introduced in analogy to compound nuclei in nuclear physics, and the predicted maximum masses for light hadrons are similar to limits obtained by Bisudova, Burakovsky and Goldman from non-linear Regge trajectories. The work enables further investigation of whether the departure of data from fits around 2 GeV signals combinatorial saturation, and raises the question of whether the effective two-temperature description persists or merges at higher masses.
