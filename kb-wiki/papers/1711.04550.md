# A new treatment of nonlocality in scattering process

> Nonlocality in the scattering potential leads to an integro-differential equation.In this equation nonlocality enters through an integral over the nonlocal potential kernel. The resulting Schroedinger equation is usually handled by approximating r,r'-dependence of the nonlocal kernel. The present work proposes a novel method to solve the integro-differential equation. The method, using the mean value theorem of integral calculus, converts the nonhomogeneous term to a homogeneous term. The effective local potential in this equation turns out to be energy independent, but has relative angular momentum dependence. This method has high accuracy and is valid for any form of nonlocality. As illustrative examples, the total and differential cross sections for neutron scattering off 12C, 56Fe and 100Mo nuclei are calculated with this method in the low energy region (up to 10 MeV) and are found to be in good accord with the experiments.

# A new treatment of nonlocality in scattering process

## Key claim

The authors propose a method using the mean value theorem of integral calculus to convert the integro-differential Schroedinger equation with a nonlocal potential into a homogeneous differential equation, producing an effective local potential that is energy independent but depends on relative angular momentum. The method is claimed to have high accuracy and to be valid for any form of nonlocality, though the approximation relies on the kernel being strongly peaked and symmetric about r = r-prime, and the homogeneous solution alone shows significant variance from the full iterative solution for heavier nuclei.

## Method

The authors apply the mean value theorem of integral calculus to the nonlocal kernel in the partial-wave expanded integro-differential Schroedinger equation, approximating the integral of the product of the kernel and wave function as the wave function at r times the integral of the kernel over r-prime. This yields a homogeneous equation with an effective local potential U_eff(l;r) defined as the integral of the nonlocal kernel over r-prime. The accuracy is tested by comparing the homogeneous solution (HOMO) with an iterative scheme (FULL) that solves the original integro-differential equation, using the homogeneous solution as the starting point. Calculations use the TPM15 parameterization of the Wood-Saxon potential and a separate FoldTPM15 prescription combining a folding-model real potential with TPM15 imaginary terms.

## Key numbers

- Total and differential cross sections for neutron scattering off 12C, 56Fe, and 100Mo are calculated in the low energy region up to 10 MeV.
- The nonlocal range parameter beta = 0.90 fm is used for the Gaussian form factor in the main calculations.
- The iterative convergence criterion requires the absolute difference between logarithmic derivatives at the matching radius to be less than or equal to 10^-6.
- The approximation of |r + r-prime| by (r + r-prime) in the nonlocal kernel is found to be accurate within 1 percent in the considered energy range for n-12C and n-56Fe scattering.
- Wave functions for n-56Fe scattering at 10 MeV are compared for beta = 0.9 fm and beta = 0.5 fm, showing significant changes in the nuclear interior and beyond.

## Context

This work extends the treatment of nonlocality in nucleon-nucleus scattering beyond the gradient approximation of Perey and Buck (1962), which produces an energy-dependent local equivalent potential but may be unreliable for nuclei away from stability. It also contrasts with the Lanczos method approach of Tian, Pang, and Ma (2015) and the separable kernel methods of Ali and Ahmad, as well as the Chebyshev polynomial expansion of Rawitscher. The method enables routine analysis of scattering data with nonlocal potentials, and the authors note that different form factors with the same normalization and rms radius should give similar results for scattering observables, while varying the range parameter beta significantly affects wave functions in the nuclear interior.
