# Glitching pulsars: unraveling the interactions of general relativity with quantum fields in the strong field regimes

> We present a modification of our previous model for the mechanisms underlying the glitch phenomena in pulsars and young neutron stars. Accordingly, pulsars are born with embryonic cores comprising of purely incompressible superconducting gluon-quark superfluid (henceforth SuSu-cores). As the ambient medium cools and spins down due to emission of magnetic dipole radiation, the mass and size of SuSu-cores are set to grow discretely with time, in accordance with the Onsager-Feynmann analysis of superfluidity. Presently, we propose that the spacetime embedding glitching pulsars is dynamical and of bimetric nature: inside SuSu-cores the spacetime must be flat, whereas the surrounding region, where the matter is compressible and dissipative, the spacetime is Schwarzschild. It is further proposed that the topological change of spacetime is derived by the strong nuclear force, whose operating length scales is found to increase with time to reach O(1) cm at the end of the luminous lifetimes of pulsars. The model presented here model is in line with the recent radio and GW observations of pulsars and NSs.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that glitching pulsars contain a bimetric spacetime structure: a flat Minkowski spacetime inside a superconducting gluon-quark superfluid (SuSu) core, surrounded by a Schwarzschild spacetime in the outer compressible region, with the topological change driven by the strong nuclear force whose operating length scale grows to order 1 cm at the end of a pulsar's luminous lifetime.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation modified to include a scalar field energy contribution representing the bag energy required for quark deconfinement, combined with the Onsager-Feynmann equation for quantized vortex circulation in the superfluid core. They employ an explicit adaptive mesh refinement (EAMR) technique with aspect ratios up to 100 million to resolve the boundary layer between the SuSu-core and the ambient dissipative medium, computing pressure and energy density profiles for five selected evolutionary epochs corresponding to different core radii.

## Key numbers

- The critical supranuclear density for quark deconfinement is set at \(\rho_c = 3 \rho_0\), where \(\rho_0\) is nuclear density
- The total density inside SuSu-cores amounts to \(\rho_{tot} = \rho_b + \rho_\phi = 2 \rho_c \approx 6 \rho_0\)
- The initial pulsar mass is \(1.33 \mathcal{M}_\odot\) with compactness parameter \(\alpha_s = 1/2\)
- The boundary layer width relative to core radius at birth is \(\delta R^{BL}_n / R^n_{SB} \approx 1.4 \times 10^{-10}\), at 1000 years (Crab age) is \(2.2 \times 10^{-8}\), at 10000 years (Vela age) is \(1.6 \times 10^{-6}\), and at 10 Myr is \(3.8 \times 10^{-6}\)
- The initial glitch sequence parameter is \(\alpha_c^{(n=0)} = 3.5 \times 10^{-10}\) with initial angular frequency \(\Omega^{(n=0)} = 1400\)/s and magnetic field \(B^{(n=0)} = 10^{13}\) Gauss
- The moment of inertia of the ambient medium for Vela is approximately \(10^{-2}\) times that of Crab
- The SuSu-core radius at the Vela pulsar's current epoch satisfies \(R^{now, Vela}_{SB} \geq 10^{-2} R^{Vela}_\star\)
- The SuSu-core radii modeled are \(R_{SuSu} = 0.333, 0.525, 0.78525, 0.8575\) in units of \(\tilde{R} = R_S / \alpha_s\)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous glitch model (Hujeirat 2018) by introducing a dynamical bimetric spacetime framework, building on the observation that no neutron stars or black holes have been observed in the mass range \(2 \mathcal{M}_\odot < \mathcal{M} < 5 \mathcal{M}_\odot\). The model connects to studies of quark-gluon plasma properties from RHIC and LHC experiments (LHCb 2015, QGP 2019), to the GW170817 neutron star merger event, and to theoretical work on the convergence of equations of state to the incompressible limit \(P = \varepsilon\) at supranuclear densities (Camenzind 2007, Hujeirat 2018). The scenario enables predictions for the evolution of pulsars into invisible dark energy objects over cosmological timescales and suggests that gravitational wave emission from individual glitch events would be below current detector sensitivity.
