# Hamiltonian lattice QCD at finite density: equation of state in the strong coupling limit

> The equation of state of Hamiltonian lattice QCD at finite density is examined in the strong coupling limit by constructing a solution to the equation of motion corresponding to an effective Hamiltonian describing the ground state of the many body system. This solution exactly diagonalizes the Hamiltonian to second order in field operators for all densities and is used to evaluate the vacuum energy density from which we obtain the equation of state. We find that up to and beyond the chiral symmetry restoration density the pressure of the quark Fermi sea can be negative indicating its mechanical instability. Our result is in qualitative agreement with continuum models and should be verifiable by future lattice simulations of strongly coupled QCD at finite density.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in the strong coupling limit of Hamiltonian lattice QCD at finite density, the pressure of the quark Fermi sea can become negative up to and beyond the chiral symmetry restoration density, indicating mechanical instability; this result is obtained by constructing a solution that exactly diagonalizes the effective Hamiltonian to second order in field operators for all densities, but the authors note the result is only in qualitative agreement with continuum models and should be verifiable by future lattice simulations.

## Method

The authors begin with Smit's effective Hamiltonian describing the ground state of strongly coupled QCD, valid to order O(1/g²) in the strong coupling expansion, and extend it to finite chemical potential by replacing the current quark mass term with a term including a bare quark chemical potential μ₀. They then construct an ansatz for the quark field at finite temperature and chemical potential using a generalized thermal Bogoliubov transformation, take the T→0 limit, and derive coupled equations for the dynamical quark mass M(p) and the total chemical potential μ_tot by equating the equation of motion from the effective Hamiltonian with that of a free lattice Dirac Hamiltonian at finite chemical potential. These equations are solved to lowest order in the 1/N_c expansion, and the vacuum energy density is evaluated from the diagonalized Hamiltonian to obtain the equation of state.

## Key numbers

- The energy required to excite one color electric flux link in the strong coupling limit is E = 1/K, where K = 2N_c/(N_c²-1)·1/g², and the extension of the effective Hamiltonian to finite T and/or μ is valid as long as T, μ < 1/K.
- The quark number density n = ∑_p θ(μ_tot - ω(p)) saturates to unity above a sufficiently large value of μ_tot, which is a lattice artifact from the sin²(p·n̂_l) term in ω(p).
- The vacuum energy density difference between the Wigner-Weyl (M(q)=0) and Nambu-Goldstone (M(q)≠0) phases is positive, ΔE > 0, confirming the true ground state is in the phase with broken chiral symmetry.
- The gap equation for the dynamical quark mass includes a term (3/2)K(1 - r²)∑_p (1 - β_p²) M(p)/ω(p) to lowest order in N_c, where r is the Wilson parameter (0 ≤ r ≤ 1) and β_p² = θ(μ_tot - ω(p)) at T=0.
- The total chemical potential is μ_tot = μ₀ + (1/4)(K/N_c)∑_l∑_p β_p²[2N_c(1+r²) - 2(1-r²)cos(p+q)·n̂_l], where μ₀ is the bare quark chemical potential.

## Context

This work extends earlier analytical studies of strong coupling QCD at finite temperature and density in both Euclidean formulations (Damgaard et al., 1985; Ilgenfritz et al., 1985; Bilic et al., 1992) and Hamiltonian formulations (Patel, 1984; Vandepeutte et al., 1984; Le Yaouanc et al., 1988; Green et al., 2000), which investigated the chiral phase transition using effective actions or Hamiltonians with composite meson and baryon fields in the mean field approximation. Unlike those prior approaches, the authors do not introduce composite fields but explicitly construct a solution to the field equations of motion that diagonalizes the Hamiltonian to second order, and their result of negative pressure is in qualitative agreement with continuum effective QCD models (Buballa et al., 1996; Alford et al., 1998). The work enables future lattice simulations of strongly coupled QCD at finite density to verify the predicted mechanical instability, and the formalism admits systematic extension to finite temperature and description of bound states via the N-quantum approach.
