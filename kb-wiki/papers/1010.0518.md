# Influence of nuclear de-excitation on observables relevant for space exploration

> The composition of the space radiation environment inside spacecrafts is modified by the interaction with shielding material, with equipment and even with the astronauts' bodies. Accurate quantitative estimates of the effects of nuclear reactions are necessary, for example, for dose estimation and prediction of single-event-upset rates. To this end, it is necessary to construct predictive models for nuclear reactions, which usually consist of an intranuclear-cascade or quantum-molecular-dynamics stage, followed by a nuclear-de-excitation stage. While it is generally acknowledged that it is necessary to accurately simulate the first reaction stage, transport-code users often neglect or underestimate the importance of the choice of the de-excitation code. The purpose of this work is to prove that the de-excitation model is in fact a non-negligible source of uncertainty for the prediction of several observables of crucial importance for space applications. For some particular observables, the systematic uncertainty due to the de-excitation model actually dominates the total uncertainty. Our point will be illustrated by making use of nucleon-nucleus calculations performed with several intranuclear-cascade/de-excitation models, such as the Liège Intranuclear Cascade model (INCL) and Isabel (for the cascade part) and ABLA07, Dresner, GEM, GEMINI++ and SMM (on the de-excitation side).

## Key claim

The choice of nuclear de-excitation model introduces a non-negligible systematic uncertainty for several observables relevant to space radioprotection, and for some observables this uncertainty can dominate over the uncertainty from the intranuclear-cascade stage; the authors demonstrate this specifically for nucleon-nucleus reactions in the 0.1–10 GeV incident-energy range, using two cascade models and five de-excitation codes.

## Method

The authors performed thin-target nucleon-nucleus calculations using two intranuclear-cascade models (INCL4 and Isabel) for the dynamical stage, coupled to five different de-excitation codes (ABLA07, Dresner, GEM, GEMINI++, and SMM). They compared the resulting charge-changing cross sections, secondary neutron and proton production cross sections, and recoil-energy distributions for selected target-projectile combinations (p+⁵⁶Fe at 1 GeV, p+²⁷Al at 1 GeV, n+²⁸Si at 200 MeV) to isolate the sensitivity to the de-excitation model.

## Key numbers

- For 1-GeV p+⁵⁶Fe charge-changing cross sections, the spread among de-excitation models is largest for light fragments in the charge range 3 ≤ Z ≤ 15, where uncertainties from cascade and de-excitation are comparable.
- For secondary neutron effective dose from 1-GeV p+²⁷Al, the uncertainty due to de-excitation is suppressed by fluence-to-dose coefficients but remains of the same order as the cascade uncertainty; neither exceeds 10%.
- For secondary proton dose to a thin biological target from 1-GeV p+²⁷Al, different de-excitation models yield doses that differ by as much as 75%, and the result is almost independent of the cascade model.
- For 200-MeV n+²⁸Si recoil-energy distributions of fragments with Z=11,12 and Z≥13, the main source of uncertainty is the cascade model, with de-excitation models responsible only for minor modulations.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of uncertainty sources in nuclear reaction modeling for space radiation transport, where prior work by Durante (2005) established the composition of Galactic Cosmic Rays and Sato et al. (2006) provided fluence-to-dose conversion coefficients. The study directly compares five de-excitation models (ABLA07, Dresner, GEM, GEMINI++, SMM) that are default options in major transport codes such as MCNPX, PHITS, and Geant4, and demonstrates that transport-code users often neglect the importance of the de-excitation choice. The results enable future thick-target calculations with realistic geometries and radiation sources, provided that multiple de-excitation models become available within the same transport code.
