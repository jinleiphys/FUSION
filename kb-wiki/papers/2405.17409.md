# Ultraheavy Ultrahigh-Energy Cosmic Rays

> We investigate the propagation of ultraheavy (UH) nuclei as ultrahigh-energy cosmic rays (UHECRs). We show that their energy loss lengths at $\lesssim300$ EeV are significantly longer than those of protons and intermediate-mass nuclei, and that the highest-energy cosmic rays with energies beyond $\sim100$ EeV, including the Amaterasu particle, may be UH-UHECRs. For the first time, we derive constraints on the contribution of UH-UHECR sources, and find that the current data are consistent with energy generation rate densities of UHECRs from collapsars and neutron star mergers. Our model predicts that the mean value of the depth of shower maximum is lower than that for iron nuclei beyond 100 EeV, which can be tested with future composition measurements, e.g., AugerPrime and the Global Cosmic Ray Observatory. In addition, the spectral tension between the Telescope Array (TA) and the Pierre Auger Observatory can be alleviated by considering the enhanced contribution of UHECRs -- including UH nuclei -- from a nearby transient.

## Key claim
Ultraheavy (UH) nuclei with mass numbers A > 56 can have energy loss lengths at energies below about 300 EeV that are significantly longer than those of protons and intermediate-mass nuclei, making them plausible candidates for the highest-energy cosmic rays beyond about 100 EeV including the Amaterasu particle; however, the authors qualify that including UH-UHECRs improves fits to Telescope Array data but does not improve fits to Pierre Auger Observatory data, and the derived constraints are therefore more conservatively interpreted as upper limits on the UH-UHECR contribution.

## Method
The authors used CRPropa 3.2 extended with a newly generated photodisintegration cross-section table for nuclei with atomic numbers up to Z=92 and mass numbers up to A=238, computed using the nuclear reaction network Talys 1.96, and implemented unstable UH-nuclei decay using NuDat 3. They performed one-dimensional propagation of UHECR nuclei through intergalactic space including interactions with the cosmic microwave background and extragalactic background light, then fitted the resulting spectra and composition moments (mean and standard deviation of Xmax) to data from the Pierre Auger Observatory and Telescope Array using two composition models with and without iron-group nuclei.

## Key numbers
- Energy loss length of UH nuclei at energies below about 300 EeV is significantly longer than that of protons and intermediate-mass nuclei; the photodisintegration energy loss length is approximately 1.3 (A/195)^(-0.21) Mpc
- The Bethe-Heitler pair production energy loss length is approximately 32 (Z/78)^(-2) (A/195) Mpc
- Energy generation rate density of UH-UHECRs constrained from Auger data: Q_UH-UHECR^Auger less than about (0.1 - 15) x 10^42 erg Mpc^(-3) yr^(-1)
- Energy generation rate density of UH-UHECRs constrained from TA data: Q_UH-UHECR^TA less than about (1.4 - 5.6) x 10^43 erg Mpc^(-3) yr^(-1)
- Best-fit values for Model A with Pt from TA data: Q_UH-UHECR^TA = 2.0 x 10^43 erg Mpc^(-3) yr^(-1)
- The Amaterasu particle energy is 244 +/- 29 (stat.) +51/-76 (syst.) EeV
- The cosmic-ray luminosity density of binary neutron star mergers is about 10^43.5 erg Mpc^(-3) yr^(-1)

## Context
This work extends previous studies of UHECR composition and propagation (Hillas 1985, Nagano 2000, Kotera 2011, Alves Batista 2019) and specifically builds on earlier work considering heavy nuclei from collapsars (Murase 2008, Zhang 2017, Zhang 2018, Bhattacharya 2021) and compact binary mergers (Takami 2013, Kyutoku 2016, Kimura 2018, Rodrigues 2018, Murase 2018). The authors provide the first detailed constraints on UH-UHECR contributions to the observed flux, finding consistency with both collapsar and binary neutron star merger source models. The results enable future tests with AugerPrime and the Global Cosmic Ray Observatory, and suggest that the spectral tension between Telescope Array and Pierre Auger Observatory can be alleviated by considering an enhanced contribution from a nearby transient such as a low-luminosity gamma-ray burst at about 5 Mpc distance.
