# From Entropy and Jet Quenching to Deconfinement?

> The challenge of demonstrating that the matter produced in heavy ion collisions is a deconfined quark-gluon plasma, as predicted by lattice QCD calculations, is the challenge of measuring the number of thermodynamic degrees of freedom ν~ ε/T^4 at the time t0 at which the matter comes into approximate local thermal equilibrium and begins to behave like a hydrodynamic fluid. Data from experiments done at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider have been used to estimate t0 and to put a lower bound on the energy density ε(t0). However, measuring νhas seemed out of reach, because no current data serve even as qualitative proxies for the temperature T(t0). We point out that νmay equally appropriately be defined via ν~s^4/epsilon^3, where s is the entropy density, which can be estimated from the measured final state entropy. This estimate is based on the testable assumption of an isentropic expansion. The observation of jet quenching has the potential to provide an upper bound on the energy density at early times. Our goal is to motivate such an analysis by pointing out that it would set a lower bound on ν.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the effective number of thermodynamic degrees of freedom \(\nu\) in the matter produced in heavy ion collisions can be measured via \(\nu \sim s^4/\varepsilon^3\) (where \(s\) is entropy density and \(\varepsilon\) is energy density) without needing to measure temperature directly, provided that an upper bound on \(\varepsilon(t_0)\) can be obtained from jet quenching data and a lower bound on \(s(t_0)\) from final state entropy under the testable assumption of isentropic expansion.

## Method
The authors propose using the measured charged particle multiplicity at chemical freezeout in central Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_\text{NN}}=200\) GeV to estimate the entropy per unit rapidity \(dS/dy\), then applying a Bjorken argument to obtain a lower bound on the entropy density \(s(t_0)\) at the thermalization time \(t_0 \sim 1\) fm/c. They further propose that jet quenching data, analyzed via the transport coefficient \(\hat q\), can provide an upper bound on the energy density \(\varepsilon(t_0)\) at the same early time, using the relation between \(\hat q\) and \(\varepsilon\) derived by Baier (2002) for weakly interacting media. Combining these bounds via \(\nu = 0.96\, s^4/\varepsilon^3\) would yield a lower bound on \(\nu\), which can be compared to lattice QCD predictions to test for deconfinement.

## Key numbers
- The entropy per hadron at chemical freezeout (\(T_{\text{ch}}=170\pm10\) MeV) is about 7.25, with a theoretical uncertainty of \(\pm 6\%\).
- For the 6% most central Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=200\,A\)GeV, \(dN_{\text{ch}}/d\eta = 665\pm 26\) at mid-rapidity, yielding \(dS/dy \approx 5100\pm 400\) at chemical freezeout.
- The Bjorken argument gives a lower bound on the entropy density at \(t_0=1\) fm/c of \(s(t_0) \ge 33\pm 3\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The lower bound on energy density from Bjorken arguments is \(\varepsilon(1\,\text{fm}/c) > 5\) GeV/fm\(^{-3}\).
- Lattice QCD calculations yield \(\nu \approx 27\) at \(T=1.5\,T_c\) using the definition \(\nu \sim s^4/\varepsilon^3\), compared to \(\nu \approx 37\) using \(\nu \sim \varepsilon/T^4\).
- For an ideal gas of ultrarelativistic pions, \(\nu=3\); for an ideal gas of noninteracting gluons and three flavors of massless quarks, \(\nu=47.5\).

## Context
This work extends the Bjorken (1982) argument for estimating initial energy density and entropy density in heavy ion collisions, and builds on the analysis of Pal and Pratt (2003) who extracted entropy from HBT and multiplicity data at \(\sqrt{s}=130\,A\)GeV. The paper directly addresses the challenge of demonstrating deconfinement, which lattice QCD calculations predict as a crossover rather than a sharp phase transition. The proposed method would enable a direct comparison between experimental data and lattice QCD thermodynamics, and motivates further theoretical work on the relation between jet quenching observables and energy density, as well as experimental searches for event-by-event fluctuations that could indicate a first-order phase transition.
