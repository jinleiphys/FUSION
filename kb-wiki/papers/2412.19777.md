# Application of normalizing flows to nuclear many-body perturbation theory

> Many-body perturbation theory provides a powerful framework to study the ground state and thermodynamic properties of nuclear matter as well as associated single-particle potentials and response functions within a systematic order-by-order expansion. However, computational challenges can emerge beyond the lowest orders of perturbation theory, especially when computing both single-particle potentials and response functions, which in general are complex-valued and require Cauchy principal value calculations of high-dimensional integrals. We demonstrate that normalizing flows are suitable for Monte Carlo importance sampling of both regular and irregular functions appearing in nuclear many-body calculations. Normalizing flows are a class of machine learning models that can be used to build and sample from complicated distributions through a bijective mapping from a simple base distribution. Furthermore, a well-trained model for a certain target integrand can be efficiently transferred to calculate related integrals with varying physical conditions. These features can enable more efficient tabulations of nuclear physics inputs to numerical simulations of supernovae and neutron star mergers across varying physical conditions and nuclear force models.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that normalizing flows can serve as a comprehensive Monte Carlo importance sampling tool for nuclear many-body perturbation theory calculations, specifically for complex-valued integrands with poles (requiring Cauchy principal value integration) that appear in nucleon self-energies and response functions, and that a well-trained model can be transferred not only across thermodynamic phase space points but also across different kinematic regimes in momentum-energy space.

## Method

The authors construct normalizing flows using rational quadratic spline coupling transforms, where neural networks generate the spline parameters (bin widths, bin heights, and knot derivatives) to map a uniform base distribution to a target distribution approximating the normalized absolute value of the integrand. They train the flow by minimizing the Pearson chi-squared divergence between the transformed probability density and the target distribution using the Adam optimizer in PyTorch. The method is applied to two test cases: the second-order perturbation theory contribution to the nuclear matter grand canonical potential (a regular integrand) and the zeroth-order density-density response function (an integrand with poles requiring Cauchy principal value treatment).

## Key numbers

- In previous work (Brady et al., 2021), 5000 samples drawn from a uniform distribution over a 7-dimensional domain and transformed via a normalizing flow achieved a relative precision of sigma/I approximately 2 x 10^{-3} for a second-order perturbation theory calculation of the finite-temperature equation of state.
- Compared to state-of-the-art importance sampling algorithms (Hahn, 2005; Lepage, 2020) previously used in many-body perturbation theory calculations of the equation of state (Drischler et al., 2019), normalizing flows exhibited an improvement in sampling efficiency by at least a factor of 100.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous application of normalizing flows to the finite-temperature nuclear equation of state (Brady et al., 2021) by addressing the more challenging cases of complex-valued nucleon self-energies and response functions, which require Cauchy principal value integration of integrands with poles. The approach provides a computationally efficient alternative to nonperturbative methods such as quantum Monte Carlo (Akmal et al., 1998; Gezerlis et al., 2013), which are too expensive for systematic exploration of uncertainties from different nuclear potentials, and enables more efficient tabulations of nuclear physics inputs for supernova and neutron star merger simulations across varying physical conditions and nuclear force models.
