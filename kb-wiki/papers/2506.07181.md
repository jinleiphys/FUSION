# What is the Quark-Gluon Plasma made of?

> This article surveys our present understanding of the internal structure of the fully developed quark-gluon plasma at temperatures outside the crossover region. The theoretical part of the review covers perturbative and nonperturbative approaches to quark-gluon plasma structure, in particular, hard-thermal loop effective theory, lattice QCD and the functional renormalization group. The phenomenological part of the review scrutinizes the information that has been derived from bulk observables and hard probes in relativistic heavy ion collisions in terms of how it informs our knowledge about the structure of the quark-gluon plasma. The final section lists possible avenues for future progress.

# What is the Quark-Gluon Plasma made of?

## Key claim

The quark-gluon plasma (QGP) at temperatures above approximately 200 MeV is not a weakly coupled gas of nearly free quarks and gluons, but rather a strongly coupled medium in which quark- and gluon-like quasiparticles coexist with a well-defined phonon (sound) mode; the relative importance of these degrees of freedom depends on momentum, with phonons dominating below about 2T (≈ 0.5 GeV/c) and gluon-like quasiparticles dominating at higher momenta, at the realistic coupling g(T) ≈ 2 found in heavy ion collisions.

## Method

The review surveys theoretical approaches including hard-thermal loop (HTL) perturbation theory at next-to-leading and next-to-next-to-leading order, lattice gauge theory (LGT) in Euclidean space, and functional renormalization group (fRG) techniques. Phenomenological information is drawn from bulk observables (soft probes) and hard probes in relativistic heavy ion collisions, interpreted through transport theory and linear response theory.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range for "fully developed" QGP: T > T_c + 3ΔT ≈ 200 MeV, where ΔT ≈ 15 MeV is the width of the chiral susceptibility peak (Borsanyi 2020).
- Realistic gauge coupling at thermal scale: g(T) ≈ 2 (α_s ≈ 0.3), derived from α_s(1.5 GeV) = 0.326 ± 0.019 from lattice QCD (Bazavov 2012) with thermal scale 2πT = 1.5 GeV (T ≈ 250 MeV).
- Phonon mode is well-defined for momenta |k| < 2T ≈ 0.5 GeV/c; gluon mode is better defined for momenta above this range at g=2.
- Gluon quasiparticle lifetime at thermal momenta: γ⁻¹ ≈ T⁻¹ ≈ 0.5 fm/c.
- Kinematic shear viscosity: η/s ≈ 0.1–0.2 from HTL perturbation theory.
- Effective infrared gluon mass from fRG: approximately 750 MeV at T = 300 MeV (Cyrol 2017).
- Constituent light quark mass from fRG: m_{u,d} ≈ 350 MeV, with quark-gluon coupling screening below k ≈ 700 MeV (Braun 2014).
- Quark-meson coupling saturates at h ≈ 13 in the infrared (Braun 2014).
- Chiral symmetry restoration: quark condensate begins melting around T ≈ 100 MeV and effectively disappears above T ≈ 200 MeV (fRG, in agreement with lattice QCD).
- Debye screening in NLO HTL provides semi-quantitative description in distance region 0.3 fm ≤ r ≤ 0.6 fm for T > 300 MeV (Bazavov 2018).
- Best current estimates for a QCD critical point location: (μ_B, T) ≈ (600 MeV, 100 MeV) (Hippert 2023, Clarke 2024, Borsanyi 2025).
- Lattice QCD equation of state computed for μ_B/T ≤ 3.5 with controlled errors (Borsanyi 2021).
- fRG transverse gluon spectral function computed at T = 2.77 T_c (Haas 2013).

## Context

This review synthesizes fifty years of theoretical and experimental investigation, extending the original conjectures of Collins and Perry (1975) based on asymptotic freedom and of Cabibbo and Parisi (1975) based on Hagedorn's limiting temperature. It shows that both conjectures were only partially correct: the QGP is indeed composed of interacting quarks and gluons, but the interaction is so strong that the constituents are not "nearly free." The work builds on HTL perturbation theory (Braaten 1989, Taylor 1990, Blaizot 2001), lattice QCD results (Borsanyi 2010, Bazavov 2011, HotQCD 2014), and fRG approaches (Braun 2014, Fu 2019). It enables future experimental and theoretical investigation of open questions, including the structure of QGP at high net-baryon density, the existence of a critical point, and the role of chromomagnetic instabilities in isotropization.
