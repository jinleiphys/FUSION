# Nuclear two point correlation functions on a quantum-computer

> The calculation of dynamic response functions is expected to be an early application benefiting from rapidly developing quantum hardware resources. The ability to calculate real-time quantities of strongly-correlated quantum systems is one of the most exciting applications that can easily reach beyond the capabilities of traditional classical hardware. Response functions of fermionic systems at moderate momenta and energies corresponding roughly to the Fermi energy of the system are a potential early application because the relevant operators are nearly local and the energies can be resolved in moderately short real time, reducing the spatial resolution and gate depth required. This is particularly the case in quasielastic electron and neutrino scattering from nuclei, a topic of great interest in the nuclear and particle physics communities and directly related to experiments designed to probe neutrino properties. In this work we use current quantum hardware and error mitigation protocols to calculate response functions for a highly simplified nuclear model through calculations of a 2-point real time correlation function for a modified Fermi-Hubbard model in two dimensions with three distinguishable nucleons on four lattice sites.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that current quantum hardware, combined with error mitigation protocols, can calculate real-time two-point correlation functions for a highly simplified nuclear model (three distinguishable nucleons on a four-site two-dimensional lattice), but only for short evolution times where the Trotter approximation remains valid and for momentum transfers where gate counts are low.

## Method
The authors map a modified Fermi-Hubbard model with two- and three-body interactions, representing a triton-like system on a 2D lattice with periodic boundary conditions, onto a 4-qubit Hilbert space using a first-quantization encoding. They implement the Hadamard test circuit from Somma (2002) to compute the real-time two-point correlation function, using first-order Trotter-Suzuki decompositions of the time evolution operator with four different time orderings (A1, A2, B1, B2). The circuits are executed on the IBM Ourense five-qubit device, and the raw results are post-processed with readout error mitigation and zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE).

## Key numbers
- Lattice spacing: 1.4 fm (from Rokash 2013, via table 1 of Roggero 2020A).
- Initial state fidelity relative to exact ground state: 96.2%.
- CNOT gate counts for the B2 time ordering range from 8 to 15 across different correlator structures, while B1 ranges from 26 to 29.
- The error mitigation procedure brings hardware results close to numerical results for momentum transfer q = (0,1), but for q = (1,1) the mitigated imaginary part deviates significantly from exact results.
- Trotter approximation is a good approximation for times around 0.1–0.2 (in the paper's dimensionless time units).

## Context
This work extends the approach of Roggero (2020A), who introduced the lattice model and initial state preparation, by implementing the real-time correlation function calculation on actual quantum hardware rather than in simulation. It contrasts with classical methods such as Quantum Monte Carlo calculations of imaginary-time two-point functions (Carlson 1992, Lovato 2016) and the short-time approximation (Pastore 2020), which avoid the ill-posed inversion problem of Euclidean data. The results enable future studies of response functions at lower momenta and energies on quantum devices, though higher-order Trotter formulas and improved error mitigation will be needed for longer evolution times.
