# Neutrino flavor instabilities in a time-dependent supernova model

> A dense neutrino medium such as that inside a core-collapse supernova can experience collective flavor conversion or oscillations because of the neutral-current weak interaction among the neutrinos. This phenomenon has been studied in a restricted, stationary supernova model which possesses the (spatial) spherical symmetry about the center of the supernova and the (directional) axial symmetry around the radial direction. Recently it has been shown that these spatial and directional symmetries can be broken spontaneously by collective neutrino oscillations. In this paper we analyze the neutrino flavor instabilities in a time-dependent supernova model. Our results show that collective neutrino oscillations start at approximately the same radius in both the stationary and time-dependent supernova models unless there exist very rapid variations in local physical conditions on timescales of a few microseconds or shorter. Our results also suggest that collective neutrino oscillations can vary rapidly with time in the regimes where they do occur which need to be studied in time-dependent supernova models.

## Key claim

Collective neutrino oscillations in a time-dependent supernova model begin at approximately the same radius as in the stationary (Bulb) model, unless local physical conditions vary on timescales of a few microseconds or shorter; however, once collective oscillations begin, they can vary rapidly with time, meaning time-dependent models are needed to capture the full dynamics.

## Method

The authors performed a linear flavor-stability analysis on a time-dependent version of the neutrino Bulb model, which retains spatial spherical symmetry and directional axial symmetry but removes the assumption of time-translation symmetry. They derived the equation of motion for the neutrino flavor density matrix in frequency space, showing that the time-dependent model differs from the stationary model only by a replacement of the matter potential term λ → λ − ϖ, where ϖ is the Fourier frequency representing temporal variation. They then computed the maximum exponential growth rates κ_max for various Fourier modes ϖ using two sets of neutrino spectra: a single-energy spectrum and a continuous Fermi-Dirac spectrum, both assuming an inverted neutrino mass hierarchy.

## Key numbers

- For the single-energy spectrum, the peak of κ_max for the ϖ=0 mode corresponds to a distance of 107 km from the center of the supernova.
- For the continuous Fermi-Dirac spectrum, the peak of κ_max for the ϖ=0 mode corresponds to a distance of 149 km from the center of the supernova.
- The single-energy spectrum used vacuum oscillation frequency ω₀ = 0.68 km⁻¹, with number fluxes N_νe = 1.25×10⁵⁶ s⁻¹, N_ν̄e = 8.32×10⁵⁵ s⁻¹, and N_νx/ν̄x = 5.20×10⁵⁵ s⁻¹.
- The continuous Fermi-Dirac spectra had average energies ⟨E_νe⟩ = 9.4 MeV, ⟨E_ν̄e⟩ = 13.0 MeV, ⟨E_νx/ν̄x⟩ = 15.8 MeV, and luminosities L_νe = 4.1×10⁵¹ erg s⁻¹, L_ν̄e = 4.3×10⁵¹ erg s⁻¹, L_νx/ν̄x = 7.9×10⁵¹ erg s⁻¹.
- Both cases assumed a neutrino sphere radius R = 10 km and mass-squared difference Δm² = −2.4×10⁻³ eV² (inverted hierarchy).
- Frequency modes with |ϖ| ≲ 100 km⁻¹ showed approximately the same instability region and growth rates as the stationary (ϖ=0) mode.
- The causality constraint in the spherical Bulb model limits allowed oscillation frequencies to ϖ ≲ R⁻¹ ≈ (30 μs)⁻¹.

## Context

This work extends the stationary Bulb model (Duan 2006) by analyzing whether the time-translation symmetry can be broken spontaneously by collective neutrino oscillations, a question raised by Raffelt (2013). It builds on recent studies showing that spatial and directional symmetries can be broken spontaneously (Raffelt 2013, Mirizzi 2013, Duan 2013, Mangano 2014, Duan 2014, Mirizzi 2015, Chakraborty 2015, Abbar 2015). The results show that while the onset radius for collective oscillations is similar in stationary and time-dependent models, the strong time dependence of oscillations in the unstable regime means that future work must use time-dependent supernova models to accurately describe neutrino flavor evolution.
