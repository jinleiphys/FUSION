# Hydrodynamic models of particle production

> Viscous hydrodynamics gives a satisfactory description of the transverse momentum spectra, of the elliptic and triangular flow, and of the femtoscopic correlations for particles produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. On general grounds, a similar collective behavior has been predicted for proton-lead (p-Pb) collisions at the LHC. We present results of the hydrodynamics calculation of the elliptic and triangular flow in p-Pb. We discuss the mass dependence of flow coefficients and of the average transverse momentum for identified particles.

# Hydrodynamic models of particle production

## Key claim

Viscous hydrodynamics can provide a semi-quantitative description of elliptic and triangular flow in p-Pb collisions at LHC energies, but the agreement with data is limited by large model uncertainties on initial eccentricity, system size, and non-flow correlations that are not implemented in the models.

## Method

The authors perform event-by-event relativistic viscous hydrodynamic calculations using fluctuating initial conditions from a Glauber Monte Carlo model, applied to p-Pb collisions at LHC energies. They compute the harmonic flow coefficients v₂ and v₃, the average transverse momentum, and the mass dependence of these observables for identified pions, kaons, and protons, then compare the results to experimental data from the ALICE and CMS Collaborations.

## Key numbers

- Root mean square radius of the p-Pb source: 1–2 fm, depending on the energy deposition model and centrality
- Initial eccentricity ε₂ in central d-A collisions: approximately 0.5, giving large elliptic flow
- Interferometry radii grow as a power of multiplicity N^{1/3}, with different slopes for A-A and p-p collisions
- Freeze-out hypersurface shown for an event with 19 participant nucleons

## Context

This work extends hydrodynamic modeling from heavy-ion collisions (where collective flow is well established) to small systems such as p-Pb at LHC energies, following predictions by Bozek (2011) that significant collective expansion could occur. The results are consistent with experimental observations of double-ridge structures (Bozek 2012), elliptic and triangular flow (Bozek 2013, Qin 2013, Werner 2013), and mass hierarchy of average transverse momentum (Bozek 2013, Pierog 2013). The findings enable the use of p-Pb collisions as a testing ground for the onset of collectivity in small systems, though the authors note that the hydrodynamic model becomes increasingly questionable for more peripheral collisions due to large non-flow contributions and the importance of the non-thermal corona.
