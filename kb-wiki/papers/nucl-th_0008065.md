# Nuclear Aspects of Nucleosynthesis in Massive Stars

> Preliminary results of a new set of stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis calculations for massive stars are presented. These results were obtained with an extended reaction network up to Bi. The discussion focuses on the importance of nuclear rates in pre- and post-explosive nucleosynthesis. The need for further experiments to study specific reactions and nuclear properties (optical alpha+nucleus potentials) is emphasized.

## Key claim

The paper presents preliminary results from a new set of stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis calculations for massive stars, using an extended reaction network up to Bi, but emphasizes that these are first results for a 15 solar mass model and a more extended report will be published elsewhere.

## Method

The authors performed stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis calculations for a 15 solar mass star using a reaction network containing about 2350 isotopes, with reaction rates obtained from the NON-SMOKER statistical model code based on the FRDM mass model. They included updated experimental cross sections for light and heavy nuclei, updated beta-decay rates, new predictions of weak rates, updated neutrino loss rates and opacity tables (OPAL95), and considered mass loss due to stellar wind. The calculations consistently followed production of all isotopes up to Bi during pre-supernova evolution and the type II supernova explosion.

## Key numbers

- Reaction network during the explosive phase contains about 2350 isotopes
- The stellar model is for a 15 solar mass star
- The reaction rate library includes rates for all possible targets from proton-dripline to neutron-dripline and between Ne and Bi, making it the most extensive published library of theoretical reaction rates to date
- The revised weak rates lead to an increase of the central electron fraction (Ye) and smaller Fe core masses at the onset of core collapse

## Context

This work extends previous investigations on pre- and post-collapse evolution and nucleosynthesis by Woosley and Weaver (1995) and others, using improved stellar and nuclear physics inputs. The new calculations enable consistent study of nucleosynthesis in type II supernova models over a wide range of nuclear masses, and identify the (alpha,n)-(alpha,gamma) branching on 22Ne as an important candidate for further laboratory study that sensitively determines the strength of the s-process in supernova models. The paper also emphasizes the need for further experiments to study optical alpha+nucleus potentials, building on earlier work by McFadden and Satchler (1966) and more recent folding potential studies by Mohr (1994) and Atzrott et al. (1996).
