# Linearized fluctuating hydrodynamics via random polynomials

> We argue that an ensemble of backgrounds best understands hydrodynamic dispersion relations in a medium with few degrees of freedom and is therefore subject to strong thermal fluctuations. In the linearized regime, dispersion relations become describeable by polynomials with random coefficients. We give a short review of this theory and perform a numerical study of the distribution of the roots of polynomials whose coefficients are of the order of a Knudsen series but fluctuate in accordance with canonical fluctuations of temperature. We find that, remarkably, the analytic structure of the poles of fluctuating dispersion relations is very different from deterministic ones, particularly regarding the distribution of imaginary parts with respect to real components. We argue that this provides evidence that hydrodynamic behavior persists, and is enhanced, by non-perturbative background fluctuations.

# Linearized fluctuating hydrodynamics via random polynomials

## Key claim

The authors argue that in systems with few degrees of freedom where thermal fluctuations are strong, hydrodynamic dispersion relations are best described by an ensemble of backgrounds rather than a single deterministic background; the analytic structure of the poles of these fluctuating dispersion relations differs significantly from deterministic ones, particularly in the distribution of imaginary parts relative to real components, providing evidence that hydrodynamic behavior persists and is enhanced by non-perturbative background fluctuations.

## Method

The authors perform a numerical study of random polynomials whose coefficients follow a Knudsen series expansion but fluctuate according to canonical temperature fluctuations, using a Gaussian distribution for temperature with width determined by the number of particles N and heat capacity per degree of freedom. They generate 10^5 realizations of the dispersion relation at each momentum bin, fitting the resulting distributions of real and imaginary parts of the roots to Gaussian distributions, and compare the ratio of imaginary to real parts for both the randomly generated modes and the deterministic hydrodynamic modes from the Müller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) formalism at two different relaxation times (τ₁ = (2−ln2)/(2πT) and τ₂ = 0.1/(2πT)).

## Key numbers

- Temperature fluctuation width: ⟨(ΔT)²⟩ = 2T²/(N c_v), where c_v = g with g = 6 (accounting for three momentum directions and three spatial displacements)
- The distribution for random coefficients uses: D(N, x) = (1/2)√(gN/π) × e^{−gN(x−1)²/4} / (1 − Erf(√(gN)/2)/2), for x ≥ 0
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio fixed at η/s = 1/(4π)
- Speed of sound fixed at c_s² = 1/3
- Low-momentum bins: m = [1, 2, ..., 100] with k = 4πT m/1000; high-momentum bins: m = [300, 301, ..., 500]
- Two relaxation times studied: τ₁T = (2−ln2)/(2π) (corresponding to N=4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills result from Baier (2007)) and τ₂T = 0.1/(2π)
- Series expansions truncated at order 4 and order 10 in momentum

## Context

This work extends the understanding of hydrodynamic behavior in small systems, building on the observation by Nagle (2018) that hydrodynamics appears applicable in systems with few degrees of freedom, contradicting naive fluctuation-dissipation thinking where mean free path and fluctuation scale are comparable. The authors connect their random polynomial approach to the Kubo formula formalism of Kovtun (2012) and the dispersion relation structure from Grozdanov (2019, 2020), and compare their results to the MIS formalism. The work enables future study of how thermal fluctuations modify the propagation of hydrodynamic modes, particularly in the regime where the Knudsen number is not small, and suggests that the fluctuating regime is far from a trivial extrapolation of deterministic hydrodynamics.
