# XCDCC: Core Excitation in the Breakup of Exotic Nuclei

> The eXtended Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (XCDCC) method is developed to treat reactions where core degrees of freedom play a role. The projectile is treated as a multi-configuration coupled channels system generated from a valence particle coupled to a deformed core which is allowed to excite. The coupled channels initial state breaks up into a coupled channels continuum which is discretized into bins, similarly to the original CDCC method. Core collective degrees of freedom are also included in the interaction of the core and the target, so that dynamical effects can occur during the reaction. We present results for the breakup of $^{17}$C=$^{16}$C+n and $^{11}$Be=$^{10}$Be+n on $^{9}$Be. Results show that the total cross section increases with core deformation. More importantly, the relative percentage of the various components of the initial state are modified during the reaction process through dynamical effects. This implies that comparing spectroscopic factors from structure calculations with experimental cross sections requires more detailed reaction models that go beyond the single particle model.

## Key claim

The XCDCC method demonstrates that core excitation and de-excitation during the reaction process modify the relative percentages of the various components of the projectile's initial state, implying that comparing spectroscopic factors from structure calculations with experimental cross sections requires reaction models that go beyond the single-particle model, particularly when core deformation is present.

## Method

The eXtended Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (XCDCC) method generalizes the standard CDCC approach by treating the projectile as a multi-configuration coupled channels system generated from a valence particle coupled to a deformed core that is allowed to excite. The projectile's coupled channels initial state breaks up into a coupled channels continuum which is discretized into bins, and core collective degrees of freedom are included in the core-target interaction so that dynamical effects can occur during the reaction. The method is applied to the breakup of 17C=16C+n and 11Be=10Be+n on a 9Be target.

## Key numbers

- For 17C breakup on 9Be at approximately 60 A MeV, the total cross section increases with core deformation (quadrupole deformation parameter β2=0.55 used for 16C core)
- The 17C ground state (JPπ=2+) contains four coupled channels with relative probabilities: [d⊗0+] at 0.175, [s⊗2+] at 0.103, [d⊗2+] at 0.721, and [g⊗2+] at 0.001
- The 16C core has two states {0+,2+} with excitation energy E2+=1.766 MeV
- The 17C rms radius calculated for the deformed model is 2.77 fm, compared to the experimental value of 3.04±0.11 fm (St. Laurent 1989)
- For 11Be, the ground state (JP=0+) has two coupled channels: [s⊗0+] at 0.883 and [d⊗2+] at 0.117 relative probabilities, using deformation β2=0.67 from Nunes (2001)

## Context

This work extends the standard CDCC method (originally developed by the Kyushu group and others) to include core excitation degrees of freedom, addressing limitations identified in earlier single-particle models. Prior work by Tarutina (2004) studied interference effects between states with the same core spin in Coulomb dissociation using a semi-classical first-order approximation, while Batham (2005) used the eikonal approximation to calculate total cross sections for deformed core plus nucleon systems. The XCDCC method enables fully quantal treatment of dynamical core excitation during breakup reactions, which is necessary for properly interpreting spectroscopic factors from experiments such as those at MSU (Aumann 2000, Maddalena 2001) and for understanding discrepancies found in 8B breakup studies (Mortimer 2002, Summers 2005).
