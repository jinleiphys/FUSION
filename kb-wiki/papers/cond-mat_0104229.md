# Dynamical local field, compressibility and frequency sum rules for quasiparticles

> The finite temperature dynamical response function including the dynamical local field is derived within a quasiparticle picture for interacting one-, two- and three dimensional Fermi systems. The correlations are assumed to be given by a density dependent effective mass, quasiparticle energy shift and relaxation time. The latter one describes disorder or collisional effects. This parameterization of correlations includes local density functionals as a special case and is therefore applicable for density functional theories. With a single static local field, the third order frequency sum rule can be fulfilled simultaneously with the compressibility sum rule by relating the effective mass and quasiparticle energy shift to the structure function or pair correlation function. Consequently, solely local density functionals without taking into account effective masses cannot fulfill both sum rules simultaneously with a static local field. The comparison to the Monte-Carlo data seems to support such quasiparticle picture.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a single static local field factor can simultaneously satisfy both the compressibility sum rule and the third-order frequency sum rule, provided the quasiparticle picture includes a density-dependent effective mass and energy shift, contradicting prior assertions that a static local field alone cannot fulfill both constraints.

## Method

The author derives a finite-temperature dynamical response function within a quasiparticle picture where correlations are parameterized by a density-dependent effective mass, a quasiparticle energy shift, and a relaxation time. The dynamical local field is extracted from this response function, and its static and high-frequency limits are compared to the compressibility sum rule and the third-order frequency sum rule, respectively. The effective mass and energy shift are then related to the static structure factor or pair correlation function via the sum-rule constraints, yielding a self-consistent scheme analogous to the Singwi-Tosi-Land-Sjölander (STLS) approach but with an additional effective-mass term.

## Key numbers

* For the unpolarized electron gas in three dimensions at zero temperature, the density parameter is the Bruckner parameter \( r_s = (3/4 \pi n)^{1/3} / a_0 \), where \( n \) is the density and \( a_0 \) the Bohr radius.
* The first frequency moment (energy-weighted sum rule) is \( \langle \omega \rangle = n q^2 / m \), where \( n \) is the density, \( q \) the wavevector, and \( m \) the mass.
* The third-order frequency moment is \( \langle \omega^3 \rangle = 2 E_{\text{int}} q^4 / m^2 + n q^6 / (4 m^3) - (n^2 q^4 / m^2) V_q \tilde I(q) \), where \( E_{\text{int}} \) is the interacting kinetic energy and \( \tilde I(q) \) is defined via the static structure factor.
* In the small-wavevector limit, the static local field required for the compressibility sum rule is \( \lim_{q \to 0} f_q(0) = \partial \Delta / \partial n - (3 / (2 n^2 \mathcal{K}_0)) \, \partial \ln m / \partial \ln n \), where \( \mathcal{K}_0 \) is the free compressibility.
* In the large-wavevector limit, the third-order sum rule gives \( \lim_{q \to \infty} \tilde I(q) = \frac{2}{3} (1 - g_0) - 1 \), where \( g_0 \) is the pair correlation function at zero distance.

## Context

This work extends the line of static local-field corrections initiated by Hubbard (1957) and refined by Singwi et al. (1968) and Pathak and Vashishta (1973), who showed that a static local field cannot simultaneously satisfy the compressibility and third-order sum rules for Coulomb systems (Vashishta and Singwi, 1972; Ichimaru, 1984). The paper resolves this "sum-rule puzzle" by incorporating a density-dependent effective mass into the quasiparticle picture, enabling a single static local field to meet both constraints. This enables a parameterization of the response function from experimental or Monte-Carlo static structure factors, and provides a link to time-dependent density functional theories.
