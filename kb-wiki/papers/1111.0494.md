# Correlation between muonic levels and nuclear structure in muonic atoms

> A method that deals with the nucleons and the muon unitedly is employed to investigate the muonic lead, with which the correlation between the muon and nucleus can be studied distinctly. A "kink" appears in the muonic isotope shift at a neutron magic number where the nuclear shell structure plays a key role. This behavior may have very important implications for the experimentally probing the shell structure of the nuclei far away from the $β$-stable line. We investigate the variations of the nuclear structure due to the interaction with the muon in the muonic atom and find that the nuclear structure remains basically unaltered. Therefore, the muon is a clean and reliable probe for studying the nuclear structure. In addition, a correction that the muon-induced slight change in the proton density distribution in turn shifts the muonic levels is investigated. This correction to muonic level is as important as the Lamb shift and high order vacuum polarization correction, but is larger than anomalous magnetic moment and electron shielding correction.

## Key claim

A "kink" appears in the muonic isotope shift at the neutron magic number N=126 for lead isotopes, which originates from the nuclear shell effect and may serve as a signal to experimentally probe the shell structure of nuclei far from the beta-stable line. The nuclear structure remains basically unaltered by the presence of the muon, confirming that the muon is a clean and reliable probe for nuclear structure studies.

## Method

The authors extended the relativistic mean field (RMF) approach to include a negatively charged muon, treating nucleons and the muon in a unified relativistic framework without adjustable parameters. They solved the Dirac equation for nucleons and muons, and the Klein-Gordon equations for mesons, in coordinate space using a mesh size of 0.01 fm, with pairing correlations accounted in the BCS formalism. The NL3 and NLSH parameter sets were employed, and the lowest order vacuum polarization correction was calculated additionally to obtain the final muonic levels.

## Key numbers

- Muonic 1s1/2 level in 208Pb: -10598.29 keV (NL3) and -10608.49 keV (NLSH), compared to experimental value of -10593.13 ± 0.41 keV
- Muonic 1s1/2 level in 204Pb: -10615.76 keV (NL3) and -10627.72 keV (NLSH), compared to experimental value of -10614.88 ± 0.41 keV
- Mean speed of the muon in 1s1/2 orbit for muon-208Pb: v = 0.31c
- Correction to muonic 1s1/2 levels due to muon-induced change in proton density: -1.5 keV (NL3) and -1.2 keV (NLSH) for all Pb isotopes studied (204, 206, 208, 210)
- Nucleon densities reduced by about 0.1% at the edge of the nucleus (~7 fm) and enhanced in the interior by about 0.1%
- Nuclear rms radii reduced by only about 0.02% due to the bound muon
- For comparison: anomalous magnetic moment correction to 1s1/2 in muonic 206Pb is 0.445 keV, Lamb shift correction is 2.302 keV, and high order vacuum polarization correction is -1.75 keV

## Context

This work extends the relativistic mean field approach to muonic atoms, providing a more microscopic treatment compared to earlier works based on Migdal theory (Migdal 1977) or methods that fit Fermi-type charge distributions to experimental muonic levels. The approach establishes a direct link between nuclear physics and atomic physics, and the finding that the muon-induced changes to nuclear structure are slight confirms that previous experiments on nuclear charge distributions using muonic X-rays were theoretically sound. The "kink" in muonic isotope shifts at magic numbers could enable probing shell structure of exotic nuclei at future radioactive beam facilities, complementing Coulomb excitation measurements of first excited 2+ states, as proposed by Strasser (2000) for muonic atom spectroscopy with radioactive isotope beams.
