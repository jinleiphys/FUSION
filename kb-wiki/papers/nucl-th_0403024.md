# Is friction responsible for the reduction of fusion rates far below the Coulomb barrier?

> The fusion of two interacting heavy ions traditionally has been interpreted in terms of the penetration of the projectile into the target. Observed rates well below the Coulomb barrier are considerably lower than estimates obtained from penetration factors. One approach in the analysis of the data invokes coupling to non-elastic channels in the scattering as the source of the depletion. Another is to analyze those data in terms of tunneling in semi-classical models, with the observed depletion being taken as evidence of a ``friction'' under the barrier. A complementary approach is to consider such tunneling in terms of a fully quantal model. We investigate tunneling with both one-dimensional and three-dimensional models in a fully quantal approach to investigate possible sources for such a friction. We find that the observed phenomenon may not be explained by friction. However, we find that under certain conditions tunneling may be enhanced or diminished by up to 50%, which finds analogy with observation, without the invocation of a friction under the barrier.

## Key claim

The observed reduction of fusion rates far below the Coulomb barrier cannot be explained by a "friction" mechanism; however, under certain conditions involving time-dependent fluctuations of the barrier, tunneling may be enhanced or diminished by up to 50%, which finds analogy with observation without invoking a friction under the barrier.

## Method

The authors solved the time-dependent Schrodinger equation for wave packets incident on Gaussian barriers in one dimension, comparing a reference potential with potentials modified by either spatial fluctuations (with invariant classical action) or time-dependent oscillations. They also solved the radial time-independent Schrodinger equation for a three-dimensional model using a staircase potential approximating a nuclear attractive well plus Coulomb barrier, introducing random fluctuations to the step heights while maintaining the classical action invariant to within 0.01%.

## Key numbers

- For the one-dimensional reference potential with incident momentum K = 1.06, the asymptotic transmitted norm T(infinity) = 0.203 (approximately 20% transmission)
- For spatial fluctuations at K = 0.6, the "ears-up" potential gave T(infinity) = 0.0226 compared to the reference value of 0.0239
- For spatial fluctuations at K = 0.42, the "ears-down" potential produced a significant depletion relative to the reference
- Time-dependent fluctuations with gamma = 0.2 produced variations in transmission of up to 50% for K = 0.6
- For the three-dimensional staircase potential, the ratio of average log transmission index to classical action was approximately -1.2 to within a few percent across all energies from 0.1 to 0.7 (dimensionless units)
- The transition between decreased transmission at lower energies and increased transmission at higher energies in the 3D model occurred near E ~ 0.3 (dimensionless units)

## Context

This work extends the debate between two approaches to explaining sub-barrier fusion suppression: coupling to non-elastic channels (as discussed in Hagino (2003) and Jiang (2004)) versus semi-classical models invoking a "friction" under the barrier following Caldeira and Leggett (1981). The authors provide a fully quantal alternative to the semi-classical Langevin approach, finding that spatial fluctuations produce changes of only about 10% in transmission, insufficient to explain the observed depletion, while time-dependent fluctuations can produce the observed 50% variations. This contradicts the friction hypothesis and suggests that time-dependent coupling effects, rather than a dissipative mechanism, may be responsible for the observed fusion suppression.
