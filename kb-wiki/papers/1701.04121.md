# Observing a scale anomaly and a universal quantum phase transition in graphene

> One of the most interesting predictions resulting from quantum physics, is the violation of classical symmetries, collectively referred to as anomalies. A remarkable class of anomalies occurs when the continuous scale symmetry of a scale free quantum system is broken into a discrete scale symmetry for a critical value of a control parameter. This is an example of a (zero temperature) quantum phase transition. Such an anomaly takes place for the quantum inverse square potential known to describe 'Efimov physics'. Broken continuous scale symmetry into discrete scale symmetry also appears for a charged and massless Dirac fermion in an attractive $1/r$ Coulomb potential. The purpose of this article is to demonstrate the universality of this quantum phase transition and to present convincing experimental evidence of its existence for a charged and massless fermion in an attractive Coulomb potential as realised in graphene.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a charged vacancy in graphene realizes a universal quantum phase transition (QPT) from a single quasi-bound state to an infinite geometric ladder of quasi-bound states, driven by the dimensionless coupling parameter β = Zα. The transition occurs at the critical value βc = 1/2, and the experimental observation of two intertwined ladders provides evidence that parity symmetry is broken by the vacancy.

## Method

The authors created single-atom vacancies in graphene by sputtering with He+ ions and used scanning tunnelling microscopy (STM) to inject positive charge into the vacancies via voltage pulses, thereby tuning the effective Coulomb charge Z. They measured the differential conductance dI/dV as a function of bias voltage, which is proportional to the derivative of the scattering phase shift dη/dE, and compared the positions of resonant quasi-bound states to the theoretical spectrum derived from the massless Dirac equation with an attractive 1/r Coulomb potential regularized by a cutoff length L and a boundary condition parameter h.

## Key numbers

- Critical coupling for the QPT: βc = 1/2 (for the s-wave channels m = 0, -1 in d = 2)
- Cutoff length determined from experiment: L ≃ 0.2 nm, satisfying the low-energy condition E1L/ℏvF ≃ 0.03 ≪ 1
- Fitted exponent b for the ratio E2/E1 = e^{-b/√(β² - 1/4)}: b = 3.145 with standard error Δb = 0.06, consistent with the predicted value π
- Efimov fixed coupling value: βE = 1.1236, corresponding to s0 ≃ 1.00624
- Fermi velocity in graphene: vF ≃ 10⁶ m/s
- Error bar on resonance energies: ±1 mV

## Context

This work extends the theoretical understanding of scale anomalies from the inverse-square potential (Case 1950, Landau 1991) and Efimov physics (Efimov 1970, 1971) to the experimentally accessible platform of graphene, where the effective fine structure constant αG is of order unity. The results directly connect to prior observations of Efimov states in ultracold caesium atoms (Kraemer 2006, Huang 2014) and demonstrate universality across disparate physical systems. The ability to tune β continuously in graphene enables systematic study of the QPT, which was previously only accessible at the fixed Efimov value in cold atoms, and motivates further investigation of related transitions in flavoured QED3 (Appelquist 1988) and the XY model.
