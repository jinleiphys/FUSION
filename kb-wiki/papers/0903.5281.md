# The ridge laboratory

> Mechanisms proposed to explain the jet-related correlation of long range in rapidity (the ridge) observed in high-energy heavy-ion collisions are reviewed. Limitations of a model using the combined effect of transverse flow and a direction bias of partons suffering energy loss are discussed. The influence of the time scale of the correlation mechanism on the rapidity range is investigated.

# The ridge laboratory

## Key claim

The ridge phenomenon in heavy-ion collisions likely originates from jet-medium interactions, but no existing model quantitatively explains all experimental observations; models based on final-state interactions face causality constraints that limit the rapidity range, while initial-state models are not yet quantified against data.

## Method

The paper reviews proposed mechanisms for the ridge, a correlation structure narrow in azimuthal angle (Δφ) but broad in pseudorapidity (Δη) observed in central Au+Au collisions, focusing on a model combining parton energy loss with transverse flow (Shuryak, 2007). It examines the directional bias of hard-scattered partons near the surface, the quenching probability parameterized by an absorption length l_abs, and the resulting trigger distribution. The author then derives causal constraints on the time scale of correlation formation, extending earlier estimates (Dumitru, 2008) to include finite longitudinal velocity of emitted gluons, yielding a maximum rapidity range for final-state mechanisms.

## Key numbers

- The ridge persists up to trigger particle momenta of p_T ≈ 8 GeV/c in central Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV/nucleon (STAR).
- The ridge shape is consistent with a constant for Δη < 1.8, requiring correlation generation at very early times.
- A typical quenching length of l_abs = 0.5 fm yields an inclusive suppression R_AA ≈ 0.01, much stronger than observed experimentally; more realistic values require l_abs ≈ 3 fm.
- For a gluon emission angle of 60°, the maximum rapidity range from final-state mechanisms is Δy_max ≈ 1.3.
- The PHOBOS experiment reports a semi-soft ridge persisting to Δη ≈ 4.

## Context

This review extends discussions of the ridge first observed by STAR (2005) and later by PHOBOS (2008), comparing models by Voloshin (2006), Dumitru (2008), Gavin (2008), Ma (2006), Shuryak (2007), Wong (2007), and Hwa (2007). It identifies limitations in the Shuryak (2007) flow-bias model, particularly its over-suppression and insufficient angular correlation, and highlights the need for causal constraints on final-state mechanisms. The work enables future discrimination between initial-state and final-state ridge mechanisms at LHC, where larger rapidity range and higher p_T reach will clarify the origin, and suggests the ridge could serve as a laboratory for studying parton energy loss and bulk matter properties.
