# Nuclear Photoabsorption at Photon Energies between 300 and 850 Mev

> We construct the formula for the photonuclear total absorption cross section using the projection method and the unitarity relation. Our treatment is very effective when interference effects in the absorption processes on a nucleon are strong. The disappearance of the peak around the position of the $D_{13}$ resonance in the nuclear photoabsorption can be explained with the cooperative effect of the interference in two-pion production processes,the Fermi motion, the collision broadenings of $Δ$ and $N^*$, and the pion distortion in the nuclear medium. The change of the interference effect by the medium plays an important role.

## Key claim
The disappearance of the $D_{13}$ resonance peak in nuclear photoabsorption can be explained by the cooperative effect of interference in two-pion production processes, Fermi motion, collision broadenings of $\Delta$ and $N^*$, and pion distortion in the nuclear medium, with the change of the interference effect by the medium playing an important role. The authors claim this explains the strong reduction of the cross section without requiring the large collision widths for the $D_{13}$ and $F_{15}$ resonances assumed in prior work by Alberico et al. and Kondratyuk et al.

## Method
The authors constructed a formula for the photonuclear total absorption cross section using the projection operator technique and the unitarity relation, focusing on the second resonance energy region where the $N^*$(1520) resonance and two-pion photoproduction via $\pi\Delta$ and $\rho N$ channels dominate. They applied the formalism to evaluate cross sections in the energy range 300 to 850 MeV using a Fermi gas model for the nucleus, incorporating medium corrections including mass shifts, collision widths for $\Delta$ and $N^*$, pion distortion, and Pauli blocking effects.

## Key numbers
- The mass number dependence of total cross sections varies with photon energy as: $A^{0.8}$ around 300 MeV, $A^{1.7}$ around 500 MeV, and $A^{0.65}$ around 750 MeV.
- The one-pion production cross section on a free proton shows a small peak around 720 MeV.
- Two-pion photoproduction starts to grow from 400 MeV and increases up to around 800 MeV.
- The $\gamma N \rightarrow \pi^{0} \pi^{0} N$ reaction cross section is smaller than 10 percent of the total two-pion production reaction.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous letter (Hirata et al., 2000) and addresses the experimental finding from Frascati measurements that excitation peaks around the $D_{13}$(1520 MeV) and $F_{15}$(1680 MeV) resonances disappear in nuclear photoabsorption, with strong reduction above 600 MeV compared to hydrogen and deuteron data. It contradicts earlier theoretical attempts by Alberico et al. and Kondratyuk et al. that required large collision widths for these resonances, which were criticized as hardly justified by other authors. The formalism enables calculation of the mass number dependence of total cross sections and can be applied to understand the role of interference effects in the nuclear medium.
