# Constraining new physics with a novel measurement of the $^{23}$Ne $β$-decay branching ratio

> Measurements of the beta-neutrino correlation coefficient (a$_{βν}$) in nuclear beta decay, together with the Fierz interference term (b$_F$), provide a robust test for the existence of exotic interactions beyond the Standard Model of Particle Physics. The extraction of these quantities from the recoil ion spectra in $β$-decay requires accurate knowledge, decay branching ratios, and high-precision calculations of higher order nuclear effects. Here, we report on a new measurement of the $^{23}$Ne $β$-decay branching ratio, which allows a reanalysis of existing high-precision measurements. Together with new theoretical calculations of nuclear structure effects, augmented with robust theoretical uncertainty, this measurement improves on the current knowledge of a$_{βν}$ in $^{23}$Ne by an order of magnitude, and strongly constrains the Fierz term in beta decays, making this one of the first extractions to constrain both terms simultaneously. Together, these results place bounds on the existence of exotic tensor interactions and pave the way for new, even higher precision, experiments.

# Constraining new physics with a novel measurement of the $^{23}$Ne $\beta$-decay branching ratio

## Key claim
The new measurement of the $^{23}$Ne branching ratio to the first excited state of $^{23}$Na, combined with novel theoretical calculations of recoil-order corrections, improves the extraction of the beta-neutrino correlation coefficient $a_{\beta\nu}$ by an order of magnitude and simultaneously constrains the Fierz interference term $b_F$, but the results remain consistent with the Standard Model and do not yet provide evidence for beyond-Standard-Model tensor interactions.

## Method
The $^{23}$Ne atoms were produced via an (n,p) reaction on a natural abundance NaCl target using neutrons from a (d,n) reaction on a liquid lithium target (LiLiT). The branching ratio to the 440 keV first excited state of $^{23}$Na was measured by detecting beta particles with plastic scintillators in coincidence with gamma rays detected by a High Purity Germanium (HPGe) detector, with measurements taken at two detector distances (16 cm and 20 cm) to reduce systematic effects. The measured branching ratio was then used as a prior in a Bayesian reanalysis of the existing recoil ion spectra data from Carlson et al. (1963), incorporating new theoretical calculations of next-to-leading-order recoil and shape corrections based on a multipole expansion formalism.

## Key numbers
- Branching ratio to the 440 keV first excited state: $0.3310 \pm 0.0027$ (weighted average of $0.3293 \pm 0.0027$ at 16 cm and $0.3354 \pm 0.0043$ at 20 cm), an improvement by a factor of 3 over the currently accepted value and an order of magnitude improvement over the best direct measurement
- Extracted beta-neutrino correlation coefficient: $a_{\beta\nu} = -0.3331 \pm 0.0028 \pm 0.0004 \pm 0.0002$ (statistical, pressure effects, theory uncertainties respectively)
- Extracted Fierz interference term: $b_F = 0.0007 \pm 0.0049 \pm 0.0003 \pm 0.0001$, a factor of 2 better than the best available measurement in a pure Gamow-Teller decay
- Tensor coupling constraints: $(C_T + C'_T)/C_A = 0.0007 \pm 0.0049$ and $(C_T - C'_T)/C_A = 0.0001 \pm 0.0823$
- Theoretical uncertainty on recoil-order corrections: approximately $0.0004$, determined from next-to-next-to-leading-order estimates

## Context
This work extends the measurement of Carlson et al. (1963) of the $^{23}$Ne recoil ion spectra, which was limited by branching ratio uncertainty to approximately 10% relative uncertainty on $a_{\beta\nu}$. The new branching ratio measurement and theoretical calculations using the formalism of Glick-Magid et al. (2021) for recoil-order corrections, with shell-model wave functions from USDB and USD interactions, enable simultaneous extraction of $a_{\beta\nu}$ and $b_F$ at precision competitive with global fits such as those by Falkowski et al. (2020). The results pave the way for new, higher-precision experiments underway at the Hebrew University, anticipated to improve on this result by another order of magnitude.
