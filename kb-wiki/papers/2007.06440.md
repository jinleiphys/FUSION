# Probing chemical freeze-out criteria in relativistic nuclear collisions with coarse grained transport simulations

> We introduce a novel approach based on elastic and inelastic scattering rates to extract the hyper-surface of the chemical freeze-out from a hadronic transport model in the energy range from E$_\mathrm{lab}=1.23$ AGeV to $\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}}=62.4$ GeV. For this study, the Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model combined with a coarse-graining method is employed. The chemical freeze-out distribution is reconstructed from the pions through several decay and re-formation chains involving resonances and taking into account inelastic, pseudo-elastic and string excitation reactions. The extracted average temperature and baryon chemical potential are then compared to statistical model analysis. Finally we investigate various freeze-out criteria suggested in the literature. We confirm within this microscopic dynamical simulation, that the chemical freeze-out at all energies coincides with $\langle E\rangle/\langle N\rangle\approx1$ GeV, while other criteria, like $s/T^3=7$ and $n_\mathrm{B}+n_\mathrm{\bar{B}}\approx0.12$ fm$^{-3}$ are limited to higher collision energies.

# Probing chemical freeze-out criteria in relativistic nuclear collisions with coarse grained transport simulations

## Key claim
The chemical freeze-out line in relativistic nuclear collisions is not necessarily indicative of a QCD phase transition, but emerges from the local competition between inelastic scattering rates and expansion rates in a purely hadronic transport model (UrQMD) that contains no explicit phase transition. The authors confirm that the Cleymans-Redlich criterion of average energy per particle near 1 GeV holds across all investigated energies, while other criteria like constant entropy density or baryon density are only valid at higher collision energies.

## Method
The authors used the Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) hadronic transport model in cascade mode to simulate central Au+Au collisions from E_lab = 1.23 AGeV to sqrt(s_NN) = 62.4 GeV. They developed a novel algorithm that traces each final-state pion backward through its decay and re-formation chains (involving resonances like Delta and rho, string excitations, and absorption processes) to determine its chemical freeze-out space-time coordinate. These coordinates were then combined with a coarse-graining procedure that computes local energy-momentum tensors and net-baryon currents on a space-time grid, from which temperature and baryon chemical potential were extracted using a Hadron Resonance Gas equation of state.

## Key numbers
- Average chemical freeze-out time remains approximately constant at about 7 fm for sqrt(s_NN) >= 7.7 GeV, ranging from 13.7 fm at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.4 GeV to 8.4 fm at sqrt(s_NN) = 62.4 GeV
- Chemical freeze-out temperature saturates at approximately 150 MeV at high energies, while kinetic freeze-out temperature saturates at approximately 130 MeV, with a constant difference of 20 +/- 5 MeV
- Average energy per particle at chemical freeze-out varies between 0.987 GeV (at sqrt(s_NN) = 27.0 GeV) and 1.194 GeV (at sqrt(s_NN) = 3.3 GeV), confirming the approximate 1 GeV criterion
- Entropy density s/T^3 at chemical freeze-out ranges from 31.5 at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.4 GeV down to 6.0-6.4 for sqrt(s_NN) >= 19.6 GeV, approaching the proposed criterion of 7 only at higher energies
- Total baryon plus anti-baryon density n_B + n_antiB at chemical freeze-out drops from 0.334 fm^-3 at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.4 GeV to 0.081 fm^-3 at sqrt(s_NN) = 62.4 GeV, approaching 0.12 fm^-3 only near sqrt(s_NN) = 19.6 GeV

## Context
This work extends the study of chemical freeze-out criteria originally proposed by Cleymans and Redlich (1998) (constant energy per particle), Cleymans et al. (2005) (constant entropy density s/T^3 = 7), and Braun-Munzinger (2002) (constant baryon density). It builds on previous coarse-graining studies of kinetic freeze-out by Inghirami et al. (2019) and scattering rate analyses by Bleicher et al. (2002). The results enable a dynamical understanding of chemical freeze-out as a kinetic process driven by the competition between scattering and expansion rates, rather than a signal of the QCD phase transition, and provide a microscopic validation of the Cleymans-Redlich criterion across the full energy range from GSI/FAIR to RHIC BES-II.
