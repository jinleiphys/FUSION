# Neutron injection during primordial nucleosynthesis alleviates the primordial 7Li problem

> We present a parametrized study of the effects of free thermal neutron injection on primordial nucleosynthesis, where both the rate and the time scale of injection are varied. This generic approach is found to yield a successful solution for reducing the 7Li abundance without causing significant problems to other elemental abundances. Our analysis demonstrates that hadronic injection, possibly due to decays or annihilations of dark matter particles with a mass of about 1 to 30 GeV, provides a possible solution to an outstanding problem in the standard Big Bang model.

## Key claim

A parametrized injection of free thermal neutrons during primordial nucleosynthesis, with injection rate and time scale as free parameters, can reduce the predicted 7Li abundance to match observations without significantly disturbing other light-element abundances, provided the neutrons are essentially thermal. The authors do not include a full treatment of neutron production and thermalization in their code, so the work is a generic, model-independent study that identifies the required injection parameters rather than a demonstration that any specific dark matter candidate works.

## Method

The authors extended their standard Big-Bang nucleosynthesis (SBBN) code, which includes 59 nuclides from neutrons to 23Na linked by 391 reactions and 33 beta-decay processes, by adding a proton-to-neutron conversion term with a time-dependent rate lambda(t). They considered five injection scenarios: constant rate at all times; constant rate only above a cutoff temperature Tc; constant rate only below Tc; exponential decay with lifetime tau_x; and a rate proportional to T^3. The code was run for a range of lambda_0 values and the resulting abundances of 4He, D, 3He, 7Li, 6Li, 9Be, 10B, 11B, and CNO isotopes were compared to observational constraints.

## Key numbers

- The primordial 7Li/H abundance from SBBN calculations is (5.14 +/- 0.50) x 10^-10, while observations of metal-poor halo stars give (1.58 +/- 0.31) x 10^-10 (Sbordone 2010), a factor of 3-4 discrepancy.
- Neutron injection at a constant rate of lambda_0 approximately 10^-8 s^-1 when T > 0.3 GK alleviates the 7Li problem without significantly affecting other isotopes.
- For exotic particle decays, the required injection rate is lambda_0 = (1.5-2) x 10^-8 s^-1.
- For dark matter annihilations, the required injection rate is lambda_0 = (3-5) x 10^-9 s^-1.
- The dark matter mass range of interest for producing neutrons is roughly 1 to 30 GeV.
- At a temperature of about 90 keV, a dark matter mass M_X less than or about 30 GeV and a canonical annihilation cross section of 3 x 10^-26 cm^3 s^-1 can achieve the needed injection rate.
- The 6Li abundance in the injection models at lambda_0 approximately 10^-8 n s^-1 is in the range (1-6) x 10^-14.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of hadronic injection during BBN (Reno 1987, Jedamzik 2004, 2006) and addresses the long-standing discrepancy between the predicted and observed primordial 7Li abundance, known as the "primordial lithium problem." The authors use the most up-to-date nuclear reaction network from Coc (2011) and show that neutron injection from decays or annihilations of dark matter particles in the 1-30 GeV mass range can resolve the discrepancy. The results motivate more detailed models of specific dark matter candidates, such as neutralinos in the Next-to-Minimal Supersymmetric Standard Model (Albornoz Vasquez 2011), and suggest that such candidates could be tested through direct detection signals and gamma-ray observations of dwarf spheroidal galaxies.
