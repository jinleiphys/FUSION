# Deciphering the measured ratios of Iodine-131 to Cesium-137 at the Fukushima reactors

> We calculate the relative abundance of the radioactive isotopes Iodine-131 and Cesium-137 produced by nuclear fission in reactors and compare it with data taken at the troubled Fukushima Dai-ichi nuclear power plant. The ratio of radioactivities of these two isotopes can be used to obtain information about when the nuclear reactions terminated.

# Deciphering the measured ratios of Iodine-131 to Cesium-137 at the Fukushima reactors

## Key claim

The ratio of I-131 to Cs-137 radioactivities measured in water samples near the Fukushima Dai-ichi reactors can be used to estimate when nuclear fission terminated, but the data show anomalously high ratios in the Unit-4 cooling pool and Unit-2 sub-drain that may indicate chain nuclear reactions reignited after the earthquake, provided chemical and transport effects do not account for the discrepancies.

## Method

The author derived a time-dependent formula for the I-131/Cs-137 radioactivity ratio assuming constant fission-product production during reactor operation followed by exponential decay after shutdown, using cumulative thermal fission yields of U-235 from the IAEA database. The theoretical ratio was compared with TEPCO measurements from sea water at the Southern Discharge Channel, Unit-4 cooling pool water, sub-drain water near each reactor building, Intake Canal water, and water inside and outside silt fences at bar screens, for the first 100 days after March 11, 2011.

## Key numbers

- Cumulative thermal fission yields for U-235: f_I = 2.88 × 10⁻² for I-131, f_Cs = 6.22 × 10⁻² for Cs-137
- Half-lives: τ_I = 8 days for I-131, τ_Cs = 30 years for Cs-137
- Theoretical ratio at earthquake time t_X: R_I/Cs(t_X) = 240 / Δt, where Δt is reactor operating time in months; for Δt = 7 months, R = 34; for Δt = 12 months, R = 20
- By April 12 (32 days after X-day), the ratio is reduced by factor 2^(32/8) = 16, giving R = 2.1 (for 7-month operation) or 1.3 (for 12-month operation)
- Unit-4 cooling pool water on April 12: 220 Bq/cc of I-131 and 93 Bq/cc of Cs-137, giving ratio 2.4
- Upper bound for freshly produced fission products (if fission ended on X-day): R_max = 630 × (1/2)^((t - t_f)/τ_I)
- Unit-4 cooling pool total I-131 activity estimated as 3.3 × 10¹¹ Bq on April 13; if from reignited fission, total fissions N_f = 1.3 × 10²⁰, producing extra heat ΔU = 4.1 × 10⁹ J
- Air sample inside Unit-2 reactor building 99 days after X-day: I-131/Cs-137 ratio of 0.51

## Context

This work extends the concept of radiometric dating (analogous to carbon-14 dating using C-14/C-12 ratios) to the iodine-cesium ratio in fission products, a method the author calls "iodine-cesium dating." The analysis directly compares theoretical predictions with TEPCO monitoring data from the Fukushima Dai-ichi accident, revealing anomalies in the Unit-4 cooling pool and Unit-2 sub-drain that suggest possible reignition of chain reactions after the earthquake and tsunami. The author notes that chemical differences between iodine and cesium (e.g., solubility in fresh versus sea water, behavior in aerosols) could affect the measured ratios, and suggests that using Cs-134 and Cs-137 instead could avoid such chemistry issues, though Cs-134's 2-year half-life is too long for the available data. The work enables future investigations into whether criticality was re-established in melted fuel, and calls for more direct information about reactor interior conditions to resolve the open questions.
