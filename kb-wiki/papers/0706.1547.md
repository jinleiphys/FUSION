# Two-loop Corrections for Nuclear Matter in a Covariant Effective Field Theory

> Although one-loop calculations provide a realistic description of bulk and single-particle nuclear properties, it is necessary to examine loop corrections to develop a systematic finite-density power-counting scheme for the nuclear many-body problem when loops are included. Moreover, it is imperative to study exchange and correlation corrections systematically to make reliable predictions for other nuclear observables. One must also verify that the natural sizes of the one-loop parameters are not destroyed by explicit inclusion of many-body corrections. The loop expansion is applied to a chiral effective hadronic lagrangian; with the techniques of Infrared Regularization, it is possible to separate out the short-range contributions and to write them as local products of fields that are already present in our lagrangian. (The appropriate field variables must be re-defined at each order in loops.) The corresponding parameters implicitly include short-range effects to all orders in the interaction, so these effects need not be calculated explicitly. The remaining (long-range) contributions that must be calculated are nonlocal and resemble those in conventional nuclear-structure calculations. Calculations at the two-loop level are carried out to illustrate these techniques at finite densities and to verify that the coupling parameters remain natural when fitted to the empirical properties of equilibrium nuclear matter.

## Key claim

Although one-loop calculations already provide a realistic description of bulk and single-particle nuclear properties, explicit two-loop corrections must be examined to develop a systematic finite-density power-counting scheme and to verify that the natural sizes of the one-loop parameters are not destroyed by inclusion of many-body corrections; the short-range two-loop contributions can be absorbed into local terms already present in the effective lagrangian, leaving only three finite long-range integrals that must be calculated explicitly.

## Method

The authors apply the loop expansion to a chiral effective hadronic lagrangian containing scalar, vector, and pion mesons coupled to nucleons, using Infrared Regularization techniques to separate short-range from long-range contributions. The nucleon propagator is split into Feynman (vacuum) and Density (medium) parts, and the two-loop energy density integrals are decomposed into exchange, Lamb-shift, and vacuum-fluctuation contributions; the short-range Lamb-shift and vacuum-fluctuation terms are expressed as local products of fields already present in the lagrangian and absorbed into redefined coefficients, while only the finite exchange integrals for scalar, vector, and pion exchange are explicitly evaluated. The resulting two-loop energy is then fitted to the empirical equilibrium point of nuclear matter to check whether the coupling parameters remain natural.

## Key numbers

- The one-loop vacuum-fluctuation contribution to the energy density is expanded as a series in \(g_S\phi_0/M\): \(\Delta{\cal E}_{VF}(M^*) = \frac{M^4}{4\pi^2}\left\{ \frac{(g_S\phi_0)^5}{5M^5} + \frac{(g_S\phi_0)^6}{30M^6} + \frac{(g_S\phi_0)^7}{105M^7} + \cdots \right\}\), showing that this term is unnaturally large if naturalness is assumed.
- The two-loop calculation involves three finite integrals representing contributions from scalar meson, vector meson, and pion exchange, each with two factors of the nucleon propagator and one meson propagator.
- The effective nucleon mass is defined as \(M^* \equiv M - g_S\phi_0\), and the energy density at one-loop level includes the term \(\frac{\gamma}{(2\pi)^3}\int d^3k E^*(k)\theta(k_F-|\vec{k}|)\) with \(E^*(k) \equiv (\vec{k}^2 + {M^*}^2)^{1/2}\) and spin-isospin degeneracy \(\gamma\).

## Context

This work extends the Quantum Hadrodynamics (QHD) framework originally proposed by Walecka (1974) and developed as an effective field theory by Furnstahl, Serot, and others (1997, 1999, 2003, 2004). It addresses the problem encountered in earlier loop-expansion studies by Furnstahl (1989) and Tang (1995), where explicit calculation of short-range dynamics produced enormous contributions that rendered the loop expansion useless. The techniques demonstrated here enable systematic inclusion of many-body corrections while preserving naturalness, and the authors note that consideration of three-loop and higher contributions is left for future investigations (McIntire, 2007).
