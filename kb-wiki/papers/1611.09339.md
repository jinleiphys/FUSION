# The effect of ionic correlations on radiative properties in the solar interior and terrestrial experiments

> Intending to solve the decade old problem of solar opacity, we report substantial photoabsorption uncertainty due to the effect of ion-ion correlations. By performing detailed opacity calculations of the solar mixture, we find that taking into account the ionic structure changes the Rosseland opacity near the convection zone by about 10%. We also report about 15% difference in the Rosseland opacity for iron, which was recently measured at the Sandia Z facility, where the temperature reached that prevailing in the convection zone boundary while the density is 2.5 times lower. Finally, we propose a method to measure opacities at solar temperatures and densities that were never reached in the past via laboratory radiation flow experiments, by using plastic foams doped with permilles of dominant photon absorbers in the Sun. The method is advantageous for an experimental study of solar opacities that may lead to a resolution of the solar problem.

## Key claim

The authors report that including ion-ion correlations in opacity calculations changes the Rosseland opacity of the solar mixture near the convection zone boundary by about 10%, and for iron at Sandia Z-facility conditions by about 15%, but they note that both the Ion-Sphere and Ion-Correlation models contain uncontrolled approximations, so the difference between them should be considered a measure of uncertainty rather than a definitive correction.

## Method

The authors used the STAR opacity code to calculate bound-bound and bound-free atomic transitions in the Super-Transition-Array framework with fully relativistic Dirac equation treatment, comparing two plasma models: the standard Ion-Sphere model, where each ion is enclosed in a Wigner-Seitz sphere with a step-function ionic distribution, and the Ion-Correlation model of Rozsnyai, which includes a more realistic ionic structure solved self-consistently with the electronic structure. The IC calculations were performed in the Debye-Huckel approximation, which results from the more accurate HNC approximation in the limit of low plasma coupling. Opacity calculations were performed for a 24-element solar mixture along a thermodynamic path from a solar model calibrated with the AGSS09 abundances.

## Key numbers

* Rosseland opacity of the solar mixture near the convection zone boundary changes by approximately 10% when including ion-ion correlations
* Rosseland opacity for iron at Sandia Z-facility conditions (temperature T=182 eV, free electron density n_e=3.1x10^22 cm^-3) shows approximately 15% difference between Ion-Sphere and Ion-Correlation models
* The Sandia Z experiment reached temperatures matching the convection zone boundary (T=180 eV) but at electron densities 2.5 times lower than solar conditions
* For doped plastic foams at R/R_sun=0.72 (T=180 eV, n_e=8x10^22 cm^-3), the ion-correlation effect on Rosseland opacity ranges from 6.8% (CH foam) to 10.2% (CH4 foam)
* For doped plastic foams at R/R_sun=0.58 (T=280 eV, n_e=3.1x10^23 cm^-3), the ion-correlation effect ranges from 4.8% (CH foam) to 7.2% (CH4 foam)
* The National Ignition Facility can achieve drive temperatures up to approximately 300 eV, covering solar conditions from the CZB (R/R_sun=0.72) down to R/R_sun approximately 0.55
* Plastic foam densities required for solar interior conditions range from 0.3 to 1.3 g/cm^3, with maximal available density approximately 1 g/cm^3

## Context

This work extends the earlier Ion-Correlation model calculations by Rozsnyai (1991, 1992) to the modern solar opacity problem, which arose after Asplund (2009) revised solar metallic abundances downward, creating a discrepancy with helioseismic measurements. The authors show that ion-correlation effects produce opacity variations that move in the direction needed to resolve the solar composition problem, complementing their earlier work on line-broadening uncertainties (Krief 2016). The paper proposes a new experimental approach using radiation flow experiments at facilities like NIF, which is complementary to the monochromatic iron opacity measurements at Sandia Z by Bailey (2015) and could access solar plasma conditions never before achieved in the laboratory.
