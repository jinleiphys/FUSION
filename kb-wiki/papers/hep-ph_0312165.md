# Screened potential and the baryon spectrum

> We show that in a quark model scheme the use of a screened potential, suggested by lattice QCD, instead of an infinitely rising one with the interquark distance, provides a more adequate description of the high-energy baryon spectrum. In particular an almost perfect parallelism between the predicted and observed number of states comes out throwing new light about the so-called missing resonance problem.

# Screened potential and the baryon spectrum

## Key claim

The authors show that replacing the infinitely rising linear confining potential with a screened potential (which saturates at large interquark distances) in a nonrelativistic quark model yields a one-to-one correspondence between predicted three-quark bound states and experimentally observed baryon resonances up to 2500 MeV, thereby resolving the missing resonance problem, provided that states above the one-quark ionization thresholds are interpreted as metastable states requiring a hadronization mechanism.

## Method

The authors solve the three-quark Schrödinger equation for nonstrange baryons using both the hyperspherical harmonic expansion method (up to K=24) and the Faddeev method (including configurations up to l=5 and λ=5), employing a screened Bhaduri-type potential that multiplies the linear confinement, Coulomb, and spin-spin terms by a Yukawa screening factor (1−e^{−μr})/(μr). The OGE parameters (Coulomb strength, spin-spin strength and range) are fixed from the low-lying spectrum, while the screened linear potential parameters (strength and screening parameter μ_b) are determined by fitting the highest well-established four-star resonances: N(7/2−) at 2190 MeV, N(9/2+) at 2220 MeV, N(9/2−) at 2250 MeV, and Δ(11/2+) at 2420 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Screening parameter μ_b = 1.05 fm^{−1}
- Linear potential strength parameter 1/(2ā²) = 1/(2 × 0.0184²) ≈ 1477 MeV fm^{−1} (compared to 470.5 MeV fm^{−1} in the nonscreened case)
- Coulomb strength reduced from κ = 102.67 MeV fm to κ̄ = 10.0 MeV fm (approximately one tenth of the nonscreened value)
- Spin-spin strength increased from κ_σ = 102.67 MeV fm to κ̄_σ = 120.0 MeV fm
- One-quark ionization thresholds: 1007 MeV for (l=0, s=0, t=0), 1156 MeV for (0,1,1), 1388 MeV for (1,0,1), and 1402 MeV for (1,1,0)
- No three-quark bound states exist for l + λ > 4; for l ≥ 5 no bound states can be accommodated
- Differences between hyperspherical harmonic and Faddeev results are at most 5 MeV
- Except for N(1440) and Δ(1910), results differ at most 100 MeV from data for three- and four-star resonances

## Context

This work extends earlier studies using screened potentials for the nonstrange baryon spectrum by Zhang et al. (1993) and for heavy mesons by González et al. (2003), improving upon the variational method with Gaussian trial wave functions that was restricted to J ≤ 7/2 and lacked threshold stability analysis. The paper directly addresses the missing resonance problem identified by Koniuk and Isgur (1980) and Capstick and Roberts (1993), where quark models with linear confinement predict many more baryon states above 1 GeV excitation energy than experimentally observed. The screened potential approach offers an alternative to the explanation that missing states are too inelastic to be detected, and instead suggests that the three-quark bound state spectrum is finite, with higher-energy states transitioning into the continuum via hadronization. The results enable future experimental efforts by the CLAS collaboration at TJNAF and the BES collaboration to test predictions in the 2500–3000 MeV region, where the model may indicate metastable states or the presence of exotic non-three-quark configurations.
