# Is it possible to reconstruct the freeze-out duration of heavy-ion collisions using tomography?

> We investigate what conditions allow us to extract the relative distribution of freeze-out space and time points in an arbitrary reference frame using tomography and source imaging. The source function may be extracted from the two-particle correlation function measured in heavy-ion collisions using imaging techniques. This imaged source function is related to the relative distribution of freeze-out space and time points through a generalization of the Radon transform found in tomography. Using tomography, the imaged source function may be converted into the relative freeze-out distribution in the frame of interest. We describe how the tomography may be performed in practice.

## Key claim
It is possible to reconstruct the relative distribution of freeze-out space and time points from two-particle correlation functions using tomography, but only if the "momentum averaging approximation" holds, meaning the probability of emitting a pair with a given relative separation is independent of the total pair momentum, which can be checked experimentally by verifying that the sideward radius parameter does not depend on total pair momentum.

## Method
The author shows that the imaged source function \( S_{\vec{P}}(\vec{r}') \), extracted model-independently from two-particle correlation functions via imaging techniques, is a generalized Radon transform (line integral along a boosted direction) of the relative distribution of space-time emission points \( \tilde{\cal S}(r) \) in the lab frame. An explicit inversion formula is given, and two practical inversion methods are described: filtered back-projection and Algebraic Reconstructive Tomography (ART) using Basis spline expansions.

## Key numbers
- The sideward Gaussian radius parameter \( R_S = R \) is independent of the boost velocity \( \vec{\beta} \) in the Gaussian model, providing an experimental check of the momentum averaging approximation.
- The outward and longitudinal radius parameters become \( R_{O/L} = R / \sqrt{1 - \beta^2_{O/L}(R^2 + \tau^2)/(R^2 + \beta^2 \tau^2)} \), showing anomalously large values for large emission durations \( \tau \).
- A long/out cross term \( R^2_{OL} = -R^2 \beta_L \beta_O \sqrt{(R^2 + \tau^2)/(R^2 + \beta^2 \tau^2)} \) appears in the Gaussian model.

## Context
This work extends the model-independent source imaging techniques developed by Brown and collaborators (1997, 1998) from extracting the source function \( S(\vec{r}) \) in the pair center-of-mass frame to reconstructing the full space-time distribution of emission points in the lab frame. It addresses the ambiguity that the imaged source function folds time dependence into a line integral along the boost direction. The approach enables direct measurement of the freeze-out duration of heavy-ion collisions, which is relevant for detecting the long-lived quark-gluon plasma predicted at RHIC (Rischke 1996, Bass 1999). The momentum averaging approximation is checked against NA49 data at CERN-SPS, where the sideward pion radius parameter is relatively flat as a function of pair rapidity and transverse momentum.
