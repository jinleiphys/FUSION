# Hard probes of short-range nucleon-nucleon correlations

> One of the primary goals of nuclear physics is providing a complete description of the structure of atomic nuclei. While mean-field calculations provide detailed information on the nuclear shell structure for a wide range of nuclei, they do not capture the complete structure of nuclei, in particular the impact of small, dense structures in nuclei. The strong, short-range component of the nucleon-nucleon potential yields hard interactions between nucleons which are close together, generating a high-momentum tail to the nucleon momentum distribution, with momenta well in excess of the Fermi momentum. This high-momentum component of the nuclear wave-function is one of the most poorly understood parts of nuclear structure. Utilizing high-energy probes, we can isolate scattering from high-momentum nucleons, and use these measurements to examine the structure and impact of short-range nucleon-nucleon correlations. Over the last decade we have moved from looking for evidence of such short-range structures to mapping out their strength in nuclei and examining their isospin structure. This has been made possible by high-luminosity and high-energy accelerators, coupled with an improved understanding of the reaction mechanism issues involved in studying these structures. We review the general issues related to short-range correlations, survey recent experiments aimed at probing these short-range structures, and lay out future possibilities to further these studies.

## Key claim

High-energy scattering experiments can isolate short-range nucleon-nucleon correlations (SRCs) from mean-field nuclear structure by selecting kinematics where the reconstructed initial nucleon momentum exceeds the Fermi momentum, but the interpretation relies on the Plane-Wave Impulse Approximation and the assumption that the high-momentum tail of the momentum distribution is universal across nuclei and dominated by deuteron-like iso-singlet pairs for momenta between 300 and 600 MeV/c.

## Method

The authors review inclusive A(e,e')X, semi-inclusive A(e,e'N)X, and triple-coincidence A(e,e'NN)X electroproduction reactions at momentum transfers Q^2 above 1-2 GeV^2 and Bjorken x > 1, where quasi-elastic scattering from high-momentum nucleons dominates and meson exchange currents and intermediate-state resonance contributions are suppressed. They use the light-cone momentum fraction alpha_i (or Bjorken x as a proxy) to separate two-nucleon SRCs (1 < alpha_i < 2) from three-nucleon SRCs (2 < alpha_i < 3), and rely on the eikonal regime to argue that final-state interactions preserve the key kinematical correlations between struck and spectator nucleons.

## Key numbers

- Spectroscopic factors from proton knock-out experiments are typically 30-40% below shell-model expectations across many nuclei and several nuclear shells.
- The tensor-dominated regime for 2N SRCs is identified for nucleon momenta 300 < k < 600 MeV/c, where the n/p ratio in 3He approaches unity.
- The repulsive core of the NN potential is expected to dominate for k > k_max ~ 800 MeV/c.
- Meson exchange current contributions are suppressed for Q^2 > 1 GeV^2 by an additional factor of Q^{-4} relative to direct SRC production.
- Delta-isobar contributions remain sizable for Q^2 <= 4 GeV^2 and are suppressed by working at x > 1 (low energy transfer).
- The minimum kinematic requirements for SRC studies are an energy transfer of at least 0.5 GeV and Q^2 of 1-2 GeV^2 or higher.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on short-range correlations seen in valence knock-out experiments (Kelly 1996, Lapikas 1993) and continuum A(e,e'p) reactions (Marchand 1987, LeGoff 1994), where missing strength relative to mean-field predictions and peaks in missing momentum spectra indicated SRC effects but did not cleanly isolate them. The paper surveys high-energy experiments from the last decade that provide more direct probes of SRCs, building on theoretical frameworks including the singularity theorem (Frankfurt 1981, Frankfurt 2008) and Green's function Monte Carlo calculations (Forest 1996, Schiavilla 2006, Wiringa 2008). It enables future systematic studies of SRCs across light and heavy nuclei, including separation of two- and three-nucleon correlations, studies of isospin structure, and investigations of relativistic and bound-nucleon effects.
