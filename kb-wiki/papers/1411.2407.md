# Comment on "Three-dimensional hydrodynamic simulations of the combustion of a neutron star into a quark star"

> If strange matter is absolutely stable, the ordinary nuclei decay to strangelets, while neutron stars convert into strange stars. Lifetimes of the ordinary nuclei are constrained experimentally to be above $\sim 10^{33}$ years, while lifetimes of the metastable neutron stars depend on the neutron star masses and can exceed the age of the Universe. As a consequence, the neutron stars and the strange stars can coexist in the Universe. We point out that numerical simulations of the conversion of neutron stars to strange stars, performed by M. Herzog and F. K. Roepke in Phys. Rev. D 84, 083002 (2011) [arXiv:1109.0539], are focused on a region in the parameter space of strange matter, in which low-mass neutron stars and strange stars are coexistent, whereas massive neutron stars are unstable and short lived on an astronomical timescale.

# Comment on "Three-dimensional hydrodynamic simulations of the combustion of a neutron star into a quark star"

## Key claim

The numerical simulations of neutron star to strange star conversion by Herzog and Roepke (2011) explore a region of strange matter parameter space where low-mass neutron stars and strange stars can coexist, but this region is largely excluded by the requirement that massive neutron stars (around 2.0 solar masses) must also be stable and long-lived. Most of the simulated scenarios therefore correspond to a universe without the massive neutron stars that are actually observed.

## Method

The authors re-examine the constraints on strange quark matter (SQM) parameters from the MIT bag model, specifically the bag constant B and the binding energy per baryon, using two independent conditions: the stability of ordinary nuclei against decay to strangelets (which gives B^(1/4) > 145 MeV) and the stability of neutron stars against conversion to strange stars (which gives B^(1/4) > 155 MeV for a 1.4 solar mass protoneutron star). They then apply these constraints to the parameter space explored in the hydrodynamic simulations of Herzog and Roepke (2011), identifying which parts of that parameter space are ruled out by the existence of observed massive neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- B^(1/4) > 145 MeV from the requirement that ordinary nuclei are stable against decay to strangelets (for alpha_c = 0, m_s = 100 MeV)
- B^(1/4) > 155 MeV from the requirement that a 1.4 solar mass protoneutron star does not undergo a phase transition with conservation of strangeness (for alpha_c = 0)
- The crossover temperature for two-flavor QCD from lattice data is T_c(n_f = 2) = 175 +/- 10 MeV, which exceeds the upper limit of 122 +/- 7 MeV required for absolutely stable SQM
- Pulsars with masses around 2.0 solar masses have been reported, ruling out three of six examined equations of state for nuclear matter
- For the Baron-Cooperstein-Kahana equation of state, the allowed binding energy of SQM is less than 30 MeV (for alpha_c = 0, m_s = 100 MeV)

## Context

This comment extends the constraints on absolutely stable strange quark matter originally derived by Farhi and Jaffe (1984) from nuclear stability, and by Krivoruchenko and collaborators (1987, 1991a, 1991b) from protoneutron star stability. It directly contradicts the parameter space explored in the hydrodynamic simulations of Herzog and Roepke (2011), showing that most of their simulated scenarios are incompatible with the existence of observed massive neutron stars. The work enables future searches for strange stars through long-duration soft neutrino bursts from neutron star conversion, observation of two pulsars with equal masses but different radii, or detection of sub-millisecond rotation periods (below 0.5 milliseconds) that would indicate low-mass strange stars bound by the strong force rather than gravity.
