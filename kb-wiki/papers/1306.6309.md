# Isospin dependence of nucleon Correlations in ground state nuclei

> The dispersive optical model (DOM) as presently implemented can investigate the isospin (nucleon asymmetry) dependence of the Hartree-Fock-like potential relevant for nucleons near the Fermi energy. Data constraints indicate that a Lane-type potential adequately describes its asymmetry dependence. Correlations beyond the mean-field can also be described in this framework, but this requires an extension that treats the non-locality of the Hartree-Fock-like potential properly. The DOM has therefore been extended to properly describe ground-state properties of nuclei as a function of nucleon asymmetry in addition to standard ingredients like elastic nucleon scattering data and level structure. Predictions of nucleon correlations at larger nucleon asymmetries can then be made after data at smaller asymmetries constrain the potentials that represent the nucleon self-energy. A simple extrapolation for Sn isotopes generates predictions for increasing correlations of minority protons with increasing neutron number. Such predictions can be investigated by performing experiments with exotic beams. The predicted neutron properties for the double closed-shell 132Sn nucleus exhibit similar correlations as those in 208Pb. Future relevance of these studies for understanding the properties of all nucleons, including those with high momentum, and the role of three-body forces in nuclei are briefly discussed. Such an implementation will require a proper treatment of the non-locality of the imaginary part of the potentials and a description of high-momentum nucleons as experimentally constrained by the (e,e'p) reactions performed at Jefferson Lab.

## Key claim

The dispersive optical model (DOM) can be extended to describe ground-state properties of nuclei as a function of nucleon asymmetry, and a simple extrapolation for Sn isotopes generates predictions for increasing correlations of minority protons with increasing neutron number, but these predictions depend on the assumption that a Lane-type potential adequately describes the asymmetry dependence of the Hartree-Fock-like potential.

## Method

The authors extended the dispersive optical model (DOM) to include a non-local Hartree-Fock-like potential and renormalized imaginary potentials, allowing the DOM potential to represent the nucleon self-energy. They fitted DOM parameters to elastic nucleon scattering angular distributions, analyzing powers, reaction and total cross sections, single-particle energies, and spectroscopic factors from (e,e'p) data for Ca, Ni, Fe, Cr, Zr, Mo, Sn, and Pb isotopes. The Dyson equation was then solved to obtain single-particle propagators and spectral functions, from which spectroscopic factors and occupation numbers were extracted.

## Key numbers

- The potential part of the symmetry energy was found to be V1 = 18.4 MeV, compared to 13.1 MeV from Varner et al. (1991)
- The total symmetry energy obtained was 30-31 MeV, comparable to 30.6 MeV from Seeger's mass formula and 36.8 MeV from the droplet model
- The isoscalar effective mass in the center of the nucleus increases from 0.49 m in Ca nuclei to 0.62 m in Pb nuclei
- The difference in proton and neutron effective masses in the center of 208Pb was 0.054 m
- Predicted spectroscopic factors for the proton 0g9/2 hole state decrease by approximately 30% from 102Sn to 130Sn
- The f7/2 neutron spectroscopic factor deduced from 132Sn(d,p) transfer data was 0.72, compared to 0.77 from the DOM potential
- DOM extrapolations for 36Ca generated spectroscopic factors of 0.70 for the proton d3/2 orbit and 0.65 for the neutron s1/2 orbit

## Context

This work extends the dispersive optical model approach of Mahaux and Sartor (1991) by incorporating non-local Hartree-Fock-like potentials, enabling proper calculation of spectral functions and occupation numbers without approximations. The results enable predictions for nuclei further from stability, such as Sn isotopes approaching the neutron drip line, and provide testable predictions for experiments at rare isotope facilities. The paper also identifies discrepancies with heavy-ion knockout reactions (Gade 2004, 2008) and transfer reactions (Lee 2010, Flavigny 2013) regarding the asymmetry dependence of correlations, and points to needed future developments including non-local imaginary potentials and abandonment of symmetric surface absorption assumptions.
