# Connecting spatial moments and momentum densities

> The precision of experimental data and analysis techniques is a key feature of any discovery attempt. A striking example is the proton radius puzzle where the accuracy of the spectroscopy of muonic atoms challenges traditional electron scattering measurements. The present work proposes a novel method for the determination of spatial moments from densities expressed in the momentum space. This method provides a direct access to even, odd, and more generally any real, negative and positive moment with order larger than $-3$. As an illustration, the application of this method to the electric form factor of the proton is discussed in detail.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a novel integral method for determining spatial moments from momentum-space densities that can access even, odd, and any real positive or negative moments of order greater than -3, overcoming the limitation of the standard derivative method which only accesses even moments. The method is presented as potentially more accurate than the derivative technique because integral forms are expected to be less dependent on point-to-point systematics, though the authors acknowledge that the practical constraint is obtaining an appropriate description of data over a large momentum-transfer domain.

## Method

The method expresses spatial moments as integrals of the Fourier transform (form factor) in momentum space, regularized either by subtracting counterterms from a MacLaurin expansion (principal value regularization, IM1) or by introducing an exponential convergence factor (exponential regularization, IM2). The authors test the method on the dipole parameterization and Kelly's polynomial ratio parameterization of the proton electric form factor, comparing the numerical results from both regularization schemes against configuration-space integrals. They then study the convergence of truncated moments as a function of a momentum cutoff Q to determine the saturation momentum needed for experimental application.

## Key numbers

- The proton charge radius from electron scattering is 0.879(8) fm (Bernauer 2010) and from muonic hydrogen spectroscopy is 0.84184(67) fm (Pohl 2010), a difference that implies "unbearable constraints on the systematics of lepton scattering experiments"
- The exponential regularization (IM2) saturates at about 6 fm^-1 for odd moments, a momentum region "well covered by proton electromagnetic form factors data"
- At a maximum squared momentum transfer of 2 GeV^2, the IM2 prescription permits determination of any positive moments, while the IM1 prescription is "of very limited success" even when considering data up to ~10 GeV^2
- The Kelly parameterization predicts a zero crossing of G_E(k^2) at k^2_0 = 14.7 GeV^2
- For the dipole parameterization, the first odd moment coefficients are v_1(0^+) = 3/(2Λ) and v_3(0^+) = 5/(2Λ^3) with Λ^2 = 16.1 fm^-2

## Context

This work addresses the proton radius puzzle, where the discrepancy between electron scattering measurements (Bernauer 2010) and muonic spectroscopy (Pohl 2010) has motivated scrutiny of the standard derivative method for extracting the charge radius from form factor data. The authors note that improving the derivative method to competitive precision "does not appear reachable with current knowledge and technologies" (Hoballah 2019). The integral method extends prior mathematical work by Günter (1962) and Schwartz (1965) on distribution theory, and the authors demonstrate its application to the Kelly parameterization (Kelly 2004) of the proton electric form factor. The method enables access to odd and negative moments, which probe the high-momentum behavior of the form factor and the central part of the corresponding density, potentially allowing discrimination between different form factor models.
