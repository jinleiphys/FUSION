# Quantum thermodynamics of nonequilibrium processes in lattice gauge theories

> A key objective in nuclear and high-energy physics is to describe nonequilibrium dynamics of matter, e.g., in the early universe and in particle colliders, starting from the Standard Model. Classical-computing methods, via the framework of lattice gauge theory, have experienced limited success in this mission. Quantum simulation of lattice gauge theories holds promise for overcoming computational limitations. Because of local constraints (Gauss's laws), lattice gauge theories have an intricate Hilbert-space structure. This structure complicates the definition of thermodynamic properties of systems coupled to reservoirs during equilibrium and nonequilibrium processes. We show how to define thermodynamic quantities such as work and heat using strong-coupling thermodynamics, a framework that has recently burgeoned within the field of quantum thermodynamics. Our definitions suit instantaneous quenches, simple nonequilibrium processes undertaken in quantum simulators. To illustrate our framework, we compute the work and heat exchanged during a quench in a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ lattice gauge theory coupled to matter in 1+1 dimensions. The thermodynamic quantities, as functions of the quench parameter, evidence a phase transition. For general thermal states, we derive a simple relation between a quantum many-body system's entanglement Hamiltonian, measurable with quantum-information-processing tools, and the Hamiltonian of mean force, used to define strong-coupling thermodynamic quantities.

## Key claim

Strong-coupling quantum thermodynamics, rather than weak-coupling thermodynamics, is necessary to define thermodynamic quantities such as work and heat in lattice gauge theories because the local constraints (Gauss's laws) make the system-reservoir interaction energy non-negligible; the authors show that these definitions can be applied to instantaneous quenches and that the resulting quantities can signal a phase transition.

## Method

The authors partition a lattice gauge theory into a system (S) and a reservoir (R), decomposing the total Hamiltonian into system, reservoir, and interaction terms. They define the system's internal energy using the Hamiltonian of mean force, and define work and heat for instantaneous quenches using only system degrees of freedom. They compute these quantities for a 1+1-dimensional Z2 lattice gauge theory coupled to hardcore bosonic matter, quenching the chemical potential from zero to positive values, and compare the thermodynamic signatures to the chiral condensate order parameter.

## Key numbers

- The ratio |⟨V_{S∪R}⟩/⟨H_S⟩| is non-negligible (≥ 0.1) in the initial and final states at all μf values used, justifying the strong-coupling approach.
- The quench protocol uses N=6 lattice sites, with system size N_S=4, hopping strength J=1/2, electric-field strength ε=1/2, matter mass m=1/2, initial chemical potential μ_i=0, and inverse temperature β=10.
- The dissipated work W_diss remains near zero until around the μ_f value where the apparent phase transition occurs, then increases, clearly indicating the transition.
- The chiral condensate Σ suddenly changes from finite to vanishing values as μ_f increases, with the critical value μ_f^c defined where dΣ/dμ_f is maximized.

## Context

This work extends the framework of strong-coupling quantum thermodynamics, developed by Miller (2018), Rivas (2020), and Oruganti (2024), to lattice gauge theories, which have previously been studied thermodynamically only via Euclidean path-integral Monte Carlo methods (Bazavov 2014, Ratti 2018). The results enable quantum simulation experiments to measure thermodynamic quantities in gauge theories using entanglement Hamiltonian tomography, as demonstrated by Kokail (2021) and Mueller (2023), and open the door to studying phase transitions, including topological and dynamical ones, in non-Abelian and higher-dimensional gauge theories.
