# Electrical conductivity of the quark-gluon plasma: perspective from lattice QCD

> A discussion on the electrical conductivity of the quark-gluon plasma as determined by lattice QCD is given. After a reminder of basic definitions and expectations, various methods for spectral reconstruction are reviewed, including the use of Ansätze and sum rules, the Maximum Entropy and Backus-Gilbert methods, and Tikhonov regularisation. A comprehensive overview of lattice QCD results obtained so far is given, including a comparison of the different lattice formulations. A noticeable consistency for the conductivities obtained is seen, in spite of the differences in the lattice setups and spectral reconstruction methods. It is found that in the case of quenched QCD little temperature dependence of $σ/T$ is seen in the temperature range investigated, while for QCD with dynamical quarks a reduction of $σ/T$ in the vicinity of the thermal crossover is observed, compared to its value in the QGP. Several open questions are posed at the end.

# Electrical conductivity of the quark-gluon plasma: perspective from lattice QCD

## Key claim

The paper reviews lattice QCD determinations of the electrical conductivity of the quark-gluon plasma and finds a noticeable consistency across different lattice formulations and spectral reconstruction methods, despite the substantial differences in setups. However, the authors note that in quenched QCD little temperature dependence of sigma/T is seen in the temperature range investigated, while for QCD with dynamical quarks a reduction of sigma/T in the vicinity of the thermal crossover is observed compared to its value in the QGP, and several open questions remain.

## Method

The paper reviews the Kubo formula relating the electrical conductivity to the slope of the current-current spectral function at zero energy and momentum, and the inversion problem of extracting this spectral function from Euclidean lattice correlators computed at a finite number of temporal points. Various spectral reconstruction methods are surveyed, including Ansatze combining transport peaks with perturbative continuum contributions, the Maximum Entropy Method (MEM), the Backus-Gilbert method, and Tikhonov regularisation. The review compares results from approximately 10 lattice QCD papers spanning simulations with N_f=0 (quenched), N_f=2, and N_f=2+1 dynamical quark flavours, using both isotropic and anisotropic lattices, with quarks ranging from heavier-than-physical to physical masses.

## Key numbers

- For free massless quarks at leading order, the Euclidean correlator at the midpoint is G_ii(tau=1/2T) = N_c T^3, with N_c=3, arising from equal contributions from the transport and continuum terms.
- The electromagnetic charge factor for connected contributions is C_em = (5/9)e^2 for u,d quarks and (6/9)e^2 for u,d,s quarks; for disconnected contributions it is (1/9)e^2 for u,d and 0 for u,d,s.
- The number of lattice QCD papers attempting to compute the conductivity on the lattice is approximately 10, spanning from Gupta (2003) to Astrakhantsev (2019).
- The temporal extent N_tau determines the number of available Euclidean time points for spectral reconstruction, with the number of independent points on the order of N_tau/2.

## Context

This review extends and compares the body of lattice QCD conductivity calculations initiated by Gupta (2003) and continued by Aarts (2007), Ding (2010), Francis (2011), Brandt (2012), Amato (2013), Aarts (2014), Brandt (2015), Ding (2016), and Astrakhantsev (2019). The paper situates lattice results alongside complementary approaches including weak-coupling Feynman diagram calculations by Aarts (2002) and Gagnon (2006), kinetic theory by Arnold (2000, 2003), effective models by Cassing (2013) and Marty (2013), and holographic methods at strong coupling by Policastro (2002) and Teaney (2006). The review enables future work by identifying open questions and the need for more robust spectral reconstruction methods, particularly for extracting the transport peak from Euclidean data.
