# Integrating Out, Twice:The Open-System Case That Neural-Network Ensemble Theory Is Missing

> Averaging a neural network over its random parameters and marginalizing a Gaussian sector are the same operation, the Schur complement of the eliminated block, and when that block is closed it returns a covariance and its inverse. That is all a network ensemble produces, the closed case. The open case is missing, and nuclear reaction theory has it worked out. Projecting a scattering problem onto a chosen set of channels, with the rest carrying probability irreversibly to a continuum, leaves a non-Hermitian effective generator that conserves and itemizes exactly what it loses: the nuclear optical model and its generalized optical theorem. I set the two cases side by side using only the moments of a distribution, the algebra of Gaussians, and block inversion, no field theory, and give the closed-case dictionary in full: the neural tangent kernel is the Fisher sensitivity kernel, the infinite-width Gaussian limit is the Gaussian-process emulator, and the lazy-to-feature transition is the validity boundary of a reduced-basis emulator. I then test the open export on a truncated attention map, a token-level transfer operator, and a sparse expert router, and report a mostly negative result. The conserved flux ledger ports wherever openness is genuinely present, but its distinctive content is absent, an artifact of the chosen partition, or pinned near a floor by the training objective, and the operationally useful uncertainty turns out to be epistemic, living in the closed half of the correspondence, not the open one. The negative has a structural reason this note makes precise: the open case needs an eliminated sector with a continuous spectrum and wave-like, not relaxational, dynamics, which mainstream learning's finite or dissipative objects do not supply. This is a note, not a result; its main finding is that negative one, and its value is the map that locates it.

# Integrating Out, Twice: The Open-System Case That Neural-Network Ensemble Theory Is Missing

## Key claim

The paper claims that averaging a neural network over its random parameters and marginalizing a Gaussian sector are algebraically the same operation (the Schur complement of the eliminated block), but that neural-network ensemble theory only realizes the "closed" case where the eliminated block returns a real, symmetric, lossless covariance and its inverse. The "open" case, where probability escapes irreversibly to a continuum and produces a non-Hermitian effective generator with a conserved flux ledger, is missing from machine learning and is fully worked out in nuclear reaction theory through the optical model and Feshbach projection; the paper tests whether this open structure can be exported to learning objects and reports a mostly negative result, with the structural reason being that the open case requires an eliminated sector with a continuous spectrum and wave-like dynamics that mainstream learning's finite or dissipative objects do not supply.

## Method

The author sets the closed (network ensemble) and open (Feshbach projection) cases side by side using only the moments of a probability distribution, the algebra of Gaussians, and block matrix inversion, explicitly avoiding field-theoretic methods. The closed-case dictionary is given in full: the neural tangent kernel equals the Fisher sensitivity kernel, the infinite-width Gaussian limit equals the Gaussian-process emulator, and the lazy-to-feature transition equals the validity boundary of a reduced-basis emulator. The open-case structure is then tested numerically on three learning objects: a truncated attention map, a token-level transfer operator, and a sparse expert router, with the conserved flux ledger examined for each.

## Key numbers

- The absorption cross section is given by $\sigma_{\rm abs} = -(2/\hbar v) \langle \chi | W | \chi \rangle$, where $W \le 0$ is the anti-Hermitian part of the effective interaction (Section 4.2).
- In continuum-discretized coupled-channels calculations, the absorption cross section decomposes as $\sigma_{\rm abs} = \sigma_D + \sigma_B + \sigma_{\rm int}$ (direct, breakup, and interference parts), and dropping the off-diagonal imaginary couplings biases the separate pieces by tens of percent (Section 4.2).
- The energy-weighted integral of the inelastic cross section satisfies $\int \sigma_I(E) E dE \approx -(2\pi^2\hbar^2/\mu) \text{Tr}[W]$, a sum rule relating total absorptive capacity to the trace of the anti-Hermitian operator (Section 4.3).
- The dispersive optical model gives $\text{Re} \mathcal{U}(E) = \mathcal{U}_{\text{static}} + (1/\pi) \text{P} \int [\text{Im} \mathcal{U}(E')/(E' - E)] dE'$, a dispersion relation coupling the real and imaginary parts of the optical potential through analyticity (Section 4.3).

## Context

This note extends the formal correspondence between statistical elimination in neural-network ensembles and Feshbach projection in nuclear reaction theory, building on the Feshbach projection formalism (Feshbach, 1958, 1962) and the nuclear optical model. The author's own recent work on differentiable emulators (Lei, 2026) and Bayesian uncertainty quantification of optical potentials (Navarro Perez and Lei, 2019) sits at the interface this paper maps. The paper enables a precise structural comparison between the closed-case effective theories that machine learning already uses (Gaussian-process emulators, neural tangent kernels) and the open-case effective theory (the optical potential with its generalized optical theorem) that machine learning lacks, while the numerical tests on attention maps, transfer operators, and expert routers establish that the open-case structure does not naturally arise in current learning architectures.
