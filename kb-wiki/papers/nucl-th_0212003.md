# Giant resonances in the deformed continuum

> Giant resonances in the continuum for deformed nuclei are studied with the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory in real time and real space. The continuum effect is effectively taken into account by introducing a complex Absorbing Boundary Condition (ABC).

## Key claim
The Absorbing Boundary Condition (ABC) method, combined with real-time time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) on a three-dimensional grid, can treat the single-nucleon continuum in deformed nuclei, but the box boundary condition fails for excitation energies above approximately 23 MeV due to reflection artifacts.

## Method
The authors performed real-time TDHF calculations on a 3D coordinate grid using the Skyrme energy functional with the SGII parameter set. An impulsive dipole external field was applied to the Hartree-Fock ground state of deformed $^{24}$Mg, and the time evolution of the dipole moment was computed up to 10 $\hbar$/MeV with an absorbing potential active beyond 10 fm radius. The energy-dependent E1 response was obtained by Fourier transforming the time-dependent dipole moment.

## Key numbers
- The highest occupied single-particle energies in $^{24}$Mg are $-14.4$ MeV for neutrons and $-9.7$ MeV for protons (SGII interaction).
- The Coulomb barrier height for protons is $4\sim 5$ MeV, so the nucleonic continuum becomes effective above $14\sim 15$ MeV excitation energy.
- The box boundary condition calculation provides reliable results only when the strength function is averaged over an energy width of 6 MeV, because reflection artifacts become significant at $t\ge 1$ $\hbar$/MeV.
- The ABC calculation shows that the box boundary condition significantly changes the E1 strength at energies $E\ge 23$ MeV, where damping from fast nucleon emission is important.
- A smoothing parameter of $\Gamma=1$ MeV was used in the Fourier transform to obtain the oscillator strength.

## Context
This work extends the ABC method, previously used in quantum chemistry and for electronic systems in molecules and clusters (Nakatsukasa and Yabana 2001), to nuclear giant resonances in deformed nuclei. It addresses the difficulty of constructing Green's functions in multi-dimensional space for deformed systems, which had limited the applicability of the standard continuum RPA approach (Shibata and Bertsch 1975). The method enables future studies of excited states and responses in nuclei near drip lines (Nakatsukasa and Yabana 2002).
