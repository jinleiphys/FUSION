# AC nuclear Stark effect in H-atom via super-intense laser-atom interaction

> We investigate the nuclear Stark effect induced in hydrogen-like atomic nuclei under super-intense laser fields. Since laser wavelengths are generally larger than nuclear dimensions, direct laser-nucleus interaction is unfeasible. Instead, this effect is induced indirectly through electron oscillations in the laser field, which produce a periodic electric field that shifts the nuclear energy levels. Using perturbation theory, we derive an expression for the energy shift and dynamic polarizability of the nucleus as a function of laser parameters. Our findings reveal that the Nuclear Stark effect can be controlled by adjusting the laser frequency and intensity, potentially enabling applications in nuclear and quantum optical systems.

## Key claim
The paper claims that an AC nuclear Stark shift in hydrogen-like atoms can be induced indirectly via electron oscillations in a super-intense laser field, but this result is derived under the condition that the laser frequency is below 0.499 atomic units and the field strength satisfies a non-relativistic limit, with the electron oscillation amplitude kept smaller than the Bohr radius to avoid ionization.

## Method
The authors use second-order time-dependent perturbation theory to calculate the energy shift of nuclear states in hydrogen, starting from the time-dependent Schrödinger equation. They model the electron's oscillatory motion under a laser field of the form \(\mathcal{E}(t) = \mathcal{E}_0 \cos(\omega t)\hat{z}\), compute the average electric field experienced by the nucleus from the electron's motion, and derive an expression for the dynamic polarizability of the nucleus. The nuclear Stark shift is then evaluated as a function of laser frequency, intensity, and time, subject to constraints that prevent ionization and relativistic effects.

## Key numbers
- The energy splitting between two nuclear states of the H-atom is \(2.94 \times 10^4\) atomic units.
- To avoid ionization, the laser frequency must satisfy \(\omega < 0.499\) atomic units.
- The non-relativistic condition requires \(q = \frac{\mathcal{E}_0}{emc} < 1\).
- In one simulation, the laser electric field amplitude is set to \(\mathcal{E} = 0.1\) atomic units with time \(t = 1\) atomic unit.
- In another simulation, the laser frequency is \(\omega = 0.1\) atomic units and time is \(t = 10^2\) atomic units.
- In a time-dependent simulation, the laser interaction time is varied from 1 femtosecond to 5 femtoseconds, with \(\omega = 0.1\) atomic units and \(\mathcal{E} = 0.01\) atomic units.

## Context
This work extends the concept of the AC Stark shift, previously studied in atomic systems (Author a13), to nuclear energy levels, building on earlier studies of the nuclear Stark effect under static fields (Author a16, Author a17). The indirect mechanism via electron oscillations addresses the limitation that laser wavelengths are too large to directly probe nuclear structures. The results enable future experimental and theoretical investigations into controlling nuclear energy levels with intense laser fields, potentially impacting nuclear and quantum optical systems.
