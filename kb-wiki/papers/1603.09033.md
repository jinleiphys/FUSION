# Phase transition of strongly interacting matter with a chemical potential dependent Polyakov loop potential

> We construct a hadron-quark two-phase model based on the Walecka-quantum hadrodynamics and the improved Polyakov-Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model with an explicit chemical potential dependence of Polyakov-loop potential ($μ$PNJL model). With respect to the original PNJL model, the confined-deconfined phase transition is largely affected at low temperature and large chemical potential. Using the two-phase model, we investigate the equilibrium transition between hadronic and quark matter at finite chemical potentials and temperatures. The numerical results show that the transition boundaries from nuclear to quark matter move towards smaller chemical potential (lower density) when the $μ$-dependent Polyakov loop potential is taken. In particular, for charge asymmetric matter, we compute the local asymmetry of $u, d$ quarks in the hadron-quark coexisting phase, and analyse the isospin-relevant observables possibly measurable in heavy-ion collision (HIC) experiments. In general new HIC data on the location and properties of the mixed phase would bring relevant information on the expected chemical potential dependence of the Polyakov Loop contribution.

## Key claim

The paper claims that including a chemical-potential-dependent Polyakov loop potential (the μPNJL model) shifts the hadron-quark phase transition boundaries toward smaller baryon chemical potentials (lower densities) compared to the original PNJL model, but this shift is conditional on the parameter β, which governs the strength of the back-reaction of the matter sector on the glue sector; for β ≤ 0.4 the two-phase model predicts a transition to confined quark matter, which the authors consider unphysical.

## Method

The authors construct a two-phase model combining a nonlinear Walecka-type quantum hadrodynamics description for nuclear matter with an improved Polyakov–Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (μPNJL) model for quark matter, where the Polyakov-loop potential's transition temperature T₀ is made dependent on quark chemical potential via a renormalization-group-inspired formula with a parameter β. They impose Gibbs conditions (thermal, chemical, and mechanical equilibrium) between the hadronic and quark phases at finite temperature and baryon chemical potential, and enforce global isospin conservation to compute the equilibrium phase boundaries and the local asymmetry of u and d quarks in the mixed phase.

## Key numbers

* T₀ = 270 MeV is the confinement-deconfinement transition temperature in pure Yang-Mills theory at vanishing chemical potential; for two flavors at μ = 0, T₀ is rescaled to 208 MeV.
* The parameter β governs the curvature of T₀(μ); values from 0 to 1 are explored, with β = 0 corresponding to the standard PNJL model (constant T₀ = 208 MeV).
* For the μPNJL model, the quark model parameters are: cutoff Λ = 651 MeV, coupling G = 5.04 GeV⁻², current quark masses m_{u,d} = 5.5 MeV, fitted to f_π = 92.3 MeV and m_π = 139.3 MeV.
* The largest asymmetry parameter for stable nuclei is α = 0.227 (²³⁸U + ²³⁸U collision); calculations use α = 0.2 and explore α up to 0.35.
* A critical value β = 0.89 is identified: for β > 0.89, no equilibrium transition exists for α = 0.2 in the two-phase model.

## Context

This work extends previous two-phase model studies by the same group (Shao et al., 2011–2015) that focused on isospin-asymmetric matter and proposed observable effects on meson yield ratios. It addresses the limitation of the original PNJL model, which produces a "quarkyonic phase" at low temperature and high density where quarks remain confined even after chiral restoration, by incorporating a chemical-potential-dependent Polyakov loop potential following the functional renormalization group approach of Schaefer (2007) and Herbst et al. (2011, 2013). The results enable predictions for the location and properties of the hadron-quark mixed phase that could be tested in upcoming heavy-ion collision experiments at NICA, FAIR, J-PARC, and the RHIC BES II program.
