# Nuclear quantum optics with x-ray laser pulses

> The direct interaction of nuclei with super-intense laser fields is studied. We show that present and upcoming high-frequency laser facilities, especially together with a moderate acceleration of the target nuclei, do allow for resonant laser-nucleus interaction. These direct interactions may be utilized for the optical measurement of nuclear properties such as the transition frequency and the dipole moment, thus opening the field of nuclear quantum optics. As ultimate goal, one may hope that direct laser-nucleus interactions could become a versatile tool to enhance preparation, control and detection in nuclear physics.

# Nuclear quantum optics with x-ray laser pulses

## Key claim

Present and upcoming high-frequency laser facilities, especially together with a moderate acceleration of target nuclei to match photon and transition frequency, do allow for resonant laser-nucleus interaction, opening the field of nuclear quantum optics. The authors show this is possible despite the traditionally dismissed small interaction matrix elements, provided the target nuclei are accelerated to Doppler-shift the laser frequency into resonance with nuclear transitions.

## Method

The authors model the nucleus as a pure two-level system described by a state vector and master equation, treating the laser-nucleus interaction via electric dipole transitions driven by Gaussian laser pulses. They calculate the time evolution of the nuclear inversion (population difference between ground and excited states) under the influence of laser pulses with varying intensity, detuning, and coherence properties, using parameters from existing x-ray laser design reports (TESLA XFEL at DESY and XRL at GSI) and nuclear data for specific E1 transitions in actinide and rare-earth nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Transition energy in $^{223}$Ra: 50.1 keV, with dipole moment 0.12 e·fm, ground state lifetime 11.435 d, excited state lifetime 730 ps
- Transition energy in $^{181}$Ta: 6.2 keV, with dipole moment 0.04 e·fm, stable ground state, excited state lifetime 6×10^6 ps
- Transition energy in $^{153}$Sm: 35.8 keV, with dipole moment >0.75 e·fm, ground state lifetime 47 h, excited state lifetime <100 ps
- For $^{223}$Ra, a π pulse achieving full inversion requires intensity I_π ≈ 4×10^22 W/cm^2 in the nuclear rest frame
- For TESLA XFEL (SASE 1) with 12.4 keV photons and $^{223}$Ra target accelerated with 0.1% energy resolution, the resonant photon flux is approximately 4.1×10^18 photons/second within the transition width
- Signal yield estimate: 5.4×10^{-10} signal photons per laser pulse per single target nucleus, yielding about 1.4 emitted photons per day for a single nucleus
- With 2.5×10^10 particles per bunch (density 5.3×10^8 cm^{-3}), estimated 2.6×10^{-4} signal photons per pulse and 6.8×10^5 photons per day
- With SIS100/FAIR parameters (density 10^11 cm^{-3}), estimated 5.3×10^{-2} signal photons per pulse and 1.4×10^8 photons per day

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of direct laser-nucleus interactions that were traditionally dismissed due to small interaction matrix elements (Matinyan 1998), with exceptions for x-ray laser interactions with beta decay (Becker 1983) and x-ray-driven gamma emission (Carroll 2001). The paper enables the optical, model-independent measurement of nuclear properties such as transition frequencies and dipole moments, free from assumptions of the rotational model formula typically used to extract dipole moments from reduced transition probabilities. The approach could also allow controlled preparation of excited nuclei for studying nuclear reactions with excited states, and potentially enable nuclear analogs of atomic quantum optics phenomena such as photon echos, coherent trapping, and electromagnetically induced transparency.
