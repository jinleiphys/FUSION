# SQM 2006: Theory Summary and Perspectives

> In this write-up of my SQM 2006 Theory Summary talk I focus on a selection of key contributions which I consider to have a large impact on the current status of the field of strangeness physics or which may have the potential to significantly advance strangeness -- or in general flavor physics -- in the near future.

# SQM 2006: Theory Summary and Perspectives

## Key claim
The field of strangeness physics has matured from a quark-gluon plasma signature into a versatile tool for characterizing QCD matter, but the fundamental mechanisms behind the statistical model's success in describing hadron yields remain poorly understood. The author selects contributions he considers to have large impact or potential to advance flavor physics, explicitly noting the write-up is not a comprehensive summary.

## Method
The paper reviews selected theory contributions presented at the SQM 2006 conference, organized into sections on hadron yields and ratios, excitation functions, transport theory, hadronization via parton recombination, color glass condensate, conserved charge correlations, anomalous viscosity, heavy-quark production and diffusion, and charmonium spectral functions. The author evaluates each topic's current status, open questions, and implications for future research, drawing connections between strangeness physics and heavy-quark physics.

## Key numbers
- Statistical model fits to CERN/SPS and RHIC data yield $\gamma_s$ values close to 1, indicating chemical freeze-out occurred near hadrochemical equilibrium.
- For an ideal hadron gas at $T=170$ MeV and zero baryon chemical potential $\mu_B=0$, the baryon-strangeness correlator $C_{BS} = 0.66$.
- Lattice QCD calculations at $T=1.5\, T_C$ yield $C_{BS} \approx 1$, indicating uncorrelated quark flavors as in an ideal QGP.
- The phase transition temperature $T_C$ is assumed to lie in the range $170\pm 10$ MeV.
- Chemical freeze-out occurs around $T=170$ MeV, while spectral shape requires hydrodynamic evolution to $T=110$ MeV.
- The shear viscosity lower bound is $\eta_{\rm min} = s/4\pi$.
- The 1S charmonia states ($J/\psi$, $\psi'$, $\eta_C$) survive to temperatures above $1.5\, T_C$, while 1P states ($\chi_C$) dissolve around $1.2\, T_C$.
- The nuclear suppression factor $R_{AA}$ below 4 GeV/$c$ is close to 1 for protons and lambdas, while about 0.3 for pions.
- The proton over positively charged pion ratio is equal or above 1 for $p_T > 1.5$ GeV/$c$ and approximately constant up to 4 GeV/$c$.
- Microscopic transport models UrQMD and RQMD reach only 60% of the measured elliptic flow $v_2$ magnitude at RHIC.

## Context
This theory summary reviews the state of strangeness physics at the SQM 2006 conference, extending the long-standing program of using strangeness as a QGP signature initiated by Rafelski (1982) and Koch (1986). The paper contrasts the success of statistical models (Braun-Munzinger 1994, Letessier 1995, Rafelski 2001) with unresolved dynamical questions about chemical equilibration, and highlights the recombination plus fragmentation model (Fries 2003, Greco 2003, Hwa 2002) as the standard model for hadronization at intermediate transverse momenta. The work enables future generalization of strangeness-based probes into the heavy-quark sector at higher beam energies, particularly through conserved charge correlations (Koch 2005, Majumder 2006) and heavy-quark diffusion studies (Moore 2004, van Hees 2004).
