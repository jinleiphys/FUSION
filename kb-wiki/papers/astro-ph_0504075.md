# Skyrmion Stars: Astrophysical motivations and implications

> We study mass-radius relations for compact stars employing an equation of state (EOS) of dense matter based on a Skyrme fluid. The zero-temperature mean-field model is based on mesonic excitations, incorporates the scale breaking of QCD, and accommodates baryons (nucleons) which arise as a solitonic configuration of mesonic fields. Stable configurations are obtained for central densities $ρ_c/ρ_n\leq 5.0$ where $ρ_n=2.575\times 10^{14}$ g/cc is the nuclear saturation density. These {\it Skyrmion Stars} are mostly fluid, with a crust which we describe by the EOS of Baym, Pethick and Sutherland. Their masses and radii are in the range $0.4 \le M/M_{\odot} \le 3.6$ and $13 {\rm km} \le R \le 23 {\rm km}$, respectively. The minimum spin period is computed to be between $0.7 {\rm ms}\le P \le 2.1 {\rm ms}$. They appear to have a mass-radius curve quite different from either neutron or quark stars, and provide a suitable description of the heavier mass neutron stars discovered recently due to the inherently stiff EOS. Within the same model, we compute the dominant neutrino emissivity in neutron-rich $β$-equilibrated matter, and determine the cooling behaviour of Skyrmion stars.

# Skyrmion Stars: Astrophysical motivations and implications

## Key claim

Skyrmion stars, modeled as a fluid of unit-baryon-number skyrmions in a mean-field approach with isospin-breaking effects from the rho meson, can produce stable compact star configurations with masses up to 3.6 solar masses and radii between 13 and 23 km, but only for central densities at or below 5.0 times nuclear saturation density (rho_n = 2.575 x 10^14 g/cc). The authors hedge that the evidence for large neutron star masses (above 2 solar masses) is still being debated and awaits confirmation, and that their model avoids gravitational collapse instability by treating the star as an ensemble of N=1 skyrmions rather than a single large topological object.

## Method

The authors construct a zero-temperature mean-field equation of state from the Skyrme Lagrangian augmented with dilaton (sigma), vector omega, and rho meson fields to incorporate scale breaking of QCD and isospin asymmetry, fitting parameters to nuclear saturation properties (binding energy -16 MeV per nucleon, symmetry energy 32 MeV, compressibility 270 MeV, effective mass M/M0 = 0.60). They solve the general-relativistic Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations (including rotation via the RNS code) using this EOS with a crust described by the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland EOS, and compute neutrino emissivities from direct and modified Urca processes to determine cooling behavior.

## Key numbers

- Stable configurations exist for central densities rho_c/rho_n <= 5.0, where rho_n = 2.575 x 10^14 g/cc (nuclear saturation density)
- Mass range: 0.4 <= M/M_sun <= 3.6 (rotating); radii range: 13 km <= R <= 23 km (static), 18.6 km <= R <= 23.0 km (rotating equatorial circumferential radius)
- Minimum spin period: 0.7 ms <= P <= 2.1 ms
- Direct Urca neutrino emission threshold: proton fraction y >= 1/9, corresponding to density approximately 2.5 rho_n
- Direct Urca neutrino emissivity: epsilon_nu^dUrca = 1.2 x 10^27 (m_p*/GeV)(m_n*/GeV)(rho_V/rho_n)^(1/3) y^(1/3) Theta(y - y_crit) T_9^6 erg cm^-3 s^-1
- Modified Urca neutrino emissivity: epsilon_nu^MUrca = 2.45 x 10^21 (m_p*/GeV)(m_n*/GeV)^3 alpha_Murca (rho_V/rho_n)^(1/3) y^(1/3) T_9^8 erg cm^-3 s^-1
- Coupling constants: g_V = 7.54 (from symmetric matter binding energy fit), g_rho = 7.70 (from symmetry energy fit)
- Dilaton potential parameters: a1 = -29689.7, a2 = -274771.0, a3 = 222839.0, a4 = -66865.6
- Crust constitutes less than 5% of total mass for rotating configurations, averaging 20% for static configurations

## Context

This work extends the earlier Skyrme fluid model of Ouyed and Butler (1998) by including isospin-breaking effects via the rho meson to address asymmetric (neutron-rich) matter, which is more realistic than the symmetric or pure neutron matter considered previously. The mass-radius curves differ qualitatively from both conventional neutron star EOS (such as those by Glendenning 1997, Mueller and Serot 1996, and Weber and Weigel 1989) and quark star models (Li et al. 1999), showing increasing radius with mass in a certain mass window similar to self-bound quark stars. The results enable comparison with recent observations of potentially massive neutron stars (e.g., PSR J0751+1807 at 2.1 solar masses from Nice et al. 2005) and provide cooling curves that can be tested against surface temperature data from pulsars such as Vela and Geminga.
