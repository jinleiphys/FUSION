# Relativistic nuclear energy density functional constrained by low-energy QCD

> A relativistic nuclear energy density functional is developed, guided by two important features that establish connections with chiral dynamics and the symmetry breaking pattern of low-energy QCD: a) strong scalar and vector fields related to in-medium changes of QCD vacuum condensates; b) the long- and intermediate-range interactions generated by one-and two-pion exchange, derived from in-medium chiral perturbation theory, with explicit inclusion of $Δ(1232)$ excitations. Applications are presented for binding energies, radii of proton and neutron distributions and other observables over a wide range of spherical and deformed nuclei from $^{16}O$ to $^{210}Po$. Isotopic chains of $Sn$ and $Pb$ nuclei are studied as test cases for the isospin dependence of the underlying interactions. The results are at the same level of quantitative comparison with data as the best phenomenological relativistic mean-field models.

## Key claim

The authors construct a relativistic nuclear energy density functional that connects low-energy QCD with finite-nuclei phenomenology, claiming that the resulting description of binding energies, radii, and other observables across a wide range of spherical and deformed nuclei (from 16O to 210Po) matches the quantitative accuracy of the best phenomenological relativistic mean-field models, but only after fine-tuning a few short-range contact parameters (the constants b_i and a_i) that encode unresolved short-distance dynamics.

## Method

The authors start from in-medium chiral perturbation theory (ChPT) including explicit Δ(1232) excitations, computed to three-loop order in the energy density, to derive the exchange-correlation part of the nuclear energy density functional. They map the resulting density-dependent nucleon self-energies in nuclear matter onto those of a covariant point-coupling model with density-dependent contact interactions, where the isoscalar scalar and vector background fields are guided by in-medium QCD sum rules relating them to changes of the chiral condensate and quark density. The self-consistent single-nucleon Dirac equations (the relativistic analogue of Kohn-Sham equations) are then solved in the no-sea approximation for finite nuclei, with the few remaining contact parameters (b_3, b_5, b_6, a_3, a_5, a_6) adjusted to reproduce nuclear matter properties and then fine-tuned against a broad range of nuclear observables.

## Key numbers

- The chiral vacuum condensate value: ⟨q̄q⟩₀ ≈ −(240 MeV)³ ≈ −1.8 fm⁻³ (at a renormalization scale of about 1 GeV)
- The nucleon sigma term: σ_N ≈ 50 MeV
- The pion decay constant: f_π = 92.4 MeV
- The pion mass: m_π = 138 MeV
- The Δ-N mass difference: Δ ≡ M_Δ − M_N ≈ 2.1 m_π
- The characteristic scale of spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking: 4πf_π ≈ 1.16 GeV
- The estimated isoscalar scalar and vector couplings from QCD sum rules: G_S^(0) ≈ −11 fm² and G_V^(0) ≈ 11 fm² (for σ_N ≈ 48 MeV and m_u + m_d ≈ 12 MeV)
- The incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter from the chiral calculation: K_0 = 304 MeV
- The scale Λ chosen for contact terms: Λ = 2πf_π ≈ 0.58 GeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous approach (Finelli 2002, 2003) that did not include explicit Δ(1232) degrees of freedom, which had shown promising results only for N≈Z nuclei. The inclusion of chiral πNΔ-dynamics significantly improves the isospin properties of nuclear matter (as shown in Fri. 2004) and enables the description of N≠Z nuclei, with isotopic chains of Sn and Pb studied as test cases. The approach is an alternative to the program initiated by Furnstahl and collaborators (2003, 2005) that synthesizes effective field theory methods with density functional theory via an effective action formalism; the present work instead emphasizes direct connections to QCD condensates and in-medium chiral perturbation theory. The results enable future applications to exotic nuclei far from stability and provide a microscopic foundation for the phenomenological relativistic mean-field models that have been the standard tool for nuclear structure calculations.
