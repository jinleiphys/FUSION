# Anthropic constraints on fermion masses

> We summarize the results of previous research on the constraints imposed on quark masses by the anthropically-motivated requirement that there exist stable nuclei with the right charge to form complex molecules. We also mention an upper bound on the mass of the lightest lepton, derived from the requirement that such nuclei be stable against electron capture.

## Key claim

The paper argues that anthropic reasoning can place quantitative constraints on the masses of the three light quarks and the electron, but these constraints depend on the specific "slice" through parameter space chosen (here, fixing the electron mass and adjusting Λ_QCD to keep the lightest baryon multiplet mass fixed), and the analysis is limited to worlds with at most three light quarks because baryon masses can only be reliably estimated for those cases.

## Method

The author uses first-order perturbation theory in flavor SU(3) symmetry breaking, with parameters extracted from the observed baryon spectrum, to compute baryon masses as functions of the light quark masses (u, d, s). Nuclear masses are then estimated using either analog nuclei or a generalized semi-empirical mass formula, and stability against strong and weak decay processes is checked to classify worlds as "congenial" (stable hydrogen and carbon) or "uncongenial." For the electron mass, the author derives bounds from the requirement that nuclei be stable against electron capture and from the condition that the Bohr radius exceed nuclear size.

## Key numbers

- The Λ baryon could be as little as 20 MeV heavier than the proton and neutron and still not form stable nuclei.
- Worlds in which the electron is more than ~10^5 times heavier than in our world are anthropically forbidden, based on the Bohr radius (0.5 × 10^{-10} m) versus nuclear size (~10^{-15} m).
- Stability of hydrogen requires m_e < M_n - M_p + 8.5 MeV (where B(³H) = 8.5 MeV).
- Stability of heavy nuclei requires m_e ≲ 10 MeV, derived from m_e ≲ ½(M_n - M_p) + dB_max/dA, where dB_max/dA ≈ 8 MeV for medium to heavy nuclei.
- The s quark mass cannot be smaller than its observed value by more than about an order of magnitude, or stable carbon likely would not exist.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Jenkins and collaborators (2008) on "congenial" universes, which itself built on earlier anthropic studies by Weinberg (1987) on the cosmological constant and by Hogan (2000) on nucleosynthesis with varying quark masses. The results enable future work to explore whether universes that are structurally capable of supporting organic chemistry actually synthesize the necessary elements in sufficient abundance, though the author notes this is too ambitious given current understanding. The paper also identifies open questions about possible lower anthropic bounds on the electron mass, which would affect recombination temperatures and organic chemistry but are difficult to quantify.
