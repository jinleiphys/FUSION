# Gross features of finite nuclei at finite temperatures

> A simple expression is obtained for the low temperature behavior of the energy and entropy of finite nuclei for $20\leq A\leq 250$. The dependence on $A$ of these quantities is for the most part due to the presence of the asymmetry energy.

## Key claim

The low-temperature excitation energy of finite nuclei (20 ≤ A ≤ 250) follows a simple quadratic form in temperature, and the coefficient of the T² term depends linearly on mass number A, with the asymmetry energy term being largely responsible for that A dependence.

## Method

The authors used a finite-temperature extension of the semi-empirical mass formula with temperature-dependent coefficients determined from a least-squares fit to the canonical-ensemble average excitation energy of 313 nuclei. The partition function for each nucleus was constructed from experimentally known discrete excited states supplemented by Fermi gas and exponential level-density parametrizations from Gilbert and Cameron (1965). The excitation energy was calculated for stable isotopes and fitted quadratically in temperature to extract the coefficient b in the relation E/A = a + bT².

## Key numbers

- The binding energy per nucleon at zero temperature is approximately α ≈ 8 MeV for A ≥ 20.
- The zero-temperature coefficients of the mass formula are: α(0) = -16.11 MeV, β(0) = 20.21 MeV, γ(0) = 20.65 MeV, η(0) = 48.00 MeV, κ(0) = 0.8076 MeV, and δ(0) = 33.0 MeV.
- When all terms of the binding energy are included, the linear fit gives b = -2.032 × 10⁻⁴ A + 0.152 (MeV⁻¹).
- When the asymmetry term (term 3) is excluded, the fit gives b = -1.617 × 10⁻⁵ A + 0.140 (MeV⁻¹), with b varying only from 0.141 to 0.137 for A > 40.
- The entropy per particle at low temperature is S = (1/2) b T with the same b expression.

## Context

This work extends the Fermi gas model prediction of a T² behavior for excitation energy (Bohr and Mottelson 1975, Preston 1962) to finite nuclei across the periodic table, using a temperature-dependent semi-empirical mass formula developed by Dean et al. (1993). The results show that the asymmetry term introduces the dominant A dependence in the quadratic coefficient, which the simple Fermi gas model misses because it does not treat protons and neutrons separately. The parametrization enables simple estimates of thermal properties for medium-to-heavy nuclei and has previously been used to identify the pairing phase transition in symmetric nuclear matter (Rios et al. 2001) and its remnants in finite nuclei (Khan et al. 2005).
