# Parity Violating Measurements of Neutron Densities

> Parity violating electron nucleus scattering is a clean and powerful tool for measuring the spatial distributions of neutrons in nuclei with unprecedented accuracy. Parity violation arises from the interference of electromagnetic and weak neutral amplitudes, and the $Z^0$ of the Standard Model couples primarily to neutrons at low $Q^2$. The data can be interpreted with as much confidence as electromagnetic scattering. After briefly reviewing the present theoretical and experimental knowledge of neutron densities, we discuss possible parity violation measurements, their theoretical interpretation, and applications. The experiments are feasible at existing facilities. We show that theoretical corrections are either small or well understood, which makes the interpretation clean. The quantitative relationship to atomic parity nonconservation observables is examined, and we show that the electron scattering asymmetries can be directly applied to atomic PNC because the observables have approximately the same dependence on nuclear shape.

## Key claim

Parity violating electron scattering can measure neutron densities in nuclei with accuracy comparable to electromagnetic scattering for charge densities, provided that Coulomb distortion corrections (which are of order 20% for heavy nuclei like 208Pb) are accurately calculated and applied.

## Method

The authors propose measuring the parity-violating asymmetry \(A_{LR}\) in elastic electron scattering from spin-zero nuclei, where the asymmetry arises from interference between photon and Z0 exchange. Because the Z0 couples primarily to neutrons at low momentum transfer, the asymmetry directly yields the neutron form factor \(F_n(Q^2)\) after correcting for Coulomb distortions via numerical solution of the Dirac equation, and the neutron density is then extracted from the form factor.

## Key numbers

- The root-mean-square neutron radius in 208Pb is thought to be about 0.25 fm larger than the proton radius \(R_p \approx 5.45\) fm.
- The spread between two typical mean-field predictions for \(R_n - R_p\) in 208Pb is approximately 0.2 fm (Skyrme force gives ~0.1 fm, relativistic mean field gives ~0.3 fm).
- A 1% measurement of \(R_n\) with accuracy of about 0.05 fm can distinguish between the two forces and cleanly observe the neutron skin.
- For 208Pb at 0.85 GeV and 6° scattering angle (\(q = 0.45\) fm\(^{-1}\)), the running time to reach 1% accuracy in \(R_n\) is approximately \(T \approx 7 / (P^2 I \Omega)\) days, where \(P \approx 0.8\), \(I \approx 50 \mu\)A, and \(\Omega\) is the solid angle in steradians.
- For 138Ba at 1.0 GeV, the optimum momentum transfer is \(q = 0.53\) fm\(^{-1}\).
- Coulomb distortion corrections for 208Pb are of order \(Z\alpha/\pi \approx 20\%\).

## Context

This work extends the suggestion by Donnelly, Dubach and Sick (1989) that parity violating electron scattering can measure neutron densities, and builds on experimental progress from the SLAC experiment (Prescott et al., 1979), the Bates 12C experiment (Souder et al., 1990), the Mainz 9Be experiment (Heil et al., 1989), SAMPLE (Mueller et al., 1997), and HAPPEX (Aniol et al., 1999). The measurements would provide the first clean observation of the neutron skin in a stable nucleus, test mean-field theories such as those of Furnstahl, Serot and Tang (1997) and Ring et al. (1997), and directly inform atomic parity nonconservation experiments in cesium (Wood et al., 1997; Bennett and Wieman, 1999) by constraining the nuclear shape dependence that enters both observables in the same way.
