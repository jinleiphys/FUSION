# Theoretical Methods for Giant Resonances

> The Random Phase Approximation (RPA) and its variations and extensions are, without any doubt, the most widely used tools to describe Giant Resonances within a microscopic theory. In this chapter, we will start by discussing how RPA comes out naturally if one seeks a state with a harmonic time dependence in the space of one particle-one hole excitations on top of the ground state. It will be also shown that RPA is the simplest approach in which a ``collective'' state emerges. These are basic arguments that appear in other textbooks but are also unavoidable as a starting point for further discussions. In the rest of the chapter, we will give emphasis to developments that have taken place in the last decades: alternatives to RPA like the Finite Amplitude Method (FAM), state-of-the-art calculations with well-established Energy Density Functionals (EDFs), and progress in {\em ab initio} calculations. We will discuss extensions of RPA using as a red thread the various enlargements of the one particle-one hole model space. The importance of the continuum, and the exclusive observables like the decay products of Giant Resonances, will be also touched upon.

## Key claim

The Random Phase Approximation (RPA) and its extensions remain the most widely used microscopic tools for describing Giant Resonances, but the chapter emphasizes that these methods assume small-amplitude harmonic oscillations around the ground state and that the full time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) approach cannot fully describe dissipation features like the damping of Giant Resonances because only the one-body density is involved.

## Method

The chapter reviews the theoretical framework starting from the time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) equation and its linearization to obtain the quasi-particle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) equations, which describe small-amplitude oscillations around the ground state. The Finite Amplitude Method (FAM) is presented as an alternative to direct QRPA matrix diagonalization, where the variation of the Hamiltonian with respect to density variations is computed numerically without evaluating two-body matrix elements. The reduction to standard RPA in non-superfluid nuclei is shown, along with a schematic Tamm-Dancoff approximation (TDA) model that illustrates how collective states emerge from coherent particle-hole excitations.

## Key numbers

- The Isovector Giant Dipole Resonance (IVGDR) energy follows E_IVGDR ≈ 80 A^(-1/3) MeV, with the unperturbed particle-hole energy at approximately 41 A^(-1/3) MeV
- Giant Resonances appear in the nuclear response around approximately 10-30 MeV
- The schematic TDA model shows that the coherent state transition probability becomes N times larger than the single-particle value M^2, where N is the number of degenerate particle-hole excitations
- The ISGMR peak energy in 208Pb is experimentally measured at 13.9 MeV (TAMU) and 13.7 MeV (RCNP)
- The nucleus 240Pu has an axial deformation associated with β=0.28

## Context

This chapter extends the foundational work on Giant Resonances reviewed in monographs by Bortignon (1998) and Harakeh (2001), and builds on the seminal schematic model of Brown (1959). The discussion connects to modern developments including the Finite Amplitude Method introduced by Nakatsukasa (2007) and extended with pairing by Avogadro (2011), state-of-the-art Energy Density Functional calculations using Skyrme, Gogny, and covariant functionals, and progress in ab initio approaches. The work enables testing nuclear Hamiltonians and extracting the nuclear Equation of State, particularly the nuclear matter compressibility from the Isoscalar Giant Monopole Resonance (ISGMR), as discussed by Blaizot (1980) and Garg (2018).
