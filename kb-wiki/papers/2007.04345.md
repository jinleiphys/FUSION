# Hydrodynamics of simply spinning black holes & hydrodynamics for spinning quantum fluids

> We find hydrodynamic behavior in large simply spinning five-dimensional Anti-de Sitter black holes. These are dual to spinning quantum fluids through the AdS/CFT correspondence constructed from string theory. Due to the spatial anisotropy introduced by the angular momentum in the system, hydrodynamic transport coefficients split into one group longitudinal and another transverse to the angular momentum. Analytic expressions are provided for the two shear viscosities, the longitudinal momentum diffusion coefficient, two speeds of sound, and two sound attenuation coefficients. Known relations between these coefficients are generalized to include dependence on angular momentum. The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio varies between zero and 1/(4$π$) depending on the direction of the shear. These results can be applied to heavy ion collisions, in which the most vortical fluid was reported recently. In passing, we show that large simply spinning five-dimensional Myers-Perry black holes are perturbatively stable for all angular momenta below extremality.

## Key claim

The paper finds that in a spinning quantum fluid (holographically dual to a simply spinning five-dimensional Anti-de Sitter black hole), the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio is not universally \(1/(4\pi)\) but instead varies between zero and \(1/(4\pi)\) depending on the direction of the shear relative to the angular momentum, with the longitudinal specific shear viscosity given by \(\eta_{||}/s = (1/(4\pi))(1-a^2)\) for dimensionless angular momentum \(-1 < a < 1\).

## Method

The authors consider linear metric perturbations of large simply spinning five-dimensional Myers-Perry black holes in global AdS\(_5\), using the AdS/CFT correspondence to map these to perturbations of a dual spinning quantum fluid. They compute the lowest quasinormal modes in three channels (shear/tensor, momentum diffusion/vector, and sound/scalar) by expanding perturbations in Wigner-D functions on the \(S^3\) and solving the linearized Einstein equations. Three distinct large black hole limits are considered: (i) large horizon radius \(r_+ \gg L\) without rescaling, (ii) a strict \(r_+ \to \infty\) limit performed in the fluctuation equations while preserving the \(S^3 \times \mathbb{R}\) structure, and (iii) a planar limit that turns the black hole into a boosted black brane with flat boundary \(\mathbb{R}^{3,1}\).

## Key numbers

- Longitudinal specific shear viscosity: \(\eta_{||}/s = (1/(4\pi))(1-a^2)\) for dimensionless angular momentum \(-1 < a < 1\).
- Transverse specific shear viscosity: \(\eta_{\perp}/s = 1/(4\pi)\), independent of angular momentum \(a\).
- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio varies between zero and \(1/(4\pi)\) depending on the direction of the shear.
- Large simply spinning five-dimensional Myers-Perry black holes are perturbatively stable for all angular momenta below extremality, \(-L < a < L = 1\) (with the AdS radius \(L\) set to unity).
- In the strict large black hole limit (case ii), the temperature asymptotes to \(T \sim \sqrt{L^2-a^2} \, r_+/(\pi L^3)\) and the entropy density to \(s \sim r_+^3/(4 G_5 L^2 \sqrt{L^2-a^2})\).

## Context

This work extends the fluid/gravity correspondence to rotating systems with spatial anisotropy, generalizing known relations for transport coefficients to include dependence on angular momentum. It builds on prior work by Hawking (1998, 1999) and Gibbons (2004) on rotating black hole thermodynamics, and on studies of anisotropic hydrodynamics by Martinez (2010), Ryblewski (2010), and Florkowski (2012). The results contradict the widespread belief that at large 't Hooft coupling the shear viscosity over entropy density must be \(1/(4\pi)\) in all directions, and provide input for hydrodynamic simulations of the quark-gluon plasma in heavy ion collisions, where the most vortical fluid was reported by the STAR collaboration (2017). The analytic expressions enable future work on spin currents and spin-orbit coupling in rotating quantum fluids.
