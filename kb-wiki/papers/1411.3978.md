# Nuclear masses near $N=82$ that influence $r$-process abundances

> Nuclear masses are one of the key ingredients of nuclear physics that go into astrophysical simulations of the $r$ process. Nuclear masses effect $r$-process abundances by entering into calculations of Q-values, neutron capture rates, photo-dissociation rates, beta-decay rates, branching ratios and the properties of fission. Most of the thousands of short-lived neutron-rich nuclei which are believed to participate in the $r$ process lack any experimental verification, thus the identification of the most influential nuclei is of paramount importance. We have conducted mass sensitivity studies near the $N=82$ closed shell in the context of a main $r$-process. Our studies take into account how an uncertainty in a single nuclear mass propagates to influence the relevant quantities of neighboring nuclei and finally to $r$-process abundances. We identify influential nuclei in various astrophysical conditions using the FRDM mass model. We show that our conclusions regarding these key nuclei are still retained when a superposition of astrophysical trajectories is considered.

## Key claim
The authors show that the specific nuclei near the N=82 closed shell identified as most influential for r-process abundances in separate astrophysical trajectories remain the same when a weighted superposition of those trajectories is considered, demonstrating the robustness of their conclusions.

## Method
The authors performed self-consistent mass sensitivity studies by varying individual nuclear masses by ±1.0 MeV in the FRDM mass model and propagating those variations to neutron capture rates, photo-dissociation rates, beta-decay rates, and beta-delayed neutron emission probabilities of neighboring nuclei. They used the TALYS statistical model code for reaction rates and a QRPA model for weak decay properties, then computed a sensitivity metric F that quantifies the difference between baseline and varied abundance patterns for four separate r-process trajectories (two hot winds, a cold wind, and a neutron star merger). Finally, they combined these trajectories into a weighted superposition and recalculated the sensitivity metric.

## Key numbers
- Mass variations of ±1.0 MeV were applied to individual nuclei near the N=82 closed shell
- Four astrophysical trajectories were used: hot wind with entropy s/k=100, hot wind with entropy s/k=200, cold wind, and neutron star merger
- The superposition weights were w_i = 0.8 (hot wind s/k=100), 1.0 (hot wind s/k=200), 1.5 (cold wind), and 3.5 (neutron star merger)
- The hot wind trajectories contribute to sensitivity at higher A while cold wind and neutron star merger trajectories highlight sensitivities at lower A

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous series of sensitivity studies on individual neutron capture rates (Mumpower 2012), beta-decay rates, and binding energies (Brett 2012) that culminated in self-consistent mass propagation studies (Mumpower 2014a, 2014b). The results show that key nuclei identified in separate trajectories remain influential when using a superposition that approximates solar r-process residuals from Arlandini (1999), providing motivation for measurements at future radioactive beam facilities like FRIB.
