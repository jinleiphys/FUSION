# Quantum Friction: Cooling Quantum Systems with Unitary Time Evolution

> We introduce a type of quantum dissipation -- local quantum friction -- by adding to the Hamiltonian a local potential that breaks time-reversal invariance so as to cool the system. Unlike the Kossakowski-Lindblad master equation, local quantum friction directly effects unitary evolution of the wavefunctions rather than the density matrix: it may thus be used to cool fermionic many-body systems with thousands of wavefunctions that must remain orthogonal. In addition to providing an efficient way to simulate quantum dissipation and non-equilibrium dynamics, local quantum friction coupled with adiabatic state preparation significantly speeds up many-body simulations, making the solution of the time-dependent Schrödinger equation significantly simpler than the solution of its stationary counterpart.

## Key claim

The authors introduce a "local quantum friction" potential that breaks time-reversal invariance and can be added to the Hamiltonian to cool a quantum system during unitary time evolution, but the method only removes irrotational currents and may stall near stationary excited states if the initial wavefunctions are poorly chosen.

## Method

The authors add a local potential \(U_t = -\beta \hbar (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{j}_t)/\rho_t\) to the time-dependent Schrödinger equation, where \(\mathbf{j}_t\) is the probability current density and \(\rho_t\) is the local density, with \(\beta\) a dimensionless constant of order unity. This potential is applied during quasi-adiabatic state preparation, where the Hamiltonian is slowly switched from a solvable \(H_0\) to the target \(H_1\), and the friction term removes excess energy introduced by non-adiabatic transitions. The method is implemented for fermionic time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) by evolving thousands of orthogonal single-particle wavefunctions with the same single-particle Hamiltonian plus the friction potential.

## Key numbers

- A single diagonalization for both proton and neutron single-particle Hamiltonians on the JaguarPF computer used 217,800 of 224,256 processor cores and took about 6 hours of wall-time (about one million CPU hours).
- For the unitary Fermi gas example, the single-particle Hamiltonian is a 32,768 × 32,768 matrix, and 15,322 coupled nonlinear partial differential equations in 3D and time are solved.
- The unitary Fermi gas solution using twenty-four GPUs on the UW Hyak cluster takes about one hour to converge to the ground state.
- For the symmetrized Woods-Saxon potential on a 32³ spatial lattice, the 20 lowest bound states can be found in under half an hour on a laptop.
- For fission of a heavy nucleus, a volume of 40 × 40 × 60 fm³ with resolution of at least 1 fm (energy cutoff ~200 MeV) requires N ~ 400,000 components per single-particle wavefunction including pairing and spin.

## Context

This work extends the approach of adiabatic state preparation (Pfitzner 1994, Bulgac 2013) by adding a friction term to overcome the problem of narrowly avoided level crossings and Landau-Zener transitions that require long evolution times. The method enables efficient preparation of initial states for TDDFT simulations of nuclear systems, trapped cold fermionic atoms, and electronic structure, where traditional diagonalization methods scale as O(N³) per iteration while real-time evolution scales as O(N² log N). The authors suggest this could be extended to create a quantum equivalent of the Langevin equation by adding a fluctuating potential, similar to the stochastic projected Gross-Pitaevskii equation (Rooney 2012).
