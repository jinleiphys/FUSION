# Remarkable long-range-systematic in the binding energies of alpha-nuclei II

> In this letter I present further data that show the remarkable evidence for the existence of an alpha-cluster stucture in the ground states of even-even N=Z nuclei. Such a remarkable systematic was observed 20 years ago for these nuclei at A<= 72 and is extended here up to A=100.

## Key claim

The paper presents evidence that the two-nucleon separation energies of even-even N=Z nuclei (alpha-nuclei) remain approximately constant and equal to the separation energies of a bare alpha-particle (about 30 MeV) up to A=100, extending a systematic first observed 20 years ago for nuclei up to A=72. The author hedges that this systematic is "remarkable" but notes that the peaks in alpha-separation energies disappear above n_alpha=14, suggesting the cluster structure may not persist for the heaviest nuclei studied.

## Method

The author computed nuclear two-nucleon separation energies (for proton-proton, neutron-neutron, and neutron-proton pairs) and alpha-particle separation energies from experimental binding energies of even-even N=Z nuclei, using the most recent nuclear binding energy tables from the National Nuclear Data Center. The separation energies were corrected for Coulomb energy differences using a simple formula (U^C = (3/5)(e^2/r_0)Z(Z-1)/A^{1/3} with r_0=1.25 fm) to isolate the nuclear contribution. The corrected separation energies were then plotted as a function of the number of alpha-particles (n_alpha) in the mother nucleus.

## Key numbers

- The removal of a 2N-pair from an alpha-nucleus costs about the same energy as for a bare alpha-particle: approximately 30 MeV.
- Peaks in the nuclear alpha-separation energies (corrected for Coulomb) occur at n_alpha = 4, 7, and 14, followed by breaks in the systematic.
- Above n_alpha = 14, the peaks in alpha-separation energies disappear.
- The systematic was originally observed for nuclei at A ≤ 72 and is now extended up to A = 100.

## Context

This work extends a systematic first reported by Gross (1983) for alpha-nuclei up to A=72, using the considerably expanded table of nuclear binding energies now available from the National Nuclear Data Center. The observed peaks at n_alpha=4,7,14 resemble the stable three-dimensional clusters of closed-packed spheres (icosahedral structures) seen in atomic clusters of heavier noble gases like Xe, where the shift from 13 to 14 is well known for lighter clusters like Ar. The results enable further investigation of alpha-cluster structure in ground states of heavy N=Z nuclei and may connect nuclear structure to cluster physics in other systems.
