# A minimal implementation of Yang-Mills theory on a digital quantum computer

> We present a minimal implementation of SU($N$) pure Yang-Mills theory in $3+1$ dimensions for digital quantum simulation, designed to enable quantum advantage. Building on the orbifold lattice simulation protocol with logarithmic scaling in the local Hilbert-space truncation, we introduce further simplified Hamiltonians. Furthermore, we test simple methods that improve the convergence to the infinite mass limit, thereby removing the requirement of a large scalar mass to obtain the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian. For the SU(2) theory, we can cut the resource requirement further by utilizing the embedding of $\mathrm{SU}(2)\cong\mathrm{S}^3$ into $\mathbb{R}^4$. Monte Carlo simulations of the Euclidean path integral were used to benchmark the accuracy of these new analytical improvements to the theory. These results provide further support for the noncompact-variable-based approach as a practical framework for quantum simulation of non-Abelian gauge theories.

## Key claim

The authors show that the orbifold lattice approach to quantum simulation of SU(N) Yang-Mills theory can be significantly simplified by removing interaction terms that vanish in the infinite scalar mass limit, and that for SU(2) the group manifold can be embedded into ℝ⁴ instead of ℝ⁸, halving the number of scalar degrees of freedom per link, while still recovering the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian in the large-mass limit.

## Method

The authors start from the orbifold lattice Hamiltonian for pure Yang-Mills theory in 3+1 dimensions, which uses complex link variables Z embedded in ℝ^(2N²) with additional scalar fields. They systematically remove terms from the Hamiltonian that become negligible in the infinite scalar mass limit (the Kogut-Susskind limit), producing two simplified Hamiltonians H₁ and H₂. For SU(2) specifically, they exploit the isomorphism SU(2) ≅ S³ to embed the group into ℝ⁴ instead of ℝ⁸, using four real variables per link instead of eight. They validate these analytical simplifications through classical Monte Carlo simulations of the Euclidean path integral, benchmarking the accuracy of the new Hamiltonians against the original orbifold formulation.

## Key numbers

- The original orbifold lattice embeds SU(N) into ℝ^(2N²); for SU(2) this is ℝ⁸, while the new embedding uses ℝ⁴, halving the number of scalar degrees of freedom per link.
- The simplified Hamiltonian H₂ removes the term proportional to |∑_j (Z_j Z̄_j − Z̄_{j−ĵ} Z_{j−ĵ})|² from the original orbifold Hamiltonian, keeping only the plaquette-like term and the kinetic term.
- The constraint term ΔL for the ℝ⁴ embedding uses a mass parameter m² and the condition ∑_{a=1}⁴ (x_{j,n;a})² = 2a^(D−3)/g_D², with the potential proportional to m² g_D²/(16 a^(D−3)) times the squared deviation from this constraint.

## Context

This work extends the orbifold lattice approach developed by Kaplan (2002) and later applied to quantum simulation by Buser et al. (2020) and Bergner et al. (2024, 2025). It directly addresses the longstanding obstacle identified by Byrnes and Yamamoto (2005) regarding the difficulty of representing compact unitary link variables on quantum computers. The simplified Hamiltonians enable more efficient quantum circuit construction using only Cartesian coordinates, quantum Fourier transforms, CNOT gates, and single-qubit rotations, as previously demonstrated by Hanada et al. (2025) and Halimeh et al. (2024). The results support the noncompact-variable-based approach as a practical framework for quantum simulation of non-Abelian gauge theories, potentially enabling quantum advantage for problems like real-time dynamics and systems with sign problems in four-dimensional SU(3) Yang-Mills theory.
