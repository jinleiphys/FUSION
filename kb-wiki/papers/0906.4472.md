# Nuclear Many-Body Physics Where Structure And Reactions Meet

> The path from understanding a simple reaction problem of scattering or tunneling to contemplating the quantum nuclear many-body system, where structure and continuum of reaction-states meet, overlap and coexist, is a complex and nontrivial one. In this presentation we discuss some of the intriguing aspects of this route.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the interplay between internal structure and reaction dynamics in open quantum many-body systems leads to complex, non-perturbative effects, such as resonant behavior and threshold cusps, which are not captured by treating the projectile as a structureless particle; the authors demonstrate this through both an exactly solvable one-dimensional model and a realistic Continuum Shell Model calculation.

## Method

The authors first construct an exactly solvable one-dimensional scattering model where a composite projectile (two particles bound by a harmonic oscillator potential) scatters off a delta-function potential acting on only one constituent particle, solving the resulting infinite set of linear equations via truncation. They then apply the Continuum Shell Model, using a projection formalism to derive an energy-dependent non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian, to compute neutron scattering cross-sections for \(^{6}\)He in its \(0^{+}\) ground state, with the intrinsic shell model Hamiltonian defined within the p-shell valence space and the continuum described by a Woods-Saxon potential.

## Key numbers

- For the one-dimensional model, the transmission probability for the ground-state channel shows resonant peaks that do not coincide with threshold energies, in contrast to the non-composite limit where reflection \(R = (1 + 2[\mathcal{E}/\Delta])^{-1}\) depends only on kinetic energy relative to the delta strength.
- In the \(^{6}\)He-neutron scattering example, a narrow resonance in \(^{7}\)He is found at \(E_r = -1.02\) MeV with a width of 91 keV, corresponding to a spectroscopic factor \(C^2S = 0.498\).
- The threshold to the second decay channel (decay to a \(2^{+}\), 1.89 MeV excited state in \(^{6}\)He) is at 0.515 MeV.
- The second \(3/2^{-}\) resonance in \(^{7}\)He (5.510 MeV excitation energy) appears at 4.494 MeV only when all other decay channels are ignored; this state has a large width to decay into the \(2^{+}\) final state in \(^{6}\)He with \(C^2S = 1.03\), making the resonance peak impossible to observe.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical unification of nuclear structure and reactions, building on the projection formalism of Feshbach (1958) and the Continuum Shell Model approach developed by Volya and collaborators (2003, 2005). The one-dimensional model provides an exactly solvable benchmark for understanding the role of internal degrees of freedom in scattering, while the realistic \(^{6}\)He-neutron example demonstrates how the Continuum Shell Model can treat coupled channels and overlapping resonances, as previously applied to the chain of He isotopes by Volya (2007). The results enable future theoretical developments toward a fully unified description of structure and reactions, particularly for exotic nuclei where the interplay between bound states and the continuum is critical.
