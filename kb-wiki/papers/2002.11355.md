# Multi-messenger constraints on the neutron-star equation of state and the Hubble constant

> Observations of neutron-star mergers based on distinct messengers, including gravitational waves and electromagnetic signals, can be used to study the behavior of matter denser than an atomic nucleus, and to measure the expansion rate of the Universe described by the Hubble constant. We perform a joint analysis of the gravitational-wave signal GW170817 with its electromagnetic counterparts AT2017gfo and GRB170817A, and the gravitational-wave signal GW190425, both originating from neutron-star mergers. We combine these with previous measurements of pulsars using X-ray and radio observations, and nuclear-theory computations using chiral effective field theory to constrain the neutron-star equation of state. We find that the radius of a $1.4$ solar mass neutron star is $11.75^{+0.86}_{-0.81}\ \rm km$ at $90\%$ confidence and the Hubble constant is $66.2^{+4.4}_{-4.2}\ \rm km \,Mpc^{-1}\, s^{-1}$ at $1σ$ uncertainty.

## Key claim

By jointly analyzing gravitational-wave signals GW170817 and GW190425, their electromagnetic counterparts, pulsar mass measurements, and chiral effective field theory calculations, the authors obtain a neutron-star radius of \(11.75^{+0.86}_{-0.81}\) km (90% confidence) and a Hubble constant of \(66.2^{+4.4}_{-4.2}\) km Mpc\(^{-1}\) s\(^{-1}\) (1\(\sigma\)), with the caveat that the inclusion of GW190425 does not improve precision but slightly shifts the median value within the uncertainty.

## Method

The authors constructed a set of 5000 equations of state (EOSs) constrained at low densities (up to 1.5 times nuclear saturation density) by chiral effective field theory calculations and at higher densities by a model-agnostic parametric expansion in the speed-of-sound plane. They then applied a multi-step Bayesian analysis that sequentially incorporated constraints from: maximum neutron-star mass limits from radio pulsars and the GW170817 remnant; the NICER mass-radius measurement of PSR J0030+0451; a reanalysis of the GW170817 waveform using the IMRPhenomPv2_NRTidalv2 model; a kilonova light-curve analysis of AT2017gfo using a Gaussian-process regression framework; and the gravitational-wave signal GW190425. For the Hubble constant measurement, they combined distance and inclination constraints from the gravitational-wave waveform, the kilonova light curve, and radio observations of the GRB170817A afterglow.

## Key numbers

* Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: \(11.75^{+0.86}_{-0.81}\) km at 90% confidence, from the full multi-messenger analysis including GW170817, AT2017gfo, GRB170817A, GW190425, NICER, and radio pulsar measurements.
* Hubble constant: \(66.2^{+4.4}_{-4.2}\) km Mpc\(^{-1}\) s\(^{-1}\) at 1\(\sigma\) uncertainty, from combining gravitational-wave, kilonova, and radio afterglow measurements of GW170817.
* Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star before including GW190425: \(11.67^{+0.95}_{-0.87}\) km at 90% confidence.
* Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star under the assumption that GW190425 produced no detectable electromagnetic signal: \(11.74^{+0.88}_{-0.77}\) km at 90% confidence.
* Total mass of GW190425: \(3.4_{-0.1}^{+0.3} M_\odot\) at 90% confidence.
* Distance to GW190425: \(159_{-71}^{+69}\) Mpc at 90% confidence level.
* Redshift of host galaxy NGC 4993: \(z = 0.009783 \pm 0.000023\).
* Upper bound on maximum neutron-star mass from the GW170817 remnant: \(M_{\rm max} \leq 2.16^{+0.17}_{-0.15} M_\odot\) at 2\(\sigma\) uncertainty.

## Context

This work extends previous multi-messenger analyses by combining gravitational-wave data from both GW170817 and GW190425 with electromagnetic observations (kilonova AT2017gfo and gamma-ray burst GRB170817A), NICER results for PSR J0030+0451, radio pulsar mass measurements, and chiral effective field theory calculations in a unified Bayesian framework. It builds upon earlier studies such as those by Annala et al. (2017), Capano et al. (2019), Coughlin et al. (2018), Radice et al. (2018), and Raaijmakers et al. (2019), which used subsets of these data. The results enable improved constraints on the neutron-star equation of state and provide an independent measurement of the Hubble constant that, while not resolving the tension between supernova and cosmic microwave background measurements, favors the Planck value.
