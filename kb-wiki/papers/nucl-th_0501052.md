# A doorway to Borromean halo nuclei: the Samba configuration

> We exploit the possibility of new configurations in three-body halo nuclei - Samba type - (the neutron-core form a bound system) as a doorway to Borromean systems. The nuclei $^{12}$Be, $^{15}$B, $^{23}$N and $^{27}$F are of such nature, in particular $^{23}$N with a half-life of 37.7 s and a halo radius of 6.07 fm is an excellent example of Samba-halo configuration. The fusion below the barrier of the Samba halo nuclei with heavy targets could reveal the so far elusive enhancement and a dominance of one-neutron over two-neutron transfers, in contrast to what was found recently for the Borromean halo nucleus $^6$He + $^{238}$U.

# A doorway to Borromean halo nuclei: the Samba configuration

## Key claim
The paper proposes that certain three-body halo nuclei, called "Samba" type, where the neutron-core subsystem is bound and only the di-neutron subsystem is unbound, serve as doorway configurations that precede Borromean halo nuclei in isotopic chains. The authors suggest that fusion below the Coulomb barrier using Samba halo nuclei with heavy targets could reveal an enhancement and a dominance of one-neutron over two-neutron transfers, in contrast to what was found for the Borromean halo nucleus $^6$He + $^{238}$U.

## Method
The authors surveyed isotopes in the proton p-shell region and fluorine isotopes using data from the Nuclear Wallet Cards (Sixth edition, 2000), identifying five candidate Samba halo nuclei: $^{12}$Be, $^{15}$B, $^{20}$C, $^{23}$N, and $^{27}$F. They calculated reduced dipole transition strengths $B(E1)$ using a simple cluster model with a Yukawa-type ground-state wave function and a plane wave for the final continuum state, and computed halo radii using a subtracted Faddeev three-body formalism with zero-range $nn$ and $n-c$ interactions, renormalized to remove ultraviolet divergences.

## Key numbers
- $^{23}$N: half-life of 37.7 s and halo radius of 6.07 fm, presented as an excellent example of a Samba-halo configuration.
- $^{15}$B: halo radius 5.15 fm, nuclear radius 2.91 fm, compared to 2.50 fm for a normal $A=15$ nucleus.
- $^{12}$Be: $B(E1)$ value of 0.043 $e^2$fm$^2$ (calculated), compared to experimental value 0.051(13) $e^2$fm$^2$ and to 0.61 $e^2$fm$^2$ for the Borromean nucleus $^6$He.
- $^{12}$Be: one-neutron separation energy $E_n = 0.504$ MeV, two-neutron separation energy $E_{2n} = 3.669$ MeV, root-mean-square neutron-CM radius $\sqrt{\langle r^2_{n-CM}\rangle} = 4.81$ fm.
- $^{15}$B: $E_n = 0.973$ MeV, $E_{2n} = 3.734$ MeV, $\sqrt{\langle r^2_{n-CM}\rangle} = 5.15$ fm.

## Context
This work extends the study of Borromean halo nuclei, such as $^{11}$Li and $^6$He reviewed by Jensen et al. (2004) and by Hansen et al. (1995), by identifying a new class of three-body halo configurations called Samba nuclei. The name is inspired by Robicheaux (1999), who described "Tango" halo systems like the hypertriton $^3_\Lambda$H$_1$ where only one two-body subsystem is bound; in Samba nuclei, two of the two-body subsystems are bound. The paper enables experimental searches for fusion enhancement using Samba halo nuclei like $^{23}$N with heavy targets such as $^{208}$Pb, in contrast to the lack of enhancement found for $^6$He + $^{238}$U by Raabe and Navin (2004). The authors also predict that $^{25}$N may exist as a Borromean halo nucleus following the Samba $^{23}$N, though no data on its separation energies or lifetime are available.
