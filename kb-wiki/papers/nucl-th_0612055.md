# Properties of stellar matter in supernova explosions and nuclear multifragmentation

> During the collapse of massive stars, and the supernova type-II explosions, stellar matter reaches densities and temperatures which are similar to the ones obtained in intermediate-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions. The nuclear multifragmentation reactions can be used for determination of properties of nuclear matter at subnuclear densities, in the region of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition. It is demonstrated that the modified properties of hot nuclei (in particular, their symmetry energy) extracted from the multifragmentation data can essentially influence nuclear composition of stellar matter. The effects on weak processes, and on the nucleosynthesis are also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the reduced symmetry energy of hot nuclei, as extracted from nuclear multifragmentation experiments (where the symmetry energy coefficient drops from about 25 MeV to approximately 15 MeV), can substantially alter the nuclear composition of stellar matter during supernova explosions, but the magnitude of the effect on weak interaction rates depends strongly on density and temperature conditions.

## Method

The authors use the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) to connect experimental multifragmentation data with astrophysical conditions, generalizing it to include electron, neutrino, and photon interactions in stellar matter. They compare fragment yields from multifragmentation of gold sources at various excitation energies with yields calculated for supernova environments at similar densities and temperatures, and compute electron-capture rates using an independent particle model with Gamow-Teller transitions.

## Key numbers

- The symmetry energy coefficient γ for hot nuclei extracted from multifragmentation of tin isotopes decreases from approximately 25 MeV (cold isolated nuclei) down to about 15 MeV for more central collisions.
- The nuclear phase diagram region accessible in multifragmentation corresponds to densities ρ ∼ 0.1 ρ₀ (where ρ₀ ≈ 0.15 fm⁻³ is normal nuclear density) and temperatures T ≈ 3–8 MeV.
- During stellar collapse and explosion, temperatures T ≈ (0.5–10) MeV and baryon densities ρ ≈ (10⁻⁵–2) ρ₀ can be reached.
- At relatively high densities ρ ∼ 0.1 ρ₀, the electron-capture rate changes only by 20–50% when adopting the reduced symmetry energy coefficient.
- At small densities, the effect of reduced γ on electron-capture rates could be dramatic, by two to three orders of magnitude.
- Isentropic trajectories for supernova explosions have entropies per baryon S/B = 1, 2, and 4.

## Context

This work extends the connection between laboratory multifragmentation studies and astrophysical supernova conditions previously explored by Botvina (2004, 2005), using the symmetry energy extraction from multifragmentation data by LeFevre and collaborators. The paper enables improved modeling of weak interaction rates and nucleosynthesis in supernova environments by incorporating experimentally constrained in-medium modifications of nuclear properties, and it builds on the statistical equilibrium approach used in nuclear reaction network calculations (Langanke, 2003; network references).
