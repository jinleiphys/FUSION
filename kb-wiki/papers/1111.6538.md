# Mapping the hydrodynamic response to the initial geometry in heavy-ion collisions

> We investigate how the initial geometry of a heavy-ion collision is transformed into final flow observables by solving event-by-event ideal hydrodynamics with realistic fluctuating initial conditions. We study quantitatively to what extent anisotropic flow (v_n) is determined by the initial eccentricity epsilon_n for a set of realistic simulations, and we discuss which definition of epsilon_n gives the best estimator of v_n. We find that the common practice of using an r^2 weight in the definition of varepsilon_n in general results in a poorer predictor of v_n than when using r^n weight, for n > 2. We similarly study the importance of additional properties of the initial state. For example, we show that in order to correctly predict v_4 and v_5 for non-central collisions, one must take into account nonlinear terms proportional to (epsilon_2)^2 and (epsilon_2)*(epsilon_3), respectively. We find that it makes no difference whether one calculates the eccentricities over a range of rapidity, or in a single slice at z=0, nor is it important whether one uses an energy or entropy density weight. This knowledge will be important for making a more direct link between experimental observables and hydrodynamic initial conditions, the latter being poorly constrained at present.

## Key claim

The paper quantitatively tests how well anisotropic flow coefficients \(v_n\) (n=2-5) can be predicted from the initial transverse geometry in event-by-event ideal hydrodynamics, finding that the common practice of using an \(r^2\) weight in the definition of eccentricity \(\varepsilon_n\) is a poorer predictor for \(n>2\) than using an \(r^n\) weight, and that for \(v_4\) and \(v_5\) in non-central collisions, nonlinear terms involving \(\varepsilon_2^2\) and \(\varepsilon_2\varepsilon_3\) respectively are required for a good prediction.

## Method

The authors performed event-by-event ideal hydrodynamic simulations of Au-Au collisions at top RHIC energy using the NeXSPheRIO code, which uses initial conditions from the NeXus event generator. For each of 150 events in 10% centrality classes plus 115 zero-impact-parameter events, they solved ideal hydrodynamics and computed \(v_2\) through \(v_5\) from approximately \(6\times10^5\) Monte-Carlo generated particles per event. They then defined a best linear estimator for each \(v_n\) from various moments of the initial transverse energy (or entropy) density profile, minimizing the mean-square error between the estimator and the calculated flow, and quantified the quality of each estimator by the ratio of the rms estimator value to the rms \(v_n\).

## Key numbers

* For elliptic flow \(v_2\), the best estimator using participant eccentricity \(\varepsilon_2\) reproduces over 80% of the rms \(v_2\) in central collisions; the proportionality constant \(k\) is approximately 0.16 and decreases slowly with centrality.
* Relative statistical errors on \(v_2\) through \(v_5\) in a single event are 3.7%, 5.7%, 9.8%, and 20% respectively; rms errors on event-plane angles \(\Psi_2\) through \(\Psi_5\) are \(1^\circ\), \(1^\circ\), \(1.5^\circ\), and \(2^\circ\).
* For triangular flow \(v_3\), the estimator using \(\varepsilon_3\) (with \(r^3\) weight) is a very good predictor; \(\varepsilon_{2,3}\) (with \(r^2\) weight) is worse below 30% centrality but slightly better above 40%.
* For quadrangular flow \(v_4\), the estimator using only \(\varepsilon_4\) gives reasonable predictions only for central collisions and becomes anticorrelated for the most peripheral points; adding a nonlinear term proportional to \(\varepsilon_2^2\) yields an excellent predictor for all centralities.
* For pentagonal flow \(v_5\), the estimator using only \(\varepsilon_5\) is poor for non-central collisions; adding a nonlinear term proportional to \(\varepsilon_2\varepsilon_3\) yields an excellent predictor for all centralities.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of how initial geometry fluctuations, first studied for elliptic flow by Alver (2006) and for triangular flow by Alver and Roland (2010), map onto final flow observables. It quantitatively tests the cumulant expansion proposed by Teaney and Yan (2010) and the alternative basis of Gubser and Yarom (2010), finding that the lowest-order moments with appropriate radial weighting are generally best for \(v_2\) and \(v_3\), while higher harmonics require nonlinear combinations. The results enable a more direct link between experimental flow data and poorly constrained models of hydrodynamic initial conditions, allowing future work to rule out or constrain specific initial-state models such as those from NeXus or other event generators.
