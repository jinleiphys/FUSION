# Uncertainties of Euclidean Time Extrapolation in Lattice Effective Field Theory

> Extrapolations in Euclidean time form a central part of Nuclear Lattice Effective Field Theory (NLEFT) calculations using the Projection Monte Carlo method, as the sign problem in many cases prevents simulations at large Euclidean time. We review the next-to-next-to-leading order NLEFT results for the alpha nuclei up to $^{28}$Si, with emphasis on the Euclidean time extrapolations, their expected accuracy and potential pitfalls. We also discuss possible avenues for improving the reliability of Euclidean time extrapolations in NLEFT.

# Uncertainties of Euclidean Time Extrapolation in Lattice Effective Field Theory

## Key claim
The Euclidean time extrapolation method in Nuclear Lattice Effective Field Theory (NLEFT) can reliably extract ground-state energies for alpha nuclei up to $^{28}$Si, but the accuracy depends critically on using multiple trial states and multi-exponential fits; the method is not adequate when trial states have only small overlap with the ground state or when sign oscillations degrade data quality quickly.

## Method
The authors perform Projection Monte Carlo (PMC) calculations using a lattice with spacing $a = 1.97$ fm and temporal spacing $a_t = 1.32$ fm in a periodic $L = 6$ cube (side length $11.82$ fm). They first apply a low-energy SU(4)-symmetric filter to generate trial states, then evolve with the full leading-order (LO) Hamiltonian and compute transient energies $E_A(t)$ and operator expectation values $X_A^\mathcal{O}(t)$. Extrapolation to infinite Euclidean time uses correlated multi-exponential fits (Eqs. 3 and 4) with $k_\mathrm{max}=3$ for $^4$He and $k_\mathrm{max}=2$ for $A \geq 8$, simultaneously fitting data from 2 to 6 distinct trial states that differ in the SU(4) coupling strength $C_\mathrm{SU(4)}$.

## Context
This work reviews and extends the NLEFT calculations of alpha nuclei from $^4$He to $^{28}$Si previously reported by Epelbaum et al. (2011, 2012) and the Hoyle state studies. The Euclidean time extrapolation method addresses the sign problem that limits direct PMC simulations at large Euclidean time, a challenge common to auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo approaches. The multi-trial-state "triangulation" technique improves upon single-state extrapolations, which the authors show can suffer from spurious early convergence and inconsistent results (e.g., for $^{12}$C, single-trial-state fits with $k_\mathrm{max}=1$ give LO energies ranging from $-92.1(2)$ to $-95.0(2)$ MeV). The method enables future applications to more difficult cases by suggesting coupled multi-channel projections (the adiabatic projection method of Rupak (2013) and Pine (2013)) that could accelerate convergence by evolving multiple initial states simultaneously.
