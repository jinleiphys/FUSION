# On the ill-posed character of the Lorentz integral transform

> An exact inversion formula for the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) is provided together with the spectrum of the LIT kernel. The exponential increase of the inverse Fourier transform of the LIT kernel entering the inversion formula explains the ill-posed character of the LIT approach. Also the continuous spectrum of the LIT kernel, which approaches zero points necessarily to the same defect. A possible cure is discussed and numerically illustrated.

## Key claim

The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) is inherently ill-posed because the inverse Fourier transform of its kernel grows exponentially, but this ill-posed character can be "healed" without any regularization technique if the threshold behaviour of the unknown response function is known from general physical principles and used to replace the Fourier transform of the LIT for large arguments.

## Method

The authors derive an exact inversion formula for the LIT by Fourier-transforming the convolution integral, yielding R(x) = (1/2π) ∫ dk [L̃(k)/K̃_LIT(k)] e^{-ikx} where K̃_LIT(k) = √(1/2π) e^{-σ_i|k|}. They numerically test this inversion on a model response R(x) = √x e^{-ax} Θ(x) with a=0.05 MeV⁻¹, using Filon's integration formula with 20001 grid points, and simulate realistic numerical errors by truncating the LIT values to three significant digits. They then replace the numerically unstable Fourier components of the LIT for k > k_thres with an asymptotic form derived from the known threshold behaviour of the response.

## Key numbers

- For the test response R(x) = √x e^{-ax} with a=0.05 MeV⁻¹, using N=20001 mesh points and x_max=3000 MeV, the numerical error in the real part of the Fourier transform is about 1.7% at k=0.8 MeV⁻¹ and about 6.9% at k=2.0 MeV⁻¹.
- In calculation A (k_max=0.8 MeV⁻¹, high-precision LIT), the inversion yields ε=2.2×10⁻³ (L₂ norm relative error).
- In calculation B (LIT truncated to three significant digits, k_max=0.8 MeV⁻¹), the inversion yields ε=5×10⁻² with large oscillations.
- In calculation C (asymptotic correction for k>k_thres=0.25 MeV⁻¹, k_max=10 MeV⁻¹), the inversion yields ε=4.2×10⁻⁴ with oscillations removed.
- The Fourier transform of the LIT kernel is K̃_LIT(k)=√(1/2π) e^{-σ_i|k|}, with spectrum μ_LIT(k₀)=e^{-σ_i|k₀|}.

## Context

This work extends the mathematical understanding of the LIT method, which has been extensively applied in photo- and electronuclear physics for ab initio calculations on few-nucleon systems beyond mass number A=4 (Efros et al., 2007). The paper provides the first exact inversion formula for the LIT and explicitly demonstrates that the ill-posed character arises from the exponential growth of the inverse kernel at large k, not from small-k behaviour. The proposed cure, using known threshold behaviour to supply asymptotic Fourier components, enables inversion without the regularization techniques (such as basis-function expansions with limited N, as discussed in Andreasi and Leidemann, 2005) that previously limited numerical resolution.
