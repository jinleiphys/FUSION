# Bose-Einstein correlations and thermal cluster formation in high-energy collisions

> The blast wave model is generalized to include the production of thermal clusters, as suggested by the apparent success of the statistical model of particle production at high energies. The formulae for the HBT correlation functions and the corresponding HBT radii are derived.

# Bose-Einstein correlations and thermal cluster formation in high-energy collisions

## Key claim

The blast wave model is generalized to include the production of thermal clusters as an intermediate step between freeze-out and particle emission, and the authors derive formulae for HBT correlation functions and radii under the assumption that the cluster distribution in space-time is described by an arbitrary positive function g(x*) normalized to unity, with the cluster's finite size and lifetime modifying the interpretation of HBT measurements.

## Method

The authors start from the blast wave model featuring a boost-invariant, azimuthally symmetric fluid expanding transversely according to the Hubble law, and generalize the Cooper-Frye formula to include thermal clusters. The source function is written as an integrated product of the cluster distribution (depending only on transverse distance r_c) and the particle emission distribution from each cluster, where the cluster four-velocity follows the Hubble-like transverse expansion with parameter ω. The HBT correlation functions are then evaluated from the Fourier transform of the source function, and the HBT radii are extracted from the Gaussian fit to the correlation function separately for the long, side, and out directions.

## Context

This work extends the blast wave model originally introduced by Siemens and Rasmussen (1979) and adapted to ultra-relativistic energies by Schnedermann, Sollfrank, and Heinz (1993), as well as similar studies by Retiere and Lisa (2004) and Kisiel et al. (2006) for heavy-ion collisions. The approach differs from Torrieri et al. (2007), who assumed Gaussian particle distributions within clusters, by allowing arbitrary particle distributions within a cluster, and from Zhang et al. (2007), who described each cluster's evolution hydrodynamically, by modeling a single large hydrodynamic system breaking into clusters then into pions. The derived formulae provide tools to extract cluster parameters from HBT measurements, potentially enabling verification of the statistical model of particle production on a more fundamental level.
