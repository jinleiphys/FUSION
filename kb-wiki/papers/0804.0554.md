# Searching for Neutrinos from WIMP Annihilations in the Galactic Stellar Disk

> Weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs) are a viable candidate for the relic abundance of dark matter (DM) produced in the early universe. So far WIMPs have eluded direct detection through interactions with baryonic matter. Neutrino emission from accumulated WIMP annihilations in the solar core has been proposed as a signature of DM, but has not yet been detected. These null results may be due to small scale DM density fluctuations in the halo with the density of our local region being lower than the average (around 0.3 GeV/cm^3 ). However, the accumulated neutrino signal from WIMP annihilations in the Galactic stellar disk would be insensitive to local density variations. Inside the disk, dark matter can be captured by stars causing an enhanced annihilation rate and therefore a potentially higher neutrino flux than what would be observed from elsewhere in the halo. We estimate a neutrino flux from the WIMP annihilations in the stellar disk to be enhanced by more than an order of magnitude compared to the neutrino fluxes from the halo. We offer a conservative estimate for this enhanced flux, based on the WIMP-nucleon cross-sections obtained from direct-detection experiments by assuming a density of around 0.3 GeV/cm^3 for the local DM. We also compare the detectability of these fluxes with a signal of diffuse high energy neutrinos produced in the Milky Way by the interaction of cosmic rays (CRs) with the interstellar medium (ISM). These comparative signals should be observable by large neutrino detectors.

## Key claim

The accumulated neutrino signal from WIMP annihilations in the Galactic stellar disk is estimated to be enhanced by more than an order of magnitude compared to neutrino fluxes from the halo, but this estimate is conservative because it relies on WIMP-nucleon cross-sections obtained from direct-detection experiments that assume a local dark matter density of around 0.3 GeV/cm^3, which may be lower than the true value due to small-scale density fluctuations.

## Method

The authors calculated the neutrino flux from WIMP annihilations in stars by integrating the emissivity along lines of sight in the Galactic disk midplane, using a Navarro-Frenk-White profile for the dark matter density and a sech-squared profile for the stellar mass density. They assumed WIMP capture and annihilation rates are in equilibrium, used a top quark decay chain (t -> W -> nu) for neutrino production, and applied an exponential survival probability for neutrinos escaping the stellar core with a transparency energy of 150 GeV. They compared this flux to the neutrino flux from general dark matter annihilations in the halo and from cosmic ray interactions with the interstellar medium, using a parameterized emissivity based on a power-law cosmic ray spectrum with spectral index 1.63.

## Key numbers

- Local dark matter density assumed: 0.3 GeV/cm^3 (used as the average value for the Milky Way halo)
- Dark matter density in the local region: 6.5 x 10^-25 g/cm^3 (rho_odot)
- Scale radius for NFW profile: 20 kpc
- Thickness of the Galactic disk: 200 pc
- Surface mass density of stars at Galactic center: 567 M_sun/pc^2 (mu_o)
- WIMP-nucleon cross-section used: 0.5 - 1 pb (conservative estimate from direct-detection experiments)
- Dark matter annihilation cross-section: 2 x 10^-26 cm^3/s
- Neutrino energy threshold for detector: 50 GeV (E_min)
- Transparency energy for neutrino escape from stars: 150 GeV (E_k)
- For M_chi = 1000 GeV or lower, the neutrino signal from WIMP annihilation in stars is above the flux from CR-ISM interactions for approximately 5 to 10 degrees from the Galactic center
- For E_min = 250 GeV or lower, the neutrino signal from WIMP annihilation in stars is above the flux from CR-ISM interactions for approximately 5 to 10 degrees from the Galactic center

## Context

This work extends the analyses done on solar capture and annihilation of WIMPs (Press and Spergel 1985; Silk, Olive, and Srednicki 1985) to all stars in the Milky Way disk, motivated by null results from direct detection experiments and solar neutrino searches that may be explained by local dark matter density fluctuations (Taylor and Babul 2005). The authors compare their predicted neutrino fluxes to the diffuse high-energy neutrino signal from cosmic ray interactions with the interstellar medium, which has been studied by Berezinsky and Zatsepin (1970), Berezinsky and Smirnov (1975), and Stecker (1979). The results suggest that upcoming large neutrino detectors such as IceCube and ANTARES could potentially observe these signals, enabling a new indirect detection method for dark matter that is insensitive to local density variations.
