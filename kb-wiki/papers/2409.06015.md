# Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

> One of the most compelling pieces of evidence of the Hot Big Bang model is the realisation and confirmation that some nuclides were created shortly after the Big Bang. This process is referred to as Big Bang nucleosynthesis (or, sometimes, primordial nucleosynthesis), and is the end-product of putting neutrons and protons in a hot, expanding Universe. Big Bang nucleosynthesis currently provides our earliest test of cosmology, and it is the only experiment currently designed that is simultaneously sensitive to all four known fundamental forces: the gravitational force, the electromagnetic force, the strong force and the weak force. Our theoretical understanding of Big Bang nucleosynthesis and the measurement of the primordial abundances together represents one of the strongest pillars of the standard cosmological model. In this chapter, we will develop an intuitive understanding of Big Bang nucleosynthesis, discuss modern calculations of this process, and provide a summary of the current state-of-the-art measurements that have been made. Overall, Big Bang nucleosynthesis is in remarkable agreement with various cosmological probes, and it is this agreement that serves to strengthen our confidence in the general picture of cosmology that we have today.

## Key claim

Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) is in remarkable agreement with various cosmological probes, but this agreement depends on adopting the baryon-to-photon ratio derived from the cosmic microwave background (CMB), which makes BBN a parameter-free theory; any deviation from the Standard Model expansion rate parameterized by \(N_{\rm eff}=3.043\) would indicate physics not captured by the current modern perspective.

## Method

The chapter develops an intuitive analytical calculation of BBN by first determining the neutron-to-proton ratio from weak interaction freeze-out at \(k_{\rm B}T \simeq 0.8\) MeV, then tracking the deuteron bottleneck and the subsequent nuclear reaction network that synthesizes \(^{4}\)He, \(^{3}\)He, \(^{3}\)H, \(^{7}\)Li, and \(^{7}\)Be. Modern precision calculations solve the coupled Boltzmann equations with a full nuclear reaction network, accounting for the expansion rate set by the radiation density (photons, electron-positron pairs, and neutrinos) and the baryon-to-photon ratio \(\eta\), which is the only free parameter.

## Key numbers

- Neutron-to-proton ratio at freeze-out: \(N_{\rm n}/N_{\rm p} \sim 1/5\) (when \(k_{\rm B}T \simeq 0.8\) MeV).
- Neutron mean lifetime: \(\tau_n \sim 880\) s (half-life \(\sim 610\) s).
- Deuterium binding energy: \(k_{\rm B}T = 2.224\) MeV; deuteron bottleneck overcome when photon temperature drops to \(k_{\rm B}T \simeq 0.06\) MeV, roughly 300 s after freeze-out.
- Primordial helium number abundance: \(y_{\rm P} \simeq 0.08\); corresponding mass fraction \(Y_{\rm P} \simeq 0.24\).
- Standard Model effective number of neutrino species: \(N_{\rm eff} = 3.043\) (after accounting for incomplete neutrino decoupling during electron-positron annihilation).
- Relation between baryon-to-photon ratio and present-day baryon density: \(\eta = (273.78 \pm 0.18) \times 10^{-10} \times \Omega_{\rm B,0} h^{2}\).
- Freeze-out times: \(^{1}\)H and \(^{4}\)He freeze out \(\sim 6\) minutes after the Big Bang; \(^{7}\)Be freezes out at \(\sim 4\) hours; \(^{2}\)H, \(^{3}\)H, \(^{3}\)He, and \(^{7}\)Li freeze out at \(\sim 8\)–\(10\) hours.
- \(^{7}\)Be decays to \(^{7}\)Li via electron capture starting at redshift \(z \sim 35,000\) (\(\sim 600\) years after the Big Bang) until \(z \sim 27,500\) (\(\sim 1000\) years after the Big Bang).

## Context

This chapter provides a pedagogical review of BBN, extending the foundational calculations of Alpher, Bethe, and Gamow (1948) and the later refined works of Hoyle and Tayler (1964), Peebles (1966), and Wagoner, Fowler, and Hoyle (1967). It enables precision tests of cosmology by linking BBN predictions to CMB measurements (e.g., from WMAP) and to observational determinations of primordial abundances from quasar absorption line spectroscopy, H II regions, and stellar abundances (e.g., the Spite Plateau for lithium). The chapter also highlights that the absence of stable nuclides with mass number 5 or 8 prevents production of elements heavier than \(^{7}\)Li during BBN, a key constraint that future work on non-standard physics (e.g., extra relativistic species parameterized by \(\Delta N_{\nu}\)) must respect.
