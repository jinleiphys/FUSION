# Steps Toward Quantum Simulations of Hadronization and Energy-Loss in Dense Matter

> A framework for simulating the real-time dynamics of composite particles in a simple model of dense matter that is amenable to quantum computers is developed. As a demonstration, we perform classical simulations of heavy-hadrons propagating through a dense medium in the Schwinger model. Measurements of the time-dependent energy and charge density are used to identify mechanisms responsible for energy loss and hadron production (hadronization). A study of entanglement dynamics highlights the importance of quantum coherence between the particles that make up the dense medium. Throughout this work, care is taken to isolate, and remove, phenomena that arise solely from a finite lattice spacing. It is found that signatures of entanglement are more sensitive to lattice artifacts than other observables. Toward quantum simulations, we present an efficient method and the corresponding quantum circuits for preparing ground states in the presence of heavy mesons. These circuits are used to estimate the resources required to simulate in-medium energy loss and hadronization in the Schwinger model using quantum computers.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that classical simulations of heavy-hadrons propagating through dense matter in the Schwinger model can identify energy-loss and hadronization mechanisms, but the authors emphasize that lattice artifacts from broken Lorentz symmetry produce spurious energy loss and hadron production even in vacuum, requiring careful subtraction; the framework is presented as a step toward quantum simulations rather than a complete solution.

## Method

The authors simulate real-time dynamics of heavy external charges (analogous to heavy quarks in HQET) moving along classical trajectories through the Schwinger model on a 1+1D lattice with Kogut-Susskind staggered fermions and open boundary conditions. They compute the time-dependent state via Trotterized time evolution, measuring total energy, charge density, and entanglement measures (single-site entropy and mutual information) as functions of the heavy-charge position, while isolating lattice artifacts by comparing vacuum propagation to propagation through a medium of static heavy charges.

## Key numbers

- System sizes limited to L=12 spatial sites (24 staggered sites) for classical simulations
- Hadron mass inverse ranges from 1.3 to 1.8 in lattice units, ensuring confinement length is well contained within the simulation volume
- Time step Δt=0.25 used for velocities v>0.4, increasing to Δt=2.0 for v≤0.05
- Maximum group velocity of light degrees of freedom given by v⋆ = (1/2)(√(m²+1)-m), which reduces to c=1/2 in the m→0 limit
- Energy loss in vacuum shows quadratic dependence on velocity at low v, scaling as O(a²v²) from Lorentz-breaking operators
- Heavy hadron mass Λ̅ scales as ~a, while vacuum energy loss ΔE/Δx scales at least as ~a³

## Context

This work extends previous classical simulations of jet and hadron production in the Schwinger model (Li 2020, Bauer 2021, Barata 2021, Florio 2023) and string breaking studies (Hebenstreit 2013, Pichler 2015, Sala 2018, Magnifico 2019) by explicitly isolating lattice artifacts in energy-loss and entanglement observables. The authors build on their prior work (Farrell 2024) showing that confinement allows truncation of long-range Coulomb interactions to O(ξL) gates, making large-scale quantum simulations feasible. The results enable future quantum simulations of in-medium energy loss and hadronization by providing validated quantum circuits for ground-state preparation in the presence of heavy mesons, with resource estimates for scaling beyond the L=12 limit of classical computation.
