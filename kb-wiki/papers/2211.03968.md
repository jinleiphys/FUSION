# Electromagnetic fields in ultra-peripheral relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Ultraperipheral heavy-ion collisions (UPCs) offer unique opportunities to study processes under strong electromagnetic fields. In these collisions, highly charged fast-moving ions carry strong electromagnetic fields that can be effectively treated as photon fluxes. The exchange of photons can induce photonuclear and two-photon interactions, and excite ions. This excitation of the ions results in Coulomb dissociation with the emission of photons, neutrons, and other particles. Additionally, the electromagnetic fields generated by the ions can be sufficiently strong to enforce mutual interactions between the two colliding ions. Consequently, the two colliding ions experienced an electromagnetic force that pushed them in opposite directions, causing a back-to-back correlation in the emitted neutrons. Using a Monte Carlo simulation, we qualitatively demonstrated that the above electromagnetic effect is large enough to be observed in UPCs, which would provide a clear means to study strong electromagnetic fields and their effects.

## Key claim

Using a Monte Carlo simulation, the authors qualitatively demonstrate that the electromagnetic fields generated in ultra-peripheral heavy-ion collisions (UPCs) are strong enough to produce a measurable back-to-back correlation in the transverse momentum of neutrons emitted from Coulomb dissociation of the two colliding nuclei, providing a clean means to detect strong electromagnetic fields without the obscuring effects of hadronic interactions.

## Method

The authors performed Monte Carlo simulations for Au+Au UPCs at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 200\) GeV, calculating the electromagnetic fields from the Liénard-Wiechert potentials and the resulting momentum transfer per nucleon via integration of the Lorentz force. Neutron emission probabilities and multiplicities were modeled using the STARlight methodology with Woods-Saxon nuclear density distributions, and neutron energies were generated using Evaluated Nuclear Data File (ENDF) tables for \(^{208}\)Pb as a proxy for Au. The correlation between the first-order harmonic planes constructed from neutrons emitted from each nucleus was then analyzed.

## Key numbers

- The electric and magnetic fields produced by one Au nucleus acting on another at impact parameter \(b = 20\) fm and \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 200\) GeV are approximately \(eE \sim 0.2 m_{\pi}^2\) and \(eB \sim 0.2 m_{\pi}^2\).
- The Lorentz factor \(\gamma\) for Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 200\) GeV is approximately 107.
- The estimated transverse momentum per nucleon induced by the electromagnetic field is approximately 2 MeV, based on \(\Delta p = Z(eE + v_z eB)\Delta t / A \approx 0.4 m_{\pi}^2 \times 0.12\,{\rm fm} \times 79/197 \sim 2\) MeV, with \(Z = 79\), \(A = 197\), and \(R = 6.38\) fm for Au.
- The excitation energy of the giant dipole resonance (GDR) for heavy nuclei is approximately 10-14 MeV, and the emitted neutrons have momenta of approximately 10 MeV.
- For Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 200\) GeV at RHIC, a neutron with a maximum transverse momentum of 140 MeV/c corresponds to a deflection of approximately 2.5 cm, within the acceptance of the zero-degree calorimeters (ZDCs) positioned \(\pm 18\) m from the interaction point.

## Context

This work extends the study of electromagnetic processes in UPCs, which have previously been used to study photonuclear and two-photon interactions producing particles such as \(e^+e^-\), \(\pi^+\pi^-\), and vector mesons (STAR, PHENIX, ALICE, CMS, ATLAS, 2002-2019). The proposed back-to-back neutron correlation provides a new observable to directly detect the strong electromagnetic fields, which are otherwise extremely challenging to measure experimentally, and offers a method to determine the impact parameter direction. This could enable future studies of spin-polarization-related physics and shed light on chiral and spin-related effects in hadronic heavy-ion collisions, such as the chiral magnetic effect (Kharzeev, 1998; Huang, 2015) and hyperon spin polarization (Liang, 2004; STAR, 2017).
