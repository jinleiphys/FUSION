# High Energy Particles in the Solar Corona

> Collective Ampere law interactions producing magnetic flux tubes piercing through sunspots into and then out of the solar corona allow for low energy nuclear reactions in a steady state and high energy particle reactions if a magnetic flux tube explodes in a violent event such as a solar flare. Filamentous flux tubes themselves are vortices of Ampere currents circulating around in a tornado fashion in a roughly cylindrical geometry. The magnetic field lines are parallel to and largely confined within the core of the vortex. The vortices may thereby be viewed as long current carrying coils surrounding magnetic flux and subject to inductive Faraday and Ampere laws. These laws set the energy scales of (i) low energy solar nuclear reactions which may regularly occur and (ii) high energy electro-weak interactions which occur when magnetic flux coils explode into violent episodic events such as solar flares or coronal mass ejections.

## Key claim

Magnetic flux tubes in the solar corona can act as step-up transformers, transferring kinetic energy collectively from many low-energy charged particles in the photosphere to a smaller number of high-energy particles in the corona; this mechanism can produce particle energies up to ~300 GeV during explosive events like solar flares, and also enables steady-state low-energy nuclear reactions in non-exploding flux tubes.

## Method

The authors model magnetic flux tubes as long current-carrying coils (vortices of Ampere currents) with magnetic field lines confined within the core, subject to Faraday and Ampere laws. They treat the photosphere as a primary coil and the corona as a secondary coil in a transformer circuit, using the Faraday law voltage pulse from magnetic flux loss during a flux tube explosion to estimate the energy scale of accelerated particles. For steady-state low-energy reactions, they compute the magnetic energy available from small changes in circulating current to drive inverse beta decay (electron-proton capture producing a neutron and neutrino).

## Key numbers

- Solar flare/coronal mass ejection energy scale: \( e\overline{\rm V} \sim 300\ {\rm GeV} \), derived from \( R \sim 10^4\ {\rm km} \), \( B \sim 1\ {\rm kG} \), \( \Delta t \sim 10^2\ {\rm s} \), and \( \Lambda \sim 3\times 10^7\ {\rm km} \)
- Conversion factor: \( ecB \equiv 29.9792458\ [{\rm GeV/km}] \times [B/{\rm kG}] \)
- Steady-state low-energy reaction magnetic energy: \( W_{\rm magnetic} \sim 15\ {\rm GeV} \), from \( R \sim 10^2\ {\rm km} \), \( B \sim 1\ {\rm kG} \), \( v/c \sim 10^{-2} \)
- Relativistic particles observed from solar flares can reach up to \( \sim 10^2\ {\rm GeV} \)

## Context

This work extends the betatron acceleration concept (Kerst 1941, Serber 1941) to solar magnetic flux tubes, proposing that transformer action can explain both the high temperature of the solar corona and the production of relativistic particles in flares and coronal mass ejections. It builds on observations of magnetic flux tubes (Hale 1908, Hale 1919, Priest 1998) and solar flare particle events (Forbush 1946, Dorman 1993, Reams 2004, Belov 2005, Bostanjyan 2007). The mechanism suggests that nuclear reactions may occur well above the solar core, potentially explaining ground-level cosmic ray enhancements (Shea 2001, Cliver 2006) and offering an alternative to the standard picture where only core fusion powers the sun.
