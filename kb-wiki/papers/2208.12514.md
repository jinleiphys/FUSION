# The lambda hyperon and the hyperon puzzle

> Neutron stars provide unique conditions to study cold dense nuclear matter at extreme densities. Due to these extreme conditions additional hadronic degrees of freedom are expected to be populated,including hyperons. This talk will focus on the influence of hyperons on the neutron star equation ofstate. In particular the contribution of the lambda hyperon will be discussed, as a first approximation to describing exotic neutron star equations of state. The system under consideration is where the strong nuclear force is described by the exchange of mesons and applying the relativistic mean field theory to study dense nuclear matter. As expected, the inclusion of the lambda hyperon softens the neutron star equation of state (EoS). A softer EoS will reduce the maximum mass attainable by the modeled neutron star with such EoS. While hyperons are certainly not unexpected in high density systems, but there presence seems to be contradicted by observations of high mass neutron stars. This contradiction is known as the "hyperon puzzle". The expected influx of observational data from massive new radio-telescopes like the Square Kilometer Array (SKA) will provide observations that can be supported and evolve theoretical models of nuclear matter. Therefore, the study of hyperonic matter is not only relevant to nuclear theory, but also locally to Botswana as an African partner country of the SKA.

## Key claim

The inclusion of the lambda hyperon softens the neutron star equation of state (EoS) and reduces the maximum attainable mass, but the severity of this softening and the resulting "hyperon puzzle" is sensitive to the choice of nuclear matter parameter set (QHD-I versus NL3), meaning the contradiction with high-mass pulsar observations may be model-dependent rather than absolute.

## Method

The authors applied relativistic mean field (RMF) theory to describe the strong nuclear force via exchange of scalar sigma, omega vector, and isovector rho mesons, using the QHD-I and NL3 parameter sets for nucleon-meson coupling strengths. They calculated the equation of state for three systems: pure neutron matter, beta-equilibrated charge neutral matter without hyperons, and beta-equilibrated charge neutral matter including the lambda hyperon. The resulting EoS were then used in the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to obtain mass-radius relationships for neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- The lambda hyperon has a bare mass of 1115 MeV, compared to the nucleon mass of 939 MeV.
- For the QHD-I parameter set, lambda hyperon states begin to populate at a baryon density of 1.69 times the nuclear saturation density (rho_sat = 0.153 fm^-3).
- For the NL3 parameter set, lambda hyperons enter the system at 2.31 times the saturation density.
- The observed pulsars PSR J1614-2230 has a mass of (1.97 +/- 0.04) solar masses and PSR J0348+0432 has a mass of (2.01 +/- 0.04) solar masses.
- Neutron star densities are in the range of 4 to 8 times the nuclear saturation density.
- Neutron star radii are in the range of 10 to 12 km, with masses around 1 to 2 solar masses.

## Context

This work extends the study of the "hyperon puzzle" first identified in the context of relativistic mean field models by Serot (1992) and Glendenning (2000), where the inclusion of hyperons softens the EoS and produces maximum masses incompatible with observations of high-mass pulsars by Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013). The authors show that the density at which hyperons appear depends on the parameter set (QHD-I versus NL3), suggesting that the puzzle may be mitigated by model choice. This enables future work to include the full baryon octet and to use observational data from the Square Kilometer Array (SKA) to constrain theoretical models.
