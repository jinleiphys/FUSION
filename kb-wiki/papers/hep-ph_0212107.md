# Dynamical renormalization group approach to transport in ultrarelativistic plasmas: the electrical conductivity in high temperature QED

> The DC electrical conductivity of an ultrarelativistic QED plasma is studied in real time by implementing the dynamical renormalization group. The conductivity is obtained from the realtime dependence of a dissipative kernel related to the retarded photon polarization. Pinch singularities in the imaginary part of the polarization are manifest as growing secular terms that in the perturbative expansion of this kernel. The leading secular terms are studied explicitly and it is shown that they are insensitive to the anomalous damping of hard fermions as a result of a cancellation between self-energy and vertex corrections. The resummation of the secular terms via the dynamical renormalization group leads directly to a renormalization group equation in real time, which is the Boltzmann equation for the (gauge invariant) fermion distribution function. A direct correspondence between the perturbative expansion and the linearized Boltzmann equation is established, allowing a direct identification of the self energy and vertex contributions to the collision term.We obtain a Fokker-Planck equation in momentum space that describes the dynamics of the departure from equilibrium to leading logarithmic order in the coupling.This determines that the transport time scale is given by t_{tr}=(24 pi)/[e^4 T \ln(1/e)}]. The solution of the Fokker-Planck equation approaches asymptotically the steady- state solution as sim e^{-t/(4.038 t_{tr})}.The steady-state solution leads to the conductivity sigma = 15.698 T/[e^2 ln(1/e)] to leading logarithmic order. We discuss the contributions beyond leading logarithms as well as beyond the Boltzmann equation. The dynamical renormalization group provides a link between linear response in quantum field theory and kinetic theory.

## Key claim

The DC electrical conductivity of an ultrarelativistic QED plasma is computed to leading logarithmic order via the dynamical renormalization group, which resums secular terms (the real-time manifestation of pinch singularities) and yields a Fokker-Planck equation whose steady-state solution gives the conductivity; the result is insensitive to the anomalous damping of hard fermions due to a cancellation between self-energy and vertex corrections.

## Method

The authors study the real-time relaxation of an initially prepared magnetic field fluctuation as an initial value problem in linear response, relating the DC conductivity to the time integral of a dissipative kernel derived from the retarded photon polarization. They identify secular terms in the perturbative expansion of this kernel, resum them via the dynamical renormalization group, and show that the resulting renormalization group equation in real time is the Boltzmann equation for the gauge-invariant fermion distribution function. The leading logarithmic contribution to the collision term is expanded in powers of momentum exchange to obtain a Fokker-Planck equation, which is solved by expansion in eigenfunctions of a positive definite Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

* Transport time scale: \( t_\mathrm{tr} = \frac{24\pi}{e^4 T \ln(1/e)} \)
* Asymptotic approach to steady state: \( \sim e^{-t/(4.038\, t_\mathrm{tr})} \)
* DC electrical conductivity to leading logarithmic order: \( \sigma = \frac{15.698\, T}{e^2 \ln(1/e)} \)

## Context

This work extends the program of calculating transport coefficients from microscopic quantum field theory, building on the ladder-diagram resummation approach of Basagoiti (2002) and Aarts (2002) which established equivalence with the steady-state Boltzmann equation, and on the kinetic-theory calculations of Baym (1990) and Heiselberg (1991) using hard thermal loop resummation. The dynamical renormalization group provides a direct real-time link between linear response in quantum field theory and the time-dependent Boltzmann equation, enabling systematic inclusion of corrections beyond leading logarithmic order and beyond the Boltzmann equation itself.
