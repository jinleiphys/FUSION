# Concurrent tests of Lorentz invariance in $β$-decay experiments

> Modern experiments on neutron and allowed nuclear $β$ decay search for new semileptonic interactions, beyond the ``left-handed'' electroweak force. We show that ongoing and planned $β$-decay experiments, with isotopes at rest and in flight, can be exploited as sensitive tests of Lorentz invariance. The variety of correlations that involve the nuclear spin, the direction of the emitted $β$ particle, and the recoil direction of the daughter nucleus allow for relatively simple experiments that give direct bounds on Lorentz violation. The pertinent observables are decay-rate asymmetries and their dependence on sidereal time. We discuss the potential of several asymmetries that together cover a large part of the parameter space for Lorentz violation in the gauge sector. High counting statistics is required.

## Key claim

Ongoing and planned experiments on neutron and allowed nuclear beta decay, with isotopes at rest and in flight, can be exploited as sensitive tests of Lorentz invariance, provided that decay-rate asymmetries are recorded with sidereal-time stamps and that high counting statistics is achieved.

## Method

The authors derive the allowed-beta-decay rate including Lorentz violation from a modified low-energy W-boson propagator, parameterized by a tensor chi. They identify specific decay-rate asymmetries that are proportional to components of chi, such as the Fermi asymmetry A_F, the Gamow-Teller asymmetry A_GT, and spin-dependent asymmetries A_J, A_betanu, and A_Jbeta. These asymmetries are expressed in terms of the Lorentz-violating tensor components in the Sun-centered frame, where they oscillate with the sidereal rotation frequency of Earth. The analysis also considers the enhancement of sensitivity from Lorentz boosts when decaying nuclei are in flight, e.g., at proposed beta-beam facilities.

## Key numbers

*   The best existing upper bounds on linear combinations of the Lorentz-violating components X^{munu} are of order 10^{-6} to 10^{-8}, derived from forbidden beta-decay experiments.
*   A statistical precision of 10^{-n} requires at least 10^{2n} events; for an experiment like that of Newman (1976), this would require one year of data taking with a source of 1 Curie.
*   The most recent measurement of the spin asymmetry A_J in the beta+ decay of 20Na gives |tilde{chi}_i^{x,y}| < 5 x 10^{-4} with 90% confidence (Sytema 2015).
*   Comparing the best value of the longitudinal beta polarization ratio P_F/P_GT = 1.0010(27) to the Standard Model prediction gives -1.3 x 10^{-3} < X^{00}_r < 2.0 x 10^{-3} with 90% confidence.
*   Measuring the asymmetries A_Jbeta or A_betanu better than 10^{-6} requires coincident event rates exceeding 3 x 10^4 per second for a year.
*   A beta-beam facility using 6He could achieve production rates of 10^{12}/s with a Lorentz factor gamma = 100 (Autin 2003).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for Lorentz and CPT violation in beta decay developed by Noordmans (2013a, 2013b) and Vos (2015a). It builds on experimental bounds from forbidden beta decays (Newman 1976, Ullman 1978) and a first experiment on allowed beta decay with polarized 20Na (Muller 2013, Sytema 2015). The paper shows that ongoing high-precision allowed-beta-decay experiments searching for new semileptonic interactions can, with moderate modifications, simultaneously provide competitive tests of Lorentz invariance, covering a large part of the parameter space for Lorentz violation in the gauge sector.
