# Nuclear charge radii: Density functional theory meets Bayesian neural networks

> The distribution of electric charge in atomic nuclei is fundamental to our understanding of the complex nuclear dynamics and a quintessential observable to validate nuclear structure models. We explore a novel approach that combines sophisticated models of nuclear structure with Bayesian neural networks (BNN) to generate predictions for the charge radii of thousands of nuclei throughout the nuclear chart. A class of relativistic energy density functionals is used to provide robust predictions for nuclear charge radii. In turn, these predictions are refined through Bayesian learning for a neural network that is trained using residuals between theoretical predictions and the experimental data. Although predictions obtained with density functional theory provide a fairly good description of experiment, our results show significant improvement (better than 40%) after BNN refinement. Moreover, these improved results for nuclear charge radii are supplemented with theoretical error bars. We have successfully demonstrated the ability of the BNN approach to significantly increase the accuracy of nuclear models in the predictions of nuclear charge radii. However, as many before us, we failed to uncover the underlying physics behind the intriguing behavior of charge radii along the calcium isotopic chain.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that combining relativistic density functional theory with Bayesian neural network (BNN) refinement reduces the root-mean-square deviation between predicted and experimental nuclear charge radii by better than 40% across the nuclear chart, but they explicitly acknowledge that this combined approach fails to uncover the underlying physics behind the anomalous behavior of charge radii along the calcium isotopic chain.

## Method

The authors first compute nuclear charge radii for nuclei with Z≥20 and A≥40 using three relativistic energy density functionals (NL3, FSUGold, FSUGarnet) and two macroscopic models (extended-liquid-drop and Zhang parametrization). They then train a feed-forward Bayesian neural network with 40 hidden nodes on the residuals between theoretical predictions and experimental data from the 2004 Angeli compilation (722 nuclei), using Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling to generate posterior distributions for the network weights. The trained network is validated against 98 additional nuclei from the 2013 Angeli compilation that were not used in training.

## Key numbers

- The BNN refinement reduced the rms deviation for the validation set from 0.0412 fm to 0.0280 fm for the NL3 functional, a 32% improvement
- For the FSUGold functional, the validation set rms deviation improved from 0.0432 fm to 0.0298 fm, a 31% improvement
- The extended-liquid-drop model showed the largest relative improvement on the learning set: from 0.0628 fm to 0.0210 fm, a 67% reduction
- The Zhang parametrization on the learning set improved from 0.0384 fm to 0.0207 fm, a 46% reduction
- The experimental database consisted of 820 nuclei beyond Ca-40, with 722 in the learning set (2004 compilation) and 98 in the validation set (2013 compilation)
- The neural network used 161 connection weights (1+4H with H=40 hidden nodes) calibrated from 500 thermalization sets and 100 accumulated iterations

## Context

This work extends the BNN approach previously applied to nuclear masses by Utama et al. (2015) to the prediction of nuclear charge radii, building on the Strutinsky energy theorem framework that separates nuclear observables into smooth and fluctuating components. The authors compare their global BNN method against local Garvey-Kelson relations developed by Piekarewicz (2009) for charge radii, showing that BNN provides superior extrapolation far from known regions, particularly for neutron-deficient lead isotopes where the Garvey-Kelson relations require iterative predictions that propagate errors. The approach enables predictions with quantified uncertainties for thousands of nuclei across the nuclear chart, though the calcium isotope puzzle identified by Ruiz et al. (2016) remains unresolved.
