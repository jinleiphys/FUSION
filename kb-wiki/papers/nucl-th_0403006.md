# Missing levels in correlated spectra

> Complete spectroscopy (measurements of a complete sequence of consecutive levels) is often considered as a prerequisite to extract fluctuation properties of spectra. It is shown how this goal can be achieved even if only a fraction of levels are observed. The case of levels behaving as eigenvalues of random matrices, of current interest in nuclear physics, is worked out in detail.

# Missing levels in correlated spectra

## Key claim
The paper shows that the fluctuation properties of a complete spectrum can be recovered from an incomplete one, provided the missing levels are removed at random and the fraction \(f\) of observed levels is known. The key hedge is that the analysis assumes random sampling of levels, which is justified for compound nucleus resonances where level positions and widths are uncorrelated, but may not hold in other contexts.

## Method
The authors derive analytic relations between the statistical measures of a complete spectrum and those of an incomplete spectrum where a fraction \(f\) of levels is observed at random. They start from the \(n\)-point correlation functions, showing they are simply rescaled by \(f^n\) and the unfolded variables are scaled by \(1/f\). They then derive explicit formulas for the number variance \(\sigma^2(L)\), the Dyson-Mehta \(\Delta\)-statistic \(\delta(L)\), the \(E(n,s)\) functions, and the spacing distributions \(p(n,s)\) in terms of the corresponding complete-spectrum quantities. These results are applied to the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) as the model for chaotic nuclear spectra.

## Key numbers
- For an incomplete GOE spectrum, the number variance becomes \(\sigma^2(L) = (1-f)L + f^2 \Sigma^2(L/f)\), where \(\Sigma^2(L)\) is the complete GOE number variance; the linear term \((1-f)L\) replaces the logarithmic increase of the complete case.
- The Dyson-Mehta statistic becomes \(\delta(L) = (1-f)L/15 + f^2 \Delta(L/f)\), again acquiring a linear term \((1-f)L/15\).
- The nearest neighbor distribution (NND) for incomplete GOE spectra is given by \(p(s) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty (1-f)^k P(k, s/f)\), where \(P(k,s)\) are the complete GOE spacing distributions; the slope at the origin is increased by a factor \(1/f\) relative to the GOE value.
- For large \(s/f\), the NND asymptotically approaches \(p(s) \sim \frac{1}{1-f} \exp\left[ \frac{s}{f} \ln(1-f) \right]\), which is a Poisson distribution as \(f \to 0\).
- Analysis of \(^{238}\)U neutron resonances and \(^{48}\)Ti proton resonances using Eq. (28) concluded that about 10% of resonances are missing in both cases.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of missing-level effects in nuclear spectra, particularly the ansatz for the NND first proposed by Bilpuch (1976) and later analyzed by Mitchell (1988) using Shannon entropy maximization. The paper provides a complete theoretical framework connecting all standard fluctuation measures (number variance, \(\Delta\)-statistic, spacing distributions) for randomly thinned spectra, generalizing the GOE case. It enables experimentalists to correct for incomplete detection in compound nucleus resonance data, as already demonstrated for \(^{238}\)U and \(^{48}\)Ti, and to estimate the fraction of missing levels when the underlying statistics (GOE) are known. The results also apply to other systems with random-matrix correlations, such as microwave cavities and elastodynamics resonators.
