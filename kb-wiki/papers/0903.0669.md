# Correlation Probes of a QCD Critical Point

> Critical opalescence is a characteristic experimental signature of a second order phase transition in solid state physics. A new, experimentally accessible measure of opacity and of attenuation length in heavy ion reactions is suggested, as a combination of HBT radii and nuclear modification factors. This opacity is maximal when $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$, the system size and centrality correspond to the critical point of QCD. To characterize the phase transition at this critical point, the critical exponent of the correlation function can be determined by measuring the Lévy index of stability of the Bose-Einstein or HBT correlations. The exponent of the correlation length can be determined from fits to the multiplicity distribution in various pseudorapidity intervals, also as a function of colliding energy, system size, centrality and (chemical) freeze-out temperature. These two critical exponents determine the remaining four critical exponents and the universality class of this second order phase transition. As a control experiment, the determination of the critical exponent of the specific heat capacity is proposed, from event-by-event fluctuation measurements. To measure opacity precisely, well calibrated high transverse momentum probes are needed, such as given by the excitation function of $γ$ + jet correlation functions.

# Correlation Probes of a QCD Critical Point

## Key claim
Critical opalescence, characterized by a maximum in opacity and a minimum in attenuation length, can serve as an experimental signature of the QCD critical point in heavy ion collisions, provided that the opacity is properly defined as the ratio of the nuclear modification factor to the HBT radius rather than the nuclear modification factor alone.

## Method
The author defines a new experimental measure of opacity as κ = -ln(R_AA)/R_HBT, where R_AA is the nuclear modification factor and R_HBT is the Hanbury Brown-Twiss radius measuring the characteristic source size. This opacity is evaluated using PHENIX neutral pion data at p_t = 4.75 GeV and published HBT radii from Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV as a function of centrality. To characterize the critical point itself, the author proposes measuring the Lévy index of stability from Bose-Einstein correlations to determine the critical exponent η of the correlation function, and extracting the correlation length exponent ν from fits to multiplicity distributions in pseudorapidity intervals as a function of energy, system size, centrality, and freeze-out temperature.

## Key numbers
- Opacity κ in 0-5% central Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV: 0.35 ± 0.04 fm⁻¹, evaluated at p_t = 4.75 GeV/c
- Attenuation length λ in 0-5% central Au+Au collisions: 2.9 ± 0.3 fm
- Opacity κ in 50-60% peripheral Au+Au collisions: 0.15 ± 0.05 fm⁻¹
- Attenuation length λ in 50-60% peripheral collisions: 6.5 ± 2.0 fm
- Nuclear modification factor R_AA decreases from 0.72 in 50-60% centrality to 0.19 in 0-5% centrality
- HBT radii scale with centrality as R_i = p_0 + p_1 N_part^(1/3), with p_0 ≈ 0.83 ± 0.25 fm and p_1 ≈ 0.54 ± 0.05 fm
- Lattice QCD prediction for the critical end point location: T_E = 164 ± 2 MeV and μ_E = 360 ± 40 MeV from Fodor and Katz (2004)
- Earlier prediction from Fodor and Katz (2001): T_E = 160 ± 3.5 MeV and μ_E = 725 ± 35 MeV

## Context
This work extends the analogy between critical opalescence in condensed matter physics and heavy ion collisions, building on the RHIC discoveries of jet suppression (PHENIX, 2001), perfect fluid behavior (the four RHIC collaborations, 2004-2005), and high-temperature superfluidity (Lacey, 2006; Csorgo, 2008). The proposed opacity measure corrects a shortcoming of the nuclear modification factor R_AA, which does not account for the distance traveled through the medium. The paper connects to the theoretical framework of Rajagopal (1992) on critical exponents in QCD and to the lattice predictions of Fodor and Katz (2001, 2004) for the critical point location. The method enables experimental determination of the universality class of the QCD critical point by measuring two independent critical exponents (η from correlation functions and ν from multiplicity distributions), from which the remaining four exponents can be derived using scaling relations.
