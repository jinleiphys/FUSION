# Why are hydrodynamic theories applicable beyond the hydrodynamic regime?

> We present an alternative approach to deriving second-order non-conformal hydrodynamics from the relativistic Boltzmann equation. We demonstrate how constitutive relations for shear and bulk stresses can be transformed into dynamical evolution equations, resulting in Israel-Stewart-like (ISL) hydrodynamics. To understand the far-from-equilibrium applicability of such ISL theories, we investigate the one-dimensional boost-invariant Boltzmann equation using special moments of the distribution function for a system with finite particle mass. Our analysis reveals that the mathematical structure of the ISL equations is akin to that of moment equations, enabling them to approximately replicate even the collisionless dynamics. We conclude that this particular feature is important in extending the applicability of ISL theories beyond the hydrodynamic regime.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Israel-Stewart-like (ISL) hydrodynamic theories remain applicable far from equilibrium not because of any intrinsic hydrodynamic property, but because their mathematical structure is identical to that of truncated moment equations from kinetic theory, allowing them to approximately reproduce even collisionless (free-streaming) dynamics.

## Method

The authors derive second-order non-conformal hydrodynamic equations from the relativistic Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation using a Chapman-Enskog-like expansion of the distribution function up to second order in gradients. They then transform the resulting constitutive relations for shear and bulk stresses into dynamical evolution equations (ISL hydrodynamics). To test far-from-equilibrium applicability, they analyze the one-dimensional boost-invariant (Bjorken) Boltzmann equation by constructing two families of special moments of the distribution function (L-moments and M-moments) for a system with finite particle mass, and compare the moment hierarchy to the ISL equations.

## Key numbers

- The free-streaming fixed points of the evolution equation for the moment L0 occur at g0 = -1 and g0 = -2 (exact values); a naive truncation ignoring the L2 moment gives approximate values of -0.93 and -2.21, showing the truncated moment equations capture the collisionless regime.
- The hydrodynamic fixed point is given by g* = -a0 + (c0/2)(1 - 3P/epsilon), and its location is exactly captured even by the truncated moment equations.
- The relaxation-time approximation collision kernel introduces terms proportional to 1/tau_R in the moment equations, where tau_R is the relaxation time; the dimensionless ratio w = tau/tau_R controls the transition between collisionless (w << 1) and hydrodynamic (w >> 1) regimes.

## Context

This work extends the moment method approach of Blaizot (2017, 2019) and Jaiswal (2022) for the Bjorken flow to explain the surprising success of Israel-Stewart-type hydrodynamics in heavy-ion collision simulations, where gradients are large and conventional hydrodynamics should break down. The analysis shows that ISL equations work beyond the hydrodynamic regime because they share the same mathematical structure as the truncated moment equations from kinetic theory, not because of any hydrodynamic property. This enables a deeper understanding of why hydrodynamic simulations of heavy-ion collisions remain accurate at early times, and provides a framework for systematically improving far-from-equilibrium descriptions by including higher moments.
