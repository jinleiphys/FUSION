# Relevance of complex branch points for partial wave analysis

> A central issue in hadron spectroscopy is to deduce --- and interpret --- resonance parameters, namely pole positions and residues, from experimental data, for those are the quantities to be compared to lattice QCD or model calculations. However, not every structure in the observables derives from a resonance pole: the origin might as well be branch points, either located on the real axis (when a new channel comprised of stable particles opens) or in the complex plane (when at least one of the intermediate particles is unstable). In this paper we demonstrate first the existence of such branch points in the complex plane and then show on the example of the piN P11 partial wave that it is not possible to distinguish the structures induced by the latter from a true pole signal based on elastic data alone.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that branch points in the complex energy plane arise unavoidably from three-body intermediate states with strongly correlated particle pairs (such as the rho meson in pi-pi-N systems), and shows that on the example of the piN P11 partial wave, these complex branch points cannot be distinguished from true resonance poles based on elastic scattering data alone.

## Method

The authors derive the existence and position of complex branch points using general S-matrix properties, specifically by analyzing the three-body phase space decomposition and the spectral function of an unstable intermediate particle (the rho meson) in the unitarity relation for the piN amplitude. They then compare the Juelich coupled-channel model, which explicitly includes the rhoN complex branch point, with a Carnegie-Mellon-Berkeley type model that contains only real-axis branch points and uses resonance poles to fit the same elastic piN data in the P11 partial wave around 1.7 GeV.

## Key numbers

* The complex branch points from the rhoN intermediate state are located at sqrt(s) = M_N + m_rho +/- i*Gamma/2, which evaluates to approximately 1700 +/- 75i MeV for the rho meson parameters.
* The third branch point from the three-body threshold is located at sqrt(s) = 2*m_pi + M_N.
* In the fit of the CMB model to the Juelich model amplitude, a pole is found at 1698 - 130i MeV, which simulates the missing complex branch point.
* The threshold behavior of the complex branch points z_{b1,2} scales as (sqrt(s) - z_{b1,2})^{(2L+1)/2}, where L is the orbital angular momentum of the quasi-particle coupling to the stable particle.
* The threshold behavior of the three-body branch point z_{b3} scales as (sqrt(s) - (2*m_pi + M_N))^{ell+L+2}, where ell is the decay angular momentum of the quasi-particle.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical understanding of analytic structures in hadronic scattering amplitudes beyond the standard two-body thresholds and resonance poles, building on earlier theoretical work by Cutkosky (1990) and others on complex branch points. The paper demonstrates that modern partial wave analyses such as the GWU/SAID analysis (Arndt, 2006; Arndt, 2008), the Juelich approach (Krehl, 1999; Doering, 2009), and the EBAC approach (Julia-Diaz, 2007) all contain these branch points, but that analyses omitting them may produce false resonance signals. The result enables a more reliable extraction of the baryon resonance spectrum by showing that global analyses of multiple reaction channels, rather than high-precision elastic data alone, are necessary to distinguish true resonances from branch point effects.
