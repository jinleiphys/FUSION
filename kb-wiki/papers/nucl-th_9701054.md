# Particle Interferometry in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> By measuring hadronic single-particle spectra and two-particle correlations in heavy-ion collisions, the size and dynamical state of the collision fireball at freeze-out can be reconstructed. I discuss the relevant theoretical methods and their limitations. By applying the formalism to recent pion correlation data from Pb+Pb collisions at CERN we demonstrate that the collision zone has undergone strong transverse growth before freeze-out (by a factor 2-3 in each direction), and that it expands both longitudinally and transversally. From the thermal and flow energy density at freeze-out the energy density at the onset of transverse expansion can be estimated from conservation laws. It comfortably exceeds the critical value for the transition to color deconfined matter.

## Key claim

By combining single-particle spectra and two-particle correlations from Pb+Pb collisions at CERN, the collision zone is shown to have undergone strong transverse growth (by a factor 2-3 in each direction) before freeze-out, and the energy density at the onset of transverse expansion comfortably exceeds the critical value for the transition to color deconfined matter, though the author notes that whether this energy density was thermalized is a different question.

## Method

The author uses a simple analytical model for a finite thermalized source that expands both longitudinally and transversally, with the emission function parametrized by a Gaussian in transverse radius, longitudinal proper time, and spacetime rapidity, plus a Boltzmann factor containing the flow velocity. The Yano-Koonin-Podgoretskii (YKP) parametrization of the correlation function is employed to extract the HBT radius parameters, which measure the space-time regions of homogeneity of the source. The model is fitted to NA49 collaboration data from 158 A GeV/c Pb+Pb collisions, with the freeze-out temperature set by hand to 100 MeV and the transverse flow parameter adjusted to reproduce the transverse momentum dependence of the transverse radius parameter.

## Key numbers

* Transverse geometric (Gaussian) radius of the source: R = 7 fm, corresponding to an r.m.s. radius of about 10 fm for the pion source, compared to an r.m.s. radius of 3.2 fm for the original Pb nucleus projected on the transverse plane, implying a transverse expansion by a linear factor of 3.
* Average transverse flow velocity: ⟨v_f⟩ ≈ 0.58, from the fitted flow parameter η_f = 0.6.
* Freeze-out temperature set by hand: T = 100 MeV.
* Thermal energy density of a hadron resonance gas at T=100 MeV: about 50 MeV/fm³.
* Estimated energy density at the onset of transverse expansion: about 2 GeV/fm³, derived from 0.050 GeV/fm³ × 1.5 × 27 (where 27 is the estimated volume expansion factor).
* Critical energy density for deconfined quark-gluon matter from lattice QCD: ε_cr ≤ 0.9 GeV/fm³.
* Longitudinal extension parameter: Δη = 1.2, adjusted to reproduce the width of the pion rapidity distribution.
* Transverse homogeneity length at small K⊥: about 5.5 fm.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of hadronic single-particle spectra, which had previously been understood within a simple hydrodynamical model by Lee and Heinz (1989) and Stachel (1994), by adding two-particle correlation measurements to resolve the ambiguity inherent in single-particle spectra. The approach uses the YKP parametrization developed by Chapman, Nix, and Heinz (1995) and further analyzed by Heinz, Tomasik, and Wiedemann (1996). The paper demonstrates that combining single-particle spectra and two-particle correlations allows separate determination of freeze-out temperature and transverse flow, and that the resulting energy density estimate supports the formation of deconfined quark-gluon matter in Pb+Pb collisions at CERN, enabling future back-extrapolations to the hot and dense early stages of the collision.
