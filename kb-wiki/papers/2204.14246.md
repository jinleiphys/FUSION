# The fast neutron induced fission of $^{240}$Pu and $^{242}$Pu

> We report the measurement of the TKE release in the fast neutron induced fission of $^{240}$Pu and $^{242}$Pu. The results are compared to the predictions of the GEF model, the CGMF model, and the model of Denisov and Sedykh as well as previous experimental work on these reactions. Our absolute measurements of the TKE release are in good agreement with the previous measurements of Nethaway \textit{et al.} for the interaction of 14.8 MeV neutrons with $^{240}$Pu \cite{Nethaway} and of Winkelmann and Aumann for the interaction of 15 MeV neutrons with $^{242}$Pu \cite{Winkelmann}. The general trends of the measured TKE values agree with phenomenological models but the variances of the TKE distributions are significantly less than predicted by various models. The mean post neutron emission TKE release decreases non- linearly with increasing neutron energy and can be represented as TKE(MeV) = 175.8 $\pm$ 0.3 - (2. 4 $\pm$ 0.8)log$_{10}$E$_n$ - (1.4 $\pm$0.4)log$_{10}$E$_{n}^2$ for $^{240}$Pu and TKE(MeV) = 177.1 $\pm$ 0.3 - (1.2 $\pm$ 0.9)log$_{10}$E$_n$ - (1.8 $\pm$0.5)log$_{10}$E$_{n}^2$ for $^{242}$Pu.

## Key claim

The mean post-neutron-emission total kinetic energy (TKE) release in fast neutron induced fission of ²⁴⁰Pu and ²⁴²Pu decreases non-linearly with increasing neutron energy, and the variances of the TKE distributions are significantly smaller than predicted by the GEF, CGMF, and Denisov-Sedykh models, even though the absolute TKE values agree with previous measurements at specific energies.

## Method

Coincident fission fragments from ²⁴⁰Pu and ²⁴²Pu targets (72.3 and 63.0 μg/cm², respectively, on carbon backings) were detected using nine pairs of Si PIN diodes at the LANSCE-WNR facility, with neutron energies determined by time-of-flight over a 13.85 m flight path. The 2E method was applied iteratively to convert measured post-neutron-emission product energies into pre-neutron-emission fragment masses and TKE, using neutron multiplicities from the GEF model (for all energies) and the CGMF model (for En < 20 MeV) to correct for prompt neutron evaporation. Pulse-height defects were calibrated using a ²⁵²Cf source via the Schmitt method, and fragment energy losses in the target and backing were corrected using Northcliffe-Schilling tables.

## Key numbers

- For ²⁴⁰Pu(n,f): post-neutron TKE at En = 0.78 MeV is 175.9 ± 0.5 MeV; at En = 73.36 MeV it is 166.6 ± 0.5 MeV
- For ²⁴²Pu(n,f): post-neutron TKE at En = 0.85 MeV is 177.3 ± 0.4 MeV; at En = 76.55 MeV it is 168.5 ± 0.4 MeV
- The TKE release for ²⁴²Pu(n,f) is approximately 1% higher than for ²⁴⁰Pu(n,f) across the entire energy range
- The variance of the post-neutron TKE distribution for ²⁴⁰Pu ranges from σ² = 147.6 ± 0.4 (at En = 1.25 MeV) to σ² = 200.2 ± 0.4 (at En = 36.27 MeV)
- The variance of the post-neutron TKE distribution for ²⁴²Pu ranges from σ² = 144.7 ± 0.3 (at En = 11.16 MeV) to σ² = 190.4 ± 0.4 (at En = 76.55 MeV)
- The mean post-neutron TKE for ²⁴⁰Pu follows TKE(MeV) = 175.8 ± 0.3 - (2.4 ± 0.8)log₁₀En - (1.4 ± 0.4)(log₁₀En)²
- The mean post-neutron TKE for ²⁴²Pu follows TKE(MeV) = 177.1 ± 0.3 - (1.2 ± 0.9)log₁₀En - (1.8 ± 0.5)(log₁₀En)²
- The GEF model overestimates the TKE for ²⁴⁰Pu(n,f) by approximately 1.5 MeV (less than 1%)
- Vorob'eva (1974) reported mean kinetic energies of 178.2 ± 0.5 MeV for ²⁴⁰Pu(n,f) at En = 1.3 MeV and 178.6 ± 0.5 MeV for ²⁴²Pu(n,f) at En = 1.1 MeV

## Context

This work extends the sparse experimental data on fast neutron induced fission of ²⁴⁰Pu and ²⁴²Pu, where only Vorob'eva (1974) at low energies and Nethaway (for 14.8 MeV on ²⁴⁰Pu) and Winkelmann and Aumann (for 15 MeV on ²⁴²Pu) had previously reported TKE measurements. The results are compared to the GEF model (Schmidt, 2016), the CGMF model (Becker, 2013), and the Denisov-Sedykh model (Denisov and Sedykh, 2019), revealing that while absolute TKE trends are reproduced, the models significantly overpredict the width of the TKE distributions. The finding that ²⁴²Pu has higher TKE than ²⁴⁰Pu contradicts the simple Z²/A¹/³ scaling expected from the Viola systematics and suggests microscopic effects dominate over macroscopic behavior, consistent with spontaneous fission measurements by Wagemans (1984) and Dematte (1997). The data enable improved parameterizations for fast reactor and nuclear weapon design applications, and provide constraints for future fission model development.
