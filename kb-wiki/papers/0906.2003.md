# A faint type of supernova from a white dwarf with a helium-rich companion

> Supernovae (SNe) are thought to arise from two different physical processes. The cores of massive, short-lived stars undergo gravitational core collapse and typically eject a few solar masses during their explosion. These are thought to appear as as type Ib/c and II SNe, and are associated with young stellar populations. A type Ia SN is thought to arise from the thermonuclear detonation of a white dwarf star composed mainly of carbon and oxygen, whose mass approaches the Chandrasekhar limit. Such SNe are observed in both young and old stellar environments. Here we report our discovery of the faint type Ib SN 2005E in the halo of the nearby isolated galaxy, NGC 1032. The lack of any trace of recent star formation near the SN location (Fig. 1), and the very low derived ejected mass (~0.3 M_sun), argue strongly against a core-collapse origin for this event. Spectroscopic observations and the derived nucleosynthetic output show that the SN ejecta have high velocities and are dominated by helium-burning products, indicating that SN 2005E was neither a subluminous nor a regular SN Ia (Fig. 2). We have therefore found a new type of stellar explosion, arising from a low-mass, old stellar system, likely involving a binary with a primary white dwarf and a helium-rich secondary. The SN ejecta contain more calcium than observed in any known type of SN and likely additional large amounts of radioactive 44Ti. Such SNe may thus help resolve fundamental physical puzzles, extending from the composition of the primitive solar system and that of the oldest stars, to the Galactic production of positrons.

## Key claim

The paper reports the discovery of SN 2005E, a faint type Ib supernova in the halo of NGC 1032, which the authors argue represents a new type of stellar explosion from a low-mass, old stellar system rather than a core-collapse or standard type Ia event, based on the lack of star formation near its location, very low ejected mass, and helium-dominated ejecta with unusually high calcium content.

## Method

The authors performed optical spectroscopy and photometry of SN 2005E from discovery through the nebular phase, using the Katzman Automatic Imaging Telescope, Palomar 5-m Hale telescope, and Keck I 10-m telescope. They analyzed the nebular spectrum with a spectral-fitting code to derive elemental abundances and ejected mass, and combined these with light curve modeling and ejecta velocity measurements to constrain the progenitor system. They also conducted deep H-alpha imaging and ultraviolet observations of the explosion site to search for star formation tracers, and compared the host-galaxy distribution of eight calcium-rich subluminous type Ib/c supernovae against other supernova types.

## Key numbers

- Ejected mass: M_ej approximately 0.275 M_sun from nebular spectral analysis, consistent with M_ej approximately 0.3 plus or minus 0.1 M_sun from light curve and velocity data
- Calcium mass in ejecta: 0.135 M_sun, which is approximately 0.49 of the total ejecta mass and 5 to 10 times more than typical supernovae of any variety
- Relative calcium fraction 25 to 350 times higher than any reported values for other supernovae
- Ejecta velocity approximately 11,000 km per second
- Projected distance from galaxy center: 22.9 kpc; projected distance above disk: 11.3 kpc
- Host galaxy distance: 34 Mpc
- Upper limit on star formation rate at supernova position: 1.8 times 10 to the minus 5 M_sun per year
- Radio flux at 8.46 GHz: 11 plus or minus 53 microJy, corresponding to a radio luminosity limit (2 sigma) of 1.8 times 10 to the 26 erg per second per Hz
- Point-source detection limits at supernova location: r less than 25.3 mag (3 sigma) and u less than 24.7 mag (3 sigma), corresponding to absolute magnitudes M_r less than minus 7.5 and M_u less than minus 8.1 at 3 sigma
- Masses derived for other elements in ejecta: 0.1 M_sun carbon, 0.037 M_sun oxygen, 0.003 M_sun radioactive nickel
- Estimated rate of such events: approximately 10 percent of the type Ia supernova rate
- Estimated Ti-44 yield per event: 0.014 to 0.14 M_sun

## Context

This work extends the previously identified class of calcium-rich type Ib supernovae (Filippenko 2003) and provides the first clear identification of a new explosion mechanism distinct from both core-collapse and type Ia supernovae. The authors argue that SN 2005E and similar events are consistent with theoretical models of helium detonation on a white dwarf (the ".Ia" model of Bildsten 2007), accretion-induced collapse of a white dwarf (Metzger 2009), or helium detonation in double white dwarf systems (Iben 1987), and suggest that such explosions could help explain the origin of calcium-44 in the primitive solar system and old halo stars, as well as contribute to Galactic positron production through Ti-44 decay.
