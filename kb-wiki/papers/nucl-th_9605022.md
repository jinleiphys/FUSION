# Nuclear G-Matrix Elements from Nonlocal Potentials

> We study effects of nonlocality in the nuclear force on the G-matrix elements for finite nuclei. Nuclear G-matrix elements for $\O16$ are calculated in the harmonic oscillator basis from a nonlocal potential which models quark exchange effects between two nucleons. We employ a simple form of potential that gives the same phase shifts as a realistic local nucleon potential. The G-matrix elements calculated from the nonlocal potential show moderate increase in repulsion from those derived from the local potential.

## Key claim

The nonlocal Gaussian soft-core potential, which models quark-exchange effects and is constructed to be phase-shift equivalent to a local potential, produces G-matrix elements for \(^{16}\text{O}\) that show a moderate increase in repulsion compared to those from the local potential; however, the effect is typically only a few percent or less and is therefore negligibly small relative to other many-body effects in nuclear physics.

## Method

G-matrix elements for \(^{16}\text{O}\) were calculated in a harmonic-oscillator basis by solving the Bethe-Goldstone equation with the Eden–Emery approximation for the Pauli-blocking operator and the Barret–Hewitt–MacCarthy matrix-inversion method. A local Tamagaki G3RS potential was used as the reference, and a local-equivalent nonlocal potential was constructed by replacing the short-range central part with a Gaussian nonlocal form of range \(b = 0.5\) fm, adjusting the strength and range parameters to reproduce the same scattering phase shifts up to 300 MeV. The starting energy was averaged over the Fermi surface, and a gap energy of 40 MeV was employed.

## Context

This work extends earlier G-matrix calculations for finite nuclei using the Reid soft-core and Paris potentials (Barret–Hewitt–MacCarthy, 1971; Anantaraman–Toki–Bertsch, 1983; Hoshino–Kohno–Toki, 1991) by introducing a nonlocal potential motivated by the quark cluster model (QCM). The results show that the nonlocality from quark exchange produces only a few-percent repulsive shift in the G-matrix, which is smaller than typical many-body uncertainties (Brown, 1985). This suggests that such quark-originated nonlocal effects are unlikely to be detectable in conventional nuclear phenomena, though the method enables future tests in heavier systems or with different nonlocal forms.
