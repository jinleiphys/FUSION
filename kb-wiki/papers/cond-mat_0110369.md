# Thermodynamics of small Fermi systems: quantum statistical fluctuations

> We investigate the probability distribution of the quantum fluctuations of thermodynamic functions of finite, ballistic, phase-coherent Fermi gases. Depending on the chaotic or integrable nature of the underlying classical dynamics, on the thermodynamic function considered, and on temperature, we find that the probability distributions are dominated either (i) by the local fluctuations of the single-particle spectrum on the scale of the mean level spacing, or (ii) by the long-range modulations of that spectrum produced by the short periodic orbits. In case (i) the probability distributions are computed using the appropriate local universality class, uncorrelated levels for integrable systems and random matrix theory for chaotic ones. In case (ii) all the moments of the distributions can be explicitly computed in terms of periodic orbit theory, and are system-dependent, non-universal, functions. The dependence on temperature and number of particles of the fluctuations is explicitly computed in all cases, and the different relevant energy scales are displayed.

## Key claim

The probability distributions of quantum thermodynamic fluctuations in finite, ballistic Fermi gases are dominated either by universal local spectral fluctuations (on the scale of the mean level spacing) or by system-dependent long-range modulations from short periodic orbits, depending on the thermodynamic function, the chaotic or integrable nature of the classical dynamics, and the temperature, with the caveat that some functions (e.g., total energy, response functions, and particle-number fluctuations in integrable systems) are dominated by non-universal shell effects at all temperatures.

## Method

The authors decompose the single-particle density of states into a smooth bulk part and an oscillatory part expressed semiclassically as a sum over classical periodic orbits (Gutzwiller trace formula for chaotic systems, Berry-Tabor formula for integrable systems). They then insert this oscillatory density into the grand potential and its derivatives (energy, particle number, entropy, specific heat, response functions) to obtain the fluctuating parts of thermodynamic functions. The variance and higher moments of these fluctuations are computed either from the spectral form factor (using random matrix theory for chaotic systems or uncorrelated levels for integrable systems) for universal low-temperature cases, or directly from periodic-orbit sums for system-dependent cases at higher temperatures.

## Key numbers

- Mean level spacing δ = 1/ρ̄, where ρ̄ = (2V/√π)(m/(2πħ²))^(3/2) E_F^(1/2) for a three-dimensional cavity
- Heisenberg time τ_H = h/δ
- Shell energy scale E_c = h/τ_min, where τ_min is the period of the shortest periodic orbit
- Dimensionless shell-strength parameter g = E_c/δ = τ_H/τ_min, proportional to (k_F L)^(d-1) in d dimensions
- Thermal cutoff parameter τ_c = h/(2π² k_B T); the relevant thermal energy scale is 2π² k_B T
- For chaotic systems with time-reversal symmetry (β=1), the form factor K(τ) = 2τ - τ log(1 + 2τ/τ_H) for τ < τ_H
- For chaotic systems without time-reversal symmetry (β=2), K(τ) = τ for τ < τ_H
- For integrable systems, K_int(τ) = τ_H (constant)
- For short times τ ≪ τ_H, K_rmt(τ) = (2/β) τ
- For the response function, Q²(τ) ∝ τ for chaotic systems and Q²(τ) ∝ τ² for integrable systems

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical theory of shell effects in finite Fermi systems (Brack, 1993; Balian and Bloch, 1972) and the random-matrix theory classification of spectral fluctuations (Bohigas, Giannoni, and Schmit, 1984; Berry and Tabor, 1977) to the full probability distributions of thermodynamic fluctuations. It provides a unified statistical framework that connects the universal local fluctuations (described by random matrix theory or uncorrelated levels) with system-specific long-range modulations (from periodic orbit theory), enabling predictions for nuclei, quantum dots, and metallic clusters. The analysis parallels approaches used for disordered metals (Montambaux, 1996) but applies to ballistic, phase-coherent systems where the relevant energy scales are the mean level spacing δ, the shell energy E_c, and the thermal energy 2π²k_BT.
