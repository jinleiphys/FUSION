# Fission fragment charge and mass distributions in 239Pu(n,f) in the adiabatic nuclear energy density functional theory

> Accurate knowledge of fission fragment yields is an essential ingredient of numerous applications ranging from the formation of elements in the r-process to fuel cycle optimization for nuclear energy. The need for a predictive theory applicable where no data is available is an incentive to develop a fully microscopic approach to fission dynamics. In this work, we calculate the pre-neutron emission charge and mass distributions of the fission fragments formed in the neutron-induced fission of 239Pu using a microscopic method based on nuclear energy density functional (EDF) method, where large amplitude collective motion is treated adiabatically using the time dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) under the Gaussian overlap approximation (GOA). Fission fragment distributions are extracted from the flux of the collective wave packet through the scission line. We find that the main characteristics of the fission charge and mass distributions can be well reproduced by existing energy functionals even in two-dimensional collective spaces. Theory and experiment agree typically within 2 mass units for the position of the asymmetric peak. As expected, calculations are sensitive to the structure of the initial state and the prescription for the collective inertia. We emphasize that results are also sensitive to the continuity of the collective landscape near scission. Our analysis confirms that the adiabatic approximation provides an effective scheme to compute fission fragment yields. It also suggests that, at least in the framework of nuclear DFT, three-dimensional collective spaces may be a prerequisite to reach 10% accuracy in predicting pre-neutron emission fission fragment yields.

## Key claim

The adiabatic time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) under the Gaussian overlap approximation (GOA) can reproduce the main characteristics of fission fragment charge and mass distributions for 239Pu(n,f) using two-dimensional collective spaces, but three-dimensional collective spaces may be required to reach 10% accuracy in predicting pre-neutron emission yields.

## Method

The authors performed constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using either the SkM* Skyrme functional or the D1S Gogny functional to map the potential energy surface of 240Pu as a function of axial quadrupole (Q20) and octupole (Q30) moments. They then solved the TDGCM+GOA equation using the finite element solver FELIX to propagate a collective wave packet on this surface, extracting fission fragment distributions from the time-integrated flux of the wave packet through a scission line. The collective inertia tensor was computed in the perturbative cranking approximation using either the GCM or ATDHFB formula.

## Key numbers

- Theory and experiment agree within 2 mass units for the position of the asymmetric peak in the fission fragment mass distribution
- The initial state was constructed with average energy 1 MeV above the inner fission barrier
- The energy spread parameter sigma for the Gaussian superposition of quasi-bound states was set to 0.5 MeV
- The highest quasi-bound state among the first 100 computed lies roughly 16 MeV above the inner barrier energy
- The Skyrme EDF calculations used a one-center harmonic oscillator basis with up to Nmax = 30 shells
- The Gogny D1S calculations used a two-center basis with up to Nmax = 11 shells
- The pairing cut-off in the quasi-particle space was set at Ecut = 60 MeV for surface-volume pairing calculations

## Context

This work extends the TDGCM approach to fission fragment distributions previously applied by Goutte (2005) and Younes (2012) to the benchmark case of 240Pu, providing a comprehensive study of sensitivity to initial state, collective inertia, and energy functional choice. The results suggest that while two-dimensional collective spaces can reproduce main features, three-dimensional spaces may be necessary to reach the 10% accuracy level required for applications, and the analysis confirms that the adiabatic approximation provides an effective scheme for computing fission fragment yields.
