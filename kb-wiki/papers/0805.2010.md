# Faddeev and Glauber Calculations at Intermediate Energies in a Model for n+d Scattering

> Obtaining cross sections for nuclear reactions at intermediate energies based on the Glauber formulation has a long tradition. Only recently the energy regime of a few hundred MeV has become accessible to ab-initio Faddeev calculations of three-body scattering. In order to go to higher energies, the Faddeev equation for three-body scattering is formulated and directly solved without employing a partial wave decomposition. In the simplest form the Faddeev equation for interacting scalar particles is a three-dimensional integral equation in five variables, from which the total cross section, the cross sections for elastic scattering and breakup reactions, as well as differential cross sections are obtained. The same observables are calculated based on the Glauber formulation. The first order Glauber calculation and the Glauber rescattering corrections are compared in detail with the corresponding terms of the Faddeev multiple scattering series for projectile energies between 100 MeV and 2 GeV.

# Faddeev and Glauber Calculations at Intermediate Energies in a Model for n+d Scattering

## Key claim
The Glauber approximation for n+d scattering, when using the same two-body t-matrix as input, agrees with exact Faddeev calculations for total cross sections starting at about 1 GeV projectile energy, but fails to reproduce the differential cross section in diffraction minima even up to 2 GeV. The naive expectation that the n+d total cross section reduces to twice the two-nucleon cross section is not fulfilled in the energy regime up to 2 GeV due to persistent rescattering contributions.

## Method
The authors solved the nonrelativistic Faddeev equation for three identical bosons directly in momentum space without partial wave decomposition, using vector Jacobi momenta and angles between them. They compared the Faddeev multiple scattering series (first order, second order, and fully converged) with Glauber calculations using the same Malfliet-Tjon-type two-body interaction and deuteron wave function. The Glauber calculations employed two different choices for the unit vector defining the perpendicular plane for momentum transfer projection: along the incident laboratory momentum or along the sum of incoming and outgoing momenta.

## Key numbers
- The two-body interaction was fitted to support a bound state ("deuteron") with binding energy E_d = -2.23 MeV
- Projectile energies considered ranged from 100 MeV to 2 GeV
- At 500 MeV, first order Faddeev and Glauber calculations for differential cross section start to deviate at theta_l approximately 50 degrees, corresponding to momentum transfer of about 740 MeV
- At 1.5 GeV, first order calculations deviate from fully converged Faddeev result around theta_l approximately 12 degrees, corresponding to momentum transfer of roughly 350 MeV
- At 200 MeV, the two choices for the unit vector in Glauber calculations differ at theta_l approximately 25 degrees, corresponding to momentum transfer of about 270 MeV
- At 100 MeV, the two choices for the unit vector differ at theta_l approximately 25 degrees, corresponding to momentum transfer of roughly 180 MeV
- The second order Glauber correction to total cross section (delta sigma) only agrees with the Faddeev second order term starting around 1 GeV
- The Franco-Glauber approximation of taking the forward scattering amplitude out of the integral gave a second order contribution almost double the size of the original correction at all energies considered

## Context
This work extends the comparison between Glauber theory and exact Faddeev calculations that was initiated in Elster (1998), where the high-energy limit of the Faddeev multiple scattering series was derived analytically but could not be tested against exact calculations. The authors use the same nonrelativistic Faddeev formulation developed by Liu (2004) and Lin (2007, 2008) that enabled calculations up to 2 GeV. The study tests the range of validity of the Glauber formulation, which has been widely applied in nuclear reactions from electron scattering to heavy ion collisions, following the original Glauber (1959) formulation and the specific n+d scattering formalism of Harrington (1964) and Franco and Glauber (1966). The results enable future work to determine at which energies the eikonal approximation becomes valid and whether Glauber rescattering corrections are sufficient.
