# The Relativistic N-body Problem in a Separable Two-Body Basis

> We use Dirac's constraint dynamics to obtain a Hamiltonian formulation of the relativistic N-body problem in a separable two-body basis in which the particles interact pair-wise through scalar and vector interactions. The resultant N-body Hamiltonian is relativistically covariant. It can be easily separated in terms of the center-of-mass and the relative motion of any two-body subsystem. It can also be separated into an unperturbed Hamiltonian with a residual interaction. In a system of two-body composite particles, the solutions of the unperturbed Hamiltonian are relativistic two-body internal states, each of which can be obtained by solving a relativistic Schrödinger-like equation. The resultant two-body wave functions can be used as basis states to evaluate reaction matrix elements in the general N-body problem. We prove a relativistic version of the post-prior equivalence which guarantees a unique evaluation of the reaction matrix element, independent of the ways of separating the Hamiltonian into unperturbed and residual interactions. Since an arbitrary reaction matrix element involves composite particles in motion, we show explicitly how such matrix elements can be evaluated in terms of the wave functions of the composite particles and the relevant Lorentz transformations.

## Key claim

The paper presents a Hamiltonian formulation of the relativistic N-body problem using Dirac's constraint dynamics, where the N-body Hamiltonian can be separated into an unperturbed part (describing composite two-body subsystems) and a residual interaction, enabling the use of relativistic two-body wave functions as basis states for evaluating reaction matrix elements. The authors prove a relativistic version of post-prior equivalence, but the formalism relies on the approximation that many-body forces (W_i) are often ignored in practical computations, and the compatibility conditions for the constraints become very complicated with no known closed-form solutions when such forces are included.

## Method

The authors start from Dirac's constraint dynamics, assigning each particle a generalized mass-shell constraint with pair-wise scalar and vector interactions. They construct the total N-body Hamiltonian as a sum of these constraints with Lagrange multipliers chosen as λ_i = 1/(2m_i), which yields a form analogous to the non-relativistic N-body Hamiltonian. The Hamiltonian is then separated into unperturbed two-body Hamiltonians (each describing a composite particle) plus a residual interaction, allowing the use of relativistic two-body bound state wave functions (obtained from a Schrödinger-like equation) as basis states for evaluating reaction matrix elements. The authors prove the relativistic post-prior equivalence by showing that the matrix element ⟨ψ'_a|H|ψ_a⟩ equals both ⟨ψ'_a|V_I|ψ_a⟩ and ⟨ψ'_a|V'_I|ψ_a⟩, independent of how the total Hamiltonian is split into unperturbed and residual parts.

## Key numbers

- The relativistic two-body bound state mass M is related to the non-relativistic eigenvalue E by M = √(2μE + m₁²) + √(2μE + m₂²), where μ = m₁m₂/(m₁+m₂) is the non-relativistic reduced mass.
- In the weak binding limit, M = m₁ + m₂ + E, recovering the familiar non-relativistic result.
- For scalar interactions, the potential V₁₂ = (2m_M S + S²)/(2μ), where m_M = m₁m₂/M.
- For time-like vector interactions, V₁₂ = (2ε_M A - A²)/(2μ), where ε_M = (M² - m₁² - m₂²)/(2M).
- The differential cross section for two-composite-particle scattering in first-Born approximation is dσ/dt = (1/(64π s))(ħ²/|p_A^c|²)|M_fi|², where M_fi = (2π)³√(2E_A^c 2E_B^c 2E_C^c 2E_D^c) h_fi, and h_fi has dimension of 1/(mass)².

## Context

This work extends the relativistic two-body constraint dynamics developed by Crater, Wong, and others (Crater 1982, 1988, 1992, 1996; Todorov 1971; Sazdjian 1985) to the N-body problem, building on Sazdjian's earlier investigation (Sazdjian 1989) but with a focus on a separable two-body basis. The formalism enables the generalization of non-relativistic reaction models (such as the distorted-wave Born approximation described by Satchler 1980 and the quark-interchange model of Barnes and Swanson 1992) to relativistic energies, allowing calculation of reaction matrix elements for processes like meson-meson scattering in high-energy heavy-ion collisions. The post-prior equivalence proof ensures that reaction matrix elements are uniquely defined regardless of how the Hamiltonian is partitioned, which is essential for practical perturbation expansions.
