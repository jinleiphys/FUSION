# Probing the size and binding energy of the hypertriton in heavy ion collisions

> The hypertriton is predicted to have a small binding energy (a weighted average of about 150 keV), consistent with a large matter radius (~ 10 fm), larger than the historical 11Li halo discovered more than 35 years ago. But the reported experimental values of the binding energy of the hypertriton range from 70 to 400 keV. In this work I discuss the electromagnetic response and interaction radius of the hypertriton and how high energy heavy ion collisions (~ 1 - 2 GeV/nucleon) can help achieving a higher accuracy for the determination of its size and binding energy.

## Key claim

The electromagnetic response and interaction cross sections of the hypertriton in heavy ion collisions at 1.5 GeV/nucleon are sensitive to its binding energy, with Coulomb breakup cross sections showing a much stronger dependence (up to 600% variation across the range of possible binding energies) than interaction cross sections, suggesting that combined measurements could determine the hypertriton size and binding energy with higher accuracy than current experiments.

## Method

The author modeled the hypertriton as a Lambda particle bound to a deuteron core using a Woods-Saxon potential with radius 2.5 fm and diffuseness 0.65 fm, adjusting the potential depth to reproduce Lambda-deuteron separation energies of 100, 150, 200, 300, and 500 keV. The electromagnetic dipole response function was calculated for transitions from the s-wave ground state to p-wave continuum states, and the Coulomb dissociation cross section was obtained using the equivalent photon method with absorption accounted for via Glauber multiple scattering theory. Interaction cross sections were computed from the transparency functions using free nucleon-nucleon cross sections (45.8 mb for pp, 40 mb for pn) and a Lambda-nucleon cross section of 35 mb.

## Key numbers

- The weighted average binding energy of the hypertriton from all existing data is 148 ± 40 keV (shaded band from statistical model analysis)
- The predicted matter radius of the hypertriton is approximately 10 fm, based on the relation R = sqrt(hbar^2/(4*mu*B_Lambda))
- For a carbon target at 1.5 GeV/nucleon, the Coulomb breakup cross section ranges from 4.1 mb (B_Lambda = 500 keV) to 22.9 mb (B_Lambda = 100 keV)
- For a lead target at 1.5 GeV/nucleon, the Coulomb breakup cross section ranges from 656 mb (B_Lambda = 500 keV) to 3820 mb (B_Lambda = 100 keV)
- For a carbon target at 1.5 GeV/nucleon, the interaction cross section ranges from 749 mb (B_Lambda = 500 keV) to 842 mb (B_Lambda = 100 keV)
- For a lead target at 1.5 GeV/nucleon, the interaction cross section ranges from 2490 mb (B_Lambda = 500 keV) to 3098 mb (B_Lambda = 100 keV)
- The maximum of the dipole response function occurs at E_max = 3*B_Lambda/5, matching analytical predictions from Bersus (1992)
- The analytical model of Eq. (dBan) reproduces the numerical response function to within 4% for all binding energies considered at low excitation energies (E less than 3*B_Lambda)

## Context

This work extends the study of weakly-bound nuclear systems from halo nuclei (such as Li-11 discovered by Tanihata in 1985) to hypernuclei, specifically the hypertriton which may be the largest known halo system. The binding energy of the hypertriton is a critical benchmark for the Lambda-nucleon interaction, which is important for resolving the "hyperon puzzle" in neutron star equations of state (Lattimer, 2021). The proposed method using active targets in heavy ion collisions could complement existing measurements from emulsion experiments (Juric, 1973; Davis, 2005) and recent heavy ion experiments at STAR (Adam, 2020) and ALICE (2022), potentially enabling more precise determination of the hypertriton binding energy and size.
