# Is Rho-Meson Melting Compatible with Chiral Restoration?

> Utilizing in-medium vector spectral functions which describe dilepton data in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation of QCD and Weinberg sum rules at finite temperature. The starting point is our recent study in vacuum, where the sum rules have been quantitatively satisfied using phenomenological axial-/vector spectral functions which describe hadronic tau-decay data. In the medium, the temperature dependence of condensates and chiral order parameters is taken from thermal lattice QCD where available, and otherwise estimated from a hadron resonance gas. Since little is known about the in-medium axial-vector spectral function, we model it with a Breit-Wigner ansatz allowing for smooth temperature variations of its width and mass parameters. Our study thus amounts to testing the compatibility of the $ρ$-broadening found in dilepton experiments with (the approach toward) chiral restoration, and thereby searching for viable in-medium axial-vector spectral functions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the in-medium broadening ("melting") of the rho meson, as observed in dilepton data from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, is compatible with the approach toward chiral symmetry restoration, provided a specific temperature-dependent axialvector (a1) spectral function is assumed. The conclusion is conditional: the compatibility holds when the a1 meson's mass decreases toward the rho mass and its width increases substantially, with the combined constraints of QCD sum rules and Weinberg sum rules narrowing the possible in-medium a1 behavior.

## Method

The authors evaluate finite-temperature QCD sum rules and Weinberg sum rules using in-medium vector spectral functions from a microscopic hadronic model (consistent with dilepton data) and a Breit-Wigner parameterization of the axialvector spectral function with four temperature-dependent parameters (mass, coupling, and two width parameters). The temperature dependence of condensates and chiral order parameters on the operator product expansion side is taken from thermal lattice QCD where available, and otherwise estimated from a hadron resonance gas including all confirmed hadrons with mass up to 2 GeV. Chiral mixing is applied to the excited rho' and a1' states, and the sum rules are simultaneously satisfied by minimizing a combined deviation measure for the axialvector QCD sum rule and the first two Weinberg sum rules.

## Key numbers

- Pseudo-critical temperature for chiral restoration: T_pc approximately 160 MeV
- Vacuum chiral mass splitting between rho and a1: approximately 0.5 GeV
- Quark condensate vanishes slightly above T = 170 MeV in the hadron resonance gas model
- Gluon condensate suppression at T = 170 MeV: 13%
- QCD sum rule deviation in the vector channel (d_V): ranges from 0.43% to 0.67% for T = 0-170 MeV
- QCD sum rule deviation in the axialvector channel (d_A): remains below 0.6% for all temperatures
- Weinberg sum rule 1 and 2 deviations: below 0.1% for all temperatures
- Ratio r_{-1} (absolute-value moment of spectral difference, normalized to vacuum): decreases from 1.0 at T=0 to 0.14 at T=170 MeV
- Ratio r_0: decreases from 1.0 at T=0 to 0.12 at T=170 MeV
- Ratio r_1: decreases from 1.0 at T=0 to 0.15 at T=170 MeV
- Vector dominance coupling reduction at T = 170 MeV: up to 7%
- Vacuum gluon condensate value: 0.017 GeV^4
- Vacuum 4-quark factorization parameter kappa: 2.1

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous vacuum sum rule analysis (Hohler and Rapp, 2012) where QCD and Weinberg sum rules were quantitatively satisfied using spectral functions from hadronic tau-decay data. It builds on microscopic calculations of the in-medium rho spectral function (Rapp and collaborators, 1999-2013) that describe dilepton data from experiments such as NA60 (Arnaldi, 2008) and STAR (Geurts, 2012). The approach contrasts with earlier attempts to calculate the in-medium a1 using chiral Lagrangians with local gauging (Urban, 2001), which encountered difficulties reproducing vacuum tau-decay data. The results enable future microscopic calculations of the in-medium a1 spectral function and provide a direct theoretical link between dilepton measurements and chiral symmetry restoration in heavy-ion collisions.
