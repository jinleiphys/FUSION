# BCS-pairing and nuclear vibrations

> On the basis of time-dependent mean-field picture, we discuss the nature of the low-frequency quadrupole vibrations from small-amplitude to large-amplitude regimes as representatives of surface shape vibrations of a superfluid droplet (nucleus). We consider full five-dimensional quadrupole dynamics including three-dimensional rotations restoring the broken symmetries as well as axially symmetric and asymmetric shape fluctuations. We show that the intimate connections between the BCS pairing and collective vibrations reveal through the inertial masses governing their collective kinetic energies.

# BCS-pairing and nuclear vibrations

## Key claim

The paper argues that low-frequency quadrupole vibrations in nuclei are intimately connected to BCS pairing correlations, with this connection revealed through the inertial masses governing collective kinetic energies. The authors qualify that this relationship is most clearly demonstrated for low-frequency (slow) quadrupole vibrations rather than for the full diversity of nuclear vibrational phenomena, and that the time-dependent mean-field approach provides the clearest correspondence between quantum and classical aspects of surface shape vibrations.

## Method

The authors employ the time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) theory to describe collective motion as a moving self-consistent mean field, starting from small-amplitude vibrations around spherical equilibrium using the quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) and extending to large-amplitude regimes. They derive a five-dimensional (5D) quadrupole collective Hamiltonian that includes vibrational kinetic energy terms with inertial masses \(D_{\beta\beta}\), \(D_{\beta\gamma}\), and \(D_{\gamma\gamma}\), rotational kinetic energy with moments of inertia \(\mathcal{J}_k\), and a collective potential \(V(\beta,\gamma)\). The adiabatic self-consistent collective coordinate (ASCC) method and its practical approximation, the constrained HFB + local QRPA (LQRPA) method, are used to determine the microscopic structure of the collective coordinates and inertial functions.

## Key numbers

- The first excited \(2^+\) states in even-even nuclei have excitation energies much lower than the energy gap \(2\Delta\) characterizing nuclear superfluidity, with ratios \(E(2^+)/2\Delta\) less than about 0.1 for rotational nuclei.
- The rotational moment of inertia evaluated from \(E(2^+)\) is about half of the rigid-body value, providing evidence for the superfluid phase of nuclear ground states.
- The high-frequency quadrupole giant resonance carries about 90% of the energy-weighted sum-rule value for the quadrupole operator.
- In the QRPA, the quadrupole vibrational modes can be regarded as phonons of a 5D harmonic oscillator, with two-phonon states expected as a triplet with \(I^\pi = 0^+, 2^+, 4^+\).
- The vibrational kinetic energy after quantization (Eq. 13) involves the inertial functions through the quantities \(W = \beta^{-2}[D_{\beta\beta}D_{\gamma\gamma} - D_{\beta\gamma}^2]\) and \(R = D_1 D_2 D_3\).

## Context

This work extends the foundational ideas of Bohr and Mottelson (1975, 1976) on moving self-consistent mean fields and the unified description of single-particle and collective motions, as well as the microscopic derivation of the 5D quadrupole collective Hamiltonian by Belyaev (1965) and Baranger and Kumar (1968). The paper builds on the pairing-plus-quadrupole (P+Q) model developed by Kisslinger and Sorensen (1963) and Bes and Sorensen (1969), and the boson expansion method reviewed by Klein (1991). It enables future microscopic quantum theory of large-amplitude collective motion (LACM) applicable to shape coexistence phenomena, oblate-prolate shape fluctuations in nuclei such as \(^{68}\)Se and \(^{76}\)Se, and vibrational modes near the neutron drip line where weakly bound single-particle states and continuum excitations become important.
