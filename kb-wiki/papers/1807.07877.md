# Low-energy corrections to the eikonal description of elastic scattering and breakup of one-neutron halo nuclei in nuclear-dominated reactions

> Background: The eikonal approximation is a high-energy reaction model which is very computationally efficient and provides a simple interpretation of the collision. Unfortunately, it is not valid at energies around 10 MeV/nucleon, the range of energy of HIE-ISOLDE at CERN and the future ReA12 at MSU. Fukui etal. [Phys. Rev. C 90, 034617 (2014)] have shown that a simple semiclassical correction of the projectile-target deflection could improve the description of breakup of halo nuclei on heavy targets down to 20 MeV/nucleon. Purpose: We study two similar corrections, which aim at improving the projectile-target relative motion within the eikonal approximation, with the goal to extend its range of validity down to 10 MeV/nucleon in nuclear-dominated collisions, viz. on light targets. The semiclassical correction substitutes the impact parameter by the distance of closest approach of the corresponding classical trajectory. The exact continued $S$-matrix correction replaces the eikonal phase by the exact phase shift. Both corrections successfully describe the elastic scattering of one-neutron halo nuclei. Method: We extend these corrections and study their efficiency in describing the breakup channel. We evaluate them in the case of $^{11}\mathrm{Be}$ impinging on $^{12}\mathrm{C}$ at 20 and 10 MeV/nucleon. Results: Albeit efficient to reproduce the elastic channel, these corrections do not improve the description of the breakup of halo nuclei within the eikonal approximation down to 20 MeV/nucleon. Conclusions: Our analysis of these corrections shows that improving the projectile-target relative motion is not the ultimate answer to extend the eikonal approximation down to low energies. We suggest another avenue to reach this goal.

## Key claim

The semiclassical correction (using a complex distance of closest approach) and the exact continued S-matrix correction, which both improve the eikonal description of elastic scattering of one-neutron halo nuclei on light targets down to 10 MeV/nucleon, do **not** improve the eikonal description of breakup observables at these energies; the authors conclude that improving the projectile-target relative motion is not sufficient to extend the eikonal approximation to low-energy breakup.

## Method

The authors computed elastic scattering and breakup cross sections for the one-neutron halo nucleus 11Be impinging on a 12C target at 20 and 10 MeV/nucleon. They compared the standard eikonal approximation and the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) against Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) calculations taken as reference. They then tested two corrections: (1) a semiclassical correction replacing the impact parameter by the complex distance of closest approach of the classical trajectory, and (2) an exact continued S-matrix correction replacing the eikonal phase by the exact phase shift for each fragment.

## Key numbers

* The 11Be projectile is modeled with a 10Be core and a valence neutron bound by 0.5 MeV.
* The d-wave resonance in the 10Be-n continuum is produced at energy E_d = 1.27 MeV with width Gamma_d = 0.16 MeV.
* Calculations were performed at beam energies of 20 and 10 MeV/nucleon.
* The CDCC model space included c-n partial waves up to l_max = 6, continuum energy up to E_max = 10 MeV, and total angular momentum up to J_max = 20,000.
* The standard eikonal model underestimates the breakup cross section by approximately 30% over the whole energy range at 20 MeV/nucleon.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Fukui et al. (2014) who showed a semiclassical correction improves breakup description on heavy targets down to 20 MeV/nucleon, and by Hebborn and Capel (2017) who showed the same correction works for elastic scattering on light targets. The present paper tests these corrections for breakup on light targets and finds they fail, contradicting the expectation that the same approach would work for all reaction channels. The authors suggest that future work should address the missing couplings between angular momenta caused by neglecting second-order derivatives in the eikonal Schrodinger equation, possibly through a perturbative approach.
