# Introduction to Effective Field Theories in QCD

> We present a simple introduction to the techniques of effective field theory (EFT) and their application to QCD. For problems with more than one energy scale, the EFT approach is a useful alternative to more traditional model-building strategies. The most relevant such problem for this discussion is that of making contact between QCD and the hadronic phase of matter. As a simple example, an EFT calculation of the bound states of hydrogen within QED is sketched. A more significant demonstration of the power of EFTs, the construction of the chiral Lagrangian and chiral perturbation theory, is also included. The results provide us with the road map to a complete QCD-based theory of nuclear matter at nonzero temperatures and densities, a vital component to a quantitative understanding of the phase transition from hadron gas to quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim

Effective field theory (EFT) provides a controlled, systematic expansion for constructing QCD-based theories of the hadronic phase, but the approach requires a conceptual leap beyond mesonic EFT because nucleons bind and the nucleon-nucleon scattering length is large, causing perturbative expansions in density to fail before the quark-gluon plasma phase transition.

## Method

The authors present pedagogical lecture notes introducing the techniques of effective field theory, beginning with the formal procedure of integrating out high-energy degrees of freedom above a cutoff Λ to obtain an effective Lagrangian, then applying Weinberg's "theorem" that the most general Lagrangian consistent with symmetries yields the most general S-matrix. They illustrate the method with a classical example (gravitational potential near Earth's surface), then derive the non-relativistic QED effective Lagrangian by integrating out antiparticle components in the heavy-fermion formalism, and finally analyze two-fermion scattering in QED to show how infrared-enhanced diagrams from two-fermion reducible contributions require resummation via the Lippmann-Schwinger equation.

## Key numbers

- The typical energy scale of QCD: M_QCD ~ 1 GeV
- The typical momentum scale of nucleons in a nucleus: M_nuc ~ 100 MeV (includes pion decay constant f_π ≈ 93 MeV, pion mass, and delta-nucleon mass difference)
- The typical binding energy scale: M_nuc²/M_QCD ~ 10 MeV
- Binding energy of ⁴He: 28.296 MeV
- Binding energy per nucleon of infinite nuclear matter: 16 MeV
- Electron mass: m_e ≈ 0.511 MeV
- Fine-structure constant: α ≈ 1/137
- Hydrogen atom electron momentum: p ~ α m_e = 3.6 keV
- Hydrogen atom binding energy: B ~ (1/2)α² m_e = 13.6 eV
- Chiral symmetry breaking scale: M_χ = 4π f_π
- The expansion parameter for dilute gases: ρ a³, where a is the scattering length; bound-state physics occurs when ρ^(1/3) ≳ 1/a

## Context

This work extends the pedagogical tradition of EFT introductions for nuclear physics, building on reviews by van Kolck (1999) and lectures by Phillips (2002). It references the EFT expansion of the pion gas free energy density at zero baryon density by Gerber and Leutwyler (1989), which reveals a transition temperature T_c where the expansion diverges, and notes that including higher-mass mesons (Rafelski) yields results in good agreement with lattice QCD. The paper enables the construction of a complete QCD-based theory of nuclear matter at nonzero temperatures and densities, which is necessary for quantitative understanding of the hadron gas to quark-gluon plasma phase transition.
