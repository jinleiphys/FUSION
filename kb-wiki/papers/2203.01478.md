# Simulating excited states of the Lipkin model on a quantum computer

> We simulate the excited states of the Lipkin model using the recently proposed Quantum Equation of Motion (qEOM) method. The qEOM generalizes the EOM on classical computers and gives access to collective excitations based on quasi-boson operators $\hat{O}^\dagger_n(α)$ of increasing configuration complexity $α$. We show, in particular, that the accuracy strongly depends on the fermion to qubit encoding. Standard encoding leads to large errors, but the use of symmetries and the Gray code reduces the quantum resources and improves significantly the results on current noisy quantum devices. With this encoding scheme, we use IBM quantum machines to compute the energy spectrum for a system of $N=2, 3$ and $4$ particles and compare the accuracy against the exact solution. We found that the results of the approach with $α= 2$, an analog of the second random phase approximation (SRPA), are, in principle, more accurate than with $α= 1$, which corresponds to the random phase approximation (RPA), but the SRPA is more amenable to noise for large coupling strengths. Thus, the proposed scheme shows potential for achieving higher spectroscopic accuracy by implementations with higher configuration complexity, if a proper error mitigation method is applied.

## Key claim

The accuracy of the Quantum Equation of Motion (qEOM) method for simulating excited states of the Lipkin model depends strongly on the fermion-to-qubit encoding scheme, and while the second random phase approximation (SRPA, alpha=2) is in principle more accurate than the random phase approximation (RPA, alpha=1), the SRPA is more susceptible to noise for large coupling strengths, requiring proper error mitigation to achieve higher spectroscopic accuracy.

## Method

The authors implemented the qEOM algorithm on IBM quantum computers for the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick (LMG) model with N=2, 3, and 4 particles, using a Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) to prepare the ground state and then computing commutator expectation values for the generalized eigenvalue equation matrices A, B, C, and D on the quantum device. They compared two encoding schemes: a standard Fock-space encoding and a more efficient scheme exploiting symmetries of the Hamiltonian combined with Gray code encoding to reduce quantum resources. The excitation operators were constructed at configuration complexity alpha=1 (RPA) and alpha=2 (SRPA), and the resulting energy spectra were compared against exact analytical solutions and classical Hartree-Fock and RPA results.

## Key numbers

- For N=2 particles, the exact energy eigenvalues are E = +sqrt(epsilon^2 + V^2) - W, E = -2W, and E = -sqrt(epsilon^2 + V^2) - W, corresponding to the states |1,1>, |1,0>, and |1,-1> respectively
- The Hartree-Fock ground state energy for the LMG model is E_HF = -(N/2)*epsilon for weak coupling (tilde{v} < 1) and E_HF = -(N/2)*[epsilon^2 + (N-1)^2*V^2]/[2(N-1)V] for strong coupling (tilde{v} > 1), where tilde{v} = V(N-1)/epsilon
- The RPA ground state energy is E_RPA = E_HF + (omega - A)/2, with omega = sqrt(A^2 - |B|^2), where A and B have piecewise definitions depending on whether tilde{v} < 1 or tilde{v} > 1
- The effective interaction strength boundary between weak and strong coupling regions is tilde{v} = 1
- The full Fock-space encoding requires a Hilbert space of size 2^(2N), while the individual spin basis reduces this to 2^N, and further symmetry exploitation using the total spin J = N/2 reduces the problem to matrices of dimension (2J+1) x (2J+1)

## Context

This work extends the qEOM method previously applied in quantum chemistry (Ollitrault, 2020) to nuclear physics using the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model as a benchmark, building upon prior work by Cervia et al. (2021) who introduced an encoding scheme for the Lipkin model and simulated its ground state energy for N=2 particles on a quantum computer. The study enables future investigations of whether higher configuration complexity (alpha > 3) can achieve spectroscopic accuracy for nuclear systems, which remains challenging on classical computers for medium-mass and heavy nuclei with N ~ 100 particles.
