# Modeling Kilonova Light Curves: Dependence on Nuclear Inputs

> The mergers of binary neutron stars, as well as black hole-neutron star systems, are expected to produce an electromagnetic counterpart that can be analyzed to infer the element synthesis that occurred in these events. We investigate one source of uncertainties pertinent to lanthanide-rich outflows: the nuclear inputs to rapid neutron capture nucleosynthesis calculations. We begin by examining thirty-two different combinations of nuclear inputs: eight mass models, two types of spontaneous fission rates, and two types of fission daughter product distributions. We find that such nuclear physics uncertainties typically generate at least one order of magnitude uncertainty in key quantities such as the nuclear heating (one and a half orders of magnitude at one day post-merger), the bolometric luminosity (one order of magnitude at five days post-merger), and the inferred mass of material from the bolometric luminosity (factor of eight when considering the eight to ten days region). Since particular nuclear processes are critical for determining the electromagnetic signal, we provide tables of key nuclei undergoing $β$-decay, $α$-decay, and spontaneous fission important for heating at different times, identifying decays that are common among the many nuclear input combinations.

## Key claim

Nuclear physics uncertainties in mass models, fission rates, and fission daughter product distributions generate at least one order of magnitude uncertainty in key kilonova observables for lanthanide-rich outflows, with the specific magnitude depending on the ejecta electron fraction and the time post-merger.

## Method

The authors used the PRISM reaction network to simulate nucleosynthesis for thirty-two combinations of nuclear inputs: eight mass models (FRDM2012, DZ33, HFB22, HFB27, UNEDF1, SLY4, TF, ETFSI, WS3), two spontaneous fission rate prescriptions (Karpov-Zagrebaev and Xu-Ren), and two fission daughter product distributions (symmetric and double Gaussian). They adopted a parameterized wind trajectory with initial entropy s/k=40 and expansion timescale 20 ms, varying initial electron fraction across eight values (0.02, 0.12, 0.14, 0.16, 0.18, 0.21, 0.24, 0.28). The nucleosynthesis outputs were fed into a semi-analytic light curve model with 100 concentric shells, using analytic thermalization efficiencies from Kasen and Barnes (2019) and temperature-dependent opacities calculated from modified compositions that preserve d-block and f-block mass fractions.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear heating uncertainty spans one and a half orders of magnitude at one day post-merger across the nuclear input combinations
- Bolometric luminosity uncertainty spans one order of magnitude at five days post-merger
- Inferred ejecta mass from bolometric luminosity varies by a factor of eight in the eight to ten day region
- All simulations with Ye=0.24 and Ye=0.16 produce lanthanide plus actinide mass fractions larger than 10^{-3}, the threshold for a red kilonova
- Fiducial model uses ejecta mass 0.05 solar masses and characteristic ejecta velocity 0.15c
- Expansion timescale of 20 ms and initial entropy per baryon s/k=40 were adopted for the base trajectory

## Context

This work extends previous studies of kilonova modeling by Metzger (2010), Barnes (2016), and Wanajo (2018) by systematically surveying a wider range of nuclear mass models and fission properties than earlier works. The results quantify how nuclear input uncertainties propagate through nucleosynthesis calculations to affect light curve predictions, enabling future observers to better interpret the red component of kilonovae from binary neutron star mergers like GW170817. The paper identifies key nuclei undergoing beta-decay, alpha-decay, and spontaneous fission that are common across many nuclear input combinations, providing tables that can guide experimental nuclear physics efforts.
