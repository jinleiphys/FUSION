# Global Trends of Nuclear Radii and Binding Energies from the NUCLEI-PACK Framework

> I present a proof-of-concept study of nuclear radii and binding energies within NUCLEI-PACK, a novel semi-classical framework based on optimized sphere packing of nucleons and clusters. In this approach, proton and neutron positions are determined through a constrained packing algorithm with uniform nucleon radii, followed by global optimization across the nuclear chart ($1 \leq A \leq 250$). From these geometric configurations, I compute charge and matter radii, as well as binding energies. The model successfully reproduces global trends in nuclear size and stability, while maintaining transparency and computational efficiency. A fit of the Coulomb and surface terms of the semi-empirical mass formula shows remarkable agreement with theoretical expectations. These results establish the feasibility of NUCLEI-PACK as a systematic tool for describing nuclear bulk properties across the chart of nuclides.

# Global Trends of Nuclear Radii and Binding Energies from the NUCLEI-PACK Framework

## Key claim

The NUCLEI-PACK framework, a semi-classical sphere-packing model using equal-radius nucleons, reproduces global trends in nuclear charge radii, matter radii, and binding energies across the nuclear chart (1 ≤ A ≤ 250) as a proof-of-concept, but the equal-sphere assumption (protons and neutrons having identical radii) is a simplification that the author identifies as needing replacement with binary sphere packing for more realistic results.

## Method

The author initializes nuclear geometries from best-known solutions of the Packing Equal Spheres in a Sphere (PESS) benchmark for each mass number A, rescaling coordinates so all nucleons have radius r₀ = 1.0 fm. Protons are assigned to evenly spaced radial intervals within the sorted list of nucleon distances from the center using a floor function, with the remaining nucleons assigned as neutrons. Charge and matter radii are computed from these coordinates using both point-nucleon and Gaussian-smearing models (σ = 0.85 fm), while binding energies are calculated via a Woods–Saxon mean-field potential plus a two-body Coulomb sum over all proton pairs, with parameters fitted to experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit scaling factor for the microscopic Coulomb potential compared to the semi-empirical mass formula: C = 0.9955, remarkably close to unity
- Surface nucleon count scaling: N_surface ≈ c A^(2/3) with c = 3.25, implying an effective energy penalty per surface nucleon of a_n ≈ 5.2 MeV (derived from a_surf ≈ 17 MeV)
- Woods–Saxon mean-field fit parameters: V_r = 13.35 MeV, r_v = 1.2 fm, a_v = 0.6 fm, V_s = 4.5 MeV, r_s = 2.0 fm, a_s = 1.2 fm
- Intrinsic proton charge radius squared: ⟨R_p²⟩ = (0.877 fm)²; intrinsic neutron charge radius squared: ⟨R_n²⟩ = -0.1161 fm²; Darwin–Foldy correction: Δ_DF = 0.033 fm²
- Gaussian smearing width for nucleon densities: σ_p = σ_n = 0.85 fm
- Fermi formula half-density radius: c = 1.31 A^(1/3) - 0.84 fm, with diffuseness a = 0.51 fm

## Context

This work extends the geometric tradition of nuclear modeling that includes Meitner and Frisch's (1939) analogy between nucleons and random close packing of hard spheres, Pauling's (1965) close-packed spheron theory, and the face-centered-cubic lattice theory of nuclei. It also builds on recent applications of random close packing to estimate nucleon densities in neutron stars (Bra23), infer effective nucleon sizes (Kai24), and explain neutron enrichment via binary-mixture RCP theory (Zac22, Anzivino 2024). The framework enables future incorporation of quantum effects such as pairing and spin–orbit coupling, and applications to neutron-skin thickness, r-process nucleosynthesis, and neutron star composition.
