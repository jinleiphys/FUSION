# Interplay between quantum shells and orientation in quasi-fission

> The quasi-fission mechanism hinders fusion in heavy systems through breakup within zeptoseconds into two fragments with partial mass equilibration. Its dependence on the structure of both the collision partners and the final fragments is a key question. Our original approach is to combine an experimental measurement of the fragments' mass-angle correlations in $^{40}$Ca$+^{238}$U with microscopic quantum calculations. We demonstrate an unexpected interplay between the orientation of the prolate deformed $^{238}$U with quantum shell effects in the fragments. In particular, calculations show that only collisions with the tip of $^{238}$U produce quasi-fission fragments in the magic $Z=82$ region, whilst collisions with the side are the only one which may result in fusion.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates an unexpected interplay between the orientation of the prolate deformed 238U nucleus and quantum shell effects in quasi-fission fragments, showing that only collisions with the tip of 238U produce fragments near the magic Z=82 region, while collisions with the side are the only ones that may result in fusion.

## Method
Experimental mass-angle distributions (MAD) of fragments from 40Ca+238U collisions were measured at three energies near the capture barrier using position-sensitive multiwire proportional counters. Time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations with the SLy4d Skyrme interaction, including spin-orbit coupling, were performed for axial (tip) and equatorial (side) orientations of the prolate 238U nucleus at various angular momenta. The TDHF results were compared to the experimental MAD by assuming Gaussian fluctuations around the calculated fragment masses and angles.

## Key numbers
- Measured MAD at center-of-mass energies of 186.6 MeV (~0.95 times the capture barrier B), 205.9 MeV (~1.04B), and 225.4 MeV (~1.14B)
- TDHF calculations show axial collisions produce quasi-fission fragments with mass ratio MR ~0.22-0.27 for the light fragment
- Contact time for axial orientation is less than 10 zeptoseconds, while equatorial orientation shows contact times over 30 zeptoseconds
- Total kinetic energy of final fragments from equatorial collisions predicted to be ~243 MeV
- Ratio of fusion to capture cross-sections from TDHF: 0.09 ± 0.07 at 205.9 MeV and 0.16 ± 0.06 at 225.4 MeV
- Axial collisions produce heavy fragments with proton number Z close to 82 (magic number) and neutron number N ~122

## Context
This work extends experimental studies of quasi-fission by Tok85, She87, Hin92, Hin95, Hin96, Itk04, Kny07, Hin08, Nis08, Koz10, Rie11, Itk11, Lin12, Nis12, Sim12a, Rie13, Wil13, and Koz14, which identified the roles of deformation, shell structure, fissility, and energy in quasi-fission. The TDHF approach improves upon classical dynamical models (Dia01, Ada03, Zag05, Ari09, Min10, Ari12) by providing a quantum microscopic description without parametrized shapes. The findings are relevant to understanding fusion hindrance in reactions forming superheavy elements, where all planned targets are deformed.
