# Improvements to the macroscopic-microscopic approach of nuclear fission

> The well established macroscopic-microscopic (mac-mic) description of nuclear fission enables the prediction of fission fragment yields for a broad range of fissioning systems. In this work, we present several key enhancements to this approach. We improve upon the microscopic sector of nuclear potential energy surfaces by magnifying the resolution of the Lipkin-Nogami equations and strengthening the Strutinsky procedure, thus reducing spurious effects from the continuum. We further present a novel deterministic method for calculating fission dynamics under the assumption of strongly damped nucleonic motion. Our technique directly determines the evolution of the scissioned shape distribution according to the number of random walk steps rather than the statistical accumulation of fission events. We show that our new technique is equivalent to the Metropolis random walk pioneered over the past decade by Randrup and colleagues. It further improves upon it, as we remove the need for altering the nuclear landscape via a biased potential. With our final improvement, we calculate fission fragments mass and charge distributions using particle number projection, which affords the simultaneous calculation of both mass and charge yield distributions. Fission fragments are thus calculated from the quantum mechanical $A$-body states of the potential energy surface rather than the collective mass asymmetry variable ($α_{\rm g}$) of the Finite-Range Liquid-Drop Model (FRLDM) used in past work. We highlight the success of our enhancements by predicting the odd-even staggering and the charge polarization for the neutron-induced fission of ${}^{233}$U and ${}^{235}$U.

## Key claim

The authors present several enhancements to the macroscopic-microscopic (mac-mic) description of nuclear fission, including a novel deterministic method for calculating fission dynamics under strongly damped nucleonic motion that is equivalent to the Metropolis random walk approach of Randrup and colleagues but removes the need for a biased potential; the method is demonstrated for neutron-induced fission of 233U and 235U, but the paper does not claim general applicability to all fissioning systems without further validation.

## Method

The potential energy surface (PES) is calculated using the macroscopic Finite-Range Liquid-Drop Model (FRLDM) with microscopic corrections, where the Strutinsky procedure is improved by using a basis of N0=20 harmonic oscillator shells (up from N0=12 in past work) to reduce spurious continuum effects, and the Lipkin-Nogami equations are solved with a new analytical Jacobian method. Fission dynamics are simulated using a deterministic algorithm that directly computes the evolution of the scissioned shape distribution according to the number of random walk steps, rather than accumulating statistics from many Monte Carlo trajectories. Fragment mass and charge distributions are obtained using particle number projection from the quantum mechanical A-body states of the PES, replacing the previous reliance on the collective mass asymmetry variable (alpha_g) of the FRLDM.

## Key numbers

- The Strutinsky procedure uses N0=20 harmonic oscillator shells, tested up to N0=30 with no anomalies found, compared to the previous limit of N0=12 in past work.
- The level-density parameter is taken as a = A/8 for calculating the local effective temperature T = [E*(chi)/a]^(1/2).
- The neck radius threshold for defining scissioned configurations is denoted r_neck (no numerical value given in the text).
- The pairing gap Delta is approximated by an effective pairing gap: bar_Delta = r_mic B_s / N^(1/3) for neutrons and bar_Delta = r_mic B_s / Z^(1/3) for protons.

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic fission approach pioneered by Randrup and colleagues (2011-2013) and applied by Moller and collaborators (2012-2015) and Mumpower (2020), who used Metropolis random walk methods on the FRLDM potential energy surface. The improvements enable faster calculation of fission fragment yields without statistical uncertainty from Monte Carlo sampling, particularly for systems with excitation energy close to the fission barrier, and allow simultaneous prediction of both mass and charge yield distributions through particle number projection rather than relying solely on the collective asymmetry coordinate.
