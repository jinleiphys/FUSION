# Mapping the QCD Phase Diagram

> I review recent theoretical developments which show how a key qualitative feature of the QCD phase diagram, namely a critical point which in a sense defines the landscape which heavy ion collision experiments are seeking to map, can be discovered. The map of the phase diagram which I sketch is based on reasonable inference from universality, lattice gauge theory and models; the discovery of the critical point would provide an experimental foundation for the central qualitative feature of the landscape. I also review recent progress in our understanding of cold, dense quark matter, as may occur in the cores of neutron stars. In this regime, quarks form Cooper pairs. The formation of such superconducting phases requires only weak attractive interactions, as provided by one-gluon exchange at asymptotically high density; these phases may nevertheless break chiral symmetry (by locking flavor symmetries to color symmetry) and may have excitations which are indistinguishable from those in a confined phase. Mapping this part of the phase diagram will require a better understanding of how the presence of color superconductivity and color-flavor locking affects neutron star phenomenology.

# Mapping the QCD Phase Diagram

## Key claim

The QCD phase diagram likely features a critical point E at nonzero temperature and baryon chemical potential where the line of first-order phase transitions ends, but this picture is based on "reasonable inference from universality, lattice gauge theory and models" rather than experimental confirmation; the discovery of this critical point would transform the map from a theoretical sketch into one with a solid experimental foundation.

## Method

The author reviews theoretical developments combining universality arguments, lattice gauge theory results, and model calculations (including Nambu-Jona-Lasinio-type models, random matrix models, and instanton liquid models) to construct the QCD phase diagram as a function of temperature T and baryon chemical potential μ. For the high-density regime, the analysis uses weak-coupling methods valid at asymptotically high density and model calculations with parameters fixed to vacuum phenomenology to estimate gaps and critical temperatures in color-superconducting phases.

## Key numbers

- Lattice simulations suggest T_c ∼ 140–170 MeV for the chiral transition at zero baryon density
- At SPS with √s = 17 GeV (beam energy 158 AGeV), fireballs freeze out near μ ∼ 200 MeV
- At AGS with beam energy 11 AGeV (√s = 5 GeV), fireballs freeze out near μ ∼ 500–600 MeV
- Crude models suggest μ_E could be ∼ 600–800 MeV in the absence of the strange quark, with Δμ ∼ 120 MeV for μ_E ∼ 800 MeV
- Conservative estimate: Δμ/μ_E ∼ 20–30% for planning experimental strategy
- NA49 measured √F = 1.002 ± 0.002 for the ratio of the width of the true event-by-event distribution of mean p_T to the width in mixed events
- Critical fluctuations can increase √F by 10–20%, fifty times the statistical error in the present measurement
- Gaps in color-superconducting phases range from several tens of MeV up to about 100 MeV
- Critical temperatures for diquark condensates can be as large as about T_c ∼ 50 MeV
- At asymptotically high density, the gap takes the form Δ ∼ 256π⁴ μ g(μ)⁻⁵ exp[-3π²/√2 g(μ)]

## Context

This review extends the line of work on the QCD phase diagram by Pisarski and Wilczek (1984) on the O(4) universality class of the chiral transition, and by Stephanov, Rajagopal, and Shuryak (1998, 1999) on the critical point signatures. It also builds on the color superconductivity work by Barrois (1977), Bailin and Love (1984), Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1998), and Rapp et al. (1998). The paper enables experimental searches for the critical point at the SPS and RHIC, and motivates further theoretical work connecting neutron star phenomenology to the high-density phases of QCD.
