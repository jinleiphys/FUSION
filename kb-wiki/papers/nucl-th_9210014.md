# Monte Carlo methods for the nuclear shell model

> We present novel Monte Carlo methods for treating the interacting shell model that allow exact calculations much larger than those heretofore possible. The two-body interaction is linearized by an auxiliary field; Monte Carlo evaluation of the resulting functional integral gives ground-state or thermal expectation values of few-body operators. The ``sign problem'' generic to quantum Monte Carlo calculations is absent in a number of cases. We discuss the favorable scaling of these methods with nucleon numb er and basis size and their suitability to parallel computation.

# Monte Carlo methods for the nuclear shell model

## Key claim

The authors present Monte Carlo methods for the interacting nuclear shell model that enable exact calculations for systems much larger than those accessible by direct diagonalization, but the method is demonstrated only for interactions of the "pairing plus multipole" form and the sign problem is absent only for certain cases (e.g., T_Z=0 nuclei with that interaction form); for general residual interactions the sign problem can arise and is only partially mitigated by ad hoc adjustments.

## Method

The two-body Hamiltonian is linearized into a sum of squares of one-body operators via a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation, introducing auxiliary fields at each imaginary-time slice. The imaginary-time evolution operator exp(-beta H) is then evaluated as a functional integral over these auxiliary fields, with the integrand expressed in terms of one-body evolution operators acting on Slater determinants. Monte Carlo integration (Metropolis algorithm) over the auxiliary fields yields ground-state or thermal expectation values of few-body operators, with the quantum mechanics reduced to matrix manipulations on N x N matrices and A N-dimensional single-particle wavefunctions.

## Key numbers

- For 24Mg in the sd-shell (24/8 nucleons/orbitals), the J=0,T=0 basis size is 325 states (28,503 m-scheme determinants); Monte Carlo required 607 node-hours for 3000 samples, versus 0.04 hours for diagonalization
- For 28Si in the sd-shell (24/12), the J=0,T=0 basis size is 839 states (93,710 m-scheme determinants); Monte Carlo required 670 node-hours versus 0.4 hours for diagonalization
- For 48Cr in the pf-shell (40/8), the J=0,T=0 basis size is 9,741 states (1,963,461 m-scheme determinants); Monte Carlo required 3,353 node-hours versus an estimated 40 hours for diagonalization
- For 60Zn in the pf-shell (40/20), the J=0,T=0 basis size is 5,053,574 states (2.5 x 10^9 m-scheme determinants); Monte Carlo required 3,558 node-hours versus an estimated 4 x 10^6 hours for diagonalization
- The number of auxiliary fields scales as the square of the number of single-particle orbits: 144 for the sd-shell and 400 for the pf-shell
- Useful results are obtained for beta |V_alpha| less than or approximately equal to 20, compared to beta |V_alpha| ~ 120 for a typical condensed-matter Hubbard model at 100 K

## Context

This work extends Monte Carlo methods previously applied to the Hubbard model (White, Scalapino, Sugar, Loh, Gubernaitis, and Scalettar, 1989) and coordinate-space fermion systems (Sugiyama and Koonin, 1986) to the nuclear shell model, which differs in having smaller numbers of single-particle orbitals, fully non-local operators, and smaller beta values of interest. The method goes beyond the Static Path Approximation (SPA) of Arve, Bertsch, Lauritzen, and Puddu (1988) and others, which used uncontrolled approximations with time-independent fields and only schematic interactions. The approach enables full pf-shell calculations of nuclei like 48Cr that were previously at the computational limit of diagonalization codes such as OXBASH (Rae, Etchegoyen, and Brown) or the Glasgow-Los Alamos-Seattle code (Haxton), and makes calculations for 60Zn feasible where diagonalization would require in excess of 2 Gb mass storage and 1000 times more memory.
