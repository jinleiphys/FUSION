# Weak Magnetism Correction to Allowed Beta-decay for Reactor Antineutrino Spectra

> The weak magnetism correction and its uncertainty to nuclear beta-decay play a major role in determining the significance of the reactor neutrino anomaly. Here we examine the common approximation used for one-body weak magnetism in the calculation of fission antineutrino spectra, wherein matrix elements of the orbital angular momentum operator contribution to the magnetic dipole current are assumed to be proportional to those of the spin operator. Although we find this approximation invalid for a large set of nuclear structure situations, we conclude that it is valid for the relevant allowed beta-decays between fission fragments. In particular, the uncertainty in the fission antineutrino due to the uncertainty in the one-body weak magnetism correction is found to be less than 1%. Thus, the dominant uncertainty from weak magnetism for reactor neutrino fluxes lies in the uncertainty in the two-body meson-exchange magnetic dipole current.

# Weak Magnetism Correction to Allowed Beta-decay for Reactor Antineutrino Spectra

## Key claim
The common approximation that matrix elements of the orbital angular momentum operator are proportional to those of the spin operator (with proportionality constant -1/2) is invalid for a large set of nuclear structure situations, but it is valid for the specific allowed beta-decays between fission fragments that dominate reactor antineutrino spectra. The resulting uncertainty in the fission antineutrino spectrum from the one-body weak magnetism correction is less than 1%, meaning the dominant uncertainty lies in the two-body meson-exchange magnetic dipole current.

## Method
The authors examined the ratio δ_{LS} = ⟨J_f||Λ||J_i⟩ / ⟨J_f||Σ||J_i⟩, which determines the orbital contribution to the weak magnetism correction. They performed Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the Skyrme SLY4 parameterization with a zero-range density-dependent pairing force and the Lipkin-Nogami method for particle-number restoration, studying fission fragments from ²³⁵U, ²³⁸U, ²³⁹Pu, and ²⁴¹Pu. They also carried out shell-model calculations using the glekpn interaction and model space for A∼100 fission fragments, computing allowed beta-decay transitions to the lowest 20 positive and negative parity states for eight nuclei contributing significantly to reactor antineutrino spectra.

## Key numbers
- The average value of δ_{LS} for fission fragment transitions was found to be −0.42 ± 0.99, based on 1897 data points covering 97.43% of the transitions examined.
- The uncertainty in the fission antineutrino spectrum due to the one-body weak magnetism correction is less than 1% when restricting to two standard deviations.
- For a single beta-decay transition with endpoint energy 6.0 + m_e c² MeV, changing δ_{LS} to ±4.0 (more than three standard deviations from the mean) changes the spectrum shape by less than 2% at any energy.
- The HFB calculations used a pairing strength V₀ of −272.76 MeV fm³, which gives an empirical neutron pairing gap for ¹²⁰Sn of 1.245 MeV.
- The equivalent-spectrum cutoff was 60 MeV, and calculations were performed in a spherical basis of 14 major harmonic-oscillator shells.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous examination of finite-size corrections using Zemach moments (Zemach 2016) and addresses the weak magnetism approximation used in the reevaluations of reactor antineutrino spectra by Mueller (2011) and Huber (2011), which led to the reactor neutrino anomaly. The approximation δ_{LS} = −1/2 was previously assumed in the work of Vogel (2007) and Hayes (2014). The paper shows that while this approximation fails for general beta-decay transitions (as demonstrated by the broad distribution of δ_{LS} for nuclei with neutrons and protons in the same shell), it works for fission fragments because their dominant transitions involve spin-orbit partners (πg9/2 to νg7/2 for A∼100 and πh11/2 to νh9/2 for A>130). The results enable future work to focus on the two-body meson-exchange current contribution to weak magnetism, which remains unexamined and is identified as the dominant remaining uncertainty.
