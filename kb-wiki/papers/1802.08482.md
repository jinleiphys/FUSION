# The electric quadrupole channel of the 7.8 eV $~^{229}\mathrm{Th}$ transition

> The unique isomeric transition at 7.8 eV in $~^{229}\mathrm{Th}$ has a magnetic dipole ($M1$) and an electric quadrupole ($E2$) multipole mixing. So far, the $E2$ component has been widely disregarded. Here, we investigate the nuclear physics nature and the impact of the $E2$ decay channel for the nuclear coupling to the atomic shell based on the newest theoretical predictions for the corresponding reduced nuclear transition probabilities. Our results show that the contribution of the $E2$ channel is dominant or at least of the same order of magnitude for internal conversion or electronic bridge transitions involving the atomic orbitals $7p$, $6d$ and $5f$. Notable exceptions are the internal conversion of the $7s$ electron and the electronic bridge between the electronic states $7s$ and $7p$, for which the $M1$ component dominates by two to three orders of magnitude. Caution is therefore advised when considering isomeric excitation or decay via nuclear coupling to the atomic shell, as the involved orbitals determine which multipole transition component dominates.

## Key claim

The electric quadrupole (E2) component of the 7.8 eV isomeric transition in ²²⁹Th, previously considered negligible, is shown to dominate or match the magnetic dipole (M1) component for internal conversion and electronic bridge processes involving the 7p, 6d, and 5f atomic orbitals, but only when the 7s orbital is not the primary participant.

## Method

The authors calculated internal conversion (IC) rates for neutral Th atoms and Th⁺/Th²⁺ ions using relativistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock (DHF) wave functions from the GRASP2K package and continuum wave functions from the RATIP package, incorporating the most recent theoretical reduced transition probabilities B(M1)=0.0076 W.u. and B(E2)=27 W.u. from Minkov and Pálffy (2017). Electronic bridge (EB) enhancement factors for Th³⁺ ions were computed using a B-spline basis in a cavity of radius 90 a.u. with virtual orbitals up to n=30, with M1 matrix elements corrected via the random phase approximation (RPA) and E2 matrix elements taken directly from DHF calculations.

## Key numbers

- B(M1) = 0.0076 W.u. and B(E2) = 27 W.u. for the isomeric transition, from Minkov and Pálffy (2017)
- Radiative width ratio Γ_γ(E2)/Γ_γ(M1) = 6.9 × 10⁻¹⁰, confirming E2 is negligible for radiative decay
- For IC in neutral Th: Γ_IC(E2)/Γ_IC(M1) = 2.9 × 10⁻³ for the 7s orbital, but 23 for the 7p₃/₂ orbital and 2.7 for the 6d₅/₂ orbital
- For IC from the 5f6d² state in Th⁺ at 30223 cm⁻¹: E2 IC rates range from 35 to 410 s⁻¹, comparable to or exceeding M1 rates (36 to 225 s⁻¹) for final states with minimum energies 9.0–10.8 eV
- For EB in Th³⁺ at E_m = 7.6 eV: β(E2) = 3.4 × 10⁷ for 5f₅/₂ → 7s₁/₂, compared to β(M1) = 0.082; β(E2) = 6.6 × 10⁸ for 7s₁/₂ → 7p₁/₂, compared to β(M1) = 23

## Context

This work extends the nuclear structure model of Minkov and Pálffy (2017), which predicted the reduced transition probabilities for the ²²⁹Th isomer, and contradicts earlier assumptions from Dyk98 and Tkalya (2015) that the E2 component contributes less than 10⁻³ to IC rates. It also updates the EB calculations of Porsev and Flambaum (2010), which used B(E2)=1 W.u. and concluded E2 was negligible. The results enable more accurate modeling of isomer excitation and decay via atomic shell coupling, particularly for experimental schemes involving Th ions where the 7s orbital is absent, and inform ongoing efforts to directly measure the isomeric transition energy.
