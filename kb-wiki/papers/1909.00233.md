# Magic numbers of cylindrical symmetry

> In nuclear physics a magic number is defined as the nucleon number, which is separated by a significantly large single-particle energy gap from the next nucleon. Magic numbers define the nuclear shells, which are considered to be active, only if they are partially occupied by nucleons. As a consequence the single particle interactions of the valence nucleons lead to the description of the collective properties of the whole nucleus in the shell model theory. But phenomena as the island of inversion, the shape coexistence and the break down of the N=20 magic number reveal that the above definition of a magic number is deficient. A complementary definition should rely on the selection rules of the single particle interactions. Specifically the selection rules of the quadrupole-quadrupole interaction lead to two sets of magic numbers, namely the harmonic oscillator magic numbers 2, 8 20, 40, 70, 112, ... and the spin-orbit SO-like magic numbers 2, 6, 14, 28, 50, 82, 126, ... The underlying symmetries are respectively the spherical symmetry of the 3D isotropic harmonic oscillator and the cylindrical symmetry of the 3D anisotropic harmonic oscillator with two frequencies equal. The above two sets of magic numbers along with the Elliott SU(3) symmetry framework predict long standing and puzzling phenomena in nuclear physics.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard definition of magic numbers (based solely on large single-particle energy gaps) is deficient, and proposes a complementary definition based on the selection rules of the quadrupole-quadrupole interaction. This leads to two distinct sets of magic numbers: the harmonic oscillator set (2, 8, 20, 40, 70, 112, ...) from spherical symmetry and the spin-orbit SO-like set (2, 6, 14, 28, 50, 82, 126, ...) from cylindrical symmetry, which together with the Elliott SU(3) framework can explain shape coexistence without free parameters.

## Method

The authors derive the quadrupole-quadrupole interaction from the Taylor expansion of a central two-body force, showing it emerges from the fourth-order term. They then analyze the Nilsson model Hamiltonian for deformed nuclei, which contains a distortion term proportional to the \(q_0\) component of the quadrupole moment, and connect it to the Elliott SU(3) symmetry of the three-dimensional isotropic harmonic oscillator. Using the SU(3) to SU(2) x U(1) decomposition appropriate for cylindrical symmetry, they show how the two sets of magic numbers arise from the selection rules of the quadrupole-quadrupole interaction, and apply the highest-weight irreducible representation method to predict ground state properties.

## Key numbers

* Harmonic oscillator magic numbers: 2, 8, 20, 40, 70, 112, 168, ...
* Spin-orbit SO-like magic numbers: 2, 6, 14, 28, 50, 82, 126, ...
* The deformation parameter \(\delta\) is related to the Bohr-Mottelson \(\beta\) as \(\delta \approx 0.95 \beta\)
* The quadrupole-quadrupole interaction strength is given by \(\chi/2 = \hbar\omega / (4N_0)\), where \(\hbar\omega\) is measured in MeV
* The dimensionless mean square radius used in deformation formulas is \(\bar r^2 = 0.87^2 A^{1/3}\)

## Context

This work extends the Elliott SU(3) symmetry framework (Elliott, 1958) and the Nilsson model (Nilsson, 1955) by providing a unified explanation for shape coexistence phenomena that have remained puzzling, such as the island of inversion and the breakdown of the N=20 magic number. It builds on earlier approximate SU(3) approaches beyond the sd shell (pseudo-SU(3), quasi-SU(3), and proxy-SU(3)) and aims to predict the borders of shape coexistence regions and the deformation parameters of coexisting bands without any free parameters, addressing open questions about why shape coexistence appears in certain regions of the nuclear chart but not others.
