# Extrapolating from neural network models: a cautionary tale

> We present three different methods to estimate error bars on the predictions made using a neural network. All of them represent lower bounds for the extrapolation errors. For example, we did not include an analysis on robustness against small perturbations of the input data. At first, we illustrate the methods through a simple toy model, then, we apply them to some realistic cases related to nuclear masses. By using theoretical data simulated either with a liquid-drop model or a Skyrme energy density functional, we benchmark the extrapolation performance of the neural network in regions of the Segrè chart far away from the ones used for the training and validation. Finally, we discuss how error bars can help identifying when the extrapolation becomes too uncertain and thus unreliable

## Key claim

The three methods presented for estimating error bars on neural network predictions all represent lower bounds for the extrapolation errors, and the analysis does not include robustness against small perturbations of the input data.

## Method

The authors trained feed-forward neural networks with 3 layers (16-8-16 neurons) using ReLU activation on synthetic nuclear binding energies generated from a liquid-drop model and the Skyrme SLy4 energy density functional. They separated the theoretical masses into a training/validation set corresponding to experimentally measured nuclei (approximately 2400 nuclei) and an extrapolation set, then benchmarked three error estimation methods: epoch-averaging (storing weights every 1000 epochs), bootstrap (100 resampled training/validation splits), and dropout (5% neuron dropout rate during prediction).

## Key numbers

- Training set RMS of approximately 50 keV and validation set RMS of approximately 100 keV for both the liquid-drop and Skyrme SLy4 neural networks
- For the toy model parabola fit, the mean square error on the training set was approximately 5 x 10^-5
- The dropout method required 30000 epochs to reach a stable plateau, compared to 15000 epochs for the other methods, and produced a root mean square of approximately 150 keV for both training and validation sets
- The bootstrap method used 100 bootstrap iterations with 100 neural networks trained for 15000 epochs each

## Context

This work extends the discussion of theoretical error estimation in nuclear physics from Dobaczewski (2014) and Roca-Maza (2015) to neural network models, which cannot use standard covariance matrix approaches due to their large number of parameters (typically thousands). The authors benchmark against synthetic data from the liquid-drop model (Weizsacker 1935) and Skyrme SLy4 (Chabanat 1998, Stoitsov 2006) to validate extrapolation performance, building on recent applications of neural networks to nuclear mass predictions by Utama (2016, 2017), Neufcourt (2018), and Pastore (2020). The work enables more reliable assessment of neural network extrapolations in regions of the Segrè chart far from training data, particularly relevant for r-process nucleosynthesis and neutron star crust composition studies.
