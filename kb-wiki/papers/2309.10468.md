# K Isomers in Transuranium Nuclei

> K isomers in transuranium nuclei have become a most interesting subject in nuclear structure investigation in many laboratories within the recent twenty years. In this paper an overview on the present day situation will be given. It will focus on the conditions for occuring of this kind of isomers, their decay properties and systematics in their properties as far as experimental data are available.

## Key claim

K isomers in transuranium nuclei are long-lived excited states that arise primarily from multi-quasiparticle configurations in deformed nuclei, where the projection K of the total angular momentum onto the symmetry axis leads to hindered electromagnetic transitions; the paper provides an overview of experimental data and systematics, noting that while general trends exist, there is no simple universal scaling of hindrance factors with K difference and multipolarity as originally suggested by Rusinov (1961).

## Method

The paper reviews experimental data on K isomers in transuranium nuclei (Z ≥ 100) obtained via complete fusion reactions, in-flight separation of evaporation residues using magnetic gas-filled separators or velocity filters, and implantation into silicon detector arrays for measuring alpha decay or spontaneous fission. Gamma rays were registered by germanium detector arrays placed around the stop detector, with conversion electrons registered in the stop detector serving as a calorimeter to discriminate isomer decay events from background radiation. Data analysis involved correlations between implantation signals, (conversion electron, gamma) coincidences, and subsequent alpha decay or spontaneous fission of the implanted isotope.

## Context

This review extends the earlier compilations of K-hindrance factors by L öbner (1968) and Kondev et al. (2015), and updates the discussion of coupling rules originally formulated by Nordheim (1950, 1951), Brennan and Bernstein (1960), and Gallagher (1962). The paper covers experimental techniques developed at SHIP since the late 1970s (Hofmann and M ünzenberg, 1979) and the conversion-electron calorimeter method proposed by Jones (2002). It enables future work on heavier transuranium nuclei where production rates are smaller and only conversion-electron measurements may be feasible for isomer identification, though excitation energies and decay patterns become harder to establish.
