# Low-energy operators in effective theories

> Modern effective-theory techniques are applied to the nuclear many-body problem. A novel approach is proposed for the renormalization of operators in a manner consistent with the construction of the effective potential. To test this approach, a one-dimensional, yet realistic, nucleon-nucleon potential is introduced. An effective potential is then constructed by tuning its parameters to reproduce the exact effective-range expansion and a variety of bare operators are renormalized in a fashion compatible with this construction. Predictions for the expectation values of these effective operators in the ground state reproduce the results of the exact theory with remarkable accuracy (at the 0.5% level). This represents a marked improvement over a widely practiced approach that uses effective interactions but retains bare operators. Further, it is shown that this improvement is more impressive as the operator becomes more sensitive to the short-range structure of the potential. We illustrate the main ideas of this work using the elastic form factor of the deuteron as an example.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a novel method for renormalizing operators in effective theories that is consistent with the construction of the effective potential, and demonstrates that this consistent renormalization yields ground-state expectation values accurate to within 0.5% of exact results, representing a marked improvement over the widely practiced approach of using effective interactions while retaining bare operators.

## Method

The authors introduce a one-dimensional nucleon-nucleon potential with short-range repulsive and medium-range attractive exponential terms, then construct an effective gaussian-cutoff potential whose parameters are tuned to reproduce the exact effective-range expansion (scattering length and effective range). They extend this same fitting procedure to operators, parametrizing effective operators with a short-range gaussian modification and tuning the parameters to match the low-momentum behavior of exact matrix elements between scattering states. Ground-state expectation values are then computed using three schemes: bare operators with bare wavefunctions (exact), bare operators with effective wavefunctions (inconsistent), and effective operators with effective wavefunctions (consistent).

## Key numbers

- Bare potential parameters: short-range mass m_s = 400 MeV, medium-range mass m_v = 783 MeV, strengths V_s = -506 MeV and V_v = +1142.49 MeV, chosen to give deuteron binding energy E_b = -2.2245 MeV and rms radius r_rms = 1.875 fm
- Bare scattering length a_0 = 5.247 fm and effective range r_0 = 1.521 fm
- Gaussian cutoff parameter fixed at a = 1.16 fm
- Effective potential parameters: c = -0.039 and d = -0.160
- Root-mean-square radius: exact (B+B) = 1.87977 fm, effective (E+E) = 1.87988 fm (0.01% discrepancy), inconsistent (B+E) = 1.87834 fm (0.09% discrepancy)
- Elastic form factor at q = 2 fm^{-1}: exact (B+B) = 0.02019, effective (E+E) = 0.02012 (0.35% discrepancy), inconsistent (B+E) = 0.01706 (18.4% discrepancy)
- Dirac delta expectation value: exact (B+B) = 0.01521 fm^{-1}, effective (E+E) = 0.01523 fm^{-1} (0.13% discrepancy), inconsistent (B+E) = 0.04981 fm^{-1} (327% discrepancy)
- Effective operator parameters for x^2 operator: c = 1.520, d = -0.305
- Effective operator parameters for delta operator: c = 0.250, d = -0.035

## Context

This work extends the effective-theory approach to the nuclear many-body problem by combining concepts from Lepage (1997) and the COntractor REnormalization (CORE) approach of Morningstar and Weinstein (1994, 1996), as previously adapted by two of the authors (Piekarewicz and Shepard) for low-dimensional quantum magnets (1996, 1998, 1999) and later applied to the nuclear problem by Mueller et al. (2002). The paper addresses a criticism levied by Haxton et al. (1999, 2001) that traditional shell-model approaches lack consistency between effective potential construction and operator renormalization. The results enable future combination of similarity-transformation methods with effective interactions for the three-body system and ultimately for the nuclear many-body problem, with the method needing extension to three spatial dimensions.
