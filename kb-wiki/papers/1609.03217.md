# Decoherence and determinism in a one-dimensional cloud-chamber model

> The hypothesis by Sparenberg et al. (2013) that the particular linear tracks appearing in the measurement of a spherically-emitting radioactive source in a cloud chamber are determined by the (random) positions of atoms or molecules inside the chamber is further explored in the framework of a recently established one-dimensional model by Carlone et al. (2015). In this model, meshes of localized spins 1/2 play the role of the cloud-chamber atoms and the spherical wave is replaced by a linear superposition of two wave packets moving from the origin to the left and to the right, evolving deterministically according to the Schrödinger equation. We first revisit these results using a time-dependent approach, where the wave packets impinge on a symmetric two-sided detector. We discuss the evolution of the wave function in the configuration space and stress the interest of a non-symmetric detector in a quantum-measurement perspective. Next we use a time-independent approach to study the scattering of a plane wave on a single-sided detector. Preliminary results are obtained, analytically for the single-spin case and numerically for up to 8 spins. They show that the spin-excitation probabilities are sometimes very sensitive to the parameters of the model, which corroborates the idea that the measurement result could be determined by the atom positions. The possible origin of decoherence and entropy increase in future models is finally discussed.

## Key claim

The paper explores the hypothesis that the particular linear tracks observed in a cloud chamber measurement of a spherically-emitting radioactive source are determined by the (random) positions of atoms or molecules inside the chamber, with the caveat that this is tested in a simplified one-dimensional model where the spherical wave is replaced by a linear superposition of two wave packets moving left and right, and the detector is represented by meshes of localized spins 1/2.

## Method

The authors first revisit a previously established one-dimensional model using a time-dependent approach with a symmetric two-sided detector of spins 1/2, then develop a time-independent approach for a single-sided detector to avoid artificial reflections. For the single-spin case, the stationary Schrodinger equation is solved analytically to obtain reflection and transmission coefficients; for up to 8 spins, a numerical algorithm constructs and solves a 2N x 2^N-dimensional linear-algebra system from continuity equations at the spin positions.

## Key numbers

* For a two-spin symmetric detector with a double gaussian wave packet, the probability of exciting the left atom is 24%, the right atom is 24%, and the probability of no excitation is 52% at time t2; the probability of exciting both atoms simultaneously is practically 0.
* For a single spin with epsilon=beta=0, the maximum excitation probability is 50%, occurring at the optimal coupling strength gamma_max = 2k0.
* For a regularly-spaced N-spin network with k0=pi, epsilon=0.01, beta=0.5, gamma=3, the ground-state probability decreases from 0.60514 (N=2) to 0.15813 (N=8), while the track-detection probability (more-than-one-spin excitation) increases from 0.10494 (N=2) to 0.47143 (N=8).
* For k0=400/3, epsilon=0.04, beta=10^-4, and gamma=800/3 (gamma_max), the track-detection probability for N=4 is 0.066758, which is anomalously smaller than for N=2 (0.24907) and N=3 (0.2812).

## Context

This work extends the one-dimensional cloud-chamber model of Carlone (2015) and the apparatus-hidden-variable hypothesis of Sparenberg (2013) for the Mott problem (Mott, 1929). The results suggest that track-formation probabilities depend on the detector microscopic state (number and positions of atoms), potentially due to an "inverse-resonance" phenomenon. The approach enables future studies of randomly-spaced spin meshes, Anderson localization effects, and ultimately a full three-dimensional quantum model with realistic particle-atom interactions, which could provide a microscopic foundation for macroscopic stopping-power models like the Bethe formula (Bethe, 1930).
