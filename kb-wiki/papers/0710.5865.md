# Fluctuation of the download network

> The scaling behavior of fluctuation for a download network which we have investigated a few years ago based upon Zhang's Encophysics web page has been presented. A power law scaling, namely $σ\sim < f> ^ α$ exists between the dispersion $σ$ and average flux $<f>$ of the download rates. The fluctuation exponent $α$ is neither 1/2 nor 1 which was claimed as two universal fluctuation classes in previous publication, instead it varies from 1/2 to 1 with the time window in which the download data were accumulated. The crossover behavior of fluctuation exponents can be qualitatively understood by the external driving fluctuation model for a small-size system or a network traffic model which suggests congestion as the origin.

# Fluctuation of the download network

## Key claim

The fluctuation exponent α for the download network is neither 1/2 nor 1 (the two universal classes claimed by Menezes and Barabási), but instead varies from 0.60 to 0.89 depending on the time window over which download data are accumulated, suggesting that the universal classes are not fixed for this system.

## Method

The authors analyzed download rates of papers from Zhang's Econophysics web page over three time windows (6.5 months, 16 months, and 31 months starting from 2004/08/31). For each rank-ordered paper, they computed the average daily download flux ⟨f⟩ and its dispersion σ (root mean square of the download rate distribution), then fit a power law σ ∝ ⟨f⟩^α to extract the fluctuation exponent α for each time window.

## Key numbers

- Fluctuation exponent α = 0.60 for the time window from 2004/08/31 to 2005/03/13 (approximately 6.5 months)
- Fluctuation exponent α = 0.89 for the time window from 2004/08/31 to 2007/03/26 (approximately 31 months)
- The average download flux ⟨f⟩ as a function of rank follows an exponential decay ⟨f⟩ ∝ exp(-rank/R), where the half-lifetime decay exponent R increases with the accumulation time period
- The rank-ordered download distribution follows Zipf's law N ∝ rank^(-γ), with the Zipf exponent γ showing non-constant behavior over time, including a bump during 2006

## Context

This work extends the fluctuation scaling analysis of Menezes and Barabási (2005), who proposed two universal fluctuation classes (α = 1/2 for internally driven dynamics like Internet traffic, α = 1 for externally driven dynamics like Web traffic), and the model of Duch and Arenas (2006) which showed exponents between 1/2 and 1 depending on network parameters. The results support the view that universal fluctuation classes are not fixed, and the crossover behavior can be qualitatively understood by either the external driving fluctuation model for small-size systems or a network traffic model where congestion is the origin of increasing α. The work enables future quantitative model simulation and investigation of possible Δ-scaling of network fluctuation.
