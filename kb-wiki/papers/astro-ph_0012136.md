# Evolution of a Neutron Star From its Birth to Old Age

> The main stages in the evolution of a neutron star, from its birth as a proto-neutron star, to its old age as a cold, catalyzed configuration, are described. A proto-neutron star is formed in the aftermath of a successful supernova explosion and its evolution is dominated by neutrino diffusion. Its neutrino signal is a valuable diagnostic of its internal structure and composition. During its transformation from a hot, lepton-rich to a cold, catalyzed remnant, the possibility exists that it can collapse into a black hole, which abruptly terminates neutrino emissions. The essential microphysics, reviewed herein, that controls its evolution are the equation of state of dense matter and its associated neutrino opacities. Several simulations of the proto-neutron star evolution, involving different assumptions about the composition of dense matter, are described. After its evolution into a nearly isothermal neutron star a hundred or so years after its birth, it may be observable through its thermal emission in X-rays during its life in the next million years. Its surface temperature will depend upon the rapidity of neutrino emission processes in its core, which depends on the composition of dense matter and whether or not its constituents exhibit superfluidity and superconductivity. Observations of thermal emission offer the best hope of a determination of the radius of a neutron star. The implications for the underlying dense matter equation of state of an accurate radius determination are explored.

## Key claim

The evolution of a neutron star from its birth as a proto-neutron star to its old age as a cold, catalyzed configuration is controlled by the equation of state of dense matter and its associated neutrino opacities, with the possibility of black hole formation during the transformation if the star's mass exceeds the maximum mass supported by the hot, lepton-rich matter or if strange matter appears after deleptonization.

## Method

The authors review simulations of proto-neutron star evolution using the equilibrium diffusion approximation to solve the Boltzmann equation for neutrino transport in a spherically symmetric, non-magnetic, stationary metric. They employ finite-temperature field-theoretical models with baryon interactions mediated by sigma, omega, and rho mesons to compute the equation of state for matter containing nucleons only, nucleons with hyperons, nucleons with a kaon condensate, and nucleons with quarks (using both MIT bag and Nambu Jona-Lasinio models). The evolution equations for energy and lepton number transport are solved in a Lagrangian framework with the enclosed baryon number as the independent variable.

## Key numbers

- The unshocked core mass immediately after core bounce is approximately 0.7 solar masses
- Neutrino diffusion deleptonizes the core on time scales of 10-15 seconds
- High-energy neutrinos in the core have energies of 200-300 MeV, while escaping neutrinos have energies of 10-20 MeV
- Core temperatures during deleptonization reach 30-60 MeV
- After approximately 50 seconds, the neutrino mean free path becomes comparable to the stellar radius and the star becomes transparent to neutrinos
- The surface temperature remains relatively warm at about 3 x 10^6 K for up to 100 years after birth
- The nearby neutron star RX J185635-3754 has a distance of approximately 60 pc
- The equilibrium density used for nucleon-meson coupling calibration is n0 = 0.153 fm^-3, with equilibrium energy per particle E/A = -16.3 MeV, effective mass M* = 0.78M, compression modulus K0 = 240 MeV, and symmetry energy a_sym = 32.5 MeV
- The kaon optical potential at saturation density is in the range -(50-200) MeV

## Context

This review extends the earlier work of Burrows and Lattimer (1986, 1990) on proto-neutron star evolution and the work of Prakash et al. (1997) on the equation of state of dense matter. The paper enables predictions for neutrino signals from galactic supernovae that could be detected by Super-Kamiokande and SNO, and provides a framework for interpreting X-ray observations from satellites such as Chandra and XMM to determine neutron star radii and constrain the dense matter equation of state.
