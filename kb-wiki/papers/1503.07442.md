# Vector interaction enhanced bag model for astrophysical applications

> For quark matter studies in astrophysics the thermodynamic bag model (tdBAG) has been widely used. Despite its success it fails to account for various phenomena expected from Quantum-Chromo-Dynamics (QCD). We suggest a straightforward extension of tdBAG in order to take the dynamical breaking of chiral symmetry and the influence of vector interactions explicitly into account. As for tdBAG the model mimics confinement in a phenomenological approach. It is based on an analysis of the Nambu--Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model at finite density. Furthermore, we demonstrate how NJL and bag models in this regime follow from the more general and QCD based framework of Dyson-Schwinger (DS) equations in medium by assuming a simple gluon contact interaction. Based on our simple and novel model, we construct quark hadron hybrid equations of state (EoS) and study systematically chiral and deconfinement phase transitions, the appearance of $s$-quarks and the role of vector interaction. We further study these aspects for matter in beta-equilibrium at zero temperature, with particular focus on the current ~2 solar masses maximum mass constraint for neutron stars. Our approach indicates that the currently only theoretical evidence for the hypothesis of stable strange matter is an artifact of tdBAG and results from neglecting the dynamical breaking of chiral symmetry.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the thermodynamic bag model (tdBAG) fails to account for dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (D$\chi$SB) and vector interactions, and that the only theoretical evidence for absolutely stable strange quark matter is an artifact of tdBAG resulting from neglecting D$\chi$SB. The authors propose a vector interaction enhanced bag model (vBAG) that reproduces Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model results while remaining as convenient to apply as the original tdBAG.

## Method

The authors derive the vBAG model by starting from the Dyson-Schwinger (DS) equations for in-medium quark propagators, then reducing them to the NJL model by assuming a contact interaction for the gluon propagator. They fit the pressure of the chirally restored branch of the NJL model to an ideal Fermi gas with bare quark masses shifted by a flavor-dependent chiral bag constant $B_\chi^f$, then add vector interactions via a coupling constant $K_v$ and introduce a deconfinement bag constant $B_{dc}$ to match the deconfinement and chiral restoration chemical potentials. The resulting hybrid equations of state are constructed using a Maxwell transition from the nuclear model TM1 to quark matter.

## Key numbers

- For parameter set IV, the two-flavor chiral bag constant is $B_\chi^{u,d} = (181.6\ \text{MeV})^4$, with a chiral critical chemical potential of $\mu_\chi = 1148\ \text{MeV}$ for isospin-symmetric two-flavor matter
- The Maxwell construction without modifications gives a deconfinement critical chemical potential of $\mu_{dc} = 1651\ \text{MeV}$, an interval of about 500 MeV between chiral restoration and deconfinement
- For neutron star applications, the chiral bag constants used are $B_{(u,d),\chi}^{1/4} = 137.6\ \text{MeV}$ and $B_{s,\chi}^{1/4} = 224.1\ \text{MeV}$ (set I)
- The canonical vector coupling studied is $G_V = 1/2\ G_S$, which nearly reproduces the nuclear model EoS TM1 in a "masquerade" effect
- The observed pulsars PSR J1614-2230 ($1.97 \pm 0.04\ M_\odot$) and PSR J0348-0432 ($2.01 \pm 0.04\ M_\odot$) provide the $\sim 2\ M_\odot$ maximum mass constraint for neutron stars

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamic bag model of Farhi (1984) by incorporating dynamical chiral symmetry breaking and vector interactions, which the original tdBAG explicitly omitted. The vBAG model bridges the gap between tdBAG and NJL-type models (Nambu, 1961; Klevansky, 1992; Buballa, 2003) by showing both can be derived from Dyson-Schwinger equations under a contact interaction approximation. The model enables systematic studies of hybrid neutron star equations of state with simultaneous chiral and deconfinement phase transitions, and contradicts the hypothesis of absolutely stable strange matter introduced by Witten (1984) and supported by Farhi (1984).
