# The anomalous magnetic moment of the muon in the Standard Model

> We review the present status of the Standard Model calculation of the anomalous magnetic moment of the muon. This is performed in a perturbative expansion in the fine-structure constant $α$ and is broken down into pure QED, electroweak, and hadronic contributions. The pure QED contribution is by far the largest and has been evaluated up to and including $\mathcal{O}(α^5)$ with negligible numerical uncertainty. The electroweak contribution is suppressed by $(m_μ/M_W)^2$ and only shows up at the level of the seventh significant digit. It has been evaluated up to two loops and is known to better than one percent. Hadronic contributions are the most difficult to calculate and are responsible for almost all of the theoretical uncertainty. The leading hadronic contribution appears at $\mathcal{O}(α^2)$ and is due to hadronic vacuum polarization, whereas at $\mathcal{O}(α^3)$ the hadronic light-by-light scattering contribution appears. Given the low characteristic scale of this observable, these contributions have to be calculated with nonperturbative methods, in particular, dispersion relations and the lattice approach to QCD. The largest part of this review is dedicated to a detailed account of recent efforts to improve the calculation of these two contributions with either a data-driven, dispersive approach, or a first-principle, lattice-QCD approach. The final result reads $a_μ^\text{SM}=116\,591\,810(43)\times 10^{-11}$ and is smaller than the Brookhaven measurement by 3.7$σ$. The experimental uncertainty will soon be reduced by up to a factor four by the new experiment currently running at Fermilab, and also by the future J-PARC experiment. This and the prospects to further reduce the theoretical uncertainty in the near future-which are also discussed here-make this quantity one of the most promising places to look for evidence of new physics.

## Key claim

The Standard Model prediction for the muon anomalous magnetic moment is \(a_\mu^\text{SM}=116\,591\,810(43)\times 10^{-11}\), which is \(3.7\sigma\) smaller than the Brookhaven E821 experimental value; this discrepancy is presented as a tantalizing but not yet discovery-level hint of new physics, with the theoretical uncertainty dominated by hadronic contributions that are evaluated using data-driven dispersive methods for hadronic vacuum polarization (HVP) and a combination of dispersive phenomenology and lattice QCD for hadronic light-by-light (HLbL) scattering.

## Method

The calculation proceeds by summing the pure QED contribution (evaluated perturbatively up to \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha^5)\)), the electroweak contribution (evaluated up to two loops with leading logarithmic beyond-two-loop estimates), and the hadronic contributions. The leading hadronic vacuum polarization at \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha^2)\) is obtained via a data-driven dispersive integral over \(e^+e^-\to\text{hadrons}\) cross sections, using a merging procedure that accounts for tensions among data sets, especially in the \(\pi\pi\) channel; subleading HVP contributions at NLO and NNLO are included. The hadronic light-by-light contribution at \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha^3)\) is evaluated using a dispersive approach for the dominant pseudoscalar-pole and two-pion intermediate states, supplemented by model-dependent estimates for scalar, tensor, and axial-vector contributions, and combined with a lattice QCD result for the light-quark part via a weighted average.

## Key numbers

- QED contribution: \(a_\mu^\text{QED} = 116\,584\,718.931(104) \times 10^{-11}\), based on the fine-structure constant from Cs atom interferometry (Parker 2018).
- Electroweak contribution: \(a_\mu^\text{EW} = 153.6(1.0) \times 10^{-11}\).
- Leading-order HVP from \(e^+e^-\) data: \(a_\mu^\text{HVP LO} = 6931(40) \times 10^{-11}\).
- NLO HVP: \(a_\mu^\text{HVP NLO} = -98.3(7) \times 10^{-11}\).
- NNLO HVP: \(a_\mu^\text{HVP NNLO} = 12.4(1) \times 10^{-11}\).
- Total HVP (LO+NLO+NNLO): \(6845(40) \times 10^{-11}\).
- HLbL from combined phenomenology and lattice QCD (plus NLO): \(92(18) \times 10^{-11}\).
- Lattice QCD HVP LO (world average): \(7116(184) \times 10^{-11}\).
- Final SM prediction: \(a_\mu^\text{SM} = 116\,591\,810(43) \times 10^{-11}\).
- Difference from Brookhaven experiment: \(\Delta a_\mu = 279(76) \times 10^{-11}\), corresponding to \(3.7\sigma\).

## Context

This white paper is the first product of the Muon \(g-2\) Theory Initiative, updating and replacing earlier SM evaluations that relied on older \(e^+e^-\) data and model-dependent HLbL estimates (the “Glasgow consensus”). It extends the dispersive approach to HLbL (Colangelo 2014, Hoferichter 2019) and incorporates a mature lattice QCD calculation of HLbL (Blum 2020), while for HVP it adopts a conservative data-driven result that accounts for tensions among \(e^+e^-\) data sets (Davier 2017, Keshavarzi 2018, Colangelo 2018). The result enables direct comparison with the upcoming Fermilab Muon \(g-2\) experiment (expected to reduce experimental uncertainty by a factor of four) and the future J-PARC experiment, and it motivates further improvements in dispersive HVP (via new \(e^+e^-\) data and the MUonE project) and lattice QCD calculations of both HVP and HLbL.
