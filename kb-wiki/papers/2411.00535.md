# Heavy element abundances from a universal primordial distribution

> We present a freeze-out approach to the formation of heavy elements in expanding nuclear matter. Applying concepts used in the description of heavy-ion collisions or ternary fission, we determine the abundances of heavy elements taking into account in-medium effects such as Pauli blocking and the Mott effect, which describes the dissolution of nuclei at high densities of nuclear matter. With this approach, we search for a universal primordial distribution in an equilibrium state from which the gross structure of the solar abundances of heavy elements freezes out via radioactive decay of the excited states. The universal primordial state is characterized by the Lagrangian parameters of temperature and chemical potentials of neutrons and protons. We show that such a state exists and determine a temperature of 5.266 MeV, a neutron chemical potential of 940.317 MeV and a proton chemical potential of 845.069 MeV, at a baryon number density of 0.013 fm$^{-3}$ and a proton fraction of 0.13. Heavy neutron-rich nuclei such as the hypothesized double-magic nucleus $^{358}$Sn appear in the primordial distribution and contribute to the observed abundances after fission. We discuss astrophysical scenarios for the realization of this universal primordial distribution for heavy element nucleosynthesis, including supernova explosions, neutron star mergers and the inhomogeneous Big Bang. The latter scenario may be of interest in the light of early massive objects observed with the James Webb Space Telescope and opens new perspectives to explain universality of the observed r-process patterns and the lack of observations of population III stars.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a universal primordial distribution of heavy nuclei, characterized by a freeze-out temperature of 5.266 MeV, a neutron chemical potential of 940.317 MeV, and a proton chemical potential of 845.069 MeV at a baryon number density of 0.013 fm\(^{-3}\) and a proton fraction of 0.13, can reproduce the gross structure of solar heavy-element abundances after radioactive decay and fission, but they hedge that this state is inferred from a quasi-equilibrium freeze-out concept rather than from a direct dynamical simulation of any specific astrophysical site.

## Method

The authors apply a freeze-out approach based on the Zubarev nonequilibrium statistical operator method, which combines a generalized Gibbs distribution with in-medium corrections including Pauli blocking and the Mott effect. They determine the primordial distribution of nuclei by solving for the Lagrange parameters (temperature and chemical potentials) that reproduce the observed solar accumulated mass fractions for heavy nuclei with mass number \(A \ge 76\), using a generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck equation to account for cluster formation in dense matter. The primordial distribution is then evolved through an afterburner stage that includes radioactive decay and fission to produce the final solar abundance pattern.

## Key numbers

* Temperature at freeze-out: 5.266 MeV, at a baryon number density of 0.013 fm\(^{-3}\) and a proton fraction of 0.13.
* Neutron chemical potential at freeze-out: 940.317 MeV.
* Proton chemical potential at freeze-out: 845.069 MeV.
* Baryon saturation density given for reference: 0.15 fm\(^{-3}\).
* For comparison, freeze-out parameters from ternary fission of \(^{242}\)Pu: temperature 1.29 MeV, baryon number density \(6.7 \times 10^{-5}\) fm\(^{-3}\), proton fraction 0.035 (Natowitz 2022).
* For comparison, freeze-out parameters from heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies: temperatures 5–10 MeV, baryon number densities 0.02–0.04 fm\(^{-3}\) (Qin 2011).
* For comparison, freeze-out parameters from ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions: temperature 157 MeV, baryon chemical potential 0 (Andronic 2017).

## Context

This work extends the freeze-out concept previously applied to heavy-ion collisions and ternary fission (Qin 2011, Natowitz 2022) to the problem of heavy-element nucleosynthesis, offering an alternative to the standard Burbidge (1957) scenario of element buildup from light nuclei in specific astrophysical sites. The authors propose that an inhomogeneous Big Bang nucleosynthesis scenario, as discussed by Applegate (1987) and Malaney (1990), could provide the dense, neutron-rich environment needed for this universal primordial distribution, potentially explaining the observed universality of r-process patterns and the non-detection of population III stars. The approach enables future nuclear reaction network calculations to use this primordial distribution as an initial condition, rather than starting from the standard homogeneous Big Bang nucleosynthesis abundances.
