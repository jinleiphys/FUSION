# Improved real-time dynamics from imaginary frequency lattice simulations

> The computation of real-time properties, such as transport coefficients or bound state spectra of strongly interacting quantum fields in thermal equilibrium is a pressing matter. Since the sign problem prevents a direct evaluation of these quantities, lattice data needs to be analytically continued from the Euclidean domain of the simulation to Minkowski time, in general an ill-posed inverse problem. Here we report on a novel approach to improve the determination of real-time information in the form of spectral functions by setting up a simulation prescription in imaginary frequencies. By carefully distinguishing between initial conditions and quantum dynamics one obtains access to correlation functions also outside the conventional Matsubara frequencies. In particular the range between $ω_0$ and $ω_1=2πT$, which is most relevant for the inverse problem may be more highly resolved. In combination with the fact that in imaginary frequencies the kernel of the inverse problem is not an exponential but only a rational function we observe significant improvements in the reconstruction of spectral functions, demonstrated in a simple 0+1 dimensional scalar field theory toy model.

## Key claim
The authors claim that by formulating thermal field theory as an initial value problem on the Schwinger-Keldysh contour and analytically continuing to a non-compact imaginary time axis, one can resolve correlation functions at imaginary frequencies between the conventional Matsubara frequencies, which significantly improves the reconstruction of spectral functions compared to standard Euclidean lattice simulations. The improvement is demonstrated in a 0+1 dimensional scalar field theory toy model, but the authors hedge that the approach requires further clarification of deviations observed near zero Matsubara frequency and extension to genuine field theories.

## Method
The authors implement stochastic quantization via real-valued Langevin updates on two concurrent grids: a compact Euclidean domain for the thermal initial conditions (fields φ_E) and a non-compact imaginary frequency domain for the fields φ^+ from which the spectral function is extracted. The kinetic term of the action is evaluated in imaginary frequencies where it is diagonal, while the interaction term (φ^4) is evaluated in imaginary time via discrete Fourier transforms, with temperature information imprinted by replacing φ^+(t=0) with φ_E(τ=0) in the interacting drift term. Spectral functions are reconstructed from the simulated correlators using the BR Bayesian inference method.

## Key numbers
- For the 0+1 dimensional scalar toy model with λ=24, m=1, β=1 on N_τ=16 grid points, 10^6 Monte Carlo samples yielded statistical errors of ΔG/G ~ 10^{-3} for the Euclidean correlator.
- The spectral reconstruction from standard Euclidean simulations with N_τ=16, 32, 64, and 128 points all failed to reproduce the correct peak position, while the novel approach with N_ω=512 reproduced the peak position with a deviation of only around one per mille.
- The imaginary frequency kernel used for reconstruction is K(μ,ω)=2μ/(μ^2+P(ω)), where P(ω) is the lattice dispersion relation, in contrast to the exponential cosh/sinh kernel in the Euclidean formulation.
- Preliminary results for a (3+1)d complex scalar field used λ=1, m^2=0.2 on 8^3×N_τ×N_ω grids with N_τ=4 and N_ω=32.

## Context
This work extends the standard approach to thermal lattice field theory where Euclidean time is compactified and correlators are only accessible at Matsubara frequencies ω_n=2πT n, as used in lattice QCD computations of transport coefficients by Pasztor (2016), Meyer (2007-2009), and others. The authors build on their own prior work (Pawlowski 2016) to formulate a non-compact Euclidean time setup. The approach aims to overcome the exponential information loss inherent in the standard Euclidean inverse problem, which was illustrated by Pasztor (2016) showing that Euclidean correlators are only marginally sensitive to sizable changes in spectral functions at small frequencies. The method enables improved determination of real-time properties such as transport coefficients and bound state spectra, with the BR method (Burnier 2013) used for spectral reconstruction.
