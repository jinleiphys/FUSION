# Shell structure and orbit bifurcations in finite fermion systems

> We first give an overview of the shell-correction method which was developed by V. M. Strutinsky as a practicable and efficient approximation to the general selfconsistent theory of finite fermion systems suggested by A. B. Migdal and collaborators. Then we present in more detail a semiclassical theory of shell effects, also developed by Strutinsky following original ideas of M. Gutzwiller. We emphasize, in particular, the influence of orbit bifurcations on shell structure. We first give a short overview of semiclassical trace formulae, which connect the shell oscillations of a quantum system with a sum over periodic orbits of the corresponding classical system, in what is usually called the "periodic orbit theory". We then present a case study in which the gross features of a typical double-humped nuclear fission barrier, including the effects of mass asymmetry, can be obtained in terms of the shortest periodic orbits of a cavity model with realistic deformations relevant for nuclear fission. Next we investigate shell structures in a spheroidal cavity model which is integrable and allows for far-going analytical computation. We show, in particular, how period-doubling bifurcations are closely connected to the existence of the so-called "superdeformed" energy minimum which corresponds to the fission isomer of actinide nuclei. Finally, we present a general class of radial power-law potentials which approximate well the shape of a Woods-Saxon potential in the bound region, give analytical trace formulae for it and discuss various limits (including the harmonic oscillator and the spherical box potentials).

## Key claim

The paper argues that the gross features of nuclear shell structure, including the double-humped fission barrier and the superdeformed fission isomer minimum, can be understood semiclassically in terms of the shortest periodic orbits of the corresponding classical system, with orbit bifurcations playing a crucial role in enhancing shell effects at large deformations.

## Method

The authors review the Strutinsky shell-correction method and develop semiclassical trace formulae based on Gutzwiller's periodic orbit theory, extended to systems with continuous symmetries. They apply an improved stationary-phase method (ISPM) that uses finite integration limits defined by the classically allowed phase space region to handle divergences at bifurcation points, deriving contributions from each periodic orbit free of singularities. The method is applied to three model systems: a cavity model with realistic fission deformations, a spheroidal cavity model, and radial power-law potentials approximating the Woods-Saxon potential.

## Key numbers

- The shell-correction energy for a typical actinide nucleus yields a second minimum in the deformation energy lying above the ground state by about 3 MeV, at a deformation much larger than that of the ground state.
- For neutron number N=150 in a Woods-Saxon potential with spheroidal shape, the ground state minimum occurs at deformation parameter eta ~ 1.25, while the isomeric state appears at much larger deformations eta ~ 1.9-2.1.
- The remaining higher-order terms in the Strutinsky energy theorem beyond the first-order shell-correction were found to be less than ~ 1 MeV in all (not too small) nuclei.
- The most probable ratio of fragment masses in actinide fission is ~ 1.3-1.5.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical approach pioneered by Strutinsky (1966) and Gutzwiller (1971) to understand nuclear shell effects in terms of classical periodic orbits. It builds on the shell-correction method that Strutinsky developed following Migdal's theory of finite fermion systems, and on earlier applications to spheroidal cavities by Strutinsky and collaborators. The paper enables a deeper semiclassical understanding of the fission isomer phenomenon discovered experimentally by Polikanov (1962) and the mass asymmetry of fission fragments, and provides analytical trace formulae for power-law potentials that can be applied to future studies of shell structure in deformed nuclei and other finite fermion systems such as metal clusters and quantum dots.
