# More on the relation between the two physically inequivalent decompositions of the nucleon spin and momentum

> In a series of papers, we have established the existence of two gauge-invariant decompositions of the nucleon spin, which are physically nonequivalent. The orbital angular momenta of quarks and gluons appearing in these two decompositions are gauge-invariant dynamical orbital angular momenta and "generalized" canonical orbital angular momenta with gauge-invariance, respectively. The key quantity, which characterizes the difference between these two types of orbital angular momenta is what-we-call the {\it potential angular momentum}. We argue that the physical meaning of the potential angular momentum in the nucleon can be made more transparent, by investigating a related but much simpler example from electrodynamics. We also make clear several remaining issues in the spin and momentum decomposition problem of the nucleon. We clarify the relationship between the evolution equations of orbital angular momenta corresponding to the two different decompositions above. We also try to answer the question whether the two different decompositions of the nucleon momentum really lead to different evolution equations, thereby predicting conflicting asymptotic values for the quark and gluon momentum fractions in the nucleon.

## Key claim

The paper argues that there are exactly two physically inequivalent gauge-invariant decompositions of the nucleon spin, distinguished by whether the orbital angular momenta are "dynamical" (mechanical) or "generalized canonical" types; the difference between them is a gauge-invariant "potential angular momentum" term, and contrary to some claims, both decompositions of the nucleon momentum lead to the same evolution equations and asymptotic limits for quark and gluon momentum fractions.

## Method

The author establishes two gauge-invariant decompositions of the nucleon spin by decomposing the gluon field into physical and pure-gauge parts, imposing only the pure-gauge condition and gauge transformation properties without fixing the gauge explicitly. The physical meaning of the potential angular momentum is clarified by analogy with the QED system of charged particles and photons, where the longitudinal-transverse decomposition of the vector potential is unique once the Lorentz frame is fixed. The author then derives sum rules relating the orbital angular momenta in each decomposition to generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and polarized parton distribution functions (PDFs), and analyzes the evolution equations for both decompositions at the 1-loop level.

## Key numbers

* The intrinsic quark spin contribution to the nucleon spin was determined to be around 1/3 (from COMPASS and HERMES measurements cited in the paper).
* The quark orbital angular momentum in decomposition (I) is given by \( L_q = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-1}^1 x [H^q(x,0,0) + E^q(x,0,0)] dx - \frac{1}{2} \int_{-1}^1 \Delta q(x) dx \), where the first term is the second moment of the GPD combination and the second is the first moment of the polarized quark distribution.
* The gluon orbital angular momentum in decomposition (I) is given by \( L_g = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-1}^1 x [H^g(x,0,0) + E^g(x,0,0)] dx - \int_0^1 \Delta g(x) dx \), where the first term is the second moment of the gluon GPD combination and the second is the first moment of the polarized gluon distribution.
* The canonical orbital angular momentum in decomposition (II) can be related to a weighted integral of the GTMD \( F^q_{1,4} \) as \( L_{can} = - \int dx \, d^2 q_T \, \frac{\bm{q}^2_T}{M^2} F^q_{1,4}(x, 0, q^2_T, 0, 0) \).

## Context

This work extends the author's previous series of papers (Wakamatsu, 2010, 2011a, 2011b) establishing two gauge-invariant decompositions of the nucleon spin, and addresses the controversy raised by Chen et al. (2008, 2009) regarding the feasibility of a complete decomposition. The paper clarifies that the dynamical orbital angular momentum from decomposition (I) is the quantity extracted from GPDs and polarized PDFs, consistent with Ji's finding (1997), while the canonical orbital angular momentum from decomposition (II) may be accessible through GTMDs following Hatta's work (2012). The analysis resolves the question of whether the two decompositions of nucleon momentum predict different asymptotic values for momentum fractions, concluding they do not, and explains why the dynamical and canonical orbital angular momenta obey the same evolution equation at the 1-loop level despite being physically inequivalent.
