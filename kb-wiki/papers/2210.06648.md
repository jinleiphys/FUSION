# Evidence for 3XMM J185246.6+003317 as a massive magnetar with a low magnetic field

> 3XMM J185246.6+003317 is a transient magnetar located in the vicinity of the supernova remnant Kes\,79. So far, observations have only set upper limits to its surface magnetic field and spindown, and there is no estimate for its mass and radius. Using ray-tracing modelling and Bayesian inference for the analysis of several light curves spanning a period of around three weeks, we have found that it may be one of the most massive neutron stars to date. In addition, our analysis suggests a multipolar magnetic field structure with a subcritical field strength and a carbon atmosphere composition. Due to the time-resolution limitation of the available light curves, we estimate the surface magnetic field and the mass to be $\log_{10} (B/{\rm G}) = 11.89^{+0.19}_{-0.93}$ and $M=2.09^{+0.16}_{-0.09}$~$M_{\odot}$ at $1σ$ confidence level, while the radius is estimated to be $R=12.02^{+1.44}_{-1.42}$ km at $2σ$ confidence level. They were verified by simulations, i.e., data injections with known model parameters, and their subsequent recovery. The best-fitting model has three small hot spots, two of them in the southern hemisphere. These are, however, just first estimates and conclusions, based on a simple ray-tracing model with anisotropic emission; we also estimate the impact of modelling on the parameter uncertainties and the relevant phenomena on which to focus in more precise analyses. We interpret the above best-fitting results as due to accretion of supernova layers/interstellar medium onto 3XMM J185246.6+003317 leading to burying and a subsequent re-emergence of the magnetic field, and a carbon atmosphere being formed possibly due to hydrogen/helium diffusive nuclear burning. Finally, we briefly discuss some consequences of our findings for superdense matter constraints.

## Key claim

Using ray-tracing modeling and Bayesian inference on XMM-Newton light curves spanning about three weeks, the authors find that the transient magnetar 3XMM J185246.6+003317 may be one of the most massive neutron stars known, but caution that these are first estimates based on a simple ray-tracing model with anisotropic emission and limited time resolution, and that the radius is only reliable at 2-sigma confidence.

## Method

The authors analyzed five XMM-Newton EPIC-MOS2 observations from 2008 September to October, extracting background-subtracted light curves in the 0.3-10 keV and 3-8 keV bands and folding them at the approximately 11.56-second spin period. They modeled the pulse profiles using a Schwarzschild-metric ray-tracing code with up to three hot spots on the neutron star surface, employing magnetic carbon atmosphere spectral tables from Ho and collaborators, and performed Bayesian parameter estimation via Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) with the emcee algorithm. The model was convolved with the 2.6-second timing resolution of the instrument, and synthetic data injections were used to verify parameter recovery.

## Key numbers

- Mass: M = 2.09^{+0.16}_{-0.09} solar masses at 1-sigma confidence level
- Surface magnetic field: log10(B/G) = 11.89^{+0.19}_{-0.93} at 1-sigma confidence level
- Radius: R = 12.02^{+1.44}_{-1.42} km at 2-sigma confidence level
- Spin period: P approximately 11.6 seconds, with period derivative less than 1.4 x 10^{-13} s/s
- Best-fitting model AIC = 362.36 with chi-squared per degree of freedom = 280.36/230 = 1.22 for three hot spots and a carbon atmosphere
- Compactness: C = 0.256 for the median mass and radius values, with range 0.246 to 0.268
- Tidal deformability: Lambda = 23 for the median compactness, at the threshold of detectability by third-generation gravitational-wave detectors

## Context

This work extends the application of ray-tracing pulse profile modeling, previously used for pulsars like PSR J0740+6620 (Miller 2021, Riley 2021), to a magnetar for the first time, providing mass and radius estimates where only upper limits on magnetic field and spindown existed before (Zhou 2014, Rea 2014). The inferred high mass and low magnetic field support the interpretation of 3XMM J185246.6+003317 as a low-B magnetar with a buried and re-emerging magnetic field due to accretion from the supernova remnant Kes 79, and the results enable future comparisons with equations of state from chiral effective field theory (Hebeler 2013) and the SLy4 EOS (Douchin 2001), as well as constraints on superdense matter from multi-messenger observations.
