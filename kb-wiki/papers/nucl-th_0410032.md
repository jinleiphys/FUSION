# Reaction cross section described by a black sphere approximation of nuclei

> We identify a length scale that simultaneously accounts for the observed proton-nucleus reaction cross section and diffraction peak in the proton elastic differential cross section. This scale is the nuclear radius, $a$, deduced from proton elastic scattering data of incident energies higher than $\sim800$ MeV, by assuming that the target nucleus is a ``black'' sphere. The values of $a$ are determined so as to reproduce the angle of the first diffraction maximum in the scattering data for stable nuclei. We find that the absorption cross section, $πa^2$, agrees with the empirical total reaction cross section for C, Sn, and Pb to within error bars. This agreement persists in the case of the interaction cross section measured for a carbon target. We also find that $\sqrt{3/5}a$ systematically deviates from the empirically deduced values of the root-mean-square matter radius for nuclei having mass less than about 50, while it almost completely agrees with the deduced values for $A\gtrsim50$. This tendency suggests a significant change of the nuclear matter distribution from a rectangular one for $A\lesssim50$, which is consistent with the behavior of the empirical charge distribution.

## Key claim

The black sphere radius \(a\), determined solely from the angle of the first diffraction maximum in proton elastic scattering at incident energies above \(\sim800\) MeV, simultaneously reproduces the empirical total reaction cross section (via \(\pi a^2\)) for stable nuclei, but the derived root-mean-square radius \(\sqrt{3/5}a\) systematically deviates from the empirical matter radius for nuclei with mass number \(A<50\), suggesting a non-rectangular nucleon distribution for light nuclei.

## Method

The authors determined the black sphere radius \(a\) for stable nuclei from He to Pb by matching the first diffraction peak angle in measured proton-nucleus elastic differential cross sections at incident energies \(T_p\gtrsim800\) MeV to the analytic diffraction formula for a circular black disk, \(2pa\sin(\theta_M/2)=5.1356\). They then compared the geometrical cross section \(\pi a^2\) to empirical total reaction cross sections for C, Sn, and Pb, and compared the derived root-mean-square radius \(r_{\rm BS}=\sqrt{3/5}a\) to matter radii from conventional optical-model analyses. The approach was extended to nucleus-nucleus interaction cross sections using \(\pi(a_1+a_2)^2\) for projectiles on a carbon target.

## Key numbers

- For C, \(\sigma_{\rm BS}=217.003\pm3.45\) mb (at 800 MeV) agrees with \(\sigma_R=209\pm22\) mb (at 860 MeV); for Sn, \(\sigma_{\rm BS}=1117.486\pm49.6\) mb (800 MeV) agrees with \(\sigma_R=1100\pm30\) mb (860 MeV); for Pb, \(\sigma_{\rm BS}=1723.590\pm88.5\) mb (800 MeV) agrees with \(\sigma_R=1680\pm40\) mb (860 MeV).
- The black sphere radius \(a\) yields \(r_{\rm BS}\sim0.94A^{1/3}\) fm when staggering is smoothed out.
- For \(A>50\), \(r_{\rm BS}\) agrees almost completely with the empirical root-mean-square matter radius \(r_m\); for \(A<50\), \(r_{\rm BS}\) is systematically smaller than \(r_m\) by about 0.2 fm.
- For neutron-rich \({}^{6}\)He, \(r_{\rm BS}=1.76\pm0.03\) fm versus \(r_m=2.45\pm0.10\) fm; for \({}^{8}\)He, \(r_{\rm BS}=1.94\pm0.02\) fm versus \(r_m=2.53\pm0.08\) fm; for \({}^{11}\)Li, \(r_{\rm BS}=2.30\pm0.04\) fm versus \(r_m\approx3.1\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous black-sphere analysis for heavy nuclei (Kohama, Iida, Oyamatsu 2004) to light stable nuclei and to reaction cross sections. It contradicts the expectation that conventional optical potentials can simultaneously describe elastic scattering and reaction cross sections at 800-1000 MeV, as those potentials tend to overestimate the reaction cross section (Ray 1979). The finding that \(\pi a^2\) matches measured reaction cross sections without adjustable parameters provides a simpler alternative to earlier fitting formulas based on the \(A^{1/3}\) law (Karol 1975, Kox 1987). The systematic deviation of \(r_{\rm BS}\) from \(r_m\) for \(A<50\) enables a new diagnostic for the shape of the nucleon distribution, and the application to neutron-rich He and Li isotopes suggests a method for identifying neutron halos via the difference between \(r_m\) and \(r_{\rm BS}\).
