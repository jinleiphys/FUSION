# The physics of neutrino cross sections: theoretical studies

> The present status of neutrino cross section physics is reviewed focusing on the recent theoretical developments in quasielastic scattering, multi-nucleon contributions to the inclusive scattering and pion production on nucleons and nuclei. A good understanding of these processes is crucial to meet the precision needs of neutrino oscillation experiments. Some of the challenges that arise in the consistent description of MiniBooNE and MINERvA recent data are discussed.

# The physics of neutrino cross sections: theoretical studies

## Key claim
The paper reviews the current theoretical understanding of neutrino-nucleus cross sections, arguing that while advanced nuclear models incorporating multinucleon effects (2p2h) and random phase approximation (RPA) correlations can explain MiniBooNE quasielastic data with an axial nucleon form factor consistent with deuterium measurements (M_A ~ 1 GeV), significant tensions remain between MiniBooNE and MINERvA datasets that cannot be fully resolved by current models.

## Method
The author surveys theoretical developments in charged-current quasielastic (CCQE) scattering, multinucleon contributions to inclusive scattering, and pion production on nucleons and nuclei. For CCQE, the paper compares relativistic Fermi gas models with more advanced approaches including spectral functions, Green function formalism, RPA correlations, and superscaling, and examines how 2p2h excitations fill the gap between predictions and MiniBooNE data. For pion production, the analysis focuses on the axial transition form factor C^A_5 for the Delta(1232) resonance, comparing fits to ANL and BNL bubble-chamber data with and without imposing Watson's theorem, and discusses final state interactions in nuclear targets.

## Key numbers
- Axial mass from early CCQE experiments on deuterium: M_A = 1.016 ± 0.026 GeV (Bodek, 2007)
- Axial radius squared from pion electroproduction: ⟨r_A²⟩ = 0.455 ± 0.012 fm² (Bernard, 1992)
- Axial radius squared from z-expansion extraction: ⟨r_A²⟩ = 0.46(22) fm² (Meyer, 2016)
- Goldberger-Treiman relation prediction for axial transition form factor: C^A_5(0)|_GTR = 1.15 - 1.2
- C^A_5(0) from fit without Watson's theorem: 1.00 ± 0.11 (Hernandez, 2007)
- C^A_5(0) from fit restoring Watson's theorem: 1.12 ± 0.11 (Alvarez-Ruso, 2015)
- C^A_5(0) from reanalyzed ANL/BNL data with Watson's theorem: 1.14 ± 0.07 (Alvarez-Ruso, 2015)
- C^A_5(0) from reanalyzed data without Watson's theorem: 1.05 ± 0.07
- Two-nucleon contributions account for approximately 30% of sum rule strength in ¹²C (Lovato, 2014)
- T2K global fit required 27% reduction in 2p2h contribution and M_A increase to 1.15 GeV (Wilkinson, 2016)

## Context
This review extends the theoretical framework for neutrino cross sections developed over the past decade, building on the classic CCQE formalism of Llewellyn Smith (1971) and the superscaling approach of Caballero (2005). It addresses the long-standing discrepancy between MiniBooNE quasielastic data (Aguilar-Arevalo, 2010) and nuclear model predictions, which was originally attributed to multinucleon effects by Martini (2009) and Nieves (2011). The paper highlights tensions between MiniBooNE and MINERvA datasets that were quantified by the T2K Neutrino Interaction Working Group (Wilkinson, 2016), and calls for more flexible and kinematically complete implementations of theoretical models in event generators to enable meaningful global fits. For pion production, it resolves inconsistencies between ANL and BNL bubble-chamber data through flux renormalization (Wilkinson, 2014) and demonstrates that Watson's theorem restoration reconciles extracted form factors with the Goldberger-Treiman relation.
