# Neural-network solution of subtracted three-body Faddeev integral equations near the Efimov limit

> We apply a deep-neural-network (DNN) ansatz to the symmetrized spectator vector of the subtracted three-body Faddeev integral equation for identical bosons near the Efimov limit. The network is trained by minimizing the residual of the discretized integral equation, while the positive binding scale associated with the three-body energy is treated as a trainable parameter. Deterministic diagonalization of the same discretized kernel is used only as an a posteriori numerical benchmark. As preliminary validation, the neural-solver strategy is tested on the analytically solvable hydrogen radial problem. At unitarity, the DNN reproduces the Efimov ground-state binding scale with a DNN--deterministic deviation of $0.022\%$, while the first excited state is recovered to $0.002\%$. The deterministic solver recovers the universal Efimov scaling ratio $e^{2π/s_0}\simeq 515.03$, and the neural method traces the bound-state branches as a function of the inverse scattering length $1/a$ by continuation from the unitary solution. These results indicate that DNN-based residual minimization can provide a compact and differentiable representation of a renormalized few-body integral-equation solution in a regime governed by discrete scale invariance.

## Key claim
A deep neural network (DNN) can solve the subtracted three-body Faddeev integral equation for identical bosons near the Efimov limit by treating the binding energy as a trainable parameter and minimizing the residual of the discretized kernel, but the accuracy degrades for very shallow bound states (deviations up to ~3.4%) and near thresholds, as the method is validated against a deterministic diagonalization of the same discretized kernel rather than an exact analytical solution.

## Method
The symmetrized spectator vector of the subtracted Faddeev integral equation is represented by a feed-forward neural network with 3 hidden layers of 64 neurons each and tanh activation, taking the logarithmic momentum as input. The network is trained by minimizing the normalized squared residual of the discretized integral equation, with the three-body binding energy $\epsilon_3$ treated as a trainable parameter $\epsilon_3 = \exp(s_3)$ and optimized jointly with the network weights using the Adam optimizer. A deterministic diagonalization of the same discretized kernel is used only as an a posteriori benchmark, and the DNN solution is continued across the Efimov spectrum by initializing from the converged solution at a neighboring value of the inverse scattering length $1/a$.

## Key numbers
- At unitarity ($1/a = 0$), the DNN reproduces the Efimov ground-state binding scale with a deviation of $0.022\%$ from the deterministic benchmark, and the first excited state to $0.002\%$.
- The deterministic solver recovers the universal Efimov scaling ratio $e^{2\pi/s_0} \simeq 515.03$ for the two shallowest pairs of bound states.
- The DNN-predicted energies for the third and fourth bound states at unitarity deviate from the deterministic benchmark by $2.890\%$ and $3.359\%$, respectively.
- The median DNN-deterministic relative deviations across the continuation in $1/a$ are $1.6\%$ for the ground state branch ($n=0$), $3.8\%$ for $n=1$, $0.9\%$ for $n=2$, and $2.1\%$ for $n=3$.

## Context
This work extends the application of physics-informed neural networks from differential-equation solvers (e.g., Lagaris (1998), Raissi (2019)) to a renormalized integral-equation formulation central to few-body physics, specifically the subtracted Faddeev equation for the Efimov effect. It contrasts with recent neural-network studies of few-body systems that rely on wave-function-based or variational formulations, such as those by Li (2026) for deuteron and triton calculations or Saito (2018) for few-body neural quantum states. The results enable a compact, differentiable representation of a renormalized few-body solution governed by discrete scale invariance, which could be extended to include three-body forces, unequal masses, or scattering states.
