# The Dynamical Dipole Radiation in Dissipative Collisions with Exotic Beams

> Heavy Ion Collisions (HIC) represent a unique tool to probe the in-medium nuclear interaction in regions away from saturation. In this work we present a selection of reaction observables in dissipative collisions particularly sensitive to the isovector part of the interaction, i.e. to the symmetry term of the nuclear Equation of State (EoS). At low energies the behavior of the symmetry energy around saturation influences dissipation and fragment production mechanisms. We will first discuss the recently observed Dynamical Dipole Radiation, due to a collective neutron-proton oscillation during the charge equilibration in fusion and deep-inelastic collisions. We will review in detail all the main properties, yield, spectrum, damping and angular distributions, revealing important isospin effects. Reactions induced by unstable 132Sn beams appear to be very promising tools to test the sub-saturation Isovector EoS. Predictions are also presented for deep-inelastic and fragmentation collisions induced by neutron rich projectiles. The importance of studying violent collisions with radioactive beams at low and Fermi energies is finally stressed.

# The Dynamical Dipole Radiation in Dissipative Collisions with Exotic Beams

## Key claim

The dynamical dipole radiation observed in dissipative heavy-ion collisions provides a sensitive probe of the symmetry energy below saturation density, but the yield depends critically on the interplay between the initial dipole moment and the fusion dynamics, not simply on the static entrance-channel asymmetry. The effect is maximized at beam energies around 10 AMeV, where the collective dipole response develops before charge equilibration via random nucleon exchange is complete.

## Method

The authors performed semiclassical Landau-Vlasov transport simulations with Skyrme-type effective interactions, incorporating mean-field dynamics, two-body collisions, and stochastic forces. They tested two different behaviors of the symmetry energy below saturation: Asysoft (smoothly decreasing toward low densities) and Asystiff (rapid decrease). The bremsstrahlung photon emission probability was computed from the Fourier transform of the dipole acceleration extracted from the transport calculations, allowing absolute evaluation of pre-equilibrium gamma-ray yields.

## Key numbers

- Initial dipole moment D(t=0) for the 132Sn+58Ni system at 10 AMeV is approximately 50 fm, about two times larger than the largest values in the table for stable-beam reactions (e.g., 22.1 fm for 40Ca+100Mo, 18.2 fm for 32S+100Mo)
- For 32S+100Mo at 6 AMeV and 9 AMeV (b=2 fm), the bremsstrahlung spectra show a net increase in prompt dipole yield when passing from the lower to the higher incident energy
- Percent increase in gamma-ray intensity at the compound nucleus GDR energy region: 16% (8-18 MeV) for 40Ca+100Mo at E*=71 MeV; 36% (8-20 MeV) for 16O+98Mo at E*=110 MeV; 1.6 +/- 2.0% (8-21 MeV) for 32S+100Mo at E*=117 MeV; 18.0 +/- 2.0% (8-21 MeV) for 32S+100Mo at E*=173.5 MeV; 12.0 +/- 2.0% (8-21 MeV) for 36Ar+96Zr at E*=280 MeV
- The symmetry energy influence on the dynamical dipole yield can be of the order of 20%, and thus well detectable
- The angular distribution parameter a2 is close to -1 for the prompt dipole radiation, indicating a strong dipole-like photon angular distribution

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for the dynamical dipole mode first suggested by Chomaz (1993) and Bortignon (1993), building on Brink's arguments about the time needed to build a Giant Dipole Resonance in hot compound nuclei. The authors provide quantitative predictions for experiments at the proposed SPIRAL2 facility using 132Sn beams, following earlier experimental observations by Flibotte (1996), Cinausero (1997), Pierroutsakou (2001, 2005), and Amore (2000). The results enable future experiments to distinguish between Asysoft and Asystiff symmetry energy behaviors through measurements of prompt dipole yields, centroid energies, and pre-equilibrium neutron-to-proton ratios, as well as through isospin transport in neck fragmentation at Fermi energies where the Chimera Collaboration has already shown evidence favoring a stiff symmetry energy.
