# Fermionic Glauber Operators and Quark Reggeization

> We derive, in the framework of soft-collinear effective field theory (SCET), a Lagrangian describing the $t$-channel exchange of Glauber quarks in the Regge limit. The Glauber quarks are not dynamical, but are incorporated through non-local fermionic potential operators. These operators are power suppressed in $|t|/s$ relative to those describing Glauber gluon exchange, but give the first non-vanishing contributions in the Regge limit to processes such as $q\bar q \to gg$ and $q\bar q \to γγ$. They therefore represent an interesting subset of power corrections to study. The structure of the operators, which describe certain soft and collinear emissions to all orders through Wilson lines, is derived from the symmetries of the effective theory combined with constraints from power and mass dimension counting, as well as through explicit matching calculations. Lightcone singularities in the fermionic potentials are regulated using a rapidity regulator, whose corresponding renormalization group evolution gives rise to the Reggeization of the quark at the amplitude level and the BFKL equation at the cross section level. We verify this at one-loop, deriving the Regge trajectory of the quark in the $3$ color channel, as well as the leading logarithmic BFKL equation. Results in the $\bar 6$ and $15$ color channels are obtained by the simultaneous exchange of a Glauber quark and a Glauber gluon. SCET with quark and gluon Glauber operators therefore provides a framework to systematically study the structure of QCD amplitudes in the Regge limit, and derive constraints on higher order amplitudes.

## Key claim

The paper derives, within soft-collinear effective theory (SCET), non-local fermionic potential operators describing $t$-channel exchange of Glauber quarks in the Regge limit; these operators are power suppressed in $|t|/s$ relative to Glauber gluon exchange but provide the first non-vanishing contributions to processes such as $q\bar q \to gg$ and $q\bar q \to \gamma\gamma$, and their rapidity renormalization yields quark Reggeization at amplitude level and the BFKL equation at cross-section level, verified at one-loop.

## Method

The authors construct an effective field theory for forward scattering by extending SCET to include fermionic Glauber operators, which are fixed by symmetries of the effective theory, power and mass dimension counting, and explicit tree-level matching calculations. The operators incorporate soft and collinear gluon emissions to all orders through Wilson lines, and lightcone singularities are regulated using a rapidity regulator whose renormalization group evolution is computed at one-loop to derive the quark Regge trajectory and the leading logarithmic BFKL equation. The framework also handles simultaneous exchange of a Glauber quark and a Glauber gluon for color channels beyond the fundamental representation.

## Key numbers

* The fermionic Glauber operators are power suppressed by $|t|/s$ relative to Glauber gluon exchange, scaling as $\mathcal{O}(\lambda)$ in the SCET power counting parameter $\lambda \ll 1$.
* The one-loop Regge trajectory for the quark in the $3$ color channel is derived, matching the gluon trajectory up to Casimir scaling ($C_A \to C_F$).
* The leading logarithmic BFKL equation for $q\bar q \to \gamma\gamma$ is obtained, equivalent to the standard BFKL equation up to Casimir scaling.
* Results for the $\bar 6$ and $15$ color channels are obtained by simultaneous exchange of a Glauber quark and a Glauber gluon.

## Context

This work extends the SCET-based framework for forward scattering developed by Rothstein and Stewart (2016), which previously treated only Glauber gluon exchange, to include fermionic Glauber exchange. It provides an operator-based description of quark Reggeization, complementing earlier studies by Fadin and Sherman (1976, 1977) and Bogdan (2006) who proved quark Reggeization to leading logarithmic order. The framework enables systematic study of subleading power corrections in the Regge limit of QCD and can interface with standard SCET for hard scattering, facilitating constraints on higher-order amplitudes.
