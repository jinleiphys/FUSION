# Nuclear weak-interaction processes in stars

> Recent experimental data and progress in nuclear structure modeling have lead to improved descriptions of astrophysically important weak-interaction processes. The review discusses these advances and their applications to hydrostatic solar and stellar burning, to the slow and rapid neutron-capture processes, to neutrino nucleosynthesis, and to explosive hydrogen burning. Special emphasis is given to the weak-interaction processes associated with core-collapse supernovae. Despite some significant progress, important improvements are still warranted. Such improvements are expected to come from future radioactive ion-beam facilities.

# Nuclear weak-interaction processes in stars

## Key claim

Despite significant progress in nuclear many-body theory and experimental measurements that have improved the description of stellar weak-interaction rates, important improvements are still warranted and are expected to come from future radioactive ion-beam facilities; the review treats weak interactions within the standard model and does not address searches for new weakly interacting particles or exotic components of the weak interaction beyond the standard model.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework for semileptonic weak processes in stars, including beta-decay, electron capture, and neutrino-nucleus reactions, using a current-current Hamiltonian density in the lowest-order approximation. They describe the nuclear many-body models employed, primarily the Shell Model for medium-mass nuclei (A ≤ 70) where complete pf-shell diagonalizations are possible, and the Random Phase Approximation for heavier nuclei and forbidden transitions, and apply these to calculate rates for hydrostatic burning, core-collapse supernovae, s- and r-process nucleosynthesis, neutrino nucleosynthesis, and explosive hydrogen burning.

## Key numbers

- The maximum shell-model dimension currently attained is 2.3 × 10^9 M=0 Slater determinants for a complete diagonalization of ^60Zn (Caurier, 2002; Mazzocchi et al., 2001).
- The chlorine detector threshold energy is 814 keV for the ^37Cl(ν_e,e^-)^37Ar reaction.
- The gallium detector threshold energy is 233.2 keV for the ^71Ga(ν_e,e^-)^71Ge reaction.
- The Super-Kamiokande observational threshold is set to approximately 7 MeV for inelastic ν+e^- scattering.
- The SNO detector threshold energy for deuteron dissociation is of order 6 MeV.
- The astrophysical S-factor for the ^7Be(p,γ)^8B reaction is in the range 18–20 eV b from recent direct and indirect measurements (Davids et al., 2001), with a slightly larger value from a very recent direct measurement (Junghans et al., 2001; Junghans and Snover, 2002).
- The uncertainty in the p+p fusion reaction rate is estimated to be about 1% (Kamionkowski and Bahcall, 1994).
- The chlorine detector observes 76% of the predicted flux from ^8B and ^7Be neutrinos according to the BP2000 solar model (Bahcall, Pinsonneault, and Basu, 2001).
- The gallium detectors observe 54% of the predicted flux from the p+p fusion reaction according to the same model.

## Context

This review extends and updates earlier comprehensive reviews of stellar nuclear reaction networks and nucleosynthesis by Wallerstein et al. (1997), Arnould and Takahashi (1999), Boyd (2000), and Smith and Rehm (2001). The authors emphasize that the solar neutrino problem has been resolved by neutrino oscillations rather than by improved nuclear physics input, as solar models passed stringent tests from helioseismology (Christensen-Dalsgaard et al., 1996). The review enables future work by identifying where radioactive ion-beam facilities are needed to measure properties of neutron-rich nuclei on the r-process path, building on experimental advances by Pfeiffer, Kratz et al. (2001).
