# Examination of the directed flow puzzle in heavy-ion collisions

> Recent STAR data for the directed flow of protons, antiprotons, and charged pions obtained within the beam energy scan program are analyzed within the parton-hadron-string-dynamics (PHSD and HSD) transport models and a 3-fluid hydrodynamics (3FD) approach. Both versions of the kinetic approach, HSD and PHSD, are used to clarify the role of partonic degrees of freedom. The PHSD results, simulating a partonic phase and its coexistence with a hadronic one, are roughly consistent with data. The hydrodynamic results are obtained for two equations of state (EoS), a pure hadronic EoS and an EoS with a crossover type transition. The latter case is favored by the STAR experimental data. Special attention is paid to the description of antiproton directed flow based on the balance of $p\bar{p}$ annihilation and the inverse processes for $p\bar{p}$ pair creation from multimeson interactions. Generally, the semiqualitative agreement between the measured data and the model results supports the idea of a crossover type of quark-hadron transition that softens the nuclear EoS but shows no indication of a first-order phase transition.

## Key claim

The STAR beam energy scan data for directed flow of protons, antiprotons, and charged pions are semiqualitatively consistent with both the PHSD transport model (which includes a crossover quark-hadron transition) and the 3-fluid hydrodynamic model with a crossover equation of state, but the data show no indication of a first-order phase transition; however, the agreement is not perfect, particularly at lower collision energies below about 11.5 GeV where the models overestimate antiproton and pion flow slopes.

## Method

The authors analyzed STAR data from Au+Au collisions at center-of-mass energies from 7.7 to 200 GeV using two complementary approaches: the parton-hadron-string-dynamics (PHSD) transport model and its hadronic-only version (HSD), and a 3-fluid hydrodynamics (3FD) model. The PHSD/HSD calculations simulated semicentral collisions (impact parameter 7 fm) with event-by-event fluctuations and experimental acceptance cuts (0.2 to 2 GeV/c transverse momentum), while the 3FD model was run with two equations of state (purely hadronic and crossover) for energies up to 30 GeV. The directed flow slope at midrapidity was extracted using both linear and cubic fits to the v1(y) distributions.

## Key numbers

- Proton directed flow slope F is positive at low energies and crosses zero at about sqrt(s_NN) = 20 GeV in PHSD/HSD, while the experimental crossing point is at about half that energy; calculated proton slopes at high energies are about -(0.01-0.02) compared to measured values of about -0.005
- Antiproton slope in PHSD shows a wide shallow negative minimum at sqrt(s_NN) approximately 30 GeV, while the measured slope is monotonically increasing with energy
- Charged pion slopes at sqrt(s_NN) less than about 11 GeV show a negative minimum deeper than the measured one in both PHSD and HSD
- The 3FD model calculations were performed only up to sqrt(s_NN) = 30 GeV due to numerical demands; results with crossover EoS describe STAR data better than purely hadronic EoS

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of directed flow as a signature of the quark-hadron phase transition, following predictions by Rischke (1995, 1996) and Stoecker (2005) that a first-order phase transition would produce a "softest point collapse" or "wiggle" structure in the v1 excitation function. The analysis directly addresses the "directed flow puzzle" raised by the Frankfurt group (SAP14, 2014) who could not obtain conclusive results at energies below 20 GeV. The findings support a crossover-type transition consistent with lattice QCD results, contradicting earlier hydrodynamic calculations by Brachmann (2000) and Csernai (1999) that required a first-order transition to produce the wiggle structure. The results enable future work to refine the equation of state at high baryon density and to investigate the remaining discrepancies at low beam energies.
