# Comprehensive Assessment of $\mathrm{Th}^{3+}$ Properties for Nuclear Clock and Fundamental Physics Applications

> By employing singles, doubles, and triples excitations within the relativistic coupled-cluster framework, we perform comprehensive calculations of a wide range of atomic properties for the Th$^{3+}$ ion. These properties are essential for advancing nuclear clock technology and probing fundamental physics. Combining our isotope shift parameters with experimental data, we estimate highly accurate values of the differential nuclear charge radii for $^{232,229}$Th and $^{229m,229}$Th. Additionally, we determine the nuclear magnetic dipole and electric quadrupole moments for both the ground and isomeric states of $^{229}$Th by combining measured hyperfine structure constants with our theoretical calculations. Our precise evaluations of electric dipole polarizabilities and hyperfine-induced quadrupole moments are critical for assessing systematic uncertainties in $^{229}$Th$^{3+}$-based nuclear clock. Notably, we observe unexpectedly significant contributions from higher-order relativistic effects and excitations involving orbitals with higher angular momentum, which markedly influence the energies of the ground state and its fine-structure partner. These results highlight the substantial challenges in achieving highly accurate predictions for these properties.

# Comprehensive Assessment of Th³⁺ Properties for Nuclear Clock and Fundamental Physics Applications

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that higher-order relativistic effects and excitations involving orbitals with angular momentum beyond l=6 contribute unexpectedly large corrections to the energies of Th³⁺, challenging the accuracy achievable with standard computational truncations. Despite achieving 1-2% agreement with experimental energies, the paper cautions that further improvements require inclusion of even higher-level excitations in the relativistic coupled-cluster framework.

## Method
The authors employ the relativistic coupled-cluster method with singles, doubles, and triples excitations (RCCSDT) starting from the Dirac-Hartree-Fock (DHF) method, using the Dirac-Coulomb Hamiltonian with subsequent corrections from Breit and vacuum polarization interactions. Isotope shift constants are computed via the finite-field approach, and hyperfine structure constants are evaluated within the expectation value evaluation framework. Calculations are performed with orbital basis sets up to l=9 to capture contributions from high-angular-momentum orbitals.

## Key numbers
- The ground state 5F₅/₂ energy is calculated as -229455 cm⁻¹, compared to the experimental value of -231065 cm⁻¹ (1% accuracy)
- The field shift constant F for the ground state is 54.1(15) GHz/fm², compared to 55.0(55) GHz/fm² from Dzuba (2023) and 51.4 GHz/fm² from Si (2025)
- The weighted average differential nuclear charge radius δ⟨r²⟩²³²·²²⁹ = 0.316(9) fm², increasing from 0.305(2) fm² when only field shift is considered
- The isomeric differential charge radius δ⟨r²⟩²²⁹ᵐ·²²⁹ = 0.0112(1) fm²
- Recommended magnetic dipole moment μ_I = 0.3618(12) μ_N for the ²²⁹Th ground state
- Recommended electric quadrupole moment Q_n = 2.91(3) b for the ²²⁹Th ground state
- The scalar electric dipole polarizability α_d^S = 14.33(26) a.u. and tensor polarizability α_d^T = -4.78(20) a.u. for the ground state
- The hyperfine-induced quadrupole moment Θ(F)⁽⁰⁾ for both the F=5 level of the ground state and F=4 level of the isomeric state is 0.614(9) a.u.

## Context
This work extends previous calculations of Th³⁺ properties by Dzuba (2023) and Si (2025), resolving approximately 8% discrepancies in field shift constants and 7% differences in isomeric charge radius estimates. The calculations provide improved precision for nuclear moments compared to earlier atomic studies by Safronova (2013), Li (2021), and Porsev (2021), and show better agreement with nuclear model predictions for the quadrupole moment. The results enable more accurate assessment of systematic uncertainties in the proposed ²²⁹Th³⁺ nuclear clock, which targets fractional uncertainties as low as 10⁻¹⁹, and support searches for temporal variations of fundamental constants and dark matter interactions.
