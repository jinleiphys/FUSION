# Hydrodynamic Models for Heavy Ion Collisions

> Application of hydrodynamics for modeling of heavy-ion collisions is reviewed. We consider several physical observables that can be calculated in this approach and compare them to the experimental measurements.

## Key claim

This review argues that hydrodynamic modeling can describe the bulk expansion of matter produced in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, but only under the strong assumption that the system stays in local thermodynamic equilibrium throughout the expansion; the approach cannot be strictly justified because the produced system (several thousand particles at RHIC) hardly forms a macroscopic system in the proper thermodynamic sense, and the high-momentum partons are clearly not thermalized.

## Method

The authors review the formulation of ideal (non-viscous) relativistic hydrodynamics for heavy-ion collisions, including the energy-momentum conservation equations and continuity equations for conserved densities such as baryon number. They describe how initial conditions are obtained either from perturbative QCD calculations of minijet production with a saturation cutoff or from the color glass condensate effective field theory, and how the equation of state of strongly interacting matter (including the transition from quark-gluon plasma to hadron resonance gas) is incorporated. The review then discusses how hydrodynamic results are linked to observable quantities such as hadron spectra and electromagnetic emission, and compares these calculations to experimental measurements mainly from RHIC.

## Key numbers

- Total multiplicities exceeding 1000 particles per unit rapidity ($dN/dy \gsim 1000$) are produced in central gold-gold collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV at RHIC.
- The measured average transverse momentum is $p_T \gsim 0.5$ GeV, indicating a total energy per unit rapidity interval of $dE_T/dy \gsim 500$ GeV.
- At proper time $\tau = 1$ fm/$c$, the implied particle density is $\sim 10$ fm$^{-3}$ and energy density is $\sim 5$ GeV fm$^{-3}$.
- The saturation scale is $p_{\rm sat} = 1.16$ GeV at RHIC ($\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV) and 2.03 GeV at the LHC ($\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5500$ GeV), with formation times of 0.170 fm/$c$ and 0.100 fm/$c$, respectively.
- For the larger saturation scale scenario in the color glass condensate approach, the initially produced transverse energy is approximately 2.5 times bigger than the measured one.

## Context

This review extends the line of work applying relativistic hydrodynamics to heavy-ion collisions that began with Bjorken (1983) and was developed through numerical simulations by various groups. It contrasts the pQCD plus saturation approach of Eskola and collaborators with the color glass condensate framework of McLerran, Krasnitz, and Lappi, and discusses how neither binary-collision scaling nor wounded-nucleon scaling alone reproduces the centrality dependence of multiplicity at RHIC, requiring a linear combination of both. The review enables future work by providing a comprehensive framework for calculating observables such as elliptic flow and electromagnetic emission, and by identifying open questions about thermalization timescales and the role of viscosity.
