# Comment on "Phase-space consideration on barrier transmission in a time-dependent variational approach with superposed wave packets (arXiv:2201.02966)"

> We reply to the criticisms of our publication (N. Hasegawa, K. Hagino, and Y. Tanimura, Phys. Lett. B808, 135693 (2020)) made by A. Ono in his recent article, arXiv:2201.02966.

# Comment on "Phase-space consideration on barrier transmission in a time-dependent variational approach with superposed wave packets (arXiv:2201.02966)"

## Key claim

The authors defend their earlier use of the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) for many-particle tunneling, arguing that the fixed-width Gaussian single-particle wave functions in their approach behave classically and therefore the broad momentum distribution of each Gaussian does not fully contribute to the time-dependent dynamics, contrary to the criticism by Ono (2022).

## Method

The authors analyze the form of the relative wave function between two nuclei in their TDGCM calculation, which is a superposition of Gaussian wave packets with a time-independent width of 1.0 fm$^{-2}$. They compare this approach to entangled trajectory molecular dynamics (ETMD) from quantum chemistry, where classical trajectories are entangled through a linear superposition of Slater determinants to recover quantum mechanical behavior. They also compute the mean energy and energy variance of the initial superposition of two Gaussian functions and compare them to the average barrier height.

## Key numbers

- The width parameter $\nu_r$ in the Gaussian wave packets was taken as 1.0 fm$^{-2}$ in the original work (Hasegawa, Hagino, and Tanimura, 2020).
- The mean energy of the superposition of two Gaussian functions in the original calculation was 0.11 MeV.
- The energy variance due to the superposition was 6.5 $\times$ 10$^{-3}$ MeV.
- The average barrier height in the original calculation was 0.13 MeV.
- The energy variance (6.5 $\times$ 10$^{-3}$ MeV) is much smaller than the barrier height (0.13 MeV).

## Context

This comment responds to a criticism by Ono (2022) of the authors' earlier work (Hasegawa, Hagino, and Tanimura, 2020) on many-particle tunneling using TDGCM. The authors draw a parallel between their method and the entangled trajectory molecular dynamics approach developed in quantum chemistry by Donoso and Martens (2001) and by Donoso, Zheng, and Martens (2003). They also note that the spurious cross-channel correlation problem, known from time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory (Ring and Schuck, 1980; and another reference), complicates the interpretation of individual Slater determinant energies in the final state. This work clarifies the appropriate interpretation of energy spreading in TDGCM calculations with fixed-width Gaussian wave packets.
