# Dynamical response of the nuclear pasta in neutron star crusts

> The nuclear pasta -- a novel state of matter having nucleons arranged in a variety of complex shapes -- is expected to be found in the crust of neutron stars and in core-collapse supernovae at subnuclear densities of about $10^{14}$ g/cm$^3$. Due to frustration, a phenomenon that emerges from the competition between short-range nuclear attraction and long-range Coulomb repulsion, the nuclear pasta displays a preponderance of unique low-energy excitations. These excitations could have a strong impact on many transport properties, such as neutrino propagation through stellar environments. The excitation spectrum of the nuclear pasta is computed via a molecular-dynamics simulation involving up to 100,000 nucleons. The dynamic response of the pasta displays a classical plasma oscillation in the 1-2 MeV region. In addition, substantial strength is found at low energies. Yet this low-energy strength is missing from a simple ion model containing a single-representative heavy nucleus. The low-energy strength observed in the dynamic response of the pasta is likely to be a density wave involving the internal degrees of freedom of the clusters.

## Key claim

The nuclear pasta's dynamical response shows a classical plasma oscillation in the 1-2 MeV region plus substantial low-energy strength that is missing from a simple ion model containing a single representative heavy nucleus; the low-energy strength is likely a density wave involving the internal degrees of freedom of the clusters, but the authors state this interpretation needs to be verified in future work.

## Method

The authors performed semi-classical molecular-dynamics simulations of up to 100,000 nucleons (neutrons and protons) interacting via a two-body potential fitted to reproduce nuclear saturation and including screened Coulomb repulsion, at a fixed temperature of 1 MeV. They computed the dynamical response function S(q,ω) from the time-dependent density-density correlation function for baryon densities of 0.01 fm⁻³ and 0.05 fm⁻³ with a proton fraction Yp=0.2, and compared the results to a simplified ion model containing a single representative heavy nucleus (A=100, Z=28) with only screened Coulomb interactions.

## Key numbers

- Baryon densities simulated: ρ = 0.01 fm⁻³ (about 1/15 of normal nuclear density) and ρ = 0.05 fm⁻³ (about 1/3 of normal nuclear density)
- Temperature fixed at T = 1 MeV throughout all simulations
- Proton-to-baryon fraction Yp = 0.2 used for all simulations
- Simulations used up to 100,000 nucleons (80,000 neutrons and 20,000 protons) at ρ = 0.05 fm⁻³, and 40,000 nucleons at ρ = 0.01 fm⁻³
- Plasma oscillation frequency in the 1-2 MeV region observed in the dynamical response
- At ρ = 0.05 fm⁻³, the dynamical response shows a peak just below ω = 1 MeV at low momentum transfers (q = 0.05, 0.11, and 0.19 fm⁻¹)
- At ρ = 0.01 fm⁻³, the ion model used 288 ions with average mass A=100 and charge Z=28, with 28% free neutrons and 72% heavy ions
- The cluster form factor used a uniform density sphere of radius 6.68 fm
- Simulation time steps of Δt = 1-2 fm/c, with thermalization for 28,000 fm/c and production runs of 388,000 fm/c (100,000 nucleon case) and 720,000 fm/c (40,000 nucleon case)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous calculations of the static structure factor of nuclear pasta (Horowitz et al., 2004a,b) to compute the dynamical response, which may influence energy equilibration between matter and muon/tau neutrinos in core-collapse supernovae. It contrasts with the random phase approximation calculation by Khan, Sandulescu, and Van Giai (2005) who used a spherical Wigner-Seitz unit cell that may be inadequate for long-wavelength collective modes extending across many unit cells, and with the quantum-molecular-dynamics simulations of Watanabe and collaborators who used a more complicated interaction. The results enable future full quantum calculations to verify the semiclassical findings and further study of transport properties such as thermal conductivity, viscosity, diffusion, and opacity in neutron star crusts.
