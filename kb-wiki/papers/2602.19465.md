# Microscopic Optical Potential from Brueckner-Hartree-Fock Theory

> Modern Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) calculations are very successful in describing various properties of symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter. Within BHF theory a microscopic optical potential (MOP) for nucleon-nucleus scattering is developed. First, we parametrize the energy and density dependence of complex optical potentials in nuclear matter based on BHF calculations and then we construct the MOP for finite nuclei with the local density approximation extended to include the finite-range effects. The density distribution and the spin-orbit contribution are calculated from the Hartree-Fock (HF) approximation with LNS5 Skyrme interaction, the latter being constrained by the BHF results. The central real and imaginary potentials turn out to be quantitatively consistent with the phenomenological global Koning-Delaroche (KD) potentials. The performance of MOP is evaluated by considering neutron/proton scattering on $^{40,48}$Ca. The elastic scattering differential cross sections, analyzing powers and total/reaction cross sections are analyzed in the energy below 200 MeV. A good agreement between the theoretical results and the measurements is achieved. Since our results are presented in the analytic forms, they can thus be used easily in the analysis of the experimental data of the nucleon scattering on exotic nuclei.

## Key claim
The paper claims that a microscopic optical potential (MOP) built from Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) theory, using the Argonne V18 nucleon-nucleon interaction plus a consistent three-body force, can reproduce elastic scattering observables for neutron/proton on $^{40,48}$Ca below 200 MeV with accuracy comparable to the phenomenological Koning-Delaroche (KD) potential, but with the hedge that the imaginary part shows qualitative differences at low energies and the spin-orbit potential is taken from a separate Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculation rather than derived microscopically.

## Method
The authors first solved the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone equation in symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter using the Argonne V18 interaction with a microscopic three-body force, computing the complex nucleon self-energy for densities from 0.06 to 0.20 fm$^{-3}$ and energies from 10 to 200 MeV. They parametrized the resulting isoscalar and isovector components of the optical potential in nuclear matter using the Jeukenne-Lejeune-Mahaux ansatz, then constructed the MOP for finite nuclei by applying the improved local density approximation (ILDA) with a Gaussian folding width of 1.4 fm, using nuclear densities and spin-orbit potentials from Hartree-Fock calculations with the LNS5 Skyrme interaction constrained by BHF results.

## Key numbers
- Energy range considered: 10 to 200 MeV for the nuclear matter calculations.
- Densities considered: 0.06, 0.08, 0.10, 0.12, 0.14, 0.16, 0.18, and 0.20 fm$^{-3}$.
- Gaussian folding width parameter $t$ = 1.4 fm used in the ILDA.
- Parameter $D$ = 600 MeV$^2$ used in the imaginary isoscalar potential formula.
- Parameter $F$ = 1 MeV used in the imaginary isovector potential formula.
- The real central part of MOP shows good agreement with KD potentials at 10, 40, and 100 MeV for both $^{40}$Ca and $^{48}$Ca, but at 160 MeV the MOP depths are smaller than KD.
- For n+$^{48}$Ca, the MOP results show much better agreement with experimental data than the global KD results at all energies.

## Context
This work extends the nuclear-matter approach to microscopic optical potentials pioneered by Jeukenne, Lejeune, and Mahaux (1977) and later developed by Bauge et al. (1998), using modern BHF calculations with three-body forces that have been successful in describing nuclear matter properties (Zuo et al., 2002; Cao et al., 2006). The paper builds on previous BHF-based optical potential studies (Li et al., 2009; Li et al., 2008) that were limited to nuclear matter, now extending to finite nuclei via ILDA and testing on $^{40,48}$Ca. This enables future applications to nucleon scattering on exotic nuclei where phenomenological potentials like Koning-Delaroche (2003) are unreliable, providing analytic forms that can be readily used in experimental data analysis.
