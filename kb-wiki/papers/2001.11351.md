# Production of $^4{\rm Li}$ and $p\!-\!^3{\rm He}$ correlation function in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> The thermal and coalescence models both describe well yields of light nuclei produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions at LHC. We propose to measure the yield of $^4{\rm Li}$ and compare it to that of $^4{\rm He}$ to falsify one of the models. Since the masses of $^4{\rm He}$ and $^4{\rm Li}$ are almost equal, the yield of $^4{\rm Li}$ is about 5 times bigger than that of $^4{\rm He}$ in the thermal model because of different numbers of spin states of the two nuclides. Their internal structures are, however, very different: the alpha particle is well bound and compact while $^4{\rm Li}$ is weakly bound and loose. Consequently, the ratio of yields of $^4{\rm Li}$ to $^4{\rm He}$ is significantly smaller in the coalescence model and it strongly depends on the collision centrality. Since the nuclide $^4{\rm Li}$ is unstable and it decays into $^3{\rm He}$ and $p$, the yield of $^4{\rm Li}$ can be experimentally obtained through a measurement of the $p\!-\!^3{\rm He}$ correlation function. The function carries information not only about the yield of $^4{\rm Li}$ but also about the source of $^3{\rm He}$ and allows one to determine through a source-size measurement whether of $^3{\rm He}$ is directly emitted from the fireball or it is formed afterwards. We compute the correlation function taking into account the $s-$wave scattering and Coulomb repulsion together with the resonance interaction responsible for the $^4{\rm Li}$ nuclide. We discuss how to infer information about an origin of $^3{\rm He}$ from the correlation function, and finally a method to obtain the yield of $^4{\rm Li}$ is proposed.

## Key claim

The ratio of yields of \(^4{\rm Li}\) to \(^4{\rm He}\) can distinguish between the thermal model (which predicts a ratio of about 4.3 at a chemical freeze-out temperature of 156 MeV) and the coalescence model (which predicts a significantly smaller ratio that depends strongly on the collision centrality), thereby falsifying one of the two models.

## Method

The authors computed the formation rates of \(^4{\rm He}\) and \(^4{\rm Li}\) within the coalescence model using Gaussian source functions and parameterized nuclear wave functions, deriving analytic expressions for the ratio of yields as a function of the source radius \(R_s\) and the unknown RMS radius of \(^4{\rm Li}\) (\(R_{\rm Li}\)). They then derived the \(p\!-\!^3{\rm He}\) correlation function for two scenarios: one where \(^3{\rm He}\) is treated as an elementary particle emitted directly from the fireball, and another where \(^3{\rm He}\) is formed as a bound state of three nucleons via final-state interactions. The correlation function includes \(s\)-wave scattering, Coulomb repulsion, and the resonance interaction responsible for the \(^4{\rm Li}\) nuclide.

## Key numbers

* The thermal model predicts a yield ratio of \(^4{\rm Li}\) to \(^4{\rm He}\) of about 4.3 at a chemical freeze-out temperature of 156 MeV, reduced from 5 due to the mass difference of 20 MeV between the two nuclides.
* For the coalescence model, with \(R_{\rm Li}=3\) fm and a source radius \(R_s \approx 7\) fm (central heavy-ion collisions), the ratio of formation rates \({\cal A}_4^{\rm Li}/{\cal A}_4^{\rm He}\) is about 3; for \(R_s \approx 4\) fm (40-60% centrality), the ratio drops below 2.
* The RMS radius of \(^4{\rm He}\) is \(R_\alpha = 1.68\) fm, and the RMS radius of the \(^3{\rm He}\) cluster is \(R_c = 1.97\) fm.
* The lifetime of \(^4{\rm Li}\) is about \(30~{\rm fm}/c\) and its decay width is 6 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Bazak and Mrowczynski, 2018) on comparing \(^4{\rm He}\) and \(^4{\rm Li}\) yields, and it refines the coalescence model calculations. It contrasts with a recent computation by Xi et al. (2019), which did not include resonance interference in the \(p\!-\!^3{\rm He}\) correlation function and produced a resonance peak that was too narrow. The proposed measurement of the \(p\!-\!^3{\rm He}\) correlation function can also determine whether \(^3{\rm He}\) is directly emitted from the fireball or formed afterwards, by comparing the inferred source radius with that from proton-proton correlations (e.g., from Acharya et al., 2018, or Adam et al., 2015).
