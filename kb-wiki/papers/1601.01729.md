# The Role of Nucleon Strangeness in Supernova Explosions

> Recent hydrodynamical simulations of supernova (SN) evolution have highlighted the importance of a thorough control over microscopic physics responsible for such internal processes as neutrino heating. In particular, it has been suggested that modifications to the neutrino-nucleon elastic cross section can potentially play a crucial role in producing successful supernova explosions. One possible source of such corrections can be found in a nonzero value for the nucleon's strange helicity content $Δs$. In the present analysis, however, we show that theoretical and experimental progress over the past decade has suggested a comparatively small magnitude for $Δs$, such that its sole effect is not sufficient to provide the physics leading to supernova explosions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a nonzero strange helicity content of the nucleon (Δs) cannot be the single decisive effect that enables core-collapse supernova explosions, because theoretical and experimental progress over the past decade has shown that Δs is too small to produce the ~15% reduction in neutrino-nucleon opacities that would be needed.

## Method

The authors survey and critically assess experimental determinations of Δs from spin-polarized deep inelastic scattering data (including HERMES, COMPASS, and global PDF analyses) and theoretical calculations from lattice QCD, the Cloudy Bag Model, and light-front wave function models. They then use a light-front wave function model to show that a value of Δs = -0.2 would require implausibly large strange quark momentum fractions xS⁺ that are excluded by global analyses of unpolarized DIS data.

## Key numbers

- The value Δs = -0.2 used in the Melson et al. (2015) supernova simulation lies well beyond the range of modern determinations
- Global PDF analyses yield an average Δs = -0.120 ± 0.021 (Diehl et al., 2007)
- HERMES (2007) measured Δs = -0.09 ± 0.02 at Q² = 10 GeV²
- COMPASS (2010) obtained Δs = -0.02 ± 0.02 (extrapolation) and Δs = -0.1 ± 0.02 (DSSV) at Q² = 3 GeV²
- The average of Leader et al. (2014) and Sato et al. (2016) gives Δs = -0.103 ± 0.013
- The mean of all experimental/phenomenological determinations is Δs = -0.087 ± 0.009
- Lattice QCD calculations average Δs = -0.022 ± 0.005
- The χQCD Collaboration (Gong et al., 2015) obtained Δs = -0.084 ± 0.024 using the anomalous Ward identity
- The Cloudy Bag Model (Bass, 2009) yields Δs = -0.02 ± 0.01
- The light-front wave function model (Hobbs et al., 2014) gives -0.041 ≤ Δs ≤ -0.039
- A value Δs = -0.2 would require xS⁺ ≥ 0.062, while CTEQ global analyses find the 90% limit 0.018 ≤ xS⁺ ≤ 0.04

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Horowitz (2002), which obtained Δs = -0.1 ± 0.1 from BNL neutrino-proton scattering data, and directly addresses the supernova simulations of Melson et al. (2015), which used Δs = -0.2 to produce an explosion ~300 ms after shock bounce in a 20 M⊙ progenitor star. The paper shows that such a large value is incompatible with modern experimental data and theoretical calculations, and recommends using Δs = -0.1 as a well-motivated upper limit for future hydrodynamical simulations, with Δs = -0.04 as a more conservative value consistent with theoretical computations.
