# Constraints on the Variation of the QCD Interaction Scale $Λ_{\text{QCD}}$

> Laboratory and astrophysical tests of ''constant variation'' have so far concentrated on the dimensionless fine-structure constant $α$ and on the electron or quark mass ratios $X_{e,q}=m_{e,q}/Λ_{\text{QCD}}$, treating the QCD scale $Λ_{\text{QCD}}$ as unchangeable. Certain beyond Standard Model frameworks, most notably those with a dark matter or dark energy scalar field $ϕ$ coupling with the gluon field, would make $Λ_{\text{QCD}}$ itself time dependent while leaving $α$ and the electron mass untouched. Under the minimal assumption that this gluonic channel is the sole $ϕ$ interaction, we recast state-of-the-art atomic clock comparisons into $\dotΛ_{\text{QCD}}/Λ_{\text{QCD}}=(3.2 \pm 3.5) \times 10^{-17} \ \text{yr}^{-1}$ limits, translate the isotope yields of the 1.8-Gyr-old Oklo natural reactor into a complementary geophysical limit of $|δΛ_{\text{QCD}}/Λ_{\text{QCD}}|<2\times10^{-9}$ over that time span, corresponding to the linear drift limit $|\dotΛ_{\text{QCD}}/Λ_{\text{QCD}}|<1\times10^{-18} \text{yr}^{-1}$, and show that the proposed $8.4$ eV $^{229}$Th nuclear clock would amplify a putative $Λ_{\text{QCD}}$ drift by four orders of magnitude compared with present atomic clocks. We also obtain constraints from quasar absorption spectra and Big Bang Nucleosynthesis data.

## Key claim

Under the minimal assumption that a scalar field \(\phi\) couples only to gluons (leaving \(\alpha\) and electron mass untouched), the authors reinterpret existing atomic clock, Oklo reactor, quasar absorption, and Big Bang Nucleosynthesis data to place constraints on the time variation of the QCD scale \(\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\) itself, rather than treating it as fixed. The abstract's quoted drift limit from atomic clocks is conditional on this single-channel coupling scenario.

## Method

The authors recast measurements of ratios of atomic clock transition frequencies (e.g., \(^{171}\text{Yb}^+\)/Cs, Al\(^+\)/Hg\(^+\), Yb\(^+\) E3/E2) into limits on \(\dot{\Lambda}_{\text{QCD}}/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\) by calculating the sensitivity of each transition to the dimensionless ratios \(X_e = m_e/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\) and \(X_q = m_q/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\), using chiral perturbation theory to relate nucleon magnetic moments, nuclear radii, and binding energies to \(\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\) and quark masses. They also translate the isotope yield data from the 1.8-Gyr-old Oklo natural reactor into a geophysical bound on \(\delta\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\), and estimate the sensitivity of the proposed \(^{229}\)Th nuclear clock to \(\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\) drift by analyzing the dependence of the isomer energy on hadron masses via the Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation and nuclear binding energy calculations from Wiringa (2009).

## Key numbers

*   From the \(^{171}\text{Yb}^+\)/Cs atomic clock comparison (Lange 2021): \(\dot{\Lambda}_{\text{QCD}}/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}} = (3.2 \pm 3.5) \times 10^{-17} \ \text{yr}^{-1}\).
*   From the Oklo natural reactor (1.8 Gyr ago): \(|\delta\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}| < 2 \times 10^{-9}\) over that time span, corresponding to \(|\dot{\Lambda}_{\text{QCD}}/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}| < 1 \times 10^{-18} \ \text{yr}^{-1}\).
*   From the Al\(^+\)/Hg\(^+\) optical clock comparison: \(\dot{\Lambda}_{\text{QCD}}/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}} = (3.2 \pm 4.9) \times 10^{-15} \ \text{yr}^{-1}\).
*   From the Yb\(^+\) E3/E2 optical clock comparison: \(\dot{\Lambda}_{\text{QCD}}/\Lambda_{\text{QCD}} = (7.2 \pm 11) \times 10^{-17} \ \text{yr}^{-1}\).
*   The proposed \(^{229}\)Th nuclear clock (8.36 eV transition) would amplify a putative \(\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\) drift by four orders of magnitude compared with present atomic clocks, with the binding energy sensitivity coefficient to \(\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\) being \(\sim 5\) times larger than to the quark mass \(m_q\).

## Context

This work extends the standard framework of fundamental constant variation, which has focused on \(\alpha\) and the mass ratios \(X_{e,q}\) (e.g., Uzan review, Webb 1999, Flambaum & Shuryak 2002, Thomas 2005, Tedesco 2006), by allowing \(\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\) to vary independently via a gluonic coupling to a scalar field. It reinterprets existing clock constraints from Banerjee (2023) and Filzinger (2023) and Oklo bounds from earlier analyses, and shows that the \(^{229}\)Th nuclear clock proposed by Peik (2003) and Flambaum (2006) would be a highly sensitive probe of \(\Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\) variation, enabling future searches for dark matter or dark energy couplings to gluons.
