# Hadron Physics at J-PARC

> The aim of the hadron physics research programs conducted at J-PARC is to explore the structure of hadronic matter using the world's highest-intensity meson beams. Since the first beam was extracted at the hadron experimental facility (HEF) in February 2009, a wide variety of physics experiments have been proposed and performed to address open questions regarding quantum chromodynamics (QCD) at low energy. The high-intensity $K^-$ and high-momentum beams available at J-PARC open a new era in hadron and nuclear physics, in which strange and charm quarks play an important role. We review the programs focused on addressing the hadron structure as strongly interacting composite particles, the origin of hadron mass, and interactions between hadrons under broken flavor SU(3) symmetry.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the hadron physics program at J-PARC, claiming that the world's highest-intensity meson beams enable exploration of hadron structure, the origin of hadron mass, and baryon-baryon interactions under broken flavor SU(3) symmetry, but the actual results show that many key searches (such as for the Θ⁺ pentaquark) have produced null results with only upper limits, and many experiments remain in planning or forthcoming stages.

## Method

The authors describe the J-PARC accelerator complex and Hadron Experimental Facility (HEF), which provides 30 GeV primary proton beams and secondary beams of pions, kaons, and antiprotons through multiple beamlines (K1.8, K1.8BR, K1.1, KL, and high-p). They review specific experiments including missing-mass spectroscopy for pentaquark searches via the (π⁻, K⁻) reaction using the Superconducting Kaon Spectrometer (SKS), invariant mass reconstruction for H-dibaryon searches via (K⁻, K⁺) reactions using a Hyperon Spectrometer with a time projection chamber, and planned measurements of vector meson spectral functions and meson-nuclear bound states.

## Key numbers

- The E19 experiment achieved a missing mass resolution of 1.72 MeV (FWHM) for Θ⁺ production at an incident pion momentum of 1.92 GeV/c, and 2.13 MeV (FWHM) at 2.01 GeV/c.
- The E19 experiment set an upper limit of 0.28 μb/sr at 90% confidence level for the Θ⁺ production cross section in the mass region 1.510 to 1.550 GeV/c², an order of magnitude lower than the previous KEK-PS E522 result of 2.9 μb/sr.
- The decay width upper limit at 90% CL was derived to be less than 0.36 MeV for assumed spin-parity 1/2⁺ and less than 1.9 MeV for 1/2⁻.
- The E42 experiment expects to accumulate 10,000 ΛΛ events over 33 days of data taking, with an expected double Λ invariant mass resolution of approximately 1.5 MeV/c² at the ΞN mass threshold.
- As of the end of 2019, the MR-SX beam power reached 51 kW with 5.4×10¹³ protons per pulse in a 5.2 s repetition cycle.
- The K1.8 beamline provides a maximum central momentum of approximately 2 GeV/c, with a total length of 45.8 m.
- The K1.8BR beamline delivers low-momentum kaon beams of 0.7–1.1 GeV/c, with a total length of 31.3 m.

## Context

This review extends the experimental program previously conducted at the KEK 12 GeV proton synchrotron (KEK-PS), including the E522 and E224 experiments that found suggestive but statistically weak signals for the Θ⁺ pentaquark and H-dibaryon. The paper describes how J-PARC's higher intensity beams enable experiments with significantly improved statistics (the E42 experiment expects about 120 times more statistics than E522) and resolution, while also enabling new measurements of vector meson spectral functions (E16) and meson-nuclear bound states (E26, E29) that address predictions from QCD sum rules by Brown (1991), Hatsuda (1991), and Klingl (1997) regarding chiral symmetry restoration in nuclear matter.
