# Nuclear Model of Binding alpha-particles

> The model of binding alpha-particles in nuclei is suggested. It is shown good (with the accuracy of 1-2%) description of the experimental binding energies in light and medium nuclear systems. Our preliminary calculations show enhancement of the binding energy for super heavy nuclei with Z~120.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that atomic nuclei can be modeled as a tight packing of alpha-particles, and claims that this model describes experimental binding energies in light and medium nuclei with 1-2% accuracy; however, the abstract's hedge is that the binding energy enhancement for superheavy nuclei with Z~120 is based on preliminary calculations whose exact Z value and binding energy depend on the details of the potential.

## Method

The authors treat the nucleus as a collection of alpha-particles interacting via a Lennard-Jones potential (depth V0, bonding length sigma). They place alpha-particles sequentially on a grid by minimizing the total potential energy at each step, then compute the total binding energy as the sum of the alpha-particle self-energy (28.296 MeV per alpha), the Lennard-Jones bond energy (V0 times the number of bonds B_alpha), and a Coulomb term. The unit bond energy is approximated as V0 ~ 5 MeV from the assumption of six equal nucleon bonds per close-packed tetrahedral alpha particle.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy of a single alpha-particle: 28.296 MeV
- Lennard-Jones potential depth V0: approximately 5 MeV (derived as E_B(alpha)/6)
- Bonding length sigma between alpha-particles: approximately 3 fm
- Accuracy of binding energy description for light and medium nuclei: 1-2%
- Predicted region of enhanced binding for superheavy nuclei: Z ~ 120, corresponding to a completed icosahedral shell of 55 alpha-particles

## Context

This work extends the cluster model approach to nuclear structure, where nuclei are built from alpha-particle subunits rather than individual nucleons. The authors note that a similar mechanism of alpha-particle formation analogous to Cooper pairs was considered by Koh (no year given in text), and one of the authors (Gridnev, 2002) previously predicted fullerene-like nuclei in the Z~120 region. The model enables further study of shell effects in heavy and superheavy nuclei that go beyond traditional single-particle shell models, with the structure of heavy even-even nuclei described as an Fe nucleus coated with a Bose condensate of alpha-particles.
