# Singularity Structures in Coulomb-Type Potentials in Two Body Dirac Equations of Constraint Dynamics

> Two Body Dirac Equations (TBDE) of Dirac's relativistic constraint dynamics have been successfully applied to obtain a covariant nonperturbative description of QED and QCD bound states. Coulomb-type potentials in these applications lead naively in other approaches to singular relativistic corrections at short distances that require the introduction of either perturbative treatments or smoothing parameters. We examine the corresponding singular structures in the effective potentials of the relativistic Schroedinger equation obtained from the Pauli reduction of the TBDE. We find that the relativistic Schroedinger equation lead in fact to well-behaved wave function solutions when the full potential and couplings of the system are taken into account. The most unusual case is the coupled triplet system with S=1 and L={(J-1),(J+1)}. Without the inclusion of the tensor coupling, the effective S-state potential would become attractively singular. We show how including the tensor coupling is essential in order that the wave functions be well-behaved at short distances. For example, the S-state wave function becomes simply proportional to the D-state wave function and dips sharply to zero at the origin, unlike the usual S-state wave functions. Furthermore, this behavior is similar in both QED and QCD, independent of the asymptotic freedom behavior of the assumed QCD vector potential. Light- and heavy-quark meson states can be described well by using a simplified linear-plus-Coulomb-type QCD potential apportioned appropriately between world scalar and vector potentials. We use this potential to exhibit explicitly the origin of the large pi-rho splitting and effective chiral symmetry breaking. The TBDE formalism developed here may be used to study quarkonia in quark-gluon plasma environments.

## Key claim

The relativistic Schrödinger equation obtained from the Pauli reduction of the Two Body Dirac Equations (TBDE) yields well-behaved wave function solutions at short distances when the full potential and couplings are taken into account, even though the effective potentials appear singular in a perturbative or weak-potential treatment. The most unusual case is the coupled triplet system with S=1 and L={(J-1),(J+1)}, where inclusion of the tensor coupling is essential to obtain physically acceptable wave functions.

## Method

The authors performed a Pauli reduction of the Two Body Dirac Equations of constraint dynamics to obtain an effective relativistic Schrödinger equation for two interacting spin-one-half particles. They examined the short-distance behavior of the resulting quasipotentials (including central, Darwin, spin-orbit, spin-spin, and tensor terms) for Coulomb-type vector potentials in both QED and QCD contexts. For the QCD case, they introduced a simplified linear-plus-Coulomb-type potential with asymptotic freedom, replacing Richardson's potential with a modified form V(r) = (8πΛ²r)/27 - (16π)/(27r ln(e² + 1/(Λr)²)).

## Key numbers

- For the ground state of the Dirac equation in a Coulomb potential, the exact eigenvalue is E = m√(1-α²) = m(1 - α²/2 - α⁴/8 + O(α⁶)), matching the perturbative result through order α⁴
- For the equal-mass singlet states in QED with A = -α/r, the exact eigenvalue for the ground state is w = 2m - mα²/4 - 21mα⁴/64 + O(α⁶)
- The small-r behavior of the radial wave function for the singlet states is v₀ ~ r^(½√(1-4α²)+½), giving ψ₀ = v₀/r ~ r^(½√(1-4α²)-½)
- For the Dirac equation ground state, the small-r behavior of the radial part is ψ ~ x^(√(1-α²)-1/2), which dips toward the origin

## Context

This work extends the TBDE formalism previously applied to QED and QCD bound states by Crater, Van Alstine, and collaborators (1990s), and addresses singularity problems that arise in other approaches such as the Breit equation. The authors show that the TBDE avoids the need for perturbative treatments or smoothing parameters that are required in other relativistic approaches. The simplified QCD potential introduced here, based on the Richardson potential (1979) and the Cornell potential (1978), is intended for future application to quarkonium bound states in quark-gluon plasma environments.
