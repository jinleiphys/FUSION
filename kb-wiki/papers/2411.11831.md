# Let there be neutrons! Hadronic photoproduction from a large flux of high-energy photons

> We propose that neutrons may be generated in high-energy, high-flux photon environments via photo-induced reactions on pre-existing baryons. These photohadronic interactions are expected to occur in astrophysical jets and surrounding material. Historically, these reactions have been attributed to the production of high-energy cosmic rays and neutrinos. We estimate the photoproduction off of protons in the context of gamma-ray bursts, where it is expected there will be sufficient baryonic material that may be encompassing or entrained in the jet. We show that typical stellar baryonic material, even material completely devoid of neutrons, can become inundated with neutrons in situ via hadronic photoproduction. Consequently, this mechanism provides a means for collapsars and other astrophysical sites containing substantial flux of high-energy photons to be favorable for neutron-capture nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim
The authors propose that neutrons can be generated in situ via hadronic photoproduction in high-energy, high-flux photon environments such as gamma-ray burst jets, even from baryonic material initially devoid of neutrons. The mechanism is conditional on the presence of sufficient baryonic material entrained in or surrounding the jet, and the authors estimate that this process could make collapsars and similar sites favorable for neutron-capture nucleosynthesis where they may have previously been ruled out.

## Method
The authors model the photon flux in the gamma-ray burst jet head region using a piecewise power-law spectrum (Band function) and estimate the physical flux at the source by scaling observed fluxes to the jet interaction radius. They compute hadronic photoproduction cross sections for single-pion processes (gamma + p -> pi+ + n, gamma + n -> pi- + p, and scattering channels) using the ANL-Osaka model, which integrates effective field theories with coupled-channel approaches and describes approximately 50,000 data points. The neutron production rate is calculated by convolving the photon flux with the cross section and proton number density, and the authors assess the role of inverse meson collision processes through mean free path and decay length estimates.

## Key numbers
- The photon flux at the peak of the spectrum at the jet head is estimated as A = 10^37 ph/cm^2/s/keV, with flux at 10^6 keV on the order of Phi_gamma ~ 10^33 ph/cm^2/s/keV, assuming a typical GRB distance of d_l ~ 10^28 cm and jet interaction radius of r_jh ~ 10^10 cm.
- All hadronic photoproduction cross sections peak around E_gamma = 300 MeV due to the Delta(1232) resonance; higher energy photons with E_gamma > 700 MeV favor neutron production, and all cross sections tend toward zero above E_gamma ~ 1800 MeV.
- For inverse pion processes, the number of interactions is estimated as n^pi_ints ~ 3.56 for a 20 MeV pion at baryonic density rho_b ~ 1 g/cm^3, with mean free path lambda ~ 8.3 x 10^2 cm and propagation distance d ~ 298.7 cm.
- The inverse Compton photon flux from the cocoon region is estimated as ~10^22 ph/cm^2/s/keV at 10^6 keV at a radius of r_IC ~ 10^13 cm.
- The photon flux through previously ejected shells is estimated as A ~ 10^29 ph/cm^2/s/keV at the peak (~500 keV), with flux at 10^6 keV roughly Phi_gamma ~ 4 x 10^21 ph/cm^2/s/keV for a typical prompt emission power-law index of beta = -2.

## Context
This work extends the study of photohadronic interactions, traditionally associated with high-energy cosmic rays and neutrinos (Berezinskii 1975, Mannheim 1995, Waxman 1998, Murase 2013), to the production of neutrons for nucleosynthesis in gamma-ray burst environments. The authors build on the collapsar model for long GRBs (Woosley 1993, MacFadyen and Woosley 1998) and the Blandford-Znajek process for jet launching (Blandford and Znajek 1977). The paper enables future studies of neutron-capture nucleosynthesis in GRB cocoon and shell regions, which have been relatively unexplored compared to neutron star merger sites (Lattimer 1974, Freiburghaus 1999, Rosswog 2018) and accretion disks around black holes (Fujimoto 2007, Siegel 2019, Anand 2024).
