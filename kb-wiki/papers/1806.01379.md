# Beam Spin Asymmetry in Electroproduction of Pseudoscalar or Scalar Meson Production off the Scalar Target

> We discuss the electroproduction of pseudoscalar ($0^{-+}$) or scalar ($0^{++}$) meson production off the scalar target. The most general formulation of the differential cross section for the $0^{-+}$ or $0^{++}$ meson production process involves only one or two hadronic form factors, respectively, on a scalar target. The Rosenbluth-type separation of the differential cross section provides the explicit relation between the hadronic form factors and the different parts of the differential cross section in a completely model-independent manner. The absence of the beam spin asymmetry for the pseudoscalar meson production provides a benchmark for the experimental data analysis. The measurement of the beam spin asymmetry for the scalar meson production may also provide a unique opportunity not only to explore the imaginary part of the hadronic amplitude in the general formulation but also to examine the significance of the chiral-odd generalized parton distribution (GPD) contribution in the leading-twist GPD formulation.

## Key claim

The beam spin asymmetry (BSA) for pseudoscalar meson electroproduction off a scalar target is identically zero due to the symmetric hadronic tensor arising from a single hadronic form factor, while the BSA for scalar meson electroproduction is generally non-zero because two independent hadronic form factors allow an antisymmetric component; this non-zero BSA, if observed experimentally, would reveal the significance of the chiral-odd generalized parton distribution (GPD) contribution relative to the chiral-even GPD contribution in the leading-twist GPD formulation.

## Method

The authors construct the most general hadronic currents for pseudoscalar (0⁻⁺) and scalar (0⁺⁺) meson electroproduction off a spinless target using a "DNA" method based on the backbone of the Compton tensor, yielding one form factor for the pseudoscalar case and two form factors for the scalar case. They perform a Rosenbluth-type separation of the 5-fold differential cross section in the target rest frame, expressing each term (transverse, longitudinal, longitudinal-transverse interference, and beam spin asymmetry) in terms of the hadronic form factors. The results are compared with the leading-twist GPD formulation, where helicity non-flip (chiral-even) and helicity flip (chiral-odd) amplitudes are evaluated at the quark level to determine their relative scaling with Q.

## Key numbers

- For pseudoscalar meson production, the differential cross section takes the form dσ^PS = dσ^PS_T (1 - ε cos2φ), where ε is the polarization parameter; the BSA is identically zero.
- For scalar meson production, the BSA term dσ^S_BSA is proportional to F₁F₂* - F₂F₁* and is given explicitly by S_A = -κ e⁴ [sinθ sinφ / (2 M x² y)] (m² + Q² - t) √[Q²(y-1) + M² x² y²] √[x²(t² - 4m²M²) + Q⁴ + 2Q² t x].
- In the quark-level helicity amplitude analysis, the leading-order helicity non-flip (chiral-even) amplitude scales as J^S_{↑,↑,-1} ~ Q² Δ_⊥, while the leading-order helicity flip (chiral-odd) amplitude scales as J^S_{↑,↓,0} ~ Q Δ_⊥, indicating a nominal suppression of the chiral-odd contribution by a factor of 1/Q relative to the chiral-even contribution.

## Context

This work extends the general formalism for virtual Compton scattering off scalar targets developed by Metz and by Ji and collaborators (2017) to the case of meson electroproduction, providing a model-independent framework for extracting hadronic form factors from experimental data. The predicted vanishing BSA for pseudoscalar meson production (e.g., π⁰) off a scalar target (e.g., ⁴He) serves as a benchmark for experimental data analysis, while the non-vanishing BSA for scalar meson production (e.g., f₀(980)) offers a unique test of the chiral-odd GPD contribution, complementing recent BSA measurements in DVCS off ⁴He by Hattawy et al. (2017). The results enable direct extraction of both real and imaginary parts of the scalar meson form factors F₁ and F₂ from future experiments at facilities such as the JLab 12 GeV upgrade.
