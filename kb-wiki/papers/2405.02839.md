# Bayesian uncertainty quantification for synthesizing superheavy elements

> To improve the theoretical prediction power for synthesizing superheavy elements beyond Og, a Bayesian uncertainty quantification method is employed to evaluate the uncertainty of the calculated evaporation residue cross sections (ERCS) for the first time. The key parameters of the dinuclear system (DNS) model, such as the diffusion parameter $\textit{a}$, the damping factor $E_\mathrm{d}$, and the level-density parameter ratio $a_\mathrm{f}/a_\mathrm{n}$ are systematically constrained by the Bayesian analysis of recent ERCS data. One intriguing behavior is shown that the optimal incident energies (OIE) corresponding to the largest ERCS weakly depend on the fission process. We also find that these parameters are strongly correlated and the uncertainty propagation considering the parameters independently is not reasonable. The 2$σ$ confidence level of posterior distributions for $a = 0.586_{-0.002}^{+0.002}$ fm, $E_\mathrm{d} = 25.65_{-3.41}^{+3.43}$ MeV, and $a_\mathrm{f}/a_\mathrm{n} = 1.081_{-0.021}^{+0.021}$ are obtained. Furthermore, the confidence levels of the ERCS and OIE for synthesizing Z = 119 via the reactions($^{54}\mathrm{Cr}+^{243}\mathrm{Am}$), (${}^{50}\mathrm{Ti}+{}^{249}\mathrm{Bk}$), and (${}^{51}\mathrm{V}+{}^{248}\mathrm{Cm}$) are predicted. This work sets the stage for future analyses to explore the OIE and reaction systems for the synthesis of superheavy elements.

# Bayesian uncertainty quantification for synthesizing superheavy elements

## Key claim

The optimal incident energies (OIE) for synthesizing superheavy elements via heavy-ion fusion-evaporation reactions depend weakly on the fission process, being instead closely associated with capture and fusion processes; this finding is conditional on the Bayesian analysis of the dinuclear system (DNS) model parameters constrained by experimental data from Dubna for reactions with calcium-48 beams.

## Method

The authors employed Bayesian inference with Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling using the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm, combined with a Gaussian process emulator and principal component analysis as a surrogate for the computationally expensive DNS-sysu model. They constrained three key parameters, the diffuseness parameter *a*, the damping factor *E*_d, and the level-density parameter ratio *a*_f/*a*_n, by fitting calculated evaporation residue cross sections (ERCS) and optimal excitation energies to experimental data for the reactions calcium-48 with americium-243, plutonium-242, and uranium-238. The posterior distributions were then used to predict ERCS and OIE for three candidate reactions to synthesize element Z = 119.

## Key numbers

- Diffusion parameter *a* = 0.586 fm with 2σ confidence interval 0.585 to 0.589 fm
- Damping factor *E*_d = 25.65 MeV with 2σ confidence interval 22.24 to 29.08 MeV
- Level-density parameter ratio *a*_f/*a*_n = 1.081 with 2σ confidence interval 1.059 to 1.102
- For the reaction titanium-50 + berkelium-249, the OIE is predicted to be 223.95 MeV, with the corresponding ERCS in the range 0.958 to 2.76 pb at the 2σ confidence level
- For the reaction chromium-54 + americium-243, the OIE confidence interval is 238.1 to 240.2 MeV
- For the reaction vanadium-51 + curium-248, the OIE confidence interval is 227.1 to 229.3 MeV
- The Pearson correlation coefficient between *E*_d and *a*_f/*a*_n is 0.97

## Context

This work extends the dinuclear system model (DNS-sysu) approach previously applied by Zhu (2021) and others to superheavy element synthesis, by introducing Bayesian uncertainty quantification for the first time in this context. It directly addresses the failure of the GSI experiment by Khuyagbaatar (2020) to observe element Z = 119, suggesting the incident energy used (around 234.4 MeV center-of-mass) was much higher than the optimal value of 223.95 MeV. The strong correlation found between *E*_d and *a*_f/*a*_n contradicts the common practice of treating these parameters independently in uncertainty propagation, as done in earlier work by Ignatyuk (1975, 1979) and Adamian (2000). The results enable more reliable predictions of optimal beam energies and cross sections for future experiments hunting elements beyond oganesson.
