# The extremes of neutron richness

> A neutron star is pictured as a gigantic nucleus overwhelmed by the number of neutrons, unlike real atomic nuclei, that have a similar number of neutrons and protons. Is this true? What if we could find, or create nuclei without protons? How far can we go in neutron richness? Our common sense tells us that these neutral nuclei should not exist, but if they do they would change our knowledge on neutron stars, on the properties of nuclei in general, and ultimately on the nucleon-nucleon interaction itself, the building block of matter. This huge potential impact has pushed some ambitious nuclear physicists to search for them since the 1960s. The first positive hints appeared only in the XXI century, and nowadays several collaborations are trying to corner these weird objects and give a definite answer to this crucial question. In this \edit{review} we will go through this fascinating quest, that started with humble experiments and has now reached a stage of ambitious and sophisticated projects, both in experiment and theory.

# The extremes of neutron richness

## Key claim

This review examines the experimental quest for bound or resonant multineutron systems (nuclei with Z=0), concluding that while no definitive observation has been made, the possibility of a tetraneutron state near threshold remains open because the standard liquid drop model overestimates the asymmetry term for very neutron-rich light nuclei, and modified versions leave room for a bound or near-threshold resonant state.

## Method

The paper categorizes and reviews all experimental searches for multineutrons since the 1960s into three methodological categories: two-step activation reactions (where a bound multineutron produced in a first reaction is used to transform a sample nucleus), missing-mass measurements using pion double charge exchange reactions on 3,4He targets, and missing-mass measurements using transfer reactions between stable nuclei. For each category, the author describes the experimental principle, the specific reactions employed, and the results obtained.

## Key numbers

- The standard liquid drop model predicts multineutrons should be unbound by about 15 MeV/neutron, with the tetraneutron in particular unbound by about 70 MeV
- The 4n separation energy S4n(8He) = 3.1 MeV has been the upper limit of tetraneutron binding energy for decades; recent mass measurements have decreased S4n(19B) to 1.5(4) MeV
- 5H is unbound but has a positive binding energy of about 6 MeV (the 4n+p system is 6 MeV lighter than free five nucleons)
- The triton has a binding energy of 7.7 MeV
- In atomic physics analog studies, the critical number of 3He atoms needed to form a bound system is N approximately 30
- Neutron detection efficiencies for x neutrons decrease roughly as (epsilon_1n)^x, with typical single-neutron efficiencies of 10-50%
- The 1977 Detraz experiment used a 24 GeV proton beam on tungsten; the 1980 de Boer experiment used 3He ions on tellurium
- The 1965 Gilly experiment and subsequent pion experiments through 1999 found no peaks in the pi+ spectrum from 3,4He(pi-,pi+)3,4n reactions

## Context

This review extends the long-standing search for multineutron systems that began with experiments by Schiffer (1963) and Cierjacks (1965), and it contextualizes the recent positive hints from Marques (2002) and Kisamori (2016) within the broader history of null results. The paper connects the multineutron question to neutron star modeling, as discussed in 4n_stars_USAL_EJPA155 (2019), and to the fundamental understanding of the nucleon-nucleon interaction. The review enables future experimental and theoretical work by providing a comprehensive catalog of past approaches and their limitations, particularly noting that modern sophisticated projects are now underway in both experiment and theory.
