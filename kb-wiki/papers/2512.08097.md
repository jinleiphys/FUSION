# Wavefunction-Based Emulation of Coupled-Channels Scattering with Non-Affinely Parametrized Interactions

> Physics based emulators offer a fast and reliable replacement for an exact solution of the scattering problem in nuclear physics. Previous work developed a reduced-basis emulator for single-channel elastic scattering using an optical potential. Since many reactions of interest can be cast as a coupled-channel problem, the purpose of this work is to extend the RBM to a coupled-channel framework (CC-RBM). Although the framework derived is general, in this work we apply it to reactions where the Hamiltonian coupling term comes from assuming a rotational structure model for the target. From a set of training coupled-channel wavefunctions, we perform a singular value decomposition to obtain a reduced set of basis wavefunctions, and then solve the extended (Petrov-)Galerkin equations. In addition, the empirical interpolation method is used to expand the potentials. We apply the CC-RBM method to elastic and inelastic scattering of neutrons on 48Ca including a quadrupole coupling to populate the first 2+ state, and neutrons on 208Pb, including an octupole coupling to populate its first 3- state. We demonstrate that the CC-RBM calculated cross sections match those obtained using traditional finite-difference methods. We show that the CC-RBM results can reliably reproduce the nuclear scattering cross sections at different energy regimes. The computational accuracy versus time plots demonstrate that the CC-RBM method efficiently increases precision with increasing basis size. Most importantly, for the precisions required in reaction calculations (a percent on the cross section), we find the CC-RBM method offers roughly one and a half orders of magnitude gain in computational speed compared to the traditional coupled-channels solver. However, we also discuss how this scaling becomes less favorable, the larger the number of channels included in the coupled-channel set.

## Key claim

The paper extends the reduced basis method (RBM) from single-channel elastic scattering to a coupled-channel framework (CC-RBM) for nuclear reactions, demonstrating that the emulator achieves roughly one and a half orders of magnitude gain in computational speed compared to traditional coupled-channels solvers when percent-level precision on cross sections is required, though this scaling becomes less favorable as the number of channels increases.

## Method

The authors generalize the reduced basis method to coupled-channel equations (CC-RBM) by performing a singular value decomposition on a set of training coupled-channel wavefunctions to obtain a reduced set of basis wavefunctions, then solving the extended Petrov-Galerkin equations in that basis. The empirical interpolation method is used to expand the potentials, enabling treatment of non-affinely parametrized interactions such as Woods-Saxon potentials. The method is applied to neutron-nucleus inelastic scattering using a collective rotational model for target excitation, with calculations performed for neutrons on 48Ca (quadrupole coupling to the first 2+ state) and neutrons on 208Pb (octupole coupling to the first 3- state).

## Key numbers

- The CC-RBM method offers roughly one and a half orders of magnitude gain in computational speed compared to the traditional coupled-channels solver for the precisions required in reaction calculations (a percent on the cross section)
- The emulator was benchmarked against coupled-channel calculations obtained using traditional finite-difference methods (such as Numerov) for the 48Ca(n,n')48Ca(2+) system at Elab = 12 MeV and the 208Pb(n,n')208Pb(3-) system
- The first four PCA components were used to construct the reduced basis for the two coupled channels in the 48Ca(n,n')48Ca(2+) system at Elab = 12 MeV and Jπ = 0+

## Context

This work extends the Reduced Order Scattering Emulator (ROSE) framework developed by Odell (2024), which focused on single-channel elastic scattering using an optical potential, to the more general coupled-channel framework needed for inelastic scattering and other reactions. The CC-RBM method enables efficient Bayesian uncertainty quantification studies that require many repeated solutions of the coupled-channel equations, and represents the first step toward developing emulators for more complex reaction types including transfer, breakup, and fusion processes.
