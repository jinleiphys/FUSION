# Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-BCS approach to $^{229m}$Th and neighboring nuclei

> The microscopic origin of the $^{229m}$Th isomer and its possible manifestation also in neighboring nuclei is explored within a selfconsistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer approach. By using the well established SIII Skyrme parametrization, without any special adjustments related to low-energy isomer, the single-particle spectrum provided by the model reproduces the correct isomer $K^π=3/2^{+}$ spin and parity, and the relative proximity of the isomeric state to the $K^π=5/2^{+}$ ground state, yet on the keV scale. We show that this approach may provide microscopic estimates for some related observables, such as the quadrupole and octupole moments, deformation parameters as well as magnetic dipole moments. Its ability to provide a prediction for the M1 isomer transition probability is discussed. The obtained $^{229}$Th single-particle structure is compared with that provided by calculations in neighbouring actinide isotopes and isotones, allowing us to assess the more general role of the considered mechanism for the formation of low-energy isomers.

## Key claim

The Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-BCS approach with the SIII parametrization, without any special adjustments for the low-energy isomer, reproduces the correct spin and parity of the $^{229m}$Th isomer ($K^\pi = 3/2^+$) and its proximity to the $5/2^+$ ground state on the keV scale, but only when reflection symmetry is imposed; including octupole deformation pushes the $3/2^+$ state significantly higher, showing that the isomer formation mechanism depends critically on the treatment of reflection asymmetry.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-BCS calculations with time-odd terms and seniority pairing correlations, using the SIII Skyrme parametrization, for the even-even core $^{228}$Th and the odd-mass nucleus $^{229}$Th. They separately blocked the $5/2^+$, $3/2^+$, $5/2^-$, and $7/2^-$ neutron orbitals near the Fermi level, both with imposed reflection symmetry (axial quadrupole deformation only) and without reflection symmetry (allowing axial quadrupole-octupole deformation), and extended the calculations to neighboring isotopes $^{227}$Th and $^{231}$Th and isotones $^{227}$Ra and $^{231}$U.

## Key numbers

- In the reflection-symmetric calculation for $^{229}$Th, the $3/2^+$ isomeric state lies 17.1 keV above the $5/2^+$ ground state, with a magnetic moment of $-0.3128\,\mu_N$ for the isomer and $0.7366\,\mu_N$ for the ground state.
- In the reflection-symmetric calculation for the $^{228}$Th core, the $3/2^+$ and $5/2^+$ neutron orbitals are nearly degenerate, with the $5/2^+$ orbital only 11 keV above the $3/2^+$ Fermi level.
- In the reflection-asymmetric (octupole-deformed) calculation for $^{228}$Th, the $3/2^{\langle+\rangle}$ orbital appears about 360 keV above the $5/2^{\langle+\rangle}$ level, a much larger separation than the 11 keV in the symmetric case.
- The pairing strengths fitted to the experimental $2^+_1$ energy of $^{228}$Th (57.8 keV) gave $G_n = 16.0$ MeV and $G_p = 14.4$ MeV for the reflection-symmetric solution, yielding a theoretical $E^*(2^+_1) = 58.4$ keV; a re-adjustment for the reflection-asymmetric solution gave $G_n = 16.5$ MeV and $G_p = 14.9$ MeV, yielding $E^*(2^+_1) = 58.0$ keV.
- The reflection-asymmetric solution for $^{228}$Th has a total energy 0.5 MeV lower than the reflection-symmetric solution, with an octupole deformation $\beta_3^m \approx 0.12$–$0.13$.

## Context

This work extends the earlier CQOM-DSM model approach by Minkov and Pálffy (2017, 2019, 2021), which explained the $^{229m}$Th isomer as a quasi-parity-doublet bandhead, by testing the isomer formation mechanism at the deeper microscopic mean-field level using a self-consistent Skyrme energy-density functional. It contrasts with the HFB calculations of Litvinova (2009) by including time-reversal symmetry breaking via selfconsistent blocking and by considering reflection-asymmetric (octupole) deformation, which the authors find crucial for understanding the isomer properties. The results enable future microscopic predictions for isomer-related observables in $^{229}$Th and neighboring actinide nuclei, and provide a framework to assess how the intrinsic conditions for low-energy isomers evolve with changing nuclear structure.
