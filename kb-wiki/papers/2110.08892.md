# Hybrid stars with color superconducting cores in an extended FCM model

> We investigate the influence of repulsive vector interactions and color superconductivity on the structure of neutron stars using an extended version of the field correlator method (FCM) for the description of quark matter. The hybrid equation of state is constructed using the Maxwell description, which assumes a sharp hadron-quark phase transition. The equation of state of hadronic matter is computed for a density-dependent relativistic lagrangian treated in the mean-field approximation, with parameters given by the SW4L nuclear model. This model described the interactions among baryons in terms of $σ, ω, ρ, σ^*$, and $ϕ$ mesons. Quark matter is assumed to be in either the CFL or the 2SC+s color superconducting phase. The possibility of sequential (hadron-quark, quark-quark) transitions in ultra-dense matter is investigated. Observed data related to massive pulsars, gravitational-wave events, and NICER are used to constrain the parameters of the extended FCM model. The successful equations of state are used to explore the mass-radius relationship, radii, and tidal deformabilities of hybrid stars. A special focus lies on investigating consequences that slow or fast conversions of quark-hadron matter have on the stability and the mass-radius relationship of hybrid stars. We find that if slow conversion should occur, a new branch of stable massive stars would exist whose members have radii that are up to 1.5~km smaller than those of conventional neutron stars of the same mass. Such objects could be possible candidates for the stellar high-mass object of the GW190425 binary system.

## Key claim

If the conversion of hadronic matter to quark matter at the phase boundary is slow (nucleation timescale much larger than the fluid oscillation timescale), a new branch of stable hybrid stars exists whose radii are up to 1.5 km smaller than those of conventional neutron stars of the same mass, and such objects could be candidates for the high-mass component of the GW190425 binary merger.

## Method

The authors construct hybrid star equations of state using the Maxwell construction (sharp hadron-quark phase transition) with hadronic matter described by the SW4L density-dependent relativistic mean-field model including the full baryon octet and Delta resonances, and quark matter described by an extended field correlator method (FCM) model that includes repulsive vector interactions and either 2SC+s or CFL color superconductivity. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to compute mass-radius relations and tidal deformabilities, and apply constraints from massive pulsars (PSR J1614-2230, PSR J0348+0432, PSR J0740+6620), the gravitational-wave event GW170817, and NICER observations to restrict the FCM parameter space (V1, G2, Kv, Delta). The stability of hybrid stars is analyzed under both rapid and slow conversion scenarios at the hadron-quark interface.

## Key numbers

- Radii of hybrid stars in the slow-conversion branch are up to 1.5 km smaller than conventional neutron stars of the same mass
- The hadron-quark transition pressure must be larger than about 200 MeV fm^-3 to satisfy the 2 solar mass constraint
- The critical surface tension for a Maxwell (sharp) phase transition is sigma_HQ ~ 70 MeV fm^-2
- The FCM model parameters are fixed at V1 = 20 MeV and G2 = 0.009 GeV^4, following Mariani (2016)
- The SW4L hadronic model gives nuclear saturation density n0 = 0.15 fm^-3, binding energy E0 = -16.0 MeV, incompressibility K0 = 250 MeV, symmetry energy J0 = 30.3 MeV, and symmetry energy slope L0 = 46.5 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous FCM-based hybrid star studies by Mariani (2016), Plumari (2013), Logoteta (2013), and Burgio (2016) by including both vector interactions and color superconductivity (2SC+s and CFL phases) in the quark matter description, and by analyzing the slow versus rapid conversion stability criterion introduced by Pereira (2018). The results enable future observational discrimination between rapid and slow conversion scenarios using mass-radius measurements from NICER and gravitational-wave events, and suggest that the high-mass object in GW190425 could be a hybrid star with a color superconducting core rather than a conventional neutron star.
