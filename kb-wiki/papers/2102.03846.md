# Isochronic evolution and the radioactive decay of r-process nuclei

> We report on the creation and application of a novel decay network that uses the latest data from experiment and evaluation. We use the network to simulate the late-time phase of the rapid neutron capture (r) process. In this epoch, the bulk of nuclear reactions, such as radiative capture, have ceased and nuclear decays are the dominant transmutation channels. We find that the decay from short-lived to long-lived species naturally leads to an isochronic evolution in which nuclei with similar half-lives are populated at the same time. We consider random perturbations along each isobaric chain to initial solar-like r-process compositions to demonstrate the isochronic nature of the late-time phase of the r-process. Our analysis shows that detailed knowledge of the final isotopic composition allows for the prediction of late-time evolution with a high degree of confidence despite uncertainties that exist in astrophysical conditions and the nuclear physics properties of the most neutron-rich nuclei. We provide the time-dependent nuclear composition in the Appendix as supplemental material.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that after about one minute of evolution, the radioactive decay of r-process nuclei becomes "isochronic": the abundance pattern converges to a unique solution regardless of the initial distribution of abundance along each isobaric chain, provided the final isotopic abundances are known and all initial nuclei have half-lives shorter than one second.

## Method

The authors constructed a novel decay network solver called Jade, which uses a matrix exponential method with adaptive timestep refinement based on the matrix 1-norm of the decay rate matrix. They simulated one million randomly perturbed initial compositions along the neutron dripline for mass numbers 69 ≤ A ≤ 204, constrained to reproduce the solar r-process residuals, and evolved them through the Jade solver over timesteps spanning the first 10 years of radioactive decay.

## Key numbers

- At 1 minute into the evolution, the most abundant components have a typical relative uncertainty δY < 10%
- By 1 hour, all abundances across one million samples agree to within δY < 2.5%, with most abundances agreeing to well within δY ≪ 1%
- By 15 minutes into the decay, all one million r-process composition samples agree to within 1%
- By 1 day, all abundances agree to within one part in ten thousand
- The maximum timestep constraint uses C ≈ 100 in the relation Δt · ‖Λ‖₁ ≤ C
- The benchmark test shows agreement between Jade and exact Bateman Equations across timescales from 10⁻³ s to 10⁶ s and abundances from 10⁻¹⁴ to 1

## Context

This work extends sensitivity studies of r-process nucleosynthesis (Mumpower 2014, Surman 2015) by focusing on the late-time decay phase after neutron capture ceases, where nuclear properties are better constrained experimentally. The isochronic evolution concept enables reliable prediction of late-time observables such as kilonova light curves (associated with GW170817/AT2017gfo) without requiring detailed knowledge of the most neutron-rich nuclei, complementing direct nucleosynthesis modeling approaches (Lippuner 2017, Zhu 2018). The authors provide abundance tables for timesteps from 15 minutes to 10 years as supplemental material for use in future kilonova and r-process studies.
