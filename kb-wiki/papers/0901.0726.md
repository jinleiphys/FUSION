# Momentum Kick Model Analysis of PHENIX Near-Side Ridge Data and Photon Jet

> We analyze PHENIX near-side ridge data for central Au+Au collisions at \sqrt{s_{NN}}=200 GeV with the momentum kick model, in which a near-side jet emerges near the surface, kicks medium partons, loses energy, and fragments into the trigger particle and fragmentation products. The kicked medium partons subsequently materialize as the observed ridge particles, which carry direct information on the early parton momentum distribution and the magnitude of the momentum kick. We find that the PHENIX ridge data can be described well by the momentum kick model and the extracted early partons momentum distribution has a thermal-like transverse distribution and a rapidity plateau structure. We also find that the parton-parton scattering between the jet parton and the medium parton involves the exchange of a non-perturbative pomeron, for jet partons in momentum range considered in the near-side ridge measurements.

# Momentum Kick Model Analysis of PHENIX Near-Side Ridge Data and Photon Jet

## Key claim

The PHENIX near-side ridge data for central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV can be described well by the momentum kick model, but only after accounting for the interplay between jet and ridge components in the small Delta-eta region (|eta^trig, eta^assoc|<0.35), where the jet component dominates at Delta-eta~0 while the ridge component dominates for |Delta-eta|>0.7. The extracted momentum kick magnitude q_L=0.8 GeV per jet-(medium parton) collision is consistent with the exchange of a non-perturbative pomeron rather than a perturbative QCD scattering process.

## Method

The momentum kick model was applied to PHENIX near-side ridge data for central (0-20%) Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, using a parametrized jet component from pp collisions (Eq. jetfun) with an attenuation factor f_J that varies with p_t^assoc (0.632 for p_t^assoc<2 GeV, 0.82 for 2-3 GeV, 1.0 for >3 GeV). The ridge component was calculated from the normalized initial medium parton momentum distribution (Eq. dis2) with parameters a=0.5, T=0.50 GeV, and m_d=1 GeV, where medium partons receive a longitudinal momentum kick q_L along the jet direction and subsequently materialize via parton-hadron duality. The total yield per trigger was obtained as the sum of jet and ridge components, with acceptance corrections applied for the PHENIX pseudorapidity acceptance |eta|<0.35.

## Key numbers

- Momentum kick magnitude: q_L = 0.8 GeV per jet-(medium parton) collision, extracted from PHENIX ridge data
- Number of attenuated kicked partons: f_R <N_k> = 3.0 for central (0-20%) Au+Au collisions
- Medium parton distribution temperature: T = 0.50 GeV
- Medium parton distribution fall-off parameter: a = 0.5
- Medium parton distribution mass parameter: m_d = 1 GeV
- Jet survival factor f_J: 0.632 for p_t^assoc < 2 GeV, 0.82 for 2 < p_t^assoc < 3 GeV, 1.0 for p_t^assoc > 3 GeV
- pp jet component parameters for PHENIX: N_jet = 0.15 + 0.10 * <p_t^trig>/GeV, T_jet = 0.19 GeV + 0.06 * <p_t^trig>, sigma_phi0 = 0.50, m_a = 1.1 GeV
- pp jet component parameters for STAR (4-6 GeV trigger): N_jet = 0.75, T_jet = 0.55 GeV, sigma_phi0 = 0.50, m_a = 1.1 GeV

## Context

This work extends the momentum kick model previously applied to STAR near-side ridge data (Wong 2007, 2008) and successfully predicted PHOBOS data at large rapidities (Wong 2008), now testing it against PHENIX data covering many p_t^trig x p_t^assoc combinations in a small pseudorapidity region. The extracted momentum kick magnitude q_L=0.8 GeV is compared with non-perturbative pomeron exchange models (Landshoff 1988, Nachtmann 1991, Dosch 1987-2001) and found consistent with parton-parton scattering involving a non-perturbative pomeron rather than perturbative QCD. The analysis enables future discrimination between ridge models by comparing ridge yields for hadron triggers versus photon jet triggers: the momentum kick model predicts greatly reduced ridge yield for photon jets, while the position-flow model (Shuryak 2007, Voloshin 2005) and back-splash models predict the ridge structure would remain.
