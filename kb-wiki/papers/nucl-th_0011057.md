# Charge symmetry breaking of the nucleon-nucleon interaction: $ρ$-$ω$ mixing VERSUS nucleon mass splitting

> We investigate three models for the charge symmetry breaking (CSB) of the nucleon-nucleon ($NN$) interaction (based upon $ρ$-$ω$ mixing, nucleon mass splitting, and phenomenology) that all reproduce the empirical value for the CSB of the $^1S_0$ scattering length ($Δa_{CSB}$) accurately. We reveal that these models make very different predictions for CSB in $^3P_J$ waves and examine the impact of this on some observable quantities of $A\geq 3$ nuclear systems. It turns out that the $^3$H-$^3$He binding energy difference is essentially ruled by $Δa_{CSB}$ and not very sensitive to CSB from $P$ waves. However, the Coulomb displacement energies (which are the subject of the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly) receive about 50% of their CSB contribution from $NN$ partial waves beyond $^1S_0$. Consequently, the predictions by the various CSB models differ here substantially (10-20%). Unfortunately, the evaluation of the leading Coulomb contributions carry a large uncertainty such that no discrimination between the competing CSB models can presently be made. To decide the issue we suggest to look into nuclear few-body reactions that are sensitive to CSB of the nuclear force.

## Key claim

The paper shows that three different models for charge symmetry breaking (CSB) of the nucleon-nucleon interaction, based on ρ-ω mixing, nucleon mass splitting, and phenomenology, all reproduce the empirical CSB of the ¹S₀ scattering length equally well, but make drastically different predictions for CSB in ³P_J waves. However, the authors cannot discriminate between these models using existing data because the ³H-³He binding energy difference is insensitive to P-wave CSB, while the Coulomb displacement energies (relevant to the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly) carry large uncertainties from leading Coulomb contributions that prevent definitive conclusions.

## Method

The authors constructed three CSB models: a ρ-ω mixing potential added to the CD-Bonn pp potential, a nucleon mass splitting model based on the Bonn meson-exchange framework, and the phenomenological Argonne V₁₈ potential. They calculated CSB phase shift differences for all three models in various partial waves, then performed momentum-space Faddeev calculations for the three-nucleon system and Brueckner-Hartree-Fock plus Green's function calculations for nuclear matter and ¹⁶O to evaluate binding energy differences and Coulomb displacement energies.

## Key numbers

- Empirical CSB of the ¹S₀ scattering length: Δa_CSB = 1.6 ± 0.6 fm (from a_pp^N = -17.3 ± 0.4 fm and a_nn^N = -18.9 ± 0.4 fm)
- ρ-ω mixing matrix element used: ⟨ρ⁰|H_m|ω⟩ = -0.00402 GeV² (consistent with empirical -0.00452 ± 0.0006 GeV²)
- Both the ρ-ω and nucleon mass splitting models produce Δa_CSB = 1.508 fm; Argonne V₁₈ gives Δa_CSB = 1.654 fm
- ³H-³He binding energy difference from CSB: 60-66 keV across models (empirical residual is 67 ± 9 keV)
- CSB contribution from partial waves beyond ¹S₀ is only 2-5 keV (about 6%) for the ³H-³He difference
- In nuclear matter at k_F = 1.35 fm⁻¹, about 50% of the CSB energy difference comes from L>0 partial waves
- ρ-ω model produces about 20% more CSB energy difference in nuclear matter than the nucleon mass splitting model
- Static Coulomb energy for ³H-³He: 648 ± 4 keV

## Context

This work extends the long-standing debate on the origin of charge symmetry breaking in nuclear forces, following Coon and Barrett (1987) who showed ρ-ω mixing could explain the CSB scattering length, and later work by Goldman, Henderson, and Thomas (1992) questioning the off-shell behavior of ρ-ω mixing. The nucleon mass splitting model was developed by Li and Machleidt (1998). The paper directly addresses the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly (Nolen and Schiffer, 1969) and finds that while CSB models differ substantially in their predictions for Coulomb displacement energies (10-20%), the large uncertainties in Coulomb contributions prevent discrimination. The authors suggest that future few-body nuclear reactions sensitive to CSB in P-waves could resolve which mechanism nature uses.
