# Monopole Excitation and Nuclear Compressibility: Present and Future Perspectives

> Isoscalar giant resonances are nuclear collective excitations associated with the oscillation in phase of protons and neutrons according to a certain multipolarity $L$. In particular, the isoscalar giant monopole resonance ($L=0$) is the strongest nuclear compression mode, and its excitation energy is directly related to the compression modulus for finite nuclei. Typically, microscopic calculations are utilized to establish a relationship between the experimental compression modulus and the nuclear incompressibility that is a crucial parameter of the equation of state for nuclear matter. The incompressibility of nuclear matter has been determined with an accuracy of 10 to 20\% using relativistic and non-relativistic microscopic models for describing the monopole distributions in ${}^{208}$Pb and ${}^{90}$Zr isotopes. However, the same theoretical models are not able to describe data for open-shell nuclei, such as those of tin and cadmium isotopes. In fact, only effective interactions with a softer nuclear-matter incompressibility are able to predict the centroid energy of monopole distributions for open-shell nuclei. An unified description of the monopole resonance in ${}^{208}$Pb and other open-shell nuclei remains unsolved from the theory side. Most of this uncertainty is due to our poor knowledge of the symmetry energy, which is another essential component of the equation of state of nuclear matter. Therefore, new experimental data along isotopic chains covering a wide range in $N/Z$ ratios, including neutron-deficient and neutron-rich nuclei, are of paramount importance for determining both the nuclear-matter incompressibility and the symmetry energy more precisely.

## Key claim

The nuclear-matter incompressibility has been determined with 10 to 20% accuracy using relativistic and non-relativistic models for closed-shell nuclei like 208Pb and 90Zr, but these same models fail to describe open-shell nuclei such as tin and cadmium isotopes, where only effective interactions with a softer nuclear-matter incompressibility can predict the centroid energy of monopole distributions.

## Method

The authors review experimental and theoretical studies of the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR) using inelastic alpha-particle scattering at forward angles, where multipole-decomposition analysis (MDA) with distorted-wave Born approximation (DWBA) calculations extracts the monopole strength distributions. Microscopic random-phase approximation (RPA) and quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) calculations based on Skyrme, Gogny, and relativistic mean-field models are used to relate the experimental compression modulus of finite nuclei to the nuclear-matter incompressibility. The review also covers recent approaches incorporating pairing correlations through Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) methods and particle-vibration coupling (PVC) effects to address discrepancies for open-shell nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear-matter incompressibility accepted value: K_nm = 240 ± 20 MeV, derived from consensus between relativistic and non-relativistic models describing 208Pb and 90Zr
- Non-relativistic models predict K_nm = 220–235 MeV; relativistic models predict K_nm = 250–270 MeV
- K_tau extracted from cadmium isotopes: −555 ± 75 MeV; from tin isotopes: −550 ± 100 MeV; from heavy-ion reactions: −370 ± 120 MeV
- K_Coul constrained to 5.2(7) MeV
- Pairing correlations reduce ISGMR centroid energies by several hundreds of keV in open-shell nuclei
- Particle-vibration coupling can shift ISGMR centroids downward by up to 1–2 MeV in open-shell nuclei
- ISGMR centroid energy scales approximately as 80 A^(−1/3) MeV for medium-heavy nuclei

## Context

This review extends the long-standing work on isoscalar giant resonances from facilities such as RCNP, Texas A&M University, and iThemba LABS, and addresses the unresolved "softness" problem where open-shell nuclei like tin and cadmium isotopes show lower incompressibility than closed-shell nuclei. The paper discusses how the puzzle relates to the symmetry energy parameters (slope L, curvature K_sym) and recent neutron-skin measurements in 208Pb from PhysRevLett.126.172502 and PhysRevLett.126.172503. Future experiments using inverse kinematics with active targets and storage rings at radioactive beam facilities will enable measurements along isotopic chains with wide N/Z ratios, including neutron-deficient and neutron-rich nuclei, to better constrain both the nuclear-matter incompressibility and symmetry energy.
