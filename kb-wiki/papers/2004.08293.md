# Quarkyonic Matter Equation of State in Beta-Equilibrium

> Quark matter may appear due to a hadronic-quark transition in the core of a hybrid star. Quarkyonic matter is an approach in which both quarks and nucleons appear as quasi-particles in a crossover transition, and provides an explicit realization of early ideas concerning quark matter (e.g., the MIT bag model). This description has recently been employed by McLerran and Reddy to model chargeless (pure neutron) matter with an approach that has the virtue that the speed of sound rises quickly at a neutron-quark transition so as to satisfy observational constraints on the neutron star maximum mass ($\gtrsim2M_\odot$) and the radius of a $1.4M_\odot$ star ($R_{1.4}\lesssim 13.5$ km). Traditional models involving first-order transitions result in softer pressure-energy density relations that have difficulty satisfying these constraints except with very narrow choices of parameters. We propose a variation of quarkyonic matter involving protons and leptons whose energy can be explicitly minimized to achieve both chemical and beta equilibrium, which cannot be done in the chargeless formulation. Quarkyonic stellar models are able to satisfy observed mass and radius constraints with a wide range of model parameters, avoiding the obligatory fine-tuning of conventional hybrid star models, including requiring the transition density to be very close to the nuclear saturation density. Our formulation fits experimental and theoretical properties of the nuclear symmetry energy and pure neutron matter, and contains as few as three free parameters. This makes it an ideal tool for the study of high-density matter that is an efficient alternative to piecewise polytrope or spectral decomposition methods.

## Key claim

The authors propose a variation of quarkyonic matter that includes protons and leptons, enabling explicit energy minimization to achieve both chemical and beta equilibrium, which the earlier chargeless formulation by McLerran and Reddy could not satisfy. This model satisfies observational constraints on neutron star maximum mass and radius with a wide range of model parameters, avoiding the fine-tuning required by conventional hybrid star models.

## Method

The authors construct a quarkyonic matter model where nucleons are restricted to momentum shells near the Fermi surface above a transition density n_t, while d and u quarks fill the lowest momentum states as non-interacting fermions. They replace the earlier assumption of fixed momentum relations between nucleons and quarks with conditions of chemical equilibrium (mu_d = 2/3 mu_n - 1/3 mu_p, mu_u = 2/3 mu_p - 1/3 mu_n) and beta equilibrium (mu_e = mu_mu = mu_n - mu_p = mu_d - mu_u), using a nucleon potential with six sub-parameters fit to symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter properties, with the symmetry energy slope L as the only free nucleonic parameter.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density n_s = 0.16 fm^-3
- Bulk binding energy B = 16 MeV for symmetric nuclear matter
- Incompressibility parameter K_1/2 = 220 MeV at n_s
- Symmetry energy S_v = 31 MeV
- Symmetry energy slope L in range 30 MeV to 70 MeV
- Quark masses determined at transition: m_d = 241.07 MeV, m_u = 391.28 MeV for the standard case with L=50 MeV, Lambda=1400 MeV, n_t=0.3 fm^-3
- Maximum neutron star mass constraint: M_max >= 2.14 M_sun from PSR J0740+6620
- Radius constraint: R_1.4 <= 13.5 km from GW170817 and NICER observations
- Transition density n_t explored in range 0.3 to 0.5 fm^-3
- Lambda parameter explored in range 800 to 1700 MeV

## Context

This work extends the quarkyonic matter approach of McLerran and Reddy (2019) by incorporating protons, leptons, and the conditions of chemical and beta equilibrium, which the chargeless 2-flavor formulation could not satisfy. The model provides a physically-motivated alternative to piecewise polytropes (Read 2009), spectral decomposition (Lindblom 2010), and constant-sound-speed parameterizations, enabling systematic study of high-density matter in neutron stars while satisfying constraints from GW170817, NICER observations of PSR J0030+0451, and nuclear symmetry energy measurements.
