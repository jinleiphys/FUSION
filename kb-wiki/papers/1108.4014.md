# Recent Progress in Applying Gauge/Gravity Duality to Quark-Gluon Plasma Physics

> We give a brief overview of the basic philosophy behind applying gauge/gravity duality to problems in heavy ion physics. Recent progress regarding viscosities, anomalous hydrodynamics, energy loss, as well as thermalization will be reviewed.

## Key claim

Gauge/gravity duality provides solvable toy models for strong-coupling dynamics that can give qualitative insights into quark-gluon plasma behavior, but these models are not QCD itself and should be expected to have systematic errors of order 100% since there is no small expansion parameter that corrects for calculating with the wrong theory.

## Method

The paper reviews holographic calculations of hydrodynamic transport coefficients (shear viscosity, second-order coefficients, and anomalous transport), heavy quark and light quark energy loss via string dynamics in a five-dimensional black hole background, and thermalization studied through numerical simulations of colliding shockwaves in the holographic dual. For energy loss, thousands of string configurations falling into the black hole were numerically simulated, and analytic approximations were obtained in the high-energy limit.

## Key numbers

- The KSS value for shear viscosity to entropy density ratio: η/s = ħ/(4π) ≈ 0.08 (dimensionless), which is smaller than water (η/(ħ s) ≈ 30) and superfluid helium (≈ 10)
- A counterexample to the viscosity bound: in an N=2 supersymmetric gauge theory with Sp(N) gauge group, η/s = ħ/(4π) × (1 - 1/(2N) + O(1/N²))
- Heavy quark momentum loss rate: dp/dt = -μ p with μ = (π/2)√λ T²/m, inversely proportional to quark mass
- Maximal stopping distance for light quarks scales as E^(1/3), while the most likely distance scales as E^(1/4); at weak coupling the scaling is E^(1/2)
- Thermalization time from shockwave collisions: about 4/μ from when shocks start to overlap; for RHIC parameters (μ ≈ 2.3 GeV) this gives about 0.35 fm/c
- Pitch viscosity: 2.3 × 10¹¹ cP; quark-gluon liquid viscosity: about 10¹⁴ cP

## Context

This review extends the earlier work of Policastro, Son, and Starinets (2001) on holographic viscosity calculations and the KSS bound proposal by Kovtun, Son, and Starinets (2004). It discusses counterexamples by Kats and Petrov (2007) and others showing the bound can be violated by higher curvature corrections. The anomalous hydrodynamics section builds on holographic calculations by Erdmenger et al. (2008) and Banerjee et al. (2008) that identified the chiral vortical effect, later proven generally by Son and Surowka (2009). The thermalization results from Chesler and Yaffe (2010) provide the first holographic simulation of colliding shockwaves relevant to heavy-ion collisions. The work enables experimental tests of scaling laws for energy loss at RHIC and LHC, and provides benchmark values for second-order hydrodynamic coefficients used in numerical simulations of the fireball evolution.
