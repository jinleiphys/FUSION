# R-Process Nucleosynthesis In Neutrino-Driven Winds From A Typical Neutron Star With M = 1.4 Msun

> We study the effects of the outer boundary conditions in neutrino-driven winds on the r-process nucleosynthesis. We perform numerical simulations of hydrodynamics of neutrino-driven winds and nuclear reaction network calculations of the r-process. As an outer boundary condition of hydrodynamic calculations, we set a pressure upon the outermost layer of the wind, which is approaching toward the shock wall. Varying the boundary pressure, we obtain various asymptotic thermal temperature of expanding material in the neutrino-driven winds for resulting nucleosynthesis. We find that the asymptotic temperature slightly lower than those used in the previous studies of the neutrino-driven winds can lead to a successful r-process abundance pattern, which is in a reasonable agreement with the solar system r-process abundance pattern even for the typical proto-neutron star mass Mns ~ 1.4 Msun. A slightly lower asymptotic temperature reduces the charged particle reaction rates and the resulting amount of seed elements and lead to a high neutron-to-seed ratio for successful r-process. This is a new idea which is different from the previous models of neutrino-driven winds from very massive (Mns ~ 2.0 Msun) and compact (Rns ~ 10 km) neutron star to get a short expansion time and a high entropy for a successful r-process abundance pattern. Although such a large mass is sometimes criticized from observational facts on a neutron star mass, we dissolve this criticism by reconsidering the boundary condition of the wind. We also explore the relation between the boundary condition and neutron star mass, which is related to the progenitor mass, for successful r-process.

## Key claim

A successful r-process abundance pattern, in reasonable agreement with the solar system r-process pattern, can be obtained from neutrino-driven winds of a typical proto-neutron star with mass 1.4 solar masses, provided the outer boundary pressure is chosen sufficiently low (10^20 dyn/cm^2) to produce an asymptotic temperature around T9 = 0.4, which is slightly lower than the 0.1 MeV (T9 ~ 1.3) used in previous studies.

## Method

The authors performed one-dimensional, general relativistic, spherically symmetric hydrodynamic simulations of neutrino-driven winds from a proto-neutron star with mass 1.4 solar masses and radius 10 km, using an implicit Lagrangian code with neutrino heating and cooling. They varied the outer boundary pressure (10^20, 10^21, 10^22 dyn/cm^2) as a parameter, extracted a single Lagrangian trajectory from each simulation, and then computed the r-process nucleosynthesis using a nuclear reaction network of over 3000 species connected by more than 10,000 reactions.

## Key numbers

- Outer boundary pressure P_out = 10^20 dyn/cm^2 produced an asymptotic temperature T_out = 0.4 x 10^9 K, a dynamical timescale tau_dyn = 2.32 x 10^-2 s, entropy S = 200 k_B, initial electron fraction Y_e,i = 0.43, final alpha abundance Y_alpha,out = 0.196, and final seed abundance Y_seed,out = 9.2 x 10^-4.
- Outer boundary pressure P_out = 10^21 dyn/cm^2 produced T_out = 0.7 x 10^9 K, tau_dyn = 2.54 x 10^-2 s, S = 180 k_B, Y_e,i = 0.43, Y_alpha,out = 0.189, and Y_seed,out = 2.0 x 10^-3.
- Outer boundary pressure P_out = 10^22 dyn/cm^2 produced T_out = 1.3 x 10^9 K, tau_dyn = 3.34 x 10^-2 s, S = 170 k_B, Y_e,i = 0.44, Y_alpha,out = 0.174, and Y_seed,out = 2.4 x 10^-3.
- The model with P_out = 10^20 dyn/cm^2 reproduced the three characteristic r-process abundance peaks at mass numbers A ~ 80, 130, and 195.
- Neutrino luminosities were set to 1 x 10^51 erg/s per species, with total luminosity 6 x 10^51 erg/s; average neutrino energies were 10 MeV (electron-type), 20 MeV (electron-type anti-neutrinos), and 30 MeV (muon- and tau-neutrinos).

## Context

This work extends the neutrino-driven wind r-process scenario by showing that a successful r-process does not require a very massive (2.0 solar masses) and compact (10 km) neutron star as in Otsuki et al. (2000) and Sumiyoshi et al. (2000), but can occur for a typical 1.4 solar mass neutron star if the outer boundary temperature is lower than the 0.1 MeV value adopted from Woosley et al. (1994). The result enables future studies to constrain the progenitor mass range and supernova explosion dynamics by linking the outer boundary pressure to the envelope mass and shock propagation, and it resolves the criticism that the required neutron star masses were inconsistent with observational mass measurements.
