# In-Medium Modifications of Scalar Charm Mesons in Nuclear Matter

> Employing QCD sum rules the in-medium modifications of scalar charm mesons in a cold nuclear matter environment are estimated. The mass splitting of D*-D*-bar is quantified.

## Key claim

Using QCD sum rules, the authors estimate that scalar charm mesons (D* and anti-D*) experience a mass splitting in cold nuclear matter, but the absolute values of the masses remain uncertain due to the strong dependence of the mean mass on the chosen continuum thresholds.

## Method

The authors employ Borel-transformed QCD sum rules for the current-current correlator of scalar open charm mesons, expanding the operator product expansion (OPE) to include non-perturbative condensates. They perform a low-density expansion in nuclear matter density n, deriving the leading-order coefficient for the mass splitting, and also go beyond the linear density approximation by including quadratic terms and allowing the continuum thresholds to vary with density.

## Key numbers

* Charm quark mass set to m_c = 1.3 GeV.
* The linear density coefficient for the mass splitting, alpha_Delta_m, is evaluated for Borel masses around M = 1.37 GeV (the minimum of the vacuum Borel curve for the scalar D meson).
* The threshold splitting parameter alpha_Delta_omega is estimated to be in the range 10^2 to 10^3 GeV^{-1}, with a linear interpolation from vacuum to n = 0.01 fm^{-3} giving alpha_Delta_omega approximately 7 x 10^2 GeV^{-1}.
* The mean threshold value in vacuum is taken as omega_0^2(0) = 7.5 GeV^2, with variations considered from 6.0 GeV^2 to 9.0 GeV^2.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous QCD sum rule study of pseudoscalar open charm mesons (Hilger, 2009) to the lowest scalar excitations (D* mesons). It is motivated by the FAIR project, where the CBM and PANDA collaborations plan to investigate charm mesons in nuclear matter. The results show a robust pattern of mass splitting for scalar D*-anti-D* mesons that resembles the pseudoscalar case, but the mean mass determination remains uncertain, requiring more model-independent studies for future precision measurements at FAIR.
