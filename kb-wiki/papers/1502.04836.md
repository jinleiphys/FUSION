# Gamow-Teller response and its spreading mechanism in doubly magic nuclei

> The scope of the paper is to apply a state-of-the-art beyond mean-field model to the description of the Gamow-Teller response in atomic nuclei. This topic recently attracted considerable renewed interest, due, in particular, to the possibility of performing experiments in unstable nuclei. We study the cases of $^{48}$Ca, $^{78}$Ni, $^{132}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb. Our model is based on a fully self-consistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus random phase approximation. The same Skyrme interaction is used to calculate the coupling between particles and vibrations, which leads to the mixing of the Gamow-Teller resonance with a set of doorway states and to its fragmentation. We compare our results with available experimental data. The microscopic coupling mechanism is also discussed in some detail.

## Key claim

The particle-vibration coupling (PVC) model, applied on top of a fully self-consistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus random phase approximation (RPA), can reproduce the line shape and spreading width of the Gamow-Teller resonance (GTR) in doubly magic nuclei, but the agreement with experiment depends on the chosen Skyrme interaction and the nucleus; the model systematically shifts the RPA peak energy downward by about 1.2 MeV and produces a full width at half maximum (FWHM) of approximately 3.5 MeV in 208Pb, though the total calculated strength integrated up to 25 MeV in 208Pb is about 29% larger than the experimental value.

## Method

The authors performed a fully self-consistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock (HF) calculation of the ground state, followed by a charge-exchange RPA to obtain the Gamow-Teller (GT) strength and the properties of collective phonons (natural parity states up to 6+). They then introduced particle-vibration coupling (PVC) at lowest order, using the same Skyrme interaction to compute the coupling vertices and the energy-dependent self-energy that mixes the GT resonance with doorway states (a particle-hole pair coupled to a phonon). The resulting complex matrix was diagonalized to obtain the fragmented GT strength distribution, with an averaging parameter Δ (typically 200 keV or 1 MeV) to simulate experimental resolution.

## Key numbers

- In 208Pb, the RPA+PVC calculation with the SGII interaction and Δ=1 MeV gives a GTR peak energy of 19.6 MeV and a FWHM of 3.6 MeV, compared to the experimental peak at 19.2 MeV and width of 5.0 MeV.
- In 208Pb, the RPA+PVC calculation with Δ=0.2 MeV splits the main peak into two subpeaks at 19.2 MeV (FWHM 1.2 MeV) and 20.6 MeV (FWHM 2.0 MeV).
- In 48Ca, the RPA+PVC calculation with Δ=0.2 MeV reproduces the lowest experimental peak at 2.8 MeV with a FWHM of 0.4 MeV (experiment: 3 MeV, 0.4 MeV), and gives a main peak centroid at 10.5 MeV with FWHM 1.2 MeV (experiment: two peaks at 8.2 and 10.8 MeV with widths 1.5 and 3.9 MeV).
- In 208Pb, the total GT- strength ∑B(GT-) from RPA is 132.95, and the RPA+PVC calculation exhausts 95.2% of the Ikeda sum rule up to 60 MeV (97.3% with Δ=0.2 MeV); the experimental strength integrated up to 25 MeV is about 79, corresponding to 71% of the RPA+PVC result.
- In 132Sn, the RPA+PVC calculation with Δ=1 MeV gives a GTR peak at 14.8 MeV with FWHM 3.6 MeV; with Δ=0.2 MeV the main peak fragments into three subpeaks at 13.8, 15.0, and 15.8 MeV.
- In 78Ni, the RPA+PVC calculation with Δ=1 MeV gives a GTR peak at 10.5 MeV with FWHM 5.6 MeV; with Δ=0.2 MeV a two-peak structure appears at 8.8 MeV (FWHM 1.8 MeV) and 11.6 MeV (FWHM 1.4 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent Skyrme HF+RPA+PVC approach previously developed by the same group (Niu 2012) to a systematic study of the GT response in four doubly magic nuclei (48Ca, 78Ni, 132Sn, 208Pb). It is similar in spirit to the relativistic time blocking approximation (RTBA) calculations of Litvinova (2014) for 208Pb, and it builds on earlier PVC treatments of giant resonances (Bertsch 1983, Colo 1994). The results enable predictions for the GT strength distribution in exotic nuclei like 78Ni and 132Sn, which are relevant for beta-decay half-lives in the r-process and for ongoing or planned charge-exchange experiments at radioactive beam facilities.
