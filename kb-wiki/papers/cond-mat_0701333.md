# Rapidly rotating boson molecules with long or short range repulsion: an exact diagonalization study

> The Hamiltonian for a small number, N <= 11, of bosons in a rapidly rotating harmonic trap, interacting via a short range (contact potential) or a long range (Coulomb) interaction, is studied via an exact diagonalization in the lowest Landau level. Our analysis shows that, for both low and high fractional fillings, the bosons localize and form rotating boson molecules (RBMs) consisting of concentric polygonal rings. Focusing on systems with the number of trapped atoms sufficiently large to form multi-ring bosonic molecules, we find that, as a function of the rotational frequency and regardless of the type of repulsive interaction, the ground-state angular momenta grow in specific steps that coincide with the number of localized bosons on each concentric ring. Comparison of the conditional probability distributions (CPDs) for both interactions suggests that the degree of crystalline correlations appears to depend more on the fractional filling nu than on the range of the interaction. The RBMs behave as nonrigid rotors, i.e., the concentric rings rotate independently of each other. At filling fractions nu < 1/2, we observe well developed crystallinity in the CPDs (two-point correlation functions). For larger filling fractions nu > 1/2, observation of similar molecular patterns requires consideration of even higher-order correlation functions.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for small numbers of bosons (N ≤ 11) in a rapidly rotating harmonic trap, the bosons form rotating boson molecules (RBMs) with concentric polygonal ring structures for both short-range (delta) and long-range (Coulomb) repulsive interactions, but the degree of crystalline correlations depends more on the fractional filling fraction ν than on the interaction range, and for high fillings (ν ≥ 1/2) the molecular patterns are only visible in higher-order N-point correlation functions rather than in the two-point conditional probability distributions.

## Method

The authors performed exact diagonalization of the many-body Hamiltonian restricted to the lowest Landau level (LLL) for N = 6, 9, and 11 bosons in a rotating harmonic trap, using permanents constructed from single-particle LLL wave functions as the basis. They computed ground-state angular momenta as a function of rotational frequency Ω for both Coulomb and delta-function repulsive interactions, and probed crystalline structure by calculating conditional probability distributions (CPDs) and N-point correlation functions from the exact wavefunctions.

## Key numbers

- N ≤ 11 bosons studied in the lowest Landau level
- For N=6 bosons, ground-state angular momenta L_gs increase in steps of 5 or 6, corresponding to (1,5) and (0,6) polygonal ring arrangements
- The first bosonic Laughlin state occurs at L = N(N-1), which for N=6 is L=30 and for N=9 is L=72
- For N=6 with delta interaction, the ground-state angular momenta terminate at L=30 (the Laughlin value) at Ω/ω_⊥ = 1
- For N=6 with Coulomb interaction, ground-state angular momenta diverge as Ω/ω_⊥ → 1, allowing values larger than L=30
- For N=9 bosons with Coulomb interaction at L_gs=93, the RBM structure corresponds to a (2,7) polygonal-ring arrangement
- Crystalline features are well developed for filling fractions ν < 1/2 for all sizes studied

## Context

This work extends the concept of rotating electron molecules (REMs) or Wigner molecules, previously established for electrons in 2D quantum dots under high magnetic fields by Yannouleas, Landman, and collaborators (2000-2006), to bosonic systems in rotating traps. It builds on earlier exact diagonalization studies of rapidly rotating bosons by Wilkin, Viefers, and Regnault (2000-2005) that did not recognize molecular formation, and on variational RBM wavefunction work by Romanovsky, Yannouleas, and Landman (2006). The findings enable future experimental investigation of strongly correlated bosonic states in rotating traps and optical lattices, particularly for systems with long-range dipole-dipole interactions, and suggest that higher-order correlation measurements (such as Hanbury Brown-Twiss or shot-noise interferometry) are necessary to detect crystalline order at high filling fractions.
