# Structure properties of ${}^{226}$Th and ${}^{256,258,260}$Fm fission fragments: mean field analysis with the Gogny force

> The constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method is used with the Gogny interaction D1S to calculate potential energy surfaces of fissioning nuclei ${}^{226}$Th and ${}^{256,258,260}$Fm up to very large deformations. The constraints employed are the mass quadrupole and octupole moments. In this subspace of collective coordinates, many scission configurations are identified ranging from symmetric to highly asymmetric fragmentations. Corresponding fragment properties at scission are derived yielding fragment deformations, deformation energies, energy partitioning, neutron binding energies at scission, neutron multiplicities, charge polarization and total fragment kinetic energies.

## Key claim

The constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method with the Gogny D1S interaction can identify scission configurations and derive fragment properties for ${}^{226}$Th and ${}^{256,258,260}$Fm, but the authors caution that the fragment properties derived "will not necessarily all correspond to those observed in experiments, since some of the configurations found at scission may not occur with significant probability in the fission process."

## Method

The constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) method was used with the Gogny D1S interaction to calculate potential energy surfaces of fissioning nuclei as functions of axial mass quadrupole ($q_{20}$) and octupole ($q_{30}$) moments. Scission points were identified using a density criterion: a post-scission configuration is one where the minimal density along the symmetry axis falls below 0.06 fm${}^{-3}$. For each scission point, a sharp cut at the neck position defined the light and heavy fragments, from which fragment deformations, energies, neutron binding energies, neutron multiplicities, charge polarization, and total kinetic energies were calculated.

## Key numbers

- Scission lines were mapped with mesh sizes $\Delta q_{20}=2$ b and $\Delta q_{30}=1$ b${}^{3/2}$ near scission, using approximately two to three hundred scission points per nucleus
- For ${}^{226}$Th, symmetric scission occurs at $q_{2s}=500$ b; for Fm isotopes, symmetric scission configurations are found at $q_{2s}=270$ b
- The difference in energy between symmetric ($A_{\text{frag}}=A/2$) and asymmetric ($A_{\text{frag}}\simeq 145$) fragmentation along the scission line decreases from 22 MeV to 16 MeV as the Fm isotope mass increases from $A=256$ to $A=260$
- Fragment deformation energies reach maxima of $E_{\text{def}}\sim 15-20$ MeV near $A_{\text{frag}}\sim 120$ and minima close to zero near $A_{\text{frag}}\sim 145$ and $A_{\text{frag}}\sim 130$
- The difference between light and heavy fragment deformation energies ranges from 23 MeV to -15 MeV
- One-neutron binding energies of nascent fragments globally decrease from approximately 7 MeV to 3-4 MeV with increasing fragment mass
- For ${}^{256}$Fm, the calculated number of emitted neutrons is 30% smaller than experimental data from Gindler (1979)

## Context

This work extends prior mean-field studies of fission using Gogny and Skyrme forces, particularly those on bimodal fission in ${}^{256-258}$Fm isotopes by Warda (2002-2004), Bonneau (2005-2006), and Staszczak (2005), and the time-dependent calculations for ${}^{238}$U by Goutte (2005). The calculated fragment properties are intended to serve as inputs for phenomenological evaporation models aimed at calculating prompt neutron emission and gamma-ray decay of fission fragments, as developed by Lemaire (2005-2006) and Kornilov (2007). The authors note that fragment yields are not addressed as they require a dynamical treatment.
