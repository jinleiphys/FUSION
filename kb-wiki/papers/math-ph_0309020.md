# On the Quantum Density of States and Partitioning an Integer

> This paper exploits the connection between the quantum many-particle density of states and the partitioning of an integer in number theory. For $N$ bosons in a one dimensional harmonic oscillator potential, it is well known that the asymptotic (N -> infinity) density of states is identical to the Hardy-Ramanujan formula for the partitions p(n), of a number n into a sum of integers. We show that the same statistical mechanics technique for the density of states of bosons in a power-law spectrum yields the partitioning formula for p^s(n), the latter being the number of partitions of n into a sum of s-th powers of a set of integers. By making an appropriate modification of the statistical technique, we are also able to obtain d^s(n) for distinct partitions. We find that the distinct square partitions d^2(n) show pronounced oscillations as a function of n about the smooth curve derived by us. The origin of these oscillations from the quantum point of view is discussed. After deriving the Erdos-Lehner formula for restricted partitions for the $s=1$ case by our method, we generalize it to obtain a new formula for distinct restricted partitions.

# On the Quantum Density of States and Partitioning an Integer

## Key claim
The paper shows that the statistical mechanics technique for computing the many-particle density of states of bosons in a power-law spectrum yields the Hardy-Ramanujan formula for partitions of an integer into sums of s-th powers, but this identification holds only asymptotically (N → ∞) and the resulting smooth density of states does not capture the pronounced oscillations seen in distinct square partitions d²(n), which are linked to the oscillatory part of the single-particle density of states in a square-well potential.

## Method
The authors compute the N-particle density of states via inverse Laplace transform of the canonical partition function, using the saddle-point approximation to extract the smooth (average) part. For bosons with single-particle spectrum ε_m = m^s, the canonical partition function is the generating function for partitions p^s(n); for distinct partitions, they use a fermionic partition function with chemical potential μ=0, which generates d^s(n). Finite-N (restricted) partitions are treated by including corrections from the exact N-boson partition function for s=1, and for distinct restricted partitions they derive a new formula using a relation between d_N(n) and sums of p_i(n).

## Key numbers
- For s=1 (harmonic oscillator), the asymptotic bosonic density of states is ρ̄_∞(E) = exp[π√(2E/3)] / (4√3 E), which is the Hardy-Ramanujan formula for p(n).
- For s=2 (square-well spectrum), the asymptotic bosonic density of states is ρ̄_∞²(E) = √(2/3) κ₂ / (2π)^(3/2) × exp[3κ₂ E^(1/3)] / E^(7/6), where κ₂ = (C(2)/2)^(2/3) and C(2) = Γ(3/2) ζ(3/2).
- For distinct partitions with s=1, the asymptotic formula is ρ̄_∞(F)(E) = exp[π√(E/3)] / (4 × 3^(1/4) E^(3/4)).
- For distinct square partitions d²(n), the ratio of oscillation amplitude to the smooth average decreases from about 1.5 to 0.2 as n increases to 1000.
- The finite-N correction for restricted partitions (Erdos-Lehner formula) is ρ̄_N(E) = ρ̄_∞(E) × exp[-(√(6E)/π - 1/2) exp(-πN/√(6E))], valid for C(1) << E << C(1)N² where C(1) ≈ 1.645.

## Context
This work extends the well-known connection between the quantum density of states for bosons in a 1D harmonic trap and the Hardy-Ramanujan formula (Grossmann and Holthaus, 1995) to general power-law spectra (s-th powers). It also generalizes the Erdos-Lehner formula (Erdos and Lehner, 1941) for restricted partitions to obtain a new asymptotic formula for distinct restricted partitions. The oscillations in d²(n) are linked to the oscillatory part of the single-particle density of states in a square-well potential, analogous to trace formula approaches (Brack and Bhaduri, 1997). The results enable further study of the connection between number-theoretic partition functions and quantum many-body systems, particularly the role of single-particle level fluctuations and Pauli blocking in generating oscillatory behavior.
