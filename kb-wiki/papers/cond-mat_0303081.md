# Metastable states, anomalous distributions and correlations in the HMF model

> We study the microscopic dynamics of the metastable Quasi-Stationary States (QSS) in the Hamiltonian Mean Field (HMF) model, a Hamiltonian system of N classical inertial spins with infinite-range interactions which shows a second order phase transition. In order to understand the origin of metastability, which appears in an energy region below the critical point, we consider two different classes of out-of-equilibrium initial conditions, both leading to QSS, and having respectively initial magnetization equal to one (M1 IC) and equal to zero (M0 IC). We compare the corresponding $μ$-space, the resulting velocity pdfs and correlations, and the eventual aging features of the microscopic dynamics. In both cases the model exhibits non-gaussian pdfs, though anomalous correlations are present only when the system is started with an initial magnetization equal to one. In the M0 IC case the relaxation to equilibrium is almost exponential, while,for M1 IC, when correlations and aging are found, the decay is a power-law and the overall behavior can be very well reproduced by a Tsallis q-exponential function. These results contribute to clarify the overall scenario, which is more complex than previously expected and stress the importance of the dynamics in the relaxation process. The nonextensive statistical mechanics formalism proposed by Tsallis seems to be valid, in the out-of-equilibrium phase, when correlations and strong long-term memory effects emerge. This regime becomes stable if the $N\to \infty $ limit is performed before the $t\to \infty $ limit.

# Metastable states, anomalous distributions and correlations in the HMF model

## Key claim

The Hamiltonian Mean Field (HMF) model exhibits metastable Quasi-Stationary States (QSS) below the critical energy, but the microscopic dynamics and the applicability of Tsallis nonextensive statistical mechanics depend critically on the initial condition: only when the system starts with initial magnetization equal to one (M1 IC) do strong velocity correlations, aging, and q-exponential decay appear, while starting with zero magnetization (M0 IC) yields an almost exponential relaxation to equilibrium with no such anomalous features.

## Method

The authors performed numerical simulations of the HMF model, a system of N fully coupled classical inertial spins with Hamiltonian dynamics, using a fourth-order Yoshida symplectic integrator with energy conservation ΔE/E = 10⁻⁵. They prepared two classes of out-of-equilibrium initial conditions: M1 IC (all angles zero, velocities uniformly distributed) and M0 IC (both angles and velocities uniformly distributed), both with total momentum initially zero. For each initial condition, they computed the time evolution of temperature, magnetization, μ-space structures, velocity probability density functions (pdfs), two-time velocity autocorrelation functions, and aging properties for system sizes up to N=10000, averaging over ensembles of up to 1000 events.

## Key numbers

- Energy density studied: U = 0.69, which lies in the anomalous region between 1/2 and the critical point U_c = 3/4
- Critical temperature: T_c = 1/2; equilibrium temperature at U=0.69 is T_eq = 0.476
- For M1 IC, the plateau temperature T_QSS(N) converges to T_∞ = 0.38 as N → ∞
- For M1 IC, the velocity autocorrelation decay is fitted by a q-exponential with q = 1.55; for M0 IC, q = 1.12 (close to exponential, q=1)
- For M1 IC aging data, the scaling function C(t+t_w, t_w) = f(t / t_w^β) with β = 1/4 yields a power-law tail with exponent λ = 1.54, corresponding to a q-exponential with q = 1.65
- Equilibrium magnetization at U=0.69: M_eq = 0.307

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the HMF model by Latora, Rapisarda, and collaborators (2001-2002) that first identified QSS and their connection to Tsallis statistics, and directly addresses criticism raised by Zanette (2003) regarding the existence of a true plateau in the kinetic energy. The authors also compare with results by Yamaguchi (2003) on velocity autocorrelations and by Montemurro, Tamarit, and Anteneodo (2003) on aging in the HMF model. The findings clarify that Tsallis nonextensive formalism applies to Hamiltonian long-range systems only when long-range correlations and fractal structures in phase space are present, and that the QSS regime becomes stable only when the N → ∞ limit is taken before the t → ∞ limit.
