# FRIB and the GW170817 Kilonova

> In July 2018 an FRIB Theory Alliance program was held on the implications of GW170817 and its associated kilonova for r-process nucleosynthesis. Topics of discussion included the astrophysical and nuclear physics uncertainties in the interpretation of the GW170817 kilonova, what we can learn about the astrophysical site or sites of the r process from this event, and the advances in nuclear experiment and theory most crucial to pursue in light of the new data. Here we compile a selection of scientific contributions to the workshop, broadly representative of progress in r-process studies since the GW170817 event.

## Key claim

The GW170817 kilonova provides the first strong evidence for an astrophysical site of r-process element production and may suggest that the majority of the r-process material in the galaxy originates in neutron star mergers, but this claim requires careful examination because each piece of the chain of evidence, from merger simulations through nuclear network calculations to radiation transport, carries large uncertainties.

## Method

The paper compiles contributions from a July 2018 FRIB Theory Alliance workshop on the implications of GW170817 for r-process nucleosynthesis. Participants presented state-of-the-art numerical simulations of neutron star mergers and post-merger phases, nuclear reaction network calculations (using codes such as SkyNet and PRISM), and multicomponent kilonova models incorporating anisotropic ejecta, neutrino microphysics, and detailed opacities. The workshop also examined how the kilonova data fit with complementary r-process data from spectroscopic observations of low-metallicity stars and solar isotopic r-process residuals.

## Key numbers

- Total ejecta mass required to explain the GW170817 kilonova light curves: 0.03–0.06 solar masses, based on multicomponent anisotropic kilonova modeling by Perego et al. (2017).
- Effective gray opacities inferred for the two-component kilonova model: 1 cm² g⁻¹ (light r-process, blue component) and 10 cm² g⁻¹ (heavy r-process, red component), from Wollaeger et al. (2018).
- Post-merger accretion disks can evaporate 30–40% of their original disk mass into unbound outflows, as reported by Siegel et al. (2017) and Fernandez et al. (2018).
- The LIGO/Virgo 90% confidence upper limit on the binary tidal deformability parameter \(\tilde\Lambda\) is 800; Radice et al. (2017) used full-GR simulations to set a complementary lower bound on \(\tilde\Lambda\) from the requirement that sufficient ejecta survive to explain the kilonova.
- The relative inclination between the merger axis and the line of sight was constrained to 30 degrees, compatible with gravitational wave and gamma-ray burst afterglow measurements (Perego et al., 2017).
- The mean electron fraction of post-merger disk outflows is kept below a critical threshold of \(Y_\mathrm{e} \approx 0.25\) for lanthanide and actinide production (Siegel et al., 2017).

## Context

This workshop report extends the earlier ICNT/JINA-CEE program "The r process of nucleosynthesis: connecting FRIB with the cosmos" (Horowitz et al., 2018) by focusing specifically on the implications of the GW170817 event. The contributions address the major open questions in r-process astrophysics: identifying the astrophysical site(s) of r-process nucleosynthesis, quantifying nuclear physics uncertainties in the thousands of neutron-rich nuclei involved, and developing realistic kilonova models that can be tested against future multimessenger observations. The work enables more reliable interpretation of future kilonova detections and guides experimental priorities at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB).
