# A classical two-body Hamiltonian model and its mean field approximation

> We extend a recent billiard model of the nuclear N-body Hamiltonian to consider a finite two-body interaction. This permits a treatment of the Hamiltonian by a mean field theory, and also allows the possibility to model reactions between nuclei. The density and the mean field potential can be accurately described by a scaling function which shows the qualitative features of the liquid drop picture of the nucleus.

# Classical two-body Hamiltonian model and its mean field approximation

## Key claim
The paper extends a billiard model of the nuclear N-body Hamiltonian to include a finite two-body interaction, and shows that the resulting mean field theory reproduces the liquid drop picture of the nucleus. However, the model does not yield saturation of density or binding energy with increasing N due to the absence of a repulsive potential core, and the authors note that the fermionic nature of the nuclear many-body problem will make considerable differences from this classical model.

## Method
The authors define a classical N-body Hamiltonian with a finite square-well two-body interaction of depth V0 and range a, then derive self-consistent mean field equations using a canonical distribution function at temperature T. They solve these equations numerically by iteration, starting from a constant density inside a sphere of diameter a, and compare the mean field results with direct numerical integration of the N-body equations of motion for N=300 particles over roughly 10^5 interactions.

## Key numbers
- Surface thickness parameter epsilon/a = (3*gamma)^(-1/2), where gamma = N*V0/kT
- For a nucleus with mass A approximately 75, radius a/2 approximately 5 fm (from 1.2*A^(1/3)), surface thickness epsilon approximately 1 fm, yielding gamma approximately 30
- Mean field potential depth N*V0 approximately 50 MeV to correspond to a typical Woods-Saxon potential
- Temperature kT approximately 2 MeV for the above parameter values

## Context
This work extends the billiard model of Papenbrock (1999) by introducing a finite two-body interaction, enabling mean field treatment and modeling of nuclear reactions. The mean field results show qualitative features of the liquid drop model described by Bohr and Mottelson (1969), and the authors compare their mean field approximation with direct N-body simulations. The model enables future studies of nuclear reactions at moderate temperatures, though the authors caution that the fermionic nature of real nuclei will produce significant differences, particularly in chemical potentials and nucleon evaporation rates.
