# Determining impact parameters of heavy-ion collisions at low-intermediate incident energies using deep learning with convolutional neural network

> A deep learning based method with the convolutional neural network (CNN) algorithm for determining the impact parameters is developed using the constrained molecular dynamics model simulations, focusing on the heavy-ion collisions at the low-intermediate incident energies from several ten to one hundred MeV/nucleon in which the emissions of heavy fragments with the charge numbers larger than 3 become crucial. To make the CNN applicable in the task of the impact parameter determination at the present energy range, specific improvements are made in the input selection, the CNN construction and the CNN training. It is demonstrated from the comparisons of the deep CNN method and the conventional methods with the impact parameter-sensitive observables, that the deep CNN method shows better performance for determining the impact parameters, especially leading to the capability of providing better recognition of the central collision events. With a proper consideration of the experimental filter effect in both training and testing processes to keep consistency with the actual experiments, the good performance of the deep CNN method holds, and shows significantly better in terms of predicting the impact parameters and recognizing the central collision events, compared to that of the conventional methods, demonstrating the superiority of the present deep CNN method. The deep CNN method with the consideration of the filter effect is applied in the deduction of nuclear stopping power. Higher accuracy for the stopping power deduction is achieved benefitting from the better impact parameter determination using the deep CNN method, compared to using the the conventional methods. This result reveals the importance to select a reliable impact parameter determination method in the experimental deduction of the nuclear stopping power as well as other observables.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a deep convolutional neural network (CNN) can determine impact parameters of heavy-ion collisions at low-intermediate energies (50–100 MeV/nucleon) with significantly better accuracy than conventional methods, particularly for central collisions, provided that the input includes momentum distributions from both light (Z ≤ 2) and heavy (Z > 3) charged fragments and that the experimental filter effect is incorporated in training.

## Method

The constrained molecular dynamics (CoMD) model was used to simulate 300,000 events of ¹²⁴Sn+¹²⁴Sn collisions at 50, 70, and 100 MeV/nucleon with uniform impact parameter distribution (0–12 fm). Two-dimensional histograms of absolute transverse momentum per nucleon versus absolute longitudinal momentum per nucleon were constructed separately for light (Z ≤ 2) and heavy (Z > 2) charged particles, combined into a single 50×50 pixel input image per event, and used to train a CNN with two convolutional layers (32 kernels each, 5×5 size), max pooling, batch normalization, dropout regularization, and a fully-connected output layer. The CNN was trained using mean square error loss for 50 epochs, and its performance was tested on an independent set of 100,000 events with a natural b·db impact parameter distribution.

## Key numbers

- Mean absolute error Δb_CNN for impact parameter prediction: 0.44 fm at 50 MeV/nucleon, 0.40 fm at 70 MeV/nucleon, and 0.38 fm at 100 MeV/nucleon (from testing data sets of ¹²⁴Sn+¹²⁴Sn collisions).
- Validation mean square error MSE_v(50) for the selected CNN (2 layers × 32 kernels): 0.60 fm² at 50 MeV/nucleon, 0.43 fm² at 70 MeV/nucleon, and 0.32 fm² at 100 MeV/nucleon.
- Training used 240,000 events per energy (80% of 300,000 simulated events), with 60,000 validation events and 100,000 independent testing events.
- Maximum impact parameter b_max = 12 fm, calculated as 1.2 × (A_P^(1/3) + A_T^(1/3)) for ¹²⁴Sn+¹²⁴Sn.

## Context

This work extends the deep learning approach for impact parameter determination, previously applied at relativistic energies (around 1 GeV/nucleon) by Li et al. (2020, 2021) using CNN and LightGBM, down to the low-intermediate energy range (50–100 MeV/nucleon) where heavy fragment emission (Z > 3) becomes important. It addresses the known limitation identified by Bass et al. (1996) and Zhang et al. (2018) that conventional methods using observables like charged particle multiplicity lose accuracy for central collisions at these energies. The method enables more reliable extraction of nuclear stopping power and other observables that depend on accurate impact parameter determination in experiments at facilities such as the Radioactive Isotope Beam Factory.
