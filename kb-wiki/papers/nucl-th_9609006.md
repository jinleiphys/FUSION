# Can one extract source radii from transport theories?

> To know the space time evolution of a heavy ion reaction is of great interest, especially in cases where the measured spectra do not allow to ascertain the underlying reaction mechanism. In recent times it became popular to believe that the comparison of Hanbury-Brown Twiss correlation functions obtained from classical or semiclassical transport theories, like Boltzmann Uehling Uhlenbeck (BUU), Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD), VENUS, RQMD or ARC, with experiments may provide this insight. It is the purpose of this article to show that this conjecture encounters serious problems. The models which are suited to be compared with the experiments at CERN and Brookhaven are not able to predict a correlation function. Any agreement with existing data has to be considered as accidental. The models suited for lower energies can in principle predict correlation functions. The systematic error may be too large to be of use as far as quantitative conclusions are concerned.

## Key claim

The paper argues that extracting source radii from Hanbury-Brown Twiss (HBT) correlation functions using classical or semiclassical transport theories encounters serious systematic problems; for high-energy models (CERN and Brookhaven) any agreement with data is accidental, while for lower-energy models the systematic error may be too large for quantitative conclusions.

## Method

The author derives the formal relation between the two-body Wigner density and the HBT correlation function, then examines how three classes of transport models (cascade models, Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD), and one-body transport theories like BUU/VUU/LV) approximate or fail to provide the required two-body Wigner density. For each model class, the paper evaluates the validity of the "smoothness assumption" and the treatment of quantum mechanical (anti)symmetrization, using a simplified chaotic source to quantify systematic errors.

## Key numbers

- For a source at normal nuclear matter density, the width parameter A = A_M^(2/3) fm^2, giving A = 21.5 fm^2 for A_M = 100 and A = 34 fm^2 for A_M = 200.
- The momentum-space slope parameter B = 40 / T [MeV] fm^2 for a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.
- In standard QMD and IQMD, the wave function width parameter L has values 4.33 fm^2 and 8.66 fm^2, respectively.
- The ratio of extracted radii with and without the smoothness assumption, F = R / R^SA, ranges between 0.87 for small systems at low temperature (5 MeV) and 0.98 for large systems at high temperature (80 MeV).
- For particles emitted from a compound nucleus or spectator matter, the smoothness assumption produces an error in mass number determination of about 20%.
- For fireball nucleons, the smoothness assumption produces an error of about 4% on the density.

## Context

This work extends the critique of HBT methods in heavy-ion physics previously discussed by Zajc and Gyulassy, and specifically addresses the popular practice of using transport models (BUU, QMD, VENUS, RQMD, ARC) to extract source radii. The paper shows that cascade models (VENUS, RQMD, ARC) cannot predict any correlation function because they treat particles classically with sharp momenta, eliminating the quantum interference essential for HBT. For QMD and BUU-type models, the smoothness assumption introduces systematic errors that are comparable to or larger than the physical variations one seeks to measure, questioning the method's ability to discriminate between different reaction mechanisms.
