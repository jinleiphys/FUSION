# Channels of Stellar-mass Black Hole Formation

> On the basis of a large collection of detailed 3D core-collapse supernova simulations carried to late times, we identify four channels of stellar mass black hole formation. Our examples for Channel 1 involve the formation of lower-gap and above black holes in energetic asymmetric supernova explosions. Our Channel 2 example involves a modest supernova explosion that may leave behind a lower-gap to $\sim$10 $M_{\odot}$ black hole. The latter may not be easily distinguishable from ``standard" supernovae that birth neutron stars. Our Channel 3 example experiences an aborted core-collapse explosion, more often in the context of a low-metallicity progenitor, whose residue is a black hole with a mass perhaps up to $\sim$40 $M_{\odot}$. The latter may be accompanied by a pulsational-pair instability supernova (PPISN). Channel 4 is the only quiescent or ``silent" scenario for which perhaps $\sim$5 to 15 $M_{\odot}$ black holes are left. Where appropriate, we estimate $^{56}$Ni yields, explosion energies, approximate recoil speeds, and residual black hole masses. The progenitor mass density and binding energy profiles at collapse influence the outcome in a systematic way. We speculate that the statistics and prevalence of these various channels depend not only on still evolving supernova theory, but on remaining issues with the theory of massive star evolution, binary interaction, wind mass loss, metallicity, and the nuclear equation of state. Importantly, we suggest, but have not proven, that the silent channel for black hole formation may not be the dominant formation modality.

## Key claim

Based on a large collection of detailed 3D core-collapse supernova simulations carried to late times, the authors identify four distinct channels of stellar-mass black hole formation, but they explicitly state they have not proven that the traditionally assumed "silent" channel is the dominant formation modality, and they do not attempt to generate theoretical distribution functions of final black hole masses.

## Method

The authors employed their 3D code Fornax to simulate the dynamical collapse and explosion phase to many seconds after bounce, starting with initial progenitor models created by KEPLER. After black hole formation, simulations were continued by mapping into either the hydrodynamic component of Fornax with a point mass and diode inner boundary condition or the FLASH code with a similar inner boundary, using the Helmholtz equation of state for post-Fornax simulations. The 3D FLASH capability allowed simulating to hours and days after explosion to capture late-time fallback accretion and the evolution of the residual black hole mass.

## Key numbers

- Channel 1: formation of lower-gap and above black holes in energetic asymmetric supernova explosions (no specific mass numbers given for this channel in the text)
- Channel 2: modest supernova explosion leaving behind a lower-gap to approximately 10 solar mass black hole
- Channel 3: aborted core-collapse explosion, more often in low-metallicity progenitors, leaving a black hole with mass perhaps up to approximately 40 solar masses, possibly accompanied by a pulsational-pair instability supernova (PPISN)
- Channel 4: quiescent or "silent" scenario leaving perhaps approximately 5 to 15 solar mass black holes
- Observed black hole masses in X-ray binaries range between approximately 4 and approximately 21 solar masses, with peak near approximately 8 solar masses (Ozel 2010, Farr 2011)
- Gaia astrometric mission found three non-accreting black holes with masses of approximately 9 solar masses (BH1 and BH2, El-Badry 2023) and approximately 33 solar masses (BH3, Gaia Collaboration 2024)
- Gaia BH3 orbits a low-metallicity star with [Fe/H] approximately -2.5
- All three Gaia black holes reside in highly eccentric orbits (approximately 0.4, approximately 0.5, and approximately 0.7 respectively)
- The number of stellar-mass black holes in the galaxy may be as many as 10^8 (Kaczmarek 2025)
- LVK collaboration identified a possible black hole in the mass interval approximately 2.5 to 4.5 solar masses (GW230529, Abac 2024)
- For the 20 solar mass model, the spiral SASI mode becomes significant at around 0.4 seconds post bounce and lasts for about 0.3 seconds until the model explodes at about 0.7 seconds post bounce

## Context

This work extends the modern theory of core-collapse supernovae that has emerged from 3D simulations by groups including Muller (2016), Bollig (2021), and the authors' own prior work (Burrows 2020, Burrows 2024), challenging the traditional assumption that all stellar-mass black hole formation would be "silent" with no supernova display (as proposed by Kochanek 2008, Gerke 2015, Adams 2017). The paper contradicts simple prescriptions that use compactness as an "explodability" condition (Sukhbold 2016, Woosley 2016) and instead demonstrates that high compactness models can explode vigorously. The four-channel framework enables future work on the true galactic black hole mass distribution function, the mapping of initial progenitor to final residual black hole mass, and the interpretation of gravitational wave detections from LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA.
