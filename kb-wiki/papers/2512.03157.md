# Effective Field Theory Perspective On King Non-linearity

> Precision spectroscopic measurements of isotope shifts have recently reached a high level of accuracy. Tests of King non-linearity (NL) along isotope chains have been proposed as a tool to search for fifth-force mediators. At the same time, these tests can potentially teach us about the structure of heavy nuclei at unprecedented precision, where King NL has already been observed in several systems. A robust interpretation of the existing data, however, is hampered by incomplete control over the Standard Model (SM) contributions. We develop a systematic effective field theory framework, matching the SM onto scalar non-relativistic QED in the infinite nuclear mass limit and then onto quantum-mechanical potentials. This approach organizes all nuclear effects into a small set of Wilson coefficients and cleanly separates short- and long-distance physics. We show that the commonly used treatment of the $\langle r^2\rangle^2$ term needs to be reconsidered, as it arises only at second-order in perturbation theory, and we derive the long-range $1/r^4$ potential from nuclear polarizability. Applying the framework to hydrogen-like systems, we provide a transparent classification of SM sources of King NL relevant for current and future isotope-shift experiments. The formalism can be applied to learn about the shape of the heavy scalar nuclei at a higher level of precision and detail than what was previously attainable.

## Key claim

The paper develops a systematic effective field theory (EFT) framework for King non-linearity in isotope shifts, showing that the commonly used treatment of the ⟨r²⟩² term is incorrect because it arises only at second-order in quantum-mechanical perturbation theory, and that nuclear polarizability induces a long-range 1/r⁴ potential; the framework organizes all Standard Model nuclear effects into a small set of Wilson coefficients, but the authors work in the infinite nuclear mass limit and restrict to spin-0 nuclei, so the full quantitative interpretation of existing data requires extending the analysis to finite mass and including spin-dependent effects.

## Method

The authors match the Standard Model onto scalar non-relativistic QED (NRQED) in the infinite nuclear mass limit, then match NRQED onto quantum-mechanical potentials (pNRQED) for hydrogen-like systems with spin-0 nuclei. This two-step matching organizes nuclear effects into Wilson coefficients corresponding to the charge radius squared ⟨r²⟩, the fourth moment ⟨r⁴⟩, and electric and magnetic polarizabilities α_E and β_M, cleanly separating short-distance nuclear physics from long-distance atomic physics. The resulting potentials are then applied within quantum-mechanical perturbation theory to classify Standard Model sources of King non-linearity.

## Key numbers

- The hierarchy of scales is given by r_N ∼ 10⁻¹⁵ m (nuclear scale) and a ∼ 10⁻¹⁰ m (atomic scale), with expansion parameters ε = r_N/a ∼ 10⁻⁵ and α ∼ 10⁻².
- For Ca (mass number A ∼ 40), the nuclear charge radius gives 1/r_N ∼ 1/(3.5 fm) ∼ 0.06 GeV and nuclear mass M ∼ 40 GeV; for Yb (A ∼ 170), 1/r_N ∼ 1/(5 fm) ∼ 0.04 GeV and M ∼ 160 GeV.
- Estimated electric polarizability α_E for Ca isotopes (A = 40–46) ranges from 19 to 24 fm³ using the hydrodynamic model, and from 26 to 31 fm³ using photo-absorption data; for Yb isotopes (A = 168–174), α_E ranges from 170 to 180 fm³ (hydrodynamic) and 210 to 220 fm³ (photo-absorption), with about 20% modeling uncertainty.
- The two-photon exchange Wilson coefficient d₂ contains the Friar radius ⟨r³⟩_(2) ∼ O(1/Λ_N³) and a 1/M suppressed term d₂^(1/M)/(m_e²M) that is numerically the most important term for Ca and Yb since Λ_N³ ∼ 20 m_e²M for Ca and ∼ 2 m_e²M for Yb.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering observation of King non-linearity in Yb⁺ by Counts (2020) and subsequent studies by Hur (2022) and Door (2024), which recognized that ⟨r⁴⟩ and ⟨r²⟩² are distinct contributions, contrary to earlier assumptions. The EFT framework provides a systematic organization of Standard Model corrections that was missing in previous analyses, enabling future isotope-shift experiments to cleanly separate nuclear structure effects from possible fifth-force signals mediated by new light particles, as originally proposed by Berengut (2017) and Delaunay (2016).
