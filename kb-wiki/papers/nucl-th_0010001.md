# Few-Body Problems in Hadron Spectroscopy

> Some rigorous results can be derived using a very simple approach to hadron spectroscopy, in which a static potential is associated with non-relativistic kinematics. Several regularities of the experimental spectrum are explained by such models. It is underlined that certain methods developed for hadronic physics have found applications in other fields, in particular atomic physics. A few results can be extended to cases involving spin-dependent forces or relativistic kinematics.

## Key claim

Many regularities of the experimental hadron spectrum can be explained by a simple non-relativistic quark model with a static, flavour-independent potential, but the ordering of radial versus orbital excitations in the baryon sector (the Roper resonance problem) contradicts this simple picture and requires either flavour-dependent potentials, spin-dependent forces, or a non-conventional interpretation of the resonance.

## Method

The paper reviews rigorous results derived from the Schrödinger equation for two-body (meson) and three-body (baryon) systems with static potentials, including theorems on level ordering (Coulomb theorem), convexity properties of energy levels as functions of inverse masses, and Hall-Post inequalities relating N-body to (N-1)-body ground-state energies. It also discusses the hyperspherical expansion method for baryons, variational calculations, and the Schwinger trick for estimating short-range correlations.

## Key numbers

- For heavy quarkonia: 1/2 (Upsilon - Psi) is approximately 3.2 GeV, while (B - D) is approximately 3.3 GeV, showing the effect of binding energy differences.
- The spin-averaged ground state mass of (b anti-c) meson is bounded by 6.26 < (b anti-c) < 6.43 GeV from rigorous inequalities.
- The first measurement of the (b anti-c) meson gives 6.4 +/- 0.4 GeV.
- A less efficient but simpler bound gives (b anti-c) > (b anti-s) + (c anti-c) - (c anti-s) approximately 6.52 GeV.
- The critical mass ratio for stability of the (anti-q anti-q Q Q) four-quark system is estimated to be about M/m approximately 2.2.

## Context

This review extends the rigorous results summarized by Quigg and Rosner and by Martin and Grosse on quarkonium level ordering and convexity properties, and applies similar methods to baryon spectroscopy. It addresses the long-standing Roper resonance problem, which has been vigorously revisited by Glozman and collaborators, and connects quark-model inequalities to earlier work on the stability of matter by Lieb and others and to Hall-Post inequalities from nuclear physics. The results enable rigorous tests of flavour independence in hadron spectroscopy and provide tools for estimating masses of yet-unobserved hadrons such as doubly-heavy baryons and multiquark states.
