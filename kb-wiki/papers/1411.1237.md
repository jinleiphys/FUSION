# Non-observable nature of the nuclear shell structure. Meaning, illustrations and consequences

> The concept of single-nucleon shells constitutes a basic pillar of our understanding of nuclear structure. Effective single-particle energies (ESPEs) introduced by French and Baranger represent the most appropriate tool to relate many-body observables to a single-nucleon shell structure. As briefly discussed in [T. Duguet, G. Hagen, Phys. Rev. C {\bf 85}, 034330 (2012)], the dependence of ESPEs on one-nucleon transfer probability matrices makes them purely theoretical quantities that "run" with the non-observable resolution scale $λ$ employed in the calculation. Given that ESPEs provide a way to interpret the many-body problem in terms of simpler theoretical ingredients, the goal is to specify the terms, i.e. the exact sense and conditions, in which this interpretation can be conducted meaningfully. State-of-the-art multi-reference in-medium similarity renormalization group and self-consistent Gorkov Green's function many-body calculations are employed to corroborate the formal analysis. This is done by comparing the behavior of several observables and of non-observable ESPEs (and spectroscopic factors) under (quasi) unitary similarity renormalization group transformations of the Hamiltonian parameterized by the resolution scale $λ$. The non-observable nature of the nuclear shell structure, i.e. the fact that it constitutes an intrinsically theoretical object with no counterpart in the empirical world, must be recognized and assimilated. Eventually, practitioners can refer to nuclear shells and spectroscopic factors in their analyses of nuclear phenomena if, and only if, they use consistent structure and reaction theoretical schemes based on a fixed resolution scale they have agreed on prior to performing their analysis and comparisons.

## Key claim

Effective single-particle energies (ESPEs) are intrinsically non-observable quantities that "run" with the resolution scale λ employed in the calculation, meaning that the nuclear shell structure has no counterpart in the empirical world and cannot be determined uniquely from experimental data; however, practitioners can refer to nuclear shells and spectroscopic factors meaningfully if and only if they use consistent structure and reaction schemes based on a fixed resolution scale agreed upon prior to analysis.

## Method

The authors employ state-of-the-art multi-reference in-medium similarity renormalization group (MR-IMSRG) and self-consistent Gorkov Green's function (G-SCGF) many-body calculations based on chiral effective field theory two-nucleon and three-nucleon interactions. They compare the behavior of several observables and of non-observable ESPEs (and spectroscopic factors) under (quasi) unitary similarity renormalization group transformations of the Hamiltonian parameterized by the resolution scale λ, using calculations for oxygen, calcium, and nickel isotopes.

## Key numbers

- The ESPE Fermi gap in 22O and 24O changes by more than 1 MeV when varying the 2N interaction resolution scale from 1.8 to 2.2 fm−1, while the 2+ excitation energy varies by 400 keV in 22O and only 30 keV in 24O over the same range

## Context

This work extends the brief discussion and limited numerical illustrations from Duguet and Hagen (2012) regarding the scale dependence of ESPEs, and parallels similar discussions of non-observable quantities in other fields such as the one-nucleon momentum distribution (Furnstahl, 2001) and spectroscopic factors (Jennings, 2011). The paper formalizes the non-observable nature of the nuclear shell structure introduced by French and Baranger, and enables practitioners to establish meaningful correlations between observables and shell structure only by fixing the resolution scale arbitrarily but consistently across structure and reaction calculations.
