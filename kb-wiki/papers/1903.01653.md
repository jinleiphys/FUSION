# Axial Kinetic Theory and Spin Transport for Fermions with Arbitrary Mass

> We derive the quantum kinetic theory for fermions with arbitrary mass in a background electromagnetic field from the Wigner-function approach. Since spin of massive fermions is a dynamical degree of freedom, the kinetic equations with the leading-order quantum corrections describe entangled dynamics of not only the vector- and axial-charge distributions but also of the spin polarization. Therefore, we obtain one scalar and one axial-vector kinetic equations with magnetization currents pertinent to the spin-orbit interaction. We show that our results smoothly reduce to the massless limit where the spin of massless fermions is no longer an independent dynamical degree of freedom but is enslaved by the chirality and momentum and the accordingly kinetic equations turn into the chiral kinetic theory for Weyl fermions. We provide a kinetic theory covering both the massive and massless cases, and hence resolves the problem in constructing the bridge between them. Such generalization may be crucial for applications to various physical systems. Based on our kinetic equations, we discuss the anomalous currents transported by massive fermions in thermal equilibrium.

## Key claim

The authors derive a quantum kinetic theory for fermions with arbitrary mass, called the axial kinetic theory (AKT), that smoothly reduces to the chiral kinetic theory (CKT) for massless Weyl fermions in the massless limit, thereby resolving the problem of constructing a bridge between the massive and massless cases; the key condition is that the spin of massive fermions is a dynamical degree of freedom, whereas for massless fermions it is enslaved by chirality and momentum.

## Method

The authors start from the Wigner-function approach for a massive Dirac field in a background electromagnetic field, applying an \(\hbar\) expansion to the Kadanoff-Baym equation while dropping self-energy contributions. They decompose the Wigner functions into vector and axial components, solve the master equations perturbatively up to \(\mathcal{O}(\hbar^1)\), and obtain one scalar kinetic equation (SKE) for the vector distribution and one axial-vector kinetic equation (AKE) for the spin four-vector and axial distribution. The axial part is further constrained by constructing Wigner functions directly from second quantization of free Dirac fields, which fixes the magnetization-current term.

## Key numbers

* The zeroth-order vector and axial Wigner functions are \((\mathcal{V}_0/\mathcal{A}_0)^{\mu}=2\pi (q/a)^{\mu}\delta(q^2-m^2)f_{V/A}\), where \(f_{V/A}\) are the vector/axial distribution functions and \(a^{\mu}\) is the spin four-vector.
* The \(\mathcal{O}(\hbar^1)\) vector Wigner function includes a magnetization-current term \(G^{\mu} = \frac{ \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}n_{\nu}}{2q\cdot n}[ \Delta_{\rho}(a_{\sigma}f_A)+F_{\rho\sigma}f_A]\), where \(n^{\mu}\) is a local frame vector.
* The \(\mathcal{O}(\hbar^1)\) axial Wigner function includes a magnetization-current term \(H^{\mu}=S^{\mu\nu}_{m(n)}\Delta_{\nu}f_V\) with \(S^{\mu\nu}_{m(n)}=\frac{\epsilon^{\mu\nu\alpha\beta}q_{\alpha}n_{\beta}}{2(q\cdot n+m)}\).
* In thermal equilibrium with constant magnetic field and thermal vorticity, the axial current yields the chiral separation effect and axial chiral vortical effect with conductivities \(\sigma_{B/\omega}=\frac{\hbar}{2\pi^2}\int^{\infty}_0d|{\bf q}|g_{B/\omega}f^{(-/+)}_{0}(E_{\bf q})\), where \(g_{B}=1\) and \(g_{\omega}=(2E_{\bf q}^2-m^2)/E_{\bf q}\).

## Context

This work extends the chiral kinetic theory developed for massless fermions by Hidaka et al. (2016, 2017) and others to massive fermions, addressing the issue that previous kinetic theories derived in the rest frame (e.g., Weickgenannt et al. (2019), Gao et al. (2019)) break down for small mass. The AKT enables the study of spin transport and anomalous currents in systems where mass effects are important, such as \(\Lambda\) hyperon polarization in heavy-ion collisions (STAR Collaboration, 2017, 2018) and chiral-plasma instabilities in astrophysics (Akamatsu et al., 2013; Ohnishi et al., 2014; Grabowska et al., 2014).
