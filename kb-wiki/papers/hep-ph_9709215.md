# Direct extraction of the chiral quark condensate and bounds on the light quark masses

> We select sum rules from which one can extract directly reliable limits on the size of the chiral symmetry breaking light quark condensate $<\barψψ>$. Combined results from the nucleon and $B^*-B$ mass-splitting sum rules give a result compatible with the standard value: $<\barψψ> (1 GeV) \simeq (-229 MeV)^3$, through the determinations of the quark-gluon mixed condensate. The vector form factor of the $D\rar K^*lν$ semi-leptonic decay leads to the range $0.6\leq <\barψψ> (-229 MeV)^3 \leq 1.5$. The upper limit combined with the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner (GMOR) relation implies the interesting lower bound on the sum of light quark masses:$(m_u+m_d) (1 GeV) \geq$ 9.4 MeV, which combined with the ratio of light quark masses from chiral perturbation theory leads to $m_s (1 GeV) \geq (121\pm 12)$ MeV. The lower limit combined with the positivity of the $m_q^2$ contribution to the GMOR relation leads to the upper bound : $(m_u+m_d) (1 GeV) \leq$ 15.7 MeV, which is independent on the nature of chiral symmetry breaking.

## Key claim
The chiral quark condensate can be extracted directly from experimental data using selected QCD sum rules, yielding a value compatible with the standard \( \langle \bar\psi\psi \rangle (1\ \text{GeV}) \simeq (-229\ \text{MeV})^3 \), but with a range of \( 0.6 \leq \langle \bar\psi\psi \rangle / (-229\ \text{MeV})^3 \leq 1.5 \) from the \( D \to K^* \ell \nu \) decay analysis, which depends on the value of the charm quark mass.

## Method
The authors combine results from two independent QCD sum-rule approaches: (1) nucleon sum rules for the form factor \( F_2 \) and the \( B^* \)-\( B \) mass-splitting sum rule, which together determine the mixed condensate \( \langle \bar{\psi} \sigma G \psi \rangle \) and the ratio \( M_0^2 \), yielding the chiral condensate; (2) a three-point function sum rule for the vector form factor \( V(0) \) of the semileptonic decay \( D \to K^* \ell \nu \), where stability in the sum-rule variables \( \tau_1 \) and \( \tau_2 \) is required to extract the condensate from experimental data. The GMOR relation then translates the condensate bounds into bounds on the light quark masses.

## Key numbers
- Standard chiral condensate: \( \langle \bar\psi\psi \rangle (1\ \text{GeV}) = -[(229 \pm 9)\ \text{MeV}]^3 \).
- Mixed condensate ratio from nucleon sum rules: \( M_0^2 = (0.8 \pm 0.1)\ \text{GeV}^2 \).
- Mixed condensate from \( B^* \)-\( B \) splitting: \( \langle \bar{\psi} \sigma G \psi \rangle = -(9 \pm 1) \times 10^{-3}\ \text{GeV}^5 \).
- Chiral condensate from combined nucleon and \( B^* \)-\( B \) sum rules: \( \langle \bar\psi\psi \rangle (M_N) = -[(225 \pm 9)\ \text{MeV}]^3 \).
- Range from \( D \to K^* \ell \nu \) vector form factor: \( 0.6 \leq \langle \bar\psi\psi \rangle / (-0.229\ \text{GeV})^3 \leq 1.5 \).
- Lower bound on sum of light quark masses from GMOR relation: \( (m_u + m_d)(1\ \text{GeV}) \geq 9.4\ \text{MeV} \).
- Upper bound on sum of light quark masses: \( (m_u + m_d)(1\ \text{GeV}) \leq 15.7\ \text{MeV} \).
- Strange quark mass lower bound: \( m_s(1\ \text{GeV}) \geq (121 \pm 12)\ \text{MeV} \).
- Estimate from combined sum rules: \( (m_u + m_d)(1\ \text{GeV}) \simeq 15\ \text{MeV} \), \( m_s(1\ \text{GeV}) \simeq 182\ \text{MeV} \).

## Context
This work extends the standard QCD sum-rule approach of Shifman, Vainshtein, and Zakharov (1979) and earlier nucleon sum-rule analyses by Ioffe (1981) and Dosch, Jamin, and Narison (1989) to directly extract the chiral condensate from data, avoiding correlations with other condensates. The results contradict smaller condensate values proposed in generalized chiral perturbation theory (Stern et al.) and disagree with lattice extrapolations (LATT), while agreeing with pseudoscalar sum-rule determinations (PSEU, PSEUDO, EDUAR) and scalar sum-rule results (SCALAR). The bounds on light quark masses enable a test of the nature of chiral symmetry breaking, independent of whether the GMOR relation or generalized chiral perturbation theory holds.
