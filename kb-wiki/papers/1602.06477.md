# Coulomb breakup of 22C in a four-body model

> Breakup cross sections are determined for the Borromean nucleus 22C by using a four-body eikonal model, including Coulomb corrections. Bound and continuum states are constructed within a 20C + n + n three-body model in hyperspherical coordinates. We compute continuum states with the correct asymptotic behavior through the R-matrix method. For the n+ n potential, we use the Minnesota interaction. As there is no precise experimental information on 21C, we define different parameter sets for the 20C + n potentials. These parameter sets provide different scattering lengths, and resonance energies of an expected 3/2+ excited state. Then we analyze the 22C ground-state energy and rms radius, as well as E1 strength distributions and breakup cross sections. The E1 strength distribution presents an enhancement at low energies. Its amplitude is associated with the low binding energy, rather than with a three-body resonance. We show that the shape of the cross section at low energies is sensitive to the ground-state properties. In addition, we suggest the existence of a low-energy 2+ resonance, which should be observable in breakup experiments.

# Coulomb breakup of 22C in a four-body model

## Key claim
The low-energy enhancement in the E1 strength distribution of 22C is associated with the low binding energy of the ground state rather than with a three-body resonance, and the shape of the breakup cross section at low energies is sensitive to the ground-state properties. The authors suggest the existence of a low-energy 2+ resonance that should be observable in breakup experiments, but note that the lack of precise experimental information on 21C and the very low binding energy of 22C (|E0| ≤ 0.3 MeV) make calculations more time-consuming and introduce significant uncertainties.

## Method
The authors use a four-body eikonal model with Coulomb corrections to compute breakup cross sections of 22C on 208Pb at 240 MeV/nucleon. Bound and continuum states of the 20C + n + n three-body projectile are constructed in hyperspherical coordinates, with continuum states computed using the R-matrix method to obtain correct asymptotic behavior. Three different parameter sets for the 20C + n potential are defined, providing different scattering lengths and resonance energies of an expected 3/2+ excited state in 21C, while the n + n potential uses the Minnesota interaction with mixture parameter u = 1.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state energy E0 = -0.10 MeV for set 1, -0.47 MeV for set 2, and -0.46 MeV for set 3
- Scattering length a0 = -2.8 fm for sets 1 and 2, and -47.6 fm for set 3
- 3/2+ resonance energy in 21C: ER = 0.83 MeV (set 1), 0.59 MeV (set 2), 0.92 MeV (set 3)
- Width of the 3/2+ resonance in 21C for set 1: 0.09 MeV
- Experimental rms matter radius of 22C: 5.4 ± 0.9 fm (Tanaka et al., 2010)
- Experimental rms radius of 20C core: 2.98 ± 0.05 fm (Ozawa et al., 2001)
- Experimental limit on 21C scattering length: |a0| < 2.8 fm (Mosby et al., 2013)
- Experimental limit on two-neutron separation energy: S2n < 300 keV (Gaudefroy et al., 2012)
- Predicted 2+ resonance energy: 0.83 MeV for set 1
- Maximum hypermomentum Kmax = 40 for bound states, Kmax = 30, 25, 20 for J = 0+, 1-, 2+ continuum partial waves respectively
- Channel radius a ≈ 90 fm with N ≈ 100 Lagrange functions, R-matrix propagated up to 400 fm

## Context
This work extends previous four-body eikonal calculations applied to 6He (Bayegan et al., 2009) and 11Li (Pinilla et al., 2012) to the heaviest known Borromean nucleus 22C. It improves upon the equivalent photon method traditionally used for Coulomb breakup studies (Nakamura et al., 2006; Kido et al., 2010; Hagino and Sagawa, 2007) by including three-body continuum wave functions with correct asymptotic behavior, multipolarities beyond dipole, and consistent treatment of Coulomb and nuclear effects. The results confirm the conclusion of Ershov et al. (2012) regarding the role of ground-state binding, and the predicted 2+ resonance is consistent with shell-model arguments and preliminary experimental data (Enthofer, 2015). The work enables future experimental measurements of 22C breakup to constrain theoretical models and ground-state properties.
