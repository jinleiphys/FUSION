# Bjorken Initial Energy Density and Viscous Longitudinal Hydrodynamic Evolution in Xe-Xe Collisions

> We present a systematic study of the Bjorken initial energy density in Xe-Xe collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.44$ TeV, estimated using charged-particle multiplicity data and a generalized transverse overlap geometry applicable beyond the most central collisions. The dependence of the extracted energy density is examined by adopting both a constant formation time and a centrality-dependent formation time derived from Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.02$ TeV. Corresponding Bjorken energy density estimates for Pb-Pb collisions are also presented for comparison. Taking the Bjorken energy density and formation time as initial conditions, the subsequent longitudinal evolution of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) formed in these collisions is studied. Both ideal and first-order viscous boost-invariant hydrodynamics are employed to assess the influence of dissipation. We observe that viscous effects slow the longitudinal expansion and lead to entropy production dominated by early-time dynamics. The lifetime of the QGP is observed to increase with centrality and is substantially enhanced by viscous effects. These effects are highly sensitive to the choice of formation time, particularly in peripheral collisions. A comparative analysis of Xe-Xe and Pb-Pb collisions demonstrates that the longitudinal evolution is primarily controlled by the initial energy density scale set by the Bjorken prescription. Consequently, when this scale is comparable, both systems exhibit nearly identical evolution patterns, while appreciable distinctions emerge in peripheral collisions due to system-size and geometric effects.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the longitudinal evolution of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) in Xe-Xe collisions at 5.44 TeV is primarily controlled by the initial energy density scale set by the Bjorken prescription, but this conclusion is conditional on the choice of formation time (fixed at 0.6 fm versus centrality-dependent values extracted from Pb-Pb data), with viscous effects and system-size distinctions becoming appreciable only in peripheral collisions.

## Method

The authors estimated the Bjorken initial energy density for Xe-Xe collisions at 5.44 TeV using charged-particle rapidity density data from ALICE and a generalized elliptic transverse overlap geometry valid for all centralities. They then used these initial conditions (energy density and formation time) to solve coupled differential equations for ideal and first-order viscous boost-invariant hydrodynamics, assuming a conformal equation of state and a shear viscosity proportional to energy density over temperature.

## Key numbers

* Bjorken initial energy density for 0-5% central Xe-Xe collisions at 5.44 TeV with fixed formation time (0.6 fm): 17.299 GeV/fm^3
* Bjorken initial energy density for 70-90% peripheral Xe-Xe collisions at 5.44 TeV with fixed formation time (0.6 fm): 3.901 GeV/fm^3
* Bjorken initial energy density for 0-5% central Xe-Xe collisions at 5.44 TeV with varying formation time (0.700 fm): 14.828 GeV/fm^3
* Bjorken initial energy density for 70-90% peripheral Xe-Xe collisions at 5.44 TeV with varying formation time (1.519 fm): 1.541 GeV/fm^3
* Bjorken initial energy density for 0-10% central Pb-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV with fixed formation time (0.6 fm): 20.232 GeV/fm^3
* Formation time extracted for 0-10% central Pb-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV: 0.758 fm
* Formation time extracted for 70-80% peripheral Pb-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV: 1.354 fm

## Context

This work extends the Bjorken energy density estimation framework of Prasad (2021) and Bjorken (1983) to Xe-Xe collisions, using a generalized elliptic overlap geometry that goes beyond the circular approximation valid only for most central collisions. The study enables systematic comparison of QGP longitudinal evolution between Xe-Xe and Pb-Pb systems, showing that when the initial energy density scale is comparable, both systems exhibit nearly identical evolution patterns, while system-size and geometric effects produce appreciable distinctions in peripheral collisions.
