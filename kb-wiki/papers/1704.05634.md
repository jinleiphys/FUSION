# Analytical solutions for the radial Scarf II potential

> The real Scarf II potential is discussed as a radial problem. This potential has been studied extensively as a one-dimensional problem, and now these results are used to construct its bound and resonance solutions for $l=0$ by setting the origin at some arbitrary value of the coordinate. The solutions with appropriate boundary conditions are composed as the linear combination of the two independent solutions of the Schrödinger equation. The asymptotic expression of these solutions is used to construct the $S_0(k)$ s-wave $S$-matrix, the poles of which supply the $k$ values corresponding to the bound, resonance and anti-bound solutions. The location of the discrete energy eigenvalues is analyzed, and the relation of the solutions of the radial and one-dimensional Scarf II potentials is discussed. It is shown that the generalized Woods--Saxon potential can be generated from the Rosen--Morse II potential in the same way as the radial Scarf II potential is obtained from its one-dimensional correspondent. Based on this analogy, possible applications are also pointed out.

## Key claim

The paper constructs analytical bound and resonance solutions for the radial Scarf II potential for s-wave (l=0) by cutting the one-dimensional potential at an arbitrary coordinate and imposing a vanishing boundary condition at that point, rather than treating it as a genuine three-dimensional radial problem with a regular origin.

## Method

The authors take the known one-dimensional Scarf II potential and define a radial version by setting the origin at an arbitrary point x = r0 on the real axis, with r in [0, infinity). They construct the general s-wave solution as a linear combination of the two independent hypergeometric solutions of the one-dimensional Schrodinger equation, imposing the boundary condition that the wave function vanishes at r = 0 (x = r0). The asymptotic form of this solution for r approaching infinity is used to construct the S0(k) s-wave S-matrix, whose poles on the complex k-plane are identified with bound, resonance, and anti-bound states.

## Key numbers

* Bound-state energy eigenvalues for the one-dimensional Scarf II potential are given by E_n = -c^2 (n + (alpha+beta+1)/2)^2, where c is a scaling factor and alpha, beta are parameters.
* With the reparametrization alpha = -s - 1/2 - i*lambda and beta = -s - 1/2 + i*lambda, the bound-state energies become E_n = -c^2 (s - n)^2, independent of lambda.
* The transmission coefficient T(k) poles at -n = -s - ik/c give bound-state energies E_n = -c^2 (s-n)^2, while poles at -n = s+1 - ik/c correspond to anti-bound states, and poles at -n = -i*lambda + 1/2 - ik/c and -n = i*lambda + 1/2 - ik/c correspond to resonances with E_n = -c^2 (n - 1/2 +/- i*lambda)^2.
* For a specific test case with parameters s=2.0, lambda=1.0, c=1.0, and r0 = -2.017825 (coinciding with a node of the one-dimensional n=3 wave function), the radial problem yields a bound state at E_2 = -0.64, matching the one-dimensional E_3 value.

## Context

This work extends the extensive one-dimensional studies of the Scarf II (Gendenshtein) potential, which has been analyzed for bound states, scattering, PT-symmetry, and spectral singularities by Gendenshtein (1983), Kwasniewicz and Sienkiewicz (1988), and Ahmed (2001, 2004, 2009, 2013, 2015), into the radial domain for the first time. The authors draw an analogy with the generalized Woods-Saxon potential, which can be generated from the one-dimensional Rosen-Morse II potential in the same way, referencing the analytical S-matrix construction for the Woods-Saxon potential by Bencze (1966) and noting that earlier bound-state wave functions from the Rosen-Morse II connection by Berkdemir (2008) did not satisfy the correct boundary conditions at the origin. This work enables applications in nuclear physics, such as modeling potentials with surface barriers for charged particle interactions, bubble nuclei with depleted central density, and hypernuclei with soft repulsive cores.
