# The Cosmological Evolution of the Average Mass Per Baryon

> Subsequent to the early Universe quark-hadron transition the universal baryon number is carried by nucleons: neutrons and protons. The total number of nucleons is preserved as the Universe expands, but as it cools lighter protons are favored over heavier neutrons reducing the average mass per baryon. During primordial nucleosynthesis free nucleons are transformed into bound nuclides, primarily helium, and the nuclear binding energies are radiated away, further reducing the average mass per baryon. In particular, the reduction in the average mass per baryon resulting from Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) modifies the numerical factor relating the baryon (nucleon) mass and number densities. Here the average mass per baryon, m_B, is tracked from the early Universe to the present. The result is used to relate the present ratio of baryons to photons (by number) to the present baryon mass density at a level of accuracy commensurate with that of recent cosmological data, as well as to estimate the energy released during post-BBN stellar nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

The average mass per baryon evolves from 939.17 MeV shortly after the quark-hadron transition to 937.12 MeV after Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN), and this post-BBN value modifies the conversion factor between the baryon-to-photon ratio and the baryon mass density parameter to \(\eta_{10}/\Omega_{\rm B}h^{2} = 273.9 \pm 0.3\) (for primordial helium mass fraction Y_P = 0.25), a result that is accurate to 0.1% but depends on the adopted CMB temperature and Newton's constant.

## Method

The author tracks the average mass per baryon \(m_{\rm B} = \rho_{\rm B}/n_{\rm B}\) from the quark-hadron transition through BBN to the present, using the neutron-to-proton ratio from weak equilibrium and detailed BBN calculations. Post-BBN, the mass per baryon is computed from the atomic masses of hydrogen, deuterium, helium-3, and helium-4, using primordial abundances predicted by BBN for a given baryon-to-photon ratio (e.g., from WMAP data). The conversion factor between \(\eta_{10}\) and \(\Omega_{\rm B}h^{2}\) is then derived from the post-BBN \(m_{\rm B}\), the CMB temperature, and the critical density.

## Key numbers

- Pre-BBN average mass per baryon at \(T = 100\) MeV: \(m_{\rm B} = 939.17\) MeV.
- Pre-BBN average mass per baryon at \(T = 70\) keV (onset of BBN): \(m_{\rm B} \approx 938.88\) MeV.
- Post-BBN average mass per baryon (for Y_P = 0.2482): \(m_{\rm B} = 937.12\) MeV.
- Conversion factor: \(\eta_{10}/\Omega_{\rm B}h^{2} = 273.9 \pm 0.3\) (for Y_P = 0.25, T_{\gamma 0} = 2.725 K).
- Present CBR photon number density: \(n_{\gamma 0} = 410.50 \pm 0.45\) cm\(^{-3}\) (for T_{\gamma 0} = 2.725 K).
- Solar neighborhood average mass per baryon (BP04+ abundances): \(m_{\rm B\odot} = 936.87\) MeV.
- Decrease in average mass per baryon from post-BBN to solar neighborhood: \(\sim 0.26\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the standard BBN framework by providing a precise, time-dependent calculation of the average mass per baryon, correcting approximations in earlier analyses such as those in CAMB/RECFast codes (which assume \(m_{\rm He}=4m_{\rm H}\) and use an outdated Newton's constant). The result enables accurate conversion between the baryon mass density parameter \(\Omega_{\rm B}h^{2}\) (as measured by WMAP, Spergel et al. 2006) and the baryon-to-photon ratio \(\eta_{10}\) used in BBN calculations, and it allows estimation of the energy released during stellar nucleosynthesis. The paper also notes that the conversion coefficient used by Trotta and Hansen (2004) (near 275) differs by 0.4%, while that of Ichikawa and Takahashi (2006) (273.49) is closer but still off by more than the quoted uncertainty.
