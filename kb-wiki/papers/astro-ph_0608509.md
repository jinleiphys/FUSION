# Neutrino signals from the formation of black hole: a probe of equation of state of dense matter

> The gravitational collapse of a non-rotating, black-hole-forming massive star is studied by neutrino-radiation-hydrodynamical simulations for two different sets of realistic equation of state of dense matter. We show that the event will produce as many neutrinos as the ordinary supernova, but with distinctive characteristics in luminosities and spectra that will be an unmistakable indication of black hole formation. More importantly, the neutrino signals are quite sensitive to the difference of equation of state and can be used as a useful probe into the properties of dense matter. The event will be unique in that they will be shining only by neutrinos (and, possibly, gravitational waves) but not by photons, and hence they should be an important target of neutrino astronomy.

## Key claim

Neutrino signals from the non-rotating collapse of a massive star to a black hole are highly sensitive to the stiffness of the dense-matter equation of state (EOS), but the authors note that the simulations use only nucleonic degrees of freedom and that the results serve as an upper limit on the duration of neutrino emission, since exotic phases (hyperons, quark matter) would cause earlier collapse.

## Method

The authors performed general relativistic neutrino-radiation-hydrodynamical simulations in spherical symmetry of the collapse of a 40 solar mass presupernova model (Woosley and Weaver 1995) with a 1.98 solar mass iron core. They used two realistic equations of state: the Lattimer and Swesty (LS) EOS (incompressibility 180 MeV, maximum cold neutron star mass 1.8 solar masses) and the Shen et al. (SH) EOS (incompressibility 281 MeV, maximum cold neutron star mass 2.2 solar masses). The Boltzmann equation was solved for electron neutrinos, electron antineutrinos, and muon/tau neutrinos and antineutrinos, with weak interaction rates from Bruenn (1985) plus plasmon and nucleon-nucleon bremsstrahlung processes.

## Key numbers

* The LS model formed a black hole at 0.57 s after bounce, when the enclosed baryon mass reached 2.10 solar masses and gravitational mass 1.99 solar masses.
* The SH model formed a black hole at 1.35 s after bounce, when the enclosed baryon mass reached 2.66 solar masses and gravitational mass 2.38 solar masses.
* The accretion rate for the 40 solar mass progenitor was about 1 solar mass per second at 0.4 s after bounce, compared to about 0.2 solar mass per second for a canonical 15 solar mass supernova model.
* The temperature near the inner core surface reached around 100 MeV at the beginning of re-collapse and exceeded 100 MeV for both models, with the LS model reaching 191 MeV and the SH model reaching 170 MeV.
* The maximum masses of cold neutron star are 1.8 solar masses for LS-EOS and 2.2 solar masses for SH-EOS.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of black hole formation from massive stars, which had focused on quasistatic evolutions of mass-accreting proto-neutron stars (Burrows 1988) or delayed collapse after successful supernova explosions (Glebbeek 1995, Baumgarte 1996a, 1996b, Pons 1999, 2001a, 2001b). It differs from long-term hydrodynamical simulations by Bruenn (2001) and Liebendoerfer (2004) by focusing specifically on the EOS dependence of neutrino signals. The results enable future work to use neutrino detection at Super-Kamiokande, SNO, and other facilities to constrain the EOS of hot dense matter, and provide a baseline for including exotic phases such as hyperons, pion condensations, and deconfined quarks.
