# Reaction-diffusion equation for quark-hadron transition in heavy-ion collisions

> Reaction-diffusion equations with suitable boundary conditions have special propagating solutions which very closely resemble the moving interfaces in a first order transition. We show that the dynamics of chiral order parameter for chiral symmetry breaking transition in heavy-ion collisions, with dissipative dynamics, is governed by one such equation, specifically, the Newell-Whitehead equation. Further, required boundary conditions are automatically satisfied due to the geometry of the collision. The chiral transition is, therefore, completed by a propagating interface, exactly as for a first order transition, even though the transition actually is a crossover for relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Same thing also happens when we consider the initial confinement-deconfinement transition with Polyakov loop order parameter. The resulting equation, again with dissipative dynamics, can then be identified with the reaction-diffusion equation known as the Fitzhugh-Nagumo equation which is used in population genetics. We discuss the implications of these results for heavy-ion collisions. We also discuss possible extensions for the case of early universe.

# Reaction-diffusion equation for quark-hadron transition in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
Even though the quark-hadron transition in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is a crossover (not first-order), the chiral order parameter and the Polyakov loop order parameter each obey reaction-diffusion equations whose traveling front solutions produce moving interfaces that behave exactly like phase boundaries in a first-order transition. This result holds only in the strong-dissipation limit and requires that the effective potential has a shape corresponding to spontaneous symmetry breaking at the relevant stage of plasma evolution.

## Method
The authors start from the field equation for the chiral O(4) order parameter (with explicit symmetry breaking term H) and the effective Lagrangian for the Polyakov loop in pure gauge theory. They take the extreme dissipative limit by neglecting the second-order time derivative term, then rescale variables to map the chiral equation onto the Newell-Whitehead equation and the Polyakov-loop equation onto the Fitzhugh-Nagumo equation. They solve the full equations (including the second derivative) numerically in 1D using a leapfrog algorithm, first with constant large dissipation and uniform temperature to verify the analytical traveling-front solution, then with time-dependent dissipation η = 1/τ and temperature following Bjorken scaling T(τ) = T₀(τ₀/τ)^{1/3} to model expanding QGP.

## Key numbers
- For the chiral transition with constant η = 10 fm⁻¹ and T = 150 MeV, the numerically measured front velocity v = 0.21 agrees with the scaled analytical value v = (1/η)(3/√2) = 0.21.
- For the chiral transition with constant η = 1/0.14 fm⁻¹ ≈ 7.14 fm⁻¹ and T = 150 MeV, the numerical front velocity ranges from 0.41 to 0.35, close to the expected value v = 3m(T)/(η√2) = 0.42.
- The chiral model parameters are λ = 4.5, m_σ = 600 MeV, T_c = 200 MeV, and H = (120 MeV)³; the vacuum expectation value of φ is ξ = 75.18 MeV and the central maximum is at φ₀ = -25.93 MeV.
- For the Polyakov loop case, the initial thermalization time τ₀ is taken as 0.14 fm (LHC) and the temperature is set to T = 500 MeV (well above the C-D transition temperature T_c = 182 MeV) to ensure no potential barrier exists.
- For the Polyakov loop with constant η = 1/0.01 fm⁻¹ = 100 fm⁻¹ and T = 500 MeV, a well-defined traveling wave solution is obtained starting from initial time τ₀ = 0.14 fm.

## Context
This work extends the recognition that reaction-diffusion traveling fronts (common in population genetics and chemical systems) can appear in high-energy physics, as noted in several recent works (cited as "hep" in the text). It directly builds on the field equations for the chiral transition from B. S. (cited as "bynsk") and on the Polyakov-loop effective potential from the Z(3) studies (cited as "zn" and previous works "gupta, gupta2"). The results challenge the conventional assumption that the chiral transition and the confinement-deconfinement transition are the same transition, because the authors find that traveling fronts exist for one order parameter at stages where the other order parameter shows no such structure. The work enables re-evaluation of first-order-transition phenomena (strangelet formation, baryon concentration, fluctuations, thermal photon and dilepton production, J/ψ suppression, elliptic flow) in heavy-ion collisions even though the underlying QCD transition is a crossover, and suggests possible applications to the early universe via inflationary temperature fluctuations.
