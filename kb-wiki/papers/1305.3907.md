# Generalized Beth--Uhlenbeck approach to mesons and diquarks in hot, dense quark matter

> An important first step in the program of hadronization of chiral quark models is the bosonization in meson and diquark channels. This procedure is presented at finite temperatures and chemical potentials for the SU(2) flavor case of the NJL model with special emphasis on the mixing between scalar meson and scalar diquark modes which occurs in the 2SC color superconducting phase. The thermodynamic potential is obtained in the gaussian approximation for the meson and diquark fields and it is given the Beth-Uhlenbeck form. This allows a detailed discussion of bound state dissociation in hot, dense matter (Mott effect) in terms of the in-medium scattering phase shift of two-particle correlations. It is shown for the case without meson-diquark mixing that the phase shift can be separated into a continuum and a resonance part. In the latter, the Mott transition manifests itself by a change of the phase shift at threshold by π in accordance with Levinson's theorem, when a bound state transforms to a resonance in the scattering continuum. The consequences for the contribution of pionic correlations to the pressure are discussed by evaluating the Beth-Uhlenbeck equation of state in different approximations. A similar discussion is performed for the scalar diquark channel in the normal phase. Further developments and applications of the developed approach are outlined.

## Key claim

The paper presents a generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck approach for mesons and diquarks in hot, dense quark matter within the SU(2) flavor NJL model, showing that the thermodynamic potential can be expressed in a form that allows detailed discussion of bound state dissociation (Mott effect) via in-medium scattering phase shifts, but only for the case without meson-diquark mixing; the mixing that occurs in the 2SC color superconducting phase is noted but not fully treated in the phase shift analysis.

## Method

The authors perform a bosonization of the NJL model via Hubbard-Stratonovich transformations at finite temperature and chemical potential, introducing auxiliary meson and diquark fields. They obtain the thermodynamic potential in the gaussian approximation for fluctuations around the mean-field solutions, then rewrite it in the Beth-Uhlenbeck form. The in-medium scattering phase shifts for two-particle correlations (pion, sigma meson, scalar diquark) are computed and separated into continuum and resonance parts, with the Mott transition identified by a change of the phase shift at threshold by pi in accordance with Levinson's theorem.

## Key numbers

- The scalar coupling constant G_S, vector coupling G_V, and diquark coupling G_D are related via Fierz transformation of a color-current interaction yielding G_V = G_S/2 and G_D = 3/4 G_S (these are parameter relations, not numerical values from fits)
- The thermodynamic potential is evaluated using fermionic Matsubara frequencies z_n = (2n+1)pi T and the Fermi distribution function n(x) = [exp(x/T)+1]^{-1}
- The gap equation for the diquark condensate involves an integral over momentum with factors [1-2n(E_p^-)]/E_p^- + [1-2n(E_p^+)]/E_p^+, where E_p^+/- = sqrt((E_p +/- mu*)^2 + |Delta_MF|^2)
- The mean-field thermodynamic potential contains terms with 2T ln(1 + exp(-E_p^+/T)) and 2T ln(1 + exp(-E_p^-/T)) for paired quarks, and T ln(1 + exp(-xi_p^+/T)) + T ln(1 + exp(-xi_p^-/T)) for unpaired blue quarks

## Context

This work extends the Beth-Uhlenbeck approach previously applied to mesonic correlations by Hufner (1994) to include diquark degrees of freedom, building on the hadronization program of Cahill (1988) and Reinhardt (1989) for chiral quark models. The approach is complementary to lattice QCD calculations by Bazavov (2011) and Borsanyi (2010), which are restricted to vanishing or small chemical potential due to the fermion sign problem. The formalism prepares the ground for a future Beth-Uhlenbeck description of nuclear matter, where at zero temperature the structure of a Walecka model for nuclear matter should emerge under specified conditions, following earlier work by Bentz (2001) and Lawley (2006).
