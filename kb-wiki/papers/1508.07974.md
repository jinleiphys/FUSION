# Initial state and thermalization in the Color Glass Condensate framework

> In this review, I present the description of the early stages of heavy ion collisions at high energy in the Color Glass Condensate framework, from the pre-collision high energy nuclear wavefunction to the point where hydrodynamics may start becoming applicable.

## Key claim

This review argues that the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) framework can describe the early stages of heavy ion collisions from the pre-collision wavefunction to the onset of hydrodynamics, but the transition from CGC to a hydrodynamic description remains an outstanding problem that has not yet reached a satisfactory state of understanding.

## Method

The paper reviews the CGC effective theory, which treats fast partons as a static longitudinal color current while describing slower partons as full gauge fields, with a probability distribution W[rho] for the color charge density. It covers leading-order calculations, next-to-leading order contributions with JIMWLK evolution, and the Classical Statistical Approximation as a resummation scheme to handle instabilities in the classical Yang-Mills equations that arise beyond leading order.

## Key numbers

- The QCD running coupling at one loop is given by alpha_s(E) = (2 pi N_c) / ((11 N_c - 2 N_f) log(E / Lambda_QCD)), where N_c is the number of colors and N_f the number of quark flavors
- The deconfinement transition temperature for QCD with 3 light quark flavors is around 160 MeV
- The saturation momentum Q_s^2 scales approximately as A^(1/3) x^(-0.3), with typical values in the range 2-4 GeV^2 for nuclei at LHC energy
- For gold or lead nuclei, the factor A^(1/3) in Q_s^2 is approximately 6
- Lattice calculations can reproduce the spectrum of light hadrons with an accuracy of the order of 5 percent
- The total duration of a heavy ion collision is of the order of 10 fm/c

## Context

This review extends the line of work on gluon saturation initiated by Gribov, Levin, and Ryskin (1983), Mueller and Qiu (1986), and Blaizot and Mueller (1987), and builds on the CGC framework developed by McLerran and Venugopalan (1994) and subsequent authors. It connects to the BFKL equation by Balitsky, Lipatov, Kuraev, and Fadin (1978) and the DGLAP formalism by Altarelli and Parisi (1977), Gribov and Lipatov (1972), and Dokshitzer (1977). The review identifies the matching between CGC and hydrodynamics as the key open question, noting that a small viscosity (suggested by hydrodynamic success) is more naturally obtained in the strong coupling limit but can also arise at weak coupling if occupation numbers are inversely proportional to g^2.
