# Electric dipole-forbidden nuclear transitions driven by super-intense laser fields

> Electric dipole-forbidden transitions of nuclei interacting with super-intense laser fields are investigated considering stable isotopes with suitable low-lying first excited states. Different classes of transitions are identified, and all magnetic sublevels corresponding to the near-resonantly driven nuclear transition are included in the description of the nuclear quantum system. We find that large transition matrix elements and convenient resonance energies qualify nuclear M1 transitions as good candidates for the coherent driving of nuclei. We discuss the implications of resonant interaction of intense laser fields with nuclei beyond the dipole approximation for the controlled preparation of excited nuclear states and important aspects of possible experiments aimed at observing these effects.

# Electric dipole-forbidden nuclear transitions driven by super-intense laser fields

## Key claim

Nuclear magnetic dipole (M1) transitions, rather than electric dipole (E1) transitions, are identified as the most promising candidates for resonant coherent driving of nuclei by super-intense laser fields, provided the nuclear transition energy is below approximately 12.4 keV for direct interaction or up to 100 keV with moderate target acceleration. This contrasts with atomic quantum optics where E1 transitions dominate, because in nuclei the symmetrical structure makes higher multipolarities the rule and low-lying E1 transitions are rare.

## Method

The authors develop a semiclassical density-matrix formalism for a nuclear few-level system (ground and first excited state including all magnetic sublevels) interacting with a classical laser field, expanding the plane-wave vector potential in spherical multipoles to account for electric dipole-forbidden transitions. They calculate interaction matrix elements for M1, E2, and E1 transitions using experimental reduced transition probabilities from the literature, then solve the optical Bloch equations to obtain excited-state populations and signal photon rates for specific isotopes, using parameters from the planned European XFEL (12.4 keV photon energy, 100 fs pulses, 40 kHz repetition rate, peak intensity 6×10¹⁵ W/cm²).

## Key numbers

- The largest reduced interaction matrix element for isotopes with first excited states below 12.4 keV is 3.84×10⁻¹⁰ eV for the 9.746 keV M1 transition in ¹⁸⁷₇₆Os, with a radiative decay rate of 9.43×10⁵ s⁻¹
- For isotopes requiring acceleration (transitions above 12.4 keV), the largest reduced interaction matrix element is 2.27×10⁻⁷ eV for the 35.843 keV E1 transition in ¹⁵³₆₂Sm, with a radiative decay rate of 2.55×10⁹ s⁻¹
- The highest signal rate for isotopes below 12.4 keV is 80.75 photons/second for the 9.746 keV M1 transition in ¹⁸⁷₇₆Os, accounting for internal conversion (α=17.09 for ⁸³₃₆Kr up to α=920 for ¹⁵¹₆₂Sm)
- The highest signal rate per nucleus per pulse is 3×10⁻¹⁰ photons/nucleus/pulse for the E1 transition in ¹⁵³₆₂Sm, two times larger than for the M1 transition in ¹⁶⁵₆₇Ho
- The European XFEL parameters used: photon energy up to 12.4 keV, average spectral brightness 1.6×10²⁵ photons/(sec·mrad²·mm²·0.1% bandwidth), pulse duration 100 fs, repetition rate 40 kHz, peak intensity 6×10¹⁵ W/cm²

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Thomas et al. (2007) that considered only electric dipole transitions in laser-nucleus interactions, showing that the restriction to E1 transitions unnecessarily limits the choice of suitable isotopes. The authors demonstrate that M1 transitions in nuclei such as ¹⁸⁷₇₆Os, ¹⁶⁹₆₉Tm, and ¹⁶⁵₆₇Ho have interaction matrix elements comparable to or larger than those of E1 transitions at similar energies, which is opposite to the atomic case. This opens the possibility of nuclear quantum optics experiments, such as nuclear Rabi oscillations, photon echoes, and coherent control, using upcoming X-ray free-electron laser facilities, though the very narrow nuclear resonance widths (many orders of magnitude smaller than the laser bandwidth) mean that only a tiny fraction of incident photons are resonant, limiting signal rates to at most ~10⁻¹⁰ photons per nucleus per pulse for direct interaction cases.
