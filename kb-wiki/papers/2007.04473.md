# Myth of scattering in finite volume

> In this notes, we illustrate why the infinite volume scattering amplitude is in fact dispensable when it comes to formulating few-body quantization condition in finite volume. Only subprocess interactions or interactions associated subprocess amplitudes are essential and fundamental ingredients of quantization conditions. After these ingredients are determined, infinite volume scattering amplitude can be computed separately. The underlying reasons are rooted in facts that (1) the final physical process is generated by all subprocess or interactions among particles; (2) the ultimate goal of quantization condition in finite volume is to find stationary solutions of few-body system. That is to say, in the end, it all comes down to the solving of eigenvalue problem, $\hat{H} | n \rangle = E_n | n \rangle $.

## Key claim

The infinite volume scattering amplitude is not a necessary component for formulating few-body quantization conditions in finite volume; only subprocess interactions or their associated subprocess amplitudes are essential. The total few-body amplitude can be computed separately after these basic ingredients are determined from lattice data.

## Method

The paper uses quantum mechanical operator formalism to compare two-body and three-body scattering in finite and infinite volumes. For two-body systems, the author shows how the Lüscher formula relates finite volume quantization conditions to infinite volume scattering amplitudes. For three-body systems, the author employs Faddeev equations to decompose the three-body problem into coupled equations involving two-body subprocess amplitudes, demonstrating that the quantization condition depends only on these subprocess amplitudes or interaction potentials, not on the full three-body scattering amplitude.

## Key numbers

- Two-body quantization condition: solutions of \(1/\hat{t}^{(L)}(E) = 1/\hat{V} - \hat{G}^{(L)}_0(E) = 0\) can be found on the entire real \(E\) axis in finite volume, whereas in infinite volume stationary bound state solutions exist only below two-particle threshold.
- Three-body quantization condition: \(\det \left[ \mathbb{I} - \hat{\mathcal{K}}(E) \hat{G}_0(E) \right] = 0\) determines stationary state solutions, with \(\hat{\mathcal{K}}(E)\) containing subprocess amplitudes \(\hat{t}_i(E)\).
- The relation connecting finite and infinite volume subprocess amplitudes: \(1/\hat{t}^{(L)}_i(E) = 1/\hat{t}^{(\infty)}_i(E) + \hat{G}^{(\infty)}_0(E) - \hat{G}^{(L)}_0(E)\).

## Context

This work extends the understanding of finite volume quantization conditions beyond the two-body Lüscher formula (Lüscher, 1990) to few-body systems. It clarifies that while two-body quantization conditions can be expressed directly in terms of infinite volume scattering amplitudes, few-body systems require a different approach using subprocess amplitudes as fundamental ingredients. The paper builds on the extensive body of work on few-hadron scattering in finite volume by groups including Kreuzer, Hansen, Briceno, Hammer, Mai, Doring, Romero-Lopez, and the author's own prior work (Guo, 2016-2020). This enables lattice QCD practitioners to extract subprocess interactions from finite volume spectra without needing to compute the full infinite volume scattering amplitude as an intermediate step.
