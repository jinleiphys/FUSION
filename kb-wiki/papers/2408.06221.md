# Prominent bump in the two-neutron separation energies of neutron-rich lanthanum isotopes revealed by high-precision mass spectrometry

> We report on high-precision atomic mass measurements of $^{148\text{-}153}$La and $^{151}$Ce performed with the JYFLTRAP double Penning trap using the Phase-Imaging Ion-Cyclotron-Resonance technique. The masses of $^{152,153}$La were experimentally determined for the first time. We confirm the sharp kink in the two-neutron separation energies at the neutron number ${N=93}$ in the cerium (${Z=58}$) isotopic chain. Our precision mass measurements of the most exotic neutron-rich lanthanum (${Z=57}$) isotopes reveal a sudden increase in two-neutron separation energies from ${N=92}$ to ${N=93}$. Unlike in the cerium isotopic chain, the kink is not sharp but extends to ${N=94}$ forming a prominent bump. The gain in energy is about 0.4 MeV, making it one of the strongest changes in two-neutron separation energies over the whole chart of nuclides, away from nuclear shell closures. The results call for further studies to elucidate the structure of neutron-rich lanthanum isotopes.

## Key claim

High-precision mass measurements of neutron-rich lanthanum isotopes reveal a sudden increase in two-neutron separation energies from N=92 to N=93 that, unlike the sharp kink observed in cerium, extends to N=94 forming a prominent bump of about 0.4 MeV; the authors note this is one of the strongest changes in two-neutron separation energies away from nuclear shell closures, but caution that the nature of the structural change cannot be determined from masses alone.

## Method

The authors used the JYFLTRAP double Penning trap mass spectrometer at the IGISOL facility, employing the Phase-Imaging Ion-Cyclotron-Resonance (PI-ICR) technique to measure atomic masses of neutron-rich fission fragments produced by a 25-MeV proton beam on a natural uranium target. The masses of 148-153La and 151Ce were determined from frequency ratios relative to a 133Cs reference, with the magnetic field strength calibrated using the reference ion. The mass-selective buffer-gas cooling technique in the first trap selected ions of interest, while the PI-ICR method in the second trap determined cyclotron frequencies from detected radial eigenmotion phase spots on a position-sensitive detector.

## Key numbers

- Mass excess of 148La: -62698.5(28) keV, measured with 2.8 keV uncertainty
- Mass excess of 149La: -59988.3(28) keV, deviating by 232(200) keV from the AME20 literature value of -60220(200) keV
- Mass excess of 150La: -56309.4(35) keV, consistent with AME20 value of -56311.1(25) keV
- Mass excess of 151La: -53524.5(29) keV, with 2.9 keV uncertainty (previous AME20 value was -53310(440) keV)
- Mass excess of 152La: -49178(11) keV, experimentally determined for the first time
- Mass excess of 153La: -46032(16) keV (weighted average), experimentally determined for the first time
- Mass excess of 151Ce: -61219.7(27) keV, consistent with AME20 value of -61225(18) keV
- The gain in two-neutron separation energy at the bump is about 0.4 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous mass measurements of neutron-rich rare-earth isotopes performed with JYFLTRAP (Vilen 2018, Jaries 2024) and the Canadian Penning trap (Orford 2019, Orford 2022), specifically addressing the poorly known masses of lanthanum isotopes around N=93. The observed bump in two-neutron separation energies for lanthanum, not reproduced by any studied mass models including DZ, WS4, FRDM2012, UNEDF1, SKP, and BSkG3, correlates with a predicted onset of quadrupole deformation at N>=92 and may be explained by the 9/2+[404] proton extruder orbital mechanism analogous to neutron extruder orbitals in the N=60 and N=90 regions. The results call for further spectroscopic studies to elucidate the structure of neutron-rich lanthanum isotopes and are relevant for r-process nucleosynthesis calculations.
