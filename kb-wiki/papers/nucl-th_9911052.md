# Parity splitting in the alternating parity bands of some actinide nuclei

> An angular momentum dependence of the parity splitting in the alternating parity bands of nuclei with strong octupole correlations is considered basing on the model of the octupole motion in a one-dimensional potential well conserving axial symmetry. A sign reversal of the parity splitting at higher values of the angular momentum is interpreted as a result of the Coriolis coupling to K=1 octupole mode. New experimental data on the spectra of the alternating parity octupole bands are used.

# Parity splitting in the alternating parity bands of some actinide nuclei

## Key claim
The angular momentum dependence of parity splitting in alternating parity bands of actinide nuclei can be described by combining two effects: barrier penetration in a one-dimensional octupole potential (which produces positive parity splitting at low angular momentum) and Coriolis coupling to the K=1 octupole mode (which produces a sign reversal to negative parity splitting at higher angular momentum). The model is phenomenological and the treatment of the sign reversal is qualitative, requiring a more detailed microscopic approach for full confirmation.

## Method
The authors model octupole motion in a one-dimensional potential well with two minima symmetrically located at positive and negative values of the axially symmetric octupole deformation parameter a₃₀. The barrier height depends on angular momentum through a rotational energy term, and the parity splitting from barrier penetration is parameterized as an exponentially decreasing function of I(I+1). Coriolis coupling between K=0 and K=1 intrinsic states is treated using coherent state wave functions for the octupole phonons, with the K=1 phonon energy ω fixed at 1050 keV for all nuclei. The model contains five parameters, of which two (ω and n) are fixed for all nuclei, leaving three free parameters per nucleus to fit experimental data.

## Key numbers
- Parity splitting at the beginning of rotational bands is several hundreds of keV; even in octupole-deformed nuclei the splitting is 200–300 keV.
- The negative parity splitting at higher angular momentum is 5–10 times smaller than the positive parity splitting observed at the beginning of the rotational band.
- For ²²⁴Ra and ²²⁶Ra, the K=1 octupole phonon energy ω is known: 1053 keV for ²²⁴Ra and 1048 keV for ²²⁶Ra; the authors take ω = 1050 keV for all nuclei considered.
- The parameter n (average number of K=0 octupole bosons in even-I states at large deformation) is taken as n = 3 for all nuclei.
- The parameter b in the expressions for n±(I) is taken as b = 0.15; using b = 0.25 does not change the fit essentially.
- The fitted values of the Coriolis strength parameter give J₀ not exceeding 1.1 for all nuclei, with a minimum of J₀ = 0.6 for ²²⁶Ra.
- The attenuation factor of the Coriolis interaction takes values between 0.28 and 0.44 across the fitted nuclei.
- Using a common attenuation factor of 0.33 for all nuclei, deviations from the best fit are: up to 20 keV for ²²⁰Ra, up to 6 keV for ²²⁶Ra, and up to 5 keV for ²²²Th.
- The parameter Δ̃E(0) (parity splitting at zero angular momentum from barrier penetration alone) takes values between 200 keV and 300 keV for the nuclei studied.
- In ²²⁶Ra, E(2⁺₁) = 68 keV; in ²²⁰Ra, E(2⁺₁) = 178 keV.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous model (Jolos et al., 1997–1999) which described positive parity splitting at low to moderate angular momenta using a one-dimensional octupole potential with two minima. The new contribution is the inclusion of Coriolis coupling to K=1 octupole modes to explain the sign reversal of parity splitting at higher angular momentum, an effect previously discussed in the context of odd-A nuclei (Leander, 1987; Brink, 1987). The model is applied to new experimental data from the Liverpool group (Cocks, 1999) on ²²⁰,²²²,²²⁴,²²⁶Ra and ²²²,²²⁴Th. The authors note that microscopic models with parity projection, such as those by the Madrid group (Egido, 1999), can obtain parity splitting naturally. The paper enables future work on more detailed microscopic treatments and suggests that Coriolis mixing among several intrinsic states may be needed to explain cases where parity splitting changes sign a second time (Mikhailov, 1999).
