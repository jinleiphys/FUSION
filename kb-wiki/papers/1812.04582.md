# Impact of $^{16}$O($γ$,$α$)$^{12}$C measurements on the $^{12}$C($α,γ$)$^{16}$O astrophysical reaction rate

> The $^{12}$C($α,γ$)$^{16}$O reaction, an important component of stellar helium burning, plays a key role in nuclear astrophysics. It has direct impact on the evolution and final state of massive stars, while also influencing the elemental abundances resulting from nucleosynthesis in such stars. Providing a reliable estimate for the energy dependence of this reaction at stellar helium burning temperatures has been a major goal for the field. In this work, we study the role of potential new measurements of the inverse reaction, $^{16}$O($γ,α$)$^{12}$C, in reducing the overall uncertainty. A multilevel R-matrix analysis is used to make extrapolations of the astrophysical S factor for this reaction to the stellar energy of 300 keV. The statistical precision of the S-factor extrapolation is determined by performing multiple fits to existing E1 and E2 ground state capture data, including the impact of possible future measurements of the $^{16}$O($γ,α$)$^{12}$C reaction. In particular, we consider a proposed JLab experiment that will make use of a high-intensity low-energy bremsstrahlung beam that impinges on an oxygen-rich single-fluid bubble chamber in order to measure the total cross section for the inverse reaction. The importance of low energy data as well as high precision data is investigated.

# Impact of $^{16}$O($\gamma$,$\alpha$)$^{12}$C measurements on the $^{12}$C($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{16}$O astrophysical reaction rate

## Key claim

New measurements of the inverse $^{16}$O($\gamma$,$\alpha$)$^{12}$C reaction, such as a proposed JLab experiment using a high-intensity low-energy bremsstrahlung beam on an oxygen-rich bubble chamber, can reduce the statistical uncertainty in the extrapolated astrophysical S-factor for $^{12}$C($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{16}$O at 300 keV, but the reduction is modest unless the projected JLab statistical errors are reduced by at least a factor of two. The impact is most significant when low-energy data (below 1.6 MeV) are included, as fits using only data above 1.6 MeV produce systematically higher S-factor projections.

## Method

A multilevel R-matrix analysis was performed using five E1 resonance levels and four E2 resonance levels, fitting existing ground-state capture S-factor data from multiple experiments (Dyer 1974, Kremer 1988, Redder 1987, Ouellet 1992, Roters 1999, Gialanella 2001, Kunz 2001, Assuncao 2006, Makii 2009, Plag 2012). The authors generated pseudo-data by random Gaussian variation about the best fit, then performed 1000 fits with and without projected JLab $^{16}$O($\gamma$,$\alpha$)$^{12}$C data points at energies from 590 keV to 1190 keV, using an L-maximization procedure rather than chi-squared minimization to reduce the impact of large-error data. Fits were performed for two channel radii (5.43 fm and 6.5 fm) to test model dependence.

## Key numbers

- Total S-factor at 300 keV from fit to all existing E1+E2 data with 5.43 fm channel radius: 112.3 keV-b, with standard deviation 7.2 keV-b
- Total S-factor at 300 keV from fit to all data plus projected JLab data (5.43 fm): 113.5 keV-b, with standard deviation 6.1 keV-b
- Total S-factor at 300 keV from fit to data published after year 2000 (5.43 fm): 123.5 keV-b, with standard deviation 6.9 keV-b
- Total S-factor at 300 keV from fit to all data with 6.5 fm channel radius: 124.9 keV-b, with standard deviation 8.3 keV-b
- Total S-factor at 300 keV from fit to all data plus projected JLab data (6.5 fm): 121.7 keV-b, with standard deviation 6.3 keV-b
- Reduced chi-squared for fit to all data: 2.3; for fit to post-2000 data: 1.7 (5.43 fm) or 1.6 (6.5 fm)
- Bound p-wave reduced width amplitude from all-data fit (6.5 fm): 0.097 (+0.006/-0.005) MeV^(1/2)
- Quantity P1*gamma11^2 from all-data fit (6.5 fm): 4.68 (+0.58/-0.48) micro-eV
- Bound d-wave reduced width amplitude from all-data fit (6.5 fm): 0.150(9) MeV^(1/2)

## Context

This work extends the comprehensive R-matrix analysis of deBoer (2017) by specifically quantifying how proposed inverse-reaction measurements could reduce extrapolation uncertainties for the $^{12}$C($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{16}$O reaction, which is critical for stellar helium burning and massive star evolution. The results show that low-energy inverse-reaction data (below 1.6 MeV) are essential for constraining the extrapolation, and that high-precision data could help resolve the model dependence seen between different channel radius choices (5.43 fm vs 6.5 fm). The bound-state reduced widths are compared with results from $^{16}$N beta-delayed alpha decay (Kirsebom 2018) and transfer reactions (Shen 2018), showing consistency for the p-wave state but some tension for the d-wave state. The work enables future experimental programs at JLab and other laboratories to optimize their measurement strategies for maximum impact on the astrophysical S-factor uncertainty.
