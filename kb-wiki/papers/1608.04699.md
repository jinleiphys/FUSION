# Corrections to nucleon capture cross sections computed in truncated Hilbert spaces

> Nucleon capture cross sections enter various astrophysical processes. The measurement of proton capture on nuclei at astrophysically relevant low energies is a challenge, and theoretical computations in this long-wavelength regime are sensitive to the long-distance asymptotics of the wave functions. A theoretical foundation for estimating and correcting errors introduced in capture cross sections due to Hilbert space truncation has so far been lacking. We derive extrapolation formulas that relate the infrared regularized capture amplitudes to the infinite basis limit and demonstrate their efficacy for proton-proton fusion. Our results are thus relevant to current calculations of few-body capture reactions such as proton-proton fusion or proton capture on the deuteron, and they also open the way for the use of {\it ab initio} many-body wave functions represented in finite Hilbert spaces in precision calculations of nucleon capture on heavier nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper derives extrapolation formulas that relate infrared-regularized capture amplitudes computed in finite Hilbert spaces to the infinite-basis limit, but these formulas are asymptotically valid only for large box sizes \(L\) and require additional conditions such as \(kL \gg \eta\) for proton capture and \(kL \gg l\) for higher partial waves.

## Method

The authors split the radial matrix element for capture reactions into an inner region (where the nuclear interaction dominates) and an outer region (where asymptotic forms of the bound and scattering wave functions are valid). They analytically evaluate the outer-region integral using the known asymptotic form of the bound-state wave function in a finite box and the asymptotic form of the scattering wave function, then derive the infrared truncation error as the difference between the infinite-basis and finite-basis matrix elements. The resulting extrapolation formulas are tested by comparing analytic predictions to numerical calculations for proton-proton fusion and neutron-proton capture using a chiral effective field theory interaction, with the bound-state deuteron wave function expanded in harmonic oscillator bases of varying size.

## Key numbers

- At \(L = 35\) fm, the relative error in the axial-vector matrix element for \(pp\) fusion at 50 keV center-of-mass energy is about 0.3%, which translates to an error in the cross section of about 0.6%.
- At the same \(L = 35\) fm, the relative error in the deuteron binding energy is about \(0.5 \times 10^{-6}\) (0.5 parts per million).
- The infrared length for the harmonic oscillator basis is given by \(L = \sqrt{2(N + 3/2 + 2)}\,b\), where \(N\) is the maximum oscillator quanta and \(b\) is the oscillator length.
- The infrared length for the hyperspherical harmonics basis is \(L_{\rm HH} = (4n + 2a + 6)\beta^{-1}\), where \(n\) is the largest degree of the Laguerre polynomial and \(a\) is a parameter.
- Extrapolation fits for \(\mathcal{I}_0(k;\eta;\infty)\) at 50 keV (\(\eta = 0.50\)) yield values around 0.4711 fm\(^{1/2}\), agreeing with the numerically-approximated infinite-basis value; at 1 MeV (\(\eta = 0.11\)) the extrapolated value is 2.592 fm\(^{1/2}\).

## Context

This work extends the framework of infrared corrections for bound-state observables developed by Furnstahl et al. (2012), More et al. (2013), and Odell et al. (2016) to the case of nucleon capture cross sections, which are essential inputs for big bang nucleosynthesis and stellar models. The authors show that capture cross sections converge much more slowly (as \(e^{-\gamma_\infty L}\)) than bound-state energies and radii (which converge as \(e^{-2\gamma_\infty L}\)), making these corrections relevant for precision calculations such as those by Marcucci et al. (2013) that aim for sub-percent accuracy in \(pp\) fusion. The results enable the use of ab initio many-body wave functions from finite Hilbert spaces for precision calculations of nucleon capture on heavier nuclei, though the authors note that for heavy nuclei at low energies the full Coulomb wave functions must replace the asymptotic sine/cosine forms used here.
