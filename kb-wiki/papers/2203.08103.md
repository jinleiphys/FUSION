# Electric dipole moments and the search for new physics

> Static electric dipole moments of nondegenerate systems probe mass scales for physics beyond the Standard Model well beyond those reached directly at high energy colliders. Discrimination between different physics models, however, requires complementary searches in atomic-molecular-and-optical, nuclear and particle physics. In this report, we discuss the current status and prospects in the near future for a compelling suite of such experiments, along with developments needed in the encompassing theoretical framework.

## Key claim

Static electric dipole moments of nondegenerate systems probe mass scales for physics beyond the Standard Model well beyond those reached directly at high energy colliders, but discrimination between different physics models requires complementary searches in atomic-molecular-and-optical, nuclear and particle physics.

## Method

The paper reviews the current status and near-future prospects for experiments searching for CP-violation via electric dipole moments (EDMs) in atoms and molecules, along with developments needed in the encompassing theoretical framework. It describes how CPV effects are revealed by measuring energy shifts when an electric field is applied, with the observable Hamiltonian proportional to the dot product of an intra-atomic/molecular angular momentum and a polar vector parallel to the applied field, and discusses the mechanisms by which Schiff screening is circumvented in paramagnetic and diamagnetic systems.

## Key numbers

- The ACME experiment using ThO currently sets the most sensitive electron EDM limit at |d_e| < 1.1 × 10⁻²⁹ e·cm
- The JILA HfF⁺ experiment set a limit of |d_e| < 1.3 × 10⁻²⁸ e·cm in its first generation result
- In atoms, full polarization would require fields of ~1 GV/cm, but the largest continuously applicable lab fields are ~300 kV/cm, giving typical atomic polarization P(E) ~ 10⁻⁴ [E/(100 kV/cm)]
- In molecules, external applied fields of ~100 kV/cm (or much smaller) are sufficient to reach |P| ≈ 1, yielding CPV-induced energy shifts 10³–10⁴ times larger than in atoms
- A proof-of-principle measurement with ~500 ²²⁵Ra atoms achieved 20 s spin coherence time with an electric field of 67 kV/cm
- The ¹⁹⁹Hg experiment at the University of Washington used vapor cells containing ~10¹⁵ atoms with E ~ 10 kV/cm (polarization P ~ 10⁻⁴) and over 100 s coherence time
- Coherence in matrix-isolated atoms has been achieved for 0.1 s
- For paramagnetic systems, sensitivity scaling is roughly S_at(Z) ~ Z³; for diamagnetic systems, S_at(Z) ∝ Z² for the NSM, with NSM itself scaling roughly as S ∝ Z²/³
- For the MQM in paramagnetic systems, S_at(Z) ∝ Z²; for quadrupole-deformed nuclei, MQM enhancement scales roughly as M ∝ Z²/³ with typical enhancement factors of 10–20
- NSMs are dramatically enhanced by a factor of typically ~300 in octupole-deformed nuclei
- Heavy molecular species with deformed nuclei can be up to ~10⁷ times more intrinsically sensitive than the current state of the art
- The ¹²⁹Xe experiments have scaling factors and intrinsic sensitivities a factor of ~6 smaller than in ¹⁹⁹Hg, use ~3 times smaller E-fields, and measure spin precession with uncertainty ~60 times less precise

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes the work of many prior experimental and theoretical efforts, including the ACME collaboration (2018), the JILA HfF⁺ experiment (Cairncross 2017), the Imperial College YbF experiment (Hudson 2011), the University of Washington ¹⁹⁹Hg experiment (Graner 2016), and the Tl atomic EDM experiment (Regan 2002). It builds on the theoretical framework for Schiff screening and its loopholes developed by Schiff (1963), Sandars (1964, 1965), Flambaum and Ginges (2002, 2004), and others. The paper enables a coordinated global program of complementary AMO, nuclear, and particle physics searches to discriminate between different new physics models, and identifies specific pathways for orders-of-magnitude improvement via laser cooling and trapping of molecules, matrix isolation techniques, and use of radioactive species with octupole-deformed nuclei.
