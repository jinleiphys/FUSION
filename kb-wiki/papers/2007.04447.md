# An Algorithm for Quantum Computation of Particle Decays

> A quantum algorithm is developed to calculate decay rates and cross sections using quantum resources that scale polynomially in the system size assuming similar scaling for state preparation and time evolution. This is done by computing finite-volume one- and two-particle Green's functions on the quantum hardware. Particle decay rates and two particle scattering cross sections are extracted from the imaginary parts of the Green's function. A $0+1$ dimensional implementation of this method is demonstrated on IBM's superconducting quantum hardware for the decay of a heavy scalar particle to a pair of light scalars.

## Key claim

A quantum algorithm can compute particle decay rates and scattering cross sections from finite-volume Green's functions using resources that scale polynomially in system size, provided that state preparation and time evolution also scale polynomially; the method is demonstrated on IBM's superconducting hardware for a 0+1 dimensional scalar decay, but the extracted decay rates agree with ideal calculations only after error mitigation and with large remaining uncertainties.

## Method

The algorithm computes the forward matrix element of the Green's function, expressed as an integral of the time-evolved overlap of the initial state, using the optical theorem to relate the imaginary part of the T-matrix to the inclusive decay rate. For a 1+1 dimensional scalar theory with a heavy particle decaying to two light particles, the Green's function is evaluated on lattices of 3, 5, and 7 sites with periodic boundary conditions, using classical matrix inversion to simulate the quantum algorithm. A 0+1 dimensional truncation of the same theory is implemented on IBM's Ourense quantum processor using the Hadamard test to measure the time-evolved overlap, with Trotterized time evolution and error mitigation by extrapolation to zero CNOT gate error.

## Key numbers

* Heavy particle mass set to 2.01 times the light particle mass, ensuring only the phi -> 2 chi decay channel is allowed.
* Lattice spacing a = 0.2 m^{-1}, where m is the light particle mass.
* For the 0+1 dimensional demonstration on IBM hardware: time step Delta t = 0.2 m^{-1}, with k = 1, 2, ..., 96 time slices; each circuit used 36 single-qubit gates and 28 CNOT gates, sampled 8000 times.
* For coupling g = 0.5: ideal decay rate Gamma = 0.070 m, extracted Gamma = (0.099 +/- 0.037) m.
* For coupling g = 1.0: ideal decay rate Gamma = 0.287 m, extracted Gamma = (0.286 +/- 0.047) m.
* Gate count scaling for computing Gamma to accuracy delta Gamma: O( log(2 delta Gamma / Gamma) / delta Gamma^3 * Gamma^2 * (2E + delta Gamma) * p(2/delta Gamma * log(sqrt(2) Gamma / delta Gamma), (delta Gamma/Gamma)^2 ) ), with lattice size scaling as O(1/delta Gamma * log(1/delta Gamma)) for theories with a mass gap.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Jordan, Lee, and Preskill (2011, 2014) for simulating scalar field theories on quantum computers, applying it specifically to extract decay rates from Green's functions rather than from direct time evolution with detector operators. It contrasts with Luscher's method (1985, 1986) used in lattice QCD, which relates decay rates to finite-volume energy shifts and becomes difficult for excited states and large lattices. The algorithm enables computation of decay rates and cross sections for processes beyond the reach of classical methods, and the author notes that comparing results from this Green's function approach with direct time evolution will allow higher-confidence determinations.
