# X-ray views of neutron star low-mass X-ray binaries

> A neutron star low-mass X-ray binary is a binary stellar system with a neutron star and a low-mass companion star rotating around each other. In this system the neutron star accretes mass from the companion, and as this matter falls into the deep potential well of the neutron star, the gravitational potential energy is released primarily in the X-ray wavelengths. Such a source was first discovered in X-rays in 1962, and this discovery formally gave birth to the "X-ray astronomy". In the subsequent decades, our knowledge of these sources has increased enormously by the observations with several X-ray space missions. Here we give a brief overview of our current understanding of the X-ray observational aspects of these systems.

## Key claim

This paper is a review of X-ray observational aspects of neutron star low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs), summarizing the current understanding gained from past and current X-ray space missions. The author notes that the reference list will not be exhaustive in this short review, indicating it is a selective overview rather than a comprehensive survey.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of X-ray astronomy from the 1962 discovery of Scorpius X-1 through current missions (RXTE, XMM-Newton, Chandra, Suzaku), then describes the basic theory of geometrically thin accretion disks in Newtonian gravity following the Shakura & Sunyaev (1973) model. It presents selected observational features of neutron star LMXBs including outbursts, thermonuclear X-ray bursts (type-I and superbursts), type-II bursts, and dips/eclipses, discussing the physical mechanisms inferred from spectral and timing data.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star mass is about one solar mass ($M_{\odot}$) with a radius of $\sim 10$ km
- Core density of neutron stars is $5-10$ times the nuclear density
- Neutron star magnetic fields range between $10^7-10^{15}$ Gauss
- Neutron star spin frequencies in LMXBs are typically $\sim 300-600$ Hz
- The Chandrasekhar mass limit is about $1.4 M_{\odot}$
- The first discovered pulsar had a 1.377 sec period detected at 81.5 MHz
- 187 LMXBs (containing black hole or neutron star) were known from our galaxy, Large Magellanic Cloud and Small Magellanic Cloud according to the Liu et al. (2007) catalogue
- Optical to X-ray luminosity ratio for LMXBs is normally less than 0.1
- Thermonuclear bursts emit $\sim 10^{39}$ ergs in a few seconds
- Ignition occurs at a depth of $\approx 10$ m at a column density of $\sim 10^8$ gm cm$^{-2}$
- Accretion rate regime boundaries: \.{m} $ < 900$ gm cm$^{-2}$ sec$^{-1}$ ($Z_{\rm CNO}/0.01$)$^{1/2}$ for regime 1; \.{m} $> 2\times10^3$ gm cm$^{-2}$ sec$^{-1}$ ($Z_{\rm CNO}/0.01$)$^{13/18}$ for regime 3
- Superbursts release $\sim 10^{42}$ ergs with decay time $\sim 1-3$ hours
- About 15 superbursts have been detected from 10 sources (Cooper et al. 2009)
- Type-II burst energy from the rapid burster ranges from $\sim 1\times10^{38}$ ergs to $\sim 7\times10^{40}$ ergs

## Context

This review extends the observational understanding of neutron star LMXBs built from key X-ray missions starting with the 1962 discovery by Giacconi et al. (1962) and the first astronomy satellite Uhuru (1970). It builds on the theoretical framework of accretion disks from Shakura & Sunyaev (1973), thermonuclear burst theory from Joss (1977) and Lamb & Lamb (1978), and the work on neutron star structure and equation of state constraints by Lattimer & Prakash (2007). The paper enables future work on constraining neutron star parameters through spectral and timing observations, particularly using thermonuclear bursts to probe stellar radii and cooling curves, and studying thermonuclear flame spreading on rapidly spinning neutron stars as modeled by Spitkovsky et al. (2002).
