# General aspects of effective field theories and few-body applications

> Effective field theory provides a powerful framework to exploit a separation of scales in physical systems. In these lectures, we discuss some general aspects of effective field theories and their application to few-body physics. In particular, we consider an effective field theory for non-relativistic particles with resonant short-range interactions where certain parts of the interaction need to be treated nonperturbatively. As an application, we discuss the so-called pionless effective field theory for low-energy nuclear physics. The extension to include long-range interactions mediated by photon and pion-exchange is also addressed.

## Key claim

Effective field theory (EFT) provides a systematic and predictive framework for low-energy nuclear physics by exploiting a separation of scales, but the power counting scheme that orders the infinite number of terms in the Lagrangian must be bounded from below to define a sensible EFT, and certain interactions with bound states require nonperturbative resummation rather than simple perturbative expansion.

## Method

The authors present pedagogical derivations of EFT construction, starting from relativistic field theories and showing how integrating out heavy particles generates contact interactions among the remaining light degrees of freedom. They demonstrate the nonrelativistic reduction of both bosonic and fermionic fields, using a Foldy-Wouthuysen transformation for Dirac fermions to decouple particle and antiparticle modes, and illustrate how many-body forces emerge from sequential integration of heavy fields. The framework is then applied to non-relativistic bosons with short-range interactions and large scattering length, followed by extension to low-energy nucleons in pionless EFT and inclusion of long-range interactions from photon and pion exchange.

## Key numbers

* The expansion parameter in EFT is the ratio Q/Mhi, where Q is a typical low-energy momentum and Mhi is the hard scale where the EFT breaks down; the expansion takes the form M = sum_nu (Q/Mhi)^nu F(Q/Lambda, g_i)
* For the example of heavy-particle exchange between light particles, the leading-order scattering amplitude expansion is T ~ g^2/Mhi^2 + g^2 q^2/Mhi^4 + ... where g is the heavy-light coupling constant and q^2 is the squared 4-momentum transfer
* In the nonrelativistic reduction of a bosonic field, the kinetic energy is E_p = p^2/(2m) up to corrections of higher order in 1/m, valid when E_p << m
* The Foldy-Wouthuysen transformation for fermions is carried out up to quadratic order in 1/MN, where MN is the nucleon mass, with the first transformation using S = -i gamma^0 O/(2MN)

## Context

These lectures extend the pedagogical EFT literature by Weinberg (1978) and Lepage (1989), and build on the renormalization group framework of Wilson (1983). The work provides a foundation for applying EFT to few-body nuclear systems, particularly through pionless EFT for low-energy nucleons, and enables systematic calculations of nuclear observables with controlled error estimates. The discussion of nonrelativistic reduction via Foldy-Wouthuysen transformation connects to the heavy fermion formalism of Jenkins (1990) and its comparison by Gardestig (2007).
