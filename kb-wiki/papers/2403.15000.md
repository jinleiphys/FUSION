# Heavy multiquark systems as clusters of smaller units -- a diffusion Monte Carlo calculation --

> Multiquark systems appear less frequently than mesons and baryons despite the enormous world-wide experimental effort that has been made during the last two decades. In this work, we will propose a possible explanation for that fact, restricting ourselves to the case of sets including only $c$ and $\bar{c}$ quarks. We will show that those multiquarks can be thought as different combinations of smaller units that associate together to produce colorless assemblies with a definite value of the total spin. For instance, for the $cccccc$ hexaquark with $S=0$, we have three possibilities: a set of six undistinguishable $c$ quarks, an association of two $ccc$ baryons, or a set of three $cc$ diquarks close together. This means we can have three different values for the mass of an open-charm hexaquark with $S=0$. Using the diffusion Monte Carlo method, we calculate all possible combinations compatible with tetraquark $cc \bar{c} \bar{c}$, pentaquark $cccc \bar{c}$, open-charm $cccccc$ and hidden-charm $ccc \bar{c} \bar{c} \bar{c}$ hexaquark structures with the minimum value of total spin ($S=0$ or $S=1/2$). We consider compact structures with radial wave functions including interactions between all the quarks in the cluster. We find that, in all cases, the mass of the multiquark decreases with the number of small units that conform the set of quarks. For instance, an open charm hexaquark made up of three diquarks has a smaller mass than a set of six of $c$ undistinguishable units. When the pieces that conform the multiquark are themselves colorless with a definite value of the total spin, the cluster splits into those smaller units that separate infinitely from each other.

## Key claim

Multiquark systems made only of charm quarks and antiquarks can be thought of as different combinations of smaller colorless units (diquarks, baryons, mesons) that associate together; the mass of the multiquark decreases with the number of such smaller units, and when the constituent pieces are themselves colorless with definite total spin, the cluster splits into those smaller units that separate infinitely from each other.

## Method

The authors used a diffusion Monte Carlo (DMC) method to solve the many-body Schrodinger equation for fully-charmed tetraquark, pentaquark, open-charm hexaquark, and hidden-charm hexaquark systems with minimum total spin (S=0 or S=1/2). The Hamiltonian included a pairwise Coulomb plus linear-confining plus hyperfine spin-spin interaction (the AL1 potential from Semay (1994) and Silvestre-Brac (1996)), with parameters fitted to 36 mesons and 53 baryons. For each multiquark system, the authors constructed antisymmetric spin-color trial wave functions for different clustering assumptions (e.g., all quarks indistinguishable, diquark-diquark, baryon-meson, baryon-baryon) and evolved them in imaginary time to obtain ground-state masses and interquark mean-square radii.

## Context

This work extends the application of diffusion Monte Carlo methods to multiquark systems, following earlier studies of fully-heavy tetraquarks by Gordillo (2020) and others. The results provide a stability hierarchy among different clustering arrangements of charm quarks, showing that configurations which can dissociate into smaller colorless hadrons (mesons, baryons) are always more bound than compact multiquark clusters. This offers a possible explanation for why mesons and baryons have been overwhelmingly more abundant in experiments than multiquark states, despite extensive searches since the discovery of the X(3872) by Belle (2003). The work enables future studies of multiquark systems with light quarks and of the resonance properties of compact clusters that lie slightly above dissociation thresholds.
