# Longitudinal Dynamics of High Baryon Density Matter in High Energy Heavy-Ion Collisions

> In high energy heavy-ion collisions, the two colliding nuclei pass through each other leaving behind an almost baryon free central rapidity region. Most of the baryons are carried away by the nuclear remnants and are located in the so-called fragmentation regions. In previous papers \cite{Li:2016wzh,Li:2018ini}, it has been argued that very high baryon densities, more than ten times larger than the normal nuclear density, can be achieved in these fragmentation regions. In this paper, we assume the high baryon density matter is thermalized at the same time as the baryon-free quark-gluon plasma in the central rapidity region. We perform a 1+1D (temporal + longitudinal) hydrodynamic simulation covering both the fragmentation regions and the central rapidity region with the baryon diffusion equation included. Baryons are found to diffuse from the fragmentation regions to the central rapidity region driven by fugacity gradients. The baryon chemical potential at freezeout monotonically increases from the central rapidity region to the fragmentation regions, suggesting a rapidity scan in high energy heavy-ion collisions might be helpful in searching for the critical point of the QCD phase diagram.

## Key claim

The authors assume that the high baryon density matter in the fragmentation regions of high-energy heavy-ion collisions thermalizes at the same time as the baryon-free quark-gluon plasma in the central rapidity region, and they perform a 1+1D hydrodynamic simulation to show that baryons diffuse from the fragmentation regions toward the central region driven by fugacity gradients.

## Method

The authors performed a 1+1D (temporal plus longitudinal) relativistic hydrodynamic simulation using the Kurganov-Tadmor finite-volume scheme, solving the conservation equations for energy-momentum and baryon current together with a baryon diffusion equation (Cattaneo-type) in Milne coordinates. They initialized the simulation at proper time τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c for central Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV, using a crossover equation of state from Albright, Kapusta and Young (2014, 2015) that is valid at finite baryon chemical potential but contains no critical point or first-order phase transition. The baryon transport coefficient κ_B was taken from kinetic theory in the relaxation time approximation, with the relaxation time parameterized as τ_V = C_B/T with C_B = 0.4.

## Key numbers

- Initial energy density in the central rapidity region: ε(η=0) = 30 GeV/fm³ at τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c for central Au+Au at √s_NN = 200 GeV
- Initial proper baryon density in the fragmentation regions: approximately 3.0 baryons/fm³ at τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c, which is around 20 times larger than normal nuclear density
- Range of central boost-invariant region: η_f = 3.6 with Gaussian width σ_η = 0.1
- Initial total energy per transverse area: E^tot/S_A = 284.49 GeV/fm²
- Initial total baryon number per transverse area: N_B^tot/S_A = 4.2857 fm⁻²
- Initial total entropy per transverse area: S^tot/S_A = 339.16 fm⁻²
- Numerical grid spacing: Δη = 0.04 and Δτ = 0.02 fm/c
- Energy conservation violation: less than 0.1%; entropy conservation violation: less than 0.2%; baryon number violation: up to 2% at the ending time

## Context

This work extends the high baryon density initial conditions developed by Li (2016, 2018) for the fragmentation regions of high-energy heavy-ion collisions, which were based on the McLerran-Venugopalan model. It contrasts with the conventional Bjorken boost-invariant approach that ignores baryon content in the central rapidity region, and with the Beam Energy Scan program at RHIC (Adamczyk 2017) which lowers collision energy to achieve finite baryon chemical potential. The study enables future 3+1D hydrodynamic simulations that include baryon diffusion, and suggests that a rapidity scan in high-energy collisions could serve as an alternative experimental approach to search for the QCD critical point, complementing the energy scan approach at facilities like FAIR and NICA.
