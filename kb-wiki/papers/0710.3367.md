# Schiff Theorem Revisited

> We carefully rederive the Schiff theorem and prove that the usual expression of the Schiff moment operator is correct and should be applied for calculations of atomic electric dipole moments. The recently discussed corrections to the definition of the Schiff moment are absent.

# Schiff Theorem Revisited

## Key claim
The standard expression for the Schiff moment operator (Equation 1) is correct and should be used for calculations of atomic electric dipole moments; the recently proposed corrections involving two-body correlations in the Schiff moment operator are absent when the nuclear dipole moment is treated as a c-number expectation value rather than as an operator.

## Method
The authors rederive the Schiff theorem by performing a unitary transformation of the atomic Hamiltonian using a generator U proportional to the c-number expectation value of the nuclear electric dipole moment. They expand the nuclear charge density in gradients of the delta-function to extract the Schiff moment operator, and compare their derivation to the alternative approach of Haxthausen et al. (2007) where the nuclear dipole moment is treated as an operator.

## Key numbers
- The Schiff moment operator is given by: S_k = (1/10) ∫ (x² x_k - (5/3)⟨x²⟩_ch x_k - (2/3)⟨Q_kk'⟩ x_k') ρ(x) d³x
- For nuclei with spin I=1/2 (such as ¹⁹⁹Hg, ¹²⁹Xe, ²²⁵Ra), the quadrupole tensor expectation value ⟨Q_kk'⟩ = 0
- The hyperfine interaction contribution grows ∝ Z, while the Schiff moment contribution is ∝ Z² multiplied by a relativistic factor that is of order 1 for Z=1 and increases to 10 for Z=80
- The correction from the relativistic character of electron wave functions starts with term ∝ (Zα)²
- For strongly deformed nuclei with spin I>1/2, the quadrupole term correction does not exceed 20%

## Context
This paper reaffirms the standard Schiff moment formalism originally developed by Schiff (1963) and refined by Flambaum, Khriplovich, and Sushkov (1984), against recent criticisms by Haxthausen et al. (2007) who claimed additional two-body correlation terms appear. The work enables continued interpretation of atomic EDM experiments in ¹⁹⁹Hg (Griffin et al., 2001) and ¹²⁹Xe (Jacobs et al., 1995), and supports planning for future experiments with nuclei such as ²²⁵Ra. The authors note that relativistic corrections to the Schiff moment were already treated by Flambaum and Ginges (2002), and that hyperfine magnetic interaction effects (which dominate in light nuclei like hydrogen and helium) are negligible for the heavy nuclei of experimental interest.
