# Solver Recommendation For Transport Problems in Slabs Using Machine Learning

> The use of machine learning algorithms to address classification problems is on the rise in many research areas. The current study is aimed at testing the potential of using such algorithms to auto-select the best solvers for transport problems in uniform slabs. Three solvers are used in this work: Richardson, diffusion synthetic acceleration, and nonlinear diffusion acceleration. Three parameters are manipulated to create different transport problem scenarios. Five machine learning algorithms are applied: linear discriminant analysis, K-nearest neighbors, support vector machine, random forest, and neural networks. We present and analyze the results of these algorithms for the test problems, showing that random forest and K-nearest neighbors are potentially the best suited candidates for this type of classification problem.

## Key claim

Machine learning algorithms can auto-select the best solver for uniform-slab transport problems with high accuracy, but the study is limited to three solvers (Richardson, DSA, NDA) and three problem parameters (spatial mesh, S_N order, scattering ratio) in a one-speed, steady-state, isotropic-scattering slab; the authors state this is a "preliminary work" and that validity "needs to be investigated with more complex transport problems."

## Method

The authors solved the one-speed, steady-state, slab-geometry transport equation with isotropic scattering and a uniform isotropic source for 4,545 combinations of three features: five S_N orders (2, 4, 8, 16, 32), nine spatial mesh refinements (2^i cells for i=2..10), and 101 scattering ratios (c=0 to 1.0 in steps of 0.01). For each combination they ran three solvers (Richardson iteration, diffusion synthetic acceleration, and nonlinear diffusion acceleration) and labeled the "best" solver by two criteria: fewest transport sweeps and shortest runtime to a tolerance of 10^-5. They then trained five classifiers (linear discriminant analysis, K-nearest neighbors, support vector machine, neural networks, random forest) on this labeled dataset using 4-fold cross-validation with 25 repetitions, evaluating performance by accuracy and Cohen's kappa statistic.

## Key numbers

* Random forest achieved the highest average accuracy of 0.972 (standard deviation 0.005) and kappa of 0.946 (0.010) when classifying best solver by iteration count.
* Random forest achieved accuracy of 0.960 (0.005) and kappa of 0.915 (0.011) when classifying best solver by runtime.
* K-nearest neighbors was the second-best model in both scenarios, with accuracy 0.919 (iteration) and 0.952 (runtime), and was about 15 times faster than random forest (10.88 s vs 181.37 s for iteration; 10.59 s vs 151.11 s for runtime).
* Linear discriminant analysis was the fastest model (2.39 s for iteration, 2.25 s for runtime) but the worst performer, with accuracy 0.612 (iteration) and 0.876 (runtime).
* In the random forest model, the scattering ratio had the highest variable importance (mean decrease in Gini impurity = 976.24), followed by number of cells (739.83) and S_N order (483.79).
* Richardson iteration was the best solver in less than 1% of cases by iteration count; DSA was best in more than 68% of cases by runtime.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts in computer science and mathematics on using machine learning to choose optimal solvers for linear systems (Bhowmick, Xu, and others), but is the first to specifically target particle transport problems. The authors note that no prior study had addressed transport solvers. The results enable future work on more complex transport problems with additional features and solvers, and suggest that random forest and K-nearest neighbors are the most promising algorithms for building solver recommender systems in this domain.
