# Grand Canonical Model Predictions For Nuclear Fragmentation

> The grand canonical ensemble has been used to make predictions for composite yields using simple models for nuclear fragmentation. While this gives correct model prediction for high energy collisions, it can give very inaccurate results at intermediate energy.

# Grand Canonical Model Predictions For Nuclear Fragmentation

## Key claim

The grand canonical ensemble (GCM) can give very inaccurate predictions for composite yields in nuclear multifragmentation at intermediate energies (around 4 MeV temperature), even for large systems with A=200, although it becomes accurate at high temperatures (above 20 MeV). The canonical model (CM), which strictly enforces particle number conservation, is shown to be the correct approach when thermal and chemical equilibrium is assumed, and its approximations to quantum statistics remain valid at low temperatures.

## Method

The authors compare fragment yields calculated using the grand canonical ensemble (GCM) with those from the canonical model (CM) for two nuclear systems: A=200, Z=80 and A=50, Z=25. The CM partition function is computed using a recursion relation that enforces exact conservation of proton and neutron numbers. The authors then verify the accuracy of the CM's Maxwell-Boltzmann approximation by comparing with an exact calculation that includes proper Fermi and Bose statistics for a system with Z=25, N=25 at temperatures from 3 to 30 MeV, using experimental binding energies for light composites and empirical mass formulas for heavier nuclei.

## Key numbers

- For A=200, Z=80 at T=4 MeV: GCM and CM predictions for isotope yields are "significantly different" (shown in Fig. 1)
- For A=50, Z=25 at T=4 MeV: GCM and CM predictions again show large discrepancies (shown in Fig. 2)
- GCM and CM predictions are "quite close" for temperatures T >= 20 MeV
- Exact calculation for Z=25, N=25 at T=3 MeV: approximate CM yields for protons (0.307) vs exact (0.306), for neutrons (0.032 vs 0.031), for deuterons (0.050 vs 0.051), for 4He (0.679 vs 0.696), and for Z>12 (0.945 vs 0.945)
- At T=6 MeV: approximate CM yields for protons (1.174) vs exact (1.117), for neutrons (0.898 vs 0.856), for deuterons (1.177 vs 1.195), for 4He (2.489 vs 2.573)
- At T=30 MeV: approximate CM yields for protons (10.937) vs exact (10.512), for neutrons (10.893 vs 10.468), for deuterons (7.664 vs 7.885), for 4He (0.379 vs 0.395)
- Energy per nucleon at T=3 MeV: approximate (-7.863 MeV) vs exact (-7.861 MeV); at T=30 MeV: approximate (28.914 MeV) vs exact (28.844 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the canonical model calculations for nuclear multifragmentation that became computationally feasible through recursion relations developed by Chase and Mekjian (1994) and applied by Das Gupta and Mekjian (1998) and Bhattacharyya, Das Gupta, and Mekjian (1999). The paper directly contradicts the widespread historical use of the grand canonical ensemble for intermediate-energy collisions, as employed by Mekjian (1977), Gosset, Kapusta, and Westfall (1978), and Das Gupta and Mekjian (1981), and specifically questions its use at lower beam energies by Pal et al. (1995) and Das et al. (1996). The results enable reliable use of the canonical model for studying first-order phase transitions in nuclear matter, where the temperature at which the rise in yields after a minimum disappears signals the transition, and where the specific heat at constant volume differs dramatically between GCM and CM as shown by Das, Das Gupta, and Mekjian (2003).
