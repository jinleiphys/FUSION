# SNIF: A Futuristic Neutrino Probe for Undeclared Nuclear Fission Reactors

> Today reactor neutrino experiments are at the cutting edge of fundamental research in particle physics. Understanding the neutrino is far from complete, but thanks to the impressive progress in this field over the last 15 years, a few research groups are seriously considering that neutrinos could be useful for society. The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) works with its Member States to promote safe, secure and peaceful nuclear technologies. In a context of international tension and nuclear renaissance, neutrino detectors could help IAEA to enforce the Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons (NPT). In this article we discuss a futuristic neutrino application to detect and localize an undeclared nuclear reactor from across borders. The SNIF (Secret Neutrino Interactions Finder) concept proposes to use a few hundred thousand tons neutrino detectors to unveil clandestine fission reactors. Beyond previous studies we provide estimates of all known background sources as a function of the detector's longitude, latitude and depth, and we discuss how they impact the detectability.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a detector containing 10^34 free protons (approximately 138,000 tons of liquid scintillator) could detect undeclared nuclear fission reactors at distances of 100 to 500 km, but only if operated at sufficient depth to suppress cosmogenic backgrounds and if the analysis threshold is raised to 2.6 MeV visible energy to eliminate accidental backgrounds.

## Method

The authors modeled the expected neutrino signal from a clandestine reactor with thermal powers between 10 MW_th and 2 GW_th, using inverse beta decay detection on free protons in a liquid scintillator detector. They computed all known neutrino backgrounds from the world's 201 commercial nuclear power stations (1,134 GW_th total thermal power) and geoneutrinos from the Earth's crust and mantle, then added non-neutrino backgrounds including accidental coincidences, fast neutrons, and cosmogenic isotopes (^9Li/^8He) as a function of detector depth. Background rates were rescaled from measurements by KamLAND and Borexino experiments to the SNIF detector scale.

## Key numbers

- A 100 MW_th reactor at 100 km distance would produce 450 events per year in a 10^34 proton detector without oscillations, but only 250 events per year with neutrino oscillations included
- The baseline detector contains 10^34 free protons, corresponding to 138,000 tons of linearalkylbenzene liquid scintillator in a volume of 160,000 m^3
- The analysis threshold is set at E_vis > 2.6 MeV, which reduces signal statistics by 28% but eliminates accidental background contributions
- At 500 m water depth the muon rate is 560 Hz with 11% dead time; at 4,000 m depth the muon rate drops to 0.3 Hz with 0.006% dead time
- The cosmogenic ^9Li/^8He background rate extrapolated from KamLAND to SNIF is 85.9 +/- 12.2 events per 10^34 H.year at 2,500 m depth with 0.9 MeV threshold

## Context

This work extends earlier far-field reactor monitoring concepts by Learned (1979) and Guillian (2006) that proposed using gigaton-scale water Cherenkov detectors in deep oceans. The SNIF concept provides a comprehensive treatment of all known background sources as a function of detector position and depth, which previous studies did not include. The paper enables future feasibility assessments for neutrino-based nuclear non-proliferation monitoring, identifying that cosmogenic backgrounds from ^9Li/^8He drive the required operating depth to approximately 3,500 m, and that commercial reactor neutrino backgrounds create a geographical asymmetry favoring detection in the southern hemisphere where fewer power stations exist.
