# Numerical Solution of Altarelli-Parisi Equations

> We discuss numerical solution of Altarelli-Parisi equations in a Laguerre-polynomial method and in a brute-force method. In the Laguerre method, we get good accuracy by taking about twenty Laguerre polynomials in the flavor-nonsinglet case. Excellent evolution results are obtained in the singlet case by taking only ten Laguerre polynomials. The accuracy becomes slightly worse in the small and large $x$ regions, especially in the nonsinglet case. These problems could be implemented by using the brute-force method; however, running CPU time could be significantly longer than the one in the Laguerre method.

## Key claim

The Laguerre-polynomial method provides excellent accuracy for solving the Altarelli-Parisi equations with only ten polynomials in the flavor-singlet case, but the accuracy degrades in the nonsinglet case at very small (x less than 0.001) and very large (x greater than 0.9) momentum fractions, where a brute-force method can recover accuracy at the cost of significantly longer CPU time.

## Method

The authors solve the leading-order and next-to-leading-order Altarelli-Parisi integrodifferential equations using two approaches: a Laguerre-polynomial expansion method, which transforms the evolution equations into sums over a finite number of expansion coefficients, and a brute-force method that discretizes the evolution variable t and the momentum fraction x into small steps (with x divided into equal steps on a logarithmic scale). The Laguerre method is applied to both flavor-nonsinglet and singlet quark distributions, as well as to the gluon distribution, using the HMRS-B parton distributions at Q² = 4 GeV² as initial conditions and evolving to Q² = 200 GeV² in the MS-bar renormalization scheme.

## Key numbers

- In the flavor-nonsinglet case, good accuracy is obtained by taking about twenty Laguerre polynomials.
- In the flavor-singlet case, excellent evolution results are obtained by taking only ten Laguerre polynomials.
- The accuracy becomes slightly worse in the nonsinglet case for x less than 0.001 and x greater than 0.9.
- In the brute-force method, numerical convergence is achieved by taking N_x approximately equal to 200 grid points in x, with N_t = 100 points in the evolution variable t.
- Typical running time is a few seconds on SUN-IPX or VAX-4000/500 for obtaining a single x-distribution curve using the Laguerre method.

## Context

This work extends the Laguerre-polynomial approach previously developed by the same group (Kobayashi, Konuma, Kumano, 1994) for nonsinglet evolution (program LAG2NS) to the singlet case, and compares it with a brute-force method from earlier literature (referred to as BRUTE). The results enable efficient Q² evolution calculations for testing perturbative QCD against experimental data, such as the CDHSW neutrino data and CCFR F₃ distributions, and provide a practical tool for analyzing scaling violation in nucleon structure functions.
