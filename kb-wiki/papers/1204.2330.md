# Multimessengers from core-collapse supernovae: multidimensionality as a key to bridge theory and observation

> Core-collapse supernovae are dramatic explosions marking the catastrophic end of massive stars. The only means to get direct information about the supernova engine is from observations of neutrinos emitted by the forming neutron star, and through gravitational waves which are produced when the hydrodynamic flow or the neutrino flux is not perfectly spherically symmetric. The multidimensionality of the supernova engine, which breaks the sphericity of the central core such as convection, rotation, magnetic fields, and hydrodynamic instabilities of the supernova shock, is attracting great attention as the most important ingredient to understand the long-veiled explosion mechanism. Based on our recent work, we summarize properties of gravitational waves, neutrinos, and explosive nucleosynthesis obtained in a series of our multidimensional hydrodynamic simulations and discuss how the mystery of the central engines can be unraveled by deciphering these multimessengers produced under the thick veils of massive stars.

## Key claim

Multidimensional hydrodynamic simulations of core-collapse supernovae show that the breaking of spherical symmetry through convection, rotation, magnetic fields, and hydrodynamic instabilities is the essential ingredient for understanding the explosion mechanism, but the authors caution that current simulation results depend on next-generation calculations with more sophisticated neutrino transport and general relativistic treatments.

## Method

The authors performed a series of multidimensional (2D and 3D) hydrodynamic simulations of core-collapse supernovae using the isotropic diffusion source approximation (IDSA) scheme for spectral neutrino transport, implemented in the ZEUS code. They compared non-rotating and rapidly rotating progenitor models, specifically a 13 solar mass progenitor from Nomoto (1988) and an 11.2 solar mass progenitor from Woosley, Heger, and Weaver (2002), to study how standing-accretion-shock-instability (SASI), convection, and rotation affect neutrino-driven explosions and the resulting multimessenger signals.

## Key numbers

- For the 13 solar mass progenitor of Nomoto (1988), the 2D simulation showed explosion initiation at approximately 200 ms after bounce, with an explosion energy of 0.1 Bethe (10^50 erg) at 500 ms postbounce
- For the 11.2 solar mass progenitor of Woosley, Heger, and Weaver (2002), the 3D simulation showed explosion initiation at approximately 100 ms after bounce, with an explosion energy of 0.01 Bethe (10^51 erg) at 300 ms postbounce
- The 2D rotating 13 solar mass model produced a stronger explosion compared to the corresponding non-rotating model
- Current estimates predict one Galactic core-collapse supernova event every 40 plus or minus 10 years
- If a supernova occurs at Galactic center distance (approximately 10 kpc), about 10^5 antineutrino-electron events are estimated to be detectable by IceCube

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to understand the neutrino-heating mechanism for core-collapse supernovae, which was first proposed by Wilson (1985) and Bethe and Wilson (1985) but failed in spherically-symmetric (1D) simulations as shown by Rampp and Janka (2000), Liebendoerfer et al. (2001), Thompson et al. (2003), and Sumiyoshi et al. (2005). The authors compare their results with those from other major groups including the MPA group (Janka and Mueller), the Princeton+ group (Burrows, Murphy, Ott, Livne, Dessart), the Oak Ridge+ group (Bruenn, Mezzacappa, Messer), and the Basel group (Liebendoerfer, Thielemann). The work enables future multimessenger astronomy by providing predictions for gravitational wave signatures, neutrino signals, and explosive nucleosynthesis that can be tested with next-generation detectors such as Advanced LIGO/VIRGO (expected completion by 2015) and the Large-scale Cryogenic Gravitational-wave Telescope (LCGT, first data expected in 2016).
