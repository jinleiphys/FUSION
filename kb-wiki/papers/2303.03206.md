# Quasielastic (p, n) reactions described by a microscopic optical model based on the Gogny force

> In this work we want to study quasielastic (p, n) exchange reactions using a semi-microscopic optical model derived in a previous work [1] based on a nuclear matter approach where the real and imaginary parts are given by the first and second order terms, respectively, of the mass operator obtained by a Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculation using a G-matrix built up with an effective Gogny interaction. The study of these quasielastic reactions is performed within a Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) to evaluate the wave functions in the entrance and exit channels, which in turn are used to compute the transition matrix elements. This model, which is free of adjustable parameters, provide a reasonable good agreement with the considered experimental data, namely differential cross sections, analyzing powers and total cross sections, of different reactions spanned along the periodic table at several energies.

## Key claim

The semi-microscopic optical model based on the Gogny force (MOP-G), which contains no adjustable parameters fitted to scattering data, provides a "reasonable good agreement" with experimental differential cross sections for quasielastic (p,n) charge exchange reactions across several targets and energies, but the model's predictions for analyzing powers are poor and the total cross sections are underestimated at higher bombarding energies (35 and 45 MeV).

## Method

The authors used a Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) to compute transition matrix elements for quasielastic (p,n) reactions from target ground states to isobaric analog states. The distorted waves in the entrance and exit channels were obtained from a microscopic optical model potential (MOP-G) built from a Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculation using a G-matrix with the effective Gogny interaction, where the real and imaginary parts of the central potential come from first and second order terms of the mass operator. The isoscalar and isovector parts of the Lane potential were constructed from the proton and neutron optical potentials, and the transition amplitude was evaluated using the off-diagonal matrix element of the isovector potential.

## Key numbers

- For the $^{48}$Ca(p,n)$^{48}$Sc reaction at 35 MeV, the real part of the central isovector potential predicted by MOP-G has a central strength of about 17 MeV.
- Total cross sections for $^{48}$Ca(p,n)$^{48}$Sc: MOP-G gives 10.92 mb at 25 MeV (experiment: 10.6 ± 1.2 mb), 7.45 mb at 35 MeV (experiment: 10.2 ± 1.1 mb), and 6.09 mb at 45 MeV (experiment: 8.4 ± 1.0 mb).
- Total cross sections for $^{90}$Zr(p,n)$^{90}$Nb: MOP-G gives 6.21 mb at 25 MeV (experiment: 6.7 ± 0.8 mb), 3.36 mb at 35 MeV (experiment: 4.8 ± 0.5 mb), and 2.28 mb at 45 MeV (experiment: 4.4 ± 0.5 mb).
- Total cross sections for $^{120}$Sn(p,n)$^{120}$Sb: MOP-G gives 8.97 mb at 25 MeV (experiment: 8.5 ± 1.0 mb), 4.62 mb at 35 MeV (experiment: 5.6 ± 0.6 mb), and 2.98 mb at 45 MeV (experiment: 5.8 ± 0.7 mb).
- Total cross sections for $^{208}$Pb(p,n)$^{208}$Bi: MOP-G gives 8.62 mb at 25 MeV (experiment: 9.6 ± 1.1 mb), 4.86 mb at 35 MeV (experiment: 6.8 ± 0.8 mb), and 2.57 mb at 45 MeV (experiment: 5.4 ± 0.6 mb).
- Analyzing powers were computed for $^{56}$Fe(p,n)$^{56}$Co, $^{64}$Ni(p,n)$^{64}$Cu, $^{70}$Zn(p,n)$^{70}$Ga, and $^{96}$Zr(p,n)$^{96}$Nb at 22.8 MeV and compared to experimental data from Gosset (1976).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic optical model (MOP-G) developed for nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering (Lopez 2021) to quasielastic charge exchange reactions, following the Lane formalism. The results are compared with the phenomenological Koning-Delaroche (KD) optical potential (Koning 2003) and with the microscopic optical potential of Whitehead, Lim, and Holt (WLH) based on chiral forces (Whitehead 2022). The study also connects to earlier phenomenological analyses of (p,n) reactions by Patterson (1975) and Doering (1975), and to the work of Danielewicz (2017) linking differential cross sections to nuclear symmetry energy. The poor description of analyzing powers and the too-strong decrease of total cross sections at higher energies indicate limits of optical models not fitted to charge exchange data, suggesting that renormalization factors or more sophisticated methods beyond the simple Lane model may be needed.
