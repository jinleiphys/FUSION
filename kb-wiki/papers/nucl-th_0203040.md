# Building light nuclei from neutrons, protons, and pions

> In these lectures I first explain, in a rather basic fashion, the construction of effective field theories. I then discuss some recent developments in the application of such theories to two- and three-nucleon systems.

## Key claim

These lectures present effective field theory (EFT) as a systematic approximation to underlying dynamics, valid in a specified regime, where the expansion parameter is a ratio of two physical scales; the approach is applied to two- and three-nucleon systems, with the claim that the EFT can make predictions of arbitrary accuracy in principle, provided the expansion converges.

## Method

The paper first explains the construction of effective field theories using simple examples (gravity near Earth's surface, hydrogen atom, light scattering from atoms, Fermi electroweak theory, and a scalar theory in 5+1 dimensions) to illustrate how a ratio of scales generates an expansion parameter and symmetries constrain corrections. It then applies the EFT framework to the nucleon-nucleon system, addressing the special issue of shallow bound states, and defines an EFT called EFT(π̸) that contains only neutrons and protons as dynamical degrees of freedom. The extension to the three-nucleon problem is discussed, drawing on work by Bedaque, Hammer, and van Kolck, and a brief tour of results in chiral perturbation theory (χPT) with pions is given.

## Key numbers

* The Bohr radius for hydrogen is approximately 0.5 Å, corresponding to an energy scale of about 4 keV, with the typical electron momentum ~1/r_b.
* The expansion parameter for finite-proton-size effects in hydrogen is r_p/r_b ~ 1 fm / 0.5 Å, with the first non-zero correction at order (r_p/r_b)^2.
* The Fermi constant G_F ~ 1/(100 GeV)^2, derived from the Z boson mass M_Z ~ 100 GeV.
* In the scalar theory in 5+1 dimensions, the coupling λ has dimension -2, so λ = a/Λ² with a of order one, and the leading-order threshold amplitude T_th^LO = a/Λ².

## Context

This paper extends the pedagogical tradition of Kaplan (1995) on effective field theory construction and provides an introduction to nuclear EFTs for two- and three-nucleon systems. It draws on the work of Bedaque, Hammer, and van Kolck for the three-body problem and references the reviews by van Kolck (1999) and Beane (2000) for comprehensive coverage. The lectures enable further application of EFT methods to light nuclei and electromagnetic reactions, and should be read in conjunction with the lectures of Leutwyler on chiral perturbation theory.
