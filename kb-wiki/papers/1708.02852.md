# Repulsive baryonic interactions and lattice QCD observables at imaginary chemical potential

> The first principle lattice QCD methods allow to calculate the thermodynamic observables at finite temperature and imaginary chemical potential. These can be compared to the predictions of various phenomenological models. We argue that Fourier coefficients with respect to imaginary baryochemical potential are sensitive to modeling of baryonic interactions. As a first application of this sensitivity, we consider the hadron resonance gas (HRG) model with repulsive baryonic interactions, which are modeled by means of the excluded volume correction. The Fourier coefficients of the imaginary part of the net-baryon density at imaginary baryochemical potential -- corresponding to the fugacity or virial expansion at real chemical potential -- are calculated within this model, and compared with the $N_t = 12$ lattice data. The lattice QCD behavior of the first four Fourier coefficients up to $T \simeq 185$ MeV is described fairly well by an interacting HRG with a single baryon-baryon eigenvolume interaction parameter $b \simeq 1$ fm$^3$, while the available lattice data on the difference $χ_2^B - χ_4^B$ of baryon number susceptibilities is reproduced up to $T \simeq 175$ MeV.

# Repulsive baryonic interactions and lattice QCD observables at imaginary chemical potential

## Key claim

Fourier coefficients of the net-baryon density at imaginary baryochemical potential are sensitive to the modeling of baryonic interactions, and the first four coefficients up to T ≃ 185 MeV are described fairly well by an interacting hadron resonance gas with a single baryon-baryon eigenvolume parameter b ≃ 1 fm³, while the difference χ₂ᴮ − χ₄ᴮ of baryon number susceptibilities is reproduced only up to T ≃ 175 MeV.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD simulations using a tree-level Symanzik improved gauge action with 2+1+1 flavors of four times stout smeared staggered quarks on 48³ × 12 lattices, generating configurations at imaginary baryochemical potential in the temperature range 135 ≤ T ≤ 230 MeV. They measured the imaginary part of the first-order net baryon susceptibility χ₁ᴮ and carried out a discrete Fourier transform to obtain the Fourier coefficients b₁, b₂, b₃, and b₄, which correspond to partial pressures from different baryon-number sectors. These lattice results were compared to the predictions of the hadron resonance gas model with excluded-volume repulsive interactions between baryon-baryon and antibaryon-antibaryon pairs, using a single eigenvolume parameter b.

## Key numbers

- The excluded-volume hadron resonance gas model with baryonic eigenvolume parameter b = 1 fm³ describes the first four Fourier coefficients up to T ≃ 185 MeV.
- The difference χ₂ᴮ − χ₄ᴮ of baryon number susceptibilities is reproduced by the EV-HRG model up to T ≃ 175 MeV.
- The second Fourier coefficient b₂ starts to notably deviate from zero at about T ≃ 160 MeV.
- The second-order truncation for calculating χ₂ᴮ − χ₄ᴮ works well up to T ≃ 150 MeV, the third order up to T ≃ 160 MeV, and the fourth order up to T ≃ 170 MeV.
- Lattice calculations were performed on 48³ × 12 lattices with roughly 1000-2000 configurations at each simulation point.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Vovchenko (2016) on van der Waals interactions between baryons in lattice QCD observables, applying similar ideas to observables at imaginary chemical potential. The Fourier coefficient approach differs from earlier lattice studies that estimated coefficients by fitting truncated fugacity expansions (D'Elia, 2007; Takahashi, 2014; Bornyakov, 2016; Boyda, 2017), and from the method of Huovinen (2017) that connected virial coefficients to conserved charge fluctuations at zero chemical potential. The results enable future studies of the baryon-rich region of the QCD phase diagram that is inaccessible to direct lattice simulations, and suggest the need to reconcile the excluded-volume model with the van der Waals hadron resonance gas model that describes nuclear ground state properties.
