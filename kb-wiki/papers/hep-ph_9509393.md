# The Strangeness Radius and Magnetic Moment of the Nucleon Revisited

> We update Jaffe's estimate of the strange isoscalar radius and magnetic moment of the nucleon. We make use of a recent dispersion--theoretical fit to the nucleon electromagnetic form factors and an improved description of symmetry breaking in the vector nonet. We find $μ_s = -0.24 \pm 0.03$~n.m. and $r_s^2 = 0.21 \pm 0.03$~fm$^2$. The strange formfactor $F_2^s (t)$ follows a dipole with a cut--off mass of 1.46~GeV, $F_2^s (t)= μ_s (1-t/2.14 \, {\rm GeV}^2 )^{-2}$. These numbers should be considered as upper limits on the strange vector current matrix--elements in the nucleon.

## Key claim

The authors update Jaffe's (1989) estimate of the strange quark contributions to the nucleon's isoscalar radius and magnetic moment, finding values that should be considered upper limits because the assumed large-momentum-transfer fall-off of the strange form factors may be too slow.

## Method

The authors use a recent dispersion-theoretical fit to the nucleon electromagnetic form factors (Merchant, Miller, and Drechsel, 1995) that incorporates perturbative QCD constraints at high momentum transfer, the neutron-atom scattering length determination of the neutron charge radius, and new data across a wide range of momentum transfer. They combine this with an improved description of symmetry breaking in the vector meson nonet (Harada and Schechter, 1995) to extract the strange Dirac and Pauli form factors from the isoscalar channel, assuming the second pole in the isoscalar form factors corresponds to the phi(1020) meson.

## Key numbers

- Strange magnetic moment: μ_s = -0.24 ± 0.03 nuclear magnetons (n.m.)
- Strange squared radius: r_s^2 = 0.21 ± 0.03 fm^2
- Strange Pauli form factor F_2^s(t) follows a dipole form with cutoff mass 1.46 GeV, parametrized as F_2^s(t) = μ_s (1 - t/2.14 GeV^2)^{-2}
- For the alternative mixing angle ε = 0.0325, the values shift to μ_s = -0.25 n.m. and r_s^2 = 0.23 fm^2

## Context

This work updates Jaffe's (1989) original estimate of strange matrix elements in the nucleon, which used the dispersion-theory fits of Höhler et al. (1976). The new analysis incorporates improved constraints from perturbative QCD, better data, and a refined treatment of ω-φ mixing from Harada and Schechter (1995). The results are compatible with the wide range of predictions from hadron models (μ_s = -0.003 to -0.45 n.m., r_s^2 = -0.25 to 0.22 fm^2) and provide upper limits that can be tested by dedicated experiments such as SAMPLE at MIT-Bates and proposals at CEBAF and MAMI.
