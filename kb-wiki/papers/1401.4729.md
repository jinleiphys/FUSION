# Combinatorial level densities by the real-time method

> Levels densities of independent-particle Hamiltonians can be calculated easily by using the real-time representation of the evolution operator together with the fast Fourier transform. We describe the method and implement it with a set of Python programs. Examples are provided for the total and partial levels densities of a heavy deformed nucleus (Dy-164). The partial level densities that may be calculated are the projected ones on neutron number, proton number, azimuthal angular momentum, and parity.

## Key claim

The real-time method using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) provides a simple and transparent way to compute total and partial level densities for independent-particle Hamiltonians, including projections onto neutron number, proton number, azimuthal angular momentum, and parity, but the method is only fast if the number of simultaneous projections is limited (three-dimensional transforms take on the order of an hour on a laptop, and higher-order transforms would be quite time-consuming).

## Method

The method computes the trace of the time evolution operator G(t) = Tr(exp(-iHt)) = product over orbitals of (1 + exp(-i*epsilon_i*t)), then applies a discrete Fast Fourier Transform to obtain the level density as a function of energy. For partial level densities, additional Fourier transforms are performed over gauge angles for particle number and rotation angles for angular momentum, requiring multi-dimensional FFTs. For projections beyond three dimensions, the authors use a Gaussian approximation based on the first and second moments extracted from small-angle Fourier transforms.

## Key numbers

- Total number of states in the single-particle space for the Dy-164 calculation: 2^40 = 1.1 × 10^12
- Energy binning used: ΔE = 0.2 MeV
- Number of single-particle orbitals used: N_p = 40
- Excitation energy range of the single-particle space: from zero to ~120 MeV
- Number of time points required for the FFT: at least 600 (120/0.2)
- The three-fold projection (N, Z, J_z) reduces the level density by more than two orders of magnitude in the 5-10 MeV range of excitation energies
- The approximate Gaussian projection method gives results indistinguishable from exact folding for energies over 6 MeV

## Context

This work extends the one-dimensional FFT approach first used by Berger and Martinot (1974) for level densities, providing a more transparent formulation using the trace of the real-time Green's function that can be easily implemented with multi-dimensional FFTs. The method enables calculation of partial level densities for heavy deformed nuclei like Dy-164, which is useful for building independent-particle level densities as a starting point for more sophisticated approaches such as the interacting shell model (see reference se13) and shell-model Monte Carlo (see reference al99). The codes are made available for download, allowing others to compute level densities with projections onto neutron number, proton number, azimuthal angular momentum, and parity.
