# New Developments in Relativistic Viscous Hydrodynamics

> Starting with a brief introduction into the basics of relativistic fluid dynamics, I discuss our current knowledge of a relativistic theory of fluid dynamics in the presence of (mostly shear) viscosity. Derivations based on the generalized second law of thermodynamics, kinetic theory, and a complete second-order gradient expansion are reviewed. The resulting fluid dynamic equations are shown to be consistent for all these derivations, when properly accounting for the respective region of applicability, and can be applied to both weakly and strongly coupled systems. In its modern formulation, relativistic viscous hydrodynamics can directly be solved numerically. This has been useful for the problem of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions, and I will review the setup and results of a hydrodynamic description of experimental data for this case.

## Key claim

Relativistic viscous hydrodynamics can be formulated in a causal manner by including second-order gradient terms (such as relaxation times), but the relativistic Navier-Stokes equation alone is acausal and cannot be solved numerically for general flow profiles; the Müller-Israel-Stewart theory derived from an extended entropy current or kinetic theory provides a causal extension, though the validity of the algebraic entropy current assumption remains questionable.

## Method

The paper reviews three derivations of relativistic viscous hydrodynamics: (1) from the generalized second law of thermodynamics using an extended entropy current that includes quadratic terms in the viscous stress tensor (Müller-Israel-Stewart theory), (2) from kinetic theory via the Boltzmann equation with a small out-of-equilibrium correction to the distribution function, and (3) from a complete second-order gradient expansion of the energy-momentum tensor. The causality properties are analyzed by studying small perturbations around equilibrium and computing the dispersion relations for transverse shear modes and longitudinal sound modes, extracting the limiting group velocities at large wavenumber.

## Key numbers

- The diffusion equation for transverse velocity perturbations gives a dispersion relation \(\omega = \frac{\eta_0}{\epsilon_0+p_0} k^2\), leading to a diffusion speed \(v_T(k) = 2 \frac{\eta_0}{\epsilon_0+p_0} k\) that grows without bound with wavenumber \(k\)
- The Maxwell-Cattaneo law introduces a relaxation time \(\tau_\pi\) and gives a finite maximum propagation speed for shear waves: \(v_T^{\rm max} = \sqrt{\frac{\eta_0}{(\epsilon_0+p_0)\tau_\pi}}\)
- For sound modes in Müller-Israel-Stewart theory, the limiting group velocity at large wavenumber is \(v_L^{\rm max} = \sqrt{c_s^2 + \frac{4}{3} \frac{\eta_0}{\tau_\pi(\epsilon_0+p_0)} + \frac{\zeta_0}{\tau_\Pi(\epsilon_0+p_0)}}\)
- For an ultrarelativistic Boltzmann gas, the equilibrium energy density is \(\epsilon = \frac{3 T^4}{\pi^2}\) and the pressure is \(p = \frac{T^4}{\pi^2}\), giving the equation of state \(\epsilon = 3p\) with speed of sound squared \(c_s^2 = \frac{1}{3}\)

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on relativistic viscous hydrodynamics by Eckart and Landau-Lifshitz, which suffered from acausality as shown by Hiscock and Lindblom (1983). It builds on the Müller-Israel-Stewart theory developed by Müller (1967) and Israel and Stewart (1979), and connects to kinetic theory approaches such as those by Israel and Stewart (1979). The paper enables numerical simulations of relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC by providing causal viscous hydrodynamic equations that can be solved directly, and it identifies open questions regarding the validity of the algebraic entropy current assumption, with recent work by Loganayagam (2008), Bhattacharyya et al. (2008), and Romatschke (2009) suggesting the entropy current may not be purely algebraic.
