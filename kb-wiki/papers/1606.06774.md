# Elementary quantum mechanics of the neutron with an electric dipole moment

> The neutron, in addition to possibly having a permanent electric dipole moment as a consequence of violation of time-reversal invariance, develops an induced electric dipole moment in the presence of an external electric field. We present here a unified non-relativistic description of these two phenomena, in which the dipole moment operator, $\vec{\cal D}$, is not constrained to lie along the spin operator. Although the expectation value of $\vec{\cal D}$ in the neutron is less than $10^{-13}$ of the neutron radius, $r_n$, the expectation value of $\vec {\cal D}\,^2$ is of order $r_n^2$. We determine the spin motion in external electric and magnetic fields, as employed in past and future searches for a permanent dipole moment, and show that the neutron electric polarizability, although entering the neutron energy in an external electric field, does not affect the spin motion. In a simple non-relativistic model we show that the expectation value of the permanent dipole is, to lowest order, proportional to the product of the time reversal-violating coupling strength and the electric polarizability of the neutron.

# Elementary quantum mechanics of the neutron with an electric dipole moment

## Key claim

The neutron's electric dipole moment operator is not constrained to lie along the spin operator, even though the expectation value of the dipole moment must align with the spin in the absence of external fields; the induced dipole moment from an external electric field is parallel to the field, not the spin, and the electric polarizability does not affect spin precession in searches for a permanent dipole moment.

## Method

The authors develop a non-relativistic quantum mechanical description of the neutron in which the dipole moment operator and spin operator are independent vector operators obeying independent commutation relations. They write the neutron ground state as a superposition of the CP-conserving state and a small admixture of odd-parity excited states mixed in by time-reversal-violating interactions, and compute expectation values of the dipole operator in the presence and absence of external electric and magnetic fields. They also introduce a phenomenological CP-violating interaction Hamiltonian proportional to the dot product of the spin and dipole operators to illustrate the relationship between the permanent dipole moment and the electric polarizability.

## Key numbers

- The current experimental upper limit on the neutron electric dipole moment is $3\times 10^{-26}$ e-cm (ILL experiment).
- The expectation value of the dipole operator in the neutron is less than $10^{-13}$ of the neutron radius $r_n$.
- The expectation value of the square of the dipole operator, $\langle \vec{\cal D}^2 \rangle$, is of order $r_n^2$, estimated as approximately $0.77$ fm$^2$ considering only the N(1520) and N(1535) excited states.
- The neutron electric polarizability $\chi_n = 1.26 \times 10^{-3}$ fm$^3$; equivalently, $\chi_n E = 0.86 \times 10^{-31} E_4$ e-cm, where $E_4$ is the applied field in units of $10^4$ V/cm.
- Even with a static laboratory electric field of $10^6$ V/cm, the induced dipole moment is some 300 times smaller than the current experimental upper bound on the permanent dipole moment.
- The effective mass associated with the dipole moment is estimated to be approximately 130 MeV.
- The CP-violating coupling constant $a$ in the phenomenological model is bounded above by $a < 10^{-11}$ fm$^{-2}$.

## Context

This work provides a unified non-relativistic framework for understanding both the permanent and induced electric dipole moments of the neutron, extending the formal treatment dating back to Salpeter (1958) and the Wigner-Eckart theorem arguments. The paper complements the principal focus of previous work on the neutron electric dipole moment, which concentrated on the response to external fields, and relates the permanent dipole moment to the electric polarizability measured in Compton scattering experiments by Schmiedmayer et al. (1991), Baranov et al. (1997), and Kossert et al. (2002). The results enable future interpretation of ongoing experimental searches for the neutron electric dipole moment at facilities such as the Spallation Neutron Source, and raise questions about extending the picture to the proton, atomic nuclei, and the electron.
