# Probing the neutron-skin of unstable nuclei with heavy ion collisions

> To improve the constraints of symmetry energy at subsaturation density, measuring and accumulating more neutron skin data for neutron rich unstable nuclei is naturally required. Aiming to probe the neutron skin of unstable nuclei by using low-intermediate energy heavy ion collisions, we develop a new version of improved quantum molecular dynamics model, in which the neutron skin of the initial nucleus and the mean field potential in nucleon propagation are consistently treated. Our calculations show that the three observables, such as the cross sections of the primary projectile-like residues with $A>100$ ($σ_{A>100}$), the difference of $σ_{A>100}$ between $^{132}$Sn+$^{124}$Sn and $^{124}$Sn+$^{124}$Sn systems ($δσ_{A>100}$), and the neutron to proton yield ratio ($R(n/p)$) in the transverse direction, could be used to measure the neutron skin of the unstable nuclei and to constrain the slope of the symmetry energy in the future.

# Probing the neutron-skin of unstable nuclei with heavy ion collisions

## Key claim
The authors develop a version of the improved quantum molecular dynamics model (ImQMD-L) in which the neutron skin of the initial nucleus and the mean-field potential during nucleon propagation are treated consistently using the same Skyrme energy density functional. They claim that three observables, the cross section of primary projectile-like residues with A>100, the difference of that cross section between 132Sn+124Sn and 124Sn+124Sn systems, and the neutron-to-proton yield ratio in the transverse direction, could be used to measure the neutron skin of unstable nuclei and constrain the slope of the symmetry energy, but only if the systematic uncertainties from the transport model can be suppressed.

## Method
The authors used the restricted density variational (RDV) method with the same Skyrme energy density functional as in the ImQMD-L model to compute Woods-Saxon type neutron and proton density distributions for 124Sn and 132Sn, obtaining half-density radii and diffuseness parameters for five values of the symmetry energy slope L (30, 50, 70, 90, 110 MeV). They then initialized the ImQMD-L model by sampling nucleon wave-packet centroids within the neutron and proton half-density radii from the RDV results, and simulated peripheral collisions of 124Sn+124Sn and 132Sn+124Sn at 200 MeV/u with impact parameters from 5 fm to b_max, running 5000 events per impact parameter.

## Key numbers
- For 124Sn, the neutron skin thickness Δr_np ranges from 0.165 fm (L=30 MeV) to 0.263 fm (L=110 MeV); for 132Sn, Δr_np ranges from 0.232 fm (L=30 MeV) to 0.361 fm (L=110 MeV).
- For 124Sn+124Sn, the cross section σ_{A>100} increases by a factor of about 5.2% as ΔR (sum of projectile and target neutron skins) increases from 0.33 fm to 0.526 fm (L from 30 to 110 MeV).
- For 132Sn+124Sn, σ_{A>100} increases by a factor of about 5.4% as ΔR increases from 0.397 fm to 0.624 fm.
- The probability P_{A>100}(b) becomes nonzero in the impact parameter range b=6.6-10.0 fm for 124Sn+124Sn and b=6.2-9.2 fm for 132Sn+124Sn.
- Projectile-like residues are distributed within θ_cm < 4 degrees.
- The neutron-to-proton yield ratio R(n/p) for emitted nucleons with kinetic energy E_k < 80 MeV is greater for thin neutron skin (small L) than for thick neutron skin (large L).

## Context
This work extends previous efforts to constrain the symmetry energy at subsaturation density using neutron skin data, particularly following the tension created by the PREX-II result on 208Pb (Adhikari 2021) which favored a stiff symmetry energy (L=106±37 MeV). The authors build on earlier transport model calculations by Hartnack (2018) and Wei (2014) that treated the neutron skin and the mean-field potential separately, and they aim to reduce theoretical uncertainties by using a consistent Skyrme energy density functional for both the initial nuclear structure and the dynamical evolution. The proposed observables could enable future measurements of neutron skins for very neutron-rich unstable nuclei produced at next-generation isotope facilities via projectile fragmentation.
