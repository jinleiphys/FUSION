# Formation of light (anti)nuclei

> The production mechanism of light nuclei, such as deuteron, helium-3, tritium and their antiparticles, has recently attracted an increased attention from the astroparticle and heavy ion communities. The expected low astrophysical background of light antinulei makes them ideal probes for exotic astrophysical processes, such as dark matter annihilations. At the same time, they can be used to measure two-nucleon correlations and density fluctuations in heavy ion collisions, which may shed light on the QCD phase diagram. Motivated by the importance of light antinuclei in cosmic ray studies, we developed a new coalescence model for light (anti)nuclei that includes both the size of the formation region, which is process dependent, and momentum correlations in a semi-classical picture. We have employed the model as an afterburner to the event generators Pythia 8 and QGSJET II, and find that the model agrees well with experimental data on antideuteron and antihelium-3 production in $e^+e^-$, $pp$, $p$Be and $p$Al collisions at various energies. In this paper, we review this model and update existing fits to experimental data based on new insights.

## Key claim

The WiFunC (Wigner Function with Correlations) coalescence model removes the apparent energy dependence of the coalescence momentum \(p_0\) seen in earlier analyses by explicitly including the process-dependent size of the nucleon formation region, and the model agrees with experimental data using a single universal spatial spread parameter \(\sigma = (1.0 \pm 0.1)\ \mathrm{fm}\).

## Method

The author reviews the WiFunC model, which computes the probability that a given nucleon pair or triplet coalesces into a light nucleus by projecting the two-nucleon Wigner function onto the nucleus Wigner function, using a semi-classical factorization of spatial and momentum distributions. The spatial nucleon distributions are taken as Gaussians with a process-dependent width \(\sigma\) that combines a point-like contribution (from perturbative cascade and hadronisation) with a geometrical contribution from the finite size of the colliding particles. The model is implemented as an afterburner to the event generators Pythia 8 and QGSJET II, and the single free parameter \(\sigma\) is fitted to experimental data on antideuteron and antihelium-3 production in \(e^+e^-\), \(pp\), \(p\)Be, and \(p\)Al collisions at various energies.

## Key numbers

- The best-fit spatial spread parameter for antideuteron production is \(\sigma = (1.0 \pm 0.1)\ \mathrm{fm}\), constant across collision systems and center-of-mass energies.
- For antihelium-3 production at 7 TeV measured by ALICE, fitting with \(b = 3^{1/3}\sigma\) yields \(\sigma = (0.93 \pm 0.04)\ \mathrm{fm}\) for Pythia 8 and \(\sigma = (1.07 \pm 0.03)\ \mathrm{fm}\) for QGSJET II, consistent with the antideuteron fits.
- The deuteron wave function is approximated by a sum of two Gaussians with parameters \(\Delta = 0.581\), \(d_1 = 3.979\ \mathrm{fm}\), and \(d_2 = 0.890\ \mathrm{fm}\).
- The helium-3 rms charge radius is \(r_\mathrm{rms}^{\mathrm{^3He}} = 1.96\ \mathrm{fm}\); the tritium rms charge radius is \(r_\mathrm{rms}^{t} = 1.76\ \mathrm{fm}\).
- The geometrical contribution to the spatial spread for nuclei scales as \(R_A \simeq a_0 A^{1/3}\) with \(a_0 \simeq 1\ \mathrm{fm}\).

## Context

This work extends the WiFunC model developed by Kachelriess et al. (2019, 2020) and updates fits to experimental data from ALICE (2018) and other experiments, addressing the energy dependence of the coalescence momentum \(p_0\) noted by Gomez-Coral et al. (2018). The model provides a unified description of light (anti)nucleus production in small interacting systems (\(e^+e^-\), \(pp\), \(p\)N collisions) and can be used to predict antinuclei fluxes for dark matter searches by AMS-02 and GAPS, as well as to interpret two-nucleon correlation measurements in heavy ion collisions. Future work should test the model at low energies and in forward directions, and could exploit space-time information from event generators like Pythia 8 or UrQMD to evaluate the coalescence probability directly.
