# Branching ratio and information entropy for the p+$^{12}$C reaction in the extended-quantum-molecular-dynamics model

> The reactions of p+$^{12}$C with triangular 3$α$ structure and spherical structure with incident energies ranging from 5 to 200 MeV/nucleon are simulated by the in the extended-quantum-molecular-dynamics (EQMD) model together with the GEMINI decay process. This paper presents the incident energy dependence of the branching ratios of fragment production and multiplicity, as well as the associated event information entropy. The results indicate that the triangular 3$α$ $^{12}$C has an extra branching ratio for the quasi-elastic reaction compared to the spherical $^{12}$C. This peculiarity of the branching ratios of the triangular 3$α$ $^{12}$C appears as a small dent in the curves of the event information entropy (both the fragment information entropy and the multiplicity information entropy). Therefore, we propose that the event information entropy could be a probe for detecting the $α$-cluster structure.

# Branching ratio and information entropy for the p+$^{12}$C reaction in the extended-quantum-molecular-dynamics model

## Key claim
The event information entropy (both fragment information entropy and multiplicity information entropy) could serve as a probe for detecting the $\alpha$-cluster structure in $^{12}$C, because the triangular 3$\alpha$ $^{12}$C produces a small dent in the entropy curves at around 50 MeV incident energy that is absent for spherical $^{12}$C. This dent corresponds to an extra quasi-elastic reaction component in the triangular 3$\alpha$ structure, but the effect is most clearly visible in the direct dynamics results (EQMD only) and becomes partially smeared by the GEMINI decay process.

## Method
The authors used the extended-quantum-molecular-dynamics (EQMD) model to simulate p+$^{12}$C reactions at incident energies from 5 to 200 MeV/nucleon, with two different initial $^{12}$C structures: a triangular 3$\alpha$-cluster configuration (binding energy 87.1 MeV) and a spherical Wood-Saxon distribution (binding energy 104.5 MeV). For each structure, 10$^6$ events were generated with impact parameters from 0 to 4 fm, and the primary fragments from EQMD were subsequently de-excited using the GEMINI statistical decay model, with each fragment undergoing ten decay simulations. Fragment branching ratios, multiplicity branching ratios, and their associated event information entropies were computed and compared between the two structures, both with and without the GEMINI decay stage.

## Key numbers
- Incident energy range: 5 to 200 MeV/nucleon for the proton beam
- Binding energy of triangular 3$\alpha$ $^{12}$C: 87.1 MeV; spherical $^{12}$C: 104.5 MeV
- Event statistics: 10$^6$ events per structure; each fragment undergoes 10 GEMINI decay simulations
- Impact parameter range: 0 to 4 fm
- The extra quasi-elastic branching ratio for triangular $^{12}$C peaks at approximately $E \approx 50$ MeV in the direct dynamics results
- The decay threshold for light fragments ($^{2}$H, $^{3}$H, $^{3}$He, $^{4}$He) is shifted by about 15 MeV to higher energy for spherical $^{12}$C compared to triangular $^{12}$C
- The dent in the fragment information entropy for triangular $^{12}$C appears at $E \approx 50$ MeV (direct dynamics) and at $E \approx 75$ MeV (after decay process)
- The dent in the multiplicity information entropy for triangular $^{12}$C appears at $E \approx 50$ MeV

## Context
This work extends the use of event information entropy, originally proposed by Ma (2000) to study nuclear liquid-gas phase transitions in heavy-ion collisions, to the problem of $\alpha$-cluster structure detection in light nuclei. The EQMD model, developed by Maruyama et al., is employed because it can describe $\alpha$-cluster structures and nuclear ground states, unlike standard QMD models. The study builds on prior work by He et al. and Huang et al. on $\alpha$-cluster structures in light nuclei, and on Deng et al. who showed that information entropy is indicative of QCD phase changes in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. The results suggest that event information entropy could serve as a new experimental observable for identifying $\alpha$-clustering, complementing traditional approaches such as knockout reactions and transfer reactions.
