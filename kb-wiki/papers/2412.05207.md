# Masquerading hybrid stars with dark matter

> We investigate the influence of dark matter on hybrid stars. Using a two-fluid approach, where normal and dark matter components interact only gravitationally, we explore how dark matter can trigger the appearance of quark matter in neutron stars for unprecedented low masses. Our findings reveal that dark matter increases the central pressure of neutron stars, potentially leading to the formation of hybrid stars with quark cores even at very low compact star masses. The critical mass for the appearance of quark matter decreases with increasing dark matter content. We introduce the concept of "masquerading hybrid stars", where dark matter admixed stars exhibit similar mass-radius relations to purely hadronic stars, making it challenging to distinguish between them based solely on these parameters. Additionally, we identify a unique class of objects termed "dark oysters", characterized by a large dark matter halo and a small normal matter core, highlighting the diverse structural possibilities for compact stars influenced by dark matter.

## Key claim

Dark matter can trigger the appearance of quark matter in neutron stars at unprecedentedly low masses, producing "masquerading hybrid stars" whose mass-radius relations closely resemble those of purely hadronic stars; however, this effect depends on the specific choice of dark matter particle mass and interaction strength, and the hybrid EoS parameters are chosen so that without dark matter no stable hybrid stars exist.

## Method

The authors solve the coupled two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations for normal matter (hadronic and quark phases) and dark matter, which interact only gravitationally. The normal matter hybrid EoS is constructed via Maxwell construction from a Quantum Hadrodynamics (QHD) model (NL3*ωρ parametrization) for nucleons and hyperons and a modified MIT Bag model for u-d-s quark matter, while the dark matter is modeled as a non-self-annihilating self-interacting Fermi gas with particle masses of 5 GeV and 100 GeV and interaction strengths ranging from weakly (y=10⁻¹) to strongly (y=10³) interacting. Stability is assessed using the criterion that the total number of fluid elements of each species must be stationary under variations of central energy density.

## Key numbers

- Dark matter particle masses considered: m_D = 5 GeV and m_D = 100 GeV
- Dark matter interaction strength parameter y explored: from y = 10⁻¹ (weakly interacting) to y = 10³ (strongly interacting)
- Hadron-quark phase transition occurs at pressure P₀ = 361 MeV/fm³ and baryon chemical potential μ₀ = 1698 MeV, with hadronic energy density ε_H = 1003 MeV/fm³ and quark energy density ε_Q = 1280 MeV/fm³
- Nuclear saturation density n₀ = 0.150 fm⁻³, with saturation energy E/A = 16.3 MeV, compression modulus K = 258 MeV, symmetry energy E_sym = 30.7 MeV, and slope parameter L = 42 MeV
- Maximum pressure for the normal matter EoS is limited to 1000 MeV/fm³, corresponding to a density around 12 n₀
- Bag pressure B^(1/4) = 155 MeV, vector coupling G_V = 1.0 fm², and b₄ = 1.2 for the quark matter model

## Context

This work extends the study of dark matter effects on hybrid stars beyond previous analyses by Lenzi (2023) and Pal (2024), which considered only one fluid with normal matter interacting with dark matter through meson exchange, whereas the present work is the first to use a two-fluid approach where the components interact only gravitationally. The findings introduce the concept of "masquerading hybrid stars" that mimic purely hadronic stars in mass-radius relations, and identify a new class of objects called "dark oysters" with a large dark matter halo and a small normal matter core, opening the possibility that dark matter-admixed stars may be observationally indistinguishable from standard neutron stars.
