# Normalization of neutron-proton differential cross sections

> The np differential cross section below 350 MeV neutron laboratory energy is studied using the energy-dependent Nijmegen partial-wave analysis PWA93. We analyze in detail three experiments, performed at LAMPF, at TRIUMF, and at the TSL facility in Uppsala. The issue of normalization of $np$ cross sections is discussed, where we distinguish between measured and calculated normalizations. This analysis leads to improved treatments of the LAMPF and TRIUMF data over PWA93. It is shown that the LAMPF and TRIUMF data at Tlab = 212 MeV are in good agreement with PWA93, that the LAMPF data at Tlab = 162 MeV is also in good agreement with PWA93, but that the Uppsala data at 162 MeV is in strong disagreement with PWA93. The reason for the disagreement is, almost certainly, a systematic flaw in the slope of the Uppsala data.

# Normalization of neutron-proton differential cross sections

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that careful treatment of relative normalizations between angular subsets of np differential cross section data can significantly improve fits to partial-wave analyses, but that the Uppsala data at 162 MeV contains systematic slope discrepancies that make it impossible to include in a statistically consistent data base, contrary to the claim that LAMPF data dominates the PWA solutions.

## Method

The authors used the energy-dependent Nijmegen partial-wave analysis PWA93 to reanalyze np differential cross section data from three experiments (LAMPF, TRIUMF, and Uppsala) at neutron laboratory energies of 162 MeV and approximately 212 MeV. They distinguished between "measured" normalizations (experimentally determined with associated errors) and "calculated" normalizations (determined via model calculations), and introduced separate relative normalization factors for each angular subset of the data, allowing these to be floated with assigned errors in the chi-squared minimization.

## Key numbers

- LAMPF data at 162.0 MeV: 42 data points after removing one outlier, chi-squared = 56 with absolute normalization N_abs = 1.090(7)
- LAMPF data at 211.5 MeV: 43 data points, chi-squared = 31 with absolute normalization N_abs determined to 0.7% accuracy or better
- TRIUMF P-phase at 212 MeV: 39 data points, after floating relative normalization of the most forward subset, chi-squared(data) = 29.6 with chi-squared(norm) = 7.7
- Uppsala data at 162 MeV: 54 data points, chi-squared = 393 with N_abs = 1.0109(24), which is 33 standard deviations from the expected chi-squared of 53 +/- 10
- For the Uppsala data, the slope difference between subsets 4 and 5 in the overlap region (151-167 degrees) is b_5 - b_4 = 0.034(13), a 2.6 standard deviation discrepancy
- With relative normalizations introduced, the LAMPF data F-ratio improved from 1.038 (PWA93) to 1.025, and the Uppsala data F-ratio improved from 5.34 to 2.92 (still unacceptable)

## Context

This work extends the Nijmegen partial-wave analysis PWA93 (Stoks et al., 1993) by improving the treatment of normalization for np differential cross section data. The analysis contradicts claims by the Uppsala group (Eriksson et al., 1995; Rahm et al., 1997, 1998) that LAMPF data dominated the PWA solutions; the authors show that even when all LAMPF data is removed, the Uppsala data still shows 27 standard deviation discrepancies with the remaining data base including spin observables. The improved normalization techniques enable more data to be retained in future partial-wave analyses, with calculated normalizations achieving 0.4-0.8% accuracy compared to 2-10% for measured normalizations.
