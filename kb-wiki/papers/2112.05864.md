# Probing the incompressibility of nuclear matter at ultra-high density through the prompt collapse of asymmetric neutron star binaries

> Using 250 neutron star merger simulations with microphysics, we explore for the first time the role of nuclear incompressibility in the prompt collapse threshold for binaries with different mass ratios. We demonstrate that observations of prompt collapse thresholds, either from binaries with two different mass ratios or with one mass ratio but combined with the knowledge of the maximum neutron star mass or compactness, will constrain the incompressibility at the maximum neutron star density, $K_{\rm max}$, to within tens of percent. This, otherwise inaccessible, measure of $K_{\rm max}$ can potentially reveal the presence of hyperons or quarks inside neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that observations of the prompt collapse threshold in neutron star mergers at two different mass ratios, or at one mass ratio combined with knowledge of the maximum neutron star mass or compactness, can constrain the nuclear incompressibility at the maximum neutron star density, \(K_{\rm max}\), to within tens of percent. This measurement is otherwise inaccessible and can potentially reveal the presence of hyperons or quarks inside neutron stars.

## Method

The authors performed 250 numerical relativity simulations of irrotational binary neutron star mergers with finite-temperature, composition-dependent microphysical equations of state, covering total gravitational masses from 2.786 to 3.3 solar masses and mass ratios \(q\) from 0.6 to 1.0. They determined the threshold mass for prompt collapse to a black hole, \(M_{\rm th}(q)\), for each mass ratio by monitoring the maximum rest-mass density throughout the computational domain, and then fitted the ratio \(f(q) \equiv M_{\rm th}(q)/M_{\rm th}(q=1)\) with a bilinear function in \(q\) separated at \(\tilde{q}=0.725\), extracting slopes \(\alpha_l\) and \(\alpha_h\) that correlate with \(K_{\rm max}\).

## Key numbers

* The threshold mass ratio \(f(q) \equiv M_{\rm th}(q)/M_{\rm th}(q=1)\) varies between 3% and 8% across a factor of 1.7 in \(q\), with larger variations for equations of state having smaller \(K_{\rm max}\).
* The fitted slopes are \(\alpha_l = -(22 \pm 1)~{\rm TeV}^{-1} K_{\rm max} + (0.58 \pm 0.01)\) and \(\alpha_h = -(4.7 \pm 1.0)~{\rm TeV}^{-1} K_{\rm max} + (0.064 \pm 0.017)\).
* Using \(M_{\rm th}(q=0.65)\) and \(M_{\rm th}(q=0.85)\) for the SFHo equation of state, \(K_{\rm max}\) is recovered to within 2% of its actual value; using \(M_{\rm th}(q=0.7)\) and \(M_{\rm th}(q=0.85)\) recovers it to within 15%.
* Using \(M_{\rm th}(q=0.7)\) and \(M_{\rm th}(q=1)\) recovers \(K_{\rm max}\) within 3.5%.
* For the independent TNTYST equation of state, using \(M_{\rm th}(q=0.7)\) and \(M_{\rm th}(q=0.9)\) recovers \(K_{\rm max}\) to within 25% (approximately 6 GeV).
* To determine \(K_{\rm max}\) with at least 30% accuracy at 90% confidence level using \(q=0.85\) and \(q=0.65\), the required uncertainties are \(\Delta M_{\rm th} \lesssim 0.025 M_\odot\) and \(\Delta q \lesssim 0.05\); for \(q=0.85\) and \(q=0.70\), the requirements are \(\Delta M_{\rm th} \lesssim 0.01 M_\odot\) and \(\Delta q \lesssim 0.025\).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of prompt collapse in equal-mass binaries by Shibata (2005), Hotokezaka (2011), Bauswein (2013), Koppel (2019), and Kashyap (2021), and in asymmetric binaries by Bauswein (2020) and Tootle (2021), by using the largest set of numerical relativity simulations with microphysical equations of state to date. The results contradict the suggestion of a quasi-universal relation by Tootle (2021), instead revealing a systematic equation-of-state dependence that is controlled by the incompressibility at maximum density. This enables a new method to constrain the nuclear incompressibility at densities 4-7 times nuclear saturation density, which is otherwise inaccessible, and to discriminate between purely nucleonic equations of state and those containing hyperons or undergoing a phase transition to quark matter.
